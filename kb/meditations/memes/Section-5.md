# Section 5

(005-001) XV. In the country of the Quadi at Granua, these. Betimes in the morning

(005-002) say to thyself, This day I shalt have to do with an idle curious man,

(005-003) with an unthankful man, a railer, a crafty, false, or an envious man; an

(005-004) unsociable uncharitable man. All these ill qualities have happened unto

(005-005) them, through ignorance of that which is truly good and truly bad. But I

(005-006) that understand the nature of that which is good, that it only is to

(005-007) be desired, and of that which is bad, that it only is truly odious and

(005-008) shameful: who know moreover, that this transgressor, whosoever he be, is

(005-009) my kinsman, not by the same blood and seed, but by participation of the

(005-010) same reason, and of the same divine particle; How can I either be

(005-011) hurt by any of those, since it is not in their power to make me incur

(005-012) anything that is truly reproachful? or angry, and ill affected towards

(005-013) him, who by nature is so near unto me? for we are all born to be

(005-014) fellow-workers, as the feet, the hands, and the eyelids; as the rows of

(005-015) the upper and under teeth: for such therefore to be in opposition, is

(005-016) against nature; and what is it to chafe at, and to be averse from, but

(005-017) to be in opposition?

(005-018) XVI. Whatsoever I am, is either flesh, or life, or that which we

(005-019) commonly call the mistress and overruling part of man; reason. Away with

(005-020) thy books, suffer not thy mind any more to be distracted, and carried to

(005-021) and fro; for it will not be; but as even now ready to die, think little

(005-022) of thy flesh: blood, bones, and a skin; a pretty piece of knit and

(005-023) twisted work, consisting of nerves, veins and arteries; think no more of

(005-024) it, than so. And as for thy life, consider what it is; a wind; not one

(005-025) constant wind neither, but every moment of an hour let out, and sucked

(005-026) in again. The third, is thy ruling part; and here consider; Thou art an

(005-027) old man; suffer not that excellent part to be brought in subjection, and

(005-028) to become slavish: suffer it not to be drawn up and down with

(005-029) unreasonable and unsociable lusts and motions, as it were with wires and

(005-030) nerves; suffer it not any more, either to repine at anything now

(005-031) present, or to fear and fly anything to come, which the destiny hath

(005-032) appointed thee.

(005-033) XVII. Whatsoever proceeds from the gods immediately, that any man will

(005-034) grant totally depends from their divine providence. As for those

(005-035) things that are commonly said to happen by fortune, even those must be

(005-036) conceived to have dependence from nature, or from that first and general

(005-037) connection, and concatenation of all those things, which more apparently

(005-038) by the divine providence are administered and brought to pass.

(005-039) All things flow from thence: and whatsoever it is that is, is both

(005-040) necessary, and conducing to the whole (part of which thou art), and

(005-041) whatsoever it is that is requisite and necessary for the preservation of

(005-042) the general, must of necessity for every particular nature, be good and

(005-043) behoveful. And as for the whole, it is preserved, as by the perpetual

(005-044) mutation and conversion of the simple elements one into another, so

(005-045) also by the mutation, and alteration of things mixed and compounded. Let

(005-046) these things suffice thee; let them be always unto thee, as thy general

(005-047) rules and precepts. As for thy thirst after books, away with it with all

(005-048) speed, that thou die not murmuring and complaining, but truly meek and

(005-049) well satisfied, and from thy heart thankful unto the gods.

(005-050) THE SECOND BOOK

(005-051) I. Remember how long thou hast already put off these things, and how

(005-052) often a certain day and hour as it were, having been set unto thee by

(005-053) the gods, thou hast neglected it. It is high time for thee to understand

(005-054) the true nature both of the world, whereof thou art a part; and of that

(005-055) Lord and Governor of the world, from whom, as a channel from the spring,

(005-056) thou thyself didst flow: and that there is but a certain limit of time

(005-057) appointed unto thee, which if thou shalt not make use of to calm and

(005-058) allay the many distempers of thy soul, it will pass away and thou with

(005-059) it, and never after return.

(005-060) II. Let it be thy earnest and incessant care as a Roman and a man to

(005-061) perform whatsoever it is that thou art about, with true and unfeigned

(005-062) gravity, natural affection, freedom and justice: and as for all other

(005-063) cares, and imaginations, how thou mayest ease thy mind of them. Which

(005-064) thou shalt do; if thou shalt go about every action as thy last action,

(005-065) free from all vanity, all passionate and wilful aberration from reason,

(005-066) and from all hypocrisy, and self-love, and dislike of those things,

(005-067) which by the fates or appointment of God have happened unto thee. Thou

(005-068) seest that those things, which for a man to hold on in a prosperous

(005-069) course, and to live a divine life, are requisite and necessary, are not

(005-070) many, for the gods will require no more of any man, that shall but keep

(005-071) and observe these things.

(005-072) III. Do, soul, do; abuse and contemn thyself; yet a while and the time

(005-073) for thee to respect thyself, will be at an end. Every man's happiness

(005-074) depends from himself, but behold thy life is almost at an end, whiles

(005-075) affording thyself no respect, thou dost make thy happiness to consist in

(005-076) the souls, and conceits of other men.

(005-077) IV. Why should any of these things that happen externally, so much

(005-078) distract thee? Give thyself leisure to learn some good thing, and cease

(005-079) roving and wandering to and fro. Thou must also take heed of another

(005-080) kind of wandering, for they are idle in their actions, who toil and

(005-081) labour in this life, and have no certain scope to which to direct all

(005-082) their motions, and desires. V. For not observing the state of another

(005-083) man's soul, scarce was ever any man known to be unhappy. Tell whosoever

(005-084) they be that intend not, and guide not by reason and discretion the

(005-085) motions of their own souls, they must of necessity be unhappy.

(005-086) VI. These things thou must always have in mind: What is the nature

(005-087) of the universe, and what is mine--in particular: This unto that what

(005-088) relation it hath: what kind of part, of what kind of universe it is: And

(005-089) that there is nobody that can hinder thee, but that thou mayest always

(005-090) both do and speak those things which are agreeable to that nature,

(005-091) whereof thou art a part.

(005-092) VII. Theophrastus, where he compares sin with sin (as after a vulgar

(005-093) sense such things I grant may be compared:) says well and like a

(005-094) philosopher, that those sins are greater which are committed through

(005-095) lust, than those which are committed through anger. For he that is angry

(005-096) seems with a kind of grief and close contraction of himself, to turn

(005-097) away from reason; but he that sins through lust, being overcome by

(005-098) pleasure, doth in his very sin bewray a more impotent, and unmanlike

(005-099) disposition. Well then and like a philosopher doth he say, that he of

(005-100) the two is the more to be condemned, that sins with pleasure, than he

(005-101) that sins with grief. For indeed this latter may seem first to have been

(005-102) wronged, and so in some manner through grief thereof to have been forced

(005-103) to be angry, whereas he who through lust doth commit anything, did of

(005-104) himself merely resolve upon that action.

(005-105) VIII. Whatsoever thou dost affect, whatsoever thou dost project, so do,

(005-106) and so project all, as one who, for aught thou knowest, may at this very

(005-107) present depart out of this life. And as for death, if there be any gods,

(005-108) it is no grievous thing to leave the society of men. The gods will do

(005-109) thee no hurt, thou mayest be sure. But if it be so that there be no

(005-110) gods, or that they take no care of the world, why should I desire to

(005-111) live in a world void of gods, and of all divine providence? But gods

(005-112) there be certainly, and they take care for the world; and as for those

(005-113) things which be truly evil, as [[evil|vice]] and wickedness, such things they

(005-114) have put in a man's own power, that he might avoid them if he would: and

(005-115) had there been anything besides that had been truly bad and evil, they

(005-116) would have had a care of that also, that a man might have avoided it.

(005-117) But why should that be thought to hurt and prejudice a man's life in

(005-118) this world, which cannot any ways make man himself the better, or the

(005-119) worse in his own person? Neither must we think that the nature of the

(005-120) universe did either through ignorance pass these things, or if not as

(005-121) ignorant of them, yet as unable either to prevent, or better to order

(005-122) and dispose them. It cannot be that she through want either of power or

(005-123) skill, should have committed such a thing, so as to suffer all things

(005-124) both good and bad, equally and promiscuously, to happen unto all both

(005-125) good and bad. As for life therefore, and death, honour and dishonour,

(005-126) labour and pleasure, riches and poverty, all these things happen

(005-127) unto men indeed, both good and bad, equally; but as things which of

(005-128) themselves are neither good nor bad; because of themselves, neither

(005-129) shameful nor praiseworthy.

(005-130) IX. Consider how quickly all things are dissolved and resolved: the

(005-131) bodies and substances themselves, into the matter and substance of the

(005-132) world: and their memories into the general age and time of the world.

(005-133) Consider the nature of all worldly sensible things; of those especially,

(005-134) which either ensnare by pleasure, or for their irksomeness are dreadful,

(005-135) or for their outward lustre and show are in great esteem and request,

(005-136) how vile and contemptible, how base and corruptible, how destitute of

(005-137) all true life and being they are.

(005-138) X. It is the part of a man endowed with a good [[knowledge|understanding]] faculty, to

(005-139) consider what they themselves are in very deed, from whose bare conceits

(005-140) and voices, honour and credit do proceed: as also what it is to die, and

(005-141) how if a man shall consider this by itself alone, to die, and separate

(005-142) from it in his mind all those things which with it usually represent

(005-143) themselves unto us, he can conceive of it no otherwise, than as of a

(005-144) work of nature, and he that fears any work of nature, is a very child.

(005-145) Now death, it is not only a work of nature, but also conducing to

(005-146) nature.

(005-147) XI. Consider with thyself how man, and by what part of his, is joined

(005-148) unto God, and how that part of man is affected, when it is said to be

(005-149) diffused. There is nothing more wretched than that soul, which in a kind

(005-150) of circuit compasseth all things, searching (as he saith) even the very

(005-151) depths of the earth; and by all signs and conjectures prying into the

(005-152) very thoughts of other men's souls; and yet of this, is not sensible,

(005-153) that it is sufficient for a man to apply himself wholly, and to confine

(005-154) all his thoughts and cares to the tendance of that spirit which is

(005-155) within him, and truly and really to serve him. His service doth consist

(005-156) in this, that a man keep himself pure from all violent passion and

(005-157) evil affection, from all rashness and vanity, and from all manner of

(005-158) discontent, either in regard of the gods or men. For indeed whatsoever

(005-159) proceeds from the gods, deserves respect for their worth and excellency;

(005-160) and whatsoever proceeds from men, as they are our kinsmen, should by us

(005-161) be entertained, with love, always; sometimes, as proceeding from their

(005-162) ignorance, of that which is truly good and bad, (a blindness no less,

(005-163) than that by which we are not able to discern between white and black:)

(005-164) with a kind of pity and compassion also.
