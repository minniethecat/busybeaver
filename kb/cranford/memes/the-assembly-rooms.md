# The Assembly Rooms

The Cranford Assembly Rooms occupy a unique position in the town's social geography: a space of aristocratic memory, genteel entertainment, and — in the novel's present — faded grandeur. Attached to the George Inn, the Assembly Room was added "about a hundred years before" by the county families who "met together there once a month during the winter to dance and play at cards" [018-158, 018-159, 018-160, 018-161]. It is, in its decay, the most vivid architectural expression of Cranford's relationship with the aristocracy — not a living connection but a "mouldy odour of aristocracy" that "lingered about the place" [018-169, 018-170].

## Description

The Assembly Room's interior bears the traces of its former splendour and its present dilapidation. The "salmon-coloured paint had faded into a drab; great pieces of plaster had chipped off from the fine wreaths and festoons on its walls" [018-166, 018-167, 018-168, 018-169]. Yet the room retains the power to evoke its past: Miss Matty and Mrs Forrester "bridle up as they entered, and walk mincingly up the room, as if there were a number of genteel observers, instead of two little boys with a stick of toffee between them with which to beguile the time" [018-171, 018-172, 018-173, 018-174, 018-175]. The gap between the room's aristocratic memories and its present reality — toffee-eating boys as the sole audience — is a miniature of Cranford's broader social condition.

Adjoining the Assembly Room is a cloak-room containing "the strange, quaint old mirror" before which Miss Matty adjusts her "pretty new cap" [018-143, 018-144, 018-145, 018-146]. This mirror has reflected generations of Cranford beauty, and Miss Matty's moment before it — "a sigh or two to her departed youth, and the remembrance of the last time she had been there" [018-145] — is one of the novel's most delicate passages of private retrospection within a public space.

## Aristocratic Ghosts

The Assembly Room's history is populated by spectral figures of glamour and misfortune. "Many a county beauty had first swung through the minuet that she afterwards danced before Queen Charlotte in this very room" [018-161, 018-162, 018-163]. It was "said that one of the Gunnings had graced the apartment with her beauty" [018-164] — the Gunning sisters being among the most celebrated beauties of the eighteenth century, their connection lending the room an almost national significance. And "a rich and beautiful widow, Lady Williams, had here been smitten with the noble figure of a young artist" [018-165, 018-166] — a romance that, the narrator adds dryly, ended poorly for the lady: "a pretty bargain poor Lady Williams had of her handsome husband, if all tales were true" [018-167]. The Assembly Room's ghosts are not merely glamorous; they are cautionary.

## The Brunoni Performance

The Assembly Rooms host the novel's most exotic entertainment: Signor Brunoni's magic show. The conjuror is engaged to exhibit "his wonderful magic in the Cranford Assembly Rooms on Wednesday and Friday evening" [017-181, 017-182]. The performance transforms the faded room into a space of cosmopolitan excitement, drawing the entire town. Miss Pole's investigative visit to the Assembly Room — conducted before the official performance, under the pretext of looking for a dropped glove — sets in motion the chain of misunderstandings that culminates in [[the-great-cranford-panic]]. The passage leading to the Assembly Room, divided with "great clothes-maids, over which Crosby's men were tacking" [018-054, 018-055], becomes the stage for Miss Pole's fateful glimpse of the supposed robbers.

Later, after Peter Jenkyns's return, the Assembly Room hosts a culminating social evening with "the signor in the Assembly Room" and "Mrs Jamieson on one side, and my lady, Mrs Gordon, on the other" [034-041, 034-087, 034-088] — the room's faded aristocracy at last re-inhabited by a genuinely reconciled community.
