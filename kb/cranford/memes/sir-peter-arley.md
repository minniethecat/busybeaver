# Sir Peter Arley

Sir Peter Arley is a baronet and landowner whose estate, Arley Hall, lies in the
neighbourhood of Cranford. Though he never appears directly in the novel's
action, his name recurs in the conversation of the Cranford ladies as a
reference point for aristocratic propriety and social connection.

## Aristocratic Patron

Sir Peter Arley represents the landed gentry whose presence — however distant —
shapes the social imagination of Cranford. Miss Jenkyns, in her youth, "used to
visit at Sir Peter Arley's" [009-032], and this connection was a source of
enduring pride. She "tried to remodel the Cranford assemblies upon the model of
the Arley parties" [009-032], importing aristocratic standards into the
provincial social calendar.

The connection was not merely social but also literary. The Rector, Miss
Jenkyns's father, once "sent the poetry to Sir Peter Arley, as my husband
desires" [010-094], demonstrating that the baronet's patronage extended to
intellectual and cultural matters as well.

## Godfather to Peter Jenkyns

Sir Peter Arley served as godfather to Peter Jenkyns, Miss Matty's beloved
brother. When speaking of the absent Peter, the Cranford ladies would refer to
"his godfather, Sir Peter Arley" [011-073], suggesting that this aristocratic
connection was part of Peter's identity and perhaps contributed to the high
hopes once held for his career.

The fact that Peter — mischievous, unruly, practical-joking Peter — should have
the neighbourhood's leading baronet as his godfather is one of Gaskell's quiet
ironies. Peter's eventual disgrace and departure for India stands in stark
contrast to the respectable future that such a connection might have promised.

## The Question of Proper Address

Sir Peter's significance to Cranford life is most vividly captured in the
anxious question of how he and his wife should be addressed. When a formal
letter needed to be written, the proper forms were debated with great
seriousness: "Sir Peter Arley" was the correct form for the baronet, just as
"Lady Arley" was correct for his wife [015-111]. That the Cranford ladies
required a consultation to determine these forms — treating the question as
a matter of near-legal precision — reveals the gap between their provincial
world and the aristocratic circles they admired.

## The Arley Estate

Arley Hall, Sir Peter's seat, is referenced several times in the novel. The
estate represents a standard of grandeur against which Cranford domestic life is
measured. Miss Jenkyns's own rectory, with its old rooms and its three maids,
was arranged in conscious imitation of Arley Hall's domestic order — though the
differences in scale and resources were vast.

## Significance

Sir Peter Arley functions in *Cranford* as a kind of absent centre of social
gravity. He is never seen, never speaks, and never acts directly in the
narrative — yet his name carries weight. His baronetcy, his estate, his role as
godfather, and the correct form of his name are all matters of serious
discussion among the Cranford ladies. Gaskell uses him to illustrate the
provincial English obsession with rank: even a distant, largely indifferent
aristocrat can become a social touchstone for an entire town.
