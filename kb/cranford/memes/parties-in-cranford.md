# Parties in Cranford

Evening parties form the backbone of social life in Cranford. The town's genteel ladies maintain a regular rhythm of card-playing, tea-drinking, and refreshments, all governed by unwritten rules of economy, rank, and propriety.

## The Rhythm of Evening Parties

Cranford parties are exclusively female gatherings — gentlemen who settle in the town are "either fairly frightened to death by being the only man in the Cranford evening parties, or accounted for by being with his regiment, his ship, or closely engaged in business" ([003-001]). The ladies take pride in this arrangement, having "almost persuaded ourselves that to be a man was to be 'vulgar'" ([004-025]).

Parties typically center on card-tables set with green baize tops "by daylight, just as usual" ([004-032]). Mrs Forrester hosts in her "baby-house of a dwelling" where a "little charity-school maiden" is pressed into service as a makeshift servant, yet everyone "took this novel proceeding as the most natural thing in the world" ([002-028]).

## The Economics of Party-Giving

Cranford's genteel poverty shapes party economics in distinctive ways. Decanters are "only filled when there was a party" and leftover wine is carefully examined at a family council before the next occasion — dregs given to the poor, or, if a good deal remained "left at the last party (five months ago, it might be)," it was "added to some of a fresh bottle, brought up from the cellar" ([014-010]).

The eatables at ordinary parties are "of the slightest description" — wafer bread-and-butter and sponge-biscuits. But on special occasions, hosts like Miss Barker produce extravagant feasts: "scalloped oysters, potted lobsters, jelly, a dish called 'little Cupids'" — macaroons sopped in brandy under a refined name ([015-053]). This creates tension: the ladies pride themselves on gentility that "never ate suppers in general" yet, "like most non-supper-eaters," they are "particularly hungry on all special occasions" ([015-054]).

## Card-Table Politics

The card-table is a stage for Cranford's social dynamics. Seating arrangements matter deeply — when the Honourable Mrs Jamieson sits "at a card-table nearest Miss Jessie," the company worries what she would "say or think if she found out she was in the same room with a shop-keeper's niece" ([005-028]).

Miss Barker, though ignorant of the game of Preference, manages "her arduous task well" — repeating whispers to deaf Mrs Forrester "distorting her face considerably," while ensuring sleeping Mrs Jamieson is not disturbed. "Four ladies' heads, with niddle-noddling caps, all nearly meeting over the middle of the table in their eagerness to whisper quick enough and loud enough" ([015-039]).

## Mrs Jamieson's Party: The Social Apex

The Honourable Mrs Jamieson occupies "the post of honour" in Cranford society. Her party invitations — or exclusions — carry extraordinary weight. After a social slight, "notes of invitation" brought round by Mr Mulliner himself (in a "large basket" for three notes "though they might easily have gone into his waistcoat pocket" ([017-006])) trigger elaborate social calculations.

Miss Pole, initially outraged at having been excluded from calling, reverses her position because "one would not like Mrs Jamieson to think that anything she could do, or say, was of consequence enough to give offence" — confessing it "would be too flattering to Mrs Jamieson" ([017-015]). The resolution involves new caps and a lecture on "the great Christian principle of 'Forgive and forget'" ([017-021]).

## Walking Home from Parties

After a party, the ladies never admit to practical motives. "If we walked to or from a party, it was because the night was _so_ fine, or the air _so_ refreshing, not because sedan-chairs were expensive" ([003-016]). Miss Matty, asked about astronomy on one such walk, offers the party-line answer: "Such a refreshment after the excitement of a party!" ([019-004]).
