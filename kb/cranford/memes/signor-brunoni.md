# Signor Brunoni

Signor Brunoni is an itinerant magician whose arrival in Cranford serves as a
major plot catalyst. His performance at the Assembly Rooms, the rumours
surrounding his identity, and his subsequent disappearance spark the chain of
events that culminate in the Great Cranford Panic.

## Arrival and Performance

Signor Brunoni was to "exhibit his wondrous arts" [017-180] at the Cranford
Assembly Rooms. The town was abuzz with anticipation — a foreign performer was a
rare and exotic event in Cranford's quiet social calendar. The sign outside the
George Inn advertised his forthcoming show, and the Cranford ladies debated
whether it was proper to attend.

The performance itself featured magic tricks and illusions typical of a
Victorian travelling showman. Gaskell describes the act in detail through the
eyes of the Cranford audience: Signor Brunoni produced wonders that both
delighted and unsettled the ladies, who were unused to such deliberate
deceptions presented as entertainment [018-067].

## Identity and Mystery

A central question throughout Signor Brunoni's presence in Cranford is his true
identity. He was "discovered to be English" [019-107], not Italian as his stage
name suggested. Miss Pole was the first to voice suspicion: "That's not Signor
Brunoni!" she declared, noting that the figure before them "had not got that
muffy sort of thing about his chin" [019-042] that the real performer had worn.

The magician himself "rose and announced himself as Signor Brunoni" [019-038],
but when this was disputed, he quietly admitted to being the performer's
assistant substituting for the genuine article. This revelation, however, only
deepened the mystery rather than resolving it.

## The Panic Connection

The circumstances of Signor Brunoni's visit became retrospectively sinister
after the rash of burglaries that swept Cranford. He "spoke broken English like
a Frenchman" [019-161], and in the xenophobic atmosphere of the panic, "there
could be no doubt Signor Brunoni was a Frenchman" [019-166] — a nationality that
carried suspicions of criminal intent in provincial Victorian England.

Miss Pole later asked, "Could Signor Brunoni be at the bottom of this?"
[020-125], connecting the magician's mysterious comings and goings with the wave
of thefts. The ladies speculated that his magic "arts" might include the
dark arts of burglary, and his foreignness made him an easy target for their
fears.

## Resolution

The truth about Signor Brunoni emerges through the resolution of the panic
narrative. The real Brunoni had fallen ill, which is why his assistant had
performed in his place [024-018]. More significantly, the actual Brunoni proved
to be a humble Englishman whose real name was Samuel Brown — the long-lost
brother of Miss Jessie Brown. His foreign persona was merely theatrical
costume, and his connection to the burglaries was entirely imaginary.

## Significance

Signor Brunoni functions as a narrative device that exposes Cranford's
provincial anxieties. His foreign-sounding name and exotic profession make him
an object of fascination and, later, of fear. Gaskell uses Brunoni to satirise
the xenophobia and credulity of small-town life — the same ladies who were
delighted by his magic tricks are quick to suspect him of the most outlandish
crimes. His ultimate revelation as Captain Brown's son-in-law ties the panic
narrative back to the novel's central themes of mistaken identity and social
judgment.
