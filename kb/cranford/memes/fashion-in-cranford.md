# Fashion in Cranford

Fashion in Cranford operates under a paradox that the town's ladies have elevated to a principle: they attend to dress with almost religious seriousness while simultaneously declaring themselves independent of fashion's demands. The result is a distinctive sartorial culture in which caps, gowns, turbans, and cravats become vehicles for social expression, class performance, and gentle comedy.

## Independence from Fashion

The Cranford position on fashion is stated definitively in the novel's opening pages. The ladies' dress "is very independent of fashion; as they observe, 'What does it signify how we dress here at Cranford, where everybody knows us?' And if they go from home, their reason is equally cogent, 'What does it signify how we dress here, where nobody knows us?'" [001-089, 001-090, 001-091, 001-092]. This circular logic, impervious to refutation, encapsulates the Cranford genius for converting limitation into principle. The materials of their clothes are "in general, good and plain" [001-093], suggesting that while they reject fashion's caprice, they do not reject quality.

Yet this professed independence is itself a form of fashion consciousness. The Cranford ladies are intensely aware of what they wear and what others wear; they simply operate on an alternative timescale. Miss Jenkyns's style — a cravat, "a little bonnet like a jockey-cap," an appearance "of a strong-minded woman" [003-102, 003-103, 003-104] — is a deliberate construction, as much a sartorial statement as any London fashion-plate. Miss Pole's headgear, described as "old-fashioned gigs," was sufficiently imposing to make "an awful impression on the children in Cranford" [014-107, 014-108] — fashion as a form of social authority, its power registered even by those too young to understand it.

## The Ritual of the Fashion-Room

The visit to the fashion show-room forms one of the novel's most delicately observed set-pieces. When the "new fashions were arrived, and would be exhibited on the following Tuesday at his rooms in High Street" [025-104, 025-105], Miss Matty prepares to purchase a new silk gown. But the protocols are elaborate. "It is not etiquette to go till after twelve; but then, you see, all Cranford will be there, and one does not like to be too curious about dress and trimmings and caps with all the world looking on. It is never genteel to be over-curious on these occasions" [026-043, 026-044, 026-045, 026-046].

The solution is a pre-etiquette reconnaissance: a private viewing before the official hour, so that the ladies may "examine the things at our leisure, and see exactly how my new silk gown must be made; and then, after twelve, we could go with our minds disengaged, and free from thoughts of dress" [026-051, 026-052, 026-053, 026-054]. The notion that one might enter the fashion-room with a mind "disengaged" from thoughts of dress is, of course, a characteristically Cranford self-deception.

The fashion-room itself occupies "what was once a loft, though now a fashion show-room" [026-070, 026-071], a detail that captures the provisional, improvised quality of Cranford's engagement with metropolitan commerce. The inspection of the fashions is conducted "with as minute and curious an interest as if the gown to be made after them had been bought" [027-037, 027-038] — the pleasure lies as much in the looking as in the buying, an elegant economy of the gaze.

## Caps, Turbans, and the Tyranny of Accessories

Headgear occupies a disproportionately significant place in Cranford's sartorial system. For a social event, the ladies acquire "new caps to suit the fashion of the day," dressed always with "chaste elegance and propriety, as Miss Barker once prettily expressed it" [016-081, 016-082, 016-083]. The cap is both a necessity of respectable dress and a site of competitive display: Mrs Forrester, Miss Matty, and Miss Pole appear at a memorable occasion with "three new caps, and a greater array of brooches than had ever been seen together at one time since Cranford was a town" [016-084, 016-085, 016-086].

The turban carries its own complex associations. When Miss Matty enquires whether "turbans were in fashion" [017-168], the question carries the weight of both aspiration and anxiety — a turban represents metropolitan sophistication, yet one that is always at risk of arriving in Cranford slightly too late. A local woman, hearing that "some of the county families likely to come," requests a cap from the town — she is "perhaps, too old to care about dress, but a new cap she must have" [017-170, 017-171, 017-172]. The new cap becomes a talisman of social readiness, a way of meeting the gaze of one's social superiors with dignity.

Miss Matty herself had been "only waiting for" the arrival of the new fashions before buying herself a new silk gown [025-105, 025-106], and she had "rejected" the narrator's proposal to send to Drumble for patterns, "gently implying that she had not forgotten her disappointment about the sea-green turban" [025-107, 025-108, 025-109]. Fashion in Cranford is a matter of local loyalty as much as aesthetic judgment; a pattern from Drumble cannot substitute for the communal ritual of inspecting the fashions in situ.

## The Fashion-Room as Dramatic Irony

The fashion-room scene operates as one of the novel's most effective passages of dramatic irony. The narrator notes that she could not see that "the little event in the shop below" — the refusal of the Town and County Bank note — had "in the least damped Miss Matty's curiosity as to the make of sleeves or the sit of skirts" [027-039, 027-040]. Miss Matty even "once or twice exchanged congratulations with me on our private and leisurely view of the bonnets and shawls" [027-041, 027-042]. The reader knows, even as Miss Matty admires silks, that her income has been obliterated. The juxtaposition of fashion's gentle pleasures against imminent financial catastrophe is Gaskell at her most quietly devastating.
