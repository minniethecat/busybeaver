# Arley Hall

Arley Hall, the seat of Sir Peter Arley, exists in Cranford's imaginative geography as a point of aristocratic reference — a place the Cranford ladies do not inhabit but against which they measure themselves. It is visited, spoken of, aspired to; but it is not, in any meaningful sense, part of Cranford. It belongs to the county, not the town, and the distance between them is the measure of Cranford's social position.

## The Model of Grandeur

Arley Hall enters the novel most vividly as the template for Miss Jenkyns's domestic ambitions. She "used to visit at Sir Peter Arley's, and tried to remodel the quiet rectory establishment on the plans of Arley Hall, where they kept thirty servants" [009-032, 009-033, 009-034]. The gap between Arley Hall's thirty servants and the rectory's "three maids and a man" [013-072] is the comic measure of Miss Jenkyns's aspirations — and also, in a gentler register, the measure of her energy and will. She may not succeed in turning the rectory into Arley Hall, but the attempt is a declaration of standards.

## A Symbol of Connection

Arley Hall also functions as a marker of social connection. The rector's daughter "visited at Arley Hall" [030-055], a distinction that impressed the young Miss Matty's future maid, Martha, and helped cement her lifelong loyalty. The infant Peter was taken by his parents to "spend a day or two at Arley Hall" [010-063], wrapped in the cloak his mother had decorated for him — a detail that links the grand estate with the most intimate of domestic objects. Arley Hall, in these scattered references, is less a place than a horizon: the visible limit of Cranford's social world, the point at which gentility shades into genuine aristocracy.
