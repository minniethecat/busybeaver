# Section 5

[005-001] She was conscious of her sister’s presence, though not, I think, of

[005-002] ours. We stood a little behind the curtain: Miss Jessie knelt with her

[005-003] face near her sister’s, in order to catch the last soft awful whispers.

[005-004] “Oh, Jessie! Jessie! How selfish I have been! God forgive me for

[005-005] letting you sacrifice yourself for me as you did! I have so loved

[005-006] you—and yet I have thought only of myself. God forgive me!”

[005-007] “Hush, love! hush!” said Miss Jessie, sobbing.

[005-008] “And my father, my dear, dear father! I will not complain now, if God

[005-009] will give me strength to be patient. But, oh, Jessie! tell my father

[005-010] how I longed and yearned to see him at last, and to ask his forgiveness.

[005-011] He can never know now how I loved him—oh! if I might but tell him,

[005-012] before I die! What a life of sorrow his has been, and I have done so

[005-013] little to cheer him!”

[005-014] A light came into Miss Jessie’s face. “Would it comfort you, dearest,

[005-015] to think that he does know?—would it comfort you, love, to know that his

[005-016] cares, his sorrows”—Her voice quivered, but she steadied it into

[005-017] calmness—“Mary! he has gone before you to the place where the weary are

[005-018] at rest. He knows now how you loved him.”

[005-019] A strange look, which was not distress, came over Miss Brown’s face.

[005-020] She did not speak for some time, but then we saw her lips form the

[005-021] words, rather than heard the sound—“Father, mother, Harry, Archy;”—then,

[005-022] as if it were a new idea throwing a filmy shadow over her darkened

[005-023] mind—“But you will be alone, Jessie!”

[005-024] Miss Jessie had been feeling this all during the silence, I think; for

[005-025] the tears rolled down her cheeks like rain, at these words, and she

[005-026] could not answer at first. Then she put her hands together tight, and

[005-027] lifted them up, and said—but not to us—“Though He slay me, yet will I

[005-028] trust in Him.”

[005-029] In a few moments more Miss Brown lay calm and still—never to sorrow or

[005-030] murmur more.

[005-031] After this second funeral, [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]] insisted that Miss Jessie should

[005-032] come to stay with her rather than go back to the desolate house, which,

[005-033] in fact, we learned from Miss Jessie, must now be given up, as she had

[005-034] not wherewithal to maintain it. She had something above twenty pounds a

[005-035] year, besides the interest of the money for which the furniture would

[005-036] sell; but she could not live upon that: and so we talked over her

[005-037] qualifications for earning money.

[005-038] “I can sew neatly,” said she, “and I like nursing. I think, too, I

[005-039] could manage a house, if any one would try me as housekeeper; or I would

[005-040] go into a shop as saleswoman, if they would have patience with me at

[005-041] first.”

[005-042] Miss Jenkyns declared, in an angry voice, that she should do no such

[005-043] thing; and talked to herself about “some people having no idea of their

[005-044] rank as a captain’s daughter,” nearly an hour afterwards, when she

[005-045] brought Miss Jessie up a basin of delicately-made arrowroot, and stood

[005-046] over her like a dragoon until the last spoonful was finished: then she

[005-047] disappeared. Miss Jessie began to tell me some more of the plans which

[005-048] had suggested themselves to her, and insensibly fell into talking of the

[005-049] days that were past and gone, and interested me so much I neither knew

[005-050] nor heeded how time passed. We were both startled when Miss Jenkyns

[005-051] reappeared, and caught us crying. I was afraid lest she would be

[005-052] displeased, as she often said that crying hindered digestion, and I knew

[005-053] she wanted Miss Jessie to get strong; but, instead, she looked queer and

[005-054] excited, and fidgeted round us without saying anything. At last she

[005-055] spoke.

[005-056] “I have been so much startled—no, I’ve not been at all startled—don’t

[005-057] mind me, my dear Miss Jessie—I’ve been very much surprised—in fact, I’ve

[005-058] had a caller, whom you knew once, my dear Miss Jessie”—

[005-059] Miss Jessie went very white, then flushed scarlet, and looked eagerly at

[005-060] Miss Jenkyns.

[005-061] “A gentleman, my dear, who wants to know if you would see him.”

[005-062] “Is it?—it is not”—stammered out Miss Jessie—and got no farther.

[005-063] “This is his card,” said Miss Jenkyns, giving it to Miss Jessie; and

[005-064] while her head was bent over it, Miss Jenkyns went through a series of

[005-065] winks and odd faces to me, and formed her lips into a long sentence, of

[005-066] which, of course, I could not understand a word.

[005-067] “May he come up?” asked Miss Jenkyns at last.

[005-068] “Oh, yes! certainly!” said Miss Jessie, as much as to say, this is your

[005-069] house, you may show any visitor where you like. She took up some

[005-070] knitting of [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]]’s and began to be very busy, though I could see

[005-071] how she trembled all over.

[005-072] Miss Jenkyns rang the bell, and told the servant who answered it to show

[005-073] Major Gordon upstairs; and, presently, in walked a tall, fine,

[005-074] frank-looking man of forty or upwards. He shook hands with Miss Jessie;

[005-075] but he could not see her eyes, she kept them so fixed on the ground.

[005-076] Miss Jenkyns asked me if I would come and help her to tie up the

[005-077] preserves in the store-room; and though Miss Jessie plucked at my gown,

[005-078] and even looked up at me with begging eye, I durst not refuse to go

[005-079] where Miss Jenkyns asked. Instead of tying up preserves in the

[005-080] store-room, however, we went to talk in the dining-room; and there Miss

[005-081] Jenkyns told me what Major Gordon had told her; how he had served in the

[005-082] same regiment with [[captain-brown|Captain Brown]], and had become acquainted with Miss

[005-083] Jessie, then a sweet-looking, blooming girl of eighteen; how the

[005-084] acquaintance had grown into love on his part, though it had been some

[005-085] years before he had spoken; how, on becoming possessed, through the will

[005-086] of an uncle, of a good estate in Scotland, he had offered and been

[005-087] refused, though with so much agitation and evident distress that he was

[005-088] sure she was not indifferent to him; and how he had discovered that the

[005-089] obstacle was the fell disease which was, even then, too surely

[005-090] threatening her sister. She had mentioned that the surgeons foretold

[005-091] intense suffering; and there was no one but herself to nurse her poor

[005-092] Mary, or cheer and comfort her father during the time of illness. They

[005-093] had had long discussions; and on her refusal to pledge herself to him as

[005-094] his wife when all should be over, he had grown angry, and broken off

[005-095] entirely, and gone abroad, believing that she was a cold-hearted person

[005-096] whom he would do well to forget. He had been travelling in the East,

[005-097] and was on his return home when, at Rome, he saw the account of Captain

[005-098] Brown’s death in _Galignani_.

[005-099] Just then Miss Matty, who had been out all the morning, and had only

[005-100] lately returned to the house, burst in with a face of dismay and

[005-101] outraged propriety.

[005-102] “Oh, goodness me!” she said. “Deborah, there’s a gentleman sitting in

[005-103] the drawing-room with his arm round Miss Jessie’s waist!” Miss Matty’s

[005-104] eyes looked large with terror.

[005-105] [Picture: “With his arm around Miss Jessie’s waist!”]

[005-106] Miss Jenkyns snubbed her down in an instant.

[005-107] “The most proper place in the world for his arm to be in. Go away,

[005-108] Matilda, and mind your own business.” This from her sister, who had

[005-109] hitherto been a model of feminine decorum, was a blow for poor Miss

[005-110] Matty, and with a double shock she left the room.

[005-111] The last time I ever saw poor Miss Jenkyns was many years after this.

[005-112] [[lady-glenmire|Mrs Gordon]] had kept up a warm and affectionate intercourse with all at

[005-113] [[cranford-town|Cranford]]. Miss Jenkyns, Miss Matty, and [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] had all been to visit

[005-114] her, and returned with wonderful accounts of her house, her husband, her

[005-115] dress, and her looks. For, with happiness, something of her early bloom

[005-116] returned; she had been a year or two younger than we had taken her for.

[005-117] Her eyes were always lovely, and, as Mrs Gordon, her dimples were not

[005-118] out of place. At the time to which I have referred, when I last saw

[005-119] Miss Jenkyns, that lady was old and feeble, and had lost something of

[005-120] her strong mind. Little Flora Gordon was staying with the Misses

[005-121] Jenkyns, and when I came in she was reading aloud to Miss Jenkyns, who

[005-122] lay feeble and changed on the sofa. Flora put down the _Rambler_ when I

[005-123] came in.

[005-124] “Ah!” said Miss Jenkyns, “you find me changed, my dear. I can’t see as

[005-125] I used to do. If Flora were not here to read to me, I hardly know how I

[005-126] should get through the day. Did you ever read the _Rambler_? It’s a

[005-127] wonderful book—wonderful! and the most improving reading for Flora”

[005-128] (which I daresay it would have been, if she could have read half the

[005-129] words without spelling, and could have understood the meaning of a

[005-130] third), “better than that strange old book, with the queer name, poor

[005-131] Captain Brown was killed for reading—that book by Mr Boz, you know—‘Old

[005-132] Poz’; when I was a girl—but that’s a long time ago—I acted Lucy in ‘Old

[005-133] Poz.’” She babbled on long enough for Flora to get a good long spell at

[005-134] the “Christmas Carol,” which Miss Matty had left on the table.

[005-135] CHAPTER III.

[005-136] A LOVE AFFAIR OF LONG AGO

[005-137] I THOUGHT that probably my connection with Cranford would cease after

[005-138] Miss Jenkyns’s death; at least, that it would have to be kept up by

[005-139] [[letter-writing|correspondence]], which bears much the same relation to personal

[005-140] intercourse that the [[reading-in-cranford|books]] of dried plants I sometimes see (“Hortus

[005-141] Siccus,” I think they call the thing) do to the living and fresh flowers

[005-142] in the lanes and meadows. I was pleasantly surprised, therefore, by

[005-143] receiving a letter from Miss Pole (who had always come in for a

[005-144] supplementary week after my annual visit to Miss Jenkyns) proposing that

[005-145] I should go and stay with her; and then, in a couple of days after my

[005-146] acceptance, came a note from Miss Matty, in which, in a rather

[005-147] circuitous and very humble manner, she told me how much pleasure I

[005-148] should confer if I could spend a week or two with her, either before or

[005-149] after I had been at Miss Pole’s; “for,” she said, “since my dear

[005-150] sister’s death I am well aware I have no attractions to offer; it is

[005-151] only to the kindness of my friends that I can owe their company.”

[005-152] Of course I promised to come to dear Miss Matty as soon as I had ended

[005-153] my visit to Miss Pole; and the day after my arrival at Cranford I went

[005-154] to see her, much wondering what the house would be like without Miss

[005-155] Jenkyns, and rather dreading the changed aspect of things. Miss Matty

[005-156] began to cry as soon as she saw me. She was evidently nervous from

[005-157] having anticipated my call. I comforted her as well as I could; and I

[005-158] found the best consolation I could give was the honest praise that came

[005-159] from my heart as I spoke of the deceased. Miss Matty slowly shook her

[005-160] head over each virtue as it was named and attributed to her sister; and

[005-161] at last she could not restrain the tears which had long been silently

[005-162] flowing, but hid her face behind her handkerchief and sobbed aloud.

[005-163] “Dear Miss Matty,” said I, taking her hand—for indeed I did not know in

[005-164] what way to tell her how sorry I was for her, left deserted in the

[005-165] world. She put down her handkerchief and said—

[005-166] “My dear, I’d rather you did not call me Matty. She did not like it;

[005-167] but I did many a thing she did not like, I’m afraid—and now she’s gone!

[005-168] If you please, my love, will you call me Matilda?”

[005-169] I promised faithfully, and began to practise the new name with Miss Pole

[005-170] that very day; and, by degrees, Miss Matilda’s feeling on the subject

[005-171] was known through Cranford, and we all tried to drop the more familiar

[005-172] name, but with so little success that by-and-by we gave up the attempt.
