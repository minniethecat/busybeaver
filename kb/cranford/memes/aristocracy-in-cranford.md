# Aristocracy in Cranford

## Definition

Cranford's relationship with the aristocracy is one of the novel's most revealing preoccupations. The ladies simultaneously revere and resent titled persons, measure themselves against aristocratic standards while quietly undermining aristocratic pretensions, and treat any connection to the nobility as the highest social currency — even as the novel's plot demonstrates that aristocratic rank guarantees neither wisdom nor virtue. The aristocracy in Cranford is both an ideal to be worshipped and a target to be, ever so gently, mocked.

## Manifestations in Cranford

The novel traces a clear arc in its treatment of the aristocracy, from deference to independence:

- **The Honourable Mrs Jamieson**: As the widow of a baron's eldest son, Mrs Jamieson is Cranford's resident aristocrat. Her social supremacy is absolute — the ladies defer to her opinions, adjust their behaviour to her standards, and treat her presence as conferring legitimacy on any gathering. When she falls asleep at a party, "gentility went out too" [014-070] — aristocratic presence and social value are literally identical.
- **An interview with aristocracy**: Miss Pole's report of an "evening's interview with aristocracy" [016-113] captures the Cranford attitude perfectly: an encounter with nobility is an *interview*, a formal event to be reported and analyzed. The language of diplomatic mission applied to a social call reveals the ladies' intense consciousness of rank.
- **The mouldy odour of aristocracy**: Visiting an aristocratic country house, the narrator notes that a "mouldy odour of aristocracy lingered about the place" [018-162]. The adjective "mouldy" is devastating — aristocratic pretension is literally decaying, and the narrator's nose detects what her social training tells her to ignore.
- **Grateful to the aristocracy**: The ladies are described as "too grateful to the aristocracy" [019-157] for any attention, however slight. The "too" is crucial — their gratitude exceeds what the aristocracy deserves, and the narrator knows it.
- **Who says the aristocracy are proud?**: The narrator's rhetorical question — "Who says that the aristocracy are proud? Here was a lady by" [022-143] — uses ironic reversal to make the point. The aristocracy may not be "proud" in the expected way, but Cranford's deference to them reveals the pride that matters is on the other side.
- **The aristocracy to become a Hoggins!**: When Lady Glenmire marries Mr Hoggins, the scandal is expressed in terms of aristocratic betrayal: "the aristocracy to become a Hoggins! She, who..." [031-016]. The horror is that aristocracy has voluntarily descended to the professional class — a violation of the order Cranford has spent the whole novel maintaining.

## Key Characters

- **Mrs Jamieson** — the embodiment of aristocracy's social power in Cranford. Her title is her identity; without it, she would be merely a wealthy widow. With it, she is Cranford's undisputed social sovereign.
- **Lady Glenmire** — the aristocrat who defects. By marrying Mr Hoggins, she performs the novel's most radical act: she treats aristocratic rank as something that can be set aside. Her choice is the novel's quiet democratic argument.
- **Mr Hoggins** — the professional man who marries an aristocrat. His unapologetic masculinity and professional competence represent the rising middle class that is rendering the old aristocratic order obsolete — and the novel is on his side.

## Narrative Function

1. **Social satire**: The ladies' obsession with rank is played for gentle comedy, but the satire cuts both ways. The aristocracy's pretensions are exposed as "mouldy" and hollow, even as the ladies' deference is shown to be slightly absurd.
2. **Class mobility theme**: Lady Glenmire's marriage to Mr Hoggins is the novel's culminating argument about class. Aristocratic rank is not destiny; character and affection can override it. The marriage is Cranford's quiet revolution.
3. **Gaskell's politics**: As a Unitarian minister's wife in Manchester, Gaskell was steeped in a religious tradition that emphasized individual conscience over inherited rank. The novel's treatment of aristocracy — respectful but never reverent, amused but never cruel — reflects this dissenting sensibility.

## Victorian Context

The mid-Victorian period was one of intense negotiation between the old aristocratic order and the rising middle class. The Reform Acts of 1832 and 1867 expanded the franchise and redistributed political power away from the landed aristocracy. Novels of the period — from *Vanity Fair* to *Middlemarch* — are full of aristocrats whose titles no longer guarantee their authority. Gaskell's treatment of aristocracy in *Cranford* is distinctive for its domestic scale: she is not interested in parliamentary reform or political economy, but in how rank plays out in the drawing room, at the tea table, and in the marriage plot.
