# Courage

## Definition

Courage in *Cranford* takes forms that would puzzle a conventional hero. It is the courage to face a wandering cow in darkness, to admit to poverty without shame, and — most remarkably — to do what is right even when it conflicts with the town's most cherished social codes. Gaskell treats courage not as the absence of fear but as the willingness to act despite it, and she locates this virtue not on battlefields but in drawing-rooms.

## Manifestations in Cranford

Courage in Cranford is tested in domestic rather than military arenas:

- **Courage enough to do an ungenteel thing**: The narrator observes that it requires "courage enough to do an ungenteel thing" — a formulation that captures the social bravery demanded by Cranford life, where the greatest risk is not physical harm but loss of caste (008-011)
- **Courage of her thought**: Miss Matty is observed "in admiration of the courage of her thought" — her moral bravery in entertaining an unconventional idea earns the narrator's respect (016-122)
- **A great deal of courage and savoir faire**: Captain Brown is noted for having "a great deal of courage and _savoir faire_" — courage paired with social grace, the ideal combination for navigating Cranford society (017-007)
- **Encouragement silenced Miss Pole**: Even Miss Pole, the town's most intrepid talker, can be silenced by a figure of sufficient authority — a reminder that courage in Cranford is exercised within strict social bounds (019-046)
- **Fearing they were lending encouragement**: The ladies' anxiety about inadvertently "lending encouragement to something that was not" proper captures how moral courage is shadowed by social caution (019-070)
- **Our courage came back with the daylight**: After a night of imagined terrors, "our courage came back with the daylight" — courage as a natural rhythm tied to the domestic cycle of light and dark (020-130)
- **A stock of reputation for courage**: One character has accumulated "a stock of reputation for courage to fall back upon" — courage as social capital, a reputation earned and spent (021-085)
- **An encouragement to see**: The sight of a neighbour's lighted window serves as "an encouragement to see" — courage as a communal resource, reinforced by the proximity of others (023-114)

The pattern across these references is striking: courage in Cranford is rarely solitary. It is spoken of, encouraged, lent, and borrowed — a shared virtue sustained by the community.

## Key Characters

- **Miss Matty Jenkyns** — the novel's most profound example of courage. Her decision to open a tea shop after losing her savings requires the "courage to do an ungenteel thing" — to risk social standing for the sake of honest survival.
- **Captain Brown** — brings a soldier's courage into Cranford's domestic sphere, though his bravery is ultimately helpless against the railway accident that claims his life.
- **Peter Jenkyns** — whose youthful transgressions required a different kind of courage — the courage to leave and the courage to return.

## Narrative Function

Courage operates as:

1. **Moral thermometer**: A character's courage — or lack of it — reveals their moral quality.
2. **Community resource**: The novel shows courage as something that can be borrowed from others when one's own stock is depleted.
3. **Quiet heroism**: Gaskell redefines heroism for a domestic sphere, locating it in everyday acts rather than grand gestures.

## Victorian Context

Victorian ideals of courage were heavily gendered. Men were expected to demonstrate physical and moral courage in public life; women's courage was understood as endurance, patience, and moral fortitude within the domestic sphere. Gaskell complicates this division by showing Cranford women exercising courage in forms that are no less real for being domestic — and by placing the soldier Captain Brown in a setting where his military courage is of limited use.
