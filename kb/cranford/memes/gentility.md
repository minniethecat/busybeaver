# Gentility

## Definition

Gentility in *Cranford* is the all-important social quality that separates the Cranford ladies from the world of trade, labour, and vulgarity. More than mere politeness, gentility is an identity — a claim to membership in the gentry class that must be constantly performed, policed, and defended against threats real and imagined. The word "gentility" appears throughout the novel as both a cherished ideal and a target of Gaskell's gentle satire.

## Manifestations in Cranford

The word gentility runs through the novel like a refrain, invoked at moments of social anxiety and triumph alike:

- **A patent of gentility**: The possession of a certain cow is "considered to confer a patent of gentility upon its owner" — the comic elevation of a cow to a status symbol captures the absurdity of Cranford's social calculus (014-058)
- **Gentility went out**: When the Honourable Mrs Jamieson falls asleep at a party, "gentility went out too" — the departure of the room's highest-ranking person strips the gathering of its social legitimacy (014-070)
- **Oh, gentility!**: At one gathering, the narrator's internal exclamation — "Oh, gentility!" — reflects her affectionate exasperation at the elaborate protocols of Cranford hospitality (015-030)
- **Gentility never ate suppers**: The ladies' refusal to eat supper at evening parties is a badge of gentility, a social distinction maintained at the cost of going hungry (015-041)
- **Throwing gentility into reserves**: When jam-making season demands economy, the ladies redirect their gentility from clothes to preserves — a comic image of gentility as a transferable resource (024-128)
- **Loss of gentility**: When Miss Matty faces financial ruin, what others fear for her is not starvation but "loss of gentility" — the social death that poverty threatens to bring (030-170)
- **Gentility will have nothing else**: The ladies' fixed belief that "gentility will have nothing else to do" reflects their vision of social rank as destiny (031-072)

The cumulative effect of these references is a portrait of a community where gentility is simultaneously a genuine moral standard and an object of affectionate comedy.

## Key Characters

- **Mrs Jamieson** — the town's highest-ranking resident by birth, the Honourable Mrs Jamieson embodies gentility as class performance. Her very presence confers or withdraws social legitimacy.
- **Miss Pole** — the town's most articulate defender of gentility, constantly invoking it to justify social distinctions while undermining it through her own irrepressible curiosity.
- **Miss Matty Jenkyns** — gentility as moral quality rather than social performance. When she loses her money, she does not lose her gentility because it resides in her character, not her income.

## Narrative Function

Gentility serves multiple narrative purposes:

1. **Comic engine**: The gap between the ladies' genteel pretensions and their economic reality generates much of the novel's humour.
2. **Social critique**: Gaskell exposes the fragility of a class system that could be threatened by something as trivial as a tradesman's visit or the quality of one's carpet.
3. **Thematic thread**: The novel traces a quiet transformation — from gentility-as-rank to gentility-as-character. Miss Matty's tea shop is the novel's culminating image of this shift.

## Victorian Context

The Victorian era's rapid economic change created intense anxiety about class boundaries. New industrial wealth challenged old landed hierarchies, and the word "gentility" became a battleground — claimed by the established gentry, aspired to by the rising middle class, and satirized by novelists who recognized its contradictions. Gaskell, as the wife of a Unitarian minister in industrial Manchester, was particularly well positioned to observe these tensions. Her Cranford ladies cling to gentility precisely because the world around them is making it obsolete.
