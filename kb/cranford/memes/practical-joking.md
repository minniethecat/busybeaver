# Practical Joking

## Definition

Practical joking in *Cranford* functions as a socially charged form of humour that tests the boundaries of gentility. While the Cranford ladies participate in gossip and mild social comedy, the novel draws a sharp line between acceptable feminine wit and the destructive masculine pranking embodied by Peter Jenkyns. Practical jokes in this world are not harmless amusement — they carry real social consequences.

## Manifestations in Cranford

The most sustained treatment of practical joking comes through the story of Peter Jenkyns, the rector's son whose compulsive joking leads to family rupture:

- **Peter's dressing as his sister**: To mock his father's preference for his elder sister Deborah, Peter dressed in her clothes and walked through the village holding a baby — a joke that so enraged the rector that he beat Peter publicly with his cane (011-151)
- **The ultimate joke**: Peter's final and most devastating prank involved staging his own departure, which his father never recovered from
- **Poor Peter**: Throughout the novel, Miss Matty refers to him as "poor Peter" — a label that encapsulates how practical joking, in her memory, is inseparable from pity and loss (011-037, 011-055)

The ladies of Cranford discuss Peter's jokes with a mixture of fascination and reproof, never quite condemning him but never celebrating his humour either. Miss Matty's characteristic refrain — "he was always in mischief, poor Peter" (011-064) — captures the town's ambivalent stance.

## Key Characters

- **Peter Jenkyns** — the novel's archetype of the practical joker. His inability to resist a joke costs him his family, his home, and his inheritance. He returns only decades later, transformed into a respectable figure (011-064, 024-033)
- **Reverend Jenkyns** — Peter's father, whose inability to tolerate his son's humour precipitates the family tragedy
- **Miss Matty Jenkyns** — the narrator of Peter's story, whose gentle framing of "poor Peter" shapes how Cranford remembers him (011-100)

## Narrative Function

Gaskell uses practical joking to explore several themes:

1. **Gender and humour**: Female wit in Cranford is conversational and social; male joking is physical, disruptive, and dangerous. The contrast critiques Victorian assumptions about gendered behaviour.
2. **The cost of nonconformity**: Peter's story is a cautionary tale about what happens to those who cannot fit the mould of respectable gentility — exile.
3. **Memory and forgiveness**: The novel's treatment of Peter's jokes — always remembered, never fully condemned — mirrors Cranford's broader pattern of softening hard truths through euphemism and selective memory.

## Victorian Context

The mid-Victorian period saw an intensifying discourse around "manliness" and proper masculine conduct. Practical jokes, once an accepted part of Regency and early Victorian masculine culture, were increasingly seen as ungentlemanly — a shift reflected in the conduct literature of the 1840s and 1850s. Peter's story dramatises this cultural transition: his jokes belong to an older, rougher world that Cranford and the novel both leave behind.
