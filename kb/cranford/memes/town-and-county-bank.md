# Town and County Bank

The Town and County Bank is the financial institution whose collapse forms the central economic crisis of *Cranford*. Its failure transforms Miss Matty Jenkyns from a gentlewoman of modest independent means into a figure of genteel poverty, and in doing so, tests — and ultimately affirms — the bonds of the Cranford community.

## The Investment

The bank shares were originally purchased by Miss Deborah Jenkyns, the elder sister, who was "quite the woman of business, and always judged for herself" [026-037]. This investment was, the narrator's father later observes, "the only unwise step that clever woman had ever taken" [026-010, 026-011]. Notably, the narrator's father had "prophesied to Miss Jenkyns years ago" against the investment, but Deborah — against her character — had acted against his advice [026-010, 026-011, 026-012]. For many years, however, the investment appeared sound: the bank paid "eight per cent. all these years" [026-038, 026-039], a return that would have seemed to vindicate Deborah's business judgment.

## The Collapse

The crisis arrives through a letter — "a very civil invitation, signed 'Edwin Wilson,' asking me to attend an important meeting of the shareholders of the Town and County Bank, to be held in Drumble" [026-016, 026-017, 026-018]. Miss Matty, characteristically modest about her business acumen, is initially flattered: "I remember their sending one to Deborah just like this... I am afraid I could not help them much" [026-026, 026-027, 026-028].

The true gravity of the situation becomes apparent not at the meeting but in a shop, when a farmer attempts to pay with a Town and County Bank note and the shopman refuses it: "I am not sure, sir, but I believe we have received a warning against notes issued by this bank only this morning" [026-120, 026-121]. The farmer's countenance falls "suddenly into dismay and bewilderment" [026-124]. When Miss Matty, who had been absorbed in choosing silk for a new gown, finally catches the name of the bank, she asks quietly: "That is our bank, is it not? — the Town and County Bank?" [026-141].

The narrator's immediate instinct is to protect Miss Matty from the truth, to distract her with fabric and ribbons. But Miss Matty, with characteristic dignity, refuses evasion. The bank's notes, Mr Johnson confirms, are "little better than waste paper" [026-139].

## Miss Matty's Response

Miss Matty's reaction to her financial ruin reveals her moral character. When asked about her duty to redeem the bank's now-worthless notes still circulating in the community, she responds with touching humility: "My dear, I never feel as if my mind was what people call very strong; and it's often hard enough work for me to settle what I ought to do with the case right before me" [027-078, 027-079]. Yet she resolves to do what honour demands, even at the cost of her own security. She decides to use what little remains of her capital to satisfy the bank notes held by Cranford tradespeople, an act of moral scrupulosity that leaves her with an income reduced to "a very small sum" [030-143, 030-144, 030-145].

## Narrative Function

The bank failure is the novel's crucible. It strips away the economic pretense of Cranford's "elegant economy" and reveals the fragility of the spinster's position in Victorian society — a woman without male protection, whose modest inheritance could be wiped out by a distant bank's collapse. Yet the crisis also produces the novel's most affirmative communal response: Miss Matty's friends secretly contribute to her support, and she is enabled to set up as a tea-dealer, transforming her reduced circumstances into a dignified, if modest, independence [030-148, 030-149, 030-152].
