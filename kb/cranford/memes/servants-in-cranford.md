# Servants in Cranford

## Definition

Servants in *Cranford* occupy a paradoxical position: they are simultaneously indispensable and invisible, the subject of endless conversation and yet rarely granted voice or interiority. The Cranford ladies' relationship with their servants reveals the precariousness of their own gentility — the "standing grievance" (006-022) of servant management is, at bottom, an anxiety about class status and economic vulnerability.

## The Ideal of the "Neat Maid-Servant"

The Cranford ladies pride themselves on "keeping their neat maid-servants in admirable" order (001-076). This phrasing is revealing: the servant is kept, not employed; her neatness is a reflection of her mistress's competence. The "neat maid-servant" who "had received her last" instructions (002-078) embodies the Cranford ideal — competent, unobtrusive, and entirely subordinate to the rhythms of gentility.

The narrator notes with approval how one household managed to spare the "pretty maid-servant's labour by waiting on empty" chairs (002-096) — a small kindness that demonstrates the better side of the mistress-servant relationship. Yet the adjective "pretty" applied to the servant is itself telling: even in praise, the servant is aestheticised, valued for her appearance as much as her work.

## The Standing Grievance

Miss Matty's household exposes the servant problem in its most acute form:

> "This subject of servants was a standing grievance, and I could not wonder at it" (006-022)

Miss Matty's servants are alternately described as: a "rough, honest-looking" new hire (006-057); labourers who hold her "at the mercy of her old servants" (006-005); and the clever servant who holds "in the power of her clever servant" influence over her mistress (006-103). The repeated language of power and mercy reverses the expected hierarchy — it is the mistress who is at the mercy of the servant, not the reverse.

The narrator's comment that she "would have trained a servant in a week to make a better fi" (006-020) suggests that the problem lies as much with Miss Matty's gentle indecisiveness as with the servants themselves. The "pretty neat servant-maids had their choice of des" (006-026) — in a town like Cranford, good servants held the upper hand.

## Martha: The Faithful Exception

Martha, Miss Matty's eventual "faithful" servant (008-160), is the exception that proves the rule. Her loyalty to Miss Matty — staying on through reduced circumstances — represents the idealised servant relationship that Cranford's ladies aspire to but rarely achieve. Martha's fidelity is personal rather than contractual; she serves Miss Matty not because of wages but because of affection.

## Servants and Class Anxiety

The Cranford ladies' preoccupation with servants reflects deeper economic anxieties:

- **"Servants' hall"**: The mention that one hostess had "a regular servants' hall" (001-140) signals a level of domestic establishment that most Cranford ladies could only aspire to
- **"Servant-of-all-work"**: Miss Matty's reduced circumstances require her to "be content with a servant-of-all-work" (013-073) — the single maid-of-all-work is a visible marker of diminished status
- **Followers**: The concern that "maid-servants did not pick up followers" (020-152) reflects anxiety about servants' private lives and their potential to disrupt the household

## Victorian Context

The "servant problem" was a genuine social concern in mid-Victorian England. Industrialisation created alternative forms of employment for working-class women, and good domestic servants became harder to find and keep. The census of 1851 recorded over a million female domestic servants in England and Wales — the largest single occupational category for women. Gaskell's treatment of the subject is characteristically nuanced: she acknowledges the genuine frustrations of employers while also recognising the humanity and agency of the servants themselves.
