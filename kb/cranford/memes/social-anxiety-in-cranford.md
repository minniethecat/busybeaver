## Definition

Beneath Cranford's genteel surface runs a current of anxiety. The town's "elegant economy" [001-161] is not merely a charming eccentricity but a constant, low-grade pressure: the fear of被发现 (being found out) as less than one appears. This anxiety manifests in nervous hands, trembling voices, and obsessive attention to propriety. It is the emotional cost of maintaining appearances in a community where everyone watches everyone else and where social status is both precarious and paramount.

## Manifestations in Cranford

Financial anxiety is the most pervasive form. The Cranford ladies' "elegant economy" is a performance designed to disguise genteel poverty. When Miss Matty's investments in the Town and County Bank fail, her anxiety becomes acute: she is "nervously acquiescent" [030-098], then experiences "great cause for anxiety" [022-120] over her reduced circumstances. Martha's loyalty is tested against the "constant anxiety" [006-034] of servant-employer relations in a household where money is scarce.

Social-performance anxiety governs Cranford interactions. Miss Pole is "evidently nervous from" [005-156] a social encounter; Miss Matty experiences "nervous irritability occasioned by her" [003-056] awareness of social expectations. The two-pronged fork dinner at Mr Holbrook's crystallises social anxiety: Miss Matty picks up her peas one by one while Miss Pole leaves them untouched — each woman navigating the terror of appearing "vulgar" through different strategies.

The Cranford panic episodes represent collective anxiety at its most dramatic. The rumour of robberies transforms into "this nervous feeling of hers become" [032-185] a town-wide hysteria. Signor Brunoni's magic show, with its unfamiliar masculine spectacle, triggers "trembling nervous hands" [002-064] in the ladies. The panic demonstrates how Cranford's anxiety, typically managed through gossip and mutual reassurance, can escalate into genuine terror when the community's coping mechanisms fail.

Miss Matty's "present nervous state" [008-178] is a recurring motif. She trembles when speaking of money, grows "nervously acquiescent" [030-098] under pressure, and experiences "a little fluttered and nervous" [032-080] anticipation at life's transitions. Her anxiety is the price of her gentility — a woman raised to be ornamental finds herself unequipped for economic reality, and her nervous system registers the mismatch.

The town's obsession with "propriety" [003-039] is anxiety in institutional form. Every social rule — calling hours, mourning customs, tea-serving rituals — exists to manage the anxiety of social misstep. When these rules are violated (as when Captain Brown reads Boz aloud, or when Lady Glenmire marries the surgeon Mr Hoggins), the community's anxiety spikes, requiring collective processing through gossip to restore equilibrium.
