# Lady Arley

Lady Arley is the wife of Sir Peter Arley, the aristocratic landowner whose
estate lies near Cranford. She appears in the novel only through references and
recollections, yet she serves an important function as a benchmark of proper
aristocratic behaviour against which Cranford's social aspirations are measured.

## Social Position

Lady Arley occupies a position in the strict hierarchy of rural English society
well above the Cranford ladies. She is mentioned alongside her husband Sir Peter
as one of the "great" people of the neighbourhood. Miss Jenkyns, the arbiter of
Cranford propriety, "used to visit at Sir Peter Arley's" [009-032] in her youth,
and this connection was a source of considerable social capital.

## The Question of Address

One of the most revealing episodes concerning Lady Arley involves the question
of how she should be properly addressed. When Miss Matty, in a moment of social
uncertainty, needed to know the correct form, she appealed: "Now you knew Lady
Arley — will you kindly tell me the manner in which she was addressed?" [015-092]
The question was of grave importance — getting the form of address wrong would
be a social disaster. The proper answer was "Lady Arley" or "your ladyship,"
with the narrator adding reassuringly that "Sir Peter Arley" was the correct
form for her husband [015-111].

This anxiety over titles captures Cranford's obsessive attention to social
minutiae. That Lady Arley should be consulted not in person but as a remembered
precedent — a kind of living etiquette manual — reveals how remote and yet how
important the aristocracy was to Cranford's self-conception.

## Influence on Cranford Manners

Lady Arley's influence extended into the smallest details of Cranford behaviour.
The narrator notes that Mrs Jamieson had adopted "a manner she had caught from
Lady Arley" [026-048], suggesting that the aristocrat's style was consciously
imitated by those lower in the social hierarchy. Mrs Jamieson, as the
Honourable widow of a governor's son, was Cranford's closest link to genuine
aristocracy, and her imitation of Lady Arley was part of maintaining that
distinction.

## Relationship with Miss Jenkyns

Miss Jenkyns's connection to the Arley household was one of her proudest
accomplishments. She "used to visit at Sir Peter Arley's, and tried to remodel
the Cranford assemblies upon the model of the Arley parties" [009-032]. This
ambition — to make Cranford society resemble that of a titled country house —
was characteristic of Miss Jenkyns's determination to elevate her town's social
standing.

However, the narrator gently notes that Lady Arley "did not come much oftener to
see us" [015-098] than the formal obligations of neighbourhood required,
suggesting that the relationship was more significant to Cranford than to the
Arleys themselves.

## Significance

Lady Arley, though barely present in the novel's action, embodies the social
world that Cranford both aspires to and can never fully join. She is a
touchstone of aristocratic correctness — her very name is a formula to be
remembered and reproduced. Her remoteness from the daily life of the town only
increases her prestige: the less she visits, the more her example is invoked.
Gaskell uses Lady Arley to illustrate the peculiar dynamics of provincial social
hierarchy, where proximity to rank — even at several removes — is a form of
currency.
