# Reading in Cranford

## Definition

Reading in *Cranford* is far more than a private pastime — it is a social performance, a moral barometer, and a marker of cultural allegiance. What one reads, how one reads, and to whom one reads define a character's place in Cranford's social order. The novel's most famous death — Captain Brown — is explicitly caused by reading: "Captain Brown was killed for reading — that book by Mr Boz" (005-131), a detail that Gaskell deploys with dark humour and narrative economy.

## The Johnson vs. Dickens Controversy

The novel's central literary conflict is between two modes of reading, embodied by two dead men:

- **Dr Johnson / Miss Jenkyns**: The Johnsonian mode — elevated, moral, "improving" — represented by Rasselas and the eighteenth-century tradition of reading as moral education. Miss Jenkyns "could not" approve of the Captain reading Pickwick, wishing "he had only been reading a higher style of" literature (003-174).
- **Dickens / Captain Brown**: The Dickensian mode — entertaining, popular, emotionally immediate. Captain Brown reads *The Pickwick Papers* aloud and is killed by a train while absorbed in his reading — the ultimate, absurd consequence of reading as immersive pleasure.

Gaskell does not take sides decisively. The Johnsonian mode is associated with Miss Jenkyns's rigidity and the past; the Dickensian mode with vitality, kindness, and the present. Yet the novel itself participates in both traditions — as morally serious as Johnson, as engaging as Dickens.

## Reading Aloud as Social Ritual

Reading aloud recurs throughout the novel as a gesture of community:

- **Captain Brown reading to the ladies**: "when I came in she was reading aloud to Miss Jenkyns" (005-121) — the scene establishes reading as a shared, domestic activity rather than solitary consumption
- **Mr Holbrook's "beautiful reading"**: The narrator is "ashamed to hear his beautiful reading" (008-066) — reading aloud becomes an index of character, revealing sensibility and cultivation
- **Reading old letters**: "I never knew what sad work the reading of old letters" could do (009-168) — reading extends beyond books to personal archives, connecting the living to the dead
- **The *St James's Chronicle***: Miss Pole's monopoly on the newspaper — "she always had the reading of it first" (016-109) — shows reading as a resource to be hoarded and distributed

## Reading as Moral Index

What characters read reflects their moral nature. Captain Brown reads for pleasure and human connection — and dies for it, in a detail that is both tragic and faintly comic. Miss Jenkyns reads for moral improvement and social distinction. Miss Matty reads aloud to comfort others, her reading an extension of her kindness. Mr Holbrook reads Tennyson's poetry with evident feeling, his "beautiful reading" (008-066) revealing the sensibility beneath his rough yeoman exterior.

## Victorian Context

The 1850s saw a revolution in reading: cheap serial fiction, circulating libraries, and rising literacy rates made books accessible to the middle class in unprecedented ways. The debate between "improving" and "entertaining" reading was genuine and heated. Gaskell's own position — a professional author writing serialised fiction for *Household Words* — placed her squarely in the Dickens camp, yet her depiction of the Johnsonian tradition is respectful rather than dismissive. The novel's synthesis of moral seriousness and narrative pleasure mirrors its own implicit argument about the value of popular fiction.
