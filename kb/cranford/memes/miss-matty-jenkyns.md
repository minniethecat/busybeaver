# Miss Matty Jenkyns

## Character Overview

Miss Matilda "Matty" Jenkyns is the central character and moral heart of Elizabeth Gaskell's *Cranford* (1853). She is the younger daughter of the late Rector Jenkyns, a gentle spinster living in reduced circumstances in the small town of Cranford. Miss Matty embodies the novel's themes of kindness, adaptation to changing circumstances, and the quiet dignity of provincial life.

## Biography

Miss Matty is first introduced as the sister of the formidable Miss Deborah Jenkyns. After Deborah's death, Matty must learn to manage her own affairs, having long lived in her sister's shadow (003-090). Her father, the former rector, had named her Matilda, but she was "being called Miss Matty, when Miss Jenkyns was not by" — a small act of individuation within a constrained life (003-090).

Her financial circumstances become precarious when the bank in which her savings are invested fails. Miss Matty faces genteel poverty with quiet courage, eventually opening a small tea shop with the help of her friends (030-134). This business venture represents a significant moment of personal growth and adaptation.

## Relationships

Miss Matty's most important relationships include:

- **Miss Deborah Jenkyns** — her domineering elder sister, whose death in the early chapters liberates but also disorients Matty
- **Mary Smith** — the narrator, a younger friend who frequently visits from Drumble and serves as the reader's eyes in Cranford
- **Miss Pole** — a gossipy but loyal friend who helps Matty navigate social and practical challenges
- **Mr. Peter Jenkyns** — her long-lost brother, whose return from India in the novel's closing chapters brings Matty great joy (033-001)
- **Captain Brown** — a kind-hearted army officer whose death in a railway accident deeply affects the Cranford community

## Role in the Narrative

Miss Matty serves as the moral center of *Cranford*. Through her eyes and experiences, Gaskell explores themes of:

- **Genteel poverty** — the struggle to maintain social appearances on limited means (030-134)
- **Female community** — the network of women who support each other in a town with few men
- **Change and continuity** — the tension between preserving tradition and adapting to new economic and social realities

Her character arc from timid dependent to independent businesswoman mirrors the broader social changes of mid-Victorian England.

## Quotations

> "Miss Pole and Miss Matty wrote to me by the same post. They were evidently excited, though they tried to be calm" (003-136)

> "Miss Matty humbly apologised for her little bad spelling" (003-141) — revealing both her modesty and the limited education afforded to women of her generation
