# Jem Hearn

Jem Hearn is a young joiner (carpenter) of Cranford and the sweetheart of Martha,
Miss Matty's faithful servant. Though he never appears directly in the narrative,
his presence is felt through Martha's frequent references to him, which reveal a
steady, hardworking tradesman who earns the modest but respectable wage of
"three-and-sixpence a-day" [009-079].

## Trade and Character

Jem Hearn is described as a "steady young man, fit for any girl" [008-174] by
Martha, who evidently holds him in high esteem. He works as a joiner — a skilled
woodworker who makes and fits the joints of furniture and building frames — a trade
that placed him among the respectable artisan class of Cranford. At "six foot one
in his stocking-feet" [009-079], he is an imposing physical presence.

## Relationship with Martha

Martha's attachment to Jem Hearn is one of the few romantic subplots among the
servant class in the novel. She speaks of him with pride and affection, and she is
confident that "Jem Hearn will be only too proud to go with me" [011-142] when
escorting her home. However, Martha has "given missus my word" [008-174] not to
admit him — a promise that causes her genuine distress when she has to shut "the
door in Jem Hearn's face" [008-174].

Martha's dilemma between loyalty to her mistress and her feelings for Jem Hearn
mirrors, in the servant sphere, the romantic tensions that the Cranford ladies
themselves navigate in their own social world.

## Social Standing

When Martha brings Jem Hearn to meet Miss Matty, she introduces him with apologetic
diminishment: "Please, ma'am, he's only Jem Hearn" [029-001]. This phrase — "only
Jem Hearn" — speaks volumes about the social gulf between the servant class and the
genteel spinsters of Cranford. Yet Martha's pride in him is unmistakable, and she
freely reports his opinions: "Jem Hearn said so as well" [016-002], quoting him
with the same authority the Cranford ladies accord to Dr Johnson or the _St James's
Chronicle_.

## Significance

Jem Hearn represents the world of Cranford's working-class men — steady, skilled
tradesmen who exist at the periphery of the novel's social drama but whose labour
and presence sustain the town. His courtship of Martha provides a counterpoint to
the more rarefied romantic concerns of the novel's central characters, reminding
the reader that love and loyalty operate at every level of Cranford society.
