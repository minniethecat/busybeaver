# Duty

## Definition

Duty in *Cranford* is the moral framework that governs individual conduct and sustains the community through crisis. It operates on multiple levels — from the formal obligations of a rector's daughter to the informal but binding duties of friendship and neighbourhood. Unlike the abstract virtue it might be in a conduct manual, duty in Cranford is tested by real economic hardship, and the novel's climax turns on the question of what one owes to others when one has almost nothing to give.

## Manifestations in Cranford

The language of duty runs through the novel, invoked by character after character to explain or justify their actions:

- **A lady's duty**: When Mrs Jamieson faces social awkwardness, she acts not from personal preference but "as a lady" — an appeal to duty that elevates personal conduct to a class obligation (014-140)
- **The rector's daughter**: Miss Matty bears the weight of her late father's position; she is described as acting from "duty, as a deceased rector's daughter" — her identity is inseparable from inherited obligation (016-069)
- **The bank failure**: When the Town and County Bank collapses, Miss Matty's first thought is not for her own loss but for her duty to others who might have lost more. She insists she "would think it her duty to offer sovereigns for" those in need — a stunning act of self-abnegation (027-072)
- **Mr Johnson is only doing his duty**: Even the bank manager's failure is framed in the language of duty, suggesting how deeply this concept permeates all social roles (026-157)

The most poignant invocation comes when Miss Matty speaks of duty as "not only a duty, but a pleasure — a true pleasure" — transforming obligation into something approaching joy (029-129).

## Key Characters

- **Miss Matty Jenkyns** — the novel's supreme example of duty embodied. Her entire life is shaped by duty to her father, her community, and her own moral code. When the bank fails, she faces the ultimate test of duty against survival (027-072, 031-106)
- **Mrs Jamieson** — represents duty as social performance, using class obligation to justify exclusion and hierarchy
- **Mary Smith** — the narrator who observes Cranford's duty-code from outside, sometimes admiring, sometimes questioning

## Narrative Function

Duty serves as the novel's moral architecture:

1. **Crisis and resolution**: When economic catastrophe strikes, it is duty — not wealth or status — that provides the framework for collective response. The community rallies around Miss Matty because it is their duty.
2. **Character revelation**: A character's relationship to duty reveals their moral core. Miss Matty's self-sacrificing duty contrasts with Mrs Jamieson's self-serving version.
3. **Social cohesion**: Shared understanding of duty enables Cranford to function without formal institutions. It is the glue that holds the town together when banks fail and fortunes collapse.

## Victorian Context

Duty was a central concept in Victorian moral philosophy, powerfully articulated by Thomas Carlyle and embedded in the era's religious and social thought. For women of the gentry class, duty was particularly freighted — it was one of the few forms of moral agency available in a system that denied them economic and political power. Miss Matty's appeal to duty is not merely personal virtue but a reflection of a culture in which women's moral authority was exercised through obligation rather than rights.
