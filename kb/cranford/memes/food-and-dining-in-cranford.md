# Food and Dining in Cranford

Food and dining in Cranford are never merely matters of sustenance. Every meal,
every refreshment, every orange is governed by an elaborate code of etiquette that
reveals the town's deepest anxieties about class, propriety, and the precariousness
of gentility. Gaskell uses food as a lens through which to examine Cranford's
social structure with characteristic wit and affection.

## The Orange Ritual

The most famous food passage in the novel concerns the eating of oranges. "When
oranges came in, a curious proceeding was gone through" [006-031]: Miss Jenkyns
and Miss Matty would each take an orange in silence, then "withdraw to the privacy
of their own rooms to indulge in sucking oranges" [006-032]. The reason was that
sucking was the only proper way to enjoy an orange — cutting caused the juice to
run — yet sucking carried "the unpleasant association with a ceremony frequently
gone through by little babies" [006-032]. The orange ritual thus condenses
Cranford's entire social philosophy: a desperate attempt to reconcile bodily needs
with the demands of decorum.

After Miss Jenkyns's death, Miss Matty was "quite horrified" [006-034] when the
narrator suggested she remain in the dining-parlour to enjoy her orange. "Miss
Jenkyns's rules were made more stringent than ever, because the framer of them was
gone where there could be no appeal" [006-035].

## Supper and Gentility

The Cranford ladies "never ate suppers in general" [014-022] — supper being
considered somewhat common — yet they were, "like most non-supper-eaters,
particularly hungry on all special occasions" [014-022]. At Miss Barker's party,
the guests were served "all sorts of good things for supper — scalloped oysters,
potted lobsters, jelly, a dish called 'little Cupids'" [014-020], the latter
"in great favour with the Cranford ladies, although too expensive to be given,
except on solemn occasions" [014-021].

## Tea and Hospitality

Tea is the social lubricant of Cranford. Mrs Forrester's maid "disturbed the ladies
on the sofa by a request that she might get the tea-tray out from underneath"
[001-137], and despite the informality of this proceeding, "everyone took this
novel proceeding as the most natural thing in the world" [001-138]. On more formal
occasions, a hostess would spend "all the morning making tea-bread and
sponge-cakes" [001-147], the effort of preparation carefully concealed behind an
air of casual hospitality.

## Coals and Basic Provision

At the humblest level, food appears in the form of payment and provision. Miss Pole
promised a cottage boy's parents "a hundredweight of coals at Christmas, and his
supper every evening, for the loan of him at nights" [021-118] during the panic.
The boy, "half-starved at home" [021-121], ate "hearty suppers" [021-121] at Miss
Pole's — a reminder that beneath Cranford's genteel surface, real hunger existed.
