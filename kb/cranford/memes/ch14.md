# Section 14

[014-001] “Yes. [[mrs-jamieson|Mrs Jamieson]] most kindly and condescendingly said she would be

[014-002] happy to come. One little stipulation she made, that she should bring

[014-003] Carlo. I told her that if I had a weakness, it was for dogs.”

[014-004] “And [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]]?” questioned [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]], who was thinking of her pool at

[014-005] Preference, in which Carlo would not be available as a partner.

[014-006] “I am going to ask Miss Pole. Of course, I could not think of asking

[014-007] her until I had asked you, madam—the rector’s daughter, madam. Believe

[014-008] me, I do not forget the situation my father held under yours.”

[014-009] “And [[mrs-forrester|Mrs Forrester]], of course?”

[014-010] “And Mrs Forrester. I thought, in fact, of going to her before I went

[014-011] to Miss Pole. Although her circumstances are changed, madam, she was

[014-012] born a Tyrrell, and we can never forget her alliance to the Bigges, of

[014-013] Bigelow Hall.”

[014-014] Miss Matty cared much more for the little circumstance of her being a

[014-015] very good card-player.

[014-016] “[[mrs-fitz-adam|Mrs Fitz-Adam]]—I suppose”—

[014-017] “No, madam. I must draw a line somewhere. Mrs Jamieson would not, I

[014-018] think, like to meet Mrs Fitz-Adam. I have the greatest respect for Mrs

[014-019] Fitz-Adam—but I cannot think her fit society for such ladies as Mrs

[014-020] Jamieson and Miss Matilda Jenkyns.”

[014-021] Miss Betty Barker bowed low to Miss Matty, and pursed up her mouth. She

[014-022] looked at me with sidelong dignity, as much as to say, although a

[014-023] retired milliner, she was no democrat, and understood the difference of

[014-024] ranks.

[014-025] “May I beg you to come as near half-past six to my little dwelling, as

[014-026] possible, Miss Matilda? Mrs Jamieson dines at five, but has kindly

[014-027] promised not to delay her visit beyond that time—half-past six.” And

[014-028] with a swimming curtsey Miss Betty Barker took her leave.

[014-029] My prophetic soul foretold a visit that afternoon from Miss Pole, who

[014-030] usually came to call on Miss Matilda after any event—or indeed in sight

[014-031] of any event—to talk it over with her.

[014-032] “Miss Betty told me it was to be a choice and select few,” said Miss

[014-033] Pole, as she and Miss Matty compared notes.

[014-034] “Yes, so she said. Not even Mrs Fitz-Adam.”

[014-035] Now Mrs Fitz-Adam was the widowed sister of the [[cranford-town|Cranford]] surgeon, whom I

[014-036] have named before. Their parents were respectable farmers, content with

[014-037] their station. The name of these good people was Hoggins. [[mr-hoggins|Mr Hoggins]]

[014-038] was the Cranford doctor now; we disliked the name and considered it

[014-039] coarse; but, as [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]] said, if he changed it to Piggins it would

[014-040] not be much better. We had hoped to discover a relationship between him

[014-041] and that Marchioness of Exeter whose name was Molly Hoggins; but the

[014-042] man, careless of his own interests, utterly ignored and denied any such

[014-043] relationship, although, as dear Miss Jenkyns had said, he had a sister

[014-044] called Mary, and the same Christian names were very apt to run in

[014-045] families.

[014-046] Soon after Miss Mary Hoggins married Mr Fitz-Adam, she disappeared from

[014-047] the neighbourhood for many years. She did not move in a sphere in

[014-048] [[the-cranford-ladies|Cranford society]] sufficiently high to make any of us care to know what

[014-049] Mr Fitz-Adam was. He died and was gathered to his fathers without our

[014-050] ever having thought about him at all. And then Mrs Fitz-Adam reappeared

[014-051] in Cranford (“as bold as a lion,” Miss Pole said), a well-to-do [[widowhood-in-cranford|widow]],

[014-052] dressed in rustling black silk, so soon after her husband’s death that

[014-053] poor Miss Jenkyns was justified in the remark she made, that “bombazine

[014-054] would have shown a deeper sense of her loss.”

[014-055] I remember the convocation of ladies who assembled to decide whether or

[014-056] not Mrs Fitz-Adam should be called upon by the old blue-blooded

[014-057] inhabitants of Cranford. She had taken a large rambling house, which

[014-058] had been usually considered to confer a patent of gentility upon its

[014-059] tenant, because, once upon a time, seventy or eighty years before, the

[014-060] [[spinsterhood|spinster]] daughter of an earl had resided in it. I am not sure if the

[014-061] inhabiting this house was not also believed to convey some unusual power

[014-062] of intellect; for the earl’s daughter, Lady Jane, had a sister, Lady

[014-063] Anne, who had married a general officer in the time of the American war,

[014-064] and this general officer had written one or two comedies, which were

[014-065] still acted on the London boards, and which, when we saw them

[014-066] advertised, made us all draw up, and feel that Drury Lane was paying a

[014-067] very pretty compliment to Cranford. Still, it was not at all a settled

[014-068] thing that Mrs Fitz-Adam was to be visited, when dear Miss Jenkyns died;

[014-069] and, with her, something of the clear knowledge of the strict code of

[014-070] gentility went out too. As Miss Pole observed, “As most of the ladies

[014-071] of good family in Cranford were elderly spinsters, or widows without

[014-072] children, if we did not relax a little, and become less exclusive,

[014-073] by-and-by we should have no society at all.”

[014-074] Mrs Forrester continued on the same side.

[014-075] “She had always understood that Fitz meant something aristocratic; there

[014-076] was Fitz-Roy—she thought that some of the King’s children had been

[014-077] called Fitz-Roy; and there was Fitz-Clarence, now—they were the children

[014-078] of dear good King William the Fourth. Fitz-Adam!—it was a pretty name,

[014-079] and she thought it very probably meant ‘Child of Adam.’ No one, who had

[014-080] not some good blood in their veins, would dare to be called Fitz; there

[014-081] was a deal in a name—she had had a cousin who spelt his name with two

[014-082] little ffs—ffoulkes—and he always looked down upon capital [[letter-writing|letters]] and

[014-083] said they belonged to lately-invented families. She had been afraid he

[014-084] would die a bachelor, he was so very choice. When he met with a Mrs

[014-085] ffarringdon, at a watering-place, he took to her immediately; and a very

[014-086] pretty genteel woman she was—a widow, with a very good fortune; and ‘my

[014-087] cousin,’ Mr ffoulkes, married her; and it was all owing to her two

[014-088] little ffs.”

[014-089] Mrs Fitz-Adam did not stand a chance of meeting with a Mr Fitz-anything

[014-090] in Cranford, so that could not have been her motive for settling there.

[014-091] Miss Matty thought it might have been the hope of being admitted into

[014-092] the society of the place, which would certainly be a very agreeable rise

[014-093] for _ci-devant_ Miss Hoggins; and if this had been her hope it would be

[014-094] cruel to disappoint her.

[014-095] So everybody called upon Mrs Fitz-Adam—everybody but Mrs Jamieson, who

[014-096] used to show how honourable she was by never seeing Mrs Fitz-Adam when

[014-097] they met at the Cranford parties. There would be only eight or ten

[014-098] ladies in the room, and Mrs Fitz-Adam was the largest of all, and she

[014-099] invariably used to stand up when Mrs Jamieson came in, and curtsey very

[014-100] low to her whenever she turned in her direction—so low, in fact, that I

[014-101] think Mrs Jamieson must have looked at the wall above her, for she never

[014-102] moved a muscle of her face, no more than if she had not seen her. Still

[014-103] Mrs Fitz-Adam persevered.

[014-104] The spring evenings were getting bright and long when three or four

[014-105] ladies in calashes met at [[miss-barker|Miss Barker]]’s door. Do you know what a

[014-106] calash is? It is a covering worn over caps, not unlike the heads

[014-107] fastened on old-fashioned gigs; but sometimes it is not quite so large.

[014-108] This kind of head-gear always made an awful impression on the children

[014-109] in Cranford; and now two or three left off their play in the quiet

[014-110] sunny little street, and gathered in wondering silence round Miss Pole,

[014-111] Miss Matty, and myself. We were silent too, so that we could hear

[014-112] loud, suppressed whispers inside Miss Barker’s house: “Wait, Peggy! wait

[014-113] till I’ve run upstairs and washed my hands. When I cough, open the

[014-114] door; I’ll not be a minute.”

[014-115] And, true enough it was not a minute before we heard a noise, between a

[014-116] sneeze and a crow; on which the door flew open. Behind it stood a

[014-117] round-eyed maiden, all aghast at the honourable company of calashes, who

[014-118] marched in without a word. She recovered presence of mind enough to

[014-119] usher us into a small room, which had been the shop, but was now

[014-120] converted into a temporary dressing-room. There we unpinned and shook

[014-121] ourselves, and arranged our features before the glass into a sweet and

[014-122] gracious company-face; and then, bowing backwards with “After you,

[014-123] ma’am,” we allowed Mrs Forrester to take precedence up the narrow

[014-124] staircase that led to Miss Barker’s drawing-room. There she sat, as

[014-125] stately and composed as though we had never heard that odd-sounding

[014-126] cough, from which her throat must have been even then sore and rough.

[014-127] Kind, gentle, shabbily-dressed Mrs Forrester was immediately conducted

[014-128] to the second place of honour—a seat arranged something like Prince

[014-129] Albert’s near the Queen’s—good, but not so good. The place of

[014-130] pre-eminence was, of course, reserved for the Honourable Mrs Jamieson,

[014-131] who presently came panting up the stairs—Carlo rushing round her on her

[014-132] progress, as if he meant to trip her up.

[014-133] And now Miss Betty Barker was a proud and happy woman! She stirred the

[014-134] fire, and shut the door, and sat as near to it as she could, quite on

[014-135] the edge of her chair. When Peggy came in, tottering under the weight

[014-136] of [[tea-drinking|the tea-tray]], I noticed that Miss Barker was sadly afraid lest Peggy

[014-137] should not keep her distance sufficiently. She and her mistress were on

[014-138] very familiar terms in their every-day intercourse, and Peggy wanted now

[014-139] to make several little confidences to her, which Miss Barker was on

[014-140] thorns to hear, but which she thought it her duty, as a lady, to

[014-141] repress. So she turned away from all Peggy’s asides and signs; but she

[014-142] made one or two very malapropos answers to what was said; and at last,

[014-143] seized with a bright idea, she exclaimed, “Poor, sweet Carlo! I’m

[014-144] forgetting him. Come downstairs with me, poor ittie doggie, and it shall

[014-145] have its tea, it shall!”

[014-146] In a few minutes she returned, bland and benignant as before; but I

[014-147] thought she had forgotten to give the “poor ittie doggie” anything to

[014-148] eat, judging by the avidity with which he swallowed down chance pieces

[014-149] of cake. The tea-tray was abundantly loaded—I was pleased to see it, I

[014-150] was so hungry; but I was afraid the ladies present might think it

[014-151] vulgarly heaped up. I know they would have done at their own houses;

[014-152] but somehow the heaps disappeared here. I saw Mrs Jamieson eating

[014-153] seed-cake, slowly and considerately, as she did everything; and I was

[014-154] rather surprised, for I knew she had told us, on the occasion of her

[014-155] last party, that she never had it in her house, it reminded her so much

[014-156] of scented soap. She always gave us Savoy biscuits. However, Mrs

[014-157] Jamieson was kindly indulgent to Miss Barker’s want of knowledge of the

[014-158] customs of high life; and, to spare her feelings, ate three large pieces

[014-159] of seed-cake, with a placid, ruminating expression of countenance, not

[014-160] unlike a cow’s.

[014-161] After tea there was some little demur and difficulty. We were six in

[014-162] number; four could play at Preference, and for the other two there was

[014-163] Cribbage. But all, except myself (I was rather afraid of the Cranford

[014-164] ladies at cards, for it was the most earnest and serious business they

[014-165] ever engaged in), were anxious to be of the “pool.” Even Miss Barker,

[014-166] while declaring she did not know Spadille from Manille, was evidently

[014-167] hankering to take a hand. The dilemma was soon put an end to by a

[014-168] singular kind of noise. If a baron’s daughter-in-law could ever be

[014-169] supposed to snore, I should have said Mrs Jamieson did so then; for,

[014-170] overcome by the heat of the room, and inclined to doze by nature, the

[014-171] temptation of that very comfortable arm-chair had been too much for her,

[014-172] and Mrs Jamieson was nodding. Once or twice she opened her eyes with an

[014-173] effort, and calmly but unconsciously smiled upon us; but by-and-by, even

[014-174] her [[kindness-in-cranford|benevolence]] was not equal to this exertion, and she was sound

[014-175] asleep.
