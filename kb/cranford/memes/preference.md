# Preference (Card Game)

## Definition

Preference is the card game at the heart of Cranford's social life. More than mere
entertainment, it functions as the structured ritual around which evening parties
are organised, social hierarchies are reinforced, and the town's unwritten codes of
conduct are performed. The game provides a rare space in which Cranford ladies may
compete openly — within tightly regulated bounds. In a society where "all the
holders of houses, above a certain rank, were ladies" [001-098], the card-table
became the one arena where hierarchy was simultaneously enacted and momentarily
suspended by the democracy of the deal.

## Manifestations in Cranford

Card-playing is the organising principle of Cranford evening entertainment, and
preference is the chosen game:

- **The ritual preparation**: Before a card party, "candles, and clean packs of cards, were arranged on each table" [002-077] — the ceremonial aspect of preparation signals the importance of the event
- **The table arrangement**: The tea-trays were "placed each on the middle of a card-table" [002-087]; "the china was delicate egg-shell; the old-fashioned silver glittered with polishing" [002-088] — creating a formal, almost altar-like setting for play
- **Earnest engagement**: When the ladies play, it is "the most earnest" activity [014-164] — preference is taken seriously, not as a casual pastime
- **The social drama**: Miss Barker whispers at the card-table [015-003], and the table itself is described as "an animated scene" [015-013], suggesting that much more than cards is being exchanged

## The Card-Table as Social Map

The seating at the card-table is never accidental. Mrs Jamieson, the social arbiter
of Cranford, positions herself at "a card-table nearest Miss Jessie" [002-115] — a
seating choice that simultaneously signals her approval of the young woman and
reinforces her own centrality. When Miss Jenkyns "tried to drown this confession by
a terrible cough" [002-116], it was because she had just remembered that Miss
Jessie's uncle was a shopkeeper — and that Mrs Jamieson, at the neighbouring
card-table, might overhear. The card-table thus becomes the stage on which
Cranford's class anxieties are performed, the proximity of bodies making visible
the distances of rank.

After the cards are put away, "comparing of cards, and talking over previous games"
[002-125] occupies the company — the game extending beyond its formal duration into
the social analysis that follows.

## Conversation-Cards and Puzzle-Cards

The card-table also fosters more whimsical forms of entertainment. Miss Pole
recounts "a kaleidoscope, conversation-cards, puzzle-cards (tied together to an
interminable string)" [016-149][016-150] among the diversions at a gathering.
These "conversation-cards" and "puzzle-cards" represent a gentler, more whimsical
alternative to the earnest competition of preference — a reminder that play in
Cranford could also be purely sociable.

## Key Characters

- **Mrs Jamieson** — uses her position at the card-table to reinforce her social authority. Her choice of seat is never incidental
- **Miss Barker** — whispers secrets at the card-table, using the game's formal structure as cover for informal communication
- **Miss Pole** — described as "a very good card-player" [014-015], her skill at preference reflects her broader competence and sharp intelligence

## Narrative Function

The card game serves the novel in several ways:

1. **Social microcosm**: The card-table is Cranford society in miniature — hierarchical, rule-bound, but animated by personal relationships
2. **Safe competition**: In a world where ladies are not supposed to compete openly, preference provides an acceptable arena for rivalry
3. **Plot mechanism**: Conversations at the card-table advance the novel's gossip-driven plot. Information is traded as cards are played

## Victorian Context

Preference was a trick-taking card game popular in early-to-mid nineteenth-century
England, related to whist. It was considered a respectable game for mixed company
and was particularly associated with provincial gentry gatherings. By the 1850s,
whist was beginning to eclipse preference in fashion, though Cranford —
characteristically — holds to the older form. The game required four players,
making it a naturally social activity that reinforced the importance of the right
number of guests.
