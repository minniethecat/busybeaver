# Section 33

[033-001] I don’t believe Mr Peter came home from [[india-in-cranford|India]] as rich as a nabob; he

[033-002] even considered himself poor, but neither he nor [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]] cared much

[033-003] about that. At any rate, he had enough to live upon “very genteelly” at

[033-004] [[cranford-town|Cranford]]; he and Miss Matty together. And a day or two after his

[033-005] arrival, the shop was closed, while troops of little urchins gleefully

[033-006] awaited the shower of comfits and lozenges that came from time to time

[033-007] down upon their faces as they stood up-gazing at Miss Matty’s

[033-008] drawing-room windows. Occasionally Miss Matty would say to them

[033-009] (half-hidden behind the curtains), “My dear children, don’t make

[033-010] yourselves ill;” but a strong arm pulled her back, and a more rattling

[033-011] shower than ever succeeded. A part of the [[tea-drinking|tea]] was sent in presents to

[033-012] the Cranford ladies; and some of it was distributed among the old people

[033-013] who remembered Mr Peter in the days of his frolicsome youth. The Indian

[033-014] muslin gown was reserved for darling Flora Gordon (Miss Jessie Brown’s

[033-015] daughter). The Gordons had been on the Continent for the last few

[033-016] years, but were now expected to return very soon; and Miss Matty, in her

[033-017] sisterly pride, anticipated great delight in the joy of showing them Mr

[033-018] Peter. The pearl necklace disappeared; and about that time many

[033-019] handsome and useful presents made their appearance in the households of

[033-020] [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] and [[mrs-forrester|Mrs Forrester]]; and some rare and delicate Indian ornaments

[033-021] graced the drawing-rooms of [[mrs-jamieson|Mrs Jamieson]] and [[mrs-fitz-adam|Mrs Fitz-Adam]]. I myself

[033-022] was not forgotten. Among other things, I had the handsomest-bound and

[033-023] best edition of Dr Johnson’s works that could be procured; and dear Miss

[033-024] Matty, with tears in her eyes, begged me to consider it as a present

[033-025] from her sister as well as herself. In short, no one was forgotten;

[033-026] and, what was more, every one, however insignificant, who had shown

[033-027] kindness to Miss Matty at any time, was sure of Mr Peter’s cordial

[033-028] regard.

[033-029] CHAPTER XVI.

[033-030] PEACE TO CRANFORD

[033-031] IT was not surprising that Mr Peter became such a favourite at Cranford.

[033-032] The ladies vied with each other who should admire him most; and no

[033-033] wonder, for their quiet lives were astonishingly stirred up by the

[033-034] arrival from India—especially as the person arrived told more wonderful

[033-035] stories than Sindbad the Sailor; and, as Miss Pole said, was quite as

[033-036] good as an Arabian Night any evening. For my own part, I had vibrated

[033-037] all my life between Drumble and Cranford, and I thought it was quite

[033-038] possible that all Mr Peter’s stories might be true, although wonderful;

[033-039] but when I found that, if we swallowed an anecdote of tolerable

[033-040] magnitude one week, we had the dose considerably increased the next, I

[033-041] began to have my doubts; especially as I noticed that when his sister

[033-042] was present the accounts of Indian life were comparatively tame; not

[033-043] that she knew more than we did, perhaps less. I noticed also that when

[033-044] the rector came to call, Mr Peter talked in a different way about the

[033-045] countries he had been in. But I don’t think the ladies in Cranford

[033-046] would have considered him such a wonderful traveller if they had only

[033-047] heard him talk in the quiet way he did to him. They liked him the

[033-048] better, indeed, for being what they called “so very Oriental.”

[033-049] One day, at a select party in his honour, which Miss Pole gave, and from

[033-050] which, as Mrs Jamieson honoured it with her presence, and had even

[033-051] offered to send Mr Mulliner to wait, Mr and Mrs Hoggins and Mrs

[033-052] Fitz-Adam were necessarily excluded—one day at Miss Pole’s, Mr Peter

[033-053] said he was tired of sitting upright against the hard-backed uneasy

[033-054] chairs, and asked if he might not indulge himself in sitting

[033-055] cross-legged. Miss Pole’s consent was eagerly given, and down he went

[033-056] with the utmost gravity. But when Miss Pole asked me, in an audible

[033-057] whisper, “if he did not remind me of the Father of the Faithful?” I

[033-058] could not help thinking of poor Simon Jones, the lame tailor, and while

[033-059] Mrs Jamieson slowly commented on the elegance and convenience of the

[033-060] attitude, I remembered how we had all followed that lady’s lead in

[033-061] condemning [[mr-hoggins|Mr Hoggins]] for vulgarity because he simply crossed his legs

[033-062] as he sat still on his chair. Many of Mr Peter’s ways of eating were a

[033-063] little strange amongst such ladies as Miss Pole, and Miss Matty, and Mrs

[033-064] Jamieson, especially when I recollected the untasted green peas and

[033-065] two-pronged forks at poor [[mr-holbrook|Mr Holbrook]]’s dinner.

[033-066] The mention of that gentleman’s name recalls to my mind a conversation

[033-067] between Mr Peter and Miss Matty one evening in the summer after he

[033-068] returned to Cranford. The day had been very hot, and Miss Matty had

[033-069] been much oppressed by the weather, in the heat of which her brother

[033-070] revelled. I remember that she had been unable to nurse [[martha|Martha]]’s baby,

[033-071] which had become her favourite employment of late, and which was as much

[033-072] at home in her arms as in its mother’s, as long as it remained a

[033-073] light-weight, portable by one so fragile as Miss Matty. This day to

[033-074] which I refer, Miss Matty had seemed more than usually feeble and

[033-075] languid, and only revived when the sun went down, and her sofa was

[033-076] wheeled to the open window, through which, although it looked into the

[033-077] principal street of Cranford, the fragrant smell of the neighbouring

[033-078] hayfields came in every now and then, borne by the soft breezes that

[033-079] stirred the dull air of the summer twilight, and then died away. The

[033-080] silence of the sultry atmosphere was lost in the murmuring noises which

[033-081] came in from many an open window and door; even the children were abroad

[033-082] in the street, late as it was (between ten and eleven), enjoying the

[033-083] game of play for which they had not had spirits during the heat of the

[033-084] day. It was a source of satisfaction to Miss Matty to see how few

[033-085] candles were lighted, even in the apartments of those houses from which

[033-086] issued the greatest signs of life. Mr Peter, Miss Matty, and I had all

[033-087] been quiet, each with a separate reverie, for some little time, when Mr

[033-088] Peter broke in—

[033-089] “Do you know, little Matty, I could have sworn you were on the high road

[033-090] to matrimony when I left England that last time! If anybody had told me

[033-091] you would have lived and died an old maid then, I should have laughed in

[033-092] their faces.”

[033-093] Miss Matty made no reply, and I tried in vain to think of some subject

[033-094] which should effectually turn the conversation; but I was very stupid;

[033-095] and before I spoke he went on—

[033-096] “It was Holbrook, that fine manly fellow who lived at [[woodley|Woodley]], that I

[033-097] used to think would carry off my little Matty. You would not think it

[033-098] now, I dare say, Mary; but this sister of mine was once a very pretty

[033-099] girl—at least, I thought so, and so I’ve a notion did poor Holbrook.

[033-100] What business had he to die before I came home to thank him for all his

[033-101] kindness to a good-for-nothing cub as I was? It was that that made me

[033-102] first think he cared for you; for in all our fishing expeditions it was

[033-103] Matty, Matty, we talked about. Poor Deborah! What a lecture she read

[033-104] me on having asked him home to lunch one day, when she had seen the

[033-105] Arley carriage in the town, and thought that my lady might call. Well,

[033-106] that’s long years ago; more than half a life-time, and yet it seems like

[033-107] yesterday! I don’t know a fellow I should have liked better as a

[033-108] brother-in-law. You must have played your cards badly, my little Matty,

[033-109] somehow or another—wanted your brother to be a good go-between, eh,

[033-110] little one?” said he, putting out his hand to take hold of hers as she

[033-111] lay on the sofa. “Why, what’s this? you’re shivering and shaking,

[033-112] Matty, with that confounded open window. Shut it, Mary, this minute!”

[033-113] I did so, and then stooped down to kiss Miss Matty, and see if she

[033-114] really were chilled. She caught at my hand, and gave it a hard

[033-115] squeeze—but unconsciously, I think—for in a minute or two she spoke to

[033-116] us quite in her usual voice, and smiled our uneasiness away, although

[033-117] she patiently submitted to the prescriptions we enforced of a warm bed

[033-118] and a glass of weak negus. I was to leave Cranford the next day, and

[033-119] before I went I saw that all the effects of the open window had quite

[033-120] vanished. I had superintended most of the alterations necessary in the

[033-121] house and household during the latter weeks of my stay. The shop was

[033-122] once more a parlour: the empty resounding rooms again furnished up to

[033-123] the very garrets.

[033-124] There had been some talk of establishing Martha and Jem in another

[033-125] house, but Miss Matty would not hear of this. Indeed, I never saw her

[033-126] so much roused as when Miss Pole had assumed it to be the most desirable

[033-127] arrangement. As long as Martha would remain with Miss Matty, Miss Matty

[033-128] was only too thankful to have her about her; yes, and Jem too, who was a

[033-129] very pleasant man to have in the house, for she never saw him from

[033-130] week’s end to week’s end. And as for the probable children, if they

[033-131] would all turn out such little darlings as her god-daughter, Matilda,

[033-132] she should not mind the number, if Martha didn’t. Besides, the next was

[033-133] to be called Deborah—a point which Miss Matty had reluctantly yielded to

[033-134] Martha’s stubborn determination that her first-born was to be Matilda.

[033-135] So Miss Pole had to lower her colours, and even her voice, as she said

[033-136] to me that, as Mr and Mrs Hearn were still to go on living in the same

[033-137] house with Miss Matty, we had certainly done a wise thing in hiring

[033-138] Martha’s niece as an auxiliary.

[033-139] I left Miss Matty and Mr Peter most comfortable and contented; the only

[033-140] subject for regret to the tender heart of the one, and the social

[033-141] friendly nature of the other, being the unfortunate quarrel between Mrs

[033-142] Jamieson and the plebeian Hogginses and their following. In joke, I

[033-143] prophesied one day that this would only last until Mrs Jamieson or Mr

[033-144] Mulliner were ill, in which case they would only be too glad to be

[033-145] friends with Mr Hoggins; but Miss Matty did not like my looking forward

[033-146] to anything like illness in so light a manner, and before the year was

[033-147] out all had come round in a far more satisfactory way.

[033-148] I received two Cranford [[letter-writing|letters]] on one auspicious October morning. Both

[033-149] Miss Pole and Miss Matty wrote to ask me to come over and meet the

[033-150] Gordons, who had returned to England alive and well with their two

[033-151] children, now almost grown up. Dear Jessie Brown had kept her old kind

[033-152] nature, although she had changed her name and station; and she wrote to

[033-153] say that she and Major Gordon expected to be in Cranford on the

[033-154] fourteenth, and she hoped and begged to be remembered to Mrs Jamieson

[033-155] (named first, as became her honourable station), Miss Pole and Miss

[033-156] Matty—could she ever forget their kindness to her poor father and

[033-157] sister?—Mrs Forrester, Mr Hoggins (and here again came in an allusion to

[033-158] kindness shown to the dead long ago), his new wife, who as such must

[033-159] allow [[lady-glenmire|Mrs Gordon]] to desire to make her acquaintance, and who was,

[033-160] moreover, an old Scotch friend of her husband’s. In short, every one

[033-161] was named, from the rector—who had been appointed to Cranford in the

[033-162] interim between [[captain-brown|Captain Brown]]’s death and Miss Jessie’s marriage, and

[033-163] was now associated with the latter event—down to Miss Betty Barker. All

[033-164] were asked to the luncheon; all except Mrs Fitz-Adam, who had come to

[033-165] live in Cranford since Miss Jessie Brown’s days, and whom I found rather

[033-166] moping on account of the omission. People wondered at Miss Betty

[033-167] Barker’s being included in the honourable list; but, then, as Miss Pole

[033-168] said, we must remember the disregard of the genteel proprieties of life

[033-169] in which the poor captain had educated his girls, and for his sake we

[033-170] swallowed our pride. Indeed, Mrs Jamieson rather took it as a

[033-171] compliment, as putting Miss Betty (formerly _her_ maid) on a level with

[033-172] “those Hogginses.”
