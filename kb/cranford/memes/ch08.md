# Section 8

[008-001] When the ducks and green peas came, we looked at each other in dismay;

[008-002] we had only two-pronged, black-handled forks. It is true the steel was

[008-003] as bright as silver; but what were we to do? [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]] picked up her

[008-004] peas, one by one, on the point of the prongs, much as Aminé ate her

[008-005] grains of rice after her previous feast with the Ghoul. [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]]

[008-006] sighed over her delicate young peas as she left them on one side of her

[008-007] plate untasted, for they _would_ drop between the prongs. I looked at

[008-008] my host: the peas were going wholesale into his capacious mouth,

[008-009] shovelled up by his large round-ended knife. I saw, I imitated, I

[008-010] survived! My friends, in spite of my precedent, could not muster up

[008-011] courage enough to do an ungenteel thing; and, if [[mr-holbrook|Mr Holbrook]] had not

[008-012] been so heartily hungry, he would probably have seen that the good peas

[008-013] went away almost untouched.

[008-014] After dinner, a clay pipe was brought in, and a spittoon; and, asking us

[008-015] to retire to another room, where he would soon join us, if we disliked

[008-016] tobacco-smoke, he presented his pipe to Miss Matty, and requested her to

[008-017] fill the bowl. This was a compliment to a lady in his youth; but it was

[008-018] rather inappropriate to propose it as an honour to Miss Matty, who had

[008-019] been trained by her sister to hold smoking of every kind in utter

[008-020] abhorrence. But if it was a shock to her refinement, it was also a

[008-021] gratification to her feelings to be thus selected; so she daintily

[008-022] stuffed the strong tobacco into the pipe, and then we withdrew.

[008-023] “It is very pleasant dining with a bachelor,” said Miss Matty softly, as

[008-024] we settled ourselves in the counting-house. “I only hope it is not

[008-025] improper; so many pleasant things are!”

[008-026] “What a number of [[reading-in-cranford|books]] he has!” said Miss Pole, looking round the room.

[008-027] “And how dusty they are!”

[008-028] “I think it must be like one of the great Dr Johnson’s rooms,” said Miss

[008-029] Matty. “What a superior man your cousin must be!”

[008-030] “Yes!” said Miss Pole, “he’s a great reader; but I am afraid he has got

[008-031] into very uncouth habits with living alone.”

[008-032] “Oh! uncouth is too hard a word. I should call him eccentric; very

[008-033] clever people always are!” replied Miss Matty.

[008-034] [Picture: Now, what colour are ash-buds in March]

[008-035] When Mr Holbrook returned, he proposed a walk in the fields; but the two

[008-036] elder ladies were afraid of damp, and dirt, and had only very unbecoming

[008-037] calashes to put on over their caps; so they declined, and I was again

[008-038] his companion in a turn which he said he was obliged to take to see

[008-039] after his men. He strode along, either wholly forgetting my existence,

[008-040] or soothed into silence by his pipe—and yet it was not silence exactly.

[008-041] He walked before me with a stooping gait, his hands clasped behind him;

[008-042] and, as some tree or cloud, or glimpse of distant upland pastures,

[008-043] struck him, he quoted poetry to himself, saying it out loud in a grand

[008-044] sonorous voice, with just the emphasis that true feeling and

[008-045] appreciation give. We came upon an old cedar tree, which stood at one

[008-046] end of the house—

[008-047] “The cedar spreads his dark-green layers of shade.”

[008-048] “Capital term—‘layers!’ Wonderful man!” I did not know whether he was

[008-049] speaking to me or not; but I put in an assenting “wonderful,” although I

[008-050] knew nothing about it, just because I was tired of being forgotten, and

[008-051] of being consequently silent.

[008-052] He turned sharp round. “Ay! you may say ‘wonderful.’ Why, when I saw

[008-053] the review of his poems in _Blackwood_, I set off within an hour, and

[008-054] walked seven miles to Misselton (for the horses were not in the way) and

[008-055] ordered them. Now, what colour are ash-buds in March?”

[008-056] Is the man going mad? thought I. He is very like Don Quixote.

[008-057] “What colour are they, I say?” repeated he vehemently.

[008-058] “I am sure I don’t know, sir,” said I, with the meekness of ignorance.

[008-059] “I knew you didn’t. No more did I—an old fool that I am!—till this

[008-060] young man comes and tells me. Black as ash-buds in March. And I’ve

[008-061] lived all my life in the country; more shame for me not to know. Black:

[008-062] they are jet-black, madam.” And he went off again, swinging along to

[008-063] the music of some rhyme he had got hold of.

[008-064] When we came back, nothing would serve him but he must read us the poems

[008-065] he had been speaking of; and Miss Pole encouraged him in his proposal, I

[008-066] thought, because she wished me to hear his beautiful reading, of which

[008-067] she had boasted; but she afterwards said it was because she had got to a

[008-068] difficult part of her crochet, and wanted to count her stitches without

[008-069] having to talk. Whatever he had proposed would have been right to Miss

[008-070] Matty; although she did fall sound asleep within five minutes after he

[008-071] had begun a long poem, called “Locksley Hall,” and had a comfortable

[008-072] nap, unobserved, till he ended; when the cessation of his voice wakened

[008-073] her up, and she said, feeling that something was expected, and that Miss

[008-074] Pole was counting—

[008-075] “What a pretty book!”

[008-076] “Pretty, madam! it’s beautiful! Pretty, indeed!”

[008-077] “Oh yes! I meant beautiful!” said she, fluttered at his disapproval of

[008-078] her word. “It is so like that beautiful poem of Dr Johnson’s my sister

[008-079] used to read—I forget the name of it; what was it, my dear?” turning to

[008-080] me.

[008-081] “Which do you mean, ma’am? What was it about?”

[008-082] “I don’t remember what it was about, and I’ve quite forgotten what the

[008-083] name of it was; but it was written by Dr Johnson, and was very

[008-084] beautiful, and very like what Mr Holbrook has just been reading.”

[008-085] “I don’t remember it,” said he reflectively. “But I don’t know Dr

[008-086] Johnson’s poems well. I must read them.”

[008-087] As we were getting into the fly to return, I heard Mr Holbrook say he

[008-088] should call on the ladies soon, and inquire how they got home; and this

[008-089] evidently pleased and fluttered Miss Matty at the time he said it; but

[008-090] after we had lost sight of the old house among the trees her sentiments

[008-091] towards the master of it were gradually absorbed into a distressing

[008-092] wonder as to whether [[martha|Martha]] had broken her word, and seized on the

[008-093] opportunity of her mistress’s absence to have a “follower.” Martha

[008-094] looked good, and steady, and composed enough, as she came to help us

[008-095] out; she was always careful of Miss Matty, and to-night she made use of

[008-096] this unlucky speech—

[008-097] “Eh! dear ma’am, to think of your going out in an evening in such a thin

[008-098] shawl! It’s no better than muslin. At your age, ma’am, you should be

[008-099] careful.”

[008-100] “My age!” said Miss Matty, almost speaking crossly, for her, for she was

[008-101] usually gentle—“My age! Why, how old do you think I am, that you talk

[008-102] about my age?”

[008-103] “Well, ma’am, I should say you were not far short of sixty: but folks’

[008-104] looks is often against them—and I’m sure I meant no harm.”

[008-105] “Martha, I’m not yet fifty-two!” said Miss Matty, with grave emphasis;

[008-106] for probably the remembrance of her youth had come very vividly before

[008-107] her this day, and she was annoyed at finding that golden time so far

[008-108] away in the past.

[008-109] But she never spoke of any former and more intimate acquaintance with Mr

[008-110] Holbrook. She had probably met with so little sympathy in her early

[008-111] love, that she had shut it up close in her heart; and it was only by a

[008-112] sort of watching, which I could hardly avoid since Miss Pole’s

[008-113] confidence, that I saw how faithful her poor heart had been in its

[008-114] sorrow and its silence.

[008-115] She gave me some good reason for wearing her best cap every day, and sat

[008-116] near the window, in spite of her rheumatism, in order to see, without

[008-117] being seen, down into the street.

[008-118] He came. He put his open palms upon his knees, which were far apart, as

[008-119] he sat with his head bent down, whistling, after we had replied to his

[008-120] inquiries about our safe return. Suddenly he jumped up—

[008-121] “Well, madam! have you any commands for Paris? I am going there in a

[008-122] week or two.”

[008-123] “To Paris!” we both exclaimed.

[008-124] “Yes, madam! I’ve never been there, and always had a wish to go; and I

[008-125] think if I don’t go soon, I mayn’t go at all; so as soon as the hay is

[008-126] got in I shall go, before harvest time.”

[008-127] We were so much astonished that we had no commissions.

[008-128] Just as he was going out of the room, he turned back, with his favourite

[008-129] exclamation—

[008-130] “God bless my soul, madam! but I nearly forgot half my errand. Here are

[008-131] the poems for you you admired so much the other evening at my house.”

[008-132] He tugged away at a parcel in his coat-pocket. “Good-bye, miss,” said

[008-133] he; “good-bye, Matty! take care of yourself.” And he was gone. But he

[008-134] had given her a book, and he had called her Matty, just as he used to do

[008-135] thirty years ago.

[008-136] “I wish he would not go to Paris,” said Miss Matilda anxiously. “I

[008-137] don’t believe frogs will agree with him; he used to have to be very

[008-138] careful what he ate, which was curious in so strong-looking a young

[008-139] man.”

[008-140] Soon after this I took my leave, giving many an injunction to Martha to

[008-141] look after her mistress, and to let me know if she thought that Miss

[008-142] Matilda was not so well; in which case I would volunteer a visit to my

[008-143] old friend, without noticing Martha’s intelligence to her.

[008-144] Accordingly I received a line or two from Martha every now and then;

[008-145] and, about November I had a note to say her mistress was “very low and

[008-146] sadly off her food”; and the account made me so uneasy that, although

[008-147] Martha did not decidedly summon me, I packed up my things and went.

[008-148] I received a warm welcome, in spite of the little flurry produced by my

[008-149] impromptu visit, for I had only been able to give a day’s notice. Miss

[008-150] Matilda looked miserably ill; and I prepared to comfort and cosset her.

[008-151] I went down to have a private talk with Martha.

[008-152] “How long has your mistress been so poorly?” I asked, as I stood by the

[008-153] kitchen fire.

[008-154] “Well! I think it’s better than a fortnight; it is, I know; it was one

[008-155] Tuesday, after Miss Pole had been, that she went into this moping way.

[008-156] I thought she was tired, and it would go off with a night’s rest; but

[008-157] no! she has gone on and on ever since, till I thought it my duty to

[008-158] write to you, ma’am.”

[008-159] “You did quite right, Martha. It is a comfort to think she has so

[008-160] faithful a servant about her. And I hope you find your place

[008-161] comfortable?”

[008-162] “Well, ma’am, missus is very kind, and there’s plenty to eat and drink,

[008-163] and no more work but what I can do easily—but—” Martha hesitated.

[008-164] “But what, Martha?”

[008-165] “Why, it seems so hard of missus not to let me have any followers;

[008-166] there’s such lots of young fellows in the town; and many a one has as

[008-167] much as offered to keep company with me; and I may never be in such a

[008-168] likely place again, and it’s like wasting an opportunity. Many a girl

[008-169] as I know would have ’em unbeknownst to missus; but I’ve given my word,

[008-170] and I’ll stick to it; or else this is just the house for missus never to

[008-171] be the wiser if they did come: and it’s such a capable kitchen—there’s

[008-172] such dark corners in it—I’d be bound to hide any one. I counted up last

[008-173] Sunday night—for I’ll not deny I was crying because I had to shut the

[008-174] door in Jem Hearn’s face, and he’s a steady young man, fit for any girl;

[008-175] only I had given missus my word.” Martha was all but crying again; and

[008-176] I had little comfort to give her, for I knew, from old experience, of

[008-177] the horror with which both the Miss Jenkynses looked upon “followers”;

[008-178] and in Miss Matty’s present nervous state this dread was not likely to

[008-179] be lessened.
