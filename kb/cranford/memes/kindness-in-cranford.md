# Kindness in Cranford

## Definition

Kindness in *Cranford* is not a passive sentiment but an active, organising principle of community life. It is "somewhat dictatorial" (001-077), practical rather than sentimental, and expressed through small deeds — a jar of potpourri, an errand run, a secret collection taken up — rather than grand gestures. The narrator's exclamation — "what a town Cranford is for kindness!" (004-021) — is not hyperbole but empirical observation.

## The Kindness Economy

Cranford operates on an informal economy of kindness that runs parallel to, and sometimes substitutes for, the cash economy. The novel catalogues small kindnesses with the same care it gives to financial transactions:

- **Miss Matty's errands**: She goes "about on her errands of kindness more shabby than ever" (025-077) — the shabbiness of her dress making the kindness more, not less, visible
- **Captain Brown's "infinite kindness of heart"** (003-026): His practical kindness — fetching a doctor through the snow, reading aloud to invalids — establishes his worth in a town initially suspicious of men
- **Community response to Miss Matty's ruin**: When Miss Matty's bank fails, her friends organise in secret to support her — kindness channelled through discretion
- **"The best kindness"**: The narrator describes "the best kindness such an awkward chap as me could" manage (029-056), acknowledging the limitations of goodwill while insisting on its value

## Kindness as Social Control

Gaskell is clear-eyed about the controlling dimension of Cranford kindness. The town's benevolence is "somewhat dictatorial" (001-077) — kindness administered with firm opinions about how the recipient should live. When Miss Matty's friends intervene in her affairs, their kindness carries an edge of social regulation. The "Christian kindness shown by" the community (022-081) is genuine but also normative: it expects conformity in return.

## Miss Matty as Kindness Incarnate

Miss Matty Jenkyns is the novel's most complete embodiment of kindness. Her gentle nature, her refusal to judge, and her practical solicitude for others make her the moral centre of Cranford. Her kindness is:

- **Unselfconscious**: She does not perform kindness for social credit; she simply acts
- **Concrete**: She makes potpourri for "someone who had no garden" (004-040), a gift calibrated to the recipient's actual need
- **Inherited**: Her kindness echoes the kindnesses of earlier generations, part of a moral lineage

## The Limits of Kindness

The novel acknowledges that kindness has limits. It cannot prevent death, cannot restore lost fortunes, and cannot undo the mistakes of the past. Miss Matty's kindness does not save her from poverty; it is the quality that makes her poverty bearable. The novel's realism lies in this recognition — kindness is not a solution to life's problems but the best response to them.

## Victorian Context

Victorian philanthropy, particularly women's philanthropy, was a significant social force. Middle-class women, excluded from formal politics, channelled their energies into charitable work. The "dictatorial" quality of Cranford kindness reflects a genuine historical pattern: Victorian charity was often paternalistic, even when sincerely motivated. Gaskell's treatment is admiring but not naive — she sees both the value and the limitations of the kindness economy.
