# Vulgarity in Cranford

## Definition

"Vulgar" is, in the narrator's own words, "a tremendous word in Cranford" [001-155]. More than a description of coarse behaviour, "vulgarity" functions as a social weapon — the label that Cranford ladies deploy to police the boundaries of acceptable conduct, distinguish themselves from those below them, and manage the perpetual anxiety of genteel poverty. Anything that draws attention to money, appetite, or bodily existence risks the charge. The word is so powerful that it shapes what Cranford residents may say, eat, wear, and even think.

## Manifestations in Cranford

The charge of vulgarity is levelled throughout the novel at a remarkable range of targets:

- **Vulgarity of wealth**: Spending money conspicuously is the cardinal sin. A comfortable provision is deemed "vulgar and ostentatious" — the Cranford code demands that one have money without appearing to spend it [001-162]. The phrase "vulgarity of wealth" recurs as a settled judgement [017-129].
- **Vulgarity of noise**: "The vulgarity of making any noise in a public place" is invoked as a self-evident social rule — silence and stillness are badges of refinement [019-019].
- **Vulgarity of being a man**: The Cranford ladies have "established the rule that to be a man was to be 'vulgar'" — a sweeping judgement that encapsulates the town's Amazonian social order and its suspicion of the male sex [002-071].
- **Vulgarity of eating**: When a good-sized piece of food is offered, it is dismissed as "vulgar" — the ladies' refusal to acknowledge appetite is central to their performance of gentility [017-044].
- **Vulgarity of literature**: Captain Brown's preference for Dickens over Johnson is condemned as "vulgar, and below the dignity of literature" [002-165]. Literature itself is subject to class judgement.
- **Vulgarity by association**: Mrs Jamieson "tabooed" certain people as "vulgar" — the power to declare others vulgar is the power to define the social circle [025-018].
- **Vulgarity of ostentation**: Even among the ladies themselves, there is a refined awareness that they "would not, if they could, be vulgarly ostentatious" — the self-conscious performance of modesty is itself a gentility signal [029-121].
- **Vulgarity of practicality**: When Mr Hoggins simply crosses his legs, the ladies condemn him for "vulgarity" — the charge is applied to the smallest physical behaviours [033-061].
- **Vulgarity of truth**: The narrator admits that "we blinded ourselves to the vulgar fact that we were, all of us, people of very moderate means" — the most threatening vulgarity of all is economic reality [001-183].

## Key Characters

- **Miss Jenkyns** — the novel's supreme arbiter of vulgarity. Her Johnsonian pronouncements establish the intellectual dimension of the vulgar/refined distinction, and her dismissal of Captain Brown's reading tastes marks the novel's first major social conflict.
- **Mrs Jamieson** — as the Honourable Mrs Jamieson, she possesses the social authority to declare people and practices vulgar by fiat. Her "taboo" on certain persons enforces the boundary between gentility and vulgarity with aristocratic finality.
- **Mr Hoggins** — the village surgeon whose very physicality — crossing his legs, existing as a man in Cranford space — is deemed vulgar by the ladies, despite his professional competence and genuine kindness.

## Narrative Function

The concept of vulgarity serves three interconnected narrative functions:

1. **Social map**: The things Cranford calls "vulgar" draw an exact map of what the ladies fear most — economic exposure, bodily appetite, male intrusion, and social mobility from below.
2. **Comic irony**: The reader consistently perceives what the ladies do not — that charging everything with "vulgarity" is itself slightly absurd, and that the ladies' genteel performances sit on a foundation of economic precarity.
3. **Thematic argument**: Gaskell uses the ladies' obsession with vulgarity to critique a class system that equates refinement with virtue and that leaves gentlewomen starving rather than violating the code. The novel's quiet resolution — Miss Matty opening a tea shop — is the ultimate refutation of Cranford's vulgarity doctrine.

## Victorian Context

Victorian discourse on vulgarity was extensive and class-bound. Conduct manuals like those of Sarah Stickney Ellis and Isabella Beeton codified the behaviours that separated the "vulgar" from the "genteel." The anxiety was particularly acute for unmarried women of limited means, who had nothing but their gentility to distinguish them from the working classes. Gaskell, writing from the perspective of a minister's wife in industrial Manchester, understood intimately how fragile the boundary between gentility and vulgarity could be — and how much energy middle-class women expended maintaining it.
