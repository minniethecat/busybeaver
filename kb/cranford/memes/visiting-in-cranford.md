# Visiting in Cranford

Visiting is the central mechanism of social life in Cranford, governed by an elaborate code of rules, hours, and obligations. The town's ladies maintain a system of morning calls, formal visits, and the careful observation of social rank that structures their daily existence.

## Rules and Regulations

Cranford has "rules and regulations for visiting and calls" that are "announced to any young people who might be staying in the town, with all the solemnity with which the old Manx laws were read once a year on the Tinwald Mount" ([007-002]). The fundamental rule is clear: "from twelve to three are our calling hours" ([007-003]).

A three-day protocol governs first calls: a new resident may expect visitors after three days have passed — "It is the third day; I dare say your mamma has told you, my dear, never to let more than three days elapse between receiving a call and returning it" ([007-005]). The entire system reinforces community cohesion through mutual obligation.

## The Morning Call

Morning calls — which in Cranford occur between twelve and three — are brief, formal visits with elaborate preparation. When a knock is heard at the door, the ladies "flew to their rooms, to change cap and collars" ([025-013]). Miss Matty, on one occasion, returns in such "flurry" from changing that she appears with "one cap on the top of the other" — and is "quite unconscious of it herself" ([014-003]).

A caller's knock is distinctive — "three distinct taps" ([025-013]) — and the arrival of any visitor becomes immediate community knowledge. Miss Pole "usually came to call on Miss Matilda after any event — or indeed in sight of any event — to talk it over with her" ([015-001]).

## Visiting and Social Rank

Visiting patterns encode Cranford's social hierarchy. When Mrs Jamieson temporarily excludes certain ladies from calling, the community is thrown into crisis. The ladies debate whether they should attend her party: Miss Pole argues pragmatically that refusing would mean "letting down of ourselves," while Miss Matty, on principle, simply "could not have brought myself to say the things Mrs Jamieson did about our not calling" ([017-015]).

A caller's social status confers prestige on the visited. Miss Matty treasured the memory that "the rector's daughter" had spoken to her "in that pretty way" — for she "visited at Arley Hall" ([033-012]).

## Distinguished Visitors

Visitors from outside Cranford's circle generate intense interest. When Lord Mauleverer stays at the Angel Inn, "such a piece of news as Lord Mauleverer's visit was not to be lost on the Cranford letter-writers: they made the most of it" — Miss Matty humbly apologizing for writing alongside her more capable sister, yet her account containing the richest detail of the "commotion occasioned by his lordship's visit" ([008-002]).

Lady Glenmire's extended visit to Mrs Jamieson similarly transforms Cranford's social landscape: "a series of small festivals were always held in Cranford on the arrival of a visitor at any of our friends' houses" ([016-004]).

## The Gentleman-Visitor Problem

A gentleman visitor presents a particular challenge. Miss Matty, faced with hosting a male caller after her sister Deborah's death, asks helplessly: "If Deborah had been alive she would have known what to do with a gentleman-visitor. Must I put razors in his dressing-room? Dear! dear! and I've got none" ([007-016]). The visit, despite its awkwardness, "greatly excited Cranford, and even stirred up" the apothecary to new heights of social involvement ([007-017]).

## Visiting and Exclusion

The power to exclude from visiting is Cranford's ultimate social sanction. Mrs Jamieson excludes the Cranford ladies from calling when her sister-in-law Lady Glenmire arrives, provoking weeks of outrage. Miss Pole's cap — "a very smart cap, which she was anxious to show to an admiring world" — ultimately triumphs over principle, and the ladies attend the party under "the great Christian principle of 'Forgive and forget'" ([017-021]).

The most severe case of visiting exclusion involves Lady Glenmire's marriage to Mr Hoggins the surgeon: "Would they be visited? Would Mrs Jamieson let us? Or must we choose between the Honourable Mrs Jamieson and the degraded Lady Glenmire?" ([025-026]). The rector's daughter settles the matter by ruling that "an unmarried woman retains the station her father occupied" — so "Cranford was allowed to visit Miss Matty; and, whether allowed or not, it intended to visit Lady Glenmire" ([033-001]).
