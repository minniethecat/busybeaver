# Section 26

[026-001] I have wandered a long way from the two [[letter-writing|letters]] that awaited us on the

[026-002] breakfast-table that Tuesday morning. Mine was from my father. Miss

[026-003] [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Matty]]’s was printed. My father’s was just a man’s letter; I mean it was

[026-004] very dull, and gave no information beyond that he was well, that they

[026-005] had had a good deal of rain, that trade was very stagnant, and there

[026-006] were many disagreeable rumours afloat. He then asked me if I knew

[026-007] whether Miss Matty still retained her shares in the Town and County

[026-008] Bank, as there were very unpleasant reports about it; though nothing

[026-009] more than he had always foreseen, and had prophesied to [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]]

[026-010] years ago, when she would invest their little property in it—the only

[026-011] unwise step that clever woman had ever taken, to his knowledge (the only

[026-012] time she ever acted against his advice, I knew). However, if anything

[026-013] had gone wrong, of course I was not to think of leaving Miss Matty while

[026-014] I could be of any use, etc.

[026-015] “Who is your letter from, my dear? Mine is a very civil invitation,

[026-016] signed ‘Edwin Wilson,’ asking me to attend an important meeting of the

[026-017] shareholders of the [[town-and-county-bank|Town and County Bank]], to be held in Drumble, on

[026-018] Thursday the twenty-first. I am sure, it is very attentive of them to

[026-019] remember me.”

[026-020] I did not like to hear of this “important meeting,” for, though I did

[026-021] not know much about business, I feared it confirmed what my father said:

[026-022] however, I thought, ill news always came fast enough, so I resolved to

[026-023] say nothing about my alarm, and merely told her that my father was well,

[026-024] and sent his kind regards to her. She kept turning over and admiring

[026-025] her letter. At last she spoke—

[026-026] “I remember their sending one to Deborah just like this; but that I did

[026-027] not wonder at, for everybody knew she was so clear-headed. I am afraid

[026-028] I could not help them much; indeed, if they came to accounts, I should

[026-029] be quite in the way, for I never could do sums in my head. Deborah, I

[026-030] know, rather wished to go, and went so far as to order a new bonnet for

[026-031] the occasion: but when the time came she had a bad cold; so they sent

[026-032] her a very polite account of what they had done. Chosen a director, I

[026-033] think it was. Do you think they want me to help them to choose a

[026-034] director? I am sure I should choose your father at once!”

[026-035] “My father has no shares in the bank,” said I.

[026-036] “Oh, no! I remember. He objected very much to Deborah’s buying any, I

[026-037] believe. But she was quite the woman of business, and always judged for

[026-038] herself; and here, you see, they have paid eight per cent. all these

[026-039] years.”

[026-040] It was a very uncomfortable subject to me, with my half-knowledge; so I

[026-041] thought I would change the conversation, and I asked at what time she

[026-042] thought we had better go and see the fashions. “Well, my dear,” she

[026-043] said, “the thing is this: it is not etiquette to go till after twelve;

[026-044] but then, you see, all [[cranford-town|Cranford]] will be there, and one does not like to

[026-045] be too curious about dress and trimmings and caps with all the world

[026-046] looking on. It is never genteel to be over-curious on these occasions.

[026-047] Deborah had the knack of always looking as if the latest fashion was

[026-048] nothing new to her; a manner she had caught from Lady Arley, who did

[026-049] see all the new modes in London, you know. So I thought we would just

[026-050] slip down this morning, soon after breakfast—for I do want half-a-pound

[026-051] of [[tea-drinking|tea]]—and then we could go up and examine the things at our leisure,

[026-052] and see exactly how my new silk gown must be made; and then, after

[026-053] twelve, we could go with our minds disengaged, and free from thoughts

[026-054] of dress.”

[026-055] We began to talk of Miss Matty’s new silk gown. I discovered that it

[026-056] would be really the first time in her life that she had had to choose

[026-057] anything of consequence for herself: for Miss Jenkyns had always been

[026-058] the more decided character, whatever her taste might have been; and it

[026-059] is astonishing how such people carry the world before them by the mere

[026-060] force of will. Miss Matty anticipated the sight of the glossy folds

[026-061] with as much delight as if the five sovereigns, set apart for the

[026-062] purchase, could buy all the silks in the shop; and (remembering my own

[026-063] loss of two hours in a toyshop before I could tell on what wonder to

[026-064] spend a silver threepence) I was very glad that we were going early,

[026-065] that dear Miss Matty might have leisure for the delights of perplexity.

[026-066] If a happy sea-green could be met with, the gown was to be sea-green: if

[026-067] not, she inclined to maize, and I to silver gray; and we discussed the

[026-068] requisite number of breadths until we arrived at the shop-door. We were

[026-069] to buy the tea, select the silk, and then clamber up the iron corkscrew

[026-070] stairs that led into what was once a loft, though now a fashion

[026-071] show-room.

[026-072] The young men at Mr Johnson’s had on their best looks; and their best

[026-073] cravats, and pivoted themselves over the counter with surprising

[026-074] activity. They wanted to show us upstairs at once; but on the principle

[026-075] of business first and pleasure afterwards, we stayed to purchase the

[026-076] tea. Here Miss Matty’s absence of mind betrayed itself. If she was

[026-077] made aware that she had been drinking green tea at any time, she always

[026-078] thought it her duty to lie awake half through the night afterward (I

[026-079] have known her take it in ignorance many a time without such effects),

[026-080] and consequently green tea was prohibited the house; yet to-day she

[026-081] herself asked for the obnoxious article, under the impression that she

[026-082] was talking about the silk. However, the mistake was soon rectified;

[026-083] and then the silks were unrolled in good truth. By this time the shop

[026-084] was pretty well filled, for it was Cranford market-day, and many of the

[026-085] farmers and country people from the neighbourhood round came in,

[026-086] sleeking down their hair, and glancing shyly about, from under their

[026-087] eyelids, as anxious to take back some notion of the unusual gaiety to

[026-088] the mistress or the lasses at home, and yet feeling that they were out

[026-089] of place among the smart shopmen and gay shawls and summer prints. One

[026-090] honest-looking man, however, made his way up to the counter at which we

[026-091] stood, and boldly asked to look at a shawl or two. The other country

[026-092] folk confined themselves to the grocery side; but our neighbour was

[026-093] evidently too full of some kind intention towards mistress, wife or

[026-094] daughter, to be shy; and it soon became a question with me, whether he

[026-095] or Miss Matty would keep their shopmen the longest time. He thought

[026-096] each shawl more beautiful than the last; and, as for Miss Matty, she

[026-097] smiled and sighed over each fresh bale that was brought out; one colour

[026-098] set off another, and the heap together would, as she said, make even the

[026-099] rainbow look poor.

[026-100] “I am afraid,” said she, hesitating, “whichever I choose I shall wish I

[026-101] had taken another. Look at this lovely crimson! it would be so warm in

[026-102] winter. But spring is coming on, you know. I wish I could have a gown

[026-103] for every season,” said she, dropping her voice—as we all did in

[026-104] Cranford whenever we talked of anything we wished for but could not

[026-105] afford. “However,” she continued in a louder and more cheerful tone, “it

[026-106] would give me a great deal of trouble to take care of them if I had

[026-107] them; so, I think, I’ll only take one. But which must it be, my dear?”

[026-108] And now she hovered over a lilac with yellow spots, while I pulled out a

[026-109] quiet sage-green that had faded into insignificance under the more

[026-110] brilliant colours, but which was nevertheless a good silk in its humble

[026-111] way. Our attention was called off to our neighbour. He had chosen a

[026-112] shawl of about thirty shillings’ value; and his face looked broadly

[026-113] happy, under the anticipation, no doubt, of the pleasant surprise he

[026-114] would give to some Molly or Jenny at home; he had tugged a leathern

[026-115] purse out of his breeches-pocket, and had offered a five-pound note in

[026-116] payment for the shawl, and for some parcels which had been brought round

[026-117] to him from the grocery counter; and it was just at this point that he

[026-118] attracted our notice. The shopman was examining the note with a

[026-119] puzzled, doubtful air.

[026-120] “Town and County Bank! I am not sure, sir, but I believe we have

[026-121] received a warning against notes issued by this bank only this morning.

[026-122] I will just step and ask Mr Johnson, sir; but I’m afraid I must trouble

[026-123] you for payment in cash, or in a note of a different bank.”

[026-124] I never saw a man’s countenance fall so suddenly into dismay and

[026-125] bewilderment. It was almost piteous to see the rapid change.

[026-126] “Dang it!” said he, striking his fist down on the table, as if to try

[026-127] which was the harder, “the chap talks as if notes and gold were to be

[026-128] had for the picking up.”

[026-129] Miss Matty had forgotten her silk gown in her interest for the man. I

[026-130] don’t think she had caught the name of the bank, and in my nervous

[026-131] cowardice I was anxious that she should not; and so I began admiring the

[026-132] yellow-spotted lilac gown that I had been utterly condemning only a

[026-133] minute before. But it was of no use.

[026-134] “What bank was it? I mean, what bank did your note belong to?”

[026-135] “Town and County Bank.”

[026-136] “Let me see it,” said she quietly to the shopman, gently taking it out

[026-137] of his hand, as he brought it back to return it to the farmer.

[026-138] Mr Johnson was very sorry, but, from information he had received, the

[026-139] notes issued by that bank were little better than waste paper.

[026-140] “I don’t understand it,” said Miss Matty to me in a low voice. “That is

[026-141] our bank, is it not?—the Town and County Bank?”

[026-142] “Yes,” said I. “This lilac silk will just match the ribbons in your new

[026-143] cap, I believe,” I continued, holding up the folds so as to catch the

[026-144] light, and wishing that the man would make haste and be gone, and yet

[026-145] having a new wonder, that had only just sprung up, how far it was wise

[026-146] or right in me to allow Miss Matty to make this expensive purchase, if

[026-147] the affairs of the bank were really so bad as the refusal of the note

[026-148] implied.

[026-149] But Miss Matty put on the soft dignified manner, peculiar to her, rarely

[026-150] used, and yet which became her so well, and laying her hand gently on

[026-151] mine, she said—

[026-152] “Never mind the silks for a few minutes, dear. I don’t understand you,

[026-153] sir,” turning now to the shopman, who had been attending to the farmer.

[026-154] “Is this a forged note?”

[026-155] “Oh, no, ma’am. It is a true note of its kind; but you see, ma’am, it

[026-156] is a joint-stock bank, and there are reports out that it is likely to

[026-157] break. Mr Johnson is only doing his duty, ma’am, as I am sure Mr Dobson

[026-158] knows.”

[026-159] But Mr Dobson could not respond to the appealing bow by any answering

[026-160] smile. He was turning the note absently over in his fingers, looking

[026-161] gloomily enough at the parcel containing the lately-chosen shawl.

[026-162] “It’s hard upon a poor man,” said he, “as earns every farthing with the

[026-163] sweat of his brow. However, there’s no help for it. You must take back

[026-164] your shawl, my man; Lizzle must go on with her cloak for a while. And

[026-165] yon figs for the little ones—I promised them to ’em—I’ll take them; but

[026-166] the ’bacco, and the other things”—
