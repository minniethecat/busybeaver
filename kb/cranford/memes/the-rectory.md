# The Rectory

The rectory is the childhood home of the Jenkyns family — Deborah, Matty, and Peter — and the most emotionally freighted domestic space in Cranford's imaginative geography. An ancient, rambling building, it is the site of the novel's central family tragedy and the physical anchor for Miss Matty's deepest memories.

## Description

"The rectory was a very old house — steps up into a room, steps down into a room, all through" [012-038, 012-039]. Its architectural irregularity, with its unexpected steps connecting rooms at different levels, mirrors the unpredictability of the family life it contains — a space where a boy might vanish between one room and the next. The establishment was substantial enough to keep "three maids and a man" [013-072], and Miss Jenkyns (Deborah) attempted to remodel its quiet order on the grander plans of Arley Hall, "where they kept thirty servants" [009-032, 009-033, 009-034].

## Key Events

The rectory is the setting for the novel's most traumatic domestic episode: the disappearance of Peter Jenkyns. After his father discovers Peter's parodic impersonation of him — dressed in his sister's clothes and carrying a pillow for a baby — Peter vanishes. The family searches the house in growing desperation: "Even my father, who had not liked to join in the search at first, helped us before long" [012-037, 012-038]. They call through the old house, but "no Peter was there" [012-033]. He is never found at the rectory again, and the house becomes a place of loss.

The rectory also witnessed the quiet rhythms of family life: Miss Matty's long childhood illness, nursed by Deborah [009-034, 009-035]; the annual making of cowslip wine, a tradition that ended forever with Peter's disappearance — "There was no making of cowslip wine that year at the rectory — nor, indeed, ever after" [012-028, 012-029]; and Mr Holbrook's occasional visits, when he taught Peter to fish [012-078, 012-079, 012-080].

## Inhabitants

The rectory was home to the Reverend Jenkyns (the rector), his wife, and their three children: [[miss-jenkyns-deborah]], [[miss-matty-jenkyns]], and Peter Jenkyns. [[mr-holbrook]] was "an occasional visitor" [012-078], connecting the rectory to the broader Cranford social world.

## Loss and Aftermath

After the rector's death, "our circumstances were changed; and, instead of living at the rectory, and keeping three maids and a man, we had to come to this small house, and be content with a servant-of-all-work" [013-071, 013-072, 013-073]. The fall from the rectory to the small Cranford house marks the decisive shift in the Jenkyns family's fortunes — the architectural embodiment of their descent into [[genteel-poverty]]. Years later, when Miss Matty must sell the family furniture, her father's initial objection — "the old rectory furniture, however carefully used and reverently treated, would fetch but little" [030-140, 030-141] — speaks to how deeply the rectory's material culture is bound up with the family's identity. Yet the furniture's return to "the rectory and be arranged on the accustomed walls once more" [031-094] provides a small, elegiac restoration.
