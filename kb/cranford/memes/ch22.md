# Section 22

[022-001] We preserved a discreet silence as to the headless lady while we were

[022-002] putting on our things to go home, for there was no knowing how near the

[022-003] ghostly head and ears might be, or what spiritual connection they might

[022-004] be keeping up with the unhappy body in [[darkness-lane|Darkness Lane]]; and, therefore,

[022-005] even [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] felt that it was as well not to speak lightly on such

[022-006] subjects, for fear of vexing or insulting that woebegone trunk. At

[022-007] least, so I conjecture; for, instead of the busy clatter usual in the

[022-008] operation, we tied on our cloaks as sadly as mutes at a funeral. Miss

[022-009] [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Matty]] drew the curtains round the windows of the chair to shut out

[022-010] disagreeable sights, and the men (either because they were in spirits

[022-011] that their labours were so nearly ended, or because they were going down

[022-012] hill), set off at such a round and merry pace, that it was all Miss Pole

[022-013] and I could do to keep up with them. She had breath for nothing beyond

[022-014] an imploring “Don’t leave me!” uttered as she clutched my arm so tightly

[022-015] that I could not have quitted her, ghost or no ghost. What a relief it

[022-016] was when the men, weary of their burden and their quick trot, stopped

[022-017] just where Headingley Causeway branches off from Darkness Lane! Miss

[022-018] Pole unloosed me and caught at one of the men—

[022-019] “Could not you—could not you take Miss Matty round by Headingley

[022-020] Causeway?—the pavement in Darkness Lane jolts so, and she is not very

[022-021] strong.”

[022-022] A smothered voice was heard from the inside of the chair—

[022-023] “Oh! pray go on! What is the matter? What is the matter? I will give

[022-024] you sixpence more to go on very fast; pray don’t stop here.”

[022-025] “And I’ll give you a shilling,” said Miss Pole, with tremulous dignity,

[022-026] “if you’ll go by Headingley Causeway.”

[022-027] The two men grunted acquiescence and took up the chair, and went along

[022-028] the causeway, which certainly answered Miss Pole’s kind purpose of

[022-029] saving Miss Matty’s bones; for it was covered with soft, thick mud, and

[022-030] even a fall there would have been easy till the getting-up came, when

[022-031] there might have been some difficulty in extrication.

[022-032] CHAPTER XI.

[022-033] SAMUEL BROWN

[022-034] THE next morning I met [[lady-glenmire|Lady Glenmire]] and Miss Pole setting out on a long

[022-035] walk to find some old woman who was famous in the neighbourhood for her

[022-036] skill in knitting woollen stockings. Miss Pole said to me, with a smile

[022-037] half-kindly and half-contemptuous upon her countenance, “I have been

[022-038] just telling Lady Glenmire of our poor friend [[mrs-forrester|Mrs Forrester]], and her

[022-039] terror of ghosts. It comes from living so much alone, and listening to

[022-040] the bug-a-boo stories of that Jenny of hers.” She was so calm and so

[022-041] much above superstitious fears herself that I was almost ashamed to say

[022-042] how glad I had been of her Headingley Causeway proposition the night

[022-043] before, and turned off the conversation to something else.

[022-044] In the afternoon Miss Pole called on Miss Matty to tell her of the

[022-045] adventure—the real adventure they had met with on their morning’s walk.

[022-046] They had been perplexed about the exact path which they were to take

[022-047] across the fields in order to find the knitting old woman, and had

[022-048] stopped to inquire at a little wayside public-house, standing on the

[022-049] high road to London, about three miles from [[cranford-town|Cranford]]. The good woman

[022-050] had asked them to sit down and rest themselves while she fetched her

[022-051] husband, who could direct them better than she could; and, while they

[022-052] were sitting in the sanded parlour, a little girl came in. They thought

[022-053] that she belonged to the landlady, and began some trifling conversation

[022-054] with her; but, on Mrs Roberts’s return, she told them that the little

[022-055] thing was the only child of a couple who were staying in the house. And

[022-056] then she began a long story, out of which Lady Glenmire and Miss Pole

[022-057] could only gather one or two decided facts, which were that, about six

[022-058] weeks ago, a light spring-cart had broken down just before their door,

[022-059] in which there were two men, one woman, and this child. One of the men

[022-060] was seriously hurt—no bones broken, only “shaken,” the landlady called

[022-061] it; but he had probably sustained some severe internal injury, for he

[022-062] had languished in their house ever since, attended by his wife, the

[022-063] mother of this little girl. Miss Pole had asked what he was, what he

[022-064] looked like. And Mrs Roberts had made answer that he was not like a

[022-065] gentleman, nor yet like a common person; if it had not been that he and

[022-066] his wife were such decent, quiet people, she could almost have thought

[022-067] he was a mountebank, or something of that kind, for they had a great box

[022-068] in the cart, full of she did not know what. She had helped to unpack

[022-069] it, and take out their linen and clothes, when the other man—his

[022-070] twin-brother, she believed he was—had gone off with the horse and cart.

[022-071] Miss Pole had begun to have her suspicions at this point, and expressed

[022-072] her idea that it was rather strange that the box and cart and horse and

[022-073] all should have disappeared; but good Mrs Roberts seemed to have become

[022-074] quite indignant at Miss Pole’s implied suggestion; in fact, Miss Pole

[022-075] said she was as angry as if Miss Pole had told her that she herself was

[022-076] a swindler. As the best way of convincing the ladies, she bethought her

[022-077] of begging them to see the wife; and, as Miss Pole said, there was no

[022-078] doubting the honest, worn, bronzed face of the woman, who at the first

[022-079] tender word from Lady Glenmire, burst into tears, which she was too weak

[022-080] to check until some word from the landlady made her swallow down her

[022-081] sobs, in order that she might testify to the Christian kindness shown by

[022-082] Mr and Mrs Roberts. Miss Pole came round with a swing to as vehement a

[022-083] belief in the sorrowful tale as she had been sceptical before; and, as

[022-084] a proof of this, her energy in the poor sufferer’s behalf was nothing

[022-085] daunted when she found out that he, and no other, was our Signor

[022-086] Brunoni, to whom all Cranford had been attributing all manner of evil

[022-087] this six weeks past! Yes! his wife said his proper name was Samuel

[022-088] Brown—“Sam,” she called him—but to the last we preferred calling him

[022-089] “the Signor”; it sounded so much better.

[022-090] The end of their conversation with the Signora Brunoni was that it was

[022-091] agreed that he should be placed under medical advice, and for any

[022-092] expense incurred in procuring this Lady Glenmire promised to hold

[022-093] herself responsible, and had accordingly gone to [[mr-hoggins|Mr Hoggins]] to beg him

[022-094] to ride over to the “Rising Sun” that very afternoon, and examine into

[022-095] the signor’s real state; and, as Miss Pole said, if it was desirable to

[022-096] remove him to Cranford to be more immediately under Mr Hoggins’s eye,

[022-097] she would undertake to see for lodgings and arrange about the rent. Mrs

[022-098] Roberts had been as kind as could be all throughout, but it was evident

[022-099] that their long residence there had been a slight inconvenience.

[022-100] Before Miss Pole left us, Miss Matty and I were as full of the morning’s

[022-101] adventure as she was. We talked about it all the evening, turning it in

[022-102] every possible light, and we went to bed anxious for the morning, when

[022-103] we should surely hear from someone what Mr Hoggins thought and

[022-104] recommended; for, as Miss Matty observed, though Mr Hoggins did say

[022-105] “Jack’s up,” “a fig for his heels,” and called Preference “Pref.” she

[022-106] believed he was a very worthy man and a very clever surgeon. Indeed, we

[022-107] were rather proud of our doctor at Cranford, as a doctor. We often

[022-108] wished, when we heard of Queen Adelaide or the Duke of Wellington being

[022-109] ill, that they would send for Mr Hoggins; but, on consideration, we were

[022-110] rather glad they did not, for, if we were ailing, what should we do if

[022-111] Mr Hoggins had been appointed physician-in-ordinary to the Royal Family?

[022-112] As a surgeon we were proud of him; but as a man—or rather, I should

[022-113] say, as a gentleman—we could only shake our heads over his name and

[022-114] himself, and wished that he had read Lord Chesterfield’s Letters in the

[022-115] days when his manners were susceptible of improvement. Nevertheless, we

[022-116] all regarded his dictum in the signor’s case as infallible, and when he

[022-117] said that with care and attention he might rally, we had no more fear

[022-118] for him.

[022-119] But, although we had no more fear, everybody did as much as if there was

[022-120] great cause for anxiety—as indeed there was until Mr Hoggins took charge

[022-121] of him. Miss Pole looked out clean and comfortable, if homely,

[022-122] lodgings; Miss Matty sent the sedan-chair for him, and [[martha|Martha]] and I

[022-123] aired it well before it left Cranford by holding a warming-pan full of

[022-124] red-hot coals in it, and then shutting it up close, smoke and all, until

[022-125] the time when he should get into it at the “Rising Sun.” Lady Glenmire

[022-126] undertook the medical department under Mr Hoggins’s directions, and

[022-127] rummaged up all [[mrs-jamieson|Mrs Jamieson]]’s medicine glasses, and spoons, and

[022-128] bed-tables, in a free-and-easy way, that made Miss Matty feel a little

[022-129] anxious as to what that lady and Mr Mulliner might say, if they knew.

[022-130] Mrs Forrester made some of the bread-jelly, for which she was so famous,

[022-131] to have ready as a refreshment in the lodgings when he should arrive. A

[022-132] present of this bread-jelly was the highest mark of favour dear Mrs

[022-133] Forrester could confer. Miss Pole had once asked her for the receipt,

[022-134] but she had met with a very decided rebuff; that lady told her that she

[022-135] could not part with it to any one during her life, and that after her

[022-136] death it was bequeathed, as her executors would find, to Miss Matty.

[022-137] What Miss Matty, or, as Mrs Forrester called her (remembering the clause

[022-138] in her will and the dignity of the occasion), Miss Matilda Jenkyns—might

[022-139] choose to do with the receipt when it came into her possession—whether

[022-140] to make it public, or to hand it down as an heirloom—she did not know,

[022-141] nor would she dictate. And a mould of this admirable, digestible,

[022-142] unique bread-jelly was sent by Mrs Forrester to our poor sick conjuror.

[022-143] Who says that the [[aristocracy-in-cranford|aristocracy]] are proud? Here was a lady by birth a

[022-144] Tyrrell, and descended from the great Sir Walter that shot King Rufus,

[022-145] and in whose veins ran the blood of him who murdered the little princes

[022-146] in the Tower, going every day to see what dainty dishes she could

[022-147] prepare for Samuel Brown, a mountebank! But, indeed, it was wonderful

[022-148] to see what kind feelings were called out by this poor man’s coming

[022-149] amongst us. And also wonderful to see how the great Cranford panic,

[022-150] which had been occasioned by his first coming in his Turkish dress,

[022-151] melted away into thin air on his second coming—pale and feeble, and with

[022-152] his heavy, filmy eyes, that only brightened a very little when they fell

[022-153] upon the countenance of his faithful wife, or their pale and sorrowful

[022-154] little girl.

[022-155] Somehow we all forgot to be afraid. I daresay it was that finding out

[022-156] that he, who had first excited our love of the marvellous by his

[022-157] unprecedented arts, had not sufficient every-day gifts to manage a

[022-158] shying horse, made us feel as if we were ourselves again. Miss Pole

[022-159] came with her little basket at all hours of the evening, as if her

[022-160] lonely house and the unfrequented road to it had never been infested by

[022-161] that “murderous gang”; Mrs Forrester said she thought that neither Jenny

[022-162] nor she need mind the headless lady who wept and wailed in Darkness

[022-163] Lane, for surely the power was never given to such beings to harm those

[022-164] who went about to try to do what little good was in their power, to

[022-165] which Jenny tremblingly assented; but the mistress’s theory had little

[022-166] effect on the maid’s practice until she had sewn two pieces of red

[022-167] flannel in the shape of a cross on her inner garment.
