# Letters and Correspondence in Cranford

Letters occupy a central place in Cranford society — as historical records, as plot devices, and as expressions of family identity. The novel's "Old Letters" chapter builds an entire narrative around the reading and ritual burning of family correspondence, while Peter Jenkyns's letters from India form a crucial offstage presence.

## The Old Letters Ritual

After Miss Jenkyns's death, Miss Matty undertakes the task of "looking over all the old family letters, and destroying such as ought not to be allowed to fall into the hands of strangers" ([010-001]). Each letter carries a docket in the deceased sister's precise handwriting, transforming private correspondence into an archival family history.

The earliest bundle — "Letters interchanged between my ever-honoured father and my dearly-beloved mother, prior to their marriage, in July 1774" ([010-002]) — reveals the rector at twenty-seven writing "eager, passionate ardour; short homely sentences, right fresh from the heart" — a "curious contrast" to the "grand Latinised, Johnsonian style" of his published sermon ([010-011]).

His young bride's letters, by contrast, are "principally occupied in asking her lover to use his influence with her parents" for a white "Paduasoy" dress ([010-020]). The Paduasoy becomes a running motif through the correspondence, eventually being "made into a christening cloak for the baby" and worn on visits to Arley Hall ([010-061]).

## Deborah Jenkyns's Epistolary Art

Miss Jenkyns's own letters occupy a special place in the family archive. Miss Matty believes "Deborah's letters were so very superior! Any one might profit by reading them" — comparing them favorably to the published letters of Mrs Chapone and Mrs Carter ([010-136]). Deborah writes on "the square sheet which we have learned to call old-fashioned," her hand "admirably calculated, together with her use of many-syllabled words, to fill up a sheet" ([010-168]).

Her signature technique is "crossing" — writing perpendicular lines across a filled page to squeeze in more content — which "poor Miss Matty got sadly puzzled with, for the words gathered size like snowballs, and towards the end of her letter Miss Jenkyns used to become quite sesquipedalian" ([010-171]). In one letter to her father, "slightly theological and controversial in its tone," Deborah references "Herod, Tetrarch of Idumea" — which Miss Matty reads as "Herod Petrarch of Etruria, and was just as well pleased as if she had been right" ([010-174]).

## The Burning of Letters

The ritual destruction of letters becomes a meditation on mortality and memory. "One by one she dropped them into the middle of the fire, watching each blaze up, die out, and rise away, in faint, white, ghostly semblance, up the chimney" ([010-035]). The narrator reflects: "I, like her, was fascinated into watching the destruction of those letters, into which the honest warmth of a manly heart had been poured forth" ([010-038]).

Miss Matty confesses she "did grudge burning these letters" — especially Deborah's, which "lasted us two nights" of reading aloud "with a proper emphasis, and without stumbling over the big words" ([010-143]).

## Letters as Plot Devices

Beyond the Old Letters chapter, correspondence drives the novel's action. Major Jenkyns's letter from India — after "twenty or thirty years" abroad, returning "with an invalid wife who had never been introduced to her English relations" — throws Miss Matty into a panic about hosting a gentleman visitor ([007-016]).

Lord Mauleverer's visit to Cranford becomes "such a piece of news" that "was not to be lost on the Cranford letter-writers: they made the most of it" — Miss Pole and Miss Matty writing to the narrator "by the same post" with competing accounts ([008-002]).

## Material Culture of Letters

The physical form of letters reveals social history. The oldest correspondence is on "very yellow" paper with "very brown" ink, some sheets bearing "the old original post, with the stamp in the corner representing a post-boy riding for life and twanging his horn" ([010-156]). Letters are "fastened with a great round red wafer; for it was before Miss Edgeworth's 'patronage' had banished wafers from polite society" ([010-159]). Franks "were in great request, and were even used as a means of paying debts by needy members of Parliament" ([010-161]).
