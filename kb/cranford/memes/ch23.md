# Section 23

[023-001] I found [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]] covering her penny ball—the ball that she used to

[023-002] roll under her bed—with gay-coloured worsted in rainbow stripes.

[023-003] “My dear,” said she, “my heart is sad for that little careworn child.

[023-004] Although her father is a conjuror, she looks as if she had never had a

[023-005] good game of play in her life. I used to make very pretty balls in this

[023-006] way when I was a girl, and I thought I would try if I could not make

[023-007] this one smart and take it to Phœbe this afternoon. I think ‘the gang’

[023-008] must have left the neighbourhood, for one does not hear any more of

[023-009] their violence and robbery now.”

[023-010] We were all of us far too full of the signor’s precarious state to talk

[023-011] either about robbers or ghosts. Indeed, [[lady-glenmire|Lady Glenmire]] said she never

[023-012] had heard of any actual [[the-great-cranford-panic|robberies]], except that two little boys had

[023-013] stolen some apples from Farmer Benson’s orchard, and that some eggs had

[023-014] been missed on a market-day off Widow Hayward’s stall. But that was

[023-015] expecting too much of us; we could not acknowledge that we had only had

[023-016] this small foundation for all our panic. [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] drew herself up at

[023-017] this remark of Lady Glenmire’s, and said “that she wished she could

[023-018] agree with her as to the very small reason we had had for alarm, but

[023-019] with the recollection of a man disguised as a woman who had endeavoured

[023-020] to force himself into her house while his confederates waited outside;

[023-021] with the knowledge gained from Lady Glenmire herself, of the footprints

[023-022] seen on [[mrs-jamieson|Mrs Jamieson]]’s flower borders; with the fact before her of the

[023-023] audacious robbery committed on [[mr-hoggins|Mr Hoggins]] at his own door”—But here Lady

[023-024] Glenmire broke in with a very strong expression of doubt as to whether

[023-025] this last story was not an entire fabrication founded upon the theft of

[023-026] a cat; she grew so red while she was saying all this that I was not

[023-027] surprised at Miss Pole’s manner of bridling up, and I am certain, if

[023-028] Lady Glenmire had not been “her ladyship,” we should have had a more

[023-029] emphatic contradiction than the “Well, to be sure!” and similar

[023-030] fragmentary ejaculations, which were all that she ventured upon in my

[023-031] lady’s presence. But when she was gone Miss Pole began a long

[023-032] congratulation to Miss Matty that so far they had escaped marriage,

[023-033] which she noticed always made people credulous to the last degree;

[023-034] indeed, she thought it argued great natural credulity in a woman if she

[023-035] could not keep herself from being married; and in what Lady Glenmire had

[023-036] said about Mr Hoggins’s robbery we had a specimen of what people came to

[023-037] if they gave way to such a weakness; evidently Lady Glenmire would

[023-038] swallow anything if she could believe the poor vamped-up story about a

[023-039] neck of mutton and a pussy with which he had tried to impose on Miss

[023-040] Pole, only she had always been on her guard against believing too much

[023-041] of what men said.

[023-042] We were thankful, as Miss Pole desired us to be, that we had never been

[023-043] married; but I think, of the two, we were even more thankful that the

[023-044] robbers had left [[cranford-town|Cranford]]; at least I judge so from a speech of Miss

[023-045] Matty’s that evening, as we sat over the fire, in which she evidently

[023-046] looked upon a husband as a great protector against thieves, burglars,

[023-047] and ghosts; and said that she did not think that she should dare to be

[023-048] always warning young people against matrimony, as Miss Pole did

[023-049] continually; to be sure, marriage was a risk, as she saw, now she had

[023-050] had some experience; but she remembered the time when she had looked

[023-051] forward to being married as much as any one.

[023-052] “Not to any particular person, my dear,” said she, hastily checking

[023-053] herself up, as if she were afraid of having admitted too much; “only the

[023-054] old story, you know, of ladies always saying, ‘_When_ I marry,’ and

[023-055] gentlemen, ‘_If_ I marry.’” It was a joke spoken in rather a sad tone,

[023-056] and I doubt if either of us smiled; but I could not see Miss Matty’s

[023-057] face by the flickering fire-light. In a little while she continued—

[023-058] “But, after all, I have not told you the truth. It is so long ago, and

[023-059] no one ever knew how much I thought of it at the time, unless, indeed,

[023-060] my dear mother guessed; but I may say that there was a time when I did

[023-061] not think I should have been only Miss Matty Jenkyns all my life; for

[023-062] even if I did meet with any one who wished to marry me now (and, as Miss

[023-063] Pole says, one is never too safe), I could not take him—I hope he would

[023-064] not take it too much to heart, but I could _not_ take him—or any one but

[023-065] the person I once thought I should be married to; and he is dead and

[023-066] gone, and he never knew how it all came about that I said ‘No,’ when I

[023-067] had thought many and many a time—Well, it’s no matter what I thought.

[023-068] God ordains it all, and I am very happy, my dear. No one has such kind

[023-069] friends as I,” continued she, taking my hand and holding it in hers.

[023-070] If I had never known of [[mr-holbrook|Mr Holbrook]], I could have said something in this

[023-071] pause, but as I had, I could not think of anything that would come in

[023-072] naturally, and so we both kept silence for a little time.

[023-073] “My father once made us,” she began, “keep a diary, in two columns; on

[023-074] one side we were to put down in the morning what we thought would be the

[023-075] course and events of the coming day, and at night we were to put down on

[023-076] the other side what really had happened. It would be to some people

[023-077] rather a sad way of telling their lives,” (a tear dropped upon my hand

[023-078] at these words)—“I don’t mean that mine has been sad, only so very

[023-079] different to what I expected. I remember, one winter’s evening, sitting

[023-080] over our bedroom fire with Deborah—I remember it as if it were

[023-081] yesterday—and we were planning our future lives, both of us were

[023-082] planning, though only she talked about it. She said she should like to

[023-083] marry an archdeacon, and write his charges; and you know, my dear, she

[023-084] never was married, and, for aught I know, she never spoke to an

[023-085] unmarried archdeacon in her life. I never was ambitious, nor could I

[023-086] have written charges, but I thought I could manage a house (my mother

[023-087] used to call me her right hand), and I was always so fond of little

[023-088] children—the shyest babies would stretch out their little arms to come

[023-089] to me; when I was a girl, I was half my leisure time nursing in the

[023-090] neighbouring cottages; but I don’t know how it was, when I grew sad and

[023-091] grave—which I did a year or two after this time—the little things drew

[023-092] back from me, and I am afraid I lost the knack, though I am just as fond

[023-093] of children as ever, and have a strange yearning at my heart whenever I

[023-094] see a mother with her baby in her arms. Nay, my dear” (and by a sudden

[023-095] blaze which sprang up from a fall of the unstirred coals, I saw that her

[023-096] eyes were full of tears—gazing intently on some vision of what might

[023-097] have been), “do you know I dream sometimes that I have a little

[023-098] child—always the same—a little girl of about two years old; she never

[023-099] grows older, though I have dreamt about her for many years. I don’t

[023-100] think I ever dream of any words or sound she makes; she is very

[023-101] noiseless and still, but she comes to me when she is very sorry or very

[023-102] glad, and I have wakened with the clasp of her dear little arms round my

[023-103] neck. Only last night—perhaps because I had gone to sleep thinking of

[023-104] this ball for Phœbe—my little darling came in my dream, and put up her

[023-105] mouth to be kissed, just as I have seen real babies do to real mothers

[023-106] before going to bed. But all this is nonsense, dear! only don’t be

[023-107] frightened by Miss Pole from being married. I can fancy it may be a

[023-108] very happy state, and a little credulity helps one on through life very

[023-109] smoothly—better than always doubting and doubting and seeing

[023-110] difficulties and disagreeables in everything.”

[023-112] If I had been inclined to be daunted from matrimony, it would not have

[023-113] been Miss Pole to do it; it would have been the lot of poor Signor

[023-114] Brunoni and his wife. And yet again, it was an encouragement to see

[023-115] how, through all their cares and sorrows, they thought of each other and

[023-116] not of themselves; and how keen were their joys, if they only passed

[023-117] through each other, or through the little Phœbe.

[023-118] The signora told me, one day, a good deal about their lives up to this

[023-119] period. It began by my asking her whether Miss Pole’s story of the

[023-120] twin-brothers were true; it sounded so wonderful a likeness, that I

[023-121] should have had my doubts, if Miss Pole had not been unmarried. But the

[023-122] signora, or (as we found out she preferred to be called) Mrs Brown, said

[023-123] it was quite true; that her brother-in-law was by many taken for her

[023-124] husband, which was of great assistance to them in their profession;

[023-125] “though,” she continued, “how people can mistake Thomas for the real

[023-126] Signor Brunoni, I can’t conceive; but he says they do; so I suppose I

[023-127] must believe him. Not but what he is a very good man; I am sure I don’t

[023-128] know how we should have paid our bill at the ‘Rising Sun’ but for the

[023-129] money he sends; but people must know very little about art if they can

[023-130] take him for my husband. Why, Miss, in the ball trick, where my husband

[023-131] spreads his fingers wide, and throws out his little finger with quite an

[023-132] air and a grace, Thomas just clumps up his hand like a fist, and might

[023-133] have ever so many balls hidden in it. Besides, he has never been in

[023-134] [[india-in-cranford|India]], and knows nothing of the proper sit of a turban.”

[023-135] “Have you been in India?” said I, rather astonished.

[023-136] “Oh, yes! many a year, ma’am. Sam was a sergeant in the 31st; and when

[023-137] the regiment was ordered to India, I drew a lot to go, and I was more

[023-138] thankful than I can tell; for it seemed as if it would only be a slow

[023-139] death to me to part from my husband. But, indeed, ma’am, if I had known

[023-140] all, I don’t know whether I would not rather have died there and then

[023-141] than gone through what I have done since. To be sure, I’ve been able to

[023-142] comfort Sam, and to be with him; but, ma’am, I’ve lost six children,”

[023-143] said she, looking up at me with those strange eyes that I’ve never

[023-144] noticed but in mothers of dead children—with a kind of wild look in

[023-145] them, as if seeking for what they never more might find. “Yes! Six

[023-146] children died off, like little buds nipped untimely, in that cruel

[023-147] India. I thought, as each died, I never could—I never would—love a

[023-148] child again; and when the next came, it had not only its own love, but

[023-149] the deeper love that came from the thoughts of its little dead brothers

[023-150] and sisters. And when Phœbe was coming, I said to my husband, ‘Sam,

[023-151] when the child is born, and I am strong, I shall leave you; it will cut

[023-152] my heart cruel; but if this baby dies too, I shall go mad; the madness

[023-153] is in me now; but if you let me go down to Calcutta, carrying my baby

[023-154] step by step, it will, maybe, work itself off; and I will save, and I

[023-155] will hoard, and I will beg—and I will die, to get a passage home to

[023-156] England, where our baby may live?’ God bless him! he said I might go;

[023-157] and he saved up his pay, and I saved every pice I could get for washing

[023-158] or any way; and when Phœbe came, and I grew strong again, I set off.

[023-159] It was very lonely; through the thick forests, dark again with their

[023-160] heavy trees—along by the river’s side (but I had been brought up near

[023-161] the Avon in Warwickshire, so that flowing noise sounded like home)—from

[023-162] station to station, from Indian village to village, I went along,

[023-163] carrying my child. I had seen one of the officer’s ladies with a little

[023-164] picture, ma’am—done by a Catholic foreigner, ma’am—of the Virgin and the

[023-165] little Saviour, ma’am. She had him on her arm, and her form was softly

[023-166] curled round him, and their cheeks touched. Well, when I went to bid

[023-167] good-bye to this lady, for whom I had washed, she cried sadly; for she,

[023-168] too, had lost her children, but she had not another to save, like me;

[023-169] and I was bold enough to ask her would she give me that print. And she

[023-170] cried the more, and said her children were with that little blessed

[023-171] Jesus; and gave it me, and told me that she had heard it had been

[023-172] painted on the bottom of a cask, which made it have that round shape.

[023-173] And when my body was very weary, and my heart was sick (for there were

[023-174] times when I misdoubted if I could ever reach my home, and there were

[023-175] times when I thought of my husband, and one time when I thought my baby

[023-176] was dying), I took out that picture and looked at it, till I could have

[023-177] thought the mother spoke to me, and comforted me. And the natives were

[023-178] very kind. We could not understand one another; but they saw my baby on

[023-179] my breast, and they came out to me, and brought me rice and milk, and

[023-180] sometimes flowers—I have got some of the flowers dried. Then, the next

[023-181] morning, I was so tired; and they wanted me to stay with them—I could

[023-182] tell that—and tried to frighten me from going into the deep woods,

[023-183] which, indeed, looked very strange and dark; but it seemed to me as if

[023-184] Death was following me to take my baby away from me; and as if I must go

[023-185] on, and on—and I thought how God had cared for mothers ever since the

[023-186] world was made, and would care for me; so I bade them good-bye, and set

[023-187] off afresh. And once when my baby was ill, and both she and I needed

[023-188] rest, He led me to a place where I found a kind Englishman lived, right

[023-189] in the midst of the natives.”
