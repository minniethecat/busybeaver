[001-057] CHAPTER I.

[001-058] OUR SOCIETY

[001-059] IN the first place, [[cranford-town|Cranford]] is in possession of the Amazons; all the

[001-060] holders of houses above a certain rent are women. If a married couple

[001-061] come to settle in the town, somehow the gentleman disappears; he is

[001-062] either fairly frightened to death by being the only man in the Cranford

[001-063] [[parties-in-cranford|evening parties]], or he is accounted for by being with his regiment, his

[001-064] ship, or closely engaged in business all the week in the great

[001-065] neighbouring commercial town of Drumble, distant only twenty miles on a

[001-066] railroad. In short, whatever does become of the gentlemen, they are

[001-067] not at Cranford. What could they do if they were there? The surgeon

[001-068] has his round of thirty miles, and sleeps at Cranford; but every man

[001-069] cannot be a surgeon. For keeping the trim gardens full of choice

[001-070] flowers without a weed to speck them; for frightening away little boys

[001-071] who look wistfully at the said flowers through the railings; for rushing

[001-072] out at the geese that occasionally venture in to the gardens if the

[001-073] gates are left open; for deciding all questions of [[reading-in-cranford|literature]] and

[001-074] politics without troubling themselves with unnecessary reasons or

[001-075] arguments; for obtaining clear and correct knowledge of everybody’s

[001-076] affairs in the parish; for keeping their neat [[servants-in-cranford|maid-servants]] in admirable

[001-077] order; for kindness (somewhat dictatorial) to the poor, and real tender

[001-078] good offices to each other whenever they are in distress, the ladies of

[001-079] Cranford are quite sufficient. “A man,” as one of them observed to me

[001-080] once, “is _so_ in the way in the house!” Although the ladies of

[001-081] Cranford know all each other’s proceedings, they are exceedingly

[001-082] indifferent to each other’s opinions. Indeed, as each has her own

[001-083] individuality, not to say eccentricity, pretty strongly developed,

[001-084] nothing is so easy as verbal retaliation; but, somehow, good-will

[001-085] reigns among them to a considerable degree.

[001-086] The Cranford ladies have only an occasional little quarrel, spirited out

[001-087] in a few peppery words and angry jerks of the head; just enough to

[001-088] prevent the even tenor of their lives from becoming too flat. Their

[001-089] dress is very independent of fashion; as they observe, “What does it

[001-090] signify how we dress here at Cranford, where everybody knows us?” And

[001-091] if they go from home, their reason is equally cogent, “What does it

[001-092] signify how we dress here, where nobody knows us?” The materials of

[001-093] their clothes are, in general, good and plain, and most of them are

[001-094] nearly as scrupulous as Miss Tyler, of cleanly memory; but I will

[001-095] answer for it, the last gigot, the last tight and scanty petticoat in

[001-096] wear in England, was seen in Cranford—and seen without a smile.

[001-097] [Picture: A magnificent family red silk umbrella]

[001-098] I can testify to a magnificent family red silk umbrella, under which a

[001-099] gentle little [[spinsterhood|spinster]], left alone of many brothers and sisters, used to

[001-100] patter to church on rainy days. Have you any red silk umbrellas in

[001-101] London? We had a tradition of the first that had ever been seen in

[001-102] Cranford; and the little boys mobbed it, and called it “a stick in

[001-103] petticoats.” It might have been the very red silk one I have described,

[001-104] held by a strong father over a troop of little ones; the poor little

[001-105] lady—the survivor of all—could scarcely carry it.

[001-106] Then there were rules and regulations for visiting and calls; and they

[001-107] were announced to any young people who might be staying in the town,

[001-108] with all the solemnity with which the old Manx laws were read once a

[001-109] year on the Tinwald Mount.

[001-110] “Our friends have sent to inquire how you are after your journey

[001-111] to-night, my dear” (fifteen miles in a gentleman’s carriage); “they will

[001-112] give you some rest to-morrow, but the next day, I have no doubt, they

[001-113] will call; so be at liberty after twelve—from twelve to three are our

[001-114] calling hours.”

[001-115] Then, after they had called—

[001-116] “It is the third day; I dare say your mamma has told you, my dear, never

[001-117] to let more than three days elapse between receiving a call and

[001-118] returning it; and also, that you are never to stay longer than a quarter

[001-119] of an hour.”

[001-120] “But am I to look at my watch? How am I to find out when a quarter of

[001-121] an hour has passed?”

[001-122] “You must keep thinking about the time, my dear, and not allow yourself

[001-123] to forget it in conversation.”

[001-124] As everybody had this rule in their minds, whether they received or paid

[001-125] a call, of course no absorbing subject was ever spoken about. We kept

[001-126] ourselves to short sentences of small talk, and were punctual to our

[001-127] time.

[001-128] I imagine that a few of the gentlefolks of Cranford were poor, and had

[001-129] some difficulty in making both ends meet; but they were like the

[001-130] Spartans, and concealed their smart under a smiling face. We none of us

[001-131] spoke of money, because that subject savoured of commerce and trade, and

[001-132] though some might be poor, we were all aristocratic. The Cranfordians

[001-133] had that kindly _esprit de corps_ which made them overlook all

[001-134] deficiencies in success when some among them tried to conceal their

[001-135] poverty. When [[mrs-forrester|Mrs Forrester]], for instance, gave a party in her

[001-136] baby-house of a dwelling, and the little maiden disturbed the ladies on

[001-137] the sofa by a request that she might get the tea-tray out from

[001-138] underneath, everyone took this novel proceeding as the most natural

[001-139] thing in the world, and talked on about household forms and ceremonies

[001-140] as if we all believed that our hostess had a regular servants’ hall,

[001-141] second table, with housekeeper and steward, instead of the one little

[001-142] [[kindness-in-cranford|charity]]-school maiden, whose short ruddy arms could never have been

[001-143] strong enough to carry the tray upstairs, if she had not been assisted

[001-144] in private by her mistress, who now sat in state, pretending not to know

[001-145] what cakes were sent up, though she knew, and we knew, and she knew that

[001-146] we knew, and we knew that she knew that we knew, she had been busy all

[001-147] the morning making tea-bread and sponge-cakes.

[001-148] There were one or two consequences arising from this general but

[001-149] unacknowledged poverty, and this very much acknowledged gentility, which

[001-150] were not amiss, and which might be introduced into many circles of

[001-151] society to their great improvement. For instance, the inhabitants of

[001-152] Cranford kept early hours, and clattered home in their pattens, under

[001-153] the guidance of a lantern-bearer, about nine o’clock at night; and the

[001-154] whole town was abed and asleep by half-past ten. Moreover, it was

[001-155] considered “vulgar” (a tremendous word in Cranford) to give anything

[001-156] expensive, in the way of eatable or drinkable, at the evening

[001-157] entertainments. Wafer bread-and-butter and sponge-biscuits were all

[001-158] that [[mrs-jamieson|the Honourable Mrs Jamieson]] gave; and she was sister-in-law to the

[001-159] late Earl of Glenmire, although she did practise such “[[genteel-poverty|elegant economy]].”

[001-160] “Elegant economy!” How naturally one falls back into the phraseology of

[001-161] Cranford! There, economy was always “elegant,” and money-spending

[001-162] always “vulgar and ostentatious”; a sort of sour-grapeism which made us

[001-163] very peaceful and satisfied. I never shall forget the dismay felt when

[001-164] a certain [[captain-brown|Captain Brown]] came to live at Cranford, and openly spoke about

[001-165] his being poor—not in a whisper to an intimate friend, the doors and

[001-166] windows being previously closed, but in the public street! in a loud

[001-167] military voice! alleging his poverty as a reason for not taking a

[001-168] particular house. The ladies of Cranford were already rather moaning

[001-169] over the invasion of their territories by a man and a gentleman. He was

[001-170] a half-pay captain, and had obtained some situation on a neighbouring

[001-171] railroad, which had been vehemently petitioned against by the little

[001-172] town; and if, in addition to his masculine gender, and his connection

[001-173] with the obnoxious railroad, he was so brazen as to talk of being

[001-174] poor—why, then, indeed, he must be sent to Coventry. Death was as true

[001-175] and as common as poverty; yet people never spoke about that, loud out in

[001-176] the streets. It was a word not to be mentioned to ears polite. We had

[001-177] tacitly agreed to ignore that any with whom we associated on terms of

[001-178] visiting equality could ever be prevented by poverty from doing anything

[001-179] that they wished. If we walked to or from a party, it was because the

[001-180] night was _so_ fine, or the air _so_ refreshing, not because

[001-181] sedan-chairs were expensive. If we wore prints, instead of summer

[001-182] silks, it was because we preferred a washing material; and so on, till

[001-183] we blinded ourselves to the vulgar fact that we were, all of us, people

[001-184] of very moderate means. Of course, then, we did not know what to make

[001-185] of a man who could speak of poverty as if it was not a disgrace. Yet,

[001-186] somehow, Captain Brown made himself respected in Cranford, and was

[001-187] called upon, in spite of all resolutions to the contrary. I was

[001-188] surprised to hear his opinions quoted as authority at a visit which I

[001-189] paid to Cranford about a year after he had settled in the town. My own

[001-190] friends had been among the bitterest opponents of any proposal to visit

[001-191] the Captain and his daughters, only twelve months before; and now he was

[001-192] even admitted in the tabooed hours before twelve. True, it was to

[001-193] discover the cause of a smoking chimney, before the fire was lighted;

[001-194] but still Captain Brown walked upstairs, nothing daunted, spoke in a

[001-195] voice too large for the room, and joked quite in the way of a tame man

[001-196] about the house. He had been blind to all the small slights, and

[001-197] omissions of trivial ceremonies, with which he had been received. He

[001-198] had been friendly, though the Cranford ladies had been cool; he had

[001-199] answered small sarcastic compliments in good faith; and with his manly

[001-200] frankness had overpowered all the shrinking which met him as a man who

[001-201] was not ashamed to be poor. And, at last, his excellent masculine common

[001-202] sense, and his facility in devising expedients to overcome domestic

[001-203] dilemmas, had gained him an extraordinary place as authority among the

[001-204] Cranford ladies. He himself went on in his course, as unaware of his

[001-205] popularity as he had been of the reverse; and I am sure he was startled

[001-206] one day when he found his advice so highly esteemed as to make some

[001-207] counsel which he had given in jest to be taken in sober, serious

[001-208] earnest.
