# Section 7

[007-001] “Leave me, leave me to repose.”

[007-002] And _now_ I come to the love affair.

[007-003] It seems that [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] had a cousin, once or twice removed, who had

[007-004] offered to [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]] long ago. Now this cousin lived four or five

[007-005] miles from [[cranford-town|Cranford]] on his own estate; but his property was not large

[007-006] enough to entitle him to rank higher than a yeoman; or rather, with

[007-007] something of the “pride which apes humility,” he had refused to push

[007-008] himself on, as so many of his class had done, into the ranks of the

[007-009] squires. He would not allow himself to be called [[mr-holbrook|Thomas Holbrook]],

[007-010] _Esq._; he even sent back [[letter-writing|letters]] with this address, telling the

[007-011] post-mistress at Cranford that his name was _Mr_ Thomas Holbrook,

[007-012] yeoman. He rejected all domestic innovations; he would have the house

[007-013] door stand open in summer and shut in winter, without knocker or bell to

[007-014] summon a servant. The closed fist or the knob of a stick did this

[007-015] office for him if he found the door locked. He despised every

[007-016] refinement which had not its root deep down in humanity. If people were

[007-017] not ill, he saw no necessity for moderating his voice. He spoke the

[007-018] dialect of the country in perfection, and constantly used it in

[007-019] conversation; although Miss Pole (who gave me these particulars) added,

[007-020] that he read aloud more beautifully and with more feeling than any one

[007-021] she had ever heard, except the late rector.

[007-022] “And how came Miss Matilda not to marry him?” asked I.

[007-023] “Oh, I don’t know. She was willing enough, I think; but you know Cousin

[007-024] Thomas would not have been enough of a gentleman for the rector and Miss

[007-025] Jenkyns.”

[007-026] “Well! but they were not to marry him,” said I, impatiently.

[007-027] “No; but they did not like Miss Matty to marry below her rank. You know

[007-028] she was the rector’s daughter, and somehow they are related to Sir Peter

[007-029] Arley: [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]] thought a deal of that.”

[007-030] “Poor Miss Matty!” said I.

[007-031] “Nay, now, I don’t know anything more than that he offered and was

[007-032] refused. Miss Matty might not like him—and Miss Jenkyns might never

[007-033] have said a word—it is only a guess of mine.”

[007-034] “Has she never seen him since?” I inquired.

[007-035] “No, I think not. You see [[woodley|Woodley]], Cousin Thomas’s house, lies half-way

[007-036] between Cranford and Misselton; and I know he made Misselton his

[007-037] market-town very soon after he had offered to Miss Matty; and I don’t

[007-038] think he has been into Cranford above once or twice since—once, when I

[007-039] was walking with Miss Matty, in [[cranford-high-street|High Street]], and suddenly she darted

[007-040] from me, and went up [[shire-lane|Shire Lane]]. A few minutes after I was startled by

[007-041] meeting Cousin Thomas.”

[007-042] “How old is he?” I asked, after a pause of castle-building.

[007-043] “He must be about seventy, I think, my dear,” said Miss Pole, blowing up

[007-044] my castle, as if by gun-powder, into small fragments.

[007-045] Very soon after—at least during my long visit to Miss Matilda—I had the

[007-046] opportunity of seeing Mr Holbrook; seeing, too, his first encounter with

[007-047] his former love, after thirty or forty years’ separation. I was helping

[007-048] to decide whether any of the new assortment of coloured silks which they

[007-049] had just received at the shop would do to match a grey and black

[007-050] mousseline-delaine that wanted a new breadth, when a tall, thin, Don

[007-051] Quixote-looking old man came into the shop for some woollen gloves. I

[007-052] had never seen the person (who was rather striking) before, and I

[007-053] watched him rather attentively while Miss Matty listened to the shopman.

[007-054] The stranger wore a blue coat with brass buttons, drab breeches, and

[007-055] gaiters, and drummed with his fingers on the counter until he was

[007-056] attended to. When he answered the shop-boy’s question, “What can I have

[007-057] the pleasure of showing you to-day, sir?” I saw Miss Matilda start, and

[007-058] then suddenly sit down; and instantly I guessed who it was. She had

[007-059] made some inquiry which had to be carried round to the other shopman.

[007-060] “Miss Jenkyns wants the black sarsenet two-and-twopence the yard”; and

[007-061] Mr Holbrook had caught the name, and was across the shop in two strides.

[007-062] “Matty—Miss Matilda—Miss Jenkyns! God bless my soul! I should not have

[007-063] known you. How are you? how are you?” He kept shaking her hand in a

[007-064] way which proved the warmth of his friendship; but he repeated so often,

[007-065] as if to himself, “I should not have known you!” that any sentimental

[007-066] [[love-and-courtship|romance]] which I might be inclined to build was quite done away with by

[007-067] his manner.

[007-068] However, he kept talking to us all the time we were in the shop; and

[007-069] then waving the shopman with the unpurchased gloves on one side, with

[007-070] “Another time, sir! another time!” he walked home with us. I am happy

[007-071] to say my client, Miss Matilda, also left the shop in an equally

[007-072] bewildered state, not having purchased either green or red silk. Mr

[007-073] Holbrook was evidently full with honest loud-spoken joy at meeting his

[007-074] old love again; he touched on the changes that had taken place; he even

[007-075] spoke of Miss Jenkyns as “Your poor sister! Well, well! we have all our

[007-076] faults”; and bade us good-bye with many a hope that he should soon see

[007-077] Miss Matty again. She went straight to her room, and never came back

[007-078] till our early [[tea-drinking|tea-time]], when I thought she looked as if she had been

[007-079] crying.

[007-081] CHAPTER IV.

[007-082] A VISIT TO AN OLD BACHELOR

[007-083] A FEW days after, a note came from Mr Holbrook, asking us—impartially

[007-084] asking both of us—in a formal, old-fashioned style, to spend a day at

[007-085] his house—a long June day—for it was June now. He named that he had

[007-086] also invited his cousin, Miss Pole; so that we might join in a fly,

[007-087] which could be put up at his house.

[007-088] I expected Miss Matty to jump at this invitation; but, no! Miss Pole

[007-089] and I had the greatest difficulty in persuading her to go. She thought

[007-090] it was improper; and was even half annoyed when we utterly ignored the

[007-091] idea of any impropriety in her going with two other ladies to see her

[007-092] old lover. Then came a more serious difficulty. She did not think

[007-093] Deborah would have liked her to go. This took us half a day’s good hard

[007-094] talking to get over; but, at the first sentence of relenting, I seized

[007-095] the opportunity, and wrote and despatched an acceptance in her

[007-096] name—fixing day and hour, that all might be decided and done with.

[007-097] The next morning she asked me if I would go down to the shop with her;

[007-098] and there, after much hesitation, we chose out three caps to be sent

[007-099] home and tried on, that the most becoming might be selected to take with

[007-100] us on Thursday.

[007-101] She was in a state of silent agitation all the way to Woodley. She had

[007-102] evidently never been there before; and, although she little dreamt I

[007-103] knew anything of her early story, I could perceive she was in a tremor

[007-104] at the thought of seeing the place which might have been her home, and

[007-105] round which it is probable that many of her innocent girlish

[007-106] imaginations had clustered. It was a long drive there, through paved

[007-107] jolting lanes. Miss Matilda sat bolt upright, and looked wistfully out

[007-108] of the windows as we drew near the end of our journey. The aspect of

[007-109] the country was quiet and pastoral. Woodley stood among fields; and

[007-110] there was an old-fashioned garden where roses and currant-bushes touched

[007-111] each other, and where the feathery asparagus formed a pretty background

[007-112] to the pinks and gilly-flowers; there was no drive up to the door. We

[007-113] got out at a little gate, and walked up a straight box-edged path.

[007-114] “My cousin might make a drive, I think,” said Miss Pole, who was afraid

[007-115] of ear-ache, and had only her cap on.

[007-116] “I think it is very pretty,” said Miss Matty, with a soft plaintiveness

[007-117] in her voice, and almost in a whisper, for just then Mr Holbrook

[007-118] appeared at the door, rubbing his hands in very effervescence of

[007-119] hospitality. He looked more like my idea of Don Quixote than ever, and

[007-120] yet the likeness was only external. His respectable housekeeper stood

[007-121] modestly at the door to bid us welcome; and, while she led the elder

[007-122] ladies upstairs to a bedroom, I begged to look about the garden. My

[007-123] request evidently pleased the old gentleman, who took me all round the

[007-124] place and showed me his six-and-twenty cows, named after the different

[007-125] letters of the alphabet. As we went along, he surprised me occasionally

[007-126] by repeating apt and beautiful quotations from the poets, ranging easily

[007-127] from Shakespeare and George Herbert to those of our own day. He did

[007-128] this as naturally as if he were thinking aloud, and their true and

[007-129] beautiful words were the best expression he could find for what he was

[007-130] thinking or feeling. To be sure he called Byron “my Lord Byrron,” and

[007-131] pronounced the name of Goethe strictly in accordance with the English

[007-132] sound of the letters—“As Goethe says, ‘Ye ever-verdant palaces,’” &c.

[007-133] Altogether, I never met with a man, before or since, who had spent so

[007-134] long a life in a secluded and not impressive country, with

[007-135] ever-increasing delight in the daily and yearly change of season and

[007-136] beauty.

[007-137] When he and I went in, we found that dinner was nearly ready in the

[007-138] kitchen—for so I suppose the room ought to be called, as there were oak

[007-139] dressers and cupboards all round, all over by the side of the fireplace,

[007-140] and only a small Turkey carpet in the middle of the flag-floor. The

[007-141] room might have been easily made into a handsome dark oak dining-parlour

[007-142] by removing the oven and a few other appurtenances of a kitchen, which

[007-143] were evidently never used, the real cooking-place being at some

[007-144] distance. The room in which we were expected to sit was a

[007-145] stiffly-furnished, ugly apartment; but that in which we did sit was what

[007-146] Mr Holbrook called the counting-house, where he paid his labourers their

[007-147] weekly wages at a great desk near the door. The rest of the pretty

[007-148] sitting-room—looking into the orchard, and all covered over with dancing

[007-149] tree-shadows—was filled with [[reading-in-cranford|books]]. They lay on the ground, they

[007-150] covered the walls, they strewed the table. He was evidently half

[007-151] ashamed and half proud of his extravagance in this respect. They were

[007-152] of all kinds—poetry and wild weird tales prevailing. He evidently chose

[007-153] his books in accordance with his own tastes, not because such and such

[007-154] were classical or established favourites.

[007-155] “Ah!” he said, “we farmers ought not to have much time for reading; yet

[007-156] somehow one can’t help it.”

[007-157] “What a pretty room!” said Miss Matty, _sotto voce_.

[007-158] “What a pleasant place!” said I, aloud, almost simultaneously.

[007-159] “Nay! if you like it,” replied he; “but can you sit on these great,

[007-160] black leather, three-cornered chairs? I like it better than the best

[007-161] parlour; but I thought ladies would take that for the smarter place.”

[007-162] It was the smarter place, but, like most smart things, not at all

[007-163] pretty, or pleasant, or home-like; so, while we were at dinner, the

[007-164] servant-girl dusted and scrubbed the counting-house chairs, and we sat

[007-165] there all the rest of the day.

[007-166] We had pudding before meat; and I thought Mr Holbrook was going to make

[007-167] some apology for his old-fashioned ways, for he began—

[007-168] “I don’t know whether you like newfangled ways.”

[007-169] “Oh, not at all!” said Miss Matty.

[007-170] “No more do I,” said he. “My housekeeper _will_ have these in her new

[007-171] fashion; or else I tell her that, when I was a young man, we used to

[007-172] keep strictly to my father’s rule, ‘No broth, no ball; no ball, no

[007-173] beef’; and always began dinner with broth. Then we had suet puddings,

[007-174] boiled in the broth with the beef: and then the meat itself. If we did

[007-175] not sup our broth, we had no ball, which we liked a deal better; and the

[007-176] beef came last of all, and only those had it who had done justice to the

[007-177] broth and the ball. Now folks begin with sweet things, and turn their

[007-178] dinners topsy-turvy.”
