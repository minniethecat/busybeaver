# Section 11

[011-001] I can’t quite remember the date, but I think it was in 1805 that Miss

[011-002] Jenkyns wrote the longest series of [[letter-writing|letters]]—on occasion of her absence

[011-003] on a visit to some friends near Newcastle-upon-Tyne. These friends were

[011-004] intimate with the commandant of the garrison there, and heard from him

[011-005] of all the preparations that were being made to repel the invasion of

[011-006] Buonaparte, which some people imagined might take place at the mouth of

[011-007] the Tyne. [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]] was evidently very much alarmed; and the first

[011-008] part of her letters was often written in pretty intelligible English,

[011-009] conveying particulars of the preparations which were made in the family

[011-010] with whom she was residing against the dreaded event; the bundles of

[011-011] clothes that were packed up ready for a flight to Alston Moor (a wild

[011-012] hilly piece of ground between Northumberland and Cumberland); the signal

[011-013] that was to be given for this flight, and for the simultaneous turning

[011-014] out of the volunteers under arms—which said signal was to consist (if I

[011-015] remember rightly) in ringing the church bells in a particular and

[011-016] ominous manner. One day, when Miss Jenkyns and her hosts were at a

[011-017] dinner-party in Newcastle, this warning summons was actually given (not

[011-018] a very wise proceeding, if there be any truth in the moral attached to

[011-019] the fable of the Boy and the Wolf; but so it was), and Miss Jenkyns,

[011-020] hardly recovered from her fright, wrote the next day to describe the

[011-021] sound, the breathless shock, the hurry and alarm; and then, taking

[011-022] breath, she added, “How trivial, my dear father, do all our

[011-023] apprehensions of the last evening appear, at the present moment, to calm

[011-024] and enquiring minds!” And here [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]] broke in with—

[011-025] “But, indeed, my dear, they were not at all trivial or trifling at the

[011-026] time. I know I used to wake up in the night many a time and think I

[011-027] heard the tramp of the French entering [[cranford-town|Cranford]]. Many people talked of

[011-028] hiding themselves in the salt mines—and meat would have kept capitally

[011-029] down there, only perhaps we should have been thirsty. And my father

[011-030] preached a whole set of sermons on the occasion; one set in the

[011-031] mornings, all about David and Goliath, to spirit up the people to

[011-032] fighting with spades or bricks, if need were; and the other set in the

[011-033] afternoons, proving that Napoleon (that was another name for Bony, as we

[011-034] used to call him) was all the same as an Apollyon and Abaddon. I

[011-035] remember my father rather thought he should be asked to print this last

[011-036] set; but the parish had, perhaps, had enough of them with hearing.”

[011-037] [[peter-jenkyns|Peter Marmaduke Arley Jenkyns]] (“poor Peter!” as Miss Matty began to call

[011-038] him) was at school at Shrewsbury by this time. The rector took up his

[011-039] pen, and rubbed up his Latin once more, to correspond with his boy. It

[011-040] was very clear that the lad’s were what are called show letters. They

[011-041] were of a highly mental description, giving an account of his studies,

[011-042] and his intellectual hopes of various kinds, with an occasional

[011-043] quotation from the classics; but, now and then, the animal nature broke

[011-044] out in such a little sentence as this, evidently written in a trembling

[011-045] hurry, after the letter had been inspected: “Mother dear, do send me a

[011-046] cake, and put plenty of citron in.” The “mother dear” probably answered

[011-047] her boy in the form of cakes and “goody,” for there were none of her

[011-048] letters among this set; but a whole collection of the rector’s, to whom

[011-049] the Latin in his boy’s letters was like a trumpet to the old war-horse.

[011-050] I do not know much about Latin, certainly, and it is, perhaps, an

[011-051] ornamental language, but not very useful, I think—at least to judge from

[011-052] the bits I remember out of the rector’s letters. One was, “You have not

[011-053] got that town in your map of Ireland; but _Bonus Bernardus non videt

[011-054] omnia_, as the Proverbia say.” Presently it became very evident that

[011-055] “poor Peter” got himself into many scrapes. There were letters of

[011-056] stilted penitence to his father, for some wrong-doing; and among them

[011-057] all was a badly-written, badly-sealed, badly-directed, blotted note:—“My

[011-058] dear, dear, dear, dearest mother, I will be a better boy; I will,

[011-059] indeed; but don’t, please, be ill for me; I am not worth it; but I will

[011-060] be good, darling mother.”

[011-061] Miss Matty could not speak for crying, after she had read this note.

[011-062] She gave it to me in silence, and then got up and took it to her sacred

[011-063] recesses in her own room, for fear, by any chance, it might get burnt.

[011-064] “Poor Peter!” she said; “he was always in scrapes; he was too easy.

[011-065] They led him wrong, and then left him in the lurch. But he was too fond

[011-066] of mischief. He could never resist a joke. Poor Peter!”

[011-067] CHAPTER VI.

[011-068] POOR PETER

[011-069] POOR Peter’s career lay before him rather pleasantly mapped out by kind

[011-070] friends, but _Bonus Bernardus non videt omnia_, in this map too. He was

[011-071] to win honours at the Shrewsbury School, and carry them thick to

[011-072] Cambridge, and after that, a living awaited him, the gift of his

[011-073] godfather, Sir Peter Arley. Poor Peter! his lot in life was very

[011-074] different to what his friends had hoped and planned. Miss Matty told me

[011-075] all about it, and I think it was a relief when she had done so.

[011-076] He was the darling of his mother, who seemed to dote on all her

[011-077] children, though she was, perhaps, a little afraid of Deborah’s superior

[011-078] acquirements. Deborah was the favourite of her father, and when Peter

[011-079] disappointed him, she became his pride. The sole honour Peter brought

[011-080] away from Shrewsbury was the reputation of being the best good fellow

[011-081] that ever was, and of being the captain of the school in the art of

[011-082] practical joking. His father was disappointed, but set about remedying

[011-083] the matter in a manly way. He could not afford to send Peter to read

[011-084] with any tutor, but he could read with him himself; and Miss Matty told

[011-085] me much of the awful preparations in the way of dictionaries and

[011-086] lexicons that were made in her father’s study the morning Peter began.

[011-087] “My poor mother!” said she. “I remember how she used to stand in the

[011-088] hall, just near enough the study-door, to catch the tone of my father’s

[011-089] voice. I could tell in a moment if all was going right, by her face.

[011-090] And it did go right for a long time.”

[011-091] “What went wrong at last?” said I. “That tiresome Latin, I dare say.”

[011-092] “No! it was not the Latin. Peter was in high favour with my father, for

[011-093] he worked up well for him. But he seemed to think that the Cranford

[011-094] people might be joked about, and made fun of, and they did not like it;

[011-095] nobody does. He was always hoaxing them; ‘hoaxing’ is not a pretty

[011-096] word, my dear, and I hope you won’t tell your father I used it, for I

[011-097] should not like him to think that I was not choice in my language, after

[011-098] living with such a woman as Deborah. And be sure you never use it

[011-099] yourself. I don’t know how it slipped out of my mouth, except it was

[011-100] that I was thinking of poor Peter and it was always his expression. But

[011-101] he was a very gentlemanly boy in many things. He was like dear Captain

[011-102] Brown in always being ready to help any old person or a child. Still,

[011-103] he did like joking and making fun; and he seemed to think the old ladies

[011-104] in Cranford would believe anything. There were many old ladies living

[011-105] here then; we are principally ladies now, I know, but we are not so old

[011-106] as the ladies used to be when I was a girl. I could laugh to think of

[011-107] some of Peter’s jokes. No, my dear, I won’t tell you of them, because

[011-108] they might not shock you as they ought to do, and they were very

[011-109] shocking. He even took in my father once, by dressing himself up as a

[011-110] lady that was passing through the town and wished to see the Rector of

[011-111] Cranford, ‘who had published that admirable Assize Sermon.’ Peter said

[011-112] he was awfully frightened himself when he saw how my father took it all

[011-113] in, and even offered to copy out all his Napoleon Buonaparte sermons for

[011-114] her—him, I mean—no, her, for Peter was a lady then. He told me he was

[011-115] more terrified than he ever was before, all the time my father was

[011-116] speaking. He did not think my father would have believed him; and yet if

[011-117] he had not, it would have been a sad thing for Peter. As it was, he was

[011-118] none so glad of it, for my father kept him hard at work copying out all

[011-119] those twelve Buonaparte sermons for the lady—that was for Peter himself,

[011-120] you know. He was the lady. And once when he wanted to go fishing,

[011-121] Peter said, ‘Confound the woman!’—very bad language, my dear, but Peter

[011-122] was not always so guarded as he should have been; my father was so angry

[011-123] with him, it nearly frightened me out of my wits: and yet I could hardly

[011-124] keep from laughing at the little curtseys Peter kept making, quite

[011-125] slyly, whenever my father spoke of the lady’s excellent taste and sound

[011-126] discrimination.”

[011-128] “Did Miss Jenkyns know of these tricks?” said I.

[011-129] “Oh, no! Deborah would have been too much shocked. No, no one knew but

[011-130] me. I wish I had always known of Peter’s plans; but sometimes he did

[011-131] not tell me. He used to say the old ladies in the town wanted something

[011-132] to talk about; but I don’t think they did. They had the _St James’s

[011-133] Chronicle_ three times a week, just as we have now, and we have plenty

[011-134] to say; and I remember the clacking noise there always was when some of

[011-135] the ladies got together. But, probably, schoolboys talk more than

[011-136] ladies. At last there was a terrible, sad thing happened.” Miss Matty

[011-137] got up, went to the door, and opened it; no one was there. She rang the

[011-138] bell for [[martha|Martha]], and when Martha came, her mistress told her to go for

[011-139] eggs to a farm at the other end of the town.

[011-140] “I will lock the door after you, Martha. You are not afraid to go, are

[011-141] you?”

[011-142] “No, ma’am, not at all; Jem Hearn will be only too proud to go with me.”

[011-143] Miss Matty drew herself up, and as soon as we were alone, she wished

[011-144] that Martha had more maidenly reserve.

[011-145] “We’ll put out the candle, my dear. We can talk just as well by

[011-146] firelight, you know. There! Well, you see, Deborah had gone from home

[011-147] for a fortnight or so; it was a very still, quiet day, I remember,

[011-148] overhead; and the lilacs were all in flower, so I suppose it was spring.

[011-149] My father had gone out to see some sick people in the parish; I

[011-150] recollect seeing him leave the house with his wig and shovel-hat and

[011-151] cane. What possessed our poor Peter I don’t know; he had the sweetest

[011-152] temper, and yet he always seemed to like to plague Deborah. She never

[011-153] laughed at his jokes, and thought him ungenteel, and not careful enough

[011-154] about improving his mind; and that vexed him.

[011-155] “Well! he went to her room, it seems, and dressed himself in her old

[011-156] gown, and shawl, and bonnet; just the things she used to wear in

[011-157] Cranford, and was known by everywhere; and he made the pillow into a

[011-158] little—you are sure you locked the door, my dear, for I should not like

[011-159] anyone to hear—into—into a little baby, with white long clothes. It was

[011-160] only, as he told me afterwards, to make something to talk about in the

[011-161] town; he never thought of it as affecting Deborah. And he went and

[011-162] walked up and down in the Filbert walk—just half-hidden by the rails,

[011-163] and half-seen; and he cuddled his pillow, just like a baby, and talked

[011-164] to it all the nonsense people do. Oh dear! and my father came stepping

[011-165] stately up the street, as he always did; and what should he see but a

[011-166] little black crowd of people—I daresay as many as twenty—all peeping

[011-167] through his garden rails. So he thought, at first, they were only

[011-168] looking at a new rhododendron that was in full bloom, and that he was

[011-169] very proud of; and he walked slower, that they might have more time to

[011-170] admire. And he wondered if he could make out a sermon from the

[011-171] occasion, and thought, perhaps, there was some relation between the

[011-172] rhododendrons and the lilies of the field. My poor father! When he

[011-173] came nearer, he began to wonder that they did not see him; but their

[011-174] heads were all so close together, peeping and peeping! My father was

[011-175] amongst them, meaning, he said, to ask them to walk into the garden with

[011-176] him, and admire the beautiful vegetable production, when—oh, my dear, I

[011-177] tremble to think of it—he looked through the rails himself, and saw—I

[011-178] don’t know what he thought he saw, but old Clare told me his face went

[011-179] quite grey-white with anger, and his eyes blazed out under his frowning

[011-180] black brows; and he spoke out—oh, so terribly!—and bade them all stop

[011-181] where they were—not one of them to go, not one of them to stir a step;

[011-182] and, swift as light, he was in at the garden door, and down the Filbert

[011-183] walk, and seized hold of poor Peter, and tore his clothes off his

[011-184] back—bonnet, shawl, gown, and all—and threw the pillow among the people

[011-185] over the railings: and then he was very, very angry indeed, and before

[011-186] all the people he lifted up his cane and flogged Peter!
