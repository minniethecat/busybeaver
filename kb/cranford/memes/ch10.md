# Section 10

[010-001] The earliest set of [[letter-writing|letters]] were two bundles tied together, and ticketed

[010-002] (in [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]]’s handwriting) “Letters interchanged between my

[010-003] ever-honoured father and my dearly-beloved mother, prior to their

[010-004] marriage, in July 1774.” I should guess that the rector of [[cranford-town|Cranford]] was

[010-005] about twenty-seven years of age when he wrote those letters; and Miss

[010-006] [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Matty]] told me that her mother was just eighteen at the time of her

[010-007] wedding. With my idea of the rector derived from a picture in the

[010-008] dining-parlour, stiff and stately, in a huge full-bottomed wig, with

[010-009] gown, cassock, and bands, and his hand upon a copy of the only sermon he

[010-010] ever published—it was strange to read these letters. They were full of

[010-011] eager, passionate ardour; short homely sentences, right fresh from the

[010-012] heart (very different from the grand Latinised, Johnsonian style of the

[010-013] printed sermon preached before some judge at assize time). His letters

[010-014] were a curious contrast to those of his girl-bride. She was evidently

[010-015] rather annoyed at his demands upon her for expressions of love, and

[010-016] could not quite understand what he meant by repeating the same thing

[010-017] over in so many different ways; but what she was quite clear about was a

[010-018] longing for a white “Paduasoy”—whatever that might be; and six or seven

[010-019] letters were principally occupied in asking her lover to use his

[010-020] influence with her parents (who evidently kept her in good order) to

[010-021] obtain this or that article of dress, more especially the white

[010-022] “Paduasoy.” He cared nothing how she was dressed; she was always lovely

[010-023] enough for him, as he took pains to assure her, when she begged him to

[010-024] express in his answers a predilection for particular pieces of finery,

[010-025] in order that she might show what he said to her parents. But at length

[010-026] he seemed to find out that she would not be married till she had a

[010-027] “trousseau” to her mind; and then he sent her a letter, which had

[010-028] evidently accompanied a whole box full of finery, and in which he

[010-029] requested that she might be dressed in everything her heart desired.

[010-030] This was the first letter, ticketed in a frail, delicate hand, “From my

[010-031] dearest John.” Shortly afterwards they were married, I suppose, from

[010-032] the intermission in their correspondence.

[010-033] “We must burn them, I think,” said Miss Matty, looking doubtfully at me.

[010-034] “No one will care for them when I am gone.” And one by one she dropped

[010-035] them into the middle of the fire, watching each blaze up, die out, and

[010-036] rise away, in faint, white, ghostly semblance, up the chimney, before

[010-037] she gave another to the same fate. The room was light enough now; but

[010-038] I, like her, was fascinated into watching the destruction of those

[010-039] letters, into which the honest warmth of a manly heart had been poured

[010-040] forth.

[010-041] The next letter, likewise docketed by Miss Jenkyns, was endorsed,

[010-042] “Letter of pious congratulation and exhortation from my venerable

[010-043] grandfather to my beloved mother, on occasion of my own birth. Also

[010-044] some practical remarks on the desirability of keeping warm the

[010-045] extremities of infants, from my excellent grandmother.”

[010-046] The first part was, indeed, a severe and forcible picture of the

[010-047] responsibilities of mothers, and a warning against the evils that were

[010-048] in the world, and lying in ghastly wait for the little baby of two days

[010-049] old. His wife did not write, said the old gentleman, because he had

[010-050] forbidden it, she being indisposed with a sprained ankle, which (he

[010-051] said) quite incapacitated her from holding a pen. However, at the foot

[010-052] of the page was a small “T.O.,” and on turning it over, sure enough,

[010-053] there was a letter to “my dear, dearest Molly,” begging her, when she

[010-054] left her room, whatever she did, to go _up_ stairs before going _down_:

[010-055] and telling her to wrap her baby’s feet up in flannel, and keep it warm

[010-056] by the fire, although it was summer, for babies were so tender.

[010-057] It was pretty to see from the letters, which were evidently exchanged

[010-058] with some frequency between the young mother and the grandmother, how

[010-059] the girlish vanity was being weeded out of her heart by love for her

[010-060] baby. The white “Paduasoy” figured again in the letters, with almost as

[010-061] much vigour as before. In one, it was being made into a christening

[010-062] cloak for the baby. It decked it when it went with its parents to

[010-063] spend a day or two at [[arley-hall|Arley Hall]]. It added to its charms, when it was

[010-064] “the prettiest little baby that ever was seen. Dear mother, I wish you

[010-065] could see her! Without any pershality, I do think she will grow up a

[010-066] regular bewty!” I thought of Miss Jenkyns, grey, withered, and

[010-067] wrinkled, and I wondered if her mother had known her in the courts of

[010-068] heaven: and then I knew that she had, and that they stood there in

[010-069] angelic guise.

[010-070] There was a great gap before any of the rector’s letters appeared. And

[010-071] then his wife had changed her mode of her endorsement. It was no longer

[010-072] from, “My dearest John;” it was from “My Honoured Husband.” The letters

[010-073] were written on occasion of the publication of the same sermon which was

[010-074] represented in the picture. The preaching before “My Lord Judge,” and

[010-075] the “publishing by request,” was evidently the culminating point—the

[010-076] event of his life. It had been necessary for him to go up to London to

[010-077] superintend it through the press. Many friends had to be called upon

[010-078] and consulted before he could decide on any printer fit for so onerous a

[010-079] task; and at length it was arranged that J. and J. Rivingtons were to

[010-080] have the honourable responsibility. The worthy rector seemed to be

[010-081] strung up by the occasion to a high literary pitch, for he could hardly

[010-082] write a letter to his wife without cropping out into Latin. I remember

[010-083] the end of one of his letters ran thus: “I shall ever hold the virtuous

[010-084] qualities of my Molly in remembrance, _dum memor ipse mei_, _dum

[010-085] spiritus regit artus_,” which, considering that the English of his

[010-086] correspondent was sometimes at fault in grammar, and often in spelling,

[010-087] might be taken as a proof of how much he “idealised his Molly;” and, as

[010-088] Miss Jenkyns used to say, “People talk a great deal about idealising

[010-089] now-a-days, whatever that may mean.” But this was nothing to a fit of

[010-090] writing classical poetry which soon seized him, in which his Molly

[010-091] figured away as “Maria.” The letter containing the _carmen_ was

[010-092] endorsed by her, “Hebrew verses sent me by my honoured husband. I thowt

[010-093] to have had a letter about killing the pig, but must wait. Mem., to

[010-094] send the poetry to Sir Peter Arley, as my husband desires.” And in a

[010-095] post-scriptum note in his handwriting it was stated that the Ode had

[010-096] appeared in the _Gentleman’s Magazine_, December 1782.

[010-097] Her letters back to her husband (treasured as fondly by him as if they

[010-098] had been _M. T. Ciceronis Epistolæ_) were more satisfactory to an absent

[010-099] husband and father than his could ever have been to her. She told him

[010-100] how Deborah sewed her seam very neatly every day, and read to her in the

[010-101] [[reading-in-cranford|books]] he had set her; how she was a very “forrard,” good child, but

[010-102] would ask questions her mother could not answer, but how she did not let

[010-103] herself down by saying she did not know, but took to stirring the fire,

[010-104] or sending the “forrard” child on an errand. Matty was now the mother’s

[010-105] darling, and promised (like her sister at her age), to be a great

[010-106] beauty. I was reading this aloud to Miss Matty, who smiled and sighed a

[010-107] little at the hope, so fondly expressed, that “little Matty might not be

[010-108] vain, even if she were a bewty.”

[010-109] “I had very pretty hair, my dear,” said Miss Matilda; “and not a bad

[010-110] mouth.” And I saw her soon afterwards adjust her cap and draw herself

[010-111] up.

[010-112] But to return to Mrs Jenkyns’s letters. She told her husband about the

[010-113] poor in the parish; what homely domestic medicines she had administered;

[010-114] what kitchen physic she had sent. She had evidently held his

[010-115] displeasure as a rod in pickle over the heads of all the ne’er-do-wells.

[010-116] She asked for his directions about the cows and pigs; and did not

[010-117] always obtain them, as I have shown before.

[010-118] The kind old grandmother was dead when a little boy was born, soon after

[010-119] the publication of the sermon; but there was another letter of

[010-120] exhortation from the grandfather, more stringent and admonitory than

[010-121] ever, now that there was a boy to be guarded from the snares of the

[010-122] world. He described all the various sins into which men might fall,

[010-123] until I wondered how any man ever came to a natural death. The gallows

[010-124] seemed as if it must have been the termination of the lives of most of

[010-125] the grandfather’s friends and acquaintance; and I was not surprised at

[010-126] the way in which he spoke of this life being “a vale of tears.”

[010-127] It seemed curious that I should never have heard of this brother before;

[010-128] but I concluded that he had died young, or else surely his name would

[010-129] have been alluded to by his sisters.

[010-130] By-and-by we came to packets of Miss Jenkyns’s letters. These Miss

[010-131] Matty did regret to burn. She said all the others had been only

[010-132] interesting to those who loved the writers, and that it seemed as if it

[010-133] would have hurt her to allow them to fall into the hands of strangers,

[010-134] who had not known her dear mother, and how good she was, although she

[010-135] did not always spell quite in the modern fashion; but Deborah’s letters

[010-136] were so very superior! Any one might profit by reading them. It was a

[010-137] long time since she had read Mrs Chapone, but she knew she used to think

[010-138] that Deborah could have said the same things quite as well; and as for

[010-139] Mrs Carter! people thought a deal of her letters, just because she had

[010-140] written “Epictetus,” but she was quite sure Deborah would never have

[010-141] made use of such a common expression as “I canna be fashed!”

[010-142] [Picture: I made use of the time to think of many other things]

[010-143] Miss Matty did grudge burning these letters, it was evident. She would

[010-144] not let them be carelessly passed over with any quiet reading, and

[010-145] skipping, to myself. She took them from me, and even lighted the second

[010-146] candle in order to read them aloud with a proper emphasis, and without

[010-147] stumbling over the big words. Oh dear! how I wanted facts instead of

[010-148] reflections, before those letters were concluded! They lasted us two

[010-149] nights; and I won’t deny that I made use of the time to think of many

[010-150] other things, and yet I was always at my post at the end of each

[010-151] sentence.

[010-152] The rector’s letters, and those of his wife and mother-in-law, had all

[010-153] been tolerably short and pithy, written in a straight hand, with the

[010-154] lines very close together. Sometimes the whole letter was contained on

[010-155] a mere scrap of paper. The paper was very yellow, and the ink very

[010-156] brown; some of the sheets were (as Miss Matty made me observe) the old

[010-157] original post, with the stamp in the corner representing a post-boy

[010-158] riding for life and twanging his horn. The letters of Mrs Jenkyns and

[010-159] her mother were fastened with a great round red wafer; for it was before

[010-160] Miss Edgeworth’s “patronage” had banished wafers from polite society.

[010-161] It was evident, from the tenor of what was said, that franks were in

[010-162] great request, and were even used as a means of paying debts by needy

[010-163] members of Parliament. The rector sealed his epistles with an immense

[010-164] coat of arms, and showed by the care with which he had performed this

[010-165] [[etiquette|ceremony]] that he expected they should be cut open, not broken by any

[010-166] thoughtless or impatient hand. Now, Miss Jenkyns’s letters were of a

[010-167] later date in form and writing. She wrote on the square sheet which we

[010-168] have learned to call old-fashioned. Her hand was admirably calculated,

[010-169] together with her use of many-syllabled words, to fill up a sheet, and

[010-170] then came the pride and delight of crossing. Poor Miss Matty got sadly

[010-171] puzzled with this, for the words gathered size like snowballs, and

[010-172] towards the end of her letter Miss Jenkyns used to become quite

[010-173] sesquipedalian. In one to her father, slightly theological and

[010-174] controversial in its tone, she had spoken of Herod, Tetrarch of Idumea.

[010-175] Miss Matty read it “Herod Petrarch of Etruria,” and was just as well

[010-176] pleased as if she had been right.
