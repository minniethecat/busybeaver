# Medicine in Cranford

Medicine in Cranford is represented primarily through the figure of Mr Hoggins, the
town surgeon, whose professional role and social position illuminate the
complexities of class in a small provincial community. Gaskell's portrayal of
medical practice in Cranford reveals the tensions between professional expertise and
social acceptability that characterized Victorian provincial life.

## The Surgeon's Position

The medical profession occupied an ambiguous place in the Victorian social
hierarchy. The narrator notes that a gentleman "cannot be a surgeon" [001-069] —
the implication being that surgery, as a manual and sometimes bloody trade, was
beneath gentility. Yet the surgeon was indispensable to the community. This tension
is personified in Mr Hoggins, whose very name — blunt, almost comic — marks him as
socially inferior to the Cranford ladies, even as his medical skills command their
grudging respect.

Mr Hoggins is described as "the widowed sister of the Cranford surgeon" [014-035]
— or rather, through his sister, who serves as a go-between in Cranford's social
network. The surgeon himself remains somewhat at the periphery, his professional
identity keeping him at one remove from the inner circle of Cranford ladies.

## During the Panic

Mr Hoggins's most dramatic appearance occurs during the great Cranford panic, when
he was "attacked at his own house" [021-003] on the "robbery line" — part of the
wave of panic that swept the town. This episode, in which even the steady surgeon
falls victim to the collective hysteria, illustrates how thoroughly the panic
penetrated every level of Cranford society.

## Medical Foreknowledge

The narrator also records that "the surgeons foretold" [005-090] certain medical
outcomes, presenting the surgeon as a figure of authoritative knowledge. This
foreknowledge, however, is tinged with the fatalism characteristic of an era when
medical science could often predict but rarely prevent — a sombre counterpoint to
the novel's prevailing tone of gentle comedy.
