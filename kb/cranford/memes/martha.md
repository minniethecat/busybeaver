# Martha

## Character Overview

Martha is Miss Matilda Jenkyns's household maid in Elizabeth Gaskell's *Cranford* (1853). She is introduced as "blunt and plain-spoken to a fault; otherwise she was a brisk, well-meaning, but very ignorant girl" (006-107). Despite her rough edges, Martha proves to be a faithful and resourceful servant whose loyalty becomes crucial to Miss Matty's survival after the failure of the Town and County Bank.

## Biography

Martha first appears when the narrator, Mary Smith, arrives to stay with Miss Matty and evaluates the household staff. Mary determines that if Martha "were trustworthy," she would instruct her "not to trouble her mistress with every little decision" (006-104). Martha soon demonstrates her competence but also her frank nature, openly telling Miss Matty during tea-service training, "I'll do it as you tell me, ma'am, but I like lads best" (006-144) — a remark that shocks the Cranford ladies.

Martha's most significant storyline concerns her desire for "followers" (suitors). She confesses to Mary Smith that her mistress forbids male visitors, lamenting that "it seems so hard of missus not to let me have any followers; there's such lots of young fellows in the town" (008-158). She names Jem Hearn, "a steady young man, fit for any girl" (008-174), but declares she keeps her word to her mistress despite the temptation. In a moment of quiet grace, Miss Matty — after learning of her own financial ruin — calls Martha back after prayers and grants her permission: "if you meet with such a young man, and tell me, and I find he is respectable, I have no objection to his coming to see you once a week. God forbid that I should grieve any young hearts" (009-071).

When Miss Matty's bank fails and her income evaporates, it is Martha who writes to Mary Smith to report her mistress's decline: she had been "moping" for more than a fortnight (008-150). Martha's vigilance and loyalty are instrumental in bringing Mary back to Cranford to help.

## Role in the Narrative

Martha serves as a representative of the honest, plain-spoken working class in contrast to the decorous gentility of the Cranford ladies. Her frank speech about "lads" and "followers" repeatedly punctures the town's careful social delicacy (006-144). Yet her steadfastness during Miss Matty's financial crisis reveals the novel's broader theme: true worth lies in character rather than social position. Martha's tearful loyalty — "I've given my word, and I'll stick to it" (008-174) — mirrors the novel's moral compass, valuing fidelity over pretense.

After Miss Matty opens a tea shop to support herself, Martha becomes a business partner as well as a servant, embodying the adaptive, pragmatic spirit the novel celebrates.
