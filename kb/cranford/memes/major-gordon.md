# Major Gordon

Major Gordon is a military officer who appears late in the novel as the suitor and
eventual husband of Jessie Brown, Captain Brown's younger daughter. His courtship
of Jessie, conducted with honour and patience, provides one of the novel's few
conventionally happy romantic endings.

## First Appearance

Major Gordon first visits Cranford in the aftermath of Captain Brown's death. Miss
Jenkyns, acting as guardian to the orphaned Brown sisters, receives him. He is
described as "a tall, fine, frank-looking man of forty or upwards" [004-082], a
description that immediately establishes him as a figure of mature integrity. He
had "served in the same regiment with Captain Brown" [004-090], and had known
Jessie Brown when she was "a sweet-looking, blooming girl of eighteen" [004-091].

When he first sees Jessie after her father's death, she cannot meet his eyes — "she
kept them so fixed on the ground" [004-083] — a gesture that conveys both her grief
and her consciousness of the feelings between them.

## Marriage to Jessie Brown

Major Gordon and Jessie Brown eventually married, and she "changed her name and
station" [031-057] to become Mrs Gordon. The marriage is presented as a happy one:
years later, Jessie writes to say "she and Major Gordon expected to be in Cranford
on the fourteenth" [031-058], eager to revisit the town where their love story
began. The couple had children, "now almost grown up" [031-056], and Jessie "had
kept her old kind nature" [031-056] despite her elevation in social rank.

## Significance

Major Gordon's role in the novel is primarily symbolic. His courtship and marriage
to Jessie Brown represents what Miss Jenkyns calls the "silver lining" to the
clouds of Captain Brown's death. The narrator reflects on how the tragedy of
Captain Brown's fatal accident — "dashing down the street after that naughty
child" (his younger daughter) — was nevertheless the catalyst that brought Major
Gordon back into Jessie's life:

> "The poor motherless child who had been the innocent cause of all this
> sorrow, had been the innocent messenger of comfort to the hearts that
> mourned" [004-099]

Major Gordon thus embodies one of Gaskell's central themes: that even the
darkest events may bring unforeseen blessings. His steady, honourable character
stands in contrast to Cranford's more eccentric male figures, presenting an ideal
of quiet masculine virtue.
