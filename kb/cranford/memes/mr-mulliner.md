# Mr Mulliner

Mr Mulliner is the butler to the Honourable Mrs Jamieson, and one of the most
memorable servant characters in Cranford. Though he speaks only in "gruff
monosyllables" [016-128], his presence looms large over the social life of the
town's upper echelon. The Cranford ladies regard him as "an object of great awe"
[016-123], and his imposing, silent manner dominates every encounter at Mrs
Jamieson's house.

## Appearance and Manner

Mr Mulliner is described as having his head covered with hair powder, which
"extended itself over his coat-collar down to his very waist" [016-104]. This
powdered and imposing back was always seen engaged in reading the _St James's
Chronicle_ — a habit that partly explained why the newspaper took so long to
reach the other Cranford ladies who shared the subscription [016-105].

In his most gracious moods, Mr Mulliner "looked like a sulky cockatoo"
[016-128]. His face is described as wooden — "not a muscle of that wooden face
had relaxed" [016-135] — even when Miss Pole ventured a small joke intended for
his amusement. He never forgot "his condescension in coming to live at Cranford"
[016-124], carrying himself with an aristocratic hauteur that surpassed even
that of his mistress.

## Role at Mrs Jamieson's

Mr Mulliner serves as both gatekeeper and enforcer of ceremony. When Mrs
Jamieson sent out invitations for a party, "Mr Mulliner himself brought them
round" [016-018], carrying three small notes in a large basket to "impress his
mistress with an idea of their great weight" [016-022], though they could easily
have fit in his waistcoat pocket. He invariably ignored the existence of back
doors, delivering every note with a louder rat-tat than Mrs Jamieson herself
[016-019].

His sitting-room was located in the front of the house — "in one of them Mr
Mulliner was reported to sit" [016-102] among the kitchens, housekeepers' rooms,
and pantries. From this domain he controlled the newspaper, the bell system, and
the pace of all domestic proceedings.

Mrs Jamieson herself was oddly helpless without him. When guests arrived, she
"looked helplessly beyond us at Mr Mulliner, as if she hoped he would place us
in chairs, for, if he did not, she never could" [016-155].

## The St James's Chronicle Incident

One of the more revealing episodes concerning Mr Mulliner is the delay of the
_St James's Chronicle_. The Cranford ladies shared a subscription, but the
newspaper was invariably late in its rounds. The reason, it was insinuated, was
that Mr Mulliner insisted on reading it first in his pantry [016-105][020-146].
When Mrs Jamieson fidgeted and kept saying "I can't think why Mulliner does not
come" [017-032], Miss Pole suspected that "Mr Mulliner had to finish the _St
James's Chronicle_" [017-030] before attending to his duties.

On this matter, Mr Mulliner "was very impressive, shaking his head" [020-138] as
if the very suggestion of his monopolising the paper was beneath his dignity.

## Loyalty as "Faithful Clansman"

Despite his hauteur, Mr Mulliner was fiercely loyal to his mistress's interests.
When Lady Glenmire married Mr Hoggins, a surgeon and social inferior, Mr
Mulliner "like a faithful clansman, espoused his mistress's side" [020-146],
insinuating that the newspaper delay was somehow connected to the scandal. He
later escorted Mrs Jamieson to Cheltenham [020-150], remaining in her service
through the social upheaval that the marriage caused.

## Significance

Mr Mulliner represents the servant class in Cranford at its most elevated. He is
not merely a functionary but a social presence in his own right — one who
commands as much fear and respect as any of the town's leading ladies. His
character illustrates Gaskell's satirical eye for the hierarchies that exist
_within_ hierarchies: just as the Cranford ladies maintain their own elaborate
social rankings, so Mr Mulliner maintains his own dominion over the pantries,
bells, and newspapers of Mrs Jamieson's establishment.
