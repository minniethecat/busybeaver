## Overview

The regiment is the unnamed military unit that serves as an institutional backdrop for several male characters in *Cranford*. It connects Captain Brown, Major Gordon, and Samuel Brown (Signor Brunoni) — all of whom have regimental ties. While the regiment itself never appears directly in the narrative (no military scenes take place in Cranford), it functions as an invisible masculine institution that periodically deposits men into the Amazonian town, each arrival disrupting Cranford's feminine equilibrium.

## Role in Cranford

Captain Brown's regimental background is mentioned early: he is "accounted for by being with his regiment" [001-063] when absent from Cranford, implying a career of military service that has shaped his decisive, practical character. His "sonorous bass" in church [002-058] and brisk gallantry [002-060] are markers of regimental bearing transposed into civilian life.

Major Gordon served in the "same regiment with Captain Brown" [005-082], creating a web of regimental connection that extends to India — where Peter Jenkyns was also stationed. When "the regiment was ordered to India" [023-137], it sets in motion the events that bring Samuel Brown back to England under the guise of Signor Brunoni.

The regiment thus functions as a narrative connector: it explains how men know each other, how news travels between India and Cranford, and how the masculine world intrudes upon the feminine. It is the institutional counterpart to Cranford's informal female networks of gossip and mutual surveillance.

## Key Characters

- [[captain-brown]] — the regiment's most visible representative in Cranford, whose military bearing shapes his interactions with the ladies
- [[signor-brunoni]] — Samuel Brown, a soldier whose regimental service in India leads to his theatrical reappearance in Cranford
- [[major-gordon]] — a fellow officer who marries Miss Jessie Brown, extending the regimental network into Cranford's domestic sphere
- [[peter-jenkyns]] — though not in the regiment per se, his India posting parallels the regimental trajectory and connects the military-imperial theme
