# The George Inn

The George Inn is Cranford's principal public house and one of the novel's key social spaces beyond the drawing-room. It functions variously as a lodging for travellers, a venue for communal meals, a refuge during the panic, and the stage for Peter Jenkyns's eventual return and reconciliation. Unlike the feminine domestic interiors that dominate Cranford's geography, the George is a more porous, public space — a threshold where the town meets the outside world.

## Description

The George is substantial enough to offer rooms for lodgers and a "great parlour" capable of hosting a luncheon party [019-170, 025-035, 025-036]. It sits under a carriage-way, its entrance a transitional space between the street and the interior [022-021]. Betty, Miss Matty's maid, has "a second-cousin who is chambermaid there" [020-023, 020-024], connecting the inn to the domestic servant network that threads through Cranford.

## Key Events

The George Inn is the setting for several of the novel's most socially charged scenes:

**The Panic Vigil.** During the great Cranford panic over the supposed gang of robbers, Miss Pole's adventure originates at the George — she "chanced to go into the 'George'" to visit Betty's cousin and stumbled into what she believed was evidence of criminal activity, "seeing two men where only one was believed to be" [020-023, 020-024, 020-025, 023-062, 023-063, 023-064]. Mrs Forrester later dismisses Miss Pole's adventure at "the 'George Inn'" as a product of overheated imagination [023-062], but only after the entire town has been thrown into a state of collective terror. During this crisis, Betty is sent to "sleep with her cousin at the 'George'" [024-036, 024-037] while her mistress seeks refuge with Miss Matty.

**Signor Brunoni's Lodgings.** The conjuror Signor Brunoni stays at the George during his visit to Cranford. His carpet-bag is deposited there [035-080, 035-081], and his wife and child are found lodging there — the revelation that transforms the town's fear of a mysterious foreigner into compassion for a sick family.

**The Gordon Luncheon.** After Peter Jenkyns's return, a reconciliatory luncheon is held at the George for Major and Mrs Gordon and their daughter Flora [036-040, 036-041, 036-042]. The "great parlour at the 'George'" [036-066] becomes the setting for the novel's climactic social gathering — a scene of restored community, with "Major and Mrs Gordon and pretty Flora and Mr Ludovic all as bright and handsome and friendly as could be" [036-066, 036-067, 036-068]. Even Mrs Jamieson, who had previously declined to associate with anyone below the rank of Honourable, "was really going to the lunch at the 'George'" [036-038, 036-039] — a sign of the social thaw Peter's return has effected. Miss Matty declares the occasion reminds her of "the Preston Guild" from her youth [036-043, 036-044], linking the inn to Cranford's deepest traditions of communal celebration.

## Symbolic Significance

The George Inn represents the permeable boundary between Cranford and the wider world. It is where strangers lodge, where news arrives, and where the town's carefully maintained social distinctions are temporarily suspended in the name of hospitality. It is fitting that the novel's resolution — the restoration of family, the healing of old wounds, the knitting together of Cranford society — takes place in its great parlour.
