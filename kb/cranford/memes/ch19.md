# Section 19

[019-001] We stopped short at the second front row; I could hardly understand why,

[019-002] until I heard [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] ask a stray waiter if any of the county families

[019-003] were expected; and when he shook his head, and believed not, Mrs

[019-004] Forrester and [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]] moved forwards, and our party represented a

[019-005] conversational square. The front row was soon augmented and enriched by

[019-006] [[lady-glenmire|Lady Glenmire]] and [[mrs-jamieson|Mrs Jamieson]]. We six occupied the two front rows, and

[019-007] our aristocratic seclusion was respected by the groups of shopkeepers

[019-008] who strayed in from time to time and huddled together on the back

[019-009] benches. At least I conjectured so, from the noise they made, and the

[019-010] sonorous bumps they gave in sitting down; but when, in weariness of the

[019-011] obstinate green curtain that would not draw up, but would stare at me

[019-012] with two odd eyes, seen through holes, as in the old tapestry story, I

[019-013] would fain have looked round at the merry chattering people behind me,

[019-014] Miss Pole clutched my arm, and begged me not to turn, for “it was not

[019-015] the thing.” What “the thing” was, I never could find out, but it must

[019-016] have been something eminently dull and tiresome. However, we all sat

[019-017] eyes right, square front, gazing at the tantalising curtain, and hardly

[019-018] speaking intelligibly, we were so afraid of being caught in the

[019-019] vulgarity of making any noise in a place of public amusement. Mrs

[019-020] Jamieson was the most fortunate, for she fell asleep.

[019-021] At length the eyes disappeared—the curtain quivered—one side went up

[019-022] before the other, which stuck fast; it was dropped again, and, with a

[019-023] fresh effort, and a vigorous pull from some unseen hand, it flew up,

[019-024] revealing to our sight a magnificent gentleman in the Turkish costume,

[019-025] seated before a little table, gazing at us (I should have said with the

[019-026] same eyes that I had last seen through the hole in the curtain) with

[019-027] calm and condescending dignity, “like a being of another sphere,” as I

[019-028] heard a sentimental voice ejaculate behind me.

[019-029] “That’s not Signor Brunoni!” said Miss Pole decidedly; and so audibly

[019-030] that I am sure he heard, for he glanced down over his flowing beard at

[019-031] our party with an air of mute reproach. “Signor Brunoni had no

[019-032] beard—but perhaps he’ll come soon.” So she lulled herself into

[019-033] patience. Meanwhile, Miss Matty had reconnoitred through her eye-glass,

[019-034] wiped it, and looked again. Then she turned round, and said to me, in a

[019-035] kind, mild, sorrowful tone—

[019-036] “You see, my dear, turbans _are_ worn.”

[019-037] But we had no time for more conversation. The Grand Turk, as Miss Pole

[019-038] chose to call him, arose and announced himself as Signor Brunoni.

[019-039] “I don’t believe him!” exclaimed Miss Pole, in a defiant manner. He

[019-040] looked at her again, with the same dignified upbraiding in his

[019-041] countenance. “I don’t!” she repeated more positively than ever.

[019-042] “Signor Brunoni had not got that muffy sort of thing about his chin, but

[019-043] looked like a close-shaved Christian gentleman.”

[019-044] Miss Pole’s energetic speeches had the good effect of wakening up Mrs

[019-045] Jamieson, who opened her eyes wide, in sign of the deepest attention—a

[019-046] proceeding which silenced Miss Pole and encouraged the Grand Turk to

[019-047] proceed, which he did in very broken English—so broken that there was no

[019-048] cohesion between the parts of his sentences; a fact which he himself

[019-049] perceived at last, and so left off speaking and proceeded to action.

[019-050] Now we _were_ astonished. How he did his tricks I could not imagine;

[019-051] no, not even when Miss Pole pulled out her pieces of paper and began

[019-052] reading aloud—or at least in a very audible whisper—the separate

[019-053] “receipts” for the most common of his tricks. If ever I saw a man frown

[019-054] and look enraged, I saw the Grand Turk frown at Miss Pole; but, as she

[019-055] said, what could be expected but unchristian looks from a Mussulman? If

[019-056] Miss Pole were sceptical, and more engrossed with her receipts and

[019-057] diagrams than with his tricks, Miss Matty and [[mrs-forrester|Mrs Forrester]] were

[019-058] mystified and perplexed to the highest degree. Mrs Jamieson kept taking

[019-059] her spectacles off and wiping them, as if she thought it was something

[019-060] defective in them which made the legerdemain; and Lady Glenmire, who had

[019-061] seen many curious sights in Edinburgh, was very much struck with the

[019-062] tricks, and would not at all agree with Miss Pole, who declared that

[019-063] anybody could do them with a little practice, and that she would,

[019-064] herself, undertake to do all he did, with two hours given to study the

[019-065] Encyclopædia and make her third finger flexible.

[019-066] At last Miss Matty and Mrs Forrester became perfectly awestricken. They

[019-067] whispered together. I sat just behind them, so I could not help hearing

[019-068] what they were saying. Miss Matty asked Mrs Forrester “if she thought

[019-069] it was quite right to have come to see such things? She could not help

[019-070] fearing they were lending encouragement to something that was not

[019-071] quite”— A little shake of the head filled up the blank. Mrs Forrester

[019-072] replied, that the same thought had crossed her mind; she too was feeling

[019-073] very uncomfortable, it was so very strange. She was quite certain that

[019-074] it was her pocket-handkerchief which was in that loaf just now; and it

[019-075] had been in her own hand not five minutes before. She wondered who had

[019-076] furnished the bread? She was sure it could not be Dakin, because he was

[019-077] the churchwarden. Suddenly Miss Matty half-turned towards me—

[019-078] “Will you look, my dear—you are a stranger in the town, and it won’t

[019-079] give rise to unpleasant reports—will you just look round and see if the

[019-080] rector is here? If he is, I think we may conclude that this wonderful

[019-081] man is sanctioned by the Church, and that will be a great relief to my

[019-082] mind.”

[019-083] I looked, and I saw the tall, thin, dry, dusty rector, sitting

[019-084] surrounded by National School boys, guarded by troops of his own sex

[019-085] from any approach of the many [[cranford-town|Cranford]] [[spinsterhood|spinsters]]. His kind face was all

[019-086] agape with broad smiles, and the boys around him were in chinks of

[019-087] laughing. I told Miss Matty that the Church was smiling approval, which

[019-088] set her mind at ease.

[019-089] [Picture: Afraid of matrimonial reports]

[019-090] I have never named Mr Hayter, the rector, because I, as a well-to-do and

[019-091] happy young woman, never came in contact with him. He was an old

[019-092] bachelor, but as afraid of matrimonial reports getting abroad about him

[019-093] as any girl of eighteen: and he would rush into a shop or dive down an

[019-094] entry, sooner than encounter any of the Cranford ladies in the street;

[019-095] and, as for the Preference parties, I did not wonder at his not

[019-096] accepting invitations to them. To tell the truth, I always suspected

[019-097] Miss Pole of having given very vigorous chase to Mr Hayter when he first

[019-098] came to Cranford; and not the less, because now she appeared to share so

[019-099] vividly in his dread lest her name should ever be coupled with his. He

[019-100] found all his interests among the poor and helpless; he had treated the

[019-101] National School boys this very night to the performance; and virtue was

[019-102] for once its own reward, for they guarded him right and left, and clung

[019-103] round him as if he had been the queen-bee and they the swarm. He felt

[019-104] so safe in their environment that he could even afford to give our party

[019-105] a bow as we filed out. Miss Pole ignored his presence, and pretended to

[019-106] be absorbed in convincing us that we had been cheated, and had not seen

[019-107] Signor Brunoni after all.

[019-108] CHAPTER X.

[019-109] THE PANIC

[019-110] I THINK a series of circumstances dated from Signor Brunoni’s visit to

[019-111] Cranford, which seemed at the time connected in our minds with him,

[019-112] though I don’t know that he had anything really to do with them. All at

[019-113] once all sorts of uncomfortable rumours got afloat in the town. There

[019-114] were one or two [[the-great-cranford-panic|robberies]]—real _bonâ fide_ robberies; men had up before

[019-115] the magistrates and committed for trial—and that seemed to make us all

[019-116] afraid of being robbed; and for a long time, at Miss Matty’s, I know, we

[019-117] used to make a regular expedition all round the kitchens and cellars

[019-118] every night, Miss Matty leading the way, armed with the poker, I

[019-119] following with the hearth-brush, and [[martha|Martha]] carrying the shovel and

[019-120] fire-irons with which to sound the alarm; and by the accidental hitting

[019-121] together of them she often frightened us so much that we bolted

[019-122] ourselves up, all three together, in the back-kitchen, or store-room, or

[019-123] wherever we happened to be, till, when our affright was over, we

[019-124] recollected ourselves and set out afresh with double valiance. By day

[019-125] we heard strange stories from the shopkeepers and cottagers, of carts

[019-126] that went about in the dead of night, drawn by horses shod with felt,

[019-127] and guarded by men in dark clothes, going round the town, no doubt in

[019-128] search of some unwatched house or some unfastened door.

[019-129] Miss Pole, who affected great [[courage|bravery]] herself, was the principal person

[019-130] to collect and arrange these reports so as to make them assume their

[019-131] most fearful aspect. But we discovered that she had begged one of Mr

[019-132] Hoggins’s worn-out hats to hang up in her lobby, and we (at least I) had

[019-133] doubts as to whether she really would enjoy the little adventure of

[019-134] having her house broken into, as she protested she should. Miss Matty

[019-135] made no secret of being an arrant coward, but she went regularly through

[019-136] her housekeeper’s duty of inspection—only the hour for this became

[019-137] earlier and earlier, till at last we went the rounds at half-past six,

[019-138] and Miss Matty adjourned to bed soon after seven, “in order to get the

[019-139] night over the sooner.”

[019-140] Cranford had so long piqued itself on being an honest and moral town

[019-141] that it had grown to fancy itself too genteel and well-bred to be

[019-142] otherwise, and felt the stain upon its character at this time doubly.

[019-143] But we comforted ourselves with the assurance which we gave to each

[019-144] other that the robberies could never have been committed by any Cranford

[019-145] person; it must have been a stranger or strangers who brought this

[019-146] disgrace upon the town, and occasioned as many precautions as if we were

[019-147] living among the Red Indians or the French.

[019-148] This last comparison of our nightly state of defence and fortification

[019-149] was made by Mrs Forrester, whose father had served under General

[019-150] Burgoyne in the American war, and whose husband had fought the French in

[019-151] Spain. She indeed inclined to the idea that, in some way, the French

[019-152] were connected with the small thefts, which were ascertained facts, and

[019-153] the burglaries and highway robberies, which were rumours. She had been

[019-154] deeply impressed with the idea of French spies at some time in her life;

[019-155] and the notion could never be fairly eradicated, but sprang up again

[019-156] from time to time. And now her theory was this:—The Cranford people

[019-157] respected themselves too much, and were too grateful to the [[aristocracy-in-cranford|aristocracy]]

[019-158] who were so kind as to live near the town, ever to disgrace their

[019-159] bringing up by being dishonest or immoral; therefore, we must believe

[019-160] that the robbers were strangers—if strangers, why not foreigners?—if

[019-161] foreigners, who so likely as the French? Signor Brunoni spoke broken

[019-162] English like a Frenchman; and, though he wore a turban like a Turk, Mrs

[019-163] Forrester had seen a print of Madame de Staël with a turban on, and

[019-164] another of Mr Denon in just such a dress as that in which the conjuror

[019-165] had made his appearance, showing clearly that the French, as well as the

[019-166] Turks, wore turbans. There could be no doubt Signor Brunoni was a

[019-167] Frenchman—a French spy come to discover the weak and undefended places

[019-168] of England, and doubtless he had his accomplices. For her part, she,

[019-169] Mrs Forrester, had always had her own opinion of Miss Pole’s adventure

[019-170] at the “[[the-george-inn|George Inn]]”—seeing two men where only one was believed to be.

[019-171] French people had ways and means which, she was thankful to say, the

[019-172] English knew nothing about; and she had never felt quite easy in her

[019-173] mind about going to see that conjuror—it was rather too much like a

[019-174] forbidden thing, though the rector was there. In short, Mrs Forrester

[019-175] grew more excited than we had ever known her before, and, being an

[019-176] officer’s daughter and [[widowhood-in-cranford|widow]], we looked up to her opinion, of course.
