# Love and Courtship

## Definition

Love in *Cranford* operates in the shadows — it is felt deeply, spoken of rarely, and consummated only through extraordinary luck or narrative intervention. Unlike the marriage-plot machinery of conventional Victorian fiction, Cranford's love stories are marked by deferral, secrecy, and loss. The novel's central romance — between Miss Matty and Mr Holbrook — is defined not by its fulfilment but by its long deferral and ultimate impossibility.

## Miss Matty's Love Story

The love between Miss Matty and Mr Holbrook is the novel's quiet heart. Their acquaintance "had grown into love on his part" (005-084) some thirty or forty years before the novel's present, but family objection — Miss Jenkyns's disapproval of a "yeoman" suitor — prevented their marriage. Miss Matty "had shut it up close" (008-111), never speaking of the loss, yet the evidence of her preserved feeling emerges poignantly:

> "He can never know now how I loved him—oh! if I might but tell him" (005-011)

The narrator discovers "yellow bundles of love-letters, sixty or seventy" (009-162) among Miss Matty's treasured possessions — material proof of a love that defined her life but never found expression in marriage. When Mr Holbrook dies suddenly in Paris (009-063), the possibility of reunion is extinguished forever, and Miss Matty's grief is all the more affecting for being entirely private.

## Miss Jessie Brown and Major Gordon

The novel's one conventional love plot — Miss Jessie Brown's marriage to Major Gordon — serves as a counterpoint to Miss Matty's story. Miss Jessie, "always lovely" with eyes "always lovely" (005-117), marries upward after her father's death, becoming Mrs Gordon. Her love for her father ("how I loved him" — 005-005) is presented as the moral basis for her romantic reward, suggesting that filial devotion, rather than romantic passion, is the truest form of love in Cranford's moral economy.

## Love in the Archive

The discovery of family letters provides a second register of love in the novel — love as written, preserved, and re-read:

> "ever-honoured father and my dearly-beloved mother, prior to their marriage, in July 1774" (010-003)

These letters, "interesting to those who loved the writers" (010-132), suggest that love in Cranford's world is fundamentally archival — a matter of paper, memory, and the material traces left behind. The epistolary love of the previous generation mirrors Miss Matty's own preserved love-letters, creating a chain of romantic feeling carried across generations through writing.

## Forms of Love

The novel distinguishes several forms of love, none of them trivial:

1. **Romantic love deferred**: Miss Matty and Mr Holbrook — the love that waits thirty years and is answered only by death
2. **Filial love**: Miss Jessie's devotion to Captain Brown, Miss Matty's loyalty to Miss Jenkyns
3. **Love as community feeling**: The Cranford ladies' affection for one another, expressed through "Christian kindness" (022-081)
4. **Married love**: Represented primarily in letters and retrospect — Mrs Brown's dying words, the brief image of Mrs Gordon's happiness

## Victorian Context

Gaskell's treatment of love reflects the constraints of middle-class Victorian womanhood. For a woman of Miss Matty's station, marriage was the only socially sanctioned form of romantic fulfilment — yet the novel refuses to suggest that Miss Matty's life was wasted because she never married. Instead, it finds value in her quiet endurance, her kindness, and the love she inspired in others. This was a quietly radical position for a novel of 1853.
