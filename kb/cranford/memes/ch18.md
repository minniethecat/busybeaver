# Section 18

[018-001] I was very glad to accept the invitation from my dear [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]],

[018-002] independently of the conjuror, and most particularly anxious to prevent

[018-003] her from disfiguring her small, gentle, mousey face with a great

[018-004] Saracen’s head turban; and accordingly, I bought her a pretty, neat,

[018-005] middle-aged cap, which, however, was rather a disappointment to her

[018-006] when, on my arrival, she followed me into my bedroom, ostensibly to poke

[018-007] the fire, but in reality, I do believe, to see if the sea-green turban

[018-008] was not inside the cap-box with which I had travelled. It was in vain

[018-009] that I twirled the cap round on my hand to exhibit back and side fronts:

[018-010] her heart had been set upon a turban, and all she could do was to say,

[018-011] with resignation in her look and voice—

[018-012] “I am sure you did your best, my dear. It is just like the caps all the

[018-013] ladies in [[cranford-town|Cranford]] are wearing, and they have had theirs for a year, I

[018-014] dare say. I should have liked something newer, I confess—something more

[018-015] like the turbans Miss Betty Barker tells me Queen Adelaide wears; but it

[018-016] is very pretty, my dear. And I dare say lavender will wear better than

[018-017] sea-green. Well, after all, what is dress, that we should care anything

[018-018] about it? You’ll tell me if you want anything, my dear. Here is the

[018-019] bell. I suppose turbans have not got down to Drumble yet?”

[018-020] So saying, the dear old lady gently bemoaned herself out of the room,

[018-021] leaving me to dress for the evening, when, as she informed me, she

[018-022] expected [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] and [[mrs-forrester|Mrs Forrester]], and she hoped I should not feel

[018-023] myself too much tired to join the party. Of course I should not; and I

[018-024] made some haste to unpack and arrange my dress; but, with all my speed,

[018-025] I heard the arrivals and the buzz of conversation in the next room

[018-026] before I was ready. Just as I opened the door, I caught the words, “I

[018-027] was foolish to expect anything very genteel out of the Drumble shops;

[018-028] poor girl! she did her best, I’ve no doubt.” But, for all that, I had

[018-029] rather that she blamed Drumble and me than disfigured herself with a

[018-030] turban.

[018-031] Miss Pole was always the person, in the trio of Cranford ladies now

[018-032] assembled, to have had adventures. She was in the habit of spending the

[018-033] morning in rambling from shop to shop, not to purchase anything (except

[018-034] an occasional reel of cotton or a piece of tape), but to see the new

[018-035] articles and report upon them, and to collect all the stray pieces of

[018-036] intelligence in the town. She had a way, too, of demurely popping

[018-037] hither and thither into all sorts of places to gratify her curiosity on

[018-038] any point—a way which, if she had not looked so very genteel and prim,

[018-039] might have been considered impertinent. And now, by the expressive way

[018-040] in which she cleared her throat, and waited for all minor subjects (such

[018-041] as caps and turbans) to be cleared off the course, we knew she had

[018-042] something very particular to relate, when the due pause came—and I defy

[018-043] any people possessed of common modesty to keep up a conversation long,

[018-044] where one among them sits up aloft in silence, looking down upon all the

[018-045] things they chance to say as trivial and contemptible compared to what

[018-046] they could disclose, if properly entreated. Miss Pole began—

[018-047] “As I was stepping out of Gordon’s shop to-day, I chanced to go into the

[018-048] ‘George’ (my Betty has a second-cousin who is chambermaid there, and I

[018-049] thought Betty would like to hear how she was), and, not seeing anyone

[018-050] about, I strolled up the staircase, and found myself in the passage

[018-051] leading to [[the-assembly-rooms|the Assembly Room]] (you and I remember the Assembly Room, I am

[018-052] sure, Miss Matty! and the minuets de la cour!); so I went on, not

[018-053] thinking of what I was about, when, all at once, I perceived that I was

[018-054] in the middle of the preparations for to-morrow night—the room being

[018-055] divided with great clothes-maids, over which Crosby’s men were tacking

[018-056] red flannel; very dark and odd it seemed; it quite bewildered me, and I

[018-057] was going on behind the screens, in my absence of mind, when a

[018-058] gentleman (quite the gentleman, I can assure you) stepped forwards and

[018-059] asked if I had any business he could arrange for me. He spoke such

[018-060] pretty broken English, I could not help thinking of Thaddeus of Warsaw,

[018-061] and the Hungarian Brothers, and Santo Sebastiani; and while I was busy

[018-062] picturing his past life to myself, he had bowed me out of the room. But

[018-063] wait a minute! You have not heard half my story yet! I was going

[018-064] downstairs, when who should I meet but Betty’s second-cousin. So, of

[018-065] course, I stopped to speak to her for Betty’s sake; and she told me that

[018-066] I had really seen the conjuror—the gentleman who spoke broken English

[018-067] was Signor Brunoni himself. Just at this moment he passed us on the

[018-068] stairs, making such a graceful bow! in reply to which I dropped a

[018-069] curtsey—all foreigners have such polite manners, one catches something

[018-070] of it. But when he had gone downstairs, I bethought me that I had

[018-071] dropped my glove in the Assembly Room (it was safe in my muff all the

[018-072] time, but I never found it till afterwards); so I went back, and, just

[018-073] as I was creeping up the passage left on one side of the great screen

[018-074] that goes nearly across the room, who should I see but the very same

[018-075] gentleman that had met me before, and passed me on the stairs, coming

[018-076] now forwards from the inner part of the room, to which there is no

[018-077] entrance—you remember, Miss Matty—and just repeating, in his pretty

[018-078] broken English, the inquiry if I had any business there—I don’t mean

[018-079] that he put it quite so bluntly, but he seemed very determined that I

[018-080] should not pass the screen—so, of course, I explained about my glove,

[018-081] which, curiously enough, I found at that very moment.”

[018-082] Miss Pole, then, had seen the conjuror—the real, live conjuror! and

[018-083] numerous were the questions we all asked her. “Had he a beard?” “Was

[018-084] he young, or old?” “Fair, or dark?” “Did he look”—(unable to shape my

[018-085] question prudently, I put it in another form)—“How did he look?” In

[018-086] short, Miss Pole was the heroine of the evening, owing to her morning’s

[018-087] encounter. If she was not the rose (that is to say the conjuror) she

[018-088] had been near it.

[018-089] Conjuration, sleight of hand, magic, witchcraft, were the subjects of

[018-090] the evening. Miss Pole was slightly sceptical, and inclined to think

[018-091] there might be a scientific solution found for even the proceedings of

[018-092] the Witch of Endor. Mrs Forrester believed everything, from ghosts to

[018-093] death-watches. Miss Matty ranged between the two—always convinced by

[018-094] the last speaker. I think she was naturally more inclined to Mrs

[018-095] Forrester’s side, but a desire of proving herself a worthy sister to

[018-096] [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]] kept her equally balanced—Miss Jenkyns, who would never

[018-097] allow a servant to call the little rolls of tallow that formed

[018-098] themselves round candles “winding-sheets,” but insisted on their being

[018-099] spoken of as “roley-poleys!” A sister of hers to be superstitious! It

[018-100] would never do.

[018-101] After [[tea-drinking|tea]], I was despatched downstairs into the dining-parlour for that

[018-102] volume of the old Encyclopædia which contained the nouns beginning with

[018-103] C, in order that Miss Pole might prime herself with scientific

[018-104] explanations for the tricks of the following evening. It spoilt the

[018-105] pool at Preference which Miss Matty and Mrs Forrester had been looking

[018-106] forward to, for Miss Pole became so much absorbed in her subject, and

[018-107] the plates by which it was illustrated, that we felt it would be cruel

[018-108] to disturb her otherwise than by one or two well-timed yawns, which I

[018-109] threw in now and then, for I was really touched by the meek way in which

[018-110] the two ladies were bearing their disappointment. But Miss Pole only

[018-111] read the more zealously, imparting to us no more information than this—

[018-112] “Ah! I see; I comprehend perfectly. A represents the ball. Put A

[018-113] between B and D—no! between C and F, and turn the second joint of the

[018-114] third finger of your left hand over the wrist of your right H. Very

[018-115] clear indeed! My dear Mrs Forrester, conjuring and witchcraft is a mere

[018-116] affair of the alphabet. Do let me read you this one passage?”

[018-117] Mrs Forrester implored Miss Pole to spare her, saying, from a child

[018-118] upwards, she never could understand being read aloud to; and I dropped

[018-119] the pack of cards, which I had been shuffling very audibly, and by this

[018-120] discreet movement I obliged Miss Pole to perceive that Preference was to

[018-121] have been the order of the evening, and to propose, rather unwillingly,

[018-122] that the pool should commence. The pleasant brightness that stole over

[018-123] the other two ladies’ faces on this! Miss Matty had one or two twinges

[018-124] of self-reproach for having interrupted Miss Pole in her studies: and

[018-125] did not remember her cards well, or give her full attention to the game,

[018-126] until she had soothed her conscience by offering to lend the volume of

[018-127] the Encyclopædia to Miss Pole, who accepted it thankfully, and said

[018-128] Betty should take it home when she came with the lantern.

[018-129] The next evening we were all in a little gentle flutter at the idea of

[018-130] the gaiety before us. Miss Matty went up to dress betimes, and hurried

[018-131] me until I was ready, when we found we had an hour-and-a-half to wait

[018-132] before the “doors opened at seven precisely.” And we had only twenty

[018-133] yards to go! However, as Miss Matty said, it would not do to get too

[018-134] much absorbed in anything, and forget the time; so she thought we had

[018-135] better sit quietly, without lighting the candles, till five minutes to

[018-136] seven. So Miss Matty dozed, and I knitted.

[018-137] At length we set off; and at the door under the carriage-way at the

[018-138] “George,” we met Mrs Forrester and Miss Pole: the latter was discussing

[018-139] the subject of the evening with more vehemence than ever, and throwing

[018-140] X’s and B’s at our heads like hailstones. She had even copied one or

[018-141] two of the “receipts”—as she called them—for the different tricks, on

[018-142] backs of [[letter-writing|letters]], ready to explain and to detect Signor Brunoni’s arts.

[018-143] We went into the cloak-room adjoining the Assembly Room; Miss Matty gave

[018-144] a sigh or two to her departed youth, and the remembrance of the last

[018-145] time she had been there, as she adjusted her pretty new cap before the

[018-146] strange, quaint old mirror in the cloak-room. The Assembly Room had

[018-147] been added to the inn, about a hundred years before, by the different

[018-148] county families, who met together there once a month during the winter

[018-149] to dance and play at cards. Many a county beauty had first swung

[018-150] through the minuet that she afterwards danced before Queen Charlotte in

[018-151] this very room. It was said that one of the Gunnings had graced the

[018-152] apartment with her beauty; it was certain that a rich and beautiful

[018-153] [[widowhood-in-cranford|widow]], Lady Williams, had here been smitten with the noble figure of a

[018-154] young artist, who was staying with some family in the neighbourhood for

[018-155] professional purposes, and accompanied his patrons to the Cranford

[018-156] Assembly. And a pretty bargain poor Lady Williams had of her handsome

[018-157] husband, if all tales were true. Now, no beauty blushed and dimpled

[018-158] along the sides of the Cranford Assembly Room; no handsome artist won

[018-159] hearts by his bow, _chapeau bras_ in hand; the old room was dingy; the

[018-160] salmon-coloured paint had faded into a drab; great pieces of plaster had

[018-161] chipped off from the fine wreaths and festoons on its walls; but still a

[018-162] mouldy odour of [[aristocracy-in-cranford|aristocracy]] lingered about the place, and a dusty

[018-163] recollection of the days that were gone made Miss Matty and Mrs

[018-164] Forrester bridle up as they entered, and walk mincingly up the room, as

[018-165] if there were a number of genteel observers, instead of two little boys

[018-166] with a stick of toffee between them with which to beguile the time.
