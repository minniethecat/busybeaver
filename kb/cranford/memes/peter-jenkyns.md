# Peter Jenkyns

## Character Overview

Peter Marmaduke Arley Jenkyns is the younger brother of Deborah and Matilda Jenkyns in Elizabeth Gaskell's *Cranford* (1853). Universally referred to by Miss Matty as "poor Peter" (011-037), he is the tragicomic figure whose youthful high spirits collide with his father's rigid authority, resulting in disgrace, exile to India, and an eventual redemptive return that closes the novel on a note of restored joy.

## Biography

Peter was the darling of his mother and the despair of his father, the Rector of Cranford. He was sent to Shrewsbury School, where his letters home were "of a highly mental description, giving an account of his studies, and his intellectual hopes" (011-037). His future was mapped out for him: "He was to win honours at the Shrewsbury School, and carry them thick to Cambridge, and after that, a living awaited him, the gift of his godfather, Sir Peter Arley" (011-073). But "Poor Peter! his lot in life was very different to what his friends had hoped and planned" (011-073).

The defining event of Peter's youth was a prank that went disastrously wrong. He dressed himself in his sister Deborah's "old gown, and shawl, and bonnet" and made a pillow into "a little baby" — purely "to make something to talk about in the town" (011-151). Parading in the garden while cradling his pretend infant, Peter attracted a crowd of townspeople peering through the railings. His father, the Rector, discovered the scene and "seized hold of poor Peter, and tore his clothes off his back — bonnet, shawl, gown, and all — and threw the pillow among the people over the railings: and then he was very, very angry indeed, and before all the people he lifted up his cane and flogged Peter!" (011-186).

The public humiliation drove Peter to run away. He went to sea and eventually to India, where he became "the Aga Jenkyns of Chunderabaddad" (024-033), a figure of exotic mystery to the Cranford ladies. His letters were rare, and his exact fate remained uncertain. Miss Matty never stopped hoping for his return: "the sound always goes past — and Peter never comes" (013-082).

In the novel's closing chapters, Peter finally returns. The narrator learns from him directly that "he had been on the point of returning to England" when he received news of his sister's financial distress, and hurried home (033-001). His return brings comfort, companionship, and restored prosperity to Miss Matty. The Cranford ladies are charmed — "Mr Peter, Miss Matty, and I had all the rest of the day to ourselves" (033-085) — and his presence represents the novel's final resolution of loss into joy.

## Role in the Narrative

Peter Jenkyns embodies the theme of exile and return that structures *Cranford*'s emotional arc. His boyhood prank and subsequent punishment expose the destructive potential of rigid patriarchal authority — the Rector's violent response to a joke destroys his family's happiness for decades. Peter's disappearance casts a long shadow over the Jenkyns household, and Miss Matty's enduring hope for his return is one of the novel's most poignant motifs.

His eventual reappearance is the novel's climactic restorative act. The "poor Peter" who left in disgrace returns as a worldly, wealthy, and kind-hearted man who immediately sets about making his sister comfortable. The novel's final pages — with Peter cheerfully integrated into Cranford society — affirm Gaskell's belief in the possibility of healing and reconciliation.

## Relationships

- **Miss Deborah Jenkyns** — his stern elder sister, the target of his most notorious prank
- **Miss Matty Jenkyns** — his gentle younger sister, who mourned his loss for decades and whose joy at his return crowns the novel
- **Sir Peter Arley** — his godfather, who had planned a clerical living for him
