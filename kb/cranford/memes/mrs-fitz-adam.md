# Mrs Fitz-Adam

## Character Overview

Mrs Fitz-Adam is the widowed sister of the Cranford surgeon in Elizabeth Gaskell's *Cranford* (1853). Born Mary Hoggins, she married a Mr Fitz-Adam and disappeared from Cranford society for many years, only to return as a wealthy widow. Her story illustrates the novel's recurring theme of social mobility and the tension between inherited rank and material wealth.

## Biography

Mrs Fitz-Adam was originally "Miss Mary Hoggins," the sister of Mr Hoggins, the Cranford surgeon (014-046). Her family occupied a distinctly middle-class position in Cranford's social hierarchy — respectable but not genteel. After her marriage to Mr Fitz-Adam, she "disappeared from the neighbourhood for many years" (014-046). The Cranford ladies never knew who Mr Fitz-Adam was: "He died and was gathered to his fathers without our ever having thought about him at all" (014-046).

When Mrs Fitz-Adam reappeared as a widow, she was "home-sick for her own countyman" and "determined to settle again at Cranford" (014-035). However, she returned transformed: she was now wealthy, and this created a social predicament for the old Cranford families. Mrs Jamieson, the town's titled resident, declared that the late Miss Deborah Jenkyns "would never have visited" Mrs Fitz-Adam (014-068), wielding the memory of Cranford's most rigid arbiter of propriety to justify continued exclusion.

Yet Mrs Fitz-Adam's wealth and persistence proved difficult to resist. She was described as "the largest of all" the ladies in the room and "she too was cordial and hearty" (014-103). Eventually, "everybody called upon Mrs Fitz-Adam — everybody but Mrs Jamieson" (014-095). The narrator notes that even Mrs Jamieson "used to show how honourable she was by never seeing Mrs Fitz-Adam when they met" (014-095) — a snub that became awkward as Mrs Fitz-Adam's social standing rose.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs Fitz-Adam embodies the tension between old and new money that pervades *Cranford*. Her transformation from the surgeon's sister into a wealthy widow challenges the Cranford ladies' inherited notions of social hierarchy. The detail that Mr Fitz-Adam's name was "a pretty name, and a very old one" — linked to the reign of King William IV (014-078) — suggests that ancestry could be manufactured or selectively deployed.

Her persistent good nature and refusal to be offended by slights contrast favorably with Mrs Jamieson's cold exclusivity. Mrs Fitz-Adam also appears in minor domestic scenes, such as lending a cookery-book (024-148), confirming her integration into Cranford's daily life.

## Relationships

- **Mr Hoggins** — her brother, the Cranford surgeon, whose social position she transcended through marriage
- **Mrs Jamieson** — the titled lady who persistently refuses to visit her, representing old-money exclusivity
