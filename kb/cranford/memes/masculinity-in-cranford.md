## Definition

In Cranford, masculinity occupies a paradoxical position. The town is "in possession of the Amazons" [001-059], with women holding property, setting social rules, and constituting the public sphere. Men, by contrast, "disappear" from the town [001-061] — they are absent landlords, distant regiment officers, or dead husbands. When men do appear, they are treated as foreign bodies, alternately fascinating and threatening to the established feminine order.

## Manifestations in Cranford

Captain Brown is the novel's primary masculine intrusion. He arrives as "a man and a gentleman" [001-169], and his "masculine gender, and his connection with the obnoxious railroad" [001-172] immediately mark him as an outsider. Yet he "made himself respected in Cranford" [001-186] through "his excellent masculine common sense" [001-201]. His presence introduces vigorous male energy — he reads Boz aloud [002-134], walks into bed-ridden people's rooms unannounced [001-194], and dispenses practical advice like "Get her a flannel waistcoat" [002-015].

Mr Holbrook represents a gentler masculinity. The "old gentleman" [007-123] lives outside Cranford's feminine circuit but embodies a rural gentleman ideal. His courtship of Miss Matty revives a romance thwarted by the Jenkyns rectory's class snobbery — he "would not have been enough of a gentleman for the rector and Miss Jenkyns" [007-024]. His sudden death extinguishes this alternative masculine model.

Signor Brunoni brings exotic masculine spectacle to Cranford: "a magnificent gentleman in the Turkish costume" [019-024] who performs magic. His broken English and theatrical presence temporarily transform Cranford's drawing-room into a space of masculine performance, culminating in the revelation that he is actually Samuel Brown, "quite the gentleman" [018-058] — a returned soldier.

The masculine spectrum in Cranford extends to Peter Jenkyns, whose boyhood "practical joking" [011-101] — a form of masculine rebellion against the rectory's feminine authority — leads to his exile to India, and whose eventual return as a mature man restores the Jenkyns family. Even Mr Hoggins, the "vulgar" surgeon who marries Lady Glenmire, represents a rough masculinity that challenges Cranford's class-based understanding of what makes a "gentleman."

Mrs Jamieson's sister-in-law, described as "masculine-looking — a perfect virago" [020-169], presents the female counterpart: a woman whose masculine traits threaten rather than complement Cranford's social fabric, suggesting that masculinity outside male bodies is perceived as monstrosity rather than asset.
