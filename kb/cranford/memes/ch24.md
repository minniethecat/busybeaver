# Section 24

[024-001] “And you reached Calcutta safely at last?”

[024-002] “Yes, safely! Oh! when I knew I had only two days’ journey more before

[024-003] me, I could not help it, ma’am—it might be idolatry, I cannot tell—but I

[024-004] was near one of the native temples, and I went into it with my baby to

[024-005] thank God for His great mercy; for it seemed to me that where others had

[024-006] prayed before to their God, in their joy or in their agony, was of

[024-007] itself a sacred place. And I got as servant to an invalid lady, who

[024-008] grew quite fond of my baby aboard-ship; and, in two years’ time, Sam

[024-009] earned his discharge, and came home to me, and to our child. Then he

[024-010] had to fix on a trade; but he knew of none; and once, once upon a time,

[024-011] he had learnt some tricks from an Indian juggler; so he set up

[024-012] conjuring, and it answered so well that he took Thomas to help him—as

[024-013] his man, you know, not as another conjuror, though Thomas has set it up

[024-014] now on his own hook. But it has been a great help to us that likeness

[024-015] between the twins, and made a good many tricks go off well that they

[024-016] made up together. And Thomas is a good brother, only he has not the

[024-017] fine carriage of my husband, so that I can’t think how he can be taken

[024-018] for Signor Brunoni himself, as he says he is.”

[024-019] “Poor little Phœbe!” said I, my thoughts going back to the baby she

[024-020] carried all those hundred miles.

[024-021] “Ah! you may say so! I never thought I should have reared her, though,

[024-022] when she fell ill at Chunderabaddad; but that good, kind [[peter-jenkyns|Aga Jenkyns]]

[024-023] took us in, which I believe was the very saving of her.”

[024-024] “Jenkyns!” said I.

[024-025] “Yes, Jenkyns. I shall think all people of that name are kind; for here

[024-026] is that nice old lady who comes every day to take Phœbe a walk!”

[024-027] But an idea had flashed through my head; could the Aga Jenkyns be the

[024-028] lost Peter? True he was reported by many to be dead. But, equally

[024-029] true, some had said that he had arrived at the dignity of Great Lama of

[024-030] Thibet. [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]] thought he was alive. I would make further inquiry.

[024-031] CHAPTER XII.

[024-032] ENGAGED TO BE MARRIED

[024-033] WAS the “poor Peter” of [[cranford-town|Cranford]] the Aga Jenkyns of Chunderabaddad, or

[024-034] was he not? As somebody says, that was the question.

[024-035] In my own home, whenever people had nothing else to do, they blamed me

[024-036] for want of discretion. Indiscretion was my bug-bear fault. Everybody

[024-037] has a bug-bear fault, a sort of standing characteristic—a _pièce de

[024-038] résistance_ for their friends to cut at; and in general they cut and

[024-039] come again. I was tired of being called indiscreet and incautious; and

[024-040] I determined for once to prove myself a model of prudence and wisdom. I

[024-041] would not even hint my suspicions respecting the Aga. I would collect

[024-042] evidence and carry it home to lay before my father, as the family friend

[024-043] of the two Miss Jenkynses.

[024-044] In my search after facts, I was often reminded of a description my

[024-045] father had once given of a ladies’ committee that he had had to preside

[024-046] over. He said he could not help thinking of a passage in Dickens, which

[024-047] spoke of a chorus in which every man took the tune he knew best, and

[024-048] sang it to his own satisfaction. So, at this charitable committee,

[024-049] every lady took the subject uppermost in her mind, and talked about it

[024-050] to her own great contentment, but not much to the advancement of the

[024-051] subject they had met to discuss. But even that committee could have

[024-052] been nothing to the Cranford ladies when I attempted to gain some clear

[024-053] and definite information as to poor Peter’s height, appearance, and when

[024-054] and where he was seen and heard of last. For instance, I remember

[024-055] asking [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] (and I thought the question was very opportune, for I

[024-056] put it when I met her at a call at [[mrs-forrester|Mrs Forrester]]’s, and both the ladies

[024-057] had known Peter, and I imagined that they might refresh each other’s

[024-058] memories)—I asked Miss Pole what was the very last thing they had ever

[024-059] heard about him; and then she named the absurd report to which I have

[024-060] alluded, about his having been elected Great Lama of Thibet; and this

[024-061] was a signal for each lady to go off on her separate idea. Mrs

[024-062] Forrester’s start was made on the veiled prophet in Lalla Rookh—whether

[024-063] I thought he was meant for the Great Lama, though Peter was not so ugly,

[024-064] indeed rather handsome, if he had not been freckled. I was thankful to

[024-065] see her double upon Peter; but, in a moment, the delusive lady was off

[024-066] upon Rowland’s Kalydor, and the merits of cosmetics and hair oils in

[024-067] general, and holding forth so fluently that I turned to listen to Miss

[024-068] Pole, who (through the llamas, the beasts of burden) had got to Peruvian

[024-069] bonds, and the share market, and her poor opinion of joint-stock banks

[024-070] in general, and of that one in particular in which Miss Matty’s money

[024-071] was invested. In vain I put in “When was it—in what year was it that

[024-072] you heard that Mr Peter was the Great Lama?” They only joined issue to

[024-073] dispute whether llamas were carnivorous [[pets-in-cranford|animals]] or not; in which dispute

[024-074] they were not quite on fair grounds, as Mrs Forrester (after they had

[024-075] grown warm and cool again) acknowledged that she always confused

[024-076] carnivorous and graminivorous together, just as she did horizontal and

[024-077] perpendicular; but then she apologised for it very prettily, by saying

[024-078] that in her day the only use people made of four-syllabled words was to

[024-079] teach how they should be spelt.

[024-080] The only fact I gained from this conversation was that certainly Peter

[024-081] had last been heard of in [[india-in-cranford|India]], “or that neighbourhood”; and that this

[024-082] scanty intelligence of his whereabouts had reached Cranford in the year

[024-083] when Miss Pole had bought her Indian muslin gown, long since worn out

[024-084] (we washed it and mended it, and traced its decline and fall into a

[024-085] window-blind before we could go on); and in a year when Wombwell came to

[024-086] Cranford, because Miss Matty had wanted to see an elephant in order that

[024-087] she might the better imagine Peter riding on one; and had seen a

[024-088] boa-constrictor too, which was more than she wished to imagine in her

[024-089] fancy-pictures of Peter’s locality; and in a year when [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]] had

[024-090] learnt some piece of poetry off by heart, and used to say, at all the

[024-091] Cranford parties, how Peter was “surveying mankind from China to Peru,”

[024-092] which everybody had thought very grand, and rather appropriate, because

[024-093] India was between China and Peru, if you took care to turn the globe to

[024-094] the left instead of the right.

[024-095] I suppose all these inquiries of mine, and the consequent curiosity

[024-096] excited in the minds of my friends, made us blind and deaf to what was

[024-097] going on around us. It seemed to me as if the sun rose and shone, and

[024-098] as if the rain rained on Cranford, just as usual, and I did not notice

[024-099] any sign of the times that could be considered as a prognostic of any

[024-100] uncommon event; and, to the best of my belief, not only Miss Matty and

[024-101] Mrs Forrester, but even Miss Pole herself, whom we looked upon as a kind

[024-102] of prophetess, from the knack she had of foreseeing things before they

[024-103] came to pass—although she did not like to disturb her friends by telling

[024-104] them her foreknowledge—even Miss Pole herself was breathless with

[024-105] astonishment when she came to tell us of the astounding piece of news.

[024-106] But I must recover myself; the contemplation of it, even at this

[024-107] distance of time, has taken away my breath and my grammar, and unless I

[024-108] subdue my emotion, my spelling will go too.

[024-109] We were sitting—Miss Matty and I—much as usual, she in the blue chintz

[024-110] easy-chair, with her back to the light, and her knitting in her hand, I

[024-111] reading aloud the _St James’s Chronicle_. A few minutes more, and we

[024-112] should have gone to make the little alterations in dress usual before

[024-113] calling-time (twelve o’clock) in Cranford. I remember the scene and the

[024-114] date well. We had been talking of the signor’s rapid recovery since

[024-115] the warmer weather had set in, and praising [[mr-hoggins|Mr Hoggins]]’s skill, and

[024-116] lamenting his want of refinement and manner (it seems a curious

[024-117] coincidence that this should have been our subject, but so it was), when

[024-118] a knock was heard—a caller’s knock—three distinct taps—and we were

[024-119] flying (that is to say, Miss Matty could not walk very fast, having had

[024-120] a touch of rheumatism) to our rooms, to change cap and collars, when

[024-121] Miss Pole arrested us by calling out, as she came up the stairs, “Don’t

[024-122] go—I can’t wait—it is not twelve, I know—but never mind your dress—I

[024-123] must speak to you.” We did our best to look as if it was not we who had

[024-124] made the hurried movement, the sound of which she had heard; for, of

[024-125] course, we did not like to have it supposed that we had any old clothes

[024-126] that it was convenient to wear out in the “sanctuary of home,” as Miss

[024-127] Jenkyns once prettily called the back parlour, where she was tying up

[024-128] preserves. So we threw our gentility with double force into our

[024-129] manners, and very genteel we were for two minutes while Miss Pole

[024-130] recovered breath, and excited our curiosity strongly by lifting up her

[024-131] hands in amazement, and bringing them down in silence, as if what she

[024-132] had to say was too big for words, and could only be expressed by

[024-133] pantomime.

[024-134] “What do you think, Miss Matty? What _do_ you think? [[lady-glenmire|Lady Glenmire]] is

[024-135] to marry—is to be married, I mean—Lady Glenmire—Mr Hoggins—Mr Hoggins is

[024-136] going to marry Lady Glenmire!”

[024-137] “Marry!” said we. “Marry! Madness!”

[024-138] [Picture: What do you think, Miss Matty]

[024-139] “Marry!” said Miss Pole, with the decision that belonged to her

[024-140] character. “_I_ said marry! as you do; and I also said, ‘What a fool my

[024-141] lady is going to make of herself!’ I could have said ‘Madness!’ but I

[024-142] controlled myself, for it was in a public shop that I heard of it.

[024-143] Where feminine delicacy is gone to, I don’t know! You and I, Miss

[024-144] Matty, would have been ashamed to have known that our marriage was

[024-145] spoken of in a grocer’s shop, in the hearing of shopmen!”

[024-146] “But,” said Miss Matty, sighing as one recovering from a blow, “perhaps

[024-147] it is not true. Perhaps we are doing her injustice.”

[024-148] “No,” said Miss Pole. “I have taken care to ascertain that. I went

[024-149] straight to [[mrs-fitz-adam|Mrs Fitz-Adam]], to borrow a cookery-book which I knew she

[024-150] had; and I introduced my congratulations _à propos_ of the difficulty

[024-151] gentlemen must have in house-keeping; and Mrs Fitz-Adam bridled up, and

[024-152] said that she believed it was true, though how and where I could have

[024-153] heard it she did not know. She said her brother and Lady Glenmire had

[024-154] come to an understanding at last. ‘Understanding!’ such a coarse word!

[024-155] But my lady will have to come down to many a want of refinement. I have

[024-156] reason to believe Mr Hoggins sups on bread-and-cheese and beer every

[024-157] night.

[024-158] “Marry!” said Miss Matty once again. “Well! I never thought of it.

[024-159] Two people that we know going to be married. It’s coming very near!”

[024-160] “So near that my heart stopped beating when I heard of it, while you

[024-161] might have counted twelve,” said Miss Pole.

[024-162] “One does not know whose turn may come next. Here, in Cranford, poor

[024-163] Lady Glenmire might have thought herself safe,” said Miss Matty, with a

[024-164] gentle pity in her tones.

[024-165] “Bah!” said Miss Pole, with a toss of her head. “Don’t you remember

[024-166] poor dear [[captain-brown|Captain Brown]]’s song ‘Tibbie Fowler,’ and the line—
