# Mr Johnson

Mr Johnson is the proprietor of the principal shop in Cranford, a general
merchant whose establishment provides everything from tea and dry goods to the
latest fashions. He is a "civil" and kindly tradesman who plays a significant role
during the financial crisis that threatens Miss Matty's livelihood.

## His Shop and Trade

Mr Johnson's shop appears to be the largest retail establishment in Cranford. It
boasts "young men" assistants who "pivoted themselves over the counter with
surprising activity" [025-024], and an upstairs showroom where he displayed "the
fashions" [025-002]. His inventory was comprehensive — he sold tea "among his
numerous commodities" [029-048], making him the sole competitor to Miss Matty's
later tea-selling venture.

When Miss Matty and her friends visited the shop to see the fashions, the young men
"had on their best looks, and their best cravats" [025-024], eager to impress the
Cranford ladies.

## The Town and County Bank Crisis

Mr Johnson's most consequential appearance occurs during the financial panic
surrounding the Town and County Bank. When a farmer attempted to pay with a note
from that bank, Mr Johnson was forced to refuse it: "from information he had
received, the notes issued by that bank were little better than waste paper"
[025-044]. Despite the awkwardness of the situation, his refusal was described as a
matter of duty — "Mr Johnson is only doing his duty, ma'am" [025-054] — rather than
unkindness.

When Miss Matty nobly exchanged her own gold sovereigns for the farmer's worthless
note, the "civil Mr Johnson was awaiting us" [025-061] downstairs. He had been
informed of the exchange, and "with much good feeling and real kindness, but with a
little want of tact" [025-062], he attempted to address the matter — showing
genuine concern for Miss Matty's welfare, even if his handling of the delicate
situation was somewhat clumsy.

## Kindness to Miss Matty

Mr Johnson's character is most clearly revealed in his dealings with Miss Matty
after her financial ruin. When she took up tea-selling, initially she had
"scruples of conscience at selling tea when there was already Mr Johnson in the
town" [029-048]. But Mr Johnson "kindly put at rest all Miss Matty's scruples and
fear of injuring his business" [029-051], and furthermore "repeatedly sent
customers to her" [029-052] — an act of generosity that transcended commercial
competition.

In Drumble, it was observed, such an arrangement "would have put a stop to all
competition directly" [029-050]; but in Cranford, "it answered very well"
[029-051].

## Significance

Mr Johnson embodies the Cranford ethos of neighbourly cooperation over competitive
self-interest. Though he is a tradesman rather than a gentleman, he demonstrates
the quiet decency and practical kindness that Gaskell presents as the town's
defining virtue. His willingness to support Miss Matty's tea business — at
potential cost to his own — illustrates the Cranford principle that community
welfare outweighs commercial advantage.
