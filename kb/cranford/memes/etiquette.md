# Etiquette

## Definition

Etiquette in *Cranford* is the unwritten but rigidly enforced code of social behaviour that governs every interaction in the town — from the proper hour to pay a morning call to the correct form of address for a person of rank. It is the practical expression of gentility, the set of rules through which the Cranford ladies demonstrate their membership in polite society and police the boundaries of their world.

## Manifestations in Cranford

The novel's treatment of etiquette is both reverent and gently mocking:

- **The etiquettes of address**: The narrator observes the ladies' careful attention to the "etiquettes of address" — the precise forms of greeting, title, and deference that mark social rank (015-104)
- **The etiquettes of high life**: In Cranford, the ladies "studied the etiquettes of high life" with the dedication of scholars — their social knowledge is a form of expertise, however comic its object (015-108)
- **It is not etiquette to go till after twelve**: The rule governing the proper hour for morning calls is stated with the force of natural law. To call before noon is not merely inconvenient — it is a breach of etiquette that reflects on one's breeding (026-043)

The scarcity of direct references to "etiquette" in the novel is itself revealing: the ladies do not need to name the rules they live by. The entire fabric of Cranford social life — the morning calls, the card parties, the careful calculations of who sits where and who pours tea — is etiquette made visible.

## Key Characters

- **Mrs Jamieson** — the arbiter of etiquette by virtue of her rank. Her pronouncements on what is and is not done carry the weight of law in Cranford.
- **Miss Pole** — the town's most enthusiastic student of etiquette, forever interpreting and applying the rules with a mixture of precision and creative improvisation.
- **Miss Matty Jenkyns** — follows etiquette not from social ambition but from a deep-seated sense of propriety inherited from her father and sister. Her adherence to the forms of polite life persists even when she can no longer afford them.

## Narrative Function

Etiquette serves several roles in the novel:

1. **Social map**: The rules of etiquette map Cranford's social hierarchy with precision, telling us at every moment who ranks above whom.
2. **Comic material**: The disproportion between the triviality of the rules and the seriousness with which they are observed generates comedy.
3. **Character revelation**: How a character relates to etiquette — whether they uphold it, flout it, or are confused by it — reveals essential qualities about them.

## Victorian Context

The Victorian period saw an explosion of conduct books and etiquette manuals — from the upper-class authority of *The Habits of Good Society* to the aspiring middle-class guides that promised to teach "the usages of the best society." This literature reflected the era's acute class mobility and the corresponding need for clear markers of belonging. Cranford's ladies, isolated in their rural town with no society to correct them, must rely on memory and study to maintain standards they learned long ago — a position that makes their devotion to etiquette touching as well as comic.
