# Followers

"Followers" is the Cranford euphemism for suitors — the men who call upon, court, or simply admire the town's women. The term appears almost exclusively in relation to servant-girls, and the anxiety it generates reveals much about Cranford's rigid yet precarious social order: a world in which the romantic lives of maidservants become a matter of intense concern for their mistresses, and in which the boundary between proper conduct and social transgression is policed with the same meticulous attention given to candles and tea.

## Followers and Servants

The term "followers" is employed most frequently in connection with the town's servant-maids. In Cranford, "pretty neat servant-maids had their choice of desirable 'followers'" [006-026] — the quotation marks around the word suggesting it belongs to a vocabulary peculiar to Cranford's domestic economy. The mistresses, we are told, possessed "a mysterious dread of men and matrimony" [006-027], fearing that their comely maids might have their heads "turned by the joiner, or the butcher, or the gardener" — men who, by the necessities of their trade, were obliged to call at the house, "and who, as ill-luck would have it, were generally handsome and unmarried" [006-029, 006-030, 006-031].

The prohibition against followers was formalized in the articles of a servant's engagement. Fanny, Miss Matilda's servant, was explicitly "forbidden, by the articles of her engagement, to have 'followers'" [006-035, 006-036]. The rule appears to have been standard throughout Cranford households, a reflection of the peculiar anxiety that the town's female-headed households felt about the intrusion of masculine presence — even at the level of the kitchen.

## Martha's Crisis

The most extended treatment of the followers question comes through Martha, Miss Matty's loyal maid. Martha is tempted by the abundance of eligible young men in Cranford and feels the prohibition as a genuine hardship: "there's such lots of young fellows in the town; and many a one has as much as offered to keep company with me; and I may never be in such a likely place again, and it's like wasting an opportunity" [008-166, 008-167, 008-168].

Martha's predicament is rendered with both sympathy and comedy. She has given her word to her mistress and means to keep it, but cannot help enumerating the practical temptations: "it's such a capable kitchen — there's such dark corners in it — I'd be bound to hide any one" [008-171, 008-172]. Her honesty is genuinely touching: she has already "shut the door in Jem Hearn's face, and he's a steady young man, fit for any girl" [008-174]. The narrator notes "the horror with which both the Miss Jenkynses looked upon 'followers'" [008-177] as the root cause, and acknowledges that in Miss Matty's nervous state, this dread was unlikely to lessen.

## Miss Matty's Resolution

The resolution of the followers question is characteristic of Miss Matty's gentle, humane judgment. Rather than maintaining the absolute prohibition her sister Deborah would have enforced, Miss Matty softens the rule: "I did say you were not to have followers; but if you meet with such a young man, and tell me, and I find he is respectable, I have no objection to his coming to see you once a week" [009-072, 009-073, 009-074]. Her benediction — "God forbid that I should grieve any young hearts" [009-075] — is spoken as if "providing for some distant contingency" [009-076, 009-077], but it marks a quiet revolution in Cranford domestic policy: the acknowledgement that even servants are entitled to love.

## The Irony of Lady Glenmire

The followers motif reaches its comic apotheosis in the case of Lady Glenmire, the aristocratic sister-in-law of Mrs Jamieson. The narrator observes with satisfaction that Lady Glenmire, who had been brought to Cranford partly "to keep off followers from her maids," ended by setting up "a follower of her own" [025-017] — and that follower none other than Mr Hoggins, the surgeon whom Mrs Jamieson had "tabooed as vulgar, and inadmissible to Cranford society" [025-018, 025-019]. The reversal is exquisite: the guardians of social propriety cannot control the romantic impulses even of their own relations.
