# Section 4

[004-001] “She suffers a great deal, and she must suffer more: we do what we can

[004-002] to alleviate her pain;—God’s will be done!” He took off his hat at

[004-003] these last words. I found, from [[miss-matty-jenkyns|Miss Matty]], that everything had been

[004-004] done, in fact. A medical man, of high repute in that country

[004-005] neighbourhood, had been sent for, and every injunction he had given was

[004-006] attended to, regardless of expense. Miss Matty was sure they denied

[004-007] themselves many things in order to make the invalid comfortable; but

[004-008] they never spoke about it; and as for Miss Jessie!—“I really think she’s

[004-009] an angel,” said poor Miss Matty, quite overcome. “To see her way of

[004-010] bearing with Miss Brown’s crossness, and the bright face she puts on

[004-011] after she’s been sitting up a whole night and scolded above half of it,

[004-012] is quite beautiful. Yet she looks as neat and as ready to welcome the

[004-013] Captain at breakfast-time as if she had been asleep in the Queen’s bed

[004-014] all night. My dear! you could never laugh at her prim little curls or

[004-015] her pink bows again if you saw her as I have done.” I could only feel

[004-016] very penitent, and greet Miss Jessie with double respect when I met her

[004-017] next. She looked faded and pinched; and her lips began to quiver, as if

[004-018] she was very weak, when she spoke of her sister. But she brightened,

[004-019] and sent back the tears that were glittering in her pretty eyes, as she

[004-020] said—

[004-021] “But, to be sure, what a town [[cranford-town|Cranford]] is for kindness! I don’t suppose

[004-022] any one has a better dinner than usual cooked but the best part of all

[004-023] comes in a little covered basin for my sister. The poor people will

[004-024] leave their earliest vegetables at our door for her. They speak short

[004-025] and gruff, as if they were ashamed of it: but I am sure it often goes to

[004-026] my heart to see their thoughtfulness.” The tears now came back and

[004-027] overflowed; but after a minute or two she began to scold herself, and

[004-028] ended by going away the same cheerful Miss Jessie as ever.

[004-029] “But why does not this Lord Mauleverer do something for the man who

[004-030] saved his life?” said I.

[004-031] “Why, you see, unless [[captain-brown|Captain Brown]] has some reason for it, he never

[004-032] speaks about being poor; and he walked along by his lordship looking as

[004-033] happy and cheerful as a prince; and as they never called attention to

[004-034] their dinner by apologies, and as Miss Brown was better that day, and

[004-035] all seemed bright, I daresay his lordship never knew how much care there

[004-036] was in the background. He did send game in the winter pretty often, but

[004-037] now he is gone abroad.”

[004-038] I had often occasion to notice the use that was made of fragments and

[004-039] small opportunities in Cranford; the rose-leaves that were gathered ere

[004-040] they fell to make into a potpourri for someone who had no garden; the

[004-041] little bundles of lavender flowers sent to strew the drawers of some

[004-042] town-dweller, or to burn in the chamber of some invalid. Things that

[004-043] many would despise, and actions which it seemed scarcely worth while to

[004-044] perform, were all attended to in Cranford. [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Jenkyns]] stuck an apple

[004-045] full of cloves, to be heated and smell pleasantly in Miss Brown’s room;

[004-046] and as she put in each clove she uttered a Johnsonian sentence. Indeed,

[004-047] she never could think of the Browns without talking Johnson; and, as

[004-048] they were seldom absent from her thoughts just then, I heard many a

[004-049] rolling, three-piled sentence.

[004-050] Captain Brown called one day to thank Miss Jenkyns for many little

[004-051] kindnesses, which I did not know until then that she had rendered. He

[004-052] had suddenly become like an old man; his deep bass voice had a quavering

[004-053] in it, his eyes looked dim, and the lines on his face were deep. He did

[004-054] not—could not—speak cheerfully of his daughter’s state, but he talked

[004-055] with manly, pious resignation, and not much. Twice over he said, “What

[004-056] Jessie has been to us, God only knows!” and after the second time, he

[004-057] got up hastily, shook hands all round without speaking, and left the

[004-058] room.

[004-059] That afternoon we perceived little groups in the street, all listening

[004-060] with faces aghast to some tale or other. Miss Jenkyns wondered what

[004-061] could be the matter for some time before she took the undignified step

[004-062] of sending Jenny out to inquire.

[004-063] Jenny came back with a white face of terror. “Oh, ma’am! Oh, Miss

[004-064] Jenkyns, ma’am! Captain Brown is killed by them nasty cruel railroads!”

[004-065] and she burst into tears. She, along with many others, had experienced

[004-066] the poor Captain’s kindness.

[004-067] “How?—where—where? Good God! Jenny, don’t waste time in crying, but

[004-068] tell us something.” Miss Matty rushed out into the street at once, and

[004-069] collared the man who was telling the tale.

[004-070] [Picture: She brought the affrighted carter ... into the

[004-071] drawing-room]

[004-072] “Come in—come to my sister at once, Miss Jenkyns, the rector’s daughter.

[004-073] Oh, man, man! say it is not true,” she cried, as she brought the

[004-074] affrighted carter, sleeking down his hair, into the drawing-room, where

[004-075] he stood with his wet boots on the new carpet, and no one regarded it.

[004-076] “Please, mum, it is true. I seed it myself,” and he shuddered at the

[004-077] recollection. “The Captain was a-reading some new book as he was deep

[004-078] in, a-waiting for the down train; and there was a little lass as wanted

[004-079] to come to its mammy, and gave its sister the slip, and came toddling

[004-080] across the line. And he looked up sudden, at the sound of the train

[004-081] coming, and seed the child, and he darted on the line and cotched it up,

[004-082] and his foot slipped, and the train came over him in no time. O Lord,

[004-083] Lord! Mum, it’s quite true, and they’ve come over to tell his

[004-084] daughters. The child’s safe, though, with only a bang on its shoulder as

[004-085] he threw it to its mammy. Poor Captain would be glad of that, mum,

[004-086] wouldn’t he? God bless him!” The great rough carter puckered up his

[004-087] manly face, and turned away to hide his tears. I turned to Miss

[004-088] Jenkyns. She looked very ill, as if she were going to faint, and signed

[004-089] to me to open the window.

[004-090] “Matilda, bring me my bonnet. I must go to those girls. God pardon me,

[004-091] if ever I have spoken contemptuously to the Captain!”

[004-092] Miss Jenkyns arrayed herself to go out, telling Miss Matilda to give the

[004-093] man a glass of wine. While she was away, Miss Matty and I huddled over

[004-094] the fire, talking in a low and awestruck voice. I know we cried quietly

[004-095] all the time.

[004-096] Miss Jenkyns came home in a silent mood, and we durst not ask her many

[004-097] questions. She told us that Miss Jessie had fainted, and that she and

[004-098] [[miss-pole|Miss Pole]] had had some difficulty in bringing her round; but that, as

[004-099] soon as she recovered, she begged one of them to go and sit with her

[004-100] sister.

[004-101] “[[mr-hoggins|Mr Hoggins]] says she cannot live many days, and she shall be spared this

[004-102] shock,” said Miss Jessie, shivering with feelings to which she dared not

[004-103] give way.

[004-104] “But how can you manage, my dear?” asked Miss Jenkyns; “you cannot bear

[004-105] up, she must see your tears.”

[004-106] “God will help me—I will not give way—she was asleep when the news came;

[004-107] she may be asleep yet. She would be so utterly miserable, not merely at

[004-108] my father’s death, but to think of what would become of me; she is so

[004-109] good to me.” She looked up earnestly in their faces with her soft true

[004-110] eyes, and Miss Pole told Miss Jenkyns afterwards she could hardly bear

[004-111] it, knowing, as she did, how Miss Brown treated her sister.

[004-112] However, it was settled according to Miss Jessie’s wish. Miss Brown was

[004-113] to be told her father had been summoned to take a short journey on

[004-114] railway business. They had managed it in some way—Miss Jenkyns could

[004-115] not exactly say how. Miss Pole was to stop with Miss Jessie. Mrs

[004-116] Jamieson had sent to inquire. And this was all we heard that night; and

[004-117] a sorrowful night it was. The next day a full account of the fatal

[004-118] accident was in the county paper which Miss Jenkyns took in. Her eyes

[004-119] were very weak, she said, and she asked me to read it. When I came to

[004-120] the “gallant gentleman was deeply engaged in the perusal of a number of

[004-121] ‘Pickwick,’ which he had just received,” Miss Jenkyns shook her head

[004-122] long and solemnly, and then sighed out, “Poor, dear, infatuated man!”

[004-123] The corpse was to be taken from the station to the parish church, there

[004-124] to be interred. Miss Jessie had set her heart on following it to the

[004-125] grave; and no dissuasives could alter her resolve. Her restraint upon

[004-126] herself made her almost obstinate; she resisted all Miss Pole’s

[004-127] entreaties and Miss Jenkyns’ advice. At last Miss Jenkyns gave up the

[004-128] point; and after a silence, which I feared portended some deep

[004-129] displeasure against Miss Jessie, Miss Jenkyns said she should accompany

[004-130] the latter to the funeral.

[004-131] “It is not fit for you to go alone. It would be against both propriety

[004-132] and humanity were I to allow it.”

[004-133] Miss Jessie seemed as if she did not half like this arrangement; but her

[004-134] obstinacy, if she had any, had been exhausted in her determination to go

[004-135] to the interment. She longed, poor thing, I have no doubt, to cry alone

[004-136] over the grave of the dear father to whom she had been all in all, and

[004-137] to give way, for one little half-hour, uninterrupted by sympathy and

[004-138] unobserved by friendship. But it was not to be. That afternoon Miss

[004-139] Jenkyns sent out for a yard of black crape, and employed herself busily

[004-140] in trimming the little black silk bonnet I have spoken about. When it

[004-141] was finished she put it on, and looked at us for approbation—admiration

[004-142] she despised. I was full of sorrow, but, by one of those whimsical

[004-143] thoughts which come unbidden into our heads, in times of deepest grief,

[004-144] I no sooner saw the bonnet than I was reminded of a helmet; and in that

[004-145] hybrid bonnet, half helmet, half jockey-cap, did Miss Jenkyns attend

[004-146] Captain Brown’s funeral, and, I believe, supported Miss Jessie with a

[004-147] tender, indulgent firmness which was invaluable, allowing her to weep

[004-148] her passionate fill before they left.

[004-149] Miss Pole, Miss Matty, and I, meanwhile attended to Miss Brown: and hard

[004-150] work we found it to relieve her querulous and never-ending complaints.

[004-151] But if we were so weary and dispirited, what must Miss Jessie have been!

[004-152] Yet she came back almost calm as if she had gained a new strength. She

[004-153] put off her mourning dress, and came in, looking pale and gentle,

[004-154] thanking us each with a soft long pressure of the hand. She could even

[004-155] smile—a faint, sweet, wintry smile—as if to reassure us of her power to

[004-156] endure; but her look made our eyes fill suddenly with tears, more than

[004-157] if she had cried outright.

[004-158] It was settled that Miss Pole was to remain with her all the watching

[004-159] livelong night; and that Miss Matty and I were to return in the morning

[004-160] to relieve them, and give Miss Jessie the opportunity for a few hours of

[004-161] sleep. But when the morning came, Miss Jenkyns appeared at the

[004-162] breakfast-table, equipped in her helmet-bonnet, and ordered Miss Matty

[004-163] to stay at home, as she meant to go and help to nurse. She was

[004-164] evidently in a state of great friendly excitement, which she showed by

[004-165] eating her breakfast standing, and scolding the household all round.

[004-166] No nursing—no energetic strong-minded woman could help Miss Brown now.

[004-167] There was that in the room as we entered which was stronger than us all,

[004-168] and made us shrink into solemn awestruck helplessness. Miss Brown was

[004-169] dying. We hardly knew her voice, it was so devoid of the complaining

[004-170] tone we had always associated with it. Miss Jessie told me afterwards

[004-171] that it, and her face too, were just what they had been formerly, when

[004-172] her mother’s death left her the young anxious head of the family, of

[004-173] whom only Miss Jessie survived.
