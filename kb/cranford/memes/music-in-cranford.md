# Music in Cranford

Music occupies an ambiguous place in Cranford society — it is both one of the
polite accomplishments expected of a gentlewoman and, when performed by outsiders
like Signor Brunoni, a source of exotic entertainment and mild scandal. The novel
uses musical performance as a lens through which to examine Cranford's complicated
relationship with art, class, and the world beyond its borders.

## Genteel Accomplishment

At the upper end of Cranford society, musical ability is a mark of refinement. Miss
Jenkyns is described as "musical" [002-108], and she "beat time" [002-108] with
authority during performances. When company gathered, "Miss Jenkyns proposed music"
[002-122] as the proper evening entertainment — a genteel alternative to cards or
gossip. The ability to play an instrument or sing was one of the accomplishments
that distinguished a lady, though the novel treats such displays with a characteristically
gentle irony.

The narrator recalls "the music of some rhyme" [008-063] that characters would
recite or sing — domestic, unpretentious performances that contrast sharply with
the professional entertainments that arrive later in the novel.

## Signor Brunoni's Performances

The most sustained musical episode occurs with the arrival of Signor Brunoni, the
itinerant magician who "was going to exhibit his wondrous arts" [017-180] in
Cranford. While Brunoni is primarily a conjuror, his performances have a musical
dimension that captivates Cranford. The Angel Hotel hosted "the Brunonian meals"
[003-125] and entertainments, bringing a rare touch of theatrical spectacle to the
quiet town.

The ladies' attempt "to detect Signor Brunoni's arts" [018-142] reveals their
ambivalence toward professional entertainment — fascinated but also suspicious,
eager for novelty but protective of their own discernment.

## The Nightingale and the Musician

In one of the novel's few explicit meditations on music and art, Gaskell invokes
the classical fable of "the nightingale and the musician, who strove one against
the other" to "produce the most admirable music, till poor Philomel dropped dead"
[020-050][020-051]. This tragic image — art pushed to the point of death — stands
as a warning against the competitive and commercial spirit that Cranford's amateur
tradition implicitly rejects.
