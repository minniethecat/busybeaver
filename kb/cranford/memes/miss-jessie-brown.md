# Miss Jessie Brown

Miss Jessie Brown is the younger daughter of Captain Brown. She is introduced
early in *Cranford* and her character provides warmth, music, and romantic hope
in a novel otherwise dominated by ageing spinsters. Her marriage to Major Gordon
at the novel's end provides one of the story's few unambiguous happy endings.

## Appearance and Personality

Miss Jessie was "ten years younger than her sister, and twenty shades prettier"
[002-035]. Her face was "round and dimpled" [002-035], with "large blue
wondering eyes, looking straight at you" [002-041]. Her nose was unformed and
snub, and her lips were "red and dewy" [002-042]. She wore her hair in "little
rows of curls" that heightened her childlike appearance [002-043].

Miss Jenkyns, in a passion against Captain Brown, once declared "that she
thought it was time for Miss Jessie to leave off her dimples, and not always to
be trying to look like a child" [002-038]. But the narrator reflects that "there
was something childlike in her face; and there will be, I think, till she dies,
though she should live to a hundred" [002-040].

She had "something of her father's jauntiness of gait and manner" [002-046], and
a feminine observer might detect that her attire cost "about two pounds per
annum more than Miss Brown's" [002-048] — a significant sum in Captain Brown's
modest budget that hinted at a certain vanity or perhaps simply a father's
indulgence of his prettier daughter.

## Musical Talent

Miss Jessie possessed a lively talent for music. Unlike her sister, she "could
not play" the elderly piano — "which I think had been a spinet in its youth"
[002-106] — but she _could_ sing, and her singing was a social asset. After her
father's death, her singing becomes associated with her courtship by Major
Gordon.

## The "No Tact" Incident

Despite her charm, Miss Jessie was not without fault. The narrator notes that
"Miss Jessie Brown (who had no tact, as we all agreed the next day)" [002-117]
committed a social blunder at Mrs Jamieson's card party. She had somehow
irritated the formidable Mrs Jamieson, who was heard to remark that Miss Jessie
was merely a "shop-keeper's niece" [002-117] — a cutting reference to Captain
Brown's humble origins. This moment reveals the class prejudices that lurk
beneath Cranford's genteel surface, and Miss Jessie's inability to navigate them
marks her as an outsider despite her pretty face.

## After Captain Brown's Death

The death of Captain Brown is a turning point for both sisters. Miss Jessie,
despite her apparent frivolity, bore the loss with quiet dignity. Miss Pole and
Miss Jessie "had set up a kind of intimacy" [003-047] during this period,
suggesting that the younger Miss Brown found unexpected supporters among the
Cranford ladies.

## Marriage to Major Gordon

Miss Jessie's romantic fate is one of the satisfying resolutions of the novel.
Major Gordon, a military officer who had served with Captain Brown in India,
fell in love with her. The narrator later refers to "darling Flora Gordon (Miss
Jessie Brown's child)" [033-014], confirming that the marriage was fruitful and
happy. Her transformation from the dimpled, tactless girl into Mrs Gordon
represents one of Cranford's few successful transitions from youth to maturity.

## Significance

Miss Jessie Brown serves as a foil to the Cranford spinsters. Where they are
ageing, she is youthful; where they are cautious, she is spontaneous; where they
guard their speech, she has "no tact." Her marriage to Major Gordon — like Lady
Glenmire's marriage to Mr Hoggins — demonstrates that love can cross the rigid
social boundaries of Cranford society, though the cost of such crossings is
frequently a departure from the town itself.
