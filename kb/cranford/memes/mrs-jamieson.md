# The Honourable Mrs Jamieson

**The Honourable Mrs Jamieson** is the highest-ranking figure in the social world of [[cranford-town|Cranford]], a widow whose courtesy title — she was the sister-in-law of a baron — places her above every other resident. Her presence governs the rhythms of the town's visiting and dining customs, and her approval is the ultimate social currency. Gaskell treats her with a characteristic blend of irony and affection: she is at once the apex of Cranford gentility and a woman who, at evening parties, quietly falls asleep.

## Rank and Title

Mrs Jamieson's social standing derives from her connection to the peerage: she was sister-in-law to the late Lord Glenmire, and as such bore the honorific "the Honourable." This title set her decisively apart in a town where the [[genteel-poverty|economic means]] of the residents were shrinking but their consciousness of rank remained acute. The narrator introduces her early in the novel as the sponsor of a card party — "the Honourable Mrs Jamieson gave" — and the very act of her giving was itself a marker of the party's importance (001-158).

> The Honourable Mrs Jamieson has only just quitted me; and I thought the best thing I could do was to come and tell you (003-110).

The other ladies of Cranford treated her with elaborate deference. Mrs Forrester "invariably used to stand up when Mrs Jamieson came in, and curtsey very low" (014-099). The height of Mrs Jamieson's social insulation is captured in her treatment of Mrs Fitz-Adam — a woman whose late husband had "been in trade" and was therefore "not a lady" by the standards Mrs Jamieson upheld (014-095).

## A Presence Marked by Absence

Mrs Jamieson appears in the novel as much through others' speech as through her own actions. She is discussed, deferred to, and invoked as an authority, but her own contributions to Cranford's social life are often minimal. At Miss Barker's party, she sits in the seat of honour and then — famously — falls asleep:

> I should have said Mrs Jamieson did so then; for, as sure as I am sitting here, she was nodding (014-169).

This moment of human frailty at the pinnacle of the hierarchy is characteristic of Gaskell's method. Mrs Jamieson's dignity is real, but so is her sleepiness. The comedy does not undercut her status; it humanises it.

## Lady Glenmire and the Family Connection

Mrs Jamieson's world is disrupted — and subtly democratised — by the arrival of her sister-in-law, Lady Glenmire, a woman of genuine title who proves far less rigid than Mrs Jamieson herself. When Lady Glenmire marries the country surgeon Mr Hoggins, the social logic that Mrs Jamieson had enforced collapses. The marriage is a pivotal moment in the novel, demonstrating that Cranford's hierarchy — so carefully maintained by figures like [[miss-jenkyns-deborah|Miss Deborah Jenkyns]] and Mrs Jamieson — is ultimately permeable.
