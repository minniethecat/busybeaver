# John Knox

John Knox (1514–1572) appears in *Sulla Libertà* as the personification of the Calvinist ideal that Mill subjects to sustained critique. Mill's assessment is characteristically nuanced — he does not deny Knox's virtues but questions whether the Knoxian model of human character represents the highest form of human development.

## Vita e contesto storico

John Knox was the leading figure of the Scottish Reformation and the founder of Presbyterianism. A student of John Calvin in Geneva, Knox returned to Scotland to lead the Protestant revolution against the Catholic regency. His fervent preaching, political activism, and *History of the Reformation in Scotland* established him as one of the most influential figures in Scottish history.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Mill uses Knox to illustrate his argument about competing ideals of human character:

**Knox vs. Alcibiades vs. Pericles**: In a striking passage, Mill compares three types: John Knox (the Christian ascetic), Alcibiades (the pagan libertine), and Pericles (the balanced ideal):

> "Può sembrar preferibile essere Giovanni Knox ad Alcibiade; ma vale ancora meglio essere Pericle, che l'uno o l'altro; e un Pericle, s'esistesse oggidì, non sarebbe privo di qualcuna delle buone qualità che appartenevano a Giovanni Knox." — *Sulla Libertà*, (003-015)

The point is not that Knox lacks virtues — he has genuine qualities — but that a fully developed human being integrates pagan self-development (*Selbstentwicklung*) with Christian self-mastery (*Selbstbeherrschung*). Knox represents only half the ideal.

**The Calvinist ideal critiqued**: Mill uses Knox's tradition to illustrate "la teoria calvinista" — the doctrine that "la colpa capitale dell'uomo è di avere una volontà indipendente" (the capital crime of man is having an independent will). Against this self-suppressing ideal, Mill posits the Humboldtian vision of harmonious development of all faculties.
