# Alcibiade

Alcibiades (c. 450–404 BCE) appears in *Sulla Libertà* only once, but in a crucial comparative passage. Mill uses him as the extreme case of pagan self-development without self-restraint — brilliant, charismatic, and utterly unprincipled.

## Vita e contesto storico

Alcibiades was an Athenian statesman, orator, and general during the Peloponnesian War. Famed for his beauty, intelligence, and ambition, he switched allegiances between Athens, Sparta, and Persia with opportunistic brilliance. He was a student of Socrates — a fact that makes his moral failings particularly significant for Mill's argument.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Alcibiades serves as one point in Mill's three-way comparison of human ideals:

> "Può sembrar preferibile essere Giovanni Knox ad Alcibiade; ma vale ancora meglio essere Pericle, che l'uno o l'altro." — *Sulla Libertà*, (003-015)

The comparison reveals Mill's nuanced moral psychology:
- **Alcibiades** = full pagan *Selbstentwicklung* (self-development) without Christian *Selbstbeherrschung* (self-mastery) — energy without discipline
- **John Knox** = Christian self-mastery without pagan self-development — discipline without fullness
- **Pericles** = the synthesis — a fully developed human being who integrates both ideals

Alcibiades represents the danger of spontaneity without moral constraint. But Mill's point is not to reject spontaneity — it is to reject *one-sided* development. The ideal is balance, not suppression.
