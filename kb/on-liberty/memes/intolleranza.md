# Intolleranza

*Intolleranza* (intolerance) is, for Mill, the **default human disposition** — the natural tendency to impose one's own opinions and preferences on others. Liberty is the artificial achievement that must constantly combat this natural intolerance.

## Definizione

Mill locates the roots of intolerance in human psychology rather than political institutions. Intolerance arises wherever people genuinely care about something — it is the impulse to suppress what one deeply disapproves of:

> "È così naturale alla specie umana l'intolleranza per tutto quello che veramente le preme, che la libertà religiosa non fu attuata quasi in nessun luogo, salvo là dove l'indifferenza, che non ama di vedersi turbata nella sua pace da dispute teologiche, ha fatto sentire il suo peso sulla bilancia." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-014)

This is a devastating observation: religious liberty succeeded not because people embraced the principle of toleration but because they stopped caring enough to persecute. Where belief is still sincere and intense, the majority has not renounced its claim to be obeyed (001-015).

## Ruolo nell'argomentazione di Mill

Intolerance is the **psychological obstacle** that liberty must overcome:

**The religious paradigm**: Mill uses religious intolerance as his primary case study. The *odium theologicum* — the hatred that theological disagreement generates — is "uno dei casi più sicuri del sentimento morale" (one of the most certain cases of moral sentiment). The sincere bigot genuinely feels that persecution is a moral duty.

**The shallow toleration problem**: Mill exposes that most "toleration" is selective — people tolerate what they don't care about and persecute what they do. One tolerates dissent on church government but not on dogma; another tolerates everyone except papists or unitarians; a few go further but stop at belief in God and a future life:

> "Nello spirito di quasi tutte le persone di fede, anche nei paesi più tolleranti, il diritto non è ammesso senza tacite riserve." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-015)

**Intolerance as self-deception**: Mill's psychological analysis reveals that people do not recognize their intolerance as such. They believe their feelings on such subjects are better than reasons and render reasons unnecessary:

> "Si è avvezzi a credere... che i nostri sentimenti su soggetti di tal natura valgano meglio di ragioni e rendano queste inutili." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-010)

**The modern form**: While legal persecution has diminished, social intolerance persists. Mill warns against "la forte e crescente inclinazione ad estendere esageratamente il potere della società sull'individuo" (the strong and growing inclination to extend society's power over the individual excessively).
