# Socrate

Socrates (470–399 BCE) is the **paradigmatic martyr for free thought** in Mill's *Sulla Libertà*. Mill opens Chapter II's defense of liberty of thought and discussion by invoking Socrates' trial and execution — the Athenian philosopher condemned to death for "corrupting the youth" and "impiety." For Mill, Socrates proves that no age is immune to the suppression of truth.

## Vita e contesto storico

Socrates was an Athenian philosopher who transformed Western philosophy by turning its attention from natural philosophy to ethics and the examination of human life. He wrote nothing; his teachings survive through the dialogues of his students Plato and Xenophon. In 399 BCE, he was tried by an Athenian jury, convicted of impiety and corrupting the youth, and sentenced to death by hemlock. He accepted the verdict rather than flee, becoming the foundational martyr of Western philosophy.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Mill deploys Socrates as the **opening exhibit** in his case for freedom of thought. The argument unfolds in several layers:

**The most virtuous man killed by the most civilized city**: Mill emphasizes the contrast — Socrates was not killed by barbarians but by the Athenians, the most enlightened people of antiquity. This proves that no society, however advanced, is immune to the suppression of truth:

> "If ever there was a case of a man being put to death by his countrymen in a manner that must shock all ages and nations with the barbarity and injustice of the act, it was the case of Socrates." — *Sulla Libertà*, (002-005)

**The crime of challenging authority**: Socrates' real crime, Mill argues, was not impiety but intellectual independence — he questioned received opinions and exposed the ignorance of those who claimed to know. The charges of corrupting youth and denying the gods were pretexts for suppressing an inconvenient truth-teller.

**The historical pattern**: Socrates is not a unique case. Mill uses him alongside Jesus Christ to establish a pattern — those who challenge established opinion are persecuted in every age, and later ages recognize the persecutors as wrong. This pattern underwrites the fallibilist argument: if we cannot trust past ages to correctly identify truth, why should we trust our own?

**The application to modern times**: Mill's use of Socrates is not merely historical. The implicit argument is that if even Athens — the most intellectually free society of antiquity — could kill its wisest citizen for his opinions, then no contemporary society can claim to be beyond similar error.

## Opere principali

Socrates wrote nothing. His philosophy comes to us through:
- Plato's dialogues (especially *Apology*, *Crito*, *Phaedo*)
- Xenophon's *Memorabilia* and *Apology*
- Aristophanes' satirical portrayal in *The Clouds*
