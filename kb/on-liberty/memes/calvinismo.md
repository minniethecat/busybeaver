# Calvinismo

Calvinism occupies a unique position in *Sulla Libertà* as the **theological antagonist** of Mill's ideal of human development. Mill singles out Calvinist doctrine not because it is uniquely powerful but because it articulates in pure form the impulse to suppress individuality that he sees pervading modern society.

## Natura e contesto storico

Calvinism emerged from John Calvin's Reformation in Geneva (1536 onward). Its doctrines — total depravity, unconditional election, limited atonement — emphasized human corruption and divine sovereignty. Calvin's Geneva was a theocracy where moral conduct was enforced by church and state alike. The Scottish Reformation under John Knox spread Calvinism throughout Scotland and, through Puritanism, to England and America.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Mill presents Calvinism as the **limit case** of the anti-individuality impulse:

**The Calvinist ideal**: Mill distills Calvinist anthropology to its essence: "la colpa capitale dell'uomo è di avere una volontà indipendente; tutto il bene di cui l'umanità è capace è compreso nell'obbedienza" (the capital crime of man is to have an independent will; all the good of which humanity is capable consists in obedience).

Against this, Mill posits a competing ideal — the harmonious development of all human faculties, combining pagan *Selbstentwicklung* (self-development) with Christian *Selbstbeherrschung* (self-mastery). Pericles, not John Knox, is the model of human excellence.

**Beyond Calvinism**: Mill's point is not merely to critique Calvinism but to show that the Calvinist impulse — the desire to suppress spontaneity and enforce conformity — pervades modern society even among those who reject Calvinist theology. The "narrow theory of life" persists "sotto una tal forma insidiosa" (in insidious form) long after its theological foundations have eroded.
