# Tolleranza

*Tolleranza* (toleration) occupies a pivotal position in Mill's historical narrative of liberty. He traces how religious toleration emerged not from principled commitment but from the exhaustion of religious warfare — and argues that this contingent origin points toward a deeper philosophical foundation.

## Definizione

Mill distinguishes between toleration as a **pragmatic settlement** and toleration as a **principled commitment**. Historically, religious liberty arose when no single church or sect could dominate — minorities, seeing they could not become majorities, were forced to advocate for freedom of dissent:

> "Le minoranze, vedendo che esse non avevano probabilità di mutarsi in maggioranze, furono costrette a sostenere la libera dissidenza religiosa in confronto di quelli che non potevano convertire." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-014)

But Mill is critical of the shallowness of most "toleration" — it is often toleration of everything *except what one deeply cares about*:

> "Nello spirito di quasi tutte le persone di fede, anche nei paesi più tolleranti, il diritto non è ammesso senza tacite riserve. Una persona lascierà dire i dissidenti in materia di governo ecclesiastico, ma non in materia di dogma." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-015)

## Ruolo nell'argomentazione di Mill

Toleration serves as both a **historical precedent** and a **logical extension** point:

**The historical lesson**: Religious toleration is Mill's primary historical example of how liberty claims were first established. It is "quasi solo su questo campo di battaglia che i diritti dell'individuo contro la società sono stati rivendicati sulla base di principî bene stabiliti" (almost exclusively on this battlefield that individual rights against society have been defended on well-established principles).

**The incompleteness of existing toleration**: Mill argues that the principle of toleration, having been won for religion, must now be extended to all domains of thought and conduct. The logic that protects religious dissent must also protect political, moral, and personal dissent.

**From toleration to liberty**: Mill's deeper ambition is to move beyond "toleration" — which implies a superior party graciously permitting dissent — to "liberty" as a right. Toleration is a stepping stone, not the destination.

**The limits of toleration**: Mill does not advocate unlimited toleration. The harm principle applies: toleration ends where conduct harms others. But mere offense, even deep moral offense, does not justify suppression.

## Citazioni chiave

> "È così naturale alla specie umana l'intolleranza per tutto quello che veramente le preme, che la libertà religiosa non fu attuata quasi in nessun luogo, salvo là dove l'indifferenza, che non ama di vedersi turbata nella sua pace da dispute teologiche, ha fatto sentire il suo peso sulla bilancia." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-014)
