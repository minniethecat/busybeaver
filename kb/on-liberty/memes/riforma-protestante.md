# Riforma protestante

The Protestant Reformation (1517–1648) represents, in Mill's historical narrative, the **first great victory** for individual conscience against institutional authority — though a deeply imperfect and incomplete one.

## Contesto storico

Beginning with Martin Luther's 95 Theses (1517), the Reformation shattered the religious unity of Western Christendom. What began as a theological dispute about indulgences became a comprehensive challenge to papal authority that transformed European politics, culture, and thought. The Reformation produced not only new churches but new conceptions of the relationship between individual belief and institutional power.

## Il ruolo nell'argomentazione di Mill

The Reformation is a complex presence in Mill's argument:

**The origin of religious liberty**: Mill traces modern religious liberty to the exhaustion of Reformation-era religious warfare. Religious toleration emerged not from principle but from stalemate — "quando fu sbollito l'ardore della lotta senza dare completa vittoria ad alcun partito" (when the heat of battle cooled without giving complete victory to any party). Minorities, unable to become majorities, were forced to advocate for freedom of dissent.

**The incomplete revolution**: Mill's assessment is characteristically double-edged. The Reformation broke the authority of the universal Church, but it did not establish the principle of individual liberty. The reformers quickly became persecutors in their own right. Calvin's Geneva was no more tolerant than the Rome it rejected.

**The Calvinist counter-example**: Mill's deepest critique of the Reformation legacy targets Calvinism specifically — the doctrine that human nature is "completely corrupt" and that the only virtue is obedience. This represents everything Mill opposes: the suppression of spontaneity, individuality, and self-development in favor of submission to authority, even if that authority is now theological rather than ecclesiastical.
