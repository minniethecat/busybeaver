# Fallibilità

*Fallibilità* (fallibility) — or more precisely, the denial of infallibility — is the **epistemic foundation** of Mill's argument for liberty of thought and discussion. It is the premise from which he derives the conclusion that no person or group has the right to silence an opinion.

## Definizione

Mill does not prove human fallibility; he treats it as self-evident and draws out its implications. The key move is to show that the *assumption of infallibility* is implicit in every act of censorship:

> "Se l'opinione è vera, gli uomini sono privati dell'occasione di cambiare l'errore per la verità; se è falsa, essi perdono un beneficio quasi altrettanto grande: la percezione più chiara e l'impressione più viva della verità, risultante dalla sua collisione con l'errore." — *Sulla Libertà*, (002-003)

The practical consequence is that certainty about an opinion's falsity is never a sufficient justification for suppressing it — because certainty itself is a product of fallible human judgment:

> "Non vi sono due secoli nè, quasi, due paesi che su questo siano arrivati alla stessa conclusione; e la conclusione di un secolo o di un paese è argomento di stupore per un altro." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-010)

## Ruolo nell'argomentazione di Mill

Fallibility operates as the **premise** for Mill's three-case argument in Chapter II:

**Case 1 — The suppressed opinion may be true**: If we suppress it, we assume our own infallibility. History teaches that ages have persecuted truths later recognized as fundamental — Socrates, Jesus, and Galileo are Mill's examples.

**Case 2 — The suppressed opinion may be partially true**: Most received opinions are half-truths. The suppressed opinion often contains the neglected half. Only through collision of partial truths does the whole truth emerge.

**Case 3 — The suppressed opinion may be wholly false**: Even in this case, suppressing it is harmful because truth unchallenged becomes dead dogma. We need error to keep truth vital.

In all three cases, the appeal to fallibility forecloses the claim that anyone is entitled to decide for others what opinions may be heard.

**Beyond Chapter II**: Fallibility extends beyond opinions to ways of living. Just as no one possesses complete truth in matters of opinion, no one possesses the complete model of a good life. This connects fallibility to individuality — "è vantaggioso concedere un libero slancio ai diversi caratteri" (it is advantageous to give free scope to different characters) precisely because we cannot know in advance which ways of living will prove best (003-002).
