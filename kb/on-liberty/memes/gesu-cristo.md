# Gesù Cristo

Mill invokes Jesus Christ in *Sulla Libertà* primarily as a case study in the ethics of belief and the history of religious toleration. Christ appears not as a theological figure but as an **ethical exemplar** whose teachings illustrate both the power and the incompleteness of individual moral insight.

## Vita e contesto storico

Jesus of Nazareth (c. 4 BCE – c. 30 CE) was a Jewish preacher and religious reformer in Roman-occupied Judea. His ethical teachings, recorded in the Gospels, emphasized compassion, forgiveness, and inner moral transformation over outward legal compliance. Mill references Christ within the context of Christianity's historical development and the church's later departure from the spirit of its founder's teachings.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Christ serves several argumentative functions in Mill's text:

**As an example of ethical incompleteness**: Mill argues that even Christ's moral teachings, while containing profound insights, do not constitute a complete ethical system. He uses this to illustrate the broader point that no single individual, however inspired, can provide a complete moral code for all humanity — ethical truth is progressive and cumulative, not revealed once and for all.

**In the history of religious toleration**: Mill traces how Christianity, founded by a teacher who "non fece alcun male" (did no harm), became itself a persecuting institution. This historical irony demonstrates how institutionalized belief systems inevitably drift from their founder's spirit:

> "I grandi scrittori a cui il mondo deve ciò ch'egli possiede di libertà religiosa hanno rivendicato la libertà di coscienza come un diritto inalienabile." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-014)

**The odium theologicum**: Mill uses Christian persecution — both by and of Christians — to illustrate the psychology of intolerance. The sincere bigot feels moral certainty in persecuting heretics; this is the "odium theologicum" that Mill identifies as one of the most dangerous manifestations of moral certainty.

## Opere principali

The ethical teachings attributed to Jesus are preserved in the four canonical Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John). Mill does not discuss these texts directly but engages with their interpretation and institutional legacy.
