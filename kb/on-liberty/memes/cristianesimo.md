# Cristianesimo

Christianity occupies a central place in Mill's historical analysis of liberty — not as a theological system but as an **institutional case study** in how religious authority evolves from persecuted minority to persecuting establishment, and how the principles of individual liberty emerged from the exhaustion of religious conflict.

## Natura e contesto storico

Christianity began as a Jewish reform movement in the 1st century CE, spread throughout the Roman Empire despite periodic persecution, and became the Empire's official religion under Constantine (Edict of Milan, 313 CE). By the medieval period, the Church was the dominant institutional authority in Western Europe. The Reformation (16th century) fragmented this unity, producing multiple competing Christian traditions — each of which, Mill notes, was as intolerant as the Church it rejected when it had the power to be so.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Christianity functions in Mill's argument as the **paradigm case** for the analysis of authority, toleration, and individual conscience:

**From persecuted to persecutor**: The historical trajectory of Christianity — from a persecuted minority to a persecuting institution — demonstrates Mill's claim that intolerance is not specific to any particular doctrine but is inherent in the possession of unchecked power over opinion.

**The Calvinist critique**: Mill reserves his strongest criticism for the Calvinist tradition within Christianity, which he presents as the theological expression of the impulse to suppress individuality. Calvinism's doctrine that "la colpa capitale dell'uomo è di avere una volontà indipendente" (the capital crime of man is having an independent will) makes it the antithesis of Mill's ideal of human development.

**The origin of religious liberty**: Paradoxically, Mill argues that religious liberty emerged not from Christian principles but from their exhaustion — when no sect could dominate, all were forced to accept toleration as a practical necessity. The lesson is that liberty is the product not of virtue but of the balance of power.
