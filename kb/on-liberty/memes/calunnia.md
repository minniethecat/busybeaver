# Calunnia

In *Sulla Libertà*, *calunnia* (calumny) is not merely personal slander — it is, for Mill, the sharpest weapon of social tyranny in the domain of thought and discussion. When legal penalties for dissenting opinions recede, calumny steps in as the informal mechanism by which dominant opinion silences its challengers.

## Definizione

Mill identifies calumny as the gravest offense that can be committed in public debate:

> "La peggior offesa di questo genere che in una polemica si possa commettere è di vituperare come uomini pericolosi ed immorali quelli che professano l'opinione contraria alla nostra." — *Sulla Libertà*, (002-092)

This is not disagreement on the merits — it is the attack on the *character* of the opponent. The aim is not to refute an argument but to discredit the person making it, rendering their views unworthy of consideration.

Mill observes a structural asymmetry in how calumny operates: those who hold unpopular opinions are disproportionately its victims, because they lack the numbers and influence to defend themselves:

> "Gli uomini che professano un'opinione impopolare sono specialmente esposti a tali calunnie, perchè in generale sono poco numerosi e punto influenti e nessuno s'interessa di veder loro resa giustizia." — *Sulla Libertà*, (002-092)

Conversely, those attacking dominant opinions cannot employ calumny without destroying their own cause — they must be "studiatamente temperato" (studiously temperate) to be heard at all.

## Ruolo nell'argomentazione di Mill

Calumny occupies a strategic position in Mill's argument for freedom of discussion. His analysis places it at the intersection of three themes:

**The limits of law**. Mill has already argued that legal penalties for opinion are unjustified (Ch. 2). But he recognizes that removing legal coercion does not remove coercion — it merely shifts its form. Calumny is the leading example of how social pressure replaces legal prohibition:

> "Già da molto tempo, l'unico torto delle penalità legali è quello di sostenere e di rafforzare lo stigmate sociale. Questo stigmate soltanto è veramente efficace." — *Sulla Libertà*, (002-047)

**The ethics of discussion**. Mill devotes considerable attention (PN 002-089 through 002-095) to the proper conduct of intellectual debate. He distinguishes between substantive criticism — which is legitimate and essential — and personal vituperation — which is not. Sophistry, suppression of facts, and misrepresentation of opposing views are lesser offenses; calumny, the imputation of bad character, is the worst.

**The asymmetry of power**. Calumny is not a symmetrical weapon. Those defending established opinions can use it with impunity — they are praised for "onesto zelo" (honest zeal). Those challenging orthodoxy cannot. This asymmetry means that the norms of "polite" discussion, when selectively enforced, become instruments for preserving intellectual conformity.

Mill's conclusion is nuanced: he does not advocate legal prohibition of calumny, but insists on the moral duty of honest discussion. The remedy is not censorship but the cultivation of intellectual integrity — the willingness to "rendere onore a quella persona... che ha la calma di scorgere e l'onestà di riconoscere che cosa sono in realtà i suoi avversarî" (honor the person who has the calm to see and the honesty to recognize what their opponents and their opinions really are) (002-094).
