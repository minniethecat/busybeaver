# Carattere

In *Sulla Libertà*, *carattere* (character) is the inner shape of a human being — not innate temperament but the cultivated result of choices made under conditions of freedom. For Mill, the formation of character is the central project of a free life, and the chief casualty of social despotism.

## Definizione

Mill defines character by its contrast with mere conformity. A person of character is one whose conduct flows from their own judgment and disposition, not from custom or social expectation:

> "Colui che lascia che il mondo, o la parte del mondo in cui vive, scelga per lui il suo piano di vita, non ha bisogno d'altra facoltà che di quella della imitazione scimmiesca. Colui invece che sceglie per sè stesso il suo piano di vita, esercita tutte le sue facoltà." — *Sulla Libertà*, (003-007)

Character, in this sense, is synonymous with self-directedness. It requires the exercise of judgment, discrimination, and will — the very faculties that atrophy under conditions of enforced conformity.

Mill connects character explicitly to the development of individuality. The "carattere particolare di un uomo" (a man's particular character) is what distinguishes him from the mass, and it is precisely this distinctiveness that social pressure works to eliminate (003-006).

> "In ragione dello sviluppo della sua individualità, ogni persona diventa più valutabile a sè stessa, ed è perciò capace di essere più valutabile agli altri." — *Sulla Libertà*, (003-016)

## Ruolo nell'argomentazione di Mill

Character serves as the bridge between Mill's arguments for liberty of thought (Chapter II) and liberty of action (Chapter III). Its role unfolds across several key transitions:

**Character as the living result of conviction**. Mill's discussion of character begins in Chapter II, where he describes Socrates as having both a free, searching intelligence and "un carattere che lo portava a compenetrare... gli insegnamenti morali e religiosi del suo paese" (a character that led him to absorb the moral and religious teachings of his country) (002-032). Genuine character requires engagement, not passive reception.

**Character and the ethics of social pressure**. Chapter III extends the argument: if society demands not just outward compliance but inner conformity of character, it oversteps the legitimate bounds of social authority:

> "Si afferma che il giudizio o il carattere particolare di un uomo non deve punto influire sulle sue relazioni coi propri simili." — *Sulla Libertà*, (003-006)

**The degradation of character under despotism of custom**. In his most forceful passages, Mill warns that social conformity produces a debased human type:

> "Sia per risultare un tipo di carattere basso, abbietto, servile, capace forse di sottomettersi a qualunque cosa, ma non capace di sollevarsi fino alla grandezza." — *Sulla Libertà*, (002-084)

**Character as a public good**. In the concluding movement of Chapter III, Mill argues that strong character is not merely a private benefit but a collective resource. Society benefits from the diversity of well-developed characters because each contributes a distinctive perspective and energy.
