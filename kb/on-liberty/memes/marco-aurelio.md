# Marco Aurelio

Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE) serves Mill as a particularly pointed illustration of how even the most philosophically enlightened individuals can be instruments of intolerance. The Stoic emperor — author of the *Meditations*, one of the most humane works of antiquity — was also a persecutor of Christianity.

## Vita e contesto storico

Marcus Aurelius was Roman emperor from 161 to 180 CE, the last of the "Five Good Emperors." A devoted student of Stoic philosophy, his *Meditations* (written in Greek during military campaigns) remains one of the most widely read works of ancient philosophy. Despite his personal virtue, philosophical depth, and genuine commitment to justice, his reign saw the persecution of Christians — a policy Mill finds both morally instructive and philosophically significant.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Mill uses Marcus Aurelius as his **strongest counterexample** to the idea that good intentions prevent injustice:

**The paradox of the virtuous persecutor**: Marcus Aurelius was, Mill argues, "the most gentle and amiable of the pagans" — a man of profound moral seriousness. Yet he authorized the persecution of Christians because he sincerely believed Christianity to be a dangerous superstition threatening the social order. His virtue did not prevent his intolerance; it may even have intensified it, because his strong moral convictions made him certain of Christianity's wrongness.

**The insufficiency of personal virtue**: The example of Marcus Aurelius demonstrates that no amount of personal virtue, intelligence, or philosophical cultivation guarantees correct judgment about which opinions to suppress. If even the wisest emperor could be wrong about Christianity, then no contemporary authority can be trusted to decide which opinions may be heard.

**The structural argument**: Mill's point is not to condemn Marcus Aurelius personally but to demonstrate a structural truth about censorship — it is always exercised by fallible human beings whose judgments are shaped by the prejudices of their age. The solution is not better censors but no censors at all.

**The paired example**: Marcus Aurelius is often read alongside Socrates — the best of pagans persecuted; the best of Athenians was persecuted. Together they show that the problem of intolerance is not a matter of good vs. evil individuals but of the institutional and psychological dynamics that make even the wise suppress dissent.
