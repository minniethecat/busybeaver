# Jeremy Bentham

Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) is the founder of modern utilitarianism and the intellectual tradition from which Mill's own philosophy emerged. While Bentham is not directly named in *Sulla Libertà* with the frequency of Humboldt, he is the background presence against which Mill defines his own, more expansive version of the utilitarian principle.

## Vita e contesto storico

Bentham was an English philosopher, jurist, and social reformer whose principle of utility — "the greatest happiness of the greatest number" — became the cornerstone of the philosophical radical movement. His disciples, including James Mill (John Stuart's father), formed a circle that profoundly shaped 19th-century British reform. John Stuart Mill was educated to be Benthamism's heir — and spent his intellectual career both extending and transforming the Benthamite legacy.

## Ruolo in *Sulla Libertà*

Bentham's role in *Sulla Libertà* is that of an **inherited framework to be transformed**:

**The utility principle, redefined**: Mill opens his argument by declaring allegiance to utility as "la soluzione suprema di qualunque questione morale" (the ultimate solution to any moral question). But he immediately qualifies this:

> "Ma dev'essere l'utilità nel senso più vasto della parola, l'utilità fondata sui vantaggi permanenti dell'uomo, considerato come essere progressivo." — *Sulla Libertà*, (001-021)

This is aimed directly at the Benthamite tradition: Mill retains the utilitarian structure but rejects Bentham's narrow, quantitative hedonism in favor of a qualitative, developmental utilitarianism centered on "the permanent interests of man as a progressive being."

**From calculation to cultivation**: Where Bentham's utilitarianism focused on maximizing pleasure and minimizing pain, Mill's version emphasizes the cultivation of distinctly human faculties. Individuality, liberty, and self-development are not merely means to happiness — they are constitutive of the kind of happiness worth having.

**The silent reform**: Mill's relationship to Bentham in *Sulla Libertà* is characteristically diplomatic. He does not directly criticize Bentham; instead, he redefines utility so thoroughly that the Benthamite framework is effectively replaced by a richer conception that can accommodate liberty, individuality, and progress as intrinsic goods rather than mere instruments.

## Opere principali

- *Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation* (1789)
- *Fragment on Government* (1776)
- *Deontology* (1834, posthumous)

Mill's own assessment of Bentham appears in his essays "Bentham" (1838) and "Utilitarianism" (1861).
