## The Roman's Landfall

For Conrad, the North Foreland is the threshold of the Thames — the point where the river announces itself to the approaching mariner. He imagines the commander of "the first Roman galley" turning his beaked prow "to the westward under the brow of the North Foreland," gazing with "intense absorption upon the estuary of the Thames." This is not merely a historical fancy but an assertion of the Foreland's permanence as a seamark: what the Roman saw, the nineteenth-century sailor still recognised.

> The commander of the first Roman galley must have looked with an intense absorption upon the estuary of the Thames as he turned the beaked prow of his ship to the westward under the brow of the North Foreland. (030-002)

## Between the Forelands

The North Foreland, together with Orfordness to the north, marks the mouth of the Thames. Conrad describes the inward-bound ships coming up "from under the sea horizon closing the mouth of the river between Orfordness and North Foreland." The two headlands form a symbolic gateway: to pass between them is to enter the river's domain, leaving the open sea behind. For the outward-bound ship, the Foreland is the last English landmark — the point where home recedes into the distance.

> In the offing the inward-bound ships come up singly and in bunches from under the sea horizon closing the mouth of the river between Orfordness and North Foreland. (031-002)

## The Thames Approaches

The sea-reach of the Thames, from the Nore up to Gravesend, is framed by the low Kentish shore on one side and the Essex marshes on the other. The North Foreland is the one emphatic geographical feature in an otherwise featureless landscape — "there are no features to this land, no conspicuous, far-famed landmarks for the eye." Its chalk cliffs, modest compared to Dover's, nonetheless provide a definite visual anchor at the southern edge of the estuary.

> There are no features to this land, no conspicuous, far-famed landmarks for the eye; there is nothing so far down to tell you of the greatest agglomeration of mankind on earth dwelling no more than five and twenty miles away. (030-003)

## A Seamark for Two Millennia

Conrad's insistence on the Roman perspective is deliberate. The North Foreland has been a seamark since before recorded history. Its name — "Foreland," the land that stands forward — captures its function: it is the land that projects into the sea, the first solid ground that a ship approaching from the east or south would see. Conrad's own experience of approaching the Thames from the sea gave him an intimate appreciation of what this landmark meant to the homeward-bound sailor.
