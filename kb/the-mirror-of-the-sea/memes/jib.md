## Definition

The jib is a triangular fore-and-aft sail set on the stay running from the foremast head to the jibboom — the long spar projecting forward beyond the bow. It is the forwardmost working sail of a square-rigged vessel and, together with other headsails, plays a crucial role in balancing the ship: a well-trimmed jib lifts the bow, reduces weather helm, and drives the vessel closer to the wind than square sails alone can manage.

Though it is a comparatively small sail, the jib's position at the extreme forward end of the ship gives it a leverage out of all proportion to its area. A backed jib can swing the bow through the eye of the wind in a tack; a straining jib, sheeted hard, can claw a ship off a lee shore when nothing else will draw. Conrad knew this well, and his prose returns repeatedly to the image of the jibboom and its sail — the ship's forwardmost reach into the sea and weather.

## The Jibboom's Reach

For Conrad, the jibboom represents the ship's forward extension into the unknown — a spar that literally reaches ahead of the hull, probing the weather before the rest of the vessel arrives:

> Thus would the mate's voice repeat the thought of the master, both gazing ahead, while under their feet the ship rushes at some twelve knots in the direction of the lee shore; and only a couple of miles in front of her swinging and dripping jibboom, carried naked with an upward slant like a spear, a grey horizon closes the view with a multitude of waves surging upwards violently as if to strike at the stooping clouds.
>
> (026-010)

The jibboom is "carried naked with an upward slant like a spear" — a weapon, or perhaps a probe, thrust into the storm. The ship rushes towards the lee shore at twelve knots, and the jibboom stands at the forward edge of the catastrophe, "swinging and dripping," the first spar to meet whatever lies ahead.

In harbour, the jibboom becomes a different thing — a mark of the ship's stature, an ornament of her identity:

> Their spars dwarfed with their loftiness the corrugated-iron sheds, their jibbooms extended far over the shore, their white-and-gold figureheads, almost dazzling in their purity, overhung the straight, long quay above the mud and dirt of the wharfside, with the busy figures of groups and single men moving to and fro, restless and grimy under their soaring immobility.
>
> (033-005)

Here the jibboom is a gesture of pride, reaching "far over the shore" as if the ship were claiming the land itself. The contrast between the soaring immobility of the spars and the grimy restlessness of the men below is one that Conrad draws with particular pleasure.

## In Collision

The jibboom's reach, so majestic in harbour and so perilous at sea, also makes it the first point of contact in a collision:

> Ultimately they brought us up with the jibboom of a Dutch brig poking through our spanker — nothing worse.
>
> (009-007)

The dry understatement — "nothing worse" — is pure Conrad. A Dutch brig's jibboom has pierced the *spanker* (the aftermost fore-and-aft sail) of the narrator's vessel. The jibboom, extended far forward, has become a spear in earnest. Yet in the crowded anchorages and narrow channels of Conrad's day, such minor collisions were part of the ordinary hazards of seafaring.

## Related Concepts

The jib is one of the headsails, a family of triangular sails set forward of the foremast that includes the flying jib, outer jib, inner jib, and fore-topmast staysail. Together with the [[foresail]] and the square sails on the foremast, the jib drives the ship forward and helps balance the pressure of the after-sails. The jibboom, from which the jib is flown, is itself a descendant of the bowsprit — the fixed spar projecting from the bow — and in large ships the jibboom was an extension beyond the [[bowsprit]], the two together forming the head-rig.
