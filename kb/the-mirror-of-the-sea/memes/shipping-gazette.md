# Shipping Gazette

The ***Shipping Gazette*** was a London-based maritime newspaper that chronicled the movements, arrivals, departures, and losses of the world's merchant shipping. For seamen, shipowners, and the families of sailors in Conrad's era, its columns were the daily record of their profession — the place where a vessel's name migrated, inexorably, from "spoken" to "overdue" to the harrowing finality of "missing."

## The Columns of Fate

The *Shipping Gazette*'s most consequential feature was its "Shipping Intelligence" column, where the fate of every vessel on the high seas was recorded in the formal, unadorned language of maritime commerce. The headings told their own story:

> On some days there appears the heading "Overdue" — an ominous threat of loss and sorrow trembling yet in the balance of fate. (016-003)

A ship that passed from "Overdue" to "Missing" had crossed the final threshold:

> Only a very few days more — appallingly few to the hearts which had set themselves bravely to hope against hope — three weeks, a month later, perhaps, the name of ships under the blight of the "Overdue" heading shall appear again in the column of "Shipping Intelligence," but under the final declaration of "Missing." (016-004)

The formal notice that followed was, in Conrad's view, a kind of funeral oration — the last public record of a vessel's existence:

> "The ship, or barque, or brig So-and-So, bound from such a port, with such and such cargo, for such another port, having left at such and such a date, last spoken at sea on such a day, and never having been heard of since, was posted today as missing." Such in its strictly official eloquence is the form of funeral orations on ships that, perhaps wearied with a long struggle, or in some unguarded moment that may come to the readiest of us, had let themselves be overwhelmed by a sudden blow from the enemy. (016-005)

## The Unholy Fascination

For Conrad, the *Shipping Gazette*'s "missing" column exercised a dark imaginative pull — not morbid but profoundly human, the response of a brother-sailor to an unknowable end:

> The unholy fascination of dread dwells in the thought of the last moments of a ship reported as "missing" in the columns of the *Shipping Gazette*. Nothing of her ever comes to light — no grating, no lifebuoy, no piece of boat or branded oar — to give a hint of the place and date of her sudden end. The *Shipping Gazette* does not even call her "lost with all hands." She remains simply "missing"; she has disappeared enigmatically into a mystery of fate as big as the world, where your imagination of a brother-sailor, of a fellow-servant and lover of ships, may range unchecked. (017-001)

No survivor ever returned from a "missing" ship to tell the tale:

> Nobody ever comes back from a "missing" ship to tell how hard was the death of the craft, and how sudden and overwhelming the last anguish of her men. (017-010)

## Overdue: The Suspense Before Finality

Where "missing" brought finality, "overdue" brought the torment of uncertainty — hope suspended, speculation running wild:

> But if the word "missing" brings all hope to an end and settles the loss of the underwriters, the word "overdue" confirms the fears already born in many homes ashore, and opens the door of speculation in the market of risks. (018-001)

A grim secondary market emerged around overdue vessels:

> There is a class of optimists ready to reinsure an "overdue" ship at a heavy premium. (018-002)

Yet the transition from "overdue" to "missing" was not always irreversible. Conrad records the rare but real possibility of reprieve — the overdue ship that suddenly reappears:

> For if a "missing" ship has never turned up within the memory of seamen of my generation, the name of an "overdue" ship, trembling as it were on the edge of the fatal heading, has been known to appear as "arrived." (018-003)

The official reversal, when it came, was terse but momentous:

> "The ship So-and-So, bound to such a port, and posted as 'overdue,' has been reported yesterday as having arrived safely at her destination." (018-006)

## Maritime Context

The *Shipping Gazette* served as the authoritative record of merchant shipping movements in an era before wireless telegraphy. Shipowners, insurers at [[lloyds-of-london|Lloyd's of London]], and the families of seamen relied upon its columns for news of vessels that might be weeks or months out of sight. The progression from "overdue" to "missing" was a ritual of the sea — a formal acknowledgement that a ship and her crew had been claimed by the [[sea|ocean]], their fate known only to the waters that had swallowed them.

The absence of evidence — "no grating, no lifebuoy, no piece of boat or branded oar" — was the peculiar horror of the "missing" designation. A ship "lost with all hands" at least had a known fate; a ship merely "missing" occupied a liminal space between existence and oblivion, leaving families and colleagues suspended in an uncertainty that could last for years.

The steamers of Conrad's time, "whose life, fed on coals and breathing the black breath of smoke into the air," were not immune:

> The menace of the "overdue" and the finality of "missing" come very quickly to steamers whose life, fed on coals and breathing the black breath of smoke into the air, goes on in disregard of wind and wave. (018-008)
