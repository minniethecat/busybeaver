## Definition

To [[Joseph Conrad|Conrad]], the wind is not merely moving air but the sovereign power that governs the world's oceans. "There is no part of the world of coasts, continents, oceans, seas, straits, capes, and islands which is not under the sway of a reigning wind, the sovereign of its typical weather. The wind rules the aspects of the sky and the action of the sea." This is not metaphor alone — in the age of sail, the wind was the absolute arbiter of a ship's fate, and Conrad's personification of the great prevailing winds as monarchs reflects the seaman's lived experience of submission to their power.

## In Conrad's Words

> There is no part of the world of coasts, continents, oceans, seas, straits, capes, and islands which is not under the sway of a reigning wind, the sovereign of its typical weather. The wind rules the aspects of the sky and the action of the sea. (025-001)

> The West Wind reigns over the seas surrounding the coasts of these kingdoms; and from the gateways of the channels, from promontories as if from watchtowers, from estuaries of rivers as if from postern gates, from passageways, inlets, straits, firths, the ships of the kingdom go out to their toil under the tremendous escort of that tireless monarch. (026-001)

> The caprice of the winds, like the wilfulness of men, is fraught with the disastrous consequences of self-indulgence. Long anger, the sense of his uncontrolled power, spoils the frank and generous nature of the West Wind. (026-003)

> The natures of those two winds that share the dominions of the great oceans are fundamentally different. It is strange that the winds which men are prone to style capricious remain true to their character in all the various regions of the earth. (029-003)

> As a ruler, the East Wind has a remarkable stability; as an invader of the high latitudes lying under the tumultuous sway of his great brother, the Wind of the West, he is extremely difficult to dislodge, by the reason of his cold craftiness and profound duplicity. (025-003)

> I had been some time at sea before I became aware of the fact that hearing plays a perceptible part in gauging the force of the wind. It was at night. The ship was one of those iron wool-clippers that the Clyde had floated out in swarms upon the world. (011-004)

> The next day there were very few ships in sight from our mastheads — seven at most, perhaps, with a few more distant specks, hull down, beyond the magic ring of the horizon. The spell of the fair wind has a subtle power to scatter a white-winged company of ships looking all the same way. (010-002)

## The Reigning Winds

Conrad maps the world's oceans by their ruling winds. The [[westerly-weather|West Wind]] dominates the North Atlantic approaches to Europe — a "tireless monarch" whose southwesterly moods bring storms to homeward-bound ships. The East Wind is "a ruler of remarkable stability," an invader of the West Wind's high-latitude territories, characterized by "cold craftiness and profound duplicity." The North and South winds are "but small princes amongst the powers of the sea," possessing no territory of their own, yet it is from their houses that the reigning dynasties draw their strength.

## The Wind as Ruler

Conrad's political metaphor is sustained and elaborate. The West Wind is "frank and generous" in his normal mood, but "spoilt" by long anger. The East Wind carries a "dagger under a black cloak." These are not whimsical comparisons but the seaman's way of understanding forces that are both predictable in their broad patterns and terrifying in their particular manifestations. The wind that has been a fair companion for days can turn, in hours, into a murderous adversary. A [[gale]] is the wind in its most personal and memorable form.

## Hearing the Wind

Conrad notes that experienced seamen learn to gauge the wind's force by sound as much as by sight. At night, when the sea is invisible, the "howling" of the wind, the thrum of the rigging, and the "thudding rhythm" of the ship's progress through the water all provide sensory data that the seasoned sailor integrates unconsciously. This multisensory awareness is part of what distinguishes the seaman from the landsman — and part of the [[fine-art-of-sailing|fine art of sailing]]. Conrad observed this phenomenon first-hand aboard the *[[tweed|Tweed]]* under [[captain-s|Captain S——]], whose hard-of-hearing mate [[mr-p-hard-of-hearing|Mr. P——]] could scarcely tell the wind's force by sound at all — and part of the [[fine-art-of-sailing|fine art of sailing]].

> "No fear! not while the chief mate's on deck. He's that deaf he can't tell how much wind there is." (011-007)

## The Wind and the Ship

The sailing-ship was built to capture the wind, and her relationship with it was her defining characteristic. Conrad describes the tall masts "holding aloft the white canvas, spread out like a snare for the invisible power of the air." The [[craft]] and the wind were partners in an ancient bargain — the ship offered her spread of canvas, and the wind answered with motion, direction, and sometimes destruction.

> The taller the ship, the further she can be seen; and her white tallness breathed upon by the wind first proclaims her size. The tall masts holding aloft the white canvas, spread out like a snare for the invisible power of the air. (010-003)
