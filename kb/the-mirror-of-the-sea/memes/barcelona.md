## Location and Identity

Barcelona, in *The Mirror of the Sea*, is the Mediterranean port that served as the operational base of the Tremolino syndicate. Doña Rita kept a small furnished house on the Prado "for the good of the cause — por el Rey!", and it was from Barcelona's old harbour that the tartane slipped out on her last voyage. Conrad's Barcelona is a city of intrigue and twilight departures — a place where political conspiracy and maritime adventure merged in the service of a lost royalist cause.

## Conrad's Description

> It was not Tremolino's fault that the syndicate depended so much on the wit and wisdom and the information of Doña Rita. She had taken a little furnished house on the Prado for the good of the cause — por el Rey! (042-001)

> Once — it was in the old harbour, just before the Tremolino's last voyage — he vanished thus overboard to my infinite consternation. Dominic and I had been talking business together aft. (043-009)

## Maritime Significance

Barcelona's significance in Conrad's narrative is inseparable from the Tremolino story. The port was the stage for the final act of the syndicate's Mediterranean adventure: the discovery that their operation had been betrayed, the hurried preparations for departure, Cesar's mysterious disappearance and ignominious return, and the silent slipping-out to sea at dusk. For Conrad, Barcelona represented the Mediterranean port in its most characteristic form — ancient, beautiful, and morally ambiguous, a place where the line between legitimate commerce and "impious stratagems against the powers of the earth" was perpetually blurred.
