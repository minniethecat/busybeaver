**Ahead** is the nautical term for the direction directly in front of a vessel's bow — the course the ship is steering, the path towards her destination, and the quarter from which hazards first appear. For the commander and the officer of the watch, looking ahead is the essential act of seamanship: the constant, vigilant scanning of the sea and sky for land, ships, or the first signs of changing weather.

## The Commander's Gaze

Conrad repeatedly depicts the master or the mate gazing steadily ahead — a posture that is at once practical (watching for dangers) and symbolic (looking towards the ship's fate). The captain's eyes, weary from years at sea, fix on the horizon ahead as though nothing lay between him and his destination.

> …I observed his weary eyes gaze steadily ahead, as if there had been nothing between him and the straight line of sea and sky. (003-008)

The impulse to go on deck and look ahead is an irresistible compulsion in the hours before making port, when the ship's position must be constantly verified:

> It seems unable to abide for many seconds together in the holy of holies of the captain's stateroom; it will go out on deck and gaze ahead, through straining eyes, as the appointed moment comes nearer. (002-009)

## Making Landfall

The approach to port after a long voyage is dominated by the vigilant search for what lies ahead. As the ship forges slowly towards her berth, the officer at the bow calls back to the bridge with reports of what he sees.

> And it is the voice of the keeper of the ship's anchors that will answer: "Grows right ahead, sir," or "Broad on the bow," or whatever concise and deferential shout will fit the case. (006-001)

The final moments before anchoring are charged with silent attention:

> …he waits attentive, in a silent ship that forges slowly ahead towards her picked-out berth, for the sharp shout from aft, "Let go!" (005-006)

## The Homeward Run

On the homeward passage, the knowledge that land lies somewhere ahead infuses every moment on deck. The commander's gaze is charged with the awareness of destination and the dangers of approach.

> Many times in my life, standing in long sea-boots and streaming oilskins at the elbow of my commander on the poop of a homeward-bound ship making for the Channel, and gazing ahead into the grey and tormented waste, I have heard a weary sigh shape itself into a studiously casual comment… (026-004)

The thought of the land ahead is ever-present:

> It would be merely the instinctive voicing of an ever-present thought associated closely with the consciousness of the land somewhere ahead and of the great speed of the ship. (026-008)

And ahead may lie danger — the lee shore towards which the ship rushes at twelve knots:

> …both gazing ahead, while under their feet the ship rushes at some twelve knots in the direction of the lee shore; and only a couple of miles ahead. (026-010)

## Heavy Weather

In a gale, the seas sweep past the ship from astern and race on ahead, their relentless passage a measure of the vessel's exposure:

> The solemn thundering combers caught her up from astern, passed her with a fierce boiling up of foam level with the bulwarks, swept on ahead with a swish and a roar… (023-005)
