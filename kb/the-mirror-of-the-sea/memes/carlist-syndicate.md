## Organization Identity

The Carlist syndicate was a small, secretive group of four individuals bound by loyalty to the Carlist cause — the movement supporting [[don-carlos|Don Carlos de Borbón]]'s claim to the Spanish throne. Operating between [[barcelona|Barcelona]] and Marseille, the syndicate owned the tartane *[[tremolino|Tremolino]]*, ostensibly a fruit and corkwood trader but in fact a vessel used for gun-running and smuggling in support of the Carlist insurgency. The syndicate's operations were directed by [[dona-rita|Doña Rita]] from a little furnished house on the Prado in Barcelona, with [[j-m-k-b|J. M. K. B.]], an American gentleman of means, providing resources and political cover. Conrad himself — young, impressionable, and hungry for adventure — became the syndicate's fourth member and served aboard the *Tremolino* as a junior officer.

## Maritime Context

The Carlist Wars (1833–1876) were a series of civil wars in Spain fought between supporters of the liberal line of succession and the conservative, Catholic Carlists who supported the claim of Don Carlos and his descendants. By the time of Conrad's involvement in the late 1870s, the major Carlist uprisings had been defeated, but Carlist sympathisers continued to operate clandestinely in the Mediterranean, smuggling arms and supplies.

The syndicate's use of the *Tremolino* — a small, fast tartane of ancient Mediterranean design — was typical of this clandestine world. Such vessels could slip in and out of small ports, evade revenue cutters, and maintain the appearance of legitimate trade while serving political ends. For Conrad, the syndicate represented the romance and danger of the [[mediterranean-sea|Mediterranean]] — a world of secret loyalties, shadowy operations, and the intoxicating freedom of the inland sea.

The syndicate's operations came to an end with the loss of the *Tremolino*, driven ashore on the Catalan coast by [[dominic-cervoni|Dominic Cervoni]] to prevent capture by a Spanish revenue cutter. "There was not a chip to be seen around the black rock from which the Tremolino, with the breath of fire in her, had slipped out silently into the night."
