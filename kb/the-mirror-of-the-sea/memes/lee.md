## Definition

In nautical usage, the lee is the side or direction away from the wind — the sheltered side, as distinct from the weather side which receives the wind's full force. But to the seaman, the word carries a double meaning: the lee shore is among the most dreaded phrases in the maritime vocabulary, for a sailing vessel caught between a gale and a lee shore has no escape but the skill of her crew and the grace of the wind. Conrad, who knew the terror of a lee shore intimately, uses the word with the weight of hard experience.

## The Visible Sign of Danger

The lee side of a ship in heavy weather is a place of unmistakable violence. Conrad's image of the "immense white sheet of foam level with the lee rail" (011-009) captures the physical evidence of the wind's power — a churning, blinding testimony written in water. The lee rail, buried in foam, marks the boundary between the ship and the sea's fury, and the officer who hooks on to the weather rigging and watches that lee rail disappear under the rushing whiteness knows exactly what is at stake:

> Night clouds racing overhead, wind howling, royals set, and the ship rushing on in the dark, an immense white sheet of foam level with the lee rail.
> (011-009)

In such moments, the lee side is not shelter but the very threshold of catastrophe — a reminder that the margin between safety and disaster aboard a sailing ship is measured in inches of freeboard and the angle of the deck.
