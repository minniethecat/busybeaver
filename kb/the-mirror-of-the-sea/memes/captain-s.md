## Identity

Captain S—— was a shipmaster under whom Conrad served early in his career, commanding the iron wool-clipper *[[Tweed]]*. He appears in *The Mirror of the Sea* as an illustration of a particular type of commander — the man who attributes the success of his ship not to the skill of his officers or the quality of his crew, but to the mysterious influence of his own "right feeling" and presence aboard. Conrad treats this conviction with the gentle irony of a younger officer who had seen, from the deck, that the ship's good passages depended on rather more tangible factors than the captain's invisible moral emanations.

## Conrad's Account

> The point, however, is that Captain S——, who used to say frequently, "She never made a decent passage after I left her," seemed to think that the secret of her speed lay in the captain's right feeling for his ship. (012-003)

> The second mate falling ill during the passage, I was promoted to officer of the watch, alone in charge of the deck. Thus the immense leverage of the men's quality was disclosed to me. (012-004)

> And, indeed, poor P——, quite young, and a smart seaman, was very hard of hearing. At the same time, he had the name of being the very devil of a fellow with the crew when roused. (011-008)

> So sail did get shortened more or less in time even in that ship, and her tall spars never went overboard while I served in her. However, all the time I was with them, Captain S—— and Mr. P—— did not get on very well together. (012-001)

## Character and Anecdotes

Captain S—— held a conviction that Conrad found both endearing and instructive: that a ship performed well because her master "felt right" about her. Conrad uses this belief as a foil for his own thesis — that a ship's performance at sea depends on the concerted effort of the entire crew, from the captain's decisions to the seamen's willing labour.

Yet Conrad's irony is not dismissive. There is, he implies, a kernel of truth in Captain S——'s conviction. The relationship between a commander and his ship is not purely mechanical; the [[craft]] of command includes an element of sympathy, an intuitive understanding that cannot be reduced to rules. Captain S——'s faith in his own "right feeling," however exaggerated, was an expression of something real: the [[fine-art-of-sailing|fine art]] that distinguished the great commanders from the merely competent.

The captain's relationship with his hard-of-hearing chief mate, [[mr-p-hard-of-hearing|Mr. P——]], provides a comic counterpoint. "All the time I was with them, Captain S—— and Mr. P—— did not get on very well together" — a quiet observation that captures the human frictions inevitable in the close quarters of a sailing ship.

## The Deeper Truth

Conrad's portrait of Captain S—— is more than a character sketch. It is a meditation on the nature of command itself. The captain's self-assurance, harmless in itself, reveals a deeper truth about [[seamanship]]: that the visible skill of handling a ship rests on invisible foundations of character, judgment, and — yes — feeling. Captain S—— was wrong about the mechanism but right about the mystery: a ship under a commander who loves her does, somehow, sail better.
