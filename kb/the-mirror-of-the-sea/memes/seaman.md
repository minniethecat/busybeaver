The *seaman* stands at the centre of Joseph Conrad's vision of maritime life — not as a romantic figure but as a practitioner of a demanding craft whose excellence lies in disciplined attention and accumulated experience. Throughout *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad returns again and again to the figure of the seaman as the measure by which all things nautical are judged.

> The taking of Departure, if not the last sight of the land, is, perhaps, the last professional recognition of the land on the part of a sailor. It is not the ship that takes her departure; the seaman takes his Departure by means of cross-bearings which fix the place of the first tiny pencil-cross on the white expanse of the track-chart. (001-004)

## The Seaman's Craft

For Conrad, seamanship is not merely a set of techniques but a *fine art* — something approaching the condition of artistic creation. The seaman's relationship with his vessel demands the same self-effacing dedication that the artist brings to his medium.

> To forget one's self, to surrender all personal feeling in the service of that fine art, is the only way for a seaman to the faithful discharge of his trust. (008-006)

This ideal of self-surrender is the moral core of Conrad's seaman. The good seaman does not impose himself upon his ship but rather disappears into the act of handling her, becoming, in effect, an extension of the vessel he serves.

> Luckily, too, or else there would have been more shoddy reputations for first-rate seamanship. (008-006)

The quality of speed in the age of sail was understood as a direct expression of the seaman's competence. It was not the ship that was fast — it was the seaman who made her so.

> No iron ship of yesterday ever attained the marvels of speed which the seamanship of men famous in their time had obtained from their wooden, copper-sheeted predecessors. A certain mysteriousness hangs around the quality of speed as it was displayed by the old sailing-ships commanded by a competent seaman. In those days the speed depended upon the seaman; therefore, apart from the laws, rules, and regulations for the good preservation of his cargo, he was careful of his loading — or what is technically called the trim of his ship. (014-001)

## The Making of a Seaman

Conrad presents the formation of a seaman as a slow, cumulative process — the product of years of hard service rather than any formal education. The true seaman emerges from the school of the sea itself.

> It appeared he had "served his time" in the copper-ore trade, the famous copper-ore trade of old days between Swansea and the Chilean coast, coal out and ore in, deep-loaded both ways, as if in wanton defiance of the great Cape Horn seas — a work, this, for staunch ships, and a great school of staunchness for West-Country seamen. (003-008)

The copper-ore trade, with its punishing deep-loading practice and its rounding of Cape Horn, served as a forge for seamen. Conrad's capitalization of "West-Country" hints at a particular regional pride — the men of Devon and Cornwall were understood to produce a particularly hardy breed of sailor.

The seaman's preoccupations extend to every detail of the ship's equipment. Conrad notes the peculiar intensity with which the sailor thinks about his ground tackle:

> From first to last the seaman's thoughts are very much concerned with his anchors. The anchor and the land are indissolubly connected in a sailor's thoughts. (005-001)

## Character of the Seaman

Conrad identifies two complementary qualities that make a trustworthy seaman: a profound sense of insecurity — the constant awareness that the sea will punish any lapse — and an equally profound confidence in one's own abilities. These are not contradictory but mutually reinforcing.

> If it is permissible to criticise the absent, I should say he had a little too much of the sense of insecurity which is so invaluable in a seaman. I must hasten to add that he had also the other qualification necessary to make a trustworthy seaman — that of an absolute confidence in himself. (005-008)

The seaman learns to read the sea with a gaze that sees past the surface:

> I observed his weary eyes gaze steadily ahead, as if there had been nothing between him and the straight line of sea and sky, where whatever a seaman is looking for is first bound to appear. (003-008)

## Fellowship and Language

Seamen share a fellowship that transcends individual acquaintance. Conrad captures this in a chance encounter between two seamen — one elderly, one young — united by their shared absorption in the rigging of a new ship.

> The elderly, respectable seaman, withdrawing his gaze from that multitude of spars, gave me a glance to make sure of our fellowship in the craft and mystery of the sea. We had met casually, and had got into contact as I had stopped near him, my attention being caught by the same peculiarity he was looking at in the rigging of an obviously new ship. (035-004)

This fellowship is expressed in a technical language that Conrad regards as possessing a peculiar literary quality. The language of seamen — created by "simple men with keen eyes for the real aspect of the things they see in their trade" — achieves what the artist in words strives for: precision that captures the essential.

> He is the man who watches the growth of the cable — a sailor's phrase which has all the force, precision, and imagery of technical language that, created by simple men with keen eyes for the real aspect of the things they see in their trade, achieves the just expression seizing upon the essential, which is the ambition of the artist in words. (006-001)

The language of seamanship carries an almost legal precision. Conrad is at pains to distinguish correct from incorrect usage:

> To speak with severe technicality, a ship or a fleet is "brought up" — the complementary words unpronounced and unwritten being, of course, "to an anchor." Less technically, but not less correctly, the word "anchored," with its characteristic appearance and resolute sound, ought to be good enough for the newspapers of the greatest maritime country in the world. (004-007)

## Types of Seamen

Conrad distinguishes between different classes of sailors. The yachting sailor — a figure developed by the racing of pleasure craft — represents a specialized type:

> The sailing and racing of yachts has developed a class of fore-and-aft sailors, men born and bred to the sea, fishing in winter and yachting in summer; men to whom the handling of that particular rig presents no mystery. (008-001)

And there is the commanding figure — the master whose presence itself speaks authority:

> His voice was deep, hearty, and authoritative — the voice of a very prince amongst sailors. He kept his ship in apple-pie order, which would have been seamanlike enough but for a finicking touch in its details. (009-002)

The word "seamanlike" here functions as a term of high praise — to be seamanlike is the highest compliment one sailor can pay another. Yet Conrad notes that even this can be taken to excess: the "finicking touch" suggests that seamanship, like any art, can become fussy when it loses sight of its practical purpose.
