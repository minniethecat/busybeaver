## Identity

Mr. P—— was a young seaman who served with Conrad aboard the same ship. Conrad describes him as "quite young, and a smart seaman" — a genuine compliment in the rating of professional competence — but afflicted with a profound deafness that turned every watch into a theatrical ordeal. His character sketch is one of the comic gems of *The Mirror of the Sea*, yet beneath the humour lies Conrad's respect for a man who performed his duties capably despite a handicap that his officer could not or would not accommodate.

## Conrad's Account

> And, indeed, poor P——, quite young, and a smart seaman, was very hard of hearing. At the same time, he had the name of being the very devil of a fellow with the crew when roused. (011-008)

> It generally happened in this way: Night clouds racing overhead, wind howling, royals set, and the ship rushing on in the dark, an immense white sheet of foam level with the lee rail. Mr. P——, in charge of the watch, would be strolling near the officer on the poop. In the breathless pauses of the gale the officer could be heard bellowing his order. And Mr. P——, who was not good at catching what was shouted in the wind, would say interrogatively: (011-009)

> "By Heavens, Mr. P——! I used to carry on sail in my time, but —" (011-014)

> Then, in a lull, P——'s protesting innocence would become audible: (011-016)

> Thus P—— passed away from amongst the tall spars of ships that he had tried to their utmost in many a spell of boisterous weather. He had shown me what carrying on meant, but he was not a man to learn from his experience. (012-018)

## Character and Anecdotes

The recurring spectacle of Mr. P—— on the night watch — a tall, shadowy officer bellowing into the gale while the deaf seaman stood bewildered in the roar of wind and sea — became, in Conrad's telling, a dark comedy of the sailing-ship deck. The officer could not adjust his method; the seaman could not overcome his infirmity; and the ship rushed on regardless. Conrad's account captures the human friction aboard a working vessel, where incompetence of one kind (the officer's unwillingness to adapt) fed on incapacity of another (P——'s hearing), and the ship carried both toward their destination with sublime indifference.
