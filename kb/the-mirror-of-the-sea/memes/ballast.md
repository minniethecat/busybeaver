## Definition

Ballast is heavy material — stone, iron, or water — placed in the lower part of a ship to improve her stability when she is not carrying a full cargo. A ship without cargo or ballast is dangerously top-heavy and prone to capsizing. In Conrad's treatment, ballast becomes the measure of a ship's honesty: the claim that a vessel "can sail without ballast" is exposed as a piece of commercial bravado that costs the owner nothing and may cost the crew everything.

## In Conrad's Words

> The profitable ship will carry a large load through all the hazards of the weather, and, when at rest, will stand up in dock and shift from berth to berth without ballast.
>
> (013-003)

> There is a point of perfection in a ship as a worker when she is spoken of as being able to sail without ballast.
>
> (013-003)

> It is open to any man to say that his ship will sail without ballast; and he will say it, too, with every mark of profound conviction, especially if he is not going to sail in her himself. The risk of advertising her as able to sail without ballast is not great, since the statement does not imply a warranty of her arriving anywhere. Moreover, it is strictly true that most ships will sail without ballast for some little time before they turn turtle upon the crew.
>
> (013-003)

## Ballast and Cargo: The Economics of Stability

Conrad's discussion of ballast sits within a broader meditation on the loading of ships — a craft that, in his day, was a matter of skill and judgment rather than the mechanical dumping of the steam age. The presence or absence of ballast was inseparable from the economics of cargo: a profitable ship sailed laden, and ballast was the substitute for a paying load.

> The hurry of the times, the loading and discharging organization of the docks, the use of hoisting machinery which works quickly and will not wait, the cry for prompt despatch, the very spirit of all these things are ready to give one soreness and a faint nausea.
>
> (013-002)

> The loading of ships was once a matter of skill, judgment, and knowledge.
>
> (013-005)

The older method — the chief mate with his pocketbook, carefully distributing weight through the hold — was not merely a technical practice but an art that ensured a ship would sail on an even keel, or, when necessary, "trimmed quite one foot by the stern" (014-001). The boast that a ship can "sail without ballast" belongs to this world of skilled loading, where a vessel so perfectly laden that she needed no ballast was indeed a mark of the chief mate's mastery.

## The Peril of Centre of Gravity

Conrad returns repeatedly to a ship's centre of gravity — the invisible line below which weight must be concentrated for safety. When loading is done poorly, the centre of gravity rises, producing a "cranky" ship whose every motion threatens disaster.

> Once she began, you felt that she would never stop, and this hopeless sensation, characterizing the motion of ships whose centre of gravity is brought too low by loading, was far from reassuring.
>
> (015-008)

The irony of the ballast claim is sharpest here: a ship declared "able to sail without ballast" may well do so — "for some little time before they turn turtle upon the crew" (013-003). The owner's boast is not a lie but a cruel half-truth, made possible because the consequence is borne by others.
