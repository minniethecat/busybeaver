# Figurehead

The **figurehead** is the carved ornamental figure mounted beneath the bowsprit of a sailing ship. For Conrad, writing in the early twentieth century, the figurehead was already a vanishing art — its "wandering impassivity" belonged to a world of sail that his generation was the last to witness.

## The Open-Air Gallery

In the late 1870s, the New South Dock presented a spectacle of figurehead carving that Conrad recognised as irreplaceable. Ships moored stern-to-quay extended their jibbooms far over the shore, their figureheads — "white-and-gold" and "almost dazzling in their purity" — overhanging the muddy wharfside:

> Their spars dwarfed with their loftiness the corrugated-iron sheds, their jibbooms extended far over the shore, their white-and-gold figureheads, almost dazzling in their purity, overhung the straight, long quay above the mud and dirt of the wharfside, with the busy figures of groups and single men moving to and fro, restless and grimy under their soaring immobility. (033-005)

## The Variety of the Carved Emblems

Each ship bore at her bow the carved emblem of her name, an open-air gallery of plaster-cast figures extending for a quarter of a mile along the dock. Conrad enumerates the extraordinary diversity: women with mural crowns, figures in flowing robes with gold fillets, warriors helmeted or bare, full-length kings, statesmen, lords and princesses — all white from top to toe — alongside dusky turbaned Eastern sultans bedizened in many colours:

> It was a noble gathering of the fairest and the swiftest, each bearing at the bow the carved emblem of her name, as in a gallery of plaster-casts, figures of women with mural crowns, women with flowing robes, with gold fillets on their hair or blue scarves round their waists, stretching out rounded arms as if to point the way; heads of men helmeted or bare; full lengths of warriors, of kings, of statesmen, of lords and princesses, all white from top to toe; with here and there a dusky turbaned figure, bedizened in many colours, of some Eastern sultan or hero, all inclined forward under the slant of mighty bowsprits as if eager to begin another run of 11,000 miles in their leaning attitudes. (035-003)

## The Seaman's Tribute

In clean shore togs, an elderly seaman pays tribute not to the ships themselves but to their figureheads — and draws a line between the vessels and the men who sailed them:

> "Ships"—and his keen glance, turning away from my face, ran along the vista of magnificent figureheads that in the late seventies used to overhang in a serried rank the muddy pavement by the side of the New South Dock—"ships are all right; it's the men in 'em…" (035-001)

## An Art No Eye Shall Behold Again

Conrad closes his meditation on figureheads with a question that doubles as an epitaph for the age of sail. The patient, pale company of queens and princesses, of kings and warriors and allegorical women, has run for good off the sea — their names vanished from newspapers, posters, and the minds of seamen:

> All that patient, pale company of queens and princesses, of kings and warriors, of allegorical women, of heroines and statesmen and heathen gods, crowned, helmeted, bareheaded, has run for good off the sea stretching to the last above the tumbling foam their fair, rounded arms; holding out their spears, swords, shields, tridents in the same unwearied, striving forward pose. And nothing remains but lingering perhaps in the memory of a few men, the sound of their names, vanished a long time ago from the first page of the great London dailies; from big posters in railway-stations and the doors of shipping offices; from the minds of sailors, dockmasters, pilots, and tugmen; from the hail of gruff voices and the flutter of signal flags exchanged between ships closing upon each other and drawing apart in the open immensity of the sea. (035-003)

Why attempt to capture in words an impression whose fidelity no critic can judge? Because, Conrad answers, the exhibition of shipbuilding and figurehead carving that lined the New South Dock shall never be seen again:

> These were the fine figureheads of the finest ships afloat. But why, unless for the love of the life those effigies shared with us in their wandering impassivity, should one try to reproduce in words an impression of whose fidelity there can be no critic and no judge, since such an exhibition of the art of shipbuilding and the art of figurehead carving as was seen from year's end to year's end in the open-air gallery of the New South Dock no man's eye shall behold again? (035-003)
