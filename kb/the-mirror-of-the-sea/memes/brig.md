# Brig

The **brig** is a two-masted sailing vessel, square-rigged on both fore and main masts, with a gaff-rigged spanker on the main. In the maritime world Conrad describes, the brig and her smaller cousin the brigantine are ubiquitous — the dependable carriers of coastal and ocean trade, neither as grand as the full-rigged ship nor as diminutive as the schooner.

## The Pretty Brigantine off the Cape

One of Conrad's most vivid encounters with a brigantine occurs near the Cape of Good Hope. Aboard a fine iron wool-clipper, the young second mate watches her pass close on a serene evening:

> I remember once seeing the commander — officially the master, by courtesy the captain — of a fine iron ship of the old wool fleet shaking his head at a very pretty brigantine. She was bound the other way. She was a taut, trim, neat little craft, extremely well kept; and on that serene evening when we passed her close she looked the embodiment of coquettish comfort on the sea. (023-001)

The wool-clipper captain, a man "brought up in big deep-water ships," could only mutter: "Fancy having to go about the sea in a thing like that!" (023-002). To him, the size of the craft under his feet was part of his conception of the sea itself.

## The Minor King

Conrad, as a young second mate, saw the encounter differently. The brigantine's commander appeared as a figure of quiet authority:

> To a young second mate the captain of the little pretty brigantine, sitting astride a camp stool with his chin resting on his hands that were crossed upon the rail, might have appeared a minor king amongst men. (023-003)

The two vessels passed within earshot "without a hail, reading each other's names with the naked eye" — a fleeting salute between craft of vastly different scale, each pursuing its own route round the stormy capes of the world.

## A Familiar Silhouette

The brig appears throughout Conrad's narrative as part of the ordinary fabric of sea-life — a vessel type so common that its mention requires no introduction. In the crowded waters of the Thames, a collision with a brig is treated as a minor incident:

> Ultimately they brought us up with the jibboom of a Dutch brig poking through our spanker — nothing worse. (009-007)

## The Overdue Brig

In the shipmaster's world, the brig shares the same fate as any deep-water vessel. The shipping gazette records her name alongside barques and ships when she fails to appear, her crew posted as missing:

> "The ship, or barque, or brig So-and-So, bound from such a port, with such and such cargo, for such another port, having left at such and such a date, last spoken at sea on such a day, and never having been heard of since." (016-005)

## Small vs. Great

The brigantine encounter crystallises a theme that runs through Conrad's memoir: the question of size at sea. The wool-clipper captain's gruff "Give me size" reflects one tradition. But Conrad, reflecting years later, knows that "a man brought up in big ships may yet take a peculiar delight in what we should both then have called a small craft" (023-004). The brigantine, doubling the Cape alone with her young commander, made no less a voyage for her modest tonnage.
