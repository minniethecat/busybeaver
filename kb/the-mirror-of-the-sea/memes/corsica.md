## Location and Identity

Corsica appears in *The Mirror of the Sea* not as a travel destination but as a cultural source — the island whose fierce traditions of honour, vendetta, and the *maquis* produced men like Dominic Cervoni. Conrad's portrait of Corsica is entirely refracted through the character of his padrone: the island is present as a set of values, instincts, and hereditary obligations that shaped the men who sailed its waters.

## Conrad's Description

> This is why — because in Corsica your dead will not leave you alone — Dominic's brother had to go into the maquis, into the bush on the wild mountainside, to dodge the gendarmes for the unforgivable crime of letting his knife get the better of another man's knife in a casual quarrel. (043-004)

> Astute and ruthless, he could have rivalled in resource the unfortunate son of Laertes and Anticlea. If he did not pit his craft and audacity against the very gods, it is only because the Olympian gods are dead. (042-006)

> She who was my cradle in those years had been built on the River of Savona by a famous builder of boats, was rigged in Corsica by another good man, and was manned by a crew drawn from the islands. (040-003)

## Maritime Significance

Corsica's maritime significance in Conrad's world is inseparable from its character as a Mediterranean island culture. The Corsican seaman — represented archetypally by Dominic — combined a fierce personal code with exceptional seamanship. Conrad notes that the *Tremolino* was "rigged in Corsica by another good man," suggesting a tradition of maritime craftsmanship that matched the island's reputation for producing formidable sailors. The Corsican coast was also the setting for the *Tremolino*'s final, desperate escape, when Dominic beached his beloved tartane rather than surrender her to the authorities.
