## Location and Identity

The Thames Estuary is the broad, shallow gateway through which the River Thames meets the North Sea — and through which every London-bound ship must pass. For Conrad, who approached it countless times at the end of long voyages, the estuary was charged with the complex emotions of arrival: relief, anticipation, and the subtle melancholy of a journey's end. He describes its landmarks with the precision of a navigator and the sensibility of a writer: the lightship at the Nore, the factory chimneys of Grays and Greenhithe, the "monstrous redbrick pile on the Essex shore."

## Conrad's Description

> From the offing the open estuary promises every possible fruition to adventurous hopes. That road open to enterprise and courage invites the explorer of coasts to new efforts towards the fulfilment of great expectations. (030-002)

> Amongst the great commercial streams of these islands, the Thames is the only one, I think, open to romantic feeling. (030-003)

> Coming in from the eastward, the bright colouring of the lightship marking the part of the river committed to the charge of an Admiral accentuates the dreariness and the great breadth of the Thames Estuary. (031-002)

> Then, on the slight turn of the Lower Hope Reach, clusters of factory chimneys come distinctly into view, tall and slender above the squat ranges of cement works in Grays and Greenhithe. (031-003)

> Between the crowded houses of Gravesend and the monstrous redbrick pile on the Essex shore the ship is surrendered fairly to the grasp of the river. That hint of loneliness, that soul of the sea which had accompanied her, abandons the ship. (031-004)

## Maritime Significance

The Thames Estuary was, for Conrad, the definitive arrival — the point where the sea surrendered to the land and the ship became a different creature. His descriptions emphasize the dramatic transition from the open ocean to the enclosed river: the loneliness of the sea "abandons the ship" in the narrowing reaches, replaced by the sounds and smells of industry. The estuary's lightships and landmarks were the final set of bearings a homeward-bound seaman would take, each one a step closer to London and to shore leave.
