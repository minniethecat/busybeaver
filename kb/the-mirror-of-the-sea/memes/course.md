# Course

The **course** is the direction in which a ship steers, set by the compass and plotted upon the chart. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad uses the word in its technical nautical sense — the line a vessel traces across the ocean — but also invests it with the weight of human intention: the course a man sets for his life.

## The First Course of the Passage

The moment of setting the first course after departure marks the true beginning of a voyage. The land has dropped away, and the ship commits herself to the open sea:

> I remember only one man who walked his deck with a springy step, and gave the first course of the passage in an elated voice. But he, as I learned afterwards, was leaving nothing behind him, except a welter of debts and threats of legal proceedings. (001-008)

The contrast is characteristic of Conrad's humour: most captains, he notes, give their first course sadly, burdened by thoughts of the wife, children, or "pet vice" left behind. This one captain was elated — because he had nothing to lose. The course he set was a course away from trouble, not toward a destination.

## The Track-Chart

Once the course is set, the ship begins to inscribe her passage upon the chart:

> In all the devious tracings the course of a sailing-ship leaves upon the white paper of a chart she is always aiming for that one little spot — maybe a small island in the ocean, a single headland upon the long coast of a continent, a lighthouse on a bluff, or simply the peaked form of a mountain like an ant-heap afloat upon the waters. (001-005)

The "devious tracings" are the daily pencil crosses that mark the ship's noon position — sixty, eighty, sometimes a hundred and thirty of them on a single passage. The course is never a straight line; it is a series of adjustments, tacks, and weather-routed deviations, all converging upon that single fixed point on the horizon: the Landfall.

## Course as Human Inclination

Conrad extends the navigational metaphor to describe human character. A man's "course" through life is shaped by the same interplay of intention, wind, and current that governs a ship:

> The true peace of God begins at any spot a thousand miles from the nearest land. (010-003)

The course that carries a ship into that profound peace is not merely a heading on the compass — it is the direction of a life that has chosen the sea. For Conrad, the course a seaman sets is inseparable from the kind of man he is.
