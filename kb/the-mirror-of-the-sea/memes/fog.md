## Definition

Fog at sea is the erasure of the visible world. Unlike a gale, which assaults the ship with violence, fog attacks by removal — it takes away the horizon, the sky, the landmarks by which a seaman orients himself. In Conrad's treatment, fog is a condition of moral and psychological as well as physical disorientation. It is the weather of uncertainty, of "overdue and missing" ships, of vessels that vanish without trace or explanation.

## In Conrad's Words

> A thin, silvery mist softened the calm and majestic splendour of light without shadows — seemed to render the sky less remote and the ocean less immense. It was one of the days, when the might of the sea appears as a beneficent power, a passive accomplice of human courage and endurance. (036-002)

> The captain was expatiating in a low conversational tone to the chief mate upon the danger of these derelicts, and upon his dread of coming upon them at night, when suddenly a man forward screamed out, "There's people on board of her, sir! I see them!" in a most extraordinary voice — a voice never heard before in our ship. (036-003)

> After the gradual cessation of all sound and movement on the faithful river, only the ringing of ships' bells is heard, mysterious and muffled in the white vapour from London Bridge right down to the Nore, for miles and miles in a decrescendo tinkling, to where the estuary broadens out into the North Sea. (032-007)

## The Moral Condition of Fog

Conrad associates fog with a particular state of moral uncertainty. Ships that go "missing" do so most often in fog. The fog conceals not only the physical dangers — other ships, rocks, the approaching shore — but also the human capacity to respond. In fog, the seaman is reduced to a state of anxious vigilance, straining to hear what he cannot see, dependent on the sound of bells, whistles, and the lead-line's report for his survival. This dependency on hearing rather than sight inverts the normal hierarchy of the senses at sea.

## Fog in the Thames Estuary

Conrad provides one of the most evocative fog descriptions in literature in his account of the Thames Estuary (chapter 32). Here the fog is not a danger to be avoided but an atmospheric condition that transforms the familiar river into something "mysterious and muffled." The ships' bells ring "in a decrescendo tinkling" through the white vapour, and the estuary becomes a place of ghostly sounds — a fitting home for the "faithful river" that receives all ships in the end.
