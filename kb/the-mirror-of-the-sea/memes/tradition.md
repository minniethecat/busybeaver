## Definition

Maritime tradition is the inherited body of practice, belief, and professional expectation that governs the conduct of seafarers. Unlike codified regulations, it lives in the example of older mariners, the unwritten rules of shipboard conduct, and the pride craftsmen take in work done properly. For Conrad, this tradition was not nostalgia but a living force — the accumulated wisdom without which no ship could safely round the Horn or weather a gale.

## In Conrad's Words

Seamanship itself, Conrad argues, is a tradition — an art preserved not in books but in the hands and habits of those who practise it:

> It is made up of accumulated tradition, kept alive by individual pride, rendered exact by professional opinion, and, like the higher arts, it is spurred on and sustained by discriminating praise. (007-003)

Individual captains embodied this tradition in their own way. Captain S—— of Conrad's acquaintance carried it in his insistence upon pressing every advantage:

> It was in Captain S——'s tradition rather to reprove his officers for not carrying on quite enough — in his phrase "for not taking every ounce of advantage of a fair wind." (012-001)

The tradition of fast passages was often carried from ship to ship by captains who had served in notable vessels. The captain of the *Tweed*, a celebrated wooden clipper, brought his habits into the age of iron:

> The Tweed had been a wooden vessel, and he brought the tradition of quick passages with him into the iron clipper. (011-004)

Ultimately, the great naval tradition of Britain — the tradition of Nelson and the age of fighting sail — passed not into institutions but into the keeping of individual men:

> His time had come; and, after the great sea officers, the great naval tradition passed into the keeping of a great man. (047-003)
