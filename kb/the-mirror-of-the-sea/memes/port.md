## Definition

The port is both the origin and the destination of every passage — the place from which a ship departs and to which she returns. In Joseph Conrad's maritime world, a port is not simply a geographical location but the marker that divides a seaman's life into discrete periods. A life at sea is "told out in passages from port to port," each arrival and departure closing and opening chapters in the continuous narrative of the sailor's existence. The relationship between the ship and her port is intimate: the port gives the ship her cargo, her crew, her purpose.

## In Conrad's Words

> The Departure is not the ship's going away from her port any more than the Landfall can be looked upon as the synonym of arrival. A ship may have left her port some time before; she may have been at sea, in the fullest sense of the phrase, for days; but, for all that, as long as the coast she was about to leave remained in sight, a southern hemisphere landsman might not have noticed the difference. But there is this difference, that the Departure does not take place so long as the land is in sight.
> (001-002)

> In a life whose worth is told out in passages from port to port, the splash of the anchor's fall and the thunderous rumbling of the chain are like the closing of a distant period, of which she seems conscious with a slight shudder of her whole frame. By this her rest is marked for her, and before her cable is made fast to the shore she will have assumed the inward stillness of the finished task.
> (006-004)

> "The ship, or barque, or brig So-and-So, bound from such a port, with such and such cargo, for such another port, having left at such and such a date, last spoken at sea on such a day, and never having been heard of afterwards, was posted at Lloyd's as missing." Such an entry in the shipping news is the epitaph of a ship.
> (016-005)

> London is a general cargo port, as is only proper for the greatest capital of the world to be. The Port of London remains always a general cargo port, receiving and sending out all the products of the earth.
> (032-002)

> But soon the course of the ship opens the entrance of the Medway, with its men-of-war moored in line, and the long wooden jetty of Port Victoria, with its few low buildings like the beginning of a hasty settlement upon a wild and unexplored shore. The famous Thames barges sit in brown clusters upon the water with an effect of birds floating upon a pond.
> (031-002)

## Technical Usage

In Conrad's era, the term "port" served as the universal designation for any harbour or haven where a vessel could load or discharge cargo. A ship's port of registry was inscribed on her stern, and her port of destination appeared in the shipping lists. The Port of London, stretching from the Pool of London down to Tilbury and the estuary, was the world's busiest general cargo port, handling goods from every corner of the globe. Ports had distinct hierarchies: a "port of call" was an intermediate stop; a "home port" the vessel's base; and a "foreign port" any harbour beyond national waters. The arrival into port marked the closing of the ship's log and the settling of accounts — a definitive punctuation in the life of the ship and her crew.
