## Definition

The stern is the aftmost part of a ship or boat, the rear section opposite the bow. In the maritime world Conrad knew, the stern was not merely a structural element but a vantage point from which the sea revealed its most contemplative aspect — a place from which to watch the wake recede and to meditate upon the vessel's passage through the water.

## A Place of Contemplation

Conrad repeatedly returns to the stern as a site of meditation and heightened perception. It is the place where the mariner, looking astern, sees the past recede and the future open before him. In the *Tremolino* episode, this quality is particularly pronounced:

> For a long time, leaning against the bulwark, I gazed over the stern at the grey column that seemed to stand swaying slightly in our wake always at the same distance. (044-009)

The "grey column" — a pursuing vessel — becomes, viewed from the stern, almost an abstraction, a fixed point in the wake rather than a threat. The stern perspective transforms urgency into contemplation.

> Dominic had been sitting motionless, like an inanimate black cone posed on the stern deck, near the rudder-head, with a small tassel fluttering on its sharp point, and for a time he preserved the immobility of his meditation. (044-017)

Dominic's stillness at the stern — "like an inanimate black cone" — suggests a figure who has become part of the ship itself. The stern deck, with its proximity to the rudder-head, is the domain of the helmsman and the commander; it is the place from which the ship is guided.

> I murmured, fascinated by the black hood turned immovably over the stern, as if in unlawful communion with that old sea of magicians, slave-dealers, exiles and warriors, the sea of legends and terrors, where the mariners of remote antiquity used to hear the wail of the nymphs mourning the loss of their human lovers. (044-046)

This is perhaps the most remarkable passage involving a stern in all of Conrad's work. The hooded figure gazing over the stern is not merely looking astern — he is in "unlawful communion" with the Mediterranean's entire dark history. The stern, here, becomes a portal to the past, a place where the boundary between the present voyage and the accumulated weight of maritime history dissolves.

> Dominic, with his arm round my shoulders, supported me in the stern sheets. (045-016)

## Stern Shape and Ship Character

The shape of a vessel's stern was, in the age of sail, a marker of her era and purpose. Conrad notes the "square-stern type" of the old West Indiamen — a design that spoke of an earlier, more stolid generation of ships:

> At one time they must have been full of good old slow West Indiamen of the square-stern type, that took their captivity, one imagines, as stolidly as they had faced the buffeting of the waves with the pretty old words of the sea shanty resounding day and night. (033-007)

The square stern was the mark of the eighteenth and early nineteenth century merchantman — broad, capacious, and slow. By Conrad's day, the clipper stern — finer, more elegant, built for speed — had largely displaced it. Conrad himself contrasts bow and stern design in his meditation on the *Tweed*:

> Perhaps there had been a touch of genius or the finger of good fortune in the fashioning of her lines at bow and stern. She had a great sheer, high bows, and a clumsy stern. (012-001)

The "clumsy stern" of the *Tweed* is notable precisely because it did not prevent her from becoming one of the fastest ships of her era. The passage suggests that ship design is not merely a matter of hydrodynamics — there is something ineffable, a "touch of genius," that transcends the merely rational arrangement of lines at bow and stern.

## Trim by the Stern

The relationship between bow and stern determined a ship's trim — her fore-and-aft balance in the water. A ship trimmed by the stern sat deeper aft, and this could dramatically affect her sailing qualities:

> Some ships sailed fast on an even keel, others had to be trimmed quite one foot by the stern, and I have heard of a ship that gave her best speed on a wind when so loaded as to float a couple of inches by the head. (014-001)

The art of loading a ship — determining how much weight went forward and how much aft — was, in Conrad's treatment, a subject of endless nuance. Each ship had her own preference, her own ideal trim, and the seaman who failed to discover it would never get the best from his vessel.

## Stem to Stern

The phrase "stem to stern" — from the foremost to the aftmost extremity — captures the completeness of a ship as an object. Conrad uses it to evoke the solid, unbroken mass of vessels moored in the Pool of London:

> That was in the days when, in the part called the Pool, just below London Bridge, the vessels moored stem and stern in the very strength of the tide formed one solid mass like an island covered with a forest of masts. (032-006)

The image of ships moored "stem and stern" — packed so tightly that they form "one solid mass" — is both a tribute to the density of London's maritime commerce and an elegy for a world that has vanished. The forest of masts that once covered the Pool has given way to the funnels of steamers and, eventually, to container cranes.
