## The Autocrat's Dominion

For Conrad, the Southern Ocean was the domain of the West Wind in its most untrammelled form. Here, in the "forties" south of the Equator, the wind ruled with "open, generous, frank, barbarous recklessness." It was a region where a sailing ship found no shelter, no lee shore, no respite — only the immense rolling swell and the relentless press of the westerly gales. Conrad's portrait of the Southern Ocean is also a portrait of power: the autocratic West Wind is "a great king, fit to rule over the great waters."

> The autocratic sway of the West Wind, whether forty north or forty south of the Equator, is characterized by an open, generous, frank, barbarous recklessness. For he is a great autocrat, and to be a great autocrat you must be a great barbarian. (029-004)

## A Three Days' Gale

Conrad's most vivid Southern Ocean memory is of a gale that lasted three days, leaving the ocean "tumbling heavily" and the sky "hung with rags of clouds that seemed to have been cut and hacked by the keen edge of a sou'-west gale." The detail is characteristic: it is the aftermath that stays in the memory — the exhausted sea, the torn sky, the sense of having endured something elemental.

> It was on a grey afternoon in the lull of a three days' gale that had left the Southern Ocean tumbling heavily upon our ship, under a sky hung with rags of clouds that seemed to have been cut and hacked by the keen edge of a sou'-west gale. (017-003)

## Off Kerguelen Land

In one of the most arresting passages in *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad recalls a storm near Kerguelen Land — those "tiny dots on the map of the Southern Ocean." When he looks at an atlas, he no longer sees mere cartographic marks; the "enraged physiognomy of that gale" is "as if engraved upon the paper." This is the essential Conrad: the sea not as geography but as lived experience, burned into memory.

> We were then, for reasons which it is not worth while to specify, in the close neighbourhood of Kerguelen Land; and now, when I open an atlas and look at the tiny dots on the map of the Southern Ocean, I see as if engraved upon the paper the enraged physiognomy of that gale. (023-007)

## The Rhythm of the Seasons

The Southern Ocean had its own calendar. As Conrad and his fellow "deep-water men" reckoned, the West Wind "made one year in three fairly lively for anybody having business upon the Atlantic or down there along the 'forties' of the Southern Ocean." The seaman's fatalism in the face of this rhythm — "You had to take the bitter with the sweet" — is the quiet corollary to the more dramatic passages about storm and survival.

> As we deep-water men always reckoned, he made one year in three fairly lively for anybody having business upon the Atlantic or down there along the "forties" of the Southern Ocean. You had to take the bitter with the sweet; and it cannot be denied he played carelessly with our lives and fortunes. (029-004)

## The Passage to New Zealand

The Southern Ocean lay on the great-circle route between the Cape of Good Hope or Cape Horn and the ports of Australia and New Zealand. Conrad records the loss of a steamer's propeller "down south, on her passage out to New Zealand" — a reminder that even steam power could not tame these waters. The ocean between the Antipodes and the Horn was the proving ground of ships and men alike.
