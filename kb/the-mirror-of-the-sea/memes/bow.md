## Definition

The bow is the forwardmost part of a ship or boat, the section that cuts through the water and first encounters the sea. In the age of sail, the shape of the bow — whether bluff and rounded or sharp and raked — was one of the defining features of a vessel, determining her speed and sea-keeping qualities. A ship's bow carried the carved figurehead, her name's emblem, and was where the anchors were dropped from.

## In Conrad's Words

> The spell of the fair wind has a subtle power to scatter a white-winged company of ships looking all the same way, each with its white fillet of tumbling foam under the bow. (010-002)

> Perhaps there had been a touch of genius or the finger of good fortune in the fashioning of her lines at bow and stern. (012-001)

> She had a great sheer, high bows, and a clumsy stern. (012-001)

> There is defiance in the remaining stumps of her masts, raised up like maimed limbs against the menacing scowl of a stormy sky; there is high courage in the upward sweep of her lines towards the bow. (018-009)

> Your anchor must drop from the bow of your ship with no turn of cable on any of its limbs, else you would be riding to a foul anchor. (005-007)

## Technical Usage

Conrad documents the precise nautical language of the bow as a bearing reference at sea. The phrase "broad on the bow" was used by those working the anchors on the forecastle — part of the concise, impressionistic lexicon that the author regarded as achieving "the just expression seizing upon the essential, which is the ambition of the artist in words" (006-001).

> It is the voice of the keeper of the ship's anchors that will answer: "Grows right ahead, sir," or "Broad on the bow," or whatever concise and deferential shout will fit the case. (006-001)

## Figureheads at the Bow

The bow carried more than structural purpose — it was the ship's face, bearing the carved [[figurehead]] that gave the vessel her identity. In the New South Dock, Conrad observed an "open-air gallery" of these bow-mounted effigies:

> It was a noble gathering of the fairest and the swiftest, each bearing at the bow the carved emblem of her name, as in a gallery of plaster-casts, figures of women with mural crowns, women with flowing robes, with gold fillets on their hair or blue scarves round their waists, stretching out rounded arms as if to point the way; heads of men helmeted or bare; full lengths of warriors, of kings, of statesmen, of lords and princesses, all white from top to toe. (035-003)

> All that patient, pale company of queens and princesses, of kings and warriors, of allegorical women, of heroines and statesmen and heathen gods, crowned, helmeted, bareheaded, has run for good off the sea stretching to the last above the tumbling foam their fair, rounded arms; holding out their spears, swords, shields, tridents in the same unwearied, striving forward pose. (035-003)

## Related Concepts

The bow is the forward counterpart of the [[stern]]. The [[figurehead]] was traditionally mounted at the bow. The [[anchor]] is dropped from the bow, and the [[windlass]] and [[cable]] are associated bow equipment. The shape of the bow — whether "blunt" and "honest" as Conrad described West Indiamen (033-007) — spoke to a vessel's era and purpose.
