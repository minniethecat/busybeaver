## "The Fine Docks at Blackwall"

In Conrad's panoramic survey of the Thames docks, the Victoria and Albert Docks occupy the middle distance — downstream of the West India Docks, below Blackwall, on past the entrance at Gallions Reach. They are "the fine docks at Blackwall," and the epithet is carefully chosen: these were among the largest enclosed dock systems in the world when built, capable of accommodating the biggest ships of the late Victorian era. Conrad passes them in his survey as a mariner would pass them from the deck of a ship entering the river.

> …down through the interesting group of West India Docks, the fine docks at Blackwall, on past the Galleons Reach entrance of the Victoria and Albert Docks, right down to the vast gloom of the great basins in Tilbury… (032-003)

## The Upper Limit of the Dock "Forest"

The Albert Docks mark the downstream boundary of the stretch that Conrad describes as a "virgin forest" of waterside buildings. From London Bridge to the Albert Docks, the Thames shore is "a thing grown up, not made" — a jungle of warehouses, wharves, and jetties that hides "the depths of London's infinitely varied, vigorous, seething life." Beyond the Albert Docks, the river opens out towards the Essex marshes and the isolated Tilbury basins.

> This stretch of the Thames from London Bridge to the Albert Docks is to other watersides of river ports what a virgin forest would be to a garden. (031-005)

## The Royal Docks

The Royal Victoria Dock (opened 1855) and the Royal Albert Dock (opened 1880) together formed the largest enclosed dock area in the world — over 200 acres of water. They were built to accommodate the new generation of steam ships that were too large for the older docks upstream. Conrad, who knew the London docks intimately from his years as a mate on ships loading and discharging there, understood their scale and their significance: they were the industrial heart of the world's greatest port.

## Romance in Usefulness

Like all the Thames docks, the Albert Docks share what Conrad calls "their common trait of being romantic in their usefulness." They are not beautiful in any conventional sense — Conrad concedes that "beauty" is "a vain word" for such industrial landscapes. But the sheer scale of the enterprise, the connection to all the oceans of the world, and the absorption of the docks into the life of the river give them a glamour that transcends mere aesthetics.
