## Definition

In the maritime intelligence columns of Conrad's era, the terms "overdue" and "missing" occupied a grim and formal vocabulary. A ship posted as *overdue* had passed her expected arrival date but might still appear; a ship declared *missing* had passed beyond hope. Conrad frames these two words as the poles of a cruel emotional spectrum, familiar to every sailor, shipowner, and family ashore.

## In Conrad's Words

> On some days there appears the heading "Overdue"—an ominous threat of loss and sorrow trembling yet in the balance of fate.
> (016-003)

> Only a very few days more—appallingly few to the hearts which had set themselves bravely to hope against hope—three weeks, a month later, perhaps, the name of ships under the blight of the "Overdue" heading shall appear again in the column of "Shipping Intelligence," but under the final declaration of "Missing."
> (016-004)

> But if the word "missing" brings all hope to an end and settles the loss of the underwriters, the word "overdue" confirms the fears already born in many homes ashore, and opens the door of speculation in the market of risks.
> (018-001)

> For if a "missing" ship has never turned up within the memory of seamen of my generation, the name of an "overdue" ship, trembling as it were on the edge of the fatal heading, has been known to appear as "arrived."
> (018-003)

> "The ship So-and-So, bound to such a port, and posted as 'overdue,' has been reported yesterday as having arrived safely at her destination."
> (018-006)

## The Formal Oration

Between "overdue" and "missing" lies a formal language — the shipping intelligence's version of a funeral oration, composed in the dry prose of commerce but carrying a weight of finality that no literary elegy could match:

> "The ship, or barque, or brig So-and-So, bound from such a port, with such and such cargo, for such another port, having left at such and such a date, last spoken at sea on such a day, and never having been heard of since, was posted today as missing." Such in its strictly official eloquence is the form of funeral orations on ships that, perhaps wearied with a long struggle, or in some unguarded moment that may come to the readiest of us, had let themselves be overwhelmed by a sudden blow from the enemy.
> (016-005)

The phrase "last spoken at sea" captures the peculiar isolation of the ship — the vessel whose fate can only be inferred from silence. A ship at sea disappears not in a moment but gradually, passing from "overdue" to "missing" in the columns of a gazette while, far away, families calculate the dwindling odds.

## The Unholy Fascination

Conrad does not flinch from the psychological power of the missing:

> The unholy fascination of dread dwells in the thought of the last moments of a ship reported as "missing" in the columns of the Shipping Gazette.
> (017-001)

> Nobody ever comes back from a "missing" ship to tell how hard was the death of the craft, and how sudden and overwhelming the last anguish of her men.
> (017-010)

The missing ship is a closed book — "nobody ever comes back" — and yet the imagination cannot stop returning to it. This is the "unholy fascination" that Conrad names and refuses to sentimentalise.

## The Market of Hope

Between overdue and missing, there existed a grim financial market:

> There is a class of optimists ready to reinsure an "overdue" ship at a heavy premium.
> (018-002)

> And if she drifts into an unpopulated part of the ocean she may soon become overdue.
> (018-008)

The optimists who reinsure overdue vessels are betting on arrival — and Conrad records, against all probability, that such bets occasionally paid off. An overdue ship could reappear; a missing ship, in the memory of his generation, never did. The boundary between the two headings was, in practice, absolute.

## Near-Miss Narratives

Conrad includes several stories of ships that nearly joined the missing column. The *Tremolino* herself, after her final voyage, might have become one:

> And she would have been posted really as "overdue," or maybe as "missing," had she not been sighted in a snowstorm, vaguely, like a phantom ship, making for a French port.
> (019-002)

> But in that maze there lurked all the romance of the "overdue" and a menacing hint of "missing."
> (019-005)

These near-miss stories are exceptions that prove the rule. For every vessel sighted "vaguely, like a phantom ship" through a snowstorm, many more simply ceased to exist — their names migrating, column by column, from "overdue" to "missing" in the pages of the Shipping Gazette.

## A Survivor's Tale

Conrad also records the story of a ship that escaped through a single turn of the wheel — a moment's decision that made the difference between the overdue column and the shipping intelligence's routine arrivals:

> "But for the turn of that wheel just in time, there would have been another case of a 'missing' ship."
> (017-009)

This is the counterpoint to the "unholy fascination": the knowledge that a missing ship is not a distinct species but an ordinary vessel, identical in every respect to those that arrive, distinguished only by a circumstance — a turn of the wheel, a sudden blow, an unguarded moment — that decided everything.
