# Royal

The **royal** is the highest square sail on a sailing ship's mast, set above the topgallant. It is the uppermost sail ordinarily carried, and in Conrad's time, its yard was "a good way up above her decks" — a dizzying height that made the ship's spars seem preposterously tall when the vessel heeled over.

## The Height of the Royals

Conrad conveys the sheer altitude of the royal yards through the experience of watching a ship heel:

> The man who has looked upon his ship going over too far is made aware of the preposterous tallness of a ship's spars. It seems impossible but that those gilt trucks which one had to tilt one's head back to see, now falling into the lower plane of vision, must perforce hit the very edge of the horizon. Such an experience gives you a better impression of the loftiness of your spars than any amount of running aloft could do. And yet in my time the royal yards of an average profitable ship were a good way up above her decks. (005-004)

The image is vertiginous: the royal yards, normally seen by craning the neck upward, descend to the edge of vision as the ship leans into a squall. The height of the royal becomes, in that moment, terrifyingly measurable.

## Royals Set in a Gale

The decision to carry royals in heavy weather is a judgment of seamanship — and of character. In Conrad's recollection of Captain S——, the royals are set while the ship rushes through the dark:

> Night clouds racing overhead, wind howling, royals set, and the ship rushing on in the dark, an immense white sheet of foam level with the lee rail. (011-009)

The captain, bareheaded in the gale, emerges from the companion to survey the scene: royals still set, the ship pressed to her limit. The royal sail, highest and most exposed, becomes the measure of how hard a ship is being driven — and how much trust the master places in his vessel and crew.

## The Royal and the King

Conrad's extended metaphor of the West Wind as a king — "the royal master of high latitudes" — plays on the shared etymology of *royal* and *regal*. The westerly weather's "royal mood" (026-006), his "royal treasure-house" of sunset jewels (026-009), and his "royal game of quoits with hurricanes" (027-007) all draw upon the language of kingship. The sail and the sovereign share a name because both occupy the highest place — one on the mast, the other on the throne.
