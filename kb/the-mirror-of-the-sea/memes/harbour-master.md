# Harbour-Master

The **harbour-master** (also dockmaster or berthing-master) is the port official charged with the berthing, safety, and orderly disposition of vessels within a harbour. In Conrad's London docks, these men are not mere functionaries — they are the gaolers of ships that have exchanged the liberty of the open sea for the captivity of the berth, and their relationship with the vessels under their charge is one of the most psychologically complex portraits in *The Mirror of the Sea*.

## The Gaoler of Ships

The berthing-master is introduced as the warden of a ship's dockyard captivity — a figure whose very presence underscores the transformation of the ship from a free creature of the open ocean to a prisoner of the quay:

> Chain cables and stout ropes keep her bound to stone posts at the edge of a paved shore, and a berthing-master, with brass buttons on his coat, walks about like a weather-beaten and ruddy gaoler, casting jealous, watchful glances upon the moorings that fetter a ship lying passive and still and safe, as if lost in deep regrets of her days of liberty and danger on the sea. (033-001)

The simile is deliberate and extended: the berthing-master is a "gaoler," his brass buttons the badge of office, his glances "jealous" and "watchful." The ship, by implication, is a prisoner — "passive and still and safe" — dreaming of its former life of "liberty and danger." The dock, in this rendering, is a prison; the berthing-master, its warden.

## Renegades of the Quay

But the berthing-master is not merely a gaoler — he is a renegade. Conrad insists that these dock officials were once seamen themselves, and that their present role represents a kind of moral fall:

> The swarm of renegades — dockmasters, berthing-masters, gatemen, and suchlike — appear to nurse an immense distrust of the captive ship's resignation. There never seem chains and ropes enough to satisfy their minds concerned with the safe binding of free ships to the strong, muddy, enslaved earth. (033-002)

The charge is precise: these men, having known the sea, now devote themselves to binding ships to the "strong, muddy, enslaved earth." Their distrust is not rational prudence but something darker — a gleeful tyranny over creatures they once served:

> I brand them for renegades, because most of them have been sailors in their time. As if the infirmities of old age — the grey hair, the wrinkles at the corners of the eyes, and the knotted veins of the hands — were the symptoms of moral poison, they prowl about the quays with an underhand air of gloating over the broken spirit of noble captives. (033-002)

The physical signs of age — grey hair, wrinkles, knotted veins — are recast as "symptoms of moral poison." The old sailors who have taken posts ashore are not simply retired; they have gone over to the enemy. They want "more fenders, more breasting-ropes; they want more springs, more shackles, more fetters; they want to make ships with volatile souls as motionless as square blocks of stone" (033-002). The accumulation of nautical terms — fenders, breasting-ropes, springs, shackles — becomes a kind of obsessive litany of restraint.

The passage reaches its bitter climax with an image of the dockscape as a torture chamber for ships: "Here and there cargo cranes looking like instruments of torture for ships swing cruel hooks at the end of long chains. Gangs of dock-labourers swarm with muddy feet over the gangways" (033-002). The contrast between the ship — a creature of wind and water — and the "men of the earth, earthy" who trample "unconcerned, brutal and hobnailed upon her helpless body" is one of the book's most powerful expressions of the seaman's perspective on the shore.

## The Art of Breaking Windows

The berthing-master possesses a peculiar expertise born of long practice — an eye for dangers invisible to the seaman fresh from sea. Conrad records his own astonishment at being warned that his ship's topgallant yards might break warehouse windows:

> I own that for a time I was struck dumb by the bizarre associations of yardarms and windowpanes. To break windows is the last thing one would think of in connection with a ship's topgallant yard, unless, indeed, one were an experienced berthing-master in one of the London docks. (031-007)

This is the berthing-master's gift: an intimate knowledge of the unnatural geometries produced when a ship — a structure designed to move through open water — is wedged into a space designed for warehouses and railway tracks. The old berthing-master, with his "bumpy, shiny boots" and his voice hoarse with a lifetime of North Sea fogs, sees dangers that the ship's own officers cannot imagine.

## Benevolence Amidst the Bustle

Yet Conrad's portrait is not without nuance. Even the crabbed old dockmaster is susceptible to the charm of a fine new ship:

> Even crabbed old dockmasters look at her with benevolent eyes. (035-004)

This is the moment when a new vessel — unnamed, untried, fresh from the builder's yard — arrives at the dock to load for her maiden voyage. The dockmasters, for all their "moral poison" and their obsession with fetters, are still capable of responding to the beauty of a ship. The benevolence is a flicker of the seaman they once were, before age and the shore claimed them.

## The Vanishing Figure

The harbour-master belongs to a world that has largely vanished — the world of sail, of figureheads, of the great clipper ships that once crowded the London docks. Conrad's elegy for that world is also an elegy for the men who tended its captive vessels:

> And nothing remains but lingering perhaps in the memory of a few men, the sound of their names, vanished a long time ago from the first page of the great London dailies; from big posters in railway-stations and the doors of shipping offices; from the minds of sailors, dockmasters, pilots, and tugmen; from the hail of gruff voices and the flutter of signal flags exchanged between ships closing upon each other and drawing apart in the open immensity of the sea. (035-003)

The dockmasters are listed among the custodians of memory — alongside sailors, pilots, and tugmen — as the last men who remember the names of the great ships. When those men are gone, the names will be gone with them, and the harbour-master's world — of figureheads and clipper bows, of "the finest ships afloat" (035-003) resting in the New South Dock — will have passed beyond recall.

## The Seaman's Verdict

Conrad's treatment of the harbour-master is coloured by the perspective of the seaman arriving from sea, for whom the dock represents captivity and the dock official represents the captor. It is a partial view — deliberately so — and it captures something essential about the psychological transition from the freedom of the ocean to the confinement of the harbour. The harbour-master, in this rendering, is the face of the shore: necessary, competent, and faintly malevolent, presiding over the imprisonment of creatures that were never meant to be still.
