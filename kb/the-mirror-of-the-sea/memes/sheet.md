## Definition

In nautical usage a *sheet* is a rope or line attached to the lower corner (clew) of a sail, used to trim the sail's angle to the wind. Hauling in the sheet brings the sail closer to the centreline of the ship (close-hauled, for sailing upwind); easing the sheet lets the sail out (running free, for sailing downwind). The term belongs to the fundamental vocabulary of sail-handling — every fore-and-aft sail has its sheet, and the cry "Haul the sheets!" has driven the work of seamen for centuries.

Conrad uses the word in both its technical sail-handling sense and its older meaning — a broad, flat surface or expanse. A "sheet of water" in a calm harbour, a "sheet of foam" in a gale, the "stern sheets" of a boat: all appear in *The Mirror of the Sea*, and the double sense enriches his prose, the sail-control line always hovering behind the image of a smooth or torn surface.

## Sail-Handling

Though Conrad rarely describes the mechanics of sail-trimming in isolation, the sheet is implicit in every passage about a vessel's handling. When headsails are set and trimmed, the sheets are what hold them against the wind's force:

> Technically speaking, they are "secured inboard"; and, on the forecastle head, lashed down to ringbolts with ropes and chains, under the straining sheets of the headsails, they look very idle and as if asleep.
>
> (005-001)

The image is exact: sheets under strain transmit the wind's force to the ship. A "straining sheet" is a working sheet — the ship is under way, the canvas drawing. Conrad's eye catches the contrast between the idle gear lashed on deck and the taut lines that are doing the real work.

## In Conrad's Words

Conrad's most characteristic use of "sheet" is metaphorical, turning the flat surface of calm water into an image of perfect repose:

> For a ship with her sails furled on her squared yards, and reflected from truck to waterline in the smooth gleaming sheet of a landlocked harbour, seems, indeed, to a seaman's eye the most perfect picture of slumbering repose.
>
> (006-003)

Here the "smooth gleaming sheet" of the harbour is a mirror, doubling the ship's image. The sheet is not a sail-control line but a surface — yet the nautical reader feels the ghost of the sail-sheet behind the word, the contrast between the straining line at sea and the glassy calm in port.

In storm the sheet turns violent. Conrad describes the West Wind's southwesterly mood as a world without light, illuminated only by the sea's own phosphorescence:

> The southwesterly mood of the great West Wind is a lightless mood, without sun, moon, or stars, with no gleam of light but the phosphorescent flashes of the great sheets of foam that, boiling up on each side of the ship, fling bluish gleams upon her dark and narrow hull, rolling as she runs, chased by enormous seas, distracted in the tumult.
>
> (026-011)

The "great sheets of foam" are not flat and smooth but torn and luminous — the same word now describing violence rather than repose. It is a characteristic Conradian pivot: the sheet, whether of silk or foam, is the surface upon which the sea writes its mood.

## Related Concepts

The sheet is one of several lines used to control sails, alongside [[halyard]], [[brace]], and [[tack]]. It belongs to the family of [[sail-handling]] terms that describe the seaman's primary work: trimming canvas to the wind. The sheet also connects to [[running]] — to "run free" is to ease the sheets and let the wind fill the sails from astern.
