**Astern** is the bearing that lies behind a ship's stern — the direction of what has been left behind. For Conrad, however, astern is never merely a navigational bearing. It is charged with meaning: the coast one says goodbye to, the vanishing wake of the ship, the pursuer that appears on the horizon, the menacing sky that darkens behind, and the great sea that lifts a vessel from behind and sweeps on ahead.

## The coast astern

Departure, in Conrad's philosophy, is no mere geographical event but a severance. When a ship puts to sea, the land falls astern and with it all the complications of shore life. Conrad defines departure as "the technical, as distinguished from the sentimental, 'goodbye'" — and the coast astern is the sign that the voyage has truly begun: "Henceforth he has done with the coast astern of his ship" (001-004). The world astern is the world the seaman leaves behind.

> It is the technical, as distinguished from the sentimental, "goodbye." Henceforth he has done with the coast astern of his ship. (001-004)

## The wake astern

What a ship leaves behind astern is not just water but an image of impermanence. Conrad describes how ships vanish astern "as easily as the light air-bubbles in the swirls of the ship's wake, and vanish into a great silence" (002-008). Astern is the space of things that recede, fade, and are lost.

> They seem to be left astern as easily as the light air-bubbles in the swirls of the ship's wake, and vanish into a great silence. (002-008)

## The sea from astern

Astern is not only a direction of loss. In heavy weather, the sea itself comes from astern — the great combers that lift a vessel and sweep forward, filling the trough with foam. In one of Conrad's most vivid passages, a small barque runs before a gale, the sea catching her from behind:

> The solemn thundering combers caught her up from astern, passed her with a fierce boiling up of foam level with the bulwarks, swept on ahead with a swish and a roar: and the little vessel, dipping her jibboom into the tumbling froth, would go on running in a smooth, glassy hollow, a deep valley between two ridges of the sea, hiding the horizon ahead and astern.
> (023-005)

The sea from astern is both threat and propulsion — the force that could overwhelm the ship but instead, in the right hands, drives her forward. The "deep valley between two ridges" with the horizon hidden both ahead and astern is the elemental isolation of the open sea.

## Danger from astern

Astern is not always a peaceful direction. In the desperate escape of the *Tremolino*, the direction astern takes on a sinister quality:

> He could spare no eyes and no thought but for the slight column-shape astern of us.
> (044-010)

> "I tell you she is in chase," he affirmed moodily, after one short glance astern.
> (044-014)

> Astern the sky had a menacing look.
> (045-002)

The triple repetition of astern in the *Tremolino* chapters builds a cumulative dread: the column-shape of the pursuer, the muttered conviction of the chase, the sky itself turning hostile. Astern is where trouble follows — a direction the seaman watches with as much anxiety as the course ahead.
