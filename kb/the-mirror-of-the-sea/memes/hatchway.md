A **hatchway** is a rectangular opening in a vessel's deck, fitted with a weathertight cover, through which cargo is loaded into and discharged from the holds. In the age of sail, the main-hatch was the central artery of a merchant ship's working life — everything that went into or came out of the ship passed through it.

Conrad depicts the hatchway as a site of intense labour, from the careful stowage of sailing-ship days to the frantic mechanical haste of the steamer era:

> There has been a time when a ship's chief mate, pocketbook in hand and pencil behind his ear, kept one eye aloft upon his riggers and the other down the hatchway on the stevedores, and watched the discharge of the cargo. (013-001)

The contrast between old and new methods of working cargo is one of Conrad's recurring themes. The hatchway, once a place of skilled labour under the chief mate's careful supervision, becomes in the steamer age merely a hole into which cargo is dumped by machinery:

> Her cargo is not stowed in any sense; it is simply dumped into her through six hatchways, more or less, by twelve winches or so, with clatter and hurry and racket and heat, in a cloud of steam and a mist of dust. (013-006)

The hatchway is also a place of danger — a fall into an open hatch could be fatal, and Conrad uses this peril to comic effect in his portrait of the unfortunate seaman on the *Tremolino*:

> He would vanish heels overhead into open hatchways, into scuttles, behind upended casks, according to the place where he happened to come into contact with his uncle's mighty arm. (043-008)

Even the quieter side of hatchway life finds its way into Conrad's pages. The main-hatch served as an informal gathering place — a platform where off-duty seamen could sit, smoke, and trade stories:

> They got down from their perches and told each other impolite stories in racy language, every word of which reached me distinctly over the bulwarks as I sat smoking on the main-hatch. (034-002)

The hatchway thus functions as a microcosm of shipboard life: a place of work, of danger, and of fleeting leisure — the crossroads of a vessel's vertical world.
