# *Por el Rey*

The ***Por el Rey*** was a balancelle belonging to the same [[carlist-syndicate|Carlist syndicate]] that owned the *[[tremolino|Tremolino]]*. Her name — Spanish for "For the King" — was the rallying cry of the Carlist cause, the legitimist movement that sought to restore the Spanish Bourbon monarchy under Carlos VII. She appears in Conrad's narrative as part of the syndicate's small fleet of smuggling vessels operating along the Mediterranean coast, and her name, invoked repeatedly, becomes a refrain that echoes through the intrigues of the syndicate's inner circle.

## The Carlist Context

The *Por el Rey* operated under the banner of a political cause, but Conrad leaves no doubt that the Carlist enterprise was also — perhaps primarily — a commercial one. The syndicate's vessels ran contraband and performed other lucrative services along the Spanish and French coasts, their political affiliations providing cover and connections rather than ideological purpose:

> She had taken a little furnished house on the Prado for the good of the cause — *Por el Rey*! She was always taking little houses for somebody's good, for the sick or the sorry, for broken-down artists, cleaned-out gamblers, temporarily unlucky speculators — *vieux amis* — old friends, as she used to explain apologetically, with a shrug of her fine shoulders. (042-001)

The cause was real enough — the syndicate maintained a Carlist salon in Marseille where the faithful gathered — but its day-to-day operations were indistinguishable from those of any other Mediterranean smuggling enterprise:

> But it was a Carlist salon, and as such we were made welcome. (041-005)

## Doña Rita and the Cause

The *Por el Rey* is inseparable from the figure of [[dona-rita|Doña Rita]], the syndicate's charismatic agent, whose theatrical invocations of the Carlist cause provided cover for a network of influence and intrigue that stretched from Marseille to Paris:

> One of these last was extremely amusing in the imitations, she gave us in confidence, of various highly-placed personages she was perpetually rushing off to Paris to interview in the interests of the cause — *Por el Rey*! For she was a Carlist, and of Basque blood at that, with something of a lioness in the expression of her courageous face (especially when she let her hair down), and with the volatile little soul of a sparrow dressed in fine Parisian feathers, which had the trick of coming off disconcertingly at unexpected moments. (041-007)

Doña Rita's family connections ran deep into the Carlist infrastructure:

> She had an uncle still living, a very effective Carlist, too, the priest of a little mountain parish in Guipuzcoa. (041-008)

The salon in Marseille was a place of passionate political conviction — and unpredictable physical enthusiasm. After news of a Carlist success:

> All I know is that one evening, entering incautiously the salon of the little house just after the news of a considerable Carlist success had reached the faithful, I was seized round the neck and waist and whirled recklessly along the open space before the sofa by a mysterious and unaccountable character, a retired hidalgo of a subsisting but insecure fortune, known to everybody in the house, from Doña Rita and myself downwards, as "the Catalan." (042-002)

## The Balancelle Fleet

The *Por el Rey* was one of at least two balancelles in the syndicate's service, the other being the *[[tremolino|Tremolino]]*. These vessels, with their raking masts and enormous lateen sails, were the workhorses of the western Mediterranean — fast, shallow-draught, and capable of the quick passages and sudden disappearances that smuggling required:

> The balancelle hummed and quivered as she flew along, dancing lightly under our feet. (044-001)

Conrad's account of a chase at sea reveals the balancelle's reputation among authorities:

> At daylight, however, sighting a balancelle ahead under a press of canvas, they had made sail in chase. (044-019)

The command of such a vessel in dangerous waters was entrusted to men like [[dominic-cervoni|Dominic Cervoni]], whose austere competence Conrad captures in a single moment:

> "*Prenez la barre, monsieur*," Dominic broke the silence suddenly in an austere voice. "Take hold of the tiller." He bent his hood to my ear. "The balancelle is yours. Keep her as she is running." (045-010)
