## "The Interesting Group of West India Docks"

In Conrad's panoramic survey of the Thames docks, the West India Docks occupy a distinctive place. They are not "cosy" like St. Katherine's or "venerable" like the London Docks — they are "interesting." This epithet is not faint praise but reflects Conrad's conviction that each dock has "its own peculiar physiognomy, its own expression." The West India Docks, built at the height of the sugar and rum trade with the Caribbean, embodied a specific chapter in London's maritime history.

> Beginning with the cosy little St. Katherine's Dock, lying overshadowed and black like a quiet pool amongst rocky crags, through the venerable and sympathetic London Docks… down through the interesting group of West India Docks, the fine docks at Blackwall, on past the Galleons Reach entrance of the Victoria and Albert Docks, right down to the vast gloom of the great basins in Tilbury, each of those places of restraint for ships has its own peculiar physiognomy, its own expression. (032-003)

## A Monument to Empire

The West India Docks were built between 1800 and 1802, specifically for the West India trade — sugar, rum, mahogany, and other colonial commodities. At the time of their opening, they were the largest dock enterprise in the world, with import and export docks linked by a canal across the Isle of Dogs. Conrad, who knew London's maritime geography intimately, understood that these docks were not merely engineering works but physical embodiments of the imperial economy — the places where the produce of distant colonies entered the metropolitan world.

## Romantic in Usefulness

The West India Docks share with all the Thames docks what Conrad calls "their common trait of being romantic in their usefulness." This is the central paradox of Conrad's dockside writing: the docks are not beautiful in any conventional sense, but their sheer concentration of human labour, their connection to distant places, and their absorption into the life of the river give them a "glamour" that beauty alone could not supply. The West India Docks, with their vast warehouses of sugar and rum, were among the most palpably "romantic" of the dock group — their very name a reminder of the oceanic world beyond the Thames.

> And what makes them unique and attractive is their common trait of being romantic in their usefulness. (032-003)

## The New South Dock Connection

Conrad's own early professional memories centred on the New South Dock, which belonged to the West India Dock group. It was there that he served as second mate and later as mate of the *Highland Forest*, and where he observed the daily life of the London docks at close quarters. His affection for the West India Docks is thus not merely aesthetic but autobiographical — they were the backdrop to his own formation as a seaman.
