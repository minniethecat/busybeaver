## Definition

The cabin is the enclosed living and working space of a ship's officers, most significantly the captain's quarters. In the world of *The Mirror of the Sea*, the cabin is far more than a room: it is the sanctuary of command, where the master withdraws behind a veneered door; the nerve-centre of navigation, where charts lie spread under a smoky lamp; and the place of solitude where logbooks are perused and winter is endured. The cabin exists in tension with the deck--the commander is drawn irresistibly upwards when land approaches, yet the cabin remains the "holy of holies" of his authority.

## The Cabin-Door as Boundary of Command

The captain's cabin-door is the most significant door aboard ship. In Conrad's first voyage as chief mate, the real commander remains invisible behind "a maple-wood veneered cabin-door with a white china handle." The young officer feels himself a commander, but the door reminds him of the truth: his authority is delegated, provisional. The door separates two orders of command--the one who gives the final word, and the one who carries it out. It is both barrier and reassurance: the captain is there, "backing up my self-confidence," even when unseen.

> On my first voyage as chief mate with good Captain MacW—— I remember that I felt quite flattered, and went blithely about my duties, myself a commander for all practical purposes. Still, whatever the greatness of my illusion, the fact remained that the real commander was there, backing up my self-confidence, though invisible to my eyes behind a maple-wood veneered cabin-door with a white china handle. (002-003)

## The Restlessness of Landfall

When land approaches, the cabin loses its hold on the commander. Conrad describes a peculiar transformation: the captain, who in the open sea might seek the cabin's seclusion, becomes unable to remain within it. His spirit "is tormented by an unconquerable restlessness" and he is drawn to the deck, to gaze ahead through straining eyes. The cabin, which was the seat of his authority, becomes almost intolerable as the moment of arrival nears. The holy of holies of the captain's stateroom cannot contain the anticipation of landfall.

> But it is not moved to seek seclusion, and to remain, hidden and inert, shut up in a small cabin with the solace of a good bodily appetite. When about to make the land, the spirit of the ship's commander is tormented by an unconquerable restlessness. It seems unable to abide for many seconds together in the holy of holies of the captain's stateroom; it will go out on deck and gaze ahead, through straining eyes, as the appointed moment comes nearer. (002-009)

## The Cabin as Record and Memory

The cabin is also the repository of the ship's history. The old logbooks, kept in the cabin, are a record of voyages past--the ship's luck, her behaviour, the good times she had, and the troubles she escaped. A new chief mate, perusing these logs in the cabin, enters into a dialogue with his predecessors across time. The cabin becomes a library of maritime memory, its shelves containing the narratives that give continuity to the ship's identity.

> I was already familiar with his handwriting in the old logbooks I had been perusing in my cabin with a natural curiosity, looking up the records of my new ship's luck, of her behaviour, of the good times she had had, and of the troubles she had escaped. (015-005)

## The Cabin in Extremity

In moments of crisis, the cabin takes on a quality of heightened stillness. When the ship runs aground, Conrad enters the cuddy to find his captain sitting at the head of the table "like a statue." The swing-table, which has been in constant motion for seventy days, hangs perfectly still. The captain's face, always ruddy from wind and sea, now shows a skull that "shone dead white, like a dome of ivory." The cabin here registers the unnatural stillness of a ship that has stopped--a stillness more alarming than any storm.

> There was a strange motionlessness of everything in that pretty little cabin. The swing-table which for seventy odd days had been always on the move, if ever so little, hung quite still above the soup-tureen. Nothing could have altered the rich colour of my commander's complexion, laid on generously by wind and sea; but between the two tufts of fair hair above his ears, his skull, generally suffused with the hue of blood, shone dead white, like a dome of ivory. (021-006)

In heavy weather, the cabin becomes a place of chaos. After a sea bursts the poop skylights, Conrad crawls into his bunk "boots and hat on, all standing," knowing everything is soaking wet. He remains in "a nightmarish state between waking and sleeping" before staggering back on deck. The cabin, stripped of its comforts, becomes merely another part of the embattled ship.

> We had just gone down below for a moment to commune in a battened-down cabin, with a large white chart lying limp and damp upon a cold and clammy table under the light of a smoky lamp. (027-003)

> After two hours of futile, lightheaded, inconsequent thinking upon all things under heaven in that dark, dank, wet and devastated cabin, I arose suddenly and staggered up on deck. (027-004)

## The Cabin and the Barometer

The cabin is also the site of the barometer--the instrument upon which so much navigational confidence depends. Conrad notes, with characteristic irony, that the Easterly Weather has "diabolic ingenuity" sufficient to deceive even this trusty device. The barometer screwed to the cabin bulkhead will, after years of faithful service, "almost invariably" be induced to rise just when the Easterly gale is about to deliver its worst. The cabin, for all its dignity as the nerve-centre of the ship, is still subject to the deceptions of the elements.

> After years and years of experience the most trusty instrument of the sort that ever went to sea screwed on to a ship's cabin bulkhead will, almost invariably, be induced to rise by the diabolic ingenuity of the Easterly Weather, just at the moment when the Easterly Weather, discarding its methods of hard, dry, impassive cruelty, contemplates drowning what is left of your spirit in torrents of a peculiarly cold and horrid rain. (029-001)

## The Cabin as Intimate Space

In the *Tremolino* chapters, the tiny cabin becomes a place of conspiracy. Dominic plies a *guardacosta* officer with liqueur while extracting intelligence about the patrol craft's whereabouts. The cabin shrinks from the seat of command to a space of whispered strategy, its intimacy serving the purposes of evasion.

> While he sat sipping his liqueur in the tiny cabin, Dominic plied him with questions as to the whereabouts of the guardacostas. (043-022)

And in the final crisis, the cabin becomes the repository of desperate action. Conrad runs below to retrieve ten thousand francs in gold--his secret reserve, stowed in a locker. The cabin here is the keeper of the last resource, the place where a man's contingency is stored against the worst.

> I ran down to the cabin. I had there, put away in a locker, ten thousand francs in gold of whose presence on board, so far as I was aware, not a soul except Dominic had the slightest inkling. (045-002)
