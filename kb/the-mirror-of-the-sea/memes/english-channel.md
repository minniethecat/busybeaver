## Location and Identity

The English Channel — "the narrow seas around these isles" in Conrad's phrase — is the maritime corridor between England and France, connecting the North Sea to the Atlantic Ocean. For the homeward-bound seaman, entering the Channel was both a relief and a renewed test of vigilance: the traffic thickened, the weather turned "thick," and the proximity of the land on both sides demanded constant attention. Conrad describes it as the kingdom of the West Wind's most treacherous moods.

## Conrad's Description

> The narrow seas around these isles, where British admirals keep watch and ward upon the marches of the Atlantic Ocean, are subject to the turbulent sway of the West Wind. (025-004)

> On the other hand, I have known many captains who, directly their ship had left the narrow waters of the Channel, would disappear from the sight of their ship's company altogether for some three days or more. (002-002)

> The Southwesterly Weather is the thick weather par excellence. It is not the thickness of the fog; it is rather a contraction of the horizon, a mysterious veiling of the shores with clouds that seem to lend menace to the rocky coast. (026-004)

> There are some bad nights in the kingdom of the West Wind for homeward-bound ships making for the Channel; and the days of wrath dawn upon them colourless and vague like the timid turning up of invisible suns. (026-012)

## Maritime Significance

The English Channel occupies a unique position in Conrad's maritime geography. It is simultaneously the most familiar and the most treacherous of waters — the gateway home that has wrecked thousands of ships. The prevalence of "thick weather" (the combination of fog, rain, and low cloud that affects the Channel with particular frequency) made it the ultimate test of a navigator's skill. Conrad's description of the Channel approaches under a southwesterly gale, with visibility reduced to a few hundred yards and the ship rushing toward an invisible shore, is one of the most vivid passages in *The Mirror of the Sea*.

The Channel was also, in Conrad's telling, a psychological threshold. Captains who had commanded their ships across entire oceans would retreat to their cabins the moment the ship cleared the Channel on the outward passage — and emerge again only when the home shore was sighted on the return.
