## Definition

Mist is the thin atmospheric vapour that lies upon the surface of the sea, softening outlines and blurring the boundary between water and sky. Less dense and less dangerous than the thick fog that can bring navigation to a halt, mist nevertheless transforms the seascape — rendering distant ships ghostly, muting sounds, and lending an air of calm and mystery. In Conrad's prose, mist appears most often in moments of stillness and beauty, a delicate veil drawn across the "calm and majestic splendour" of the ocean.

## In Conrad's Words

> A thin, silvery mist softened the calm and majestic splendour of light without shadows — seemed to render the sky less remote and the ocean less immense.
> (036-002)

> She was coming down on us with swelling sails, looking delicately tall and exquisitely noble through the mist.
> (036-013)

> The West Wind hangs heavy grey curtains of mist and spray before your gaze, but the Eastern interloper of the narrow seas, when he has mustered his courage and cruelty to the point of a gale, puts your eyes out.
> (029-001)

## Mist and the Sublime

Conrad distinguishes carefully between the mist of the West Wind and the blindness of the Easterly gale. The West Wind's mist — grey curtains of spray and vapour — veils the world but does not obliterate it; the mariner may be drenched and half-blinded, yet he retains some sense of his surroundings. It is a condition of difficulty rather than extinction. The East Wind, by contrast, "puts your eyes out" entirely — an image of total sensory deprivation. This distinction runs through Conrad's meteorology: mist, however troublesome, belongs to the realm of the visible and the navigable; it is, in its own way, companionable. Even in its most dramatic form — "the vastness of the open sea smothered in driving mists" (045-018) — the mist still permits the phantom-like apparition of a ship under press of canvas, a momentary vision snatched from the grey before it vanishes again into the squall.
