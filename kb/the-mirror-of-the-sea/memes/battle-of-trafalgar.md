## Background

The Battle of Trafalgar, fought on 21 October 1805 off the coast of Spain near Cape Trafalgar, was the decisive naval engagement of the Napoleonic Wars. Lord Nelson's fleet of twenty-seven ships of the line engaged and destroyed a combined French and Spanish fleet of thirty-three, without losing a single British ship. The victory annihilated Napoleonic sea power and secured British naval supremacy for a century — but at the cost of Nelson's life, struck by a musket ball on the quarterdeck of HMS *Victory*.

In *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad treats Trafalgar not as a historical event to be narrated but as the spiritual climax of the heroic age of sail — the moment when Nelson "exalted all heroism." For Conrad, Trafalgar is not one battle among many but the defining event that sealed the character of the British naval tradition, the benchmark against which all subsequent seamanship must be measured.

## Conrad's Account

Conrad's meditation on Trafalgar focuses not on tactics — the famous "Nelson touch" of breaking the enemy line in two columns — but on weather, character, and the moral meaning of the battle. In a striking passage, he reflects on the light, baffling easterly winds that nearly undid the British approach:

> It was one of these covered days of fitful sunshine, of light, unsteady winds, with a swell from the westward, and hazy in general, but with the land about the Cape at times distinctly visible. (048-004)

Conrad brings his own professional knowledge as a seaman to bear on the battle. He had served in those same waters off the Spanish coast, and he knew the treacherous variability of the wind in that corner of the ocean. The thought of those "baffling easterly airs, coming on at any time within half an hour or so, after the firing of the first shot" makes him gasp "with professional awe." For a sailor, the tactical brilliance of Trafalgar is inseparable from the physical reality of sailing a ship of the line in light and variable winds under fire:

> To this day I cannot free myself from the impression that, for some forty minutes, the fate of the great battle hung upon a breath of wind such as I have felt stealing from behind, as it were, upon my cheek while engaged in looking to the westward for the signs of the true weather. (048-004)

## Nelson at Trafalgar

For Conrad, Nelson's greatness at Trafalgar was the culmination of a career in which character and seamanship were inseparable. The famous signal — "England expects that every man will do his duty" — is not quoted by Conrad; what interests him is not Nelson the public hero but Nelson the sea-officer, the man whose "nobleness of mind" was "a prominent and beautiful part of his character":

> "Exalt," he wrote, not "augment." And therein his feeling and his pen captured the very truth. Other men there were ready and able to add to the treasure of victories the British navy has given to the nation. It was the lot of Lord Nelson to exalt all heroism. (046-006)

Nelson's death at Trafalgar, struck down at the moment of his greatest victory, completes the arc that Conrad traces through the final chapters of *The Mirror of the Sea*. The admiral who had been scarred at Calvi, who had lost an arm at Santa Cruz de Tenerife, who had held the telescope to his blind eye at Copenhagen, falls at Trafalgar — and in falling, becomes immortal:

> This heroic spirit of daring and endurance, in which all public and private differences were sunk throughout the whole fleet, is Lord Nelson's great legacy, triply sealed by the victorious impress of the Nile, Copenhagen, and Trafalgar. (047-006)

## The Legacy

Trafalgar for Conrad represents the summit of the age of sail — a world of wooden ships and iron men that the age of steam has rendered remote but not irrelevant. The battle is the ultimate proof that the sea-life produces moral greatness:

> To be so great and to remain so accessible to the affection of one's fellow-men is the mark of exceptional humanity. Lord Nelson's greatness was very human. It had a moral basis; it needed to feel its surroundings. (047-005)

Yet Conrad is not merely nostalgic. He acknowledges that the engines of the modern world would have been incomprehensible to Nelson and his men — "those who from the heat of that battle sank together to their repose in the cool depths of the ocean would not understand the watchwords of our day, would gaze with amazed eyes at the engines of our ships" (049-004). Trafalgar marks both a pinnacle and a passing — the end of one age and the beginning of another, the moment when the art of naval warfare under sail reached its highest expression and, simultaneously, its historical terminus.
