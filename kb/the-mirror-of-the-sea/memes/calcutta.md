## A Port of the Eastern Trade

Calcutta appears in Conrad's maritime world as a destination — one of those distant ports whose names were woven into the everyday talk of seamen. It was a regular port of call for the British merchant marine, a source of jute, tea, and other colonial commodities. Conrad's references to Calcutta are characteristically matter-of-fact: it is simply one of the places ships went, one of the points on the great web of imperial shipping routes that structured a seaman's life.

> It was in the dock in Dundee, where we had brought a full cargo of jute from Calcutta. (003-001)

## Captain B——'s Last Words

Conrad's most personal Calcutta memory is bound up with his memories of Captain B**, the "short, stout, dignified, perhaps a little pompous" commander under whom he served. The ship had brought "a full cargo of jute from Calcutta" to Dundee, and on the day of paying off, Captain B—— inquired after Conrad's future plans with "such an evident interest in my case that I was quite surprised." The Calcutta voyage was the occasion for a moment of professional kindness that Conrad remembered with "peculiar satisfaction."

## Ship Talk on Shore

Calcutta enters Conrad's pages a second time through the medium of shore talk — the informal, anecdotal discourse by which a ship's reputation was built. On the *Tweed*, the chief mate B—— recalled seeing a ship in Calcutta whose idiosyncrasies were the subject of dockside gossip: "on her passage up the river she had carried away both her hawsepipes." Such talk, Conrad reflects, is how a ship's "name" is "slowly established, her fame made for her, the tale of her qualities and of her defects kept."

> Some years ago he had seen her in Calcutta, and he remembered being told by somebody then, that on her passage up the river she had carried away both her hawsepipes. (035-010)

## The Hooghly Passage

The passage up the Hooghly River to Calcutta was notoriously difficult — a winding, silting channel that demanded skilled pilotage. Conrad's reference to a ship that "carried away both her hawsepipes" on the river passage hints at these navigational challenges. The Hooghly, like the Thames approaches that Conrad knew so well, was a river that tested seamanship — a place where a ship's reputation could be made or marred by a single awkward incident.
