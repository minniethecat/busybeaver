## Definition

The chain--or, in proper nautical parlance, the cable--is the heavy iron linkage connecting a ship's anchor to the vessel. It hangs in a short bight from the cat-head before letting go; it "grows" out from the hawsepipe under strain, taut as a bowstring; and when the anchor takes hold, its thunderous rumbling through the hawsepipes is the closing word of a ship's journey. Conrad treats the chain not merely as equipment but as the instrument through which the ship's most consequential moment--the arrival at anchorage--is announced.

## The Language of the Chain

Conrad is particular about the vocabulary of anchoring. He insists that the word "cast" is a journalist's error, an offence against seamanship. The anchor is not thrown overboard; it is already hanging at the cat-head, ready to be released. The chain does not merely drop--it "grows" out from the hawsepipe, aslant under the strain, announcing its passage with the thunder of iron against iron. This precision of language reveals a larger truth: for Conrad, seamanship begins with naming things correctly. The landsman who says "cast anchor" and the journalist who writes of anchors being "thrown over" are guilty of the same failure to perceive what actually happens aboard a ship.

> This insistence in using the odious word arises from the fact that a particularly benighted landsman must imagine the act of anchoring as a process of throwing something overboard, whereas the anchor ready for its work is already overboard, and is not thrown over, but simply allowed to fall. (004-005)

## The Chain as Instrument of Captivity

In port, the chain takes on a different character. It becomes a fetter, one of many bonds that tie the free-running ship to the land. The berthing-master and his underlings prowl the quays, demanding ever more chains, more ropes, more shackles--as if the ship, having dared the open sea, must now be humbled. Conrad's language here is startling: the ship becomes a "captive," the berthing-master a "gaoler." The chain that was the instrument of safe arrival has become the instrument of imprisonment.

> Chain cables and stout ropes keep her bound to stone posts at the edge of a paved shore, and a berthing-master, with brass buttons on his coat, walks about like a weather-beaten and ruddy gaoler, casting jealous, watchful glances upon the moorings that fetter a ship lying passive and still and safe, as if lost in deep regrets of her days of liberty and danger on the sea. (033-001)

> They want more fenders, more breasting-ropes; they want more springs, more shackles, more fetters; they want to make ships with volatile souls as motionless as square blocks of stone. (033-002)

## In the Open Sea

Once clear of the land, the cables disappear from the deck. The anchors are got in, the chain stowed below. This disappearance marks the ship's true condition--she is now fully committed to the sea, with nothing solid between her and the South Pole. Conrad notes the psychological shift: a sailor in the Channel keeps his anchors always ready, because the land is always near; once the narrow seas are left behind, the ship sheds her connection to the shore.

> A vessel in the Channel has her anchors always ready, her cables shackled on, and the land almost always in sight. The anchor and the land are indissolubly connected in a sailor's thoughts. But directly she is clear of the narrow seas, heading out into the world with nothing solid to speak of between her and the South Pole, the anchors are got in and the cables disappear from the deck. (005-001)

## The Closing Cadence

The chain's most symbolic moment is the end of a passage. The splash of the anchor and the rumbling of the chain are, for Conrad, the full stop at the end of a sentence--the sentence being the voyage itself. The ship herself seems conscious of this finality, answering the chain's thunder with a "slight deep shudder of all her frame." No other piece of ship's equipment is given this degree of sentience.

> In a life whose worth is told out in passages from port to port, the splash of the anchor's fall and the thunderous rumbling of the chain are like the closing of a distant period, of which she seems conscious with a slight deep shudder of all her frame. (006-004)

> Therefore the sailor will never say, "cast anchor," and the shipmaster aft will hail his chief mate on the forecastle in impressionistic phrase: "How does the cable grow?" Because "grow" is the right word for the long drift of a cable emerging aslant under the strain, taut as a bowstring above the water. (006-001)

## Perils of the Chain

The chain is also an object of risk. A parted cable means a lost anchor--and an anchor, once its cable parts, is irrecoverable. Conrad records a cautionary incident: a ship outward bound from the Downs breaks her sheer, strikes adrift, and loses an anchor and chain. The loss is told as a piece of gossip among apprentices, but beneath the casual telling is the weight of a near-disaster.

> Just now, yarning with the apprentices on board, he had heard that this very voyage, brought up in the Downs, outward bound, she broke her sheer, struck adrift, and lost an anchor and chain. (035-010)

> An anchor is forged and fashioned for faithfulness; give it ground that it can bite, and it will hold till the cable parts, and then, whatever may afterwards befall its ship, that anchor is "lost." (004-004)
