The Essex coast forms the northern shore of the Thames Estuary, stretching from the Nore up past Southend and Tilbury to the narrowing reaches of the river. In Conrad's depiction it is a landscape of flatness, marsh, and industrial imposition — the place where the sea's freedom is first surrendered to the commerce of the port.

## Conrad's Description

Conrad observes the Essex shore from the deck of an incoming ship, contrasting it with the Kentish side. The Essex bank is the side of the great red-brick building — the "monstrous ugliness" of a modern institution — and of the level marshes that stretch away beyond the river's edge.

> But on the other side, on the flat Essex side, a shapeless and desolate red edifice, a vast pile of bricks with many windows and a slate roof more inaccessible than an Alpine slope, towers over the bend in monstrous ugliness, the tallest, heaviest building for miles around.
>
> (031-003)

The ship passes close by this shore as it makes its way up from the sea:

> They follow each other, going very close by the Essex shore. Such as the beads of a rosary told by businesslike shipowners for the greater profit of the world they slip one by one into the open.
>
> (031-002)

The passage from sea to river passes between the two shores — Kent to the south, Essex to the north — and it is between them that the ship leaves the sea behind:

> Between the crowded houses of Gravesend and the monstrous redbrick pile on the Essex shore the ship is surrendered fairly to the grasp of the river. That hint of loneliness, that soul of the sea which had accompanied her as far as the Lower Hope Reach, abandons her at the turn of the first bend above.
>
> (031-004)

The Essex marsh, in its remoteness and isolation, carries a certain romance:

> These are very modern, but their remoteness and isolation upon the Essex marsh, the days of failure attending their creation, invested them with a romantic air.
>
> (032-005)

## Maritime Significance

The Essex shore was the last stretch of open land a deep-water ship passed before entering the fully industrialised river. It was the threshold between the estuary's maritime world and the port's commercial domain. For the seaman, the flat Essex bank — with its marshes, its mudflats, and its solitary red-brick monument — marked the point at which the sea surrendered its charge to the river.
