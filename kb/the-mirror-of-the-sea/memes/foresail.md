The **foresail** (or fore-course) is the lowest square sail on the foremast of a square-rigged vessel. Together with the mainsail it provided the primary driving power, and its management was central to the handling of a sailing ship in all weathers. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, the foresail appears at critical moments: as a racing sail in gales, as a storm-canvas reduced to a reefed shred, and as a sail literally torn from its bolt-ropes by the violence of the West Wind.

## Driving Power

Under reduced canvas in heavy weather, the foresail was often one of the last sails kept set. Conrad recalls a gale off the Australian coast where the foresail's low, centred pull kept the ship driving through the sea:

> Under two lower topsails and a reefed foresail the barque seemed to race with a long, steady sea that did not becalm her in the troughs.
>
> (023-005)

The foresail's value in heavy weather was its position — low on the foremast, where its centre of effort helped keep the ship's head steady and prevented her from being becalmed in the wave troughs, a dangerous condition that left a vessel at the mercy of the next comber.

## Destroyed by the Wind

The foresail's vulnerability is starkly rendered in Conrad's account of a Channel gale. He describes watching a stout new foresail — an "extra-stout" piece of canvas — simply vanish:

> Indeed, it is less than nothing, and I have seen, when the great soul of the world turned over with a heavy sigh, a perfectly new, extra-stout foresail vanish like a bit of some airy stuff much lighter than gossamer.
>
> (011-001)

The image is deliberately shocking: industrial-strength canvas, new and stout, reduced to nothing — "less than nothing" — by a single exhalation of the world. The West Wind's power is registered not in what it does to men but in what it does to their strongest contrivances.

## The Skipper's Reluctance

In one of Conrad's most psychologically acute passages, a homeward-bound captain in a Channel gale wrestles with the decision to take in the foresail. He knows he should heave-to — but with a fair wind blowing and home so close, he cannot bring himself to give the order:

> "Very well," he shouted, with an affectation of impatience, as if giving way to long entreaties. "All right. If we don't get a shift by then we'll take that foresail off her and put her head under her wing for the night."
>
> (027-012)

Conrad found the captain's phrase — "put her head under her wing for the night" — one of the most expressive he had ever heard, "in imaginative precision, in true feeling" (027-013). But he had grave doubts about the plan, and his doubts were justified:

> But as to taking the foresail off that ship before we put her head under her wing, I had my grave doubts. They were justified. That long enduring piece of canvas was confiscated by the arbitrary decree of the West Wind, to whom belong the lives of men and the contrivances of their hands within the limits of his kingdom. With the sound of a faint explosion it vanished into the thick weather bodily, leaving behind of its stout substance not so much as one solitary strip big enough to be picked into a handful of lint for, say, a wounded elephant. Torn out of its bolt-ropes, it faded like a whiff of smoke in the smoky drift of clouds shattered and torn by the shift of wind.
>
> (027-013)

The West Wind's "arbitrary decree" confiscates the sail before the captain can act. The wind is not merely a physical force but a legal authority — a king whose decrees cannot be appealed. And the sail, which had endured for countless voyages, vanishes without a trace, leaving not even a strip large enough for lint.

## Symbol of Liberty and Peril

In the dramatic *Tremolino* episode, the foresail becomes something more — a potential instrument of escape. A whispered suggestion proposes cutting the fore halyards:

> A knife drawn across the fore halyards would bring the foresail down, and put an end to our liberty in twenty minutes.
>
> (044-047)

The foresail here is both the means of flight and, should it be disabled, the end of freedom. The sail that drives the vessel forward also stands between the fugitives and capture. It is an emblem of the double-edged nature of all sail: what gives liberty can, in a moment, take it away.
