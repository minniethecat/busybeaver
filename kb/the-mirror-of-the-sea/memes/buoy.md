A **buoy** is a floating device anchored to the seabed, serving various maritime purposes: marking navigable channels, warning of hazards, providing mooring points for vessels, or serving as life-saving equipment. Buoys are the quiet sentinels of coastal waters — unglamorous but essential to safe navigation.

Conrad contrasts the modern, well-marked seaways of his own time with the ancient mariner's unguided passage through the same waters. In the Roman era, there were no buoys or beacons to mark the hidden sandbanks of the Thames estuary.

> I assume he followed the land and passed through what is at present known as Margate Roads, groping his careful way along the hidden sandbanks, whose every tail and spit has its beacon or buoy nowadays. (030-002)

The transition from open sea to river is marked by the buoyed channels that guide ships through the confined waters of the estuary — a sign that the freedom of the open sea is being exchanged for the controlled, regulated environment of the port.

> The broad inlet of the shallow North Sea passes gradually into the contracted shape of the river; but for a long time the feeling of the open water remains with the ship steering to the westward through one of the lighted and buoyed passageways of the Thames. (030-003)

## Buoys and the Limits of Sea-Freedom

Conrad locates an invisible boundary at Gravesend, where the great mooring buoys mark the surrender of the sea to human contrivance. The salt tide itself is still present, but its character changes at this buoy-marked frontier:

> The waters of the sea rush on past Gravesend, tumbling the big mooring buoys laid along the face of the town; but the sea-freedom stops short there, surrendering the salt tide to the needs, the artifices, the contrivances of toiling men. (031-004)

This boundary is reinforced by the Nore [[lightship]] — a floating beacon at the estuary's "ideal point," marking a centre of maritime memory rather than a physical hazard. Conrad was struck by the modesty of this principal navigational marker:

> I was startled, as if of necessity the principal beacon in the waterway of the greatest town on earth should have presented imposing proportions. (031-001)

In the docks, ships lie at rest on their mooring buoys — a rare moment of stillness in a life otherwise defined by motion and exposure.

> Picturesque and clean as docks go, these twin basins spread side by side the dark lustre of their glassy water sparsely peopled by a few ships laid up on buoys or tucked far away from each other. (033-007)

When a ship is lost without trace, Conrad lists the floating objects that never appear — including the lifebuoy — as tokens of an unmarked grave at sea.

> Nothing of her ever comes to light — no grating, no lifebuoy, no piece of boat or branded oar — to give a hint of the place and date of her sudden end. (017-001)
