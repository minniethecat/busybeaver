## Definition

In Conrad's maritime philosophy, a [[gale]] is not merely a meteorological event but a living adversary — a personality to be studied, respected, and ultimately known through intimate encounter. He distinguishes between mere strong winds and true gales, the latter possessing distinctive characters that impress themselves indelibly upon the memory of the seaman. A gale "of wind, the thing of mighty sound, is inarticulate" — it is the human interpreter who, "in a chance phrase, interprets the elemental passion of his enemy."

## In Conrad's Words

> Gales have their personalities, and, after all, perhaps it is not strange; for, when all is said and done, they are adversaries whose wiles you must defeat, whose violence you must resist, and yet with whom you must live in the intimacies of nights and days. (022-005)

> For, after all, a gale of wind, the thing of mighty sound, is inarticulate. It is a man who, in a chance phrase, interprets the elemental passion of his enemy. (024-001)

> If you would know the age of the earth, look upon the sea in a storm. The greyness of the whole immense surface, the wind furrows upon the faces of the waves, the great masses of foam, tossed about and waving, like matted white locks, give to the sea in a gale an appearance of hoary age, lustreless, dull, without gleams, as though it had been created before light itself. (022-003)

> Looking back after much love and much trouble, the instinct of primitive man, who seeks to personify the forces of Nature for his affection and for his fear, is awakened again in the breast of one civilized beyond that stage even in his infancy. (022-004)

> Another, strangely, recalls a silent man. And yet it was not din that was wanting; in fact, it was terrific. That one was a gale that came upon the ship swiftly, like a pampero, which last is a very sudden wind indeed. (023-008)

> It was off that other cape which is always deprived of its title as the Cape of Good Hope is robbed of its name. It was off the Horn. For a true expression of dishevelled wildness there is nothing like a gale in the bright moonlight of a high latitude. (024-002)

> The note of dread in the shouting voice, the practical truth of these words, heard years ago from a man I did not like, have stamped its peculiar character on that gale. (024-007)

> There is infinite variety in the gales of wind at sea, and except for the peculiar, terrible, and mysterious moaning that may be heard sometimes passing through the roar of a hurricane — except for that unforgettable sound, as if the soul of the universe had been goaded into a mournful groan — it is impossible to describe a gale. (024-009)

## The Personalities of Gales

Conrad devotes chapters 22–24 of *The Mirror of the Sea* to the personalities of gales. He describes them as memorable figures, each with distinctive traits: one recalls "a silent man," despite the terrific din; another came "swiftly, like a pampero"; yet another, off [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] under bright moonlight, expressed "dishevelled wildness." These are not interchangeable storms but individual adversaries whose character is stamped by a single phrase, a particular look in a shipmate's eyes, or the quality of the light under which they were endured.

## The Seaman's Relationship with the Gale

The gale is the great test of [[seamanship]]. Conrad describes the experience of riding out a gale as one of intimate cohabitation with an enemy — "you must live in the intimacies of nights and days" with it. The ship becomes a fragile refuge, and every man aboard is reduced to his essential qualities. Conrad notes how, during gales, "the seaman's thoughts were intensely personal, concentrated upon his own task of survival." The gale strips away pretence, and the [[fine-art-of-sailing]] becomes, in that extremity, a matter of life and death.

## The Gale and the Overdue Ship

Beyond the immediate terror of the gale lies its most sinister consequence: the making of "overdue" ships. Conrad connects the gale directly to the mystery of vessels that simply vanish — the [[ships-wake|ships that never arrive]]. "It was the wintry, murky time of cold gales and heavy seas. With the snapping of her tail-shaft her life seemed suddenly to depart from her big body." A steamer disabled in a gale, drifting into an unpopulated ocean, becomes the "overdue" — the most haunting word in the maritime vocabulary. For the homeward-bound ship approaching [[london|London]] or the [[thames-estuary|Thames]], a gale in the [[north-sea|North Sea]] was the final, cruelest test before the safety of port.

> It was the wintry, murky time of cold gales and heavy seas. With the snapping of her tail-shaft her life seemed suddenly to depart from her big body, and from a stubborn, arrogant existence she passed all at once into the passive state of a drifting log. (018-009)

## The Gale at Night

Conrad writes with particular power about gales experienced in darkness. At night, the [[wind]]'s force is gauged not by sight but by sound — "the wind howling, royals set, and the ship rushing on in the dark, an immense white sheet of foam level with the lee rail." The night gale compounds fear with blindness, reducing the seaman to a creature of hearing and touch. It is in these conditions that the personalities of gales are most keenly felt — and most lastingly remembered.

> Night clouds racing overhead, wind howling, royals set, and the ship rushing on in the dark, an immense white sheet of foam level with the lee rail. (011-009)
