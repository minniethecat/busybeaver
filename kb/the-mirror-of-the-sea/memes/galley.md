The **galley** is an oar-propelled vessel, typically of Mediterranean origin, whose lineage stretches from antiquity through to the early modern era. In Conrad's reflections, the galley appears as both a historical spectre — the Roman commander who first turned his beaked prow into the Thames — and as a measure of how little naval warfare has truly changed.

## The First Explorer of the Thames

Conrad imagines the Roman galley commander as the original explorer of the [[Thames Estuary]], his attention wholly absorbed by the unfamiliar shoreline:

> The commander of the first Roman galley must have looked with an intense absorption upon the estuary of the Thames as he turned the beaked prow of his ship to the westward under the brow of the North Foreland. (030-002)

In a calm summer sea, the galley's single row of sweeps — "a light one, not a [[trireme]]" — fell in easy cadence upon water "like plate-glass," the classic form of the vessel reflected beside the lonely shores that would one day become the Port of London:

> The navigation of his craft must have engrossed all the Roman's attention in the calm of a summer's day (he would choose his weather), when the single row of long sweeps (the galley would be a light one, not a trireme) could fall in easy cadence upon a sheet of water like plate-glass, reflecting faithfully the classic form of his vessel and the contour of the lonely shores close on his left hand. (030-002)

This passage is among the most lyrical in *The Mirror of the Sea*. Conrad uses the galley to collapse two millennia of maritime history: the Roman commander, navigating by oar along shores that were then wilderness, is the direct professional ancestor of the modern seaman. The "classic form" reflected in the flat calm is both a specific vessel and the enduring shape of seafaring itself.

## The Cost of Empire

Conrad draws a sardonic comparison between the fleets of antiquity and the modern [[battleship]]. At Actium, where the dominion of the known world was contested, the combined fleets of Octavian, Antony, and Cleopatra — with her galley of purple sails — probably cost less than two modern capital ships:

> A frugal mind cannot defend itself from considerable bitterness when reflecting that at the Battle of Actium (which was fought for no less a stake than the dominion of the world) the fleet of Octavianus Caesar and the fleet of Antonius, including the Egyptian division and Cleopatra's galley with purple sails, probably cost less than two modern battleships, or, as the modern naval book-jargon has it, two capital units. (037-003)

The contrast is not merely financial. Conrad's bitterness stems from the disproportion between the stakes and the tools: empires were won and lost with fleets whose entire cost would barely register in a modern admiralty budget. The galley, frail and human-powered, carried the fate of civilisations on its narrow deck.

## The Ship's Galley

Conrad also invokes the galley in its humbler, shipboard sense — the ship's kitchen, a cramped space in the deckhouse from which the [[cook]] emerges at moments of crisis:

> The watch below ran up the forecastle head in a body, the cook dashed out of the galley. (036-003)

This fleeting image, from the chapter on the *Tremolino*, captures the rhythm of shipboard life in miniature: the galley is the domain of the cook, a world apart from the drama of sails and wind, yet swept up in it when danger calls. The double meaning of "galley" — the warship of antiquity and the kitchen of a modern vessel — traces a line from the oar-benches of the Mediterranean to the [[deck]] of a nineteenth-century merchantman.

## Lineage of the Oar

The galley's lineage runs through maritime history like a thread. Conrad observes that "the seaman of the last generation, brought into sympathy with the caravels of ancient time by his sailing-ship, their lineal descendant, cannot look upon those lumbering forms navigating the naive seas of ancient woodcuts without a feeling of surprise, of affectionate derision, envy, and admiration" (022-007). The galley, the [[caravel]], the sailing-ship, and the steamer are all links in a continuous chain of maritime inheritance.

## Related Vessels

The galley shares its oar-propelled heritage with the [[trireme]] of classical warfare and finds distant Mediterranean descendants in the [[tartane]] and [[balancelle]] of Conrad's own time. Each represents a stage in the evolution from oar to sail, from the beaked prow of the Roman galley to the raking masts of the Mediterranean fishing fleet.
