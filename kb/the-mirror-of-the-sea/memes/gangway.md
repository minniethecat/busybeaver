## Definition

The gangway is the narrow plank or bridge connecting a moored vessel to the shore or quay. In Conrad's world, the gangway is the threshold between two realms: aboard the ship, order and authority reign; ashore, the land's mud, commerce, and liberty beckon. It is crossed by stevedores and dock-labourers bringing the chaos of commerce aboard, by captains descending with gold links and bluff distinction, and by watchmen who hang lanterns at its break. A simple plank, yet it bears the weight of all those who pass between sea and land.

## The Gangway as Social Threshold

Every crossing of the gangway enacts a social ritual. When the captain crosses ashore, his gold links and portly presence mark him as a prince amongst sailors; the humble beholders--stevedores, tally clerks, tide-waiters--look on in awe. The gangway is the stage upon which the hierarchy of the port is made visible. But the same plank that carries the captain's dignity also bears the muddy feet of dock-labourers who trample "unconcerned, brutal and hobnailed upon her helpless body." The gangway thus undoes as much as it enacts: it is the point at which the ship's authority is both confirmed and violated.

> His weather-beaten and handsome face, his portly presence, his shirtfronts and broad cuffs and gold links, his air of bluff distinction, impressed the humble beholders (stevedores, tally clerks, tide-waiters) as he walked ashore over the gangway of his ship lying at the Circular Quay in Sydney. (009-002)

> Gangs of dock-labourers swarm with muddy feet over the gangways. It is a moving sight this, of so many men of the earth, earthy, who never cared anything for a ship, trampling unconcerned, brutal and hobnailed upon her helpless body. (033-002)

## The Rhythm of Loading

The gangway-tender, a figure of precise authority, stands at the rail directing the great slings of cargo as they swing over and drop into the hatchways. From six in the morning till six at night, the gangway is the site of incessant labour--the "hard labour of the prison-house," as Conrad calls it, that rewards ships for winning the harbour. The gangway-tender's hand-sign is the metronome of this rhythm, each sign bringing another sling of general cargo plummeting into the hold.

> From six in the morning till six at night the hard labour of the prison-house, which rewards the valiance of ships that win the harbour, went on steadily, great slings of general cargo swinging over the rail, to drop plumb into the hatchways at the sign of the gangway-tender's hand. (033-009)

## Night at the Gangway

The gangway transforms at night. The flags are hauled down, the crowds of Sunday visitors disperse, and the night-watchman fastens a lighted lantern at the break. The gangway becomes a solitary post, crossed only by the watchman making his rounds and by officers returning late from shore. It is at night that the gangway reveals its other face: not the bustling threshold of commerce, but the quiet boundary between the ship's disciplined solitude and the city's anonymous darkness.

> On Sundays and holidays the citizens trooped down, on visiting bent, and the lonely officer on duty solaced himself by playing the cicerone--especially to the citizenesses with engaging manners and a well-developed sense of the fun that may be got out of the inspection of a ship's cabins and staterooms. The tinkle of more or less untuned cottage pianos floated out of open stern-ports till the gas-lamps began to twinkle in the streets, and the ship's night-watchman, coming sleepily on duty after his unsatisfactory day slumbers, hauled down the flags and fastened a lighted lantern at the break of the gangway. (034-001)

## Strange Arrivals

The gangway is also the site of unexpected encounters. In one of the memoir's most memorable vignettes, a creature "resembling an ostrich" dashes up the gangway on a dark winter night in Sydney--a man whose coat, ripped up the back and flapping like wings, creates an apparition so strange that Conrad, standing watch under the break of the poop, cannot identify him at first. He turns out to be a desperate stowaway seeking shelter, and the encounter becomes a small drama of authority, compassion, and the peculiar solitude of the officer on night watch.

> One wintry, blustering, dark night in July, as I stood sleepily out of the rain under the break of the poop something resembling an ostrich dashed up the gangway. I say ostrich because the creature, though it ran on two legs, appeared to help its progress by working a pair of short wings; it was a man, however, only his coat, ripped up the back and flapping in two halves above his shoulders, gave him that weird and fowl-like appearance. (034-003)

## The Gangway of the *Tremolino*

In the closing chapters of the memoir, the gangway takes on a more intimate, even elegiac quality. Dominic, the *Tremolino*'s steadfast mate, raises a lantern silently to light Conrad's steps along "the narrow, springy plank of our primitive gangway." The gangway here is not the grand structure of a wool-clipper at Circular Quay but a humble plank--and it is this very primitiveness that makes it the stage for the deepest loyalty. Dominic's silent gesture is the gangway's most human moment: a threshold crossed not by commerce or command, but by quiet companionship.

> He would raise a lantern silently to light my steps along the narrow, springy plank of our primitive gangway. "And so we are going off," he would murmur directly my foot touched the deck. (042-005)

## The Ritual of Parting

The gangway is also the place of prolonged farewells. Conrad's mate Mr. B——, a man of "disinterested affection" for his crony, the mate of the *Cicero*, would spend half an hour or more at the shore end of the gangway in "senseless and muddled discourse," each man insisting on seeing the other back to his own ship. They would perambulate the harbour three or four times before finally parting--at which point the planks of the gangway would "bend and creak" under Mr. B——'s weight, announcing his return to the ship and the end of the night's ritual.

> They would continue their senseless and muddled discourse in tones of profound friendship for half an hour or so at the shore end of our gangway, and then I would hear Mr. B—— insisting that he must see the other on board his ship... Then, through sheer weariness, or perhaps in a moment of forgetfulness, they would manage to part from each other somehow, and by-and-by the planks of our long gangway would bend and creak under the weight of Mr. B—— coming on board for good at last. (034-010)
