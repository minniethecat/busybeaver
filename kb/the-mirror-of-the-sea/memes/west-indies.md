## Location and Identity

The West Indies appear in *The Mirror of the Sea* as part of the geography of British maritime trade — one of the distant destinations that defined the routes of the merchant sailing fleet. Conrad does not describe the islands directly; they appear as points of reference in the commercial network that connected London, the Atlantic, and the tropical colonies. Their presence in his text is a reminder that every port he describes was part of a global system of trade routes.

## Conrad's Description

> These towns of the Antipodes, not so great then as they are now, took an interest in the shipping, the running links with "home," whose numbers confirmed the sense of their growing importance. (034-001)

> The narrow seas around these isles, where British admirals keep watch and ward upon the marches of the Atlantic Ocean, are subject to the turbulent sway of the West Wind. (025-004)

## Maritime Significance

The West Indies route was one of the classic passages of the British merchant marine — the sugar trade that had, in earlier centuries, been the foundation of maritime fortunes. By Conrad's era, the West Indian trade was one among many routes, but it retained its place in the mental geography of the British seaman. The mention of the West Indies in Conrad's text, however brief, situates his narrative within the broader context of the global trading network that the sailing ship served.
