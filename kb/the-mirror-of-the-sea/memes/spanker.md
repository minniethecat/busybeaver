A **spanker** is the fore-and-aft sail set from the mizzenmast of a square-rigged vessel, bent to a gaff above and a boom below. It is one of the few fore-and-aft sails on an otherwise square-rigged ship, and it serves both to drive the vessel and to aid in steering — helping the ship to balance her helm. The spanker-boom, extending far out over the stern, is a prominent spar that is particularly exposed in crowded harbours and fleet anchorages.

In Conrad's accounts, the spanker appears less as a named sail and more as a vulnerable extremity — the part of the ship that first makes contact when things go wrong in close quarters.

## In Conrad's Words

> Ultimately they brought us up with the jibboom of a Dutch brig poking through our spanker — nothing worse.
>
> (009-007)

This laconic line, delivered after a breathless account of a near-collision in a crowded anchorage, is vintage Conrad. The spanker is pierced; the damage is trivial. The tone is relief — it could have been far worse. The spanker, as the aftermost spar, represents the margin of error in close-quarters manoeuvring: the first thing to be struck, and the least catastrophic.

> Ultimately some of the minor spars did go — nothing important: spanker-booms and suchlike — because at times the frightful impetus of her rolling would part a fourfold tackle of new three-inch Manilla line as if it were weaker than packthread.
>
> (015-010)

Here the spanker-boom is classed among the "minor spars" — the expendable extremities of a ship's rig. In heavy weather, when a vessel rolls with "frightful impetus," the forces on spars can be immense. Conrad's dismissive phrase — "nothing important" — is spoken from the perspective of a seaman who understands that the loss of a spanker-boom is a nuisance, not a tragedy. The ship can sail on without it. The real danger lies elsewhere: in the parting of tackle, the shifting of cargo, the rising of the centre of gravity.

In both passages, the spanker and its boom are boundary objects — the outermost reach of the ship's rig, the first point of contact with another vessel or with the overwhelming forces of the sea. They are, in Conrad's understated judgement, the acceptable casualties of a seaman's life.
