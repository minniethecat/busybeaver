## "The Venerable and Sympathetic London Docks"

Of all the Thames docks, the London Docks held a special place in Conrad's affections. They were venerable, old-world, evocative — a place where the aroma of spices still hung in the air between the warehouses. Unlike the rail-served docks further downriver, the London Docks had "not a single line of rails in the whole of their area." This absence of railway infrastructure, which might seem a deficiency to the modern engineer, was for Conrad the mark of an earlier, more human-scaled maritime world.

> Beginning with the cosy little St. Katherine's Dock, lying overshadowed and black like a quiet pool amongst rocky crags, through the venerable and sympathetic London Docks, with not a single line of rails in the whole of their area and the aroma of spices lingering between its warehouses, with their far-famed wine-cellars… (032-003)

## Each Dock Its Own Physiognomy

Conrad's survey of the Thames docks is a study in architectural personality. Each dock, he insists, has "its own individual attractiveness," its own "peculiar physiognomy, its own expression." The St. Katherine's Dock is "cosy," the London Docks are "venerable," the West India Docks "interesting," the Victoria and Albert Docks "fine," and the great basins at Tilbury are marked by "vast gloom." What unites them all, paradoxically, is "their common trait of being romantic in their usefulness."

> The cosiness of the St. Katherine's Dock, the old-world air of the London Docks, remain impressed upon the memory. (032-004)

## Romance in Usefulness

Conrad's defence of the Thames docks is aimed at those who find them merely ugly. He concedes that "beauty" is "a vain word" when applied to these industrial landscapes, but insists that "romance has lived too long upon this river not to have thrown a mantle of glamour upon its banks." The docks are romantic not despite their industrial function but because of it — they are the physical embodiment of London's centuries-old relationship with the sea.

> When one talks of the Thames docks, "beauty" is a vain word, but romance has lived too long upon this river not to have thrown a mantle of glamour upon its banks. (032-004)

## Magnificent Undertakings

The London Docks, along with the St. Katherine's Docks, were built in the early nineteenth century when the Pool of London — the reach of the river just below London Bridge — could no longer accommodate the growing volume of trade. Conrad calls them "magnificent undertakings answering to the need of their time." His praise is not merely aesthetic but historical: these docks were the infrastructure of empire, built at the moment when London was becoming the world's greatest port.

> For a long time the stream itself could accommodate quite easily the oversea and coasting traffic… and when the trade had grown too big for the river there came the St. Katherine's Docks and the London Docks, magnificent undertakings answering to the need of their time. (032-006)

## A Great Capital's Port

Conrad is careful to distinguish London from other British ports. Unlike Liverpool, Cardiff, Newcastle, or Glasgow, London "has not the backing of great industrial districts or great fields of natural exploitation." The Thames is "an historical river… a romantic stream flowing through the centre of great affairs." The London Docks, in this reading, are not merely commercial infrastructure but monuments to the city's unique relationship with the sea — a relationship founded not on coal or iron but on the sheer gravitational pull of the imperial capital.

> It must not be forgotten that London has not the backing of great industrial districts or great fields of natural exploitation. In this it differs from Liverpool, from Cardiff, from Newcastle, from Glasgow; and therein the Thames differs from the Mersey, from the Tyne, from the Clyde. (032-006)
