## Definition

The dock is the enclosed basin where a ship rests when not at sea — a place of captivity and yet of peculiar beauty. For Conrad, the docks of London were not merely utilitarian spaces but scenes imbued with historical association and individual character, each possessing its own atmosphere. A ship in dock is "fettered to rigid shores," yet the dock is also where the ship receives her cargo, where her crew assembles, and where the life of a passage begins and ends.

## A Prisoner Meditating Upon Freedom

Conrad's most striking characterisation of a docked ship is as a captive spirit:

> A ship in dock, surrounded by quays and the walls of warehouses, has the appearance of a prisoner meditating upon freedom in the sadness of a free spirit put under restraint. (033-001)

The dock's custodians — the "swarm of renegades" as Conrad wryly calls them — treat the ship with an immense distrust, as though suspecting her of plotting escape:

> The swarm of renegades — dockmasters, berthing-masters, gatemen, and suchlike — appear to nurse an immense distrust of the captive ship's resignation. (033-002)

The gentlest breeze reminds the ship of the open sea, and Conrad captures this restlessness vividly:

> The least puff of wind stealing round the corners of the dock buildings stirs these captives fettered to rigid shores. It stirs them; the ships, I mean. You can see them, as you pass alongside, straining at their moorings with a certain discretion, a dim and furtive restlessness, which is like the beginning of a long suppressed desire to be away. (032-001)

## In Conrad's Words

> The dock is the scene of what the world would think the most serious part in the light, bounding, swaying life of a ship. There are trails of smoke on each side of the entrance — the steamers are getting their steam up — and from the depths of this floating city comes the heavy swishing sound of the screws.
> (032-002)

> For all my unkind comparisons to swans and backyards, it cannot be denied that each dock or group of docks along the north side of the river has its own individual attractiveness. Beginning with the cosy little St. Katherine's Dock, lying overshadowed and black like a quiet pool amongst rocky crags, through the venerable and sympathetic London Docks, with not a single line of railings or a single policeman within their gloomy and forbidding enclosure, to the brand-new, irreproachable, and passionless Tilbury Dock, the last and the most modern of all.
> (032-003)

## The Thames Docks: Romance Amidst Ugliness

Conrad acknowledges that "beauty" is a vain word for the Thames docks, yet insists on their romance:

> When one talks of the Thames docks, "beauty" is a vain word, but romance has lived too long upon this river not to have thrown a mantle of glamour upon its banks. (032-004)

Each dock had its own character. The London Docks retained an old-world air; the St. Katherine's Dock was cosy and intimate. Tilbury, the newest, was efficient but passionless — and yet even Tilbury shared in the historical glamour of the river:

> Even the newest of the docks, the Tilbury Dock, shares in the glamour conferred by historical associations. (032-005)

> From the first the Tilbury Docks were very efficient and ready for their task, but they had come, perhaps, too soon into the field. A great future lies before Tilbury Docks. (032-005)

In earlier days, the Pool of London — just below London Bridge — presented a quite different spectacle of ships packed stem to stern:

> That was in the days when, in the part called the Pool, just below London Bridge, the vessels moored stem and stern in the very strength of the tide formed one solid mass like an island covered with a forest of gaunt, leafless trees. (032-006)

> It was in the dock in Dundee, where we had brought a full cargo of jute from Calcutta. We had been interviewed by several reporters while the ship was coming up the river, and on the day of our arrival the newspapers had printed a column or so about us, the master, the mate, and the crew.
> (003-001)

> The profitable ship will carry a large load through all the hazards of the weather, and, when at rest, will stand up in dock and shift from berth to berth without ballast. There are ships, though, which cannot be trusted to do this, even in the smooth water of a dock.
> (013-003)

## The Berthing-Master

An entire class of dock officials populated Conrad's world: berthing-masters, dockmasters, and gatemen. Conrad captured their physical presence with the eye of a novelist — the berthing-master with "rheumaticky feet" worn out by "miles of tramping upon the flagstones of the dock side," his squat body balanced for years upon the decks of small coasters (031-007). Gangs of dock-labourers "swarm with muddy feet over the gangways" (033-002), completing the dock's teeming human landscape.

## Technical Usage

In the late nineteenth century, London's dock system was the largest in the world, stretching along both sides of the Thames from London Bridge down to Tilbury. Conrad's docks were not the modern container terminals but a complex of enclosed basins — St. Katherine's, the London Docks, the West India Docks, and the then-new Tilbury Dock — each with its own character and traditions. Ships moored alongside stone quays, secured by heavy hawsers to bollards, their bows facing the dock-gates. The process of docking required the services of a river pilot, berthing-masters, and dock labourers, and the dock itself was a microcosm of maritime commerce: stevedores, tally clerks, tide-waiters, and Customs officers all played their parts in the life of the docks.
