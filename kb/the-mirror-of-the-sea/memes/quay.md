## Definition

The quay is the threshold between ship and shore — the stone or timber edge where a vessel makes fast and where the commerce of the sea meets the commerce of the land. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, the quay appears as a recurring setting of encounter, labour, and reflection: it is where Conrad first beholds strangers who will become shipmates, where cargo is spread out in impossible bulk, and where the ship lies chained as a "hopeless prisoner." Each quay has its own character, from the muddy edge of a Dutch winter port to the sunlit Circular Quay of Sydney.

## Technical Usage

In Conrad's time, the quay was typically constructed of massive stone blocks (in European ports) or timber piles (in colonial ports). Ships made fast to bollards or stone posts along the quay, with wooden fenders protecting the hull from the grinding contact described by Conrad. The quay was the domain of stevedores, tally clerks, tide-waiters, and Customs officers. The phrase "breast in to the quay" described the careful manoeuvre of bringing a ship alongside, a delicate operation requiring attention to the set of the tide and the proximity of windows overlooking the quayside. Ports like Sydney's Circular Quay were unusual in being directly accessible from the heart of the city, not walled off behind dock-gates — Conrad particularly admired this openness.
