## Definition

The boatswain (or bosun) is the warrant officer who oversees the ship's deck crew, rigging, and ground-tackle. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, the boatswain appears at the pivotal moment of every passage: the approach to land. It is he who, at the chief mate's command, breaks out the anchors and readies the cable. Conrad also shows the boatswain in the grinding routine of heavy weather, a figure of gruff companionship--even when not personally liked--sharing a dry spot on a wave-swept deck, acknowledging the elemental fury with understated words.

## The Boatswain and the Approach to Land

The announcement that land is near comes not as a sighting but as a practical order. The chief mate, keeper of the ship's anchors and guardian of her cable, turns to the boatswain with a brisk command: "We will get the anchors over this afternoon." The boatswain is thus the first member of the crew to know--through action, not observation--that the passage is ending. His work is the physical preamble to landfall.

> The first approach to the land, as yet invisible to the crew's eyes, is announced by the brisk order of the chief mate to the boatswain: "We will get the anchors over this afternoon" or "first thing tomorrow morning," as the case may be. For the chief mate is the keeper of the ship's anchors and the guardian of her cable. (005-002)

The ritual of preparing the anchors unfolds under the boatswain's hands. He and the carpenter assist the chief mate in ranging the cable, disconnecting the windlass, and opening the compressors. Then comes the final order--"Stand clear of the cable!"--and the boatswain waits in the silent ship for the sharp shout from aft: "Let go!" His eye follows the anchor down, watching to see whether it has gone clear. This moment, the culmination of a voyage, belongs as much to the boatswain as to the master who gives the final command.

> There, too, on the approach to the land, assisted by the boatswain and the carpenter, he "gets the anchors over" with the men of his own watch, whom he knows better than the others. There he sees the cable ranged, the windlass disconnected, the compressors opened; and there, after giving his own last order, "Stand clear of the cable!" he waits attentive, in a silent ship that forges slowly ahead towards her picked-out berth, for the sharp shout from aft, "Let go!" (005-006)

## The Boatswain in Heavy Weather

The boatswain's role extends beyond the rituals of anchoring. In heavy weather, when the ship is brought-to and bowing to enormous flashing seas, the boatswain holds his post. Conrad recalls a gale in which he sought human companionship and found himself beside the boatswain--a man he "did not like"--in the only comparatively dry spot on deck. The exchange between them is brief and elemental: "Blows very hard, boatswain." The boatswain's reply is not recorded, but the fact of his presence is enough. In the extremity of weather, the hierarchies of personal liking dissolve; what remains is the shared experience of men who stand together against the sea.

> I craved human companionship, and, coming off the poop, took my place by the side of the boatswain (a man whom I did not like) in a comparatively dry spot where at worst we had water only up to our knees. Above our heads the explosive booming gusts of wind passed continuously, justifying the sailor's saying "It blows great guns." (024-003)

> And just from that need of human companionship, being very close to the man, I said, or rather shouted: "Blows very hard, boatswain." (024-004)

This vignette captures something essential about the boatswain's place in shipboard society. He is not an officer, yet he commands the deck crew. He is not a confidant, yet in the extremity of a gale he is the man the young officer stands beside. The boatswain occupies the middle ground of the ship's hierarchy--close enough to the men to know their capacities, close enough to the officers to receive their orders, and at home enough on the deck to find the one dry spot in a world of water.
