## Identity

Doña Rita was a central figure in the *Tremolino* syndicate — the informal partnership that owned and operated the tartane in a Mediterranean smuggling enterprise connected to Carlist Royalist politics. Conrad introduces her as the intelligence gatherer and social facilitator without whom the entire undertaking would have been impossible. She occupied a small furnished house on the Prado in Barcelona, where she received the syndicate members and directed their operations with a combination of political acumen and feminine charm.

## Conrad's Account

> It was not Tremolino's fault that the syndicate depended so much on the wit and wisdom and the information of Doña Rita. She had taken a little furnished house on the Prado for the good of the cause — por el Rey! (042-001)

> After half an hour's interview in the dining-room, during which they got in touch with each other in an amazing way, Rita told me afterwards that he was "perfect." (042-008)

> Anyway, he was perfect, as Doña Rita had declared. The only thing unsatisfactory (and even inexplicable) about our Dominic was his nephew, Cesar. (043-001)

## Political Role

Doña Rita operated at the intersection of smuggling, politics, and intelligence. The syndicate's Royalist affiliations placed her in a network of Carlist sympathisers and informants, a milieu in which her salon became a clearing-house for information vital to the *Tremolino*'s operations. Conrad's portrait is deliberately elliptical — he never fully explains her background or her fate — but he credits her with the practical intelligence that made the venture possible. In this she represents a type rare in Conrad's maritime writing: a woman whose influence on events at sea was both real and respected.
