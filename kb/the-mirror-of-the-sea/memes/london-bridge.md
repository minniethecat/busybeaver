## The Threshold of the Sea

For Conrad, London Bridge was more than a crossing — it was a symbolic boundary. Below it lay the sea's domain: the docks, the estuary, the open water. Above it, the river belonged to the city. The transition was palpable: the salt air gave way to the hum of industry, the freedom of the sea to the press of brick and mortar. Conrad's London Bridge marks the exact point where the sailor's world yields to the landsman's.

> Wharves, landing-places, dock-gates, waterside stairs, follow each other continuously right up to London Bridge, and the hum of men's work fills the river with a menacing, muttering note as of a breathless, ever-driving gale. (031-004)

## The Upper Reach

Above London Bridge, the Thames becomes a different river — no longer the fair waterway of the estuary but a commercial corridor, its banks "oppressed by bricks and mortar and stone." Conrad contrasts this with the open river below, where ships still carry the tang of the sea. The bridge is the hinge between these two rivers: the romantic stream of history and the industrial artery of empire.

> The waterway, so fair above and wide below, flows oppressed by bricks and mortar and stone, by blackened timber and grimed glass and rusty iron, covered with black barges, whipped up by paddles and screws, overburdened with craft, overhung with chains, overshadowed by walls making a steep gorge for its bed, filled with a haze of smoke and dust. (031-004)

## The Jungle of the Waterside

Conrad's most striking image of the Thames above London Bridge is architectural: the waterside is not planned but grown, a "virgin forest" of buildings that have sprung up "as if by accident from scattered seeds." This is the waterside of watersides — uniquely London, uniquely impenetrable, hiding "the depths of London's infinitely varied, vigorous, seething life" behind its lightless walls.

> This stretch of the Thames from London Bridge to the Albert Docks is to other watersides of river ports what a virgin forest would be to a garden. It is a thing grown up, not made. (031-005)

## The Pool of London

In the age of sail, the Pool — the reach of the Thames just below London Bridge — was the historic heart of the port. Ships moored stem and stern in the strength of the tide, their masts forming "one solid mass like an island covered with a forest of gaunt, leafless trees." Conrad witnessed the tail end of this era before the docks further downstream absorbed the traffic. The image of the Pool is his memorial to the medieval port that London once was.

> That was in the days when, in the part called the Pool, just below London Bridge, the vessels moored stem and stern in the very strength of the tide formed one solid mass like an island covered with a forest of gaunt, leafless trees… (032-006)

## Fog on the River

In fog, the entire length of the Thames — from London Bridge down to the Nore — falls silent. The ringing of ships' bells, "mysterious and muffled in the white vapour," is the only sound. For Conrad, this stillness is the river's breathing time, a reminder that even the world's busiest waterway has moments of repose.

> After the gradual cessation of all sound and movement on the faithful river, only the ringing of ships' bells is heard, mysterious and muffled in the white vapour from London Bridge right down to the Nore, for miles and miles in a decrescendo tinkling… (032-007)
