The **poop** (or *poop deck*) is the raised deck at the after (stern) end of a sailing vessel, forming the roof of the after cabin. In Conrad's maritime world, the poop is the domain of command — the place from which the master or officer of the watch oversees the ship's course, issues orders, and maintains the sovereign authority of the quarterdeck.

## Function and Authority

The poop deck is the elevated platform from which the ship is conned. On merchant vessels of Conrad's era, it was the commander's station — a place of both practical observation and symbolic authority. From its height, the officer on watch could survey the deck, the sails, and the sea ahead.

> He walks the poop darting gloomy glances, as though he wished to poison the sea, and snaps your head off savagely whenever you happen to blunder within earshot. (002-006)

The break of the poop — the forward edge of this raised deck — provided partial shelter from wind and spray, and was often where the watch below would gather during heavy weather.

> One wintry, blustering, dark night in July, as I stood sleepily out of the rain under the break of the poop something resembling an ostrich dashed up the gangway. (034-003)

## Command and Observation

The poop was the vantage point from which landfalls were sighted and the approaches to harbour were judged. Conrad repeatedly portrays the master on the poop, gazing forward, as the ship makes her way towards port or through perilous seas.

> Many times in my life, standing in long sea-boots and streaming oilskins at the elbow of my commander on the poop of a homeward-bound ship making for the Channel, and gazing ahead into the grey and tormented waste, I have heard a weary sigh shape itself into a studiously casual comment. (026-004)

## Heavy Weather

The exposed position of the poop made it a dangerous place in severe conditions. Conrad records instances of the poop being swept by heavy seas, and of men being taken from it by the violence of the ocean.

> A heavy sea took him off the poop in the run between New Zealand and the Horn. (012-017)

A heavy sea could burst through the poop skylights, flooding the cabin below — a reminder that even the commanding heights of the ship were not immune to the sea's power.

> …a heavy sea having burst the poop skylights the night before… (027-003)

In the midst of a gale, the force of the wind could pin a man against the forward edge of the poop, as Conrad experienced:

> The force of the wind, though we were running before it at the rate of some ten knots an hour, was so great that it drove me with a steady push to the front of the poop, where my commander was holding on. (027-004)

## Arrivals and Departures

The poop also frames the contrasting rituals of departure and arrival. The noisy, exultant getting of the anchor contrasts with the silent approach to a foreign roadstead, where the master stands at the break of the poop watching the ship glide to her berth.

> …this exultation at the moment of the ship's departure, make a tremendous contrast to the silent moments of her arrival in a foreign roadstead — the silent moments when, stripped of her sails, she forges ahead to her chosen berth… the master gazing intently forward from the break of the poop. (006-003)
