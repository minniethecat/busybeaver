## Event Summary

In the late 1870s, Joseph Conrad — then a young officer serving as chief mate — was tasked with loading a general cargo at Amsterdam for a passage to Samarang. The winter was bitter: the canals froze, the cargo was trapped upcountry, and the ship lay idle for weeks while Conrad endured daily letters from owners demanding action. When the thaw finally came, the cargo arrived in a rush, and Conrad — under pressure and inexperienced — followed his chief mate's misguided advice on stowage, placing too much weight low in the ship. The result was a vessel that was "much too stable": she rolled so violently and abruptly that a spar carried away, striking the chief mate and breaking his leg. Conrad reflected that it was "only poetic justice that the chief mate who had made a mistake — perhaps a half-excusable one — about the distribution of his ship's cargo should pay the penalty."

## Conrad's Narrative

> There has been a time when a ship's chief mate, pocketbook in hand and pencil behind his ear, kept one eye aloft upon his riggers and the other down the hatchway on the stevedores, and watched the disposition of his ship's cargo, knowing that even before she starts he has already made her fate for the voyage. (013-001)

> The hurry of the times, the loading and discharging organization of the docks, the use of hoisting machinery which works quickly and will not wait, the cry for prompt despatch, the very size of his ship, stand nowadays between the modern seaman and the thorough knowledge of his craft. (013-002)

> The loading of ships was once a matter of skill, judgment, and knowledge. Thick books have been written about it. Stevens on Stowage is a portly volume with the renown and weight (in its own world) of Coke on Littleton. Stevens is an agreeable writer, and his book is an admirable compendium of the science of stowing cargo. (013-005)

> I was, as the French say, biting my fists with impatience for that cargo frozen upcountry; with rage at that canal set fast, at the wintry and deserted aspect of all those ships that seemed to decay in grim depression for want of the open water. (014-003)

> Notwithstanding the little iron stove, the ink froze on the swing-table in the cabin, and I found it more convenient to go ashore stumbling over the arctic wasteland and shivering in glazed tramcars in order to write my evening letter to my owners in a gorgeous café in the centre of the town. (014-004)

> At last the cargo did come. At first it came dribbling in by rail in trucks, till the thaw set in; and then fast, in a multitude of barges, with a great rush of unbound waters. The gentle master stevedore had his hands very full at last; and the chief mate became worried in his mind as to the proper distribution of the weight of his cargo. (014-008)

> On our passage from Amsterdam to Samarang with a general cargo, of which, alas! only one-third in weight was stowed "above the beams," we had a lively time of it. It was lively, but not joyful. There was not even a single moment of ease. (015-006)

> To travel along with a cranky ship for ninety days or so is no doubt a nerve-trying experience; but in this case what was wrong with our craft was this: that by my system of loading she had been made much too stable. (015-007)

> Neither before nor since have I felt a ship roll so abruptly, so violently, so heavily. Once she began, you felt that she would never stop, and this hopeless sensation, characterizing the motion of ships whose centre of gravity is brought down too low. (015-008)

> It was only poetic justice that the chief mate who had made a mistake — perhaps a half-excusable one — about the distribution of his ship's cargo should pay the penalty. A piece of one of the minor spars that did carry away flew against the chief mate's leg and broke it. (015-011)

## Context & Background

The art of loading a sailing-ship was, in Conrad's day, a complex science. A ship's behaviour at sea depended critically on how her cargo was distributed: too much weight low made her "crank" (over-stable, with violent, jerky rolling); too much weight high made her "tender" (liable to heel dangerously). The chief mate was responsible for this distribution, balancing the ship's natural stability against the weight and volume of cargo. Stevens on Stowage, the standard reference work, was a "portly volume" comparable in its field to Coke on Littleton in law.

Conrad's Amsterdam experience illustrates the transition from this careful science to the modern practice of simply "filling up" a steamship's holds — a change he regarded as symptomatic of the decline of the sailing-ship era.

## Outcome & Aftermath

The ship made her passage to Samarang, but the ninety-day voyage was a "nerve-trying experience" for all aboard. The chief mate, who had pressed Conrad to adopt the faulty stowage plan, suffered a broken leg when a spar carried away — an injury Conrad saw as "poetic justice." The lesson was indelible: a ship's fate is determined, in large part, before she ever leaves the dock, by the skill and judgment applied to her loading.

Conrad would later contrast this careful art with the mechanised loading of steamships, where "her cargo is not stowed in any sense; it is simply dumped into her through six hatchways." For him, the decline of the stevedore's craft was part of the larger story of the sea's transformation in the age of steam.
