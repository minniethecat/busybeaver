## Location and Identity

New South Dock was one of the London docks on the Thames where Conrad observed ships in their most diminished state — not under sail but captive, tied up, their spars being repaired and their crews dispersed. It serves in *The Mirror of the Sea* as the setting for Conrad's meditation on the melancholy of ships removed from their element, surrounded by the "swarm of renegades — dockmasters, berthing-masters, gatemen, and suchlike" who constitute the shore-based infrastructure of maritime commerce.

## Conrad's Description

> The swarm of renegades — dockmasters, berthing-masters, gatemen, and suchlike — appear to nurse an immense distrust of the captive ship's resignation. There never seem chains and ropes enough to satisfy their minds. (033-002)

> It was a sight. The humblest craft that floats makes its appeal to a seaman by the faithfulness of her life; and this was the place where one beheld the aristocracy of ships. (035-003)

> In the New South Dock there was certainly no time for remorse, introspection, repentance, or any phenomena of inner life either for the captive ships or for their officers. From six in the morning till six at night the hard labour of the dock consumed every faculty. (033-009)

> The absence of picturesqueness cannot be laid to the charge of the docks opening into the Thames. In their way they are as romantic as the river they serve is unlike all the other commercial streams of the world. (032-004)

## Maritime Significance

The London docks, of which New South Dock was representative, occupy a distinctive place in Conrad's maritime world. They are the shore — the place where a ship ceases to be a ship and becomes an object of commerce, subject to the scrutiny of dockmasters and the indignities of repair. Conrad's portrait of dock life is one of the most vivid sections of *The Mirror of the Sea*: the captive ships, their officers reduced to the status of caretakers, the "hard labour" that consumed every daylight hour, and the strange romance that attached even to this world of rust, rope, and grudging officialdom.
