The **fishing-smack** is the smallest class of sea-going vessel that Conrad invokes in *The Mirror of the Sea* — a working craft whose vulnerability to wind and weather is proportional to its size, and whose crew's seamanship must compensate for what the vessel lacks in tonnage.

## In Conrad's Words

The fishing-smack appears in Conrad's great set-piece on the West Wind, where it stands at one end of a spectrum of vulnerability that runs from the humblest fishing boat to the greatest four-masted ship:

> The mere hint of his approach fills with dread every craft that swims the sea, from fishing-smacks to four-masted ships that recognize the sway of the West Wind.
>
> (029-002)

The West Wind is the great leveller — no vessel, however large or small, is exempt from the dread it inspires. The fishing-smack, as the smallest craft in this hierarchy, represents the extreme of exposure: the vessel with the least freeboard, the lightest ground-tackle, and the narrowest margin between survival and foundering when the wind turns against it.

Conrad also notes the fishing-smack's place in the seasonal rhythm of a seaman's life. The men who handle the fore-and-aft rig in yacht racing are the same men who fish through the winter:

> The sailing and racing of yachts has developed a class of fore-and-aft sailors, men born and bred to the sea, fishing in winter and yachting in summer; men to whom the handling of that particular rig presents no mystery.
>
> (008-001)

The fishing-smack, in this context, is the winter vessel — the working platform that keeps a man at sea between the summer yachting seasons. It is the school of practical seamanship, where handling a boat in heavy weather is not a sport but a livelihood.

In a quieter passage, a "fishing hamlet" appears as a landmark — a point of human habitation on an otherwise empty coast, marking the destination of a small boat's passage:

> After we had gone a little way, the fishing hamlet we were making for came into view.
>
> (045-028)

## Significance

The fishing-smack occupies the bottom rung of Conrad's maritime hierarchy, but it is a position of honour. The men who work these boats are "born and bred to the sea" — they possess an intuitive seamanship that no amount of training on larger vessels can replicate. The smack, by its very smallness, proves the point that Conrad makes throughout *The Mirror of the Sea*: that the sea makes no distinction between great and small, and that seamanship is a quality of the man, not of the ship.
