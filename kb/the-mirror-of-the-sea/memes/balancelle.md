# Balancelle

The **balancelle** is a small Mediterranean sailing craft, distinguished by two short masts raking forward and two curved yards each as long as her hull, spreading enormous lateen sails. In Conrad's memoir, this vessel carries deep personal significance: she was his cradle as a seaman.

## A Child of the Latin Lake

Conrad describes the balancelle with the precision of one who has studied her every line at sea:

> In reality, she was a true balancelle, with two short masts raking forward and two curved yards, each as long as her hull; a true child of the Latin lake, with a spread of two enormous sails resembling the wings of an ocean bird. (040-003)

The comparison to an ocean bird is exact: under those vast sails, the vessel seems to fly rather than sail.

## Built on the River of Savona

The particular balancelle Conrad knew was built by a famous builder on the River of Savona — a detail that anchors the vessel in a specific Mediterranean geography and tradition:

> She who was my cradle in those years had been built on the river of Savona by a famous builder of boats. (040-003)

## At the Tiller

Conrad's most intimate encounter with the balancelle comes in a moment of high tension, when Dominic — the vessel's taciturn master — hands over the tiller:

> "Prenez la barre, monsieur," Dominic broke the silence suddenly in an austere voice. "Take hold of the tiller." He bent his hood to my ear. "The balancelle is yours." (045-010)

This transfer of command, given in French in the darkness, is one of the most charged moments in *The Mirror of the Sea*. The balancelle becomes not merely a vessel but a test of seamanship.

## A Lively Dancer

Under full sail, the balancelle is described with kinetic energy:

> The balancelle hummed and quivered as she flew along, dancing lightly under our feet. (044-001)

The coherence of the image — the hum of the hull, the quiver of the rigging, the lightness of her motion — communicates the exhilaration of a small, responsive craft under a press of canvas in the windy Gulf of Lions.
