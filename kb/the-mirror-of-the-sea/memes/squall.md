## Definition

A squall is a sudden, sharp increase in wind speed, often lasting only minutes but capable of posing a serious threat to sailing vessels. In Conrad's maritime vocabulary, the squall is not a single phenomenon but a family of violent atmospheric events, each with its own character and menace. The term appears throughout *The Mirror of the Sea* both as a meteorological description and as a dramatic device — squalls drive the action of the *Tremolino* escape, punctuate the Westerly gales, and serve as the visible boundary between the West Wind's successive moods.

## The Northwest Squall

The shift from southwest to northwest is Conrad's great set-piece of atmospheric transformation, and the squall is its visible punctuation. As the gale turns, the character of the squalls changes entirely — from the blind, drowning torrents of the southwest to the sharp, percussive volleys of the northwest. Conrad describes the northwest squalls racing up "swift, shapeless, like a black bag full of frozen water ready to burst over your devoted head," each one barely spent before the next appears above the western horizon.

Where the southwest squall drowns and blinds, the northwest squall pelts and stings. Conrad draws the contrast with almost theatrical precision: the southwest phase is a "terrible apparatus of cloud and mists and seas and rain"; the northwest phase substitutes icicles for torrents, making the seaman's "weary eyes water as if in grief." The hail acquires an almost jewelled quality — "descending like showers of crystals and pearls, bounding off the spars, drumming on the sails, pattering on the oilskin coats" — as if the West Wind, having exhausted his rage, now turns to a cold, contemptuous pelting. Yet Conrad insists that each mood "has its own greatness, and each is hard to bear."

## Squalls as Narrative Engine

In the *Tremolino* episode, squalls serve as instruments of escape and concealment. The chase by the *guardacosta* unfolds through a succession of squalls that alternately hide and reveal the pursued and the pursuer, the chasing vessel appearing and vanishing "in a squall, only to reappear again, nearly black, resembling a post stuck upright against the slaty background of solid cloud."

The *Tremolino*'s master, Dominic, deliberately times the final dash to coincide with a squall's arrival — waiting silent minutes until the squall overtakes them, the pursuer disappearing into "a sort of murky whirlwind." Under this moving curtain of obscured visibility, Dominic executes the wrecking of the *Tremolino* on the rocks, while the *guardacosta*, still racing northward under a "frightful press of canvas," continues the chase unaware that its quarry has vanished. In Conrad's hands, the squall is not mere weather but a tactical opportunity available only to those who can read the sky and time their actions to its rhythms.
