## Identity

Dominic Cervoni was a Corsican seaman who served as the padrone (master) of the tartane *Tremolino*, the vessel at the centre of Conrad's Mediterranean smuggling enterprise. Conrad describes him in *The Mirror of the Sea* as both companion and teacher — a man of formidable physical presence, disciplined silence, and an unwavering code of personal honour rooted in his Corsican origins. He belongs to the long tradition of Mediterranean seafarers whose skill and character were forged outside the formal structures of naval service.

## Conrad's Account

> Rearranging my collar, which, truth to say, ought to have been a round one above a short jacket, but was not, I observed felicitously that I had come to say goodbye, being ready to go off to sea with him in his tremolino. (042-004)

> Astute and ruthless, he could have rivalled in resource the unfortunate son of Laertes and Anticlea. If he did not pit his craft and audacity against the very gods, it is only because the Olympian gods are dead. (042-006)

> For want of more exalted adversaries Dominic turned his audacity fertile in impious stratagems against the powers of the earth, as represented by the institution of Customhouses and every mortal belonging thereto. (042-007)

> He would raise a lantern silently to light my steps along the narrow, springy plank of our primitive gangway. "And so we are going off," he would murmur directly my foot touched the shore. (042-005)

> "I must try to make a man of him," Dominic answered hopelessly. (043-015)

> "Prenez la barre, monsieur," Dominic broke the silence suddenly in an austere voice. "Take hold of the tiller." He bent his hood to my ear. "The balancelle is yours. Take her into port." (045-010)

## The Corsican Code

Dominic's character was inseparable from his Corsican heritage. Conrad learned that Dominic's brother had been forced "into the bush on the wild mountainside, to dodge the gendarmes for the unforgivable crime of letting his knife get the better of another man's knife in a casual quarrel." The Corsican vendetta was not exotic melodrama to Dominic but ordinary family obligation — the reason he supported a nephew, Cesar, who was utterly unsuited to the sea.

> This is why — because in Corsica your dead will not leave you alone — Dominic's brother had to go into the maquis. (043-004)

## Command Style

Dominic's authority rested not on volume but on presence. His characteristic smile — "the invisible smile under his moustache" — signalled displeasure more effectively than shouting. When he did speak in command, his voice carried "an austere" quality that brooked no argument. Conrad, a young officer at the time, observed this leadership style with professional admiration. The padrone's silence was as disciplined as his action — during the desperate final escape of the *Tremolino*, Conrad resolved "to utter no sound as long as Dominic himself had the force to keep his lips closed."
