## The Soul of the Sea Departs

The Lower Hope Reach, a gentle bend in the Thames between the open estuary and the Port of London, is for Conrad a symbolic boundary of the first importance. It is here — "at the turn of the first bend above" — that "that hint of loneliness, that soul of the sea" which has accompanied the ship all the way from the open ocean finally "abandons her." The salt tang leaves the air; the sense of unlimited space gives way to the constriction of the river. The Lower Hope Reach is the exact point where the sailor's world ends and the landsman's begins.

> That hint of loneliness, that soul of the sea which had accompanied her as far as the Lower Hope Reach, abandons her at the turn of the first bend above. The salt, acrid flavour is gone out of the air, together with a sense of unlimited space opening free beyond the threshold of sandbanks below the Nore. (031-004)

## The Turn of the River

Physically, the Lower Hope Reach is the point where the Thames makes a distinct turn, bringing the industrial shore into view. "Clusters of factory chimneys come distinctly into view, tall and slender above the squat ranges of cement works in Grays and Greenhithe." Where the estuary below had been desolate and open, the river above the Hope Reach is marked by human industry — chimneys, cement works, the houses of Gravesend. The contrast is Conrad's theme: the sea surrenders to the river, nature to industry, the "soul of the sea" to "the needs, the artifices, the contrivances of toiling men."

> Then, on the slight turn of the Lower Hope Reach, clusters of factory chimneys come distinctly into view, tall and slender above the squat ranges of cement works in Grays and Greenhithe. (031-003)

## The Sea-Freedom Ends

Conrad's geography of the Thames is also a geography of freedom. Below the Lower Hope Reach, the river still carries "the feeling of the open water." Above it, the ship is "surrendered fairly to the grasp of the river." The water becomes "oppressed by bricks and mortar and stone, by blackened timber and grimed glass and rusty iron." The Lower Hope Reach is the gate through which a ship passes from one world into another.

> The waters of the sea rush on past Gravesend, tumbling the big mooring buoys laid along the face of the town; but the sea-freedom stops short there, surrendering the salt tide to the needs, the artifices, the contrivances of toiling men. (031-004)

## A Mariner's Landmark

For the practical seaman navigating the Thames, the Lower Hope Reach was a well-known turning-point — a place where the river's character changed and where the ship's approach to London began in earnest. Conrad's genius is to invest this purely navigational feature with symbolic weight, making it the hinge between two realms: the sea, which is freedom and loneliness, and the river, which is industry and constraint.
