The **topgallant** is the mast section, yard, and sail set above the topmast in a square-rigged sailing ship — the third level of spars above the deck, following the lower mast and the topmast. In the great deep-water ships of Conrad's era, the topgallant yards were the highest spars that were regularly handled, and they carried sails that caught the lightest breezes far above the sea. A ship's "loftiness aloft" — the height and order of her topgallants and royals — was a point of pride for a chief officer, a visible mark of his professional competence.

Conrad renders the topgallant not as a technical abstraction but through a single, unforgettable image: a yardarm about to go through a warehouse window.

## In Conrad's Words

> It is a strange conjunction this of roofs and mastheads, of walls and yardarms.
>
> (031-007)

Conrad opens the passage with philosophical reflection, then grounds it in a concrete memory: his ship, freshly docked in a London basin, is being made fast when an old berthing-master hobbles up the quay in alarm.

> "If you don't look sharp, you'll have your topgallant yards through the windows of that 'ere warehouse presently!"
>
> (031-007)

The berthing-master's warning is comically precise. The topgallant yard, a spar designed to catch the wind in the open ocean, is now suspended mere feet from plate-glass windows. The juxtaposition is absurd — and dangerous. Conrad continues:

> To break windows is the last thing one would think of in connection with a ship's topgallant yard, unless, indeed, one were an experienced berthing-master in one of the London docks.
>
> (031-007)

The passage captures the tension between the ship at sea and the ship in port. The same spars that once spread canvas in the Roaring Forties must now be navigated past brick walls and windowpanes. The old berthing-master, with his "rheumaticky feet" and "little blue eyes," has spent a lifetime learning to see danger in this very incongruity. His expertise lies precisely in recognising that a topgallant yard — a thing of the sea — can become, in the confines of a dock, a threat to property ashore.
