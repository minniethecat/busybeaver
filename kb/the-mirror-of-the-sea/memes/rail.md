The **rail** of a ship — the varnished teakwood rim that runs along the deck's edge — serves in Conrad's memoir as more than a structural feature. It is a place of leaning, watching, and decision: the captain "leaned over, spreading his red and freckled elbows on the rail" (036-028), the chief mate "flicking the dust off the varnished teakwood rail" (005-009) in a gesture of devotion to his vessel. The rail is where the seaman meets the sea — and where cargo, visitors, and fate cross from shore to ship.

## The vantage of command

When things go wrong at sea, the first instinct of those in authority is to go to the rail and look. Conrad's captain, after the rescue, leans over the rail to check their speed: "He took a few more turns on the poop, glancing down over the rail now and then to see how fast we were moving" (036-004). Dominic, in the dark escape of the *Tremolino*, "stepped quietly to the rail and leaned over, waiting" (043-009) at the sound of a splash — the rail the threshold between knowing and not knowing.

> He took a few more turns on the poop, glancing down over the rail now and then to see how fast we were moving. (036-004)

> At the sound of the heavy plop alongside horror held me rooted to the spot; but Dominic stepped quietly to the rail and leaned over, waiting. (043-009)

## Crossing the rail

The rail is the ship's frontier. Cargo-lighters come alongside and the ship's own tackle swings "the burden over the rail" (033-004). A new man comes aboard "right off the rail" with "sudden, spontaneous agility" (015-002), revealing character in a single movement. The rail marks the boundary between ship and shore, between the ordered world of the vessel and the chaos of the port or the open sea.

> A ship anchored in an open roadstead, with cargo-lighters alongside and her own tackle swinging the burden over the rail, is accomplishing in its sovereign steadfastness the mission of the ship. (033-004)

## Devotion to the rail

The varnished teakwood rail was an object of almost religious care. Conrad describes a chief mate who went about "flicking the dust off the varnished teakwood rail of the little craft with a silk pocket-handkerchief — a silk handkerchief, mind you!" (005-009). This devotion to the rail was not vanity but a form of seamanship — the gleaming rail the outward sign of a well-kept ship.

> And that sort of faithful and proud devotion went so far as to make him go about flicking the dust off the varnished teakwood rail of the little craft with a silk pocket-handkerchief — a silk handkerchief, mind you! (005-009)
