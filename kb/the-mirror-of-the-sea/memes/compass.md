# Compass

The **compass** is the mariner's primary instrument for determining direction at sea — a magnetic needle aligned to the earth's poles, housed within the binnacle on the ship's deck. For Conrad, the compass is both a practical tool of navigation and a symbol of the fixed purpose that guides a ship across the trackless ocean.

## The Compass in Departure

The taking of a departure — the ship's final reckoning with land — is an act centred on the compass. The navigator fixes the vessel's position by precise observation of landmarks:

> But there is this difference in the Departure: that the term does not imply so much a sea event as a definite act entailing a process — the precise observation of certain landmarks by means of the compass card. (001-002)

The Departure is "distinctly a ceremony of navigation," and the compass is its essential instrument. The seaman takes his departure "by means of cross-bearings which fix the place of the first tiny pencil-cross on the white expanse of the track-chart." From that point onward, the ship's position at each noon will be marked by another pencil cross — sixty, eighty, sometimes a hundred and thirty of them — all derived from compass bearings.

Once the departure is taken, the land drops away, and the compass becomes the ship's sole reference:

> You encompass the earth with one particular spot of it in your eye. (001-005)

## The Binnacle as Ship's Heart

The binnacle, housing the compass, stands at the physical and symbolic centre of the vessel. In Conrad's depiction, the compass is where the officer on watch pauses to gather himself:

> Leaning very much against the precipitous incline of the deck, he would take a turn or two, perfectly silent, hang on by the compass for a while, take another couple of turns, and suddenly burst out. (011-009)

The image is precise: in heavy weather, with the deck canted steeply, the compass binnacle is one of the few stable points a man can cling to — physically and mentally. The officer hangs on by it, gathers his thoughts, then "suddenly burst out" with whatever has been working in his mind.

## The Bearing

The compass gives the bearing — the angular direction of a landmark or celestial body relative to the ship's heading. In Conrad's lexicon, the bearing is the fundamental unit of positional awareness. A landfall is "good" if the landmark appears "on the expected bearing." A landfall made "on a wrong bearing, in thick weather, and during a fresh onshore gale" is the stuff of a captain's nightmares — the sort of anxiety that, Conrad notes with clinical precision, could drive a man to secret drinking.

The bearing is also the last thing a dying mariner relinquishes. Conrad's account of visiting his old captain, convalescent at home, ends with the haunting image of the man in his easy chair, gazing fixedly ahead "as if there had been nothing between him and the straight line of sea and sky, where whatever a seaman is looking for is first bound to appear." Conrad asks: "Was he looking out for a strange Landfall, or taking with an untroubled mind the bearings for his last Departure?" The compass bearing — the act of fixing one's position relative to a known point — becomes the ruling metaphor for a life's final reckonings.

## A Fixed Point in the Enchanted Circle

In a fleet becalmed, each ship seems to drift independently — yet the compass binds them all to the same coordinate system:

> There were hardly two of them heading exactly the same way, as if each had meditated breaking out of the enchanted circle at a different point of the compass. (010-001)

The phrase "point of the compass" is used here with deliberate ambiguity — it is both the navigational instrument and the abstract notion of direction. The compass, for Conrad, is never merely a device; it is the shared mental framework that makes the trackless ocean legible.
