## Definition

The cable is the heavy rope or chain connecting a ship to its anchor. In the maritime world Conrad depicts, the cable is not inert iron — it is a living thing that "grows," a word the sailor uses with the precision of an artist. Chain cable in Conrad's day was measured in shackles (each of fifteen fathoms, or ninety feet), and the length veered out — the "scope" — was a matter of careful judgement, balancing the depth of water, the holding ground, and the strength of wind and tide.

The cable mediates the most critical relationship in anchoring: that between the ship above and the anchor below. A bad cable, or a badly tended one, can ruin the best anchor on the finest holding ground. A good cable, properly ranged and fairly led, will hold a ship through a gale.

## The Cable's Demands

A well-laid anchor demands a fair pull, and the cable is the instrument of that fairness:

> Unless the pull of the cable is fair on the ring, no anchor can be trusted even on the best of holding ground.
>
> (005-007)

The "fair" pull means a straight, even strain — no twisting, no fouling around stock or fluke. A fouled cable turns the most carefully set anchor into a treacherous lump of iron. Conrad elaborates:

> Your anchor must drop from the bow of your ship with no turn of cable on any of its limbs, else you would be riding to a foul anchor.
>
> (005-007)

The anchor's "limbs" — its arms and flukes — must be free of any entanglement with the cable. Even a single turn can prevent the flukes from biting into the ground, leaving the ship to drag slowly toward disaster. The phrase "riding to a foul anchor" carries the weight of professional shame: it is a thing that should never happen, and when it does, it is always someone's fault.

## The Chief Mate's Charge

Conrad assigns the cable to the special custody of the chief mate — the officer who "watches the growth of the cable" and bears responsibility for every link and turn. This is no ceremonial guardianship but a practical and demanding trust:

> For the chief mate is the keeper of the ship's anchors and the guardian of her cable.
>
> (005-002)

The chief mate's domain is the forecastle, where the ground-tackle lives:

> There he sees the cable ranged, the windlass disconnected, the compressors opened; and there, after giving his own last order, "Stand clear of the cable!" he takes the final responsibility.
>
> (005-006)

"Stand clear of the cable!" is the last warning before the anchor drops — an acknowledgement that a running chain is a mortal danger. A man caught in the bight of a paying-out cable would be crushed or dragged overboard before anyone could intervene. The chief mate's authority at this moment is absolute.

## "How Does the Cable Grow?"

Conrad opens his essay on anchors by insisting on the seaman's peculiar vocabulary, and the cable is his prime example:

> The sailor will never say, "cast anchor," and the shipmaster aft will hail his chief mate on the forecastle in impressionistic phrase: "How does the cable grow?" Because "grow" is the right word for the long drift of a cable emerging aslant under the strain, taut as a bowstring above the water.
>
> (006-001)

The verb "grow" captures the organic quality of the cable under strain. It does not merely run out; it lengthens from the hawse-hole like something alive, stretching toward the sea-bed. The metaphor is not decorative but functional: the seaman must watch the cable as a gardener watches a plant, judging its angle, its tension, its rate of growth. "How does the cable grow?" is a question that contains within it the entire art of anchoring.

## The Cable at Sea

When a vessel clears the narrow seas and heads for the open ocean, the ritual of stowing the ground-tackle marks a psychological transition. The cable disappears from view, and with it the constant awareness of the land:

> But directly she is clear of the narrow seas, heading out into the world with nothing solid to speak of between her and the South Pole, the anchors are got in and the cables disappear from the deck.
>
> (005-001)

The cables "disappear from the deck" — stowed below in the chain locker, they will not be seen again until the ship approaches land on the other side of the world. Their disappearance marks the true beginning of the voyage, the moment when the ship commits herself wholly to the sea.

Yet even stowed, the cable is not forgotten. The great piles of chain in the lockers add weight to the hull's deepest recesses; they are part of the ship's ballast, part of her stability:

> The lightness of these forms, devised to meet the winds and the seas, makes, by contrast with the great piles of bricks, the chains and cables of their moorings, etc., a more vivid impression of ships giving themselves up to the winds and the seas.
>
> (032-001)

Here the cables are part of a larger contrast — between the solid, land-bound world of brick and chain and the free, light, sea-going world of the ships themselves.

## The Cable in Port

At the other end of the voyage, the cable returns to service, binding the ship to the shore:

> Chain cables and stout ropes keep her bound to stone posts at the edge of a paved shore, and a berthing-master, with brass buttons on his coat, walks the quay.
>
> (033-001)

The cable here is part of the apparatus of arrival — the transition from the freedom of the sea to the constraint of the land. It is the same chain that held the ship off a lee shore, now wrapped around a stone post, performing the opposite function with equal faithfulness.

## Faithfulness and Failure

The anchor itself is a thing of almost moral quality in Conrad's world — it is faithful, it keeps its promise — but only within limits:

> An anchor is forged and fashioned for faithfulness; give it ground that it can bite, and it will hold till the cable parts, and then, whatever may afterwards befall the ship, that is not the anchor's fault.
>
> (004-004)

"Till the cable parts" — the cable is the final limit of the anchor's faithfulness. An anchor that holds while its cable breaks has done its duty; the failure lies elsewhere, perhaps in the cable itself, or in the seamanship that set it, or in the weather that exceeded all calculation. The cable, like all human contrivances, has its breaking point.

In extremis, the cable may be the last thing standing between the ship and destruction. Conrad's description of a near-disaster in a gale captures this:

> But it is a wonder that we did not carry away the cables of both our anchors, for, as may be imagined, I did not stand upon the order to "Let go!"
>
> (009-007)

The phrase "carry away the cables" means to part them — to have them snap under the strain. That the cables held is attributed not to seamanship but to wonder, to luck, to something beyond the merely professional.
