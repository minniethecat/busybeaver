## Definition

Anchoring is the act of securing a ship to the seabed by means of her anchors. To Conrad, however, it is far more than a mechanical operation. It is the "beginning and the end of every passage" — the bookend ritual that marks the completion of a voyage and the promise of departure. The anchor itself is "forged and fashioned for faithfulness," a weighty symbol of hope made in iron, and the chief mate — the keeper of the ship's anchors — is its guardian. Conrad corrects the landsman's misconception: anchoring is not a process of throwing something overboard, but of "letting go" — the anchor is already poised at the bow, waiting.

## In Conrad's Words

> An anchor is a forged piece of iron, admirably adapted to its end, and technical language is an instrument wrought into perfection by ages of experience, a flawless thing for its purpose. (004-003)

> An anchor is forged and fashioned for faithfulness; give it ground that it can bite, and it will hold till the cable parts, and then, whatever may afterwards befall its ship, that anchor is "lost." (004-004)

> The anchor ready for its work is already overboard, and is not thrown over, but simply allowed to fall. It hangs at the cat-head, with its ring clear of the timber, and the chain rove through the hawse-pipe, waiting only for the order to "let go." (004-005)

> To speak with severe technicality, a ship or a fleet is "brought up" — the complementary words unpronounced and unwritten being, of course, "to an anchor." (004-007)

> From first to last the seaman's thoughts are very much concerned with his anchors. It is not so much that the anchor is a symbol of hope as that it is the heaviest object that he has to handle on board his ship at sea in the usual routine of his duties. The beginning and the end of every passage are marked by the handling of the anchors. (005-001)

> The first approach to the land, as yet invisible to the crew's eyes, is announced by the brisk order of the chief mate to the boatswain: "We will get the anchors over this afternoon" or "first thing tomorrow morning," as the case may be. For the chief mate is the keeper of the ship's anchors and the guardian of her cable. (005-002)

> This is the final word of a ship's ended journey, the closing word of her toil and of her achievement. In a life whose worth is told out in passages from port to port, the splash of the anchor's fall and the thunderous rumbling of the chain are like the closing of a distant period, of which she seems to breathe the closing sigh. (006-004)

## Technical Usage

Conrad describes the anchoring operation with characteristic precision. Before the land is even visible, the chief mate orders the anchors prepared. The boatswain and carpenter assist in ranging the cable, disconnecting the windlass, and opening the compressors. When the order "Let go!" comes from the bridge, the anchor drops from the cat-head — but it must "go clear," free of any turn of chain on its limbs. A foul anchor cannot be trusted.

The chief mate is the central figure in anchoring operations. Conrad devotes chapter 6 to this officer's relationship with his anchors — the man who "sees them go down clear and come up sometimes foul," whose professional pride is bound up in the proper handling of these massive iron instruments.

## The Anchor as Symbol

Though Conrad insists the anchor is not so much a "symbol of hope" as it is the heaviest burden of the seaman's working life, he cannot fully escape its symbolic weight. The anchor is the ship's ultimate reliance — the one object that can save her when the sea turns hostile near the land. Its "faithfulness" is a moral quality, and Conrad's language — "forged and fashioned for faithfulness" — invests the anchor with a kind of ethical duty.
