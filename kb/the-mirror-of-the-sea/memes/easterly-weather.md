The *Easterly Weather* — and its presiding spirit, the East Wind — occupies one half of Joseph Conrad's great symbolic opposition between the winds of the world. Where the West Wind is the forthright, honourable ruler of the high latitudes, the East Wind is its dark twin: a cold, treacherous tyrant whose dominion extends over the easterly latitudes with a malign persistence that Conrad portrays in almost personal terms.

> Still, the easterly winds, and, generally speaking, the easterly weather all the world over, is characterized by regularity and persistence. (025-002)

This regularity, which might seem a virtue, is in Conrad's treatment the very source of the East Wind's menace. It does not bluster and pass; it settles in, holds fast, and grinds down the sailor's spirit through sheer duration.

## The Ruler of the Easterly Weather

Conrad personifies the winds as monarchs, and the East Wind is a king in his own right — but a king of a very different character from his westerly brother:

> As a ruler, the East Wind has a remarkable stability; as an invader of the high latitudes lying under the tumultuous sway of his great brother, the Wind of the West, he is extremely difficult to dislodge. (025-003)

The relationship between the two winds is not one of equals. The West Wind permits his brother's incursions — but not without cost to the sailors caught in the interregnum:

> The West Wind keeps faith with his brother, the King of the Easterly Weather. "What we have divided we have divided," he seems to say in his gruff voice, this ruler without guile, who hurls as if in sport the whole North Atlantic against the continents. (028-002)

The irony is that the West Wind's honour — his keeping of faith with his brother — is precisely what condemns the sailor to suffer under the Easterly Weather's tyranny. The West Wind could sweep the East Wind aside, but he will not, because he has made a compact and he keeps it.

## The Character of the East Wind

Where the West Wind is loud, passionate, and transparent in his rages, the East Wind is cold, silent, and treacherous:

> The East Wind, an interloper in the dominions of Westerly weather, is an impassive-faced tyrant with a sharp poniard held behind his back for a treacherous stab. (028-004)

> In his forays into the North Atlantic the East Wind behaves like a subtle and cruel adventurer without a notion of honour or fair play. (028-005)

The East Wind's cruelty is not the honest violence of the gale but the calculated malice of the tyrant. Conrad imagines the East Wind's motivation as pure spite:

> It is my idea that the Easterly Wind helps the ships away from home in the wicked hope that they shall all come to an untimely end and be heard of no more. (028-005)

## Starvation and Captivity

The most vivid passages in Conrad's treatment of the Easterly Weather describe its capacity to hold fleets captive for weeks, imposing starvation not by direct violence but by simple, unyielding obstruction:

> It looked as if the Easterly Weather had come to stay forever, or, at least, till we had all starved to death in the held-up fleet — starved within sight, as it were, of plenty, within touch, almost, of the delicacies of the shore. (028-006)

The cruelty lies in the proximity — the sailors can see the shore, can almost touch it, but the East Wind keeps them pinned at sea. This is psychological torment as much as physical deprivation:

> It was just like the East Wind's nature to inflict starvation upon the bodies of unoffending sailors, while he corrupted their simple souls by an exasperation leading to outbursts of profanity as lurid as a volcanic eruption. (028-006)

## Unnatural Clarity

One of the most distinctive characteristics of the Easterly Weather is its preternatural clarity. The air becomes crystalline — and this very clarity is a form of malevolence, since it allows the becalmed sailor to see, with perfect distinctness, the full scale of his predicament:

> In the atmosphere of the Easterly Weather, as pellucid as a piece of crystal and refracting like a prism, we could see the appalling numbers of our helpless company, even to those who in more normal conditions would have remained invisible. It is the malicious pleasure of the East Wind to augment the power of your eyesight, in order, perhaps, that you should see better the perfect humiliation, the hopeless character of your captivity. (029-001)

This passage is remarkable for its inversion of a normally positive quality. Clear weather is what the sailor wishes for; but the Easterly Weather's clarity is a weapon, a means of rubbing the sailor's nose in his own helplessness.

> Easterly Weather is generally clear, and that is all that can be said for it — almost supernaturally clear when it likes; but whatever its mood, there is something uncanny in its nature. (029-001)

## The Deceitful Barometer

The East Wind is uniquely capable of deceiving the sailor's most trusted instrument — the barometer. Where a Westerly gale announces itself with a falling glass, the Easterly gale slips in unannounced:

> No barometer will give warning of an Easterly gale, were it ever so wet. It is simply that the wiles of the East Wind are too much for its fundamental honesty. After years and years of experience the most trusty instrument of the sort that ever went to sea screwed on to a ship's cabin bulkhead will, almost invariably, be induced to rise by the diabolic ingenuity of the Easterly weather, just as it ought to do before the come of fine weather. (029-001)

This is perhaps the most damning indictment of all: the East Wind corrupts even the instruments, making them lie. The barometer rises, as it should for fair weather — and then the Easterly gale strikes.

## The Changing Moods

For all its stereotyped character, the Easterly Weather has its variations. When it turns from dry to wet, the change is peculiarly venomous:

> But the dry, Easterly Weather, when it turns to wet, seems to rain poisoned showers upon your head. And the stormy mood of the Easterly Weather looms black upon the sky with a peculiar and amazing blackness. (029-001)

Conrad also notes a strange constancy in the East Wind's character across the globe. Whether coming off a continent or an ocean, something of its essential nature persists:

> To us here, for instance, the East Wind comes across a great continent, sweeping over the greatest body of solid land upon this earth. For the Australian east coast the East Wind is the wind of the ocean, coming across the greatest body of water upon the globe; and yet here and there its characteristics remain the same with a strange consistency. (029-003)
