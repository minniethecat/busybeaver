# The Cook

The **ship's cook** is a constant presence in Conrad's maritime memoir — rarely the subject of sustained attention, but always there at the periphery of every crisis, every moment of shipboard drama. He presides over the galley, produces the meals that sustain the crew, and appears at the run when something extraordinary happens.

## A Soup Complaint

The cook first appears in Conrad's narrative through a complaint — a mate grumbling about the soup:

> "What makes this soup so bitter? I am surprised the mate can swallow the beastly stuff. I'm sure the cook's ladled some salt water into it by mistake." (006-006)

The charge is "outrageous," Conrad notes, and the steward can only drop his eyelids bashfully in response. The soup was, in fact, perfectly fine — Conrad had a second helping. The cook, unseen but accused, stands in for all the minor injustices of shipboard life: the endless small complaints that fill the space between the great dramas of the sea. It is typical of Conrad's humour that the cook is blamed for salt water in the soup — on a ship surrounded by the ocean, the accusation is both absurd and somehow plausible.

## The Derelict Rescue

When the crew of a waterlogged Danish brig is sighted, the cook abandons his galley to join the general excitement:

> The watch below ran up the forecastle head in a body, the cook dashed out of the galley. Everybody saw the poor fellows now. They were there! (022-004)

The cook "dashed out of the galley" — a phrase that captures both the urgency of the moment and the cook's habitual location. The galley is his domain; leaving it is an event. That he runs to see the castaways alongside the rest of the crew places him within the collective life of the ship, neither officer nor able seaman, but part of the human fabric that holds a vessel together.

## The Cook's Place

The cook occupies a peculiar position in the ship's hierarchy. He is not an officer, not a deckhand, not quite a servant — yet his work touches every man aboard three times a day. Conrad, who pays close attention to the social architecture of shipboard life, notes the cook only in passing, yet the passing mentions accumulate into a portrait of a figure without whom no ship could function. The galley, with its warm stove and perpetual smell of food, is one of the few domestic spaces on a working vessel — and the cook is its sovereign.
