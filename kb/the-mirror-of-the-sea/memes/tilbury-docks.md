## The Newest of the London Docks

The Tilbury Docks were the last and furthest downstream of the great Thames dock complexes — "the most recent of all London docks, the nearest to the sea." Conrad's description of their entrance is embedded in one of the most richly detailed passages of *The Faithful River*: the ship comes up the Lower Hope Reach, past the factory chimneys of Grays and Greenhithe, past the houses of Gravesend, and there, "just round the corner, as it were, on a pier defined with stone blocks and wooden piles, a white mast… watches over a set of heavy dock-gates." The Tilbury entrance is the threshold between the industrial river and the sea beyond.

> This is the entrance to Tilbury Dock, the most recent of all London docks, the nearest to the sea. (031-003)

## Magnificent and Desolate

Conrad's portrait of Tilbury is characteristically paradoxical. The docks were "very modern" and "very efficient and ready for their task," but they had come "too soon into the field" — built for a volume of trade that had not yet materialised. The result was an impression of "utter abandonment, of wasted efficiency": vast empty basins, miles of bare quays, and "two or three ships that seemed lost like bewitched children in a forest of gaunt, hydraulic cranes." Yet Conrad sees this not as failure but as latent grandeur. Tilbury, he insists, has "a great future."

> Nothing in those days could have been more striking than the vast, empty basins, surrounded by miles of bare quays and the ranges of cargo-sheds, where two or three ships seemed lost like bewitched children in a forest of gaunt, hydraulic cranes. (032-005)

## Historical Glamour

Even the newest dock, Conrad argues, "shares in the glamour conferred by historical associations." Tilbury's history predates its docks: Queen Elizabeth had made "an anxious business progress" to Tilbury Fort "at a crisis of national history" — the approach of the Spanish Armada in 1588. The menace of invasion had "passed away," but the association lingered, lending the modern dock a romantic hinterland it would not otherwise possess.

> Even the newest of the docks, the Tilbury Dock, shares in the glamour conferred by historical associations. Queen Elizabeth has made one of her progresses down there… (032-005)

## Ready for the Future

Conrad's final judgment on Tilbury is forward-looking. The docks "shall never fill a long-felt want" because they were built in advance of need. But their very prematurity is their strength: "free of the trammels of the tide, easy of access, magnificent and desolate, they are already there, prepared to take and keep the biggest ships that float upon the sea." They are, in Conrad's phrase, "worthy of the oldest river port in the world."

> A great future lies before Tilbury Docks. They shall never fill a long-felt want… They were too early in the field. The want shall never be felt because, free of the trammels of the tide, easy of access, magnificent and desolate, they are already there… (032-005)

## Among the Dock Family

Tilbury appears in Conrad's panoramic survey of the Thames docks as the last in a series — St. Katherine's, the London Docks, the West India Docks, the Victoria and Albert Docks, and finally Tilbury, "the vast gloom of the great basins in Tilbury." Each dock, he insists, has "its own peculiar physiognomy, its own expression," and Tilbury's expression is one of solitary, premature magnificence on the Essex marsh.
