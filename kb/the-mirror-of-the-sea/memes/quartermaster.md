The **quartermaster** is a rated seaman entrusted with the steering of a vessel and the upkeep of its navigational equipment. In merchant sailing ships of Conrad's era, the quartermaster occupied a position of trust between the officers and the ordinary seamen — a role requiring steadiness, judgment, and long experience at sea.

Conrad's quartermaster is a figure of mature, sometimes ironic wisdom. He has seen enough of ships and men to speak with quiet authority:

> "Ships are all right," as my middle-aged, respectable quartermaster said with much conviction and some irony; but they are not exactly what men make them. (035-012)

This remark captures a central tension in Conrad's maritime philosophy: the ship is a human creation, yet it transcends human intentions, becoming something more — and sometimes something less — than its builders and crews intend. The quartermaster, who stands at the helm through calm and storm alike, understands this better than most.

The path to becoming a quartermaster ran through the mate's recommendation and the captain's approval. Advancement was both a matter of demonstrated competence and of personal reputation:

> And, true enough, I learned presently that the mate of the *Hyperion* had "taken down" his name for quartermaster. "We sign on Friday, and join next day for the morning tide," he remarked, in a deliberate tone. (035-008)

The quartermaster's role placed him at the physical and symbolic centre of the ship's command — the wheel. From this position he felt every sea, every shift of wind, and every response of the vessel. His hands translated the captain's or officer's orders into the ship's heading, a responsibility that demanded both obedience and an intuitive feel for the vessel's behaviour.
