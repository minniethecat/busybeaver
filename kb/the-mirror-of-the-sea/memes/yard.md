A **yard** is a long spar, tapered at both ends, attached crosswise to a ship's mast. On square-rigged sailing vessels of the nineteenth century, yards carried the principal sails and were a defining element of the ship's silhouette. Joseph Conrad's *The Mirror of the Sea* is suffused with images of yards — they appear in descriptions of ships at rest, under way, and in the grip of storms.

## Structure and function

In a square-rigged ship, each mast carries several yards at different heights. The lowest is the course yard (carrying the mainsail, foresail, or mizzen), above it the topsail yard, then the topgallant yard, and at the summit the royal yard. Each yard can be braced — rotated around the mast — to trim the sail to the wind. The yard's length and the sail it carries determine the ship's driving power.

> The tall masts holding aloft the white canvas, spread out like a snare for catching the invisible power of the air, emerge gradually from the water, sail after sail, yard after yard, growing big, till, under the towering structure of her machinery, you perceive the insignificant, tiny speck of her hull. (010-003)

Conrad frequently notes the height of yards as a measure of a ship's scale. He recalls that "in my time the royal yards of an average profitable ship were a good way up above her decks" (010-005), a remark that underscores the sheer vertical reach of merchant sailing vessels.

## At rest and under way

The image of yards with furled sails is, for Conrad, an emblem of repose. A ship lying in a harbour with her sails tightly furled upon squared yards presents "to a seaman's eye the most perfect picture of slumbering repose" (006-003). The contrast with a ship under way is sharp: "Every passage of a ship of yesterday, whose yards were braced round eagerly the very moment the pilot, with his pockets full of letters, had got over the side, was like a race" (009-001).

## In heavy weather

Yards became points of acute danger in heavy seas. Conrad describes a gale in which "it was a wonder that the men sent aloft were not flung off the yards, the yards not flung off the masts, the masts not flung overboard" (015-009). The cascading image — men from yards, yards from masts, masts from hull — conveys the violence that square-rigged ships endured in high latitudes.

## In the London docks

The yards of docked ships form part of Conrad's vivid portrait of the London river. He describes the forest-like perspective of masts where "the heavy yards seemed to be entangled and suspended" in the web of rigging (035-002). The proximity of yardarms to warehouse windows along the narrow Thames docks created what he calls "bizarre associations of yardarms and windowpanes" (031-007).
