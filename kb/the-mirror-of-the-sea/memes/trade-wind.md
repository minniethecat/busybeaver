The **Trade Winds** are the prevailing easterly surface winds that blow from the subtropical high-pressure belts toward the equatorial low-pressure zone — from the northeast in the Northern Hemisphere and from the southeast in the Southern Hemisphere. For the sailing merchantmen of Conrad's era, the trade winds were the reliable engine of oceanic passage, a gift of meteorology that made global commerce predictable.

## Conrad's Monarchical Metaphor

Conrad personifies the trade winds as settled, constitutional rulers — the benign counterpart to the passionate and arbitrary West Wind of the high latitudes. In one of *The Mirror of the Sea*'s most extended metaphors, he contrasts the steady institutional power of the trades with the personal, despotic rule of their polar brother:

> In the middle belt of the earth the Trade Winds reign supreme, undisputed, like monarchs of long-settled kingdoms, whose traditional power, checking all undue ambitions, is not so much an exercise of personal might as the working of long-established institutions. The intertropical kingdoms of the Trade Winds are favourable to the ordinary life of a merchantman.
>
> (025-001)

The metaphor is precise: a constitutional monarchy versus an absolutist regime. The trade winds rule not by whim but by institution — their steadiness is the product of natural law, as dependable as a settled political order. Under their reign, "the weather may be trusted" (025-001).

## A Seaman's Blessing

The trade-wind belts offered the merchant seaman his easiest days. The passage through their dominions was marked by a palpable relaxation of the ship's collective tension:

> The trumpet-call of strife is seldom borne on their wings to the watchful ears of men on the decks of ships. The regions ruled by the northeast and southeast Trade Winds are serene. In a southern-going ship, bound out for a long voyage, the passage through their dominions is characterized by a relaxation of strain and vigilance on the part of the seamen. Those citizens of the ocean feel sheltered under the aegis of an uncontested law, of an undisputed dynasty.
>
> (025-001)

Conrad's vocabulary here — "citizens," "aegis," "uncontested law," "undisputed dynasty" — extends the political metaphor into a vision of the trade-wind belt as a well-governed commonwealth. The seaman, in this domain, is not a subject cowering before a tyrant but a citizen sheltered by law. The contrast with the West Wind's arbitrary violence (see [[west-wind]]) could hardly be sharper.

## Not Without Danger

Yet even this constitutional realm is not entirely benign. Conrad is careful to introduce a note of caution, lest the seaman grow too comfortable:

> Yet not too implicitly. Even in the constitutional realm of Trade Winds, north and south of the Equator, ships are overtaken by strange disturbances.
>
> (025-002)

These "strange disturbances" — tropical cyclones, equatorial calms, and the unpredictable violence that could visit even the steadiest latitudes — reminded the seaman that no kingdom, however orderly, was immune to upheaval. The trade winds' serenity was statistical, not absolute.

## The Easterly Character

The trade winds are part of the broader family of easterly winds, which Conrad characterises as having "stability," "regularity," and "persistence" (025-002). This easterly character — steady, reliable, predictable — stands as the structural opposite of the westerly temperament, which is passionate, changeable, and violent. The political geography of Conrad's wind cosmology is, at its deepest level, a study in temperament: the constitutional East versus the autocratic West.

> Still, the easterly winds, and, generally speaking, the easterly weather all the world over, is characterized by regularity and persistence.
>
> (025-002)

## The Great Struggle

In Conrad's wind-politics, the trade winds are not merely a local phenomenon but one flank of a global contest:

> In the polity of winds, as amongst the tribes of the earth, the real struggle lies between East and West.
>
> (025-004)

The trade winds represent the East in this cosmic struggle — orderly, institutional, constitutional — while the West Wind represents passion, violence, and arbitrary power. Every voyage that passed from the trade-wind belts into the westerlies was a passage from one political order to another, from law to whim, from serenity to strife.

## Contrast with the Westerlies

The trade winds serve as a structural foil in Conrad's weather cosmology. Where the West Wind (see [[west-wind]]) is passionate, changeable, and dangerous — a "royal" autocrat of high latitudes — the trade winds are steady, dependable, and transparent in their moods. The passage from one wind regime to the other — crossing from the trades into the variables of the horse latitudes or the fury of the westerlies — marked each stage of a voyage and each shift in the seaman's relationship with the elements.
