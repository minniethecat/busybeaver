## Definition

Westerly weather is the meteorological regime of the high southern latitudes, characterised by persistent westerly winds that circle the globe almost without interruption by land. In the age of sail, every ship making the passage to Australia, New Zealand, or the Far East via the Cape of Good Hope or Cape Horn had to endure the westerlies of the Southern Ocean — weeks or months of running before powerful winds and heavy seas. Conrad treats these winds not as mere weather but as a geographical feature, a region of the earth's surface as real and as demanding as any continent.

## In Conrad's Words

> Down south, running before the gales of high latitudes, she made our life a burden to us. There were days when nothing would keep even on the swing-tables, when there was no position where you could fix yourself so as not to feel a constant strain upon all the muscles of your body. (015-009)

> The West Wind reigns over the seas surrounding the coasts of these kingdoms; and from the gateways of the channels, from promontories as if from watchtowers, from estuaries of rivers as if from postern gates, from passageways, inlets, straits, firths, the ships of the kingdom go out to their toil under the tremendous escort of that tireless monarch. (026-001)

> It was on a grey afternoon in the lull of a three days' gale that had left the Southern Ocean tumbling heavily upon our ship, under a sky hung with rags of clouds that seemed to have been cut and hacked by the keen edge of a sou'-west gale. (017-003)

## The Southern Ocean Westerlies

The westerlies of the Southern Ocean are the most sustained wind system on earth. Uninterrupted by large land masses, they blow with remarkable consistency between roughly 40 and 60 degrees south latitude — the latitudes sailors called the "Roaring Forties," "Furious Fifties," and "Screaming Sixties." For the sailing-ship sailor, these were the latitudes of the fastest passages and the hardest work: running before a following gale for weeks on end, the ship rolling heavily, the decks perpetually awash, the men exhausted by the constant strain.

## The Westerly as Sovereign

Conrad extends his political metaphor of the winds to the westerlies most elaborately. The West Wind is a "tireless monarch" with a "frank and generous nature" — but one that can be corrupted by "long anger" into devastating fury. In his southwesterly mood, the West Wind is "an enemy of sleep, and even of a recumbent position." This personification is not merely literary ornament: it captures the seaman's lived experience of a force that is both constant companion and unpredictable adversary, whose character must be understood and whose moods must be anticipated.
