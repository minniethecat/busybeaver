## The Cape of Storms

Conrad knew the Cape of Good Hope as every deep-water sailor did: as both a landmark and an ordeal. He invokes its Portuguese name — *Cabo Tormentoso*, the Cape of Storms — as a reminder that the cape's "good hope" is a later, more optimistic christening. For the seaman, the older name held truer: the Cape was where the Southern Ocean's gales reached their fullest expression, where the West Wind — Conrad's "great autocrat" — ruled without restraint.

> It was somewhere near the Cape — The Cape being, of course, the Cape of Good Hope, the Cape of Storms of its Portuguese discoverers. (023-001)

## The Cape and the Gale

Conrad's most concentrated meditation on the Cape of Good Hope comes in the context of his anatomy of the gale. The Cape was where he experienced weather of a violence that seared itself into memory — weather that, when he later opened an atlas, seemed "as if engraved upon the paper." The geographical mark on the map and the emotional mark on the seaman's consciousness become one: the Cape is not merely a place but an experience.

> And now, when I open an atlas and look at the tiny dots on the map of the Southern Ocean, I see as if engraved upon the paper the enraged physiognomy of that gale. (023-007)

## Robbed of Its Name

Conrad observes that the Cape of Good Hope is "always deprived of its title" — just as another cape (Cape Horn, implicitly) is "robbed of its name." For the sailor, "The Cape" needs no qualification; it is the cape, the definitive one. This linguistic shorthand, common among seamen, reflects the Cape's central place in the geography of sail — it was the turning-point between the Atlantic and the Indian Ocean, the gate to the East, and the place where a ship proved itself.

> It was off that other cape which is always deprived of its title as the Cape of Good Hope is robbed of its name. (024-002)

## The West Wind's Citadel

For Conrad, the Cape of Good Hope lies at the heart of the West Wind's southern kingdom. The "forties" — the latitudes between 40° and 50° south, where the westerlies blow unchecked around the globe — pass directly across the Cape's meridians. A ship rounding the Cape was exposed to the full force of this circumpolar weather system, with no landmass to break the wind's fetch. Conrad's reverent description of the West Wind as "a great king, fit to rule over the great waters" has its proving ground at the Cape.

## A Turning-Point of Empire

The Cape of Good Hope was more than a navigational challenge; it was the hinge of the British Empire's eastern route. Every ship bound for India, the East Indies, or Australia passed this way before the Suez Canal opened in 1869. Conrad, sailing in the twilight of the sailing-ship era, was among the last generation of seamen for whom the Cape was an unavoidable and defining passage — a place that tested ships and made reputations.
