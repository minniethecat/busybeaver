## Identity

Mr. Hudig was a Dutch merchant and shipowner for whom Conrad, as chief mate, undertook a winter expedition to collect a cargo frozen upcountry. He appears only in passing in *The Mirror of the Sea*, but his brief portrait — a man of impressive manner and "childishly imperfect" English — serves as a representative of the commercial shore world that the seaman encounters on the far side of his voyages. Conrad's treatment of Hudig is cooler than his portraits of fellow seamen: the shipowner belongs to the land, and Conrad's gaze upon him is that of a seaman observing a necessary but alien species.

## Conrad's Account

> That part of the expedition was easy enough, though the horses were painfully glistening with icicles, and the aspect of the tram-conductors' faces presented a repulsive blending of crimson and purple. (014-007)

> Of course, he meant the chief mate to keep quiet — to lay up, as a matter of fact. His manner was impressive enough, if his English was childishly imperfect when compared with the fluency of Mr. Hudig, the ship's owner. But as to the remainder of his discourse, it was full of excellent if somewhat disconnected maxims. (015-012)

## The Shipowner's World

Conrad's portrait of Mr. Hudig is one of the few glimpses *The Mirror of the Sea* offers of the commercial framework within which seafaring takes place. The shipowner exists on the boundary between the sea and the counting-house — his English may be imperfect, but his money floats the ship. Conrad neither admires nor condemns Hudig; he simply notes him as one notes a landmark: a fixed point in the geography of maritime commerce. The frozen canal that delayed Conrad's cargo expedition — "that canal set fast" — becomes a metaphor for the friction between the seaman's need for motion and the merchant's entanglement in the frozen solidity of the land.
