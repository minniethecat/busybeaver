## Definition

The Departure is the last professional sight of land — the technical, as distinguished from the sentimental, "good-bye" to the coast. Joseph Conrad distinguishes it sharply from the mere act of a ship leaving port. It is, he insists, "distinctly a ceremony of navigation": a formal, deliberate observation that closes one phase of the voyage and opens another. Unlike the Landfall — which is instantaneous, made and done at the first cry of "Land ho!" — the Departure is an extended ritual, a navigator's final reckoning with the known world before committing to the open sea.

## In Conrad's Words

> Landfall and Departure mark the rhythmical swing of a seaman's life and of a ship's career. From land to land is the most concise definition of a ship's earthly fate. (001-001)

> A "Departure" is not what a vain people of landsmen may think. The term "Landfall" is more easily understood; you fall in with the land, and it is a matter of a quick eye and of a clear atmosphere. The Departure is not the ship's going away from her port any more than the Landfall can be looked upon as the synonym of arrival. But there is this difference in the Departure: that the term does not imply so much a sea event as a definite act entailing a process — the precise observation of the last landmark by the navigating officer, the careful noting of the position, the comparison with the chart. (001-002)

> The Departure is distinctly a ceremony of navigation. A ship may have left her port some time before; she may have been at sea, in fact, for a day or more, and yet not have taken her Departure. The taking of Departure is the last official act of the navigating officer, and it is performed with a certain solemnity. (001-003)

> The taking of Departure, if not the last sight of the land, is, perhaps, the last professional recognition of the land on the part of a sailor. It is the technical, as distinguished from the sentimental, "good-bye." Henceforth he has done with the coast astern of his ship. It is a matter personal to the man. It is not the ship that takes her departure; the seaman takes his departure from the land. (001-004)

> A Departure, the last professional sight of land, is always good, or at least good enough. For, even if the weather be thick, it does not matter much to a ship having all the open sea before her bows. (001-005)

> Some commanders of ships take their Departure from the home coast sadly, in a spirit of grief and discontent. They have a wife, children perhaps, some affection at any rate, or perhaps only some pet vice, that must be left behind for a year or more. I remember only one man who walked his deck with a springy step, and gave the first course of the passage in an elated voice. (002-001)

> It is hard to say; for in that voyage from which no man returns Landfall and Departure are instantaneous, merging together into one moment of supreme and final attention. (003-009)

## Technical Usage

In the age of sail, taking a Departure was a formal navigational procedure. After the ship had cleared port and was in open water, the navigating officer would take bearings on the last visible landmarks — a prominent headland, a lighthouse, a distinctive mountain — and record the ship's position on the chart. This was the definitive starting point from which all subsequent dead reckoning would be calculated.

Conrad emphasises the ceremonial character of this act. It marked the psychological transition from coastal waters to the open ocean. The pilot had been dropped; the land was astern; the ship and her crew were now committed to the passage. The Departure was always "good, or at least good enough" because, Conrad observes, the ship had all the open sea before her bows — unlike the Landfall, which could be disastrous if the navigation was wrong.

## The Metaphysical Departure

Conrad extends the concept beyond navigation. In his memoir of Captain B—— (chapter 3), a dying master mariner gazes before him with "the Landfall's vigilant look," and Conrad cannot decide whether this last confrontation with death is a Landfall or a Departure. In death, he concludes, "Landfall and Departure are instantaneous, merging together into one moment of supreme and final attention." The Departure thus becomes a metaphor for the ultimate human passage.
