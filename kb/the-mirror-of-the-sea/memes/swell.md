A **swell** is a long-wavelength surface wave on the sea, generated by distant weather systems and travelling far beyond the wind that raised it. Unlike locally wind-driven waves, swell is regular, smooth, and can propagate across entire ocean basins. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad returns repeatedly to the image of the swell — sometimes as a thing of beauty, sometimes as a stealthy bearer of danger.

## Character and appearance

Conrad's swell is a creature of smoothness and cadence. Watching from a ship's boat after a rescue, he describes how "the run of the slight swell was so smooth that it resembled the graceful undulation of a piece of shimmering grey silk shot with gleams of green" (036-020). The regularity of the motion — "the uninterrupted cadence of motion" (036-023) — is what distinguishes swell from the chaotic chop of a wind sea.

> And the run of the slight swell was so smooth that it resembled the graceful undulation of a piece of shimmering grey silk shot with gleams of green. (036-020)

## The hidden threat

Yet the swell could carry menace. In the famous episode of the rescue of a sinking brig's crew, Conrad captures how the swell masks the approach of something far more dangerous: "At the finish there was not an oar's length between the first and second boat, with Death coming in a good third on the top of the very next smooth swell, for all one knew to the contrary" (036-011). The very regularity of the swell conceals the squall that nearly overwhelms them.

> At the finish there was not an oar's length between the first and second boat, with Death coming in a good third on the top of the very next smooth swell, for all one knew to the contrary. (036-011)

## Swell and sailing tactics

For the seaman, reading the swell was a practical skill. A westerly swell off the Cape of Good Hope carried tactical significance even for a fleet commander: Conrad notes with professional awe the predicament of ships "broadside on to the westerly swell" (048-004) and the narrow margins that swell-imposed leeway could create in naval engagements. The swell from the west was a persistent feature of the southern ocean routes — a long-period reminder of distant gales in higher latitudes.
