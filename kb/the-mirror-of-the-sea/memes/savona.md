# Savona

**Savona** is an Italian port city on the Ligurian coast of the Mediterranean, west of Genoa. In Conrad's maritime world, Savona appears not as a destination nor as a trading port but as a place of origin — the river where the *[[tremolino|Tremolino]]*, the little tartane that Conrad called his "cradle," was built by a famous builder of boats.

## Conrad's Description

The single mention of Savona in *The Mirror of the Sea* carries great weight, for it is the birthplace of the vessel around which Conrad's most personal chapters revolve. The passage is as much a portrait of the *Tremolino* herself as it is a record of her origins:

> She who was my cradle in those years had been built on the River of Savona by a famous builder of boats, was rigged in Corsica by another good man, and was described on her papers as a "tartane" of sixty tons. In reality, she was a true balancelle, with two short masts raking forward and two curved yards, each as long as her hull; a true child of the Latin lake, with a spread of two enormous sails resembling the pointed wings on a seabird's slender body, and herself, like a bird indeed, skimming rather than sailing the seas. (040-003)

## Maritime Significance

### A Centre of Traditional Boatbuilding

Savona's significance in Conrad's narrative is as a centre of traditional Mediterranean boatbuilding. The "famous builder" on the River of Savona represents a lineage of craftsmanship that produced the small, seaworthy vessels — [[tartane|tartanes]], [[balancelle|balancelles]], feluccas — that plied the "Latin lake" for centuries. The *Tremolino*, built on the River of Savona and rigged in [[corsica|Corsica]], was a product of this maritime tradition: a vessel of ancient design, hand-built by craftsmen working in methods passed down through generations.

### The Latin Lake

Conrad's phrase "a true child of the Latin lake" encapsulates the Mediterranean maritime world that Savona belonged to — a world of short-sea trading, coastal passages, and small, agile vessels whose design had changed little since the Roman era. The Mediterranean was not an ocean but a "lake" encircled by Latin shores, and its vessels — tartanes, balancelles — were distinct from the square-rigged ships of the northern seas. They were built for quick manoeuvre, shallow draught, and the sudden squalls of enclosed waters.

### The Craft of Tradition

The "famous builder of boats" on the River of Savona, though unnamed by Conrad, represents the anonymous masters of traditional shipbuilding whose skills were passed orally and manually — never written down, never codified, but expressed in the hulls of the vessels they launched. That Savona produced a vessel capable of carrying Conrad safely through his Mediterranean adventures — including a [[gale-off-cape-horn|gale]] and the dramatic flight from a coastguard [[xm|vessel]] — is testimony to the quality of its boatbuilding tradition.
