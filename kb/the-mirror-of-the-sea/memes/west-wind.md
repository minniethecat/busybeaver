# West Wind

The **West Wind** is the prevailing wind of the North Atlantic, blowing from the western quarter and governing the weather of the great ocean passages between Europe and America. In Conrad's prose, it is not a meteorological phenomenon but a living sovereign — the greatest of the winds, whose moods range from tyrannical fury to melancholy splendour, and whose kingdom extends across the entire breadth of the northern ocean.

## The King and His Dominion

Conrad's portrait of the West Wind opens with a declaration of sovereignty that brooks no qualification:

> The West Wind is the greatest king. The East rules between the Tropics. They have shared each ocean between them. Each has his genius of supreme rule. (028-001)

The West Wind is "a barbarian, of a northern type" — a figure of almost mythological stature. Conrad imagines him "seated masterfully with a double-edged sword on his knees upon the painted and gilt clouds of the sunset, bowing his shock head of golden locks, a flaming beard over his breast, imposing, colossal, mighty-limbed, with a thundering voice, distended cheeks and fierce blue eyes, urging the speed of his gales" (028-001). This is not description but invocation — a prose portrait that draws upon the language of Norse mythology and the visual conventions of Renaissance painting to render the wind as a visible, almost tangible presence.

By contrast, the East Wind — the King of Easterly Weather, who rules between the Tropics — is "a spare Southerner with clear-cut features, black-browed and dark-eyed, grey-robed, upright in sunshine, resting a smooth-shaven cheek in the palm of his hand, impenetrable, secret, full of wiles, fine-drawn, keen — meditating aggressions" (028-001). The contrast between the two kings — northern bluntness against southern subtlety, open violence against veiled cunning — is one of the book's most sustained and vivid imaginative constructions.

## The Southwesterly Mood

The West Wind's southwesterly phase is the most dangerous of his moods — a prolonged assault that wears men down through the accumulated attrition of sleeplessness and relentless violence:

> The southwesterly mood of the West Wind is an enemy of sleep, and even of a recumbent position, in the responsible officers of a ship. After two hours of futile, lightheaded, inconsequent thinking upon all things under heaven in that dark, dank, wet and devastated cabin, I arose suddenly and staggered up on deck. (027-004)

The detail is characteristic of Conrad's method: the physical discomfort of the officer unable even to lie down is made to stand for the larger oppression of the gale. The wind is "still oppressing his kingdom and its outlying dependencies, even as far as the Bay of Biscay, in the dismal secrecy of thick, very thick, weather" (027-004). The southwesterly mood is a siege — not a single assault but a sustained campaign of attrition.

## The Northwest Phase: Shifting Moods

The passage from the southwesterly to the northwesterly phase is a dramatic transformation — one of the most vivid set-pieces in *The Mirror of the Sea*:

> Heralded by the increasing fierceness of the squalls, sometimes by a faint flash of lightning like the signal of a lighted torch waved far away behind the clouds, the shift of wind comes at last, the crucial moment of the change from the brooding and veiled violence of the southwest gale to the sparkling, flashing, cutting, clear-eyed anger of the King's northwesterly mood. (027-001)

The shift is not a moderation but a change of character. The northwest phase brings "a fury bejewelled with stars, mayhap bearing the crescent of the moon on its brow, shaking the last vestiges of its torn cloud-mantle in inky-black squalls, with hail and sleet descending like showers of crystals and pearls, bounding off the spars, drumming on the sails, pattering on the oilskin coats, whitening the decks of homeward-bound ships" (027-001). The language is paradoxically beautiful — "bejewelled with stars," "showers of crystals and pearls" — even as it describes conditions of the utmost physical misery.

> Each mood of the great autocrat has its own greatness, and each is hard to bear. Only the northwest phase of that mighty display is not demoralizing to the same extent, because between the hail and sleet squalls of a northwesterly gale one can see a long way ahead. (027-001)

The gift of visibility, even in the teeth of the gale, is what distinguishes the northwest phase — a seaman can see the next squall coming, and that knowledge, however grim, is a form of psychological relief.

## Arbitrary Decree

The West Wind governs absolutely within his dominions. Men and their contrivances alike belong to him, and his decrees are irreversible:

> That long enduring piece of canvas was confiscated by the arbitrary decree of the West Wind, to whom belong the lives of men and the contrivances of their hands within the limits of his kingdom. With the sound of a faint explosion it vanished into the thick weather bodily, leaving behind of its stout substance not so much as one solitary strip big enough to be picked into a handful of lint for, say, a wounded elephant. (027-013)

The foresail — "that long enduring piece of canvas" — is simply erased from existence. The wind's "arbitrary decree" is a legal metaphor that underscores the king's absolute authority: there is no appeal, no negotiation. The comic hyperbole of "a wounded elephant" serves to emphasize the totality of the destruction — not even a scrap remains.

## The Clearing Mood and Its Surprises

The West Wind's clearing mood is, paradoxically, as treacherous as his rage. After days of hiding the celestial bodies behind cloud, he may suddenly reveal them with an effect that can lead even the most experienced mariner astray:

> But, in truth, this was one of the greatest surprises ever sprung by the clearing up mood of the West Wind upon one of the most accomplished of his courtiers. (027-016)

The "most accomplished of his courtiers" — Conrad's commander, the master of the ship — had been completely deceived by a brief clearing into believing he had made a perfect landfall. The West Wind, in his clearing mood, is a trickster: he grants a glimpse of the world only to mislead.

## Faith with the Easterly King

Despite his violence, the West Wind is no usurper. He keeps faith with his brother, the King of Easterly Weather, observing an ancient division of the sea:

> The West Wind keeps faith with his brother, the King of the Easterly Weather. "What we have divided we have divided," he seems to say in his gruff voice, this ruler without guile. (028-002)

The pact between the two kings is the foundation of the world's weather. The West Wind accepts that the Tropics belong to his brother, and he never intrudes upon that dominion. His own kingdom is the northern ocean, and within it he is absolute — but he is an honest barbarian, "violent without craftiness, and furious without malice" (028-001).

The passage concludes with the West Wind's own voice — a rare moment of direct speech attributed to the elements — in which he claims the right to his "kingly sport": flinging gales like quoits across the Atlantic, "from one end of my dismal kingdom to the other: over the Great Banks or along the edges of pack-ice — this one with true aim right into the bight of the Bay of Biscay, that other upon the fjords of Norway, across the North Sea where the fishermen of many nations look watchfully into my angry eye" (028-002).

## The Seaman's Relationship

To the working seaman, the West Wind is not a distant abstraction but an intimate adversary — a presence felt in every hour on deck, in every watch, in every effort to eat or sleep or simply stand upright. Conrad's personification is not a literary conceit but an accurate transcription of the mariner's experience: when you have spent weeks in the grip of a Westerly gale, the wind does indeed seem to possess a personality, a will, and a temper. The seaman who has weathered the West Wind's southwesterly mood knows him as personally as he knows his own shipmates — and with considerably less affection.
