## Location and Identity

The Owers is a shoal or bank lying off the Sussex coast in the English Channel, south of Selsey Bill. A light-vessel — a manned lightship anchored permanently in position — has marked this hazard to navigation since the late eighteenth century. For the seaman approaching or leaving the Channel, the Owers light-vessel was a familiar and essential waypoint, one of the fixed points in a world of moving water and shifting weather.

Lightships like the Owers occupied a special place in maritime life. Unlike a lighthouse built on rock or headland, the lightship rode at anchor in the open sea, its crew enduring every gale and groundswell that passed. It was the most exposed form of navigation aid, and the men who served aboard lightships were a breed apart — patient, steadfast, and in Conrad's imagination, witnesses to every mood the sea could muster.

## In Conrad's Words

Conrad invokes the Owers in a passage of extraordinary geographic sweep, casting the waters around Britain as a fortress garrisoned by promontories, estuaries, and headlands — all standing watch against the elemental powers:

> The West Wind reigns over the seas surrounding the coasts of these kingdoms; and from the gateways of the channels, from promontories as if from watchtowers, from estuaries of rivers as if from postern gates, from passageways, inlets, straits, firths, the garrison of the Isle and the crews of the ships that go to and fro keep their watch on the western horizon from the Scilly Isles to the Owers, from the Owers to the Lizard, and from the Lizard to the Land's End.
>
> (026-001)

This is one of the great geographical sentences in *The Mirror of the Sea*. The Owers is placed in a chain of watch-points — Scilly, Owers, Lizard, Land's End — each a station in the silent, continuous observation that maritime Britain keeps upon the western sea. The light-vessel, anonymous and unglamorous, is enrolled in a kind of national sentry duty.

In a later passage describing the West Wind's gale-driven fury, Conrad returns to imagery that evokes the Owers' exposed position:

> You behold another phase of his passion, a fury bejewelled with stars, mayhap bearing the crescent of the moon on its brow, shaking the last vestiges of its torn cloud-mantle in inky-black squalls, with hail and sleet descending like showers of crystals and pearls, bounding off the spars, drumming on the sails, pattering on the oilskin coats, whitening the decks of homeward-bound ships.
>
> (027-001)

Though the Owers is not named directly here, the ships that are "homeward-bound" — making for the Channel after a long passage — would steer by the Owers light as one of their first marks of home. The squalls, hail, and sleet that Conrad describes are precisely what the crew of the Owers light-vessel endured, year after year, as their station.

## Maritime Significance

For the deep-water sailor approaching the English Channel from the Western Ocean, the Owers light-vessel was a landmark of passage. After weeks or months at sea, the sight of the Owers light — or the sound of its fog signal in thick weather — was the first certain sign that home waters had been reached. It stood at the threshold between the great oceans and the narrow seas, between the long passage and the landfall.
