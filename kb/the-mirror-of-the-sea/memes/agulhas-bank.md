The Agulhas Bank is a broad, shallow area of sea lying off the southern tip of Africa, where the warm Agulhas Current sweeps down the east coast and meets the cold waters of the Southern Ocean. It is one of the great waypoints of the deep-water sailing routes — the turning-point where a ship homeward bound from the East Indies rounds the Cape of Good Hope and enters the Atlantic.

## Conrad's Description

Conrad invokes the Agulhas in his great chapter on the West Wind, where he catalogues the places on the globe where the might of that wind can be met — and sometimes defied — on equal terms:

> His mightiness was sometimes very overwhelming; but even when you had to defy him openly, as on the banks of the Agulhas homeward bound from the East Indies, or on the outward passage round the Horn, he struck at you fairly his stinging blows (full in the face, too), and it was your business not to get too much staggered.
>
> (029-005)

The passage is characteristic of Conrad's personification of the West Wind. The wind is "His mightiness" — a sovereign whose authority can be "overwhelming" but who can, on occasion, be "defied openly." The "banks of the Agulhas" is named alongside Cape Horn as one of the two places on earth where this defiance is possible — and necessary. A ship homeward bound from the East Indies cannot avoid the Agulhas; she must cross it, and in crossing it she must take whatever the West Wind chooses to deliver.

Conrad's phrasing is precise: "he struck at you fairly his stinging blows (full in the face, too)." The parenthesis is a masterstroke — the wind does not strike from behind or from the quarter, where a ship can run before it. It strikes "full in the face," directly on the bow, where the ship must meet it head-on or be driven backward. The Agulhas, in Conrad's telling, is not a place of shelter but a place of confrontation — an arena where ship and wind meet on equal terms, and the only acceptable outcome is "not to get too much staggered."

## Maritime Significance

The Agulhas Bank was a critical waypoint on the great sailing route from the East Indies to Europe. After crossing the Indian Ocean, a homeward-bound ship would encounter the Agulhas Current — one of the swiftest ocean currents in the world — and, in the wrong conditions, the full force of a Southern Ocean gale blowing against it. The combination of current and contrary wind could produce the steep, breaking seas that earned the Agulhas its formidable reputation among deep-water seamen. For Conrad, the Agulhas was one of those places on the globe — like Cape Horn, like the Western Approaches — where the sea reminded a man what he was up against.
