The **snowstorm** is, in Conrad's catalogue of maritime hazards, one of the principal enemies of safe navigation. It belongs to that class of weather phenomena — alongside fog and driving rain — that rob the seaman of his most essential faculty: the ability to see.

## In Conrad's Words

Conrad names the snowstorm early in *The Mirror of the Sea*, in the opening chapter on Landfalls and Departures, as one of the conditions that can turn a routine landfall into a catastrophe:

> Fogs, snowstorms, gales thick with clouds and rain — those are the enemies of good Landfalls.
>
> (001-005)

The snowstorm is singled out not for its violence — a gale can be more dangerous in that respect — but for its capacity to blind. A landfall made on a wrong bearing, in thick weather, is the stuff of a captain's nightmares; a landfall attempted in a snowstorm is a landfall abandoned altogether, for there is no bearing to be had when the horizon has been swallowed by white.

The most haunting appearance of a snowstorm in Conrad's text comes in the chapter on overdue and missing vessels, where he recounts the story of a steamer given up for lost:

> And she would have been posted really as "overdue," or maybe as "missing," had she not been sighted in a snowstorm, vaguely, like a strange rolling island, by a whaler going north from her Polar cruising ground.
>
> (019-002)

The image is ghostly and precise. The broken-down steamer, adrift and powerless, is not "seen" in the ordinary sense — she is "sighted" only "vaguely," an apparition that materialises out of the white swirl of the storm. The whaler's crew, staring into a world of driven snowflakes, catch a glimpse of something that does not belong there — "a strange rolling island" — and by that improbable encounter a ship and her company are saved from being posted as missing.

Conrad returns to the image, deepening its spectral quality:

> She must have looked ghostly enough, that broken-down steamer, rolling in that snowstorm — a dark apparition in a world of white snowflakes to the staring eyes of that whaler's crew.
>
> (019-004)

The snowstorm here is not merely weather; it is a medium of transformation. It turns a steel steamer into a ghost, a dark shape in a white void, seen only because the whaler happened to cross her path. Conrad's choice of words — "apparition," "ghostly," "staring eyes" — elevates the passage from reportage to something approaching the supernatural. The snowstorm, in his telling, is the condition under which the ordinary physics of seeing break down, and a ship becomes a vision.

## Significance

The snowstorm occupies a particular niche in Conrad's weather taxonomy. It is not the most violent of the sea's moods — a gale or a hurricane can tear a ship apart — but it is the most disorienting. In a snowstorm, the mariner loses his cardinal reference: the line between sea and sky, the distinction between water and air, the ability to distinguish a ship from a wave. It is the weather of erasure, and in Conrad's hands it becomes the weather of threshold experiences — the moments when a ship hovers between being found and being lost.
