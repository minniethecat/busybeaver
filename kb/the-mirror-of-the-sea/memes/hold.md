# Hold

The **hold** is the interior compartment of a ship below the main deck, used for the stowage of cargo, stores, and ballast. Throughout *The Mirror of the Sea*, the hold appears not only as a physical space — the belly of the vessel — but as the locus of a ship's working life: the place where cargo is landed, where stowaways hide, and where a seaman's labour meets the material reality of trade.

## Cargo and the Ship's Belly

The hold is where the commercial purpose of a ship is made manifest. Conrad's ships carry copper-ore, coal, and sundry goods, loaded deep into the hold. The work of loading and unloading is part of the rhythm of port life:

> The night humours of the town descended from the street to the waterside in the still watches of the night: larrikins rushing down in bands to settle some quarrel by a stand-up fight, away from the police, in an indistinct ring half hidden by piles of cargo... (024-004)

Here the cargo — just landed from the hold — becomes the backdrop for the nocturnal dramas of the dockside. The hold, invisible beneath the hatch, connects the ship to the commerce and the rough life of the port.

## The Main-Hatch

The main-hatch, covering the hold, is a threshold between the world above and the world below. It is where Conrad sits during his night-watch, smoking and observing the quayside life:

> I sat smoking on the main-hatch. On one occasion I had an hour or so of a most intellectual conversation with a person whom I could not see distinctly, a gentleman from England, he said, with a cultivated voice, I on deck and he on the quay sitting on the case of a piano (landed out of our hold that very afternoon)... (024-005)

The detail is precise: a piano case, landed from the hold earlier that day, now serves as a seat for a chance encounter. The hold is the unseen origin of this vignette — the source from which cargo emerges to become part of the human world.

## The Hold and Stranding

In the chapter on stranding, Conrad draws an analogy between the ship taking the ground and the ground taking hold of the ship:

> It is as if an invisible hand had been stealthily uplifted from the bottom to catch hold of her keel as it glides through the water. (008-003)

> "Taking the ground" is the professional expression for a ship that is stranded in gentle circumstances. But the feeling is more as if the ground had taken hold of her. (008-005)

The verb "hold" here shifts from the literal (cargo hold) to the figurative — the sea-bottom grasping the ship's keel. Conrad extends this metaphor to the mind of the captain, who must "keep firm hold of your responsibility during the hours of sleep" (008-008).

## The Held-Up Fleet

In the chapter on the Easterly Weather, Conrad describes a fleet of merchant ships held stationary at the entrance to the English Channel by the obstinate East Wind:

> I have seen him, like a wizened robber sheik of the sea, hold up large caravans of ships to the number of three hundred or more at the very gates of the English Channel. (027-002)

For six weeks, the ships are "held up" — unable to move, short-rationed, humiliated. The hold here is not physical but meteorological: a mass detention imposed by wind, a collective captivity that reverses the ship's natural purpose of movement.

## A Seaman's Creed

Conrad concludes one of his reflections on ships with a simple declaration of faith, using "hold" in its most fundamental sense — to grasp, to believe, to keep fast:

> "Ships are all right." They are. They who live with the sea have got to hold by that creed first and last. (031-005)

This is the seaman's irreducible conviction: that ships are worthy vessels of trust, and that this belief must be held fast against all evidence of disaster and loss. The word "hold" here carries the weight of the anchor's bite, the cargo's security, and the sailor's unshakeable faith.
