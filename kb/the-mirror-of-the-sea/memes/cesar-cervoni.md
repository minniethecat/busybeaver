## Identity

Cesar Cervoni was the nephew of Dominic Cervoni and a seaman aboard the tartane *Tremolino*. In Conrad's account, he stands as the dark negative of his uncle's virtues — where Dominic was disciplined, silent, and loyal, Cesar was lazy, venomous, and treacherous. Conrad treats him not as a villain but as a creature of almost biological repulsiveness, a "spawn of a starved devil" inexplicably attached to a family of "good sturdy flesh and blood."

## Conrad's Account

> The only thing unsatisfactory (and even inexplicable) about our Dominic was his nephew, Cesar. It was startling to see a desolate expression of shame on the face of that man whose character contained no element of falsehood. (043-001)

> To look at Cesar was not pleasant. His parchment skin, showing dead white on his cranium through the thin wisps of dirty brown hair, seemed to be glued directly and tightly upon his big bones. Without being in any way deformed, he was the nearest approach which I have ever seen or could imagine to what is commonly understood by the word "monster." (043-007)

> It was for this venomous performance rather than for his lies, impudence, and laziness that his uncle used to knock him down. It must not be imagined that it was anything in the nature of a brutal assault. (043-008)

> "Canallia! Let him hang there," was his answer. And he went on talking over the business in hand calmly, while I tried vainly to dismiss from my mind the picture of Cesar steeped to the chin in the still water, with his hands stretched out. (043-013)

> He faced us, drawing back his lips and gnashing his teeth, and did not shrink an inch before the murderous glances of his uncle. (043-032)

## Character and Anecdotes

Cesar's defining moment came just before the *Tremolino*'s last voyage. He disappeared ashore for hours, returning coatless — having sold it for pocket money. When Dominic knocked him overboard into the old harbour in fury, Cesar clung to a mooring rope with his gnarled, spider-like hands and would not answer a single question about where he had been. Conrad, watching from the deck, recognised in the scene something beyond ordinary discipline: a family tragedy enacted in gestures of violence and silence.

Cesar was also suspected — though never proven — of betraying the syndicate to the authorities. Dominic believed the boy wanted to be a locksmith "to learn how to pick locks," and Conrad records this with the dark humour of a man who had seen the worst of human nature at close quarters and survived.
