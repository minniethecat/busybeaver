## Definition

The helm is the steering mechanism of a ship — in Conrad's day, typically a wheel connected by chains and rods to the rudder. But in Conrad's treatment, the helm is more than machinery: it is the point at which human will meets the elemental forces of wind and sea. The helmsman's hands on the wheel mediate between the captain's command and the ship's response, translating intention into motion. "There is a feeling of power in the first touch of the spokes," Conrad writes, a tactile intimacy with the vessel that no other station affords.

## The Helmsman's Art

Steering a sailing-ship is not the mechanical task it may appear. The helmsman must feel the ship's response through the wheel, anticipating her tendency to gripe (turn into the wind) or run off (turn away from it). He must compensate for the varying pressure of wind on sails, the set of currents, and the motion of the sea — all while keeping a steady course by compass. A good helmsman steers not by rigid adherence to the compass card but by sensing the ship's motion and applying gentle, continuous correction. Conrad describes this as a form of dialogue between man and craft.

## The Helm as Command

On a merchant ship, the helm is both a station of labour and a symbol of authority. The captain or officer of the watch gives the course; the helmsman executes it. But in heavy weather, the distinction blurs. The officer may take the wheel himself, and in that moment the helm becomes the direct instrument of command — the place where decisions are made and immediately enacted, where the survival of ship and crew may depend on a single turn of the spokes.
