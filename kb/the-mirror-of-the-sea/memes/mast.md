## Definition and Function

A **mast** is a tall vertical spar on a sailing vessel that supports the sails, yards, and rigging. In the age of sail, the mast was the structural backbone of the ship's propulsion system — without masts, a sailing ship was helpless, reduced to drift. A full-rigged ship typically carried three masts: the foremast (forward), the mainmast (centre, the tallest), and the mizzenmast (aft). Each mast was composed of overlapping sections — the lower mast, the topmast, and the topgallant mast — stepped one above the other and secured by shrouds and stays.

## The Mast as Pillar of Power

Conrad's prose returns repeatedly to the mast as a symbol of the ship's vitality and vulnerability. In his vision, masts are not mere timber but the "pillars" that capture the invisible power of the wind, the conduit between heaven and hull:

> The tall masts are the pillars supporting the balanced planes that, motionless and silent, catch from the air the ship's motive-power, as it were a gift from Heaven vouchsafed to the audacity of man. (010-004)

This image — the mast as a "pillar" bearing "balanced planes" — draws on architectural language to elevate the mast from equipment to monument. The sails are "motionless and silent" even as they propel the ship; the mast stands immobile in the centre of a fury of motion. The "gift from Heaven" is the wind itself, which no human effort can summon — only receive.

## The Masthead Lookout

The mast also serves as the lookout's perch. From the masthead, seamen scanned the horizon for other vessels, landfalls, and weather signs — a solitary vigil high above the deck, where the roll of the ship is most violent and the view most commanding:

> The next day there were very few ships in sight from our mastheads — seven at most, perhaps, with a few more distant specks, hull down, beyond the magic ring of the horizon. (010-002)

The phrase "hull down" is itself a masthead observation — a vessel so distant that only her masts and sails are visible above the curvature of the earth. The lookout at the masthead lives in a world of such partial sightings, interpreting the meaning of distant specks against an empty sea.

## The Peril of the Mast in Storm

The extreme forces a mast must endure are never far from Conrad's mind. In a gale, a sudden shift of wind — the veering or backing that accompanies a storm's passage — could snap all three masts from a ship in an instant. The seaman watches for this shift with every nerve alert:

> And every nerve on the alert you watch for the clearing-up mood of the Western King, that shall come with a shift of wind as likely as not to whip all the three masts out of your ship in the twinkling of an eye. (026-012)

The "Western King" is the west wind, ruler of the North Atlantic. Its "clearing-up mood" — the shift that follows the storm's centre — is, perversely, the moment of greatest danger. A wind that has been blowing steadily from one quarter may, in clearing, swing violently, catching the ship aback and bringing the masts down.

## The Maimed Mast

A ship deprived of her masts is a tragic sight in Conrad's maritime vision. He describes a wrecked vessel's remaining mast-stumps with the language of bodily injury — the ship as a living creature that has suffered amputation:

> There is defiance in the remaining stumps of her masts, raised up like maimed limbs against the menacing scowl of a stormy sky. (018-009)

The word "defiance" transforms the passive wreck into an active agent — even in ruin, the mast-stumps "raise up" against the sky, refusing to yield. The stormy sky is personified with a "menacing scowl," so that the wreck scene becomes a confrontation between two antagonists, the maimed ship and the hostile heaven.

## Masts Against the City

Conrad often juxtaposes the lofty masts of ships against the flatness of urban docklands, creating a visual tension between the maritime and the terrestrial. In the London docks, masts peek out incongruously from behind warehouse roofs — a reminder that these ships, penned in by brick and stone, belong to a different element:

> It is a strange conjunction this of roofs and mastheads, of walls and yardarms. (031-007)

> They lie concealed in the intricate growth of houses with a few stalks of mastheads here and there overtopping the roof of some four-story warehouse. (031-006)

The word "stalks" is deliberate — the masts are living things, organic growths pushing up through the dead strata of brick, a remnant of the wild sea in the heart of the tame city.

## The Master and His Masts

For a ship's captain, the mast is not an abstract structure but a personal burden. Its immense leverage — the mechanical force exerted by wind on canvas transmitted through the mast to the hull — becomes a matter of intimate concern:

> Thus the immense leverage of the ship's tall masts became a matter very near my own heart. (012-004)

This is Conrad at his most personally revealing. As a young officer suddenly placed in command, he felt the weight of the masts — their "leverage" — as a physical sensation, a pressure on his own body. The mast becomes the point at which the abstract forces of wind, sea, and sail intersect with the human responsibility of command.

## The Man of Masts and Sails

For Conrad, the mast represents the fundamental difference between those who know the sea intimately and those who merely navigate it. The "man of masts and sails" is a distinct human type — one for whom the sea is not an obstacle to be crossed but a companion to be understood:

> Here speaks the man of masts and sails, to whom the sea is not a navigable element, but an intimate companion. (022-006)

This formulation — "not a navigable element, but an intimate companion" — is the core of Conrad's maritime philosophy. The mast, as the interface between the human and the elemental, is the instrument through which this companionship is mediated. To know masts and sails is to know the sea itself.
