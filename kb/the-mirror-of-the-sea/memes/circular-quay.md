## The Centre of Antipodean Maritime Life

Circular Quay was, in Conrad's time, the focal point of Sydney's maritime identity — not a walled dock but an open quay integrated into the fabric of the city. From the heart of Sydney, "down the vista of important streets," the wool-clippers were visible at their berths, their presence a daily confirmation of the colony's connection to the wider world. Conrad's affection for this place is unmistakable: it represents the port as it should be, open and alive.

> This was especially the case in Sydney, where, from the heart of the fair city, down the vista of important streets, could be seen the wool-clippers lying at the Circular Quay — no walled prison-house of a dock that, but the integral part of one of the finest, most beautiful, vast, and safe bays the sun ever shone upon. (034-001)

## The Wool-Clipper Era

The clippers that lay at Circular Quay were not the fleeting visitors that modern steam-liners would become. They stayed for months, loading wool, their names becoming "household words" in the colonial town. On Sundays, citizens came down to inspect the ships; officers played cicerone to visitors; the tinkle of cottage pianos drifted from open stern-ports. This was a social as much as a commercial maritime world — one that would vanish with the coming of steam.

> Now great steam-liners lie at these berths, always reserved for the sea aristocracy — grand and imposing enough ships, but here today and gone next week; whereas the general cargo, emigrant, and passenger clippers of my time, rigged with heavy spars, and built on fine lines, used to remain for months together waiting for their load of wool. (034-001)

## A Community of Ships

The ships moored around Circular Quay formed a floating community with its own hierarchies, reputations, and gossip. Conrad records how crews of different vessels, moored "all about the Circular Quay," formed opinions of each other's ships — some bearing "bad names," others admired. The Quay was the stage on which the small dramas of shipboard life played out, visible to all.

> We, belonging to other vessels moored all about the Circular Quay in Sydney, used to shake our heads at her with a great sense of the unblemished virtue of our own well-loved ships. (016-007)

## The Night Watchman's Sydney

Conrad's evenings as night-watchman on a ship at Circular Quay gave him a unique view of the harbour's night life. The sounds of the town — larrikins brawling, cabmen swapping stories, mysterious propositions from the quay — reached him across the water. It was a cosmopolitan world: Chinese eating-houses, the cry of the saveloy pedlar, and the endless perambulations of Mr. B—— and the mate of the *Cicero*, who would see each other to their respective ships and back again, "out of pure and disinterested affection."

> He used to go ashore every night to foregather in some hotel's parlour with his crony, the mate of the barque *Cicero*, lying on the other side of the Circular Quay. (034-010)

## Captain MacW—— at Circular Quay

Among the ships at Circular Quay was one commanded by a captain whom Conrad describes as the "P.R.A. of the sea-craft" — the President of the Royal Academy of seamanship. Handsome, well-dressed, with gold links and an air of "bluff distinction," he impressed the stevedores and clerks who saw him walk ashore. Yet Conrad's judgment is wry: this commander was all style and no substance, his seamanship "on stereotyped lines, unsuggestive, empty of any lesson that one could lay to heart."

> His weather-beaten and handsome face, his portly presence, his shirtfronts and broad cuffs and gold links, his air of bluff distinction, impressed the humble beholders (stevedores, tally clerks, tide-waiters) as he walked ashore over the gangway of his ship lying at the Circular Quay in Sydney. (009-002)
