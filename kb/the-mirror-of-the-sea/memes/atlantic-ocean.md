## Location and Identity

The Atlantic Ocean, in Conrad's maritime cosmology, is not a passive body of water but an active agent — the realm of the West Wind, whose "battalions of Atlantic rollers" roll ceaselessly toward the shores of Europe. It is the ocean that British seamen know most intimately, the waters that lie between the home ports and the wider world. For Conrad, the Atlantic was the defining theatre of the sailing ship: its gales were the great test of seamanship, its prevailing westerlies the engine of commerce, and its variable moods the constant preoccupation of every officer on deck.

## Conrad's Description

> The narrow seas around these isles, where British admirals keep watch and ward upon the marches of the Atlantic Ocean, are subject to the turbulent sway of the West Wind. (025-004)

> He is the warlord who sends his battalions of Atlantic rollers to the assault of our seaboard. The compelling voice of the West Wind musters up to his service all the might of the ocean. (026-002)

> The West Wind reigns over the seas surrounding the coasts of these kingdoms; and from the gateways of the channels, from promontories as if from watchtowers, from estuaries of rivers as if from posting stations, his emissaries carry his power to the innermost shores. (026-001)

> The southwesterly mood of the West Wind is an enemy of sleep, and even of a recumbent position, in the responsible officers of a ship. After two hours of futile, lightheaded, inconsequent thinking upon all sorts of subjects, one gets up and tries to read. (027-004)

## Maritime Significance

The Atlantic was the ocean that British seamen of Conrad's era knew best — the highway to the Americas, the route to the Cape, and the home waters of the nation that ruled the waves. Conrad's extended meditation on the West Wind (chapters 25–27 of *The Mirror of the Sea*) is effectively a portrait of the North Atlantic in its most characteristic mood: the prevailing westerlies that drove sailing ships homeward from the far corners of the world. The Atlantic rollers, the "southwesterly weather," and the "thick weather" of the Channel approaches were all variations on a single theme — the ocean as a living force that shaped the character of those who sailed upon it.
