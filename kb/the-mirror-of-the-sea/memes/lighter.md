A **lighter** is a flat-bottomed barge employed in harbour and roadstead to transfer cargo between a ship and the shore. In the days before deep-water docks with their own cranes, most cargo was loaded and discharged by lighter — the ship anchored in the stream, the lighters came alongside, and the cargo was hoisted out by the ship's own gear. The lighter was the unglamorous workhorse of maritime commerce, essential but almost invisible in the literature of the sea.

Conrad mentions the lighter in two sharply contrasting contexts: as a vessel of everyday harbour work, and — in one extraordinary passage — as a symbol of cosmic insignificance.

## In Conrad's Words

> A ship anchored in an open roadstead, with cargo-lighters alongside and her own tackle swinging the burden over the rail, is accomplishing in her way a useful and profitable function.
>
> (033-004)

This passage captures the lighter in its ordinary role: the workaday transfer of goods from ship to shore. The ship is "accomplishing a useful and profitable function," and the lighter is the silent partner in that transaction, receiving the swinging burden over the rail.

But Conrad's deepest reflection on the lighter comes in a passage of almost vertiginous scale:

> Indeed, it is less than nothing, and I have seen, when the great soul of the world turned over with a heavy sigh, a perfectly new, extra-stout cargo-lighter go swimming upside down in a sort of unwilling, sorry manner amongst the lights of a steamer.
>
> (011-001)

The context here is the sudden, unpredictable violence of the sea intruding into the sheltered basin of a dock — one of those moments when "the great soul of the world" stirs and the works of men are overturned with a sigh. The "perfectly new, extra-stout cargo-lighter" — built strong for heavy work — is reduced to floating upside down, "in a sort of unwilling, sorry manner." The anthropomorphism is precise: the lighter seems almost embarrassed by its predicament, a stout and sensible vessel made ridiculous by the sea's casual power.

## The Lighter's Place

The lighter occupied a strange position in the maritime world. It was a vessel, but not a sea-going one; it worked in harbours and estuaries, never venturing beyond sheltered water. Its crew were watermen rather than seamen — skilled in tides and channels rather than in ocean navigation. Yet without the lighter, the great deep-water ships could not discharge their cargoes. The lighter was the link between the ship and the shore, the last vessel in the chain of maritime commerce — and, as Conrad shows, as vulnerable as any ship to the sea's indifference.
