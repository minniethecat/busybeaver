## Identity

Mr. B—— was the chief mate of a wool clipper laid up in Sydney's Circular Quay, where Conrad served as her night-watchman. He is one of the most memorably drawn minor characters in *The Mirror of the Sea* — a man defined by his nightly escapes ashore, his crony in the *Cicero*, and the strange, almost theatrical quality of his eventual fate.

## Conrad's Account

> On examining now, after many years, the residue of the feeling which was the outcome of the contact of our personalities, I discover, without much surprise, a certain flavour of dislike. Upon the whole, I think that a chief mate who never impressed me in any other way than by the disagreeable effect of his personality was the man for me. (005-008)

> The bond between us was the ship; and therein a ship, though she has female attributes and is loved very unreasonably, is different from a woman. That I should have been tremendously smitten with my first ship is intelligible. (005-009)

> He used to go ashore every night to foregather in some hotel's parlour with his crony, the mate of the barque Cicero, lying on the other side of the Circular Quay. Late at night I would hear from afar his hard, dreary laugh. (034-010)

> A stupid job, and fit only for an old man, my comrades used to tell me, to be the night-watchman of a captive (though honoured) ship. And generally the oldest of the able seamen in a ship's crew does get it. (034-002)

## Character and Anecdotes

Mr. B—— was defined by routine: every evening, as soon as the day's pretence of work was done, he would vanish ashore to meet his friend, the mate of the barque *Cicero*. Conrad, as night-watchman, would hear him return — sometimes singing, always with "a hard, dreary laugh." Though Conrad acknowledges "a certain flavour of dislike" toward the man, the retrospective account is coloured by something closer to affection: the recognition that they were both prisoners of the same ship, bound by the same lonely duty, passing each other in the night like sentinels on adjacent walls.
