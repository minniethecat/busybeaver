## Definition

In the language of the sea, a **passage** is the voyage of a ship from one port to another, considered as a completed act of navigation. Unlike the more general term "voyage," which may encompass an entire trading circuit or years at sea, a passage is a discrete unit of maritime accomplishment: the ship departs, crosses the sea, and arrives. The quality of a passage—whether it was "good" or "bad," fast or slow—reflects upon the skill of the master, the qualities of the vessel, and the favour of wind and weather.

## The Passage as Professional Measure

For Conrad's generation of seamen, a ship's reputation was built upon her passages. A "decent passage" was the hallmark of a well-found ship under capable command. Conrad's Captain S——, one of the memorable figures of the memoir, regarded a ship's performance on passage as a reflection of his own professional identity:

> The point, however, is that Captain S——, who used to say frequently, "She never made a decent passage after I left her," seemed to think that the secret of her exemplary behaviour consisted in the quality of his own personal regard for the ship. (012-003)

This remark reveals the deep identification between master and vessel: the passage is not merely the ship's achievement but the captain's—a testament to his seamanship, his care, his *regard* for the ship. A "decent passage" is the currency of professional reputation.

## Art and the Passage

The execution of a passage involves every element of the seaman's art. Conrad connects the passage to the "fine art" of sailing in his extended meditation on ship-handling (chapters VII–IX), where the master's skill in trimming sails, reading weather, and choosing courses determines whether the passage is swift or laboured. A fast passage under sail required not merely favourable winds but the constant, minute adjustments of sheets and braces that extracted every fraction of a knot from the available breeze.

## The Passage as Narrative Arc

In Conrad's memoir, the passage serves an additional function: it is the narrative unit of a seaman's life. Each passage—London to Sydney, the Channel to the Caribbean, the Thames to the Mediterranean—is a story in miniature, with its own departure, its own weather, its own moments of tension and relief. The accumulation of passages constitutes a career, each one a chapter in the "intimate revelation" (Author's Note) that the book undertakes.

## Passages and Ships

Not all ships make good passages. Conrad observes a fundamental truth of maritime economics: "There are profitable ships and unprofitable ships. The profitable ship will carry a large load through all the hazards of the weather" (013-003). A ship's profitability depended on her ability to make reliable passages—swift enough to satisfy charterers, safe enough to satisfy underwriters. The ship that could not make her passages was, in the unforgiving arithmetic of the sea, a failure.
