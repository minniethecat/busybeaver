## Definition

A sail is a surface — traditionally of canvas — set upon a ship's masts and yards to catch the wind and drive the vessel forward. To Conrad, sail was more than equipment: it was the medium through which the seaman conversed with the wind, the visible sign of a ship's intent. The handling of sail, particularly in the fore-and-aft rig of small craft, he considered "a fine art" whose mastery was already fading from the modern world.

## The Fine Art of Sailing

Conrad devotes an entire chapter (VIII, "The Fine Art") to the proposition that the handling of sailing vessels — particularly fore-and-aft rigged yachts — constitutes a genuine art form, one whose practitioners deserve recognition as artists rather than mere technicians:

> It is their striving for victory that has elevated the sailing of pleasure craft to the dignity of a fine art in that special sense. (008-001)

This "special sense" is crucial — Conrad is not claiming that all sailing is art, but that the competitive pursuit of sailing perfection, the relentless drive to make a vessel move faster and more gracefully through the water, produces something that transcends mere technical competence. The racing of yachts, by imposing the discipline of time allowances based on tonnage alone, forced designers and sailors to pursue pure efficiency:

> His contention is that racing, without time allowances for anything else but tonnage — that is, for size — has fostered the fine art of sailing to the pitch of perfection. Every sort of demand is made upon the master of a sailing-yacht, and to be penalized in proportion to your success may be of advantage to the sport itself, but it has an obviously deteriorating effect upon the artistic quality of the handling. (007-008)

The fore-and-aft rig, which allowed a vessel to sail closer to the wind than the older square rig, was the technical foundation of this art. Its sails — the mainsail, jib, staysail — worked together as a single aerodynamic system:

> It requires less effort in handling; the trimming of the sailplanes to the wind can be done with speed and accuracy; the unbroken spread of the sail-area is of infinite advantage; and the greatest possible speed is obtained for the vessel. (008-001)

## Sail as Visual Spectacle

Conrad is acutely sensitive to the aesthetic dimension of sail — not merely its function but its appearance. A ship under full sail is, in his treatment, one of the supreme sights of the maritime world:

> The tall masts holding aloft the white canvas, spread out like a snare for catching the invisible power of the air, emerge gradually from the water, sail after sail, yard after yard, growing big, till, under the towering structure of her machinery, you perceive the absurdly small figure of a man. (010-003)

The image of sail as a "snare for catching the invisible power of the air" is characteristic of Conrad's method — the literal function of the sail (trapping wind) is rendered as something almost magical. And the contrast between the towering "machinery" of canvas, mast, and yard, and the "absurdly small figure of a man" who commands it all, captures the essential disproportion at the heart of the sailing ship.

The setting of sail, in Conrad's observation, is not merely functional but beautiful in its own right. He compares it, memorably, to the unfolding of a bird's wings:

> The setting of their sails resembles more than anything else the unfolding of a bird's wings; the facility of their evolutions is a pleasure to the eye. (008-002)

The enormous mainsail of a cutter, viewed from shore, carries a quality of silent majesty:

> The enormous mainsail of a cutter, as she draws slowly past a point of land or the end of a jetty under your admiring gaze, invests her with an air of lofty and silent majesty. (008-003)

## The Ship Under Sail: Two States

A ship with her sails furled, sitting in a harbour, presents a very different aspect from the same ship under way. Conrad notes the peculiar contrast between the two states:

> For a ship with her sails furled on her squared yards, and reflected from truck to waterline in the smooth gleaming sheet of a landlocked harbour, seems, indeed, to a seaman's eye the most perfect picture of a sea-going vessel that it is possible to conceive. (006-003)

The furled sails of the ship at anchor represent potential, readiness — the ship is complete and beautiful, but inert. The contrast with the noisy, exultant moment of departure is telling:

> And this noisiness, this exultation at the moment of the ship's departure, make a tremendous contrast to the silent moments of her arrival in a foreign roadstead — the silent moments when, stripped of her sails, she forges ahead, drawn by the invisible power of her own momentum. (006-003)

## Sail as a Snare

Conrad returns repeatedly to the image of sail as a trap — a device for catching something invisible. This metaphor renders the physical function of the sail in terms that approach the supernatural:

> The tall masts holding aloft the white canvas, spread out like a snare for catching the invisible power of the air. (010-003)

The wind is "invisible power" — something real but ungraspable, known only by its effects. The sail is the mechanism that translates this invisible force into visible motion, the ship's progress through the water being the only evidence that the wind exists at all.

## The Receding Art

Conrad's treatment of sail is pervaded by a sense of elegy. The art of handling sailing vessels is, in his view, already receding from the world, displaced by the steamer and its mechanical certainties:

> And the sailing of any vessel afloat is an art whose fine form seems already receding from us on its way to the overshadowed Valley of Oblivion. (008-007)

This is not merely nostalgia. Conrad's point is more specific: the art of sail required a kind of knowledge — intuitive, particular, embodied — that the steamer does not demand. The sailing-ship seaman had to know his vessel as one knows a living thing:

> It requires not only the knowledge of the general principles of sailing, but a particular acquaintance with the character of the craft. (008-004)

## Sail and the Seaman's Vocation

For Conrad, the handling of sail is the definitive expression of the seaman's craft. The departure of a sailing ship — the moment when canvas is spread to the wind — is the moment when the seaman truly begins his work:

> A ship may have left her port some time before; she may have been at sea, in the fullest sense of the phrase, for days; but, for all that, as long as the coast she was about to leave remained in sight, a southern-going ship of yesterday had not yet departed from the Western world. (001-003)

And the ultimate departure — death itself — is figured in maritime terms as the final setting of sail on a voyage whose destination no chart can show:

> Evidently he was reluctant to take his final cross-bearings of this earth for a Departure on the only voyage to an unknown destination a sailor ever undertakes. (003-007)

## Types of Sail

In Conrad's maritime world, sails came in two broad families. The square sails — carried on yards set at right angles to the mast — were the traditional rig of deep-water ships, massive and labour-intensive, requiring crews to go aloft in all weathers. The fore-and-aft sails — set along the line of the keel — were the rig of coasters, pilot boats, and yachts, capable of sailing closer to the wind and handled with fewer men. It was the fore-and-aft rig that Conrad considered the medium of the "fine art," its sails forming a continuous, unbroken plane that could be trimmed with precision and speed.
