The *chief mate* occupies a distinct and vital position in the hierarchy of the sailing ship as Joseph Conrad portrays it. Neither master nor common sailor, the chief mate inhabits the forward part of the vessel — the forecastle-head, where the ship's ground tackle lives — and serves as the indispensable link between the commanding authority aft and the working crew forward.

> For the chief mate is the keeper of the ship's anchors and the guardian of her cable. There are good ships and bad ships, comfortable ships and ships where, from first day to last of the voyage, there is no rest for a chief mate's body and soul. (005-002)

## Keeper of the Anchors

The chief mate's most distinctive responsibility is the care of the ship's anchors and cables. Conrad dwells on this connection at length, finding in it a symbolic dimension — the chief mate is the officer most intimately concerned with the moment when ship meets seabed, when the voyage pauses and the vessel becomes, temporarily, a fixed point in the world.

> It is difficult to disconnect the idea of ships' anchors from the idea of the ship's chief mate — the man who sees them go down clear and come up sometimes foul; because not even the most unremitting care can accomplish that. Then the business of "getting the anchor" and securing it afterwards is unduly prolonged, and made a weariness to the chief mate. (006-001)

The approach to land is announced not by the master but by the chief mate, whose voice is the first to break the silence of the sea passage with the concrete business of making the ship ready for anchorage:

> The first approach to the land, as yet invisible to the crew's eyes, is announced by the brisk order of the chief mate to the boatswain: "We will get the anchors over this afternoon" or "first thing tomorrow," depending upon the state of the atmospheric poise. (005-002)

## Domain and Authority

Conrad locates the chief mate physically and symbolically in the forward part of the ship. When the cry of "All hands on deck!" goes up, it is the chief mate who comes forward — the only representative of the afterguard (the officers) to engage directly with the crew in the ship's most critical moments:

> It is the chief mate, the only figure of the ship's afterguard, who comes bustling forward at the cry of "All hands on deck!" (005-005)

This forward position is both physical and psychological. The chief mate belongs to the forecastle-head in a way that the master never can:

> It is in the forepart of her that her chief mate feels most at home. (005-005)

## Cargo and Consequence

The chief mate bears responsibility for the stowage and distribution of cargo — a task whose importance is revealed most starkly when it goes wrong. Conrad recounts a chief mate who made an error in cargo distribution and paid for it:

> It was only poetic justice that the chief mate who had made a mistake — perhaps a half-excusable one — about the distribution of his ship's cargo should pay the penalty. A piece of one of the minor spars that did carry away flew against the chief mate's back, and sent him sliding on his face for quite a considerable distance along the main deck. (015-011)

The phrase "poetic justice" is characteristic of Conrad's view of the sea — it punishes errors directly, often with a precision that seems almost intentional. The chief mate's mistake in cargo distribution leads to a spar carrying away, and that same spar strikes him down: the error returns upon the erring officer.

## The Chief Mate's Life

Conrad himself served as chief mate and writes of the experience with personal warmth. His first voyage as chief mate, under Captain MacW——, was a moment of genuine fulfilment:

> On my first voyage as chief mate with good Captain MacW—— I remember that I felt quite flattered, and went blithely about my duties, myself a commander for all practical purposes. (002-003)

This is the paradox of the chief mate's position: subordinate to the master, yet for long stretches of the watch effectively in command. The chief mate is a commander-in-waiting, learning the art of command while exercising its practical responsibilities.
