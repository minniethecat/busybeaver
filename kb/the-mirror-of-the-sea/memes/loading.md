The *loading* of a sailing ship was, in Joseph Conrad's estimation, far more than a routine commercial operation. It was a matter of "skill, judgment, and knowledge" — a craft bordering on art, whose mastery separated the competent seaman from the merely adequate one, and whose decline marked the passing of the great age of sail.

> The loading of ships was once a matter of skill, judgment, and knowledge. (013-005)

This single sentence, freighted with the weight of the word "once," encapsulates Conrad's elegiac view of the subject. Loading was *once* an art; the implication is clear — it is no longer.

## Loading as Seamanship

In the age of sail, the speed of a ship depended directly on the seaman who commanded her — and a crucial part of that command was the proper loading and trim of the vessel. Conrad is explicit about this connection:

> In those days the speed depended upon the seaman; therefore, apart from the laws, rules, and regulations for the good preservation of his cargo, he was careful of his loading — or what is technically called the trim of his ship. (014-001)

The word *trim* is the technical term for the ship's fore-and-aft balance in the water — a matter determined entirely by how the cargo is distributed. A ship that is down by the head or down by the stern will sail poorly; a ship in proper trim will find her natural speed.

## The Decline of Loading Skill

Conrad identifies the mechanization of the docks as the force that destroyed the art of loading. The hurry of modern commerce, the introduction of steam hoisting machinery, and the pressure for rapid turnaround all conspired to eliminate the careful judgment that had characterized the old ways:

> The hurry of the times, the loading and discharging organization of the docks, the use of hoisting machinery which works quickly and will not wait, the cry for prompt despatch, the very size of his ship — all this makes the loading of a ship no longer a matter of individual skill but of mechanical routine. (013-002)

This passage is a miniature of Conrad's larger argument throughout *The Mirror of the Sea*: the replacement of individual craftsmanship by mechanical process is a loss, not a gain. The stevedore who once exercised judgment is now merely a cog in a machine.

## The Consequences of Bad Loading

A poorly loaded ship does not merely sail slowly — she becomes actively dangerous. Conrad describes the condition of a "cranky" ship — one whose centre of gravity has been brought too low by injudicious loading:

> To travel along with a cranky ship for ninety days or so is no doubt a nerve-trying experience; but in this case what was wrong with our craft was this: that by my system of loading she had been made too stable. Once she began, you felt that she would never stop, and this hopeless sensation, characterizing the motion of ships whose centre of gravity is brought down too low in loading, made everyone on board weary of life. (015-007, 015-008)

The irony here is characteristic of Conrad: a ship can be too stable. A vessel loaded too deep — with her centre of gravity forced too low by over-heavy cargo stowed too low — develops a quick, violent rolling motion that is far more exhausting to crew and ship alike than the slower, easier motion of a properly loaded vessel. The art of loading, then, is the art of finding the middle way between instability and excessive stiffness.

## The Specialised Loading Dock

The New South Dock in London represented a particular concentration of the loading trade — a place where the wool-clippers, the fastest and most beautiful sailing ships of their era, were loaded for their outward passages to the Colonies:

> The New South Dock was especially a loading dock for the Colonies in those great (and last) days of smart wool-clippers, good to look at and — well — exciting to handle. (033-009)

Conrad's hesitation — the dash before "well" and the qualifying "exciting to handle" — captures the ambivalence of the sailor's relationship with these beautiful but demanding vessels. Loading them was an art; sailing them, an excitement that shaded into ordeal.
