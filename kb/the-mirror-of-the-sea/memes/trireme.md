## Definition

The trireme was the standard heavy warship of classical antiquity, propelled by three superimposed banks of oars on each side. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad invokes the trireme not to describe it directly, but to define it by negation: the vessel that a prudent Roman commander would *not* have chosen when exploring the treacherous sandbanks of the Thames estuary. The mention serves as a precise detail in Conrad's imaginative reconstruction of the first Roman approach to Britain, grounding his speculation in the material realities of ancient seamanship.

## In Conrad's Words

> The navigation of his craft must have engrossed all the Roman's attention in the calm of a summer's day (he would choose his weather), when the single row of long sweeps (the galley would be a light one, not a trireme) could fall in easy cadence upon a sheet of water like plate-glass, reflecting faithfully the classic form of his vessel and the contour of the lonely shores close on his left hand.
