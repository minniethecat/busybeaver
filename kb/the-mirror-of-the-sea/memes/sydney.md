## Location and Identity

Sydney, in Conrad's *The Mirror of the Sea*, appears not as a city but as a port — specifically, the Circular Quay where deep-sea sailing ships were laid up between voyages. Conrad served there as night-watchman of a wool-clipper, an experience that provided the setting for some of his most memorable character sketches (Mr. B——, the mate of the *Cicero*) and his meditation on the melancholy of ships removed from their element. Sydney for him was the far edge of the maritime world, a place where ships and men waited in suspended animation.

## Conrad's Description

> These towns of the Antipodes, not so great then as they are now, took an interest in the shipping, the running links with "home," whose numbers confirmed the sense of their growing importance. (034-001)

> A stupid job, and fit only for an old man, my comrades used to tell me, to be the night-watchman of a captive (though honoured) ship. And generally the oldest of the able seamen in a ship's crew does get it. (034-002)

> He used to go ashore every night to foregather in some hotel's parlour with his crony, the mate of the barque Cicero, lying on the other side of the Circular Quay. (034-010)

> There were other experiences, less painful and more funny for the most part, with one amongst them of a dramatic complexion; but the greatest experience of them all was Mr. B——, our chief mate himself. (034-009)

## Maritime Significance

Sydney in the 1890s was the great antipodean port of call for British shipping — the destination for emigrants and the loading point for Australian wool. Conrad's experience there as a ship-keeper placed him in the peculiar position of guarding a vessel that was not going anywhere, surrounded by other vessels in the same condition. His description of the Circular Quay as a gathering-place of captive ships — each with its own night-watchman, its own chief mate escaping ashore, its own slow accumulation of rust and barnacles — is a minor masterpiece of maritime melancholy.
