**Windward** refers to the side or direction from which the wind is blowing. On a sailing vessel, the windward side is the exposed, weather-beaten flank — the side that takes the brunt of wind and spray, where officers and crew brace themselves against the elements, and from which the first signs of approaching weather are read.

## The Exposed Side

In Conrad's accounts of heavy weather, the windward side of a ship is where men station themselves to observe the sea and sky, hooked into the rigging to avoid being swept away by the wind's force.

> Mr. P——, in charge of the deck, hooked on to the windward mizzen rigging in a state of perfect serenity; myself, the third mate, also hooked on somewhere to windward of the slanting poop, in a state of the utmost preparedness to jump at the very first hint of some sort of order… (011-009)

The physical posture required on the windward side — leaning into the wind, hooked onto the rigging — is itself a measure of the conditions. To maintain one's place on the windward deck demands constant attention and physical exertion.

## Amidst the Gale

During a sudden pampero-like gale, the distinction between windward and leeward becomes a matter of survival — the crew is flung to one side or the other depending on where they were caught when the ship heeled over.

> Before we knew very well what was coming all the sails we had set had burst; the furled ones were blowing loose, ropes flying, sea hissing — it hissed tremendously — wind howling, and the ship lying on her side, so that half of the crew were swimming and the other half clawing desperately at whatever came to hand, according to the side of the deck each man had been caught on by the catastrophe, either to leeward or to windward. (023-008)

## Reading the Sky

The windward sky is where a seaman looks for the first intimations of changing weather. Before a gale announces itself with full violence, experienced eyes search the horizon to windward for the subtle signs that precede the storm.

> A look in the eyes of a shipmate, a low murmur in the most sheltered spot where the watch on duty are huddled together, a meaning moan from one to the other with a glance at the windward sky, a sigh of weariness, a gesture of disgust passing into the keeping of the great wind, become part and parcel of the gale. (024-008)

## Tactical Significance

In the age of sail, being to windward of an enemy conferred a decisive tactical advantage — it gave the windward ship the initiative to engage or disengage at will. Lord Nelson's tactical genius lay partly in his willingness to surrender the windward position in favour of a direct approach that would bring his fleet into crushing close action. The windward position, once the cornerstone of fleet tactics, was both a physical reality and a strategic concept that dominated naval thinking for centuries.
