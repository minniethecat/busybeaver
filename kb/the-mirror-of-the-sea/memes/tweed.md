## Vessel Identity

The *[[Tweed]]* was an iron wool-clipper in which Conrad served as a young officer. Built on the Clyde during the great age of the sailing-ship, she was "one of those iron wool-clippers that the Clyde had floated out in swarms upon the world during the seventh decade of the last century." She was, Conrad notes with characteristic understatement, "a good ship but not a lucky one." It was aboard the *Tweed* that Conrad was promoted to officer of the watch — "alone in charge of the deck" — after the second mate fell ill, an experience that revealed to him "the immense leverage of the men's quality."

## Conrad's Account

> The ship was one of those iron wool-clippers that the Clyde had floated out in swarms upon the world during the seventh decade of the last century. It was a good ship but not a lucky one. (011-004)

> On my first voyage as chief mate with good Captain MacW—— I remember that I felt quite flattered, and went blithely about my duties. Still, whatever the greatness of my illusion, the fact remained that the real commander was there. (002-003)

> The point, however, is that Captain S——, who used to say frequently, "She never made a decent passage after I left her," seemed to think that the secret of her speed lay in the captain's right feeling for his ship. (012-003)

> The second mate falling ill during the passage, I was promoted to officer of the watch, alone in charge of the deck. Thus the immense leverage of the men's quality was disclosed to me. (012-004)

> So sail did get shortened more or less in time even in that ship, and her tall spars never went overboard while I served in her. (012-001)

## A School for Command

The *Tweed* served, for Conrad, as an unexpected school for command. When the second mate fell ill during the passage, Conrad — then a junior officer — was promoted to officer of the watch, "alone in charge of the deck." The experience was transformative: "Thus the immense leverage of the men's quality was disclosed to me." It was aboard the *Tweed* that Conrad first grasped the truth that a ship's performance depends on the quality of every man aboard, not merely the captain's skill or intentions.

## The Captain's Right Feeling

Captain S——'s conviction about the secret of his ship's speed — that it lay in "the captain's right feeling for his ship" — provides Conrad with material for both comedy and philosophy. The conviction was demonstrably false: the *Tweed*'s passages depended on [[wind]], tide, and the coordinated effort of her crew. Yet Conrad is not entirely dismissive. The relationship between a commander and his [[craft]] does have an element of sympathy, an intuitive dimension that cannot be reduced to seamanship manuals. Captain S——'s error was not in believing that "right feeling" mattered, but in believing that it mattered exclusively — and that it emanated from him alone.

## Crew and Character

The *Tweed*'s crew represented, for Conrad, a microcosm of the sailing-ship world. [[captain-s|Captain S——]] with his eccentric faith in his own moral influence. [[mr-p-hard-of-hearing|Mr. P——]], the hard-of-hearing chief mate who could not "tell how much wind there is" yet was "the very devil of a fellow with the crew when roused." And Conrad himself, young and observant, learning that [[seamanship]] was a collective art, not a solo performance. The [[gale|gales]] they weathered together, the [[north-sea|North Sea]] passages they endured — these experiences, filtered through memory, became the foundation of *The Mirror of the Sea*.

## Fate

The *Tweed* was, by Conrad's account, "not a lucky one." She ended her career as many sailing-ships did — obsolete in an age of steam, her tall spars and white canvas replaced by the "pulsating tremor" and "iron heart" of the steamship. Conrad does not record her final fate, but his epitaph is implicit: she was a vessel of the great age of sail, and her like would not be seen again.
