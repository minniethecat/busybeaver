## Definition

Running — more fully, "running before the wind" — is the point of sail in which a vessel sails directly downwind, the wind coming from dead astern. It is the fastest point of sail, the ship rushing forward with the wind at her back, but it is also a demanding one: the helmsman must hold a steady course to prevent an accidental gybe, and in heavy weather the ship may be pooped by a following sea. Conrad knew the exhilaration and the danger of running intimately, and his descriptions of ships running before gales are among the most vivid passages in *The Mirror of the Sea*.

## In Conrad's Words

> Down south, running before the gales of high latitudes, she made our life a burden to us.
> (015-009)

> I have heard a reserved, silent man, with no nerves to speak of, after three days of hard running in thick southwesterly weather, burst out passionately: "I wish to God we could get sight of something!"
> (027-002)

> The force of the wind, though we were running before it at the rate of some ten knots an hour, was so great that it drove me with a steady push to the front of the poop, where my commander was holding on.
> (027-004)

> The very first Christmas night I ever spent away from land was employed in running before a Gulf of Lyons gale, which made the old ship groan in every timber as she skipped before it over the short seas.
> (039-001)

## The Moral Weight of Speed

Running before the wind, for all its exhilaration, carries a peculiar psychological burden. The ship, travelling at her utmost speed with the wind dead astern, is committed to her course — there is no turning back into the gale, no easy escape if the land lies ahead. Conrad captures this tension between velocity and vulnerability: the ship that "skips before" the gale is also the ship whose crew, after days of hard running, yearns desperately for "sight of something" — any fixed point, any evidence that they are not hurtling blind through an endless, hostile sea. The West Wind himself, in his southwest mood, "strews the seeds of anxiety upon the decks of scudding ships" and "sprinkles with grey hairs the heads of shipmasters in the homeward-bound ships running for the Channel" (026-003). Running is thus not merely a point of sail but a state of moral trial — speed purchased with the coin of constant, gnawing uncertainty.
