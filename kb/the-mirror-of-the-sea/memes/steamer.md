# Steamer

The **steamer** — the steamship — is the vessel of the modern age in Conrad's maritime world. Where the sailing ship contends with the sea in a relationship of mutual respect and intimate dependence, the steamer disdains it, her life sustained not by wind and canvas but by coal, iron, and the pulsating engine within her ribs. Conrad's treatment of the steamer is one of the book's central thematic tensions: the passing of the age of sail and the rise of a new, mechanical relationship between man and ocean.

## The Mechanical Heart

The steamer's defining feature is her engine — a mechanical heart whose silence means death. Conrad draws the contrast with the sailing ship in a passage of concentrated force:

> It beats and throbs like a pulsating heart within her iron ribs, and when it stops, the steamer, whose life is not so much a contest as the disdainful ignoring of the sea, sickens and dies upon the waves. The sailing-ship, with her unthrobbing body, seemed to lead mysteriously a sort of unearthly existence, bordering upon the magic of the invisible forces, sustained by the inspiration of life-giving and death-dealing winds. (019-001)

The opposition is absolute. The sailing ship is "unearthly" and "magical," sustained by invisible forces that are both "life-giving and death-dealing." The steamer, by contrast, carries her power within herself — it is material, mechanical, and ultimately mortal. When it fails, she does not drift as the sailing ship might, jury-rigged and limping; she simply dies.

## The Most Helpless Vessel

Of all vessels disabled at sea, Conrad singles out the steamer that has lost her propeller as the most pitiable case — a vessel stripped of the very quality that defines her:

> Of all ships disabled at sea, a steamer who has lost her propeller is the most helpless. And if she drifts into an unpopulated part of the ocean she may soon become overdue. The menace of the "overdue" and the finality of "missing" come very quickly to steamers whose life, fed on coals and breathing the black breath of smoke into the air, goes on in disregard of wind and wave. (018-008)

The language is pointed. The steamer's life is "fed on coals" — a deliberate contrast with the sailing ship, which is fed by the wind. The steamer breathes "the black breath of smoke" — an image of industrial pollution set against the clean air of the sailing vessel. And the steamer's cardinal sin is that she goes on "in disregard of wind and wave" — she does not acknowledge the sea as an adversary or a companion; she simply ignores it.

When the engine fails, this disregard is revealed as hubris. The steamer without her propeller becomes something worse than a sailing ship in a calm — she becomes a corpse:

> So that big steamer, dying by a sudden stroke, drifted, an unwieldy corpse, away from the track of other ships. (019-002)

The word "corpse" is not hyperbole. For a seaman, "there is no harder trial...than to feel a dead ship under his feet" (019-002). The steamer without power is not merely disabled but dead — a hulk deprived of its animating principle.

## The Spectral Steamer

One of the most haunting images in Conrad's memoir is that of a specific disabled steamer, sighted in a snowstorm in the high southern latitudes:

> She must have looked ghostly enough, that broken-down steamer, rolling in that snowstorm — a dark apparition in a world of white snowflakes to the staring eyes of that whaler's crew. (019-004)

The image is almost painterly: the dark hull of the disabled steamer against the white swirl of the snowstorm, sighted by a whaler — itself a vessel of the old maritime order — in the remotest waters of the Southern Ocean. The whaler's captain "unromantically reported having sighted a disabled steamer in latitude somewhere about 50 degrees S. and a longitude still more uncertain" (019-004). Eventually, "other steamers came out to look for her, and ultimately towed her away from the cold edge of the world into a harbour with docks and workshops, where, with many blows of hammers, her pulsating heart of steel was set going again to go forth presently in the renewed pride of its strength, fed on fire and water, breathing black smoke into the air, pulsating, throbbing, shouldering its arrogant way against the great rollers in blind disdain of winds and sea" (019-004).

The closing phrases — "blind disdain," "arrogant way" — carry the full weight of Conrad's judgment. The steamer, restored to life, returns to her characteristic posture: she does not navigate the sea, she shoulders through it. She does not respect the waves, she disdains them.

## The Highway and the Companion

This fundamental distinction between the steamer and the sailing ship is elaborated in one of the book's most important theoretical passages:

> Your modern ship which is a steamship makes her passages on other principles than yielding to the weather and humouring the sea. She receives smashing blows, but she advances; it is a slogging fight, and not a scientific campaign. The machinery, the steel, the fire, the steam, have stepped in between the man and the sea. (022-006)

The language of estrangement is precise: "machinery, the steel, the fire, the steam" have interposed themselves between the mariner and his element. The sailing-ship seaman had an "intimate companion" in the sea; the steamer's crew have a highway. Conrad underscores the distinction:

> A modern fleet of ships does not so much make use of the sea as exploit a highway. The modern ship is not the sport of the waves. Let us say that each of her voyages is a triumphant progress; and yet it is a question whether it is not a more subtle and more human triumph to be the sport of the waves and yet survive, achieving your end. (022-006)

The concession — "Let us say that each of her voyages is a triumphant progress" — is immediately undercut by the meditation that follows. To be "the sport of the waves and yet survive" is "a more subtle and more human triumph" than to bulldoze through the sea by sheer mechanical force. The steamer's triumph is material; the sailing ship's is spiritual.

## The Steamer in Port

The steamer's relationship with the shore is different from the sailing ship's. The sailing ship, when she arrived in port, settled in for months — her crew known to the town, her name "a household word" (034-001). The steamer, by contrast, is "here today and gone next week" (034-001). She belongs not to the port but to the schedule. At Sydney's Circular Quay, the great wool-clippers of Conrad's youth have been replaced:

> Now great steam-liners lie at these berths, always reserved for the sea aristocracy — grand and imposing enough ships, but here today and gone next week. (034-001)

The steam-liners are "grand and imposing," but they lack the rootedness, the presence, the slow, months-long loading that made the clipper ship part of the city's fabric. The steamer is a visitor; the sailing ship was a resident.

## The Passing of an Age

Conrad's portrait of the steamer is, in the end, an elegy for the age of sail. The steamer is not condemned — she is acknowledged as efficient, powerful, and triumphant. But the triumph is bought at a cost: the loss of intimacy between the mariner and the sea. "The machinery, the steel, the fire, the steam, have stepped in between the man and the sea" (022-006). What is left is a highway — safe, reliable, and spiritually impoverished. The steamer's engine beats like a heart, but it is a heart of iron, and when it stops, the ship that disdained the sea discovers that the sea has no more use for her.
