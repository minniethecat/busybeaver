## Definition

A Landfall is the moment when a vessel first sights land after an ocean passage. Joseph Conrad defines it as the act of "falling in with the land" — a matter of "a quick eye and of a clear atmosphere." It marks one half of the rhythmical swing of a seaman's life, paired with the Departure. From land to land, Conrad writes, is "the most concise definition of a ship's earthly fate."

## In Conrad's Words

> Landfall and Departure mark the rhythmical swing of a seaman's life and of a ship's career. From land to land is the most concise definition of a ship's earthly fate. (001-001)

> A "Departure" is not what a vain people of landsmen may think. The term "Landfall" is more easily understood; you fall in with the land, and it is a matter of a quick eye and of a clear atmosphere. (001-002)

> Your Landfall, be it a peculiarly-shaped mountain, a rocky headland, or a stretch of sand-dunes, you meet at first with a single glance. Further recognition will follow in due course; but essentially a Landfall, good or bad, is made and done with at the first cry of "Land ho!" (001-003)

> A Landfall may be good or bad. You encompass the earth with one particular spot of it in your eye. In all the devious tracings the course of a sailing-ship leaves upon the white paper of a chart she is always aiming for that one little spot — maybe a small island of the Eastern Archipelago, a lighthouse on a rocky cape, a mountain-top in the South Seas, or a strip of low sand-dunes on a tropical coast. (001-005)

> It was a distinct and complete impression, something that I don't know whether to call a Landfall or a Departure. Certainly he had gazed at times very fixedly before him with the Landfall's vigilant look, this sea-captain seated incongruously in a deep-backed chair. (003-008)

> It is hard to say; for in that voyage from which no man returns Landfall and Departure are instantaneous, merging together into one moment of supreme and final attention. (003-009)

## Technical Usage

In the age of sail, making a landfall required careful navigation. After weeks or months out of sight of land, the navigator relied on dead reckoning, celestial observations, and knowledge of prevailing winds and currents to bring the ship to its intended landfall. A good landfall — sighting the expected landmark at the expected time — confirmed the accuracy of the navigation. A bad landfall meant the ship had been set off course by unknown currents or errors in reckoning.

The cry of "Land ho!" from the lookout aloft was one of the most charged moments of any voyage. Conrad captures this in his description of the Landfall as something "made and done with at the first cry." The first glimpse — whether of "a peculiarly-shaped mountain, a rocky headland, or a stretch of sand-dunes" — was the navigator's verdict.
