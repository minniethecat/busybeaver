## Location and Identity

The Mediterranean Sea, enclosed by Europe, Africa, and Asia, is the sea that Conrad loved above all others. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, he devotes three chapters to its character, history, and personal significance. It is the sea where navigation was born, where Ulysses wandered, where the Romans "ruled without dispute," and where Conrad himself spent his most formative years as a young seaman. Its lack of tides, its steep shores, and its ancient associations combine to make it, in Conrad's phrase, "the most lovely of all the seas."

## Conrad's Description

> The cradle of oversea traffic and of the art of naval combats, the Mediterranean, apart from all the associations of adventure and glory, the common heritage of all mankind, makes a tender appeal to a seaman. (037-001)

> Happy he who, like Ulysses, has made an adventurous voyage; and there is no such sea for adventurous voyages as the Mediterranean — the inland sea which the ancients looked upon as so vast and so full of wonders. (038-001)

> It was for the Mediterranean sailors that fair-haired sirens sang among the black rocks seething in white foam and mysterious voices spoke in the darkness above the moving wave. (038-002)

> The first impulse of navigation took its visible form in that tideless basin freed from hidden shoals and treacherous currents, as if in tender regard for the infancy of the art. (038-004)

> The charm of the Mediterranean dwells in the unforgettable flavour of my early days, and to this hour this sea, upon which the Romans alone ruled without dispute, has kept for me the fascination of youthful romance. (039-001)

## Maritime Significance

Conrad's Mediterranean chapters (37–39) are an extended love letter to the sea that cradled Western navigation. He emphasizes two features that distinguish it from all other waters: the absence of tides (which made it the nursery of early seamanship, free from the hidden shoals and treacherous currents that made the Atlantic so dangerous) and the density of its history (every headland, every island, every stretch of coast is saturated with literary and historical association). For Conrad, to sail the Mediterranean was to sail through time as much as through space — the trireme, the galley, and the tartane were all present in the same waters.
