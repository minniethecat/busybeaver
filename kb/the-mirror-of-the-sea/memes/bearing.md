## Definition

In maritime navigation, a **bearing** is the angular direction of one object from another, measured in degrees clockwise from true north. For Conrad's generation of seamen, the taking of cross-bearings—measuring angles to two or more known landmarks—was the fundamental method of fixing a ship's position when in sight of land. Unlike celestial navigation, which required clear skies and complex calculations, bearings on coastal features provided the most immediate and reliable position fix available to the nineteenth-century mariner.

## Bearings and the Departure

Conrad situates the taking of bearings within the ritual of Departure—the seaman's last professional act before a voyage begins. The "taking of Departure" is described not as a sentimental farewell but as a technical procedure: the final set of bearings that fixes the ship's position relative to the land before it drops below the horizon astern.

> The taking of Departure, if not the last sight of the land, is, perhaps, the last professional recognition of the land on the part of a sailor. It is the technical, as distinguished from the sentimental, "good-bye." Henceforth he has done with the coast astern of his ship. (001-004)

This passage establishes bearings as the seaman's final professional connection to the land—a matter of precision and duty rather than emotion.

## Cross-Bearings in Practice

Conrad's narratives frequently allude to the practice of cross-bearing without naming it explicitly, reflecting how deeply embedded the technique was in the seamanship of his era. A navigator would take simultaneous bearings on two or more charted landmarks—a lighthouse, a headland, a church spire—and plot the intersecting lines of position on the chart. Where the lines crossed was the ship's position.

The reliability of this method depended on the quality of the chart and the skill of the observer. A hurried bearing taken in poor visibility could introduce errors that compounded over hours of dead reckoning, potentially leading a vessel into danger.

## Bearings and the Chart

The chart and the bearing are inseparable instruments of navigation. Conrad evokes this relationship in his description of a track-chart of the North Atlantic spread across a cabin table:

> We had just gone down below for a moment to commune in a battened-down cabin, with a large white chart lying limp and damp upon a cold and clammy table under the light of a smoky lamp. Sprawling over that seaman's silent and trusted adviser, with one elbow upon the coast of Africa and the other planted in the neighbourhood of Cape Hatteras (it was a general track-chart of the North Atlantic)... (027-003)

The chart is the "silent and trusted adviser"—the surface upon which bearings are plotted and courses laid down. Every bearing taken at sea ultimately refers back to the chart, which transforms angular measurements into geographic certainty.

## Coastal Navigation and the "Grip of the Land"

The taking of bearings was most critical in coastal waters, where Conrad describes the "grip of the land" as an ever-present danger. A missed bearing or a misidentified landmark could mean the difference between clearing a headland and running aground. Conrad's chapter on the "character of the foe" (chapters XXII–XXIV) emphasises that the sea's hostility is compounded near land, where navigational precision matters most.

> The land suddenly at night looms up right over your bows, or perhaps the cry of "Broken water ahead!" is raised, and some long mistake, some complicated edifice of self-delusion, overconfidence, and wrong reasoning is brought down in a fatal shock... (021-002)

The "complicated edifice of self-delusion" that precedes a grounding often begins with a single navigational error—a bearing misread, a landmark misidentified, a position plotted with unjustified confidence.
