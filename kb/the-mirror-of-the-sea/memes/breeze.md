## Definition

A breeze is a steady wind of moderate strength, strong enough to drive a sailing vessel at a good pace without straining her gear or threatening her spars. In the Beaufort scale that came into use during Conrad's seafaring years, the breeze occupies the middle range — force 3 (gentle breeze) through force 6 (strong breeze) — between the frustrating calm and the dangerous gale. It is the ideal sailing wind: the condition that every master hopes for and that Conrad's narrators watch for with the anxious attention of men whose craft and lives depend upon the wind's temper.

For the seaman, the breeze is not an abstract meteorological phenomenon but a physical presence — something felt on the face, heard in the rigging, and read in the pattern of ripples on the water. It is the wind that makes passages, fills sails, and brings ships home. A ship without a breeze is a ship going nowhere; a ship with a fair breeze is a ship in her element.

## The Coming of the Breeze

One of Conrad's most painterly passages describes the moment when the breeze arrives after a calm — a moment of relief and release that every sailor knows:

> The following day still saw them scattered within sight of each other and heading different ways; but when, at last, the breeze came with the darkling ripple that ran very blue on a pale sea, they all went in separate directions.
>
> (010-001)

The "darkling ripple" — a phrase that combines precise observation with poetic compression — is the visible sign of the breeze's arrival. Before the sail feels it, the eye sees it: a darkening of the water's surface, a cat's-paw of ruffled texture moving across the pale sea. The scattered ships, becalmed and drifting, suddenly come alive and "go in separate directions" — each pursuing her own course now that the wind has returned.

## The Steady Breeze

A steady breeze is the seaman's friend. It permits the kind of confident manoeuvring that a shifting or gusty wind makes impossible. Conrad describes a rare moment of audacity by an otherwise cautious captain, made possible precisely because the breeze was steady:

> Once only did he attempt a stroke of audacity, one early morning, with a steady breeze, entering a crowded roadstead.
>
> (009-005)

The phrase "steady breeze" is the key. Had the breeze been fluky or uncertain, the audacious entry into a crowded anchorage would have been recklessness rather than seamanship. The steadiness of the wind underwrites the boldness of the act.

## The Fierce Breeze

Not all breezes are gentle. Conrad's language recognises that a "fierce breeze" — what a landsman might call a strong wind — is still within the breeze category, still sailable, still manageable by a well-found ship:

> And the *Tremolino*, in a fierce breeze and in much smoother water, swung on easily under her one sail, with something appallingly careless in the joyous freedom of her motion.
>
> (044-034)

The paradox is striking: a "fierce breeze" producing "joyous freedom." The *Tremolino* under her single sail is not fighting the wind but dancing with it. The "appallingly careless" quality of her motion — the suggestion that at any moment the dance might become a disaster — only heightens the exhilaration. This is the breeze at its most thrilling: strong enough to test the ship, not so strong as to overwhelm her.

## The Failing Breeze

If the breeze's arrival is a moment of joy, its departure is a moment of anxiety. A breeze that dies leaves a sailing ship becalmed — motionless, helpless, burning through her provisions while the calendar advances:

> I waited for more light; the breeze began to fail on my face.
>
> (036-015)

The brevity of this sentence belies its weight. To feel the breeze "fail on my face" is to receive a warning — the ship will soon lose way, the passage will be delayed, the careful calculations of the log-book will be upset. The face is the seaman's anemometer; what it registers cannot be ignored.

## The Gusty Breeze

Between the steady breeze and the full gale lies the gusty breeze — a wind that comes in irregular puffs, now strong, now slack, keeping the crew in a state of constant alertness:

> The breeze was gusty; a southerly blow was making up.
>
> (044-001)

The laconic report conceals a world of professional anxiety. A gusty breeze is hard on gear — it loads and unloads the rigging unpredictably, strains the sails in irregular pulses, and makes the ship's behaviour difficult to anticipate. And "a southerly blow was making up" is the darker news still: the gusty breeze is not the day's weather but its prelude. Something worse is coming.

## The Breeze as Experience

Throughout *The Mirror of the Sea*, the breeze is not merely described but felt. Conrad's narrators experience the wind physically — on the face, in the ears, through the soles of the feet on the deck. When the young third mate keeps his watch in a freshening breeze, the wind is not an external condition but a participant in the drama:

> I was the junior in her, a third mate, keeping watch with the chief officer; and it was just during one of the night watches in a strong, freshening breeze.
>
> (011-004)

The "strong, freshening breeze" — strengthening as the night wears on — is the context within which every decision of the watch is made. Should sail be shortened? Will the breeze continue to freshen? Is this the beginning of a gale? The answers to these questions determine the safety of the ship, and they must be answered not in the comfort of a wardroom but on a dark, slanting deck, with the wind singing in the rigging and the spray coming over the weather rail.
