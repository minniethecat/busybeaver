A **spar** is any pole of timber or metal that forms part of the rigging structure of a sailing vessel. The term encompasses masts, yards, booms, gaffs, and bowsprits — the entire aerial framework from which sails are set and upon which a sailing ship's motive power depends. In Conrad's imagination, the spars of a ship are its defining feature, the visible expression of its character and ambition.

Conrad repeatedly returns to the image of tall spars as the embodiment of a sailing ship's majesty — and its vulnerability:

> The man who has looked upon his ship going over too far is made aware of the preposterous tallness of a ship's spars. (010-005)

> For what is the array of the strongest ropes, the tallest spars and the stoutest canvas against the mighty breath of the infinite, but thistle stalks, cobwebs and gossamer? (010-007)

This is the central paradox of the spar: it is at once the instrument of human audacity — reaching upward to capture the wind — and the most fragile point of failure when the sea turns hostile. A dismasted ship, stripped of its spars, is for Conrad the most pitiable sight a seaman can behold:

> No seaman can look without compassion upon a disabled ship, but to look at a sailing-vessel with her lofty spars gone is to look upon a defeated but indomitable warrior. (018-009)

The loss of spars in heavy weather marks the boundary between a ship that is fighting and a ship that is merely surviving:

> Ultimately some of the minor spars did go — nothing important: spanker-booms and suchlike — because at times the frightful impetus of her rolling would part a fourfold tackle of new three-inch Manilla line as if it were weaker than pack-thread. (015-010)

In harbour, the spars of laid-up ships create a distinctive landscape — a forest of masts and yards that defines the visual character of a port:

> However tightly moored, they range a little at their berths, swaying imperceptibly the spire-like assemblages of cordage and spars. (032-001)

Their spars dwarfed with their loftiness the corrugated-iron sheds, their jibbooms extended far over the shore. (033-005)

The spar, then, is more than a piece of timber. In Conrad's maritime aesthetic, it is the very measure of a ship — the index of its grace, its power, and ultimately its mortality.
