## Identity

Don Carlos (Carlos María de Borbón, 1848–1909) was the Carlist pretender to the Spanish throne, the figure in whose name the Tremolino syndicate conducted its Mediterranean operations. Conrad never presents him directly — he remains an offstage presence, invoked by the syndicate members as the political justification for their smuggling. The pretender's remoteness from the actual operations of the tartane is, in Conrad's telling, part of the absurd romance of the whole enterprise.

## Conrad's Account

> Don Carlos, no doubt, must have had many queer friends (it is the common lot of all Pretenders), but amongst them none more extravagantly fantastic than the Tremolino Syndicate, which used to meet in a villa on the Prado. (041-006)

> Whether Don Carlos was one of the "old friends," too, it's hard to say. More unlikely things have been heard of in smoking-rooms. All I know is that one evening, entering incautiously the salon of the little house just after the Syndicate had reassembled, I was seized upon by Doña Rita. (042-002)

> We four formed a "syndicate" owning the Tremolino: an international and astonishing syndicate. And we were all ardent Royalists of the snow-white Legitimist complexion — Heaven only knows why! (041-001)

## The Pretender and the Syndicate

The relationship between Don Carlos and his maritime agents was, as Conrad presents it, a study in the politics of lost causes. The Carlist cause had been militarily defeated decades before; the pretender lived in exile; yet a small network of adventurers, idealists, and opportunists continued to act in his name. Conrad's tone is unmistakably ironic — "Heaven only knows why!" — but the irony is affectionate. The Tremolino syndicate's Royalism was as much a framework for adventure as a political conviction, and Don Carlos, remote and unknowable, was the necessary symbol around which the adventure could be organised.
