## Definition

An anchor is a forged piece of iron, admirably adapted to its end — an instrument of faithfulness that connects the ship to the land beneath the sea. In Joseph Conrad's maritime philosophy, the anchor is more than equipment: it is at once the heaviest object a seaman handles at sea and a symbol of hope, the tangible link between the floating world and the solid earth.

## In Conrad's Words

> Before an anchor can ever be raised, it must be let go; and this perfectly obvious truism brings me at once to the subject of the degradation of the sea language in the daily press of this country.
> (004-001)

> An anchor is a forged piece of iron, admirably adapted to its end, and technical language is an instrument wrought into perfection by ages of experience, a flawless thing for its purpose. An anchor of yesterday (because nowadays there are contrivances like mushrooms and things like claws, of no particular expression or shape — just hooks) — an anchor of yesterday is in its way a most efficient instrument. To its perfection its size bears witness, for there is no other appliance or contrivance on board ship that is more exactly proportional to the tonnage of the vessel. For instance, the anchors of a certain ship whose gross tonnage, if I remember rightly, was a little over three hundred, had a weight of 2-1/4 tons. In all its parts it was stretched, forged, and tempered to the strains it would have to bear. Its broad flukes, peculiar to the anchors of yesterday, gave it such a hold on the bottom as no modern mushroom-shaped thing could ever attain.
> (004-003)

> It is not so much that the anchor is a symbol of hope as that it is the heaviest object that he has to handle on board his ship at sea in the usual routine of his duties. The anchor and the land are indissolubly connected in a sailor's thoughts.
> (005-001)

> An anchor is forged and fashioned for faithfulness; give it ground that it can bite, and it will hold till the cable parts, and then, whatever may afterwards befall its ship, that anchor is "lost." The honest, rough piece of iron, so simple in appearance, has parts — the shank, the stock, the crown, the flukes, the ring — and the character of these parts is invariably stamped upon the mind of the seaman by the hardest work of his life — handling heavy weights on a wet and slippery deck, in the dark, in the cold, in the driving spray.
> (004-004)

> For the anchor "to go clear" means to go clear of its own chain. Your anchor must drop from the bow of your ship with no turn of cable on any of its limbs, else you would be riding to a foul anchor. The absolute necessity of this condition is impressed upon the officer in charge by the fact that unless the anchor drops clear, it is of no use.
> (005-007)

## The Anchor as Symbol

Conrad is careful to correct any sentimental view of the anchor's symbolism. His emphasis is on its weight, its physicality, its resistance — the anchor is "the heaviest object that he has to handle on board his ship at sea in the usual routine of his duties." The symbolic dimension is not an abstraction layered on top of the anchor's practical function; it arises from that function. The anchor connects the ship to the land, and in doing so it connects the seaman — a creature of the floating world — to the solidity of earth:

> The anchor and the land are indissolubly connected in a sailor's thoughts. (005-001)

This connection is not sentimental but existential. To raise the anchor is to sever the last link with the known world; to let it go is to re-establish that link after a voyage. The anchor is the mediator between two realms.

## The Art of Letting Go

The act of anchoring is more complex than it appears. Conrad insists upon a precise vocabulary: the anchor must "go clear" — free of its own chain — or it is useless. This apparently simple requirement requires the coordinated effort of the mate on the forecastle, the crew at the windlass, and the vessel herself:

> For the anchor "to go clear" means to go clear of its own chain. Your anchor must drop from the bow of your ship with no turn of cable on any of its limbs, else you would be riding to a foul anchor. (005-007)

The phrase "ridden to a foul anchor" invokes disaster — a ship that cannot hold its ground, drifting in a gale towards a lee shore, because of a single turn of chain around a fluke. The anchor's faithfulness is conditional upon the skill of those who let it go.

## The Faithful Instrument

The anchor's defining quality, in Conrad's lexicon, is faithfulness — a moral term applied to a piece of iron. This is not mere personification but a statement about the anchor's design: it is "forged and fashioned for faithfulness," its shape dictated entirely by the demand that it must hold:

> An anchor is forged and fashioned for faithfulness; give it ground that it can bite, and it will hold till the cable parts, and then, whatever may afterwards befall its ship, that anchor is "lost." (004-004)

Note the quotation marks around "lost" — the anchor is not destroyed but abandoned, left on the sea-floor. Its faithfulness is absolute: it holds until the very cable that connects it to the ship breaks. The anchor does not fail; it is the ship, or the cable, or the ground that fails.

## The Anatomy of the Anchor

The anchor of Conrad's era — the "Admiralty pattern" anchor — was composed of distinct parts, each with a name that the seaman learned through the hardest labour of his life:

> The honest, rough piece of iron, so simple in appearance, has parts — the shank, the stock, the crown, the flukes, the ring — and the character of these parts is invariably stamped upon the mind of the seaman by the hardest work of his life — handling heavy weights on a wet and slippery deck, in the dark, in the cold, in the driving spray. (004-004)

The conditions listed — wet deck, darkness, cold, driving spray — are the usual circumstances of anchoring. The anchor is never handled in comfort. Its "character" — the specific heft and balance of each part — is learned through discomfort.

## Technical Usage

> The anchor is the symbol of hope to a shipmaster approaching the land; the first intimation of nearing the invisible shore is the order to prepare the ground-tackle. (005-002)

In Conrad's era (ca. 1870–1900), the anchor was a heavy forged-iron device with a shank, stock, crown, flukes, and ring — the so-called "Admiralty pattern" or "anchor of yesterday." By Conrad's time, newer designs such as stockless anchors (the "mushrooms and things like claws" he derides) were beginning to appear, but the traditional anchor remained the standard. The traditional phrase is that a ship is "brought up" to an anchor — never "casts" her anchor, a journalistic solecism that Conrad excoriates at length. Anchors were proportioned to the vessel's tonnage, with even modest ships carrying anchors weighing over two tons. The operation of anchoring required the coordinated labour of the crew at the windlass, the anchor being let go from the bow under the mate's supervision.
