The **windlass** is a mechanical winch mounted on the [[forecastle]] deck, used to raise and lower the [[anchor]] cable. On the merchant sailing ships of the nineteenth century, it was operated by the [[crew]] hauling on bars inserted into a barrel, turning it with collective muscle power. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, the windlass holds a privileged place as the source of the most characteristic sound of a ship departing or arriving.

## The sound of departure

For Conrad, the windlass is primarily an acoustic presence. He opens Chapter VI with a declaration: "There is no order more noisily given or taken up with lustier shouts on board a homeward-bound merchant ship than the command, 'Man the windlass!'" (006-002). The clanking of the windlass — the sound of the anchor cable being heaved in — is the ship's awakening, the noise that "made a tremendous contrast to the silent moments of her arrival" (006-003).

> There is no order more noisily given or taken up with lustier shouts on board a homeward-bound merchant ship than the command, "Man the windlass!" (006-002)

The windlass was disconnected and the compressors opened in preparation for anchoring; these were among the final acts before the anchor was let go and the ship came to rest. The windlass thus bookended every passage — its noise marked both departure and the preparations for arrival.

## Windlasses in the Downs

Perhaps the most striking windlass image in *The Mirror of the Sea* appears in Conrad's description of the [[Downs]], the great anchorage off the Kentish coast. At the first breath of an approaching gale, he writes, "I have heard upwards of ten score of windlasses spring like one into clanking life in the dead of night, filling the Downs with a panic-struck sound of anchors being torn hurriedly out of the ground" (029-002). Two hundred windlasses, all clanking simultaneously in the darkness — a chorus of ships fleeing for sea room before the wind struck.

> I have heard upwards of ten score of windlasses spring like one into clanking life in the dead of night, filling the Downs with a panic-struck sound of anchors being torn hurriedly out of the ground at the first breath of his approach. (029-002)

## The anchoring ritual

The windlass is the last piece of equipment the [[chief-mate]] inspects before the anchor is let go. Standing forward, he surveys the full anchoring system — the cable ranged and ready, the compressors opened, all prepared for the decisive moment when the ship commits herself to the bottom.

> There he sees the cable ranged, the windlass disconnected, the compressors opened; and there, after giving his own last order, "Stand clear of the cable!" (005-006)

This scene, from the chapter on anchors, positions the windlass within a larger system of seamanship: [[cable]], compressor, and anchor are all linked in the sequence of commands that brings a ship to rest. The windlass is not a tool used in isolation but a node in a coordinated ritual involving the full [[watch]].

## The rhythm of command

The windlass scene follows a ritual pattern in Conrad's narrative. The captain's curt order — "Man the windlass!" — breaks a silence that may have lasted for days, and the crew springs to the bars. It is the moment when the ship transitions from rest to action, from the land to the sea. Conrad notes how "for days his voice will not be heard raised about the decks ... till, again, when the hatches are on, and in a silent and expectant ship, he shall speak up from aft in commanding tones: 'Man the windlass!'" (006-005).

The cycle is complete: the windlass at departure, the windlass at arrival, the windlass in the Downs in the dead of night — each time its clanking marks a threshold. No other piece of ship's equipment carries such symbolic weight in Conrad's maritime world. It is, quite literally, the voice of the ship coming to life.
