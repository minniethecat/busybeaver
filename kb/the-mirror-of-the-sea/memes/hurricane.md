## Definition

A hurricane is a tropical cyclone of extreme intensity, characterised by winds of great violence revolving around a central eye. To the nineteenth-century seaman, the hurricane was the most dangerous weather phenomenon afloat — unpredictable in its precise track, capable of dismasting the stoutest ship, and preceded by a cloud of a distinctive olive hue that experienced mariners learned to recognise with dread.

## The Olive Cloud

Conrad's description of the approaching hurricane cloud is one of the most atmospheric passages in *The Mirror of the Sea*, capturing the peculiar, almost supernatural quality of the light that heralds the storm:

> The olive hue of hurricane clouds presents an aspect peculiarly appalling.
>
> (024-008)

The sentence is characteristically compressed. "Peculiarly appalling" — the adverb does not weaken the adjective but sharpens it. The horror of the hurricane is not merely its violence but its *peculiarity*, its otherness, as though the storm came from a different world with different colours and different laws.

## The Mournful Groan

Conrad distinguishes the hurricane from ordinary gales by its sound — not the roar of wind but something deeper, a voice that seems to come from beneath the world:

> There is infinite variety in the gales of wind at sea, and except for the peculiar, terrible, and mysterious moaning that may be heard sometimes passing through the roar of a hurricane — except for that unforgettable sound, as if the soul of the universe had been goaded into a mournful groan — it is, after all, very much the same in sensation as in appearance.
>
> (024-009)

The anthropomorphism here reaches its limit. The wind is no longer a king or a poet but the soul of the universe itself, *goaded* into utterance. The word "goaded" is violent — something has provoked the cosmos into a cry of pain. And what the seaman hears, passing through the hurricane's roar, is that cosmic groan: the sound of a wounded world.

## The Human Voice in the Storm

In the same passage, Conrad makes a surprising turn. What distinguishes one gale from another, he argues, is not the wind's sound but the human reaction to it — the shouts, the commands, the quality of fear in men's voices:

> It is, after all, the human voice that stamps the mark of human consciousness upon the character of a gale.
>
> (024-009)

The hurricane is a test not merely of ships but of men. The quality of a captain's voice in the storm, the steadiness or tremor of his commands — these are the true measure of the gale's character. The hurricane strips away pretence and reveals what a man is.

## Practical Consequences

The practical consequences of a hurricane at sea were stark — dismasting, leaking, and weeks of desperate pumping. Conrad records the fate of a ship overtaken by one:

> They had lost their masts and sprung a leak in a hurricane; drifted for weeks, always at the pumps, met more bad weather; the ships they sighted failed to render assistance.
>
> (036-013)

The accumulation of disasters — "drifted for weeks, always at the pumps" — captures the relentless aftermath of a hurricane encounter. The storm itself might last hours; its consequences could last weeks. The phrase "always at the pumps" is a whole world of exhaustion in four words.

## The Cosmic Game

In his most expansive vision of the West Wind's power, Conrad imagines hurricanes as playthings — quoits tossed by a barbarian king across oceans:

> He may well go on playing his royal game of quoits with hurricanes, tossing them over from the continent of republics to the continent of kingdoms, in the assurance that both the new republics and the old kingdoms, the heat of fire and the strength of iron, with the untold generations of audacious men, shall crumble to dust at the steps of his throne, and pass away, and be forgotten before his own rule comes to an end.
>
> (029-006)

The image is magnificent and terrible. Hurricanes are *quoits* — a game — thrown from one continent to another by a king who plays with civilisations as a man might toss rings at a fair. Republics and kingdoms, fire and iron, generations of audacious men — all "shall crumble to dust at the steps of his throne." The hurricane here is not merely a storm but a cosmological argument: against the wind's eternity, all human effort is vanishingly brief.
