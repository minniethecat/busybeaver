A **skylight** on board a ship is a raised framework fitted with glass panels, built over an opening in the deck to admit daylight into the interior compartments below. On sailing vessels, the skylight over the saloon or the captain's cabin was often a point of pride — elaborately fitted with polished brass and sometimes bearing coloured glass panels with the shipbuilder's motto or the vessel's name.

Conrad's most vivid skylight belongs to a Clyde-built ship, whose coloured-glass ends bore a civic inscription:

> The spars rigged up on the narrow hulls were indeed tall then, and the ship of which I think, with her coloured-glass skylight ends bearing the motto, "Let Glasgow Flourish," was certainly one of the finest vessels afloat. (011-004)

The skylight here is more than a source of light — it is a signature, a declaration of origin and pride connecting the ship to the city that built her. Even at sea, the skylight whispered "Glasgow" to those who sat beneath it.

In heavy weather, however, the skylight became a point of vulnerability. A boarding sea could smash the glass and flood the quarters below, turning the most refined space on the ship into a sodden wreck:

> But I gave it up, and crawled into my bunk instead, boots and hat on, all standing (it did not matter; everything was soaking wet, a heavy sea having burst the poop skylights the night before), to remain in a nightmarish state between sleeping and waking. (027-003)

The burst skylight marks the collapse of the boundary between the ordered interior world of the ship and the chaos outside. When the sea comes through the skylight, no space on board remains truly safe.

In moments of tension, the skylight serves as a stage prop — a surface upon which instruments are set down, a place where officers pause to collect themselves:

> He put down the glasses on the skylight brusquely, and began to walk about the poop. (036-004)

The skylight, then, is both a practical fitting and a symbolic threshold — the fragile membrane of glass and brass that separates the domestic from the elemental, civilisation from the sea.
