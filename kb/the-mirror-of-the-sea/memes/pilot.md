## Definition

A pilot is a mariner with specialised knowledge of a particular port, river, or coastal stretch, who boards an incoming or outgoing vessel to guide her safely through hazardous waters. The pilot represents local knowledge against the general competence of the ship's officers — he knows the depths over every bar, the set of every current, the position of every rock, and the idiosyncrasies of every channel. Conrad treats the pilot with respect as the guardian of the threshold between the open sea and the land — the figure who appears at the beginning and end of every passage.

## In Conrad's Words

> Every passage of a ship of yesterday, whose yards were braced round eagerly the very moment the pilot, with his pockets full of letters, had got over the side, was like a race — a race against time, against an ideal standard of achievement outstripping the expectations of common men. (009-001)

> The taking of Departure, if not the last sight of the land, is, perhaps, the last professional recognition of the land on the part of a sailor. It is the technical, as distinguished from the sentimental, "good-bye." Henceforth he has done with the coast astern of his ship. (001-004)

> The first approach to the land, as yet invisible to the crew's eyes, is announced by the brisk order of the chief mate to the boatswain: "We will get the anchors over this afternoon" or "first thing tomorrow morning," as the case may be. (005-002)

## The Pilot's Authority

The pilot occupies a unique position in the hierarchy of the ship. While the captain retains ultimate command, the pilot has effective authority over navigation in his waters. He gives orders directly to the helmsman, and even the captain will not countermand a pilot's order in the channel without grave cause. This temporary transfer of authority reflects the primacy of local knowledge over general competence — a principle that Conrad, with his deep respect for the particular and the experiential, regards as self-evident. The pilot's knowledge cannot be learned from books or charts; it is acquired only through years of repeated passage through the same waters.

## The Pilot as Threshold Figure

The pilot appears at two critical junctures: when the ship approaches the land and prepares to anchor, and when she weighs anchor and prepares to depart. In both cases, the pilot mediates the transition between the open sea and the confined waters of port, river, or channel. When the pilot departs — "with his pockets full of letters" to be posted ashore — the ship is committed to the ocean passage. His departure is, in a sense, the true beginning of the voyage.
