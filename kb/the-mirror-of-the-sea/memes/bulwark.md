A **bulwark** is the extension of a vessel's side above the level of the weather deck, forming a protective barrier against the sea. In sailing ships, the bulwark is a vital structural element that shields the deck and crew from boarding seas, whilst also serving as a mounting point for stanchions, railings, and rigging components.

In Conrad's narratives, the bulwark frequently appears as the boundary between the human world of the deck and the indifferent violence of the ocean. It is both a physical shelter and a psychological threshold — the last solid thing between a seaman and the sea.

> The solemn thundering combers caught her up from astern, passed her with a fierce boiling up of foam level with the bulwarks, swept on ahead with a swish and a roar: and the little vessel, dipping her (023-005)

> Her bulwarks were gone fore and aft, and one saw her bare deck low-lying like a raft and swept clean of boats, spars, houses—of everything except the ringbolts and the heads of the pumps. (036-011)

The loss of the bulwarks, as described in the second passage, is a mark of extreme storm damage — a ship stripped to its bare deck, rendered defenceless against the sea. The bulwark, though modest in height, represents the margin of safety that separates a working vessel from a helpless wreck.

In calmer moments, the bulwark serves a more contemplative purpose:

> For a long time, leaning against the bulwark, I gazed over the stern at the grey column that seemed to stand swaying slightly in our wake always at the same distance. (044-009)

> I crouched down again to get some shelter from the low bulwark. (044-006)

The bulwark thus functions in Conrad's prose as a locus of observation and refuge — the place where a seaman pauses to watch the sea, to take shelter, or simply to be alone with his thoughts whilst still on deck.

Even in the social life of the ship, the bulwark serves as an acoustic boundary:

> They got down from their perches and told each other impolite stories in racy language, every word of which reached me distinctly over the bulwarks as I sat smoking on the main-hatch. (034-002)

Sound carries over the bulwark, connecting the forecastle with the after-deck, the ordinary seamen with the officers. The bulwark divides but does not isolate — it is a permeable boundary in the small floating world of a ship.
