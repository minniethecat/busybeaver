A **knot** is a unit of speed used in maritime navigation, equal to one nautical mile per hour (approximately 1.852 kilometres per hour). The term derives from the historical practice of measuring a ship's speed using a chip log — a wooden board attached to a line with knots tied at regular intervals, paid out over the [[stern]] while counting the knots with a sandglass.

In Conrad's narratives, the knot is the constant, quiet measure of a ship's progress — or peril. Speed in knots appears most frequently at moments of tension: running before a gale, rushing towards a [[lee-shore]], or pushing a ship to her limit.

## 速度与危险

The knot marks the narrowing margin between safety and disaster. With only "a couple of miles" of sea-room remaining, twelve knots towards a lee shore is a countdown to catastrophe. The mate and master gaze ahead, while beneath their feet the ship rushes forward — every knot bringing the invisible land closer.

> Thus would the mate's voice repeat the thought of the master, both gazing ahead, while under their feet the ship rushes at some twelve knots in the direction of the lee shore; and only a couple of miles in front of her swinging and dripping jibboom, carried naked with an upward slant like a spear, a grey horizon closes the view with a multitude of waves surging upwards violently as if to strike at the stooping clouds. (026-010)

This instinctive awareness of speed — the "ever-present thought associated closely with the consciousness of the land somewhere ahead and of the great speed of the ship" (026-008) — is the seaman's constant companion in foul weather. The knot is not merely a measurement; it is a warning.

## 判断与骄傲

A ship's speed is a matter of professional pride and judgment. The young officer in Conrad's anecdote defends his decision to keep the ship driving at eleven knots — a speed that tests the limits of prudence.

> "Well, sir," I said in an apologetic tone, "she was going eleven knots very nicely, and I thought she would do for another half-hour or so." (012-012)

Even in the worst conditions, with decks full of water and gear flying loose, a ship could still run at ten knots — a testament to the terrifying power of a storm.

> And, wallowing as if she meant to turn over with us, the barque, her decks full of water, her gear flying in bights, ran at some ten knots an hour. (017-005)

Speed under sail, Conrad observes, was never a simple matter of hull design. It was an art: the [[seaman]]'s skill in trimming the ship, the careful balance of sail and ballast, the thousands of small adjustments that together yielded an extra knot.

> In those days the speed depended upon the seaman; therefore, apart from the laws, rules, and regulations for the good preservation of his cargo, he was careful of his loading — or what is technically called the trim of his ship. (014-001)

A mysteriousness, Conrad writes, "hangs around the quality of speed as it was displayed by the old sailing-ships commanded by a competent seaman" (014-001). The knot, in this sense, is the signature of the commander — each fraction of speed a measure of his understanding of wind, sea, and the ship beneath his feet.

## 风中的推进

The force of the wind itself is measured against the knot. Running before a gale at ten knots, the wind is so powerful that it physically drives a man across the deck:

> The force of the wind, though we were running before it at the rate of some ten knots an hour, was so great that it drove me with a steady push to the front of the poop. (027-004)

The contrast is instructive: the ship makes ten knots — a respectable clip — yet the same wind that drives her forward is strong enough to pin a man against the rail. The knot here becomes the measure not only of progress but of elemental force.

## 比喻的延伸

Beyond its technical meaning, Conrad also uses "knot" figuratively to describe a small, tight group of men bound together by shared hardship — "a small knot of men upon the great loneliness of the sea."

> A tale of difficulties overcome, of adversity defied by a small knot of men upon the great loneliness of the sea. (018-007)

This dual meaning — the technical unit of speed and the metaphorical gathering of humanity — enriches the word's presence throughout *The Mirror of the Sea*. Whether a ship is making twelve knots towards disaster or a knot of seamen is huddled together against the vastness, the image carries the same weight: concentration, intensity, and the thin line between control and chaos.
