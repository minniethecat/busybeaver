## Definition

Cargo is the *raison d'être* of the merchant ship — the commercial burden that gives purpose to every passage and defines the rhythm of a vessel's life. Conrad regards cargo not as inert freight but as an active presence that shapes the ship's behaviour at sea and the seaman's relationship with his craft. The loading and unloading of cargo mark the two poles of the seaman's working existence: "the beginning and the end of every passage," as much as the anchors themselves.

## In Conrad's Words

> A ship anchored in an open roadstead, with cargo-lighters alongside and her own tackle swinging the burden over the rail, is accomplishing in freedom a function of her life. There is no restraint; there is space: clear water around her, and a clear sky above her mastheads. (033-004)

> The sailing-ship, when I knew her in her days of perfection, was a sensible creature. When I say her days of perfection, I mean perfection of build, gear, seaworthy qualities and ease of handling, not perfection of speed. That quality has only developed with the steam-engine, and in proportion to the development of the carrying trade. (014-001)

> We men are, in fact, the servants of our creations. We remain in everlasting bondage to the productions of our brain and to the work of our hands. A man is born to serve his time on this earth, and there is something of a sacred character in the duty of service. (007-007)

## The Rhythm of Cargo

In Conrad's world, cargo imposes a cyclical rhythm on the seaman's life. A ship loads at one port, carries her burden across the ocean, discharges at another, and loads again. This cycle — load, sail, discharge, sail — is the fundamental pattern of merchant seafaring. The loading itself is a skilled operation: the proper stowage of cargo affects the ship's trim, stability, and behaviour at sea. A badly stowed cargo can make a ship crank and dangerous; a well-stowed one allows her to "find herself" in a seaway.

## Cargo and the Ship's Character

Conrad draws a connection between cargo and the evolution of ship design. The sailing-ship reached her "days of perfection" as a balanced synthesis of carrying capacity and sailing qualities. The steam-engine, he observes, shifted the emphasis toward pure carrying capacity — the "carrying trade" became paramount, and the ship's character as a "sensible creature" began to fade. This is part of Conrad's broader elegy for the sailing-ship era: the replacement of art by commerce, of the fine balance by the brute calculation of tonnage.

## The Seaman's Attitude

For all its commercial necessity, Conrad's seamen do not despise cargo. It is the substance of their livelihood and the justification of their skill. The handling of cargo — the swing of the tackle, the thud of bales on deck, the gradual settling of the ship on her marks as she takes on weight — is part of the craft's language. A seaman who knows his ship knows how she will carry her burden.
