# Tides

The **tide** is the regular rise and fall of the sea, driven by the gravitational pull of the moon and sun. In Conrad's maritime world, the tide is not merely a natural phenomenon — it is the governing rhythm of every port, every departure, and every homecoming. "The turn of every tide" determines when ships sail and when they must wait; it is a force that the mariner reckons with daily, whether in the crowded Pool of London or in open water.

## The Thames Tide

The River Thames is a tidal river, and its pulse shapes the life of the Port of London. The salt tide rushes past Gravesend, carrying the sea's freedom inland before yielding to the demands of commerce:

> The waters of the sea rush on past Gravesend, tumbling the big mooring buoys laid along the face of the town; but the sea-freedom stops short there, surrendering the salt tide to the needs, the artifices, the contrivances of toiling men. (031-004)

Above London Bridge, in the days of sail, the Pool was a dense forest of masts. Vessels moored "stem and stern in the very strength of the tide" formed "one solid mass like an island covered with a forest of gaunt, leafless trees" (032-006). The tide held these ships in place, but it was a precarious arrangement — a vessel that dragged its anchor in such crowded waters invited disaster.

The tidal rhythm governed the docks as well. At tide-time, loaded ships would slip their moorings:

> At tide-time you would see one of the loaded ships with battened-down hatches drop out of the ranks and float in the clear space of the dock, held by lines dark and slender, like the first threads of a spider's web, extending from her bows and her quarters to the mooring-posts on shore. (033-006)

The phrase "tide-time" captures the precision with which the tidal cycle structured port operations — everything waited on the water, and the water kept its own schedule.

## Flood and Ebb

The flood-tide and the ebb-tide define the two great motions of coastal navigation. In the Thames estuary, the incoming tide hurries a ship upriver with a force that seems almost alive:

> The rush of the yellow flood-tide hurries her up as if into the unknown between the two fading lines of the coast. (030-003)

The ebb carries her back to sea with equal insistence. This cycle — flood bringing ships to port, ebb releasing them to the open ocean — is the heartbeat of maritime commerce. Every departure from London is timed to the tide: the great mercantile fleet that the city sends forth goes out "upon the turn of every tide" (031-002).

For the seaman, joining a ship is inseparable from the tidal schedule. When a man signs on, the agreement includes the tide that will carry him away: "'We sign on Friday, and join next day for the morning tide'" (035-008). The phrase is a capsule of the working seaman's life — a life governed not by the clock but by the water.

## Dangers of the Tideway

The tide is not always benign. "Treacherous tides" (021-004) can drive a ship onto a lee shore, and even the most careful seamanship can be defeated by a moment's inattention to the set of the current. A vessel in a tideway demands constant vigilance — "want of careful tending in a tideway" (035-010) can undo the best preparations. Ships swing to "winds and tide" (006-001), and the prudent mariner reckons both together, for the interaction of wind against tide can produce dangerous, steep seas.

The tide's force is also visible in the great mooring buoys off Gravesend, "tumbling" under the relentless push of the water (031-004). These buoys, designed to secure the largest vessels, are themselves subject to the tide's power — a reminder that nothing moored in tidal water is ever truly at rest.

## The Anchor and the Tide

The ships' anchor is the seaman's chief defence against the tide, and the chief mate — who oversees the anchors — lives in constant awareness of the tidal grip on his vessel:

> It is difficult to disconnect the idea of ships' anchors from the idea of the ship's chief mate — the man who sees them go down clear and come up sometimes foul; because not even the most unremitting care can always prevent a ship, swinging to winds and tide, from taking an awkward turn of the cable round stock or fluke. (006-001)

The phrase "swinging to winds and tide" encapsulates the anchor watch's central anxiety: a change in the tide's direction can cause the ship to ride over her own cable, fouling the anchor and leaving the vessel helpless.

## The Tideless Mediterranean

Conrad draws a sharp contrast between the tidal waters of northern Europe and the Mediterranean Sea. The Mediterranean, he writes, is "that tideless basin freed from hidden shoals and treacherous currents" (038-004) — a sea where the first impulse of navigation found its nursery, precisely because it was kinder to the infancy of the art of sailing. Without the complication of tides, early mariners could learn the fundamentals of steering, sail-trim, and coastal pilotage in relatively forgiving conditions.

This contrast is not merely geographical but historical. The tideless Mediterranean nurtured the ancient triremes and galleys that first ventured beyond sight of land; the tidal Atlantic demanded a hardier breed of seamanship, mastered over centuries by the northern European maritime nations.

## A Seaman's Understanding

To Conrad's seamen, the tide is not an abstract scientific fact but a visceral presence — something felt through the deck, seen in the swirl of water around a mooring buoy, heard in the rush of the flood past a ship's hull. It is the medium of their working lives, the force that carries them out and brings them home, and the constant reminder that the sea, for all its apparent stillness, is never at rest.
