The **mate** in Conrad's maritime world is not a single rank but a tier of shipboard officers — chief mate, second mate, third mate — who together constitute the afterguard that runs the ship under the master. Conrad elevates the chief mate in particular to a figure of near-mythic status: the man who "keeps" the anchor, who comes bustling forward at every cry of "all hands," who embodies the practical day-to-day authority without which no ship could sail.

## The keeper of the anchor

For Conrad, the chief mate and the ship's anchor are inseparable — an association so profound that "it is difficult to disconnect the idea of ships' anchors from the idea of the ship's chief mate" (006-001). The mate is "the keeper of the ship's anchors and the guardian of her cable" (005-002). This is not a ceremonial title; the mate personally oversees every anchoring operation, every recovery, every securing of the great irons below.

> It is difficult to disconnect the idea of ships' anchors from the idea of the ship's chief mate—the man who sees them go down clear and come up sometimes foul. (006-001)

> The first approach to the land, as yet invisible to the crew's eyes, is announced by the brisk order of the chief mate to the boatswain. For the chief mate is the keeper of the ship's anchors and the guardian of her cable. (005-002)

## The indispensability of the mate

Conrad conveys the mate's importance through negation: a ship can endure even a bad master, but when "things do go wrong" it is invariably the mate who must make them right. As Conrad says with the conviction of long experience, "it is in the character of the chief mate that the ship feels the moral effect of things gone wrong" (005-005). The mate is the ship's steady hand, the one who "comes bustling forward at the cry of 'All hands on deck'" (005-005).

> But whatever the ship—good or bad, lucky or unlucky—it is in the character of the chief mate that the ship feels the moral effect of things gone wrong. (005-005)

> It is the chief mate, the only figure of the ship's afterguard, who comes bustling forward at the cry of "All hands on deck." (005-005)

## A mate's standing

Conrad himself served as chief mate and understood the precise gradations of officer rank. Of his first voyage as chief mate under Captain MacW—— he writes with evident pride: "I remember that I felt quite flattered, and went blithely about my duties" (002-003). He also records the highest compliment a captain can pay a second mate at voyage's end — a moment of wordless acknowledgment that meant more to a young officer than any formal praise.

> On my first voyage as chief mate with good Captain MacW—— I remember that I felt quite flattered, and went blithely about my duties. (002-003)
