## Definition

A lee shore is a coastline lying to leeward of a ship--the direction towards which the wind is driving her. In maritime practice it represents the most fundamental danger a sailing vessel can face: the risk of being driven onto the land by wind and sea, with no room to work off. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad personifies the Easterly Wind as the malevolent force that blinds a seaman and drives him towards the lee shore, making it the embodiment of a mariner's deepest dread.

## The Easterly Gale and the Lee Shore Inevitable

For Conrad, the lee shore is not merely a navigational hazard; it is a condition of spiritual extremity. The Easterly Wind, when it hardens into a gale, does not merely threaten the ship--it assaults the seaman's very capacity to perceive. "It puts your eyes out, puts them out completely," Conrad writes, "makes you feel blind for life upon a lee-shore." The phrase "blind for life" is extraordinary: it suggests not a temporary loss of vision but a permanent condition, as if the experience of being driven onto a lee shore in an Easterly gale leaves a mark that never fully heals.

> The West Wind hangs heavy grey curtains of mist and spray before your gaze, but the Eastern interloper of the narrow seas, when he has mustered his courage and cruelty to the point of a gale, puts your eyes out, puts them out completely, makes you feel blind for life upon a lee-shore. It is the wind, also, that brings snow. (029-001)

Conrad's contrast between the West Wind and the East Wind is instructive. The West Wind obscures; it "hangs heavy grey curtains." But the East Wind actively blinds--it is an "interloper" that "puts your eyes out." The lee shore is the fixed point towards which this blinding wind drives, making it the convergence of meteorological violence and geographical finality.

## The Lee Shore in the Narrow Seas

The narrow seas--the English Channel and the southern North Sea--are peculiarly dangerous for lee-shore scenarios. Here the land is always close, the shipping lanes are crowded, and an Easterly gale can drive a ship onto the Goodwin Sands or the Thames Estuary with terrifying speed. Conrad's evocation of the lee shore is grounded in these waters, where the margin between safety and disaster is measured in miles, and where the wind can shift from ally to enemy in a watch.

## The Lee Shore and the Commander's Anxiety

The lee shore haunts the commander's consciousness even when it is not present. In the chapter "Rulers of East and West," Conrad describes how the ship rushes at twelve knots "in the direction of the lee shore" while the master and mate gaze ahead, their voices repeating the same thought. The lee shore here is both real--a physical coast ahead--and psychological: the fixed limit of possibility towards which every decision tends.

> Thus would the mate's voice repeat the thought of the master, both gazing ahead, while under their feet the ship rushes at some twelve knots in the direction of the lee shore; and only a couple of miles in front of her swinging and dripping jibboom, carried naked with an upward slant like a spear, a grey horizon closes the view with a multitude of waves surging upwards violently as if to strike at the stooping clouds. (029-003)

## The Lee Shore in Sailing Tactics

Conrad extends the concept of the lee shore beyond navigation into naval tactics. In the closing chapters of the memoir, he reflects on Lord Nelson's tactical revolution: the abandonment of the fear of being "thrown hopelessly to leeward" that had governed fleet tactics for centuries. Nelson's genius was to disregard this ancient dread and concentrate instead on "a crushing superiority of fire." The lee shore, in this analysis, is not merely a geographical danger but a conceptual one--a fear so deeply embedded in the sailing commander's mind that it constrained tactical imagination. Nelson broke free of it, and in doing so became, for Conrad, "the first of the moderns."

> The fleet tactics of the sailing days have been governed by two points: the deadly nature of a raking fire, and the dread, natural to a commander dependent upon the winds, to find at some crucial moment part of his fleet thrown hopelessly to leeward. These two points were of the very essence of sailing tactics, and these two points have been eliminated from the modern tactical problem by the changes of propulsion and armament. Lord Nelson was the first to disregard them with conviction and audacity sustained by an unbounded trust in the men he led. (046-005)

## The Sudden Gale

The lee-shore danger is compounded by the suddenness with which a gale can strike. Conrad recalls one gale that came "like a pampero"--a very sudden wind indeed--before the crew knew what was coming. All the sails set had burst, furled sails were blowing loose, and the ship was lying on her side with half the crew swimming and the other half clawing desperately at whatever came to hand. The captain, overwhelmed by the magnitude of his mistake, stood silent at the break of the poop. The lee shore in such moments is the unspoken destination towards which all this chaos tends.

> That one was a gale that came upon the ship swiftly, like a pampero, which last is a very sudden wind indeed. Before we knew very well what was coming all the sails we had set had burst; the furled ones were blowing loose, ropes flying, sea hissing--it hissed tremendously--wind howling, and the ship lying on her side... (027-002)
