## Definition

The lookout is the seaman's duty of visual surveillance — watching for other ships, navigational hazards, and signs of land or weather. In Conrad's world, the lookout man stood forward on the deck, often near the [[anchor]]s, keeping company with "those emblems of hope" through the night watches. The position was one of solitary vigilance, where a man's attention stood between the ship and disaster.

## In Conrad's Words

> I kept a lookout. (036-015)

> Thus bound, but carefully looked after, inert and powerful, those emblems of hope make company for the lookout man in the night watches; and so the days glide by, with a long rest for those characteristically shaped pieces of iron, reposing forward, visible from almost every part of the ship's deck. (005-001)

## Related Concepts

The lookout is an essential function of the [[watch]] system, typically performed from the forward part of the ship near the [[anchor]] and [[windlass]]. The call of the lookout — reporting what he sees ahead or "broad on the [[bow]]" (006-001) — is the voice that alerts the ship to danger.
