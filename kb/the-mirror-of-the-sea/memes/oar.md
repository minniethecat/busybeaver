An **oar** is a long pole with a flattened blade at one end, used to propel and steer a small boat through the water by levering against the water's resistance. In Conrad's maritime world, the oar represents the most primitive and direct form of human engagement with the sea — muscle against water, skill against current.

Conrad uses the oar as a narrative marker of crisis. When sailors take to the oars, it is usually because something has gone wrong: a ship has foundered, a rescue is underway, or a hasty escape is in progress. The measured stroke of oars punctuates moments of high tension.

> We pulled hard for a quarter of an hour, then laid on our oars waiting for our ship. (036-013)

The crew's discipline with the oar is shown as instinctive — a reflex born of long experience. When danger looms, they respond without needing an order.

> We pulled an easy stroke; but when the master of the brig, after a glance over his shoulder, stood up with a low exclamation, my men feathered their oars instinctively, without an order, and the boat lost her way. (036-020)

In the aftermath of a shipwreck, the absence of any floating wreckage — no branded oar among the debris — deepens the mystery of a vessel's disappearance.

> Nothing of her ever comes to light — no grating, no lifebuoy, no piece of boat or branded oar — to give a hint of the place and date of her sudden end. (017-001)

## The Oar's Race With Death

Conrad elevates the oar-pulled boat to a contest in which the stakes are absolute. In his account of a rescue at sea, he frames the pulling race in stark, existential terms:

> At the finish there was not an oar's length between the first and second boat, with Death coming in a good third on the top of the very next smooth swell, for all one knew to the contrary. (036-011)

The image of Death as a competitor in a boat race — gaining on the rescuers with every stroke — distills the maritime reality that the difference between life and drowning can be measured in oar-lengths.

## Dominic's Oar: The Inland Journey

The oar takes on an almost mythic quality in the final episode of the *Tremolino*, where [[Dominic Cervoni]] carries an oar inland — a seaman's tool absurdly out of place on dry land, a visual emblem of exile from the sea. After the wreck of the tartane, Dominic's first act ashore is to arm himself with a boat's oar:

> Dominic took one of the boat's oars with him. (045-017)

He plants it in the ground as he confronts the narrator with a dramatic gesture, and later uses it to punt across a stream:

> He planted the oar upright in the soft soil, and rolling up slowly his right sleeve, extended the bare arm before my face. (045-020)

> He pulled the oar out of the ground and helped me carefully down the slope. He punted us over, then shouldered the oar again and waited till our men were at some distance before he offered me his arm. (045-028)

The image that lingers longest is the final one — Dominic walking away into the barren landscape with the oar on his shoulder:

> Unable to make a sound, I gazed after the strangely desolate figure of that seaman carrying an oar on his shoulder up a barren, rock-strewn ravine under the dreary leaden sky of Tremolino's last day. (045-032)

Conrad explicitly links this image to classical mythology, comparing Dominic to Ulysses, who was told by the shade of Tiresias that he would one day journey inland carrying an oar — a symbol of final separation from the sea.

> And Dominic Cervoni takes his place in my memory by the side of the legendary wanderer on the sea of marvels and terrors, by the side of the fatal and impious adventurer, to whom the evoked shade of the soothsayer predicted a journey inland with an oar on his shoulder. (045-033)
