# North Atlantic

The **North Atlantic** is, in Conrad's maritime geography, the heart of the [[west-wind|West Wind]]'s empire — an ocean "most thickly populated with generations of fine ships and hardy men" and the theatre of heroic deeds, adventurous exploits, and the daily toil of the world's merchant fleets. It extends from the latitudes of the Azores northward to Cape Farewell and beyond, and from the approaches to the [[english-channel|English Channel]] westward to the shores of North America. In Conrad's cosmology of wind and water, this ocean is not mere geography: it is the throne-room of a sovereign, the proving-ground of seamen, and the stage upon which the elemental drama of East and West is played out without end.

## Conrad's Description

### The West Wind Enthroned

The West Wind governs the North Atlantic as an absolute sovereign. Conrad opens his portrait of this ruler with an image of almost biblical majesty — the wind enthroned upon the western horizon, the entire ocean spread before him as a footstool:

> Clothed in a mantle of dazzling gold or draped in rags of black clouds like a beggar, the might of the Westerly Wind sits enthroned upon the western horizon with the whole North Atlantic as a footstool for his feet. (026-001)

### The Autocrat's Domain

The West Wind's rule extends across the entire breadth of the ocean:

> It was a favour of the Western King, who rules masterfully the North Atlantic from the latitude of the Azores to the latitude of Cape Farewell. A famous shove this to end a good passage with; and yet, somehow, one could not muster upon one's lips the smile of a courtier's gratitude. (026-008)

### The Heart of the Empire

Conrad describes the North Atlantic as the very stronghold of the West Wind's realm — the seat of his power and the proving ground of the world's finest seamen:

> The prevailing weather of the North Atlantic is typical of the way in which the West Wind rules his realm on which the sun never sets. North Atlantic is the heart of a great empire. It is the part of the West Wind's dominions most thickly populated with generations of fine ships and hardy men. Heroic deeds and adventurous exploits have been performed there, within the very stronghold of his sway. The best sailors in the world have been born and bred under the shadow of his sceptre, learning to manage their ships with skill and audacity before the steps of his stormy throne. (028-004)

> Fleets of victorious ships have hung upon his breath. He has tossed in his hand squadrons of war-scarred three-deckers, and shredded out in mere sport the bunting of flags hallowed in the traditions of honour and glory. He is a good friend and a dangerous enemy, without mercy to unseaworthy ships and fainthearted seamen. (028-004)

### The East Wind's Raids

When the [[east-wind|East Wind]] invades from the tropics, the North Atlantic becomes a prison for the ships that depend upon the West Wind's favour. The transition from Westerly to Easterly weather is swift and absolute — the autocrat deposed in an instant:

> He is, indeed, a worthless fellow. And forthwith, while the West Wind meditates upon the vanity of his irresistible might, the thing is done, and the Easterly weather sets in upon the North Atlantic. (028-003)

Once established, the East Wind's occupation could last weeks:

> In this particular instance the mean interloper held the road for some six weeks on end, establishing his particular administrative methods over the best part of the North Atlantic. (028-006)

> In his forays into the North Atlantic the East Wind behaves like a subtle and cruel adventurer without a notion of honour or fair play. Veiling his clear-cut, lean face in a thin layer of a hard, high cloud, I have seen him, like a wizened robber sheik of the sea, hold up large caravans of ships to the number of three hundred or more at the very gates of the English Channel. (028-005)

> Day after day through a cold streak of heavens as bare and poor as the inside of a rifled safe a rayless and despoiled sun would slink shamefacedly, without pomp or show, to hide in haste under the waters. And still the King slept on, or mourned the vanity of his might and his power, while the thin-lipped intruder put the impress of his cold and implacable spirit upon the sky and sea. (028-005)

### The Southwesterly Siege

When the West Wind is in his southwesterly mood, the North Atlantic becomes a theatre of sustained violence:

> The autocrat of the North Atlantic was still oppressing his kingdom and its outlying dependencies, even as far as the Bay of Biscay, in the dismal secrecy of thick, very thick, weather. The force of the wind, though we were running before it at the rate of some ten knots an hour, was so great that it drove me with a steady push to the front of the poop, where my commander was holding on. (027-004)

## Navigational Reality

Conrad's personification of the North Atlantic is grounded in the practical experience of navigation. The North Atlantic track-chart — spread across the chart-table of every ship making the western passage — was the seaman's daily companion:

> Sprawling over that seaman's silent and trusted adviser, with one elbow upon the coast of Africa and the other planted in the neighbourhood of Cape Hatteras (it was a general track-chart of the North Atlantic), my commander discoursed of the weather. (027-003)

This chart, marked with the tracks of thousands of passages, was the practical document of the West Wind's kingdom — the map upon which his moods were plotted, his favours recorded, and his rages noted for the benefit of those who would follow.

## Maritime Significance

The North Atlantic was the principal thoroughfare of world trade in Conrad's era — the route linking Europe to North America, and the western approaches to the British Isles. It was the ocean on which the British merchant marine built its reputation, and the proving ground where seamen earned their competence. Conrad's personification of the North Atlantic as a kingdom under the West Wind's rule is not mere metaphor: it reflects the lived experience of generations of sailors who knew this ocean's moods as intimately as they knew the character of a sovereign whose temper could shift from benign to murderous without warning.
