## Definition

Rigging is the collective term for the ropes, cables, and chains that support a sailing vessel's masts (standing rigging) and control its yards and sails (running rigging). Standing rigging — the shrouds, stays, and backstays — is fixed, set up taut with deadeyes and lanyards, and forms the permanent structural lattice that keeps the masts in column. Running rigging — halyards, braces, sheets, and tacks — is constantly in motion, hauled and eased as the ship changes course or the wind shifts.

In Conrad's world, rigging is both structure and tool. It is the taut web through which the wind sings in a gale; it is the intricate system that the seaman must know as intimately as his own body; and it is the latticework that gives the sailing ship its characteristic silhouette against sea and sky — that "web of cordage" that distinguishes the sailing vessel from the steamer and marks her as a creature of a passing age.

## Standing and Running

Though Conrad does not use the technical distinction between standing and running rigging, his descriptions implicitly honour it. The "taut rigging" that hums in the gale is standing rigging — the shrouds and stays, set up bar-tight, that must never slacken. The rigging that men haul upon, that controls the yards and sails, is running rigging — constantly handled, constantly adjusted, the medium through which the seaman's will is transmitted to the canvas.

Together, standing and running rigging form the nervous system of the sailing ship. Through the standing rigging, the ship feels the pressure of the wind. Through the running rigging, the crew responds. The rigging is where ship and crew meet, the interface through which the dialogue between the vessel and her people is conducted.

## The Rigging in a Gale

Conrad's most vivid rigging passage comes in his description of a northwesterly gale, where the rigging becomes the instrument through which the wind's voice is heard:

> A chilly blast hums in the taut rigging, causing the ship to tremble to her very keel, and the soaked men on her decks to shiver in their wet clothes to the very marrow of their bones.
>
> (027-001)

The verb "hums" is precisely chosen. Rigging in a strong wind does not whistle or shriek — it produces a deep, resonant hum, a vibration that can be felt through the entire fabric of the ship. The parallelism — the ship trembling to her keel, the men shivering to their marrow — links the rigging to both the ship's structure and the crew's endurance. The force transmitted through the rigging is total; nothing and no one escapes it.

## A Handhold in the Storm

For men on deck in heavy weather, the rigging is the only thing to cling to when the ship heels and the sea comes aboard. Conrad's portrait of Mr. P——, the chief mate, "hooked on" to the rigging during a ferocious gale is one of his most economical descriptions of seamanship under duress:

> Mr. P——, in charge of the deck, hooked on to the windward mizzen rigging in a state of perfect serenity; myself, the third mate, also hooked on somewhere to windward of the slanting poop, in a state of the utmost feasible preparation for a sudden departure into the other world.
>
> (011-009)

The contrast between the chief mate's "perfect serenity" and the young third mate's mortal terror is the contrast between experience and inexperience — but both men are "hooked on" to the rigging, using the ship's own standing gear as their anchor against the violence of the sea. The rigging is indifferent to rank; it holds both men equally, so long as they hold it.

## The Craft of Rigging

Rigging a ship is not merely a matter of setting up ropes; it is an art that involves judgement, experience, and a deep knowledge of materials. In "The Nursery of the Craft," Conrad describes a visit to an old shipwright who scrutinises the rigging of a vessel with a professional eye:

> I wondered what that worthy sea-dog had found to criticise in my ship's rigging.
>
> (031-007)

The professional pride is palpable. For a seaman, the rigging of his ship is a reflection of his competence; criticism of the rigging is criticism of the man. The rigging reveals everything about how a ship is kept — whether the seizings are neat, whether the serving is worn, whether the blocks are well greased.

In "In Captivity," Conrad describes fitting new rigging under the supervision of an older seaman whose approval matters:

> We were fitting some new lower rigging, and he hung about, approving, suggesting, giving me advice in his old manner.
>
> (034-049)

The phrase "fitting some new lower rigging" describes a major piece of ship's work — the lower shrouds are among the heaviest pieces of standing rigging, and their renewal requires heavy lifting, careful measurement, and precise alchemy with tar and serving. The old man "hung about" — not interfering, but present, a living link to an older tradition of rigging knowledge.

## Rigging as a Measure of the Man

Conrad often uses the state of a ship's rigging as an index of her crew's quality. A well-rigged ship speaks well of her people; slack rigging betokens slackness throughout. In his description of the *Tremolino*, the rigging is part of the ship's character — a Mediterranean vessel with the distinctive rig of her type. And in "The Nursery of the Craft," the young Conrad's work about the rigging is linked to his character:

> So, probably on account of my youth, innocence, and pensive habits (which made me sometimes dilatory in my work about the rigging), I was suddenly nominated to the command.
>
> (034-002)

The self-deprecating admission — dilatory in his rigging work — is a confession of the kind of fault that seamen notice. Being slow about the rigging is not like being slow about paperwork; it is a visible, physical failing, seen by every man on deck.
