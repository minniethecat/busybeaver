## Definition

A fathom is a unit of length equal to six feet (approximately 1.83 metres), used almost exclusively for measuring the depth of water. To take a sounding — to "heave the lead" and call out the depth in fathoms — was one of the most fundamental acts of seamanship in the age of sail. The fathom was the measure by which a ship knew whether she had water enough beneath her keel to proceed in safety, or whether it was time to anchor.

## In Conrad's Words

The captain's command of fathoms to the anchor cable — how much chain to veer out relative to the depth — was an act of precise professional judgement, delivered with the authority of finality:

> Once more the master is heard: "Give her forty-five fathom to the water's edge," and then he, too, is done for a time.
>
> (006-005)

The fathom also appeared in the practical life of the ship — but Conrad corrects the landsman's misconception that anchors are "thrown" overboard. The only thing ever truly cast from a ship, in that sense, is the lead, to search the depth:

> As a matter of fact, nothing is ever cast in that sense on board ship but the lead, of which a cast is taken to search the depth of water on which she floats.
>
> (004-006)

## The Fathom as Command

The order to veer cable to a specified number of fathoms was among the last words of authority spoken at the end of a voyage. It was the master's final professional act before yielding the deck to the chief mate for the harbour work:

> This is the last important order; the others are mere routine directions. Once more the master is heard: "Give her forty-five fathom to the water's edge," and then he, too, is done for a time. For days he leaves all the harbour work to his chief mate, the keeper of the ship's anchor and of the ship's routine.
>
> (006-005)

The number of fathoms ordered was not arbitrary. It was a calculation balancing the depth of the anchorage, the holding ground, the expected weather, and the swinging room available — the distilled professional experience of the commander, delivered in a single figure.

## Ten Fathoms Under

In a rare moment of self-deprecation, Conrad records a bungled anchoring approach at an unspecified port. The pilot, a man of "solid merit," attempted an artful berth between a large ship and the shore — and nearly wrecked them. Both anchors were let go with desperate speed:

> I could have kissed their rough, cold iron palms in gratitude if they had not been buried in slimy mud under ten fathoms of water.
>
> (009-007)

The ten fathoms here are both a measure of depth and a measure of deliverance — the distance between the ship's keel and disaster. The anchors held, and the foul ground of the harbour bottom became, for that moment, the dearest mud in the world.

## The Unfathomable Sea

Beyond its literal use as a measure, Conrad reaches for the fathom's figurative opposite — *unfathomable* — to express the sea's essential mystery and cruelty:

> The most amazing wonder of the deep is its unfathomable cruelty.
>
> (036-001)

The word is carefully chosen. The sea cannot be sounded; its depths cannot be measured. The fathom line, which gives the seaman his bearing in shallow waters, is useless against the oceanic abyss. What cannot be fathomed cannot be known, and what cannot be known cannot be trusted.

## The Sounding as Knowledge

Though Conrad's memoir concerns the age of sail, the fathom as a unit of knowledge persisted. The Thames estuary channels — Queen's Channel, Prince's Channel, Four-Fathom Channel — were named by the depth of water they offered, a permanent memorial to the lead-line's authority. Every captain approaching the English shore carried in his mind a map of fathoms: how many beneath the keel meant safety, how many meant the ship was standing into danger.
