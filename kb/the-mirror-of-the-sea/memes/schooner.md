# Schooner

The **schooner** is a fore-and-aft rigged sailing vessel with two (or occasionally more) masts, the foremast being shorter than the mainmast. In Conrad's hierarchy of pleasure craft, the schooner occupies a place of honour — the vessel type he recommends above all others for long voyages and for the sheer beauty of her lines at rest.

> For racing, a cutter; for a long pleasure voyage, a schooner; for cruising in home waters, the yawl; and the handling of them all is indeed a fine art. (008-004)

## At Anchor and Under Way

Conrad's aesthetic judgement is precise: a schooner is never more beautiful than when she lies still, her two masts "distributed over the hull with a swaggering rake aft." Under way, however, she displays something like intelligence:

> A schooner, yawl, or cutter in charge of a capable man seems to handle herself as if endowed with the power of reasoning and the gift of swift execution. (008-002)

> At anchor a schooner looks better; she has an aspect of greater efficiency and a better balance to the eye, with her two masts distributed over the hull with a swaggering rake aft. (008-003)

## The Fore-and-Aft Aesthetic

Conrad develops a careful comparison among the three fore-and-aft rigs. The cutter, with its enormous single mainsail, possesses "an air of lofty and silent majesty." The yawl is the rig "one comes in time to love" — the easiest to handle, a companion for home waters. But the schooner's distinction lies in its visual balance: the distribution of canvas across two masts produces, at rest, an impression of "greater efficiency and a better balance to the eye."

The broader aesthetic claim Conrad makes is that the fore-and-aft rig as a whole is "unapproachable" in its beauty. A fleet of such vessels at anchor "has its own slender graciousness. The setting of their sails resembles more than anything else the unfolding of a bird's wings; the facility of their evolutions is a pleasure to the eye. They are birds of the sea, whose swimming is like flying, and resembles more a natural function than the handling of man-invented appliances."

## The Fine Art of Handling

Conrad extends the schooner typology into a philosophy of seamanship. The handling of these craft "is indeed a fine art," requiring not just knowledge of general principles but "a particular acquaintance with the character of the craft." From this, he draws a direct parallel between ships and men:

> There may be a rule of conduct; there is no rule of human fellowship. To deal with men is as fine an art as it is to deal with ships. Both men and ships live in an unstable element, are subject to subtle and powerful influences, and want to have their merits understood rather than their faults found out.

The schooner, in this moral schema, represents the vessel whose merits are most immediately visible — her balance at anchor is a visual argument for her competence.

## The Falmouth Fruit-Schooner

In one of the book's most evocative passages, a Falmouth fruit-schooner — "the smallest of them all" — leads a homeward-bound fleet scattered by calm off the Azores. Conrad's description grants the humble trader a near-mythic quality:

> For this was the homeward-bound fleet from the far-off ends of the earth, and a Falmouth fruit-schooner, the smallest of them all, was heading the flight. One could have imagined her very fair, if not divinely tall, leaving a scent of lemons and oranges in her wake. (010-001)

The fruit-schooner reappears later in a less romantic context — as one of the victims of the East Wind's blockade of the Channel approaches. Among the held-up fleet of grain ships, timber carriers, and wool clippers, Conrad notes "one or two belated fruit schooners in company," their perishable cargoes of oranges making the delay particularly acute, the crews reduced to "sweepings of bread-lockers and scrapings of sugar-casks."

## Related Concepts

The schooner belongs to Conrad's careful typology of sailing vessels, each judged by its fitness for a specific purpose: the [[cutter]] for racing, the [[yawl]] for home waters, and the schooner — with her "swaggering rake" — for the open sea.
