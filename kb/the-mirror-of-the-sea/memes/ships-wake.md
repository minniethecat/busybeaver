## Definition

A ship's wake is the disturbed water left behind as a vessel moves through the sea — a trail of foam, bubbles, and swirling currents that extends astern, gradually widening and fading until it merges back into the undifferentiated ocean. For Conrad, the wake is both a physical phenomenon and a powerful metaphor for time, memory, and the transience of human experience at sea. The days, weeks, and months of a passage "seem to be left astern as easily as the light air-bubbles in the swirls of the ship's wake, and vanish into a great silence."

## In Conrad's Words

> Nowhere else than upon the sea do the days, weeks and months fall away quicker into the past. They seem to be left astern as easily as the light air-bubbles in the swirls of the ship's wake, and vanish into a great silence in which your ship moves on with a sort of magical effect. (002-008)

> They pass away, the days, the weeks, the months. Nothing but a gale can disturb the orderly life of the ship; and the monotony of the passage, the sense of being suspended in an eternal present, makes the past seem as remote as another life. (002-008)

> The track she had made when drifting while her heart stood still within her iron ribs looked like a tangled thread on the white paper of the chart. It was shown to me by a friend, her second officer. In that surprising tangle there were words in minute handwriting, the record of her agony. (019-005)

## The Wake as Metaphor for Time

Conrad's most sustained meditation on the wake appears early in the book (chapter 2), where he compares the passage of time at sea to the disappearance of bubbles in a ship's wake. At sea, time does not accumulate as it does on land — it flows away astern, leaving no permanent mark. The seaman lives in a perpetual present, the past dissolving behind him "into a great silence." This is both liberating and unsettling: liberating because the burdens of memory are lightened; unsettling because a life lived in such a way may amount to nothing more than a vanishing trail on the water.

## The Wake as Record

A ship's wake is her only visible record on the sea — the sole trace of her passage through a medium that closes behind her without a scar. Unlike the chart, which preserves "the devious tracings" of a ship's course on paper, the wake on the water vanishes within minutes. This impermanence is central to Conrad's elegy for the sailing-ship era: the thousands of ships that crossed the world's oceans left no permanent mark on the element they traversed.
