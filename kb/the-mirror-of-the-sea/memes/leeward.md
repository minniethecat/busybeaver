**Leeward** (pronounced *loo'ard* in nautical usage) is the side or direction away from the wind — the sheltered flank of a vessel, and the direction towards which the wind blows. In sailing terminology, leeward is the opposite of [[windward]], and the distinction between the two sides governs almost every aspect of seamanship, from ship handling to fleet tactics.

## The Running Sea

When a ship heels under press of sail, she leans away from the wind, burying her leeward rail in a boiling sheet of white foam. The leeward side is where the sea makes itself most visibly and audibly present.

> …the ship meanwhile rushing on her way with a heavier list, a noisier splutter, a more threatening hiss of the white, almost blinding, sheet of foam to leeward. (011-020)

This "sheet of foam to leeward" is the constant companion of a sailing ship running before the wind — a hissing, blinding presence that marks the boundary between the vessel and the ocean's force. The leeward side is the price the ship pays for her speed: the harder she drives, the deeper she buries her lee rail.

## The Lee Shore

The most dangerous manifestation of the leeward direction is the [[lee-shore]] — land lying downwind of a ship. When a vessel is caught between a gale and a lee shore, every knot of speed brings her closer to destruction. Conrad describes the mate and [[master]] gazing ahead while "under their feet the ship rushes at some twelve knots in the direction of the lee shore" (026-010). The lee shore is where the abstract direction "leeward" becomes a concrete and mortal threat: it is the final destination of a ship that cannot claw its way to windward.

## Caught to Leeward

In a sudden gale, being thrown to leeward could be a matter of life and death. When the ship heeled violently onto her side, men on the leeward deck found themselves swimming rather than standing.

> …the ship lying on her side, so that half of the crew were swimming and the other half clawing desperately at whatever came to hand, according to the side of the deck each man had been caught on by the catastrophe, either to leeward or to windward. (023-008)

The leeward side, submerged in the hissing sea, was the more dangerous position — those caught there had to fight not only the wind but the water itself. The distinction between leeward and windward was not academic; in an instant, it determined whether a seaman lived or drowned.

## Tactical Dread

In the age of fighting sail, being thrown to leeward was the nightmare of every fleet commander. A ship or [[squadron]] caught to leeward of the enemy lost the initiative: unable to close at will, it lay at the mercy of an opponent who held the weather gauge.

> The fleet tactics of the sailing days have been governed by two points: the deadly nature of a raking fire, and the dread, natural to a commander dependent upon the winds, to find at some crucial moment part of his fleet thrown hopelessly to leeward. (048-002)

This "dread" of the leeward position shaped naval warfare for centuries. Conrad notes that [[Lord Nelson]] was the first commander to disregard this fear with conviction — accepting the tactical risk of the lee position in exchange for the overwhelming concentration of fire that would decide the battle before the wind could betray him. Nelson's genius lay in recognising that the leeward position, for all its disadvantages, could be turned into an advantage if a commander was willing to close the distance and fight at point-blank range.
