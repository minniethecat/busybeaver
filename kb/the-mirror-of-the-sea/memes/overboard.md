## Definition

"Overboard" describes the condition of being cast, thrown, or fallen from a vessel into the sea. Conrad draws a sharp distinction between the landsman's ignorant conception and the seaman's reality: the landsman imagines anchoring as "throwing something overboard," while the seaman knows the anchor already hangs overboard, requiring only release. Yet "overboard" also carries its literal menace — spars that might go, men who vanish into the water, and gear deliberately jettisoned in extremity.

## The Landsman's Error

Conrad is characteristically sardonic about the landsman's misuse of nautical language. The "particularly benighted landsman" misconstrues anchoring as an act of throwing — but the anchor, already hanging at the cathead, needs only to be released. This is not mere pedantry: it illustrates the gap between the shore-bound mind and the seaman's intimate knowledge of his gear.

> This insistence in using the odious word arises from the fact that a particularly benighted landsman must imagine the act of anchoring as a process of throwing something overboard, whereas the anchor ready for its work is already overboard, and is not thrown over, but simply allowed to fall. (004-005)

## Spars and Safety

In Conrad's narrative, the loss of a spar overboard is the ultimate measure of a ship's vulnerability. He recalls with pride that aboard the ship of Captain S——, the tall spars "never went overboard" in his time — a testament to the captain's seamanship and the crew's vigilance. In contrast, aboard a ship caught in a savage [[gale]], the prospect of masts, yards, and men alike being "flung overboard" is the stuff of terror.

> So sail did get shortened more or less in time even in that ship, and her tall spars never went overboard while I served in her. (012-001)

> She rolled and rolled with an awful dislodging jerk and that dizzily fast sweep of her masts on every swing. It was a wonder that the men sent aloft were not flung off the yards, the yards not flung off the masts, the masts not flung overboard. (015-009)

## The Human Peril

The most vivid overboard episode in *The Mirror of the Sea* is César Cervoni's plunge in the old harbour of [[Marseille]]. [[Dominic Cervoni]]'s nephew — "amongst his other perfections, he was a consummate eavesdropper and spy" — slips overboard while attempting to eavesdrop on a private conversation. Dominic's reaction is not panic but the studied calm of a seaman who trusts the sea's rhythms: "he stepped quietly to the rail and leaned over, waiting for his nephew's miserable head to bob up for the first time."

> Once — it was in the old harbour, just before the *Tremolino*'s last voyage — he vanished thus overboard to my infinite consternation. Dominic and I had been talking business together aft, and Cesar had sneaked up behind us to listen, for, amongst his other perfections, he was a consummate eavesdropper and spy. At the sound of the heavy plop alongside horror held me rooted to the spot; but Dominic stepped quietly to the rail and leaned over, waiting for his nephew's miserable head to bob up for the first time. (043-009)

## Jettison in Extremity

To throw cargo overboard deliberately is the seaman's last resort in a crisis — a desperate act to lighten a labouring ship. During the *[[Tremolino]]*'s final voyage, Dominic gives the order without hesitation, knowing that the vessel's survival trumps every commercial consideration. Later, the discovery of cut roping stitches on a sail suggests sabotage — and Dominic expresses his fury in the same idiom: he would have thrown the yard overboard, too, if not for the trouble of it.

> Dominic gave the order to throw the whole lot overboard. (044-028)

> I would have had the yard thrown overboard, too, he said, leading me aft again, "if it had not been for the trouble. Let no sign escape you," he continued, lowering his voice, "but I am going to tell you something terrible. Listen: I have observed that the roping stitches on that sail have been cut!" (044-029)
