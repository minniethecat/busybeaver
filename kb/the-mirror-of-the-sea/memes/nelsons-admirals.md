## Identity

Conrad's portrait of Nelson in *The Mirror of the Sea* is framed by the senior admirals who commanded him and, in Conrad's view, provided the institutional structure within which individual genius could flourish. Lord Hood, whom Nelson "himself regarded as the greatest sea-officer England ever had"; Admiral Keith, who commanded the Mediterranean fleet; and Sir Hyde Parker, at Copenhagen — these figures appear in Conrad's text not as competitors to Nelson's fame but as the necessary conditions of it. They represent the professional hierarchy that produced, recognised, and supported the exceptional.

## Conrad's Account

> It was this ardour which made him great. He was a flaming example to the wooers of glorious fortune. There have been great officers before — Lord Hood, for instance, whom he himself regarded as the greatest sea-officer England ever had. (047-003)

> The distinguished Admiral had lived through it himself, and was a good judge of what was expected in those days from men and ships. A brilliant frigate captain, a man of sound judgment, of dashing bravery, and of serene mind, the Admiral shook his head at the supposition. (046-003)

> This heroic spirit of daring and endurance, in which all public and private differences were sunk throughout the whole fleet, is Lord Nelson's great legacy, triply sealed by the victorious impress of the Nile, Copenhagen, and Trafalgar. (047-006)

## The Institutional Framework of Genius

Conrad's invocation of Hood, Keith, and Hyde Parker serves a specific rhetorical purpose: to demonstrate that Nelson's greatness, far from being diminished by the existence of other capable admirals, was defined by his relationship to them. Nelson learned from Hood; he negotiated with Keith; he famously "turned his blind eye" to Hyde Parker's signal at Copenhagen. Conrad's point is that the sailing navy produced not isolated geniuses but a cadre of exceptional officers whose collective capability made individual brilliance possible. The senior admirals are not footnotes to Nelson but participants in the same tradition.

## Historical Context

Lord Hood (1724–1816) commanded the Mediterranean Fleet during the early Revolutionary Wars and was Nelson's most admired superior. Admiral Lord Keith (1746–1823) succeeded Hood in Mediterranean command. Sir Hyde Parker (1739–1807) was Nelson's commanding officer at the Battle of Copenhagen (1801), where Nelson's tactical insubordination — putting his telescope to his blind eye and declaring he "really did not see the signal" — became one of the most celebrated acts of justified disobedience in naval history.
