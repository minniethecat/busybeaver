## Identity

J. M. K. B. was, in his own phrase, "Américain, Catholique, et gentilhomme" — an American Catholic gentleman of Royalist convictions who formed part of the four-member syndicate that owned the tartane *Tremolino*. Conrad sketches him with a few indelible strokes: elegant, fatal, correct in manner and severe in appearance, wearing a white tie and large shirt-front "of the highest possible sort of starch." He belonged to a type that fascinated Conrad — the exotic and faintly absurd foreign political enthusiast whose loyalties attached him to lost causes as a matter of principle.

## Conrad's Account

> We four formed a "syndicate" owning the Tremolino: an international and astonishing syndicate. And we were all ardent Royalists of the snow-white Legitimist complexion — Heaven only knows why! (041-001)

> Poor J. M. K. B., Américain, Catholique, et gentilhomme, as he was disposed to describe himself in moments of lofty expansion! Are there still to be found in Europe gentlemen keen of face and elegantly slight of body, of distinguished aspect, with a fascinating drawing-room manner and with a dark horizon? (041-002)

> When released from the dizzy embrace, I sat down on the carpet — suddenly, without affectation. In this unpretentious attitude I became aware that J. M. K. B. had followed me into the room, elegant, fatal, correct and severe in a white tie and large shirt-front. (042-003)

## Character and Anecdotes

Conrad treats J. M. K. B. with the affectionate irony he reserved for men whose convictions exceeded their utility. The syndicate's Royalist ardour — "Heaven only knows why!" — was the shared enthusiasm of men who had attached themselves to a lost political cause as a framework for adventure. J. M. K. B.'s social graces were unimpeachable; his practical contribution to the smuggling enterprise was never specified. He appears and vanishes from Conrad's narrative like a figure from a diplomatic reception who has wandered, by some inexplicable route, onto the deck of a tartane.
