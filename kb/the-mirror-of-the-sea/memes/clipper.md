# Clipper

The **clipper** was the thoroughbred of the sailing-ship era — a vessel built for speed on fine lines, carrying canvas that strained spars and seamanship alike. In Conrad's memory, the clipper represents the zenith of maritime beauty, already passing into history as steam-liners took her berths at the Circular Quay.

## The Clyde Wool-Clippers

The 1870s saw the Clyde shipyards launch iron wool-clippers in swarms upon the world's trade routes:

> The ship was one of those iron wool-clippers that the Clyde had floated out in swarms upon the world during the seventh decade of the last century. (011-004)

These were working vessels, not yachts — "general cargo, emigrant, and passenger clippers" that "used to remain for months together waiting for their load of wool" (034-001). Yet they were "good to look at and — well — exciting to handle" (033-009).

## An Inspiring Spectacle

Conrad evokes the vanished glory of the clipper fleet at the New South Dock, where a quarter-mile of ships lay moored two and two in a forest of masts:

> To a man who has never seen the extraordinary nobility, strength, and grace that the devoted generations of shipbuilders have evolved from some pure nooks of their simple souls, the sight that could be seen five-and-twenty years ago of a large fleet of clippers moored along the north side of the New South Dock was an inspiring spectacle. (033-005)

## The London–Sydney Run

The New South Dock was "especially a loading dock for the Colonies in those great (and last) days of smart wool-clippers" (033-009). These ships knew no other ports but London and Sydney, London and Melbourne, London and Adelaide. Conrad captures the rhythm of their captivity and release:

> One could almost have believed, as her grey-whiskered second mate used to say of the old *Duke of S——*, that they knew the road to the Antipodes better than their own skippers, who, year in, year out, took them from London — the place of captivity — to some Australian port where, twenty-five years ago, though moored well and tight enough to the wooden wharves, they felt themselves no captives, but honoured guests. (033-009)

## Iron vs. Wood

The transition from wooden to iron clippers marked a generational shift. Captain S——, who had commanded the wooden *Tweed*, "brought the tradition of quick passages with him into the iron clipper" (011-004). But the iron clipper could never equal the feats of the old wooden ship that had made the *Tweed* "a name of praise upon the lips of English-speaking seamen" (012-003).

## Eclipse by Steam

By the time Conrad writes, the clipper's reign is over. "Now great steam-liners lie at these berths, always reserved for the sea aristocracy — grand and imposing enough ships, but here today and gone next week" (034-001). The clippers stayed for months; they were part of the city's life. The steamers, for all their size, pass through. The loading dock that once rang with the labour of wool-clippers preparing for their 11,000-mile runs had fallen silent, its "thick, enormous network" of rigging against the sky dissolved into memory.
