## Definition

Coasting is the practice of navigating a vessel along a coastline, keeping the land in sight or within easy reach. It stands in contrast to deep-sea navigation: where the ocean voyager steers by celestial bodies and dead reckoning across empty horizons, the coaster navigates by landmarks, lights, buoys, and the familiar contours of the shore. Conrad treats coasting not as a lesser form of seamanship but as a distinct discipline with its own skills, its own dangers, and its own intimate relationship with the land.

## In Conrad's Words

> The taking of Departure, if not the last sight of the land, is, perhaps, the last professional recognition of the land on the part of a sailor. It is the technical, as distinguished from the sentimental, "good-bye." Henceforth he has done with the coast astern of his ship. (001-004)

> There is no part of the world of coasts, continents, oceans, seas, straits, capes, and islands which is not under the sway of a reigning wind, the sovereign of its typical weather. (025-001)

> The West Wind reigns over the seas surrounding the coasts of these kingdoms; and from the gateways of the channels, from promontories as if from watchtowers, from estuaries of rivers as if from postern gates, from passageways, inlets, straits, firths, the ships of the kingdom go out to their toil. (026-001)

## The Art of Coastal Navigation

Coasting demands a different kind of knowledge from ocean navigation. The coaster must know the set of tides along his route, the depth of water over every bar, the position of every rock and shoal, the leading marks for entering every harbour. He must be able to recognise a headland from every angle, in every condition of light and weather. This knowledge is local, accumulated over years of repetition, and largely unwritten — it lives in the memories of experienced coasting masters and is passed down orally.

## The Coaster's World

Conrad distinguishes sharply between the coaster and the deep-sea sailor. The coaster inhabits a more human world — he is never more than a few hours from land, never entirely beyond the reach of shelter. But his world is also more dangerous in its own way: the proximity of land is the greatest hazard to a ship, and the coaster must thread his way through channels, across bars, and around headlands where a single error of judgment can mean disaster. The coaster's intimacy with the coast is both his greatest asset and his constant peril.
