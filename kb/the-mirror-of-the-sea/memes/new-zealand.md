## Conrad's Encounters

New Zealand appears in *The Mirror of the Sea* not as a destination described in its own right, but as a distant point on the compass — the farthest reach of the sailing routes that structured Conrad's maritime life. It is the place ships were bound for when they left England; the place from which great clippers returned laden with wool. It represents the terminus of empire, the Antipodes where British seafaring life reproduced itself in miniature around the Circular Quay and the harbour pubs of colonial towns.

> A heavy sea took him off the poop in the run between New Zealand and the Horn. (012-017)

> At any rate, it was a harmless eccentricity, and may the god of gales, who took him away so abruptly between New Zealand and the Horn, let his soul rest in some Paradise of true seamen, where no amount of carrying on will ever dismast a ship! (012-018)

## The Wool Trade and the Clipper Routes

New Zealand's place in Conrad's maritime geography is inseparable from the wool trade. The great sailing clippers that called at colonial ports spent months loading wool for the homeward passage. The link between the Antipodes and "home" was measured in the names of ships and their reliable circuits — a rhythm that the advent of steam would eventually break. Conrad's nostalgia for the sailing ship era is implicitly a nostalgia for this ordered geography, in which New Zealand was the farthest point on a known map, not yet diminished by the speed of steam.

> These towns of the Antipodes, not so great then as they are now, took an interest in the shipping, the running links with "home," whose numbers confirmed the sense of their growing importance. (034-001)

## Peril in the Southern Ocean

The passage to and from New Zealand was notoriously dangerous, crossing the stormy latitudes of the Southern Ocean and rounding the Horn. Conrad records the fate of a steamer that lost her propeller "down south, on her passage out to New Zealand" — a reminder that even the mechanical reliability of steam could not guarantee safety in these waters.

> Such a one, a big steamship, too, whose working life had been a record of faithful keeping time from land to land, in disregard of wind and sea, once lost her propeller down south, on her passage out to New Zealand. (018-008)

## The *Duke of S——*

One of Conrad's most vivid anecdotes places the old ship *Duke of S——* meeting a "violent death on the coast of New Zealand." The brief, almost incidental mention — tucked into a long reverie about the Circular Quay in Sydney — is characteristic of Conrad's method: a ship's fate noted in passing, as a seaman might hear of an old shipmate's death in a chance remark ashore.

> I have listened for hours to this most pertinacious pedlar (I wonder whether he is dead or has made a fortune), while sitting on the rail of the old *Duke of S——* (she's dead, poor thing! a violent death on the coast of New Zealand)… (034-001)
