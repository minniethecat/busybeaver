# Barque

The **barque** is a sailing vessel of three or more masts, square-rigged on all but the aftermost mast, which carries fore-and-aft sails. In the nineteenth century the barque became the dominant rig for ocean-going merchantmen — a practical compromise between the manpower demands of a full-rigged ship and the carrying capacity required for profitable trade. Conrad's memoirs are peopled with barques great and small, from the iron wool-clippers of the Clyde to the homely timber-carriers of the West India Docks.

## A Little Barque in a Great Gale

Conrad's finest barque passage describes a 400-ton vessel running before a hard gale in the Southern Ocean, somewhere between the islands of St Paul and Amsterdam and Cape Otway. The barque, under two lower topsails and a reefed foresail, races with the long seas in a display of pluck and seaworthiness:

> I well remember a three days' run got out of a little barque of 400 tons somewhere between the islands of St Paul and Amsterdam and Cape Otway on the Australian coast. It was a hard, long gale, grey clouds and green sea, heavy weather undoubtedly, but still what a sailor would call manageable. Under two lower topsails and a reefed foresail the barque seemed to race with a long, steady sea that did not becalm her in the troughs. The solemn thundering combers caught her up from astern, passed her with a fierce boiling up of foam level with the bulwarks, swept on ahead with a swish and a roar: and the little vessel, dipping her jibboom into the tumbling froth, would go on running in a smooth, glassy hollow, a deep valley between two ridges of the sea, hiding the horizon ahead and astern. (023-005)

There was, Conrad writes, "such fascination in her pluck, nimbleness, the continual exhibition of unfailing seaworthiness, in the semblance of courage and endurance" that he could not give up watching her run through the three unforgettable days. For Conrad, gales have their own physiognomy — and this one he remembers "in dignified austerity, as you would remember with pleasure the noble features of a stranger with whom you crossed swords once in knightly encounter" (023-006).

## Timber from the Gulf

In the quiet Import and Export basins beside the bustling New South Dock, Conrad observed how enormous tropical timber baulks — "roughed out of iron trunks grown in the woods about the Gulf of Mexico" — were discharged from barques of surprising modesty:

> They lay piled up in stacks of mighty boles, and it was hard to believe that all this mass of dead and stripped trees had come out of the flanks of a slender, innocent-looking little barque with, as likely as not, a homely woman's name — Ellen this or Annie that — upon her fine bows. (033-007)

## A Barque Command

At twenty-five, Conrad himself took command of a barque loading for Mauritius. In later years, encountering his old chief mate Mr B—— grown threadbare and grey, he thoughtlessly asked what the man was doing now — and instantly regretted it. "I am commanding a little barque," he had said, "loading here for Mauritius." The moment hung between them: the command Conrad held at twenty-five, and the man who had once taught him seamanship now looking for any berth at all (034-045–034-047).

## The Barque Rig

The barque rig — square yards on the fore and main, a fore-and-aft spanker on the mizzen — meant fewer hands aloft than a full-rigged ship while preserving much of the driving power. In the wool trade, the tea trade, and the timber trade alike, the barque was the economical choice: enough canvas to make a passage, few enough men to show a profit. By Conrad's era, even the great clipper ships were being re-rigged as barques in their old age, their splendid sail-plans reduced for economy's sake.
