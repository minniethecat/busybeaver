## Location and Identity

[[London]], approached from the sea by way of the [[thames-estuary|Thames Estuary]], was the port that defined Conrad's relationship with the maritime world from the perspective of a merchant seaman. He knew it not as a city of streets and squares but as a sequence of docks, reaches, and anchorages — the Lower Hope Reach, Gravesend, the Albert Docks — each with its own character, light, and sound. For Conrad, London was the point where the sea surrendered to the land, where a ship's voyage ended and the seaman stepped ashore into an alien element.

## Conrad's Description

> This stretch of the Thames from London Bridge to the Albert Docks is to other watersides of river ports what a virgin forest would be to a garden. It is a thing grown up, not made. It recalls a jungle by the confused, varied, and impenetrable aspect of the buildings that line the shore. (031-005)

> Amongst the great commercial streams of these islands, the Thames is the only one, I think, open to romantic feeling, from the fact that the sight of human labour and the sounds of human industry do not come down to the water's edge. (030-003)

> Coming in from the eastward, the bright colouring of the lightship marking the part of the river committed to the charge of an Admiral accentuates the dreariness and the great breadth of the Thames Estuary. (031-002)

> In their way they are as romantic as the river they serve is unlike all the other commercial streams of the world. The cosiness of the St. Katherine's Dock, the old-world air of the London Docks, remain impressed upon my memory. (032-004)

## Maritime Significance

London was the terminal point of Conrad's voyages — the place where cargo was discharged, crews were paid off, and ships were laid up. His descriptions of the Thames approaches capture the transitional zone between sea and city: the lightship at the Nore, the factory chimneys of Grays and Greenhithe, the "monstrous redbrick pile on the Essex shore." Each landmark was a signpost in the seaman's approach to England, read with the same navigational attention as a bearing on a headland.

The London docks were, to Conrad, a world unto themselves — a "virgin forest" of masts, sheds, and wharves that seemed to have grown organically rather than been planned. He contrasts this with the orderly ports of the continent, finding in London's chaotic waterside a peculiar romance that matched the city's own character.

## A Port of Departures

London was also, for Conrad, a place of [[departure]]. Every arrival at its docks implied, sooner or later, a departure from them — the [[fine-art-of-sailing|seaman's eternal rhythm]] of coming and going. The *[[tweed|Tweed]]*, the *[[tremolino|Tremolino]]* — many of the ships Conrad served aboard knew London's waters. The [[wind]] that carried them out of the Thames Estuary and into the [[north-sea|North Sea]] was the same West Wind that "reigns over the seas surrounding the coasts of these kingdoms."

> The West Wind reigns over the seas surrounding the coasts of these kingdoms; and from the gateways of the channels, from promontories as if from watchtowers, from estuaries of rivers as if from postern gates, the ships of the kingdom go out to their toil. (026-001)
