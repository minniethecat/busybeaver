## Vessel Identity

The *[[Tremolino]]* was a tartane — a small Mediterranean vessel of ancient lineage, lateen-rigged, built on the River of Savona by a famous builder of boats, and rigged in [[corsica|Corsica]]. She was owned by a four-member syndicate of Carlist Royalists and operated as a fruit and corkwood trader, though her real business was smuggling. Conrad served aboard her in his youth and devotes the final chapters of *The Mirror of the Sea* to her story. She was, for him, the vessel of his Mediterranean romance — the ship that embodied adventure, danger, and the intoxicating freedom of the inland sea.

## The Syndicate and the Cause

The *Tremolino* was not an ordinary smuggler. She was the instrument of a Carlist syndicate — four members bound by political loyalty to the [[don-carlos|Carlist Pretender]] and his cause. [[dona-rita|Doña Rita]], the syndicate's intelligence and soul, operated from a little furnished house on the Prado in Barcelona. [[j-m-k-b|J. M. K. B.]], the American gentleman, provided resources and cover. Conrad himself — young, impressionable, hungry for adventure — was drawn into their world, and the *Tremolino* became the vessel of his initiation into the secret currents of the Mediterranean.

## Career and Voyages

The *Tremolino*'s career was brief and clandestine. Officially a fruit and corkwood trader operating between Mediterranean ports, she was in fact a smuggling vessel whose operations were directed by the syndicate from Doña Rita's villa. Conrad's account focuses on her final voyage: a desperate flight from a Spanish revenue cutter along the Catalan coast, culminating in a night of terror and decisive [[seamanship]]. When capture became inevitable, Dominic — rather than surrender his beloved vessel — drove her onto the rocks, where she broke up and vanished, leaving "not a chip to be seen around the black rock from which the Tremolino, with the breath of fire in her, had slipped out silently into the night."

## Crew and Command

The *Tremolino* was commanded by Dominic Cervoni as padrone, with Conrad serving as a junior officer. The crew included [[cesar-cervoni|Cesar]], Dominic's wretched nephew, and was described as "drawn from the islands" — Corsicans and Sardinians whose seamanship was as natural as breathing. Conrad's account of the command dynamic — Dominic's silent authority, his own youthful enthusiasm, the crew's unquestioning competence — is one of the most vivid portraits of shipboard life in his writing.

## Fate

The *Tremolino* was lost on the rocky Catalan coast during her final voyage, driven ashore by Dominic to prevent capture by a Spanish revenue cutter. Conrad describes her end with the restraint of a seaman who has seen ships die: "There was not a chip to be seen around the black rock." She was not wrecked by incompetence or bad luck but deliberately sacrificed — a ship's death that was also an act of defiance. In Conrad's [[craft|philosophy of ships]], the *Tremolino*'s end was a kind of apotheosis: the vessel that chose destruction over dishonour.
