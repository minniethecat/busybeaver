## Vessel Identity

The *Cicero* was a barque — a three-masted sailing vessel square-rigged on the fore and main masts, fore-and-aft rigged on the mizzen — laid up at Sydney's Circular Quay during Conrad's time there as night-watchman. She lay on the opposite side of the quay from Conrad's own ship, a wool-clipper. Her name, shared with the Roman orator and statesman, was a common choice for merchant vessels in the nineteenth century, reflecting the classical education of shipowners and the fashion for naming barques after figures of gravitas and eloquence.

## Conrad's Account

The *Cicero* enters *The Mirror of the Sea* not through any description of her lines or rigging, but through a human relationship: her chief mate was the inseparable crony of Mr. B——, Conrad's own chief mate aboard the wool-clipper. Every night, the two men enacted a ritual of fellowship that Conrad, alone on the dark deck, witnessed with a mixture of exasperation and something like affection:

> He used to go ashore every night to foregather in some hotel's parlour with his crony, the mate of the barque *Cicero*, lying on the other side of the Circular Quay. Late at night I would hear from afar their stumbling footsteps and their voices raised in endless argument. The mate of the *Cicero* was seeing his friend on board. They would continue their senseless and muddled discourse in tones of profound friendship for half an hour or so at the shore end of our gangway, and then I would hear Mr. B—— insisting that he must see the other on board his ship. And away they would go, their voices, still conversing with excessive amity, being heard moving all round the harbour. It happened more than once that they would thus perambulate three or four times the distance, each seeing the other on board his ship out of pure and disinterested affection. (034-010)

The comedy of the scene — two inebriated mates escorting each other back and forth across the quay in an endless loop, neither willing to be the first to part — is Conrad at his most gently humorous. But beneath the comedy lies a deeper observation about the loneliness of the seaman's life ashore. These two men, officers of ships that would never sail again, had only each other; their ritual was an act of mutual preservation, a refusal to let the night — and with it, their only human connection — come to an end.

## Crew & Command

The mate of the *Cicero* remains unnamed in Conrad's narrative — he is simply "the mate of the barque *Cicero*" or "his crony." What Conrad tells us of him is conveyed entirely through his friendship with Mr. B——: a man who shared the same predicament (an officer on a laid-up sailing ship), the same habits (nightly drinking ashore), and the same improbable devotion. The two men, Conrad implies, were more than drinking companions — they were each other's lifeline in the suspended animation of the Circular Quay, where deep-water sailing ships and their crews waited months for cargoes that would justify their existence.
