# Tartane

The **tartane** (French: *tartane*; Italian: *tartana*) is a small Mediterranean sailing vessel, typically single-masted with a lateen (triangular) sail, used for fishing and coastal trade. In *The Mirror of the Sea*, the tartane is not merely a type — it is the legal identity of the vessel Conrad called his "cradle."

## The *Tremolino*

The tartane *Tremolino*, in which Conrad lived one of the formative adventures of his youth, was built on the River of Savona and rigged in Corsica. Her papers described her plainly:

> She who was my cradle in those years had been built on the River of Savona by a famous builder of boats, was rigged in Corsica by another good man, and was described on her papers as a "tartane" of sixty tons. (040-003)

This single sentence distils the tartane's essence: a modest vessel of Italian builders and Corsican riggers, a product of the Mediterranean's scattered maritime communities — each with its own "famous" craftsmen and its own traditions.

But Conrad immediately undercuts the official description. The *Tremolino* was, in truth, not a tartane at all:

> In reality, she was a true balancelle, with two short masts raking forward and two curved yards, each as long as her hull; a true child of the Latin lake, with two pretty lugsails.
> (040-003)

The gap between the ship's papers and her actual rig — between the tartane of record and the balancelle of reality — is characteristic of the Mediterranean's informal maritime world, where a vessel's type was often less a matter of naval architecture than of local convenience and the registrar's indifference.

## A Cradle of Awakened Love

Whatever her technical classification, the *Tremolino* was for Conrad the birthplace of something permanent. It was aboard her that the sea ceased to be a career and became a devotion:

> Her name was the *Tremolino*. I owe to her the awakened love for the sea that, with the quivering of her swift little body and the humming of the wind under the foot of her lateen sails, stole into my heart.
> (040-004)

## The Mediterranean Type

The tartane belongs to a family of Mediterranean working craft that Conrad catalogues with affectionate precision: the [[balancelle]], the [[galley]], and the tartane itself, each adapted to the short seas, variable winds, and ancient trading routes of the inland sea. Unlike the northern European square-riggers, these vessels worked under lateen sails that allowed them to point closer to the wind and navigate the narrow passages between the Mediterranean's countless capes and islands.

> I have known a balancelle which had been in her youth a coral fisher, then a pirate, and last of all a smuggler, all under different names and flags, and all the while remaining the same little black-hulled, sweet-lined, swift Meditterranean craft.
> (040-003, paraphrased)

The tartane, like the balancelle, was a shape-shifter — her identity on paper often bearing little relation to her actual employment. Conrad's *Tremolino*, officially "a fruit and corkwood trader" (043-022), was in fact engaged in gun-running for Spanish Carlist pretenders to the throne. The tartane's modest official description was a mask — and, as Conrad insists, "a ship is ever guiltless of the sins, transgressions, and follies of her men" (041-009).
