**Aloft** is a nautical term referring to the upper parts of a sailing vessel — the masts, yards, and rigging above the level of the deck. It is the vertical dimension of a ship's existence, the realm of the seaman who climbs to furl or set sail, and the first part of a ship to appear on the horizon.

## The Vertical Dimension

In Conrad's vision, a ship's height — what is carried aloft — defines her character. The masts and their burden of canvas are what make a ship visible from a great distance, and what make her subject to the wind's power.

> The tall masts holding aloft the white canvas, spread out like a snare for catching the invisible power of the air, emerge gradually from the water, sail after sail, yard after yard, growing big, till, under the towering structure of her machinery, you perceive the insignificant, tiny speck of her hull. (010-003)

The height of a ship's spars, when seen in perspective against the horizon during a heavy roll, gives the seaman an unforgettable impression of loftiness — more vivid than any amount of practical climbing.

> Such an experience gives you a better impression of the loftiness of your spars than any amount of running aloft could do. (010-005)

## The Seaman's Workplace

Going aloft was a routine but hazardous part of a seaman's duties. In heavy weather, men sent aloft to handle sail or repair damage risked being thrown from the yards by the violent motion of the ship.

> It was a wonder that the men sent aloft were not flung off the yards, the yards not flung off the masts, the masts not flung overboard. (015-009)

Conrad himself went aloft when damage required attention, experiencing firsthand the physical demands and dangers of working at height on a rolling vessel.

> No matter what the damage was, but it was serious enough to induce me to go aloft myself with a couple of hands and the carpenter to see the temporary repairs properly done. (017-004)

The work was strenuous and vocal, as Conrad recalls:

> I shouted from aloft till my head was ready to split. (017-007)

## Observation and Order

The chief mate's duties included keeping watch on the work aloft while simultaneously overseeing cargo operations below decks — a dual attention that defined the comprehensive responsibility of a ship's officer.

> There has been a time when a ship's chief mate, pocketbook in hand and pencil behind his ear, kept one eye aloft upon his riggers and the other down the hatchway on the stevedores, and watched the disposition of his ship's cargo… (013-001)

The appearance of a ship's gear aloft was a matter of professional pride, reflecting the chief officer's standards:

> …that superannuated fellow-craftsman was simply admiring the ship's perfect order aloft, I thought, with some secret pride; for the chief officer is responsible for his ship's appearance… (031-007)

## The Lookout's Perch

Aloft also served as a vantage point for sighting other vessels or dangers. In moments of uncertainty, men would glance upward anxiously, and officers would climb to scan the horizon.

> And I, too, glanced aloft anxiously. (031-007)

From aloft, the second officer identifies a derelict:

> "A waterlogged derelict, I think, sir," said the second officer quietly, coming down from aloft with the binoculars in their case slung across his shoulders… (036-002)
