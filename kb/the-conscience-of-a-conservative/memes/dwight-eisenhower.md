## Biography

Dwight D. Eisenhower (1890–1969) served as the 34th President of the United States from 1953 to 1961. Before his presidency, he was Supreme Allied Commander in Europe during [[world-war-ii|World War II]] and presided over the D-Day invasion. His two terms were marked by [[cold-war|Cold War]] tensions, the expansion of federal programs, and the rise of what came to be called "Modern [[republicanism|Republicanism]]" — an approach Goldwater viewed as a repudiation of conservative principles.

## Role in Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, Eisenhower's presidency represents the problem of "Modern [[republicanism|Republicanism]]" — a [[republic|Republic]]an administration that "gives lip-service to [[states-rights|States' Rights]]" while practicing "a federal 'partnership' in which the federal government calls the tunes" (003-004). Eisenhower "promised to restore limited government" but then "proceeded, after his election, to expand the activities of government" (002-018).

Goldwater recounts a specific moment: "In the Summer of 1952, shortly after the [[republic|Republic]]an Convention, the two men who had battled for the Presidential nomination met at Morningside Heights, New York, to discuss the problem of taxes and spending" (007-013). The meeting between Eisenhower and Taft symbolized the tension within the [[republic|Republic]]an Party between conservative and moderate wings.

## Key Events

- 1952 election: Defeated Adlai Stevenson on a platform of fiscal restraint
- Morningside Heights meeting: Discussed tax and spending policy with [[robert-taft|Senator Taft]] (007-013)
- 1956 re-election: Continued expansion of federal programs despite conservative opposition
- Summit diplomacy: Engaged with Soviet [[nikita-khrushchev|Premier Khrushchev]] at [[cold-war|Cold War]] summits (010-034)
- [[cold-war|Cold War]] policy: Managed U.S.-Soviet relations during a period of intense ideological competition

## Relation to Conservatism

Eisenhower's "Modern [[republicanism|Republicanism]]" is, for Goldwater, the primary example of how [[republic|Republic]]ans abandoned [[conservatism|Conservative]] principles. Rather than "divesting themselves of the power they have been given" (002-022), the Eisenhower administration accepted and expanded the [[new-deal|New Deal]] framework. This transformed the [[republic|Republic]]an Party into what Goldwater dismissively terms a party of "dime-store [[new-deal|New Deal]]ers" — indistinguishable from Democrats on the fundamental question of government's proper scope.
