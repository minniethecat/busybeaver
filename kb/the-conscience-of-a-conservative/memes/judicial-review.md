## Definition

Judicial review is the power of courts — ultimately the Supreme Court — to determine whether legislative and executive actions conform to the Constitution and to invalidate those that do not. Goldwater does not challenge the legitimacy of judicial review itself but critiques its expansive application, particularly in cases where the Court, in his view, exceeds its constitutional role and legislates from the bench. "I have great respect for the Supreme Court as an institution, but I cannot believe that I display that respect by submitting abjectly to abuses of power by the Court, and by condoning its unconstitutional trespass into the legislative function" (004-013).

## In Conservative Thought

The Conservative concern with judicial review is that it can become, like all unchecked power, "a self-aggrandizing force" (001-011). When the Court substitutes its policy preferences for those of the elected branches — or the states — it violates the separation of powers and the constitutional structure of limited government.

Goldwater's most pointed critique concerns *Brown v. Board of Education*. He argues that "the federal Constitution does not require the States to maintain racially mixed schools" and that "it has never been seriously argued — the argument certainly was not made by the Supreme Court — that the authors of the Fourteenth Amendment intended to alter the Constitutional scheme with respect to education" (004-010). Whatever the merits of desegregation as policy, the Court's reasoning, in his view, exceeded its constitutional authority.

## Historical Context

The scope of judicial review has expanded dramatically since *Marbury v. Madison* (1803) established the principle. Goldwater traces the modern expansion to the Warren Court era, when the Court became an active policymaker in areas — education, criminal procedure, legislative apportionment — previously governed by elected branches.

He distinguishes between respecting the Court as an institution and

> I am therefore not impressed by the claim that the Supreme Court's decision on school integration is the law of the land. The Constitution, and the laws "made in pursuance thereof," are the "supreme law of the land." The Constitution is what its authors intended it to be and said it was—not what the Supreme Court says it is.
> (004-012)
 "submitting abjectly to abuses of power" (004-013). "The Constitution, and the laws 'made in pursuance thereof,' are the 'supreme law of the land'" — not the Court's pronouncements that go beyond the Constitution's text and the framers' intent (004-012).

> (004-013)

## Key Arguments

Goldwater advances a constitutional, not policy-based, critique: the Court lacks authority to create law, and its decisions carry weight only insofar as they faithfully interpret the Constitution. "I am therefore not impressed by the claim that the Supreme Court's decision on school integration is the law of the land" (004-012) — a startling statement that clarifies his view that judicial Supremacy, as distinct from judicial review, has no constitutional basis.
