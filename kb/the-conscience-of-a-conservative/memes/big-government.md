## Overview

"Big government" refers to the expansion of federal authority beyond the bounds prescribed by the Constitution. In *The Conscience of a Conservative*, Barry Goldwater identifies the growth of centralized government power as the central threat to individual freedom, arguing that the federal government has progressively assumed functions never intended by the Founders.

## Constitutional Limits on Government

Goldwater grounds his critique in the Founding Fathers' vision. The Constitution was designed to create a government of limited, enumerated powers. As Goldwater notes, the Founders "conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized for the limited purpose of performing those functions that are necessary to the preservation of liberty" (002-002). The Tenth Amendment embodies this principle: powers not delegated to the federal government are reserved to the states or the people.

The principle of limited government is not merely a preference but a constitutional mandate. Goldwater emphasizes that "the Constitution is a document born of the principle of limited government. There is no reference by either the Founders or the Framers to any other type of government" (002-003). The Bill of Rights serves as "the most explicit set of rules for limiting the functions of government, and which is as binding today as it was when adopted" (002-004).

> It is clear that this view is in direct conflict with the Constitution which is an instrument, above all, for limiting the functions of government, and which is as binding today as when it was written.
> (002-004)

## The Natural Tendency Toward Expansion

Goldwater identifies a fundamental political dynamic: government possesses an inherent drive to grow. "Throughout history, government has proved to be the chief instrument for thwarting man's liberty," he writes (002-005). This tendency is not accidental — it is a structural feature of political power.

The mechanism is straightforward: "the tendency of government to expand in the direction of absolutism is a natural tendency" (002-008). Citizens are tempted to "confer on government vast powers in return for deceptive promises of security" (002-009). This exchange — liberty for promised security — is what Goldwater sees as the engine of government growth.

## The Leviathan State

Goldwater describes the result of unchecked federal expansion as a "Leviathan" — "a vast national authority out of touch with the people, and out of their control" (002-011). This Leviathan becomes involved in every aspect of life: it acts as "banker, mortgage broker, employer, debtor, taxer and spender in all manner of enterprises" (002-014).

The Chicago Tribune, cited by Goldwater, documented that "the federal government is now the 'biggest land owner, banker, mortgage broker, employer, debtor, taxer and spender' in the nation" (002-014). This concentration of economic power in federal hands is what Goldwater sees as the antithesis of a free society.

## Government Welfarism

A principal driver of government expansion has been the growth of welfare programs. Goldwater critiques the political debate as being stuck within narrow boundaries: the argument is "over whether the increase in government welfarism should be small or large" rather than whether the government should be involved at all [P01-004].

Congress, Goldwater observes, "annually deliberates over whether the increase in government welfarism should be small or large" [P01-004], having abandoned the question of constitutional authority. Similarly, "Congress annually deliberates over how to spend billions, having proclaimed that the federal government spends too much" [P01-005]. The result is a self-perpetuating cycle of expansion.

## Decentralization as the Alternative

Goldwater's alternative to big government is decentralized authority. "Individual liberty depends on decentralized government," he argues, and "Congress annually deliberates over measures that would further concentrate power in Washington" [P01-006]. The solution is not merely to make government smaller but to return power to the states and to the people.

The legitimate functions of government, Goldwater acknowledges, "are actually conducive to freedom" (002-006). But these functions are narrow: national defense, the administration of justice, and the protection of individual rights. Everything beyond this constitutional core represents an encroachment on liberty.
