## Definition

Education is the process of imparting knowledge, skills, and values across
generations. In the American federal system, the responsibility for education
has historically rested with state and local governments, not with the federal
government. The U.S. Constitution contains no enumerated power granting the
federal government authority over education, and the Tenth Amendment reserves
all unenumerated powers to the states or to the people. Goldwater grounds his
argument against federal involvement in education firmly in this constitutional
principle, viewing the question not primarily as one of policy preference but
of constitutional legitimacy.

## In Conservative Thought

Within the conservative philosophy articulated in *The Conscience of a
Conservative*, education serves as a central case study of federal overreach.
Goldwater devotes an entire chapter ("Some Notes on Education") to
demonstrating how federal aid to education exemplifies the broader pattern of
government expansion beyond constitutional limits. The conservative position is
not opposition to education — Goldwater affirms its importance as "one of the
great problems of our states" (009-002) — but opposition to federal
intervention that displaces local control, imposes uniform standards, and
creates dependency on Washington.

The education question illustrates two core conservative principles
simultaneously: federalism (the constitutional allocation of power) and freedom
(the preservation of diverse, locally-responsive institutions against centrally
imposed uniformity). Goldwater's argument connects education to the broader
conservative critique of centralized power: just as the federal government
should not dictate economic choices or social arrangements, it should not
dictate how communities educate their children.

## Historical Context

The debate over federal involvement in education intensified in the two decades
preceding Goldwater's book. The 1944 G.I. Bill had demonstrated the scale of
federal education spending; the 1954 *Brown v. Board of Education* decision
placed the federal judiciary at the center of education policy; and the 1958
National Defense Education Act, passed in response to Sputnik, opened a new
front of direct federal funding for schools. By 1960, federal aid to education
bills were regularly introduced in Congress, supported by a coalition of
liberal Democrats and moderate Republicans.

Goldwater's opposition drew on an older tradition of conservative skepticism
toward federal education spending, articulated by figures such as Senator
Robert A. Taft. Goldwater argued that the push for federal aid was driven
less by genuine need than by the political logic of centralized
administration: "The pressure for federal aid to education is, in my opinion,
simply one manifestation of the general trend toward centralization of power
and responsibility in Washington" (009-007).

## Key Arguments

Goldwater advances four principal objections to federal aid to education
(009-004), each corresponding to a distinct dimension of conservative thought:

**1. Constitutional Objection.** The federal government possesses no
constitutional authority over education. "No powers regarding education were
given the federal government" (004-009), and the Tenth Amendment reserves this
authority to the states. This is not a matter of policy preference but of
constitutional fidelity: "The first is that federal intervention in education
is unconstitutional. It is that simple" (009-005).

**2. Fiscal Objection.** The claim that states lack resources for education is,
Goldwater argues, illusory. Federal funds for education are not new money but
money extracted from the same citizens through federal taxation, then returned
with Washington's strings attached: "Every dollar the federal government pays
to State X for education has been taken from the citizens of State X (and every
other state) in taxes" (009-012). The administrative overhead of circulating
money through Washington makes the process inherently inefficient.

**3. Distortion Objection.** Federal aid distorts the true picture of education
costs and needs. By using federal grants to cover portions of education
spending, states obscure the real cost of education from their citizens,
undermining fiscal accountability. Goldwater argues this encourages waste and
prevents informed decision-making at the local level (009-011).

**4. Control Objection.** Federal aid inevitably brings federal control:
"Federal aid to education inevitably means federal control of education"
(009-013). Goldwater argues this is not merely a risk but an inherent feature
of federal funding — that the regulatory apparatus that accompanies federal
dollars begins to shape curriculum, administration, and priorities in ways that
reflect Washington's preferences rather than local communities' values.

Beyond these four objections, Goldwater offers a broader critique of the
philosophy underlying American education. He argues that the pursuit of
uniformity has displaced the pursuit of excellence: "In our attempt to make
education 'fun,' we have neglected the intellectual discipline that is the
only sure foundation of true learning" (009-019). The purpose of education,
he contends, is the cultivation of wisdom and individual excellence — "the
fullest possible development of the individual's potentialities" (009-022) —
not the achievement of standardized outcomes or social engineering.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Conservative Position (Goldwater) | Progressive/Liberal Position |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Constitutional basis | Education is reserved to the states (10th Amendment); no federal enumerated power (004-009) | Federal role justified under General Welfare Clause or Commerce Clause |
| Funding model | Local/state funding with local control; federal aid = taxation disguised as subsidy (009-012) | Federal aid necessary to equalize resources across districts |
| Quality vs. equality | Excellence through diversity and competition; uniform standards depress quality (009-019) | Equity requires federal standards to ensure all children receive adequate education |
| Control | Local communities and parents should control curriculum and priorities (009-013) | Federal standards protect against parochialism and ensure civil rights compliance |

## Mentions in the Text

The concept of education appears throughout Goldwater's book, concentrated in
Chapter IX ("Some Notes on Education") and Chapter IV ("And Civil Rights"):

- **Chapter II (States' Rights)**: Education listed among areas where federal
  government has improperly extended its authority (003-002), (003-005),
  (003-008)
- **Chapter IV (Civil Rights)**: Brown v. Board of Education discussed as
  constitutional question; Goldwater supports the decision's integration goal
  but argues education powers remain with states (004-008)–(004-015)
- **Chapter IX (Some Notes on Education)**: The most sustained treatment,
  presenting the four objections to federal aid and critiquing the
  philosophy of American education (009-002)–(009-022)
- **Chapter VII (Taxes and Spending)**: Federal education spending as an
  example of fiscal inefficiency (007-018)–(007-019)
