## Definition

Social Security is the federal social insurance program established by the Social Security Act of 1935, funded through payroll taxes and providing old-age pensions, survivors' benefits, and disability insurance. In Goldwater's analysis, Social Security represents both a fiscal burden — "the government takes six percent of most payrolls in Social Security Taxes and thus compels millions of individuals to postpone until later years the enjoyment of what they might otherwise have earned" (006-006) — and a moral displacement of individual responsibility.

## The Constitutional Question

A recurring theme in Goldwater's critique is the doubtfulness of Social Security's constitutional basis. The Constitution nowhere grants the federal government explicit authority to administer a retirement system. Goldwater's chapter on welfare states the problem in general terms: "I do not undertake to prove here that Social Security, old-age assistance, relief payments, etc., are unconstitutional. But neither is it certain that they are constitutional" (008-001). The fact that the Supreme Court upheld the Social Security Act in *Helvering v. Davis* (1937) does not, for Goldwater, settle the question of whether the program is consistent with a government of enumerated powers.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater approaches Social Security from multiple angles. Constitutionally, he questions the federal government's authority to administer a compulsory retirement system. Practically, he notes that Social Security expenditures, combined with other trust fund disbursements, must be counted in any realistic picture of federal spending: "if we add to the formal budget figure disbursements from the so-called trust funds for Social Security and the Federal Highway Program — as we must if we are to obtain a realistic picture of federal expenditure — the total would be exceeded" (007-013).

Philosophically, Goldwater's objection to Social Security rests on the conservative principle that "man's development, in both its spiritual and material aspects, is not something that can be directed by outside forces. Every man, for his individual good and for the good of his society, is responsible for his *own* development" (001-009). The compulsory nature of Social Security — the government taking a portion of every paycheck and deciding when and how it will be returned — violates this principle of individual responsibility.

## Fiscal Scale

Goldwater cites a January 1960 *New York Times* report noting that "the expenditures of the Department of Health, Education and Welfare in the fiscal year 1961 (including Social Security payments) would exceed $15,000,000,000. Thus the current results of New Deal legislation are Federal disbursements for human welfare in this country second only to national defense" (008-001). This scale — approximately $15 billion annually in 1961 — demonstrates the magnitude of the welfare commitment that Goldwater believed was unsustainable and fundamentally misaligned with the Constitution's vision of limited government.

## The Compulsion Principle

The conservative objection to Social Security is not primarily about the goal of retirement security but about the means: compulsory participation through payroll taxation. Goldwater articulates this distinction clearly. The program forces individuals to save in a particular way, at a particular rate, for a particular purpose — regardless of their own judgment about how best to provide for their future. "If the Conservative is less anxious than his Liberal brethren to increase Social Security benefits," Goldwater writes, it is because he recognizes that "the choices that govern his life are choices that *he* must make: they cannot be made by any other human being, or by a collectivity of human beings" (001-009).

## Distinction from Private Insurance

Social Security differs fundamentally from private insurance or voluntary retirement savings. In a free market, individuals decide how much to save and how to invest their savings. Under Social Security, the government makes these decisions for them. The "trust fund" label is misleading; unlike a private trust, Social Security funds are not segregated assets owned by the contributor but government obligations subject to political decision-making. Goldwater notes the fiscal dishonesty involved: "when these funds are used in an effort to disguise the actual fiscal situation of the Federal government, it is hardly a Conservative act to remain silent" (007-013).

## Political Context in 1960

By the time Goldwater published *The Conscience of a Conservative* in 1960, Social Security had been in operation for a quarter-century and had become politically untouchable. Republicans as well as Democrats had come to accept the program as permanent. Goldwater's willingness to question it marked him as a more radical conservative than the mainstream of his own party. His critique anticipated later debates about Social Security reform — including partial privatization proposals — that would emerge in subsequent decades.
