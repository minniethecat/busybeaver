## Definition

Property rights are the rights of individuals to acquire, possess, use, and dispose of property — rights that Goldwater grounds in natural law rather than positive legislation. "One of the foremost precepts of the natural law is man's right to the possession and the use of his property" (007-004). This right is not absolute — "every citizen has an obligation to contribute his fair share to the legitimate functions of government" (007-006) — but it establishes a presumption against government expropriation and redistribution that places the burden of justification on the state.

## In Conservative Thought

Property rights are integral to the Conservative philosophy because they link economic freedom to human dignity. Goldwater insists that "the economic and spiritual aspects of man's nature are inextricably intertwined" (001-008). A person who cannot keep the fruits of their labor is not free, regardless of what formal political rights they possess.

The Conservative defense of property rights is also a defense of limited government. If property is held by natural right and not by government grant, then government's claim on property must be justified by reference to "the legitimate functions of government" (007-006) — and not a penny more. "Government does not have an unlimited claim on the earnings of individuals" (007-004).

> Government does not have an unlimited claim on the earnings of individuals. One of the foremost precepts of the natural law is man's right to the possession and the use of his property. How can a man be truly free if he is denied the means to exercise freedom? How can he be free if the fruits of his labor are not his to dispose of, but are treated, instead, as part of a common pool of public wealth? Property and freedom are inseparable: to the extent government takes the one in the form of taxes, it intrudes on the other.
> (007-004)

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's argument for property rights has a clear structure. First, the right originates in natural law, not legislation — it is "man's right," not "the citizen's privilege." Second, "government has a right to claim a share of a person's earnings which is consistent with fairness" (007-008), but the graduated tax exceeds this limit because "its effect, and to a large extent its aim, is to bring down all men to a common level" (007-009).

Third, the progressive erosion of property rights through taxation, regulation, and welfare programs represents a systematic attack on individual autonomy. Each new program "mortgages" the recipient to the federal government, surrendering independence in exchange for benefits (008-018).

## Contrasts

Property rights stand in fundamental tension with redistributive taxation. The tax that "aims to bring down all men to a common level" (007-009) does not merely fund government — it reshapes society against the grain of natural right. The Conservative insists on the priority of property rights; the collectivist treats property as a social resource subject to political allocation.
