## Overview

Individual rights stand at the center of Barry Goldwater's conservative philosophy. In *The Conscience of a Conservative*, he grounds his political argument in the sanctity of the individual and the rights that protect personal freedom against government encroachment. These rights are not grants from the state but inherent attributes of personhood, secured — not created — by the Constitution.

## The Sanctity of the Individual

Goldwater's defense of individual rights begins with a metaphysical claim about human nature. "Every man's most sacred possession is his individual soul — which has an immortal side," he writes (001-007). This spiritual foundation distinguishes the individual from the collective and gives moral weight to the protection of personal freedom.

He elaborates: "Every man, for his individual good and for the good of his society, is responsible for the development of his own soul" (001-009). This responsibility cannot be delegated to the state or submerged in collective identity. When "the separate identity of individual human beings are ignored," the result is a political philosophy that "through its emphasis on collective action, deprives men of their freedom" (001-010).

## Rights and the Constitution

Goldwater distinguishes between different categories of rights, particularly "States' Rights, properly defined — and civil rights, properly defined" (004-003). This distinction is central to his constitutional vision: the federal government has specific enumerated powers to protect certain rights, while other matters are reserved to the states and the people.

The Tenth Amendment provides the constitutional framework: powers not delegated to the federal government are reserved. Goldwater sees this as a crucial safeguard for individual rights, because concentrated power — even when exercised with good intentions — inevitably threatens personal freedom.

> I say an imagined conflict because I deny that there can be a conflict between States' Rights, properly defined — and civil rights, properly defined. If States' "Rights" are so asserted as to encroach upon individual rights that are protected by valid federal laws, then the exercise of state power is a nullity. Conversely, if individual "rights" are so asserted as to infringe upon valid state power, then the assertion of those "rights" is a nullity. The rights themselves do not clash. The conflict arises from a failure to define the two categories of rights correctly, and to assert them lawfully.
>
> (004-003)

## Property Rights

Among individual rights, Goldwater gives special attention to property rights. He asserts "the limited claim on the earnings of individuals" by the state (007-004) and insists on "consideration to the property rights of the individual" (007-006). In the conservative tradition, property rights are inseparable from personal liberty — the freedom to keep the fruits of one's labor is fundamental to all other freedoms.

## Self-Reliance and Freedom

Goldwater connects individual rights to the principle of self-reliance. "The people's welfare depends on individual self-reliance rather than on government programs," he argues [P01-004]. This is not merely a practical preference but a moral principle: rights entail responsibilities, and the exercise of freedom requires the capacity for independent action.

"Individual liberty depends on decentralized government," Goldwater maintains [P01-006]. When power is concentrated in Washington, individual rights become permissions granted by the state rather than inherent attributes of free persons. Decentralization is thus not merely an administrative preference but a structural safeguard for personal freedom.

## The Maximum of Freedom

Goldwater articulates a clear standard: society should aim for "the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order" (001-011). This principle places the burden of proof on those who would restrict liberty, not on those who would exercise it. Government action must justify itself against the baseline presumption of individual freedom.
