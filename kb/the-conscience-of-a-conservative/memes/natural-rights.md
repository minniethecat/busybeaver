## Definition

Natural rights are those rights that belong to individuals by virtue of their human nature — not granted by government, not created by statute, but inherent in the human person and discoverable by reason. Goldwater appeals to "the natural law" as the source of "man's right to the possession and the use of his property" (007-004). This reflects the classical liberal and Thomistic tradition in which natural law provides the moral framework within which positive law must operate: valid civil rights are "asserted and therefore protected by some valid law" (004-006), but the ultimate foundation of rights is not legislative will but natural justice.

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, natural rights thinking is essential to the Conservative defense of limited government. If rights are merely grants from the state, then the state may expand or contract them at will. But if rights are inherent in human nature, then government has "no unlimited claim on the earnings of individuals" (007-004) because property rights precede and constrain the state.

This natural law framework also explains why "freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power" (002-008): the purpose of constitutional government is not to create rights but to secure pre-existing natural rights against infringement. "The legitimate functions of government are actually conducive to freedom" (002-006) precisely because those functions — protecting person and property — serve natural rights.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater invokes natural rights primarily in the context of property and taxation. "One of the foremost precepts of the natural law is man's right to the possession and the use of his property" (007-004). The graduated income tax violates this precept because "its effect, and to a large extent its aim, is to bring down all men to a common level" (007-009) — redistributing property in disregard of natural right.

The concept of natural rights also underlies the distinction between civil rights properly so called and mere policy preferences. A civil right "may be asserted by the common law, or by local or federal statutes, or by the Constitution" (004-006), but these positive law articulations recognize and protect rights that exist prior to their legal formulation. "Unless a right is incorporated in the law, it is not a civil right and is not enforceable by the instruments of the civil law" (004-006) — but the reason certain rights merit legal incorporation is that they are natural rights.

## Contrasts

Natural rights theory stands in opposition to legal positivism, which holds that rights exist only insofar as they are enacted into law. For the positivist, whatever the legislature enacts is a right. For the natural rights theorist, the validity of legislation depends on its conformity to pre-political principles of justice — principles like "man's right to the possession and the use of his property" (007-004).
