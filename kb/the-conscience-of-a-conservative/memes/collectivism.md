## Definition

Collectivism is the political philosophy that treats the individual as subordinate to some collective entity — the state, the nation, a social class, or the community as a whole. Goldwater does not define it in a single sentence but characterizes it through its effects: it "subordinates all other considerations to man's material well-being" (001-004) and treats man "as a part of a general collectivity in which the sacredness and the separate identity of individual human beings are ignored" (001-010). For the Conservative, collectivism is not merely a competing policy preference but a fundamental error about human nature.

## In Conservative Thought

The Conservative rejection of collectivism is absolute. Goldwater argues that Conservatism "has regarded man neither as a potential pawn of other men, nor as a part of a general collectivity in which the sacredness and the separate identity of individual human beings are ignored" (001-010). This is not a matter of degree — it is a categorical opposition between two incompatible views of the human person.

The Conservative understands that "each member of the species is a unique creature" whose "most sacred possession is his individual soul" (001-007). Collectivism, by denying or minimizing individual uniqueness, attacks the foundation of human dignity. This is why Goldwater insists that the conflict between Conservatism and collectivism is not economic but spiritual and philosophical.

## Historical Context

Goldwater observes that collectivism has changed its methods over time while keeping its ultimate goal unchanged. The older form was outright nationalization — "Socialism-through-Nationalization." The newer, more dangerous form is "Socialism-through-Welfarism," which "poses a far greater danger to freedom" precisely because "it is more difficult to combat" (008-010).

"The collectivists have not abandoned their ultimate goal — to subordinate the individual to the State — but their strategy has changed" (008-009). The Welfare State is "the currently favored instrument of collectivization," proceeding "by degrees, and each step is justified by a persuasive humanitarian motive" (008-010).

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's argument against collectivism rests on three premises. First, that collectivism is philosophically wrong about human nature — it denies individual uniqueness and treats persons as interchangeable units. Second, that collectivism is practically destructive — it concentrates power, erodes self-reliance, and creates permanent dependency. "The people's welfare depends on individual self reliance rather than on state paternalism" (P01-004).

Third, that collectivism is deceptively advanced: its contemporary proponents use the language of welfare, compassion, and humanitarian concern to advance policies that progressively subordinate the individual to the state. The rhetorical shift from explicit socialism to "Welfarism" is tactical, not principled.

## Contrasts

| Individualism (Conservative) | Collectivism |
|------------------------------|-------------|
| Man as unique, sacred individual | Man as part of a collectivity |
| Freedom as highest political good | Material well-being as primary |
| Limited, decentralized government | Centralized, expanding state |
| Self-reliance and responsibility | State paternalism and dependency |
| "Man's spiritual nature" (001-005) | Man's material needs |
