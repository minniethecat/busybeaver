## Definition

The General Welfare Clause appears in Article I, Section 8 of the United States Constitution, granting Congress the power to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises, to pay the Debts and provide for the common Defence and general Welfare of the United States." The interpretive question — whether this clause grants an independent power to spend for any purpose Congress deems beneficial, or merely describes the purposes for which the enumerated powers may be exercised — is, for Goldwater, the hinge on which the survival of limited government turns.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater identifies the expansive interpretation of the General Welfare Clause as one of the principal legal mechanisms by which the federal government has escaped constitutional limits. "The sponsors of the first Agriculture Adjustment Act, for instance, defended it on the ground that it promoted the general welfare" (005-010). If "general welfare" means whatever Congress says it means, then the enumeration of specific powers becomes meaningless — a point Goldwater makes repeatedly across multiple policy domains.

The Conservative position is that the General Welfare Clause is not a blank check. It is "perfectly clear" that "no power over agriculture was given to any branch of the national government" (005-010), and the same reasoning applies to education, housing, health care, and the full array of modern federal programs. The clause describes the purposes of the taxing power, not an independent grant of unlimited spending authority.

## Historical Context

The debate over the General Welfare Clause goes back to the Founding. Madison and Hamilton disagreed about its scope — Madison arguing for a narrow reading tied to enumerated powers, Hamilton for a broader interpretation. Goldwater traces the modern expansion of federal authority to the victory of the Hamiltonian reading combined with the twentieth-century rejection of constitutional limits altogether.

The pattern he identifies is consistent: "no power over agriculture," "no powers regarding education were given the federal government" (004-009), yet in each case the General Welfare Clause is invoked to justify federal programs. The clause becomes a constitutional loophole through which the entire structure of limited government is evaded.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's critique of the General Welfare Clause expansion has several components. First, it violates the constitutional design of enumerated powers. If Congress may do anything it considers conducive to the "general welfare," then the enumeration of specific powers in Article I, Section 8 is surplusage. Second, it destroys federalism by allowing the federal government to operate in domains reserved to the states. Third, it enables the very programs — agricultural subsidies, federal education aid, social welfare — that create the dependency and centralization Conservatives oppose.

## Contrasts

| Madisonian Reading | Hamiltonian/Expansive Reading |
|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Clause describes purposes of enumerated powers | Clause grants independent spending power |
| "No power over agriculture was given" (005-010) | Any program promoting "general welfare" is authorized |
| Preserves federalism | Enables centralization |
| Consistent with limited government | Equivalent to unlimited government |

## Mentions in the Text
