## Overview

The Supreme Court of the United States is the highest court in the federal judiciary, established by Article III of the Constitution. It has the power of judicial review — the authority to determine whether legislative and executive actions conform to the Constitution. This power, while well-established since *Marbury v. Madison* (1803), is the source of Goldwater's most pointed constitutional critiques.

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater states: "I have great respect for the Supreme Court as an institution, but I cannot believe that I display that respect by submitting abjectly to abuses of power by the Court, and by condoning its unconstitutional trespass into the legislative function" (004-013).

His concern is that the Court has become a policymaker rather than a constitutional interpreter. When the Court decides cases based on its own policy preferences rather than the Constitution's text and the framers' intent, it violates separation of powers: "I am therefore not impressed by the claim that the Supreme Court's decision on school integration is the law of the land. The Constitution, and the laws 'made in pursuance thereof,' are the 'supreme law of the land'" (004-012).

## Key Decisions Discussed

- *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954): Supported objectives, critiqued constitutional reasoning
- Federal authority over education: "no powers regarding education were given the federal government" (004-009)

> It so happens that I am in agreement with the objectives of the Supreme Court as stated in the Brown decision. I believe that it is both wise and just for negro children to attend the same schools as whites, and that to deny them this opportunity carries with it strong implications of inferiority. I am not prepared, however, to impose that judgment of mine on the people of Mississippi or South Carolina, or to tell them what methods should be adopted and what pace should be kept in striving toward that goal. That is their business, not mine. I believe that the problem of race relations, like all social and cultural problems, is best handled by the people directly concerned. Social and cultural change, however desirable, should not be effected by the engines of national power. Let us, through persuasion and education, seek to improve institutions we deem defective. But let us, in doing so, respect the orderly processes of the law. Any other course enthrones tyrants and dooms freedom.
> (004-015)
