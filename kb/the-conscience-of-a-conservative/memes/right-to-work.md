## Definition

Right-to-work laws prohibit agreements between employers and labor unions that make union membership or the payment of union dues a condition of employment. Under such laws — enacted at the state level under Section 14(b) of the Taft-Hartley Act — workers in unionized workplaces may choose not to join the union or pay dues while still receiving the benefits of collective bargaining. For Goldwater, the right-to-work principle is not anti-union but pro-freedom: it asserts that the individual worker's right to associate or not associate is more fundamental than the union's interest in compulsory membership.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's defense of right-to-work laws in Chapter VI ("Freedom for Labor") exemplifies his approach to labor policy: the problem is not unions per se but the coercive power that government has granted them. Compulsory unionism, in his analysis, violates the same principle of individual freedom that conservatives defend in every other domain. Just as the state may not compel a citizen to join a church or political party, it should not compel a worker to join a union.

The right-to-work argument also illustrates Goldwater's federalist approach to labor policy. Rather than proposing federal legislation to reform unions, he advocates restoring the competitive balance that federal law has distorted. Right-to-work laws, enacted by states exercising their reserved powers, create a laboratory of labor policy. States that protect worker freedom will attract industry; states that privilege union power will drive it away.

## Key Arguments

**Freedom of association requires the right not to associate** (006-011, 006-015). Goldwater grounds the right-to-work argument in the constitutional First Amendment principle: freedom of association logically entails the freedom not to associate. Compulsory union membership violates this principle by forcing workers into an organization they may oppose.

**Union power derives from government-granted privileges, not market forces** (006-008). Goldwater argues that "Big Labor's" power to coerce workers does not arise from voluntary association but from legal immunities and privileges granted by federal law. Right-to-work laws partially restore the balance by removing the state's imprimatur from compulsory membership.

**Right-to-work laws help the situation of working people** (006-027, (006-017, 006-019). Far from harming workers, Goldwater contends that right-to-work legislation improves their condition by restoring individual bargaining power, encouraging union responsiveness to member preferences, and attracting employers to states that protect worker freedom.

**The remedy is legislative, not judicial** (006-017). Goldwater proposes that Congress exercise its authority under the Taft-Hartley Act to protect state right-to-work laws, and that additional states adopt such laws. The goal is not to destroy unions but to make union membership genuinely voluntary — an expression of worker choice rather than legal compulsion.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Right to Work | Compulsory Unionism |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Worker choice | Individual decides whether to join | Union membership or dues required |
| Freedom basis | Freedom not to associate | Collective solidarity |
| Government role | Neutral arbiter protecting individual rights | Privileging union power in labor relations |
| Economic effect | Attracts investment and job growth (Goldwater's view) | Protects union wages at cost of employment |
| Constitutional basis | First Amendment freedom of association | Statutory labor law (Wagner Act framework) |
