**Nikita Khrushchev** (1894–1971) was the leader of the [[soviet-menace|Soviet Union]] from 1953
to 1964, serving as First Secretary of the [[communism|Communist]] Party and later as Premier.
In [[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]],
[[barry-goldwater|Barry Goldwater]] uses Khrushchev as the personal embodiment
of the [[soviet-menace|Soviet threat]]—and as the focal point for his critique of American summit
diplomacy, cultural exchange, and the "negotiation" strategy of the [[dwight-eisenhower|Eisenhower]]
administration.

## Summit Diplomacy and Its Discontents

At the time of the book's writing, the world was anticipating a series of
diplomatic conferences between East and West. Goldwater describes the scene
with evident skepticism (010-034):

> As I write, the world is waiting for another round of diplomatic conferences
> between East and West. A full scale summit meeting is scheduled for Spring;
> later on, President Eisenhower and Premier Khrushchev will have further talks
> in the Soviet Union. And we are told that this is only the beginning of a
> long-range American policy to try to settle world problems by "negotiation."
> (010-034)

Goldwater's objection is not to negotiation per se but to the asymmetry of its
benefits. The [[communism|Communist]]s, he argues, gain advantage from the mere act of
negotiation—elevating their status, legitimizing their positions—whether or
not any substantive agreement is reached (010-037).

## Cultural Exchange as Propaganda

Khrushchev's 1959 tour of the United States—and the broader Soviet-American
cultural exchange program—receives particular criticism. Goldwater disputes the
notion that exposing Americans to Soviet culture promotes understanding, arguing
instead that it promotes complacency (010-044):

> A performance by the Bolshoi Ballet, or a tour of the United States by Nikita
> Khrushchev, is certainly not calculated to correct that deficiency. (010-044)

The real objective of such exchanges, in Goldwater's analysis, is not to convert
Americans to [[communism|Communism]]—"by that test, of course, the operation is almost a
complete failure"—but to make Americans "tolerant of [[communism|Communism]]" (010-048). This
tolerance, he fears, undermines the national will to sustain the sacrifices
necessary to prevail in the [[cold-war|Cold War]].

## The Recognition Question

Goldwater extends his critique of engagement with Khrushchev's regime to the
very question of diplomatic recognition. The cultural exchange program, he
argues, is only a "logical extension of granting diplomatic recognition to
[[communism|Communist]] governments"—and therefore the entire edifice of engagement deserves
reexamination (010-050). While the book does not call for breaking diplomatic
relations, it questions whether the benefits of recognition—intelligence
gathering, communication channels—outweigh the costs in legitimizing a regime
dedicated to America's destruction.

## Significance in the Book's Argument

Khrushchev serves a specific rhetorical function in the book: he is the human
face of the threat that, in Goldwater's view, American policy treats with
insufficient seriousness. By focusing on the personal dimension of Soviet
leadership—Khrushchev's tour, his meetings with [[dwight-eisenhower|Eisenhower]], his role in summit
diplomacy—Goldwater makes the abstract strategic argument about [[communism|Communist]]
strategy concrete and accessible.

The Khrushchev passages also reveal a tension in Goldwater's position. While he
criticizes the [[dwight-eisenhower|Eisenhower]] administration's engagement policies, he does not
propose a complete break in relations, instead urging a recalibration that
would make "victory over [[communism|Communist]] tyranny" rather than international comity
the organizing principle of [[foreign-policy|American foreign policy]].
