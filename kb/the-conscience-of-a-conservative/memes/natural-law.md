## Definition

Natural law is the philosophical tradition holding that there exists a higher
moral order, accessible to human reason, that stands above and judges all human
laws. Rights derived from natural law are not grants from government but
inherent properties of human personhood; government's legitimate function is
to secure these pre-existing rights, not to create or redistribute them. In
the Western tradition, natural law thinking runs from Aristotle through Cicero,
Aquinas, and Locke to the American Founders, who grounded the Declaration of
Independence's appeal to "the Laws of Nature and of Nature's God" in this
tradition.

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, natural law provides the philosophical foundation for limited
government. If rights precede government — if they are "endowed by their
Creator" rather than conferred by the state — then government cannot
legitimately abridge them. This is not merely a rhetorical flourish but the
operating logic of Goldwater's entire political philosophy: "The laws of God,
and of nature, have existed since before man was created" [P01-010].
Government is not the source of rights but their servant.

Natural law also grounds Goldwater's argument for economic freedom. "One of the
foremost precepts of the natural law is man's right to the possession of his
property" (007-004). This right is not created by economic theory or political
preference; it is, in Goldwater's view, a dictate of natural justice. Taxation
that confiscates property — as Goldwater argues the graduated income tax does —
is therefore not merely unwise policy but a violation of natural right.

## Historical Context

The American Founding was deeply shaped by natural law philosophy. Jefferson's
Declaration of Independence, with its appeal to "the Laws of Nature and of
Nature's God," reflects the Lockean tradition in which individuals possess
natural rights to life, liberty, and property, and form governments to secure
those rights. The Constitution's Bill of Rights is best understood not as a
grant of rights but as a recognition and enumeration of pre-existing natural
rights that government is forbidden to violate.

By the mid-twentieth century, however, natural law thinking had been largely
displaced in American jurisprudence by legal positivism — the view that law is
whatever the sovereign declares it to be, with no higher standard of justice
to constrain it. Goldwater's invocation of natural law represents a conscious
effort to revive this older tradition as a check on the expansion of state
power. His position aligns with that of conservative thinkers like Russell Kirk
and Richard Weaver, who argued that the abandonment of transcendent moral
standards had left individual rights vulnerable to majoritarian erosion.

## Key Arguments

**Rights Precede Government.** Goldwater's central natural-law claim is that
individual rights are not created by the Constitution or by legislation but are
inherent in human nature. Government's role is declaratory and protective, not
creative: it recognizes and secures rights that already exist. This inverts the
modern assumption that rights are whatever the government says they are.

**Property as a Natural Right.** Goldwater identifies the right to property as
a direct implication of natural law. Because individuals own their labor and
its fruits by nature, government seizure of property through taxation beyond
what is necessary for legitimate government functions violates natural justice
(007-004). The graduated tax is particularly objectionable on this ground: it
is "a confiscatory tax" (007-009) that treats property not as a natural right
but as a government indulgence.

**Natural Law Constrains Government.** The most important practical implication
of natural law is that government itself is bound by moral limits that it
cannot legitimately override. No legislative majority, no matter how large, may
violate natural rights. This provides the philosophical basis for
constitutionalism: the Constitution's limits on government are not arbitrary
procedural rules but codifications of natural-law principles that would bind
government even in the Constitution's absence.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Natural Law (Conservative) | Legal Positivism (Modern Liberal) |
|-----------|---------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Source of rights | Inherent in human nature; endowed by Creator [P01-010] | Conferred by legislation and judicial decision |
| Government's role | Secure pre-existing rights; limited by moral law (007-004) | Create and allocate rights through democratic process |
| Property rights | Natural right to the fruits of one's labor | Social construct subject to redistribution for public purposes |
| Limits on government | Transcendent moral standards bind all human authority | Only procedural limits; substance determined by majority will |

## Mentions in the Text

Goldwater's references to natural law are concentrated but significant:

- **Foreword**: "The laws of God, and of nature, have existed since before man
  was created. They exist still" [P01-010]
- **Chapter I (The Conscience of a Conservative)**: The conservative
  philosophy grounded in permanent truths rather than passing fashions
  (001-012)
- **Chapter VII (Taxes and Spending)**: "One of the foremost precepts of the
  natural law is man's right to the possession of his property" (007-004)
- **Chapter V (Freedom for the Farmer)**: Natural economic laws vs.
  government intervention (005-014), (005-017)
