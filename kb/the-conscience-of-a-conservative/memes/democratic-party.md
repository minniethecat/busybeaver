The **Democratic Party** is one of the two major political parties in the United States. In *The Conscience of a Conservative* (1960), Senator Barry Goldwater identified the Democratic Party as the primary vehicle through which centralized federal power and the Welfare State had expanded in twentieth-century America.

## Abandonment of States' Rights

Goldwater traced the Democratic Party's transformation to its capture by left-wing elements. He wrote that the "abandonment of States' Rights by the national Democratic Party" was "an event that occurred some years ago when that party was captured by the Socialist ideologues in and about the labor movement" (003-003). This ideological shift, Goldwater argued, had profound consequences: it severed the party from the constitutional principle that had been the "cornerstone of the Republic" and the "chief bulwark against the encroachment of individual freedom by Big Government" (003-003).

In practice, Goldwater charged, the Democratic Party, like the Republican Party, "summons the coercive power of the federal government whenever national leaders conclude that the States are not performing satisfactorily" (003-004). This represented a structural abandonment, not merely a rhetorical one: the actual mechanisms of federal power were deployed against the States regardless of which party held office.

## Franklin Roosevelt and the New Deal

Goldwater identified Franklin D. Roosevelt as the pivotal figure in the Democratic Party's transformation: "Franklin Roosevelt's rapid conversion from Constitutionalism to the doctrine of unlimited government, is an oft-told story" (003-003). Under Roosevelt, the Democratic Party inaugurated the New Deal and Fair Deal programs that Goldwater regarded as the foundation of the modern Welfare State.

## Spending Demands and Fiscal Expansion

Goldwater documented the Democratic Party's consistent push for higher federal expenditures. He observed that "every year the Democratic national leadership demands that the federal government spend more than it is spending, and that Republicans propose to spend" (007-017). As a concrete example, he cited the Democratic National Advisory Council's manifesto issued shortly before President Eisenhower's 1961 budget: this document called "for profligate spending increases in nearly every department of the federal government," with "the demands for increases in domestic spending alone could hardly cost less than $20 billion a year" (007-017). For Goldwater, this relentless fiscal expansionism was evidence that the Democratic Party's commitment to centralized government was not merely ideological but operational and ongoing.

## Union Influence and Political Money

Goldwater criticized the Democratic Party's dependence on labor union political spending. He noted that union leaders could direct member dues to support the Democratic Party without conducting a membership poll: "the union's decision whether to support candidate X or candidate Y — whether to help the Republican Party or the Democratic Party — is not reached by a poll of the union membership" (006-025). This concentration of power in the hands of "a handful of top union officers" (006-025) was, in Goldwater's view, a corruption of democratic process.

## Dean Acheson and the Democratic Defense of the New Deal

Goldwater identified a Democratic counterpart to the Republican "Modern Republicanism" literature. Dean Acheson, Truman's Secretary of State, had published *A Democrat Looks at His Party*, in which he wrote approvingly that the New Deal "conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done" (001-012). For Goldwater, this formulation captured precisely what was wrong with the Democratic vision of government: it treated the federal apparatus as an all-purpose instrument with no constitutional limit on its scope. Goldwater saw in Acheson's words the philosophical endpoint of the Democratic Party's trajectory — a vision of government so expansive that it could justify any intervention, any program, and any expenditure, constrained only by the will of the majority and the ambitions of its leaders.

The contrast Goldwater drew was stark: where conservatives saw the Constitution as a charter of limited and enumerated powers, the Democratic Party, through figures like Acheson, had come to see the federal government as a blank check. "The federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done" was, for Goldwater, not a description of democratic governance but a formula for unlimited government.

## The "Me-Too" Republican Problem

Goldwater's critique of the Democratic Party was intertwined with his frustration at the Republican Party for imitating Democratic policies. He saw the two parties converging on an interventionist consensus, arguing that "today *neither* of our two parties maintains a meaningful commitment to the principle of States' Rights" (003-003). This convergence, in Goldwater's view, left voters without a genuine conservative alternative.

> Franklin Roosevelt's rapid conversion from Constitutionalism to the doctrine of unlimited government, is an oft-told story. But I am here concerned not so much by the abandonment of States' Rights by the national Democratic Party—an event that occurred some years ago when that party was captured by the Socialist ideologues in and about the labor movement—as by the unmistakable tendency of the Republican Party to adopt the same course. The result is that today neither of our two parties maintains a meaningful commitment to the principle of States' Rights. Thus, the cornerstone of the Republic, our chief bulwark against the encroachment of individual freedom by Big Government, is fast disappearing under the piling sands of absolutism.
> (003-003)
