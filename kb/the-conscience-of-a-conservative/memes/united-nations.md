The **United Nations** (UN) is an international organization founded in 1945
after [[world-war-ii|World War II]] to promote peace, security, and cooperation
among nations. In [[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a
Conservative]], [[barry-goldwater|Barry Goldwater]] devotes sustained attention
to the UN as a case study in what he regards as the confusion of American
foreign policy objectives—subordinating national interests to international
consensus.

## Goldwater's Critique

The book's chapter on foreign policy ("The Soviet Menace") presents a
multi-layered critique of American participation in the United Nations. The
argument proceeds from a fundamental question about the purpose of American
foreign policy (010-062):

> Is the perpetuation of an international debating forum, for its own sake, the
> primary objective of American policy? If so, there is much to be said for our
> past record of subordinating our national interests to the interests of that
> body. But if our objective is victory over Communist tyranny, a wholly
> different kind of United Nations policy would seem to be called for. (010-062)

## The Communist Presence

Goldwater emphasizes that the UN's structure gives Communist powers an
institutional foothold in international diplomacy (010-063):

> The United Nations, we must remember, is in part a Communist organization. The
> Communists always have at least one seat in its major policy-making body, the
> Security Council; and the Soviet Union's permanent veto power in that body
> allows the Kremlin to block any action, on a substantial issue, that it
> considers inimical to its interests. (010-063)

This structural feature, in Goldwater's analysis, means that "many of the
policies that emerge from the deliberations of the United Nations are not
policies that are in the best interest of the United States" (010-064). The
asymmetry, he argues, is compounded by the fact that the Soviet Union feels no
reciprocal obligation to abide by UN resolutions when they contradict Soviet
interests (010-065).

## Propaganda and Financial Burden

The UN, Goldwater contends, also serves as "a unique forum for Communist
propaganda" (010-066). While the United States can also voice its views, the
Communist advantage lies in the fact that "their lies and distortions can be
poured into the ears of millions of people in non-Communist countries" through
the UN platform.

A further objection concerns the financial structure of the organization. The
UN's system of assessed contributions, Goldwater argues, places a
"unwarranted financial burden on the American taxpayer" by applying a formula
he likens to the Marxist principle of "from each according to his ability"
(010-067).

## Sovereignty Concerns

Beyond the political and financial objections, Goldwater raises a constitutional
concern: that American involvement in the UN "may be leading to an
unconstitutional surrender of American sovereignty" (010-068). He notes that
UN activities have "already made strong inroads against the sovereign powers of
Member Nations" and warns that this trend threatens the constitutional framework
that reserves foreign policy authority to the American people through their
elected representatives.

## Reform, Not Withdrawal

Despite his extensive criticisms, Goldwater stops short of advocating American
withdrawal from the UN, acknowledging that "for a number of reasons that course
is unfeasible" (010-069). The appropriate response, in his view, is to
recalibrate American engagement so that "the nature of our commitment is such
as to advance American interests"—which would "involve changes in some of our
present attitudes toward that organization."
