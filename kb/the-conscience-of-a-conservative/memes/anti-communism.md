## Definition

Anti-Communism is the political and ideological opposition to Communist expansion, grounded in the recognition that Communism represents a fundamental threat to freedom and self-government. For Goldwater, anti-Communism is not merely one foreign policy preference among many — it is the organizing principle that should guide all American engagement with the world. "American aid should be furnished only to friendly, anti-Communist nations that are willing to join with us in the struggle for freedom" (010-029).

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's anti-Communism is distinguished from mere rhetorical opposition by its insistence on consistency and discrimination. He argues that "it makes little sense to try to promote anti-Communism by giving money to governments that are not anti-Communist, that are, indeed, far more inclined to the Soviet-type society than to a free one" (010-011). This critique targets a foreign policy that proclaims opposition to Communism while subsidizing regimes whose policies and sympathies align with the Soviet bloc.

The conservative approach to anti-Communism demands that actions match words. Foreign aid given to neutral or hostile governments not only wastes American resources but actively undermines the anti-Communist cause by strengthening regimes that oppose American interests. "Let us remember that the foreign policies of many of the allegedly neutral nations that receive our aid are not 'neutral' at all" (010-011).

## The Alliance Gap

Goldwater identifies a critical deficiency in the American anti-Communist strategy: the alliance system does not cover the territories where Communist expansion is most active. "There are huge areas of the non-Communist world that the alliances do not touch. Nor — even assuming America is strong enough to guard a worldwide defense perimeter — is there any prospect of bringing these areas into the system" (010-005). The neutral countries of the Middle East, Africa, and Southern Asia, having "refused to align themselves with the anti-Communist cause," are precisely "where the Communists are making significant strides" (010-005).

## Discriminating Aid

Goldwater's anti-Communism translates into a specific policy prescription: "American aid should be furnished only to friendly, anti-Communist nations that are willing to join with us in the struggle for freedom. Moreover, our aid should take the form of loans or technical assistance, not gifts" (010-029). This discriminating approach contrasts with the undifferentiated aid programs that Goldwater criticizes, where the criterion for receiving American assistance is not alignment with American values but the mere existence of a need or a strategic location.

## Distinction from McCarthyism

Goldwater's anti-Communism should be distinguished from the domestic anti-Communist crusades of the early 1950s. His focus is on foreign policy and the global struggle between freedom and totalitarianism, not on domestic loyalty investigations. The enemy is abroad — Soviet expansionism — and the proper response is a foreign policy that consistently and intelligently opposes it.

## The Problem of Neutralism

Goldwater identified the Western policy of engaging with neutral nations as a fundamental strategic error. He singled out specific regimes as examples of the contradiction at the heart of American foreign policy: "Is Sukarno's Indonesia neutral when it encourages Red Chinese aggression? Or Nehru's India when it censures the Western effort to recover Suez but refuses to censure the Soviet invasion of Hungary? Or Nasser's United Arab Republic which equips its armed forces with Communist weapons and Communist personnel?" (010-011). For Goldwater, these were not difficult cases requiring nuanced diplomatic judgment; they were clear-cut instances of governments that, whatever their professed neutrality, were objectively aligned against American interests and the cause of freedom.

The policy implication Goldwater drew was unambiguous: aid to such regimes was not merely wasteful but actively harmful. "Is American aid likely to make these nations less pro-Communist?" he asked rhetorically. "Has it?" (010-011). The answer, in Goldwater's view, was self-evident: nations that received American assistance while pursuing anti-American policies had no incentive to change course. American generosity was being exploited, not reciprocated, and the net effect was to strengthen adversaries rather than allies.

## Anti-Communism as a Discriminating Principle

Goldwater summarized his anti-Communist foreign policy in a single, discriminating rule: aid should go "only to friendly, anti-Communist nations that are willing to join with us in the struggle for freedom," and should "take the form of loans or technical assistance, not gifts" (010-029). This principle of discrimination — of making hard choices about who deserved American support — was, for Goldwater, the essence of a serious anti-Communist foreign policy. It stood in direct opposition to what he saw as the prevailing bipartisan approach of distributing aid broadly and indiscriminately, without regard for the recipient's alignment in the Cold War struggle.
