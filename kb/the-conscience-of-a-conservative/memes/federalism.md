## Definition

Federalism is a system of government in which sovereignty is constitutionally
divided between a central governing authority and constituent political units
(states). In the American constitutional order, the federal government
possesses only those powers enumerated in the Constitution, while all remaining
powers are reserved to the states or the people under the Tenth Amendment. This
division is not merely administrative but structural: it establishes two
distinct spheres of sovereign authority, each exercising direct power over
citizens within its domain.

Federalism is distinct from both a unitary system (in which all power flows
from a single central authority) and a confederation (in which the central
authority is subordinate to the member states). The American system is one of
dual sovereignty — "Neither the states nor the federal government is superior
to the other; each is supreme within its own sphere" (004-004).

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's conservatism is fundamentally federalist. His core argument — that
the federal government has expanded far beyond its constitutional limits —
presupposes a federalist framework in which such limits are both real and
binding. For Goldwater, federalism is not merely a structural preference but a
moral imperative: it is the institutional expression of the conservative
conviction that power must be dispersed, checked, and kept close to the people.

"Individual liberty depends on decentralized government" [P01-006]. This
sentence captures the conservative theory of federalism: liberty is best
preserved when power is distributed across multiple centers of authority, each
limited in scope and competitive with the others. When power concentrates in a
single, remote central government, it becomes unaccountable, inefficient, and
ultimately threatening to freedom.

Goldwater's federalism is grounded in the Tenth Amendment, which he treats not
as a legal technicality but as the constitutional cornerstone of limited
government: "The Tenth Amendment is not 'a general assumption of power by the
states' but a specific reservation of power to the states and to the people"
(003-011). His critique of the modern federal government is that it has
rendered this amendment a dead letter, "fallen into disrepair" (002-011)
through decades of judicial and legislative erosion.

## Historical Context

The American federal system was designed by the Framers as a compromise between
the centralizers (who favored a strong national government) and the advocates
of state sovereignty. The ratification debates of 1787–1788, recorded in *The
Federalist Papers*, reveal the centrality of federalism to the constitutional
design. James Madison in Federalist No. 45 emphasized that the powers of the
federal government are "few and defined" while those of the states are
"numerous and indefinite."

By the mid-twentieth century, however, this balance had shifted decisively. The
New Deal of the 1930s, the expansion of federal authority during World War II,
and the post-war welfare state had each contributed to what Goldwater described
as "the natural tendency of government to expand" (002-008). The Supreme
Court's broad interpretation of the Commerce Clause and the General Welfare
Clause had, in Goldwater's view, effectively removed meaningful constitutional
limits on federal power. Goldwater wrote at a moment when federalism seemed in
full retreat, and his book aimed to revive it as a living political principle.

## Key Arguments

**Federalism as Liberty's Architecture.** Goldwater argues that the
constitutional division of power between federal and state governments is not
merely a matter of administrative convenience but the structural guarantee of
individual freedom. By preventing the accumulation of power in a single center,
federalism makes tyranny more difficult: "The accumulation of power in a
central government that is remote from the people is a grave threat to their
liberties" (003-012).

**The Tenth Amendment as Enforceable Law.** Goldwater insists that the Tenth
Amendment is not a symbolic gesture but a binding constitutional command. Under
its terms, any power not delegated to the federal government is reserved to the
states. Education, law enforcement, land use, and most areas of domestic policy
are therefore, in Goldwater's view, outside federal jurisdiction. The federal
government's entry into these fields represents not a legitimate exercise of
national authority but a usurpation of powers that belong to the states and the
people (004-009).

**Federal Aid as Coercion.** A central theme in Goldwater's federalism is that
federal grants-in-aid are not neutral assistance but instruments of control.
"When the federal government offers money for a program, it effectively
dictates the terms of that program" (003-006). This mechanism — offering money
with conditions attached — allows the federal government to regulate areas it
has no constitutional authority to regulate directly, undermining federalism
through fiscal pressure rather than explicit legislation.

**The Presumption of State Authority.** Goldwater argues for a default rule:
where the Constitution is silent, the presumption must favor the states, not
the federal government. "Thanks to the Tenth Amendment, this presumption must
always be in favor of the states" (003-010). This reverses the practical
operation of modern federalism, in which the federal government is presumed to
have authority unless the Constitution explicitly prohibits it.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Federalism (Goldwater) | Centralization (Modern Liberalism) |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Source of authority | States possess original sovereignty; federal power is delegated and limited (003-010) | National government embodies the will of the whole people and may address national problems directly |
| Role of 10th Amendment | Binding reservation of power to states (003-011) | Largely symbolic; federal power expands through Commerce/General Welfare Clauses |
| Federal grants | Instruments of coercion that undermine state autonomy (003-006) | Necessary and proper tools for addressing national problems |
| Liberty protection | Dispersed power across multiple centers prevents tyranny [P01-006] | National standards protect individual rights against parochial state action |

## Mentions in the Text

Goldwater does not use the term "federalism" explicitly, but the concept
permeates the entire book:

- **Foreword**: "Individual liberty depends on decentralized government"
  [P01-006]
- **Chapter I (The Conscience of a Conservative)**: States' Rights as the
  framework for limited government (002-002)–(002-005)
- **Chapter III (States' Rights)**: The Tenth Amendment as federalism's
  constitutional anchor (003-010)–(003-012)
- **Chapter IV (And Civil Rights)**: Federalism applied to the most contentious
  domestic issue of the era (004-004), (004-009)
- **Chapter IX (Some Notes on Education)**: Federal education aid as a case
  study in federalism's erosion (009-005)
