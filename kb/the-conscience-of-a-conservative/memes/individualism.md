## Definition

Individualism, as Goldwater presents it, is the recognition that "each member of the species is a unique creature" whose "most sacred possession is his individual soul — which has an immortal side, but also a mortal one" (001-007). It is not the atomistic individualism of libertarian caricature but a philosophically grounded view of human nature that insists on the irreducible uniqueness and moral responsibility of each person. The political implications are direct: "man's development, in both its spiritual and material aspects, is not something that can be directed by outside forces. Every man, for his individual good and for the good of his society, is responsible for his own development" (001-009).

## In Conservative Thought

Individualism is the anthropological premise from which all Conservative political conclusions follow. Because each person is unique and responsible for their own development, government must respect individual autonomy by confining itself to "the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order" (001-011). The Conservative "looks upon politics as the art of achieving the maximum amount of freedom for individuals" precisely because the individual — not the state, the community, or the collective — is the fundamental unit of moral and political concern.

Goldwater's individualism is both spiritual and economic. "The economic and spiritual aspects of man's nature are inextricably intertwined. He cannot be economically free, or even economically efficient, if he is enslaved politically; conversely, man's political freedom is illusory if he is dependent for his economic needs on the State" (001-008).

## Historical Context

The American founding was built on individualist premises. The framers' system of "restraints" was "directed not only against individual tyrants, but also against a tyranny of the masses" (002-009) — protecting the individual against both the one and the many. The entire constitutional architecture assumes that "the people's welfare depends on individual self reliance rather than on state paternalism" (P01-004).

This understanding has been eroded by the rise of the Welfare State, which replaces individual responsibility with state provision, and by collectivist ideologies that treat persons as members of groups rather than as unique individuals.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's argument for individualism is philosophical rather than economic. The first thing the Conservative learns about man is that "each member of the species is a unique creature" (001-007). From this follows the rejection of systems that "treat man as a pawn" or that "ignore the sacredness and the separate identity of individual human beings" (001-010).

Individualism also has practical benefits. Self-reliance produces character; dependency produces passivity. The farmer who "wants to stand on his own feet" and is "prepared to take his chances in the free market" embodies individualist virtues (005-018). The welfare recipient who "mortgages himself to the federal government" represents individualism's erosion (008-018).

## Contrasts

Individualism stands opposed to collectivism at every point: where individualism sees unique persons, collectivism sees interchangeable members of a category. Where individualism insists on personal responsibility, collectivism substitutes state direction. The conflict is not between different policy preferences but between "two basically different concepts of the nature of man" (001-004).
