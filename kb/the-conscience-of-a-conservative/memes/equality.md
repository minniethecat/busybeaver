## Definition

Goldwater draws a fundamental distinction between two conceptions of equality. The first — equality before God and equality of civil rights — he affirms as consistent with both natural law and the American constitutional tradition. The second — the egalitarian project of using state power to level social and economic differences — he rejects as "an objective that does violence both to the charter of the Republic and the laws of Nature" (007-009).

His core claim is deceptively simple: "We are all equal in the eyes of God but we are equal *in no other respect*" (007-009). From this premise, Goldwater derives a comprehensive critique of egalitarian public policy, from the graduated income tax to centralized education.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's treatment of equality operates as a limiting principle on government action, distinguishing legitimate civil rights enforcement from illegitimate social engineering:

**1. Natural Inequality as a Fact, Not a Problem.** The conservative philosophy, Goldwater argues, "takes into account the essential differences between men, and, accordingly, makes provision for developing the different potentialities of each man" (001-005). A political philosophy that treats men as an "undifferentiated mass" consigns them "to ultimate slavery" (001-005). The differences among men — in talent, ambition, industry, and fortune — are not injustices to be corrected by the state but features of the human condition that a free society accommodates and celebrates.

**2. The Graduated Tax as an Instrument of Leveling.** Goldwater reserves particular criticism for the progressive income tax, which he characterizes as a "confiscatory tax" whose "effect, and to a large extent its aim, is to bring down all men to a common level" (007-009). He notes that "many of the leading proponents of the graduated tax frankly admit that their purpose is to redistribute the nation's wealth" (007-009). The goal is "an egalitarian society" — a goal Goldwater considers fundamentally illegitimate, regardless of how democratically it is pursued. "Artificial devices for enforcing equality among unequal men must be rejected if we would restore that charter and honor those laws" (007-009).

**3. Educational Egalitarianism as a Threat to Excellence.** In the domain of education, Goldwater identifies the same egalitarian impulse at work: "Subscribing to the egalitarian notion that every child must have the same education, we have neglected to provide an educational system which will tax the talents and stir the ambitions of our best students and which will thus insure us the kind of leaders we will need in the future" (009-017). Uniformity in education, pursued in the name of equality, produces mediocrity and squanders the nation's talent.

**4. Equality and Civil Rights — The Federalism Constraint.** Goldwater's position on civil rights is shaped by his distinction between equality of rights (which he supports in principle) and the means by which it is pursued (which he insists must respect constitutional federalism). He opposes federal intervention in education not because he favors segregation but because "the Constitution does not permit any interference whatsoever by the federal government in the field of education" (004-010). The end — racial equality — does not justify unconstitutional means. For Goldwater, the means by which equality is pursued must themselves conform to the rule of law and the constitutional allocation of powers.

**5. Historical Context — Jacobinism as Cautionary Tale.** Goldwater traces the egalitarian impulse to the French Revolution: "Throughout history, true Conservatism has been at war equally with autocrats and with 'democratic' Jacobins" (001-006). The Conservative "was equally revolted at the attempt to solve that problem by a mob tyranny that paraded under the banner of egalitarianism" (001-006). This historical parallel frames contemporary egalitarianism not as a progressive development but as a recurring temptation — the promise of equality used to justify the concentration of power.

## Mentions in the Text

Key passages where Goldwater articulates his view of equality:

> "Their aim is an egalitarian society — an objective that does violence both to the charter of the Republic and the laws of Nature. We are all equal in the eyes of God but we are equal *in no other respect*." (007-009)

> "Artificial devices for enforcing equality among unequal men must be rejected if we would restore that charter and honor those laws." (007-009)

> "Subscribing to the egalitarian notion that every child must have the same education, we have neglected to provide an educational system which will tax the talents and stir the ambitions of our best students." (009-017)

> "The Conservative knows that to regard man as part of an undifferentiated mass is to consign him to ultimate slavery." (001-005)
