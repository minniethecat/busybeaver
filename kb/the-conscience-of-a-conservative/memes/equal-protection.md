## Overview

Equal protection of the laws is a constitutional guarantee rooted in the Fourteenth Amendment, which provides that no state shall "deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws." The conservative understanding of this principle emphasizes equality *before* the law — equal treatment in the application of legal rules — rather than equality of condition or outcome.

## The Meaning of Equal Protection

The equal protection clause is not a grant of unlimited federal authority over all matters touching on equality. Its original purpose was to ensure that the states applied their laws equally to all persons, particularly in the aftermath of the Civil War. The clause was directed at remedying invidious discrimination in the administration of state law — not at creating new categories of federally enforceable entitlements (004-009).

The intentions of the Founding Fathers regarding federal power over matters such as education are beyond doubt: no powers regarding education were given the federal government, and under the Tenth Amendment, jurisdiction over the entire field was reserved to the States. The question is whether the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause modified the original constitutional design by transferring jurisdiction over education and similar matters from the states to the federal judiciary (004-009). The conservative answer is that it did not — and that the Court's expansion of the equal protection clause into a general warrant for federal supervision of state policy represents an unconstitutional trespass into the legislative sphere (004-013).

## Civil Rights and Equal Protection

The concept of equal protection is closely related to, but distinct from, the broader category of civil rights. A civil right is a right that is asserted and protected by some valid law — whether the common law, state or federal statute, or the Constitution. Unless a right is incorporated in the law, it is not a civil right and is not enforceable by the instruments of the civil government (004-006).

In the field of racial relations, some rights are clearly protected by valid laws and are therefore civil rights — the right to vote, guaranteed by the Fifteenth Amendment, is a prime example (004-007). The distinction between genuine civil rights (those grounded in valid law) and asserted claims that lack legal foundation is essential to maintaining the integrity of the equal protection guarantee.

## Equal Protection in Labor Relations

The principle of equal protection extends to economic relations. Just as the law must protect individuals equally, it must also protect the right of workers to bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing. Congress has enacted laws to ensure that employees can bargain collectively with their employers (006-011). The same legal protections should extend to the freedom of individual workers who choose *not* to associate with a union — hence the case for state right-to-work laws that forbid contracts making union membership a condition of employment (006-017).

The rule of equal treatment applies to both employers and unions. Employers are forbidden to act collusively for sound reasons; the same reasons apply to unions. Industry-wide price-fixing causes economic dislocations, and so does industry-wide wage-fixing (006-034).

## Equal Protection and Taxation

The principle of equal treatment extends to the government's power to tax. The requirements of justice are clear: government has a right to claim an equal percentage of each person's wealth, and no more. Property taxes, typically levied on this principle, reflect the understanding that the government's claim should be proportionate, not progressive — a fixed share, not an escalating demand that grows with success (007-008).

> The intentions of the founding fathers in this matter are beyond any doubt: no powers regarding education were given the federal government. Consequently, under the Tenth Amendment, jurisdiction over the entire field was reserved to the States. The remaining question is whether the Fourteenth Amendment—concretely, that amendment’s “equal protection” clause—modified the original prohibition against federal intervention.
> (004-009)
