## Overview

Totalitarianism represents the antithesis of limited government and individual liberty. In a totalitarian system, the state recognizes no limits on its authority — it claims competence over every aspect of human life and subordinates all institutions, associations, and individual rights to its will.

## The First Principle of Totalitarianism

The defining characteristic of totalitarianism is its *first principle*: that the State is competent to do all things and is limited in what it actually does only by the will of those who control the State (002-003). This principle is not merely a description of totalitarian practice but the philosophical foundation upon which all totalitarian systems are built.

This principle stands in direct opposition to the American constitutional tradition. The Constitution proceeds from the opposite premise: that government possesses only those powers delegated to it, and that all other powers remain with the states or the people. Where totalitarianism asks "what may the state not do?", constitutionalism asks "what may the state do?" — and answers with a limited enumeration.

## Manifestations in Modern Governance

The principle of totalitarianism does not always announce itself openly. It can appear incrementally through the gradual expansion of state authority, particularly when leaders of both major political parties repudiate the principle of limited government. When government officials assert that government can "do whatever needs to be done" without reference to constitutional limits, and when it is the government itself that determines what needs to be done, the first principle of totalitarianism has taken hold — regardless of the label affixed to the governing ideology (002-003).

## Totalitarianism and Collectivism

Totalitarianism shares a natural affinity with [[collectivism]]. Both subordinate the individual to a larger entity — in collectivism, the group or class; in totalitarianism, the state. The totalitarian state is the ultimate expression of collectivist logic: if the individual exists for the sake of the collective, and the state is the organized expression of the collective will, then no sphere of individual autonomy can be justified against state encroachment.

## Mentions in the Text

> Here we have, by prominent spokesmen of both political parties, an unqualified repudiation of the principle of limited government. There is no reference by either of them to the Constitution, or any attempt to define the legitimate functions of government. The government can do whatever needs to be done; note, too, the implicit but necessary assumption that it is the government itself that determines what needs to be done. Here, in full flower, is the first principle of totalitarianism.
>
> (002-003)
