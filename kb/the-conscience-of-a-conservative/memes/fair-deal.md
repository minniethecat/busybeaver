## Overview

The Fair Deal was President Harry S. Truman's domestic policy agenda, announced in his 1949 State of the Union address. It proposed an ambitious expansion of the New Deal, including national health insurance, federal aid to education, civil rights legislation, and expanded Social Security. While many of its specific proposals failed to pass Congress, the Fair Deal established the template for postwar Democratic domestic policy.

## Goldwater's View

Goldwater treats the Fair Deal as essentially continuous with the New Deal — both manifestations of the same trend toward centralized government power. In the preface to *The Conscience of a Conservative*, he writes: "it is all too clear that in spite of a Conservative revival among the people the radical ideas that were promoted by the New and Fair Deals under the guise of Liberalism still dominate the councils of our national government" (P01-003).

## The Ideological Continuity

Goldwater's coupling of "the New and Fair Deals" is deliberate. It denies the claim that the New Deal was an emergency response to the Depression while the Fair Deal represented a permanent philosophical commitment to expanded government. For Goldwater, both programs share the same underlying premise: that the federal government should be "the whole people organized to do what had to be done," as Dean Acheson approvingly described the New Deal (002-002). This premise, Goldwater argues, erases the constitutional limits on federal power.

## The Liberal Label

Goldwater's critique of the Fair Deal is also a critique of political semantics. By labeling their programs "New Deal" and "Fair Deal," Liberals appropriated the language of fairness and progress while advancing what Goldwater sees as radical expansions of federal authority. This rhetorical strategy, Goldwater argues, has been so successful that "Conservative" became a term from which even conservatives felt compelled to apologize.

## Legacy

Though many Fair Deal proposals were not enacted in Truman's time, the agenda anticipated much of the Great Society legislation of the 1960s. Medicare, federal aid to education, and civil rights legislation — all Fair Deal proposals — eventually became law. For Goldwater and his intellectual heirs, this confirmed that the Fair Deal was not a discrete program but a milestone in the continuous growth of federal power.
