## Definition

Economic freedom is the liberty of individuals to engage in production, exchange, and consumption according to their own judgment, free from government coercion or direction. Goldwater treats it not as a separate domain but as an inseparable dimension of freedom itself: "the economic and spiritual aspects of man's nature are inextricably intertwined. He cannot be economically free, or even economically efficient, if he is enslaved politically; conversely, man's political freedom is illusory if he is dependent for his economic needs on the State" (001-008).

## In Conservative Thought

Economic freedom is central to the Conservative worldview because it operationalizes the principle of limited government in the domain where government overreach is most common. Across agriculture, labor, taxation, and welfare, Goldwater documents how government intervention substitutes political decisions for individual choices and produces worse outcomes.

"Farm production, like any other production is best controlled by the natural operation of the free market" (005-014). Government subsidies do not help farmers; they create dependency and distort markets. "Most farmers want to stand on their own feet. They are prepared to take their chances in the free market" (005-018).

> Farm production, like any other production is best controlled by the natural operation of the free market. If the nation's farmers are permitted to sell their produce freely, at price consumers are willing to pay, they will, under the law of supply and demand, produce those crops the public wants, in the quantities the public demands, and at the time the public needs them.
> (005-014)

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's defense of economic freedom covers multiple domains. Agriculture: "the only way to persuade farmers to enter other fields of endeavor is to stop paying them to stay where they are" (005-017). Labor: "employers are forbidden to act collusively for sound reasons. The same reasons apply to unions" (006-034). In every domain, free individuals making free choices produce better outcomes than government planners.
