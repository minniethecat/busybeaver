## Overview

The New Deal was a series of federal programs, public works projects, financial reforms, and regulations enacted between 1933 and 1939 under President Franklin D. Roosevelt. It fundamentally transformed the relationship between the federal government and American society, establishing the administrative and welfare-state apparatus that Goldwater's Conservatism opposes.

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater identifies the New Deal as the decisive break with constitutional limited government. "The New Deal, Dean Acheson wrote approvingly, 'conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done'" (002-002). This conception — government without constitutional boundaries, defined only by what officials deem necessary — is, for Goldwater, the essence of the constitutional crisis.

The New Deal's constitutional strategy was to reinterpret the Commerce Clause and General Welfare Clause as grants of plenary authority. "The New Deal Congress replied by enacting substantially identical legislation, the second A.A.A., and now sought to justify the program as a 'regulation of interstate commerce.' This was a transparent fiction" (005-011).

> The New Deal, Dean Acheson wrote approvingly in a book called *A Democrat Looks at His Party*, "conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done."
> (002-002)

## Key Consequences

- Agriculture Adjustment Act: Federal control of farm production despite "no power over agriculture was given to any branch of the national government" (005-010)
- Social Security Act: Created permanent federal welfare programs
- Wagner Act: Federal regulation of labor relations
- Transformation of federalism: States became administrative subunits of federal programs
