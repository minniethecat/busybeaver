## Definition

Foreign aid, in Goldwater's analysis, encompasses both economic assistance and military aid provided by the United States government to foreign nations. Writing in 1960 at the height of Cold War alliance-building, Goldwater distinguishes between necessary military aid to allies confronting communist aggression and indiscriminate economic aid that he argues often strengthens socialist governments, creates dependency, and diverts American resources from domestic priorities.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's treatment of foreign aid in Chapter X ("The Soviet Menace") applies conservative principles to international economic policy. Just as domestic welfare programs create dependency at home, foreign aid programs create dependency abroad — and in both cases, the resources come from American taxpayers who received no benefit from the transaction. The conservative principle that government should not take from some citizens to give to others applies across borders as well as within them.

Goldwater does not advocate isolationism or the complete elimination of foreign aid. His position is discriminating: aid should serve American strategic interests, primarily through military and technical assistance to nations that share American values and face communist threats. Aid to neutral or hostile nations, or aid that subsidizes socialist economic policies abroad, should be terminated. The test of foreign aid is not whether it makes America feel generous but whether it advances American freedom and security.

## Key Arguments

**Much foreign aid strengthens socialism abroad** (010-027, 010-030). Goldwater observes that government-to-government aid flows to the state sector of recipient economies, reinforcing socialist economic organization. "Our foreign aid program," he writes, "is the kind that will strengthen Socialism in those countries" — the opposite of its stated purpose of promoting free societies.

**Foreign aid should be discriminating, not universal** (010-032, 010-086). Goldwater proposes confining foreign aid to "military and technical assistance" to nations that are genuine allies. Economic aid that props up neutral or hostile regimes should be eliminated. The purpose of aid is to strengthen friends, not to purchase the temporary goodwill of adversaries.

**Aid often goes to enemies rather than friends** (010-027). Goldwater notes the paradox of American foreign aid flowing to nations that vote against American interests in the United Nations and align with the Soviet bloc. Aid without political conditions is self-defeating.

**Economic strength at home is the prerequisite for effective aid abroad** (007-020). The connection to Goldwater's fiscal conservatism is explicit: a nation burdened by debt and excessive domestic spending cannot sustain an effective foreign policy. Fiscal discipline is a precondition of national strength, including the capacity to assist allies.

**The Marshall Plan model is not universally applicable** (010-086, 010-062). While Goldwater does not directly attack the Marshall Plan (which was widely considered successful by 1960), he argues that indiscriminate application of the aid model to fundamentally different circumstances has produced diminishing returns. American foreign policy "suffers from a confusion" between the unique postwar European situation and the permanent interests of American strategy.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Discriminating Aid (Goldwater) | Indiscriminate Aid (Status Quo) |
|-----------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Recipients | Allies who share American values and face communist threats | Any nation that requests assistance |
| Form | Military and technical assistance | Broad economic aid, infrastructure, budget support |
| Conditions | Political alignment with American interests | Minimal or unenforced conditions |
| Strategic purpose | Strengthen the free world in the Cold War | General development and goodwill |
| Fiscal impact | Limited to what advances clear strategic interests | Open-ended commitment to global development |
