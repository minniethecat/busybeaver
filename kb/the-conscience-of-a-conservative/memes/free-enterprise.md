## Definition

Free enterprise, in Goldwater's usage, denotes an economic order in which private individuals — not the state — make the fundamental decisions about production, investment, and exchange. It rests on "free capital, free labor, a free market" (010-030) and is grounded in "one hundred and fifty years of experience to support the belief" that this is "the way to build a strong economy" (010-030).

Goldwater treats free enterprise not as an end in itself but as the economic dimension of individual freedom. Economic liberty is inseparable from political liberty: a man "cannot be economically free, or even economically efficient, if he is enslaved politically; conversely, man's political freedom is illusory if he is dependent for his economic needs on the State" (001-006).

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's defense of free enterprise operates at multiple levels — historical, strategic, and philosophical:

**1. Free Enterprise Defeated the Marxist Prediction.** "The collectivists have found, both in this country and in other industrialized nations of the West, that free enterprise has removed the economic and social conditions that might have made a class struggle possible" (008-008). Mammoth productivity, wide distribution of wealth, high standards of living, and the trade union movement have "eliminated whatever incentive there might have been for the 'proletariat' to rise up" (008-008). Goldwater notes that "the bankruptcy of doctrinaire Marxism has been expressly acknowledged by the Socialist Party of West Germany, and by the dominant faction of the Socialist Party of Great Britain" (008-008).

**2. Collectivism's New Strategy: Welfarism Over Nationalization.** Having failed to achieve socialism through direct nationalization, the collectivists shifted to the welfare state as their primary instrument. "They have learned that Socialism can be achieved through Welfarism quite as well as through Nationalization" (008-009). The welfare state achieves the same end — subordinating the individual to the state — but "is much more compatible with the political processes of a democratic society" (008-009) because it buys votes with promises of federal benefits rather than seizing property outright.

**3. Foreign Aid as a Threat to Free Enterprise.** Goldwater extends his defense of free enterprise to foreign policy, arguing that American government-to-government aid strengthens socialism abroad. "Every one of the 'neutral' countries we are aiding is committed to a system of State Socialism. Our present policy of government-to-government aid strengthens Socialism in those countries" (010-030). Rather than promoting free enterprise, "we are not only perpetuating the inefficiency and waste that always attends government-controlled economies; by strengthening the hand of those governments, we are making it more difficult for free enterprise to take hold" (010-030). Goldwater calls for eliminating government-to-government capital assistance and "encouraging the substitution of American private investment" (010-030).

**4. The Intimate Connection Between Economic and Political Freedom.** Free enterprise is not an isolated economic preference but the economic expression of the conservative philosophy of limited government. The same principle that limits the state's power over the individual's political life — that man is a spiritual creature whose highest needs cannot be met by material provision — also limits the state's power over his economic life. Goldwater argues that "if we take from a man the personal responsibility for caring for his material needs, we take from him also the will and the opportunity to be free" (008-017).

## Mentions in the Text

Key passages where Goldwater articulates the case for free enterprise:

> "The collectivists have found, both in this country and in other industrialized nations of the West, that free enterprise has removed the economic and social conditions that might have made a class struggle possible." (008-008)

> "We Americans believe — and we can cite one hundred and fifty years of experience to support the belief — that the way to build a strong economy is to encourage the free play of economic forces: free capital, free labor, a free market." (010-030)

> "We are not only perpetuating the inefficiency and waste that always attends government-controlled economies; by strengthening the hand of those governments, we are making it more difficult for free enterprise to take hold." (010-030)
