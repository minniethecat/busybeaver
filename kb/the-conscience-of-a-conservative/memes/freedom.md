## Definition

Freedom, in Goldwater's Conservative philosophy, is the maximum sphere of individual autonomy consistent with the maintenance of social order. He defines the Conservative approach to politics as "the art of achieving the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order" (001-011). This formulation is significant: freedom is not absolute license but ordered liberty, bounded by the minimal constraints necessary for civil society.

Critically, Goldwater insists that political freedom and economic freedom are inseparable: "the economic and spiritual aspects of man's nature are inextricably intertwined. He cannot be economically free, or even economically efficient, if he is enslaved politically; conversely, man's political freedom is illusory if he is dependent for his economic needs on the State" (001-008).

## In Conservative Thought

Freedom is the telos of Conservative politics — not merely one value among many but the organizing purpose toward which all other principles are directed. The Conservative "looks upon politics as the art of achieving the maximum amount of freedom for individuals" (001-011). This sets Conservatism apart from ideologies that prioritize equality, security, or collective welfare over liberty.

The framers of the Constitution shared this understanding. They "knew from vivid, personal experience that freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power in a single authority" (002-008). The entire constitutional architecture — separation of powers, federalism, enumerated powers, the Bill of Rights — is an instrument for preserving freedom by checking power.

## Historical Context

Goldwater observes that in modern America, "the delicate balance that ideally exists between freedom and order has long since tipped against freedom practically everywhere on earth" (001-012). The growth of the administrative state, the expansion of federal regulatory authority, and the rise of the Welfare State all represent erosions of freedom in the name of order or welfare.

He identifies a persistent tension: "State power, considered in the abstract, need not restrict freedom: but absolute state power always does" (002-006). The legitimate functions of government — maintaining internal order and external defense — are "actually conducive to freedom." But when government exceeds these functions, it becomes a threat to the very liberty it was established to protect.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater examines freedom through multiple lenses. Economic freedom means the right to keep the fruits of one's labor: "One of the foremost precepts of the natural law is man's right to the possession and the use of his property" (007-004). Political freedom includes both "the right to vote for the candidate of our choice" and the right "to use our money to try to persuade other voters" (006-021).

Freedom of association is equally vital — the right of individuals "to form voluntary associations of their own choosing" (006-017), which labor union closed shops violate by "making union membership a condition of employment." Across these domains, the pattern is consistent: freedom means individual choice, and anything that coerces or compels the individual — whether government mandate or private monopoly power — diminishes freedom.

## Contrasts

The central contrast is between freedom and collectivism. The collectivist "subordinates all other considerations to man's material well-being" and is willing to sacrifice liberty for security or equality (001-004). The Conservative insists that "the enhancement of man's spiritual nature" is "the primary concern of political philosophy," and that freedom — not material comfort — is the highest political good (001-005).
