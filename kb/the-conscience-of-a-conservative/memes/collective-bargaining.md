## Overview

Collective bargaining — the process by which unions negotiate with employers on behalf of workers — receives detailed treatment in Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative*. While Goldwater acknowledges the legitimacy of voluntary collective bargaining, he argues that federal labor law has distorted the bargaining process by granting unions coercive powers that undermine individual worker freedom.

## The Legitimate Function of Collective Bargaining

Goldwater recognizes that collective bargaining serves a valid purpose in labor relations. "The union that confines its activities to collective bargaining" operates within the bounds of its proper role (006-014). When bargaining is truly voluntary — when workers freely choose representation and unions negotiate within the confines of the employment relationship — it is consistent with free market principles.

He acknowledges the historical need for legal protection of bargaining rights: "a worker's right to bargain through a collective agent needed legal protection" in an era when individual workers lacked leverage against large employers (006-011). The National Labor Relations Act of 1935 (the Wagner Act) established this framework.

> We have seen that unions perform their natural function when three conditions are observed: association with the union is voluntary; the union confines its activities to collective bargaining; the bargaining is conducted with the employer of the workers concerned.
> (006-014)

## The Coercive Turn

Goldwater's critique centers on the evolution of labor law beyond the protection of voluntary bargaining. Federal legislation and National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) decisions, he argues, have transformed collective bargaining from a voluntary process into a system of compulsion.

He describes the problem of compulsory unionism: when "Workers became the recognized bargaining agent at his plant, he refused to join" (006-016). Under federal labor law as interpreted by the NLRB, workers can be compelled to accept union representation and pay union dues even when they would prefer to negotiate individually.

## The Free Rider Problem

Goldwater engages with the economic arguments surrounding collective bargaining. Unions and their supporters argue that workers who benefit from union-negotiated contracts should contribute to the costs of negotiation — the "free rider" problem. Goldwater is skeptical: he questions whether "those employees who want collective representation in bargaining" should be able to compel all workers at a workplace to support the union (006-009).

## Collective Bargaining and the Public Interest

Goldwater extends his analysis to the impact of collective bargaining on the broader economy. Industry-wide bargaining patterns, he observes, mean that a settlement with one major employer sets a template: "$5 an hour settlement with General Motors, and from there $5 with Ford, $5 with Chrysler and so on" (006-034). This pattern bargaining removes the competitive dynamics that would otherwise produce varied outcomes reflecting different economic circumstances.

He asks whether we should consider "an abandonment of the collective bargaining process" when it produces outcomes contrary to the public interest (006-030). This is not a call to abolish collective bargaining but to recognize its limits: when bargaining results in wage increases that fuel inflation or industry-wide work rules that stifle productivity, the public has a legitimate interest in reform.

## The Free Market Alternative

Goldwater's preferred alternative is a labor policy grounded in voluntary association and individual choice. Workers should be free to join unions and bargain collectively, but they should also be free to decline. The federal government's role should be limited to protecting the voluntariness of the process — preventing coercion by either employers or unions — rather than tilting the playing field in either direction.

This approach reflects Goldwater's broader commitment to "the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order" (001-011). Collective action, in his view, is legitimate only when it rests on the genuinely free choice of the individuals involved.
