The **Social Security Act** of 1935 was a landmark piece of [[the-new-deal|New
Deal]] legislation that established a federal system of old-age pensions,
unemployment insurance, and public assistance for the needy. In
[[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]],
[[barry-goldwater|Barry Goldwater]] presents Social Security as a primary
example of the [[welfare-state|welfare state's]] expansion and its corollary
erosion of [[self-reliance|individual self-reliance]].

## The Conservative Critique

Goldwater's objection to Social Security is rooted in his broader philosophy
of [[individualism|individual responsibility]] (001-009):

> Every man, for his individual good and for the good of his society, is
> responsible for his own development. The choices that govern his life are
> choices that he must make: they cannot be made by any other human being, or
> by a collectivity of human beings. (001-009)

From this premise, a compulsory old-age insurance system is objectionable
not because its goals are unworthy but because it substitutes government
compulsion for personal choice and family responsibility.

## Economic Consequences

The book also addresses the economic dimension of Social Security taxation.
Goldwater notes that "the government takes six percent of most payrolls in
Social Security Taxes and thus compels millions of individuals to postpone
until later years the enjoyment of money they might otherwise turn to fruitful
use in providing for the needs of their families or the education of their
children" (002-016). This compulsory deferral of consumption, in his view,
represents a form of government paternalism incompatible with a free society.

## Fiscal Scale

The magnitude of the Social Security program is underscored by Goldwater's
citation of federal budget data. In fiscal year 1961, Social Security payments
administered by the Department of Health, Education and Welfare were estimated
to exceed $15 billion (008-002). Combined with other federal expenditures,
this meant that "Federal disbursements for human welfare in this country
[were] second only to national defense" (008-002). For Goldwater, this
represented a fundamental transformation of the federal government's role—from
defender of liberty to manager of welfare.

The budgetary analysis also highlights a methodological point: Social Security
spending, channeled through "trust funds," is often excluded from headline
federal budget figures. Goldwater insists that these disbursements "must" be
included "if we are to obtain a realistic picture of federal expenditures"
(007-014), anticipating a debate about government accounting that would
continue for decades.

## Relation to Conservative Principles

Social Security embodies, in Goldwater's analysis, three tendencies that
conservatism must resist. First, it substitutes collective compulsion for
[[freedom|individual choice]]. Second, it expands the federal government's
reach into areas the Constitution left to individuals, families, and states.
Third, it creates a political constituency dependent on continued government
benefits, making reform progressively more difficult—a dynamic that Goldwater
sees operating across the entire [[welfare-state|welfare state]].

The critique of Social Security in the book is not an argument against charity
or care for the elderly. The distinction Goldwater draws is between voluntary
provision—by individuals, families, churches, and communities—and compulsory
government programs that, however well-intentioned, erode the very habits of
[[self-reliance|self-reliance]] and mutual obligation that a free society
requires.
