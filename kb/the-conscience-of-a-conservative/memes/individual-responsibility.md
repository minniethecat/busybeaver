## Overview

Individual responsibility is a foundational concept in conservative thought.
It holds that each person is the primary agent accountable for their own life,
decisions, and material well-being. This principle stands in opposition to
the collectivist view that society or the state bears primary responsibility
for individual welfare.

## In Goldwater's Conservatism

Although Goldwater does not dedicate a single section to defining "individual
responsibility" as a discrete term, the concept permeates every chapter of
*The Conscience of a Conservative*. It is the ethical premise underlying his
arguments across multiple domains.

**Freedom and responsibility are inseparable.** Goldwater argues that "the
Conservative approach is a mood, a faith, a way of life — an awareness of the
duty of man to shoulder individual responsibility for his own destiny"
(001-002). Freedom without responsibility is license; responsibility without
freedom is servitude.

**The welfare state undermines responsibility.** Goldwater's critique of the
welfare state is not primarily economic but moral: by transferring
responsibility for individual welfare to the state, it "corrodes the
individual's sense of responsibility for his own and his family's welfare"
(002-005).

**States' rights as institutionalized responsibility.** Goldwater's federalism
rests on the conviction that responsibility is best exercised at the level
closest to the individual. Local and state governance are not merely more
efficient but more *responsible* — citizens can see, judge, and hold
accountable those who govern them.

**Spiritual dimension.** For Goldwater, individual responsibility has a
spiritual character. "The Conservative has learned that the economic and
spiritual aspects of man's nature are inextricably intertwined. He cannot be
economically free, or even economically efficient, if he is enslaved
politically; conversely, man's political freedom is illusory if he is
dependent for his economic needs on the State" (001-004).
