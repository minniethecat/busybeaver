# Monopoly

In Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative*, monopoly serves as a key analytical concept for understanding concentrated economic power and its relationship to freedom. Goldwater applies the concept of monopoly not only to corporations but also, and perhaps more distinctively, to labor unions.

## Historical Context

Goldwater situates the monopoly problem in American economic history:

> Eighty years ago the nation was faced with a comparable concentration of economic power. Large corporations, by gaining monopoly control over entire industries, had nullified the laws of competition that are conducive to freedom. (006-032)

The American response to corporate monopoly was the Sherman Act and other antitrust legislation, which Goldwater endorses as a legitimate exercise of government power to preserve competitive markets. He sees competition as essential to freedom — monopolies nullify the competitive laws that are "conducive to freedom."

## Union Monopoly Power

Goldwater's most distinctive argument about monopoly is his application of antitrust principles to labor unions. He draws a direct parallel between corporate and union concentration of power:

> These laws, however, have never been applied to labor unions. And I am at a loss to understand why. If it is wrong for a single corporation to dictate prices throughout an entire industry, it is also wrong for a single union — or, as is the actual case, a small group of union leaders — to dictate wages and working conditions throughout an industry. (006-032)

This argument forms part of Goldwater's broader critique of [[labor-union]] power. He contends that the same logic that justified breaking up corporate trusts should apply to union monopolies that exercise comparable control over labor markets.

## International Dimensions

Goldwater also applies the monopoly concept in the context of national security and foreign policy. He notes that after World War II, the United States possessed "an absolute monopoly in nuclear power" (010-055), but failed to exploit this advantage politically and then "frittered away" its lead through belated entry into the hydrogen bomb and guided missile fields. Similarly, he observes that America once had "a monopoly of atomic weapons, and with a conventional military establishment superior to any in the world" (010-003), advantages that were subsequently lost.

This usage expands the concept of monopoly from purely economic analysis to strategic analysis, suggesting that Goldwater saw concentrated power — whether economic or military — as carrying both opportunities and responsibilities.

## Monopoly and Freedom

The link between monopoly and freedom is central to Goldwater's analysis. Monopoly power — whether held by corporations, unions, or foreign powers — undermines freedom by concentrating decision-making authority in too few hands. The dispersal of power, whether through antitrust law, right-to-work legislation, or a prudent foreign policy, is essential to maintaining the conditions in which freedom can flourish.
