## Overview

Civil liberties, properly understood, are the fundamental freedoms that belong to individuals by virtue of their nature as human beings — they are antecedent to government and not created by it. In the conservative framework articulated by Goldwater, the distinction between civil liberties and [[civil-rights]] is essential: the former derive from natural law and the Creator, while the latter are products of valid legislation and judicial enforcement (004-003).

## The Natural Rights Foundation

The foundation of civil liberties lies in the recognition that individuals possess rights not granted by government but inherent in their humanity. These include freedom of speech, religion, association, and the right to property. When properly defined, civil liberties are protections against government action — restraints on what the state may do to the individual — rather than entitlements to government benefits (004-002).

## The Proper Distinction: Liberties vs. Rights

A crucial conceptual distinction runs through Goldwater's analysis. A civil right is a right that is asserted and protected by valid laws — it is "civil" precisely because it is grounded in the civil law (004-006, 004-007). Civil liberties, by contrast, exist independently of legislation. The confusion between these categories has allowed the expansion of government power under the banner of protecting rights. When a "right" to government-provided goods is declared — a right to education, housing, or healthcare — a new "civil right" is born by judicial or legislative fiat, fundamentally different in character from the liberties the Constitution was designed to protect (004-005).

## Liberties Under Threat

Those who profess concern for "civil rights" and "civil liberties" are often the very architects of the expanding state power that is inimical to individual liberty (006-017, 008-020). The paradox of modern governance is that the language of rights is increasingly deployed to justify state interventions that diminish the sphere of individual freedom. Western civilization's survival depends on whether it can recover an understanding of liberty rooted in restraint on government rather than empowerment of it (009-002).

> The remedy here is to give freedom of association legal protection. And that is why I strongly favor enactment of State right-to-work laws which forbid contracts that make union membership a condition of employment. These laws are aimed at removing a great blight on the contemporary American scene, and I am at a loss to understand why so many people who so often profess concern for "civil rights" and "civil liberties" are vehemently opposed to them.
> (006-017)
