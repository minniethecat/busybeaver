## Biography

Barry Morris Goldwater (1909–1998) served as a United States Senator from Arizona for five terms and was the [[republic|Republic]]an Party's nominee for President in 1964. Before entering politics, he worked in his family's department store business and served as a pilot in [[world-war-ii|World War II]]. His 1960 book *[[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]]* articulated a philosophy of limited government, [[freedom|individual freedom]], and [[constitutionalism|constitutional fidelity]] that became foundational to the modern American conservative movement.

## Role in American Politics

Goldwater's 1964 presidential campaign, though resulting in a landslide loss to Lyndon Johnson, is widely credited with launching the conservative movement that would later bring Ronald Reagan to the presidency. His uncompromising advocacy for limited government and his willingness to challenge the [[republic|Republic]]an establishment — which he saw as having abandoned conservative principles for a pale imitation of Democratic programs — made him a transformative figure in American political history.

In the [[us-congress|Senate]], Goldwater was known for his direct speaking style and his consistent votes against what he viewed as unconstitutional expansion of federal power. He chaired the [[us-congress|Senate]] Intelligence Committee and was influential in military and defense policy.

## Relation to Conservatism

Goldwater's [[conservatism|Conservatism]], as laid out in his book, rests on the belief that "the [[conservatism|Conservative]] looks upon politics as the art of achieving the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order" (001-011). He was deeply concerned that [[republic|Republic]]ans had abandoned true [[conservatism|Conservative]] principles, criticizing those who called themselves "progressive [[conservatism|Conservative]]s" as having effectively adopted the premises of their opponents (001-002).

Goldwater placed [[freedom|individual freedom]] above all other political values: "the [[conservatism|Conservative]]'s first concern will always be: *Are we maximizing freedom?*" (001-012). He was equally critical of Democrats' embrace of centralized power and of [[republic|Republic]]ans who accommodated it, arguing in Chapter II that both parties had repudiated "the principle of limited government" (002-003).

## Mentions in the Text

As the author and narrator, Goldwater's voice is present throughout the book. Key passages where he reflects on his own role and philosophy:

> "I have been much concerned that so many people today with Conservative instincts feel compelled to apologize for them." (001-002)

> "The Conservative looks upon politics as the art of achieving the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order." (001-011)

> "The challenge to Conservatives today is quite simply to demonstrate the bearing of a proven philosophy on the problems of our own time." (P01-001)
