## Overview

World War II was the deadliest conflict in human history, involving over 30 countries and resulting in an estimated 70–85 million fatalities. The war reshaped the global order: European colonial empires declined, the United States and Soviet Union emerged as rival superpowers, and the Cold War began almost immediately after the Axis surrender.

## Postwar Context and American Conservatism

The war's aftermath set the stage for the political world in which Goldwater wrote *The Conscience of a Conservative*. Several developments were crucial:

### The Cold War

The wartime alliance between the US and USSR collapsed into the Cold War — a global ideological and military struggle between liberal democracy and communism. Goldwater devoted the final chapter of his book, "The Soviet Menace," to the existential threat posed by Soviet communism (001-010).

### Domestic Transformation

The war effort expanded the federal government dramatically. According to Goldwater's analysis, Americans "built 550,000 classrooms at a cost of approximately $19 billion — almost all of which was raised at the local level" in the postwar period, demonstrating local capacity for educational infrastructure (001-009). This postwar expansion of federal authority — through the GI Bill, the National Security Act, and the emerging military-industrial complex — became a central concern for conservatives.

### American Global Leadership

After the war, "we were not only master of our own destiny but also the leader of the free world" (001-010). This new global role created tensions between conservative principles of limited government and the demands of superpower status.

## Impact on Conservative Thought

World War II and its aftermath profoundly shaped American conservatism:

1. **Anti-communism**: The Soviet threat became the organizing principle of conservative foreign policy
2. **Big government**: The war's expansion of federal power galvanized the limited-government movement
3. **National security state**: The permanent military establishment created a new dimension of government power for conservatives to reckon with

> And still the awful truth remains: We can establish the domestic conditions for maximizing freedom, along the lines I have indicated, and yet become slaves. We can do this by losing the Cold War to the Soviet Union.
> (010-002)
