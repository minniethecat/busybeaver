Harry S. Truman served as the 33rd President of the United States from 1945 to 1953, succeeding [[franklin-roosevelt|Franklin D. Roosevelt]]. His presidency was defined by the end of [[world-war-ii|World War II]], the beginning of the [[cold-war|Cold War]], and the expansion of the [[new-deal|New Deal]] welfare state through his "Fair Deal" domestic agenda.

Goldwater cites the Truman Administration as a benchmark for excessive [[government-spending|federal spending]]. He notes that [[dwight-eisenhower|President Eisenhower]]'s fiscal 1954 budget was actually lower than what his predecessor had proposed: "the budget for the coming year, which was announced at this conference — $72 billion in fiscal 1954 (President Truman had proposed $81 billion)" (007-013). This comparison serves to illustrate that even a [[republic|Republic]]an administration operating within the post-[[new-deal|New Deal]] framework was spending at levels beyond what conservatives considered prudent.

The spending trajectory under Truman is used by Goldwater to demonstrate the ratchet effect of [[big-government|government growth]]: "during the last five years of the Truman Administration the average annual deficit was $3.2 billion" (007-015). Goldwater presents these figures to argue that deficit spending had become institutionalized regardless of which party controlled the White House, and that the distinction between Democratic and [[republic|Republic]]an fiscal policy was one of degree rather than kind.

The fiscal legacy of the Truman years, in Goldwater's telling, was not merely a matter of high spending but of institutionalized deficits. Goldwater noted that "during the last five years of the Truman Administration the average annual deficit was $3.2 billion," a pattern he contrasted unfavorably with the Eisenhower Administration's record of smaller annual deficits averaging $1.8 billion over its first seven years (007-015). The comparison served to illustrate Goldwater's broader argument: that deficit spending, once normalized by a Democratic administration, became a permanent feature of federal budgeting regardless of which party held power.

Truman's Fair Deal represented, in Goldwater's view, the consolidation of the [[new-deal|New Deal]]'s expansion of federal authority. The Truman Administration's approach to domestic policy — extending federal involvement into housing, healthcare, and civil rights — established a template that Goldwater believed subsequent administrations, including [[republic|Republic]]an ones, failed to reverse. Truman thus appears in *[[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]]* not as a personal target but as a symbol of the bipartisan consensus around big government that Goldwater sought to challenge.

> In the Summer of 1952, shortly after the Republican Convention, the two men who had battled for the Presidential nomination met at Morningside Heights, New York, to discuss the problem of taxes and spending. After the conference, Senator Taft announced: “General Eisenhower emphatically agrees with me in the proposal to reduce drastically overall expenses. Our goal is about $70 billion in fiscal 1954 (President Truman had proposed $81 billion) and $60 billion in fiscal 1955 … Of course, I hope we may do better than that and that the reduction can steadily continue.” Thereafter, the idea of a $60 billion budget in 1955, plus the promise of further reductions later on, became an integral part of the Republican campaign.
> (007-013)
