## Biography

Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, economist, and revolutionary theorist whose ideas became the foundation of modern communism and, more broadly, of collectivist political philosophy. His major works include *The [[communism|Communist]] Manifesto* (1848, with Friedrich Engels) and *Das Kapital* (1867–1894). Goldwater treats Marxism not merely as a foreign ideology but as the intellectual source of domestic threats to American freedom.

## Role in Conservative Thought

Goldwater identifies Marx's doctrines as the origin point of modern [[socialism|collectivism]]. "For many years it appeared that the principal domestic threat to our freedom was contained in the doctrines of Karl Marx. The collectivists — non-[[communism|Communist]]s as well as [[communism|Communist]]s — had adopted the Marxist goal of 'the socialization of the means of production'" (008-007).

However, Goldwater's analysis goes further: he argues that [[socialism|collectivism]] has evolved beyond explicit Marxism. "The collectivists have found, both in this country and in other industrialized nations of the West, that [[economic-freedom|free enterprise]] has removed the economic and social conditions that might have made a class struggle possible" (008-008). Unable to sell outright nationalization, they shifted to "[[socialism|Socialism]]-through-[[welfare-state|Welfarism]]" — a strategy Marx might not have recognized but whose goal of subordinating the individual to the state remains the same.

## Key Ideas

Goldwater invokes Marx's famous formula — "from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs" — in critiquing the [[united-nations|United Nations]]' financial structure, which he sees as applying a "Marxist formula" to international burden-sharing (010-067). This illustrates his broader point: Marxist categories have infiltrated mainstream political discourse in ways that most Americans do not recognize.
