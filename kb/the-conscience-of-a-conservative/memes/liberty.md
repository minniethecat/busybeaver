## Definition

Liberty, as Goldwater uses the term, denotes the condition in which individuals are free from government coercion in the pursuit of their own ends. It is the primary political value of conservatism — the end for which limited government, federalism, and constitutional restraints are the means. Goldwater's definition aligns with the classical liberal tradition: liberty is *negative* liberty (freedom *from* interference) rather than *positive* liberty (the capacity to achieve certain outcomes through government assistance).

Goldwater explicitly distinguishes liberty from welfare. In what amounts to his political credo, he imagines a candidate who proclaims: "I have little interest in streamlining government or in making it more efficient, for I mean to reduce its size. I do not undertake to promote welfare, for I propose to extend freedom" (002-001). This opposition — liberty versus welfare, freedom versus government provision — is the central organizing tension of Goldwater's political thought.

## In Conservative Thought

Liberty is not merely one value among many in Goldwater's conservatism; it is the value against which every policy, program, and institution must be measured. His framework asks not "does this program achieve its stated goal?" but "does this program diminish liberty?" The answer, he consistently argues, is that government action almost always does.

Goldwater grounds his conception of liberty in two reinforcing traditions. The first is the natural rights philosophy: liberty is inherent in human nature, not granted by government, and government exists to *secure* it, not to define or distribute it. The second is the American constitutional tradition: the Framers designed a system of enumerated powers, separated institutions, and federal divisions specifically to protect liberty against the natural tendency of government to expand. When government exceeds its Constitutional bounds — whether through judicial activism, federal overreach, or executive aggrandizement — liberty is the casualty.

Goldwater's approach to liberty is strikingly absolutist. He does not engage in balancing tests or cost-benefit analyses. The question is always binary: does this measure expand or contract the sphere of individual freedom? If it contracts freedom, it is illegitimate regardless of its other merits. This is the logic behind his declaration that "I will not attempt to discover whether legislation is 'needed' before I have first determined whether it is constitutionally permissible" (002-001).

## Historical Context

The American Founders enshrined liberty as the central purpose of government in the Declaration of Independence and protected it through the structural mechanisms of the Constitution: separation of powers, federalism, and the Bill of Rights. Throughout the nineteenth century, the meaning of liberty in American political discourse evolved from a primarily negative concept (freedom from government restraint) to one that increasingly encompassed economic and social dimensions.

The Progressive Era and the New Deal marked decisive turning points. Under Franklin Roosevelt, the federal government assumed responsibility for the economic welfare of citizens — a shift that Roosevelt himself characterized as expanding the meaning of freedom to include "freedom from want." Goldwater viewed this reinterpretation not as an expansion of liberty but as its abandonment: a government that provides for its citizens also controls them, and control is the opposite of liberty.

By 1960, when Goldwater wrote, the Cold War had added another dimension to the liberty debate. The conflict with Soviet Communism was framed as a global struggle between freedom and tyranny, giving the concept of liberty an existential urgency that Goldwater exploits throughout the book. The domestic expansion of government and the external threat of Communism were, in his view, two fronts in the same war.

## Key Arguments

**Liberty depends on decentralized government.** Goldwater states this principle as self-evident: "individual liberty depends on decentralized government" (002-001). The connection is structural: when power is concentrated in a remote central government, individual citizens lose the capacity to influence the decisions that affect their lives. Federalism — the division of authority between national and state governments — is not merely an administrative convenience but a liberty-preserving mechanism.

**The people's main interest is liberty.** Goldwater imagines a model public official who, when "attacked for neglecting my constituents' 'interests,'" replies: "I was informed their main interest is liberty and that in that cause I am doing the very best I can" (002-001). This framing is polemically powerful: it casts advocates of government programs as enemies of the people's true interest, not its champions. Liberty is not one constituency among many; it is the interest that unites all citizens against the expansion of government power.

> I have little interest in streamlining government or in making it more efficient, for I mean to reduce its size. I do not undertake to promote welfare, for I propose to extend freedom. My aim is not to pass laws, but to repeal them. (002-001)

**Government is the chief threat to liberty.** "Throughout history, government has proved to be the chief instrument for thwarting man's liberty," Goldwater writes (002-001). This is not an argument against government as such — he acknowledges that government is necessary for order, justice, and defense — but a warning against complacency. Because power corrupts, and "absolute power corrupts absolutely," every grant of power to government must be treated as a potential threat to liberty and constrained accordingly.

**Federal aid is not "free."** Goldwater argues that citizens of his home state of Arizona "have long since seen through the spurious suggestion that federal aid comes 'free'" (002-001). Federal money is extracted from taxpayers, filtered through a federal bureaucracy, and returned with conditions attached. The cost is not only financial: "the power to decide how that money shall be spent is withdrawn from them and exercised by some planning board deep in the caverns of one of the federal agencies" (002-001). This represents "a great and perhaps irreparable loss — not only in their wealth, but in their priceless liberty" (002-001).

**Constitutional permissibility before legislative need.** Goldwater proposes reversing the standard legislative calculus. Instead of asking whether a program is needed and then determining whether it is constitutional, he insists that constitutionality must be the *first* question: "I will not attempt to discover whether legislation is 'needed' before I have first determined whether it is constitutionally permissible" (002-001). This reordering is essential to liberty because the "need" for government action is infinitely elastic — there is always a problem that someone believes requires federal intervention. The Constitution's limits on government power, not the felt urgency of the moment, must govern.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Liberty (Conservative) | Welfare (Liberal) |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Definition** | Freedom *from* government coercion | Freedom *through* government provision |
| **Government role** | Minimal — protect rights, then stand aside | Active — provide services and redistribute resources |
| **Federalism** | Essential — power dispersed to states and people | Obstacle — uniform national standards needed |
| **Taxation** | For limited, Constitutional purposes only | For redistribution and social programs |
| **Decision-making** | By individuals and local communities | By federal agencies and planning boards |
| **Ultimate threat** | Government power itself | Private power and market forces |

Goldwater's contrast between liberty and welfare is stark and deliberately so. He does not admit the possibility that government programs might enhance liberty by providing the material preconditions for its exercise. For Goldwater, every government program is paid for in liberty, and the cost is always too high.

Liberty appears less as a discrete topic than as the foundational assumption underlying every argument Goldwater makes. It is the thread that connects his positions on federalism, taxation, labor policy, education, and foreign affairs. When he defends States' Rights, he is defending liberty. When he opposes the graduated income tax, he is opposing a violation of liberty. The concept is so fundamental to his thought that isolating its "mentions" understates its pervasiveness.
