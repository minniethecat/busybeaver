## Definition

Limited government is the constitutional doctrine that the powers of government — particularly the federal government — are strictly confined to those expressly enumerated in the Constitution. It holds that government is not a grantor of rights but a servant of the people, possessing only such authority as the people have delegated to it. As Goldwater puts it, "the Constitution which is an instrument, above all, for limiting the functions of government, and which is as binding today as when it was written" (002-004). The doctrine rejects the notion that government may assume any power not explicitly prohibited, insisting instead that it may exercise only powers explicitly granted.

## In Conservative Thought

Limited government is the bedrock of the Conservative worldview. Goldwater argues that the framers of the Constitution "knew from vivid, personal experience that freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power in a single authority" (002-008). For Conservatives, the limitation of government power is not merely a procedural preference but a moral imperative rooted in the nature of man: because power is "a self-aggrandizing force" whose "appetite grows with eating" (001-011), only strict constitutional limits can preserve liberty.

The Conservative commitment to limited government manifests in several interconnected doctrines: the separation of powers among three branches, the reservation of ungranted powers to the states, and the principle that "their first duty as public officials is to divest themselves of the power they have been given" (002-022). Government that exceeds its constitutional mandate — "from social welfare programs, education, public power, agriculture, public housing, urban renewal" (007-019) — has abandoned the Conservative principle.

## Historical Context

The principle of limited government traces to the Founding era. Hamilton, in Federalist No. 17, predicted that federal intervention in local matters would prove "troublesome" and inefficient — "Hamilton was wrong in his prediction as to what men would do, but quite right in foreseeing the consequences of their foolhardiness" (005-008). The Founders' system of enumerated powers, the Tenth Amendment's reservation clause, and the structural checks of separated powers were all designed to prevent the concentration of authority that limited government doctrine opposes.

Goldwater documents how this principle was progressively abandoned in the twentieth century: "How did our national government grow from a servant with sharply limited powers into a master with virtually unlimited power?" (002-017). He points to "occasions when we have elevated men and political parties to power that promised to restore limited government and then proceeded, after their election, to expand the activities of government" (002-018), identifying a pattern of rhetorical commitment betrayed by practice.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater makes several key arguments for limited government. First, he observes that prominent spokesmen of both parties have offered "an unqualified repudiation of the principle of limited government" with "no reference by either of them to the Constitution" (002-003), establishing the bipartisan nature of the departure from constitutional limits.

Second, he argues that government's claim on individual earnings must be bounded: "Government does not have an unlimited claim on the earnings of individuals. One of the foremost precepts of the natural law is man's right to the possession and the use of his property" (007-004). This links limited government to property rights and natural law.

Third, he contends that abandoning limited government threatens freedom itself: "We can be conquered by bombs or by subversion; but we can also be conquered by neglect — by ignoring the Constitution and the safeguards of state prerogatives and local power" (002-020).

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Limited Government | Unlimited Government |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Source of authority | Enumerated powers in Constitution | Implied or claimed powers |
| Default rule | Power not granted is denied | Power not prohibited is permitted |
| Relationship to freedom | Protects individual liberty | "Subordinates the individual to the State" (008-009) |
| View of human nature | Power must be checked | Power can be trusted to benevolent administrators |
| Constitutional reading | Strict construction | "Handbook in political theory" approach (003-011) |
