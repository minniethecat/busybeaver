**Richard Milhous Nixon** (1913–1994) served as Vice President of the United States under Dwight D. Eisenhower (1953–1961) and later as the 37th President (1969–1974). In *The Conscience of a Conservative*, Senator Barry Goldwater referenced Nixon to illustrate the problem of rhetorical moderation in the Republican Party.

## Goldwater's Citation

Goldwater quoted Nixon's formulation of what a Republican candidate should be: "Republican candidates," the Vice President had said, "should be economic conservatives, but conservatives with a heart." (001-002)

For Goldwater, this statement exemplified the instinct to "qualify their commitment in a way that amounts to breast-beating" — a tendency among Republican politicians to apologize for or soften conservative principles rather than articulate them clearly and unapologetically. Goldwater did not dispute Nixon's substantive position on economic issues; his objection was to the rhetorical posture that suggested conservatism required a humanitarian modifier to be acceptable. (001-002)

## Significance in the Conservative Movement

The Nixon reference in Goldwater's opening chapter served a strategic purpose. By 1960, Nixon was the presumptive Republican presidential nominee, and Goldwater's challenge was to define a conservative alternative within the same party. Goldwater's treatment of Nixon was not a personal attack — Nixon had been his Senate colleague — but an argument about political communication: the conservative position, Goldwater insisted, did not need to be softened or apologized for; it needed to be stated clearly and defended on its merits. (001-002)

This distinction between conservative substance (which both Goldwater and Nixon claimed) and conservative rhetorical clarity (which Goldwater considered Nixon to lack) became a defining theme of the conservative movement in the 1960s and beyond.
