## Definition

States' Rights is the constitutional principle, codified in the Tenth Amendment, that "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution nor prohibited by it to the States are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people" (004-004). It establishes a vertical division of sovereignty in which the federal government possesses only enumerated powers, while the states retain general police powers over health, safety, morals, and welfare. Goldwater emphasizes that this is not a technicality but a structural guarantee of liberty: "the principle of States' Rights is insurance against the concentration of power in a central government" (003-012).

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, States' Rights is inseparable from the Conservative commitment to limited government. The framers understood "that freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power in a single authority" (002-008), and federalism — the reservation of ungranted powers to the states — is one of those essential restraints. Goldwater argues that the alternative to States' Rights is not efficient administration but centralized coercion: "Nothing could so far advance the cause of freedom as for state officials throughout the land to assert their rightful claims to lost state power; and for the federal government to withdraw promptly and totally from every jurisdiction which the Constitution reserves to the states" (003-013).

The Conservative defense of States' Rights is grounded in two complementary insights. First, that concentrated power is inherently dangerous regardless of who wields it. Second, that local control allows citizens to "achieve a maximum of influence" over their own governance, while centralization reduces them to supplicants.

## Historical Context

The erosion of States' Rights accelerated dramatically during the New Deal era. Goldwater traces "Franklin Roosevelt's rapid conversion from Constitutionalism to the doctrine of unlimited government" (003-003), observing that this transformation set a precedent that subsequent administrations — both Democratic and Republican — would follow. The "Modern Republicanism" of the Eisenhower era "gives lip-service to States' Rights" while practicing "a federal 'partnership' in which the federal government calls the tunes" (003-004).

This historical pattern — rhetorical commitment to federalism combined with practical centralization — is, for Goldwater, the central political failure of his time.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater advances several related arguments for States' Rights. First, he rejects the notion that the Constitution is merely "a kind of handbook in political theory, to be heeded or ignored depending on how it fits the plans of contemporary officials" (003-011). The Tenth Amendment means what it says.

Second, he distinguishes legitimate federal authority from creeping expansion: "I deny that there can be a conflict between States' Rights, properly defined — and civil rights, properly defined. If States' 'Rights' are so asserted as to encroach upon individual rights, they are asserted improperly" (004-003).

Third, he identifies a practical mechanism of federal encroachment: the use of conditional grants-in-aid that "the states must accept on Washington's terms or not at all" (003-005), which transforms federalism from a structural limit into a fiscal dependency.

## Contrasts

States' Rights should not be confused with defense of segregation or racial discrimination. Goldwater explicitly rejects "an attempt made in recent years to disparage the principle of States' Rights by equating it with defense of the South's position on racial integration" (004-002). The principle is constitutional, not sectional, and applies to all exercises of federal power beyond enumerated limits — in agriculture, labor, education, welfare, and every other domain.
