## Overview

The United States Constitution, drafted at the Constitutional Convention of 1787 and ratified in 1788, is the foundational legal document of the American republic. It establishes a federal government of enumerated powers, separated among three branches, with all ungranted powers reserved to the states or the people. For Goldwater, the Constitution is not merely a legal framework but the embodiment of the Conservative principle of limited government.

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater's constitutional argument is straightforward: "the Constitution which is an instrument, above all, for limiting the functions of government, and which is as binding today as when it was written" (002-004). The framers "knew from vivid, personal experience that freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power in a single authority" (002-008), and they designed the Constitution to provide those restraints.

He rejects the "living Constitution" approach that treats the document as "a kind of handbook in political theory, to be heeded or ignored depending on how it fits the plans of contemporary officials" (003-011). The Constitution means what it says, and its limits bind all branches of government equally.

## Key Provisions Discussed

- **Article I, Section 8 (General Welfare Clause)**: Expansive misinterpretation has enabled federal programs outside constitutional authority (005-010)
- **Tenth Amendment**: Reserves ungranted powers to the states; "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution nor prohibited by it to the States are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people" (004-004)
- **Separation of Powers**: "The system of restraints, on the face of it, was directed not only against individual tyrants, but also against a tyranny of the masses" (002-009)
