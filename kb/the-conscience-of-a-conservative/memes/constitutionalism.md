## Definition

Constitutionalism is the principle that all government authority must be exercised within the bounds of a written constitution that is "as binding today as when it was written" (002-004). It holds that the Constitution is not merely aspirational — "a kind of handbook in political theory, to be heeded or ignored depending on how it fits the plans of contemporary officials" (003-011) — but rather a binding legal instrument that defines, limits, and structures all exercises of governmental power.

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, constitutionalism is the legal expression of the Conservative principle of limited government. The framers "knew from vivid, personal experience that freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power in a single authority" (002-008), and they encoded those restraints in a written constitution precisely because they understood that unwritten limits are no limits at all.

The Conservative defense of constitutionalism operates on two levels. Structurally, the Constitution's system of "restraints" — separation of powers, federalism, enumerated powers — was "directed not only against individual tyrants, but also against a tyranny of the masses" (002-009). Substantively, the Constitution's specific prohibitions and reservations create zones of individual liberty that no government may invade.

## Historical Context

Goldwater documents a sustained retreat from constitutionalism in twentieth-century American governance. "We have not kept it. The Achesons and Larsons have had their way. The system of restraints has fallen into disrepair" (002-011). The federal government "has moved into every field in which it believes its services are needed" (002-011), disregarding the constitutional framework of enumerated powers.

This retreat was not inevitable. It occurred because "prominent spokesmen of both political parties" offered "an unqualified repudiation of the principle of limited government" with "no reference by either of them to the Constitution" (002-003). Constitutionalism failed not because the Constitution was inadequate but because officials chose to ignore it.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater makes several interrelated arguments for constitutionalism. First, the Constitution is designed as an enduring restraint, not a document of its time: "the framers were not visionaries. They knew that rules of government, however brilliantly calculated to cope with the imperfect nature of man, however carefully designed to avoid the pitfalls of political power, could — and would — be corrupted" (002-010). The answer to corruption is not to abandon constitutional limits but to enforce them.

Second, the distinction between a constitutional republic and a democracy is crucial. "Was it then a Democracy the framers created? Hardly" (002-009). The framers created a system of structural restraints precisely because they feared majoritarian tyranny as much as individual tyranny.

Third, constitutionalism requires constant vigilance: "there are occasions when we have elevated men and political parties to power that promised to restore limited government and then proceeded, after their election, to expand the activities of government" (002-018).

## Contrasts

| Constitutionalism | Living Constitution |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Constitution as binding law | Constitution as evolving guide |
| Government of enumerated powers | Government of implied powers |
| "As binding today as when it was written" (002-004) | "Handbook in political theory" (003-011) |
| Judicial restraint | Judicial legislation |
| "The system of restraints" (002-009) | "Every field in which it believes its services are needed" (002-011) |
