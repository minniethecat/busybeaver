## Definition

The New Deal refers to the series of federal programs, public works projects, financial reforms, and regulations enacted during the presidency of Franklin D. Roosevelt between 1933 and 1939. Designed as a response to the Great Depression, the New Deal established the principle that the federal government bears responsibility for the economic welfare of its citizens and has broad authority to intervene in the national economy.

For Goldwater, the New Deal represents far more than a set of historical policies. It is the watershed event in the transformation of American government from a Constitutionally-limited republic to an administrative welfare state. The New Deal's defining characteristic, in his analysis, is not any particular program but its underlying philosophy: that the federal government may do whatever "has to be done" if no one else can do it — a philosophy Goldwater regards as the negation of limited government.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's treatment of the New Deal is inseparable from his critique of "Modern Republicanism" — the Eisenhower-era tendency of Republicans to accept the New Deal's premises while arguing for more efficient administration of its programs. He quotes Dean Acheson's approving description of the New Deal: it "conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done" (001-001). He then notes that Arthur Larson, a Republican, "made much the same claim" — that the "underlying philosophy" of Modern Republicanism is "if a job has to be done to meet the needs of the people, and no one else can do it, then it is the proper function of the federal government" (001-001).

For Goldwater, this convergence is the central political tragedy of his era. The Democratic Party abandoned constitutional limits under Roosevelt; the Republican Party, rather than restoring those limits, has accepted the New Deal's premises and merely quibbled over the details. The result is that "neither of our two parties maintains a meaningful commitment to the principle of States' Rights" (003-001) — and without States' Rights, there is no effective check on the expansion of federal power.

Goldwater's critique of the New Deal operates at three levels: constitutional (the New Deal violated the enumerated-powers framework), economic (its agricultural and welfare programs are perverse in their effects), and philosophical (it substituted the principle of government-as-doer for government-as-referee). These levels are interconnected: the constitutional violation enables the bad economics, and both are justified by the faulty philosophy.

## Historical Context

The Great Depression (1929–1939) created conditions of mass unemployment, bank failures, and agricultural collapse that discredited laissez-faire economics and created political demand for federal intervention. Roosevelt took office in March 1933 and immediately launched the "First New Deal" (1933–1934), which focused on banking reform, relief programs, and agricultural price supports. The "Second New Deal" (1935–1938) added Social Security, the National Labor Relations Act (Wagner Act), and the Works Progress Administration.

The Supreme Court initially struck down several New Deal programs as exceeding Congress's constitutional authority, most notably in *Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States* (1935) and *United States v. Butler* (1936). Roosevelt responded with his "court-packing" proposal of 1937, which failed legislatively but coincided with a shift in the Court's jurisprudence. After 1937, the Court upheld New Deal legislation under an expansive reading of the Commerce Clause, effectively removing constitutional limits on federal economic regulation for the next half-century.

By the time Goldwater wrote in 1960, the New Deal's constitutional revolution was a fait accompli. The question was no longer whether the federal government could manage the economy but how extensively it should do so. Goldwater's contribution was to insist that the original question — whether the Constitution permits such management — remained the right one, even if the courts had ceased to ask it.

## Key Arguments

**The New Deal's philosophy of unlimited government.** Goldwater identifies the New Deal's core innovation as a philosophical one: the replacement of limited, enumerated federal powers with an open-ended mandate to solve problems. The New Deal "conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done" (001-001). Under this formula, constitutional limits become irrelevant — whatever "has to be done" justifies the federal action. Goldwater sees the same formula at work in Modern Republicanism, which holds that "if a job has to be done to meet the needs of the people, and no one else can do it, then it is the proper function of the federal government" (001-001). Both formulations share the same defect: they replace the Constitution's "may the government do this?" with "should someone do this?" — and the answer to the latter question is always yes.

**The *Butler* case and agricultural coercion.** Goldwater recounts the history of the Agricultural Adjustment Act as an illustration of how the New Deal corrupted constitutional interpretation. The first AAA was struck down in *United States v. Butler* (1936), but "the New Deal Congress replied by enacting substantially identical legislation" under the pretext of regulating interstate commerce (005-001). The Supreme Court, "under heavy political fire for having thwarted the 'Roosevelt Revolution,' made one of its celebrated about-faces and upheld the new act" (005-001). Goldwater calls this "perhaps the most flagrant" instance of "distorting the plain meaning of the Constitution's language" (005-001).

**Perverse agricultural policies.** Goldwater attacks the soil bank and acreage retirement programs — the modern successors to New Deal agricultural policy — on two grounds. First, they do not work: they have been "no more successful in reducing surpluses than their predecessors" (005-001). Second, and more fundamentally, they are morally perverse: "in effect, they reward people for *not producing*. For a nation that is expressing great concern over its 'economic growth,' I cannot conceive of a more absurd and self-defeating policy than one which subsidizes non-production" (005-001). This combination of practical failure and moral perversity is characteristic of Goldwater's critique of the New Deal tradition.

**The welfare state as the New Deal's legacy.** Goldwater treats the welfare programs of his own era as the direct continuation of the New Deal. He cites a news report that "the current results of New Deal legislation are Federal disbursements for human welfare in this country second only to national defense" (008-001). The $15 billion welfare budget — second only to defense spending — is presented as the fruit of the seed Roosevelt planted. The New Deal established the principle; subsequent administrations, Republican and Democratic alike, have only expanded its application.

> The New Deal, Dean Acheson wrote approvingly, "conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done." (001-001)

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Constitutional Government (Pre-New Deal) | Administrative State (Post-New Deal) |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Federal power** | Limited to enumerated powers | Open-ended — whatever "has to be done" |
| **Judicial role** | Enforce constitutional limits | Defer to legislative judgments |
| **Federalism** | States as primary domestic policymakers | States as administrative subunits |
| **Agriculture** | Free market with minimal intervention | Price supports, acreage retirement, subsidies |
| **Welfare** | Private charity + state/local programs | Federal entitlement programs |
| **Philosophy** | Government as referee | Government as doer |

Goldwater's contrast is not merely between different policies but between different *regimes* — one constitutional, one administrative. The New Deal represents the transition from the first to the second, and the task of conservatism is to reverse that transition.

The New Deal is seldom named explicitly in Goldwater's text — he refers to it directly only a handful of times — but its presence is ubiquitous. Nearly every federal program he criticizes (agricultural subsidies, welfare spending, graduated taxation, federal aid to education) traces its lineage to the New Deal or its constitutional logic. The book can be read, in significant part, as an extended argument against the New Deal's transformation of American governance.
