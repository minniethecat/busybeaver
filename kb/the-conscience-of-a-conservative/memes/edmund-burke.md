## Overview

Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was an Anglo-Irish statesman, parliamentarian, and
political philosopher. He served in the British House of Commons as a member
of the Whig Party and is best known for his *Reflections on the Revolution in
France* (1790), a foundational text of conservative thought that argues for
tradition, prudence, and organic institutional development over abstract
revolutionary principles.

## Relation to Goldwater's Conservatism

Burke is invoked in *The Conscience of a Conservative* indirectly, through
Goldwater's reference to Lord Acton. The contrast between the two figures is
instructive: Acton was "not a conservative in the Burkean sense; he was a
classical liberal who judged institutions — including the Church — by their
effect on individual freedom."

By choosing Acton over Burke as his intellectual lodestar, Goldwater aligned
American conservatism with a principle-based defense of liberty rather than a
tradition-based defense of established institutions. This choice "gave his
argument a universal, philosophical character that distinguished it from mere
nostalgia or reaction."

## Conservative Legacy

Burke's thought emphasizes several themes that resonate with, but also differ
from, Goldwater's conservatism:

- **Prescription and tradition**: Burke valued institutions that had stood the
  test of time, arguing that accumulated wisdom is superior to abstract theory.
- **Gradual reform**: He supported change that preserved institutional continuity
  rather than revolutionary rupture.
- **The social contract across generations**: Burke viewed society as "a
  partnership between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who
  are yet to be born."

Goldwater shares Burke's skepticism of utopian schemes and central planning,
but grounds his argument in natural rights and constitutional principles
rather than historical tradition — a distinctly American, and more Lockean,
form of conservatism.
