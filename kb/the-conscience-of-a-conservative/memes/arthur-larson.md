Arthur Larson served in the [[dwight-eisenhower|Eisenhower]] administration as Undersecretary of Labor and later as a special assistant and speechwriter to [[dwight-eisenhower|President Eisenhower]]. He is best known as the author of *A [[republic|Republic]]an Looks at His Party* (1956), a book that articulated the philosophy of "Modern [[republicanism|Republicanism]]" — the view that the [[republic|Republic]]an Party should accept the basic framework of the [[new-deal|New Deal]] while seeking to administer it more efficiently.

Goldwater engages with Larson's ideas at length in *[[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]]*, treating Larson's book as a case study in what conservatives should oppose. Goldwater acknowledges Larson's observation that "something had to be done" about the Depression (002-002), but takes issue with what he sees as Larson's willingness to accept the permanent expansion of federal power that followed. The core of Goldwater's critique is that Larson, like [[dean-acheson|Dean Acheson]], represents a faction of the [[republic|Republic]]an Party that has abandoned conservative principles in favor of managing the welfare state.

> The New Deal, Dean Acheson wrote approvingly in a book called *A Democrat Looks at His Party*, "conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done." A year later Mr. Larson wrote *A Republican Looks at His Party*, and made much the same claim in his book for Modern Republicans. The "underlying philosophy" of the New Republicanism, said Mr. Larson, is that "if a job has to be done to meet the needs of the people, and no one else can do it, then it is the proper function of the federal government."
> (002-002)

Goldwater argues that Larson's approach fails to address what he sees as the fundamental problem: "the [[dean-acheson|Acheson]]s and Larsons have had their way. The system they have perpetuated has not been effective" (002-011). For Goldwater, the Modern [[republicanism|Republicanism]] advocated by Larson and others was not merely insufficient — it was actively harmful, because it denied voters a genuine choice between conservative and liberal governance. By accepting the premises of the [[new-deal|New Deal]], Larson's brand of [[republicanism|Republicanism]], in Goldwater's view, had made the party complicit in the expansion of federal power.

Goldwater also critiques Larson's policy analysis, particularly on federal-state relations. He notes that "Mr. Larson devotes a good deal of space to the states' rights problem" but argues that Larson's proposed solutions — what Goldwater calls "the Democrats' approach" — amount to encouraging states to comply with federal directives rather than truly restoring state autonomy (003-010).

## Larson and the 1958 Elections

Goldwater viewed the 1958 midterm elections — in which Republicans suffered heavy losses — as a repudiation of the Larsonite approach to Republican politics. The "Modern Republicanism" that Larson championed, Goldwater argued, had failed electorally as well as philosophically: voters saw little reason to choose a diluted version of Democratic policies when they could vote for the genuine article. "The [[dean-acheson|Acheson]]s and Larsons have had their way," Goldwater wrote. "The system they have perpetuated has not been effective" (002-011). The electoral consequence was that the Republican Party, by abandoning its distinctive conservative principles, had forfeited its reason for existing as an independent political force.
