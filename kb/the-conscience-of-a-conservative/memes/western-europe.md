## Overview

Western Europe refers to the democratic nations of Europe that aligned with the United States during the Cold War. In Goldwater's analysis, Western Europe appears both as a beneficiary of American defense commitments and as a point of comparison for domestic policy — particularly labor relations — where European practices diverge from American ones.

## Postwar Recovery and American Aid

Goldwater acknowledges, though cautiously, the argument that American aid contributed to Western Europe's stability: "It can be argued, but not proved, that American aid helped prevent Western Europe from going Communist after the Second World War" (010-024). This qualified concession appears in a chapter otherwise skeptical of foreign aid, reflecting the widely accepted view that the Marshall Plan succeeded in its objectives.

## Burden-Sharing in Defense

Goldwater criticizes the disparity in defense contributions between the United States and its allies: "For many years now, our allies in Western Europe have devoted smaller portions of their national budgets to military forces than we have" (010-025). This observation supports his broader argument that the United States carries a disproportionate share of the free world's defense burden, while European allies free-ride on American military power.

## Labor Relations Compared

In discussing union membership, Goldwater contrasts European and American practices: union membership "prevails in the United States and England, but in the other countries of Western Europe and in Australia, union membership is generally on a voluntary basis" (006-019). This comparison serves Goldwater's argument against compulsory unionism in the United States — if Western European democracies can function with voluntary union membership, there is no justification for the union shop in America.

## Strategic Significance

Western Europe was the central theater of Cold War confrontation. The NATO alliance, headquartered in Western Europe, represented the primary American commitment to collective defense. Goldwater's concern about burden-sharing reflects a persistent theme in his foreign policy thinking: alliances should strengthen American security, not merely transfer American resources to partners who fail to contribute proportionally.
