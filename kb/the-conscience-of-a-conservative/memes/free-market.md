The **free market** is an economic system in which the prices of goods and
services are determined by open competition in which private individuals and
firms voluntarily exchange goods, free from government price controls, subsidies,
or other coercive interventions. In [[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The
Conscience of a Conservative]], [[barry-goldwater|Barry Goldwater]] presents the free
market not merely as an efficient mechanism for allocating resources, but as a
moral imperative grounded in individual [[freedom|freedom]] and
[[natural-rights|natural rights]].

## The Free Market as a Constitutional Principle

Goldwater's defense of the free market is rooted in the Constitution. He argues
that the Framers designed a government of [[limited-government|limited]] and
enumerated powers precisely to protect the sphere of voluntary economic exchange
from federal interference. In the chapter "Freedom for the Farmer," he traces
how federal agricultural programs—price supports, production controls, and
subsidies—represent a departure from both the constitutional text and the
economic wisdom of the Founders, including [[alexander-hamilton|Alexander Hamilton]]
himself.

The book treats the free market as the economic expression of the broader
principle that individuals, not the state, should determine the course of their
lives. Economic planning by government, in this view, is indistinguishable from
the centralized control that the Constitution was designed to prevent.

## The Agricultural Case Study

The book's most sustained analysis of the free market appears in its discussion
of American farm policy. Goldwater argues that the farm subsidy program—begun
during the [[the-new-deal|New Deal]] and expanded in subsequent decades—is a
textbook case of government intervention producing the opposite of its intended
result (005-014):

> The reason government intervention has created more problems than it has
> solved is quite simple. Farm production, like any other production is best
> controlled by the natural operation of the free market. If the nation's
> farmers are permitted to sell their produce freely, at price consumers are
> willing to pay, they will, under the law of supply and demand, end up
> producing roughly what can be consumed in national and world markets. (005-014)

The analysis proceeds from diagnosis to prescription: the only remedy for the
distortions created by government intervention is the full restoration of market
pricing (005-017):

> The only way to persuade farmers to enter other fields of endeavor is to stop
> paying inefficient farmers for produce that cannot be sold at free market
> prices. (005-017)

Goldwater anticipates the objection that this policy would be politically
untenable, countering that farmers themselves prefer independence to subsidy
(005-018):

> Most farmers want to stand on their own feet. They are prepared to take their
> chances in the free market. They have a more intimate knowledge than most of
> us of the consequences of unlimited government power, and so, it would seem,
> a greater interest than most in returning agriculture to freedom and economic
> sanity. (005-018)

### Supply and Demand as Natural Law

For Goldwater, the law of supply and demand is not merely an economic observation but reflects the natural order of voluntary human cooperation. Government intervention in pricing disrupts the signals that coordinate millions of independent decisions, creating shortages where the market would have produced abundance. The farmer who produces for a free market responds to consumer demand directly; the farmer producing for a government subsidy program responds to political incentives that bear no necessary relationship to actual need.

## Labor Unions and the Free Market

The book extends its free-market analysis beyond agriculture to labor relations.
In the chapter "Freedom for Labor," Goldwater argues that the same logic that
condemns corporate monopolies applies to labor monopolies (006-034):

> Corporate monopolies impair the operation of the free market, and thus injure
> the consuming public. So do union monopolies. (006-034)

This symmetrical treatment—opposing both corporate and union concentration of
power—reflects the book's consistent application of free-market principles. The
free market, in Goldwater's formulation, requires competitive conditions on both
sides of the bargaining table.

## Foreign Policy and Economic Freedom

In the chapter on foreign policy, the free market becomes a standard against
which American aid programs are measured. Goldwater contrasts the American
economic tradition with the policies of recipient nations (010-030):

> We Americans believe—and we can cite one hundred and fifty years of experience
> to support the belief—that the way to build a strong economy is to encourage
> the free play of economic forces: free capital, free labor, a free market. Yet
> every one of the "neutral" countries we are aiding is committed to a system of
> State Socialism. (010-030)

This passage reveals the book's conviction that the free market is not a
parochial American preference but a universal principle of prosperity—and
that foreign aid policies that ignore this principle are self-defeating.

## Relation to Conservative Thought

The free market occupies a central place in Goldwater's conservatism because it
connects three of the book's major themes: [[constitutionalism|constitutional
restraint]], [[individualism|individual responsibility]], and anti-Communism.
Economically, the free market is the mechanism that translates individual choice
into collective prosperity without the coercion inherent in state planning.
Politically, it is the system most compatible with a government of limited and
enumerated powers. Morally, it respects the dignity of the individual by
treating each person as capable of making their own economic decisions.

The concept stands in direct opposition to [[collectivism|collectivism]] in all
its forms—whether the explicit socialism of [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]] or the
incremental statism Goldwater perceived in the domestic policies of his
mid-century opponents.
