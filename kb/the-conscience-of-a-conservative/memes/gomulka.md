## Political Career

Władysław Gomułka was a Polish Communist politician who served as First Secretary of the Polish United Workers' Party from 1956 to 1970. He rose to power during the Polish October of 1956, a period of political liberalization that followed Khrushchev's "Secret Speech" denouncing Stalin. Gomułka's initial resistance to Soviet pressure and his promise of a "Polish road to socialism" generated considerable enthusiasm in Poland and hope in the West that Poland might be pulling away from the Soviet orbit.

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater presents the Gomułka episode as a cautionary tale about the failure of American foreign policy to understand Communist strategy. In the fall of 1956, "there appeared to be a breach between Gomulka's government and the Kremlin" (010-075). Western observers "joyfully proclaimed that Poland was pulling away from Communism, and hoping to hasten this movement, our government began to send the Gomulka regime American aid" (010-075).

## The Trap of American Aid

Goldwater argues that American aid to Gomułka's Poland was counterproductive on two levels. First, "our money made it easier for Gomulka's regime to deal with its economic problems" (010-075), relieving the Communist government of the consequences of its own policies. Second, and more fundamentally, the premise was wrong: "Gomulka moved into an even closer relationship with the Soviet government" (010-075). The apparent liberalization was tactical, not ideological.

## The Budapest Lesson

Goldwater contrasts the Polish situation with the simultaneous Hungarian Revolution: "Gomulka knew, as American policy-makers ought to have known, that the price of abandoning Communism is a Budapest-type blood bath" (010-075). This is the core of Goldwater's critique: American policy failed to recognize that no Communist leader could genuinely break with Moscow without facing Soviet military intervention. Gomułka's "Polish road" was never a road away from Communism — it was a tactical adjustment within the Communist system.

## Policy Implications

The Gomułka case illustrates Goldwater's broader argument that American aid should be "furnished only to friendly, anti-Communist nations that are willing to join with us in the struggle for freedom" (010-029). Aid to Communist governments, even apparently liberalizing ones, strengthens the Soviet bloc by relieving its members of economic difficulties and lending American legitimacy to Communist regimes.
