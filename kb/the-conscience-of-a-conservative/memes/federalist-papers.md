## Overview

*The Federalist Papers* are a series of 85 essays written by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay under the pseudonym "Publius," published in 1787–1788 to advocate ratification of the United States Constitution. They remain the most authoritative contemporaneous commentary on the Constitution's meaning and the framers' intentions.

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater invokes the *Federalist Papers* primarily through Hamilton's Federalist No. 17, in which Hamilton predicted the consequences of federal overreach: "Hamilton was wrong in his prediction as to what men would do, but quite right in foreseeing the consequences of their foolhardiness. Federal intervention in agriculture has, indeed, proved 'troublesome'" (005-008).

This reference is characteristic of Goldwater's argumentative strategy: the framers anticipated and warned against the very expansions of federal power that modern government has embraced. The *Federalist Papers* are not historical curiosities but living warnings whose relevance has only increased.
