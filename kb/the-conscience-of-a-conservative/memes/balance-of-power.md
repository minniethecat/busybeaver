## Overview

The balance of power is a foundational principle of American constitutional government. It rests on the recognition that power concentrated in any single institution, branch, or level of government inevitably threatens individual liberty. The Framers designed a system of competing powers — legislative, executive, and judicial; federal and state — each checking the others, precisely because they understood that the only effective restraint on power is countervailing power.

## The Fragile Equilibrium

The Founders understood that the delicate balance that preserves liberty is easily disrupted (001-012). Government represents power in the hands of some men to control the conduct of other men — a power that, considered in the abstract, contains no inherent limit on its exercise [002-005, 002-006]. The Framers did not believe that good intentions or popular mandate would restrain power; they believed only in structural restraints (002-007).

## The System of Restraints

The Constitution establishes a system of restraints on government power. On its face, this system was designed to prevent the accumulation of authority in any single branch or level (002-009). But the Framers knew that rules of government, however brilliantly designed, are only as effective as the willingness of the governed to enforce them (002-010). When that will fades — when citizens and officials alike cease to insist on the constitutional boundaries — the system of restraints falls into disuse (002-011).

> Throughout history, government has proved to be the chief instrument for thwarting man's liberty. Government represents power in the hands of some men to control and regulate the lives of other men. And power, as Lord Acton said, corrupts men. "Absolute power," he added, "corrupts absolutely."
> (002-005)

## The Erosion of Balance

In modern practice, the constitutional balance of power has been steadily eroded through several mechanisms: the expansion of federal authority at the expense of the states [003-002, 003-004]; the growth of executive and administrative power beyond its constitutional bounds; and the tendency to concentrate decision-making authority in the hands of a centralized few (002-020). The system of restraints has fallen into disrepair not through formal amendment but through the gradual acquiescence of those who should have defended it (002-011).

## The Path to Restoration

Restoring the balance of power requires more than rhetoric about "returning to the Constitution." It requires a genuine recommitment to the principle that no branch, no level, and no official possesses unlimited authority (002-018). When we entrust the conduct of our affairs to those who understand that power must be divided, checked, and balanced, we preserve liberty; when we abandon that understanding, we place liberty at risk (002-022).
