## Background

The Kennedy-Ervin Bill was the Senate's proposed labor reform legislation of 1959, introduced in response to the abuses uncovered by the McClellan Committee investigation into labor racketeering and union corruption. Sponsored by Senator John F. Kennedy and Senator Sam Ervin, the bill was advertised as a comprehensive response to the problems the McClellan Committee had exposed. It passed the Senate by a vote of 90 to 1.

## Goldwater's Opposition

Goldwater considers his vote against the Kennedy-Ervin Bill the most significant of his Senate career: "If I had to select the vote I regard as the most important of my Senate career it would be the one I cast on the Kennedy-Ervin 'Labor Reform' Bill of 1959. The Senate passed the measure 90–1; the dissenting vote was mine" (006-002).

## The Critique

Goldwater's opposition was not to the goal of labor reform but to the bill's approach. "The measure had been advertised as a cure-all for the evils uncovered by the McClellan Committee investigation. I opposed it because I felt certain that legislation which pretended to respond to the popular demand for reform would actually hurt the very people — the working people of America — it was supposed to help" (006-002). Goldwater believed the bill would impose regulatory burdens that, while appearing to address union abuses, would ultimately restrict the freedom of workers and legitimate union activities.

## The Landrum-Griffin Alternative

The House of Representatives rejected the Kennedy-Ervin approach: "The House of Representatives rejected Kennedy-Ervin, and substituted in its place a much better measure, the Landrum-Griffin bill" (006-003). Goldwater preferred Landrum-Griffin because it focused more directly on protecting individual union members' rights — including financial disclosure, democratic union governance, and restrictions on secondary boycotts — rather than expanding the regulatory power of the federal government over labor relations generally. The final legislation, the Labor-Management Reporting and Disclosure Act of 1959 (the Landrum-Griffin Act), incorporated elements of both bills.

## Historical Significance

The Kennedy-Ervin episode illustrates Goldwater's willingness to stand alone on a matter of principle, even when his position was overwhelmingly unpopular. A 90–1 vote is nearly unanimous; Goldwater's dissent was lonely but, in his view, principled. The episode also reflects his broader philosophy: legislation that appears to address a problem may do more harm than good if it expands government power rather than protecting individual freedom.
