## Definition

Military strength, in Goldwater's usage, encompasses both the material capacity to wage war and the political will to project that capacity. It includes conventional forces, nuclear arsenal, alliances, and — crucially — the moral clarity to distinguish friend from foe. Military strength is not an end in itself but the necessary condition for a foreign policy that advances freedom rather than managing its retreat. Without it, diplomacy becomes appeasement and negotiation becomes surrender.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater places military strength at the center of his foreign policy argument in Chapter X ("The Soviet Menace"). The conservative recognizes that the world is dangerous — that there exist powers whose interests are fundamentally opposed to American freedom, and whose ambitions are constrained only by the credible threat of overwhelming force. The Cold War is not a misunderstanding to be resolved through diplomacy but a conflict to be won through strength.

This view departs from both the liberal internationalism that sought accommodation with the Soviet Union and the "Modern Republicanism" that accepted coexistence as the best achievable outcome. For Goldwater, the goal is not to stabilize the Cold War but to win it — and victory requires unambiguous military superiority, not parity. The conservative's commitment to military strength flows from the same principle as his commitment to limited government: the protection of individual freedom against those who would destroy it, whether from within or without.

## Key Arguments

**Military superiority deters Soviet adventurism** ((010-015), (010-018), (010-019)). Goldwater argues that American military alliances and forward-deployed forces have discouraged Communist military expansion. As the Soviet Union draws abreast of American nuclear capabilities, however, the deterrent value of American strength erodes — requiring continuous investment to maintain the margin of superiority.

**Conventional forces matter as much as nuclear weapons** ((010-019), (010-054), (010-055), (010-056)). Nuclear weapons cannot be the only instrument of American power. Goldwater insists on a conventional military establishment rivaling the Soviet Union's, capable of responding to provocations below the nuclear threshold. A nation that can only threaten annihilation or nothing has no usable military power.

**Economic strength underpins military strength** ((007-020)). The connection between fiscal conservatism and national security runs both ways: a prosperous free economy generates the resources for military investment, while excessive government spending weakens the economic base on which military strength depends.

**Political warfare requires military backing** ((010-018)). Goldwater observes that the Cold War is fought as much through political subversion as through armed conflict. American military strength gives credibility to its political commitments — allies will not align with a power that cannot defend them.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Military Strength (Goldwater) | Accommodation / Coexistence |
|-----------|------------------------------|----------------------------|
| Goal | Victory over Soviet Communism | Stable coexistence |
| Posture | Superiority and initiative | Parity and negotiation |
| Conventional forces | Essential for flexible response | Secondary to nuclear deterrence |
| Alliances | Binding commitments backed by capability | Diplomatic arrangements |
| Moral dimension | Clear distinction between freedom and tyranny | Moral equivalence or agnosticism |
