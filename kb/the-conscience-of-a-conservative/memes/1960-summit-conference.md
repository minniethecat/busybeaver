## Overview

"As I write, the world is waiting for another round of diplomatic conferences between East and West. A full scale summit meeting is scheduled for Spring; later on, President Eisenhower and Premier Khrushchev will attend a meeting of the U.N. General Assembly" (010-034). The 1960 Paris Summit collapsed after the U-2 incident, but Goldwater is less interested in the specific events than in the structural dynamic these summits reveal.

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater uses the summit as a case study in Cold War asymmetry. The Communists enter negotiations not to reach agreements but because "the negotiations themselves will provide victory enough" (010-037). "If the Communists can obtain a formal agreement beneficial to them, so much the better. But if not the negotiations themselves will provide victory enough" — propaganda value, legitimization, and the appearance of equality with the West.

This analysis reflects Goldwater's broader argument that the West consistently underestimates Communist strategic thinking, treating negotiations as good-faith exercises when they are tactical operations.

> I maintain there is harm in talking under present conditions. There are several reasons why this is so. First of all, Communists do not look upon negotiations, as we do, as an effort to reach an agreement. For them, negotiations are simply an instrument for pursuing their war aims.
>
> (010-036)
