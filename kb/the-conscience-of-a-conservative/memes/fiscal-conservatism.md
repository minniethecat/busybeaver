## Definition

Fiscal conservatism is the economic doctrine that government spending, taxation, and debt should be restrained to the minimum necessary for essential functions. It holds that balanced budgets are a moral obligation — that each generation should pay its own way rather than passing debt to the next — and that high taxation stifles individual initiative and economic growth. In Goldwater's formulation, fiscal conservatism is not merely an accounting preference but a constitutional principle: the federal government has no authority to spend beyond its enumerated powers, and deficit spending represents a form of taxation without representation imposed on future citizens.

## In Conservative Thought

Fiscal conservatism occupies a central place in Goldwater's conservative philosophy. Government spending, he argues, is the primary mechanism by which federal power expands — each new program creates a constituency dependent on it, and each new tax to fund it reduces the citizen's freedom to direct his own resources. The conservative alternative is not mere austerity but a reassertion of constitutional limits: if the federal government confined itself to its enumerated powers, the budget would balance itself.

Goldwater's fiscal argument connects directly to his broader critique of the welfare state. Federal spending on education, agriculture, labor, and social welfare programs represents not legitimate government function but a usurpation of state authority and individual responsibility. Each dollar spent by Washington is a dollar removed from local control and personal choice. The fiscal conservative thus sees the budget not as a neutral accounting document but as a moral statement about the proper relationship between citizen and state.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater advances several interconnected arguments for fiscal conservatism in Chapter VII ("Taxes and Spending"):

**Government growth outpaces economic growth** (007-013, (007-014), (007-015)). Federal spending rose from $15.2 billion in fiscal 1951 to a proposed $37.0 billion by fiscal 1961 — more than doubling in a decade without any commensurate expansion of constitutional authority. This growth, Goldwater contends, reflects not genuine national need but the political logic of concentrated benefits and diffuse costs: each program benefits a specific constituency while the tax burden spreads across all citizens.

**Deficit spending is a moral failure** (007-014). When government spends beyond its revenues, it imposes a hidden tax on future generations who had no voice in the decision. This violates the fundamental conservative principle that each individual bears responsibility for his own choices — government should not borrow against the earnings of citizens not yet born.

**Spending drives centralization** (008-002). The growth of the Department of Health, Education and Welfare exemplifies how federal spending creates bureaucratic empires. Programs justified as temporary interventions become permanent institutions, each with its own budget, staff, and constituency. The fiscal conservative recognizes that spending reductions require not just line-item cuts but the elimination of unauthorized federal functions.

**Economic strength is the foundation of military strength** (007-020). Goldwater links fiscal and national security concerns: a nation burdened by debt and inflation cannot sustain the economic base necessary for military preparedness. Fiscal conservatism is thus not only an economic philosophy but a strategic necessity in the Cold War context.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Fiscal Conservatism | Deficit Spending / Big Government |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Budget | Balanced or surplus; spend within means | Chronic deficits; borrow from future |
| Taxation | Low, flat, and simple to minimize economic distortion | Progressive and high to fund redistribution |
| Federal role | Limited to enumerated constitutional powers | Expanding to meet perceived social needs |
| Accountability | Each generation pays its own way | Present voters benefit, future taxpayers pay |
| Economic growth | Private savings and investment drive growth | Government spending stimulates demand |

Goldwater's fiscal conservatism also stands in contrast to the "Modern Republicanism" of the Eisenhower administration, which accepted the basic framework of New Deal spending while seeking only to manage it more efficiently. For Goldwater, efficiency in an unconstitutional program is no virtue — the program itself must be eliminated.
