## Overview

Foreign policy occupies a central place in Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative*. Writing at the height of the Cold War, Goldwater argues that "our entire approach to foreign policy over the past fourteen years" has been fundamentally flawed (010-007). He advocates for a foreign policy grounded in clear anti-communist principles, military superiority, and a realistic assessment of America's strategic interests.

## The Anti-Communist Framework

Goldwater's foreign policy vision is built on an uncompromising anti-communist foundation. He rejects the idea of peaceful coexistence with the Soviet Union, arguing that "communism will not be beaten by a policy that is the common denominator of the free world's approach to the Soviet challenge" (010-065). The goal of American policy, in his view, should not be merely to contain communism but to achieve victory over it.

He proposes making this explicit: "make it the cornerstone of our foreign policy: that we would rather die than live under communism" (010-012). This moral clarity, Goldwater believes, is essential — without it, American policy drifts without direction, measuring success by process rather than outcome.

## Military Superiority

A key element of Goldwater's foreign policy critique concerns military preparedness. He argues that America's nuclear superiority has been the decisive factor in Cold War stability: "our nuclear superiority has been the alliance's shield against aggression" (010-019). The Soviet Union's acquisition of nuclear weapons fundamentally changed the strategic calculus, but Goldwater warns against self-deception about the new balance of power (010-005).

Goldwater advocates for maintaining "superiority in all of the weapons — military, as well as political — that are needed to achieve victory" (010-059). He is skeptical of arms control agreements that might disadvantage the United States, warning against "a permanent ban on the testing of nuclear weapons, and the inclination to accept an agreement that would freeze our nuclear technology at a level of parity with the Soviet Union's" (010-057).

The psychological dimension of nuclear parity is equally concerning to Goldwater: "to impending physical parity in nuclear weapons must be added a psychological factor of uncertainty" (010-056). In his view, American willingness to negotiate from a position of weakness signals a lack of resolve that adversaries will exploit.

## The Foreign Aid Program

Goldwater devotes sustained attention to the Foreign Aid program, describing it as a "prominent feature of American foreign policy" that "began modestly" but has grown into a massive and questionably effective enterprise (010-042). He poses a sharp question: "Is our foreign aid program the kind that will advance the cause of freedom?" (010-030).

His critique centers on effectiveness and strategic purpose. He advocates for "a discriminating foreign aid policy" in which "American aid should be extended only to those nations that are clearly committed to the cause of freedom" (010-086). Indiscriminate aid, in his view, props up neutralist or even hostile regimes while draining American resources.

## Confusion in Foreign Affairs

Goldwater diagnoses a deeper conceptual problem in American foreign policy: "it seems to me that our approach to foreign affairs suffers from a confusion of purpose" (010-062). The United States, he argues, has lost sight of the distinction between allies and adversaries, between freedom and tyranny.

He calls for "measuring each aspect of our foreign policy against the standard — Is it anti-communist?" (010-078). This single-minded focus reflects his conviction that the Cold War is fundamentally a struggle between incompatible systems, and that ambiguity in policy serves the interests of the adversary.

> I hesitate to restate the obvious — to say again what has been said so many times before by so many others: that the Communists' aim is to conquer the world. I repeat it because it is the beginning and the end of our knowledge about the conflict between Communism and freedom.
>
> (010-007)
