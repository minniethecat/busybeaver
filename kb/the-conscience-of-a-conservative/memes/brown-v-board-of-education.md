## Overview

*Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) was a landmark Supreme Court decision that declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional, overturning the "separate but equal" doctrine established in *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896). The unanimous decision, authored by Chief Justice Earl Warren, was a pivotal moment in the civil rights movement.

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater's position on *Brown* is nuanced and characteristic. He states plainly: "I am in agreement with the objectives of the Supreme Court as stated in the Brown decision. I believe that it is both wise and just for negro children to attend the same schools as whites" (004-015). His objection is constitutional, not substantive.

He argues that "the federal Constitution does not require the States to maintain racially mixed schools" and that "it has never been seriously argued — the argument certainly was not made by the Supreme Court — that the authors of the Fourteenth Amendment intended to alter the Constitutional scheme with respect to education" (004-010). The problem is judicial overreach: the Court achieved a desirable result through constitutionally questionable means.

## Significance

*Brown* represents, for Goldwater, the tension between desirable ends and constitutional means. He supports desegregation as policy but insists it should be pursued through lawful channels — congressional legislation, state action, and constitutional amendment — rather than judicial fiat that distorts the Constitution's meaning.


> For the federal Constitution does not require the States to maintain racially mixed schools. Despite the recent holding of the Supreme Court, I am firmly convinced — not only that integrated schools are not required — but that the Constitution does not permit any interference whatsoever by the federal government in the field of education.
>
> (004-008)
