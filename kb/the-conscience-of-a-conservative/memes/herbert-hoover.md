Herbert Hoover served as the 31st President of the United States from 1929 to 1933. After his presidency, [[harry-truman|President Truman]] and later [[dwight-eisenhower|President Eisenhower]] appointed him to chair the Commission on Organization of the Executive Branch of the Government — commonly known as the Hoover Commission — which studied ways to improve the efficiency of the [[bureaucracy|federal bureaucracy]].

Goldwater cites the Hoover Commission's work as a demonstration that significant savings in [[government-spending|federal spending]] could be achieved without cutting essential government functions. He notes approvingly that "the recommendations of the Hoover Commission which could save the government billions of dollars are adopted only in part" (007-018), implying that [[us-congress|Congress]] was unwilling to implement even the most sensible and non-ideological proposals for reducing government waste. For Goldwater, the fate of the Hoover Commission's recommendations illustrated a systematic resistance to fiscal discipline in Washington.

The Hoover Commission's approach — focusing on administrative efficiency and reorganization rather than on the philosophical question of what government should and should not do — represented a moderate, technocratic path to spending reduction. Goldwater's invocation of the Commission serves to make the point that even within the existing framework of government, there were substantial opportunities for savings that [[us-congress|Congress]] neglected. If the government could not adopt even these modest efficiency measures, Goldwater suggests, then more fundamental reforms of the [[government-spending|federal budget]] process were necessary.

## Hoover's Fiscal Legacy

Beyond the Commission's specific recommendations, Hoover represented a tradition of Republican fiscal conservatism that Goldwater sought to revive. Hoover had warned against the growth of federal spending during the New Deal era, and his post-presidential work on government efficiency provided empirical evidence for arguments Goldwater would later make in *[[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]]*. The Hoover Commission's finding that billions could be saved through reorganization alone supported Goldwater's contention that "the government must begin to *withdraw* from a whole series of programs that are outside its constitutional mandate" (007-019) — that fiscal discipline was not a matter of cutting essential services but of eliminating unconstitutional ones.

> Neither of our political parties has seriously faced up to the problem of government spending. The recommendations of the Hoover Commission which could save the taxpayer in the neighborhood of $7 billion a year have been largely ignored.
>
> (007-018)
