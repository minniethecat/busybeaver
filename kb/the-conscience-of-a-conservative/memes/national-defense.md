## Definition

National defense, in Goldwater's conservative framework, is the federal government's primary Constitutional obligation — the protection of the nation's territory, citizens, and institutions from foreign threats. It is one of the very few functions that Goldwater acknowledges as a *legitimate* exercise of federal power. Under the Constitution, the federal government is charged with providing for the common defense, and Goldwater treats this as a binding duty rather than a discretionary policy choice.

Crucially, Goldwater distinguishes between genuine national defense and the expansive interpretation that uses "defense" as a justification for unrelated federal programs. He argues that defense has been routinely invoked as a pretext: the National Defense Education Act, for instance, used defense rhetoric to justify federal intervention in education — a domain Goldwater regards as properly reserved to states and localities.

## In Conservative Thought

National defense occupies a paradoxical position in Goldwater's thought. On one hand, it is the most compelling example of a function that only the federal government can perform — states cannot independently conduct foreign policy or maintain armed forces capable of deterring Soviet aggression. On the other hand, Goldwater is acutely aware that "national defense" has become a rhetorical device for expanding federal power into domains that have nothing to do with military security.

Goldwater's solution is to insist on a strict definition: national defense means military preparedness and the conduct of foreign policy aimed at protecting American freedom. It does not mean education policy, agricultural subsidies, or welfare programs — even when those programs are branded with the word "defense." This rigor reflects his broader conservative commitment to limiting government to its Constitutional functions.

Goldwater's approach to defense also reflects his Cold War context. Writing in 1960, he viewed the Soviet Union as an existential threat that required not merely containment but "victory over Communism" (010-001). This distinguishes his position from both the dovish left (which sought accommodation) and the Eisenhower-era Republican establishment (which accepted containment as sufficient). For Goldwater, a "tolerable peace" must "follow victory over Communism" — anything less is surrender by another name (010-001).

## Historical Context

The Cold War had been underway for approximately fourteen years when Goldwater wrote *The Conscience of a Conservative*. The Soviet Union had developed nuclear weapons (1949), the Korean War had ended in stalemate (1953), and the space race had begun with the launch of Sputnik (1957). American defense spending had risen dramatically, and the permanent military establishment that George Washington had warned against had become a fixture of American life.

Domestically, the Cold War provided a ready justification for expanding federal programs. The National Defense Education Act of 1958, passed in response to Sputnik, authorized federal funding for science, mathematics, and foreign language education. The National Defense Highway Act (1956) had launched the interstate highway system under the rationale of military necessity. Goldwater viewed this pattern — using defense as a Trojan horse for domestic spending — as a threat to both limited government and genuine national security.

> Let us, moreover, keep the problem in proper perspective. The national school system is not in distress. Shortly before the Senate debate this year on increased federal aid, I asked Mr. Arthur Flemming the Secretary of Health, Education and Welfare, how many of the Nation’s school districts were in actual trouble—how many, that is, had reached their bonded limit. His answer was approximately 230. Now there are roughly 42,000 school districts in America. Thus, proponents of federal aid are talking about a problem that affects only one-half of one percent of our school districts! I cannot believe that the state governments responsible for those areas are incapable of making up whatever deficiencies exist. It so happens that the same deficiency figure—one-half of one percent—applies to my own state of Arizona. And Arizona proudly turned down federal funds under the 1958 National Defense Education Act on the grounds that Arizonans, themselves, were quite capable of closing the gap.
> (009-008)

## Key Arguments

**Defense spending does not explain the growth of government.** Goldwater directly challenges the claim that expanded federal spending is a consequence of defense needs. "We are often told that increased federal spending is simply a reflection of the increased cost of national defense," he writes. "This is untrue" (007-001). He presents data showing that purely domestic expenditures increased by 143% between 1951 and 1961, far outstripping any plausible defense-driven growth. The implication is clear: the growth of the federal government is driven by welfare-state expansion, not by military necessity.

**Welfare spending rivals defense spending.** Goldwater cites a news report estimating that Department of Health, Education and Welfare expenditures for fiscal year 1961 would exceed $15 billion — "second only to national defense" (008-001). This comparison is strategic: if welfare spending rivals defense spending, the claim that government growth is defense-driven collapses. The welfare state, not the Cold War, is the engine of federal expansion.

**The National Defense Education Act as federal overreach.** Goldwater identifies the National Defense Education Act of 1958 as a case study in how "defense" is used to justify unconstitutional federal intervention. The Act contained "no less than twelve direct controls" conditioning federal aid on compliance with federal standards (009-001). Its "acknowledged purpose" was to "persuade local educational institutions to put greater emphasis on the physical sciences and other subjects directly related to national defense" — which Goldwater regards as the federal government "helping to determine the *content* of education; and influencing content is the last, not the first, stage of control" (009-001).

**Genuine defense requires victory, not coexistence.** Goldwater's most distinctive argument about national defense is that it requires the active prosecution of the Cold War, not mere deterrence or accommodation. He rejects the goal of "peace" in the abstract: "we do not want the peace of surrender. We want a peace in which freedom and justice will prevail, and that — given the nature of Communism — is a peace in which Soviet power will no longer be in a position to threaten us and the rest of the world" (010-001). A foreign policy that ignores this imperative "will lead to our extinction as a nation" (010-001).

**State-level capacity.** When discussing federal aid to education, Goldwater notes that his home state of Arizona "proudly turned down federal funds under the 1958 National Defense Education Act on the grounds that Arizonans, themselves, were quite capable of closing the gap" (009-001). This illustrates his broader argument that states and localities can address their own needs without federal intervention — even in areas branded as defense-related.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Genuine National Defense | Defense as Pretext |
|-----------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Scope** | Military preparedness and foreign policy | Any program branded "defense" (education, highways) |
| **Constitutionality** | Expressly authorized (common defense) | Tenth Amendment violation (reserved to states) |
| **Effect on freedom** | Protects freedom from foreign threats | Erodes freedom through federal control |
| **Budget impact** | Limited to military necessity | Open-ended expansion of federal spending |
| **Ultimate goal** | Victory over Communism | Perpetual government growth |

Goldwater's argument is that conflating these two categories serves the interests of government expansion, not national security. A defense budget swollen with domestic programs is not a stronger defense — it is a weaker Constitution.

National defense appears in Goldwater's text primarily as a benchmark against which other federal spending is measured, and as a concept whose genuine meaning must be defended against those who would stretch it to cover unconstitutional federal activities. It is the legitimate function that most clearly reveals the illegitimacy of the functions that have been attached to it.
