The **Landrum-Griffin Act** (Labor-Management Reporting and Disclosure Act of 1959) was a federal labor law that established standards for union democracy and financial transparency. Senator Barry Goldwater discussed it at length in *The Conscience of a Conservative* as part of his analysis of labor policy and the proper role of federal legislation.

## Legislative History

The bill emerged from congressional debate over labor reform. The House of Representatives rejected the Kennedy-Ervin bill — a measure Goldwater considered a "piece of sheer power politics" masquerading as reform — and substituted the Landrum-Griffin bill in its place. The ensuing conference between representatives of the two houses made only minor changes to the House version; Goldwater estimated that approximately 90% of the original Landrum-Griffin bill survived in the conferees' report. (006-007)

The Senate adopted the conference report with only two dissenting votes. For Goldwater, this overwhelming support represented a significant legislative achievement. (007-007)

## Goldwater's Assessment

Goldwater considered the Landrum-Griffin Act an improvement over the Kennedy-Ervin measure, which he believed would have concentrated excessive power in the hands of union leadership while claiming to reform union practices. However, he maintained that Congress had still not fully come to grips with the fundamental problem: the relationship between compulsory unionism and individual worker freedom. (007-007)

His qualified endorsement of the bill reflected his broader philosophy: that legislation should be judged not merely by its stated intentions but by its actual effect on individual liberty. The Landrum-Griffin Act, in his view, was a step in the right direction but left unresolved the deeper conflict between union power and the rights of individual workers. (007-008)

> Though the Landrum-Griffin Bill was an improvement over the Kennedy measure, Congress has still to come to grips with the real evil in the Labor field. Graft and corruption are symptoms of the illness that besets the labor movement, not its basic cause. The basic cause is the enormous, quasi-monopolistic economic power that unions exercise.
>
> (006-004)
