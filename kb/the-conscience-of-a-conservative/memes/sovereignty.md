## Overview

Sovereignty is the principle that a nation possesses supreme and independent authority over its own territory and affairs. In the American constitutional tradition, sovereignty resides ultimately in the people, who delegate limited powers to their government through the Constitution. The preservation of national sovereignty against external encroachment is a fundamental concern of conservative governance.

## Sovereignty and the Constitution

The American Constitution establishes a framework in which sovereignty is both delegated and divided. The federal government exercises those powers enumerated in the Constitution; all other powers remain with the states or the people. This constitutional architecture was designed to prevent the concentration of sovereign power in any single institution while preserving the nation's independence in international affairs.

The Founders understood that the surrender of sovereignty — whether to a foreign power, an international body, or a domestic faction — would be tantamount to the abandonment of self-government. For this reason, the Constitution provides a specific mechanism — the amendment process — for any fundamental alteration of the sovereign powers of the United States.

## Threats to American Sovereignty

Participation in international organizations poses a distinct challenge to national sovereignty when the decisions of those organizations begin to override the constitutional processes of the member states. Activities of international bodies can make "strong inroads against the sovereign powers of Member Nations" without the consent of their people expressed through constitutional means (010-068).

The concern is not necessarily with the merits of any particular international agreement, but with the *process* by which sovereign authority is surrendered. Any surrender of sovereign American rights must take place within the framework of a proposed constitutional amendment — not "clandestinely, in the headquarters of some U.N. agency" (010-068). To permit otherwise is to allow the de facto amendment of the Constitution through executive agreement, bypassing the people whose consent is the sole legitimate source of sovereign authority.

## Sovereignty and Federalism

The principle of sovereignty operates at multiple levels within the American system. Just as the nation asserts sovereignty against external powers, the states retain sovereignty over those matters not delegated to the federal government. This dual sovereignty — [[states-rights]] alongside federal authority — is a distinctive feature of American constitutional design, providing an additional barrier against the concentration of power.

> I fear that our involvement in the United Nations may be leading to an unconstitutional surrender of American sovereignty. Many U.N. activities have already made strong inroads against the sovereign powers of Member Nations.
>
> (010-068)
