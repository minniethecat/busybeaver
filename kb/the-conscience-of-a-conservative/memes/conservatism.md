## Definition

Conservatism, as defined in Goldwater's political philosophy, is a comprehensive worldview that places the enhancement of man's spiritual nature as the primary concern of political philosophy (001-005). It is not merely an economic theory with incidental political implications, but rather a philosophy that "puts material things in their proper place — that has a structured view of the human being and of human society, in which economics plays only a subsidiary role" (001-004).

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, the Conservative position rests on three pillars of understanding about human nature. First, "each member of the species is a unique creature" whose "most sacred possession is his individual soul" (001-007). Only a philosophy accounting for essential differences between individuals can claim to be "in accord with Nature" (001-007). Second, "the economic and spiritual aspects of man's nature are inextricably intertwined" — political freedom and economic freedom are mutually dependent (001-008). Third, man's development "cannot be directed by outside forces"; every individual "is responsible for his own development" (001-009).

The Conservative worldview leads to a specific approach to politics: "the art of achieving the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order" (001-011). This balance recognizes that political power is an inherently "self-aggrandizing force" whose "appetite grows with eating" (001-011).

## Historical Context

Conservatism as Goldwater presents it traces its lineage through American founding principles. The framers of the Constitution understood that "freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power in a single authority" (002-008). The constitutional system — with its limits on federal authority, reservation of powers to the states, separation of powers among three branches, and difficult amendment procedures — embodies the Conservative principle that government must be constrained (002-008).

Goldwater distinguishes his Conservatism from what he sees as its corruptions: the "Modern Republicanism" that accepts expanded government power, and the liberal embrace of collectivism that "subordinates all other considerations to man's material well-being" (001-004). Throughout history, "true Conservatism has been at war equally with autocrats and with 'democratic' Jacobins" (001-010).

## Key Arguments

1. **The primacy of individual freedom**: The Conservative's "first concern will always be: *Are we maximizing freedom?*" (001-012). This is the overriding political challenge in an era where the balance between freedom and order "has long since tipped against freedom practically everywhere on earth" (001-012).

2. **Government as a threat to liberty**: "Throughout history, government has proved to be the chief instrument for thwarting man's liberty" (002-005). Power corrupts, and absolute power corrupts absolutely (002-005).

3. **The failure of the "Conservative demonstration"**: Goldwater blames Conservatives themselves for failing to translate widespread public support for Conservative principles into political action. "Our failure," he writes, "is the failure of the Conservative demonstration" — the inability to show how Conservative principles connect to practical governance (P01-009).

## Contrasts

Conservatism stands in direct opposition to Socialism, which Goldwater identifies as the philosophy that subordinates spiritual concerns to material ones. Liberalism, in Goldwater's framing, "tend[s] to look only at the material side of man's nature" (001-005). The welfare state represents the institutional expression of this materialist philosophy, creating dependency and eroding individual responsibility. Modern Republicanism — the accommodationist wing of Goldwater's own party — represents a betrayal of Conservative principles by accepting the premise that government should expand to meet perceived needs (002-002, 002-003).

## Mentions in the Text

Key passages where Goldwater develops his Conservative philosophy:

> "Conservatism is *not* an economic theory, though it has economic implications. The shoe is precisely on the other foot: it is Socialism that subordinates all other considerations to man's material well-being." (001-004)

> "The Conservative believes that man is, in part, an economic, an animal creature; but that he is also a spiritual creature with spiritual needs and spiritual desires." (001-005)

> "The conscience of the Conservative is pricked by *anyone* who would debase the dignity of the individual human being." (001-010)

> "The Conservative looks upon politics as the art of achieving the maximum amount of freedom for individuals that is consistent with the maintenance of social order." (001-011)
