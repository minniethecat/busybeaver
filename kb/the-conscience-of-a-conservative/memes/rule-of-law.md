## Definition

The rule of law, in Goldwater's conservative jurisprudence, is the principle that government power must be exercised within the bounds of fixed, publicly known legal rules — not according to the discretion, policy preferences, or evolving interpretations of government officials. Its highest American expression is the Constitution, which, together with "the laws 'made in pursuance thereof,' are the 'supreme law of the land'" (004-012, quoting Article VI of the Constitution).

Goldwater distinguishes the rule of law sharply from the "rule of men" (004-012). When judges or federal officials substitute their own intentions for those of the Constitution's framers, "we reject, in effect, the principle of Constitutional Government: we endorse a rule of men, not of laws" (004-012). The rule of law requires fidelity to the original meaning of the constitutional text, not to the evolving preferences of those who interpret it.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's commitment to the rule of law manifests across multiple domains of his political argument:

**1. The Tenth Amendment as Prohibitory Rule.** When critics dismiss the Tenth Amendment as merely "a general assumption" about the division of federal and state power, Goldwater forcefully rejects this reading. "The Tenth Amendment is *not* 'a general assumption,' but a prohibitory rule of law. The Tenth Amendment recognizes the States' *jurisdiction* in certain areas" (003-011). The amendment is not an advisory guideline but a binding legal constraint on federal power.

**2. The Constitution Against the Supreme Court.** Goldwater's most striking application of the rule of law is his refusal to accept that a Supreme Court decision is automatically the law of the land if it contradicts the Constitution's original meaning. "I am therefore not impressed by the claim that the Supreme Court's decision on school integration is the law of the land. *The Constitution, and the laws 'made in pursuance thereof,' are the 'supreme law of the land.'* The Constitution is what its authors intended it to be and said it was — not what the Supreme Court says it is" (004-012). For Goldwater, the rule of law means that the Constitution governs the Court, not the other way around.

**3. The Butler Case as Constitutional Precedent.** Goldwater invokes *United States v. Butler* (1936), in which the Supreme Court "promptly struck down" the first Agricultural Adjustment Act on the grounds that the general welfare clause "was simply a qualification of the taxing power and did not give Congress the power to *control* anything" (005-003). The case demonstrates, in Goldwater's view, what genuine constitutional adjudication looks like — the Court enforcing the actual limits of the text against an overreaching Congress, rather than inventing new powers or rights.

**4. The Laws of Nature as Meta-Legal Foundation.** Beyond the written Constitution, Goldwater grounds the rule of law in a higher order: "The laws of God, and of nature, have no dateline" (001-003). The principles on which the conservative political position is based "are derived from the nature of man, and from the truths that God has revealed about His creation" (001-003). This natural law foundation means that the rule of law is not merely a procedural requirement — it is a substantive commitment to a legal order that respects the given nature of man and the limits it places on what government may rightfully do.

**5. The Erosion of Legal Restraint.** Throughout his analysis, Goldwater documents how the rule of law has been systematically weakened. Federal officials ignore the Tenth Amendment when it suits their policy goals. The Supreme Court reads new meanings into the Constitution that its framers never intended. Congress asserts powers — over agriculture, education, labor relations — that the Constitution never delegated to it. The cumulative effect is a government increasingly unbound by law.

## Mentions in the Text

Key passages where Goldwater articulates the rule of law:

> "The Tenth Amendment is *not* 'a general assumption,' but a prohibitory rule of law." (003-011)

> "The Constitution, and the laws 'made in pursuance thereof,' are the 'supreme law of the land.' The Constitution is what its authors intended it to be and said it was — not what the Supreme Court says it is." (004-012)

> "If we condone the practice of substituting our own intentions for those of the Constitution's framers, we reject, in effect, the principle of Constitutional Government: we endorse a rule of men, not of laws." (004-012)

> "The laws of God, and of nature, have no dateline. The principles on which the Conservative political position is based have been established by a process that has nothing to do with the social, economic and political landscape that changes from decade to decade and from century to century." (001-003)
