**United States v. Butler**, 297 U.S. 1 (1936), was a landmark [[supreme-court|Supreme Court]]
decision that struck down the first Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA) of 1933.
In [[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]],
[[barry-goldwater|Barry Goldwater]] presents the Butler case as the high-water
mark of constitutional fidelity in the New Deal era—and its subsequent evasion
as a cautionary tale about judicial capitulation to political pressure.

## Background

The Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1933 was one of the central pillars of
[[franklin-roosevelt|Franklin D. Roosevelt's]] [[the-new-deal|New Deal]].
Designed to raise farm prices by paying farmers to reduce production, the Act
was financed by a tax on agricultural processors. The justification offered by
its sponsors rested on the General Welfare Clause of the Constitution.

## The Supreme Court's Decision

In a 6-3 decision, the Supreme Court ruled the AAA unconstitutional. Justice
Owen Roberts, writing for the majority, held that the phrase "general welfare"
in Article I, Section 8 was a qualification of the taxing power—not an
independent grant of authority to regulate any activity Congress deemed
beneficial. The Court's reasoning, as Goldwater summarizes, was that "no power
over agriculture was given to any branch of the national government" (005-010).

Goldwater presents this as the correct constitutional interpretation, consistent
with the [[limited-government|limited government]] principles of the Framers:

> The sponsors of the first Agriculture Adjustment Act, passed in 1933, tried to
> justify the law under the so-called general welfare clause of the Constitution.
> The Supreme Court promptly struck down that legislation on the grounds that the
> phrase, "general welfare," was simply a qualification of the taxing power and
> did not give Congress the power to control activities that were beyond the
> scope of federal authority. (005-010)

## The Evasion

The constitutional victory was short-lived. Congress responded to the Butler
decision by passing a second Agricultural Adjustment Act, this time justifying
the program under the Commerce Clause rather than the General Welfare Clause
(005-011):

> The New Deal Congress replied by enacting substantially identical legislation,
> the second A.A.A., and now sought to justify the program as a "regulation of
> interstate commerce." This was a transparent evasion of the Butler case; but
> the Supreme Court, which by this time was under heavy political fire for having
> thwarted the "Roosevelt Revolution," made one of its celebrated about-faces and
> upheld the new act. (005-011)

Goldwater treats this sequence—constitutional ruling, legislative evasion,
judicial capitulation—as emblematic of the broader erosion of constitutional
restraints on federal power during and after the New Deal. The federal
government, he concludes, "has usurped many powers under the guise of
'regulating commerce'" (005-011).

## Significance in Conservative Thought

The Butler case occupies a distinctive place in Goldwater's argument because it
demonstrates both that the Constitution *did* impose meaningful limits on federal
power and that those limits were deliberately circumvented rather than honestly
amended. For Goldwater, this distinguishes his constitutional objection from mere
policy disagreement: the New Deal's agricultural programs were not merely unwise
but, in the original constitutional understanding, unlawful.

The Butler story also illustrates the book's view of the [[supreme-court|Supreme
Court]] as an institution whose fidelity to the Constitution can be compromised
by political pressure—a theme that recurs in the book's discussions of other
court decisions and the appointment process.
