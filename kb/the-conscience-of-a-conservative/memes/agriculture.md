## Definition

Agriculture, in Goldwater's analysis, represents a case study in unconstitutional federal overreach. The federal government's involvement in farm policy — through production controls, price supports, and subsidy programs — exemplifies the broader pattern of government expansion that Goldwater opposes throughout *The Conscience of a Conservative*. "The teaching of the Constitution on this matter is perfectly clear. No power over agriculture was given to any branch of the national government" (005-010).

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's argument against federal agricultural programs rests on constitutional, economic, and moral grounds. Constitutionally, the power to regulate agriculture is not among the enumerated powers of Congress. The sponsors of the first Agricultural Adjustment Act had to justify it under the interstate commerce clause — a rationale Goldwater considers a "perversion" of constitutional meaning (005-010).

Economically, Goldwater argues that federal intervention has made the farm problem worse, not better. "The reason government intervention has created more problems than it has solved is quite simple. Farm production, like any other production, is best controlled by the natural operation of the free market" (005-014). Price supports create surpluses, and production controls restrict farmers' freedom.

Morally, Goldwater sees the farmer's desire for independence as the healthiest aspect of American agriculture: "Most farmers want to stand on their own feet. They are prepared to take their chances in the free market" (005-018). The subsidy system undermines this self-reliance by making farmers dependent on federal checks.

## Historical Context

The federal government's involvement in agriculture began during the New Deal with the Agricultural Adjustment Act. Goldwater traces the consequences: "Federal intervention in agriculture has, indeed, produced chaos" (005-008). The Supreme Court case *Wickard v. Filburn* (1942) upheld the second AAA, penalizing a farmer "for planting 23 acres of wheat, instead of the eleven acres the government had allotted him" — a decision Goldwater views as a landmark of constitutional overreach (005-012).

## Key Arguments

1. **Constitutional authority is lacking**: "No power over agriculture was given to any branch of the national government" (005-010). The use of the commerce clause to justify farm programs represents constitutional distortion.

2. **Government intervention creates problems**: Rather than solving the farm problem, federal programs have created surpluses and dependency. "The tragedy, of course, is that the federal government's unconstitutional intrusion into Agriculture has not brought us any closer to a solution of the 'farm problem'" (005-013).

3. **The free market is the proper regulator**: Farm production, "like any other production, is best controlled by the natural operation of the free market" (005-014).

4. **Farmers prefer independence**: "Most farmers want to stand on their own feet. They are prepared to take their chances in the free market" (005-018). The solution is "prompt and final termination of the farm subsidy program" (005-017).

5. **The surplus problem requires market solutions**: "The problem of surpluses will not be solved until we recognize that technological progress and other factors have made it possible for the needs of America...to be supplied by only a fraction of our present farm capacity" (005-016).

> Hamilton was wrong in his prediction as to what men would do, but quite right in foreseeing the consequences of their foolhardiness. Federal intervention in agriculture has, indeed, proved "troublesome." Disregard of the Constitution in this field has brought about the inevitable loss of personal freedom.
>
> (005-008)
