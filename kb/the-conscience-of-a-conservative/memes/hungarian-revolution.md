The Hungarian Revolution of 1956 was a nationwide uprising against the Soviet-backed Hungarian People's Republic. Beginning on October 23, 1956, with a student demonstration in Budapest, it rapidly grew into a broader revolt demanding political reform and independence from Soviet control. Soviet forces invaded on November 4, 1956, crushing the revolution and installing a new government under Janos Kadar.

Goldwater invokes the Hungarian Revolution as a defining example of what he saw as the failure of American foreign policy to confront Soviet aggression decisively. In "The Soviet Menace," he proposes an alternative doctrine of direct military confrontation — one that would have presented the Kremlin with an ultimatum against intervention:

> Assume also a major uprising in Eastern Europe, such as occurred in Budapest in 1956. In such a situation, we ought to present the Kremlin with an ultimatum forbidding Soviet intervention, and be prepared, if the ultimatum is rejected, to move a highly mobile task force equipped with appropriate nuclear weapons to the scene of the revolt. (010-090)

> Had we the will and the means for it in 1956, such a policy would have saved the Hungarian Revolution. (010-090)

The Hungarian Revolution thus serves, in Goldwater's argument, as both a moral indictment of passive containment and a strategic case for what he called "a policy of victory" over communism — a willingness to use American military power to support anti-communist uprisings behind the Iron Curtain.
