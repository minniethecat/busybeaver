## Definition

A civil right, in Goldwater's careful formulation, "is a right that is asserted and is therefore protected by some valid law. It may be asserted by the common law, or by local or federal statutes, or by the Constitution; but unless a right is incorporated in the law, it is not a civil right and is not enforceable by the instruments of the civil law" (004-006). This definition is significant because it distinguishes civil rights from merely desirable social outcomes, anchoring them in positive law rather than policy preferences.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's treatment of civil rights is one of the most carefully argued sections of the book. He begins by acknowledging the political reality: "An attempt has been made in recent years to disparage the principle of States' Rights by equating it with defense of the South's position on racial integration" (004-002). He rejects this equation, arguing that "there can be a conflict between States' Rights, properly defined — and civil rights, properly defined" only if one or both are improperly defined (004-003).

The Conservative position on civil rights flows from the same principles that animate the broader philosophy: the primacy of the individual, the rule of law, and the rejection of group-based claims. Every American possesses the same civil rights regardless of race — the right to vote (protected by the Fifteenth Amendment), the right to equal treatment under law. The challenge is enforcement, not definition.

## Historical Context

Goldwater acknowledges that civil rights had become "one of the most badly understood concepts in modern political usage" thanks to "extravagant and shameless misuse by people who ought to know better" (004-005). Writing in 1960, at the height of the civil rights movement, he sought to articulate a conservative civil rights position grounded in constitutional fidelity rather than sectional politics.

His own record included support for the 1957 and 1960 Civil Rights Acts and work to desegregate the Arizona National Guard and the Senate cafeteria. This biographical context explains his insistence that civil rights are a matter of law, not geography.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's argument has several components. First, he defines civil rights in strictly legal terms, rejecting the expansion of the concept to include claims not grounded in valid law. Second, he identifies specific rights clearly protected: "One of them is the right to vote. The Fifteenth Amendment provides that the right of citizens to vote shall not be abridged by the United States or any state on account of race" (004-007).

Third, he argues that enforcement of civil rights should proceed through lawful means — courts, legislation, and executive action — rather than through extra-constitutional federal interventions that themselves violate the constitutional structure. The Constitution provides remedies; the task is to apply them.
