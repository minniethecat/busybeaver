## Overview

Deficit spending occurs when government expenditures exceed government revenues in a given fiscal period, with the difference financed through public borrowing. While deficit spending is sometimes defended as a tool of economic management, conservative analysis treats persistent deficits as both a symptom and a cause of excessive government — a mechanism by which the true cost of state expansion is hidden from the taxpayers who ultimately bear it.

## The Growth of Federal Spending

The scale of federal spending has undergone a dramatic transformation over the past several decades. Federal expenditures have grown from approximately three and one-half billion dollars annually to nearly a hundred billion — an expansion that cannot be explained by population growth, inflation, or national defense alone (002-013). Purely domestic expenditures have increased by over 140% in a single decade, far outstripping any reasonable measure of necessity (007-015).

This expansion has occurred under the leadership of both major political parties. On occasion, men and political parties have been elevated to power on promises to restore limited government, only to proceed, after their election, to expand the activities of government (002-018). The persistence of deficit spending across administrations of both parties indicates that the problem is not merely one of partisan choice but of systemic incentives: government, once empowered to spend beyond its means, will consistently do so.

## Spending and Taxation

The problem of deficit spending is inseparable from the problem of taxation. There is something to be said for the proposition that spending will never be reduced so long as there is money in the federal treasury — the availability of funds, whether from taxes or borrowing, creates its own demand (007-011). The path to reducing the burden of taxation necessarily passes through the reduction of spending; it is in the area of spending that government performance has been most disappointing (007-012).

> While there is something to be said for the proposition that spending will never be reduced so long as there is money in the federal treasury, I believe that as a practical matter spending will go down only when it has to.
> (007-011)

Broken promises on spending reduction have been a recurring feature of modern governance. In 1952, Republican leaders met and emphatically agreed on the need to reduce spending. Yet instead of fulfilling that promise, federal spending greatly increased during the years that followed — rising from projections of a $60 billion budget to approximately $80 billion (007-014). The lesson is not that spending reduction is impossible, but that it cannot be achieved through rhetoric alone; it requires structural constraints on the government's power to borrow and spend.

## Grants-in-Aid and Coercive Spending

One mechanism of deficit expansion that tends to be neglected in public discussion is the system of federal grants-in-aid to the states. In recent years the federal government has continued, and in many cases increased, these "grants" in areas where the Constitution grants the federal government no authority to act. These programs are designed to stimulate state spending in health, education, and welfare — in effect, using federal dollars to direct state policy in areas reserved to the states (003-005). The true cost of such programs is hidden twice over: first, through deficit financing that obscures the taxpayer's burden, and second, through a funding mechanism that makes state governments appear to spend "freely" when in fact they are responding to federal inducements.
