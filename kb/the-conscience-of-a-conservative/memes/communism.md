## Definition

Communism, in Goldwater's framework, is not merely a competing economic system but a "revolutionary world movement that possesses not only the will to dominate absolutely every square mile of the globe, but increasingly the capacity to do so" (010-004). It operates through multiple dimensions — "a military power that rivals our own, political warfare and propaganda skills that are superior to ours, an international fifth column that operates conspiratorially in the heart of our defenses, an ideology that imbues its adherents with a sense of historical mission" — all "controlled by a ruthless despotism that brooks no deviation from the revolutionary course" (010-004).

At its core, "Communism is a political movement, and its weapons are primarily political" (010-028). This principle shapes Goldwater's entire foreign policy analysis: material aid cannot defeat a political movement because "a man's politics are, primarily, the product of his mind. Material wealth can help him further his political goals, but it will not change them" (010-028).

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's treatment of Communism is the centerpiece of his foreign policy chapters (chapters 9–10) and defines the external dimension of conservative principles. His analysis rests on four pillars:

**1. Recognize the Enemy's True Objective.** "The Communists' aim is to conquer the world" (010-007) — Goldwater insists this must be "the beginning and the end of our knowledge about the conflict between East and West" (010-007). Yet he laments that "very few of us have *believed* it" (010-007), and this failure of belief has produced a fundamentally defensive posture. While "the Communists wage war," America has "waged peace" seeking "settlements" while "the Communists seek victories" (010-009).

**2. Victory, Not Peace, Must Be the Goal.** "A tolerable peace, in other words, must *follow* victory over Communism" (010-010). Goldwater argues that "it is not to wage a struggle against Communism, but to win it" (010-080). The policy standard must be: "Is it helpful in defeating Communism?" (010-079). The Communist's offensive strategy means that "no nation at war, employing an exclusively defensive strategy, can hope to survive for long" (010-020).

**3. Understand Communist Methods.** Goldwater analyzes multiple dimensions of Communist warfare. On negotiations: "Communists do not look upon negotiations, as we do, as an effort to reach an agreement. For them, negotiations are simply an *instrument* of political warfare" (010-036). On exchanges: "The exchange program is a Communist confidence game" (010-049) — "the Kremlin's aim is not to make Americans approve of Communism... it is to make us tolerant of Communism" (010-048). On the United Nations: "The United Nations, we must remember, is in part a Communist organization" (010-063) that "provides a unique forum for Communist propaganda" (010-066).

**4. Reject Accommodationist Fallacies.** Goldwater systematically refutes what he calls the "stomach theory of Communism" (010-027) — the idea that economic prosperity prevents Communist allegiance. He also rejects the "evolution" theory of aid to Communist governments: aid "has not made Communist governments less Communist. It has not caused Communist governments to change sides in the Cold War. It has made it easier for Communist governments to keep their subjects enslaved" (010-073).

## Domestic Dimension

While Communism is primarily an external threat, Goldwater notes its domestic manifestations. The doctrines of Karl Marx shaped collectivist thinking: "The collectivists — non-Communists as well as Communists — had adopted the Marxist objective of 'socializing the means of production'" (008-007). The progressive income tax "does violence both to the charter of the Republic and the laws of Nature" (007-009), reflecting an egalitarian impulse that Goldwater associates with Communist-influenced thinking. American coercion of union members to support political candidates they oppose — "under union shop conditions, the only way an individual can avoid contributing to the political campaign of a candidate whom he may not approve is to give up his job" (006-026) — represents a domestic parallel to Communist coercion.

## Goldwater's Prescription

Goldwater proposes an offensive strategy against Communism: "declare the world Communist movement an outlaw in the community of civilized nations" (010-087); "withdraw diplomatic recognition from all Communist governments including that of the Soviet Union" (010-087); "encourage the captive peoples to revolt against their Communist rulers" (010-088); and "be prepared to undertake military operations against vulnerable Communist regimes" (010-090). These measures are designed to shift from a reactive, defensive posture — where "the Communists are on the offensive and we are on the defensive" (010-038) — to an active strategy where America dictates the terms of engagement.

## Mentions in the Text

Key passages where Goldwater defines his anti-Communist position:

> "the Communists' aim is to conquer the world. I repeat it because it is the beginning and the end of our knowledge about the conflict between East and West." (010-007)
