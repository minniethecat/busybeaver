John Emerich Edward Dalberg-Acton, 1st Baron Acton (1834–1902), was a British historian and liberal Catholic thinker, best known for his aphorism on the corrupting nature of power. His scholarship centered on the history of liberty, and he served as Regius Professor of Modern History at Cambridge.

## Life and Work

Born in Naples to a Catholic aristocratic family with roots in the English peerage, Acton was educated at Oscott College and studied under the German church historian Ignaz von Döllinger in Munich. Prevented from attending Cambridge as an undergraduate because of his Catholicism, he later returned as Regius Professor of Modern History in 1895. His major project — a comprehensive history of liberty — was never completed, but his essays and lectures exerted profound influence. He served as a Liberal Member of Parliament and was raised to the peerage in 1869.

## Goldwater's Use of Acton

Goldwater opens *The Conscience of a Conservative* by invoking Lord Acton's most famous insight to frame the central problem of government:

> Throughout history, government has proved to be the chief instrument for thwarting man's liberty. Government represents power in the hands of some men to control and regulate the lives of other men. And power, as Lord Acton said, *corrupts* men. "Absolute power," he added, "corrupts absolutely." (001-003)

This citation does more than lend historical authority to Goldwater's argument — it establishes the philosophical foundation of the entire book. Acton's dictum that power is inherently corrupting is the premise from which Goldwater derives his case for limited government, federalism, and individual liberty.

## Acton in the Conservative Tradition

The invocation of Acton was significant for the intellectual positioning of postwar American conservatism. Acton was not a conservative in the Burkean sense; he was a classical liberal who judged institutions — including the Church — by their effect on individual freedom. By starting with Acton rather than, say, Edmund Burke, Goldwater aligned American conservatism with a principle-based defense of liberty rather than a tradition-based defense of established institutions. This choice gave his argument a universal, philosophical character that distinguished it from mere nostalgia or reaction.

## The Maxim in Context

Acton's original formulation appears in a letter to Bishop Mandell Creighton in 1887: "Power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely. Great men are almost always bad men, even when they exercise influence and not authority." The context was a debate about historical judgment — specifically, whether historians should apply moral standards to popes and monarchs. Acton insisted that they should: no office, however exalted, exempts its holder from moral judgment. This insistence on holding power accountable to universal moral standards resonates with Goldwater's insistence on holding government accountable to constitutional limits.

## By invoking Acton at the outset, Goldwater positions American conservatism within a tradition of skeptical liberalism that sees concentrated power — whether monarchical, bureaucratic, or democratic — as the primary threat to human freedom. Acton's Catholic universalism and Goldwater's American constitutionalism converge on a shared conviction: that no person or institution can be trusted with unlimited authority.
