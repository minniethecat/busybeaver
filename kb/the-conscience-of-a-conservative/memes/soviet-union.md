## Definition

The Soviet Union was the communist superpower that served as the central external antagonist in Goldwater's political worldview. Throughout *The Conscience of a Conservative*, the Soviet Union is depicted not merely as a rival nation but as the institutional embodiment of an ideology fundamentally opposed to American freedom. "The Communists' aim is to conquer the world" (010-007). For Goldwater, the Soviet threat was not hypothetical — it was the defining geopolitical reality of his time.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's analysis of the Soviet Union rests on several premises. First, that Communism is an inherently expansionist ideology: "If an enemy power is bent on conquering you, and proposes to turn all of his resources to that end, he is at war with you; and you — unless you contemplate surrender — are at war with him" (010-008). Second, that the United States had failed to recognize the nature of this conflict, "waging peace" while "the Communists wage war" (010-009).

The Soviet Union's acquisition of nuclear weapons, while alarming, was not the root cause of America's deteriorating position. "The temptation is strong to blame the deterioration of America's fortunes on the Soviet Union's acquisition of nuclear weapons. But this is self-delusion. The rot had set in, the crisis was coming, long before the Soviets developed their own atomic capability" (010-005). The deeper problem was America's failure to pursue a coherent strategy for victory.

## Cold War Strategy

Goldwater devoted Chapter X to critiquing American Cold War policy and proposing an alternative. His critique focused on several failures: the defensive posture of the alliance system, the ineffectiveness of foreign aid, the dangers of summit diplomacy, and the naivety of the cultural exchange program.

On negotiations: "Communists do not look upon negotiations, as we do, as an effort to reach agreements that are mutually beneficial...they look upon them as opportunities to achieve a victory over the West" (010-036). On foreign aid: "Our present Foreign Aid program, in sum, is not only ill-administered, but ill-conceived. It has not, in the majority of cases, made the free world stronger; it has made America weaker" (010-031).

Goldwater proposed an offensive strategy: "Our strategy must be primarily offensive in nature. Given the dynamic, revolutionary character of the enemy's challenge, we cannot win merely by trying to hold our own" (010-082). He called for military superiority, not mere parity (010-083), and advocated supporting "the captive peoples to revolt against their Communist rulers" (010-088).

## The United Nations and the Soviets

Goldwater viewed the United Nations as compromised by Soviet participation. "The United Nations, we must remember, is in part a Communist organization. The Communists always have at least one seat in its major policy-making body, the Security Council" (010-063). He objected to the financial burden the U.N. placed on American taxpayers, likening it to "the Marxist formula, 'from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs'" (010-067).

## Key Arguments

1. **Communism is at war with freedom**: The conflict is not a misunderstanding but a fundamental clash between irreconcilable systems. "Our avowed national objective is 'peace.' We have, with great sincerity, 'waged' peace, while the Communists wage war" (010-009).

2. **The alliance system is inadequate**: NATO, SEATO, and other alliances are "completely defensive in nature and outlook" (010-020) and cannot protect against the most prevalent form of Communist aggression — "political penetration and internal subversion" (010-018).

3. **Negotiations benefit the Soviets**: Summit conferences and diplomatic engagement provide "victory enough" for the Communists even without formal agreements, by granting them legitimacy and propaganda opportunities (010-037).

4. **Foreign aid has failed**: The aid program "has not, in the majority of cases, made the free world stronger; it has made America weaker" (010-031), often funding governments hostile to American interests.

5. **Victory requires offensive capability**: "We must — ourselves — be prepared to undertake military operations against vulnerable Communist regimes" (010-090). Goldwater accepted "the risk of war" as preferable to surrender (010-091).

> The temptation is strong to blame the deterioration of America's fortunes on the Soviet Union's acquisition of nuclear weapons. But this is self-delusion. The rot had set in, the crumbling of our position was already observable, long before the Communists detonated their first Atom Bomb.
>
> (010-005)
