## Definition

"Freedom for Labor" is the title and theme of Chapter VI of *The Conscience of a Conservative*. For Goldwater, the freedom of the individual worker is the central principle that should govern labor policy. This means the right of workers "to form voluntary associations of their own choosing" — and, critically, the right not to be compelled to join a union as a condition of employment.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater approaches labor issues through the lens of individual freedom rather than class interest. He opposes compulsory unionism not because he is "anti-labor" but because compulsory membership violates the same principle of voluntary association that underlies all Conservative political philosophy. The charge that criticizing union power "is to be anti-labor and anti-union" is, in his view, "an argument that serves the interest of union leaders" rather than workers themselves (006-008).

The concentration of economic power in labor unions parallels the earlier concentration of power in industrial monopolies. "Eighty years ago the nation was faced with a comparable concentration of economic power. Large corporations, by gaining monopoly control over entire industries, had nullified the laws of the free market" (006-032). Just as antitrust laws addressed corporate monopoly, Goldwater argues for measures to check union monopoly power — not by government dictate but by restoring voluntary association.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater advocates for right-to-work laws that make union membership voluntary, and for restrictions on the use of union dues for political purposes. He sees the political spending of unions as a serious compromise of American freedom: "They do this by spending the money of union members with whose political views they may disagree" (006-022). His solution is that "financial contributions to political campaigns should be made by individuals and individuals alone" (006-028).

> All of us have heard the charge that to thus criticize the power of Big Labor is to be anti-labor and anti-union. This is an argument that serves the interest of union leaders, but it does not usually fit the facts, and it certainly does not do justice to my views. I believe that unionism, kept within the limits of legitimate voluntary action, is a beneficial force.
>
> (006-008)
