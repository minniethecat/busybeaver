## Definition

The Tenth Amendment to the United States Constitution states: "The powers not
delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the
States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people." Ratified in
1791 as part of the Bill of Rights, it is the constitutional expression of the
principle of enumerated powers: the federal government possesses only those
authorities specifically granted to it; all others remain with the states or
the people.

The Amendment is both a rule of construction (how the Constitution is to be
interpreted) and a substantive reservation of authority (what powers the
federal government may not exercise). For Goldwater, it is the single most
important constitutional provision for preserving limited government and
preventing the accumulation of power in Washington.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater places the Tenth Amendment at the center of his constitutional
argument. It is not a historical curiosity or a legal technicality but a
binding, enforceable limit on federal power that has been systematically
violated by decades of legislative and judicial expansion. "The Tenth
Amendment is not 'a general assumption of power by the states' but a specific
reservation of power to the states and to the people" (003-011). The quotation
marks around "a general assumption" signal Goldwater's rejection of the
prevailing Supreme Court interpretation that had rendered the Amendment
toothless.

For Goldwater, the Tenth Amendment operationalizes the conservative principle
that power must be dispersed and checked. By creating a default rule that
favors state authority in areas where the Constitution is silent, it builds
a structural bias against centralization into the constitutional order. The
Amendment's erosion is, in Goldwater's view, both a symptom and a cause of
the broader expansion of federal power that threatens individual liberty.

## Historical Context

The Tenth Amendment was the last of the Bill of Rights amendments, proposed to
address Anti-Federalist concerns that the Constitution's enumeration of certain
federal powers would be read to imply broader, unstated federal authority. Its
language closely tracks Article II of the Articles of Confederation, which had
reserved to the states "every power, jurisdiction and right" not "expressly
delegated" to the national government. The omission of "expressly" from the
Tenth Amendment became a point of legal contention: did the Amendment reserve
*all* unenumerated powers, or only those not *implied* by the enumerated ones?

By the mid-twentieth century, the Supreme Court had effectively adopted the
latter, narrower reading. In *United States v. Darby* (1941), the Court
declared the Tenth Amendment "but a truism" — a statement of the obvious that
imposed no independent limit on federal power. Goldwater's insistence on the
Amendment's binding force was, in the legal context of 1960, a radical
proposition, challenging three decades of post-New Deal jurisprudence.

## Key Arguments

**The Amendment as Prohibitory Rule.** Goldwater insists that the Tenth
Amendment is "a prohibitory rule of law" (003-011), not a symbolic
acknowledgment. It does not merely describe the constitutional structure; it
*commands* that powers not delegated to the federal government may not be
exercised by it. This reading directly challenges the Supreme Court's
"truism" doctrine and demands that federal actions be justified by specific
constitutional authorization, not merely by congressional preference.

**The Presumption for the States.** The Amendment establishes a default rule:
"Thanks to the Tenth Amendment, this presumption must always be in favor of
the states" (003-010). In any area where federal authority is unclear, the tie
goes to the states. This is the opposite of how modern government operates,
where the federal government is presumed competent unless specifically
prohibited.

**Education as a Tenth Amendment Case Study.** Goldwater uses education to
illustrate the Amendment's application: "No powers regarding education were
given the federal government. Consequently, under the Tenth Amendment,
jurisdiction over the field of education is reserved to the states" (004-009).
This is straightforward constitutional logic: if a power is not enumerated, it
does not belong to the federal government, regardless of how desirable federal
involvement might appear as a policy matter. "The first is that federal
intervention in education is unconstitutional. It is that simple" (009-005).

**Federal Aid as Evasion.** Goldwater argues that federal grants-in-aid
represent an attempt to circumvent the Tenth Amendment through fiscal pressure
rather than direct regulation. When the federal government cannot constitutionally
command the states, it instead offers them money with conditions attached,
achieving through inducement what it cannot achieve through authority. This,
Goldwater contends, is no less a violation of the Amendment's spirit.
