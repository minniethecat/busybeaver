**Eastern Europe**, in the Cold War context discussed by Senator Barry Goldwater in *The Conscience of a Conservative*, referred to the collection of nations under Soviet domination following World War II — the so-called "satellite nations" behind the Iron Curtain.

## Goldwater's Foreign Policy Argument

Goldwater placed Eastern Europe at the center of his critique of American Cold War strategy. He recounted how President Eisenhower told the Soviets he wanted to discuss the status of the satellite nations of Eastern Europe, only to be promptly advised that the Soviet Union did not consider the matter a legitimate subject for negotiations. This exchange, for Goldwater, illustrated the fundamental problem of diplomatic engagement with a power that rejected the very premise of self-determination for the nations it controlled. (010-010)

## The Free Hand Problem

Goldwater argued that as long as the United States gave Soviet military forces "a free hand in Eastern Europe," it was counterproductive to try to bribe Communist governments into becoming American friends through foreign aid. The premise of containment, as he understood it, had conceded Soviet control over the region, and no amount of economic assistance to non-Communist nations elsewhere could compensate for that strategic surrender. (010-010)

## The 1956 Hungarian Revolution

Goldwater cited the 1956 Hungarian Revolution as the most dramatic illustration of the stakes in Eastern Europe. He argued that had the United States possessed the means and the will to support the Hungarian uprising, such a policy "would have saved the Hungarian Revolution." The failure to act, in his view, demonstrated the bankruptcy of a foreign policy that talked about freedom while accepting Soviet hegemony over half of Europe. (010-010)

The "hard counsel" Goldwater offered was that the West should have been prepared to present the Kremlin with an ultimatum during the Hungarian crisis: a major uprising in Eastern Europe, combined with American military readiness, could have forced the Soviets to choose between withdrawal and general war — a choice he believed they would not have risked. This argument was central to his broader case for a more aggressive, liberation-oriented foreign policy. (010-011)
