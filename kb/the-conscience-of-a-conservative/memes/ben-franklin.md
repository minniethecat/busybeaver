**Benjamin Franklin** (1706–1790) was a Founding Father of the United States, a delegate to the Constitutional Convention, and one of the most celebrated figures of the American Enlightenment. Senator Barry Goldwater invoked Franklin in *The Conscience of a Conservative* to frame the central question of American self-government.

## "A Republic, If You Can Keep It"

Goldwater twice quoted Franklin's famous exchange at the close of the Constitutional Convention. When a woman asked Franklin, "What have you given us?", he replied: "A Republic, *if you can keep it*!" (008-008)

For Goldwater, this anecdote captured the fundamental challenge of American constitutional government: the system the Founders designed would prosper only if the governed were sufficiently determined to maintain it. The restraints the Constitution placed on government power required a citizenry willing to enforce them. Goldwater believed that mid-20th-century Americans had failed this test, allowing "the Achesons and Larsons" — his shorthand for the liberal foreign policy and domestic establishment — to dismantle the constitutional system of checks and balances. (008-008)

## Foreign Threats and Republican Survival

Goldwater returned to Franklin in his discussion of foreign policy, noting that American freedom had always depended, to an extent, on what was happening beyond American shores. "Even in Ben Franklin's day," he wrote, "Americans had to reckon with foreign threats." The Founders understood that keeping a republic meant, above all, keeping it safe from foreign transgressors — that a people could not long live free if surrounded by hostile powers. (010-010)

This invocation of Franklin served to connect Goldwater's anti-communist foreign policy to the Founding generation's own understanding of republican survival. The Cold War, in this framing, was not a departure from the concerns of the Founders but their continuation in a new and more dangerous form.

> But the framers were not visionaries. They knew that rules of government, however brilliantly calculated to cope with the imperfect nature of man, however carefully designed to avoid the pitfalls of power, would be no match for men who were determined to disregard them. In the last analysis their system of government would prosper only if the governed were sufficiently determined that it should. “What have you given us?” a woman asked Ben Franklin toward the close of the Constitutional Convention. “A Republic,” he said, “if you can keep it!”
> (002-010)
