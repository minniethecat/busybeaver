## Definition

Federal aid refers to the system of financial transfers — grants-in-aid, subsidies, and conditional funding — from the federal government to state and local governments. Goldwater identifies federal aid as a central mechanism through which the federal government extends its reach into areas the Constitution reserves to the states, arguing that "federal aid *to* education inevitably means federal control *of* education" (009-022).

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, federal aid represents a constitutional violation disguised as fiscal assistance. The power to tax and spend is used to purchase compliance from the states, eroding the federal structure. "The government must begin to *withdraw* from a whole series of programs that are outside its constitutional mandate" (007-019).

The grants-in-aid system is particularly objectionable because it operates by indirection. Federal money is offered to the states "on Washington's terms or not at all," converting the states from independent sovereigns into administrative agents of the federal government. Goldwater observes that "in recent years the federal government has continued, and in many cases has increased, federal grants-in-aid to the States in a number of areas" (003-009), accelerating the centralization of power.

## Federal Aid to Education

Goldwater devotes significant attention to federal aid to education in Chapter IX, articulating four objections. First, "federal intervention in education is unconstitutional" (009-013) — the Constitution grants no authority over education to the federal government. Second, "the alleged need for federal funds has never been convincingly demonstrated" (009-015); a 1955 White House Conference finding that school construction needs could be met without federal aid had "never heard that 1955 finding successfully contradicted" (009-016) through years of congressional hearings.

Third, "federal aid promotes the idea that federal school money is 'free' money, and thus gives the people a distorted picture of the cost of education" (009-020). The reality is that "the federal government has no funds except those it extracts from the taxpayers who reside in the various States" (009-021) — federal aid is simply tax money returned to its source, minus the cost of federal administration.

Fourth, "federal aid *to* education inevitably means federal control *of* education" (009-022). The National Defense Education Act of 1958 demonstrated this principle in practice, as federal dollars came with federal requirements.

## The Hoover Commission Recommendations

Goldwater notes that the Hoover Commission identified approximately $7 billion in annual savings through government reorganization and efficiency — recommendations that were "largely ignored" (007-017) by both political parties. This neglect reflects the political incentive structure created by federal aid: programs persist not because they serve their stated purpose but because they serve the interests of their administrators and beneficiaries.

## Distinction from General Welfare

Federal aid must be distinguished from the "general welfare" clause of the Constitution. Goldwater argues that the general welfare clause was intended as a limitation on federal power — Congress may spend only for the *general* welfare, not for particular interests — not as a grant of unlimited spending authority. Federal aid programs that benefit specific groups or regions violate this principle.

## Federal Aid as Political Dependency

Goldwater saw in the federal aid system more than a constitutional problem — he saw a mechanism of political dependency. By making states reliant on federal transfers, the aid system created a structural incentive for state officials to support further federal expansion. "State and local governments," Goldwater observed, "like individuals, are strongly tempted to spend money that comes from . . . seemingly inexhaustible federal funds" (009-021). The result was a ratchet effect: each grant program created a constituency of beneficiaries who would resist its elimination, and each new program expanded the federal government's effective jurisdiction over matters once controlled locally.

This argument went beyond the legal question of constitutionality to the practical question of governance. Even if federal aid programs were constitutional — which Goldwater denied — they were corrosive to the habits of self-government that a free society required. Citizens who looked to Washington for solutions to local problems ceased to be citizens in the republican sense and became, instead, clients of an administrative state.
