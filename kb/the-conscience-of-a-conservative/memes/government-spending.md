## Overview

Government spending is a central concern in Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative*. He argues that the growth of federal expenditure represents not merely a fiscal problem but a fundamental threat to individual liberty. When government spends beyond its constitutional limits, it necessarily encroaches on the resources and freedoms of citizens.

## The Growth of Federal Spending

Goldwater documents the dramatic expansion of government spending and its corrosive effects. "While there is some dispute about the precise amounts," he writes, the trajectory of spending growth is unmistakable (007-011). During the post-war period, federal expenditure expanded far beyond what could be justified by population growth, inflation, or legitimate constitutional functions.

The question, in Goldwater's view, is not merely technical: "the question of government spending" is fundamentally about the relationship between the citizen and the state (007-011). Every dollar the government spends is a dollar first taken from a citizen — and the decision to spend reflects a judgment that government can use that dollar more wisely than the individual who earned it.

> In a country where it is now generally understood and proclaimed that the federal government spends too much, Congress annually deliberates over whether to raise the federal budget by a few billion dollars or by many billion.
> (P01-005)

## The Failure to Confront Spending

Goldwater is sharply critical of the political class's unwillingness to address government spending honestly. "No one has really faced up to the problem of government spending," he charges (007-018). Politicians express concern about deficits and debt while continuing to appropriate funds at ever-increasing levels. The result is a permanent gap between rhetoric and reality.

He notes that "the government must begin to withdraw from a whole series of programs that are outside its constitutional mandate" (007-019). The solution is not to slow the growth of spending but to reverse it — to eliminate programs that the federal government has no constitutional authority to operate.

> The other, and the one that has the greatest impact on our daily lives, is to reduce the volume of taxes. And this takes us to the question of government spending. While there is something to be said for the proposition that spending will never be reduced so long as there is money in the federal treasury, I believe that as a practical matter spending cuts must come before tax cuts. If we reduce taxes before firm, principled decisions are made about expenditures, we will court deficit spending and the inflationary effects that invariably follow.
> (007-011)

## Spending and Economic Growth

Goldwater addresses the argument that government spending stimulates economic growth. He is skeptical of this claim, arguing that "the need for 'economic growth'" is often invoked to justify spending that primarily serves political rather than economic purposes (007-020). Genuine economic growth comes from private initiative and investment, not from government expenditure that merely redistributes existing resources.

The political dynamic compounds the fiscal problem: "leadership demands that the federal government spend more than it is spending" (007-017). Each new program creates a constituency that will defend it, making spending cuts politically difficult even when they are economically necessary.

## Limited Government and Spending Restraint

Goldwater's solution to excessive government spending is rooted in his broader philosophy of limited government. If the federal government were confined to its constitutional functions — national defense, the administration of justice, the protection of individual rights — spending would naturally contract. The problem, as he sees it, is not that government is inefficient at its proper tasks but that it has assumed tasks far beyond its proper scope.

He recalls that the Constitution was intended to create a limited government: "the Founders promised to restore limited government and then proceeded, after the adoption of the Constitution, to create one" (002-018). The modern federal budget, with its thousands of programs and trillions in expenditure, bears little resemblance to that constitutional vision.

## Taxes, Spending, and Freedom

The connection between government spending and individual freedom is direct, in Goldwater's analysis. High levels of spending require high levels of taxation, and high taxation reduces the resources individuals retain for their own purposes. "There is an interest in reducing taxes and spending" because both represent claims on the earnings of individuals (007-020).

The spending-taxation nexus creates what Goldwater sees as a vicious cycle: spending creates constituencies that demand continued spending, which requires continued taxation, which reduces economic freedom, which creates demands for government intervention, which requires more spending. Breaking this cycle requires not merely fiscal prudence but constitutional discipline.
