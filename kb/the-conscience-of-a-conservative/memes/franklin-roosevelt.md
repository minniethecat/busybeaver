## Biography

Franklin Delano Roosevelt (1882–1945) served as the 32nd President of the United States, the only president elected to four terms. His [[new-deal|New Deal]] programs fundamentally transformed the relationship between the federal government and American society, establishing the [[bureaucracy|administrative state]] that Goldwater's [[conservatism|Conservatism]] opposes. He led the United States through the Great Depression and most of [[world-war-ii|World War II]].

## Role in Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, FDR represents the archetypal departure from constitutional government. "Franklin Roosevelt's rapid conversion from [[constitutionalism|Constitutionalism]] to the doctrine of unlimited government, is an oft-told story" (003-003). This "conversion" — from a governor who had expressed constitutionalist sympathies to a president who dramatically expanded federal authority — marks the pivot point after which "the system of restraints has fallen into disrepair" (002-011).

[[the-new-deal|The New Deal]]'s expansion of federal power into agriculture, labor, welfare, and economic regulation established precedents that subsequent administrations — [[republic|Republic]]an as well as Democratic — would follow and extend. The constitutional reasoning of the [[new-deal|New Deal]], which treated the Commerce Clause and [[general-welfare|General Welfare Clause]] as grants of plenary federal authority, effectively eliminated the doctrine of enumerated powers.

## Key Events

- [[the-new-deal|The New Deal]] (1933–1939): Created the modern [[bureaucracy|administrative state]] through programs like the AAA, NRA, [[social-security-act|Social Security]], and the Wagner Act
- Court-packing plan (1937): Attempted to expand the [[supreme-court|Supreme Court]] to secure favorable rulings on [[new-deal|New Deal]] legislation
- "Conversion from [[constitutionalism|Constitutionalism]]": "abandonment of [[states-rights|States' Rights]] by the Roosevelt Administration" (003-003)
- Agriculture Adjustment Act: "The sponsors of the first Agriculture Adjustment Act, for instance, defended it on the ground that it promoted the general welfare" (005-010)


> Franklin Roosevelt's rapid conversion from Constitutionalism to the doctrine of unlimited government, is an oft-told story. But I am here concerned not so much by the abandonment of States' Rights by the national Democratic Party — an event that occurred some years ago when that party was captured by the Socialist ideologues in and about the labor movement — as by the unmistakable tendency of the Republican Party to adopt the same course.
>
> (003-003)
