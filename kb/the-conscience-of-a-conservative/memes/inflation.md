# Inflation

Inflation appears in Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative* primarily as a consequence of irresponsible government fiscal policy and concentrated economic power, rather than as an independent economic phenomenon to be managed by monetary authorities.

## Inflation and Union Power

Goldwater connects inflationary pressure to the exercise of union power in wage negotiations. He cites the steel industry controversy as a case in point:

> When the strike began, neutral observers — including government economists normally friendly to the unions — agreed that the Steel Workers' wage demands were exorbitant and would inevitably cause further inflation; and that the steel companies were quite right in insisting that certain changes in work rules were necessary to halt the upward spiral of costs. (006-029)

In this analysis, inflation is not simply a monetary phenomenon but a consequence of concentrated economic power that allows one party to extract terms beyond what competitive markets would produce.

## Deficit Spending as a Cause

Goldwater identifies deficit spending as a primary driver of inflation through government fiscal policy:

> If we reduce taxes before firm, principled decisions are made about expenditures, we will court deficit spending and the inflationary effects that invariably follow. (007-011)

This reflects Goldwater's fiscal conservatism: he insists that spending cuts must precede tax cuts, precisely because the alternative — cutting taxes while maintaining spending levels — leads to deficits and inflation. The sequence matters: discipline on the spending side must come first.

## The Scale of Government Growth

Goldwater uses inflation as a metric to demonstrate that government growth cannot be explained away by standard economic justifications:

> During the ten-year period in which federal spending will have increased by 143%, our population will have increased by roughly 18%. Nor does inflation account for the difference. In the past ten years the value of the dollar has decreased less than 20%. (007-016)

This passage is significant for what it rules out: neither population growth (18%) nor inflation (less than 20%) comes close to explaining the 143% increase in federal spending. The residual — the bulk of the increase — represents a genuine expansion of government beyond any demographic or monetary justification.

## Inflation and Economic Freedom

Goldwater's treatment of inflation fits within his broader concern for [[economic-freedom]]. Inflation erodes the value of savings, distorts economic calculation, and disproportionately harms those on fixed incomes. More fundamentally, in Goldwater's framework, inflation is a symptom of deeper problems: a government that spends beyond its means, economic actors (particularly unions) that wield excessive power, and a political class unwilling to impose fiscal discipline.
