Robert A. Taft was a United States Senator from Ohio and a prominent leader of the conservative wing of the [[republic|Republic]]an Party in the mid-20th century. Known as "Mr. Republican," Taft was a principled opponent of the [[new-deal|New Deal]]'s expansion of federal power and a staunch advocate of fiscal conservatism.

Goldwater invokes Taft as an authority on the relationship between high taxation and government spending. As Goldwater writes, "the late Senator Taft made the point often. 'You can't get socialized medicine,' he would say, 'without first getting socialized taxes'" (007-005). Taft's argument was that [[graduated-tax|progressive taxation]] laid the groundwork for government overreach into sectors like healthcare, and that the tax code itself was the primary engine of [[big-government|federal expansion]].

Taft also played a key role in the 1952 [[republic|Republic]]an presidential nomination contest. Goldwater recounts a pivotal moment at the Morningside Heights Conference in New York, where after a meeting between Taft and Dwight [[dwight-eisenhower|Eisenhower]]'s representatives, "Senator Taft announced: '[[dwight-eisenhower|General Eisenhower]] has assured me that he is a [[republic|Republic]]an and that he believes in limited government and a strong national defense'" (007-013). This meeting was part of Taft's effort to ensure that the [[republic|Republic]]an nominee, whoever it was, would adhere to conservative principles of limited government.

Beyond the 1952 nomination contest, Taft's broader influence on Goldwater's conservative philosophy is evident in the fiscal arguments Goldwater advances throughout *The Conscience of a Conservative*. Taft's principle — that government expansion in one domain inevitably creates the preconditions for expansion in others — became a recurring theme in Goldwater's analysis. The relationship Taft identified between progressive taxation and socialized medicine was, for Goldwater, a specific instance of a general law: each expansion of federal power normalizes and enables the next. "You can't get socialized medicine without first getting socialized taxes" was not merely a quip about healthcare policy; it was a structural insight about how big government grows — not through grand declarations but through the incremental linkage of one program to another, each new initiative building on the fiscal and administrative machinery of its predecessors.

Taft's legacy in Goldwater's narrative is that of a principled conservative who understood that fiscal discipline and limited government were inseparable. His warnings about the relationship between taxation and government expansion served as a foundation for Goldwater's own arguments about the size and scope of the federal government.

> In the Summer of 1952, shortly after the Republican Convention, the two men who had battled for the Presidential nomination met at Morningside Heights, New York, to discuss the problem of taxes and spending. After the conference, Senator Taft announced: "General Eisenhower emphatically agrees with me in opposing any tax reduction until the budget is balanced."
