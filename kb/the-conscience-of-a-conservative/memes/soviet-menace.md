## Definition

The "Soviet Menace" is Goldwater's term for the total threat posed by the Soviet Union — military, ideological, and political — to the United States and the free world. It encompasses the Soviet nuclear arsenal, conventional military forces, global subversion network, and communist ideology. Chapter X of *The Conscience of a Conservative* is devoted entirely to this subject, reflecting Goldwater's conviction that the Cold War was not a geopolitical rivalry to be managed but a civilizational struggle to be won.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's analysis of the Soviet menace represents the strongest expression of his foreign policy conservatism. He rejects the premise — accepted by both Democratic liberals and Eisenhower's "Modern Republicans" — that peaceful coexistence with the Soviet Union is possible or desirable. The Soviet regime, in his view, is inherently expansionist: its ideology demands global victory, and its promises of peaceful coexistence are tactical deceptions.

This position places Goldwater firmly in the tradition of anti-communist conservatism that saw the Cold War as a moral crusade. The Soviet menace is not merely a military problem but a spiritual one: it tests whether free men have the will to defend their freedom. Goldwater's prescription — unambiguous military superiority, support for anti-communist allies, and a foreign policy aimed at victory rather than stalemate — flows directly from this diagnosis.

## Key Arguments

**The Soviet Union seeks global victory, not coexistence** ((010-002), (010-004), (010-005)). Goldwater argues that Soviet professions of peaceful intent are tactical; the regime's ideology commits it to the destruction of free societies. Negotiation without strength is not diplomacy but appeasement.

**Nuclear parity is a trap** ((010-005), (010-011), (010-019)). As the Soviet Union acquires nuclear capabilities equal to America's, the strategic balance shifts from deterrence to blackmail. Goldwater warns that parity invites Soviet aggression by removing the credible threat of American nuclear response.

**The Soviet threat is ideological as well as military** ((010-010), (010-029)). Communism offers an alternative model of social organization that appeals to developing nations. Goldwater observes that many countries are "far more inclined to the Soviet-type society than to a free one" — meaning military containment must be paired with an assertive ideological offensive promoting freedom.

**Soviet diplomacy is inherently untrustworthy** ((010-034), (010-037), (010-038), (010-039), (010-042)). Goldwater catalogs Soviet treaty violations, diplomatic deceptions, and the asymmetry of Cold War engagement — the Soviet Union is always advancing while the West is always reacting. The history of Soviet agreements proves they "will not keep any agreement unless it is in their interest to do so."

**Victory is possible and necessary** ((010-010)). Goldwater's most controversial claim: the goal of American policy should not be to stabilize the Cold War but to win it — to achieve "a peace in which Soviet power will no longer be in a position to threaten the security of free nations."

## Historical Context

The Soviet menace dominated American foreign policy from the late 1940s through the 1980s. When Goldwater wrote in 1960, the Soviet Union had recently launched Sputnik (1957), crushed the Hungarian uprising (1956), and was threatening West Berlin. The U-2 incident (May 1960) had just scuttled a US-Soviet summit. Goldwater's hawkish stance reflected widespread anxiety about a "missile gap" and perceived Soviet momentum — though he was more willing than most to state the logic of confrontation explicitly.
