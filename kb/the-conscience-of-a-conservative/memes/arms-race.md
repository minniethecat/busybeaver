## Overview

An arms race occurs when two or more states engage in competitive military expansion, each responding to the other's buildup with escalatory investments of its own. The Cold War nuclear arms race between the United States and the Soviet Union — involving the development, production, and deployment of ever more powerful nuclear weapons and delivery systems — was the central strategic reality of the postwar era.

## The Cold War Nuclear Arms Race

### Origins

The nuclear arms race began with the US atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki (1945) and accelerated when the Soviet Union tested its own atomic bomb in 1949. Each side's weapons program drove the other's, creating a spiral of escalation.

### Key Milestones

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1945 | US develops atomic bomb | Nuclear age begins |
| 1949 | USSR tests atomic bomb | US nuclear monopoly ends |
| 1952 | US tests hydrogen bomb | Thermonuclear era |
| 1957 | Sputnik launch | USSR demonstrates ICBM capability |
| 1962 | Cuban Missile Crisis | Brink of nuclear war |

## Goldwater's Position

Barry Goldwater devoted the culminating chapter of *The Conscience of a Conservative* to the Soviet threat, arguing that the United States could become "slaves" by "losing the Cold War to the Soviet Union" (001-010). He contended that "American freedom has always depended on American military strength" and that the US must pursue a policy of unambiguous military superiority rather than parity or détente.

> And still the awful truth remains: We can establish the domestic conditions for maximizing freedom, along the lines I have indicated, and yet become slaves. We can do this by losing the Cold War to the Soviet Union.
> (010-002)

Goldwater's position reflected a broader conservative conviction that:
- Negotiated arms control was unreliable
- Soviet intentions were fundamentally hostile and expansionist
- American military superiority was the only reliable guarantor of freedom

## Criticisms of the Arms Race

Critics argued that the arms race:
- Diverted resources from domestic priorities
- Increased the risk of accidental nuclear war
- Created a permanent military-industrial complex
- Made genuine disarmament politically impossible
