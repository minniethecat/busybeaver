## Definition

Socialism, as Barry Goldwater uses the term, refers to a system of political economy in which the state assumes ownership or directive control over the means of production and distribution of goods. Goldwater draws a crucial distinction between classical Socialism — achieved through outright nationalization of industries — and modern "Socialism-through-Welfarism," which accomplishes the same end through taxation, regulation, and the expansion of government welfare programs. In both forms, the defining characteristic is the subordination of individual liberty to collective material goals.

Goldwater's definition is intentionally broad, encompassing not only self-identified socialists but also the welfare-state liberalism of the New Deal tradition. He argues that the ultimate goal is identical — "to subordinate the individual to the State" — even when the means differ (008-001). This expansive definition is central to his argument that American politics has drifted toward socialism without acknowledging it.

## In Conservative Thought

Socialism functions in Goldwater's thought as the definitive "other" against which Conservatism defines itself. The opening chapter establishes this opposition explicitly: "it is Socialism that subordinates all other considerations to man's material well-being. It is Conservatism that puts material things in their proper place" (001-001). This is not merely a policy disagreement but a fundamental clash of worldviews — one that sees man primarily as an economic being whose needs are met by the state, the other that sees man as a spiritual and moral being whose freedom must be protected from the state.

Goldwater's analysis of Socialism is notably strategic. He recognizes that overt nationalization proved politically untenable in democratic societies, leading socialists to adopt welfare-state mechanisms as a more subtle path to the same destination. This insight — "Socialism can be achieved through Welfarism quite as well as through Nationalization" (008-001) — is one of his most influential contributions to conservative thought. It reframes the American welfare state not as a moderate compromise between capitalism and socialism but as socialism by another name.

A key implication of Goldwater's analysis is that the Republican Party itself has been partially captured by socialist assumptions. He notes that the Democratic Party was "captured by the Socialist ideologues in and about the labor movement" and warns of "the unmistakable tendency of the Republican Party to adopt the same course" (003-001). This willingness to criticize his own party distinguishes Goldwater's conservatism from mere partisanship.

## Historical Context

Modern socialism emerged in the nineteenth century as a response to the inequalities of industrial capitalism, most influentially in the writings of Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels. By the early twentieth century, Socialist parties had gained significant strength in Europe, and socialist ideas influenced the Progressive movement in the United States. The Bolshevik Revolution of 1917 established the first explicitly socialist state, and the Great Depression of the 1930s gave impetus to state-led economic management worldwide.

In the American context, Franklin Roosevelt's New Deal marked a decisive shift toward government intervention in the economy. While Roosevelt's programs stopped short of nationalization, they established the principle that the federal government bore responsibility for the economic welfare of its citizens — a principle Goldwater viewed as socialist in substance if not in name. By the 1950s, when Goldwater wrote, the welfare state had become bipartisan consensus, with both parties accepting Social Security, federal aid to education, and agricultural subsidies as permanent features of American governance.

Goldwater wrote in the context of the Cold War, when the threat of Soviet Communism made the question of socialism acutely relevant. He argued that the distinction between Communism and democratic socialism was tactical rather than principled — both sought the expansion of state power, albeit at different speeds and by different means.

## Key Arguments

**Socialism subordinates everything to economics.** Goldwater's foundational critique is that Socialism is a form of materialism that reduces human beings to their economic functions. While critics accuse Conservatism of being "an economic theory," Goldwater counters that "the shoe is precisely on the other foot: it is Socialism that subordinates all other considerations to man's material well-being" (001-001). Conservatism, by contrast, "has a structured view of the human being and of human society, in which economics plays only a subsidiary role" (001-001).

**"Socialism-through-Welfarism."** Goldwater's most original contribution to the analysis of socialism is his concept of "Socialism-through-Welfarism." He argues that when outright nationalization proved politically impossible, collectivists adapted their strategy:

> The collectivists have not abandoned their ultimate goal — to subordinate the individual to the State — but their strategy has changed. They have learned that Socialism can be achieved through Welfarism quite as well as through Nationalization. (008-001)

This strategy is more dangerous than classical socialism precisely "because it is more difficult to combat. The evils of Nationalization are self-evident and immediate. Those of Welfarism are veiled and tend to be postponed" (008-001). The welfare state creates dependency slowly, and by the time its victims recognize their bondage, "it is too late to unlock the jail" (008-001).

**State Socialism in labor relations.** Goldwater identifies two paths by which socialism encroaches on labor relations. The first is government-enforced compulsory arbitration, which "simply transfers economic power from the unions to the government, and encourages State Socialism" (006-001). The second is the concentration of union power itself — when "a union has the power to enforce uniform conditions of employment throughout the nation its power is comparable to that of a Socialist government" (006-001).

**Unions as a bulwark against socialism.** Goldwater makes the counterintuitive argument that free trade unions, properly constrained, are actually a defense against socialism. Unions "are an alternative to, and thus discourage State Socialism" because they provide workers with a non-state mechanism for advancing their economic interests (006-001). This distinguishes Goldwater from simpler anti-union conservatives: his objection is not to collective bargaining per se but to the concentration of coercive power, whether in government or in labor monopolies.

**The bankruptcy of doctrinaire Marxism.** Goldwater observes, with some satisfaction, that classical Marxist doctrine has lost credibility even among European socialist parties. He notes "the bankruptcy of doctrinaire Marxism has been expressly acknowledged by the Socialist Party of West Germany, and by the dominant faction of the Socialist Party of Great Britain" (008-001). In the United States, the Socialist Party's "negligible strength" confirms that Americans have not been persuaded by overt socialism. The danger, however, is that socialism has simply changed its packaging.

**Foreign aid strengthens socialism abroad.** Goldwater extends his critique to American foreign policy, arguing that government-to-government aid to neutral countries "strengthens Socialism in those countries" by reinforcing state-controlled economies at the expense of free enterprise (010-001). This is a consistent application of his domestic analysis: wherever government expands, freedom contracts, regardless of the stated intentions.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Conservatism | Socialism |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **View of man** | Spiritual and moral being; economics is subsidiary | Material being; economic welfare is primary |
| **Role of state** | Limited to essential functions (justice, defense, order) | Manager of economic life and provider of welfare |
| **Property** | Private property as natural right | Property subject to collective claims |
| **Taxation** | Proportional, for revenue only | Progressive, for redistribution |
| **Freedom** | Freedom from state coercion | Freedom from economic want (via state provision) |
| **Strategy** | Constitutional limits on government power | Gradual expansion of government functions |

The distinction between "Socialism-through-Nationalization" and "Socialism-through-Welfarism" is a difference of method, not of ends. Goldwater insists that both forms share the same ultimate objective: the subordination of the individual to the state.
