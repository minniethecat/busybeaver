The **Hoover Commission** (formally the Commission on Organization of the Executive Branch of the Government) was a federal body chaired by former President Herbert Hoover, tasked with recommending reforms to improve the efficiency and reduce the cost of the federal government. In *The Conscience of a Conservative* (1960), Senator Barry Goldwater cited the Hoover Commission as evidence that neither political party was serious about reducing government spending.

## Goldwater's Citation

In his chapter on taxes and spending, Goldwater pointed to the Hoover Commission's unheeded recommendations as a case study in Washington's fiscal irresponsibility. He wrote: "The recommendations of the Hoover Commission which could save the taxpayer in the neighborhood of $7 billion a year have been largely ignored" (007-006).

## The Deeper Problem

Goldwater used the Hoover Commission example to make a larger point about the nature of government spending. Even the Commission's worthwhile recommendations, he noted, "dealing as they do for the most part with extravagance and waste, do not go to the heart of the problem" (007-006). The root issue, Goldwater argued, was not inefficiency but that "the government is engaged in activities in which it has no legitimate business" (007-006).

According to Goldwater, as long as the federal government claimed jurisdiction over social and economic fields reserved to the States by the Constitution — education, welfare, public housing, agriculture — spending could not be substantially reduced: "The only way to curtail spending substantially, is to eliminate the programs on which excess spending is consumed" (007-006).
