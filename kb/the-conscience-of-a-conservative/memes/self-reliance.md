## Definition

Self-reliance is the principle that individuals bear primary responsibility for their own welfare, development, and choices. Goldwater presents it as both a description of how freedom works and a prescription for how individuals should live: "the people's welfare depends on individual self reliance rather than on state paternalism" (P01-004).

## In Conservative Thought

Self-reliance is the human face of the Conservative conception of freedom. If "man's development, in both its spiritual and material aspects, is not something that can be directed by outside forces" and "every man is responsible for his own development" (001-009), then self-reliance is the necessary posture of a free person.

The Welfare State attacks self-reliance directly: "Consider the consequences to the recipient of welfarism. For one thing, he mortgages himself to the federal government. In return for benefits — which, in the majority of cases, he pays for — he concedes to government the right to make decisions that affect his life" (008-018). Self-reliance and state dependency are mutually exclusive.

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's case combines moral psychology and political philosophy. Morally, self-reliance cultivates the virtues that dependency destroys. Politically, self-reliant citizens are the only reliable defenders of freedom. He finds evidence in ordinary Americans: "Most farmers want to stand on their own feet. They are prepared to take their chances in the free market" (005-018).
