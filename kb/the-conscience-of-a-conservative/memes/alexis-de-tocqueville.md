## Biography

Alexis de Tocqueville (1805–1859) was a French diplomat, political thinker, and historian best known for *[[democracy|Democracy]] in America* (1835–1840), a study of American society and political institutions based on his travels through the young republic in the 1830s. His observations on American democracy, civil society, and the dangers of centralized power have made him one of the most cited political commentators in American history.

## Role in Conservative Thought

Goldwater invokes Tocqueville with deep respect, calling him "probably the most clairvoyant political observer of modern times" (002-019). Tocqueville "saw the danger when he visited this country in the 1830s. Even then he foresaw decay for a society whose government assumed responsibilities that belonged to the governed" (002-019).

This reference serves Goldwater's argument that the threat of centralized government is not a partisan invention but a danger recognized by the most perceptive observers of democracy. Tocqueville's warning — that a government that assumes too many responsibilities will eventually erode the self-reliance and freedom of its citizens — is precisely the [[conservatism|Conservative]] thesis.

> The Frenchman, Alexis de Tocqueville, probably the most clairvoyant political observer of modern times, saw the danger when he visited this country in the 1830s. Even then he foresaw decay for a society that tended to put more emphasis on its democracy than on its republicanism. He predicted that America would produce, not tyrants but "guardians."
> (002-019)

## Key Ideas

Tocqueville's insight that [[democracy|democratic]] societies are particularly vulnerable to "soft despotism" — a form of centralized government that provides for people's needs while gradually extinguishing their liberty — anticipates Goldwater's critique of the [[welfare-state|Welfare State]]. The collectivization "proceeds by degrees, and each step is justified by a persuasive humanitarian motive" (008-010) — exactly the dynamic Tocqueville described.
