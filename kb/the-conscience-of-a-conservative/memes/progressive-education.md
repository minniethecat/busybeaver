## Overview

Progressive education is the pedagogical movement associated with John Dewey
and his followers, which came to dominate American public education in the
mid-20th century. It emphasizes "learning by doing," student-centered methods,
and the social role of schools as laboratories for democratic life and social
change.

## In Goldwater's Argument

Goldwater devotes much of Chapter 9 ("Some Notes on Education") to a critique
of progressive education and its consequences. He identifies it as the
philosophical root of what he sees as the decline of American schooling.

"In varying degrees we have adopted what has been called 'progressive education'"
(009-016). For Goldwater, this adoption has produced multiple harmful effects:

> Subscribing to the egalitarian notion that every child must have the same education, we have neglected to provide an educational system which will tax the talents and stir the ambitions of our best students and which will thus insure us the kind of leaders we will need in the future.
> (009-017)

**Egalitarian leveling.** "Subscribing to the egalitarian notion that every
child must have the same education, we have neglected to provide an educational
system which will tax the talents and stir the ambitions of our best students"
(009-017). Goldwater argues that this approach deprives the nation of future
leaders by failing to challenge the most capable students.

**Emphasis on adjustment over mastery.** "In our desire to make sure that our
children learn to 'adjust' to their environment, we have given them
insufficient opportunity to acquire the knowledge that will enable them to
master their environment" (009-018).

**Neglect of academic rigor.** "In our attempt to make education 'fun,' we
have neglected the academic disciplines that develop sound minds and are
conducive to sound characters" (009-019).

**Method over content.** "Responding to the Deweyite attack on methods of
teaching, we have encouraged the teaching profession to be more concerned
with how a subject is taught than with what is taught" (009-020).

**Schools as social engineering.** "Most important of all: in our anxiety to
'improve' the world and insure 'progress' we have permitted our schools to
become laboratories for social and economic change according to the
predilections of the professional educators" (009-020).
