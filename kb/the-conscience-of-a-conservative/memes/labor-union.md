# Labor Union

Labor unions, as discussed in Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative*, represent a major concentration of economic and political power that threatens individual freedom when unions exceed their proper bounds.

## Goldwater's View of Union Power

Goldwater identifies the enormous power concentrated in union leaders' hands as a grave threat to American freedom. He distinguishes between legitimate unionism and the excessive power that federal labor legislation has enabled:

> Graft and corruption are symptoms of the illness that besets the labor movement, not the cause of it. The cause is the enormous economic and political power now concentrated in the hands of union leaders. (006-004)

He argues that federal labor laws have fundamentally shifted the balance of power:

> Thanks to some unwise provisions and to the absence of others that should have been included, the delicate balance of power we sought to achieve between labor and management has shifted, in avalanche proportions, to labor's advantage. Or, more correctly to the advantage of union leaders. (006-012)

This concentration of power, Goldwater contends, is as dangerous as the corporate monopolies of the late nineteenth century.

## Compulsory Unionism and Freedom of Association

A central concern in Goldwater's analysis is compulsory union membership, which he sees as a violation of the fundamental right of free association:

> What could be more fundamental than the freedom to associate with other men, or not to associate, as each man's conscience and reason dictates? Yet compulsory unionism is the rule rather than the exception today in the ranks of organized labor. (006-015)

He notes that millions of workers are required to join unions as a condition of employment through union shop agreements, which he argues deny workers the freedom to decide for themselves. Goldwater supports right-to-work laws as a remedy, citing evidence that union membership increased in states that enacted such legislation (006-019).

## Union Political Spending

Goldwater devotes significant attention to the political activities of labor unions, which he views as a serious compromise of American freedom:

> It is in the second of these areas, that of political contributions, that labor unions seriously compromise American freedom. They do this by spending the money of union members without prior consultation for purposes the individual members may or may not approve of. (006-022)

He specifically criticizes the AFL-CIO's Committee on Political Education (COPE) and argues that union members' dues are used for political purposes without their consent. Goldwater's proposed solution is to restrict political contributions to individuals alone:

> In order to achieve the widest possible distribution of political power, financial contributions to political campaigns should be made by individuals and individuals alone. I see no reason for labor unions—or corporations—to participate in politics. (006-028)

## The Antitrust Argument

Goldwater draws a parallel between union power and corporate monopoly power, arguing for consistent treatment under antitrust law:

> Eighty years ago the nation was faced with a comparable concentration of economic power. Large corporations, by gaining monopoly control over entire industries, had nullified the laws of competition that are conducive to freedom. We responded to that challenge by outlawing monopolies through the Sherman Act and other antitrust legislation. These laws, however, have never been applied to labor unions. (006-032)

He questions why antitrust laws that prevent corporations from dictating prices throughout an industry should not also apply to unions that exercise comparable power over wages and working conditions.

## Legislative History

Goldwater discusses the Kennedy-Ervin Labor Reform Bill of 1959, which he alone voted against in the Senate (90–1). He considered this vote the most important of his Senate career, opposing the bill because it pretended to respond to demands for safeguards against union power without actually doing so. He viewed the subsequent Landrum-Griffin Bill as an improvement but still insufficient to address the root cause of union power (006-002, 006-004).

## Economic Impact

The economic consequences of union power extend beyond labor-management relations. Goldwater notes that the wage earner is "at the mercy of national union leaders whose great power is a direct consequence of federal labor legislation" (002-016). He cites the steel industry controversy as an example of labor's ability to impose its will regardless of the cost to society, with wage demands that neutral observers agreed would inevitably cause further inflation (006-029).
