## Definition

Compulsory unionism is the practice of requiring workers to join or financially support a labor union as a condition of employment. It takes two principal forms: the "closed shop" (where only union members may be hired) and the "union shop" (where workers must join the union within a specified period after being hired). Goldwater identifies compulsory unionism as a fundamental violation of individual freedom: "What could be more fundamental than the freedom to associate with other men, or not to associate, as each man's conscience and reason dictates? Yet compulsory unionism is the rule rather than the exception today in the ranks of organized labor" (006-017).

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater, compulsory unionism is objectionable on three grounds. First, it violates the principle of free association — a worker should be able to choose whether to join a union just as he chooses whether to join a church, a political party, or any other voluntary organization. "Millions of laboring men are required to join the union" whether they wish to or not (006-017).

Second, it compels financial support for political causes the worker may oppose. "Is it morally permissible to take the money of a Republican union member, for example, and spend it on behalf of a Democrat? The travesty is deeper, of course, when the money takes the form of compulsory union dues" (006-020). Under union shop conditions, a worker who disagrees with the union's political activities has no recourse — he must pay or lose his job.

Third, it is unnecessary for the health of the labor movement. Goldwater observes that "in all of the nineteen States which have enacted right-to-work laws union membership increased after the right-to-work laws were passed" and that "the union movement throughout the world has prospered when it has been put on a voluntary basis" (006-019).

## The Free-Loader Argument

Goldwater directly addresses the union defense of compulsory membership: "The contention is that a man ought not to enjoy the benefits of an organization's activities unless he contributes his fair share of their cost. I am unaware, however, of any other organization or institution that seeks to enforce this theory by compulsion." He notes that "the Red Cross benefits all of us, directly or indirectly, but no one suggests that Red Cross donations be compulsory" (006-019).

## The UEW Case

Goldwater illustrates the human cost of compulsory unionism with the case of a Pennsylvania worker who "had been a union member in good standing for over twenty years" but refused to join the United Electrical Workers when it became the bargaining agent "on the grounds the UEW was Communist dominated — a judgment that had been made by the CIO itself when it expelled the UEW in 1950" (006-017). Under compulsory unionism, this worker faced loss of his livelihood for refusing to associate with an organization his conscience rejected.

## Compulsory Arbitration

Goldwater extends the principle to compulsory arbitration in labor disputes, arguing that government-imposed settlement "simply transfers economic power from the unions to the government, and encourages State Socialism" (006-021). The alternative is to "disperse union power and thus extend freedom in labor-management relations" (006-021).

## Right-to-Work and Economic Freedom

Goldwater saw the right-to-work movement as a direct extension of the free association principle. He noted with approval that "in all of the nineteen States which have enacted right-to-work laws union membership increased after the right-to-work laws were passed" and that "the union movement throughout the world has prospered when it has been put on a voluntary basis" (006-019). This empirical observation was crucial to Goldwater's argument: compulsory unionism was not only morally objectionable but unnecessary for the health of organized labor. The claim that unions required compulsory membership to survive was, in Goldwater's view, contradicted by the evidence.

The policy implication Goldwater drew was clear: "abolish, not regulate" (006-021). Rather than attempting to regulate union political spending or mandate internal democracy, the simpler and more principled solution was to make union membership entirely voluntary. A union dependent on voluntary support would, by the logic of the market, be responsive to its members' interests in a way that a union backed by the coercive power of the state could never be.
