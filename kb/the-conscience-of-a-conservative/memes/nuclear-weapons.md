## Overview

Nuclear weapons occupy a critical position in Barry Goldwater's strategic thinking in *The Conscience of a Conservative*. Writing during the Cold War, he analyzes the implications of nuclear parity, critiques arms control proposals, and argues that American nuclear superiority is essential for national security and the defense of freedom.

## The Strategic Landscape

Goldwater describes the transformed strategic environment following the Soviet Union's acquisition of nuclear capability. The United States began the Cold War "with a monopoly of atomic weapons, and with a conventional military establishment that was the most powerful on earth" (010-003). This monopoly provided a decisive strategic advantage, but Goldwater warns that believing the post-monopoly world is merely a continuation of the old one is "self-delusion" (010-005).

The price of avoiding nuclear war, Goldwater acknowledges, has been high: "the price we had to pay to avoid nuclear destruction" has shaped American policy in ways that have constrained freedom of action (010-004). The nuclear age imposes costs that go far beyond military budgets.

> The temptation is strong to blame the deterioration of America's fortunes on the Soviet Union's acquisition of nuclear weapons. But this is self-delusion. The rot had set in, the crumbling of our position was already observable, long before the Communists detonated their first Atom Bomb. Even in the early 1950s, when America still held unquestioned nuclear superiority, it was clear that we were losing the Cold War. Time and again in my campaign speeches of 1952 I warned my fellow Arizonans that "American Foreign Policy has brought us from a position of undisputed power, in seven short years, to the brink of possible disaster." And in the succeeding seven years, that trend, because its cause remains, has continued.
>
> (010-005)

## Nuclear Superiority as Strategy

Goldwater's central argument is that nuclear superiority — not mere parity — must be the goal of American policy. He describes how "our nuclear superiority has been the alliance's shield against aggression" throughout the Cold War (010-019). This superiority deters Soviet adventurism and provides the security foundation for the entire Western alliance.

He advocates for maintaining "an absolute monopoly in nuclear power" as the ideal, even while recognizing that this advantage has been lost (010-055). The strategic objective should be to regain superiority "in all of the weapons — military, as well as political — that are needed to achieve victory" (010-059).

## The Psychology of Parity

Goldwater is particularly concerned about the psychological dimension of nuclear parity. "To impending physical parity in nuclear weapons must be added a psychological factor of uncertainty," he writes (010-056). When adversaries believe they are approaching equality — or superiority — they become more willing to take risks. American resolve, meanwhile, is undermined by the perception of vulnerability.

This psychological factor affects both deterrence and negotiation. An America that projects confidence in its nuclear superiority deters aggression; an America that appears to accept parity invites challenges.

## Opposition to the Nuclear Test Ban

Goldwater is sharply critical of proposals for "a permanent ban on the testing of nuclear weapons" (010-057). He sees such agreements as freezing American nuclear technology at a level of parity with the Soviet Union, preventing the United States from developing new capabilities that could restore strategic advantage.

The "inclination to accept an agreement that would freeze our nuclear technology" reflects, in Goldwater's view, a deeper problem: the willingness to trade strategic advantage for the appearance of progress in arms control (010-057). He argues that the Soviet Union will exploit such agreements while continuing to pursue military advantage through other means.

## Disarmament Skepticism

Goldwater extends his skepticism to broader disarmament proposals. He questions whether "mutual disarmament in certain weapons" can be achieved "in the knowledge that their security would not be impaired" (010-054). The verification problem — knowing whether an adversary is actually complying — makes disarmament agreements inherently risky.

His position is not that arms control is always undesirable, but that it must be approached from a position of strength and with clear-eyed assessment of Soviet intentions. Agreements that constrain American power without reliably constraining Soviet power are worse than no agreement at all.

## Nuclear Weapons and Limited War

Goldwater considers the possibility of nuclear weapons designed for limited conflicts: "Assume we have developed nuclear weapons that can be used in limited warfare — weapons that are 'clean' and that can be employed with precision" (010-090). Such developments would change the strategic calculus, making nuclear options more flexible and potentially more usable.

The prospect of limited nuclear war does not, in Goldwater's view, make nuclear conflict more likely. Rather, it strengthens deterrence by ensuring that the United States has options between surrender and all-out nuclear exchange.
