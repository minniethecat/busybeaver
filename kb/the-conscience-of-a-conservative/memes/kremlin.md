## Definition

The Kremlin is the fortified complex in Moscow that serves as the seat of Russian and Soviet political power. In Goldwater's usage, "the Kremlin" functions as a metonym for the Soviet leadership — the men and apparatus that make strategic decisions about Communist expansion, military policy, and diplomacy. Goldwater consistently refers to "the Kremlin" rather than "the Soviet Union" when discussing the adversary's strategic calculations, emphasizing the centralized, calculating nature of Soviet power.

## In Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater's use of "the Kremlin" reflects his view of the Cold War as a contest of wills between a small, calculating Communist leadership and the free world. He writes that "U.S.-Soviet power may be in balance; but if we, and not they, rule out the possibility of using that power, the Kremlin can create crisis after crisis, and force the U.S., because of our greater fear of war, to back down every time" (010-011). The asymmetry is psychological: American fear of war gives the Kremlin a strategic advantage that raw power calculations do not justify.

## The Kremlin's Strategic Knowledge

Goldwater argues that the Kremlin understands America better than America understands the Kremlin: "All of the evidence is that the men in the Kremlin have a greater knowledge of America than many of our own leaders" (010-045). Soviet personnel sent to the United States are "to a man, trained agents of Soviet policy" (010-047) whose mission is to "persuade the American people to forget the ugly aspects of Soviet life" (010-047).

## Tolerating Communism

Goldwater identifies a crucial strategic objective of the Kremlin: "the Kremlin's aim is not to make Americans approve of Communism, much as they would like that; it is to make us tolerant of Communism" (010-048). Tolerant Americans are less willing to "make sacrifices to halt Communist expansion" (010-048). This insight shapes Goldwater's insistence that American foreign policy must maintain a clear moral opposition to Communism.

## The Kremlin and International Institutions

The Kremlin uses international institutions for strategic advantage. Goldwater notes that "the Soviet Union's permanent veto power" in the UN Security Council "allows the Kremlin to block any action, on a substantial issue, that is contrary to its interests" (010-063). This observation supports Goldwater's broader critique of entrusting American security to multilateral institutions.

## The Kremlin and Satellite States

Goldwater analyzes the Kremlin's relationship with satellite states in terms of permissible deviation: "The Kremlin may, for its own purposes, permit certain 'liberalization' tendencies in satellite countries; it may even permit small deviations from the approved Soviet foreign policy line" (010-074). However, "the moment a Communist government threatens to become a non-Communist one, or threatens to align itself with the West against the Soviet Union, the Kremlin will take steps to bring the defecting government into line" (010-074). The 1956 Hungarian Revolution exemplifies the consequences of crossing this line.

## Deterrence Logic

In Goldwater's view, the only language the Kremlin respects is credible military force: "we ought to present the Kremlin with an ultimatum forbidding Soviet intervention, and be prepared, if the ultimatum is rejected, to move a highly mobile task force" (010-090). The Kremlin "would also be put on notice, of course, that resort to long-range bombers and missiles would prompt automatic retaliation in kind" (010-090). This is the logic of deterrence applied to the defense of freedom globally.
