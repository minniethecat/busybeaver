The **Fourteenth Amendment** to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1868, was one of the three Reconstruction Amendments. Its key clauses — equal protection, due process, and privileges or immunities — have made it one of the most frequently litigated constitutional provisions in American history.

## The Amendment's Original Purpose

The amendment was designed to legitimize the Civil Rights Act of 1866. Its legislative history makes clear that people of all races were to be equally entitled to certain legal privileges. However, as Senator Barry Goldwater argued in *The Conscience of a Conservative*, the amendment's original scope was narrower than its later judicial interpretation.

The Fifteenth Amendment provides that no one shall be denied the franchise on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude. Similarly, the Fourteenth Amendment enforced certain legal privileges for all races. (004-005)

## Goldwater's Constitutional Analysis

Goldwater devoted significant attention to the Fourteenth Amendment in his discussion of civil rights and the proper limits of federal power. He argued that jurisdiction over education was reserved to the States under the Tenth Amendment. The question, as he framed it, was whether the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause modified the original constitutional prohibition against federal intervention in education. (004-005)

Goldwater pointed out that it had never been seriously argued — and the argument was not made by the Supreme Court — that the authors of the Fourteenth Amendment intended to alter the Constitutional scheme with regard to education. Even in the landmark school integration decision, *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954), the Supreme Court justices expressly acknowledged this limitation. (004-005)

## Evidence of Original Intent

Goldwater presented several historical facts about the amendment's ratification:

1. During the entire congressional debate on the Fourteenth Amendment, it was never once suggested by any proponent that it would outlaw segregated schools.
2. At the same time Congress approved the Fourteenth Amendment, it established schools in Washington, D.C., "for the sole use of… colored children."
3. In all the debates on the amendment by the State Legislatures, only one legislator — a man in Indiana — expressed the view that the amendment banned segregated schools. (004-006)

Goldwater concluded that the amendment was not intended to outlaw racially separate schools, nor did it authorize any federal intervention in education. The facts, he maintained, were well documented and constituted all one needed to know about the amendment's bearing on the segregation question. (006-007)

## Broader Constitutional Significance

The Fourteenth Amendment represents, in Goldwater's framework, a critical test case for constitutional interpretation. His analysis challenged the Supreme Court's application of the equal protection clause to strike down state laws, arguing that the Court had engrafted its own views onto the established law of the land rather than adhering to the amendment's original meaning. (005-006)

This argument exemplified Goldwater's broader conservative philosophy: that the Constitution means what its authors intended it to mean, and that judicial reinterpretation — however well-intentioned — undermines the rule of law and the separation of powers.

> To my knowledge it has never been seriously argued — the argument certainly was not made by the Supreme Court — that the authors of the Fourteenth Amendment intended to alter the Constitutional scheme with regard to education. Indeed, in the famous school integration decision, Brown v. Board of Education, the Supreme Court justices expressly denied that their opinion was based on this meaning.
