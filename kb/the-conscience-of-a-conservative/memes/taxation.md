## Definition

Taxation is the power of government to claim a portion of individual earnings to fund its operations. Goldwater accepts its necessity — "every citizen has an obligation to contribute his fair share to the legitimate functions of government" (007-006) — but insists the taxing power is bounded by constitutional and natural-law limits.

## In Conservative Thought

The Conservative approach to taxation begins with the premise that "government does not have an unlimited claim on the earnings of individuals" (007-004). If property rights are inherent in human nature, taxation can only be justified to the extent it serves "the legitimate functions of government."

Goldwater is particularly critical of progressive taxation: "The graduated tax is a confiscatory tax. Its effect, and to a large extent its aim, is to bring down all men to a common level" (007-009). He notes that "many of the leading proponents of the graduated tax frankly admit that their purpose is to redistribute the nation's wealth" (007-009). For the Conservative, redistribution is not a legitimate function of government.

## Historical Context

The modern federal tax system is largely a twentieth-century creation. The Sixteenth Amendment (1913) authorized the income tax, and the New Deal era transformed it into an instrument of social policy. Goldwater observes that "taxes, visible and invisible, take approximately twenty percent of the taxpayers' earnings" from even modest-income families (007-005).

> Here is an indication of how taxation currently infringes on our freedom. A family man earning $4,500 a year works, on the average, twenty-two days a month. Taxes, visible and invisible, take approximately 32% of his earnings. This means that one-third, or seven whole days, of his monthly labor goes for taxes. The average American is therefore working one-third of the time for government: a third of what he produces is not available for his own use but is confiscated and used by others who have not earned it. Let us note that by this measure the United States is already one-third “socialized.” The late Senator Taft made the point often. “You can socialize,” he said, “just as well by a steady increase in the burden of taxation beyond the 30% we have already reached as you can by government seizure. The very imposition of heavy taxes is a limit on a man’s freedom.”
> (007-005)

## Key Arguments

Goldwater's argument has three elements. First, taxation must be limited to legitimate government functions. Second, taxation must be proportional, not progressive: "government has a right to claim a share of a person's earnings which is consistent with fairness" (007-008), and fairness means equal treatment. Third, using the tax power for redistribution is unconstitutional and immoral.
