## Overview

The Cold War is the central geopolitical fact of Goldwater's world — the ongoing ideological, political, and military struggle between the United States and the Soviet Union that shapes every foreign policy decision. Goldwater devotes Chapter X, "The Soviet Menace," entirely to this subject, arguing that "American freedom has always depended, to an extent, on what is happening beyond our shores" (010-003).

## Goldwater's Analysis

Goldwater's Cold War analysis is stark and uncompromising. He argues that the West operates at an inherent disadvantage because it treats negotiations as good-faith exercises while the Communists treat them as tactical operations (010-037). "The central strategic fact of the Cold War, as it is presently fought, is that the Communists can gain from negotiations without agreements" (010-038).

His critique extends to summit diplomacy, cultural exchanges, and foreign aid — all of which, in his view, serve Soviet interests more than American ones. The problem is not merely tactical but philosophical: Americans "fail to grasp how evil the Soviet system really is" (010-044), treating Communism as a competing political system rather than a fundamentally immoral ideology.

## Key Policy Recommendations

- Reject summit diplomacy that legitimizes Communist regimes
- End foreign aid programs that subsidize neutral and hostile nations
- Acknowledge the moral dimension of the Cold War as a conflict between freedom and tyranny
- Pursue military and strategic superiority rather than parity


> We can establish the domestic conditions for maximizing freedom, along the lines I have indicated, and yet become slaves. We can do this by losing the Cold War to the Soviet Union.
>
> (010-002)
