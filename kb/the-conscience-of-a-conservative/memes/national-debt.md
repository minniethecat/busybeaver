## Overview

The national debt represents, for Barry Goldwater, not merely a fiscal problem but a symptom of deeper constitutional and moral failures. In *The Conscience of a Conservative*, he links the growth of federal debt to the expansion of government power beyond constitutional limits and warns of its implications for individual liberty and national solvency.

## The Scale of Government Expansion

Goldwater frames the national debt as a consequence of government's transformation from a limited constitutional entity into an all-encompassing "Leviathan." He asks: "How did it happen? How did our national government grow from a servant of the people into a master that regulates, taxes, and spends without apparent limit?" (002-017). The national debt is the ledger of this transformation — a record of government living beyond its constitutional means.

The federal government, Goldwater observes, has become involved in activities far beyond its original purpose: "banker, mortgage broker, employer, debtor, taxer and spender in all manner of enterprises" (002-014). Each expansion of function brings new spending obligations, and each new obligation contributes to the mounting debt.

## Interest and the Debt Burden

Goldwater notes with concern the growing burden of debt service: federal budgets must "include interest payments on the national debt" as a non-negotiable line item (011-002). These interest payments represent resources extracted from taxpayers not for current services but for past excesses — a burden passed from one generation to the next.

## Centralization and Fiscal Power

The national debt is intertwined with Goldwater's broader critique of centralized power. He observes that "national leaders conclude that the States are unable to cope with a problem, and therefore the federal government must act" (003-004). Each such determination expands federal responsibility, federal spending, and ultimately federal borrowing.

When "there is a need for national action," Goldwater notes that "if the States agree to act in concert, the need can be met without expanding federal power" (003-005). But the political incentive is to centralize both action and expenditure, adding to the debt rather than solving problems at the appropriate level of government.

## Conservatism and Fiscal Discipline

For Goldwater, fiscal discipline is not merely an accounting preference but a moral imperative. Conservatism, in his view, "holds the key to national salvation" [P01-009] precisely because it insists on limiting government to its constitutional functions — and therefore limiting its spending and debt.

The political dynamic that drives debt is self-reinforcing: "Congress annually deliberates over how to spend billions, having proclaimed that the federal government spends too much" [P01-005]. The cycle of profligate spending followed by expressions of concern, followed by more spending, is what Goldwater sees as the fundamental failure of modern governance.

## The Welfare State Connection

A significant driver of federal debt has been the expansion of welfare programs. Goldwater critiques the narrow terms of the political debate: the argument is "over whether the increase in government welfarism should be small or large" [P01-004] rather than whether the programs should exist at all. Each expansion of welfare creates permanent spending commitments that contribute to structural deficits and growing debt.
