Henry Agard Wallace (1888–1965) was an American politician who served as Secretary of Agriculture (1933–1940), Vice President under Franklin D. Roosevelt (1941–1945), and Secretary of Commerce (1945–1946). A leading New Deal liberal and agricultural reformer, he later broke with the Democratic Party to run as the Progressive Party candidate for president in 1948.

Goldwater mentions Wallace in the context of agricultural policy, specifically the farm subsidies he sees as counterproductive government intervention:

> In recent years, the government has sought to alleviate the problem of overproduction by the soil bank and acreage retirement programs. Actually, these programs are simply a modern version of the hog-killing and potato-burning schemes promoted by Henry Wallace during the New Deal. And they have been no more successful in reducing surpluses than their predecessors. (006-007)

For Goldwater, Wallace is emblematic of a policy tradition that attempts to solve economic problems through government management rather than market mechanisms. By invoking Wallace's legacy, Goldwater draws a direct line from New Deal agricultural intervention to the farm programs of the 1950s, arguing that both fail on their own terms while also violating conservative principles of limited government and economic freedom.

## Agricultural Policy and the Free Market

Goldwater's critique of Wallace's agricultural policies rests on a broader free-market argument. He contends that farm production, like any other production, is best controlled by the natural operation of supply and demand. If farmers are permitted to sell their produce freely at prices consumers are willing to pay, they will, under the law of supply and demand, end up producing roughly what can be consumed. If some farmers find they are not getting high enough prices, they will move into other kinds of economic activity — reducing agricultural production and raising incomes for those who remain. (006-006)

Government intervention, Goldwater argues, disrupts this natural process. When the government pegs prices higher than the consumer is willing to pay, the result is chronic overproduction and exorbitant costs — the very problems that acreage retirement and soil bank programs were designed, unsuccessfully, to address. For Goldwater, there is also a "positive evil" in these programs: they effectively reward people for *not producing*, an absurd and self-defeating policy for a nation concerned with economic growth. (006-007)

## Legacy in Conservative Thought

Wallace serves, in Goldwater's narrative, as a historical shorthand for the entire New Deal approach to agricultural policy — an approach that Goldwater sees as unconstitutional (since no power over agriculture was granted to the federal government), economically irrational, and corrosive to individual freedom. The line from Wallace's hog-killing schemes to the soil bank programs of the 1950s is, for Goldwater, evidence that government intervention is self-perpetuating: each failed program begets another, rather than prompting a return to free-market principles. This critique reflects the broader conservative argument that centralized economic planning, whatever its intentions, inevitably produces worse outcomes than the decentralized decisions of free individuals. (007-012)
