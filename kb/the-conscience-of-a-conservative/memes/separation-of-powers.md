## Definition

The separation of powers, as embedded in the United States Constitution, divides the authority of the federal government among three separate and co-equal branches — legislative, executive, and judicial — so that each branch, "jealous of its own prerogatives, would have a natural incentive to resist aggression by the others" (002-008). This structural division is not an end in itself but a means to prevent the accumulation of power in a single authority, which the framers understood from "vivid, personal experience" to be the prerequisite of tyranny (002-008).

Goldwater describes the Constitution as "a system of restraints against the natural tendency of government to expand in the direction of absolutism" (002-008). The separation of powers is the third of four components in this system, after the limitation of federal authority to specific delegated powers and the reservation of all other powers to the States and the people, and before the prohibition against impetuous constitutional alteration through Article V's "tortuous, but wise, amendment procedures" (002-008).

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's treatment of the separation of powers is embedded in his broader argument about the decay of constitutional government. His analysis contains several key themes:

**1. The Framers' Intentional Design.** The framers were "not only students of history, but victims of it" (002-008). Having experienced the concentration of power under British rule, they designed a government in which power is deliberately fragmented. The separation of powers was not an abstract philosophical preference but a hard-won lesson from lived experience. The framers understood that "freedom depends on effective restraints against the accumulation of power in a single authority" (002-008).

**2. A System in Disrepair.** Goldwater laments that "the system of restraints has fallen into disrepair" (002-011). The federal government has expanded beyond its delegated powers, the States have been reduced to administrative subunits, and the separation of powers has been weakened by the growth of the administrative state and the abandonment of constitutional boundaries by both political parties.

**3. Democracy vs. Republicanism.** The framers did not create a pure democracy. Goldwater emphasizes that "the system of restraints, on the face of it, was directed not only against individual tyrants, but also against a tyranny of the masses" (002-009). The framers feared that "self-seeking demagogues" might "persuade a majority of the people to confer on government vast powers in return for deceptive promises of economic gain" (002-009). The separation of powers was designed to prevent such a transfer by dispersing authority so that no single branch or faction could dominate.

**4. The Connection to Federalism.** The separation of powers at the federal level is complemented by the vertical division of power between the federal government and the States. Together, these horizontal and vertical restraints form a comprehensive system of limited government. Goldwater sees the weakening of both dimensions as mutually reinforcing: as federal power expands, the separation of powers at the federal level becomes increasingly nominal, because the administrative agencies that exercise much of modern federal power combine legislative, executive, and judicial functions in a single body.

## Mentions in the Text

Key passages where Goldwater articulates the principle of divided power:

> "And that is what the Constitution is: a system of restraints against the natural tendency of government to expand in the direction of absolutism." (002-008)

> "The third is a careful division of the federal government's power among three separate branches." (002-008)

> "Was it then a Democracy the framers created? Hardly. The system of restraints, on the face of it, was directed not only against individual tyrants, but also against a tyranny of the masses." (002-009)

> "The system of restraints has fallen into disrepair." (002-011)
