Sam J. Ervin Jr. was a United States Senator from North Carolina, serving from 1954 to 1974. A conservative Democrat, Ervin is best remembered for his later role as chairman of the [[us-congress|Senate]] Watergate Committee, but during the period of Goldwater's book he was known for his involvement in labor reform legislation.

Goldwater references Ervin in the context of the 1959 debate over labor union regulation. The Kennedy-Ervin "Labor Reform" Bill was one of several legislative proposals aimed at addressing union corruption following the McClellan Committee's investigations.

> If I had to select the vote I regard as the most important of my Senate career it would be the one I cast on the Kennedy-Ervin "Labor Reform" Bill of 1959. The Senate passed the measure 90–1; the dissenting vote was mine. (006-002)

Goldwater describes his lone opposition as a matter of conviction: the measure had been advertised as a cure-all for the evils uncovered by the McClellan Committee investigation, but Goldwater believed it was a piece of legislation that pretended to respond to popular demands for safeguards against union power without actually doing so. He feared that such a bill, by creating the appearance of reform, would preclude the possibility of meaningful legislation for some time to come. (006-002)

Goldwater opposed the Kennedy-Ervin bill, favoring instead the more stringent Landrum-Griffin measure. He notes that the [[us-congress|House of Representatives]] "rejected Kennedy-Ervin, and substituted in its place a much better measure, the Landrum-Griffin bill" (006-003). For Goldwater, the Kennedy-Ervin approach was insufficient to address what he saw as the excessive power of labor unions, particularly in their ability to compel membership and dues from workers. The legislative contest between Kennedy-Ervin and Landrum-Griffin illustrates, in Goldwater's narrative, the distinction between a fundamentally conservative approach to labor regulation and one that merely tinkered at the margins of union power.
