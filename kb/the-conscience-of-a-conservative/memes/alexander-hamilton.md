## Biography

Alexander Hamilton (1755–1804) was one of the most influential Founding Fathers
of the United States. Born in the West Indies, he rose from humble origins to
become George Washington's aide-de-camp during the Revolutionary War, a leading
advocate for the ratification of [[us-constitution|the Constitution]], and the nation's first
Secretary of the Treasury. His economic vision established the foundations of
American finance: a national bank, assumption of state debts, and a system of
tariffs and excise taxes that gave the young federal government fiscal
credibility.

Hamilton was the principal author of the [[federalist-papers|Federalist Papers]],
writing 51 of the 85 essays that defended the proposed [[us-constitution|Constitution]]. His
contributions—especially Federalist No. 17, No. 78 on judicial review, and
No. 84 on the Bill of Rights—remain central texts of American constitutional
thought. Unlike many of his contemporaries who feared centralized power,
Hamilton argued that a strong federal government was compatible with liberty,
provided its powers were enumerated and checked.

His political rivalry with [[thomas-jefferson|Thomas Jefferson]] gave rise to
America's first party system: Hamilton's Federalists versus [[thomas-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s
Democratic-[[republic|Republic]]ans. Hamilton was killed in a duel with Aaron Burr in 1804,
at the age of 47.

## Role in American Politics

### Federalist No. 17 and the Limits of Federal Power

Hamilton's Federalist No. 17 occupies a special place in Goldwater's argument because it demonstrates that even the most nationally-oriented Founder recognized constitutional boundaries on federal authority. Hamilton distinguished between powers that "are proper to be provided for by local legislation" and those requiring national coordination, arguing that the former "can never be desirable cares of a general jurisdiction." This distinction—between appropriate national power and inappropriate federal intrusion—is precisely the line Goldwater believed mid-century American government had erased. By citing Hamilton rather than a more predictable anti-Federalist source, Goldwater strengthens his claim that limited government is a constitutional principle, not a partisan preference.

Hamilton's political philosophy centered on the belief that a vigorous national
government was essential to the survival and prosperity of the American republic.
In his reports as Treasury Secretary, he argued for an expansive interpretation
of federal power under the Necessary and Proper Clause. He envisioned the United
States as a commercial and manufacturing power, in contrast to [[thomas-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s
agrarian ideal.

Yet Hamilton was no advocate of unlimited government. In the [[federalist-papers|Federalist Papers]],
he carefully distinguished between powers appropriately exercised at the
national level—defense, foreign commerce, coinage—and those better left to the
states. This distinction is precisely what [[barry-goldwater|Barry Goldwater]]
seizes upon in [[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]],
where Hamilton is cited as a prophetic voice warning against federal intrusion
into local economic regulation.

Hamilton's influence extended beyond economics. His concept of [[judicial-review|judicial review]],
articulated in Federalist No. 78, was adopted by Chief [[justice|Justice]] John Marshall in
Marbury v. Madison (1803) and remains a cornerstone of American constitutional
law. His argument that the judiciary must be independent and empowered to strike
down unconstitutional legislation shaped the role of the [[supreme-court|Supreme Court]]
for over two centuries.

## Relation to Conservatism

Goldwater invokes Hamilton as an authority in the chapter "[[freedom|Freedom]] for the
Farmer" to illustrate a core conservative principle: that [[us-constitution|the Constitution]]'s
allocation of powers between federal and state governments was designed to
protect individual [[freedom|freedom]], not merely to organize administrative
convenience. Hamilton's argument in Federalist No. 17—that federal intrusion
into "agriculture and other concerns of a similar nature" would be both
"troublesome" and "nugatory"—is presented as evidence that even the most
energetic Federalist among the Founders recognized limits on federal power.

The book draws a direct line from Hamilton's constitutional vision to the
twentieth-century debates over farm subsidies and price controls. Hamilton is
portrayed not as a defender of the welfare state, but as a constitutional
thinker who understood that the enumeration of federal powers was meant to
exclude federal management of purely local economic activities. This reading
places Hamilton within the tradition of [[limited-government|limited government]]
and [[constitutionalism|constitutionalism]] that Goldwater champions.

Goldwater's use of Hamilton also reflects a broader conservative strategy: to
claim the Founding Fathers as allies in the argument against mid-twentieth-century
liberalism. By citing Hamilton alongside other Founders, the book argues that
opposition to expansive [[regulation|federal regulation]] is not a departure from American
political tradition but its fulfillment.

## Mentions in the Text

Alexander Hamilton appears in Chapter V, "[[freedom|Freedom]] for the Farmer," where
Goldwater quotes Federalist No. 17 at length (005-003):

> "… supervision of agriculture and other concerns of a similar nature … which
> are proper to be provided for by local legislation, can never be desirable
> cares of a general jurisdiction. It is therefore improbable that there should
> exist a disposition in the federal councils to usurp the powers with which
> they are connected; because the attempt to exercise those powers would be as
> troublesome as they were nugatory."
>
> Alexander Hamilton, Federalist Papers, No. 17 (005-003)

Goldwater then reflects on the accuracy of Hamilton's prediction, noting that
while Hamilton was wrong about what men *would* do, he was prescient about the
consequences (005-008):

> Hamilton was wrong in his prediction as to what men would do, but quite right
> in foreseeing the consequences of their foolhardiness. Federal intervention in
> agriculture has, indeed, proved "troublesome." Disregard of the Constitution
> in this field has brought about the inevitable loss of personal freedom; and
> it has created economic chaos. (005-008)

The chapter concludes by returning to Hamilton's phrase "troublesome" to
characterize the entire enterprise of government price-fixing and agricultural
subsidies (005-014):

> If, however, the government interferes with this natural economic process, and
> pegs prices higher than the consumer is willing to pay, the result will be, in
> Hamilton's phrase, "troublesome." The nation will pay exorbitant prices for
> work that is not needed and for produce that cannot be consumed. (005-014)
