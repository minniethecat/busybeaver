# Regulation

Government regulation of economic activity is treated in Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative* as both a constitutional violation and a practical impediment to individual freedom and economic prosperity.

## Regulation as Government Interference

Goldwater identifies government regulation as part of a broader pattern of federal intrusion into daily life:

> The businessman is hampered by a maze of government regulations, and often by direct government competition. (002-016)

He places regulation alongside other forms of government overreach — agricultural controls, labor legislation, and Social Security taxes — as manifestations of the same impulse toward centralized control that erodes individual liberty.

## Constitutional Basis

Goldwater grounds his critique of regulation in the Constitution's enumeration of federal powers. He argues that many regulatory programs lack legitimate constitutional authority and have been justified through strained interpretations:

> The teaching of the Constitution on this matter is perfectly clear. No power over agriculture was given to any branch of the national government. (005-010)

He discusses how the first Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1933 was struck down by the Supreme Court, which ruled that the "general welfare" clause was simply a qualification of the taxing power and did not grant Congress the power to control economic activity (005-010).

## The Commerce Clause Distortion

A key element of Goldwater's critique concerns the misuse of the commerce clause to justify federal regulation:

> The New Deal Congress replied by enacting substantially identical legislation, the second A.A.A., and now sought to justify the program as a "regulation of interstate commerce." This was a transparent evasion of the Butler case. (005-011)

He views the Supreme Court's eventual acceptance of this justification — after coming under heavy political fire for having thwarted the "Roosevelt Revolution" — as a pivotal moment in the expansion of federal regulatory power. Goldwater argues that "the federal government has usurped many powers under the guise of 'regulating commerce'" (005-011), describing this as perhaps the most egregious instance of distorting the Constitution's plain meaning.

## Economic Consequences

Regulation, in Goldwater's framework, imposes tangible costs on economic activity. The "maze of government regulations" hampers business activity and creates inefficiencies that ripple through the broader economy. When combined with direct government competition in certain sectors, regulation creates an uneven playing field that disadvantages private enterprise and concentrates economic decision-making in Washington.

## Relation to Free Enterprise

Goldwater's opposition to regulation stems from his commitment to [[free-enterprise]] as an economic system. Regulation substitutes the judgment of government officials for the decentralized decisions of millions of individuals operating through market mechanisms. This substitution is objectionable on both practical grounds — government officials lack the information and incentives to make efficient economic decisions — and philosophical grounds — it replaces voluntary cooperation with coercion.
