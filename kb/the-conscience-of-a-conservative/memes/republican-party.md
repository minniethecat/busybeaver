The **Republican Party** (also known as the GOP, or Grand Old Party) is one of the two major political parties in the United States. In *The Conscience of a Conservative* (1960), Senator Barry Goldwater offered a sharp critique of the Republican Party from within its ranks, arguing that it had abandoned the principles of limited government and States' Rights.

## Goldwater's Critique

Goldwater's central charge was that the Republican Party had drifted toward the same expansive view of federal power long associated with the Democratic Party (003-003). He wrote that the Democratic Party had abandoned States' Rights "some years ago when that party was captured by the Socialist ideologues in and about the labor movement," and that the Republican Party was following "the unmistakable tendency ... to adopt the same course" (003-003). The result, Goldwater argued, was that "today *neither* of our two parties maintains a meaningful commitment to the principle of States' Rights" (003-003).

## Lip-Service to States' Rights

Goldwater acknowledged that the Republican Party gave rhetorical support to States' Rights, but insisted that deeds, not words, were what counted: "The Republican Party, to be sure, gives lip-service to States' Rights. ... But deeds are what count, and I regret to say that in actual practice, the Republican Party, like the Democratic Party, summons the coercive power of the federal government whenever national leaders conclude that the States are not performing satisfactorily" (003-004).

## Failure on Government Spending

A key area where Goldwater found the Republican Party wanting was fiscal policy. After seven years of a Republican administration under President Dwight Eisenhower, Goldwater concluded: "It is in the area of spending that the Republican Party's performance, in its seven years of power, has been most disappointing" (007-012). Despite campaign promises to drastically reduce the federal budget, spending continued to grow.

### The Scale of the Spending Increase

Goldwater presented detailed evidence that Republican governance had failed to arrest the growth of federal spending. During the ten-year period from approximately 1950 to 1960, federal spending increased by 143 percent, a figure Goldwater found indefensible (007-016). He examined and rejected several possible justifications. Population increase did not explain the growth: "During the ten-year period in which federal spending will have increased by 143%, our population will have increased by roughly 18%" (007-016). Similarly, inflation could not account for the difference, as "in the past ten years the value of the dollar has decreased less than 20%" (007-016). Nor could the increase in Gross National Product, which rose roughly 40 percent over the same period, adequately explain a 143 percent rise in federal spending (007-016).

Goldwater's conclusion was blunt: "far from arresting federal spending and the trend toward Statism we Republicans have kept the trend moving forward" (007-016).

### Comparison with the Democrats

Goldwater was careful not to suggest that a Democratic administration would have performed better. He acknowledged that "every year the Democratic national leadership demands that the federal government spend more than it is spending, and that Republicans propose to spend" (007-017). He cited the Democratic National Advisory Council's 1961 budget manifesto, which he described as "calling for profligate spending increases in nearly every department of the federal government," noting that "the demands for increases in domestic spending alone could hardly cost less than $20 billion a year" (007-017). Goldwater's point was that both parties were fiscally irresponsible; his critique of the Republicans was especially sharp because the party professed conservative principles it failed to honor in office.

## Union Dues and Political Power

Goldwater also criticized the relationship between labor unions and the political parties. He noted that union leaders could spend member dues on political campaigns without polling the membership: "the union's decision whether to support candidate X or candidate Y — whether to help the Republican Party or the Democratic Party — is not reached by a poll of the union membership" (006-025). This concentration of political spending power was, in Goldwater's view, unjust.

## The "Modern Republicanism" Debate

Goldwater's critique of the Republican Party was part of a broader intra-party debate about the direction of modern conservatism. He identified the book *A Republican Looks at His Party* by [[arthur-larson|Arthur Larson]] as "an elaborate rationalization of the 'Modern Republicanism' that has set the Republican Party on the road to merely belated Democratic triumphs" (007-007). Larson, a former Eisenhower Administration official, argued that the Republican Party should accept the basic premises of the New Deal welfare state while claiming to administer it more efficiently than the Democrats. Goldwater regarded this position not as a moderate compromise but as a complete surrender of conservative principles.

For Goldwater, the Larsonite argument was fundamentally dishonest. "The Republican Party," he wrote, "has *either* a responsibility to the principles it professes *or* it has none. If it has, it must stand by them in office; if it has not, let it say so and stop confusing the electorate, to say nothing of itself" (007-008). Goldwater believed that Republican voters deserved a clear choice, not a diluted version of the Democratic platform. The "Modern Republicanism" that Larson championed was, in Goldwater's view, precisely what had reduced the two-party system to an echo chamber of expanding government, leaving citizens with no genuine conservative option at the ballot box.
