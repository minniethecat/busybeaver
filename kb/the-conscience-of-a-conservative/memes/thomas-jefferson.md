## Overview

Thomas Jefferson was a statesman, diplomat, architect, and political philosopher who served as the third President of the United States (1801–1809). As the principal author of the Declaration of Independence (1776), he articulated the foundational American principle that "all men are created equal" and endowed with "unalienable Rights" to "Life, [[liberty|Liberty]] and the pursuit of Happiness." His vision of a republic of independent citizens, limited federal government, and strict constitutional construction has had an enduring influence on American conservative thought.

## Political Philosophy

### Limited Government

Jefferson championed a narrow interpretation of federal powers under [[us-constitution|the Constitution]]. He opposed the expansive federal authority advocated by [[alexander-hamilton|Alexander Hamilton]] and the Federalists, arguing that centralized power threatened individual liberty and self-government.

### States' Rights

Jefferson's Kentucky Resolutions (1798) asserted that the states retained sovereignty under [[us-constitution|the Constitution]] and could judge the constitutionality of federal laws. This states'-rights tradition directly informed Goldwater's argument in *[[the-conscience-of-a-conservative|The Conscience of a Conservative]]* that federal overreach violated the constitutional compact (001-003).

### Agrarianism and Independence

Jefferson believed that a republic of independent farmers was the surest foundation of liberty. He wrote that "those who labor in the earth are the chosen people of God" and warned that urbanization and industrialization threatened civic virtue.

## Presidency (1801–1809)

As president, Jefferson reduced [[government-spending|federal spending]], paid down the national debt, and repealed internal taxes. The Louisiana Purchase (1803) doubled the territory of the United States. He also confronted the Barbary pirates, establishing the precedent of using naval power to protect American commerce.

## Jeffersonian Tradition and Modern Conservatism

Goldwater's conservative philosophy draws heavily on the Jeffersonian tradition. Both emphasize:

- **Limited government**: Federal power confined to enumerated constitutional functions
- **States' rights**: Decentralization as a structural check on tyranny
- **Fiscal restraint**: Balanced budgets and minimal taxation
- **Individual liberty**: [[freedom|Freedom]] as the paramount political value

Goldwater saw himself as restoring the Jeffersonian tradition against the centralizing legacy of the [[new-deal|New Deal]] (001-007).

## Legacy

Jefferson died on July 4, 1826, the fiftieth anniversary of the Declaration of Independence. His political thought remains a touchstone for American conservatism and libertarianism. The tension between Jeffersonian limited-government principles and the expansive federal state continues to define American political debate.
