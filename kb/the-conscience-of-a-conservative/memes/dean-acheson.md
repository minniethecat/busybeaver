## Biography

Dean Gooderham Acheson (1893–1971) served as U.S. Secretary of State under [[harry-truman|President Truman]] from 1949 to 1953, shaping [[foreign-policy|Cold War foreign policy]] including the NATO alliance and the Marshall Plan. He later wrote *A Democrat Looks at His Party* (1955), a book Goldwater cites extensively as evidence of the liberal abandonment of constitutional limits.

## Role in Conservative Thought

Goldwater uses Acheson as a primary exhibit of the bipartisan rejection of limited government. In *A Democrat Looks at His Party*, Acheson "wrote approvingly" that "the [[new-deal|New Deal]] conceived of the federal government as the whole people organized to do what had to be done" (002-002). For Goldwater, this statement — from a leading Democratic intellectual — is "an unqualified repudiation of the principle of limited government" with "no reference by either of them to [[us-constitution|the Constitution]]" (002-003).

Together with [[arthur-larson|Arthur Larson]]'s *A [[republic|Republic]]an Looks at His Party*, Acheson's book demonstrates that "prominent spokesmen of both political parties" have abandoned constitutionalism. "The Achesons and Larsons have had their way. The system of restraints has fallen into disrepair" (002-011).

## Key Ideas

Acheson's conception of the federal government as "the whole people organized to do what had to be done" (002-002) represents, for Goldwater, the logical endpoint of unlimited government: a state that recognizes no constitutional boundaries, justified by its claimed benevolence. This vision "is in direct conflict with [[us-constitution|the Constitution]] which is an instrument, above all, for limiting the functions of government" (002-004).
