**Americans for Democratic Action** (ADA) is an American liberal political advocacy organization founded in 1947. In *The Conscience of a Conservative* (1960), Senator Barry Goldwater cited the ADA as an example of a left-wing organization that had abandoned traditional Marxist nationalization in favor of a Welfare State approach to expanding government power.

## Goldwater's Reference

In his discussion of the evolution of collectivist strategy in America, Goldwater noted the decline of classical Marxist approaches. He observed that "the bankruptcy of doctrinaire Marxism has been expressly acknowledged by the Socialist Party of West Germany, and by the dominant faction of the Socialist Party of Great Britain" (008-006). In the United States, he argued, the same shift was evident: "In this country the abandonment of the Marxist approach (outside the Communist Party, of course) is attested to by the negligible strength of the Socialist Party, and more tellingly perhaps, by the content of left wing literature and by the programs of left wing political organizations such as the Americans For Democratic Action" (008-006).

## Welfarism as Strategic Shift

Goldwater's mention of the ADA was part of a larger argument about how collectivists had changed tactics. Rather than pursuing outright nationalization of industry — which had proven politically unpopular — left-wing organizations were now advancing their goals through the expansion of government welfare programs. Goldwater warned that "Socialism-through-Welfarism poses a far greater danger to freedom than Socialism-through-Nationalization precisely because it is more difficult to combat" (008-010).

The ADA exemplified, in Goldwater's view, a political strategy that sought to expand the scope of federal government not through overt seizure of private enterprise, but through the gradual accumulation of federal programs justified by humanitarian appeals. This strategy, he argued, made it "hard ... to make out a case for State ownership" while making it "very different with the rhetoric of humanitarianism" (008-006).

### Why Welfarism Is More Dangerous

Goldwater elaborated on why the welfare approach was more insidious than outright nationalization. The penalties of nationalization, he argued, are "immediate and palpable" — everyone can see when the government takes over a steel mill. By contrast, "those of Welfarism are veiled and tend to be postponed" (008-010). The damage to freedom, he warned, would be felt only later: "after its beneficiaries have become its victims, after dependence on government has turned into bondage and it is too late to unlock the jail" (008-010).

A second factor was political: "Welfarism's strong emotional appeal to many voters, and the consequent temptations it presents the average politician" (008-011). Since welfare programs could be presented as compassionate, politicians faced electoral pressure to expand them, even at the cost of long-term liberty.

### The Consequences of Welfarism

Goldwater traced the ultimate political consequences of the welfare model: "the State that is able to deal with its citizens as wards and dependents has gathered unto itself unlimited political and economic power and is thus able to rule as absolutely as any oriental despot" (008-014). On the individual level, he argued that welfarism "transforms the individual from a dignified, industrious, self-reliant spiritual being into a dependent animal creature without his knowing it" (008-018). This gradual erosion of individual character was, for Goldwater, one of the central moral hazards of the political philosophy the ADA represented.
