The Korean War (1950–1953) was a Cold War conflict between communist North Korea (supported by China and the Soviet Union) and South Korea (supported by United Nations forces, predominantly the United States). It ended in an armistice that largely restored the pre-war boundary at the 38th parallel, leaving the Korean Peninsula divided to the present day.

## Origins and Course of the War

The war began on June 25, 1950, when North Korean forces crossed the 38th parallel into South Korea. The United Nations Security Council, in the absence of the Soviet Union (which was boycotting proceedings), authorized a multinational force under U.S. command to repel the invasion. General Douglas MacArthur led a dramatic amphibious landing at Inchon in September 1950, which turned the tide. However, when UN forces approached the Yalu River on China's border, Chinese "volunteers" entered the war in massive numbers, driving UN forces back. The conflict settled into a bloody stalemate along the 38th parallel, where it remained until the armistice of July 27, 1953.

## Goldwater's Critique

Goldwater references the Korean War in the context of United Nations decision-making and its impact on American foreign policy effectiveness. He argues that entrusting American policy to the UN has repeatedly weakened the U.S. position against communism:

> In nearly every case in which we have called upon the United Nations to do our thinking for us, and to make our policy for us — whether during the Korean War, or in the Suez crisis, or following the revolution in Iraq — we have been a less effective foe of Communism than we otherwise might have been. (010-013)

## The UN and the Limitations of Collective Security

The Korean War serves, in Goldwater's analysis, as a case study of what he sees as the failure of collective security through the UN — the principle that American national interests are best served by independent action rather than multilateral consensus. He contends that the war demonstrated the practical limits of fighting a war under UN auspices: the objectives were constrained by the need to maintain the coalition, and the outcome was a negotiated stalemate rather than a clear victory.

Goldwater also criticizes the broader pattern of American reliance on international organizations at the expense of decisive national action:

> The United Nations was not designed to make American foreign policy. It was designed to provide a forum for the discussion of international problems and, hopefully, for their peaceful resolution. (010-013)

## Legacy

The Korean War established a precedent for American involvement in Cold War conflicts under multilateral auspices — a precedent that Goldwater regarded as deeply problematic. The conflict's unresolved conclusion, with Korea remaining divided, became for conservatives a symbol of the inadequacy of limited-war strategies that sought "containment" rather than victory.
