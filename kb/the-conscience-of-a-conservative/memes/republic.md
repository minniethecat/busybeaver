## Definition

A Republic is a form of government in which the people delegate authority to elected representatives bound by a constitutional framework, as opposed to direct democracy or authoritarian rule. In Goldwater's political thought, the Republic is not merely a technical description of American government — it is "the cornerstone of the Republic, our chief bulwark against the encroachment of individual freedom by Big Government" (003-003). The distinction between a republic and a pure democracy is central to conservative political theory: where democracy emphasizes majority will, a republic constrains all power — including majority power — through constitutional limits.

## In Conservative Thought

For Goldwater and the Founders, the Republic represented a deliberate design choice against the dangers of unchecked power. The Constitution's framers were not visionaries who assumed their rules would automatically hold; rather, "they knew that rules of government, however brilliantly calculated to cope with the imperfect nature of man, however carefully designed to avoid the pitfalls of power, would be no match for men who were determined to disregard them" (002-009). The survival of the Republic thus depends on an engaged citizenry committed to constitutional limits. Benjamin Franklin captured this conditional nature of republican government in his famous reply at the close of the Constitutional Convention: "A Republic, if you can keep it!" (002-010).

The Republic is the container within which individual freedom operates. Goldwater's call to "restore the Republic" (002-022) is a call to restore the constitutional restraints that prevent any branch or level of government from accumulating excessive power. The principal threats to the Republic are States' Rights erosion — "the cornerstone of the Republic, our chief bulwark against the encroachment of individual freedom by Big Government, is fast disappearing under the piling sands of absolutism" (003-003) — and the progressive income tax, which "does violence both to the charter of the Republic and the laws of Nature" (007-009).

## Democracy vs. Republicanism

Alexis de Tocqueville, whom Goldwater calls "probably the most clairvoyant political observer of modern times" (002-019), identified the distinction between democracy and republicanism as essential to American liberty. Tocqueville "foresaw decay for a society that tended to put more emphasis on its democracy than on its republicanism" (002-019), warning that such a society would produce "guardians" who would manage the people into complacency, allowing them to "console themselves for being in tutelage by the reflection that they have chosen their own guardians" (002-019).

This distinction illuminates Goldwater's critique of modern American governance: when representative government becomes unmoored from constitutional constraints, it slides from republic toward pure democracy — and from democracy toward administrative despotism. The Republic requires that elected officials understand "their first duty as public officials is to divest themselves of the power they have been given" (002-022).

## National Defense and the Republic

The Republic's survival also depends on external security. Goldwater notes that "our forebearers knew that 'keeping a Republic' meant, above all, keeping it safe from foreign transgressors; they knew that a people cannot live and work freely, and develop national institutions conducive to freedom, except in peace and with independence" (010-003). The Founders understood that republican self-government could only flourish in conditions of national security — a principle Goldwater invokes to argue for a robust anti-Communist foreign policy. By 1960, Goldwater warns, "we have come full circle and our national existence is once again threatened as it was in the early days of the Republic" (010-004).
