## Definition

Limited war is armed conflict constrained in geographic scope, objectives, or the weapons employed — particularly conventional warfare conducted below the threshold of general nuclear exchange. Goldwater raises the problem of limited war in the context of a nuclear stalemate: "in a nuclear stalemate, where neither side is prepared to risk all-out nuclear war, Communist armies will be able to launch limited wars without fear of retaliation by our Strategic Air Command" (010-006). The concept thus represents both a strategic vulnerability and an imperative for American military preparedness.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's analysis of limited war is driven by a changing strategic reality. During the early Cold War, American nuclear superiority served as a deterrent to Communist conventional aggression. But "as the Soviet Union draws abreast of us in nuclear strength, that weakness could prove our undoing" (010-006). When both sides possess the capacity to destroy each other, the threat of nuclear retaliation loses credibility as a response to conventional provocations.

The result is a strategic gap: "the moment our leaders really accept this, strategic nuclear weapons will be neutralized and Communist armies will be able to launch limited wars without fear of retaliation" (010-006). The Communists, with their massive conventional manpower advantage, could pursue territorial gains through limited aggression while betting that the United States would not escalate to nuclear war over a peripheral conflict.

## The Conservative Response

Goldwater's answer is not to accept the inevitability of limited war defeats but to build the capacity to fight and win limited wars: "We must also develop a limited war capacity. For this latter purpose, we should make every effort to achieve decisive superiority in small, clean nuclear weapons" (010-024). This position — advocating tactical nuclear superiority for limited war scenarios — distinguishes Goldwater's approach from both advocates of "massive retaliation" (who rely solely on strategic nuclear deterrence) and advocates of purely conventional forces (who would concede the Communist manpower advantage).

## Nuclear Testing and Limited War

The limited war problem is connected, in Goldwater's analysis, to the nuclear testing debate. "There are two preliminary reasons why such proposals [for a permanent nuclear test ban] ought to be firmly rejected. First, there is no reliable means of preventing the Communists from secretly breaking such an agreement" (010-009). A test ban that froze American nuclear technology while permitting covert Soviet advances would compound the limited war vulnerability by denying the United States the "small, clean nuclear weapons" Goldwater considers essential.

## Military Superiority Over Parity

The limited war imperative is part of Goldwater's broader insistence on military superiority rather than mere parity: "Mere parity will not do. Since we can never match the Communists in manpower, our equipment and weapons must more than offset his advantage in numbers" (010-024). This reflects the conservative conviction that weakness invites aggression, and that peace is best preserved through unmistakable strength.

## Limited War and the Lessons of Korea

The Korean War (1950–1953) served as the practical backdrop for Goldwater's limited war analysis. The conflict demonstrated both the possibility of Communist conventional aggression in the nuclear age and the difficulty the United States faced in responding to such aggression with purely conventional forces. Goldwater's advocacy for "small, clean nuclear weapons" (010-024) reflected his judgment that the United States could not match Communist manpower in every potential theater of limited conflict and must therefore rely on technological superiority to offset numerical inferiority.

The Korean precedent also illustrated what Goldwater saw as the danger of fighting limited wars for limited objectives. A war fought to restore the status quo ante — to the 38th parallel rather than to unification — was, in his view, a war that had not been won. "Our objective," he insisted, "must be victory" (010-024). Limited war, properly understood, was not a substitute for the will to prevail but a different domain in which that will must be exercised.
