## Definition

Justice, in the conservative framework articulated by Barry Goldwater, is not an abstract ideal of social equity but a concrete standard rooted in impartiality, proportionality, and the rule of law. It requires that government treat all citizens equally under uniform rules — a principle that applies as much to taxation as it does to criminal law. Goldwater's notion of justice draws on the classical liberal tradition: the state exists in part to administer justice, but it must do so without becoming an instrument of economic redistribution or social leveling.

Goldwater distinguishes between *procedural justice* — the impartial administration of law — and *distributive justice*, which he views as a euphemism for confiscation. The former is a legitimate function of government; the latter, he argues, is a corruption of the concept that subordinates individual rights to collective claims.

## In Conservative Thought

Justice occupies a central place in Goldwater's conservative philosophy as one of the few functions he acknowledges as a proper role of government. In articulating the legitimate powers of the state, he lists "administering justice" alongside maintaining internal order, defending against foreign enemies, and removing obstacles to free commerce (002-001). This placement is significant: justice is not an afterthought but one of the constitutive purposes for which government is instituted.

The conservative understanding of justice is inseparable from the concept of limited government. Because government power tends to expand and corrupt, the administration of justice must be constrained by constitutional boundaries. When courts or legislators exceed these boundaries — as Goldwater argues the Supreme Court did in *Brown v. Board of Education* — they undermine the very justice they claim to serve. The conservative position is that justice requires not merely good outcomes but legitimate processes grounded in constitutional text and the intentions of its authors.

## Historical Context

The American concept of justice descends from English common law and the natural rights philosophy of John Locke, which held that the purpose of government is to secure the rights of life, liberty, and property. The Framers embedded this understanding in the Constitution through provisions for an independent judiciary, due process, and the equal protection of the laws.

By the mid-twentieth century, however, the meaning of justice had become contested terrain. The New Deal era had expanded the federal government's role in economic redistribution, often in the name of "social justice." The Supreme Court under Chief Justice Earl Warren had embarked on a series of decisions — culminating in *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) — that Goldwater and other conservatives viewed as judicial legislation rather than constitutional interpretation. Goldwater's treatment of justice must be understood against this backdrop: he is arguing not for a novel theory but for a recovery of the classical understanding against what he saw as its modern perversion.

## Key Arguments

**Justice as a legitimate function of government.** Goldwater identifies the administration of justice as one of the few activities that government is actually instituted to perform. The *legitimate* functions of government, he writes, "are actually conducive to freedom. Maintaining internal order, keeping foreign foes at bay, administering justice, removing obstacles to the free interchange of goods — the exercise of these powers makes it possible for men to follow their chosen pursuits with maximum freedom" (002-001). This framing is essential: justice is not a rival to freedom but its enabler.

**Taxation and proportional justice.** Goldwater's most sustained discussion of justice appears in his treatment of taxation. He argues that justice demands *proportional* rather than *graduated* taxation: "government has a right to claim an equal percentage of each man's wealth, and no more" (007-001). The graduated income tax, under which a high earner pays a larger *percentage*, is "repugnant to my notions of justice" because it punishes success and violates the natural right to property (007-001). This is an application of the formal equality principle: justice means identical rules applied identically to all.

> The idea that a man who makes $100,000 a year should be forced to contribute ninety percent of his income to the cost of government, while the man who makes $10,000 is made to pay twenty percent is repugnant to my notions of justice. I do not believe in punishing success. (007-001)

**Economic justice through voluntary association.** In discussing labor unions, Goldwater acknowledges that unions "can be an instrument for achieving economic justice for the working man" (006-001). However, this justice is achieved through voluntary association — the free exercise of the right to organize — not through government compulsion. Unions properly conceived are "an expression of freedom" and "an alternative to, and thus discourage, State Socialism" (006-001). Economic justice, in this view, is the outcome of free bargaining, not state-mandated redistribution.

**Justice in foreign policy.** Goldwater extends the concept of justice to international affairs. He argues that American foreign aid has, in effect, subsidized wealthy allies: "we have permitted them to transfer to their domestic economy funds which, in justice, should have been used in the common defense effort" (010-001). He also critiques the United Nations funding formula — "from each according to his ability" — as incompatible with "the American concept of justice" (010-001). The Marxist formula, he contends, imposes disproportionate obligations on the United States without corresponding benefits.

**Judicial activism as a threat to justice.** Goldwater criticizes the Supreme Court's reasoning in *Brown v. Board of Education*, specifically Chief Justice Warren's acknowledgment that the Court was "not being guided by the intentions of the amendment's authors" (004-001). For Goldwater, a court that disregards the original meaning of the constitutional text is not administering justice but imposing its own preferences. This argument connects justice to constitutionalism: there can be no justice without fidelity to law.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Conservative Justice | Liberal/Socialist Justice |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **Basis** | Equal rules, equal percentage | Equal outcomes, progressive redistribution |
| **Taxation** | Flat/proportional rate | Graduated/progressive rate |
| **Role of state** | Impartial arbiter of rules | Active redistributor of resources |
| **Economic justice** | Achieved through free association and contract | Achieved through state intervention |
| **Judicial role** | Interpret law as written | Shape law to meet social needs |
| **International** | Proportional burden-sharing | Ability-to-pay formula |

The fundamental difference is between a *procedural* conception of justice (equal treatment under equal rules) and a *distributive* conception (equalization of outcomes). Goldwater consistently argues for the former and identifies the latter as a corruption that serves the expansion of state power.
