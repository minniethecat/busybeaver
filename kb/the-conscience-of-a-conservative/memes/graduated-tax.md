## Definition

A graduated tax (also called a progressive tax) is a tax system in which the
rate of taxation increases as the taxable base increases. Under the U.S.
federal income tax, as established by the Sixteenth Amendment (1913) and
subsequent legislation, higher levels of income are taxed at progressively
higher marginal rates. The stated rationale is ability-to-pay: those with
greater resources can and should contribute a larger share of their income to
public revenue.

Goldwater rejects this rationale. He argues that the graduated tax is not a
revenue measure at all but a tool of social policy designed to redistribute
wealth and equalize economic outcomes — objectives he considers both
economically destructive and morally illegitimate.

## In Conservative Thought

In Goldwater's conservative framework, the graduated tax represents the
convergence of multiple pathologies of modern government: the erosion of
property rights, the use of tax policy for social engineering, and the
expansion of federal power beyond constitutional limits. The graduated tax
is "a confiscatory tax" (007-009) — not in the hyperbolic sense of high rates
but in the principled sense that it treats property not as a natural right
but as a government concession subject to redistribution at political will.

Goldwater's objection is not merely to the rate of taxation but to its
structure. A proportional tax (one that takes the same percentage from all
income levels) treats citizens equally before the law. A graduated tax, by
contrast, discriminates among citizens based on their economic success,
penalizing the productive and rewarding the dependent. This, Goldwater argues,
is incompatible with a free society: "The graduated tax is a confiscatory tax.
Its effect, and to a large extent its aim, is to bring down all men to a common
level" (007-009).

## Historical Context

The modern federal income tax was established by the Sixteenth Amendment in
1913, initially with a top rate of 7%. The graduated structure expanded
dramatically during the 1930s and 1940s: by 1944, the top marginal rate reached
94%, and although it declined somewhat in the post-war period, it remained
above 90% through the 1950s when Goldwater was writing. The federal government
had become, as Goldwater observed, "the biggest taxpayer and spender in all
history" (002-014).

Goldwater wrote during a period when the graduated tax was largely
uncontroversial in mainstream politics. Both parties accepted its legitimacy;
debates were over rates and brackets, not the principle of progressivity.
Goldwater's willingness to challenge the graduated tax as such — to call it
"confiscatory" and demand its abolition — placed him outside the post-war
consensus and marked him as a distinctive voice in American conservatism.

## Key Arguments

**The Confiscation Argument.** Goldwater's central claim is that the graduated
tax, by design, confiscates property rather than merely raising revenue: "The
graduated tax is a confiscatory tax" (007-009). The key word is
"confiscatory": it suggests that the tax does not merely take a share of
income but seeks to take *progressively more* from those who earn more, with
the aim not of funding government but of reducing inequality. This, Goldwater
argues, is not taxation but redistribution — and redistribution is not a
legitimate function of government under the Constitution.

**The Property Rights Argument.** Drawing on natural law, Goldwater argues
that property is a natural right: "One of the foremost precepts of the natural
law is man's right to the possession of his property" (007-004). The graduated
tax violates this right by treating property as contingent on government
forbearance rather than inherent in the person.

**The Economic Argument.** Goldwater argues that the graduated tax penalizes
the very activities — work, investment, innovation — that drive economic
growth. By taking a larger share from those who produce the most, it
discourages productive effort and channels resources into tax avoidance rather
than wealth creation. The economic consequence is a less dynamic, less
prosperous society for everyone.

**The Revenue Sufficiency Argument.** Goldwater contends that the graduated
structure is unnecessary for revenue purposes: "The government needs the
revenues it now receives. But if the government needs the graduated tax for
revenue purposes, the answer is to reduce government spending" (007-008). The
graduated tax exists not because government *needs* the money but because
government *wants* the power to reshape society through the tax code.

## Contrasts

| Dimension | Graduated/Progressive Tax | Proportional/Flat Tax |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Principle | Ability to pay; those with more should contribute a higher share | Equal treatment; all citizens pay the same rate |
| Effect on property | Confiscatory — treats property as government concession (007-009) | Respects property as natural right |
| Social purpose | Redistribution and income equalization | Revenue for legitimate government functions only |
| Incentive structure | Penalizes productivity and success | Neutral toward economic choices |

## Mentions in the Text

("Taxes and Spending"):

- **Chapter VII**: The confiscatory nature of progressive taxation (007-009)
- **Chapter VII**: The revenue sufficiency argument against graduated rates
  (007-008)
