# Bureaucracy

The federal bureaucracy is treated in Barry Goldwater's *The Conscience of a Conservative* as both a practical inefficiency and a philosophical threat to the American system of decentralized government. Goldwater critiques the bureaucracy across multiple domains — welfare, education, and fiscal policy — arguing that it displaces local knowledge and individual responsibility with centralized administrative control.

## The Broker's Fee

Goldwater characterizes the federal bureaucracy as an intermediary that extracts costs without adding value:

> States and local governments have found increasing portions of their revenues consist of funds collected by the federal government, processed through Washington, and returned to them minus a broker's fee taken by the federal bureaucracy. (003-012)

This "broker's fee" is the administrative overhead of federal programs — the cost of sending money to Washington only to have it returned with conditions and deductions. Goldwater argues that this circular flow of funds serves no purpose beyond expanding bureaucratic power.

## Welfare and Bureaucracy

In the context of welfare policy, Goldwater sees the federal bureaucracy as transforming charitable impulses into mechanical government operations. He contrasts the bureaucratic approach with private charity:

> Let us, then, not blunt the noble impulses of mankind by reducing charity to a mechanical operation of the federal government. (008-019)

The bureaucracy, in this view, substitutes impersonal administration for genuine human concern. Rather than allowing welfare to be "a private concern" promoted by "individuals and families, by churches, private hospitals, religious service organizations, community charities and other institutions," the federal approach channels resources — and moral responsibility — through an administrative apparatus that is remote from both donors and recipients (008-019).

## Education and Centralized Control

Goldwater extends his critique of bureaucracy to education, arguing against the concentration of educational authority in federal hands:

> I believe that our ability to cope with the great crises that lie ahead will be enhanced in direct ratio as we recapture the lost art of learning, and will diminish in direct ratio as we give responsibility for training our children's minds to the federal bureaucracy. (009-003)

The passage reflects Goldwater's conviction that education is properly a local and parental responsibility, not a federal one. Turning educational authority over to "the federal bureaucracy" diminishes rather than enhances society's capacity to address future challenges.

## Bureaucracy and Taxation

Goldwater connects the growth of bureaucracy to high levels of taxation, arguing that the two reinforce each other:

> High taxes, for which government Welfarism is so largely responsible, are in no small part the price we pay for the luxury of processing the money through the federal bureaucracy. (008-019)

This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: welfare programs require high taxes, which fund the bureaucracy that administers the programs, which in turn requires still more funding. The "luxury" of centralized administration is paid for by taxpayers who could otherwise retain and direct their own resources.

## Bureaucracy and Federalism

Goldwater's critique of bureaucracy is rooted in his commitment to [[federalism]]. The federal bureaucracy concentrates decision-making in Washington, overriding the principle that "essentially local problems are best dealt with by the people most directly concerned" (003-012). This displacement of local knowledge by centralized administration is both inefficient — Washington cannot know local conditions as well as local actors — and undemocratic — decisions are made by administrators remote from popular control.
