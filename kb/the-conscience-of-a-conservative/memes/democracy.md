## Definition

In Goldwater's framework, democracy — understood as unfiltered majority rule — is not the form of government that the American founders established. He draws a sharp distinction between democracy and republicanism: "Was it then a *Democracy* the framers created? Hardly. The system of restraints, on the face of it, was directed not only against individual tyrants, but also against a tyranny of the masses" (002-009). The framers understood that a pure democracy, without constitutional restraints, could be as dangerous to individual liberty as autocracy.

Goldwater's analysis draws heavily on Alexis de Tocqueville, who "foresaw decay for a society that tended to put more emphasis on its democracy than on its republicanism" (002-019). De Tocqueville predicted that America would produce "not tyrants but 'guardians'" and that the American people would "console themselves for being in tutelage by the reflection that they have chosen their own guardians" (002-019).

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater's treatment of democracy is cautionary and inseparable from his defense of constitutional republicanism. His analysis rests on several key arguments:

**1. The Danger of Unchecked Majority Rule.** The framers were "well aware of the danger posed by self-seeking demagogues — that they might persuade a majority of the people to confer on government vast powers in return for deceptive promises of economic gain" (002-009). The Constitution's system of restraints — enumerated powers, federalism, separation of powers, the amendment process — was designed specifically to prevent majorities from voting away the rights of individuals and the sovereignty of the States.

**2. The Erosion of Freedom Through Democratic Welfare.** Goldwater sees the welfare state as the modern mechanism through which democratic majorities are induced to surrender their freedom. "All too often we have put men in office who have suggested spending a little more on this, a little more on that, who have proposed a new welfare program, who have thought of another variety of 'security.' We have taken the bait, preferring to put off to another day the recapture of freedom and the restoration of our constitutional system" (002-017). The pattern Goldwater identifies is: democratic majorities, tempted by promises of material benefits, confer ever-expanding powers on the federal government, which then becomes increasingly detached from constitutional limits.

**3. Democracy as an Instrument of Collectivization.** The collectivists have discovered that "Welfarism is much more compatible with the political processes of a democratic society" (008-009) than outright nationalization. Rather than seizing property directly, the modern collectivist state uses the democratic process to expand welfare programs, financed by taxation, achieving the same subordination of the individual to the state through more palatable means. "They understand that private property can be confiscated as effectively by taxation as by expropriating it" (008-009).

**4. The Republican Alternative.** Against pure democracy, Goldwater upholds the republican model: a government of limited, enumerated powers, constrained by constitutional boundaries, federalism, and the separation of powers. In this model, the people rule — but only within the bounds of a Constitution that protects individual rights and state sovereignty from majoritarian encroachment. The distinction is not merely semantic: "We have gone the way of many a democratic society that has lost its freedom by persuading itself that if 'the people' rule, all is well" (002-017).

## Mentions in the Text

Key passages where Goldwater articulates his view of democracy:

> "Was it then a Democracy the framers created? Hardly. The system of restraints, on the face of it, was directed not only against individual tyrants, but also against a tyranny of the masses." (002-009)

> "The Frenchman, Alexis de Tocqueville, probably the most clairvoyant political observer of modern times, saw the danger when he visited this country in the 1830s. Even then he foresaw decay for a society that tended to put more emphasis on its democracy than on its republicanism." (002-019)

> "Every man allows himself to be put in lead-strings, because he sees that it is not a person nor a class of persons, but the people at large that hold the end of his chain." (002-019, quoting de Tocqueville)
