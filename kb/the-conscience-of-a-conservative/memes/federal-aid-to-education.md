## Definition

Federal aid to education refers to the provision of federal funds to state and local school systems. In Goldwater's analysis, this policy represents a particularly dangerous form of federal overreach because it combines constitutional violation with the corruption of education itself. He devotes Chapter IX to arguing that the problems of American education are not primarily financial and cannot be solved by federal funding.

## In Conservative Thought

Goldwater offers four distinct objections to federal aid to education. First, "federal intervention in education is unconstitutional" (009-005). The Constitution grants the federal government no authority over education, and "under the Tenth Amendment, education is a function of the States" (004-009).

Second, "the alleged need for federal funds has never been convincingly demonstrated" (009-006). Goldwater cites a 1955 finding that "there was no evidence available to show a shortage of classrooms due to lack of funds" and notes that "congressional committees have listened to hundreds of hours of testimony in favor of federal aid, but they have never heard that 1955 finding successfully refuted" (009-007).

Third, federal aid "promotes the idea that federal school money is 'free' money, and thus gives the people a distorted picture of the cost of education" (009-011). In truth, "the federal government has no funds except those it extracts from the taxpayers who reside in the various States" (009-012).

Fourth and most consequential: "federal aid to education inevitably means federal control of education" (009-013). Goldwater observes that "for many years, advocates of federal aid denied that aid implies control, but in recent years this argument has been abandoned" (009-013). Once the federal government provides funds, "Congress cannot be expected to appropriate the people's money and make no provision for its proper use" (009-014).

## Historical Context

The debate over federal aid to education intensified in the post-World War II era. Between 1949-50 and 1959-60, student enrollment grew from 25 million to over 44 million (009-010). Despite this growth, Goldwater maintained that "the national school system is not in distress" (009-008) and that local communities were capable of meeting their educational needs without federal intervention.

The Supreme Court's decision in *Brown v. Board of Education* added a civil rights dimension to the federal aid debate. Goldwater took the position that while he agreed "with the objectives of the Supreme Court as stated in the Brown decision" (004-015), the federal government had no constitutional authority to enforce school integration through funding conditions.

## Key Arguments

1. **Constitutional**: Education is a state function under the Tenth Amendment; no federal authority exists to fund or control it.
2. **Fiscal**: The need for federal funds has never been proven; state and local revenues are sufficient when properly allocated.
3. **Economic distortion**: Federal aid misrepresents the true cost of education by making it appear "free" to local recipients.
4. **Inevitable federal control**: "Federal aid to education inevitably means federal control of education" (009-013). Money from Washington comes with Washington's strings attached.
5. **The real problem is philosophical**: "The fundamental explanation of this distortion of values is that we have forgotten the purpose of education" (009-021). The influence of John Dewey's progressive educational philosophy, not lack of funding, is the deeper problem.

> I agree with lobbyists for federal school aid that education is one of the great problems of our day. I am afraid, however, that their views and mine regarding the nature of the problem are many miles apart. They tend to see the problem in quantitative terms — not enough schools, not enough teachers, not enough money. I see it in qualitative terms.
>
> (009-002)
