## Overview

[[hafiz-cassimi|Hafiz Cassimi]] is the son of the Turkish-Egyptian banker of the same name, and a figure in [[london|London]] society through his family's connection to [[major-druce|Major Druce]] and [[princess-dullah-veih|the Princess]] Dullah-Veih. He becomes the primary suspect in the disappearance of Lucie, the Swiss secretary to [[mrs-druce|Mrs. Druce]], though Loveday's investigation ultimately exonerates him.

## Appearance

Loveday's first detailed observation of Cassimi at [[mrs-druce|Mrs. Druce]]'s reception describes him vividly:

> He was a swarthy, well-featured man, with bold, black eyes, and lips that had the habit of parting now and again, not to smile, but as if for no other purpose than to show a double row of gleaming white teeth. The European dress he wore seemed to accord ill with the man; and Loveday could fancy that those black eyes and that double row of white teeth would have shown to better advantage beneath a turban or a fez cap. [004-056]

## Role in "A Princess's Vengeance"

### Major Druce's Suspicions

[[major-druce|Major Druce]]'s suspicions "centre solely upon a certain [[hafiz-cassimi|Hafiz Cassimi]]" [004-013]. The Major describes him to [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]] as "a thoroughgoing Iago" [004-015], alleging that:

- Cassimi "fell desperately in love with the little Swiss girl almost at first sight and pestered her with his attentions" [004-013]
- "Cassimi has stolen the photograph" of Lucie from the Major's drawer
- "Cassimi has inveigled the girl out of the house on some pretext — perhaps out of the country also" [004-015]

The Major even suggests "it might be as well to communicate with the police at Cairo, with as little delay as possible" [004-015].

### Background

Cassimi's family connection to [[princess-dullah-veih|the Princess]] runs deep. The Princess was "left an orphan at an early age, and was consigned to the guardianship of the elder Cassimi by her relatives" [004-021]. Both father and son are described as "very advanced and European in their ideas," and "by them she was taken to Cairo for her education" [004-021]. The Princess and young Cassimi are said to be "like brother and sister to each other" [004-013].

### Loveday's Assessment

Loveday is not favorably impressed: "off and on I have been studying Mr. Cassimi, and I must admit I do not like his smileless smile" [004-061]. She notes his "nonchalant and impassive" reaction when the Major dramatically announces her identity as a detective [004-149]. Unlike the other suspects who react nervously, Cassimi remains composed — a response that helps Loveday eliminate him from suspicion.

### The Real Culprit

The actual abductor of Lucie is not Cassimi but Lebrun, the butler, who had secretly been engaged to the Swiss girl all along. Cassimi's attention to Lucie was genuine but incidental — one of "additional complications" arising "from the fact that your attentions to the little Swiss girl had drawn Mr. Cassimi's notice to her numerous attractions, and there was the danger of you two young men posing as rivals" [004-138].

## Characteristics

Cassimi represents a type of red herring common in detective fiction — the foreign outsider whose exotic appearance and ambiguous manner make him a convenient suspect. His "smileless smile" [004-061] and composed demeanor under pressure mark him as a man of self-possession, while his European dress and education place him at the intersection of Eastern and Western cultures in Victorian [[london|London]] society.
