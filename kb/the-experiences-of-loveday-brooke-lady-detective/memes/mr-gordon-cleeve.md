## Overview

Gordon Cleeve is "the only son of Sir Gordon Cleeve, a wealthy baronet" [007-021] and one of two rivals for the affection of [[miss-golding|René Golding]]. His reaction to rejection — violent threats followed by sudden departure on a world tour — makes him the most obvious suspect in [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]]'s disappearance, though the investigation ultimately clears him.

## The Love Triangle

Cleeve and [[lord-guilleroy|Lord Guilleroy]] are the only two men who "appear to have found the slightest favour in [[[miss-golding|Miss Golding]]'s] eyes" [007-021]. The young woman "seems to have coquetted pretty equally with these two" [007-021] before abruptly shifting her favor entirely to [[lord-guilleroy|Lord Guilleroy]].

## The Rejection

The reason for [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]]'s sudden change of heart is revealed in a revealing domestic detail: "one day [[miss-ren|René]] saw him cruelly thrashing one of his setters, and after that she cut him dead — would have nothing whatever to do with him" [007-050]. This incident of casual cruelty exposes Cleeve's true character and provides [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]] with an instinctive moral justification for ending their relationship.

## Violent Threats

Cleeve's response to rejection is extreme:

> Gordon Cleeve does not sit down quietly under this treatment. He threatens to shoot first his rival, then himself, then Miss Golding; finally, does none of these three things, but starts off on a three years' journey round the world. [007-021]

This escalation — from romantic disappointment to threats of triple [[murder|homicide]] — makes him the natural suspect when [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]] disappears shortly afterward. The timing is incriminating: his departure occurs "on the 19th; the day before [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]] disappeared" [007-023].

## As a Red Herring

[[inspector-ramsay|Inspector Ramsay]] initially follows the Cleeve lead, tracing him "down to Brindisi" and confirming "that he went on board the *Buckingham*, en route for Alexandria" [007-023]. [[inspector-ramsay|Ramsay]] concludes that Cleeve "can, by any possibility, be connected with the affair" [007-023] — but the sheer obviousness of his guilt makes him an unlikely culprit.

[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] uses Cleeve as a strategic device rather than a genuine suspect. She tells the maid Lena that she requires information "respecting Mr. Gordon Cleeve" [007-138] — but this is a feint. As she later admits, "I feigned suspicion of Mr. Gordon Cleeve, and promised her rewards if she would bring me tidings of his doings. This was to pave the way to dismissing her on a journey to Italy" [007-175]. The false suspicion of Cleeve gives her cover to dispatch Lena to Italy, where the maid's movements lead [[lord-guilleroy|Lord Guilleroy]] to the real [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]].

## Characteristics

Cleeve is never seen directly but is characterized through others' reports: impulsive, hot-tempered, cruel to animals, and prone to melodramatic threats he does not carry out. He is a classic detective-fiction red herring — the obvious suspect whose very obviousness argues against his guilt. His world tour conveniently removes him from the scene, leaving the field open for the real resolution of the mystery.
