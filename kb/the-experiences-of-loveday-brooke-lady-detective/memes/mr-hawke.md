The Reverend Anthony Hawke is a clergyman of the Church of England who, at the time of "Drawn Daggers," resides in Tavistock Square, [[london|London]]. He acts as the English guardian of [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]], the daughter of [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] Monroe of Peking, who is sent to England to escape an undesirable suitor [005-009]. Mr. Hawke is the client who brings both the necklace mystery and the alarming "[[the-daggers|drawn daggers]]" threats to [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]'s detective agency.

## Role in the Stories

Mr. Hawke serves as the client and primary informant in "Drawn Daggers." He first approaches [[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]]'s Agency regarding the disappearance of [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]'s diamond necklace, and subsequently reports receiving threatening sketches of [[the-daggers|daggers]] through the morning post [005-013]. His alarm drives the investigation, though [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s methods often leave him bewildered and amazed.

## Key Appearances

Mr. Hawke appears throughout "Drawn Daggers":

- Visits [[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]]'s Agency to report the necklace's disappearance and is introduced to [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] [005-006]
- Reveals that [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]'s necklace, valued at over nine hundred pounds, has vanished from her jewel-case [005-009]
- Reports receiving threatening "drawn dagger" sketches in the morning post on multiple occasions [005-013]
- Is "much disturbed" by each new development in the case [005-024]
- Expresses astonishment at Loveday's revelations about [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] and the true meaning of the [[the-daggers|daggers]] [005-115]

## Biography

Mr. Hawke gave up his church living some twenty years before the events of the story, upon marrying a wealthy lady [005-009]. He resides in Tavistock Square with his wife and household staff. At the time of the story, his nephew Jack, home on leave from his regiment in India, is also staying with them [005-051].

His household consists of himself, his wife, a butler, a cook, a housemaid, and his wife's maid — who at the time of the story is doing double duty as [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]'s maid [005-055]. Mr. Hawke agreed to act as [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]'s guardian at the request of her father, [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] Monroe, who added a postscript to his letter asking that the necklace be deposited at the bank with "as little delay as possible" [005-009].

## Characteristics

Mr. Hawke is portrayed as a well-meaning but somewhat anxious and easily alarmed gentleman. His face "brightened" and "fell" in rapid succession as Loveday questioned him [005-029], and he is described as looking "much disturbed and worried" during the investigation [005-093]. He repeatedly expresses amazement at Loveday's deductive leaps — "Dear me! dear me! How is it possible for you to know all this?" [005-117].

He has a tendency toward self-deprecation and mild assertion — when explaining he came to [[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]]'s Agency while his wife was away, he adds defensively that he does "not mean to imply that I am not master in my own house" [005-035]. He is also capable of wry humour, noting that [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] and his wife together gave "about a hundred reasons" for not calling the police [005-037].

## Relationships

- **[[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]** — his ward, sent from Peking under his guardianship
- **[[mrs-hawke|Mrs. Hawke]]** — his wealthy wife, described as having "a very firm, resolute manner" [005-009]
- **Jack Hawke** — his nephew, home from India with his regiment, with whom [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] reportedly "gets on better... than with anyone else" [005-051]
- **[[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]** — the agency head he consults
- **[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]** — the detective assigned to his case

## Significance

Mr. Hawke represents the typical Victorian gentleman client — respectable, somewhat naive about criminal matters, and easily unsettled by mysterious threats. His genuine concern for his ward and his willingness to seek professional help despite his wife's objections demonstrate a conscientious nature. The resolution of his case — in which the "[[the-daggers|daggers]]" are revealed to be the [[mr-danvers|Danvers]] family crest, part of a secret communication between [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] and her suitor — leaves him bewildered by the "intricate" and "bewildering" web of [[fraud|deception]] within his own household [005-144].
