## Overview

The Rev. Charles Turner is the Vicar of East Downes and a man "of good means" [006-009] who has been "refurnishing the Vicarage, and paying away large sums for old oak furniture and for pictures" [006-009] since his marriage about six months before the events of the story. He becomes [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]'s client when a signed blank cheque for six hundred pounds is stolen from his home.

## Appearance

Loveday observes Mr. Turner from a distance: he is "sanguine-complexioned, red-haired, and wore a distinctly troubled expression of countenance" [006-052]. This troubled look is characteristic — he is a man under financial and domestic strain.

## Role in "The Ghost of Fountain Lane"

### The Stolen Cheque

The case begins when Turner is "suddenly called from home by the death of a relative" [006-009]. Before leaving, he gives his wife four signed blank cheques drawn on the [[west-sussex-bank|West Sussex Bank]]. Upon his return a week later, he discovers one cheque missing — filled in for six hundred pounds and cashed in gold by a man "of gentlemanly appearance" [006-009].

### Inconsistent Behavior

Turner's conduct puzzles the investigators. When he first discovers the loss, "he was very excited and irate" and had to be "persuaded that there was no need to telegraph to [[london|London]] for half-a-dozen detectives" [006-027]. But when [[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]] visits East Downes to investigate, Turner's attitude has "altogether changed":

> He was exceedingly reluctant to answer any questions, lost his temper when I pressed them, and as good as told me that he wished he had not moved in the matter at all. [006-027]

[[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]] correctly deduces that this "sudden change of demeanour" points toward [[mrs-turner|Mrs. Turner]] — "a man must have a very strong reason for wishing to sit idle under a loss of six hundred pounds" [006-027].

### Financial Strain

Turner's domestic life is marked by financial tension. He is overheard saying indignantly to his wife, "only a week or two ago I gave you nearly £500 to pay your debts in [[brighton|Brighton]], and now there comes another bill" [006-146]. Despite his good means, his wife's spending habits and debts contracted before their marriage create ongoing pressure.

## Characteristics

Turner is a man caught between his pastoral duties and domestic troubles. His handwriting reveals something of his character — it is "that round, schoolboy's hand, so easy to imitate" with "no flourishes, nothing distinctive about it" [006-023], suggesting a straightforward, uncomplicated nature ill-equipped for the complexities of his wife's secret Catholicism and the criminal conspiracy that has infiltrated his household.

He is also, by his own admission, somewhat naive — his confidence that Loveday "would never open his doors to you" [006-035] proves well-founded, as he would not willingly admit a female detective into his home, forcing Loveday to lodge with [[mrs-brown|Mrs. Brown]] in the village instead.

## Resolution

The cheque was stolen not by [[mrs-turner|Mrs. Turner]] but by the former maid [[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]], who passed it to her associate Rogers (a revivalist preacher and Wesleyan minister). Rogers filled in the amount and cashed it before fleeing to New York. Turner's initial suspicion of his wife — and his consequent reluctance to pursue the investigation — was unfounded, though the financial pressures within the marriage were real enough.
