[[richard-golding|Richard Golding]] is a wealthy Australian merchant who settled at [[langford-hall|Langford Hall]], [[langford-cross|Langford Cross]], [[langford-cross|Leicestershire]], approximately a dozen years before the events of "Missing!" He is the father of [[miss-golding|Irené Golding]], whose sudden disappearance forms the central mystery of the seventh and final story in the collection [007-006].

## Role in the Stories

Mr. Golding is the bereaved father and client in "Missing!" whose daughter, [[irene-golding|Irené]], vanishes without explanation. [[inspector-ramsay|Inspector Ramsay]] of the local police engages [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] through [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]'s agency to investigate the disappearance, and Mr. Golding welcomes her to stay at [[langford-hall|Langford Hall]] [007-031]. His emotional state throughout the investigation provides the story's emotional core.

## Key Appearances

Mr. Golding appears throughout "Missing!":

- First referenced in the handbill offering a five-hundred-pound reward for information about his missing daughter [007-006]
- Greets [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] warmly upon her arrival at [[langford-hall|Langford Hall]], though visibly broken by the ordeal [007-042]
- Struggles to maintain composure during dinner conversations about the case [007-115]
- Receives a hopeful but ultimately fruitless telegram from the [[paris|Paris police]] [007-066]
- By the story's end, is reported "delirious with brain fever" following the tragic discovery [007-136]

## Biography

Mr. Golding made his fortune as a merchant in Australia before coming to England. On his journey home from Australia, he traveled through Italy, where he fell in love with and married a young woman he met in Naples. They had one child — [[irene-golding|Irené]], who inherited her mother's dark hair, olive complexion, and striking beauty [007-015].

After his wife's death, Mr. Golding moved to England and settled at [[langford-hall|Langford Hall]]. He did not remarry, but at the time of the story, he was engaged to [[mrs-greenhow|Mrs. Greenhow]], a widow who had acted as chaperon to his daughter and housekeeper to himself for about a year [007-015]. This impending marriage was a source of tension with [[irene-golding|Irené]], who reportedly did not get on well with her future stepmother.

Mr. Golding gave his daughter a liberal allowance and "exacted no accounts" of her spending [007-019]. He is described as a man who keeps up appearances "with great difficulty" and is "on the verge of a breakdown" after his daughter's disappearance [007-031].

## Characteristics

Mr. Golding is portrayed as a devoted father, utterly devastated by his daughter's disappearance. His face is "wan and haggard," his head bowed, his voice "strained and weak," and he seems "incapable of speaking on any save the one topic that filled his thoughts" [007-042]. He is reported to have not had "half an hour's sleep" since [[irene-golding|Irené]] left home [007-031].

Despite his grief, he shows moments of irritability, particularly when the discussion turns to [[mr-gordon-cleeve|Gordon Cleeve]] — the young man whom [[irene-golding|Irené]] had rejected before her disappearance [007-111]. He is protective of his daughter's [[respectability|reputation]] and defensive about the household staff, whom he feels the police have "too rigorously cross-examined" [007-063].

## Relationships

- **[[miss-golding|Irené Golding]]** — his only daughter, whose disappearance is the central mystery
- **[[mrs-greenhow|Mrs. Greenhow]]** — his fiancée, a widow serving as housekeeper and chaperon
- **[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]** — the detective he welcomes into his home to investigate
- **[[lord-guilleroy|Lord Guilleroy]]** — the young nobleman devotedly attached to [[irene-golding|Irené]]
- **[[mr-gordon-cleeve|Gordon Cleeve]]** — the rejected suitor whom Mr. Golding defends, noting [[irene-golding|Irené]] cut him off after seeing him "cruelly thrashing one of his setters" [007-058]

## Significance

Mr. Golding represents the emotional stakes of detective fiction — the human cost behind the mystery. His progressive physical and mental decline as the investigation unfolds mirrors the darkening tone of the narrative. The story ends tragically for him: after the recovery of his daughter's body, he succumbs to brain fever [007-136]. His case is the most somber of [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s investigations, departing from the usual pattern of resolution and restoration.
