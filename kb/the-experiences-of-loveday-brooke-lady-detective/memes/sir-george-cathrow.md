Sir George Cathrow is an elderly baronet and the owner of [[craigen-court|Craigen Court]], the country house that is the setting for the Christmas Eve jewellery robbery in "[[the-black-bag|The Black Bag]] Left on a Doorstep." He and his wife [[lady-cathrow|Lady Cathrow]] are the victims of the [[robbery|theft]] of £30,000 worth of jewellery [001-004].

## Role in the Stories

Sir George is the male victim and indirect client in the first story. While he does not directly engage [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] — the investigation is arranged through [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]'s agency without his knowledge — his household is the stage for the entire mystery. His habits, household arrangements, and social customs provide crucial context for understanding how the robbery was executed [001-004].

## Key Appearances

Sir George appears throughout "[[the-black-bag|The Black Bag]] Left on a Doorstep":

- He is first introduced as the owner of [[craigen-court|Craigen Court]], the scene of the robbery [001-004]
- His custom of leaving the dining-room window unshuttered during dinner is identified as a key security feature that the thieves circumvented [001-006]
- His "invariable custom" of filling his house and giving a large dinner on Christmas Eve is noted [001-007]
- [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] is instructed not to meet him, as her presence in the house is kept secret — she poses as "Jane Smith," a visiting niece of the housekeeper [001-042]
- He and [[lady-cathrow|Lady Cathrow]] are described as having been "goodness itself" to the servants during the investigation, insisting that "we are all innocent till we are proved guilty" [001-209]
- He promptly summons [[mr-bates|Mr. Bates]] from [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] when new evidence comes to light [001-263]

## Biography

Sir George Cathrow is an elderly man with no children and few relatives, which accounts for the "large amount of time" he and his wife spend entertaining friends [001-007]. He is the owner of [[craigen-court|Craigen Court]], an "oddly-built old place, jutting out in all directions" [001-004]. The house, despite its architectural quirks, is a centre of country society, particularly during the Christmas season.

## Characteristics

Sir George is portrayed as a man of good humour and wit. He takes particular pride in a pun he coined — referring to his wife's dressing-room, located directly above the dining-room, as the "safest" room in the house [001-006]. He is also known for his hospitality: his "invariable custom" is to fill his house with guests for a large Christmas Eve dinner [001-007].

He displays a notably fair-minded and humane attitude toward his servants during the crisis. The housekeeper reports that Sir George and [[lady-cathrow|Lady Cathrow]] "have been goodness itself to us through this trying time" and insist that "none of our duties are to be in any way altered" [001-209] — a marked contrast to the suspicion that typically falls on domestic staff in such cases.

His sense of humour is further attested by a previous Christmas entertainment, in which he was seen "laughing fit to kill himself" at [[harry-emmett|Harry Emmett]]'s comic recitation of "The Noble Convict" [001-189].

## Relationships

- **[[lady-cathrow|Lady Cathrow]]** — his wife, whose £30,000 jewellery collection is stolen
- **[[harry-emmett|Harry Emmett]]** — a former footman in the household, later suspected in the robbery
- **[[mrs-williams|Mrs. Williams]]** — the housekeeper at [[craigen-court|Craigen Court]]
- **[[colonel-james|Colonel James]]** — a friend whose footman, [[harry-emmett|Harry Emmett]], frequently visited [[craigen-court|Craigen Court]]
- **[[mr-bates|Mr. Bates]]** — the [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] detective stationed in the village

## Significance

Sir George Cathrow represents the archetypal Victorian country gentleman — hospitable, good-natured, and somewhat naive about security. His famous pun about the "safest room" becomes grimly ironic when the thieves bypass the dining-room window entirely, entering instead through the ground-floor cloakroom window [001-004]. His fair treatment of his servants provides narrative contrast to the investigative focus on the servants' hall, and his household serves as a microcosm of the class dynamics that [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] must navigate in her work.
