The drawn daggers are the defining physical objects of "[[ch05-drawn-daggers|Drawn Daggers]]," the fifth story in the collection.

They are ink sketches of daggers — "roughly drawn, in ink, two daggers, about six inches in length, with remarkably pointed and sharp-looking blades" — mailed to [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] in a sequence of threatening letters [[005-015]].

The daggers are never wielded as weapons; their threat is entirely symbolic. Yet in the hands of [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]], they become instruments of revelation, though not of the kind their sender anticipated.

## 描述

The daggers first appear as drawings enclosed in letters received by [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]], a young woman staying at [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George Cathrow]]'s country house:

> "Enclosed in the envelope were roughly drawn, in ink, two daggers, about six inches in length, with remarkably pointed and sharp-looking blades. Beneath the drawing were the words: 'Yours will be the third.'" [[005-015]]

A second letter contains "one dagger only" [[005-017]]. A third bears the sketch of "one dagger only" as well [[005-020]].

And in the culminating threat, the letter-writer promises: "I will send an instalment of three daggers, in which case I may consider my account with you closed" [[005-033]].

The daggers are never described as finely rendered or artistically accomplished. They are "roughly drawn, in ink" — functional images, crude representations of threat rather than aesthetic objects.

The "remarkably pointed and sharp-looking blades" are the only detail noted, emphasizing the menace the drawings are meant to convey. The daggers are reduced to their essential, threatening geometry: a blade, sharp and pointed.

Loveday's analysis of the daggers focuses on their physical characteristics. In her final exposition, she explains the crucial forensic detail:

> "To begin with, the blade of the dagger of common life is, as a rule, curved, while the blade of the dagger you drew — and I use the word 'dagger' in its broadest sense — was perfectly straight." [[005-124]]

This observation — the straightness of the drawn blades — becomes the key to unlocking the case.

## 在故事中的出现

The drawn daggers appear at specific points in the narrative:

1. **First appearance (PN 005-015):** [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] receives the first threatening letter, enclosing "roughly drawn, in ink, two daggers, about six inches in length." The message — "Yours will be the third" — establishes the daggers as instruments of psychological terror.

2. **Second and third letters (PN 005-017, 005-020):** Two subsequent letters arrive, each bearing "one dagger only" — a countdown that intensifies the threat [[005-017]].

3. **The installment of three (PN 005-103, 005-111):** The sender's promise to "send an instalment of three daggers" is fulfilled when Loveday receives "not only the three daggers for which you prepared me, but a fourth thrown in" [[005-111]].

4. **Forensic analysis (PN 005-124):** In the denouement, Loveday explains that the "straight" blade is inconsistent with the "curved" blade of an actual dagger [[005-124]].

5. **The revelation (PN 005-130):** Loveday reveals that the sender was within the household all along [[005-130]].

## 叙事功能

The drawn daggers function in the narrative in three overlapping roles.

**As threat**, the daggers work through escalation: two daggers, then one, then three. The count is never random — each change in number carries a message.

The reduction to "one dagger only" in the second letter suggests a narrowing of the threat. And the "instalment of three daggers" represents the culmination, the settling of accounts.

**As clue**, the daggers are distinguished by a single physical detail: the straightness of their blades. Loveday's analysis hinges on the observation that "the blade of the dagger of common life is, as a rule, curved" while the drawn blades are "perfectly straight" [[005-124]].

This forensic detail — the kind of observation that distinguishes Loveday's method throughout the collection — transforms the daggers from symbols of menace into physical evidence.

**As metafictional commentary**, the daggers embody the story's title in a double sense. As Loveday herself observes: "The episode of the drawn daggers — drawn in a double sense — should prove an interesting one" [[005-028]].

The daggers are "drawn" both as images (sketched in ink) and as weapons (drawn from a sheath in threat). This wordplay signals the detective genre's perennial concerns with the gap between appearance and reality.

## 文学意义

The drawn daggers belong to a long tradition of threatening documents in detective fiction — from the "poison pen" letters of Victorian sensation novels to the cryptic warnings received by Sherlock Holmes's clients.

But Pirkis's treatment is notable for the specificity of Loveday's forensic analysis. The daggers are not merely atmospheric; they are material evidence, subject to the same kind of scrutiny Loveday applies to handwriting, footprints, or the ashes in a grate.

The story's title — "Drawn Daggers" — encodes the double meaning that Loveday herself flags: daggers "drawn in a double sense." This self-conscious wordplay positions the daggers as both threat and clue, both symbol and evidence.

The daggers are not dangerous in themselves (they are only drawings), yet they function as effective threats precisely because they invoke the real danger they represent. The gap between the drawing and the dagger is the space in which Loveday's detective intelligence operates.

> "Now we come to the daggers!" Loveday exclaims in her denouement. "I feel sure you are going to be quite as interested in them as I have been." [[005-121]]

The daggers also represent a distinct type of detective-story object: the **false-threat object**, an item whose apparent meaning (a death threat) conceals its actual significance (a clue to the sender's identity).

In this, they parallel [[the-black-bag|the black bag]] from chapter 001 — both are objects whose surface interpretation is systematically wrong, and whose true meaning is accessible only through the kind of penetrating analysis that defines [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s professional method.
