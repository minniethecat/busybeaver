## Overview

Mrs. Hawke is the wife of [[mr-hawke|the Rev. Anthony Hawke]] and mistress of the household in Tavistock Square where the central events of "Drawn Daggers" take place. She is characterized by a "very firm, resolute manner" [005-041] and plays a pivotal role in the discovery of the missing diamond necklace belonging to [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]].

## Role in the Story

When [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]], the supposed heiress from Peking, comes to stay at the Hawke household, it is Mrs. Hawke who takes decisive action regarding the young woman's valuable diamond necklace. She "told [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] plainly that she would not have the responsibility of those diamonds in the house, and insisted that there and then they should be sent off to the bankers" [005-041]. It is upon this insistence that [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] goes upstairs and returns to announce the necklace has disappeared.

Mrs. Hawke initially objects to calling in the police. She privately tells her husband that "girls were always careless with their jewellery" and suggests [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] "might have lost the necklace in Peking, and never have brought it to England at all" [005-039]. She supports [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] in not wishing to involve the authorities.

## Key Appearances

Mrs. Hawke is away visiting during the early part of the investigation, returning by train on Friday. Her absence and the timing of her return create complications for [[mr-hawke|her husband]], who nervously tells [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] to make herself scarce before Mrs. Hawke arrives [005-060, 005-063].

When Loveday warns [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] that she is "laying too much stress upon [Mrs. Hawke's] possible knowledge of her mistress's movements" [005-130], it is Mrs. Hawke's maid who provides crucial evidence — confirming that [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]'s room displayed "the neatness of a maid — not of a lady" [005-130], a detail that helps Loveday unravel the conspiracy.

## Characteristics

Mrs. Hawke is portrayed as a figure of authority within her household. Even her husband, a clergyman, defers to her judgment and fears her disapproval. When a telegram arrives announcing her early return, [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] is visibly agitated: "Now, [[loveday-brooke|Miss Brooke]], what are we to do? I told you how much Mrs. Hawke objected to the investigation of this matter" [005-094].

She is pragmatic and unsentimental — her theory about the missing necklace (that [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] simply lost it abroad) reflects a commonsense approach to problems. Yet her firmness also serves the investigation: it is her insistence on banking the diamonds that forces the revelation of the [[robbery|theft]].

## Relationship to the Case

Mrs. Hawke's role is ultimately that of an unwitting facilitator. Her demand that the necklace be secured at the bank triggers the chain of events that exposes the [[disguise|impersonation]] scheme. Her maid's [[evidence|testimony]] about the room's arrangement provides the final piece of evidence Loveday needs. Though she was reluctant to involve the police, her household management and the loyalty of her servants prove instrumental to the investigation's success.
