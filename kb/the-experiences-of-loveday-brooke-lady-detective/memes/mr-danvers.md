## Overview

Mr. Danvers is the young man with whom [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] (whose real name is Mary O'Grady) is secretly in love. Though he never appears directly in "Drawn Daggers," his existence and his family crest are central to the resolution of the mystery.

## Background

[[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] — actually Mary O'Grady, impersonating the Peking heiress — and Mr. Danvers had "arranged" their plan "before she left Peking" [005-114]. He was to "leave Peking within a fortnight of her departure for England, and that he was to remain at Plymouth until he received a certain signal from her" [005-116].

The signal system they devised involved the Danvers family crest — a cross-like design — which was mistaken by the conspirators for a dagger. Loveday explains:

> In this connection, the Danvers crest, which it is possible that they mistook for a dagger, suggested itself naturally, for no doubt Miss Monroe had many impressions of it on her lover's letters. [005-130]

## Role in the Plot

Danvers never appears in the narrative but is the motivation for the entire [[disguise|impersonation]] scheme. The real [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]], a wealthy heiress from Peking, wanted to "get the young daughter out of the way of young Danvers's impertinent attentions" [005-049]. In response, the girls arranged for Mary O'Grady to take [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]'s place in England while the real [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] stayed at Mary's home in Cork.

The "[[the-daggers|daggers]]" (crosses) sent to [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] were actually signals:
- "dagger (or cross) number one was sent to notify the safe arrival of [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] and Mrs. O'Grady at Cork" [005-130]
- "The two [[the-daggers|daggers]] or crosses you subsequently received were sent on the day of Mr. Danvers's arrival at Plymouth, and were, I should say, sketched by his hand" [005-130]
- The final three crosses signaled the marriage — and Mary O'Grady's release from her role

## Resolution

After Loveday unravels the conspiracy, [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George Cathrow]] accepts the situation. Loveday suggests "it would be best for [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] — Mrs. Danvers I mean — to come to my house and be married from there" [005-158], confirming the couple's happy ending.

## The Danvers Crest

The crest is described as a cross that "had been handed down as the crest of the Danvers family" [005-124]. Its resemblance to a dagger made it a perfect secret signal — alarming enough to draw attention within the household, yet meaningless to anyone not part of the conspiracy.
