Miss Monroe is the daughter of [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] Monroe and an heiress to her mother's large fortune. She was sent from Peking, China, to England under the guardianship of the Reverend [[mr-hawke|Anthony Hawke]] and his wife, in order to remove her from the attentions of a suitor her father considered unsuitable — a young man named [[mr-danvers|William Wentworth Danvers]], who was sent to Peking to study the language for a customs position [005-051].

## Role in the Stories

Miss Monroe is the central figure around whom the mystery of "Drawn Daggers" revolves. Her valuable diamond necklace, valued at over nine hundred pounds and inherited from her mother, is reported missing shortly after her arrival in England [005-009]. The case is brought to [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]'s agency by her guardian, the Reverend [[mr-hawke|Anthony Hawke]], after Miss Monroe herself expressed strong reluctance to involve the police [005-035].

## Key Appearances

Miss Monroe appears exclusively in the fifth story, "Drawn Daggers":

- She is first mentioned when [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] visits [[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]]'s agency to report the disappearance of her necklace [005-009]
- Her room is examined by [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] during her investigation at the Hawke residence in Tavistock Square [005-084]
- She is present at meals during Loveday's stay at the Hawke household [005-100]
- She walks out of the house alone on the morning following Loveday's investigation [005-111]

## Biography

Miss Monroe made the long journey from Peking to England accompanied initially by an elderly Chinese maid. However, at Malta, the maid suffered such severe seasickness that she was allowed to disembark and await passage back to China [005-043]. After this, Miss Monroe engaged one of the steerage passengers to act as her maid for the remainder of the voyage [005-043].

Her father, [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] Monroe, had arranged for her to travel with friends as far as Hong Kong, after which she continued on the steamer *Colombo* with only her maid. She chose to land at Plymouth rather than at the [[london|London]] docks, and telegraphed [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] to meet her at Waterloo Station instead [005-043].

Miss Monroe is described as "very quiet, very shy and reserved" — she "goes nowhere, sees no one" [005-051]. When old China friends of her father called to see her, she immediately claimed a headache and went to bed [005-051].

## Characteristics

Miss Monroe is portrayed as a young woman of independent spirit and remarkable self-sufficiency. The Hawkes' maid, who waited on her temporarily, declared that "she scarcely requires a maid" and that she had never "had dealings with such a young lady" — a testament to Miss Monroe's self-reliance [005-088].

Her room was described as exceptionally orderly and comfortable — "everything in the room was, so to speak, squared to the quarter of an inch, and yet everything that a lady could require in dressing lay ready to hand" [005-086]. This extreme neatness was later revealed to be connected to her secret arrangement with a fellow passenger.

Miss Monroe appears to have been reluctant to come to England, with [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] noting "it was a great wrench for her to leave her home and friends in that sudden fashion" [005-051].

## Relationships

- **[[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] Monroe** — her father, who sent her to England to separate her from [[mr-danvers|Danvers]]
- **[[mr-hawke|Rev. Anthony Hawke]]** — her guardian in England, at whose Tavistock Square house she resided
- **[[mrs-hawke|Mrs. Hawke]]** — her guardian's wife, who insisted the necklace be deposited at the bank
- **[[mr-danvers|William Wentworth Danvers]]** — the young suitor in Peking from whom her father wished to separate her; his family crest (a crusader's cross) was the true meaning behind the "[[the-daggers|drawn daggers]]" sent to the Hawke household [005-124]
- **Mrs. O'Grady and Miss O'Grady** — steerage passengers on the *Colombo*; Miss O'Grady was engaged by Miss Monroe to act as her maid and later, in a twist of the plot, impersonated her at the Hawke residence [005-114]

## Significance

Miss Monroe's case exemplifies the kind of intricate domestic mystery at which [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] excelled. The mystery of the missing necklace is ultimately revealed to be a scheme orchestrated by Miss Monroe herself — she had arranged with Miss O'Grady to impersonate her at the Hawke household, while the real Miss Monroe remained free to pursue her relationship with [[mr-danvers|Danvers]]. The "[[the-daggers|drawn daggers]]" that so alarmed [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] turned out to be the [[mr-danvers|Danvers]] family crest — a crusader's cross — sent as a form of communication [005-124].
