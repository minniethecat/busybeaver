# Miss Monroe's Necklace

The diamond necklace belonging to [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] is the object at the centre of the [[robbery|theft]] investigation in [[ch05-drawn-daggers|Drawn Daggers]], the fifth [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] story. The case is introduced to Loveday by [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]] with characteristic directness: "as for the lost necklace — well, I should have thought the police could have managed that" (005-001). As with many of Loveday's cases, what appears to be a straightforward [[robbery|theft]] proves to be embedded in a far more complex set of personal relationships.

## The Circumstances of Loss

The necklace was lost while [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] was a guest at the home of [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George Cathrow]], who felt a particular obligation to resolve the matter: "[[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] considered himself bound, as the necklace had been lost under my roof" (005-033). This sense of gentlemanly responsibility — the host's duty to protect a guest's property — provides the social frame for Loveday's professional engagement.

The loss of the necklace is entangled with the story's central mystery: the series of threatening drawings — [[the-daggers|drawn daggers]] — sent to [[mr-hawke|the Reverend Hawke]]. Loveday perceives a connection that others miss, noting that she cannot consider the necklace "apart from the loss of the necklace" in its wider context (005-028), and that she is "inclined to believe that the [[robbery|theft]] of the necklace and the receiving of [[the-daggers|the dagger drawings]] are closely connected" (005-029).

## Value and Significance

The necklace's monetary value is never precisely stated, and one character remarks that he "certainly did not consider the necklace was worth the fuss I was making" (005-037). This undervaluation is itself a clue — the necklace matters not for its price but for what its [[robbery|theft]] reveals about the relationships within the household.
