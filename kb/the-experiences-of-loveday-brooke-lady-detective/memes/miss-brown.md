Miss Brown is the village schoolmistress of East Downes and the daughter of [[mrs-brown|Mrs. Brown]], at whose cottage [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] lodges during her investigation in "Drawn Daggers" (Story 6).

## Introduction

Miss Brown is introduced when Loveday arrives at the Brown cottage. She is described as "a well-spoken young woman of about eight-and-twenty" [006-057], and her mother presents her "evidently with a little pride" [006-057]. The description establishes her as respectable, educated, and a credit to her mother.

## Character and Effect

Miss Brown's most notable characteristic is the repressive effect her presence has on her mother's conversation. [[mr-clampe|Mr. Clampe]] had warned Loveday that if left alone, [[mrs-brown|Mrs. Brown]] would "run on like a mill-stream, and tell you as much as you'll want to know about everyone and everything" [006-056]. However, during tea:

> Conversation flagged somewhat; Miss Brown's presence evidently acted repressively on her mother, and it was not until the meal was over and Loveday was being shown to her room by Mrs. Brown that opportunity to continue the talk was found. [006-063]

This dynamic suggests Miss Brown either disapproves of her mother's gossiping or simply possesses a dignity that naturally restrains it. She serves tea — "in about a quarter of an hour's time, Miss Brown came in with the tea-tray in her hand" [006-061] — fulfilling the domestic duties expected of a daughter in the household.

## Significance

Miss Brown is a minor background figure who contributes to the village atmosphere of East Downes and the portrait of the Brown household. Her role is functional: her presence at tea creates the necessary pause in [[mrs-brown|Mrs. Brown]]'s flow of gossip, allowing the narrative to shift scenes naturally from the tea table to Loveday's room, where more private conversation can resume.
