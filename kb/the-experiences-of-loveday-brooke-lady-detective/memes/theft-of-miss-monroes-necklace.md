# Theft of Miss Monroe's Necklace

The **[[robbery|theft]] of [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]]'s necklace** was a case investigated by [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] in "[[drawn-daggers|Drawn Daggers]]." [[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]], the daughter of [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] Monroe of Peking, had her valuable diamond necklace stolen while staying at the home of the [[mr-hawke|Rev. Anthony Hawke]], in a case intertwined with threatening drawings of [[the-daggers|daggers]].

## Background

[[miss-monroe|Miss Monroe]] had been sent from Peking to England by her father, [[sir-george-cathrow|Sir George]] Monroe, to place her under the guardianship of the [[mr-hawke|Rev. Anthony Hawke]] — partly to distance her from an undesirable suitor. She brought with her a valuable diamond necklace, which her father had notified [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] to expect.

> "Sir George wrote to tell me that his daughter was bringing her necklace with her and that he would feel obliged if I would keep it safely for her until her return to Peking." [005-041]

Shortly after her arrival at [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]]'s residence, the necklace went missing. The loss was compounded by the arrival of threatening drawings — first a single dagger, then two [[the-daggers|daggers]] drawn on paper — sent through the post from Cork, Ireland.

## The Daggers

The [[drawn-daggers-incident|dagger drawings]] formed a puzzling secondary element of the case. Two separate envelopes arrived, both addressed to [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]] in a "round, school-boyish, copybook sort of hand" [005-019] and bearing Cork and [[london|London]] postmarks.

[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] examined both drawings with her magnifying glass and determined they were drawn by different hands: "Dagger number one was, evidently, drawn by a timid, uncertain and inartistic hand — see how the lines wave and how they have been patched here and there. The person who drew the other [[the-daggers|daggers]], I should say, could do better work; the outline, though rugged, is bold and free." [005-022]

## Investigation

[[ebenezer-dyer|Mr. Dyer]] initially believed [[the-daggers|the dagger drawings]] and the necklace [[robbery|theft]] were separate matters: "I am inclined to believe that when we have gone a little further into the matter we shall find that each circumstance belongs to a different group of facts." [005-028]

[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] disagreed with this assessment. Her investigation traced the connection between the two elements, uncovering a plot involving multiple parties — including [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]]'s own nephew — who had conspired to steal the necklace while using [[the-daggers|the dagger drawings]] to create confusion and fear.

## Resolution

The case was resolved when Loveday identified that the necklace had been taken by someone within the household and linked [[the-daggers|the dagger drawings]] to the conspiracy. The necklace was recovered, and Loveday noted pointedly to [[mr-hawke|Mr. Hawke]]: "I think I have to thank those '[[the-daggers|drawn daggers]],' that caused you so much concern" [005-120] for providing the clue that broke the case.

The resolution confirmed Loveday's principle that the obvious explanation is not always correct — as she told [[ebenezer-dyer|Mr. Dyer]]: "Sometimes the explanation that is obvious is the one to be rejected, not accepted." [005-002]
