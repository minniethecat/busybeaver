## Overview

Mr. Jameson is a "wealthy man and very popular in the neighbourhood" [003-023] whose residence, [[north-cape|North Cape]], becomes the target of the [[robbery|burglary]] gang that [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] and [[inspector-gunning|Inspector Gunning]] are tracking. His house is notable for being "lighted throughout with electricity" [003-141] — a modern convenience that the burglars attempt to exploit.

## Role in "The Redhill Sisterhood"

Jameson's house, [[north-cape|North Cape]], is marked on [[inspector-gunning|Inspector Gunning]]'s specially prepared map [003-044] as one of the country houses potentially at risk. The description of his "cups and epergnes" [003-023] — trophies and ornamental silver centerpieces — establishes him as a man of substantial possessions worth stealing.

When the burglars strike, Jameson proves more than a passive victim. Having "received timely warning from the police of the intended attack, he, with his two sons, all well armed, sat in darkness in the inner hall awaiting the coming of the thieves" [003-141].

## The Burglary

The gang's plan was sophisticated: "two being told off to enter and rob the house, and two to remain at the engine-shed, where the electricity is stored, so that, at a given signal, should need arise, the wires might be unswitched, the inmates of the house thrown into sudden darkness and confusion" [003-141].

But Jameson was prepared. When the burglars entered through a servants' staircase window, the police cut off their retreat while "Mr. Jameson and his two sons, at the same moment, attacked them in front, and thus overwhelmed by numbers, the scoundrels were easily secured" [003-141].

Jameson and his sons also provided "timely assistance" at the engine-house, where the sharpest struggle took place — "it is more than likely that one of the burglars, a powerfully-built man, would have escaped" without their help [003-141].

## Characteristics

Jameson represents the active, public-spirited country gentleman — wealthy, popular, and willing to personally defend his property alongside the police. His preparedness (arming himself and his sons, cooperating with police intelligence) contrasts with the passively victimized households in other stories and contributes to the successful capture of the entire gang.

## The Electrically-Lit House

[[north-cape|North Cape]]'s electrical lighting is a plot-significant detail. The burglars' plan to "unswitch the wires" to create darkness for their escape demonstrates how new technology could be exploited by criminals. Jameson's house thus becomes a case study in the intersection of modern convenience and criminal innovation in late-Victorian detective fiction.
