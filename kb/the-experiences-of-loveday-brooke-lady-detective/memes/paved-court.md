## Overview

Paved Court is a narrow blind alley leading off the [[london|London]] and [[brighton|Brighton coach road]], in the vicinity of [[redhill|Redhill]]. It is described as a poor, neglected turning where the suspected headquarters of the [[redhill-sisterhood-burglaries|Redhill Sisterhood]] [[robbery|burglary]] ring — numbers 7 and 8 — are located. [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] conducts sur[[disguise|veil]]lance on Paved Court as part of her investigation.

## Location

Paved Court branches off the [[london|London]] and [[brighton|Brighton coach road]]. It is situated in a poor district, its location marked by the lighted shops where the thoroughfare diverges.

> "A narrow turning known as Paved Court, a narrow turning leading off the London and Brighton coach road." [003-004]

> "The lighted shops showed her where Paved Court led off from the thoroughfare." [003-028]

## Description

Paved Court is characterized as a blind alley in a neglected quarter. Despite its poverty, it is the focus of [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s sur[[disguise|veil]]lance because it contains the two houses — numbers 7 and 8 — believed to be the operational base of the [[redhill|Redhill]] [[the-redhill-sisterhood|Sisterhood]].

> "Her eyes fixed upon numbers 7 and 8 Paved Court, not so much as a door opening or a footstep sounding on the pavement escaped her." [003-039]

The alley's blind-end configuration makes it ideal for covert observation — there is only one way in and out, allowing Loveday to monitor all movement.

## Significance

Paved Court represents the "hidden in plain sight" locations that recur in Victorian detective fiction — a nondescript alley that conceals a criminal enterprise. [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s patient sur[[disguise|veil]]lance of this single location, waiting for any sign of activity, demonstrates her methodical approach to investigation. The contrast between the poverty of Paved Court and the wealthy country houses targeted by the [[the-redhill-sisterhood|Sisterhood]] underscores the social dimensions of the crime.
