# Alexander Henderson

Alexander Henderson — "usually known as old Sandy — lodge-keeper to [[mr-craven|Mr. Craven]], of [[troyte-s-hill|Troyte's Hill, Cumberland]]" (002-006) — is the murder victim whose death drives the second [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] story. His [[murder|killing]] and Loveday's subsequent investigation reveal the dark secrets concealed beneath the respectable surface of the Craven family, and his character — reconstructed through the [[evidence|testimony]] of those who knew him — emerges as a complex figure whose position of trust became the cause of his death.

## Role in the Stories

Sandy appears only as a victim — dead before the story begins. Yet his character is carefully reconstructed through [[witness|witness testimony]]. He "occupied alone, having neither kith nor kin of any degree" (002-006) a two-room lodge on the [[troyte-s-hill|Craven estate]], his isolation making him both vulnerable and, in some sense, powerful. As a lodge-keeper with intimate knowledge of the family's movements and secrets, Sandy held a form of influence that "was not altogether a favourite among them, on account of the overbearing manner in which he used his influence with his master" (002-008).

## Key Appearances

The discovery of Sandy's body establishes the story's atmosphere. "On the morning of September 6th, some children going up to the house with milk from the farm, noticed that Sandy's bedroom window stood wide open. Curiosity prompted them to peep in" (002-006), and what they found set in motion the investigation that would ultimately expose [[mr-craven|old Mr. Craven]]'s concealed insanity.

## Relationships

Sandy's relationship with the Craven household was defined by the tension between service and power. As a confidential servant, he knew the family's private affairs; as a man with "neither kith nor kin," he had no social ties to restrain his use of that knowledge. His conflict with young [[harry-craven|Harry Craven]] — the "terrible scene that occurred between them only the day before the murder" (002-028) — was the visible manifestation of deeper tensions that ran through the household.

## Significance

Sandy's murder serves as the catalyst that forces the Craven family's secrets into the open. A victim who stood at the intersection of class, service, and family dysfunction, his death — and Loveday's solution of it — demonstrates how the most isolated figures in a household may hold the keys to its darkest truths.
