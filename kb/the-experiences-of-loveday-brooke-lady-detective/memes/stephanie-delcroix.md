# Stephanie Delcroix

Stephanie Delcroix is the young French lady's maid at [[craigen-court|Craigen Court]] whose position and circumstances make her the immediate focus of suspicion after the [[robbery|jewel robbery]]. Her case illustrates a recurring pattern in [[the-experiences-of-loveday-brooke-lady-detective|the Loveday Brooke stories]]: the vulnerable servant whose foreignness, emotional temperament, and access make her the convenient target of official suspicion, and whose innocence Loveday must establish before the real criminals can be found.

## Role in the Stories

Stephanie appears in "[[the-black-bag|The Black Bag]] Left on a Doorstep," where she is suspected of complicity in the [[craigen-court|Craigen Court]] [[robbery|jewel robbery]]. Her role as [[lady-cathrow|Lady Cathrow]]'s maid gave her access to the dressing room and the safe: "it was her duty to clear the dressing-room directly after her mistress left it; put away any jewellery that might be lying about, lock the safe, and keep the key till her mistress came up to bed" (001-010). On the night of the robbery, however, she admits that "instead of so doing, directly her mistress left the dressing-room, she ran down to the housekeeper's room to see if any letters had come for her, and remained chatting with the other servants" (001-010) — a dereliction of duty that made the robbery possible.

## Under Suspicion

The official investigation concentrates on Stephanie and her romantic entanglements. "They are instituting a keen enquiry as to the possible lovers the girl may have" (001-014), and her relationship with [[harry-emmett|Harry Emmett]], the charming footman, appears to provide motive and opportunity. Her behaviour under suspicion — "she goes from one fit of hysterics into another" (001-012) — does nothing to help her case, and "is not calculated to remove suspicion" (001-012).

## Loveday's Vindication

Loveday, working [[disguise|undercover]] within the household, takes a different view. She recognizes that Stephanie's emotional collapse is consistent with innocence rather than guilt — the distress of a young woman who knows she is suspected and cannot prove her innocence. Loveday's investigation ultimately traces the real criminals — [[harry-emmett|Emmett]] and his accomplices — and establishes that Stephanie was their unwitting dupe rather than their [[accomplice|co-conspirator]].

## Significance

Stephanie represents the vulnerable position of the foreign servant in the Victorian country house — isolated by language and culture, dependent on the goodwill of her employers, and the easiest target when something goes wrong. Her case demonstrates Loveday's commitment to clearing the innocent even when the evidence of circumstance points convincingly toward their guilt.
