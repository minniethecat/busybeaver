Mrs. Greenhow is a widow who, for about a year before the events of "Missing!", has served as housekeeper at [[langford-hall|Langford Hall]] and chaperon to [[miss-golding|Irené Golding]]. She is engaged to marry [[mr-golding|Richard Golding]], becoming the second [[mrs-golding|Mrs. Golding]] — a prospect that has created considerable tension with his daughter [007-015].

## Role in the Stories

Mrs. Greenhow occupies an ambiguous and somewhat suspicious position in "Missing!" As the woman who stands to benefit from [[miss-golding|Irené Golding]]'s removal from the household, she is naturally a person of interest in the investigation. Her relationship with the missing girl is described as strained — [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s "hasty, imperious temper" overwhelmed her, and she was "quite incapable of holding her own with her" [007-015].

## Key Appearances

Mrs. Greenhow appears throughout "Missing!":

- She is first described by [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]] as [[mr-golding|Mr. Golding]]'s intended bride and the chaperon who "did not get on at all well" with [[irene-golding|Irené]] [007-015]
- She greets [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] upon her arrival at [[langford-hall|Langford Hall]], suggesting tea and rest before discussing the case [007-044]
- She repeatedly interjects during conversations about the missing girl, undermining [[mr-golding|Mr. Golding]]'s more generous accounts [007-050]
- She pointedly brings up [[mr-gordon-cleeve|Gordon Cleeve]]'s farewell note and [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s rejection of him [007-056]
- After the tragic discovery of [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s body, Loveday observes that Mrs. Greenhow is essentially "hard-natured" despite her soft exterior, and would have "ordered bonfires and a general rejoicing" if she dared [007-138]

## Biography

Mrs. Greenhow is a widow who entered the Golding household approximately one year before the events of the story. She serves the dual role of housekeeper and chaperon to [[miss-golding|Irené Golding]]. Her engagement to [[mr-golding|Mr. Golding]] was to culminate in marriage, and she was scheduled to leave [[langford-hall|Langford Hall]] that very month to make preparations for the wedding — plans that were disrupted by [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s disappearance [007-015].

Her position in the household is complex: she is neither a servant nor yet the mistress, but occupies an intermediate status that [[irene-golding|Irené]], with her imperious temperament, apparently refused to respect. The dynamic between the two women suggests a household in quiet turmoil before the disappearance.

## Characteristics

[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s first impression of Mrs. Greenhow is notably sharp. She is described as "a small, slight woman, with fluffy hair and green-grey eyes" whose "voice suggested a purr; her eyes, a scratch" — a memorable feline comparison that signals duplicity [007-044]. Loveday immediately classifies her as "Cat-tribe! the velvet paw and the hidden claw — the exact antithesis, I should say, to one of [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]]'s temperament" [007-044].

Her manner of speech is characterized by a "sweet" tone that belies the sharpness of her observations. She undermines [[mr-golding|Mr. Golding]]'s fond descriptions of his daughter with pointed corrections — noting that [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s portrait was uncharacteristically flattering, and reminding the company of [[mr-gordon-cleeve|Gordon Cleeve]]'s rejected overtures [007-050].

Later in the case, Loveday's assessment hardens: she describes Mrs. Greenhow as "a terribly empty-headed, but at the same time essentially hard-natured little woman" [007-138]. She observes that the woman is "unfeignedly glad at what she considers the ending of the affair" and suspects she would have openly celebrated [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s death.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-golding|Richard Golding]]** — her employer and fiancé
- **[[miss-golding|Irené Golding]]** — her charge, with whom she had a deeply antagonistic relationship
- **[[lord-guilleroy|Lord Guilleroy]]** — she speaks of him with approval, welcoming his involvement
- **[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]** — the detective, whom she views with growing suspicion and impatience
- **Maddalena (Lena)** — [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s maid, about whom Mrs. Greenhow provides information to the investigation

## Significance

Mrs. Greenhow represents the archetype of the unsympathetic stepmother figure in Victorian fiction — superficially pleasant but fundamentally self-interested. Her "velvet paw and hidden claw" characterization makes her a natural suspect in the reader's mind, and her barely concealed relief at [[irene-golding|Irené]]'s death, while not proving criminal involvement, reveals a hardness of character that contrasts sharply with [[mr-golding|Mr. Golding]]'s devastated grief. She serves as a reminder that the domestic tensions underlying a mystery may be as significant as the crime itself.
