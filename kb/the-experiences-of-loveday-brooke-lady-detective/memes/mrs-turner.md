Mrs. Turner is the young wife of the Reverend [[mr-turner|Charles Turner]], Vicar of East Downes. She is the central suspect in the cheque fraud case that initially brings [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] to East Downes in "[[the-ghost-of-fountain-lane|The Ghost of Fountain Lane]]." Married to the Vicar for approximately six months, she is described as a "decidedly handsome" but "hard-featured" woman with a "scornful curl to her upper lip" [006-041].

## Role in the Stories

Mrs. Turner is a suspect in the [[robbery|theft]] and fraudulent cashing of one of her husband's signed blank cheques for £600. The cheque was presented and cashed in gold during the Vicar's absence from home following a relative's death. [[investigation|Investigation]] reveals that Mrs. Turner paid off nearly £500 in pre-marriage debts in gold during the same period [006-017]. Her role in the story is intertwined with the deeper mystery of [[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]], whom the Vicar continues to employ at £30 a year despite his wife's refusal to have anything to do with her [006-057].

## Key Appearances

Mrs. Turner appears in the background of "[[the-ghost-of-fountain-lane|The Ghost of Fountain Lane]]":

- She is first introduced through the cheque case described by [[mr-clampe|Mr. Clampe]]: her husband left her four signed blank cheques, and she initially claimed to have locked them away immediately, then corrected her statement [006-007]
- She is observed driving past in an open carriage with her husband, dressed "in the extreme of [[london|London]] fashion" [006-041]
- [[mrs-brown|Mrs. Brown]] provides extensive local gossip about her — she drives "regularly every Sunday into [[brighton|Brighton]] to a Catholic Church to say her prayers to candles and images," scandalizing the Wesleyan sensibilities of the village [006-066]
- She refuses to employ [[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]], the former maid of her late mother-in-law, leaving the Vicar to pay Maria £30 a year "for doing nothing" [006-063]

## Biography

Mrs. Turner married the Reverend [[mr-turner|Charles Turner]] approximately six months before the events of the story. Before her marriage, she contracted debts in [[brighton|Brighton]] amounting to nearly £500 — debts she paid off in gold during her husband's absence [006-017]. Her background appears to be more metropolitan and fashionable than rural East Downes, and her [[london|London]]-style dress and Catholic religious practices mark her as an outsider in the predominantly Wesleyan village.

The late Mrs. Turner — the Vicar's mother — was a woman of "low" church sympathies, "almost chapel," who regularly attended the Countess of Huntingdon's church in [[brighton|Brighton]] [006-066]. The contrast between the old Mrs. Turner's evangelical piety and the young Mrs. Turner's Roman Catholic leanings is a source of village gossip and disapproval.

## Characteristics

Mrs. Turner is physically "a decidedly handsome woman" but "hard-featured" with a "scornful curl to her upper lip" [006-041]. [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] is "not favourably impressed" with her appearance. She dresses "in the extreme of [[london|London]] fashion," setting her apart from the rural community [006-041].

Her most notable characteristic, from the village perspective, is her religious nonconformity — but of an unexpected kind. Whereas the late Mrs. Turner's "low" church tendencies pushed toward Nonconformity, the young Mrs. Turner has embraced Catholicism, driving to [[brighton|Brighton]] every Sunday for Mass [006-066]. This reversal scandalizes the village in a way that marks the generational and cultural gap between the old Vicar's mother and the new Vicar's wife.

Her refusal to employ [[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]] — "she's not good enough for her" — suggests a certain social fastidiousness, though the true reason for her reluctance may be more complex than mere snobbery [006-063].

## Relationships

- **Reverend [[mr-turner|Charles Turner]]** — her husband, the Vicar of East Downes
- **[[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]]** — former maid to the late Mrs. Turner, whom she refuses to employ
- **The late Mrs. Turner** — her mother-in-law, a woman of evangelical leanings
- **[[mrs-brown|Mrs. Brown]]** — village gossip who disapproves of her Catholicism

## Significance

Mrs. Turner represents the theme of appearance versus reality that runs through [[the-experiences-of-loveday-brooke-lady-detective|the Loveday Brooke stories]]. On the surface, she is the obvious suspect in a straightforward case of cheque fraud — a woman with debts who had access to blank cheques and paid those debts in gold. But the deeper mystery of the story concerns not her financial dishonesty but the strange behaviour of [[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]], which connects to the "ghost" of the title. Mrs. Turner's conspicuous difference from village norms — her fashion, her religion, her cold manner — makes her a red herring whose obvious suspiciousness masks the true strangeness at the heart of the case.
