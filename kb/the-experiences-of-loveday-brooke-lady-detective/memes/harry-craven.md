Harry Craven is the son of [[mr-craven|old Mr. Craven]] of [[troyte-s-hill|Troyte's Hill]] and the central suspect in the murder of [[alexander-henderson|Sandy]], the lodge-keeper, in "[[the-murder-at-troytes-hill|The Murder at Troyte's Hill]]" (Story 2). Though initially believed guilty, he is ultimately exonerated by [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s investigation.

## The Murder Case

[[inspector-griffiths|Inspector Griffiths]] builds a strong circumstantial case against Harry Craven. He discovers that [[harry-emmett|Harry]] and [[alexander-henderson|Sandy]] "stood towards each other" on bad terms, and that "a terrible scene occurred between them only the day before the murder" [002-028]. More damningly, [[inspector-griffiths|Griffiths]] learns that "on the night of the murder he left the house shortly after ten o'clock, and no one, so far as I have been able to ascertain, knows at what hour he returned" [002-028]. The medical evidence placed the time of death between ten and eleven at night.

## Flight Under an Alias

As the evidence mounts, Harry Craven flees. He visits a low-class inn run by McQueen shortly after eleven o'clock on the night of the murder, "bringing with him a valuable piece of plate — a handsome epergne — and requested him to lend him a hundred pounds on it" [002-117]. McQueen gives him only ten sovereigns. [[harry-emmett|Harry]] then sails from [[london|London Docks]] for Natal under the alias "Harold Cousins" in the Bonnie Dundee [002-186].

His mother, who "had passed the greater part of her life" in Natal [002-187], is the one who "got her scapegrace son among her early friends" [002-187].

## Loveday's Exoneration

Loveday discovers that it was not [[harry-emmett|Harry]] but his father — [[mr-craven|old Mr. Craven]] — who committed the murder. On his way back to the house after pawning the family plate, [[harry-emmett|Harry]] likely encountered his father with the murder weapon. Loveday explains:

> The son, most likely, on his way back to the house after pledging the family plate, had met old Mr. Craven with the hammer in his hand. Seeing, no doubt, how impossible it would be for him to clear himself without incriminating his father, he preferred flight to Natal to giving evidence at the inquest. [002-185]

## Significance

Harry Craven is a classic red-herring suspect in [[the-experiences-of-loveday-brooke-lady-detective|the Loveday Brooke stories]]. The circumstantial evidence against him appears overwhelming — bad blood with the victim, a suspicious absence at the time of the murder, pawning family valuables, and fleeing the country under a false name. Yet Loveday sees through the evidence to the truth, identifying [[mr-craven|old Mr. Craven]] as the real killer and the son as a man protecting his father.
