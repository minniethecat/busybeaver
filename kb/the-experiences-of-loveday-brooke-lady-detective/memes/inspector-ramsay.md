Inspector Ramsay is the local police inspector stationed at [[langford-cross|Langford Cross]], [[langford-cross|Leicestershire]], who brings the case of [[miss-golding|Irené Golding]]'s disappearance to [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]'s agency. He is described as "a lanky, bony Scotchman, sandy-haired and slow of speech" [007-031].

## Role in the Stories

Inspector Ramsay serves as the official police presence in "Missing!" and the conduit through which the case reaches [[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]]'s Agency. After ten days of fruitless investigation by the local police — during which "every river, far and near has been dragged; every wood scoured; every railway official at every station for miles round has been driven nearly mad" — he writes to [[ebenezer-dyer|Dyer]] requesting [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s services [007-006].

## Key Appearances

Inspector Ramsay appears in the early chapters of "Missing!":

- He writes to [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]] requesting Loveday's involvement, suggesting the investigation should look "within" the house rather than only outside it [007-006]
- He meets Loveday at [[langford-cross|Langford Cross Station]] upon her arrival, alongside [[lord-guilleroy|Lord Guilleroy]] [007-031]
- He declines to share any theory of his own, stating he would "rather not bias your mind in any direction by any theory of mine" — a sentiment Loveday privately considers would be "rather a waste of time to attempt" [007-031]
- He has already traced [[mr-gordon-cleeve|Gordon Cleeve]]'s movements to Brindisi and confirmed his departure for Alexandria, ruling him out as a suspect [007-019]
- He is noted to have made "such a dead set at Lena," the maid Maddalena, that "the girl felt herself insulted, grew sullen, and refused to open her lips" [007-063]
- Later in the investigation, he traces the missing girl's movements from the [[paris|Gare du Midi]] in [[paris|Paris]] "step by step to her arrival at [[langford-cross|Langford Cross]]" [007-136]
- He is "a little inclined to criticize" what he calls Loveday's "leisurely handling" of the case [007-138]

## Biography

Inspector Ramsay is a Scotsman serving as the chief police inspector for the [[langford-cross|Langford Cross]] district in [[langford-cross|Leicestershire]]. His professional approach reflects the methodical but sometimes heavy-handed style of provincial policing — thorough in physical searches but less adept at the psychological and domestic dimensions of investigation that are [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s specialty.

## Characteristics

Ramsay is physically distinctive: "lanky, bony," with sandy hair and a notably slow, sententious manner of speech [007-031]. His professional caution manifests as a reluctance to share theories or "bias" an investigator's mind — though Loveday privately considers this more a cover for having no theory at all.

His [[detective-methodology|investigative method]]s are direct and physical: dragging rivers, scouring woods, cross-questioning railway officials. When these exhaustive external searches yield nothing, he belatedly recognizes that "a promising field for investigation may lie within" the house itself — prompting him to call in Loveday [007-006]. His heavy-handed approach to the servants, particularly Lena, is criticized as counterproductive [007-063].

Despite his limitations, he is capable of effective police work: his tracing of [[miss-golding|Miss Golding]]'s movements from [[paris|Paris]] back to [[langford-cross|Langford Cross]] represents solid, methodical [[investigation|detective work]].

## Relationships

- **[[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]]** — the agency head he contacts for assistance
- **[[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]** — the detective he enlists, whose methods he later critiques
- **[[lord-guilleroy|Lord Guilleroy]]** — the local nobleman with whom he coordinates
- **M. Dulau** — his counterpart in the [[paris|Paris police]]
- **Lena (Maddalena)** — the Golding maid he alienates through aggressive interrogation

## Significance

Inspector Ramsay represents the contrast between official police methodology and [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]]'s more subtle, domestic approach to detection. His failure to solve the case through exhaustive external searching — and his belated recognition that the solution may lie within the household — validates the necessity of the private detective's psychological and social intelligence. His later criticism of Loveday's methods ("leisurely handling") illustrates the institutional tension between the visible, physical work of policing and the less visible, more cerebral work of detection that Loveday practices.
