# West Sussex Bank

The West Sussex Bank is the financial institution at the centre of the [[ch06-the-ghost-of-fountain-lane|The Ghost of Fountain Lane]] case, the sixth [[loveday-brooke|Loveday Brooke]] story. The [[robbery|theft]] of a cheque "drawn on the West Sussex Bank" (006-009) triggers the investigation that leads Loveday to uncover a complex [[fraud|fraudulent scheme]] involving forged signatures, a staged ghostly apparition, and a network of conspirators in [[brighton|Brighton]].

## The Stolen Cheque

The case begins when the Revered [[charles-turner|Charles Turner]], Vicar of East Downes, discovers that a cheque for a substantial sum has been stolen and presented at the Bank. The cheque, which was the "property of the [[mr-turner|Rev. Charles Turner]]" (006-009), bore the signatures of the executors of a recently deceased parishioner's estate. The Bank accepted the cheque and paid out in gold, apparently without detecting the forgery. [[ebenezer-dyer|Ebenezer Dyer]] is engaged to investigate, and the question of who bears the financial loss — "the Bank or [[mr-turner|Mr. Turner]]?" (006-026) — becomes an early point of tension.

[[mrs-turner|Mrs. Turner]], acting in her husband's absence from home, "paid off certain debts contracted in her husband's name" (006-029) using the proceeds — a detail that complicates the investigation and initially casts suspicion upon the vicarage household. The Bank's acceptance of the forged cheque, and the subsequent payment in gold, makes it both victim and unwitting facilitator of the crime.

## The Bank in Victorian Provincial Life

The West Sussex Bank represents the kind of county banking establishment that served provincial communities in Victorian England. Unlike the great [[london|London]] banks, a county bank like West Sussex operated on personal knowledge and local [[respectability|reputation]]. The Turners' connection to the Bank was presumably of long standing — the vicar would have been a known and trusted customer — which makes the [[fraud|forgery]] both easier to execute and harder to detect. The case illustrates the vulnerabilities of a banking system that relied on personal recognition rather than systematic verification of signatures.

## Role in the Investigation

Loveday's approach to the Bank is characteristically indirect. Rather than beginning with the Bank's records, she investigates the social world surrounding the vicarage — the elderly [[mrs-turner|Mrs. Turner]] the elder, her former maid [[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]], and the [[wesleyan-chapel-gordon-street|Wesleyan chapel]] in Gordon Street where the conspirators gathered. The Bank is the scene of the crime but not the scene of Loveday's [[investigation]], which unfolds in the homes, chapels, and lodging-houses of [[brighton|Brighton]].
