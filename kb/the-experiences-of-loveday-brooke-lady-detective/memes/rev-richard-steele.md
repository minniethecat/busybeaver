The Rev. Richard Steele is a former Wesleyan minister who appears in "Drawn Daggers" (Story 6) as part of the fraudulent scheme to extract money from [[mrs-turner|Mrs. Turner]]. He is one of three conspirators for whom Loveday secures [[warrant|arrest warrant]]s.

## Background

Steele was formerly "Wesleyan minister of a chapel in Gordon Street, [[brighton|Brighton]]" [006-101]. At some point he was "inhibited" — barred from preaching — and left the established Wesleyan communion. Together with [[john-rogers|John Rogers]], a former elder of the same chapel who had "seceded from their communion" [006-135], Steele embarked on a new career as an itinerant apocalyptic preacher.

## The Doomsday Scheme

Steele and Rogers "were now going about the country preaching that the world would come to an end on Thursday, April 11th, 1901" [006-135]. Their message included the promise that true believers "would be translated without tasting death" and that "this translation would take place in the land of Judaea" [006-135]. A society was supposedly formed to provide homes for the multitudes of saints expected to gather there.

The scheme's true purpose was financial: "subscriptions for this society would be gladly received by either gentleman" [006-135]. The stolen cheque from the Turner household, marked with the pencilled figures "144,000" — the number of the elect from the Book of Revelation — was traced to this conspiracy.

## Hypnotic Influence

[[mr-clampe|Mr. Clampe]] suggests Steele possessed a kind of "hypnotic power (unconsciously exercised)" [006-147] over his followers. Loveday does not entirely dismiss this theory, though she challenges it with a broader observation about the contagious nature of belief:

> "Don't you think that ghost-seeing is quite as catching as scarlet-fever or measles? Let one member of a family see a much individualized and easily described ghost, and ten to one others in the same house will see it before the week is over." [006-148]

## Arrest

At the conclusion of the case, Loveday directs that [[warrant|warrants]] be issued for three people: [[maria-lisle|Maria Lisle]], Richard Steele, and [[john-rogers|John Rogers]] [006-101]. The discovery of the conspiracy is, as Loveday notes, thanks to "that ridiculous ghost in [[fountain-lane|Fountain Lane]]" [006-101] — the staged apparition that first drew her attention to the criminal scheme.

## Significance

Richard Steele represents the type of religious fraud that preyed on vulnerable people in Victorian England. His apocalyptic preaching, combined with the hypnotic influence of his personality, enabled a sophisticated financial crime. He and his [[accomplice|co-conspirator]]s exploited the religious anxieties of the period — specifically the expectation of the world's end — for profit.
