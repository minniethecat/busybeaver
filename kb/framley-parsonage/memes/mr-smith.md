# Mr. Smith

Mr. Smith is a Member of Parliament and would-be orator whose comic lecture on the geography of the South Sea islands at Barchester serves as one of the novel's set-pieces of social satire. He is a political ally of [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]] and moves within the Chaldicotes set, though his earnest self-importance makes him a figure of gentle ridicule.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Smith appears primarily in connection with the Chaldicotes circle, to which he is attached through political and social ties. It is said of him that he "had not quite fulfilled the prophecies of his early youth" — a phrase that captures the mixture of ambition and inadequacy that defines his character (004-011). He is a man who has risen to a certain station, but who, in Trollope's characteristic anatomy of political mediocrity, never quite delivers on the promise he once seemed to hold.

His great scene is the lecture at Barchester, where he takes the dais to enlighten the county on "those grand and magnificent islands which lie far away, beyond the Indies, in the Southern Ocean" (013-047). The lecture, attended by the bishop, Mrs. Proudie, Mark Robarts, and the Chaldicotes party, is a masterclass in comic anticlimax — Mr. Smith runs "on from platitude to truism, and from truism back to platitude" while his audience fidgets, the bishop falls asleep, and Mr. Sowerby's irreverent commentary drifts up from the front bench (013-083) (013-096).

## Character Traits

- **Ponderous earnestness**: Mr. Smith takes himself very seriously, a quality that Trollope mines for comedy. When Mr. Sowerby mutters about the hard benches, Mr. Smith "dashed his gloves down to the table" — a gesture that reveals both his sensitivity to slight and his comic lack of proportion (013-003).
- **Political ambition**: He is part of the parliamentary world that includes Mr. Supplehouse and Mr. Sowerby, and it is suggested that his political career depends on the patronage of men like the Duke of Omnium (042-127) (048-075).
- **Vulnerability**: Beneath the self-importance, Mr. Smith is vulnerable — "Poor Mr. Smith, having been so rudely dragged from his high horse" — and the narrator's sympathy, though amused, is genuine (013-136).

## Relationships

- **[[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]**: The Chaldicotes MP who is both patron and tormentor of Mr. Smith. It is Sowerby who assures Mark Robarts that "Mr. Smith will ask for it" in the matter of the money bill, and whose irreverent asides punctuate the lecture (038-092).
- **[[miss-dunstable|Miss Dunstable]]**: The heiress's teasing inquiry — "How is poor Mr. Smith to-day?" — is delivered with an air of "intense, anxious solicitude" that mocks the very concern it professes (051-009).
- **[[mrs-harold-smith|Mrs. Harold Smith]]**: Not directly related (Harold Smith is a separate character), but the shared surname creates the kind of social echo Trollope deploys throughout the Barsetshire novels.
- **Miss Jones**: Near the novel's conclusion, it is noted that Mr. Smith "in asking Miss Jones to do him the honour of becoming Mrs. Smith" provides a resolution that is, like much in his life, modest but satisfactory (056-044).

## Key Scenes

- **The Barchester lecture (Chapter 13)**: Mr. Smith's great moment on the dais, complete with maps, a drink of water, and an audience that includes sleeping bishops and snickering gentlemen. The lecture on Papua, the Philippines, Borneo and the Moluccas "with a map in his hand" is one of Trollope's finest set-pieces of institutional tedium (013-047) (013-058) (013-079).
- **The political dinner (Chapter 48)**: Mr. Smith is spoken of as one of those men "such as Mr. Smith" who sustain the political machinery of the nation — a backhanded compliment that captures both his utility and his interchangeability (048-081).
- **The Chaldicotes gathering (Chapter 12)**: Mr. Smith takes "just one glass of claret" before departing with Mr. Sowerby, a fleeting appearance that places him within the novel's social geography (012-165).

## Social & Historical Context

Mr. Smith is Trollope's portrait of the Victorian backbench MP: a man of respectable abilities and no genius, whose political career is advanced not by talent but by connection. The lecture scene is a satire not merely of one man's pomposity but of the entire machinery of provincial self-improvement — the Mechanics' Institutes, the public lectures, the maps and pointers and glasses of water that were the apparatus of mid-Victorian earnestness. That the bishop falls asleep and Mr. Sowerby mutters irreverently are not exceptions to the lecture's spirit but its truest audience response. Trollope, who sat in the Commons himself, knew the type intimately and drew it with the affection of recognition (013-077) (013-096).
