## Section 65

[065-001]“Your hero, then,” I hear some well-balanced critic say, “is not

[065-002]worth very much.”

[065-003]In the first place Lord Lufton is not my hero; and in the next place,

[065-004]a man may be very imperfect and yet worth a great deal. A man may be

[065-005]as imperfect as Lord Lufton, and yet worthy of a good mother and a

[065-006]good wife. If not, how many of us are unworthy of the mothers and

[065-007]wives we have! It is my belief that few young men settle themselves

[065-008]down to the work of the world, to the begetting of children, and

[065-009]carving and paying and struggling and fretting for the same, without

[065-010]having first been in love with four or five possible mothers for

[065-011]them, and probably with two or three at the same time. And yet these

[065-012]men are, as a rule, worthy of the excellent wives that ultimately

[065-013]fall to their lot. In this way Lord Lufton had, to a certain extent,

[065-014]been in love with Griselda. There had been one moment in his life

[065-015]in which he would have offered her his hand, had not her discretion

[065-016]been so excellent; and though that moment never returned, still he

[065-017]suffered from some feeling akin to disappointment when he learned

[065-018]that Griselda had been won and was to be worn. He was, then, a dog

[065-019]in the manger, you will say. Well; and are we not all dogs in the

[065-020]manger, more or less actively? Is not that manger-doggishness one of

[065-021]the most common phases of the human heart?

[065-022]But not the less was Lord Lufton truly in love with Lucy Robarts.

[065-023]Had he fancied that any Dumbello was carrying on a siege before

[065-024]that fortress, his vexation would have manifested itself in a very

[065-025]different manner. He could joke about Griselda Grantly with a frank

[065-026]face and a happy tone of voice; but had he heard of any tidings of a

[065-027]similar import with reference to Lucy, he would have been past all

[065-028]joking, and I much doubt whether it would not even have affected his

[065-029]appetite.

[065-030]“Mother,” he said to Lady Lufton a day or two after the declaration

[065-031]of Griselda’s engagement, “I am going to Norway to fish.”

[065-032]“To Norway,—to fish!”

[065-033]“Yes. We’ve got rather a nice party. Clontarf is going, and

[065-034]Culpepper—”

[065-035]“What, that horrid man!”

[065-036]“He’s an excellent hand at fishing;—and Haddington Peebles,

[065-037]and—and—there’ll be six of us altogether; and we start this day

[065-038]week.”

[065-039]“That’s rather sudden, Ludovic.”

[065-040]“Yes, it is sudden; but we’re sick of London. I should not care to

[065-041]go so soon myself, but Clontarf and Culpepper say that the season

[065-042]is early this year. I must go down to Framley before I start—about

[065-043]my horses; and therefore I came to tell you that I shall be there

[065-044]to-morrow.”

[065-045]“At Framley to-morrow! If you could put it off for three days I

[065-046]should be going myself.”

[065-047]But Lord Lufton could not put it off for three days. It may be that

[065-048]on this occasion he did not wish for his mother’s presence at Framley

[065-049]while he was there; that he conceived that he should be more at his

[065-050]ease in giving orders about his stable if he were alone while so

[065-051]employed. At any rate he declined her company, and on the following

[065-052]morning did go down to Framley by himself.

[065-053]“Mark,” said Mrs. Robarts, hurrying into her husband’s book-room

[065-054]about the middle of the day, “Lord Lufton is at home. Have you heard

[065-055]it?”

[065-056]“What! here at Framley?”

[065-057]“He is over at Framley Court; so the servants say. Carson saw him in

[065-058]the paddock with some of the horses. Won’t you go and see him?”

[065-059]“Of course I will,” said Mark, shutting up his papers. “Lady Lufton

[065-060]can’t be here, and if he is alone he will probably come and dine.”

[065-061]“I don’t know about that,” said Mrs. Robarts, thinking of poor Lucy.

[065-062]“He is not in the least particular. What does for us will do for

[065-063]him. I shall ask him, at any rate.” And without further parley the

[065-064]clergyman took up his hat and went off in search of his friend.

[065-065]Lucy Robarts had been present when the gardener brought in tidings of

[065-066]Lord Lufton’s arrival at Framley, and was aware that Fanny had gone

[065-067]to tell her husband.

[065-068]“He won’t come here, will he?” she said, as soon as Mrs. Robarts

[065-069]returned.

[065-070]“I can’t say,” said Fanny. “I hope not. He ought not to do so, and I

[065-071]don’t think he will. But Mark says that he will ask him to dinner.”

[065-072]“Then, Fanny, I must be taken ill. There is nothing else for it.”

[065-073]“I don’t think he will come. I don’t think he can be so cruel.

[065-074]Indeed, I feel sure that he won’t; but I thought it right to tell

[065-075]you.”

[065-076]Lucy also conceived that it was improbable that Lord Lufton should

[065-077]come to the parsonage under the present circumstances; and she

[065-078]declared to herself that it would not be possible that she should

[065-079]appear at table if he did do so; but, nevertheless, the idea of his

[065-080]being at Framley was, perhaps, not altogether painful to her. She did

[065-081]not recognize any pleasure as coming to her from his arrival, but

[065-082]still there was something in his presence which was, unconsciously

[065-083]to herself, soothing to her feelings. But that terrible question

[065-084]remained;—how was she to act if it should turn out that he was

[065-085]coming to dinner?

[065-086]“If he does come, Fanny,” she said, solemnly, after a pause, “I must

[065-087]keep to my own room, and leave Mark to think what he pleases. It will

[065-088]be better for me to make a fool of myself there, than in his presence

[065-089]in the drawing-room.”

[065-090]Mark Robarts took his hat and stick and went over at once to the

[065-091]home paddock, in which he knew that Lord Lufton was engaged with the

[065-092]horses and grooms. He also was in no supremely happy frame of mind,

[065-093]for his correspondence with Mr. Tozer was on the increase. He had

[065-094]received notice from that indefatigable gentleman that certain

[065-095]“overdue bills” were now lying at the bank in Barchester, and were

[065-096]very desirous of his, Mr. Robarts’s, notice. A concatenation of

[065-097]certain peculiarly unfortunate circumstances made it indispensably

[065-098]necessary that Mr. Tozer should be repaid, without further loss of

[065-099]time, the various sums of money which he had advanced on the credit

[065-100]of Mr. Robarts’s name, &c. &c. &c. No absolute threat was put forth,

[065-101]and, singular to say, no actual amount was named. Mr. Robarts,

[065-102]however, could not but observe, with a most painfully accurate

[065-103]attention, that mention was made, not of an overdue bill, but of

[065-104]overdue bills. What if Mr. Tozer were to demand from him the instant

[065-105]repayment of nine hundred pounds? Hitherto he had merely written to

[065-106]Mr. Sowerby, and he might have had an answer from that gentleman this

[065-107]morning, but no such answer had as yet reached him. Consequently he

[065-108]was not, at the present moment, in a very happy frame of mind.

[065-109]He soon found himself with Lord Lufton and the horses. Four or five

[065-110]of them were being walked slowly about the paddock in the care of as

[065-111]many men or boys, and the sheets were being taken off them—off one

[065-112]after another, so that their master might look at them with the more

[065-113]accuracy and satisfaction. But though Lord Lufton was thus doing his

[065-114]duty, and going through his work, he was not doing it with his whole

[065-115]heart,—as the head groom perceived very well. He was fretful about

[065-116]the nags, and seemed anxious to get them out of his sight as soon as

[065-117]he had made a decent pretext of looking at them.

[065-118]“How are you, Lufton?” said Robarts, coming forward. “They told me

[065-119]that you were down, and so I came across at once.”

[065-120]“Yes; I only got here this morning, and should have been over with

[065-121]you directly. I am going to Norway for six weeks or so, and it seems

[065-122]that the fish are so early this year, that we must start at once. I

[065-123]have a matter on which I want to speak to you before I leave; and,

[065-124]indeed, it was that which brought me down more than anything else.”

[065-125]There was something hurried and not altogether easy about his manner

[065-126]as he spoke, which struck Robarts, and made him think that this

[065-127]promised matter to be spoken of would not be agreeable in discussion.

[065-128]He did not know whether Lord Lufton might not again be mixed up with

[065-129]Tozer and the bills.

[065-130]“You will dine with us to-day,” he said, “if, as I suppose, you are

[065-131]all alone.”

[065-132]“Yes, I am all alone.”

[065-133]“Then you’ll come?”

[065-134]“Well, I don’t quite know. No, I don’t think I can go over to dinner.

[065-135]Don’t look so disgusted. I’ll explain it all to you just now.”

[065-136]What could there be in the wind; and how was it possible that Tozer’s

[065-137]bill should make it inexpedient for Lord Lufton to dine at the

[065-138]parsonage? Robarts, however, said nothing further about it at the

[065-139]moment, but turned off to look at the horses.

[065-140]“They are an uncommonly nice set of animals,” said he.

[065-141]“Well, yes; I don’t know. When a man has four or five horses to look

[065-142]at, somehow or other he never has one fit to go. That chestnut mare

[065-143]is a picture, now that nobody wants her; but she wasn’t able to carry

[065-144]me well to hounds a single day last winter. Take them in, Pounce;

[065-145]that’ll do.”

[065-146]“Won’t your lordship run your eye over the old black ’oss?” said

[065-147]Pounce, the head groom, in a melancholy tone; “he’s as fine, sir—as

[065-148]fine as a stag.”

[065-149]“To tell you the truth, I think they’re too fine; but that’ll do;

[065-150]take them in. And now, Mark, if you’re at leisure, we’ll take a turn

[065-151]round the place.”

[065-152]Mark, of course, was at leisure, and so they started on their walk.

[065-153]“You’re too difficult to please about your stable,” Robarts began.

[065-154]“Never mind the stable now,” said Lord Lufton. “The truth is, I am

[065-155]not thinking about it. Mark,” he then said, very abruptly, “I want

[065-156]you to be frank with me. Has your sister ever spoken to you about

[065-157]me?”

[065-158]“My sister; Lucy?”

[065-159]“Yes; your sister Lucy.”

[065-160]“No, never; at least nothing especial; nothing that I can remember at

[065-161]this moment.”

[065-162]“Nor your wife?”

[065-163]“Spoken about you!—Fanny? Of course she has, in an ordinary way. It

[065-164]would be impossible that she should not. But what do you mean?”

[065-165]“Have either of them told you that I made an offer to your sister?”

[065-166]“That you made an offer to Lucy?”

[065-167]“Yes, that I made an offer to Lucy.”

[065-168]“No; nobody has told me so. I have never dreamed of such a thing;

[065-169]nor, as far as I believe, have they. If anybody has spread such a

[065-170]report, or said that either of them have hinted at such a thing, it

[065-171]is a base lie. Good heavens! Lufton, for what do you take them?”

[065-172]“But I did,” said his lordship.

[065-173]“Did what?” said the parson.

[065-174]“I did make your sister an offer.”

[065-175]“You made Lucy an offer of marriage!”

[065-176]“Yes, I did;—in as plain language as a gentleman could use to a

[065-177]lady.”

[065-178]“And what answer did she make?”

[065-179]“She refused me. And now, Mark, I have come down here with the

[065-180]express purpose of making that offer again. Nothing could be more

[065-181]decided than your sister’s answer. It struck me as being almost

[065-182]uncourteously decided. But still it is possible that circumstances

[065-183]may have weighed with her, which ought not to weigh with her. If her

[065-184]love be not given to any one else, I may still have a chance of it.

[065-185]It’s the old story of faint heart, you know: at any rate, I mean to

[065-186]try my luck again; and thinking over it with deliberate purpose, I

[065-187]have come to the conclusion that I ought to tell you before I see

[065-188]her.”
