## Section 92

[092-001]“The bailiffs have taken possession of everything at the parsonage.”

[092-002]CHAPTER XLIV.

[092-003]THE PHILISTINES AT THE PARSONAGE.

[092-004]It has been already told how things went on between the Tozers, Mr.

[092-005]Curling, and Mark Robarts during that month. Mr. Forrest had drifted

[092-006]out of the business altogether, as also had Mr. Sowerby, as far as

[092-007]any active participation in it went. Letters came frequently from

[092-008]Mr. Curling to the parsonage, and at last came a message by special

[092-009]mission to say that the evil day was at hand. As far as Mr. Curling’s

[092-010]professional experience would enable him to anticipate or foretell

[092-011]the proceedings of such a man as Tom Tozer, he thought that the

[092-012]sheriff’s officers would be at Framley Parsonage on the following

[092-013]morning. Mr. Curling’s experience did not mislead him in this

[092-014]respect.

[092-015]“And what will you do, Mark?” said Fanny, speaking through her tears,

[092-016]after she had read the letter which her husband handed to her.

[092-017]“Nothing. What can I do? They must come.”

[092-018]“Lord Lufton came to-day. Will you not go to him?”

[092-019]“No. If I were to do so it would be the same as asking him for the

[092-020]money.”

[092-021]“Why not borrow it of him, dearest? Surely it would not be so much

[092-022]for him to lend.”

[092-023]“I could not do it. Think of Lucy, and how she stands with him.

[092-024]Besides I have already had words with Lufton about Sowerby and his

[092-025]money matters. He thinks that I am to blame, and he would tell me

[092-026]so; and then there would be sharp things said between us. He would

[092-027]advance me the money if I pressed for it, but he would do so in a way

[092-028]that would make it impossible that I should take it.”

[092-029]There was nothing more then to be said. If she had had her own way

[092-030]Mrs. Robarts would have gone at once to Lady Lufton, but she could

[092-031]not induce her husband to sanction such a proceeding. The objection

[092-032]to seeking assistance from her ladyship was as strong as that which

[092-033]prevailed as to her son. There had already been some little beginning

[092-034]of ill-feeling, and under such circumstances it was impossible to ask

[092-035]for pecuniary assistance. Fanny, however, had a prophetic assurance

[092-036]that assistance out of these difficulties must in the end come to

[092-037]them from that quarter, or not come at all; and she would fain, had

[092-038]she been allowed, make everything known at the big house.

[092-039]On the following morning they breakfasted at the usual hour, but in

[092-040]great sadness. A maid-servant, whom Mrs. Robarts had brought with her

[092-041]when she married, told her that a rumour of what was to happen had

[092-042]reached the kitchen. Stubbs, the groom, had been in Barchester on the

[092-043]preceding day, and, according to his account—so said Mary—everybody

[092-044]in the city was talking about it. “Never mind, Mary,” said Mrs.

[092-045]Robarts, and Mary replied, “Oh, no, of course not, ma’am.”

[092-046]In these days Mrs. Robarts was ordinarily very busy, seeing that

[092-047]there were six children in the house, four of whom had come to her

[092-048]but ill supplied with infantine belongings; and now, as usual, she

[092-049]went about her work immediately after breakfast. But she moved about

[092-050]the house very slowly, and was almost unable to give her orders

[092-051]to the servants, and spoke sadly to the children who hung about

[092-052]her wondering what was the matter. Her husband at the same time

[092-053]took himself to his book-room, but when there did not attempt any

[092-054]employment. He thrust his hands into his pockets, and, leaning

[092-055]against the fire-place, fixed his eyes upon the table before him

[092-056]without looking at anything that was on it; it was impossible for him

[092-057]to betake himself to his work. Remember what is the ordinary labour

[092-058]of a clergyman in his study, and think how fit he must have been for

[092-059]such employment! What would have been the nature of a sermon composed

[092-060]at such a moment, and with what satisfaction could he have used the

[092-061]sacred volume in referring to it for his arguments? He, in this

[092-062]respect, was worse off than his wife; she did employ herself, but he

[092-063]stood there without moving, doing nothing, with fixed eyes, thinking

[092-064]what men would say of him.

[092-065]Luckily for him this state of suspense was not long, for within half

[092-066]an hour of his leaving the breakfast-table the footman knocked at his

[092-067]door—that footman with whom at the beginning of his difficulties he

[092-068]had made up his mind to dispense, but who had been kept on because of

[092-069]the Barchester prebend.

[092-070]“If you please, your reverence, there are two men outside,” said the

[092-071]footman.

[092-072]Two men! Mark knew well enough what men they were, but he could

[092-073]hardly take the coming of two such men to his quiet country parsonage

[092-074]quite as a matter of course.

[092-075]“Who are they, John?” said he, not wishing any answer, but because

[092-076]the question was forced upon him.

[092-077]“I’m afeard they’re—bailiffs, sir.”

[092-078]“Very well, John; that will do; of course they must do what they

[092-079]please about the place.”

[092-080]And then, when the servant left him, he still stood without moving,

[092-081]exactly as he had stood before. There he remained for ten minutes,

[092-082]but the time went by very slowly. When about noon some circumstance

[092-083]told him what was the hour, he was astonished to find that the day

[092-084]had not nearly passed away.

[092-085]And then another tap was struck on the door,—a sound which he well

[092-086]recognized,—and his wife crept silently into the room. She came

[092-087]close up to him before she spoke, and put her arm within his:

[092-088]“Mark,” she said, “the men are here; they are in the yard.”

[092-089][Illustration: “Mark,” she said, “the men are here.”]

[092-090]“I know it,” he answered gruffly.

[092-091]“Will it be better that you should see them, dearest?”

[092-092]“See them; no; what good can I do by seeing them? But I shall see

[092-093]them soon enough; they will be here, I suppose, in a few minutes.”

[092-094]“They are taking an inventory, cook says; they are in the stable

[092-095]now.”

[092-096]“Very well; they must do as they please; I cannot help them.”

[092-097]“Cook says that if they are allowed their meals and some beer, and if

[092-098]nobody takes anything away, they will be quite civil.”

[092-099]“Civil! But what does it matter? Let them eat and drink what they

[092-100]please, as long as the food lasts. I don’t suppose the butcher will

[092-101]send you more.”

[092-102]“But, Mark, there’s nothing due to the butcher,—only the regular

[092-103]monthly bill.”

[092-104]“Very well; you’ll see.”

[092-105]“Oh, Mark, don’t look at me in that way. Do not turn away from me.

[092-106]What is to comfort us if we do not cling to each other now?”

[092-107]“Comfort us! God help you! I wonder, Fanny, that you can bear to stay

[092-108]in the room with me.”

[092-109]“Mark, dearest Mark, my own dear, dearest husband! who is to be true

[092-110]to you, if I am not? You shall not turn from me. How can anything

[092-111]like this make a difference between you and me?” And then she threw

[092-112]her arms round his neck and embraced him.

[092-113]It was a terrible morning to him, and one of which every incident

[092-114]will dwell on his memory to the last day of his life. He had been

[092-115]so proud in his position—had assumed to himself so prominent a

[092-116]standing—had contrived, by some trick which he had acquired, to

[092-117]carry his head so high above the heads of neighbouring parsons. It

[092-118]was this that had taken him among great people, had introduced him to

[092-119]the Duke of Omnium, had procured for him the stall at Barchester. But

[092-120]how was he to carry his head now? What would the Arabins and Grantlys

[092-121]say? How would the bishop sneer at him, and Mrs. Proudie and her

[092-122]daughters tell of him in all their quarters? How would Crawley look

[092-123]at him—Crawley, who had already once had him on the hip? The stern

[092-124]severity of Crawley’s face loomed upon him now. Crawley, with his

[092-125]children half naked, and his wife a drudge, and himself half starved,

[092-126]had never had a bailiff in his house at Hogglestock! And then his

[092-127]own curate, Evans, whom he had patronized, and treated almost as a

[092-128]dependant—how was he to look his curate in the face and arrange with

[092-129]him for the sacred duties of the next Sunday?

[092-130]His wife still stood by him, gazing into his face; and as he looked

[092-131]at her and thought of her misery, he could not control his heart

[092-132]with reference to the wrongs which Sowerby had heaped on him. It was

[092-133]Sowerby’s falsehood and Sowerby’s fraud which had brought upon him

[092-134]and his wife this terrible anguish. “If there be justice on earth he

[092-135]will suffer for it yet,” he said at last, not speaking intentionally

[092-136]to his wife, but unable to repress his feelings.

[092-137]“Do not wish him evil, Mark; you may be sure he has his own sorrows.”

[092-138]“His own sorrows! No; he is callous to such misery as this. He has

[092-139]become so hardened in dishonesty that all this is mirth to him. If

[092-140]there be punishment in heaven for falsehood—”

[092-141]“Oh, Mark, do not curse him!”

[092-142]“How am I to keep myself from cursing when I see what he has brought

[092-143]upon you?”

[092-144]“‘Vengeance is mine, saith the Lord,’” answered the young wife, not

[092-145]with solemn, preaching accent, as though bent on reproof, but with

[092-146]the softest whisper into his ear. “Leave that to Him, Mark; and for

[092-147]us, let us pray that He may soften the hearts of us all;—of him who

[092-148]has caused us to suffer, and of our own.”

[092-149]Mark was not called upon to reply to this, for he was again disturbed

[092-150]by a servant at the door. It was the cook this time herself, who had

[092-151]come with a message from the men of the law. And she had come, be it

[092-152]remembered, not from any necessity that she as cook should do this

[092-153]line of work; for the footman, or Mrs. Robarts’ maid, might have

[092-154]come as well as she. But when things are out of course servants are

[092-155]always out of course also. As a rule, nothing will induce a butler

[092-156]to go into a stable, or persuade a housemaid to put her hand to

[092-157]a frying-pan. But now that this new excitement had come upon the

[092-158]household—seeing that the bailiffs were in possession, and that the

[092-159]chattels were being entered in a catalogue, everybody was willing to

[092-160]do everything—everything but his or her own work. The gardener was

[092-161]looking after the dear children; the nurse was doing the rooms before

[092-162]the bailiffs should reach them; the groom had gone into the kitchen

[092-163]to get their lunch ready for them; and the cook was walking about

[092-164]with an inkstand, obeying all the orders of these great potentates.

[092-165]As far as the servants were concerned, it may be a question whether

[092-166]the coming of the bailiffs had not hitherto been regarded as a treat.

[092-167]“If you please, ma’am,” said Jemima cook, “they wishes to know in

[092-168]which room you’d be pleased to have the inmin-tory took fust. ’Cause,

[092-169]ma’am, they wouldn’t disturb you nor master more than can be avoided.

[092-170]For their line of life, ma’am, they is very civil—very civil

[092-171]indeed.”

[092-172]“I suppose they may go into the drawing-room,” said Mrs. Robarts, in

[092-173]a sad low voice. All nice women are proud of their drawing-rooms, and

[092-174]she was very proud of hers. It had been furnished when money was

[092-175]plenty with them, immediately after their marriage, and everything

[092-176]in it was pretty, good, and dear to her. O ladies, who have

[092-177]drawing-rooms in which the things are pretty, good, and dear to you,

[092-178]think of what it would be to have two bailiffs rummaging among them

[092-179]with pen and inkhorn, making a catalogue preparatory to a sheriff’s

[092-180]auction; and all without fault or extravagance of your own! There

[092-181]were things there that had been given to her by Lady Lufton, by Lady

[092-182]Meredith, and other friends, and the idea did occur to her that it

[092-183]might be possible to save them from contamination; but she would not

[092-184]say a word, lest by so saying she might add to Mark’s misery.
