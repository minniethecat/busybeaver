## Section 99

[099-001]If Mr. Robarts will allow us to tender to him our advice,

[099-002]he will lose no time in going through such ceremony as may

[099-003]be necessary again to place the stall at the disposal of

[099-004]the Crown!

[099-005]I may here observe that poor Harold Smith, when he read this,

[099-006]writhing in agony, declared it to be the handiwork of his hated

[099-007]enemy, Mr. Supplehouse. He knew the mark; so, at least, he said; but

[099-008]I myself am inclined to believe that his animosity misled him. I

[099-009]think that one greater than Mr. Supplehouse had taken upon himself

[099-010]the punishment of our poor vicar.

[099-011]This was very dreadful to them all at Framley, and, when first read,

[099-012]seemed to crush them to atoms. Poor Mrs. Robarts, when she heard it,

[099-013]seemed to think that for them the world was over. An attempt had been

[099-014]made to keep it from her, but such attempts always fail, as did this.

[099-015]The article was copied into all the good-natured local newspapers,

[099-016]and she soon discovered that something was being hidden. At last it

[099-017]was shown to her by her husband, and then for a few hours she was

[099-018]annihilated; for a few days she was unwilling to show herself; and

[099-019]for a few weeks she was very sad. But after that the world seemed to

[099-020]go on much as it had done before; the sun shone upon them as warmly

[099-021]as though the article had not been written; and not only the sun

[099-022]of heaven, which, as a rule, is not limited in his shining by any

[099-023]display of pagan thunder, but also the genial sun of their own

[099-024]sphere, the warmth and light of which were so essentially necessary

[099-025]to their happiness. Neighbouring rectors did not look glum, nor

[099-026]did the rectors’ wives refuse to call. The people in the shops at

[099-027]Barchester did not regard her as though she were a disgraced woman,

[099-028]though it must be acknowledged that Mrs. Proudie passed her in the

[099-029]close with the coldest nod of recognition.

[099-030]On Mrs. Proudie’s mind alone did the article seem to have any

[099-031]enduring effect. In one respect it was, perhaps, beneficial; Lady

[099-032]Lufton was at once induced by it to make common cause with her own

[099-033]clergyman, and thus the remembrance of Mr. Robarts’ sins passed away

[099-034]the quicker from the minds of the whole Framley Court household.

[099-035]And, indeed, the county at large was not able to give to the matter

[099-036]that undivided attention which would have been considered its due

[099-037]at periods of no more than ordinary interest. At the present moment

[099-038]preparations were being made for a general election, and although no

[099-039]contest was to take place in the eastern division, a very violent

[099-040]fight was being carried on in the west; and the circumstances

[099-041]of that fight were so exciting that Mr. Robarts and his article

[099-042]were forgotten before their time. An edict had gone forth from

[099-043]Gatherum Castle directing that Mr. Sowerby should be turned out, and

[099-044]an answering note of defiance had been sounded from Chaldicotes,

[099-045]protesting on behalf of Mr. Sowerby, that the duke’s behest would not

[099-046]be obeyed.

[099-047]There are two classes of persons in this realm who are

[099-048]constitutionally inefficient to take any part in returning members

[099-049]to Parliament—peers, namely, and women; and yet it was soon known

[099-050]through the whole length and breadth of the county that the present

[099-051]electioneering fight was being carried on between a peer and a

[099-052]woman. Miss Dunstable had been declared the purchaser of the Chace

[099-053]of Chaldicotes, as it were, just in the very nick of time; which

[099-054]purchase—so men in Barsetshire declared, not knowing anything of the

[099-055]facts—would have gone altogether the other way, had not the giants

[099-056]obtained temporary supremacy over the gods. The duke was a supporter

[099-057]of the gods, and therefore, so Mr. Fothergill hinted, his money had

[099-058]been refused. Miss Dunstable was prepared to beard this ducal friend

[099-059]of the gods in his own county, and therefore her money had been

[099-060]taken. I am inclined, however, to think that Mr. Fothergill knew

[099-061]nothing about it, and to opine that Miss Dunstable, in her eagerness

[099-062]for victory, offered to the Crown more money than the property was

[099-063]worth in the duke’s opinion, and that the Crown took advantage of her

[099-064]anxiety, to the manifest profit of the public at large.

[099-065]And it soon became known also that Miss Dunstable was, in fact, the

[099-066]proprietor of the whole Chaldicotes estate, and that in promoting

[099-067]the success of Mr. Sowerby as a candidate for the county, she was

[099-068]standing by her own tenant. It also became known, in the course

[099-069]of the battle, that Miss Dunstable had herself at last succumbed,

[099-070]and that she was about to marry Dr. Thorne of Greshamsbury, or the

[099-071]“Greshamsbury apothecary,” as the adverse party now delighted to call

[099-072]him. “He has been little better than a quack all his life,” said Dr.

[099-073]Fillgrave, the eminent physician of Barchester, “and now he is going

[099-074]to marry a quack’s daughter.” By which, and the like to which, Dr.

[099-075]Thorne did not allow himself to be much annoyed.

[099-076]But all this gave rise to a very pretty series of squibs arranged

[099-077]between Mr. Fothergill and Mr. Closerstill, the electioneering agent.

[099-078]Mr. Sowerby was named “the lady’s pet,” and descriptions were given

[099-079]of the lady who kept this pet, which were by no means flattering to

[099-080]Miss Dunstable’s appearance, or manners, or age. And then the western

[099-081]division of the county was asked in a grave tone—as counties and

[099-082]boroughs are asked by means of advertisements stuck up on blind walls

[099-083]and barn doors—whether it was fitting and proper that it should be

[099-084]represented by a woman. Upon which the county was again asked whether

[099-085]it was fitting and proper that it should be represented by a duke.

[099-086]And then the question became more personal as against Miss Dunstable,

[099-087]and inquiry was urged whether the county would not be indelibly

[099-088]disgraced if it were not only handed over to a woman, but handed over

[099-089]to a woman who sold the oil of Lebanon. But little was got by this

[099-090]move, for an answering placard explained to the unfortunate county

[099-091]how deep would be its shame if it allowed itself to become the

[099-092]appanage of any peer, but more especially of a peer who was known to

[099-093]be the most immoral lord that ever disgraced the benches of the upper

[099-094]house.

[099-095]And so the battle went on very prettily, and, as money was allowed

[099-096]to flow freely, the West Barsetshire world at large was not ill

[099-097]satisfied. It is wonderful how much disgrace of that kind a borough

[099-098]or county can endure without flinching; and wonderful, also, seeing

[099-099]how supreme is the value attached to the constitution by the realm at

[099-100]large, how very little the principles of that constitution are valued

[099-101]by the people in detail. The duke, of course, did not show himself.

[099-102]He rarely did on any occasion, and never on such occasions as this;

[099-103]but Mr. Fothergill was to be seen everywhere. Miss Dunstable, also,

[099-104]did not hide her light under a bushel; though I here declare, on the

[099-105]faith of an historian, that the rumour spread abroad of her having

[099-106]made a speech to the electors from the top of the porch over the

[099-107]hotel-door at Courcy was not founded on fact. No doubt she was at

[099-108]Courcy, and her carriage stopped at the hotel; but neither there

[099-109]nor elsewhere did she make any public exhibition. “They must have

[099-110]mistaken me for Mrs. Proudie,” she said, when the rumour reached her

[099-111]ears.

[099-112]But there was, alas! one great element of failure on Miss Dunstable’s

[099-113]side of the battle. Mr. Sowerby himself could not be induced to fight

[099-114]it as became a man. Any positive injunctions that were laid upon him

[099-115]he did, in a sort, obey. It had been a part of the bargain that he

[099-116]should stand the contest, and from that bargain he could not well go

[099-117]back; but he had not the spirit left to him for any true fighting on

[099-118]his own part. He could not go up on the hustings, and there defy the

[099-119]duke. Early in the affair Mr. Fothergill challenged him to do so, and

[099-120]Mr. Sowerby never took up the gauntlet.

[099-121]“We have heard,” said Mr. Fothergill, in that great speech which he

[099-122]made at the Omnium Arms at Silverbridge—“we have heard much during

[099-123]this election of the Duke of Omnium, and of the injuries which he

[099-124]is supposed to have inflicted on one of the candidates. The duke’s

[099-125]name is very frequent in the mouths of the gentlemen,—and of the

[099-126]lady,—who support Mr. Sowerby’s claims. But I do not think that

[099-127]Mr. Sowerby himself has dared to say much about the duke. I defy Mr.

[099-128]Sowerby to mention the duke’s name upon the hustings.”

[099-129]And it so happened that Mr. Sowerby never did mention the duke’s

[099-130]name.

[099-131]It is ill fighting when the spirit is gone, and Mr. Sowerby’s spirit

[099-132]for such things was now well nigh broken. It is true that he had

[099-133]escaped from the net in which the duke, by Mr. Fothergill’s aid,

[099-134]had entangled him; but he had only broken out of one captivity into

[099-135]another. Money is a serious thing; and when gone cannot be had back

[099-136]by a shuffle in the game, or a fortunate blow with the battledore, as

[099-137]may political power, or reputation, or fashion. One hundred thousand

[099-138]pounds gone, must remain as gone, let the person who claims to have

[099-139]had the honour of advancing it be Mrs. B. or my Lord C. No lucky

[099-140]dodge can erase such a claim from the things that be—unless, indeed,

[099-141]such dodge be possible as Mr. Sowerby tried with Miss Dunstable. It

[099-142]was better for him, undoubtedly, to have the lady for a creditor than

[099-143]the duke, seeing that it was possible for him to live as a tenant in

[099-144]his own old house under the lady’s reign. But this he found to be a

[099-145]sad enough life, after all that was come and gone.

[099-146]The election on Miss Dunstable’s part was lost. She carried on

[099-147]the contest nobly, fighting it to the last moment, and sparing

[099-148]neither her own money nor that of her antagonist; but she carried

[099-149]it on unsuccessfully. Many gentlemen did support Mr. Sowerby

[099-150]because they were willing enough to emancipate their county from

[099-151]the duke’s thraldom; but Mr. Sowerby was felt to be a black

[099-152]sheep, as Lady Lufton had called him, and at the close of the

[099-153]election he found himself banished from the representation of West

[099-154]Barsetshire;—banished for ever, after having held the county for

[099-155]five-and-twenty years.

[099-156]Unfortunate Mr. Sowerby! I cannot take leave of him here without

[099-157]some feeling of regret, knowing that there was that within him which

[099-158]might, under better guidance, have produced better things. There are

[099-159]men, even of high birth, who seem as though they were born to be

[099-160]rogues; but Mr. Sowerby was, to my thinking, born to be a gentleman.

[099-161]That he had not been a gentleman—that he had bolted from his

[099-162]appointed course, going terribly on the wrong side of the posts—let

[099-163]us all acknowledge. It is not a gentlemanlike deed, but a very

[099-164]blackguard action, to obtain a friend’s acceptance to a bill in an

[099-165]unguarded hour of social intercourse. That and other similar doings

[099-166]have stamped his character too plainly. But, nevertheless, I claim a

[099-167]tear for Mr. Sowerby, and lament that he has failed to run his race

[099-168]discreetly, in accordance with the rules of the Jockey Club.

[099-169]He attempted that plan of living as a tenant in his old house at

[099-170]Chaldicotes and of making a living out of the land which he farmed;

[099-171]but he soon abandoned it. He had no aptitude for such industry,

[099-172]and could not endure his altered position in the county. He soon

[099-173]relinquished Chaldicotes of his own accord, and has vanished away, as

[099-174]such men do vanish—not altogether without necessary income; to which

[099-175]point in the final arrangement of their joint affairs, Mrs. Thorne’s

[099-176]man of business—if I may be allowed so far to anticipate—paid

[099-177]special attention.
