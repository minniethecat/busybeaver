## Section 6

[006-001]At dinner Mark found himself seated between Miss Proudie and the lady

[006-002]whom he had heard named as Miss Dunstable. Of the former he was not

[006-003]very fond, and, in spite of his host’s petition, was not inclined to

[006-004]play bachelor parson for her benefit. With the other lady he would

[006-005]willingly have chatted during the dinner, only that everybody else at

[006-006]table seemed to be intent on doing the same thing. She was neither

[006-007]young, nor beautiful, nor peculiarly ladylike; yet she seemed

[006-008]to enjoy a popularity which must have excited the envy of Mr.

[006-009]Supplehouse, and which certainly was not altogether to the taste

[006-010]of Mrs. Proudie—who, however, fêted her as much as did the others.

[006-011]So that our clergyman found himself unable to obtain more than an

[006-012]inconsiderable share of the lady’s attention.

[006-013]“Bishop,” said she, speaking across the table, “we have missed you so

[006-014]all day! we have had no one on earth to say a word to us.”

[006-015]“My dear Miss Dunstable, had I known that— But I really was engaged

[006-016]on business of some importance.”

[006-017]“I don’t believe in business of importance; do you, Mrs. Smith?”

[006-018]“Do I not?” said Mrs. Smith. “If you were married to Mr. Harold Smith

[006-019]for one week, you’d believe in it.”

[006-020]“Should I, now? What a pity that I can’t have that chance of

[006-021]improving my faith! But you are a man of business, also, Mr.

[006-022]Supplehouse; so they tell me.” And she turned to her neighbour on her

[006-023]right hand.

[006-024]“I cannot compare myself to Harold Smith,” said he. “But perhaps I

[006-025]may equal the bishop.”

[006-026]“What does a man do, now, when he sits himself down to business? How

[006-027]does he set about it? What are his tools? A quire of blotting paper,

[006-028]I suppose, to begin with?”

[006-029]“That depends, I should say, on his trade. A shoemaker begins by

[006-030]waxing his thread.”

[006-031]“And Mr. Harold Smith—?”

[006-032]“By counting up his yesterday’s figures, generally, I should say;

[006-033]or else by unrolling a ball of red tape. Well-docketed papers and

[006-034]statistical facts are his forte.”

[006-035]“And what does a bishop do? Can you tell me that?”

[006-036]“He sends forth to his clergy either blessings or blowings-up,

[006-037]according to the state of his digestive organs. But Mrs. Proudie can

[006-038]explain all that to you with the greatest accuracy.”

[006-039]“Can she, now? I understand what you mean, but I don’t believe a word

[006-040]of it. The bishop manages his own affairs himself, quite as much as

[006-041]you do, or Mr. Harold Smith.”

[006-042]“I, Miss Dunstable?”

[006-043]“Yes, you.”

[006-044]“But I, unluckily, have not a wife to manage them for me.”

[006-045]“Then you should not laugh at those who have, for you don’t know what

[006-046]you may come to yourself, when you’re married.”

[006-047]Mr. Supplehouse began to make a pretty speech, saying that he would

[006-048]be delighted to incur any danger in that respect to which he might

[006-049]be subjected by the companionship of Miss Dunstable. But before he

[006-050]was half through it, she had turned her back upon him, and begun a

[006-051]conversation with Mark Robarts.

[006-052]“Have you much work in your parish, Mr. Robarts?” she asked. Now,

[006-053]Mark was not aware that she knew his name, or the fact of his having

[006-054]a parish, and was rather surprised by the question. And he had not

[006-055]quite liked the tone in which she had seemed to speak of the bishop

[006-056]and his work. His desire for her further acquaintance was therefore

[006-057]somewhat moderated, and he was not prepared to answer her question

[006-058]with much zeal.

[006-059]“All parish clergymen have plenty of work, if they choose to do it.”

[006-060]“Ah, that is it; is it not, Mr. Robarts? If they choose to do it? A

[006-061]great many do—many that I know, do; and see what a result they have.

[006-062]But many neglect it—and see what a result _they_ have. I think it

[006-063]ought to be the happiest life that a man can lead, that of a parish

[006-064]clergyman, with a wife and family, and a sufficient income.”

[006-065]“I think it is,” said Mark Robarts, asking himself whether the

[006-066]contentment accruing to him from such blessings had made him

[006-067]satisfied at all points. He had all these things of which Miss

[006-068]Dunstable spoke, and yet he had told his wife, the other day, that he

[006-069]could not afford to neglect the acquaintance of a rising politician

[006-070]like Harold Smith.

[006-071]“What I find fault with is this,” continued Miss Dunstable, “that we

[006-072]expect clergymen to do their duty, and don’t give them a sufficient

[006-073]income—give them hardly any income at all. Is it not a scandal,

[006-074]that an educated gentleman with a family should be made to work half

[006-075]his life, and perhaps the whole, for a pittance of seventy pounds a

[006-076]year?”

[006-077]Mark said that it was a scandal, and thought of Mr. Evan Jones and

[006-078]his daughter;—and thought also of his own worth, and his own house,

[006-079]and his own nine hundred a year.

[006-080]“And yet you clergymen are so proud—aristocratic would be the

[006-081]genteel word, I know—that you won’t take the money of common,

[006-082]ordinary poor people. You must be paid from land and endowments, from

[006-083]tithe and church property. You can’t bring yourself to work for what

[006-084]you earn, as lawyers and doctors do. It is better that curates should

[006-085]starve than undergo such ignominy as that.”

[006-086]“It is a long subject, Miss Dunstable.”

[006-087]“A very long one; and that means that I am not to say any more about

[006-088]it.”

[006-089]“I did not mean that exactly.”

[006-090]“Oh! but you did though, Mr. Robarts. And I can take a hint of that

[006-091]kind when I get it. You clergymen like to keep those long subjects

[006-092]for your sermons, when no one can answer you. Now if I have a longing

[006-093]heart’s desire for anything at all in this world, it is to be able to

[006-094]get up into a pulpit, and preach a sermon.”

[006-095]“You can’t conceive how soon that appetite would pall upon you, after

[006-096]its first indulgence.”

[006-097]“That would depend upon whether I could get people to listen to me.

[006-098]It does not pall upon Mr. Spurgeon, I suppose.” Then her attention

[006-099]was called away by some question from Mr. Sowerby, and Mark Robarts

[006-100]found himself bound to address his conversation to Miss Proudie.

[006-101]Miss Proudie, however, was not thankful, and gave him little but

[006-102]monosyllables for his pains.

[006-103]“Of course you know Harold Smith is going to give us a lecture about

[006-104]these islanders,” Mr. Sowerby said to him, as they sat round the fire

[006-105]over their wine after dinner. Mark said that he had been so informed,

[006-106]and should be delighted to be one of the listeners.

[006-107]“You are bound to do that, as he is going to listen to you the day

[006-108]afterwards—or, at any rate, to pretend to do so, which is as much as

[006-109]you will do for him. It’ll be a terrible bore—the lecture, I mean,

[006-110]not the sermon.” And he spoke very low into his friend’s ear. “Fancy

[006-111]having to drive ten miles after dusk, and ten miles back, to hear

[006-112]Harold Smith talk for two hours about Borneo! One must do it, you

[006-113]know.”

[006-114]“I daresay it will be very interesting.”

[006-115]“My dear fellow, you haven’t undergone so many of these things as I

[006-116]have. But he’s right to do it. It’s his line of life; and when a man

[006-117]begins a thing he ought to go on with it. Where’s Lufton all this

[006-118]time?”

[006-119]“In Scotland, when I last heard from him; but he’s probably at Melton

[006-120]now.”

[006-121]“It’s deuced shabby of him, not hunting here in his own county. He

[006-122]escapes all the bore of going to lectures, and giving feeds to the

[006-123]neighbours; that’s why he treats us so. He has no idea of his duty,

[006-124]has he?”

[006-125]“Lady Lufton does all that, you know.”

[006-126]“I wish I’d a Mrs. Sowerby _mère_ to do it for me. But then Lufton

[006-127]has no constituents to look after—lucky dog! By-the-by, has he

[006-128]spoken to you about selling that outlying bit of land of his in

[006-129]Oxfordshire? It belongs to the Lufton property, and yet it doesn’t.

[006-130]In my mind it gives more trouble than it’s worth.”

[006-131]Lord Lufton had spoken to Mark about this sale, and had explained to

[006-132]him that such a sacrifice was absolutely necessary, in consequence

[006-133]of certain pecuniary transactions between him, Lord Lufton, and Mr.

[006-134]Sowerby. But it was found impracticable to complete the business

[006-135]without Lady Lufton’s knowledge, and her son had commissioned Mr.

[006-136]Robarts not only to inform her ladyship, but to talk her over, and

[006-137]to appease her wrath. This commission he had not yet attempted to

[006-138]execute, and it was probable that this visit to Chaldicotes would not

[006-139]do much to facilitate the business.

[006-140]“They are the most magnificent islands under the sun,” said Harold

[006-141]Smith to the bishop.

[006-142]“Are they, indeed!” said the bishop, opening his eyes wide, and

[006-143]assuming a look of intense interest.

[006-144]“And the most intelligent people.”

[006-145]“Dear me!” said the bishop.

[006-146]“All they want is guidance, encouragement, instruction—”

[006-147]“And Christianity,” suggested the bishop.

[006-148]“And Christianity, of course,” said Mr. Smith, remembering that he

[006-149]was speaking to a dignitary of the Church. It was well to humour such

[006-150]people, Mr. Smith thought. But the Christianity was to be done in the

[006-151]Sunday sermon, and was not part of his work.

[006-152]“And how do you intend to begin with them?” asked Mr. Supplehouse,

[006-153]the business of whose life it had been to suggest difficulties.

[006-154]“Begin with them—oh—why—it’s very easy to begin with them. The

[006-155]difficulty is to go on with them, after the money is all spent. We’ll

[006-156]begin by explaining to them the benefits of civilization.”

[006-157]“Capital plan!” said Mr. Supplehouse. “But how do you set about it,

[006-158]Smith?”

[006-159]“How do we set about it? How did we set about it with Australia and

[006-160]America? It is very easy to criticize; but in such matters the great

[006-161]thing is to put one’s shoulder to the wheel.”

[006-162]“We sent our felons to Australia,” said Supplehouse, “and they began

[006-163]the work for us. And as to America, we exterminated the people

[006-164]instead of civilizing them.”

[006-165]“We did not exterminate the inhabitants of India,” said Harold Smith,

[006-166]angrily.

[006-167]“Nor have we attempted to Christianize them, as the bishop so

[006-168]properly wishes to do with your islanders.”

[006-169]“Supplehouse, you are not fair,” said Mr. Sowerby, “neither to Harold

[006-170]Smith nor to us;—you are making him rehearse his lecture, which is

[006-171]bad for him; and making us hear the rehearsal, which is bad for us.”

[006-172]“Supplehouse belongs to a clique which monopolizes the wisdom of

[006-173]England,” said Harold Smith; “or, at any rate, thinks that it

[006-174]does. But the worst of them is that they are given to talk leading

[006-175]articles.”

[006-176]“Better that, than talk articles which are not leading,” said Mr.

[006-177]Supplehouse. “Some first-class official men do that.”

[006-178]“Shall I meet you at the duke’s next week, Mr. Robarts?” said the

[006-179]bishop to him, soon after they had gone into the drawing-room.

[006-180]Meet him at the duke’s!—the established enemy of Barsetshire

[006-181]mankind, as Lady Lufton regarded his grace! No idea of going to the

[006-182]duke’s had ever entered our hero’s mind; nor had he been aware that

[006-183]the duke was about to entertain any one.

[006-184]“No, my lord; I think not. Indeed, I have no acquaintance with his

[006-185]grace.”

[006-186]“Oh—ah! I did not know. Because Mr. Sowerby is going; and so are the

[006-187]Harold Smiths, and, I think, Mr. Supplehouse. An excellent man is

[006-188]the duke;—that is, as regards all the county interests,” added the

[006-189]bishop, remembering that the moral character of his bachelor grace

[006-190]was not the very best in the world.

[006-191]And then his lordship began to ask some questions about the church

[006-192]affairs of Framley, in which a little interest as to Framley Court

[006-193]was also mixed up, when he was interrupted by a rather sharp voice,

[006-194]to which he instantly attended.
