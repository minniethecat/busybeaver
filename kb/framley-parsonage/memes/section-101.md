## Section 101

[101-001]“We shall call her Mrs. Thorne instead of Miss Dunstable, and I

[101-002]really think that that will be all the difference,” said Mrs. Harold

[101-003]Smith.

[101-004]To Mrs. Harold Smith that probably was all the difference, but it was

[101-005]not so to the persons most concerned.

[101-006]According to the plan of life arranged between the doctor and his

[101-007]wife she was still to keep up her house in London, remaining there

[101-008]during such period of the season as she might choose, and receiving

[101-009]him when it might appear good to him to visit her; but he was to be

[101-010]the master in the country. A mansion at the Chace was to be built,

[101-011]and till such time as that was completed, they would keep on the old

[101-012]house at Greshamsbury. Into this, small as it was, Mrs. Thorne,—in

[101-013]spite of her great wealth,—did not disdain to enter. But subsequent

[101-014]circumstances changed their plans. It was found that Mr. Sowerby

[101-015]could not or would not live at Chaldicotes; and, therefore, in the

[101-016]second year of their marriage, that place was prepared for them.

[101-017]They are now well known to the whole county as Dr. and Mrs. Thorne

[101-018]of Chaldicotes,—of Chaldicotes, in distinction to the well-known

[101-019]Thornes of Ullathorne in the eastern division. Here they live

[101-020]respected by their neighbours, and on terms of alliance both with

[101-021]the Duke of Omnium and with Lady Lufton. “Of course those dear old

[101-022]avenues will be very sad to me,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, when at the

[101-023]end of a London season she was invited down to Chaldicotes; and as

[101-024]she spoke she put her handkerchief up to her eyes.

[101-025]“Well, dear, what can I do?” said Mrs. Thorne. “I can’t cut them

[101-026]down; the doctor would not let me.”

[101-027]“Oh, no,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, sighing; and in spite of her

[101-028]feelings she did visit Chaldicotes.

[101-029]But it was October before Lord Lufton was made a happy man;—that

[101-030]is, if the fruition of his happiness was a greater joy than the

[101-031]anticipation of it. I will not say that the happiness of marriage is

[101-032]like the Dead Sea fruit,—an apple which, when eaten, turns to bitter

[101-033]ashes in the mouth. Such pretended sarcasm would be very false.

[101-034]Nevertheless, is it not the fact that the sweetest morsel of love’s

[101-035]feast has been eaten, that the freshest, fairest blush of the flower

[101-036]has been snatched and has passed away, when the ceremony at the altar

[101-037]has been performed, and legal possession has been given? There is an

[101-038]aroma of love, an undefinable delicacy of flavour, which escapes and

[101-039]is gone before the church portal is left, vanishing with the maiden

[101-040]name, and incompatible with the solid comfort appertaining to the

[101-041]rank of wife. To love one’s own spouse, and to be loved by her, is

[101-042]the ordinary lot of man, and is a duty exacted under penalties. But

[101-043]to be allowed to love youth and beauty that is not one’s own—to know

[101-044]that one is loved by a soft being who still hangs cowering from the

[101-045]eye of the world as though her love were all but illicit—can it be

[101-046]that a man is made happy when a state of anticipation such as this is

[101-047]brought to a close? No; when the husband walks back from the altar,

[101-048]he has already swallowed the choicest dainties of his banquet. The

[101-049]beef and pudding of married life are then in store for him;—or

[101-050]perhaps only the bread and cheese. Let him take care lest hardly a

[101-051]crust remain,—or perhaps not a crust.

[101-052]But before we finish, let us go back for one moment to the

[101-053]dainties,—to the time before the beef and pudding were

[101-054]served,—while Lucy was still at the parsonage, and Lord Lufton still

[101-055]staying at Framley Court. He had come up one morning, as was now

[101-056]frequently his wont, and, after a few minutes’ conversation, Mrs.

[101-057]Robarts had left the room,—as not unfrequently on such occasions was

[101-058]her wont. Lucy was working and continued her work, and Lord Lufton

[101-059]for a moment or two sat looking at her; then he got up abruptly, and

[101-060]standing before her, thus questioned her:—

[101-061]“Lucy,” said he.

[101-062]“Well, what of Lucy now? Any particular fault this morning?”

[101-063]“Yes, a most particular fault. When I asked you, here, in this room,

[101-064]on this very spot, whether it was possible that you should love

[101-065]me—why did you say that it was impossible?”

[101-066]Lucy, instead of answering at the moment, looked down upon the

[101-067]carpet, to see if his memory were as good as hers. Yes; he was

[101-068]standing on the exact spot where he had stood before. No spot in all

[101-069]the world was more frequently clear before her own eyes.

[101-070]“Do you remember that day, Lucy?” he said again.

[101-071]“Yes, I remember it,” she said.

[101-072]“Why did you say it was impossible?”

[101-073]“Did I say impossible?”

[101-074]She knew that she had said so. She remembered how she had waited till

[101-075]he had gone, and that then, going to her own room, she had reproached

[101-076]herself with the cowardice of the falsehood. She had lied to him

[101-077]then; and now—how was she punished for it?

[101-078]“Well, I suppose it was possible,” she said.

[101-079]“But why did you say so when you knew it would make me so miserable?”

[101-080]“Miserable! nay, but you went away happy enough! I thought I had

[101-081]never seen you look better satisfied.”

[101-082]“Lucy!”

[101-083]“You had done your duty and had had such a lucky escape! What

[101-084]astonishes me is that you should have ever come back again. But the

[101-085]pitcher may go to the well once too often, Lord Lufton.”

[101-086]“But will you tell me the truth now?”

[101-087]“What truth?”

[101-088]“That day, when I came to you,—did you love me at all then?”

[101-089]“We’ll let bygones be bygones, if you please.”

[101-090]“But I swear you shall tell me. It was such a cruel thing to answer

[101-091]me as you did, unless you meant it. And yet you never saw me again

[101-092]till after my mother had been over for you to Mrs. Crawley’s.”

[101-093]“It was absence that made me—care for you.”

[101-094]“Lucy, I swear I believe you loved me then.”

[101-095]“Ludovic, some conjuror must have told you that.”

[101-096]She was standing as she spoke, and, laughing at him, she held up her

[101-097]hands and shook her head. But she was now in his power, and he had

[101-098]his revenge,—his revenge for her past falsehood and her present

[101-099]joke. How could he be more happy when he was made happy by having her

[101-100]all his own, than he was now?

[101-101]And in these days there again came up that petition as to her

[101-102]riding—with very different result now than on that former occasion.

[101-103]There were ever so many objections, then. There was no habit, and

[101-104]Lucy was—or said that she was—afraid; and then, what would Lady

[101-105]Lufton say? But now Lady Lufton thought it would be quite right;

[101-106]only were they quite sure about the horse? Was Ludovic certain that

[101-107]the horse had been ridden by a lady? And Lady Meredith’s habits

[101-108]were dragged out as a matter of course, and one of them chipped and

[101-109]snipped and altered, without any compunction. And as for fear, there

[101-110]could be no bolder horsewoman than Lucy Robarts. It was quite clear

[101-111]to all Framley that riding was the very thing for her. “But I never

[101-112]shall be happy, Ludovic, till you have got a horse properly suited

[101-113]for her,” said Lady Lufton.

[101-114]And then, also, came the affair of her wedding garments, of her

[101-115]_trousseau_,—as to which I cannot boast that she showed capacity

[101-116]or steadiness at all equal to that of Lady Dumbello. Lady Lufton,

[101-117]however, thought it a very serious matter; and as, in her opinion,

[101-118]Mrs. Robarts did not go about it with sufficient energy, she took the

[101-119]matter mainly into her own hands, striking Lucy dumb by her frowns

[101-120]and nods, deciding on everything herself, down to the very tags of

[101-121]the boot-ties.

[101-122]“My dear, you really must allow me to know what I am about;” and

[101-123]Lady Lufton patted her on the arm as she spoke. “I did it all for

[101-124]Justinia, and she never had reason to regret a single thing that

[101-125]I bought. If you’ll ask her, she’ll tell you so.”

[101-126]Lucy did not ask her future sister-in-law, seeing that she had no

[101-127]doubt whatever as to her future mother-in-law’s judgment on the

[101-128]articles in question. Only the money! And what could she want with

[101-129]six dozen pocket-handkerchiefs all at once? There was no question

[101-130]of Lord Lufton’s going out as governor-general to India! But twelve

[101-131]dozen pocket-handkerchiefs had not been too many for Griselda’s

[101-132]imagination.

[101-133]And Lucy would sit alone in the drawing-room at Framley Court,

[101-134]filling her heart with thoughts of that evening when she had first

[101-135]sat there. She had then resolved, painfully, with inward tears, with

[101-136]groanings of her spirit, that she was wrongly placed in being in that

[101-137]company. Griselda Grantly had been there, quite at her ease, petted

[101-138]by Lady Lufton, admired by Lord Lufton; while she had retired out of

[101-139]sight, sore at heart, because she felt herself to be no fit companion

[101-140]to those around her. Then he had come to her, making matters almost

[101-141]worse by talking to her, bringing the tears into her eyes by his

[101-142]good-nature, but still wounding her by the feeling that she could not

[101-143]speak to him at her ease.

[101-144]But things were at a different pass with her now. He had chosen

[101-145]her—her out of all the world, and brought her there to share with

[101-146]him his own home, his own honours, and all that he had to give. She

[101-147]was the apple of his eye, and the pride of his heart. And the stern

[101-148]mother, of whom she had stood so much in awe, who at first had

[101-149]passed her by as a thing not to be noticed, and had then sent out

[101-150]to her that she might be warned to keep herself aloof, now hardly

[101-151]knew in what way she might sufficiently show her love, regard, and

[101-152]solicitude.

[101-153]I must not say that Lucy was not proud in these moments—that her

[101-154]heart was not elated at these thoughts. Success does beget pride, as

[101-155]failure begets shame. But her pride was of that sort which is in no

[101-156]way disgraceful to either man or woman, and was accompanied by pure

[101-157]true love, and a full resolution to do her duty in that state of life

[101-158]to which it had pleased her God to call her. She did rejoice greatly

[101-159]to think that she had been chosen, and not Griselda. Was it possible

[101-160]that having loved she should not so rejoice, or that, rejoicing, she

[101-161]should not be proud of her love?

[101-162]They spent the whole winter abroad, leaving the dowager Lady Lufton

[101-163]to her plans and preparations for their reception at Framley Court;

[101-164]and in the following spring they appeared in London, and there set

[101-165]up their staff. Lucy had some inner tremblings of the spirit, and

[101-166]quiverings about the heart, at thus beginning her duty before the

[101-167]great world, but she said little or nothing to her husband on the

[101-168]matter. Other women had done as much before her time, and by courage

[101-169]had gone through with it. It would be dreadful enough, that position

[101-170]in her own house with lords and ladies bowing to her, and stiff

[101-171]members of Parliament for whom it would be necessary to make small

[101-172]talk; but, nevertheless, it was to be endured. The time came, and she

[101-173]did endure it. The time came, and before the first six weeks were

[101-174]over she found that it was easy enough. The lords and ladies got into

[101-175]their proper places and talked to her about ordinary matters in a

[101-176]way that made no effort necessary, and the members of Parliament

[101-177]were hardly more stiff than the clergymen she had known in the

[101-178]neighbourhood of Framley.
