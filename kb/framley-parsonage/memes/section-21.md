## Section 21

[021-001]All this interfered greatly with Mark’s wise resolution as to

[021-002]the Sowerby-bill incubus. In the first place he could not get to

[021-003]Barchester as soon as he had intended, and then an idea came across

[021-004]him that possibly it might be well that he should borrow the money of

[021-005]his brother John, explaining the circumstances, of course, and paying

[021-006]him due interest. But he had not liked to broach the subject when

[021-007]they were there in Exeter, standing, as it were, over their father’s

[021-008]grave, and so the matter was postponed. There was still ample time

[021-009]for arrangement before the bill would come due, and he would not tell

[021-010]Fanny till he had made up his mind what that arrangement would be. It

[021-011]would kill her, he said to himself over and over again, were he to

[021-012]tell her of it without being able to tell her also that the means of

[021-013]liquidating the debt were to be forthcoming.

[021-014]And now I must say a word about Lucy Robarts. If one might only go on

[021-015]without those descriptions, how pleasant it would all be! But Lucy

[021-016]Robarts has to play a forward part in this little drama, and those

[021-017]who care for such matters must be made to understand something of her

[021-018]form and likeness. When last we mentioned her as appearing, though

[021-019]not in any prominent position, at her brother’s wedding, she was only

[021-020]sixteen; but now, at the time of her father’s death, somewhat over

[021-021]two years having since elapsed, she was nearly nineteen. Laying aside

[021-022]for the sake of clearness that indefinite term of girl—for girls

[021-023]are girls from the age of three up to forty-three, if not previously

[021-024]married—dropping that generic word, we may say that then, at that

[021-025]wedding of her brother, she was a child; and now, at the death of her

[021-026]father, she was a woman.

[021-027]Nothing, perhaps, adds so much to womanhood, turns the child so

[021-028]quickly into a woman, as such death-bed scenes as these. Hitherto

[021-029]but little had fallen to Lucy to do in the way of woman’s duties. Of

[021-030]money transactions she had known nothing, beyond a jocose attempt

[021-031]to make her annual allowance of twenty-five pounds cover all her

[021-032]personal wants—an attempt which was made jocose by the loving bounty

[021-033]of her father. Her sister, who was three years her elder—for John

[021-034]came in between them—had managed the house; that is, she had made

[021-035]the tea and talked to the housekeeper about the dinners. But Lucy had

[021-036]sat at her father’s elbow, had read to him of evenings when he went

[021-037]to sleep, had brought him his slippers and looked after the comforts

[021-038]of his easy-chair. All this she had done as a child; but when she

[021-039]stood at the coffin head, and knelt at the coffin side, then she was

[021-040]a woman.

[021-041]She was smaller in stature than either of her three sisters, to all

[021-042]of whom had been acceded the praise of being fine women—a eulogy

[021-043]which the people of Exeter, looking back at the elder sisters, and

[021-044]the general remembrance of them which pervaded the city, were not

[021-045]willing to extend to Lucy. “Dear—dear!” had been said of her; “poor

[021-046]Lucy is not like a Robarts at all; is she, now, Mrs. Pole?”—for

[021-047]as the daughters had become fine women, so had the sons grown into

[021-048]stalwart men. And then Mrs. Pole had answered: “Not a bit; is she,

[021-049]now? Only think what Blanche was at her age. But she has fine eyes,

[021-050]for all that; and they do say she is the cleverest of them all.”

[021-051]And that, too, is so true a description of her, that I do not know

[021-052]that I can add much to it. She was not like Blanche; for Blanche had

[021-053]a bright complexion, and a fine neck, and a noble bust, _et vera

[021-054]incessu patuit Dea_—a true goddess, that is, as far as the eye

[021-055]went. She had a grand idea, moreover, of an apple-pie, and had not

[021-056]reigned eighteen months at Creamclotted Hall before she knew all the

[021-057]mysteries of pigs and milk, and most of those appertaining to cider

[021-058]and green geese.

[021-059]Lucy had no neck at all worth speaking of,—no neck, I mean, that

[021-060]ever produced eloquence; she was brown, too, and had addicted herself

[021-061]in nowise, as she undoubtedly should have done, to larder utility. In

[021-062]regard to the neck and colour, poor girl, she could not help herself;

[021-063]but in that other respect she must be held as having wasted her

[021-064]opportunities.

[021-065]But then what eyes she had! Mrs. Pole was right there. They flashed

[021-066]upon you—not always softly; indeed not often softly, if you were a

[021-067]stranger to her; but whether softly or savagely, with a brilliancy

[021-068]that dazzled you as you looked at them. And who shall say of what

[021-069]colour they were? Green probably, for most eyes are green—green or

[021-070]grey, if green be thought uncomely for an eye-colour. But it was not

[021-071]their colour, but their fire, which struck one with such surprise.

[021-072]Lucy Robarts was thoroughly a brunette. Sometimes the dark tint

[021-073]of her cheek was exquisitely rich and lovely, and the fringes of

[021-074]her eyes were long and soft, and her small teeth, which one so

[021-075]seldom saw, were white as pearls, and her hair, though short, was

[021-076]beautifully soft—by no means black, but yet of so dark a shade of

[021-077]brown. Blanche, too, was noted for fine teeth. They were white and

[021-078]regular and lofty as a new row of houses in a French city. But then

[021-079]when she laughed she was all teeth; as she was all neck when she sat

[021-080]at the piano. But Lucy’s teeth!—it was only now and again, when in

[021-081]some sudden burst of wonder she would sit for a moment with her lips

[021-082]apart, that the fine finished lines and dainty pearl-white colour of

[021-083]that perfect set of ivory could be seen. Mrs. Pole would have said

[021-084]a word of her teeth also, but that to her they had never been made

[021-085]visible.

[021-086]“But they do say that she is the cleverest of them all,” Mrs. Pole

[021-087]had added, very properly. The people of Exeter had expressed such an

[021-088]opinion, and had been quite just in doing so. I do not know how it

[021-089]happens, but it always does happen, that everybody in every small

[021-090]town knows which is the brightest-witted in every family. In this

[021-091]respect Mrs. Pole had only expressed public opinion, and public

[021-092]opinion was right. Lucy Robarts was blessed with an intelligence

[021-093]keener than that of her brothers or sisters.

[021-094]“To tell the truth, Mark, I admire Lucy more than I do Blanche.”

[021-095]This had been said by Mrs. Robarts within a few hours of her having

[021-096]assumed that name. “She’s not a beauty, I know, but yet I do.”

[021-097]“My dearest Fanny!” Mark had answered in a tone of surprise.

[021-098]“I do then; of course people won’t think so; but I never seem to care

[021-099]about regular beauties. Perhaps I envy them too much.”

[021-100]What Mark said next need not be repeated, but everybody may be

[021-101]sure that it contained some gross flattery for his young bride. He

[021-102]remembered this, however, and had always called Lucy his wife’s pet.

[021-103]Neither of the sisters had since that been at Framley; and though

[021-104]Fanny had spent a week at Exeter on the occasion of Blanche’s

[021-105]marriage, it could hardly be said that she was very intimate with

[021-106]them. Nevertheless, when it became expedient that one of them should

[021-107]go to Framley, the remembrance of what his wife had said immediately

[021-108]induced Mark to make the offer to Lucy; and Jane, who was of a

[021-109]kindred soul with Blanche, was delighted to go to Creamclotted

[021-110]Hall. The acres of Heavybed House, down in that fat Totnes country,

[021-111]adjoined those of Creamclotted Hall, and Heavybed House still wanted

[021-112]a mistress.

[021-113]Fanny was delighted when the news reached her. It would of course

[021-114]be proper that one of his sisters should live with Mark under their

[021-115]present circumstances, and she was happy to think that that quiet

[021-116]little bright-eyed creature was to come and nestle with her under the

[021-117]same roof. The children should so love her—only not quite so much as

[021-118]they loved mamma; and the snug little room that looks out over the

[021-119]porch, in which the chimney never smokes, should be made ready for

[021-120]her; and she should be allowed her share of driving the pony—which

[021-121]was a great sacrifice of self on the part of Mrs. Robarts—and Lady

[021-122]Lufton’s best good-will should be bespoken. In fact, Lucy was not

[021-123]unfortunate in the destination that was laid out for her.

[021-124]Lady Lufton had of course heard of the doctor’s death, and had sent

[021-125]all manner of kind messages to Mark, advising him not to hurry home

[021-126]by any means until everything was settled at Exeter. And then she was

[021-127]told of the new-comer that was expected in the parish. When she heard

[021-128]that it was Lucy, the younger, she also was satisfied; for Blanche’s

[021-129]charms, though indisputable, had not been altogether to her taste. If

[021-130]a second Blanche were to arrive there what danger might there not be

[021-131]for young Lord Lufton!

[021-132]“Quite right,” said her ladyship, “just what he ought to do. I

[021-133]think I remember the young lady; rather small, is she not, and very

[021-134]retiring?”

[021-135]“Rather small and very retiring. What a description!” said Lord

[021-136]Lufton.

[021-137]“Never mind, Ludovic; some young ladies must be small, and some

[021-138]at least ought to be retiring. We shall be delighted to make her

[021-139]acquaintance.”

[021-140]“I remember your other sister-in-law very well,” said Lord Lufton.

[021-141]“She was a beautiful woman.”

[021-142]“I don’t think you will consider Lucy a beauty,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[021-143]“Small, retiring, and—” so far Lord Lufton had gone, when Mrs.

[021-144]Robarts finished by the word, “plain.” She had liked Lucy’s face, but

[021-145]she had thought that others probably did not do so.

[021-146]“Upon my word,” said Lady Lufton, “you don’t deserve to have a

[021-147]sister-in-law. I remember her very well, and can say that she is not

[021-148]plain. I was very much taken with her manner at your wedding, my

[021-149]dear; and thought more of her than I did of the beauty, I can tell

[021-150]you.”

[021-151]“I must confess I do not remember her at all,” said his lordship. And

[021-152]so the conversation ended.

[021-153]And then at the end of the fortnight Mark arrived with his sister.

[021-154]They did not reach Framley till long after dark—somewhere between

[021-155]six and seven—and by this time it was December. There was snow on

[021-156]the ground, and frost in the air, and no moon, and cautious men when

[021-157]they went on the roads had their horses’ shoes cocked. Such being the

[021-158]state of the weather Mark’s gig had been nearly filled with cloaks

[021-159]and shawls when it was sent over to Silverbridge. And a cart was sent

[021-160]for Lucy’s luggage, and all manner of preparations had been made.

[021-161]Three times had Fanny gone herself to see that the fire burned

[021-162]brightly in the little room over the porch, and at the moment that

[021-163]the sound of the wheels was heard she was engaged in opening her

[021-164]son’s mind as to the nature of an aunt. Hitherto papa and mamma and

[021-165]Lady Lufton were all that he had known, excepting, of course, the

[021-166]satellites of the nursery.

[021-167]And then in three minutes Lucy was standing by the fire. Those three

[021-168]minutes had been taken up in embraces between the husband and the

[021-169]wife. Let who would be brought as a visitor to the house, after a

[021-170]fortnight’s absence, she would kiss him before she welcomed any one

[021-171]else. But then she turned to Lucy, and began to assist her with her

[021-172]cloaks.

[021-173]“Oh, thank you,” said Lucy; “I’m not cold,—not very at least. Don’t

[021-174]trouble yourself: I can do it.” But here she had made a false boast,

[021-175]for her fingers had been so numbed that she could not do nor undo

[021-176]anything.
