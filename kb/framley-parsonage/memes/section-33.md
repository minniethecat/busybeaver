## Section 33

[033-001]He was a singular man; so humble and meek, so unutterably inefficient

[033-002]and awkward in the ordinary intercourse of life, but so bold and

[033-003]enterprising, almost eloquent, on the one subject which was the work

[033-004]of his mind! As he sat there, he looked into his companion’s face

[033-005]from out his sunken grey eyes with a gaze which made his victim

[033-006]quail. And then repeated his words: “I now make bold to ask you,

[033-007]Mr. Robarts, whether you are doing your best to lead such a life as

[033-008]may become a parish clergyman among his parishioners?” And again he

[033-009]paused for an answer.

[033-010]“There are but few of us,” said Mark in a low tone, “who could safely

[033-011]answer that question in the affirmative.”

[033-012]“But are there many, think you, among us who would find the question

[033-013]so unanswerable as yourself? And even were there many, would you,

[033-014]young, enterprising, and talented as you are, be content to be

[033-015]numbered among them? Are you satisfied to be a castaway after you

[033-016]have taken upon yourself Christ’s armour? If you will say so, I am

[033-017]mistaken in you, and will go my way.” There was again a pause, and

[033-018]then he went on. “Speak to me, my brother, and open your heart if it

[033-019]be possible.” And rising from his chair, he walked across the room,

[033-020]and laid his hand tenderly on Mark’s shoulder.

[033-021]Mark had been sitting lounging in his chair, and had at first, for a

[033-022]moment only, thought to brazen it out. But all idea of brazening had

[033-023]now left him. He had raised himself from his comfortable ease, and

[033-024]was leaning forward with his elbow on the table; but now, when he

[033-025]heard these words, he allowed his head to sink upon his arms, and he

[033-026]buried his face between his hands.

[033-027]“It is a terrible falling off,” continued Crawley: “terrible in the

[033-028]fall, but doubly terrible through that difficulty of returning. But

[033-029]it cannot be that it should content you to place yourself as one

[033-030]among those thoughtless sinners, for the crushing of whose sin you

[033-031]have been placed here among them. You become a hunting parson, and

[033-032]ride with a happy mind among blasphemers and mocking devils—you,

[033-033]whose aspirations were so high, who have spoken so often and so well

[033-034]of the duties of a minister of Christ; you, who can argue in your

[033-035]pride as to the petty details of your Church, as though the broad

[033-036]teachings of its great and simple lessons were not enough for your

[033-037]energies! It cannot be that I have had a hypocrite beside me in all

[033-038]those eager controversies!”

[033-039]“Not a hypocrite—not a hypocrite,” said Mark, in a tone which was

[033-040]almost reduced to sobbing.

[033-041]“But a castaway! Is it so that I must call you? No, Mr. Robarts,

[033-042]not a castaway; neither a hypocrite, nor a castaway; but one who

[033-043]in walking has stumbled in the dark and bruised his feet among

[033-044]the stones. Henceforth let him take a lantern in his hand, and

[033-045]look warily to his path, and walk cautiously among the thorns and

[033-046]rocks,—cautiously, but yet boldly, with manly courage, but Christian

[033-047]meekness, as all men should walk on their pilgrimage through this

[033-048]vale of tears.” And then without giving his companion time to stop

[033-049]him he hurried out of the room, and from the house, and without

[033-050]again seeing any others of the family, stalked back on his road to

[033-051]Hogglestock, thus tramping fourteen miles through the deep mud in

[033-052]performance of the mission on which he had been sent.

[033-053]It was some hours before Mr. Robarts left his room. As soon as he

[033-054]found that Crawley was really gone, and that he should see him no

[033-055]more, he turned the lock of his door, and sat himself down to think

[033-056]over his present life. At about eleven his wife knocked, not knowing

[033-057]whether that other strange clergyman were there or no, for none had

[033-058]seen his departure. But Mark, answering cheerily, desired that he

[033-059]might be left to his studies.

[033-060]Let us hope that his thoughts and mental resolves were then of

[033-061]service to him.

[033-062]CHAPTER XVI.

[033-063]MRS. PODGENS’ BABY.

[033-064]The hunting season had now nearly passed away, and the great ones

[033-065]of the Barsetshire world were thinking of the glories of London. Of

[033-066]these glories Lady Lufton always thought with much inquietude of

[033-067]mind. She would fain have remained throughout the whole year at

[033-068]Framley Court, did not certain grave considerations render such a

[033-069]course on her part improper in her own estimation. All the Lady

[033-070]Luftons of whom she had heard, dowager and ante-dowager, had always

[033-071]had their seasons in London, till old age had incapacitated them for

[033-072]such doings—sometimes for clearly long after the arrival of such

[033-073]period. And then she had an idea, perhaps not altogether erroneous,

[033-074]that she annually imported back with her into the country somewhat of

[033-075]the passing civilization of the times:—may we not say an idea that

[033-076]certainly was not erroneous? for how otherwise is it that the forms

[033-077]of new caps and remodelled shapes for women’s waists find their

[033-078]way down into agricultural parts, and that the rural eye learns

[033-079]to appreciate grace and beauty? There are those who think that

[033-080]remodelled waists and new caps had better be kept to the towns; but

[033-081]such people, if they would follow out their own argument, would wish

[033-082]to see ploughboys painted with ruddle and milkmaids covered with

[033-083]skins.

[033-084]For these and other reasons Lady Lufton always went to London in

[033-085]April, and stayed there till the beginning of June. But for her this

[033-086]was usually a period of penance. In London she was no very great

[033-087]personage. She had never laid herself out for greatness of that sort,

[033-088]and did not shine as a lady-patroness or state secretary in the

[033-089]female cabinet of fashion. She was dull and listless, and without

[033-090]congenial pursuits in London, and spent her happiest moments in

[033-091]reading accounts of what was being done at Framley, and in writing

[033-092]orders for further local information of the same kind.

[033-093]But on this occasion there was a matter of vital import to give

[033-094]an interest of its own to her visit to town. She was to entertain

[033-095]Griselda Grantly, and as far as might be possible to induce her son

[033-096]to remain in Griselda’s society. The plan of the campaign was to be

[033-097]as follows:—Mrs. Grantly and the archdeacon were in the first place

[033-098]to go up to London for a month, taking Griselda with them; and then,

[033-099]when they returned to Plumstead, Griselda was to go to Lady Lufton.

[033-100]This arrangement was not at all points agreeable to Lady Lufton, for

[033-101]she knew that Mrs. Grantly did not turn her back on the Hartletop

[033-102]people quite as cordially as she should do, considering the terms of

[033-103]the Lufton-Grantly family treaty. But then Mrs. Grantly might have

[033-104]alleged in excuse the slow manner in which Lord Lufton proceeded in

[033-105]the making and declaring of his love, and the absolute necessity

[033-106]which there is for two strings to one’s bow, when one string may be

[033-107]in any way doubtful. Could it be possible that Mrs. Grantly had heard

[033-108]anything of that unfortunate Platonic friendship with Lucy Robarts?

[033-109]There came a letter from Mrs. Grantly just about the end of March,

[033-110]which added much to Lady Lufton’s uneasiness, and made her more

[033-111]than ever anxious to be herself on the scene of action and to have

[033-112]Griselda in her own hands. After some communications of mere ordinary

[033-113]importance with reference to the London world in general and the

[033-114]Lufton-Grantly world in particular, Mrs. Grantly wrote confidentially

[033-115]about her daughter:

[033-116]“It would be useless to deny,” she said, with a mother’s pride and a

[033-117]mother’s humility, “that she is very much admired. She is asked out a

[033-118]great deal more than I can take her, and to houses to which I myself

[033-119]by no means wish to go. I could not refuse her as to Lady Hartletop’s

[033-120]first ball, for there will be nothing else this year like them; and

[033-121]of course when with you, dear Lady Lufton, that house will be out

[033-122]of the question. So indeed would it be with me, were I myself only

[033-123]concerned. The duke was there, of course, and I really wonder Lady

[033-124]Hartletop should not be more discreet in her own drawing-room when

[033-125]all the world is there. It is clear to me that Lord Dumbello admires

[033-126]Griselda much more than I could wish. She, dear girl, has such

[033-127]excellent sense that I do not think it likely that her head should

[033-128]be turned by it; but with how many girls would not the admiration of

[033-129]such a man be irresistible? The marquis, you know, is very feeble,

[033-130]and I am told that since this rage for building has come on, the

[033-131]Lancashire property is over two hundred thousand a year!! I do not

[033-132]think that Lord Dumbello has said much to her. Indeed it seems to me

[033-133]that he never does say much to any one. But he always stands up to

[033-134]dance with her, and I see that he is uneasy and fidgety when she

[033-135]stands up with any other partner whom he could care about. It was

[033-136]really embarrassing to see him the other night at Miss Dunstable’s,

[033-137]when Griselda was dancing with a certain friend of ours. But she did

[033-138]look very well that evening, and I have seldom seen her more

[033-139]animated!”

[033-140]All this, and a great deal more of the same sort in the same letter,

[033-141]tended to make Lady Lufton anxious to be in London. It was quite

[033-142]certain—there was no doubt of that, at any rate—that Griselda would

[033-143]see no more of Lady Hartletop’s meretricious grandeur when she had

[033-144]been transferred to Lady Lufton’s guardianship. And she, Lady Lufton,

[033-145]did wonder that Mrs. Grantly should have taken her daughter to such

[033-146]a house. All about Lady Hartletop was known to all the world. It was

[033-147]known that it was almost the only house in London at which the Duke

[033-148]of Omnium was constantly to be met. Lady Lufton herself would almost

[033-149]as soon think of taking a young girl to Gatherum Castle; and on these

[033-150]accounts she did feel rather angry with her friend Mrs. Grantly. But

[033-151]then perhaps she did not sufficiently calculate that Mrs. Grantly’s

[033-152]letter had been written purposely to produce such feelings—with the

[033-153]express view of awakening her ladyship to the necessity of action.

[033-154]Indeed in such a matter as this Mrs. Grantly was a more able woman

[033-155]than Lady Lufton—more able to see her way and to follow it out.

[033-156]The Lufton-Grantly alliance was in her mind the best, seeing that

[033-157]she did not regard money as everything. But failing that, the

[033-158]Hartletop-Grantly alliance was not bad. Regarding it as a second

[033-159]string to her bow, she thought that it was not at all bad.

[033-160]Lady Lufton’s reply was very affectionate. She declared how happy

[033-161]she was to know that Griselda was enjoying herself; she insinuated

[033-162]that Lord Dumbello was known to the world as a fool, and his mother

[033-163]as—being not a bit better than she ought to be; and then she added

[033-164]that circumstances would bring herself up to town four days sooner

[033-165]than she had expected, and that she hoped her dear Griselda would

[033-166]come to her at once. Lord Lufton, she said, though he would not sleep

[033-167]in Bruton Street—Lady Lufton lived in Bruton Street—had promised

[033-168]to pass there as much of his time as his parliamentary duties would

[033-169]permit.

[033-170]O Lady Lufton! Lady Lufton! did it not occur to you, when you wrote

[033-171]those last words, intending that they should have so strong an

[033-172]effect on the mind of your correspondent, that you were telling

[033-173]a—tarradiddle? Was it not the case that you had said to your son, in

[033-174]your own dear, kind, motherly way: “Ludovic, we shall see something

[033-175]of you in Bruton Street this year, shall we not? Griselda Grantly

[033-176]will be with me, and we must not let her be dull—must we?” And then

[033-177]had he not answered, “Oh, of course, mother,” and sauntered out of

[033-178]the room, not altogether graciously? Had he, or you, said a word

[033-179]about his parliamentary duties? Not a word! O Lady Lufton! have you

[033-180]not now written a tarradiddle to your friend?
