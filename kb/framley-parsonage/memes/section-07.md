## Section 7

[007-001]“Bishop,” said the rather sharp voice; and the bishop trotted across

[007-002]the room to the back of the sofa, on which his wife was sitting.

[007-003]“Miss Dunstable thinks that she will be able to come to us for a

[007-004]couple of days, after we leave the duke’s.”

[007-005]“I shall be delighted above all things,” said the bishop, bowing low

[007-006]to the dominant lady of the day. For be it known to all men, that

[007-007]Miss Dunstable was the great heiress of that name.

[007-008]“Mrs. Proudie is so very kind as to say that she will take me in,

[007-009]with my poodle, parrot, and pet old woman.”

[007-010]“I tell Miss Dunstable that we shall have quite room for any of her

[007-011]suite,” said Mrs. Proudie. “And that it will give us no trouble.”

[007-012]“‘The labour we delight in physics pain,’” said the gallant bishop,

[007-013]bowing low, and putting his hand upon his heart.

[007-014]In the meantime, Mr. Fothergill had got hold of Mark Robarts. Mr.

[007-015]Fothergill was a gentleman, and a magistrate of the county, but

[007-016]he occupied the position of managing man on the Duke of Omnium’s

[007-017]estates. He was not exactly his agent; that is to say, he did not

[007-018]receive his rents; but he “managed” for him, saw people, went about

[007-019]the county, wrote letters, supported the electioneering interest,

[007-020]did popularity when it was too much trouble for the duke to do it

[007-021]himself, and was, in fact, invaluable. People in West Barsetshire

[007-022]would often say that they did not know what _on earth_ the duke would

[007-023]do, if it were not for Mr. Fothergill. Indeed, Mr. Fothergill was

[007-024]useful to the duke.

[007-025]“Mr. Robarts,” he said, “I am very happy to have the pleasure of

[007-026]meeting you—very happy, indeed. I have often heard of you from our

[007-027]friend Sowerby.”

[007-028]Mark bowed, and said that he was delighted to have the honour of

[007-029]making Mr. Fothergill’s acquaintance.

[007-030]“I am commissioned by the Duke of Omnium,” continued Mr. Fothergill,

[007-031]“to say how glad he will be if you will join his grace’s party at

[007-032]Gatherum Castle, next week. The bishop will be there, and indeed

[007-033]nearly the whole set who are here now. The duke would have written

[007-034]when he heard that you were to be at Chaldicotes; but things were

[007-035]hardly quite arranged then, so his grace has left it for me to tell

[007-036]you how happy he will be to make your acquaintance in his own house.

[007-037]I have spoken to Sowerby,” continued Mr. Fothergill, “and he very

[007-038]much hopes that you will be able to join us.”

[007-039]Mark felt that his face became red when this proposition was made

[007-040]to him. The party in the county to which he properly belonged—he

[007-041]and his wife, and all that made him happy and respectable—looked

[007-042]upon the Duke of Omnium with horror and amazement; and now he had

[007-043]absolutely received an invitation to the duke’s house! A proposition

[007-044]was made to him that he should be numbered among the duke’s friends!

[007-045]And though in one sense he was sorry that the proposition was made

[007-046]to him, yet in another he was proud of it. It is not every young

[007-047]man, let his profession be what it may, who can receive overtures of

[007-048]friendship from dukes without some elation. Mark, too, had risen in

[007-049]the world, as far as he had yet risen, by knowing great people; and

[007-050]he certainly had an ambition to rise higher. I will not degrade him

[007-051]by calling him a tuft-hunter; but he undoubtedly had a feeling that

[007-052]the paths most pleasant for a clergyman’s feet were those which were

[007-053]trodden by the great ones of the earth.

[007-054]Nevertheless, at the moment he declined the duke’s invitation. He

[007-055]was very much flattered, he said, but the duties of his parish would

[007-056]require him to return direct from Chaldicotes to Framley.

[007-057]“You need not give me an answer to-night, you know,” said Mr.

[007-058]Fothergill. “Before the week is past, we will talk it over with

[007-059]Sowerby and the bishop. It will be a thousand pities, Mr. Robarts,

[007-060]if you will allow me to say so, that you should neglect such an

[007-061]opportunity of knowing his grace.”

[007-062]When Mark went to bed, his mind was still set against going to the

[007-063]duke’s; but, nevertheless, he did feel that it was a pity that he

[007-064]should not do so. After all, was it necessary that he should obey

[007-065]Lady Lufton in all things?

[007-066]CHAPTER IV.

[007-067]A MATTER OF CONSCIENCE.

[007-068]It is no doubt very wrong to long after a naughty thing. But

[007-069]nevertheless we all do so. One may say that hankering after naughty

[007-070]things is the very essence of the evil into which we have been

[007-071]precipitated by Adam’s fall. When we confess that we are all sinners,

[007-072]we confess that we all long after naughty things.

[007-073]And ambition is a great vice—as Mark Antony told us a long time

[007-074]ago—a great vice, no doubt, if the ambition of the man be with

[007-075]reference to his own advancement, and not to the advancement of

[007-076]others. But then, how many of us are there who are not ambitious in

[007-077]this vicious manner?

[007-078]And there is nothing viler than the desire to know great

[007-079]people—people of great rank, I should say; nothing worse than the

[007-080]hunting of titles and worshipping of wealth. We all know this, and

[007-081]say it every day of our lives. But presuming that a way into the

[007-082]society of Park Lane was open to us, and a way also into that of

[007-083]Bedford Row, how many of us are there who would prefer Bedford Row

[007-084]because it is so vile to worship wealth and title?

[007-085]I am led into these rather trite remarks by the necessity of putting

[007-086]forward some sort of excuse for that frame of mind in which the Rev.

[007-087]Mark Robarts awoke on the morning after his arrival at Chaldicotes.

[007-088]And I trust that the fact of his being a clergyman will not be

[007-089]allowed to press against him unfairly. Clergymen are subject to

[007-090]the same passions as other men; and, as far as I can see, give way

[007-091]to them, in one line or in another, almost as frequently. Every

[007-092]clergyman should, by canonical rule, feel a personal disinclination

[007-093]to a bishopric; but yet we do not believe that such personal

[007-094]disinclination is generally very strong.

[007-095]Mark’s first thoughts when he woke on that morning flew back to Mr.

[007-096]Fothergill’s invitation. The duke had sent a special message to say

[007-097]how peculiarly glad he, the duke, would be to make acquaintance

[007-098]with him, the parson! How much of this message had been of Mr.

[007-099]Fothergill’s own manufacture, that Mark Robarts did not consider.

[007-100]He had obtained a living at an age when other young clergymen are

[007-101]beginning to think of a curacy, and he had obtained such a living as

[007-102]middle-aged parsons in their dreams regard as a possible Paradise for

[007-103]their old years. Of course he thought that all these good things had

[007-104]been the results of his own peculiar merits. Of course he felt that

[007-105]he was different from other parsons,—more fitted by nature for

[007-106]intimacy with great persons, more urbane, more polished, and more

[007-107]richly endowed with modern clerical well-to-do aptitudes. He was

[007-108]grateful to Lady Lufton for what she had done for him; but perhaps

[007-109]not so grateful as he should have been.

[007-110]At any rate he was not Lady Lufton’s servant, nor even her dependant.

[007-111]So much he had repeated to himself on many occasions, and had gone

[007-112]so far as to hint the same idea to his wife. In his career as parish

[007-113]priest he must in most things be the judge of his own actions—and in

[007-114]many also it was his duty to be the judge of those of his patroness.

[007-115]The fact of Lady Lufton having placed him in the living, could by no

[007-116]means make her the proper judge of his actions. This he often said

[007-117]to himself; and he said as often that Lady Lufton certainly had a

[007-118]hankering after such a judgment-seat.

[007-119]Of whom generally did prime ministers and official bigwigs think it

[007-120]expedient to make bishops and deans? Was it not, as a rule, of those

[007-121]clergymen who had shown themselves able to perform their clerical

[007-122]duties efficiently, and able also to take their place with ease in

[007-123]high society? He was very well off certainly at Framley; but he

[007-124]could never hope for anything beyond Framley, if he allowed himself

[007-125]to regard Lady Lufton as a bugbear. Putting Lady Lufton and her

[007-126]prejudices out of the question, was there any reason why he ought not

[007-127]to accept the duke’s invitation? He could not see that there was any

[007-128]such reason. If any one could be a better judge on such a subject

[007-129]than himself, it must be his bishop. And it was clear that the bishop

[007-130]wished him to go to Gatherum Castle.

[007-131]The matter was still left open to him. Mr. Fothergill had especially

[007-132]explained that; and therefore his ultimate decision was as yet within

[007-133]his own power. Such a visit would cost him some money, for he knew

[007-134]that a man does not stay at great houses without expense; and then,

[007-135]in spite of his good income, he was not very flush of money. He had

[007-136]been down this year with Lord Lufton in Scotland. Perhaps it might be

[007-137]more prudent for him to return home.

[007-138]But then an idea came to him that it behoved him as a man and a

[007-139]priest to break through that Framley thraldom under which he felt

[007-140]that he did to a certain extent exist. Was it not the fact that he

[007-141]was about to decline this invitation from fear of Lady Lufton? and

[007-142]if so, was that a motive by which he ought to be actuated? It was

[007-143]incumbent on him to rid himself of that feeling. And in this spirit

[007-144]he got up and dressed.

[007-145]There was hunting again on that day; and as the hounds were to meet

[007-146]near Chaldicotes, and to draw some coverts lying on the verge of the

[007-147]Chace, the ladies were to go in carriages through the drives of the

[007-148]forest, and Mr. Robarts was to escort them on horseback. Indeed it

[007-149]was one of those hunting-days got up rather for the ladies than for

[007-150]the sport. Great nuisances they are to steady, middle-aged hunting

[007-151]men; but the young fellows like them because they have thereby an

[007-152]opportunity of showing off their sporting finery, and of doing a

[007-153]little flirtation on horseback. The bishop, also, had been minded to

[007-154]be of the party; so, at least, he had said on the previous evening;

[007-155]and a place in one of the carriages had been set apart for him: but

[007-156]since that, he and Mrs. Proudie had discussed the matter in private,

[007-157]and at breakfast his lordship declared that he had changed his mind.

[007-158]Mr. Sowerby was one of those men who are known to be very poor—as

[007-159]poor as debt can make a man—but who, nevertheless, enjoy all the

[007-160]luxuries which money can give. It was believed that he could not

[007-161]live in England out of jail but for his protection as a member of

[007-162]Parliament; and yet it seemed that there was no end to his horses

[007-163]and carriages, his servants and retinue. He had been at this work

[007-164]for a great many years, and practice, they say, makes perfect. Such

[007-165]companions are very dangerous. There is no cholera, no yellow-fever,

[007-166]no small-pox more contagious than debt. If one lives habitually among

[007-167]embarrassed men, one catches it to a certainty. No one had injured

[007-168]the community in this way more fatally than Mr. Sowerby. But still he

[007-169]carried on the game himself; and now on this morning carriages and

[007-170]horses thronged at his gate, as though he were as substantially rich

[007-171]as his friend the Duke of Omnium.

[007-172]“Robarts, my dear fellow,” said Mr. Sowerby, when they were well

[007-173]under way down one of the glades of the forest,—for the place

[007-174]where the hounds met was some four or five miles from the house of

[007-175]Chaldicotes,—“ride on with me a moment. I want to speak to you; and

[007-176]if I stay behind we shall never get to the hounds.” So Mark, who had

[007-177]come expressly to escort the ladies, rode on alongside of Mr. Sowerby

[007-178]in his pink coat.
