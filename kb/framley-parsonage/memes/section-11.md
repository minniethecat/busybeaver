## Section 11

[011-001]“Fanny is going, you know, mamma,” said Lady Meredith. “She will be

[011-002]home before you are down again.”

[011-003]“I cannot help it, my dear. Fanny must do as she pleases. I am not to

[011-004]be the judge of her actions. She has just told me so.”

[011-005]Mrs. Robarts had said nothing of the kind, but she was far too

[011-006]proud to point this out. So with a gentle step she retreated

[011-007]through the door, and then Lady Meredith, having tried what a

[011-008]conciliatory whisper with her mother would do, followed her. Alas,

[011-009]the conciliatory whisper was altogether ineffectual!

[011-010]The two ladies said nothing as they descended the stairs, but when

[011-011]they had regained the drawing-room they looked with blank horror into

[011-012]each other’s faces. What were they to do now? Of such a tragedy as

[011-013]this they had had no remotest preconception. Was it absolutely the

[011-014]case that Fanny Robarts was to walk out of Lady Lufton’s house as a

[011-015]declared enemy,—she who, before her marriage as well as since, had

[011-016]been almost treated as an adopted daughter of the family?

[011-017]“Oh, Fanny, why did you answer my mother in that way?” said Lady

[011-018]Meredith. “You saw that she was vexed. She had other things to vex

[011-019]her besides this about Mr. Robarts.”

[011-020]“And would not you answer any one who attacked Sir George?”

[011-021]“No, not my own mother. I would let her say what she pleased, and

[011-022]leave Sir George to fight his own battles.”

[011-023]“Ah, but it is different with you. You are her daughter, and Sir

[011-024]George—she would not dare to speak in that way as to Sir George’s

[011-025]doings.”

[011-026]“Indeed she would, if it pleased her. I am sorry I let you go up to

[011-027]her.”

[011-028]“It is as well that it should be over, Justinia. As those are her

[011-029]thoughts about Mr. Robarts, it is quite as well that we should know

[011-030]them. Even for all that I owe to her, and all the love I bear to you,

[011-031]I will not come to this house if I am to hear my husband abused;—not

[011-032]into any house.”

[011-033]“My dearest Fanny, we all know what happens when two angry people get

[011-034]together.”

[011-035]“I was not angry when I went up to her; not in the least.”

[011-036]“It is no good looking back. What are we to do now, Fanny?”

[011-037]“I suppose I had better go home,” said Mrs. Robarts. “I will go and

[011-038]put my things up, and then I will send James for them.”

[011-039]“Wait till after lunch, and then you will be able to kiss my mother

[011-040]before you leave us.”

[011-041]“No, Justinia; I cannot wait. I must answer Mr. Robarts by this post,

[011-042]and I must think what I have to say to him. I could not write that

[011-043]letter here, and the post goes at four.” And Mrs. Robarts got up from

[011-044]her chair, preparatory to her final departure.

[011-045]“I shall come to you before dinner,” said Lady Meredith; “and if I

[011-046]can bring you good tidings, I shall expect you to come back here with

[011-047]me. It is out of the question that I should go away from Framley

[011-048]leaving you and my mother at enmity with each other.”

[011-049]To this Mrs. Robarts made no answer; and in a very few minutes

[011-050]afterwards she was in her own nursery, kissing her children, and

[011-051]teaching the elder one to say something about papa. But, even as she

[011-052]taught him, the tears stood in her eyes, and the little fellow knew

[011-053]that everything was not right.

[011-054]And there she sat till about two, doing little odds and ends of

[011-055]things for the children, and allowing that occupation to stand as an

[011-056]excuse to her for not commencing her letter. But then there remained

[011-057]only two hours to her, and it might be that the letter would be

[011-058]difficult in the writing—would require thought and changes, and must

[011-059]needs be copied, perhaps more than once. As to the money, that she

[011-060]had in the house—as much, at least, as Mark now wanted, though the

[011-061]sending of it would leave her nearly penniless. She could, however,

[011-062]in case of personal need, resort to Davis as desired by him.

[011-063]So she got out her desk in the drawing-room and sat down and wrote

[011-064]her letter. It was difficult, though she found that it hardly took so

[011-065]long as she expected. It was difficult, for she felt bound to tell

[011-066]him the truth; and yet she was anxious not to spoil all his pleasure

[011-067]among his friends. She told him, however, that Lady Lufton was very

[011-068]angry, “unreasonably angry, I must say,” she put in, in order to

[011-069]show that she had not sided against him. “And indeed we have quite

[011-070]quarrelled, and this has made me unhappy, as it will you, dearest;

[011-071]I know that. But we both know how good she is at heart, and Justinia

[011-072]thinks that she had other things to trouble her; and I hope it will

[011-073]all be made up before you come home; only, dearest Mark, pray do not

[011-074]be longer than you said in your last letter.” And then there were

[011-075]three or four paragraphs about the babies and two about the schools,

[011-076]which I may as well omit.

[011-077]She had just finished her letter, and was carefully folding it for

[011-078]its envelope, with the two whole five-pound notes imprudently placed

[011-079]within it, when she heard a footstep on the gravel path which led

[011-080]up from a small wicket to the front-door. The path ran near the

[011-081]drawing-room window, and she was just in time to catch a glimpse

[011-082]of the last fold of a passing cloak. “It is Justinia,” she said

[011-083]to herself; and her heart became disturbed at the idea of again

[011-084]discussing the morning’s adventure. “What am I to do,” she had said

[011-085]to herself before, “if she wants me to beg her pardon? I will not own

[011-086]before her that he is in the wrong.”

[011-087]And then the door opened—for the visitor made her entrance without

[011-088]the aid of any servant—and Lady Lufton herself stood before her.

[011-089]“Fanny,” she said at once, “I have come to beg your pardon.”

[011-090]“Oh, Lady Lufton!”

[011-091]“I was very much harassed when you came to me just now;—by more

[011-092]things than one, my dear. But, nevertheless, I should not have spoken

[011-093]to you of your husband as I did, and so I have come to beg your

[011-094]pardon.”

[011-095]Mrs. Robarts was past answering by the time that this was said,—past

[011-096]answering at least in words; so she jumped up and, with her eyes

[011-097]full of tears, threw herself into her old friend’s arms. “Oh, Lady

[011-098]Lufton!” she sobbed forth again.

[011-099]“You will forgive me, won’t you?” said her ladyship, as she returned

[011-100]her young friend’s caress. “Well, that’s right. I have not been at

[011-101]all happy since you left my den this morning, and I don’t suppose you

[011-102]have. But, Fanny, dearest, we love each other too well and know each

[011-103]other too thoroughly, to have a long quarrel, don’t we?”

[011-104]“Oh, yes, Lady Lufton.”

[011-105]“Of course we do. Friends are not to be picked up on the road-side

[011-106]every day; nor are they to be thrown away lightly. And now sit down,

[011-107]my love, and let us have a little talk. There, I must take my bonnet

[011-108]off. You have pulled the strings so that you have almost choked me.”

[011-109]And Lady Lufton deposited her bonnet on the table and seated herself

[011-110]comfortably in the corner of the sofa.

[011-111]“My dear,” she said, “there is no duty which any woman owes to any

[011-112]other human being at all equal to that which she owes to her husband,

[011-113]and, therefore, you were quite right to stand up for Mr. Robarts this

[011-114]morning.”

[011-115]Upon this Mrs. Robarts said nothing, but she got her hand within that

[011-116]of her ladyship and gave it a slight squeeze.

[011-117]“And I loved you for what you were doing all the time. I did, my

[011-118]dear; though you were a little fierce, you know. Even Justinia admits

[011-119]that, and she has been at me ever since you went away. And indeed,

[011-120]I did not know that it was in you to look in that way out of those

[011-121]pretty eyes of yours.”

[011-122]“Oh, Lady Lufton!”

[011-123]“But I looked fierce enough too myself, I dare say; so we’ll say

[011-124]nothing more about that; will we? But now, about this good man of

[011-125]yours?”

[011-126]“Dear Lady Lufton, you must forgive him.”

[011-127]“Well: as you ask me, I will. We’ll have nothing more said about the

[011-128]duke, either now or when he comes back; not a word. Let me see—he’s

[011-129]to be back;—when is it?”

[011-130]“Wednesday week, I think.”

[011-131]“Ah, Wednesday. Well, tell him to come and dine up at the house on

[011-132]Wednesday. He’ll be in time, I suppose, and there shan’t be a word

[011-133]said about this horrid duke.”

[011-134]“I am so much obliged to you, Lady Lufton.”

[011-135]“But look here, my dear; believe me, he’s better off without such

[011-136]friends.”

[011-137]“Oh, I know he is; much better off.”

[011-138]“Well, I’m glad you admit that, for I thought you seemed to be in

[011-139]favour of the duke.”

[011-140]“Oh, no, Lady Lufton.”

[011-141]“That’s right, then. And now, if you’ll take my advice, you’ll use

[011-142]your influence, as a good, dear sweet wife as you are, to prevent his

[011-143]going there any more. I’m an old woman and he is a young man, and

[011-144]it’s very natural that he should think me behind the times. I’m not

[011-145]angry at that. But he’ll find that it’s better for him, better for

[011-146]him in every way, to stick to his old friends. It will be better for

[011-147]his peace of mind, better for his character as a clergyman, better

[011-148]for his pocket, better for his children and for you,—and better for

[011-149]his eternal welfare. The duke is not such a companion as he should

[011-150]seek;—nor if he is sought, should he allow himself to be led away.”

[011-151]And then Lady Lufton ceased, and Fanny Robarts kneeling at her feet

[011-152]sobbed, with her face hidden on her friend’s knees. She had not

[011-153]a word now to say as to her husband’s capability of judging for

[011-154]himself.

[011-155]“And now I must be going again; but Justinia has made me

[011-156]promise,—promise, mind you, most solemnly, that I would have you

[011-157]back to dinner to-night,—by force if necessary. It was the only

[011-158]way I could make my peace with her; so you must not leave me in the

[011-159]lurch.” Of course, Fanny said that she would go and dine at Framley

[011-160]Court.

[011-161]“And you must not send that letter, by any means,” said her ladyship

[011-162]as she was leaving the room, poking with her umbrella at the epistle,

[011-163]which lay directed on Mrs. Robarts’s desk. “I can understand very

[011-164]well what it contains. You must alter it altogether, my dear.” And

[011-165]then Lady Lufton went.

[011-166]Mrs. Robarts instantly rushed to her desk and tore open her letter.

[011-167]She looked at her watch and it was past four. She had hardly begun

[011-168]another when the postman came. “Oh, Mary,” she said, “do make him

[011-169]wait. If he’ll wait a quarter of an hour I’ll give him a shilling.”

[011-170]“There’s no need of that, ma’am. Let him have a glass of beer.”

[011-171]“Very well, Mary; but don’t give him too much, for fear he should

[011-172]drop the letters about. I’ll be ready in ten minutes.”

[011-173]And in five minutes she had scrawled a very different sort of a

[011-174]letter. But he might want the money immediately, so she would not

[011-175]delay it for a day.

[011-176]CHAPTER VI.

[011-177]MR. HAROLD SMITH’S LECTURE.

[011-178]On the whole the party at Chaldicotes was very pleasant, and the

[011-179]time passed away quickly enough. Mr. Robarts’s chief friend there,

[011-180]independently of Mr. Sowerby, was Miss Dunstable, who seemed to take

[011-181]a great fancy to him, whereas she was not very accessible to the

[011-182]blandishments of Mr. Supplehouse, nor more specially courteous

[011-183]even to her host than good manners required of her. But then Mr.

[011-184]Supplehouse and Mr. Sowerby were both bachelors, while Mark Robarts

[011-185]was a married man.
