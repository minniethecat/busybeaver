## Section 32

[032-001]In appearance he was the very opposite to Mark Robarts. He was a

[032-002]lean, slim, meagre man, with shoulders slightly curved, and pale,

[032-003]lank, long locks of ragged hair; his forehead was high, but his face

[032-004]was narrow; his small grey eyes were deeply sunken in his head, his

[032-005]nose was well-formed, his lips thin, and his mouth expressive. Nobody

[032-006]could look at him without seeing that there was a purpose and a

[032-007]meaning in his countenance. He always wore, in summer and winter,

[032-008]a long dusky gray coat, which buttoned close up to his neck and

[032-009]descended almost to his heels. He was full six feet high, but being

[032-010]so slight in build, he looked as though he were taller.

[032-011]He came at once at Lady Lufton’s bidding, putting himself into the

[032-012]gig beside the servant, to whom he spoke no single word during

[032-013]the journey. And the man, looking into his face, was struck with

[032-014]taciturnity. Now Mark Robarts would have talked with him the whole

[032-015]way from Hogglestock to Framley Court; discoursing partly as to

[032-016]horses and land, but partly also as to higher things.

[032-017]And then Lady Lufton opened her mind and told her griefs to Mr.

[032-018]Crawley, urging, however, through the whole length of her narrative,

[032-019]that Mr. Robarts was an excellent parish clergyman,—“just such a

[032-020]clergyman in his church as I would wish him to be,” she explained,

[032-021]with the view of saving herself from an expression of any of Mr.

[032-022]Crawley’s special ideas as to church teaching, and of confining him

[032-023]to the one subject-matter in hand; “but he got this living so young,

[032-024]Mr. Crawley, that he is hardly quite as steady as I could wish him to

[032-025]be. It has been as much my fault as his own in placing him in such a

[032-026]position so early in life.”

[032-027]“I think it has,” said Mr. Crawley, who might perhaps be a little

[032-028]sore on such a subject.

[032-029]“Quite so, quite so,” continued her ladyship, swallowing down with

[032-030]a gulp a certain sense of anger. “But that is done now, and is past

[032-031]cure. That Mr. Robarts will become a credit to his profession, I do

[032-032]not doubt, for his heart is in the right place and his sentiments are

[032-033]good; but I fear that at present he is succumbing to temptation.”

[032-034]“I am told that he hunts two or three times a week. Everybody round

[032-035]us is talking about it.”

[032-036]“No, Mr. Crawley; not two or three times a week; very seldom above

[032-037]once, I think. And then I do believe he does it more with the view of

[032-038]being with Lord Lufton than anything else.”

[032-039]“I cannot see that that would make the matter better,” said Mr.

[032-040]Crawley.

[032-041]“It would show that he was not strongly imbued with a taste which I

[032-042]cannot but regard as vicious in a clergyman.”

[032-043]“It must be vicious in all men,” said Mr. Crawley. “It is in itself

[032-044]cruel, and leads to idleness and profligacy.”

[032-045]Again Lady Lufton made a gulp. She had called Mr. Crawley thither to

[032-046]her aid, and felt that it would be inexpedient to quarrel with him.

[032-047]But she did not like to be told that her son’s amusement was idle and

[032-048]profligate. She had always regarded hunting as a proper pursuit for

[032-049]a country gentleman. It was, indeed, in her eyes one of the peculiar

[032-050]institutions of country life in England, and it may be almost said

[032-051]that she looked upon the Barsetshire hunt as something sacred. She

[032-052]could not endure to hear that a fox was trapped, and allowed her

[032-053]turkeys to be purloined without a groan. Such being the case, she

[032-054]did not like being told that it was vicious, and had by no means

[032-055]wished to consult Mr. Crawley on that matter. But nevertheless, she

[032-056]swallowed down her wrath.

[032-057]“It is at any rate unbecoming in a clergyman,” she said; “and as I

[032-058]know that Mr. Robarts places a high value on your opinion, perhaps

[032-059]you will not object to advise him to discontinue it. He might

[032-060]possibly feel aggrieved were I to interfere personally on such a

[032-061]question.”

[032-062]“I have no doubt he would,” said Mr. Crawley. “It is not within a

[032-063]woman’s province to give counsel to a clergyman on such a subject,

[032-064]unless she be very near and very dear to him—his wife, or mother, or

[032-065]sister.”

[032-066]“As living in the same parish, you know, and being, perhaps—” the

[032-067]leading person in it, and the one who naturally rules the others.

[032-068]Those would have been the fitting words for the expression of her

[032-069]ladyship’s ideas; but she remembered herself, and did not use them.

[032-070]She had made up her mind that, great as her influence ought to be,

[032-071]she was not the proper person to speak to Mr. Robarts as to his

[032-072]pernicious, unclerical habits, and she would not now depart from her

[032-073]resolve by attempting to prove that she was the proper person.

[032-074]“Yes,” said Mr. Crawley, “just so. All that would entitle him to

[032-075]offer you his counsel if he thought that your mode of life was such

[032-076]as to require it, but could by no means justify you in addressing

[032-077]yourself to him.”

[032-078]This was very hard upon Lady Lufton. She was endeavouring with all

[032-079]her woman’s strength to do her best, and endeavouring so to do it

[032-080]that the feelings of the sinner might be spared; and yet the ghostly

[032-081]comforter whom she had evoked to her aid, treated her as though she

[032-082]were arrogant and overbearing. She acknowledged the weakness of her

[032-083]own position with reference to her parish clergyman by calling in the

[032-084]aid of Mr. Crawley; and under such circumstances, he might, at any

[032-085]rate, have abstained from throwing that weakness in her teeth.

[032-086]“Well, sir; I hope my mode of life may not require it; but that is

[032-087]not exactly to the point: what I wish to know is, whether you will

[032-088]speak to Mr. Robarts?”

[032-089]“Certainly I will,” said he.

[032-090]“Then I shall be much obliged to you. But, Mr. Crawley, pray—pray,

[032-091]remember this: I would not on any account wish that you should be

[032-092]harsh with him. He is an excellent young man, and—”

[032-093]“Lady Lufton, if I do this, I can only do it in my own way, as best

[032-094]I may, using such words as God may give me at the time. I hope that

[032-095]I am harsh to no man; but it is worse than useless, in all cases, to

[032-096]speak anything but the truth.”

[032-097]“Of course—of course.”

[032-098]“If the ears be too delicate to hear the truth, the mind will be too

[032-099]perverse to profit by it.” And then Mr. Crawley got up to take his

[032-100]leave.

[032-101]But Lady Lufton insisted that he should go with her to luncheon. He

[032-102]hummed and ha’d and would fain have refused, but on this subject she

[032-103]was peremptory. It might be that she was unfit to advise a clergyman

[032-104]as to his duties, but in a matter of hospitality she did know what

[032-105]she was about. Mr. Crawley should not leave the house without

[032-106]refreshment. As to this, she carried her point; and Mr. Crawley—when

[032-107]the matter before him was cold roast-beef and hot potatoes,

[032-108]instead of the relative position of a parish priest and his

[032-109]parishioner—became humble, submissive, and almost timid. Lady Lufton

[032-110]recommended Madeira instead of Sherry, and Mr. Crawley obeyed at

[032-111]once, and was, indeed, perfectly unconscious of the difference. Then

[032-112]there was a basket of seakale in the gig for Mrs. Crawley; that he

[032-113]would have left behind had he dared, but he did not dare. Not a word

[032-114]was said to him as to the marmalade for the children which was hidden

[032-115]under the seakale, Lady Lufton feeling well aware that that would

[032-116]find its way to its proper destination without any necessity for his

[032-117]co-operation. And then Mr. Crawley returned home in the Framley Court

[032-118]gig.

[032-119]Three or four days after this he walked over to Framley Parsonage.

[032-120]This he did on a Saturday, having learned that the hounds never

[032-121]hunted on that day; and he started early, so that he might be sure to

[032-122]catch Mr. Robarts before he went out on his parish business. He was

[032-123]quite early enough to attain this object, for when he reached the

[032-124]parsonage door at about half-past nine, the vicar, with his wife and

[032-125]sister, were just sitting down to breakfast.

[032-126]“Oh, Crawley,” said Robarts, before the other had well spoken, “you

[032-127]are a capital fellow;” and then he got him into a chair, and Mrs.

[032-128]Robarts had poured him out tea, and Lucy had surrendered to him a

[032-129]knife and plate, before he knew under what guise to excuse his coming

[032-130]among them.

[032-131]“I hope you will excuse this intrusion,” at last he muttered; “but I

[032-132]have a few words of business to which I will request your attention

[032-133]presently.”

[032-134]“Certainly,” said Robarts, conveying a broiled kidney on to the plate

[032-135]before Mr. Crawley; “but there is no preparation for business like

[032-136]a good breakfast. Lucy, hand Mr. Crawley the buttered toast. Eggs,

[032-137]Fanny; where are the eggs?” And then John, in livery, brought in the

[032-138]fresh eggs. “Now we shall do. I always eat my eggs while they’re hot,

[032-139]Crawley, and I advise you to do the same.”

[032-140]To all this Mr. Crawley said very little, and he was not at all at

[032-141]home under the circumstances. Perhaps a thought did pass across his

[032-142]brain, as to the difference between the meal which he had left on his

[032-143]own table, and that which he now saw before him; and as to any cause

[032-144]which might exist for such difference. But, if so, it was a very

[032-145]fleeting thought, for he had far other matter now fully occupying his

[032-146]mind. And then the breakfast was over, and in a few minutes the two

[032-147]clergymen found themselves together in the parsonage study.

[032-148]“Mr. Robarts,” began the senior, when he had seated himself

[032-149]uncomfortably on one of the ordinary chairs at the farther side of

[032-150]the well-stored library table, while Mark was sitting at his ease

[032-151]in his own arm-chair by the fire, “I have called upon you on an

[032-152]unpleasant business.”

[032-153]Mark’s mind immediately flew off to Mr. Sowerby’s bill, but he could

[032-154]not think it possible that Mr. Crawley could have had anything to do

[032-155]with that.

[032-156]“But as a brother clergyman, and as one who esteems you much and

[032-157]wishes you well, I have thought myself bound to take this matter in

[032-158]hand.”

[032-159]“What matter is it, Crawley?”

[032-160]“Mr. Robarts, men say that your present mode of life is one that is

[032-161]not befitting a soldier in Christ’s army.”

[032-162]“Men say so! what men?”

[032-163]“The men around you, of your own neighbourhood; those who watch

[032-164]your life, and know all your doings; those who look to see you

[032-165]walking as a lamp to guide their feet, but find you consorting with

[032-166]horse-jockeys and hunters, galloping after hounds, and taking your

[032-167]place among the vainest of worldly pleasure-seekers. Those who have a

[032-168]right to expect an example of good living, and who think that they do

[032-169]not see it.”

[032-170]Mr. Crawley had gone at once to the root of the matter, and in doing

[032-171]so had certainly made his own task so much the easier. There is

[032-172]nothing like going to the root of the matter at once when one has on

[032-173]hand an unpleasant piece of business.

[032-174]“And have such men deputed you to come here?”

[032-175]“No one has or could depute me. I have come to speak my own mind, not

[032-176]that of any other. But I refer to what those around you think and

[032-177]say, because it is to them that your duties are due. You owe it to

[032-178]those around you to live a godly, cleanly life;—as you owe it also,

[032-179]in a much higher way, to your Father who is in heaven. I now make

[032-180]bold to ask you whether you are doing your best to lead such a life

[032-181]as that?” And then he remained silent, waiting for an answer.
