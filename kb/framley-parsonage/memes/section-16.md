## Section 16

[016-001]“But you had a diversion at the lecture, I am told,” continued the

[016-002]duke. “There was a second performer, was there not, who almost

[016-003]eclipsed poor Harold Smith?” And then Mr. Sowerby gave an amusing

[016-004]sketch of the little Proudie episode.

[016-005]“It has, of course, ruined your brother-in-law for ever as a

[016-006]lecturer,” said the duke, laughing.

[016-007]“If so, we shall feel ourselves under the deepest obligations to Mrs.

[016-008]Proudie,” said Mr. Sowerby. And then Harold Smith himself came up and

[016-009]received the duke’s sincere and hearty congratulations on the success

[016-010]of his enterprise at Barchester.

[016-011]Mark Robarts had now turned away, and his attention was suddenly

[016-012]arrested by the loud voice of Miss Dunstable, who had stumbled across

[016-013]some very dear friends in her passage through the rooms, and who by

[016-014]no means hid from the public her delight upon the occasion.

[016-015]“Well—well—well!” she exclaimed, and then she seized upon a very

[016-016]quiet-looking, well-dressed, attractive young woman who was walking

[016-017]towards her, in company with a gentleman. The gentleman and lady, as

[016-018]it turned out, were husband and wife. “Well—well—well! I hardly

[016-019]hoped for this.” And then she took hold of the lady and kissed her

[016-020]enthusiastically, and after that grasped both the gentleman’s hands,

[016-021]shaking them stoutly.

[016-022]“And what a deal I shall have to say to you!” she went on. “You’ll

[016-023]upset all my other plans. But, Mary, my dear, how long are you going

[016-024]to stay here? I go—let me see—I forget when, but it’s all put down

[016-025]in a book upstairs. But the next stage is at Mrs. Proudie’s. I shan’t

[016-026]meet you there, I suppose. And now, Frank, how’s the governor?”

[016-027]The gentleman called Frank declared that the governor was all

[016-028]right—“mad about the hounds, of course, you know.”

[016-029]“Well, my dear, that’s better than the hounds being mad about him,

[016-030]like the poor gentleman they’ve put into a statue. But talking of

[016-031]hounds, Frank, how badly they manage their foxes at Chaldicotes!

[016-032]I was out hunting all one day—”

[016-033]“You out hunting!” said the lady called Mary.

[016-034]“And why shouldn’t I go out hunting? I’ll tell you what, Mrs. Proudie

[016-035]was out hunting, too. But they didn’t catch a single fox; and, if you

[016-036]must have the truth, it seemed to me to be rather slow.”

[016-037]“You were in the wrong division of the county,” said the gentleman

[016-038]called Frank.

[016-039]“Of course I was. When I really want to practise hunting I’ll go to

[016-040]Greshamsbury; not a doubt about that.”

[016-041]“Or to Boxall Hill,” said the lady; “you’ll find quite as much zeal

[016-042]there as at Greshamsbury.”

[016-043]“And more discretion, you should add,” said the gentleman.

[016-044]“Ha! ha! ha!” laughed Miss Dunstable; “your discretion indeed! But

[016-045]you have not told me a word about Lady Arabella.”

[016-046]“My mother is quite well,” said the gentleman.

[016-047]“And the doctor? By-the-by, my dear, I’ve had such a letter from the

[016-048]doctor; only two days ago. I’ll show it you upstairs to-morrow. But

[016-049]mind, it must be a positive secret. If he goes on in this way he’ll

[016-050]get himself into the Tower, or Coventry, or a blue-book, or some

[016-051]dreadful place.”

[016-052]“Why; what has he said?”

[016-053]“Never you mind, Master Frank: I don’t mean to show you the letter,

[016-054]you may be sure of that. But if your wife will swear three times on

[016-055]a poker and tongs that she won’t reveal, I’ll show it to her. And so

[016-056]you are quite settled at Boxall Hill, are you?”

[016-057]“Frank’s horses are settled; and the dogs nearly so,” said Frank’s

[016-058]wife; “but I can’t boast much of anything else yet.”

[016-059]“Well, there’s a good time coming. I must go and change my things

[016-060]now. But, Mary, mind you get near me this evening; I have such a deal

[016-061]to say to you.” And then Miss Dunstable marched out of the room.

[016-062]All this had been said in so loud a voice that it was, as a matter

[016-063]of course, overheard by Mark Robarts—that part of the conversation

[016-064]of course I mean which had come from Miss Dunstable. And then Mark

[016-065]learned that this was young Frank Gresham of Boxall Hill, son of

[016-066]old Mr. Gresham of Greshamsbury. Frank had lately married a great

[016-067]heiress; a greater heiress, men said, even than Miss Dunstable;

[016-068]and as the marriage was hardly as yet more than six months old the

[016-069]Barsetshire world was still full of it.

[016-070]“The two heiresses seem to be very loving, don’t they?” said Mr.

[016-071]Supplehouse. “Birds of a feather flock together, you know. But they

[016-072]did say some little time ago that young Gresham was to have married

[016-073]Miss Dunstable himself.”

[016-074]“Miss Dunstable! why she might almost be his mother,” said Mark.

[016-075]“That makes but little difference. He was obliged to marry money, and

[016-076]I believe there is no doubt that he did at one time propose to Miss

[016-077]Dunstable.”

[016-078]“I have had a letter from Lufton,” Mr. Sowerby said to him the next

[016-079]morning. “He declares that the delay was all your fault. You were to

[016-080]have told Lady Lufton before he did anything, and he was waiting to

[016-081]write about it till he heard from you. It seems that you never said a

[016-082]word to her ladyship on the subject.”

[016-083]“I never did, certainly. My commission from Lufton was to break the

[016-084]matter to her when I found her in a proper humour for receiving it.

[016-085]If you knew Lady Lufton as well as I do, you would know that it is

[016-086]not every day that she would be in a humour for such tidings.”

[016-087]“And so I was to be kept waiting indefinitely because you two between

[016-088]you were afraid of an old woman! However I have not a word to say

[016-089]against her, and the matter is settled now.”

[016-090]“Has the farm been sold?”

[016-091]“Not a bit of it. The dowager could not bring her mind to suffer

[016-092]such profanation for the Lufton acres, and so she sold five

[016-093]thousand pounds out of the funds and sent the money to Lufton as a

[016-094]present;—sent it to him without saying a word, only hoping that it

[016-095]would suffice for his wants. I wish I had a mother, I know.”

[016-096]Mark found it impossible at the moment to make any remark upon what

[016-097]had been told him, but he felt a sudden qualm of conscience and a

[016-098]wish that he was at Framley instead of at Gatherum Castle at the

[016-099]present moment. He knew a good deal respecting Lady Lufton’s income

[016-100]and the manner in which it was spent. It was very handsome for a

[016-101]single lady, but then she lived in a free and open-handed style; her

[016-102]charities were noble; there was no reason why she should save money,

[016-103]and her annual income was usually spent within the year. Mark knew

[016-104]this, and he knew also that nothing short of an impossibility to

[016-105]maintain them would induce her to lessen her charities. She had now

[016-106]given away a portion of her principal to save the property of her

[016-107]son—her son, who was so much more opulent than herself,—upon whose

[016-108]means, too, the world made fewer effectual claims.

[016-109]And Mark knew, too, something of the purpose for which this money had

[016-110]gone. There had been unsettled gambling claims between Sowerby and

[016-111]Lord Lufton, originating in affairs of the turf. It had now been

[016-112]going on for four years, almost from the period when Lord Lufton had

[016-113]become of age. He had before now spoken to Robarts on the matter

[016-114]with much bitter anger, alleging that Mr. Sowerby was treating him

[016-115]unfairly, nay, dishonestly—that he was claiming money that was not

[016-116]due to him; and then he declared more than once that he would bring

[016-117]the matter before the Jockey Club. But Mark, knowing that Lord Lufton

[016-118]was not clear-sighted in these matters, and believing it to be

[016-119]impossible that Mr. Sowerby should actually endeavour to defraud his

[016-120]friend, had smoothed down the young lord’s anger, and recommended

[016-121]him to get the case referred to some private arbiter. All this had

[016-122]afterwards been discussed between Robarts and Mr. Sowerby himself,

[016-123]and hence had originated their intimacy. The matter was so referred,

[016-124]Mr. Sowerby naming the referee; and Lord Lufton, when the matter was

[016-125]given against him, took it easily. His anger was over by that time.

[016-126]“I’ve been clean done among them,” he said to Mark, laughing; “but

[016-127]it does not signify; a man must pay for his experience. Of course,

[016-128]Sowerby thinks it all right; I am bound to suppose so.” And then

[016-129]there had been some further delay as to the amount, and part of the

[016-130]money had been paid to a third person, and a bill had been given, and

[016-131]heaven and the Jews only know how much money Lord Lufton had paid

[016-132]in all; and now it was ended by his handing over to some wretched

[016-133]villain of a money-dealer, on behalf of Mr. Sowerby, the enormous sum

[016-134]of five thousand pounds, which had been deducted from the means of

[016-135]his mother, Lady Lufton!

[016-136]Mark, as he thought of all this, could not but feel a certain

[016-137]animosity against Mr. Sowerby—could not but suspect that he was a

[016-138]bad man. Nay, must he not have known that he was very bad? And yet he

[016-139]continued walking with him through the duke’s grounds, still talking

[016-140]about Lord Lufton’s affairs, and still listening with interest to

[016-141]what Sowerby told him of his own.

[016-142]“No man was ever robbed as I have been,” said he. “But I shall win

[016-143]through yet, in spite of them all. But those Jews, Mark”—he had

[016-144]become very intimate with him in these latter days—“whatever you do,

[016-145]keep clear of them. Why, I could paper a room with their signatures;

[016-146]and yet I never had a claim upon one of them, though they always have

[016-147]claims on me!”

[016-148]I have said above that this affair of Lord Lufton’s was ended; but

[016-149]it now appeared to Mark that it was not _quite_ ended. “Tell Lufton,

[016-150]you know,” said Sowerby, “that every bit of paper with his name has

[016-151]been taken up, except what that ruffian Tozer has. Tozer may have

[016-152]one bill, I believe,—something that was not given up when it was

[016-153]renewed. But I’ll make my lawyer Gumption get that up. It may cost

[016-154]ten pounds or twenty pounds, not more. You’ll remember that when you

[016-155]see Lufton, will you?”

[016-156]“You’ll see Lufton in all probability before I shall.”

[016-157]“Oh, did I not tell you? He’s going to Framley Court at once; you’ll

[016-158]find him there when you return.”

[016-159]“Find him at Framley!”

[016-160]“Yes; this little _cadeau_ from his mother has touched his filial

[016-161]heart. He is rushing home to Framley to pay back the dowager’s hard

[016-162]moidores in soft caresses. I wish I had a mother; I know that.”

[016-163]And Mark still felt that he feared Mr. Sowerby, but he could not make

[016-164]up his mind to break away from him.

[016-165]And there was much talk of politics just then at the castle. Not that

[016-166]the duke joined in it with any enthusiasm. He was a Whig—a huge

[016-167]mountain of a colossal Whig—all the world knew that. No opponent

[016-168]would have dreamed of tampering with his whiggery, nor would any

[016-169]brother Whig have dreamed of doubting it. But he was a Whig who gave

[016-170]very little practical support to any set of men, and very little

[016-171]practical opposition to any other set. He was above troubling himself

[016-172]with such sublunar matters. At election time he supported, and always

[016-173]carried, Whig candidates; and in return he had been appointed lord

[016-174]lieutenant of the county by one Whig minister, and had received the

[016-175]Garter from another. But these things were matters of course to a

[016-176]Duke of Omnium. He was born to be a lord lieutenant and a knight of

[016-177]the Garter.

[016-178]But not the less on account of his apathy, or rather quiescence,

[016-179]was it thought that Gatherum Castle was a fitting place in which

[016-180]politicians might express to each other their present hopes and

[016-181]future aims, and concoct together little plots in a half-serious

[016-182]and half-mocking way. Indeed it was hinted that Mr. Supplehouse and

[016-183]Harold Smith, with one or two others, were at Gatherum for this

[016-184]express purpose. Mr. Fothergill, too, was a noted politician, and

[016-185]was supposed to know the duke’s mind well; and Mr. Green Walker, the

[016-186]nephew of the marchioness, was a young man whom the duke desired to

[016-187]have brought forward. Mr. Sowerby also was the duke’s own member, and

[016-188]so the occasion suited well for the interchange of a few ideas.
