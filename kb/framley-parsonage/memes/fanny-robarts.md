# Fanny Robarts

Fanny Robarts is the wife of [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]], the vicar of Framley, and one of the most intimate friends of [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]. Her steadfast loyalty, quiet strength, and unwavering domestic virtue sustain her husband through his financial crisis and form the moral centre of the Framley household.

## Role in the Novel

Fanny Robarts serves as the anchor of domestic stability at Framley Parsonage. While her husband Mark becomes entangled in the political and financial machinations of the [[chaldicotes|Chaldicotes]] set, Fanny remains at home, preserving the household's moral and emotional integrity. Her role is not one of dramatic action but of quiet endurance — she represents the private sphere of Victorian womanhood against which the public follies of the male characters are measured (010-120) (011-014).

As Lady Lufton's closest confidante among the younger generation, Fanny also functions as a bridge between Framley Court and the parsonage. Lady Lufton treats her with a favour that amounts almost to maternal affection, granting her access to the "most private sanctum" of Framley Court — a privilege extended to almost no one else (044-005).

## Character Traits

- **Loyalty**: Fanny's devotion to her husband never wavers, even when Mark's financial recklessness threatens to destroy their domestic peace. She absorbs the shock of his disclosures without recrimination (010-120).
- **Humility**: When Lady Lufton confronts the Robarts household over perceived slights, Fanny kneels at her friend's feet, sobbing — a gesture of submission that paradoxically underscores her moral superiority to the proud and imperious Lady Lufton (011-151).
- **Domestic strength**: Fanny's world is bounded by hearth and nursery, yet within that sphere she exercises a quiet authority that even Lady Lufton respects.

## Relationships

- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: Fanny's husband, the vicar of Framley. Her steady affection provides the emotional ballast that his more mercurial nature requires, particularly after his financial entanglement with [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]].
- **[[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]**: The great lady of Framley Court treats Fanny as a favoured protégée. Their relationship is warm but unequal; Fanny's kneeling submission at a moment of crisis reveals both the depth of their bond and its inherent hierarchy (011-151) (044-005).
- **[[lucy-robarts|Lucy Robarts]]**: Fanny's sister-in-law, whose romance with [[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]] becomes one of Fanny's deepest concerns. When Lucy's happiness is at stake, Fanny finds herself caught between loyalty to her sister-in-law and deference to Lady Lufton.

## Key Scenes

- **The overflowing cup (Chapter 10)**: Fanny receives news that fills her "cup full to the overflowing," a moment of pure joy that is one of the novel's few unclouded domestic triumphs (010-120).
- **Kneeling at Lady Lufton's feet (Chapter 11)**: In a scene of intense emotional drama, Fanny kneels before Lady Lufton and sobs, her face hidden on her friend's knees. Lady Lufton's response — "And then Lady Lufton ceased" — marks a rare moment when the great lady's authority yields to Fanny's emotional truth (011-151).
- **The diplomatic question (Chapter 27)**: Lady Lufton considers whether to speak to her son or to Fanny about a delicate matter, a moment that reveals Fanny's centrality to the communications between Framley Court and the parsonage (027-099).

## Social & Historical Context

Fanny Robarts embodies the Victorian ideal of the clergyman's wife — a figure expected to combine domestic competence with moral exemplarity. In Trollope's Barsetshire, the parson's wife occupied a liminal social position: gentle by association with the Church, yet dependent on the goodwill of the local landowning family — in this case, the Luftons of Framley Court. Fanny's relationship with Lady Lufton perfectly illustrates this dynamic of patronage and personal affection that structured rural English society (044-005).
