## Section 90

[090-001]Lucy, as I have said, became mistress of the house at Hogglestock

[090-002]and made herself absolutely ascendant over Mr. Crawley. Jellies, and

[090-003]broth, and fruit, and even butter, came from Lufton Court, which she

[090-004]displayed on the table, absolutely on the cloth before him, and yet

[090-005]he bore it. I cannot say that he partook of these delicacies with any

[090-006]freedom himself, but he did drink his tea when it was given to him

[090-007]although it contained Framley cream;—and, had he known it, Bohea

[090-008]itself from the Framley chest. In truth, in these days, he had given

[090-009]himself over to the dominion of this stranger; and he said nothing

[090-010]beyond, “Well, well,” with two uplifted hands, when he came upon her

[090-011]as she was sewing the buttons on to his own shirts—sewing on the

[090-012]buttons and perhaps occasionally applying her needle elsewhere,—not

[090-013]without utility.

[090-014]He said to her at this period very little in the way of thanks. Some

[090-015]protracted conversations they did have, now and again, during the

[090-016]long evenings; but even in these he did not utter many words as to

[090-017]their present state of life. It was on religion chiefly that he

[090-018]spoke, not lecturing her individually, but laying down his ideas as

[090-019]to what the life of a Christian should be, and especially what should

[090-020]be the life of a minister. “But though I can see this, Miss Robarts,”

[090-021]he said, “I am bound to say that no one has fallen off so frequently

[090-022]as myself. I have renounced the devil and all his works; but it is by

[090-023]word of mouth only—by word of mouth only. How shall a man crucify

[090-024]the old Adam that is within him, unless he throw himself prostrate in

[090-025]the dust and acknowledge that all his strength is weaker than water?”

[090-026]To this, often as it might be repeated, she would listen patiently,

[090-027]comforting him by such words as her theology would supply; but then,

[090-028]when this was over, she would again resume her command and enforce

[090-029]from him a close obedience to her domestic behests.

[090-030]At the end of the month Lord Lufton came back to Framley Court. His

[090-031]arrival there was quite unexpected; though, as he pointed out when

[090-032]his mother expressed some surprise, he had returned exactly at the

[090-033]time named by him before he started.

[090-034]“I need not say, Ludovic, how glad I am to have you,” said she,

[090-035]looking to his face and pressing his arm; “the more so, indeed,

[090-036]seeing that I hardly expected it.”

[090-037]He said nothing to his mother about Lucy the first evening, although

[090-038]there was some conversation respecting the Robarts family.

[090-039]“I am afraid Mr. Robarts has embarrassed himself,” said Lady Lufton,

[090-040]looking very seriously. “Rumours reach me which are most distressing.

[090-041]I have said nothing to anybody as yet—not even to Fanny; but I can

[090-042]see in her face, and hear in the tones of her voice, that she is

[090-043]suffering some great sorrow.”

[090-044]“I know all about it,” said Lord Lufton.

[090-045]“You know all about it, Ludovic?”

[090-046]“Yes; it is through that precious friend of mine, Mr. Sowerby, of

[090-047]Chaldicotes. He has accepted bills for Sowerby; indeed, he told me

[090-048]so.”

[090-049]“What business had he at Chaldicotes? What had he to do with such

[090-050]friends as that? I do not know how I am to forgive him.”

[090-051]“It was through me that he became acquainted with Sowerby. You must

[090-052]remember that, mother.”

[090-053]“I do not see that that is any excuse. Is he to consider that

[090-054]all your acquaintances must necessarily be his friends also? It

[090-055]is reasonable to suppose that you in your position must live

[090-056]occasionally with a great many people who are altogether unfit

[090-057]companions for him as a parish clergyman. He will not remember this,

[090-058]and he must be taught it. What business had he to go to Gatherum

[090-059]Castle?”

[090-060]“He got his stall at Barchester by going there.”

[090-061]“He would be much better without his stall, and Fanny has the sense

[090-062]to know this. What does he want with two houses? Prebendal stalls are

[090-063]for older men than he—for men who have earned them, and who at the

[090-064]end of their lives want some ease. I wish with all my heart that he

[090-065]had never taken it.”

[090-066]“Six hundred a year has its charms all the same,” said Lufton,

[090-067]getting up and strolling out of the room.

[090-068]“If Mark really be in any difficulty,” he said, later in the evening,

[090-069]“we must put him on his legs.”

[090-070]“You mean, pay his debts?”

[090-071]“Yes; he has no debts except these acceptances of Sowerby’s.”

[090-072]“How much will it be, Ludovic?”

[090-073]“A thousand pounds, perhaps, more or less. I’ll find the money,

[090-074]mother; only I shan’t be able to pay you quite as soon as I

[090-075]intended.” Whereupon his mother got up, and throwing her arms round

[090-076]his neck declared that she would never forgive him if he ever said

[090-077]a word more about her little present to him. I suppose there is no

[090-078]pleasure a mother can have more attractive than giving away her money

[090-079]to an only son.

[090-080]Lucy’s name was first mentioned at breakfast the next morning. Lord

[090-081]Lufton had made up his mind to attack his mother on the subject early

[090-082]in the morning—before he went up to the parsonage; but as matters

[090-083]turned out Miss Robarts’ doings were necessarily brought under

[090-084]discussion without reference to Lord Lufton’s special aspirations

[090-085]regarding her. The fact of Mrs. Crawley’s illness had been mentioned,

[090-086]and Lady Lufton had stated how it had come to pass that all the

[090-087]Crawleys’ children were at the parsonage.

[090-088]“I must say that Fanny has behaved excellently,” said Lady Lufton.

[090-089]“It was just what might have been expected from her. And indeed,” she

[090-090]added, speaking in an embarrassed tone, “so has Miss Robarts. Miss

[090-091]Robarts has remained at Hogglestock and nursed Mrs. Crawley through

[090-092]the whole.”

[090-093]“Remained at Hogglestock—through the fever!” exclaimed his lordship.

[090-094]“Yes, indeed,” said Lady Lufton.

[090-095]“And is she there now?”

[090-096]“Oh, yes; I am not aware that she thinks of leaving just yet.”

[090-097]“Then I say that it is a great shame—a scandalous shame!”

[090-098]“But, Ludovic, it was her own doing.”

[090-099]“Oh, yes; I understand. But why should she be sacrificed? Were there

[090-100]no nurses in the country to be hired, but that she must go and remain

[090-101]there for a month at the bedside of a pestilent fever? There is no

[090-102]justice in it.”

[090-103]“Justice, Ludovic? I don’t know about justice, but there was great

[090-104]Christian charity. Mrs. Crawley has probably owed her life to Miss

[090-105]Robarts.”

[090-106]“Has she been ill? Is she ill? I insist upon knowing whether she

[090-107]is ill. I shall go over to Hogglestock myself immediately after

[090-108]breakfast.”

[090-109]To this Lady Lufton made no reply. If Lord Lufton chose to go to

[090-110]Hogglestock she could not prevent him. She thought, however, that it

[090-111]would be much better that he should stay away. He would be quite as

[090-112]open to the infection as Lucy Robarts; and, moreover, Mrs. Crawley’s

[090-113]bedside would be as inconvenient a place as might be selected for

[090-114]any interview between two lovers. Lady Lufton felt at the present

[090-115]moment that she was cruelly treated by circumstances with reference

[090-116]to Miss Robarts. Of course it would have been her part to lessen,

[090-117]if she could do so without injustice, that high idea which her

[090-118]son entertained of the beauty and worth of the young lady; but,

[090-119]unfortunately, she had been compelled to praise her and to load her

[090-120]name with all manner of eulogy. Lady Lufton was essentially a true

[090-121]woman, and not even with the object of carrying out her own views in

[090-122]so important a matter would she be guilty of such deception as she

[090-123]might have practised by simply holding her tongue; but nevertheless

[090-124]she could hardly reconcile herself to the necessity of singing Lucy’s

[090-125]praises.

[090-126]After breakfast Lady Lufton got up from her chair, but hung about the

[090-127]room without making any show of leaving. In accordance with her usual

[090-128]custom she would have asked her son what he was going to do; but

[090-129]she did not dare so to inquire now. Had he not declared, only a few

[090-130]minutes since, whither he would go? “I suppose I shall see you at

[090-131]lunch?” at last she said.

[090-132]“At lunch? Well, I don’t know. Look here, mother. What am I to say to

[090-133]Miss Robarts when I see her?” and he leaned with his back against the

[090-134]chimney-piece as he interrogated his mother.

[090-135]“What are you to say to her, Ludovic?”

[090-136]“Yes; what am I to say,—as coming from you? Am I to tell her that

[090-137]you will receive her as your daughter-in-law?”

[090-138]“Ludovic, I have explained all that to Miss Robarts herself.”

[090-139]“Explained what?”

[090-140]“I have told her that I did not think that such a marriage would make

[090-141]either you or her happy.”

[090-142]“And why have you told her so? Why have you taken upon yourself to

[090-143]judge for me in such a matter, as though I were a child? Mother, you

[090-144]must unsay what you have said.”

[090-145]Lord Lufton, as he spoke, looked full into his mother’s face; and

[090-146]he did so, not as though he were begging from her a favour, but

[090-147]issuing to her a command. She stood near him, with one hand on the

[090-148]breakfast-table, gazing at him almost furtively, not quite daring

[090-149]to meet the full view of his eye. There was only one thing on earth

[090-150]which Lady Lufton feared, and that was her son’s displeasure. The

[090-151]sun of her earthly heaven shone upon her through the medium of his

[090-152]existence. If she were driven to quarrel with him, as some ladies of

[090-153]her acquaintance were driven to quarrel with their sons, the world

[090-154]to her would be over. Not but what facts might be so strong as to

[090-155]make it absolutely necessary that she should do this. As some people

[090-156]resolve that, under certain circumstances, they will commit suicide,

[090-157]so she could see that, under certain circumstances, she must consent

[090-158]even to be separated from him. She would not do wrong,—not that

[090-159]which she knew to be wrong,—even for his sake. If it were necessary

[090-160]that all her happiness should collapse and be crushed in ruin around

[090-161]her, she must endure it, and wait God’s time to relieve her from so

[090-162]dark a world. The light of the sun was very dear to her, but even

[090-163]that might be purchased at too dear a cost.

[090-164]“I told you before, mother, that my choice was made, and I asked you

[090-165]then to give your consent; you have now had time to think about it,

[090-166]and therefore I have come to ask you again. I have reason to know

[090-167]that there will be no impediment to my marriage if you will frankly

[090-168]hold out your hand to Lucy.”

[090-169]The matter was altogether in Lady Lufton’s hands, but, fond as she

[090-170]was of power, she absolutely wished that it were not so. Had her son

[090-171]married without asking her and then brought Lucy home as his wife,

[090-172]she would undoubtedly have forgiven him; and much as she might have

[090-173]disliked the match, she would, ultimately, have embraced the bride.

[090-174]But now she was compelled to exercise her judgment. If he married

[090-175]imprudently, it would be her doing. How was she to give her expressed

[090-176]consent to that which she believed to be wrong?

[090-177]“Do you know anything against her; any reason why she should not be

[090-178]my wife?” continued he.

[090-179]“If you mean as regards her moral conduct, certainly not,” said Lady

[090-180]Lufton. “But I could say as much as that in favour of a great many

[090-181]young ladies whom I should regard as very ill suited for such a

[090-182]marriage.”

[090-183]“Yes; some might be vulgar, some might be ill-tempered, some might be

[090-184]ugly; others might be burdened with disagreeable connections. I can

[090-185]understand that you should object to a daughter-in-law under any of

[090-186]these circumstances. But none of these things can be said of Miss

[090-187]Robarts. I defy you to say that she is not in all respects what a

[090-188]lady should be.”
