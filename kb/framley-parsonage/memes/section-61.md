## Section 61

[061-001]“There will be no contest, of course,” said Lady Lufton,

[061-002]confidentially. “There seldom is in East Barsetshire, I am happy to

[061-003]say. But if there were, every tenant at Framley would vote on the

[061-004]right side; I can assure you of that. Lord Lufton was saying so to me

[061-005]only this morning.”

[061-006]Frank Gresham made a pretty little speech in reply, such as young

[061-007]sucking politicians are expected to make; and this, with sundry other

[061-008]small courteous murmurings, detained the Lufton party for a minute or

[061-009]two in the ante-chamber. In the meantime the world was pressing on

[061-010]and passing through to the four or five large reception-rooms—the

[061-011]noble suite, which was already piercing poor Mrs. Proudie’s heart

[061-012]with envy to the very core. “These are the sort of rooms,” she said

[061-013]to herself unconsciously, “which ought to be provided by the country

[061-014]for the use of its bishops.”

[061-015]“But the people are not brought enough together,” she said to her

[061-016]lord.

[061-017]“No, no; I don’t think they are,” said the bishop.

[061-018]“And that is so essential for a conversazione,” continued Mrs.

[061-019]Proudie. “Now in Gloucester Place—.” But we will not record all

[061-020]her adverse criticisms, as Lady Lufton is waiting for us in the

[061-021]ante-room.

[061-022]And now another arrival of moment had taken place;—an arrival

[061-023]indeed of very great moment. To tell the truth, Miss Dunstable’s

[061-024]heart had been set upon having two special persons; and though

[061-025]no stone had been left unturned,—no stone which could be turned

[061-026]with discretion,—she was still left in doubt as to both these two

[061-027]wondrous potentates. At the very moment of which we are now speaking,

[061-028]light and airy as she appeared to be—for it was her character to be

[061-029]light and airy—her mind was torn with doubts. If the wished-for two

[061-030]would come, her evening would be thoroughly successful; but if not,

[061-031]all her trouble would have been thrown away, and the thing would

[061-032]have been a failure; and there were circumstances connected with the

[061-033]present assembly which made Miss Dunstable very anxious that she

[061-034]should not fail. That the two great ones of the earth were Tom Towers

[061-035]of the _Jupiter_, and the Duke of Omnium, need hardly be expressed in

[061-036]words.

[061-037]And now, at this very moment, as Lady Lufton was making her civil

[061-038]speeches to young Gresham, apparently in no hurry to move on, and

[061-039]while Miss Dunstable was endeavouring to whisper something into the

[061-040]doctor’s ear, which would make him feel himself at home in this new

[061-041]world, a sound was heard which made that lady know that half her wish

[061-042]had at any rate been granted to her. A sound was heard—but only by

[061-043]her own and one other attentive pair of ears. Mrs. Harold Smith had

[061-044]also caught the name, and knew that the duke was approaching.

[061-045]There was great glory and triumph in this; but why had his grace come

[061-046]at so unchancy a moment? Miss Dunstable had been fully aware of the

[061-047]impropriety of bringing Lady Lufton and the Duke of Omnium into the

[061-048]same house at the same time; but when she had asked Lady Lufton, she

[061-049]had been led to believe that there was no hope of obtaining the duke;

[061-050]and then, when that hope had dawned upon her, she had comforted

[061-051]herself with the reflection that the two suns, though they might for

[061-052]some few minutes be in the same hemisphere, could hardly be expected

[061-053]to clash, or come across each other’s orbits. Her rooms were large

[061-054]and would be crowded; the duke would probably do little more than

[061-055]walk through them once, and Lady Lufton would certainly be surrounded

[061-056]by persons of her own class. Thus Miss Dunstable had comforted

[061-057]herself. But now all things were going wrong, and Lady Lufton would

[061-058]find herself in close contiguity to the nearest representative of

[061-059]Satanic agency, which, according to her ideas, was allowed to walk

[061-060]this nether English world of ours. Would she scream? or indignantly

[061-061]retreat out of the house?—or would she proudly raise her head, and

[061-062]with outstretched hand and audible voice, boldly defy the devil and

[061-063]all his works? In thinking of these things as the duke approached

[061-064]Miss Dunstable almost lost her presence of mind.

[061-065]But Mrs. Harold Smith did not lose hers. “So here at last is the

[061-066]duke,” she said, in a tone intended to catch the express attention of

[061-067]Lady Lufton.

[061-068]Mrs. Smith had calculated that there might still be time for her

[061-069]ladyship to pass on and avoid the interview. But Lady Lufton, if she

[061-070]heard the words, did not completely understand them. At any rate

[061-071]they did not convey to her mind at the moment the meaning they were

[061-072]intended to convey. She paused to whisper a last little speech to

[061-073]Frank Gresham, and then looking round, found that the gentleman who

[061-074]was pressing against her dress was—the Duke of Omnium!

[061-075]On this great occasion, when the misfortune could no longer be

[061-076]avoided, Miss Dunstable was by no means beneath herself or her

[061-077]character. She deplored the calamity, but she now saw that it was

[061-078]only left to her to make the best of it. The duke had honoured her

[061-079]by coming to her house, and she was bound to welcome him, though in

[061-080]doing so she should bring Lady Lufton to her last gasp.

[061-081]“Duke,” she said, “I am greatly honoured by this kindness on the part

[061-082]of your grace. I hardly expected that you would be so good to me.”

[061-083]“The goodness is all on the other side,” said the duke, bowing over

[061-084]her hand.

[061-085]And then in the usual course of things this would have been all. The

[061-086]duke would have walked on and shown himself, would have said a word

[061-087]or two to Lady Hartletop, to the bishop, to Mr. Gresham, and such

[061-088]like, and would then have left the rooms by another way, and quietly

[061-089]escaped. This was the duty expected from him, and this he would have

[061-090]done, and the value of the party would have been increased thirty per

[061-091]cent. by such doing; but now, as it was, the news-mongers of the West

[061-092]End were likely to get much more out of him.

[061-093]Circumstances had so turned out that he had absolutely been pressed

[061-094]close against Lady Lufton, and she, when she heard the voice, and was

[061-095]made positively acquainted with the fact of the great man’s presence

[061-096]by Miss Dunstable’s words, turned round quickly, but still with much

[061-097]feminine dignity, removing her dress from the contact. In doing this

[061-098]she was brought absolutely face to face with the duke, so that each

[061-099]could not but look full at the other. “I beg your pardon,” said the

[061-100]duke. They were the only words that had ever passed between them,

[061-101]nor have they spoken to each other since; but simple as they were,

[061-102]accompanied by the little by-play of the speakers, they gave rise

[061-103]to a considerable amount of ferment in the fashionable world. Lady

[061-104]Lufton, as she retreated back on to Dr. Easyman, curtseyed low;

[061-105]she curtseyed low and slowly, and with a haughty arrangement of

[061-106]her drapery that was all her own; but the curtsey, though it was

[061-107]eloquent, did not say half so much,—did not reprobate the habitual

[061-108]iniquities of the duke with a voice nearly as potent as that which

[061-109]was expressed in the gradual fall of her eye and the gradual pressure

[061-110]of her lips. When she commenced her curtsey she was looking full

[061-111]in her foe’s face. By the time that she had completed it her eyes

[061-112]were turned upon the ground, but there was an ineffable amount of

[061-113]scorn expressed in the lines of her mouth. She spoke no word, and

[061-114]retreated, as modest virtue and feminine weakness must ever retreat,

[061-115]before barefaced vice and virile power; but nevertheless she was held

[061-116]by all the world to have had the best of the encounter. The duke,

[061-117]as he begged her pardon, wore in his countenance that expression of

[061-118]modified sorrow which is common to any gentleman who is supposed

[061-119]by himself to have incommoded a lady. But over and above this,—or

[061-120]rather under it,—there was a slight smile of derision, as though

[061-121]it were impossible for him to look upon the bearing of Lady Lufton

[061-122]without some amount of ridicule. All this was legible to eyes so

[061-123]keen as those of Miss Dunstable and Mrs. Harold Smith, and the duke

[061-124]was known to be a master of this silent inward sarcasm; but even by

[061-125]them,—by Miss Dunstable and Mrs. Harold Smith,—it was admitted that

[061-126]Lady Lufton had conquered. When her ladyship again looked up, the

[061-127]duke had passed on; she then resumed the care of Miss Grantly’s hand,

[061-128]and followed in among the company.

[061-129][Illustration: Lady Lufton and the Duke of Omnium.]

[061-130]“That is what I call unfortunate,” said Miss Dunstable, as soon as

[061-131]both belligerents had departed from the field of battle. “The fates

[061-132]sometimes will be against one.”

[061-133]“But they have not been at all against you here,” said Mrs. Harold

[061-134]Smith. “If you could arrive at her ladyship’s private thoughts

[061-135]to-morrow morning, you would find her to be quite happy in having

[061-136]met the duke. It will be years before she has done boasting of her

[061-137]triumph, and it will be talked of by the young ladies of Framley for

[061-138]the next three generations.”

[061-139]The Gresham party, including Dr. Thorne, had remained in the

[061-140]ante-chamber during the battle. The whole combat did not occupy above

[061-141]two minutes, and the three of them were hemmed off from escape by

[061-142]Lady Lufton’s retreat into Dr. Easyman’s lap; but now they, too,

[061-143]essayed to pass on.

[061-144]“What, you will desert me,” said Miss Dunstable. “Very well; but I

[061-145]shall find you out by-and-by. Frank, there is to be some dancing in

[061-146]one of the rooms,—just to distinguish the affair from Mrs. Proudie’s

[061-147]conversazione. It would be stupid, you know, if all conversaziones

[061-148]were alike; wouldn’t it? So I hope you will go and dance.”

[061-149]“There will, I presume, be another variation at feeding time,” said

[061-150]Mrs. Harold Smith.

[061-151]“Oh, yes; certainly; I am the most vulgar of all wretches in

[061-152]that respect. I do love to set people eating and drinking.—Mr.

[061-153]Supplehouse, I am delighted to see you; but do tell me—” and then

[061-154]she whispered with great energy into the ear of Mr. Supplehouse, and

[061-155]Mr. Supplehouse again whispered into her ear. “You think he will,

[061-156]then?” said Miss Dunstable.

[061-157]Mr. Supplehouse assented; he did think so; but he had no warrant for

[061-158]stating the circumstance as a fact. And then he passed on, hardly

[061-159]looking at Mrs. Harold Smith as he passed.

[061-160]“What a hang-dog countenance he has,” said that lady.

[061-161]“Ah! you’re prejudiced, my dear, and no wonder; as for myself I

[061-162]always liked Supplehouse. He means mischief; but then mischief is his

[061-163]trade, and he does not conceal it. If I were a politician I should as

[061-164]soon think of being angry with Mr. Supplehouse for turning against me

[061-165]as I am now with a pin for pricking me. It’s my own awkwardness, and

[061-166]I ought to have known how to use the pin more craftily.”

[061-167]“But you must detest a man who professes to stand by his party, and

[061-168]then does his best to ruin it.”

[061-169]“So many have done that, my dear; and with much more success than Mr.

[061-170]Supplehouse! All is fair in love and war,—why not add politics to

[061-171]the list? If we could only agree to do that, it would save us from

[061-172]such a deal of heartburning, and would make none of us a bit the

[061-173]worse.”

[061-174]Miss Dunstable’s rooms, large as they were—“a noble suite of

[061-175]rooms certainly, though perhaps a little too—too—too scattered,

[061-176]we will say, eh, bishop?”—were now nearly full, and would have

[061-177]been inconveniently crowded, were it not that many who came only

[061-178]remained for half-an-hour or so. Space, however, had been kept for

[061-179]the dancers—much to Mrs. Proudie’s consternation. Not that she

[061-180]disapproved of dancing in London, as a rule; but she was indignant

[061-181]that the laws of a conversazione, as re-established by herself in the

[061-182]fashionable world, should be so violently infringed.
