# Parliament

**Parliament** — the House of Commons and House of Lords at Westminster — functions in _Framley Parsonage_ as the distant but decisive arena of national power. Though never directly depicted, Parliament's influence permeates the novel: it is the object of political ambition, the source of the electoral drama, and the institution that gives meaning to the political manoeuvring of characters from the Duke of Omnium to [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]. The Barsetshire county seat in the Commons is the prize over which the [[election|election]] is fought, and Parliament itself represents the summit of the political world that Trollope, a failed parliamentary candidate himself, portrays with characteristic ambivalence.

## Overview

Parliament in the 1840s — the decade of the novel's setting — was an institution in transition. The Reform Act of 1832 had expanded the electorate and reduced the most egregious forms of electoral corruption, but the House of Commons remained dominated by the landed interest. The House of Lords, composed of hereditary peers and bishops of the Church of England, exercised significant legislative power. Political parties — Whig and Tory — were loose coalitions of interest rather than modern disciplined organizations, and the monarch (Queen Victoria, from 1837) retained meaningful political influence.

> "At election time he supported, and always" had supported, the Whig interest (PN 016-172)

The novel's Parliament is a Whig Parliament. The Duke of Omnium, the great Whig magnate, exercises his influence through the distribution of seats and the management of elections. His agent [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]] connects the county politics of Barsetshire to the national politics of Westminster, embodying the intermediary role — part political secretary, part fixer, part financial predator — that the unreformed electoral system required.

## In the Novel

Parliament appears in _Framley Parsonage_ primarily as an object of desire and a source of expense. "At election time he supported" the Whig interest (PN 016-172) — this describes the Duke of Omnium, but it could describe any of the novel's political actors. Parliament is something to be entered, influenced, or managed; it is never something to be served. The political characters — Sowerby, Fothergill, Green Walker, Closerstill — are operators rather than legislators, their ambitions focused on the getting of seats rather than the doing of business.

> "In this matter of the coming election he declared" his independence (PN 078-082)

The novel's treatment of Parliament is satirical but not cynical. Trollope, who would later write the Palliser novels — the greatest fictional treatment of parliamentary politics in English — understood Parliament as a genuine arena of national life. But in _Framley Parsonage_, Parliament serves a more limited narrative function: it provides the backdrop against which the novel's themes of power, patronage, and moral responsibility are explored.

## Historical Context

The Parliament of the 1840s was shaped by the aftershocks of the 1832 Reform Act. The Act had abolished rotten boroughs, extended the franchise to the middle classes, and created a more politically conscious electorate. But it had not created democracy: the working classes remained disenfranchised, and the landed interest retained enormous influence through the distribution of seats in county constituencies.

The 1840s saw Parliament engaged with the great questions of the age: the repeal of the Corn Laws (1846), the extension of the factory acts, the regulation of railways, and the continuing reform of the Church of England. The ecclesiastical reforms of the 1830s — the creation of the Ecclesiastical Commissioners, the reform of cathedral chapters, the limitation of pluralism — were themselves parliamentary acts, reminding us that the Church whose inner life the novel depicts was, in its legal and institutional existence, a creation of Parliament.

Trollope's own political views — he described himself as an "advanced conservative Liberal" — inform the novel's treatment of Parliament. He believed in gradual reform, in the competence of the governing class, and in the importance of political institutions as expressions of national character. His Parliament is flawed, occasionally corrupt, often absurd — but it is also essential, the mechanism through which a free people governs itself.

## Key Figures

- **The Duke of Omnium**: The great Whig patron whose parliamentary influence extends across multiple counties. "At election time he supported" (PN 016-172) the Whig interest, controlling seats as a landowner controls farms — through a mixture of ownership, influence, and expectation.

- **[[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]**: The intermediary who connects Barsetshire to Westminster. His role — half political secretary, half independent operator — reflects the informal networks through which parliamentary politics was conducted before the professionalization of party organization.

- **Mr. Closerstill**: The "well-known electioneering agent" (PN 078-142) who represents the rising professional class of political managers — the men who actually ran elections in the reformed system.
