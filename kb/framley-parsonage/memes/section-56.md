## Section 56

[056-001]Lucy then got up from the sofa, and walked twice the length of the

[056-002]room before she spoke. Mrs. Robarts had all the ordinary curiosity—I

[056-003]was going to say, of a woman, but I mean to say, of humanity; and she

[056-004]had, moreover, all the love of a sister. She was both curious and

[056-005]anxious, and remained sitting where she was, silent, and with her

[056-006]eyes fixed on her companion.

[056-007]“Did I say so?” Lucy said at last. “No, Fanny; you have mistaken me:

[056-008]I did not say that. Ah, yes, about the cow and the dog. All that was

[056-009]true. I was telling you of what his soft words had been while I was

[056-010]becoming such a fool. Since that he has said more.”

[056-011]“What more has he said, Lucy?”

[056-012]“I yearn to tell you, if only I can trust you;” and Lucy knelt down

[056-013]at the feet of Mrs. Robarts, looking up into her face and smiling

[056-014]through the remaining drops of her tears. “I would fain tell you,

[056-015]but I do not know you yet,—whether you are quite true. I could be

[056-016]true,—true against all the world, if my friend told me. I will

[056-017]tell you, Fanny, if you say that you can be true. But if you doubt

[056-018]yourself, if you must whisper all to Mark—then let us be silent.”

[056-019]There was something almost awful in this to Mrs. Robarts. Hitherto,

[056-020]since their marriage, hardly a thought had passed through her mind

[056-021]which she had not shared with her husband. But now all this had come

[056-022]upon her so suddenly, that she was unable to think whether it would

[056-023]be well that she should become the depositary of such a secret,—not

[056-024]to be mentioned to Lucy’s brother, not to be mentioned to her own

[056-025]husband. But who ever yet was offered a secret and declined it? Who

[056-026]at least ever declined a love secret? What sister could do so? Mrs.

[056-027]Robarts therefore gave the promise, smoothing Lucy’s hair as she did

[056-028]so, and kissing her forehead and looking into her eyes, which, like

[056-029]a rainbow, were the brighter for her tears. “And what has he said to

[056-030]you, Lucy?”

[056-031]“What? Only this, that he asked me to be his wife.”

[056-032]“Lord Lufton proposed to you?”

[056-033]“Yes; proposed to me. It is not credible, is it? You cannot bring

[056-034]yourself to believe that such a thing happened, can you?” And Lucy

[056-035]rose again to her feet, as the idea of the scorn with which she

[056-036]felt that others would treat her—with which she herself treated

[056-037]herself—made the blood rise to her cheek. “And yet it is not a

[056-038]dream. I think that it is not a dream. I think that he really did.”

[056-039]“Think, Lucy!”

[056-040]“Well, I may say that I am sure.”

[056-041]“A gentleman would not make you a formal proposal, and leave you in

[056-042]doubt as to what he meant.”

[056-043]“Oh dear, no. There was no doubt at all of that kind; none in the

[056-044]least. Mr. Smith in asking Miss Jones to do him the honour of

[056-045]becoming Mrs. Smith never spoke more plainly. I was alluding to the

[056-046]possibility of having dreamt it all.”

[056-047]“Lucy!”

[056-048]“Well, it was not a dream. Here, standing here, on this very spot—on

[056-049]that flower of the carpet—he begged me a dozen times to be his wife.

[056-050]I wonder whether you and Mark would let me cut it out and keep it.”

[056-051]“And what answer did you make to him?”

[056-052]“I lied to him and told him that I did not love him.”

[056-053]“You refused him?”

[056-054]“Yes; I refused a live lord. There is some satisfaction in having

[056-055]that to think of, is there not? Fanny, was I wicked to tell that

[056-056]falsehood?”

[056-057]“And why did you refuse him?”

[056-058]“Why? Can you ask? Think what it would have been to go down to

[056-059]Framley Court, and to tell her ladyship in the course of conversation

[056-060]that I was engaged to her son. Think of Lady Lufton. But yet it was

[056-061]not that, Fanny. Had I thought that it was good for him, that he

[056-062]would not have repented, I would have braved anything—for his sake.

[056-063]Even your frown, for you would have frowned. You would have thought

[056-064]it sacrilege for me to marry Lord Lufton! You know you would.”

[056-065]Mrs. Robarts hardly knew how to say what she thought, or indeed what

[056-066]she ought to think. It was a matter on which much meditation would be

[056-067]required before she could give advice, and there was Lucy expecting

[056-068]counsel from her at that very moment. If Lord Lufton really loved

[056-069]Lucy Robarts, and was loved by Lucy Robarts, why should not they two

[056-070]become man and wife? And yet she did feel that it would be—perhaps

[056-071]not sacrilege, as Lucy had said, but something almost as troublesome.

[056-072]What would Lady Lufton say, or think, or feel? What would she say,

[056-073]and think, and feel as to that parsonage from which so deadly a blow

[056-074]would fall upon her? Would she not accuse the vicar and the vicar’s

[056-075]wife of the blackest ingratitude? Would life be endurable at Framley

[056-076]under such circumstances as those?

[056-077]“What you tell me so surprises me, that I hardly as yet know how to

[056-078]speak about it,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[056-079]“It was amazing, was it not? He must have been insane at the time;

[056-080]there can be no other excuse made for him. I wonder whether there is

[056-081]anything of that sort in the family?”

[056-082]“What; madness?” said Mrs. Robarts, quite in earnest.

[056-083]“Well, don’t you think he must have been mad when such an idea as

[056-084]that came into his head? But you don’t believe it; I can see that.

[056-085]And yet it is as true as heaven. Standing exactly here, on this spot,

[056-086]he said that he would persevere till I accepted his love. I wonder

[056-087]what made me specially observe that both his feet were within the

[056-088]lines of that division.”

[056-089]“And you would not accept his love?”

[056-090]“No; I would have nothing to say to it. Look you, I stood here, and

[056-091]putting my hand upon my heart,—for he bade me to do that,—I said

[056-092]that I could not love him.”

[056-093]“And what then?”

[056-094]“He went away,—with a look as though he were heart-broken. He crept

[056-095]away slowly, saying that he was the most wretched soul alive. For a

[056-096]minute I believed him, and could almost have called him back. But,

[056-097]no, Fanny; do not think that I am over proud, or conceited about my

[056-098]conquest. He had not reached the gate before he was thanking God for

[056-099]his escape.”

[056-100]“That I do not believe.”

[056-101]“But I do; and I thought of Lady Lufton too. How could I bear that

[056-102]she should scorn me, and accuse me of stealing her son’s heart? I

[056-103]know that it is better as it is; but tell me—is a falsehood always

[056-104]wrong, or can it be possible that the end should justify the means?

[056-105]Ought I to have told him the truth, and to have let him know that I

[056-106]could almost kiss the ground on which he stood?”

[056-107]This was a question for the doctors which Mrs. Robarts would not take

[056-108]upon herself to answer. She would not make that falsehood matter of

[056-109]accusation, but neither would she pronounce for it any absolution. In

[056-110]that matter Lucy must regulate her own conscience. “And what shall

[056-111]I do next?” said Lucy, still speaking in a tone that was half tragic

[056-112]and half jeering.

[056-113]“Do?” said Mrs. Robarts.

[056-114]“Yes, something must be done. If I were a man I should go to

[056-115]Switzerland, of course; or, as the case is a bad one, perhaps as far

[056-116]as Hungary. What is it that girls do? they don’t die now-a-days, I

[056-117]believe.”

[056-118]“Lucy, I do not believe that you care for him one jot. If you were in

[056-119]love you would not speak of it like that.”

[056-120]“There, there. That’s my only hope. If I could laugh at myself till

[056-121]it had become incredible to you, I also, by degrees, should cease to

[056-122]believe that I had cared for him. But, Fanny, it is very hard. If

[056-123]I were to starve, and rise before daybreak, and pinch myself, or do

[056-124]some nasty work,—clean the pots and pans and the candlesticks; that

[056-125]I think would do the most good. I have got a piece of sack-cloth, and

[056-126]I mean to wear that, when I have made it up.”

[056-127]“You are joking now, Lucy, I know.”

[056-128]“No, by my word; not in the spirit of what I am saying. How shall

[056-129]I act upon my heart, if I do not do it through the blood and the

[056-130]flesh?”

[056-131]“Do you not pray that God will give you strength to bear these

[056-132]troubles?”

[056-133]“But how is one to word one’s prayer, or how even to word one’s

[056-134]wishes? I do not know what is the wrong that I have done. I say it

[056-135]boldly; in this matter I cannot see my own fault. I have simply found

[056-136]that I have been a fool.”

[056-137]It was now quite dark in the room, or would have been so to any one

[056-138]entering it afresh. They had remained there talking till their eyes

[056-139]had become accustomed to the gloom, and would still have remained,

[056-140]had they not suddenly been disturbed by the sound of a horse’s feet.

[056-141]“There is Mark,” said Fanny, jumping up and running to the bell, that

[056-142]lights might be ready when he should enter.

[056-143]“I thought he remained in Barchester to-night.”

[056-144]“And so did I; but he said it might be doubtful. What shall we do if

[056-145]he has not dined?”

[056-146]That, I believe, is always the first thought in the mind of a good

[056-147]wife when her husband returns home. Has he had his dinner? What can

[056-148]I give him for dinner? Will he like his dinner? Oh dear, oh dear!

[056-149]there is nothing in the house but cold mutton. But on this occasion

[056-150]the lord of the mansion had dined, and came home radiant with

[056-151]good-humour, and owing, perhaps, a little of his radiance to the

[056-152]dean’s claret. “I have told them,” said he, “that they may keep

[056-153]possession of the house for the next two months, and they have agreed

[056-154]to that arrangement.”

[056-155]“That is very pleasant,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[056-156]“And I don’t think we shall have so much trouble about the

[056-157]dilapidations after all.”

[056-158]“I am very glad of that,” said Mrs. Robarts. But nevertheless she was

[056-159]thinking much more of Lucy than of the house in Barchester Close.

[056-160]“You won’t betray me,” said Lucy, as she gave her sister-in-law a

[056-161]parting kiss at night.

[056-162]“No; not unless you give me permission.”

[056-163]“Ah; I shall never do that.”

[056-164]CHAPTER XXVII.

[056-165]SOUTH AUDLEY STREET.

[056-166]The Duke of Omnium had notified to Mr. Fothergill his wish that some

[056-167]arrangement should be made about the Chaldicotes mortgages, and Mr.

[056-168]Fothergill had understood what the duke meant as well as though his

[056-169]instructions had been written down with all a lawyer’s verbosity.

[056-170]The duke’s meaning was this, that Chaldicotes was to be swept up

[056-171]and garnered, and made part and parcel of the Gatherum property. It

[056-172]had seemed to the duke that that affair between his friend and Miss

[056-173]Dunstable was hanging fire, and, therefore, it would be well that

[056-174]Chaldicotes should be swept up and garnered. And, moreover, tidings

[056-175]had come into the western division of the county that young Frank

[056-176]Gresham of Boxall Hill was in treaty with the Government for the

[056-177]purchase of all that Crown property called the Chace of Chaldicotes.

[056-178]It had been offered to the duke, but the duke had given no definite

[056-179]answer. Had he got his money back from Mr. Sowerby, he could have

[056-180]forestalled Mr. Gresham; but now that did not seem to be probable,

[056-181]and his grace was resolved that either the one property or the other

[056-182]should be duly garnered. Therefore Mr. Fothergill went up to town,

[056-183]and therefore Mr. Sowerby was, most unwillingly, compelled to have a

[056-184]business interview with Mr. Fothergill. In the meantime, since last

[056-185]we saw him, Mr. Sowerby had learned from his sister the answer which

[056-186]Miss Dunstable had given to his proposition, and knew that he had no

[056-187]further hope in that direction.
