## Section 22

[022-001]They were all in black, of course; but the sombreness of Lucy’s

[022-002]clothes struck Fanny much more than her own. They seemed to have

[022-003]swallowed her up in their blackness, and to have made her almost

[022-004]an emblem of death. She did not look up, but kept her face turned

[022-005]towards the fire, and seemed almost afraid of her position.

[022-006]“She may say what she likes, Fanny,” said Mark, “but she is very

[022-007]cold. And so am I,—cold enough. You had better go up with her to her

[022-008]room. We won’t do much in the dressing way to-night; eh, Lucy?”

[022-009]In the bedroom Lucy thawed a little, and Fanny, as she kissed her,

[022-010]said to herself that she had been wrong as to that word “plain.”

[022-011]Lucy, at any rate, was not plain.

[022-012]“You will be used to us soon,” said Fanny, “and then I hope we shall

[022-013]make you comfortable.” And she took her sister-in-law’s hand and

[022-014]pressed it.

[022-015]Lucy looked up at her, and her eyes then were tender enough. “I am

[022-016]sure I shall be happy here,” she said, “with you. But—but—dear

[022-017]papa!” And then they got into each other’s arms, and had a great bout

[022-018]of kissing and crying. “Plain,” said Fanny to herself, as at last

[022-019]she got her guest’s hair smoothed and the tears washed from her

[022-020]eyes—“plain! She has the loveliest countenance that I ever looked at

[022-021]in my life!”

[022-022]“Your sister is quite beautiful,” she said to Mark, as they talked

[022-023]her over alone before they went to sleep that night.

[022-024]“No, she’s not beautiful; but she’s a very good girl, and clever

[022-025]enough too, in her sort of way.”

[022-026]“I think her perfectly lovely. I never saw such eyes in my life

[022-027]before.”

[022-028]“I’ll leave her in your hands then; you shall get her a husband.”

[022-029]“That mayn’t be so easy. I don’t think she’d marry anybody.”

[022-030]“Well, I hope not. But she seems to me to be exactly cut out for an

[022-031]old maid;—to be aunt Lucy for ever and ever to your bairns.”

[022-032]“And so she shall, with all my heart. But I don’t think she will,

[022-033]very long. I have no doubt she will be hard to please; but if I were

[022-034]a man I should fall in love with her at once. Did you ever observe

[022-035]her teeth, Mark?”

[022-036]“I don’t think I ever did.”

[022-037]“You wouldn’t know whether any one had a tooth in their head, I

[022-038]believe.”

[022-039]“No one except you, my dear; and I know all yours by heart.”

[022-040]“You are a goose.”

[022-041]“And a very sleepy one; so, if you please, I’ll go to roost.” And

[022-042]thus there was nothing more said about Lucy’s beauty on that

[022-043]occasion.

[022-044]For the first two days Mrs. Robarts did not make much of her

[022-045]sister-in-law. Lucy, indeed, was not demonstrative; and she was,

[022-046]moreover, one of those few persons—for they are very few—who are

[022-047]contented to go on with their existence without making themselves the

[022-048]centre of any special outward circle. To the ordinary run of minds

[022-049]it is impossible not to do this. A man’s own dinner is to himself

[022-050]so important that he cannot bring himself to believe that it is a

[022-051]matter utterly indifferent to every one else. A lady’s collection of

[022-052]baby-clothes, in early years, and of house linen and curtain-fringes

[022-053]in later life, is so very interesting to her own eyes, that she

[022-054]cannot believe but what other people will rejoice to behold it. I

[022-055]would not, however, be held as regarding this tendency as evil. It

[022-056]leads to conversation of some sort among people, and perhaps to a

[022-057]kind of sympathy. Mrs. Jones will look at Mrs. White’s linen-chest,

[022-058]hoping that Mrs. White may be induced to look at hers. One can only

[022-059]pour out of a jug that which is in it. For the most of us, if we do

[022-060]not talk of ourselves, or at any rate of the individual circles of

[022-061]which we are the centres, we can talk of nothing. I cannot hold with

[022-062]those who wish to put down the insignificant chatter of the world.

[022-063]As for myself, I am always happy to look at Mrs. Jones’s linen,

[022-064]and never omit an opportunity of giving her the details of my own

[022-065]dinners.

[022-066]But Lucy Robarts had not this gift. She had come there as a stranger

[022-067]into her sister-in-law’s house, and at first seemed as though she

[022-068]would be contented in simply having her corner in the drawing-room

[022-069]and her place at the parlour-table. She did not seem to need the

[022-070]comforts of condolence and open-hearted talking. I do not mean to say

[022-071]that she was moody, that she did not answer when she was spoken to,

[022-072]or that she took no notice of the children; but she did not at once

[022-073]throw herself and all her hopes and sorrows into Fanny’s heart, as

[022-074]Fanny would have had her do.

[022-075]Mrs. Robarts herself was what we call demonstrative. When she was

[022-076]angry with Lady Lufton she showed it. And as since that time her love

[022-077]and admiration for Lady Lufton had increased, she showed that also.

[022-078]When she was in any way displeased with her husband, she could

[022-079]not hide it, even though she tried to do so, and fancied herself

[022-080]successful;—no more than she could hide her warm, constant,

[022-081]overflowing woman’s love. She could not walk through a room hanging

[022-082]on her husband’s arm without seeming to proclaim to every one there

[022-083]that she thought him the best man in it. She was demonstrative, and

[022-084]therefore she was the more disappointed in that Lucy did not rush at

[022-085]once with all her cares into her open heart.

[022-086]“She is so quiet,” Fanny said to her husband.

[022-087]“That’s her nature,” said Mark. “She always was quiet as a child.

[022-088]While we were smashing everything, she would never crack a teacup.”

[022-089]“I wish she would break something now,” said Fanny, “and then perhaps

[022-090]we should get to talk about it.” But she did not on this account

[022-091]give over loving her sister-in-law. She probably valued her the more,

[022-092]unconsciously, for not having those aptitudes with which she herself

[022-093]was endowed.

[022-094]And then after two days Lady Lufton called; of course it may be

[022-095]supposed that Fanny had said a good deal to her new inmate about Lady

[022-096]Lufton. A neighbour of that kind in the country exercises so large an

[022-097]influence upon the whole tenor of one’s life, that to abstain from

[022-098]such talk is out of the question. Mrs. Robarts had been brought up

[022-099]almost under the dowager’s wing, and of course she regarded her as

[022-100]being worthy of much talking. Do not let persons on this account

[022-101]suppose that Mrs. Robarts was a tuft-hunter, or a toad-eater. If they

[022-102]do not see the difference they have yet got to study the earliest

[022-103]principles of human nature.

[022-104]Lady Lufton called, and Lucy was struck dumb. Fanny was particularly

[022-105]anxious that her ladyship’s first impression should be favourable,

[022-106]and to effect this, she especially endeavoured to throw the two

[022-107]together during that visit. But in this she was unwise. Lady Lufton,

[022-108]however, had woman-craft enough not to be led into any egregious

[022-109]error by Lucy’s silence.

[022-110]“And what day will you come and dine with us?” said Lady Lufton,

[022-111]turning expressly to her old friend Fanny.

[022-112]“Oh, do you name the day. We never have many engagements, you know.”

[022-113]“Will Thursday do, Miss Robarts? You will meet nobody you know, only

[022-114]my son; so you need not regard it as going out. Fanny here will tell

[022-115]you that stepping over to Framley Court is no more going out, than

[022-116]when you go from one room to another in the parsonage. Is it, Fanny?”

[022-117]Fanny laughed and said that that stepping over to Framley Court

[022-118]certainly was done so often that perhaps they did not think so much

[022-119]about it as they ought to do.

[022-120]“We consider ourselves a sort of happy family here, Miss Robarts,

[022-121]and are delighted to have the opportunity of including you in the

[022-122]ménage.”

[022-123]Lucy gave her ladyship one of her sweetest smiles, but what she said

[022-124]at that moment was inaudible. It was plain, however, that she could

[022-125]not bring herself even to go as far as Framley Court for her dinner

[022-126]just at present. “It was very kind of Lady Lufton,” she said to

[022-127]Fanny; “but it was so very soon, and—and—and if they would only

[022-128]go without her, she would be so happy.” But as the object was to go

[022-129]with her—expressly to take her there—the dinner was adjourned for a

[022-130]short time—_sine die_.

[022-131]CHAPTER XI.

[022-132]GRISELDA GRANTLY.

[022-133]It was nearly a month after this that Lucy was first introduced to

[022-134]Lord Lufton, and then it was brought about only by accident. During

[022-135]that time Lady Lufton had been often at the parsonage, and had in a

[022-136]certain degree learned to know Lucy; but the stranger in the parish

[022-137]had never yet plucked up courage to accept one of the numerous

[022-138]invitations that had reached her. Mr. Robarts and his wife had

[022-139]frequently been at Framley Court, but the dreaded day of Lucy’s

[022-140]initiation had not yet arrived.

[022-141]She had seen Lord Lufton in church, but hardly so as to know him, and

[022-142]beyond that she had not seen him at all. One day, however—or rather,

[022-143]one evening, for it was already dusk—he overtook her and Mrs.

[022-144]Robarts on the road walking towards the vicarage. He had his gun on

[022-145]his shoulder, three pointers were at his heels, and a gamekeeper

[022-146]followed a little in the rear.

[022-147]“How are you, Mrs. Robarts?” he said, almost before he had overtaken

[022-148]them. “I have been chasing you along the road for the last half mile.

[022-149]I never knew ladies walk so fast.”

[022-150]“We should be frozen if we were to dawdle about as you gentlemen do,”

[022-151]and then she stopped and shook hands with him. She forgot at the

[022-152]moment that Lucy and he had not met, and therefore she did not

[022-153]introduce them.

[022-154]“Won’t you make me known to your sister-in-law?” said he, taking off

[022-155]his hat, and bowing to Lucy. “I have never yet had the pleasure of

[022-156]meeting her, though we have been neighbours for a month and more.”

[022-157]Fanny made her excuses and introduced them, and then they went on

[022-158]till they came to Framley Gate, Lord Lufton talking to them both, and

[022-159]Fanny answering for the two, and there they stopped for a moment.

[022-160]“I am surprised to see you alone,” Mrs. Robarts had just said; “I

[022-161]thought that Captain Culpepper was with you.”

[022-162]“The captain has left me for this one day. If you’ll whisper I’ll

[022-163]tell you where he has gone. I dare not speak it out loud, even to the

[022-164]woods.”

[022-165]“To what terrible place can he have taken himself? I’ll have no

[022-166]whisperings about such horrors.”

[022-167]“He has gone to—to—but you’ll promise not to tell my mother?”

[022-168]“Not tell your mother! Well, now you have excited my curiosity! where

[022-169]can he be?”

[022-170]“Do you promise, then?”

[022-171]“Oh, yes! I will promise, because I am sure Lady Lufton won’t ask me

[022-172]as to Captain Culpepper’s whereabouts. We won’t tell; will we, Lucy?”

[022-173]“He has gone to Gatherum Castle for a day’s pheasant-shooting. Now,

[022-174]mind, you must not betray us. Her ladyship supposes that he is shut

[022-175]up in his room with a toothache. We did not dare to mention the name

[022-176]to her.”

[022-177]And then it appeared that Mrs. Robarts had some engagement which made

[022-178]it necessary that she should go up and see Lady Lufton, whereas Lucy

[022-179]was intending to walk on to the parsonage alone.

[022-180]“And I have promised to go to your husband,” said Lord Lufton; “or

[022-181]rather to your husband’s dog, Ponto. And I will do two other good

[022-182]things—I will carry a brace of pheasants with me, and protect Miss

[022-183]Robarts from the evil spirits of the Framley roads.” And so Mrs.

[022-184]Robarts turned in at the gate, and Lucy and his lordship walked off

[022-185]together.
