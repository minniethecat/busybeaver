# The Jupiter

**_The Jupiter_** is Anthony Trollope's fictionalized representation of _The Times_ newspaper in _Framley Parsonage_ and the broader _Chronicles of Barsetshire_. It is the voice of public opinion, the arbiter of political reputation, and the institution that most clearly represents the rising power of the press in Victorian public life. Its pronouncements — on politics, on Church affairs, on the character of public men — carry a weight that rivals that of [[parliament|Parliament]] itself, embodying the nineteenth-century emergence of the press as a "fourth estate" of the realm.

## Overview

The Jupiter (named for the king of the Roman gods, an ironic nod to its pretension to Olympian authority) appears in the Barsetshire novels as a looming, impersonal force. It speaks with the voice of thunder — definitive, unquestionable, and often devastating. Its editorials can make or break reputations; its political endorsements can swing elections; its moral judgments can shape the terms of public debate. Trollope, who knew the world of Victorian journalism from the inside (he was a prolific periodical contributor and edited the _St. Paul's Magazine_), portrays the Jupiter with a mixture of respect and satirical scepticism.

The Jupiter is not a character but a presence — it is invoked rather than depicted, its authority assumed rather than argued. When a character says "The Jupiter says" something, the statement requires no further authority: the newspaper has spoken, and its speech carries the force of institutional consensus. This is, for Trollope, both a tribute to the genuine power of the Victorian press and a warning about the dangers of unaccountable authority.

## In the Novel

The Jupiter's role in _Framley Parsonage_ is most evident in its coverage of ecclesiastical and political affairs. The newspaper's treatment of the [[election|parliamentary election]] shapes public perception of the candidates and their patrons; its editorial line on Church matters influences the climate within which the Barsetshire clergy operate. [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]], for all her local power, is acutely aware that the Jupiter reaches where her own influence cannot.

The Jupiter represents the force of modern publicity — the newspaper that everyone reads and whose judgments everyone must take into account. In a novel concerned with the tension between personal obligation (the patronage relationship, the bonds of family and friendship) and impersonal forces (the market, public opinion, political interest), the Jupiter stands as the supreme example of the latter: an institution that speaks to everyone and answers to no one.

## Historical Context

The Jupiter is transparently modelled on _The Times_ of London, which by the 1840s had achieved a position of unprecedented influence in British public life. Under the editorship of Thomas Barnes (1817–1841) and then John Thadeus Delane (1841–1877), _The Times_ became known as "The Thunderer" — a nickname that Trollope's "Jupiter" directly echoes. The paper's circulation, its independence from party control, and the quality of its reporting and editorial writing gave it a reach and authority that no previous newspaper had possessed.

The 1840s was the decade in which _The Times_ consolidated this position. Its coverage of the Corn Law debates, its exposure of administrative incompetence during the Crimean War (1854–1856), and its general assumption of the role of public watchdog established a model of the press as "fourth estate" that remains influential. Trollope's portrait of the Jupiter captures this moment of ascendancy while also registering a novelist's scepticism about the concentration of unaccountable power in any single institution.

## Key Figures

- **The Editor**: Never named in _Framley Parsonage_, the editor of the Jupiter is a figure of mysterious authority, corresponding to Delane of _The Times_. His anonymity is itself a source of power — the Jupiter speaks with an institutional voice that transcends any individual.

- **The Correspondents**: The Jupiter's reporters and leader-writers are the anonymous craftsmen of public opinion. Their reports from Barsetshire and Westminster shape the narrative within which political and ecclesiastical actors must operate.

- **The Readers**: The novel's characters are all, in different ways, readers of the Jupiter. [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]] reads it for political and ecclesiastical news; [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]] reads it to track the currents of opinion that will affect his electoral calculations; [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]] reads it as part of the mental furniture of the educated gentleman.
