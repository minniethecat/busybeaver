## Section 42

[042-001]Mark’s face again became clouded. “I rather think,” said he, “that I

[042-002]shall decline the presentation.”

[042-003]“Decline it!” said Sowerby, who, having used his utmost efforts

[042-004]to obtain it, would have been more absolutely offended by such

[042-005]vacillation on the vicar’s part than by any personal abuse which

[042-006]either he or Lord Lufton could heap upon him.

[042-007]“I think I shall,” said Mark.

[042-008]“And why?”

[042-009]Mark looked up at Lord Lufton, and then remained silent for a moment.

[042-010]“There can be no occasion for such a sacrifice under the present

[042-011]circumstances,” said his lordship.

[042-012]“And under what circumstances could there be occasion for it?” asked

[042-013]Sowerby. “The Duke of Omnium has used some little influence to get

[042-014]the place for you as a parish clergyman belonging to his county, and

[042-015]I should think it monstrous if you were now to reject it.”

[042-016]And then Robarts openly stated the whole of his reasons, explaining

[042-017]exactly what Lord Lufton had said with reference to the bill

[042-018]transactions, and to the allegation which would be made as to the

[042-019]stall having been given in payment for the accommodation.

[042-020]“Upon my word that’s too bad,” said Sowerby.

[042-021]“Now, Sowerby, I won’t be lectured,” said Lord Lufton.

[042-022]“I have done my lecture,” said he, aware, perhaps, that it would

[042-023]not do for him to push his friend too far, “and I shall not give a

[042-024]second. But, Robarts, let me tell you this: as far as I know, Harold

[042-025]Smith has had little or nothing to do with the appointment. The duke

[042-026]has told the Prime Minister that he was very anxious that a parish

[042-027]clergyman from the county should go into the chapter, and then, at

[042-028]Lord Brock’s request, he named you. If under those circumstances you

[042-029]talk of giving it up, I shall believe you to be insane. As for the

[042-030]bill which you accepted for me, you need have no uneasiness about it.

[042-031]The money will be ready; but of course, when that time comes, you

[042-032]will let me have the hundred and thirty for—”

[042-033]And then Mr. Sowerby took his leave, having certainly made himself

[042-034]master of the occasion. If a man of fifty have his wits about him,

[042-035]and be not too prosy, he can generally make himself master of the

[042-036]occasion, when his companions are under thirty.

[042-037]Robarts did not stay at the Albany long after him, but took his

[042-038]leave, having received some assurances of Lord Lufton’s regret for

[042-039]what had passed and many promises of his friendship for the future.

[042-040]Indeed Lord Lufton was a little ashamed of himself. “And as for

[042-041]the prebend, after what has passed, of course you must accept it.”

[042-042]Nevertheless his lordship had not omitted to notice Mr. Sowerby’s

[042-043]hint about the horse and the hundred and thirty pounds.

[042-044]Robarts, as he walked back to his hotel, thought that he certainly

[042-045]would accept the Barchester promotion, and was very glad that he had

[042-046]said nothing on the subject to his brother. On the whole his spirits

[042-047]were much raised. That assurance of Sowerby’s about the bill was very

[042-048]comforting to him; and strange to say, he absolutely believed it. In

[042-049]truth Sowerby had been so completely the winning horse at the late

[042-050]meeting, that both Lord Lufton and Robarts were inclined to believe

[042-051]almost anything he said;—which was not always the case with either

[042-052]of them.

[042-053]CHAPTER XX.

[042-054]HAROLD SMITH IN THE CABINET.

[042-055]For a few days the whole Harold Smith party held their heads very

[042-056]high. It was not only that their man had been made a cabinet

[042-057]minister; but a rumour had got abroad that Lord Brock, in selecting

[042-058]him, had amazingly strengthened his party, and done much to cure the

[042-059]wounds which his own arrogance and lack of judgment had inflicted

[042-060]on the body politic of his government. So said the Harold-Smithians,

[042-061]much elated. And when we consider what Harold had himself achieved,

[042-062]we need not be surprised that he himself was somewhat elated also.

[042-063]It must be a proud day for any man when he first walks into a

[042-064]cabinet. But when a humble-minded man thinks of such a phase of life,

[042-065]his mind becomes lost in wondering what a cabinet is. Are they gods

[042-066]that attend there or men? Do they sit on chairs, or hang about on

[042-067]clouds? When they speak, is the music of the spheres audible in their

[042-068]Olympian mansion, making heaven drowsy with its harmony? In what way

[042-069]do they congregate? In what order do they address each other? Are the

[042-070]voices of all the deities free and equal? Is plodding Themis from

[042-071]the Home Department, or Ceres from the Colonies, heard with as rapt

[042-072]attention as powerful Pallas of the Foreign Office, the goddess that

[042-073]is never seen without her lance and helmet? Does our Whitehall Mars

[042-074]make eyes there at bright young Venus of the Privy Seal, disgusting

[042-075]that quaint tinkering Vulcan, who is blowing his bellows at our

[042-076]Exchequer, not altogether unsuccessfully? Old Saturn of the Woolsack

[042-077]sits there mute, we will say, a relic of other days, as seated in

[042-078]this divan. The hall in which he rules is now elsewhere. Is our

[042-079]Mercury of the Post Office ever ready to fly nimbly from globe to

[042-080]globe, as great Jove may order him, while Neptune, unaccustomed to

[042-081]the waves, offers needful assistance to the Apollo of the India

[042-082]Board? How Juno sits apart, glum and huffy, uncared for, Council

[042-083]President though she be, great in name, but despised among gods—that

[042-084]we can guess. If Bacchus and Cupid share Trade and the Board of Works

[042-085]between them, the fitness of things will have been as fully consulted

[042-086]as is usual. And modest Diana of the Petty Bag, latest summoned to

[042-087]these banquets of ambrosia,—does she not cling retiring near the

[042-088]doors, hardly able as yet to make her low voice heard among her

[042-089]brother deities? But Jove, great Jove—old Jove, the King of Olympus,

[042-090]hero among gods and men, how does he carry himself in these councils

[042-091]summoned by his voice? Does he lie there at his ease, with his purple

[042-092]cloak cut from the firmament around his shoulders? Is his thunderbolt

[042-093]ever at his hand to reduce a recreant god to order? Can he proclaim

[042-094]silence in that immortal hall? Is it not there, as elsewhere, in all

[042-095]places, and among all nations, that a king of gods and a king of men

[042-096]is and will be king, rules and will rule, over those who are smaller

[042-097]than himself?

[042-098]Harold Smith, when he was summoned to the august hall of divine

[042-099]councils, did feel himself to be a proud man; but we may perhaps

[042-100]conclude that at the first meeting or two he did not attempt to take

[042-101]a very leading part. Some of my readers may have sat at vestries, and

[042-102]will remember how mild, and for the most part, mute, is a new-comer

[042-103]at their board. He agrees generally, with abated enthusiasm; but

[042-104]should he differ, he apologizes for the liberty. But anon, when the

[042-105]voices of his colleagues have become habitual in his ears—when the

[042-106]strangeness of the room is gone, and the table before him is known

[042-107]and trusted—he throws off his awe and dismay, and electrifies his

[042-108]brotherhood by the vehemence of his declamation and the violence of

[042-109]his thumping. So let us suppose it will be with Harold Smith, perhaps

[042-110]in the second or third season of his cabinet practice. Alas! alas!

[042-111]that such pleasures should be so fleeting!

[042-112]And then, too, there came upon him a blow which somewhat modified his

[042-113]triumph—a cruel, dastard blow, from a hand which should have been

[042-114]friendly to him, from one to whom he had fondly looked to buoy him

[042-115]up in the great course that was before him. It had been said by his

[042-116]friends that in obtaining Harold Smith’s services the Prime Minister

[042-117]had infused new young healthy blood into his body. Harold himself had

[042-118]liked the phrase, and had seen at a glance how it might have been

[042-119]made to tell by some friendly Supplehouse or the like. But why should

[042-120]a Supplehouse out of Elysium be friendly to a Harold Smith within it?

[042-121]Men lapped in Elysium, steeped to the neck in bliss, must expect to

[042-122]see their friends fall off from them. Human nature cannot stand it.

[042-123]If I want to get anything from my old friend Jones, I like to see him

[042-124]shoved up into a high place. But if Jones, even in his high place,

[042-125]can do nothing for me, then his exaltation above my head is an insult

[042-126]and an injury. Who ever believes his own dear intimate companion to

[042-127]be fit for the highest promotion? Mr. Supplehouse had known Mr. Smith

[042-128]too closely to think much of his young blood.

[042-129]Consequently, there appeared an article in the _Jupiter_, which was

[042-130]by no means complimentary to the ministry in general. It harped a

[042-131]good deal on the young blood view of the question, and seemed to

[042-132]insinuate that Harold Smith was not much better than diluted water.

[042-133]“The Prime Minister,” the article said, “having lately recruited

[042-134]his impaired vigour by a new infusion of aristocratic influence of

[042-135]the highest moral tone, had again added to himself another tower of

[042-136]strength chosen from among the people. What might he not hope, now

[042-137]that he possessed the services of Lord Brittleback and Mr. Harold

[042-138]Smith! Renovated in a Medea’s caldron of such potency, all his effete

[042-139]limbs—and it must be acknowledged that some of them had become very

[042-140]effete—would come forth young and round and robust. A new energy

[042-141]would diffuse itself through every department; India would be saved

[042-142]and quieted; the ambition of France would be tamed; even-handed

[042-143]reform would remodel our courts of law and parliamentary elections;

[042-144]and Utopia would be realized. Such, it seems, is the result expected

[042-145]in the ministry from Mr. Harold Smith’s young blood!”

[042-146]This was cruel enough, but even this was hardly so cruel as the words

[042-147]with which the article ended. By that time irony had been dropped,

[042-148]and the writer spoke out earnestly his opinion upon the matter. “We

[042-149]beg to assure Lord Brock,” said the article, “that such alliances as

[042-150]these will not save him from the speedy fall with which his arrogance

[042-151]and want of judgment threaten to overwhelm it. As regards himself we

[042-152]shall be sorry to hear of his resignation. He is in many respects the

[042-153]best statesman that we possess for the emergencies of the present

[042-154]period. But if he be so ill-judged as to rest on such men as Mr.

[042-155]Harold Smith and Lord Brittleback for his assistants in the work

[042-156]which is before him, he must not expect that the country will support

[042-157]him. Mr. Harold Smith is not made of the stuff from which cabinet

[042-158]ministers should be formed.”

[042-159]Mr. Harold Smith, as he read this, seated at his breakfast-table,

[042-160]recognized, or said that he recognized, the hand of Mr. Supplehouse

[042-161]in every touch. That phrase about the effete limbs was Supplehouse

[042-162]all over, as was also the realization of Utopia. “When he wants to

[042-163]be witty, he always talks about Utopia,” said Mr. Harold Smith—to

[042-164]himself: for Mrs. Harold was not usually present in the flesh at

[042-165]these matutinal meals.

[042-166]And then he went down to his office, and saw in the glance of every

[042-167]man that he met an announcement that that article in the _Jupiter_

[042-168]had been read. His private secretary tittered in evident allusion to

[042-169]the article, and the way in which Buggins took his coat made it clear

[042-170]that it was well known in the messengers’ lobby. “He won’t have to

[042-171]fill up my vacancy when I go,” Buggins was saying to himself. And

[042-172]then in the course of the morning came the cabinet council, the

[042-173]second that he had attended, and he read in the countenance of every

[042-174]god and goddess there assembled that their chief was thought to have

[042-175]made another mistake. If Mr. Supplehouse could have been induced to

[042-176]write in another strain, then indeed that new blood might have been

[042-177]felt to have been efficacious.
