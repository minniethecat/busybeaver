## Section 36

[036-001]In these days Mrs. Proudie considered herself to be by no means the

[036-002]least among bishops’ wives. She had opened the season this year in a

[036-003]new house in Gloucester Place, at which the reception rooms, at any

[036-004]rate, were all that a lady bishop could desire. Here she had a front

[036-005]drawing-room of very noble dimensions, a second drawing-room rather

[036-006]noble also, though it had lost one of its back corners awkwardly

[036-007]enough, apparently in a jostle with the neighbouring house; and then

[036-008]there was a third—shall we say drawing-room, or closet?—in which

[036-009]Mrs. Proudie delighted to be seen sitting, in order that the world

[036-010]might know that there was a third room; altogether a noble suite, as

[036-011]Mrs. Proudie herself said in confidence to more than one clergyman’s

[036-012]wife from Barsetshire. “A noble suite, indeed, Mrs. Proudie!” the

[036-013]clergymen’s wives from Barsetshire would usually answer.

[036-014]For some time Mrs. Proudie was much at a loss to know by what sort

[036-015]of party or entertainment she would make herself famous. Balls and

[036-016]suppers were of course out of the question. She did not object to

[036-017]her daughters dancing all night at other houses—at least, of late

[036-018]she had not objected, for the fashionable world required it, and the

[036-019]young ladies had perhaps a will of their own—but dancing at her

[036-020]house—absolutely under the shade of the bishop’s apron—would be a

[036-021]sin and a scandal. And then as to suppers—of all modes in which one

[036-022]may extend one’s hospitality to a large acquaintance, they are the

[036-023]most costly.

[036-024]“It is horrid to think that we should go out among our friends for

[036-025]the mere sake of eating and drinking,” Mrs. Proudie would say to

[036-026]the clergymen’s wives from Barsetshire. “It shows such a sensual

[036-027]propensity.”

[036-028]“Indeed it does, Mrs. Proudie; and is so vulgar too!” those ladies

[036-029]would reply.

[036-030]But the elder among them would remember with regret the unsparing,

[036-031]open-handed hospitality of Barchester palace in the good old days of

[036-032]Bishop Grantly—God rest his soul! One old vicar’s wife there was

[036-033]whose answer had not been so courteous—

[036-034]“When we are hungry, Mrs. Proudie,” she had said, “we do all have

[036-035]sensual propensities.”

[036-036]“It would be much better, Mrs. Athill, if the world would provide

[036-037]for all that at home,” Mrs. Proudie had rapidly replied; with which

[036-038]opinion I must here profess that I cannot by any means bring myself

[036-039]to coincide.

[036-040]But a conversazione would give play to no sensual propensity, nor

[036-041]occasion that intolerable expense which the gratification of sensual

[036-042]propensities too often produces. Mrs. Proudie felt that the word was

[036-043]not all that she could have desired. It was a little faded by old use

[036-044]and present oblivion, and seemed to address itself to that portion

[036-045]of the London world that is considered blue, rather than fashionable.

[036-046]But, nevertheless, there was a spirituality about it which suited

[036-047]her, and one may also say an economy. And then as regarded fashion,

[036-048]it might perhaps not be beyond the power of a Mrs. Proudie to regild

[036-049]the word with a newly burnished gilding. Some leading person must

[036-050]produce fashion at first hand, and why not Mrs. Proudie?

[036-051]Her plan was to set the people by the ears talking, if talk they

[036-052]would, or to induce them to show themselves there inert if no more

[036-053]could be got from them. To accommodate with chairs and sofas as many

[036-054]as the furniture of her noble suite of rooms would allow, especially

[036-055]with the two chairs and padded bench against the wall in the back

[036-056]closet—the small inner drawing-room, as she would call it to the

[036-057]clergymen’s wives from Barsetshire—and to let the others stand

[036-058]about upright, or “group themselves,” as she described it. Then four

[036-059]times during the two hours’ period of her conversazione tea and cake

[036-060]were to be handed round on salvers. It is astonishing how far a

[036-061]very little cake will go in this way, particularly if administered

[036-062]tolerably early after dinner. The men can’t eat it, and the women,

[036-063]having no plates and no table, are obliged to abstain. Mrs. Jones

[036-064]knows that she cannot hold a piece of crumbly cake in her hand till

[036-065]it be consumed without doing serious injury to her best dress. When

[036-066]Mrs. Proudie, with her weekly books before her, looked into the

[036-067]financial upshot of her conversazione, her conscience told her that

[036-068]she had done the right thing.

[036-069]Going out to tea is not a bad thing, if one can contrive to dine

[036-070]early, and then be allowed to sit round a big table with a tea

[036-071]urn in the middle. I would, however, suggest that breakfast cups

[036-072]should always be provided for the gentlemen. And then with pleasant

[036-073]neighbours,—or more especially with a pleasant neighbour,—the

[036-074]affair is not, according to my taste, by any means the worst phase

[036-075]of society. But I do dislike that handing round, unless it be of a

[036-076]subsidiary thimbleful when the business of the social intercourse has

[036-077]been dinner.

[036-078]And indeed this handing round has become a vulgar and an intolerable

[036-079]nuisance among us second-class gentry with our eight hundred a

[036-080]year—there or thereabouts;—doubly intolerable as being destructive

[036-081]of our natural comforts, and a wretchedly vulgar aping of men

[036-082]with large incomes. The Duke of Omnium and Lady Hartletop are

[036-083]undoubtedly wise to have everything handed round. Friends of mine who

[036-084]occasionally dine at such houses tell me that they get their wine

[036-085]quite as quickly as they can drink it, that their mutton is brought

[036-086]to them without delay, and that the potato-bearer follows quick upon

[036-087]the heels of carnifer. Nothing can be more comfortable, and we may no

[036-088]doubt acknowledge that these first-class grandees do understand their

[036-089]material comforts. But we of the eight hundred can no more come up to

[036-090]them in this than we can in their opera-boxes and equipages. May I

[036-091]not say that the usual tether of this class, in the way of carnifers,

[036-092]cup-bearers, and the rest, does not reach beyond neat-handed Phyllis

[036-093]and the greengrocer? and that Phyllis, neat-handed as she probably

[036-094]is, and the greengrocer, though he be ever so active, cannot

[036-095]administer a dinner to twelve people who are prohibited by a

[036-096]Medo-Persian law from all self-administration whatever? And may I not

[036-097]further say that the lamentable consequence to us eight hundreders

[036-098]dining out among each other is this, that we too often get no dinner

[036-099]at all. Phyllis, with the potatoes, cannot reach us till our mutton

[036-100]is devoured, or in a lukewarm state past our power of managing; and

[036-101]Ganymede, the greengrocer, though we admire the skill of his necktie

[036-102]and the whiteness of his unexceptionable gloves, fails to keep us

[036-103]going in Sherry.

[036-104]Seeing a lady the other day in this strait, left without a small

[036-105]modicum of stimulus which was no doubt necessary for her good

[036-106]digestion, I ventured to ask her to drink wine with me. But when I

[036-107]bowed my head at her, she looked at me with all her eyes, struck with

[036-108]amazement. Had I suggested that she should join me in a wild Indian

[036-109]war-dance, with nothing on but my paint, her face could not have

[036-110]shown greater astonishment. And yet I should have thought she might

[036-111]have remembered the days when Christian men and women used to drink

[036-112]wine with each other.

[036-113]God be with the good old days when I could hobnob with my friend over

[036-114]the table as often as I was inclined to lift my glass to my lips, and

[036-115]make a long arm for a hot potato whenever the exigencies of my plate

[036-116]required it.

[036-117]I think it may be laid down as a rule in affairs of hospitality, that

[036-118]whatever extra luxury or grandeur we introduce at our tables when

[036-119]guests are with us, should be introduced for the advantage of the

[036-120]guest and not for our own. If, for instance, our dinner be served in

[036-121]a manner different from that usual to us, it should be so served in

[036-122]order that our friends may with more satisfaction eat our repast than

[036-123]our everyday practice would produce on them. But the change should

[036-124]by no means be made to their material detriment in order that our

[036-125]fashion may be acknowledged. Again, if I decorate my sideboard and

[036-126]table, wishing that the eyes of my visitors may rest on that which

[036-127]is elegant and pleasant to the sight, I act in that matter with

[036-128]a becoming sense of hospitality; but if my object be to kill Mrs.

[036-129]Jones with envy at the sight of all my silver trinkets, I am a very

[036-130]mean-spirited fellow. This, in a broad way, will be acknowledged; but

[036-131]if we would bear in mind the same idea at all times,—on occasions

[036-132]when the way perhaps may not be so broad, when more thinking may

[036-133]be required to ascertain what is true hospitality,—I think we

[036-134]of the eight hundred would make a greater advance towards really

[036-135]entertaining our own friends than by any rearrangement of the actual

[036-136]meats and dishes which we set before them.

[036-137]Knowing as we do, that the terms of the Lufton-Grantly alliance had

[036-138]been so solemnly ratified between the two mothers, it is perhaps

[036-139]hardly open to us to suppose that Mrs. Grantly was induced to take

[036-140]her daughter to Mrs. Proudie’s by any knowledge which she may have

[036-141]acquired that Lord Dumbello had promised to grace the bishop’s

[036-142]assembly. It is certainly the fact that high contracting parties

[036-143]do sometimes allow themselves a latitude which would be considered

[036-144]dishonest by contractors of a lower sort; and it may be possible that

[036-145]the archdeacon’s wife did think of that second string with which

[036-146]her bow was furnished. Be that as it may, Lord Dumbello was at Mrs.

[036-147]Proudie’s, and it did so come to pass that Griselda was seated at

[036-148]the corner of a sofa close to which was a vacant space in which his

[036-149]lordship could—“group himself.”

[036-150]They had not been long there before Lord Dumbello did group himself.

[036-151]“Fine day,” he said, coming up and occupying the vacant position by

[036-152]Miss Grantly’s elbow.

[036-153]“We were driving to-day, and we thought it rather cold,” said

[036-154]Griselda.

[036-155]“Deuced cold,” said Lord Dumbello, and then he adjusted his white

[036-156]cravat and touched up his whiskers. Having got so far, he did not

[036-157]proceed to any other immediate conversational efforts; nor did

[036-158]Griselda. But he grouped himself again as became a marquis, and gave

[036-159]very intense satisfaction to Mrs. Proudie.

[036-160]“This is so kind of you, Lord Dumbello,” said that lady, coming up to

[036-161]him and shaking his hand warmly; “so very kind of you to come to my

[036-162]poor little tea-party.”

[036-163]“Uncommonly pleasant, I call it,” said his lordship. “I like this

[036-164]sort of thing—no trouble, you know.”

[036-165]“No; that is the charm of it: isn’t it? no trouble, or fuss,

[036-166]or parade. That’s what I always say. According to my ideas,

[036-167]society consists in giving people facility for an interchange of

[036-168]thoughts—what we call conversation.”

[036-169]“Aw, yes, exactly.”

[036-170]“Not in eating and drinking together—eh, Lord Dumbello? And yet the

[036-171]practice of our lives would seem to show that the indulgence of those

[036-172]animal propensities can alone suffice to bring people together. The

[036-173]world in this has surely made a great mistake.”

[036-174]“I like a good dinner all the same,” said Lord Dumbello.

[036-175]“Oh, yes, of course—of course. I am by no means one of those who

[036-176]would pretend to preach that our tastes have not been given to us for

[036-177]our enjoyment. Why should things be nice if we are not to like them?”

[036-178]“A man who can really give a good dinner has learned a great deal,”

[036-179]said Lord Dumbello, with unusual animation.

[036-180]“An immense deal. It is quite an art in itself; and one which I, at

[036-181]any rate, by no means despise. But we cannot always be eating—can

[036-182]we?”

[036-183]“No,” said Lord Dumbello, “not always.” And he looked as though he

[036-184]lamented that his powers should be so circumscribed.
