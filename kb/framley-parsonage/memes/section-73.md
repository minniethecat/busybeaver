## Section 73

[073-001]“I will have them all moved from this till you are better,” said

[073-002]Lucy, boldly.

[073-003]“Moved!” said Mr. Crawley, who even now—even in his present

[073-004]strait—felt a repugnance to the idea that any one should relieve him

[073-005]of any portion of his burden.

[073-006]“Yes,” said Lucy; “I am sure it will be better that you should lose

[073-007]them for a week or two, till Mrs. Crawley may be able to leave her

[073-008]room.”

[073-009]“But where are they to go?” said he, very gloomily.

[073-010]As to this Lucy was not as yet able to say anything. Indeed when she

[073-011]left Framley Parsonage there had been no time for discussion. She

[073-012]would go back and talk it all over with Fanny, and find out in what

[073-013]way the children might be best put out of danger. Why should they

[073-014]not all be harboured at the parsonage, as soon as assurance could

[073-015]be felt that they were not tainted with the poison of the fever? An

[073-016]English lady of the right sort will do all things but one for a sick

[073-017]neighbour; but for no neighbour will she wittingly admit contagious

[073-018]sickness within the precincts of her own nursery.

[073-019]Lucy unloaded her jellies and her febrifuges, Mr. Crawley frowning

[073-020]at her bitterly the while. It had come to this with him, that food

[073-021]had been brought into his house, as an act of charity, in his very

[073-022]presence, and in his heart of hearts he disliked Lucy Robarts in that

[073-023]she had brought it. He could not cause the jars and the pots to be

[073-024]replaced in the pony-carriage, as he would have done had the position

[073-025]of his wife been different. In her state it would have been barbarous

[073-026]to refuse them, and barbarous also to have created the _fracas_ of a

[073-027]refusal; but each parcel that was introduced was an additional weight

[073-028]laid on the sore withers of his pride, till the total burden became

[073-029]almost intolerable. All this his wife saw and recognized even in her

[073-030]illness, and did make some slight ineffectual efforts to give him

[073-031]ease; but Lucy in her new power was ruthless, and the chicken to make

[073-032]the chicken-broth was taken out of the basket under his very nose.

[073-033]But Lucy did not remain long. She had made up her mind what it

[073-034]behoved her to do herself, and she was soon ready to return to

[073-035]Framley. “I shall be back again, Mr. Crawley,” she said, “probably

[073-036]this evening, and I shall stay with her till she is better.” “Nurses

[073-037]don’t want rooms,” she went on to say, when Mr. Crawley muttered

[073-038]something as to there being no bed-chamber. “I shall make up some

[073-039]sort of a litter near her; you’ll see that I shall be very snug.” And

[073-040]then she got into the pony-chaise, and drove herself home.

[073-041]CHAPTER XXXV.

[073-042]THE STORY OF KING COPHETUA.

[073-043]Lucy as she drove herself home had much as to which it was necessary

[073-044]that she should arouse her thoughts. That she would go back and nurse

[073-045]Mrs. Crawley through her fever she was resolved. She was free agent

[073-046]enough to take so much on herself, and to feel sure that she could

[073-047]carry it through. But how was she to redeem her promise about the

[073-048]children? Twenty plans ran through her mind, as to farm-houses in

[073-049]which they might be placed, or cottages which might be hired for

[073-050]them; but all these entailed the want of money; and at the present

[073-051]moment, were not all the inhabitants of the parsonage pledged to a

[073-052]dire economy? This use of the pony-carriage would have been illicit

[073-053]under any circumstances less pressing than the present, for it had

[073-054]been decided that the carriage, and even poor Puck himself, should

[073-055]be sold. She had, however, given her promise about the children, and

[073-056]though her own stock of money was very low, that promise should be

[073-057]redeemed.

[073-058]When she reached the parsonage she was of course full of her schemes,

[073-059]but she found that another subject of interest had come up in her

[073-060]absence, which prevented her from obtaining the undivided attention

[073-061]of her sister-in-law to her present plans. Lady Lufton had returned

[073-062]that day, and immediately on her return had sent up a note addressed

[073-063]to Miss Lucy Robarts, which note was in Fanny’s hands when Lucy

[073-064]stepped out of the pony-carriage. The servant who brought it had

[073-065]asked for an answer, and a verbal answer had been sent, saying that

[073-066]Miss Robarts was away from home, and would herself send a reply when

[073-067]she returned. It cannot be denied that the colour came to Lucy’s

[073-068]face, and that her hand trembled when she took the note from Fanny in

[073-069]the drawing-room. Everything in the world to her might depend on what

[073-070]that note contained; and yet she did not open it at once, but stood

[073-071]with it in her hand, and when Fanny pressed her on the subject, still

[073-072]endeavoured to bring back the conversation to the subject of Mrs.

[073-073]Crawley.

[073-074]But yet her mind was intent on the letter, and she had already

[073-075]augured ill from the handwriting and even from the words of the

[073-076]address. Had Lady Lufton intended to be propitious, she would have

[073-077]directed her letter to Miss Robarts, without the Christian name; so

[073-078]at least argued Lucy,—quite unconsciously, as one does argue in such

[073-079]matters. One forms half the conclusions of one’s life without any

[073-080]distinct knowledge that the premises have even passed through one’s

[073-081]mind.

[073-082]They were now alone together, as Mark was out.

[073-083]“Won’t you open her letter?” said Mrs. Robarts.

[073-084]“Yes, immediately; but, Fanny, I must speak to you about Mrs. Crawley

[073-085]first. I must go back there this evening, and stay there; I have

[073-086]promised to do so, and shall certainly keep my promise. I have

[073-087]promised also that the children shall be taken away, and we must

[073-088]arrange about that. It is dreadful, the state she is in. There is no

[073-089]one to see to her but Mr. Crawley, and the children are altogether

[073-090]left to themselves.”

[073-091]“Do you mean that you are going back to stay?”

[073-092]“Yes, certainly; I have made a distinct promise that I would do

[073-093]so. And about the children; could not you manage for the children,

[073-094]Fanny,—not perhaps in the house; at least not at first perhaps?”

[073-095]And yet during all the time that she was thus speaking and pleading

[073-096]for the Crawleys, she was endeavouring to imagine what might be the

[073-097]contents of that letter which she held between her fingers.

[073-098]“And is she so very ill?” asked Mrs. Robarts.

[073-099]“I cannot say how ill she may be, except this, that she certainly has

[073-100]typhus fever. They have had some doctor or doctor’s assistant from

[073-101]Silverbridge; but it seems to me that they are greatly in want of

[073-102]better advice.”

[073-103]“But, Lucy, will you not read your letter? It is astonishing to me

[073-104]that you should be so indifferent about it.”

[073-105]Lucy was anything but indifferent, and now did proceed to tear the

[073-106]envelope. The note was very short, and ran in these words,—

[073-107]MY DEAR MISS ROBARTS,—I am particularly anxious to see

[073-108]you, and shall feel much obliged to you if you can step

[073-109]over to me here, at Framley Court. I must apologize for

[073-110]taking this liberty with you, but you will probably feel

[073-111]that an interview here would suit us both better than one

[073-112]at the parsonage. Truly yours,

[073-113]M. LUFTON.

[073-114]“There; I am in for it now,” said Lucy, handing the note over to Mrs.

[073-115]Robarts. “I shall have to be talked to as never poor girl was talked

[073-116]to before; and when one thinks of what I have done, it is hard.”

[073-117]“Yes; and of what you have not done.”

[073-118]“Exactly; and of what I have not done. But I suppose I must go,” and

[073-119]she proceeded to re-tie the strings of her bonnet, which she had

[073-120]loosened.

[073-121]“Do you mean that you are going over at once?”

[073-122]“Yes; immediately. Why not? it will be better to have it over, and

[073-123]then I can go to the Crawleys. But, Fanny, the pity of it is that I

[073-124]know it all as well as though it had been already spoken; and what

[073-125]good can there be in my having to endure it? Can’t you fancy the

[073-126]tone in which she will explain to me the conventional inconveniences

[073-127]which arose when King Cophetua would marry the beggar’s daughter? how

[073-128]she will explain what Griselda went through;—not the archdeacon’s

[073-129]daughter, but the other Griselda?”

[073-130]“But it all came right with her.”

[073-131]“Yes; but then I am not Griselda, and she will explain how it would

[073-132]certainly all go wrong with me. But what’s the good when I know it

[073-133]all beforehand? Have I not desired King Cophetua to take himself and

[073-134]sceptre elsewhere?”

[073-135]And then she started, having first said another word or two about

[073-136]the Crawley children, and obtained a promise of Puck and the

[073-137]pony-carriage for the afternoon. It was also almost agreed that

[073-138]Puck on his return to Framley should bring back the four children

[073-139]with him; but on this subject it was necessary that Mark should be

[073-140]consulted. The present scheme was to prepare for them a room outside

[073-141]the house, once the dairy, at present occupied by the groom and his

[073-142]wife; and to bring them into the house as soon as it was manifest

[073-143]that there was no danger from infection. But all this was to be

[073-144]matter for deliberation.

[073-145]Fanny wanted her to send over a note, in reply to Lady Lufton’s, as

[073-146]harbinger of her coming; but Lucy marched off, hardly answering this

[073-147]proposition.

[073-148]“What’s the use of such a deal of ceremony?” she said. “I know she’s

[073-149]at home; and if she is not, I shall only lose ten minutes in going.”

[073-150]And so she went, and on reaching the door of Framley Court house

[073-151]found that her ladyship was at home. Her heart almost came to her

[073-152]mouth as she was told so, and then, in two minutes’ time, she found

[073-153]herself in the little room upstairs. In that little room we found

[073-154]ourselves once before,—you, and I, O my reader;—but Lucy had never

[073-155]before visited that hallowed precinct. There was something in its

[073-156]air calculated to inspire awe in those who first saw Lady Lufton

[073-157]sitting bolt upright in the cane-bottomed arm-chair, which she always

[073-158]occupied when at work at her books and papers; and this she knew when

[073-159]she determined to receive Lucy in that apartment. But there was there

[073-160]another arm-chair, an easy, cozy chair, which stood by the fireside;

[073-161]and for those who had caught Lady Lufton napping in that chair of an

[073-162]afternoon, some of this awe had perhaps been dissipated.

[073-163]“Miss Robarts,” she said, not rising from her chair, but holding out

[073-164]her hand to her visitor; “I am much obliged to you for having come

[073-165]over to me here. You, no doubt, are aware of the subject on which I

[073-166]wish to speak to you, and will agree with me that it is better that

[073-167]we should meet here than over at the parsonage.”

[073-168]In answer to which Lucy merely bowed her head, and took her seat on

[073-169]the chair which had been prepared for her.

[073-170]“My son,” continued her ladyship, “has spoken to me on the subject

[073-171]of—I think I understand, Miss Robarts, that there has been no

[073-172]engagement between you and him?”

[073-173]“None whatever,” said Lucy. “He made me an offer and I refused

[073-174]him.” This she said very sharply;—more so undoubtedly than the

[073-175]circumstances required; and with a brusqueness that was injudicious

[073-176]as well as uncourteous. But at the moment, she was thinking of her

[073-177]own position with reference to Lady Lufton—not to Lord Lufton; and

[073-178]of her feelings with reference to the lady—not to the gentleman.

[073-179]“Oh,” said Lady Lufton, a little startled by the manner of the

[073-180]communication. “Then I am to understand that there is nothing now

[073-181]going on between you and my son;—that the whole affair is over?”
