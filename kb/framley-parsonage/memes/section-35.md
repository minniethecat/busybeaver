## Section 35

[035-001]“It is, it is; and I am so glad that you acknowledge it.”

[035-002]“I should be worse than a brute did I not do so; but, nevertheless, I

[035-003]cannot allow her to lead me in all things. Were I to do so, I should

[035-004]cease to be a man.”

[035-005]“Where can you find any one who will counsel you so truly?”

[035-006]“But, nevertheless, I must rule myself. I do not know whether my

[035-007]suspicions may be perfectly just, but I fancy that she has created

[035-008]this estrangement between you and me. Has it not been so?”

[035-009]“Certainly not by speaking to me,” said Lucy, blushing ruby-red

[035-010]through every vein of her deep-tinted face. But though she could not

[035-011]command her blood, her voice was still under her control—her voice

[035-012]and her manner.

[035-013]“But has she not done so? You, I know, will tell me nothing but the

[035-014]truth.”

[035-015]“I will tell you nothing on this matter, Lord Lufton, whether true or

[035-016]false. It is a subject on which it does not concern me to speak.”

[035-017]“Ah! I understand,” he said; and rising from his chair, he stood

[035-018]against the chimney-piece with his back to the fire. “She cannot

[035-019]leave me alone to choose for myself my own friends, and my own—;”

[035-020]but he did not fill up the void.

[035-021]“But why tell me this, Lord Lufton?”

[035-022]“No! I am not to choose my own friends, though they be among the best

[035-023]and purest of God’s creatures. Lucy, I cannot think that you have

[035-024]ceased to have a regard for me. That you had a regard for me, I am

[035-025]sure.”

[035-026]She felt that it was almost unmanly of him thus to seek her out,

[035-027]and hunt her down, and then throw upon her the whole weight of

[035-028]the explanation that his coming thither made necessary. But,

[035-029]nevertheless, the truth must be told, and with God’s help she would

[035-030]find strength for the telling of it.

[035-031]“Yes, Lord Lufton, I had a regard for you—and have. By that word you

[035-032]mean something more than the customary feeling of acquaintance which

[035-033]may ordinarily prevail between a gentleman and lady of different

[035-034]families, who have known each other so short a time as we have done.”

[035-035]“Yes, something much more,” said he, with energy.

[035-036]“Well, I will not define the much—something closer than that.”

[035-037]“Yes, and warmer, and dearer, and more worthy of two human creatures

[035-038]who value each other’s minds and hearts.”

[035-039]“Some such closer regard I have felt for you—very foolishly. Stop!

[035-040]You have made me speak, and do not interrupt me now. Does not your

[035-041]conscience tell you that in doing so I have unwisely deserted

[035-042]those wise old grandmother’s tramways of which you spoke just now?

[035-043]It has been pleasant to me to do so. I have liked the feeling of

[035-044]independence with which I have thought that I might indulge in an

[035-045]open friendship with such as you are. And your rank, so different

[035-046]from my own, has doubtless made this more attractive.”

[035-047]“Nonsense!”

[035-048]“Ah! but it has. I know it now. But what will the world say of me as

[035-049]to such an alliance?”

[035-050]“The world!”

[035-051]“Yes, the world! I am not such a philosopher as to disregard it,

[035-052]though you may afford to do so. The world will say that I, the

[035-053]parson’s sister, set my cap at the young lord, and that the young

[035-054]lord had made a fool of me.”

[035-055]“The world shall say no such thing!” said Lord Lufton, very

[035-056]imperiously.

[035-057]“Ah! but it will. You can no more stop it, than King Canute could the

[035-058]waters. Your mother has interfered wisely to spare me from this; and

[035-059]the only favour that I can ask you is, that you will spare me also.”

[035-060]And then she got up as though she intended at once to walk forth to

[035-061]her visit to Mrs. Podgens’ baby.

[035-062]“Stop, Lucy!” he said, putting himself between her and the door.

[035-063]“It must not be Lucy any longer, Lord Lufton; I was madly foolish

[035-064]when I first allowed it.”

[035-065]“By heavens! but it shall be Lucy—Lucy before all the world. My

[035-066]Lucy, my own Lucy—my heart’s best friend, and chosen love. Lucy,

[035-067]there is my hand. How long you may have had my heart, it matters not

[035-068]to say now.”

[035-069]The game was at her feet now, and no doubt she felt her triumph. Her

[035-070]ready wit and speaking lip, not her beauty, had brought him to her

[035-071]side; and now he was forced to acknowledge that her power over him

[035-072]had been supreme. Sooner than leave her he would risk all. She did

[035-073]feel her triumph; but there was nothing in her face to tell him that

[035-074]she did so.

[035-075]As to what she would now do she did not for a moment doubt. He had

[035-076]been precipitated into the declaration he had made, not by his love,

[035-077]but by his embarrassment. She had thrown in his teeth the injury

[035-078]which he had done her, and he had then been moved by his generosity

[035-079]to repair that injury by the noblest sacrifice which he could make.

[035-080]But Lucy Robarts was not the girl to accept a sacrifice.

[035-081]He had stepped forward as though he were going to clasp her round the

[035-082]waist, but she receded, and got beyond the reach of his hand. “Lord

[035-083]Lufton!” she said, “when you are more cool you will know that this is

[035-084]wrong. The best thing for both of us now is to part.”

[035-085]“Not the best thing, but the very worst, till we perfectly understand

[035-086]each other.”

[035-087]“Then perfectly understand me, that I cannot be your wife.”

[035-088]“Lucy! do you mean that you cannot learn to love me?”

[035-089]“I mean that I shall not try. Do not persevere in this, or you will

[035-090]have to hate yourself for your own folly.”

[035-091]“But I will persevere till you accept my love, or say with your hand

[035-092]on your heart that you cannot and will not love me.”

[035-093]“Then I must beg you to let me go,” and having so said, she paused

[035-094]while he walked once or twice hurriedly up and down the room. “And,

[035-095]Lord Lufton,” she continued, “if you will leave me now, the words

[035-096]that you have spoken shall be as though they had never been uttered.”

[035-097]“I care not who knows that they have been uttered. The sooner that

[035-098]they are known to all the world, the better I shall be pleased,

[035-099]unless indeed—”

[035-100]“Think of your mother, Lord Lufton.”

[035-101]“What can I do better than give her as a daughter the best and

[035-102]sweetest girl I have ever met? When my mother really knows you, she

[035-103]will love you as I do. Lucy, say one word to me of comfort.”

[035-104]“I will say no word to you that shall injure your future comfort. It

[035-105]is impossible that I should be your wife.”

[035-106]“Do you mean that you cannot love me?”

[035-107]“You have no right to press me any further,” she said; and sat down

[035-108]upon the sofa, with an angry frown upon her forehead.

[035-109]“By heavens,” he said, “I will take no such answer from you till you

[035-110]put your hand upon your heart, and say that you cannot love me.”

[035-111]“Oh, why should you press me so, Lord Lufton?”

[035-112]“Why! because my happiness depends upon it; because it behoves me to

[035-113]know the very truth. It has come to this, that I love you with my

[035-114]whole heart, and I must know how your heart stands towards me.”

[035-115]She had now again risen from the sofa, and was looking steadily in

[035-116]his face.

[035-117]“Lord Lufton,” she said, “I cannot love you,” and as she spoke she

[035-118]did put her hand, as he had desired, upon her heart.

[035-119]“Then God help me! for I am very wretched. Good-bye, Lucy,” and he

[035-120]stretched out his hand to her.

[035-121]“Good-bye, my lord. Do not be angry with me.”

[035-122]“No, no, no!” and without further speech he left the room and the

[035-123]house, and hurried home. It was hardly surprising that he should that

[035-124]evening tell his mother that Griselda Grantly would be a companion

[035-125]sufficiently good for his sister. He wanted no such companion.

[035-126]And when he was well gone—absolutely out of sight from the

[035-127]window—Lucy walked steadily up to her room, locked the door, and

[035-128]then threw herself on the bed. Why—oh! why had she told such

[035-129]a falsehood? Could anything justify her in a lie? Was it not a

[035-130]lie—knowing as she did that she loved him with all her loving heart?

[035-131][Illustration: “Was it not a lie?”]

[035-132]But, then, his mother! and the sneers of the world, which would have

[035-133]declared that she had set her trap, and caught the foolish young

[035-134]lord! Her pride would not have submitted to that. Strong as her love

[035-135]was, yet her pride was, perhaps, stronger—stronger at any rate

[035-136]during that interview.

[035-137]But how was she to forgive herself the falsehood she had told?

[035-138]CHAPTER XVII.

[035-139]MRS. PROUDIE’S CONVERSAZIONE.

[035-140]It was grievous to think of the mischief and danger into which

[035-141]Griselda Grantly was brought by the worldliness of her mother in

[035-142]those few weeks previous to Lady Lufton’s arrival in town—very

[035-143]grievous, at least, to her ladyship, as from time to time she heard

[035-144]of what was done in London. Lady Hartletop’s was not the only

[035-145]objectionable house at which Griselda was allowed to reap fresh

[035-146]fashionable laurels. It had been stated openly in the _Morning Post_

[035-147]that that young lady had been the most admired among the beautiful at

[035-148]one of Miss Dunstable’s celebrated _soirées_, and then she was heard

[035-149]of as gracing the drawing-room at Mrs. Proudie’s conversazione.

[035-150]Of Miss Dunstable herself Lady Lufton was not able openly to allege

[035-151]any evil. She was acquainted, Lady Lufton knew, with very many people

[035-152]of the right sort, and was the dear friend of Lady Lufton’s highly

[035-153]conservative and not very distant neighbours, the Greshams. But then

[035-154]she was also acquainted with so many people of the bad sort. Indeed,

[035-155]she was intimate with everybody, from the Duke of Omnium to old

[035-156]Dowager Lady Goodygaffer, who had represented all the cardinal

[035-157]virtues for the last quarter of a century. She smiled with equal

[035-158]sweetness on treacle and on brimstone; was quite at home at Exeter

[035-159]Hall, having been consulted—so the world said, probably not with

[035-160]exact truth—as to the selection of more than one disagreeably Low

[035-161]Church bishop; and was not less frequent in her attendance at the

[035-162]ecclesiastical doings of a certain terrible prelate in the Midland

[035-163]counties, who was supposed to favour stoles and vespers, and to have

[035-164]no proper Protestant hatred for auricular confession and fish on

[035-165]Fridays. Lady Lufton, who was very staunch, did not like this, and

[035-166]would say of Miss Dunstable that it was impossible to serve both God

[035-167]and Mammon.

[035-168]But Mrs. Proudie was much more objectionable to her. Seeing how

[035-169]sharp was the feud between the Proudies and the Grantlys down in

[035-170]Barsetshire, how absolutely unable they had always been to carry a

[035-171]decent face towards each other in church matters, how they headed two

[035-172]parties in the diocese, which were, when brought together, as oil and

[035-173]vinegar, in which battles the whole Lufton influence had always been

[035-174]brought to bear on the Grantly side;—seeing all this, I say, Lady

[035-175]Lufton was surprised to hear that Griselda had been taken to Mrs.

[035-176]Proudie’s evening exhibition. “Had the archdeacon been consulted

[035-177]about it,” she said to herself, “this would never have happened.”

[035-178]But there she was wrong, for in matters concerning his daughter’s

[035-179]introduction to the world the archdeacon never interfered.

[035-180]On the whole, I am inclined to think that Mrs. Grantly understood

[035-181]the world better than did Lady Lufton. In her heart of hearts Mrs.

[035-182]Grantly hated Mrs. Proudie—that is, with that sort of hatred one

[035-183]Christian lady allows herself to feel towards another. Of course Mrs.

[035-184]Grantly forgave Mrs. Proudie all her offences, and wished her well,

[035-185]and was at peace with her, in the Christian sense of the word, as

[035-186]with all other women. But under this forbearance and meekness, and

[035-187]perhaps, we may say, wholly unconnected with it, there was certainly

[035-188]a current of antagonistic feeling which, in the ordinary unconsidered

[035-189]language of every day, men and women do call hatred. This raged and

[035-190]was strong throughout the whole year in Barsetshire, before the eyes

[035-191]of all mankind. But, nevertheless, Mrs. Grantly took Griselda to Mrs.

[035-192]Proudie’s evening parties in London.
