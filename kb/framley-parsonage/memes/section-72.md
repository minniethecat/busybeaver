## Section 72

[072-001]“But you say that you are not engaged.”

[072-002]“No, I am not; but I have made my offer to her, and I have not been

[072-003]rejected. She has confessed that she—loves me,—not to myself, but

[072-004]to her brother. Under these circumstances, may I count upon your

[072-005]obliging me?”

[072-006]There was something in his manner which almost frightened his mother,

[072-007]and made her think that there was more behind than was told to her.

[072-008]Generally speaking, his manner was open, gentle, and unguarded; but

[072-009]now he spoke as though he had prepared his words, and was resolved on

[072-010]being harsh as well as obstinate.

[072-011]“I am so much taken by surprise, Ludovic, that I can hardly give you

[072-012]an answer. If you ask me whether I approve of such a marriage, I must

[072-013]say that I do not; I think that you would be throwing yourself away

[072-014]in marrying Miss Robarts.”

[072-015]“That is because you do not know her.”

[072-016]“May it not be possible that I know her better than you do, dear

[072-017]Ludovic? You have been flirting with her—”

[072-018]“I hate that word; it always sounds to me to be vulgar.”

[072-019]“I will say making love to her, if you like it better; and gentlemen

[072-020]under these circumstances will sometimes become infatuated.”

[072-021]“You would not have a man marry a girl without making love to her.

[072-022]The fact is, mother, that your tastes and mine are not exactly the

[072-023]same; you like silent beauty, whereas I like talking beauty, and

[072-024]then—”

[072-025]“Do you call Miss Robarts beautiful?”

[072-026]“Yes, I do; very beautiful; she has the beauty that I admire.

[072-027]Good-bye now, mother; I shall not see you again before I start. It

[072-028]will be no use writing, as I shall be away so short a time, and I

[072-029]don’t quite know where we shall be. I shall come down to Framley

[072-030]immediately I return, and shall learn from you how the land lies. I

[072-031]have told you my wishes, and you will consider how far you think it

[072-032]right to fall in with them.” He then kissed her, and without waiting

[072-033]for her reply he took his leave.

[072-034]Poor Lady Lufton, when she was left to herself, felt that her

[072-035]head was going round and round. Was this to be the end of all her

[072-036]ambition,—of all her love for her son? and was this to be the result

[072-037]of all her kindness to the Robartses? She almost hated Mark Robarts

[072-038]as she reflected that she had been the means of bringing him and his

[072-039]sister to Framley. She thought over all his sins, his absences from

[072-040]the parish, his visit to Gatherum Castle, his dealings with reference

[072-041]to that farm which was to have been sold, his hunting, and then his

[072-042]acceptance of that stall, given, as she had been told, through the

[072-043]Omnium interest. How could she love him at such a moment as this?

[072-044]And then she thought of his wife. Could it be possible that Fanny

[072-045]Robarts, her own friend Fanny, would be so untrue to her as to

[072-046]lend any assistance to such a marriage as this; as not to use all

[072-047]her power in preventing it? She had spoken to Fanny on this very

[072-048]subject,—not fearing for her son, but with a general idea of the

[072-049]impropriety of intimacies between such girls as Lucy and such men as

[072-050]Lord Lufton, and then Fanny had agreed with her. Could it be possible

[072-051]that even she must be regarded as an enemy?

[072-052]And then by degrees Lady Lufton began to reflect what steps she had

[072-053]better take. In the first place, should she give in at once, and

[072-054]consent to the marriage? The only thing quite certain to her was

[072-055]this, that life would be not worth having if she were forced into a

[072-056]permanent quarrel with her son. Such an event would probably kill

[072-057]her. When she read of quarrels in other noble families—and the

[072-058]accounts of such quarrels will sometimes, unfortunately, force

[072-059]themselves upon the attention of unwilling readers—she would hug

[072-060]herself, with a spirit that was almost pharisaical, reflecting that

[072-061]her destiny was not like that of others. Such quarrels and hatreds

[072-062]between fathers and daughters, and mothers and sons, were in her eyes

[072-063]disreputable to all the persons concerned. She had lived happily with

[072-064]her husband, comfortably with her neighbours, respectably with the

[072-065]world, and, above all things, affectionately with her children.

[072-066]She spoke everywhere of Lord Lufton as though he were nearly

[072-067]perfect,—and in so speaking, she had not belied her convictions.

[072-068]Under these circumstances, would not any marriage be better than a

[072-069]quarrel?

[072-070]But then, again, how much of the pride of her daily life would be

[072-071]destroyed by such a match as that! And might it not be within her

[072-072]power to prevent it without any quarrel? That her son would be

[072-073]sick of such a chit as Lucy before he had been married to her six

[072-074]months—of that Lady Lufton entertained no doubt, and therefore her

[072-075]conscience would not be disquieted in disturbing the consummation of

[072-076]an arrangement so pernicious. It was evident that the matter was not

[072-077]considered as settled even by her son; and also evident that he

[072-078]regarded the matter as being in some way dependent on his mother’s

[072-079]consent. On the whole, might it not be better for her—better for

[072-080]them all—that she should think wholly of her duty, and not of the

[072-081]disagreeable results to which that duty might possibly lead? It

[072-082]could not be her duty to accede to such an alliance; and therefore

[072-083]she would do her best to prevent it. Such, at least, should be her

[072-084]attempt in the first instance.

[072-085]Having so decided, she next resolved on her course of action.

[072-086]Immediately on her arrival at Framley, she would send for Lucy

[072-087]Robarts, and use all her eloquence—and perhaps also a little of that

[072-088]stern dignity for which she was so remarkable—in explaining to that

[072-089]young lady how very wicked it was on her part to think of forcing

[072-090]herself into such a family as that of the Luftons. She would explain

[072-091]to Lucy that no happiness could come of it, that people placed by

[072-092]misfortune above their sphere are always miserable; and, in short,

[072-093]make use of all those excellent moral lessons which are so customary

[072-094]on such occasions. The morality might, perhaps, be thrown away;

[072-095]but Lady Lufton depended much on her dignified sternness. And then,

[072-096]having so resolved, she prepared for her journey home.

[072-097]Very little had been said at Framley Parsonage about Lord Lufton’s

[072-098]offer after the departure of that gentleman; very little, at least,

[072-099]in Lucy’s presence. That the parson and his wife should talk about it

[072-100]between themselves was a matter of course; but very few words were

[072-101]spoken on the matter either by or to Lucy. She was left to her own

[072-102]thoughts, and possibly to her own hopes.

[072-103]And then other matters came up at Framley which turned the current of

[072-104]interest into other tracks. In the first place there was the visit

[072-105]made by Mr. Sowerby to the Dragon of Wantly, and the consequent

[072-106]revelation made by Mark Robarts to his wife. And while that latter

[072-107]subject was yet new, before Fanny and Lucy had as yet made up their

[072-108]minds as to all the little economies which might be practised in the

[072-109]household without serious detriment to the master’s comfort, news

[072-110]reached them that Mrs. Crawley of Hogglestock had been stricken with

[072-111]fever. Nothing of the kind could well be more dreadful than this.

[072-112]To those who knew the family it seemed impossible that their most

[072-113]ordinary wants could be supplied if that courageous head were even

[072-114]for a day laid low; and then the poverty of poor Mr. Crawley was

[072-115]such that the sad necessities of a sick bed could hardly be supplied

[072-116]without assistance.

[072-117]“I will go over at once,” said Fanny.

[072-118]“My dear!” said her husband, “it is typhus, and you must first think

[072-119]of the children. I will go.”

[072-120]“What on earth could you do, Mark?” said his wife. “Men on such

[072-121]occasions are almost worse than useless; and then they are so much

[072-122]more liable to infection.”

[072-123]“I have no children, nor am I a man,” said Lucy, smiling; “for both

[072-124]of which exemptions I am thankful. I will go, and when I come back

[072-125]I will keep clear of the bairns.”

[072-126]So it was settled, and Lucy started in the pony-carriage, carrying

[072-127]with her such things from the parsonage storehouse as were thought

[072-128]to be suitable to the wants of the sick lady at Hogglestock. When

[072-129]she arrived there, she made her way into the house, finding the door

[072-130]open, and not being able to obtain the assistance of the servant

[072-131]girl in ushering her in. In the parlour she found Grace Crawley,

[072-132]the eldest child, sitting demurely in her mother’s chair nursing an

[072-133]infant. She, Grace herself, was still a young child, but not the

[072-134]less, on this occasion of well-understood sorrow, did she go through

[072-135]her task not only with zeal but almost with solemnity. Her brother,

[072-136]a boy of six years old, was with her, and he had the care of

[072-137]another baby. There they sat in a cluster, quiet, grave, and silent,

[072-138]attending on themselves, because it had been willed by fate that no

[072-139]one else should attend on them.

[072-140]“How is your mamma, dear Grace?” said Lucy, walking up to her, and

[072-141]holding out her hand.

[072-142]“Poor mamma is very ill, indeed,” said Grace.

[072-143]“And papa is very unhappy,” said Bobby, the boy.

[072-144]“I can’t get up because of baby,” said Grace; “but Bobby can go and

[072-145]call papa out.”

[072-146]“I will knock at the door,” said Lucy, and so saying she walked up to

[072-147]the bedroom door, and tapped against it lightly. She repeated this

[072-148]for the third time before she was summoned in by a low hoarse voice,

[072-149]and then on entering she saw Mr. Crawley standing by the bedside with

[072-150]a book in his hand. He looked at her uncomfortably, in a manner which

[072-151]seemed to show that he was annoyed by this intrusion, and Lucy was

[072-152]aware that she had disturbed him while at prayers by the bedside of

[072-153]his wife. He came across the room, however, and shook hands with her,

[072-154]and answered her inquiries in his ordinary grave and solemn voice.

[072-155]“Mrs. Crawley is very ill,” he said, “very ill. God has stricken us

[072-156]heavily, but His will be done. But you had better not go to her, Miss

[072-157]Robarts. It is typhus.”

[072-158]The caution, however, was too late; for Lucy was already by the

[072-159]bedside, and had taken the hand of the sick woman, which had been

[072-160]extended on the coverlid to greet her. “Dear Miss Robarts,” said a

[072-161]weak voice; “this is very good of you; but it makes me unhappy to see

[072-162]you here.”

[072-163]Lucy lost no time in taking sundry matters into her own hands, and

[072-164]ascertaining what was most wanted in that wretched household. For

[072-165]it was wretched enough. Their only servant, a girl of sixteen, had

[072-166]been taken away by her mother as soon as it became known that Mrs.

[072-167]Crawley was ill with fever. The poor mother, to give her her due,

[072-168]had promised to come down morning and evening herself, to do such

[072-169]work as might be done in an hour or so; but she could not, she said,

[072-170]leave her child to catch the fever. And now, at the period of Lucy’s

[072-171]visit, no step had been taken to procure a nurse, Mr. Crawley having

[072-172]resolved to take upon himself the duties of that position. In his

[072-173]absolute ignorance of all sanatory measures, he had thrown himself on

[072-174]his knees to pray; and if prayers—true prayers—might succour his

[072-175]poor wife, of such succour she might be confident. Lucy, however,

[072-176]thought that other aid also was wanting to her.

[072-177]“If you can do anything for us,” said Mrs. Crawley, “let it be for

[072-178]the poor children.”
