## Section 25

[025-001]And then the gentlemen came in, and there was another stir in the

[025-002]room. Lady Lufton got up and bustled about; she poked the fire and

[025-003]shifted the candles, spoke a few words to Dr. Grantly, whispered

[025-004]something to her son, patted Lucy on the cheek, told Fanny, who was a

[025-005]musician, that they would have a little music, and ended by putting

[025-006]her two hands on Griselda’s shoulders and telling her that the fit

[025-007]of her frock was perfect. For Lady Lufton, though she did dress old

[025-008]herself, as Lucy had said, delighted to see those around her neat and

[025-009]pretty, jaunty and graceful.

[025-010]“Dear Lady Lufton!” said Griselda, putting up her hand so as to

[025-011]press the end of her ladyship’s fingers. It was the first piece of

[025-012]animation she had shown, and Lucy Robarts watched it all.

[025-013]And then there was music. Lucy neither played nor sang; Fanny did

[025-014]both, and for an amateur did both well. Griselda did not sing, but

[025-015]she played; and did so in a manner that showed that neither her own

[025-016]labour nor her father’s money had been spared in her instruction.

[025-017]Lord Lufton sang also, a little, and Captain Culpepper a very little;

[025-018]so that they got up a concert among them. In the meantime the doctor

[025-019]and Mark stood talking together on the rug before the fire; the two

[025-020]mothers sat contented, watching the billings and the cooings of their

[025-021]offspring—and Lucy sat alone, turning over the leaves of a book of

[025-022]pictures. She made up her mind fully, then and there, that she was

[025-023]quite unfitted by disposition for such work as this. She cared for

[025-024]no one, and no one cared for her. Well, she must go through with it

[025-025]now; but another time she would know better. With her own book and a

[025-026]fireside she never felt herself to be miserable as she was now.

[025-027]She had turned her back to the music, for she was sick of seeing

[025-028]Lord Lufton watch the artistic motion of Miss Grantly’s fingers, and

[025-029]was sitting at a small table as far away from the piano as a long

[025-030]room would permit, when she was suddenly roused from a reverie of

[025-031]self-reproach by a voice close behind her: “Miss Robarts,” said the

[025-032]voice, “why have you cut us all?” and Lucy felt that though she heard

[025-033]the words plainly, nobody else did. Lord Lufton was now speaking to

[025-034]her as he had before spoken to Miss Grantly.

[025-035]“I don’t play, my lord,” said Lucy, “nor yet sing.”

[025-036]“That would have made your company so much more valuable to us, for

[025-037]we are terribly badly off for listeners. Perhaps you don’t like

[025-038]music?”

[025-039]“I do like it,—sometimes very much.”

[025-040]“And when are the sometimes? But we shall find it all out in time. We

[025-041]shall have unravelled all your mysteries, and read all your riddles,

[025-042]by—when shall I say?—by the end of the winter. Shall we not?”

[025-043]“I do not know that I have got any mysteries.”

[025-044]“Oh, but you have! It is very mysterious in you to come and sit here,

[025-045]with your back to us all—”

[025-046]“Oh, Lord Lufton; if I have done wrong—!” and poor Lucy almost

[025-047]started from her chair, and a deep flush came across her dark cheek.

[025-048]“No—no; you have done no wrong. I was only joking. It is we who have

[025-049]done wrong in leaving you to yourself—you who are the greatest

[025-050]stranger among us.”

[025-051]“I have been very well, thank you. I don’t care about being left

[025-052]alone. I have always been used to it.”

[025-053]“Ah! but we must break you of the habit. We won’t allow you to make a

[025-054]hermit of yourself. But the truth is, Miss Robarts, you don’t know us

[025-055]yet, and therefore you are not quite happy among us.”

[025-056]“Oh! yes, I am; you are all very good to me.”

[025-057]“You must let us be good to you. At any rate, you must let me be so.

[025-058]You know, don’t you, that Mark and I have been dear friends since we

[025-059]were seven years old. His wife has been my sister’s dearest friend

[025-060]almost as long; and now that you are with them, you must be a dear

[025-061]friend too. You won’t refuse the offer; will you?”

[025-062]“Oh, no,” she said, quite in a whisper; and, indeed, she could hardly

[025-063]raise her voice above a whisper, fearing that tears would fall from

[025-064]her tell-tale eyes.

[025-065]“Dr. and Mrs. Grantly will have gone in a couple of days, and then we

[025-066]must get you down here. Miss Grantly is to remain for Christmas, and

[025-067]you two must become bosom friends.”

[025-068]Lucy smiled, and tried to look pleased, but she felt that she and

[025-069]Griselda Grantly could never be bosom friends—could never have

[025-070]anything in common between them. She felt sure that Griselda despised

[025-071]her, little, brown, plain, and unimportant as she was. She herself

[025-072]could not despise Griselda in turn; indeed she could not but admire

[025-073]Miss Grantly’s great beauty and dignity of demeanour; but she knew

[025-074]that she could never love her. It is hardly possible that the

[025-075]proud-hearted should love those who despise them; and Lucy Robarts

[025-076]was very proud-hearted.

[025-077]“Don’t you think she is very handsome?” said Lord Lufton.

[025-078]“Oh, very,” said Lucy. “Nobody can doubt that.”

[025-079]“Ludovic,” said Lady Lufton—not quite approving of her son’s

[025-080]remaining so long at the back of Lucy’s chair—“won’t you give us

[025-081]another song? Mrs. Robarts and Miss Grantly are still at the piano.”

[025-082]“I have sung away all that I knew, mother. There’s Culpepper has not

[025-083]had a chance yet. He has got to give us his dream—how he ‘dreamt

[025-084]that he dwelt in marble halls!’”

[025-085]“I sang that an hour ago,” said the captain, not over pleased.

[025-086]“But you certainly have not told us how ‘your little lovers came!’”

[025-087]The captain, however, would not sing any more. And then the party was

[025-088]broken up, and the Robartses went home to their parsonage.

[025-089]CHAPTER XII.

[025-090]THE LITTLE BILL.

[025-091]Lucy, during those last fifteen minutes of her sojourn in the Framley

[025-092]Court drawing-room, somewhat modified the very strong opinion she

[025-093]had before formed as to her unfitness for such society. It was very

[025-094]pleasant sitting there in that easy chair, while Lord Lufton stood at

[025-095]the back of it saying nice, soft, good-natured words to her. She was

[025-096]sure that in a little time she could feel a true friendship for him,

[025-097]and that she could do so without any risk of falling in love with

[025-098]him. But then she had a glimmering of an idea that such a friendship

[025-099]would be open to all manner of remarks, and would hardly be

[025-100]compatible with the world’s ordinary ways. At any rate it would be

[025-101]pleasant to be at Framley Court, if he would come and occasionally

[025-102]notice her. But she did not admit to herself that such a visit would

[025-103]be intolerable if his whole time were devoted to Griselda Grantly.

[025-104]She neither admitted it, nor thought it; but nevertheless, in a

[025-105]strange unconscious way, such a feeling did find entrance in her

[025-106]bosom.

[025-107]And then the Christmas holidays passed away. How much of this

[025-108]enjoyment fell to her share, and how much of this suffering she

[025-109]endured, we will not attempt accurately to describe. Miss Grantly

[025-110]remained at Framley Court up to Twelfth Night, and the Robartses

[025-111]also spent most of the season at the house. Lady Lufton, no doubt,

[025-112]had hoped that everything might have been arranged on this occasion

[025-113]in accordance with her wishes, but such had not been the case. Lord

[025-114]Lufton had evidently admired Miss Grantly very much; indeed, he

[025-115]had said so to his mother half-a-dozen times; but it may almost be

[025-116]questioned whether the pleasure Lady Lufton derived from this was not

[025-117]more than neutralized by an opinion he once put forward that Griselda

[025-118]Grantly wanted some of the fire of Lucy Robarts.

[025-119]“Surely, Ludovic, you would never compare the two girls,” said Lady

[025-120]Lufton.

[025-121]“Of course not. They are the very antipodes to each other. Miss

[025-122]Grantly would probably be more to my taste; but then I am wise enough

[025-123]to know that it is so because my taste is a bad taste.”

[025-124]“I know no man with a more accurate or refined taste in such

[025-125]matters,” said Lady Lufton. Beyond this she did not dare to go. She

[025-126]knew very well that her strategy would be vain should her son once

[025-127]learn that she had a strategy. To tell the truth, Lady Lufton was

[025-128]becoming somewhat indifferent to Lucy Robarts. She had been very kind

[025-129]to the little girl; but the little girl seemed hardly to appreciate

[025-130]the kindness as she should do—and then Lord Lufton would talk to

[025-131]Lucy, “which was so unnecessary, you know;” and Lucy had got into

[025-132]a way of talking quite freely with Lord Lufton, having completely

[025-133]dropped that short, spasmodic, ugly exclamation of “my lord.”

[025-134]And so the Christmas festivities were at an end, and January wore

[025-135]itself away. During the greater part of this month Lord Lufton did

[025-136]not remain at Framley, but was nevertheless in the county, hunting

[025-137]with the hounds of both divisions, and staying at various houses. Two

[025-138]or three nights he spent at Chaldicotes; and one—let it only be told

[025-139]in an under voice—at Gatherum Castle! Of this he said nothing to

[025-140]Lady Lufton. “Why make her unhappy?” as he said to Mark. But Lady

[025-141]Lufton knew it, though she said not a word to him—knew it, and was

[025-142]unhappy. “If he would only marry Griselda, there would be an end of

[025-143]that danger,” she said to herself.

[025-144]But now we must go back for a while to the vicar and his little bill.

[025-145]It will be remembered, that his first idea with reference to that

[025-146]trouble, after the reading of his father’s will, was to borrow the

[025-147]money from his brother John. John was down at Exeter at the time, and

[025-148]was to stay one night at the parsonage on his way to London. Mark

[025-149]would broach the matter to him on the journey, painful though it

[025-150]would be to him to tell the story of his own folly to a brother

[025-151]so much younger than himself, and who had always looked up to him,

[025-152]clergyman and full-blown vicar as he was, with a deference greater

[025-153]than that which such difference in age required.

[025-154]The story was told, however; but was told all in vain, as Mark found

[025-155]out before he reached Framley. His brother John immediately declared

[025-156]that he would lend him the money, of course—eight hundred, if

[025-157]his brother wanted it. He, John, confessed that, as regarded the

[025-158]remaining two, he should like to feel the pleasure of immediate

[025-159]possession. As for interest, he would not take any—take interest

[025-160]from a brother! of course not. Well, if Mark made such a fuss about

[025-161]it, he supposed he must take it; but would rather not. Mark should

[025-162]have his own way, and do just what he liked.

[025-163]This was all very well, and Mark had fully made up his mind that his

[025-164]brother should not be kept long out of his money. But then arose the

[025-165]question, how was that money to be reached? He, Mark, was executor,

[025-166]or one of the executors under his father’s will, and, therefore, no

[025-167]doubt, could put his hand upon it; but his brother wanted five months

[025-168]of being of age, and could not therefore as yet be put legally in

[025-169]possession of the legacy.

[025-170]“That’s a bore,” said the assistant private secretary to the Lord

[025-171]Petty Bag, thinking, perhaps, as much of his own immediate wish for

[025-172]ready cash as he did of his brother’s necessities. Mark felt that it

[025-173]was a bore, but there was nothing more to be done in that direction.

[025-174]He must now find out how far the bankers could assist him.

[025-175]Some week or two after his return to Framley he went over to

[025-176]Barchester, and called there on a certain Mr. Forrest, the manager

[025-177]of one of the banks, with whom he was acquainted; and with many

[025-178]injunctions as to secrecy told this manager the whole of his story.

[025-179]At first he concealed the name of his friend Sowerby, but it soon

[025-180]appeared that no such concealment was of any avail. “That’s Sowerby,

[025-181]of course,” said Mr. Forrest. “I know you are intimate with him; and

[025-182]all his friends go through that, sooner or later.”
