## Section 53

[053-001]“But I thought she was to remain with Lady Lufton?”

[053-002]“Well; so she will, for a little time. There is no one with whom I

[053-003]would so soon trust her out of my own care as with Lady Lufton. She

[053-004]is all that one can desire.”

[053-005]“Exactly; and as far as Griselda is concerned, I cannot say that I

[053-006]think she is to be pitied.”

[053-007]“Not to be pitied, perhaps,” said Mrs. Grantly. “But, you see,

[053-008]archdeacon, Lady Lufton, of course, has her own views.”

[053-009]“Her own views?”

[053-010]“It is hardly any secret that she is very anxious to make a match

[053-011]between Lord Lufton and Griselda. And though that might be a very

[053-012]proper arrangement if it were fixed—”

[053-013]“Lord Lufton marry Griselda!” said the archdeacon, speaking quick and

[053-014]raising his eyebrows. His mind had as yet been troubled by but few

[053-015]thoughts respecting his child’s future establishment. “I had never

[053-016]dreamt of such a thing.”

[053-017]“But other people have done more than dream of it, archdeacon. As

[053-018]regards the match itself, it would, I think, be unobjectionable. Lord

[053-019]Lufton will not be a very rich man, but his property is respectable,

[053-020]and as far as I can learn his character is on the whole good. If they

[053-021]like each other, I should be contented with such a marriage. But,

[053-022]I must own, I am not quite satisfied at the idea of leaving her all

[053-023]alone with Lady Lufton. People will look on it as a settled thing,

[053-024]when it is not settled—and very probably may not be settled; and

[053-025]that will do the poor girl harm. She is very much admired; there can

[053-026]be no doubt of that; and Lord Dumbello—”

[053-027]The archdeacon opened his eyes still wider. He had had no idea that

[053-028]such a choice of sons-in-law was being prepared for him; and, to

[053-029]tell the truth, was almost bewildered by the height of his wife’s

[053-030]ambition. Lord Lufton, with his barony and twenty thousand a year,

[053-031]might be accepted as just good enough; but failing him there was an

[053-032]embryo marquis, whose fortune would be more than ten times as great,

[053-033]all ready to accept his child! And then he thought, as husbands

[053-034]sometimes will think, of Susan Harding as she was when he had gone

[053-035]a-courting to her under the elms before the house in the warden’s

[053-036]garden at Barchester, and of dear old Mr. Harding, his wife’s father,

[053-037]who still lived in humble lodgings in that city; and as he thought,

[053-038]he wondered at and admired the greatness of that lady’s mind.

[053-039]“I never can forgive Lord De Terrier,” said the lady, connecting

[053-040]various points together in her own mind.

[053-041]“That’s nonsense,” said the archdeacon. “You must forgive him.”

[053-042]“And I must confess that it annoys me to leave London at present.”

[053-043]“It can’t be helped,” said the archdeacon, somewhat gruffly; for he

[053-044]was a man who, on certain points, chose to have his own way—and had

[053-045]it.

[053-046]“Oh, no: I know it can’t be helped,” said Mrs. Grantly, in a tone

[053-047]which implied a deep injury. “I know it can’t be helped. Poor

[053-048]Griselda!” And then they went to bed.

[053-049]On the next morning Griselda came to her, and in an interview that

[053-050]was strictly private, her mother said more to her than she had ever

[053-051]yet spoken, as to the prospects of her future life. Hitherto, on this

[053-052]subject, Mrs. Grantly had said little or nothing. She would have been

[053-053]well pleased that her daughter should have received the incense of

[053-054]Lord Lufton’s vows—or, perhaps, as well pleased had it been the

[053-055]incense of Lord Dumbello’s vows—without any interference on her

[053-056]part. In such case her child, she knew, would have told her with

[053-057]quite sufficient eagerness, and the matter in either case would have

[053-058]been arranged as a very pretty love match. She had no fear of any

[053-059]impropriety or of any rashness on Griselda’s part. She had thoroughly

[053-060]known her daughter when she boasted that Griselda would never indulge

[053-061]in an unauthorized passion. But as matters now stood, with those two

[053-062]strings to her bow, and with that Lufton-Grantly alliance treaty

[053-063]in existence—of which she, Griselda herself, knew nothing—might

[053-064]it not be possible that the poor child should stumble through want

[053-065]of adequate direction? Guided by these thoughts, Mrs. Grantly had

[053-066]resolved to say a few words before she left London. So she wrote

[053-067]a line to her daughter, and Griselda reached Mount Street at two

[053-068]o’clock in Lady Lufton’s carriage, which, during the interview,

[053-069]waited for her at the beer-shop round the corner.

[053-070]“And papa won’t be Bishop of Westminster?” said the young lady, when

[053-071]the doings of the giants had been sufficiently explained to make her

[053-072]understand that all those hopes were over.

[053-073]“No, my dear; at any rate not now.”

[053-074]“What a shame! I thought it was all settled. What’s the good, mamma,

[053-075]of Lord De Terrier being prime minister, if he can’t make whom he

[053-076]likes a bishop?”

[053-077]“I don’t think that Lord De Terrier has behaved at all well to your

[053-078]father. However, that’s a long question, and we can’t go into it

[053-079]now.”

[053-080]“How glad those Proudies will be!”

[053-081]Griselda would have talked by the hour on this subject had her mother

[053-082]allowed her, but it was necessary that Mrs. Grantly should go to

[053-083]other matters. She began about Lady Lufton, saying what a dear woman

[053-084]her ladyship was; and then went on to say that Griselda was to remain

[053-085]in London as long as it suited her friend and hostess to stay there

[053-086]with her; but added, that this might probably not be very long, as it

[053-087]was notorious that Lady Lufton, when in London, was always in a hurry

[053-088]to get back to Framley.

[053-089]“But I don’t think she is in such a hurry this year, mamma,” said

[053-090]Griselda, who in the month of May preferred Bruton Street to

[053-091]Plumstead, and had no objection whatever to the coronet on the panels

[053-092]of Lady Lufton’s coach.

[053-093]And then Mrs. Grantly commenced her explanation—very cautiously.

[053-094]“No, my dear, I daresay she is not in such a hurry this year,—that

[053-095]is, as long as you remain with her.”

[053-096]“I am sure she is very kind.”

[053-097]“She is very kind, and you ought to love her very much. I know I do.

[053-098]I have no friend in the world for whom I have a greater regard than

[053-099]for Lady Lufton. It is that which makes me so happy to leave you with

[053-100]her.”

[053-101]“All the same, I wish that you and papa had remained up; that is, if

[053-102]they had made papa a bishop.”

[053-103]“It’s no good thinking of that now, my dear. What I particularly

[053-104]wanted to say to you was this: I think you should know what are the

[053-105]ideas which Lady Lufton entertains.”

[053-106]“Her ideas!” said Griselda, who had never troubled herself much in

[053-107]thinking about other people’s thoughts.

[053-108]“Yes, Griselda. While you were staying down at Framley Court, and

[053-109]also, I suppose, since you have been up here in Bruton Street, you

[053-110]must have seen a good deal of—Lord Lufton.”

[053-111]“He doesn’t come very often to Bruton Street,—that is to say, not

[053-112]_very_ often.”

[053-113]“H-m,” ejaculated Mrs. Grantly, very gently. She would willingly have

[053-114]repressed the sound altogether, but it had been too much for her. If

[053-115]she found reason to think that Lady Lufton was playing her false, she

[053-116]would immediately take her daughter away, break up the treaty, and

[053-117]prepare for the Hartletop alliance. Such were the thoughts that ran

[053-118]through her mind. But she knew all the while that Lady Lufton was not

[053-119]false. The fault was not with Lady Lufton; nor, perhaps, altogether

[053-120]with Lord Lufton. Mrs. Grantly had understood the full force of the

[053-121]complaint which Lady Lufton had made against her daughter; and though

[053-122]she had of course defended her child, and on the whole had defended

[053-123]her successfully, yet she confessed to herself that Griselda’s chance

[053-124]of a first-rate establishment would be better if she were a little

[053-125]more impulsive. A man does not wish to marry a statue, let the

[053-126]statue be ever so statuesque. She could not teach her daughter to be

[053-127]impulsive, any more than she could teach her to be six feet high; but

[053-128]might it not be possible to teach her to seem so? The task was a very

[053-129]delicate one, even for a mother’s hand.

[053-130]“Of course he cannot be at home now as much as he was down in the

[053-131]country, when he was living in the same house,” said Mrs. Grantly,

[053-132]whose business it was to take Lord Lufton’s part at the present

[053-133]moment. “He must be at his club, and at the House of Lords, and in

[053-134]twenty places.”

[053-135]“He is very fond of going to parties, and he dances beautifully.”

[053-136]“I am sure he does. I have seen as much as that myself, and I think

[053-137]I know some one with whom he likes to dance.” And the mother gave her

[053-138]daughter a loving little squeeze.

[053-139]“Do you mean me, mamma?”

[053-140]“Yes, I do mean you, my dear. And is it not true? Lady Lufton says

[053-141]that he likes dancing with you better than with any one else in

[053-142]London.”

[053-143]“I don’t know,” said Griselda, looking down upon the ground.

[053-144]Mrs. Grantly thought that this upon the whole was rather a good

[053-145]opening. It might have been better. Some point of interest more

[053-146]serious in its nature than that of a waltz might have been found on

[053-147]which to connect her daughter’s sympathies with those of her future

[053-148]husband. But any point of interest was better than none; and it is so

[053-149]difficult to find points of interest in persons who by their nature

[053-150]are not impulsive.

[053-151]“Lady Lufton says so, at any rate,” continued Mrs. Grantly, ever so

[053-152]cautiously. “She thinks that Lord Lufton likes no partner better.

[053-153]What do you think yourself, Griselda?”

[053-154]“I don’t know, mamma.”

[053-155]“But young ladies must think of such things, must they not?”

[053-156]“Must they, mamma?”

[053-157]“I suppose they do, don’t they? The truth is, Griselda, that Lady

[053-158]Lufton thinks that if— Can you guess what it is she thinks?”

[053-159]“No, mamma.” But that was a fib on Griselda’s part.

[053-160]“She thinks that my Griselda would make the best possible wife in the

[053-161]world for her son; and I think so too. I think that her son will be

[053-162]a very fortunate man if he can get such a wife. And now what do you

[053-163]think, Griselda?”

[053-164]“I don’t think anything, mamma.”

[053-165]But that would not do. It was absolutely necessary that she should

[053-166]think, and absolutely necessary that her mother should tell her so.

[053-167]Such a degree of unimpulsiveness as this would lead to—heaven knows

[053-168]what results! Lufton-Grantly treaties and Hartletop interests would

[053-169]be all thrown away upon a young lady who would not think anything of

[053-170]a noble suitor sighing for her smiles. Besides, it was not natural.

[053-171]Griselda, as her mother knew, had never been a girl of headlong

[053-172]feeling; but still she had had her likes and her dislikes. In that

[053-173]matter of the bishopric she was keen enough; and no one could evince

[053-174]a deeper interest in the subject of a well-made new dress than

[053-175]Griselda Grantly. It was not possible that she should be indifferent

[053-176]as to her future prospects, and she must know that those prospects

[053-177]depended mainly on her marriage. Her mother was almost angry with

[053-178]her, but nevertheless she went on very gently:

[053-179]“You don’t think anything! But, my darling, you must think. You must

[053-180]make up your mind what would be your answer if Lord Lufton were to

[053-181]propose to you. That is what Lady Lufton wishes him to do.”

[053-182]“But he never will, mamma.”

[053-183]“And if he did?”

[053-184]“But I’m sure he never will. He doesn’t think of such a thing at

[053-185]all—and—and—”

[053-186]“And what, my dear?”

[053-187]“I don’t know, mamma.”

[053-188]“Surely you can speak out to me, dearest! All I care about is your

[053-189]happiness. Both Lady Lufton and I think that it would be a happy

[053-190]marriage if you both cared for each other enough. She thinks that

[053-191]he is fond of you. But if he were ten times Lord Lufton I would not

[053-192]tease you about it if I thought that you could not learn to care

[053-193]about him. What was it you were going to say, my dear?”
