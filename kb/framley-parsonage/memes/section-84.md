## Section 84

[084-001]At this period of the family glory the archdeacon himself was kept a

[084-002]little in abeyance, and was hardly allowed free intercourse with his

[084-003]own magnificent child. Indeed, to give him his due, it must be said

[084-004]of him that he would not consent to walk in the triumphal procession

[084-005]which moved with stately step, to and fro, through the Barchester

[084-006]regions. He kissed his daughter and blessed her, and bade her love

[084-007]her husband and be a good wife; but such injunctions as these,

[084-008]seeing how splendidly she had done her duty in securing to herself a

[084-009]marquis, seemed out of place and almost vulgar. Girls about to marry

[084-010]curates or sucking barristers should be told to do their duty in that

[084-011]station of life to which God might be calling them; but it seemed to

[084-012]be almost an impertinence in a father to give such an injunction to a

[084-013]future marchioness.

[084-014]“I do not think that you have any ground for fear on her behalf,”

[084-015]said Mrs. Grantly, “seeing in what way she has hitherto conducted

[084-016]herself.”

[084-017]“She has been a good girl,” said the archdeacon, “but she is about to

[084-018]be placed in a position of great temptation.”

[084-019]“She has a strength of mind suited for any position,” replied Mrs.

[084-020]Grantly, vain-gloriously.

[084-021]But nevertheless even the archdeacon moved about through the close

[084-022]at Barchester with a somewhat prouder step since the tidings of this

[084-023]alliance had become known there. The time had been—in the latter

[084-024]days of his father’s lifetime—when he was the greatest man of the

[084-025]close. The dean had been old and infirm, and Dr. Grantly had wielded

[084-026]the bishop’s authority. But since that things had altered. A new

[084-027]bishop had come there, absolutely hostile to him. A new dean had

[084-028]also come, who was not only his friend, but the brother-in-law of

[084-029]his wife; but even this advent had lessened the authority of the

[084-030]archdeacon. The vicars choral did not hang upon his words as they

[084-031]had been wont to do, and the minor canons smiled in return to

[084-032]his smile less obsequiously when they met him in the clerical

[084-033]circles of Barchester. But now it seemed that his old supremacy was

[084-034]restored to him. In the minds of many men an archdeacon, who was the

[084-035]father-in-law of a marquis, was himself as good as any bishop. He did

[084-036]not say much of his new connection to others beside the dean, but he

[084-037]was conscious of the fact, and conscious also of the reflected glory

[084-038]which shone around his own head.

[084-039]But as regards Mrs. Grantly it may be said that she moved in an

[084-040]unending procession of stately ovation. It must not be supposed that

[084-041]she continually talked to her friends and neighbours of Lord Dumbello

[084-042]and the marchioness. She was by far too wise for such folly as that.

[084-043]The coming alliance having been once announced, the name of Hartletop

[084-044]was hardly mentioned by her out of her own domestic circle. But she

[084-045]assumed, with an ease that was surprising even to herself, the airs

[084-046]and graces of a mighty woman. She went through her work of morning

[084-047]calls as though it were her business to be affable to the country

[084-048]gentry. She astonished her sister, the dean’s wife, by the simplicity

[084-049]of her grandeur; and condescended to Mrs. Proudie in a manner which

[084-050]nearly broke that lady’s heart. “I shall be even with her yet,” said

[084-051]Mrs. Proudie to herself, who had contrived to learn various very

[084-052]deleterious circumstances respecting the Hartletop family since the

[084-053]news about Lord Dumbello and Griselda had become known to her.

[084-054]Griselda herself was carried about in the procession, taking but

[084-055]little part in it of her own, like an Eastern god. She suffered her

[084-056]mother’s caresses and smiled in her mother’s face as she listened to

[084-057]her own praises, but her triumph was apparently within. To no one did

[084-058]she say much on the subject, and greatly disgusted the old family

[084-059]housekeeper by declining altogether to discuss the future Dumbello

[084-060]_ménage_. To her aunt, Mrs. Arabin, who strove hard to lead her

[084-061]into some open-hearted speech as to her future aspirations, she was

[084-062]perfectly impassive. “Oh, yes, aunt, of course,” and “I’ll think

[084-063]about it, aunt Eleanor,” or “Of course I shall do that if Lord

[084-064]Dumbello wishes it.” Nothing beyond this could be got from her; and

[084-065]so, after half-a-dozen ineffectual attempts, Mrs. Arabin abandoned

[084-066]the matter.

[084-067]But then there arose the subject of clothes—of the wedding

[084-068]_trousseau_! Sarcastic people are wont to say that the tailor makes

[084-069]the man. Were I such a one, I might certainly assert that the

[084-070]milliner makes the bride. As regarding her bridehood, in distinction

[084-071]either to her girlhood or her wifehood—as being a line of plain

[084-072]demarcation between those two periods of a woman’s life—the milliner

[084-073]does do much to make her. She would be hardly a bride if the

[084-074]_trousseau_ were not there. A girl married without some such

[084-075]appendage would seem to pass into the condition of a wife without any

[084-076]such line of demarcation. In that moment in which she finds herself

[084-077]in the first fruition of her marriage finery she becomes a bride; and

[084-078]in that other moment, when she begins to act upon the finest of these

[084-079]things as clothes to be packed up, she becomes a wife.

[084-080]When this subject was discussed Griselda displayed no lack of a

[084-081]becoming interest. She went to work steadily, slowly, and almost with

[084-082]solemnity, as though the business in hand were one which it would be

[084-083]wicked to treat with impatience. She even struck her mother with awe

[084-084]by the grandeur of her ideas and the depth of her theories. Nor let

[084-085]it be supposed that she rushed away at once to the consideration of

[084-086]the great fabric which was to be the ultimate sign and mark of her

[084-087]status, the quintessence of her briding, the outer veil, as it were,

[084-088]of the tabernacle—namely, her wedding-dress. As a great poet works

[084-089]himself up by degrees to that inspiration which is necessary for

[084-090]the grand turning point of his epic, so did she slowly approach the

[084-091]hallowed ground on which she would sit, with her ministers around

[084-092]her, when about to discuss the nature, the extent, the design, the

[084-093]colouring, the structure, and the ornamentation of that momentous

[084-094]piece of apparel. No; there was much indeed to be done before she

[084-095]came to this; and as the poet, to whom I have already alluded, first

[084-096]invokes his muse, and then brings his smaller events gradually out

[084-097]upon his stage, so did Miss Grantly with sacred fervour ask her

[084-098]mother’s aid, and then prepare her list of all those articles

[084-099]of under-clothing which must be the substratum for the visible

[084-100]magnificence of her _trousseau_.

[084-101]Money was no object. We all know what that means; and frequently

[084-102]understand, when the words are used, that a blaze of splendour is to

[084-103]be attained at the cheapest possible price. But, in this instance,

[084-104]money was no object;—such an amount of money, at least, as could by

[084-105]any possibility be spent on a lady’s clothes, independently of her

[084-106]jewels. With reference to diamonds and such like, the archdeacon

[084-107]at once declared his intention of taking the matter into his own

[084-108]hands—except in so far as Lord Dumbello, or the Hartletop interest,

[084-109]might be pleased to participate in the selection. Nor was Mrs.

[084-110]Grantly sorry for such a decision. She was not an imprudent woman,

[084-111]and would have dreaded the responsibility of trusting herself on

[084-112]such an occasion among the dangerous temptations of a jeweller’s

[084-113]shop. But as far as silks and satins went—in the matter of

[084-114]French bonnets, muslins, velvets, hats, riding-habits, artificial

[084-115]flowers, head-gilding, curious nettings, enamelled buckles, golden

[084-116]tagged bobbins, and mechanical petticoats—as regarded shoes, and

[084-117]gloves, and corsets, and stockings, and linen, and flannel, and

[084-118]calico—money, I may conscientiously assert, was no object. And,

[084-119]under these circumstances, Griselda Grantly went to work with a

[084-120]solemn industry and a steady perseverance that was beyond all praise.

[084-121]“I hope she will be happy,” Mrs. Arabin said to her sister, as the

[084-122]two were sitting together in the dean’s drawing-room.

[084-123]“Oh, yes; I think she will. Why should she not?” said the mother.

[084-124]“Oh, no; I know of no reason. But she is going up into a station so

[084-125]much above her own in the eyes of the world that one cannot but feel

[084-126]anxious for her.”

[084-127]“I should feel much more anxious if she were going to marry a poor

[084-128]man,” said Mrs. Grantly. “It has always seemed to me that Griselda

[084-129]was fitted for a high position; that nature intended her for rank and

[084-130]state. You see that she is not a bit elated. She takes it all as if

[084-131]it were her own by right. I do not think that there is any danger

[084-132]that her head will be turned, if you mean that.”

[084-133]“I was thinking rather of her heart,” said Mrs. Arabin.

[084-134]“She never would have taken Lord Dumbello without loving him,” said

[084-135]Mrs. Grantly, speaking rather quickly.

[084-136]“That is not quite what I mean either, Susan. I am sure she would not

[084-137]have accepted him had she not loved him. But it is so hard to keep

[084-138]the heart fresh among all the grandeurs of high rank; and it is

[084-139]harder for a girl to do so who has not been born to it, than for one

[084-140]who has enjoyed it as her birthright.”

[084-141]“I don’t quite understand about fresh hearts,” said Mrs. Grantly,

[084-142]pettishly. “If she does her duty, and loves her husband, and fills

[084-143]the position in which God has placed her with propriety, I don’t know

[084-144]that we need look for anything more. I don’t at all approve of the

[084-145]plan of frightening a young girl when she is making her first outset

[084-146]into the world.”

[084-147]“No; I would not frighten her. I think it would be almost difficult

[084-148]to frighten Griselda.”

[084-149]“I hope it would. The great matter with a girl is whether she has

[084-150]been brought up with proper notions as to a woman’s duty. Of course

[084-151]it is not for me to boast on this subject. Such as she is, I, of

[084-152]course, am responsible. But I must own that I do not see occasion to

[084-153]wish for any change.” And then the subject was allowed to drop.

[084-154]Among those of her relations who wondered much at the girl’s fortune,

[084-155]but allowed themselves to say but little, was her grandfather, Mr.

[084-156]Harding. He was an old clergyman, plain and simple in his manners,

[084-157]and not occupying a very prominent position, seeing that he was only

[084-158]precentor to the chapter. He was loved by his daughter, Mrs. Grantly,

[084-159]and was treated by the archdeacon, if not invariably with the highest

[084-160]respect, at least always with consideration and regard. But, old

[084-161]and plain as he was, the young people at Plumstead did not hold him

[084-162]in any great reverence. He was poorer than their other relatives,

[084-163]and made no attempt to hold his head high in Barsetshire circles.

[084-164]Moreover, in these latter days, the home of his heart had been at the

[084-165]deanery. He had, indeed, a lodging of his own in the city, but was

[084-166]gradually allowing himself to be weaned away from it. He had his own

[084-167]bedroom in the dean’s house, his own arm-chair in the dean’s library,

[084-168]and his own corner on a sofa in Mrs. Dean’s drawing-room. It was

[084-169]not, therefore, necessary that he should interfere greatly in this

[084-170]coming marriage; but still it became his duty to say a word of

[084-171]congratulation to his granddaughter,—and perhaps to say a word of

[084-172]advice.

[084-173]“Grizzy, my dear,” he said to her—he always called her Grizzy, but

[084-174]the endearment of the appellation had never been appreciated by the

[084-175]young lady—“come and kiss me, and let me congratulate you on your

[084-176]great promotion. I do so very heartily.”

[084-177]“Thank you, grandpapa,” she said, touching his forehead with her

[084-178]lips, thus being, as it were, very sparing with her kiss. But those

[084-179]lips now were august and reserved for nobler foreheads than that of

[084-180]an old cathedral hack. For Mr. Harding still chanted the Litany from

[084-181]Sunday to Sunday, unceasingly, standing at that well-known desk in

[084-182]the cathedral choir; and Griselda had a thought in her mind that

[084-183]when the Hartletop people should hear of the practice they would not

[084-184]be delighted. Dean and archdeacon might be very well, and if her

[084-185]grandfather had even been a prebendary, she might have put up with

[084-186]him; but he had, she thought, almost disgraced his family in being,

[084-187]at his age, one of the working menial clergy of the cathedral. She

[084-188]kissed him, therefore, sparingly, and resolved that her words with

[084-189]him should be few.
