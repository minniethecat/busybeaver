## Section 24

[024-001]Lady Lufton had hitherto been fortunate in her matrimonial

[024-002]speculations. She had selected Sir George for her daughter, and Sir

[024-003]George, with the utmost good nature, had fallen in with her views.

[024-004]She had selected Fanny Monsell for Mr. Robarts, and Fanny Monsell

[024-005]had not rebelled against her for a moment. There was a prestige of

[024-006]success about her doings, and she felt almost confident that her dear

[024-007]son Ludovic must fall in love with Griselda.

[024-008]As to the lady herself, nothing, Lady Lufton thought, could be much

[024-009]better than such a match for her son. Lady Lufton, I have said, was a

[024-010]good churchwoman, and the archdeacon was the very type of that branch

[024-011]of the Church which she venerated. The Grantlys, too, were of a good

[024-012]family,—not noble, indeed; but in such matters Lady Lufton did not

[024-013]want everything. She was one of those persons who, in placing their

[024-014]hopes at a moderate pitch, may fairly trust to see them realized. She

[024-015]would fain that her son’s wife should be handsome; this she wished

[024-016]for his sake, that he might be proud of his wife, and because men

[024-017]love to look on beauty. But she was afraid of vivacious beauty,

[024-018]of those soft, sparkling feminine charms which are spread out as

[024-019]lures for all the world, soft dimples, laughing eyes, luscious lips,

[024-020]conscious smiles, and easy whispers. What if her son should bring her

[024-021]home a rattling, rapid-spoken, painted piece of Eve’s flesh such as

[024-022]this? Would not the glory and joy of her life be over, even though

[024-023]such child of their first mother should have come forth to the

[024-024]present day ennobled by the blood of two dozen successive British

[024-025]peers?

[024-026]And then, too, Griselda’s money would not be useless. Lady Lufton,

[024-027]with all her high-flown ideas, was not an imprudent woman. She knew

[024-028]that her son had been extravagant, though she did not believe that he

[024-029]had been reckless; and she was well content to think that some balsam

[024-030]from the old bishop’s coffers should be made to cure the slight

[024-031]wounds which his early imprudence might have inflicted on the carcase

[024-032]of the family property. And thus, in this way, and for these reasons,

[024-033]Griselda Grantly had been chosen out from all the world to be the

[024-034]future Lady Lufton.

[024-035]Lord Lufton had met Griselda more than once already; had met

[024-036]her before these high contracting parties had come to any terms

[024-037]whatsoever, and had evidently admired her. Lord Dumbello had remained

[024-038]silent one whole evening in London with ineffable disgust, because

[024-039]Lord Lufton had been rather particular in his attentions; but then

[024-040]Lord Dumbello’s muteness was his most eloquent mode of expression.

[024-041]Both Lady Hartletop and Mrs. Grantly, when they saw him, knew very

[024-042]well what he meant. But that match would not exactly have suited Mrs.

[024-043]Grantly’s views. The Hartletop people were not in her line. They

[024-044]belonged altogether to another set, being connected, as we have heard

[024-045]before, with the Omnium interest—“those _horrid_ Gatherum people,”

[024-046]as Lady Lufton would say to her, raising her hands and eyebrows, and

[024-047]shaking her head. Lady Lufton probably thought that they ate babies

[024-048]in pies during their midnight orgies at Gatherum Castle; and that

[024-049]widows were kept in cells, and occasionally put on racks for the

[024-050]amusement of the duke’s guests.

[024-051]When the Robarts’s party entered the drawing-room the Grantlys were

[024-052]already there, and the archdeacon’s voice sounded loud and imposing

[024-053]in Lucy’s ears, as she heard him speaking while she was yet on the

[024-054]threshold of the door.

[024-055]“My dear Lady Lufton, I would believe anything on earth about

[024-056]her—anything. There is nothing too outrageous for her. Had she

[024-057]insisted on going there with the bishop’s apron on, I should not

[024-058]have been surprised.” And then they all knew that the archdeacon was

[024-059]talking about Mrs. Proudie, for Mrs. Proudie was his bugbear.

[024-060]Lady Lufton after receiving her guests introduced Lucy to Griselda

[024-061]Grantly. Miss Grantly smiled graciously, bowed slightly, and then

[024-062]remarked in the lowest voice possible that it was exceedingly cold.

[024-063]A low voice, we know, is an excellent thing in woman.

[024-064]Lucy, who thought that she was bound to speak, said that it was cold,

[024-065]but that she did not mind it when she was walking. And then Griselda

[024-066]smiled again, somewhat less graciously than before, and so the

[024-067]conversation ended. Miss Grantly was the elder of the two, and having

[024-068]seen most of the world, should have been the best able to talk,

[024-069]but perhaps she was not very anxious for a conversation with Miss

[024-070]Robarts.

[024-071]“So, Robarts, I hear that you have been preaching at Chaldicotes,”

[024-072]said the archdeacon, still rather loudly. “I saw Sowerby the other

[024-073]day, and he told me that you gave them the fag end of Mrs. Proudie’s

[024-074]lecture.”

[024-075]“It was ill-natured of Sowerby to say the fag end,” said Robarts. “We

[024-076]divided the matter into thirds. Harold Smith took the first part, I

[024-077]the last—”

[024-078]“And the lady the intervening portion. You have electrified the

[024-079]county between you; but I am told that she had the best of it.”

[024-080]“I was so sorry that Mr. Robarts went there,” said Lady Lufton, as

[024-081]she walked into the dining-room leaning on the archdeacon’s arm.

[024-082]“I am inclined to think he could not very well have helped himself,”

[024-083]said the archdeacon, who was never willing to lean heavily on a

[024-084]brother parson, unless on one who had utterly and irrevocably gone

[024-085]away from his side of the Church.

[024-086]“Do you think not, archdeacon?”

[024-087]“Why, no: Sowerby is a friend of Lufton’s—”

[024-088]“Not particularly,” said poor Lady Lufton, in a deprecating tone.

[024-089]“Well, they have been intimate; and Robarts, when he was asked to

[024-090]preach at Chaldicotes, could not well refuse.”

[024-091]“But then he went afterwards to Gatherum Castle. Not that I am vexed

[024-092]with him at all now, you understand. But it is such a dangerous

[024-093]house, you know.”

[024-094]“So it is.—But the very fact of the duke’s wishing to have a

[024-095]clergyman there, should always be taken as a sign of grace, Lady

[024-096]Lufton. The air was impure, no doubt; but it was less impure with

[024-097]Robarts there than it would have been without him. But, gracious

[024-098]heavens! what blasphemy have I been saying about impure air? Why, the

[024-099]bishop was there!”

[024-100]“Yes, the bishop was there,” said Lady Lufton, and they both

[024-101]understood each other thoroughly.

[024-102]Lord Lufton took out Mrs. Grantly to dinner, and matters were so

[024-103]managed that Miss Grantly sat on his other side. There was no

[024-104]management apparent in this to anybody; but there she was, while

[024-105]Lucy was placed between her brother and Captain Culpepper. Captain

[024-106]Culpepper was a man with an enormous moustache, and a great aptitude

[024-107]for slaughtering game; but as he had no other strong characteristics,

[024-108]it was not probable that he would make himself very agreeable to poor

[024-109]Lucy.

[024-110]She had seen Lord Lufton once, for two minutes, since the day of that

[024-111]walk, and then he had addressed her quite like an old friend. It had

[024-112]been in the parsonage drawing-room, and Fanny had been there. Fanny

[024-113]now was so well accustomed to his lordship, that she thought but

[024-114]little of this, but to Lucy it had been very pleasant. He was not

[024-115]forward or familiar, but kind, and gentle, and pleasant; and Lucy did

[024-116]feel that she liked him.

[024-117]Now, on this evening, he had hitherto hardly spoken to her; but then

[024-118]she knew that there were other people in the company to whom he was

[024-119]bound to speak. She was not exactly humble-minded in the usual sense

[024-120]of the word; but she did recognize the fact that her position was

[024-121]less important than that of other people there, and that therefore

[024-122]it was probable that to a certain extent she would be overlooked.

[024-123]But not the less would she have liked to occupy the seat to which

[024-124]Miss Grantly had found her way. She did not want to flirt with Lord

[024-125]Lufton; she was not such a fool as that; but she would have liked to

[024-126]have heard the sound of his voice close to her ear, instead of that

[024-127]of Captain Culpepper’s knife and fork.

[024-128]This was the first occasion on which she had endeavoured to dress

[024-129]herself with care since her father had died; and now, sombre though

[024-130]she was in her deep mourning, she did look very well.

[024-131]“There is an expression about her forehead that is full of poetry,”

[024-132]Fanny had said to her husband.

[024-133]“Don’t you turn her head, Fanny, and make her believe that she is a

[024-134]beauty,” Mark had answered.

[024-135]“I doubt it is not so easy to turn her head, Mark. There is more in

[024-136]Lucy than you imagine, and so you will find out before long.” It was

[024-137]thus that Mrs. Robarts prophesied about her sister-in-law. Had she

[024-138]been asked she might perhaps have said that Lucy’s presence would be

[024-139]dangerous to the Grantly interest at Framley Court.

[024-140]Lord Lufton’s voice was audible enough as he went on talking to Miss

[024-141]Grantly—his voice, but not his words. He talked in such a way that

[024-142]there was no appearance of whispering, and yet the person to whom he

[024-143]spoke, and she only, could hear what he said. Mrs. Grantly the while

[024-144]conversed constantly with Lucy’s brother, who sat at Lucy’s left

[024-145]hand. She never lacked for subjects on which to speak to a country

[024-146]clergyman of the right sort, and thus Griselda was left quite

[024-147]uninterrupted.

[024-148]But Lucy could not but observe that Griselda herself seemed to have

[024-149]very little to say,—or at any rate to say very little. Every now and

[024-150]then she did open her mouth, and some word or brace of words would

[024-151]fall from it. But for the most part she seemed to be content in

[024-152]the fact that Lord Lufton was paying her attention. She showed no

[024-153]animation, but sat there still and graceful, composed and classical,

[024-154]as she always was. Lucy, who could not keep her ears from listening

[024-155]or her eyes from looking, thought that had she been there she would

[024-156]have endeavoured to take a more prominent part in the conversation.

[024-157]But then Griselda Grantly probably knew much better than Lucy did

[024-158]how to comport herself in such a situation. Perhaps it might be that

[024-159]young men, such as Lord Lufton, liked to hear the sound of their own

[024-160]voices.

[024-161]“Immense deal of game about here,” Captain Culpepper said to her

[024-162]towards the end of the dinner. It was the second attempt he had made;

[024-163]on the former he had asked her whether she knew any of the fellows of

[024-164]the 9th.

[024-165]“Is there?” said Lucy. “Oh! I saw Lord Lufton the other day with a

[024-166]great armful of pheasants.”

[024-167]“An armful! Why we had seven cartloads the other day at Gatherum.”

[024-168]“Seven carts full of pheasants!” said Lucy, amazed.

[024-169]“That’s not so much. We had eight guns, you know. Eight guns will do

[024-170]a deal of work when the game has been well got together. They manage

[024-171]all that capitally at Gatherum. Been at the duke’s, eh?”

[024-172]Lucy had heard the Framley report as to Gatherum Castle, and said

[024-173]with a sort of shudder that she had never been at that place. After

[024-174]this, Captain Culpepper troubled her no further.

[024-175]When the ladies had taken themselves to the drawing-room Lucy found

[024-176]herself hardly better off than she had been at the dinner-table. Lady

[024-177]Lufton and Mrs. Grantly got themselves on to a sofa together, and

[024-178]there chatted confidentially into each other’s ears. Her ladyship

[024-179]had introduced Lucy and Miss Grantly, and then she naturally thought

[024-180]that the young people might do very well together. Mrs. Robarts did

[024-181]attempt to bring about a joint conversation, which should include the

[024-182]three, and for ten minutes or so she worked hard at it. But it did

[024-183]not thrive. Miss Grantly was monosyllabic, smiling, however, at every

[024-184]monosyllable; and Lucy found that nothing would occur to her at that

[024-185]moment worthy of being spoken. There she sat, still and motionless,

[024-186]afraid to take up a book, and thinking in her heart how much happier

[024-187]she would have been at home at the parsonage. She was not made for

[024-188]society; she felt sure of that; and another time she would let Mark

[024-189]and Fanny come to Framley Court by themselves.
