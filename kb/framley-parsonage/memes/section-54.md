## Section 54

[054-001]“Lord Lufton thinks a great deal more of Lucy Robarts than he does

[054-002]of—of—of any one else, I believe,” said Griselda, showing now some

[054-003]little animation by her manner, “dumpy little black thing that she

[054-004]is.”

[054-005]“Lucy Robarts!” said Mrs. Grantly, taken by surprise at finding that

[054-006]her daughter was moved by such a passion as jealousy, and feeling

[054-007]also perfectly assured that there could not be any possible ground

[054-008]for jealousy in such a direction as that. “Lucy Robarts, my dear! I

[054-009]don’t suppose Lord Lufton ever thought of speaking to her, except in

[054-010]the way of civility.”

[054-011]“Yes, he did, mamma! Don’t you remember at Framley?”

[054-012]Mrs. Grantly began to look back in her mind, and she thought she

[054-013]did remember having once observed Lord Lufton talking in rather a

[054-014]confidential manner with the parson’s sister. But she was sure that

[054-015]there was nothing in it. If that was the reason why Griselda was so

[054-016]cold to her proposed lover, it would be a thousand pities that it

[054-017]should not be removed.

[054-018]“Now you mention her, I do remember the young lady,” said Mrs.

[054-019]Grantly, “a dark girl, very low, and without much figure. She seemed

[054-020]to me to keep very much in the background.”

[054-021]“I don’t know much about that, mamma.”

[054-022]“As far as I saw her, she did. But, my dear Griselda, you should not

[054-023]allow yourself to think of such a thing. Lord Lufton, of course, is

[054-024]bound to be civil to any young lady in his mother’s house, and I am

[054-025]quite sure that he has no other idea whatever with regard to Miss

[054-026]Robarts. I certainly cannot speak as to her intellect, for I do not

[054-027]think she opened her mouth in my presence; but—”

[054-028]“Oh! she has plenty to say for herself, when she pleases. She’s a sly

[054-029]little thing.”

[054-030]“But, at any rate, my dear, she has no personal attractions whatever,

[054-031]and I do not at all think that Lord Lufton is a man to be taken

[054-032]by—by—by anything that Miss Robarts might do or say.”

[054-033]As those words “personal attractions” were uttered, Griselda managed

[054-034]so to turn her neck as to catch a side view of herself in one of the

[054-035]mirrors on the wall, and then she bridled herself up, and made a

[054-036]little play with her eyes, and looked, as her mother thought, very

[054-037]well. “It is all nothing to me, mamma, of course,” she said.

[054-038]“Well, my dear, perhaps not. I don’t say that it is. I do not wish to

[054-039]put the slightest constraint upon your feelings. If I did not have

[054-040]the most thorough dependence on your good sense and high principles,

[054-041]I should not speak to you in this way. But as I have, I thought it

[054-042]best to tell you that both Lady Lufton and I should be well pleased

[054-043]if we thought that you and Lord Lufton were fond of each other.”

[054-044]“I am sure he never thinks of such a thing, mamma.”

[054-045]“And as for Lucy Robarts, pray get that idea out of your head; if not

[054-046]for your sake, then for his. You should give him credit for better

[054-047]taste.”

[054-048]But it was not so easy to take anything out of Griselda’s head that

[054-049]she had once taken into it. “As for tastes, mamma, there is no

[054-050]accounting for them,” she said; and then the colloquy on that subject

[054-051]was over. The result of it on Mrs. Grantly’s mind was a feeling

[054-052]amounting almost to a conviction in favour of the Dumbello interest.

[054-053]CHAPTER XXVI.

[054-054]IMPULSIVE.

[054-055]I trust my readers will all remember how Puck the pony was beaten

[054-056]during that drive to Hogglestock. It may be presumed that Puck

[054-057]himself on that occasion did not suffer much. His skin was not so

[054-058]soft as Mrs. Robarts’s heart. The little beast was full of oats and

[054-059]all the good things of this world, and therefore, when the whip

[054-060]touched him, he would dance about and shake his little ears, and run

[054-061]on at a tremendous pace for twenty yards, making his mistress think

[054-062]that he had endured terrible things. But, in truth, during those

[054-063]whippings Puck was not the chief sufferer.

[054-064]Lucy had been forced to declare—forced by the strength of her own

[054-065]feelings, and by the impossibility of assenting to the propriety of a

[054-066]marriage between Lord Lufton and Miss Grantly—, she had been forced

[054-067]to declare that she did care about Lord Lufton as much as though

[054-068]he were her brother. She had said all this to herself,—nay, much

[054-069]more than this—very often. But now she had said it out loud to her

[054-070]sister-in-law; and she knew that what she had said was remembered,

[054-071]considered, and had, to a certain extent, become the cause of

[054-072]altered conduct. Fanny alluded very seldom to the Luftons in casual

[054-073]conversation, and never spoke about Lord Lufton, unless when her

[054-074]husband made it impossible that she should not speak of him. Lucy had

[054-075]attempted on more than one occasion to remedy this, by talking about

[054-076]the young lord in a laughing and, perhaps, half-jeering way; she

[054-077]had been sarcastic as to his hunting and shooting, and had boldly

[054-078]attempted to say a word in joke about his love for Griselda. But she

[054-079]felt that she had failed; that she had failed altogether as regarded

[054-080]Fanny; and that as to her brother, she would more probably be the

[054-081]means of opening his eyes, than have any effect in keeping them

[054-082]closed. So she gave up her efforts and spoke no further word about

[054-083]Lord Lufton. Her secret had been told, and she knew that it had been

[054-084]told.

[054-085]At this time the two ladies were left a great deal alone together in

[054-086]the drawing-room at the parsonage; more, perhaps, than had ever yet

[054-087]been the case since Lucy had been there. Lady Lufton was away, and

[054-088]therefore the almost daily visit to Framley Court was not made; and

[054-089]Mark in these days was a great deal at Barchester, having, no doubt,

[054-090]very onerous duties to perform before he could be admitted as one of

[054-091]that chapter. He went into, what he was pleased to call residence,

[054-092]almost at once. That is, he took his month of preaching, aiding also

[054-093]in some slight and very dignified way, in the general Sunday morning

[054-094]services. He did not exactly live at Barchester, because the house

[054-095]was not ready. That at least was the assumed reason. The chattels of

[054-096]Dr. Stanhope, the late prebendary, had not been as yet removed, and

[054-097]there was likely to be some little delay, creditors asserting their

[054-098]right to them. This might have been very inconvenient to a gentleman

[054-099]anxiously expecting the excellent house which the liberality of past

[054-100]ages had provided for his use; but it was not so felt by Mr. Robarts.

[054-101]If Dr. Stanhope’s family or creditors would keep the house for the

[054-102]next twelve months, he would be well pleased. And by this arrangement

[054-103]he was enabled to get through his first month of absence from the

[054-104]church of Framley without any notice from Lady Lufton, seeing that

[054-105]Lady Lufton was in London all the time. This also was convenient,

[054-106]and taught our young prebendary to look on his new preferment more

[054-107]favourably than he had hitherto done.

[054-108]Fanny and Lucy were thus left much alone: and as out of the full

[054-109]head the mouth speaks, so is the full heart more prone to speak at

[054-110]such periods of confidence as these. Lucy, when she first thought

[054-111]of her own state, determined to endow herself with a powerful gift

[054-112]of reticence. She would never tell her love, certainly; but neither

[054-113]would she let concealment feed on her damask cheek, nor would she

[054-114]ever be found for a moment sitting like Patience on a monument. She

[054-115]would fight her own fight bravely within her own bosom, and conquer

[054-116]her enemy altogether. She would either preach, or starve, or weary

[054-117]her love into subjection, and no one should be a bit the wiser. She

[054-118]would teach herself to shake hands with Lord Lufton without a quiver,

[054-119]and would be prepared to like his wife amazingly—unless indeed that

[054-120]wife should be Griselda Grantly. Such were her resolutions; but at

[054-121]the end of the first week they were broken into shivers and scattered

[054-122]to the winds.

[054-123]They had been sitting in the house together the whole of one wet day;

[054-124]and as Mark was to dine in Barchester with the dean, they had had

[054-125]dinner early, eating with the children almost in their laps. It is

[054-126]so that ladies do, when their husbands leave them to themselves. It

[054-127]was getting dusk towards evening, and they were still sitting in the

[054-128]drawing-room, the children now having retired, when Mrs. Robarts for

[054-129]the fifth time since her visit to Hogglestock began to express her

[054-130]wish that she could do some good to the Crawleys,—to Grace Crawley

[054-131]in particular, who, standing up there at her father’s elbow, learning

[054-132]Greek irregular verbs, had appeared to Mrs. Robarts to be an especial

[054-133]object of pity.

[054-134]“I don’t know how to set about it,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[054-135]Now any allusion to that visit to Hogglestock always drove Lucy’s

[054-136]mind back to the consideration of the subject which had most occupied

[054-137]it at the time. She at such moments remembered how she had beaten

[054-138]Puck, and how in her half-bantering but still too serious manner

[054-139]she had apologized for doing so, and had explained the reason. And

[054-140]therefore she could not interest herself about Grace Crawley as

[054-141]vividly as she should have done.

[054-142]“No; one never does,” she said.

[054-143]“I was thinking about it all that day as I drove home,” said Fanny.

[054-144]“The difficulty is this: What can we do with her?”

[054-145]“Exactly,” said Lucy, remembering the very point of the road at which

[054-146]she had declared that she did like Lord Lufton very much.

[054-147]“If we could have her here for a month or so and then send her to

[054-148]school;—but I know Mr. Crawley would not allow us to pay for her

[054-149]schooling.”

[054-150]“I don’t think he would,” said Lucy, with her thoughts far removed

[054-151]from Mr. Crawley and his daughter Grace.

[054-152]“And then we should not know what to do with her; should we?”

[054-153]“No; you would not.”

[054-154]“It would never do to have the poor girl about the house here, with

[054-155]no one to teach her anything. Mark would not teach her Greek verbs,

[054-156]you know.”

[054-157]“I suppose not.”

[054-158]“Lucy, you are not attending to a word I say to you, and I don’t

[054-159]think you have for the last hour. I don’t believe you know what I am

[054-160]talking about.”

[054-161]“Oh, yes, I do—Grace Crawley; I’ll try and teach her if you like,

[054-162]only I don’t know anything myself.”

[054-163]“That’s not what I mean at all, and you know I would not ask you to

[054-164]take such a task as that on yourself. But I do think you might talk

[054-165]it over with me.”

[054-166]“Might I? very well; I will. What is it? Oh, Grace Crawley—you

[054-167]want to know who is to teach her the irregular Greek verbs. Oh dear,

[054-168]Fanny, my head does ache so: pray don’t be angry with me.” And then

[054-169]Lucy, throwing herself back on the sofa, put one hand up painfully to

[054-170]her forehead, and altogether gave up the battle.

[054-171]Mrs. Robarts was by her side in a moment. “Dearest Lucy, what is

[054-172]it makes your head ache so often now? you used not to have those

[054-173]headaches.”

[054-174]“It’s because I’m growing stupid: never mind. We will go on about

[054-175]poor Grace. It would not do to have a governess, would it?”

[054-176]“I can see that you are not well, Lucy,” said Mrs. Robarts, with a

[054-177]look of deep concern. “What is it, dearest? I can see that something

[054-178]is the matter.”

[054-179]“Something the matter! No, there’s not; nothing worth talking of.

[054-180]Sometimes I think I’ll go back to Devonshire and live there. I could

[054-181]stay with Blanche for a time, and then get a lodging in Exeter.”
