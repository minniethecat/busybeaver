# Gentleman

The term **gentleman** in _Framley Parsonage_ operates as both a social classification and a moral evaluation, reflecting the Victorian era's complex negotiation between birth, wealth, education, and conduct as the defining elements of masculine identity. A "gentleman" in Trollope's Barsetshire may be so by birth, by profession, by education, or — most importantly — by behaviour. The word recurs throughout the novel as a marker of inclusion and exclusion, approval and condemnation, shaping the reader's understanding of every male character from [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]] to [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]].

## Definition

In its Victorian clerical context, "gentleman" designated a man whose education, manners, and social position entitled him to move in polite society. The term had both objective criteria — "He was a gentleman" (PN 001-067) could mean he was born to landed gentry or had attended Oxford or Cambridge — and subjective ones: a gentleman behaved with honour, paid his debts, and treated his inferiors with appropriate condescension.

> "He was a gentleman" (PN 001-067)

The clergyman-gentleman represented a particularly important category. The Church of England's social status depended on its clergy being recognized as gentlemen — a status that reforms of the 1830s and 1840s sought to professionalize and secure. "That a man could not be a good parish clergyman" without being a gentleman (PN 001-157) expresses a conviction that spiritual authority required social authority as its foundation.

## In the Novel

Trollope uses "gentleman" as a diagnostic term throughout the novel. Mark Robarts is consistently referred to as a gentleman (PN 004-048), and his gentlemanly status is precisely what is threatened by his financial entanglement with Sowerby. A gentleman who cannot pay his debts ceases, in a crucial sense, to be a gentleman — the social and the moral collapse into each other.

> "He was a gentleman, too, of high breeding and good" character (PN 005-080)

The novel's moral geography is mapped through gentlemanliness. The Chaldicotes set — [[sowerby|Sowerby]], Fothergill, and their circle — are gentlemen by birth but not, the novel suggests, by conduct. Their treatment of Robarts is ungentlemanly because it exploits a social connection for financial gain. The distinction between being a gentleman and merely being "gentlemanlike" (PN 004-038) — the external performance without the internal substance — is one the novel insists upon.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period saw an intense cultural debate about what constituted a gentleman. The old landed definition — a gentleman was a man who lived on inherited property and did not work — was challenged by the rising professional classes (clergy, lawyers, doctors) and the industrial middle class. Thomas Carlyle, John Henry Newman, and Samuel Smiles all contributed to a redefinition that emphasized character, education, and service over mere birth. Newman's _The Idea of a University_ (1852) argued that a liberal education produced "a gentleman" — a claim that elevated the university-trained clergyman above the merely well-born.

Trollope participates in this debate through fiction. His gentleman-clergymen — Mark Robarts, Septimus Harding (in _The Warden_), Francis Arabin (in _Barchester Towers_) — are professionals whose gentlemanly status derives from education and conduct as much as from birth. At the same time, Trollope retains a conservative suspicion of the merely self-made, and his novels consistently affirm the value of inherited social forms.
