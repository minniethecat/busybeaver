## Section 67

[067-001]“And is that answer to be final?” said Mark,—somewhat cruelly, for

[067-002]Lucy had not yet been told that her lover had made any repetition of

[067-003]his proposal. Fanny, however, determined that no injustice should be

[067-004]done, and therefore she at last continued the story.

[067-005]“We know that you did give him an answer, dearest; but gentlemen

[067-006]sometimes will not put up with one answer on such a subject. Lord

[067-007]Lufton has declared to Mark that he means to ask again. He has come

[067-008]down here on purpose to do so.”

[067-009]“And Lady Lufton—” said Lucy, speaking hardly above a whisper, and

[067-010]still hiding her face as she leaned against her sister’s shoulder.

[067-011]“Lord Lufton has not spoken to his mother about it,” said Mark; and

[067-012]it immediately became clear to Lucy, from the tone of her brother’s

[067-013]voice, that he, at least, would not be pleased, should she accept her

[067-014]lover’s vow.

[067-015]“You must decide out of your own heart, dear,” said Fanny,

[067-016]generously. “Mark and I know how well you have behaved, for I have

[067-017]told him everything.” Lucy shuddered and leaned closer against her

[067-018]sister as this was said to her. “I had no alternative, dearest, but

[067-019]to tell him. It was best so; was it not? But nothing has been told

[067-020]to Lord Lufton. Mark would not let him come here to-day, because it

[067-021]would have flurried you, and he wished to give you time to think.

[067-022]But you can see him to-morrow morning,—can you not? and then answer

[067-023]him.”

[067-024]Lucy now stood perfectly silent, feeling that she dearly loved her

[067-025]sister-in-law for her sisterly kindness—for that sisterly wish to

[067-026]promote a sister’s love; but still there was in her mind a strong

[067-027]resolve not to allow Lord Lufton to come there under the idea that

[067-028]he would be received as a favoured lover. Her love was powerful, but

[067-029]so also was her pride; and she could not bring herself to bear the

[067-030]scorn which would lay in Lady Lufton’s eyes. “His mother will despise

[067-031]me, and then he will despise me too,” she said to herself; and with

[067-032]a strong gulp of disappointed love and ambition she determined to

[067-033]persist.

[067-034]“Shall we leave you now, dear; and speak of it again to-morrow

[067-035]morning, before he comes?” said Fanny.

[067-036]“That will be the best,” said Mark. “Turn it in your mind every way

[067-037]to-night. Think of it when you have said your prayers—and, Lucy,

[067-038]come here to me;”—then, taking her in his arms, he kissed her with a

[067-039]tenderness that was not customary with him towards her. “It is fair,”

[067-040]said he, “that I should tell you this: that I have perfect confidence

[067-041]in your judgment and feeling; and that I will stand by you as your

[067-042]brother in whatever decision you may come to. Fanny and I both think

[067-043]that you have behaved excellently, and are both of us sure that you

[067-044]will do what is best. Whatever you do I will stick to you;—and so

[067-045]will Fanny.”

[067-046]“Dearest, dearest Mark!”

[067-047]“And now we will say nothing more about it till to-morrow morning,”

[067-048]said Fanny.

[067-049]But Lucy felt that this saying nothing more about it till to-morrow

[067-050]morning would be tantamount to an acceptance on her part of Lord

[067-051]Lufton’s offer. Mrs. Robarts knew, and Mr. Robarts also now knew, the

[067-052]secret of her heart; and if, such being the case, she allowed Lord

[067-053]Lufton to come there with the acknowledged purpose of pleading his

[067-054]own suit, it would be impossible for her not to yield. If she were

[067-055]resolved that she would not yield, now was the time for her to stand

[067-056]her ground and make her fight.

[067-057]“Do not go, Fanny; at least not quite yet,” she said.

[067-058]“Well, dear?”

[067-059]“I want you to stay while I tell Mark. He must not let Lord Lufton

[067-060]come here to-morrow.”

[067-061]“Not let him!” said Mrs. Robarts.

[067-062]Mr. Robarts said nothing, but he felt that his sister was rising in

[067-063]his esteem from minute to minute.

[067-064]“No; Mark must bid him not come. He will not wish to pain me when it

[067-065]can do no good. Look here, Mark;” and she walked over to her brother,

[067-066]and put both her hands upon his arm. “I do love Lord Lufton. I had no

[067-067]such meaning or thought when I first knew him. But I do love him—I

[067-068]love him dearly;—almost as well as Fanny loves you, I suppose. You

[067-069]may tell him so if you think proper—nay, you must tell him so, or he

[067-070]will not understand me. But tell him this, as coming from me: that I

[067-071]will never marry him, unless his mother asks me.”

[067-072]“She will not do that, I fear,” said Mark, sorrowfully.

[067-073]“No; I suppose not,” said Lucy, now regaining all her courage.

[067-074]“If I thought it probable that she should wish me to be her

[067-075]daughter-in-law, it would not be necessary that I should make such a

[067-076]stipulation. It is because she will not wish it; because she would

[067-077]regard me as unfit to—to—to mate with her son. She would hate me,

[067-078]and scorn me; and then he would begin to scorn me, and perhaps would

[067-079]cease to love me. I could not bear her eye upon me, if she thought

[067-080]that I had injured her son. Mark, you will go to him now; will you

[067-081]not? and explain this to him;—as much of it as is necessary. Tell

[067-082]him, that if his mother asks me I will—consent. But that as I know

[067-083]that she never will, he is to look upon all that he has said as

[067-084]forgotten. With me it shall be the same as though it were forgotten.”

[067-085]Such was her verdict, and so confident were they both of her

[067-086]firmness—of her obstinacy Mark would have called it on any other

[067-087]occasion,—that they, neither of them, sought to make her alter it.

[067-088]“You will go to him now,—this afternoon; will you not?” she said;

[067-089]and Mark promised that he would. He could not but feel that he

[067-090]himself was greatly relieved. Lady Lufton might probably hear that

[067-091]her son had been fool enough to fall in love with the parson’s

[067-092]sister, but under existing circumstances she could not consider

[067-093]herself aggrieved either by the parson or by his sister. Lucy was

[067-094]behaving well, and Mark was proud of her. Lucy was behaving with

[067-095]fierce spirit, and Fanny was grieving for her.

[067-096]“I’d rather be by myself till dinner-time,” said Lucy, as Mrs.

[067-097]Robarts prepared to go with her out of the room. “Dear Fanny, don’t

[067-098]look unhappy; there’s nothing to make us unhappy. I told you I should

[067-099]want goat’s milk, and that will be all.”

[067-100]Robarts, after sitting for an hour with his wife, did return again to

[067-101]Framley Court; and, after a considerable search, found Lord Lufton

[067-102]returning home to a late dinner.

[067-103]“Unless my mother asks her,” said he, when the story had been told

[067-104]him. “That is nonsense. Surely you told her that such is not the way

[067-105]of the world.”

[067-106]Robarts endeavoured to explain to him that Lucy could not endure to

[067-107]think that her husband’s mother should look on her with disfavour.

[067-108]“Does she think that my mother dislikes her—her specially?” asked

[067-109]Lord Lufton.

[067-110]No; Robarts could not suppose that that was the case; but Lady Lufton

[067-111]might probably think that a marriage with a clergyman’s sister would

[067-112]be a mésalliance.

[067-113]“That is out of the question,” said Lord Lufton; “as she has

[067-114]especially wanted me to marry a clergyman’s daughter for some time

[067-115]past. But, Mark, it is absurd talking about my mother. A man in these

[067-116]days is not to marry as his mother bids him.”

[067-117]Mark could only assure him, in answer to all this, that Lucy was very

[067-118]firm in what she was doing, that she had quite made up her mind, and

[067-119]that she altogether absolved Lord Lufton from any necessity to speak

[067-120]to his mother, if he did not think well of doing so. But all this was

[067-121]to very little purpose.

[067-122]“She does love me then?” said Lord Lufton.

[067-123]“Well,” said Mark, “I will not say whether she does or does not. I

[067-124]can only repeat her own message. She cannot accept you, unless she

[067-125]does so at your mother’s request.” And having said that again, he

[067-126]took his leave, and went back to the parsonage.

[067-127]Poor Lucy, having finished her interview with so much dignity, having

[067-128]fully satisfied her brother, and declined any immediate consolation

[067-129]from her sister-in-law, betook herself to her own bed-room. She had

[067-130]to think over what she had said and done, and it was necessary that

[067-131]she should be alone to do so. It might be that, when she came to

[067-132]reconsider the matter, she would not be quite so well satisfied

[067-133]as was her brother. Her grandeur of demeanour and slow propriety

[067-134]of carriage lasted her till she was well into her own room. There

[067-135]are animals who, when they are ailing in any way, contrive to hide

[067-136]themselves, ashamed, as it were, that the weakness of their suffering

[067-137]should be witnessed. Indeed, I am not sure whether all dumb animals

[067-138]do not do so more or less; and in this respect Lucy was like a dumb

[067-139]animal. Even in her confidences with Fanny she made a joke of her own

[067-140]misfortunes, and spoke of her heart ailments with self-ridicule. But

[067-141]now, having walked up the staircase with no hurried step, and having

[067-142]deliberately locked the door, she turned herself round to suffer in

[067-143]silence and solitude—as do the beasts and birds.

[067-144]She sat herself down on a low chair, which stood at the foot of her

[067-145]bed, and, throwing back her head, held her handkerchief across her

[067-146]eyes and forehead, holding it tight in both her hands; and then she

[067-147]began to think. She began to think and also to cry, for the tears

[067-148]came running down from beneath the handkerchief; and low sobs were to

[067-149]be heard,—only that the animal had taken itself off, to suffer in

[067-150]solitude.

[067-151]Had she not thrown from her all her chances of happiness? Was it

[067-152]possible that he should come to her yet again,—a third time? No;

[067-153]it was not possible. The very mode and pride of this, her second

[067-154]rejection of him, made it impossible. In coming to her determination,

[067-155]and making her avowal, she had been actuated by the knowledge that

[067-156]Lady Lufton would regard such a marriage with abhorrence. Lady Lufton

[067-157]would not and could not ask her to condescend to be her son’s bride.

[067-158]Her chance of happiness, of glory, of ambition, of love, was all

[067-159]gone. She had sacrificed everything, not to virtue, but to pride;

[067-160]and she had sacrificed not only herself, but him. When first he came

[067-161]there—when she had meditated over his first visit—she had hardly

[067-162]given him credit for deep love; but now—there could be no doubt that

[067-163]he loved her now. After his season in London, his days and nights

[067-164]passed with all that was beautiful, he had returned there, to that

[067-165]little country parsonage, that he might again throw himself at her

[067-166]feet. And she—she had refused to see him, though she loved him with

[067-167]all her heart; she had refused to see him, because she was so vile a

[067-168]coward that she could not bear the sour looks of an old woman!

[067-169]“I will come down directly,” she said, when Fanny at last knocked at

[067-170]the door, begging to be admitted. “I won’t open it, love, but I will

[067-171]be with you in ten minutes; I will, indeed.” And so she was; not,

[067-172]perhaps, without traces of tears, discernible by the experienced eye

[067-173]of Mrs. Robarts, but yet with a smooth brow, and voice under her own

[067-174]command.

[067-175]“I wonder whether she really loves him,” Mark said to his wife that

[067-176]night.

[067-177]“Love him!” his wife had answered; “indeed she does; and, Mark, do

[067-178]not be led away by the stern quiet of her demeanour. To my thinking

[067-179]she is a girl who might almost die for love.”
