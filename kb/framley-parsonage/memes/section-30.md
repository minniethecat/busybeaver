## Section 30

[030-001]The vicar did look at his bones, examining the brute in a very

[030-002]knowing and unclerical manner. He lifted the animal’s four feet, one

[030-003]after another, handling the frogs, and measuring with his eye the

[030-004]proportion of the parts; he passed his hand up and down the legs,

[030-005]spanning the bones of the lower joint; he peered into his eyes, took

[030-006]into consideration the width of his chest, the dip of his back, the

[030-007]form of his ribs, the curve of his haunches, and his capabilities

[030-008]for breathing when pressed by work. And then he stood away a little,

[030-009]eyeing him from the side, and taking in a general idea of the form

[030-010]and make of the whole. “He seems to stand over a little, I think,”

[030-011]said the parson.

[030-012]“It’s the lie of the ground. Move him about, Bob. There now, let him

[030-013]stand there.”

[030-014]“He’s not perfect,” said Mark. “I don’t quite like his heels; but no

[030-015]doubt he’s a nicish cut of a horse.”

[030-016]“I rather think he is. If he were perfect, as you say, he would not

[030-017]be going into your stables for a hundred and thirty. Do you ever

[030-018]remember to have seen a perfect horse?”

[030-019]“Your mare Mrs. Gamp was as nearly perfect as possible.”

[030-020]“Even Mrs. Gamp had her faults. In the first place she was a bad

[030-021]feeder. But one certainly doesn’t often come across anything much

[030-022]better than Mrs. Gamp.” And thus the matter was talked over between

[030-023]them with much stable conversation, all of which tended to make

[030-024]Sowerby more and more oblivious of his friend’s sacred profession,

[030-025]and perhaps to make the vicar himself too frequently oblivious of it

[030-026]also. But no: he was not oblivious of it. He was even mindful of it;

[030-027]but mindful of it in such a manner that his thoughts on the subject

[030-028]were nowadays always painful.

[030-029]There is a parish called Hogglestock lying away quite in the northern

[030-030]extremity of the eastern division of the county—lying also on the

[030-031]borders of the western division. I almost fear that it will become

[030-032]necessary, before this history be completed, to provide a map of

[030-033]Barsetshire for the due explanation of all these localities. Framley

[030-034]is also in the northern portion of the county, but just to the

[030-035]south of the grand trunk line of railway from which the branch to

[030-036]Barchester strikes off at a point some thirty miles nearer to London.

[030-037]The station for Framley Court is Silverbridge, which is, however, in

[030-038]the western division of the county. Hogglestock is to the north of

[030-039]the railway, the line of which, however, runs through a portion of

[030-040]the parish, and it adjoins Framley, though the churches are as much

[030-041]as seven miles apart. Barsetshire, taken altogether, is a pleasant

[030-042]green tree-becrowded county, with large bosky hedges, pretty damp

[030-043]deep lanes, and roads with broad grass margins running along them.

[030-044]Such is the general nature of the county; but just up in its northern

[030-045]extremity this nature alters. There it is bleak and ugly, with low

[030-046]artificial hedges and without wood; not uncultivated, as it is all

[030-047]portioned out into new-looking large fields, bearing turnips and

[030-048]wheat and mangel, all in due course of agricultural rotation; but it

[030-049]has none of the special beauties of English cultivation. There is not

[030-050]a gentleman’s house in the parish of Hogglestock besides that of the

[030-051]clergyman; and this, though it is certainly the house of a gentleman,

[030-052]can hardly be said to be fit to be so. It is ugly, and straight, and

[030-053]small. There is a garden attached to the house, half in front of

[030-054]it and half behind; but this garden, like the rest of the parish,

[030-055]is by no means ornamental, though sufficiently useful. It produces

[030-056]cabbages, but no trees: potatoes of, I believe, an excellent

[030-057]description, but hardly any flowers, and nothing worthy of the name

[030-058]of a shrub. Indeed the whole parish of Hogglestock should have been

[030-059]in the adjoining county, which is by no means so attractive as

[030-060]Barsetshire;—a fact well known to those few of my readers who are

[030-061]well acquainted with their own country.

[030-062]Mr. Crawley, whose name has been mentioned in these pages, was the

[030-063]incumbent of Hogglestock. On what principle the remuneration of our

[030-064]parish clergymen was settled when the original settlement was made,

[030-065]no deepest, keenest lover of middle-aged ecclesiastical black-letter

[030-066]learning can, I take it, now say. That the priests were to be paid

[030-067]from tithes of the parish produce, out of which tithes certain other

[030-068]good things were to be bought and paid for, such as church repairs

[030-069]and education, of so much the most of us have an inkling. That

[030-070]a rector, being a big sort of parson, owned the tithes of his

[030-071]parish in full,—or at any rate that part of them intended for the

[030-072]clergyman,—and that a vicar was somebody’s deputy, and therefore

[030-073]entitled only to little tithes, as being a little body: of so much we

[030-074]that are simple in such matters have a general idea. But one cannot

[030-075]conceive that even in this way any approximation could have been

[030-076]made, even in those old mediæval days, towards a fair proportioning

[030-077]of the pay to the work. At any rate, it is clear enough that there is

[030-078]no such approximation now.

[030-079]And what a screech would there not be among the clergy of the Church,

[030-080]even in these reforming days, if any over-bold reformer were to

[030-081]suggest that such an approximation should be attempted? Let those who

[030-082]know clergymen, and like them, and have lived with them, only fancy

[030-083]it! Clergymen to be paid, not according to the temporalities of any

[030-084]living which they may have acquired either by merit or favour, but

[030-085]in accordance with the work to be done! O Doddington! and O Stanhope,

[030-086]think of this, if an idea so sacrilegious can find entrance into your

[030-087]warm ecclesiastical bosoms! Ecclesiastical work to be bought and paid

[030-088]for according to its quantity and quality!

[030-089]But, nevertheless, one may prophesy that we Englishmen must come to

[030-090]this, disagreeable as the idea undoubtedly is. Most pleasant-minded

[030-091]churchmen feel, I think, on this subject pretty much in the same way.

[030-092]Our present arrangement of parochial incomes is beloved as being

[030-093]time-honoured, gentlemanlike, English, and picturesque. We would fain

[030-094]adhere to it closely as long as we can, but we know that we do so

[030-095]by the force of our prejudices, and not by that of our judgment.

[030-096]A time-honoured, gentlemanlike, English, picturesque arrangement

[030-097]is so far very delightful. But are there not other attributes very

[030-098]desirable—nay, absolutely necessary—in respect to which this

[030-099]time-honoured, picturesque arrangement is so very deficient?

[030-100]How pleasant it was, too, that one bishop should be getting fifteen

[030-101]thousand a year and another with an equal cure of parsons only four!

[030-102]That a certain prelate could get twenty thousand one year and his

[030-103]successor in the same diocese only five the next! There was something

[030-104]in it pleasant, and picturesque; it was an arrangement endowed with

[030-105]feudal charms, and the change which they have made was distasteful to

[030-106]many of us. A bishop with a regular salary, and no appanage of land

[030-107]and land-bailiffs, is only half a bishop. Let any man prove to me the

[030-108]contrary ever so thoroughly—let me prove it to my own self ever so

[030-109]often—my heart in this matter is not thereby a whit altered. One

[030-110]liked to know that there was a dean or two who got his three thousand

[030-111]a year, and that old Dr. Purple held four stalls, one of which was

[030-112]golden, and the other three silver-gilt! Such knowledge was always

[030-113]pleasant to me! A golden stall! How sweet is the sound thereof to

[030-114]church-loving ears!

[030-115]But bishops have been shorn of their beauty, and deans are in

[030-116]their decadence. A utilitarian age requires the fatness of the

[030-117]ecclesiastical land, in order that it may be divided out into

[030-118]small portions of provender, on which necessary working clergymen

[030-119]may live,—into portions so infinitesimally small that working

[030-120]clergymen can hardly live. And the full-blown rectors and vicars,

[030-121]with full-blown tithes—with tithes when too full-blown for strict

[030-122]utilitarian principles—will necessarily follow. Stanhope and

[030-123]Doddington must bow their heads, with such compensation for temporal

[030-124]rights as may be extracted,—but probably without such compensation

[030-125]as may be desired. In other trades, professions, and lines of life,

[030-126]men are paid according to their work. Let it be so in the Church.

[030-127]Such will sooner or later be the edict of a utilitarian, reforming,

[030-128]matter-of-fact House of Parliament.

[030-129]I have a scheme of my own on the subject, which I will not introduce

[030-130]here, seeing that neither men nor women would read it. And with

[030-131]reference to this matter, I will only here further explain that all

[030-132]these words have been brought about by the fact, necessary to be here

[030-133]stated, that Mr. Crawley only received one hundred and thirty pounds

[030-134]a year for performing the whole parochial duty of the parish of

[030-135]Hogglestock. And Hogglestock is a large parish. It includes two

[030-136]populous villages, abounding in brickmakers, a race of men very

[030-137]troublesome to a zealous parson who won’t let men go rollicking to

[030-138]the devil without interference. Hogglestock has full work for two

[030-139]men; and yet all the funds therein applicable to parson’s work is

[030-140]this miserable stipend of one hundred and thirty pounds a year. It

[030-141]is a stipend neither picturesque, nor time-honoured, nor feudal, for

[030-142]Hogglestock takes rank only as a perpetual curacy.

[030-143]Mr. Crawley has been mentioned before as a clergyman of whom Mr.

[030-144]Robarts said, that he almost thought it wrong to take a walk out of

[030-145]his own parish. In so saying Mark Robarts of course burlesqued his

[030-146]brother parson; but there can be no doubt that Mr. Crawley was a

[030-147]strict man,—a strict, stern, unpleasant man, and one who feared God

[030-148]and his own conscience. We must say a word or two of Mr. Crawley and

[030-149]his concerns.

[030-150]He was now some forty years of age, but of these he had not been in

[030-151]possession even of his present benefice for more than four or five.

[030-152]The first ten years of his life as a clergyman had been passed in

[030-153]performing the duties and struggling through the life of a curate

[030-154]in a bleak, ugly, cold parish on the northern coast of Cornwall.

[030-155]It had been a weary life and a fearful struggle, made up of duties

[030-156]ill requited and not always satisfactorily performed, of love and

[030-157]poverty, of increasing cares, of sickness, debt, and death. For Mr.

[030-158]Crawley had married almost as soon as he was ordained, and children

[030-159]had been born to him in that chill, comfortless Cornish cottage.

[030-160]He had married a lady well educated and softly nurtured, but not

[030-161]dowered with worldly wealth. They two had gone forth determined

[030-162]to fight bravely together; to disregard the world and the world’s

[030-163]ways, looking only to God and to each other for their comfort. They

[030-164]would give up ideas of gentle living, of soft raiment, and delicate

[030-165]feeding. Others,—those that work with their hands, even the

[030-166]bettermost of such workers—could live in decency and health upon

[030-167]even such provision as he could earn as a clergyman. In such manner

[030-168]would they live, so poorly and so decently, working out their work,

[030-169]not with their hands but with their hearts.

[030-170]And so they had established themselves, beginning the world with

[030-171]one bare-footed little girl of fourteen to aid them in their small

[030-172]household matters; and for a while they had both kept heart, loving

[030-173]each other dearly, and prospering somewhat in their work. But a man

[030-174]who has once walked the world as a gentleman knows not what it is to

[030-175]change his position, and place himself lower down in the social rank.

[030-176]Much less can he know what it is so to put down the woman whom he

[030-177]loves. There are a thousand things, mean and trifling in themselves,

[030-178]which a man despises when he thinks of them in his philosophy, but

[030-179]to dispense with which puts his philosophy to so stern a proof. Let

[030-180]any plainest man who reads this think of his usual mode of getting

[030-181]himself into his matutinal garments, and confess how much such a

[030-182]struggle would cost him.
