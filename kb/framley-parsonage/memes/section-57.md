## Section 57

[057-001]There was no further hope thence of absolute deliverance, but there

[057-002]had been a tender of money services. To give Mr. Sowerby his due,

[057-003]he had at once declared that it would be quite out of the question

[057-004]that he should now receive any assistance of that sort from Miss

[057-005]Dunstable; but his sister had explained to him that it would be a

[057-006]mere business transaction; that Miss Dunstable would receive her

[057-007]interest; and that, if she would be content with four per cent.,

[057-008]whereas the duke received five, and other creditors six, seven,

[057-009]eight, ten, and heaven only knows how much more, it might be well for

[057-010]all parties. He, himself, understood, as well as Fothergill had done,

[057-011]what was the meaning of the duke’s message. Chaldicotes was to be

[057-012]gathered up and garnered, as had been done with so many another fair

[057-013]property lying in those regions. It was to be swallowed whole, and

[057-014]the master was to walk out from his old family hall, to leave the

[057-015]old woods that he loved, to give up utterly to another the parks and

[057-016]paddocks and pleasant places which he had known from his earliest

[057-017]infancy, and owned from his earliest manhood.

[057-018]There can be nothing more bitter to a man than such a surrender.

[057-019]What, compared to this, can be the loss of wealth to one who has

[057-020]himself made it, and brought it together, but has never actually seen

[057-021]it with his bodily eyes? Such wealth has come by one chance, and

[057-022]goes by another: the loss of it is part of the game which the man is

[057-023]playing; and if he cannot lose as well as win, he is a poor, weak,

[057-024]cowardly creature. Such men, as a rule, do know how to bear a mind

[057-025]fairly equal to adversity. But to have squandered the acres which

[057-026]have descended from generation to generation; to be the member of

[057-027]one’s family that has ruined that family; to have swallowed up in

[057-028]one’s own maw all that should have graced one’s children, and one’s

[057-029]grandchildren! It seems to me that the misfortunes of this world can

[057-030]hardly go beyond that!

[057-031]Mr. Sowerby, in spite of his recklessness and that dare-devil gaiety

[057-032]which he knew so well how to wear and use, felt all this as keenly

[057-033]as any man could feel it. It had been absolutely his own fault. The

[057-034]acres had come to him all his own, and now, before his death, every

[057-035]one of them would have gone bodily into that greedy maw. The duke

[057-036]had bought up nearly all the debts which had been secured upon the

[057-037]property, and now could make a clean sweep of it. Sowerby, when he

[057-038]received that message from Mr. Fothergill, knew well that this was

[057-039]intended; and he knew well also, that when once he should cease to be

[057-040]Mr. Sowerby of Chaldicotes, he need never again hope to be returned

[057-041]as member for West Barsetshire. This world would for him be all over.

[057-042]And what must such a man feel when he reflects that this world is for

[057-043]him all over?

[057-044]On the morning in question he went to his appointment, still bearing

[057-045]a cheerful countenance. Mr. Fothergill, when in town on such business

[057-046]as this, always had a room at his service in the house of Messrs.

[057-047]Gumption and Gagebee, the duke’s London law agents, and it was

[057-048]thither that Mr. Sowerby had been summoned. The house of business of

[057-049]Messrs. Gumption and Gagebee was in South Audley Street; and it may

[057-050]be said that there was no spot on the whole earth which Mr. Sowerby

[057-051]so hated as he did the gloomy, dingy back sitting-room up-stairs

[057-052]in that house. He had been there very often, but had never been

[057-053]there without annoyance. It was a horrid torture-chamber, kept for

[057-054]such dread purposes as these, and no doubt had been furnished, and

[057-055]papered, and curtained with the express object of finally breaking

[057-056]down the spirits of such poor country gentlemen as chanced to be

[057-057]involved. Everything was of a brown crimson,—of a crimson that had

[057-058]become brown. Sunlight, real genial light of the sun, never made its

[057-059]way there, and no amount of candles could illumine the gloom of that

[057-060]brownness. The windows were never washed; the ceiling was of a dark

[057-061]brown; the old Turkey carpet was thick with dust, and brown withal.

[057-062]The ungainly office-table, in the middle of the room, had been

[057-063]covered with black leather, but that was now brown. There was a

[057-064]bookcase full of dingy brown law books in a recess on one side of

[057-065]the fireplace, but no one had touched them for years, and over the

[057-066]chimney-piece hung some old legal pedigree table, black with soot.

[057-067]Such was the room which Mr. Fothergill always used in the business

[057-068]house of Messrs. Gumption and Gagebee, in South Audley Street, near

[057-069]to Park Lane.

[057-070]I once heard this room spoken of by an old friend of mine, one

[057-071]Mr. Gresham of Greshamsbury, the father of Frank Gresham, who was

[057-072]now about to purchase that part of the Chace of Chaldicotes which

[057-073]belonged to the Crown. He also had had evil days, though now happily

[057-074]they were past and gone; and he, too, had sat in that room, and

[057-075]listened to the voice of men who were powerful over his property,

[057-076]and intended to use that power. The idea which he left on my mind

[057-077]was much the same as that which I had entertained, when a boy, of

[057-078]a certain room in the castle of Udolpho. There was a chair in that

[057-079]Udolpho room in which those who sat were dragged out limb by limb,

[057-080]the head one way and the legs another; the fingers were dragged off

[057-081]from the hands, and the teeth out from the jaws, and the hair off

[057-082]the head, and the flesh from the bones, and the joints from their

[057-083]sockets, till there was nothing left but a lifeless trunk seated in

[057-084]the chair. Mr. Gresham, as he told me, always sat in the same seat,

[057-085]and the tortures he suffered when so seated, the dislocations of his

[057-086]property which he was forced to discuss, the operations on his very

[057-087]self which he was forced to witness, made me regard that room as

[057-088]worse than the chamber of Udolpho. He, luckily—a rare instance of

[057-089]good fortune—had lived to see all his bones and joints put together

[057-090]again, and flourishing soundly; but he never could speak of the room

[057-091]without horror.

[057-092]“No consideration on earth,” he once said to me, very solemnly,—“I

[057-093]say none, should make me again enter that room.” And indeed this

[057-094]feeling was so strong with him, that from the day when his affairs

[057-095]took a turn he would never even walk down South Audley Street. On

[057-096]the morning in question into this torture-chamber Mr. Sowerby went,

[057-097]and there, after some two or three minutes, he was joined by Mr.

[057-098]Fothergill.

[057-099]Mr. Fothergill was, in one respect, like to his friend Sowerby. He

[057-100]enacted two altogether different persons on occasions which were

[057-101]altogether different. Generally speaking, with the world at large, he

[057-102]was a jolly, rollicking, popular man, fond of eating and drinking,

[057-103]known to be devoted to the duke’s interests, and supposed to be

[057-104]somewhat unscrupulous, or at any rate hard, when they were concerned;

[057-105]but in other respects a good-natured fellow; and there was a report

[057-106]about that he had once lent somebody money, without charging him

[057-107]interest or taking security. On the present occasion Sowerby saw

[057-108]at a glance that he had come thither with all the aptitudes and

[057-109]appurtenances of his business about him. He walked into the room with

[057-110]a short, quick step; there was no smile on his face as he shook hands

[057-111]with his old friend; he brought with him a box laden with papers and

[057-112]parchments, and he had not been a minute in the room before he was

[057-113]seated in one of the old dingy chairs.

[057-114]“How long have you been in town, Fothergill?” said Sowerby, still

[057-115]standing with his back against the chimney. He had resolved on only

[057-116]one thing—that nothing should induce him to touch, look at, or

[057-117]listen to any of those papers. He knew well enough that no good would

[057-118]come of that. He also had his own lawyer, to see that he was pilfered

[057-119]according to rule.

[057-120]“How long? Since the day before yesterday. I never was so busy in my

[057-121]life. The duke, as usual, wants to have everything done at once.”

[057-122]“If he wants to have all that I owe him paid at once, he is like to

[057-123]be out in his reckoning.”

[057-124]“Ah, well; I’m glad you are ready to come quickly to business,

[057-125]because it’s always best. Won’t you come and sit down here?”

[057-126]“No, thank you; I’ll stand.”

[057-127]“But we shall have to go through these figures, you know.”

[057-128]“Not a figure, Fothergill. What good would it do? None to me, and

[057-129]none to you either, as I take it; if there is anything wrong,

[057-130]Potter’s fellows will find it out. What is it the duke wants?”

[057-131]“Well; to tell the truth, he wants his money.”

[057-132]“In one sense, and that the main sense, he has got it. He gets his

[057-133]interest regularly, does not he?”

[057-134]“Pretty well for that, seeing how times are. But, Sowerby, that’s

[057-135]nonsense. You understand the duke as well as I do, and you know very

[057-136]well what he wants. He has given you time, and if you had taken any

[057-137]steps towards getting the money, you might have saved the property.”

[057-138]“A hundred and eighty thousand pounds! What steps could I take to

[057-139]get that? Fly a bill, and let Tozer have it to get cash on it in the

[057-140]City!”

[057-141]“We hoped you were going to marry.”

[057-142]“That’s all off.”

[057-143]“Then I don’t think you can blame the duke for looking for his own.

[057-144]It does not suit him to have so large a sum standing out any longer.

[057-145]You see, he wants land, and will have it. Had you paid off what you

[057-146]owed him, he would have purchased the Crown property; and now, it

[057-147]seems, young Gresham has bid against him, and is to have it. This has

[057-148]riled him, and I may as well tell you fairly, that he is determined

[057-149]to have either money or marbles.”

[057-150]“You mean that I am to be dispossessed.”

[057-151]“Well, yes; if you choose to call it so. My instructions are to

[057-152]foreclose at once.”

[057-153]“Then I must say the duke is treating me most uncommonly ill.”

[057-154]“Well, Sowerby, I can’t see it.”

[057-155]“I can, though. He has his money like clock-work; and he has bought

[057-156]up these debts from persons who would have never disturbed me as long

[057-157]as they got their interest.”

[057-158]“Haven’t you had the seat?”

[057-159]“The seat! and is it expected that I am to pay for that?”

[057-160]“I don’t see that any one is asking you to pay for it. You are like

[057-161]a great many other people that I know. You want to eat your cake and

[057-162]have it. You have been eating it for the last twenty years, and now

[057-163]you think yourself very ill-used because the duke wants to have his

[057-164]turn.”

[057-165]“I shall think myself very ill-used if he sells me out—worse than

[057-166]ill-used. I do not want to use strong language, but it will be more

[057-167]than ill-usage. I can hardly believe that he really means to treat me

[057-168]in that way.”

[057-169]“It is very hard that he should want his own money!”

[057-170]“It is not his money that he wants. It is my property.”

[057-171]“And has he not paid for it? Have you not had the price of your

[057-172]property? Now, Sowerby, it is of no use for you to be angry; you have

[057-173]known for the last three years what was coming on you as well as I

[057-174]did. Why should the duke lend you money without an object? Of course

[057-175]he has his own views. But I do say this; he has not hurried you; and

[057-176]had you been able to do anything to save the place you might have

[057-177]done it. You have had time enough to look about you.”
