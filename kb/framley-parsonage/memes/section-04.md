## Section 4

[004-001]Mrs. Harold Smith was the very opposite of her lord. She was a

[004-002]clever, bright woman, good-looking for her time of life—and she was

[004-003]now over forty—with a keen sense of the value of all worldly things,

[004-004]and a keen relish for all the world’s pleasures. She was neither

[004-005]laborious, nor well-informed, nor perhaps altogether honest—what

[004-006]woman ever understood the necessity or recognized the advantage of

[004-007]political honesty?—but then she was neither dull nor pompous, and

[004-008]if she was conceited, she did not show it. She was a disappointed

[004-009]woman, as regards her husband; seeing that she had married him on the

[004-010]speculation that he would at once become politically important; and

[004-011]as yet Mr. Smith had not quite fulfilled the prophecies of his early

[004-012]life.

[004-013]And Lady Lufton, when she spoke of the Chaldicotes set, distinctly

[004-014]included, in her own mind, the Bishop of Barchester, and his wife

[004-015]and daughter. Seeing that Bishop Proudie was, of course, a man much

[004-016]addicted to religion and to religious thinking, and that Mr. Sowerby

[004-017]himself had no peculiar religious sentiments whatever, there would

[004-018]not at first sight appear to be ground for much intercourse, and

[004-019]perhaps there was not much of such intercourse; but Mrs. Proudie

[004-020]and Mrs. Harold Smith were firm friends of four or five years’

[004-021]standing—ever since the Proudies came into the diocese; and

[004-022]therefore the bishop was usually taken to Chaldicotes whenever Mrs.

[004-023]Smith paid her brother a visit. Now Bishop Proudie was by no means

[004-024]a High Church dignitary, and Lady Lufton had never forgiven him for

[004-025]coming into that diocese. She had, instinctively, a high respect

[004-026]for the episcopal office; but of Bishop Proudie himself she hardly

[004-027]thought better than she did of Mr. Sowerby, or of that fabricator of

[004-028]evil, the Duke of Omnium. Whenever Mr. Robarts would plead that in

[004-029]going anywhere he would have the benefit of meeting the bishop, Lady

[004-030]Lufton would slightly curl her upper lip. She could not say in words,

[004-031]that Bishop Proudie—bishop as he certainly must be called—was no

[004-032]better than he ought to be; but by that curl of her lip she did

[004-033]explain to those who knew her that such was the inner feeling of her

[004-034]heart.

[004-035]And then it was understood—Mark Robarts, at least, had so heard,

[004-036]and the information soon reached Framley Court—that Mr. Supplehouse

[004-037]was to make one of the Chaldicotes party. Now Mr. Supplehouse was a

[004-038]worse companion for a gentlemanlike, young, High Church, conservative

[004-039]county parson than even Harold Smith. He also was in Parliament, and

[004-040]had been extolled during the early days of that Russian war by some

[004-041]portion of the metropolitan daily press, as the only man who could

[004-042]save the country. Let him be in the ministry, the _Jupiter_ had said,

[004-043]and there would be some hope of reform, some chance that England’s

[004-044]ancient glory would not be allowed in these perilous times to go

[004-045]headlong to oblivion. And upon this the ministry, not anticipating

[004-046]much salvation from Mr. Supplehouse, but willing, as they usually

[004-047]are, to have the _Jupiter_ at their back, did send for that

[004-048]gentleman, and gave him some footing among them. But how can a man

[004-049]born to save a nation, and to lead a people, be content to fill the

[004-050]chair of an under-secretary? Supplehouse was not content, and soon

[004-051]gave it to be understood that his place was much higher than any

[004-052]yet tendered to him. The seals of high office, or war to the knife,

[004-053]was the alternative which he offered to a much-belaboured Head of

[004-054]Affairs—nothing doubting that the Head of Affairs would recognize

[004-055]the claimant’s value, and would have before his eyes a wholesome fear

[004-056]of the _Jupiter_. But the Head of Affairs, much belaboured as he was,

[004-057]knew that he might pay too high even for Mr. Supplehouse and the

[004-058]_Jupiter_; and the saviour of the nation was told that he might swing

[004-059]his tomahawk. Since that time he had been swinging his tomahawk, but

[004-060]not with so much effect as had been anticipated. He also was very

[004-061]intimate with Mr. Sowerby, and was decidedly one of the Chaldicotes

[004-062]set.

[004-063]And there were many others included in the stigma whose sins were

[004-064]political or religious rather than moral. But they were gall and

[004-065]wormwood to Lady Lufton, who regarded them as children of the Lost

[004-066]One, and who grieved with a mother’s grief when she knew that her son

[004-067]was among them, and felt all a patron’s anger when she heard that her

[004-068]clerical _protégé_ was about to seek such society. Mrs. Robarts might

[004-069]well say that Lady Lufton would be annoyed.

[004-070]“You won’t call at the house before you go, will you?” the wife asked

[004-071]on the following morning. He was to start after lunch on that day,

[004-072]driving himself in his own gig, so as to reach Chaldicotes, some

[004-073]twenty-four miles distant, before dinner.

[004-074]“No, I think not. What good should I do?”

[004-075]“Well, I can’t explain; but I think I should call: partly, perhaps,

[004-076]to show her that as I had determined to go, I was not afraid of

[004-077]telling her so.”

[004-078]“Afraid! That’s nonsense, Fanny. I’m not afraid of her. But I don’t

[004-079]see why I should bring down upon myself the disagreeable things she

[004-080]will say. Besides, I have not time. I must walk up and see Jones

[004-081]about the duties; and then, what with getting ready, I shall have

[004-082]enough to do to get off in time.”

[004-083]He paid his visit to Mr. Jones, the curate, feeling no qualms

[004-084]of conscience there, as he rather boasted of all the members of

[004-085]Parliament he was going to meet, and of the bishop who would be with

[004-086]them. Mr. Evan Jones was only his curate, and in speaking to him on

[004-087]the matter he could talk as though it were quite the proper thing for

[004-088]a vicar to meet his bishop at the house of a county member. And one

[004-089]would be inclined to say that it was proper: only why could he not

[004-090]talk of it in the same tone to Lady Lufton? And then, having kissed

[004-091]his wife and children, he drove off, well pleased with his prospect

[004-092]for the coming ten days, but already anticipating some discomfort on

[004-093]his return.

[004-094]On the three following days, Mrs. Robarts did not meet her ladyship.

[004-095]She did not exactly take any steps to avoid such a meeting, but she

[004-096]did not purposely go up to the big house. She went to her school as

[004-097]usual, and made one or two calls among the farmers’ wives, but put

[004-098]no foot within the Framley Court grounds. She was braver than her

[004-099]husband, but even she did not wish to anticipate the evil day.

[004-100]On the Saturday, just before it began to get dusk, when she was

[004-101]thinking of preparing for the fatal plunge, her friend, Lady

[004-102]Meredith, came to her.

[004-103]“So, Fanny, we shall again be so unfortunate as to miss Mr. Robarts,”

[004-104]said her ladyship.

[004-105]“Yes. Did you ever know anything so unlucky? But he had promised Mr.

[004-106]Sowerby before he heard that you were coming. Pray do not think that

[004-107]he would have gone away had he known it.”

[004-108]“We should have been sorry to keep him from so much more amusing a

[004-109]party.”

[004-110]“Now, Justinia, you are unfair. You intend to imply that he has gone

[004-111]to Chaldicotes, because he likes it better than Framley Court; but

[004-112]that is not the case. I hope Lady Lufton does not think that it is.”

[004-113]Lady Meredith laughed as she put her arm round her friend’s waist.

[004-114]“Don’t lose your eloquence in defending him to me,” she said. “You’ll

[004-115]want all that for my mother.”

[004-116]“But is your mother angry?” asked Mrs. Robarts, showing by her

[004-117]countenance, how eager she was for true tidings on the subject.

[004-118]“Well, Fanny, you know her ladyship as well as I do. She thinks so

[004-119]very highly of the vicar of Framley, that she does begrudge him to

[004-120]those politicians at Chaldicotes.”

[004-121]“But, Justinia, the bishop is to be there, you know.”

[004-122]“I don’t think that that consideration will at all reconcile my

[004-123]mother to the gentleman’s absence. He ought to be very proud, I know,

[004-124]to find that he is so much thought of. But come, Fanny, I want you to

[004-125]walk back with me, and you can dress at the house. And now we’ll go

[004-126]and look at the children.”

[004-127]After that, as they walked together to Framley Court, Mrs. Robarts

[004-128]made her friend promise that she would stand by her if any serious

[004-129]attack were made on the absent clergyman.

[004-130]“Are you going up to your room at once?” said the vicar’s wife,

[004-131]as soon as they were inside the porch leading into the hall. Lady

[004-132]Meredith immediately knew what her friend meant, and decided that the

[004-133]evil day should not be postponed. “We had better go in and have it

[004-134]over,” she said, “and then we shall be comfortable for the evening.”

[004-135]So the drawing-room door was opened, and there was Lady Lufton alone

[004-136]upon the sofa.

[004-137]“Now, mamma,” said the daughter, “you mustn’t scold Fanny much about

[004-138]Mr. Robarts. He has gone to preach a charity sermon before the

[004-139]bishop, and under those circumstances, perhaps, he could not refuse.”

[004-140]This was a stretch on the part of Lady Meredith—put in with much

[004-141]good nature, no doubt; but still a stretch; for no one had supposed

[004-142]that the bishop would remain at Chaldicotes for the Sunday.

[004-143]“How do you do, Fanny?” said Lady Lufton, getting up. “I am not

[004-144]going to scold her; and I don’t know how you can talk such nonsense,

[004-145]Justinia. Of course, we are very sorry not to have Mr. Robarts; more

[004-146]especially as he was not here the last Sunday that Sir George was

[004-147]with us. I do like to see Mr. Robarts in his own church, certainly;

[004-148]and I don’t like any other clergyman there as well. If Fanny takes

[004-149]that for scolding, why—”

[004-150]“Oh! no, Lady Lufton; and it’s so kind of you to say so. But Mr.

[004-151]Robarts was so sorry that he had accepted this invitation to

[004-152]Chaldicotes, before he heard that Sir George was coming, and—”

[004-153]“Oh, I know that Chaldicotes has great attractions which we cannot

[004-154]offer,” said Lady Lufton.

[004-155]“Indeed, it was not that. But he was asked to preach, you know; and

[004-156]Mr. Harold Smith—” Poor Fanny was only making it worse. Had she been

[004-157]worldly wise, she would have accepted the little compliment implied

[004-158]in Lady Lufton’s first rebuke, and then have held her peace.

[004-159]“Oh, yes; the Harold Smiths! They are irresistible, I know. How could

[004-160]any man refuse to join a party, graced both by Mrs. Harold Smith and

[004-161]Mrs. Proudie—even though his duty should require him to stay away?”

[004-162]“Now, mamma—” said Justinia.

[004-163]“Well, my dear, what am I to say? You would not wish me to tell a

[004-164]fib. I don’t like Mrs. Harold Smith—at least, what I hear of her;

[004-165]for it has not been my fortune to meet her since her marriage. It

[004-166]may be conceited; but to own the truth, I think that Mr. Robarts

[004-167]would be better off with us at Framley than with the Harold Smiths at

[004-168]Chaldicotes,—even though Mrs. Proudie be thrown into the bargain.”

[004-169]It was nearly dark, and therefore the rising colour in the face of

[004-170]Mrs. Robarts could not be seen. She, however, was too good a wife to

[004-171]hear these things said without some anger within her bosom. She could

[004-172]blame her husband in her own mind; but it was intolerable to her that

[004-173]others should blame him in her hearing.

[004-174]“He would undoubtedly be better off,” she said; “but then, Lady

[004-175]Lufton, people can’t always go exactly where they will be best off.

[004-176]Gentlemen sometimes must—”

[004-177]“Well—well, my dear, that will do. He has not taken you, at any

[004-178]rate; and so we will forgive him.” And Lady Lufton kissed her. “As it

[004-179]is,”—and she affected a low whisper between the two young wives—“as

[004-180]it is, we must e’en put up with poor old Evan Jones. He is to be here

[004-181]to-night, and we must go and dress to receive him.”
