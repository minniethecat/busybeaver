## Section 39

[039-001]On the morning after our vicar’s arrival in London he attended at

[039-002]the Petty Bag office. It was situated in the close neighbourhood

[039-003]of Downing Street and the higher governmental gods; and though the

[039-004]building itself was not much, seeing that it was shored up on one

[039-005]side, that it bulged out in the front, was foul with smoke, dingy

[039-006]with dirt, and was devoid of any single architectural grace or modern

[039-007]scientific improvement, nevertheless its position gave it a status in

[039-008]the world which made the clerks in the Lord Petty Bag’s office quite

[039-009]respectable in their walk in life. Mark had seen his friend Sowerby

[039-010]on the previous evening, and had then made an appointment with him

[039-011]for the following morning at the new minister’s office. And now he

[039-012]was there a little before his time, in order that he might have a few

[039-013]moments’ chat with his brother.

[039-014]When Mark found himself in the private secretary’s room he was quite

[039-015]astonished to see the change in his brother’s appearance which the

[039-016]change in his official rank had produced. Jack Robarts had been

[039-017]a well-built, straight-legged, lissome young fellow, pleasant to

[039-018]the eye because of his natural advantages, but rather given to a

[039-019]harum-skarum style of gait, and occasionally careless, not to say

[039-020]slovenly, in his dress. But now he was the very pink of perfection.

[039-021]His jaunty frock-coat fitted him to perfection; not a hair of his

[039-022]head was out of place; his waistcoat and trousers were glossy and

[039-023]new, and his umbrella, which stood in the umbrella-stand in the

[039-024]corner, was tight, and neat, and small, and natty.

[039-025]“Well, John, you’ve become quite a great man,” said his brother.

[039-026]“I don’t know much about that,” said John; “but I find that I have an

[039-027]enormous deal of fagging to go through.”

[039-028]“Do you mean work? I thought you had about the easiest berth in the

[039-029]whole Civil Service.”

[039-030]“Ah! that’s just the mistake that people make. Because we don’t cover

[039-031]whole reams of foolscap paper at the rate of fifteen lines to a page,

[039-032]and five words to a line, people think that we private secretaries

[039-033]have got nothing to do. Look here,” and he tossed over scornfully a

[039-034]dozen or so of little notes. “I tell you what, Mark; it is no easy

[039-035]matter to manage the patronage of a cabinet minister. Now I am bound

[039-036]to write to every one of these fellows a letter that will please him;

[039-037]and yet I shall refuse to every one of them the request which he

[039-038]asks.”

[039-039]“That must be difficult.”

[039-040]“Difficult is no word for it. But, after all, it consists chiefly in

[039-041]the knack of the thing. One must have the wit ‘from such a sharp and

[039-042]waspish word as No to pluck the sting.’ I do it every day, and I

[039-043]really think that the people like it.”

[039-044]“Perhaps your refusals are better than other people’s acquiescences.”

[039-045]“I don’t mean that at all. We private secretaries have all to do the

[039-046]same thing. Now, would you believe it? I have used up three lifts of

[039-047]note-paper already in telling people that there is no vacancy for a

[039-048]lobby messenger in the Petty Bag office. Seven peeresses have asked

[039-049]for it for their favourite footmen. But there—there’s the Lord Petty

[039-050]Bag!”

[039-051]A bell rang and the private secretary, jumping up from his

[039-052]note-paper, tripped away quickly to the great man’s room.

[039-053]“He’ll see you at once,” said he, returning. “Buggins, show the

[039-054]Reverend Mr. Robarts to the Lord Petty Bag.”

[039-055]Buggins was the messenger for whose not vacant place all the

[039-056]peeresses were striving with so much animation. And then Mark,

[039-057]following Buggins for two steps, was ushered into the next room.

[039-058]If a man be altered by becoming a private secretary, he is much more

[039-059]altered by being made a cabinet minister. Robarts, as he entered the

[039-060]room, could hardly believe that this was the same Harold Smith whom

[039-061]Mrs. Proudie bothered so cruelly in the lecture-room at Barchester.

[039-062]Then he was cross, and touchy, and uneasy, and insignificant. Now,

[039-063]as he stood smiling on the hearthrug of his official fireplace, it

[039-064]was quite pleasant to see the kind, patronizing smile which lighted

[039-065]up his features. He delighted to stand there, with his hands in

[039-066]his trousers’ pocket, the great man of the place, conscious of his

[039-067]lordship, and feeling himself every inch a minister. Sowerby had come

[039-068]with him, and was standing a little in the background, from which

[039-069]position he winked occasionally at the parson over the minister’s

[039-070]shoulder.

[039-071]“Ah, Robarts, delighted to see you. How odd, by-the-by, that your

[039-072]brother should be my private secretary!” Mark said that it was a

[039-073]singular coincidence.

[039-074]“A very smart young fellow, and, if he minds himself, he’ll do well.”

[039-075]“I’m quite sure he’ll do well,” said Mark.

[039-076]“Ah! well, yes; I think he will. And now, what can I do for you,

[039-077]Robarts?”

[039-078]Hereupon Mr. Sowerby struck in, making it apparent by his explanation

[039-079]that Mr. Robarts himself by no means intended to ask for anything;

[039-080]but that, as his friends had thought that this stall at Barchester

[039-081]might be put into his hands with more fitness than in those of any

[039-082]other clergyman of the day, he was willing to accept the piece of

[039-083]preferment from a man whom he respected so much as he did the new

[039-084]Lord Petty Bag.

[039-085]The minister did not quite like this, as it restricted him from

[039-086]much of his condescension, and robbed him of the incense of a

[039-087]petition which he had expected Mark Robarts would make to him. But,

[039-088]nevertheless, he was very gracious.

[039-089]“He could not take upon himself to declare,” he said, “what might be

[039-090]Lord Brock’s pleasure with reference to the preferment at Barchester

[039-091]which was vacant. He had certainly already spoken to his lordship

[039-092]on the subject, and had perhaps some reason to believe that his own

[039-093]wishes would be consulted. No distinct promise had been made, but

[039-094]he might perhaps go so far as to say that he expected such result.

[039-095]If so, it would give him the greatest pleasure in the world to

[039-096]congratulate Mr. Robarts on the possession of the stall—a stall

[039-097]which he was sure Mr. Robarts would fill with dignity, piety, and

[039-098]brotherly love.” And then, when he had finished, Mr. Sowerby gave a

[039-099]final wink, and said that he regarded the matter as settled.

[039-100]“No, not settled, Nathaniel,” said the cautious minister.

[039-101]“It’s the same thing,” rejoined Sowerby. “We all know what all

[039-102]that flummery means. Men in office, Mark, never do make a distinct

[039-103]promise,—not even to themselves of the leg of mutton which is

[039-104]roasting before their kitchen fires. It is so necessary in these days

[039-105]to be safe; is it not, Harold?”

[039-106]“Most expedient,” said Harold Smith, shaking his head wisely. “Well,

[039-107]Robarts, who is it now?” This he said to his private secretary,

[039-108]who came to notice the arrival of some bigwig. “Well, yes. I will

[039-109]say good morning, with your leave, for I am a little hurried. And

[039-110]remember, Mr. Robarts, I will do what I can for you; but you must

[039-111]distinctly understand that there is no promise.”

[039-112]“Oh, no promise at all,” said Sowerby—“of course not.” And then,

[039-113]as he sauntered up Whitehall towards Charing Cross, with Robarts

[039-114]on his arm, he again pressed upon him the sale of that invaluable

[039-115]hunter, who was eating his head off his shoulders in the stable at

[039-116]Chaldicotes.

[039-117]CHAPTER XIX.

[039-118]MONEY DEALINGS.

[039-119]Mr. Sowerby, in his resolution to obtain this good gift for the Vicar

[039-120]of Framley, did not depend quite alone on the influence of his near

[039-121]connection with the Lord Petty Bag. He felt the occasion to be one

[039-122]on which he might endeavour to move even higher powers than that,

[039-123]and therefore he had opened the matter to the duke—not by direct

[039-124]application, but through Mr. Fothergill. No man who understood

[039-125]matters ever thought of going direct to the duke in such an affair as

[039-126]that. If one wanted to speak about a woman or a horse or a picture

[039-127]the duke could, on occasions, be affable enough.

[039-128]But through Mr. Fothergill the duke was approached. It was

[039-129]represented, with some cunning, that this buying over of the Framley

[039-130]clergyman from the Lufton side would be a praiseworthy spoiling of

[039-131]the Amalekites. The doing so would give the Omnium interest a hold

[039-132]even in the cathedral close. And then it was known to all men that

[039-133]Mr. Robarts had considerable influence over Lord Lufton himself. So

[039-134]guided, the Duke of Omnium did say two words to the Prime Minister,

[039-135]and two words from the duke went a great way, even with Lord Brock.

[039-136]The upshot of all this was, that Mark Robarts did get the stall; but

[039-137]he did not hear the tidings of his success till some days after his

[039-138]return to Framley.

[039-139]Mr. Sowerby did not forget to tell him of the great effort—the

[039-140]unusual effort, as he of Chaldicotes called it—which the duke had

[039-141]made on the subject. “I don’t know when he has done such a thing

[039-142]before,” said Sowerby; “and you may be quite sure of this, he would

[039-143]not have done it now, had you not gone to Gatherum Castle when he

[039-144]asked you: indeed, Fothergill would have known that it was vain to

[039-145]attempt it. And I’ll tell you what, Mark—it does not do for me to

[039-146]make little of my own nest, but I truly believe the duke’s word will

[039-147]be more efficacious than the Lord Petty Bag’s solemn adjuration.”

[039-148]Mark, of course, expressed his gratitude in proper terms, and did buy

[039-149]the horse for a hundred and thirty pounds. “He’s as well worth it,”

[039-150]said Sowerby, “as any animal that ever stood on four legs; and my

[039-151]only reason for pressing him on you is, that when Tozer’s day does

[039-152]come round, I know you will have to stand to us to something about

[039-153]that tune.” It did not occur to Mark to ask him why the horse should

[039-154]not be sold to some one else, and the money forthcoming in the

[039-155]regular way. But this would not have suited Mr. Sowerby.

[039-156]Mark knew that the beast was good, and as he walked to his lodgings

[039-157]was half proud of his new possession. But then, how would he justify

[039-158]it to his wife, or how introduce the animal into his stables without

[039-159]attempting any justification in the matter? And yet, looking to the

[039-160]absolute amount of his income, surely he might feel himself entitled

[039-161]to buy a new horse when it suited him. He wondered what Mr. Crawley

[039-162]would say when he heard of the new purchase. He had lately fallen

[039-163]into a state of much wondering as to what his friends and neighbours

[039-164]would say about him.

[039-165]He had now been two days in town, and was to go down after breakfast

[039-166]on the following morning so that he might reach home by Friday

[039-167]afternoon. But on that evening, just as he was going to bed, he was

[039-168]surprised by Lord Lufton coming into the coffee-room at his hotel.

[039-169]He walked in with a hurried step, his face was red, and it was clear

[039-170]that he was very angry.

[039-171]“Robarts,” said he, walking up to his friend and taking the hand that

[039-172]was extended to him, “do you know anything about this man, Tozer?”

[039-173]“Tozer—what Tozer? I have heard Sowerby speak of such a man.”

[039-174]“Of course you have. If I do not mistake you have written to me about

[039-175]him yourself.”

[039-176]“Very probably. I remember Sowerby mentioning the man with reference

[039-177]to your affairs. But why do you ask me?”

[039-178]“This man has not only written to me, but has absolutely forced his

[039-179]way into my rooms when I was dressing for dinner; and absolutely had

[039-180]the impudence to tell me that if I did not honour some bill which he

[039-181]holds for eight hundred pounds he would proceed against me.”
