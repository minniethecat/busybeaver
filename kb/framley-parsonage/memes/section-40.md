## Section 40

[040-001]“But you settled all that matter with Sowerby?”

[040-002]“I did settle it at a very great cost to me. Sooner than have a fuss

[040-003]I paid him through the nose—like a fool that I was—everything that

[040-004]he claimed. This is an absolute swindle, and if it goes on I will

[040-005]expose it as such.”

[040-006]Robarts looked round the room, but luckily there was not a soul in

[040-007]it but themselves. “You do not mean to say that Sowerby is swindling

[040-008]you?” said the clergyman.

[040-009]“It looks very like it,” said Lord Lufton; “and I tell you fairly

[040-010]that I am not in a humour to endure any more of this sort of thing.

[040-011]Some years ago I made an ass of myself through that man’s fault. But

[040-012]four thousand pounds should have covered the whole of what I really

[040-013]lost. I have now paid more than three times that sum; and, by

[040-014]heavens! I will not pay more without exposing the whole affair.”

[040-015]“But, Lufton, I do not understand. What is this bill?—has it your

[040-016]name to it?”

[040-017]“Yes, it has: I’ll not deny my name, and if there be absolute need I

[040-018]will pay it; but if I do so, my lawyer shall sift it, and it shall go

[040-019]before a jury.”

[040-020]“But I thought all those bills were paid?”

[040-021]“I left it to Sowerby to get up the old bills when they were renewed,

[040-022]and now one of them that has in truth been already honoured is

[040-023]brought against me.”

[040-024]Mark could not but think of the two documents which he himself had

[040-025]signed, and both of which were now undoubtedly in the hands of Tozer,

[040-026]or of some other gentleman of the same profession;—which both

[040-027]might be brought against him, the second as soon as he should have

[040-028]satisfied the first. And then he remembered that Sowerby had said

[040-029]something to him about an outstanding bill, for the filling up of

[040-030]which some trifle must be paid, and of this he reminded Lord Lufton.

[040-031]“And do you call eight hundred pounds a trifle? If so, I do not.”

[040-032]“They will probably make no such demand as that.”

[040-033]“But I tell you they do make such a demand, and have made it. The

[040-034]man whom I saw, and who told me that he was Tozer’s friend, but who

[040-035]was probably Tozer himself, positively swore to me that he would be

[040-036]obliged to take legal proceedings if the money were not forthcoming

[040-037]within a week or ten days. When I explained to him that it was an old

[040-038]bill that had been renewed, he declared that his friend had given

[040-039]full value for it.”

[040-040]“Sowerby said that you would probably have to pay ten pounds to

[040-041]redeem it. I should offer the man some such sum as that.”

[040-042]“My intention is to offer the man nothing, but to leave the affair

[040-043]in the hands of my lawyer with instructions to him to spare

[040-044]none;—neither myself nor any one else. I am not going to allow such

[040-045]a man as Sowerby to squeeze me like an orange.”

[040-046]“But, Lufton, you seem as though you were angry with me.”

[040-047]“No, I am not. But I think it is as well to caution you about this

[040-048]man; my transactions with him lately have chiefly been through you,

[040-049]and therefore—”

[040-050]“But they have only been so through his and your wish: because I have

[040-051]been anxious to oblige you both. I hope you don’t mean to say that I

[040-052]am concerned in these bills.”

[040-053]“I know that you are concerned in bills with him.”

[040-054]“Why, Lufton, am I to understand, then, that you are accusing me

[040-055]of having any interest in these transactions which you have called

[040-056]swindling?”

[040-057]“As far as I am concerned there has been swindling, and there is

[040-058]swindling going on now.”

[040-059]“But you do not answer my question. Do you bring any accusation

[040-060]against me? If so, I agree with you that you had better go to your

[040-061]lawyer.”

[040-062]“I think that is what I shall do.”

[040-063]“Very well. But upon the whole, I never heard of a more unreasonable

[040-064]man, or of one whose thoughts are more unjust than yours. Solely with

[040-065]the view of assisting you, and solely at your request, I spoke to

[040-066]Sowerby about these money transactions of yours. Then at his request,

[040-067]which originated out of your request, he using me as his ambassador

[040-068]to you, as you had used me as yours to him, I wrote and spoke to you.

[040-069]And now this is the upshot.”

[040-070]“I bring no accusation against you, Robarts; but I know you have

[040-071]dealings with this man. You have told me so yourself.”

[040-072]“Yes, at his request to accommodate him, I have put my name to a

[040-073]bill.”

[040-074]“Only to one?”

[040-075]“Only to one; and then to that same renewed, or not exactly to that

[040-076]same, but to one which stands for it. The first was for four hundred

[040-077]pounds; the last for five hundred.”

[040-078]“All which you will have to make good, and the world will of course

[040-079]tell you that you have paid that price for this stall at Barchester.”

[040-080]This was terrible to be borne. He had heard much lately which had

[040-081]frightened and scared him, but nothing so terrible as this; nothing

[040-082]which so stunned him, or conveyed to his mind so frightful a reality

[040-083]of misery and ruin. He made no immediate answer, but standing on the

[040-084]hearthrug with his back to the fire, looked up the whole length of

[040-085]the room. Hitherto his eyes had been fixed upon Lord Lufton’s face,

[040-086]but now it seemed to him as though he had but little more to do with

[040-087]Lord Lufton. Lord Lufton and Lord Lufton’s mother were neither now to

[040-088]be counted among those who wished him well. Upon whom indeed could he

[040-089]now count, except that wife of his bosom upon whom he was bringing

[040-090]all this wretchedness?

[040-091]In that moment of agony ideas ran quickly through his brain. He would

[040-092]immediately abandon this preferment at Barchester, of which it might

[040-093]be said with so much colour that he had bought it. He would go to

[040-094]Harold Smith, and say positively that he declined it. Then he would

[040-095]return home and tell his wife all that had occurred;—tell the whole

[040-096]also to Lady Lufton, if that might still be of any service. He would

[040-097]make arrangement for the payment of both those bills as they might be

[040-098]presented, asking no questions as to the justice of the claim, making

[040-099]no complaint to any one, not even to Sowerby. He would put half his

[040-100]income, if half were necessary, into the hands of Forrest the banker,

[040-101]till all was paid. He would sell every horse he had. He would part

[040-102]with his footman and groom, and at any rate strive like a man to get

[040-103]again a firm footing on good ground. Then, at that moment, he loathed

[040-104]with his whole soul the position in which he found himself placed,

[040-105]and his own folly which had placed him there. How could he reconcile

[040-106]it to his conscience that he was there in London with Sowerby and

[040-107]Harold Smith, petitioning for Church preferment to a man who should

[040-108]have been altogether powerless in such a matter, buying horses,

[040-109]and arranging about past due bills? He did not reconcile it to his

[040-110]conscience. Mr. Crawley had been right when he told him that he was a

[040-111]castaway.

[040-112]Lord Lufton, whose anger during the whole interview had been extreme,

[040-113]and who had become more angry the more he talked, had now walked once

[040-114]or twice up and down the room; and as he so walked the idea did occur

[040-115]to him that he had been unjust. He had come there with the intention

[040-116]of exclaiming against Sowerby, and of inducing Robarts to convey to

[040-117]that gentleman, that if he, Lord Lufton, were made to undergo any

[040-118]further annoyance about this bill, the whole affair should be thrown

[040-119]into the lawyer’s hands; but instead of doing this, he had brought

[040-120]an accusation against Robarts. That Robarts had latterly become

[040-121]Sowerby’s friend rather than his own in all these horrid money

[040-122]dealings, had galled him; and now he had expressed himself in terms

[040-123]much stronger than he had intended to use.

[040-124]“As to you personally, Mark,” he said, coming back to the spot on

[040-125]which Robarts was standing, “I do not wish to say anything that shall

[040-126]annoy you.”

[040-127]“You have said quite enough, Lord Lufton.”

[040-128]“You cannot be surprised that I should be angry and indignant at the

[040-129]treatment I have received.”

[040-130]“You might, I think, have separated in your mind those who have

[040-131]wronged you, if there has been such wrong, from those who have

[040-132]only endeavoured to do your will and pleasure for you. That I, as

[040-133]a clergyman, have been very wrong in taking any part whatsoever

[040-134]in these matters, I am well aware. That as a man I have been

[040-135]outrageously foolish in lending my name to Mr. Sowerby, I also know

[040-136]well enough: it is perhaps as well that I should be told of this

[040-137]somewhat rudely; but I certainly did not expect the lesson to come

[040-138]from you.”

[040-139]“Well, there has been mischief enough. The question is, what we had

[040-140]better now both do?”

[040-141]“You have said what you mean to do. You will put the affair into the

[040-142]hands of your lawyer.”

[040-143]“Not with any object of exposing you.”

[040-144]“Exposing me, Lord Lufton! Why, one would think that I had had the

[040-145]handling of your money.”

[040-146]“You will misunderstand me. I think no such thing. But do you not

[040-147]know yourself that if legal steps be taken in this wretched affair,

[040-148]your arrangements with Sowerby will be brought to light?”

[040-149]“My arrangements with Sowerby will consist in paying or having to

[040-150]pay, on his account, a large sum of money, for which I have never had

[040-151]and shall never have any consideration whatever.”

[040-152]“And what will be said about this stall at Barchester?”

[040-153]“After the charge which you brought against me just now, I shall

[040-154]decline to accept it.”

[040-155]At this moment three or four other gentlemen entered the room, and

[040-156]the conversation between our two friends was stopped. They still

[040-157]remained standing near the fire, but for a few minutes neither of

[040-158]them said anything. Robarts was waiting till Lord Lufton should go

[040-159]away, and Lord Lufton had not yet said that which he had come to say.

[040-160]At last he spoke again, almost in a whisper: “I think it will be best

[040-161]to ask Sowerby to come to my rooms to-morrow, and I think also that

[040-162]you should meet him there.”

[040-163]“I do not see any necessity for my presence,” said Robarts. “It seems

[040-164]probable that I shall suffer enough for meddling with your affairs,

[040-165]and I will do so no more.”

[040-166]“Of course I cannot make you come; but I think it will be only just

[040-167]to Sowerby, and it will be a favour to me.”

[040-168]Robarts again walked up and down the room for half-a-dozen times,

[040-169]trying to resolve what it would most become him to do in the present

[040-170]emergency. If his name were dragged before the courts,—if he

[040-171]should be shown up in the public papers as having been engaged in

[040-172]accommodation bills, that would certainly be ruinous to him. He had

[040-173]already learned from Lord Lufton’s innuendoes what he might expect to

[040-174]hear as the public version of his share in these transactions! And

[040-175]then his wife,—how would she bear such exposure?

[040-176]“I will meet Mr. Sowerby at your rooms to-morrow, on one condition,”

[040-177]he at last said.

[040-178]“And what is that?”

[040-179]“That I receive your positive assurance that I am not suspected

[040-180]by you of having had any pecuniary interest whatever in any money

[040-181]matters with Mr. Sowerby, either as concerns your affairs or those of

[040-182]anybody else.”

[040-183]“I have never suspected you of any such thing. But I have thought

[040-184]that you were compromised with him.”
