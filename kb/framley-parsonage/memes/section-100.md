## Section 100

[100-001]And thus Lord Dumbello, the duke’s nominee, got in, as the duke’s

[100-002]nominee had done for very many years past. There was no Nemesis

[100-003]here—none as yet. Nevertheless, she with the lame foot will

[100-004]assuredly catch him, the duke, if it be that he deserve to be caught.

[100-005]With us his grace’s appearance has been so unfrequent that I think we

[100-006]may omit to make any further inquiry as to his concerns.

[100-007]One point, however, is worthy of notice, as showing the good sense

[100-008]with which we manage our affairs here in England. In an early portion

[100-009]of this story the reader was introduced to the interior of Gatherum

[100-010]Castle, and there saw Miss Dunstable entertained by the duke in the

[100-011]most friendly manner. Since those days the lady has become the duke’s

[100-012]neighbour, and has waged a war with him, which he probably felt to

[100-013]be very vexatious. But, nevertheless, on the next great occasion at

[100-014]Gatherum Castle, Doctor and Mrs. Thorne were among the visitors, and

[100-015]to no one was the duke more personally courteous than to his opulent

[100-016]neighbour, the late Miss Dunstable.

[100-017]CHAPTER XLVIII.

[100-018]HOW THEY WERE ALL MARRIED, HAD TWO CHILDREN,

[100-019]AND LIVED HAPPY EVER AFTER.

[100-020]Dear, affectionate, sympathetic readers, we have four couple of

[100-021]sighing lovers with whom to deal in this our last chapter, and I,

[100-022]as leader of the chorus, disdain to press you further with doubts

[100-023]as to the happiness of any of that quadrille. They were all made

[100-024]happy, in spite of that little episode which so lately took place at

[100-025]Barchester; and in telling of their happiness—shortly, as is now

[100-026]necessary—we will take them chronologically, giving precedence to

[100-027]those who first appeared at the hymeneal altar.

[100-028]In July, then, at the cathedral, by the father of the bride, assisted

[100-029]by his examining chaplain, Olivia Proudie, the eldest daughter of

[100-030]the Bishop of Barchester, was joined in marriage to the Rev. Tobias

[100-031]Tickler, incumbent of the Trinity district church in Bethnal Green.

[100-032]Of the bridegroom, in this instance, our acquaintance has been so

[100-033]short, that it is not, perhaps, necessary to say much. When coming to

[100-034]the wedding he proposed to bring his three darling children with him;

[100-035]but in this measure he was, I think prudently, stopped by advice,

[100-036]rather strongly worded, from his future valued mother-in-law. Mr.

[100-037]Tickler was not an opulent man, nor had he hitherto attained any

[100-038]great fame in his profession; but, at the age of forty-three he still

[100-039]had sufficient opportunity before him, and now that his merit has

[100-040]been properly viewed by high ecclesiastical eyes the refreshing dew

[100-041]of deserved promotion will no doubt fall upon him. The marriage was

[100-042]very smart, and Olivia carried herself through the trying ordeal with

[100-043]an excellent propriety of conduct.

[100-044]Up to that time, and even for a few days longer, there was doubt at

[100-045]Barchester as to that strange journey which Lord Dumbello undoubtedly

[100-046]did take to France. When a man so circumstanced will suddenly go to

[100-047]Paris, without notice given even to his future bride, people must

[100-048]doubt; and grave were the apprehensions expressed on this occasion by

[100-049]Mrs. Proudie, even at her child’s wedding-breakfast. “God bless you,

[100-050]my dear children,” she said, standing up at the head of her table as

[100-051]she addressed Mr. Tickler and his wife; “when I see your perfect

[100-052]happiness—perfect, that is, as far as human happiness can be made

[100-053]perfect in this vale of tears—and think of the terrible calamity

[100-054]which has fallen on our unfortunate neighbours, I cannot but

[100-055]acknowledge His infinite mercy and goodness. The Lord giveth, and the

[100-056]Lord taketh away.” By which she intended, no doubt, to signify that

[100-057]whereas Mr. Tickler had been given to her Olivia, Lord Dumbello had

[100-058]been taken away from the archdeacon’s Griselda. The happy couple

[100-059]then went in Mrs. Proudie’s carriage to the nearest railway station

[100-060]but one, and from thence proceeded to Malvern, and there spent the

[100-061]honeymoon.

[100-062]And a great comfort it was, I am sure, to Mrs. Proudie when

[100-063]authenticated tidings reached Barchester that Lord Dumbello had

[100-064]returned from Paris, and that the Hartletop-Grantly alliance was

[100-065]to be carried to its completion. She still, however, held her

[100-066]opinion—whether correctly or not, who shall say?—that the young

[100-067]lord had intended to escape. “The archdeacon has shown great firmness

[100-068]in the way in which he has done it,” said Mrs. Proudie; “but whether

[100-069]he has consulted his child’s best interests in forcing her into

[100-070]a marriage with an unwilling husband, I for one must take leave

[100-071]to doubt. But then, unfortunately, we all know how completely the

[100-072]archdeacon is devoted to worldly matters.”

[100-073]In this instance the archdeacon’s devotion to worldly matters was

[100-074]rewarded by that success which he no doubt desired. He did go up

[100-075]to London, and did see one or two of Lord Dumbello’s friends. This

[100-076]he did, not obtrusively, as though in fear of any falsehood or

[100-077]vacillation on the part of the viscount, but with that discretion

[100-078]and tact for which he has been so long noted. Mrs. Proudie declares

[100-079]that during the few days of his absence from Barsetshire he himself

[100-080]crossed to France and hunted down Lord Dumbello at Paris. As to this

[100-081]I am not prepared to say anything; but I am quite sure, as will be

[100-082]all those who knew the archdeacon, that he was not a man to see his

[100-083]daughter wronged as long as any measure remained by which such wrong

[100-084]might be avoided.

[100-085]But, be that as it may—that mooted question as to the archdeacon’s

[100-086]journey to Paris—Lord Dumbello was forthcoming at Plumstead on the

[100-087]5th of August, and went through his work like a man. The Hartletop

[100-088]family, when the alliance was found to be unavoidable, endeavoured to

[100-089]arrange that the wedding should be held at Hartletop Priory, in order

[100-090]that the clerical dust and dinginess of Barchester Close might not

[100-091]soil the splendour of the marriage gala doings; for, to tell the

[100-092]truth, the Hartletopians, as a rule, were not proud of their new

[100-093]clerical connections. But on this subject Mrs. Grantly was very

[100-094]properly inexorable; nor, when an attempt was made on the bride to

[100-095]induce her to throw over her mamma at the last moment and pronounce

[100-096]for herself that she would be married at the priory, was it attended

[100-097]with any success. The Hartletopians knew nothing of the Grantly fibre

[100-098]and calibre, or they would have made no such attempt. The marriage

[100-099]took place at Plumstead, and on the morning of the day Lord Dumbello

[100-100]posted over from Barchester to the rectory. The ceremony was

[100-101]performed by the archdeacon, without assistance, although the dean,

[100-102]and the precentor, and two other clergymen, were at the ceremony.

[100-103]Griselda’s propriety of conduct was quite equal to that of Olivia

[100-104]Proudie; indeed, nothing could exceed the statuesque grace and fine

[100-105]aristocratic bearing with which she carried herself on the occasion.

[100-106]The three or four words which the service required of her she said

[100-107]with ease and dignity; there was neither sobbing nor crying to

[100-108]disturb the work or embarrass her friends, and she signed her name in

[100-109]the church books as “Griselda Grantly” without a tremor—and without

[100-110]a regret.

[100-111]Mrs. Grantly kissed her and blessed her in the hall as she was about

[100-112]to step forward to her travelling carriage, leaning on her father’s

[100-113]arm, and the child put up her face to her mother for a last whisper.

[100-114]“Mamma,” she said, “I suppose Jane can put her hand at once on the

[100-115]moire antique when we reach Dover?” Mrs. Grantly smiled and nodded,

[100-116]and again blessed her child. There was not a tear shed—at least, not

[100-117]then—nor a sign of sorrow to cloud for a moment the gay splendour

[100-118]of the day. But the mother did bethink herself, in the solitude of

[100-119]her own room, of those last words, and did acknowledge a lack of

[100-120]something for which her heart had sighed. She had boasted to her

[100-121]sister that she had nothing to regret as to her daughter’s education;

[100-122]but now, when she was alone after her success, did she feel that she

[100-123]could still support herself with that boast? For, be it known, Mrs.

[100-124]Grantly had a heart within her bosom and a faith within her heart.

[100-125]The world, it is true, had pressed upon her sorely with all its

[100-126]weight of accumulated clerical wealth, but it had not utterly crushed

[100-127]her—not her, but only her child. For the sins of the father, are

[100-128]they not visited on the third and fourth generation?

[100-129]But if any such feeling of remorse did for awhile mar the fulness

[100-130]of Mrs. Grantly’s joy, it was soon dispelled by the perfect success

[100-131]of her daughter’s married life. At the end of the autumn the bride

[100-132]and bridegroom returned from their tour, and it was evident to all

[100-133]the circle at Hartletop Priory that Lord Dumbello was by no means

[100-134]dissatisfied with his bargain. His wife had been admired everywhere

[100-135]to the top of his bent. All the world at Ems, and at Baden, and

[100-136]at Nice, had been stricken by the stately beauty of the young

[100-137]viscountess. And then, too, her manner, style, and high dignity

[100-138]of demeanour altogether supported the reverential feeling which

[100-139]her grace and form at first inspired. She never derogated from her

[100-140]husband’s honour by the fictitious liveliness of gossip, or allowed

[100-141]any one to forget the peeress in the woman. Lord Dumbello soon found

[100-142]that his reputation for discretion was quite safe in her hands, and

[100-143]that there were no lessons as to conduct in which it was necessary

[100-144]that he should give instruction.

[100-145]Before the winter was over she had equally won the hearts of all

[100-146]the circle at Hartletop Priory. The duke was there and declared to

[100-147]the marchioness that Dumbello could not possibly have done better.

[100-148]“Indeed, I do not think he could,” said the happy mother. “She sees

[100-149]all that she ought to see, and nothing that she ought not.”

[100-150]And then, in London, when the season came, all men sang all manner

[100-151]of praises in her favour, and Lord Dumbello was made aware that he

[100-152]was reckoned among the wisest of his age. He had married a wife who

[100-153]managed everything for him, who never troubled him, whom no woman

[100-154]disliked, and whom every man admired. As for feast of reason and

[100-155]for flow of soul, is it not a question whether any such flows and

[100-156]feasts are necessary between a man and his wife? How many men can

[100-157]truly assert that they ever enjoy connubial flows of soul, or that

[100-158]connubial feasts of reason are in their nature enjoyable? But a

[100-159]handsome woman at the head of your table, who knows how to dress, and

[100-160]how to sit, and how to get in and out of her carriage—who will not

[100-161]disgrace her lord by her ignorance, or fret him by her coquetry, or

[100-162]disparage him by her talent—how beautiful a thing it is! For my own

[100-163]part I think that Griselda Grantly was born to be the wife of a great

[100-164]English peer.

[100-165]“After all, then,” said Miss Dunstable, speaking of Lady

[100-166]Dumbello—she was Mrs. Thorne at this time—“after all, there is some

[100-167]truth in what our quaint latter-day philosopher tells us—‘Great are

[100-168]thy powers, O Silence!’”

[100-169]The marriage of our old friends Dr. Thorne and Miss Dunstable was the

[100-170]third on the list, but that did not take place till the latter end of

[100-171]September. The lawyers on such an occasion had no inconsiderable work

[100-172]to accomplish, and though the lady was not coy, nor the gentleman

[100-173]slow, it was not found practicable to arrange an earlier wedding. The

[100-174]ceremony was performed at St. George’s, Hanover Square, and was not

[100-175]brilliant in any special degree. London at the time was empty, and

[100-176]the few persons whose presence was actually necessary were imported

[100-177]from the country for the occasion. The bride was given away by Dr.

[100-178]Easyman, and the two bridesmaids were ladies who had lived with Miss

[100-179]Dunstable as companions. Young Mr. Gresham and his wife were there,

[100-180]as was also Mrs. Harold Smith, who was not at all prepared to drop

[100-181]her old friend in her new sphere of life.
