## Section 80

[080-001]“Oh, of course she was,” says Mrs. Gresham, with that sort of

[080-002]acrimony which one pretty young woman so frequently expresses with

[080-003]reference to another. “But if one could always tell of a woman, as

[080-004]you say you can of a man, I should be able to tell of you. Now, I

[080-005]wonder whether you are a marrying woman? I have never been able to

[080-006]make up my mind yet.”

[080-007]Miss Dunstable remained silent for a few moments, as though she were

[080-008]at first minded to take the question as being, in some sort, one made

[080-009]in earnest; but then she attempted to laugh it off. “Well, I wonder

[080-010]at that,” said she, “as it was only the other day I told you how many

[080-011]offers I had refused.”

[080-012]“Yes; but you did not tell me whether any had been made that you

[080-013]meant to accept.”

[080-014]“None such was ever made to me. Talking of that, I shall never forget

[080-015]your cousin, the Honourable George.”

[080-016]“He is not my cousin.”

[080-017]“Well, your husband’s. It would not be fair to show a man’s letters;

[080-018]but I should like to show you his.”

[080-019]“You are determined, then, to remain single?”

[080-020]“I didn’t say that. But why do you cross-question me so?”

[080-021]“Because I think so much about you. I am afraid that you will become

[080-022]so afraid of men’s motives as to doubt that any one can be honest.

[080-023]And yet sometimes I think you would be a happier woman and a better

[080-024]woman, if you were married.”

[080-025]“To such an one as the Honourable George, for instance?”

[080-026]“No, not to such an one as him; you have probably picked out the

[080-027]worst.”

[080-028]“Or to Mr. Sowerby?”

[080-029]“Well, no; not to Mr. Sowerby, either. I would not have you marry any

[080-030]man that looked to you for your money principally.”

[080-031]“And how is it possible that I should expect any one to look to me

[080-032]principally for anything else? You don’t see my difficulty, my dear?

[080-033]If I had only five hundred a year, I might come across some decent

[080-034]middle-aged personage, like myself, who would like me, myself, pretty

[080-035]well, and would like my little income—pretty well also. He would not

[080-036]tell me any violent lie, and perhaps no lie at all. I should take to

[080-037]him in the same sort of way, and we might do very well. But, as it

[080-038]is, how is it possible that any disinterested person should learn to

[080-039]like me? How could such a man set about it? If a sheep have two

[080-040]heads, is not the fact of the two heads the first and, indeed, only

[080-041]thing which the world regards in that sheep? Must it not be so as a

[080-042]matter of course? I am a sheep with two heads. All this money which

[080-043]my father put together, and which has been growing since like grass

[080-044]under May showers, has turned me into an abortion. I am not the

[080-045]giantess eight feet high, or the dwarf that stands in the man’s

[080-046]hand,—”

[080-047]“Or the two-headed sheep—”

[080-048]“But I am the unmarried woman with—half a dozen millions of

[080-049]money—as I believe some people think. Under such circumstances have

[080-050]I a fair chance of getting my own sweet bit of grass to nibble, like

[080-051]any ordinary animal with one head? I never was very beautiful, and I

[080-052]am not more so now than I was fifteen years ago.”

[080-053]“I am quite sure it is not that which hinders it. You would not call

[080-054]yourself plain; and even plain women are married every day, and are

[080-055]loved, too, as well as pretty women.”

[080-056]“Are they? Well, we won’t say more about that; but I don’t expect a

[080-057]great many lovers on account of my beauty. If ever you hear of such

[080-058]an one, mind you tell me.”

[080-059]It was almost on Mrs. Gresham’s tongue to say that she did know of

[080-060]one such—meaning her uncle. But in truth, she did not know any such

[080-061]thing; nor could she boast to herself that she had good grounds for

[080-062]feeling that it was so—certainly none sufficient to justify her in

[080-063]speaking of it. Her uncle had said no word to her on the matter, and

[080-064]had been confused and embarrassed when the idea of such a marriage

[080-065]was hinted to him. But, nevertheless, Mrs. Gresham did think that

[080-066]each of these two was well inclined to love the other, and that they

[080-067]would be happier together than they would be single. The difficulty,

[080-068]however, was very great, for the doctor would be terribly afraid of

[080-069]being thought covetous in regard to Miss Dunstable’s money; and it

[080-070]would hardly be expected that she should be induced to make the first

[080-071]overture to the doctor.

[080-072]“My uncle would be the only man that I can think of that would be at

[080-073]all fit for you,” said Mrs. Gresham, boldly.

[080-074]“What, and rob poor Lady Scatcherd!” said Miss Dunstable.

[080-075]“Oh, very well. If you choose to make a joke of his name in that way,

[080-076]I have done.”

[080-077]“Why, God bless the girl! what does she want me to say? And as for

[080-078]joking, surely that is innocent enough. You’re as tender about the

[080-079]doctor as though he were a girl of seventeen.”

[080-080]“It’s not about him; but it’s such a shame to laugh at poor dear Lady

[080-081]Scatcherd. If she were to hear it she’d lose all comfort in having my

[080-082]uncle near her.”

[080-083]“And I’m to marry him, so that she may be safe with her friend!”

[080-084]“Very well; I have done.” And Mrs. Gresham, who had already got up

[080-085]from her seat, employed herself very sedulously in arranging flowers

[080-086]which had been brought in for the drawing-room tables. Thus they

[080-087]remained silent for a minute or two, during which she began to

[080-088]reflect that, after all, it might probably be thought that she also

[080-089]was endeavouring to catch the great heiress for her uncle.

[080-090]“And now you are angry with me,” said Miss Dunstable.

[080-091]“No, I am not.”

[080-092]“Oh, but you are. Do you think I’m such a fool as not to see when a

[080-093]person’s vexed? You wouldn’t have twitched that geranium’s head off

[080-094]if you’d been in a proper frame of mind.”

[080-095]“I don’t like that joke about Lady Scatcherd.”

[080-096]“And is that all, Mary? Now do try and be true, if you can. You

[080-097]remember the bishop? _Magna est veritas._”

[080-098]“The fact is you’ve got into such a way of being sharp, and saying

[080-099]sharp things among your friends up in London, that you can hardly

[080-100]answer a person without it.”

[080-101]“Can’t I? Dear, dear, what a Mentor you are, Mary! No poor lad that

[080-102]ever ran up from Oxford for a spree in town got so lectured for his

[080-103]dissipation and iniquities as I do. Well, I beg Dr. Thorne’s pardon,

[080-104]and Lady Scatcherd’s, and I won’t be sharp any more; and I will—let

[080-105]me see, what was it I was to do? Marry him myself, I believe; was not

[080-106]that it?”

[080-107]“No; you’re not half good enough for him.”

[080-108]“I know that. I’m quite sure of that. Though I am so sharp, I’m very

[080-109]humble. You can’t accuse me of putting any very great value on

[080-110]myself.”

[080-111]“Perhaps not as much as you ought to do—on yourself.”

[080-112]“Now, what do you mean, Mary? I won’t be bullied and teased, and have

[080-113]innuendos thrown out at me, because you’ve got something on your

[080-114]mind, and don’t quite dare to speak it out. If you have got anything

[080-115]to say, say it.”

[080-116]But Mrs. Gresham did not choose to say it at that moment. She held

[080-117]her peace, and went on arranging her flowers—now with a more

[080-118]satisfied air, and without destruction to the geraniums. And when she

[080-119]had grouped her bunches properly she carried the jar from one part of

[080-120]the room to another, backwards and forwards, trying the effect of the

[080-121]colours, as though her mind was quite intent upon her flowers, and

[080-122]was for the moment wholly unoccupied with any other subject.

[080-123]But Miss Dunstable was not the woman to put up with this. She sat

[080-124]silent in her place, while her friend made one or two turns about the

[080-125]room; and then she got up from her seat also. “Mary,” she said, “give

[080-126]over about those wretched bits of green branches and leave the jars

[080-127]where they are. You’re trying to fidget me into a passion.”

[080-128]“Am I?” said Mrs. Gresham, standing opposite to a big bowl, and

[080-129]putting her head a little on one side, as though she could better

[080-130]look at her handiwork in that position.

[080-131]“You know you are; and it’s all because you lack courage to speak

[080-132]out. You didn’t begin at me in this way for nothing.”

[080-133]“I do lack courage. That’s just it,” said Mrs. Gresham, still giving

[080-134]a twist here and a set there to some of the small sprigs which

[080-135]constituted the background of her bouquet. “I do lack courage—to

[080-136]have ill motives imputed to me. I was thinking of saying something,

[080-137]and I am afraid, and therefore I will not say it. And now, if you

[080-138]like, I will be ready to take you out in ten minutes.”

[080-139]But Miss Dunstable was not going to be put off in this way. And to

[080-140]tell the truth, I must admit that her friend Mrs. Gresham was not

[080-141]using her altogether well. She should either have held her peace on

[080-142]the matter altogether,—which would probably have been her wiser

[080-143]course,—or she should have declared her own ideas boldly, feeling

[080-144]secure in her own conscience as to her own motives. “I shall not stir

[080-145]from this room,” said Miss Dunstable, “till I have had this matter

[080-146]out with you. And as for imputations,—my imputing bad motives to

[080-147]you,—I don’t know how far you may be joking, and saying what you

[080-148]call sharp things to me; but you have no right to think that I should

[080-149]think evil of you. If you really do think so, it is treason to the

[080-150]love I have for you. If I thought that you thought so, I could not

[080-151]remain in the house with you. What! you are not able to know the

[080-152]difference which one makes between one’s real friends and one’s mock

[080-153]friends! I don’t believe it of you, and I know you are only striving

[080-154]to bully me.” And Miss Dunstable now took her turn of walking up and

[080-155]down the room.

[080-156]“Well, she shan’t be bullied,” said Mrs. Gresham, leaving her

[080-157]flowers, and putting her arm round her friend’s waist;—“at least,

[080-158]not here, in this house, although she is sometimes such a bully

[080-159]herself.”

[080-160]“Mary, you have gone too far about this to go back. Tell me what

[080-161]it was that was on your mind, and as far as it concerns me, I will

[080-162]answer you honestly.”

[080-163]Mrs. Gresham now began to repent that she had made her little

[080-164]attempt. That uttering of hints in a half-joking way was all very

[080-165]well, and might possibly bring about the desired result, without the

[080-166]necessity of any formal suggestion on her part; but now she was so

[080-167]brought to book that she must say something formal. She must commit

[080-168]herself to the expression of her own wishes, and to an expression

[080-169]also of an opinion as to what had been the wishes of her friend; and

[080-170]this she must do without being able to say anything as to the wishes

[080-171]of that third person.

[080-172]“Well,” she said, “I suppose you know what I meant.”

[080-173]“I suppose I did,” said Miss Dunstable; “but it is not at all the

[080-174]less necessary that you should say it out. I am not to commit myself

[080-175]by my interpretation of your thoughts, while you remain perfectly

[080-176]secure in having only hinted your own. I hate hints, as I do—the

[080-177]mischief. I go in for the bishop’s doctrine. _Magna est veritas._”

[080-178]“Well, I don’t know,” said Mrs. Gresham.
