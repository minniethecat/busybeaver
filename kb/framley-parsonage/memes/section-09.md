## Section 9

[009-001]I know what my own darling will think, and I know that she

[009-002]will not be pleased, and I must put off my defence till I

[009-003]return to her from this ogre-land,—if ever I do get back

[009-004]alive. But joking apart, Fanny, I think that I should

[009-005]have been wrong to stand out, when so much was said about

[009-006]it. I should have been seeming to take upon myself to

[009-007]sit in judgment upon the duke. I doubt if there be a

[009-008]single clergyman in the diocese, under fifty years of

[009-009]age, who would have refused the invitation under such

[009-010]circumstances,—unless it be Crawley, who is so mad on the

[009-011]subject that he thinks it almost wrong to take a walk out

[009-012]of his own parish.

[009-013]I must stay at Gatherum Castle over Sunday week—indeed,

[009-014]we only go there on Friday. I have written to Jones about

[009-015]the duties. I can make it up to him, as I know he wishes

[009-016]to go into Wales at Christmas. My wanderings will all be

[009-017]over then, and he may go for a couple of months if he

[009-018]pleases. I suppose you will take my classes in the school

[009-019]on Sunday, as well as your own; but pray make them have a

[009-020]good fire. If this is too much for you, make Mrs. Podgens

[009-021]take the boys. Indeed I think that will be better.

[009-022]Of course you will tell her ladyship of my whereabouts.

[009-023]Tell her from me, that as regards the bishop, as well as

[009-024]regarding another great personage, the colour has been

[009-025]laid on perhaps a little too thickly. Not that Lady Lufton

[009-026]would ever like him. Make her understand that my going to

[009-027]the duke’s has almost become a matter of conscience with

[009-028]me. I have not known how to make it appear that it would

[009-029]be right for me to refuse, without absolutely making a

[009-030]party matter of it. I saw that it would be said, that I,

[009-031]coming from Lady Lufton’s parish, could not go to the Duke

[009-032]of Omnium’s. This I did not choose.

[009-033]I find that I shall want a little more money before I

[009-034]leave here, five or ten pounds—say ten pounds. If you

[009-035]cannot spare it, get it from Davis. He owes me more than

[009-036]that, a good deal.

[009-037]And now, God bless and preserve you, my own love. Kiss my

[009-038]darling bairns for papa, and give them my blessing.

[009-039]Always and ever your own,

[009-040]M. R.

[009-041]And then there was written, on an outside scrap which was folded

[009-042]round the full-written sheet of paper, “Make it as smooth at Framley

[009-043]Court as possible.”

[009-044]However strong, and reasonable, and unanswerable the body of Mark’s

[009-045]letter may have been, all his hesitation, weakness, doubt, and fear,

[009-046]were expressed in this short postscript.

[009-047]CHAPTER V.

[009-048]AMANTIUM IRÆ AMORIS INTEGRATIO.

[009-049]And now, with my reader’s consent, I will follow the postman with

[009-050]that letter to Framley; not by its own circuitous route indeed, or by

[009-051]the same mode of conveyance; for that letter went into Barchester by

[009-052]the Courcy night mail-cart, which, on its road, passes through the

[009-053]villages of Uffley and Chaldicotes, reaching Barchester in time for

[009-054]the up mail-train to London. By that train, the letter was sent

[009-055]towards the metropolis as far as the junction of the Barset branch

[009-056]line, but there it was turned in its course, and came down again by

[009-057]the main line as far as Silverbridge; at which place, between six

[009-058]and seven in the morning, it was shouldered by the Framley footpost

[009-059]messenger, and in due course delivered at the Framley Parsonage

[009-060]exactly as Mrs. Robarts had finished reading prayers to the four

[009-061]servants. Or, I should say rather, that such would in its usual

[009-062]course have been that letter’s destiny. As it was, however, it

[009-063]reached Silverbridge on Sunday, and lay there till the Monday, as

[009-064]the Framley people have declined their Sunday post. And then again,

[009-065]when the letter was delivered at the parsonage, on that wet Monday

[009-066]morning, Mrs. Robarts was not at home. As we are all aware, she was

[009-067]staying with her ladyship at Framley Court.

[009-068]“Oh, but it’s mortial wet,” said the shivering postman as he handed

[009-069]in that and the vicar’s newspaper. The vicar was a man of the world,

[009-070]and took the _Jupiter_.

[009-071]“Come in, Robin postman, and warm theeself awhile,” said Jemima the

[009-072]cook, pushing a stool a little to one side, but still well in front

[009-073]of the big kitchen fire.

[009-074]“Well, I dudna jist know how it’ll be. The wery ’edges ’as eyes

[009-075]and tells on me in Silverbridge, if I so much as stops to pick a

[009-076]blackberry.”

[009-077]“There bain’t no hedges here, mon, nor yet no blackberries; so sit

[009-078]thee down and warm theeself. That’s better nor blackberries I’m

[009-079]thinking,” and she handed him a bowl of tea with a slice of buttered

[009-080]toast.

[009-081]Robin postman took the proffered tea, put his dripping hat on the

[009-082]ground, and thanked Jemima cook. “But I dudna jist know how it’ll

[009-083]be,” said he; “only it do pour so tarnation heavy.” Which among us,

[009-084]O my readers, could have withstood that temptation?

[009-085]Such was the circuitous course of Mark’s letter; but as it left

[009-086]Chaldicotes on Saturday evening, and reached Mrs. Robarts on the

[009-087]following morning, or would have done, but for that intervening

[009-088]Sunday, doing all its peregrinations during the night, it may be held

[009-089]that its course of transport was not inconveniently arranged. We,

[009-090]however, will travel by a much shorter route.

[009-091]Robin, in the course of his daily travels, passed, first the

[009-092]post-office at Framley, then the Framley Court back entrance, and

[009-093]then the vicar’s house, so that on this wet morning Jemima cook was

[009-094]not able to make use of his services in transporting this letter back

[009-095]to her mistress; for Robin had got another village before him,

[009-096]expectant of its letters.

[009-097]“Why didn’t thee leave it, mon, with Mr. Applejohn at the Court?” Mr.

[009-098]Applejohn was the butler who took the letter-bag. “Thee know’st as

[009-099]how missus was there.”

[009-100]And then Robin, mindful of the tea and toast, explained to her

[009-101]courteously how the law made it imperative on him to bring the letter

[009-102]to the very house that was indicated, let the owner of the letter be

[009-103]where she might; and he laid down the law very satisfactorily with

[009-104]sundry long-worded quotations. Not to much effect, however, for the

[009-105]housemaid called him an oaf; and Robin would decidedly have had the

[009-106]worst of it had not the gardener come in and taken his part. “They

[009-107]women knows nothin’, and understands nothin’,” said the gardener.

[009-108]“Give us hold of the letter. I’ll take it up to the house. It’s the

[009-109]master’s fist.” And then Robin postman went on one way, and the

[009-110]gardener, he went the other. The gardener never disliked an excuse

[009-111]for going up to the Court gardens, even on so wet a day as this.

[009-112]Mrs. Robarts was sitting over the drawing-room fire with Lady

[009-113]Meredith, when her husband’s letter was brought to her. The Framley

[009-114]Court letter-bag had been discussed at breakfast; but that was now

[009-115]nearly an hour since, and Lady Lufton, as was her wont, was away

[009-116]in her own room writing her own letters, and looking after her own

[009-117]matters: for Lady Lufton was a person who dealt in figures herself,

[009-118]and understood business almost as well as Harold Smith. And on that

[009-119]morning she also had received a letter which had displeased her not

[009-120]a little. Whence arose this displeasure neither Mrs. Robarts nor Lady

[009-121]Meredith knew; but her ladyship’s brow had grown black at breakfast

[009-122]time; she had bundled up an ominous-looking epistle into her bag

[009-123]without speaking of it, and had left the room immediately that

[009-124]breakfast was over.

[009-125]“There’s something wrong,” said Sir George.

[009-126]“Mamma does fret herself so much about Ludovic’s money matters,” said

[009-127]Lady Meredith. Ludovic was Lord Lufton,—Ludovic Lufton, Baron Lufton

[009-128]of Lufton, in the county of Oxfordshire.

[009-129]“And yet I don’t think Lufton gets much astray,” said Sir George,

[009-130]as he sauntered out of the room. “Well, Justy; we’ll put off going

[009-131]then till to-morrow; but remember, it must be the first train.”

[009-132]Lady Meredith said she would remember, and then they went into the

[009-133]drawing-room, and there Mrs. Robarts received her letter.

[009-134]Fanny, when she read it, hardly at first realized to herself the

[009-135]idea that her husband, the clergyman of Framley, the family clerical

[009-136]friend of Lady Lufton’s establishment, was going to stay with the

[009-137]Duke of Omnium. It was so thoroughly understood at Framley Court that

[009-138]the duke and all belonging to him was noxious and damnable. He was a

[009-139]Whig, he was a bachelor, he was a gambler, he was immoral in every

[009-140]way, he was a man of no church principle, a corrupter of youth, a

[009-141]sworn foe of young wives, a swallower up of small men’s patrimonies;

[009-142]a man whom mothers feared for their sons, and sisters for their

[009-143]brothers; and worse again, whom fathers had cause to fear for their

[009-144]daughters, and brothers for their sisters;—a man who, with his

[009-145]belongings, dwelt, and must dwell, poles asunder from Lady Lufton and

[009-146]her belongings!

[009-147]And it must be remembered that all these evil things were fully

[009-148]believed by Mrs. Robarts. Could it really be that her husband was

[009-149]going to dwell in the halls of Apollyon, to shelter himself beneath

[009-150]the wings of this very Lucifer? A cloud of sorrow settled upon her

[009-151]face, and then she read the letter again very slowly, not omitting

[009-152]the tell-tale postscript.

[009-153]“Oh, Justinia!” at last she said.

[009-154]“What, have you got bad news, too?”

[009-155]“I hardly know how to tell you what has occurred. There; I suppose

[009-156]you had better read it;” and she handed her husband’s epistle to Lady

[009-157]Meredith,—keeping back, however, the postscript.

[009-158]“What on earth will her ladyship say now?” said Lady Meredith, as she

[009-159]folded the paper, and replaced it in the envelope.

[009-160]“What had I better do, Justinia? how had I better tell her?” And then

[009-161]the two ladies put their heads together, bethinking themselves how

[009-162]they might best deprecate the wrath of Lady Lufton. It had been

[009-163]arranged that Mrs. Robarts should go back to the parsonage after

[009-164]lunch, and she had persisted in her intention after it had been

[009-165]settled that the Merediths were to stay over that evening. Lady

[009-166]Meredith now advised her friend to carry out this determination

[009-167]without saying anything about her husband’s terrible iniquities, and

[009-168]then to send the letter up to Lady Lufton as soon as she reached the

[009-169]parsonage. “Mamma will never know that you received it here,” said

[009-170]Lady Meredith.

[009-171]But Mrs. Robarts would not consent to this. Such a course seemed to

[009-172]her to be cowardly. She knew that her husband was doing wrong; she

[009-173]felt that he knew it himself; but still it was necessary that she

[009-174]should defend him. However terrible might be the storm, it must break

[009-175]upon her own head. So she at once went up and tapped at Lady Lufton’s

[009-176]private door; and as she did so Lady Meredith followed her.

[009-177]“Come in,” said Lady Lufton, and the voice did not sound soft and

[009-178]pleasant. When they entered, they found her sitting at her little

[009-179]writing table, with her head resting on her arm, and that letter

[009-180]which she had received that morning was lying open on the table

[009-181]before her. Indeed there were two letters now there, one from a

[009-182]London lawyer to herself, and the other from her son to that London

[009-183]lawyer. It needs only be explained that the subject of those letters

[009-184]was the immediate sale of that outlying portion of the Lufton

[009-185]property in Oxfordshire, as to which Mr. Sowerby once spoke. Lord

[009-186]Lufton had told the lawyer that the thing must be done at once,

[009-187]adding that his friend Robarts would have explained the whole affair

[009-188]to his mother. And then the lawyer had written to Lady Lufton, as

[009-189]indeed was necessary; but unfortunately Lady Lufton had not hitherto

[009-190]heard a word of the matter.
