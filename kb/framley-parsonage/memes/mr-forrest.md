# Mr. Forrest

Mr. Forrest is the manager of a bank in Barchester, a man whose professional discretion and practical good sense make him an unlikely but crucial ally to [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]] during the young vicar's entanglement with [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]'s financial machinations (025-176).

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Forrest's narrative role is to provide a counterweight to the moral and financial chaos that Sowerby's bill represents. When Mark Robarts, desperate for advice, visits the bank and confides his predicament, Forrest's calm, worldly response — "It's never very convenient to hand out four hundred pounds at a blow. Nobody will expect you to pay it!" — offers both practical relief and a subtle lesson in the ways of the commercial world (026-004). His role is limited but significant: he is the professional man who understands how money actually works, as distinct from the landed gentlemen who merely spend it.

Forrest appears again at critical points in Robarts's financial journey: when Robarts remits money with Forrest's assistance (044-160), and later when Robarts acknowledges a continuing obligation of "a hundred and fifty pounds" owed to him (070-035). Through it all, Forrest remains the steady, unflappable figure of commercial Barchester.

## Character Traits

- **Professional discretion**: Forrest understands the delicate nature of a clergyman's financial embarrassment and handles it with tact, assuring Robarts that his secret is safe — "I will give you plenty of time" (026-017).
- **Worldly wisdom**: Unlike the anxious Robarts, Forrest has seen such situations before. "That's Sowerby, of course," he says at once upon hearing the story; "all his friends go through that, sooner or later" (025-181). His knowledge of Sowerby's habits is matter-of-fact rather than judgmental.
- **Practical helpfulness**: Forrest does not moralise or refuse assistance. Instead, he explains the practical mechanics of bill renewal — interest, commissions, and the eventual necessity of payment — and offers to give Robarts warning when the bill reaches Barchester (026-013) (026-017).

## Relationships

- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: The young vicar of Framley comes to Forrest as a client seeking advice about a financial entanglement. Forrest treats him with professional courtesy and a degree of protective concern that goes beyond mere business obligation (025-176) (026-017).
- **[[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]**: Though Forrest and Sowerby do not interact directly in the text, Forrest knows Sowerby's ways intimately — he recognises the pattern of the accommodation bill immediately and understands precisely how Sowerby operates (025-181).

## Key Scenes

- **The confidential interview (Chapters 25–26)**: Mark Robarts visits Forrest at the Barchester bank and reveals the whole story of Sowerby's bill. Forrest's calm, almost casual response — the bill is "probably in London," and when it arrives he will "give you plenty of time" — transforms Robarts's terror into manageable anxiety. "Mark's heart was somewhat lighter as he left the bank" (026-020). This scene is one of the novel's quiet turning-points: the moment when Robarts learns that financial difficulty, faced openly, is less terrifying than financial secrecy (025-176) (026-001) (026-013) (026-017).
- **The remittance (Chapter 44)**: Forrest assists Robarts in remitting money, a practical step toward resolving his obligations (044-160).
- **The continuing debt (Chapter 70)**: Robarts acknowledges an ongoing debt to Forrest of a hundred and fifty pounds — evidence that Forrest's assistance has been sustained over time, not merely a single intervention (070-035).

## Social & Historical Context

Mr. Forrest represents the rising professional class of Victorian England — the bank manager whose competence and discretion made him indispensable to the commercial life of a county town. Trollope, who spent his career in the Post Office and understood institutional hierarchies intimately, draws Forrest with the respect due to a man who knows his business. The bank manager occupies a social position between the gentry he serves and the tradesmen he superintends — not quite a gentleman, yet privy to the most private affairs of gentlemen. Forrest's combination of professional knowledge and personal discretion reflects the Victorian ideal of the trustworthy man of business, a figure upon whom the fragile edifice of credit — both financial and social — depended.
