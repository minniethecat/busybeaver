## Section 28

[028-001]“Just explain to her that any young lady who talks so much to the

[028-002]same young gentleman will certainly be observed—that people will

[028-003]accuse her of setting her cap at Lord Lufton. Not that I suspect

[028-004]her—I give her credit for too much proper feeling: I know her

[028-005]education has been good, and her principles are upright. But people

[028-006]will talk of her. You must understand that, Fanny, as well as I do.”

[028-007]Fanny could not help meditating whether proper feeling, education,

[028-008]and upright principles did forbid Lucy Robarts to fall in love with

[028-009]Lord Lufton; but her doubts on this subject, if she held any, were

[028-010]not communicated to her ladyship. It had never entered into her mind

[028-011]that a match was possible between Lord Lufton and Lucy Robarts, nor

[028-012]had she the slightest wish to encourage it now that the idea was

[028-013]suggested to her. On such a matter she could sympathize with Lady

[028-014]Lufton, though she did not completely agree with her as to the

[028-015]expediency of any interference. Nevertheless, she at once offered to

[028-016]speak to Lucy.

[028-017]“I don’t think that Lucy has any idea in her head upon the subject,”

[028-018]said Mrs. Robarts.

[028-019]“I dare say not—I don’t suppose she has. But young ladies sometimes

[028-020]allow themselves to fall in love, and then to think themselves very

[028-021]ill-used, just because they have had no idea in their head.”

[028-022]“I will put her on her guard if you wish it, Lady Lufton.”

[028-023]“Exactly, my dear; that is just it. Put her on her guard—that is all

[028-024]that is necessary. She is a dear, good, clever girl, and it would be

[028-025]very sad if anything were to interrupt our comfortable way of getting

[028-026]on with her.”

[028-027]Mrs. Robarts knew to a nicety the exact meaning of this threat. If

[028-028]Lucy would persist in securing to herself so much of Lord Lufton’s

[028-029]time and attention, her visits to Framley Court must become less

[028-030]frequent. Lady Lufton would do much, very much, indeed, for her

[028-031]friends at the parsonage; but not even for them could she permit her

[028-032]son’s prospects in life to be endangered.

[028-033]There was nothing more said between them, and Mrs. Robarts got up to

[028-034]take her leave, having promised to speak to Lucy.

[028-035]“You manage everything so perfectly,” said Lady Lufton, as she

[028-036]pressed Mrs. Robarts’ hand, “that I am quite at ease now that I find

[028-037]you will agree with me.” Mrs. Robarts did not exactly agree with her

[028-038]ladyship, but she hardly thought it worth her while to say so.

[028-039]Mrs. Robarts immediately started off on her walk to her own home, and

[028-040]when she had got out of the grounds into the road, where it makes a

[028-041]turn towards the parsonage, nearly opposite to Podgens’ shop, she

[028-042]saw Lord Lufton on horseback, and Lucy standing beside him. It was

[028-043]already nearly five o’clock, and it was getting dusk; but as she

[028-044]approached, or rather as she came suddenly within sight of them, she

[028-045]could see that they were in close conversation. Lord Lufton’s face

[028-046]was towards her, and his horse was standing still; he was leaning

[028-047]over towards his companion, and the whip, which he held in his right

[028-048]hand, hung almost over her arm and down her back, as though his hand

[028-049]had touched and perhaps rested on her shoulder. She was standing by

[028-050]his side, looking up into his face, with one gloved hand resting on

[028-051]the horse’s neck. Mrs. Robarts, as she saw them, could not but own

[028-052]that there might be cause for Lady Lufton’s fears.

[028-053]But then Lucy’s manner, as Mrs. Robarts approached, was calculated to

[028-054]dissipate any such fears, and to prove that there was no ground for

[028-055]them. She did not move from her position, or allow her hand to drop,

[028-056]or show that she was in any way either confused or conscious. She

[028-057]stood her ground, and when her sister-in-law came up was smiling and

[028-058]at her ease.

[028-059]“Lord Lufton wants me to learn to ride,” said she.

[028-060]“To learn to ride!” said Fanny, not knowing what answer to make to

[028-061]such a proposition.

[028-062]“Yes,” said he. “This horse would carry her beautifully: he is as

[028-063]quiet as a lamb, and I made Gregory go out with him yesterday with a

[028-064]sheet hanging over him like a lady’s habit, and the man got up into a

[028-065]lady’s saddle.”

[028-066]“I think Gregory would make a better hand of it than Lucy.”

[028-067]“The horse cantered with him as though he had carried a lady all his

[028-068]life, and his mouth is like velvet; indeed, that is his fault—he is

[028-069]too soft-mouthed.”

[028-070]“I suppose that’s the same sort of thing as a man being

[028-071]soft-hearted,” said Lucy.

[028-072]“Exactly: you ought to ride them both with a very light hand. They

[028-073]are difficult cattle to manage, but very pleasant when you know how

[028-074]to do it.”

[028-075]“But you see I don’t know how to do it,” said Lucy.

[028-076]“As regards the horse, you will learn in two days, and I do hope you

[028-077]will try. Don’t you think it will be an excellent thing for her, Mrs.

[028-078]Robarts?”

[028-079]“Lucy has got no habit,” said Mrs. Robarts, making use of the excuse

[028-080]common on all such occasions.

[028-081]“There is one of Justinia’s in the house, I know. She always leaves

[028-082]one here, in order that she may be able to ride when she comes.”

[028-083]“She would not think of taking such a liberty with Lady Meredith’s

[028-084]things,” said Fanny, almost frightened at the proposal.

[028-085]“Of course it is out of the question, Fanny,” said Lucy, now speaking

[028-086]rather seriously. “In the first place, I would not take Lord Lufton’s

[028-087]horse; in the second place, I would not take Lady Meredith’s habit;

[028-088]in the third place, I should be a great deal too much frightened;

[028-089]and, lastly, it is quite out of the question for a great many other

[028-090]very good reasons.”

[028-091]“Nonsense,” said Lord Lufton.

[028-092]“A great deal of nonsense,” said Lucy, laughing, “but all of it

[028-093]of Lord Lufton’s talking. But we are getting cold—are we not,

[028-094]Fanny?—so we will wish you good-night.” And then the two ladies

[028-095]shook hands with him, and walked on towards the parsonage.

[028-096]That which astonished Mrs. Robarts the most in all this was the

[028-097]perfectly collected manner in which Lucy spoke and conducted herself.

[028-098]This connected, as she could not but connect it, with the air of

[028-099]chagrin with which Lord Lufton received Lucy’s decision, made it

[028-100]manifest to Mrs. Robarts that Lord Lufton was annoyed because Lucy

[028-101]would not consent to learn to ride; whereas she, Lucy herself, had

[028-102]given her refusal in a firm and decided tone, as though resolved that

[028-103]nothing more should be said about it.

[028-104]They walked on in silence for a minute or two, till they reached the

[028-105]parsonage gate, and then Lucy said, laughing, “Can’t you fancy me

[028-106]sitting on that great big horse? I wonder what Lady Lufton would say

[028-107]if she saw me there, and his lordship giving me my first lesson?”

[028-108]“I don’t think she would like it,” said Fanny.

[028-109]“I’m sure she would not. But I will not try her temper in that

[028-110]respect. Sometimes I fancy that she does not even like seeing Lord

[028-111]Lufton talking to me.”

[028-112]“She does not like it, Lucy, when she sees him flirting with you.”

[028-113]This Mrs. Robarts said rather gravely, whereas Lucy had been speaking

[028-114]in a half-bantering tone. As soon as even the word flirting was out

[028-115]of Fanny’s mouth, she was conscious that she had been guilty of

[028-116]an injustice in using it. She had wished to say something which

[028-117]would convey to her sister-in-law an idea of what Lady Lufton would

[028-118]dislike; but in doing so, she had unintentionally brought against her

[028-119]an accusation.

[028-120]“Flirting, Fanny!” said Lucy, standing still in the path, and looking

[028-121]up into her companion’s face with all her eyes. “Do you mean to say

[028-122]that I have been flirting with Lord Lufton?”

[028-123]“I did not say that.”

[028-124]“Or that I have allowed him to flirt with me?”

[028-125]“I did not mean to shock you, Lucy.”

[028-126]“What did you mean, Fanny?”

[028-127]“Why, just this: that Lady Lufton would not be pleased if he paid you

[028-128]marked attentions, and if you received them;—just like that affair

[028-129]of the riding; it was better to decline it.”

[028-130]“Of course I declined it; of course I never dreamt of accepting such

[028-131]an offer. Go riding about the country on his horses! What have I

[028-132]done, Fanny, that you should suppose such a thing?”

[028-133]“You have done nothing, dearest.”

[028-134]“Then why did you speak as you did just now?”

[028-135]“Because I wished to put you on your guard. You know, Lucy, that I

[028-136]do not intend to find fault with you; but you may be sure, as a rule,

[028-137]that intimate friendships between young gentlemen and young ladies

[028-138]are dangerous things.”

[028-139]They then walked up to the hall-door in silence. When they had

[028-140]reached it, Lucy stood in the doorway instead of entering it, and

[028-141]said, “Fanny, let us take another turn together, if you are not

[028-142]tired.”

[028-143]“No, I’m not tired.”

[028-144]“It will be better that I should understand you at once,”—and then

[028-145]they again moved away from the house. “Tell me truly now, do you

[028-146]think that Lord Lufton and I have been flirting?”

[028-147]“I do think that he is a little inclined to flirt with you.”

[028-148]“And Lady Lufton has been asking you to lecture me about it?”

[028-149]Poor Mrs. Robarts hardly knew what to say. She thought well of all

[028-150]the persons concerned, and was very anxious to behave well by all

[028-151]of them;—was particularly anxious to create no ill feeling, and

[028-152]wished that everybody should be comfortable, and on good terms with

[028-153]everybody else. But yet the truth was forced out of her when this

[028-154]question was asked so suddenly.

[028-155]“Not to lecture you, Lucy,” she said at last.

[028-156]“Well, to preach to me, or to talk to me, or to give me a lesson;

[028-157]to say something that shall drive me to put my back up against Lord

[028-158]Lufton?”

[028-159]“To caution you, dearest. Had you heard what she said, you would

[028-160]hardly have felt angry with Lady Lufton.”

[028-161]“Well, to caution me. It is such a pleasant thing for a girl to be

[028-162]cautioned against falling in love with a gentleman, especially when

[028-163]the gentleman is very rich, and a lord, and all that sort of thing!”

[028-164]“Nobody for a moment attributes anything wrong to you, Lucy.”

[028-165]“Anything wrong—no. I don’t know whether it would be anything wrong,

[028-166]even if I were to fall in love with him. I wonder whether they

[028-167]cautioned Griselda Grantly when she was here? I suppose when young

[028-168]lords go about, all the girls are cautioned as a matter of course.

[028-169]Why do they not label him ‘dangerous’?” And then again they were

[028-170]silent for a moment, as Mrs. Robarts did not feel that she had

[028-171]anything further to say on the matter.

[028-172]“‘Poison’ should be the word with any one so fatal as Lord Lufton;

[028-173]and he ought to be made up of some particular colour, for fear he

[028-174]should be swallowed in mistake.”

[028-175]“You will be safe, you see,” said Fanny, laughing, “as you have been

[028-176]specially cautioned as to this individual bottle.”

[028-177]“Ah! but what’s the use of that after I have had so many doses? It is

[028-178]no good telling me about it now, when the mischief is done,—after I

[028-179]have been taking it for I don’t know how long. Dear! dear! dear! and

[028-180]I regarded it as a mere commonplace powder, good for the complexion.

[028-181]I wonder whether it’s too late, or whether there’s any antidote?”

[028-182]Mrs. Robarts did not always quite understand her sister-in-law, and

[028-183]now she was a little at a loss. “I don’t think there’s much harm done

[028-184]yet on either side,” she said, cheerily.

[028-185]“Ah! you don’t know, Fanny. But I do think that if I die—as I

[028-186]shall—I feel I shall;—and if so, I do think it ought to go very

[028-187]hard with Lady Lufton. Why didn’t she label him ‘dangerous’ in time?”

[028-188]And then they went into the house and up to their own rooms.
