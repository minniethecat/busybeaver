# Miss Dunstable

Miss Dunstable is the great heiress of Barsetshire, a woman whose immense fortune attracts suitors and schemers alike, yet whose independent spirit and sharp tongue set her apart from the conventional marriage-market heroine. Trollope presents her as a figure of unusual candour and self-possession — a woman who understands exactly how her wealth shapes the behaviour of those around her, and who refuses to be either flattered or deceived by it (007-007).

## Role in the Novel

Miss Dunstable's fortune — derived from her father's patent medicine, the celebrated "Oil of Lebanon" — places her at the centre of the novel's satirical examination of money and marriage. While characters like [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]] and various clergymen circle her with thinly veiled mercenary intent, Miss Dunstable remains clear-eyed about their motivations. When told of a clergyman's interest, she does not affect to be flattered, instead responding with the ironic observation that this is "a clergyman hunt, while he is himself the hunted" — turning the predatory logic of the marriage market back upon her would-be suitors (005-149).

She appears primarily at social gatherings — Mrs. Proudie's gatherings, the Chaldicotes set, and various dinner parties — where her presence is sought not merely for her wealth but for the liveliness she brings to conversation. Mrs. Harold Smith in particular seeks to attach Miss Dunstable to her circle, seeing her as a social asset as well as a financial one (008-075). It is a measure of Miss Dunstable's own social intelligence that she permits herself to be managed while remaining unmistakably her own woman; she will go where she is wanted, but on her own terms (005-158).

## Character Traits

- **Independence**: Miss Dunstable speaks plainly and refuses to be managed by others. When cornered by a persistent suitor, she does not simper or evade but directly confronts the situation (006-071). She "thinks that she will be able" to manage her own affairs — and the narrative invites us to conclude that she is right (007-003).
- **Self-knowledge**: She is acutely aware that her fortune, not her person, attracts attention. Rather than resenting this, she treats it with ironic detachment, using her position to observe and critique the society that pursues her (006-049). When Mark Robarts is "affected by the companionship of Miss Dunstable," the reader understands that he is affected as much by the idea of her fortune as by her conversation (006-049).
- **Wit**: Her conversation is marked by sharp rejoinders. When the topic of hunting arises, she cuts through the sporting enthusiasm with the dry observation that nobody had actually caught any foxes — a deflation of masculine pretension that is characteristic of her social role (008-114). When asked about the hunting, she turns the question back with the query "that's hunting, is it?" — a technique of ironic interrogation that leaves her interlocutors uncertain whether they are being mocked or merely questioned (008-107).

## Relationships

- **[[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]**: The financially desperate MP regards Miss Dunstable as a potential solution to his money troubles. She appears to take an amused interest in him, understanding his game while seeming to enjoy the spectacle (011-180).
- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: The young vicar finds himself drawn into the Dunstable orbit during his visits to Chaldicotes. Miss Dunstable treats him with friendly interest, and Robarts, like others, finds her company unexpectedly pleasant. His discomfort at being caught up in her conversation — at once drawn in and made aware of his own social naivety — is characteristic of the effect she has on the men who encounter her (006-049) (012-081).
- **[[mrs-harold-smith|Mrs. Harold Smith]]**: A social ally who champions Miss Dunstable's presence at gatherings and facilitates her integration into the Chaldicotes set. The two women, both intelligent and socially adept, form a kind of alliance of wit against the heavier personalities of Barsetshire society (008-075).

## Key Scenes

- **The clergyman hunt (Chapter 5)**: Miss Dunstable's first significant appearance establishes her position as quarry and observer simultaneously — she is being hunted, but she sees the hunters coming. Her ironic self-awareness transforms what could be a victim's position into one of power (005-149) (005-158).
- **Conversation with Mark Robarts (Chapter 6)**: A sustained dialogue in which Miss Dunstable probes Robarts's assumptions about her and about the clerical life he represents. "My dear Miss Dunstable, had I known that — but I really..." Robarts stammers, and in his hesitation the reader sees the power dynamic at work: the heiress, not the clergyman, controls the conversation (006-015) (006-042) (006-071).
- **The Chaldicotes gathering (Chapter 8)**: At the centre of a country-house party, Miss Dunstable exercises her social power, dispensing wit and deflating pretensions with equal ease. She announces that she will make a man "her poet" and keep him in company — a half-mocking declaration of the social authority her fortune confers, transforming the traditional dynamic of male patronage into something altogether more ambiguous (008-130) (008-107) (008-114).

## Social & Historical Context

Miss Dunstable belongs to a recognisable Victorian type: the heiress whose wealth derives from trade rather than land, and whose social position is therefore powerful but precarious. The "Oil of Lebanon" that made her father's fortune was a patent medicine — a product of the commercial middle class, not the landed gentry. Her presence in Barsetshire drawing-rooms thus represents the penetration of new money into old hierarchies, and her independence — she is unmarried, unencumbered, and entirely self-possessed — challenges the patriarchal assumptions that structure those hierarchies.

The figure of the heiress was a staple of nineteenth-century fiction, but Trollope's treatment of the type is distinguished by his refusal to sentimentalise. Miss Dunstable is not a damsel awaiting rescue, nor a trapped soul yearning for liberation; she is a woman who has mastered the rules of the game and plays it with amused competence. Her wit is not a decorative accomplishment but a defensive weapon, honed by years of watching men calculate the value of her fortune. The novel's satirical gaze cuts both ways — at the mercenary suitors who pursue her, certainly, but also at a society that has structured the relations between men and women so thoroughly around money that genuine feeling is almost impossible to distinguish from calculation (007-007) (007-010).

## Critical Commentary

> Miss Dunstable has been read as one of Trollope's most modern creations — a character whose independence and self-possession anticipate the "New Woman" of later Victorian fiction while remaining plausibly embedded in the social world of the 1850s. Her fortune is both her cage and her key: it attracts the predators she must fend off, but it also buys her the freedom to fend them off on her own terms. In this respect she is a more radical figure than the novel's ostensible heroine, Lucy Robarts, whose triumph depends upon a man's choice; Miss Dunstable's triumph is that she does not need to be chosen at all.
