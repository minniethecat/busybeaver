# Ecclesiastical Living

A Church of England endowed parochial office providing a clergyman with income, residence, and pastoral authority over a parish. In the Victorian era depicted in *Framley Parsonage*, church livings were a central mechanism of clerical career advancement and patronage.

## Definition

A living (or benefice) was a church office endowed with property and income, typically comprising a parsonage house, glebe lands, and tithe revenues. The holder of a living was entitled to its income for life, subject to performing the spiritual duties of the parish. Appointments to livings were controlled by patrons — landowners, bishops, or colleges — who held the right of presentation (known as the advowson). The value of livings varied enormously: "the living of Framley was worth £900 a year" (001-118), a comfortable sum for a country clergyman, while others provided barely enough to sustain a gentleman.

## In the Novel

The living of Framley is central to the plot, as it defines the social and financial position of Mark Robarts, the young vicar. The living "was in the gift of Lady Lufton" (001-112), making Robarts dependent on her patronage from the very beginning. He "had obtained a living at an age when other young clergymen were barely beginning to hope for one" (007-100), a stroke of fortune that shaped his character and his subsequent difficulties. The novel explores the tensions between gratitude to a patron and personal independence: Robarts chafes under Lady Lufton's expectations despite owing his position entirely to her.

## Historical Context

In the Victorian Church of England, the system of private patronage was increasingly controversial. Reformers attacked it as simony and argued that spiritual offices should not be treated as property. Yet for gentlemen like Mark Robarts, a good living was the essential foundation of a clerical career — without one, a clergyman could not marry or maintain his social standing. Trollope, who knew the system intimately from his work at the Post Office and his own failed parliamentary ambitions, used the living system to explore broader themes of obligation, independence, and the uneasy relationship between Church and aristocracy.
