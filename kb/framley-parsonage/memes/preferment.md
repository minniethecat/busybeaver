# Preferment

Preferment was the term used in the Victorian Church of England for promotion to a higher ecclesiastical office — a more valuable living, a cathedral position, a bishopric, or a deanery. Unlike secular career advancement, preferment depended almost entirely on patronage rather than merit, making it one of the most discussed and resented features of the established Church.

## Definition

To be preferred was to be selected, by a patron, for a better position. The term carried connotations of favor and partiality that its users were keenly aware of. A clergyman might spend years hoping for preferment, cultivating the right connections, and writing careful letters to bishops and influential laymen. The moral hazard was obvious: a system that rewarded connection over devotion risked filling the Church's best positions with the worst men — a danger Trollope explores with characteristic ambivalence.

## In the Novel

The "poor piece of preferment" that falls to the dean (031-107) illustrates how even advancement could disappoint. Mark Robarts himself receives a form of preferment when Lady Lufton grants him the Framley living, but his ambition is stirred by his association with worldly men who promise further advancement: "petitioning for Church preferment to a man who should" (040-107) become his connection to power. The novel shows preferment as a double-edged sword: desired by every clergyman, yet morally compromising for those who pursue it too eagerly.

## Historical Context

Trollope's own experience gave him exceptional insight into the preferment system. His mother, the novelist Frances Trollope, was a tireless campaigner for her son's advancement at the Post Office, and Anthony himself spent years navigating the politics of preferment in both church and state. His depiction of clerical ambition is neither cynical nor naive — he understood that good men could desire promotion without being corrupted by the pursuit, and that bad men could rise through a system that was fundamentally unjust.
