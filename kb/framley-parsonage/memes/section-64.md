## Section 64

[064-001]On the morning after the party, Lady Lufton and Griselda had

[064-002]breakfasted together as usual, but each felt that the manner of the

[064-003]other was altered. Lady Lufton thought that her young friend was

[064-004]somewhat less attentive, and perhaps less meek in her demeanour, than

[064-005]usual; and Griselda felt that Lady Lufton was less affectionate. Very

[064-006]little, however, was said between them, and Lady Lufton expressed no

[064-007]surprise when Griselda begged to be left alone at home, instead of

[064-008]accompanying her ladyship when the carriage came to the door.

[064-009]Nobody called in Bruton Street that afternoon—no one, at least, was

[064-010]let in—except the archdeacon. He came there late in the day, and

[064-011]remained with his daughter till Lady Lufton returned. Then he took

[064-012]his leave, with more abruptness than was usual with him, and without

[064-013]saying anything special to account for the duration of his visit.

[064-014]Neither did Griselda say anything special; and so the evening wore

[064-015]away, each feeling in some unconscious manner that she was on less

[064-016]intimate terms with the other than had previously been the case.

[064-017]On the next day also Griselda would not go out, but at four o’clock

[064-018]a servant brought a letter to her from Mount Street. Her mother had

[064-019]arrived in London and wished to see her at once. Mrs. Grantly sent

[064-020]her love to Lady Lufton, and would call at half-past five, or at any

[064-021]later hour at which it might be convenient for Lady Lufton to see

[064-022]her. Griselda was to stay and dine in Mount Street; so said the

[064-023]letter. Lady Lufton declared that she would be very happy to see

[064-024]Mrs. Grantly at the hour named; and then, armed with this message,

[064-025]Griselda started for her mother’s lodgings.

[064-026]“I’ll send the carriage for you,” said Lady Lufton. “I suppose about

[064-027]ten will do.”

[064-028]“Thank you,” said Griselda, “that will do very nicely;” and then she

[064-029]went.

[064-030]Exactly at half-past five Mrs. Grantly was shown into Lady Lufton’s

[064-031]drawing-room. Her daughter did not come with her, and Lady Lufton

[064-032]could see by the expression of her friend’s face that business was

[064-033]to be discussed. Indeed, it was necessary that she herself should

[064-034]discuss business, for Mrs. Grantly must now be told that the family

[064-035]treaty could not be ratified. The gentleman declined the alliance,

[064-036]and poor Lady Lufton was uneasy in her mind at the nature of the task

[064-037]before her.

[064-038]“Your coming up has been rather unexpected,” said Lady Lufton, as

[064-039]soon as her friend was seated on the sofa.

[064-040]“Yes, indeed; I got a letter from the archdeacon only this morning,

[064-041]which made it absolutely necessary that I should come.”

[064-042]“No bad news, I hope?” said Lady Lufton.

[064-043]“No; I can’t call it bad news. But, dear Lady Lufton, things won’t

[064-044]always turn out exactly as one would have them.”

[064-045]“No, indeed,” said her ladyship, remembering that it was incumbent

[064-046]on her to explain to Mrs. Grantly now at this present interview the

[064-047]tidings with which her mind was fraught. She would, however, let Mrs.

[064-048]Grantly first tell her own story, feeling, perhaps, that the one

[064-049]might possibly bear upon the other.

[064-050]“Poor dear Griselda!” said Mrs. Grantly, almost with a sigh. “I need

[064-051]not tell you, Lady Lufton, what my hopes were regarding her.”

[064-052]“Has she told you anything—anything that—”

[064-053]“She would have spoken to you at once—and it was due to you that she

[064-054]should have done so—but she was timid; and not unnaturally so. And

[064-055]then it was right that she should see her father and me before she

[064-056]quite made up her own mind. But I may say that it is settled now.”

[064-057]“What is settled?” asked Lady Lufton.

[064-058]“Of course it is impossible for any one to tell beforehand how these

[064-059]things will turn out,” continued Mrs. Grantly, beating about the bush

[064-060]rather more than was necessary. “The dearest wish of my heart was to

[064-061]see her married to Lord Lufton. I should so much have wished to have

[064-062]her in the same county with me, and such a match as that would have

[064-063]fully satisfied my ambition.”

[064-064]“Well, I should rather think it might!” Lady Lufton did not say this

[064-065]out loud, but she thought it. Mrs. Grantly was absolutely speaking of

[064-066]a match between her daughter and Lord Lufton as though she would have

[064-067]displayed some amount of Christian moderation in putting up with it!

[064-068]Griselda Grantly might be a very nice girl; but even she—so thought

[064-069]Lady Lufton at the moment—might possibly be priced too highly.

[064-070]“Dear Mrs. Grantly,” she said, “I have foreseen for the last few days

[064-071]that our mutual hopes in this respect would not be gratified. Lord

[064-072]Lufton, I think;—but perhaps it is not necessary to explain— Had

[064-073]you not come up to town I should have written to you,—probably

[064-074]to-day. Whatever may be dear Griselda’s fate in life, I sincerely

[064-075]hope that she may be happy.”

[064-076]“I think she will,” said Mrs. Grantly, in a tone that expressed much

[064-077]satisfaction.

[064-078]“Has—has anything—”

[064-079]“Lord Dumbello proposed to Griselda the other night, at Miss

[064-080]Dunstable’s party,” said Mrs. Grantly, with her eyes fixed upon

[064-081]the floor, and assuming on the sudden much meekness in her manner;

[064-082]“and his lordship was with the archdeacon yesterday, and again this

[064-083]morning. I fancy he is in Mount Street at the present moment.”

[064-084]“Oh, indeed!” said Lady Lufton. She would have given worlds to have

[064-085]possessed at the moment sufficient self-command to have enabled her

[064-086]to express in her tone and manner unqualified satisfaction at the

[064-087]tidings. But she had not such self-command, and was painfully aware

[064-088]of her own deficiency.

[064-089]“Yes,” said Mrs. Grantly. “And as it is all so far settled, and as

[064-090]I know you are so kindly anxious about dear Griselda, I thought

[064-091]it right to let you know at once. Nothing can be more upright,

[064-092]honourable, and generous, than Lord Dumbello’s conduct; and, on the

[064-093]whole, the match is one with which I and the archdeacon cannot but be

[064-094]contented.”

[064-095]“It is certainly a great match,” said Lady Lufton. “Have you seen

[064-096]Lady Hartletop yet?”

[064-097]Now Lady Hartletop could not be regarded as an agreeable connection,

[064-098]but this was the only word which escaped from Lady Lufton that could

[064-099]be considered in any way disparaging, and, on the whole, I think that

[064-100]she behaved well.

[064-101]“Lord Dumbello is so completely his own master that that has not been

[064-102]necessary,” said Mrs. Grantly. “The marquis has been told, and the

[064-103]archdeacon will see him either to-morrow or the day after.”

[064-104]There was nothing left for Lady Lufton but to congratulate her

[064-105]friend, and this she did in words perhaps not very sincere, but

[064-106]which, on the whole, were not badly chosen.

[064-107]“I am sure I hope she will be very happy,” said Lady Lufton, “and

[064-108]I trust that the alliance”—the word was very agreeable to Mrs.

[064-109]Grantly’s ear—“will give unalloyed gratification to you and to her

[064-110]father. The position which she is called to fill is a very splendid

[064-111]one, but I do not think that it is above her merits.”

[064-112]This was very generous, and so Mrs. Grantly felt it. She had expected

[064-113]that her news would be received with the coldest shade of civility,

[064-114]and she was quite prepared to do battle if there were occasion. But

[064-115]she had no wish for war, and was almost grateful to Lady Lufton for

[064-116]her cordiality.

[064-117]“Dear Lady Lufton,” she said, “it is so kind of you to say so. I have

[064-118]told no one else, and of course would tell no one till you knew it.

[064-119]No one has known her and understood her so well as you have done. And

[064-120]I can assure you of this: that there is no one to whose friendship

[064-121]she looks forward in her new sphere of life with half so much

[064-122]pleasure as she does to yours.”

[064-123]Lady Lufton did not say much further. She could not declare that

[064-124]she expected much gratification from an intimacy with the future

[064-125]Marchioness of Hartletop. The Hartletops and Luftons must, at any

[064-126]rate for her generation, live in a world apart, and she had now said

[064-127]all that her old friendship with Mrs. Grantly required. Mrs. Grantly

[064-128]understood all this quite as well as did Lady Lufton; but then Mrs.

[064-129]Grantly was much the better woman of the world.

[064-130]It was arranged that Griselda should come back to Bruton Street for

[064-131]that night, and that her visit should then be brought to a close.

[064-132]“The archdeacon thinks that for the present I had better remain up in

[064-133]town,” said Mrs. Grantly, “and under the very peculiar circumstances

[064-134]Griselda will be—perhaps more comfortable with me.”

[064-135]To this Lady Lufton entirely agreed; and so they parted, excellent

[064-136]friends, embracing each other in a most affectionate manner.

[064-137]That evening Griselda did return to Bruton Street, and Lady Lufton

[064-138]had to go through the further task of congratulating her. This was

[064-139]the more disagreeable of the two, especially so as it had to be

[064-140]thought over beforehand. But the young lady’s excellent good sense

[064-141]and sterling qualities made the task comparatively an easy one. She

[064-142]neither cried, nor was impassioned, nor went into hysterics, nor

[064-143]showed any emotion. She did not even talk of her noble Dumbello—her

[064-144]generous Dumbello. She took Lady Lufton’s kisses almost in silence,

[064-145]thanked her gently for her kindness, and made no allusion to her own

[064-146]future grandeur.

[064-147]“I think I should like to go to bed early,” she said, “as I must see

[064-148]to my packing up.”

[064-149]“Richards will do all that for you, my dear.”

[064-150]“Oh, yes, thank you, nothing can be kinder than Richards. But I’ll

[064-151]just see to my own dresses.” And so she went to bed early.

[064-152]Lady Lufton did not see her son for the next two days, but when she

[064-153]did, of course she said a word or two about Griselda.

[064-154]“You have heard the news, Ludovic?” she asked.

[064-155]“Oh, yes: it’s at all the clubs. I have been overwhelmed with

[064-156]presents of willow branches.”

[064-157]“You, at any rate, have got nothing to regret,” she said.

[064-158]“Nor you either, mother. I am sure that you do not think you have.

[064-159]Say that you do not regret it. Dearest mother, say so for my sake. Do

[064-160]you not know in your heart of hearts that she was not suited to be

[064-161]happy as my wife,—or to make me happy?”

[064-162]“Perhaps not,” said Lady Lufton, sighing. And then she kissed her

[064-163]son, and declared to herself that no girl in England could be good

[064-164]enough for him.

[064-165]CHAPTER XXXI.

[064-166]SALMON FISHING IN NORWAY.

[064-167]Lord Dumbello’s engagement with Griselda Grantly was the talk of the

[064-168]town for the next ten days. It formed, at least, one of two subjects

[064-169]which monopolized attention, the other being that dreadful rumour,

[064-170]first put in motion by Tom Towers at Miss Dunstable’s party, as to a

[064-171]threatened dissolution of Parliament.

[064-172]“Perhaps, after all, it will be the best thing for us,” said Mr.

[064-173]Green Walker, who felt himself to be tolerably safe at Crewe

[064-174]Junction.

[064-175]“I regard it as a most wicked attempt,” said Harold Smith, who was

[064-176]not equally secure in his own borough, and to whom the expense of

[064-177]an election was disagreeable. “It is done in order that they may

[064-178]get time to tide over the autumn. They won’t gain ten votes by a

[064-179]dissolution, and less than forty would hardly give them a majority.

[064-180]But they have no sense of public duty—none whatever. Indeed, I don’t

[064-181]know who has.”

[064-182]“No, by Jove; that’s just it. That’s what my aunt Lady Hartletop

[064-183]says; there is no sense of duty left in the world. By-the-by, what an

[064-184]uncommon fool Dumbello is making himself!” And then the conversation

[064-185]went off to that other topic.

[064-186]Lord Lufton’s joke against himself about the willow branches was all

[064-187]very well, and nobody dreamed that his heart was sore in that matter.

[064-188]The world was laughing at Lord Dumbello for what it chose to call

[064-189]a foolish match, and Lord Lufton’s friends talked to him about it

[064-190]as though they had never suspected that he could have made an ass

[064-191]of himself in the same direction; but, nevertheless, he was not

[064-192]altogether contented. He by no means wished to marry Griselda; he

[064-193]had declared to himself a dozen times since he had first suspected

[064-194]his mother’s manœuvres, that no consideration on earth should

[064-195]induce him to do so; he had pronounced her to be cold, insipid, and

[064-196]unattractive in spite of her beauty; and yet he felt almost angry

[064-197]that Lord Dumbello should have been successful. And this, too,

[064-198]was the more inexcusable, seeing that he had never forgotten Lucy

[064-199]Robarts, had never ceased to love her, and that, in holding those

[064-200]various conversations within his own bosom, he was as loud in Lucy’s

[064-201]favour as he was in dispraise of Griselda.
