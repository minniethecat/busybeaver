## Section 59

[059-001]But it must not be supposed that she was so poor in genius as to call

[059-002]her party openly by a name borrowed for the nonce from Mrs. Proudie.

[059-003]It was only among her specially intimate friends, Mrs. Harold Smith

[059-004]and some few dozen others, that she indulged in this little joke.

[059-005]There had been nothing in the least pretentious about the card with

[059-006]which she summoned her friends to her house on this occasion. She

[059-007]had merely signified in some ordinary way, that she would be glad to

[059-008]see them as soon after nine o’clock on Thursday evening, the ——

[059-009]instant, as might be convenient. But all the world understood that

[059-010]all the world was to be gathered together at Miss Dunstable’s house

[059-011]on the night in question,—that an effort was to be made to bring

[059-012]together people of all classes, gods and giants, saints and sinners,

[059-013]those rabid through the strength of their morality, such as our

[059-014]dear friend Lady Lufton, and those who were rabid in the opposite

[059-015]direction, such as Lady Hartletop, the Duke of Omnium, and Mr.

[059-016]Sowerby. An orthodox martyr had been caught from the East, and an

[059-017]oily latter-day St. Paul from the other side of the water—to the

[059-018]horror and amazement of Archdeacon Grantly, who had come up all the

[059-019]way from Plumstead to be present on the occasion. Mrs. Grantly also

[059-020]had hankered to be there; but when she heard of the presence of the

[059-021]latter-day St. Paul, she triumphed loudly over her husband, who had

[059-022]made no offer to take her. That Lords Brock and De Terrier were to

[059-023]be at the gathering was nothing. The pleasant king of the gods and

[059-024]the courtly chief of the giants could shake hands with each other

[059-025]in any house with the greatest pleasure; but men were to meet who,

[059-026]in reference to each other, could shake nothing but their heads or

[059-027]their fists. Supplehouse was to be there, and Harold Smith, who now

[059-028]hated his enemy with a hatred surpassing that of women—or even

[059-029]of politicians. The minor gods, it was thought, would congregate

[059-030]together in one room, very bitter in their present state of

[059-031]banishment; and the minor giants in another, terribly loud in

[059-032]their triumph. That is the fault of the giants, who, otherwise,

[059-033]are not bad fellows; they are unable to endure the weight of any

[059-034]temporary success. When attempting Olympus—and this work of

[059-035]attempting is doubtless their natural condition—they scratch and

[059-036]scramble, diligently using both toes and fingers, with a mixture

[059-037]of good-humoured virulence and self-satisfied industry that

[059-038]is gratifying to all parties. But whenever their efforts are

[059-039]unexpectedly, and for themselves unfortunately successful, they are

[059-040]so taken aback that they lose the power of behaving themselves with

[059-041]even gigantesque propriety.

[059-042]Such, so great and so various, was to be the intended gathering

[059-043]at Miss Dunstable’s house. She herself laughed, and quizzed

[059-044]herself—speaking of the affair to Mrs. Harold Smith as though it

[059-045]were an excellent joke, and to Mrs. Proudie as though she were simply

[059-046]emulous of rivalling those world-famous assemblies in Gloucester

[059-047]Place; but the town at large knew that an effort was being made, and

[059-048]it was supposed that even Miss Dunstable was somewhat nervous. In

[059-049]spite of her excellent joking it was presumed that she would be

[059-050]unhappy if she failed.

[059-051]To Mrs. Frank Gresham she did speak with some little seriousness.

[059-052]“But why on earth should you give yourself all this trouble?” that

[059-053]lady had said, when Miss Dunstable owned that she was doubtful,

[059-054]and unhappy in her doubts, as to the coming of one of the great

[059-055]colleagues of Mr. Supplehouse. “When such hundreds are coming, big

[059-056]wigs and little wigs of all shades, what can it matter whether Mr.

[059-057]Towers be there or not?”

[059-058]But Miss Dunstable had answered almost with a screech,—“My dear, it

[059-059]will be nothing without him. You don’t understand; but the fact is,

[059-060]that Tom Towers is everybody and everything at present.”

[059-061]And then, by no means for the first time, Mrs. Gresham began to

[059-062]lecture her friend as to her vanity; in answer to which lecture Miss

[059-063]Dunstable mysteriously hinted, that if she were only allowed her full

[059-064]swing on this occasion,—if all the world would now indulge her, she

[059-065]would— She did not quite say what she would do, but the inference

[059-066]drawn by Mrs. Gresham was this: that if the incense now offered on

[059-067]the altar of Fashion were accepted, Miss Dunstable would at once

[059-068]abandon the pomps and vanities of this wicked world, and all the

[059-069]sinful lusts of the flesh.

[059-070]“But the doctor will stay, my dear? I hope I may look on that as

[059-071]fixed.”

[059-072]Miss Dunstable, in making this demand on the doctor’s time, showed an

[059-073]energy quite equal to that with which she invoked the gods that Tom

[059-074]Towers might not be absent. Now, to tell the truth, Dr. Thorne had at

[059-075]first thought it very unreasonable that he should be asked to remain

[059-076]up in London in order that he might be present at an evening party,

[059-077]and had for a while pertinaciously refused; but when he learned that

[059-078]three or four prime ministers were expected, and that it was possible

[059-079]that even Tom Towers might be there in the flesh, his philosophy also

[059-080]had become weak, and he had written to Lady Arabella to say that his

[059-081]prolonged absence for two days further must be endured, and that the

[059-082]mild tonics, morning and evening, might be continued.

[059-083]But why should Miss Dunstable be so anxious that Dr. Thorne should

[059-084]be present on this grand occasion? Why, indeed, should she be so

[059-085]frequently inclined to summon him away from his country practice, his

[059-086]compounding board, and his useful ministrations to rural ailments?

[059-087]The doctor was connected with her by no ties of blood. Their

[059-088]friendship, intimate as it was, had as yet been but of short date.

[059-089]She was a very rich woman, capable of purchasing all manner of advice

[059-090]and good counsel, whereas he was so far from being rich, that any

[059-091]continued disturbance to his practice might be inconvenient to him.

[059-092]Nevertheless, Miss Dunstable seemed to have no more compunction in

[059-093]making calls upon his time, than she might have felt had he been her

[059-094]brother. No ideas on this matter suggested themselves to the doctor

[059-095]himself. He was a simple-minded man, taking things as they came,

[059-096]and especially so taking things that came pleasantly. He liked Miss

[059-097]Dunstable, and was gratified by her friendship, and did not think

[059-098]of asking himself whether she had a right to put him to trouble and

[059-099]inconvenience. But such ideas did occur to Mrs. Gresham, the doctor’s

[059-100]niece. Had Miss Dunstable any object, and if so, what object? Was

[059-101]it simply veneration for the doctor, or was it caprice? Was it

[059-102]eccentricity—or could it possibly be love?

[059-103]In speaking of the ages of these two friends it may be said in round

[059-104]terms that the lady was well past forty, and that the gentleman was

[059-105]well past fifty. Under such circumstances could it be love? The lady,

[059-106]too, was one who had had offers almost by the dozen,—offers from men

[059-107]of rank, from men of fashion, and from men of power; from men endowed

[059-108]with personal attractions, with pleasant manners, with cultivated

[059-109]tastes, and with eloquent tongues. Not only had she loved none such,

[059-110]but by none such had she been cajoled into an idea that it was

[059-111]possible that she could love them. That Dr. Thorne’s tastes were

[059-112]cultivated, and his manners pleasant, might probably be admitted by

[059-113]three or four old friends in the country who valued him; but the

[059-114]world in London, that world to which Miss Dunstable was accustomed,

[059-115]and which was apparently becoming dearer to her day by day, would not

[059-116]have regarded the doctor as a man likely to become the object of a

[059-117]lady’s passion.

[059-118]But nevertheless the idea did occur to Mrs. Gresham. She had been

[059-119]brought up at the elbow of this country practitioner; she had lived

[059-120]with him as though she had been his daughter; she had been for years

[059-121]the ministering angel of his household; and, till her heart had

[059-122]opened to the natural love of womanhood, all her closest sympathies

[059-123]had been with him. In her eyes the doctor was all but perfect; and it

[059-124]did not seem to her to be out of the question that Miss Dunstable

[059-125]should have fallen in love with her uncle.

[059-126]Miss Dunstable once said to Mrs. Harold Smith that it was possible

[059-127]that she might marry, the only condition then expressed being this,

[059-128]that the man elected should be one who was quite indifferent as to

[059-129]money. Mrs. Harold Smith, who, by her friends, was presumed to know

[059-130]the world with tolerable accuracy, had replied that such a man Miss

[059-131]Dunstable would never find in this world. All this had passed in that

[059-132]half comic vein of banter which Miss Dunstable so commonly used when

[059-133]conversing with such friends as Mrs. Harold Smith; but she had spoken

[059-134]words of the same import more than once to Mrs. Gresham; and Mrs.

[059-135]Gresham, putting two and two together as women do, had made four

[059-136]of the little sum; and, as the final result of the calculation,

[059-137]determined that Miss Dunstable would marry Dr. Thorne if Dr. Thorne

[059-138]would ask her.

[059-139]And then Mrs. Gresham began to bethink herself of two other

[059-140]questions. Would it be well that her uncle should marry Miss

[059-141]Dunstable? and if so, would it be possible to induce him to make such

[059-142]a proposition? After the consideration of many pros and cons, and the

[059-143]balancing of very various arguments, Mrs. Gresham thought that the

[059-144]arrangement on the whole might not be a bad one. For Miss Dunstable

[059-145]she herself had a sincere affection, which was shared by her

[059-146]husband. She had often grieved at the sacrifices Miss Dunstable

[059-147]made to the world, thinking that her friend was falling into vanity,

[059-148]indifference, and an ill mode of life; but such a marriage as this

[059-149]would probably cure all that. And then as to Dr. Thorne himself, to

[059-150]whose benefit were of course applied Mrs. Gresham’s most earnest

[059-151]thoughts in this matter, she could not but think that he would be

[059-152]happier married than he was single. In point of temper, no woman

[059-153]could stand higher than Miss Dunstable; no one had ever heard of her

[059-154]being in an ill humour; and then though Mrs. Gresham was gifted with

[059-155]a mind which was far removed from being mercenary, it was impossible

[059-156]not to feel that some benefit must accrue from the bride’s wealth.

[059-157]Mary Thorne, the present Mrs. Frank Gresham, had herself been a

[059-158]great heiress. Circumstances had weighted her hand with enormous

[059-159]possessions, and hitherto she had not realized the truth of that

[059-160]lesson which would teach us to believe that happiness and riches are

[059-161]incompatible. Therefore she resolved that it might be well if the

[059-162]doctor and Miss Dunstable were brought together.

[059-163]But could the doctor be induced to make such an offer? Mrs. Gresham

[059-164]acknowledged a terrible difficulty in looking at the matter from that

[059-165]point of view. Her uncle was fond of Miss Dunstable; but she was sure

[059-166]that an idea of such a marriage had never entered his head; that

[059-167]it would be very difficult—almost impossible—to create such an

[059-168]idea; and that if the idea were there, the doctor could hardly be

[059-169]instigated to make the proposition. Looking at the matter as a whole,

[059-170]she feared that the match was not practicable.

[059-171]On the day of Miss Dunstable’s party, Mrs. Gresham and her uncle

[059-172]dined together alone in Portman Square. Mr. Gresham was not yet in

[059-173]Parliament, but an almost immediate vacancy was expected in his

[059-174]division of the county, and it was known that no one could stand

[059-175]against him with any chance of success. This threw him much among the

[059-176]politicians of his party—those giants, namely, whom it would be his

[059-177]business to support—and on this account he was a good deal away from

[059-178]his own house at the present moment.
