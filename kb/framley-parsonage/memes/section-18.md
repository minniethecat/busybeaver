## Section 18

[018-001]Young Gresham did feel rather flattered. There were not many men in

[018-002]the county to whom such an offer could be made without an absurdity.

[018-003]It might be doubted whether the duke himself could purchase the Chace

[018-004]of Chaldicotes with ready money; but that he, Gresham, could do

[018-005]so—he and his wife between them—no man did doubt. And then Mr.

[018-006]Gresham thought of a former day when he had once been at Gatherum

[018-007]Castle. He had been poor enough then, and the duke had not treated

[018-008]him in the most courteous manner in the world. How hard it is for

[018-009]a rich man not to lean upon his riches! harder, indeed, than for a

[018-010]camel to go through the eye of a needle.

[018-011]All Barsetshire knew—at any rate all West Barsetshire—that Miss

[018-012]Dunstable had been brought down in those parts in order that Mr.

[018-013]Sowerby might marry her. It was not surmised that Miss Dunstable

[018-014]herself had had any previous notice of this arrangement, but it was

[018-015]supposed that the thing would turn out as a matter of course. Mr.

[018-016]Sowerby had no money, but then he was witty, clever, good-looking,

[018-017]and a member of Parliament. He lived before the world, represented an

[018-018]old family, and had an old place. How could Miss Dunstable possibly

[018-019]do better? She was not so young now, and it was time that she should

[018-020]look about her.

[018-021]The suggestion as regarded Mr. Sowerby was certainly true, and was

[018-022]not the less so as regarded some of Mr. Sowerby’s friends. His

[018-023]sister, Mrs. Harold Smith, had devoted herself to the work, and with

[018-024]this view had run up a dear friendship with Miss Dunstable. The

[018-025]bishop had intimated, nodding his head knowingly, that it would

[018-026]be a very good thing. Mrs. Proudie had given in her adherence. Mr.

[018-027]Supplehouse had been made to understand that it must be a case of

[018-028]“Paws off” with him, as long as he remained in that part of the

[018-029]world; and even the duke himself had desired Fothergill to manage it.

[018-030]“He owes me an enormous sum of money,” said the duke, who held all

[018-031]Mr. Sowerby’s title-deeds, “and I doubt whether the security will be

[018-032]sufficient.”

[018-033]“Your grace will find the security quite sufficient,” said Mr.

[018-034]Fothergill; “but nevertheless it would be a good match.”

[018-035]“Very good,” said the duke. And then it became Mr. Fothergill’s duty

[018-036]to see that Mr. Sowerby and Miss Dunstable became man and wife as

[018-037]speedily as possible.

[018-038]Some of the party, who were more wide awake than others, declared

[018-039]that he had made the offer; others, that he was just going to do so;

[018-040]and one very knowing lady went so far at one time as to say that he

[018-041]was making it at that moment. Bets also were laid as to the lady’s

[018-042]answer, as to the terms of the settlement, and as to the period

[018-043]of the marriage,—of all which poor Miss Dunstable of course knew

[018-044]nothing.

[018-045]Mr. Sowerby, in spite of the publicity of his proceedings, proceeded

[018-046]in the matter very well. He said little about it to those who joked

[018-047]with him, but carried on the fight with what best knowledge he had

[018-048]in such matters. But so much it is given to us to declare with

[018-049]certainty, that he had not proposed on the evening previous to the

[018-050]morning fixed for the departure of Mark Robarts.

[018-051]During the last two days Mr. Sowerby’s intimacy with Mark had grown

[018-052]warmer and warmer. He had talked to the vicar confidentially about

[018-053]the doings of these bigwigs now present at the castle, as though

[018-054]there were no other guest there with whom he could speak in so free

[018-055]a manner. He confided, it seemed, much more in Mark than in his

[018-056]brother-in-law, Harold Smith, or in any of his brother members of

[018-057]Parliament, and had altogether opened his heart to him in this affair

[018-058]of his anticipated marriage. Now Mr. Sowerby was a man of mark in the

[018-059]world, and all this flattered our young clergyman not a little.

[018-060]On that evening before Robarts went away Sowerby asked him to come up

[018-061]into his bedroom when the whole party was breaking up, and there got

[018-062]him into an easy-chair while he, Sowerby, walked up and down the

[018-063]room.

[018-064]“You can hardly tell, my dear fellow,” said he, “the state of nervous

[018-065]anxiety in which this puts me.”

[018-066]“Why don’t you ask her and have done with it? She seems to me to be

[018-067]fond of your society.”

[018-068]“Ah, it is not that only; there are wheels within wheels;” and then

[018-069]he walked once or twice up and down the room, during which Mark

[018-070]thought that he might as well go to bed.

[018-071]“Not that I mind telling you everything,” said Sowerby. “I am

[018-072]infernally hard up for a little ready money just at the present

[018-073]moment. It may be, and indeed I think it will be, the case that I

[018-074]shall be ruined in this matter for the want of it.”

[018-075]“Could not Harold Smith give it you?”

[018-076]“Ha, ha, ha! you don’t know Harold Smith. Did you ever hear of his

[018-077]lending a man a shilling in his life?”

[018-078]“Or Supplehouse?”

[018-079]“Lord love you! You see me and Supplehouse together here, and he

[018-080]comes and stays at my house, and all that; but Supplehouse and I are

[018-081]no friends. Look you here, Mark—I would do more for your little

[018-082]finger than for his whole hand, including the pen which he holds in

[018-083]it. Fothergill indeed might—but then I know Fothergill is pressed

[018-084]himself at the present moment. It is deuced hard, isn’t it? I must

[018-085]give up the whole game if I can’t put my hand upon £400 within the

[018-086]next two days.”

[018-087]“Ask her for it, herself.”

[018-088]“What, the woman I wish to marry! No, Mark, I’m not quite come to

[018-089]that. I would sooner lose her than that.”

[018-090]Mark sat silent, gazing at the fire and wishing that he was in his

[018-091]own bedroom. He had an idea that Mr. Sowerby wished him to produce

[018-092]this £400; and he knew also that he had not £400 in the world, and

[018-093]that if he had he would be acting very foolishly to give it to Mr.

[018-094]Sowerby. But nevertheless he felt half fascinated by the man, and

[018-095]half afraid of him.

[018-096]“Lufton owes it to me to do more than this,” continued Mr. Sowerby;

[018-097]“but then Lufton is not here.”

[018-098]“Why, he has just paid five thousand pounds for you.”

[018-099]“Paid five thousand pounds for me! Indeed he has done no such thing:

[018-100]not a sixpence of it came into my hands. Believe me, Mark, you don’t

[018-101]know the whole of that yet. Not that I mean to say a word against

[018-102]Lufton. He is the soul of honour; though so deucedly dilatory in

[018-103]money matters. He thought he was right all through that affair, but

[018-104]no man was ever so confoundedly wrong. Why, don’t you remember that

[018-105]that was the very view you took of it yourself?”

[018-106]“I remember saying that I thought he was mistaken.”

[018-107]“Of course he was mistaken. And dearly the mistake cost me. I had to

[018-108]make good the money for two or three years. And my property is not

[018-109]like his—I wish it were.”

[018-110]“Marry Miss Dunstable, and that will set it all right for you.”

[018-111]“Ah! so I would if I had this money. At any rate I would bring it to

[018-112]the point. Now, I tell you what, Mark; if you’ll assist me at this

[018-113]strait I’ll never forget it. And the time will come round when I may

[018-114]be able to do something for you.”

[018-115]“I have not got a hundred, no, not fifty pounds by me in the world.”

[018-116]“Of course you’ve not. Men don’t walk about the streets with £400 in

[018-117]their pockets. I don’t suppose there’s a single man here in the house

[018-118]with such a sum at his bankers’, unless it be the duke.”

[018-119]“What is it you want then?”

[018-120]“Why, your name, to be sure. Believe me, my dear fellow, I would not

[018-121]ask you really to put your hand into your pocket to such a tune as

[018-122]that. Allow me to draw on you for that amount at three months. Long

[018-123]before that time I shall be flush enough.” And then, before Mark

[018-124]could answer, he had a bill stamp and pen and ink out on the table

[018-125]before him, and was filling in the bill as though his friend had

[018-126]already given his consent.

[018-127]“Upon my word, Sowerby, I had rather not do that.”

[018-128]“Why! what are you afraid of?”—Mr. Sowerby asked this very sharply.

[018-129]“Did you ever hear of my having neglected to take up a bill when it

[018-130]fell due?” Robarts thought that he had heard of such a thing; but

[018-131]in his confusion he was not exactly sure, and so he said nothing.

[018-132]“No, my boy; I have not come to that. Look here: just you write,

[018-133]‘Accepted, Mark Robarts,’ across that, and then you shall never hear

[018-134]of the transaction again;—and you will have obliged me for ever.”

[018-135]“As a clergyman it would be wrong of me,” said Robarts.

[018-136]“As a clergyman! Come, Mark! If you don’t like to do as much as that

[018-137]for a friend, say so; but don’t let us have that sort of humbug. If

[018-138]there be one class of men whose names would be found more frequent on

[018-139]the backs of bills in the provincial banks than another, clergymen

[018-140]are that class. Come, old fellow, you won’t throw me over when I am

[018-141]so hard pushed.”

[018-142]Mark Robarts took the pen and signed the bill. It was the first time

[018-143]in his life that he had ever done such an act. Sowerby then shook

[018-144]him cordially by the hand, and he walked off to his own bedroom a

[018-145]wretched man.

[018-146]CHAPTER IX.

[018-147]THE VICAR’S RETURN.

[018-148]The next morning Mr. Robarts took leave of all his grand friends with

[018-149]a heavy heart. He had lain awake half the night thinking of what he

[018-150]had done and trying to reconcile himself to his position. He had not

[018-151]well left Mr. Sowerby’s room before he felt certain that at the end

[018-152]of three months he would again be troubled about that £400. As he

[018-153]went along the passage all the man’s known antecedents crowded upon

[018-154]him much quicker than he could remember them when seated in that

[018-155]arm-chair with the bill stamp before him, and the pen and ink ready

[018-156]to his hand. He remembered what Lord Lufton had told him—how he had

[018-157]complained of having been left in the lurch; he thought of all the

[018-158]stories current through the entire county as to the impossibility

[018-159]of getting money from Chaldicotes; he brought to mind the known

[018-160]character of the man, and then he knew that he must prepare himself

[018-161]to make good a portion at least of that heavy payment.

[018-162]Why had he come to this horrid place? Had he not everything at home

[018-163]at Framley which the heart of man could desire? No; the heart of man

[018-164]can desire deaneries—the heart, that is, of the man vicar; and the

[018-165]heart of the man dean can desire bishoprics; and before the eyes

[018-166]of the man bishop does there not loom the transcendental glory of

[018-167]Lambeth? He had owned to himself that he was ambitious; but he had to

[018-168]own to himself now also that he had hitherto taken but a sorry path

[018-169]towards the object of his ambition.

[018-170]On the next morning at breakfast-time, before his horse and gig

[018-171]arrived for him, no one was so bright as his friend Sowerby. “So you

[018-172]are off, are you?” said he.

[018-173]“Yes, I shall go this morning.”

[018-174]“Say everything that’s kind from me to Lufton. I may possibly see him

[018-175]out hunting; otherwise we shan’t meet till the spring. As to my going

[018-176]to Framley, that’s out of the question. Her ladyship would look for

[018-177]my tail, and swear that she smelt brimstone. By-bye, old fellow!”
