## Section 47

[047-001]And then, by degrees, there was confidence between them, and the

[047-002]poverty-stricken helpmate of the perpetual curate was able to speak

[047-003]of the weight of her burden to the well-to-do young wife of the

[047-004]Barchester prebendary. “It was hard,” the former said, “to feel

[047-005]herself so different from the wives of other clergymen around

[047-006]her—to know that they lived softly, while she, with all the work

[047-007]of her hands, and unceasing struggle of her energies, could hardly

[047-008]manage to place wholesome food before her husband and children. It

[047-009]was a terrible thing—a grievous thing to think of, that all the

[047-010]work of her mind should be given up to such subjects as these. But,

[047-011]nevertheless, she could bear it,” she said, “as long as he would

[047-012]carry himself like a man, and face his lot boldly before the world.”

[047-013]And then she told how he had been better there at Hogglestock than

[047-014]in their former residence down in Cornwall, and in warm language she

[047-015]expressed her thanks to the friend who had done so much for them.

[047-016]“Mrs. Arabin told me that she was so anxious you should go to them,”

[047-017]said Mrs. Robarts.

[047-018]“Ah, yes; but that I fear is impossible. The children, you know, Mrs.

[047-019]Robarts.”

[047-020]“I would take care of two of them for you.”

[047-021]“Oh, no; I could not punish you for your goodness in that way. But

[047-022]he would not go. He could go and leave me at home. Sometimes I have

[047-023]thought that it might be so, and I have done all in my power to

[047-024]persuade him. I have told him that if he could mix once more with the

[047-025]world, with the clerical world, you know, that he would be better

[047-026]fitted for the performance of his own duties. But he answers me

[047-027]angrily, that it is impossible—that his coat is not fit for the

[047-028]dean’s table,” and Mrs. Crawley almost blushed as she spoke of such a

[047-029]reason.

[047-030]“What! with an old friend like Dr. Arabin? Surely that must be

[047-031]nonsense.”

[047-032]“I know that it is. The dean would be glad to see him with any coat.

[047-033]But the fact is that he cannot bear to enter the house of a rich man

[047-034]unless his duty calls him there.”

[047-035]“But surely that is a mistake?”

[047-036]“It is a mistake. But what can I do? I fear that he regards the rich

[047-037]as his enemies. He is pining for the solace of some friend to whom

[047-038]he could talk—for some equal, with a mind educated like his own, to

[047-039]whose thoughts he could listen, and to whom he could speak his own

[047-040]thoughts. But such a friend must be equal, not only in mind, but in

[047-041]purse; and where can he ever find such a man as that?”

[047-042]“But you may get better preferment.”

[047-043]“Ah, no; and if he did, we are hardly fit for it now. If I could

[047-044]think that I could educate my children; if I could only do something

[047-045]for my poor Grace—”

[047-046]In answer to this Mrs. Robarts said a word or two, but not much.

[047-047]She resolved, however, that if she could get her husband’s leave,

[047-048]something should be done for Grace. Would it not be a good work? and

[047-049]was it not incumbent on her to make some kindly use of all the goods

[047-050]with which Providence had blessed herself?

[047-051]And then they went back to the sitting-room, each again with a young

[047-052]child in her arms, Mrs. Crawley having stowed away in the kitchen the

[047-053]chicken broth and the leg of pork and the supply of eggs. Lucy had

[047-054]been engaged the while with the children, and when the two married

[047-055]ladies entered, they found that a shop had been opened at which

[047-056]all manner of luxuries were being readily sold and purchased at

[047-057]marvellously easy prices; the guava jelly was there, and the oranges,

[047-058]and the sugar-plums, red and yellow and striped; and, moreover, the

[047-059]gingerbread had been taken down in the audacity of their commercial

[047-060]speculations, and the nuts were spread out upon a board, behind which

[047-061]Lucy stood as shop-girl, disposing of them for kisses.

[047-062]“Mamma, mamma,” said Bobby, running up to his mother, “you must buy

[047-063]something of her,” and he pointed with his fingers at the shop-girl.

[047-064]“You must give her two kisses for that heap of barley-sugar.” Looking

[047-065]at Bobby’s mouth at the time, one would have said that his kisses

[047-066]might be dispensed with.

[047-067]When they were again in the pony-carriage, behind the impatient Puck,

[047-068]and were well away from the door, Fanny was the first to speak.

[047-069]“How very different those two are,” she said; “different in their

[047-070]minds and in their spirit!”

[047-071]“But how much higher toned is her mind than his! How weak he is in

[047-072]many things, and how strong she is in everything! How false is his

[047-073]pride, and how false his shame!”

[047-074]“But we must remember what he has to bear. It is not every one that

[047-075]can endure such a life as his without false pride and false shame.”

[047-076]“But she has neither,” said Lucy.

[047-077]“Because you have one hero in a family, does that give you a right to

[047-078]expect another?” said Mrs. Robarts. “Of all my own acquaintance, Mrs.

[047-079]Crawley, I think, comes nearest to heroism.”

[047-080]And then they passed by the Hogglestock school, and Mr. Crawley, when

[047-081]he heard the noise of the wheels, came out.

[047-082]“You have been very kind,” said he, “to remain so long with my poor

[047-083]wife.”

[047-084]“We had a great many things to talk about, after you went.”

[047-085]“It is very kind of you, for she does not often see a friend,

[047-086]now-a-days. Will you have the goodness to tell Mr. Robarts that I

[047-087]shall be here at the school, at eleven o’clock to-morrow?”

[047-088]And then he bowed, taking off his hat to them, and they drove on.

[047-089]“If he really does care about her comfort, I shall not think so badly

[047-090]of him,” said Lucy.

[047-091]CHAPTER XXIII.

[047-092]THE TRIUMPH OF THE GIANTS.

[047-093]And now about the end of April news arrived almost simultaneously

[047-094]in all quarters of the habitable globe that was terrible in its

[047-095]import to one of the chief persons of our history;—some may

[047-096]think to the chief person in it. All high parliamentary people

[047-097]will doubtless so think, and the wives and daughters of such. The

[047-098]Titans warring against the gods had been for awhile successful.

[047-099]Typhœus and Mimas, Porphyrion and Rhœcus, the giant brood of

[047-100]old, steeped in ignorance and wedded to corruption, had scaled the

[047-101]heights of Olympus, assisted by that audacious flinger of deadly

[047-102]ponderous missiles, who stands ever ready armed with his terrific

[047-103]sling—Supplehouse, the Enceladus of the press. And in this universal

[047-104]cataclasm of the starry councils, what could a poor Diana do, Diana

[047-105]of the Petty Bag, but abandon her pride of place to some rude Orion?

[047-106]In other words, the ministry had been compelled to resign, and with

[047-107]them Mr. Harold Smith.

[047-108]“And so poor Harold is out, before he has well tasted the sweets of

[047-109]office,” said Sowerby, writing to his friend the parson; “and as far

[047-110]as I know, the only piece of Church patronage which has fallen in

[047-111]the way of the ministry since he joined it, has made its way down to

[047-112]Framley—to my great joy and contentment.” But it hardly tended to

[047-113]Mark’s joy and contentment on the same subject that he should be so

[047-114]often reminded of the benefit conferred upon him.

[047-115]Terrible was this break-down of the ministry, and especially to

[047-116]Harold Smith, who to the last had had confidence in that theory of

[047-117]new blood. He could hardly believe that a large majority of the House

[047-118]should vote against a government which he had only just joined. “If

[047-119]we are to go on in this way,” he said to his young friend Green

[047-120]Walker, “the Queen’s government cannot be carried on.” That alleged

[047-121]difficulty as to carrying on the Queen’s government has been

[047-122]frequently mooted in late years since a certain great man first

[047-123]introduced the idea. Nevertheless, the Queen’s government is carried

[047-124]on, and the propensity and aptitude of men for this work seems to be

[047-125]not at all on the decrease. If we have but few young statesmen, it is

[047-126]because the old stagers are so fond of the rattle of their harness.

[047-127]“I really do not see how the Queen’s government is to be carried on,”

[047-128]said Harold Smith to Green Walker, standing in a corner of one of the

[047-129]lobbies of the House of Commons on the first of those days of awful

[047-130]interest, in which the Queen was sending for one crack statesman

[047-131]after another; and some anxious men were beginning to doubt whether

[047-132]or no we should, in truth, be able to obtain the blessing of another

[047-133]cabinet. The gods had all vanished from their places. Would the

[047-134]giants be good enough to do anything for us or no? There were men who

[047-135]seemed to think that the giants would refuse to do anything for us.

[047-136]“The House will now be adjourned over till Monday, and I would not be

[047-137]in her Majesty’s shoes for something,” said Mr. Harold Smith.

[047-138]“By Jove! no,” said Green Walker, who in these days was a stanch

[047-139]Harold Smithian, having felt a pride in joining himself on as a

[047-140]substantial support to a cabinet minister. Had he contented himself

[047-141]with being merely a Brockite, he would have counted as nobody. “By

[047-142]Jove! no,” and Green Walker opened his eyes and shook his head, as

[047-143]he thought of the perilous condition in which her Majesty must be

[047-144]placed. “I happen to know that Lord —— won’t join them unless he

[047-145]has the Foreign Office,” and he mentioned some hundred-handed Gyas

[047-146]supposed to be of the utmost importance to the counsels of the

[047-147]Titans.

[047-148]“And that, of course, is impossible. I don’t see what on earth

[047-149]they are to do. There’s Sidonia; they do say that he’s making some

[047-150]difficulty now.” Now Sidonia was another giant, supposed to be very

[047-151]powerful.

[047-152]“We all know that the Queen won’t see him,” said Green Walker, who,

[047-153]being a member of Parliament for the Crewe Junction, and nephew to

[047-154]Lady Hartletop, of course had perfectly correct means of ascertaining

[047-155]what the Queen would do, and what she would not.

[047-156]“The fact is,” said Harold Smith, recurring again to his own

[047-157]situation as an ejected god, “that the House does not in the least

[047-158]understand what it is about;—doesn’t know what it wants. The

[047-159]question I should like to ask them is this: do they intend that the

[047-160]Queen shall have a government, or do they not? Are they prepared to

[047-161]support such men as Sidonia and Lord De Terrier? If so, I am their

[047-162]obedient humble servant; but I shall be very much surprised, that’s

[047-163]all.” Lord De Terrier was at this time recognized by all men as the

[047-164]leader of the giants.

[047-165]“And so shall I,—deucedly surprised. They can’t do it, you know.

[047-166]There are the Manchester men. I ought to know something about them

[047-167]down in my country; and I say they can’t support Lord De Terrier. It

[047-168]wouldn’t be natural.”

[047-169]“Natural! Human nature has come to an end, I think,” said Harold

[047-170]Smith, who could hardly understand that the world should conspire to

[047-171]throw over a government which he had joined, and that, too, before

[047-172]the world had waited to see how much he would do for it; “the fact

[047-173]is this, Walker, we have no longer among us any strong feeling of

[047-174]party.”

[047-175]“No, not a d——,” said Green Walker, who was very energetic in his

[047-176]present political aspirations.

[047-177]“And till we can recover that, we shall never be able to have a

[047-178]government firm-seated and sure-handed. Nobody can count on men

[047-179]from one week to another. The very members who in one month place

[047-180]a minister in power, are the very first to vote against him in the

[047-181]next.”
