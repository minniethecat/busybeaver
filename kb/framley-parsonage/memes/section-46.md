## Section 46

[046-001]CHAPTER XXII.

[046-002]HOGGLESTOCK PARSONAGE.

[046-003]At the end of the last chapter, we left Lucy Robarts waiting for an

[046-004]introduction to Mrs. Crawley, who was sitting with one baby in her

[046-005]lap while she was rocking another who lay in a cradle at her feet.

[046-006]Mr. Crawley, in the meanwhile, had risen from his seat with his

[046-007]finger between the leaves of an old grammar out of which he had been

[046-008]teaching his two elder children. The whole Crawley family was thus

[046-009]before them when Mrs. Robarts and Lucy entered the sitting-room.

[046-010][Illustration: The Crawley Family.]

[046-011]“This is my sister-in-law, Lucy,” said Mrs. Robarts. “Pray don’t move

[046-012]now, Mrs. Crawley; or if you do, let me take baby.” And she put out

[046-013]her arms and took the infant into them, making him quite at home

[046-014]there; for she had work of this kind of her own, at home, which she

[046-015]by no means neglected, though the attendance of nurses was more

[046-016]plentiful with her than at Hogglestock.

[046-017]Mrs. Crawley did get up, and told Lucy that she was glad to see her,

[046-018]and Mr. Crawley came forward, grammar in hand, looking humble and

[046-019]meek. Could we have looked into the innermost spirit of him and his

[046-020]life’s partner, we should have seen that mixed with the pride of his

[046-021]poverty there was some feeling of disgrace that he was poor, but that

[046-022]with her, regarding this matter, there was neither pride nor shame.

[046-023]The realities of life had become so stern to her that the outward

[046-024]aspects of them were as nothing. She would have liked a new gown

[046-025]because it would have been useful; but it would have been nothing to

[046-026]her if all the county knew that the one in which she went to church

[046-027]had been turned three times. It galled him, however, to think that he

[046-028]and his were so poorly dressed.

[046-029]“I am afraid you can hardly find a chair, Miss Robarts,” said Mr.

[046-030]Crawley.

[046-031]“Oh, yes; there is nothing here but this young gentleman’s library,”

[046-032]said Lucy, moving a pile of ragged, coverless books on to the table.

[046-033]“I hope he’ll forgive me for moving them.”

[046-034]“They are not Bob’s,—at least, not the most of them,—but mine,”

[046-035]said the girl.

[046-036]“But some of them are mine,” said the boy; “ain’t they, Grace?”

[046-037]“And are you a great scholar?” asked Lucy, drawing the child to her.

[046-038]“I don’t know,” said Grace, with a sheepish face. “I am in Greek

[046-039]Delectus and the irregular verbs.”

[046-040]“Greek Delectus and the irregular verbs!” And Lucy put up her hands

[046-041]with astonishment.

[046-042]“And she knows an ode of Horace all by heart,” said Bob.

[046-043]“An ode of Horace!” said Lucy, still holding the young shamefaced

[046-044]female prodigy close to her knees.

[046-045]“It is all that I can give them,” said Mr. Crawley, apologetically.

[046-046]“A little scholarship is the only fortune that has come in my way,

[046-047]and I endeavour to share that with my children.”

[046-048]“I believe men say that it is the best fortune any of us can have,”

[046-049]said Lucy, thinking, however, in her own mind, that Horace and the

[046-050]irregular Greek verbs savoured too much of precocious forcing in a

[046-051]young lady of nine years old. But, nevertheless, Grace was a pretty,

[046-052]simple-looking girl, and clung to her ally closely, and seemed to

[046-053]like being fondled. So that Lucy anxiously wished that Mr. Crawley

[046-054]could be got rid of and the presents produced.

[046-055]“I hope you have left Mr. Robarts quite well,” said Mr. Crawley, with

[046-056]a stiff, ceremonial voice, differing very much from that in which he

[046-057]had so energetically addressed his brother clergyman when they were

[046-058]alone together in the study at Framley.

[046-059]“He is quite well, thank you. I suppose you have heard of his good

[046-060]fortune?”

[046-061]“Yes; I have heard of it,” said Mr. Crawley, gravely. “I hope that

[046-062]his promotion may tend in every way to his advantage here and

[046-063]hereafter.”

[046-064]It seemed, however, to be manifest from the manner in which he

[046-065]expressed his kind wishes, that his hopes and expectations did not go

[046-066]hand-in-hand together.

[046-067]“By-the-by, he desired us to say that he will call here to-morrow; at

[046-068]about eleven, didn’t he say, Fanny?”

[046-069]“Yes; he wishes to see you about some parish business, I think,” said

[046-070]Mrs. Robarts, looking up for a moment from the anxious discussion in

[046-071]which she was already engaged with Mrs. Crawley on nursery matters.

[046-072]“Pray tell him,” said Mr. Crawley, “that I shall be happy to see him;

[046-073]though, perhaps, now that new duties have been thrown upon him, it

[046-074]will be better that I should visit him at Framley.”

[046-075]“His new duties do not disturb him much as yet,” said Lucy. “And his

[046-076]riding over here will be no trouble to him.”

[046-077]“Yes; there he has the advantage over me. I unfortunately have no

[046-078]horse.”

[046-079]And then Lucy began petting the little boy, and by degrees slipped a

[046-080]small bag of gingerbread-nuts out of her muff into his hands. She had

[046-081]not the patience necessary for waiting, as had her sister-in-law.

[046-082]The boy took the bag, peeped into it, and then looked up into her

[046-083]face.

[046-084]“What is that, Bob?” said Mr. Crawley.

[046-085]“Gingerbread,” faltered Bobby, feeling that a sin had been committed,

[046-086]though, probably, feeling also that he himself could hardly as yet be

[046-087]accounted as deeply guilty.

[046-088]“Miss Robarts,” said the father, “we are very much obliged to you;

[046-089]but our children are hardly used to such things.”

[046-090]“I am a lady with a weak mind, Mr. Crawley, and always carry things

[046-091]of this sort about with me when I go to visit children; so you must

[046-092]forgive me, and allow your little boy to accept them.”

[046-093]“Oh, certainly. Bob, my child, give the bag to your mamma, and she

[046-094]will let you and Grace have them, one at a time.” And then the bag in

[046-095]a solemn manner was carried over to their mother, who, taking it from

[046-096]her son’s hands, laid it high on a bookshelf.

[046-097]“And not one now?” said Lucy Robarts, very piteously. “Don’t be so

[046-098]hard, Mr. Crawley,—not upon them, but upon me. May I not learn

[046-099]whether they are good of their kind?”

[046-100]“I am sure they are very good; but I think their mamma will prefer

[046-101]their being put by for the present.”

[046-102]This was very discouraging to Lucy. If one small bag of

[046-103]gingerbread-nuts created so great a difficulty, how was she to

[046-104]dispose of the pot of guava jelly and box of bonbons, which were

[046-105]still in her muff; or how distribute the packet of oranges with which

[046-106]the pony-carriage was laden? And there was jelly for the sick child,

[046-107]and chicken broth, which was, indeed, another jelly; and, to tell the

[046-108]truth openly, there was also a joint of fresh pork and a basket of

[046-109]eggs from the Framley Parsonage farmyard, which Mrs. Robarts was to

[046-110]introduce, should she find herself capable of doing so; but which

[046-111]would certainly be cast out with utter scorn by Mr. Crawley, if

[046-112]tendered in his immediate presence. There had also been a suggestion

[046-113]as to adding two or three bottles of port; but the courage of the

[046-114]ladies had failed them on that head, and the wine was not now added

[046-115]to their difficulties.

[046-116]Lucy found it very difficult to keep up a conversation with Mr.

[046-117]Crawley—the more so, as Mrs. Robarts and Mrs. Crawley presently

[046-118]withdrew into a bedroom, taking the two younger children with them.

[046-119]“How unlucky,” thought Lucy, “that she has not got my muff with her!”

[046-120]But the muff lay in her lap, ponderous with its rich enclosures.

[046-121]“I suppose you will live in Barchester for a portion of the year

[046-122]now,” said Mr. Crawley.

[046-123]“I really do not know as yet; Mark talks of taking lodgings for his

[046-124]first month’s residence.”

[046-125]“But he will have the house, will he not?”

[046-126]“Oh, yes; I suppose so.”

[046-127]“I fear he will find it interfere with his own parish—with his

[046-128]general utility there: the schools, for instance.”

[046-129]“Mark thinks that, as he is so near, he need not be much absent from

[046-130]Framley, even during his residence. And then Lady Lufton is so good

[046-131]about the schools.”

[046-132]“Ah! yes; but Lady Lufton is not a clergyman, Miss Robarts.”

[046-133]It was on Lucy’s tongue to say that her ladyship was pretty nearly as

[046-134]bad, but she stopped herself.

[046-135]At this moment Providence sent great relief to Miss Robarts in the

[046-136]shape of Mrs. Crawley’s red-armed maid-of-all-work, who, walking up

[046-137]to her master, whispered into his ear that he was wanted. It was the

[046-138]time of day at which his attendance was always required in his parish

[046-139]school; and that attendance being so punctually given, those who

[046-140]wanted him looked for him there at this hour, and if he were absent,

[046-141]did not scruple to send for him.

[046-142]“Miss Robarts, I am afraid you must excuse me,” said he, getting up

[046-143]and taking his hat and stick. Lucy begged that she might not be at

[046-144]all in the way, and already began to speculate how she might best

[046-145]unload her treasures. “Will you make my compliments to Mrs. Robarts,

[046-146]and say that I am sorry to miss the pleasure of wishing her good-bye?

[046-147]But I shall probably see her as she passes the school-house.” And

[046-148]then, stick in hand, he walked forth, and Lucy fancied that Bobby’s

[046-149]eyes immediately rested on the bag of gingerbread-nuts.

[046-150]“Bob,” said she, almost in a whisper, “do you like sugar-plums?”

[046-151]“Very much indeed,” said Bob, with exceeding gravity, and with his

[046-152]eye upon the window to see whether his father had passed.

[046-153]“Then come here,” said Lucy. But as she spoke the door again opened,

[046-154]and Mr. Crawley reappeared. “I have left a book behind me,” he

[046-155]said; and, coming back through the room, he took up the well-worn

[046-156]prayer-book which accompanied him in all his wanderings through

[046-157]the parish. Bobby, when he saw his father, had retreated a few

[046-158]steps back, as also did Grace, who, to confess the truth, had been

[046-159]attracted by the sound of sugar-plums, in spite of the irregular

[046-160]verbs. And Lucy withdrew her hand from her muff, and looked guilty.

[046-161]Was she not deceiving the good man—nay, teaching his own children to

[046-162]deceive him? But there are men made of such stuff that an angel could

[046-163]hardly live with them without some deceit.

[046-164]“Papa’s gone now,” whispered Bobby; “I saw him turn round the

[046-165]corner.” He, at any rate, had learned his lesson—as it was natural

[046-166]that he should do.

[046-167]Some one else, also, had learned that papa was gone; for while Bob

[046-168]and Grace were still counting the big lumps of sugar-candy, each

[046-169]employed the while for inward solace with an inch of barley-sugar,

[046-170]the front-door opened, and a big basket, and a bundle done up in a

[046-171]kitchen-cloth, made surreptitious entrance into the house, and

[046-172]were quickly unpacked by Mrs. Robarts herself on the table in Mrs.

[046-173]Crawley’s bedroom.

[046-174]“I did venture to bring them,” said Fanny, with a look of shame, “for

[046-175]I know how a sick child occupies the whole house.”

[046-176]“Ah! my friend,” said Mrs. Crawley, taking hold of Mrs. Robarts’ arm

[046-177]and looking into her face, “that sort of shame is over with me. God

[046-178]has tried us with want, and for my children’s sake I am glad of such

[046-179]relief.”

[046-180]“But will he be angry?”

[046-181]“I will manage it. Dear Mrs. Robarts, you must not be surprised at

[046-182]him. His lot is sometimes very hard to bear: such things are so much

[046-183]worse for a man than for a woman.”

[046-184]Fanny was not quite prepared to admit this in her own heart, but she

[046-185]made no reply on that head. “I am sure I hope we may be able to be

[046-186]of use to you,” she said, “if you will only look upon me as an old

[046-187]friend, and write to me if you want me. I hesitate to come frequently

[046-188]for fear that I should offend him.”
