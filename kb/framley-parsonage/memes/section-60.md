## Section 60

[060-001]“Politics make a terrible demand on a man’s time,” he said to his

[060-002]wife; and then went down to dine at his club in Pall Mall with sundry

[060-003]other young philogeants. On men of that class politics do make a

[060-004]great demand—at the hour of dinner and thereabouts.

[060-005]“What do you think of Miss Dunstable?” said Mrs. Gresham to her

[060-006]uncle, as they sat together over their coffee. She added nothing to

[060-007]the question, but asked it in all its baldness.

[060-008]“Think about her!” said the doctor. “Well, Mary; what do you think

[060-009]about her? I dare say we think the same.”

[060-010]“But that’s not the question. What do you think about her? Do you

[060-011]think she’s honest?”

[060-012]“Honest? Oh, yes, certainly—very honest, I should say.”

[060-013]“And good-tempered?”

[060-014]“Uncommonly good-tempered.”

[060-015]“And affectionate?”

[060-016]“Well; yes,—and affectionate. I should certainly say that she is

[060-017]affectionate.”

[060-018]“I’m sure she’s clever.”

[060-019]“Yes, I think she’s clever.”

[060-020]“And, and—and womanly in her feelings.” Mrs. Gresham felt that she

[060-021]could not quite say lady-like, though she would fain have done so had

[060-022]she dared.

[060-023]“Oh, certainly,” said the doctor. “But, Mary, why are you dissecting

[060-024]Miss Dunstable’s character with so much ingenuity?”

[060-025]“Well, uncle, I will tell you why; because—” and Mrs. Gresham, while

[060-026]she was speaking, got up from her chair, and going round the table to

[060-027]her uncle’s side, put her arm round his neck till her face was close

[060-028]to his, and then continued speaking as she stood behind him out of

[060-029]his sight—“because—I think that Miss Dunstable is—is very fond of

[060-030]you; and that it would make her happy if you would—ask her to be

[060-031]your wife.”

[060-032]“Mary!” said the doctor, turning round with an endeavour to look his

[060-033]niece in the face.

[060-034]“I am quite in earnest, uncle—quite in earnest. From little things

[060-035]that she has said, and little things that I have seen, I do believe

[060-036]what I now tell you.”

[060-037]“And you want me to—”

[060-038]“Dear uncle; my own one darling uncle, I want you only to do that

[060-039]which will make you—make you happy. What is Miss Dunstable to me

[060-040]compared to you?” And then she stooped down and kissed him.

[060-041]The doctor was apparently too much astounded by the intimation given

[060-042]him to make any further immediate reply. His niece, seeing this,

[060-043]left him that she might go and dress; and when they met again in the

[060-044]drawing-room Frank Gresham was with them.

[060-045]CHAPTER XXIX.

[060-046]MISS DUNSTABLE AT HOME.

[060-047]Miss Dunstable did not look like a love-lorn maiden, as she stood in

[060-048]a small ante-chamber at the top of her drawing-room stairs receiving

[060-049]her guests. Her house was one of those abnormal mansions, which are

[060-050]to be seen here and there in London, built in compliance rather with

[060-051]the rules of rural architecture, than with those which usually govern

[060-052]the erection of city streets and town terraces. It stood back from

[060-053]its brethren, and alone, so that its owner could walk round it. It

[060-054]was approached by a short carriageway; the chief door was in the back

[060-055]of the building; and the front of the house looked on to one of the

[060-056]parks. Miss Dunstable in procuring it had had her usual luck. It had

[060-057]been built by an eccentric millionnaire at an enormous cost; and

[060-058]the eccentric millionnaire, after living in it for twelve months,

[060-059]had declared that it did not possess a single comfort, and that

[060-060]it was deficient in most of those details which, in point of

[060-061]house accommodation, are necessary to the very existence of man.

[060-062]Consequently the mansion was sold, and Miss Dunstable was the

[060-063]purchaser. Cranbourn House it had been named, and its present owner

[060-064]had made no change in this respect; but the world at large very

[060-065]generally called it Ointment Hall, and Miss Dunstable herself as

[060-066]frequently used that name for it as any other. It was impossible to

[060-067]quiz Miss Dunstable with any success, because she always joined in

[060-068]the joke herself.

[060-069]Not a word further had passed between Mrs. Gresham and Dr. Thorne on

[060-070]the subject of their last conversation; but the doctor as he entered

[060-071]the lady’s portals amongst a tribe of servants and in a glare of

[060-072]light, and saw the crowd before him and the crowd behind him, felt

[060-073]that it was quite impossible that he should ever be at home there.

[060-074]It might be all right that a Miss Dunstable should live in this way,

[060-075]but it could not be right that the wife of Dr. Thorne should so live.

[060-076]But all this was a matter of the merest speculation, for he was

[060-077]well aware—as he said to himself a dozen times—that his niece had

[060-078]blundered strangely in her reading of Miss Dunstable’s character.

[060-079]When the Gresham party entered the ante-room into which the staircase

[060-080]opened, they found Miss Dunstable standing there surrounded by a few

[060-081]of her most intimate allies. Mrs. Harold Smith was sitting quite

[060-082]close to her; Dr. Easyman was reclining on a sofa against the wall,

[060-083]and the lady who habitually lived with Miss Dunstable was by his

[060-084]side. One or two others were there also, so that a little running

[060-085]conversation was kept up, in order to relieve Miss Dunstable of the

[060-086]tedium which might otherwise be engendered by the work she had in

[060-087]hand. As Mrs. Gresham, leaning on her husband’s arm, entered the

[060-088]room, she saw the back of Mrs. Proudie, as that lady made her way

[060-089]through the opposite door, leaning on the arm of the bishop.

[060-090]Mrs. Harold Smith had apparently recovered from the annoyance which

[060-091]she must no doubt have felt when Miss Dunstable so utterly rejected

[060-092]her suit on behalf of her brother. If any feeling had existed, even

[060-093]for a day, calculated to put a stop to the intimacy between the two

[060-094]ladies, that feeling had altogether died away, for Mrs. Harold Smith

[060-095]was conversing with her friend, quite in the old way. She made some

[060-096]remark on each of the guests as they passed by, and apparently did

[060-097]so in a manner satisfactory to the owner of the house, for Miss

[060-098]Dunstable answered with her kindest smiles, and in that genial, happy

[060-099]tone of voice which gave its peculiar character to her good humour:

[060-100]“She is quite convinced that you are a mere plagiarist in what you

[060-101]are doing,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, speaking of Mrs. Proudie.

[060-102]“And so I am. I don’t suppose there can be anything very original

[060-103]now-a-days about an evening party.”

[060-104]“But she thinks you are copying her.”

[060-105]“And why not? I copy everybody that I see, more or less. You did not

[060-106]at first begin to wear big petticoats out of your own head? If Mrs.

[060-107]Proudie has any such pride as that, pray don’t rob her of it. Here’s

[060-108]the doctor and the Greshams. Mary, my darling, how are you?” and in

[060-109]spite of all her grandeur of apparel, Miss Dunstable took hold of

[060-110]Mrs. Gresham and kissed her—to the disgust of the dozen-and-a-half

[060-111]of the distinguished fashionable world who were passing up the stairs

[060-112]behind.

[060-113]The doctor was somewhat repressed in his mode of address by the

[060-114]communication which had so lately been made to him. Miss Dunstable

[060-115]was now standing on the very top of the pinnacle of wealth, and

[060-116]seemed to him to be not only so much above his reach, but also so

[060-117]far removed from his track in life, that he could not in any way put

[060-118]himself on a level with her. He could neither aspire so high nor

[060-119]descend so low; and thinking of this he spoke to Miss Dunstable as

[060-120]though there were some great distance between them,—as though there

[060-121]had been no hours of intimate friendship down at Greshamsbury. There

[060-122]had been such hours, during which Miss Dunstable and Dr. Thorne had

[060-123]lived as though they belonged to the same world: and this at any rate

[060-124]may be said of Miss Dunstable, that she had no idea of forgetting

[060-125]them.

[060-126]Dr. Thorne merely gave her his hand, and then prepared to pass on.

[060-127]“Don’t go, doctor,” she said; “for heaven’s sake, don’t go yet. I

[060-128]don’t know when I may catch you if you get in there. I shan’t be able

[060-129]to follow you for the next two hours. Lady Meredith, I am so much

[060-130]obliged to you for coming—your mother will be here, I hope. Oh, I am

[060-131]so glad! From her you know that is quite a favour. You, Sir George,

[060-132]are half a sinner yourself, so I don’t think so much about it.”

[060-133]“Oh, quite so,” said Sir George; “perhaps rather the largest half.”

[060-134]“The men divide the world into gods and giants,” said Miss Dunstable.

[060-135]“We women have our divisions also. We are saints or sinners according

[060-136]to our party. The worst of it is, that we rat almost as often as you

[060-137]do.” Whereupon Sir George laughed and passed on.

[060-138]“I know, doctor, you don’t like this kind of thing,” she continued,

[060-139]“but there is no reason why you should indulge yourself altogether in

[060-140]your own way, more than another—is there, Frank?”

[060-141]“I am not so sure but he does like it,” said Mr. Gresham. “There are

[060-142]some of your reputed friends whom he owns that he is anxious to see.”

[060-143]“Are there? Then there is some hope of his ratting too. But he’ll

[060-144]never make a good staunch sinner; will he, Mary? You’re too old to

[060-145]learn new tricks; eh, doctor?”

[060-146]“I am afraid I am,” said the doctor, with a faint laugh.

[060-147]“Does Dr. Thorne rank himself among the army of saints?” asked Mrs.

[060-148]Harold Smith.

[060-149]“Decidedly,” said Miss Dunstable. “But you must always remember that

[060-150]there are saints of different orders; are there not, Mary? and nobody

[060-151]supposes that the Franciscans and the Dominicans agree very well

[060-152]together. Dr. Thorne does not belong to the school of St. Proudie,

[060-153]of Barchester; he would prefer the priestess whom I see coming round

[060-154]the corner of the staircase, with a very famous young novice at her

[060-155]elbow.”

[060-156]“From all that I can hear, you will have to reckon Miss Grantly among

[060-157]the sinners,” said Mrs. Harold Smith—seeing that Lady Lufton with

[060-158]her young friend was approaching—“unless, indeed, you can make a

[060-159]saint of Lady Hartletop.”

[060-160]And then Lady Lufton entered the room, and Miss Dunstable came

[060-161]forward to meet her with more quiet respect in her manner than she

[060-162]had as yet shown to many of her guests. “I am much obliged to you for

[060-163]coming, Lady Lufton,” she said, “and the more so, for bringing Miss

[060-164]Grantly with you.”

[060-165]Lady Lufton uttered some pretty little speech, during which Dr.

[060-166]Thorne came up and shook hands with her; as did also Frank Gresham

[060-167]and his wife. There was a county acquaintance between the Framley

[060-168]people and the Greshamsbury people, and therefore there was a little

[060-169]general conversation before Lady Lufton passed out of the small

[060-170]room into what Mrs. Proudie would have called the noble suite of

[060-171]apartments. “Papa will be here,” said Miss Grantly; “at least so I

[060-172]understand. I have not seen him yet myself.”

[060-173]“Oh, yes, he has promised me,” said Miss Dunstable; “and the

[060-174]archdeacon, I know, will keep his word. I should by no means have the

[060-175]proper ecclesiastical balance without him.”

[060-176]“Papa always does keep his word,” said Miss Grantly, in a tone

[060-177]that was almost severe. She had not at all understood poor Miss

[060-178]Dunstable’s little joke, or at any rate she was too dignified to

[060-179]respond to it.

[060-180]“I understand that old Sir John is to accept the Chiltern Hundreds

[060-181]at once,” said Lady Lufton, in a half whisper to Frank Gresham. Lady

[060-182]Lufton had always taken a keen interest in the politics of East

[060-183]Barsetshire, and was now desirous of expressing her satisfaction that

[060-184]a Gresham should again sit for the county. The Greshams had been old

[060-185]county members in Barsetshire, time out of mind.

[060-186]“Oh, yes; I believe so,” said Frank, blushing. He was still young

[060-187]enough to feel almost ashamed of putting himself forward for such

[060-188]high honours.
