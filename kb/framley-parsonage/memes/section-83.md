## Section 83

[083-001]Greshamsbury, — June, 185—.

[083-002]MY DEAR MISS DUNSTABLE,—

[083-003]When he had got so far, he leaned back in his chair and looked at the

[083-004]paper. How on earth was he to find words to say that which he now

[083-005]wished to have said? He had never written such a letter in his life,

[083-006]or anything approaching to it, and now found himself overwhelmed with

[083-007]a difficulty of which he had not previously thought. He spent another

[083-008]half-hour in looking at the paper, and was at last nearly deterred by

[083-009]this new difficulty. He would use the simplest, plainest language, he

[083-010]said to himself over and over again; but it is not always easy to use

[083-011]simple, plain language,—by no means so easy as to mount on stilts,

[083-012]and to march along with sesquipedalian words, with pathos, spasms,

[083-013]and notes of interjection. But the letter did at last get itself

[083-014]written, and there was not a note of interjection in it.

[083-015]MY DEAR MISS DUNSTABLE,—I think it right to confess that

[083-016]I should not be now writing this letter to you, had I not

[083-017]been led to believe by other judgment than my own that the

[083-018]proposition which I am going to make would be regarded

[083-019]by you with favour. Without such other judgment I should,

[083-020]I own, have feared that the great disparity between you

[083-021]and me in regard to money would have given to such a

[083-022]proposition an appearance of being false and mercenary.

[083-023]All I ask of you now, with confidence, is to acquit me of

[083-024]such fault as that.

[083-025]When you have read so far you will understand what I mean.

[083-026]We have known each other now somewhat intimately, though

[083-027]indeed not very long, and I have sometimes fancied that

[083-028]you were almost as well pleased to be with me as I have

[083-029]been to be with you. If I have been wrong in this, tell me

[083-030]so simply, and I will endeavour to let our friendship run

[083-031]on as though this letter had not been written. But if I

[083-032]have been right, and if it be possible that you can think

[083-033]that a union between us will make us both happier than we

[083-034]are single, I will plight you my word and troth with good

[083-035]faith, and will do what an old man may do to make the

[083-036]burden of the world lie light upon your shoulders. Looking

[083-037]at my age I can hardly keep myself from thinking that I

[083-038]am an old fool: but I try to reconcile myself to that by

[083-039]remembering that you yourself are no longer a girl. You

[083-040]see that I pay you no compliments, and that you need

[083-041]expect none from me.

[083-042]I do not know that I could add anything to the truth of

[083-043]this, if I were to write three times as much. All that is

[083-044]necessary is, that you should know what I mean. If you do

[083-045]not believe me to be true and honest already, nothing that

[083-046]I can write will make you believe it.

[083-047]God bless you. I know you will not keep me long in

[083-048]suspense for an answer.

[083-049]Affectionately your friend,

[083-050]THOMAS THORNE.

[083-051]When he had finished he meditated again for another half-hour whether

[083-052]it would not be right that he should add something about her money.

[083-053]Would it not be well for him to tell her—it might be said in a

[083-054]postscript—that with regard to all her wealth she would be free to

[083-055]do what she chose? At any rate he owed no debts for her to pay, and

[083-056]would still have his own income, sufficient for his own purposes. But

[083-057]about one o’clock he came to the conclusion that it would be better

[083-058]to leave the matter alone. If she cared for him, and could trust him,

[083-059]and was worthy also that he should trust her, no omission of such a

[083-060]statement would deter her from coming to him: and if there were no

[083-061]such trust, it would not be created by any such assurance on his

[083-062]part. So he read the letter over twice, sealed it, and took it up,

[083-063]together with his bed candle, into his bed-room. Now that the letter

[083-064]was written it seemed to be a thing fixed by fate that it must go.

[083-065]He had written it that he might see how it looked when written; but

[083-066]now that it was written, there remained no doubt but that it must be

[083-067]sent. So he went to bed, with the letter on the toilette-table beside

[083-068]him; and early in the morning—so early as to make it seem that the

[083-069]importance of the letter had disturbed his rest—he sent it off by a

[083-070]special messenger to Boxall Hill.

[083-071]“I’se wait for an answer?” said the boy.

[083-072]“No,” said the doctor: “leave the letter, and come away.”

[083-073]The breakfast hour was not very early at Boxall Hill in these summer

[083-074]months. Frank Gresham, no doubt, went round his farm before he came

[083-075]in for prayers, and his wife was probably looking to the butter

[083-076]in the dairy. At any rate, they did not meet till near ten, and

[083-077]therefore, though the ride from Greshamsbury to Boxall Hill was

[083-078]nearly two hours’ work, Miss Dunstable had her letter in her own room

[083-079]before she came down.

[083-080]She read it in silence as she was dressing, while the maid was with

[083-081]her in the room; but she made no sign which could induce her Abigail

[083-082]to think that the epistle was more than ordinarily important.

[083-083]She read it, and then quietly refolding it and placing it in the

[083-084]envelope, she put it down on the table at which she was sitting.

[083-085]It was full fifteen minutes afterwards that she begged her servant

[083-086]to see if Mrs. Gresham were still in her own room. “Because I want

[083-087]to see her for five minutes, alone, before breakfast,” said Miss

[083-088]Dunstable.

[083-089]“You traitor; you false, black traitor!” were the first words which

[083-090]Miss Dunstable spoke when she found herself alone with her friend.

[083-091]“Why, what’s the matter?”

[083-092]“I did not think there was so much mischief in you, nor so keen and

[083-093]commonplace a desire for match-making. Look here. Read the first four

[083-094]lines; not more, if you please; the rest is private. Whose is the

[083-095]other judgment of whom your uncle speaks in his letter?”

[083-096]“Oh, Miss Dunstable! I must read it all.”

[083-097]“Indeed you’ll do no such thing. You think it’s a love-letter, I dare

[083-098]say; but indeed there’s not a word about love in it.”

[083-099]“I know he has offered. I shall be so glad, for I know you like him.”

[083-100]“He tells me that I am an old woman, and insinuates that I may

[083-101]probably be an old fool.”

[083-102]“I am sure he does not say that.”

[083-103]“Ah! but I’m sure that he does. The former is true enough, and I

[083-104]never complain of the truth. But as to the latter, I am by no means

[083-105]so certain that it is true—not in the sense that he means it.”

[083-106]“Dear, dearest woman, don’t go on in that way now. Do speak out to

[083-107]me, and speak without jesting.”

[083-108]“Whose was the other judgment to whom he trusts so implicitly? Tell

[083-109]me that.”

[083-110]“Mine, mine, of course. No one else can have spoken to him about it.

[083-111]Of course I talked to him.”

[083-112]“And what did you tell him?”

[083-113]“I told him—”

[083-114]“Well, out with it. Let me have the real facts. Mind, I tell you

[083-115]fairly that you had no right to tell him anything. What passed

[083-116]between us, passed in confidence. But let us hear what you did say.”

[083-117]“I told him that you would have him if he offered.” And Mrs. Gresham,

[083-118]as she spoke, looked into her friend’s face doubtingly, not knowing

[083-119]whether in very truth Miss Dunstable were pleased with her or

[083-120]displeased. If she were displeased, then how had her uncle been

[083-121]deceived!

[083-122]“You told him that as a fact?”

[083-123]“I told him that I thought so.”

[083-124]“Then I suppose I am bound to have him,” said Miss Dunstable,

[083-125]dropping the letter on to the floor in mock despair.

[083-126]“My dear, dear, dearest woman!” said Mrs. Gresham, bursting into

[083-127]tears, and throwing herself on to her friend’s neck.

[083-128]“Mind you are a dutiful niece,” said Miss Dunstable. “And now let me

[083-129]go and finish dressing.”

[083-130]In the course of the afternoon, an answer was sent back to

[083-131]Greshamsbury, in these words:—

[083-132]DEAR DR. THORNE,—I do and will trust you in everything;

[083-133]and it shall be as you would have it. Mary writes to you;

[083-134]but do not believe a word she says. I never will again,

[083-135]for she has behaved so bad in this matter.

[083-136]Yours affectionately and very truly,

[083-137]MARTHA DUNSTABLE.

[083-138]“And so I am going to marry the richest woman in England,” said Dr.

[083-139]Thorne to himself, as he sat down that day to his mutton-chop.

[083-140]CHAPTER XL.

[083-141]INTERNECINE.

[083-142]It must be conceived that there was some feeling of triumph at

[083-143]Plumstead Episcopi, when the wife of the rector returned home with

[083-144]her daughter, the bride elect of the Lord Dumbello. The heir of the

[083-145]Marquis of Hartletop was, in wealth, the most considerable unmarried

[083-146]young nobleman of the day; he was noted, too, as a man difficult to

[083-147]be pleased, as one who was very fine and who gave himself airs,—and

[083-148]to have been selected as the wife of such a man as this was a great

[083-149]thing for the daughter of a parish clergyman. We have seen in what

[083-150]manner the happy girl’s mother communicated the fact to Lady Lufton,

[083-151]hiding, as it were, her pride under a veil; and we have seen also how

[083-152]meekly the happy girl bore her own great fortune, applying herself

[083-153]humbly to the packing of her clothes, as though she ignored her own

[083-154]glory.

[083-155]But nevertheless there was triumph at Plumstead Episcopi. The mother,

[083-156]when she returned home, began to feel that she had been thoroughly

[083-157]successful in the great object of her life. While she was yet in

[083-158]London she had hardly realized her satisfaction, and there were

[083-159]doubts then whether the cup might not be dashed from her lips before

[083-160]it was tasted. It might be that even the son of the Marquis of

[083-161]Hartletop was subject to parental authority, and that barriers should

[083-162]spring up between Griselda and her coronet; but there had been

[083-163]nothing of the kind. The archdeacon had been closeted with the

[083-164]marquis, and Mrs. Grantly had been closeted with the marchioness;

[083-165]and though neither of those noble persons had expressed themselves

[083-166]gratified by their son’s proposed marriage, so also neither of them

[083-167]had made any attempt to prevent it. Lord Dumbello was a man who had

[083-168]a will of his own,—as the Grantlys boasted amongst themselves. Poor

[083-169]Griselda! the day may perhaps come when this fact of her lord’s

[083-170]masterful will may not to her be matter of much boasting. But in

[083-171]London, as I was saying, there had been no time for an appreciation

[083-172]of the family joy. The work to be done was nervous in its nature,

[083-173]and self-glorification might have been fatal; but now, when they

[083-174]were safe at Plumstead, the great truth burst upon them in all its

[083-175]splendour.

[083-176]Mrs. Grantly had but one daughter, and the formation of that child’s

[083-177]character and her establishment in the world had been the one main

[083-178]object of the mother’s life. Of Griselda’s great beauty the Plumstead

[083-179]household had long been conscious; of her discretion also, of her

[083-180]conduct, and of her demeanour there had been no doubt. But the father

[083-181]had sometimes hinted to the mother that he did not think that Grizzy

[083-182]was quite so clever as her brothers. “I don’t agree with you at all,”

[083-183]Mrs. Grantly had answered. “Besides, what you call cleverness is not

[083-184]at all necessary in a girl; she is perfectly ladylike; even you won’t

[083-185]deny that.” The archdeacon had never wished to deny it, and was now

[083-186]fain to admit that what he had called cleverness was not necessary in

[083-187]a young lady.
