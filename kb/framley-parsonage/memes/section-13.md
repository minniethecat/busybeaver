## Section 13

[013-001]And then the others of the party sat upon a front bench covered with

[013-002]red cloth. “We shall find this very hard and very narrow about the

[013-003]second hour,” said Mr. Sowerby, and Mr. Smith on his dais again

[013-004]overheard the words, and dashed his gloves down to the table. He felt

[013-005]that all the room would hear it.

[013-006]And there were one or two gentlemen on the second seat who shook

[013-007]hands with some of our party. There was Mr. Thorne of Ullathorne,

[013-008]a good-natured old bachelor, whose residence was near enough

[013-009]to Barchester to allow of his coming in without much personal

[013-010]inconvenience; and next to him was Mr. Harding, an old clergyman

[013-011]of the chapter, with whom Mrs. Proudie shook hands very graciously,

[013-012]making way for him to seat himself close behind her if he would so

[013-013]please. But Mr. Harding did not so please. Having paid his respects

[013-014]to the bishop he returned quietly to the side of his old friend Mr.

[013-015]Thorne, thereby angering Mrs. Proudie, as might easily be seen by her

[013-016]face. And Mr. Chadwick also was there, the episcopal man of business

[013-017]for the diocese; but he also adhered to the two gentlemen above

[013-018]named.

[013-019]And now that the bishop and the ladies had taken their places, Mr.

[013-020]Harold Smith relifted his gloves and again laid them down, hummed

[013-021]three times distinctly, and then began.

[013-022]“It was,” he said, “the most peculiar characteristic of the present

[013-023]era in the British islands that those who were high placed before the

[013-024]world in rank, wealth, and education were willing to come forward

[013-025]and give their time and knowledge without fee or reward, for the

[013-026]advantage and amelioration of those who did not stand so high in the

[013-027]social scale.” And then he paused for a moment, during which Mrs.

[013-028]Smith remarked to Miss Dunstable that that was pretty well for a

[013-029]beginning; and Miss Dunstable replied, “that as for herself she felt

[013-030]very grateful to rank, wealth, and education.” Mr. Sowerby winked

[013-031]to Mr. Supplehouse, who opened his eyes very wide and shrugged his

[013-032]shoulders. But the Barchesterians took it all in good part and gave

[013-033]the lecturer the applause of their hands and feet.

[013-034]And then, well pleased, he recommenced—“I do not make these remarks

[013-035]with reference to myself—”

[013-036]“I hope he’s not going to be modest,” said Miss Dunstable.

[013-037]“It will be quite new if he is,” replied Mrs. Smith.

[013-038]“—so much as to many noble and talented lords and members of the

[013-039]Lower House who have lately from time to time devoted themselves to

[013-040]this good work.” And then he went through a long list of peers and

[013-041]members of Parliament, beginning, of course, with Lord Boanerges, and

[013-042]ending with Mr. Green Walker, a young gentleman who had lately been

[013-043]returned by his uncle’s interest for the borough of Crewe Junction,

[013-044]and had immediately made his entrance into public life by giving a

[013-045]lecture on the grammarians of the Latin language as exemplified at

[013-046]Eton school.

[013-047]“On the present occasion,” Mr. Smith continued, “our object is to

[013-048]learn something as to those grand and magnificent islands which lie

[013-049]far away, beyond the Indies, in the Southern Ocean; the lands of

[013-050]which produce rich spices and glorious fruits, and whose seas are

[013-051]imbedded with pearls and corals,—Papua and the Philippines, Borneo

[013-052]and the Moluccas. My friends, you are familiar with your maps, and

[013-053]you know the track which the equator makes for itself through those

[013-054]distant oceans.” And then many heads were turned down, and there was

[013-055]a rustle of leaves; for not a few of those “who stood not so high in

[013-056]the social scale” had brought their maps with them, and refreshed

[013-057]their memories as to the whereabouts of these wondrous islands.

[013-058]And then Mr. Smith also, with a map in his hand, and pointing

[013-059]occasionally to another large map which hung against the wall, went

[013-060]into the geography of the matter. “We might have found that out from

[013-061]our atlases, I think, without coming all the way to Barchester,”

[013-062]said that unsympathizing helpmate, Mrs. Harold, very cruelly—most

[013-063]illogically too, for there be so many things which we could find out

[013-064]ourselves by search, but which we never do find out unless they be

[013-065]specially told us; and why should not the latitude and longitude of

[013-066]Labuan be one—or rather two of these things?

[013-067]And then, when he had duly marked the path of the line through

[013-068]Borneo, Celebes, and Gilolo, through the Macassar strait and the

[013-069]Molucca passage, Mr. Harold Smith rose to a higher flight. “But

[013-070]what,” said he, “avails all that God can give to man, unless man will

[013-071]open his hand to receive the gift? And what is this opening of the

[013-072]hand but the process of civilization—yes, my friends, the process

[013-073]of civilization? These South Sea islanders have all that a kind

[013-074]Providence can bestow on them; but that all is as nothing without

[013-075]education. That education and that civilization it is for you to

[013-076]bestow upon them—yes, my friends, for you; for you, citizens of

[013-077]Barchester as you are.” And then he paused again, in order that the

[013-078]feet and hands might go to work. The feet and hands did go to work,

[013-079]during which Mr. Smith took a slight drink of water.

[013-080]He was now quite in his element and had got into the proper way of

[013-081]punching the table with his fists. A few words dropping from Mr.

[013-082]Sowerby did now and again find their way to his ears, but the sound

[013-083]of his own voice had brought with it the accustomed charm, and he ran

[013-084]on from platitude to truism, and from truism back to platitude, with

[013-085]an eloquence that was charming to himself.

[013-086]“Civilization,” he exclaimed, lifting up his eyes and hands to the

[013-087]ceiling. “Oh, civilization—”

[013-088]“There will not be a chance for us now for the next hour and a half,”

[013-089]said Mr. Supplehouse, groaning.

[013-090]Harold Smith cast one eye down at him, but it immediately flew back

[013-091]to the ceiling.

[013-092]“Oh, civilization! thou that ennoblest mankind and makest him equal

[013-093]to the gods, what is like unto thee?” Here Mrs. Proudie showed

[013-094]evident signs of disapprobation, which no doubt would have been

[013-095]shared by the bishop, had not that worthy prelate been asleep. But

[013-096]Mr. Smith continued unobservant; or, at any rate, regardless.

[013-097]“What is like unto thee? Thou art the irrigating stream which makest

[013-098]fertile the barren plain. Till thou comest all is dark and dreary;

[013-099]but at thy advent the noontide sun shines out, the earth gives forth

[013-100]her increase; the deep bowels of the rocks render up their tribute.

[013-101]Forms which were dull and hideous become endowed with grace and

[013-102]beauty, and vegetable existence rises to the scale of celestial life.

[013-103]Then, too, Genius appears clad in a panoply of translucent armour,

[013-104]grasping in his hand the whole terrestrial surface, and making every

[013-105]rood of earth subservient to his purposes;—Genius, the child of

[013-106]civilization, the mother of the Arts!”

[013-107]The last little bit, taken from the Pedigree of Progress, had a

[013-108]great success, and all Barchester went to work with its hands

[013-109]and feet;—all Barchester, except that ill-natured aristocratic

[013-110]front-row together with the three arm-chairs at the corner of it.

[013-111]The aristocratic front-row felt itself to be too intimate with

[013-112]civilization to care much about it; and the three arm-chairs, or

[013-113]rather that special one which contained Mrs. Proudie, considered

[013-114]that there was a certain heathenness, a pagan sentimentality almost

[013-115]amounting to infidelity, contained in the lecturer’s remarks, with

[013-116]which she, a pillar of the Church, could not put up, seated as she

[013-117]was now in public conclave.

[013-118]“It is to civilization that we must look,” continued Mr. Harold

[013-119]Smith, descending from poetry to prose as a lecturer well knows how,

[013-120]and thereby showing the value of both—“for any material progress in

[013-121]these islands; and—”

[013-122]“And to Christianity,” shouted Mrs. Proudie, to the great amazement

[013-123]of the assembled people and to the thorough wakening of the bishop,

[013-124]who, jumping up in his chair at the sound of the well-known voice,

[013-125]exclaimed, “Certainly, certainly.”

[013-126]“Hear, hear, hear,” said those on the benches who particularly

[013-127]belonged to Mrs. Proudie’s school of divinity in the city, and among

[013-128]the voices was distinctly heard that of a new verger in whose behalf

[013-129]she had greatly interested herself.

[013-130]“Oh, yes, Christianity of course,” said Harold Smith, upon whom the

[013-131]interruption did not seem to operate favourably.

[013-132]“Christianity and Sabbath-day observance,” exclaimed Mrs. Proudie,

[013-133]who, now that she had obtained the ear of the public, seemed well

[013-134]inclined to keep it. “Let us never forget that these islanders can

[013-135]never prosper unless they keep the Sabbath holy.”

[013-136]Poor Mr. Smith, having been so rudely dragged from his high horse,

[013-137]was never able to mount it again, and completed the lecture in a

[013-138]manner not at all comfortable to himself. He had there, on the table

[013-139]before him, a huge bundle of statistics with which he had meant to

[013-140]convince the reason of his hearers after he had taken full possession

[013-141]of their feelings. But they fell very dull and flat. And at the

[013-142]moment when he was interrupted he was about to explain that that

[013-143]material progress to which he had alluded could not be attained

[013-144]without money; and that it behoved them, the people of Barchester

[013-145]before him, to come forward with their purses like men and brothers.

[013-146]He did also attempt this; but from the moment of that fatal onslaught

[013-147]from the arm-chair, it was clear to him and to every one else, that

[013-148]Mrs. Proudie was now the hero of the hour. His time had gone by, and

[013-149]the people of Barchester did not care a straw for his appeal.

[013-150]From these causes the lecture was over full twenty minutes earlier

[013-151]than any one had expected, to the great delight of Messrs. Sowerby

[013-152]and Supplehouse, who, on that evening, moved and carried a vote of

[013-153]thanks to Mrs. Proudie. For they had gay doings yet before they went

[013-154]to their beds.

[013-155]“Robarts, here one moment,” Mr. Sowerby said, as they were standing

[013-156]at the door of the Mechanics’ Institute. “Don’t you go off with Mr.

[013-157]and Mrs. Bishop. We are going to have a little supper at the Dragon

[013-158]of Wantly, and after what we have gone through upon my word we want

[013-159]it. You can tell one of the palace servants to let you in.”

[013-160]Mark considered the proposal wistfully. He would fain have joined the

[013-161]supper-party had he dared; but he, like many others of his cloth, had

[013-162]the fear of Mrs. Proudie before his eyes.

[013-163]And a very merry supper they had; but poor Mr. Harold Smith was not

[013-164]the merriest of the party.

[013-165]CHAPTER VII.

[013-166]SUNDAY MORNING.

[013-167]It was, perhaps, quite as well on the whole for Mark Robarts, that he

[013-168]did not go to that supper party. It was eleven o’clock before they

[013-169]sat down, and nearly two before the gentlemen were in bed. It must

[013-170]be remembered that he had to preach, on the coming Sunday morning,

[013-171]a charity sermon on behalf of a mission to Mr. Harold Smith’s

[013-172]islanders; and, to tell the truth, it was a task for which he had now

[013-173]very little inclination.

[013-174]When first invited to do this, he had regarded the task seriously

[013-175]enough, as he always did regard such work, and he completed his

[013-176]sermon for the occasion before he left Framley; but, since that, an

[013-177]air of ridicule had been thrown over the whole affair, in which he

[013-178]had joined without much thinking of his own sermon, and this made him

[013-179]now heartily wish that he could choose a discourse upon any other

[013-180]subject.

[013-181]He knew well that the very points on which he had most insisted, were

[013-182]those which had drawn most mirth from Miss Dunstable and Mrs. Smith,

[013-183]and had oftenest provoked his own laughter; and how was he now to

[013-184]preach on those matters in a fitting mood, knowing, as he would know,

[013-185]that those two ladies would be looking at him, would endeavour to

[013-186]catch his eye, and would turn him into ridicule as they had already

[013-187]turned the lecturer?
