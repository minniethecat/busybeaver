# Lord De Terrier

Lord De Terrier is the recognised leader of the Tory opposition during the events of *Framley Parsonage* — a man whose political position is acknowledged even by those who doubt his capacity, and whose name, like that of Lord Petty Bag, carries a faint whiff of Trollopian satire (047-163).

## Role in the Novel

Lord De Terrier's narrative function is to represent the alternative to Lord Brock's Whig ministry — the possibility, always present in the novel's political calculations, that the wheel of patronage might turn and distribute its favours in a different direction. When the political world speculates about the survival of Brock's government, the question is always whether "they can't support Lord De Terrier" — whether the alternative is viable enough to precipitate a change (047-167).

Harold Smith's political manoeuvrings take place in the space between Brock and De Terrier: he "has been a good deal with Lord De Terrier," cultivating the opposition leader even as he serves in Brock's ministry — the characteristic posture of the ambitious politician who serves one master while keeping a foot in the other camp (049-064).

## Character Traits

- **Symbolic leadership**: De Terrier is less a character than a political position — the leader of the opposition whose function is to exist as an alternative rather than to govern as a minister. The question "can they support Lord De Terrier?" is about party viability, not personal merit (047-167).
- **Political instrumentality**: Like Lord Brock, De Terrier is a figure whose power lies in what he can give — places, preferments, the currency of patronage that makes the political world turn. Harold Smith's cultivation of De Terrier is an investment in a possible future government (049-064).

## Relationships

- **[[lord-brock|Lord Brock]]**: His political antagonist. The relationship between Prime Minister and Leader of the Opposition defines the political landscape within which all the novel's ecclesiastical preferments are distributed.
- **[[mr-harold-smith|Harold Smith]]**: A political operator who maintains connections with both Brock and De Terrier. Smith's cultivation of De Terrier is the prudent hedging of a man who understands that today's opposition may be tomorrow's government (049-064).

## Key Scenes

- **The question of support (Chapter 47)**: "Can they support such men as Sidonia and Lord De Terrier?" The question, posed in political debate, captures the perpetual uncertainty of party leadership — not whether De Terrier is capable, but whether his party can sustain him (047-161) (047-167).
- **Harold Smith's cultivation (Chapters 48–49)**: Smith's visits to Lord De Terrier — "he has been a good deal with Lord De Terrier" — are the small change of political ambition, the quiet work of a man who understands that political survival requires friends on both sides of the House (049-064).

## Social & Historical Context

The name "De Terrier" is one of Trollope's minor satirical touches — a suggestion, perhaps, of the tenacity (terrier-like) with which politicians cling to office, or of the small-dog yapping of parliamentary opposition. In the political landscape of the 1850s, the Tory party — still recovering from the trauma of the Corn Law repeal — was led by Lord Derby, a man of considerable talents who nonetheless struggled to present a credible alternative to the Whig-Liberal ascendancy. Trollope's portrait of De Terrier is gentler than his satire of Lord Petty Bag, but it shares the same understanding: political leadership in the unreformed parliament was as much about managing personal connections as about articulating principles.
