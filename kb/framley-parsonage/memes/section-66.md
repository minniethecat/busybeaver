## Section 66

[066-001]Lord Lufton in love with Lucy! As these words repeated themselves

[066-002]over and over again within Mark Robarts’s mind, his mind added

[066-003]to them notes of surprise without end. How had it possibly come

[066-004]about,—and why? In his estimation his sister Lucy was a very simple

[066-005]girl—not plain indeed, but by no means beautiful; certainly not

[066-006]stupid, but by no means brilliant. And then, he would have said, that

[066-007]of all men whom he knew, Lord Lufton would have been the last to

[066-008]fall in love with such a girl as his sister. And now, what was he

[066-009]to say or do? What views was he bound to hold? In what direction

[066-010]should he act? There was Lady Lufton on the one side, to whom

[066-011]he owed everything. How would life be possible to him in that

[066-012]parsonage—within a few yards of her elbow—if he consented to

[066-013]receive Lord Lufton as the acknowledged suitor of his sister? It

[066-014]would be a great match for Lucy, doubtless; but— Indeed, he could

[066-015]not bring himself to believe that Lucy could in truth become the

[066-016]absolute reigning queen of Framley Court.

[066-017]“Do you think that Fanny knows anything of all this?” he said, after

[066-018]a moment or two.

[066-019]“I cannot possibly tell. If she does it is not with my knowledge. I

[066-020]should have thought that you could best answer that.”

[066-021]“I cannot answer it at all,” said Mark. “I, at least, have had no

[066-022]remotest idea of such a thing.”

[066-023]“Your ideas of it now need not be at all remote,” said Lord Lufton,

[066-024]with a faint smile; “and you may know it as a fact. I did make her an

[066-025]offer of marriage; I was refused; I am going to repeat it; and I am

[066-026]now taking you into my confidence, in order that, as her brother, and

[066-027]as my friend, you may give me such assistance as you can.” They then

[066-028]walked on in silence for some yards, after which Lord Lufton added:

[066-029]“And now I’ll dine with you to-day if you wish it.”

[066-030]Mr. Robarts did not know what to say; he could not bethink himself

[066-031]what answer duty required of him. He had no right to interfere

[066-032]between his sister and such a marriage, if she herself should wish

[066-033]it; but still there was something terrible in the thought of it! He

[066-034]had a vague conception that it must come to evil; that the project

[066-035]was a dangerous one; and that it could not finally result happily for

[066-036]any of them. What would Lady Lufton say? That undoubtedly was the

[066-037]chief source of his dismay.

[066-038]“Have you spoken to your mother about this?” he said.

[066-039]“My mother? no; why speak to her till I know my fate? A man does not

[066-040]like to speak much of such matters if there be a probability of his

[066-041]being rejected. I tell you because I do not like to make my way into

[066-042]your house under a false pretence.”

[066-043]“But what would Lady Lufton say?”

[066-044]“I think it probable that she would be displeased on the first

[066-045]hearing it; that in four-and-twenty hours she would be reconciled;

[066-046]and that after a week or so Lucy would be her dearest favourite and

[066-047]the prime minister of all her machinations. You don’t know my mother

[066-048]as well as I do. She would give her head off her shoulders to do me a

[066-049]pleasure.”

[066-050]“And for that reason,” said Mark Robarts, “you ought, if possible, to

[066-051]do her pleasure.”

[066-052]“I cannot absolutely marry a wife of her choosing, if you mean that,”

[066-053]said Lord Lufton.

[066-054]They went on walking about the garden for an hour, but they hardly

[066-055]got any farther than the point to which we have now brought them.

[066-056]Mark Robarts could not make up his mind on the spur of the moment;

[066-057]nor, as he said more than once to Lord Lufton, could he be at all

[066-058]sure that Lucy would in any way be guided by him. It was, therefore,

[066-059]at last settled between them that Lord Lufton should come to the

[066-060]parsonage immediately after breakfast on the following morning. It

[066-061]was agreed also that the dinner had better not come off, and Robarts

[066-062]promised that he would, if possible, have determined by the morning

[066-063]as to what advice he would give his sister.

[066-064]He went direct home to the parsonage from Framley Court, feeling that

[066-065]he was altogether in the dark till he should have consulted his wife.

[066-066]How would he feel if Lucy were to become Lady Lufton? and how would

[066-067]he look Lady Lufton in the face in telling her that such was to be

[066-068]his sister’s destiny? On returning home he immediately found his

[066-069]wife, and had not been closeted with her five minutes before he knew,

[066-070]at any rate, all that she knew.

[066-071]“And you mean to say that she does love him?” said Mark.

[066-072]“Indeed she does; and is it not natural that she should? When I saw

[066-073]them so much together I feared that she would. But I never thought

[066-074]that he would care for her.”

[066-075]Even Fanny did not as yet give Lucy credit for half her

[066-076]attractiveness. After an hour’s talking the interview between the

[066-077]husband and wife ended in a message to Lucy, begging her to join them

[066-078]both in the book-room.

[066-079]“Aunt Lucy,” said a chubby little darling, who was taken up into his

[066-080]aunt’s arms as he spoke, “papa and mamma ’ant ’oo in te tuddy, and I

[066-081]musn’t go wis ’oo.”

[066-082]Lucy, as she kissed the boy and pressed his face against her own,

[066-083]felt that her blood was running quick to her heart.

[066-084]“Musn’t ’oo go wis me, my own one?” she said, as she put her

[066-085]playfellow down; but she played with the child only because she

[066-086]did not wish to betray even to him that she was hardly mistress of

[066-087]herself. She knew that Lord Lufton was at Framley; she knew that her

[066-088]brother had been to him; she knew that a proposal had been made that

[066-089]he should come there that day to dinner. Must it not therefore be the

[066-090]case that this call to a meeting in the study had arisen out of Lord

[066-091]Lufton’s arrival at Framley? and yet, how could it have done so? Had

[066-092]Fanny betrayed her in order to prevent the dinner invitation? It

[066-093]could not be possible that Lord Lufton himself should have spoken on

[066-094]the subject! And then she again stooped to kiss the child, rubbed

[066-095]her hands across her forehead to smooth her hair, and erase, if

[066-096]that might be possible, the look of care which she wore, and then

[066-097]descended slowly to her brother’s sitting-room.

[066-098]Her hand paused for a second on the door ere she opened it, but she

[066-099]had resolved that, come what might, she would be brave. She pushed it

[066-100]open and walked in with a bold front, with eyes wide open, and a slow

[066-101]step.

[066-102]“Frank says that you want me,” she said.

[066-103]Mr. Robarts and Fanny were both standing up by the fireplace, and

[066-104]each waited a second for the other to speak when Lucy entered the

[066-105]room; and then Fanny began,—

[066-106]“Lord Lufton is here, Lucy.”

[066-107]“Here! Where? At the parsonage?”

[066-108]“No, not at the parsonage; but over at Framley Court,” said Mark.

[066-109]“And he promises to call here after breakfast to-morrow,” said Fanny.

[066-110]And then again there was a pause. Mrs. Robarts hardly dared to look

[066-111]Lucy in the face. She had not betrayed her trust, seeing that the

[066-112]secret had been told to Mark, not by her, but by Lord Lufton; but she

[066-113]could not but feel that Lucy would think that she had betrayed it.

[066-114]“Very well,” said Lucy, trying to smile; “I have no objection in

[066-115]life.”

[066-116]“But, Lucy, dear,”—and now Mrs. Robarts put her arm round her

[066-117]sister-in-law’s waist,—“he is coming here especially to see you.”

[066-118]“Oh; that makes a difference. I am afraid that I shall be—engaged.”

[066-119]“He has told everything to Mark,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[066-120]Lucy now felt that her bravery was almost deserting her. She hardly

[066-121]knew which way to look or how to stand. Had Fanny told everything

[066-122]also? There was so much that Fanny knew that Lord Lufton could not

[066-123]have known. But, in truth, Fanny had told all—the whole story of

[066-124]Lucy’s love, and had described the reasons which had induced her to

[066-125]reject her suitor; and had done so in words which, had Lord Lufton

[066-126]heard them, would have made him twice as passionate in his love.

[066-127]And then it certainly did occur to Lucy to think why Lord Lufton

[066-128]should have come to Framley and told all this history to her brother.

[066-129]She attempted for a moment to make herself believe that she was angry

[066-130]with him for doing so. But she was not angry. She had not time to

[066-131]argue much about it, but there came upon her a gratified sensation of

[066-132]having been remembered, and thought of, and—loved. Must it not be

[066-133]so? Could it be possible that he himself would have told this tale to

[066-134]her brother, if he did not still love her? Fifty times she had said

[066-135]to herself that his offer had been an affair of the moment, and fifty

[066-136]times she had been unhappy in so saying. But this new coming of his

[066-137]could not be an affair of the moment. She had been the dupe, she had

[066-138]thought, of an absurd passion on her own part; but now—how was it

[066-139]now? She did not bring herself to think that she should ever be Lady

[066-140]Lufton. She had still, in some perversely obstinate manner, made up

[066-141]her mind against that result. But yet, nevertheless, it did in some

[066-142]unaccountable manner satisfy her to feel that Lord Lufton had himself

[066-143]come down to Framley and himself told this story.

[066-144]“He has told everything to Mark,” said Mrs. Robarts; and then again

[066-145]there was a pause for a moment, during which these thoughts passed

[066-146]through Lucy’s mind.

[066-147]“Yes,” said Mark, “he has told me all, and he is coming here

[066-148]to-morrow morning that he may receive an answer from yourself.”

[066-149]“What answer?” said Lucy, trembling.

[066-150]“Nay, dearest; who can say that but yourself?” and her sister-in-law,

[066-151]as she spoke, pressed close against her. “You must say that

[066-152]yourself.”

[066-153]Mrs. Robarts in her long conversation with her husband had pleaded

[066-154]strongly on Lucy’s behalf, taking, as it were, a part against Lady

[066-155]Lufton. She had said that if Lord Lufton persevered in his suit, they

[066-156]at the parsonage could not be justified in robbing Lucy of all that

[066-157]she had won for herself, in order to do Lady Lufton’s pleasure.

[066-158]“But she will think,” said Mark, “that we have plotted and intrigued

[066-159]for this. She will call us ungrateful, and will make Lucy’s life

[066-160]wretched.” To which the wife had answered, that all that must be

[066-161]left in God’s hands. They had not plotted or intrigued. Lucy, though

[066-162]loving the man in her heart of hearts, had already once refused him,

[066-163]because she would not be thought to have snatched at so great a

[066-164]prize. But if Lord Lufton loved her so warmly that he had come down

[066-165]there in this manner, on purpose, as he himself had put it, that he

[066-166]might learn his fate, then—so argued Mrs. Robarts—they two, let

[066-167]their loyalty to Lady Lufton be ever so strong, could not justify it

[066-168]to their consciences to stand between Lucy and her lover. Mark had

[066-169]still somewhat demurred to this, suggesting how terrible would be

[066-170]their plight if they should now encourage Lord Lufton, and if he,

[066-171]after such encouragement, when they should have quarrelled with Lady

[066-172]Lufton, should allow himself to be led away from his engagement by

[066-173]his mother. To which Fanny had answered that justice was justice, and

[066-174]that right was right. Everything must be told to Lucy, and she must

[066-175]judge for herself.

[066-176]“But I do not know what Lord Lufton wants,” said Lucy, with her eyes

[066-177]fixed upon the ground, and now trembling more than ever. “He did come

[066-178]to me, and I did give him an answer.”
