## Section 29

[029-001]It was difficult for any one to understand Lucy’s state of mind at

[029-002]present, and it can hardly be said that she understood it herself.

[029-003]She felt that she had received a severe blow in having been thus made

[029-004]the subject of remark with reference to Lord Lufton. She knew that

[029-005]her pleasant evenings at Framley Court were now over, and that she

[029-006]could not again talk to him in an unrestrained tone and without

[029-007]embarrassment. She had felt the air of the whole place to be very

[029-008]cold before her intimacy with him, and now it must be cold again.

[029-009]Two homes had been open to her, Framley Court and the parsonage; and

[029-010]now, as far as comfort was concerned, she must confine herself to

[029-011]the latter. She could not again be comfortable in Lady Lufton’s

[029-012]drawing-room.

[029-013]But then she could not help asking herself whether Lady Lufton was

[029-014]not right. She had had courage enough, and presence of mind, to joke

[029-015]about the matter when her sister-in-law spoke to her, and yet she

[029-016]was quite aware that it was no joking matter. Lord Lufton had not

[029-017]absolutely made love to her, but he had latterly spoken to her

[029-018]in a manner which she knew was not compatible with that ordinary

[029-019]comfortable masculine friendship with the idea of which she had once

[029-020]satisfied herself. Was not Fanny right when she said that intimate

[029-021]friendships of that nature were dangerous things?

[029-022]Yes, Lucy, very dangerous. Lucy, before she went to bed that night,

[029-023]had owned to herself that they were so; and lying there with

[029-024]sleepless eyes and a moist pillow, she was driven to confess that the

[029-025]label would in truth be now too late, that the caution had come to

[029-026]her after the poison had been swallowed. Was there any antidote? That

[029-027]was all that was left for her to consider. But, nevertheless, on the

[029-028]following morning she could appear quite at her ease. And when Mark

[029-029]had left the house after breakfast, she could still joke with Fanny

[029-030]as to Lady Lufton’s poison cupboard.

[029-033]And then there was that other trouble in Lady Lufton’s mind, the

[029-034]sins, namely, of her selected parson. She had selected him, and she

[029-035]was by no means inclined to give him up, even though his sins against

[029-036]parsondom were grievous. Indeed she was a woman not prone to give

[029-037]up anything, and of all things not prone to give up a _protégé_.

[029-038]The very fact that she herself had selected him was the strongest

[029-039]argument in his favour.

[029-040]But his sins against parsondom were becoming very grievous in her

[029-041]eyes, and she was at a loss to know what steps to take. She hardly

[029-042]dared to take him to task, him himself. Were she to do so, and should

[029-043]he then tell her to mind her own business—as he probably might do,

[029-044]though not in those words—there would be a schism in the parish; and

[029-045]almost anything would be better than that. The whole work of her life

[029-046]would be upset, all the outlets of her energy would be impeded if not

[029-047]absolutely closed, if a state of things were to come to pass in which

[029-048]she and the parson of her parish should not be on good terms.

[029-049]But what was to be done? Early in the winter he had gone to

[029-050]Chaldicotes and to Gatherum Castle, consorting with gamblers, Whigs,

[029-051]atheists, men of loose pleasure, and Proudieites. That she had

[029-052]condoned; and now he was turning out a hunting parson on her hands.

[029-053]It was all very well for Fanny to say that he merely looked at the

[029-054]hounds as he rode about his parish. Fanny might be deceived. Being

[029-055]his wife, it might be her duty not to see her husband’s iniquities.

[029-056]But Lady Lufton could not be deceived. She knew very well in what

[029-057]part of the county Cobbold’s Ashes lay. It was not in Framley

[029-058]parish, nor in the next parish to it. It was half-way across to

[029-059]Chaldicotes—in the western division; and she had heard of that run

[029-060]in which two horses had been killed, and in which Parson Robarts had

[029-061]won such immortal glory among West Barsetshire sportsmen. It was not

[029-062]easy to keep Lady Lufton in the dark as to matters occurring in her

[029-063]own county.

[029-064]All these things she knew, but as yet had not noticed, grieving over

[029-065]them in her own heart the more on that account. Spoken grief relieves

[029-066]itself; and when one can give counsel, one always hopes at least

[029-067]that that counsel will be effective. To her son she had said, more

[029-068]than once, that it was a pity that Mr. Robarts should follow the

[029-069]hounds.—“The world has agreed that it is unbecoming in a clergyman,”

[029-070]she would urge, in her deprecatory tone. But her son would by no

[029-071]means give her any comfort. “He doesn’t hunt, you know—not as I do,”

[029-072]he would say. “And if he did, I really don’t see the harm of it. A

[029-073]man must have some amusement, even if he be an archbishop.” “He has

[029-074]amusement at home,” Lady Lufton would answer. “What does his wife

[029-075]do—and his sister?” This allusion to Lucy, however, was very soon

[029-076]dropped.

[029-077]Lord Lufton would in no wise help her. He would not even passively

[029-078]discourage the vicar, or refrain from offering to give him a seat in

[029-079]going to the meets. Mark and Lord Lufton had been boys together, and

[029-080]his lordship knew that Mark in his heart would enjoy a brush across

[029-081]the country quite as well as he himself; and then what was the harm

[029-082]of it?

[029-083]Lady Lufton’s best aid had been in Mark’s own conscience. He had

[029-084]taken himself to task more than once, and had promised himself that

[029-085]he would not become a sporting parson. Indeed, where would be his

[029-086]hopes of ulterior promotion, if he allowed himself to degenerate

[029-087]so far as that? It had been his intention, in reviewing what he

[029-088]considered to be the necessary proprieties of clerical life, in

[029-089]laying out his own future mode of living, to assume no peculiar

[029-090]sacerdotal strictness; he would not be known as a denouncer of

[029-091]dancing or of card-tables, of theatres or of novel-reading; he would

[029-092]take the world around him as he found it, endeavouring by precept

[029-093]and practice to lend a hand to the gradual amelioration which

[029-094]Christianity is producing; but he would attempt no sudden or majestic

[029-095]reforms. Cake and ale would still be popular, and ginger be hot in

[029-096]the mouth, let him preach ever so—let him be never so solemn a

[029-097]hermit; but a bright face, a true trusting heart, a strong arm, and

[029-098]an humble mind, might do much in teaching those around him that men

[029-099]may be gay and yet not profligate, that women may be devout and yet

[029-100]not dead to the world.

[029-101]Such had been his ideas as to his own future life; and though many

[029-102]would think that as a clergyman he should have gone about his work

[029-103]with more serious devotion of thought, nevertheless there was some

[029-104]wisdom in them;—some folly also, undoubtedly, as appeared by the

[029-105]troubles into which they led him.

[029-106]“I will not affect to think that to be bad,” said he to himself,

[029-107]“which in my heart of hearts does not seem to be bad.” And thus he

[029-108]resolved that he might live without contamination among hunting

[029-109]squires. And then, being a man only too prone by nature to do as

[029-110]others did around him, he found by degrees that that could hardly be

[029-111]wrong for him which he admitted to be right for others.

[029-112]But still his conscience upbraided him, and he declared to himself

[029-113]more than once that after this year he would hunt no more. And then

[029-114]his own Fanny would look at him on his return home on those days in a

[029-115]manner that cut him to the heart. She would say nothing to him. She

[029-116]never inquired in a sneering tone, and with angry eyes, whether he

[029-117]had enjoyed his day’s sport; but when he spoke of it, she could not

[029-118]answer him with enthusiasm; and in other matters which concerned him

[029-119]she was always enthusiastic.

[029-120]After a while, too, he made matters worse, for about the end of March

[029-121]he did another very foolish thing. He almost consented to buy an

[029-122]expensive horse from Sowerby—an animal which he by no means wanted,

[029-123]and which, if once possessed, would certainly lead him into further

[029-124]trouble. A gentleman, when he has a good horse in his stable,

[029-125]does not like to leave him there eating his head off. If he be a

[029-126]gig-horse, the owner of him will be keen to drive a gig; if a hunter,

[029-127]the happy possessor will wish to be with a pack of hounds.

[029-128]“Mark,” said Sowerby to him one day, when they were out together,

[029-129]“this brute of mine is so fresh, I can hardly ride him; you are young

[029-130]and strong; change with me for an hour or so.” And then they did

[029-131]change, and the horse on which Robarts found himself mounted went

[029-132]away with him beautifully.

[029-133]“He’s a splendid animal,” said Mark, when they again met.

[029-134]“Yes, for a man of your weight. He’s thrown away upon me;—too much

[029-135]of a horse for my purposes. I don’t get along now quite as well as I

[029-136]used to do. He is a nice sort of hunter; just rising six, you know.”

[029-137]How it came to pass that the price of the splendid animal was

[029-138]mentioned between them, I need not describe with exactness. But it

[029-139]did come to pass that Mr. Sowerby told the parson that the horse

[029-140]should be his for £130.

[029-141]“And I really wish you’d take him,” said Sowerby. “It would be the

[029-142]means of partially relieving my mind of a great weight.”

[029-143]Mark looked up into his friend’s face with an air of surprise, for he

[029-144]did not at the moment understand how this should be the case.

[029-145]“I am afraid, you know, that you will have to put your hand into your

[029-146]pocket sooner or later about that accursed bill—” Mark shrank as the

[029-147]profane word struck his ears—“and I should be glad to think that you

[029-148]had got something in hand in the way of value.”

[029-149]“Do you mean that I shall have to pay the whole sum of £500?”

[029-150]“Oh! dear, no; nothing of the kind. But something I dare say you will

[029-151]have to pay: if you like to take Dandy for a hundred and thirty, you

[029-152]can be prepared for that amount when Tozer comes to you. The horse is

[029-153]dog cheap, and you will have a long day for your money.”

[029-154]Mark at first declared, in a quiet, determined tone, that he did not

[029-155]want the horse; but it afterwards appeared to him that if it were so

[029-156]fated that he must pay a portion of Mr. Sowerby’s debts, he might as

[029-157]well repay himself to any extent within his power. It would be as

[029-158]well perhaps that he should take the horse and sell him. It did not

[029-159]occur to him that by so doing he would put it in Mr. Sowerby’s power

[029-160]to say that some valuable consideration had passed between them with

[029-161]reference to this bill, and that he would be aiding that gentleman

[029-162]in preparing an inextricable confusion of money-matters between them.

[029-163]Mr. Sowerby well knew the value of this. It would enable him to make

[029-164]a plausible story, as he had done in that other case of Lord Lufton.

[029-165]“Are you going to have Dandy?” Sowerby said to him again.

[029-166]“I can’t say that I will just at present,” said the parson. “What

[029-167]should I want of him now the season’s over?”

[029-168]“Exactly, my dear fellow; and what do I want of him now the season’s

[029-169]over? If it were the beginning of October instead of the end of

[029-170]March, Dandy would be up at two hundred and thirty instead of one: in

[029-171]six months’ time that horse will be worth anything you like to ask

[029-172]for him. Look at his bone.”
