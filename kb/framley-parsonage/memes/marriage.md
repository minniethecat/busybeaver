# Lord Lufton's Marriage to Lucy Robarts

The **marriage of Lord Lufton to Lucy Robarts** is the romantic climax of _Framley Parsonage_ and the event through which Trollope delivers the novel's most sustained argument for the claims of personal merit over social station. The courtship — opposed by [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]], complicated by Lucy's own reticence, and ultimately blessed by the very woman who sought to prevent it — constitutes the emotional and moral heart of the novel.

## Summary

[[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]], the heir to [[framley-court|Framley Court]], falls in love with [[lucy-robarts|Lucy Robarts]], the sister of his friend [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]] and a young woman of no fortune and modest family. His mother, Lady Lufton, regards the match as impossible — a betrayal of family duty that would degrade the Lufton name. She attempts to redirect her son's affections toward more suitable objects, while Lucy herself, conscious of the social distance between them, resists Lord Lufton's advances out of a principled unwillingness to enter a family that does not want her.

> "Happy marriages, men say, are made in heaven, and I believe it. Most marriages are fairly happy" (PN 043-092–093)

The turning point comes when Lady Lufton, through a series of events that reveal Lucy's strength of character, comes to recognize the worth of the woman she has spurned. Her eventual acceptance — "You made Lucy an offer of marriage!" (PN 065-175) — is both a personal conversion and a symbolic concession: the old order yielding, however reluctantly, to the claims of individual merit.

> "You made Lucy an offer of marriage!" (PN 065-175)

## Key Participants

- **[[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]]**: The suitor, whose determination to marry for love rather than advantage pits him against his mother's authority. His persistence — and his willingness to defy Lady Lufton — marks his emergence from boyish dependence to adult independence.

- **[[lucy-robarts|Lucy Robarts]]**: The beloved, whose quiet dignity and moral strength make her one of Trollope's most admired heroines. She refuses Lord Lufton not from lack of feeling but from a principled refusal to be the cause of family discord. "Her marriage" was "hardly as yet more than" a possibility she dared not hope for (PN 016-068).

- **[[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]**: The obstacle, whose opposition to the marriage is rooted in genuine conviction rather than mere snobbery. Her eventual acceptance of Lucy represents her finest moral achievement — the triumph of love and Christian charity over pride and convention.

- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: Lucy's brother, whose own social entanglement with the Lufton family complicates his position. His financial dependence on Lady Lufton makes him a reluctant participant in the drama.

- **[[fanny-robarts|Fanny Robarts]]**: Mark's wife, who supports Lucy's romance while navigating the delicate diplomacy required by Lady Lufton's opposition.

## Narrative Significance

The marriage plot functions as the novel's argument for moral meritocracy. Lucy Robarts has neither fortune, nor title, nor powerful connections — the conventional assets of the Victorian marriage market. What she possesses is integrity, intelligence, and a strength of character that the novel presents as intrinsically superior to the advantages of birth. Her eventual triumph — marriage to Lord Lufton with Lady Lufton's blessing — is the novel's verdict on the relative claims of birth and worth.

This argument is made more powerful by Trollope's refusal to sentimentalize it. Lady Lufton's opposition is not portrayed as mere villainy but as a reasonable (if limited) expression of her understanding of duty. Her conversion is gradual, earned by Lucy's demonstrated character rather than by any melodramatic revelation. The marriage, when it comes, feels like a genuine moral victory rather than a conventional happy ending.
