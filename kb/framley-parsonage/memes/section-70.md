## Section 70

[070-001]“Robarts, you will be sorry some day for the cruelty of your words.”

[070-002]“I wonder whether you will ever be sorry for the cruelty of your

[070-003]doings, or whether these things are really a joke to you.”

[070-004]“I am at this moment a ruined man,” said Sowerby. “Everything is

[070-005]going from me,—my place in the world, the estate of my family, my

[070-006]father’s house, my seat in Parliament, the power of living among my

[070-007]countrymen, or, indeed, of living anywhere;—but all this does not

[070-008]oppress me now so much as the misery which I have brought upon you.”

[070-009]And then Sowerby also turned away his face, and wiped from his eyes

[070-010]tears which were not artificial.

[070-011]Robarts was still walking up and down the room, but it was not

[070-012]possible for him to continue his reproaches after this. This is

[070-013]always the case. Let a man endure to heap contumely on his own head,

[070-014]and he will silence the contumely of others—for the moment. Sowerby,

[070-015]without meditating on the matter, had had some inkling of this, and

[070-016]immediately saw that there was at last an opening for conversation.

[070-017]“You are unjust to me,” said he, “in supposing that I have now no

[070-018]wish to save you. It is solely in the hope of doing so that I have

[070-019]come here.”

[070-020]“And what is your hope? That I should accept another brace of bills,

[070-021]I suppose.”

[070-022]“Not a brace; but one renewed bill for—”

[070-023]“Look here, Mr. Sowerby. On no earthly consideration that can be put

[070-024]before me will I again sign my name to any bill in the guise of an

[070-025]acceptance. I have been very weak, and am ashamed of my weakness; but

[070-026]so much strength as that, I hope, is left to me. I have been very

[070-027]wicked, and am ashamed of my wickedness; but so much right principle

[070-028]as that, I hope, remains. I will put my name to no other bill; not

[070-029]for you, not even for myself.”

[070-030]“But, Robarts, under your present circumstances that will be

[070-031]madness.”

[070-032]“Then I will be mad.”

[070-033]“Have you seen Forrest? If you will speak to him I think you will

[070-034]find that everything can be accommodated.”

[070-035]“I already owe Mr. Forrest a hundred and fifty pounds, which I

[070-036]obtained from him when you pressed me for the price of that horse,

[070-037]and I will not increase the debt. What a fool I was again there.

[070-038]Perhaps you do not remember that, when I agreed to buy the horse, the

[070-039]price was to be my contribution to the liquidation of these bills.”

[070-040]“I do remember it; but I will tell you how that was.”

[070-041]“It does not signify. It has been all of a piece.”

[070-042]“But listen to me. I think you would feel for me if you knew all

[070-043]that I have gone through. I pledge you my solemn word that I

[070-044]had no intention of asking you for the money when you took the

[070-045]horse;—indeed I had not. But you remember that affair of Lufton’s,

[070-046]when he came to you at your hotel in London and was so angry about an

[070-047]outstanding bill.”

[070-048]“I know that he was very unreasonable as far as I was concerned.”

[070-049]“He was so; but that makes no difference. He was resolved, in his

[070-050]rage, to expose the whole affair; and I saw that, if he did so, it

[070-051]would be most injurious to you, seeing that you had just accepted

[070-052]your stall at Barchester.” Here the poor prebendary winced terribly.

[070-053]“I moved heaven and earth to get up that bill. Those vultures stuck

[070-054]to their prey when they found the value which I attached to it, and

[070-055]I was forced to raise above a hundred pounds at the moment to obtain

[070-056]possession of it, although every shilling absolutely due on it had

[070-057]long since been paid. Never in my life did I wish to get money as I

[070-058]did to raise that hundred and twenty pounds; and as I hope for mercy

[070-059]in my last moments, I did that for your sake. Lufton could not have

[070-060]injured me in that matter.”

[070-061]“But you told him that you got it for twenty-five pounds.”

[070-062]“Yes, I told him so. I was obliged to tell him that, or I should have

[070-063]apparently condemned myself by showing how anxious I was to get it.

[070-064]And you know I could not have explained all this before him and you.

[070-065]You would have thrown up the stall in disgust.”

[070-066]Would that he had! That was Mark’s wish now,—his futile wish. In

[070-067]what a slough of despond had he come to wallow in consequence of his

[070-068]folly on that night at Gatherum Castle! He had then done a silly

[070-069]thing, and was he now to rue it by almost total ruin? He was sickened

[070-070]also with all these lies. His very soul was dismayed by the dirt

[070-071]through which he was forced to wade. He had become unconsciously

[070-072]connected with the lowest dregs of mankind, and would have to see his

[070-073]name mingled with theirs in the daily newspapers. And for what had he

[070-074]done this? Why had he thus filed his mind and made himself a disgrace

[070-075]to his cloth? In order that he might befriend such a one as Mr.

[070-076]Sowerby!

[070-077]“Well,” continued Sowerby, “I did get the money, but you would

[070-078]hardly believe the rigour of the pledge which was exacted from me

[070-079]for repayment. I got it from Harold Smith, and never, in my worst

[070-080]straits, will I again look to him for assistance. I borrowed it only

[070-081]for a fortnight; and in order that I might repay it, I was obliged to

[070-082]ask you for the price of the horse. Mark, it was on your behalf that

[070-083]I did all this,—indeed it was.”

[070-084]“And now I am to repay you for your kindness by the loss of all that

[070-085]I have in the world.”

[070-086]“If you will put the affair into the hands of Mr. Forrest, nothing

[070-087]need be touched,—not a hair of a horse’s back; no, not though you

[070-088]should be obliged to pay the whole amount yourself, gradually out

[070-089]of your income. You must execute a series of bills, falling due

[070-090]quarterly, and then—”

[070-091]“I will execute no bill, I will put my name to no paper in the

[070-092]matter; as to that my mind is fully made up. They may come and do

[070-093]their worst.”

[070-094]Mr. Sowerby persevered for a long time, but he was quite unable to

[070-095]move the parson from this position. He would do nothing towards

[070-096]making what Mr. Sowerby called an arrangement, but persisted that he

[070-097]would remain at home at Framley, and that any one who had a claim

[070-098]upon him might take legal steps.

[070-099]“I shall do nothing myself,” he said; “but if proceedings against

[070-100]me be taken, I shall prove that I have never had a shilling of the

[070-101]money.” And in this resolution he quitted the Dragon of Wantly.

[070-102]Mr. Sowerby at one time said a word as to the expediency of borrowing

[070-103]that sum of money from John Robarts; but as to this Mark would say

[070-104]nothing. Mr. Sowerby was not the friend with whom he now intended to

[070-105]hold consultation in such matters. “I am not at present prepared,” he

[070-106]said, “to declare what I may do; I must first see what steps others

[070-107]take.” And then he took his hat and went off; and mounting his horse

[070-108]in the yard of the Dragon of Wantly—that horse which he had now so

[070-109]many reasons to dislike—he slowly rode back home.

[070-110]Many thoughts passed through his mind during that ride, but only one

[070-111]resolution obtained for itself a fixture there. He must now tell his

[070-112]wife everything. He would not be so cruel as to let it remain untold

[070-113]until a bailiff were at the door, ready to walk him off to the county

[070-114]gaol, or until the bed on which they slept was to be sold from under

[070-115]them. Yes, he would tell her everything,—immediately, before his

[070-116]resolution could again have faded away. He got off his horse in the

[070-117]yard, and seeing his wife’s maid at the kitchen door, desired her to

[070-118]beg her mistress to come to him in the book-room. He would not allow

[070-119]one half-hour to pass towards the waning of his purpose. If it be

[070-120]ordained that a man shall drown, had he not better drown and have

[070-121]done with it?

[070-122]Mrs. Robarts came to him in his room, reaching him in time to touch

[070-123]his arm as he entered it.

[070-124]“Mary says you want me. I have been gardening, and she caught me just

[070-125]as I came in.”

[070-126]“Yes, Fanny, I do want you. Sit down for a moment.” And walking

[070-127]across the room, he placed his whip in its proper place.

[070-128]“Oh, Mark, is there anything the matter?”

[070-129]“Yes, dearest; yes. Sit down, Fanny; I can talk to you better if you

[070-130]will sit.”

[070-131]But she, poor lady, did not wish to sit. He had hinted at some

[070-132]misfortune, and therefore she felt a longing to stand by him and

[070-133]cling to him.

[070-134]“Well, there; I will if I must; but, Mark, do not frighten me. Why is

[070-135]your face so very wretched?”

[070-136]“Fanny, I have done very wrong,” he said. “I have been very foolish.

[070-137]I fear that I have brought upon you great sorrow and trouble.” And

[070-138]then he leaned his head upon his hand and turned his face away from

[070-139]her.

[070-140]“Oh, Mark, dearest Mark, my own Mark! what is it?” and then she was

[070-141]quickly up from her chair, and went down on her knees before him. “Do

[070-142]not turn from me. Tell me, Mark! tell me, that we may share it.”

[070-143]“Yes, Fanny, I must tell you now; but I hardly know what you will

[070-144]think of me when you have heard it.”

[070-145]“I will think that you are my own husband, Mark; I will think

[070-146]that—that chiefly, whatever it may be.” And then she caressed his

[070-147]knees, and looked up in his face, and, getting hold of one of his

[070-148]hands, pressed it between her own. “Even if you have been foolish,

[070-149]who should forgive you if I cannot?”

[070-150]And then he told it her all, beginning from that evening when Mr.

[070-151]Sowerby had got him into his bedroom, and going on gradually, now

[070-152]about the bills, and now about the horses, till his poor wife was

[070-153]utterly lost in the complexity of the accounts. She could by no

[070-154]means follow him in the details of his story; nor could she quite

[070-155]sympathize with him in his indignation against Mr. Sowerby, seeing

[070-156]that she did not comprehend at all the nature of the renewing of a

[070-157]bill. The only part to her of importance in the matter was the amount

[070-158]of money which her husband would be called upon to pay;—that and

[070-159]her strong hope, which was already a conviction, that he would never

[070-160]again incur such debts.

[070-161]“And how much is it, dearest, altogether?”

[070-162]“These men claim nine hundred pounds of me.”

[070-163]“Oh, dear! that is a terrible sum.”

[070-164]“And then there is the hundred and fifty which I have borrowed from

[070-165]the bank—the price of the horse, you know; and there are some other

[070-166]debts,—not a great deal, I think; but people will now look for every

[070-167]shilling that is due to them. If I have to pay it all, it will be

[070-168]twelve or thirteen hundred pounds.”

[070-169]“That will be as much as a year’s income, Mark; even with the stall.”

[070-170]That was the only word of reproach she said,—if that could be called

[070-171]a reproach.

[070-172]“Yes,” he said; “and it is claimed by men who will have no pity in

[070-173]exacting it at any sacrifice, if they have the power. And to think

[070-174]that I should have incurred all this debt without having received

[070-175]anything for it. Oh, Fanny, what will you think of me!”
