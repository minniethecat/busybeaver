[001-061]CHAPTER I.

[001-062]“OMNES OMNIA BONA DICERE.”

[001-063]When young Mark Robarts was leaving college, his father might well

[001-064]declare that all men began to say all good things to him, and to

[001-065]extol his fortune in that he had a son blessed with so excellent a

[001-066]disposition.

[001-067]This father was a physician living at Exeter. He was a gentleman

[001-068]possessed of no private means, but enjoying a lucrative practice,

[001-069]which had enabled him to maintain and educate a family with all the

[001-070]advantages which money can give in this country. Mark was his eldest

[001-071]son and second child; and the first page or two of this narrative

[001-072]must be consumed in giving a catalogue of the good things which

[001-073]chance and conduct together had heaped upon this young man’s head.

[001-074]His first step forward in life had arisen from his having been

[001-075]sent, while still very young, as a private pupil to the house of a

[001-076]clergyman, who was an old friend and intimate friend of his father’s.

[001-077]This clergyman had one other, and only one other, pupil—the young

[001-078]Lord Lufton; and between the two boys, there had sprung up a close

[001-079]alliance.

[001-080]While they were both so placed, Lady Lufton had visited her son,

[001-081]and then invited young Robarts to pass his next holidays at Framley

[001-082]Court. This visit was made; and it ended in Mark going back to Exeter

[001-083]with a letter full of praise from the widowed peeress. She had been

[001-084]delighted, she said, in having such a companion for her son, and

[001-085]expressed a hope that the boys might remain together during the

[001-086]course of their education. Dr. Robarts was a man who thought much of

[001-087]the breath of peers and peeresses, and was by no means inclined to

[001-088]throw away any advantage which might arise to his child from such a

[001-089]friendship. When, therefore, the young lord was sent to Harrow, Mark

[001-090]Robarts went there also.

[001-091]That the lord and his friend often quarrelled, and occasionally

[001-092]fought,—the fact even that for one period of three months they never

[001-093]spoke to each other—by no means interfered with the doctor’s hopes.

[001-094]Mark again and again stayed a fortnight at Framley Court, and Lady

[001-095]Lufton always wrote about him in the highest terms.

[001-096]And then the lads went together to Oxford, and here Mark’s good

[001-097]fortune followed him, consisting rather in the highly respectable

[001-098]manner in which he lived, than in any wonderful career of collegiate

[001-099]success. His family was proud of him, and the doctor was always ready

[001-100]to talk of him to his patients; not because he was a prizeman, and

[001-101]had gotten medals and scholarships, but on account of the excellence

[001-102]of his general conduct. He lived with the best set—he incurred no

[001-103]debts—he was fond of society, but able to avoid low society—liked

[001-104]his glass of wine, but was never known to be drunk; and, above all

[001-105]things, was one of the most popular men in the university.

[001-106]Then came the question of a profession for this young Hyperion,

[001-107]and on this subject, Dr. Robarts was invited himself to go over to

[001-108]Framley Court to discuss the matter with Lady Lufton. Dr. Robarts

[001-109]returned with a very strong conception that the Church was the

[001-110]profession best suited to his son.

[001-111]Lady Lufton had not sent for Dr. Robarts all the way from Exeter for

[001-112]nothing. The living of Framley was in the gift of the Lufton family,

[001-113]and the next presentation would be in Lady Lufton’s hands, if it

[001-114]should fall vacant before the young lord was twenty-five years of

[001-115]age, and in the young lord’s hands if it should fall afterwards. But

[001-116]the mother and the heir consented to give a joint promise to Dr.

[001-117]Robarts. Now, as the present incumbent was over seventy, and as the

[001-118]living was worth £900 a year, there could be no doubt as to the

[001-119]eligibility of the clerical profession.

[001-120]And I must further say, that the dowager and the doctor were

[001-121]justified in their choice by the life and principles of the young

[001-122]man—as far as any father can be justified in choosing such a

[001-123]profession for his son, and as far as any lay impropriator can be

[001-124]justified in making such a promise. Had Lady Lufton had a second son,

[001-125]that second son would probably have had the living, and no one would

[001-126]have thought it wrong;—certainly not if that second son had been

[001-127]such a one as Mark Robarts.

[001-128]Lady Lufton herself was a woman who thought much on religious

[001-129]matters, and would by no means have been disposed to place any one in

[001-130]a living, merely because such a one had been her son’s friend. Her

[001-131]tendencies were High Church, and she was enabled to perceive that

[001-132]those of young Mark Robarts ran in the same direction. She was very

[001-133]desirous that her son should make an associate of his clergyman, and

[001-134]by this step she would insure, at any rate, that. She was anxious

[001-135]that the parish vicar should be one with whom she could herself fully

[001-136]co-operate, and was perhaps unconsciously wishful that he might in

[001-137]some measure be subject to her influence. Should she appoint an elder

[001-138]man, this might probably not be the case to the same extent; and

[001-139]should her son have the gift, it might probably not be the case at

[001-140]all. And therefore it was resolved that the living should be given to

[001-141]young Robarts.

[001-142]He took his degree—not with any brilliancy, but quite in the manner

[001-143]that his father desired; he then travelled for eight or ten months

[001-144]with Lord Lufton and a college don, and almost immediately after his

[001-145]return home was ordained.

[001-146]The living of Framley is in the diocese of Barchester; and, seeing

[001-147]what were Mark’s hopes with reference to that diocese, it was by no

[001-148]means difficult to get him a curacy within it. But this curacy he was

[001-149]not allowed long to fill. He had not been in it above a twelvemonth,

[001-150]when poor old Dr. Stopford, the then vicar of Framley, was gathered

[001-151]to his fathers, and the full fruition of his rich hopes fell upon his

[001-152]shoulders.

[001-153]But even yet more must be told of his good fortune before we can come

[001-154]to the actual incidents of our story. Lady Lufton, who, as I have

[001-155]said, thought much of clerical matters, did not carry her High Church

[001-156]principles so far as to advocate celibacy for the clergy. On the

[001-157]contrary, she had an idea that a man could not be a good parish

[001-158]parson without a wife. So, having given to her favourite a position

[001-159]in the world, and an income sufficient for a gentleman’s wants, she

[001-160]set herself to work to find him a partner in those blessings.

[001-161]And here also, as in other matters, he fell in with the views of

[001-162]his patroness—not, however, that they were declared to him in that

[001-163]marked manner in which the affair of the living had been broached.

[001-164]Lady Lufton was much too highly gifted with woman’s craft for that.

[001-165]She never told the young vicar that Miss Monsell accompanied her

[001-166]ladyship’s married daughter to Framley Court expressly that he, Mark,

[001-167]might fall in love with her; but such was in truth the case.

[001-168]Lady Lufton had but two children. The eldest, a daughter, had been

[001-169]married some four or five years to Sir George Meredith, and this

[001-170]Miss Monsell was a dear friend of hers. And now looms before me the

[001-171]novelist’s great difficulty. Miss Monsell,—or, rather, Mrs. Mark

[001-172]Robarts,—must be described. As Miss Monsell, our tale will have

[001-173]to take no prolonged note of her. And yet we will call her Fanny

[001-174]Monsell, when we declare that she was one of the pleasantest

[001-175]companions that could be brought near to a man, as the future partner

[001-176]of his home, and owner of his heart. And if high principles without

[001-177]asperity, female gentleness without weakness, a love of laughter

[001-178]without malice, and a true loving heart, can qualify a woman to be a

[001-179]parson’s wife, then was Fanny Monsell qualified to fill that station.

[001-180]In person she was somewhat larger than common. Her face would have

[001-181]been beautiful but that her mouth was large. Her hair, which was

[001-182]copious, was of a bright brown; her eyes also were brown, and, being

[001-183]so, were the distinctive feature of her face, for brown eyes are not

[001-184]common. They were liquid, large, and full either of tenderness or of

[001-185]mirth. Mark Robarts still had his accustomed luck, when such a girl

[001-186]as this was brought to Framley for his wooing.

[001-187]And he did woo her—and won her. For Mark himself was a handsome

[001-188]fellow. At this time the vicar was about twenty-five years of age,

[001-189]and the future Mrs. Robarts was two or three years younger. Nor did

[001-190]she come quite empty-handed to the vicarage. It cannot be said that

[001-191]Fanny Monsell was an heiress, but she had been left with a provision

[001-192]of some few thousand pounds. This was so settled, that the interest

[001-193]of his wife’s money paid the heavy insurance on his life which

[001-194]young Robarts effected, and there was left to him, over and above,

[001-195]sufficient to furnish his parsonage in the very best style of

[001-196]clerical comfort,—and to start him on the road of life rejoicing.

[001-197]So much did Lady Lufton do for her _protégé_, and it may well be

[001-198]imagined that the Devonshire physician, sitting meditative over his

[001-199]parlour fire, looking back, as men will look back on the upshot of

[001-200]their life, was well contented with that upshot, as regarded his

[001-201]eldest offshoot, the Rev. Mark Robarts, the vicar of Framley.

[001-202]But little has as yet been said, personally, as to our hero himself,

[001-203]and perhaps it may not be necessary to say much. Let us hope that by

[001-204]degrees he may come forth upon the canvas, showing to the beholder

[001-205]the nature of the man inwardly and outwardly. Here it may suffice

[001-206]to say that he was no born heaven’s cherub, neither was he a born

[001-207]fallen devil’s spirit. Such as his training made him, such he was.

[001-208]He had large capabilities for good—and aptitudes also for evil,

[001-209]quite enough: quite enough to make it needful that he should repel

[001-210]temptation as temptation only can be repelled. Much had been done to

[001-211]spoil him, but in the ordinary acceptation of the word he was not

[001-212]spoiled. He had too much tact, too much common sense, to believe

[001-213]himself to be the paragon which his mother thought him. Self-conceit

[001-214]was not, perhaps, his greatest danger. Had he possessed more of it,

[001-215]he might have been a less agreeable man, but his course before him

[001-216]might on that account have been the safer.
