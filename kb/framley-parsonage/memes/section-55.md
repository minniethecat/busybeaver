## Section 55

[055-001]“Go back to Devonshire!” and Mrs. Robarts looked as though she

[055-002]thought that her sister-in-law was going mad. “Why do you want to go

[055-003]away from us? This is to be your own, own home, always now.”

[055-004]“Is it? Then I am in a bad way. Oh dear, oh dear, what a fool I am!

[055-005]What an idiot I’ve been! Fanny, I don’t think I can stay here; and I

[055-006]do so wish I’d never come. I do—I do—I do, though you look at me so

[055-007]horribly,” and jumping up she threw herself into her sister-in-law’s

[055-008]arms and began kissing her violently. “Don’t pretend to be wounded,

[055-009]for you know that I love you. You know that I could live with you all

[055-010]my life, and think you were perfect—as you are; but—”

[055-011]“Has Mark said anything?”

[055-012]“Not a word,—not a ghost of a syllable. It is not Mark; oh, Fanny!”

[055-013]“I am afraid I know what you mean,” said Mrs. Robarts in a low

[055-014]tremulous voice, and with deep sorrow painted on her face.

[055-015]“Of course you do; of course you know; you have known it all along:

[055-016]since that day in the pony-carriage. I knew that you knew it. You do

[055-017]not dare to mention his name: would not that tell me that you know

[055-018]it? And I, I am hypocrite enough for Mark; but my hypocrisy won’t

[055-019]pass muster before you. And, now, had I not better go to Devonshire?”

[055-020]“Dearest, dearest Lucy.”

[055-021]“Was I not right about that labelling? O heavens! what idiots we

[055-022]girls are! That a dozen soft words should have bowled me over like a

[055-023]ninepin, and left me without an inch of ground to call my own. And

[055-024]I was so proud of my own strength; so sure that I should never be

[055-025]missish, and spoony, and sentimental! I was so determined to like him

[055-026]as Mark does, or you—”

[055-027]“I shall not like him at all if he has spoken words to you that he

[055-028]should not have spoken.”

[055-029]“But he has not.” And then she stopped a moment to consider. “No, he

[055-030]has not. He never said a word to me that would make you angry with

[055-031]him if you knew of it. Except, perhaps, that he called me Lucy; and

[055-032]that was my fault, not his.”

[055-033]“Because you talked of soft words.”

[055-034]“Fanny, you have no idea what an absolute fool I am, what an

[055-035]unutterable ass. The soft words of which I tell you were of the kind

[055-036]which he speaks to you when he asks you how the cow gets on which he

[055-037]sent you from Ireland, or to Mark about Ponto’s shoulder. He told me

[055-038]that he knew papa, and that he was at school with Mark, and that as

[055-039]he was such good friends with you here at the parsonage, he must be

[055-040]good friends with me too. No; it has not been his fault. The soft

[055-041]words which did the mischief were such as those. But how well his

[055-042]mother understood the world! In order to have been safe, I should not

[055-043]have dared to look at him.”

[055-044]“But, dearest Lucy—”

[055-045]“I know what you are going to say, and I admit it all. He is no hero.

[055-046]There is nothing on earth wonderful about him. I never heard him say

[055-047]a single word of wisdom, or utter a thought that was akin to poetry.

[055-048]He devotes all his energies to riding after a fox or killing poor

[055-049]birds, and I never heard of his doing a single great action in my

[055-050]life. And yet—”

[055-051]Fanny was so astounded by the way her sister-in-law went on, that she

[055-052]hardly knew how to speak. “He is an excellent son, I believe,” at

[055-053]last she said.

[055-054]“Except when he goes to Gatherum Castle. I’ll tell you what he has:

[055-055]he has fine straight legs, and a smooth forehead, and a good-humoured

[055-056]eye, and white teeth. Was it possible to see such a catalogue of

[055-057]perfections, and not fall down, stricken to the very bone? But it was

[055-058]not that that did it all, Fanny. I could have stood against that. I

[055-059]think I could at least. It was his title that killed me. I had never

[055-060]spoken to a lord before. Oh, me! what a fool, what a beast I have

[055-061]been!” And then she burst out into tears.

[055-062]Mrs. Robarts, to tell the truth, could hardly understand poor Lucy’s

[055-063]ailment. It was evident enough that her misery was real; but yet she

[055-064]spoke of herself and her sufferings with so much irony, with so near

[055-065]an approach to joking, that it was very hard to tell how far she was

[055-066]in earnest. Lucy, too, was so much given to a species of badinage

[055-067]which Mrs. Robarts did not always quite understand, that the latter

[055-068]was afraid sometimes to speak out what came uppermost to her tongue.

[055-069]But now that Lucy was absolutely in tears, and was almost breathless

[055-070]with excitement, she could not remain silent any longer. “Dearest

[055-071]Lucy, pray do not speak in that way; it will all come right. Things

[055-072]always do come right when no one has acted wrongly.”

[055-073]“Yes, when nobody has done wrongly. That’s what papa used to call

[055-074]begging the question. But I’ll tell you what, Fanny; I will not be

[055-075]beaten. I will either kill myself or get through it. I am so heartily

[055-076]self-ashamed that I owe it to myself to fight the battle out.”

[055-077]“To fight what battle, dearest?”

[055-078]“This battle. Here, now, at the present moment, I could not meet Lord

[055-079]Lufton. I should have to run like a scared fowl if he were to show

[055-080]himself within the gate; and I should not dare to go out of the

[055-081]house, if I knew that he was in the parish.”

[055-082]“I don’t see that, for I am sure you have not betrayed yourself.”

[055-083]“Well, no; as for myself, I believe I have done the lying and the

[055-084]hypocrisy pretty well. But, dearest Fanny, you don’t know half; and

[055-085]you cannot and must not know.”

[055-086]“But I thought you said there had been nothing whatever between you.”

[055-087]“Did I? Well, to you I have not said a word that was not true. I said

[055-088]that he had spoken nothing that it was wrong for him to say. It could

[055-089]not be wrong—. But never mind. I’ll tell you what I mean to do. I

[055-090]have been thinking of it for the last week—only I shall have to tell

[055-091]Mark.”

[055-092]“If I were you I would tell him all.”

[055-093]“What, Mark! If you do, Fanny, I’ll never, never, never speak to you

[055-094]again. Would you—when I have given you all my heart in true sisterly

[055-095]love?”

[055-096]Mrs. Robarts had to explain that she had not proposed to tell

[055-097]anything to Mark herself, and was persuaded, moreover, to give a

[055-098]solemn promise that she would not tell anything to him unless

[055-099]specially authorized to do so.

[055-100]“I’ll go into a home, I think,” continued Lucy. “You know what those

[055-101]homes are?” Mrs. Robarts assured her that she knew very well, and

[055-102]then Lucy went on: “A year ago I should have said that I was the last

[055-103]girl in England to think of such a life, but I do believe now that it

[055-104]would be the best thing for me. And then I’ll starve myself, and flog

[055-105]myself, and in that way I’ll get back my own mind and my own soul.”

[055-106]“Your own soul, Lucy!” said Mrs. Robarts, in a tone of horror.

[055-107]“Well, my own heart, if you like it better; but I hate to hear myself

[055-108]talking about hearts. I don’t care for my heart. I’d let it go—with

[055-109]this young popinjay lord or anyone else, so that I could read, and

[055-110]talk, and walk, and sleep, and eat, without always feeling that I was

[055-111]wrong here—here—here,” and she pressed her hand vehemently against

[055-112]her side. “What is it that I feel, Fanny? Why am I so weak in body

[055-113]that I cannot take exercise? Why cannot I keep my mind on a book for

[055-114]one moment? Why can I not write two sentences together? Why should

[055-115]every mouthful that I eat stick in my throat? Oh, Fanny, is it his

[055-116]legs, think you, or is it his title?”

[055-117]Through all her sorrow,—and she was very sorrowful,—Mrs. Robarts

[055-118]could not help smiling. And, indeed, there was every now and then

[055-119]something even in Lucy’s look that was almost comic. She acted the

[055-120]irony so well with which she strove to throw ridicule on herself! “Do

[055-121]laugh at me,” she said. “Nothing on earth will do me so much good as

[055-122]that; nothing, unless it be starvation and a whip. If you would only

[055-123]tell me that I must be a sneak and an idiot to care for a man because

[055-124]he is good-looking and a lord!”

[055-125]“But that has not been the reason. There is a great deal more in Lord

[055-126]Lufton than that; and since I must speak, dear Lucy, I cannot but say

[055-127]that I should not wonder at your being in love with him, only—only

[055-128]that—”

[055-129]“Only what? Come, out with it. Do not mince matters, or think that I

[055-130]shall be angry with you because you scold me.”

[055-131]“Only that I should have thought that you would have been too guarded

[055-132]to have—have cared for any gentleman till—till he had shown that he

[055-133]cared for you.”

[055-134]“Guarded! Yes, that’s it; that’s just the word. But it’s he that

[055-135]should have been guarded. He should have had a fire-guard hung before

[055-136]him—or a love-guard, if you will. Guarded! Was I not guarded, till

[055-137]you all would drag me out? Did I want to go there? And when I was

[055-138]there, did I not make a fool of myself, sitting in a corner, and

[055-139]thinking how much better placed I should have been down in the

[055-140]servants’ hall. Lady Lufton—she dragged me out, and then cautioned

[055-141]me, and then, then— Why is Lady Lufton to have it all her own way?

[055-142]Why am I to be sacrificed for her? I did not want to know Lady

[055-143]Lufton, or any one belonging to her.”

[055-144]“I cannot think that you have any cause to blame Lady Lufton, nor,

[055-145]perhaps, to blame anybody very much.”

[055-146]“Well, no, it has been all my own fault; though for the life of me,

[055-147]Fanny, going back and back, I cannot see where I took the first false

[055-148]step. I do not know where I went wrong. One wrong thing I did, and it

[055-149]is the only thing that I do not regret.”

[055-150]“What was that, Lucy?”

[055-151]“I told him a lie.”

[055-152]Mrs. Robarts was altogether in the dark, and feeling that she was

[055-153]so, she knew that she could not give counsel as a friend or a sister.

[055-154]Lucy had begun by declaring—so Mrs. Robarts thought—that nothing

[055-155]had passed between her and Lord Lufton but words of most trivial

[055-156]import, and yet she now accused herself of falsehood, and declared

[055-157]that that falsehood was the only thing which she did not regret!

[055-158]“I hope not,” said Mrs. Robarts. “If you did, you were very unlike

[055-159]yourself.”

[055-160]“But I did, and were he here again, speaking to me in the same way,

[055-161]I should repeat it. I know I should. If I did not, I should have

[055-162]all the world on me. You would frown on me, and be cold. My darling

[055-163]Fanny, how would you look if I really displeasured you?”

[055-164]“I don’t think you will do that, Lucy.”

[055-165]“But if I told him the truth I should, should I not? Speak now. But

[055-166]no, Fanny, you need not speak. It was not the fear of you; no, nor

[055-167]even of her: though Heaven knows that her terrible glumness would be

[055-168]quite unendurable.”

[055-169]“I cannot understand you, Lucy. What truth or what untruth can you

[055-170]have told him if, as you say, there has been nothing between you but

[055-171]ordinary conversation?”
