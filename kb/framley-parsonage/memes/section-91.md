## Section 91

[091-001]But her father was a doctor of medicine, she is the sister of the

[091-002]parish clergyman, she is only five feet two in height, and is so

[091-003]uncommonly brown! Had Lady Lufton dared to give a catalogue of her

[091-004]objections, such would have been its extent and nature. But she did

[091-005]not dare to do this.

[091-006]“I cannot say, Ludovic, that she is possessed of all that you should

[091-007]seek in a wife.” Such was her answer.

[091-008]“Do you mean that she has not got money?”

[091-009]“No, not that; I should be very sorry to see you making money your

[091-010]chief object, or indeed any essential object. If it chanced that your

[091-011]wife did have money, no doubt you would find it a convenience. But

[091-012]pray understand me, Ludovic; I would not for a moment advise you to

[091-013]subject your happiness to such a necessity as that. It is not because

[091-014]she is without fortune—”

[091-015]“Then why is it? At breakfast you were singing her praises, and

[091-016]saying how excellent she is.”

[091-017]“If I were forced to put my objection into one word, I should say—”

[091-018]and then she paused, hardly daring to encounter the frown which was

[091-019]already gathering itself on her son’s brow.

[091-020]“You would say what?” said Lord Lufton, almost roughly.

[091-021]“Don’t be angry with me, Ludovic; all that I think, and all that I

[091-022]say on this subject, I think and say with only one object—that of

[091-023]your happiness. What other motive can I have for anything in this

[091-024]world?” And then she came close to him and kissed him.

[091-025]“But tell me, mother, what is this objection; what is this terrible

[091-026]word that is to sum up the list of all poor Lucy’s sins, and prove

[091-027]that she is unfit for married life?”

[091-028]“Ludovic, I did not say that. You know that I did not.”

[091-029]“What is the word, mother?”

[091-030]And then at last Lady Lufton spoke it out. “She is—insignificant. I

[091-031]believe her to be a very good girl, but she is not qualified to fill

[091-032]the high position to which you would exalt her.”

[091-033]“Insignificant!”

[091-034]“Yes, Ludovic, I think so.”

[091-035]“Then, mother, you do not know her. You must permit me to say that

[091-036]you are talking of a girl whom you do not know. Of all the epithets

[091-037]of opprobrium which the English language could give you, that would

[091-038]be nearly the last which she would deserve.”

[091-039]“I have not intended any opprobrium.”

[091-040]“Insignificant!”

[091-041]“Perhaps you do not quite understand me, Ludovic.”

[091-042]“I know what insignificant means, mother.”

[091-043]“I think that she would not worthily fill the position which your

[091-044]wife should take in the world.”

[091-045]“I understand what you say.”

[091-046]“She would not do you honour at the head of your table.”

[091-047]“Ah, I understand. You want me to marry some bouncing Amazon, some

[091-048]pink and white giantess of fashion who would frighten the little

[091-049]people into their proprieties.”

[091-050]“Oh, Ludovic! you are intending to laugh at me now.”

[091-051]“I was never less inclined to laugh in my life—never, I can assure

[091-052]you. And now I am more certain than ever that your objection to Miss

[091-053]Robarts arises from your not knowing her. You will find, I think,

[091-054]when you do know her, that she is as well able to hold her own as

[091-055]any lady of your acquaintance;—ay, and to maintain her husband’s

[091-056]position, too. I can assure you that I shall have no fear of her on

[091-057]that score.”

[091-058]“I think, dearest, that perhaps you hardly—”

[091-059]“I think this, mother, that in such a matter as this I must choose

[091-060]for myself. I have chosen; and I now ask you, as my mother, to go to

[091-061]her and bid her welcome. Dear mother, I will own this, that I should

[091-062]not be happy if I thought that you did not love my wife.” These last

[091-063]words he said in a tone of affection that went to his mother’s heart,

[091-064]and then he left the room.

[091-065]Poor Lady Lufton, when she was alone, waited till she heard her son’s

[091-066]steps retreating through the hall, and then betook herself up-stairs

[091-067]to her customary morning work. She sat down at last as though about

[091-068]so to occupy herself; but her mind was too full to allow of her

[091-069]taking up her pen. She had often said to herself, in days which

[091-070]to her were not as yet long gone by, that she would choose a bride

[091-071]for her son, and that then she would love the chosen one with all

[091-072]her heart. She would dethrone herself in favour of this new queen,

[091-073]sinking with joy into her dowager state, in order that her son’s wife

[091-074]might shine with the greater splendour. The fondest day-dreams of her

[091-075]life had all had reference to the time when her son should bring home

[091-076]a new Lady Lufton, selected by herself from the female excellence of

[091-077]England, and in which she might be the first to worship her new idol.

[091-078]But could she dethrone herself for Lucy Robarts? Could she give up

[091-079]her chair of state in order to place thereon the little girl from

[091-080]the parsonage? Could she take to her heart, and treat with absolute

[091-081]loving confidence, with the confidence of an almost idolatrous

[091-082]mother, that little chit who, a few months since, had sat awkwardly

[091-083]in one corner of her drawing-room, afraid to speak to any one? And

[091-084]yet it seemed that it must come to this—to this—or else those

[091-085]day-dreams of hers would in nowise come to pass.

[091-086]She sat herself down, trying to think whether it were possible that

[091-087]Lucy might fill the throne; for she had begun to recognize it as

[091-088]probable that her son’s will would be too strong for her; but her

[091-089]thoughts would fly away to Griselda Grantly. In her first and only

[091-090]matured attempt to realize her day-dreams, she had chosen Griselda

[091-091]for her queen. She had failed there, seeing that the fates had

[091-092]destined Miss Grantly for another throne;—for another and a higher

[091-093]one, as far as the world goes. She would have made Griselda the wife

[091-094]of a baron, but fate was about to make that young lady the wife of a

[091-095]marquis. Was there cause of grief in this? Did she really regret that

[091-096]Miss Grantly, with all her virtues, should be made over to the house

[091-097]of Hartletop? Lady Lufton was a woman who did not bear disappointment

[091-098]lightly; but nevertheless she did almost feel herself to have been

[091-099]relieved from a burden when she thought of the termination of the

[091-100]Lufton-Grantly marriage treaty. What if she had been successful, and,

[091-101]after all, the prize had been other than she had expected? She was

[091-102]sometimes prone to think that that prize was not exactly all that

[091-103]she had once hoped. Griselda looked the very thing that Lady Lufton

[091-104]wanted for a queen;—but how would a queen reign who trusted only to

[091-105]her looks? In that respect it was perhaps well for her that destiny

[091-106]had interposed. Griselda, she was driven to admit, was better suited

[091-107]to Lord Dumbello than to her son.

[091-108]But still—such a queen as Lucy! Could it ever come to pass that the

[091-109]lieges of the kingdom would bow the knee in proper respect before so

[091-110]puny a sovereign? And then there was that feeling which, in still

[091-111]higher quarters, prevents the marriage of princes with the most noble

[091-112]of their people. Is it not a recognized rule of these realms that

[091-113]none of the blood royal shall raise to royal honours those of the

[091-114]subjects who are by birth un-royal! Lucy was a subject of the house

[091-115]of Lufton in that she was the sister of the parson and a resident

[091-116]denizen of the parsonage. Presuming that Lucy herself might do for

[091-117]queen—granting that she might have some faculty to reign, the crown

[091-118]having been duly placed on her brow—how, then, about that clerical

[091-119]brother near the throne? Would it not come to this, that there would

[091-120]no longer be a queen at Framley?

[091-121]And yet she knew that she must yield. She did not say so to herself.

[091-122]She did not as yet acknowledge that she must put out her hand to

[091-123]Lucy, calling her by name as her daughter. She did not absolutely say

[091-124]as much to her own heart;—not as yet. But she did begin to bethink

[091-125]herself of Lucy’s high qualities, and to declare to herself that the

[091-126]girl, if not fit to be a queen, was at any rate fit to be a woman.

[091-127]That there was a spirit within that body, insignificant though the

[091-128]body might be, Lady Lufton was prepared to admit. That she had

[091-129]acquired the power—the chief of all powers in this world—of

[091-130]sacrificing herself for the sake of others; that, too, was evident

[091-131]enough. That she was a good girl, in the usual acceptation of the

[091-132]word good, Lady Lufton had never doubted. She was ready-witted too,

[091-133]prompt in action, gifted with a certain fire. It was that gift of

[091-134]fire which had won for her, so unfortunately, Lord Lufton’s love. It

[091-135]was quite possible for her also to love Lucy Robarts; Lady Lufton

[091-136]admitted that to herself;—but then who could bow the knee before

[091-137]her, and serve her as a queen? Was it not a pity that she should be

[091-138]so insignificant?

[091-139]But, nevertheless, we may say that as Lady Lufton sate that morning

[091-140]in her own room for two hours without employment, the star of Lucy

[091-141]Robarts was gradually rising in the firmament. After all, love was

[091-142]the food chiefly necessary for the nourishment of Lady Lufton,—the

[091-143]only food absolutely necessary. She was not aware of this herself,

[091-144]nor probably would those who knew her best have so spoken of her.

[091-145]They would have declared that family pride was her daily pabulum, and

[091-146]she herself would have said so too, calling it, however, by some less

[091-147]offensive name. Her son’s honour, and the honour of her house!—of

[091-148]those she would have spoken as the things dearest to her in this

[091-149]world. And this was partly true, for had her son been dishonoured,

[091-150]she would have sunk with sorrow to the grave. But the one thing

[091-151]necessary to her daily life was the power of loving those who were

[091-152]near to her.

[091-153]Lord Lufton, when he left the dining-room, intended at once to go up

[091-154]to the parsonage, but he first strolled round the garden in order

[091-155]that he might make up his mind what he would say there. He was angry

[091-156]with his mother, having not had the wit to see that she was about

[091-157]to give way and yield to him, and he was determined to make it

[091-158]understood that in this matter he would have his own way. He had

[091-159]learned that which it was necessary that he should know as to Lucy’s

[091-160]heart, and such being the case he would not conceive it possible that

[091-161]he should be debarred by his mother’s opposition. “There is no son

[091-162]in England loves his mother better than I do,” he said to himself;

[091-163]“but there are some things which a man cannot stand. She would have

[091-164]married me to that block of stone if I would have let her; and now,

[091-165]because she is disappointed there— Insignificant! I never in my life

[091-166]heard anything so absurd, so untrue, so uncharitable, so— She’d like

[091-167]me to bring a dragon home, I suppose. It would serve her right if I

[091-168]did,—some creature that would make the house intolerable to her.”

[091-169]“She must do it though,” he said again, “or she and I will quarrel,”

[091-170]and then he turned off towards the gate, preparing to go to the

[091-171]parsonage.

[091-172]“My lord, have you heard what has happened?” said the gardener,

[091-173]coming to him at the gate. The man was out of breath and almost

[091-174]overwhelmed by the greatness of his own tidings.

[091-175]“No; I have heard nothing. What is it?”
