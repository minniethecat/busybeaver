## Section 86

[086-001]“It is a hard case,” said Mark, pleading his own cause. “Though these

[086-002]men have this claim against me I have never received a shilling

[086-003]either in money or money’s worth.”

[086-004]“And yet your name is to the bills!” said the dean.

[086-005]“Yes, my name is to the bills, certainly, but it was to oblige a

[086-006]friend.”

[086-007]And then the dean, having given his advice, rode away. He could not

[086-008]understand how a clergyman, situated as was Mr. Robarts, could find

[086-009]himself called upon by friendship to attach his name to accommodation

[086-010]bills which he had not the power of liquidating when due!

[086-011]On that evening they were both wretched enough at the parsonage.

[086-012]Hitherto Mark had hoped that perhaps, after all, no absolutely

[086-013]hostile steps would be taken against him with reference to these

[086-014]bills. Some unforeseen chance might occur in his favour, or the

[086-015]persons holding them might consent to take small instalments of

[086-016]payment from time to time; but now it seemed that the evil day was

[086-017]actually coming upon him at a blow. He had no longer any secrets from

[086-018]his wife. Should he go to a lawyer? and if so, to what lawyer? And

[086-019]when he had found his lawyer, what should he say to him? Mrs. Robarts

[086-020]at one time suggested that everything should be told to Lady Lufton.

[086-021]Mark, however, could not bring himself to do that. “It would seem,”

[086-022]he said, “as though I wanted her to lend me the money.”

[086-023]On the following morning Mark did ride into Barchester, dreading,

[086-024]however, lest he should be arrested on his journey, and he did see a

[086-025]lawyer. During his absence two calls were made at the parsonage—one

[086-026]by a very rough-looking individual, who left a suspicious document

[086-027]in the hands of the servant, purporting to be an invitation—not to

[086-028]dinner—from one of the judges of the land; and the other call was

[086-029]made by Lady Lufton in person.

[086-030]Mrs. Robarts had determined to go down to Framley Court on that day.

[086-031]In accordance with her usual custom she would have been there within

[086-032]an hour or two of Lady Lufton’s return from London, but things

[086-033]between them were not now as they usually had been. This affair of

[086-034]Lucy’s must make a difference, let them both resolve to the contrary

[086-035]as they might. And, indeed, Mrs. Robarts had found that the closeness

[086-036]of her intimacy with Framley Court had been diminishing from day to

[086-037]day since Lucy had first begun to be on friendly terms with Lord

[086-038]Lufton. Since that she had been less at Framley Court than usual;

[086-039]she had heard from Lady Lufton less frequently by letter during her

[086-040]absence than she had done in former years, and was aware that she was

[086-041]less implicitly trusted with all the affairs of the parish. This had

[086-042]not made her angry, for she was in a manner conscious that it must be

[086-043]so. It made her unhappy, but what could she do? She could not blame

[086-044]Lucy, nor could she blame Lady Lufton. Lord Lufton she did blame, but

[086-045]she did so in the hearing of no one but her husband.

[086-046]Her mind, however, was made up to go over and bear the first brunt

[086-047]of her ladyship’s arguments, when she was stopped by her ladyship’s

[086-048]arrival. If it were not for this terrible matter of Lucy’s love—a

[086-049]matter on which they could not now be silent when they met—there

[086-050]would be twenty subjects of pleasant, or, at any rate, not unpleasant

[086-051]conversation. But even then there would be those terrible bills

[086-052]hanging over her conscience, and almost crushing her by their weight.

[086-053]At the moment in which Lady Lufton walked up to the drawing-room

[086-054]window, Mrs. Robarts held in her hand that ominous invitation from

[086-055]the judge. Would it not be well that she should make a clean breast

[086-056]of it all, disregarding what her husband had said? It might be well:

[086-057]only this—she had never yet done anything in opposition to her

[086-058]husband’s wishes. So she hid the slip within her desk, and left the

[086-059]matter open to consideration.

[086-060]The interview commenced with an affectionate embrace, as was a matter

[086-061]of course. “Dear Fanny,” and “Dear Lady Lufton,” was said between

[086-062]them with all the usual warmth. And then the first inquiry was made

[086-063]about the children, and the second about the school. For a minute or

[086-064]two Mrs. Robarts thought that, perhaps, nothing was to be said about

[086-065]Lucy. If it pleased Lady Lufton to be silent she, at least, would not

[086-066]commence the subject.

[086-067]Then there was a word or two spoken about Mrs. Podgens’ baby, after

[086-068]which Lady Lufton asked whether Fanny were alone.

[086-069]“Yes,” said Mrs. Robarts. “Mark has gone over to Barchester.”

[086-070]“I hope he will not be long before he lets me see him. Perhaps he can

[086-071]call to-morrow. Would you both come and dine to-morrow?”

[086-072]“Not to-morrow, I think, Lady Lufton; but Mark, I am sure, will go

[086-073]over and call.”

[086-074]“And why not come to dinner? I hope there is to be no change among

[086-075]us, eh, Fanny?” and Lady Lufton as she spoke looked into the other’s

[086-076]face in a manner which almost made Mrs. Robarts get up and throw

[086-077]herself on her old friend’s neck. Where was she to find a friend

[086-078]who would give her such constant love as she had received from Lady

[086-079]Lufton? And who was kinder, better, more honest than she?

[086-080]“Change! no, I hope not, Lady Lufton;” and as she spoke the tears

[086-081]stood in her eyes.

[086-082]“Ah, but I shall think there is if you will not come to me as you

[086-083]used to do. You always used to come and dine with me the day I came

[086-084]home, as a matter of course.”

[086-085]What could she say, poor woman, to this?

[086-086]“We were all in confusion yesterday about poor Mrs. Crawley, and the

[086-087]dean dined here; he had been over at Hogglestock to see his friend.”

[086-088]“I have heard of her illness, and will go over and see what ought

[086-089]to be done. Don’t you go, do you hear, Fanny? You with your young

[086-090]children! I should never forgive you if you did.”

[086-091]And then Mrs. Robarts explained how Lucy had gone there, had sent

[086-092]the four children back to Framley, and was herself now staying at

[086-093]Hogglestock with the object of nursing Mrs. Crawley. In telling the

[086-094]story she abstained from praising Lucy with all the strong language

[086-095]which she would have used had not Lucy’s name and character been

[086-096]at the present moment of peculiar import to Lady Lufton; but

[086-097]nevertheless she could not tell it without dwelling much on Lucy’s

[086-098]kindness. It would have been ungenerous to Lady Lufton to make much

[086-099]of Lucy’s virtue at this present moment, but unjust to Lucy to make

[086-100]nothing of it.

[086-101]“And she is actually with Mrs. Crawley now?” asked Lady Lufton.

[086-102]“Oh, yes; Mark left her there yesterday afternoon.”

[086-103]“And the four children are all here in the house?”

[086-104]“Not exactly in the house—that is, not as yet. We have arranged a

[086-105]sort of quarantine hospital over the coach-house.”

[086-106]“What, where Stubbs lives?”

[086-107]“Yes; Stubbs and his wife have come into the house, and the children

[086-108]are to remain up there till the doctor says that there is no danger

[086-109]of infection. I have not even seen my visitors myself as yet,” said

[086-110]Mrs. Robarts with a slight laugh.

[086-111]“Dear me!” said Lady Lufton. “I declare you have been very prompt.

[086-112]And so Miss Robarts is over there! I should have thought Mr. Crawley

[086-113]would have made a difficulty about the children.”

[086-114]“Well, he did; but they kidnapped them,—that is, Lucy and Mark did.

[086-115]The dean gave me such an account of it. Lucy brought them out by twos

[086-116]and packed them in the pony-carriage, and then Mark drove off at a

[086-117]gallop while Mr. Crawley stood calling to them in the road. The dean

[086-118]was there at the time and saw it all.”

[086-119]“That Miss Lucy of yours seems to be a very determined young lady

[086-120]when she takes a thing into her head,” said Lady Lufton, now sitting

[086-121]down for the first time.

[086-122]“Yes, she is,” said Mrs. Robarts, having laid aside all her pleasant

[086-123]animation, for the discussion which she dreaded was now at hand.

[086-124]“A very determined young lady,” continued Lady Lufton. “Of course, my

[086-125]dear Fanny, you know all this about Ludovic and your sister-in-law?”

[086-126]“Yes, she has told me about it.”

[086-127]“It is very unfortunate—very.”

[086-128]“I do not think Lucy has been to blame,” said Mrs. Robarts; and as

[086-129]she spoke the blood was already mounting to her cheeks.

[086-130]“Do not be too anxious to defend her, my dear, before any one accuses

[086-131]her. Whenever a person does that it looks as though their cause were

[086-132]weak.”

[086-133]“But my cause is not weak as far as Lucy is concerned; I feel quite

[086-134]sure that she has not been to blame.”

[086-135]“I know how obstinate you can be, Fanny, when you think it necessary

[086-136]to dub yourself any one’s champion. Don Quixote was not a better

[086-137]knight-errant than you are. But is it not a pity to take up your

[086-138]lance and shield before an enemy is within sight or hearing? But that

[086-139]was ever the way with your Don Quixotes.”

[086-140]“Perhaps there may be an enemy in ambush.” That was Mrs. Robarts’

[086-141]thought to herself, but she did not dare to express it, so she

[086-142]remained silent.

[086-143]“My only hope is,” continued Lady Lufton, “that when my back is

[086-144]turned you fight as gallantly for me.”

[086-145]“Ah, you are never under a cloud, like poor Lucy.”

[086-146]“Am I not? But, Fanny, you do not see all the clouds. The sun does

[086-147]not always shine for any of us, and the down-pouring rain and the

[086-148]heavy wind scatter also my fairest flowers,—as they have done hers,

[086-149]poor girl. Dear Fanny, I hope it may be long before any cloud comes

[086-150]across the brightness of your heaven. Of all the creatures I know you

[086-151]are the one most fitted for quiet continued sunshine.”

[086-152]And then Mrs. Robarts did get up and embrace her friend, thus hiding

[086-153]the tears which were running down her face. Continued sunshine

[086-154]indeed! A dark spot had already gathered on her horizon which was

[086-155]likely to fall in a very waterspout of rain. What was to come of that

[086-156]terrible notice which was now lying in the desk under Lady Lufton’s

[086-157]very arm?

[086-158]“But I am not come here to croak like an old raven,” continued Lady

[086-159]Lufton, when she had brought this embrace to an end. “It is probable

[086-160]that we all may have our sorrows; but I am quite sure of this,—that

[086-161]if we endeavour to do our duties honestly, we shall all find our

[086-162]consolation and all have our joys also. And now, my dear, let you

[086-163]and I say a few words about this unfortunate affair. It would not be

[086-164]natural if we were to hold our tongues to each other; would it?”

[086-165]“I suppose not,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[086-166]“We should always be conceiving worse than the truth,—each as to the

[086-167]other’s thoughts. Now, some time ago, when I spoke to you about your

[086-168]sister-in-law and Ludovic—I daresay you remember—”

[086-169]“Oh, yes, I remember.”

[086-170]“We both thought then that there would really be no danger. To tell

[086-171]you the plain truth I fancied, and indeed hoped, that his affections

[086-172]were engaged elsewhere; but I was altogether wrong then; wrong in

[086-173]thinking it, and wrong in hoping it.”

[086-174]Mrs. Robarts knew well that Lady Lufton was alluding to Griselda

[086-175]Grantly, but she conceived that it would be discreet to say nothing

[086-176]herself on that subject at present. She remembered, however, Lucy’s

[086-177]flashing eye when the possibility of Lord Lufton making such a

[086-178]marriage was spoken of in the pony-carriage, and could not but feel

[086-179]glad that Lady Lufton had been disappointed.

[086-180]“I do not at all impute any blame to Miss Robarts for what has

[086-181]occurred since,” continued her ladyship. “I wish you distinctly to

[086-182]understand that.”
