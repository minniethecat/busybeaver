# Sir George Meredith

Sir George Meredith is a landed baronet residing in the neighbourhood of Framley, the husband of Lady Meredith and a member of the local gentry whose presence at Framley Court and Chaldicotes places him at the centre of the novel's social world (001-169).

## Role in the Novel

Sir George appears throughout *Framley Parsonage* as one of the solid, dependable figures of the county establishment. Married "some four or five years" to Lady Meredith, he is the result of a successful maternal campaign: Lady Lufton's sister "had selected Sir George for her daughter," and Lady Lufton herself regards the match with satisfaction — "she did not know that she had ever been so happy as on the day when that marriage was declared" (001-169) (002-010). His presence at Framley Court is noted and valued: Lady Lufton observes pointedly that a certain Sunday's congregation was thinner "especially as he was not here the last Sunday that Sir George was" (004-146).

At Chaldicotes, Sir George participates in the social rituals of the hunting set — the dinners, the conversation, the easy camaraderie of country gentlemen. When Lady Lufton declares triumphantly "So you have caught Sir George, after all," it is with the satisfaction of a woman who values his company and his consequence (019-145). He is a man who can laugh and "pass on" when teased — comfortable in his position, unthreatened by the social manoeuvring around him (059-197).

## Character Traits

- **Reliability**: Sir George is the kind of man whose presence or absence is noticed — a reliable constituent of county society whose attendance at church and at dinner carries social weight (004-146).
- **Good humour**: At Chaldicotes, Sir George is teased about sleeping through a performance and responds with a light touch. "What, slept through it all?" he asks — a man secure enough to laugh at himself (019-168) (019-184).
- **Social consequence**: As a baronet and landowner, Sir George occupies a secure place in the hierarchy. His marriage to Lady Meredith — the daughter of a peer's family — represents the union of title and gentility that the county set valued (001-169).

## Relationships

- **[[lady-meredith|Lady Meredith]]**: His wife, selected for him by her mother and married with Lady Lufton's delighted approval. Their marriage of four or five years is presented as a success of the kind that vindicates maternal interference (001-169).
- **[[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]**: His aunt by marriage, who regards him with particular favour. Her satisfaction in his marriage and her pleasure in his company suggest a relationship of genuine warmth (002-010) (019-145).
- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: The vicar of Framley moves in the same social circles. At Chaldicotes, Robarts finds himself constrained by Sir George's presence — "As Sir George Meredith was there, Robarts could say nothing then" — suggesting that Sir George's solid respectability imposes a certain decorum on those around him (019-199).

## Key Scenes

- **The marriage backstory (Chapters 1–2)**: Trollope introduces Sir George through the frame of his marriage — a match engineered by Lady Lufton's sister and celebrated by Lady Lufton herself. The backstory establishes Sir George as a known quantity, a man whose character and position have been thoroughly vetted by the women who manage county society (001-169) (002-010).
- **At Framley Church (Chapter 4)**: Sir George's absence from church is noted — a small detail that reveals how closely the social calendar of Framley is calibrated to the presence or absence of its significant figures (004-146).
- **At Chaldicotes (Chapter 19)**: Sir George is part of the house party, participating in the easy banter of the hunting set. Lady Lufton's satisfaction at "catching" him there, and his own equable response to teasing, paint a portrait of a man comfortable in his social world (019-145) (019-168) (019-199).

## Social & Historical Context

A baronetcy — a hereditary knighthood, the highest rank below the peerage — conferred social prestige without legislative responsibility. Sir George Meredith represents the class of landed baronets who formed the backbone of county society in Victorian England: not grand enough to command a duke's attention, but substantial enough to merit a peer's notice; not involved in national politics, but indispensable to local governance and society. Trollope draws him with the affection due to a type he knew well — the country gentleman whose virtues are reliability, good humour, and an unshakeable sense of his own place in the order of things.
