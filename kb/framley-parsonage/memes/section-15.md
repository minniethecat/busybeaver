## Section 15

[015-001]“Then let me have the pair,” said Mark, almost frantic with delay.

[015-002]“Nonsense, Robarts; we are ready now. He won’t want them, James.

[015-003]Come, Supplehouse, have you done?”

[015-004]“Then I am to hurry myself, am I?” said Mrs. Harold Smith. “What

[015-005]changeable creatures you men are! May I be allowed half a cup more

[015-006]tea, Mr. Robarts?”

[015-007]Mark, who was now really angry, turned away to the window. There

[015-008]was no charity in these people, he said to himself. They knew the

[015-009]nature of his distress, and yet they only laughed at him. He did not,

[015-010]perhaps, reflect that he had assisted in the joke against Harold

[015-011]Smith on the previous evening.

[015-012]“James,” said he, turning to the waiter, “let me have that pair of

[015-013]horses immediately, if you please.”

[015-014]“Yes, sir; round in fifteen minutes, sir: only Ned, sir, the

[015-015]post-boy, sir; I fear he’s at his breakfast, sir; but we’ll have him

[015-016]here in less than no time, sir!”

[015-017]But before Ned and the pair were there, Mrs. Smith had absolutely got

[015-018]her bonnet on, and at ten they started. Mark did share the phaeton

[015-019]with Harold Smith, but the phaeton did not go any faster than the

[015-020]other carriages. They led the way, indeed, but that was all; and when

[015-021]the vicar’s watch told him that it was eleven, they were still a

[015-022]mile from Chaldicotes’ gate, although the horses were in a lather of

[015-023]steam; and they had only just entered the village when the church

[015-024]bells ceased to be heard.

[015-025]“Come, you are in time, after all,” said Harold Smith. “Better time

[015-026]than I was last night.” Robarts could not explain to him that the

[015-027]entry of a clergyman into church, of a clergyman who is going to

[015-028]assist in the service, should not be made at the last minute, that it

[015-029]should be staid and decorous, and not done in scrambling haste, with

[015-030]running feet and scant breath.

[015-031]“I suppose we’ll stop here, sir,” said the postilion, as he pulled

[015-032]up his horses short at the church-door, in the midst of the people

[015-033]who were congregated together ready for the service. But Mark had not

[015-034]anticipated being so late, and said at first that it was necessary

[015-035]that he should go on to the house; then, when the horses had again

[015-036]begun to move, he remembered that he could send for his gown, and

[015-037]as he got out of the carriage he gave his orders accordingly. And

[015-038]now the other two carriages were there, and so there was a noise

[015-039]and confusion at the door—very unseemly, as Mark felt it; and the

[015-040]gentlemen spoke in loud voices, and Mrs. Harold Smith declared

[015-041]that she had no prayer-book, and was much too tired to go in at

[015-042]present;—she would go home and rest herself, she said. And two

[015-043]other ladies of the party did so also, leaving Miss Dunstable to go

[015-044]alone;—for which, however, she did not care one button. And then one

[015-045]of the party, who had a nasty habit of swearing, cursed at something

[015-046]as he walked in close to Mark’s elbow; and so they made their way up

[015-047]the church as the absolution was being read, and Mark Robarts felt

[015-048]thoroughly ashamed of himself. If his rising in the world brought him

[015-049]in contact with such things as these, would it not be better for him

[015-050]that he should do without rising?

[015-051]His sermon went off without any special notice. Mrs. Harold Smith was

[015-052]not there, much to his satisfaction; and the others who were did not

[015-053]seem to pay any special attention to it. The subject had lost its

[015-054]novelty, except with the ordinary church congregation, the farmers

[015-055]and labourers of the parish; and the “quality” in the squire’s great

[015-056]pew were content to show their sympathy by a moderate subscription.

[015-057]Miss Dunstable, however, gave a ten-pound note, which swelled up the

[015-058]sum total to a respectable amount—for such a place as Chaldicotes.

[015-059]“And now I hope I may never hear another word about New Guinea,” said

[015-060]Mr. Sowerby, as they all clustered round the drawing-room fire after

[015-061]church. “That subject may be regarded as having been killed and

[015-062]buried; eh, Harold?”

[015-063]“Certainly murdered last night,” said Mrs. Harold, “by that awful

[015-064]woman, Mrs. Proudie.”

[015-065]“I wonder you did not make a dash at her and pull her out of the

[015-066]arm-chair,” said Miss Dunstable. “I was expecting it, and thought

[015-067]that I should come to grief in the scrimmage.”

[015-068]“I never knew a lady do such a brazen-faced thing before,” said Miss

[015-069]Kerrigy, a travelling friend of Miss Dunstable’s.

[015-070]“Nor I—never; in a public place, too,” said Dr. Easyman, a medical

[015-071]gentleman, who also often accompanied her.

[015-072]“As for brass,” said Mr. Supplehouse, “she would never stop at

[015-073]anything for want of that. It is well that she has enough, for the

[015-074]poor bishop is but badly provided.”

[015-075]“I hardly heard what it was she did say,” said Harold Smith; “so I

[015-076]could not answer her, you know. Something about Sundays, I believe.”

[015-077]“She hoped you would not put the South Sea islanders up to Sabbath

[015-078]travelling,” said Mr. Sowerby.

[015-079]“And specially begged that you would establish Lord’s-day schools,”

[015-080]said Mrs. Smith; and then they all went to work and picked Mrs.

[015-081]Proudie to pieces from the top ribbon of her cap down to the sole of

[015-082]her slipper.

[015-083]“And then she expects the poor parsons to fall in love with her

[015-084]daughters. That’s the hardest thing of all,” said Miss Dunstable.

[015-085]But, on the whole, when our vicar went to bed he did not feel that he

[015-086]had spent a profitable Sunday.

[015-087]CHAPTER VIII.

[015-088]GATHERUM CASTLE.

[015-089]On the Tuesday morning Mark did receive his wife’s letter and the

[015-090]ten-pound note, whereby a strong proof was given of the honesty of

[015-091]the post-office people in Barsetshire. That letter, written as it had

[015-092]been in a hurry, while Robin post-boy was drinking a single mug of

[015-093]beer,—well, what of it if it was half filled a second time?—was

[015-094]nevertheless eloquent of his wife’s love and of her great triumph.

[015-095]I have only half a moment to send you the money [she

[015-096]said], for the postman is here waiting. When I see you

[015-097]I’ll explain why I am so hurried. Let me know that you

[015-098]get it safe. It is all right now, and Lady Lufton was

[015-099]here not a minute ago. She did not quite like it; about

[015-100]Gatherum Castle I mean; but you’ll hear _nothing about

[015-101]it_. Only remember that _you must dine_ at Framley Court

[015-102]on Wednesday week. _I have promised for you._ You will:

[015-103]won’t you, dearest? I shall come and fetch you away if you

[015-104]attempt to stay longer than you have said. But I’m sure

[015-105]you won’t. God bless you, my own one! Mr. Jones gave

[015-106]us the same sermon he preached the second Sunday after

[015-107]Easter. Twice in the same year is too often. God bless

[015-108]you! The children _are quite well_. Mark sends a big

[015-109]kiss.—Your own F.

[015-110]Robarts, as he read this letter and crumpled the note up into his

[015-111]pocket, felt that it was much more satisfactory than he deserved. He

[015-112]knew that there must have been a fight, and that his wife, fighting

[015-113]loyally on his behalf, had got the best of it; and he knew also that

[015-114]her victory had not been owing to the goodness of her cause. He

[015-115]frequently declared to himself that he would not be afraid of Lady

[015-116]Lufton; but nevertheless these tidings that no reproaches were to be

[015-117]made to him afforded him great relief.

[015-118]On the following Friday they all went to the duke’s, and found that

[015-119]the bishop and Mrs. Proudie were there before them; as were also

[015-120]sundry other people, mostly of some note, either in the estimation of

[015-121]the world at large or of that of West Barsetshire. Lord Boanerges was

[015-122]there, an old man who would have his own way in everything, and who

[015-123]was regarded by all men—apparently even by the duke himself—as

[015-124]an intellectual king, by no means of the constitutional kind,—as

[015-125]an intellectual emperor, rather, who took upon himself to rule all

[015-126]questions of mind without the assistance of any ministers whatever.

[015-127]And Baron Brawl was of the party, one of her Majesty’s puisne judges,

[015-128]as jovial a guest as ever entered a country house; but given to be

[015-129]rather sharp withal in his jovialities. And there was Mr. Green

[015-130]Walker, a young but rising man, the same who lectured not long since

[015-131]on a popular subject to his constituents at the Crewe Junction. Mr.

[015-132]Green Walker was a nephew of the Marchioness of Hartletop, and the

[015-133]Marchioness of Hartletop was a friend of the Duke of Omnium’s. Mr.

[015-134]Mark Robarts was certainly elated when he ascertained who composed

[015-135]the company of which he had been so earnestly pressed to make a

[015-136]portion. Would it have been wise in him to forego this on account of

[015-137]the prejudices of Lady Lufton?

[015-138]As the guests were so many and so great, the huge front portals of

[015-139]Gatherum Castle were thrown open, and the vast hall, adorned with

[015-140]trophies—with marble busts from Italy and armour from Wardour

[015-141]Street,—was thronged with gentlemen and ladies, and gave forth

[015-142]unwonted echoes to many a footstep. His grace himself, when Mark

[015-143]arrived there with Sowerby and Miss Dunstable—for in this instance

[015-144]Miss Dunstable did travel in the phaeton while Mark occupied a

[015-145]seat in the dicky—his grace himself was at this moment in the

[015-146]drawing-room, and nothing could exceed his urbanity.

[015-147]“Oh, Miss Dunstable,” he said, taking that lady by the hand, and

[015-148]leading her up to the fire, “now I feel for the first time that

[015-149]Gatherum Castle has not been built for nothing.”

[015-150]“Nobody ever supposed it was, your grace,” said Miss Dunstable. “I am

[015-151]sure the architect did not think so when his bill was paid.” And Miss

[015-152]Dunstable put her toes up on the fender to warm them with as much

[015-153]self-possession as though her father had been a duke also, instead of

[015-154]a quack doctor.

[015-155]“We have given the strictest orders about the parrot,” said the

[015-156]duke—

[015-157]“Ah! but I have not brought him after all,” said Miss Dunstable.

[015-158]—“and I have had an aviary built on purpose,—just such as parrots

[015-159]are used to in their own country. Well, Miss Dunstable, I do call

[015-160]that unkind. Is it too late to send for him?”

[015-161]“He and Dr. Easyman are travelling together. The truth was, I could

[015-162]not rob the doctor of his companion.”

[015-163]“Why? I have had another aviary built for him. I declare, Miss

[015-164]Dunstable, the honour you are doing me is shorn of half its glory.

[015-165]But the poodle—I still trust in the poodle.”

[015-166]“And your grace’s trust shall not in that respect be in vain. Where

[015-167]is he, I wonder?” And Miss Dunstable looked round as though she

[015-168]expected that somebody would certainly have brought her dog in after

[015-169]her. “I declare I must go and look for him,—only think if they

[015-170]were to put him among your grace’s dogs,—how his morals would be

[015-171]destroyed!”

[015-172]“Miss Dunstable, is that intended to be personal?” But the lady had

[015-173]turned away from the fire, and the duke was able to welcome his other

[015-174]guests.

[015-175]This he did with much courtesy. “Sowerby,” he said, “I am glad to

[015-176]find that you have survived the lecture. I can assure you I had fears

[015-177]for you.”

[015-178]“I was brought back to life after considerable delay by the

[015-179]administration of tonics at the Dragon of Wantly. Will your grace

[015-180]allow me to present to you Mr. Robarts, who on that occasion was not

[015-181]so fortunate. It was found necessary to carry him off to the palace,

[015-182]where he was obliged to undergo very vigorous treatment.”

[015-183]And then the duke shook hands with Mr. Robarts, assuring him that he

[015-184]was most happy to make his acquaintance. He had often heard of him

[015-185]since he came into the county; and then he asked after Lord Lufton,

[015-186]regretting that he had been unable to induce his lordship to come to

[015-187]Gatherum Castle.
