## Section 14

[014-001]In this he did injustice to one of the ladies, unconsciously. Miss

[014-002]Dunstable, with all her aptitude for mirth, and we may almost fairly

[014-003]say for frolic, was in no way inclined to ridicule religion or

[014-004]anything which she thought to appertain to it. It may be presumed

[014-005]that among such things she did not include Mrs. Proudie, as she was

[014-006]willing enough to laugh at that lady; but Mark, had he known her

[014-007]better, might have been sure that she would have sat out his sermon

[014-008]with perfect propriety.

[014-009]As it was, however, he did feel considerable uneasiness; and in the

[014-010]morning he got up early with the view of seeing what might be done

[014-011]in the way of emendation. He cut out those parts which referred most

[014-012]specially to the islands,—he rejected altogether those names over

[014-013]which they had all laughed together so heartily,—and he inserted a

[014-014]string of general remarks, very useful, no doubt, which he flattered

[014-015]himself would rob his sermon of all similarity to Harold Smith’s

[014-016]lecture. He had, perhaps, hoped, when writing it, to create some

[014-017]little sensation; but now he would be quite satisfied if it passed

[014-018]without remark.

[014-019]But his troubles for that Sunday were destined to be many. It had

[014-020]been arranged that the party at the hotel should breakfast at eight,

[014-021]and start at half-past eight punctually, so as to enable them to

[014-022]reach Chaldicotes in ample time to arrange their dresses before they

[014-023]went to church. The church stood in the grounds, close to that long

[014-024]formal avenue of lime-trees, but within the front gates. Their walk,

[014-025]therefore, after reaching Mr. Sowerby’s house, would not be long.

[014-026]Mrs. Proudie, who was herself an early body, would not hear of her

[014-027]guest—and he a clergyman—going out to the inn for his breakfast

[014-028]on a Sunday morning. As regarded that Sabbath-day journey to

[014-029]Chaldicotes, to that she had given her assent, no doubt with much

[014-030]uneasiness of mind; but let them have as little desecration as

[014-031]possible. It was, therefore, an understood thing that he was to

[014-032]return with his friends; but he should not go without the advantage

[014-033]of family prayers and family breakfast. And so Mrs. Proudie on

[014-034]retiring to rest gave the necessary orders, to the great annoyance of

[014-035]her household.

[014-036]To the great annoyance, at least, of her servants! The bishop himself

[014-037]did not make his appearance till a much later hour. He in all things

[014-038]now supported his wife’s rule; in all things, now, I say; for

[014-039]there had been a moment, when in the first flush and pride of his

[014-040]episcopacy other ideas had filled his mind. Now, however, he gave no

[014-041]opposition to that good woman with whom Providence had blessed him;

[014-042]and in return for such conduct that good woman administered in all

[014-043]things to his little personal comforts. With what surprise did the

[014-044]bishop now look back upon that unholy war which he had once been

[014-045]tempted to wage against the wife of his bosom?

[014-046]Nor did any of the Miss Proudies show themselves at that early hour.

[014-047]They, perhaps, were absent on a different ground. With them Mrs.

[014-048]Proudie had not been so successful as with the bishop. They had

[014-049]wills of their own which became stronger and stronger every day.

[014-050]Of the three with whom Mrs. Proudie was blessed one was already in

[014-051]a position to exercise that will in a legitimate way over a very

[014-052]excellent young clergyman in the diocese, the Rev. Optimus Grey; but

[014-053]the other two, having as yet no such opening for their powers of

[014-054]command, were perhaps a little too much inclined to keep themselves

[014-055]in practice at home.

[014-056]But at half-past seven punctually Mrs. Proudie was there, and so was

[014-057]the domestic chaplain; so was Mr. Robarts, and so were the household

[014-058]servants,—all excepting one lazy recreant. “Where is Thomas?” said

[014-059]she of the Argus eyes, standing up with her book of family prayers in

[014-060]her hand. “So please you, ma’am, Tummas be bad with the tooth-ache.”

[014-061]“Tooth-ache!” exclaimed Mrs. Proudie; but her eyes said more terrible

[014-062]things than that. “Let Thomas come to me before church.” And then

[014-063]they proceeded to prayers. These were read by the chaplain, as it was

[014-064]proper and decent that they should be; but I cannot but think that

[014-065]Mrs. Proudie a little exceeded her office in taking upon herself

[014-066]to pronounce the blessing when the prayers were over. She did it,

[014-067]however, in a clear, sonorous voice, and perhaps with more personal

[014-068]dignity than was within the chaplain’s compass.

[014-069]Mrs. Proudie was rather stern at breakfast, and the vicar of Framley

[014-070]felt an unaccountable desire to get out of the house. In the first

[014-071]place she was not dressed with her usual punctilious attention to the

[014-072]proprieties of her high situation. It was evident that there was to

[014-073]be a further toilet before she sailed up the middle of the cathedral

[014-074]choir. She had on a large loose cap with no other strings than those

[014-075]which were wanted for tying it beneath her chin, a cap with which the

[014-076]household and the chaplain were well acquainted, but which seemed

[014-077]ungracious in the eyes of Mr. Robarts after all the well-dressed

[014-078]holiday doings of the last week. She wore also a large, loose,

[014-079]dark-coloured wrapper, which came well up round her neck, and which

[014-080]was not buoyed out, as were her dresses in general, with an under

[014-081]mechanism of petticoats. It clung to her closely, and added to the

[014-082]inflexibility of her general appearance. And then she had encased her

[014-083]feet in large carpet slippers, which no doubt were comfortable, but

[014-084]which struck her visitor as being strange and unsightly.

[014-085]“Do you find a difficulty in getting your people together for early

[014-086]morning prayers?” she said, as she commenced her operations with the

[014-087]teapot.

[014-088]“I can’t say that I do,” said Mark. “But then we are seldom so early

[014-089]as this.”

[014-090]“Parish clergymen should be early, I think,” said she. “It sets a

[014-091]good example in the village.”

[014-092]“I am thinking of having morning prayers in the church,” said Mr.

[014-093]Robarts.

[014-094]“That’s nonsense,” said Mrs. Proudie, “and usually means worse than

[014-095]nonsense. I know what that comes to. If you have three services on

[014-096]Sunday and domestic prayers at home, you do very well.” And so saying

[014-097]she handed him his cup.

[014-098]“But I have not three services on Sunday, Mrs. Proudie.”

[014-099]“Then I think you should have. Where can the poor people be so well

[014-100]off on Sundays as in church? The bishop intends to express a very

[014-101]strong opinion on this subject in his next charge; and then I am sure

[014-102]you will attend to his wishes.”

[014-103]To this Mark made no answer, but devoted himself to his egg.

[014-104]“I suppose you have not a very large establishment at Framley?” asked

[014-105]Mrs. Proudie.

[014-106]“What, at the parsonage?”

[014-107]“Yes; you live at the parsonage, don’t you?”

[014-108]“Certainly—well; not very large, Mrs. Proudie; just enough to do the

[014-109]work, make things comfortable, and look after the children.”

[014-110]“It is a very fine living,” said she; “very fine. I don’t remember

[014-111]that we have anything so good ourselves,—except it is Plumstead, the

[014-112]archdeacon’s place. He has managed to butter his bread pretty well.”

[014-113]“His father was Bishop of Barchester.”

[014-114]“Oh, yes, I know all about him. Only for that he would barely have

[014-115]risen to be an archdeacon, I suspect. Let me see; yours is £800, is

[014-116]it not, Mr. Robarts? And you such a young man! I suppose you have

[014-117]insured your life highly.”

[014-118]“Pretty well, Mrs. Proudie.”

[014-119]“And then, too, your wife had some little fortune, had she not? We

[014-120]cannot all fall on our feet like that; can we, Mr. White?” and Mrs.

[014-121]Proudie in her playful way appealed to the chaplain.

[014-122]Mrs. Proudie was an imperious woman; but then so also was Lady

[014-123]Lufton; and it may therefore be said that Mr. Robarts ought to have

[014-124]been accustomed to feminine domination; but as he sat there munching

[014-125]his toast he could not but make a comparison between the two. Lady

[014-126]Lufton in her little attempts sometimes angered him; but he certainly

[014-127]thought, comparing the lay lady and the clerical together, that the

[014-128]rule of the former was the lighter and the pleasanter. But then Lady

[014-129]Lufton had given him a living and a wife, and Mrs. Proudie had given

[014-130]him nothing.

[014-131]Immediately after breakfast Mr. Robarts escaped to the Dragon of

[014-132]Wantly, partly because he had had enough of the matutinal Mrs.

[014-133]Proudie, and partly also in order that he might hurry his friends

[014-134]there. He was already becoming fidgety about the time, as Harold

[014-135]Smith had been on the preceding evening, and he did not give Mrs.

[014-136]Smith credit for much punctuality. When he arrived at the inn he

[014-137]asked if they had done breakfast, and was immediately told that not

[014-138]one of them was yet down. It was already half-past eight, and they

[014-139]ought to be now under way on the road.

[014-140]He immediately went to Mr. Sowerby’s room, and found that gentleman

[014-141]shaving himself. “Don’t be a bit uneasy,” said Mr. Sowerby. “You and

[014-142]Smith shall have my phaeton, and those horses will take you there in

[014-143]an hour. Not, however, but what we shall all be in time. We’ll send

[014-144]round to the whole party and ferret them out.” And then Mr. Sowerby,

[014-145]having evoked manifold aid with various peals of the bell, sent

[014-146]messengers, male and female, flying to all the different rooms.

[014-147]“I think I’ll hire a gig and go over at once,” said Mark. “It would

[014-148]not do for me to be late, you know.”

[014-149]“It won’t do for any of us to be late; and it’s all nonsense about

[014-150]hiring a gig. It would be just throwing a sovereign away, and we

[014-151]should pass you on the road. Go down and see that the tea is made,

[014-152]and all that; and make them have the bill ready; and, Robarts, you

[014-153]may pay it too, if you like it. But I believe we may as well leave

[014-154]that to Baron Borneo—eh?”

[014-155]And then Mark did go down and make the tea, and he did order the

[014-156]bill; and then he walked about the room, looking at his watch, and

[014-157]nervously waiting for the footsteps of his friends. And as he was so

[014-158]employed, he bethought himself whether it was fit that he should be

[014-159]so doing on a Sunday morning; whether it was good that he should be

[014-160]waiting there, in painful anxiety, to gallop over a dozen miles in

[014-161]order that he might not be too late with his sermon; whether his

[014-162]own snug room at home, with Fanny opposite to him, and his bairns

[014-163]crawling on the floor, with his own preparations for his own quiet

[014-164]service, and the warm pressure of Lady Lufton’s hand when that

[014-165]service should be over, was not better than all this.

[014-166]He could not afford not to know Harold Smith, and Mr. Sowerby, and

[014-167]the Duke of Omnium, he had said to himself. He had to look to rise

[014-168]in the world, as other men did. But what pleasure had come to him as

[014-169]yet from these intimacies? How much had he hitherto done towards his

[014-170]rising? To speak the truth he was not over well pleased with himself,

[014-171]as he made Mrs. Harold Smith’s tea and ordered Mr. Sowerby’s

[014-172]mutton-chops on that Sunday morning.

[014-173]At a little after nine they all assembled; but even then he could

[014-174]not make the ladies understand that there was any cause for hurry;

[014-175]at least Mrs. Smith, who was the leader of the party, would not

[014-176]understand it. When Mark again talked of hiring a gig, Miss Dunstable

[014-177]indeed said that she would join him; and seemed to be so far earnest

[014-178]in the matter that Mr. Sowerby hurried through his second egg in

[014-179]order to prevent such a catastrophe. And then Mark absolutely did

[014-180]order the gig; whereupon Mrs. Smith remarked that in such case she

[014-181]need not hurry herself; but the waiter brought up word that all

[014-182]the horses of the hotel were out, excepting one pair, neither of

[014-183]which could go in single harness. Indeed, half of their stable

[014-184]establishment was already secured by Mr. Sowerby’s own party.
