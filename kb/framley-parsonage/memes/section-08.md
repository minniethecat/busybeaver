## Section 8

[008-001]“My dear fellow, Fothergill tells me that you have some hesitation

[008-002]about going to Gatherum Castle.”

[008-003]“Well, I did decline, certainly. You know I am not a man of pleasure,

[008-004]as you are. I have some duties to attend to.”

[008-005]“Gammon!” said Mr. Sowerby; and as he said it he looked with a kind

[008-006]of derisive smile into the clergyman’s face.

[008-007]“It is easy enough to say that, Sowerby; and perhaps I have no right

[008-008]to expect that you should understand me.”

[008-009]“Ah, but I do understand you; and I say it is gammon. I would be the

[008-010]last man in the world to ridicule your scruples about duty, if this

[008-011]hesitation on your part arose from any such scruple. But answer me

[008-012]honestly, do you not know that such is not the case?”

[008-013]“I know nothing of the kind.”

[008-014]“Ah, but I think you do. If you persist in refusing this invitation

[008-015]will it not be because you are afraid of making Lady Lufton angry? I

[008-016]do not know what there can be in that woman that she is able to hold

[008-017]both you and Lufton in leading-strings.”

[008-018]Robarts, of course, denied the charge and protested that he was not

[008-019]to be taken back to his own parsonage by any fear of Lady Lufton.

[008-020]But though he made such protest with warmth, he knew that he did so

[008-021]ineffectually. Sowerby only smiled and said that the proof of the

[008-022]pudding was in the eating.

[008-023]“What is the good of a man keeping a curate if it be not to save him

[008-024]from that sort of drudgery?” he asked.

[008-025]“Drudgery! If I were a drudge how could I be here to-day?”

[008-026]“Well, Robarts, look here. I am speaking now, perhaps, with more of

[008-027]the energy of an old friend than circumstances fully warrant; but

[008-028]I am an older man than you, and as I have a regard for you I do not

[008-029]like to see you throw up a good game when it is in your hands.”

[008-030]“Oh, as far as that goes, Sowerby, I need hardly tell you that I

[008-031]appreciate your kindness.”

[008-032]“If you are content,” continued the man of the world, “to live at

[008-033]Framley all your life, and to warm yourself in the sunshine of the

[008-034]dowager there, why, in such case, it may perhaps be useless for you

[008-035]to extend the circle of your friends; but if you have higher ideas

[008-036]than these, I think you will be very wrong to omit the present

[008-037]opportunity of going to the duke’s. I never knew the duke go so much

[008-038]out of his way to be civil to a clergyman as he has done in this

[008-039]instance.”

[008-040]“I am sure I am very much obliged to him.”

[008-041]“The fact is, that you may, if you please, make yourself popular

[008-042]in the county; but you cannot do it by obeying all Lady Lufton’s

[008-043]behests. She is a dear old woman, I am sure.”

[008-044]“She is, Sowerby; and you would say so, if you knew her.”

[008-045]“I don’t doubt it; but it would not do for you or me to live exactly

[008-046]according to her ideas. Now, here, in this case, the bishop of the

[008-047]diocese is to be one of the party, and he has, I believe, already

[008-048]expressed a wish that you should be another.”

[008-049]“He asked me if I were going.”

[008-050]“Exactly; and Archdeacon Grantly will be there.”

[008-051]“Will he?” asked Mark. Now, that would be a great point gained, for

[008-052]Archdeacon Grantly was a close friend of Lady Lufton.

[008-053]“So I understand from Fothergill. Indeed, it will be very wrong of

[008-054]you not to go, and I tell you so plainly; and what is more, when you

[008-055]talk about your duty—you having a curate as you have—why, it is

[008-056]gammon.” These last words he spoke looking back over his shoulder

[008-057]as he stood up in his stirrups, for he had caught the eye of the

[008-058]huntsman, who was surrounded by his hounds, and was now trotting on

[008-059]to join him.

[008-060]During a great portion of the day, Mark found himself riding by the

[008-061]side of Mrs. Proudie, as that lady leaned back in her carriage. And

[008-062]Mrs. Proudie smiled on him graciously, though her daughter would not

[008-063]do so. Mrs. Proudie was fond of having an attendant clergyman; and

[008-064]as it was evident that Mr. Robarts lived among nice people—titled

[008-065]dowagers, members of Parliament, and people of that sort—she was

[008-066]quite willing to instal him as a sort of honorary chaplain _pro tem_.

[008-067]“I’ll tell you what we have settled, Mrs. Harold Smith and I,” said

[008-068]Mrs. Proudie to him. “This lecture at Barchester will be so late on

[008-069]Saturday evening, that you had all better come and dine with us.”

[008-070]Mark bowed and thanked her, and declared that he should be very happy

[008-071]to make one of such a party. Even Lady Lufton could not object to

[008-072]this, although she was not especially fond of Mrs. Proudie.

[008-073]“And then they are to sleep at the hotel. It will really be too late

[008-074]for ladies to think of going back so far at this time of the year. I

[008-075]told Mrs. Harold Smith, and Miss Dunstable, too, that we could manage

[008-076]to make room at any rate for them. But they will not leave the other

[008-077]ladies; so they go to the hotel for that night. But, Mr. Robarts, the

[008-078]bishop will never allow you to stay at the inn, so of course you will

[008-079]take a bed at the palace.”

[008-080]It immediately occurred to Mark that as the lecture was to be given

[008-081]on Saturday evening, the next morning would be Sunday; and, on that

[008-082]Sunday, he would have to preach at Chaldicotes. “I thought they were

[008-083]all going to return the same night,” said he.

[008-084]“Well, they did intend it; but you see Mrs. Smith is afraid.”

[008-085]“I should have to get back here on the Sunday morning, Mrs. Proudie.”

[008-086]“Ah, yes, that is bad—very bad, indeed. No one dislikes any

[008-087]interference with the Sabbath more than I do. Indeed, if I am

[008-088]particular about anything it is about that. But some works are works

[008-089]of necessity, Mr. Robarts; are they not? Now you must necessarily

[008-090]be back at Chaldicotes on Sunday morning!” And so the matter was

[008-091]settled. Mrs. Proudie was very firm in general in the matter of

[008-092]Sabbath-day observances; but when she had to deal with such persons

[008-093]as Mrs. Harold Smith, it was expedient that she should give way a

[008-094]little. “You can start as soon as it’s daylight, you know, if you

[008-095]like it, Mr. Robarts,” said Mrs. Proudie.

[008-096]There was not much to boast of as to the hunting, but it was a very

[008-097]pleasant day for the ladies. The men rode up and down the grass roads

[008-098]through the Chace, sometimes in the greatest possible hurry as though

[008-099]they never could go quick enough; and then the coachmen would drive

[008-100]very fast also, though they did not know why, for a fast pace of

[008-101]movement is another of those contagious diseases. And then again

[008-102]the sportsmen would move at an undertaker’s pace, when the fox had

[008-103]traversed and the hounds would be at a loss to know which was the

[008-104]hunt and which was the heel; and then the carriage also would go

[008-105]slowly, and the ladies would stand up and talk. And then the time for

[008-106]lunch came; and altogether the day went by pleasantly enough.

[008-107]“And so that’s hunting, is it?” said Miss Dunstable.

[008-108]“Yes, that’s hunting,” said Mr. Sowerby.

[008-109]“I did not see any gentleman do anything that I could not do myself,

[008-110]except there was one young man slipped off into the mud; and I

[008-111]shouldn’t like that.”

[008-112]“But there was no breaking of bones, was there, my dear?” said Mrs.

[008-113]Harold Smith.

[008-114]“And nobody caught any foxes,” said Miss Dunstable. “The fact is,

[008-115]Mrs. Smith, that I don’t think much more of their sport than I do of

[008-116]their business. I shall take to hunting a pack of hounds myself after

[008-117]this.”

[008-118]“Do, my dear, and I’ll be your whipper-in. I wonder whether Mrs.

[008-119]Proudie would join us.”

[008-120]“I shall be writing to the duke to-night,” said Mr. Fothergill to

[008-121]Mark, as they were all riding up to the stable-yard together. “You

[008-122]will let me tell his grace that you will accept his invitation—will

[008-123]you not?”

[008-124]“Upon my word, the duke is very kind,” said Mark.

[008-125]“He is very anxious to know you, I can assure you,” said Fothergill.

[008-126]What could a young flattered fool of a parson do, but say that he

[008-127]would go? Mark did say that he would go; and in the course of the

[008-128]evening his friend Mr. Sowerby congratulated him, and the bishop

[008-129]joked with him and said that he knew that he would not give up good

[008-130]company so soon; and Miss Dunstable said she would make him her

[008-131]chaplain as soon as Parliament would allow quack doctors to have such

[008-132]articles—an allusion which Mark did not understand, till he learned

[008-133]that Miss Dunstable was herself the proprietress of the celebrated

[008-134]Oil of Lebanon, invented by her late respected father, and patented

[008-135]by him with such wonderful results in the way of accumulated fortune;

[008-136]and Mrs. Proudie made him quite one of their party, talking to him

[008-137]about all manner of church subjects; and then at last, even Miss

[008-138]Proudie smiled on him, when she learned that he had been thought

[008-139]worthy of a bed at a duke’s castle. And all the world seemed to be

[008-140]open to him.

[008-141]But he could not make himself happy that evening. On the next morning

[008-142]he must write to his wife; and he could already see the look of

[008-143]painful sorrow which would fall upon his Fanny’s brow when she

[008-144]learned that her husband was going to be a guest at the Duke of

[008-145]Omnium’s. And he must tell her to send him money, and money was

[008-146]scarce. And then, as to Lady Lufton, should he send her some message,

[008-147]or should he not? In either case he must declare war against her. And

[008-148]then did he not owe everything to Lady Lufton? And thus in spite of

[008-149]all his triumphs he could not get himself to bed in a happy frame of

[008-150]mind.

[008-151]On the next day, which was Friday, he postponed the disagreeable

[008-152]task of writing. Saturday would do as well; and on Saturday morning,

[008-153]before they all started for Barchester, he did write. And his letter

[008-154]ran as follows:—

[008-155]Chaldicotes, — November, 185—.

[008-156]DEAREST LOVE,—You will be astonished when I tell you how

[008-157]gay we all are here, and what further dissipations are in

[008-158]store for us. The Arabins, as you supposed, are not of

[008-159]our party; but the Proudies are,—as you supposed also.

[008-160]Your suppositions are always right. And what will you

[008-161]think when I tell you that I am to sleep at the palace

[008-162]on Saturday? You know that there is to be a lecture in

[008-163]Barchester on that day. Well; we must all go, of course,

[008-164]as Harold Smith, one of our set here, is to give it. And

[008-165]now it turns out that we cannot get back the same night

[008-166]because there is no moon; and Mrs. Bishop would not allow

[008-167]that my cloth should be contaminated by an hotel;—very

[008-168]kind and considerate, is it not?

[008-169]But I have a more astounding piece of news for you than

[008-170]this. There is to be a great party at Gatherum Castle

[008-171]next week, and they have talked me over into accepting an

[008-172]invitation which the duke sent expressly to me. I refused

[008-173]at first; but everybody here said that my doing so would

[008-174]be so strange; and then they all wanted to know my reason.

[008-175]When I came to render it, I did not know what reason I had

[008-176]to give. The bishop is going, and he thought it very odd

[008-177]that I should not go also, seeing that I was asked.
