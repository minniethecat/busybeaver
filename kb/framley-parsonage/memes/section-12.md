## Section 12

[012-001]With Mr. Sowerby Robarts had more than one communication respecting

[012-002]Lord Lufton and his affairs, which he would willingly have avoided

[012-003]had it been possible. Sowerby was one of those men who are always

[012-004]mixing up business with pleasure, and who have usually some scheme

[012-005]in their mind which requires forwarding. Men of this class have, as

[012-006]a rule, no daily work, no regular routine of labour; but it may be

[012-007]doubted whether they do not toil much more incessantly than those who

[012-008]have.

[012-009]“Lufton is so dilatory,” Mr. Sowerby said. “Why did he not arrange

[012-010]this at once, when he promised it? And then he is so afraid of that

[012-011]old woman at Framley Court. Well, my dear fellow, say what you will;

[012-012]she is an old woman and she’ll never be younger. But do write to

[012-013]Lufton and tell him that this delay is inconvenient to me; he’ll do

[012-014]anything for you, I know.”

[012-015]Mark said that he would write, and, indeed, did do so; but he did not

[012-016]at first like the tone of the conversation into which he was dragged.

[012-017]It was very painful to him to hear Lady Lufton called an old woman,

[012-018]and hardly less so to discuss the propriety of Lord Lufton’s parting

[012-019]with his property. This was irksome to him, till habit made it easy.

[012-020]But by degrees his feelings became less acute, and he accustomed

[012-021]himself to his friend Sowerby’s mode of talking.

[012-022]And then on the Saturday afternoon they all went over to Barchester.

[012-023]Harold Smith during the last forty-eight hours had become crammed

[012-024]to overflowing with Sarawak, Labuan, New Guinea, and the Salomon

[012-025]Islands. As is the case with all men labouring under temporary

[012-026]specialities, he for the time had faith in nothing else, and was

[012-027]not content that any one near him should have any other faith. They

[012-028]called him Viscount Papua and Baron Borneo; and his wife, who headed

[012-029]the joke against him, insisted on having her title. Miss Dunstable

[012-030]swore that she would wed none but a South Sea islander; and to Mark

[012-031]was offered the income and duties of Bishop of Spices. Nor did the

[012-032]Proudie family set themselves against these little sarcastic quips

[012-033]with any overwhelming severity. It is sweet to unbend oneself at

[012-034]the proper opportunity, and this was the proper opportunity for Mrs.

[012-035]Proudie’s unbending. No mortal can be seriously wise at all hours;

[012-036]and in these happy hours did that usually wise mortal, the bishop,

[012-037]lay aside for awhile his serious wisdom.

[012-038]“We think of dining at five to-morrow, my Lady Papua,” said the

[012-039]facetious bishop; “will that suit his lordship and the affairs of

[012-040]State? he! he! he!” And the good prelate laughed at the fun.

[012-041]How pleasantly young men and women of fifty or thereabouts can joke

[012-042]and flirt and poke their fun about, laughing and holding their sides,

[012-043]dealing in little innuendoes and rejoicing in nicknames when they

[012-044]have no Mentors of twenty-five or thirty near them to keep them in

[012-045]order. The vicar of Framley might perhaps have been regarded as such

[012-046]a Mentor, were it not for that capability of adapting himself to the

[012-047]company immediately around him on which he so much piqued himself.

[012-048]He therefore also talked to my Lady Papua, and was jocose about

[012-049]the Baron,—not altogether to the satisfaction of Mr. Harold Smith

[012-050]himself.

[012-051]For Mr. Harold Smith was in earnest and did not quite relish these

[012-052]jocundities. He had an idea that he could in about three months talk

[012-053]the British world into civilizing New Guinea, and that the world of

[012-054]Barsetshire would be made to go with him by one night’s efforts.

[012-055]He did not understand why others should be less serious, and was

[012-056]inclined to resent somewhat stiffly the amenities of our friend Mark.

[012-057]“We must not keep the Baron waiting,” said Mark, as they were

[012-058]preparing to start for Barchester.

[012-059]“I don’t know what you mean by the Baron, sir,” said Harold Smith.

[012-060]“But perhaps the joke will be against you, when you are getting

[012-061]up into your pulpit to-morrow and sending the hat round among the

[012-062]clodhoppers of Chaldicotes.”

[012-063]“Those who live in glass houses shouldn’t throw stones; eh, Baron?”

[012-064]said Miss Dunstable. “Mr. Robarts’s sermon will be too near akin to

[012-065]your lecture to allow of his laughing.”

[012-066]“If we can do nothing towards instructing the outer world till it’s

[012-067]done by the parsons,” said Harold Smith, “the outer world will have

[012-068]to wait a long time, I fear.”

[012-069]“Nobody can do anything of that kind short of a member of Parliament

[012-070]and a would-be minister,” whispered Mrs. Harold.

[012-071]And so they were all very pleasant together, in spite of a little

[012-072]fencing with edge-tools; and at three o’clock the _cortége_ of

[012-073]carriages started for Barchester, that of the bishop, of course,

[012-074]leading the way. His lordship, however, was not in it.

[012-075]“Mrs. Proudie, I’m sure you’ll let me go with you,” said Miss

[012-076]Dunstable, at the last moment, as she came down the big stone steps.

[012-077]“I want to hear the rest of that story about Mr. Slope.”

[012-078]Now this upset everything. The bishop was to have gone with his wife,

[012-079]Mrs. Smith, and Mark Robarts; and Mr. Sowerby had so arranged matters

[012-080]that he could have accompanied Miss Dunstable in his phaeton. But no

[012-081]one ever dreamed of denying Miss Dunstable anything. Of course Mark

[012-082]gave way; but it ended in the bishop declaring that he had no special

[012-083]predilection for his own carriage, which he did in compliance with a

[012-084]glance from his wife’s eye. Then other changes of course followed,

[012-085]and, at last, Mr. Sowerby and Harold Smith were the joint occupants

[012-086]of the phaeton.

[012-087]The poor lecturer, as he seated himself, made some remark such as

[012-088]those he had been making for the last two days—for out of a full

[012-089]heart the mouth speaketh. But he spoke to an impatient listener.

[012-090]“D—— the South Sea islanders,” said Mr. Sowerby. “You’ll have it

[012-091]all your own way in a few minutes, like a bull in a china-shop; but

[012-092]for Heaven’s sake let us have a little peace till that time comes.”

[012-093]It appeared that Mr. Sowerby’s little plan of having Miss Dunstable

[012-094]for his companion was not quite insignificant; and, indeed, it may be

[012-095]said that but few of his little plans were so. At the present moment

[012-096]he flung himself back in the carriage and prepared for sleep. He

[012-097]could further no plan of his by a _tête-à-tête_ conversation with his

[012-098]brother-in-law.

[012-099]And then Mrs. Proudie began her story about Mr. Slope, or rather

[012-100]recommenced it. She was very fond of talking about this gentleman,

[012-101]who had once been her pet chaplain but was now her bitterest foe; and

[012-102]in telling the story, she had sometimes to whisper to Miss Dunstable,

[012-103]for there were one or two fie-fie little anecdotes about a married

[012-104]lady, not altogether fit for young Mr. Robarts’s ears. But Mrs.

[012-105]Harold Smith insisted on having them out loud, and Miss Dunstable

[012-106]would gratify that lady in spite of Mrs. Proudie’s winks.

[012-107]“What, kissing her hand, and he a clergyman!” said Miss Dunstable.

[012-108]“I did not think they ever did such things, Mr. Robarts.”

[012-109]“Still waters run deepest,” said Mrs. Harold Smith.

[012-110]“Hush-h-h,” looked, rather than spoke, Mrs. Proudie. “The grief

[012-111]of spirit which that bad man caused me nearly broke my heart, and

[012-112]all the while, you know, he was courting—” and then Mrs. Proudie

[012-113]whispered a name.

[012-114]“What, the dean’s wife!” shouted Miss Dunstable, in a voice which

[012-115]made the coachman of the next carriage give a chuck to his horses as

[012-116]he overheard her.

[012-117]“The archdeacon’s sister-in-law!” screamed Mrs. Harold Smith.

[012-118]“What might he not have attempted next?” said Miss Dunstable.

[012-119]“She wasn’t the dean’s wife then, you know,” said Mrs. Proudie,

[012-120]explaining.

[012-121]“Well, you’ve a gay set in the chapter, I must say,” said Miss

[012-122]Dunstable. “You ought to make one of them in Barchester, Mr.

[012-123]Robarts.”

[012-124]“Only perhaps Mrs. Robarts might not like it,” said Mrs. Harold

[012-125]Smith.

[012-126]“And then the schemes which he tried on with the bishop!” said Mrs.

[012-127]Proudie.

[012-128]“It’s all fair in love and war, you know,” said Miss Dunstable.

[012-129]“But he little knew whom he had to deal with when he began that,”

[012-130]said Mrs. Proudie.

[012-131]“The bishop was too many for him,” suggested Mrs. Harold Smith, very

[012-132]maliciously.

[012-133]“If the bishop was not, somebody else was; and he was obliged to

[012-134]leave Barchester in utter disgrace. He has since married the wife of

[012-135]some tallow-chandler.”

[012-136]“The wife!” said Miss Dunstable. “What a man!”

[012-137]“Widow, I mean; but it’s all one to him.”

[012-138]“The gentleman was clearly born when Venus was in the ascendant,”

[012-139]said Mrs. Smith. “You clergymen usually are, I believe, Mr. Robarts.”

[012-140]So that Mrs. Proudie’s carriage was by no means the dullest as they

[012-141]drove into Barchester that day; and by degrees our friend Mark became

[012-142]accustomed to his companions, and before they reached the palace he

[012-143]acknowledged to himself that Miss Dunstable was very good fun.

[012-144]We cannot linger over the bishop’s dinner, though it was very good of

[012-145]its kind; and as Mr. Sowerby contrived to sit next to Miss Dunstable,

[012-146]thereby overturning a little scheme made by Mr. Supplehouse, he again

[012-147]shone forth in unclouded good humour. But Mr. Harold Smith became

[012-148]impatient immediately on the withdrawal of the cloth. The lecture was

[012-149]to begin at seven, and according to his watch that hour had already

[012-150]come. He declared that Sowerby and Supplehouse were endeavouring to

[012-151]delay matters in order that the Barchesterians might become vexed

[012-152]and impatient; and so the bishop was not allowed to exercise his

[012-153]hospitality in true episcopal fashion.

[012-154]“You forget, Sowerby,” said Supplehouse, “that the world here for the

[012-155]last fortnight has been looking forward to nothing else.”

[012-156]“The world shall be gratified at once,” said Mrs. Harold, obeying a

[012-157]little nod from Mrs. Proudie. “Come, my dear,” and she took hold of

[012-158]Miss Dunstable’s arm, “don’t let us keep Barchester waiting. We shall

[012-159]be ready in a quarter-of-an-hour, shall we not, Mrs. Proudie?” and so

[012-160]they sailed off.

[012-161]“And we shall have time for one glass of claret,” said the bishop.

[012-162]“There; that’s seven by the cathedral,” said Harold Smith, jumping

[012-163]up from his chair as he heard the clock. “If the people have come it

[012-164]would not be right in me to keep them waiting, and I shall go.”

[012-165]“Just one glass of claret, Mr. Smith, and we’ll be off,” said the

[012-166]bishop.

[012-167]“Those women will keep me an hour,” said Harold, filling his glass,

[012-168]and drinking it standing. “They do it on purpose.” He was thinking

[012-169]of his wife, but it seemed to the bishop as though his guest were

[012-170]actually speaking of Mrs. Proudie.

[012-171]It was rather late when they all found themselves in the big room of

[012-172]the Mechanics’ Institute; but I do not know whether this on the whole

[012-173]did them any harm. Most of Mr. Smith’s hearers, excepting the party

[012-174]from the palace, were Barchester tradesmen with their wives and

[012-175]families; and they waited, not impatiently, for the big people. And

[012-176]then the lecture was gratis, a fact which is always borne in mind

[012-177]by an Englishman when he comes to reckon up and calculate the way

[012-178]in which he is treated. When he pays his money, then he takes his

[012-179]choice; he may be impatient or not as he likes. His sense of justice

[012-180]teaches him so much, and in accordance with that sense he usually

[012-181]acts.

[012-182]So the people on the benches rose graciously when the palace

[012-183]party entered the room. Seats for them had been kept in the front.

[012-184]There were three arm-chairs, which were filled, after some little

[012-185]hesitation, by the bishop, Mrs. Proudie, and Miss Dunstable—Mrs.

[012-186]Smith positively declining to take one of them; though, as she

[012-187]admitted, her rank as Lady Papua of the islands did give her some

[012-188]claim. And this remark, as it was made quite out loud, reached Mr.

[012-189]Smith’s ears as he stood behind a little table on a small raised

[012-190]dais, holding his white kid gloves; and it annoyed him and rather put

[012-191]him out. He did not like that joke about Lady Papua.
