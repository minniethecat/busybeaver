## Section 69

[069-001]“Well, Tozer,” said Mr. Sowerby, absolutely shaking hands with the

[069-002]dirty miscreant, “I wanted to see your brother.”

[069-003]“John ain’t here, and ain’t like; but it’s all as one.”

[069-004]“Yes, yes; I suppose it is. I know you two hunt in couples.”

[069-005]“I don’t know what you mean about hunting, Mr. Sowerby. You gents ’as

[069-006]all the hunting, and we poor folk ’as all the work. I hope you’re

[069-007]going to make up this trifle of money we’re out of so long.”

[069-008]“It’s about that I’ve called. I don’t know what you call long, Tozer;

[069-009]but the last bill was only dated in February.”

[069-010]“It’s overdue; ain’t it?”

[069-011]“Oh, yes; it’s overdue. There’s no doubt about that.”

[069-012]“Well; when a bit of paper is come round, the next thing is to take

[069-013]it up. Them’s my ideas. And to tell you the truth, Mr. Sowerby, we

[069-014]don’t think as ’ow you’ve been treating us just on the square lately.

[069-015]In that matter of Lord Lufton’s you was down on us uncommon.”

[069-016]“You know I couldn’t help myself.”

[069-017]“Well; and we can’t help ourselves now. That’s where it is, Mr.

[069-018]Sowerby. Lord love you; we know what’s what, we do. And so, the fact

[069-019]is we’re uncommon low as to the ready just at present, and we must

[069-020]have them few hundred pounds. We must have them at once, or we must

[069-021]sell up that clerical gent. I’m dashed if it ain’t as hard to get

[069-022]money from a parson as it is to take a bone from a dog. ’E’s ’ad ’is

[069-023]account, no doubt, and why don’t ’e pay?”

[069-024]Mr. Sowerby had called with the intention of explaining that he was

[069-025]about to proceed to Barchester on the following day with the express

[069-026]view of “making arrangements” about this bill; and had he seen John

[069-027]Tozer, John would have been compelled to accord to him some little

[069-028]extension of time. Both Tom and John knew this; and, therefore,

[069-029]John—the soft-hearted one—kept out of the way. There was no danger

[069-030]that Tom would be weak; and, after some half-hour of parley, he was

[069-031]again left by Mr. Sowerby, without having evinced any symptom of

[069-032]weakness.

[069-033]“It’s the dibs as we want, Mr. Sowerby; that’s all,” were the last

[069-034]words which he spoke as the member of Parliament left the room.

[069-035]Mr. Sowerby then got into another cab, and had himself driven to his

[069-036]sister’s house. It is a remarkable thing with reference to men who

[069-037]are distressed for money—distressed as was now the case with Mr.

[069-038]Sowerby—that they never seem at a loss for small sums, or deny

[069-039]themselves those luxuries which small sums purchase. Cabs, dinners,

[069-040]wine, theatres, and new gloves are always at the command of men who

[069-041]are drowned in pecuniary embarrassments, whereas those who don’t owe

[069-042]a shilling are so frequently obliged to go without them! It would

[069-043]seem that there is no gratification so costly as that of keeping out

[069-044]of debt. But then it is only fair that, if a man has a hobby, he

[069-045]should pay for it.

[069-046]Any one else would have saved his shilling, as Mrs. Harold Smith’s

[069-047]house was only just across Oxford Street, in the neighbourhood of

[069-048]Hanover Square; but Mr. Sowerby never thought of this. He had never

[069-049]saved a shilling in his life, and it did not occur to him to begin

[069-050]now. He had sent word to her to remain at home for him, and he now

[069-051]found her waiting.

[069-052]“Harriet,” said he, throwing himself back into an easy chair, “the

[069-053]game is pretty well up at last.”

[069-054]“Nonsense,” said she. “The game is not up at all if you have the

[069-055]spirit to carry it on.”

[069-056]“I can only say that I got a formal notice this morning from the

[069-057]duke’s lawyer, saying that he meant to foreclose at once;—not from

[069-058]Fothergill, but from those people in South Audley Street.”

[069-059]“You expected that,” said his sister.

[069-060]“I don’t see how that makes it any better; besides, I am not quite

[069-061]sure that I did expect it; at any rate I did not feel certain. There

[069-062]is no doubt now.”

[069-063]“It is better that there should be no doubt. It is much better that

[069-064]you should know on what ground you have to stand.”

[069-065]“I shall soon have no ground to stand on, none at least of my

[069-066]own,—not an acre,” said the unhappy man, with great bitterness in

[069-067]his tone.

[069-068]“You can’t in reality be poorer now than you were last year. You

[069-069]have not spent anything to speak of. There can be no doubt that

[069-070]Chaldicotes will be ample to pay all you owe the duke.”

[069-071]“It’s as much as it will; and what am I to do then? I almost think

[069-072]more of the seat than I do of Chaldicotes.”

[069-073]“You know what I advise,” said Mrs. Smith. “Ask Miss Dunstable to

[069-074]advance the money on the same security which the duke holds. She will

[069-075]be as safe then as he is now. And if you can arrange that, stand for

[069-076]the county against him; perhaps you may be beaten.”

[069-077]“I shouldn’t have a chance.”

[069-078]“But it would show that you are not a creature in the duke’s hands.

[069-079]That’s my advice,” said Mrs. Smith, with much spirit; “and if you

[069-080]wish, I’ll broach it to Miss Dunstable, and ask her to get her lawyer

[069-081]to look into it.”

[069-082]“If I had done this before I had run my head into that other

[069-083]absurdity!”

[069-084]“Don’t fret yourself about that; she will lose nothing by such an

[069-085]investment, and therefore you are not asking any favour of her.

[069-086]Besides, did she not make the offer? and she is just the woman to do

[069-087]this for you now, because she refused to do that other thing for you

[069-088]yesterday. You understand most things, Nathaniel; but I am not sure

[069-089]that you understand women; not, at any rate, such a woman as her.”

[069-090]It went against the grain with Mr. Sowerby, this seeking of pecuniary

[069-091]assistance from the very woman whose hand he had attempted to gain

[069-092]about a fortnight since; but he allowed his sister to prevail. What

[069-093]could any man do in such straits that would not go against the grain?

[069-094]At the present moment he felt in his mind an infinite hatred against

[069-095]the duke, Mr. Fothergill, Gumption and Gagebee, and all the tribes

[069-096]of Gatherum Castle and South Audley Street; they wanted to rob him

[069-097]of that which had belonged to the Sowerbys before the name of Omnium

[069-098]had been heard of in the county, or in England! The great leviathan

[069-099]of the deep was anxious to swallow him up as a prey! He was to be

[069-100]swallowed up, and made away with, and put out of sight, without a

[069-101]pang of remorse! Any measure which could now present itself as the

[069-102]means of staving off so evil a day would be acceptable; and therefore

[069-103]he gave his sister the commission of making this second proposal

[069-104]to Miss Dunstable. In cursing the duke—for he did curse the duke

[069-105]lustily—it hardly occurred to him to think that, after all, the duke

[069-106]only asked for his own.

[069-107]As for Mrs. Harold Smith, whatever may be the view taken of her

[069-108]general character as a wife and a member of society, it must be

[069-109]admitted that as a sister she had virtues.

[069-110]CHAPTER XXXIII.

[069-111]CONSOLATION.

[069-112]On the next day, at two o’clock punctually, Mark Robarts was at the

[069-113]“Dragon of Wantly,” walking up and down the very room in which the

[069-114]party had breakfasted after Harold Smith’s lecture, and waiting for

[069-115]the arrival of Mr. Sowerby. He had been very well able to divine what

[069-116]was the business on which his friend wished to see him, and he had

[069-117]been rather glad than otherwise to receive the summons. Judging of

[069-118]his friend’s character by what he had hitherto seen, he thought that

[069-119]Mr. Sowerby would have kept out of the way, unless he had it in his

[069-120]power to make some provision for these terrible bills. So he walked

[069-121]up and down the dingy room, impatient for the expected arrival, and

[069-122]thought himself wickedly ill-used in that Mr. Sowerby was not there

[069-123]when the clock struck a quarter to three. But when the clock struck

[069-124]three, Mr. Sowerby was there, and Mark Robarts’s hopes were nearly at

[069-125]an end.

[069-126]“Do you mean that they will demand nine hundred pounds?” said

[069-127]Robarts, standing up and glaring angrily at the member of Parliament.

[069-128]“I fear that they will,” said Sowerby. “I think it is best to tell

[069-129]you the worst, in order that we may see what can be done.”

[069-130]“I can do nothing, and will do nothing,” said Robarts. “They may do

[069-131]what they choose—what the law allows them.”

[069-132]And then he thought of Fanny and his nursery, and Lucy refusing in

[069-133]her pride Lord Lufton’s offer, and he turned away his face that the

[069-134]hard man of the world before him might not see the tear gathering in

[069-135]his eye.

[069-136]“But, Mark, my dear fellow—” said Sowerby, trying to have recourse

[069-137]to the power of his cajoling voice.

[069-138]Robarts, however, would not listen.

[069-139]“Mr. Sowerby,” said he, with an attempt at calmness which betrayed

[069-140]itself at every syllable, “it seems to me that you have robbed

[069-141]me. That I have been a fool, and worse than a fool, I know well;

[069-142]but—but—but I thought that your position in the world would

[069-143]guarantee me from such treatment as this.”

[069-144]Mr. Sowerby was by no means without feeling, and the words which he

[069-145]now heard cut him very deeply—the more so because it was impossible

[069-146]that he should answer them with an attempt at indignation. He had

[069-147]robbed his friend, and, with all his wit, knew no words at the

[069-148]present moment sufficiently witty to make it seem that he had not

[069-149]done so.

[069-150]“Robarts,” said he, “you may say what you like to me now; I shall not

[069-151]resent it.”

[069-152]“Who would care for your resentment?” said the clergyman, turning on

[069-153]him with ferocity. “The resentment of a gentleman is terrible to a

[069-154]gentleman; and the resentment of one just man is terrible to another.

[069-155]Your resentment!”—and then he walked twice the length of the room,

[069-156]leaving Sowerby dumb in his seat. “I wonder whether you ever thought

[069-157]of my wife and children when you were plotting this ruin for me!” And

[069-158]then again he walked the room.

[069-159]“I suppose you will be calm enough presently to speak of this with

[069-160]some attempt to make a settlement?”

[069-161]“No; I will make no such attempt. These friends of yours, you tell

[069-162]me, have a claim on me for nine hundred pounds, of which they demand

[069-163]immediate payment. You shall be asked in a court of law how much of

[069-164]that money I have handled. You know that I have never touched—have

[069-165]never wanted to touch—one shilling. I will make no attempt at any

[069-166]settlement. My person is here, and there is my house. Let them do

[069-167]their worst.”

[069-168]“But, Mark—”

[069-169]“Call me by my name, sir, and drop that affectation of regard. What

[069-170]an ass I have been to be so cozened by a sharper!”

[069-171]Sowerby had by no means expected this. He had always known that

[069-172]Robarts possessed what he, Sowerby, would have called the spirit of

[069-173]a gentleman. He had regarded him as a bold, open, generous fellow,

[069-174]able to take his own part when called on to do so, and by no means

[069-175]disinclined to speak his own mind; but he had not expected from him

[069-176]such a torrent of indignation, or thought that he was capable of such

[069-177]a depth of anger.

[069-178]“If you use such language as that, Robarts, I can only leave you.”

[069-179]“You are welcome. Go. You tell me that you are the messenger of these

[069-180]men who intend to work nine hundred pounds out of me. You have done

[069-181]your part in the plot, and have now brought their message. It seems

[069-182]to me that you had better go back to them. As for me, I want my time

[069-183]to prepare my wife for the destiny before her.”
