## Section 75

[075-001]Lucy muttered something, which was intended to signify that any

[075-002]such dinner-party had better not be made up with the intention of

[075-003]including her, and then took her leave. She had decidedly had the

[075-004]best of the interview, and there was a consciousness of this in her

[075-005]heart as she allowed Lady Lufton to shake hands with her. She had

[075-006]stopped her antagonist short on each occasion on which an attempt had

[075-007]been made to produce the homily which had been prepared, and during

[075-008]the interview had spoken probably three words for every one which

[075-009]her ladyship had been able to utter. But, nevertheless, there was

[075-010]a bitter feeling of disappointment about her heart as she walked

[075-011]back home; and a feeling, also, that she herself had caused her own

[075-012]unhappiness. Why should she have been so romantic and chivalrous and

[075-013]self-sacrificing, seeing that her romance and chivalry had all been

[075-014]to his detriment as well as to hers,—seeing that she sacrificed

[075-015]him as well as herself? Why should she have been so anxious to play

[075-016]into Lady Lufton’s hands? It was not because she thought it right,

[075-017]as a general social rule, that a lady should refuse a gentleman’s

[075-018]hand, unless the gentleman’s mother were a consenting party to the

[075-019]marriage. She would have held any such doctrine as absurd. The lady,

[075-020]she would have said, would have had to look to her own family and no

[075-021]further. It was not virtue but cowardice which had influenced her,

[075-022]and she had none of that solace which may come to us in misfortune

[075-023]from a consciousness that our own conduct has been blameless. Lady

[075-024]Lufton had inspired her with awe, and any such feeling on her part

[075-025]was mean, ignoble, and unbecoming the spirit with which she wished to

[075-026]think that she was endowed. That was the accusation which she brought

[075-027]against herself, and it forbade her to feel any triumph as to the

[075-028]result of her interview.

[075-029]When she reached the parsonage, Mark was there, and they were of

[075-030]course expecting her. “Well,” said she, in her short, hurried manner,

[075-031]“is Puck ready again? I have no time to lose, and I must go and pack

[075-032]up a few things. Have you settled about the children, Fanny?”

[075-033]“Yes; I will tell you directly; but you have seen Lady Lufton?”

[075-034]“Seen her! Oh, yes, of course I have seen her. Did she not send for

[075-035]me? and in that case it was not on the cards that I should disobey

[075-036]her.”

[075-037]“And what did she say?”

[075-038]“How green you are, Mark; and not only green, but impolite also, to

[075-039]make me repeat the story of my own disgrace. Of course she told me

[075-040]that she did not intend that I should marry my lord, her son; and of

[075-041]course I said that under those circumstances I should not think of

[075-042]doing such a thing.”

[075-043]“Lucy, I cannot understand you,” said Fanny, very gravely. “I am

[075-044]sometimes inclined to doubt whether you have any deep feeling in the

[075-045]matter or not. If you have, how can you bring yourself to joke about

[075-046]it?”

[075-047]“Well, it is singular; and sometimes I doubt myself whether I have.

[075-048]I ought to be pale, ought I not? and very thin, and to go mad by

[075-049]degrees? I have not the least intention of doing anything of the

[075-050]kind, and, therefore, the matter is not worth any further notice.”

[075-051]“But was she civil to you, Lucy?” asked Mark; “civil in her manner,

[075-052]you know?”

[075-053]“Oh, uncommonly so. You will hardly believe it, but she actually

[075-054]asked me to dine. She always does, you know, when she wants to

[075-055]show her good-humour. If you’d broken your leg, and she wished to

[075-056]commiserate you, she’d ask you to dinner.”

[075-057]“I suppose she meant to be kind,” said Fanny, who was not disposed to

[075-058]give up her old friend, though she was quite ready to fight Lucy’s

[075-059]battle, if there were any occasion for a battle to be fought.

[075-060]“Lucy is so perverse,” said Mark, “that it is impossible to learn

[075-061]from her what really has taken place.”

[075-062]“Upon my word, then, you know it all as well as I can tell you. She

[075-063]asked me if Lord Lufton had made me an offer. I said, yes. She asked

[075-064]next, if I meant to accept it. Not without her approval, I said. And

[075-065]then she asked us all to dinner. That is exactly what took place, and

[075-066]I cannot see that I have been perverse at all.” After that she threw

[075-067]herself into a chair, and Mark and Fanny stood looking at each other.

[075-068]“Mark,” she said, after a while, “don’t be unkind to me. I make as

[075-069]little of it as I can, for all our sakes. It is better so, Fanny,

[075-070]than that I should go about moaning, like a sick cow;” and then they

[075-071]looked at her, and saw that the tears were already brimming over from

[075-072]her eyes.

[075-073]“Dearest, dearest Lucy,” said Fanny, immediately going down on her

[075-074]knees before her, “I won’t be unkind to you again.” And then they had

[075-075]a great cry together.

[075-076]CHAPTER XXXVI.

[075-077]KIDNAPPING AT HOGGLESTOCK.

[075-078]The great cry, however, did not take long, and Lucy was soon in the

[075-079]pony-carriage again. On this occasion her brother volunteered to

[075-080]drive her, and it was now understood that he was to bring back with

[075-081]him all the Crawley children. The whole thing had been arranged;

[075-082]the groom and his wife were to be taken into the house, and the

[075-083]big bedroom across the yard, usually occupied by them, was to be

[075-084]converted into a quarantine hospital until such time as it might be

[075-085]safe to pull down the yellow flag. They were about half way on their

[075-086]road to Hogglestock when they were overtaken by a man on horseback,

[075-087]whom, when he came up beside them, Mr. Robarts recognized as Dr.

[075-088]Arabin, Dean of Barchester, and head of the chapter to which he

[075-089]himself belonged. It immediately appeared that the dean also was

[075-090]going to Hogglestock, having heard of the misfortune that had

[075-091]befallen his friends there; he had, he said, started as soon as the

[075-092]news reached him, in order that he might ascertain how best he might

[075-093]render assistance. To effect this he had undertaken a ride of nearly

[075-094]forty miles, and explained that he did not expect to reach home again

[075-095]much before midnight.

[075-096]“You pass by Framley?” said Robarts.

[075-097]“Yes, I do,” said the dean.

[075-098]“Then of course you will dine with us as you go home; you and your

[075-099]horse also, which will be quite as important.” This having been duly

[075-100]settled, and the proper ceremony of introduction having taken place

[075-101]between the dean and Lucy, they proceeded to discuss the character of

[075-102]Mr. Crawley.

[075-103]“I have known him all my life,” said the dean, “having been at school

[075-104]and college with him, and for years since that I was on terms of the

[075-105]closest intimacy with him; but in spite of that, I do not know how to

[075-106]help him in his need. A prouder-hearted man I never met, or one less

[075-107]willing to share his sorrows with his friends.”

[075-108]“I have often heard him speak of you,” said Mark.

[075-109]“One of the bitterest feelings I have is that a man so dear to me

[075-110]should live so near to me, and that I should see so little of him.

[075-111]But what can I do? He will not come to my house; and when I go to

[075-112]his he is angry with me because I wear a shovel hat and ride on

[075-113]horseback.”

[075-114]“I should leave my hat and my horse at the borders of the last

[075-115]parish,” said Lucy, timidly.

[075-116]“Well; yes, certainly; one ought not to give offence even in such

[075-117]matters as that; but my coat and waistcoat would then be equally

[075-118]objectionable. I have changed,—in outward matters I mean,—and he

[075-119]has not. That irritates him, and unless I could be what I was in the

[075-120]old days, he will not look at me with the same eyes;” and then he

[075-121]rode on, in order, as he said, that the first pang of the interview

[075-122]might be over before Robarts and his sister came upon the scene.

[075-123]Mr. Crawley was standing before his door, leaning over the little

[075-124]wooden railing, when the dean trotted up on his horse. He had come

[075-125]out after hours of close watching to get a few mouthfuls of the

[075-126]sweet summer air, and as he stood there he held the youngest of his

[075-127]children in his arms. The poor little baby sat there, quiet indeed,

[075-128]but hardly happy. This father, though he loved his offspring with

[075-129]an affection as intense as that which human nature can supply, was

[075-130]not gifted with the knack of making children fond of him; for it

[075-131]is hardly more than a knack, that aptitude which some men have of

[075-132]gaining the good graces of the young. Such men are not always the

[075-133]best fathers or the safest guardians; but they carry about with them

[075-134]a certain duc ad me which children recognize, and which in three

[075-135]minutes upsets all the barriers between five and five-and-forty.

[075-136]But Mr. Crawley was a stern man, thinking ever of the souls and

[075-137]minds of his bairns—as a father should do; and thinking also that

[075-138]every season was fitted for operating on these souls and minds—as,

[075-139]perhaps, he should not have done either as a father or as a teacher.

[075-140]And consequently his children avoided him when the choice was given

[075-141]them, thereby adding fresh wounds to his torn heart, but by no means

[075-142]quenching any of the great love with which he regarded them.

[075-143]He was standing there thus with a placid little baby in his arms—a

[075-144]baby placid enough, but one that would not kiss him eagerly, and

[075-145]stroke his face with her soft little hands, as he would have had her

[075-146]do—when he saw the dean coming towards him. He was sharp-sighted

[075-147]as a lynx out in the open air, though now obliged to pore over his

[075-148]well-fingered books with spectacles on his nose; and thus he knew his

[075-149]friend from a long distance, and had time to meditate the mode of his

[075-150]greeting. He too doubtless had come, if not with jelly and chicken,

[075-151]then with money and advice;—with money and advice such as a thriving

[075-152]dean might offer to a poor brother clergyman; and Mr. Crawley, though

[075-153]no husband could possibly be more anxious for a wife’s safety than

[075-154]he was, immediately put his back up and began to bethink himself how

[075-155]these tenders might be rejected.

[075-156]“How is she?” were the first words which the dean spoke as he pulled

[075-157]up his horse close to the little gate, and put out his hand to take

[075-158]that of his friend.

[075-159]“How are you, Arabin?” said he. “It is very kind of you to come so

[075-160]far, seeing how much there is to keep you at Barchester. I cannot

[075-161]say that she is any better, but I do not know that she is worse.

[075-162]Sometimes I fancy that she is delirious, though I hardly know. At any

[075-163]rate her mind wanders, and then after that she sleeps.”

[075-164]“But is the fever less?”

[075-165]“Sometimes less and sometimes more, I imagine.”

[075-166]“And the children?”

[075-167]“Poor things; they are well as yet.”

[075-168]“They must be taken from this, Crawley, as a matter of course.”

[075-169]Mr. Crawley fancied that there was a tone of authority in the dean’s

[075-170]advice, and immediately put himself into opposition.

[075-171]“I do not know how that may be; I have not yet made up my mind.”

[075-172]“But, my dear Crawley—”

[075-173]“Providence does not admit of such removals in all cases,” said he.

[075-174]“Among the poorer classes the children must endure such perils.”

[075-175]“In many cases it is so,” said the dean, by no means inclined to make

[075-176]an argument of it at the present moment; “but in this case they need

[075-177]not. You must allow me to make arrangements for sending for them, as

[075-178]of course your time is occupied here.”
