## Section 31

[031-001]And then children had come. The wife of the labouring man does rear

[031-002]her children, and often rears them in health, without even so many

[031-003]appliances of comfort as found their way into Mrs. Crawley’s cottage;

[031-004]but the task to her was almost more than she could accomplish. Not

[031-005]that she ever fainted or gave way: she was made of the sterner metal

[031-006]of the two, and could last on while he was prostrate.

[031-007]And sometimes he was prostrate—prostrate in soul and spirit. Then

[031-008]would he complain with bitter voice, crying out that the world was

[031-009]too hard for him, that his back was broken with his burden, that his

[031-010]God had deserted him. For days and days, in such moods, he would stay

[031-011]within his cottage, never darkening the door or seeing other face

[031-012]than those of his own inmates. Those days were terrible both to him

[031-013]and her. He would sit there unwashed, with his unshorn face resting

[031-014]on his hand, with an old dressing-gown hanging loose about him,

[031-015]hardly tasting food, seldom speaking, striving to pray, but striving

[031-016]so frequently in vain. And then he would rise from his chair, and,

[031-017]with a burst of frenzy, call upon his Creator to remove him from this

[031-018]misery.

[031-019]In these moments she never deserted him. At one period they had had

[031-020]four children, and though the whole weight of this young brood rested

[031-021]on her arms, on her muscles, on her strength of mind and body, she

[031-022]never ceased in her efforts to comfort him. Then at length, falling

[031-023]utterly upon the ground, he would pour forth piteous prayers for

[031-024]mercy, and, after a night of sleep, would once more go forth to his

[031-025]work.

[031-026]But she never yielded to despair: the struggle was never beyond

[031-027]her powers of endurance. She had possessed her share of woman’s

[031-028]loveliness, but that was now all gone. Her colour quickly faded, and

[031-029]the fresh, soft tints soon deserted her face and forehead. She became

[031-030]thin, and rough, and almost haggard: thin, till her cheek-bones were

[031-031]nearly pressing through her skin, till her elbows were sharp, and her

[031-032]finger-bones as those of a skeleton. Her eye did not lose its lustre,

[031-033]but it became unnaturally bright, prominent, and too large for her

[031-034]wan face. The soft brown locks which she had once loved to brush

[031-035]back, scorning, as she would boast to herself, to care that they

[031-036]should be seen, were now sparse enough and all untidy and unclean.

[031-037]It was matter of little thought now whether they were seen or no.

[031-038]Whether he could be made fit to go into his pulpit—whether they

[031-039]might be fed—those four innocents—and their backs kept from the

[031-040]cold wind—that was now the matter of her thought.

[031-041]And then two of them died, and she went forth herself to see them

[031-042]laid under the frost-bound sod, lest he should faint in his work over

[031-043]their graves. For he would ask aid from no man—such at least was his

[031-044]boast through all.

[031-045]Two of them died, but their illness had been long; and then debts

[031-046]came upon them. Debt, indeed, had been creeping on them with slow

[031-047]but sure feet during the last five years. Who can see his children

[031-048]hungry, and not take bread if it be offered? Who can see his wife

[031-049]lying in sharpest want, and not seek a remedy if there be a remedy

[031-050]within reach? So debt had come upon them, and rude men pressed for

[031-051]small sums of money—for sums small to the world, but impossibly

[031-052]large to them. And he would hide himself within there, in that cranny

[031-053]of an inner chamber—hide himself with deep shame from the world,

[031-054]with shame, and a sinking heart, and a broken spirit.

[031-055]But had such a man no friend? it will be said. Such men, I take it,

[031-056]do not make many friends. But this man was not utterly friendless.

[031-057]Almost every year one visit was paid to him in his Cornish curacy

[031-058]by a brother clergyman, an old college friend, who, as far as

[031-059]might in him lie, did give aid to the curate and his wife. This

[031-060]gentleman would take up his abode for a week at a farmer’s in the

[031-061]neighbourhood, and though he found Mr. Crawley in despair, he would

[031-062]leave him with some drops of comfort in his soul. Nor were the

[031-063]benefits in this respect all on one side. Mr. Crawley, though at some

[031-064]periods weak enough for himself, could be strong for others; and,

[031-065]more than once, was strong to the great advantage of this man whom

[031-066]he loved. And then, too, pecuniary assistance was forthcoming—in

[031-067]those earlier years not in great amount, for this friend was not then

[031-068]among the rich ones of the earth—but in amount sufficient for that

[031-069]moderate hearth, if only its acceptance could have been managed. But

[031-070]in that matter there were difficulties without end. Of absolute money

[031-071]tenders Mr. Crawley would accept none. But a bill here and there was

[031-072]paid, the wife assisting; and shoes came for Kate—till Kate was

[031-073]placed beyond the need of shoes; and cloth for Harry and Frank found

[031-074]its way surreptitiously in beneath the cover of that wife’s solitary

[031-075]trunk—cloth with which those lean fingers worked garments for the

[031-076]two boys, to be worn—such was God’s will—only by the one.

[031-077]Such were Mr. and Mrs. Crawley in their Cornish curacy, and during

[031-078]their severest struggles. To one who thinks that a fair day’s work is

[031-079]worth a fair day’s wages, it seems hard enough that a man should work

[031-080]so hard and receive so little. There will be those who think that the

[031-081]fault was all his own in marrying so young. But still there remains

[031-082]that question, Is not a fair day’s work worth a fair day’s wages?

[031-083]This man did work hard—at a task perhaps the hardest of any that a

[031-084]man may do; and for ten years he earned some seventy pounds a year.

[031-085]Will any one say that he received fair wages for his fair work, let

[031-086]him be married or single? And yet there are so many who would fain

[031-087]pay their clergy, if they only knew how to apply their money! But

[031-088]that is a long subject, as Mr. Robarts had told Miss Dunstable.

[031-089]Such was Mr. Crawley in his Cornish curacy.

[031-090]CHAPTER XV.

[031-091]LADY LUFTON’S AMBASSADOR.

[031-092]And then, in the days which followed, that friend of Mr. Crawley’s,

[031-093]whose name, by-the-by, is yet to be mentioned, received quick

[031-094]and great promotion. Mr. Arabin by name he was then;—Dr. Arabin

[031-095]afterwards, when that quick and great promotion reached its climax.

[031-096]He had been simply a Fellow of Lazarus in those former years. Then he

[031-097]became Vicar of St. Ewold’s, in East Barsetshire, and had not yet got

[031-098]himself settled there when he married the Widow Bold, a widow with

[031-099]belongings in land and funded money, and with but one small baby as

[031-100]an encumbrance. Nor had he even yet married her,—had only engaged

[031-101]himself so to do, when they made him Dean of Barchester—all which

[031-102]may be read in the diocesan and county chronicles.

[031-103]And now that he was wealthy, the new dean did contrive to pay the

[031-104]debts of his poor friend, some lawyer of Camelford assisting him.

[031-105]It was but a paltry schedule after all, amounting in the total to

[031-106]something not much above a hundred pounds. And then, in the course

[031-107]of eighteen months, this poor piece of preferment fell in the dean’s

[031-108]way, this incumbency of Hogglestock with its stipend reaching one

[031-109]hundred and thirty pounds a year. Even that was worth double the

[031-110]Cornish curacy, and there was, moreover, a house attached to it. Poor

[031-111]Mrs. Crawley, when she heard of it, thought that their struggles

[031-112]of poverty were now well nigh over. What might not be done with a

[031-113]hundred and thirty pounds by people who had lived for ten years on

[031-114]seventy?

[031-115]And so they moved away out of that cold, bleak country, carrying with

[031-116]them their humble household gods, and settled themselves in another

[031-117]country, cold and bleak also, but less terribly so than the former.

[031-118]They settled themselves, and again began their struggles against

[031-119]man’s hardness and the devil’s zeal. I have said that Mr. Crawley was

[031-120]a stern, unpleasant man; and it certainly was so. The man must be

[031-121]made of very sterling stuff, whom continued and undeserved misfortune

[031-122]does not make unpleasant. This man had so far succumbed to grief,

[031-123]that it had left upon him its marks, palpable and not to be effaced.

[031-124]He cared little for society, judging men to be doing evil who did

[031-125]care for it. He knew as a fact, and believed with all his heart,

[031-126]that these sorrows had come to him from the hand of God, and that

[031-127]they would work for his weal in the long run; but not the less did

[031-128]they make him morose, silent, and dogged. He had always at his

[031-129]heart a feeling that he and his had been ill-used, and too often

[031-130]solaced himself, at the devil’s bidding, with the conviction that

[031-131]eternity would make equal that which life in this world had made so

[031-132]unequal;—the last bait that with which the devil angles after those

[031-133]who are struggling to elude his rod and line.

[031-134]The Framley property did not run into the parish of Hogglestock; but

[031-135]nevertheless Lady Lufton did what she could in the way of kindness to

[031-136]these new-comers. Providence had not supplied Hogglestock with a Lady

[031-137]Lufton, or with any substitute in the shape of lord or lady, squire

[031-138]or squiress. The Hogglestock farmers, male and female, were a rude,

[031-139]rough set, not bordering in their social rank on the farmer gentle;

[031-140]and Lady Lufton, knowing this, and hearing something of these

[031-141]Crawleys from Mrs. Arabin, the dean’s wife, trimmed her lamps, so

[031-142]that they should shed a wider light, and pour forth some of their

[031-143]influence on that forlorn household.

[031-144]And as regards Mrs. Crawley, Lady Lufton by no means found that

[031-145]her work and good-will were thrown away. Mrs. Crawley accepted her

[031-146]kindness with thankfulness, and returned to some of the softnesses of

[031-147]life under her hand. As for dining at Framley Court, that was out of

[031-148]the question. Mr. Crawley, she knew, would not hear of it, even if

[031-149]other things were fitting and appliances were at command. Indeed Mrs.

[031-150]Crawley at once said that she felt herself unfit to go through such

[031-151]a ceremony with anything like comfort. The dean, she said, would

[031-152]talk of their going to stay at the deanery; but she thought it quite

[031-153]impossible that either of them should endure even that. But, all the

[031-154]same, Lady Lufton was a comfort to her; and the poor woman felt that

[031-155]it was well to have a lady near her in case of need.

[031-156]The task was much harder with Mr. Crawley, but even with him it was

[031-157]not altogether unsuccessful. Lady Lufton talked to him of his parish

[031-158]and of her own; made Mark Robarts go to him, and by degrees did

[031-159]something towards civilizing him. Between him and Robarts too there

[031-160]grew up an intimacy rather than a friendship. Robarts would submit

[031-161]to his opinion on matters of ecclesiastical and even theological law,

[031-162]would listen to him with patience, would agree with him where he

[031-163]could, and differ from him mildly when he could not. For Robarts

[031-164]was a man who made himself pleasant to all men. And thus, under

[031-165]Lady Lufton’s wing, there grew up a connection between Framley and

[031-166]Hogglestock, in which Mrs. Robarts also assisted.

[031-167]And now that Lady Lufton was looking about her, to see how she might

[031-168]best bring proper clerical influence to bear upon her own recreant

[031-169]fox-hunting parson, it occurred to her that she might use Mr. Crawley

[031-170]in the matter. Mr. Crawley would certainly be on her side as far as

[031-171]opinion went, and would have no fear as to expressing his opinion to

[031-172]his brother clergyman. So she sent for Mr. Crawley.
