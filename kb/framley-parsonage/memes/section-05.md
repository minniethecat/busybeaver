## Section 5

[005-001]And so they went off. Lady Lufton was quite good enough at heart to

[005-002]like Mrs. Robarts all the better for standing up for her absent lord.

[005-003]CHAPTER III.

[005-004]CHALDICOTES.

[005-005]Chaldicotes is a house of much more pretension than Framley Court.

[005-006]Indeed, if one looks at the ancient marks about it, rather than

[005-007]at those of the present day, it is a place of very considerable

[005-008]pretension. There is an old forest, not altogether belonging to the

[005-009]property, but attached to it, called the Chace of Chaldicotes. A

[005-010]portion of this forest comes up close behind the mansion, and of

[005-011]itself gives a character and celebrity to the place. The Chace of

[005-012]Chaldicotes—the greater part of it, at least—is, as all the world

[005-013]knows, Crown property, and now, in these utilitarian days, is to be

[005-014]disforested. In former times it was a great forest, stretching half

[005-015]across the country, almost as far as Silverbridge; and there are bits

[005-016]of it, here and there, still to be seen at intervals throughout the

[005-017]whole distance; but the larger remaining portion, consisting of aged

[005-018]hollow oaks, centuries old, and wide-spreading withered beeches,

[005-019]stands in the two parishes of Chaldicotes and Uffley. People still

[005-020]come from afar to see the oaks of Chaldicotes, and to hear their feet

[005-021]rustle among the thick autumn leaves. But they will soon come no

[005-022]longer. The giants of past ages are to give way to wheat and turnips;

[005-023]a ruthless Chancellor of the Exchequer, disregarding old associations

[005-024]and rural beauty, requires money returns from the lands; and the

[005-025]Chace of Chaldicotes is to vanish from the earth’s surface.

[005-026]Some part of it, however, is the private property of Mr. Sowerby,

[005-027]who hitherto, through all his pecuniary distresses, has managed to

[005-028]save from the axe and the auction-mart that portion of his paternal

[005-029]heritage. The house of Chaldicotes is a large stone building,

[005-030]probably of the time of Charles the Second. It is approached on both

[005-031]fronts by a heavy double flight of stone steps. In the front of

[005-032]the house a long, solemn, straight avenue through a double row of

[005-033]lime-trees, leads away to lodge-gates, which stand in the centre of

[005-034]the village of Chaldicotes; but to the rear the windows open upon

[005-035]four different vistas, which run down through the forest: four open

[005-036]green rides, which all converge together at a large iron gateway,

[005-037]the barrier which divides the private grounds from the Chace. The

[005-038]Sowerbys, for many generations, have been rangers of the Chace of

[005-039]Chaldicotes, thus having almost as wide an authority over the Crown

[005-040]forest as over their own. But now all this is to cease, for the

[005-041]forest will be disforested.

[005-042]It was nearly dark as Mark Robarts drove up through the avenue of

[005-043]lime-trees to the hall-door; but it was easy to see that the house,

[005-044]which was dead and silent as the grave through nine months of the

[005-045]year, was now alive in all its parts. There were lights in many

[005-046]of the windows, and a noise of voices came from the stables, and

[005-047]servants were moving about, and dogs barked, and the dark gravel

[005-048]before the front steps was cut up with many a coach-wheel.

[005-049]“Oh, be that you, sir, Mr. Robarts?” said a groom, taking the

[005-050]parson’s horse by the head, and touching his own hat. “I hope I see

[005-051]your reverence well?”

[005-052]“Quite well, Bob, thank you. All well at Chaldicotes?”

[005-053]“Pretty bobbish, Mr. Robarts. Deal of life going on here now, sir.

[005-054]The bishop and his lady came this morning.”

[005-055]“Oh—ah—yes! I understood they were to be here. Any of the young

[005-056]ladies?”

[005-057]“One young lady. Miss Olivia, I think they call her, your reverence.”

[005-058]“And how’s Mr. Sowerby?”

[005-059]“Very well, your reverence. He, and Mr. Harold Smith, and Mr.

[005-060]Fothergill—that’s the duke’s man of business, you know—is getting

[005-061]off their horses now in the stable-yard there.”

[005-062]“Home from hunting—eh, Bob?”

[005-063]“Yes, sir, just home, this minute.” And then Mr. Robarts walked into

[005-064]the house, his portmanteau following on a footboy’s shoulder.

[005-065]It will be seen that our young vicar was very intimate at

[005-066]Chaldicotes; so much so that the groom knew him, and talked to him

[005-067]about the people in the house. Yes; he was intimate there: much more

[005-068]than he had given the Framley people to understand. Not that he had

[005-069]wilfully and overtly deceived any one; not that he had ever spoken a

[005-070]false word about Chaldicotes. But he had never boasted at home that

[005-071]he and Sowerby were near allies. Neither had he told them there

[005-072]how often Mr. Sowerby and Lord Lufton were together in London. Why

[005-073]trouble women with such matters? Why annoy so excellent a woman as

[005-074]Lady Lufton?

[005-075]And then Mr. Sowerby was one whose intimacy few young men would

[005-076]wish to reject. He was fifty, and had lived, perhaps, not the most

[005-077]salutary life; but he dressed young, and usually looked well. He was

[005-078]bald, with a good forehead, and sparkling moist eyes. He was a clever

[005-079]man, and a pleasant companion, and always good-humoured when it

[005-080]so suited him. He was a gentleman, too, of high breeding and good

[005-081]birth, whose ancestors had been known in that county—longer, the

[005-082]farmers around would boast, than those of any other landowner in it,

[005-083]unless it be the Thornes of Ullathorne, or perhaps the Greshams of

[005-084]Greshamsbury—much longer than the De Courcys at Courcy Castle. As

[005-085]for the Duke of Omnium, he, comparatively speaking, was a new man.

[005-086]And then he was a member of Parliament, a friend of some men in

[005-087]power, and of others who might be there; a man who could talk about

[005-088]the world as one knowing the matter of which he talked. And moreover,

[005-089]whatever might be his ways of life at other times, when in the

[005-090]presence of a clergyman he rarely made himself offensive to clerical

[005-091]tastes. He neither swore, nor brought his vices on the carpet, nor

[005-092]sneered at the faith of the Church. If he was no churchman himself,

[005-093]he at least knew how to live with those who were.

[005-094]How was it possible that such a one as our vicar should not relish

[005-095]the intimacy of Mr. Sowerby? It might be very well, he would say to

[005-096]himself, for a woman like Lady Lufton to turn up her nose at him—for

[005-097]Lady Lufton, who spent ten months of the year at Framley Court, and

[005-098]who during those ten months, and for the matter of that, during the

[005-099]two months also which she spent in London, saw no one out of her own

[005-100]set. Women did not understand such things, the vicar said to himself;

[005-101]even his own wife—good, and nice, and sensible, and intelligent as

[005-102]she was—even she did not understand that a man in the world must

[005-103]meet all sorts of men; and that in these days it did not do for a

[005-104]clergyman to be a hermit.

[005-105]’Twas thus that Mark Robarts argued when he found himself called upon

[005-106]to defend himself before the bar of his own conscience for going to

[005-107]Chaldicotes and increasing his intimacy with Mr. Sowerby. He did know

[005-108]that Mr. Sowerby was a dangerous man; he was aware that he was over

[005-109]head and ears in debt, and that he had already entangled young Lord

[005-110]Lufton in some pecuniary embarrassment; his conscience did tell him

[005-111]that it would be well for him, as one of Christ’s soldiers, to look

[005-112]out for companions of a different stamp. But nevertheless he went

[005-113]to Chaldicotes, not satisfied with himself indeed, but repeating to

[005-114]himself a great many arguments why he should be so satisfied.

[005-115]He was shown into the drawing-room at once, and there he found Mrs.

[005-116]Harold Smith, with Mrs. and Miss Proudie, and a lady whom he had

[005-117]never before seen, and whose name he did not at first hear mentioned.

[005-118]“Is that Mr. Robarts?” said Mrs. Harold Smith, getting up to greet

[005-119]him, and screening her pretended ignorance under the veil of the

[005-120]darkness. “And have you really driven over four-and-twenty miles of

[005-121]Barsetshire roads on such a day as this to assist us in our little

[005-122]difficulties? Well, we can promise you gratitude at any rate.”

[005-123]And then the vicar shook hands with Mrs. Proudie, in that deferential

[005-124]manner which is due from a vicar to his bishop’s wife; and Mrs.

[005-125]Proudie returned the greeting with all that smiling condescension

[005-126]which a bishop’s wife should show to a vicar. Miss Proudie was not

[005-127]quite so civil. Had Mr. Robarts been still unmarried, she also could

[005-128]have smiled sweetly; but she had been exercising smiles on clergymen

[005-129]too long to waste them now on a married parish parson.

[005-130]“And what are the difficulties, Mrs. Smith, in which I am to assist

[005-131]you?”

[005-132]“We have six or seven gentlemen here, Mr. Robarts, and they always go

[005-133]out hunting before breakfast, and they never come back—I was going

[005-134]to say—till after dinner. I wish it were so, for then we should not

[005-135]have to wait for them.”

[005-136]“Excepting Mr. Supplehouse, you know,” said the unknown lady, in a

[005-137]loud voice.

[005-138]“And he is generally shut up in the library, writing articles.”

[005-139]“He’d be better employed if he were trying to break his neck like the

[005-140]others,” said the unknown lady.

[005-141]“Only he would never succeed,” said Mrs. Harold Smith. “But perhaps,

[005-142]Mr. Robarts, you are as bad as the rest; perhaps you, too, will be

[005-143]hunting to-morrow.”

[005-144]“My dear Mrs. Smith!” said Mrs. Proudie, in a tone denoting slight

[005-145]reproach, and modified horror.

[005-146]“Oh! I forgot. No, of course, you won’t be hunting, Mr. Robarts;

[005-147]you’ll only be wishing that you could.”

[005-148]“Why can’t he?” said the lady with the loud voice.

[005-149]“My dear Miss Dunstable! a clergyman hunt, while he is staying in the

[005-150]same house with the bishop? Think of the proprieties!”

[005-151]“Oh—ah! The bishop wouldn’t like it—wouldn’t he? Now, do tell me,

[005-152]sir, what would the bishop do to you if you did hunt?”

[005-153]“It would depend upon his mood at the time, madam,” said Mr. Robarts.

[005-154]“If that were very stern, he might perhaps have me beheaded before

[005-155]the palace gates.”

[005-156]Mrs. Proudie drew herself up in her chair, showing that she did

[005-157]not like the tone of the conversation; and Miss Proudie fixed her

[005-158]eyes vehemently on her book, showing that Miss Dunstable and her

[005-159]conversation were both beneath her notice.

[005-160]“If these gentlemen do not mean to break their necks to-night,” said

[005-161]Mrs. Harold Smith, “I wish they’d let us know it. It’s half-past six

[005-162]already.”

[005-163]And then Mr. Robarts gave them to understand that no such catastrophe

[005-164]could be looked for that day, as Mr. Sowerby and the other sportsmen

[005-165]were within the stable-yard when he entered the door.

[005-166]“Then, ladies, we may as well dress,” said Mrs. Harold Smith. But

[005-167]as she moved towards the door, it opened, and a short gentleman,

[005-168]with a slow, quiet step, entered the room; but was not yet to be

[005-169]distinguished through the dusk by the eyes of Mr. Robarts. “Oh!

[005-170]bishop, is that you?” said Mrs. Smith. “Here is one of the luminaries

[005-171]of your diocese.” And then the bishop, feeling through the dark, made

[005-172]his way up to the vicar and shook him cordially by the hand. “He

[005-173]was delighted to meet Mr. Robarts at Chaldicotes,” he said—“quite

[005-174]delighted. Was he not going to preach on behalf of the Papuan Mission

[005-175]next Sunday? Ah! so he, the bishop, had heard. It was a good work,

[005-176]an excellent work.” And then Dr. Proudie expressed himself as much

[005-177]grieved that he could not remain at Chaldicotes, and hear the sermon.

[005-178]It was plain that his bishop thought no ill of him on account of his

[005-179]intimacy with Mr. Sowerby. But then he felt in his own heart that he

[005-180]did not much regard his bishop’s opinion.

[005-181]“Ah, Robarts, I’m delighted to see you,” said Mr. Sowerby, when they

[005-182]met on the drawing-room rug before dinner. “You know Harold Smith?

[005-183]Yes, of course you do. Well, who else is there? Oh! Supplehouse. Mr.

[005-184]Supplehouse, allow me to introduce to you my friend Mr. Robarts. It

[005-185]is he who will extract the five-pound note out of your pocket next

[005-186]Sunday for these poor Papuans whom we are going to Christianize.

[005-187]That is, if Harold Smith does not finish the work out of hand at his

[005-188]Saturday lecture. And, Robarts, you have seen the bishop, of course:”

[005-189]this he said in a whisper. “A fine thing to be a bishop, isn’t it?

[005-190]I wish I had half your chance. But, my dear fellow, I’ve made such a

[005-191]mistake; I haven’t got a bachelor parson for Miss Proudie. You must

[005-192]help me out, and take her in to dinner.” And then the great gong

[005-193]sounded, and off they went in pairs.
