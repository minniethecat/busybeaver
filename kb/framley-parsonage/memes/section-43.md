## Section 43

[043-001]All this was a great drawback to his happiness, but still it could

[043-002]not rob him of the fact of his position. Lord Brock could not ask him

[043-003]to resign because the _Jupiter_ had written against him; nor was Lord

[043-004]Brock the man to desert a new colleague for such a reason. So Harold

[043-005]Smith girded his loins, and went about the duties of the Petty Bag

[043-006]with new zeal. “Upon my word, the _Jupiter_ is right,” said young

[043-007]Robarts to himself, as he finished his fourth dozen of private notes

[043-008]explanatory of everything in and about the Petty Bag Office. Harold

[043-009]Smith required that his private secretary’s notes should be so

[043-010]terribly precise.

[043-011]But nevertheless, in spite of his drawbacks, Harold Smith was happy

[043-012]in his new honours, and Mrs. Harold Smith enjoyed them also. She

[043-013]certainly, among her acquaintance, did quiz the new cabinet minister

[043-014]not a little, and it may be a question whether she was not as hard

[043-015]upon him as the writer in the _Jupiter_. She whispered a great deal

[043-016]to Miss Dunstable about new blood, and talked of going down to

[043-017]Westminster Bridge to see whether the Thames were really on fire. But

[043-018]though she laughed, she triumphed, and though she flattered herself

[043-019]that she bore her honours without any outward sign, the world knew

[043-020]that she was triumphing, and ridiculed her elation.

[043-021]About this time she also gave a party—not a pure-minded

[043-022]conversazione like Mrs. Proudie, but a downright wicked worldly

[043-023]dance, at which there were fiddles, ices, and champagne sufficient to

[043-024]run away with the first quarter’s salary accruing to Harold from the

[043-025]Petty Bag Office. To us this ball is chiefly memorable from the fact

[043-026]that Lady Lufton was among the guests. Immediately on her arrival in

[043-027]town she received cards from Mrs. H. Smith for herself and Griselda,

[043-028]and was about to send back a reply at once declining the honour.

[043-029]What had she to do at the house of Mr. Sowerby’s sister? But it

[043-030]so happened that at that moment her son was with her, and as he

[043-031]expressed a wish that she should go, she yielded. Had there been

[043-032]nothing in his tone of persuasion more than ordinary,—had it merely

[043-033]had reference to herself,—she would have smiled on him for his kind

[043-034]solicitude, have made out some occasion for kissing his forehead as

[043-035]she thanked him, and would still have declined. But he had reminded

[043-036]her both of himself and Griselda. “You might as well go, mother, for

[043-037]the sake of meeting me,” he said; “Mrs. Harold caught me the other

[043-038]day, and would not liberate me till I had given her a promise.”

[043-039]“That is an attraction certainly,” said Lady Lufton. “I do like going

[043-040]to a house when I know that you will be there.”

[043-041]“And now that Miss Grantly is with you—you owe it to her to do the

[043-042]best you can for her.”

[043-043]“I certainly do, Ludovic; and I have to thank you for reminding me

[043-044]of my duty so gallantly.” And so she said that she would go to Mrs.

[043-045]Harold Smith’s. Poor lady! She gave much more weight to those few

[043-046]words about Miss Grantly than they deserved. It rejoiced her heart

[043-047]to think that her son was anxious to meet Griselda—that he should

[043-048]perpetrate this little _ruse_ in order to gain his wish. But he had

[043-049]spoken out of the mere emptiness of his mind, without thought of what

[043-050]he was saying, excepting that he wished to please his mother.

[043-051]But nevertheless he went to Mrs. Harold Smith’s, and when there he

[043-052]did dance more than once with Griselda Grantly—to the manifest

[043-053]discomfiture of Lord Dumbello. He came in late, and at the moment

[043-054]Lord Dumbello was moving slowly up the room, with Griselda on his

[043-055]arm, while Lady Lufton was sitting near looking on with unhappy eyes.

[043-056]And then Griselda sat down, and Lord Dumbello stood mute at her

[043-057]elbow.

[043-058]“Ludovic,” whispered his mother, “Griselda is absolutely bored by

[043-059]that man, who follows her like a ghost. Do go and rescue her.”

[043-060]He did go and rescue her, and afterwards danced with her for the

[043-061]best part of an hour consecutively. He knew that the world gave Lord

[043-062]Dumbello the credit of admiring the young lady, and was quite alive

[043-063]to the pleasure of filling his brother nobleman’s heart with jealousy

[043-064]and anger. Moreover, Griselda was in his eyes very beautiful, and had

[043-065]she been one whit more animated, or had his mother’s tactics been but

[043-066]a thought better concealed, Griselda might have been asked that night

[043-067]to share the vacant throne at Lufton, in spite of all that had been

[043-068]said and sworn in the drawing-room of Framley Parsonage.

[043-069]It must be remembered that our gallant, gay Lothario had passed some

[043-070]considerable number of days with Miss Grantly in his mother’s house,

[043-071]and the danger of such contiguity must be remembered also. Lord

[043-072]Lufton was by no means a man capable of seeing beauty unmoved or of

[043-073]spending hours with a young lady without some approach to tenderness.

[043-074]Had there been no such approach, it is probable that Lady Lufton

[043-075]would not have pursued the matter. But, according to her ideas on

[043-076]such subjects, her son Ludovic had on some occasions shown quite

[043-077]sufficient partiality for Miss Grantly to justify her in her hopes,

[043-078]and to lead her to think that nothing but opportunity was wanted.

[043-079]Now, at this ball of Mrs. Smith’s, he did, for a while, seem to be

[043-080]taking advantage of such opportunity, and his mother’s heart was

[043-081]glad. If things should turn out well on this evening she would

[043-082]forgive Mrs. Harold Smith all her sins.

[043-083]And for a while it looked as though things would turn out well. Not

[043-084]that it must be supposed that Lord Lufton had come there with any

[043-085]intention of making love to Griselda, or that he ever had any fixed

[043-086]thought that he was doing so. Young men in such matters are so often

[043-087]without any fixed thoughts! They are such absolute moths. They amuse

[043-088]themselves with the light of the beautiful candle, fluttering about,

[043-089]on and off, in and out of the flame with dazzled eyes, till in a rash

[043-090]moment they rush in too near the wick, and then fall with singed

[043-091]wings and crippled legs, burnt up and reduced to tinder by the

[043-092]consuming fire of matrimony. Happy marriages, men say, are made in

[043-093]heaven, and I believe it. Most marriages are fairly happy, in spite

[043-094]of Sir Cresswell Cresswell; and yet how little care is taken on earth

[043-095]towards such a result!

[043-096]“I hope my mother is using you well?” said Lord Lufton to Griselda,

[043-097]as they were standing together in a doorway between the dances.

[043-098]“Oh, yes: she is very kind.”

[043-099]“You have been rash to trust yourself in the hands of so very staid

[043-100]and demure a person. And, indeed, you owe your presence here at Mrs.

[043-101]Harold Smith’s first cabinet ball altogether to me. I don’t know

[043-102]whether you are aware of that.”

[043-103]“Oh, yes: Lady Lufton told me.”

[043-104]“And are you grateful or otherwise? Have I done you an injury or a

[043-105]benefit? Which do you find best, sitting with a novel in the corner

[043-106]of a sofa in Bruton Street, or pretending to dance polkas here with

[043-107]Lord Dumbello?”

[043-108]“I don’t know what you mean. I haven’t stood up with Lord Dumbello

[043-109]all the evening. We were going to dance a quadrille, but we didn’t.”

[043-110]“Exactly; just what I say;—pretending to do it. Even that’s a good

[043-111]deal for Lord Dumbello; isn’t it?” And then Lord Lufton, not being a

[043-112]pretender himself, put his arm round her waist, and away they went up

[043-113]and down the room, and across and about, with an energy which showed

[043-114]that what Griselda lacked in her tongue she made up with her feet.

[043-115]Lord Dumbello, in the meantime, stood by, observant, thinking to

[043-116]himself that Lord Lufton was a glib-tongued, empty-headed ass, and

[043-117]reflecting that if his rival were to break the tendons of his leg in

[043-118]one of those rapid evolutions, or suddenly come by any other dreadful

[043-119]misfortune, such as the loss of all his property, absolute blindness,

[043-120]or chronic lumbago, it would only serve him right. And in that frame

[043-121]of mind he went to bed, in spite of the prayer which no doubt he said

[043-122]as to his forgiveness of other people’s trespasses.

[043-123]And then, when they were again standing, Lord Lufton, in the little

[043-124]intervals between his violent gasps for fresh breath, asked Griselda

[043-125]if she liked London. “Pretty well,” said Griselda, gasping also a

[043-126]little herself.

[043-127]“I am afraid—you were very dull—down at Framley.”

[043-128]“Oh, no;—I liked it—particularly.”

[043-129]“It was a great bore when you went—away, I know. There wasn’t a

[043-130]soul—about the house worth speaking to.” And they remained silent

[043-131]for a minute till their lungs had become quiescent.

[043-132]“Not a soul,” he continued—not of falsehood prepense, for he was not

[043-133]in fact thinking of what he was saying. It did not occur to him at

[043-134]the moment that he had truly found Griselda’s going a great relief,

[043-135]and that he had been able to do more in the way of conversation with

[043-136]Lucy Robarts in one hour than with Miss Grantly during a month of

[043-137]intercourse in the same house. But, nevertheless, we should not be

[043-138]hard upon him. All is fair in love and war; and if this was not love,

[043-139]it was the usual thing that stands as a counterpart for it.

[043-140]“Not a soul,” said Lord Lufton. “I was very nearly hanging myself in

[043-141]the park next morning;—only it rained.”

[043-142]“What nonsense! You had your mother to talk to.”

[043-143]“Oh, my mother,—yes; and you may tell me too, if you please, that

[043-144]Captain Culpepper was there. I do love my mother dearly; but do you

[043-145]think that she could make up for your absence?” And his voice was

[043-146]very tender, and so were his eyes.

[043-147]“And Miss Robarts; I thought you admired her very much?”

[043-148]“What, Lucy Robarts?” said Lord Lufton, feeling that Lucy’s name

[043-149]was more than he at present knew how to manage. Indeed that name

[043-150]destroyed all the life there was in that little flirtation. “I do

[043-151]like Lucy Robarts, certainly. She is very clever; but it so happened

[043-152]that I saw little or nothing of her after you were gone.”

[043-153]To this Griselda made no answer, but drew herself up, and looked

[043-154]as cold as Diana when she froze Orion in the cave. Nor could she

[043-155]be got to give more than monosyllabic answers to the three or four

[043-156]succeeding attempts at conversation which Lord Lufton made. And then

[043-157]they danced again, but Griselda’s steps were by no means so lively as

[043-158]before.

[043-159]What took place between them on that occasion was very little more

[043-160]than what has been here related. There may have been an ice or a

[043-161]glass of lemonade into the bargain, and perhaps the faintest possible

[043-162]attempt at hand-pressing. But if so, it was all on one side. To such

[043-163]overtures as that Griselda Grantly was as cold as any Diana.

[043-164]But little as all this was, it was sufficient to fill Lady Lufton’s

[043-165]mind and heart. No mother with six daughters was ever more anxious

[043-166]to get them off her hands, than Lady Lufton was to see her son

[043-167]married,—married, that is, to some girl of the right sort. And now

[043-168]it really did seem as though he were actually going to comply with

[043-169]her wishes. She had watched him during the whole evening, painfully

[043-170]endeavouring not to be observed in doing so. She had seen Lord

[043-171]Dumbello’s failure and wrath, and she had seen her son’s victory and

[043-172]pride. Could it be the case that he had already said something, which

[043-173]was still allowed to be indecisive only through Griselda’s coldness?

[043-174]Might it not be the case, that by some judicious aid on her part,

[043-175]that indecision might be turned into certainty, and that coldness

[043-176]into warmth? But then any such interference requires so delicate a

[043-177]touch,—as Lady Lufton was well aware.
