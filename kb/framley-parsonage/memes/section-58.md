## Section 58

[058-001]Sowerby still stood in the place in which he had first fixed himself,

[058-002]and now for awhile he remained silent. His face was very stern, and

[058-003]there was in his countenance none of those winning looks which often

[058-004]told so powerfully with his young friends,—which had caught Lord

[058-005]Lufton and had charmed Mark Robarts. The world was going against him,

[058-006]and things around him were coming to an end. He was beginning to

[058-007]perceive that he had in truth eaten his cake, and that there was now

[058-008]little left for him to do,—unless he chose to blow out his brains.

[058-009]He had said to Lord Lufton that a man’s back should be broad enough

[058-010]for any burden with which he himself might load it. Could he now

[058-011]boast that his back was broad enough and strong enough for this

[058-012]burden? But he had even then, at that bitter moment, a strong

[058-013]remembrance that it behoved him still to be a man. His final ruin was

[058-014]coming on him, and he would soon be swept away out of the knowledge

[058-015]and memory of those with whom he had lived. But, nevertheless, he

[058-016]would bear himself well to the last. It was true that he had made his

[058-017]own bed, and he understood the justice which required him to lie upon

[058-018]it.

[058-019]During all this time Fothergill occupied himself with the papers.

[058-020]He continued to turn over one sheet after another, as though he

[058-021]were deeply engaged in money considerations and calculations. But,

[058-022]in truth, during all that time he did not read a word. There was

[058-023]nothing there for him to read. The reading and the writing, and the

[058-024]arithmetic in such matters, are done by underlings—not by such big

[058-025]men as Mr. Fothergill. His business was to tell Sowerby that he was

[058-026]to go. All those records there were of very little use. The duke had

[058-027]the power; Sowerby knew that the duke had the power; and Fothergill’s

[058-028]business was to explain that the duke meant to exercise his power.

[058-029]He was used to the work, and went on turning over the papers and

[058-030]pretending to read them, as though his doing so were of the greatest

[058-031]moment.

[058-032]“I shall see the duke myself,” Mr. Sowerby said at last, and there

[058-033]was something almost dreadful in the sound of his voice.

[058-034]“You know that the duke won’t see you on a matter of this kind. He

[058-035]never speaks to anyone about money; you know that as well as I do.”

[058-036]“By ——, but he shall speak to me. Never speak to anyone about

[058-037]money! Why is he ashamed to speak of it when he loves it so dearly?

[058-038]He shall see me.”

[058-039]“I have nothing further to say, Sowerby. Of course I shan’t ask his

[058-040]grace to see you; and if you force your way in on him you know what

[058-041]will happen. It won’t be my doing if he is set against you. Nothing

[058-042]that you say to me in that way,—nothing that anybody ever

[058-043]says,—goes beyond myself.”

[058-044]“I shall manage the matter through my own lawyer,” said Sowerby; and

[058-045]then he took his hat, and, without uttering another word, left the

[058-046]room.

[058-047]We know not what may be the nature of that eternal punishment to

[058-048]which those will be doomed who shall be judged to have been evil at

[058-049]the last; but methinks that no more terrible torment can be devised

[058-050]than the memory of self-imposed ruin. What wretchedness can exceed

[058-051]that of remembering from day to day that the race has been all run,

[058-052]and has been altogether lost; that the last chance has gone, and has

[058-053]gone in vain; that the end has come, and with it disgrace, contempt,

[058-054]and self-scorn—disgrace that never can be redeemed, contempt that

[058-055]never can be removed, and self-scorn that will eat into one’s vitals

[058-056]for ever?

[058-057]Mr. Sowerby was now fifty; he had enjoyed his chances in life; and as

[058-058]he walked back, up South Audley Street, he could not but think of the

[058-059]uses he had made of them. He had fallen into the possession of a fine

[058-060]property on the attainment of his manhood; he had been endowed with

[058-061]more than average gifts of intellect; never-failing health had been

[058-062]given to him, and a vision fairly clear in discerning good from evil;

[058-063]and now to what a pass had he brought himself!

[058-064]And that man Fothergill had put all this before him in so terribly

[058-065]clear a light! Now that the day for his final demolishment had

[058-066]arrived, the necessity that he should be demolished—finished away at

[058-067]once, out of sight and out of mind—had not been softened, or, as it

[058-068]were, half hidden, by any ambiguous phrase. “You have had your cake,

[058-069]and eaten it—eaten it greedily. Is not that sufficient for you?

[058-070]Would you eat your cake twice? Would you have a succession of cakes?

[058-071]No, my friend; there is no succession of these cakes for those who

[058-072]eat them greedily. Your proposition is not a fair one, and we who

[058-073]have the whip-hand of you will not listen to it. Be good enough to

[058-074]vanish. Permit yourself to be swept quietly into the dunghill. All

[058-075]that there was about you of value has departed from you; and allow me

[058-076]to say that you are now—rubbish.” And then the ruthless besom comes

[058-077]with irresistible rush, and the rubbish is swept into the pit, there

[058-078]to be hidden for ever from the sight.

[058-079]And the pity of it is this—that a man, if he will only restrain

[058-080]his greed, may eat his cake and yet have it; ay, and in so doing

[058-081]will have twice more the flavour of the cake than he who with

[058-082]gourmandizing maw will devour his dainty all at once. Cakes in this

[058-083]world will grow by being fed on, if only the feeder be not too

[058-084]insatiate. On all which wisdom Mr. Sowerby pondered with sad heart

[058-085]and very melancholy mind as he walked away from the premises of

[058-086]Messrs. Gumption and Gagebee.

[058-087]His intention had been to go down to the House after leaving Mr.

[058-088]Fothergill, but the prospect of immediate ruin had been too much for

[058-089]him, and he knew that he was not fit to be seen at once among the

[058-090]haunts of men. And he had intended also to go down to Barchester

[058-091]early on the following morning—only for a few hours, that he might

[058-092]make further arrangements respecting that bill which Robarts had

[058-093]accepted for him. That bill—the second one—had now become due, and

[058-094]Mr. Tozer had been with him.

[058-095]“Now it ain’t no use in life, Mr. Sowerby,” Tozer had said. “I ain’t

[058-096]got the paper myself, nor didn’t ’old it, not two hours. It went away

[058-097]through Tom Tozer; you knows that, Mr. Sowerby, as well as I do.”

[058-098]Now, whenever Tozer, Mr. Sowerby’s Tozer, spoke of Tom Tozer, Mr.

[058-099]Sowerby knew that seven devils were being evoked, each worse than

[058-100]the first devil. Mr. Sowerby did feel something like sincere regard,

[058-101]or rather love, for that poor parson whom he had inveigled into

[058-102]mischief, and would fain save him, if it were possible, from the

[058-103]Tozer fang. Mr. Forrest, of the Barchester bank, would probably take

[058-104]up that last five hundred pound bill, on behalf of Mr. Robarts,—only

[058-105]it would be needful that he, Sowerby, should run down and see that

[058-106]this was properly done. As to the other bill—the former and lesser

[058-107]one—as to that, Mr. Tozer would probably be quiet for a while.

[058-108]Such had been Sowerby’s programme for these two days; but now—what

[058-109]further possibility was there now that he should care for Robarts,

[058-110]or any other human being; he that was to be swept at once into the

[058-111]dung-heap?

[058-112]In this frame of mind he walked up South Audley Street, and crossed

[058-113]one side of Grosvenor Square, and went almost mechanically into Green

[058-114]Street. At the farther end of Green Street, near to Park Lane, lived

[058-115]Mr. and Mrs. Harold Smith.

[058-116]CHAPTER XXVIII.

[058-117]DR. THORNE.

[058-118]When Miss Dunstable met her friends, the Greshams—young Frank

[058-119]Gresham and his wife—at Gatherum Castle, she immediately asked after

[058-120]one Dr. Thorne, who was Mrs. Gresham’s uncle. Dr. Thorne was an old

[058-121]bachelor, in whom both as a man and a doctor Miss Dunstable was

[058-122]inclined to place much confidence. Not that she had ever entrusted

[058-123]the cure of her bodily ailments to Dr. Thorne—for she kept a doctor

[058-124]of her own, Dr. Easyman, for this purpose—and it may moreover be

[058-125]said that she rarely had bodily ailments requiring the care of any

[058-126]doctor. But she always spoke of Dr. Thorne among her friends as a man

[058-127]of wonderful erudition and judgment; and had once or twice asked and

[058-128]acted on his advice in matters of much moment. Dr. Thorne was not

[058-129]a man accustomed to the London world; he kept no house there, and

[058-130]seldom even visited the metropolis; but Miss Dunstable had known him

[058-131]at Greshamsbury, where he lived, and there had for some months past

[058-132]grown up a considerable intimacy between them. He was now staying at

[058-133]the house of his niece, Mrs. Gresham; but the chief reason of his

[058-134]coming up had been a desire expressed by Miss Dunstable, that he

[058-135]should do so. She had wished for his advice; and at the instigation

[058-136]of his niece he had visited London and given it.

[058-137]The special piece of business as to which Dr. Thorne had thus been

[058-138]summoned from the bedsides of his country patients, and especially

[058-139]from the bedside of Lady Arabella Gresham, to whose son his niece was

[058-140]married, related to certain large money interests, as to which one

[058-141]might have imagined that Dr. Thorne’s advice would not be peculiarly

[058-142]valuable. He had never been much versed in such matters on his own

[058-143]account, and was knowing neither in the ways of the share market, nor

[058-144]in the prices of land. But Miss Dunstable was a lady accustomed to

[058-145]have her own way, and to be indulged in her own wishes without being

[058-146]called on to give adequate reasons for them.

[058-147]“My dear,” she had said to young Mrs. Gresham, “if your uncle don’t

[058-148]come up to London now, when I make such a point of it, I shall think

[058-149]that he is a bear and a savage; and I certainly will never speak to

[058-150]him again,—or to Frank—or to you; so you had better see to it.”

[058-151]Mrs. Gresham had not probably taken her friend’s threat as meaning

[058-152]quite all that it threatened. Miss Dunstable habitually used strong

[058-153]language; and those who knew her well, generally understood when

[058-154]she was to be taken as expressing her thoughts by figures of speech.

[058-155]In this instance she had not meant it all; but, nevertheless, Mrs.

[058-156]Gresham had used violent influence in bringing the poor doctor up to

[058-157]London.

[058-158]“Besides,” said Miss Dunstable, “I have resolved on having the doctor

[058-159]at my conversazione, and if he won’t come of himself, I shall go down

[058-160]and fetch him. I have set my heart on trumping my dear friend Mrs.

[058-161]Proudie’s best card; so I mean to get everybody!”

[058-162]The upshot of all this was, that the doctor did come up to town, and

[058-163]remained the best part of a week at his niece’s house in Portman

[058-164]Square—to the great disgust of the Lady Arabella, who conceived

[058-165]that she must die if neglected for three days. As to the matter

[058-166]of business, I have no doubt but that he was of great use. He

[058-167]was possessed of common sense and an honest purpose; and I am

[058-168]inclined to think that they are often a sufficient counterpoise to

[058-169]a considerable amount of worldly experience. If one could have the

[058-170]worldly experience also—! True! but then it is so difficult to get

[058-171]everything. But with that special matter of business we need not

[058-172]have any further concern. We will presume it to have been discussed

[058-173]and completed, and will now dress ourselves for Miss Dunstable’s

[058-174]conversazione.
