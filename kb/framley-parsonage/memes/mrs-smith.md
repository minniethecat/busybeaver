# Mrs. Smith

Mrs. Smith is the innkeeper at Chaldicotes, a key figure in the social infrastructure of the novel whose establishment serves as the gathering-place for the hunting and political set that includes [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]], [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]], and [[miss-dunstable|Miss Dunstable]]. She is no mere servant; her wit and authority make her a participant in, rather than merely a facilitator of, the social life she hosts.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Smith runs the inn at Chaldicotes, the country house where much of the novel's male social life — hunting, dining, political talk — takes place. She is on familiar terms with the gentlemen who frequent her establishment, and her exchanges with Mrs. Proudie and Miss Dunstable reveal a woman as quick with a retort as she is attentive to her guests' comfort. When Mark Robarts arrives at Chaldicotes, it is Mrs. Smith who first receives him and explains the "difficulties" of managing gentlemen who go out hunting before breakfast and never come back (005-130).

Her presence at the Chaldicotes lecture — where she sits among the party and exchanges remarks with the bishop himself — demonstrates that she moves easily across the social boundaries that constrain other characters. She calls the bishop "one of the luminaries of your diocese" with an irony that is both deferential and knowing (005-170).

## Character Traits

- **Practical authority**: Mrs. Smith manages the practicalities of a busy country inn, organising gigs, meals, and hunting parties. When a gig is required, she remarks with characteristic practicality that there is a fly to be had, but it won't carry more than two (014-175) (014-180).
- **Sharp wit**: Her exchanges with Mrs. Proudie reveal a woman who gives as good as she gets. When Mrs. Proudie addresses her in a tone of "slight depreciation," Mrs. Smith replies without flinching (005-144). To Miss Dunstable's teasing about business, she retorts: "If you were married to Mr. Harold Smith for one week, you'd believe in it" (006-018).
- **Social intuition**: She knows when to speak and when to hold her tongue, navigating the complex social dynamics of the Chaldicotes set with practised ease.

## Relationships

- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: The vicar is a regular guest at Chaldicotes, and Mrs. Smith knows him well enough to address him with the familiarity of an old acquaintance. She tells him frankly of the gentlemanly chaos of her establishment (005-130).
- **[[mrs-proudie|Mrs. Proudie]]**: The bishop's wife and the innkeeper's wife meet in a clash of social spheres. Mrs. Proudie's condescension toward Mrs. Smith — her tone of "modified horror" — reveals the class tensions that underlie even the most civil of exchanges (005-144).
- **[[miss-dunstable|Miss Dunstable]]**: The heiress treats Mrs. Smith with the easy familiarity she extends to everyone, teasing her about business and enjoying her company at Chaldicotes gatherings (006-017).

## Key Scenes

- **The arrival at Chaldicotes (Chapter 5)**: Mrs. Smith greets Mark Robarts and briefs him on the "difficulties" of managing a household full of hunting gentlemen, setting the tone for his stay at the great house (005-130).
- **The dinner party (Chapter 6)**: At table, Mrs. Smith holds her own against Miss Dunstable's teasing and Mrs. Proudie's condescension, her sharp retorts marking her as more than a servant (006-017) (006-018).
- **The lecture at Barchester (Chapter 13)**: Mrs. Smith sits among the company for Mr. Smith's lecture on the South Sea islands, her mirth with Miss Dunstable a quiet counterpoint to the ponderous proceedings on the dais (013-182).
- **The ball (Chapter 43)**: At her own ball, Mrs. Smith presides over the social rituals of the neighbourhood, her establishment transformed into a theatre of courtship and connection (043-079).

## Social & Historical Context

Mrs. Smith represents a social type familiar to Victorian readers: the innkeeper's wife who, through proximity to the gentry and a natural wit, occupies an ambiguous position between service and society. In a world of rigid hierarchies, the innkeeper — like the clergyman, the lawyer, the doctor — is one of the few who can move between classes. Mrs. Smith's establishment at Chaldicotes is the physical site of this boundary-crossing: it is where the bishop shakes hands with the vicar, where the political agent dines with the country gentleman, and where a sharp-tongued woman can tell a bishop's wife exactly what she thinks — so long as she does it with a smile (005-170) (005-144).
