## Section 89

[089-001]“I shall resign the stall,” he said to his wife that night. “I think

[089-002]I may say that I have made up my mind as to that.”

[089-003]“But, Mark, will not people say that it is odd?”

[089-004]“I cannot help it—they must say it. Fanny, I fear that we shall have

[089-005]to bear the saying of harder words than that.”

[089-006]“Nobody can ever say that you have done anything that is unjust or

[089-007]dishonourable. If there are such men as Mr. Sowerby—”

[089-008]“The blackness of his fault will not excuse mine.” And then again he

[089-009]sat silent, hiding his eyes, while his wife, sitting by him, held his

[089-010]hand.

[089-011]“Don’t make yourself wretched, Mark. Matters will all come right yet.

[089-012]It cannot be that the loss of a few hundred pounds should ruin you.”

[089-013]“It is not the money—it is not the money!”

[089-014]“But you have done nothing wrong, Mark.”

[089-015]“How am I to go into the church, and take my place before them all,

[089-016]when every one will know that bailiffs are in the house?” And then,

[089-017]dropping his head on to the table, he sobbed aloud.

[089-018]Mark Robarts’ mistake had been mainly this,—he had thought to touch

[089-019]pitch and not to be defiled. He, looking out from his pleasant

[089-020]parsonage into the pleasant upper ranks of the world around him, had

[089-021]seen that men and things in those quarters were very engaging. His

[089-022]own parsonage, with his sweet wife, were exceedingly dear to him, and

[089-023]Lady Lufton’s affectionate friendship had its value; but were not

[089-024]these things rather dull for one who had lived in the best sets at

[089-025]Harrow and Oxford;—unless, indeed, he could supplement them with

[089-026]some occasional bursts of more lively life? Cakes and ale were as

[089-027]pleasant to his palate as to the palates of those with whom he had

[089-028]formerly lived at college. He had the same eye to look at a horse,

[089-029]and the same heart to make him go across a country, as they. And

[089-030]then, too, he found that men liked him,—men and women also; men and

[089-031]women who were high in worldly standing. His ass’s ears were tickled,

[089-032]and he learned to fancy that he was intended by nature for the

[089-033]society of high people. It seemed as though he were following his

[089-034]appointed course in meeting men and women of the world at the houses

[089-035]of the fashionable and the rich. He was not the first clergyman that

[089-036]had so lived and had so prospered. Yes, clergymen had so lived, and

[089-037]had done their duties in their sphere of life altogether to the

[089-038]satisfaction of their countrymen—and of their sovereigns. Thus

[089-039]Mark Robarts had determined that he would touch pitch, and escape

[089-040]defilement if that were possible. With what result those who have

[089-041]read so far will have perceived.

[089-042]Late on the following afternoon who should drive up to the parsonage

[089-043]door but Mr. Forrest, the bank manager from Barchester—Mr. Forrest,

[089-044]to whom Sowerby had always pointed as the _Deus ex machinâ_ who, if

[089-045]duly invoked, could relieve them all from their present troubles, and

[089-046]dismiss the whole Tozer family—not howling into the wilderness, as

[089-047]one would have wished to do with that brood of Tozers, but so gorged

[089-048]with prey that from them no further annoyance need be dreaded? All

[089-049]this Mr. Forrest could do; nay, more, most willingly would do! Only

[089-050]let Mark Robarts put himself into the banker’s hand, and blandly sign

[089-051]what documents the banker might desire.

[089-052]“This is a very unpleasant affair,” said Mr. Forrest as soon as

[089-053]they were closeted together in Mark’s book-room. In answer to which

[089-054]observation the parson acknowledged that it was a very unpleasant

[089-055]affair.

[089-056]“Mr. Sowerby has managed to put you into the hands of about the worst

[089-057]set of rogues now existing, in their line of business, in London.”

[089-058]“So I supposed; Curling told me the same.” Curling was the Barchester

[089-059]attorney whose aid he had lately invoked.

[089-060]“Curling has threatened them that he will expose their whole trade;

[089-061]but one of them who was down here, a man named Tozer, replied, that

[089-062]you had much more to lose by exposure than he had. He went further

[089-063]and declared that he would defy any jury in England to refuse him his

[089-064]money. He swore that he discounted both bills in the regular way of

[089-065]business; and, though this is of course false, I fear that it will

[089-066]be impossible to prove it so. He well knows that you are a clergyman,

[089-067]and that, therefore, he has a stronger hold on you than on other

[089-068]men.”

[089-069]“The disgrace shall fall on Sowerby,” said Robarts, hardly actuated

[089-070]at the moment by any strong feeling of Christian forgiveness.

[089-071]“I fear, Mr. Robarts, that he is somewhat in the condition of the

[089-072]Tozers. He will not feel it as you will do.”

[089-073]“I must bear it, Mr. Forrest, as best I may.”

[089-074]“Will you allow me, Mr. Robarts, to give you my advice? Perhaps

[089-075]I ought to apologize for intruding it upon you; but as the bills

[089-076]have been presented and dishonoured across my counter, I have, of

[089-077]necessity, become acquainted with the circumstances.”

[089-078]“I am sure I am very much obliged to you,” said Mark.

[089-079]“You must pay this money, or, at any rate, the most considerable

[089-080]portion of it;—the whole of it, indeed, with such deduction as a

[089-081]lawyer may be able to induce these hawks to make on the sight of the

[089-082]ready money. Perhaps £750 or £800 may see you clear of the whole

[089-083]affair.”

[089-084]“But I have not a quarter of that sum lying by me.”

[089-085]“No, I suppose not; but what I would recommend is this:

[089-086]that you should borrow the money from the bank, on your own

[089-087]responsibility,—with the joint security of some friend who may be

[089-088]willing to assist you with his name. Lord Lufton probably would do

[089-089]it.”

[089-090]“No, Mr. Forrest—”

[089-091]“Listen to me first, before you make up your mind. If you took this

[089-092]step, of course you would do so with the fixed intention of paying

[089-093]the money yourself,—without any further reliance on Sowerby or on

[089-094]any one else.”

[089-095]“I shall not rely on Mr. Sowerby again; you may be sure of that.”

[089-096]“What I mean is that you must teach yourself to recognize the debt as

[089-097]your own. If you can do that, with your income you can surely pay it,

[089-098]with interest, in two years. If Lord Lufton will assist you with

[089-099]his name I will so arrange the bills that the payments shall be made

[089-100]to fall equally over that period. In that way the world will know

[089-101]nothing about it, and in two years’ time you will once more be a free

[089-102]man. Many men, Mr. Robarts, have bought their experience much dearer

[089-103]than that, I can assure you.”

[089-104]“Mr. Forrest, it is quite out of the question.”

[089-105]“You mean that Lord Lufton will not give you his name.”

[089-106]“I certainly shall not ask him; but that is not all. In the first

[089-107]place my income will not be what you think it, for I shall probably

[089-108]give up the prebend at Barchester.”

[089-109]“Give up the prebend! give up six hundred a year!”

[089-110]“And, beyond this, I think I may say that nothing shall tempt me to

[089-111]put my name to another bill. I have learned a lesson which I hope I

[089-112]may never forget.”

[089-113]“Then what do you intend to do?”

[089-114]“Nothing!”

[089-115]“Then those men will sell every stick of furniture about the place.

[089-116]They know that your property here is enough to secure all that they

[089-117]claim.”

[089-118]“If they have the power, they must sell it.”

[089-119]“And all the world will know the facts.”

[089-120]“So it must be. Of the faults which a man commits he must bear the

[089-121]punishment. If it were only myself!”

[089-122]“That’s where it is, Mr. Robarts. Think what your wife will have to

[089-123]suffer in going through such misery as that! You had better take my

[089-124]advice. Lord Lufton, I am sure—”

[089-125]But the very name of Lord Lufton, his sister’s lover, again gave him

[089-126]courage. He thought, too, of the accusations which Lord Lufton had

[089-127]brought against him on that night when he had come to him in the

[089-128]coffee-room of the hotel, and he felt that it was impossible that he

[089-129]should apply to him for such aid. It would be better to tell all to

[089-130]Lady Lufton! That she would relieve him, let the cost to herself be

[089-131]what it might, he was very sure. Only this;—that in looking to her

[089-132]for assistance he would be forced to bite the dust in very deed.

[089-133]“Thank you, Mr. Forrest, but I have made up my mind. Do not

[089-134]think that I am the less obliged to you for your disinterested

[089-135]kindness,—for I know that it is disinterested; but this I think I

[089-136]may confidently say, that not even to avert so terrible a calamity

[089-137]will I again put my name to any bill. Even if you could take my own

[089-138]promise to pay without the addition of any second name, I would not

[089-139]do it.”

[089-140]There was nothing for Mr. Forrest to do under such circumstances but

[089-141]simply to drive back to Barchester. He had done the best for the

[089-142]young clergyman according to his lights, and perhaps, in a worldly

[089-143]view, his advice had not been bad. But Mark dreaded the very name of

[089-144]a bill. He was as a dog that had been terribly scorched, and nothing

[089-145]should again induce him to go near the fire.

[089-146]“Was not that the man from the bank?” said Fanny, coming into the

[089-147]room when the sound of the wheels had died away.

[089-148]“Yes; Mr. Forrest.”

[089-149]“Well, dearest?”

[089-150]“We must prepare ourselves for the worst.”

[089-151]“You will not sign any more papers, eh, Mark?”

[089-152]“No; I have just now positively refused to do so.”

[089-153]“Then I can bear anything. But, dearest, dearest Mark, will you not

[089-154]let me tell Lady Lufton?”

[089-155]Let them look at the matter in any way the punishment was very heavy.

[089-156]CHAPTER XLIII.

[089-157]IS SHE NOT INSIGNIFICANT?

[089-158]And now a month went by at Framley without any increase of comfort to

[089-159]our friends there, and also without any absolute development of the

[089-160]ruin which had been daily expected at the parsonage. Sundry letters

[089-161]had reached Mr. Robarts from various personages acting in the Tozer

[089-162]interest, all of which he referred to Mr. Curling, of Barchester.

[089-163]Some of these letters contained prayers for the money, pointing out

[089-164]how an innocent widow lady had been induced to invest her all on

[089-165]the faith of Mr. Robarts’ name, and was now starving in a garret,

[089-166]with her three children, because Mr. Robarts would not make good

[089-167]his own undertakings. But the majority of them were filled with

[089-168]threats;—only two days longer would be allowed and then the

[089-169]sheriff’s officers would be enjoined to do their work; then one day

[089-170]of grace would be added, at the expiration of which the dogs of war

[089-171]would be unloosed. These, as fast as they came, were sent to Mr.

[089-172]Curling, who took no notice of them individually, but continued his

[089-173]endeavour to prevent the evil day. The second bill Mr. Robarts would

[089-174]take up—such was Mr. Curling’s proposition; and would pay by two

[089-175]instalments of £250 each, the first in two months, and the second

[089-176]in four. If this were acceptable to the Tozer interest—well; if it

[089-177]were not, the sheriff’s officers must do their worst and the Tozer

[089-178]interest must look for what it could get. The Tozer interest would

[089-179]not declare itself satisfied with these terms, and so the matter went

[089-180]on. During which the roses faded from day to day on the cheeks of

[089-181]Mrs. Robarts, as under such circumstances may easily be conceived.

[089-182]In the meantime Lucy still remained at Hogglestock and had there

[089-183]become absolute mistress of the house. Poor Mrs. Crawley had been

[089-184]at death’s door; for some days she was delirious, and afterwards

[089-185]remained so weak as to be almost unconscious; but now the worst

[089-186]was over, and Mr. Crawley had been informed, that as far as human

[089-187]judgment might pronounce, his children would not become orphans nor

[089-188]would he become a widower. During these weeks Lucy had not once been

[089-189]home nor had she seen any of the Framley people. “Why should she

[089-190]incur the risk of conveying infection for so small an object?” as she

[089-191]herself argued, writing by letters, which were duly fumigated before

[089-192]they were opened at the parsonage. So she remained at Hogglestock,

[089-193]and the Crawley children, now admitted to all the honours of the

[089-194]nursery, were kept at Framley. They were kept at Framley, although it

[089-195]was expected from day to day that the beds on which they lay would be

[089-196]seized for the payment of Mr. Sowerby’s debts.
