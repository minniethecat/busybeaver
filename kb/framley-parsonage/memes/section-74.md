## Section 74

[074-001]“That depends entirely upon you.”

[074-002]“On me! does it?”

[074-003]“I do not know what your son may have told you, Lady Lufton. For

[074-004]myself, I do not care to have any secrets from you in this matter;

[074-005]and as he has spoken to you about it, I suppose that such is his

[074-006]wish also. Am I right in presuming that he has spoken to you on the

[074-007]subject?”

[074-008]“Yes, he has; and it is for that reason that I have taken the liberty

[074-009]of sending for you.”

[074-010]“And may I ask what he has told you? I mean, of course, as regards

[074-011]myself,” said Lucy.

[074-012]Lady Lufton, before she answered this question, began to reflect

[074-013]that the young lady was taking too much of the initiative in this

[074-014]conversation, and was, in fact, playing the game in her own fashion,

[074-015]which was not at all in accordance with those motives which had

[074-016]induced Lady Lufton to send for her.

[074-017]“He has told me that he made you an offer of marriage,” replied Lady

[074-018]Lufton; “a matter which, of course, is very serious to me, as his

[074-019]mother; and I have thought, therefore, that I had better see you,

[074-020]and appeal to your own good sense and judgment and high feeling. Of

[074-021]course you are aware—”

[074-022]Now was coming the lecture to be illustrated by King Cophetua and

[074-023]Griselda, as Lucy had suggested to Mrs. Robarts; but she succeeded in

[074-024]stopping it for awhile.

[074-025]“And did Lord Lufton tell you what was my answer?”

[074-026]“Not in words. But you yourself now say that you refused him; and I

[074-027]must express my admiration for your good—”

[074-028]“Wait half a moment, Lady Lufton. Your son did make me an offer. He

[074-029]made it to me in person, up at the parsonage, and I then refused

[074-030]him;—foolishly, as I now believe, for I dearly love him. But I did

[074-031]so from a mixture of feelings which I need not, perhaps, explain;

[074-032]that most prominent, no doubt, was a fear of your displeasure. And

[074-033]then he came again, not to me but to my brother, and urged his suit

[074-034]to him. Nothing can have been kinder to me, more noble, more loving,

[074-035]more generous, than his conduct. At first I thought, when he was

[074-036]speaking to myself, that he was led on thoughtlessly to say all that

[074-037]he did say. I did not trust his love, though I saw that he did trust

[074-038]it himself. But I could not but trust it when he came again—to my

[074-039]brother, and made his proposal to him. I don’t know whether you will

[074-040]understand me, Lady Lufton; but a girl placed as I am feels ten times

[074-041]more assurance in such a tender of affection as that, than in one

[074-042]made to herself, at the spur of the moment, perhaps. And then you

[074-043]must remember that I—I myself—I loved him from the first. I was

[074-044]foolish enough to think that I could know him and not love him.”

[074-045]“I saw all that going on,” said Lady Lufton, with a certain

[074-046]assumption of wisdom about her; “and took steps which I hoped would

[074-047]have put a stop to it in time.”

[074-048]“Everybody saw it. It was a matter of course,” said Lucy, destroying

[074-049]her ladyship’s wisdom at a blow. “Well; I did learn to love him, not

[074-050]meaning to do so; and I do love him with all my heart. It is no use

[074-051]my striving to think that I do not; and I could stand with him at the

[074-052]altar to-morrow and give him my hand, feeling that I was doing my

[074-053]duty by him, as a woman should do. And now he has told you of his

[074-054]love, and I believe in that as I do in my own—” And then for a

[074-055]moment she paused.

[074-056]“But, my dear Miss Robarts—” began Lady Lufton.

[074-057]Lucy, however, had now worked herself up into a condition of power,

[074-058]and would not allow her ladyship to interrupt her in her speech.

[074-059]“I beg your pardon, Lady Lufton; I shall have done directly, and then

[074-060]I will hear you. And so my brother came to me, not urging this suit,

[074-061]expressing no wish for such a marriage, but allowing me to judge

[074-062]for myself, and proposing that I should see your son again on the

[074-063]following morning. Had I done so, I could not but have accepted him.

[074-064]Think of it, Lady Lufton. How could I have done other than accept

[074-065]him, seeing that in my heart I had accepted his love already?”

[074-066]“Well?” said Lady Lufton, not wishing now to put in any speech of her

[074-067]own.

[074-068]“I did not see him—I refused to do so—because I was a coward. I

[074-069]could not endure to come into this house as your son’s wife, and be

[074-070]coldly looked on by your son’s mother. Much as I loved him, much as I

[074-071]do love him, dearly as I prize the generous offer which he came down

[074-072]here to repeat to me, I could not live with him to be made the object

[074-073]of your scorn. I sent him word, therefore, that I would have him when

[074-074]you would ask me, and not before.”

[074-075]And then, having thus pleaded her cause—and pleaded as she believed

[074-076]the cause of her lover also—she ceased from speaking, and prepared

[074-077]herself to listen to the story of King Cophetua.

[074-078]But Lady Lufton felt considerable difficulty in commencing her

[074-079]speech. In the first place she was by no means a hard-hearted or

[074-080]a selfish woman; and were it not that her own son was concerned,

[074-081]and all the glory which was reflected upon her from her son, her

[074-082]sympathies would have been given to Lucy Robarts. As it was, she did

[074-083]sympathize with her, and admire her, and to a certain extent like

[074-084]her. She began also to understand what it was that had brought

[074-085]about her son’s love, and to feel that but for certain unfortunate

[074-086]concomitant circumstances the girl before her might have made

[074-087]a fitting Lady Lufton. Lucy had grown bigger in her eyes while

[074-088]sitting there and talking, and had lost much of that missish want

[074-089]of importance—that lack of social weight which Lady Lufton in her

[074-090]own opinion had always imputed to her. A girl that could thus speak

[074-091]up and explain her own position now, would be able to speak up and

[074-092]explain her own, and perhaps some other positions at any future time.

[074-093]But not for all or any of these reasons did Lady Lufton think of

[074-094]giving way. The power of making or marring this marriage was placed

[074-095]in her hands, as was very fitting, and that power it behoved her

[074-096]to use, as best she might use it, to her son’s advantage. Much as

[074-097]she might admire Lucy, she could not sacrifice her son to that

[074-098]admiration. The unfortunate concomitant circumstances still remained,

[074-099]and were of sufficient force, as she thought, to make such a marriage

[074-100]inexpedient. Lucy was the sister of a gentleman, who by his peculiar

[074-101]position as parish clergyman of Framley was unfitted to be the

[074-102]brother-in-law of the owner of Framley. Nobody liked clergymen better

[074-103]than Lady Lufton, or was more willing to live with them on terms

[074-104]of affectionate intimacy, but she could not get over the feeling

[074-105]that the clergyman of her own parish,—or of her son’s,—was a part

[074-106]of her own establishment, of her own appanage,—or of his,—and

[074-107]that it could not be well that Lord Lufton should marry among his

[074-108]own—dependants. Lady Lufton would not have used the word, but she

[074-109]did think it. And then, too, Lucy’s education had been so deficient.

[074-110]She had had no one about her in early life accustomed to the ways

[074-111]of,—of what shall I say, without making Lady Lufton appear more

[074-112]worldly than she was? Lucy’s wants in this respect, not to be defined

[074-113]in words, had been exemplified by the very way in which she had just

[074-114]now stated her case. She had shown talent, good temper, and sound

[074-115]judgment; but there had been no quiet, no repose about her. The

[074-116]species of power in young ladies which Lady Lufton most admired was

[074-117]the _vis inertiæ_ belonging to beautiful and dignified reticence; of

[074-118]this poor Lucy had none. Then, too, she had no fortune, which, though

[074-119]a minor evil, was an evil; and she had no birth, in the high-life

[074-120]sense of the word, which was a greater evil. And then, though her

[074-121]eyes had sparkled when she confessed her love, Lady Lufton was not

[074-122]prepared to admit that she was possessed of positive beauty. Such

[074-123]were the unfortunate concomitant circumstances which still induced

[074-124]Lady Lufton to resolve that the match must be marred.

[074-125]But the performance of her part in this play was much more difficult

[074-126]than she had imagined, and she found herself obliged to sit silent

[074-127]for a minute or two, during which, however, Miss Robarts made no

[074-128]attempt at further speech.

[074-129]“I am greatly struck,” Lady Lufton said at last, “by the excellent

[074-130]sense you have displayed in the whole of this affair; and you must

[074-131]allow me to say, Miss Robarts, that I now regard you with very

[074-132]different feelings from those which I entertained when I left

[074-133]London.” Upon this Lucy bowed her head, slightly but very stiffly;

[074-134]acknowledging rather the former censure implied than the present

[074-135]eulogium expressed.

[074-136]“But my feelings,” continued Lady Lufton, “my strongest feelings in

[074-137]this matter, must be those of a mother. What might be my conduct if

[074-138]such a marriage did take place, I need not now consider. But I must

[074-139]confess that I should think such a marriage very—very ill-judged.

[074-140]A better hearted young man than Lord Lufton does not exist, nor one

[074-141]with better principles, or a deeper regard for his word; but he is

[074-142]exactly the man to be mistaken in any hurried outlook as to his

[074-143]future life. Were you and he to become man and wife, such a marriage

[074-144]would tend to the happiness neither of him nor of you.”

[074-145]It was clear that the whole lecture was now coming; and as Lucy

[074-146]had openly declared her own weakness, and thrown all the power of

[074-147]decision into the hands of Lady Lufton, she did not see why she

[074-148]should endure this.

[074-149]“We need not argue about that, Lady Lufton,” she said. “I have told

[074-150]you the only circumstances under which I would marry your son; and

[074-151]you, at any rate, are safe.”

[074-152]“No; I was not wishing to argue,” answered Lady Lufton, almost

[074-153]humbly; “but I was desirous of excusing myself to you, so that you

[074-154]should not think me cruel in withholding my consent. I wished to make

[074-155]you believe that I was doing the best for my son.”

[074-156]“I am sure that you think you are, and therefore no excuse is

[074-157]necessary.”

[074-158]“No; exactly; of course it is a matter of opinion, and I do think so.

[074-159]I cannot believe that this marriage would make either of you happy,

[074-160]and therefore I should be very wrong to express my consent.”

[074-161]“Then, Lady Lufton,” said Lucy, rising from her chair, “I suppose we

[074-162]have both now said what is necessary, and I will therefore wish you

[074-163]good-bye.”

[074-164]“Good-bye, Miss Robarts. I wish I could make you understand how very

[074-165]highly I regard your conduct in this matter. It has been above all

[074-166]praise, and so I shall not hesitate to say when speaking of it to

[074-167]your relatives.” This was disagreeable enough to Lucy, who cared

[074-168]but little for any praise which Lady Lufton might express to her

[074-169]relatives in this matter. “And pray,” continued Lady Lufton, “give

[074-170]my best love to Mrs. Robarts, and tell her that I shall hope to see

[074-171]her over here very soon, and Mr. Robarts also. I would name a day for

[074-172]you all to dine, but perhaps it will be better that I should have a

[074-173]little talk with Fanny first.”
