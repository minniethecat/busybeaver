## Section 19

[019-001]The German student when he first made his bargain with the devil felt

[019-002]an indescribable attraction to his new friend; and such was the case

[019-003]now with Robarts. He shook Sowerby’s hand very warmly, said that

[019-004]he hoped he should meet him soon somewhere, and professed himself

[019-005]specially anxious to hear how that affair with the lady came off.

[019-006]As he had made his bargain—as he had undertaken to pay nearly

[019-007]half-a-year’s income for his dear friend—ought he not to have as

[019-008]much value as possible for his money? If the dear friendship of

[019-009]this flash member of Parliament did not represent that value, what

[019-010]else did do so? But then he felt, or fancied that he felt, that Mr.

[019-011]Sowerby did not care for him so much this morning as he had done on

[019-012]the previous evening. “By-bye,” said Mr. Sowerby, but he spoke no

[019-013]word as to such future meetings, nor did he even promise to write.

[019-014]Mr. Sowerby probably had many things on his mind; and it might be

[019-015]that it behoved him, having finished one piece of business,

[019-016]immediately to look to another.

[019-017]The sum for which Robarts had made himself responsible—which he so

[019-018]much feared that he would be called upon to pay—was very nearly

[019-019]half-a-year’s income; and as yet he had not put by one shilling since

[019-020]he had been married. When he found himself settled in his parsonage,

[019-021]he found also that all the world regarded him as a rich man. He had

[019-022]taken the dictum of all the world as true, and had set himself to

[019-023]work to live comfortably. He had no absolute need of a curate; but he

[019-024]could afford the £70—as Lady Lufton had said rather injudiciously;

[019-025]and by keeping Jones in the parish he would be acting charitably to a

[019-026]brother clergyman, and would also place himself in a more independent

[019-027]position. Lady Lufton had wished to see her pet clergyman well-to-do

[019-028]and comfortable; but now, as matters had turned out, she much

[019-029]regretted this affair of the curate. Mr. Jones, she said to herself,

[019-030]more than once, must be made to depart from Framley.

[019-031]He had given his wife a pony-carriage, and for himself he had a

[019-032]saddle-horse, and a second horse for his gig. A man in his position,

[019-033]well-to-do as he was, required as much as that. He had a footman

[019-034]also, and a gardener, and a groom. The two latter were absolutely

[019-035]necessary, but about the former there had been a question. His wife

[019-036]had been decidedly hostile to the footman; but, in all such matters

[019-037]as that, to doubt is to be lost. When the footman had been discussed

[019-038]for a week it became quite clear to the master that he also was a

[019-039]necessary.

[019-040]As he drove home that morning he pronounced to himself the doom of

[019-041]that footman, and the doom also of that saddle-horse. They at any

[019-042]rate should go. And then he would spend no more money in trips

[019-043]to Scotland; and above all, he would keep out of the bedrooms of

[019-044]impoverished members of Parliament at the witching hour of midnight.

[019-045]Such resolves did he make to himself as he drove home; and bethought

[019-046]himself wearily how that £400 might be made to be forthcoming. As to

[019-047]any assistance in the matter from Sowerby,—of that he gave himself

[019-048]no promise.

[019-049]But he almost felt himself happy again as his wife came out into the

[019-050]porch to meet him, with a silk shawl over her head, and pretending to

[019-051]shiver as she watched him descending from his gig.

[019-052]“My dear old man,” she said, as she led him into the warm

[019-053]drawing-room with all his wrappings still about him, “you must be

[019-054]starved.” But Mark during the whole drive had been thinking too much

[019-055]of that transaction in Mr. Sowerby’s bedroom to remember that the air

[019-056]was cold. Now he had his arm round his own dear Fanny’s waist; but

[019-057]was he to tell her of that transaction? At any rate he would not do

[019-058]it now, while his two boys were in his arms, rubbing the moisture

[019-059]from his whiskers with their kisses. After all, what is there equal

[019-060]to that coming home?

[019-061]“And so Lufton is here. I say, Frank, gently, old boy,”—Frank was

[019-062]his eldest son—“you’ll have baby into the fender.”

[019-063]“Let me take baby; it’s impossible to hold the two of them, they

[019-064]are so strong,” said the proud mother. “Oh, yes, he came home early

[019-065]yesterday.”

[019-066]“Have you seen him?”

[019-067]“He was here yesterday, with her ladyship; and I lunched there

[019-068]to-day. The letter came, you know, in time to stop the Merediths.

[019-069]They don’t go till to-morrow, so you will meet them after all. Sir

[019-070]George is wild about it, but Lady Lufton would have her way. You

[019-071]never saw her in such a state as she is.”

[019-072]“Good spirits, eh?”

[019-073]“I should think so. All Lord Lufton’s horses are coming, and he’s to

[019-074]be here till March.”

[019-075]“Till March!”

[019-076]“So her ladyship whispered to me. She could not conceal her triumph

[019-077]at his coming. He’s going to give up Leicestershire this year

[019-078]altogether. I wonder what has brought it all about?” Mark knew very

[019-079]well what had brought it about; he had been made acquainted, as the

[019-080]reader has also, with the price at which Lady Lufton had purchased

[019-081]her son’s visit. But no one had told Mrs. Robarts that the mother had

[019-082]made her son a present of five thousand pounds.

[019-083]“She’s in a good humour about everything now,” continued Fanny; “so

[019-084]you need say nothing at all about Gatherum Castle.”

[019-085]“But she was very angry when she first heard it; was she not?”

[019-086]“Well, Mark, to tell the truth, she was; and we had quite a scene

[019-087]there up in her own room up-stairs,—Justinia and I. She had heard

[019-088]something else that she did not like at the same time; and then—but

[019-089]you know her way. She blazed up quite hot.”

[019-090]“And said all manner of horrid things about me.”

[019-091]“About the duke she did. You know she never did like the duke; and

[019-092]for the matter of that, neither do I. I tell you that fairly, Master

[019-093]Mark!”

[019-094]“The duke is not so bad as he’s painted.”

[019-095]“Ah, that’s what you say about another great person. However, he

[019-096]won’t come here to trouble us, I suppose. And then I left her, not in

[019-097]the best temper in the world; for I blazed up too, you must know.”

[019-098]“I am sure you did,” said Mark, pressing his arm round her waist.

[019-099]“And then we were going to have a dreadful war, I thought; and I came

[019-100]home and wrote such a doleful letter to you. But what should happen

[019-101]when I had just closed it, but in came her ladyship—all alone,

[019-102]and—. But I can’t tell you what she did or said, only she behaved

[019-103]beautifully; just like herself too; so full of love and truth and

[019-104]honesty. There’s nobody like her, Mark; and she’s better than all the

[019-105]dukes that ever wore—whatever dukes do wear.”

[019-106]“Horns and hoofs; that’s their usual apparel, according to you and

[019-107]Lady Lufton,” said he, remembering what Mr. Sowerby had said of

[019-108]himself.

[019-109]“You may say what you like about me, Mark, but you shan’t abuse

[019-110]Lady Lufton. And if horns and hoofs mean wickedness and dissipation,

[019-111]I believe it’s not far wrong. But get off your big coat and make

[019-112]yourself comfortable.” And that was all the scolding that Mark

[019-113]Robarts got from his wife on the occasion of his great iniquity.

[019-114]“I will certainly tell her about this bill transaction,” he said to

[019-115]himself; “but not to-day; not till after I have seen Lufton.”

[019-116]That evening they dined at Framley Court, and there they met the

[019-117]young lord; they found also Lady Lufton still in high good-humour.

[019-118]Lord Lufton himself was a fine, bright-looking young man; not so tall

[019-119]as Mark Robarts, and with perhaps less intelligence marked on his

[019-120]face; but his features were finer, and there was in his countenance a

[019-121]thorough appearance of good-humour and sweet temper. It was, indeed,

[019-122]a pleasant face to look upon, and dearly Lady Lufton loved to gaze at

[019-123]it.

[019-124]“Well, Mark, so you have been among the Philistines?” that was his

[019-125]lordship’s first remark. Robarts laughed as he took his friend’s

[019-126]hands, and bethought himself how truly that was the case; that he

[019-127]was, in very truth, already “himself in bonds under Philistian

[019-128]yoke.” Alas, alas, it is very hard to break asunder the bonds of the

[019-129]latter-day Philistines. When a Samson does now and then pull a temple

[019-130]down about their ears, is he not sure to be engulfed in the ruin with

[019-131]them? There is no horse-leech that sticks so fast as your latter-day

[019-132]Philistine.

[019-133]“So you have caught Sir George, after all,” said Lady Lufton; and

[019-134]that was nearly all she did say in allusion to his absence. There

[019-135]was afterwards some conversation about the lecture, and from her

[019-136]ladyship’s remarks, it certainly was apparent that she did not like

[019-137]the people among whom the vicar had been lately staying; but she said

[019-138]no word that was personal to him himself, or that could be taken

[019-139]as a reproach. The little episode of Mrs. Proudie’s address in the

[019-140]lecture-room had already reached Framley, and it was only to be

[019-141]expected that Lady Lufton should enjoy the joke. She would affect to

[019-142]believe that the body of the lecture had been given by the bishop’s

[019-143]wife; and afterwards, when Mark described her costume at that Sunday

[019-144]morning breakfast-table, Lady Lufton would assume that such had been

[019-145]the dress in which she had exercised her faculties in public.

[019-146]“I would have given a five-pound note to have heard it,” said Sir

[019-147]George.

[019-148]“So would not I,” said Lady Lufton. “When one hears of such things

[019-149]described so graphically as Mr. Robarts now tells it, one can hardly

[019-150]help laughing. But it would give me great pain to see the wife of one

[019-151]of our bishops place herself in such a situation. For he is a bishop

[019-152]after all.”

[019-153]“Well, upon my word, my lady, I agree with Meredith,” said Lord

[019-154]Lufton. “It must have been good fun. As it did happen, you know,—as

[019-155]the Church was doomed to the disgrace, I should like to have heard

[019-156]it.”

[019-157]“I know you would have been shocked, Ludovic.”

[019-158]“I should have got over that in time, mother. It would have been

[019-159]like a bull-fight I suppose—horrible to see no doubt, but extremely

[019-160]interesting. And Harold Smith, Mark; what did he do all the while?”

[019-161]“It didn’t take so very long, you know,” said Robarts.

[019-162]“And the poor bishop,” said Lady Meredith; “how did he look? I really

[019-163]do pity him.”

[019-164]“Well, he was asleep, I think.”

[019-165]“What, slept through it all?” said Sir George.

[019-166]“It awakened him; and then he jumped up and said something.”

[019-167]“What, out loud too?”

[019-168]“Only one word or so.”

[019-169]“What a disgraceful scene!” said Lady Lufton. “To those who remember

[019-170]the good old man who was in the diocese before him it is perfectly

[019-171]shocking. He confirmed you, Ludovic, and you ought to remember

[019-172]him. It was over at Barchester, and you went and lunched with him

[019-173]afterwards.”

[019-174]“I do remember; and especially this, that I never ate such tarts

[019-175]in my life, before or since. The old man particularly called my

[019-176]attention to them, and seemed remarkably pleased that I concurred in

[019-177]his sentiments. There are no such tarts as those going in the palace

[019-178]now, I’ll be bound.”

[019-179]“Mrs. Proudie will be very happy to do her best for you if you will

[019-180]go and try,” said Sir George.

[019-181]“I beg that he will do no such thing,” said Lady Lufton, and that was

[019-182]the only severe word she said about any of Mark’s visitings.

[019-183]As Sir George Meredith was there, Robarts could say nothing then to

[019-184]Lord Lufton about Mr. Sowerby and Mr. Sowerby’s money affairs; but he

[019-185]did make an appointment for a _tête-à-tête_ on the next morning.
