## Section 102

[102-001]She had not been long in town before she met Lady Dumbello. At this

[102-002]interview also she had to overcome some little inward emotion. On the

[102-003]few occasions on which she had met Griselda Grantly at Framley they

[102-004]had not much progressed in friendship, and Lucy had felt that she had

[102-005]been despised by the rich beauty. She also in her turn had disliked,

[102-006]if she had not despised, her rival. But how would it be now? Lady

[102-007]Dumbello could hardly despise her, and yet it did not seem possible

[102-008]that they should meet as friends. They did meet, and Lucy came

[102-009]forward with a pretty eagerness to give her hand to Lady Lufton’s

[102-010]late favourite. Lady Dumbello smiled slightly—the same old smile

[102-011]which had come across her face when they two had been first

[102-012]introduced in the Framley drawing-room; the same smile without the

[102-013]variation of a line,—took the offered hand, muttered a word or

[102-014]two, and then receded. It was exactly as she had done before. She

[102-015]had never despised Lucy Robarts. She had accorded to the parson’s

[102-016]sister the amount of cordiality with which she usually received her

[102-017]acquaintance; and now she could do no more for the peer’s wife. Lady

[102-018]Dumbello and Lady Lufton have known each other ever since, and have

[102-019]occasionally visited at each other’s houses, but the intimacy between

[102-020]them has never gone beyond this.

[102-021]The dowager came up to town for about a month, and while there was

[102-022]contented to fill a second place. She had no desire to be the great

[102-023]lady in London. But then came the trying period when they commenced

[102-024]their life together at Framley Court. The elder lady formally

[102-025]renounced her place at the top of the table,—formally persisted in

[102-026]renouncing it though Lucy with tears implored her to resume it. She

[102-027]said also, with equal formality—repeating her determination over and

[102-028]over again to Mrs. Robarts with great energy—that she would in no

[102-029]respect detract by interference of her own from the authority of the

[102-030]proper mistress of the house; but, nevertheless, it is well known to

[102-031]every one at Framley that old Lady Lufton still reigns paramount in

[102-032]the parish.

[102-033]“Yes, my dear; the big room looking into the little garden to the

[102-034]south was always the nursery; and if you ask my advice, it will still

[102-035]remain so. But, of course, any room you please—”

[102-036]And the big room looking into the little garden to the south is still

[102-037]the nursery at Framley Court.
