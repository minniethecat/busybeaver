## Section 37

[037-001]And then Mrs. Proudie passed on to Mrs. Grantly. The two ladies were

[037-002]quite friendly in London; though down in their own neighbourhood

[037-003]they waged a war so internecine in its nature. But nevertheless Mrs.

[037-004]Proudie’s manner might have showed to a very close observer that she

[037-005]knew the difference between a bishop and an archdeacon. “I am so

[037-006]delighted to see you,” said she. “No, don’t mind moving; I won’t sit

[037-007]down just at present. But why didn’t the archdeacon come?”

[037-008]“It was quite impossible; it was indeed,” said Mrs. Grantly. “The

[037-009]archdeacon never has a moment in London that he can call his own.”

[037-010]“You don’t stay up very long, I believe.”

[037-011]“A good deal longer than we either of us like, I can assure you.

[037-012]London life is a perfect nuisance to me.”

[037-013]“But people in a certain position must go through with it, you know,”

[037-014]said Mrs. Proudie. “The bishop, for instance, must attend the House.”

[037-015]“Must he?” asked Mrs. Grantly, as though she were not at all well

[037-016]informed with reference to this branch of a bishop’s business. “I am

[037-017]very glad that archdeacons are under no such liability.”

[037-018]“Oh, no; there’s nothing of that sort,” said Mrs. Proudie, very

[037-019]seriously. “But how uncommonly well Miss Grantly is looking! I do

[037-020]hear that she has quite been admired.”

[037-021]This phrase certainly was a little hard for the mother to bear. All

[037-022]the world had acknowledged, so Mrs. Grantly had taught herself to

[037-023]believe, that Griselda was undoubtedly the beauty of the season.

[037-024]Marquises and lords were already contending for her smiles, and

[037-025]paragraphs had been written in newspapers as to her profile. It was

[037-026]too hard to be told, after that, that her daughter had been “quite

[037-027]admired.” Such a phrase might suit a pretty little red-cheeked

[037-028]milkmaid of a girl.

[037-029]“She cannot, of course, come near your girls in that respect,” said

[037-030]Mrs. Grantly, very quietly. Now the Miss Proudies had not elicited

[037-031]from the fashionable world any very loud encomiums on their beauty.

[037-032]Their mother felt the taunt in its fullest force, but she would not

[037-033]essay to do battle on the present arena. She jotted down the item in

[037-034]her mind, and kept it over for Barchester and the chapter. Such debts

[037-035]as those she usually paid on some day, if the means of doing so were

[037-036]at all within her power.

[037-037]“But there is Miss Dunstable, I declare,” she said, seeing that that

[037-038]lady had entered the room; and away went Mrs. Proudie to welcome her

[037-039]distinguished guest.

[037-040]“And so this is a conversazione, is it?” said that lady, speaking, as

[037-041]usual, not in a suppressed voice. “Well, I declare, it’s very nice.

[037-042]It means conversation, don’t it, Mrs. Proudie?”

[037-043]“Ha, ha, ha! Miss Dunstable, there is nobody like you, I declare.”

[037-044]“Well, but don’t it? and tea and cake? and then, when we’re tired of

[037-045]talking, we go away,—isn’t that it?”

[037-046]“But you must not be tired for these three hours yet.”

[037-047]“Oh, I’m never tired of talking; all the world knows that. How do,

[037-048]bishop? A very nice sort of thing this conversazione, isn’t it now?”

[037-049]The bishop rubbed his hands together and smiled, and said that he

[037-050]thought it was rather nice.

[037-051]“Mrs. Proudie is so fortunate in all her little arrangements,” said

[037-052]Miss Dunstable.

[037-053]“Yes, yes,” said the bishop. “I think she is happy in these matters.

[037-054]I do flatter myself that she is so. Of course, Miss Dunstable, you

[037-055]are accustomed to things on a much grander scale.”

[037-056]“I! Lord bless you, no! Nobody hates grandeur so much as I do. Of

[037-057]course I must do as I am told. I must live in a big House, and have

[037-058]three footmen six feet high. I must have a coachman with a top-heavy

[037-059]wig, and horses so big that they frighten me. If I did not, I should

[037-060]be made out a lunatic and declared unable to manage my own affairs.

[037-061]But as for grandeur, I hate it. I certainly think that I shall have

[037-062]some of these conversaziones. I wonder whether Mrs. Proudie will come

[037-063]and put me up to a wrinkle or two.”

[037-064]The bishop again rubbed his hands, and said that he was sure she

[037-065]would. He never felt quite at his ease with Miss Dunstable, as he

[037-066]rarely could ascertain whether or no she was earnest in what she was

[037-067]saying. So he trotted off, muttering some excuse as he went, and

[037-068]Miss Dunstable chuckled with an inward chuckle at his too evident

[037-069]bewilderment. Miss Dunstable was by nature kind, generous, and

[037-070]open-hearted; but she was living now very much with people on whom

[037-071]kindness, generosity, and open-heartedness were thrown away. She

[037-072]was clever also, and could be sarcastic; and she found that those

[037-073]qualities told better in the world around her than generosity and an

[037-074]open heart. And so she went on from month to month, and year to year,

[037-075]not progressing in a good spirit as she might have done, but still

[037-076]carrying within her bosom a warm affection for those she could

[037-077]really love. And she knew that she was hardly living as she should

[037-078]live,—that the wealth which she affected to despise was eating into

[037-079]the soundness of her character, not by its splendour, but by the

[037-080]style of life which it had seemed to produce as a necessity. She knew

[037-081]that she was gradually becoming irreverent, scornful, and prone to

[037-082]ridicule; but yet, knowing this and hating it, she hardly knew how to

[037-083]break from it.

[037-084]She had seen so much of the blacker side of human nature that

[037-085]blackness no longer startled her as it should do. She had been the

[037-086]prize at which so many ruined spendthrifts had aimed; so many pirates

[037-087]had endeavoured to run her down while sailing in the open waters of

[037-088]life, that she had ceased to regard such attempts on her money-bags

[037-089]as unmanly or over-covetous. She was content to fight her own battle

[037-090]with her own weapons, feeling secure in her own strength of purpose

[037-091]and strength of wit.

[037-092]Some few friends she had whom she really loved,—among whom her inner

[037-093]self could come out and speak boldly what it had to say with its own

[037-094]true voice. And the woman who thus so spoke was very different from

[037-095]that Miss Dunstable whom Mrs. Proudie courted, and the Duke of Omnium

[037-096]fêted, and Mrs. Harold Smith claimed as her bosom friend. If only she

[037-097]could find among such one special companion on whom her heart might

[037-098]rest, who would help her to bear the heavy burdens of her world!

[037-099]But where was she to find such a friend?—she with her keen wit,

[037-100]her untold money, and loud laughing voice. Everything about her was

[037-101]calculated to attract those whom she could not value, and to scare

[037-102]from her the sort of friend to whom she would fain have linked her

[037-103]lot.

[037-104]And then she met Mrs. Harold Smith, who had taken Mrs. Proudie’s

[037-105]noble suite of rooms in her tour for the evening, and was devoting

[037-106]to them a period of twenty minutes. “And so I may congratulate you,”

[037-107]Miss Dunstable said eagerly to her friend.

[037-108]“No, in mercy’s name do no such thing, or you may too probably have

[037-109]to uncongratulate me again; and that will be so unpleasant.”

[037-110]“But they told me that Lord Brock had sent for him yesterday.” Now at

[037-111]this period Lord Brock was Prime Minister.

[037-112]“So he did, and Harold was with him backwards and forwards all the

[037-113]day. But he can’t shut his eyes and open his mouth, and see what God

[037-114]will send him, as a wise and prudent man should do. He is always for

[037-115]bargaining, and no Prime Minister likes that.”

[037-116]“I would not be in his shoes if, after all, he has to come home and

[037-117]say that the bargain is off.”

[037-118]“Ha, ha, ha! Well, I should not take it very quietly. But what can we

[037-119]poor women do, you know? When it is settled, my dear, I’ll send you a

[037-120]line at once.” And then Mrs. Harold Smith finished her course round

[037-121]the rooms, and regained her carriage within the twenty minutes.

[037-122]“Beautiful profile, has she not?” said Miss Dunstable, somewhat later

[037-123]in the evening, to Mrs. Proudie. Of course, the profile spoken of

[037-124]belonged to Miss Grantly.

[037-125]“Yes, it is beautiful, certainly,” said Mrs. Proudie. “The pity is

[037-126]that it means nothing.”

[037-127]“The gentlemen seem to think that it means a good deal.”

[037-128]“I am not sure of that. She has no conversation, you see; not a word.

[037-129]She has been sitting there with Lord Dumbello at her elbow for the

[037-130]last hour, and yet she has hardly opened her mouth three times.”

[037-131]“But, my dear Mrs. Proudie, who on earth could talk to Lord

[037-132]Dumbello?”

[037-133]Mrs. Proudie thought that her own daughter Olivia would undoubtedly

[037-134]be able to do so, if only she could get the opportunity. But, then,

[037-135]Olivia had so much conversation.

[037-136]And while the two ladies were yet looking at the youthful pair, Lord

[037-137]Dumbello did speak again. “I think I have had enough of this now,”

[037-138]said he, addressing himself to Griselda.

[037-139]“I suppose you have other engagements,” said she.

[037-140]“Oh, yes; and I believe I shall go to Lady Clantelbrocks.” And then

[037-141]he took his departure. No other word was spoken that evening between

[037-142]him and Miss Grantly beyond those given in this chronicle, and yet

[037-143]the world declared that he and that young lady had passed the evening

[037-144]in so close a flirtation as to make the matter more than ordinarily

[037-145]particular; and Mrs. Grantly, as she was driven home to her lodgings,

[037-146]began to have doubts in her mind whether it would be wise to

[037-147]discountenance so great an alliance as that which the head of the

[037-148]great Hartletop family now seemed so desirous to establish. The

[037-149]prudent mother had not yet spoken a word to her daughter on these

[037-150]subjects, but it might soon become necessary to do so. It was all

[037-151]very well for Lady Lufton to hurry up to town, but of what service

[037-152]would that be, if Lord Lufton were not to be found in Bruton Street?

[037-153]CHAPTER XVIII.

[037-154]THE NEW MINISTER’S PATRONAGE.

[037-155]At that time, just as Lady Lufton was about to leave Framley for

[037-156]London, Mark Robarts received a pressing letter, inviting him also to

[037-157]go up to the metropolis for a day or two—not for pleasure, but on

[037-158]business. The letter was from his indefatigable friend Sowerby.

[037-159]“My dear Robarts,” the letter ran:—

[037-160]I have just heard that poor little Burslem, the

[037-161]Barsetshire prebendary, is dead. We must all die some day,

[037-162]you know,—as you have told your parishioners from the

[037-163]Framley pulpit more than once, no doubt. The stall must

[037-164]be filled up, and why should not you have it as well as

[037-165]another? It is six hundred a year and a house. Little

[037-166]Burslem had nine, but the good old times are gone.

[037-167]Whether the house is letable or not under the present

[037-168]ecclesiastical régime, I do not know. It used to be so,

[037-169]for I remember Mrs. Wiggins, the tallow-chandler’s widow,

[037-170]living in old Stanhope’s house.

[037-171]Harold Smith has just joined the Government as Lord Petty

[037-172]Bag, and could, I think, at the present moment get this

[037-173]for asking. He cannot well refuse me, and, if you will

[037-174]say the word, I will speak to him. You had better come up

[037-175]yourself; but say the word “Yes,” or “No,” by the wires.

[037-176]If you say “Yes,” as of course you will, do not fail to

[037-177]come up. You will find me at the “Travellers,” or at the

[037-178]House. The stall will just suit you,—will give you no

[037-179]trouble, improve your position, and give some little

[037-180]assistance towards bed and board, and rack and manger.

[037-181]Yours ever faithfully,

[037-182]N. SOWERBY.

[037-183]Singularly enough, I hear that your brother is private

[037-184]secretary to the new Lord Petty Bag. I am told that his

[037-185]chief duty will consist in desiring the servants to call

[037-186]my sister’s carriage. I have only seen Harold once since

[037-187]he accepted office; but my Lady Petty Bag says that he has

[037-188]certainly grown an inch since that occurrence.
