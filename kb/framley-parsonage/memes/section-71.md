## Section 71

[071-001]But she swore to him that she would think nothing of it;—that she

[071-002]would never bear it in her mind against him,—that it could have no

[071-003]effect in lessening her trust in him. Was he not her husband? She was

[071-004]so glad she knew it, that she might comfort him. And she did comfort

[071-005]him, making the weight seem lighter and lighter on his shoulders

[071-006]as he talked of it. And such weights do thus become lighter. A

[071-007]burden that will crush a single pair of shoulders will, when equally

[071-008]divided—when shared by two, each of whom is willing to take the

[071-009]heavier part—become light as a feather. Is not that sharing of the

[071-010]mind’s burdens one of the chief purposes for which a man wants a

[071-011]wife? For there is no folly so great as keeping one’s sorrows hidden.

[071-012]And this wife cheerfully, gladly, thankfully took her share. To

[071-013]endure with her lord all her lord’s troubles was easy to her; it was

[071-014]the work to which she had pledged herself. But to have thought that

[071-015]her lord had troubles not communicated to her—that would have been

[071-016]to her the one thing not to be borne.

[071-017]And then they discussed their plans;—what mode of escape they might

[071-018]have out of this terrible money difficulty. Like a true woman, Mrs.

[071-019]Robarts proposed at once to abandon all superfluities. They would

[071-020]sell all their horses; they would not sell their cows, but would sell

[071-021]the butter that came from them; they would sell the pony-carriage,

[071-022]and get rid of the groom. That the footman must go was so much a

[071-023]matter of course, that it was hardly mentioned. But then, as to

[071-024]that house at Barchester, the dignified prebendal mansion in the

[071-025]close—might they not be allowed to leave it unoccupied for one year

[071-026]longer,—perhaps to let it? The world of course must know of their

[071-027]misfortune; but if that misfortune was faced bravely, the world

[071-028]would be less bitter in its condemnation. And then, above all things,

[071-029]everything must be told to Lady Lufton.

[071-030]“You may, at any rate, believe this, Fanny,” said he, “that for no

[071-031]consideration which can be offered to me will I ever put my name to

[071-032]another bill.”

[071-033]The kiss with which she thanked him for this was as warm and generous

[071-034]as though he had brought to her that day news of the brightest; and

[071-035]when he sat, as he did that evening, discussing it all, not only with

[071-036]his wife, but with Lucy, he wondered how it was that his troubles

[071-037]were now so light.

[071-038]Whether or no a man should have his own private pleasures, I will

[071-039]not now say; but it never can be worth his while to keep his sorrows

[071-040]private.

[071-041]CHAPTER XXXIV.

[071-042]LADY LUFTON IS TAKEN BY SURPRISE.

[071-043]Lord Lufton, as he returned to town, found some difficulty in

[071-044]resolving what step he would next take. Sometimes, for a minute or

[071-045]two, he was half inclined to think—or rather to say to himself—that

[071-046]Lucy was perhaps not worth the trouble which she threw in his way.

[071-047]He loved her very dearly, and would willingly make her his wife,

[071-048]he thought or said at such moments; but— Such moments, however,

[071-049]were only moments. A man in love seldom loves less because his love

[071-050]becomes difficult. And thus, when those moments were over, he would

[071-051]determine to tell his mother at once, and urge her to signify her

[071-052]consent to Miss Robarts. That she would not be quite pleased he knew;

[071-053]but if he were firm enough to show that he had a will of his own in

[071-054]this matter, she would probably not gainsay him. He would not ask

[071-055]this humbly, as a favour, but request her ladyship to go through the

[071-056]ceremony as though it were one of those motherly duties which she

[071-057]as a good mother could not hesitate to perform on behalf of her son.

[071-058]Such was the final resolve with which he reached his chambers in the

[071-059]Albany.

[071-060]On the next day he did not see his mother. It would be well, he

[071-061]thought, to have his interview with her immediately before he started

[071-062]for Norway, so that there might be no repetition of it; and it was on

[071-063]the day before he did start that he made his communication, having

[071-064]invited himself to breakfast in Brook Street on the occasion.

[071-065]“Mother,” he said, quite abruptly, throwing himself into one of the

[071-066]dining-room arm-chairs, “I have a thing to tell you.”

[071-067]His mother at once knew that the thing was important, and with her

[071-068]own peculiar motherly instinct imagined that the question to be

[071-069]discussed had reference to matrimony. Had her son desired to speak

[071-070]to her about money, his tone and look would have been different; as

[071-071]would also have been the case—in a different way—had he entertained

[071-072]any thought of a pilgrimage to Pekin, or a prolonged fishing

[071-073]excursion to the Hudson Bay territories.

[071-074]“A thing, Ludovic! well; I am quite at liberty.”

[071-075]“I want to know what you think of Lucy Robarts?”

[071-076]Lady Lufton became pale and frightened, and the blood ran cold to her

[071-077]heart. She had feared more than rejoiced in conceiving that her son

[071-078]was about to talk of love, but she had feared nothing so bad as this.

[071-079]“What do I think of Lucy Robarts?” she said, repeating her son’s

[071-080]words in a tone of evident dismay.

[071-081]“Yes, mother; you have said once or twice lately that you thought I

[071-082]ought to marry, and I am beginning to think so too. You selected one

[071-083]clergyman’s daughter for me, but that lady is going to do much better

[071-084]with herself—”

[071-085]“Indeed she is not,” said Lady Lufton sharply.

[071-086]“And therefore I rather think I shall select for myself another

[071-087]clergyman’s sister. You don’t dislike Miss Robarts, I hope?”

[071-088]“Oh, Ludovic!”

[071-089]It was all that Lady Lufton could say at the spur of the moment.

[071-090]“Is there any harm in her? Have you any objection to her? Is there

[071-091]anything about her that makes her unfit to be my wife?”

[071-092]For a moment or two Lady Lufton sat silent, collecting her thoughts.

[071-093]She thought that there was very great objection to Lucy Robarts,

[071-094]regarding her as the possible future Lady Lufton. She could hardly

[071-095]have stated all her reasons, but they were very cogent. Lucy Robarts

[071-096]had, in her eyes, neither beauty, nor style, nor manner, nor even the

[071-097]education which was desirable. Lady Lufton was not herself a worldly

[071-098]woman. She was almost as far removed from being so as a woman could

[071-099]be in her position. But, nevertheless, there were certain worldly

[071-100]attributes which she regarded as essential to the character of any

[071-101]young lady who might be considered fit to take the place which she

[071-102]herself had so long filled. It was her desire in looking for a wife

[071-103]for her son to combine these with certain moral excellences which

[071-104]she regarded as equally essential. Lucy Robarts might have the moral

[071-105]excellences, or she might not; but as to the other attributes Lady

[071-106]Lufton regarded her as altogether deficient. She could never look

[071-107]like a Lady Lufton, or carry herself in the county as a Lady Lufton

[071-108]should do. She had not that quiet personal demeanour—that dignity of

[071-109]repose—which Lady Lufton loved to look upon in a young married woman

[071-110]of rank. Lucy, she would have said, could be nobody in a room except

[071-111]by dint of her tongue, whereas Griselda Grantly would have held her

[071-112]peace for a whole evening, and yet would have impressed everybody

[071-113]by the majesty of her presence. Then again Lucy had no money—and,

[071-114]again, Lucy was only the sister of her own parish clergyman. People

[071-115]are rarely prophets in their own country, and Lucy was no prophet

[071-116]at Framley; she was none, at least, in the eyes of Lady Lufton.

[071-117]Once before, as may be remembered, she had had fears on this

[071-118]subject—fears, not so much for her son, whom she could hardly bring

[071-119]herself to suspect of such a folly, but for Lucy, who might be

[071-120]foolish enough to fancy that the lord was in love with her. Alas!

[071-121]alas! her son’s question fell upon the poor woman at the present

[071-122]moment with the weight of a terrible blow.

[071-123]“Is there anything about her which makes her unfit to be my wife?”

[071-124]Those were her son’s last words.

[071-125]“Dearest Ludovic, dearest Ludovic!” and she got up and came over to

[071-126]him, “I do think so; I do, indeed.”

[071-127]“Think what?” said he, in a tone that was almost angry.

[071-128]“I do think that she is unfit to be your wife. She is not of that

[071-129]class from which I would wish to see you choose.”

[071-130]“She is of the same class as Griselda Grantly.”

[071-131]“No, dearest. I think you are in error there. The Grantlys have moved

[071-132]in a different sphere of life. I think you must feel that they are—”

[071-133]“Upon my word, mother, I don’t. One man is Rector of Plumstead, and

[071-134]the other is Vicar of Framley. But it is no good arguing that. I want

[071-135]you to take to Lucy Robarts. I have come to you on purpose to ask it

[071-136]of you as a favour.”

[071-137]“Do you mean as your wife, Ludovic?”

[071-138]“Yes; as my wife.”

[071-139]“Am I to understand that you are—are engaged to her?”

[071-140]“Well, I cannot say that I am—not actually engaged to her. But you

[071-141]may take this for granted, that, as far as it lies in my power, I

[071-142]intend to become so. My mind is made up, and I certainly shall not

[071-143]alter it.”

[071-144]“And the young lady knows all this?”

[071-145]“Certainly.”

[071-146]“Horrid, sly, detestable, underhand girl,” Lady Lufton said to

[071-147]herself, not being by any means brave enough to speak out such

[071-148]language before her son. What hope could there be if Lord Lufton had

[071-149]already committed himself by a positive offer? “And her brother, and

[071-150]Mrs. Robarts; are they aware of it?”

[071-151]“Yes; both of them.”

[071-152]“And both approve of it?”

[071-153]“Well, I cannot say that. I have not seen Mrs. Robarts, and do not

[071-154]know what may be her opinion. To speak my mind honestly about Mark,

[071-155]I do not think he does cordially approve. He is afraid of you, and

[071-156]would be desirous of knowing what you think.”

[071-157]“I am glad, at any rate, to hear that,” said Lady Lufton, gravely.

[071-158]“Had he done anything to encourage this, it would have been very

[071-159]base.” And then there was another short period of silence.

[071-160]Lord Lufton had determined not to explain to his mother the whole

[071-161]state of the case. He would not tell her that everything depended

[071-162]on her word—that Lucy was ready to marry him only on condition

[071-163]that she, Lady Lufton, would desire her to do so. He would not

[071-164]let her know that everything depended on her—according to Lucy’s

[071-165]present verdict. He had a strong disinclination to ask his mother’s

[071-166]permission to get married; and he would have to ask it were he to

[071-167]tell her the whole truth. His object was to make her think well of

[071-168]Lucy, and to induce her to be kind, and generous, and affectionate

[071-169]down at Framley. Then things would all turn out comfortably when he

[071-170]again visited that place, as he intended to do on his return from

[071-171]Norway. So much he thought it possible he might effect, relying on

[071-172]his mother’s probable calculation that it would be useless for her

[071-173]to oppose a measure which she had no power of stopping by authority.

[071-174]But were he to tell her that she was to be the final judge, that

[071-175]everything was to depend on her will, then, so thought Lord Lufton,

[071-176]that permission would in all probability be refused.

[071-177]“Well, mother, what answer do you intend to give me?” he said. “My

[071-178]mind is positively made up. I should not have come to you had not

[071-179]that been the case. You will now be going down home, and I would wish

[071-180]you to treat Lucy as you yourself would wish to treat any girl to

[071-181]whom you knew that I was engaged.”
