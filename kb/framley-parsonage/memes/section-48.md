## Section 48

[048-001]“We must put a stop to that sort of thing, otherwise we shall never

[048-002]do any good.”

[048-003]“I don’t mean to deny that Brock was wrong with reference to Lord

[048-004]Brittleback. I think that he was wrong, and I said so all through.

[048-005]But, heavens on earth—!” and instead of completing his speech Harold

[048-006]Smith turned away his head, and struck his hands together in token of

[048-007]his astonishment at the fatuity of the age. What he probably meant to

[048-008]express was this: that if such a good deed as that late appointment

[048-009]made at the Petty Bag Office were not held sufficient to atone for

[048-010]that other evil deed to which he had alluded, there would be an end

[048-011]of all justice in sublunary matters. Was no offence to be forgiven,

[048-012]even when so great virtue had been displayed?

[048-013]“I attribute it all to Supplehouse,” said Green Walker, trying to

[048-014]console his friend.

[048-015]“Yes,” said Harold Smith, now verging on the bounds of parliamentary

[048-016]eloquence, although he still spoke with bated breath, and to one

[048-017]solitary hearer. “Yes; we are becoming the slaves of a mercenary and

[048-018]irresponsible press—of one single newspaper. There is a man endowed

[048-019]with no great talent, enjoying no public confidence, untrusted as a

[048-020]politician, and unheard of even as a writer by the world at large,

[048-021]and yet, because he is on the staff of the _Jupiter_, he is able to

[048-022]overturn the government and throw the whole country into dismay. It

[048-023]is astonishing to me that a man like Lord Brock should allow himself

[048-024]to be so timid.” And nevertheless it was not yet a month since Harold

[048-025]Smith had been counselling with Supplehouse how a series of strong

[048-026]articles in the _Jupiter_, together with the expected support of the

[048-027]Manchester men, might probably be effective in hurling the minister

[048-028]from his seat. But at that time the minister had not revigorated

[048-029]himself with young blood. “How the Queen’s government is to be

[048-030]carried on, that is the question now,” Harold Smith repeated. A

[048-031]difficulty which had not caused him much dismay at that period, about

[048-032]a month since, to which we have alluded.

[048-033]At this moment Sowerby and Supplehouse together joined them, having

[048-034]come out of the House, in which some unimportant business had been

[048-035]completed after the minister’s notice of adjournment.

[048-036]“Well, Harold,” said Sowerby, “what do you say to your governor’s

[048-037]statement?”

[048-038]“I have nothing to say to it,” said Harold Smith, looking up very

[048-039]solemnly from under the penthouse of his hat, and, perhaps, rather

[048-040]savagely. Sowerby had supported the government at the late crisis;

[048-041]but why was he now seen herding with such a one as Supplehouse?

[048-042]“He did it pretty well, I think,” said Sowerby.

[048-043]“Very well, indeed,” said Supplehouse; “as he always does those sort

[048-044]of things. No man makes so good an explanation of circumstances, or

[048-045]comes out with so telling a personal statement. He ought to keep

[048-046]himself in reserve for those sort of things.”

[048-047]“And who in the meantime is to carry on the Queen’s government?” said

[048-048]Harold Smith, looking very stern.

[048-049]“That should be left to men of lesser mark,” said he of the

[048-050]_Jupiter_. “The points as to which one really listens to a minister,

[048-051]the subjects about which men really care, are always personal. How

[048-052]many of us are truly interested as to the best mode of governing

[048-053]India? But in a question touching the character of a prime minister

[048-054]we all muster together like bees round a sounding cymbal.”

[048-055]“That arises from envy, malice, and all uncharitableness,” said

[048-056]Harold Smith.

[048-057]“Yes; and from picking and stealing, evil speaking, lying, and

[048-058]slandering,” said Mr. Sowerby.

[048-059]“We are so prone to desire and covet other men’s places,” said

[048-060]Supplehouse.

[048-061]“Some men are so,” said Sowerby; “but it is the evil speaking, lying,

[048-062]and slandering, which does the mischief. Is it not, Harold?”

[048-063]“And in the meantime how is the Queen’s government to be carried on?”

[048-064]said Mr. Green Walker.

[048-065]On the following morning it was known that Lord De Terrier was with

[048-066]the Queen at Buckingham Palace, and at about twelve a list of the new

[048-067]ministry was published, which must have been in the highest degree

[048-068]satisfactory to the whole brood of giants. Every son of Tellus was

[048-069]included in it, as were also very many of the daughters. But then,

[048-070]late in the afternoon, Lord Brock was again summoned to the palace,

[048-071]and it was thought in the West End among the clubs that the gods had

[048-072]again a chance. “If only,” said the _Purist_, an evening paper which

[048-073]was supposed to be very much in the interest of Mr. Harold Smith, “if

[048-074]only Lord Brock can have the wisdom to place the right men in the

[048-075]right places. It was only the other day that he introduced Mr. Smith

[048-076]into his government. That this was a step in the right direction

[048-077]every one has acknowledged, though unfortunately it was made too

[048-078]late to prevent the disturbance which has since occurred. It now

[048-079]appears probable that his lordship will again have an opportunity

[048-080]of selecting a list of statesmen with the view of carrying on the

[048-081]Queen’s government; and it is to be hoped that such men as Mr. Smith

[048-082]may be placed in situations in which their talents, industry, and

[048-083]acknowledged official aptitudes, may be of permanent service to the

[048-084]country.”

[048-085]Supplehouse, when he read this at the club with Mr. Sowerby at his

[048-086]elbow, declared that the style was too well marked to leave any doubt

[048-087]as to the author; but we ourselves are not inclined to think that Mr.

[048-088]Harold Smith wrote the article himself, although it may be probable

[048-089]that he saw it in type.

[048-090]But the _Jupiter_ the next morning settled the whole question, and

[048-091]made it known to the world that, in spite of all the sendings and

[048-092]re-sendings, Lord Brock and the gods were permanently out, and Lord

[048-093]De Terrier and the giants permanently in. That fractious giant who

[048-094]would only go to the Foreign Office had, in fact, gone to some sphere

[048-095]of much less important duty, and Sidonia, in spite of the whispered

[048-096]dislike of an illustrious personage, opened the campaign with all the

[048-097]full appanages of a giant of the highest standing. “We hope,” said

[048-098]the _Jupiter_, “that Lord Brock may not yet be too old to take a

[048-099]lesson. If so, the present decision of the House of Commons, and we

[048-100]may say of the country also, may teach him not to put his trust in

[048-101]such princes as Lord Brittleback, or such broken reeds as Mr. Harold

[048-102]Smith.” Now this parting blow we always thought to be exceedingly

[048-103]unkind, and altogether unnecessary, on the part of Mr. Supplehouse.

[048-104]“My dear,” said Mrs. Harold, when she first met Miss Dunstable

[048-105]after the catastrophe was known, “how am I possibly to endure this

[048-106]degradation?” And she put her deeply-laced handkerchief up to her

[048-107]eyes.

[048-108]“Christian resignation,” suggested Miss Dunstable.

[048-109]“Fiddlestick!” said Mrs. Harold Smith. “You millionnaires always talk

[048-110]of Christian resignation, because you never are called on to resign

[048-111]anything. If I had any Christian resignation, I shouldn’t have

[048-112]cared for such pomps and vanities. Think of it, my dear; a cabinet

[048-113]minister’s wife for only three weeks!”

[048-114]“How does poor Mr. Smith endure it?”

[048-115]“What? Harold? He only lives on the hope of vengeance. When he has

[048-116]put an end to Mr. Supplehouse, he will be content to die.”

[048-117]And then there were further explanations in both Houses of

[048-118]Parliament, which were altogether satisfactory. The high-bred,

[048-119]courteous giants assured the gods that they had piled Pelion on Ossa

[048-120]and thus climbed up into power, very much in opposition to their own

[048-121]good-wills; for they, the giants themselves, preferred the sweets of

[048-122]dignified retirement. But the voice of the people had been too strong

[048-123]for them; the effort had been made, not by themselves, but by others,

[048-124]who were determined that the giants should be at the head of affairs.

[048-125]Indeed, the spirit of the times was so clearly in favour of giants

[048-126]that there had been no alternative. So said Briareus to the Lords,

[048-127]and Orion to the Commons. And then the gods were absolutely happy in

[048-128]ceding their places; and so far were they from any uncelestial envy

[048-129]or malice which might not be divine, that they promised to give the

[048-130]giants all the assistance in their power in carrying on the work of

[048-131]government; upon which the giants declared how deeply indebted they

[048-132]would be for such valuable counsel and friendly assistance. All this

[048-133]was delightful in the extreme; but not the less did ordinary men seem

[048-134]to expect that the usual battle would go on in the old customary way.

[048-135]It is easy to love one’s enemy when one is making fine speeches; but

[048-136]so difficult to do so in the actual everyday work of life.

[048-137]But there was and always has been this peculiar good point about the

[048-138]giants, that they are never too proud to follow in the footsteps

[048-139]of the gods. If the gods, deliberating painfully together, have

[048-140]elaborated any skilful project, the giants are always willing to

[048-141]adopt it as their own, not treating the bantling as a foster-child,

[048-142]but praising it and pushing it so that men should regard it as the

[048-143]undoubted offspring of their own brains. Now just at this time there

[048-144]had been a plan much thought of for increasing the number of the

[048-145]bishops. Good active bishops were very desirable, and there was a

[048-146]strong feeling among certain excellent churchmen that there could

[048-147]hardly be too many of them. Lord Brock had his measure cut and dry.

[048-148]There should be a Bishop of Westminster to share the Herculean

[048-149]toils of the metropolitan prelate, and another up in the North to

[048-150]Christianize the mining interests and wash white the blackamoors of

[048-151]Newcastle: Bishop of Beverley he should be called. But, in opposition

[048-152]to this, the giants, it was known, had intended to put forth the

[048-153]whole measure of their brute force. More curates, they said, were

[048-154]wanting, and district incumbents; not more bishops rolling in

[048-155]carriages. That bishops should roll in carriages was very good; but

[048-156]of such blessings the English world for the present had enough. And

[048-157]therefore Lord Brock and the gods had had much fear as to their

[048-158]little project.

[048-159]But now, immediately on the accession of the giants, it was known

[048-160]that the bishop bill was to be gone on with immediately. Some small

[048-161]changes would be effected so that the bill should be gigantic rather

[048-162]than divine; but the result would be altogether the same. It must,

[048-163]however, be admitted that bishops appointed by ourselves may be very

[048-164]good things, whereas those appointed by our adversaries will be

[048-165]anything but good. And, no doubt, this feeling went a long way with

[048-166]the giants. Be that as it may, the new bishop bill was to be their

[048-167]first work of government, and it was to be brought forward and

[048-168]carried, and the new prelates selected and put into their chairs all

[048-169]at once,—before the grouse should begin to crow and put an end to

[048-170]the doings of gods as well as giants.

[048-171]Among other minor effects arising from this decision was the

[048-172]following, that Archdeacon and Mrs. Grantly returned to London, and

[048-173]again took the lodgings in which they had before been staying. On

[048-174]various occasions also during the first week of this second sojourn,

[048-175]Dr. Grantly might be seen entering the official chambers of the First

[048-176]Lord of the Treasury. Much counsel was necessary among high churchmen

[048-177]of great repute before any fixed resolution could wisely be made in

[048-178]such a matter as this; and few churchmen stood in higher repute than

[048-179]the Archdeacon of Barchester. And then it began to be rumoured in

[048-180]the world that the minister had disposed at any rate of the see of

[048-181]Westminster.

[048-182]This present time was a very nervous one for Mrs. Grantly. What

[048-183]might be the aspirations of the archdeacon himself, we will not

[048-184]stop to inquire. It may be that time and experience had taught

[048-185]him the futility of earthly honours, and made him content with

[048-186]the comfortable opulence of his Barsetshire rectory. But there

[048-187]is no theory of church discipline which makes it necessary that

[048-188]a clergyman’s wife should have an objection to a bishopric. The

[048-189]archdeacon probably was only anxious to give a disinterested aid to

[048-190]the minister, but Mrs. Grantly did long to sit in high places, and

[048-191]be at any rate equal to Mrs. Proudie. It was for her children, she

[048-192]said to herself, that she was thus anxious,—that they should have a

[048-193]good position before the world, and the means of making the best of

[048-194]themselves. “One is able to do nothing, you know, shut up there, down

[048-195]at Plumstead,” she had remarked to Lady Lufton on the occasion of

[048-196]her first visit to London, and yet the time was not long past when

[048-197]she had thought that rectory house at Plumstead to be by no means

[048-198]insufficient or contemptible.
