## Section 2

[002-001]In person he was manly, tall, and fair-haired, with a square

[002-002]forehead, denoting intelligence rather than thought, with clear white

[002-003]hands, filbert nails, and a power of dressing himself in such a

[002-004]manner that no one should ever observe of him that his clothes were

[002-005]either good or bad, shabby or smart.

[002-006]Such was Mark Robarts when at the age of twenty-five, or a little

[002-007]more, he married Fanny Monsell. The marriage was celebrated in his

[002-008]own church, for Miss Monsell had no home of her own, and had been

[002-009]staying for the last three months at Framley Court. She was given

[002-010]away by Sir George Meredith, and Lady Lufton herself saw that the

[002-011]wedding was what it should be, with almost as much care as she had

[002-012]bestowed on that of her own daughter. The deed of marrying, the

[002-013]absolute tying of the knot, was performed by the Very Reverend the

[002-014]Dean of Barchester, an esteemed friend of Lady Lufton’s. And Mrs.

[002-015]Arabin, the dean’s wife, was of the party, though the distance from

[002-016]Barchester to Framley is long, and the roads deep, and no railway

[002-017]lends its assistance. And Lord Lufton was there of course; and people

[002-018]protested that he would surely fall in love with one of the four

[002-019]beautiful bridesmaids, of whom Blanche Robarts, the vicar’s second

[002-020]sister, was by common acknowledgment by far the most beautiful.

[002-021]And there was there another and a younger sister of Mark’s—who did

[002-022]not officiate at the ceremony, though she was present—and of whom no

[002-023]prediction was made, seeing that she was then only sixteen, but of

[002-024]whom mention is made here, as it will come to pass that my readers

[002-025]will know her hereafter. Her name was Lucy Robarts.

[002-026]And then the vicar and his wife went off on their wedding tour, the

[002-027]old curate taking care of the Framley souls the while.

[002-028]And in due time they returned; and after a further interval, in due

[002-029]course, a child was born to them; and then another; and after that

[002-030]came the period at which we will begin our story. But before doing

[002-031]so, may I not assert that all men were right in saying all manner of

[002-032]good things to the Devonshire physician, and in praising his luck in

[002-033]having such a son?

[002-034]“You were up at the house to-day, I suppose?” said Mark to his wife,

[002-035]as he sat stretching himself in an easy chair in the drawing-room,

[002-036]before the fire, previously to his dressing for dinner. It was a

[002-037]November evening, and he had been out all day, and on such occasions

[002-038]the aptitude for delay in dressing is very powerful. A strong-minded

[002-039]man goes direct from the hall-door to his chamber without

[002-040]encountering the temptation of the drawing-room fire.

[002-041]“No; but Lady Lufton was down here.”

[002-042]“Full of arguments in favour of Sarah Thompson?”

[002-043]“Exactly so, Mark.”

[002-044]“And what did you say about Sarah Thompson?”

[002-045]“Very little as coming from myself; but I did hint that you thought,

[002-046]or that I thought that you thought, that one of the regular trained

[002-047]schoolmistresses would be better.”

[002-048]“But her ladyship did not agree?”

[002-049]“Well, I won’t exactly say that;—though I think that perhaps she did

[002-050]not.”

[002-051]“I am sure she did not. When she has a point to carry, she is very

[002-052]fond of carrying it.”

[002-053]“But then, Mark, her points are generally so good.”

[002-054]“But, you see, in this affair of the school she is thinking more of

[002-055]her _protégée_ than she does of the children.”

[002-056]“Tell her that, and I am sure she will give way.”

[002-057]And then again they were both silent. And the vicar having thoroughly

[002-058]warmed himself, as far as this might be done by facing the fire,

[002-059]turned round and began the operation _à tergo_.

[002-060]“Come, Mark, it is twenty minutes past six. Will you go and dress?”

[002-061]“I’ll tell you what, Fanny: she must have her way about Sarah

[002-062]Thompson. You can see her to-morrow and tell her so.”

[002-063]“I am sure, Mark, I would not give way, if I thought it wrong. Nor

[002-064]would she expect it.”

[002-065]“If I persist this time, I shall certainly have to yield the next;

[002-066]and then the next may probably be more important.”

[002-067]“But if it’s wrong, Mark?”

[002-068]“I didn’t say it was wrong. Besides, if it is wrong, wrong in some

[002-069]infinitesimal degree, one must put up with it. Sarah Thompson is very

[002-070]respectable; the only question is whether she can teach.”

[002-071]The young wife, though she did not say so, had some idea that her

[002-072]husband was in error. It is true that one must put up with wrong,

[002-073]with a great deal of wrong. But no one need put up with wrong that

[002-074]he can remedy. Why should he, the vicar, consent to receive an

[002-075]incompetent teacher for the parish children, when he was able to

[002-076]procure one that was competent? In such a case,—so thought Mrs.

[002-077]Robarts to herself,—she would have fought the matter out with Lady

[002-078]Lufton.

[002-079]On the next morning, however, she did as she was bid, and signified

[002-080]to the dowager that all objection to Sarah Thompson would be

[002-081]withdrawn.

[002-082]“Ah! I was sure he would agree with me,” said her ladyship, “when

[002-083]he learned what sort of person she is. I know I had only to

[002-084]explain;”—and then she plumed her feathers, and was very gracious;

[002-085]for, to tell the truth, Lady Lufton did not like to be opposed in

[002-086]things which concerned the parish nearly.

[002-087]“And, Fanny,” said Lady Lufton, in her kindest manner, “you are not

[002-088]going anywhere on Saturday, are you?”

[002-089]“No, I think not.”

[002-090]“Then you must come to us. Justinia is to be here, you know”—Lady

[002-091]Meredith was named Justinia—“and you and Mr. Robarts had better stay

[002-092]with us till Monday. He can have the little book-room all to himself

[002-093]on Sunday. The Merediths go on Monday; and Justinia won’t be happy if

[002-094]you are not with her.”

[002-095]It would be unjust to say that Lady Lufton had determined not to

[002-096]invite the Robartses if she were not allowed to have her own way

[002-097]about Sarah Thompson. But such would have been the result. As it

[002-098]was, however, she was all kindness; and when Mrs. Robarts made some

[002-099]little excuse, saying that she was afraid she must return home in the

[002-100]evening, because of the children, Lady Lufton declared that there was

[002-101]room enough at Framley Court for baby and nurse, and so settled the

[002-102]matter in her own way, with a couple of nods and three taps of her

[002-103]umbrella.

[002-104]This was on a Tuesday morning, and on the same evening, before

[002-105]dinner, the vicar again seated himself in the same chair before the

[002-106]drawing-room fire, as soon as he had seen his horse led into the

[002-107]stable.

[002-108]“Mark,” said his wife, “the Merediths are to be at Framley on

[002-109]Saturday and Sunday; and I have promised that we will go up and stay

[002-110]over till Monday.”

[002-111]“You don’t mean it! Goodness gracious, how provoking!”

[002-112]“Why? I thought you wouldn’t mind it. And Justinia would think it

[002-113]unkind if I were not there.”

[002-114]“You can go, my dear, and of course will go. But as for me, it is

[002-115]impossible.”

[002-116]“But why, love?”

[002-117]“Why? Just now, at the school-house, I answered a letter that was

[002-118]brought to me from Chaldicotes. Sowerby insists on my going over

[002-119]there for a week or so; and I have said that I would.”

[002-120]“Go to Chaldicotes for a week, Mark?”

[002-121]“I believe I have even consented to ten days.”

[002-122]“And be away two Sundays?”

[002-123]“No, Fanny, only one. Don’t be so censorious.”

[002-124]“Don’t call me censorious, Mark; you know I am not so. But I am so

[002-125]sorry. It is just what Lady Lufton won’t like. Besides, you were away

[002-126]in Scotland two Sundays last month.”

[002-127]“In September, Fanny. And that is being censorious.”

[002-128]“Oh, but, Mark, dear Mark; don’t say so. You know I don’t mean it.

[002-129]But Lady Lufton does not like those Chaldicotes people. You know Lord

[002-130]Lufton was with you the last time you were there; and how annoyed she

[002-131]was!”

[002-132]“Lord Lufton won’t be with me now, for he is still in Scotland. And

[002-133]the reason why I am going is this: Harold Smith and his wife will be

[002-134]there, and I am very anxious to know more of them. I have no doubt

[002-135]that Harold Smith will be in the government some day, and I cannot

[002-136]afford to neglect such a man’s acquaintance.”

[002-137]“But, Mark, what do you want of any government?”

[002-138]“Well, Fanny, of course I am bound to say that I want nothing;

[002-139]neither in one sense do I; but nevertheless, I shall go and meet the

[002-140]Harold Smiths.”

[002-141]“Could you not be back before Sunday?”

[002-142]“I have promised to preach at Chaldicotes. Harold Smith is going to

[002-143]lecture at Barchester, about the Australasian archipelago, and I am

[002-144]to preach a charity sermon on the same subject. They want to send out

[002-145]more missionaries.”

[002-146]“A charity sermon at Chaldicotes!”

[002-147]“And why not? The house will be quite full, you know; and I dare say

[002-148]the Arabins will be there.”

[002-149]“I think not; Mrs. Arabin may get on with Mrs. Harold Smith, though

[002-150]I doubt that; but I’m sure she’s not fond of Mrs. Smith’s brother.

[002-151]I don’t think she would stay at Chaldicotes.”

[002-152]“And the bishop will probably be there for a day or two.”

[002-153]“That is much more likely, Mark. If the pleasure of meeting Mrs.

[002-154]Proudie is taking you to Chaldicotes, I have not a word more to say.”

[002-155]“I am not a bit more fond of Mrs. Proudie than you are, Fanny,” said

[002-156]the vicar, with something like vexation in the tone of his voice,

[002-157]for he thought that his wife was hard upon him. “But it is generally

[002-158]thought that a parish clergyman does well to meet his bishop now and

[002-159]then. And as I was invited there, especially to preach while all

[002-160]these people are staying at the place, I could not well refuse.”

[002-161]And then he got up, and taking his candlestick, escaped to his

[002-162]dressing-room.

[002-163]“But what am I to say to Lady Lufton?” his wife said to him, in the

[002-164]course of the evening.

[002-165]“Just write her a note, and tell her that you find I had promised to

[002-166]preach at Chaldicotes next Sunday. You’ll go, of course?”

[002-167]“Yes: but I know she’ll be annoyed. You were away the last time she

[002-168]had people there.”

[002-169]“It can’t be helped. She must put it down against Sarah Thompson. She

[002-170]ought not to expect to win always.”

[002-171]“I should not have minded it, if she had lost, as you call it, about

[002-172]Sarah Thompson. That was a case in which you ought to have had your

[002-173]own way.”

[002-174]“And this other is a case in which I shall have it. It’s a pity that

[002-175]there should be such a difference; isn’t it?”

[002-176]Then the wife perceived that, vexed as she was, it would be better

[002-177]that she should say nothing further; and before she went to bed, she

[002-178]wrote the note to Lady Lufton, as her husband recommended.

[002-179]CHAPTER II.

[002-180]THE FRAMLEY SET, AND THE CHALDICOTES SET.

[002-181]It will be necessary that I should say a word or two of some of the

[002-182]people named in the few preceding pages, and also of the localities

[002-183]in which they lived.

[002-184]Of Lady Lufton herself enough, perhaps, has been written to introduce

[002-185]her to my readers. The Framley property belonged to her son; but

[002-186]as Lufton Park—an ancient ramshackle place in another county—had

[002-187]heretofore been the family residence of the Lufton family, Framley

[002-188]Court had been apportioned to her for her residence for life. Lord

[002-189]Lufton himself was still unmarried; and as he had no establishment

[002-190]at Lufton Park—which indeed had not been inhabited since his

[002-191]grandfather died—he lived with his mother when it suited him to

[002-192]live anywhere in that neighbourhood. The widow would fain have seen

[002-193]more of him than he allowed her to do. He had a shooting-lodge

[002-194]in Scotland, and apartments in London, and a string of horses in

[002-195]Leicestershire—much to the disgust of the county gentry around him,

[002-196]who held that their own hunting was as good as any that England could

[002-197]afford. His lordship, however, paid his subscription to the East

[002-198]Barsetshire pack, and then thought himself at liberty to follow his

[002-199]own pleasure as to his own amusement.
