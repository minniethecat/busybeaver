# Mr. Gresham

Mr. Gresham of Greshamsbury is the richest commoner in the county — a landed gentleman whose acres, wealth, and political influence place him at the apex of Barsetshire society, commanding the attention even of dukes (017-136).

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Gresham appears in *Framley Parsonage* primarily as a social benchmark — the man whose wealth and position define what it means to be a great county figure. He is the father of Frank Gresham, whose marriage to a great heiress "a greater heiress, men said, even than [[miss-dunstable|Miss Dunstable]]" has recently set all Barsetshire talking (016-066). The Gresham family's affairs are of interest to everyone: the Duke of Omnium himself is "especially attentive to young Mr. Frank Gresham" at Gatherum Castle, not because he cares about Frank personally but because "his acres were so extensive and his money so plentiful that he was worth a duke's notice" (017-136).

Mr. Gresham's political identity as "a strong conservative" places him in the natural opposition to the Whig ascendancy represented by the Duke of Omnium and Lord Brock — a reminder that county politics in Trollope's world is as much about land and interest as it is about principle (017-136).

## Character Traits

- **Landed wealth**: Mr. Gresham's defining characteristic is his position as a great landowner. His acres are his identity, and his influence flows from them as naturally as the rent-roll.
- **Conservative principle**: As a "strong conservative," Gresham represents the traditional landed interest — the class that Trollope, for all his political liberalism, regarded as the natural governors of England (017-136).
- **Paternal status**: Mr. Gresham is defined as much by his relationship to his son Frank as by his own achievements. The novel treats him as the established generation — the father whose son's brilliant marriage has enhanced the family name (016-066) (057-071).

## Relationships

- **Frank Gresham**: His son, the young man whose marriage to a great heiress has made the Gresham name the talk of Barsetshire. The father-son relationship is one of shared glory — the elder Gresham's acres and the younger Gresham's marriage together constitute the family's power (016-066) (057-071).
- **[[miss-dunstable|Miss Dunstable]]**: The great heiress who seizes upon Frank Gresham and his wife with characteristic enthusiasm at the Gatherum Castle gathering. She recognises in the Greshams a social force comparable to her own (017-136).
- **[[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]**: Sowerby is "almost more than civil" to Frank Gresham, recognizing that the young man's wealth makes him worth cultivating — and perhaps worth borrowing from (017-188).

## Key Scenes

- **The Gatherum Castle introduction (Chapters 16–17)**: Miss Dunstable's loud announcement of Frank Gresham's identity — "young Frank Gresham of Boxall Hill, son of old Mr. Gresham of Greshamsbury" — introduces the Gresham name into the novel's social landscape. The Barsetshire world, Mark Robarts learns, "was still full of" the Gresham marriage (016-066).
- **The Duke's attention (Chapter 17)**: At the Gatherum Castle gathering, the Duke of Omnium is "especially attentive" to Frank Gresham, a mark of favour that signals the young man's importance. The scene is a study in the calculus of social attention — dukes notice those whose acres justify notice (017-136).

## Social & Historical Context

The Greshams of Greshamsbury are Trollope's portrait of the old landed interest — the class that owned England, or had owned it until the twin forces of industrial wealth and political reform began to erode their primacy. Mr. Gresham's position as "the richest commoner in the county" marks him as belonging to that class of untitled landed gentlemen who, in the English counties, often wielded more real influence than the peerage. His son's marriage to an heiress — the union of old land and new money — is the characteristic Victorian solution to the problem of landed decline: marry wealth, consolidate acres, and maintain the position that land alone could no longer sustain.
