## Section 10

[010-001]In her eyes the sale of family property was horrible; the fact that a

[010-002]young man with some fifteen or twenty thousand a year should require

[010-003]subsidiary money was horrible; that her own son should have not

[010-004]written to her himself was horrible; and it was also horrible that

[010-005]her own pet, the clergyman whom she had brought there to be her

[010-006]son’s friend, should be mixed up in the matter,—should be cognizant

[010-007]of it while she was not cognizant,—should be employed in it as a

[010-008]go-between and agent in her son’s bad courses. It was all horrible,

[010-009]and Lady Lufton was sitting there with a black brow and an uneasy

[010-010]heart. As regarded our poor parson, we may say that in this matter

[010-011]he was blameless, except that he had hitherto lacked the courage to

[010-012]execute his friend’s commission.

[010-013]“What is it, Fanny?” said Lady Lufton as soon as the door was opened;

[010-014]“I should have been down in half-an-hour, if you wanted me,

[010-015]Justinia.”

[010-016]“Fanny has received a letter which makes her wish to speak to you at

[010-017]once,” said Lady Meredith. “What letter, Fanny?”

[010-018]Poor Fanny’s heart was in her mouth; she held it in her hand, but had

[010-019]not yet quite made up her mind whether she would show it bodily to

[010-020]Lady Lufton.

[010-021]“From Mr. Robarts,” she said.

[010-022]“Well; I suppose he is going to stay another week at Chaldicotes. For

[010-023]my part I should be as well pleased;” and Lady Lufton’s voice was

[010-024]not friendly, for she was thinking of that farm in Oxfordshire. The

[010-025]imprudence of the young is very sore to the prudence of their elders.

[010-026]No woman could be less covetous, less grasping than Lady Lufton; but

[010-027]the sale of a portion of the old family property was to her as the

[010-028]loss of her own heart’s blood.

[010-029]“Here is the letter, Lady Lufton; perhaps you had better read it;”

[010-030]and Fanny handed it to her, again keeping back the postscript. She

[010-031]had read and re-read the letter downstairs, but could not make out

[010-032]whether her husband had intended her to show it. From the line of the

[010-033]argument she thought that he must have done so. At any rate he said

[010-034]for himself more than she could say for him, and so, probably, it was

[010-035]best that her ladyship should see it.

[010-036]Lady Lufton took it, and read it, and her face grew blacker and

[010-037]blacker. Her mind was set against the writer before she began it, and

[010-038]every word in it tended to make her feel more estranged from him.

[010-039]“Oh, he is going to the palace, is he?—well; he must choose his own

[010-040]friends. Harold Smith one of his party! It’s a pity, my dear, he did

[010-041]not see Miss Proudie before he met you, he might have lived to be the

[010-042]bishop’s chaplain. Gatherum Castle! You don’t mean to tell me that he

[010-043]is going there? Then I tell you fairly, Fanny, that I have done with

[010-044]him.”

[010-045]“Oh, Lady Lufton, don’t say that,” said Mrs. Robarts, with tears in

[010-046]her eyes.

[010-047]“Mamma, mamma, don’t speak in that way,” said Lady Meredith.

[010-048]“But my dear, what am I to say? I must speak in that way. You would

[010-049]not wish me to speak falsehoods, would you? A man must choose for

[010-050]himself, but he can’t live with two different sets of people; at

[010-051]least, not if I belong to one and the Duke of Omnium to the other.

[010-052]The bishop going indeed! If there be anything that I hate it is

[010-053]hypocrisy.”

[010-054]“There is no hypocrisy in that, Lady Lufton.”

[010-055]“But I say there is, Fanny. Very strange, indeed! ‘Put off his

[010-056]defence!’ Why should a man need any defence to his wife if he acts in

[010-057]a straightforward way? His own language condemns him: ‘Wrong to stand

[010-058]out!’ Now, will either of you tell me that Mr. Robarts would really

[010-059]have thought it wrong to refuse that invitation? I say that that is

[010-060]hypocrisy. There is no other word for it.”

[010-061]By this time the poor wife, who had been in tears, was wiping them

[010-062]away and preparing for action. Lady Lufton’s extreme severity gave

[010-063]her courage. She knew that it behoved her to fight for her husband

[010-064]when he was thus attacked. Had Lady Lufton been moderate in her

[010-065]remarks Mrs. Robarts would not have had a word to say.

[010-066]“My husband may have been ill-judged,” she said, “but he is no

[010-067]hypocrite.”

[010-068]“Very well, my dear, I dare say you know better than I; but to me it

[010-069]looks extremely like hypocrisy: eh, Justinia?”

[010-070]“Oh, mamma, do be moderate.”

[010-071]“Moderate! That’s all very well. How is one to moderate one’s

[010-072]feelings when one has been betrayed?”

[010-073]“You do not mean that Mr. Robarts has betrayed you?” said the wife.

[010-074]“Oh, no; of course not.” And then she went on reading the letter:

[010-075]“‘Seem to have been standing in judgment upon the duke.’ Might he

[010-076]not use the same argument as to going into any house in the kingdom,

[010-077]however infamous? We must all stand in judgment one upon another in

[010-078]that sense. ‘Crawley!’ Yes; if he were a little more like Mr. Crawley

[010-079]it would be a good thing for me, and for the parish, and for you too,

[010-080]my dear. God forgive me for bringing him here; that’s all.”

[010-081]“Lady Lufton, I must say that you are very hard upon him—very hard.

[010-082]I did not expect it from such a friend.”

[010-083]“My dear, you ought to know me well enough to be sure that I shall

[010-084]speak my mind. ‘Written to Jones’—yes; it is easy enough to write to

[010-085]poor Jones. He had better write to Jones, and bid him do the whole

[010-086]duty. Then he can go and be the duke’s domestic chaplain.”

[010-087]“I believe my husband does as much of his own duty as any clergyman

[010-088]in the whole diocese,” said Mrs. Robarts, now again in tears.

[010-089]“And you are to take his work in the school; you and Mrs. Podgens.

[010-090]What with his curate and his wife and Mrs. Podgens, I don’t see why

[010-091]he should come back at all.”

[010-092]“Oh, mamma,” said Justinia, “pray, pray don’t be so harsh to her.”

[010-093]“Let me finish it, my dear;—oh, here I come. ‘Tell her ladyship my

[010-094]whereabouts.’ He little thought you’d show me this letter.”

[010-095]“Didn’t he?” said Mrs. Robarts, putting out her hand to get it back,

[010-096]but in vain. “I thought it was for the best; I did indeed.”

[010-097]“I had better finish it now, if you please. What is this? How does

[010-098]he dare send his ribald jokes to me in such a matter? No, I do not

[010-099]suppose I ever shall like Dr. Proudie; I have never expected it. A

[010-100]matter of conscience with him! Well—well, well. Had I not read it

[010-101]myself, I could not have believed it of him. I would not positively

[010-102]have believed it. ‘Coming from my parish he could not go to the Duke

[010-103]of Omnium!’ And it is what I would wish to have said. People fit for

[010-104]this parish should not be fit for the Duke of Omnium’s house. And I

[010-105]had trusted that he would have this feeling more strongly than any

[010-106]one else in it. I have been deceived—that’s all.”

[010-107]“He has done nothing to deceive you, Lady Lufton.”

[010-108]“I hope he will not have deceived you, my dear. ‘More money;’ yes,

[010-109]it is probable that he will want more money. There is your letter,

[010-110]Fanny. I am very sorry for it. I can say nothing more.” And she

[010-111]folded up the letter and gave it back to Mrs. Robarts.

[010-112]“I thought it right to show it you,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[010-113]“It did not much matter whether you did or no; of course I must have

[010-114]been told.”

[010-115]“He especially begs me to tell you.”

[010-116]“Why, yes; he could not very well have kept me in the dark in such

[010-117]a matter. He could not neglect his own work, and go and live with

[010-118]gamblers and adulterers at the Duke of Omnium’s without my knowing

[010-119]it.”

[010-120]And now Fanny Robarts’s cup was full, full to the overflowing. When

[010-121]she heard these words she forgot all about Lady Lufton, all about

[010-122]Lady Meredith, and remembered only her husband,—that he was her

[010-123]husband, and, in spite of his faults, a good and loving husband;—and

[010-124]that other fact also she remembered, that she was his wife.

[010-125]“Lady Lufton,” she said, “you forget yourself in speaking in that way

[010-126]of my husband.”

[010-127]“What!” said her ladyship; “you are to show me such a letter as that,

[010-128]and I am not to tell you what I think?”

[010-129]“Not if you think such hard things as that. Even you are not

[010-130]justified in speaking to me in that way, and I will not hear it.”

[010-131]“Heighty-tighty!” said her ladyship.

[010-132]“Whether or no he is right in going to the Duke of Omnium’s, I will

[010-133]not pretend to judge. He is the judge of his own actions, and neither

[010-134]you nor I.”

[010-135]“And when he leaves you with the butcher’s bill unpaid and no money

[010-136]to buy shoes for the children, who will be the judge then?”

[010-137]“Not you, Lady Lufton. If such bad days should ever come—and neither

[010-138]you nor I have a right to expect them—I will not come to you in my

[010-139]troubles; not after this.”

[010-140]“Very well, my dear. You may go to the Duke of Omnium if that suits

[010-141]you better.”

[010-142]“Fanny, come away,” said Lady Meredith. “Why should you try to anger

[010-143]my mother?”

[010-144]“I don’t want to anger her; but I won’t hear him abused in that way

[010-145]without speaking up for him. If I don’t defend him, who will? Lady

[010-146]Lufton has said terrible things about him; and they are not true.”

[010-147]“Oh, Fanny!” said Justinia.

[010-148]“Very well, very well!” said Lady Lufton. “This is the sort of return

[010-149]that one gets.”

[010-150]“I don’t know what you mean by return, Lady Lufton: but would you

[010-151]wish me to stand by quietly and hear such things said of my husband?

[010-152]He does not live with such people as you have named. He does not

[010-153]neglect his duties. If every clergyman were as much in his parish,

[010-154]it would be well for some of them. And in going to such a house as

[010-155]the Duke of Omnium’s it does make a difference that he goes there

[010-156]in company with the bishop. I can’t explain why, but I know that it

[010-157]does.”

[010-158]“Especially when the bishop is coupled up with the devil, as Mr.

[010-159]Robarts has done,” said Lady Lufton; “he can join the duke with them

[010-160]and then they’ll stand for the three Graces, won’t they, Justinia?”

[010-161]And Lady Lufton laughed a bitter little laugh at her own wit.

[010-162]“I suppose I may go now, Lady Lufton.”

[010-163]“Oh, yes, certainly, my dear.”

[010-164]“I am sorry if I have made you angry with me; but I will not allow

[010-165]any one to speak against Mr. Robarts without answering them. You have

[010-166]been very unjust to him; and even though I do anger you, I must say

[010-167]so.”

[010-168]“Come, Fanny; this is too bad,” said Lady Lufton. “You have been

[010-169]scolding me for the last half-hour because I would not congratulate

[010-170]you on this new friend that your husband has made, and now you are

[010-171]going to begin it all over again. That is more than I can stand. If

[010-172]you have nothing else particular to say, you might as well leave me.”

[010-173]And Lady Lufton’s face as she spoke was unbending, severe, and harsh.

[010-174]Mrs. Robarts had never before been so spoken to by her old friend;

[010-175]indeed she had never been so spoken to by any one, and she hardly

[010-176]knew how to bear herself.

[010-177]“Very well, Lady Lufton,” she said; “then I will go. Good-bye.”

[010-178]“Good-bye,” said Lady Lufton, and turning herself to her table she

[010-179]began to arrange her papers. Fanny had never before left Framley

[010-180]Court to go back to her own parsonage without a warm embrace. Now she

[010-181]was to do so without even having her hand taken. Had it come to this,

[010-182]that there was absolutely to be a quarrel between them,—a quarrel

[010-183]for ever?
