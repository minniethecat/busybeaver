## Section 98

[098-001]The letter said:—

[098-002]MY OWN OWN DARLING,

[098-003]How am I to congratulate you, and be eager enough in

[098-004]wishing you joy? I do wish you joy, and am so very happy.

[098-005]I write now chiefly to say that I shall be over with

[098-006]you about twelve to-morrow, and that I _must_ bring you

[098-007]away with me. If I did not some one else, by no means so

[098-008]trustworthy, would insist on doing it.

[098-009]But this, though it was thus stated to be the chief part of the

[098-010]letter, and though it might be so in matter, was by no means so in

[098-011]space. It was very long, for Mrs. Robarts had sat writing it till

[098-012]past midnight.

[098-013]I will not say anything about him [she went on to say,

[098-014]after two pages had been filled with his name], but I must

[098-015]tell you how beautifully she has behaved. You will own

[098-016]that she is a dear woman; will you not?

[098-017]Lucy had already owned it many times since the visit of yesterday,

[098-018]and had declared to herself, as she has continued to declare ever

[098-019]since, that she had never doubted it.

[098-020]She took us by surprise when we got into the drawing-room

[098-021]before dinner, and she told us first of all that she had

[098-022]been to see you at Hogglestock. Lord Lufton, of course,

[098-023]could not keep the secret, but brought it out instantly.

[098-024]I can’t tell you now how he told it all, but I am sure you

[098-025]will believe that he did it in the best possible manner.

[098-026]He took my hand and pressed it half a dozen times, and

[098-027]I thought he was going to do something else; but he did

[098-028]not, so you need not be jealous. And she was so nice to

[098-029]Mark, saying such things in praise of you, and paying all

[098-030]manner of compliments to your father. But Lord Lufton

[098-031]scolded her immensely for not bringing you. He said it was

[098-032]lackadaisical and nonsensical; but I could see how much he

[098-033]loved her for what she had done; and she could see it too,

[098-034]for I know her ways, and know that she was delighted with

[098-035]him. She could not keep her eyes off him all the evening,

[098-036]and certainly I never did see him look so well.

[098-037]And then while Lord Lufton and Mark were in the

[098-038]dining-room, where they remained a terribly long time, she

[098-039]would make me go through the house that she might show

[098-040]me your rooms, and explain how you were to be mistress

[098-041]there. She has got it all arranged to perfection, and I am

[098-042]sure she has been thinking about it for years. Her great

[098-043]fear at present is that you and he should go and live at

[098-044]Lufton. If you have any gratitude in you, either to her or

[098-045]me, you will not let him do this. I consoled her by saying

[098-046]that there are not two stones upon one another at Lufton

[098-047]as yet; and I believe such is the case. Besides, everybody

[098-048]says that it is the ugliest spot in the world. She went

[098-049]on to declare, with tears in her eyes, that if you were

[098-050]content to remain at Framley, she would never interfere in

[098-051]anything. I do think that she is the best woman that ever

[098-052]lived.

[098-053]So much as I have given of this letter formed but a small portion of

[098-054]it, but it comprises all that it is necessary that we should know.

[098-055]Exactly at twelve o’clock on that day Puck the pony appeared, with

[098-056]Mrs. Robarts and Grace Crawley behind him, Grace having been brought

[098-057]back as being capable of some service in the house. Nothing that was

[098-058]confidential, and very little that was loving, could be said at the

[098-059]moment, because Mr. Crawley was there, waiting to bid Miss Robarts

[098-060]adieu; and he had not as yet been informed of what was to be the

[098-061]future fate of his visitor. So they could only press each other’s

[098-062]hands and embrace, which to Lucy was almost a relief; for even to

[098-063]her sister-in-law she hardly as yet knew how to speak openly on this

[098-064]subject.

[098-065]“May God Almighty bless you, Miss Robarts,” said Mr. Crawley, as

[098-066]he stood in his dingy sitting-room ready to lead her out to the

[098-067]pony-carriage. “You have brought sunshine into this house, even in

[098-068]the time of sickness, when there was no sunshine; and He will bless

[098-069]you. You have been the Good Samaritan, binding up the wounds of the

[098-070]afflicted, pouring in oil and balm. To the mother of my children

[098-071]you have given life, and to me you have brought light, and comfort,

[098-072]and good words,—making my spirit glad within me as it had not been

[098-073]gladdened before. All this hath come of charity, which vaunteth not

[098-074]itself and is not puffed up. Faith and hope are great and beautiful,

[098-075]but charity exceedeth them all.” And having so spoken, instead of

[098-076]leading her out, he went away and hid himself.

[098-077]How Puck behaved himself as Fanny drove him back to Framley, and

[098-078]how those two ladies in the carriage behaved themselves—of that,

[098-079]perhaps, nothing further need be said.

[098-080]CHAPTER XLVII.

[098-081]NEMESIS.

[098-082]But in spite of all these joyful tidings it must, alas! be remembered

[098-083]that Pœna, that just but Rhadamanthine goddess, whom we moderns

[098-084]ordinarily call Punishment, or Nemesis when we wish to speak of her

[098-085]goddess-ship, very seldom fails to catch a wicked man though she

[098-086]have sometimes a lame foot of her own, and though the wicked man may

[098-087]possibly get a start of her. In this instance the wicked man had been

[098-088]our unfortunate friend Mark Robarts; wicked in that he had wittingly

[098-089]touched pitch, gone to Gatherum Castle, ridden fast mares across the

[098-090]country to Cobbold’s Ashes, and fallen very imprudently among the

[098-091]Tozers; and the instrument used by Nemesis was Mr. Tom Towers of the

[098-092]_Jupiter_, than whom, in these our days, there is no deadlier scourge

[098-093]in the hands of that goddess.

[098-094]In the first instance, however, I must mention, though I will not

[098-095]relate, a little conversation which took place between Lady Lufton

[098-096]and Mr. Robarts. That gentleman thought it right to say a few words

[098-097]more to her ladyship respecting those money transactions. He could

[098-098]not but feel, he said, that he had received that prebendal stall from

[098-099]the hands of Mr. Sowerby; and under such circumstances, considering

[098-100]all that had happened, he could not be easy in his mind as long

[098-101]as he held it. What he was about to do would, he was aware, delay

[098-102]considerably his final settlement with Lord Lufton; but Lufton, he

[098-103]hoped, would pardon that, and agree with him as to the propriety of

[098-104]what he was about to do.

[098-105]On the first blush of the thing Lady Lufton did not quite go along

[098-106]with him. Now that Lord Lufton was to marry the parson’s sister it

[098-107]might be well that the parson should be a dignitary of the Church;

[098-108]and it might be well, also, that one so nearly connected with her son

[098-109]should be comfortable in his money matters. There loomed also, in the

[098-110]future, some distant possibility of higher clerical honours for a

[098-111]peer’s brother-in-law; and the top rung of the ladder is always more

[098-112]easily attained when a man has already ascended a step or two. But,

[098-113]nevertheless, when the matter came to be fully explained to her, when

[098-114]she saw clearly the circumstances under which the stall had been

[098-115]conferred, she did agree that it had better be given up.

[098-116]And well for both of them it was—well for them all at Framley—that

[098-117]this conclusion had been reached before the scourge of Nemesis had

[098-118]fallen. Nemesis, of course, declared that her scourge had produced

[098-119]the resignation; but it was generally understood that this was a

[098-120]false boast, for all clerical men at Barchester knew that the stall

[098-121]had been restored to the chapter, or, in other words, into the hands

[098-122]of the Government, before Tom Towers had twirled the fatal lash above

[098-123]his head. But the manner of the twirling was as follows:—

[098-124]It is with difficulty enough [said the article in the

[098-125]_Jupiter_], that the Church of England maintains at the

[098-126]present moment that ascendancy among the religious sects

[098-127]of this country which it so loudly claims. And perhaps

[098-128]it is rather from an old-fashioned and time-honoured

[098-129]affection for its standing than from any intrinsic merits

[098-130]of its own that some such general acknowledgment of its

[098-131]ascendancy is still allowed to prevail. If, however, the

[098-132]patrons and clerical members of this Church are bold

[098-133]enough to disregard all general rules of decent behaviour,

[098-134]we think we may predict that this chivalrous feeling

[098-135]will be found to give way. From time to time we hear of

[098-136]instances of such imprudence, and are made to wonder at

[098-137]the folly of those who are supposed to hold the State

[098-138]Church in the greatest reverence.

[098-139]Among those positions of dignified ease to which fortunate

[098-140]clergymen may be promoted are the stalls of the canons

[098-141]or prebendaries in our cathedrals. Some of these, as is

[098-142]well known, carry little or no emolument with them, but

[098-143]some are rich in the good things of this world. Excellent

[098-144]family houses are attached to them, with we hardly know

[098-145]what domestic privileges, and clerical incomes, moreover,

[098-146]of an amount which, if divided, would make glad the hearts

[098-147]of many a hard-working clerical slave. Reform has been

[098-148]busy even among these stalls, attaching some amount of

[098-149]work to the pay, and paring off some superfluous wealth

[098-150]from such of them as were over full; but reform has been

[098-151]lenient with them, acknowledging that it was well to have

[098-152]some such places of comfortable and dignified retirement

[098-153]for those who have worn themselves out in the hard work of

[098-154]their profession. There has of late prevailed a taste for

[098-155]the appointment of young bishops, produced no doubt by a

[098-156]feeling that bishops should be men fitted to get through

[098-157]really hard work; but we have never heard that young

[098-158]prebendaries were considered desirable. A clergyman

[098-159]selected for such a position should, we have always

[098-160]thought, have earned an evening of ease by a long day of

[098-161]work, and should, above all things, be one whose life

[098-162]has been, and therefore in human probability will be,

[098-163]so decorous as to be honourable to the cathedral of his

[098-164]adoption.

[098-165]We were, however, the other day given to understand

[098-166]that one of these luxurious benefices, belonging to the

[098-167]cathedral of Barchester, had been bestowed on the Rev.

[098-168]Mark Robarts, the vicar of a neighbouring parish, on the

[098-169]understanding that he should hold the living and the

[098-170]stall together; and on making further inquiry we were

[098-171]surprised to learn that this fortunate gentleman is as yet

[098-172]considerably under thirty years of age. We were desirous,

[098-173]however, of believing that his learning, his piety, and

[098-174]his conduct, might be of a nature to add peculiar grace to

[098-175]his chapter, and therefore, though almost unwillingly, we

[098-176]were silent. But now it has come to our ears, and, indeed,

[098-177]to the ears of all the world, that this piety and conduct

[098-178]are sadly wanting; and judging of Mr. Robarts by his

[098-179]life and associates, we are inclined to doubt even the

[098-180]learning. He has at this moment, or at any rate had but

[098-181]a few days since, an execution in his parsonage house at

[098-182]Framley, on the suit of certain most disreputable bill

[098-183]discounters in London; and probably would have another

[098-184]execution in his other house in Barchester close, but for

[098-185]the fact that he has never thought it necessary to go into

[098-186]residence.

[098-187]Then followed some very stringent, and, no doubt, much-needed advice

[098-188]to those clerical members of the Church of England who are supposed

[098-189]to be mainly responsible for the conduct of their brethren; and the

[098-190]article ended as follows:—

[098-191]Many of these stalls are in the gift of the respective

[098-192]deans and chapters, and in such cases the dean and

[098-193]chapters are bound to see that proper persons are

[098-194]appointed; but in other instances the power of selection

[098-195]is vested in the Crown, and then an equal responsibility

[098-196]rests on the government of the day. Mr. Robarts, we learn,

[098-197]was appointed to the stall in Barchester by the late Prime

[098-198]Minister, and we really think that a grave censure rests

[098-199]on him for the manner in which his patronage has been

[098-200]exercised. It may be impossible that he should himself in

[098-201]all such cases satisfy himself by personal inquiry. But

[098-202]our government is altogether conducted on the footing of

[098-203]vicarial responsibility. _Quod facit per alium, facit per

[098-204]se_, is in a special manner true of our ministers, and

[098-205]any man who rises to high position among them must abide

[098-206]by the danger thereby incurred. In this peculiar case

[098-207]we are informed that the recommendation was made by a

[098-208]very recently admitted member of the Cabinet, to whose

[098-209]appointment we alluded at the time as a great mistake. The

[098-210]gentleman in question held no high individual office of

[098-211]his own; but evil such as this which has now been done at

[098-212]Barchester, is exactly the sort of mischief which follows

[098-213]the exaltation of unfit men to high positions, even though

[098-214]no great scope for executive failure may be placed within

[098-215]their reach.
