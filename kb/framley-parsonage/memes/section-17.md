## Section 17

[017-001]The then prime minister, angry as many men were with him, had not

[017-002]been altogether unsuccessful. He had brought the Russian war to a

[017-003]close, which, if not glorious, was at any rate much more so than

[017-004]Englishmen at one time had ventured to hope. And he had had wonderful

[017-005]luck in that Indian mutiny. It is true that many of those even who

[017-006]voted with him would declare that this was in no way attributable

[017-007]to him. Great men had risen in India and done all that. Even his

[017-008]minister there, the governor whom he had sent out, was not allowed

[017-009]in those days any credit for the success which was achieved under

[017-010]his orders. There was great reason to doubt the man at the helm. But

[017-011]nevertheless he had been lucky. There is no merit in a public man

[017-012]like success!

[017-013]But now, when the evil days were well nigh over, came the question

[017-014]whether he had not been too successful. When a man has nailed fortune

[017-015]to his chariot-wheels he is apt to travel about in rather a proud

[017-016]fashion. There are servants who think that their masters cannot do

[017-017]without them; and the public also may occasionally have some such

[017-018]servant. What if this too successful minister were one of them!

[017-019]And then a discreet, commonplace, zealous member of the Lower

[017-020]House does not like to be jeered at, when he does his duty by his

[017-021]constituents and asks a few questions. An all-successful minister who

[017-022]cannot keep his triumph to himself, but must needs drive about in a

[017-023]proud fashion, laughing at commonplace zealous members—laughing even

[017-024]occasionally at members who are by no means commonplace, which is

[017-025]outrageous!—may it not be as well to ostracize him for awhile?

[017-026]“Had we not better throw in our shells against him?” says Mr. Harold

[017-027]Smith.

[017-028]“Let us throw in our shells, by all means,” says Mr. Supplehouse,

[017-029]mindful as Juno of his despised charms. And when Mr. Supplehouse

[017-030]declares himself an enemy, men know how much it means. They know that

[017-031]that much-belaboured head of affairs must succumb to the terrible

[017-032]blows which are now in store for him. “Yes, we will throw in our

[017-033]shells.” And Mr. Supplehouse rises from his chair with gleaming

[017-034]eyes. “Has not Greece as noble sons as him? ay, and much nobler,

[017-035]traitor that he is. We must judge a man by his friends,” says Mr.

[017-036]Supplehouse; and he points away to the East, where our dear allies

[017-037]the French are supposed to live, and where our head of affairs is

[017-038]supposed to have too close an intimacy.

[017-039]They all understand this, even Mr. Green Walker. “I don’t know that

[017-040]he is any good to any of us at all, now,” says the talented member

[017-041]for the Crewe Junction. “He’s a great deal too uppish to suit my

[017-042]book; and I know a great many people that think so too. There’s my

[017-043]uncle—”

[017-044]“He’s the best fellow in the world,” said Mr. Fothergill, who felt,

[017-045]perhaps, that that coming revelation about Mr. Green Walker’s uncle

[017-046]might not be of use to them; “but the fact is one gets tired of the

[017-047]same man always. One does not like partridge every day. As for me, I

[017-048]have nothing to do with it myself; but I would certainly like to

[017-049]change the dish.”

[017-050]“If we’re merely to do as we are bid, and have no voice of our own,

[017-051]I don’t see what’s the good of going to the shop at all,” said Mr.

[017-052]Sowerby.

[017-053]“Not the least use,” said Mr. Supplehouse. “We are false to our

[017-054]constituents in submitting to such a dominion.”

[017-055]“Let’s have a change, then,” said Mr. Sowerby. “The matter’s pretty

[017-056]much in our own hands.”

[017-057]“Altogether,” said Mr. Green Walker. “That’s what my uncle always

[017-058]says.”

[017-059]“The Manchester men will only be too happy for the chance,” said

[017-060]Harold Smith.

[017-061]“And as for the high and dry gentlemen,” said Mr. Sowerby, “it’s not

[017-062]very likely that they will object to pick up the fruit when we shake

[017-063]the tree.”

[017-064]“As to picking up the fruit, that’s as may be,” said Mr. Supplehouse.

[017-065]Was he not the man to save the nation; and if so, why should he not

[017-066]pick up the fruit himself? Had not the greatest power in the country

[017-067]pointed him out as such a saviour? What though the country at the

[017-068]present moment needed no more saving, might there not, nevertheless,

[017-069]be a good time coming? Were there not rumours of other wars still

[017-070]prevalent—if indeed the actual war then going on was being brought

[017-071]to a close without his assistance by some other species of salvation?

[017-072]He thought of that country to which he had pointed, and of that

[017-073]friend of his enemies, and remembered that there might be still work

[017-074]for a mighty saviour. The public mind was now awake, and understood

[017-075]what it was about. When a man gets into his head an idea that the

[017-076]public voice calls for him, it is astonishing how great becomes his

[017-077]trust in the wisdom of the public. _Vox populi vox Dei._ “Has it not

[017-078]been so always?” he says to himself, as he gets up and as he goes to

[017-079]bed. And then Mr. Supplehouse felt that he was the master mind there

[017-080]at Gatherum Castle, and that those there were all puppets in his

[017-081]hand. It is such a pleasant thing to feel that one’s friends are

[017-082]puppets, and that the strings are in one’s own possession. But what

[017-083]if Mr. Supplehouse himself were a puppet?

[017-084]Some months afterwards, when the much-belaboured head of affairs

[017-085]was in very truth made to retire, when unkind shells were thrown in

[017-086]against him in great numbers, when he exclaimed, “_Et tu, Brute!_”

[017-087]till the words were stereotyped upon his lips, all men in all places

[017-088]talked much about the great Gatherum Castle confederation. The Duke

[017-089]of Omnium, the world said, had taken into his high consideration the

[017-090]state of affairs, and seeing with his eagle’s eye that the welfare

[017-091]of his countrymen at large required that some great step should be

[017-092]initiated, he had at once summoned to his mansion many members of the

[017-093]Lower House, and some also of the House of Lords,—mention was here

[017-094]especially made of the all-venerable and all-wise Lord Boanerges; and

[017-095]men went on to say that there, in deep conclave, he had made known

[017-096]to them his views. It was thus agreed that the head of affairs, Whig

[017-097]as he was, must fall. The country required it, and the duke did his

[017-098]duty. This was the beginning, the world said, of that celebrated

[017-099]confederation, by which the ministry was overturned, and—as the

[017-100]_Goody Twoshoes_ added,—the country saved. But the _Jupiter_ took

[017-101]all the credit to itself; and the _Jupiter_ was not far wrong. All

[017-102]the credit was due to the _Jupiter_—in that, as in everything else.

[017-103]In the meantime the Duke of Omnium entertained his guests in

[017-104]the quiet princely style, but did not condescend to have much

[017-105]conversation on politics either with Mr. Supplehouse or with Mr.

[017-106]Harold Smith. And as for Lord Boanerges, he spent the morning on

[017-107]which the above-described conversation took place in teaching Miss

[017-108]Dunstable to blow soap-bubbles on scientific principles.

[017-109]“Dear, dear!” said Miss Dunstable, as sparks of knowledge came

[017-110]flying in upon her mind. “I always thought that a soap-bubble was a

[017-111]soap-bubble, and I never asked the reason why. One doesn’t, you know,

[017-112]my lord.”

[017-113]“Pardon me, Miss Dunstable,” said the old lord, “one does; but nine

[017-114]hundred and ninety-nine do not.”

[017-115]“And the nine hundred and ninety-nine have the best of it,” said Miss

[017-116]Dunstable. “What pleasure can one have in a ghost after one has seen

[017-117]the phosphorus rubbed on?”

[017-118]“Quite true, my dear lady. ‘If ignorance be bliss, ’tis folly to be

[017-119]wise.’ It all lies in the ‘if.’”

[017-120]Then Miss Dunstable began to sing:—

[017-121]“‘What tho’ I trace each herb and flower

[017-122]That sips the morning dew—’

[017-123]—you know the rest, my lord.”

[017-124]Lord Boanerges did know almost everything, but he did not know that;

[017-125]and so Miss Dunstable went on:—

[017-126]“‘Did I not own Jehovah’s power

[017-127]How vain were all I knew.’”

[017-128]“Exactly, exactly, Miss Dunstable,” said his lordship; “but why not

[017-129]own the power and trace the flower as well? perhaps one might help

[017-130]the other.”

[017-131]Upon the whole I am afraid that Lord Boanerges got the best of it.

[017-132]But then that is his line. He has been getting the best of it all his

[017-133]life.

[017-134]It was observed by all that the duke was especially attentive to

[017-135]young Mr. Frank Gresham, the gentleman on whom and on whose wife Miss

[017-136]Dunstable had seized so vehemently. This Mr. Gresham was the richest

[017-137]commoner in the county, and it was rumoured that at the next election

[017-138]he would be one of the members for the East Riding. Now the duke had

[017-139]little or nothing to do with the East Riding, and it was well known

[017-140]that young Gresham would be brought forward as a strong conservative.

[017-141]But, nevertheless, his acres were so extensive and his money so

[017-142]plentiful that he was worth a duke’s notice. Mr. Sowerby also was

[017-143]almost more than civil to him, as was natural, seeing that this very

[017-144]young man by a mere scratch of his pen could turn a scrap of paper

[017-145]into a bank-note of almost fabulous value.

[017-146]“So you have the East Barsetshire hounds at Boxall Hill; have you

[017-147]not?” said the duke.

[017-148]“The hounds are there,” said Frank. “But I am not the master.”

[017-149]“Oh! I understood—”

[017-150]“My father has them. But he finds Boxall Hill more centrical than

[017-151]Greshamsbury. The dogs and horses have to go shorter distances.”

[017-152]“Boxall Hill is very centrical.”

[017-153]“Oh, exactly!”

[017-154]“And your young gorse coverts are doing well?”

[017-155]“Pretty well—gorse won’t thrive everywhere, I find. I wish it

[017-156]would.”

[017-157]“That’s just what I say to Fothergill; and then where there’s much

[017-158]woodland you can’t get the vermin to leave it.”

[017-159]“But we haven’t a tree at Boxall Hill,” said Mrs. Gresham.

[017-160]“Ah, yes; you’re new there, certainly; you’ve enough of it at

[017-161]Greshamsbury in all conscience. There’s a larger extent of wood there

[017-162]than we have; isn’t there, Fothergill?”

[017-163]Mr. Fothergill said that the Greshamsbury woods were very extensive,

[017-164]but that, perhaps, he thought—

[017-165]“Oh, ah! I know,” said the duke. “The Black Forest in its old days

[017-166]was nothing to Gatherum woods, according to Fothergill. And then

[017-167]again, nothing in East Barsetshire could be equal to anything in West

[017-168]Barsetshire. Isn’t that it; eh, Fothergill?”

[017-169]Mr. Fothergill professed that he had been brought up in that faith

[017-170]and intended to die in it.

[017-171]“Your exotics at Boxall Hill are very fine, magnificent!” said Mr.

[017-172]Sowerby.

[017-173]“I’d sooner have one full-grown oak standing in its pride alone,”

[017-174]said young Gresham, rather grandiloquently, “than all the exotics in

[017-175]the world.”

[017-176]“They’ll come in due time,” said the duke.

[017-177]“But the due time won’t be in my days. And so they’re going to cut

[017-178]down Chaldicotes forest, are they, Mr. Sowerby?”

[017-179]“Well, I can’t tell you that. They are going to disforest it. I have

[017-180]been ranger since I was twenty-two, and I don’t yet know whether that

[017-181]means cutting down.”

[017-182]“Not only cutting down, but rooting up,” said Mr. Fothergill.

[017-183]“It’s a murderous shame,” said Frank Gresham; “and I will say one

[017-184]thing, I don’t think any but a Whig government would do it.”

[017-185]“Ha, ha, ha!” laughed his grace. “At any rate I’m sure of this,” he

[017-186]said, “that if a conservative government did do so, the Whigs would

[017-187]be just as indignant as you are now.”

[017-188]“I’ll tell you what you ought to do, Mr. Gresham,” said Sowerby: “put

[017-189]in an offer for the whole of the West Barsetshire crown property;

[017-190]they will be very glad to sell it.”

[017-191]“And we should be delighted to welcome you on this side of the

[017-192]border,” said the duke.
