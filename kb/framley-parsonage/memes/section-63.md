## Section 63

[063-001]“Oh, dear, yes; you are not at all sharp at keeping your secrets

[063-002]from me. And, mother, at one time, for a day or so, I thought that I

[063-003]could oblige you. You have been so good to me, that I would almost do

[063-004]anything for you.”

[063-005]“Oh, no, no, no,” she said, deprecating his praise, and the sacrifice

[063-006]which he seemed to offer of his own hopes and aspirations. “I would

[063-007]not for worlds have you do so for my sake. No mother ever had a

[063-008]better son, and my only ambition is for your happiness.”

[063-009]“But, mother, she would not make me happy. I was mad enough for a

[063-010]moment to think that she could do so—for a moment I did think so.

[063-011]There was one occasion on which I would have asked her to take me,

[063-012]but—”

[063-013]“But what, Ludovic?”

[063-014]“Never mind; it passed away; and now I shall never ask her. Indeed

[063-015]I do not think she would have me. She is ambitious, and flying at

[063-016]higher game than I am. And I must say this for her, that she knows

[063-017]well what she is doing, and plays her cards as though she had been

[063-018]born with them in her hand.”

[063-019]“You will never ask her?”

[063-020]“No, mother; had I done so, it would have been for love of you—only

[063-021]for love of you.”

[063-022]“I would not for worlds that you should do that.”

[063-023]“Let her have Dumbello; she will make an excellent wife for him, just

[063-024]the wife that he will want. And you, you will have been so good to

[063-025]her in assisting her to such a matter.”

[063-026]“But, Ludovic, I am so anxious to see you settled.”

[063-027]“All in good time, mother!”

[063-028]“Ah, but the good time is passing away. Years run so very quickly. I

[063-029]hope you think about marrying, Ludovic.”

[063-030]“But, mother, what if I brought you a wife that you did not approve?”

[063-031]“I will approve of any one that you love; that is—”

[063-032]“That is, if you love her also; eh, mother?”

[063-033]“But I rely with such confidence on your taste. I know that you can

[063-034]like no one that is not lady-like and good.”

[063-035]“Lady-like and good! Will that suffice?” said he, thinking of Lucy

[063-036]Robarts.

[063-037]“Yes; it will suffice, if you love her. I don’t want you to care for

[063-038]money. Griselda will have a fortune that would have been convenient;

[063-039]but I do not wish you to care for that.” And thus, as they stood

[063-040]together in Miss Dunstable’s crowded room, the mother and son settled

[063-041]between themselves that the Lufton-Grantly alliance treaty was not

[063-042]to be ratified. “I suppose I must let Mrs. Grantly know,” said Lady

[063-043]Lufton to herself, as Griselda returned to her side. There had

[063-044]not been above a dozen words spoken between Lord Dumbello and his

[063-045]partner, but that young lady also had now fully made up her mind that

[063-046]the treaty above mentioned should never be brought into operation.

[063-047]We must go back to our hostess, whom we should not have left for so

[063-048]long a time, seeing that this chapter is written to show how well

[063-049]she could conduct herself in great emergencies. She had declared

[063-050]that after awhile she would be able to leave her position near the

[063-051]entrance door, and find out her own peculiar friends among the crowd;

[063-052]but the opportunity for doing so did not come till very late in the

[063-053]evening. There was a continuation of arrivals; she was wearied to

[063-054]death with making little speeches, and had more than once declared

[063-055]that she must depute Mrs. Harold Smith to take her place.

[063-056]That lady stuck to her through all her labours with admirable

[063-057]constancy, and made the work bearable. Without some such constancy

[063-058]on a friend’s part, it would have been unbearable. And it must

[063-059]be acknowledged that this was much to the credit of Mrs. Harold

[063-060]Smith. Her own hopes with reference to the great heiress had all

[063-061]been shattered, and her answer had been given to her in very plain

[063-062]language. But, nevertheless, she was true to her friendship, and was

[063-063]almost as willing to endure fatigue on the occasion as though she had

[063-064]a sister-in-law’s right in the house.

[063-065]At about one o’clock her brother came. He had not yet seen Miss

[063-066]Dunstable since the offer had been made, and had now with difficulty

[063-067]been persuaded by his sister to show himself.

[063-068]“What can be the use?” said he. “The game is up with me

[063-069]now;”—meaning, poor, ruined ne’er-do-well, not only that that game

[063-070]with Miss Dunstable was up, but that the great game of his whole life

[063-071]was being brought to an uncomfortable termination.

[063-072]“Nonsense,” said his sister. “Do you mean to despair because a man

[063-073]like the Duke of Omnium wants his money? What has been good security

[063-074]for him will be good security for another;” and then Mrs. Harold

[063-075]Smith made herself more agreeable than ever to Miss Dunstable.

[063-076]When Miss Dunstable was nearly worn out, but was still endeavouring

[063-077]to buoy herself up by a hope of the still-expected great arrival—for

[063-078]she knew that the hero would show himself only at a very late hour

[063-079]if it were to be her good fortune that he showed himself at all—Mr.

[063-080]Sowerby walked up the stairs. He had schooled himself to go through

[063-081]this ordeal with all the cool effrontery which was at his command;

[063-082]but it was clearly to be seen that all his effrontery did not stand

[063-083]him in sufficient stead, and that the interview would have been

[063-084]embarrassing had it not been for the genuine good-humour of the lady.

[063-085]“Here is my brother,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, showing by the

[063-086]tremulousness of the whisper that she looked forward to the meeting

[063-087]with some amount of apprehension.

[063-088]“How do you do, Mr. Sowerby?” said Miss Dunstable, walking almost

[063-089]into the doorway to welcome him. “Better late than never.”

[063-090]“I have only just got away from the House,” said he, as he gave her

[063-091]his hand.

[063-092]“Oh, I know well that you are _sans reproche_ among senators;—as Mr.

[063-093]Harold Smith is _sans peur_;—eh, my dear?”

[063-094]“I must confess that you have contrived to be uncommonly severe upon

[063-095]them both,” said Mrs. Harold, laughing; “and as regards poor Harold,

[063-096]most undeservedly so: Nathaniel is here, and may defend himself.”

[063-097]“And no one is better able to do so on all occasions. But, my dear

[063-098]Mr. Sowerby, I am dying of despair. Do you think he’ll come?”

[063-099]“He? who?”

[063-100]“You stupid man—as if there were more than one he! There were two,

[063-101]but the other has been.”

[063-102]“Upon my word, I don’t understand,” said Mr. Sowerby, now again at

[063-103]his ease. “But can I do anything? shall I go and fetch any one? Oh,

[063-104]Tom Towers! I fear I can’t help you. But here he is at the foot of

[063-105]the stairs!” And then Mr. Sowerby stood back with his sister to make

[063-106]way for the great representative man of the age.

[063-107]“Angels and ministers of grace, assist me!” said Miss Dunstable. “How

[063-108]on earth am I to behave myself? Mr. Sowerby, do you think that I

[063-109]ought to kneel down? My dear, will he have a reporter at his back

[063-110]in the royal livery?” And then Miss Dunstable advanced two or three

[063-111]steps—not into the doorway, as she had done for Mr. Sowerby—put out

[063-112]her hand, and smiled her sweetest on Mr. Towers, of the _Jupiter_.

[063-113]“Mr. Towers,” she said, “I am delighted to have this opportunity of

[063-114]seeing you in my own house.”

[063-115]“Miss Dunstable, I am immensely honoured by the privilege of being

[063-116]here,” said he.

[063-117]“The honour done is all conferred on me,” and she bowed and curtseyed

[063-118]with very stately grace. Each thoroughly understood the badinage of

[063-119]the other; and then, in a few moments, they were engaged in very easy

[063-120]conversation.

[063-121]“By-the-by, Sowerby, what do you think of this threatened

[063-122]dissolution?” said Tom Towers.

[063-123]“We are all in the hands of Providence,” said Mr. Sowerby, striving

[063-124]to take the matter without any outward show of emotion. But the

[063-125]question was one of terrible import to him, and up to this time he

[063-126]had heard of no such threat. Nor had Mrs. Harold Smith, nor Miss

[063-127]Dunstable, nor had a hundred others who now either listened to the

[063-128]vaticinations of Mr. Towers, or to the immediate report made of

[063-129]them. But it is given to some men to originate such tidings, and the

[063-130]performance of the prophecy is often brought about by the authority

[063-131]of the prophet. On the following morning the rumour that there would

[063-132]be a dissolution was current in all high circles. “They have no

[063-133]conscience in such matters; no conscience whatever,” said a small

[063-134]god, speaking of the giants,—a small god, whose constituency was

[063-135]expensive.

[063-136]Mr. Towers stood there chatting for about twenty minutes, and then

[063-137]took his departure without making his way into the room. He had

[063-138]answered the purpose for which he had been invited, and left Miss

[063-139]Dunstable in a happy frame of mind.

[063-140]“I am very glad that he came,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, with an air of

[063-141]triumph.

[063-142]“Yes, I am glad,” said Miss Dunstable, “though I am thoroughly

[063-143]ashamed that I should be so. After all, what good has he done to me

[063-144]or to any one?” And having uttered this moral reflection, she made

[063-145]her way into the rooms, and soon discovered Dr. Thorne standing by

[063-146]himself against the wall.

[063-147]“Well, doctor,” she said, “where are Mary and Frank? You do not look

[063-148]at all comfortable, standing here by yourself.”

[063-149]“I am quite as comfortable as I expected, thank you,” said he. “They

[063-150]are in the room somewhere, and, as I believe, equally happy.”

[063-151]“That’s spiteful in you, doctor, to speak in that way. What would you

[063-152]say if you were called on to endure all that I have gone through this

[063-153]evening?”

[063-154]“There is no accounting for tastes, but I presume you like it.”

[063-155]“I am not so sure of that. Give me your arm, and let me get some

[063-156]supper. One always likes the idea of having done hard work, and one

[063-157]always likes to have been successful.”

[063-158]“We all know that virtue is its own reward,” said the doctor.

[063-159]“Well, that is something hard upon me,” said Miss Dunstable, as she

[063-160]sat down to table. “And you really think that no good of any sort can

[063-161]come from my giving such a party as this?”

[063-162]“Oh, yes; some people, no doubt, have been amused.”

[063-163]“It is all vanity in your estimation,” said Miss Dunstable; “vanity

[063-164]and vexation of spirit. Well; there is a good deal of the latter,

[063-165]certainly. Sherry, if you please. I would give anything for a glass

[063-166]of beer, but that is out of the question. Vanity and vexation of

[063-167]spirit! And yet I meant to do good.”

[063-168]“Pray, do not suppose that I am condemning you, Miss Dunstable.”

[063-169]“Ah, but I do suppose it. Not only you, but another also, whose

[063-170]judgment I care for perhaps more than yours; and that, let me tell

[063-171]you, is saying a great deal. You do condemn me, Dr. Thorne, and I

[063-172]also condemn myself. It is not that I have done wrong, but the game

[063-173]is not worth the candle.”

[063-174]“Ah; that’s the question.”

[063-175]“The game is not worth the candle. And yet it was a triumph to have

[063-176]both the duke and Tom Towers. You must confess that I have not

[063-177]managed badly.”

[063-178]Soon after that the Greshams went away, and in an hour’s time or so,

[063-179]Miss Dunstable was allowed to drag herself to her own bed.

[063-180]That is the great question to be asked on all such occasions, “Is the

[063-181]game worth the candle?”

[063-182]CHAPTER XXX.

[063-183]THE GRANTLY TRIUMPH.

[063-184]It has been mentioned cursorily—the reader, no doubt, will have

[063-185]forgotten it—that Mrs. Grantly was not specially invited by her

[063-186]husband to go up to town with a view of being present at Miss

[063-187]Dunstable’s party. Mrs. Grantly said nothing on the subject, but she

[063-188]was somewhat chagrined; not on account of the loss she sustained with

[063-189]reference to that celebrated assembly, but because she felt that her

[063-190]daughter’s affairs required the supervision of a mother’s eye. She

[063-191]also doubted the final ratification of that Lufton-Grantly treaty,

[063-192]and, doubting it, she did not feel quite satisfied that her daughter

[063-193]should be left in Lady Lufton’s hands. She had said a word or two to

[063-194]the archdeacon before he went up, but only a word or two, for she

[063-195]hesitated to trust him in so delicate a matter. She was, therefore,

[063-196]not a little surprised at receiving, on the second morning after

[063-197]her husband’s departure, a letter from him desiring her immediate

[063-198]presence in London. She was surprised; but her heart was filled

[063-199]rather with hope than dismay, for she had full confidence in her

[063-200]daughter’s discretion.
