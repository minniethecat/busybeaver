# Miss Grantly

Miss Grantly — Griselda — is the elder daughter of Archdeacon Grantly of Barchester, a young woman whose remarkable beauty and anticipated fortune place her at the centre of the novel's marriage plots. Her statuesque composure and monosyllabic manner make her an object of admiration and speculation, though her inner life remains largely opaque to those around her (024-067).

## Role in the Novel

Miss Grantly enters the narrative through the social world of Framley Court, where her beauty immediately commands attention. Lord Lufton, the young master of Framley, is "evidently" much taken with her, and Lady Lufton herself begins to contemplate the match with favour — seeing in Miss Grantly precisely the sort of well-connected, well-dowered bride she envisions for her son (025-114) (054-066).

Her role is that of the conventional romantic rival: where Lucy Robarts is warm, spontaneous, and relatively poor, Miss Grantly is cool, composed, and wealthy. The contrast between the two young women structures much of the novel's romantic tension. Yet Trollope resists making Miss Grantly merely a foil; she is given her own distinct trajectory, eventually becoming the object of Lord Dumbello's attentions and then — in a turn that reverberates through the later chapters — being jilted by him (094-071).

## Character Traits

- **Beauty and dignity**: Miss Grantly possesses "great beauty and dignity of demeanour," the kind of physical presence that causes conversation to falter and heads to turn when she enters a room (025-073). Her beauty is of the statuesque variety — admired from a distance, cool to the touch.
- **Reserve**: She is notably "monosyllabic, smiling, however, though not speaking much" — a young woman who has learned that saying little is often the safest course in the treacherous waters of Barsetshire society (024-183). Her silence can be read as haughtiness or as shyness; the novel leaves the question delicately open.
- **Poise**: Even when disaster strikes — when Lord Dumbello withdraws his attentions — Miss Grantly maintains her composure. She does not collapse into melodrama; she endures with the self-possession that is both her greatest strength and, perhaps, her greatest liability (062-108).

## Relationships

- **[[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]**: Lady Lufton regards Miss Grantly as a highly suitable match for her son, and actively promotes the connection. The older woman sees in Griselda the qualities she values: birth, beauty, fortune, and a properly unassertive temperament (054-066).
- **[[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]]**: The young lord admired Miss Grantly "very much" — indeed, the narrative suggests he was more taken with her than with any other young woman before Lucy Robarts entered his life. That his interest wanes says less about Miss Grantly's deficiencies than about the nature of attraction itself (025-114) (087-034).
- **[[lord-dumbello|Lord Dumbello]]**: The eventual object of Miss Grantly's matrimonial hopes — and the agent of her disappointment. His courtship and subsequent withdrawal form one of the novel's most painful subplots, handled by Trollope with characteristic restraint (062-145) (094-071).
- **[[lucy-robarts|Lucy Robarts]]**: The two are introduced at Framley Court, where they form a relationship that is cordial but constrained — each aware, on some level, that they occupy competing positions in the romantic economy of the household (024-179).

## Key Scenes

- **Introduction at Framley Court (Chapter 24)**: Miss Grantly makes her entrance into the novel's social world, seated beside Lord Lufton at dinner while Lucy Robarts watches from across the table. The scene establishes the triangular tension that will drive much of the subsequent plot (024-103) (024-124).
- **Christmas at Framley (Chapter 25)**: Miss Grantly remains at Framley for Christmas, and her presence there — at the piano with Mrs. Robarts, responding to Lord Lufton's attentions — deepens the sense of her as a serious contender for his hand. Lady Lufton observes the developing situation with satisfaction (025-066) (025-081).
- **The Dumbello courtship (Chapters 60–62)**: Miss Grantly's relationship with Lord Dumbello develops through a series of decorous encounters, culminating in an understanding that seems to promise a brilliant match — and then unravels with devastating quietness (060-156) (062-145).
- **The jilting (Chapter 94)**: The news that Lord Dumbello has "jilted Miss Grantly" ripples through Barsetshire society, transforming her from an object of envy to an object of sympathy — and testing the depths of her much-remarked composure (094-071).

## Social & Historical Context

Miss Grantly occupies the position of the ideal Victorian clergyman's daughter: beautiful but not forward, accomplished but not intellectual, possessed of expectations but not yet in possession of a fortune. Her father, Archdeacon Grantly, is one of the most powerful figures in the Barchester ecclesiastical establishment, and her marriage prospects are a matter of family strategy as much as personal inclination.

The novel treats her situation with a degree of sympathy that is easy to miss. In a world where a woman's value was measured largely by the match she made, Miss Grantly plays the game by the rules she has been taught — and loses anyway. Her famous reserve may be less a character flaw than a rational adaptation to a system in which speaking one's mind carried genuine risk. The jilting she endures was, in Victorian terms, a social catastrophe; that Trollope allows her to survive it with dignity intact is one of the novel's quiet kindnesses.

## Critical Commentary

> Miss Grantly has often been read as the "ice maiden" to Lucy Robarts's "warm hearth" — a contrast that certainly operates within the novel's romantic symbolism. But such a reading risks replicating the very assumptions the novel subtly questions: that a woman's value lies in her accessibility, her warmth, her willingness to be known. Miss Grantly's opacity may be not a deficiency but a defence — the armour of a young woman who has learned, perhaps too well, that everything she says and does will be weighed in the balance of the marriage market.
