## Section 34

[034-001]In these days we are becoming very strict about truth with our

[034-002]children; terribly strict occasionally, when we consider the natural

[034-003]weakness of the moral courage at the ages of ten, twelve, and

[034-004]fourteen. But I do not know that we are at all increasing the measure

[034-005]of strictness with which we, grown-up people, regulate our own truth

[034-006]and falsehood. Heaven forbid that I should be thought to advocate

[034-007]falsehood in children; but an untruth is more pardonable in them than

[034-008]in their parents. Lady Lufton’s tarradiddle was of a nature that

[034-009]is usually considered excusable—at least with grown people; but,

[034-010]nevertheless, she would have been nearer to perfection could she have

[034-011]confined herself to the truth. Let us suppose that a boy were to

[034-012]write home from school, saying that another boy had promised to come

[034-013]and stay with him, that other having given no such promise—what a

[034-014]very naughty boy would that first boy be in the eyes of his pastors

[034-015]and masters!

[034-016]That little conversation between Lord Lufton and his mother—in which

[034-017]nothing was said about his lordship’s parliamentary duties—took

[034-018]place on the evening before he started for London. On that occasion

[034-019]he certainly was not in his best humour, nor did he behave to his

[034-020]mother in his kindest manner. He had then left the room when she

[034-021]began to talk about Miss Grantly; and once again in the course of the

[034-022]evening, when his mother, not very judiciously, said a word or two

[034-023]about Griselda’s beauty, he had remarked that she was no conjuror,

[034-024]and would hardly set the Thames on fire.

[034-025]“If she were a conjuror!” said Lady Lufton, rather piqued, “I should

[034-026]not now be going to take her out in London. I know many of those sort

[034-027]of girls whom you call conjurors; they can talk for ever, and always

[034-028]talk either loudly or in a whisper. I don’t like them, and I am sure

[034-029]that you do not in your heart.”

[034-030]“Oh, as to liking them in my heart—that is being very particular.”

[034-031]“Griselda Grantly is a lady, and as such I shall be happy to have her

[034-032]with me in town. She is just the girl that Justinia will like to have

[034-033]with her.”

[034-034]“Exactly,” said Lord Lufton. “She will do exceedingly well for

[034-035]Justinia.”

[034-036]Now this was not good-natured on the part of Lord Lufton; and his

[034-037]mother felt it the more strongly, inasmuch as it seemed to signify

[034-038]that he was setting his back up against the Lufton-Grantly alliance.

[034-039]She had been pretty sure that he would do so in the event of his

[034-040]suspecting that a plot was being laid to catch him; and now it almost

[034-041]appeared that he did suspect such a plot. Why else that sarcasm as to

[034-042]Griselda doing very well for his sister?

[034-043]And now we must go back and describe a little scene at Framley which

[034-044]will account for his lordship’s ill-humour and suspicions, and

[034-045]explain how it came to pass that he so snubbed his mother. This scene

[034-046]took place about ten days after the evening on which Mrs. Robarts

[034-047]and Lucy were walking together in the parsonage garden, and during

[034-048]those ten days Lucy had not once allowed herself to be entrapped

[034-049]into any special conversation with the young peer. She had dined at

[034-050]Framley Court during that interval, and had spent a second evening

[034-051]there; Lord Lufton had also been up at the parsonage on three or

[034-052]four occasions, and had looked for her in her usual walks; but,

[034-053]nevertheless, they had never come together in their old familiar

[034-054]way, since the day on which Lady Lufton had hinted her fears to Mrs.

[034-055]Robarts.

[034-056]Lord Lufton had very much missed her. At first he had not attributed

[034-057]this change to a purposed scheme of action on the part of any one;

[034-058]nor, indeed, had he much thought about it, although he had felt

[034-059]himself to be annoyed. But as the period fixed for his departure

[034-060]grew near, it did occur to him as very odd that he should never hear

[034-061]Lucy’s voice unless when she said a few words to his mother, or to

[034-062]her sister-in-law. And then he made up his mind that he would speak

[034-063]to her before he went, and that the mystery should be explained to

[034-064]him.

[034-065]And he carried out his purpose, calling at the parsonage on one

[034-066]special afternoon; and it was on the evening of the same day that

[034-067]his mother sang the praises of Griselda Grantly so inopportunely.

[034-068]Robarts, he knew, was then absent from home, and Mrs. Robarts was

[034-069]with his mother down at the house, preparing lists of the poor people

[034-070]to be specially attended to in Lady Lufton’s approaching absence.

[034-071]Taking advantage of this, he walked boldly in through the parsonage

[034-072]garden; asked the gardener, with an indifferent voice, whether either

[034-073]of the ladies were at home, and then caught poor Lucy exactly on the

[034-074]doorstep of the house.

[034-075]“Were you going in or out, Miss Robarts?”

[034-076]“Well, I was going out,” said Lucy; and she began to consider how

[034-077]best she might get quit of any prolonged encounter.

[034-078]“Oh, going out, were you? I don’t know whether I may offer to—”

[034-079]“Well, Lord Lufton, not exactly, seeing that I am about to pay a

[034-080]visit to our near neighbour, Mrs. Podgens. Perhaps you have no

[034-081]particular call towards Mrs. Podgens’ just at present, or to her new

[034-082]baby?”

[034-083]“And have you any very particular call that way?”

[034-084]“Yes, and especially to Baby Podgens. Baby Podgens is a real little

[034-085]duck—only just two days old.” And Lucy, as she spoke, progressed

[034-086]a step or two, as though she were determined not to remain there

[034-087]talking on the doorstep.

[034-088]A slight cloud came across his brow as he saw this, and made him

[034-089]resolve that she should not gain her purpose. He was not going to be

[034-090]foiled in that way by such a girl as Lucy Robarts. He had come there

[034-091]to speak to her, and speak to her he would. There had been enough of

[034-092]intimacy between them to justify him in demanding, at any rate, as

[034-093]much as that.

[034-094]“Miss Robarts,” he said, “I am starting for London to-morrow, and if

[034-095]I do not say good-bye to you now, I shall not be able to do so at

[034-096]all.”

[034-097]“Good-bye, Lord Lufton,” she said, giving him her hand, and smiling

[034-098]on him with her old genial, good-humoured, racy smile. “And mind you

[034-099]bring into Parliament that law which you promised me for defending my

[034-100]young chickens.”

[034-101]He took her hand, but that was not all that he wanted. “Surely Mrs.

[034-102]Podgens and her baby can wait ten minutes. I shall not see you again

[034-103]for months to come, and yet you seem to begrudge me two words.”

[034-104]“Not two hundred if they can be of any service to you,” said she,

[034-105]walking cheerily back into the drawing-room; “only I did not think it

[034-106]worth while to waste your time, as Fanny is not here.”

[034-107]She was infinitely more collected, more master of herself than he

[034-108]was. Inwardly, she did tremble at the idea of what was coming, but

[034-109]outwardly she showed no agitation—none as yet; if only she could so

[034-110]possess herself as to refrain from doing so, when she heard what he

[034-111]might have to say to her.

[034-112]He hardly knew what it was for the saying of which he had so

[034-113]resolutely come thither. He had by no means made up his mind that he

[034-114]loved Lucy Robarts; nor had he made up his mind that, loving her, he

[034-115]would, or that, loving her, he would not, make her his wife. He had

[034-116]never used his mind in the matter in any way, either for good or

[034-117]evil. He had learned to like her and to think that she was very

[034-118]pretty. He had found out that it was very pleasant to talk to her;

[034-119]whereas, talking to Griselda Grantly, and, indeed, to some other

[034-120]young ladies of his acquaintance, was often hard work. The half-hours

[034-121]which he had spent with Lucy had always been satisfactory to him. He

[034-122]had found himself to be more bright with her than with other people,

[034-123]and more apt to discuss subjects worth discussing; and thus it had

[034-124]come about that he thoroughly liked Lucy Robarts. As to whether his

[034-125]affection was Platonic or anti-Platonic he had never asked himself;

[034-126]but he had spoken words to her, shortly before that sudden cessation

[034-127]of their intimacy, which might have been taken as anti-Platonic by

[034-128]any girl so disposed to regard them. He had not thrown himself at

[034-129]her feet, and declared himself to be devoured by a consuming passion;

[034-130]but he had touched her hand as lovers touch those of women whom they

[034-131]love; he had had his confidences with her, talking to her of his own

[034-132]mother, of his sister, and of his friends; and he had called her his

[034-133]own dear friend Lucy.

[034-134]All this had been very sweet to her, but very poisonous also. She had

[034-135]declared to herself very frequently that her liking for this young

[034-136]nobleman was as purely a feeling of mere friendship as was that of

[034-137]her brother; and she had professed to herself that she would give

[034-138]the lie to the world’s cold sarcasms on such subjects. But she had

[034-139]now acknowledged that the sarcasms of the world on that matter,

[034-140]cold though they may be, are not the less true; and having so

[034-141]acknowledged, she had resolved that all close alliance between

[034-142]herself and Lord Lufton must be at an end. She had come to a

[034-143]conclusion, but he had come to none; and in this frame of mind he

[034-144]was now there with the object of reopening that dangerous friendship

[034-145]which she had had the sense to close.

[034-146]“And so you are going to-morrow?” she said, as soon as they were both

[034-147]within the drawing-room.

[034-148]“Yes: I’m off by the early train to-morrow morning, and Heaven knows

[034-149]when we may meet again.”

[034-150]“Next winter, shall we not?”

[034-151]“Yes, for a day or two, I suppose. I do not know whether I shall pass

[034-152]another winter here. Indeed, one can never say where one will be.”

[034-153]“No, one can’t; such as you, at least, cannot. I am not of a

[034-154]migratory tribe myself.”

[034-155]“I wish you were.”

[034-156]“I’m not a bit obliged to you. Your nomade life does not agree with

[034-157]young ladies.”

[034-158]“I think they are taking to it pretty freely, then. We have

[034-159]unprotected young women all about the world.”

[034-160]“And great bores you find them, I suppose?”

[034-161]“No; I like it. The more we can get out of old-fashioned grooves the

[034-162]better I am pleased. I should be a radical to-morrow—a regular man

[034-163]of the people,—only I should break my mother’s heart.”

[034-164]“Whatever you do, Lord Lufton, do not do that.”

[034-165]“That is why I have liked you so much,” he continued, “because you

[034-166]get out of the grooves.”

[034-167]“Do I?”

[034-168]“Yes; and go along by yourself, guiding your own footsteps; not

[034-169]carried hither and thither, just as your grandmother’s old tramway

[034-170]may chance to take you.”

[034-171]“Do you know I have a strong idea that my grandmother’s old tramway

[034-172]will be the safest and the best after all? I have not left it very

[034-173]far, and I certainly mean to go back to it.”

[034-174]“That’s impossible! An army of old women, with coils of ropes made

[034-175]out of time-honoured prejudices, could not drag you back.”

[034-176]“No, Lord Lufton, that is true. But one—” and then she stopped

[034-177]herself. She could not tell him that one loving mother, anxious for

[034-178]her only son, had sufficed to do it. She could not explain to him

[034-179]that this departure from the established tramway had already broken

[034-180]her own rest, and turned her peaceful happy life into a grievous

[034-181]battle.

[034-182]“I know that you are trying to go back,” he said. “Do you think that

[034-183]I have eyes and cannot see? Come, Lucy, you and I have been friends,

[034-184]and we must not part in this way. My mother is a paragon among women.

[034-185]I say it in earnest;—a paragon among women: and her love for me is

[034-186]the perfection of motherly love.”
