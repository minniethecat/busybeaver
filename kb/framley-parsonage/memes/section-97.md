## Section 97

[097-001]“Oh, no,” said Lucy.

[097-002]“He is the best of sons, and the best of men, and I am sure that he

[097-003]will be the best of husbands.”

[097-004]Lucy had an idea, by instinct, however, rather than by sight, that

[097-005]Lady Lufton’s eyes were full of tears as she spoke. As for herself

[097-006]she was altogether blinded and did not dare to lift her face or to

[097-007]turn her head. As for the utterance of any sound, that was quite out

[097-008]of the question.

[097-009]“And now I have come here, Lucy, to ask you to be his wife.”

[097-010]She was quite sure that she heard the words. They came plainly to

[097-011]her ears, leaving on her brain their proper sense, but yet she could

[097-012]not move or make any sign that she had understood them. It seemed

[097-013]as though it would be ungenerous in her to take advantage of such

[097-014]conduct and to accept an offer made with so much self-sacrifice. She

[097-015]had not time at the first moment to think even of his happiness,

[097-016]let alone her own, but she thought only of the magnitude of the

[097-017]concession which had been made to her. When she had constituted Lady

[097-018]Lufton the arbiter of her destiny she had regarded the question of

[097-019]her love as decided against herself. She had found herself unable to

[097-020]endure the position of being Lady Lufton’s daughter-in-law while Lady

[097-021]Lufton would be scorning her, and therefore she had given up the

[097-022]game. She had given up the game, sacrificing herself, and, as far as

[097-023]it might be a sacrifice, sacrificing him also. She had been resolute

[097-024]to stand to her word in this respect, but she had never allowed

[097-025]herself to think it possible that Lady Lufton should comply with the

[097-026]conditions which she, Lucy, had laid upon her. And yet such was the

[097-027]case, as she so plainly heard. “And now I have come here, Lucy, to

[097-028]ask you to be his wife.”

[097-029]How long they sat together silent, I cannot say; counted by minutes

[097-030]the time would not probably have amounted to many, but to each of

[097-031]them the duration seemed considerable. Lady Lufton, while she was

[097-032]speaking, had contrived to get hold of Lucy’s hand, and she sat,

[097-033]still holding it, trying to look into Lucy’s face,—which, however,

[097-034]she could hardly see, so much was it turned away. Neither, indeed,

[097-035]were Lady Lufton’s eyes perfectly dry. No answer came to her

[097-036]question, and therefore, after a while, it was necessary that she

[097-037]should speak again.

[097-038]“Must I go back to him, Lucy, and tell him that there is some other

[097-039]objection—something besides a stern old mother; some hindrance,

[097-040]perhaps, not so easily overcome?”

[097-041]“No,” said Lucy, and it was all which at the moment she could say.

[097-042]“What shall I tell him, then? Shall I say yes—simply yes?”

[097-043]“Simply yes,” said Lucy.

[097-044]“And as to the stern old mother who thought her only son too precious

[097-045]to be parted with at the first word—is nothing to be said to her?”

[097-046]“Oh, Lady Lufton!”

[097-047]“No forgiveness to be spoken, no sign of affection to be given?

[097-048]Is she always to be regarded as stern and cross, vexatious and

[097-049]disagreeable?”

[097-050]Lucy slowly turned round her head and looked up into her companion’s

[097-051]face. Though she had as yet no voice to speak of affection she could

[097-052]fill her eyes with love, and in that way make to her future mother

[097-053]all the promises that were needed.

[097-054]“Lucy, dearest Lucy, you must be very dear to me now.” And then they

[097-055]were in each other’s arms, kissing each other.

[097-056]Lady Lufton now desired her coachman to drive up and down for some

[097-057]little space along the road while she completed her necessary

[097-058]conversation with Lucy. She wanted at first to carry her back to

[097-059]Framley that evening, promising to send her again to Mrs. Crawley

[097-060]on the following morning—“till some permanent arrangement could be

[097-061]made,” by which Lady Lufton intended the substitution of a regular

[097-062]nurse for her future daughter-in-law, seeing that Lucy Robarts was

[097-063]now invested in her eyes with attributes which made it unbecoming

[097-064]that she should sit in attendance at Mrs. Crawley’s bedside. But Lucy

[097-065]would not go back to Framley on that evening; no, nor on the next

[097-066]morning. She would be so glad if Fanny would come to her there, and

[097-067]then she would arrange about going home.

[097-068]“But, Lucy, dear, what am I to say to Ludovic? Perhaps you would feel

[097-069]it awkward if he were to come to see you here.”

[097-070]“Oh, yes, Lady Lufton; pray tell him not to do that.”

[097-071]“And is that all that I am to tell him?”

[097-072]“Tell him—tell him—He won’t want you to tell him anything;—only I

[097-073]should like to be quiet for a day, Lady Lufton.”

[097-074]“Well, dearest, you shall be quiet; the day after to-morrow

[097-075]then.—Mind we must not spare you any longer, because it will be

[097-076]right that you should be at home now. He would think it very hard if

[097-077]you were to be so near, and he was not to be allowed to look at you.

[097-078]And there will be some one else who will want to see you. I shall

[097-079]want to have you very near to me, for I shall be wretched, Lucy, if I

[097-080]cannot teach you to love me.” In answer to which Lucy did find voice

[097-081]enough to make sundry promises.

[097-082]And then she was put out of the carriage at the little wicket gate,

[097-083]and Lady Lufton was driven back to Framley. I wonder whether the

[097-084]servant when he held the door for Miss Robarts was conscious that he

[097-085]was waiting on his future mistress. I fancy that he was, for these

[097-086]sort of people always know everything, and the peculiar courtesy of

[097-087]his demeanour as he let down the carriage steps was very observable.

[097-088]Lucy felt almost beside herself as she returned upstairs, not knowing

[097-089]what to do, or how to look, and with what words to speak. It behoved

[097-090]her to go at once to Mrs. Crawley’s room, and yet she longed to be

[097-091]alone. She knew that she was quite unable either to conceal her

[097-092]thoughts or express them; nor did she wish at the present moment to

[097-093]talk to any one about her happiness,—seeing that she could not at

[097-094]the present moment talk to Fanny Robarts. She went, however, without

[097-095]delay into Mrs. Crawley’s room, and with that little eager way of

[097-096]speaking quickly which is so common with people who know that they

[097-097]are confused, said that she feared she had been a very long time

[097-098]away.

[097-099]“And was it Lady Lufton?”

[097-100]“Yes; it was Lady Lufton.”

[097-101]“Why, Lucy; I did not know that you and her ladyship were such

[097-102]friends.”

[097-103]“She had something particular she wanted to say,” said Lucy, avoiding

[097-104]the question, and avoiding also Mrs. Crawley’s eyes; and then she

[097-105]sate down in her usual chair.

[097-106]“It was nothing unpleasant, I hope.”

[097-107]“No, nothing at all unpleasant; nothing of that kind.—Oh, Mrs.

[097-108]Crawley, I’ll tell you some other time, but pray do not ask me now.”

[097-109]And then she got up and escaped, for it was absolutely necessary that

[097-110]she should be alone.

[097-111]When she reached her own room—that in which the children usually

[097-112]slept—she made a great effort to compose herself, but not altogether

[097-113]successfully. She got out her paper and blotting-book intending, as

[097-114]she said to herself, to write to Fanny, knowing, however, that the

[097-115]letter when written would be destroyed; but she was not able even

[097-116]to form a word. Her hand was unsteady and her eyes were dim and her

[097-117]thoughts were incapable of being fixed. She could only sit, and

[097-118]think, and wonder, and hope; occasionally wiping the tears from her

[097-119]eyes, and asking herself why her present frame of mind was so painful

[097-120]to her? During the last two or three months she had felt no fear

[097-121]of Lord Lufton, had always carried herself before him on equal

[097-122]terms, and had been signally capable of doing so when he made his

[097-123]declaration to her at the parsonage; but now she looked forward with

[097-124]an undefined dread to the first moment in which she should see him.

[097-125]And then she thought of a certain evening she had passed at Framley

[097-126]Court, and acknowledged to herself that there was some pleasure in

[097-127]looking back to that. Griselda Grantly had been there, and all the

[097-128]constitutional powers of the two families had been at work to render

[097-129]easy a process of love-making between her and Lord Lufton. Lucy had

[097-130]seen and understood it all, without knowing that she understood it,

[097-131]and had, in a certain degree, suffered from beholding it. She had

[097-132]placed herself apart, not complaining—painfully conscious of some

[097-133]inferiority, but, at the same time, almost boasting to herself that

[097-134]in her own way she was the superior. And then he had come behind

[097-135]her chair, whispering to her, speaking to her his first words of

[097-136]kindness and good-nature, and she had resolved that she would be his

[097-137]friend—his friend, even though Griselda Grantly might be his wife.

[097-138]What those resolutions were worth had soon become manifest to her.

[097-139]She had soon confessed to herself the result of that friendship, and

[097-140]had determined to bear her punishment with courage. But now—

[097-141]She sate so for about an hour, and would fain have so sat out the

[097-142]day. But as this could not be, she got up, and having washed her face

[097-143]and eyes returned to Mrs. Crawley’s room. There she found Mr. Crawley

[097-144]also, to her great joy, for she knew that while he was there no

[097-145]questions would be asked of her. He was always very gentle to her,

[097-146]treating her with an old-fashioned polished respect—except when

[097-147]compelled on that one occasion by his sense of duty to accuse her of

[097-148]mendacity respecting the purveying of victuals—, but he had never

[097-149]become absolutely familiar with her as his wife had done; and it was

[097-150]well for her now that he had not done so, for she could not have

[097-151]talked about Lady Lufton.

[097-152]In the evening, when the three were present, she did manage to say

[097-153]that she expected Mrs. Robarts would come over on the following day.

[097-154]“We shall part with you, Miss Robarts, with the deepest regret,” said

[097-155]Mr. Crawley; “but we would not on any account keep you longer. Mrs.

[097-156]Crawley can do without you now. What she would have done, had you not

[097-157]come to us, I am at a loss to think.”

[097-158]“I did not say that I should go,” said Lucy.

[097-159]“But you will,” said Mrs. Crawley. “Yes, dear, you will. I know that

[097-160]it is proper now that you should return. Nay, but we will not have

[097-161]you any longer. And the poor dear children, too,—they may return.

[097-162]How am I to thank Mrs. Robarts for what she has done for us?”

[097-163]It was settled that if Mrs. Robarts came on the following day Lucy

[097-164]should go back with her; and then, during the long watches of the

[097-165]night—for on this last night Lucy would not leave the bed-side of

[097-166]her new friend till long after the dawn had broken—she did tell Mrs.

[097-167]Crawley what was to be her destiny in life. To herself there seemed

[097-168]nothing strange in her new position; but to Mrs. Crawley it was

[097-169]wonderful that she—she, poor as she was—should have an embryo

[097-170]peeress at her bedside, handing her her cup to drink, and smoothing

[097-171]her pillow that she might be at rest. It was strange, and she could

[097-172]hardly maintain her accustomed familiarity. Lucy felt this, at the

[097-173]moment.

[097-174]“It must make no difference, you know,” said she, eagerly; “none at

[097-175]all, between you and me. Promise me that it shall make no

[097-176]difference.”

[097-177]The promise was, of course, exacted; but it was not possible that

[097-178]such a promise should be kept.

[097-179]Very early on the following morning—so early that it woke her while

[097-180]still in her first sleep—there came a letter for her from the

[097-181]parsonage. Mrs. Robarts had written it, after her return home from

[097-182]Lady Lufton’s dinner.
