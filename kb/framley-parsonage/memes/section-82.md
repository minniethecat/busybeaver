## Section 82

[082-001]“It frets me dreadfully that I cannot get to see Mary,” Lady Arabella

[082-002]said, as soon as the first ordinary question as to her ailments had

[082-003]been asked and answered.

[082-004]“She’s quite well and will be over to see you before long.”

[082-005]“Now I beg that she won’t. She never thinks of coming when there

[082-006]can be no possible objection, and travelling, at the present moment,

[082-007]would be—” Whereupon the Lady Arabella shook her head very gravely.

[082-008]“Only think of the importance of it, doctor,” she said. “Remember the

[082-009]enormous stake there is to be considered.”

[082-010]“It would not do her a ha’porth of harm if the stake were twice as

[082-011]large.”

[082-012]“Nonsense, doctor, don’t tell me; as if I didn’t know myself. I was

[082-013]very much against her going to London this spring, but of course what

[082-014]I said was overruled. It always is. I do believe Mr. Gresham went

[082-015]over to Boxall Hill, on purpose to induce her to go. But what does he

[082-016]care? He’s fond of Frank; but he never thinks of looking beyond the

[082-017]present day. He never did, as you know well enough, doctor.”

[082-018]“The trip did her all the good in the world,” said Dr. Thorne,

[082-019]preferring anything to a conversation respecting the squire’s sins.

[082-020]“I very well remember that when I was in that way it wasn’t thought

[082-021]that such trips would do me any good. But, perhaps, things are

[082-022]altered since then.”

[082-023]“Yes, they are,” said the doctor. “We don’t interfere so much

[082-024]now-a-days.”

[082-025]“I know I never asked for such amusements when so much depended on

[082-026]quietness. I remember before Frank was born—and, indeed, when all of

[082-027]them were born— But as you say, things were different then; and I

[082-028]can easily believe that Mary is a person quite determined to have her

[082-029]own way.”

[082-030]“Why, Lady Arabella, she would have stayed at home without wishing to

[082-031]stir if Frank had done so much as hold up his little finger.”

[082-032]“So did I always. If Mr. Gresham made the slightest hint I gave way.

[082-033]But I really don’t see what one gets in return for such implicit

[082-034]obedience. Now this year, doctor, of course I should have liked

[082-035]to have been up in London for a week or two. You seemed to think

[082-036]yourself that I might as well see Sir Omicron.”

[082-037]“There could be no possible objection, I said.”

[082-038]“Well; no; exactly; and as Mr. Gresham knew I wished it, I think he

[082-039]might as well have offered it. I suppose there can be no reason now

[082-040]about money.”

[082-041]“But I understood that Mary specially asked you and Augusta?”

[082-042]“Yes; Mary was very good. She did ask me. But I know very well that

[082-043]Mary wants all the room she has got in London. The house is not at

[082-044]all too large for herself. And, for the matter of that, my sister,

[082-045]the countess, was very anxious that I should be with her. But one

[082-046]does like to be independent if one can, and for one fortnight I do

[082-047]think that Mr. Gresham might have managed it. When I knew that he was

[082-048]so dreadfully out at elbows I never troubled him about it,—though,

[082-049]goodness knows, all that was never my fault.”

[082-050]“The squire hates London. A fortnight there in warm weather would

[082-051]nearly be the death of him.”

[082-052]“He might at any rate have paid me the compliment of asking me.

[082-053]The chances are ten to one I should not have gone. It is that

[082-054]indifference that cuts me so. He was here just now, and, would you

[082-055]believe it?—”

[082-056]But the doctor was determined to avoid further complaint for the

[082-057]present day. “I wonder what you would feel, Lady Arabella, if the

[082-058]squire were to take it into his head to go away and amuse himself,

[082-059]leaving you at home. There are worse men than Mr. Gresham, if you

[082-060]will believe me.” All this was an allusion to Earl de Courcy, her

[082-061]ladyship’s brother, as Lady Arabella very well understood; and the

[082-062]argument was one which was very often used to silence her.

[082-063]“Upon my word, then, I should like it better than his hanging about

[082-064]here doing nothing but attend to those nasty dogs. I really sometimes

[082-065]think that he has no spirit left.”

[082-066]“You are mistaken there, Lady Arabella,” said the doctor, rising with

[082-067]his hat in his hand and making his escape without further parley.

[082-068]As he went home he could not but think that that phase of married

[082-069]life was not a very pleasant one. Mr. Gresham and his wife were

[082-070]supposed by the world to live on the best of terms. They always

[082-071]inhabited the same house, went out together when they did go out,

[082-072]always sat in their respective corners in the family pew, and in

[082-073]their wildest dreams after the happiness of novelty never thought of

[082-074]Sir Cresswell Cresswell. In some respects—with regard, for instance,

[082-075]to the continued duration of their joint domesticity at the family

[082-076]mansion of Greshamsbury—they might have been taken for a pattern

[082-077]couple. But yet, as far as the doctor could see, they did not seem

[082-078]to add much to the happiness of each other. They loved each other,

[082-079]doubtless, and had either of them been in real danger, that danger

[082-080]would have made the other miserable; but yet it might well be a

[082-081]question whether either would not be more comfortable without the

[082-082]other.

[082-083]The doctor, as was his custom, dined at five, and at seven he went

[082-084]up to the cottage of his old friend Lady Scatcherd. Lady Scatcherd

[082-085]was not a refined woman, having in her early days been a labourer’s

[082-086]daughter and having then married a labourer. But her husband had

[082-087]risen in the world—as has been told in those chronicles before

[082-088]mentioned,—and his widow was now Lady Scatcherd with a pretty

[082-089]cottage and a good jointure. She was in all things the very opposite

[082-090]to Lady Arabella Gresham; nevertheless, under the doctor’s auspices,

[082-091]the two ladies were in some measure acquainted with each other. Of

[082-092]her married life, also, Dr. Thorne had seen something, and it may

[082-093]be questioned whether the memory of that was more alluring than the

[082-094]reality now existing at Greshamsbury.

[082-095]Of the two women Dr. Thorne much preferred his humbler friend, and

[082-096]to her he made his visits not in the guise of a doctor, but as a

[082-097]neighbour. “Well, my lady,” he said, as he sat down by her on a broad

[082-098]garden seat—all the world called Lady Scatcherd “my lady,”—“and how

[082-099]do these long summer days agree with you? Your roses are twice better

[082-100]out than any I see up at the big house.”

[082-101]“You may well call them long, doctor. They’re long enough surely.”

[082-102]“But not too long. Come, now, I won’t have you complaining. You don’t

[082-103]mean to tell me that you have anything to make you wretched? You had

[082-104]better not, for I won’t believe you.”

[082-105]“Eh; well; wretched! I don’t know as I’m wretched. It’d be wicked to

[082-106]say that, and I with such comforts about me.”

[082-107]“I think it would, almost.” The doctor did not say this harshly, but

[082-108]in a soft, friendly tone, and pressing her hand gently as he spoke.

[082-109]“And I didn’t mean to be wicked. I’m very thankful for

[082-110]everything—leastways, I always try to be. But, doctor, it is so

[082-111]lonely like.”

[082-112]“Lonely! not more lonely than I am.”

[082-113]“Oh, yes; you’re different. You can go everywheres. But what can a

[082-114]lone woman do? I’ll tell you what, doctor; I’d give it all up to have

[082-115]Roger back with his apron on and his pick in his hand. How well I

[082-116]mind his look when he’d come home o’ nights.”

[082-117]“And yet it was a hard life you had then, eh, old woman? It would be

[082-118]better for you to be thankful for what you’ve got.”

[082-119]“I am thankful. Didn’t I tell you so before?” said she, somewhat

[082-120]crossly. “But it’s a sad life, this living alone. I declares I envy

[082-121]Hannah, ’cause she’s got Jemima to sit in the kitchen with her. I

[082-122]want her to sit with me sometimes, but she won’t.”

[082-123]“Ah! but you shouldn’t ask her. It’s letting yourself down.”

[082-124]“What do I care about down or up? It makes no difference, as he’s

[082-125]gone. If he had lived one might have cared about being up, as you

[082-126]call it. Eh, deary; I’ll be going after him before long, and it will

[082-127]be no matter then.”

[082-128]“We shall all be going after him, sooner or later; that’s sure

[082-129]enough.”

[082-130]“Eh, dear, that’s true, surely. It’s only a span long, as Parson

[082-131]Oriel tells us when he gets romantic in his sermons. But it’s a hard

[082-132]thing, doctor, when two is married, as they can’t have their span,

[082-133]as he calls it, out together. Well, I must only put up with it, I

[082-134]suppose, as others does. Now, you’re not going, doctor? You’ll stop

[082-135]and have a dish of tea with me. You never see such cream as Hannah

[082-136]has from the Alderney cow. Do’ey now, doctor.”

[082-137]But the doctor had his letter to write, and would not allow himself

[082-138]to be tempted even by the promise of Hannah’s cream. So he went

[082-139]his way, angering Lady Scatcherd by his departure as he had

[082-140]before angered the squire, and thinking as he went which was most

[082-141]unreasonable in her wretchedness, his friend Lady Arabella, or his

[082-142]friend Lady Scatcherd. The former was always complaining of an

[082-143]existing husband who never refused her any moderate request; and the

[082-144]other passed her days in murmuring at the loss of a dead husband,

[082-145]who in his life had ever been to her imperious and harsh, and had

[082-146]sometimes been cruel and unjust.

[082-147]The doctor had his letter to write, but even yet he had not quite

[082-148]made up his mind what he would put into it; indeed, he had not

[082-149]hitherto resolved to whom it should be written. Looking at the matter

[082-150]as he had endeavoured to look at it, his niece, Mrs. Gresham, would

[082-151]be his correspondent; but if he brought himself to take this jump

[082-152]in the dark, in that case he would address himself direct to Miss

[082-153]Dunstable.

[082-154]He walked home, not by the straightest road, but taking a

[082-155]considerable curve, round by narrow lanes, and through thick

[082-156]flower-laden hedges,—very thoughtful. He was told that she wished to

[082-157]marry him; and was he to think only of himself? And as to that pride

[082-158]of his about money, was it in truth a hearty, manly feeling; or was

[082-159]it a false pride, of which it behoved him to be ashamed as it did

[082-160]of many cognate feelings? If he acted rightly in this matter, why

[082-161]should he be afraid of the thoughts of any one? A life of solitude

[082-162]was bitter enough, as poor Lady Scatcherd had complained. But then,

[082-163]looking at Lady Scatcherd, and looking also at his other near

[082-164]neighbour, his friend the squire, there was little thereabouts to

[082-165]lead him on to matrimony. So he walked home slowly through the lanes,

[082-166]very meditative, with his hands behind his back.

[082-167]Nor when he got home was he much more inclined to any resolute line

[082-168]of action. He might have drunk his tea with Lady Scatcherd, as well

[082-169]as have sat there in his own drawing-room, drinking it alone; for he

[082-170]got no pen and paper, and he dawdled over his teacup with the utmost

[082-171]dilatoriness, putting off, as it were, the evil day. To only one

[082-172]thing was he fixed—to this, namely, that that letter should be

[082-173]written before he went to bed.

[082-174]Having finished his tea, which did not take place till near eleven,

[082-175]he went downstairs to an untidy little room which lay behind his

[082-176]depôt of medicines, and in which he was wont to do his writing;

[082-177]and herein he did at last set himself down to his work. Even at

[082-178]that moment he was in doubt. But he would write his letter to Miss

[082-179]Dunstable and see how it looked. He was almost determined not to send

[082-180]it; so, at least, he said to himself: but he could do no harm by
