## Section 76

[076-001]Miss Robarts, though she had mentioned her intention of staying with

[076-002]Mrs. Crawley, had said nothing of the Framley plan with reference to

[076-003]the children.

[076-004]“What you mean is that you intend to take the burden off my

[076-005]shoulders—in fact, to pay for them. I cannot allow that, Arabin.

[076-006]They must take the lot of their father and their mother, as it is

[076-007]proper that they should do.”

[076-008]Again the dean had no inclination for arguing, and thought it might

[076-009]be well to let the question of the children drop for a little while.

[076-010]“And is there no nurse with her?” said he.

[076-011]“No, no; I am seeing to her myself at the present moment. A woman

[076-012]will be here just now.”

[076-013]“What woman?”

[076-014]“Well; her name is Mrs. Stubbs; she lives in the parish. She will put

[076-015]the younger children to bed, and—and—but it’s no use troubling you

[076-016]with all that. There was a young lady talked of coming, but no doubt

[076-017]she has found it too inconvenient. It will be better as it is.”

[076-018]“You mean Miss Robarts; she will be here directly; I passed her as

[076-019]I came here;” and as Dr. Arabin was yet speaking, the noise of the

[076-020]carriage wheels was heard upon the road.

[076-021]“I will go in now,” said Mr. Crawley, “and see if she still sleeps;”

[076-022]and then he entered the house, leaving the dean at the door still

[076-023]seated upon his horse. “He will be afraid of the infection, and I

[076-024]will not ask him to come in,” said Mr. Crawley to himself.

[076-025]“I shall seem to be prying into his poverty, if I enter unasked,”

[076-026]said the dean to himself. And so he remained there till Puck, now

[076-027]acquainted with the locality, stopped at the door.

[076-028]“Have you not been in?” said Robarts.

[076-029]“No; Crawley has been at the door talking to me; he will be here

[076-030]directly, I suppose;” and then Mark Robarts also prepared himself to

[076-031]wait till the master of the house should reappear.

[076-032]But Lucy had no such punctilious misgivings; she did not much care

[076-033]now whether she offended Mr. Crawley or no. Her idea was to place

[076-034]herself by the sick woman’s bedside, and to send the four children

[076-035]away;—with their father’s consent if it might be; but certainly

[076-036]without it if that consent were withheld. So she got down from the

[076-037]carriage, and taking certain packages in her hand made her way direct

[076-038]into the house.

[076-039]“There’s a big bundle under the seat, Mark,” she said; “I’ll come and

[076-040]fetch it directly, if you’ll drag it out.”

[076-041]For some five minutes the two dignitaries of the Church remained at

[076-042]the door, one on his cob and the other in his low carriage, saying a

[076-043]few words to each other and waiting till some one should again appear

[076-044]from the house. “It is all arranged, indeed it is,” were the first

[076-045]words which reached their ears, and these came from Lucy. “There will

[076-046]be no trouble at all, and no expense, and they shall all come back as

[076-047]soon as Mrs. Crawley is able to get out of bed.”

[076-048]“But, Miss Robarts, I can assure—” That was Mr. Crawley’s voice,

[076-049]heard from him as he followed Miss Robarts to the door; but one of

[076-050]the elder children had then called him into the sick room, and Lucy

[076-051]was left to do her worst.

[076-052]“Are you going to take the children back with you?” said the dean.

[076-053]“Yes; Mrs. Robarts has prepared for them.”

[076-054]“You can take greater liberties with my friend here than I can.”

[076-055]“It is all my sister’s doing,” said Robarts. “Women are always bolder

[076-056]in such matters than men.” And then Lucy reappeared, bringing Bobby

[076-057]with her, and one of the younger children.

[076-058]“Do not mind what he says,” said she, “but drive away when you have

[076-059]got them all. Tell Fanny I have put into the basket what things I

[076-060]could find, but they are very few. She must borrow things for Grace

[076-061]from Mrs. Granger’s little girl”—(Mrs. Granger was the wife of a

[076-062]Framley farmer);—“and, Mark, turn Puck’s head round, so that you may

[076-063]be off in a moment. I’ll have Grace and the other one here directly.”

[076-064]And then, leaving her brother to pack Bobby and his little sister on

[076-065]the back part of the vehicle, she returned to her business in the

[076-066]house. She had just looked in at Mrs. Crawley’s bed, and finding her

[076-067]awake, had smiled on her, and deposited her bundle in token of her

[076-068]intended stay, and then, without speaking a word, had gone on her

[076-069]errand about the children. She had called to Grace to show her where

[076-070]she might find such things as were to be taken to Framley, and having

[076-071]explained to the bairns, as well as she might, the destiny which

[076-072]immediately awaited them, prepared them for their departure without

[076-073]saying a word to Mr. Crawley on the subject. Bobby and the elder

[076-074]of the two infants were stowed away safely in the back part of the

[076-075]carriage, where they allowed themselves to be placed without saying

[076-076]a word. They opened their eyes and stared at the dean, who sat by on

[076-077]his horse, and assented to such orders as Mr. Robarts gave them,—no

[076-078]doubt with much surprise, but nevertheless in absolute silence.

[076-079]“Now, Grace, be quick, there’s a dear,” said Lucy, returning with

[076-080]the infant in her arms. “And, Grace, mind you are very careful about

[076-081]baby; and bring the basket; I’ll give it you when you are in.” Grace

[076-082]and the other child were then packed on to the other seat, and a

[076-083]basket with children’s clothes put in on the top of them. “That’ll

[076-084]do, Mark; good-bye; tell Fanny to be sure and send the day after

[076-085]to-morrow, and not to forget—” and then she whispered into her

[076-086]brother’s ear an injunction about certain dairy comforts which might

[076-087]not be spoken of in the hearing of Mr. Crawley. “Good-bye, dears;

[076-088]mind you are good children; you shall hear about mamma the day after

[076-089]to-morrow,” said Lucy; and Puck, admonished by a sound from his

[076-090]master’s voice, began to move just as Mr. Crawley reappeared at the

[076-091]house door.

[076-092]“Oh, oh, stop!” he said. “Miss Robarts, you really had better not—”

[076-093]“Go on, Mark,” said Lucy, in a whisper, which, whether audible or not

[076-094]by Mr. Crawley, was heard very plainly by the dean. And Mark, who had

[076-095]slightly arrested Puck by the reins on the appearance of Mr. Crawley,

[076-096]now touched the impatient little beast with his whip; and the vehicle

[076-097]with its freight darted off rapidly, Puck shaking his head and going

[076-098]away with a tremendously quick short trot which soon separated Mr.

[076-099]Crawley from his family.

[076-100]“Miss Robarts,” he began, “this step has been taken altogether

[076-101]without—”

[076-102]“Yes,” said she, interrupting him. “My brother was obliged to return

[076-103]at once. The children, you know, will remain all together at the

[076-104]parsonage; and that, I think, is what Mrs. Crawley will best like. In

[076-105]a day or two they will be under Mrs. Robarts’s own charge.”

[076-106]“But, my dear Miss Robarts, I had no intention whatever of putting

[076-107]the burden of my family on the shoulders of another person. They must

[076-108]return to their own home immediately—that is, as soon as they can be

[076-109]brought back.”

[076-110]“I really think Miss Robarts has managed very well,” said the dean.

[076-111]“Mrs. Crawley must be so much more comfortable to think that they are

[076-112]out of danger.”

[076-113]“And they will be quite comfortable at the parsonage,” said Lucy.

[076-114]“I do not at all doubt that,” said Mr. Crawley; “but too much of such

[076-115]comforts will unfit them for their home; and—and I could have wished

[076-116]that I had been consulted more at leisure before the proceeding had

[076-117]been taken.”

[076-118]“It was arranged, Mr. Crawley, when I was here before, that the

[076-119]children had better go away,” pleaded Lucy.

[076-120]“I do not remember agreeing to such a measure, Miss Robarts;

[076-121]however— I suppose they cannot be had back to-night?”

[076-122]“No, not to-night,” said Lucy. “And now I will go in to your wife.”

[076-123]And then she returned to the house, leaving the two gentlemen at the

[076-124]door. At this moment a labourer’s boy came sauntering by, and the

[076-125]dean, obtaining possession of his services for the custody of his

[076-126]horse, was able to dismount and put himself on a more equal footing

[076-127]for conversation with his friend.

[076-128]“Crawley,” said he, putting his hand affectionately on his friend’s

[076-129]shoulder, as they both stood leaning on the little rail before the

[076-130]door; “that is a good girl—a very good girl.”

[076-131]“Yes,” said he slowly; “she means well.”

[076-132]“Nay, but she does well; she does excellently. What can be better

[076-133]than her conduct now? While I was meditating how I might possibly

[076-134]assist your wife in this strait—”

[076-135]“I want no assistance; none, at least, from man,” said Crawley,

[076-136]bitterly.

[076-137]“Oh, my friend, think of what you are saying! Think of the wickedness

[076-138]which must accompany such a state of mind! Have you ever known any

[076-139]man able to walk alone, without assistance from his brother men?”

[076-140]Mr. Crawley did not make any immediate answer, but putting his arms

[076-141]behind his back and closing his hands, as was his wont when he walked

[076-142]alone thinking of the general bitterness of his lot in life, began to

[076-143]move slowly along the road in front of his house. He did not invite

[076-144]the other to walk with him, but neither was there anything in his

[076-145]manner which seemed to indicate that he had intended to be left to

[076-146]himself. It was a beautiful summer afternoon, at that delicious

[076-147]period of the year when summer has just burst forth from the growth

[076-148]of spring; when the summer is yet but three days old, and all the

[076-149]various shades of green which nature can put forth are still in their

[076-150]unsoiled purity of freshness. The apple blossoms were on the trees,

[076-151]and the hedges were sweet with May. The cuckoo at five o’clock was

[076-152]still sounding his soft summer call with unabated energy, and even

[076-153]the common grasses of the hedgerows were sweet with the fragrance of

[076-154]their new growth. The foliage of the oaks was complete, so that every

[076-155]bough and twig was clothed; but the leaves did not yet hang heavy in

[076-156]masses, and the bend of every bough and the tapering curve of every

[076-157]twig were visible through their light green covering. There is no

[076-158]time of the year equal in beauty to the first week in summer; and

[076-159]no colour which nature gives, not even the gorgeous hues of autumn,

[076-160]which can equal the verdure produced by the first warm suns of May.

[076-161]Hogglestock, as has been explained, has little to offer in the way

[076-162]of landskip beauty, and the clergyman’s house at Hogglestock was not

[076-163]placed on a green slopy bank of land, retired from the road, with its

[076-164]windows opening on to a lawn, surrounded by shrubs, with a view of

[076-165]the small church tower seen through them; it had none of that beauty

[076-166]which is so common to the cozy houses of our spiritual pastors in

[076-167]the agricultural parts of England. Hogglestock Parsonage stood bleak

[076-168]beside the road, with no pretty paling lined inside by hollies and

[076-169]laburnum, Portugal laurels and rose-trees. But, nevertheless, even

[076-170]Hogglestock was pretty now. There were apple-trees there covered with

[076-171]blossom, and the hedgerows were in full flower. There were thrushes

[076-172]singing, and here and there an oak-tree stood in the roadside,

[076-173]perfect in its solitary beauty.

[076-174]“Let us walk on a little,” said the dean. “Miss Robarts is with her

[076-175]now, and you will be better for leaving the room for a few minutes.”

[076-176]“No,” said he; “I must go back; I cannot leave that young lady to do

[076-177]my work.”

[076-178]“Stop, Crawley!” And the dean, putting his hand upon him, stayed him

[076-179]in the road. “She is doing her own work, and if you were speaking of

[076-180]her with reference to any other household than your own, you would

[076-181]say so. Is it not a comfort to you to know that your wife has a woman

[076-182]near her at such a time as this; and a woman, too, who can speak to

[076-183]her as one lady does to another?”
