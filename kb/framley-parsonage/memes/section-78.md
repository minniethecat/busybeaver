## Section 78

[078-001]It was soon put beyond a rumour, and became manifest enough, that Mr.

[078-002]Sowerby did not intend to retire from the county in obedience to the

[078-003]duke’s behests. A placard was posted through the whole division in

[078-004]which no allusion was made by name to the duke, but in which Mr.

[078-005]Sowerby warned his friends not to be led away by any report that he

[078-006]intended to retire from the representation of West Barsetshire. “He

[078-007]had sat,” the placard said, “for the same county during the full

[078-008]period of a quarter of a century, and he would not lightly give up an

[078-009]honour that had been extended to him so often and which he prized so

[078-010]dearly. There were but few men now in the House whose connection with

[078-011]the same body of constituents had remained unbroken so long as had

[078-012]that which bound him to West Barsetshire; and he confidently hoped

[078-013]that that connection might be continued through another period of

[078-014]coming years till he might find himself in the glorious position of

[078-015]being the father of the county members of the House of Commons.” The

[078-016]placard said much more than this, and hinted at sundry and various

[078-017]questions, all of great interest to the county; but it did not say

[078-018]one word of the Duke of Omnium, though every one knew what the

[078-019]duke was supposed to be doing in the matter. He was, as it were, a

[078-020]great Llama, shut up in a holy of holies, inscrutable, invisible,

[078-021]inexorable,—not to be seen by men’s eyes or heard by their ears,

[078-022]hardly to be mentioned by ordinary men at such periods as these

[078-023]without an inward quaking. But nevertheless, it was he who was

[078-024]supposed to rule them. Euphemism required that his name should

[078-025]be mentioned at no public meetings in connection with the coming

[078-026]election; but, nevertheless, most men in the county believed that

[078-027]he could send his dog up to the House of Commons as member for West

[078-028]Barsetshire if it so pleased him.

[078-029]It was supposed, therefore, that our friend Sowerby would have no

[078-030]chance; but he was lucky in finding assistance in a quarter from

[078-031]which he certainly had not deserved it. He had been a staunch friend

[078-032]of the gods during the whole of his political life,—as, indeed, was

[078-033]to be expected, seeing that he had been the duke’s nominee; but,

[078-034]nevertheless, on the present occasion, all the giants connected with

[078-035]the county came forward to his rescue. They did not do this with the

[078-036]acknowledged purpose of opposing the duke; they declared that they

[078-037]were actuated by a generous disinclination to see an old county

[078-038]member put from his seat;—but the world knew that the battle was to

[078-039]be waged against the great Llama. It was to be a contest between the

[078-040]powers of aristocracy and the powers of oligarchy, as those powers

[078-041]existed in West Barsetshire,—and, it may be added, that democracy

[078-042]would have very little to say to it, on one side or on the other.

[078-043]The lower order of voters, the small farmers and tradesmen, would no

[078-044]doubt range themselves on the side of the duke, and would endeavour

[078-045]to flatter themselves that they were thereby furthering the views of

[078-046]the liberal side; but they would in fact be led to the poll by an

[078-047]old-fashioned, time-honoured adherence to the will of their great

[078-048]Llama; and by an apprehension of evil if that Llama should arise and

[078-049]shake himself in his wrath. What might not come to the county if the

[078-050]Llama were to walk himself off, he with his satellites and armies and

[078-051]courtiers? There he was, a great Llama; and though he came among them

[078-052]but seldom, and was scarcely seen when he did come, nevertheless—and

[078-053]not the less but rather the more—was obedience to him considered as

[078-054]salutary and opposition regarded as dangerous. A great rural Llama is

[078-055]still sufficiently mighty in rural England.

[078-056]But the priest of the temple, Mr. Fothergill, was frequent enough

[078-057]in men’s eyes, and it was beautiful to hear with how varied a voice

[078-058]he alluded to the things around him and to the changes which were

[078-059]coming. To the small farmers, not only on the Gatherum property

[078-060]but on others also, he spoke of the duke as a beneficent influence,

[078-061]shedding prosperity on all around him, keeping up prices by his

[078-062]presence, and forbidding the poor rates to rise above one and

[078-063]fourpence in the pound by the general employment which he occasioned.

[078-064]Men must be mad, he thought, who would willingly fly in the duke’s

[078-065]face. To the squires from a distance he declared that no one had a

[078-066]right to charge the duke with any interference;—as far, at least, as

[078-067]he knew the duke’s mind. People would talk of things of which they

[078-068]understood nothing. Could any one say that he had traced a single

[078-069]request for a vote home to the duke? All this did not alter the

[078-070]settled conviction on men’s minds; but it had its effect, and tended

[078-071]to increase the mystery in which the duke’s doings were enveloped.

[078-072]But to his own familiars, to the gentry immediately around him, Mr.

[078-073]Fothergill merely winked his eye. They knew what was what, and so

[078-074]did he. The duke had never been bit yet in such matters, and Mr.

[078-075]Fothergill did not think that he would now submit himself to any such

[078-076]operation.

[078-077]I never heard in what manner and at what rate Mr. Fothergill received

[078-078]remuneration for the various services performed by him with reference

[078-079]to the duke’s property in Barsetshire; but I am very sure that,

[078-080]whatever might be the amount, he earned it thoroughly. Never was

[078-081]there a more faithful partisan, or one who, in his partisanship, was

[078-082]more discreet. In this matter of the coming election he declared

[078-083]that he himself,—personally, on his own hook,—did intend to bestir

[078-084]himself actively on behalf of Lord Dumbello. Mr. Sowerby was an old

[078-085]friend of his, and a very good fellow. That was true. But all the

[078-086]world must admit that Sowerby was not in the position which a county

[078-087]member ought to occupy. He was a ruined man, and it would not be for

[078-088]his own advantage that he should be maintained in a position which

[078-089]was fit only for a man of property. He knew—he, Fothergill—that Mr.

[078-090]Sowerby must abandon all right and claim to Chaldicotes; and if so,

[078-091]what would be more absurd than to acknowledge that he had a right and

[078-092]claim to the seat in Parliament? As to Lord Dumbello, it was probable

[078-093]that he would soon become one of the largest landowners in the

[078-094]county; and, as such, who could be more fit for the representation?

[078-095]Beyond this, Mr. Fothergill was not ashamed to confess—so he

[078-096]said—that he hoped to hold Lord Dumbello’s agency. It would be

[078-097]compatible with his other duties, and therefore, as a matter of

[078-098]course, he intended to support Lord Dumbello;—he himself, that is.

[078-099]As to the duke’s mind in the matter—! But I have already explained

[078-100]how Mr. Fothergill disposed of that.

[078-101]In these days, Mr. Sowerby came down to his own house—for ostensibly

[078-102]it was still his own house—but he came very quietly, and his arrival

[078-103]was hardly known in his own village. Though his placard was stuck

[078-104]up so widely, he himself took no electioneering steps; none, at

[078-105]least, as yet. The protection against arrest which he derived from

[078-106]Parliament would soon be over, and those who were most bitter against

[078-107]the duke averred that steps would be taken to arrest him, should he

[078-108]give sufficient opportunity to the myrmidons of the law. That he

[078-109]would, in such case, be arrested was very likely; but it was not

[078-110]likely that this would be done in any way at the duke’s instance. Mr.

[078-111]Fothergill declared indignantly that this insinuation made him very

[078-112]angry; but he was too prudent a man to be very angry at anything, and

[078-113]he knew how to make capital on his own side of charges such as these

[078-114]which overshot their own mark.

[078-115]Mr. Sowerby came down very quietly to Chaldicotes, and there he

[078-116]remained for a couple of days, quite alone. The place bore a very

[078-117]different aspect now to that which we noticed when Mark Robarts drove

[078-118]up to it, in the early pages of this little narrative. There were no

[078-119]lights in the windows now, and no voices came from the stables; no

[078-120]dogs barked, and all was dead and silent as the grave. During the

[078-121]greater portion of those two days he sat alone within the house,

[078-122]almost unoccupied. He did not even open his letters, which lay piled

[078-123]on a crowded table in the small breakfast parlour in which he sat;

[078-124]for the letters of such men come in piles, and there are few of

[078-125]them which are pleasant in the reading. There he sat, troubled with

[078-126]thoughts which were sad enough, now and then moving to and fro the

[078-127]house, but for the most part occupied in thinking over the position

[078-128]to which he had brought himself. What would he be in the world’s

[078-129]eye, if he ceased to be the owner of Chaldicotes, and ceased also

[078-130]to be the member for his county? He had lived ever before the world,

[078-131]and, though always harassed by encumbrances, had been sustained and

[078-132]comforted by the excitement of a prominent position. His debts and

[078-133]difficulties had hitherto been bearable, and he had borne them with

[078-134]ease so long that he had almost taught himself to think that they

[078-135]would never be unendurable. But now,—

[078-136]The order for foreclosing had gone forth, and the harpies of the law,

[078-137]by their present speed in sticking their claws into the carcase of

[078-138]his property, were atoning to themselves for the delay with which

[078-139]they had hitherto been compelled to approach their prey. And the

[078-140]order as to his seat had gone forth also. That placard had been drawn

[078-141]up by the combined efforts of his sister, Miss Dunstable, and a

[078-142]certain well-known electioneering agent, named Closerstill, presumed

[078-143]to be in the interest of the giants. But poor Sowerby had but little

[078-144]confidence in the placard. No one knew better than he how great was

[078-145]the duke’s power.

[078-146]He was hopeless, therefore, as he walked about through those empty

[078-147]rooms, thinking of his past life and of that life which was to come.

[078-148]Would it not be well for him that he were dead, now that he was dying

[078-149]to all that had made the world pleasant! We see and hear of such men

[078-150]as Mr. Sowerby, and are apt to think that they enjoy all that the

[078-151]world can give, and that they enjoy that all without payment either

[078-152]in care or labour; but I doubt that, with even the most callous

[078-153]of them, their periods of wretchedness must be frequent, and that

[078-154]wretchedness very intense. Salmon and lamb in February and green

[078-155]pease and new potatoes in March can hardly make a man happy,

[078-156]even though nobody pays for them; and the feeling that one is an

[078-157]_antecedentem scelestum_ after whom a sure, though lame, Nemesis

[078-158]is hobbling, must sometimes disturb one’s slumbers. On the present

[078-159]occasion Scelestus felt that his Nemesis had overtaken him. Lame as

[078-160]she had been, and swift as he had run, she had mouthed him at last,

[078-161]and there was nothing left for him but to listen to the “whoop” set

[078-162]up at the sight of his own death-throes.

[078-163]It was a melancholy, dreary place now, that big house of Chaldicotes;

[078-164]and though the woods were all green with their early leaves, and the

[078-165]gardens thick with flowers, they also were melancholy and dreary.

[078-166]The lawns were untrimmed and weeds were growing through the gravel,

[078-167]and here and there a cracked Dryad, tumbled from her pedestal and

[078-168]sprawling in the grass, gave a look of disorder to the whole place.

[078-169]The wooden trellis-work was shattered here and bending there, the

[078-170]standard rose-trees were stooping to the ground, and the leaves of

[078-171]the winter still encumbered the borders. Late in the evening of the

[078-172]second day Mr. Sowerby strolled out, and went through the gardens

[078-173]into the wood. Of all the inanimate things of the world this wood

[078-174]of Chaldicotes was the dearest to him. He was not a man to whom his

[078-175]companions gave much credit for feelings or thoughts akin to poetry,

[078-176]but here, out in the Chace, his mind would be almost poetical. While

[078-177]wandering among the forest trees, he became susceptible of the

[078-178]tenderness of human nature: he would listen to the birds singing,

[078-179]and pick here and there a wild flower on his path. He would watch

[078-180]the decay of the old trees and the progress of the young, and make

[078-181]pictures in his eyes of every turn in the wood. He would mark the

[078-182]colour of a bit of road as it dipped into a dell, and then, passing

[078-183]through a water-course, rose brown, rough, irregular, and beautiful

[078-184]against the bank on the other side. And then he would sit and think

[078-185]of his old family: how they had roamed there time out of mind in

[078-186]those Chaldicotes woods, father and son and grandson in regular

[078-187]succession, each giving them over, without blemish or decrease, to

[078-188]his successor. So he would sit; and so he did sit even now, and,

[078-189]thinking of these things, wished that he had never been born.
