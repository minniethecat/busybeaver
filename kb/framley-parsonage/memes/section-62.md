## Section 62

[062-001]“Conversaziones will come to mean nothing,” she said to the bishop,

[062-002]putting great stress on the latter word, “nothing at all, if they are

[062-003]to be treated in this way.”

[062-004]“No, they won’t; nothing in the least,” said the bishop.

[062-005]“Dancing may be very well in its place,” said Mrs. Proudie.

[062-006]“I have never objected to it myself; that is, for the laity,” said

[062-007]the bishop.

[062-008]“But when people profess to assemble for higher objects,” said Mrs.

[062-009]Proudie, “they ought to act up to their professions.”

[062-010]“Otherwise they are no better than hypocrites,” said the bishop.

[062-011]“A spade should be called a spade,” said Mrs. Proudie.

[062-012]“Decidedly,” said the bishop, assenting.

[062-013]“And when I undertook the trouble and expense of introducing

[062-014]conversaziones,” continued Mrs. Proudie, with an evident feeling that

[062-015]she had been ill-used, “I had no idea of seeing the word so—so—so

[062-016]misinterpreted;” and then observing certain desirable acquaintances

[062-017]at the other side of the room, she went across, leaving the bishop to

[062-018]fend for himself.

[062-019]Lady Lufton, having achieved her success, passed on to the dancing,

[062-020]whither it was not probable that her enemy would follow her, and she

[062-021]had not been there very long before she was joined by her son. Her

[062-022]heart at the present moment was not quite satisfied at the state of

[062-023]affairs with reference to Griselda. She had gone so far as to tell

[062-024]her young friend what were her own wishes; she had declared her

[062-025]desire that Griselda should become her daughter-in-law; but in answer

[062-026]to this Griselda herself had declared nothing. It was, to be sure,

[062-027]no more than natural that a young lady so well brought up as Miss

[062-028]Grantly should show no signs of a passion till she was warranted in

[062-029]showing them by the proceedings of the gentleman; but notwithstanding

[062-030]this—fully aware as she was of the propriety of such reticence—Lady

[062-031]Lufton did think that to her Griselda might have spoken some word

[062-032]evincing that the alliance would be satisfactory to her. Griselda,

[062-033]however, had spoken no such word, nor had she uttered a syllable to

[062-034]show that she would accept Lord Lufton if he did offer. Then again

[062-035]she had uttered no syllable to show that she would not accept him;

[062-036]but, nevertheless, although she knew that the world had been talking

[062-037]about her and Lord Dumbello, she stood up to dance with the future

[062-038]marquess on every possible occasion. All this did give annoyance

[062-039]to Lady Lufton, who began to bethink herself that if she could not

[062-040]quickly bring her little plan to a favourable issue, it might be well

[062-041]for her to wash her hands of it. She was still anxious for the match

[062-042]on her son’s account. Griselda would, she did not doubt, make a good

[062-043]wife; but Lady Lufton was not so sure as she once had been that

[062-044]she herself would be able to keep up so strong a feeling for her

[062-045]daughter-in-law as she had hitherto hoped to do.

[062-046]“Ludovic, have you been here long?” she said, smiling as she always

[062-047]did smile when her eyes fell upon her son’s face.

[062-048]“This instant arrived; and I hurried on after you, as Miss Dunstable

[062-049]told me that you were here. What a crowd she has! Did you see Lord

[062-050]Brock?”

[062-051]“I did not observe him.”

[062-052]“Or Lord De Terrier? I saw them both in the centre room.”

[062-053]“Lord De Terrier did me the honour of shaking hands with me as I

[062-054]passed through.”

[062-055]“I never saw such a mixture of people. There is Mrs. Proudie going

[062-056]out of her mind because you are all going to dance.”

[062-057]“The Miss Proudies dance,” said Griselda Grantly.

[062-058]“But not at conversaziones. You don’t see the difference. And I saw

[062-059]Spermoil there, looking as pleased as Punch. He had quite a circle of

[062-060]his own round him, and was chattering away as though he were quite

[062-061]accustomed to the wickednesses of the world.”

[062-062]“There certainly are people here whom one would not have wished to

[062-063]meet, had one thought of it,” said Lady Lufton, mindful of her late

[062-064]engagement.

[062-065]“But it must be all right, for I walked up the stairs with the

[062-066]archdeacon. That is an absolute proof, is it not, Miss Grantly?”

[062-067]“I have no fears. When I am with your mother I know I must be safe.”

[062-068]“I am not so sure of that,” said Lord Lufton, laughing. “Mother, you

[062-069]hardly know the worst of it yet. Who is here, do you think?”

[062-070]“I know whom you mean; I have seen him,” said Lady Lufton, very

[062-071]quietly.

[062-072]“We came across him just at the top of the stairs,” said Griselda,

[062-073]with more animation in her face than ever Lord Lufton had seen there

[062-074]before.

[062-075]“What; the duke?”

[062-076]“Yes, the duke,” said Lady Lufton. “I certainly should not have come

[062-077]had I expected to be brought in contact with that man. But it was an

[062-078]accident, and on such an occasion as this it could not be helped.”

[062-079]Lord Lufton at once perceived, by the tone of his mother’s voice and

[062-080]by the shades of her countenance that she had absolutely endured some

[062-081]personal encounter with the duke, and also that she was by no means

[062-082]so indignant at the occurrence as might have been expected. There she

[062-083]was, still in Miss Dunstable’s house, and expressing no anger as to

[062-084]Miss Dunstable’s conduct. Lord Lufton could hardly have been more

[062-085]surprised had he seen the duke handing his mother down to supper; he

[062-086]said, however, nothing further on the subject.

[062-087]“Are you going to dance, Ludovic?” said Lady Lufton.

[062-088]“Well, I am not sure that I do not agree with Mrs. Proudie in

[062-089]thinking that dancing would contaminate a conversazione. What are

[062-090]your ideas, Miss Grantly?”

[062-091]Griselda was never very good at a joke, and imagined that Lord Lufton

[062-092]wanted to escape the trouble of dancing with her. This angered her.

[062-093]For the only species of love-making, or flirtation, or sociability

[062-094]between herself as a young lady, and any other self as a young

[062-095]gentleman, which recommended itself to her taste, was to be found in

[062-096]the amusement of dancing. She was altogether at variance with Mrs.

[062-097]Proudie on this matter, and gave Miss Dunstable great credit for her

[062-098]innovation. In society Griselda’s toes were more serviceable to her

[062-099]than her tongue, and she was to be won by a rapid twirl much more

[062-100]probably than by a soft word. The offer of which she would approve

[062-101]would be conveyed by two all but breathless words during a spasmodic

[062-102]pause in a waltz; and then as she lifted up her arm to receive the

[062-103]accustomed support at her back, she might just find power enough to

[062-104]say, “You—must ask—papa.” After that she would not care to have the

[062-105]affair mentioned till everything was properly settled.

[062-106]“I have not thought about it,” said Griselda, turning her face away

[062-107]from Lord Lufton.

[062-108]It must not, however, be supposed that Miss Grantly had not thought

[062-109]about Lord Lufton, or that she had not considered how great might be

[062-110]the advantage of having Lady Lufton on her side if she made up her

[062-111]mind that she did wish to become Lord Lufton’s wife. She knew well

[062-112]that now was her time for a triumph, now in this very first season of

[062-113]her acknowledged beauty; and she knew also that young, good-looking

[062-114]bachelor lords do not grow on hedges like blackberries. Had Lord

[062-115]Lufton offered to her, she would have accepted him at once without

[062-116]any remorse as to the greater glories which might appertain to a

[062-117]future Marchioness of Hartletop. In that direction she was not

[062-118]without sufficient wisdom. But then Lord Lufton had not offered

[062-119]to her, nor given any signs that he intended to do so; and to

[062-120]give Griselda Grantly her due, she was not a girl to make a first

[062-121]overture. Neither had Lord Dumbello offered; but he had given

[062-122]signs,—dumb signs, such as birds give to each other, quite as

[062-123]intelligible as verbal signs to a girl who preferred the use of her

[062-124]toes to that of her tongue.

[062-125]“I have not thought about it,” said Griselda, very coldly, and at

[062-126]that moment a gentleman stood before her and asked her hand for the

[062-127]next dance. It was Lord Dumbello; and Griselda, making no reply

[062-128]except by a slight bow, got up and put her hand within her partner’s

[062-129]arm.

[062-130]“Shall I find you here, Lady Lufton, when we have done?” she said;

[062-131]and then started off among the dancers. When the work before one is

[062-132]dancing the proper thing for a gentleman to do is, at any rate, to

[062-133]ask a lady; this proper thing Lord Lufton had omitted, and now the

[062-134]prize was taken away from under his very nose.

[062-135]There was clearly an air of triumph about Lord Dumbello as he walked

[062-136]away with the beauty. The world had been saying that Lord Lufton was

[062-137]to marry her, and the world had also been saying that Lord Dumbello

[062-138]admired her. Now this had angered Lord Dumbello, and made him feel

[062-139]as though he walked about, a mark of scorn, as a disappointed suitor.

[062-140]Had it not been for Lord Lufton, perhaps he would not have cared so

[062-141]much for Griselda Grantly; but circumstances had so turned out that

[062-142]he did care for her, and felt it to be incumbent upon him as the

[062-143]heir to a marquisate to obtain what he wanted, let who would have a

[062-144]hankering after the same article. It is in this way that pictures are

[062-145]so well sold at auctions; and Lord Dumbello regarded Miss Grantly

[062-146]as being now subject to the auctioneer’s hammer, and conceived that

[062-147]Lord Lufton was bidding against him. There was, therefore, an air

[062-148]of triumph about him as he put his arm round Griselda’s waist and

[062-149]whirled her up and down the room in obedience to the music.

[062-150]Lady Lufton and her son were left together looking at each other.

[062-151]Of course he had intended to ask Griselda to dance, but it cannot

[062-152]be said that he very much regretted his disappointment. Of course

[062-153]also Lady Lufton had expected that her son and Griselda would stand

[062-154]up together, and she was a little inclined to be angry with her

[062-155]_protégée_.

[062-156]“I think she might have waited a minute,” said Lady Lufton.

[062-157]“But why, mother? There are certain things for which no one ever

[062-158]waits: to give a friend, for instance, the first passage through a

[062-159]gate out hunting, and such like. Miss Grantly was quite right to take

[062-160]the first that offered.”

[062-161]Lady Lufton had determined to learn what was to be the end of this

[062-162]scheme of hers. She could not have Griselda always with her, and if

[062-163]anything were to be arranged it must be arranged now, while both of

[062-164]them were in London. At the close of the season Griselda would return

[062-165]to Plumstead, and Lord Lufton would go—nobody as yet knew where. It

[062-166]would be useless to look forward to further opportunities. If they

[062-167]did not contrive to love each other now, they would never do so. Lady

[062-168]Lufton was beginning to fear that her plan would not work, but she

[062-169]made up her mind that she would learn the truth then and there,—at

[062-170]least as far as her son was concerned.

[062-171]“Oh, yes; quite so;—if it is equal to her with which she dances,”

[062-172]said Lady Lufton.

[062-173]“Quite equal, I should think—unless it be that Dumbello is

[062-174]longer-winded than I am.”

[062-175]“I am sorry to hear you speak of her in that way, Ludovic.”

[062-176]“Why sorry, mother?”

[062-177]“Because I had hoped—that you and she would have liked each other.”

[062-178]This she said in a serious tone of voice, tender and sad, looking up

[062-179]into his face with a plaintive gaze, as though she knew that she were

[062-180]asking of him some great favour.

[062-181]“Yes, mother, I have known that you have wished that.”

[062-182]“You have known it, Ludovic!”
