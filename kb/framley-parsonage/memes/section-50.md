## Section 50

[050-001]Mr. Sowerby was not fighting this campaign alone, without the aid of

[050-002]any ally. Indeed, no man ever had a more trusty ally in any campaign

[050-003]than he had in this. And it was this ally, the only faithful comrade

[050-004]that clung to him through good and ill during his whole life, who

[050-005]first put it into his head that Miss Dunstable was a woman and might

[050-006]be married.

[050-007]“A hundred needy adventurers have attempted it, and failed already,”

[050-008]Mr. Sowerby had said, when the plan was first proposed to him.

[050-009]“But, nevertheless, she will some day marry some one; and why not you

[050-010]as well as another?” his sister had answered. For Mrs. Harold Smith

[050-011]was the ally of whom I have spoken.

[050-012]Mrs. Harold Smith, whatever may have been her faults, could boast of

[050-013]this virtue—that she loved her brother. He was probably the only

[050-014]human being that she did love. Children she had none; and as for her

[050-015]husband, it had never occurred to her to love him. She had married

[050-016]him for a position; and being a clever woman, with a good digestion

[050-017]and command of her temper, had managed to get through the world

[050-018]without much of that unhappiness which usually follows ill-assorted

[050-019]marriages. At home she managed to keep the upper hand, but she did

[050-020]so in an easy, good-humoured way that made her rule bearable; and

[050-021]away from home she assisted her lord’s political standing, though

[050-022]she laughed more keenly than any one else at his foibles. But the

[050-023]lord of her heart was her brother; and in all his scrapes, all his

[050-024]extravagances, and all his recklessness, she had ever been willing to

[050-025]assist him. With the view of doing this she had sought the intimacy

[050-026]of Miss Dunstable, and for the last year past had indulged every

[050-027]caprice of that lady. Or rather, she had had the wit to learn that

[050-028]Miss Dunstable was to be won, not by the indulgence of caprices, but

[050-029]by free and easy intercourse, with a dash of fun, and, at any rate,

[050-030]a semblance of honesty. Mrs. Harold Smith was not, perhaps, herself

[050-031]very honest by disposition; but in these latter days she had taken up

[050-032]a theory of honesty for the sake of Miss Dunstable—not altogether in

[050-033]vain, for Miss Dunstable and Mrs. Harold Smith were certainly very

[050-034]intimate.

[050-035]“If I am to do it at all, I must not wait any longer,” said Mr.

[050-036]Sowerby to his sister a day or two after the final break-down of the

[050-037]gods. The affection of the sister for the brother may be imagined

[050-038]from the fact that at such a time she could give up her mind to

[050-039]such a subject. But, in truth, her husband’s position as a cabinet

[050-040]minister was as nothing to her compared with her brother’s position

[050-041]as a county gentleman.

[050-042]“One time is as good as another,” said Mrs. Harold Smith.

[050-043]“You mean that you would advise me to ask her at once.”

[050-044]“Certainly. But you must remember, Nat, that you will have no easy

[050-045]task. It will not do for you to kneel down and swear that you love

[050-046]her.”

[050-047]“If I do it at all, I shall certainly do it without kneeling—you may

[050-048]be sure of that, Harriet.”

[050-049]“Yes, and without swearing that you love her. There is only one way

[050-050]in which you can be successful with Miss Dunstable—you must tell her

[050-051]the truth.”

[050-052]“What!—tell her that I am ruined, horse, foot, and dragoons, and

[050-053]then bid her help me out of the mire?”

[050-054]“Exactly: that will be your only chance, strange as it may appear.”

[050-055]“This is very different from what you used to say, down at

[050-056]Chaldicotes.”

[050-057]“So it is; but I know her much better than I did when we were there.

[050-058]Since then I have done but little else than study the freaks of her

[050-059]character. If she really likes you—and I think she does—she could

[050-060]forgive you any other crime but that of swearing that you loved her.”

[050-061]“I should hardly know how to propose without saying something about

[050-062]it.”

[050-063]“But you must say nothing—not a word; you must tell her that you are

[050-064]a gentleman of good blood and high station, but sadly out at elbows.”

[050-065]“She knows that already.”

[050-066]“Of course she does; but she must know it as coming directly from

[050-067]your own mouth. And then tell her that you propose to set yourself

[050-068]right by marrying her—by marrying her for the sake of her money.”

[050-069]“That will hardly win her, I should say.”

[050-070]“If it does not, no other way, that I know of, will do so. As I told

[050-071]you before, it will be no easy task. Of course you must make her

[050-072]understand that her happiness shall be cared for; but that must not

[050-073]be put prominently forward as your object. Your first object is her

[050-074]money, and your only chance for success is in telling the truth.”

[050-075]“It is very seldom that a man finds himself in such a position as

[050-076]that,” said Sowerby, walking up and down his sister’s room; “and,

[050-077]upon my word, I don’t think I am up to the task. I should certainly

[050-078]break down. I don’t believe there’s a man in London could go to a

[050-079]woman with such a story as that, and then ask her to marry him.”

[050-080]“If you cannot, you may as well give it up,” said Mrs. Harold

[050-081]Smith. “But if you can do it—if you can go through with it in that

[050-082]manner—my own opinion is that your chance of success would not be

[050-083]bad. The fact is,” added the sister after awhile, during which her

[050-084]brother was continuing his walk and meditating on the difficulties

[050-085]of his position—“the fact is, you men never understand a woman; you

[050-086]give her credit neither for her strength, nor for her weakness. You

[050-087]are too bold, and too timid: you think she is a fool and tell her so,

[050-088]and yet never can trust her to do a kind action. Why should she not

[050-089]marry you with the intention of doing you a good turn? After all, she

[050-090]would lose very little: there is the estate, and if she redeemed it,

[050-091]it would belong to her as well as to you.”

[050-092]“It would be a good turn, indeed. I fear I should be too modest to

[050-093]put it to her in that way.”

[050-094]“Her position would be much better as your wife than it is at

[050-095]present. You are good-humoured and good-tempered, you would intend to

[050-096]treat her well, and, on the whole, she would be much happier as Mrs.

[050-097]Sowerby, of Chaldicotes, than she can be in her present position.”

[050-098]“If she cared about being married, I suppose she could be a peer’s

[050-099]wife to-morrow.”

[050-100]“But I don’t think she cares about being a peer’s wife. A needy peer

[050-101]might perhaps win her in the way that I propose to you; but then a

[050-102]needy peer would not know how to set about it. Needy peers have

[050-103]tried—half a dozen I have no doubt—and have failed, because they

[050-104]have pretended that they were in love with her. It may be difficult,

[050-105]but your only chance is to tell her the truth.”

[050-106]“And where shall I do it?”

[050-107]“Here if you choose; but her own house will be better.”

[050-108]“But I never can see her there—at least, not alone. I believe that

[050-109]she never is alone. She always keeps a lot of people round her in

[050-110]order to stave off her lovers. Upon my word, Harriet, I think I’ll

[050-111]give it up. It is impossible that I should make such a declaration to

[050-112]her as that you propose.”

[050-113]“Faint heart, Nat—you know the rest.”

[050-114]“But the poet never alluded to such wooing as that you have

[050-115]suggested. I suppose I had better begin with a schedule of my debts,

[050-116]and make reference, if she doubts me, to Fothergill, the sheriff’s

[050-117]officers, and the Tozer family.”

[050-118]“She will not doubt you, on that head; nor will she be a bit

[050-119]surprised.”

[050-120]Then there was again a pause, during which Mr. Sowerby still walked

[050-121]up and down the room, thinking whether or no he might possibly have

[050-122]any chance of success in so hazardous an enterprise.

[050-123]“I tell you what, Harriet,” at last he said; “I wish you’d do it for

[050-124]me.”

[050-125]“Well,” said she, “if you really mean it, I will make the attempt.”

[050-126]“I am sure of this, that I shall never make it myself. I positively

[050-127]should not have the courage to tell her in so many words, that I

[050-128]wanted to marry her for her money.”

[050-129]“Well, Nat, I will attempt it. At any rate, I am not afraid of her.

[050-130]She and I are excellent friends, and, to tell the truth, I think I

[050-131]like her better than any other woman that I know; but I never should

[050-132]have been intimate with her, had it not been for your sake.”

[050-133]“And now you will have to quarrel with her, also for my sake?”

[050-134]“Not at all. You’ll find that whether she accedes to my proposition

[050-135]or not, we shall continue friends. I do not think that she would die

[050-136]for me—nor I for her. But as the world goes we suit each other. Such

[050-137]a little trifle as this will not break our loves.”

[050-138]And so it was settled. On the following day Mrs. Harold Smith was to

[050-139]find an opportunity of explaining the whole matter to Miss Dunstable,

[050-140]and was to ask that lady to share her fortune—some incredible

[050-141]number of thousands of pounds—with the bankrupt member for West

[050-142]Barsetshire, who in return was to bestow on her—himself and his

[050-143]debts.

[050-144]Mrs. Harold Smith had spoken no more than the truth in saying that

[050-145]she and Miss Dunstable suited one another. And she had not improperly

[050-146]described their friendship. They were not prepared to die, one for

[050-147]the sake of the other. They had said nothing to each other of mutual

[050-148]love and affection. They never kissed, or cried, or made speeches,

[050-149]when they met or when they parted. There was no great benefit for

[050-150]which either had to be grateful to the other; no terrible injury

[050-151]which either had forgiven. But they suited each other; and this, I

[050-152]take it, is the secret of most of our pleasantest intercourse in the

[050-153]world.

[050-154]And it was almost grievous that they should suit each other, for

[050-155]Miss Dunstable was much the worthier of the two, had she but known

[050-156]it herself. It was almost to be lamented that she should have found

[050-157]herself able to live with Mrs. Harold Smith on terms that were

[050-158]perfectly satisfactory to herself. Mrs. Harold Smith was worldly,

[050-159]heartless—to all the world but her brother—and, as has been above

[050-160]hinted, almost dishonest. Miss Dunstable was not worldly, though it

[050-161]was possible that her present style of life might make her so; she

[050-162]was affectionate, fond of truth, and prone to honesty, if those

[050-163]around would but allow her to exercise it. But she was fond of ease

[050-164]and humour, sometimes of wit that might almost be called broad, and

[050-165]she had a thorough love of ridiculing the world’s humbugs. In all

[050-166]these propensities Mrs. Harold Smith indulged her.

[050-167]Under these circumstances they were now together almost every day.

[050-168]It had become quite a habit with Mrs. Harold Smith to have herself

[050-169]driven early in the forenoon to Miss Dunstable’s house; and that

[050-170]lady, though she could never be found alone by Mr. Sowerby, was

[050-171]habitually so found by his sister. And after that they would go out

[050-172]together, or each separately, as fancy or the business of the day

[050-173]might direct them. Each was easy to the other in this alliance, and

[050-174]they so managed that they never trod on each other’s corns.

[050-175]On the day following the agreement made between Mr. Sowerby and Mrs.

[050-176]Harold Smith, that lady as usual called on Miss Dunstable, and soon

[050-177]found herself alone with her friend in a small room which the heiress

[050-178]kept solely for her own purposes. On special occasions persons of

[050-179]various sorts were there admitted; occasionally a parson who had a

[050-180]church to build, or a dowager laden with the last morsel of town

[050-181]slander, or a poor author who could not get due payment for the

[050-182]efforts of his brain, or a poor governess on whose feeble stamina the

[050-183]weight of the world had borne too hardly. But men who by possibility

[050-184]could be lovers did not make their way thither, nor women who could

[050-185]be bores. In these latter days, that is, during the present London

[050-186]season, the doors of it had been oftener opened to Mrs. Harold Smith

[050-187]than to any other person.
