## Section 85

[085-001]“You are going to be a great lady, Grizzy,” said he.

[085-002]“Umph!” said she.

[085-003]What was she to say when so addressed?

[085-004]“And I hope you will be happy,—and make others happy.”

[085-005]“I hope I shall,” said she.

[085-006]“But always think most about the latter, my dear. Think about the

[085-007]happiness of those around you, and your own will come without

[085-008]thinking. You understand that; do you not?”

[085-009]“Oh, yes, I understand,” she said.

[085-010]As they were speaking Mr. Harding still held her hand, but Griselda

[085-011]left it with him unwillingly, and therefore ungraciously, looking as

[085-012]though she were dragging it from him.

[085-013]“And Grizzy—I believe it is quite as easy for a rich countess to be

[085-014]happy, as for a dairymaid—”

[085-015]Griselda gave her head a little chuck which was produced by two

[085-016]different operations of her mind. The first was a reflection that

[085-017]her grandpapa was robbing her of her rank. She was to be a rich

[085-018]marchioness. And the second was a feeling of anger at the old man for

[085-019]comparing her lot to that of a dairymaid.

[085-020]“Quite as easy, I believe,” continued he; “though others will tell

[085-021]you that it is not so. But with the countess as with the dairymaid,

[085-022]it must depend on the woman herself. Being a countess—that fact

[085-023]alone won’t make you happy.”

[085-024]“Lord Dumbello at present is only a viscount,” said Griselda. “There

[085-025]is no earl’s title in the family.”

[085-026]“Oh! I did not know,” said Mr. Harding, relinquishing his

[085-027]granddaughter’s hand; and, after that, he troubled her with no

[085-028]further advice.

[085-029]Both Mrs. Proudie and the bishop had called at Plumstead since Mrs.

[085-030]Grantly had come back from London, and the ladies from Plumstead,

[085-031]of course, returned the visit. It was natural that the Grantlys and

[085-032]Proudies should hate each other. They were essentially church people,

[085-033]and their views on all church matters were antagonistic. They had

[085-034]been compelled to fight for supremacy in the diocese, and neither

[085-035]family had so conquered the other as to have become capable of

[085-036]magnanimity and good-humour. They did hate each other, and this

[085-037]hatred had, at one time, almost produced an absolute disseverance of

[085-038]even the courtesies which are so necessary between a bishop and his

[085-039]clergy. But the bitterness of this rancour had been overcome, and the

[085-040]ladies of the families had continued on visiting terms.

[085-041]But now this match was almost more than Mrs. Proudie could bear.

[085-042]The great disappointment which, as she well knew, the Grantlys had

[085-043]encountered in that matter of the proposed new bishopric had for

[085-044]the moment mollified her. She had been able to talk of poor dear

[085-045]Mrs. Grantly! “She is heartbroken, you know, in this matter, and the

[085-046]repetition of such misfortunes is hard to bear,” she had been heard

[085-047]to say, with a complacency which had been quite becoming to her. But

[085-048]now that complacency was at an end. Olivia Proudie had just accepted

[085-049]a widowed preacher at a district church in Bethnal Green,—a man with

[085-050]three children, who was dependent on pew-rents; and Griselda Grantly

[085-051]was engaged to the eldest son of the Marquis of Hartletop! When women

[085-052]are enjoined to forgive their enemies it cannot be intended that such

[085-053]wrongs as these should be included.

[085-054]But Mrs. Proudie’s courage was nothing daunted. It may be boasted of

[085-055]her that nothing could daunt her courage. Soon after her return to

[085-056]Barchester, she and Olivia—Olivia being very unwilling—had driven

[085-057]over to Plumstead, and, not finding the Grantlys at home, had left

[085-058]their cards; and now, at a proper interval, Mrs. Grantly and Griselda

[085-059]returned the visit. It was the first time that Miss Grantly had been

[085-060]seen by the Proudie ladies since the fact of her engagement had

[085-061]become known.

[085-062]The first bevy of compliments that passed might be likened to a crowd

[085-063]of flowers on a hedge rosebush. They were beautiful to the eye but

[085-064]were so closely environed by thorns that they could not be plucked

[085-065]without great danger. As long as the compliments were allowed to

[085-066]remain on the hedge—while no attempt was made to garner them and

[085-067]realize their fruits for enjoyment—they did no mischief; but the

[085-068]first finger that was put forth for such a purpose was soon drawn

[085-069]back, marked with spots of blood.

[085-070]“Of course it is a great match for Griselda,” said Mrs. Grantly, in

[085-071]a whisper the meekness of which would have disarmed an enemy whose

[085-072]weapons were less firmly clutched than those of Mrs. Proudie; “but,

[085-073]independently of that, the connection is one which is gratifying in

[085-074]many ways.”

[085-075]“Oh, no doubt,” said Mrs. Proudie.

[085-076]“Lord Dumbello is so completely his own master,” continued Mrs.

[085-077]Grantly, and a slight, unintended semi-tone of triumph mingled itself

[085-078]with the meekness of that whisper.

[085-079]“And is likely to remain so, from all I hear,” said Mrs. Proudie, and

[085-080]the scratched hand was at once drawn back.

[085-081]“Of course the estab—,” and then Mrs. Proudie, who was blandly

[085-082]continuing her list of congratulations, whispered her sentence close

[085-083]into the ear of Mrs. Grantly, so that not a word of what she said

[085-084]might be audible by the young people.

[085-085]“I never heard a word of it,” said Mrs. Grantly, gathering herself

[085-086]up, “and I don’t believe it.”

[085-087]“Oh, I may be wrong; and I’m sure I hope so. But young men will be

[085-088]young men, you know;—and children will take after their parents. I

[085-089]suppose you will see a great deal of the Duke of Omnium now.”

[085-090]But Mrs. Grantly was not a woman to be knocked down and trampled on

[085-091]without resistance; and though she had been lacerated by the rosebush

[085-092]she was not as yet placed altogether _hors de combat_. She said some

[085-093]word about the Duke of Omnium very tranquilly, speaking of him merely

[085-094]as a Barsetshire proprietor, and then, smiling with her sweetest

[085-095]smile, expressed a hope that she might soon have the pleasure of

[085-096]becoming acquainted with Mr. Tickler; and as she spoke she made a

[085-097]pretty little bow towards Olivia Proudie. Now Mr. Tickler was the

[085-098]worthy clergyman attached to the district church at Bethnal Green.

[085-099]“He’ll be down here in August,” said Olivia, boldly, determined not

[085-100]to be shamefaced about her love affairs.

[085-101]“You’ll be starring it about the Continent by that time, my dear,”

[085-102]said Mrs. Proudie to Griselda. “Lord Dumbello is well known at

[085-103]Homburg and Ems, and places of that sort; so you will find yourself

[085-104]quite at home.”

[085-105]“We are going to Rome,” said Griselda, majestically.

[085-106]“I suppose Mr. Tickler will come into the diocese soon,” said Mrs.

[085-107]Grantly. “I remember hearing him very favourably spoken of by Mr.

[085-108]Slope, who was a friend of his.”

[085-109]Nothing short of a fixed resolve on the part of Mrs. Grantly that the

[085-110]time had now come in which she must throw away her shield and stand

[085-111]behind her sword, declare war to the knife, and neither give nor take

[085-112]quarter, could have justified such a speech as this. Any allusion to

[085-113]Mr. Slope acted on Mrs. Proudie as a red cloth is supposed to act on

[085-114]a bull; but when that allusion connected the name of Mr. Slope in

[085-115]a friendly bracket with that of Mrs. Proudie’s future son-in-law

[085-116]it might be certain that the effect would be terrific. And there

[085-117]was more than this: for that very Mr. Slope had once entertained

[085-118]audacious hopes—hopes not thought to be audacious by the young lady

[085-119]herself—with reference to Miss Olivia Proudie. All this Mrs. Grantly

[085-120]knew, and, knowing it, still dared to mention his name.

[085-121]The countenance of Mrs. Proudie became darkened with black anger,

[085-122]and the polished smile of her company manners gave place before the

[085-123]outraged feelings of her nature.

[085-124]“The man you speak of, Mrs. Grantly,” said she, “was never known as a

[085-125]friend by Mr. Tickler.”

[085-126]“Oh, indeed,” said Mrs. Grantly. “Perhaps I have made a mistake. I am

[085-127]sure I have heard Mr. Slope mention him.”

[085-128]“When Mr. Slope was running after your sister, Mrs. Grantly, and was

[085-129]encouraged by her as he was, you perhaps saw more of him than I did.”

[085-130]“Mrs. Proudie, that was never the case.”

[085-131]“I have reason to know that the archdeacon conceived it to be so, and

[085-132]that he was very unhappy about it.” Now this, unfortunately, was a

[085-133]fact which Mrs. Grantly could not deny.

[085-134]“The archdeacon may have been mistaken about Mr. Slope,” she said,

[085-135]“as were some other people at Barchester. But it was you, I think,

[085-136]Mrs. Proudie, who were responsible for bringing him here.”

[085-137]Mrs. Grantly, at this period of the engagement, might have inflicted

[085-138]a fatal wound by referring to poor Olivia’s former love affairs, but

[085-139]she was not destitute of generosity. Even in the extremest heat of

[085-140]the battle she knew how to spare the young and tender.

[085-141]“When I came here, Mrs. Grantly, I little dreamed what a depth of

[085-142]wickedness might be found in the very close of a cathedral city,”

[085-143]said Mrs. Proudie.

[085-144]“Then, for dear Olivia’s sake, pray do not bring poor Mr. Tickler to

[085-145]Barchester.”

[085-146]“Mr. Tickler, Mrs. Grantly, is a man of assured morals and of a

[085-147]highly religious tone of thinking. I wish every one could be so safe

[085-148]as regards their daughters’ future prospects as I am.”

[085-149]“Yes, I know he has the advantage of being a family man,” said Mrs.

[085-150]Grantly, getting up. “Good morning, Mrs. Proudie; good day, Olivia.”

[085-151]“A great deal better that than—” But the blow fell upon the empty

[085-152]air; for Mrs. Grantly had already escaped on to the staircase while

[085-153]Olivia was ringing the bell for the servant to attend the front-door.

[085-154]Mrs. Grantly, as she got into her carriage, smiled slightly, thinking

[085-155]of the battle, and as she sat down she gently pressed her daughter’s

[085-156]hand. But Mrs. Proudie’s face was still dark as Acheron when her

[085-157]enemy withdrew, and with angry tone she sent her daughter to her

[085-158]work. “Mr. Tickler will have great reason to complain if, in your

[085-159]position, you indulge such habits of idleness,” she said. Therefore

[085-160]I conceive that I am justified in saying that in that encounter Mrs.

[085-161]Grantly was the conqueror.

[085-162]CHAPTER XLI.

[085-163]DON QUIXOTE.

[085-164]On the day on which Lucy had her interview with Lady Lufton the dean

[085-165]dined at Framley Parsonage. He and Robarts had known each other

[085-166]since the latter had been in the diocese, and now, owing to Mark’s

[085-167]preferment in the chapter, had become almost intimate. The dean was

[085-168]greatly pleased with the manner in which poor Mr. Crawley’s children

[085-169]had been conveyed away from Hogglestock, and was inclined to open his

[085-170]heart to the whole Framley household. As he still had to ride home

[085-171]he could only allow himself to remain half an hour after dinner, but

[085-172]in that half-hour he said a great deal about Crawley, complimented

[085-173]Robarts on the manner in which he was playing the part of the Good

[085-174]Samaritan, and then by degrees informed him that it had come to his,

[085-175]the dean’s, ears, before he left Barchester, that a writ was in the

[085-176]hands of certain persons in the city, enabling them to seize—he did

[085-177]not know whether it was the person or the property of the vicar of

[085-178]Framley.

[085-179]The fact was that these tidings had been conveyed to the dean with

[085-180]the express intent that he might put Robarts on his guard; but the

[085-181]task of speaking on such a subject to a brother clergyman had been so

[085-182]unpleasant to him that he had been unable to introduce it till the

[085-183]last five minutes before his departure.

[085-184]“I hope you will not put it down as an impertinent interference,”

[085-185]said the dean, apologizing.

[085-186]“No,” said Mark; “no, I do not think that.” He was so sad at heart

[085-187]that he hardly knew how to speak of it.

[085-188]“I do not understand much about such matters,” said the dean; “but I

[085-189]think, if I were you, I should go to a lawyer. I should imagine that

[085-190]anything so terribly disagreeable as an arrest might be avoided.”
