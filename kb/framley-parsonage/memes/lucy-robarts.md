# Lucy Robarts

Lucy Robarts is the younger sister of [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]], the vicar of Framley. Her romance with [[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]] — opposed at every turn by his mother, [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]] — forms one of the central plots of _Framley Parsonage_ and stands as one of Trollope's most admired portrayals of quiet moral strength.

## Role in the Novel

Lucy Robarts is introduced early but not described in detail until the novel's twenty-first chapter, when Trollope pauses the narrative to remark: "And now I must say a word about Lucy Robarts" (021-014). This deliberate narrative halt signals her importance — she is the novel's heroine, but a heroine of an unusual kind: neither wealthy, nor well-connected, nor conventionally dazzling.

Lucy arrives at Framley Parsonage as a dependent — the vicar's unmarried sister, come to live with her brother and his wife [[fanny-robarts|Fanny Robarts]]. Her position is precarious: she has no fortune, no powerful family connections, and no obvious path to an advantageous marriage. It is precisely this vulnerability that makes Lady Lufton regard her as a threat — a young woman who might ensnare the heir to Framley Court (027-076).

## Character Traits

- **Moral courage**: Lucy's defining trait is her refusal to compromise her principles, even when doing so would secure her material advantage. When Lady Lufton offers to arrange a respectable marriage for her elsewhere — essentially a bribe to remove her from Lord Lufton's path — Lucy's "upright principles" forbid her from accepting (028-008).
- **Intelligence**: Lucy "was blessed with an intelligence keener than that of her brothers or sisters" (021-092). Her sharp mind and quick perception set her apart from the more conventional young women of Barsetshire society.
- **Pride**: Lucy is "very proud-hearted" — a quality that Trollope presents not as a flaw but as the foundation of her moral integrity. She will not be patronised, and she cannot love those who despise her (025-075).
- **Physical distinctiveness**: Lucy is "thoroughly a brunette" with dark colouring that "struck one with such surprise" — her fire is in her eyes, not in conventional beauty or social graces (021-072). She lacks the easy conversational gifts that make other young women admired in company (022-066).

## Relationships

- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: Her elder brother, the vicar of Framley. His household provides Lucy's home and social context, though his own financial troubles create complications.
- **[[fanny-robarts|Fanny Robarts]]**: Her sister-in-law, who becomes both friend and ally. Fanny's affection for Lucy and her understanding of Lord Lufton's feelings place her in a delicate position between Lucy and Lady Lufton.
- **[[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]]**: The man who falls in love with her, despite — or perhaps because of — her difference from the fashionable young ladies his mother parades before him. A genuine gulf separates them socially, and Trollope is explicit that nothing "possible" can exist between the two under the rules of their world (028-011). That the gulf is eventually bridged is the novel's central romantic achievement.
- **[[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]**: The formidable opponent who regards Lucy as a social-climbing interloper. Their quiet contest of wills — Lucy's moral dignity against Lady Lufton's social power — is one of the novel's most compelling dynamics (027-076) (028-011).
- **[[griselda-grantly|Griselda Grantly]]**: The young woman Lady Lufton intends for her son. The contrast between Griselda's cold, statuesque perfection and Lucy's warm, spirited intelligence is central to Lord Lufton's romantic choice (023-128).

## Key Scenes

- **The first description (Chapter 21)**: Trollope's extended introduction of Lucy, dwelling on her dark beauty, keen intelligence, and social reticence, establishes her as a heroine of substance rather than mere charm (021-014) (021-072).
- **Watching Griselda (Chapter 25)**: Lucy observes the formal visit of the Grantlys to Framley Court, watching the "animation" with which Lady Lufton greets her preferred daughter-in-law. The scene is entirely from Lucy's perspective, and her silent observation — proud, perceptive, and wounded — is a masterclass in Trollope's use of free indirect discourse (025-012).
- **The comparison (Chapter 25)**: Lord Lufton's remark that Griselda "wanted some of the fire of Lucy Robarts" reaches his mother's ears and crystallises her alarm. For Lady Lufton, the comparison of a Grantly — unfavourably! — with a penniless parson's sister is an intolerable sign of where her son's affections truly lie (025-118).

## Social & Historical Context

Lucy Robarts represents a recurring figure in Victorian fiction: the governess-like dependent whose moral worth far exceeds her social capital. Like Jane Eyre — to whom she is sometimes compared — Lucy must navigate a world in which her intelligence and integrity are the only currency she possesses. The Victorian marriage market valued fortune and connection above all else; Lucy's triumph, when it comes, is a triumph of character over calculation.

Yet Trollope complicates the pattern in a characteristic way. Where Brontë's Jane Eyre burns with barely concealed passion, Lucy's strength is quieter, more domestic, more reconcilable with the social order she ultimately joins. The "gulf" between Lord Lufton and Lucy is real and socially defined (028-011), but Trollope does not need to overturn the entire class system to close it — a shift in Lady Lufton's perception, brought about by Lucy's demonstrated moral worth, is enough. This is the Barsetshire version of the romance plot: not revolutionary but reformist, not fiery but steady, and in the end perhaps more credible for it.

## Critical Commentary

> Lucy Robarts has been read by critics as Trollope's answer to the Byronic heroine — dark where convention demanded fair, proud where it expected pliant, and intelligent in a world that rewarded feminine wit only when it was carefully circumscribed. Her refusal of Lady Lufton's bribe marks her as one of Trollope's most morally self-possessed characters; she will not be bought, and in that refusal she becomes, paradoxically, the most suitable mistress Framley Court could hope for.
