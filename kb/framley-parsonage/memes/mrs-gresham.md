# Mrs. Gresham

Mrs. Gresham is a gentle but morally earnest figure who appears at the periphery of the Barsetshire chronicles — the niece of Dr. Thorne, the wife of young Mr. Gresham of Boxall Hill, and a woman whose quiet domestic influence becomes a vehicle for Trollope's exploration of persuasion, duty, and the limits of familial obligation (058-120).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Gresham occupies a modest place in the narrative, but her scenes carry disproportionate thematic weight. She is the conduit through which Dr. Thorne's character is illuminated from a domestic angle — not the doctor in his professional capacity, but the uncle sitting by his niece's fireside, the object of her affectionate concern for his unmarried state. Her home at Boxall Hill serves as the setting for some of the most psychologically acute passages in the novel, as she attempts — with varying degrees of success — to influence her uncle's views on marriage (058-133) (059-061).

## Character Traits

- **Moral earnestness**: Mrs. Gresham is deeply concerned with doing right, and this concern extends to the lives of those she loves. Her attempts to persuade Dr. Thorne toward matrimony are not meddlesome but genuinely motivated by a belief that his happiness depends upon it (059-118).
- **Affectionate persistence**: Though gifted with "various arguments," she does not browbeat. Trollope presents her as one who returns to her theme not from stubbornness but from love — a distinction that the narrator makes with characteristic care (059-143).
- **Domestic sensibility**: She is a creature of the home, and her understanding of the world is filtered through domestic concerns. When she thinks of Dr. Thorne's solitary life, she does so in terms of comfort, companionship, and the warmth of a shared hearth (058-147).

## Relationships

- **Dr. Thorne**: Her uncle and the central object of her moral concern. Their relationship is one of mutual affection, though she finds him frustratingly resistant to her schemes for his matrimonial happiness. The phrase "if your uncle don't" — spoken to her by another — captures the singular focus of her campaign (058-120) (058-147).
- **Mr. Gresham**: Her husband, the young master of Boxall Hill. Their marriage appears to be a happy one, though the narrative gives us only glimpses of their domestic life (017-159).

## Key Scenes

- **The Boxall Hill fireside (Chapter 58)**: Mrs. Gresham receives her uncle at home, and the domestic setting frames their conversation about his future. It is here that the campaign for Dr. Thorne's marriage begins in earnest, though the doctor proves an elusive target (058-133) (058-151).
- **The earnest meditation (Chapter 59)**: In a passage of sustained psychological interest, Mrs. Gresham turns over the question of her uncle's marriage in her mind — weighing arguments, considering obstacles, and finally resolving to apply herself with renewed energy to the task (059-061) (059-066) (059-143).

## Social & Historical Context

Mrs. Gresham belongs to the landed gentry of Barsetshire — the Boxall Hill estate places her among the county's established families — but her role in the narrative is not that of the great lady. She represents instead the quiet domestic virtues that Trollope consistently valued: loyalty, affection, and a concern for the welfare of others that stops short of meddling. Her campaign to see Dr. Thorne married reflects a Victorian assumption that the unmarried state, especially for a man of substance, was a kind of incompleteness — a view that the novel itself examines with some ambivalence (059-099) (059-163).
