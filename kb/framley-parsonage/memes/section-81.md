## Section 81

[081-001]“Ah! but I do,” said Miss Dunstable. “And therefore go on, or for

[081-002]ever hold your peace.”

[081-003]“That’s just it,” said Mrs. Gresham.

[081-004]“What’s just it?” said Miss Dunstable.

[081-005]“The quotation out of the Prayer Book which you finished just now.

[081-006]‘If any of you know cause or just impediment why these two persons

[081-007]should not be joined together in holy matrimony, ye are to declare

[081-008]it. This is the first time of asking.’ Do you know any cause, Miss

[081-009]Dunstable?”

[081-010]“Do you know any, Mrs. Gresham?”

[081-011]“None, on my honour!” said the younger lady, putting her hand upon

[081-012]her breast.

[081-013]“Ah! but do you not?” and Miss Dunstable caught hold of her arm, and

[081-014]spoke almost abruptly in her energy.

[081-015]“No, certainly not. What impediment? If I did, I should not have

[081-016]broached the subject. I declare I think you would both be very happy

[081-017]together. Of course, there is one impediment; we all know that. That

[081-018]must be your look out.”

[081-019]“What do you mean? What impediment?”

[081-020]“Your own money.”

[081-021]“Psha! Did you find that an impediment in marrying Frank Gresham?”

[081-022]“Ah! the matter was so different there. He had much more to give than

[081-023]I had, when all was counted. And I had no money when we—when we were

[081-024]first engaged.” And the tears came into her eyes as she thought of

[081-025]the circumstances of her early love;—all of which have been narrated

[081-026]in the county chronicles of Barsetshire, and may now be read by men

[081-027]and women interested therein.

[081-028]“Yes; yours was a love match. I declare, Mary, I often think that you

[081-029]are the happiest woman of whom I ever heard; to have it all to give,

[081-030]when you were so sure that you were loved while you yet had nothing.”

[081-031]“Yes; I was sure,” and she wiped the sweet tears from her eyes, as

[081-032]she remembered a certain day when a certain youth had come to her,

[081-033]claiming all kinds of privileges in a very determined manner. She had

[081-034]been no heiress then. “Yes; I was sure. But now with you, dear, you

[081-035]can’t make yourself poor again. If you can trust no one—”

[081-036]“I can. I can trust him. As regards that I do trust him altogether.

[081-037]But how can I tell that he would care for me?”

[081-038]“Do you not know that he likes you?”

[081-039]“Ah, yes; and so he does Lady Scatcherd.”

[081-040]“Miss Dunstable!”

[081-041]“And why not Lady Scatcherd, as well as me? We are of the same

[081-042]kind—come from the same class.”

[081-043]“Not quite that, I think.”

[081-044]“Yes, from the same class; only I have managed to poke myself up

[081-045]among dukes and duchesses, whereas she has been content to remain

[081-046]where God placed her. Where I beat her in art, she beats me in

[081-047]nature.”

[081-048]“You know you are talking nonsense.”

[081-049]“I think that we are both doing that—absolute nonsense; such as

[081-050]schoolgirls of eighteen talk to each other. But there is a relief in

[081-051]it; is there not? It would be a terrible curse to have to talk sense

[081-052]always. Well, that’s done; and now let us go out.”

[081-053]Mrs. Gresham was sure after this that Miss Dunstable would be a

[081-054]consenting party to the little arrangement which she contemplated.

[081-055]But of that she had felt but little doubt for some considerable time

[081-056]past. The difficulty lay on the other side, and all that she had as

[081-057]yet done was to convince herself that she would be safe in assuring

[081-058]her uncle of success if he could be induced to take the enterprise in

[081-059]hand. He was to come to Boxall Hill that evening, and to remain there

[081-060]for a day or two. If anything could be done in the matter, now would

[081-061]be the time for doing it. So at least thought Mrs. Gresham.

[081-062]The doctor did come, and did remain for the allotted time at Boxall

[081-063]Hill; but when he left, Mrs. Gresham had not been successful. Indeed,

[081-064]he did not seem to enjoy his visit as was usual with him; and there

[081-065]was very little of that pleasant friendly intercourse which for some

[081-066]time past had been customary between him and Miss Dunstable. There

[081-067]were no passages of arms between them; no abuse from the doctor

[081-068]against the lady’s London gaiety; no raillery from the lady as to

[081-069]the doctor’s country habits. They were very courteous to each other,

[081-070]and, as Mrs. Gresham thought, too civil by half; nor, as far as she

[081-071]could see, did they ever remain alone in each other’s company for

[081-072]five minutes at a time during the whole period of the doctor’s visit.

[081-073]What, thought Mrs. Gresham to herself,—what if she had set these two

[081-074]friends at variance with each other, instead of binding them together

[081-075]in the closest and most durable friendship!

[081-076]But still she had an idea that, as she had begun to play this game,

[081-077]she must play it out. She felt conscious that what she had done must

[081-078]do evil, unless she could so carry it on as to make it result in

[081-079]good. Indeed, unless she could so manage, she would have done a

[081-080]manifest injury to Miss Dunstable in forcing her to declare her

[081-081]thoughts and feelings. She had already spoken to her uncle in London,

[081-082]and though he had said nothing to show that he approved of her

[081-083]plan, neither had he said anything to show that he disapproved it.

[081-084]Therefore she had hoped through the whole of those three days that he

[081-085]would make some sign,—at any rate to her; that he would in some way

[081-086]declare what were his own thoughts on this matter. But the morning of

[081-087]his departure came, and he had declared nothing.

[081-088]“Uncle,” she said, in the last five minutes of his sojourn there,

[081-089]after he had already taken leave of Miss Dunstable and shaken hands

[081-090]with Mrs. Gresham, “have you ever thought of what I said to you up in

[081-091]London?”

[081-092]“Yes, Mary; of course I have thought about it. Such an idea as that,

[081-093]when put into a man’s head, will make itself thought about.”

[081-094]“Well; and what next? Do talk to me about it. Do not be so hard and

[081-095]unlike yourself.”

[081-096]“I have very little to say about it.”

[081-097]“I can tell you this for certain, you may if you like.”

[081-098]“Mary! Mary!”

[081-099]“I would not say so if I were not sure that I should not lead you

[081-100]into trouble.”

[081-101]“You are foolish in wishing this, my dear; foolish in trying to tempt

[081-102]an old man into a folly.”

[081-103]“Not foolish if I know that it will make you both happier.”

[081-104]He made her no further reply, but stooping down that she might kiss

[081-105]him, as was his wont, went his way, leaving her almost miserable in

[081-106]the thought that she had troubled all these waters to no purpose.

[081-107]What would Miss Dunstable think of her? But on that afternoon Miss

[081-108]Dunstable seemed to be as happy and even-tempered as ever.

[081-109]CHAPTER XXXIX.

[081-110]HOW TO WRITE A LOVE LETTER.

[081-111]Dr. Thorne, in the few words which he spoke to his niece before he

[081-112]left Boxall Hill, had called himself an old man; but he was as yet on

[081-113]the right side of sixty by five good years, and bore about with him

[081-114]less of the marks of age than most men of fifty-five do bear. One

[081-115]would have said in looking at him that there was no reason why he

[081-116]should not marry if he found that such a step seemed good to him;

[081-117]and looking at the age of the proposed bride, there was nothing

[081-118]unsuitable in that respect.

[081-119]But nevertheless he felt almost ashamed of himself, in that he

[081-120]allowed himself even to think of the proposition which his niece had

[081-121]made. He mounted his horse that day at Boxall Hill—for he made all

[081-122]his journeys about the county on horseback—and rode slowly home to

[081-123]Greshamsbury, thinking not so much of the suggested marriage as of

[081-124]his own folly in thinking of it. How could he be such an ass at his

[081-125]time of life as to allow the even course of his way to be disturbed

[081-126]by any such idea? Of course he could not propose to himself such a

[081-127]wife as Miss Dunstable without having some thoughts as to her wealth;

[081-128]and it had been the pride of his life so to live that the world might

[081-129]know that he was indifferent about money. His profession was all in

[081-130]all to him,—the air which he breathed as well as the bread which

[081-131]he ate; and how could he follow his profession if he made such a

[081-132]marriage as this? She would expect him to go to London with her; and

[081-133]what would he become, dangling at her heels there, known only to the

[081-134]world as the husband of the richest woman in the town? The kind of

[081-135]life was one which would be unsuitable to him;—and yet, as he rode

[081-136]home, he could not resolve to rid himself of the idea. He went on

[081-137]thinking of it, though he still continued to condemn himself for

[081-138]keeping it in his thoughts. That night at home he would make up his

[081-139]mind, so he declared to himself; and would then write to his niece

[081-140]begging her to drop the subject. Having so far come to a resolution

[081-141]he went on meditating what course of life it might be well for him

[081-142]to pursue if he and Miss Dunstable should, after all, become man and

[081-143]wife.

[081-144]There were two ladies whom it behoved him to see on the day of

[081-145]his arrival—whom, indeed, he generally saw every day except when

[081-146]absent from Greshamsbury. The first of these—first in the general

[081-147]consideration of the people of the place—was the wife of the squire,

[081-148]Lady Arabella Gresham, a very old patient of the doctor’s. Her it was

[081-149]his custom to visit early in the afternoon; and then, if he were able

[081-150]to escape the squire’s daily invitation to dinner, he customarily

[081-151]went to the other, Lady Scatcherd, when the rapid meal in his own

[081-152]house was over. Such, at least, was his summer practice.

[081-153]“Well, doctor, how are they at Boxall Hill?” said the squire,

[081-154]waylaying him on the gravel sweep before the door. The squire was

[081-155]very hard set for occupation in these summer months.

[081-156]“Quite well, I believe.”

[081-157]“I don’t know what’s come to Frank. I think he hates this place now.

[081-158]He’s full of the election, I suppose.”

[081-159]“Oh, yes; he told me to say he should be over here soon. Of course

[081-160]there’ll be no contest, so he need not trouble himself.”

[081-161]“Happy dog, isn’t he, doctor? to have it all before him instead of

[081-162]behind him. Well, well; he’s as good a lad as ever lived,—as ever

[081-163]lived. And let me see; Mary’s time—” And then there were a few very

[081-164]important words spoken on that subject.

[081-165]“I’ll just step up to Lady Arabella now,” said the doctor.

[081-166]“She’s as fretful as possible,” said the squire. “I’ve just left

[081-167]her.”

[081-168]“Nothing special the matter, I hope?”

[081-169]“No, I think not; nothing in your way, that is; only specially

[081-170]cross, which always comes in my way. You’ll stop and dine to-day, of

[081-171]course?”

[081-172]“Not to-day, squire.”

[081-173]“Nonsense; you will. I have been quite counting on you. I have a

[081-174]particular reason for wanting to have you to-day,—a most particular

[081-175]reason.” But the squire always had his particular reasons.

[081-176]“I’m very sorry, but it is impossible to-day. I shall have a letter

[081-177]to write that I must sit down to seriously. Shall I see you when I

[081-178]come down from her ladyship?”

[081-179]The squire turned away sulkily, almost without answering him, for he

[081-180]now had no prospect of any alleviation to the tedium of the evening;

[081-181]and the doctor went up-stairs to his patient.

[081-182]For Lady Arabella, though it cannot be said that she was ill, was

[081-183]always a patient. It must not be supposed that she kept her bed and

[081-184]swallowed daily doses, or was prevented from taking her share in such

[081-185]prosy gaieties as came from time to time in the way of her prosy

[081-186]life; but it suited her turn of mind to be an invalid and to have a

[081-187]doctor; and as the doctor whom her good fates had placed at her elbow

[081-188]thoroughly understood her case, no great harm was done.
