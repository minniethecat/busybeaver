## Section 41

[041-001]“And so I am—I am liable for these bills. But you ought to have

[041-002]known, and do know, that I have never received a shilling on account

[041-003]of such liability. I have endeavoured to oblige a man whom I regarded

[041-004]first as your friend, and then as my own; and this has been the

[041-005]result.”

[041-006]Lord Lufton did at last give him the assurance that he desired,

[041-007]as they sat with their heads together over one of the coffee-room

[041-008]tables; and then Robarts promised that he would postpone his return

[041-009]to Framley till the Saturday, so that he might meet Sowerby at Lord

[041-010]Lufton’s chambers in the Albany on the following afternoon. As soon

[041-011]as this was arranged, Lord Lufton took his leave and went his way.

[041-012]After that poor Mark had a very uneasy night of it. It was clear

[041-013]enough that Lord Lufton had thought, if he did not still think, that

[041-014]the stall at Barchester was to be given as pecuniary recompense in

[041-015]return for certain money accommodation to be afforded by the nominee

[041-016]to the dispenser of this patronage. Nothing on earth could be worse

[041-017]than this. In the first place it would be simony; and then it would

[041-018]be simony beyond all description mean and simoniacal. The very

[041-019]thought of it filled Mark’s soul with horror and dismay. It might

[041-020]be that Lord Lufton’s suspicions were now at rest; but others would

[041-021]think the same thing, and their suspicions it would be impossible to

[041-022]allay; those others would consist of the outer world, which is always

[041-023]so eager to gloat over the detected vice of a clergyman.

[041-024]And then that wretched horse which he had purchased, and the purchase

[041-025]of which should have prohibited him from saying that nothing of value

[041-026]had accrued to him in these transactions with Mr. Sowerby! what was

[041-027]he to do about that? And then of late he had been spending, and had

[041-028]continued to spend, more money than he could well afford. This very

[041-029]journey of his up to London would be most imprudent, if it should

[041-030]become necessary for him to give up all hope of holding the prebend.

[041-031]As to that he had made up his mind; but then again he unmade it, as

[041-032]men always do in such troubles. That line of conduct which he had

[041-033]laid down for himself in the first moments of his indignation against

[041-034]Lord Lufton, by adopting which he would have to encounter poverty,

[041-035]and ridicule, and discomfort, the annihilation of his high hopes,

[041-036]and the ruin of his ambition—that, he said to himself over and over

[041-037]again, would now be the best for him. But it is so hard for us to

[041-038]give up our high hopes, and willingly encounter poverty, ridicule,

[041-039]and discomfort!

[041-040]On the following morning, however, he boldly walked down to the

[041-041]Petty Bag office, determined to let Harold Smith know that he was no

[041-042]longer desirous of the Barchester stall. He found his brother there,

[041-043]still writing artistic notes to anxious peeresses on the subject of

[041-044]Buggins’ non-vacant situation; but the great man of the place, the

[041-045]Lord Petty Bag himself, was not there. He might probably look in when

[041-046]the House was beginning to sit, perhaps at four or a little after;

[041-047]but he certainly would not be at the office in the morning. The

[041-048]functions of the Lord Petty Bag he was no doubt performing elsewhere.

[041-049]Perhaps he had carried his work home with him—a practice which the

[041-050]world should know is not uncommon with civil servants of exceeding

[041-051]zeal.

[041-052]Mark did think of opening his heart to his brother, and of leaving

[041-053]his message with him. But his courage failed him, or perhaps it might

[041-054]be more correct to say that his prudence prevented him. It would be

[041-055]better for him, he thought, to tell his wife before he told any one

[041-056]else. So he merely chatted with his brother for half an hour and then

[041-057]left him.

[041-058]The day was very tedious till the hour came at which he was to attend

[041-059]at Lord Lufton’s rooms; but at last it did come, and just as the

[041-060]clock struck, he turned out of Piccadilly into the Albany. As he was

[041-061]going across the court before he entered the building, he was greeted

[041-062]by a voice just behind him.

[041-063]“As punctual as the big clock on Barchester tower,” said Mr. Sowerby.

[041-064]“See what it is to have a summons from a great man, Mr. Prebendary.”

[041-065]He turned round and extended his hand mechanically to Mr. Sowerby,

[041-066]and as he looked at him he thought he had never before seen him

[041-067]so pleasant in appearance, so free from care, and so joyous in

[041-068]demeanour.

[041-069]“You have heard from Lord Lufton,” said Mark in a voice that was

[041-070]certainly very lugubrious.

[041-071]“Heard from him! oh, yes, of course I have heard from him. I’ll tell

[041-072]you what it is, Mark,” and he now spoke almost in a whisper as they

[041-073]walked together along the Albany passage, “Lufton is a child in money

[041-074]matters—a perfect child. The dearest, finest fellow in the world,

[041-075]you know; but a very baby in money matters.” And then they entered

[041-076]his lordship’s rooms.

[041-077]Lord Lufton’s countenance also was lugubrious enough, but this did

[041-078]not in the least abash Sowerby, who walked quickly up to the young

[041-079]lord with his gait perfectly self-possessed and his face radiant with

[041-080]satisfaction.

[041-081]“Well, Lufton, how are you?” said he. “It seems that my worthy friend

[041-082]Tozer has been giving you some trouble?”

[041-083]Then Lord Lufton with a face by no means radiant with satisfaction

[041-084]again began the story of Tozer’s fraudulent demand upon him. Sowerby

[041-085]did not interrupt him, but listened patiently to the end;—quite

[041-086]patiently, although Lord Lufton, as he made himself more and more

[041-087]angry by the history of his own wrongs, did not hesitate to pronounce

[041-088]certain threats against Mr. Sowerby, as he had pronounced them before

[041-089]against Mark Robarts. He would not, he said, pay a shilling, except

[041-090]through his lawyer; and he would instruct his lawyer, that before he

[041-091]paid anything, the whole matter should be exposed openly in court. He

[041-092]did not care, he said, what might be the effect on himself or any one

[041-093]else. He was determined that the whole case should go to a jury.

[041-094]“To grand jury, and special jury, and common jury, and Old Jewry, if

[041-095]you like,” said Sowerby. “The truth is, Lufton, you lost some money,

[041-096]and as there was some delay in paying it, you have been harassed.”

[041-097]“I have paid more than I lost three times over,” said Lord Lufton,

[041-098]stamping his foot.

[041-099]“I will not go into that question now. It was settled, as I thought,

[041-100]some time ago by persons to whom you yourself referred it. But will

[041-101]you tell me this: Why on earth should Robarts be troubled in this

[041-102]matter? What has he done?”

[041-103]“Well, I don’t know. He arranged the matter with you.”

[041-104]“No such thing. He was kind enough to carry a message from you to me,

[041-105]and to convey back a return message from me to you. That has been his

[041-106]part in it.”

[041-107]“You don’t suppose that I want to implicate him: do you?”

[041-108]“I don’t think you want to implicate any one, but you are hot-headed

[041-109]and difficult to deal with, and very irrational into the bargain.

[041-110]And, what is worse, I must say you are a little suspicious. In all

[041-111]this matter I have harassed myself greatly to oblige you, and in

[041-112]return I have got more kicks than halfpence.”

[041-113]“Did not you give this bill to Tozer—the bill which he now holds?”

[041-114]“In the first place he does not hold it; and in the next place I did

[041-115]not give it to him. These things pass through scores of hands before

[041-116]they reach the man who makes the application for payment.”

[041-117]“And who came to me the other day?”

[041-118]“That, I take it, was Tom Tozer, a brother of our Tozer’s.”

[041-119]“Then he holds the bill, for I saw it with him.”

[041-120]“Wait a moment; that is very likely. I sent you word that you would

[041-121]have to pay for taking it up. Of course they don’t abandon those sort

[041-122]of things without some consideration.”

[041-123]“Ten pounds, you said,” observed Mark.

[041-124]“Ten or twenty; some such sum as that. But you were hardly so soft

[041-125]as to suppose that the man would ask for such a sum. Of course he

[041-126]would demand the full payment. There is the bill, Lord Lufton,” and

[041-127]Sowerby, producing a document, handed it across the table to his

[041-128]lordship. “I gave five-and-twenty pounds for it this morning.”

[041-129]Lord Lufton took the paper and looked at it. “Yes,” said he, “that’s

[041-130]the bill. What am I to do with it now?”

[041-131]“Put it with the family archives,” said Sowerby,—“or behind the

[041-132]fire, just which you please.”

[041-133]“And is this the last of them? Can no other be brought up?”

[041-134]“You know better than I do what paper you may have put your hand to.

[041-135]I know of no other. At the last renewal that was the only outstanding

[041-136]bill of which I was aware.”

[041-137]“And you have paid five-and-twenty pounds for it?”

[041-138]“I have. Only that you have been in such a tantrum about it, and

[041-139]would have made such a noise this afternoon if I had not brought it,

[041-140]I might have had it for fifteen or twenty. In three or four days they

[041-141]would have taken fifteen.”

[041-142]“The odd ten pounds does not signify, and I’ll pay you the

[041-143]twenty-five, of course,” said Lord Lufton, who now began to feel a

[041-144]little ashamed of himself.

[041-145]“You may do as you please about that.”

[041-146]“Oh! it’s my affair, as a matter of course. Any amount of that kind I

[041-147]don’t mind,” and he sat down to fill in a cheque for the money.

[041-148]“Well, now, Lufton, let me say a few words to you,” said Sowerby,

[041-149]standing with his back against the fireplace, and playing with a

[041-150]small cane which he held in his hand. “For heaven’s sake try and be a

[041-151]little more charitable to those around you. When you become fidgety

[041-152]about anything, you indulge in language which the world won’t stand,

[041-153]though men who know you as well as Robarts and I may consent to put

[041-154]up with it. You have accused me, since I have been here, of all

[041-155]manner of iniquity—”

[041-156]“Now, Sowerby—”

[041-157]“My dear fellow, let me have my say out. You have accused me, I say,

[041-158]and I believe that you have accused him. But it has never occurred to

[041-159]you, I daresay, to accuse yourself.”

[041-160]“Indeed it has.”

[041-161]“Of course you have been wrong in having to do with such men as

[041-162]Tozer. I have also been very wrong. It wants no great moral authority

[041-163]to tell us that. Pattern gentlemen don’t have dealings with Tozer,

[041-164]and very much the better they are for not having them. But a man

[041-165]should have back enough to bear the weight which he himself puts on

[041-166]it. Keep away from Tozer, if you can, for the future; but if you do

[041-167]deal with him, for heaven’s sake keep your temper.”

[041-168]“That’s all very fine, Sowerby; but you know as well as I do—”

[041-169]“I know this,” said the devil, quoting Scripture, as he folded up the

[041-170]check for twenty-five pounds, and put it in his pocket, “that when a

[041-171]man sows tares, he won’t reap wheat, and it’s no use to expect it. I

[041-172]am tough in these matters, and can bear a great deal—that is, if I

[041-173]be not pushed too far,” and he looked full into Lord Lufton’s face as

[041-174]he spoke; “but I think you have been very hard upon Robarts.”

[041-175]“Never mind me, Sowerby; Lord Lufton and I are very old friends.”

[041-176]“And may therefore take a liberty with each other. Very well. And

[041-177]now I’ve done my sermon. My dear dignitary, allow me to congratulate

[041-178]you. I hear from Fothergill that that little affair of yours has been

[041-179]definitely settled.”
