## Section 87

[087-001]“I do not see how any one could blame her. She has behaved so nobly.”

[087-002]“It is of no use inquiring whether any one can. It is sufficient that

[087-003]I do not.”

[087-004]“But I think that is hardly sufficient,” said Mrs. Robarts,

[087-005]pertinaciously.

[087-006]“Is it not?” asked her ladyship, raising her eyebrows.

[087-007]“No. Only think what Lucy has done and is doing. If she had chosen to

[087-008]say that she would accept your son I really do not know how you could

[087-009]have justly blamed her. I do not by any means say that I would have

[087-010]advised such a thing.”

[087-011]“I am glad of that, Fanny.”

[087-012]“I have not given any advice; nor is it needed. I know no one more

[087-013]able than Lucy to see clearly, by her own judgment, what course she

[087-014]ought to pursue. I should be afraid to advise one whose mind is so

[087-015]strong, and who, of her own nature, is so self-denying as she is.

[087-016]She is sacrificing herself now, because she will not be the means of

[087-017]bringing trouble and dissension between you and your son. If you ask

[087-018]me, Lady Lufton, I think you owe her a deep debt of gratitude. I do,

[087-019]indeed. And as for blaming her—what has she done that you possibly

[087-020]could blame?”

[087-021]“Don Quixote on horseback!” said Lady Lufton. “Fanny, I shall always

[087-022]call you Don Quixote, and some day or other I will get somebody to

[087-023]write your adventures. But the truth is this, my dear: there has been

[087-024]imprudence. You may call it mine, if you will—though I really hardly

[087-025]see how I am to take the blame. I could not do other than ask Miss

[087-026]Robarts to my house, and I could not very well turn my son out of it.

[087-027]In point of fact, it has been the old story.”

[087-028]“Exactly; the story that is as old as the world, and which will

[087-029]continue as long as people are born into it. It is a story of God’s

[087-030]own telling!”

[087-031]“But, my dear child, you do not mean that every young gentleman and

[087-032]every young lady should fall in love with each other directly they

[087-033]meet! Such a doctrine would be very inconvenient.”

[087-034]“No, I do not mean that. Lord Lufton and Miss Grantly did not fall in

[087-035]love with each other, though you meant them to do so. But was it not

[087-036]quite as natural that Lord Lufton and Lucy should do so instead?”

[087-037]“It is generally thought, Fanny, that young ladies should not give

[087-038]loose to their affections until they have been certified of their

[087-039]friends’ approval.”

[087-040]“And that young gentlemen of fortune may amuse themselves as they

[087-041]please! I know that is what the world teaches, but I cannot agree to

[087-042]the justice of it. The terrible suffering which Lucy has to endure

[087-043]makes me cry out against it. She did not seek your son. The moment

[087-044]she began to suspect that there might be danger she avoided him

[087-045]scrupulously. She would not go down to Framley Court, though her not

[087-046]doing so was remarked by yourself. She would hardly go out about the

[087-047]place lest she should meet him. She was contented to put herself

[087-048]altogether in the background till he should have pleased to leave the

[087-049]place. But he—he came to her here, and insisted on seeing her. He

[087-050]found her when I was out, and declared himself determined to speak to

[087-051]her. What was she to do? She did try to escape, but he stopped her at

[087-052]the door. Was it her fault that he made her an offer?”

[087-053]“My dear, no one has said so.”

[087-054]“Yes, but you do say so when you tell me that young ladies should not

[087-055]give play to their affections without permission. He persisted in

[087-056]saying to her, here, all that it pleased him, though she implored him

[087-057]to be silent. I cannot tell the words she used, but she did implore

[087-058]him.”

[087-059]“I do not doubt that she behaved well.”

[087-060]“But he—he persisted, and begged her to accept his hand. She refused

[087-061]him then, Lady Lufton—not as some girls do, with a mock reserve, not

[087-062]intending to be taken at their words—but steadily, and, God forgive

[087-063]her, untruly. Knowing what your feelings would be, and knowing what

[087-064]the world would say, she declared to him that he was indifferent to

[087-065]her. What more could she do in your behalf?” And then Mrs. Robarts

[087-066]paused.

[087-067]“I shall wait till you have done, Fanny.”

[087-068]“You spoke of girls giving loose to their affections. She did not do

[087-069]so. She went about her work exactly as she had done before. She did

[087-070]not even speak to me of what had passed—not then, at least. She

[087-071]determined that it should all be as though it had never been. She

[087-072]had learned to love your son; but that was her misfortune and she

[087-073]would get over it as she might. Tidings came to us here that he was

[087-074]engaged, or about to engage himself, to Miss Grantly.”

[087-075]“Those tidings were untrue.”

[087-076]“Yes, we know that now; but she did not know it then. Of course she

[087-077]could not but suffer; but she suffered within herself.” Mrs. Robarts,

[087-078]as she said this, remembered the pony-carriage and how Puck had been

[087-079]beaten. “She made no complaint that he had ill-treated her—not even

[087-080]to herself. She had thought it right to reject his offer; and there,

[087-081]as far as he was concerned, was to be an end of it.”

[087-082]“That would be a matter of course, I should suppose.”

[087-083]“But it was not a matter of course, Lady Lufton. He returned from

[087-084]London to Framley on purpose to repeat his offer. He sent for her

[087-085]brother— You talk of a young lady waiting for her friends’ approval.

[087-086]In this matter who would be Lucy’s friends?”

[087-087]“You and Mr. Robarts, of course.”

[087-088]“Exactly; her only friends. Well, Lord Lufton sent for Mark and

[087-089]repeated his offer to him. Mind you, Mark had never heard a word of

[087-090]this before, and you may guess whether or no he was surprised. Lord

[087-091]Lufton repeated his offer in the most formal manner and claimed

[087-092]permission to see Lucy. She refused to see him. She has never seen

[087-093]him since that day when, in opposition to all her efforts, he made

[087-094]his way into this room. Mark,—as I think very properly,—would have

[087-095]allowed Lord Lufton to come up here. Looking at both their ages and

[087-096]position he could have had no right to forbid it. But Lucy positively

[087-097]refused to see your son, and sent him a message instead, of the

[087-098]purport of which you are now aware—that she would never accept him

[087-099]unless she did so at your request.”

[087-100]“It was a very proper message.”

[087-101]“I say nothing about that. Had she accepted him I would not have

[087-102]blamed her:—and so I told her, Lady Lufton.”

[087-103]“I cannot understand your saying that, Fanny.”

[087-104]“Well; I did say so. I don’t want to argue now about myself,—whether

[087-105]I was right or wrong, but I did say so. Whatever sanction I could

[087-106]give she would have had. But she again chose to sacrifice herself,

[087-107]although I believe she regards him with as true a love as ever a girl

[087-108]felt for a man. Upon my word I don’t know that she is right. Those

[087-109]considerations for the world may perhaps be carried too far.”

[087-110]“I think that she was perfectly right.”

[087-111]“Very well, Lady Lufton; I can understand that. But after such

[087-112]sacrifice on her part—a sacrifice made entirely to you—how can you

[087-113]talk of ‘not blaming her’? Is that the language in which you speak

[087-114]of those whose conduct from first to last has been superlatively

[087-115]excellent? If she is open to blame at all, it is—it is—”

[087-116]But here Mrs. Robarts stopped herself. In defending her sister she

[087-117]had worked herself almost into a passion; but such a state of feeling

[087-118]was not customary to her, and now that she had spoken her mind she

[087-119]sank suddenly into silence.

[087-120]“It seems to me, Fanny, that you almost regret Miss Robarts’

[087-121]decision,” said Lady Lufton.

[087-122]“My wish in this matter is for her happiness, and I regret anything

[087-123]that may mar it.”

[087-124]“You think nothing then of our welfare, and yet I do not know to whom

[087-125]I might have looked for hearty friendship and for sympathy in

[087-126]difficulties, if not to you?”

[087-127]Poor Mrs. Robarts was almost upset by this. A few months ago, before

[087-128]Lucy’s arrival, she would have declared that the interests of Lady

[087-129]Lufton’s family would have been paramount with her, after and next to

[087-130]those of her own husband. And even now, it seemed to argue so black

[087-131]an ingratitude on her part—this accusation that she was indifferent

[087-132]to them! From her childhood upwards she had revered and loved Lady

[087-133]Lufton, and for years had taught herself to regard her as an epitome

[087-134]of all that was good and gracious in woman. Lady Lufton’s theories of

[087-135]life had been accepted by her as the right theories, and those whom

[087-136]Lady Lufton had liked she had liked. But now it seemed that all these

[087-137]ideas which it had taken a life to build up were to be thrown to the

[087-138]ground, because she was bound to defend a sister-in-law whom she had

[087-139]only known for the last eight months. It was not that she regretted a

[087-140]word that she had spoken on Lucy’s behalf. Chance had thrown her and

[087-141]Lucy together, and, as Lucy was her sister, she should receive from

[087-142]her a sister’s treatment. But she did not the less feel how terrible

[087-143]would be the effect of any disseverance from Lady Lufton.

[087-144]“Oh, Lady Lufton,” she said, “do not say that.”

[087-145]“But, Fanny, dear, I must speak as I find. You were talking about

[087-146]clouds just now, and do you think that all this is not a cloud in my

[087-147]sky? Ludovic tells me that he is attached to Miss Robarts, and you

[087-148]tell me that she is attached to him; and I am called upon to decide

[087-149]between them. Her very act obliges me to do so.”

[087-150]“Dear Lady Lufton,” said Mrs. Robarts, springing from her seat. It

[087-151]seemed to her at the moment as though the whole difficulty were to be

[087-152]solved by an act of grace on the part of her old friend.

[087-153]“And yet I cannot approve of such a marriage,” said Lady Lufton.

[087-154]Mrs. Robarts returned to her seat, saying nothing further.

[087-155]“Is not that a cloud on one’s horizon?” continued her ladyship. “Do

[087-156]you think that I can be basking in the sunshine while I have such a

[087-157]weight upon my heart as that? Ludovic will soon be home, but instead

[087-158]of looking to his return with pleasure I dread it. I would prefer

[087-159]that he should remain in Norway. I would wish that he should stay

[087-160]away for months. And, Fanny, it is a great addition to my misfortune

[087-161]to feel that you do not sympathize with me.”

[087-162]Having said this, in a slow, sorrowful, and severe tone, Lady Lufton

[087-163]got up and took her departure. Of course Mrs. Robarts did not let her

[087-164]go without assuring her that she did sympathize with her,—did love

[087-165]her as she ever had loved her. But wounds cannot be cured as easily

[087-166]as they may be inflicted, and Lady Lufton went her way with much real

[087-167]sorrow at her heart. She was proud and masterful, fond of her own

[087-168]way, and much too careful of the worldly dignities to which her lot

[087-169]had called her: but she was a woman who could cause no sorrow to

[087-170]those she loved without deep sorrow to herself.

[087-171]CHAPTER XLII.

[087-172]TOUCHING PITCH.

[087-173]In these hot midsummer days, the end of June and the beginning of

[087-174]July, Mr. Sowerby had but an uneasy time of it. At his sister’s

[087-175]instance, he had hurried up to London, and there had remained for

[087-176]days in attendance on the lawyers. He had to see new lawyers, Miss

[087-177]Dunstable’s men of business, quiet old cautious gentlemen whose

[087-178]place of business was in a dark alley behind the Bank, Messrs. Slow

[087-179]and Bideawhile by name, who had no scruple in detaining him for

[087-180]hours while they or their clerks talked to him about anything or

[087-181]about nothing. It was of vital consequence to Mr. Sowerby that this

[087-182]business of his should be settled without delay, and yet these men,

[087-183]to whose care this settling was now confided, went on as though law

[087-184]processes were a sunny bank on which it delighted men to bask easily.

[087-185]And then, too, he had to go more than once to South Audley Street,

[087-186]which was a worse infliction; for the men in South Audley Street

[087-187]were less civil now than had been their wont. It was well understood

[087-188]there that Mr. Sowerby was no longer a client of the duke’s, but

[087-189]his opponent; no longer his nominee and dependant, but his enemy in

[087-190]the county. “Chaldicotes,” as old Mr. Gumption remarked to young Mr.

[087-191]Gagebee; “Chaldicotes, Gagebee, is a cooked goose, as far as Sowerby

[087-192]is concerned. And what difference could it make to him whether the

[087-193]duke is to own it or Miss Dunstable? For my part I cannot understand

[087-194]how a gentleman like Sowerby can like to see his property go into the

[087-195]hands of a gallipot wench whose money still smells of bad drugs. And

[087-196]nothing can be more ungrateful,” he said, “than Sowerby’s conduct.

[087-197]He has held the county for five-and-twenty years without expense;

[087-198]and now that the time for payment has come, he begrudges the price.”

[087-199]He called it no better than cheating, he did not—he, Mr. Gumption.

[087-200]According to his ideas Sowerby was attempting to cheat the duke. It

[087-201]may be imagined, therefore, that Mr. Sowerby did not feel any very

[087-202]great delight in attending at South Audley Street.
