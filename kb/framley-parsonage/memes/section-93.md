## Section 93

[093-001]“And then the dining-room,” said Jemima cook, in a tone almost of

[093-002]elation.

[093-003]“Yes; if they please.”

[093-004]“And then master’s book-room here; or perhaps the bedrooms, if you

[093-005]and master be still here.”

[093-006]“Any way they please, cook; it does not much signify,” said Mrs.

[093-007]Robarts. But for some days after that Jemima was by no means a

[093-008]favourite with her.

[093-009]The cook was hardly out of the room before a quick footstep was heard

[093-010]on the gravel before the window, and the hall door was immediately

[093-011]opened.

[093-012]“Where is your master?” said the well-known voice of Lord Lufton; and

[093-013]then in half a minute he also was in the book-room.

[093-014]“Mark, my dear fellow, what’s all this?” said he, in a cheery tone

[093-015]and with a pleasant face. “Did not you know that I was here? I came

[093-016]down yesterday; landed from Hamburg only yesterday morning. How do

[093-017]you do, Mrs. Robarts? This is a terrible bore, isn’t it?”

[093-018]Robarts, at the first moment, hardly knew how to speak to his old

[093-019]friend. He was struck dumb by the disgrace of his position; the more

[093-020]so as his misfortune was one which it was partly in the power of Lord

[093-021]Lufton to remedy. He had never yet borrowed money since he had filled

[093-022]a man’s position, but he had had words about money with the young

[093-023]peer, in which he knew that his friend had wronged him; and for this

[093-024]double reason he was now speechless.

[093-025]“Mr. Sowerby has betrayed him,” said Mrs. Robarts, wiping the tears

[093-026]from her eyes. Hitherto she had said no word against Sowerby, but now

[093-027]it was necessary to defend her husband.

[093-028]“No doubt about it. I believe he has always betrayed every one who

[093-029]has ever trusted him. I told you what he was, some time since; did I

[093-030]not? But, Mark, why on earth have you let it go so far as this? Would

[093-031]not Forrest help you?”

[093-032]“Mr. Forrest wanted him to sign more bills, and he would not do

[093-033]that,” said Mrs. Robarts, sobbing.

[093-034]“Bills are like dram-drinking,” said the discreet young lord: “when

[093-035]one once begins, it is very hard to leave off. Is it true that the

[093-036]men are here now, Mark?”

[093-037]“Yes, they are in the next room.”

[093-038]“What, in the drawing-room?”

[093-039]“They are making out a list of the things,” said Mrs. Robarts.

[093-040]“We must stop that at any rate,” said his lordship, walking off

[093-041]towards the scene of the operations; and as he left the room Mrs.

[093-042]Robarts followed him, leaving her husband by himself.

[093-043]“Why did you not send down to my mother?” said he, speaking hardly

[093-044]above a whisper, as they stood together in the hall.

[093-045]“He would not let me.”

[093-046]“But why not go yourself? or why not have written to me,—considering

[093-047]how intimate we are?”

[093-048]Mrs. Robarts could not explain to him that the peculiar intimacy

[093-049]between him and Lucy must have hindered her from doing so, even if

[093-050]otherwise it might have been possible; but she felt such was the

[093-051]case.

[093-052]“Well, my men, this is bad work you’re doing here,” said he, walking

[093-053]into the drawing-room. Whereupon the cook curtseyed low, and the

[093-054]bailiffs, knowing his lordship, stopped from their business and

[093-055]put their hands to their foreheads. “You must stop this, if you

[093-056]please,—at once. Come, let’s go out into the kitchen, or some place

[093-057]outside. I don’t like to see you here with your big boots and the pen

[093-058]and ink among the furniture.”

[093-059]“We ain’t a-done no harm, my lord, so please your lordship,” said

[093-060]Jemima cook.

[093-061]“And we is only a-doing our bounden dooties,” said one of the

[093-062]bailiffs.

[093-063]“As we is sworn to do, so please your lordship,” said the other.

[093-064]“And is wery sorry to be unconwenient, my lord, to any gen’leman or

[093-065]lady as is a gen’leman or lady. But accidents will happen, and then

[093-066]what can the likes of us do?” said the first.

[093-067]“Because we is sworn, my lord,” said the second. But, nevertheless,

[093-068]in spite of their oaths, and in spite also of the stern necessity

[093-069]which they pleaded, they ceased their operations at the instance of

[093-070]the peer. For the name of a lord is still great in England.

[093-071]“And now leave this, and let Mrs. Robarts go into her drawing-room.”

[093-072]“And, please your lordship, what is we to do? Who is we to look to?”

[093-073]In satisfying them absolutely on this point Lord Lufton had to

[093-074]use more than his influence as a peer. It was necessary that he

[093-075]should have pen and paper. But with pen and paper he did satisfy

[093-076]them;—satisfy them so far that they agreed to return to Stubbs’

[093-077]room, the former hospital, due stipulation having been made for the

[093-078]meals and beer, and there await the order to evacuate the premises

[093-079]which would no doubt, under his lordship’s influence, reach them on

[093-080]the following day. The meaning of all which was that Lord Lufton had

[093-081]undertaken to bear upon his own shoulder the whole debt due by Mr.

[093-082]Robarts.

[093-083]And then he returned to the book-room where Mark was still standing

[093-084]almost on the spot in which he had placed himself immediately after

[093-085]breakfast. Mrs. Robarts did not return, but went up among the

[093-086]children to counter-order such directions as she had given for the

[093-087]preparation of the nursery for the Philistines. “Mark,” he said,

[093-088]“do not trouble yourself about this more than you can help. The men

[093-089]have ceased doing anything, and they shall leave the place to-morrow

[093-090]morning.”

[093-091]“And how will the money—be paid?” said the poor clergyman.

[093-092]“Do not bother yourself about that at present. It shall so be managed

[093-093]that the burden shall fall ultimately on yourself—not on any one

[093-094]else. But I am sure it must be a comfort to you to know that your

[093-095]wife need not be driven out of her drawing-room.”

[093-096]“But, Lufton, I cannot allow you—after what has passed—and at the

[093-097]present moment—”

[093-098]“My dear fellow, I know all about it, and I am coming to that just

[093-099]now. You have employed Curling, and he shall settle it; and upon my

[093-100]word, Mark, you shall pay the bill. But, for the present emergency,

[093-101]the money is at my banker’s.”

[093-102]“But, Lufton—”

[093-103]“And to deal honestly, about Curling’s bill I mean, it ought to be as

[093-104]much my affair as your own. It was I that brought you into this mess

[093-105]with Sowerby, and I know now how unjust about it I was to you up in

[093-106]London. But the truth is that Sowerby’s treachery had nearly driven

[093-107]me wild. It has done the same to you since, I have no doubt.”

[093-108]“He has ruined me,” said Robarts.

[093-109]“No, he has not done that. No thanks to him though; he would not have

[093-110]scrupled to do it had it come in his way. The fact is, Mark, that you

[093-111]and I cannot conceive the depth of fraud in such a man as that. He

[093-112]is always looking for money; I believe that in all his hours of most

[093-113]friendly intercourse,—when he is sitting with you over your wine,

[093-114]and riding beside you in the field,—he is still thinking how he can

[093-115]make use of you to tide him over some difficulty. He has lived in

[093-116]that way till he has a pleasure in cheating, and has become so clever

[093-117]in his line of life that if you or I were with him again to-morrow he

[093-118]would again get the better of us. He is a man that must be absolutely

[093-119]avoided; I, at any rate, have learned to know so much.”

[093-120]In the expression of which opinion Lord Lufton was too hard upon

[093-121]poor Sowerby; as indeed we are all apt to be too hard in forming an

[093-122]opinion upon the rogues of the world. That Mr. Sowerby had been a

[093-123]rogue, I cannot deny. It is roguish to lie, and he had been a great

[093-124]liar. It is roguish to make promises which the promiser knows he

[093-125]cannot perform, and such had been Mr. Sowerby’s daily practice. It

[093-126]is roguish to live on other men’s money, and Mr. Sowerby had long

[093-127]been doing so. It is roguish, at least so I would hold it, to deal

[093-128]willingly with rogues; and Mr. Sowerby had been constant in such

[093-129]dealings. I do not know whether he had not at times fallen even into

[093-130]more palpable roguery than is proved by such practices as those

[093-131]enumerated. Though I have for him some tender feeling, knowing that

[093-132]there was still a touch of gentle bearing round his heart, an abiding

[093-133]taste for better things within him, I cannot acquit him from the

[093-134]great accusation. But, for all that, in spite of his acknowledged

[093-135]roguery, Lord Lufton was too hard upon him in his judgment. There was

[093-136]yet within him the means of repentance, could a _locus penitentiæ_

[093-137]have been supplied to him. He grieved bitterly over his own ill

[093-138]doings, and knew well what changes gentlehood would have demanded

[093-139]from him. Whether or no he had gone too far for all changes—whether

[093-140]the _locus penitentiæ_ was for him still a possibility—that was

[093-141]between him and a higher power.

[093-142]“I have no one to blame but myself,” said Mark, still speaking in the

[093-143]same heart-broken tone and with his face averted from his friend.

[093-144]The debt would now be paid, and the bailiffs would be expelled; but

[093-145]that would not set him right before the world. It would be known to

[093-146]all men—to all clergymen in the diocese—that the sheriff’s officers

[093-147]had been in charge of Framley Parsonage, and he could never again

[093-148]hold up his head in the close of Barchester.

[093-149]“My dear fellow, if we were all to make ourselves miserable for such

[093-150]a trifle as this—” said Lord Lufton, putting his arm affectionately

[093-151]on his friend’s shoulder.

[093-152]“But we are not all clergymen,” said Mark, and as he spoke he turned

[093-153]away to the window and Lord Lufton knew that the tears were on his

[093-154]cheek.

[093-155]Nothing was then said between them for some moments, after which Lord

[093-156]Lufton again spoke,—

[093-157]“Mark, my dear fellow!”

[093-158]“Well,” said Mark, with his face still turned towards the window.

[093-159]“You must remember one thing; in helping you over this stile, which

[093-160]will be really a matter of no inconvenience to me, I have a better

[093-161]right than that even of an old friend; I look upon you now as my

[093-162]brother-in-law.”

[093-163]Mark turned slowly round, plainly showing the tears upon his face.

[093-164]“Do you mean,” said he, “that anything more has taken place?”

[093-165]“I mean to make your sister my wife; she sent me word by you to say

[093-166]that she loved me, and I am not going to stand upon any nonsense

[093-167]after that. If she and I are both willing no one alive has a right to

[093-168]stand between us; and, by heavens, no one shall. I will do nothing

[093-169]secretly, so I tell you that, exactly as I have told her ladyship.”

[093-170]“But what does she say?”

[093-171]“She says nothing; but it cannot go on like that. My mother and I

[093-172]cannot live here together if she opposes me in this way. I do not

[093-173]want to frighten your sister by going over to her at Hogglestock, but

[093-174]I expect you to tell her so much as I now tell you, as coming from

[093-175]me; otherwise she will think that I have forgotten her.”

[093-176]“She will not think that.”

[093-177]“She need not; good-bye, old fellow. I’ll make it all right between

[093-178]you and her ladyship about this affair of Sowerby’s.”

[093-179]And then he took his leave and walked off to settle about the payment

[093-180]of the money.

[093-181]“Mother,” said he to Lady Lufton that evening, “you must not bring

[093-182]this affair of the bailiffs up against Robarts. It has been more my

[093-183]fault than his.”

[093-184]Hitherto not a word had been spoken between Lady Lufton and her

[093-185]son on the subject. She had heard with terrible dismay of what had

[093-186]happened, and had heard also that Lord Lufton had immediately gone

[093-187]to the parsonage. It was impossible, therefore, that she should now

[093-188]interfere. That the necessary money would be forthcoming she was

[093-189]aware, but that would not wipe out the terrible disgrace attached

[093-190]to an execution in a clergyman’s house. And then, too, he was

[093-191]her clergyman,—her own clergyman, selected, and appointed, and

[093-192]brought to Framley by herself, endowed with a wife of her own

[093-193]choosing, filled with good things by her own hand! It was a terrible

[093-194]misadventure, and she began to repent that she had ever heard the

[093-195]name of Robarts. She would not, however, have been slow to put forth

[093-196]the hand to lessen the evil by giving her own money, had this been

[093-197]either necessary or possible. But how could she interfere between

[093-198]Robarts and her son, especially when she remembered the proposed

[093-199]connection between Lucy and Lord Lufton?
