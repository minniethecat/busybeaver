## Section 49

[049-001]And then there came a question whether or no Griselda should go

[049-002]back to her mother; but this idea was very strongly opposed by Lady

[049-003]Lufton, and ultimately with success. “I really think the dear girl is

[049-004]very happy with me,” said Lady Lufton; “and if ever she is to belong

[049-005]to me more closely, it will be so well that we should know and love

[049-006]one another.”

[049-007]To tell the truth, Lady Lufton had been trying hard to know and love

[049-008]Griselda, but hitherto she had scarcely succeeded to the full extent

[049-009]of her wishes. That she loved Griselda was certain,—with that sort

[049-010]of love which springs from a person’s volition and not from the

[049-011]judgment. She had said all along to herself and others that she did

[049-012]love Griselda Grantly. She had admired the young lady’s face, liked

[049-013]her manner, approved of her fortune and family, and had selected her

[049-014]for a daughter-in-law in a somewhat impetuous manner. Therefore she

[049-015]loved her. But it was by no means clear to Lady Lufton that she did

[049-016]as yet know her young friend. The match was a plan of her own, and

[049-017]therefore she stuck to it as warmly as ever, but she began to have

[049-018]some misgivings whether or no the dear girl would be to her herself

[049-019]all that she had dreamed of in a daughter-in-law.

[049-020]“But, dear Lady Lufton,” said Mrs. Grantly, “is it not possible that

[049-021]we may put her affections to too severe a test? What, if she should

[049-022]learn to regard him, and then—”

[049-023]“Ah! if she did, I should have no fear of the result. If she showed

[049-024]anything like love for Ludovic, he would be at her feet in a moment.

[049-025]He is impulsive, but she is not.”

[049-026]“Exactly, Lady Lufton. It is his privilege to be impulsive and to

[049-027]sue for her affection, and hers to have her love sought for without

[049-028]making any demonstration. It is perhaps the fault of young ladies of

[049-029]the present day that they are too impulsive. They assume privileges

[049-030]which are not their own, and thus lose those which are.”

[049-031]“Quite true! I quite agree with you. It is probably that very feeling

[049-032]that has made me think so highly of Griselda. But then—” But then a

[049-033]young lady, though she need not jump down a gentleman’s throat, or

[049-034]throw herself into his face, may give some signs that she is made of

[049-035]flesh and blood; especially when her papa and mamma and all belonging

[049-036]to her are so anxious to make the path of her love run smooth. That

[049-037]was what was passing through Lady Lufton’s mind; but she did not say

[049-038]it all; she merely looked it.

[049-039]“I don’t think she will ever allow herself to indulge in an

[049-040]unauthorized passion,” said Mrs. Grantly.

[049-041]“I am sure she will not,” said Lady Lufton, with ready agreement,

[049-042]fearing perhaps in her heart that Griselda would never indulge in any

[049-043]passion, authorized or unauthorized.

[049-044]“I don’t know whether Lord Lufton sees much of her now,” said Mrs.

[049-045]Grantly, thinking perhaps of that promise of Lady Lufton’s with

[049-046]reference to his lordship’s spare time.

[049-047]“Just lately, during these changes, you know, everybody has been so

[049-048]much engaged. Ludovic has been constantly at the House, and then men

[049-049]find it so necessary to be at their clubs just now.”

[049-050]“Yes, yes, of course,” said Mrs. Grantly, who was not at all disposed

[049-051]to think little of the importance of the present crisis, or to wonder

[049-052]that men should congregate together when such deeds were to be done

[049-053]as those which now occupied the breasts of the Queen’s advisers.

[049-054]At last, however, the two mothers perfectly understood each other.

[049-055]Griselda was still to remain with Lady Lufton; and was to accept her

[049-056]ladyship’s son, if he could only be induced to exercise his privilege

[049-057]of asking her; but in the meantime, as this seemed to be doubtful,

[049-058]Griselda was not to be debarred from her privilege of making what use

[049-059]she could of any other string which she might have to her bow.

[049-060]“But, mamma,” said Griselda, in a moment of unwatched intercourse

[049-061]between the mother and daughter, “is it really true that they are

[049-062]going to make papa a bishop?”

[049-063]“We can tell nothing as yet, my dear. People in the world are talking

[049-064]about it. Your papa has been a good deal with Lord De Terrier.”

[049-065]“And isn’t he prime minister?”

[049-066]“Oh, yes; I am happy to say that he is.”

[049-067]“I thought the prime minister could make any one a bishop that he

[049-068]chooses,—any clergyman, that is.”

[049-069]“But there is no see vacant,” said Mrs. Grantly.

[049-070]“Then there isn’t any chance,” said Griselda, looking very glum.

[049-071]“They are going to have an Act of Parliament for making two more

[049-072]bishops. That’s what they are talking about at least. And if they

[049-073]do—”

[049-074]“Papa will be Bishop of Westminster—won’t he? And we shall live in

[049-075]London?”

[049-076]“But you must not talk about it, my dear.”

[049-077]“No, I won’t. But, mamma, a Bishop of Westminster will be higher than

[049-078]a Bishop of Barchester; won’t he? I shall so like to be able to snub

[049-079]those Miss Proudies.” It will therefore be seen that there were

[049-080]matters on which even Griselda Grantly could be animated. Like the

[049-081]rest of her family she was devoted to the Church.

[049-082]Late on that afternoon the archdeacon returned home to dine in Mount

[049-083]Street, having spent the whole of the day between the Treasury

[049-084]chambers, a meeting of Convocation, and his club. And when he did get

[049-085]home it was soon manifest to his wife that he was not laden with good

[049-086]news.

[049-087]“It is almost incredible,” he said, standing with his back to the

[049-088]drawing-room fire.

[049-089]“What is incredible?” said his wife, sharing her husband’s anxiety to

[049-090]the full.

[049-091]“If I had not learned it as fact, I would not have believed it, even

[049-092]of Lord Brock,” said the archdeacon.

[049-093]“Learned what?” said the anxious wife.

[049-094]“After all, they are going to oppose the bill.”

[049-095]“Impossible!” said Mrs. Grantly.

[049-096]“But they are.”

[049-097]“The bill for the two new bishops, archdeacon? oppose their own

[049-098]bill!”

[049-099]“Yes—oppose their own bill. It is almost incredible; but so it is.

[049-100]Some changes have been forced upon us; little things which they

[049-101]had forgotten—quite minor matters; and they now say that they

[049-102]will be obliged to divide against us on these twopenny-halfpenny,

[049-103]hair-splitting points. It is Lord Brock’s own doing too, after

[049-104]all that he said about abstaining from factious opposition to the

[049-105]government.”

[049-106]“I believe there is nothing too bad or too false for that man,” said

[049-107]Mrs. Grantly.

[049-108]“After all they said, too, when they were in power themselves, as to

[049-109]the present government opposing the cause of religion! They declare

[049-110]now that Lord De Terrier cannot be very anxious about it, as he had

[049-111]so many good reasons against it a few weeks ago. Is it not dreadful

[049-112]that there should be such double-dealing in men in such positions?”

[049-113]“It is sickening,” said Mrs. Grantly.

[049-114]And then there was a pause between them as each thought of the injury

[049-115]that was done to them.

[049-116]“But, archdeacon—”

[049-117]“Well?”

[049-118]“Could you not give up those small points and shame them into

[049-119]compliance?”

[049-120]“Nothing would shame them.”

[049-121]“But would it not be well to try?”

[049-122]The game was so good a one, and the stake so important, that Mrs.

[049-123]Grantly felt that it would be worth playing for to the last.

[049-124]“It is no good.”

[049-125]“But I certainly would suggest it to Lord De Terrier. I am sure the

[049-126]country would go along with him; at any rate the Church would.”

[049-127]“It is impossible,” said the archdeacon. “To tell the truth, it did

[049-128]occur to me. But some of them down there seemed to think that it

[049-129]would not do.”

[049-130]Mrs. Grantly sat awhile on the sofa, still meditating in her mind

[049-131]whether there might not yet be some escape from so terrible a

[049-132]downfall.

[049-133]“But, archdeacon—”

[049-134]“I’ll go upstairs and dress,” said he, in despondency.

[049-135]“But, archdeacon, surely the present ministry may have a majority on

[049-136]such a subject as that; I thought they were sure of a majority now.”

[049-137]“No; not sure.”

[049-138]“But at any rate the chances are in their favour? I do hope they’ll

[049-139]do their duty, and exert themselves to keep their members together.”

[049-140]And then the archdeacon told out the whole of the truth.

[049-141]“Lord De Terrier says that under the present circumstances he will

[049-142]not bring the matter forward this session at all. So we had better go

[049-143]back to Plumstead.”

[049-144]Mrs. Grantly then felt that there was nothing further to be said, and

[049-145]it will be proper that the historian should drop a veil over their

[049-146]sufferings.

[049-147]CHAPTER XXIV.

[049-148]MAGNA EST VERITAS.

[049-149]It was made known to the reader that in the early part of the winter

[049-150]Mr. Sowerby had a scheme for retrieving his lost fortunes, and

[049-151]setting himself right in the world, by marrying that rich heiress,

[049-152]Miss Dunstable. I fear my friend Sowerby does not, at present, stand

[049-153]high in the estimation of those who have come on with me thus far in

[049-154]this narrative. He has been described as a spendthrift and gambler,

[049-155]and as one scarcely honest in his extravagance and gambling. But

[049-156]nevertheless there are worse men than Mr. Sowerby, and I am not

[049-157]prepared to say that, should he be successful with Miss Dunstable,

[049-158]that lady would choose by any means the worst of the suitors who are

[049-159]continually throwing themselves at her feet. Reckless as this man

[049-160]always appeared to be, reckless as he absolutely was, there was still

[049-161]within his heart a desire for better things, and in his mind an

[049-162]understanding that he had hitherto missed the career of an honest

[049-163]English gentleman. He was proud of his position as member for his

[049-164]county, though hitherto he had done so little to grace it; he was

[049-165]proud of his domain at Chaldicotes, though the possession of it had

[049-166]so nearly passed out of his own hands; he was proud of the old

[049-167]blood that flowed in his veins; and he was proud also of that easy,

[049-168]comfortable, gay manner, which went so far in the world’s judgment

[049-169]to atone for his extravagance and evil practices. If only he could

[049-170]get another chance, as he now said to himself, things should go very

[049-171]differently with him. He would utterly forswear the whole company of

[049-172]Tozers. He would cease to deal in bills, and to pay heaven only knows

[049-173]how many hundred per cent. for his moneys. He would no longer prey

[049-174]upon his friends, and would redeem his title-deeds from the clutches

[049-175]of the Duke of Omnium. If only he could get another chance!

[049-176]Miss Dunstable’s fortune would do all this and ever so much more, and

[049-177]then, moreover, Miss Dunstable was a woman whom he really liked. She

[049-178]was not soft, feminine, or pretty, nor was she very young; but she

[049-179]was clever, self-possessed, and quite able to hold her own in any

[049-180]class; and as to age, Mr. Sowerby was not very young himself. In

[049-181]making such a match he would have no cause of shame. He could speak

[049-182]of it before his friends without fear of their grimaces, and ask

[049-183]them to his house, with the full assurance that the head of his

[049-184]table would not disgrace him. And then as the scheme grew clearer

[049-185]and clearer to him, he declared to himself that if he should

[049-186]be successful, he would use her well, and not rob her of her

[049-187]money—beyond what was absolutely necessary.

[049-188]He had intended to have laid his fortunes at her feet at Chaldicotes;

[049-189]but the lady had been coy. Then the deed was to have been done at

[049-190]Gatherum Castle, but the lady ran away from Gatherum Castle just at

[049-191]the time on which he had fixed. And since that, one circumstance

[049-192]after another had postponed the affair in London, till now at last

[049-193]he was resolved that he would know his fate, let it be what it might.

[049-194]If he could not contrive that things should speedily be arranged,

[049-195]it might come to pass that he would be altogether debarred from

[049-196]presenting himself to the lady as Mr. Sowerby of Chaldicotes. Tidings

[049-197]had reached him, through Mr. Fothergill, that the duke would be glad

[049-198]to have matters arranged; and Mr. Sowerby well knew the meaning of

[049-199]that message.
