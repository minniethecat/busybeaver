## Section 88

[088-001]And then rumour was spread about among all the bill-discounting

[088-002]leeches that blood was once more to be sucked from the Sowerby

[088-003]carcase. The rich Miss Dunstable had taken up his affairs; so much

[088-004]as that became known in the purlieus of the Goat and Compasses. Tom

[088-005]Tozer’s brother declared that she and Sowerby were going to make

[088-006]a match of it, and that any scrap of paper with Sowerby’s name

[088-007]on it would become worth its weight in bank-notes; but Tom Tozer

[088-008]himself—Tom, who was the real hero of the family—pooh-poohed at

[088-009]this, screwing up his nose, and alluding in most contemptuous terms

[088-010]to his brother’s softness. He knew better—as was indeed the fact.

[088-011]Miss Dunstable was buying up the squire, and by jingo she should buy

[088-012]them up—them, the Tozers, as well as others! They knew their value,

[088-013]the Tozers did;—whereupon they became more than ordinarily active.

[088-014]From them and all their brethren Mr. Sowerby at this time endeavoured

[088-015]to keep his distance, but his endeavours were not altogether

[088-016]effectual. Whenever he could escape for a day or two from the lawyers

[088-017]he ran down to Chaldicotes; but Tom Tozer in his perseverance

[088-018]followed him there, and boldly sent in his name by the servant at the

[088-019]front-door.

[088-020]“Mr. Sowerby is not just at home at the present moment,” said the

[088-021]well-trained domestic.

[088-022]“I’ll wait about then,” said Tom, seating himself on an heraldic

[088-023]stone griffin which flanked the big stone steps before the house.

[088-024]And in this way Mr. Tozer gained his purpose. Sowerby was still

[088-025]contesting the county, and it behoved him not to let his enemies say

[088-026]that he was hiding himself. It had been a part of his bargain with

[088-027]Miss Dunstable that he should contest the county. She had taken it

[088-028]into her head that the duke had behaved badly, and she had resolved

[088-029]that he should be made to pay for it. “The duke,” she said, “had

[088-030]meddled long enough;” she would now see whether the Chaldicotes

[088-031]interest would not suffice of itself to return a member for the

[088-032]county, even in opposition to the duke. Mr. Sowerby himself was so

[088-033]harassed at the time, that he would have given way on this point if

[088-034]he had had the power; but Miss Dunstable was determined, and he was

[088-035]obliged to yield to her. In this manner Mr. Tom Tozer succeeded and

[088-036]did make his way into Mr. Sowerby’s presence—of which intrusion one

[088-037]effect was the following letter from Mr. Sowerby to his friend Mark

[088-038]Robarts:—

[088-039]Chaldicotes, July, 185—.

[088-040]MY DEAR ROBARTS,—I am so harassed at the present moment

[088-041]by an infinity of troubles of my own that I am almost

[088-042]callous to those of other people. They say that prosperity

[088-043]makes a man selfish. I have never tried that, but I am

[088-044]quite sure that adversity does so. Nevertheless I am

[088-045]anxious about those bills of yours—

[088-046]“Bills of mine!” said Robarts to himself, as he walked up and down

[088-047]the shrubbery path at the parsonage, reading this letter. This

[088-048]happened a day or two after his visit to the lawyer at Barchester.

[088-049]—and would rejoice greatly if I thought that I could

[088-050]save you from any further annoyance about them. That kite,

[088-051]Tom Tozer, has just been with me, and insists that both

[088-052]of them shall be paid. He knows—no one better—that no

[088-053]consideration was given for the latter. But he knows also

[088-054]that the dealing was not with him, nor even with his

[088-055]brother, and he will be prepared to swear that he gave

[088-056]value for both. He would swear anything for five hundred

[088-057]pounds—or for half the money, for that matter. I do not

[088-058]think that the father of mischief ever let loose upon the

[088-059]world a greater rascal than Tom Tozer.

[088-060]He declares that nothing shall induce him to take one

[088-061]shilling less than the whole sum of nine hundred pounds.

[088-062]He has been brought to this by hearing that my debts are

[088-063]about to be paid. Heaven help me! The meaning of that is

[088-064]that these wretched acres, which are now mortgaged to one

[088-065]millionnaire, are to change hands and be mortgaged to

[088-066]another instead. By this exchange I may possibly obtain

[088-067]the benefit of having a house to live in for the next

[088-068]twelve months, but no other. Tozer, however, is altogether

[088-069]wrong in his scent; and the worst of it is that his malice

[088-070]will fall on you rather than on me.

[088-071]What I want you to do is this: let us pay him one hundred

[088-072]pounds between us. Though I sell the last sorry jade of a

[088-073]horse I have, I will make up fifty; and I know you can, at

[088-074]any rate, do as much as that. Then do you accept a bill,

[088-075]conjointly with me, for eight hundred. It shall be done

[088-076]in Forrest’s presence, and handed to him; and you shall

[088-077]receive back the two old bills into your own hands at the

[088-078]same time. This new bill should be timed to run ninety

[088-079]days; and I will move heaven and earth during that time to

[088-080]have it included in the general schedule of my debts which

[088-081]are to be secured on the Chaldicotes property.

[088-082]The meaning of which was that Miss Dunstable was to be cozened into

[088-083]paying the money under an idea that it was part of the sum covered by

[088-084]the existing mortgage.

[088-085]What you said the other day at Barchester, as to never

[088-086]executing another bill, is very well as regards future

[088-087]transactions. Nothing can be wiser than such a resolution.

[088-088]But it would be folly—worse than folly—if you were

[088-089]to allow your furniture to be seized when the means of

[088-090]preventing it are so ready to your hand. By leaving the

[088-091]new bill in Forrest’s hands you may be sure that you are

[088-092]safe from the claws of such birds of prey as these Tozers.

[088-093]Even if I cannot get it settled when the three months are

[088-094]over, Forrest will enable you to make any arrangement that

[088-095]may be most convenient.

[088-096]For Heaven’s sake, my dear fellow, do not refuse this.

[088-097]You can hardly conceive how it weighs upon me, this fear

[088-098]that bailiffs should make their way into your wife’s

[088-099]drawing-room. I know you think ill of me, and I do not

[088-100]wonder at it. But you would be less inclined to do so if

[088-101]you knew how terribly I am punished. Pray let me hear that

[088-102]you will do as I counsel you.

[088-103]Yours always faithfully,

[088-104]N. SOWERBY.

[088-105]In answer to which the parson wrote a very short reply:—

[088-106]Framley, July, 185—.

[088-107]MY DEAR SOWERBY,—

[088-108]I will sign no more bills on any consideration.

[088-109]Yours truly,

[088-110]MARK ROBARTS.

[088-111]And then having written this, and having shown it to his wife, he

[088-112]returned to the shrubbery walk and paced it up and down, looking

[088-113]every now and then to Sowerby’s letter as he thought over all the

[088-114]past circumstances of his friendship with that gentleman.

[088-115]That the man who had written this letter should be his friend—that

[088-116]very fact was a disgrace to him. Sowerby so well knew himself and his

[088-117]own reputation, that he did not dare to suppose that his own word

[088-118]would be taken for anything,—not even when the thing promised was an

[088-119]act of the commonest honesty. “The old bills shall be given back into

[088-120]your own hands,” he had declared with energy, knowing that his friend

[088-121]and correspondent would not feel himself secure against further fraud

[088-122]under any less stringent guarantee. This gentleman, this county

[088-123]member, the owner of Chaldicotes, with whom Mark Robarts had been so

[088-124]anxious to be on terms of intimacy, had now come to such a phase of

[088-125]life that he had given over speaking of himself as an honest man. He

[088-126]had become so used to suspicion that he argued of it as of a thing

[088-127]of course. He knew that no one could trust either his spoken or

[088-128]his written word, and he was content to speak and to write without

[088-129]attempt to hide this conviction.

[088-130]And this was the man whom he had been so glad to call his friend; for

[088-131]whose sake he had been willing to quarrel with Lady Lufton, and at

[088-132]whose instance he had unconsciously abandoned so many of the best

[088-133]resolutions of his life. He looked back now, as he walked there

[088-134]slowly, still holding the letter in his hand, to the day when he had

[088-135]stopped at the school-house and written his letter to Mr. Sowerby,

[088-136]promising to join the party at Chaldicotes. He had been so eager then

[088-137]to have his own way, that he would not permit himself to go home and

[088-138]talk the matter over with his wife. He thought also of the manner in

[088-139]which he had been tempted to the house of the Duke of Omnium, and

[088-140]the conviction on his mind at the time that his giving way to that

[088-141]temptation would surely bring him to evil. And then he remembered the

[088-142]evening in Sowerby’s bedroom, when the bill had been brought out, and

[088-143]he had allowed himself to be persuaded to put his name upon it;—not

[088-144]because he was willing in this way to assist his friend, but because

[088-145]he was unable to refuse. He had lacked the courage to say, “No,”

[088-146]though he knew at the time how gross was the error which he was

[088-147]committing. He had lacked the courage to say, “No,” and hence had

[088-148]come upon him and on his household all this misery and cause for

[088-149]bitter repentance.

[088-150]I have written much of clergymen, but in doing so I have endeavoured

[088-151]to portray them as they bear on our social life rather than to

[088-152]describe the mode and working of their professional careers. Had

[088-153]I done the latter I could hardly have steered clear of subjects on

[088-154]which it has not been my intention to pronounce an opinion, and I

[088-155]should either have laden my fiction with sermons or I should have

[088-156]degraded my sermons into fiction. Therefore I have said but little in

[088-157]my narrative of this man’s feelings or doings as a clergyman.

[088-158]But I must protest against its being on this account considered that

[088-159]Mr. Robarts was indifferent to the duties of his clerical position.

[088-160]He had been fond of pleasure and had given way to temptation,—as is

[088-161]so customarily done by young men of six-and-twenty, who are placed

[088-162]beyond control and who have means at command. Had he remained as a

[088-163]curate till that age, subject in all his movements to the eye of

[088-164]a superior, he would, we may say, have put his name to no bills,

[088-165]have ridden after no hounds, have seen nothing of the iniquities of

[088-166]Gatherum Castle. There are men of twenty-six as fit to stand alone

[088-167]as ever they will be—fit to be prime ministers, heads of schools,

[088-168]judges on the bench—almost fit to be bishops; but Mark Robarts had

[088-169]not been one of them. He had within him many aptitudes for good, but

[088-170]not the strengthened courage of a man to act up to them. The stuff of

[088-171]which his manhood was to be formed had been slow of growth, as it is

[088-172]with many men; and, consequently, when temptation was offered to him,

[088-173]he had fallen.

[088-174]But he deeply grieved over his own stumbling, and from time to time,

[088-175]as his periods of penitence came upon him, he resolved that he would

[088-176]once more put his shoulder to the wheel as became one who fights

[088-177]upon earth that battle for which he had put on his armour. Over and

[088-178]over again did he think of those words of Mr. Crawley, and now as

[088-179]he walked up and down the path, crumpling Mr. Sowerby’s letter in

[088-180]his hand, he thought of them again—“It is a terrible falling off;

[088-181]terrible in the fall, but doubly terrible through that difficulty

[088-182]of returning.” Yes; that is a difficulty which multiplies itself

[088-183]in a fearful ratio as one goes on pleasantly running down the

[088-184]path—whitherward? Had it come to that with him that he could not

[088-185]return—that he could never again hold up his head with a safe

[088-186]conscience as the pastor of his parish! It was Sowerby who had led

[088-187]him into this misery, who had brought on him this ruin? But then

[088-188]had not Sowerby paid him? Had not that stall which he now held in

[088-189]Barchester been Sowerby’s gift? He was a poor man now—a distressed,

[088-190]poverty-stricken man; but nevertheless he wished with all his

[088-191]heart that he had never become a sharer in the good things of the

[088-192]Barchester chapter.
