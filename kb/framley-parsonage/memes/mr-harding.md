# Mr. Harding

Mr. Harding — the former Warden of Hiram's Hospital and now, in the time of *Framley Parsonage*, an aged precentor of Barchester Cathedral — is the moral touchstone of Trollope's Barsetshire novels. Father of [[mrs-grantly|Mrs. Grantly]] (and thus father-in-law of [[dr-grantly|Dr. Grantly]]), grandfather of [[griselda-grantly|Griselda Grantly]], and the central figure of Trollope's earlier novel *The Warden*, he appears in *Framley Parsonage* as a figure of quiet, weathered grace — a man who has passed through the fires of public controversy and emerged with his gentleness intact.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Harding appears only briefly in *Framley Parsonage*, but his presence carries the weight of the entire Barsetshire saga. He is described as "an old clergyman of the chapter" who still chants the Litany from his stall in the cathedral — "an old cathedral hack" in the narrator's affectionately ironic phrase (013-010) (084-180). At the Barchester lecture, he quietly declines Mrs. Proudie's invitation to sit close behind her, preferring the company of his old friend Mr. Thorne — a small gesture that encapsulates a lifetime of preferring genuine friendship to ecclesiastical politics (013-013) (013-014).

In the Grantly family drama, he appears as "dear old Mr. Harding, his wife's father" — the patriarch whose own history of suffering under public scrutiny gives him a perspective denied to his more combative son-in-law (053-036). When he holds Griselda's hand, the gesture is tender, but when he learns she is not engaged after all, he relinquishes it with a gentle "Oh! I did not know" — a moment of quiet pathos that Trollope renders with characteristic understatement (085-010) (085-026).

## Character Traits

- **Gentleness**: Mr. Harding's defining quality is his gentleness — a trait that in Trollope's hands becomes not weakness but a form of moral strength. He is kind where others are ambitious, quiet where others are loud.
- **Unworldliness**: Unlike his son-in-law the Archdeacon, Mr. Harding has no appetite for ecclesiastical politics. He prefers old friends to advantageous seats, the Litany to the drawing-room.
- **Endurance**: The man who was once the Warden of Hiram's Hospital, and who suffered the public ordeal chronicled in *The Warden*, has endured. His presence at Barchester is a reminder that goodness can survive the world's attentions.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-grantly|Mrs. Grantly]]**: His daughter, now the wife of the Archdeacon and a formidable presence in her own right. The Grantlys are the family into which Mr. Harding's quiet virtues have been absorbed and, to some degree, transformed (053-036).
- **[[dr-grantly|Dr. Grantly]]**: His son-in-law, whose worldly ambition is in constant, unstated tension with Mr. Harding's own unworldly disposition.
- **[[griselda-grantly|Griselda Grantly]]**: His granddaughter, whom he holds in tender affection. Their brief scene together — his hand holding hers, his quiet withdrawal when he learns there is no engagement — is one of the novel's most delicately rendered family moments (085-010) (085-026).
- **[[mrs-proudie|Mrs. Proudie]]**: The bishop's wife who tries to place him close to her at the lecture — a symbolic attempt to claim his moral authority for her own sphere — and who is quietly but firmly rebuffed (013-011) (013-012) (013-013).

## Key Scenes

- **The Barchester lecture (Chapter 13)**: Mr. Harding attends the lecture on the South Sea islands. Mrs. Proudie shakes his hand "very graciously" and makes way for him to sit close behind her — but he "did not so please," instead returning quietly to sit with his old friend Mr. Thorne, "thereby angering Mrs. Proudie, as might easily be seen by her face" (013-010) (013-011) (013-012) (013-013).
- **The family reference (Chapter 53)**: He is spoken of as "dear old Mr. Harding, his wife's father" — a brief reference that situates him within the Grantly family web and reminds the reader of his long history (053-036).
- **With Griselda (Chapter 85)**: In a touching moment, Mr. Harding holds his granddaughter's hand as they speak, only to relinquish it when he realises the marriage that would have made her Lady Dumbello is not, after all, to be (085-010) (085-026).
- **The cathedral stall (Chapter 84)**: The image of Mr. Harding as "an old cathedral hack" still chanting the Litany from his stall distils a lifetime of faithful, unspectacular service into a single indelible picture (084-180).

## Social & Historical Context

Mr. Harding is one of the great creations of Victorian fiction — a genuinely good man placed at the centre of institutional corruption and emerging, not unscathed, but unbroken. Readers of *Framley Parsonage* who know *The Warden* will recognise in the "old clergyman of the chapter" the same man who once resigned the wardenship of Hiram's Hospital rather than profit from what he had come to believe was an unjust arrangement. His quiet presence in *Framley Parsonage* — declining Mrs. Proudie's patronage, holding his granddaughter's hand, chanting the Litany — is Trollope's way of reminding us that goodness persists, not in grand gestures, but in small refusals, day by day (013-010) (084-180).
