## Section 77

[077-001]“These are comforts which we have no right to expect. I could not

[077-002]have done much for poor Mary; but what a man could have done should

[077-003]not have been wanting.”

[077-004]“I am sure of it; I know it well. What any man could do by himself

[077-005]you would do—excepting one thing.” And the dean as he spoke looked

[077-006]full into the other’s face.

[077-007]“And what is there I would not do?” said Crawley.

[077-008]“Sacrifice your own pride.”

[077-009]“My pride?”

[077-010]“Yes; your own pride.”

[077-011]“I have had but little pride this many a day. Arabin, you do not know

[077-012]what my life has been. How is a man to be proud who—” And then he

[077-013]stopped himself, not wishing to go through the catalogue of those

[077-014]grievances, which, as he thought, had killed the very germs of pride

[077-015]within him, or to insist by spoken words on his poverty, his wants,

[077-016]and the injustice of his position. “No; I wish I could be proud; but

[077-017]the world has been too heavy to me, and I have forgotten all that.”

[077-018]“How long have I known you, Crawley?”

[077-019]“How long? Ah dear! a life-time nearly, now.”

[077-020]“And we were like brothers once.”

[077-021]“Yes; we were equal as brothers then—in our fortunes, our tastes,

[077-022]and our modes of life.”

[077-023]“And yet you would begrudge me the pleasure of putting my hand in my

[077-024]pocket, and relieving the inconveniences which have been thrown on

[077-025]you, and those you love better than yourself, by the chances of your

[077-026]fate in life.”

[077-027]“I will live on no man’s charity,” said Crawley, with an abruptness

[077-028]which amounted almost to an expression of anger.

[077-029]“And is not that pride?”

[077-030]“No—yes;—it is a species of pride, but not that pride of which

[077-031]you spoke. A man cannot be honest if he have not some pride. You

[077-032]yourself;—would you not rather starve than become a beggar?”

[077-033]“I would rather beg than see my wife starve,” said Arabin.

[077-034]Crawley when he heard these words turned sharply round, and stood

[077-035]with his back to the dean, with his hands still behind him, and with

[077-036]his eyes fixed upon the ground.

[077-037]“But in this case there is no question of begging,” continued the

[077-038]dean. “I, out of those superfluities which it has pleased God to put

[077-039]at my disposal, am anxious to assist the needs of those whom I love.”

[077-040]“She is not starving,” said Crawley, in a voice very bitter, but

[077-041]still intended to be exculpatory of himself.

[077-042]“No, my dear friend; I know she is not, and do not you be angry

[077-043]with me because I have endeavoured to put the matter to you in the

[077-044]strongest language I could use.”

[077-045]“You look at it, Arabin, from one side only; I can only look at it

[077-046]from the other. It is very sweet to give; I do not doubt that. But

[077-047]the taking of what is given is very bitter. Gift bread chokes in

[077-048]a man’s throat and poisons his blood, and sits like lead upon the

[077-049]heart. You have never tried it.”

[077-050]“But that is the very fault for which I blame you. That is the pride

[077-051]which I say you ought to sacrifice.”

[077-052]“And why should I be called on to do so? Is not the labourer worthy

[077-053]of his hire? Am I not able to work, and willing? Have I not always

[077-054]had my shoulder to the collar, and is it right that I should now be

[077-055]contented with the scraps from a rich man’s kitchen? Arabin, you

[077-056]and I were equal once and we were then friends, understanding each

[077-057]other’s thoughts and sympathizing with each other’s sorrows. But it

[077-058]cannot be so now.”

[077-059]“If there be such inability, it is all with you.”

[077-060]“It is all with me,—because in our connection the pain would all be

[077-061]on my side. It would not hurt you to see me at your table with worn

[077-062]shoes and a ragged shirt. I do not think so meanly of you as that.

[077-063]You would give me your feast to eat though I were not clad a tithe as

[077-064]well as the menial behind your chair. But it would hurt me to know

[077-065]that there were those looking at me who thought me unfit to sit in

[077-066]your rooms.”

[077-067]“That is the pride of which I speak;—false pride.”

[077-068]“Call it so if you will; but, Arabin, no preaching of yours can alter

[077-069]it. It is all that is left to me of my manliness. That poor broken

[077-070]reed who is lying there sick,—who has sacrificed all the world to

[077-071]her love for me,—who is the mother of my children, and the partner

[077-072]of my sorrows and the wife of my bosom,—even she cannot change me in

[077-073]this, though she pleads with the eloquence of all her wants. Not even

[077-074]for her can I hold out my hand for a dole.”

[077-075]They had now come back to the door of the house, and Mr. Crawley,

[077-076]hardly conscious of what he was doing, was preparing to enter.

[077-077]“Will Mrs. Crawley be able to see me if I come in?” said the dean.

[077-078]“Oh, stop; no; you had better not do so,” said Mr. Crawley. “You, no

[077-079]doubt, might be subject to infection, and then Mrs. Arabin would be

[077-080]frightened.”

[077-081]“I do not care about it in the least,” said the dean.

[077-082]“But it is of no use; you had better not. Her room, I fear, is

[077-083]quite unfit for you to see; and the whole house, you know, may be

[077-084]infected.”

[077-085]Dr. Arabin by this time was in the sitting-room; but seeing that his

[077-086]friend was really anxious that he should not go farther, he did not

[077-087]persist.

[077-088]“It will be a comfort to us, at any rate, to know that Miss Robarts

[077-089]is with her.”

[077-090]“The young lady is very good—very good indeed,” said Crawley; “but

[077-091]I trust she will return to her home to-morrow. It is impossible that

[077-092]she should remain in so poor a house as mine. There will be nothing

[077-093]here of all the things that she will want.”

[077-094]The dean thought that Lucy Robarts’s wants during her present

[077-095]occupation of nursing would not be so numerous as to make her

[077-096]continued sojourn in Mrs. Crawley’s sick room impossible, and

[077-097]therefore took his leave with a satisfied conviction that the poor

[077-098]lady would not be left wholly to the somewhat unskilful nursing of

[077-099]her husband.

[077-100]CHAPTER XXXVII.

[077-101]MR. SOWERBY WITHOUT COMPANY.

[077-102]And now there were going to be wondrous doings in West Barsetshire,

[077-103]and men’s minds were much disturbed. The fiat had gone forth from the

[077-104]high places, and the Queen had dissolved her faithful Commons. The

[077-105]giants, finding that they could effect little or nothing with the old

[077-106]House, had resolved to try what a new venture would do for them, and

[077-107]the hubbub of a general election was to pervade the country. This

[077-108]produced no inconsiderable irritation and annoyance, for the House

[077-109]was not as yet quite three years old; and members of Parliament,

[077-110]though they naturally feel a constitutional pleasure in meeting

[077-111]their friends and in pressing the hands of their constituents, are,

[077-112]nevertheless, so far akin to the lower order of humanity that they

[077-113]appreciate the danger of losing their seats; and the certainty of

[077-114]a considerable outlay in their endeavours to retain them is not

[077-115]agreeable to the legislative mind.

[077-116]Never did the old family fury between the gods and giants rage higher

[077-117]than at the present moment. The giants declared that every turn which

[077-118]they attempted to take in their country’s service had been thwarted

[077-119]by faction, in spite of those benign promises of assistance made

[077-120]to them only a few weeks since by their opponents; and the gods

[077-121]answered by asserting that they were driven to this opposition by

[077-122]the Bœotian fatuity of the giants. They had no doubt promised

[077-123]their aid, and were ready to give it to measures that were decently

[077-124]prudent; but not to a bill enabling government at its will to pension

[077-125]aged bishops! No; there must be some limit to their tolerance, and

[077-126]when such attempts as these were made that limit had been clearly

[077-127]passed.

[077-128]All this had taken place openly only a day or two after that casual

[077-129]whisper dropped by Tom Towers at Miss Dunstable’s party—by Tom

[077-130]Towers, that most pleasant of all pleasant fellows. And how should

[077-131]he have known it,—he who flutters from one sweetest flower of the

[077-132]garden to another,

[077-133]“Adding sugar to the pink, and honey to the rose,

[077-134]So loved for what he gives, but taking nothing as he goes”?

[077-135]But the whisper had grown into a rumour, and the rumour into a

[077-136]fact, and the political world was in a ferment. The giants, furious

[077-137]about their bishops’ pension bill, threatened the House—most

[077-138]injudiciously; and then it was beautiful to see how indignant members

[077-139]got up, glowing with honesty, and declared that it was base to

[077-140]conceive that any gentleman in that House could be actuated in his

[077-141]vote by any hopes or fears with reference to his seat. And so matters

[077-142]grew from bad to worse, and these contending parties never hit at

[077-143]each other with such envenomed wrath as they did now;—having entered

[077-144]the ring together so lately with such manifold promises of good-will,

[077-145]respect, and forbearance!

[077-146]But going from the general to the particular, we may say that nowhere

[077-147]was a deeper consternation spread than in the electoral division

[077-148]of West Barsetshire. No sooner had the tidings of the dissolution

[077-149]reached the county than it was known that the duke intended to change

[077-150]his nominee. Mr. Sowerby had now sat for the division since the

[077-151]Reform Bill! He had become one of the county institutions, and by the

[077-152]dint of custom and long establishment had been borne with and even

[077-153]liked by the county gentlemen, in spite of his well-known pecuniary

[077-154]irregularities. Now all this was to be changed. No reason had as yet

[077-155]been publicly given, but it was understood that Lord Dumbello was to

[077-156]be returned, although he did not own an acre of land in the county.

[077-157]It is true that rumour went on to say that Lord Dumbello was about

[077-158]to form close connections with Barsetshire. He was on the eve of

[077-159]marrying a young lady, from the other division indeed, and was

[077-160]now engaged, so it was said, in completing arrangements with the

[077-161]government for the purchase of that noble crown property usually

[077-162]known as the Chace of Chaldicotes. It was also stated—this

[077-163]statement, however, had hitherto been only announced in confidential

[077-164]whispers—that Chaldicotes House itself would soon become the

[077-165]residence of the marquis. The duke was claiming it as his own—would

[077-166]very shortly have completed his claims and taken possession;—and

[077-167]then, by some arrangement between them, it was to be made over to

[077-168]Lord Dumbello.

[077-169]But very contrary rumours to these got abroad also. Men said—such

[077-170]as dared to oppose the duke, and some few also who did not dare

[077-171]to oppose him when the day of battle came—that it was beyond his

[077-172]grace’s power to turn Lord Dumbello into a Barsetshire magnate. The

[077-173]crown property—such men said—was to fall into the hands of young

[077-174]Mr. Gresham, of Boxall Hill, in the other division, and that the

[077-175]terms of purchase had been already settled. And as to Mr. Sowerby’s

[077-176]property and the house of Chaldicotes—these opponents of the Omnium

[077-177]interest went on to explain—it was by no means as yet so certain

[077-178]that the duke would be able to enter it and take possession. The

[077-179]place was not to be given up to him quietly. A great fight would be

[077-180]made, and it was beginning to be believed that the enormous mortgages

[077-181]would be paid off by a lady of immense wealth. And then a dash of

[077-182]romance was not wanting to make these stories palatable. This lady of

[077-183]immense wealth had been courted by Mr. Sowerby, had acknowledged her

[077-184]love,—but had refused to marry him on account of his character. In

[077-185]testimony of her love, however, she was about to pay all his debts.
