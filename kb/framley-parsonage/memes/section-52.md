## Section 52

[052-001]“Oh! I have no doubt that they are honourable,” said Miss Dunstable.

[052-002]“He does not want to deceive me in that way, I am quite sure.”

[052-003]It was impossible to help laughing, and Mrs. Harold Smith did laugh.

[052-004]“Upon my word, you would provoke a saint,” said she.

[052-005]“I am not likely to get into any such company by the alliance that

[052-006]you are now suggesting to me. There are not many saints usually at

[052-007]Chaldicotes, I believe;—always excepting my dear bishop and his

[052-008]wife.”

[052-009]“But, my dear, what am I to say to Nathaniel?”

[052-010]“Tell him, of course, how much obliged to him I am.”

[052-011]“Do listen to me one moment. I daresay that I have done wrong to

[052-012]speak to you in such a bold, unromantic way.”

[052-013]“Not at all. The truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth.

[052-014]That’s what we agreed upon. But one’s first efforts in any line are

[052-015]always apt to be a little uncouth.”

[052-016]“I will send Nathaniel to you himself.”

[052-017]“No, do not do so. Why torment either him or me? I do like

[052-018]your brother; in a certain way I like him much. But no earthly

[052-019]consideration would induce me to marry him. Is it not so glaringly

[052-020]plain that he would marry me for my money only, that you have not

[052-021]even dared to suggest any other reason?”

[052-022]“Of course it would have been nonsense to say that he had no regard

[052-023]whatever towards your money.”

[052-024]“Of course it would—absolute nonsense. He is a poor man with a good

[052-025]position, and he wants to marry me because I have got that which

[052-026]he wants. But, my dear, I do not want that which he has got, and

[052-027]therefore the bargain would not be a fair one.”

[052-028]“But he would do his very best to make you happy.”

[052-029]“I am so much obliged to him; but you see, I am very happy as I am.

[052-030]What should I gain?”

[052-031]“A companion whom you confess that you like.”

[052-032]“Ah! but I don’t know that I should like too much even of such a

[052-033]companion as your brother. No, my dear—it won’t do. Believe me when

[052-034]I tell you, once for all, that it won’t do.”

[052-035]“Do you mean, then, Miss Dunstable, that you’ll never marry?”

[052-036]“To-morrow—if I met any one that I fancied, and he would have me.

[052-037]But I rather think that any that I may fancy won’t have me. In the

[052-038]first place, if I marry any one, the man must be quite indifferent to

[052-039]money.”

[052-040]“Then you’ll not find him in this world, my dear.”

[052-041]“Very possibly not,” said Miss Dunstable.

[052-042]All that was further said upon the subject need not be here repeated.

[052-043]Mrs. Harold Smith did not give up her cause quite at once, although

[052-044]Miss Dunstable had spoken so plainly. She tried to explain how

[052-045]eligible would be her friend’s situation as mistress of Chaldicotes,

[052-046]when Chaldicotes should owe no penny to any man: and went so far

[052-047]as to hint that the master of Chaldicotes, if relieved of his

[052-048]embarrassments and known as a rich man, might in all probability be

[052-049]found worthy of a peerage when the gods should return to Olympus. Mr.

[052-050]Harold Smith, as a cabinet minister, would, of course, do his best.

[052-051]But it was all of no use. “It’s not my destiny,” said Miss Dunstable,

[052-052]“and therefore do not press it any longer.”

[052-053]“But we shall not quarrel,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, almost tenderly.

[052-054]“Oh, no—why should we quarrel?”

[052-055]“And you won’t look glum at my brother?”

[052-056]“Why should I look glum at him? But, Mrs. Smith, I’ll do more than

[052-057]not looking glum at him. I do like you, and I do like your brother,

[052-058]and if I can in any moderate way assist him in his difficulties, let

[052-059]him tell me so.”

[052-060]Soon after this, Mrs. Harold Smith went her way. Of course, she

[052-061]declared in a very strong manner that her brother could not think of

[052-062]accepting from Miss Dunstable any such pecuniary assistance as that

[052-063]offered—and, to give her her due, such was the feeling of her mind

[052-064]at the moment; but as she went to meet her brother and gave him an

[052-065]account of this interview, it did occur to her that possibly Miss

[052-066]Dunstable might be a better creditor than the Duke of Omnium for the

[052-067]Chaldicotes property.

[052-068]CHAPTER XXV.

[052-069]NON-IMPULSIVE.

[052-070]It cannot be held as astonishing, that that last decision on the

[052-071]part of the giants in the matter of the two bishoprics should

[052-072]have disgusted Archdeacon Grantly. He was a politician, but not a

[052-073]politician as they were. As is the case with all exoteric men, his

[052-074]political eyes saw a short way only, and his political aspirations

[052-075]were as limited. When his friends came into office, that bishop bill,

[052-076]which as the original product of his enemies had been regarded by

[052-077]him as being so pernicious—for was it not about to be made law in

[052-078]order that other Proudies and such like might be hoisted up into

[052-079]high places and large incomes, to the terrible detriment of the

[052-080]Church?—that bishop bill, I say, in the hands of his friends, had

[052-081]appeared to him to be a means of almost national salvation. And then,

[052-082]how great had been the good fortune of the giants in this matter! Had

[052-083]they been the originators of such a measure they would not have had

[052-084]a chance of success; but now—now that the two bishops were falling

[052-085]into their mouths out of the weak hands of the gods, was not their

[052-086]success ensured? So Dr. Grantly had girded up his loins and marched

[052-087]up to the fight, almost regretting that the triumph would be so easy.

[052-088]The subsequent failure was very trying to his temper as a party man.

[052-089]It always strikes me that the supporters of the Titans are in this

[052-090]respect much to be pitied. The giants themselves, those who are

[052-091]actually handling Pelion and breaking their shins over the lower

[052-092]rocks of Ossa, are always advancing in some sort towards the councils

[052-093]of Olympus. Their highest policy is to snatch some ray from heaven.

[052-094]Why else put Pelion on Ossa, unless it be that a furtive hand, making

[052-095]its way through Jove’s windows, may pluck forth a thunderbolt or two,

[052-096]or some article less destructive, but of manufacture equally divine?

[052-097]And in this consists the wisdom of the higher giants—that, in spite

[052-098]of their mundane antecedents, theories, and predilections, they can

[052-099]see that articles of divine manufacture are necessary. But then

[052-100]they never carry their supporters with them. Their whole army is an

[052-101]army of martyrs. “For twenty years I have stuck to them, and see

[052-102]how they have treated me!” Is not that always the plaint of an old

[052-103]giant-slave? “I have been true to my party all my life, and where am

[052-104]I now?” he says. Where, indeed, my friend? Looking about you, you

[052-105]begin to learn that you cannot describe your whereabouts. I do not

[052-106]marvel at that. No one finds himself planted at last in so terribly

[052-107]foul a morass, as he would fain stand still for ever on dry ground.

[052-108]Dr. Grantly was disgusted; and although he was himself too true and

[052-109]thorough in all his feelings, to be able to say aloud that any Giant

[052-110]was wrong, still he had a sad feeling within his heart that the world

[052-111]was sinking from under him. He was still sufficiently exoteric to

[052-112]think that a good stand-up fight in a good cause was a good thing.

[052-113]No doubt he did wish to be Bishop of Westminster, and was anxious to

[052-114]compass that preferment by any means that might appear to him to be

[052-115]fair. And why not? But this was not the end of his aspirations. He

[052-116]wished that the giants might prevail in everything, in bishoprics as

[052-117]in all other matters; and he could not understand that they should

[052-118]give way on the very first appearance of a skirmish. In his open talk

[052-119]he was loud against many a god; but in his heart of hearts he was

[052-120]bitter enough against both Porphyrion and Orion.

[052-121]“My dear doctor, it would not do;—not in this session; it would not

[052-122]indeed.” So had spoken to him a half-fledged but especially esoteric

[052-123]young monster-cub at the Treasury, who considered himself as up to

[052-124]all the dodges of his party, and regarded the army of martyrs who

[052-125]supported it as a rather heavy, but very useful collection of fogeys.

[052-126]Dr. Grantly had not cared to discuss the matter with the half-fledged

[052-127]monster-cub. The best licked of all the monsters, the Giant most like

[052-128]a god of them all, had said a word or two to him; and he also had

[052-129]said a word or two to that Giant. Porphyrion had told him that the

[052-130]bishop bill would not do; and he, in return, speaking with warm face,

[052-131]and blood in his cheeks, had told Porphyrion that he saw no reason

[052-132]why the bill should not do. The courteous Giant had smiled as he

[052-133]shook his ponderous head, and then the archdeacon had left him,

[052-134]unconsciously shaking some dust from his shoes, as he paced the

[052-135]passages of the Treasury chambers for the last time. As he walked

[052-136]back to his lodgings in Mount Street, many thoughts, not altogether

[052-137]bad in their nature, passed through his mind. Why should he trouble

[052-138]himself about a bishopric? Was he not well as he was, in his rectory

[052-139]down at Plumstead? Might it not be ill for him at his age to

[052-140]transplant himself into new soil, to engage in new duties, and live

[052-141]among new people? Was he not useful at Barchester, and respected

[052-142]also; and might it not be possible, that up there at Westminster, he

[052-143]might be regarded merely as a tool with which other men could work?

[052-144]He had not quite liked the tone of that specially esoteric young

[052-145]monster-cub, who had clearly regarded him as a distinguished fogey

[052-146]from the army of martyrs. He would take his wife back to Barsetshire,

[052-147]and there live contented with the good things which Providence had

[052-148]given him.

[052-149]Those high political grapes had become sour, my sneering friends will

[052-150]say. Well? Is it not a good thing that grapes should become sour

[052-151]which hang out of reach? Is he not wise who can regard all grapes as

[052-152]sour which are manifestly too high for his hand? Those grapes of the

[052-153]Treasury bench, for which gods and giants fight, suffering so much

[052-154]when they are forced to abstain from eating, and so much more when

[052-155]they do eat,—those grapes are very sour to me. I am sure that they

[052-156]are indigestible, and that those who eat them undergo all the ills

[052-157]which the Revalenta Arabica is prepared to cure. And so it was now

[052-158]with the archdeacon. He thought of the strain which would have been

[052-159]put on his conscience had he come up there to sit in London as Bishop

[052-160]of Westminster; and in this frame of mind he walked home to his wife.

[052-161]During the first few moments of his interview with her all his

[052-162]regrets had come back upon him. Indeed, it would have hardly suited

[052-163]for him then to have preached this new doctrine of rural contentment.

[052-164]The wife of his bosom, whom he so fully trusted—had so fully

[052-165]loved—wished for grapes that hung high upon the wall, and he knew

[052-166]that it was past his power to teach her at the moment to drop her

[052-167]ambition. Any teaching that he might effect in that way, must come

[052-168]by degrees. But before many minutes were over he had told her of

[052-169]her fate and of his own decision. “So we had better go back to

[052-170]Plumstead,” he said; and she had not dissented.

[052-171]“I am sorry for poor Griselda’s sake,” Mrs. Grantly had remarked

[052-172]later in the evening, when they were again together.
