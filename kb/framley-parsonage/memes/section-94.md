## Section 94

[094-001]“Your fault, Ludovic?”

[094-002]“Yes, mother. It was I who introduced him to Mr. Sowerby; and, to

[094-003]tell the truth, I do not think he would ever have been intimate

[094-004]with Sowerby if I had not given him some sort of a commission with

[094-005]reference to money matters then pending between Mr. Sowerby and me.

[094-006]They are all over now,—thanks to you, indeed.”

[094-007]“Mr. Robarts’ character as a clergyman should have kept him from such

[094-008]troubles, if no other feeling did so.”

[094-009]“At any rate, mother, oblige me by letting it pass by.”

[094-010]“Oh, I shall say nothing to him.”

[094-011]“You had better say something to her, or otherwise it will be

[094-012]strange; and even to him I would say a word or two,—a word in

[094-013]kindness, as you so well know how. It will be easier to him in that

[094-014]way, than if you were to be altogether silent.”

[094-015]No further conversation took place between them at the time, but

[094-016]later in the evening she brushed her hand across her son’s forehead,

[094-017]sweeping the long silken hairs into their place, as she was wont to

[094-018]do when moved by any special feeling of love. “Ludovic,” she said,

[094-019]“no one, I think, has so good a heart as you. I will do exactly as

[094-020]you would have me about this affair of Mr. Robarts and the money.”

[094-021]And then there was nothing more said about it.

[094-022]CHAPTER XLV.

[094-023]PALACE BLESSINGS.

[094-024]And now, at this period, terrible rumours found their way into

[094-025]Barchester, and flew about the cathedral towers and round the

[094-026]cathedral door; ay, and into the canons’ houses and the humbler

[094-027]sitting-rooms of the vicars choral. Whether they made their way from

[094-028]thence up to the bishop’s palace, or whether they descended from

[094-029]the palace to the close, I will not pretend to say. But they were

[094-030]shocking, unnatural, and no doubt grievous to all those excellent

[094-031]ecclesiastical hearts which cluster so thickly in those quarters.

[094-032]The first of these had reference to the new prebendary, and to the

[094-033]disgrace which he had brought on the chapter; a disgrace, as some of

[094-034]them boasted, which Barchester had never known before. This, however,

[094-035]like most other boasts, was hardly true; for within but a very few

[094-036]years there had been an execution in the house of a late prebendary,

[094-037]old Dr. Stanhope; and on that occasion the doctor himself had been

[094-038]forced to fly away to Italy, starting in the night, lest he also

[094-039]should fall into the hands of the Philistines, as well as his chairs

[094-040]and tables.

[094-041]“It is a scandalous shame,” said Mrs. Proudie, speaking not of the

[094-042]old doctor, but of the new offender; “a scandalous shame: and it

[094-043]would only serve him right if the gown were stripped from his back.”

[094-044]“I suppose his living will be sequestrated,” said a young minor canon

[094-045]who attended much to the ecclesiastical injunctions of the lady of

[094-046]the diocese, and was deservedly held in high favour. If Framley were

[094-047]sequestrated, why should not he, as well as another, undertake the

[094-048]duty—with such stipend as the bishop might award?

[094-049]“I am told that he is over head and ears in debt,” said the future

[094-050]Mrs. Tickler, “and chiefly for horses which he has bought and not

[094-051]paid for.”

[094-052]“I see him riding very splendid animals when he comes over for the

[094-053]cathedral duties,” said the minor canon.

[094-054]“The sheriff’s officers are in the house at present, I am told,” said

[094-055]Mrs. Proudie.

[094-056]“And is not he in jail?” said Mrs. Tickler.

[094-057]“If not, he ought to be,” said Mrs. Tickler’s mother.

[094-058]“And no doubt soon will be,” said the minor canon; “for I hear that

[094-059]he is linked up with a most discreditable gang of persons.”

[094-060]This was what was said in the palace on that heading; and though,

[094-061]no doubt, more spirit and poetry was displayed there than in the

[094-062]houses of the less gifted clergy, this shows the manner in which the

[094-063]misfortune of Mr. Robarts was generally discussed. Nor, indeed, had

[094-064]he deserved any better treatment at their hands. But his name did

[094-065]not run the gauntlet for the usual nine days; nor, indeed, did his

[094-066]fame endure at its height for more than two. This sudden fall was

[094-067]occasioned by other tidings of a still more distressing nature; by

[094-068]a rumour which so affected Mrs. Proudie that it caused, as she said,

[094-069]her blood to creep. And she was very careful that the blood of others

[094-070]should creep also, if the blood of others was equally sensitive. It

[094-071]was said that Lord Dumbello had jilted Miss Grantly.

[094-072]From what adverse spot in the world these cruel tidings fell upon

[094-073]Barchester I have never been able to discover. We know how quickly

[094-074]rumour flies, making herself common through all the cities. That

[094-075]Mrs. Proudie should have known more of the facts connected with the

[094-076]Hartletop family than any one else in Barchester was not surprising,

[094-077]seeing that she was so much more conversant with the great world in

[094-078]which such people lived. She knew, and was therefore correct enough

[094-079]in declaring, that Lord Dumbello had already jilted one other young

[094-080]lady—the Lady Julia Mac Mull, to whom he had been engaged three

[094-081]seasons back, and that therefore his character in such matters was

[094-082]not to be trusted. That Lady Julia had been a terrible flirt and

[094-083]greatly given to waltzing with a certain German count, with whom

[094-084]she had since gone off—that, I suppose, Mrs. Proudie did not know,

[094-085]much as she was conversant with the great world,—seeing that she

[094-086]said nothing about it to any of her ecclesiastical listeners on the

[094-087]present occasion.

[094-088]“It will be a terrible warning, Mrs. Quiverful, to us all; a most

[094-089]useful warning to us—not to trust to the things of this world.

[094-090]I fear they made no inquiry about this young nobleman before they

[094-091]agreed that his name should be linked with that of their daughter.”

[094-092]This she said to the wife of the present warden of Hiram’s Hospital,

[094-093]a lady who had received favours from her, and was therefore bound to

[094-094]listen attentively to her voice.

[094-095]“But I hope it may not be true,” said Mrs. Quiverful, who, in spite

[094-096]of the allegiance due by her to Mrs. Proudie, had reasons of her own

[094-097]for wishing well to the Grantly family.

[094-098]“I hope so, indeed,” said Mrs. Proudie, with a slight tinge of anger

[094-099]in her voice; “but I fear that there is no doubt. And I must confess

[094-100]that it is no more than we had a right to expect. I hope that it may

[094-101]be taken by all of us as a lesson, and an ensample, and a teaching

[094-102]of the Lord’s mercy. And I wish you would request your husband—from

[094-103]me, Mrs. Quiverful—to dwell on this subject in morning and evening

[094-104]lecture at the hospital on Sabbath next, showing how false is the

[094-105]trust which we put in the good things of this world;” which behest,

[094-106]to a certain extent, Mr. Quiverful did obey, feeling that a quiet

[094-107]life in Barchester was of great value to him; but he did not go so

[094-108]far as to caution his hearers, who consisted of the aged bedesmen of

[094-109]the hospital, against matrimonial projects of an ambitious nature.

[094-110]In this case, as in all others of the kind, the report was known to

[094-111]all the chapter before it had been heard by the archdeacon or his

[094-112]wife. The dean heard it, and disregarded it; as did also the dean’s

[094-113]wife—at first; and those who generally sided with the Grantlys in

[094-114]the diocesan battles pooh-poohed the tidings, saying to each other

[094-115]that both the archdeacon and Mrs. Grantly were very well able to take

[094-116]care of their own affairs. But dripping water hollows a stone; and at

[094-117]last it was admitted on all sides that there was ground for fear,—on

[094-118]all sides, except at Plumstead.

[094-119]“I am sure there is nothing in it; I really am sure of it,” said Mrs.

[094-120]Arabin, whispering to her sister; “but after turning it over in my

[094-121]mind, I thought it right to tell you. And yet I don’t know now but I

[094-122]am wrong.”

[094-123]“Quite right, dearest Eleanor,” said Mrs. Grantly. “And I am much

[094-124]obliged to you. But we understand it, you know. It comes, of course,

[094-125]like all other Christian blessings, from the palace.” And then there

[094-126]was nothing more said about it between Mrs. Grantly and her sister.

[094-127]But on the following morning there arrived a letter by post,

[094-128]addressed to Mrs. Grantly, bearing the postmark of Littlebath. The

[094-129]letter ran:—

[094-130]MADAM,

[094-131]It is known to the writer that Lord Dumbello has arranged

[094-132]with certain friends how he may escape from his present

[094-133]engagement. I think, therefore, that it is my duty as a

[094-134]Christian to warn you of this.

[094-135]Yours truly,

[094-136]A WELLWISHER.

[094-137]Now it had happened that the embryo Mrs. Tickler’s most intimate

[094-138]bosom friend and confidante was known at Plumstead to live at

[094-139]Littlebath, and it had also happened—most unfortunately—that

[094-140]the embryo Mrs. Tickler, in the warmth of her neighbourly regard,

[094-141]had written a friendly line to her friend Griselda Grantly,

[094-142]congratulating her with all female sincerity on her splendid nuptials

[094-143]with the Lord Dumbello.

[094-144]“It is not her natural hand,” said Mrs. Grantly, talking the matter

[094-145]over with her husband, “but you may be sure it has come from her. It

[094-146]is a part of the new Christianity which we learn day by day from the

[094-147]palace teaching.”

[094-148]But these things had some effect on the archdeacon’s mind. He had

[094-149]learned lately the story of Lady Julia Mac Mull, and was not sure

[094-150]that his son-in-law—as ought to be about to be—had been entirely

[094-151]blameless in that matter. And then in these days Lord Dumbello made

[094-152]no great sign. Immediately on Griselda’s return to Plumstead he had

[094-153]sent her a magnificent present of emeralds, which, however, had come

[094-154]to her direct from the jewellers, and might have been—and probably

[094-155]was—ordered by his man of business. Since that he had neither come,

[094-156]nor sent, nor written. Griselda did not seem to be in any way annoyed

[094-157]by this absence of the usual sign of love, and went on steadily

[094-158]with her great duties. “Nothing,” as she told her mother, “had been

[094-159]said about writing, and, therefore, she did not expect it.” But the

[094-160]archdeacon was not quite at his ease. “Keep Dumbello up to his P’s

[094-161]and Q’s, you know,” a friend of his had whispered to him at his

[094-162]club. By heavens, yes. The archdeacon was not a man to bear with

[094-163]indifference a wrong in such a quarter. In spite of his clerical

[094-164]profession, few men were more inclined to fight against personal

[094-165]wrongs—and few men more able.

[094-166]“Can there be anything wrong, I wonder?” said he to his wife. “Is

[094-167]it worth while that I should go up to London?” But Mrs. Grantly

[094-168]attributed it all to the palace doctrine. What could be more natural,

[094-169]looking at all the circumstances of the Tickler engagement? She

[094-170]therefore gave her voice against any steps being taken by the

[094-171]archdeacon.

[094-172]A day or two after that Mrs. Proudie met Mrs. Arabin in the close

[094-173]and condoled with her openly on the termination of the marriage

[094-174]treaty;—quite openly, for Mrs. Tickler—as she was to be—was with

[094-175]her mother, and Mrs. Arabin was accompanied by her sister-in-law,

[094-176]Mary Bold.

[094-177]“It must be very grievous to Mrs. Grantly, very grievous indeed,”

[094-178]said Mrs. Proudie, “and I sincerely feel for her. But, Mrs. Arabin,

[094-179]all these lessons are sent to us for our eternal welfare.”

[094-180]“Of course,” said Mrs. Arabin. “But as to this special lesson, I am

[094-181]inclined to doubt that it—”

[094-182]“Ah-h! I fear it is too true. I fear there is no room for doubt. Of

[094-183]course you are aware that Lord Dumbello is off for the Continent.”

[094-184]Mrs. Arabin was not aware of it, and she was obliged to admit as

[094-185]much.
