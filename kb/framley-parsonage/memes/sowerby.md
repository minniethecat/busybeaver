# Mr. Sowerby

Mr. Nathaniel Sowerby of [[chaldicotes|Chaldicotes]] is the novel's most compelling antagonist — a charming, politically connected, and financially unscrupulous Whig MP whose schemes entangle [[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]] in a web of debt from which he struggles to escape. Sowerby is both a villain and, in Trollope's characteristic fashion, a figure who elicits a measure of sympathy: a man undone by the very system of political influence and landed expectation that he manipulates so deftly.

## Role in the Novel

Sowerby is the catalyst of the novel's financial plot. Through his friendship with Mark Robarts — cultivated at the [[chaldicotes|Chaldicotes]] gatherings and in London — he persuades the young vicar to guarantee bills of exchange, incurring debts that Mark has no means to repay (006-131) (006-133). This transaction sets in motion the central crisis of the novel: Mark's fall from financial innocence and the threat of ruin that hangs over Framley Parsonage.

Beyond his direct entanglement of Mark, Sowerby functions as a representative of a wider social pathology: the system by which political influence was bought and sold through personal relationships, and by which landed estates — like Chaldicotes itself — were consumed by the debts incurred to sustain a parliamentary career. Sowerby's own estate is threatened with extinction, as "Chaldicotes was to vanish from the earth's surface" under the pressure of his debts (004-016).

## Character Traits

- **Charm**: Sowerby's most dangerous quality is his personal magnetism. He moves easily through both London political circles and Barsetshire society, and his friendship is sought by men far above his moral station — including [[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]] (005-072).
- **Financial recklessness**: Sowerby "was known to be a very poor man, with a very large estate," having "wasted, men said, much on electioneering" (003-124). His debts are legendary, and his schemes for avoiding them grow ever more desperate.
- **Political ambition**: As a Whig MP seated "mainly by the interest of that great Whig autocrat the Duke of Omnium," Sowerby embodies the unreformed political system of the 1850s, in which parliamentary seats were effectively owned by great magnates (003-106) (003-108).
- **Moral blindness**: Sowerby does not see himself as a villain. He regards his financial operations as the normal business of a gentleman in politics, and his use of Mark Robarts's signature as a routine accommodation between friends. This blindness makes him more dangerous than a conscious schemer would be.

## Relationships

- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: Sowerby's principal victim in the novel. He cultivates Mark's friendship, draws him into the Chaldicotes circle, and persuades him to guarantee bills that Mark cannot honour. The relationship is predatory, but Sowerby would not describe it as such (006-131) (006-133).
- **[[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]]**: A frequent companion in London, whose youth and impressionability make him a natural target for Sowerby's influence. Lady Lufton regards this association with deep suspicion, fearing that her son may be drawn into Sowerby's "baneful" orbit (005-072) (003-122).
- **[[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]**: Sowerby's natural enemy. Lady Lufton sees him as "an impersonation of Lucifer upon earth" and fears his influence over both her son and her vicar. Her judgment of his character proves entirely correct (003-108).
- **Harold Smith**: Sowerby's brother-in-law and political ally, who "in early life married a sister of Mr. Sowerby." Smith is another member of the Chaldicotes set whose political ambitions are served by Sowerby's connections (003-169).
- **The Duke of Omnium**: Sowerby's political patron, the great Whig magnate whose interest secures Sowerby's parliamentary seat. The Duke's shadowy power — exercised through men like Sowerby — represents everything Lady Lufton most fears about the modern political world.

## Key Scenes

- **Introduction at Chaldicotes (Chapter 3)**: Trollope's first extended description of Sowerby establishes him as a figure of ambiguous menace — Whig, unmarried, fifty years old, "a very poor man" with a large estate, and the intimate of the dangerously powerful Duke of Omnium (003-092) (003-106) (003-124).
- **The financial entanglement (Chapter 6)**: Sowerby persuades Mark to involve himself in "pecuniary transactions" — the moment that sets the novel's financial plot in motion. The transaction is presented as a favour between friends, but its consequences will shadow the rest of the narrative (006-131) (006-133).
- **The Chaldicotes gathering (Chapter 5)**: The social world of Chaldicotes is displayed in full, with Sowerby at its centre, entertaining Lord Lufton and other guests in a style that his debts cannot sustain. The scene captures the precarious glamour of his position (005-072).
- **Miss Dunstable's interest (Chapter 11)**: Sowerby speaks "constantly" of Miss Dunstable, and she seems to take an amused interest in him — a relationship that reveals Sowerby's persistent if increasingly desperate search for a financial solution through marriage (011-180).

## Critical Commentary

Sowerby occupies an important place in Trollope's gallery of charming rogues — figures like Burgo Fitzgerald in _Can You Forgive Her?_ or Sir Felix Carbury in _The Way We Live Now_ — whose personal magnetism is inseparable from their moral weakness. What distinguishes Sowerby from more villainous versions of the type is Trollope's evident sympathy for the predicament of a man whose social position demands an expenditure he cannot sustain. The system of parliamentary patronage that Sowerby serves is, in Trollope's analysis, as culpable as the man himself: Sowerby is not a cause but a symptom, the product of an unreformed political economy that rewarded connection over competence and consumption over thrift (003-124) (004-016).

## Social & Historical Context

Sowerby is a study in the political economy of mid-Victorian England. As a member of Parliament for a seat controlled by the Duke of Omnium, he belongs to a system of patronage that was already under attack from reformers. His financial predicament — a large estate consumed by electioneering expenses and the costs of maintaining a political lifestyle — was not unusual among the lesser gentry who sought parliamentary careers. Trollope, who stood for Parliament himself (unsuccessfully) in 1868, knew this world intimately, and his portrait of Sowerby is as much social critique as character study. The "Chaldicotes set" represents a social type that Trollope regarded with fascinated disapproval: men who lived beyond their means on the credit of their connections, perpetually one step ahead of ruin (003-124) (004-016).
