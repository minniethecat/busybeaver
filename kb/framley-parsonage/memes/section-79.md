## Section 79

[079-001]It was dark night when he returned to the house, and as he did so he

[079-002]resolved that he would quit the place altogether, and give up the

[079-003]battle as lost. The duke should take it and do as he pleased with

[079-004]it; and as for the seat in Parliament, Lord Dumbello, or any other

[079-005]equally gifted young patrician, might hold it for him. He would

[079-006]vanish from the scene and betake himself to some land from whence he

[079-007]would be neither heard nor seen, and there—starve. Such were now his

[079-008]future outlooks into the world; and yet, as regards health and all

[079-009]physical capacities, he knew that he was still in the prime of his

[079-010]life. Yes; in the prime of his life! But what could he do with what

[079-011]remained to him of such prime? How could he turn either his mind or

[079-012]his strength to such account as might now be serviceable? How could

[079-013]he, in his sore need, earn for himself even the barest bread? Would

[079-014]it not be better for him that he should die? Let not any one covet

[079-015]the lot of a spendthrift, even though the days of his early pease and

[079-016]champagne seem to be unnumbered; for that lame Nemesis will surely be

[079-017]up before the game has been all played out.

[079-018]When Mr. Sowerby reached his house he found that a message by

[079-019]telegraph had arrived for him in his absence. It was from his sister,

[079-020]and it informed him that she would be with him that night. She was

[079-021]coming down by the mail train, had telegraphed to Barchester for

[079-022]post-horses, and would be at Chaldicotes about two hours after

[079-023]midnight. It was therefore manifest enough that her business was of

[079-024]importance.

[079-025]Exactly at two the Barchester post-chaise did arrive, and Mrs. Harold

[079-026]Smith, before she retired to her bed, was closeted for about an hour

[079-027]with her brother.

[079-028]“Well,” she said, the following morning, as they sat together at the

[079-029]breakfast-table, “what do you say to it now? If you accept her offer

[079-030]you should be with her lawyer this afternoon.”

[079-031]“I suppose I must accept it,” said he.

[079-032]“Certainly, I think so. No doubt it will take the property out of

[079-033]your own hands as completely as though the duke had it, but it will

[079-034]leave you the house, at any rate, for your life.”

[079-035]“What good will the house be, when I can’t keep it up?”

[079-036]“But I am not so sure of that. She will not want more than her fair

[079-037]interest; and as it will be thoroughly well managed, I should think

[079-038]that there would be something over—something enough to keep up the

[079-039]house. And then, you know, we must have some place in the country.”

[079-040]“I tell you fairly, Harriet, that I will have nothing further to do

[079-041]with Harold in the way of money.”

[079-042]“Ah! that was because you would go to him. Why did you not come to

[079-043]me? And then, Nathaniel, it is the only way in which you can have a

[079-044]chance of keeping the seat. She is the queerest woman I ever met, but

[079-045]she seems resolved on beating the duke.”

[079-046]“I do not quite understand it, but I have not the slightest

[079-047]objection.”

[079-048]“She thinks that he is interfering with young Gresham about the crown

[079-049]property. I had no idea that she had so much business at her fingers’

[079-050]ends. When I first proposed the matter she took it up quite as a

[079-051]lawyer might, and seemed to have forgotten altogether what occurred

[079-052]about that other matter.”

[079-053]“I wish I could forget it also,” said Mr. Sowerby.

[079-054]“I really think that she does. When I was obliged to make some

[079-055]allusion to it—at least I felt myself obliged, and was sorry

[079-056]afterwards that I did—she merely laughed—a great loud laugh as she

[079-057]always does, and then went on about the business. However, she was

[079-058]clear about this, that all the expenses of the election should be

[079-059]added to the sum to be advanced by her, and that the house should be

[079-060]left to you without any rent. If you choose to take the land round

[079-061]the house you must pay for it, by the acre, as the tenants do. She

[079-062]was as clear about it all as though she had passed her life in a

[079-063]lawyer’s office.”

[079-064]My readers will now pretty well understand what last step that

[079-065]excellent sister, Mrs. Harold Smith, had taken on her brother’s

[079-066]behalf, nor will they be surprised to learn that in the course of

[079-067]the day Mr. Sowerby hurried back to town and put himself into

[079-068]communication with Miss Dunstable’s lawyer.

[079-069]CHAPTER XXXVIII.

[079-070]IS THERE CAUSE OR JUST IMPEDIMENT?

[079-071]I now purpose to visit another country house in Barsetshire, but

[079-072]on this occasion our sojourn shall be in the eastern division, in

[079-073]which, as in every other county in England, electioneering matters

[079-074]are paramount at the present moment. It has been mentioned that Mr.

[079-075]Gresham, junior, young Frank Gresham as he was always called, lived

[079-076]at a place called Boxall Hill. This property had come to his wife by

[079-077]will, and he was now settled there,—seeing that his father still

[079-078]held the family seat of the Greshams at Greshamsbury.

[079-079]At the present moment Miss Dunstable was staying at Boxall Hill

[079-080]with Mrs. Frank Gresham. They had left London,—as, indeed, all the

[079-081]world had done, to the terrible dismay of the London tradesmen. This

[079-082]dissolution of Parliament was ruining everybody except the country

[079-083]publicans, and had of course destroyed the London season among other

[079-084]things.

[079-085]Mrs. Harold Smith had only just managed to catch Miss Dunstable

[079-086]before she left London; but she did do so, and the great heiress

[079-087]had at once seen her lawyers, and instructed them how to act with

[079-088]reference to the mortgages on the Chaldicotes property. Miss

[079-089]Dunstable was in the habit of speaking of herself and her own

[079-090]pecuniary concerns as though she herself were rarely allowed to

[079-091]meddle in their management; but this was one of those small jokes

[079-092]which she ordinarily perpetrated; for in truth few ladies, and

[079-093]perhaps not many gentlemen, have a more thorough knowledge of their

[079-094]own concerns or a more potent voice in their own affairs, than was

[079-095]possessed by Miss Dunstable. Circumstances had lately brought her

[079-096]much into Barsetshire and she had there contracted very intimate

[079-097]friendships. She was now disposed to become, if possible, a

[079-098]Barsetshire proprietor, and with this view had lately agreed with

[079-099]young Mr. Gresham that she would become the purchaser of the Crown

[079-100]property. As, however, the purchase had been commenced in his name,

[079-101]it was so to be continued; but now, as we are aware, it was rumoured

[079-102]that, after all, the duke, or, if not the duke, then the Marquis of

[079-103]Dumbello, was to be the future owner of the Chace. Miss Dunstable,

[079-104]however, was not a person to give up her object if she could attain

[079-105]it, nor, under the circumstances, was she at all displeased at

[079-106]finding herself endowed with the power of rescuing the Sowerby

[079-107]portion of the Chaldicotes property from the duke’s clutches. Why

[079-108]had the duke meddled with her, or with her friend, as to the other

[079-109]property? Therefore it was arranged that the full amount due to the

[079-110]duke on mortgage should be ready for immediate payment; but it was

[079-111]arranged also that the security as held by Miss Dunstable should be

[079-112]very valid.

[079-113]Miss Dunstable, at Boxall Hill or at Greshamsbury, was a very

[079-114]different person from Miss Dunstable in London; and it was this

[079-115]difference which so much vexed Mrs. Gresham; not that her friend

[079-116]omitted to bring with her into the country her London wit and

[079-117]aptitude for fun, but that she did not take with her up to town the

[079-118]genuine goodness and love of honesty which made her loveable in the

[079-119]country. She was as it were two persons, and Mrs. Gresham could not

[079-120]understand that any lady should permit herself to be more worldly at

[079-121]one time of the year than at another—or in one place than in any

[079-122]other.

[079-123]“Well, my dear, I am heartily glad we’ve done with that,” Miss

[079-124]Dunstable said to her, as she sat herself down to her desk in the

[079-125]drawing-room on the first morning after her arrival at Boxall Hill.

[079-126][Illustration: Mrs. Gresham and Miss Dunstable.]

[079-127]“What does ‘that’ mean?” said Mrs. Gresham.

[079-128]“Why, London and smoke and late hours, and standing on one’s legs

[079-129]for four hours at a stretch on the top of one’s own staircase, to be

[079-130]bowed at by any one who chooses to come. That’s all done—for one

[079-131]year, at any rate.”

[079-132]“You know you like it.”

[079-133]“No, Mary; that’s just what I don’t know. I don’t know whether I like

[079-134]it or not. Sometimes, when the spirit of that dearest of all women,

[079-135]Mrs. Harold Smith, is upon me, I think that I do like it; but then

[079-136]again, when other spirits are on me, I think that I don’t.”

[079-137]“And who are the owners of the other spirits?”

[079-138]“Oh! you are one, of course. But you are a weak little thing, by no

[079-139]means able to contend with such a Samson as Mrs. Harold. And then you

[079-140]are a little given to wickedness yourself, you know. You’ve learned

[079-141]to like London well enough since you sat down to the table of Dives.

[079-142]Your uncle,—he’s the real impracticable, unapproachable Lazarus who

[079-143]declares that he can’t come down because of the big gulf. I wonder

[079-144]how he’d behave, if somebody left him ten thousand a year?”

[079-145]“Uncommonly well, I am sure.”

[079-146]“Oh, yes; he is a Lazarus now, so of course we are bound to speak

[079-147]well of him; but I should like to see him tried. I don’t doubt but

[079-148]what he’d have a house in Belgrave Square, and become noted for his

[079-149]little dinners before the first year of his trial was over.”

[079-150]“Well, and why not? You would not wish him to be an anchorite?”

[079-151]“I am told that he is going to try his luck,—not with ten thousand a

[079-152]year, but with one or two.”

[079-153]“What do you mean?”

[079-154]“Jane tells me that they all say at Greshamsbury that he is going to

[079-155]marry Lady Scatcherd.” Now Lady Scatcherd was a widow living in those

[079-156]parts; an excellent woman, but one not formed by nature to grace

[079-157]society of the highest order.

[079-158]“What!” exclaimed Mrs. Gresham, rising up from her chair while her

[079-159]eyes flashed with anger at such a rumour.

[079-160]“Well, my dear, don’t eat me. I don’t say it is so; I only say that

[079-161]Jane said so.”

[079-162]“Then you ought to send Jane out of the house.”

[079-163]“You may be sure of this, my dear: Jane would not have told me if

[079-164]somebody had not told her.”

[079-165]“And you believed it?”

[079-166]“I have said nothing about that.”

[079-167]“But you look as if you had believed it.”

[079-168]“Do I? Let us see what sort of a look it is, this look of faith.”

[079-169]And Miss Dunstable got up and went to the glass over the fire-place.

[079-170]“But, Mary, my dear, ain’t you old enough to know that you should not

[079-171]credit people’s looks? You should believe nothing now-a-days; and

[079-172]I did not believe the story about poor Lady Scatcherd. I know the

[079-173]doctor well enough to be sure that he is not a marrying man.”

[079-174]“What a nasty, hackneyed, false phrase that is—that of a marrying

[079-175]man! It sounds as though some men were in the habit of getting

[079-176]married three or four times a month.”

[079-177]“It means a great deal all the same. One can tell very soon whether a

[079-178]man is likely to marry or no.”

[079-179]“And can one tell the same of a woman?”

[079-180]“The thing is so different. All unmarried women are necessarily in

[079-181]the market; but if they behave themselves properly they make no

[079-182]signs. Now there was Griselda Grantly; of course she intended to get

[079-183]herself a husband, and a very grand one she has got; but she always

[079-184]looked as though butter would not melt in her mouth. It would have

[079-185]been very wrong to call her a marrying girl.”
