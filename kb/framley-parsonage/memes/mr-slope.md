# Mr. Slope

Mr. Slope, the former chaplain to the Bishop of Barchester, is one of Trollope's most memorable creations — the evangelical clergyman whose ambition, audacity, and romantic presumption made him the antagonist of *Barchester Towers*. Though he does not appear directly in *Framley Parsonage*, his name reverberates through its drawing-rooms with a power that testifies to the scandal he once provoked. To mention him in Mrs. Proudie's presence is to deploy a weapon of formidable destructive capacity.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Slope exists in *Framley Parsonage* as an absence that is more potent than many presences. He is the man who once "entertained audacious hopes — hopes not thought to be audacious by the young lady herself — with reference to Miss Olivia Proudie" (085-117). He is the spectre [[mrs-grantly|Mrs. Grantly]] invokes when she wishes to wound Mrs. Proudie, throwing "Mr. Slope in her face — there, in her own drawing-room" with devastating effect (095-038). He is the name that acts on Mrs. Proudie "as a red cloth is supposed to act on a bull" (085-113).

The Slope references in the novel operate as a kind of shared history — a reminder that the Barsetshire world existed before *Framley Parsonage* opened and will continue after it closes. The characters' reactions to his name reveal their own histories and character: Mrs. Grantly's calculated cruelty in mentioning him, Mrs. Proudie's volcanic rage at hearing him, and the narrator's knowing aside about his romantic pursuit of Olivia Proudie.

## Character Traits

- **Ambition**: Mr. Slope's defining quality in *Barchester Towers* was his determination to rise — through the bishop's favour, through marriage, through any means available. His ambition was evangelical in doctrine but deeply worldly in method.
- **Audacity**: That he "had once entertained audacious hopes" with reference to the bishop's own daughter is a measure of the man's breathtaking presumption (085-117).
- **Spectral persistence**: Though absent, he haunts the novel. "When Mr. Slope was running after your sister, Mrs. Grantly, and was encouraged by her as he was, you perhaps saw more of him than I did" — Mrs. Proudie's counter-thrust shows how the memory of Slope is a wound that has never fully healed (085-128).

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-proudie|Mrs. Proudie]]**: The bishop's wife for whom the name "Mr. Slope" is an instrument of pure provocation. Her reaction — the "black anger" that replaces "the polished smile of her company manners" — is one of the novel's most vivid emotional transformations (085-113) (085-121).
- **[[mrs-grantly|Mrs. Grantly]]**: She wields the name of Slope as a weapon against Mrs. Proudie, knowing its effect with the precision of an experienced combatant. Her mention of Slope as "a friend" of Mr. Tickler is a calculated act of domestic warfare (085-107) (085-108).
- **Olivia Proudie**: The bishop's daughter who was once the object of Slope's "audacious hopes," and whose romantic history is deployed against her mother with devastating effect (085-117).
- **[[dr-grantly|Dr. Grantly]]**: The Archdeacon who, like his wife, has reason to remember Slope as a threat to the ecclesiastical order of Barchester, and who "may have been mistaken about Mr. Slope" in his suspicions about the chaplain's intentions toward his sister-in-law (085-134).

## Key Scenes

- **Mrs. Grantly's provocation (Chapter 85)**: In a conversation with Mrs. Proudie about the Tickler match, Mrs. Grantly casually mentions that she remembers Mr. Tickler "being very favourably spoken of by Mr. Slope, who was a friend of his." The effect is immediate and explosive — Mrs. Proudie's countenance becomes "darkened with black anger" and war is declared (085-106) (085-113) (085-121).
- **The reprisal (Chapter 95)**: Mrs. Proudie gets her revenge when Mrs. Grantly's own daughter's reputation is under threat. "Mrs. Grantly had had the imprudence to throw Mr. Slope in her face — there, in her own drawing-room, and she was resolved to be revenged" (095-038) (095-039).

## Social & Historical Context

Mr. Slope belongs to the great tradition of Trollopian antagonists: the man of talent without scruple, energy without principle, who rises through institutions he means to reshape in his own image. His evangelicalism — the low-church enthusiasm that in the 1850s was challenging the comfortable high-and-dry ascendancy of men like Archdeacon Grantly — made him a figure of genuine historical significance as well as of fictional vitality. That he has departed from Barchester by the time of *Framley Parsonage* does not diminish his power; it confirms it. A man whose name can still terrify a bishop's wife years after his departure is a man who has left his mark on the world (085-113) (095-038).
