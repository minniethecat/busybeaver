## Section 68

[068-001]On the next day Lord Lufton left Framley; and started, according to

[068-002]his arrangements, for the Norway salmon fishing.

[068-003]CHAPTER XXXII.

[068-004]THE GOAT AND COMPASSES.

[068-005]Harold Smith had been made unhappy by that rumour of a dissolution;

[068-006]but the misfortune to him would be as nothing compared to the

[068-007]severity with which it would fall on Mr. Sowerby. Harold Smith might

[068-008]or might not lose his borough, but Mr. Sowerby would undoubtedly lose

[068-009]his county; and, in losing that, he would lose everything. He felt

[068-010]very certain now that the duke would not support him again, let who

[068-011]would be master of Chaldicotes; and as he reflected on these things

[068-012]he found it very hard to keep up his spirits.

[068-013]Tom Towers, it seems, had known all about it, as he always does. The

[068-014]little remark which had dropped from him at Miss Dunstable’s, made,

[068-015]no doubt, after mature deliberation, and with profound political

[068-016]motives, was the forerunner, only by twelve hours, of a very general

[068-017]report that the giants were going to the country. It was manifest

[068-018]that the giants had not a majority in Parliament, generous as had

[068-019]been the promises of support disinterestedly made to them by the

[068-020]gods. This indeed was manifest, and therefore they were going to

[068-021]the country, although they had been deliberately warned by a very

[068-022]prominent scion of Olympus that if they did do so that disinterested

[068-023]support must be withdrawn. This threat did not seem to weigh much,

[068-024]and by two o’clock on the day following Miss Dunstable’s party, the

[068-025]fiat was presumed to have gone forth. The rumour had begun with Tom

[068-026]Towers, but by that time it had reached Buggins at the Petty Bag

[068-027]Office.

[068-028]“It won’t make no difference to hus, sir; will it, Mr. Robarts?” said

[068-029]Buggins, as he leaned respectfully against the wall near the door, in

[068-030]the room of the private secretary at that establishment.

[068-031]A good deal of conversation, miscellaneous, special, and political,

[068-032]went on between young Robarts and Buggins in the course of the day;

[068-033]as was natural, seeing that they were thrown in these evil times very

[068-034]much upon each other. The Lord Petty Bag of the present ministry was

[068-035]not such a one as Harold Smith. He was a giant indifferent to his

[068-036]private notes, and careless as to the duties even of patronage; he

[068-037]rarely visited the office, and as there were no other clerks in the

[068-038]establishment—owing to a root and branch reform carried out in the

[068-039]short reign of Harold Smith—to whom could young Robarts talk, if not

[068-040]to Buggins?

[068-041]“No; I suppose not,” said Robarts, as he completed on his

[068-042]blotting-paper an elaborate picture of a Turk seated on his divan.

[068-043]“’Cause, you see, sir, we’re in the Upper ’Ouse, now;—as I always

[068-044]thinks we hought to be. I don’t think it ain’t constitutional for

[068-045]the Petty Bag to be in the Commons, Mr. Robarts. Hany ways, it never

[068-046]usen’t.”

[068-047]“They’re changing all those sort of things now-a-days, Buggins,” said

[068-048]Robarts, giving the final touch to the Turk’s smoke.

[068-049]“Well; I’ll tell you what it is, Mr. Robarts: I think I’ll go. I

[068-050]can’t stand all these changes. I’m turned of sixty now, and don’t

[068-051]want any ’stifflicates. I think I’ll take my pension and walk. The

[068-052]hoffice ain’t the same place at all since it come down among the

[068-053]Commons.” And then Buggins retired sighing, to console himself with a

[068-054]pot of porter behind a large open office ledger, set up on end on a

[068-055]small table in the little lobby outside the private secretary’s room.

[068-056]Buggins sighed again as he saw that the date made visible in the open

[068-057]book was almost as old as his own appointment; for such a book as

[068-058]this lasted long in the Petty Bag Office. A peer of high degree had

[068-059]been Lord Petty Bag in those days; one whom a messenger’s heart could

[068-060]respect with infinite veneration, as he made his unaccustomed visits

[068-061]to the office with much solemnity—perhaps four times during the

[068-062]season. The Lord Petty Bag then was highly regarded by his staff, and

[068-063]his coming among them was talked about for some hours previously and

[068-064]for some days afterwards; but Harold Smith had bustled in and out

[068-065]like the managing clerk in a Manchester house. “The service is going

[068-066]to the dogs,” said Buggins to himself, as he put down the porter pot

[068-067]and looked up over the book at a gentleman who presented himself at

[068-068]the door.

[068-069]“Mr. Robarts in his room?” said Buggins, repeating the gentleman’s

[068-070]words. “Yes, Mr. Sowerby; you’ll find him there; first door to the

[068-071]left.” And then, remembering that the visitor was a county member—a

[068-072]position which Buggins regarded as next to that of a peer—he got up,

[068-073]and, opening the private secretary’s door, ushered in the visitor.

[068-074]Young Robarts and Mr. Sowerby had, of course, become acquainted

[068-075]in the days of Harold Smith’s reign. During that short time the

[068-076]member for East Barset had on most days dropped in at the Petty Bag

[068-077]Office for a minute or two, finding out what the energetic cabinet

[068-078]minister was doing, chatting on semi-official subjects, and teaching

[068-079]the private secretary to laugh at his master. There was nothing,

[068-080]therefore, in his present visit which need appear to be singular, or

[068-081]which required any immediate special explanation. He sat himself down

[068-082]in his ordinary way, and began to speak of the subject of the day.

[068-083]“We’re all to go,” said Sowerby.

[068-084]“So I hear,” said the private secretary. “It will give me no trouble,

[068-085]for, as the respectable Buggins says, we’re in the Upper House now.”

[068-086]“What a delightful time those lucky dogs of lords do have!” said

[068-087]Sowerby. “No constituents, no turning out, no fighting, no necessity

[068-088]for political opinions,—and, as a rule, no such opinions at all!”

[068-089]“I suppose you’re tolerably safe in East Barsetshire?” said Robarts.

[068-090]“The duke has it pretty much his own way there.”

[068-091]“Yes; the duke does have it pretty much his own way. By-the-by, where

[068-092]is your brother?”

[068-093]“At home,” said Robarts; “at least I presume so.”

[068-094]“At Framley or at Barchester? I believe he was in residence at

[068-095]Barchester not long since.”

[068-096]“He’s at Framley now, I know. I got a letter only yesterday from

[068-097]his wife, with a commission. He was there, and Lord Lufton had just

[068-098]left.”

[068-099]“Yes; Lufton was down. He started for Norway this morning. I want to

[068-100]see your brother. You have not heard from him yourself, have you?”

[068-101]“No; not lately. Mark is a bad correspondent. He would not do at all

[068-102]for a private secretary.”

[068-103]“At any rate, not to Harold Smith. But you are sure I should not

[068-104]catch him at Barchester?”

[068-105]“Send down by telegraph, and he would meet you.”

[068-106]“I don’t want to do that. A telegraph message makes such a fuss in

[068-107]the country, frightening people’s wives, and setting all the horses

[068-108]about the place galloping.”

[068-109]“What is it about?”

[068-110]“Nothing of any great consequence. I didn’t know whether he might

[068-111]have told you. I’ll write down by to-night’s post, and then he can

[068-112]meet me at Barchester to-morrow. Or do you write. There’s nothing

[068-113]I hate so much as letter-writing;—just tell him that I called,

[068-114]and that I shall be much obliged if he can meet me at the Dragon of

[068-115]Wantly—say at two to-morrow. I will go down by the express.”

[068-116]Mark Robarts, in talking over this coming money trouble with Sowerby,

[068-117]had once mentioned that if it were necessary to take up the bill for

[068-118]a short time he might be able to borrow the money from his brother.

[068-119]So much of the father’s legacy still remained in the hands of the

[068-120]private secretary as would enable him to produce the amount of the

[068-121]latter bill, and there could be no doubt that he would lend it if

[068-122]asked. Mr. Sowerby’s visit to the Petty Bag Office had been caused by

[068-123]a desire to learn whether any such request had been made,—and also

[068-124]by a half-formed resolution to make the request himself if he should

[068-125]find that the clergyman had not done so. It seemed to him to be

[068-126]a pity that such a sum should be lying about, as it were, within

[068-127]reach, and that he should not stoop to put his hands upon it. Such

[068-128]abstinence would be so contrary to all the practice of his life that

[068-129]it was as difficult to him as it is for a sportsman to let pass a

[068-130]cock-pheasant. But yet something like remorse touched his heart as he

[068-131]sat there balancing himself on his chair in the private secretary’s

[068-132]room, and looking at the young man’s open face.

[068-133]“Yes; I’ll write to him,” said John Robarts; “but he hasn’t said

[068-134]anything to me about anything particular.”

[068-135]“Hasn’t he? It does not much signify. I only mentioned it because I

[068-136]thought I understood him to say that he would.” And then Mr. Sowerby

[068-137]went on swinging himself. How was it that he felt so averse to

[068-138]mention that little sum of £500 to a young man like John Robarts,

[068-139]a fellow without wife or children or calls on him of any sort, who

[068-140]would not even be injured by the loss of the money, seeing that

[068-141]he had an ample salary on which to live? He wondered at his own

[068-142]weakness. The want of the money was urgent on him in the extreme. He

[068-143]had reasons for supposing that Mark would find it very difficult to

[068-144]renew the bills, but he, Sowerby, could stop their presentation if he

[068-145]could get this money at once into his own hands.

[068-146]“Can I do anything for you?” said the innocent lamb, offering his

[068-147]throat to the butcher.

[068-148]But some unwonted feeling numbed the butcher’s fingers, and blunted

[068-149]his knife. He sat still for half a minute after the question, and

[068-150]then jumping from his seat, declined the offer. “No, no; nothing,

[068-151]thank you. Only write to Mark, and say that I shall be there

[068-152]to-morrow,” and then, taking his hat, he hurried out of the office.

[068-153]“What an ass I am,” he said to himself as he went: “as if it were of

[068-154]any use now to be particular!”

[068-155]He then got into a cab and had himself driven half way up Portman

[068-156]Street towards the New Road, and walking from thence a few hundred

[068-157]yards down a cross-street he came to a public-house. It was called

[068-158]the “Goat and Compasses,”—a very meaningless name, one would say;

[068-159]but the house boasted of being a place of public entertainment very

[068-160]long established on that site, having been a tavern out in the

[068-161]country in the days of Cromwell. At that time the pious landlord,

[068-162]putting up a pious legend for the benefit of his pious customers, had

[068-163]declared that—“God encompasseth us.” The “Goat and Compasses” in

[068-164]these days does quite as well; and, considering the present character

[068-165]of the house, was perhaps less unsuitable than the old legend.

[068-166]“Is Mr. Austen here?” asked Mr. Sowerby of the man at the bar.

[068-167]“Which on ’em? Not Mr. John; he ain’t here. Mr. Tom is in—the little

[068-168]room on the left-hand side.” The man whom Mr. Sowerby would have

[068-169]preferred to see was the elder brother, John; but as he was not to

[068-170]be found, he did go into the little room. In that room he found—Mr.

[068-171]Austen, Junior, according to one arrangement of nomenclature, and Mr.

[068-172]Tom Tozer according to another. To gentlemen of the legal profession

[068-173]he generally chose to introduce himself as belonging to the

[068-174]respectable family of the Austens; but among his intimates he had

[068-175]always been—Tozer.

[068-176]Mr. Sowerby, though he was intimate with the family, did not love

[068-177]the Tozers; but he especially hated Tom Tozer. Tom Tozer was a

[068-178]bull-necked, beetle-browed fellow, the expression of whose face was

[068-179]eloquent with acknowledged roguery. “I am a rogue,” it seemed to say.

[068-180]“I know it; all the world knows it: but you’re another. All the world

[068-181]don’t know that, but I do. Men are all rogues, pretty nigh. Some are

[068-182]soft rogues, and some are ’cute rogues. I am a ’cute one; so mind

[068-183]your eye.” It was with such words that Tom Tozer’s face spoke out;

[068-184]and though a thorough liar in his heart, he was not a liar in his

[068-185]face.
