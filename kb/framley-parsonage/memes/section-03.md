## Section 3

[003-001]Framley itself was a pleasant country place, having about it nothing

[003-002]of seignorial dignity or grandeur, but possessing everything

[003-003]necessary for the comfort of country life. The house was a low

[003-004]building of two stories, built at different periods, and devoid of

[003-005]all pretensions to any style of architecture; but the rooms, though

[003-006]not lofty, were warm and comfortable, and the gardens were trim and

[003-007]neat beyond all others in the county. Indeed, it was for its gardens

[003-008]only that Framley Court was celebrated.

[003-009]Village there was none, properly speaking. The high road went winding

[003-010]about through the Framley paddocks, shrubberies, and wood-skirted

[003-011]home fields, for a mile and a half, not two hundred yards of which

[003-012]ran in a straight line; and there was a cross-road which passed

[003-013]down through the domain, whereby there came to be a locality called

[003-014]Framley Cross. Here stood the “Lufton Arms,” and here, at Framley

[003-015]Cross, the hounds occasionally would meet; for the Framley woods were

[003-016]drawn in spite of the young lord’s truant disposition; and then, at

[003-017]the Cross also, lived the shoemaker, who kept the post-office.

[003-018]Framley church was distant from this just a quarter of a mile, and

[003-019]stood immediately opposite to the chief entrance to Framley Court. It

[003-020]was but a mean, ugly building, having been erected about a hundred

[003-021]years since, when all churches then built were made to be mean and

[003-022]ugly; nor was it large enough for the congregation, some of whom were

[003-023]thus driven to the dissenting chapels, the Sions and Ebenezers, which

[003-024]had got themselves established on each side of the parish, in putting

[003-025]down which Lady Lufton thought that her pet parson was hardly as

[003-026]energetic as he might be. It was, therefore, a matter near to Lady

[003-027]Lufton’s heart to see a new church built, and she was urgent in her

[003-028]eloquence, both with her son and with the vicar, to have this good

[003-029]work commenced.

[003-030]Beyond the church, but close to it, were the boys’ school and girls’

[003-031]school, two distinct buildings, which owed their erection to Lady

[003-032]Lufton’s energy; then came a neat little grocer’s shop, the neat

[003-033]grocer being the clerk and sexton, and the neat grocer’s wife, the

[003-034]pew-opener in the church. Podgens was their name, and they were great

[003-035]favourites with her ladyship, both having been servants up at the

[003-036]house.

[003-037]And here the road took a sudden turn to the left, turning, as it

[003-038]were, away from Framley Court; and just beyond the turn was the

[003-039]vicarage, so that there was a little garden path running from the

[003-040]back of the vicarage grounds into the churchyard, cutting the

[003-041]Podgenses off into an isolated corner of their own;—from whence, to

[003-042]tell the truth, the vicar would have been glad to banish them and

[003-043]their cabbages, could he have had the power to do so. For has not

[003-044]the small vineyard of Naboth been always an eyesore to neighbouring

[003-045]potentates?

[003-046]The potentate in this case had as little excuse as Ahab, for nothing

[003-047]in the parsonage way could be more perfect than his parsonage. It

[003-048]had all the details requisite for the house of a moderate gentleman

[003-049]with moderate means, and none of those expensive superfluities which

[003-050]immoderate gentlemen demand, or which themselves demand—immoderate

[003-051]means. And then the gardens and paddocks were exactly suited to it;

[003-052]and everything was in good order;—not exactly new, so as to be raw

[003-053]and uncovered, and redolent of workmen; but just at that era of their

[003-054]existence in which newness gives way to comfortable homeliness.

[003-055]Other village at Framley there was none. At the back of the Court, up

[003-056]one of those cross-roads, there was another small shop or two, and

[003-057]there was a very neat cottage residence, in which lived the widow of

[003-058]a former curate, another _protégé_ of Lady Lufton’s; and there was a

[003-059]big, staring brick house, in which the present curate lived; but this

[003-060]was a full mile distant from the church, and farther from Framley

[003-061]Court, standing on that cross-road which runs from Framley Cross in a

[003-062]direction away from the mansion. This gentleman, the Rev. Evan Jones,

[003-063]might, from his age, have been the vicar’s father; but he had been

[003-064]for many years curate of Framley; and though he was personally

[003-065]disliked by Lady Lufton, as being Low Church in his principles, and

[003-066]unsightly in his appearance, nevertheless, she would not urge his

[003-067]removal. He had two or three pupils in that large brick house, and

[003-068]if turned out from these and from his curacy, might find it difficult

[003-069]to establish himself elsewhere. On this account mercy was extended

[003-070]to the Rev. E. Jones, and, in spite of his red face and awkward

[003-071]big feet, he was invited to dine at Framley Court, with his plain

[003-072]daughter, once in every three months.

[003-073]Over and above these, there was hardly a house in the parish of

[003-074]Framley, outside the bounds of Framley Court, except those of farmers

[003-075]and farm labourers; and yet the parish was of large extent.

[003-076]Framley is in the eastern division of the county of Barsetshire,

[003-077]which, as all the world knows, is, politically speaking, as true

[003-078]blue a county as any in England. There have been backslidings even

[003-079]here, it is true; but then, in what county have there not been such

[003-080]backslidings? Where, in these pinchbeck days, can we hope to find

[003-081]the old agricultural virtue in all its purity? But, among those

[003-082]backsliders, I regret to say, that men now reckon Lord Lufton. Not

[003-083]that he is a violent Whig, or perhaps that he is a Whig at all.

[003-084]But he jeers and sneers at the old county doings; declares, when

[003-085]solicited on the subject, that, as far as he is concerned, Mr. Bright

[003-086]may sit for the county, if he pleases; and alleges, that being

[003-087]unfortunately a peer, he has no right even to interest himself in

[003-088]the question. All this is deeply regretted, for, in the old days,

[003-089]there was no portion of the county more decidedly true blue than that

[003-090]Framley district; and, indeed, up to the present day, the dowager is

[003-091]able to give an occasional helping hand.

[003-092]Chaldicotes is the seat of Nathaniel Sowerby, Esq., who, at the

[003-093]moment supposed to be now present, is one of the members for the

[003-094]Western Division of Barsetshire. But this Western Division can boast

[003-095]none of the fine political attributes which grace its twin brother.

[003-096]It is decidedly Whig, and is almost governed in its politics by one

[003-097]or two great Whig families.

[003-098]It has been said that Mark Robarts was about to pay a visit to

[003-099]Chaldicotes, and it has been hinted that his wife would have been as

[003-100]well pleased had this not been the case. Such was certainly the fact;

[003-101]for she, dear, prudent, excellent wife as she was, knew that Mr.

[003-102]Sowerby was not the most eligible friend in the world for a young

[003-103]clergyman, and knew, also, that there was but one other house in the

[003-104]whole county the name of which was so distasteful to Lady Lufton.

[003-105]The reasons for this were, I may say, manifold. In the first place,

[003-106]Mr. Sowerby was a Whig, and was seated in Parliament mainly by the

[003-107]interest of that great Whig autocrat the Duke of Omnium, whose

[003-108]residence was more dangerous even than that of Mr. Sowerby, and whom

[003-109]Lady Lufton regarded as an impersonation of Lucifer upon earth. Mr.

[003-110]Sowerby, too, was unmarried—as indeed, also, was Lord Lufton, much

[003-111]to his mother’s grief. Mr. Sowerby, it is true, was fifty, whereas

[003-112]the young lord was as yet only twenty-six, but, nevertheless, her

[003-113]ladyship was becoming anxious on the subject. In her mind, every

[003-114]man was bound to marry as soon as he could maintain a wife; and she

[003-115]held an idea—a quite private tenet, of which she was herself but

[003-116]imperfectly conscious—that men in general were inclined to neglect

[003-117]this duty for their own selfish gratifications, that the wicked ones

[003-118]encouraged the more innocent in this neglect, and that many would

[003-119]not marry at all, were not an unseen coercion exercised against them

[003-120]by the other sex. The Duke of Omnium was the very head of all such

[003-121]sinners, and Lady Lufton greatly feared that her son might be made

[003-122]subject to the baneful Omnium influence, by means of Mr. Sowerby and

[003-123]Chaldicotes.

[003-124]And then Mr. Sowerby was known to be a very poor man, with a very

[003-125]large estate. He had wasted, men said, much on electioneering, and

[003-126]more in gambling. A considerable portion of his property had already

[003-127]gone into the hands of the duke, who, as a rule, bought up everything

[003-128]around him that was to be purchased. Indeed it was said of him by his

[003-129]enemies, that so covetous was he of Barsetshire property, that he

[003-130]would lead a young neighbour on to his ruin, in order that he might

[003-131]get his land. What—oh! what if he should come to be possessed in

[003-132]this way of any of the fair acres of Framley Court? What if he should

[003-133]become possessed of them all? It can hardly be wondered at that Lady

[003-134]Lufton should not like Chaldicotes.

[003-135]The Chaldicotes set, as Lady Lufton called them, were in every way

[003-136]opposed to what a set should be according to her ideas. She liked

[003-137]cheerful, quiet, well-to-do people, who loved their Church, their

[003-138]country, and their Queen, and who were not too anxious to make a

[003-139]noise in the world. She desired that all the farmers round her should

[003-140]be able to pay their rents without trouble, that all the old women

[003-141]should have warm flannel petticoats, that the working men should

[003-142]be saved from rheumatism by healthy food and dry houses, that they

[003-143]should all be obedient to their pastors and masters—temporal as well

[003-144]as spiritual. That was her idea of loving her country. She desired

[003-145]also that the copses should be full of pheasants, the stubble-field

[003-146]of partridges, and the gorse covers of foxes;—in that way, also, she

[003-147]loved her country. She had ardently longed, during that Crimean war,

[003-148]that the Russians might be beaten—but not by the French, to the

[003-149]exclusion of the English, as had seemed to her to be too much the

[003-150]case; and hardly by the English under the dictatorship of Lord

[003-151]Palmerston. Indeed, she had had but little faith in that war after

[003-152]Lord Aberdeen had been expelled. If, indeed, Lord Derby could have

[003-153]come in!

[003-154]But now as to this Chaldicotes set. After all, there was nothing

[003-155]so very dangerous about them; for it was in London, not in the

[003-156]country, that Mr. Sowerby indulged, if he did indulge, his bachelor

[003-157]mal-practices. Speaking of them as a set, the chief offender was

[003-158]Mr. Harold Smith, or perhaps his wife. He also was a member of

[003-159]Parliament, and, as many thought, a rising man. His father had been

[003-160]for many years a debater in the House, and had held high office.

[003-161]Harold, in early life, had intended himself for the cabinet; and if

[003-162]working hard at his trade could ensure success, he ought to obtain

[003-163]it sooner or later. He had already filled more than one subordinate

[003-164]station, had been at the Treasury, and for a month or two at the

[003-165]Admiralty, astonishing official mankind by his diligence. Those

[003-166]last-named few months had been under Lord Aberdeen, with whom he had

[003-167]been forced to retire. He was a younger son, and not possessed of any

[003-168]large fortune. Politics as a profession was therefore of importance

[003-169]to him. He had in early life married a sister of Mr. Sowerby; and

[003-170]as the lady was some six or seven years older than himself, and had

[003-171]brought with her but a scanty dowry, people thought that in this

[003-172]matter Mr. Harold Smith had not been perspicacious. Mr. Harold Smith

[003-173]was not personally a popular man with any party, though some judged

[003-174]him to be eminently useful. He was laborious, well-informed, and, on

[003-175]the whole, honest; but he was conceited, long-winded, and pompous.
