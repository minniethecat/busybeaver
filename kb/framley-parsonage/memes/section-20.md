## Section 20

[020-001]“You must come down and see my nags, Mark; they came to-day. The

[020-002]Merediths will be off at twelve, and then we can have an hour

[020-003]together.” Mark said he would, and then went home with his wife under

[020-004]his arm.

[020-005]“Well, now, is not she kind?” said Fanny, as soon as they were out on

[020-006]the gravel together.

[020-007]“She is kind; kinder than I can tell you just at present. But did you

[020-008]ever know anything so bitter as she is to the poor bishop? And really

[020-009]the bishop is not so bad.”

[020-010]“Yes; I know something much more bitter; and that is what she thinks

[020-011]of the bishop’s wife. And you know, Mark, it was so unladylike, her

[020-012]getting up in that way. What must the people of Barchester think of

[020-013]her?”

[020-014]“As far as I could see the people of Barchester liked it.”

[020-015]“Nonsense, Mark; they could not. But never mind that now. I want you

[020-016]to own that she is good.” And then Mrs. Robarts went on with another

[020-017]long eulogy on the dowager. Since that affair of the pardon-begging

[020-018]at the parsonage Mrs. Robarts hardly knew how to think well enough of

[020-019]her friend. And the evening had been so pleasant after the dreadful

[020-020]storm and threatenings of hurricanes; her husband had been so well

[020-021]received after his lapse of judgment; the wounds that had looked so

[020-022]sore had been so thoroughly healed, and everything was so pleasant.

[020-023]How all of this would have been changed had she known of that little

[020-024]bill!

[020-025]At twelve the next morning the lord and the vicar were walking

[020-026]through the Framley stables together. Quite a commotion had been

[020-027]made there, for the larger portion of these buildings had of late

[020-028]years seldom been used. But now all was crowding and activity.

[020-029]Seven or eight very precious animals had followed Lord Lufton from

[020-030]Leicestershire, and all of them required dimensions that were thought

[020-031]to be rather excessive by the Framley old-fashioned groom. My lord,

[020-032]however, had a head man of his own who took the matter quite into his

[020-033]own hands.

[020-034]Mark, priest as he was, was quite worldly enough to be fond of a good

[020-035]horse; and for some little time allowed Lord Lufton to descant on the

[020-036]merit of this four-year-old filly, and that magnificent Rattlebones

[020-037]colt, out of a Mousetrap mare; but he had other things that lay

[020-038]heavy on his mind, and after bestowing half an hour on the stud, he

[020-039]contrived to get his friend away to the shrubbery walks.

[020-040]“So you have settled with Sowerby,” Robarts began by saying.

[020-041]“Settled with him; yes, but do you know the price?”

[020-042]“I believe that you have paid five thousand pounds.”

[020-043]“Yes, and about three before; and that in a matter in which I did not

[020-044]really owe one shilling. Whatever I do in future, I’ll keep out of

[020-045]Sowerby’s grip.”

[020-046]“But you don’t think he has been unfair to you.”

[020-047]“Mark, to tell you the truth I have banished the affair from my mind,

[020-048]and don’t wish to take it up again. My mother has paid the money to

[020-049]save the property, and of course I must pay her back. But I think

[020-050]I may promise that I will not have any more money dealings with

[020-051]Sowerby. I will not say that he is dishonest, but at any rate he is

[020-052]sharp.”

[020-053]“Well, Lufton; what will you say when I tell you that I have put my

[020-054]name to a bill for him, for four hundred pounds?”

[020-055]“Say; why I should say—; but you’re joking; a man in your position

[020-056]would never do such a thing.”

[020-057]“But I have done it.”

[020-058]Lord Lufton gave a long low whistle.

[020-059]“He asked me the last night that I was there, making a great

[020-060]favour of it, and declaring that no bill of his had ever yet been

[020-061]dishonoured.”

[020-062]Lord Lufton whistled again. “No bill of his dishonoured! Why the

[020-063]pocket-books of the Jews are stuffed full of his dishonoured papers!

[020-064]And you have really given him your name for four hundred pounds?”

[020-065]“I have certainly.”

[020-066]“At what date?”

[020-067]“Three months.”

[020-068]“And have you thought where you are to get the money?”

[020-069]“I know very well that I can’t get it; not at least by that time. The

[020-070]bankers must renew it for me, and I must pay it by degrees. That is,

[020-071]if Sowerby really does not take it up.”

[020-072]“It is just as likely that he will take up the national debt.”

[020-073]Robarts then told him about the projected marriage with Miss

[020-074]Dunstable, giving it as his opinion that the lady would probably

[020-075]accept the gentleman.

[020-076]“Not at all improbable,” said his lordship, “for Sowerby is an

[020-077]agreeable fellow; and if it be so, he will have all that he wants

[020-078]for life. But his creditors will gain nothing. The duke, who has his

[020-079]title-deeds, will doubtless get his money, and the estate will in

[020-080]fact belong to the wife. But the small fry, such as you, will not get

[020-081]a shilling.”

[020-082]Poor Mark! He had had an inkling of this before; but it had hardly

[020-083]presented itself to him in such certain terms. It was, then, a

[020-084]positive fact, that in punishment for his weakness in having signed

[020-085]that bill he would have to pay, not only four hundred pounds, but

[020-086]four hundred pounds with interest, and expenses of renewal, and

[020-087]commission, and bill stamps. Yes; he had certainly got among the

[020-088]Philistines during that visit of his to the duke. It began to appear

[020-089]to him pretty clearly that it would have been better for him to have

[020-090]relinquished altogether the glories of Chaldicotes and Gatherum

[020-091]Castle.

[020-092]And now, how was he to tell his wife?

[020-093]CHAPTER X.

[020-094]LUCY ROBARTS.

[020-095]And now how was he to tell his wife? That was the consideration heavy

[020-096]on Mark Robarts’ mind when last we left him; and he turned the matter

[020-097]often in his thoughts before he could bring himself to a resolution.

[020-098]At last he did do so, and one may say that it was not altogether a

[020-099]bad one, if only he could carry it out.

[020-100]He would ascertain in what bank that bill of his had been discounted.

[020-101]He would ask Sowerby, and if he could not learn from him, he would go

[020-102]to the three banks in Barchester. That it had been taken to one of

[020-103]them he felt tolerably certain. He would explain to the manager his

[020-104]conviction that he would have to make good the amount, his inability

[020-105]to do so at the end of the three months, and the whole state of his

[020-106]income; and then the banker would explain to him how the matter might

[020-107]be arranged. He thought that he could pay £50 every three months with

[020-108]interest. As soon as this should have been concerted with the banker,

[020-109]he would let his wife know all about it. Were he to tell her at the

[020-110]present moment, while the matter was all unsettled, the intelligence

[020-111]would frighten her into illness.

[020-112]But on the next morning there came to him tidings by the hands of

[020-113]Robin postman, which for a long while upset all his plans. The letter

[020-114]was from Exeter. His father had been taken ill, and had very quickly

[020-115]been pronounced to be in danger. That evening—the evening on which

[020-116]his sister wrote—the old man was much worse, and it was desirable

[020-117]that Mark should go off to Exeter as quickly as possible. Of

[020-118]course he went to Exeter—again leaving the Framley souls at the

[020-119]mercy of the Welsh Low Churchman. Framley is only four miles from

[020-120]Silverbridge, and at Silverbridge he was on the direct road to the

[020-121]west. He was therefore at Exeter before nightfall on that day.

[020-122]But nevertheless he arrived there too late to see his father again

[020-123]alive. The old man’s illness had been sudden and rapid, and he

[020-124]expired without again seeing his eldest son. Mark arrived at the

[020-125]house of mourning just as they were learning to realize the full

[020-126]change in their position.

[020-127]The doctor’s career had been on the whole successful, but

[020-128]nevertheless he did not leave behind him as much money as the world

[020-129]had given him credit for possessing. Who ever does? Dr. Robarts had

[020-130]educated a large family, had always lived with every comfort, and

[020-131]had never possessed a shilling but what he had earned himself. A

[020-132]physician’s fees come in, no doubt, with comfortable rapidity as soon

[020-133]as rich old gentlemen and middle-aged ladies begin to put their faith

[020-134]in him; but fees run out almost with equal rapidity when a wife and

[020-135]seven children are treated to everything that the world considers

[020-136]most desirable. Mark, we have seen, had been educated at Harrow and

[020-137]Oxford, and it may be said, therefore, that he had received his

[020-138]patrimony early in life. For Gerald Robarts, the second brother, a

[020-139]commission had been bought in a crack regiment. He also had been

[020-140]lucky, having lived and become a captain in the Crimea; and the

[020-141]purchase-money was lodged for his majority. And John Robarts, the

[020-142]youngest, was a clerk in the Petty Bag Office, and was already

[020-143]assistant private secretary to the Lord Petty Bag himself—a place of

[020-144]considerable trust, if not hitherto of large emolument; and on his

[020-145]education money had been spent freely, for in these days a young man

[020-146]cannot get into the Petty Bag Office without knowing at least three

[020-147]modern languages; and he must be well up in trigonometry too, in

[020-148]bible theology, or in one dead language—at his option.

[020-149]And the doctor had four daughters. The two elder were married,

[020-150]including that Blanche with whom Lord Lufton was to have fallen in

[020-151]love at the vicar’s wedding. A Devonshire squire had done this in the

[020-152]lord’s place; but on marrying her it was necessary that he should

[020-153]have a few thousand pounds, two or three perhaps, and the old doctor

[020-154]had managed that they should be forthcoming. The elder also had not

[020-155]been sent away from the paternal mansion quite empty-handed. There

[020-156]were, therefore, at the time of the doctor’s death two children left

[020-157]at home, of whom one only, Lucy, the younger, will come much across

[020-158]us in the course of our story.

[020-159]Mark stayed for ten days at Exeter, he and the Devonshire squire

[020-160]having been named as executors in the will. In this document it was

[020-161]explained that the doctor trusted that provision had been made for

[020-162]most of his children. As for his dear son Mark, he said, he was aware

[020-163]that he need be under no uneasiness. On hearing this read Mark smiled

[020-164]sweetly, and looked very gracious; but, nevertheless, his heart did

[020-165]sink somewhat within him, for there had been a hope that a small

[020-166]windfall, coming now so opportunely, might enable him to rid himself

[020-167]at once of that dreadful Sowerby incubus. And then the will went on

[020-168]to declare that Mary, and Gerald, and Blanche, had also, by God’s

[020-169]providence, been placed beyond want. And here, looking into the

[020-170]squire’s face, one might have thought that his heart fell a little

[020-171]also; for he had not so full a command of his feelings as his

[020-172]brother-in-law, who had been so much more before the world. To John,

[020-173]the assistant private secretary, was left a legacy of a thousand

[020-174]pounds; and to Jane and Lucy certain sums in certain four per cents.,

[020-175]which were quite sufficient to add an efficient value to the hands

[020-176]of those young ladies in the eyes of most prudent young would-be

[020-177]Benedicts. Over and beyond this there was nothing but the furniture,

[020-178]which he desired might be sold, and the proceeds divided among them

[020-179]all. It might come to sixty or seventy pounds a piece, and pay the

[020-180]expenses incidental on his death.

[020-181]And then all men and women there and thereabouts said that old Dr.

[020-182]Robarts had done well. His life had been good and prosperous, and

[020-183]his will was just. And Mark, among others, so declared,—and was so

[020-184]convinced in spite of his own little disappointment. And on the third

[020-185]morning after the reading of the will Squire Crowdy, of Creamclotted

[020-186]Hall, altogether got over his grief, and said that it was all right.

[020-187]And then it was decided that Jane should go home with him,—for

[020-188]there was a brother squire who, it was thought, might have an eye to

[020-189]Jane;—and Lucy, the younger, should be taken to Framley Parsonage.

[020-190]In a fortnight from the receipt of that letter Mark arrived at his

[020-191]own house with his sister Lucy under his wing.
