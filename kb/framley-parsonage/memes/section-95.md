## Section 95

[095-001]“He started four days ago, by way of Boulogne,” said Mrs. Tickler,

[095-002]who seemed to be very well up in the whole affair. “I am so sorry for

[095-003]poor dear Griselda. I am told she has got all her things. It is such

[095-004]a pity, you know.”

[095-005]“But why should not Lord Dumbello come back from the Continent?” said

[095-006]Miss Bold, very quietly.

[095-007]“Why not, indeed? I’m sure I hope he may,” said Mrs. Proudie. “And no

[095-008]doubt he will, some day. But if he be such a man as they say he is,

[095-009]it is really well for Griselda that she should be relieved from such

[095-010]a marriage. For, after all, Mrs. Arabin, what are the things of this

[095-011]world?—dust beneath our feet, ashes between our teeth, grass cut for

[095-012]the oven, vanity, vexation, and nothing more!”—well pleased with

[095-013]which variety of Christian metaphors Mrs. Proudie walked on, still

[095-014]muttering, however, something about worms and grubs, by which she

[095-015]intended to signify her own species and the Dumbello and Grantly

[095-016]sects of it in particular.

[095-017]This now had gone so far that Mrs. Arabin conceived herself bound in

[095-018]duty to see her sister, and it was then settled in consultation at

[095-019]Plumstead that the archdeacon should call officially at the palace

[095-020]and beg that the rumour might be contradicted. This he did early on

[095-021]the next morning and was shown into the bishop’s study, in which he

[095-022]found both his lordship and Mrs. Proudie. The bishop rose to greet

[095-023]him with special civility, smiling his very sweetest on him, as

[095-024]though of all his clergy the archdeacon were the favourite; but Mrs.

[095-025]Proudie wore something of a gloomy aspect, as though she knew that

[095-026]such a visit at such an hour must have reference to some special

[095-027]business. The morning calls made by the archdeacon at the palace in

[095-028]the way of ordinary civility were not numerous.

[095-029]On the present occasion he dashed at once into his subject. “I have

[095-030]called this morning, Mrs. Proudie,” said he, “because I wish to ask

[095-031]a favour from you.” Whereupon Mrs. Proudie bowed.

[095-032]“Mrs. Proudie will be most happy, I am sure,” said the bishop.

[095-033]“I find that some foolish people have been talking in Barchester

[095-034]about my daughter,” said the archdeacon; “and I wish to ask Mrs.

[095-035]Proudie—”

[095-036]Most women under such circumstances would have felt the awkwardness

[095-037]of their situation, and would have prepared to eat their past

[095-038]words with wry faces. But not so Mrs. Proudie. Mrs. Grantly had had

[095-039]the imprudence to throw Mr. Slope in her face—there, in her own

[095-040]drawing-room, and she was resolved to be revenged. Mrs. Grantly, too,

[095-041]had ridiculed the Tickler match, and no too great niceness should now

[095-042]prevent Mrs. Proudie from speaking her mind about the Dumbello match.

[095-043]“A great many people are talking about her, I am sorry to say,” said

[095-044]Mrs. Proudie; “but, poor dear, it is not her fault. It might have

[095-045]happened to any girl; only, perhaps, a little more care—; you’ll

[095-046]excuse me, Dr. Grantly.”

[095-047]“I have come here to allude to a report which has been spread about

[095-048]in Barchester, that the match between Lord Dumbello and my daughter

[095-049]has been broken off; and—”

[095-050]“Everybody in Barchester knows it, I believe,” said Mrs. Proudie.

[095-051]—“and,” continued the archdeacon, “to request that that report may

[095-052]be contradicted.”

[095-053]“Contradicted! Why, he has gone right away,—out of the country!”

[095-054]“Never mind where he has gone to, Mrs. Proudie; I beg that the report

[095-055]may be contradicted.”

[095-056]“You’ll have to go round to every house in Barchester then,” said

[095-057]she.

[095-058]“By no means,” replied the archdeacon. “And perhaps it may be right

[095-059]that I should explain to the bishop that I came here because—”

[095-060]“The bishop knows nothing about it,” said Mrs. Proudie.

[095-061]“Nothing in the world,” said his lordship. “And I am sure I hope that

[095-062]the young lady may not be disappointed.”

[095-063]—“because the matter was so distinctly mentioned to Mrs. Arabin by

[095-064]yourself yesterday.”

[095-065]“Distinctly mentioned! Of course it was distinctly mentioned. There

[095-066]are some things which can’t be kept under a bushel, Dr. Grantly; and

[095-067]this seems to be one of them. Your going about in this way won’t make

[095-068]Lord Dumbello marry the young lady.”

[095-069]That was true; nor would it make Mrs. Proudie hold her tongue.

[095-070]Perhaps the archdeacon was wrong in his present errand, and so he now

[095-071]began to bethink himself. “At any rate,” said he, “when I tell you

[095-072]that there is no ground whatever for such a report you will do me the

[095-073]kindness to say that, as far as you are concerned, it shall go no

[095-074]further. I think, my lord, I am not asking too much in asking that.”

[095-075]“The bishop knows nothing about it,” said Mrs. Proudie again.

[095-076]“Nothing at all,” said the bishop.

[095-077]“And as I must protest that I believe the information which has

[095-078]reached me on this head,” said Mrs. Proudie, “I do not see how it is

[095-079]possible that I should contradict it. I can easily understand your

[095-080]feelings, Dr. Grantly. Considering your daughter’s position the match

[095-081]was, as regards earthly wealth, a very great one. I do not wonder

[095-082]that you should be grieved at its being broken off; but I trust that

[095-083]this sorrow may eventuate in a blessing to you and to Miss Griselda.

[095-084]These worldly disappointments are precious balms, and I trust you

[095-085]know how to accept them as such.”

[095-086]The fact was that Dr. Grantly had done altogether wrong in coming to

[095-087]the palace. His wife might have some chance with Mrs. Proudie, but

[095-088]he had none. Since she had come to Barchester he had had only two or

[095-089]three encounters with her, and in all of these he had gone to the

[095-090]wall. His visits to the palace always resulted in his leaving the

[095-091]presence of the inhabitants in a frame of mind by no means desirable,

[095-092]and he now found that he had to do so once again. He could not

[095-093]compel Mrs. Proudie to say that the report was untrue; nor could he

[095-094]condescend to make counter hits at her about her own daughter, as his

[095-095]wife would have done. And thus, having utterly failed, he got up and

[095-096]took his leave.

[095-097]But the worst of the matter was, that, in going home, he could not

[095-098]divest his mind of the idea that there might be some truth in the

[095-099]report. What if Lord Dumbello had gone to the Continent resolved

[095-100]to send back from thence some reason why it was impossible that he

[095-101]should make Miss Grantly his wife? Such things had been done before

[095-102]now by men in his rank. Whether or no Mrs. Tickler had been the

[095-103]letter-writing wellwisher from Littlebath, or had induced her friend

[095-104]to be so, it did seem manifest to him, Dr. Grantly, that Mrs. Proudie

[095-105]absolutely believed the report which she promulgated so diligently.

[095-106]The wish might be father to the thought, no doubt; but that the

[095-107]thought was truly there, Dr. Grantly could not induce himself to

[095-108]disbelieve.

[095-109]His wife was less credulous, and to a certain degree comforted him;

[095-110]but that evening he received a letter which greatly confirmed the

[095-111]suspicions set on foot by Mrs. Proudie, and even shook his wife’s

[095-112]faith in Lord Dumbello. It was from a mere acquaintance, who in the

[095-113]ordinary course of things would not have written to him. And the bulk

[095-114]of the letter referred to ordinary things, as to which the gentleman

[095-115]in question would hardly have thought of giving himself the trouble

[095-116]to write a letter. But at the end of the note he said,—

[095-117]“Of course you are aware that Dumbello is off to Paris; I have not

[095-118]heard whether the exact day of his return is fixed.”

[095-119]“It is true then,” said the archdeacon, striking the library table

[095-120]with his hand, and becoming absolutely white about the mouth and

[095-121]jaws.

[095-122]“It cannot be,” said Mrs. Grantly; but even she was now trembling.

[095-123]“If it be so I’ll drag him back to England by the collar of his coat,

[095-124]and disgrace him before the steps of his father’s hall.”

[095-125]And the archdeacon as he uttered the threat looked his character as

[095-126]an irate British father much better than he did his other character

[095-127]as a clergyman of the Church of England. The archdeacon had been

[095-128]greatly worsted by Mrs. Proudie, but he was a man who knew how to

[095-129]fight his battles among men,—sometimes without too close a regard to

[095-130]his cloth.

[095-131]“Had Lord Dumbello intended any such thing he would have written, or

[095-132]got some friend to write by this time,” said Mrs. Grantly. “It is

[095-133]quite possible that he might wish to be off, but he would be too

[095-134]chary of his name not to endeavour to do so with decency.”

[095-135]Thus the matter was discussed, and it appeared to them both to be so

[095-136]serious that the archdeacon resolved to go at once to London. That

[095-137]Lord Dumbello had gone to France he did not doubt; but he would find

[095-138]some one in town acquainted with the young man’s intentions, and

[095-139]he would, no doubt, be able to hear when his return was expected.

[095-140]If there were real reason for apprehension he would follow the

[095-141]runagate to the Continent, but he would not do this without absolute

[095-142]knowledge. According to Lord Dumbello’s present engagements he was

[095-143]bound to present himself in August next at Plumstead Episcopi, with

[095-144]the view of then and there taking Griselda Grantly in marriage;

[095-145]but if he kept his word in this respect no one had a right to

[095-146]quarrel with him for going to Paris in the meantime. Most expectant

[095-147]bridegrooms would, no doubt, under such circumstances have declared

[095-148]their intentions to their future brides; but if Lord Dumbello

[095-149]were different from others, who had a right on that account to be

[095-150]indignant with him? He was unlike other men in other things; and

[095-151]especially unlike other men in being the eldest son of the Marquis

[095-152]of Hartletop. It would be all very well for Tickler to proclaim his

[095-153]whereabouts from week to week; but the eldest son of a marquis might

[095-154]find it inconvenient to be so precise! Nevertheless the archdeacon

[095-155]thought it only prudent to go up to London.

[095-156]“Susan,” said the archdeacon to his wife, just as he was

[095-157]starting;—at this moment neither of them were in the happiest

[095-158]spirits,—“I think I would say a word of caution to Griselda.”

[095-159]“Do you feel so much doubt about it as that?” said Mrs. Grantly. But

[095-160]even she did not dare to put a direct negative to this proposal, so

[095-161]much had she been moved by what she had heard!

[095-162]“I think I would do so, not frightening her more than I could help.

[095-163]It will lessen the blow if it be that the blow is to fall.”

[095-164]“It will kill me,” said Mrs. Grantly; “but I think that she will be

[095-165]able to bear it.”

[095-166]On the next morning Mrs. Grantly, with much cunning preparation, went

[095-167]about the task which her husband had left her to perform. It took her

[095-168]long to do, for she was very cunning in the doing of it; but at last

[095-169]it dropped from her in words that there was a possibility—a bare

[095-170]possibility—that some disappointment might even yet be in store for

[095-171]them.

[095-172]“Do you mean, mamma, that the marriage will be put off?”

[095-173]“I don’t mean to say that I think it will; God forbid! but it is just

[095-174]possible. I daresay that I am very wrong to tell you of this, but I

[095-175]know that you have sense enough to bear it. Papa has gone to London

[095-176]and we shall hear from him soon.”

[095-177]“Then, mamma, I had better give them orders not to go on with the

[095-178]marking.”

[095-180]LADY LUFTON’S REQUEST.

[095-181]The bailiffs on that day had their meals regular,—and their beer,

[095-182]which state of things, together with an absence of all duty in the

[095-183]way of making inventories and the like, I take to be the earthly

[095-184]paradise of bailiffs; and on the next morning they walked off with

[095-185]civil speeches and many apologies as to their intrusion. “They was

[095-186]very sorry,” they said, “to have troubled a gen’leman as were a

[095-187]gen’leman, but in their way of business what could they do?” To

[095-188]which one of them added a remark that, “business is business.” This

[095-189]statement I am not prepared to contradict, but I would recommend

[095-190]all men in choosing a profession to avoid any that may require an

[095-191]apology at every turn;—either an apology or else a somewhat violent

[095-192]assertion of right. Each younger male reader may perhaps reply that

[095-193]he has no thought of becoming a sheriff’s officer; but then are there

[095-194]not other cognate lines of life to which perhaps the attention of

[095-195]some such may be attracted?
