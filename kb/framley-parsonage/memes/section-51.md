## Section 51

[051-001]And now the effort was to be made with the object of which all this

[051-002]intimacy had been effected. As she came thither in her carriage, Mrs.

[051-003]Harold Smith herself was not altogether devoid of that sinking of

[051-004]the heart which is so frequently the forerunner of any difficult and

[051-005]hazardous undertaking. She had declared that she would feel no fear

[051-006]in making the little proposition. But she did feel something very

[051-007]like it; and when she made her entrance into the little room she

[051-008]certainly wished that the work was done and over.

[051-009]“How is poor Mr. Smith to-day?” asked Miss Dunstable, with an air

[051-010]of mock condolence, as her friend seated herself in her accustomed

[051-011]easy-chair. The downfall of the gods was as yet a history hardly

[051-012]three days old, and it might well be supposed that the late lord of

[051-013]the Petty Bag had hardly recovered from his misfortune.

[051-014]“Well, he is better, I think, this morning; at least I should judge

[051-015]so from the manner in which he confronted his eggs. But still I don’t

[051-016]like the way he handles the carving-knife. I am sure he is always

[051-017]thinking of Mr. Supplehouse at those moments.”

[051-018]“Poor man! I mean Supplehouse. After all, why shouldn’t he follow his

[051-019]trade as well as another? Live and let live, that’s what I say.”

[051-020]“Ay, but it’s kill and let kill with him. That is what Horace says.

[051-021]However, I am tired of all that now, and I came here to-day to talk

[051-022]about something else.”

[051-023]“I rather like Mr. Supplehouse myself,” exclaimed Miss Dunstable. “He

[051-024]never makes any bones about the matter. He has a certain work to do,

[051-025]and a certain cause to serve—namely, his own; and in order to do

[051-026]that work, and serve that cause, he uses such weapons as God has

[051-027]placed in his hands.”

[051-028]“That’s what the wild beasts do.”

[051-029]“And where will you find men honester than they? The tiger tears you

[051-030]up because he is hungry and wants to eat you. That’s what Supplehouse

[051-031]does. But there are so many among us tearing up one another without

[051-032]any excuse of hunger. The mere pleasure of destroying is reason

[051-033]enough.”

[051-034]“Well, my dear, my mission to you to-day is certainly not one of

[051-035]destruction, as you will admit when you hear it. It is one, rather,

[051-036]very absolutely of salvation. I have come to make love to you.”

[051-037]“Then the salvation, I suppose, is not for myself,” said Miss

[051-038]Dunstable.

[051-039]It was quite clear to Mrs. Harold Smith that Miss Dunstable had

[051-040]immediately understood the whole purport of this visit, and that she

[051-041]was not in any great measure surprised. It did not seem from the

[051-042]tone of the heiress’s voice, or from the serious look which at once

[051-043]settled on her face, that she would be prepared to give a very

[051-044]ready compliance. But then great objects can only be won with great

[051-045]efforts.

[051-046]“That’s as may be,” said Mrs. Harold Smith. “For you and another

[051-047]also, I hope. But I trust, at any rate, that I may not offend you?”

[051-048]“Oh, laws, no; nothing of that kind ever offends me now.”

[051-049]“Well, I suppose you’re used to it.”

[051-050]“Like the eels, my dear. I don’t mind it the least in the world—only

[051-051]sometimes, you know, it is a little tedious.”

[051-052]“I’ll endeavour to avoid that, so I may as well break the ice at

[051-053]once. You know enough of Nathaniel’s affairs to be aware that he is

[051-054]not a very rich man.”

[051-055]“Since you do ask me about it, I suppose there’s no harm in saying

[051-056]that I believe him to be a very poor man.”

[051-057]“Not the least harm in the world, but just the reverse. Whatever may

[051-058]come of this, my wish is that the truth should be told scrupulously

[051-059]on all sides; the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth.”

[051-060]“_Magna est veritas_,” said Miss Dunstable. “The Bishop of Barchester

[051-061]taught me as much Latin as that at Chaldicotes; and he did add some

[051-062]more, but there was a long word, and I forgot it.”

[051-063]“The bishop was quite right, my dear, I’m sure. But if you go to your

[051-064]Latin, I’m lost. As we were just now saying, my brother’s pecuniary

[051-065]affairs are in a very bad state. He has a beautiful property of

[051-066]his own, which has been in the family for I can’t say how many

[051-067]centuries—long before the Conquest, I know.”

[051-068]“I wonder what my ancestors were then?”

[051-069]“It does not much signify to any of us,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, with

[051-070]a moral shake of her head, “what our ancestors were; but it’s a sad

[051-071]thing to see an old property go to ruin.”

[051-072]“Yes, indeed; we none of us like to see our property going to ruin,

[051-073]whether it be old or new. I have some of that sort of feeling

[051-074]already, although mine was only made the other day out of an

[051-075]apothecary’s shop.”

[051-076]“God forbid that I should ever help you to ruin it,” said Mrs. Harold

[051-077]Smith. “I should be sorry to be the means of your losing a ten-pound

[051-078]note.”

[051-079]“_Magna est veritas_, as the dear bishop said,” exclaimed Miss

[051-080]Dunstable. “Let us have the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but

[051-081]the truth, as we agreed just now.”

[051-082]Mrs. Harold Smith did begin to find that the task before her was

[051-083]difficult. There was a hardness about Miss Dunstable when matters of

[051-084]business were concerned on which it seemed almost impossible to make

[051-085]any impression. It was not that she had evinced any determination to

[051-086]refuse the tender of Mr. Sowerby’s hand; but she was so painfully

[051-087]resolute not to have dust thrown in her eyes! Mrs. Harold Smith had

[051-088]commenced with a mind fixed upon avoiding what she called humbug;

[051-089]but this sort of humbug had become so prominent a part of her usual

[051-090]rhetoric, that she found it very hard to abandon it.

[051-091]“And that’s what I wish,” said she. “Of course my chief object is to

[051-092]secure my brother’s happiness.”

[051-093]“That’s very unkind to poor Mr. Harold Smith.”

[051-094]“Well, well, well—you know what I mean.”

[051-095]“Yes, I think I do know what you mean. Your brother is a gentleman of

[051-096]good family, but of no means.”

[051-097]“Not quite so bad as that.”

[051-098]“Of embarrassed means, then, or anything that you will; whereas I am

[051-099]a lady of no family, but of sufficient wealth. You think that if you

[051-100]brought us together and made a match of it, it would be a very good

[051-101]thing for—for whom?” said Miss Dunstable.

[051-102]“Yes, exactly,” said Mrs. Harold Smith.

[051-103]“For which of us? Remember the bishop now and his nice little bit of

[051-104]Latin.”

[051-105]“For Nathaniel then,” said Mrs. Harold Smith, boldly. “It would be a

[051-106]very good thing for him.” And a slight smile came across her face as

[051-107]she said it. “Now that’s honest, or the mischief is in it.”

[051-108]“Yes, that’s honest enough. And did he send you here to tell me

[051-109]this?”

[051-110]“Well, he did that, and something else.”

[051-111]“And now let’s have the something else. The really important part, I

[051-112]have no doubt, has been spoken.”

[051-113]“No, by no means, by no means all of it. But you are so hard on one,

[051-114]my dear, with your running after honesty, that one is not able to

[051-115]tell the real facts as they are. You make one speak in such a bald,

[051-116]naked way.”

[051-117]“Ah, you think that anything naked must be indecent; even truth.”

[051-118]“I think it is more proper-looking, and better suited, too, for the

[051-119]world’s work, when it goes about with some sort of a garment on

[051-120]it. We are so used to a leaven of falsehood in all we hear and say,

[051-121]now-a-days, that nothing is more likely to deceive us than the

[051-122]absolute truth. If a shopkeeper told me that his wares were simply

[051-123]middling, of course, I should think that they were not worth a

[051-124]farthing. But all that has nothing to do with my poor brother. Well,

[051-125]what was I saying?”

[051-126]“You were going to tell me how well he would use me, no doubt.”

[051-127]“Something of that kind.”

[051-128]“That he wouldn’t beat me; or spend all my money if I managed to have

[051-129]it tied up out of his power; or look down on me with contempt because

[051-130]my father was an apothecary! Was not that what you were going to

[051-131]say?”

[051-132]“I was going to tell you that you might be more happy as Mrs. Sowerby

[051-133]of Chaldicotes than you can be as Miss Dunstable—”

[051-134]“Of Mount Lebanon. And had Mr. Sowerby no other message to

[051-135]send?—nothing about love, or anything of that sort? I should like,

[051-136]you know, to understand what his feelings are before I take such a

[051-137]leap.”

[051-138]“I do believe he has as true a regard for you as any man of his age

[051-139]ever does have—”

[051-140]“For any woman of mine. That is not putting it in a very devoted

[051-141]way certainly; but I am glad to see that you remember the bishop’s

[051-142]maxim.”

[051-143]“What would you have me say? If I told you that he was dying for

[051-144]love, you would say, I was trying to cheat you; and now because I

[051-145]don’t tell you so, you say that he is wanting in devotion. I must say

[051-146]you are hard to please.”

[051-147]“Perhaps I am, and very unreasonable into the bargain. I ought to ask

[051-148]no questions of the kind when your brother proposes to do me so much

[051-149]honour. As for my expecting the love of a man who condescends to wish

[051-150]to be my husband, that, of course, would be monstrous. What right can

[051-151]I have to think that any man should love me? It ought to be enough

[051-152]for me to know that as I am rich, I can get a husband. What business

[051-153]can such as I have to inquire whether the gentleman who would so

[051-154]honour me really would like my company, or would only deign to put up

[051-155]with my presence in his household?”

[051-156]“Now, my dear Miss Dunstable—”

[051-157]“Of course I am not such an ass as to expect that any gentleman

[051-158]should love me; and I feel that I ought to be obliged to your brother

[051-159]for sparing me the string of complimentary declarations which are

[051-160]usual on such occasions. He, at any rate, is not tedious—or rather

[051-161]you on his behalf; for no doubt his own time is so occupied with his

[051-162]parliamentary duties that he cannot attend to this little matter

[051-163]himself. I do feel grateful to him; and perhaps nothing more will be

[051-164]necessary than to give him a schedule of the property, and name an

[051-165]early day for putting him in possession.”

[051-166]Mrs. Smith did feel that she was rather badly used. This Miss

[051-167]Dunstable, in their mutual confidences, had so often ridiculed the

[051-168]love-making grimaces of her mercenary suitors—had spoken so fiercely

[051-169]against those who had persecuted her, not because they had desired

[051-170]her money, but on account of their ill-judgment in thinking her to

[051-171]be a fool—that Mrs. Smith had a right to expect that the method

[051-172]she had adopted for opening the negotiation would be taken in a

[051-173]better spirit. Could it be possible, after all, thought Mrs. Smith to

[051-174]herself, that Miss Dunstable was like other women, and that she did

[051-175]like to have men kneeling at her feet? Could it be the case that she

[051-176]had advised her brother badly, and that it would have been better for

[051-177]him to have gone about his work in the old-fashioned way? “They are

[051-178]very hard to manage,” said Mrs. Harold Smith to herself, thinking of

[051-179]her own sex.

[051-180]“He was coming here himself,” said she, “but I advised him not to do

[051-181]so.”

[051-182]“That was so kind of you.”

[051-183]“I thought that I could explain to you more openly and more freely,

[051-184]what his intentions really are.”
