## Section 96

[096-001]On the evening of the day on which they went Mark received a note

[096-002]from Lady Lufton begging him to call early on the following morning,

[096-003]and immediately after breakfast he went across to Framley Court. It

[096-004]may be imagined that he was not in a very happy frame of mind, but he

[096-005]felt the truth of his wife’s remark that the first plunge into cold

[096-006]water was always the worst. Lady Lufton was not a woman who would

[096-007]continually throw his disgrace into his teeth, however terribly cold

[096-008]might be the first words with which she spoke of it. He strove hard

[096-009]as he entered her room to carry his usual look and bearing, and to

[096-010]put out his hand to greet her with his customary freedom, but he knew

[096-011]that he failed. And it may be said that no good man who has broken

[096-012]down in his goodness can carry the disgrace of his fall without some

[096-013]look of shame. When a man is able to do that, he ceases to be in any

[096-014]way good.

[096-015]“This has been a distressing affair,” said Lady Lufton after her

[096-016]first salutation.

[096-017]“Yes, indeed,” said he. “It has been very sad for poor Fanny.”

[096-018]“Well; we must all have our little periods of grief; and it may

[096-019]perhaps be fortunate if none of us have worse than this. She will not

[096-020]complain, herself, I am sure.”

[096-021]“She complain!”

[096-022]“No, I am sure she will not. And now all I’ve got to say, Mr.

[096-023]Robarts, is this: I hope you and Lufton have had enough to do with

[096-024]black sheep to last you your lives; for I must protest that your late

[096-025]friend Mr. Sowerby is a black sheep.”

[096-026]In no possible way could Lady Lufton have alluded to the matter with

[096-027]greater kindness than in thus joining Mark’s name with that of her

[096-028]son. It took away all the bitterness of the rebuke, and made the

[096-029]subject one on which even he might have spoken without difficulty.

[096-030]But now, seeing that she was so gentle to him, he could not but lean

[096-031]the more hardly on himself.

[096-032]“I have been very foolish,” said he, “very foolish and very wrong,

[096-033]and very wicked.”

[096-034]“Very foolish, I believe, Mr. Robarts—to speak frankly and once for

[096-035]all; but, as I also believe, nothing worse. I thought it best for

[096-036]both of us that we should just have one word about it, and now I

[096-037]recommend that the matter be never mentioned between us again.”

[096-038]“God bless you, Lady Lufton,” he said. “I think no man ever had such

[096-039]a friend as you are.”

[096-040]She had been very quiet during the interview, and almost subdued, not

[096-041]speaking with the animation that was usual to her; for this affair

[096-042]with Mr. Robarts was not the only one she had to complete that day,

[096-043]nor, perhaps, the one most difficult of completion. But she cheered

[096-044]up a little under the praise now bestowed on her, for it was the

[096-045]sort of praise she loved best. She did hope, and, perhaps, flatter

[096-046]herself, that she was a good friend.

[096-047]“You must be good enough, then, to gratify my friendship by coming up

[096-048]to dinner this evening; and Fanny, too, of course. I cannot take any

[096-049]excuse, for the matter is completely arranged. I have a particular

[096-050]reason for wishing it.” These last violent injunctions had been added

[096-051]because Lady Lufton had seen a refusal rising in the parson’s face.

[096-052]Poor Lady Lufton! Her enemies—for even she had enemies—used to

[096-053]declare of her, that an invitation to dinner was the only method of

[096-054]showing itself of which her good-humour was cognizant. But let me ask

[096-055]of her enemies whether it is not as good a method as any other known

[096-056]to be extant? Under such orders as these obedience was of course a

[096-057]necessity, and he promised that he, with his wife, would come across

[096-058]to dinner. And then, when he went away, Lady Lufton ordered her

[096-059]carriage.

[096-060]During these doings at Framley, Lucy Robarts still remained at

[096-061]Hogglestock, nursing Mrs. Crawley. Nothing occurred to take her back

[096-062]to Framley, for the same note from Fanny which gave her the first

[096-063]tidings of the arrival of the Philistines told her also of their

[096-064]departure—and also of the source from whence relief had reached

[096-065]them. “Don’t come, therefore, for that reason,” said the note, “but,

[096-066]nevertheless, do come as quickly as you can, for the whole house is

[096-067]sad without you.”

[096-068]On the morning after the receipt of this note Lucy was sitting, as

[096-069]was now usual with her, beside an old arm-chair to which her patient

[096-070]had lately been promoted. The fever had gone, and Mrs. Crawley was

[096-071]slowly regaining her strength—very slowly, and with frequent caution

[096-072]from the Silverbridge doctor that any attempt at being well too fast

[096-073]might again precipitate her into an abyss of illness and domestic

[096-074]inefficiency.

[096-075]“I really think I can get about to-morrow,” said she; “and then, dear

[096-076]Lucy, I need not keep you longer from your home.”

[096-077]“You are in a great hurry to get rid of me, I think. I suppose Mr.

[096-078]Crawley has been complaining again about the cream in his tea.”

[096-079]Mr. Crawley had on one occasion stated his assured conviction that

[096-080]surreptitious daily supplies were being brought into the house,

[096-081]because he had detected the presence of cream instead of milk in his

[096-082]own cup. As, however, the cream had been going for sundry days before

[096-083]this, Miss Robarts had not thought much of his ingenuity in making

[096-084]the discovery.

[096-085]“Ah, you do not know how he speaks of you when your back is turned.”

[096-086]“And how does he speak of me? I know you would not have the courage

[096-087]to tell me the whole.”

[096-088]“No, I have not; for you would think it absurd coming from one

[096-089]who looks like him. He says that if he were to write a poem about

[096-090]womanhood, he would make you the heroine.”

[096-091]“With a cream-jug in my hand, or else sewing buttons on to a

[096-092]shirt-collar. But he never forgave me about the mutton broth. He told

[096-093]me, in so many words, that I was a—storyteller. And for the matter

[096-094]of that, my dear, so I was.”

[096-095]“He told me that you were an angel.”

[096-096]“Goodness gracious!”

[096-097]“A ministering angel. And so you have been. I can almost feel it in

[096-098]my heart to be glad that I have been ill, seeing that I have had you

[096-099]for my friend.”

[096-100]“But you might have had that good fortune without the fever.”

[096-101]“No, I should not. In my married life I have made no friends till my

[096-102]illness brought you to me; nor should I ever really have known you

[096-103]but for that. How should I get to know any one?”

[096-104]“You will now, Mrs. Crawley; will you not? Promise that you will.

[096-105]You will come to us at Framley when you are well? You have promised

[096-106]already, you know.”

[096-107]“You made me do so when I was too weak to refuse.”

[096-108]“And I shall make you keep your promise too. He shall come, also,

[096-109]if he likes; but you shall come whether he likes or no. And I won’t

[096-110]hear a word about your old dresses. Old dresses will wear as well at

[096-111]Framley as at Hogglestock.”

[096-112]From all which it will appear that Mrs. Crawley and Lucy Robarts had

[096-113]become very intimate during this period of the nursing; as two women

[096-114]always will, or, at least should do, when shut up for weeks together

[096-115]in the same sick room.

[096-116]The conversation was still going on between them when the sound of

[096-117]wheels was heard upon the road. It was no highway that passed before

[096-118]the house, and carriages of any sort were not frequent there.

[096-119]“It is Fanny, I am sure,” said Lucy, rising from her chair.

[096-120]“There are two horses,” said Mrs. Crawley, distinguishing the noise

[096-121]with the accurate sense of hearing which is always attached to

[096-122]sickness; “and it is not the noise of the pony-carriage.”

[096-123]“It is a regular carriage,” said Lucy, speaking from the window, “and

[096-124]stopping here. It is somebody from Framley Court, for I know the

[096-125]servant.”

[096-126]As she spoke a blush came to her forehead. Might it not be Lord

[096-127]Lufton, she thought to herself,—forgetting at the moment that Lord

[096-128]Lufton did not go about the country in a close chariot with a fat

[096-129]footman. Intimate as she had become with Mrs. Crawley she had said

[096-130]nothing to her new friend on the subject of her love affair.

[096-131]The carriage stopped and down came the footman, but nobody spoke to

[096-132]him from the inside.

[096-133]“He has probably brought something from Framley,” said Lucy, having

[096-134]cream and such like matters in her mind; for cream and such like

[096-135]matters had come from Framley Court more than once during her sojourn

[096-136]there. “And the carriage, probably, happened to be coming this way.”

[096-137]But the mystery soon elucidated itself partially, or, perhaps, became

[096-138]more mysterious in another way. The red-armed little girl who had

[096-139]been taken away by her frightened mother in the first burst of the

[096-140]fever had now returned to her place, and at the present moment

[096-141]entered the room, with awestruck face, declaring that Miss Robarts

[096-142]was to go at once to the big lady in the carriage.

[096-143]“I suppose it’s Lady Lufton,” said Mrs. Crawley.

[096-144]Lucy’s heart was so absolutely in her mouth that any kind of speech

[096-145]was at the moment impossible to her. Why should Lady Lufton have

[096-146]come thither to Hogglestock, and why should she want to see her,

[096-147]Lucy Robarts, in the carriage? Had not everything between them been

[096-148]settled? And yet—! Lucy, in the moment for thought that was allowed

[096-149]to her, could not determine what might be the probable upshot of such

[096-150]an interview. Her chief feeling was a desire to postpone it for the

[096-151]present instant. But the red-armed little girl would not allow that.

[096-152]“You are to come at once,” said she.

[096-153]And then Lucy, without having spoken a word, got up and left the

[096-154]room. She walked downstairs, along the little passage, and out

[096-155]through the small garden, with firm steps, but hardly knowing whither

[096-156]she went, or why. Her presence of mind and self-possession had all

[096-157]deserted her. She knew that she was unable to speak as she should do;

[096-158]she felt that she would have to regret her present behaviour, but yet

[096-159]she could not help herself. Why should Lady Lufton have come to her

[096-160]there? She went on, and the big footman stood with the carriage door

[096-161]open. She stepped up almost unconsciously, and, without knowing how

[096-162]she got there, she found herself seated by Lady Lufton.

[096-163]To tell the truth her ladyship also was a little at a loss to know

[096-164]how she was to carry through her present plan of operations. The duty

[096-165]of beginning, however, was clearly with her, and therefore, having

[096-166]taken Lucy by the hand, she spoke.

[096-167]“Miss Robarts,” she said, “my son has come home. I don’t know whether

[096-168]you are aware of it.”

[096-169]She spoke with a low, gentle voice, not quite like herself, but Lucy

[096-170]was much too confused to notice this.

[096-171]“I was not aware of it,” said Lucy.

[096-172]She had, however, been so informed in Fanny’s letter, but all that

[096-173]had gone out of her head.

[096-174]“Yes; he has come back. He has been in Norway, you know,—fishing.”

[096-175]“Yes,” said Lucy.

[096-176]“I am sure you will remember all that took place when you came to me,

[096-177]not long ago, in my little room upstairs at Framley Court.”

[096-178]In answer to which, Lucy, quivering in every nerve, and wrongly

[096-179]thinking that she was visibly shaking in every limb, timidly answered

[096-180]that she did remember. Why was it that she had then been so bold, and

[096-181]now was so poor a coward?

[096-182]“Well, my dear, all that I said to you then I said to you thinking

[096-183]that it was for the best. You, at any rate, will not be angry with me

[096-184]for loving my own son better than I love any one else.”
