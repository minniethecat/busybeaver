# Mark Robarts

The **Rev. Mark Robarts** is the vicar of [[framley-parsonage|Framley]] and one of the central protagonists of Anthony Trollope's _Framley Parsonage_. A young clergyman of twenty-five when the novel opens, Robarts is a man of good intentions, natural cheerfulness, and social ambition whose susceptibility to the allurements of [[chaldicotes|Chaldicotes]] society and the influence of [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]] leads him into grave financial peril. His moral journey from complacent respectability through crisis to redemption forms the novel's core narrative arc.

## Role in the Novel

Mark Robarts occupies the structural centre of _Framley Parsonage_. He is introduced as the fortunate beneficiary of [[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]'s patronage — she secured him the [[living]] of Framley, a comfortable ecclesiastical post that should have guaranteed him security and status (PN 001-197). His position places him at the intersection of the novel's key social spheres: the county aristocracy represented by Lady Lufton and [[framley-court|Framley Court]], the political-masculine world of [[chaldicotes|Chaldicotes]] and [[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]], and the domestic sphere of his wife Fanny and their children.

> "When young Mark Robarts was leaving college, his father had died, and he had been taken by the hand, at his earliest outset in life, by a certain old clergyman" (PN 001-063)

Robarts's narrative trajectory follows a classic rise-and-fall-and-redemption pattern. The early chapters establish his good fortune and general likeability (PN 001-127, 001-185). His visit to Chaldicotes (PN 003-098) initiates the central crisis: drawn into debt by Sowerby and the expensive society of the Chaldicotes set, Robarts signs bills that he cannot honour, threatening his livelihood, his family's security, and his standing with Lady Lufton.

> "Such was Mark Robarts when at the age of twenty-five he was left with a living and a wife" (PN 002-006)

The crisis reaches its peak when Robarts faces the consequences of his financial recklessness — a moment of moral reckoning that tests both his character and his relationships. His eventual rescue, facilitated in part by Lady Lufton's intervention, brings the novel to its resolution without diminishing the seriousness of the lesson learned.

## Character Traits

Mark Robarts is portrayed as fundamentally decent but morally unformed at the novel's opening. His defining traits include:

- **Amiability and good nature**: Robarts is consistently described as pleasant, cheerful, and well-liked — "such a one as Mark Robarts" (PN 001-127). His social ease makes him welcome in diverse company, from Lady Lufton's drawing-room to the Chaldicotes dinner table.

- **Social ambition**: Robarts's desire to move in circles above his station is his tragic flaw. He is flattered by the attention of men like Sowerby and Fothergill, and "those of young Mark Robarts ran in the same direction" — toward expense and social display (PN 001-132).

- **Moral weakness under pressure**: When confronted with Sowerby's schemes, Robarts lacks the strength of character to refuse. He knows better but cannot act on his knowledge — "'Twas thus that Mark Robarts argued when he found himself" entangled (PN 005-105).

- **Capacity for growth**: Robarts is not static; his suffering produces genuine moral development. The novel traces his movement from boyish irresponsibility toward a more sober understanding of duty.

- **Professional competence**: Despite his personal failings, Robarts is shown to be a conscientious clergyman — his concern when "absolution was being read, and Mark Robarts felt" the weight of his office (PN 015-047) demonstrates that his vocation is not merely a source of income.

| Quality | Early Novel | Late Novel |
|---------|------------|------------|
| Social judgment | Naive, easily flattered | Wiser, more discerning |
| Financial prudence | Reckless, incurring debt | Chastened, responsible |
| Moral self-awareness | Complacent, self-justifying | Humbled, self-critical |
| Professional identity | Livelihood-focused | Vocation-focused |

## Relationships

- **[[lady-lufton|Lady Lufton]]**: Robarts's patroness and the dominant figure in his professional life. She secured him the Framley living and regards him as her _protégé_ (PN 001-197). Their relationship is structured by the obligations of patronage — her expectation of deference, his growing resentment of her control. The crisis of the novel is in part a crisis in this relationship.

- **[[fanny-robarts|Fanny Robarts]]**: Robarts's wife, who represents the domestic stability he risks. Her loyalty during his disgrace is one of the novel's moral anchors. Their marriage, portrayed with warmth and realism, grounds Robarts's character.

- **[[sowerby|Mr. Sowerby]]**: The Mephistophelean figure who leads Robarts into temptation. Sowerby's worldly charm and moral unscrupulousness make him a foil to Robarts's essential decency (PN 006-099).

- **[[lord-lufton|Lord Lufton]]**: Lady Lufton's son and Robarts's friend from youth. Their friendship complicates Robarts's relationship with Lady Lufton and places him between mother and son in their conflicts.

- **[[lucy-robarts|Lucy Robarts]]**: Mark's sister, whose own romance with Lord Lufton becomes a parallel plot to Mark's financial troubles. Her moral strength contrasts with Mark's weakness.

## Key Scenes

- **The Living of Framley (Ch. 1)**: Lady Lufton's patronage and Robarts's installation as vicar establish the novel's central social dynamic (PN 001-197).

- **The Visit to Chaldicotes (Ch. 3–7)**: Robarts's fateful visit to the Chaldicotes set, where Sowerby begins his financial manipulation. "Mark Robarts was about to pay a visit to Chaldicotes" (PN 003-098). The episode includes the crucial bill-signing that will haunt the remainder of the novel.

- **The Bill Comes Due (Ch. 15)**: The moment of reckoning when Robarts must confront the consequences of his actions. The spiritual dimension of his crisis is captured in the image of absolution being read (PN 015-047).

- **Lady Lufton's Intervention**: The climax of the patronage relationship — Lady Lufton's decision to rescue Robarts despite her disappointment demonstrates both the power and the limits of the patronage system.

## Social & Historical Context

Mark Robarts embodies the position of the early-Victorian rural clergyman — a figure caught between the old ideal of the clergyman as spiritual shepherd and the newer reality of the clergyman as gentleman and social actor. The 1830s and 1840s saw significant reform of the Church of England, including the Pluralities Act of 1838 and the Ecclesiastical Commissioners Act of 1836, which sought to professionalize the clergy and curb abuses like pluralism and non-residence.

Robarts's financial vulnerability reflects a genuine historical problem: many rural livings provided incomes inadequate for the social expectations placed on clergymen, yet the alternative — seeking preferment through social connection — exposed men to exactly the kind of temptation Robarts faces. Trollope, himself a postal official who knew the precariousness of respectable employment, portrays this dilemma with sympathetic precision.

The patronage relationship between Lady Lufton and Robarts mirrors the real structure of ecclesiastical preferment in the period. A significant proportion of Church livings were in the gift of private patrons — aristocrats, gentry, and Oxford and Cambridge colleges — making personal relationships, rather than merit or seniority, the primary mechanism of clerical advancement.
