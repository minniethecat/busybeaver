## Section 23

[023-001]Lord Lufton, though he had never before spoken to Miss Robarts, had

[023-002]already found out that she was by no means plain. Though he had

[023-003]hardly seen her except at church, he had already made himself certain

[023-004]that the owner of that face must be worth knowing, and was not sorry

[023-005]to have the present opportunity of speaking to her. “So you have

[023-006]an unknown damsel shut up in your castle,” he had once said to Mrs.

[023-007]Robarts. “If she be kept a prisoner much longer, I shall find it my

[023-008]duty to come and release her by force of arms.” He had been there

[023-009]twice with the object of seeing her, but on both occasions Lucy had

[023-010]managed to escape. Now we may say she was fairly caught, and Lord

[023-011]Lufton, taking a pair of pheasants from the gamekeeper, and swinging

[023-012]them over his shoulder, walked off with his prey.

[023-013][Illustration: Lord Lufton and Lucy Robarts.]

[023-014]“You have been here a long time,” he said, “without our having had

[023-015]the pleasure of seeing you.”

[023-016]“Yes, my lord,” said Lucy. Lords had not been frequent among her

[023-017]acquaintance hitherto.

[023-018]“I tell Mrs. Robarts that she has been confining you illegally, and

[023-019]that we shall release you by force or stratagem.”

[023-020]“I—I—I have had a great sorrow lately.”

[023-021]“Yes, Miss Robarts; I know you have; and I am only joking, you know.

[023-022]But I do hope that now you will be able to come amongst us. My mother

[023-023]is so anxious that you should do so.”

[023-024]“I am sure she is very kind, and you also—my lord.”

[023-025]“I never knew my own father,” said Lord Lufton, speaking gravely.

[023-026]“But I can well understand what a loss you have had.” And then, after

[023-027]pausing a moment, he continued, “I remember Dr. Robarts well.”

[023-028]“Do you, indeed?” said Lucy, turning sharply towards him, and

[023-029]speaking now with some animation in her voice. Nobody had yet spoken

[023-030]to her about her father since she had been at Framley. It had been as

[023-031]though the subject were a forbidden one. And how frequently is this

[023-032]the case! When those we love are dead, our friends dread to mention

[023-033]them, though to us who are bereaved no subject would be so pleasant

[023-034]as their names. But we rarely understand how to treat our own sorrow

[023-035]or those of others.

[023-036]There was once a people in some land—and they may be still there

[023-037]for what I know—who thought it sacrilegious to stay the course of a

[023-038]raging fire. If a house were being burned, burn it must, even though

[023-039]there were facilities for saving it. For who would dare to interfere

[023-040]with the course of the god? Our idea of sorrow is much the same. We

[023-041]think it wicked, or at any rate heartless, to put it out. If a man’s

[023-042]wife be dead, he should go about lugubrious, with long face, for at

[023-043]least two years, or perhaps with full length for eighteen months,

[023-044]decreasing gradually during the other six. If he be a man who can

[023-045]quench his sorrow—put out his fire as it were—in less time than

[023-046]that, let him at any rate not show his power!

[023-047]“Yes: I remember him,” continued Lord Lufton. “He came twice to

[023-048]Framley while I was a boy, consulting with my mother about Mark and

[023-049]myself,—whether the Eton floggings were not more efficacious than

[023-050]those at Harrow. He was very kind to me, foreboding all manner of

[023-051]good things on my behalf.”

[023-052]“He was very kind to every one,” said Lucy.

[023-053]“I should think he would have been—a kind, good, genial man—just

[023-054]the man to be adored by his own family.”

[023-055]“Exactly; and so he was. I do not remember that I ever heard an

[023-056]unkind word from him. There was not a harsh tone in his voice. And

[023-057]he was generous as the day.” Lucy, we have said, was not generally

[023-058]demonstrative, but now, on this subject, and with this absolute

[023-059]stranger, she became almost eloquent.

[023-060]“I do not wonder that you should feel his loss, Miss Robarts.”

[023-061]“Oh, I do feel it. Mark is the best of brothers, and, as for Fanny,

[023-062]she is too kind and too good to me. But I had always been specially

[023-063]my father’s friend. For the last year or two we had lived so much

[023-064]together!”

[023-065]“He was an old man when he died, was he not?”

[023-066]“Just seventy, my lord.”

[023-067]“Ah, then he was old. My mother is only fifty, and we sometimes call

[023-068]her the old woman. Do you think she looks older than that? We all say

[023-069]that she makes herself out to be so much more ancient than she need

[023-070]do.”

[023-071]“Lady Lufton does not dress young.”

[023-072]“That is it. She never has, in my memory. She always used to wear

[023-073]black when I first recollect her. She has given that up now; but she

[023-074]is still very sombre; is she not?”

[023-075]“I do not like ladies to dress very young, that is, ladies of—of—”

[023-076]“Ladies of fifty, we will say?”

[023-077]“Very well; ladies of fifty, if you like it.”

[023-078]“Then I am sure you will like my mother.”

[023-079]They had now turned up through the parsonage wicket, a little gate

[023-080]that opened into the garden at a point on the road nearer than the

[023-081]chief entrance.

[023-082]“I suppose I shall find Mark up at the house?” said he.

[023-083]“I daresay you will, my lord.”

[023-084]“Well, I’ll go round this way, for my business is partly in the

[023-085]stable. You see I am quite at home here, though you never have seen

[023-086]me before. But, Miss Robarts, now that the ice is broken, I hope that

[023-087]we may be friends.” He then put out his hand, and when she gave him

[023-088]hers he pressed it almost as an old friend might have done.

[023-089]And, indeed, Lucy had talked to him almost as though he were an old

[023-090]friend. For a minute or two she had forgotten that he was a lord and

[023-091]a stranger—had forgotten also to be stiff and guarded as was her

[023-092]wont. Lord Lufton had spoken to her as though he had really cared to

[023-093]know her; and she, unconsciously, had been taken by the compliment.

[023-094]Lord Lufton, indeed, had not thought much about it—excepting as

[023-095]thus, that he liked the glance of a pair of bright eyes, as most

[023-096]other young men do like it. But, on this occasion, the evening had

[023-097]been so dark, that he had hardly seen Lucy’s eyes at all.

[023-098]“Well, Lucy, I hope you liked your companion,” Mrs. Robarts said,

[023-099]as the three of them clustered round the drawing-room fire before

[023-100]dinner.

[023-101]“Oh, yes; pretty well,” said Lucy.

[023-102]“That is not at all complimentary to his lordship.”

[023-103]“I did not mean to be complimentary, Fanny.”

[023-104]“Lucy is a great deal too matter-of-fact for compliments,” said Mark.

[023-105]“What I meant was, that I had no great opportunity for judging,

[023-106]seeing that I was only with Lord Lufton for about ten minutes.”

[023-107]“Ah! but there are girls here who would give their eyes for ten

[023-108]minutes of Lord Lufton to themselves. You do not know how he’s

[023-109]valued. He has the character of being always able to make himself

[023-110]agreeable to ladies at half a minute’s warning.”

[023-111]“Perhaps he had not the half-minute’s warning in this case,” said

[023-112]Lucy,—hypocrite that she was.

[023-113]“Poor Lucy,” said her brother; “he was coming up to see Ponto’s

[023-114]shoulder, and I am afraid he was thinking more about the dog than

[023-115]you.”

[023-116]“Very likely,” said Lucy; and then they went in to dinner.

[023-117]Lucy had been a hypocrite, for she had confessed to herself, while

[023-118]dressing, that Lord Lufton had been very pleasant; but then it is

[023-119]allowed to young ladies to be hypocrites when the subject under

[023-120]discussion is the character of a young gentleman.

[023-121]Soon after that, Lucy did dine at Framley Court. Captain Culpepper,

[023-122]in spite of his enormity with reference to Gatherum Castle, was still

[023-123]staying there, as was also a clergyman from the neighbourhood of

[023-124]Barchester with his wife and daughter. This was Archdeacon Grantly,

[023-125]a gentleman whom we have mentioned before, and who was as well known

[023-126]in the diocese as the bishop himself,—and more thought about by many

[023-127]clergymen than even that illustrious prelate.

[023-128]Miss Grantly was a young lady not much older than Lucy Robarts,

[023-129]and she also was quiet, and not given to much talking in open

[023-130]company. She was decidedly a beauty, but somewhat statuesque in her

[023-131]loveliness. Her forehead was high and white, but perhaps too like

[023-132]marble to gratify the taste of those who are fond of flesh and blood.

[023-133]Her eyes were large and exquisitely formed, but they seldom showed

[023-134]much emotion. She, indeed, was impassive herself, and betrayed but

[023-135]little of her feelings. Her nose was nearly Grecian, not coming

[023-136]absolutely in a straight line from her forehead, but doing so nearly

[023-137]enough to entitle it to be considered as classical. Her mouth, too,

[023-138]was very fine—artists, at least, said so, and connoisseurs in

[023-139]beauty; but to me she always seemed as though she wanted fulness of

[023-140]lip. But the exquisite symmetry of her cheek and chin and lower face

[023-141]no man could deny. Her hair was light, and being always dressed with

[023-142]considerable care, did not detract from her appearance; but it lacked

[023-143]that richness which gives such luxuriance to feminine loveliness. She

[023-144]was tall and slight, and very graceful in her movements; but there

[023-145]were those who thought that she wanted the ease and _abandon_ of

[023-146]youth. They said that she was too composed and stiff for her age, and

[023-147]that she gave but little to society beyond the beauty of her form and

[023-148]face.

[023-149]There can be no doubt, however, that she was considered by most men

[023-150]and women to be the beauty of Barsetshire, and that gentlemen from

[023-151]neighbouring counties would come many miles through dirty roads on

[023-152]the mere hope of being able to dance with her. Whatever attractions

[023-153]she may have lacked, she had at any rate created for herself a great

[023-154]reputation. She had spent two months of the last spring in London,

[023-155]and even there she had made a sensation; and people had said that

[023-156]Lord Dumbello, Lady Hartletop’s eldest son, had been peculiarly

[023-157]struck with her.

[023-158]It may be imagined that the archdeacon was proud of her, and so

[023-159]indeed was Mrs. Grantly—more proud, perhaps, of her daughter’s

[023-160]beauty, than so excellent a woman should have allowed herself to be

[023-161]of such an attribute. Griselda—that was her name—was now an only

[023-162]daughter. One sister she had had, but that sister had died. There

[023-163]were two brothers also left, one in the Church and the other in the

[023-164]army. That was the extent of the archdeacon’s family, and as the

[023-165]archdeacon was a very rich man—he was the only child of his father,

[023-166]who had been Bishop of Barchester for a great many years; and

[023-167]in those years it had been worth a man’s while to be Bishop of

[023-168]Barchester—it was supposed that Miss Grantly would have a large

[023-169]fortune. Mrs. Grantly, however, had been heard to say, that she was

[023-170]in no hurry to see her daughter established in the world;—ordinary

[023-171]young ladies are merely married, but those of real importance are

[023-172]established:—and this, if anything, added to the value of the prize.

[023-173]Mothers sometimes depreciate their wares by an undue solicitude to

[023-174]dispose of them.

[023-175]But to tell the truth openly and at once—a virtue for which a

[023-176]novelist does not receive very much commendation—Griselda Grantly

[023-177]was, to a certain extent, already given away. Not that she, Griselda,

[023-178]knew anything about it, or that the thrice happy gentleman had been

[023-179]made aware of his good fortune; nor even had the archdeacon been

[023-180]told. But Mrs. Grantly and Lady Lufton had been closeted together

[023-181]more than once, and terms had been signed and sealed between them.

[023-182]Not signed on parchment, and sealed with wax, as is the case with

[023-183]treaties made by kings and diplomats,—to be broken by the same; but

[023-184]signed with little words, and sealed with certain pressings of the

[023-185]hand,—a treaty which between two such contracting parties would be

[023-186]binding enough. And by the terms of this treaty Griselda Grantly was

[023-187]to become Lady Lufton.
