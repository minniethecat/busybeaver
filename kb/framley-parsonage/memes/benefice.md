# Benefice

A benefice was a Church of England ecclesiastical office to which property and income were permanently attached. The holder (incumbent) enjoyed the benefice's revenues for life, subject to performing the spiritual duties of the parish. In the world of Trollope's Barsetshire novels, a good benefice was the goal of every clerical career — the difference between a life of genteel comfort and one of obscure poverty.

## Definition

A benefice consisted of the spiritual office (the cure of souls) combined with temporal endowments — the parsonage house, glebe lands, and tithe rents. Unlike a salary, the benefice income belonged to the incumbent by right, not by favor — a crucial distinction that gave clergymen a measure of independence from their patrons. The value of benefices varied enormously: some were worth thousands of pounds per year and attracted ambitious men, while others provided barely enough to support a single clergyman.

## In the Novel

The Framley living is the benefice at the heart of the novel, "worth £900 a year" (001-118) — a comfortable income that places Mark Robarts firmly among the gentry. His enjoyment of this benefice is complicated by his awareness that Lady Lufton, as patron, expects his gratitude to translate into deference. Other benefices mentioned in the novel serve as markers of ambition and disappointment: each represents a potential step up or down in the clerical hierarchy, and the "poor piece of preferment" that falls to the dean (031-107) reminds readers that even apparently desirable benefices could prove hollow.

## Historical Context

The medieval benefice system was reformed but not abolished by the Victorians. The Ecclesiastical Commissioners Act of 1836 began redistributing church wealth from wealthy bishoprics and cathedral chapters to poorer parishes, but the fundamental structure — private patronage, life tenure, property-based income — remained intact. This persistence of a pre-modern institution within a rapidly modernizing society created the tensions that Trollope exploits for both comedy and drama.
