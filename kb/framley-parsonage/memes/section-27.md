## Section 27

[027-001]And then he put away Sowerby’s letter carefully, locking it up from

[027-002]his wife’s sight. It was a letter that no parish clergyman should

[027-003]have received. So much he acknowledged to himself. But nevertheless

[027-004]it was necessary that he should keep it. And now again for a few

[027-005]hours this affair made him very miserable.

[027-006]CHAPTER XIII.

[027-007]DELICATE HINTS.

[027-008]Lady Lufton had been greatly rejoiced at that good deed which her son

[027-009]did in giving up his Leicestershire hunting, and coming to reside for

[027-010]the winter at Framley. It was proper, and becoming, and comfortable

[027-011]in the extreme. An English nobleman ought to hunt in the county where

[027-012]he himself owns the fields over which he rides; he ought to receive

[027-013]the respect and honour due to him from his own tenants; he ought to

[027-014]sleep under a roof of his own, and he ought also—so Lady Lufton

[027-015]thought—to fall in love with a young embryo bride of his own

[027-016]mother’s choosing.

[027-017]And then it was so pleasant to have him there in the house. Lady

[027-018]Lufton was not a woman who allowed her life to be what people in

[027-019]common parlance call dull. She had too many duties, and thought too

[027-020]much of them, to allow of her suffering from tedium and _ennui_.

[027-021]But nevertheless the house was more joyous to her when he was there.

[027-022]There was a reason for some little gaiety, which would never have

[027-023]been attracted thither by herself, but which, nevertheless, she did

[027-024]enjoy when it was brought about by his presence. She was younger and

[027-025]brighter when he was there, thinking more of the future and less of

[027-026]the past. She could look at him, and that alone was happiness to her.

[027-027]And then he was pleasant-mannered with her; joking with her on her

[027-028]little old-world prejudices in a tone that was musical to her ear as

[027-029]coming from him; smiling on her, reminding her of those smiles which

[027-030]she had loved so dearly when as yet he was all her own, lying there

[027-031]in his little bed beside her chair. He was kind and gracious to her,

[027-032]behaving like a good son, at any rate while he was there in her

[027-033]presence. When we add, to this, her fears that he might not be so

[027-034]perfect in his conduct when absent, we may well imagine that Lady

[027-035]Lufton was pleased to have him there at Framley Court.

[027-036]She had hardly said a word to him as to that five thousand pounds.

[027-037]Many a night, as she lay thinking on her pillow, she said to herself

[027-038]that no money had ever been better expended, since it had brought him

[027-039]back to his own house. He had thanked her for it in his own open way,

[027-040]declaring that he would pay it back to her during the coming year,

[027-041]and comforting her heart by his rejoicing that the property had not

[027-042]been sold.

[027-043]“I don’t like the idea of parting with an acre of it,” he had said.

[027-044]“Of course not, Ludovic. Never let the estate decrease in your hands.

[027-045]It is only by such resolutions as that that English noblemen and

[027-046]English gentlemen can preserve their country. I cannot bear to see

[027-047]property changing hands.”

[027-048]“Well, I suppose it’s a good thing to have land in the market

[027-049]sometimes, so that the millionnaires may know what to do with their

[027-050]money.”

[027-051]“God forbid that yours should be there!” And the widow made a little

[027-052]mental prayer that her son’s acres might be protected from the

[027-053]millionnaires and other Philistines.

[027-054]“Why, yes: I don’t exactly want to see a Jew tailor investing his

[027-055]earnings at Lufton,” said the lord.

[027-056]“Heaven forbid!” said the widow.

[027-057]All this, as I have said, was very nice. It was manifest to her

[027-058]ladyship, from his lordship’s way of talking, that no vital injury

[027-059]had as yet been done: he had no cares on his mind, and spoke freely

[027-060]about the property: but nevertheless there were clouds even now, at

[027-061]this period of bliss, which somewhat obscured the brilliancy of Lady

[027-062]Lufton’s sky. Why was Ludovic so slow in that affair of Griselda

[027-063]Grantly? why so often in these latter winter days did he saunter over

[027-064]to the parsonage? And then that terrible visit to Gatherum Castle!

[027-065]What actually did happen at Gatherum Castle, she never knew. We,

[027-066]however, are more intrusive, less delicate in our inquiries, and we

[027-067]can say. He had a very bad day’s sport with the West Barsetshire. The

[027-068]county is altogether short of foxes, and some one who understands

[027-069]the matter must take that point up before they can do any good. And

[027-070]after that he had had rather a dull dinner with the duke. Sowerby had

[027-071]been there, and in the evening he and Sowerby had played billiards.

[027-072]Sowerby had won a pound or two, and that had been the extent of the

[027-073]damage done.

[027-074]But those saunterings over to the parsonage might be more dangerous.

[027-075]Not that it ever occurred to Lady Lufton as possible that her son

[027-076]should fall in love with Lucy Robarts. Lucy’s personal attractions

[027-077]were not of a nature to give ground for such a fear as that. But he

[027-078]might turn the girl’s head with his chatter; she might be fool enough

[027-079]to fancy any folly; and, moreover, people would talk. Why should he

[027-080]go to the parsonage now more frequently than he had ever done before

[027-081]Lucy came there?

[027-082]And then her ladyship, in reference to the same trouble, hardly knew

[027-083]how to manage her invitations to the parsonage. These hitherto had

[027-084]been very frequent, and she had been in the habit of thinking that

[027-085]they could hardly be too much so; but now she was almost afraid

[027-086]to continue the custom. She could not ask the parson and his wife

[027-087]without Lucy; and when Lucy was there, her son would pass the greater

[027-088]part of the evening in talking to her, or playing chess with her. Now

[027-089]this did disturb Lady Lufton not a little.

[027-090]And then Lucy took it all so quietly. On her first arrival at Framley

[027-091]she had been so shy, so silent, and so much awestruck by the grandeur

[027-092]of Framley Court, that Lady Lufton had sympathized with her and

[027-093]encouraged her. She had endeavoured to moderate the blaze of her

[027-094]own splendour, in order that Lucy’s unaccustomed eyes might not be

[027-095]dazzled. But all this was changed now. Lucy could listen to the young

[027-096]lord’s voice by the hour together—without being dazzled in the

[027-097]least.

[027-098]Under these circumstances two things occurred to her. She would speak

[027-099]either to her son or to Fanny Robarts, and by a little diplomacy have

[027-100]this evil remedied. And then she had to determine on which step she

[027-101]would take.

[027-102]“Nothing could be more reasonable than Ludovic.” So at least she said

[027-103]to herself over and over again. But then Ludovic understood nothing

[027-104]about such matters; and he had, moreover, a habit, inherited from his

[027-105]father, of taking the bit between his teeth whenever he suspected

[027-106]interference. Drive him gently without pulling his mouth about, and

[027-107]you might take him anywhere, almost at any pace; but a smart touch,

[027-108]let it be ever so slight, would bring him on his haunches, and then

[027-109]it might be a question whether you could get him another mile that

[027-110]day. So that on the whole Lady Lufton thought that the other plan

[027-111]would be the best. I have no doubt that Lady Lufton was right.

[027-112]She got Fanny up into her own den one afternoon, and seated her

[027-113]discreetly in an easy arm-chair, making her guest take off her

[027-114]bonnet, and showing by various signs that the visit was regarded as

[027-115]one of great moment.

[027-116]“Fanny,” she said, “I want to speak to you about something that is

[027-117]important and necessary to mention, and yet it is a very delicate

[027-118]affair to speak of.” Fanny opened her eyes, and said that she hoped

[027-119]that nothing was wrong.

[027-120]“No, my dear, I think nothing is wrong: I hope so, and I think I may

[027-121]say I’m sure of it; but then it’s always well to be on one’s guard.”

[027-122]“Yes, it is,” said Fanny, who knew that something unpleasant was

[027-123]coming—something as to which she might probably be called upon to

[027-124]differ from her ladyship. Mrs. Robarts’ own fears, however, were

[027-125]running entirely in the direction of her husband;—and, indeed,

[027-126]Lady Lufton had a word or two to say on that subject also, only not

[027-127]exactly now. A hunting parson was not at all to her taste; but that

[027-128]matter might be allowed to remain in abeyance for a few days.

[027-129]“Now, Fanny, you know that we have all liked your sister-in-law,

[027-130]Lucy, very much.” And then Mrs. Robarts’ mind was immediately opened,

[027-131]and she knew the rest as well as though it had all been spoken. “I

[027-132]need hardly tell you that, for I am sure we have shown it.”

[027-133]“You have, indeed, as you always do.”

[027-134]“And you must not think that I am going to complain,” continued Lady

[027-135]Lufton.

[027-136]“I hope there is nothing to complain of,” said Fanny, speaking by

[027-137]no means in a defiant tone, but humbly as it were, and deprecating

[027-138]her ladyship’s wrath. Fanny had gained one signal victory over Lady

[027-139]Lufton, and on that account, with a prudence equal to her generosity,

[027-140]felt that she could afford to be submissive. It might, perhaps, not

[027-141]be long before she would be equally anxious to conquer again.

[027-142]“Well, no; I don’t think there is,” said Lady Lufton. “Nothing to

[027-143]complain of; but a little chat between you and me may, perhaps, set

[027-144]matters right, which, otherwise, might become troublesome.”

[027-145]“Is it about Lucy?”

[027-146]“Yes, my dear—about Lucy. She is a very nice, good girl, and a

[027-147]credit to her father—”

[027-148]“And a great comfort to us,” said Fanny.

[027-149]“I am sure she is: she must be a very pleasant companion to you, and

[027-150]so useful about the children; but—” And then Lady Lufton paused for

[027-151]a moment; for she, eloquent and discreet as she always was, felt

[027-152]herself rather at a loss for words to express her exact meaning.

[027-153]“I don’t know what I should do without her,” said Fanny, speaking

[027-154]with the object of assisting her ladyship in her embarrassment.

[027-155]“But the truth is this: she and Lord Lufton are getting into the way

[027-156]of being too much together—of talking to each other too exclusively.

[027-157]I am sure you must have noticed it, Fanny. It is not that I suspect

[027-158]any evil. I don’t think that I am suspicious by nature.”

[027-159]“Oh! no,” said Fanny.

[027-160]“But they will each of them get wrong ideas about the other, and

[027-161]about themselves. Lucy will, perhaps, think that Ludovic means more

[027-162]than he does, and Ludovic will—” But it was not quite so easy to say

[027-163]what Ludovic might do or think; but Lady Lufton went on: “I am sure

[027-164]that you understand me, Fanny, with your excellent sense and tact.

[027-165]Lucy is clever, and amusing, and all that; and Ludovic, like all

[027-166]young men, is perhaps ignorant that his attentions may be taken to

[027-167]mean more than he intends—”

[027-168]“You don’t think that Lucy is in love with him?”

[027-169]“Oh dear, no—nothing of the kind. If I thought it had come to that,

[027-170]I should recommend that she should be sent away altogether. I am sure

[027-171]she is not so foolish as that.”

[027-172]“I don’t think there is anything in it at all, Lady Lufton.”

[027-173]“I don’t think there is, my dear, and therefore I would not for

[027-174]worlds make any suggestion about it to Lord Lufton. I would not let

[027-175]him suppose that I suspected Lucy of being so imprudent. But still,

[027-176]it may be well that you should just say a word to her. A little

[027-177]management now and then, in such matters, is so useful.”

[027-178]“But what shall I say to her?”
