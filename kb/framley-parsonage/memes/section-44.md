## Section 44

[044-001]“Have you had a pleasant evening?” Lady Lufton said, when she and

[044-002]Griselda were seated together with their feet on the fender of her

[044-003]ladyship’s dressing-room. Lady Lufton had especially invited her

[044-004]guest into this, her most private sanctum, to which as a rule none

[044-005]had admittance but her daughter, and sometimes Fanny Robarts. But

[044-006]to what sanctum might not such a daughter-in-law as Griselda have

[044-007]admittance?

[044-008]“Oh, yes—very,” said Griselda.

[044-009]“It seemed to me that you bestowed most of your smiles upon Ludovic.”

[044-010]And Lady Lufton put on a look of good pleasure that such should have

[044-011]been the case.

[044-012]“Oh! I don’t know,” said Griselda: “I did dance with him two or three

[044-013]times.”

[044-014]“Not once too often to please me, my dear. I like to see Ludovic

[044-015]dancing with my friends.”

[044-016]“I am sure I am very much obliged to you, Lady Lufton.”

[044-017]“Not at all, my dear. I don’t know where he could get so nice a

[044-018]partner.” And then she paused a moment, not feeling how far she might

[044-019]go. In the meantime Griselda sat still, staring at the hot coals.

[044-020]“Indeed, I know that he admires you very much,” continued Lady

[044-021]Lufton.

[044-022]“Oh! no, I am sure he doesn’t,” said Griselda; and then there was

[044-023]another pause.

[044-024]“I can only say this,” said Lady Lufton, “that if he does do so—and

[044-025]I believe he does—it would give me very great pleasure. For you

[044-026]know, my dear, that I am very fond of you myself.”

[044-027]“Oh! thank you,” said Griselda, and stared at the coals more

[044-028]perseveringly than before.

[044-029]“He is a young man of a most excellent disposition—though he is my

[044-030]own son, I will say that—and if there should be anything between you

[044-031]and him—”

[044-032]“There isn’t, indeed, Lady Lufton.”

[044-033]“But if there ever should be, I should be delighted to think that

[044-034]Ludovic had made so good a choice.”

[044-035]“But there will never be anything of the sort, I’m sure, Lady Lufton.

[044-036]He is not thinking of such a thing in the least.”

[044-037]“Well, perhaps he may, some day. And now, good-night, my dear.”

[044-038]“Good-night, Lady Lufton.” And Griselda kissed her with the utmost

[044-039]composure, and betook herself to her own bedroom. Before she retired

[044-040]to sleep she looked carefully to her different articles of dress,

[044-041]discovering what amount of damage the evening’s wear and tear might

[044-042]have inflicted.

[044-043]CHAPTER XXI.

[044-044]WHY PUCK, THE PONY, WAS BEATEN.

[044-045]Mark Robarts returned home the day after the scene at the Albany,

[044-046]considerably relieved in spirit. He now felt that he might accept

[044-047]the stall without discredit to himself as a clergyman in doing so.

[044-048]Indeed, after what Mr. Sowerby had said, and after Lord Lufton’s

[044-049]assent to it, it would have been madness, he considered, to decline

[044-050]it. And then, too, Mr. Sowerby’s promise about the bills was very

[044-051]comfortable to him. After all, might it not be possible that he might

[044-052]get rid of all these troubles with no other drawback than that of

[044-053]having to pay £130 for a horse that was well worth the money?

[044-054]On the day after his return he received proper authentic tidings of

[044-055]his presentation to the prebend. He was, in fact, already prebendary,

[044-056]or would be as soon as the dean and chapter had gone through the form

[044-057]of instituting him in his stall. The income was already his own; and

[044-058]the house also would be given up to him in a week’s time—a part of

[044-059]the arrangement with which he would most willingly have dispensed had

[044-060]it been at all possible to do so. His wife congratulated him nicely,

[044-061]with open affection, and apparent satisfaction at the arrangement.

[044-062]The enjoyment of one’s own happiness at such windfalls depends so

[044-063]much on the free and freely expressed enjoyment of others! Lady

[044-064]Lufton’s congratulations had nearly made him throw up the whole

[044-065]thing; but his wife’s smiles re-encouraged him; and Lucy’s warm and

[044-066]eager joy made him feel quite delighted with Mr. Sowerby and the Duke

[044-067]of Omnium. And then that splendid animal, Dandy, came home to the

[044-068]parsonage stables, much to the delight of the groom and gardener,

[044-069]and of the assistant stable boy who had been allowed to creep into

[044-070]the establishment, unawares as it were, since “master” had taken so

[044-071]keenly to hunting. But this satisfaction was not shared in the

[044-072]drawing-room. The horse was seen on his first journey round to the

[044-073]stable gate, and questions were immediately asked. It was a horse,

[044-074]Mark said, “which he had bought from Mr. Sowerby some little time

[044-075]since with the object of obliging him. He, Mark, intended to sell him

[044-076]again, as soon as he could do so judiciously.” This, as I have said

[044-077]above, was not satisfactory. Neither of the two ladies at Framley

[044-078]Parsonage knew much about horses, or of the manner in which one

[044-079]gentleman might think it proper to oblige another by purchasing the

[044-080]superfluities of his stable; but they did both feel that there were

[044-081]horses enough in the parsonage stable without Dandy, and that the

[044-082]purchasing of a hunter with the view of immediately selling him

[044-083]again, was, to say the least of it, an operation hardly congenial

[044-084]with the usual tastes and pursuits of a clergyman.

[044-085]“I hope you did not give very much money for him, Mark,” said Fanny.

[044-086]“Not more than I shall get again,” said Mark; and Fanny saw from the

[044-087]form of his countenance that she had better not pursue the subject

[044-088]any further at that moment.

[044-089]“I suppose I shall have to go into residence almost immediately,”

[044-090]said Mark, recurring to the more agreeable subject of the stall.

[044-091]“And shall we all have to go and live at Barchester at once?” asked

[044-092]Lucy.

[044-093]“The house will not be furnished, will it, Mark?” said his wife. “I

[044-094]don’t know how we shall get on.”

[044-095]“Don’t frighten yourselves. I shall take lodgings in Barchester.”

[044-096]“And we shall not see you all the time,” said Mrs. Robarts with

[044-097]dismay. But the prebendary explained that he would be backwards and

[044-098]forwards at Framley every week, and that in all probability he would

[044-099]only sleep at Barchester on the Saturdays and Sundays—and, perhaps,

[044-100]not always then.

[044-101]“It does not seem very hard work, that of a prebendary,” said Lucy.

[044-102]“But it is very dignified,” said Fanny. “Prebendaries are dignitaries

[044-103]of the Church—are they not, Mark?”

[044-104]“Decidedly,” said he; “and their wives also, by special canon law.

[044-105]The worst of it is that both of them are obliged to wear wigs.”

[044-106]“Shall you have a hat, Mark, with curly things at the side, and

[044-107]strings through to hold them up?” asked Lucy.

[044-108]“I fear that does not come within my perquisites.”

[044-109]“Nor a rosette? Then I shall never believe that you are a dignitary.

[044-110]Do you mean to say that you will wear a hat like a common

[044-111]parson—like Mr. Crawley, for instance?”

[044-112]“Well—I believe I may give a twist to the leaf; but I am by no means

[044-113]sure till I shall have consulted the dean in chapter.”

[044-114]And thus at the parsonage they talked over the good things that were

[044-115]coming to them, and endeavoured to forget the new horse, and the

[044-116]hunting boots that had been used so often during the last winter, and

[044-117]Lady Lufton’s altered countenance. It might be that the evils would

[044-118]vanish away, and the good things alone remain to them.

[044-119]It was now the month of April, and the fields were beginning to look

[044-120]green, and the wind had got itself out of the east and was soft

[044-121]and genial, and the early spring flowers were showing their bright

[044-122]colours in the parsonage garden, and all things were sweet and

[044-123]pleasant. This was a period of the year that was usually dear to Mrs.

[044-124]Robarts. Her husband was always a better parson when the warm months

[044-125]came than he had been during the winter. The distant county friends

[044-126]whom she did not know and of whom she did not approve went away when

[044-127]the spring came, leaving their houses innocent and empty. The parish

[044-128]duty was better attended to, and perhaps domestic duties also. At

[044-129]such period he was a pattern parson and a pattern husband, atoning to

[044-130]his own conscience for past shortcomings by present zeal. And then,

[044-131]though she had never acknowledged it to herself, the absence of her

[044-132]dear friend Lady Lufton was perhaps in itself not disagreeable. Mrs.

[044-133]Robarts did love Lady Lufton heartily; but it must be acknowledged of

[044-134]her ladyship, that, with all her good qualities, she was inclined to

[044-135]be masterful. She liked to rule, and she made people feel that she

[044-136]liked it. Mrs. Robarts would never have confessed that she laboured

[044-137]under a sense of thraldom; but perhaps she was mouse enough to enjoy

[044-138]the temporary absence of her kind-hearted cat. When Lady Lufton was

[044-139]away Mrs. Robarts herself had more play in the parish.

[044-140]And Mark also was not unhappy, though he did not find it practicable

[044-141]immediately to turn Dandy into money. Indeed, just at this moment,

[044-142]when he was a good deal over at Barchester, going through those deep

[044-143]mysteries and rigid ecclesiastical examinations which are necessary

[044-144]before a clergyman can become one of a chapter, Dandy was rather a

[044-145]thorn in his side. Those wretched bills were to come due early in

[044-146]May, and before the end of April Sowerby wrote to him saying that he

[044-147]was doing his utmost to provide for the evil day; but that if the

[044-148]price of Dandy could be remitted to him _at once_, it would greatly

[044-149]facilitate his object. Nothing could be more different than Mr.

[044-150]Sowerby’s tone about money at different times. When he wanted to

[044-151]raise the wind, everything was so important; haste and superhuman

[044-152]efforts, and men running to and fro with blank acceptances in their

[044-153]hands, could alone stave off the crack of doom; but at other times,

[044-154]when retaliatory applications were made to him, he could prove with

[044-155]the easiest voice and most jaunty manner that everything was quite

[044-156]serene. Now, at this period, he was in that mood of superhuman

[044-157]efforts, and he called loudly for the hundred and thirty pounds for

[044-158]Dandy. After what had passed, Mark could not bring himself to say

[044-159]that he would pay nothing till the bills were safe; and therefore

[044-160]with the assistance of Mr. Forrest of the Bank, he did remit the

[044-161]price of Dandy to his friend Sowerby in London.

[044-162]And Lucy Robarts—we must now say a word of her. We have seen how, on

[044-163]that occasion, when the world was at her feet, she had sent her noble

[044-164]suitor away, not only dismissed, but so dismissed that he might be

[044-165]taught never again to offer to her the sweet incense of his vows. She

[044-166]had declared to him plainly that she did not love him and could not

[044-167]love him, and had thus thrown away not only riches and honour and

[044-168]high station, but more than that—much worse than that—she had flung

[044-169]away from her the lover to whose love her warm heart clung. That her

[044-170]love did cling to him, she knew even then, and owned more thoroughly

[044-171]as soon as he was gone. So much her pride had done for her, and that

[044-172]strong resolve that Lady Lufton should not scowl on her and tell her

[044-173]that she had entrapped her son.

[044-174]I know it will be said of Lord Lufton himself that, putting aside his

[044-175]peerage and broad acres, and handsome, sonsy face, he was not worth

[044-176]a girl’s care and love. That will be said because people think that

[044-177]heroes in books should be so much better than heroes got up for the

[044-178]world’s common wear and tear. I may as well confess that of absolute,

[044-179]true heroism there was only a moderate admixture in Lord Lufton’s

[044-180]composition; but what would the world come to if none but absolute

[044-181]true heroes were to be thought worthy of women’s love? What would the

[044-182]men do? and what—oh! what would become of the women? Lucy Robarts

[044-183]in her heart did not give her dismissed lover credit for much more

[044-184]heroism than did truly appertain to him;—did not, perhaps, give

[044-185]him full credit for a certain amount of heroism which did really

[044-186]appertain to him; but, nevertheless, she would have been very glad to

[044-187]take him could she have done so without wounding her pride.
