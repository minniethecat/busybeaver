# Lord Petty Bag

Lord Petty Bag is one of Trollope's most delightful satirical creations — a political functionary whose very name announces the novelist's contempt for the system of patronage that distributed government offices as spoils rather than as instruments of public service (038-166).

## Role in the Novel

The office of Lord Petty Bag is a "place of considerable emolument" — a government sinecure whose holder is sustained by public money for duties that are, at best, vaguely defined (020-143). The position passes from one political appointee to another: "the late Lord Petty Bag had resigned in disgust," and a new one must be found — not on the basis of competence, but through the familiar machinery of political connection and family influence (038-139). Harold Smith's sister is urged "to give the new Lord Petty Bag no rest till he had conferred a place" on her brother — a perfect miniature of how the patronage system actually worked: not through merit or application, but through the relentless pressure of personal entreaty (038-018).

The very name "Petty Bag" is a joke that Trollope's contemporary readers would have recognised instantly. The Petty Bag Office was a real department of the Court of Chancery, handling minor (petty) legal matters — a byword for bureaucratic obscurity and comfortable redundancy.

## Character Traits

- **Institutional absurdity**: Lord Petty Bag is less a character than an embodiment of a system — the benignly ridiculous machinery of unreformed government, in which offices exist because they have always existed, and men hold them because someone secured the place for them.
- **Political instrumentality**: The Petty Bag office is a prize to be distributed, a favour to be asked, a "place" whose value lies not in what its holder does but in what its holder can give (038-018).

## Relationships

- **[[mr-harold-smith|Harold Smith]]**: Smith's political ambitions are mediated through the Petty Bag machinery. His sister lobbies the Petty Bag office on his behalf — the classic petition of the politically connected (038-018).
- **[[mark-robarts|Mark Robarts]]**: Robarts, observing the political world from his country parsonage, encounters the Petty Bag phenomenon as part of his education in how power actually works — not through ideals but through interest (020-143).

## Key Scenes

- **The Petty Bag secretary (Chapter 20)**: Mark Robarts learns that a certain position is that of "assistant private secretary to the Lord Petty Bag himself — a place of considerable emolument" (020-143). The description — with its combination of grandeur ("himself") and vagueness ("considerable emolument") — is Trollopian satire at its most economical.
- **The sister's petition (Chapter 38)**: Harold Smith's sister is urged to "give the new Lord Petty Bag no rest" until a place is secured. The verb "give no rest" captures the grinding, personal, relentless nature of patronage politics — advancement through attrition (038-018).
- **The resignation (Chapter 38)**: "The late Lord Petty Bag had resigned in disgust" — a phrase that hints at the perpetual dissatisfaction of political placemen, who find even their sinecures insufficiently rewarding (038-139).

## Social & Historical Context

The Petty Bag Office was a real institution within the English Court of Chancery, responsible for the minor (petty) legal documents and writs that sustained the vast machinery of equity law. By Trollope's time, it had become a symbol of unreformed bureaucracy — an office whose functions had long since been superseded but whose existence continued because the patronage system required places to distribute. The "Lord" of the Petty Bag was a political appointee, not a judge; his office was a reward for political service, not a post requiring legal expertise. Trollope, who worked for decades in the Post Office and understood bureaucratic absurdity from the inside, transforms this real institution into a running joke that runs through several of the Barsetshire novels.
