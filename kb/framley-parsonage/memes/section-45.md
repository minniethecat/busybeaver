## Section 45

[045-001]That girls should not marry for money we are all agreed. A lady who

[045-002]can sell herself for a title or an estate, for an income or a set

[045-003]of family diamonds, treats herself as a farmer treats his sheep and

[045-004]oxen—makes hardly more of herself, of her own inner self, in which

[045-005]are comprised a mind and soul, than the poor wretch of her own sex

[045-006]who earns her bread in the lowest stage of degradation. But a title,

[045-007]and an estate, and an income, are matters which will weigh in the

[045-008]balance with all Eve’s daughters—as they do with all Adam’s sons.

[045-009]Pride of place, and the power of living well in front of the world’s

[045-010]eye, are dear to us all;—are, doubtless, intended to be dear. Only

[045-011]in acknowledging so much, let us remember that there are prices at

[045-012]which these good things may be too costly. Therefore, being desirous,

[045-013]too, of telling the truth in this matter, I must confess that Lucy

[045-014]did speculate with some regret on what it would have been to be Lady

[045-015]Lufton. To have been the wife of such a man, the owner of such a

[045-016]heart, the mistress of such a destiny—what more or what better could

[045-017]the world have done for her? And now she had thrown all that aside

[045-018]because she would not endure that Lady Lufton should call her a

[045-019]scheming, artful girl! Actuated by that fear she had repulsed him

[045-020]with a falsehood, though the matter was one on which it was so

[045-021]terribly expedient that she should tell the truth.

[045-022]And yet she was cheerful with her brother and sister-in-law. It

[045-023]was when she was quite alone, at night in her own room, or in her

[045-024]solitary walks, that a single silent tear would gather in the corner

[045-025]of her eye and gradually moisten her eyelids. “She never told

[045-026]her love,” nor did she allow concealment to “feed on her damask

[045-027]cheek.” In all her employments, in her ways about the house, and her

[045-028]accustomed quiet mirth, she was the same as ever. In this she showed

[045-029]the peculiar strength which God had given her. But not the less did

[045-030]she in truth mourn for her lost love and spoiled ambition.

[045-031]“We are going to drive over to Hogglestock this morning,” Fanny said

[045-032]one day at breakfast. “I suppose, Mark, you won’t go with us?”

[045-033]“Well, no; I think not. The pony-carriage is wretched for three.”

[045-034]“Oh, as for that, I should have thought the new horse might have been

[045-035]able to carry you as far as that. I heard you say you wanted to see

[045-036]Mr. Crawley.”

[045-037]“So I do; and the new horse, as you call him, shall carry me there

[045-038]to-morrow. Will you say that I’ll be over about twelve o’clock?”

[045-039]“You had better say earlier, as he is always out about the parish.”

[045-040]“Very well, say eleven. It is parish business about which I am going,

[045-041]so it need not irk his conscience to stay in for me.”

[045-042]“Well, Lucy, we must drive ourselves, that’s all. You shall be

[045-043]charioteer going, and then we’ll change coming back.” To all which

[045-044]Lucy agreed, and as soon as their work in the school was over they

[045-045]started.

[045-046]Not a word had been spoken between them about Lord Lufton since that

[045-047]evening, now more than a month ago, on which they had been walking

[045-048]together in the garden. Lucy had so demeaned herself on that occasion

[045-049]as to make her sister-in-law quite sure that there had been no love

[045-050]passages up to that time; and nothing had since occurred which had

[045-051]created any suspicion in Mrs. Robarts’ mind. She had seen at once

[045-052]that all the close intimacy between them was over, and thought that

[045-053]everything was as it should be.

[045-054]“Do you know, I have an idea,” she said in the pony-carriage that

[045-055]day, “that Lord Lufton will marry Griselda Grantly.” Lucy could

[045-056]not refrain from giving a little check at the reins which she was

[045-057]holding, and she felt that the blood rushed quickly to her heart. But

[045-058]she did not betray herself. “Perhaps he may,” she said, and then gave

[045-059]the pony a little touch with her whip.

[045-060]“Oh, Lucy, I won’t have Puck beaten. He was going very nicely.”

[045-061]“I beg Puck’s pardon. But you see when one is trusted with a whip one

[045-062]feels such a longing to use it.”

[045-063]“Oh, but you should keep it still. I feel almost certain that Lady

[045-064]Lufton would like such a match.”

[045-065]“I daresay she might. Miss Grantly will have a large fortune, I

[045-066]believe.”

[045-067]“It is not that altogether: but she is the sort of young lady that

[045-068]Lady Lufton likes. She is ladylike and very beautiful—”

[045-069]“Come, Fanny!”

[045-070]“I really think she is; not what I should call lovely, you know, but

[045-071]very beautiful. And then she is quiet and reserved; she does not

[045-072]require excitement, and I am sure is conscientious in the performance

[045-073]of her duties.”

[045-074]“Very conscientious, I have no doubt,” said Lucy, with something like

[045-075]a sneer in her tone. “But the question, I suppose, is, whether Lord

[045-076]Lufton likes her.”

[045-077]“I think he does,—in a sort of way. He did not talk to her so much

[045-078]as he did to you—”

[045-079]“Ah! that was all Lady Lufton’s fault, because she didn’t have him

[045-080]properly labelled.”

[045-081]“There does not seem to have been much harm done?”

[045-082]“Oh! by God’s mercy, very little. As for me, I shall get over it in

[045-083]three or four years I don’t doubt—that’s if I can get ass’s milk and

[045-084]change of air.”

[045-085]“We’ll take you to Barchester for that. But as I was saying, I really

[045-086]do think Lord Lufton likes Griselda Grantly.”

[045-087]“Then I really do think that he has uncommon bad taste,” said Lucy,

[045-088]with a reality in her voice differing much from the tone of banter

[045-089]she had hitherto used.

[045-090]“What, Lucy!” said her sister-in-law, looking at her. “Then I fear we

[045-091]shall really want the ass’s milk.”

[045-092]“Perhaps, considering my position, I ought to know nothing of Lord

[045-093]Lufton, for you say that it is very dangerous for young ladies to

[045-094]know young gentlemen. But I do know enough of him to understand that

[045-095]he ought not to like such a girl as Griselda Grantly. He ought to

[045-096]know that she is a mere automaton, cold, lifeless, spiritless, and

[045-097]even vapid. There is, I believe, nothing in her mentally, whatever

[045-098]may be her moral excellences. To me she is more absolutely like a

[045-099]statue than any other human being I ever saw. To sit still and be

[045-100]admired is all that she desires; and if she cannot get that, to sit

[045-101]still and not be admired would almost suffice for her. I do not

[045-102]worship Lady Lufton as you do; but I think quite well enough of her

[045-103]to wonder that she should choose such a girl as that for her son’s

[045-104]wife. That she does wish it, I do not doubt. But I shall indeed be

[045-105]surprised if he wishes it also.” And then as she finished her speech,

[045-106]Lucy again flogged the pony. This she did in vexation, because she

[045-107]felt that the tell-tale blood had suffused her face.

[045-108]“Why, Lucy, if he were your brother you could not be more eager about

[045-109]it.”

[045-110]“No, I could not. He is the only man friend with whom I was ever

[045-111]intimate, and I cannot bear to think that he should throw himself

[045-112]away. It’s horridly improper to care about such a thing, I have no

[045-113]doubt.”

[045-114]“I think we might acknowledge that if he and his mother are both

[045-115]satisfied, we may be satisfied also.”

[045-116]“I shall not be satisfied. It’s no use your looking at me, Fanny. You

[045-117]will make me talk of it, and I won’t tell a lie on the subject. I do

[045-118]like Lord Lufton very much; and I do dislike Griselda Grantly almost

[045-119]as much. Therefore I shall not be satisfied if they become man and

[045-120]wife. However, I do not suppose that either of them will ask my

[045-121]consent; nor is it probable that Lady Lufton will do so.” And then

[045-122]they went on for perhaps a quarter of a mile without speaking.

[045-123]“Poor Puck!” at last Lucy said. “He shan’t be whipped any more, shall

[045-124]he, because Miss Grantly looks like a statue? And, Fanny, don’t tell

[045-125]Mark to put me into a lunatic asylum. I also know a hawk from a

[045-126]heron, and that’s why I don’t like to see such a very unfitting

[045-127]marriage.” There was then nothing more said on the subject, and in

[045-128]two minutes they arrived at the house of the Hogglestock clergyman.

[045-129]Mrs. Crawley had brought two children with her when she came from the

[045-130]Cornish curacy to Hogglestock, and two other babies had been added to

[045-131]her cares since then. One of these was now ill with croup, and it was

[045-132]with the object of offering to the mother some comfort and solace,

[045-133]that the present visit was made. The two ladies got down from their

[045-134]carriage, having obtained the services of a boy to hold Puck, and

[045-135]soon found themselves in Mrs. Crawley’s single sitting-room. She was

[045-136]sitting there with her foot on the board of a child’s cradle, rocking

[045-137]it, while an infant about three months old was lying in her lap. For

[045-138]the elder one, who was the sufferer, had in her illness usurped the

[045-139]baby’s place. Two other children, considerably older, were also in

[045-140]the room. The eldest was a girl, perhaps nine years of age, and the

[045-141]other a boy three years her junior. These were standing at their

[045-142]father’s elbow, who was studiously endeavouring to initiate them in

[045-143]the early mysteries of grammar. To tell the truth Mrs. Robarts would

[045-144]much have preferred that Mr. Crawley had not been there, for she had

[045-145]with her and about her certain contraband articles, presents for the

[045-146]children, as they were to be called, but in truth relief for that

[045-147]poor, much-tasked mother, which they knew it would be impossible to

[045-148]introduce in Mr. Crawley’s presence.

[045-149]She, as we have said, was not quite so gaunt, not altogether so

[045-150]haggard as in the latter of those dreadful Cornish days. Lady Lufton

[045-151]and Mrs. Arabin between them, and the scanty comfort of their

[045-152]improved, though still wretched income, had done something towards

[045-153]bringing her back to the world in which she had lived in the soft

[045-154]days of her childhood. But even the liberal stipend of a hundred

[045-155]and thirty pounds a-year—liberal according to the scale by which

[045-156]the incomes of clergymen in some of our new districts are now

[045-157]apportioned—would not admit of a gentleman with his wife and four

[045-158]children living with the ordinary comforts of an artisan’s family. As

[045-159]regards the mere eating and drinking, the amounts of butcher’s meat

[045-160]and tea and butter, they of course were used in quantities which any

[045-161]artisan would have regarded as compatible only with demi-starvation.

[045-162]Better clothing for her children was necessary, and better clothing

[045-163]for him. As for her own raiment, the wives of few artisans would have

[045-164]been content to put up with Mrs. Crawley’s best gown. The stuff of

[045-165]which it was made had been paid for by her mother when she with much

[045-166]difficulty bestowed upon her daughter her modest wedding _trousseau_.

[045-167]Lucy had never seen Mrs. Crawley. These visits to Hogglestock were

[045-168]not frequent, and had generally been made by Lady Lufton and Mrs.

[045-169]Robarts together. It was known that they were distasteful to Mr.

[045-170]Crawley, who felt a savage satisfaction in being left to himself.

[045-171]It may almost be said of him that he felt angry with those who

[045-172]relieved him, and he had certainly never as yet forgiven the Dean

[045-173]of Barchester for paying his debts. The dean had also given him his

[045-174]present living; and consequently his old friend was not now so dear

[045-175]to him as when in old days he would come down to that farm-house,

[045-176]almost as penniless as the curate himself. Then they would walk

[045-177]together for hours along the rock-bound shore, listening to the

[045-178]waves, discussing deep polemical mysteries, sometimes with hot fury,

[045-179]then again with tender, loving charity, but always with a mutual

[045-180]acknowledgment of each other’s truth. Now they lived comparatively

[045-181]near together, but no opportunities arose for such discussions. At

[045-182]any rate once a quarter Mr. Crawley was pressed by his old friend to

[045-183]visit him at the deanery, and Dr. Arabin had promised that no one

[045-184]else should be in the house if Mr. Crawley objected to society. But

[045-185]this was not what he wanted. The finery and grandeur of the deanery,

[045-186]and the comfort of that warm, snug library, would silence him at

[045-187]once. Why did not Dr. Arabin come out there to Hogglestock, and tramp

[045-188]with him through the dirty lanes as they used to tramp? Then he could

[045-189]have enjoyed himself; then he could have talked; then old days would

[045-190]have come back to them. But now!—“Arabin always rides on a sleek,

[045-191]fine horse, now-a-days,” he once said to his wife with a sneer. His

[045-192]poverty had been so terrible to himself that it was not in his heart

[045-193]to love a rich friend.
