## Section 38

[038-001]This was certainly very good-natured on the part of Mr. Sowerby, and

[038-002]showed that he had a feeling within his bosom that he owed something

[038-003]to his friend the parson for the injury he had done him. And such

[038-004]was in truth the case. A more reckless being than the member for

[038-005]West Barsetshire could not exist. He was reckless for himself, and

[038-006]reckless for all others with whom he might be concerned. He could

[038-007]ruin his friends with as little remorse as he had ruined himself. All

[038-008]was fair game that came in the way of his net. But, nevertheless, he

[038-009]was good-natured, and willing to move heaven and earth to do a friend

[038-010]a good turn, if it came in his way to do so.

[038-011]He did really love Mark Robarts as much as it was given him to love

[038-012]any among his acquaintance. He knew that he had already done him an

[038-013]almost irreparable injury, and might very probably injure him still

[038-014]deeper before he had done with him. That he would undoubtedly do so,

[038-015]if it came in his way, was very certain. But then, if it also came

[038-016]in his way to repay his friend by any side blow, he would also

[038-017]undoubtedly do that. Such an occasion had now come, and he had

[038-018]desired his sister to give the new Lord Petty Bag no rest till he

[038-019]should have promised to use all his influence in getting the vacant

[038-020]prebend for Mark Robarts.

[038-021]This letter of Sowerby’s Mark immediately showed to his wife. How

[038-022]lucky, thought he to himself, that not a word was said in it about

[038-023]those accursed money transactions! Had he understood Sowerby better

[038-024]he would have known that that gentleman never said anything about

[038-025]money transactions until it became absolutely necessary. “I know you

[038-026]don’t like Mr. Sowerby,” he said; “but you must own that this is very

[038-027]good-natured.”

[038-028]“It is the character I hear of him that I don’t like,” said Mrs.

[038-029]Robarts.

[038-030]“But what shall I do now, Fanny? As he says, why should not I have

[038-031]the stall as well as another?”

[038-032]“I suppose it would not interfere with your parish?” she asked.

[038-033]“Not in the least, at the distance at which we are. I did think of

[038-034]giving up old Jones; but if I take this, of course I must keep a

[038-035]curate.”

[038-036]His wife could not find it in her heart to dissuade him from

[038-037]accepting promotion when it came in his way—what vicar’s wife would

[038-038]have so persuaded her husband? But yet she did not altogether like

[038-039]it. She feared that Greek from Chaldicotes, even when he came with

[038-040]the present of a prebendal stall in his hands. And then what would

[038-041]Lady Lufton say?

[038-042]“And do you think that you must go up to London, Mark?”

[038-043]“Oh, certainly; that is, if I intend to accept Harold Smith’s kind

[038-044]offices in the matter.”

[038-045]“I suppose it will be better to accept them,” said Fanny, feeling

[038-046]perhaps that it would be useless in her to hope that they should not

[038-047]be accepted.

[038-048]“Prebendal stalls, Fanny, don’t generally go begging long among

[038-049]parish clergymen. How could I reconcile it to the duty I owe to my

[038-050]children to refuse such an increase to my income?” And so it was

[038-051]settled that he should at once drive to Silverbridge and send off a

[038-052]message by telegraph, and that he should himself proceed to London on

[038-053]the following day. “But you must see Lady Lufton first, of course,”

[038-054]said Fanny, as soon as all this was settled.

[038-055]Mark would have avoided this if he could have decently done so, but

[038-056]he felt that it would be impolitic, as well as indecent. And why

[038-057]should he be afraid to tell Lady Lufton that he hoped to receive this

[038-058]piece of promotion from the present government? There was nothing

[038-059]disgraceful in a clergyman becoming a prebendary of Barchester. Lady

[038-060]Lufton herself had always been very civil to the prebendaries, and

[038-061]especially to little Dr. Burslem, the meagre little man who had just

[038-062]now paid the debt of nature. She had always been very fond of the

[038-063]chapter, and her original dislike to Bishop Proudie had been chiefly

[038-064]founded on his interference with the cathedral clergy,—on his

[038-065]interference, or on that of his wife or chaplain. Considering these

[038-066]things Mark Robarts tried to make himself believe that Lady Lufton

[038-067]would be delighted at his good fortune. But yet he did not believe

[038-068]it. She at any rate would revolt from the gift of the Greek of

[038-069]Chaldicotes.

[038-070]“Oh, indeed,” she said, when the vicar had with some difficulty

[038-071]explained to her all the circumstances of the case. “Well, I

[038-072]congratulate you, Mr. Robarts, on your powerful new patron.”

[038-073]“You will probably feel with me, Lady Lufton, that the benefice is

[038-074]one which I can hold without any detriment to me in my position here

[038-075]at Framley,” said he, prudently resolving to let the slur upon his

[038-076]friends pass by unheeded.

[038-077]“Well, I hope so. Of course, you are a very young man, Mr. Robarts,

[038-078]and these things have generally been given to clergymen more advanced

[038-079]in life.”

[038-080]“But you do not mean to say that you think I ought to refuse it?”

[038-081]“What my advice to you might be if you really came to me for advice,

[038-082]I am hardly prepared to say at so very short a notice. You seem to

[038-083]have made up your mind, and therefore I need not consider it. As it

[038-084]is, I wish you joy, and hope that it may turn out to your advantage

[038-085]in every way.”

[038-086]“You understand, Lady Lufton, that I have by no means got it as yet.”

[038-087]“Oh, I thought it had been offered to you: I thought you spoke of

[038-088]this new minister as having all that in his own hand.”

[038-089]“Oh, dear, no. What may be the amount of his influence in that

[038-090]respect I do not at all know. But my correspondent assures me—”

[038-091]“Mr. Sowerby, you mean. Why don’t you call him by his name?”

[038-092]“Mr. Sowerby assures me that Mr. Smith will ask for it; and thinks it

[038-093]most probable that his request will be successful.”

[038-094]“Oh, of course. Mr. Sowerby and Mr. Harold Smith together would no

[038-095]doubt be successful in anything. They are the sort of men who are

[038-096]successful nowadays. Well, Mr. Robarts, I wish you joy.” And she gave

[038-097]him her hand in token of her sincerity.

[038-098]Mark took her hand, resolving to say nothing further on that

[038-099]occasion. That Lady Lufton was not now cordial with him, as she used

[038-100]to be, he was well aware; and sooner or later he was determined

[038-101]to have the matter out with her. He would ask her why she now so

[038-102]constantly met him with a taunt, and so seldom greeted him with that

[038-103]kind old affectionate smile which he knew and appreciated so well.

[038-104]That she was honest and true, he was quite sure. If he asked her the

[038-105]question plainly, she would answer him openly. And if he could induce

[038-106]her to say that she would return to her old ways, return to them she

[038-107]would in a hearty manner. But he could not do this just at present.

[038-108]It was but a day or two since Mr. Crawley had been with him; and

[038-109]was it not probable that Mr. Crawley had been sent thither by Lady

[038-110]Lufton? His own hands were not clean enough for a remonstrance at the

[038-111]present moment. He would cleanse them, and then he would remonstrate.

[038-112]“Would you like to live part of the year in Barchester?” he said to

[038-113]his wife and sister that evening.

[038-114]“I think that two houses are only a trouble,” said his wife. “And we

[038-115]have been very happy here.”

[038-116]“I have always liked a cathedral town,” said Lucy; “and I am

[038-117]particularly fond of the close.”

[038-118]“And Barchester-close is the closest of all closes,” said Mark.

[038-119]“There is not a single house within the gateways that does not belong

[038-120]to the chapter.”

[038-121]“But if we are to keep up two houses, the additional income will soon

[038-122]be wasted,” said Fanny prudently.

[038-123]“The thing would be, to let the house furnished every summer,” said

[038-124]Lucy.

[038-125]“But I must take my residence as the terms come,” said the vicar;

[038-126]“and I certainly should not like to be away from Framley all the

[038-127]winter; I should never see anything of Lufton.” And perhaps he

[038-128]thought of his hunting, and then thought again of that cleansing of

[038-129]his hands.

[038-130]“I should not a bit mind being away during the winter,” said Lucy,

[038-131]thinking of what the last winter had done for her.

[038-132]“But where on earth should we find money to furnish one of those

[038-133]large, old-fashioned houses? Pray, Mark, do not do anything rash.”

[038-134]And the wife laid her hand affectionately on her husband’s arm. In

[038-135]this manner the question of the prebend was discussed between them on

[038-136]the evening before he started for London.

[038-137]Success had at last crowned the earnest effort with which Harold

[038-138]Smith had carried on the political battle of his life for the last

[038-139]ten years. The late Lord Petty Bag had resigned in disgust, having

[038-140]been unable to digest the Prime Minister’s ideas on Indian Reform,

[038-141]and Mr. Harold Smith, after sundry hitches in the business, was

[038-142]installed in his place. It was said that Harold Smith was not

[038-143]exactly the man whom the Premier would himself have chosen for

[038-144]that high office; but the Premier’s hands were a good deal tied

[038-145]by circumstances. The last great appointment he had made had been

[038-146]terribly unpopular,—so much so as to subject him, popular as he

[038-147]undoubtedly was himself, to a screech from the whole nation. The

[038-148]_Jupiter_, with withering scorn, had asked whether vice of every kind

[038-149]was to be considered, in these days of Queen Victoria, as a passport

[038-150]to the cabinet. Adverse members of both Houses had arrayed themselves

[038-151]in a pure panoply of morality, and thundered forth their sarcasms

[038-152]with the indignant virtue and keen discontent of political Juvenals;

[038-153]and even his own friends had held up their hands in dismay. Under

[038-154]these circumstances he had thought himself obliged in the present

[038-155]instance to select a man who would not be especially objectionable

[038-156]to any party. Now Harold Smith lived with his wife, and his

[038-157]circumstances were not more than ordinarily embarrassed. He kept no

[038-158]race-horses; and, as Lord Brock now heard for the first time, gave

[038-159]lectures in provincial towns on popular subjects. He had a seat

[038-160]which was tolerably secure, and could talk to the House by the yard

[038-161]if required to do so. Moreover, Lord Brock had a great idea that

[038-162]the whole machinery of his own ministry would break to pieces very

[038-163]speedily. His own reputation was not bad, but it was insufficient

[038-164]for himself and that lately selected friend of his. Under all these

[038-165]circumstances combined, he chose Harold Smith to fill the vacant

[038-166]office of Lord Petty Bag.

[038-167]And very proud the Lord Petty Bag was. For the last three or four

[038-168]months, he and Mr. Supplehouse had been agreeing to consign the

[038-169]ministry to speedy perdition. “This sort of dictatorship will never

[038-170]do,” Harold Smith had himself said, justifying that future vote

[038-171]of his as to want of confidence in the Queen’s government. And Mr.

[038-172]Supplehouse in this matter had fully agreed with him. He was a Juno

[038-173]whose form that wicked old Paris had utterly despised, and he, too,

[038-174]had quite made up his mind as to the lobby in which he would be found

[038-175]when that day of vengeance should arrive. But now things were much

[038-176]altered in Harold Smith’s views. The Premier had shown his wisdom

[038-177]in seeking for new strength where strength ought to be sought, and

[038-178]introducing new blood into the body of his ministry. The people would

[038-179]now feel fresh confidence, and probably the House also. As to Mr.

[038-180]Supplehouse—he would use all his influence on Supplehouse. But,

[038-181]after all, Mr. Supplehouse was not everything.
