## Section 26

[026-001]It seemed to Mark as though Mr. Forrest made very light of the whole

[026-002]transaction.

[026-003]“I cannot possibly pay the bill when it falls due,” said Mark.

[026-004]“Oh, no, of course not,” said Mr. Forrest. “It’s never very

[026-005]convenient to hand out four hundred pounds at a blow. Nobody will

[026-006]expect you to pay it!”

[026-007]“But I suppose I shall have to do it sooner or later?”

[026-008]“Well, that’s as may be. It will depend partly on how you manage with

[026-009]Sowerby, and partly on the hands it gets into. As the bill has your

[026-010]name on it, they’ll have patience as long as the interest is paid,

[026-011]and the commissions on renewal. But no doubt it will have to be met

[026-012]some day by somebody.”

[026-013]Mr. Forrest said that he was sure that the bill was not in

[026-014]Barchester; Mr. Sowerby would not, he thought, have brought it to a

[026-015]Barchester bank. The bill was probably in London, but doubtless would

[026-016]be sent to Barchester for collection. “If it comes in my way,” said

[026-017]Mr. Forrest, “I will give you plenty of time, so that you may manage

[026-018]about the renewal with Sowerby. I suppose he’ll pay the expense of

[026-019]doing that.”

[026-020]Mark’s heart was somewhat lighter as he left the bank. Mr. Forrest

[026-021]had made so little of the whole transaction that he felt himself

[026-022]justified in making little of it also. “It may be as well,” said he

[026-023]to himself, as he drove home, “not to tell Fanny anything about it

[026-024]till the three months have run round. I must make some arrangement

[026-025]then.” And in this way his mind was easier during the last of those

[026-026]three months than it had been during the two former. That feeling

[026-027]of over-due bills, of bills coming due, of accounts overdrawn, of

[026-028]tradesmen unpaid, of general money cares, is very dreadful at first;

[026-029]but it is astonishing how soon men get used to it. A load which would

[026-030]crush a man at first becomes, by habit, not only endurable, but

[026-031]easy and comfortable to the bearer. The habitual debtor goes along

[026-032]jaunty and with elastic step, almost enjoying the excitement of his

[026-033]embarrassments. There was Mr. Sowerby himself; who ever saw a cloud

[026-034]on his brow? It made one almost in love with ruin to be in his

[026-035]company. And even now, already, Mark Robarts was thinking to himself

[026-036]quite comfortably about this bill;—how very pleasantly those bankers

[026-037]managed these things. Pay it! No; no one will be so unreasonable

[026-038]as to expect you to do that! And then Mr. Sowerby certainly was a

[026-039]pleasant fellow, and gave a man something in return for his money. It

[026-040]was still a question with Mark whether Lord Lufton had not been too

[026-041]hard on Sowerby. Had that gentleman fallen across his clerical friend

[026-042]at the present moment, he might no doubt have gotten from him an

[026-043]acceptance for another four hundred pounds.

[026-044]One is almost inclined to believe that there is something pleasurable

[026-045]in the excitement of such embarrassments, as there is also in the

[026-046]excitement of drink. But then, at last, the time does come when the

[026-047]excitement is over, and when nothing but the misery is left. If there

[026-048]be an existence of wretchedness on earth it must be that of the

[026-049]elderly, worn-out _roué_, who has run this race of debt and bills

[026-050]of accommodation and acceptances,—of what, if we were not in these

[026-051]days somewhat afraid of good broad English, we might call lying and

[026-052]swindling, falsehood and fraud—and who, having ruined all whom he

[026-053]should have loved, having burnt up every one who would trust him

[026-054]much, and scorched all who would trust him a little, is at last

[026-055]left to finish his life with such bread and water as these men get,

[026-056]without one honest thought to strengthen his sinking heart, or one

[026-057]honest friend to hold his shivering hand! If a man could only think

[026-058]of that, as he puts his name to the first little bill, as to which he

[026-059]is so good-naturedly assured that it can easily be renewed!

[026-060]When the three months had nearly run out, it so happened that Robarts

[026-061]met his friend Sowerby. Mark had once or twice ridden with Lord

[026-062]Lufton as far as the meet of the hounds, and may, perhaps, have gone

[026-063]a field or two farther on some occasions. The reader must not think

[026-064]that he had taken to hunting, as some parsons do; and it is singular

[026-065]enough that whenever they do so they always show a special aptitude

[026-066]for the pursuit, as though hunting were an employment peculiarly

[026-067]congenial with a cure of souls in the country. Such a thought would

[026-068]do our vicar injustice. But when Lord Lufton would ask him what on

[026-069]earth could be the harm of riding along the roads to look at the

[026-070]hounds, he hardly knew what sensible answer to give his lordship. It

[026-071]would be absurd to say that his time would be better employed at home

[026-072]in clerical matters, for it was notorious that he had not clerical

[026-073]pursuits for the employment of half his time. In this way, therefore,

[026-074]he had got into a habit of looking at the hounds, and keeping up his

[026-075]acquaintance in the county, meeting Lord Dumbello, Mr. Green Walker,

[026-076]Harold Smith, and other such like sinners; and on one such occasion,

[026-077]as the three months were nearly closing, he did meet Mr. Sowerby.

[026-078]“Look here, Sowerby; I want to speak to you for half a moment. What

[026-079]are you doing about that bill?”

[026-080]“Bill—bill! what bill?—which bill? The whole bill, and nothing but

[026-081]the bill. That seems to be the conversation now-a-days of all men,

[026-082]morning, noon, and night.”

[026-083]“Don’t you know the bill I signed for you for four hundred pounds?”

[026-084]“Did you, though? Was not that rather green of you?”

[026-085]This did seem strange to Mark. Could it really be the fact that

[026-086]Mr. Sowerby had so many bills flying about that he had absolutely

[026-087]forgotten that occurrence in the Gatherum Castle bedroom? And then to

[026-088]be called green by the very man whom he had obliged!

[026-089]“Perhaps I was,” said Mark, in a tone that showed that he was

[026-090]somewhat piqued. “But all the same I should be glad to know how it

[026-091]will be taken up.”

[026-092]“Oh, Mark, what a ruffian you are to spoil my day’s sport in this

[026-093]way. Any man but a parson would be too good a Christian for such

[026-094]intense cruelty. But let me see—four hundred pounds? Oh, yes—Tozer

[026-095]has it.”

[026-096]“And what will Tozer do with it?”

[026-097]“Make money of it; whatever way he may go to work he will do that.”

[026-098]“But will Tozer bring it to me on the 20th?”

[026-099]“Oh, Lord, no! Upon my word, Mark, you are deliciously green. A cat

[026-100]would as soon think of killing a mouse directly she got it into her

[026-101]claws. But, joking apart, you need not trouble yourself. Maybe you

[026-102]will hear no more about it; or, perhaps, which no doubt is more

[026-103]probable, I may have to send it to you to be renewed. But you need do

[026-104]nothing till you hear from me or somebody else.”

[026-105]“Only do not let any one come down upon me for the money.”

[026-106]“There is not the slightest fear of that. Tally-ho, old fellow! He’s

[026-107]away. Tally-ho! right over by Gossetts’ barn. Come along, and never

[026-108]mind Tozer—‘Sufficient for the day is the evil thereof.’” And away

[026-109]they both went together, parson and member of Parliament.

[026-110]And then again on that occasion Mark went home with a sort of feeling

[026-111]that the bill did not matter. Tozer would manage it somehow; and it

[026-112]was quite clear that it would not do to tell his wife of it just at

[026-113]present.

[026-114]On the 21st of that month of February, however, he did receive a

[026-115]reminder that the bill and all concerning it had not merely been a

[026-116]farce. This was a letter from Mr. Sowerby, dated from Chaldicotes,

[026-117]though not bearing the Barchester post-mark, in which that gentleman

[026-118]suggested a renewal—not exactly of the old bill, but of a new one.

[026-119]It seemed to Mark that the letter had been posted in London. If I

[026-120]give it entire, I shall, perhaps, most quickly explain its purport:

[026-121]Chaldicotes,—20th February, 185—.

[026-122]MY DEAR MARK,—“Lend not thy name to the money-dealers,

[026-123]for the same is a destruction and a snare.” If that be

[026-124]not in the Proverbs, it ought to be. Tozer has given me

[026-125]certain signs of his being alive and strong this cold

[026-126]weather. As we can neither of us take up that bill

[026-127]for £400 at the moment, we must renew it, and pay him

[026-128]his commission and interest, with all the rest of his

[026-129]perquisites, and pickings, and stealings—from all which,

[026-130]I can assure you, Tozer does not keep his hands as he

[026-131]should do.

[026-132]To cover this and some other little outstanding trifles,

[026-133]I have filled in the new bill for £500, making it due

[026-134]23rd of May next. Before that time, a certain accident

[026-135]will, I trust, have occurred to your impoverished friend.

[026-136]By-the-by, I never told you how she went off from Gatherum

[026-137]Castle, the morning after you left us, with the Greshams.

[026-138]Cart-ropes would not hold her, even though the duke held

[026-139]them; which he did, with all the strength of his ducal

[026-140]hands. She would go to meet some doctor of theirs, and so

[026-141]I was put off for that time; but I think that the matter

[026-142]stands in a good train.

[026-143]Do not lose a post in sending back the bill accepted, as

[026-144]Tozer may annoy you—nay, undoubtedly will, if the matter

[026-145]be not in his hand, duly signed by both of us, the day

[026-146]after to-morrow. He is an ungrateful brute; he has lived

[026-147]on me for these eight years, and would not let me off a

[026-148]single squeeze now to save my life. But I am specially

[026-149]anxious to save you from the annoyance and cost of

[026-150]lawyers’ letters; and if delayed, it might get into the

[026-151]papers.

[026-152]Put it under cover to me, at No. 7, Duke Street, St.

[026-153]James’s. I shall be in town by that time.

[026-154]Good-bye, old fellow. That was a decent brush we had the

[026-155]other day from Cobbold’s Ashes. I wish I could get that

[026-156]brown horse from you. I would not mind going to a hundred

[026-157]and thirty.

[026-158]Yours ever,

[026-159]N. SOWERBY.

[026-160]When Mark had read it through he looked down on his table to see

[026-161]whether the old bill had fallen from the letter; but no, there was

[026-162]no enclosure, and had been no enclosure but the new bill. And then

[026-163]he read the letter through again, and found that there was no word

[026-164]about the old bill,—not a syllable, at least, as to its whereabouts.

[026-165]Sowerby did not even say that it would remain in his own hands.

[026-166]Mark did not in truth know much about such things. It might be that

[026-167]the very fact of his signing this second document would render that

[026-168]first document null and void; and from Sowerby’s silence on the

[026-169]subject, it might be argued that this was so well known to be the

[026-170]case, that he had not thought of explaining it. But yet Mark could

[026-171]not see how this should be so.

[026-172]But what was he to do? That threat of cost and lawyers, and specially

[026-173]of the newspapers, did have its effect upon him—as no doubt it was

[026-174]intended to do. And then he was utterly dumfounded by Sowerby’s

[026-175]impudence in drawing on him for £500 instead of £400, “covering,” as

[026-176]Sowerby so good-humouredly said, “sundry little outstanding trifles.”

[026-177]But at last he did sign the bill, and sent it off, as Sowerby had

[026-178]directed. What else was he to do?

[026-179]Fool that he was. A man always can do right, even though he has done

[026-180]wrong before. But that previous wrong adds so much difficulty to the

[026-181]path—a difficulty which increases in tremendous ratio, till a man at

[026-182]last is choked in his struggling, and is drowned beneath the waters.
