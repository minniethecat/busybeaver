# Odenwald

The Odenwald is the forest where the hunting expedition takes place in Adventures 15–16 of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — the site where [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is treacherously murdered by [[hagen|Hagen of Tronje]]. As a narrative space, the Odenwald represents the boundary between the courtly world of [[worms|Worms]] and the wild, where the rules of feudal hospitality collapse and heroic transparency gives way to hidden violence. The murder at the spring in the Odenwald is the single most consequential event in the epic: it transforms [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] from a noble wife into an agent of revenge and sets the entire second half of the poem in motion.

## Location & Geography

- **Type**: [[forest-and-wilderness|Forest]]ed highland region, used as royal hunting ground
- **Ruler**: Burgundian crown ([[gunther|King Gunther]]'s domain)
- **Real-world counterpart**: The Odenwald is a low mountain range in southern Germany, straddling the borders of Hesse, Bavaria, and Baden-Württemberg, between the Main and Neckar rivers. Its proximity to [[worms|Worms]] (approximately 40–50 km east) makes it a plausible royal hunting ground.
- **Associated characters**: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] (murdered here), [[hagen|Hagen of Tronje]] (the murderer), [[gunther|Gunther]] (present at the hunt), hunters and attendants of the Burgundian court

### Strategic Significance

The Odenwald functions as a liminal space in the epic's geography — neither fully within the courtly realm nor fully outside it. It is close enough to [[worms|Worms]] to serve as a day's hunting ground, yet far enough from the court that the murder can be falsely attributed to robbers (Schächer) in the forest. [[hagen|Hagen]] exploits precisely this ambiguity: the forest as a space where the rules of the court are suspended, where a hero's death can be disguised as an accident or bandit attack. The village of Odenheim, mentioned at 016-359, anchors this legendary murder in a real geographical location, lending the epic the texture of historical verisimilitude that characterizes much of the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied*'s topography.

## Key Events at This Location

- [[siegfried|Siegfried]] proposes the hunt — his last voluntary action as a free hero (015-140–149)
- The hunting party rides into the Odenwald; [[siegfried|Siegfried]] excels in the chase (016-001–050)
- [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is murdered by [[hagen|Hagen]] at the forest spring near Odenheim (016-350–359)
- [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s body is transported back to [[worms|Worms]] under cover of darkness (016-350–353)

## Notable Descriptions

> "Nach dem Odenwalde, wie ich oft gethan." — "To the Odenwald, as I have often done." (015-143)
>
> Siegfried's casual proposal of the hunt, unaware it will be his last journey. The line's offhand tone — "as I have often done" — is laden with tragic irony.

> "Vor dem Odenwalde ein Dorf liegt Odenheim." — "Before the Odenwald lies a village called Odenheim." (016-359)
>
> The narrative voice locates the murder with geographical precision: the spring where Siegfried dies is "before the Odenwald," near the village of Odenheim. This anchoring of the legendary murder in real topography is characteristic of the *Nibelungenlied*'s blend of myth and historical geography.

## Historical / Legendary Notes

- The Odenwald is a real geographical feature — a forested low mountain range in southern Germany — that was part of the Burgundian sphere of influence in the early medieval period. Its use as a hunting ground in the epic aligns with historical practice.
- In Norse versions of the Sigurd/[[siegfried|Siegfried]] legend, [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is killed in a forest or on a heath rather than at a specific named location. The *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied*'s localization of the murder to the Odenwald reflects the poem's broader topographical project of anchoring legendary events in real, recognizable geography.
- The spring (Brunnen) where [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is killed is a recurring motif in Germanic heroic legend — the hero killed while drinking from a spring appears in other traditions, including some versions of the Dietrich cycle.
- The name "Odenwald" may derive from *Odins [[forest-and-wilderness|Wald]]* (Odin's [[forest-and-wilderness|Forest]]) or from older Celtic toponyms. In the context of the epic, this etymological ambiguity is significant: the forest is both a real place and a mythic space associated with death and sacrifice.
