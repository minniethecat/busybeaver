# Êre

[[ere|Êre]] is the Middle High German concept of honor — the public reputation and renown that governs the world of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. It drives [[siegfried|Siegfried]] to [[worms|Worms]], [[hagen|Hagen]] to murder, and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] to the destruction of two kingdoms.

"With praiseworthy honors all their life's time" describes the Burgundian kings (001-023). The wounding of Êre — through insult or public humiliation — drives many of the epic's pivotal conflicts, culminating in the [[fall-of-the-burgundians|catastrophe at Etzel's court]].

## Definition

Êre operates on two levels: external Êre (public reputation, the honor others accord you) and internal Êre (personal integrity, living up to the [[heroic-code|heroic code]]).

The Burgundian court is defined by its Êre — "the court and its honors" (001-039). Characters measure their actions against "high honors" (001-044). The loss of Êre is worse than death, and its restoration justifies extreme measures.

### The Honor Wound

The [[queens-quarrel|quarrel between Brunhild and Kriemhild]] in Adventure 14 is the epic's paradigmatic Êre-conflict. When [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] reveals that [[siegfried|Siegfried]] — not [[gunther|Gunther]] — took [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s virginity, she wounds [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s Êre publicly and irreparably.

This honor wound demands satisfaction, and [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is framed as the restoration of [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s Êre. The irony is that this act destroys the Êre of the Burgundian court itself — a spiral of honor and vengeance that consumes everyone.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In Hohenstaufen courtly culture, Êre was the organizing principle of aristocratic life. Tournaments, quests, and poetic celebration ([[minne|Minne]]sang) served to acquire and display Êre. The loss of Êre could lead to social death — exclusion from court, loss of fief.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied traces the logic of Êre to its self-destructive conclusion. The demand to restore wounded honor leads to murder; the demand to avenge murdered honor leads to genocide.

The epic's final image — the flower of Burgundian knighthood dead in a burning hall at [[etzels-court|Etzel's court]] — is what Êre, pursued without limit, ultimately produces.
