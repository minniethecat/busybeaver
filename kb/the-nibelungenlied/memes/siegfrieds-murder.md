# Siegfried's Murder

[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder (Adventure 16) is the central event of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — the act that divides the epic into its two halves and transforms [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] from loving wife into implacable avenger. During a hunting expedition in the [[vosges|Vosges]] mountains, [[hagen|Hagen of Tronje]] spears [[siegfried|Siegfried]] through the single vulnerable spot on his back — the place where a linden leaf had fallen when [[siegfried|Siegfried]] bathed in dragon's blood — as the hero bends to drink from a forest spring. The murder violates every norm of hospitality, kinship, and feudal obligation, setting the stage for the apocalyptic revenge of Part II.

## Overview

- **Adventure(s)**: 16
- **Location**: The [[vosges|Vosges]] mountains ([[vosges|Wasgenwald]]), at a forest spring
- **Key Participants**: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] (victim), [[hagen|Hagen]] (murderer), [[gunther|Gunther]] (complicit king)
- **Outcome**: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] dead; [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] widowed; the Nibelung treasure becomes contested; the revenge cycle begins

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

After the queens' quarrel, [[hagen|Hagen]] resolves to kill [[siegfried|Siegfried]] to restore [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s wounded honor. He manipulates [[gunther|Gunther]] into complicity by framing the murder as necessary for Burgundian security. To discover [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s vulnerability, [[hagen|Hagen]] uses a ruse: he tells [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] that a second Saxon invasion is imminent and that he wishes to protect [[siegfried|Siegfried]] in battle. Trusting [[hagen|Hagen]]'s feigned concern, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] reveals the secret and, at [[hagen|Hagen]]'s request, sews a small cross on [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s garment to mark the vulnerable spot.

### The Event

A hunting expedition is announced. In the forest, [[siegfried|Siegfried]] outruns and outhunts all others — even capturing a live bear for sport. At a spring, thirsty from the chase, [[siegfried|Siegfried]] bends to drink. [[hagen|Hagen]], standing behind him, drives a spear through the marked spot between [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s shoulder blades. Mortally wounded, [[siegfried|Siegfried]] rises, finds his shield and sword, and pursues [[hagen|Hagen]] — but his strength fails. He dies cursing the Burgundians for their treachery: "You have murdered me without cause — this shame will cling to your race forever."

### Aftermath

[[hagen|Hagen]] has [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s body brought back to [[worms|Worms]] and laid at [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s door — an act of calculated cruelty. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], discovering her husband's corpse, immediately knows [[hagen|Hagen]] is the murderer. At [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s funeral, when [[hagen|Hagen]] approaches the bier, [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s wounds begin to bleed anew — the medieval motif of cruentation, the dead body testifying against its murderer. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief is absolute, and her transformation into the avenger of Part II begins. "Terrible revenge" (017-006) is announced by the narrator.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's peripeteia — the reversal that transforms the epic's trajectory. Part I tells the story of a hero's rise; Part II tells the story of his avenging. The murder is not merely a death but a comprehensive violation of the heroic order: hospitality (guest murdered by host), kinship (brother-in-law by brother-in-law's vassal), feudal loyalty (ally by ally), and personal trust ([[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s confidence betrayed).

### Medieval Themes

The murder dramatizes the conflict between the demands of [[ere|Êre]] and the bonds of [[triuwe|Triuwe]]. [[hagen|Hagen]] acts to restore [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s wounded honor; in doing so, he violates every bond of loyalty. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied suggests that when honor and loyalty conflict, the result is not the triumph of one value over the other but the destruction of both.
