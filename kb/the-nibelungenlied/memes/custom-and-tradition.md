# Custom and Tradition in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's social world is governed not by written law but by _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_
— custom, tradition, proper conduct. The term appears at crucial moments
throughout the poem, from the narrator's early praise of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s "höfsche
[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]" (001-012) that would be an ornament to all women, to the customs of
greeting foreign knights (007-073), the protocols of tournaments and feasts
(010-265), and the rituals of mourning and burial. _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ in the poem is both
descriptive — "this is how things are done" — and prescriptive — "this is how
things must be done." When [[hagen]]'s "grimmiger [[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]" (fierce custom) is
invoked, or when [[brunhild]] violates the custom of hostly deference, the poem
signals that the social order is under strain. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied can be read as
a study in the fragility of custom: the elaborate architecture of courtly
_[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ that the first half of the poem constructs is systematically dismantled
in the second half, revealing the violence that custom was designed to contain.

## Definition

MHG _site_ (masculine noun, plural _site_ or _siten_, from OHG _situ_) denotes
custom, habit, manner, tradition, or proper conduct. It is related to the verb
_sitten_ ("to sit, to be situated"), suggesting the established, settled
character of what _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ designates. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ appears
most frequently in the compound _höfsche Sitte_ ("courtly custom"), the
distinctive code of conduct that governs life at court: how to greet a stranger,
how to receive a gift, how to conduct oneself at table, how to mourn the dead.
But _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ can also describe characterological traits ("grimmiger [[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]" —
fierce by custom) and national practices ("des Landes [[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]n" — the customs of
the land). The term's semantic range moves between the individual (a person's
habitual manner), the collective (a community's traditional practice), and the
normative (the standard of proper conduct). This triple register makes _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_
the poem's most important vocabulary for thinking about the relationship between
individual action and social order.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The concept of _consuetudo_ (custom) was central to medieval legal and social
thought. In a world without comprehensive written law codes, custom — the
accumulated practice of a community over time — had the force of law. Canon
lawyers distinguished between _consuetudo bona_ (good custom, consistent with
divine and natural law) and _consuetudo prava_ (corrupt custom). The
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied participates in this discourse by staging the collision between
different customs: Burgundian _höfsche Sitte_ versus [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s [[isenstein|Isenstein]]
customs, Christian courtly custom versus the residual heroic ethos, the custom
of hospitality versus the custom of revenge. The poem does not resolve these
collisions — it records them, and in recording them, exposes the limits of
custom as a foundation for social order.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's narrative can be mapped as the career of _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ across three
movements. In the **first movement** (Adventures 1–13), _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ is the poem's
dominant social logic: events unfold according to established custom, from
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s courtly reception at [[worms|Worms]] to the tournaments, feasts, and weddings
that structure courtly life. Custom is depicted as effective, beautiful, and
binding — it enables cooperation across difference and produces the poem's most
spectacular displays of social harmony. In the **second movement** (Adventures
14–19), _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ becomes contested: the quarrel of the queens is a dispute about
precedence — about which custom (the custom of the host's wife or the custom
of the higher-ranking husband) takes priority. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
violates the custom of hospitality, the custom of fair combat, and the custom
of loyalty to one's lord's wife — it is a systematic assault on _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ in the
name of a higher custom (loyalty to [[gunther|Gunther]]). In the **third movement**
(Adventures 20–39), _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ is destroyed: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revenge systematically
violates every custom the poem has established — hospitality, truce, mercy to
kin, proper burial — and the poem ends not with the restoration of custom but
with its complete annihilation.

## Notable Passages

> Der Jungfrau höfsche Sitte wär eine Zier allen Fraun.
> — "The maiden's courtly custom would be an ornament to all women" — the
> poem's first deployment of _Sitte_; Kriemhild's custom is exemplary and
> universal, the gold standard of courtly conduct (001-012)

> Was ihm Zucht und Sitte der eigne Sinn gebar!
> — "What discipline and custom his own mind bore!" — Siegfried's _Sitte_
> is self-generated, the product of innate nobility rather than external
> cultivation; the line distinguishes between custom as inheritance and
> custom as personal quality (002-022)

> In dieser Burg ist Sitte, das will ich euch sagen
> — "In this castle it is the custom, I will tell you" — the announcement
> of local _Sitte_ as binding law; to enter Isenstein is to submit to its
> customs, and the warning signals the danger of unfamiliar custom (007-073)

> So grimmiger Sitte pflegt die Königin
> — "Such fierce custom the queen practices" — _Sitte_ as character trait;
> the phrase recurs (006-026, 006-086), linking Kriemhild to a severity that
> will eventually overwhelm her courtly surface

> Nach des Landes Sitte, die zu begehen Pflicht
> — "According to the land's custom, which it is a duty to observe" —
> _Sitte_ as obligation; custom is not optional but a duty (Pflicht),
> carrying the full weight of social compulsion (010-265)

> Ob seiner höfschen Sitte
> — "Because of his courtly custom" — Rüdiger's _höfsche Sitte_ impresses
> the Hunnish court; proper conduct is recognizable across cultural
> boundaries and functions as a form of diplomatic currency (020-315)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's concept of _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ parallels the Old French _coutume_
  that governs social life in the _Chanson de Roland_ and the romances of
  Chrétien de Troyes — but where French romance often treats custom as a
  problem to be overcome (the custom of Logres, the Grail castle's customs),
  the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied presents _[[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]_ as the necessary and fragile foundation
  of collective life
- In the Old Norse _Völsunga saga_, custom is primarily a matter of kinship
  obligation and blood-feud — the elaborate courtly customs of the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied (tournaments, formal greetings, gift-exchange protocols) are
  absent, replaced by the stark customs of revenge and oath-keeping
- The poem's interest in the collision of different customs (Burgundian vs.
  [[isenstein|Isenstein]]) anticipates the ethnographic curiosity of later medieval travel
  literature — customs are observed, compared, and found to be incompatible
  in ways that generate narrative conflict
