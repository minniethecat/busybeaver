# Service and Fealty in the Nibelungenlied

_Dienst_ is the word through which the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied thinks about social
bonds. It is the medium of feudal obligation — "zu aller Art Diensten ward er
ihm gerecht" (he was fit for all kinds of service, 003-226) — and the
language of courtly love — "er erwarb mit großem Dienste, daß sie ihm ward
zu [[gender-and-femininity|Weib]]" (he won her as his wife through great service, 006-140). It is
offered ("entbietet seine Dienste," 009-066), received ("man sah viel Dienst
von ihnen empfahn," 013-076), and sometimes refused ("er nahm es nicht als
Dienst an," 012-020). The poem's catastrophe unfolds when _Dienst_ obligations
collide: [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s service to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] against his service to the
Burgundians, [[hagen|Hagen]]'s service to [[gunther|Gunther]] against any higher claim of
hospitality or humanity. _Dienst_ is what holds the poem's world together,
and it is what tears it apart.

## Definition

MHG _dienst_ (from OHG _dionôst_, "service, servitude, ministry") operates in
three overlapping registers in the poem: (1) **feudal service** — the military
and political obligation of a vassal to his lord, formalized through oath and
rewarded with _[[gratitude-and-reward|Lohn]]_; (2) **courtly service** (_[[gender-and-femininity|Frau]]endienst_) — the knight's
devotion to a lady, performed through deeds of prowess and rewarded (ideally)
with her favor; (3) **general service** — the broader cultural expectation that
persons of rank will offer their capacities (_Dienste_) to those who need them,
whether as messengers, escorts, counselors, or hosts. The verb _dienen_ (to
serve) and the formula _zu Diensten sein_ (to be at someone's service) mark
the voluntary, performative character of _Dienst_: it is not mere submission
but the active offering of one's person and capacities within a framework of
mutual obligation.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

_Dienst_ in the Staufer period was the organizing principle of aristocratic
social life. The _[[feudal-bond|Lehn]]swesen_ (feudal system) structured military and
political relationships through the exchange of service for land; the
_[[minne|Minne]]wesen_ (courtly love culture) structured relations between the sexes
through the exchange of service for favor. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied participates
fully in both discourses while exposing their fragility. The poem's
insistence that _Dienst_ is voluntary — "entbietet seine Dienste" (offers
his services) rather than "ist gezwungen zu dienen" (is forced to serve) —
reflects the cultural ideal that true service must be freely given. But the
poem also shows that voluntary service creates obligations that can become
intolerable: [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] freely swore service to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], and that free oath
destroys him.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem structures its treatment of _Dienst_ around a single question: what
happens when service obligations conflict? The first half of the poem presents
_Dienst_ as unproblematic — [[siegfried|Siegfried]] serves [[gunther|Gunther]], [[gunther|Gunther]] rewards
[[siegfried|Siegfried]], the cycle of service and reward functions smoothly. But [[hagen|Hagen]]'s
refusal to recognize [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s service (012-020) introduces a destabilizing
possibility: what if service is not what it appears to be? And [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s
dilemma in the final adventures makes this possibility catastrophic: what
if service to one lord requires the betrayal of service to another? The poem
offers no resolution. [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] dies. The Burgundians die. _Dienst_ — the
bond that was supposed to hold the world together — becomes the mechanism
of its destruction.

## Notable Passages

> Zu aller Art Diensten ward er ihm gerecht.
> — Siegfried assessed as the complete vassal — the measure of a hero is his capacity for every form of service (003-226)

> Er erwarb mit großem Dienste, daß sie ihm ward zu Weib gegeben.
> — _Dienst_ as the path to love — Siegfried's heroic service wins Kriemhild, the poem's paradigmatic transaction between martial prowess and courtly reward (006-140)

> Er hatte solche Dienste gar selten sonst gethan.
> — The invisibility-cloak service — a service that cannot be acknowledged because it was performed in secret, the seed of the poem's crisis of recognition (007-038)

> Entbietet seine Dienste der Heergeselle mein.
> — Service offered across courts — the diplomatic language of _Dienst_ as the medium through which alliances are forged and maintained (009-066)

> Er nahm es nicht als Dienst an, wenn er Siegfrieden sah.
> — Hagen's refusal — the denial that Siegfried's actions constitute _Dienst_, stripping him of the social recognition that service is supposed to confer (012-020)

> Unser Dienst mit Treue bereit bis in den Tod.
> — Service with loyalty unto death — the oath that binds Rüdiger and destroys him, the poem's most compressed statement of _Dienst_'s double edge (011-011)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Dienst_ as simultaneously social bond
  and destructive force distinguishes it from the Arthurian romance tradition,
  where service (to Arthur, to the Grail, to a lady) is generally rewarded
  with integration into a higher order — in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, service
  leads to death
- In Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_, _Dienst_ is graduated: the hero
  learns to serve properly through a process of education and spiritual
  growth; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied offers no such developmental arc — characters
  serve as they have always served, and the poem's catastrophe is the result
  of service obligations that cannot be reconciled, not of service
  improperly performed
- The Old French _Chanson de Roland_ treats _service_ (servise) primarily
  as military vassalage, with Ganelon's betrayal framed as a failure of
  service; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's _Dienst_ is broader — it includes courtly
  love-service, diplomatic service, and the service of hospitality — and
  its tragedy is that these different registers of service prove
  incompatible
- The concept of _Dienst_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied has no equivalent in the
  Old Norse analogues — the _Völsunga saga_'s vocabulary of loyalty
  (_tryggð_) is kinship-based rather than service-based, reflecting the
  different social organization of the Norse material
