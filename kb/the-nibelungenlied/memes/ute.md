# Ute

Ute is the Queen Mother of Burgundy — the widow of King [[dankrat|Dankrat]], mother of the three Burgundian kings and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], and the matriarch whose wisdom frames the epic's first tragedy. Her interpretation of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s falcon dream opens the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's prophetic register.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Queen of Burgundy (dowager), Queen Mother
- **Affiliation**: [[burgundian-court|Burgundian Court]] at [[worms|Worms]]
- **Family**: [[dankrat|Dankrat]] (husband, deceased king of Burgundy), [[gunther|Gunther]] (eldest son), [[gernot|Gernot]] (second son), [[geiselher|Geiselher]] (third son), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (daughter)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 01 — introduced as [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s mother (001-025)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: The Prophetic Mother (Adventures 1-19)

Ute appears early, interpreting [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s dream of a falcon killed by two eagles as the prophecy that a noble man ([[siegfried|Siegfried]]) will marry her daughter and then be murdered (001-053). She is a constant presence at court — attending the Pentecost festival (005-061, 005-219), accompanying [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] to greet [[siegfried|Siegfried]] (010-044, 010-098), and sending messages to her daughter (012-093, 012-114). After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, Ute fades from the foreground but remains a figure of maternal authority. She is last mentioned receiving [[hagen|Hagen]] at [[worms|Worms]] in Adventure 20, surviving the Burgundian destruction by remaining behind.

### Part II: The Absent Mother (Adventures 20-39)

Ute does not journey to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court and thus survives the cataclysm. She is mentioned in the context of sending [[hagen|Hagen]]'s party on their journey (020-182) and is presumed to remain in [[worms|Worms]] during the final disaster — a rare survivor of the Burgundian house.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Ute embodies maternal care within the constraints of medieval queenship. Her reading of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s dream — "a noble man will die" — is the epic's first act of interpretation: the attempt of a mother to make sense of her daughter's fate.

### Virtues & Flaws

Ute is the most consistently benevolent figure in the epic — wise, dignified, and protective of her children. She suffers no dramatic flaw; her tragedy is that of a mother who outlives nearly all her children.

## Notable Passages

> "Ute said to her: 'The falcon you see is a noble man — may God guard him well, or he will soon be taken from you.'" (001-053)

## Scholarly Notes

- Ute's dream interpretation is a direct adaptation of a common Germanic poetic motif (the prophetic dream) found also in the Old Norse Atlakviða, though there the interpretation serves a different narrative function.
- Her survival makes her structurally analogous to figures in Germanic legend who witness rather than participate in catastrophe — the "lamenting mother" figure.
