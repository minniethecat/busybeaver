# Nibelung Treasure

The Nibelung treasure (Nibelungenhort) is the legendary hoard of gold and jewels central to the plot of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, functioning as both a symbol of power and a catalyst for the epic's tragic events. Won by [[siegfried|Siegfried]] from the Nibelung kings, it passes through multiple hands before being sunk in the [[rhine|Rhine]] by [[hagen|Hagen]], never to be recovered.

## Definition

In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, the Nibelungenhort is a vast treasure of red gold originally belonging to the Nibelung kings Schilbung and Nibelung (003-181). After [[siegfried|Siegfried]] defeats them, he gains control of the hoard along with the land of the Nibelungs — [[nibelungenland|Nibelungenland]] (003-204). The treasure is so immense that it required "twelve wagons to carry it away" from the mountain where it was guarded (003-222).

The hoard encompasses not only gold but also the sword [[balmung|Balmung]] and the invisibility cloak [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]], which [[siegfried|Siegfried]] won from the dwarf [[alberich|Alberich]] (003-219). It represents the concentrated wealth and power of an entire kingdom.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In medieval Germanic epic tradition, treasure hoards function as tangible manifestations of a ruler's power, honor, and legitimacy. The Nibelungenhort reflects the feudal practice of treasure distribution — a lord's ability to give gifts to his followers cemented political bonds. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s act of sinking the hoard is thus not merely theft but a deliberate destruction of the material basis of lordship, rendering the treasure incapable of serving its social function.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The Nibelung treasure drives the narrative across both parts of the epic. It initially establishes [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s heroic credentials and attracts the attention of the Burgundian court. After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder, the hoard becomes the object of contention between [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and [[hagen|Hagen]] — her rightful inheritance versus his political calculation that the treasure would empower [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] to raise an army against Burgundy (019-079).

The sinking of the hoard in the [[rhine|Rhine]] at [[lochheim|Lochheim]] (019-161) transforms the treasure from a concrete object into an enduring symbol of betrayal and loss. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s refusal to reveal its location, even under threat of death in [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court (028-186), cements the treasure's role as the epic's ultimate MacGuffin — its absence drives as much conflict as its presence once did.

## Comparative Notes

- The Nibelungenhort has parallels in the Old Norse Völsunga Saga, where the treasure (Andvaranaut) carries a curse — a motif absent from the German [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, where the hoard itself is not cursed but becomes a source of conflict through human greed and betrayal.
- The motif of gold sunk in the [[rhine|Rhine]] appears in Germanic legend more broadly, connected to the "[[rhine|Rhine]]gold" ([[rhine|Rhein]]gold) of Wagner's operatic adaptation, though Wagner adds the curse element from Norse sources.
- Medieval German law treated buried treasure as property of the crown; [[hagen|Hagen]]'s sinking of the hoard may reflect a legal-political dimension of denying resources to a rival claimant.
