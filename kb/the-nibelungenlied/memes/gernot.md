# Gernot

King Gernot of Burgundy is the second of the three Burgundian brothers, a figure of measured counsel in Part I and fierce combat in Part II. He is introduced among the three kings ruling from [[worms|Worms on the Rhine]] (001-013). While less prominent than [[gunther|Gunther]] and less decisive than [[hagen|Hagen]], Gernot plays a crucial role as a voice of moderation — and his death by [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s hand in Adventure 37 is the moral breaking point of the epic's final battle.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Co-King of Burgundy, alongside [[gunther|Gunther]] and [[geiselher|Geiselher]]
- **Affiliation**: [[burgundian-court|Burgundian Court]] — the three brothers rule jointly
- **Family**: [[ute|Ute]] (mother), [[gunther|Gunther]] (elder brother), [[geiselher|Geiselher]] (younger brother), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (sister)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 01 — named among the three Burgundian kings (001-013)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: The Voice of Counsel (Adventures 1–19)

Gernot is consistently portrayed as the most reasonable of the Burgundian brothers. When [[siegfried|Siegfried]] first arrives at [[worms|Worms]] and challenges the Burgundians, Gernot takes charge of the diplomatic situation, inviting [[siegfried|Siegfried]] to stay in peace (003-308). He advises against conflict and speaks for reconciliation when tensions arise with Saxon warriors. During the preparations for [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s welcome, Gernot is seen managing the court's hospitality (005-097). He stands apart from the murder plot — while [[gunther|Gunther]] hesitates and [[hagen|Hagen]] acts, Gernot is not recorded as complicit in [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death. His role in Part I is defined by presencia and moderation: the co-king who maintains order while his elder brother struggles with kingship.

### Part II: The Tragic Warrior (Adventures 20–39)

In Part II, Gernot accompanies the Burgundian retinue to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court. He fights with distinction in the hall-battle, including against [[iring|Iring]] of [[saxon-land|Denmark]] (035-061). But his defining moment comes in Adventure 37, when [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] — bound by oath to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] but agonized by his friendship with the Burgundians — must enter the battle. Gernot appeals to [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s honor to stand aside, but [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] cannot (037-193, 037-197). When combat becomes inevitable, Gernot strikes [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] down and is himself struck down in the same exchange. The two lie dead together (037-342, 037-350).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Gernot is motivated by the [[triuwe|Triuwe]] of brotherhood and the maintenance of Burgundian honor. He counsels peace in Part I because war serves no purpose; he fights ferociously in Part II because feudal obligation leaves no alternative. His tragedy is that his virtues — loyalty, moderation, courage — are irrelevant in a world where [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] has set an irreversible course toward annihilation.

### Virtues & Flaws

Gernot is the most balanced of the Burgundian brothers: wiser than [[gunther|Gunther]], fiercer than [[geiselher|Geiselher]] when provoked. His flaw, if it can be called one, is that he is a secondary figure in a story driven by protagonists more ruthless and more desperate than he is. He chooses well, acts well — but the epic's logic does not reward good choices.

## Death

Gernot is killed in Adventure 37 during the battle at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court. After [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] — tormented by conflicting oaths — enters the hall against the Burgundians, Gernot engages him. Each kills the other in a mutual death that the poem treats as the most devastating loss of the battle: two honorable men forced by circumstance and obligation to destroy each other (037-342). Their bodies lie together, symbols of the epic's repeated insistence that [[triuwe|Triuwe]], when divided, consumes the loyal as surely as the treacherous.

## Scholarly Notes

- Gernot corresponds to the historical Gundobad (or Godomar), a Burgundian prince of the 5th century, though the historical record is thinner than for [[gunther|Gunther]].
- In the Old Norse tradition, the Burgundian brothers are fewer in number; Gernot's role as a distinct character is a development of the German epic tradition, likely drawn from multiple historical Burgundian kings.
- Gernot's death at [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s hands is one of the epic's most commented-upon scenes, often analyzed as the point at which the poem demonstrates that no moral position — not even honorable conduct — can escape the catastrophe once the cycle of revenge is complete.
