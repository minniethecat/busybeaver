# Feudal Bond

The feudal bond (Middle High German: Lehn) is the foundational political institution of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's world. It defines the relationship between a lord and his vassals: the lord grants land (fief) and protection; the vassal provides military service and loyalty ([[triuwe|Triuwe]]). [[siegmund|Siegmund]] holds his realm as a feudal lord with his Lehn (002-037), and [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s companions are "those in his Lehn" (003-017); [[gunther|Gunther]] likewise summons "those in his Lehn" (003-155).

## Definition

Lehn refers both to the fief itself (the land grant) and to the relationship of vassalage it creates. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, the feudal bond is the organizing principle of political life: kings rule through their vassals, warriors fight for their lords, and the entire social hierarchy rests on chains of feudal obligation extending from the king down through his Lehn.

### Feudal Conflict

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied explores what happens when feudal bonds conflict. [[hagen|Hagen]] is [[gunther|Gunther]]'s vassal, owing him [[triuwe|Triuwe]] — but he is also the murderer of [[siegfried|Siegfried]], [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s husband. When [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] demands justice, [[gunther|Gunther]]'s feudal obligation to protect his vassal [[hagen|Hagen]] conflicts with any obligation he might have to his sister. The feudal bond, designed to create order, becomes the mechanism by which justice is blocked and revenge becomes inevitable.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied was composed during the height of the Hohenstaufen feudal system (12th–13th centuries). The Lehn was the central institution of political and military organization — far more than a property arrangement, it was a personal bond sanctified by oath and ritual.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The epic presents the feudal bond as both necessary and tragic. It enables collective action and political stability, but it also creates conflicting obligations that cannot all be honored. [[gunther|Gunther]]'s protection of [[hagen|Hagen]] is feudally correct but morally disastrous. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied suggests that the feudal system contains within itself the seeds of its own destruction — the very bonds that hold society together also make justice impossible when those at the top are the wrongdoers.
