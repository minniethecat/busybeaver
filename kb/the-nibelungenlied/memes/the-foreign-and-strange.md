# The Foreign and the Strange in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a poem about arrivals. [[siegfried|Siegfried]] arrives at [[worms|Worms]] from
[[xanten|Xanten]]. The Burgundians arrive at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court. And at every arrival, the poem
uses one word to mark what is at stake: _fremde_ (foreign, strange). "Drum sah
man viel der Fremden zu ihnen reiten in das Land" (002-048) — the foreign is
first of all a visual fact, a presence to be seen and assessed. But _fremde_
in the poem is never neutral: it triggers the poem's most elaborate rituals of
hospitality, its most anxious assessments of status, and ultimately its most
lethal failures of recognition. The progression from "den Heimischen und
Fremden bot man [[ere|Ehre]]n da genug" (002-080) — honor offered equally to native and
foreigner — to "finden wir in der Fremde den [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]" (037-240) — finding death in
the foreign land — traces the poem's darkening understanding of what it means
to be a stranger.

## Definition

MHG _vremde_ / _vremder_ (from OHG _fremidi_, "foreign, strange, unknown")
operates across several semantic registers in the poem: (1) the geographical
sense — someone from outside the territory, a foreigner arriving at court;
(2) the social sense — someone not of the household, an outsider to the
kinship group; (3) the qualitative sense — something unfamiliar, unexpected,
or not conforming to known patterns. The poem's characteristic collocation
"Heimischen und Fremden" (natives and foreigners, 002-080, 004-462, 005-010)
treats the two categories as a complete taxonomy of persons — everyone is
either native or foreign — and the poem's social world is organized around
the obligations that arise at the boundary between them.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The twelfth and thirteenth centuries saw intensified contact between Latin
Christendom and its neighbors — the Crusades, Mediterranean trade, the
Mongol expansion — and courtly literature responded with increasingly
elaborate representations of cultural encounter. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's
treatment of _Fremde_ draws on this discourse while grounding it in the
specific social realities of the Staufer court: the arrival of foreign
nobles seeking service, the negotiation of marriage alliances across
territorial boundaries, and the vulnerability of travelers dependent on
local lords for protection. The poem's repeated formula "Heimischen und
Fremden" reflects a legal-cultural distinction operative in medieval
German law codes, where the foreigner (_gast_, _fremder_) had distinct
rights and required specific guarantees of protection.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's geography of foreignness maps onto its narrative structure. The
first half of the poem (Adventures 1–19) is set in the Burgundian world,
where "Heimischen und Fremden" is a distinction managed by the court's
rituals of hospitality: honor is offered, chambers are prepared, gifts
are exchanged. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s integration — from threatening stranger to
valued ally — demonstrates the system working. But the poem's second half
(Adventures 20–39) systematically reverses this: the Burgundians themselves
become the foreigners at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, and the hospitality they once
extended is now demanded of them. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s refusal to ferry strangers
(025-227) is the pivot: at the [[danube|Danube]], the man who once assessed [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
foreignness now refuses to extend passage to others. The poem's final
adventures collapse the distinction between _heimisch_ and _fremde_
entirely — in the burning hall, everyone is a foreigner, and "in der Fremde
den [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]] finden" (037-240) is the fate not only of the Burgundians but of
the poem's entire social world.

## Notable Passages

> Drum sah man viel der Fremden zu ihnen reiten in das Land.
> — The poem's first mention of foreigners — Siegfried's party arrives at Worms, and the court comes to the windows to see (002-048)

> Den Heimischen und Fremden bot man Ehren da genug.
> — The ideal of equal honor — the Burgundian court at its best, before the distinctions between native and foreign become lethal (002-080)

> "Es kann ein Fremder sein."
> — Kriemhild's caution — the foreigner as potential threat, the instinct that will be proven tragically correct and catastrophically wrong (017-033)

> Da küsste die Fremde die Markgräfin auf den Mund.
> — The ritual of welcome — the kiss that transforms the foreign woman into an honored guest (021-096)

> Drum fahr ich keinen Fremden hinüber in ihr Land.
> — Hagen's refusal — the ferryman who will not transport strangers, the dark inversion of the hospitality that opened the poem (025-227)

> "finden wir in der Fremde den Tod"
> — The poem's final word on foreignness — the foreign land as the place of death, the destination that undoes all arrivals (037-240)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Fremde_ as a category that migrates from
  the periphery to the center — the foreign becomes inescapable — contrasts
  with the Arthurian romance's typical pattern, where the foreign (adventure,
  the forest, the giant) is encountered and then left behind as the hero
  returns to the court
- In Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_, the category of _fremde_ is
  explicitly theological: the Grail community is both native (to Munsalvaesche)
  and foreign (to the world); the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's _Fremde_ is entirely social
  and political, with no transcendental referent
- The Old Norse analogues treat foreignness differently: in the _Völsunga
  saga_, the foreign is primarily genealogical — characters are foreign because
  they come from different kinship groups — rather than spatial or cultural as
  in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
- The poem's movement from "Heimischen und Fremden" (paired, distinguished,
  manageable) to "in der Fremde den [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]" (the foreign alone, lethal) is a
  structural arc unique to the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied among medieval epics; it
  parallels the poem's treatment of peace, hospitality, and other social
  goods that are systematically stripped away
