# Wedding and Marriage in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied contains some of the most elaborate wedding feasts in
medieval literature. Two great _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]en_ structure the poem's narrative arc:
the double wedding at [[worms|Worms]] (Adventure 10), where [[siegfried|Siegfried]] marries [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]
and [[gunther|Gunther]] marries [[brunhild|Brunhild]], and [[etzel|Etzel]]'s wedding to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (Adventure 22),
which sets the poem's second half in motion. A third wedding — that of
[[geiselher|Giselher]] and [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s daughter — seals the alliance between Burgundians and
Huns in Adventure 27. Each _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_ is more than a marriage: it is a
performance of power, a transfer of wealth, a test of hospitality, and a stage
on which the poem's tensions — between public ceremony and private feeling,
between alliance and enmity — are acted out. The poem traces weddings from
celebration ("So prächtig ihre [[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]] am [[rhine|Rhein]] war bekannt," 011-085) to
massacre ("Eine üble [[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]] ist es," 036-160), mapping the degeneration of
its central social ritual into its central scene of destruction.

## Definition

MHG _hôchzît_ (from _hôch_ "high" + _zît_ "time," i.e., "high festival")
denotes both the wedding ceremony and the feast that accompanies it. The
word's etymology points to its function: a _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_ is a "high time," a
moment when ordinary time is suspended and the community gathers to witness
and celebrate a transformative social act. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_
encompasses the entire wedding complex — the betrothal, the ceremony, the
procession, the feast, the tournaments, and the distribution of gifts. A
_[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_ is measured by its duration ("Diese [[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]] währte siebzehn Tage
lang," 022-125) and its magnificence ("Der solche [[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]] hielte: es ist uns
unbekannt," 022-127 — never was such a wedding held). But the poem also uses
_[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_ ironically: "Eine üble [[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]" (036-160) and "Eine arge [[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]"
(036-176) describe the slaughter in [[etzel|Etzel]]'s hall, where the wedding feast has
become a feast of blood.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The wedding feast was one of the most important social rituals of the medieval
nobility. In the Staufer period, a royal or princely _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_ was a
political event as much as a personal one: it publicly sealed alliances,
displayed wealth through feasting and gift-giving, and provided the occasion
for tournaments and diplomatic negotiations. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's wedding
scenes draw on this historical reality while amplifying it to epic scale.
The poems seventeen-day wedding (022-125) exceeds any historical _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_
in duration; the narrator's insistence that no king ever held such a wedding
(022-127) marks the poem's weddings as legendary rather than documentary. But
the poem also draws on a darker tradition: the motif of the "murder-wedding"
(_Mordhochzeit_) found in Germanic legend, where a wedding feast becomes the
occasion for slaughter. By calling the final battle a _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_, the poem
activates this tradition and reveals the wedding ritual as always already
shadowed by its violent double.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's three weddings trace a descending arc from celebration to
catastrophe. The first wedding (Adventure 10) is magnificent but already
troubled: [[brunhild|Brunhild]] weeps (010-177), [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s invisible aid remains a
secret, and the narrator's account of the wedding's conclusion ("So endete
die [[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]," 010-468) carries an undertone of relief — the celebration is
over, but its consequences are just beginning. The second wedding (Adventure
22) is structurally hollow: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief for [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is private, her
consent to marry [[etzel|Etzel]] is strategic, and the magnificence of the feast masks
the absence of joy. The third "wedding" (Adventures 36–39) is not a wedding
at all but the poem's most savage irony — the _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_ that began as a
celebration of alliance has become the scene of total destruction. This arc
reveals _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]_ as the poem's master metaphor for the relationship between
social form and social reality: the forms persist (the feast, the hall, the
gathering of guests) even after their content has been hollowed out and
replaced by violence.

## Notable Passages

> Bei eines Fürsten Hochzeit: ich glaubte schwerlich daran.
> — At a prince's wedding: I would hardly have believed it — the narrator's
> expression of wonder at the magnificence of the Burgundian double wedding;
> the _Hochzeit_ as a scene that exceeds belief (010-112)

> So endete die Hochzeit: da schied von dannen mancher Mann.
> — So the wedding ended: many a man departed from there — the formal
> closing of the celebration; the departure of guests marks the transition
> from celebration to the long aftermath that will become the poem's
> second half (010-468)

> Diese Hochzeit währte siebzehn Tage lang.
> — This wedding lasted seventeen days — the duration of Etzel's _Hochzeit_
> as a marker of its magnificence; the number is legendary, exceeding any
> historical precedent (022-125)

> Der solche Hochzeit hielte: es ist uns unbekannt.
> — Who might hold such a wedding: it is unknown to us — the narrator's
> insistence on the unparalleled nature of Etzel's wedding; the superlative
> marks both the height of Hunnish power and the height of the poem's
> irony, since this wedding will end in massacre (022-127)

> Eine üble Hochzeit ist es, zu der die Königin uns lud.
> — It is an evil wedding to which the queen invited us — the Burgundians'
> recognition that the _Hochzeit_ has become a trap; the word that once
> meant celebration now means death (036-160)

> Eine arge Hochzeit hat uns meine Schwester Kriemhild bereitet.
> — My sister Kriemhild has prepared a terrible wedding for us — the
> _Hochzeit_ named directly as the instrument of vengeance; the family
> bond (_meine Schwester_) and the wedding bond collapse into each other
> (036-176)

> Das war ihm bei der Hochzeit die allergrößte Noth.
> — That was for him at the wedding the greatest distress of all — Etzel's
> anguish at the destruction of his hall; the _Hochzeit_ that was meant to
> be his triumph has become his tragedy (038-218)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's double wedding (Adventure 10) parallels the double
  wedding at the conclusion of Hartmann von Aue's _Erec_, where Erec and
  Enite's wedding is celebrated alongside the weddings of eighty other
  couples — but where _Erec_ uses the multiple wedding as a figure of
  restored harmony, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's double wedding plants the seeds
  of discord
- The motif of the "murder-wedding" appears in the Old Norse _Atlakviða_,
  where Atli's invitation to the Burgundians is a transparent trap — the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied complicates this tradition by making [[etzel|Etzel]] innocent of
  [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s intentions, transforming the murder-wedding from a story of
  barbarian treachery into a story of courtly vengeance
- The seventeen-day duration of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s wedding (022-125) echoes the
  biblical and classical tradition of the week-long wedding feast (Genesis
  29:27, the wedding of Peleus and Thetis) — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied claims
  for its _[[feast-and-festival|Hochzeit]]en_ a duration that places them in the company of
  mythological rather than historical weddings
- In Wolfram's _Parzival_, the wedding of Parzival and Condwiramurs is
  described with spiritual imagery — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's weddings are
  resolutely worldly, celebrations of wealth, power, and social display
  that make no gesture toward transcendence
