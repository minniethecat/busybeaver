# Iring

Iring, Margrave of [[saxon-land|Denmark]], is one of [[Etzel]]'s most valiant vassals and a warrior of exceptional courage. In Adventure 35, he volunteers to face [[Hagen]] in single combat and fights a remarkable sequence of duels — first with [[hagen|Hagen]], then with King [[Gunther]], then with the young prince [[Geiselher]] — wounding [[hagen|Hagen]] seriously before falling to the combined might of the Burgundian champions. His death is one of the epic's most sustained and heroic combat sequences.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Margrave (Markgraf) of [[saxon-land|Denmark]], vassal of [[Etzel]]
- **Affiliation**: [[Etzel|Hun Court]]
- **Family**: None mentioned in the text
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 22 — [[betrayal-and-deception|List]]ed among [[etzel|Etzel]]'s vassals alongside [[Hawart]] (022-038)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: Absence (Adventures 1–19)

Iring does not appear in the first part of the epic, which takes place in the Burgundian realm. As a Danish margrave in [[etzel|Etzel]]'s service, he enters the story only when the action shifts to the Hunnish court.

### Part II: Warrior and Martyr (Adventures 20–39)

Iring is introduced in Adventure 22 as a warrior of noted swiftness — "Iring the swift, free of all falsehood" (022-038). In Adventure 29, he is named alongside [[Hawart]] as one of the "chosen warriors" (*Degen auserkannt*) present at [[Etzel]]'s court during the Burgundians' visit (029-197).

Iring's defining hour comes in Adventure 35, which opens with his dramatic decision to confront [[Hagen]] directly. As the hall-battle rages and [[etzel|Etzel]]'s warriors fall one after another, Iring steps forward and declares his intent (035-001)(035-009). He arms himself in knightly fashion (035-013) and advances, with comrades following in support (035-016).

The ensuing combat is described in vivid, almost cinematic detail. Iring engages [[Hagen]] first, striking blows that ring through the hall (035-046). Though [[hagen|Hagen]] remains unwounded in their first exchange (035-049), Iring then turns to face King [[Gunther]] (035-058). Their duel is fierce, and Iring nearly sends [[gunther|Gunther]] to his death (035-064). The young [[Geiselher]] then enters the fray, calling out to Iring (035-069), but Iring survives his assault as well (035-076).

In a remarkable feat, Iring returns to [[Hagen]] and this time succeeds where before he failed — he strikes [[hagen|Hagen]] through the helmet, drawing blood (035-095). The wound is the first significant injury [[hagen|Hagen]] has sustained in the battle, and it marks Iring as a warrior of the highest calibre. He swings his shield over his head and fights his way back to his own lines, still unwounded (035-101)(035-105). [[Kriemhild]] herself thanks him for the blow he has dealt to her great enemy (035-109).

Yet Iring's triumph is short-lived. [[hagen|Hagen]], enraged by his wound, mocks the Dane's achievement (035-120), and Iring, standing against the wind to cool himself in his armour (035-121), declares his intention to try once more (035-125). His second assault is his last: his strength fails him, and he is cut down (035-136). The poet's terse conclusion — "Then the hero Iring found his strength of no avail" — encapsulates the tragic futility of individual valour in the face of the epic's implacable violence.

## Character Analysis

### The Exemplary Vassal

Iring represents the ideal of the loyal vassal who fights not from personal hatred but from feudal obligation. He has no quarrel with the Burgundians — his devotion is to [[Etzel]] and, by extension, to [[Kriemhild]]'s cause. When he steps forward in Adventure 35, it is with a formal declaration and the ritual arming of a knight (035-013), framing his challenge as a matter of honour rather than passion.

### Valour Without Victory

Iring's combat sequence is structurally the most elaborate single-combat passage in the hall-battle episodes. He fights three of the four principal Burgundian warriors in succession and wounds the most formidable of them all. Yet he dies. His arc is thus the purest expression of the epic's tragic vision: courage, skill, and determination are not enough. The heroic code promises glory, but the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* delivers only death.

## Death

Iring dies in Adventure 35, cut down by [[Hagen]] after a second reckless assault. Having already wounded [[hagen|Hagen]] and fought three Burgundian champions to a standstill, Iring returns to the fray once more — a decision the poet presents as driven by honour rather than tactical calculation (035-125). His strength gives out, and he falls (035-136). His death, like so many in the epic, is the result of the heroic imperative itself: the code that demands a warrior prove his worth again and again, until proof becomes fatal.

## Scholarly Notes

- Iring of [[saxon-land|Denmark]] is a figure drawn from Germanic legendary history. The name "Iring" appears in Widukind of Corvey's 10th-century *Res gestae Saxonicae* as the eponymous ancestor of the Iring tribe, and a Milky Way constellation was known in Old High German as *Irings strâza* ("Iring's Way").
- His designation as Margrave of [[saxon-land|Denmark]] (*Markgraf aus Dänenland*) reflects the geography of the epic's imagined Hunnish empire, which extends its vassal network as far as the Danish marches.
- Iring's combat sequence is notable for its structural symmetry — three duels, a retreat, a wounding blow, and a second fatal advance — a pattern that mirrors the larger rhythm of the epic: advance, achievement, retreat, overreach, and death.
