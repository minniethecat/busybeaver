# Gotelind

Gotelind is the wife of [[rudiger|Rüdiger von Bechelaren]] and the Margravine of Bechlaren. She embodies the ideal of courtly hospitality in the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied*, receiving [[Kriemhild]] with warmth and generosity during the queen's journey to marry [[Etzel]], and later welcoming [[Hagen]] and the Burgundian knights with gifts and honour. Her refined courtesy stands in quiet contrast to the violence that consumes the men around her.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Margravine (Markgräfin) of [[bechelaren|Bechlaren]]
- **Affiliation**: Margraviate of [[bechelaren|Bechlaren]], vassal-state under [[Etzel]]'s Hun Empire
- **Family**: Wife of [[rudiger|Rüdiger von Bechelaren]]; kinswoman of [[dietrich]] — Dietrich calls her "my noble cousin's child" (038-323)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 20 — Awaiting [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s return at [[bechelaren|Bechlaren]] (020-081)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: Absence (Adventures 1–19)

Gotelind does not appear in the first part of the epic, which is centred on the Burgundian court at [[Worms]]. Her world — the margraviate of [[bechelaren|Bechlaren]] on the [[danube|Danube]] frontier — belongs to the Hunnish sphere and enters the narrative only when the action moves eastward.

### Part II: Hostess and Witness (Adventures 20–39)

Gotelind enters the story in Adventure 20, when [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] returns from his embassy to [[Worms]] and sends word ahead that he is bringing [[Kriemhild]] to [[bechelaren|Bechlaren]] (020-067). Gotelind greets the Burgundian queen with unfeigned warmth: she comes to meet [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] personally, embracing her as a welcome guest (021-059)(021-079). The poet describes her as "the fair Gotelind" (*die schöne Gotelind*), an epithet that links her to the poem's ideal of courtly femininity.

During [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s stay at [[bechelaren|Bechlaren]], Gotelind presides over the household with grace and generosity. She presents gifts to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s attendants, including [[Eckewart]] (021-094), and her palace becomes a space of respite — a brief interlude of peace before the queen's fateful arrival at [[Etzel]]'s court. The bond formed between the two women endures: in Adventure 24, [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] and Gotelind send their greetings to the wedding celebrations (024-011).

Gotelind reappears during the Burgundians' journey eastward in Adventures 26–27. She welcomes [[Hagen]] to [[bechelaren|Bechlaren]] with the same hospitality she once showed [[Kriemhild]], and the encounter includes one of the most symbolically charged gift-exchanges of the epic: Gotelind offers [[hagen|Hagen]] a shield — the very shield of her kinsman Nantwin, whom [[hagen|Hagen]] had slain in battle (027-189). [[hagen|Hagen]] accepts it, in a moment that intertwines courtesy with the memory of violence.

Gotelind's final mention comes in Adventure 38, when [[dietrich]], mourning the slaughter of his warriors, names her as his kinswoman (038-323). The reference ties her back to the web of relationships shattered by the catastrophe.

## Character Analysis

### The Ideal Hostess

Gotelind is the poem's most fully realised portrait of a noblewoman who is neither a queen nor a warrior-maiden. Her domain is the household, and her weapon is hospitality. Where [[Kriemhild]]'s grief leads to destruction and [[Brunhild]]'s pride leads to conflict, Gotelind's generosity heals and sustains. She represents a different model of female power — one rooted in the rituals of welcome, gift-giving, and emotional labour that bind the feudal world together.

### Hospitality and Its Costs

Gotelind's gift of Nantwin's shield to [[Hagen]] (027-189) is emblematic of the epic's complex moral vision. On the surface, it is a gesture of supreme courtesy. Beneath the surface, it carries the memory of [[hagen|Hagen]]'s violence against her own kin. The shield becomes, in a sense, a silent accusation — and [[hagen|Hagen]]'s acceptance of it an act of remarkable audacity. The scene encapsulates the poem's understanding that courtesy and violence are not opposites but intertwined threads in the fabric of heroic society.

## Death

Gotelind does not die in the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied*. She survives the catastrophe, though her life is devastated by its consequences: her husband [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] is killed in Adventure 37, and her kinsman [[dietrich]] loses his entire retinue in Adventure 38. The epic leaves her fate unspoken.

## Scholarly Notes

- Gotelind is part of a pattern of noblewomen in the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* who model alternative forms of female agency to the violent queenship of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and [[brunhild|Brunhild]]. Along with [[Ute]], she represents the quieter virtues of maternal and domestic care.
- The shield-giving scene (027-189) has attracted considerable scholarly attention as a nexus of gift-economy, blood-feud memory, and gender dynamics — Gotelind acts without [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s permission, exercising a form of autonomous agency that complicates any simple reading of her as merely a passive hostess.
- Her name (Gotelind, from OHG *got* "God" + *lind* "soft, gentle") is etymologically a foil to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (*grim* "mask, helmet" + *hilt* "battle") — their names encode a contrast between gentleness and martial ferocity.
