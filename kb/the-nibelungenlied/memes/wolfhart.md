# Wolfhart

Wolfhart is a young and impetuous warrior in the retinue of [[dietrich]], known throughout the epic for his fiery temper and exceptional prowess in battle. He is the nephew of [[Hildebrand|Meister Hildebrand]], Dietrich's master-at-arms. Wolfhart's impatience and aggression repeatedly escalate tensions at [[Etzel]]'s court, and in the final catastrophe of Adventure 38 he fights with such berserk fury that he must be cut down by his own uncle — a death that stands as one of the poem's most tragic kin-slayings.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Knight ([[heroic-code|Recke]], Degen) in [[dietrich]]'s retinue
- **Affiliation**: The warband of [[dietrich|Dietrich von Bern]]
- **Family**: Nephew (sister's son) of [[Hildebrand|Meister Hildebrand]]; [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] is repeatedly called his *Oheim* (uncle)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 28 — Preparing the horses for Dietrich's company (028-097)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: Pre-Arrival (Adventures 1–19)

Like his uncle [[Hildebrand]], Wolfhart does not appear in the first part of the epic. His story belongs entirely to the Hunnish court and the events leading to the destruction of the Burgundians.

### Part II: The Fall of the Burgundians (Adventures 20–39)

Wolfhart first appears in Adventure 28 as part of Dietrich's retinue, characterized immediately by his martial readiness — he leads out the horses for the company (028-097). In Adventure 29, he is named alongside [[Dankwart]] among the warriors present at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court (029-199).

Wolfhart's hot temper becomes a defining trait as tensions mount. In Adventure 33, when the Burgundians grow fearful of [[Kriemhild]]'s machinations, Wolfhart scorns their caution with dismissive words (033-173). His impetuosity reaches its peak in Adventure 38, the final catastrophe. He insists on going to the hall to confront the Burgundians directly, overruling the more cautious counsel of his uncle (038-017). When [[Hildebrand]] warns him to go armed, Wolfhart retorts with characteristic defiance (038-045)(038-057).

As the general slaughter erupts, Wolfhart becomes an engine of destruction. He charges [[Volker]] the minstrel and is struck back with such force that sparks fly from the blow (038-168)(038-172). The text describes him cutting a swath through the battlefield — "many good knights had to fall before Wolfhart's hand" (038-190) — and carving his way through the press of warriors (038-233). He engages [[Geiselher]], the youngest Burgundian prince, exchanging blows in a duel that leaves both wounded (038-237)(038-241)(038-249).

Gravely wounded, Wolfhart continues fighting until he is struck down by his own uncle [[Hildebrand]] — the old master-at-arms who had tried in vain to restrain the young warrior's fury (038-259)(038-289). His fall marks one of the epic's darkest moments, as the bonds of kinship dissolve into the chaos of feudal warfare.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Wolfhart is driven by the heroic code of honor (*êre*) in its most unmediated form. Where [[Hildebrand]] tempers courage with wisdom, Wolfhart embodies the impetuous aggression of youth — he seeks battle not from malice but because fighting is the natural expression of a warrior's identity. His repeated insistence on confronting the Burgundians directly, against the counsel of his elders, reflects a value system in which caution is indistinguishable from cowardice.

### Virtues & Flaws

Wolfhart's courage is beyond question — the text consistently names him "the bold" (*der kühne*) and "the fierce" (*der grimme*), epithets that mark him as a warrior of the first rank. Yet his defining flaw is *unmâze* (excess, lack of measure): he cannot modulate his aggression, cannot heed wiser counsel, and ultimately cannot be restrained by any bond — not even the bond of kinship with [[Hildebrand]]. His death at his uncle's hands is the logical endpoint of a heroic ethos that prizes individual prowess above all other values.

## Death

Wolfhart is killed in Adventure 38 by his own uncle [[Hildebrand]] during the final battle in [[Etzel]]'s hall. After receiving a deep wound from [[Geiselher]] (038-249), Wolfhart continues fighting until [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]], seeing no other way to stop his rampaging nephew, strikes him down (038-259)(038-289). [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]'s lament — "Alas, dear Wolfhart, that I have lost you" (038-353) — closes the arc of their relationship with devastating finality.

## Scholarly Notes

- Wolfhart is a figure unique to the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* and its immediate tradition; he does not appear in the older *[[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]slied* or the Norse *Thidrekssaga*, suggesting he may be a creation of the epic poet or a late addition to the Dietrich cycle.
- The uncle-nephew duel between [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] and Wolfhart structurally mirrors the father-son duel of the *[[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]slied*, transposing the tragic kin-slaying onto a collateral rather than direct line — a shift that may reflect the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* poet's preference for embedding tragedy within the feudal retinue rather than the nuclear family.
- Wolfhart's epithets — *kühn* (bold), *grimm* (fierce), *stark* (strong) — form a lexicon of heroic masculinity that the poet uses to build the character almost entirely through martial attributes, with minimal interiority.
