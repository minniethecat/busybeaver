# Betrayal and Deception in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a poem built on betrayals. Its plot advances through a
sequence of deceptions, each one generating the conditions for the next:
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s use of the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]] to subdue [[brunhild]] on [[gunther|Gunther]]'s behalf
(007-152); [[hagen|Hagen]]'s discovery and exploitation of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s vulnerability
(014-250); [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s invitation to her brothers, concealing her intention to
destroy them (028-242). The poem traces an arc from heroic deception in the
service of marriage-alliance to murderous deception in the service of revenge,
suggesting that the two are not as distinct as the courtly world would like to
believe.

## Definition

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's vocabulary of betrayal is rich and precise. _Verrat_
denotes treason proper — the violation of a bond of loyalty. _Untreue_
(unfaithfulness) emphasizes the breach of _triuwe_, the foundational virtue of
feudal and personal loyalty. _List_ (cunning, stratagem) occupies a more
ambiguous register: it can denote heroic resourcefulness ([[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s use of
the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]]) or murderous trickery ([[hagen|Hagen]]'s false promise of protection).
The poem's tragedy arises from the degradation of _List_ — what begins as
courtly stratagem in the service of social bonds ends as annihilation.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

_[[triuwe|Triuwe]]_ (loyalty, fidelity) was the central ethical term of medieval German
courtly literature. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's systematic exploration of _untriuwe_
is therefore not merely a plot device but an ethical argument: it demonstrates
what happens when the foundational virtue of the feudal world is abandoned.
The poem's insistence that betrayal is not an aberration but a structural
feature of the heroic world — that _triuwe_ and _untriuwe_ produce each other
— is its most radical ethical claim.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Betrayal in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied follows a chain of contamination. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
deception of [[brunhild|Brünhild]] (heroic _List_ in the service of marriage-alliance)
creates the secret that [[hagen|Hagen]] exploits. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s betrayal of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
(violation of hospitality and personal trust) creates [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief, which
hardens into an even larger betrayal: the invitation to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court,
ostensibly for reconciliation, actually for annihilation. Each act of betrayal
claims to be responding to a prior one, but the chain has no clear origin —
the poem suggests that the heroic world contains betrayal as a permanent
possibility, waiting to be activated.

## Notable Passages

> So huben an zu sinnen auf Untreu und Verrat.
> — The poem's first explicit naming of betrayal: Hagen begins to plot (014-250)

> Gelobten mit Untreuen ein Birschen in den Tann.
> — A hunting expedition promised in bad faith — the poem's most concentrated phrase of betrayal (016-002)

> Die da gerathen hatten mit Untreue seinen Tod.
> — The conspirators who achieved Siegfried's death through faithlessness (016-296)

> "wohl behüten vor ihr mit den Listen mein."
> — Siegfried's stratagem against Brünhild — the enabling deception that sets the tragedy in motion (007-152)

## Comparative Notes

- The Old Norse tradition (especially _Völsunga saga_ and _Brot af Sigurðarkviðu_)
  treats Sigurd's death more explicitly as a breach of oath — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
  softens this into "Untreue" without the legal specificity of Norse oath-breaking
- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of betrayal is structurally similar to the
  _Chanson de Roland_'s treatment of Ganelon's treason, but where Ganelon is a
  single traitor tried and executed, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied distributes betrayal
  across its entire cast
- The poem's chain-of-betrayals structure anticipates the Renaissance revenge
  tragedy — each act of vengeance is itself a betrayal, generating the
  conditions for the next
