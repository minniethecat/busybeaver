# Tuln

Tuln (Tulna in the Middle High German text) is a town on the [[danube|Danube]] in the Ostarrîchi (Austrian) land, where [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] stops during her bridal journey from [[worms|Worms]] to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court. Tuln represents a transitional space in the epic's geography — the first place in the east where the Burgundian queen encounters the "foreign customs" (fremde [[courtly-breeding-and-discipline|Sitte]]) of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s people, marking her symbolic passage from one world into another.

## Location & Geography

- **Type**: Fortified river town on the [[danube|Danube]]
- **Ruler**: Within the margraviate of [[rudiger|Rüdiger of Bechelaren]], under the broader sovereignty of [[etzel|Etzel]]
- **Real-world counterpart**: Tulln an der [[danube|Donau]], Lower Austria — a historic town on the southern bank of the [[danube|Danube]], approximately 30 km west of Vienna. Tulln was an important Roman and medieval settlement (Comagena) and a significant [[danube|Danube]] crossing point.
- **Associated characters**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (passes through on her bridal journey), [[etzel|Etzel]]'s vanguard (meets her here), Ramung of Wallachia (among the princes who greet her)

### Strategic Significance

Tuln's narrative function is as a threshold marker. After departing [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]] — where [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s household offered the last warmth of the Burgundian world — Tuln is the first place where [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] enters the cultural orbit of the Huns. The poem notes that at Tuln she "became acquainted with many a foreign custom she had never before seen" (022-023). This brief line distills the epic's theme of cultural and geographical transition: Tuln is the place where the west ends and the east begins, where [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] begins her transformation from Burgundian princess to Hunnic queen. The twenty-four princes who ride ahead to greet her — led by Ramung of Wallachia — embody the exotic, multi-ethnic character of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s empire, foreshadowing the cosmopolitan court she is about to enter.

## Notable Descriptions

> "Eine Stadt liegt an der Donau im Oesterreicherland, / Die ist geheißen Tulna." — "A city lies on the Danube in the Austrian land, / It is called Tulna." (022-021–022)
>
> The narrative voice introduces Tuln with geographical precision: a city on the Danube, in Austria. This formula — naming the river, the region, the town — is characteristic of the *Nibelungenlied*'s topographical style.

> "Da ward ihr bekannt / Manche fremde Sitte, die sie noch niemals sah." — "There she became acquainted / With many a foreign custom she had never before seen." (022-022–023)
>
> These two lines, embedded within the geographical description, signal Tuln's symbolic function: it is the threshold where [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s cultural horizon expands beyond the Burgundian world. The "foreign customs" (fremde Sitte) refer to the multi-ethnic, non-Christian character of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s retinue — a recurring motif in the epic's depiction of the Huns.

## Historical / Legendary Notes

- Tulln (Roman Comagena) was an important [[danube|Danube]] crossing and fortification from the Roman period onward. During the High Middle Ages, it was a Babenberg margravial seat and a significant trading post on the [[danube|Danube]] route.
- The poem's reference to "Oesterreicherland" (the land of Austria, i.e., the Ostarrîchi or Eastern March) at 022-021 is significant: it reflects the political geography of the late 12th/early 13th century, when the Babenberg margraves of Austria were at their height, and the [[danube|Danube]] route through Tulln was a major artery of east-west travel.
- Tuln's location between [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]] ([[bechelaren|Pöchlarn]], ~50 km upstream) and Vienna (~30 km downstream) accurately reflects the real-world geography of the [[danube|Danube]] valley in Lower Austria, demonstrating the epic poet's detailed knowledge of the region.
- The mention of "Ramung, der Herzog aus Walachenland" (Ramung, Duke of Wallachia) among [[etzel|Etzel]]'s vanguard at Tuln reflects the historical ethnic diversity of the Hungarian sphere of influence, where Vlach (proto-Romanian) leaders served under the Árpád kings.
