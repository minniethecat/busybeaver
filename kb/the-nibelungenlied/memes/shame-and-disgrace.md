# Shame and Disgrace in the Nibelungenlied

If _[[ere|Êre]]_ (honor) is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's supreme positive value, _Schande_
(shame, disgrace) is its absolute negative. The poem's characters are
motivated less by the desire for honor than by the terror of shame. "Daß wirs
nicht Schande haben" (that we not have shame from it, 006-104) — the fear of
disgrace governs decisions from the courtly to the catastrophic. [[brunhild|Brünhild]]'s
accusation of _Schande_ against [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] triggers the queens' quarrel
(Adventure 14). The threat of _Schande_ is [[hagen|Hagen]]'s justification for
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder. And the poem's final act is haunted by the question of
whether the Burgundians will die "ohne Schanden" (without shame, 032-040) —
the only victory still available to them.

## Definition

_Schande_ in MHG denotes shame, disgrace, dishonor, and the public exposure
of moral failure. It is the opposite of _[[ere|Êre]]_: where honor is the public
recognition of worth, shame is the public recognition of unworthiness. The
phrase "vor allen Schanden bewahrt" (preserved from all shames, 002-006,
004-376) is a formula of complete honor — to be free of shame is to be
honorable. "Mit Schanden" (with shame, in disgrace) marks actions that
violate the moral code: "Mit Schanden geschieden sollt ihr von guten
[[heroic-code|Recke]]n sein" (with shame you shall depart from good warriors, 016-304).
"Arge Schande" (dire disgrace, 014-164) is the strongest form — the shame
that cannot be repaired.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the honor-based culture of the medieval aristocracy, shame was the social
equivalent of death. A knight who suffered public disgrace lost not merely
reputation but social existence — his word ceased to carry weight, his
protection ceased to extend to his dependents, and his presence became a source
of contamination rather than honor. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's characters inhabit
this moral universe: they would rather die than live with shame, and the
poem's catastrophe is driven by the impossibility of reconciling competing
claims to honor once the accusation of shame has been made public.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's shame-economy operates through a precise mechanism. An accusation
of _Schande_ is made — [[brunhild|Brünhild]] calls [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] a _Kebse_ (014-104). The
accusation is public — spoken before witnesses, at the door of the [[church-and-religion|Münster]].
Once public, the accusation cannot be withdrawn — [[brunhild|Brünhild]] doubles down:
"Solcher großen Schanden, ich minne nie wieder dich" (014-164). The accused
must respond — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s response triggers the chain of events that leads to
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death. The poem's genius is to show how the fear of shame, which
is supposed to maintain social order, becomes the engine of its destruction.
Every character acts to avoid _Schande_, and every action makes the final
_Schande_ — the annihilation of the Burgundian royal house — more inevitable.

## Notable Passages

> Er war vor allen Schanden immer wohl bewahrt.
> — Siegfried is preserved from all shames — the poem's founding declaration of his perfect honor (002-006)

> "Daß wirs nicht Schande haben, hört man künftig von uns sagen."
> — The fear of shame as motivation — reputation is the currency of the heroic world (006-104)

> "Solcher großen Schanden, ich minne nie wieder dich."
> — Brünhild's accusation against Kriemhild — the shame that cannot be forgiven or forgotten (014-164)

> "Mit Schanden geschieden sollt ihr von guten Recken sein."
> — Even murder is framed in the language of shame-prevention — Hagen's chilling rhetoric (016-304)

> "Daß ihr ohn Schanden erliegt in wehrlichem Tod."
> — Death without shame — the final victory, to die honorably in battle (032-040)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's shame-economy parallels the role of _níð_ (shame,
  insult) in Old Norse literature — the _níðstöng_ (shame-pole) of the sagas
  and the fatal consequences of public insult in _Njáls saga_ and _Gísla saga_
  — but the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied integrates shame into a courtly framework that the
  Norse texts lack
- The queens' quarrel (Adventure 14) is the most elaborate treatment of
  female shame-accusation in medieval heroic literature — the _Chanson de
  Roland_ has no equivalent scene; even Chrétien de Troyes's romances, which
  are deeply concerned with honor and shame, do not stage a public
  confrontation between two queens of this intensity
- The formula "vor allen Schanden bewahrt" (preserved from all shames) is
  unique to the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied and related MHG heroic poetry (_Kudrun_,
  _Dietrichs Flucht_) — it belongs to a specific South German courtly-heroic
  vocabulary of honor that does not appear in the Norse or Anglo-Saxon
  traditions
