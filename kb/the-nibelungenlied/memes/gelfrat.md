**Gelfrat** is a Bavarian margrave (border count) and lord who, together with his brother [[else-bavarian|Else]], controls the territory along the Danube that the Burgundians must cross on their fateful journey to [[hun-court|Hunland]]. His confrontation with the Burgundian army over the killing of his ferryman escalates into the first bloodshed of the journey, and he is slain by [[dankwart|Dankwart]] in the ensuing battle.

## The Bavarian March and the Ferryman

Gelfrat rules the Bavarian March (Bavarian border territory) through which the Danube flows. His ferryman operates the river crossing that the Burgundians need to traverse on their way to [[etzels-court|Etzel's court]]. When [[hagen|Hagen]] demands passage and the ferryman refuses, Hagen kills him — an act that directly challenges Gelfrat's authority over the crossing point (025-119).

> "Die uns hinüber bringen in Gelfratens Land" (025-119)

Gelfrat's brother Else is introduced as the ferryman's master: "Sein Bruder ist geheißen Gelfrat der Held" (025-177). The narrative establishes Gelfrat as a "lord in Bavaria" who guards the land (025-184), and the ferryman's death is reported to him, setting the stage for the confrontation.

## Confrontation and Battle

Upon learning of the ferryman's death and the Burgundians' unauthorized crossing, Gelfrat receives "gewisse Kunde" (certain news) and musters a force to pursue them (026-041). He gathers a substantial following — "wohl siebenhundert stießen zu Gelfrat oder noch mehr" (026-048) — demonstrating his standing as a regional lord.

> "Wohl siebenhundert stießen zu Gelfrat oder noch mehr" (026-048)

Gelfrat confronts the Burgundian rearguard, demanding to know who has trespassed on his road: "Wer reitet uns auf der Straße? Das muste Gelfrat ihm sagen" (026-068). The encounter rapidly escalates. Gelfrat and Hagen exchange hostile words (026-085), and the two warriors charge at each other:

> "Hagen und Gelfrat: nun liefen sie sich an" (026-103)

The combat is fierce, described with vivid detail. Gelfrat unhorses a Burgundian knight (026-095), and Hagen springs at him with fury: "Wie heftig auch Hagen zu Gelfraten sprang" (026-105). Despite Gelfrat's courage, the battle turns against him, and the skirmish takes a toll on both sides (026-132).

## Death and Aftermath

Gelfrat is ultimately slain by Dankwart, Hagen's brother, who later recounts: "Da erschlug Gelfraten meines Bruders Hand" (026-162). Gelfrat's death marks the first significant bloodshed on the Burgundians' journey to Hunland. It serves as a narrative omen — the initial spark of violence that foreshadows the catastrophic destruction awaiting the entire Burgundian host at Etzel's court.

His brother Else survives the skirmish and later appears at [[etzels-court|Etzel's court]], serving as a reminder of the Bavarian conflict.

## Character and Significance

Gelfrat is portrayed not as a villain but as a territorial lord defending his rights against an armed incursion. His demand for accountability ("who rides on our road?") is legally and morally justified within the feudal framework of the poem. His death at Dankwart's hands, following Hagen's unprovoked killing of the ferryman, positions the Burgundians as the aggressors in this episode. This moral ambiguity contributes to the poem's tragic architecture: the Burgundians' doom is not merely fated but earned through a chain of escalating transgressions that begins with incidents like the killing of Gelfrat.
