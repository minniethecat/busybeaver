# Falcon Dream

The falcon dream (Falkentraum) is the prophetic vision that opens [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s story in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. In Adventure 1, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] dreams she has reared "a falcon, strong, beautiful, and wild" (001-050), which is then torn apart by two eagles. Her mother [[ute|Ute]] interprets the dream: "The falcon you raise is a noble man — God must guard him, or you will soon lose him" (001-055, 001-073).

## Definition

The falcon dream is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's primary narrative device of foreshadowing. It establishes, before any character has acted, the tragic outcome toward which the entire epic moves. The falcon — a noble hunting bird associated with aristocracy and freedom — represents [[siegfried|Siegfried]]. The two eagles that tear it apart foreshadow his murderers ([[hagen|Hagen]] and, indirectly, [[gunther|Gunther]]). The dream's function is not to create suspense (will [[siegfried|Siegfried]] die?) but to establish the framework of tragic inevitability.

### Structural Function

The dream serves as the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's narrative overture. By revealing the end at the beginning, it transforms the epic from a story about what will happen into a story about how and why it happens. The reader knows [[siegfried|Siegfried]] will die; the interest lies in the mechanism — the web of honor, loyalty, and betrayal that makes the inevitable actually occur.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Prophetic dreams are a standard device of medieval epic, appearing in the Chanson de Roland, the Norse sagas, and courtly romance. They typically serve to establish divine or fateful sanction for the narrative that follows. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's falcon dream is distinctive in its naturalistic symbolism — the falcon and eagles are creatures of the real world, not supernatural visitations.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The falcon dream establishes the epic's central theme: foreknowledge does not enable prevention. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] knows, from the very first adventure, that love will bring death — yet she cannot (or will not) avoid it. The dream thus embodies the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's tragic philosophy: fate is knowable but not evitable.
