# Balmung

Balmung is the legendary sword wielded by [[siegfried|Siegfried]] in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, described as a "broad, splendid weapon" of exceptional sharpness. Originally the sword of the Nibelung kings, it was won by [[siegfried|Siegfried]] along with the Nibelung treasure and the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]] (003-203, 003-219). After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, [[hagen|Hagen]] takes possession of Balmung and wields it in the climactic battles of the epic's second part.

## Definition

Balmung is a "vast, keen sword" (003-209) of Nibelung origin, acquired by [[siegfried|Siegfried]] when he defeated the Nibelung kings Schilbung and Nibelung and claimed their treasure hoard. The sword passes from [[siegfried|Siegfried]] to [[hagen|Hagen]] after the hero's murder — [[hagen|Hagen]] later wields Balmung — "he bore Balmung, which he evilly won" (029-164) — and becomes both a symbol of [[hagen|Hagen]]'s treachery and his instrument of final defiance. In the climactic confrontation at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, [[hagen|Hagen]]'s possession of Balmung provokes [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s fury — the sword is a constant reminder of her murdered husband.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In medieval Germanic epic, the sword is not merely a weapon but a bearer of identity and legitimacy. A named sword like Balmung carries the reputation of its wielder and the weight of its history. The transfer of a hero's sword to his killer is a profound violation of the heroic code — the weapon that should pass to an heir or loyal companion instead becomes a trophy of treachery.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Balmung serves as a material link between the epic's two halves. In Part I, it is [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s instrument of heroism. In Part II, it becomes a symbol of [[hagen|Hagen]]'s guilt and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief. When [[hagen|Hagen]] appears before [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court bearing Balmung, the sword triggers her memory of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder — "He bears Balmung, which he evilly won" (029-164). The sword thus bridges the personal and political dimensions of the revenge narrative, its presence a constant reminder of unavenged wrong.

## Comparative Notes

- In the Old Norse Þiðreks saga, the sword is called Gram and has a different origin — it was pulled from the Branstock tree by [[siegmund|Sigmund]], [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s father. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied omits this motif entirely.
- Wagner's operatic adaptation (Der Ring des [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]) names the sword Nothung and reintegrates the Branstock motif from Norse sources.
- The name "Balmung" may derive from Middle High German "balme" (rock cave), suggesting an underground or chthonic origin consistent with the sword's Nibelung provenance.
