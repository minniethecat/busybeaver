# Brunhild

Brunhild, Queen of Isenstein, is the most formidable female figure in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's first part — a ruler in her own right whose superhuman physical strength makes her unmarriageable by normal means. Her defeat through [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s secret intervention and her subsequent public humiliation by [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] at the church door (Adventure 14) catalyze the epic's central crisis.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Queen of Isenstein (sovereign ruler), later Queen of Burgundy (consort)
- **Affiliation**: Independent sovereign of [[isenstein|Isenstein]], later [[burgundian-court|Burgundian Court]] by marriage
- **Family**: Unnamed father (King of [[isenstein|Isenstein]], possibly deceased), [[gunther|Gunther]] (husband)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 06 — the journey to [[isenstein|Isenstein]] to woo the warrior-queen

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: The Warrior Queen (Adventures 6–14)

Brunhild rules [[isenstein|Isenstein]] as a sovereign queen, setting three contests of strength for any suitor: throwing a massive stone, leaping after it, and casting a spear. Defeat means death for the suitor. When [[gunther|Gunther]] arrives, Brunhild expects to face him directly — instead, [[siegfried|Siegfried]], invisible under the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]], performs the feats while [[gunther|Gunther]] mimics the motions (Adventure 07). Brunhild, believing [[gunther|Gunther]] has defeated her, submits and returns to [[worms|Worms]] as his bride.

The bridal night reveals the truth to Brunhild: [[gunther|Gunther]] cannot master her physically. Again [[siegfried|Siegfried]] intervenes, subduing Brunhild in the dark while [[gunther|Gunther]] takes his place. [[siegfried|Siegfried]] takes Brunhild's ring and girdle as trophies — objects that later become devastating evidence.

The crisis erupts in Adventure 14. The two queens quarrel over precedence at the church door. Enraged by Brunhild's assertion that [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is [[gunther|Gunther]]'s vassal, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] reveals the truth: [[siegfried|Siegfried]], not [[gunther|Gunther]], took Brunhild's virginity. She produces the ring and girdle as proof. Brunhild's public humiliation is absolute.

### The Silenced Queen

After Adventure 14, Brunhild virtually disappears from the narrative. Unlike [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], whose grief drives the entire second half, Brunhild's fate is left unremarked. This silence is itself significant — the warrior queen, once the most powerful woman in the epic's world, is reduced to a voiceless consort. Her humiliation has served its narrative purpose: the catalyst for [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Brunhild is driven by the maintenance of her [[ere|Êre]] (honor) — both as a sovereign queen and as [[gunther|Gunther]]'s wife. Her demand for precedence at the church door is not petty vanity but the assertion of royal status. Her fury at [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revelation is the fury of a woman who discovers her marriage and her queenship are founded on lies.

### Virtues & Flaws

Brunhild is strong, proud, and sovereign — qualities admirable in a king but, in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's world, problematic in a queen. Her tragedy is that she is a ruler in a system that has no place for a female ruler once married. Her strength, which should be her glory, becomes the instrument of her humiliation — it is precisely because she is so strong that she requires supernatural deception to be won.

## Scholarly Notes

- Brunhild corresponds to the Old Norse Brynhildr, a Valkyrie figure whose story (in the Völsunga saga) involves a magic sleep, a ring of fire, and a tragic love triangle with Sigurd — elements entirely absent from the German [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied.
- The historical Brunhild may reflect the Merovingian queen Brunhilda of Austrasia (c. 543–613), whose rivalry with Queen Fredegund was legendary — though the connection is speculative.
- Brunhild's superhuman strength and the bridal-night deception have been analyzed through feminist and gender-studies lenses as medieval anxiety about female power.
