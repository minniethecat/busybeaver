# Counsel and Advice in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a poem structured around decisions — who to marry, who to
trust, who to kill — and nearly every consequential decision is mediated
through _Rat_ (counsel, advice). Kings go to council ("Der König gieng zu
Rathe," 004-471), friends advise friends ("Ihm riethen seine [[friendship-and-companionship|Freund]]e,"
003-017), and the poem's most catastrophic act — the murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] — is
explicitly the product of counsel: "Allda die Helden riethen zu [[siegfried|Siegfried]]ens
[[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]" (014-206). _Rat_ is the formal mechanism through which the poem's
political world operates: it transforms individual impulse into collective
action, distributes responsibility across multiple speakers, and — in its
darkest form — becomes the cover under which private vengeance masquerades as
public deliberation. The poem traces the degeneration of counsel from
functional governance ("Das ist der beste Rath," 006-043) to murderous
conspiracy (014-206), and in doing so offers a sustained meditation on the
vulnerability of deliberative institutions to capture by private interest.

## Definition

MHG _rât_ (noun) and _râten_ (verb) derive from OHG _rât_, meaning "advice,
counsel, deliberation, provision." The semantic range is broad: _Rat_ can
denote a single piece of advice ("das ist in [[triuwe|Treue]]n mein Rath," 007-092), a
formal council assembly ("Da sprach vor dem Rathe der König [[gernot|Gernot]],"
024-161), or the abstract quality of having a solution to a problem ("des hätt
ich gerne Rath," 008-149). The verb _râten_ encompasses both giving advice
("Ihm riethen seine [[friendship-and-companionship|Freund]]e," 003-017) and the act of conspiring ("Allda die
Helden riethen zu [[siegfried|Siegfried]]ens [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]," 014-206). Crucially, the poem
distinguishes between good counsel (_der beste Rath_, 006-043) and bad counsel
(_Rath aus Arglist_, 016-224) — but the distinction is only clear in
retrospect. At the moment of decision, all counsel sounds reasonable; only the
consequences reveal which advice was wise and which was fatal.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Rat_ reflects the political culture of the
Staufer period, in which the formal _consilium_ (council) was the primary
mechanism of royal decision-making. The _curia regis_ (king's court) gathered
the realm's great lords to advise the king on matters of war, marriage, and
justice. The poem transposes this institution into narrative: [[gunther|Gunther]]'s
repeated convening of his counselors mirrors the Staufer practice of
_consilium_, and the poem's interest in the quality of advice — good versus
bad, prudent versus malicious — reflects a broader twelfth- and
thirteenth-century literature of _Fürstenspiegel_ (mirrors for princes) that
sought to educate rulers in the discernment of sound counsel. But the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied goes further than the advisory literature: it shows that even
a king who seeks counsel can be destroyed, because the institution of _Rat_
presupposes good faith that the poem's world does not reliably provide.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces a three-stage degeneration of _Rat_. In the first stage
(Adventures 1–13), counsel functions properly: kings seek advice, counselors
offer prudent recommendations, and decisions are made through collective
deliberation. [[gernot|Gernot]]'s successful counsel against fighting [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
(003-332) is the paradigm — _Rat_ as the mechanism that transforms potential
conflict into alliance. In the second stage (Adventures 14–19), counsel is
captured by private vengeance: [[hagen|Hagen]]'s advice to murder [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is
presented as counsel ("Allda die Helden riethen," 014-206) but is in fact
conspiracy — the deliberative form is preserved while its content is
corrupted. The narrator marks this corruption explicitly ("Rath aus Arglist,"
016-224). In the third stage (Adventures 20–39), counsel is either ignored
([[hagen|Hagen]]'s solitary dissent against the Hunnish marriage, 020-241) or
impossible (in the burning hall, there is no time for _Rat_). The poem's
tragedy is that the institution of counsel — designed to produce prudent
collective action — becomes the instrument of collective destruction.

## Notable Passages

> Ihm riethen seine Freunde und Die in seinem Lehn.
> — His friends and those in his fief advised him — the poem's first
> deployment of _râten_, establishing counsel as the normative frame for
> heroic decision-making (003-017)

> Das war Gernotens Rath.
> — That was Gernot's counsel — the poem's paradigm of good advice: the
> younger brother who counsels peace and is heeded (003-332)

> Der König gieng zu Rathe, wie er lohne seinem Lehn.
> — The king went to council to consider how to reward his vassals — _Rat_
> as the formal mechanism through which royal gratitude is translated into
> material recompense (004-471)

> "Das ist der beste Rath."
> — "That is the best counsel" — Hagen's early advice, framed as optimal;
> the poem's repeated formula for endorsing a course of action, whose
> irony becomes apparent when Hagen's later counsel proves fatal (006-043)

> Allda die Helden riethen zu Siegfriedens Tod.
> — There the heroes counseled Siegfried's death — the poem's darkest
> deployment of _râten_: counsel becomes conspiracy, deliberation becomes
> murder-planning (014-206)

> Da war der Rath aus Arglist von den Degen geschehn.
> — The counsel was born of malice from those warriors — the narrator's
> explicit condemnation: _Rat_ qualified by _Arglist_ marks the corruption
> of the deliberative institution (016-224)

> Da riethen sie es alle; nur Hagen stands nicht dazu.
> — They all counseled it; only Hagen did not agree — the unanimity of
> advice as social pressure; Hagen's dissent is the lone exception that
> proves the rule of counsel's capture by consensus (020-241)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Rat_ contrasts with the _Chanson de
  Roland_, where Ganelon's advice to make peace is revealed as treason — in
  the Roland, bad counsel comes from a traitor; in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, bad
  counsel emerges from within the community of heroes, making it harder to
  identify and more destructive
- In Hartmann von Aue's _Iwein_, counsel is a mechanism of rehabilitation —
  Lunete's advice guides the hero back to his proper role; the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied offers no such redemptive counseling, only the progressive
  corruption of an institution
- The poem's formula "das ist der beste Rath" echoes the advisory literature
  of the Staufer period, particularly the _Welscher Gast_ of Thomasin von
  Zerklaere, which taught that good counsel required both wisdom in the
  advisor and discernment in the advised — [[gunther|Gunther]] possesses neither in
  sufficient measure
- The Old Norse _Þiðreks saga_ preserves a version of the counsel-to-murder
  scene but distributes responsibility differently — the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's version, by framing the murder as the product of
  collective deliberation rather than individual malice, makes _Rat_ itself
  the vehicle of tragedy
