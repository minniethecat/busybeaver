# Crown and Kingship in the Nibelungenlied

The crown (_Krone_) in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is never merely an ornament. It is
the poem's master symbol for legitimate rule: to wear the crown is to exercise
sovereign authority, to refuse it is to renounce power, and to see it disputed
is to witness the unravelling of the political order. The poem traces the
movement of crowns across its narrative: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s refusal of his father's
crown (002-102), the painted crown on [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s banner (004-306), the four
crowned figures at the wedding feast (010-272), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s coronation as
Hunnish queen, and the final destruction of the Burgundian royal house. The
crown is what is worn, what is given, what is claimed, and ultimately what is
lost.

## Definition

_Krone_ in MHG denotes both the physical crown (the jeweled circlet worn on
the head) and, by metonymy, the office and authority of kingship. The phrase
"unter Krone gehen" (to go under the crown, 011-058, 011-067) marks the
ceremonial entry into royal status. "Krone tragen" (to wear the crown,
002-102, 003-265, 010-103) is the poem's formula for the exercise of
sovereignty. The compound "unter Krone" appears repeatedly as a marker of
legitimate royal presence — when the four royal figures stand "unter Krone"
at the wedding feast (010-272), it is the poem's most concentrated image of
Burgundian political order, soon to be shattered.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the Staufer period, the crown was the preeminent symbol of legitimate rule.
The imperial crown of the Holy Roman Empire was understood to convey authority
directly from God. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, composed in this political context, is
deeply concerned with the legitimacy of royal authority: who may wear a crown,
how crowns are acquired and transferred, and what happens when crowns come into
conflict. The poem's fascination with "Krone tragen" reflects a culture in
which the physical act of coronation was understood to transform the person of
the ruler.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's treatment of crowns follows the same tragic arc as its treatment of
joy and celebration. The early adventures establish a stable hierarchy of
crowns: [[siegmund|Siegmund]]'s crown (refused by [[siegfried|Siegfried]]), [[gunther|Gunther]]'s crown (the
Burgundian royal crown), and the crowns acquired through marriage. The four
crowned figures at the wedding feast (010-272) represent the poem's ideal of
political order: two kings and two queens, bound by marriage and mutual
obligation, their crowns a visible guarantee of the social order. The queens'
quarrel (Adventure 14) shatters this image: the dispute over precedence is a
dispute over the meaning of crowns — whose crown ranks higher? The poem's
second half shows the progressive hollowing-out of royal authority: [[gunther|Gunther]]'s
crown cannot prevent the catastrophe, [[etzel|Etzel]]'s crown cannot protect his guests,
and the poem ends with the Burgundian royal line extinguished — no one left to
wear the crown.

## Notable Passages

> Wollte nicht Krone tragen der beiden liebes Kind.
> — Siegfried refuses his father's crown — the founding act of his heroic identity, which is defined outside the normal framework of royal succession (002-102)

> Eine gemalte Krone vor Siegfriedens Hand.
> — The painted crown on Siegfried's banner — kingship as heraldic image, martial legitimacy (004-306)

> Da sah man unter Krone alle Viere herrlich stehn.
> — All four stand under crowns — the poem's perfect image of political unity, the moment before its dissolution (010-272)

> "Die schöne Kriemhild soll unter Krone gehn!"
> — Kriemhild shall go under the crown — the ceremonial language of coronation as the marker of her new status (011-058)

> Er befahl ihm seine Krone mit Gericht und Land.
> — Gunther entrusts his crown, justice, and land to Siegfried — the crown as the totality of royal authority, transferred in trust (011-097)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of the crown as a symbol of legitimate rule
  parallels the _Chanson de Roland_ (Charlemagne's golden crown) and the
  imperial ideology of the Staufer period, but the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is unusually
  skeptical about the crown's power to protect its wearer
- The motif of the contested crown — who rightfully wears it, whose crown ranks
  higher — is distinctive to the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied; the Arthurian tradition rarely
  subjects royal authority to this kind of systematic questioning
- The Old Norse analogues (_Atlakviða_, _Völsunga saga_) do not develop the
  crown as a central symbol; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's emphasis on "Krone" and
  "unter Krone" is a product of the High Medieval courtly context
- The poem's arc from "alle Viere unter Krone" (010-272) to the extinction of
  the Burgundian royal house represents a distinctive medieval meditation on
  the fragility of political order — the crown can be worn but it cannot
  protect
