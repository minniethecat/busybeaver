# Gaze and Spectacle in the Nibelungenlied

The window (_Fenster_) in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is far more than an architectural
detail. It functions as a narrative frame that organizes the poem's most
important social encounters: the first sight of a foreign hero, the display of
martial prowess, the appraisal of status through clothing and bearing. The verb
_schauen_ (to look, to gaze, to behold) and its compounds recur throughout the
poem, marking moments where characters — especially women — become spectators,
and where the act of being seen transforms the meaning of action. In a poem
where so much hinges on reputation ([[ere]]), the gaze is
the mechanism by which reputation is produced and circulated.

## Definition

In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, the gaze is a structured social relation rather than a
neutral act of perception. Three distinct modes of looking can be identified:
the **courtly gaze** — women watching knights from windows, assessing their
bearing and prowess (003-367, 004-418, 007-003); the **heroic gaze** — the
warrior looking out upon the field or upon arriving strangers, measuring threat
and opportunity (003-161, 003-319); and the **spectacular gaze** — the crowd
watching tournaments, feasts, and public ceremonies, where the sheer number of
onlookers amplifies the significance of what is seen (006-178, 006-233). All
three modes share a common logic: to be seen is to be judged, and to be judged
well is the foundation of _[[ere|Êre]]_.

| 03 | [[siegfried|Siegfried]] declares his intent: "So will ich schauen laßen, wie noch die Hände mein" | The hero makes himself a spectacle — his deeds are performed in order to be seen, and the seeing confirms his identity (003-319) |
| 04 | The Saxon battle: warriors die "erschauen den [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]" (behold death) | The gaze can be fatal — looking upon death is itself a kind of participation in it (004-132) |
| 04 | After the battle, women gather at the windows to welcome the returning heroes | The female gaze at the window sanctions male violence — the women's presence transforms killing into knightly achievement (004-418) |
| 06–07 | The tournament before [[brunhild|Brunhild]]: women fill the windows, [[siegfried|Siegfried]] and [[gunther|Gunther]] perform | The window-scene reaches its fullest expression: the entire action is framed by female spectators, and the knights' deeds are oriented toward the windows where the women sit (006-233, 007-003, 007-031, 007-040) |
| 07 | [[siegfried|Siegfried]] looks up at the window: "So schau ich ihrer Eine in jenem Fenster an" | The gaze is reciprocal — the hero looks back at the women who watch him, creating a circuit of looking that binds the courtly scene together (007-013) |

### Associated Characters

| Character | Relationship to this concept |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| [[kriemhild]] | The poem's supreme object of the gaze — she appears at windows, is watched by knights and ladies alike, and her visibility is central to her narrative function; her withdrawal from the public gaze after [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death marks her turn toward vengeance |
| [[hagen]] | The gazer who interprets — at the window in Adventure 3 (003-161), [[hagen|Hagen]]'s gaze is analytical and political, not courtly; he sees through appearances to strategic realities |
| [[siegfried]] | The hero who makes himself a spectacle — his deeds are performed to be seen, and his identity depends on being recognized (003-319); the poem's tragedy begins when the gaze upon him becomes the gaze of envy and conspiracy |
| [[brunhild]] | The woman who refuses to be reduced to a spectacle — her extraordinary strength resists the courtly gaze, and the crisis of Adventure 7 arises from the collision between her power and the expectations of the window-scene |
| [[gunther]] | The king whose status depends on being seen correctly — his performance at the window-scene in [[isenstein|Isenstein]] is a borrowed spectacle, dependent on [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s invisible aid |

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The window as a narrative frame draws on a rich tradition in medieval German
literature. In the _[[minne|Minne]]sang_ lyric, the window is the archetypal site of
longing — the lady at the window, the lover below. In courtly romance, the
window-scene organizes tournament description: knights perform before ladies
whose gaze confers honor. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied inherits this convention but
presses it into the service of heroic epic rather than romance. The gaze here
is not primarily erotic (though it can be) but social and political: to be seen
at the window is to participate in the public life of the court, and to be
excluded from the window is to be excluded from honor.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's window-scenes trace an arc from openness and display to enclosure
and concealment. In the early adventures, windows are crowded, looking is
public, and the gaze circulates freely. By the poem's second half, the windows
close: the events at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court take place behind walls, in halls, away from
the public eye. The final battle in Adventures 33–39 is an interior catastrophe
from which there is no window to look out and no spectator to witness. The poem
that began with the spectacle of heroes being watched ends in a sealed hall
where the only looking is the looking of combatants at each other.

## Notable Passages

> "Das will ich," sprach Hagen. Zum Fenster schritt er drauf — Hagen steps to
> the window to survey the arriving strangers, and his gaze transforms unknown
> riders into the recognizable figure of Siegfried. The window here is an
> instrument of knowledge and power. (003-161)

> Da stellte sich ins Fenster manch minnigliches Kind — women fill the windows
> to watch the tournament before Brunhild. The window frames the female
> spectator as a constitutive element of the knightly spectacle. (006-233)

> "So schau ich ihrer Eine in jenem Fenster an" — Siegfried looks up from the
> tournament field and sees Kriemhild at the window. The gaze becomes
> reciprocal: the hero is both watcher and watched, and the circuit of looking
> binds the courtly scene into a unified social drama. (007-013)

## Comparative Notes

- The window-scene in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied parallels the tournament descriptions
  in Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_, where ladies at windows similarly
  frame the action — but the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's gaze is more political and less
  explicitly erotic than Wolfram's
- In Old Norse analogues (the _Völsunga saga_ and _Þiðreks saga_), the
  window-scene is largely absent — the framing of action through female
  spectators is a distinctively courtly, German contribution to the heroic
  material
- The motif of the fatal gaze (_erschauen den [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]_, 004-132) connects to
  broader medieval traditions of the destructive or transformative power of
  looking
