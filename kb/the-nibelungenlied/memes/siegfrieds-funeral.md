# Siegfried's Funeral

[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s funeral (Adventure 17) is the epic's most extended scene of mourning — three days of public grief during which [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] keeps vigil over her husband's body, the Burgundian court processes past the bier, and a medieval miracle of cruentation exposes the truth of the murder when [[hagen|Hagen]] approaches and the wounds of the dead [[siegfried|Siegfried]] begin to bleed again.

## Overview

- **Adventure(s)**: 17
- **Location**: [[worms|Worms]], the cathedral and its grounds
- **Key Participants**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (widow and chief mourner), [[hagen|Hagen]] (murderer, exposed by the miracle), [[gunther|Gunther]] (king and guilty party), Uta, [[gernot|Gernot]], [[geiselher|Geiselher]] (the Burgundian court)
- **Outcome**: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] buried; [[hagen|Hagen]]'s guilt proven by cruentation; [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s vengeance begins

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

[[siegfried|Siegfried]] has been murdered on the hunting expedition (Adventure 16). [[hagen|Hagen]], having stabbed [[siegfried|Siegfried]] through the cross-mark on his back as he drank from a spring, has had the body left at [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s door. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] discovers her husband's body at dawn and faints in grief. The court assembles; the scale of the loss becomes clear.

### The Event

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] orders the body prepared for burial and placed on the bier in the cathedral. She refuses to believe [[siegfried|Siegfried]] died from robbers, as [[gunther|Gunther]] has claimed. In a medieval ritual of identification — the "Bahrprobe" or cruentation — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] demands that all present approach the bier (017-121). As each noble approaches, [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s wounds remain dry. But when [[hagen|Hagen]] steps forward, the wounds open and bleed afresh (017-125). The miracle, witnessed by the entire court, brands [[hagen|Hagen]] as the murderer. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s accusation is now public, but the court — bound by loyalty to [[gunther|Gunther]] — does not punish [[hagen|Hagen]]. [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is buried in the cathedral with magnificent ceremony, his shield first blessed in the church (017-237).

### Aftermath

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] remains at the cathedral for three days, refusing food and water. She pays for masses for [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s soul and distributes alms to the poor. The cruentation establishes legally that [[hagen|Hagen]] killed [[siegfried|Siegfried]], but no justice follows. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief, denied the satisfaction of punishment, begins its transformation into the cold determination that will define the epic's second half.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

The funeral is the public acknowledgment of the epic's central crime. The cruentation — a miracle recognized in medieval law — establishes [[hagen|Hagen]]'s guilt beyond doubt, yet the failure to act on this divine judgment corrupts the Burgundian court. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s experience at the funeral, watching justice denied, is the crucible in which her revenge is forged.

### Medieval Themes

The cruentation (Bahrprobe) reflects the medieval belief that a murdered body would bleed in the presence of its killer — a belief grounded in the idea that blood carries the soul's truth and that God would not let murder go hidden. The ceremony of the funeral — the vigil, the masses, the distribution of alms — follows the medieval ars moriendi tradition. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s funeral transforms him into a martyr figure, his body bearing witness to the truth his killers tried to conceal.
