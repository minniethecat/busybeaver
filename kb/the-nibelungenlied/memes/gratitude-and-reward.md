# Gratitude and Reward in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied operates on an economy of reciprocity in which every service
demands acknowledgment, every gift expects thanks, and every debt —
acknowledged or not — shapes the course of events. Two words govern this
economy: _Dank_ (gratitude, thanks), the verbal currency through which honor
is recognized, and _Lohn_ (reward, recompense), the material currency through
which service is compensated. Together they form the poem's syntax of
obligation: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] fights the Saxons and receives [[gunther|Gunther]]'s thanks ("des
sagt' er allen Dank," 008-090); [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s kinsmen give bloody reward for her
sorrow ("wie gab sie blutgen Lohn," 001-075). The poem's tragedy is that the
economy of _Dank und Lohn_ — designed to bind allies through mutual
obligation — is ultimately displaced by the older economy of blood, in which
the only adequate reward for injury is death.

## Definition

MHG _danc_ (from OHG _danc_, "thought, intention, thanks") denotes the verbal
acknowledgment of a benefit received — _Dank sagen_ (to say thanks) is the
poem's most frequent formula for closing a transaction of service. It is
performative: to say thanks is to complete the exchange, to release the giver
from further obligation. MHG _lôn_ (from OHG _lôn_, "reward, wages,
recompense") denotes the material counterpart — gold, garments, land, or the
hand of a lady given in return for service. The two are often paired: "des
sagt' er allen Dank" (he said thanks to all, 008-090) may be followed by "gab
sie ihm zum Lohne" (she gave him as reward, 009-118). Crucially, the poem
distinguishes between the proper reward (_verdienten Lohn_, 016-276) and the
bloody recompense (_blutgen Lohn_, 001-075) — the same word _Lohn_ can denote
both the system working and the system failing.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The vocabulary of _Dank und Lohn_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied reflects the material
and symbolic economy of feudal service (_[[service-and-fealty|Dienst]]_). In the Staufer period,
the relationship between lord and vassal was structured by reciprocal
obligation: the vassal offered _consilium et auxilium_ (counsel and aid),
and the lord offered _beneficium_ (material reward, typically land). The
poem transposes this legal-economic framework into the language of courtly
narrative: _Dank_ is the verbal seal on the transaction, _Lohn_ its material
fulfillment. The poem's repeated invocation of _verdienten Lohn_ (deserved
reward, 016-276) reflects a cultural anxiety about the adequacy of
recompense — the fear that service rendered might exceed any possible reward,
leaving a residue of obligation that festers into grievance.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces the fate of _Dank und Lohn_ from functional social mechanism
to catastrophic failure. In the first half, the system works: thanks are
offered, rewards are given, the cycle of reciprocity binds the Burgundian
court together. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s demand for [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] as _Lohn_ (006-050) is
the system's most ambitious transaction — the hand of a princess in exchange
for heroic service — and it nearly succeeds. But the poem's opening stanza
has already planted the counter-word: _blutgen Lohn_ (bloody reward, 001-075).
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death in Adventure 16 is the moment when the economy of _Dank_
collapses into the economy of blood: the reward withheld, the thanks unsaid,
the debt that can only be settled in violence. The second half of the poem —
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s long campaign of vengeance — is an extended demonstration that
the vocabulary of reciprocity persists even after its social function has
been destroyed. Characters continue to offer thanks and promise rewards, but
every _Dank_ is now a lie and every _Lohn_ a prelude to death.

## Notable Passages

> wie gab sie blutgen Lohn!
> — The poem's opening stanza — _Lohn_ introduced as blood-reward, the dark twin of proper recompense that will dominate the poem's second half (001-075)

> Sie gaben ihm zum Lohne König Niblungs Schwert.
> — The Nibelung sword as reward for service — the material object that binds Siegfried to the economy of obligation and ultimately becomes an instrument of his death (003-197)

> So begehr ich keines Dankes nach meinen Arbeiten mehr.
> — Siegfried's refusal of thanks — the rejection of the verbal currency of reciprocity when the promised material reward (Kriemhild) is withheld (006-052)

> "Viel Dank muß ich euch sagen, Frau Brunhild."
> — Siegfried's thanks to Brunhild — a moment of proper Dank that is also a deception, since Siegfried is thanking Brunhild for a contest he won through invisible aid (007-125)

> hätt er Hagnen den verdienten Lohn gewährt.
> — The narrator's counterfactual — if Hagen had received his deserved reward, the catastrophe might have been averted; the poem's most explicit statement that the failure of reciprocity is the root of tragedy (016-276)

> So geb ich dir zum Lohne eine Spange goldesroth.
> — Hagen offering reward to the ferryman — _Lohn_ as bribe, as manipulation, as the word that once meant honor now meaning transaction (025-195)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's dual vocabulary of _Dank_ (verbal) and _Lohn_
  (material) parallels the distinction in Old French epic between _merci_
  (thanks, mercy) and _guerdon_ (reward) — but where the _Chanson de Roland_
  treats the failure of _guerdon_ as a political-military problem (Ganelon's
  grievance), the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied treats it as a structural condition of the
  heroic world
- In the Old Norse _Völsunga saga_, the equivalent vocabulary (_þökk_,
  _laun_) is less systematically deployed — the saga's emphasis on kinship
  obligation makes the economy of service-and-reward less central than in
  the more courtly [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's achievement is to show that _Dank und Lohn_ are not
  merely transactional but constitutive of social identity: when [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
  refuses thanks (006-052), he is not just declining a courtesy but
  withdrawing from the social bond that _Dank_ creates
- The poem's opening with _blutgen Lohn_ and closing with the destruction of
  all who participated in the economy of reciprocity is a structural
  irony unique among medieval epics — the _Chanson de Roland_ ends with
  Charlemagne's victory and the restoration of order; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
  ends with the demonstration that the system of rewards was always already
  a system of blood
