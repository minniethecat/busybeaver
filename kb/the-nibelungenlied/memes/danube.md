# Danube River

The Danube River (Middle High German: Donau) marks the threshold between the familiar world of Burgundy and the unknown realm of the Huns in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. The Burgundians cross it in Adventure 25, and it is on the Danube's banks that [[hagen|Hagen]] learns from the water nymphs that none of the company will return alive to [[worms|Worms]] — one of the epic's most haunting prophetic episodes.

## Location & Geography

- **Type**: Major river (navigable, prone to flooding, used as a boundary)
- **Ruler**: None — flows through multiple territories including Bavaria and the march of the margrave Ruedeger
- **Real-world counterpart**: Danube River, originating in the Black [[forest-and-wilderness|Forest]] and flowing east through Germany, Austria, Hungary, and beyond — the primary route through Central Europe
- **Associated characters**: [[hagen|Hagen]], [[gunther|Gunther]], [[dankwart|Dankwart]], the ferryman Else, the water nymphs (Hadburg, Sieglind)

### Strategic Significance

The Danube functions as the central geographical threshold in Part II of the epic. While the [[rhine|Rhine]] defines the heart of the Burgundian world, the Danube marks its eastern edge — a boundary that, once crossed, cannot be recrossed. The water nymphs' prophecy at the Danube (025-133, 025-149) transforms the river from a geographical feature into a fatal boundary: no Burgundian who crosses it will return. The killing of the ferryman (026-025) further emphasizes the irreversible nature of the crossing.

## Historical Notes

- The Danube served as the northern boundary of the Roman Empire for centuries (the Danubian Limes), a role reflected in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of the river as a boundary between worlds.
- The medieval Bavarian Ostmark (later Austria) was organized along the Danube, with castles and settlements at strategic crossing points.
- The route described in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied (from [[worms|Worms]] down the [[rhine|Rhine]], overland to the Danube, then east to Vienna and beyond) corresponds to a known medieval travel corridor.
