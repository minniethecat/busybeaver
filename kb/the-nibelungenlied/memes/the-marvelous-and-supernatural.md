# The Marvelous and Supernatural in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied opens with a promise: "Viel Wunderdinge melden die [[messenger-and-news|Mär]]en alter Zeit"
(001-001) — "Many wondrous things the tales of old time tell." The marvelous
(_Wunder_) is not merely a feature of the poem but its declared subject. From
[[siegfried]]'s superhuman strength and the [[tarnkappe]]'s power of
invisibility to the [[nibelung-treasure]]'s cursed provenance and the prophetic
dreams that foreshadow catastrophe, the supernatural runs through the poem as
both narrative motor and interpretive challenge. Unlike the fully mythologized
world of the Old Norse _Völsunga saga_, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's wonders are
embedded in a courtly Christian milieu — dwarves attend mass, dragons are
dispatched offstage, and the marvelous is constantly naturalized by the language
of courtly observation.

## Definition

MHG _wunder_ (neuter noun, from OHG _wuntar_) denotes a marvel, wonder, or
extraordinary event exceeding normal human understanding. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied,
_Wunder_ appears in three registers: (1) as **narrative frame** — the poem
presents itself as a tale of wonders from the opening stanza, invoking "old
tales" (_alten [[messenger-and-news|Mär]]en_); (2) as **superhuman feats** — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s strength, his
dragon-slaying, his combat prowess are all described as _Wunder_; and (3) as
**marvelous objects and beings** — the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]], the Nibelung treasure, the
dragon, the dwarves Schilbung and Nibelung all belong to the category of
_Wunder_. The formula "man mochte Wunder sagen" ("one might tell wonders")
recurs as a narratorial hedge — what follows is extraordinary, vouched for on
the authority of tradition rather than direct witness.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied was composed at a moment of transition around 1200, when the
fully mythologized world of the Germanic heroic age had been overlaid with
courtly romance conventions and Christian historiography. The poem retains the
wonders of heroic tradition (dragons, dwarves, treasure-hoards) while framing
them through a narrative voice that treats the marvelous with a blend of
credulity and distance. The formula "man mochte Wunder sagen" parallels the
_inquit_ formula of hagiography — the narrator reports wonders on the authority
of tradition without claiming direct knowledge. This differs from the
mythological immediacy of the _Völsunga saga_ and the allegorized marvels of
Chrétien de Troyes. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied preserves the heroic age's _Wunder_
while subjecting them to courtly observation — the marvels are real, but seen
through the eyes of a world that no longer fully believes in them.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces an arc from _Wunder_ to catastrophe. In the first half
(Adventures 1–19), _Wunder_ is concentrated in [[siegfried|Siegfried]] and his associated
objects: the dragon, the treasure, the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]], the Nibelung land. These
wonders are the infrastructure of his heroic identity. His marriage to
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] represents the integration of _Wunder_ into the courtly world. But
this integration is unstable. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is, among other
things, the elimination of _Wunder_ from the Burgundian court. After
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, the poem's register shifts: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revival (017-261) is
the last genuine _Wunder_, a wonder of suffering rather than power. The second
half is notable for its near-total absence of the marvelous — no dragons, no
treasure-hoards, no invisible cloaks. Instead, the poem offers political
calculation, betrayed hospitality, and systematic slaughter. The _Wunder_ that
opened the poem have been replaced by _[[sorrow-and-suffering|Leid]]_ (sorrow).

## Notable Passages

> Viel Wunderdinge melden die Mären alter Zeit
> — The poem's first line: _Wunder_ as the declared subject of the entire
> narrative, the audience promised wonders before any character is introduced (001-001)

> Sie schufen starke Wunder noch seitdem in Etzels Land.
> — "They wrought mighty wonders thereafter in Etzel's land" — the second
> stanza projects _Wunder_ forward, identifying the Hunnish world as the site
> of the poem's ultimate marvels (001-020)

> Er wirkte große Wunder mit des starken Armes Schwung
> — "He worked great wonders with the swing of his strong arm" — Siegfried's
> martial prowess is explicitly categorized as _Wunder_ (003-176)

> nun hört Wunder sagen
> — "Now hear wonders told" — the narrator's direct address marking the
> Nibelung treasure episode as heightened marvel (003-182)

> Von des Spießes Schwere höret Wunder sagen
> — "Of the spear's weight hear wonders told" — Brunhild's weapon is itself a
> Wunder, the marvelous extended from persons to objects (007-209)

> Es war ein großes Wunder, daß sie zu Kräften kam
> — "It was a great wonder that she came to her strength" — Kriemhild's
> revival from death-like grief, the poem's most explicitly miraculous
> moment and the last unambiguous supernatural intervention (017-261)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Wunder_ differs from the _Völsunga saga_,
  where the supernatural is embedded in a mythological framework (Odin's
  interventions, Sigurd's dragon-speech) — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied presents
  comparable marvels through a courtly narrator treating _Wunder_ as reported
  tradition rather than lived myth
- The "man mochte Wunder sagen" formula parallels historiographical conventions
  of medieval chronicles, where marvels are reported on the authority of
  sources — the narrative technique of the _Gesta_ tradition, adapted to
  vernacular heroic poetry
- In Chrétien de Troyes's Arthurian romances, wonders (_merveilles_) are
  systematically allegorized and resolved — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied offers no such
  resolution: its wonders disappear not through explanation but through the
  death of their bearer ([[siegfried|Siegfried]])
