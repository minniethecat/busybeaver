## Overview

The Nibelungenlied is a poem of things as much as of people. Five artifacts — three magical and two categories of material culture — structure the epic's action: the sword [[balmung|Balmung]] passes from Siegfried to Hagen and back to Kriemhild's hand; the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]] (cloak of invisibility) enables Siegfried's deceptions at Isenstein and in the bridal chamber; the [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelung Treasure]] is the object of conquest, gift, theft, and final concealment; and the [[horses-and-mounts|horses]] and [[weapons-and-armor|weapons]] of the epic's warrior culture provide the material foundation for its world of heroic action.

## Thematic Significance

The artifacts of the Nibelungenlied function as more than props. They are **narrative engines**: the Tarnkappe makes the Brunhild deception possible; Balmung links Siegfried's murder to Hagen's execution; the treasure's journey — won, gifted, stolen, sunk — mirrors the epic's arc from acquisition to loss. In a poem obsessed with the tension between appearance and reality ([[ere|ere]], [[triuwe|triuwe]]), magical objects literalize the problem: the Tarnkappe makes appearance controllable; the treasure makes value visible and contestable.

The artifacts also embody the **transfer problem** at the heart of heroic culture. Objects pass from hand to hand — Balmung from Nibelung kings to Siegfried to Hagen to Kriemhild — but they carry their histories with them. Each transfer adds a layer of meaning: Balmung in Hagen's hand is Siegfried's sword and the accusation of murder made steel.
