# Hunland

Hunland (Middle High German: Heunenland) is the kingdom of King [[etzel|Etzel]] in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, a powerful eastern realm that becomes [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s adopted home after her marriage to [[etzel|Etzel]]. In the epic's second part, Hunland transforms from a distant, exotic kingdom into the stage for the final cataclysm — the destruction of the Burgundians in the great hall of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s castle.

## Location & Geography

- **Type**: Kingdom (large eastern realm ruled by a powerful monarch)
- **Ruler**: King [[etzel|Etzel]] ([[etzel|Attila]]), and Queen [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]
- **Real-world counterpart**: The historical Hunnic Empire under [[etzel|Attila]] (c. 434-453), centered on the Carpathian Basin, though the epic's portrayal is thoroughly medievalized with Christian and feudal elements
- **Associated characters**: [[etzel|Etzel]], [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], [[bloedel|Bloedel]] ([[etzel|Etzel]]'s brother), Helke ([[etzel|Etzel]]'s first wife, deceased), the Hunnish warriors and nobles

### Strategic Significance

Hunland functions as the second great court of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, standing in stark contrast to [[worms|Worms]]. Where [[worms|Worms]] represents Burgundian courtly order, Hunland represents the chaotic and dangerous periphery — larger, more opulent, but less stable. The epic describes Hunland with a mixture of admiration (for [[etzel|Etzel]]'s power and wealth) and apprehension (as the distant land where death awaits). [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s transformation from wronged widow to vengeful queen is inseparable from her adoption of Hunland as her power base. The final battle, which destroys both the Burgundians and many of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s Huns, turns Hunland itself into a graveyard.

## Historical Notes

- The historical [[etzel|Attila]] ([[etzel|Etzel]]) ruled the Hunnic Empire from 434 to 453 CE, conducting devastating campaigns into both the Eastern and Western Roman Empires.
- The medieval epic transforms the historical [[etzel|Attila]] into a Christian king ruling a feudal court — a common anachronism in medieval romance.
- The epic blends the historical Huns with medieval Hungarians (the "Heunen" of Middle High German), reflecting the 12th-13th century German perception of the Kingdom of Hungary.
