# Courage and High Spirits in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied opens with a declaration of its subject: "von kühner [[heroic-code|Recke]]n
[[combat-and-warfare|Streit]]en mögt ihr nun [[the-marvelous-and-supernatural|Wunder]] hören sagen" — of bold warriors' combats you may
now hear wonders told (001-004). _Hoher Mut_ (high spirits, elevated courage)
is not merely one virtue among many in the poem's heroic world; it is the
foundational quality from which all other heroic attributes derive. The poem's
protagonists are introduced as "stark und hohen Muthes" (strong and of high
courage, 001-030), and the narrative tracks how this courage is tested, broken,
and ultimately consumed in the catastrophe that engulfs the [[burgundian-court]].

## Definition

_Hoher Mut_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a compound concept. _Mut_ in Middle High
German carries a richer semantic field than modern English "courage": it
encompasses spirit, disposition, inner state, and the will to act. The
adjective _kühn_ (bold, daring) appears over one hundred times in the poem,
almost as a fixed epithet for its heroes — "der kühne [[siegfried|Siegfried]]" (003-033),
"der kühne Degen" (002-013), "kühner [[heroic-code|Recke]]n" (001-004). _Hoher Mut_ is the
elevated form: courage fused with joy, confidence, and aristocratic
self-possession. Its opposite is not cowardice but _[[sorrow-and-suffering|Leid]]_ (sorrow,
suffering) — the poem's arc from _[[joy-and-celebration|Freude]]_ to _[[sorrow-and-suffering|Leid]]_ is also an arc from _hoher
Mut_ to its extinguishment.

## Role in the Narrative

### Key Appearances

| Adventure | Context | Significance |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| 01 | The narrator introduces the Burgundian court: "Stark und hohen Muthes; ihnen waren unterthan / Von ihren hohen Würdigkeit" | Courage is established as the defining quality of the warrior aristocracy — it is inseparable from their social eminence (001-030, 001-046) |
| 02 | [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s upbringing: "der kühne Degen voll erwuchs zum Mann" and "in hochbeherztem Muth" | [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s courage is innate — it precedes and enables all his deeds (002-007, 002-010, 002-013) |
| 03 | [[siegfried|Siegfried]] declares his intent before departing for Burgundy: "So ist mir nicht zu Muthe" | [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s _Mut_ determines his course of action — courage is the inner force that propels the narrative forward (003-057) |
| 04 | [[hagen|Hagen]] urges the Burgundians before the Saxon battle: "weilt bei den [[gender-and-femininity|Frau]]en und tragt hohen Muth" | Courage is performative — it must be displayed and maintained before the watching women (004-143) |
| 33–39 | The hall-battle: courage under conditions of certain death | The poem's final act tests whether _hoher Mut_ can survive when all hope is extinguished — the Burgundians fight on, but the elevated joy of early courage is gone |

### Associated Characters

| Character | Relationship to this concept |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| [[siegfried]] | The poem's supreme embodiment of _hoher Mut_ — "der kühne [[siegfried|Siegfried]]" (003-033) whose courage is as natural as breathing, tested in dragon-slaying, giant-killing, and single combat against the Nibelung kings |
| [[hagen]] | Courage of a darker kind — [[hagen|Hagen]]'s _Mut_ is grim (_grimmig_) rather than elevated, a courage born of loyalty and fatalism rather than joy; his provocation of the final battle is an act of terrible courage (003-293) |
| [[gunther]] | A king whose courage is socially constructed — he relies on [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s prowess while performing the gestures of boldness; the poem quietly questions whether royal courage is identical to personal courage |
| [[volker]] | The minstrel whose courage is inseparable from art — he fights and plays his fiddle simultaneously in the hall-battle, embodying courage as a form of defiant vitality |
| [[kriemhild]] | Courage transformed into something else — her initial _hoher Mut_ as a queen gives way to the terrible resolve of vengeance, a courage that consumes itself and everyone around her |

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

_Hoher Mut_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied draws on the Germanic heroic tradition's
concept of _mōd_ (Old English _mōd_, Old Norse _móðr_) — the inner spirit that
manifests as courage, pride, and wrath. Unlike the Christian virtue of
_fortitudo_, which is courage in service of a higher moral order, _hoher Mut_
is self-justifying: the hero's courage is its own reason for being. The poem
stands at a transitional moment where this older heroic ethos encounters
courtly culture — _hoher Mut_ is increasingly framed as something displayed
before ladies, tested in tournaments rather than monster-slaying, and evaluated
by its effect on courtly reputation (005-096).

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces a trajectory from _hoher Mut_ to its annihilation. The opening
adventures are saturated with elevated courage: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s bold journey to
[[worms|Worms]], the Burgundians' confident reception, the tournament and feast where
courage is displayed and celebrated. The Saxon war tests this courage in
earnest but confirms it — the Burgundians prevail, [[siegfried|Siegfried]] captures the
enemy kings, and _hoher Mut_ seems invincible (004-143). [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder
marks the turning point: an act of treachery that courage alone cannot defend
against. The poem's final act — the journey to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court and the
hall-battle — shows courage persisting without joy, becoming grimmer and more
desperate with each adventure. The last Burgundians fight with undiminished
martial courage, but the _hoher Mut_ of the poem's opening — courage fused with
celebration, confidence, and life — has been replaced by the courage of those
who know they will not survive.

## Notable Passages

> Stark und hohen Muthes; ihnen waren unterthan / Von ihren hohen Würdigkeit und von der Ritterschaft.
> — The narrator establishes the Burgundian court's defining quality: strength and high courage, from which their dignity and knightly stature derive (001-030, 001-046)

> In hochbeherztem Muth.
> — Siegfried's followers are characterized by high-hearted courage — the phrase recurs as a formula for heroic excellence (002-010)

> "So ist mir nicht zu Muthe," fiel ihm Siegfried ein.
> — Siegfried's courage determines his action: his inner state (_Mut_) propels the plot (003-057)

> Stark und kühnes Muthes mocht er wohl sein.
> — The formulaic attribution of bold courage to a warrior — here describing one of the Burgundian champions (003-150)

> "Weilt bei den Frauen und tragt hohen Muth."
> — Hagen's injunction before battle — courage is performance, witnessed and validated by the courtly audience (004-143)

## Comparative Notes

- The MHG _hoher Mut_ corresponds to the Old Norse _hugr_ (mind, spirit, courage)
  in poems like _Hávamál_ and the heroic lays of the Poetic Edda, though the
  Norse tradition places greater emphasis on stoic courage in the face of fate
- Compared to the _Chanson de Roland_, where courage is explicitly Christianized
  (Roland's _proz_ serves God and emperor), the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's _hoher Mut_ is
  a secular-aristocratic virtue — courage serves honor, not heaven
- The _[[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]slied_ shares the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's formulaic use of courage
  epithets, but its shorter scope does not develop the arc from _hoher Mut_ to
  its extinguishment
- Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_, written roughly contemporaneously,
  redefines courage as a component of chivalric _triuwe_ (loyalty) — the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's courage is more archaic and less integrated into a
  theological-moral framework
