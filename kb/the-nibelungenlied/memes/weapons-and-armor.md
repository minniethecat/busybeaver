# Weapons and Armor in the Nibelungenlied

Weapons saturate the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. Swords are named, given as gifts, drawn in
anger, and broken in combat. Armor is described with technical precision:
_Waffenhemde_ (mail-shirts of silk, 007-161), shields, helmets, and the
elaborate _Reitzeug_ (riding-gear) of the mounted warrior. The poem opens with
the sword-taking (_Schwertleite_) of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] — the ritual that transforms a
youth into a warrior (002-044) — and closes with the shattering of swords in
the hall-battle, when weapons that have served through thirty-nine adventures
are finally broken in the all-consuming violence of the poem's final act. More
than any other class of object, weapons in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied carry identity,
memory, and fate.

## Definition

The poem's weapon-vocabulary is extensive and precise. _Schwert_ (sword) is
the most frequent weapon-term, appearing over one hundred times. _Waffen_
(weapons, arms) is the generic plural, encompassing the full panoply of the
armed warrior: sword, shield, spear, helmet, and mail. _Waffenhemde_ (mail-shirt,
byrnie) appears in contexts of arming and protection. _Waffenspiel_ (weapon-play,
tournament) marks the boundary between martial exercise and actual combat
(003-365). The compound _Schwertdegen_ (sword-thane, sword-warrior) appears as
a status term: "Vierhundert Schwertdegen sollten gekleidet sein" — four hundred
sword-thanes were to be clothed, marking the size and splendor of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
retinue (002-049).

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, the sword was the preeminent symbol
of aristocratic identity. The sword-taking (_Schwertleite_) was the rite of
passage into knighthood, replacing the older Germanic _Wehrhaftmachung_
(making-weapon-worthy). Named swords — [[balmung|Balmung]] in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, Durendal
in the _Chanson de Roland_, Excalibur in Arthurian tradition — carried the
identity of their bearers and could not be wielded by the unworthy. The poem's
attention to the material qualities of weapons (sharpness, weight, provenance)
reflects a culture in which a sword was both a practical weapon and a bearer
of history, memory, and legal claim.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Weapons in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied trace a clear arc from ceremony to catastrophe.
The poem opens with weapons in their most ceremonial aspect: the sword-taking,
the gift of "[[horses-and-mounts|Ross]] und [[clothing-and-textiles|Gewand]]," the arming of warriors for display as much as
for combat. The Saxon war shifts weapons into their functional register — the
"scharfen Schwerter" that wound and kill. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder is the poem's
central weapon-event: a spear (the _Gêr_) thrown by [[hagen|Hagen]], aimed at the one
vulnerable spot on [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s body, transforms the poem's emotional register
from celebration to grief. The hall-battle consumes weapons systematically:
shields are hacked to pieces, swords shatter, and the warriors who once
received swords as gifts of honor die by the swords of their enemies. The poem
ends with the breaking of weapons — the material culture of heroic warfare is
destroyed along with the warriors who bore it.

## Notable Passages

> Wo sie das Schwert empfiengen mit Siegfried zu gleich.
> — Siegfried's sword-taking — the ceremony that transforms him from youth to warrior, the poem's founding act of weapon-investiture (002-044)

> Vierhundert Schwertdegen sollten gekleidet sein.
> — Four hundred sword-thanes — the scale of Siegfried's armed retinue, measured in the weapon that defines their status (002-049)

> Mit ihres Vaters Schwerte, das Balmung war genannt.
> — Balmung, the named sword — weapon as bearer of lineage, identity, and fate (003-203)

> Ein seiden Waffenhemde zog sich an die Maid.
> — Brünhild dons a silk mail-shirt — the arming of the warrior-queen, the convergence of martial equipment and femininity (007-161)

> Und von den scharfen Schwertern, womit man Wunden schlug.
> — And from the sharp swords with which wounds were struck — weapons as agents of bodily harm in the Saxon battle (004-255)

> "Mit dem guten Schwerte, geheißen Balmung."
> — With the good sword, called Balmung — the formula of the named weapon, repeated at moments of martial crisis (003-209)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of named swords parallels the _Chanson de
  Roland_ (Durendal) and the Arthurian tradition (Excalibur, Galatine), but
  [[balmung|Balmung]]'s trajectory — from Nibelung kings to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] to [[hagen|Hagen]] — is more
  complex than the simple hero-sword relationship of romance, tracing a path
  of contested ownership and violent transfer
- The poem's detailed attention to mail-shirts (_Waffenhemde_, _Brünne_) and
  their material qualities (silk, iron, brightness) reflects the actual armor
  technology of the Staufer period — the transition from leather and scale
  armor to full mail that was underway in the twelfth century
- The _Waffenspiel_ (tournament, 003-365, 022-071) in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
  occupies a position between older Germanic weapon-sport and the fully
  codified tournament of High Medieval chivalry — the poem captures a
  transitional moment in the cultural history of weapons
- Unlike the Norse _Völsunga saga_, where the sword Gram is reforged and
  passed from father to son, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's [[balmung|Balmung]] is seized as spoil —
  its transmission is violent rather than lineal, reflecting the poem's darker
  vision of heroic inheritance
