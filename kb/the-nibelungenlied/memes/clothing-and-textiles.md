# Clothing and Textiles in the Nibelungenlied

Few medieval epics devote as much attention to clothing as the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied.
From [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s arrival in [[worms|Worms]] clad in garments "die man für Königskinder
mochte tragen" to the silk-laden wedding celebrations, the poem treats textiles
not as decorative background but as primary instruments of social communication.
Clothing marks rank, signals intention, enacts diplomacy, and — in the poem's
darkest passages — becomes the surface upon which violence is inscribed. The
words _Kleid_ (garment), _Gewand_ (attire), and _Seide_ (silk) appear with
striking frequency, and the poem's descriptions of textile production, gift
exchange, and ceremonial dressing constitute a detailed anthropology of
thirteenth-century aristocratic material culture.

## Definition

In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's value system, clothing is a **material extension of
_[[ere|Êre]]_** (honor). A character's garments do not merely reflect their status;
they actively produce it. The poem distinguishes carefully between grades of
textile — Arabian silk (006-173), silk from Azagouc (007-202), gold-threaded
fabric (007-183) — and the quality of a character's clothing is presented as
direct evidence of their worth. The verb _zieren_ (to adorn, to decorate)
appears repeatedly in clothing contexts (002-030, 003-074, 003-089), linking
the act of dressing to the act of honoring. Conversely, the absence or
destruction of fine clothing signals catastrophe: the final battle reduces silk
and gold to blood-soaked rags.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's textile obsession reflects the historical reality of the
thirteenth-century silk trade. Silk from the Islamic world (Azagouc =
Azazouq/Az-Zawq, a trading center), Arabian silk, and gold-threaded fabrics
represented immense wealth. A single silk garment could cost more than a
knight's armor. By specifying the geographic origins of textiles, the poem
asserts the Burgundian court's connection to global trade networks and
reinforces its claim to cosmopolitan prestige. The attention to textile
production — spinning, weaving, sewing — also reflects the economic reality of
noblewomen's lives, where textile work was a primary form of domestic
governance.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Clothing in the poem functions as a **narrative barometer**. The early
adventures are saturated with descriptions of rich garments, new clothes for
every occasion, silk and gold in abundance. This textile plenitude mirrors the
poem's initial mood of celebration and expansion. As the narrative darkens,
clothing descriptions thin out. By the final adventures, the emphasis shifts
from what characters wear to what happens to their bodies. The poem's textile
economy — so lavish in the first half — is consumed by the fire and blood of
the hall-battle, leaving only the naked facts of violence.

## Notable Passages

> Mit Kleidern hieß ihn zieren seine Mutter — Queen Sieglinde adorns her son
> with garments before his departure. The mother's gift of clothing is the
> hero's first armor: before he wears mail, he wears his mother's love made
> visible in silk and gold. (002-030)

> In arabische Seide, so weiß als der Schnee — Arabian silk, white as snow,
> clothes the Burgundian knights. The simile is precise: silk is not merely
> valuable; it is luminous, and its luminosity is a form of public
> self-presentation that the poem's visual economy depends upon. (006-173)

> Der allerbesten Seide, die man jemals sah — the finest silk ever seen, worked
> with gold. The narrator's superlative is characteristic: the poem's
> rhetorical mode around clothing is one of competitive magnificence, each
> garment exceeding the last. (006-182)

## Comparative Notes

- The clothing inventories of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied have parallels in the
  _Spruchdichtung_ (gnomic poetry) tradition, where textile wealth is a
  standard topic of praise for noble patrons
- In the _Völsunga saga_, clothing receives far less attention — the Norse
  analogue is interested in weapons and magic objects rather than textiles,
  reflecting a different material culture and narrative aesthetic
- The poem's attention to female textile labor ([[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s sewing) connects to
  the broader medieval _textrix_ (female weaver/seamstress) topos, from
  Penelope to the embroiderers of the Bayeux Tapestry
