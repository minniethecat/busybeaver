# Triuwe

Triuwe is the Middle High German concept of loyalty, faithfulness, and fidelity — a cardinal virtue of medieval Germanic courtly culture. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, Triuwe governs the bonds between lords and vassals, between sworn companions, and between lovers. The epic's tragedy unfolds precisely through the violation of Triuwe: [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is a catastrophic breach of loyalty that sets the entire revenge cycle in motion.

## Definition

Triuwe encompasses multiple dimensions of loyalty: feudal Triuwe between a lord and his vassal ([[feudal-bond|Lehn]]smann), personal Triuwe between friends and sworn brothers, and marital Triuwe between husband and wife. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, characters consistently invoke Triuwe when making pledges — "I counsel you in Triuwen" (006-035) — and its absence marks moments of greatest moral failure (005-159, 005-236, 007-092).

### Violation and Consequence

The central moral event of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] — is a comprehensive violation of Triuwe on multiple levels: [[hagen|Hagen]] betrays his king's guest (hospitality Triuwe), his queen's husband (kinship Triuwe), and a sworn ally (feudal Triuwe). [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s subsequent revenge can be understood as the consequence of Triuwe's destruction: with loyalty shattered, only vengeance remains.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the Hohenstaufen period, Triuwe was the cement of feudal society — more important than written law, it was the unwritten code that made political and military cooperation possible. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, composed in this context, explores what happens when Triuwe fails: the entire social order collapses.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The epic presents Triuwe not as a simple good but as a value that can conflict with other values — particularly [[ere|Êre]] (honor) and [[revenge|Rache]] (revenge). [[hagen|Hagen]]'s loyalty to [[gunther|Gunther]] (feudal Triuwe) conflicts with his obligation to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] (guest-Triuwe). [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s marital Triuwe to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] drives her to violate kinship Triuwe with her own brothers. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied thus offers a sophisticated meditation on the tragic incompatibility of competing loyalties.
