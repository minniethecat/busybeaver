# Transport of the Nibelung Treasure

The transport of the Nibelung treasure (Adventure 19) is the event that brings the epic's most potent symbol of power — the [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelungenhort]] — from the dwarf kingdom to [[worms|Worms]]. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], after years of mourning [[siegfried|Siegfried]], is reconciled with her brothers. The treasure, [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s inheritance, is shipped to [[worms|Worms]] under the guardianship of [[alberich|Alberich]] the dwarf. Its arrival transforms [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] from bereaved widow into the wealthiest woman in Europe — and sets the stage for [[hagen|Hagen]]'s seizure and the treasure's ultimate concealment.

## Overview

- **Adventure(s)**: 19
- **Location**: [[nibelungenland|Nibelungenland]] (the dwarf kingdom) to [[worms|Worms]]
- **Key Participants**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (rightful heir), [[alberich|Alberich]] (treasure guardian), [[hagen|Hagen]] (later seize), [[gunther|Gunther]], [[gernot|Gernot]], [[geiselher|Geiselher]] (Burgundian kings)
- **Outcome**: [[wealth-and-gold|Treasure]] transported to [[worms|Worms]] under [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s control, later seized by [[hagen|Hagen]]

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

For four years after [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] has lived in grief-stricken isolation, refusing to speak to [[gunther|Gunther]] or [[hagen|Hagen]]. [[hagen|Hagen]], seeing the treasure as a source of power, convinces [[gunther|Gunther]] to reconcile with his sister. [[gunther|Gunther]] and his brothers approach [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], and [[gernot|Gernot]] — the brother she trusts — persuades her to accept the treasure as compensation for [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death.

### The Event

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] sends messengers to [[nibelungenland|Nibelungenland]] to bring the treasure to [[worms|Worms]]. [[alberich|Alberich]], the faithful dwarf king who once served [[siegfried|Siegfried]], oversees the transport of the hoard. The treasure is immense — "twelve wagons could not carry it away in four days and nights" (019-051). It includes gold, jewels, and the legendary sword [[balmung|Balmung]], which [[siegfried|Siegfried]] once wielded. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] distributes the treasure generously to knights and poor alike, winning allies and loyalty throughout the kingdom.

### Aftermath

[[hagen|Hagen]], watching [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] use the treasure to build a power base, becomes alarmed. He seizes the treasure keys on [[gunther|Gunther]]'s authority and sinks the hoard in the [[rhine|Rhine]] at a secret location known only to himself and [[gunther|Gunther]] (019-210). The loss of the treasure deepens [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s bitterness and gives her a second grievance — the theft of her inheritance — that she will pursue to the end.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

The treasure transport sets up the final conflict. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s wealth threatens [[hagen|Hagen]]'s influence; his seizure of the treasure gives [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] a concrete grievance that fuels her revenge. The treasure's location, known only to [[hagen|Hagen]] and [[gunther|Gunther]], becomes the epic's final secret — [[hagen|Hagen]]'s defiant silence at the end is the ultimate assertion of his will.

### Medieval Themes

The treasure embodies the medieval motif of gold as both gift and poison (the [[rhine|Rhine]]gold tradition). [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s generosity uses it as a political tool; [[hagen|Hagen]]'s seizure treats it as a threat to be neutralized. Sunken in the [[rhine|Rhine]], the treasure becomes a symbol of lost prosperity and the impossibility of return.
