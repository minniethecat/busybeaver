# Virtue and Excellence in the Nibelungenlied

_Tugend_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is the word through which the poem evaluates its
heroes. When the narrator wishes to praise a character, the formula is
invariable: "an allen Tugenden" (in all virtues). [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is "an allen
Tugenden ein Degen kühn und wohlgemut" (007-108). [[iring|Iring]] is "an allen Tugenden
ein [[knighthood-and-chivalric-identity|Ritter]] kühn und auch gut" (029-008). And [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] — the poem's moral
center — is "der Vater aller Tugend" (the father of all virtues, 037-276).
_Tugend_ is the sum of the qualities that make a knight worthy: courage,
loyalty, generosity, courtesy, and the capacity for right action. The poem's
tragedy is, in part, the systematic destruction of those who embody _Tugend_
most fully.

## Definition

MHG _tugent_ (from OHG _tugund_, "suitability, strength") carries a wider
semantic range than modern English "virtue." It denotes not only moral
excellence but also capability, prowess, and the qualities that make a person
worthy of their station. "Aller Fürsten Tugend" (the virtue of all princes,
016-334) is a formula for the ideal of aristocratic excellence. "Höfsche
Tugend" (courtly virtue, 020-219) specifies the courtly register: the virtues
appropriate to life at court — courtesy, measured speech, generosity, and the
proper performance of social roles. The phrase "bei aller Fürsten Tugend"
(by all princely virtue, 033-135, 037-228) serves as an oath-formula,
invoking the shared ethical standard of the warrior aristocracy.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The concept of _tugent_ in MHG courtly literature draws on multiple sources:
the classical virtue ethics transmitted through Cicero and Macrobius, the
Christian theological virtues, and the Germanic heroic tradition's emphasis on
personal excellence (_dugund_). In the courtly poetry of the Staufer period,
_tugent_ is increasingly codified as a set of specific courtly behaviors —
moderation (_mâze_), constancy (_staete_), generosity (_milte_), and the proper
performance of social roles. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied participates in this courtly
discourse of virtue while subjecting it to the pressure of the older heroic
ethos, in which virtue is demonstrated not in measured behavior but in
extremity.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's treatment of _Tugend_ is structured around the figure of [[rudiger|Rüdiger]].
Throughout the first half of the poem, _Tugend_ is an attribute distributed
among the major heroes — [[siegfried|Siegfried]], [[gunther|Gunther]], [[gernot|Gernot]] — without being
concentrated in any single figure. [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s introduction in Adventure 20
changes this: the Margrave of [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]] is the poem's most sustained portrait
of virtue in action. His hospitality to the Burgundians, his mediation between
[[etzel|Etzel]] and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], and his gift of his daughter in marriage are all
expressions of a _Tugend_ that encompasses both courtly grace and heroic
loyalty. The poem's final act subjects this virtue to an impossible test:
[[rudiger|Rüdiger]] must choose between his oath to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and his friendship with the
Burgundians. His death — "der Vater aller Tugend fand an [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] den [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]"
(037-276) — is the poem's most explicit statement that the world it describes
cannot sustain its own highest values.

## Notable Passages

> Er ist an allen Tugenden ein Degen kühn und wohlgemut.
> — The formula of complete knightly excellence — Siegfried as the benchmark of _Tugend_ (007-108)

> Sei aller Fürsten Tugend helft ihr zu jeder Frist.
> — The ideal of princely virtue invoked as a moral standard — at the hunt, on the eve of Siegfried's murder (016-334)

> "Seine große Tugend schafft mir Herzeleid."
> — Kriemhild's bitter acknowledgment: Rüdiger's very virtue is what causes her grief, because it makes him unwilling to betray his friends (028-013)

> Bei aller Fürsten Tugend geruht ihr gnädig zu sein.
> — The oath-formula invoking princely virtue — spoken at the moment when virtue can no longer protect anyone (037-228)

> Der Vater aller Tugend fand an Rüdiger den Tod.
> — The poem's epitaph for Rüdiger — "father of all virtues" — virtue itself dies with him (037-276)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Tugend_ as a quality concentrated in a
  single exemplary figure ([[rudiger|Rüdiger]]) and then destroyed parallels the
  Aristotelian pattern of tragedy, in which the fall of the virtuous character
  produces catharsis — an unusual convergence of Germanic heroic poetry and
  classical ethical thought
- Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_ develops a more elaborate and systematic
  theory of _tugent_ as the goal of chivalric education; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's
  treatment is less theoretical but more tragic — it asks not what _Tugend_ is
  but whether it can survive
- The Old Norse analogues do not develop a concept equivalent to _Tugend_ —
  the vocabulary of "dugund" in Old Norse is restricted to practical capability
  rather than moral excellence; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's courtly _Tugend_ is a
  distinctively High Medieval development
- [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s death "an [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]" (037-276) — the personification of virtue's
  death in a single character — is unique in medieval heroic literature; even
  the _Chanson de Roland_'s death of the exemplary warrior does not claim that
  virtue itself dies with him
