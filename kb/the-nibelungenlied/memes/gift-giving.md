# Gift-Giving

Gift-giving (_gabe_, _milte_) is the primary social currency of the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's courtly world. From [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s distribution of goods at
[[worms|Worms]] (002-047) to the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]] treasure's fateful journey, the exchange of
gifts governs political alliances, marks status hierarchies, and ultimately
serves as the catalyst for the poem's catastrophic conclusion.

## Definition

In the Middle High German courtly lexicon, _milte_ (generosity, open-handedness)
is the foremost virtue of a ruler, while _gabe_ (gift) is its material
expression. Gift-giving in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied operates within a system of
reciprocal obligation: a gift received demands a counter-gift, and the scale of
giving directly indexes a lord's _êre_ (honor) and political standing. The poem
distinguishes between _milte_ freely given and gifts demanded or extorted — a
distinction that becomes lethal in the second half.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

_[[generosity-and-liberality|Milte]]_ was the cardinal virtue of the medieval German court. Lords were judged
by their open-handedness — stinginess (_kargheit_) was a mark of the ignoble.
The _Freigebigkeit_ (generosity) literature of the 12th–13th centuries
emphasized the ruler's duty to distribute wealth rather than hoard it. The
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied draws on this tradition while exposing its dark side: the
obligation to give can be weaponized, and the circulation of gifts can become
the circulation of debts.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Gift-giving traces the poem's movement from courtly harmony to violent
dissolution. The early adventures celebrate the circulation of gifts as the
lifeblood of the court. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s arrival at [[worms|Worms]] with his [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]] wealth
introduces an asymmetry: his _milte_ exceeds Burgundian norms, provoking
[[hagen|Hagen]]'s anxiety. After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] transforms gift-giving into
a political instrument, using the treasure to build a power base at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s
court. The treasure's final sinking in the [[rhine|Rhine]] by [[hagen|Hagen]] is the anti-gift: the
refusal of circulation that precipitates the poem's catastrophe.

## Notable Passages

> Viel Ehre durch die Gaben, die spendet' ihre Hand:
> Sie dienten um die Gabe, die man da reichlich fand.
> — Kriemhild's gift-giving at Worms (002-047, 002-083)

> Er gibt so reiche Gaben: der Degen wähnet eben,
> Dass er nach unserm Leibe und Gut hab ausgesandt.
> — Hagen on Siegfried's excessive generosity (008-153)

> Sie bringt es mit Gaben wohl noch an den Tag,
> Dass ihrem Herzen nimmer der Tod des Helden lag.
> — Kriemhild's strategic gift-giving at Etzel's court (019-131)

## Comparative Notes

- The gift economy of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied reflects the historical practice of
  _potlatch_-style competitive giving documented in Germanic and Celtic courts
  of the early and high Middle Ages
- In the Old Norse analogues (_Völsunga saga_, _Þiðreks saga_), the treasure
  (Andvaranaut) is cursed — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied removes the supernatural curse
  and replaces it with the social dynamics of gift obligation
- Marcel Mauss's _The Gift_ (1925) provides a theoretical framework: the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied dramatizes the three obligations — to give, to receive, and to
  reciprocate — and the violence that erupts when the cycle breaks
