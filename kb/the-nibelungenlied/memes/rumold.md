Rumold is the kitchen-master (Küchenmeister) of the Burgundian court at [[worms|Worms]]. Despite his apparently domestic office, he is consistently described as a "Degen" (warrior/knight), marking him as a respected courtier whose practical wisdom provides the epic's most articulate counterpoint to the heroic fatalism that drives the Burgundians to their doom. His famous counsel against the journey to [[hun-court|Hunland]] is one of the poem's most quoted passages.

## Role at Court

Rumold is first introduced among the catalogue of courtiers at Worms: "Rumold der Küchenmeister, ein theurer Degen" — Rumold the kitchen-master, a worthy warrior (001-037). The juxtaposition of his domestic title with the martial honorific "Degen" is deliberate and recurring: the poem repeatedly emphasizes that Rumold is not merely a servant but a knight of standing.

> "Rumold der Küchenmeister, ein theurer Degen" (001-037)

He participates actively in the logistics of major court events, organizing provisions and receptions (004-386). When distinguished guests arrive, Rumold is among those who receive them with appropriate ceremony (012-213). He appears alongside other Burgundian courtiers such as [[sindold|Sindold]] and [[hunold|Hunold]] in the retinues that frame the Burgundian court's public face (009-137).

## Rumold's Counsel: The Voice of Prudence

Rumold's defining moment arrives in Adventure 24, when the Burgundian kings debate whether to accept [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s invitation to Hunland. [[hagen|Hagen]] has argued for accepting the invitation despite his own knowledge of the danger. Against this heroic fatalism, Rumold offers the voice of domestic prudence:

> "Wollt ihr nicht Hagen folgen, so rät euch Rumold" (024-177)

His advice is concrete and sensory: he reminds the kings that they have everything they need at home — "weiche Betten, gute Laken, schöne Frauen, guten Wein, und was man mehr bedarf" (soft beds, good linen, beautiful women, good wine, and whatever else one needs). His most famous line distills this domestic philosophy into an image of comfort and safety:

> "Hier, ihr Herren: das ist, was Rumold euch rath" (024-200)

When his advice is rejected, Rumold makes a personal pledge: "In Treuen, sprach da Rumold, ich will der Eine sein" — In faith, I will be the one who stays (024-205). This declaration is both loyal and tragic: Rumold will faithfully guard the kingdom while the kings march to their deaths.

## Steward of the Realm

When the Burgundian kings depart for Hunland, Rumold remains in Worms as the steward of the realm (024-173). He fulfills his role as kitchen-master on a grand scale, sending provisions and supplies to the traveling army (020-586). His practical competence as an administrator contrasts sharply with the self-destructive heroism of the warriors. In the poem's tragic architecture, Rumold is the road not taken — the embodiment of a wisdom that could have averted catastrophe had it been heeded.

Rumold also appears as a warrior-administrator in the border regions, described as "ein Degen auserkannt" — a chosen warrior (025-049), confirming that his domestic office does not diminish his martial standing.

## Character and Significance

Rumold occupies a structurally unique position in the Nibelungenlied. He is the only character who explicitly argues against the heroic course of action and survives precisely because he is left behind. His "soft bread and good wine" speech (the "Rumolds Rat") became proverbial in German literature as the archetypal voice of prudent domesticity opposing reckless adventure.

In the poem's moral universe, Rumold is neither vindicated nor mocked. His advice is objectively correct — the journey does lead to universal destruction — but the heroic code has no category for staying home. Rumold's tragedy is the tragedy of reason itself in an age of honor: he is right, and it does not matter.
