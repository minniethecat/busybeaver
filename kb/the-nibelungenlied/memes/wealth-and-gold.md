# Wealth and Gold in the Nibelungenlied

Gold saturates the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. It appears as the red-gold of bridles and
armor (003-111, 003-121), as the gift that binds loyalty (002-091, 004-347),
as the [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelungenhort]] — the immeasurable hoard that [[siegfried|Siegfried]] wins, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]
inherits, [[hagen|Hagen]] sinks in the [[rhine|Rhine]], and that finally lures the Burgundians to
their deaths. The poem tracks gold's transformation from the medium of social
cohesion — the generous lord who distributes "rothes Gold" (002-091) — into
an agent of destruction. The hoard that should have sustained a kingdom instead
consumes it: "was [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] noch mit diesem Schatze thut" (020-524).

## Definition

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's vocabulary of wealth is precise and differentiated. _Gold_
denotes the metal itself — the material of rings, bridles, shield-bosses, and
the endless river of gifts that flows through the poem's first half. _Schatz_
(treasure) refers to accumulated wealth, most crucially the [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelungenhort]] —
the treasure of the Nibelung kings that [[siegfried|Siegfried]] wins by conquest (003-191,
008-012). _Hort_ (hoard) carries a more archaic resonance, evoking the dragon's
hoard of heroic legend — wealth that is guarded, hidden, and ultimately cursed.
_[[gift-giving|Gabe]]_ (gift) is the transactional form that gold takes in the social world:
the gift establishes obligation, demonstrates _milte_ (generosity), and binds
recipient to giver in bonds of reciprocal duty.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the feudal economy of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, gold is not merely wealth — it
is the material basis of social relationships. The gift of gold creates
obligation; the distribution of treasure demonstrates _milte_ (generosity),
the cardinal virtue of the Germanic lord. The poem's critique of the gift
economy is implicit but devastating: gold that should bind society together
instead tears it apart. The [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelungenhort]] is the limit-case of this logic —
a treasure so vast that it cannot be distributed without destabilizing the
political order, and so dangerous that it must be destroyed.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied traces gold's trajectory from social lubricant to
apocalyptic catalyst. In the poem's first half, gold flows freely: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
distributes it to [[gunther|Gunther]]'s court, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] uses it to build a retinue, and
the Saxon war concludes with the orderly payment of ransoms and rewards. The
turning point is [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death: with the hero gone, the hoard becomes
an object of contention rather than distribution. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s sinking of the
treasure (019-158, 019-161) is the poem's great anti-gift — the negation of
_milte_, the refusal of circulation, the transformation of wealth into
absence. The final books show what happens when the gift economy collapses:
without gold to mediate relationships, only blood remains.

## Notable Passages

> Von rothem Golde, ihr Reitzeug wohlbestellt.
> — Of red gold, their riding gear well-appointed — the poem's characteristic image of gold as adornment and status marker (003-111)

> Noch mehr des rothen Goldes von Nibelungenland.
> — Yet more of the red gold from Nibelungenland — the seemingly inexhaustible wealth that Siegfried brings (003-195)

> "Liebe Märe, so geb ich dir mein Gold."
> — "Dear tidings, I will give you my gold" — Kriemhild's attempt to purchase information about Siegfried's death (004-347)

> Bevor von Tronje Hagen den Schatz also verbarg.
> — Before Hagen of Tronje thus hid the treasure — the act that defines the poem's second half (019-161)

> Was Kriemhild noch mit diesem Schatze thut.
> — What Kriemhild will yet do with this treasure — the question that haunts the poem after the hoard is sunk (020-524)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelungenhort]] has deep roots in Germanic legend — in the Old Norse
  tradition (_Völsunga saga_, _Reginsmál_), the hoard is explicitly cursed
  by the dwarf Andvari, and each successive owner meets a violent death; the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied softens the explicit curse but retains the structural logic
  of wealth-as-destruction
- The sinking of the treasure in the [[rhine|Rhine]] parallels the _[[rhine|Rhein]]gold_ of
  Wagner's _Ring_ cycle, where the gold returns to the river at the cycle's
  end — in both works, gold extracted from nature returns to nature, but
  only after destroying the human world that claimed it
- Compared to the _Chanson de Roland_, where wealth is largely invisible
  (the poem is concerned with honor, not gold), the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's
  relentless attention to material wealth — who has it, who gives it, who
  takes it, who destroys it — marks it as unusually economic for a heroic epic
