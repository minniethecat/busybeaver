# Kriemhild's Marriage to Etzel

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s marriage to [[etzel|Etzel]] (Adventures 20–22) is the transformative event that bridges the two halves of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. After thirteen years of mourning [[siegfried|Siegfried]], [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] accepts the proposal of [[etzel|Etzel]], King of the Huns — the most powerful ruler in Europe. The marriage is brokered by [[rudiger|Margrave Rüdiger]] of [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]], and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s journey eastward across the [[danube|Danube]] transforms her from a widow in obscurity into a queen with the power to destroy her enemies.

## Overview

- **Adventure(s)**: 20–22
- **Location**: [[worms|Worms]], [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]] ([[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s castle), [[etzels-court|Etzelnburg]] ([[etzel|Etzel]]'s fortress)
- **Key Participants**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (bride), [[etzel|Etzel]] (groom), [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] (ambassador and escort), Gotlind ([[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s wife), [[hagen|Hagen]] (hostile Burgundian advisor)
- **Outcome**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] becomes Queen of the Huns; thirteen years later, she will invite the Burgundians to their doom

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

After the loss of the Nibelung treasure, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s position in [[worms|Worms]] becomes untenable. She has lost her husband, her wealth, and her trust in her brothers. When [[etzel|Etzel]], widowed since Helke's death, seeks a new wife, [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] recommends [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (020-003). [[etzel|Etzel]], though a pagan, is drawn to her beauty and noble blood (020-009). [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] travels to [[worms|Worms]] to make the offer.

### The Event

The Burgundian court receives [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s proposal with caution — [[hagen|Hagen]] warns that [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] will use her new power to harm them (020-151). But [[gunther|Gunther]], [[gernot|Gernot]], and [[geiselher|Geiselher]] approve, and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] herself accepts after [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] swears an oath to protect her from any harm (020-180). She departs with [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s escort, traveling through Bavaria and Austria. At [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]], [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s wife Gotlind welcomes her warmly (021-045). She continues to [[etzels-court|Etzelnburg]], where [[etzel|Etzel]] greets her with magnificent ceremony, and the marriage is celebrated with a feast lasting seventeen days (022-130).

### Aftermath

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] becomes Queen of the Huns. For thirteen years, she lives quietly, bearing [[etzel|Etzel]] a son named [[ortlieb|Ortlieb]]. But her grief for [[siegfried|Siegfried]] and her hatred for [[hagen|Hagen]] never fade. With her new power, she begins to plan — and when she invites her brothers to a feast at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, the trap is set. The marriage thus functions as the epic's second great hinge, transforming [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] from victim to avenger.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

This marriage is the structural turning point of the epic. It moves the action from [[worms|Worms]] to the Hun court and gives [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] the power to force the confrontation she desires. Without it, the Burgundians would never have traveled to [[hunland|Hunland]], and the epic's second half — the catastrophe — could not occur.

### Medieval Themes

The marriage dramatizes the dual nature of political alliance. For [[etzel|Etzel]], it is a love-match and a dynastic union; for [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], it is a means to an end. The medieval ideal of marriage as reconciliation and peacemaking is inverted: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s wedding is a declaration of war.
