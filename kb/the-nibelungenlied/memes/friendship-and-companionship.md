# Friendship and Companionship in the Nibelungenlied

_Freund_ is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's most frequently deployed relational term and
its most unstable. It appears in the poem's second stanza ("Mit lieben Freunden
wollt er ein Hofgelag begehn," 002-038) and echoes through every adventure
until the final slaughter. But _Freund_ in the poem means several different
things that modern English "friend" only partially captures: it can denote a
kinsman ("seinen nächsten Freunden," 004-129), a political ally ("Habt ihr nun
guter Freunde," 004-026), a vassal ("Freund und Mannen," 004-100), or a
personal intimate ("zu Freunden kühne zwölf Mann," 003-205). The poem exploits
this semantic range to explore the vulnerability of social bonds: when the same
word can mean both "kinsman" and "ally," and both can be betrayed, the poem
asks whether any form of _Freundschaft_ can survive the pressures of honor,
vengeance, and conflicting obligation. The answer is no — the poem systematically
dismantles every friendship it builds, revealing _Freund_ as a word that names
the bonds the poem's world cannot sustain.

## Definition

MHG _vriunt_ (noun) and _vriuntschaft_ (friendship) derive from OHG _friunt_,
from Proto-Germanic _*frijōndz_, the present participle of _*frijōną_ "to
love." The word's etymology points to its original meaning: a _Freund_ is one
who loves, one who is bound by affection and obligation. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied,
the semantic field of _Freund_ is broad: it encompasses (1) blood kinship —
"seinen nächsten Freunden" (004-129) denotes the king's closest relatives;
(2) political alliance — "Freunde suchen" (004-071) means to seek allies for
war; (3) vassalic loyalty — "Freund und Mannen" (004-100) pairs the personal
bond with the feudal; and (4) personal affection — "liebe Freunde" (003-017)
marks emotional closeness. The poem moves between these meanings without
signaling the transition, making _Freund_ a word whose precise sense must be
inferred from context — and whose ambiguity is itself a source of tragic
misunderstanding.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's use of _Freund_ reflects the complex semantics of
friendship in medieval German culture. In the Staufer period, _vriuntschaft_
could denote the bond between kinsmen (the older, Germanic meaning), the bond
between lord and vassal (the feudal meaning), or the bond of personal affection
(the courtly meaning, influenced by Cicero's _De Amicitia_ via the monastic
tradition). The poem exploits all three meanings simultaneously: when [[hagen|Hagen]]
murders [[siegfried|Siegfried]], he violates _Freund_ in every sense — he kills his lord's
kinsman-by-marriage, his lord's political ally, and (arguably) his own personal
friend. This overdetermination makes the murder more than a political crime; it
is a crime against the very idea of _Freundschaft_ as a social bond.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces _Freund_ from the poem's founding value to its most bitterly
ironic word. The opening adventures establish friendship as the poem's
emotional and political baseline: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is advised by friends (003-017),
welcomed as a friend (003-312), and mourned by friends (004-328). The
vocabulary of _Freund_ saturates the first half of the poem, creating an
impression of a world held together by bonds of affection and alliance. The
murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] in Adventure 16 is the turning point: from this moment,
every invocation of _Freund_ is shadowed by the knowledge that friendship can
be betrayed. In the second half, _Freund_ becomes a word of bitter irony:
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s invitation to her "friends" (her brothers, her kinsmen) to attend
her wedding is in fact an invitation to their deaths. The poem's final
catastrophe is the annihilation of _Freundschaft_ itself — the destruction
not just of individual friends but of the possibility of friendship as a
social bond.

## Notable Passages

> Mit lieben Freunden wollt er ein Hofgelag begehn.
> — With dear friends he wished to hold a court feast — the poem's first
> deployment of _Freund_; the word marks the Burgundian court as a community
> of affection, the normative baseline the poem will destroy (002-038)

> Ihm riethen seine Freunde und Die in seinem Lehn.
> — His friends and his vassals advised him — _Freund_ as the source of
> counsel; the hero's community of advisors is defined by friendship (003-017)

> Und haben ihn zum Freunde; es geziemt uns wahrlich mehr.
> — And have him as a friend; it befits us much more — Gernot's proposal;
> _Freund_ as a political achievement, a relationship to be actively
> constructed rather than passively inherited (003-312)

> Habt ihr nun guter Freunde, so laßt es bald ersehn.
> — If you now have good friends, let it soon be seen — _Freund_ as a
> testable quality; friendship is measured by the willingness to fight for
> one's friend in time of need (004-026)

> Stäten Freunden klagen soll man des Herzens Noth.
> — To steadfast friends one should lament the heart's distress — _Freund_
> as the recipient of emotional truth; the steadfast friend is the one to
> whom grief can be confided (004-067)

> Der König hieß besenden Freund und Mannen insgemein.
> — The king ordered to summon friends and vassals alike — the pairing of
> _Freund_ and _Mannen_; the personal bond and the feudal bond as parallel
> forms of obligation, both summonable by royal command (004-100)

> Da beklagten ihre Freunde die Gefallnen in dem Streit.
> — There their friends mourned the fallen in the battle — _Freund_ as the
> community of mourners; friendship extends beyond death in the obligation
> of grief and public lamentation (004-328)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Freund_ contrasts with the _Chanson de
  Roland_, where friendship (_amisté_) between Roland and Oliver is
  idealized and unbreakable — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied shows friendship as
  always vulnerable to conflicting obligations, never pure and never secure
- In the classical tradition of Cicero's _De Amicitia_, friendship is a bond
  between virtuous men that transcends utility — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's
  _Freundschaft_ is never transcendent; it is always embedded in political
  and kinship structures that can override it
- The Old Norse _Völsunga saga_ uses _vinr_ (friend) with a similar semantic
  range, but the saga's mythological framework makes friendship subordinate
  to fate — in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, friendship fails because of human choices,
  not divine necessity
- The poem's ambiguity about whether _Freund_ means "kinsman" or "ally" or
  "intimate" reflects a transitional moment in the history of the word —
  between the older Germanic meaning (kinsman) and the emerging courtly
  meaning (personal affection) — and the poem exploits this ambiguity to
  tragic effect
