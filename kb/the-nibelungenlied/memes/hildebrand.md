# Hildebrand

Hildebrand (Meister Hildebrand) is the aged master-at-arms and most trusted retainer of [[dietrich]]. A veteran warrior renowned for his wisdom and battle prowess, he serves as Dietrich's adviser throughout the Burgundians' fateful visit to [[Etzel]]'s court and fights with grim ferocity in the climactic massacre of Adventure 38, where he kills his own nephew [[Wolfhart]] in single combat.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Meister (Master-at-Arms) to [[dietrich]]
- **Affiliation**: The retinue of [[dietrich|Dietrich von Bern]], guest-warriors at [[Etzel]]'s court
- **Family**: Uncle (Oheim) of [[Wolfhart]]
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 28 — Dietrich's retinue receives news of the Burgundians' arrival (028-029)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: Pre-Arrival (Adventures 1–19)

Hildebrand does not appear in the first part of the epic, which is set primarily at the Burgundian court in [[Worms]] and follows [[Siegfried]]'s courtship of [[Kriemhild]]. As a warrior of Dietrich's company based in the Hunnish realm, his story belongs to the second part.

### Part II: The Fall of the Burgundians (Adventures 20–39)

Hildebrand enters the narrative in Adventure 28, when [[Kriemhild]] appeals to Dietrich's retinue for support against the Burgundians. Hildebrand speaks on behalf of the warriors, respectfully declining her request while acknowledging the bond of loyalty they owe (028-029)(028-037). His measured response establishes him as a voice of seasoned judgment within Dietrich's warband.

When the Burgundians arrive at [[Etzel]]'s court, Hildebrand is among the warriors who observe the mounting tensions. In Adventure 31, he responds to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s renewed demands for vengeance with pragmatic counsel, questioning how such an attack could be justified (031-217)(031-225).

Hildebrand's most significant actions occur in Adventure 38, the final and bloodiest chapter of the epic. Dietrich sends him to parley with the Burgundians, but the negotiations fail. When the general slaughter erupts, Hildebrand fights with the ferocity of a man possessed, described as raging as though mad (038-189). He engages his own nephew [[Wolfhart]] in mortal combat — a duel that encapsulates the tragedy of the poem, as kinship bonds shatter under the weight of feudal obligation. Hildebrand kills [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] but is himself gravely wounded in the exchange (038-259)(038-285)(038-293).

Bleeding and nearing death, Hildebrand returns to [[dietrich]] to deliver the bitter news — that all the Burgundians are dead, and with them [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] and every warrior of Dietrich's own company (038-329)(038-337). His final report serves as the epic's devastating summation of the catastrophe.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Hildebrand personifies the Germanic heroic ideal of unconditional loyalty (*triuwe*). His every action is governed by his oath to [[dietrich]]. When [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] attempts to enlist him in her vendetta, he refuses not from personal scruple but because Dietrich has not sanctioned it. Even the horror of killing his own nephew cannot break this bond — Hildebrand fights and kills [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] because the logic of the battlefield leaves no alternative.

### Virtues & Flaws

Hildebrand embodies the wisdom of age — he is consistently called "the old" (*der alte*) and "Meister," titles that mark him as a counsellor rather than merely a fighter. Yet in Adventure 38, his experience gives way to a berserk fury that the text explicitly compares to madness (038-189). This duality — the wise adviser who becomes an indiscriminate killer — reflects the poem's darker vision of the heroic code: even the most disciplined warrior is ultimately consumed by violence.

## Death

Hildebrand survives the events of the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied*. Though gravely wounded by [[Wolfhart]] in their duel (038-285)(038-293), he lives to deliver the news of the catastrophe to [[dietrich]]. In the broader Germanic heroic tradition — particularly the Old High German *Hildebrandslied* — Hildebrand's fate is more ambiguous: the fragmentary older poem ends with him facing his own son Hadubrand in a tragic duel. The *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* makes no reference to this earlier legend.

## Scholarly Notes

- Hildebrand is one of the oldest figures in the Germanic heroic corpus, appearing in the 9th-century *Hildebrandslied* as the protagonist of a father-son duel that prefigures the kin-slaying motif of Adventure 38.
- His title "Meister" (*meister* in Middle High German) denotes a senior military instructor or master-at-arms, reflecting the hierarchical structure of a medieval war-band.
- The Hildebrand of the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* differs markedly from the *Hildebrandslied* figure: in the older poem he is an exile returning home; in the epic he is Dietrich's settled retainer, and the tragedy of kin-slaying is displaced onto the uncle-nephew relationship with [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] rather than the father-son relationship with Hadubrand.
