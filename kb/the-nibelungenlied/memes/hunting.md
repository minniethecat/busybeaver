# Hunting in the Nibelungenlied

Adventure 16 of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — "Wie [[siegfried|Siegfried]] erschlagen ward" — is one
of the most famous hunting scenes in medieval literature. What begins as a
display of courtly _[[entertainment-and-festivity|Kurzweil]]_ (pastime, amusement) becomes the setting for the
poem's central act of violence. The hunt is not incidental to [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
death; it is the mechanism that isolates him from the protection of the court,
strips him of his armor, and delivers him — thirsty from the chase — to the
spring where [[hagen|Hagen]]'s spear finds the one vulnerable spot between his shoulders.
The hunt is the poem's most elaborate representation of how courtly culture's
own rituals can become instruments of destruction.

## Definition

The poem uses _Jagd_ (hunt) and the compound _Jagdgesellen_ (hunting
companions) to denote both the activity and its participants. Adventure 15
ends with the question "Wollt ihr die Jagd bestellen?" (Will you arrange the
hunt?, 015-120) — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s eager query that sets the fatal sequence in
motion. The hunt in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a royal pastime (_[[entertainment-and-festivity|Kurzweil]]_,
016-125), a display of aristocratic prowess and masculine competition, and —
crucially — a space outside the normal protections of the court. In the forest,
the rules that govern behavior in the hall do not apply.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The hunt was the preeminent aristocratic pastime of the High Middle Ages.
Frederick Barbarossa and Henry the Lion were both passionate hunters, and the
elaborate ritual of the chase — with its specialized vocabulary, its hierarchy
of participants, and its strict protocols — was a central expression of
aristocratic identity. The hunt was also a space of licensed violence: the
killing of animals was a display of the same martial prowess that, in war, was
directed at human enemies. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied exploits the ambiguity of the
hunt as a space where the normal rules are suspended — where, as [[hagen|Hagen]] knows, a
man can be killed and it can be called an accident.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The hunt in Adventure 16 is a masterpiece of narrative irony. Every detail of
the hunt's description — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s eagerness, his prowess in the chase, the
elaborate preparations — is shadowed by the reader's knowledge of what will
happen. The hunt that should be the poem's supreme display of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
mastery becomes the occasion of his death. The forest — the space outside the
court — is the space where the court's protection does not reach. And the
spring where [[siegfried|Siegfried]] drinks is the poem's most potent image of vulnerability:
the hero who has slain a dragon, defeated armies, and won a queen bends to
drink water and is killed. The hunt transforms courtly culture's most
celebrated ritual into an instrument of murder.

## Notable Passages

> "Wollt ihr die Jagd bestellen?" — "Das thu ich," sprach da Hagen.
> — Siegfried proposes the hunt; Hagen accepts — the fatal agreement is made in the language of courtly pastime (015-120)

> Von den Jagdgesellen ward umhergestellt.
> — The hunting companions spread out — the elaborate ritual of the medieval chase (016-053)

> "Ich schaff uns Jagdgesellen eine Kurzweil."
> — Siegfried promises his hunting companions amusement — _Kurzweil_ (pastime) is the courtly term for the hunt that will become the setting for his death (016-125)

> Die stolzen Jagdgesellen rief man zu Tisch herbei.
> — The proud hunting companions are called to table — the hunt's festive conclusion, moments before the killing (016-190)

> Die zur Jagd mit Siegfried geritten jenen Tag.
> — Those who rode to the hunt with Siegfried that day — the formula that will be repeated as an epitaph (017-111)

## Comparative Notes

- The hunting scene in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied has no direct parallel in the Old
  Norse analogues — the _Völsunga saga_ has Sigurd killed in bed, not at a
  spring after a hunt; the hunt setting is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied-poet's original
  contribution to the [[siegfried|Siegfried]] legend
- The motif of the hero killed while drinking at a spring has been connected
  to ancient Indo-European myth (the vulnerable hero, the sacred spring), but
  the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's integration of this motif into the elaborate ritual of
  the courtly hunt is distinctively medieval
- The hunt in medieval romance (e.g., Chrétien de Troyes's _Erec et Enide_)
  typically serves as the prelude to adventure — a knight follows a hunted
  animal into the forest and encounters marvels; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied inverts
  this pattern: the hunt leads not to adventure but to death
- [[church-and-religion|Gott]]fried von Strassburg's _Tristan_ contains elaborate hunting scenes that
  celebrate the hero's mastery of the chase as a courtly art; the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's hunting scene is their dark double
