# Fall of the Burgundians

The fall of the Burgundians (Adventures 33–39) is the catastrophic climax of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s methodical destruction of [[gunther|Gunther]]'s entire court at [[etzels-court|Etzel's court]] is one of medieval literature's most absolute visions of total destruction.

What begins as a feast of apparent reconciliation, orchestrated after thirteen years of planning, escalates through provocation, battle, fire, and execution. The [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelung treasure]]'s location dies with [[hagen|Hagen]], and the epic ends with [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] herself slain by [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] — leaving only corpses and silence.

## Overview

- **Adventures**: 33–39
- **Location**: [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court ([[etzels-court|Etzelnburg]]), the Hun capital
- **Key Participants**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], [[hagen|Hagen]], [[gunther|Gunther]], [[gernot|Gernot]], [[geiselher|Geiselher]], [[etzel|Etzel]], [[rudiger|Rüdiger]], [[dietrich|Dietrich von Bern]]
- **Outcome**: Burgundian army annihilated; all kings dead; [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] killed by [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

Thirteen years after marrying [[etzel|Etzel]], [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] invites her brothers to a feast at the Hun court. [[hagen|Hagen]] warns that it is a death trap, but [[feudal-bond|feudal obligation]] compels the Burgundians to accept.

The journey eastward is marked by portents: the [[danube|Danube]] ferryman is killed by [[hagen|Hagen]], the water nymphs at the [[danube-crossing|Danube crossing]] prophesy that only the chaplain will return alive.

### The Event

The catastrophe unfolds in stages. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] demands the treasure; [[hagen|Hagen]] refuses. Provocations escalate. [[bloedel|Bloedel]] attacks the Burgundian squires; [[dankwart|Dankwart]] kills [[bloedel|Bloedel]] in response. The hall battle begins.

[[combat-and-warfare|War]]rior after warrior falls — [[gernot|Gernot]], [[geiselher|Geiselher]], [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] (forced by conflicting [[oath|oaths]] to fight his own friends), [[volker|Volker]] the minstrel, and countless others. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] orders the hall set ablaze; the Burgundians drink the blood of the fallen to survive the flames.

Finally, only [[gunther|Gunther]] and [[hagen|Hagen]] remain. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] has [[gunther|Gunther]] executed, then demands the treasure's location. When [[hagen|Hagen]] refuses, she beheads him with [[balmung|Balmung]] — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s sword. [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]], appalled, strikes [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] down.

### Aftermath

There is no aftermath — only silence. The final stanza declares "here the tale has an end." The Burgundian kingdom is extinct; the treasure is lost forever; [[etzel|Etzel]] and Dietrich are left to mourn in a world emptied of meaning. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied offers no consolation, no restoration of order.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

The fall of the Burgundians is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's telos — the endpoint toward which every prior event has been moving. It fulfills the logic of [[revenge]], the logic of [[ere|Êre]], and the logic of [[fate-and-doom|fate]] simultaneously.

The epic's refusal to provide consolation or moral resolution is its most distinctive feature: this is tragedy without catharsis. The heroic world's organizing principles — honor, loyalty, vengeance — destroy the world they were meant to sustain.

### Medieval Themes

The catastrophe at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court embodies the medieval understanding of the [[heroic-code|heroic ethos]]'s tragic dimension. Every virtue of the Burgundian warriors — [[hagen|Hagen]]'s loyalty, [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s sense of obligation, the kings' solidarity — becomes an instrument of their destruction.

The hall burning, in particular, reverses the symbolic order of the feudal hall: the center of hospitality, community, and song becomes a furnace of death.
