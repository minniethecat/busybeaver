# Boldness and Daring in the Nibelungenlied

_Kühnheit_ (boldness, daring) is one of the most frequently invoked heroic
qualities in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. The adjective _kühn_ (bold) appears as an
epithet for [[siegfried|Siegfried]] more than any other descriptor — "der kühne [[siegfried|Siegfried]]"
(003-033, 003-115), "der kühne Mann" (002-007, 003-179, 003-196), "der kühne
Degen" (002-013, 003-239) — and the abstract noun _Kühnheit_ marks the quality
as a defining attribute of the poem's heroic world. Yet boldness in the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is double-edged: it is the source of glory and the cause of
death. The poem opens by promising tales "von großer Kühnheit" (of great
boldness, 001-002) and closes with the reflection that boldness could not save
its heroes from destruction — "Was half da Kraft und Kühnheit? sie fanden doch
den [[death-in-the-nibelungenlied|Tod]]" (What availed strength and boldness? They found death nonetheless,
032-059).

## Definition

_Kühnheit_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied encompasses physical courage, audacity of
spirit, and the willingness to act without hesitation in the face of danger.
It is closely allied with _Kraft_ (strength) — the two are paired formulaically
as "Kraft und Kühnheit" (001-032, 027-068, 032-059) — but _Kühnheit_ is the
volitional element: strength is what one has, boldness is what one does with it.
The _kühne_ hero is not merely strong but _eager_ to act, sometimes rashly,
often gloriously. This eagerness distinguishes the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's conception
of boldness from the more measured _mâze_ (moderation) of courtly literature:
the poem's heroes are magnificent precisely because they exceed measure.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the medieval Germanic heroic tradition, boldness (_Kühnheit_, Old High
German _kuonheit_) was the cardinal heroic virtue. The _[[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]slied_
celebrates the _kuon_ warrior who faces impossible odds without retreating,
and Old Norse poetry praises the _djarfr_ (bold) hero who laughs in the face
of death. This tradition values boldness as an absolute good — the bold hero
is the complete hero, and hesitation is shame. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied inherits
this valuation but complicates it: the poem's bold heroes are magnificent,
but their boldness does not save them. The poem thus stands at the boundary
between the old heroic ethos, where boldness is its own justification, and
a newer, more reflective sensibility that asks what boldness costs.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Boldness in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied follows a tragic arc. In the poem's first half,
_Kühnheit_ is unambiguously glorious — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s bold deeds bring victory,
honor, and love. The Saxon war (Adventure 4) is the high point of this
trajectory: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s boldness wins a war and earns him the gratitude of
kings. But the poem's second half dismantles this valuation. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s boldness
is the boldness of murder; [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s boldness is the boldness of revenge;
and the final verdict — "Was half da Kraft und Kühnheit?" — suggests that
the heroic virtues are ultimately powerless against fate. The poem does not
reject boldness, but it shows that boldness alone cannot build a world that
lasts.

## Notable Passages

> Von kühner Recken Streiten mögt ihr nun Wunder hören — the poem's
> invitation to its audience promises tales of bold warriors in combat.
> _Kühnheit_ is the first heroic quality named in the poem, establishing
> it as the keynote of the entire narrative. (001-004)

> Er ist so kühnes Sinnes, man seh ihn freundlich an — Hagen's description
> of Siegfried to the Burgundian court: "He is of such bold spirit." The
> line captures the ambivalence of boldness — it commands respect but also
> wariness, admiration but also fear. (003-235)

> Was half da Kraft und Kühnheit? sie fanden doch den Tod — the poem's
> elegy for its heroes, and its most devastating assessment of the heroic
> code. Strength and boldness, the two pillars of heroic identity, could
> not prevent the final catastrophe. (032-059)

## Comparative Notes

- The Old Norse _Völsunga saga_ presents Sigurd's boldness (_djörfung_) in
  similar terms — he is the boldest of heroes, and his boldness is both his
  glory and his doom
- In the _[[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]slied_, the old hero's boldness (_kuonheit_) in facing
  his own son in single combat is presented as tragic nobility — the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied expands this tragic dimension to encompass an entire
  heroic world
- The courtly romance tradition (Hartmann von Aue, Wolfram von Eschenbach)
  often critiques excessive boldness (_übermuot_) as a form of pride — the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied occupies a middle ground, celebrating boldness while
  documenting its lethal consequences
