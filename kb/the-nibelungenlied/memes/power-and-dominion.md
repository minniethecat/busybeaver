# Power and Dominion in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a poem about power: who has it, how it is acquired, what
it costs, and how it destroys those who wield it. The MHG term _Gewalt_ (from
OHG _giwalt_, related to _waltan_ — "to rule, to prevail") encompasses both
legitimate authority and raw force, and the poem systematically exploits this
semantic ambiguity. When [[siegfried|Siegfried]] arrives at [[worms|Worms]], [[hagen|Hagen]] warns [[gunther|Gunther]] that
[[siegfried|Siegfried]] "mit Gewalt erwerben kann" the Nibelung treasure (003-053) — the
phrase means both "by force" and "by legitimate power." The poem's political
geography is a map of _Gewalt_ relations: [[siegfried]]'s conquest of the
Nibelung land, [[gunther]]'s precarious authority in Burgundy, [[brunhild]]'s
superhuman _Gewalt_ that must be subdued through deception, and [[kriemhild]]'s
acquisition of _Gewalt_ through her marriage to [[etzel]]. The poem's final
catastrophe is the ultimate demonstration that _Gewalt_, once unleashed, cannot
be contained by the courtly order that it was meant to serve.

## Definition

MHG _gewalt_ (feminine noun) carries a dual meaning that modern English cannot
capture in a single word: it denotes both **legitimate authority** (_potestas_,
dominion, jurisdiction) and **violent force** (_violentia_, coercion, physical
might). In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, this ambiguity is not a lexical accident but a
structural principle. The poem repeatedly stages moments where the two meanings
collide: a character claims _Gewalt_ in the sense of legitimate authority, and
the claim is tested by _Gewalt_ in the sense of violent force. The term appears
in feudal contexts (a lord's _Gewalt_ over his territory), martial contexts
(_Gewalt_ exerted in battle), and interpersonal contexts (one person's _Gewalt_
over another). The poem's political theology is implicit in this vocabulary:
all authority ultimately rests on the capacity for violence, and the courtly
order is the fragile achievement of keeping that violence in reserve.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Gewalt_ reflects the political theology of
the Staufer period (c. 1138–1254), when the relationship between _potestas_
(spiritual authority) and _violentia_ (secular force) was a central concern of
canon law and political theory. The Investiture Controversy had sharpened the
distinction between authority and force, but the poem's world predates this
distinction: in the heroic age, _Gewalt_ is unitary. The poem's political
geography — a patchwork of kingdoms (Burgundy, [[xanten|Xanten]], Iceland, the Nibelung
land, [[etzel|Etzel]]'s Hunnish empire) — maps the distribution of _Gewalt_ across
persons rather than institutions. There is no abstract "state" in the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, only persons who possess _Gewalt_ and persons who do not. This
is the political world of the _Personenverbandsstaat_ (personal-association
state) that medieval historians have identified as characteristic of the
Germanic kingdoms before the reception of Roman law.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's narrative trajectory is a study in the concentration and
precipitation of _Gewalt_. In the first half, _Gewalt_ is distributed across
multiple centers: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s personal force, [[gunther|Gunther]]'s royal authority,
[[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s physical prowess, [[hagen|Hagen]]'s operational control. The poem's plot
consists in the gradual concentration of _Gewalt_ in fewer and fewer hands.
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death eliminates the poem's most powerful individual center of
force. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s disposal of the treasure removes the material basis of
Burgundian power. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s marriage to [[etzel|Etzel]] gives her access to the poem's
largest concentration of military force. By the final adventures, _Gewalt_ has
been reduced to a binary: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s Hunnish army versus the Burgundian
remnant. The poem's final image — "Das war Gewalt bewiesen, daß Niemand da mehr
stritt" (033-163) — is the logical endpoint of this concentration: _Gewalt_
has been demonstrated so completely that no one remains to exercise it. Power,
in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, is a substance that consumes its own bearers.

## Notable Passages

> Mit Gewalt erwerben kann Niemand die Magd.
> — "No one can win the maiden by force" — Hagen's warning to Siegfried;
> the poem's first statement on the limits of _Gewalt_, establishing that
> force alone cannot secure what the will withholds (003-053)

> herrlich wenden alle die Gewalt
> — "Masterfully direct all power" — the description of Siegfried's
> comprehensive command of _Gewalt_, establishing him as the poem's supreme
> wielder of force before his first appearance at court (002-103)

> ihr, Frau, gebieten mit all der Gewalt
> — "You, lady, command with all authority" — Gunther to Brunhild; the irony
> of his deference to her domestic _Gewalt_ when her physical _Gewalt_ has
> been stripped from her through his own deception (018-009)

> durch Furcht und Gewalt
> — "Through fear and force" — the twin foundations of political obedience;
> Hagen's service to Kriemhild is characterized not as loyalty but as
> compulsion (019-108)

> Gewalt die allerhöchste, die Helke je gewann
> — "The highest authority that Helke ever won" — _Gewalt_ as legitimate
> queenship, the consort's authority exercised through courtly channels
> rather than martial force (020-379)

> Das war Gewalt bewiesen, daß Niemand da mehr stritt
> — "That was power demonstrated, such that no one there fought any longer" —
> the poem's final deployment of _Gewalt_: force has consumed its own
> conditions of possibility; there is no one left to wield it and no one left
> to resist (033-163)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's dual concept of _Gewalt_ (authority/force) parallels the
  Latin distinction between _potestas_ and _violentia_ that was central to
  medieval political thought — but where canon lawyers worked to separate the
  two, the poem insists on their identity
- In the _Chanson de Roland_, Charlemagne's _Gewalt_ is divinely sanctioned and
  ultimately vindicated — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied offers no such transcendental
  guarantee; power is immanent, personal, and self-consuming
- The Old Norse _Völsunga saga_ treats power through the lens of kinship and
  feud rather than feudal hierarchy — _Gewalt_ in the saga is distributed
  along family lines, while the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied maps it onto the geography of
  courts and kingdoms
- The poem's treatment of _Gewalt_ anticipates the political pessimism of
  Machiavelli — the separation of authority from force is a fragile illusion,
  and the moment of its collapse is the moment of political catastrophe
