# Nibelungenland

Nibelungenland is the legendary kingdom of the Nibelungs, described in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied as a land of vast wealth — "much more red gold from Nibelungenland" (003-195). It is the realm [[siegfried|Siegfried]] conquers from the Nibelung kings Schilbung and Nibelung, gaining not only the immense treasure hoard but also the loyalty of a thousand Nibelung warriors who follow him to Burgundy.

## Definition

Nibelungenland is the homeland of the [[nibelungen|Nibelung people]], ruled originally by the kings Schilbung and Nibelung before [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s conquest. The kingdom is characterized by its extraordinary mineral wealth — particularly red gold — and is associated with a mountain stronghold where the treasure was guarded by the dwarf [[alberich|Alberich]] (003-204, 003-208). After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s victory, Nibelungenland becomes part of his domain, and its warriors serve as his personal retinue.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Legendary kingdoms of immense wealth are a recurring motif in medieval Germanic epic, often located in distant or subterranean realms. Nibelungenland embodies the concept of the "other kingdom" — a place beyond the familiar feudal geography of Burgundy and the [[rhine|Rhine]], accessible only through heroic adventure. Its subterranean associations connect it to the dwarfish realms of Germanic mythology.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Nibelungenland functions as the source of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s exceptional status. Unlike [[gunther|Gunther]], whose power derives from inherited feudal position in Burgundy, [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s authority rests on conquest — he has personally won a kingdom and its wealth. This distinction between inherited and achieved kingship underlies much of the tension between [[siegfried|Siegfried]] and the Burgundian court. After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, the kingdom of Nibelungenland effectively disappears from the narrative, its treasures dispersed and its warriors leaderless — a symbolic dissolution paralleling the collapse of the Burgundian world.

## Comparative Notes

- In Old Norse tradition (Völsunga saga, Prose Edda), the realm is called Niflheim or is associated with the misty underworld — "Nibel-" may derive from "Nifl-" (mist/darkness), suggesting a chthonic origin for the kingdom.
- Wagner's Ring cycle makes the Nibelung realm explicitly subterranean (Nibelheim), ruled by [[alberich|Alberich]], connecting it to dwarfish mining and metallurgy.
- The association of the Nibelungs with extraordinary gold wealth may reflect historical memories of the Migration Period's disruption of Roman gold supplies and the legendary wealth of Germanic warlords.
