# Greeting and Salutation in the Nibelungenlied

The _Gruß_ (greeting, salutation) in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is never merely polite.
It is a public act of social recognition, a ceremonial acknowledgment that
establishes — or refuses — the standing of one person before another. The poem
is punctuated by greetings that carry enormous narrative weight: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
first greeting at the Burgundian court, which establishes his claim to equal
status (003-250); the greeting [[brunhild|Brunhild]] receives that elevates her spirits
(005-116); the hostile greeting that greets the Burgundians at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court
(028-178); and the greeting-as-mockery with which [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] receives [[hagen|Hagen]]
in the poem's final moments (039-158, 039-160). The poem's investment in the
ceremonial _Gruß_ reflects the centrality of public ritual to the courtly
society it depicts — and the fragility of that society when ritual is violated.

## Definition

The _Gruß_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a formal, public act of salutation that
encompasses verbal greeting, physical gesture (bowing, rising), and the social
recognition it confers. To give a proper _Gruß_ is to acknowledge the
recipient's status and dignity; to withhold it is to deny that status; to give
an improper or mocking _Gruß_ is to wound. The poem's language distinguishes
between the _dienstlichen Gruß_ (service-greeting, 037-164), the _feindlichen
Gruß_ (hostile greeting, 031-038), and the _Gruß im [[laughter-and-mockery|Spott]]e_ (mocking greeting,
039-160) — a taxonomy that reveals how much social information a greeting could
encode in the poem's world.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Formal greeting (_gruoz_, _gruozen_) in medieval Germanic courtly culture was
a highly elaborated ritual with precise rules about who greeted whom, in what
order, with what words, and with what physical gestures. The greeting was a
public performance of the social hierarchy — to greet first was to acknowledge
the other's status, and to fail to greet was an insult that could justify
violence. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's extensive attention to the details of greeting
reflects the poem's deep investment in the courtly social order as both
structure and subject. The poem is, among other things, a study in what happens
when the rituals that sustain that order are violated.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's greeting-economy traces the arc of its social world from order to
collapse. In the first half, greetings are performed correctly and produce
the expected effects — spirits are elevated, status is confirmed, strangers
become guests. In the second half, greetings become weapons: they are withheld,
performed with hostility, transformed into mockery. The poem's final _Gruß_
is [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s _Gruß im [[laughter-and-mockery|Spott]]e_ to [[hagen|Hagen]], a greeting that is not a greeting
but an act of terminal contempt. The trajectory from the perfect greeting
[[siegfried|Siegfried]] receives at [[worms|Worms]] to the mocking greeting [[hagen|Hagen]] receives from
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] is the trajectory of the poem's entire social world — from
ceremonial order to ritual annihilation.

## Notable Passages

> Daß wenig an dem Gruße gebrach, den er gewann — nothing was lacking in the
> greeting he received. Siegfried's first greeting at the Burgundian court is
> a perfect ritual performance, and the poem registers its perfection as a
> measure of his status. (003-250)

> kein Gruß euch hier geschieht — no greeting will be offered to you here.
> Dietrich's warning is devastating in its simplicity: the absence of the
> greeting declares that the Burgundians have passed beyond the boundary of
> the social world, into a space where ritual recognition is refused. (028-178)

> Daß ihr mir und Hagen solchen Gruß im Spotte thut — that you offer me and
> Hagen such a greeting in mockery. The poem's final _Gruß_ is not a greeting
> at all but its antithesis — a ritual of contempt where a ritual of honor
> should be. The line marks the terminal collapse of the poem's social
> vocabulary. (039-160)

## Comparative Notes

- The formal greeting as a narrative device is prominent in Middle High German
  courtly romance — Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_ uses the hero's failure
  to offer proper greeting as a sign of his incomplete socialization
- In Old Norse literature, the greeting (_kveðja_) carries similar weight —
  the _Hávamál_ includes extensive advice on proper greeting etiquette, and
  the sagas treat the withheld greeting as a declaration of feud
- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's attention to greeting ritual anticipates the elaborate
  ceremonialism of later medieval courtesy books — the poem is both a record
  of courtly practice and a critique of its fragility
