# Peace and Reconciliation in the Nibelungenlied

_Friede_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is the condition that everyone claims to want and
no one can sustain. From [[gernot|Gernot]]'s first appeal for peace in Adventure 3 to the
final impossibility of reconciliation in Adventure 36, the poem traces the
progressive failure of peace-making in a world governed by vengeance. The word
appears at every crisis point — as an oath, a plea, a diplomatic formula, and
finally as an ironic impossibility — marking the distance between the courtly
ideal of measured conflict resolution and the heroic code's demand for blood.

## Definition

MHG _vride_ (from OHG _fridu_, "peace, protection, security") denotes both the
absence of open conflict and the legal condition of being under protection
(_Friede und [[safe-conduct-and-protection|Geleit]]_). In the feudal-legal vocabulary of the poem, _Frieden
geben_ (to grant peace) and _Frieden gewähren_ (to guarantee peace) are acts
of lordship: the ruler extends his protection over persons and territory. The
paired concept _Frieden und Sühne_ (peace and reconciliation, 033-190) makes
explicit that true peace requires not merely the cessation of violence but the
ritual restoration of a ruptured social bond. The poem's tragedy is that
_Sühne_ is never achieved — peace is offered, sworn, and demanded, but the
underlying injuries ([[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder, the Nibelung treasure's theft, the
killing of the Burgundian squires) are never addressed through reconciliation,
only through further violence.

### Associated Characters

| Character | Relationship to this concept |
|-----------|----------------------------|

| [[kriemhild]] | The figure whose relationship to _Friede_ is most radically transformed — from the bride who seeks a peaceful union to the widow whose promises of peace are instruments of destruction |
| [[hagen]] | The character most explicitly honest about the impossibility of peace — his acknowledgment "Niemand Frieden schaffen kann" (033-140) is the poem's clearest statement of its own tragic logic |
| [[etzel]] | The ruler whose _Friede_ is effective (his roads are safe, 024-310) but irrelevant — [[etzel|Etzel]]'s peace cannot prevent the catastrophe in his own hall |
| [[rudiger]] | The mediator who embodies the possibility of peace through diplomacy — his death in the conflict between loyalty and friendship is the poem's demonstration that even the best mediator cannot reconcile irreconcilable obligations |

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the Staufer period's legal and political vocabulary, _Friede_ was not merely
the absence of war but an active condition maintained by royal authority. The
_Landfrieden_ (territorial peace ordinances) of the twelfth and thirteenth
centuries attempted to limit feuding by channeling disputes into judicial
processes. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's relentless depiction of peace's failure can be
read as a commentary on the limits of this project: the heroic code's demand
for _triuwe_ (loyalty) to the dead — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s obligation to avenge [[siegfried|Siegfried]],
[[hagen|Hagen]]'s obligation to his king — overrides every mechanism for reconciliation
that the courtly world offers.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem structures its treatment of _Friede_ as a series of diminishing
possibilities. In the first half (Adventures 1–19), peace is a real and
achievable condition: [[gernot|Gernot]] negotiates, [[siegfried|Siegfried]] demands guarantees, and
the court of [[worms|Worms]] functions as a space of ordered social life. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
murder at the hunt (Adventure 16) is the rupture that makes all subsequent
peace impossible — not because the act itself cannot be atoned for, but because
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] will never accept atonement. The second half of the poem is an
extended demonstration that peace offered without reconciliation is
meaningless. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s promise "mit Frieden laßen ziehn" (031-197) is
technically true — she lets them go — but only into the burning hall where
they will die. The poem's final adventures (33–39) are set in a space where
peace is repeatedly wished for (036-032) but acknowledged as impossible
(036-220). The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied ends not with peace restored but with the
destruction of everyone who might have made it.

## Notable Passages

> Da sprach zum Frieden Gernot, ein Ritter kühn und wohlgemut.
> — Gernot's first peace-making intervention — the poem's model of measured, diplomatic kingship (003-308)

> Gewährt uns stäten Frieden: das ist uns Recken noth.
> — Siegfried's demand — peace as the currency in which heroic service is rewarded, not a given but something to be negotiated and guaranteed (005-191)

> Gab ihnen Etzels Herschaft Frieden auf den Wegen.
> — Etzel's peace as effective governance — the safety of the roads is a measure of royal authority, in pointed contrast to what awaits at journey's end (024-310)

> "Ihr sollt meine Gäste mit Frieden laßen ziehn."
> — Kriemhild's false peace — the word _Friede_ weaponized as a promise that masks the intention to destroy (031-197)

> "ich zu dieser Stunde Niemand Frieden schaffen kann."
> — Hagen's admission — the moment when even the poem's most ruthless character acknowledges the loss of any possibility of peace (033-140)

> So walte stäter Friede in getreuer Freunde Bund.
> — The ideal invoked at the moment of its impossibility — lasting peace among faithful friends, spoken as the hall burns (033-188)

> Da wünschten einen Frieden die großen Ritter allbereit. / Kein Friede war zu hoffen.
> — The poem's final word on peace — universally desired, universally impossible (036-032, 036-220)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of peace as systematically impossible
  contrasts with the Arthurian romance tradition, where peace is the
  achievable endpoint of chivalric adventure — in Hartmann von Aue's _Erec_
  and _Iwein_, the hero's journey ends in a restored social order; the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied ends in a burned hall
- In the Old Norse _Völsunga saga_, the equivalent theme is _sætt_
  (settlement, reconciliation), which is attempted through marriage alliances
  and weregild but ultimately fails for the same reason: the heroic code
  demands vengeance, not compensation
- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's depiction of _Friede_ as a word used most frequently
  at moments of its violation parallels techniques found in the _Chanson de
  Roland_, where "pais" (peace) is invoked most insistently in the scenes
  immediately preceding betrayal
- The cantus firmus of peace-desire running through the final adventures
  (33–39) — every major character wishes for peace but none can create it —
  is a structural feature unique to the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied among medieval heroic
  epics; it anticipates the tragic irony of Shakespeare's histories more
  than it resembles its Germanic contemporaries
