# Blood and Wounds in the Nibelungenlied

Blood in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is never merely gore. It is the poem's most
consistent physical index of truth: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s dragon-blood bath renders him
invulnerable except for the single spot where a linden leaf fell (003-231);
his wounds flow again when [[hagen|Hagen]] approaches his corpse, identifying the
murderer beyond any possibility of denial (017-171, 017-173, 028-125). From
the blood-reddened saddles of the Saxon battlefield (004-458) to the "fließendes
Blut" that streams from wounds throughout the poem, blood marks the body as
a site where violence becomes visible, legible, and ultimately inescapable.

## Definition

_Blut_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied carries both literal and symbolic weight. Literally,
it is the substance of injury: blood flows from wounds (_Wunden_), reddens
saddles and armor, and signals the mortality of even the greatest heroes.
Symbolically, blood is a truth-medium — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s blood reveals [[hagen|Hagen]]'s guilt
(017-171), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s bloody vengeance in the hall-battle answers the blood
debt of her husband's murder, and the poem's final act is drenched in blood as
the medium through which all debts are settled. _Wunden_ (wounds) are the
physical traces of violence that persist after the blow — they can be tended
(004-439) or left to fester, and [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s wounds have an almost supernatural
persistence that transcends his death.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In medieval heroic literature, blood functions as both a physical and a
semiotic substance. The bleeding wound is visible proof of injury, but it is
also a sign that demands interpretation — whose blood, shed by whom, for what
cause? The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied inherits from Germanic tradition the motif of the
bleeding corpse that identifies its murderer (_Bahrprobe_, bier-test), a
folkloric element that the poem treats with remarkable narrative seriousness.
Blood in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is also class-inflected: noble blood (_edles Blut_)
carries different weight than common blood, and the shedding of noble blood
demands proportionate vengeance.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem constructs a blood-economy in which every shedding of blood creates
a debt that must be repaid in kind. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s dragon-blood bath (003-231)
gives him an almost supernatural body, but the single vulnerable spot marks
the limit of invulnerability — the body cannot be entirely sealed against
violence. When [[hagen|Hagen]] exploits this vulnerability, [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s blood creates
a double mark: the physical stain on [[hagen|Hagen]]'s clothing (016-267) and the
supernatural testimony of the bleeding wounds (017-171). [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s subsequent
career is an attempt to balance the blood-debt through the only currency the
poem recognizes — more blood. The hall-battle represents the blood-economy
spinning out of control, bloodshed generating bloodshed without limit until
the poem's entire cast is destroyed.

## Notable Passages

> Als er im Blut sich badete, ward hörnern seine Haut.
> — Siegfried bathes in the dragon's blood and his skin becomes horn-like — the origin of invulnerability (003-231)

> Mit fließendem Blute, die Kühnen in der Schlacht.
> — The bold ones in battle with flowing blood — the Saxon war's visual economy of blood (004-251)

> Das Blut von seinem Herzen an Hagens Gewand.
> — The blood from Siegfried's heart onto Hagen's clothing — the murderer physically marked (016-267)

> So bluten ihm die Wunden, wie es auch hier geschah; / Die Wunden floßen wieder so stark als je zuvor.
> — Siegfried's wounds bleed again as Hagen approaches — the bier-test identifies the murderer (017-171, 017-173)

> Siegfriedens Wunden die schmerzten Kriemhilden sehr.
> — Siegfried's wounds pained Kriemhild greatly — blood-memory as the engine of revenge (025-084)

## Comparative Notes

- The bier-test motif (_Bahrprobe_) appears across medieval Germanic literature
  — the belief that a corpse's wounds will bleed afresh in the presence of the
  murderer — and the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's deployment of it is among the most
  dramatically effective in the tradition
- The dragon's blood bath parallels Sigurd's acquisition of invulnerability
  in the Old Norse _Fáfnismál_ and _Völsunga saga_, though the Norse tradition
  emphasizes the tasting of the dragon's heart-blood for wisdom
- Compared to Chrétien de Troyes's romances, where blood on armor or linen is
  often associated with love-melancholy, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's blood is
  relentlessly martial — it flows from combat wounds, not from lovers'
  sleepless nights
