# Rhine

The Rhine River (Middle High German: Rîn) is the geographical and symbolic axis of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. [[worms|Worms]], the seat of the Burgundian kings, lies "at [[worms|Worms on the Rhine]]" (001-021), and [[xanten|Xanten]], [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s home, is "down by the Rhine" (002-004).

The river is the highway of heroes — "the [[heroic-code|Recke]]n should follow to the Rhine" (003-058) — and the final resting place of the [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelung treasure]], sunk beneath its waters by [[hagen|Hagen]] at [[lochheim|Lochheim]].

## Definition

The Rhine functions as more than a geographical feature. It defines the [[burgundian-march|Burgundian realm]] — "after the Burgundians was their land named" along the Rhine (001-019).

Characters travel "to the Rhine" as a metonym for journeying to the Burgundian court (003-058). The river's dual nature — life-giving artery and concealing depth — mirrors the epic's oscillation between courtly splendor and hidden darkness.

### River as Symbol

The Rhine's most significant role is as the repository of the [[nibelung-treasure|Nibelung treasure]]. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s sinking of the hoard at [[lochheim|Lochheim]] transforms the river from geography into symbol.

The treasure that drove so much of the epic's action disappears into the depths, becoming inaccessible — a material object transformed into legend. From this moment, the Rhine holds what no one can retrieve.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Rivers in medieval epic often serve as boundaries between worlds. The Rhine, however, is unusual in being the center rather than the boundary of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's world. It is the heart of Burgundian civilization, not its edge.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The Rhine's transformation from center of civilization to grave of treasure mirrors the epic's arc from order to dissolution. In Part I, it is the setting of courtly splendor. In Part II, it is where wealth is hidden from those who would use it.

By the epic's end, the river holds the hoard in its depths — a permanent reminder of what was lost, visible to none. This connects to the broader theme of [[ere|Êre]]: what was once public and displayed becomes hidden and irretrievable.

## Comparative Notes

- Wagner's Ring cycle opens with the Rhine as home of the Rhinegold and Rhine Maidens — a mythological treatment absent from the medieval epic.
- The sinking of treasure in rivers reflects widespread folklore motifs, possibly actual Migration Period votive deposits.
