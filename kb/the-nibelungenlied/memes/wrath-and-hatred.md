# Wrath and Hatred in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a poem driven by wrath. Its narrative arc moves from the
courtly world of Adventure 1 — "Von Freud und Festlichkeiten" (001-003) — to
the apocalyptic hatred of the final books, where every character burns with
_Zorn_ and _Haß_. [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s fury at the discovery of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s deception
(010-393, 010-434) catalyzes the murder plot; [[hagen|Hagen]]'s hatred of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
and later of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] drives the poem's central acts of violence; [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s
consuming _Zorn_ against [[hagen|Hagen]] — "Ihren Zorn habt ihr verdienet" (004-021)
— transforms her from grieving widow to the architect of genocide. Wrath in the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is not a temporary passion but a permanent state, a fire that,
once lit, burns until there is nothing left to consume.

## Definition

_Zorn_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied denotes a powerful, often violent anger that
drives its bearer to action. Unlike the modern conception of anger as a loss
of control, _Zorn_ in the heroic world is intimately connected to honor
(_êre_) — to be without _Zorn_ in the face of insult is to be without honor.
_Haß_ (hatred) is _Zorn_ stabilized into a permanent disposition: [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s
fury at the bridal-night deception hardens into enduring hatred of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
(010-228, 010-232); [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief at [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder calcifies into
implacable hatred of [[hagen|Hagen]] (014-240). The poem tracks the transformation of
_Zorn_ (the hot passion of the moment) into _Haß_ (the cold passion that
structures an entire life).

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the heroic ethics of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, _Zorn_ is not a vice but a
constituent element of _êre_ (honor). The hero who does not feel wrath when
insulted is not virtuous but deficient — he lacks the _muot_ (spirit, courage)
that defines heroic identity. At the same time, the poem traces the catastrophic
consequences of _Zorn_ unchecked: what begins as legitimate anger at a real
injury escalates into hatred that consumes not only its object but the entire
social world. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied thus occupies an ambiguous position between
affirming the heroic value of wrath and exposing its destructive logic.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem maps the transformation of _Zorn_ into _Haß_ across its narrative
arc. In the first half, _Zorn_ is the passion of combat and honor — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
battle-rage, [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s bridal fury, even [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murderous anger are
responses to specific provocations. In the second half, _Zorn_ hardens into
_Haß_ — a permanent, structural hatred that no longer requires provocation to
sustain itself. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s hatred of [[hagen|Hagen]] is the poem's most complete
expression of this transformation: it begins as grief and anger at a specific
murder (025-084) and ends as a hatred so absolute that she is willing to
destroy her own brothers and her own son to satisfy it. The poem's final
image — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] holding [[hagen|Hagen]]'s severed head — is the icon of _Haß_
consummated and, in the same moment, punished.

## Notable Passages

> Die erschlug im Zorne Siegfriedens Hand.
> — Siegfried's hand slew them in wrath — the poem's first act of heroic fury (003-207)

> Ihren Zorn habt ihr verdienet: wir vernahmen das.
> — You have deserved her wrath: we have heard this — the warning delivered to the Burgundians about their enemies (004-021)

> "Vor ihrem grimmen Zorne: drum laßt den Willen sein."
> — "Because of her grim wrath: therefore abandon this intention" — Brunhild's anger precedes her appearance in the poem (006-034)

> Brunhild die schöne ward vor Zorne roth.
> — Brunhild the beautiful turned red with wrath — the bridal-night fury that will destroy Siegfried (007-315)

> Unter den Frauen hob sich der Haß da grimm genug.
> — Among the women hatred arose, grim enough — the queens' quarrel transforms the court (014-064)

> Immer sei ihm Hagen zu Haß und Schaden bereit.
> — May Hagen always be ready to hate and harm him — the oath of eternal enmity (014-240)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of wrath parallels the Homeric _mênis_
  (wrath) that opens the _Iliad_ — in both poems, a hero's anger is the
  narrative's driving force, and in both poems, the consequences of that
  anger extend far beyond the hero's intentions
- The Old Norse tradition represents the same emotions differently: Guðrún's
  revenge in _Atlakviða_ is colder, more deliberate, and less
  psychologically elaborated than [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s consuming _Zorn_ in the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
- Compared to the courtly romance's treatment of anger (as a temporary loss
  of _mâze_ or measure to be overcome), the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied treats _Zorn_ and
  _Haß_ as permanent, structuring passions — they are not overcome but
  exhausted only through the destruction of their objects
