# Tarnkappe

The Tarnkappe (literally "cloak of concealment") is a magical cloak of invisibility in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, one of the treasures [[siegfried|Siegfried]] won from the dwarf [[alberich|Alberich]] after defeating the Nibelung kings. Described as "of wondrous kind" (006-063), the cloak renders its wearer invisible and imparts the strength of twelve additional men, making it one of the most powerful magical objects in the epic.

## Definition

The Tarnkappe is a magical garment of Nibelung origin, originally belonging to the dwarf [[alberich|Alberich]] who guarded the Nibelung treasure (003-219). After [[siegfried|Siegfried]] defeats [[alberich|Alberich]], he takes possession of the cloak along with the hoard. The Tarnkappe's powers are twofold: it makes the wearer invisible to all observers, and it grants superhuman strength — "the strength of twelve men" beyond the wearer's own (006-073). [[siegfried|Siegfried]] keeps the cloak throughout his life, using it strategically in critical moments.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Magical objects in medieval Germanic epic often serve as externalizations of heroic qualities. The Tarnkappe — granting invisibility and strength — represents the supernatural enhancement of the hero beyond normal human limits. Invisibility magic appears across Germanic and Celtic folklore, but the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's Tarnkappe is distinctive in combining concealment with physical empowerment.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The Tarnkappe is the instrument of the epic's foundational deception. By using it to help [[gunther|Gunther]] defeat [[brunhild|Brunhild]] in the contests at [[isenstein|Isenstein]] (007-171, 007-284) and again to subdue her in the bridal chamber (010-306), [[siegfried|Siegfried]] sets in motion the chain of events that leads to his own murder. The cloak thus embodies the tension between heroic assistance and fraudulent manipulation — [[siegfried|Siegfried]] acts loyally toward [[gunther|Gunther]], but the deception of [[brunhild|Brunhild]] plants the seeds of the eventual catastrophe. Without the Tarnkappe, [[gunther|Gunther]] could never have won [[brunhild|Brunhild]], and the conflict between the two queens would never have erupted.

## Comparative Notes

- The Old Norse Völsunga saga features a "helmet of terror" (Ægishjálmr) rather than an invisibility cloak, though the motif of magical concealment is similar.
- Wagner's Ring cycle transforms the Tarnkappe into the Tarnhelm, a magic helmet that not only grants invisibility but also shape-shifting — an expansion of the original concept.
- The Middle High German word "tarnen" (to conceal) survives in modern German "tarnen" (to camouflage), making the Tarnkappe a linguistic ancestor of modern concealment terminology.
