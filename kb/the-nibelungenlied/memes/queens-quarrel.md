# Quarrel of the Queens

The quarrel of the queens (Adventure 14) is the catalytic event of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. When [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and [[brunhild|Brunhild]] dispute who should enter the cathedral first — each claiming precedence as the wife of the greater king — the argument escalates from courtly protocol to devastating revelation.

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], enraged by [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s claim that [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is [[gunther|Gunther]]'s vassal, reveals that [[siegfried|Siegfried]], not [[gunther|Gunther]], took [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s virginity. She produces [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s ring and girdle as proof. [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s public humiliation is absolute, and [[hagen|Hagen]] vows to avenge her [[ere|honor]] by killing [[siegfried|Siegfried]] — setting the entire tragedy in motion.

## Overview

- **Adventure(s)**: 14
- **Location**: The cathedral door at [[worms|Worms]]
- **Key Participants**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (revealer), [[brunhild|Brunhild]] (humiliated), [[siegfried|Siegfried]] (subject of revelation), [[gunther|Gunther]] (absent but implicated)
- **Outcome**: [[brunhild|Brunhild]] publicly humiliated; [[hagen|Hagen]] resolves to murder [[siegfried|Siegfried]]

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

For years after the double wedding, the secret of [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s deception has been kept. [[brunhild|Brunhild]] believes [[gunther|Gunther]] defeated her himself at [[isenstein|Isenstein]]; she does not know [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s role with the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]].

However, she senses something amiss — [[siegfried|Siegfried]] and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] live as equals, not as vassals. This unease crystallizes into suspicion, culminating in [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s invitation for the couple to visit [[worms|Worms]].

### The Event

As the queens approach the cathedral, each claims the right to enter first. [[brunhild|Brunhild]] asserts that [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is [[gunther|Gunther]]'s vassal, making [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] subordinate.

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] retorts with devastating precision: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is the greater king, and moreover — "You gave yourself to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] first." She produces [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s ring and girdle as proof. [[brunhild|Brunhild]] collapses in tears before the entire court.

### Aftermath

[[brunhild|Brunhild]] demands satisfaction. [[hagen|Hagen]], seeing his queen humiliated, swears to kill [[siegfried|Siegfried]]. [[gunther|Gunther]] hesitates but does not forbid it.

From this single confrontation at the church door, everything that follows — the hunting expedition, the murder at the spring, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief, her marriage to [[etzel|Etzel]], the [[fall-of-the-burgundians|destruction at Etzel's court]] — unfolds with terrible inevitability.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

The queens' quarrel is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's structural pivot — the moment when the concealed past breaks into the present with catastrophic force.

It transforms the epic from a story of heroic achievement into a story of [[revenge]], and demonstrates that secrets, however carefully guarded, will eventually destroy what they were meant to protect.

### Medieval Themes

The quarrel crystallizes the epic's treatment of [[ere|Êre]] as both the supreme social value and the most dangerous. Both queens defend their honor; both are in some sense right; yet the defense of honor produces only destruction.

The public nature of the confrontation — at the church door, before the entire court — is crucial: in a shame-culture, public humiliation demands public redress, and redress means blood.
