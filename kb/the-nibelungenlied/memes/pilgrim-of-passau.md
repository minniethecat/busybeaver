# Pilgrim of Passau

Pilgrim of Passau (Pilgerin von Passau) was the Bishop of Passau and the uncle of the Burgundian royal siblings — [[gunther|Gunther]], [[gernot|Gernot]], [[geiselher|Geiselher]], and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]. He appears in the later adventures of the *Nibelungenlied* as a figure of ecclesiastical authority, familial concern, and narrative framing.

## Role in the Narrative

Bishop Pilgrim is first mentioned when Kriemhild passes through Passau on her journey to marry King [[etzel|Etzel]]:

> Where Bishop Pilgerin found the beautiful Kriemhild. (021-028)

As the Burgundians prepare to travel to the Hun court, Pilgrim expresses his desire to see his niece again:

> "If I could see her with me," spoke Bishop Pilgerin. (024-026)

During the Burgundians' fateful journey eastward, Pilgrim is noted among the company as the princes' uncle:

> The noble princes' uncle, Bishop Pilgerin. (026-169)

His presence in the narrative serves to anchor the fictional action within the real ecclesiastical geography of medieval Bavaria — Passau was an important bishopric and a known center of literary patronage.

## Historical Basis

The character is generally identified with the historical Bishop Pilgrim of Passau (r. 971–991 CE), a significant figure in the Christianization of Hungary and a noted patron of learning. Pilgrim's inclusion in the epic connects the fictional narrative to authentic ecclesiastical authority and to the city of Passau, which scholars believe was an important center for the poem's composition and textual transmission.

The historical Pilgrim was also involved in the production of forged documents (the "Lorch forgeries") aimed at elevating Passau's ecclesiastical status — a detail that resonates with the poem's themes of legitimacy, authority, and the manipulation of narrative.
