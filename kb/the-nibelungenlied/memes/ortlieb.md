# Ortlieb

Ortlieb is the infant son of King [[Etzel]] and Queen [[Kriemhild]], born during the years between the two parts of the epic. Baptised into the Christian faith at his mother's insistence (023-007), Ortlieb represents the dynastic union of the Hunnish and Burgundian royal houses — and his brutal murder by [[Hagen]] in the great hall at [[Etzel]]'s court becomes the flashpoint that transforms simmering hostility into open, catastrophic warfare.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Prince (Königskind) of the Huns
- **Affiliation**: [[Etzel|Hun Court]]
- **Family**: Son of [[Etzel]] (King of the Huns) and [[Kriemhild]] (Queen, formerly Princess of Burgundy)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 23 — Named at his baptism (023-007)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: Pre-Arrival (Adventures 1–19)

Ortlieb is not yet born in the first part of the epic. His birth occurs after [[Kriemhild]]'s marriage to [[Etzel]] and the passage of many years, bridging the two halves of the poem.

### Part II: The Fall of the Burgundians (Adventures 20–39)

Ortlieb's presence in the narrative is brief but pivotal. He is first mentioned by name in Adventure 23, where the poet notes he was baptised according to Christian custom (023-007). In Adventure 31, he is brought before the assembled warriors as the "young king's child," and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] makes a pointed appeal — that the Burgundians should avenge the wrongs done to her for the sake of her young son (031-290)(031-300). Her words frame Ortlieb as both a living symbol of her new life and a political instrument in her campaign for vengeance.

In Adventure 33, the tension at [[Etzel]]'s court reaches its breaking point. As the feast continues, Ortlieb is carried through the hall (033-006). It is then that [[Hagen]], having learned of [[Kriemhild]]'s plot from [[Bloedel]]'s attack on the Burgundian squires, commits the act that makes reconciliation impossible: he seizes the child and kills him before the assembled court (033-045). The murder of an innocent infant — the son of his own former queen — marks [[hagen|Hagen]]'s definitive rejection of any possibility of peace and condemns him beyond any claim to honourable conduct.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

As an infant, Ortlieb has no agency or motivations of his own. His narrative function is purely symbolic: he is the living bond between the two royal houses, the tangible proof that [[Kriemhild]] has built a new life after [[Siegfried]]'s death. His murder by [[Hagen]] is therefore not merely a personal atrocity but a calculated act of dynastic annihilation — [[hagen|Hagen]] destroys not only the child but the entire political settlement that the marriage represents.

### The Innocent Victim

Ortlieb is unique among the named characters of the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* in being wholly innocent. Unlike every other figure who dies in the epic — warriors who have chosen battle, queens who have plotted revenge — Ortlieb is a child too young to have committed any act or made any choice. His death strips away any remaining claim the Burgundians might have to the moral high ground and recasts the final battle as a slaughter in which all parties stand condemned.

## Death

Ortlieb is killed by [[Hagen]] in Adventure 33, during the feast at [[Etzel]]'s court. The murder is summarised in a single, stark line (033-045) — the brevity of the account amplifying its brutality. The killing of the child serves as the point of no return: after this act, no negotiation, truce, or reconciliation with the Burgundians is conceivable, and the path to the total destruction of Adventure 38 is irrevocably set.

## Scholarly Notes

- Ortlieb's murder by [[hagen|Hagen]] is one of the most debated episodes in *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied* scholarship. Some critics read it as the poet's condemnation of [[hagen|Hagen]]'s inhumanity; others see it as a narrative necessity — the ultimate provocation that must occur for the final tragedy to unfold.
- The child's Christian baptism (023-007) is a significant detail: it marks the uneasy coexistence of Christian and pre-Christian values in the epic world. [[hagen|Hagen]], who shows little regard for Christian morality elsewhere, compounds his crime by killing a baptised child.
- In the Norse *Thidrekssaga* and *Atlakviða*, the equivalent child figures are killed by their own mother ([[kriemhild|Gudrun]]/[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]) as part of her revenge against Atli — the *[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied*'s displacement of the infanticide from mother to enemy is one of the poet's most significant revisions of the inherited legend.
