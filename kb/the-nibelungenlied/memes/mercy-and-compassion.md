# Mercy and Compassion

Mercy (_erbarmen_, _Gnade_) occupies an uneasy position in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's
ethical landscape. The poem's characters invoke divine mercy in moments of
desperation, appeal to human mercy in scenes of supplication, and occasionally
grant it — but the poem's overriding narrative logic is one of mercy denied. The
verb _erbarmen_ (to have pity, to show compassion) and the noun _Gnade_ (grace,
mercy, favor) form a vocabulary of compassion that the poem systematically
tests, strains, and ultimately refuses to honor.

## Definition

In MHG, _erbarmen_ (verb) and _erbarmunge_ (noun) derive from the OHG
_irbarmēn_, meaning to feel compassion or pity. The term carries a strong
Christian valence — medieval German religious literature uses _erbarmen_
extensively of divine mercy — but in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied it appears primarily in
human contexts. _Gnade_ is broader in semantic range, encompassing "grace,"
"favor," "mercy," and "pardon." The two terms overlap in supplication scenes,
where characters _auf Gnade_ (on mercy/grace) place themselves at another's
disposal, and in exclamations of despair, where _[[church-and-religion|Gott]] erbarmen_ (God have mercy)
is the poem's closest approach to religious language.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In 12th–13th century German literature, _erbarmen_ and _Gnade_ are central to
both courtly and religious ethics. The courtly value of _erbarmen_ is gendered:
female figures are expected to show compassion, and the _[[minne|Minne]]sang_ tradition
makes the lady's mercy (_gnâde_) the object of the lover's petition. Religious
discourse makes divine mercy the foundation of salvation. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
draws on both traditions but subverts them: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s _erbarmen_ turns to
vengeance, divine mercy is invoked but never manifestly granted, and the one
scene of genuine human mercy ([[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s gift of his shield to [[hagen|Hagen]], 037-218)
leads directly to [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s death.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's treatment of mercy follows a chiastic structure. In the first half
(Adventures 1–19), mercy is a possibility — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] is pitied, [[hagen|Hagen]]'s capture
produces an appeal for _Gnade_, and God is invoked in moments of distress. In
the second half (Adventures 20–39), mercy becomes progressively impossible.
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s transformation from object of compassion to agent of merciless
vengeance is the poem's central ethical arc. The final supplication — [[hagen|Hagen]]'s
plea in Adventure 37 — is answered not with mercy but with beheading. The poem
closes with a narrator's reflection on what mercy (_in Gnaden_) might have
accomplished, framing the entire narrative as a study in compassion withheld.

## Notable Passages

> Daß es erbarmen muste Die in Gunthers Lehn.
> — Even Gunther's vassals are moved to pity by Kriemhild's grief (014-199)

> "Nun mög es Gott erbarmen, daß ich gewann den Sohn."
> — Kriemhild invokes divine mercy for her child after Siegfried's death (016-321)

> "Erweist mir eine Gnade aus erbarmendem Sinn."
> — Hagen appeals for mercy after his capture (017-264)

> Das möge Gott erbarmen; wie sind wir all verlorn!
> — The Burgundians cry for divine mercy as the hall burns (036-131)

> "Nun laß dich erbarmen unsres Leids Beschwer."
> — Hagen's final plea to Kriemhild, which she refuses (037-114)

> Wenn euer Gruß in Gnaden geschehen könnte sein.
> — The narrator's closing reflection on mercy denied (039-158)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of mercy contrasts with the courtly romance
  tradition, where _erbarmen_ is a reliable moral force — in Hartmann's _Iwein_,
  the hero's capacity for compassion is the measure of his chivalric worth; in
  the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, compassion is powerless against the logic of revenge
- In the Norse _Atlakviða_ and _Atlamál_, [[kriemhild|Gudrun]]'s revenge on Atli includes
  killing her own children but does not feature the same extended vocabulary of
  mercy and its denial; the MHG poem's theological register (divine mercy
  invoked but absent) is a distinctly Christian addition to the legend
- The poem's closing subjunctive (_in Gnaden geschehen könnte_) is without
  parallel in heroic epic: it is a lyric gesture that reframes the entire
  narrative as a tragedy of compassion unrealized
