# Journey and Travel in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a poem of journeys. Its narrative is structured not
around static scenes at court but around arrivals and departures: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
journey from [[xanten|Xanten]] to [[worms|Worms]], the expedition to Iceland, the bridal journey
of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, and the fatal journey of the Burgundians to the
land of the Huns. The MHG terms _Reise_ (journey, expedition) and _Fahrt_
(voyage, journey) appear at every major narrative juncture, and the verb
_reiten_ (to ride) is among the poem's most frequent. Journey is not merely
the connective tissue between events; it is the mechanism through which the
poem generates its plot. Every journey in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied changes
irreversibly the situation it leaves behind.

## Definition

The poem distinguishes between _Reise_ (a planned expedition or campaign,
from OHG _reisa_ — "raising, military expedition") and _Fahrt_ (a journey or
voyage, from _faran_ — "to go, to travel"). _Reise_ carries stronger
connotations of purpose and preparation: "Sie schickten sich zur Reise" (they
prepared themselves for the journey) is a recurring formula (004-133, 006-072,
008-180). _Fahrt_ appears more often in contexts of sea-voyage or the journey
as an undifferentiated movement through space: "Auf ihrer Fahrt ertönte
vielfaches [[joy-and-celebration|Freude]]nspiel" (on their voyage resounded manifold joy-music,
008-189). The compound _Reiseschrein_ (journey-chest, 008-162) and the formula
"[[horses-and-mounts|Ross]] und [[clothing-and-textiles|Gewand]]" (horse and clothing) for journey provisions mark the
material culture of medieval travel.

| 06–07 | The expedition to Iceland: "die Reise muß ergehn" | [[gunther|Gunther]]'s journey to win [[brunhild|Brünhild]] — a journey that, despite warnings, must happen; the phrase "die Reise muß ergehn" (the journey must take place) captures the fatalistic quality of all major journeys in the poem (006-037) |

| 20–22 | [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s bridal journey to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court | The longest journey in the poem — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] travels from [[worms|Worms]] to the land of the Huns via the [[danube|Danube]]; the journey is described with ceremonial splendor, but the narrative knows it is the prelude to annihilation |
| 25–27 | The Burgundians' fatal journey to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court | The poem's final and most elaborately narrated journey — every stage is marked by omens, warnings, and the relentless forward movement toward the hall-battle; the [[danube|Danube]] crossing is the point of no return |

### Associated Characters

| Character | Relationship to this concept |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| [[siegfried]] | The poem's supreme journeyer — his first journey to [[worms|Worms]] initiates the action, his journey to Iceland enables [[gunther|Gunther]]'s marriage, his journey to [[nibelungenland|Nibelungenland]] brings reinforcements; every journey he undertakes succeeds, until the hunting expedition that is his last |
| [[kriemhild]] | Her bridal journey from [[worms|Worms]] to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court (Adventures 20–22) is the poem's most extended travel narrative — the journey eastward literalizes her movement from Burgundian princess to Hunnish queen and from grieving widow to avenger |

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In medieval epic and romance, the journey (_Reise_, _Fahrt_, _chevauchée_) is
the fundamental narrative structure. The hero leaves the familiar court, travels
through alien space where adventures occur, and returns transformed. The
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied participates in this pattern but subverts it: its journeys are
one-way. [[siegfried|Siegfried]] leaves [[xanten|Xanten]] and never returns to rule. The Burgundians
leave [[worms|Worms]] and never return. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] leaves the [[rhine|Rhine]] and never returns. The
poem's geography is a geography of irreversible departures — each journey
consumes the possibility of return.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's journey structure follows a deliberate spatial logic. The early
journeys ([[siegfried|Siegfried]] to [[worms|Worms]], the Iceland expedition) move centrifugally
outward from the Burgundian center and return successfully — the circle closes.
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder breaks this pattern. After his death, journeys become
centripetal — they draw the characters toward a single point ([[etzel|Etzel]]'s court)
where they will converge and be destroyed. The [[danube|Danube]] crossing in Adventure 25
is the structural pivot: once the Burgundians cross the river, the geography of
return is literally destroyed ([[hagen|Hagen]] smashes the boat), and the remaining
journey is a one-way movement toward the hall. The poem's final journey is not
a journey at all but an immobility — the Burgundians trapped in the burning
hall, unable to leave, the poem's kinetic energy extinguished.

## Notable Passages

> "Nun helft mir zu der Reise nach Burgundenland."
> — Siegfried asks his parents' help for the journey to Worms — the poem's first journey-utterance, setting the entire plot in motion (003-073)

> "Die Reise muß ergehn."
> — The journey must take place — Siegfried's fatalistic declaration that Gunther's expedition to Iceland cannot be avoided (006-037)

> Sie schickten sich zur Reise: man sah sie reiten nach dem Rhein.
> — The recurring formula of journey preparation — the Burgundians prepare to depart for Iceland (008-180)

> Ihre Reise bliebe beßer ungethan.
> — Their journey would better have been left undone — the narrator's retrospective judgment on the fatal expedition (004-106)

> Auf ihrer Fahrt ertönte vielfaches Freudenspiel.
> — On their voyage resounded manifold joy-music — the journey as celebration, before it becomes a journey to death (008-189)

> "Zwanzig Reiseschreine füllt ich mir gern."
> — Kriemhild prepares twenty journey-chests for her bridal journey — the materiality of medieval travel, the wealth that will travel east and never return (008-162)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's journey structure parallels the _Chanson de Roland_ (the
  Frankish army's journey into Spain and its catastrophic return through
  Roncevaux), but where Roland's journey is a military campaign with a clear
  objective, the Burgundians' final journey is undertaken in full knowledge of
  its fatal character
- The Old Norse _Atlakviða_ compresses the Burgundians' journey to Atli's court
  into a few stanzas; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied expands it across three full adventures
  with omens, water-sprites, ferrymen, and the destruction of the boat — a
  deliberate narrative deceleration that heightens the sense of approaching doom
- The [[danube|Danube]] crossing and [[hagen|Hagen]]'s destruction of the boat (Adventure 25) has no
  parallel in the Norse tradition and appears to be the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied-poet's
  original contribution — a powerful image of the irreversibility that defines
  the poem's journey-logic
- Compared to Arthurian romance (Chrétien de Troyes, Wolfram von Eschenbach),
  where the hero's journey is a _quête_ that ends in reintegration, the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's journeys end in disintegration — the court is not restored
  but annihilated
