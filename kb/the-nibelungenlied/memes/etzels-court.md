# Etzel's Court

Etzel's Court is the royal residence of King [[etzel|Etzel]] in the land of the Huns, the destination of the Burgundian journey in the epic's second part. It is a cosmopolitan court of unparalleled splendor, hosting warriors from across Europe — Christians and pagans, Greeks and Russians, Poles and Wallachians. Here [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] orchestrates her revenge for [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder, culminating in the bloodiest massacre in medieval epic literature.

## Location & Geography

- **Type**: Royal palace / fortified court capital
- **Ruler**: King [[etzel|Etzel]] (the historical [[etzel|Attila]])
- **Real-world counterpart**: The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's [[etzel|Etzel]] court is legendary, but it is loosely based on [[etzel|Attila]]'s headquarters in Pannonia; medieval tradition associated it with Gran (Esztergom) or Buda in modern Hungary
- **Associated characters**: [[etzel|Etzel]], [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], Helke ([[etzel|Etzel]]'s first wife), [[rudiger|Rüdiger]], [[dietrich|Dietrich of Bern]], [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]

### Strategic Significance

Etzel's Court is the epic's second great setting after [[worms|Worms]], and it operates as [[worms|Worms]]'s narrative antithesis. Where [[worms|Worms]] is orderly, Christian, and governed by feudal law, [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court is multicultural, partly pagan, and ultimately lawless — a place where formal hospitality masks deadly intent. The court's scale reflects [[etzel|Etzel]]'s power as the most formidable ruler in the epic's world, but its diversity also creates the conditions for chaos: warriors of different nations and faiths cannot be bound by the same codes of honor. The massacre at Etzel's Court is the logical outcome of this instability — a feast that becomes a battlefield.

## Historical Notes

- [[etzel|Etzel]] is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's version of [[etzel|Attila]] the Hun (d. 453 CE), whose court served as a center of power for the Hunnic Empire in the Carpathian Basin.
- The historical [[etzel|Attila]] died in 453, but the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied conflates this with the destruction of the Burgundian kingdom by Hunnic foederati in 437 CE — an anachronism that shapes the entire epic.
- The epic's portrayal of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court as a multicultural center — Greeks, Russians, Poles, Wallachians, and Christians and pagans side by side — reflects the multi-ethnic reality of the 13th-century Hungarian kingdom under the Árpád dynasty.
- The name "[[etzel|Etzel]]" derives from the Old High German "Etzilo," itself from the Gothic "[[etzel|Attila]]" ("Little Father").
