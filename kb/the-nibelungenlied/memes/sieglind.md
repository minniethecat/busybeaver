Sieglind is the Queen of the Netherlands, wife of King Siegmund, and mother of the hero Siegfried. Though she appears only briefly in the Nibelungenlied, she is portrayed as a noble and virtuous queen whose son's heroic destiny overshadows her own story.

## Queen of the Netherlands

Sieglind is introduced as "Sieglind the rich" (Sieglind die reiche), a queen renowned for her wealth and courtly virtue (002-090). She rules alongside her husband Siegmund in Xanten on the Rhine. Her court maintains the traditional customs and courtly refinement befitting a royal household.

> "Sieglind die reiche der alten Sitte pflag" (002-090)

## Mother of Siegfried

Sieglind is most often referenced in connection with her son Siegfried. Throughout the epic, Siegfried is repeatedly called "Sieglind's son" (der Sieglindensohn) or "Sieglind's child" (Sieglindenkind), emphasizing his illustrious lineage (003-375, 004-283). The epithet serves to remind the audience of Siegfried's royal heritage and the grief his death will cause his mother.

> "Der's vollbrachte, sei der Sieglindensohn" (004-283)

Siegfried's skill with weapons is also attributed to his lineage — he is described as shooting his bow as "Sieglinde's child" (007-294), connecting his martial prowess to his mother's line.

## Legacy

Sieglind's role is primarily that of a background figure whose maternal connection to the hero gives her symbolic weight. The repeated references to Siegfried as "Sieglind's son" throughout the epic serve as a constant reminder of the royal bloodline that is cut short by his death — and of the grief that a mother endures when her child is taken.
