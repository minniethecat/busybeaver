# Feast and Festival

The courtly feast (_hôchgezît_, also _Hochzeit_ in the poem's orthography)
functions as the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's principal narrative scaffolding. Four major
festivals structure the poem's action — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s arrival at [[worms|Worms]], the double
wedding, [[etzel|Etzel]]'s wedding to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], and the fatal "üble Hochzeit" at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s
court — each marking a narrative threshold where political order is celebrated
and then undermined.

## Definition

In Middle High German courtly culture, _hôchgezît_ denotes a multi-day festival
combining religious observance, feasting, tournaments, gift-giving, and
political assembly. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied uses the term primarily for royal
weddings, which serve as the poem's major set-pieces. A proper _hôchgezît_
involves: mass singing, the seating of guests by rank, ceremonial service at
table, distribution of gifts (_gabe_), tournaments (_buhurt_), and dancing.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The courtly festival in 12th–13th century German epic is the primary site of
_êre_ (honor) display. The scale of a festival — its duration, the value of
gifts distributed, the number of guests — directly indexes the host's political
power. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's festival descriptions draw on the historical
_Mainzer Hoftag_ of 1184, Frederick Barbarossa's massive court festival, as a
model for royal self-representation.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces a festival arc from celebration to catastrophe. The early
festivals ([[worms|Worms]], the double wedding) display Burgundian courtly order at its
height. [[etzel|Etzel]]'s wedding (Adventure 22) marks the poem's midpoint: a festival of
unprecedented scale that transfers the center of gravity to the Hunnish court.
The final festival (Adventures 36–38) systematically inverts every element of
the ideal _hôchgezît_: gifts become demands (for the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]] treasure), music
becomes war-cries, and the feast hall becomes a funeral pyre.

## Notable Passages

> Diese Hochzeit währte siebzehn Tage lang.
> Der solche Hochzeit hielte: es ist uns unbekannt.
> — Etzel's wedding festival (022-117, 022-125, 022-127)

> Eine üble Hochzeit ist es, zu der die Königin uns entboten hat.
> — The fatal festival foreshadowed (036-160)

> Das war ihm bei der Hochzeit die allergröste Noth.
> — Etzel's lament at his ruined festival (038-218)

## Comparative Notes

- The festival-as-trap motif appears in other Germanic heroic poetry (e.g., the
  _Finnsburg Fragment_'s hall-fight), but nowhere is the inversion of festival
  into catastrophe as systematically developed as in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
- The seventeen-day duration of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s wedding echoes the biblical and
  classical tradition of the royal wedding feast (cf. Esther 1, the wedding of
  Peleus and Thetis)
- The _hôchgezît_ structure was adopted as a narrative device across Middle High
  German courtly romance (Hartmann, Wolfram), but the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's tragic
  inversion of the form is unique
