# Dwarf-Folk

The dwarf-folk (_Gezwerge_, singular _Zwerg_) appear in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied as a
subterranean race inhabiting the land of the Nibelungs, possessing enormous
wealth, magical artifacts, and superhuman strength. Distinct from the human
heroes of the Burgundian and Hunnish courts, the dwarves occupy a mythic
register that links the poem's courtly world to older strata of Germanic legend.

## Definition

In Middle High German heroic epic, _Gezwerg_ (cognate with Modern German
_Zwerg_, English "dwarf") denotes a member of a subterranean race of
supernatural beings. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's dwarves combine several traditional
attributes: they are guardians of underground treasure, possessors of magical
objects (the _[[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]]_, cloak of invisibility), and capable warriors. Unlike
the grotesque or comic dwarves of later medieval romance, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's
dwarves are formidable opponents — [[alberich|Alberich]], the dwarf-king, is described as
_kühn_ (bold), _stark_ (strong), and _wild_ (fierce).

The dwarf-folk are politically organized under kings (Schilbung and Nibelung)
and a chamberlain ([[alberich|Alberich]]), and they command armies of warriors. Their
realm, [[nibelungenland|Nibelungenland]], is a distinct geopolitical domain that [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
conquers and later draws military support from.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Dwarves in Germanic mythology are consistently associated with subterranean
spaces, treasure-hoarding, and craftsmanship. The _Prose Edda_ and _Völsunga
saga_ (Norse analogues to the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's source material) present dwarves
as the original creators of magical objects. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied retains the
dwarf-treasure association but subordinates the dwarf-folk to the human heroic
narrative: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s conquest of the dwarves is a pre-history feat, narrated
retrospectively, that establishes his superhuman stature before he enters the
Burgundian court.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The dwarf-folk serve three narrative functions. First, they provide [[siegfried|Siegfried]]
with mythic credentials — his lordship over the "wild Gezwerg" marks him as a
hero whose power extends beyond the courtly world. Second, the dwarf-made
[[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]] enables the pivotal deception of the bridal quest (Adventures 6–7),
where [[siegfried|Siegfried]] uses its invisibility to defeat [[brunhild|Brunhild]] on [[gunther|Gunther]]'s behalf.
Third, [[alberich|Alberich]]'s refusal to hand over the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]] after [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death
(019-083) signals the fragmentation of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s power — the dwarf-folk's
loyalty is personal, not transferable to the Burgundian court.

The poem's dwarf episodes are concentrated in the first half (Adventures 3–8,
19), after which the dwarf-folk vanish from the narrative. This concentration
aligns with the poem's broader movement from the mythic-heroic register of
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s story to the political-tragic register of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revenge.

## Notable Passages

> Von wilden Gezwergen hab ich hören sagen,
> Die heißen Tarnkappen, von wunderbarer Art;
> Wer sie am Leibe trägt, der mag sich wohl wagen
> Unter alle Leute, daß ihn Niemand sieht.
> — Hagen's account of the dwarf-made Tarnkappe (006-061)

> Alberich der kühne, ein wildes Gezwerg,
> Der war der Kammeräre bei dem Schatze Pfleger;
> Er hütete den Hort der Nibelungen.
> — Alberich described as a bold, wild dwarf guarding the treasure (008-046)

> Den zuckt' er ungefüge: der Zwerg schrie auf vor Schmerz.
> — Siegfried's rough handling of the dwarf Alberich (008-063)

> Die gute Tarnkappe mit Siegfried zumal,
> Daß die Tarnkappe der Held uns hat genommen,
> Die soll wiederbringen der beste Degen sein.
> — Alberich refuses to surrender the Tarnkappe after Siegfried's death (019-083, 019-086)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's dwarves share the treasure-guardian motif with the Norse
  _Völsunga saga_ and _Reginsmál_, where the dwarf Andvari guards a cursed
  hoard; however, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied strips out the curse element and treats the
  treasure as politically valuable rather than supernaturally tainted
- [[alberich|Alberich]]'s role as chamberlain parallels the figure of the loyal retainer in
  courtly romance, but his dwarf nature adds a mythic dimension absent from
  purely human vassal relationships
- The [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]] (cloak of invisibility) is a uniquely MHG contribution to the
  Nibelung legend; Norse analogues give Sigurd a helmet of terror
  (_Ægishjálmr_) rather than an invisibility cloak
- In later German tradition (the _Laurin_ legend, the Dietrich epics), dwarves
  become increasingly comic or diminutive; the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's dwarves,
  described as _stark_ and _wild_, belong to an earlier, more formidable
  conception of the dwarf-folk
