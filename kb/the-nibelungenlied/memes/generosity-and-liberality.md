# Generosity and Liberality

_Milte_ — the Middle High German term for generosity, liberality, and
open-handedness — is one of the cardinal virtues of courtly culture in the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. The adjective _milde_ (generous, liberal, bountiful) is
repeatedly applied to the poem's noble figures as an epithet of moral quality:
the Burgundian kings are _milde_ (001-017), [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] is _der milde Markgraf_
(027-253), and the distribution of gifts with _milder Hand_ (generous hand) is a
recurring gesture of political legitimacy. Yet the poem systematically exposes
the fragility of _milte_ as a social practice — the generosity that sustains
courtly order in the first half becomes impossible to maintain as the narrative
descends into revenge and annihilation.

## Definition

In the courtly value system of 12th–13th century German literature, _milte_
(Modern German _Milde_, cognate with English "mild") denotes not gentleness but
active liberality — the willingness and capacity to give freely of one's wealth,
territory, and favor. _Milte_ is distinguished from mere gift-giving (_gabe_) by
its characterological dimension: it is a stable trait of the noble personality,
not a strategic transaction. A _milder_ king is one whose hand is perpetually
open, whose generosity flows from innate nobility rather than calculation.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

_Milte_ was the preeminent virtue of the 12th–13th century German courtly
ethos. In the political philosophy articulated by the _[[minne|Minne]]sang_ poets and
didactic works like Thomasin von Zerclaere's _Der Welsche Gast_, _milte_
distinguished the legitimate ruler from the tyrant: the generous lord creates
obligation without coercion, binding his vassals through gratitude rather than
fear. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _milte_ reflects this ideal but also
exposes its vulnerability — generosity depends on surplus, and surplus
disappears in conditions of existential conflict.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's _milte_ arc follows the trajectory of the broader narrative: from
abundance to scarcity, from open-handedness to closed fists. In the first half,
_milte_ flows freely — [[siegfried|Siegfried]] distributes Nibelung treasure, the Burgundian
court hosts lavish festivals, [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] mediates between realms with gifts. In
the second half, after the Nibelung treasure is sunk in the [[rhine|Rhine]] and
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death has poisoned political relations, _milte_ becomes a memory
rather than a practice. [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s death in Adventure 37 is the symbolic
endpoint: the poem's most generous figure is killed in a battle he could not
avoid, and with him the possibility of _milte_ as a political force is
extinguished.

## Comparative Notes

- The MHG concept of _milte_ has no precise equivalent in the Norse analogues;
  the _Völsunga saga_ and Eddic poems treat gift-giving as a contractual
  obligation rather than a character virtue, reflecting the different social
  structures of the Icelandic and German courts
- In Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_ (contemporary with the
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied), _milte_ is the virtue that most directly indexes spiritual
  worth — the Grail king's generosity is a sign of divine favor. The
  [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment is more pessimistic: _milte_ is a human practice
  that history destroys
- [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s epithet _der milde_ marks him as the poem's most ethically complex
  figure — his generosity is genuine, but it cannot save him from the conflict
  between his oath to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and his bond with the Burgundians
