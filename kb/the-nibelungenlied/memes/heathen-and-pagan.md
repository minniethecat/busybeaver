# Heathen and Pagan in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied inhabits a world divided between Christians (_Christen_)
and heathens (_Heiden_). This division is not merely a background fact but a
recurring source of narrative tension. [[etzel|Etzel]] ([[etzel|Attila]] the Hun) is introduced as
"ein Heide, ein ungetaufter Mann" — a heathen, an unbaptized man (020-010) —
and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s decision to marry him is shadowed by the religious
implications of a Christian queen wedding a pagan king. The poem's final
catastrophe unfolds at a court where "Christen mit Heiden nicht stimmten
überein" — Christians and heathens did not agree together (031-010). The
religious divide is never the poem's central subject, but it is the constant
undertone of its political geography.

## Definition

The poem uses _Heide_ (heathen, pagan) and _Christen_ (Christian) as
fundamental categories of identity. _Heide_ denotes one who is not baptized —
"ein ungetaufter Mann" (020-010) — and the distinction between baptized and
unbaptized carries legal, social, and eschatological weight. The poem's
geography is organized around this divide: the Burgundian world ([[worms|Worms]], the
[[rhine|Rhine]]) is Christian; the Hunnish world ([[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, the lands beyond the
[[danube|Danube]]) is heathen. Yet the poem complicates this binary: [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court
contains both Christians and heathens (022-011, 031-010), and the boundary
between the two categories is more permeable than the initial formulation
suggests.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied was composed in a period when the crusading movement had
made the Christian–heathen binary a central category of European political
thought. Yet the poem's treatment of this binary is strikingly nuanced. [[etzel|Etzel]]
is a heathen, but he is never demonized — he is portrayed as a noble and
generous king whose court is in many ways more splendid than the Burgundian.
The poem distinguishes between religious status (baptized/unbaptized) and moral
quality, refusing to collapse one into the other. This distinguishes the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied from crusading literature like the _Chanson de Roland_, where
pagans are systematically demonized.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem deploys the Christian–heathen distinction as a structural device that
marks the boundary between two worlds. The Burgundian world (Adventures 1–19)
is implicitly Christian — characters attend Mass, swear oaths before God, and
are buried in the [[church-and-religion|Münster]]. The Hunnish world (Adventures 20–39) is explicitly
religious-diverse: Christians and heathens coexist, sometimes in harmony (the
wedding feast, 022-011), sometimes in tension (the separate eating, 031-010).
The poem's catastrophe unfolds not because heathens are evil but because the
political order that holds Christians and heathens together — [[etzel|Etzel]]'s
hospitality, [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s mediation — collapses under the weight of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s
vengeance. The religious divide is the context for tragedy, not its cause.

## Notable Passages

> "Ich bin ein Heide, ein ungetaufter Mann."
> — Rüdiger delivers Etzel's self-description — the frank acknowledgment of heathen status as a fact, not a confession (020-010)

> "Einem Heiden geben? Ich bin ein Christenweib."
> — Kriemhild's initial refusal — the religious boundary as obstacle to marriage (020-422)

> "Er ist nicht gar ein Heide, des dürft ihr sicher sein."
> — Rüdiger's reassurance — the heathen/Christian binary is softened to enable the marriage (020-477)

> Von Christen und von Heiden manches breite Heer.
> — Christians and heathens together at Etzel's wedding — the poem's image of religious coexistence (022-011)

> Daß Christen mit Heiden nicht stimmten überein.
> — Christians and heathens did not agree — the religious divide reasserts itself (031-010)

> Den Christen und den Heiden die Kost er unterschied.
> — Hagen separates the food of Christians and heathens — the religious boundary sharpened at the moment of crisis (031-279)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of the heathen–Christian divide is notably more
  nuanced than the _Chanson de Roland_, where Saracens are systematically
  demonized as worshippers of false gods — [[etzel|Etzel]] is a heathen but never a
  villain on religious grounds
- The Old Norse analogues (_Atlakviða_, _Atlamál_) do not develop the
  Christian–heathen distinction at all — this is a distinctively High Medieval
  addition to the legend, reflecting the religious geography of the Staufer
  period
- The poem's softening of the binary through [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s "nicht gar ein Heide"
  (020-477) reflects actual medieval practices of religious accommodation at
  culturally mixed courts — the Hungarian court of the Árpád dynasty, which may
  have influenced the poem's portrayal of [[etzel|Etzel]], included both Christian and
  pagan nobles
- The separate eating of Christians and heathens (031-279) echoes medieval
  dietary laws and the Fourth Lateran Council's (1215) provisions on Jewish–
  Christian commensality, suggesting the poet was aware of contemporary
  religious-separation practices
