# The Danube Crossing

The [[danube|Danube]] crossing (Adventure 25) is the most ominous passage in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — the moment when the Burgundians, journeying to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, encounter supernatural warnings that their expedition will end in death. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s encounter with the water nymphs, his murder of the ferryman, and his brutal test of the prophecy by drowning the chaplain transform the journey from a diplomatic visit into a march toward annihilation.

## Overview

- **Adventure(s)**: 25
- **Location**: The [[danube|Danube River]], at a ferry crossing
- **Key Participants**: [[hagen|Hagen]] (ferryman's killer, prophecy-hearer), [[gunther|Gunther]] (king), the Chaplain (sacrificial victim), the Water Nymphs (prophets), the Ferryman (murdered)
- **Outcome**: The Burgundians cross into [[hunland|Hunland]] knowing they will die

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

The Burgundian army has marched eastward from [[worms|Worms]] following [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s invitation. After weeks of travel, they reach the [[danube|Danube]]. The river is swollen; there is no bridge, no boat. [[hagen|Hagen]], scouting for a ferry, encounters three water nymphs bathing in the river. They offer a prophecy — but their true intention is to warn those who understand fate.

### The Event

The water nymphs prophesy that all but one will die in [[hunland|Hunland]] — only the chaplain will return to [[worms|Worms]] alive (025-029). [[hagen|Hagen]], refusing to accept the prophecy passively, decides to test it: he throws the chaplain into the river. But the chaplain, by divine providence, swims back to the Burgundian side and survives (025-050). [[hagen|Hagen]], convinced now that the prophecy is true, kills the ferryman who brings the army across (025-035), then destroys the boat to ensure no retreat. The crossing becomes a one-way journey.

### Aftermath

The Burgundians continue to [[etzels-court|Etzelnburg]] in grim silence, their fate sealed. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s actions — killing the ferryman, destroying the boat — mark him as both the protector of the army (he ensures no panic-driven retreat) and its destroyer (he eliminates any chance of escape). The prophecy of the water nymphs hangs over every subsequent event, confirming the epic's fatalistic worldview.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

The [[danube|Danube]] crossing transforms the epic's second half from political drama into tragedy: after this point, the audience knows the outcome. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s brutal testing of the prophecy — drowning the chaplain — establishes him as a figure of tragic defiance, a man who would rather fight fate than accept it.

### Medieval Themes

The episode embodies the medieval concept of fate (fatum) and its relationship to heroic action. The water nymphs represent the supernatural world intruding into the heroic, and [[hagen|Hagen]]'s response — to test the prophecy rather than submit to it — is the essence of the tragic hero. The episode also reflects the medieval geography of the [[danube|Danube]] as the boundary between Christendom and the pagan East.
