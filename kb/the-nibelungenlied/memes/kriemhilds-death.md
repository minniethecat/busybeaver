# Kriemhild's Death

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s death (Adventure 39) is the final scene of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — the moment when the avenger becomes the condemned. After orchestrating the destruction of the Burgundians, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] personally beheads the bound [[hagen|Hagen]] with [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s sword [[balmung|Balmung]]. But [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]], the aged warrior of [[dietrich|Dietrich von Bern]], cannot tolerate the killing of a bound, defenseless man by a woman. He strikes [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] down, and the epic ends with the hall filled with bodies and the declaration that "the tale has an end."

## Overview

- **Adventure(s)**: 39
- **Location**: [[etzel|Etzel]]'s great hall, amid the corpses of the fallen
- **Key Participants**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (executioner, then victim), [[hagen|Hagen]] (bound prisoner, defiant to the end), [[gunther|Gunther]] (already executed), [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] (avenger of chivalric order), Dietrich (captor of [[hagen|Hagen]] and [[gunther|Gunther]]), [[etzel|Etzel]] (witness)
- **Outcome**: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] killed; [[hagen|Hagen]] dead; the treasure's secret lost; the epic closes

## Narrative Context

### Prelude

After days of battle, [[dietrich|Dietrich von Bern]] has personally captured [[hagen|Hagen]] and [[gunther|Gunther]] and delivered them bound to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]. Dietrich has requested mercy for them, but [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revenge is absolute. She demands the treasure's location; [[hagen|Hagen]] refuses, saying that as long as [[gunther|Gunther]] lives, he will not speak. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] has [[gunther|Gunther]] beheaded and presents his head to [[hagen|Hagen]], who still refuses (039-168). She takes [[balmung|Balmung]] — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s own sword — and beheads [[hagen|Hagen]] with it (039-172).

### The Event

The moment [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] lifts [[balmung|Balmung]] and cuts off [[hagen|Hagen]]'s head, she crosses the final boundary. [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]], who has watched the entire scene, is seized with horror and fury. A bound man — a knight — has been killed by a woman, in violation of every principle of chivalric honor. Without a word, [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] draws his sword and charges at [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], cutting her down (039-175). She falls dead beside the man she has just killed. [[etzel|Etzel]], Dietrich, and [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] stand among the corpses. The epic's final stanza declares: "Here the tale has an end — of the Nibelungs' sorrow."

### Aftermath

There is no aftermath. The epic ends. The Burgundian kings are dead, their army annihilated, their treasure lost; [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], the avenger who burned a kingdom for revenge, is dead; the Huns grieve. Dietrich and [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] survive, but they have witnessed the end of worlds. The narrative offers no consolation, no judgment, no restoration.

## Thematic Analysis

### Significance to the Narrative

[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s death is the epic's final statement on revenge. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revenge is complete — she has killed all her enemies — but the manner of her act (killing a bound man) strips her of any moral standing. [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]'s swift judgment, without trial or deliberation, suggests that some lines cannot be crossed, even in a world of blood-feud. The epic ends not with resolution but with exhaustion.

### Medieval Themes

The killing of a bound man by a woman violates two medieval codes simultaneously: the prohibition against killing the defenseless and the gender hierarchy that placed women outside the sphere of combat. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s death therefore functions as a restoration of order — but it is a bitter restoration, leaving only the old men alive. The epic's refusal to moralize — it simply declares "the tale has an end" — is its most radical feature.
