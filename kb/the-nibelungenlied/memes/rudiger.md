# Rüdiger

Margrave Rüdiger von [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]] is [[etzel|Etzel]]'s most trusted vassal and the character most often cited as the embodiment of the epic's moral tragedy. A man of impeccable honor, he is introduced as [[etzel|Etzel]]'s envoy to [[worms|Worms]] to propose marriage to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (020-019). Everything he does in the poem is done in good faith — and everything leads to his destruction. His crisis of conscience in Adventure 37, torn between his oath to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and his bonds of friendship and marriage with the Burgundians, is the epic's most powerful dramatization of the incompatibility of competing loyalties.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Margrave of [[bechelaren|Bechelaren]]; [[etzel|Etzel]]'s chief diplomat and vassal
- **Affiliation**: [[hun-court|Hun Court]] — [[etzel|Etzel]]'s most powerful margrave
- **Family**: Gotlind (wife), one daughter (betrothed to [[geiselher|Geiselher]] of Burgundy)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 20 — sent by [[etzel|Etzel]] to woo [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (020-019)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part II: The Honorable Broker and the Doomed Man (Adventures 20–39)

Rüdiger's role unfolds in three acts. In Adventures 20–21, he acts as [[etzel|Etzel]]'s envoy, traveling to [[worms|Worms]] to negotiate the marriage with [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]. He is received with honor by the Burgundians, forging bonds of hospitality and friendship (020-100). [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] extracts an oath from him to avenge any wrong done to her (020-458, 020-461) — an oath that will later destroy him.

In Adventures 22–36, Rüdiger is [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s escort to the Hun court. He treats the Burgundians with continued generosity when they arrive at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court (031-106). He reveals that his daughter is betrothed to [[geiselher|Geiselher]] (037-109) — a marriage that would have sealed an alliance between the Huns and the Burgundians.

In Adventure 37, Rüdiger faces his crisis. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] demands that he fulfill his oath and attack the Burgundians (037-065). [[etzel|Etzel]] adds his own pleas (037-069). Rüdiger cannot refuse without breaking his oath; he cannot attack without breaking his bonds of friendship and his daughter's betrothal. He enters the hall weeping, fights [[gernot|Gernot]], and both fall dead (037-342, 037-350).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Rüdiger is driven by [[triuwe|Triuwe]] in its most developed form — the loyalty that binds a vassal to his lord, a host to his guest, a father to his daughter's betrothed. His tragedy is that these loyalties conflict. He cannot serve [[etzel|Etzel]] without betraying [[geiselher|Geiselher]]; he cannot honor his oath to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] without violating his friendship with the Burgundians. The poem presents his dilemma as insoluble, and his choice — any choice — as a betrayal of someone he loves.

### Virtues & Flaws

Rüdiger is called "the good Rüdiger" (der guote Rüdiger) throughout the epic — the only character consistently described by a moral epithet. His virtue is his loyalty; his flaw is the same. He has no ethical framework for prioritizing conflicting obligations. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied offers no judgment of Rüdiger, only lament: "der vater aller tugend vant an Rüdiger den tôt" (the father of all virtues found death through Rüdiger — 037-276).

## Notable Passages

> "Da sprach von Bechlaren der gute Rüdiger" (020-019) — the first appearance of Rüdiger, marked with his signature epithet

> "Sie sprach: 'So schwört mir, Rüdiger, was mir jemand thut, daß ihr der erste wäret, der rächst meine Not.'" (020-458) — Kriemhild extracts the fatal oath

> "O weh, viel edler Rüdiger, daß du uns so verloren bist!" (038-104) — the Burgundians' lament after his death

## Death

Rüdiger dies in Adventure 37, killed by [[gernot|Gernot]] in the battle at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court. After entering the hall against the Burgundians with agonized reluctance, he fights [[gernot|Gernot]] — each kills the other in the same exchange (037-342, 037-350). The death of Rüdiger is the turning point of the battle: afterward, even the victors mourn him. The lament for Rüdiger by both sides — Burgundians and Huns alike — is the epic's clearest statement that this is a tragedy without villains.

## Scholarly Notes

- Rüdiger is one of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's most studied characters, often analyzed as a figure of tragic moral choice in a world of irreconcilable obligations.
- He has no direct equivalent in the Old Norse tradition; his role appears to be a creation of the German poet, possibly drawing on the figure of a historical margrave from the Bavarian-Austrian border region.
- The episode of Rüdiger's dilemma and death (Adventure 37) is considered by many scholars to be the poem's artistic peak — the point where the epic transcends the revenge-narrative and becomes a meditation on the nature of loyalty.
