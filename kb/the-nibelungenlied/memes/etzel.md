# Etzel

Etzel (the historical Attila the Hun) is the most powerful monarch in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — king of the Huns, ruler of a vast multi-ethnic court from Vienna to the East. Yet he is also one of the most passive figures in the epic. Introduced when he seeks a new wife after Helche's death (020-002), Etzel's role is to provide the stage on which [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] enacts her revenge. He is a well-intentioned king who loses control of his own court and ends the epic weeping over the dead (039-215, 039-245).

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: King of the Huns (der künec Etzel)
- **Affiliation**: [[hun-court|Hun Court]] — ruler of the most powerful kingdom in the epic
- **Family**: Helche (first wife, deceased), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (second wife)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 20 — named as the king who seeks a new wife after Helche's death (020-002)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part II: The Instrument of Revenge (Adventures 20–39)

Etzel appears only in Part II of the epic, and his role is almost entirely defined by [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]. He sends [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] to [[worms|Worms]] to propose marriage (020-019), receives [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] with honor (020-600), and is genuinely committed to being a good husband. When [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] begins to implement her revenge, however, Etzel is unable — or unwilling — to control her.

He is a passive witness to the escalating tension at the feast: when [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] demands that [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] attack the Burgundians, Etzel supports her but cannot enforce his will without [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s consent (037-041, 037-069, 037-097). Throughout the battle, Etzel is reduced to pleading and weeping. He watches his own kingdom burn and his warriors destroy one another. In the final adventure, Etzel and Dietrich weep together over the carnage (039-215, 039-245) — the great king of the Huns powerless to stop the tragedy unfolding in his own hall.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Etzel is motivated by conventional royal concerns: securing his dynasty through marriage and maintaining the reputation of his court. He marries [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] for her beauty and status, not for strategic enmity against the Burgundians. He never intends the destruction of the Burgundians — his tragedy is that his trust in [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and his inability to control her turn his court into a slaughterhouse.

### Virtues & Flaws

Etzel embodies magnanimous kingship: he is generous, welcoming, and trusting. These become fatal flaws. His trust in [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] blinds him to her revenge plot. His inability to act decisively when the conflict escalates allows the battle to spiral beyond control. The poem presents Etzel as a good king in peacetime who is utterly incapable in crisis — a commentary on the limits of passive royal virtue.

## Notable Passages

> "Und der König Etzel um andre Frauen warb" (020-002) — the introduction of Etzel's courtship

> "Etzel und Kriemhild kamen beide hervor" (036-037) — the royal couple witnessing the battle

> "Dietrich und Etzel huben zu weinen an" (039-215, 039-245) — the final image of Etzel

## Death

Etzel does not die in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. He survives the destruction of his court, and the final image of him is one of utter desolation: weeping over the bodies of his warriors, his guests, and his wife's victims. The poem ends before his fate is resolved.

## Scholarly Notes

- Etzel is the historical Attila the Hun (d. 453 CE), here anachronistically conflated with the 5th-century Hunnic empire and repurposed for a medieval German epic. The historical Attila died of a hemorrhage on his wedding night, not in battle.
- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's Etzel is strikingly different from the Old Norse Atli, who is a brutal, avaricious king who murders [[gunther|Gunnar]] and Högni for the Nibelung treasure. The German tradition systematically transforms Attila into a benevolent but weak monarch.
- Etzel's passivity has been read by scholars as a reflection of the epic's theme of the helplessness of legitimate authority against the forces of personal vengeance.
- Etzel's court is famously multi-ethnic, including Huns, Eastern Europeans, and exiles like [[dietrich|Dietrich of Bern]] — a reflection of the historical Attila's polyglot empire.
