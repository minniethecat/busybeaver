# Beauty and Courtly Appearance in the Nibelungenlied

In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, beauty (_Schönheit_) is never a passive attribute — it is
a force that drives the narrative, a currency that shapes alliances, and a
provocation that precipitates violence. From the poem's opening description of
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] — "Der man das Lob der Schönheit vor allen Jungfrauen lieh"
(005-032) — to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s admission that his own beauty has long since faded
("Hatt ich jemals Schönheit, um die ists lange geschehn," 020-412), physical
appearance is the visible register of power, desire, and mortality. The poem
deploys _Schönheit_ and its associated vocabulary (_minniglich_, _wünniglich_,
_wohlgetan_) with systematic precision: beauty attracts suitors, provokes envy,
justifies violence, and underwrites the social order — until the same beauty
that built the courtly world helps destroy it.

## Definition

MHG _schœne_ (noun) and _schœne_ (adjective) derive from OHG _scōni_, meaning
"visible, bright, radiant." In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, _Schönheit_ encompasses both
physical attractiveness and the broader quality of courtly refinement
(_Zucht_) — a person described as _schœne_ is not merely good-looking but
properly formed according to the standards of the courtly world. The poem
distinguishes between male and female beauty: women's _Schönheit_ is typically
described in terms of radiance and brilliance (_"alsam der lichte Tac,"_ "like
the bright day"), while men's beauty is linked to martial prowess and physical
stature (_"ein waetlîcher man,"_ "a handsome/fit man"). Crucially, _Schönheit_
in the poem is public and performative — it exists to be seen, judged, and
ranked. The poem is explicit about hierarchies of beauty: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] surpasses
all other maidens (005-032); [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is the most beautiful of heroes. Beauty
creates _ordo_ — a visible order of rank that mirrors the social hierarchy.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In the courtly literature of the High Middle Ages, physical beauty was
understood as a reflection of inner virtue — the _kalokagathia_ principle
inherited from classical tradition and mediated through Christian Platonism. A
beautiful knight was presumed to be a good knight; a beautiful lady was
presumed to be virtuous. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied both deploys and subverts this
convention. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s beauty is repeatedly invoked as evidence of her worth,
yet the poem shows that beauty can mask destructive intent — her _Schönheit_
survives [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death and becomes the lure that draws the Burgundians to
their destruction. The poem's association of beauty with radiance and light
(_"alsam der lichte Tac"_) draws on the courtly _sumerlaten_ tradition of
troubadour lyric, but the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied darkens this convention: the most
beautiful woman in the poem is also its most lethal.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem maps the career of _Schönheit_ from social adhesive to social solvent.
In the first half (Adventures 1–19), beauty is functional: it attracts allies,
secures marriages, and underwrites the Burgundian court's prestige. [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
pursuit of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] is explicitly motivated by reports of her beauty (003-203),
and his own beauty impresses the Burgundian court (003-203). The quarrel of the
queens in Adventure 14 is the turning point: when [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and [[brunhild|Brunhild]]
compare their husbands' and their own beauty, _Schönheit_ ceases to be a shared
courtly value and becomes a weapon of social competition. After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
death, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s beauty persists — indeed, it is her beauty that secures
[[etzel|Etzel]]'s proposal (020-064) — but it has been hollowed out, become instrumental.
The poem's final movement shows beauty utterly divorced from virtue: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]],
still beautiful, presides over the annihilation of her kin. [[etzel|Etzel]]'s lament
(020-412) — "Hatt ich jemals Schönheit, um die ists lange geschehn" — is the
poem's epitaph for the courtly ideal of beauty: beauty fades, and with it the
world it adorned.

## Notable Passages

> Der man das Lob der Schönheit vor allen Jungfrauen lieh.
> — Kriemhild praised above all maidens for her beauty — the poem's first
> deployment of _Schönheit_ as a ranking mechanism; beauty creates hierarchy from the opening stanza (005-032)

> Ihrer hohen Schönheit willen der Degen da verblieb.
> — The hero remained for the sake of her high beauty — Siegfried's decision
> to stay at Worms is explicitly attributed to Kriemhild's appearance; beauty
> as the anchor that holds the hero in place (005-241)

> "Ihrer hohen Schönheit willen gewagt muß es sein"
> — "For the sake of her high beauty it must be dared" — Siegfried's
> formulation of the lover's gamble: beauty demands risk, and the hero's
> worth is proven by his willingness to hazard all for it (006-039)

> "Eurer Schönheit willen bestünd ich Alles gern"
> — "For the sake of your beauty I would gladly endure everything" — the
> courtly lover's pledge that makes beauty the ground of feudal service and the
> justification for any sacrifice (007-155)

> "Mich jammern immer ihre Schönheit, ihre Zucht"
> — "Her beauty and her breeding always grieve me" — Brunhild's lament;
> _Schönheit_ here paired with _Zucht_ (courtly discipline), the two attributes
> that define the ideal courtly woman and the two that provoke the most
> destructive envy (010-177)

> Ob ihrer großen Schönheit gefällt sie wohl meinem Sinn.
> — "Because of her great beauty she pleases my mind well" — Etzel's verdict on
> Kriemhild; beauty as the ground of royal decision-making, the poem's last
> functional deployment of _Schönheit_ before the catastrophe (020-064)

> Hatt ich jemals Schönheit, um die ists lange geschehn.
> — "If I ever had beauty, that is long since gone" — Etzel's acknowledgment
> of beauty's transience; the only moment in the poem where _Schönheit_ is
> explicitly linked to loss and mortality (020-412)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of beauty as a social force parallels the
  _Kudrun_ epic, where the heroine's beauty similarly drives the narrative —
  but where _Kudrun_ resolves its beauty-provoked conflicts through
  reconciliation, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied drives beauty's logic to its lethal
  conclusion
- In the Old Norse _Völsunga saga_, Brynhild's beauty is described with the
  same vocabulary of radiance, but the saga's mythological framework
  attributes her beauty to supernatural origin — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's
  courtly world treats beauty as a natural attribute whose consequences are
  entirely social
- The poem's association of _Schönheit_ with _Zucht_ (courtly discipline)
  reflects the Staufer-period ideal of _curialitas_ — beauty is not merely
  physical but the visible expression of proper breeding and training
- Unlike the French romances of Chrétien de Troyes, where beauty is often a
  static quality of the beloved, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied shows beauty as dynamic
  and consequential — it changes hands, provokes action, and ultimately
  destroys those who possess it and those who desire it
