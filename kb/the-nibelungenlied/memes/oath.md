# Oath

The sworn oath (Middle High German: Eid) is the foundational speech act of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's social world. Oaths create binding obligations that transcend personal inclination: [[alberich|Alberich]] "must swear oaths to serve him as his man" after [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s victory (003-225); warriors "swore oaths to each other, these heroes noble" before the journey to [[isenstein|Isenstein]] (006-057). When oaths are broken — as when [[brunhild|Brunhild]] accuses [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] of violating her word (010-119) — the social fabric tears.

## Definition

An Eid in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a solemn verbal declaration, typically invoking divine witness or personal honor, that creates a binding obligation. Oaths establish feudal bonds (vassalage), military alliances, and personal commitments. Their binding force is absolute — an oath-breaker (Meineidiger) forfeits honor and invites divine punishment. The oath thus functions as the legal infrastructure of a society without written contracts or codified law.

### The Irony of Oaths

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied presents oaths with deep irony. [[alberich|Alberich]]'s oath of fealty to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is one of the few that hold — yet [[alberich|Alberich]] is a dwarf, a non-human being. The human oaths — between [[gunther|Gunther]] and [[siegfried|Siegfried]], between [[hagen|Hagen]] and the Burgundian court, between [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and her brothers — all fail. The epic suggests that the oath, the most solemn institution of the heroic world, is ultimately powerless to restrain human betrayal.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The Eid was the central legal institution of medieval Germanic society, predating written law. Oaths were sworn on relics or weapons, and their violation was both a legal crime (Meineid) and a religious sin. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied reflects a society in which the oath is simultaneously the highest guarantee of order and the most fragile.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The epic's treatment of oaths is profoundly pessimistic. Oaths are sworn and broken; the consequences cascade. The oath that should bind society together instead becomes the measure of its failure. In this, the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied anticipates a modern insight: institutions are only as strong as the people who uphold them, and even the most sacred institutions cannot survive systematic bad faith.
