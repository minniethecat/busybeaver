# Misfortune and Ill-Fate

The language of misfortune pervades the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. The Middle High German
adverb _übel_ (badly, evilly, unfortunately) and the noun _Ungemach_
(affliction, distress, hardship) recur throughout the poem, forming a lexical
underscore that tracks the narrative's descent from the celebratory festivals of
the first half to the catastrophic slaughter of the second. This vocabulary is
not merely descriptive — it is often prophetic, with characters naming their own
future suffering in terms that the poem's audience knows will be fulfilled.

## Definition

In MHG usage, _übel_ functions as both an adverb ("badly," "evilly") and, in
substantivized form (_daz übel_), a noun denoting evil or misfortune. The
phrase _übel geschehen_ / _übel ergehen_ ("to fare badly," "to suffer
misfortune") appears as a formulaic expression anticipating or describing
disaster. _Ungemach_ denotes specific affliction or hardship — the Saxon
warriors suffer _Ungemach_ in battle (004-303), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s future _Ungemach_ is
prophesied (006-217), and the Burgundians endure _Ungemach_ on their journey
(017-112, 017-206). Together, these terms create a semantic network of
anticipated and realized suffering that structures the poem's emotional arc.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's vocabulary of misfortune draws on a Germanic heroic
tradition in which fate (_wurt_, OHG _wurt_) is an impersonal force that
determines outcomes regardless of human intention. But the poem complicates this
tradition by making _übel_ simultaneously fated and chosen: [[hagen|Hagen]]'s acts are
both prophesied and voluntarily committed. This dual causation — the coincidence
of fate and human agency — is one of the poem's central philosophical tensions.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The distribution of _übel_ / _Ungemach_ across the poem traces a clear arc. In
the first half (Adventures 1–19), _übel_ appears primarily as a possibility or
a prophecy — something that may happen, that is warned against, that lurks
beneath the surface of courtly celebration. After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, _übel_
becomes an accomplished fact — something that has happened, that cannot be
undone, that generates the revenge plot. In the final adventures (28–39),
_übel_ becomes an identity: Dietrich's accusation that [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] is _ein übel
[[gender-and-femininity|Weib]]_ (028-033) marks the moment when the poem's heroine, once the victim of
_übel_, is redefined as its embodiment.

## Notable Passages

> So übel nie begegnet wären ihm die Herren mein.
> — Siegfried anticipates that his enemies will fare badly against him (003-316)

> Mir ist von euern Reden, das wißet, übel geschehn.
> — Kriemhild responds to Hagen's confession of the murder (014-132)

> Gar übel hatte Hagen gerochen Brunhildens Zorn.
> — The narrator frames Siegfried's death as Hagen's excessive revenge for Brunhild's anger (017-048)

> Und kommst du zu den Heunen, so bist du übel betrogen.
> — A prophetic warning to the Burgundians before the fatal journey (025-152)

> Da sprach zu ihr der Berner: "Ihr seid ein übel Weib."
> — Dietrich's accusation against Kriemhild at the climax of the revenge (028-033)

> So ist doch immer übel und arg von mir geschehn.
> — Hagen's final admission, acknowledging a lifetime of violent acts (037-078)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's use of _übel_ as both prophecy and event mirrors the
  Norse concept of _ú-heill_ (ill-omen, misfortune) in the Eddic poems, where
  characters foresee their doom but cannot avert it
- Unlike classical tragedy (where _hamartia_ — a fatal flaw — drives the
  protagonist's fall), the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied distributes the vocabulary of
  misfortune across multiple characters, refusing to locate _übel_ in a single
  tragic agent
- The poem's most distinctive contribution is the transformation of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]
  from _übel_-sufferer to _übel_-source — a narrative arc without parallel in
  the Norse analogues, where [[kriemhild|Gudrun]]/[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] remains primarily a figure of
  grief rather than active evil
