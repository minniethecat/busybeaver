# Dietrich von Bern

Dietrich von Bern (Theodoric of Verona) is the most powerful warrior in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied and a figure unique in medieval German epic: an exiled king living at a foreign court, a warrior of near-superhuman strength who consistently chooses restraint over violence. Introduced as one of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s retainers (022-066), Dietrich spends most of the epic trying to avoid conflict. He enters the battle only when provoked beyond endurance, and his capture of [[hagen|Hagen]] and [[gunther|Gunther]] at the epic's conclusion is the closest thing to justice the poem offers.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Exiled King of Bern (Verona); retainer at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court
- **Affiliation**: [[hun-court|Hun Court]] — residing at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court in honorable exile with his retinue of Amelung warriors
- **Family**: Dietwart (father); [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] (master-at-arms, his elder); several nephews and retainers ([[wolfhart|Wolfhart]], Wolfbrand, etc.)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 22 — named among [[etzel|Etzel]]'s retinue (022-066)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part II: The Reluctant Warrior (Adventures 22–39)

Dietrich appears throughout Part II as a figure of authority and restraint. When the Burgundians first arrive at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, Dietrich welcomes them and attempts to mediate. He repeatedly refuses [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s attempts to draw him into her revenge scheme, telling her bluntly that he will not attack guests (028-217). He protects the Burgundians when possible and tries to prevent the conflict.

In Adventure 33, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] appeals to Dietrich directly, begging for his help. He refuses with dignity: "I will help no one break the peace" (033-137). His refusal enrages [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], but Dietrich holds firm.

In Adventure 37, when [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] is forced to act, Dietrich tries to stop the battle. He sends [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]] to negotiate (038-081), but the situation spirals beyond his control. When his own nephew [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] is killed by the Burgundians, Dietrich's men attack — and in the resulting battle, all of Dietrich's warriors are slain, including [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] (038-237, 038-251, 038-258).

Devastated, Dietrich arms himself and confronts the remaining Burgundians (039-001). He fights [[hagen|Hagen]] in single combat, proving superior even to the great [[hagen|Hagen]] (039-100, 039-101, 039-106). He wounds [[hagen|Hagen]] and binds him (039-117). He then fights and captures [[gunther|Gunther]] (039-149, 039-153). He delivers both to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], expecting them to be held as prisoners — not knowing she will kill them both. In the poem's final lines, Dietrich is found weeping alongside [[etzel|Etzel]], the last survivor of the catastrophe (039-215, 039-245).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Dietrich is motivated by a concept of honor that transcends the revenge-dynamics driving every other character. He is an exile — he has already lost his kingdom — and therefore has nothing to gain from the conflict at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court. His consistent refusal to participate in [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s plot is not weakness but a conscious choice to uphold the norms of hospitality and guest-right that everyone else has abandoned.

### Virtues & Flaws

Dietrich's virtues are restraint, justice, and loyalty to his own warrior code. He is the strongest warrior in the poem — the only man who can defeat [[hagen|Hagen]] in single combat — yet he avoids fighting for as long as possible. His flaw, if any, is that his moral position makes him powerless: he can defeat any opponent but he cannot stop the tragedy.

## Death

Dietrich does not die in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. He survives the destruction of [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, and the epic ends with an image of him weeping over the dead. In the wider Dietrich cycle (the historical Dietrichsaga), he eventually regains his kingdom of Bern from the usurper Ermenrich.

## Scholarly Notes

- Dietrich corresponds to Theodoric the Great (454–526 CE), the Ostrogothic king of Italy. The epic anachronistically places him a generation earlier, alongside [[etzel|Attila]] (who died in 453).
- The figure of Dietrich is the subject of an entire separate epic cycle (the Dietrichsaga) comprising some 13 medieval German poems. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied represents only a cameo appearance of this much larger legendary figure.
- Dietrich's presence at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court as an exile reflects a tradition that Theodoric, driven from Italy by Odoacer, took refuge with the Huns — an event with no historical basis but powerful legendary resonance.
- Dietrich's refusal to fight guests has been read as the poem's most explicit articulation of an alternative to the politics of revenge — an alternative that proves unable to stop the catastrophe but stands as a moral witness to it.
