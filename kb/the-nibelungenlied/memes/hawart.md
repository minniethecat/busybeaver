Hawart is a Danish hero who appears in the latter part of the Nibelungenlied. He arrives at [[etzels-court|Etzel's court]] for the wedding festivities of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and King [[etzel|Etzel]], and later fights alongside [[iring|Iring]] in the catastrophic final battle, where he and his thousand men are slain.

## Arrival at Etzel's Court

Hawart is introduced among the distinguished guests who come to Etzel's court for the wedding: "Vom Dänenlande der kühne Hawart" (022-037). He arrives from Denmark with his own retinue of warriors and is received with the honor befitting a lord of his standing. His presence establishes the international scope of Etzel's court, drawing heroes from across the Germanic world.

> "Vom Dänenlande der kühne Hawart" (022-037)

## Alliance with Iring

Hawart is closely associated with [[iring|Iring]], another Danish hero. The two are repeatedly named together as a pair of elite warriors: "Hawart und Iring, zwei Degen auserkoren" — Hawart and Iring, two chosen warriors (029-197). This pairing mirrors the Gere-Eckewart duo among the Burgundians, establishing Hawart and Iring as the Danish counterpart: two heroes bound by shared nationality and martial prowess.

Together with [[irnfried|Irnfried]], they form a trio of allied lords who ride into the tournament-like skirmishes (buhurt) that precede the full-scale battle: "Irnfried und Hawart ritten zum Buhurd hin" (031-117).

## The Final Battle

When the full battle erupts at Etzel's hall, Hawart enters the fray with formidable force: "Und Hawart der starke wohl mit tausend Mann" (035-015). His thousand warriors demonstrate his status as a powerful lord. The battle is ferocious and unrelenting. Hawart's men fight bravely but are gradually overwhelmed:

> "Es muste vor ihm weichen Hawarts Unterthan" (035-099)

The imagery intensifies as the battle reaches its climax: "Mit feuerrothem Winde" — with fire-red wind — the poet describes the onslaught against Hawart's forces (035-138). Hawart and Irnfried make a desperate sortie, leaping before the house in a final attempt to turn the tide (035-169).

## Death

Hawart meets his end in direct combat with [[hagen|Hagen]]. The two warriors come together in the chaos of the hall (035-181), and Hagen's superior prowess proves decisive:

> "Da muste Hawart sterben vor dem aus Burgundenland" (035-184)

The death of Hawart, "der Held in Hawarts Lehn" who could not recover from his wounds (035-167), is part of the cascading destruction that consumes nearly every hero present at Etzel's court. His fall, together with that of Iring and Irnfried, eliminates the Danish contingent entirely.

## Character and Significance

Hawart represents the international warrior aristocracy drawn into the vortex of Kriemhild's vengeance. He has no personal stake in the Burgundian-Hunnish conflict — he came to Etzel's court for a wedding celebration — yet he is swept into the catastrophe by the obligations of hospitality and the warrior code. His death, like those of so many others, underscores the poem's tragic vision: honor and loyalty, the highest virtues of the heroic age, become the engines of indiscriminate destruction.
