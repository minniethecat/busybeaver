# Revenge

Revenge (Middle High German: Rache) is the central narrative force of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's second part (Adventures 20–39). After [[hagen|Hagen]] murders [[siegfried|Siegfried]], [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s desire for vengeance transforms her from a grieving widow into an agent of methodical destruction, orchestrating the annihilation of her own Burgundian kin at the court of [[etzel|Etzel the Hun]].

## Definition

In the world of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, Rache is not merely an emotional impulse but a social obligation rooted in the Germanic concept of blood feud. The failure to avenge a murdered kinsman or spouse is a profound dishonor. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revenge operates on multiple levels: personal (for her murdered husband), political (against [[hagen|Hagen]] and those who protected him), and symbolic (the destruction of the world that allowed [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death to go unpunished).

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The blood feud ([[blood-and-wounds|Blut]]rache) was a recognized institution in Germanic customary law, predating and coexisting with Christian concepts of forgiveness and royal justice. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, composed during the transition from customary to codified law, reflects the tension between the old ethic of personal vengeance and emerging norms of legal process. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revenge is legally and morally ambiguous — justified by the old code, monstrous by any measure.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The epic traces revenge's corrosive logic to its ultimate conclusion. What begins as a specific grievance — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s demand for justice for [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder — escalates into total destruction. The revenge that should restore honor instead consumes the avenger, her targets, and her entire world. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied thus offers a profound critique of the revenge ethic: it shows not its failure (the revenge succeeds) but its cost (nothing remains worth having). The final image — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] beheaded by [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]], the hall filled with corpses — is revenge's logical endpoint.

## Comparative Notes

- The Old Norse Atlakviða (Lay of Atli) presents a version where [[kriemhild|Gudrun]] ([[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]) takes revenge on Atli ([[etzel|Etzel]]) himself for killing her brothers — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied reverses the direction, having [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] destroy her brothers to avenge her husband.
- Classical models of revenge tragedy (Aeschylus's Oresteia, Seneca's plays) share structural features with the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied: the cycle of violence, the female avenger, the tension between justice and destruction.
- The epic's critique of revenge resonates with the medieval Christian condemnation of vengeance as sin, yet the poem never fully repudiates the heroic code — it shows the tragedy without offering a moral resolution.
