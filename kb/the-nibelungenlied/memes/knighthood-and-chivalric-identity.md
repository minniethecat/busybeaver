# Knighthood and Chivalric Identity in the Nibelungenlied

The world of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is a world of knights. The poem's opening
stanzas establish this immediately: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s father's land is served by
"viel stolze Ritterschaft" (001-022) — much proud knighthood — and the poet
announces that the tale concerns "hohen Würdigkeit und von der Ritterschaft"
(001-046), high worthiness and knighthood. Every major character in the poem
is a knight or defined in relation to knighthood: [[siegfried]] is the knight
_par excellence_, [[gunther]] is a king who is also a knight, [[hagen]] is a
vassal-knight whose loyalty to his lord overrides all other considerations.
_Ritterschaft_ in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is not merely a military function but a
total social identity — it determines how characters dress, speak, fight, love,
and die. Yet the poem is far from a simple celebration of chivalric values. The
same _Ritterschaft_ that enables heroic achievement also constrains political
action, and the poem's catastrophe can be read as the failure of chivalric
norms to contain the forces they have unleashed.

## Definition

MHG _ritterschaft_ (feminine noun, from _ritter_ — "rider, knight," from OHG
_ritan_ — "to ride") denotes the collective body of knights, the institution of
knighthood, and the quality of being a knight. In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, the term
carries all three senses simultaneously: it is the **social estate** (the
knightly class as distinct from clergy and peasants), the **institutional
practice** (tournaments, feasts, armed service), and the **ethical code**
(bravery, loyalty, courtly conduct). The poem's knights are defined by a nexus
of related terms — _[[beauty-and-courtly-appearance|Zucht]]_ (discipline, breeding), _[[ere|Êre]]_ (honor), _[[triuwe|Triuwe]]_
(loyalty), _[[generosity-and-liberality|Milte]]_ (generosity) — that together constitute the chivalric
habitus. _Ritterschaft_ is performed rather than innate: it must be displayed
in tournament, proved in battle, and recognized by peers. The poem is attentive
to the material culture of knighthood — horses, armor, weapons, clothing — as
the visible infrastructure of chivalric identity.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

By the time the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied was composed (c. 1200), the institution of
knighthood had undergone a transformation. What had begun as a practical
military function — armored cavalry — had developed into a self-conscious
social estate with its own rituals (dubbing, the vigil of arms), its own
literature (the romance, the _[[minne|Minne]]sang_), and its own ethical vocabulary. The
poem reflects this developed chivalric culture: its knights participate in
tournaments, observe courtly etiquette, and measure themselves against an
explicit standard of knightly conduct. But the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied also preserves
elements of an older, pre-chivalric heroic ethos — the blood-feud, the demand
for vengeance regardless of social cost, the valuation of loyalty over law. The
tension between these two registers — chivalric and heroic — is one of the
poem's deepest structural principles. Characters invoke chivalric norms even as
they act according to heroic imperatives, and the gap between chivalric
profession and heroic practice becomes the space of the poem's tragedy.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces the career of _Ritterschaft_ from social glue to social
solvent. In the first half, knighthood functions as a shared framework that
enables cooperation across political boundaries: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s arrival at [[worms|Worms]]
is managed through chivalric protocol, the Saxon war is fought according to
knightly conventions, and the double wedding is the chivalric event _par
excellence_. Even [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s challenge to her suitors is framed in chivalric
terms — the contest is a knightly trial, and [[gunther|Gunther]]'s failure would be a
failure of _Ritterschaft_. After [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s death, _Ritterschaft_ shifts from
enabling framework to rhetorical cover. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is
justified in the language of knightly loyalty to [[gunther|Gunther]]'s honor, but the act
itself — an ambush at a spring — violates every norm of chivalric combat.
[[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s revenge in the poem's second half represents the systematic
dismantling of chivalric order: hospitality is betrayed, truces are broken, and
knights are burned alive in a hall rather than defeated in open combat. The
poem's final image — a heap of dead knights, the flower of _Ritterschaft_ —
is the ultimate comment on chivalric identity: it is a glorious death, but it
is death nonetheless.

## Notable Passages

> in ihren Landen diente viel stolze Ritterschaft
> — "In their lands served much proud knighthood" — the poem establishes
> knighthood as the foundation of Burgundian power before introducing any
> individual character; the knightly estate is the poem's social given (001-022)

> hohen Würdigkeit und von der Ritterschaft
> — "Of high worthiness and of knighthood" — the poet's declaration of subject
> matter pairs _Ritterschaft_ with _Würdigkeit_ (worthiness); chivalry is
> not merely a function but a quality of the person (001-046)

> daß wir auch pflegen Ritterschaft!
> — "That we also practice knighthood!" — Siegfried's challenge to the
> Burgundians; _Ritterschaft_ is something one "pflegen" (cultivates,
> practices), an ongoing performance rather than a fixed status (003-280)

> der König war gelandet mit fremder Ritterschaft
> — "The king had landed with foreign knighthood" — Etzel's Huns are
> recognized as _Ritterschaft_; the term crosses political boundaries,
> creating a shared identity that transcends national difference (010-025)

> Was darauf beseßen der Könge Ritterschaft
> — "What the kings' knighthood possessed thereupon" — _Ritterschaft_ in
> its material dimension: the equipment, horses, and possessions that are
> the knight's defining property and visible identity (025-322)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of _Ritterschaft_ differs markedly from the
  Arthurian romances of Chrétien de Troyes, where chivalry is an aspirational
  ideal that individual knights pursue through _aventure_ — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
  treats knighthood as an established social fact rather than a personal quest
- Wolfram von Eschenbach's _Parzival_ (c. 1200–1210), composed roughly
  contemporaneously with the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, presents chivalry as a spiritual
  journey culminating in the Grail kingship — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied offers no such
  transcendence; its knights remain within the immanent frame of honor, loyalty,
  and revenge
- The poem's knights share more with the heroes of the _Chanson de Roland_ than
  with Arthurian knights — like Roland, they are defined by feudal loyalty
  rather than individual _aventure_, and their deaths are political events
  rather than personal tragedies
