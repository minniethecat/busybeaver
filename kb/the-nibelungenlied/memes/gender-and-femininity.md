# Gender and Femininity in the Nibelungenlied

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied opens with a woman: "Es wuchs in Burgunden solch edel
Mägdelein" (001-005). It closes with a woman's death: [[kriemhild]] beheaded by
[[hildebrand]] for the unfeminine act of killing a man (039-001).

Between these two moments, the poem elaborates one of medieval literature's most
complex treatments of gender. Women are simultaneously the supreme objects of
courtly valuation (the _frouwe_ enthroned in beauty) and the agents of the
poem's most consequential violence. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's women are not
marginal to the heroic world; they define its terms and, ultimately, destroy
it.

## Definition

In the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, femininity is constructed around a set of interrelated
terms. _Mägdelein_ (maiden) denotes the unmarried woman, defined by beauty and
availability for marriage-alliance. _Frau_ (lady, wife) denotes the married
woman, defined by her relationship to a husband and her position in the
courtly order.

_Weib_ (woman, wife) is the more elemental term — "und ward ein schönes Weib"
(001-007) — often used when the poem registers the sheer fact of female
existence beneath its social roles. The poem's gender system is hierarchical but
unstable: women's formal subordination to male authority coexists with their
practical capacity to drive the entire narrative.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied was composed for a courtly audience around 1200, when the
_frouwe_ (lady) was the central figure of [[minne|Minne]]sang and courtly romance. In
that literature, the lady is an idealized object of male desire and service.

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied draws on this courtly discourse but also exceeds it: its
women are not only objects of male action but subjects who act, scheme, and
kill. The poem also preserves traces of an older Germanic tradition in which
women appear as inciters to vengeance (_Hetzerin_) — a role [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]
fulfills with devastating completeness.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem's gender economy is organized around visibility and valuation. Women
are constantly being looked at — "die Leute sahn ihn gerne; viel Fraun und
Mädchen schön" (002-026) — and their value is repeatedly asserted in terms of
beauty: "Der Jungfrau [[custom-and-tradition|höfsche Sitte]] wär eine Zier allen Fraun" (001-012).

But this valuation is a trap. To be seen and valued is to be made into an object
of exchange between men — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] is offered to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] as a reward for
his service to [[gunther|Gunther]]. The poem's tragedy unfolds when its women refuse to
remain objects: [[brunhild|Brünhild]] demands to know why her husband's vassal is treated as
an equal, and [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] transforms her grief into a campaign of annihilation.

The poem registers this refusal as both monstrous ("Wie könnt ein Weib aus [[revenge|Rache]]
wohl entsetzlicher tun?" (031-288)) and inevitable — the logical consequence of
a system that values women without granting them power.

## Notable Passages

> Es wuchs in Burgunden solch edel Mägdelein,
> Dass in allen Landen nichts Schönres mochte sein.
> — The poem's opening description of Kriemhild — woman as supreme object of value (001-005–006)

> Der Jungfrau höfsche Sitte wär eine Zier allen Fraun.
> — The courtly ideal of femininity: a maiden's breeding is an ornament to all women (001-012)

> Um die viel Degen mussten verlieren Leben und Leib.
> — The men who will die for this woman — the poem's gender contract stated in its first stanza (001-007)

> Wie könnt ein Weib aus Rache wohl entsetzlicher tun?
> — The narrator's final judgment on Kriemhild — female vengeance as the limit of the thinkable (031-288)

## Comparative Notes

- The Old Norse _Guðrúnarkviða_ presents a less complex heroine — [[kriemhild|Gudrun]]'s
  revenge (killing children, feeding them to Atli) is direct but lacks
  [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s ethical elaboration

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied shares with _Beowulf_ the figure of the mourning woman at
  the funeral (Wealhtheow / [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]) but gives her far greater narrative agency

- The poem's treatment of [[brunhild|Brünhild]]'s ambiguous gender status (warrior-queen who
  loses her strength when she loses her virginity) is unique in medieval
  literature and has attracted extensive feminist critical attention
