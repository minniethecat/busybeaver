# Lamentation and Mourning

Lamentation and mourning (MHG _klage_, _weinen_) constitute the dominant emotional
register of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied. From the opening stanza's coupling of "Weinen und
Klagen" with "Freud und Festlichkeiten" (001-003) to the final cataclysm, the
public and private expression of grief is the poem's most frequently staged
affective ritual.

## Definition

In the Middle High German courtly context, _klage_ (lament) is both a private
emotional response and a public performative act. Unlike modern private grief,
medieval lamentation follows ritualized forms: vocal wailing, tearing of
garments, swooning, and collective keening by women. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
distinguishes between spontaneous weeping (_weinen_) and structured lament
(_klage_), though the two frequently co-occur in climactic passages.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Lamentation in medieval Germanic epic serves multiple functions: it honors the
dead, expresses the social rupture caused by loss, and — crucially in the
[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied — foreshadows and motivates revenge. The _[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]klage_, a
separate companion poem composed shortly after the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, extends the
lament into a full narrative, testifying to the centrality of the mourning
ritual in the reception of the epic.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Lamentation structures the poem's emotional arc. The opening advertises "Weinen
und Klagen" as a programmatic theme, and each major death — [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s, the
Burgundian knights', [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s, the final massacre — is marked by extended
lament passages. The transformation of lament into the engine of revenge
([[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s grief → the destruction of the Burgundians → [[etzel|Etzel]]'s grief over
his men) makes mourning not merely a response to death but its cause in the
poem's second half.

## Notable Passages

> Viel Wunderdinge melden die Mären alter Zeit,
> Von preiswerthen Helden, von großer Kühnheit,
> Von Freud und Festlichkeiten, von Weinen und von Klagen,
> Von kühner Recken Streiten mögt ihr nun Wunder hören sagen.
> — Opening stanza (001-001–004), establishing lament as programmatic theme

> Da war anders nichts als Weinen und Klagen.
> Es halfen ihr mit Klagen viel Frauen lobesam.
> — Siegfried's death scene (017-252, 017-262)

> Die ungebärdge Klage so von Weib als Mann,
> Der Wehruf der Klage; auch ihr schufs große Noth.
> — The hall-burning, universal lament (037-403, 037-407)

## Comparative Notes

- The _[[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]klage_ (c. 1200) is a separate Middle High German poem devoted
  entirely to the aftermath and mourning of the Burgundian fall — unique in
  medieval epic for making lament the subject of a whole narrative
- In the Old Norse _Völsunga saga_ and _Poetic Edda_, Guðrún's ([[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s)
  lament is more individually focused — the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied expands it into a
  collective courtly ritual
- The "Weinen und Klagen" pairing echoes the classical _planctus_ tradition of
  Latin liturgical drama
