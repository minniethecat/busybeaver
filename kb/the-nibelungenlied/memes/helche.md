# Helche

Helche (also Helke) was the first wife of King [[etzel|Etzel]] and queen of the Huns. She died before the main events of the *Nibelungenlied* begin, but her memory casts a long shadow over the second half of the epic.

## Role in the Narrative

Helche's death is the catalyst for Etzel's courtship of [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]. The narrative presents Helche as the paragon of queenly virtue — a standard against whom Kriemhild is measured.

The narrator states that Etzel sought a new wife "when Lady Helke died" (020-001). Throughout the courtship and marriage negotiations, Helche serves as an idealized model of feminine rulership. [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] invokes her memory when persuading Kriemhild, and the narrator observes:

> Helche could not have been more beautiful. (022-063)

When Kriemhild arrives at the Hun court, her conduct is compared to "how Helche once practiced" (023-009) courtly virtues.

## Legacy

Helche's reputation for courtly excellence, generosity, and virtue establishes a benchmark that the narrative uses to both elevate and pressure Kriemhild. The comparison works doubly: it legitimizes Kriemhild's new position at the Hun court while also creating an impossible standard to live up to. Helche's death thus functions as both a narrative convenience — clearing the way for Etzel's remarriage — and a thematic device: the shadow of the idealized first wife.
