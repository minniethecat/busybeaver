# Gunther

King Gunther is the ruler of Burgundy and the eldest of the three royal brothers who govern the kingdom from [[worms|Worms on the Rhine]] (001-013). A competent king but a weak man, Gunther is defined by his dependence on others — on [[siegfried|Siegfried]] to win [[brunhild|Brunhild]], on [[hagen|Hagen]] to eliminate threats. His passivity in the face of [[hagen|Hagen]]'s schemes makes him complicit in [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s murder and, ultimately, responsible for the destruction of his entire dynasty.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: King of Burgundy
- **Affiliation**: [[burgundian-court|Burgundian Court]] — co-ruler with his brothers [[gernot|Gernot]] and [[geiselher|Geiselher]]
- **Family**: [[ute|Ute]] (mother), [[gernot|Gernot]] (brother), [[geiselher|Geiselher]] (brother), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (sister), [[brunhild|Brunhild]] (wife)
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 01 — introduced as one of the three Burgundian kings (001-013)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: The Dependent King (Adventures 1–19)

Gunther's kingship is defined by delegation. He cannot defeat [[brunhild|Brunhild]]'s challenges himself — [[siegfried|Siegfried]], hidden under the [[tarnkappe|Tarnkappe]], performs the feats of strength while Gunther goes through the motions (Adventure 07). He cannot subdue [[brunhild|Brunhild]] in the bridal chamber — [[siegfried|Siegfried]] does it for him (Adventure 10). This pattern of dependence creates a fatal dynamic: Gunther owes his queen and his kingship to [[siegfried|Siegfried]], but this very debt makes [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s existence a threat.

When [[hagen|Hagen]] proposes murdering [[siegfried|Siegfried]] after the queens' quarrel, Gunther hesitates — but he does not forbid it. His complicity through inaction is the moral nadir of his character. He allows [[siegfried|Siegfried]] to be killed, accepts the Nibelung treasure into his land (019-109), and protects [[hagen|Hagen]] from [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s demands for justice.

### Part II: The Doomed King (Adventures 20–39)

In Part II, Gunther is a king marching toward his death. He accepts [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s invitation to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court against [[hagen|Hagen]]'s warnings. At [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, he fights with dignity but without the ability to alter events. [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] has him killed before [[hagen|Hagen]] — "her brother, King Gunther, was led to his death" (Adventure 39) — wanting [[hagen|Hagen]] to witness his lord's death before his own.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Gunther is motivated by the preservation of his kingship and the [[ere|Êre]] of the Burgundian court. Every decision — accepting [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s help, allowing [[hagen|Hagen]]'s murder plot, protecting [[hagen|Hagen]] afterward — serves this goal. The irony is that each decision, while preserving [[ere|Êre]] in the short term, destroys it in the long term. Gunther's tragedy is that of the pragmatic politician whose pragmatism proves catastrophic.

### Virtues & Flaws

Gunther is not wicked — he is weak. His hospitality to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is genuine; his hesitation about the murder is real. But weakness in a king is a form of moral failure. Gunther knows what is right (protect [[siegfried|Siegfried]], deliver justice to [[hagen|Hagen]]) and does what is expedient (let [[hagen|Hagen]] act, protect the murderer). The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied suggests that a king who cannot say no to his vassals is no king at all.

## Notable Passages

> "When they won the hoard in King Gunther's land — the Nibelung treasure filled his halls." (019-109)

## Death

Gunther is killed at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court on [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s explicit orders (Adventure 39). Unlike [[hagen|Hagen]], who dies defying [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], Gunther dies as a prisoner — his kingship stripped away, his fate determined by the sister he betrayed. His death before [[hagen|Hagen]] is a calculated cruelty: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] wants [[hagen|Hagen]] to watch his lord die before meeting his own end.

## Scholarly Notes

- Gunther corresponds to the historical Gundaharius, king of the Burgundians, who died in 437 CE when the Huns destroyed the Burgundian kingdom on the [[rhine|Rhine]] — the historical kernel of the Nibelung legend.
- In the Old Norse tradition, Gunnar (Gunther) dies in a snake pit, a more heroic death than the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's ignominious execution.
- Gunther's passivity has been read as a critique of the Hohenstaufen monarchy's dependence on powerful ministeriales (unfree knights serving as royal officials) — [[hagen|Hagen]] represents the ministerialis who wields more real power than his royal master.
