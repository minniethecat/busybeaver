# Horses and Mounts in the Nibelungenlied

Horses are ubiquitous in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied, appearing in virtually every
adventure as instruments of travel, objects of gift-exchange, markers of
status, and vehicles of martial action. The poem's world is one in which the
horse is the primary technology of mobility and warfare: journeys are
undertaken on horseback, tournaments are fought from the saddle, and the
ceremonial gift of horses — "Ross und [[clothing-and-textiles|Gewand]]" (horse and clothing) — is a
recurring formula of lordly generosity (002-040, 005-004). The horse is not
merely a background detail; it is a charged symbol through which the poem
articulates wealth, power, and the aristocratic ideal of the mounted warrior.

## Definition

The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied uses two principal terms for the horse: _Ross_ (cognate
with English "steed," the noble warhorse or riding horse) and _Pferd_ (the
generic term, from Latin _paraverēdus_, a post-horse). _Ross_ is by far the
more frequent in the poem's heroic register, appearing in contexts of gift-giving,
ceremonial display, and combat. The _Pferd_ appears in more utilitarian
contexts — horses being led, watered, or rested — though the distinction is not
absolute (003-128, 003-131). The compound _Pferdgeräthe_ (horse-gear, harness)
appears in Adventure 9, marking the high craftsmanship devoted to equestrian
equipment (009-164).

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

In twelfth- and thirteenth-century aristocratic culture, the horse was the most
valuable movable possession after armor. The _destrier_ (warhorse) could cost
more than a knight's annual income. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied reflects this economic
reality in its repeated coupling of "Ross und [[clothing-and-textiles|Gewand]]" as the substance of
lordly gift-giving. The gift of a horse established or renewed a bond of
loyalty; the quality of one's horse was a direct index of one's status. The
poem's attention to horse color (snow-white, 007-042), tack (golden-red,
003-097), and gear (ringing bridles, 021-067) reflects a culture in which
equestrian display was the primary medium of aristocratic self-presentation.

### In the Nibelungenlied

Horses in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied trace the poem's arc from celebration to
catastrophe. In the early adventures, horses appear in contexts of gift-giving
and festivity: [[siegmund|Siegmund]]'s generosity at [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s sword-taking (002-040),
the splendor of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s arrival at [[worms|Worms]] (003-097), the tournament at the
wedding feast (010-069). As the poem darkens, the horse becomes an instrument
of fateful journeys: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s hunting expedition (Adventure 16), the
Burgundians' journey to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court (Adventure 25–27). The final adventures
are notably horse-poor — the hall-battle is fought on foot, in enclosed space,
and the mobility that horses once enabled has been replaced by entrapment and
immobility. The Burgundians who once rode in splendor to [[worms|Worms]] die on foot in a
burning hall.

## Notable Passages

> Heimischen und Gästen gab er Ross und Gewand.
> — Siegmund's gift-giving at Siegfried's sword-taking: the formula "Ross und Gewand" recurs throughout the poem as the archetypal expression of lordly generosity (002-040)

> Schön waren ihre Rosse, ihr Reitzeug goldesroth.
> — Siegfried's horses and their golden-red tack announce his exceptional status before he enters Worms (003-097)

> Dem Stich gehorsam trugen die Rosse pfeilgeschwind.
> — The warhorses, trained to the thrust of the lance, carry their riders arrow-swift in the Saxon battle (004-185)

> Von schneeblanker Farbe das Ross und auch das Kleid.
> — Siegfried's horse and clothing match in snow-white — a unified image of heroic radiance (007-042)

> Nun waren auch die Gäste zu Ross geseßen all.
> — The tournament begins: all the guests are mounted, and the _buhurt_ commences (010-069)

> Mit klingenden Zäumen viel Pferde wohlgethan.
> — Kriemhild's bridal departure: the ringing of bridles marks the ceremonial splendor and the irreversible journey eastward (021-067)

## Comparative Notes

- The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's treatment of horses as gifts and status markers parallels
  the role of horses in the _Chanson de Roland_ (where Charlemagne's horse
  Tencendur is named and celebrated) and in Norse sagas (where horses like
  Grani in the _Völsunga saga_ carry heroic and mythical significance)
- Unlike the Arthurian romance, where individual horses are sometimes named
  (Gringolet, Bayard), the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied does not name individual horses — they
  are valued as a class of possession rather than as individual companions
- The formula "Ross und [[clothing-and-textiles|Gewand]]" (horse and clothing) is specific to the MHG
  heroic and courtly tradition — it appears frequently in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied and
  related _[[entertainment-and-festivity|Spiel]]mannsepen_ as a shorthand for aristocratic gift-exchange
- The poem's attention to horse-gear (_Pferdgeräthe_, _Reitzeug_, _Zaum_)
  reflects the material culture of the Staufer period, in which elaborate
  equestrian equipment was a major form of aristocratic expenditure and display
