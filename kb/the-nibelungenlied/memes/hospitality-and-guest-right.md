# Hospitality and Guest-Right

Hospitality (_gastlichkeit_) and the rights of the guest (_gastrecht_) form the
moral architecture of the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's courtly world — and its most
devastating point of collapse. The poem traces an arc from the exemplary welcome
of [[siegfried|Siegfried]] at [[worms|Worms]] to the annihilation of the guest-host relationship at
[[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, where the feast hall becomes a battlefield and the host becomes
an accomplice to his guests' slaughter.

## Definition

In the medieval Germanic legal and ethical tradition, _gastrecht_ (guest-right)
is a binding obligation: the host must provide protection, shelter, and
sustenance; the guest must respect the host's authority and household. Violation
of guest-right is one of the gravest crimes in the heroic code, ranking with
kinslaying and oath-breaking. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied tests this obligation to its
breaking point, staging scenes where characters must choose between hospitality
and other competing duties — revenge, loyalty, self-preservation.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

_Gastrecht_ in the Germanic tradition is intertwined with the sanctity of the
household and the honor of the host. The _Germania_ of Tacitus (1st century CE)
already records the sacred status of the guest among Germanic tribes. In the
High Middle Ages, hospitality was codified in both secular law (the
_Sachsenspiegel_) and ecclesiastical teaching. The guest-host relationship was
understood as a covenant with quasi-religious force.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem systematically dismantles the institution of hospitality. The early
adventures celebrate it as the medium of courtly encounter — [[siegfried|Siegfried]] is
welcomed, fêted, and integrated through guest-rituals. The middle adventures
complicate it: [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s hospitality at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court is political
recruitment dressed as welcome. The final adventures destroy it: the
Burgundians arrive as guests but arm themselves; [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], the hostess,
demands blood-price from her guests; [[etzel|Etzel]], the host, stands aside as his
guests are massacred. The poem's final scene — [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] beheaded by [[hildebrand|Hildebrand]]
in her own hall — completes the annihilation of the hospitality framework.

## Notable Passages

> Da sprach der Gast zum König: "Das soll euch unversagt sein."
> — Siegfried's first exchange with Gunther, the guest-host contract established (003-256)

> Da sprach der Wirth zum Gaste: "Recht, dass ihr mich mahnt."
> — Gunther to Siegfried, the ideal host-guest relationship (010-121)

> Zu dem Gastgebote: dazu bin ich gern bereit.
> — Hagen agrees to accompany Gunther to Etzel's court, knowing the danger (027-276)

> Eine üble Hochzeit ist es, zu der die Königin uns entboten hat.
> — The guest-host covenant exposed as a death sentence (036-160)

## Comparative Notes

- The violation of guest-right is a recurring motif in Germanic heroic poetry:
  the _Finnsburg Fragment_ (hall-fight between guests and hosts), the burning of
  Njal in _Njáls saga_ (Icelandic), and the death of the Burgundians in
  _Atlakviða_ all feature hospitality catastrophes
- The Greek _xenia_ (guest-friendship) tradition provides a parallel: the
  violation of hospitality in the _Odyssey_ (the suitors in Odysseus's hall)
  justifies annihilation, as in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
- The poem's hospitality arc influenced later medieval literature's treatment
  of the "hostile feast" topos (e.g., the Red [[wedding-and-marriage|Wedding]] in modern fantasy)
