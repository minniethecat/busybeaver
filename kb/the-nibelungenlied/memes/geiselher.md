# Geiselher

Geiselher (Giselher), the youngest of the three Burgundian kings, is introduced among his brothers at [[worms|Worms on the Rhine]] (001-015). Throughout the epic, he is consistently associated with youth and kindness — described repeatedly as "the child" (daz kint) even as he becomes a formidable warrior. His betrothal to [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s daughter and his genuine attempts at reconciliation make his death one of the epic's more poignant losses.

## Identity & Status

- **Title/Role**: Co-King of Burgundy — the youngest of the three royal brothers
- **Affiliation**: [[burgundian-court|Burgundian Court]] — joint ruler with [[gunther|Gunther]] and [[gernot|Gernot]]
- **Family**: [[ute|Ute]] (mother), [[gunther|Gunther]] (brother), [[gernot|Gernot]] (brother), [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] (sister); betrothed to [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s daughter
- **First Appearance**: Adventure 01 — "Geiselher the young, a chosen knight" (001-015)

## Role in the Narrative

### Part I: The Beloved Younger Brother (Adventures 1–19)

Geiselher's role in Part I is defined by his relationship with [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]. He is the brother she trusts most — when [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] resists going to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, it is Geiselher whose intercession carries the most weight. He offers to stay with her if she refuses to go, a gesture of genuine affection (018-023, 018-030). When she does leave, Geiselher gives her good escort (018-101). He is described as "true and good" (getreu und auch gut). His youth is emphasized throughout: he is "Geiselher das Kind" (Geiselher the child), a formula that marks him as the youngest and most innocent of the brothers.

### Part II: The Honorable Warrior (Adventures 20–39)

In Part II, Geiselher matures into a capable warrior. He fights alongside [[gernot|Gernot]] against [[iring|Iring]] of [[saxon-land|Denmark]] (035-069, 035-076) and attempts to reason with [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, appealing to their shared blood (036-085). His betrothal to [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s daughter creates one of the epic's most agonizing scenes: when [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] must choose between his oath to [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] and his friendship with the Burgundians, Geiselher appeals to him as a father-in-law (037-229). After [[gernot|Gernot]] and [[rudiger|Rüdiger]] kill each other, Geiselher is the brother who mourns most openly (037-363, 037-365). In Adventure 38, Geiselher fights [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] and is killed — described as struck through helmet and armor by [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]]'s blows (038-237, 038-241, 038-256).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Geiselher is driven by familial love and personal honor. He is the only Burgundian brother whose motivations are consistently personal rather than political — he loves his sister, trusts his brothers, and honors his betrothal. His youth makes him the most sympathetic of the three kings: he has not had time to accumulate the debts and compromises that define his elder brothers.

### Virtues & Flaws

Geiselher's defining virtue is [[triuwe|Triuwe]] in its most innocent form: he is loyal to family, loyal to his betrothed, loyal to his brothers. His flaw is the same as his virtue — loyalty without calculation. At [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court, this loyalty leaves him no exit. He cannot abandon his brothers, cannot deny his betrothal, and cannot escape the hall.

## Death

Geiselher dies in Adventure 38, killed by [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] of Bern in the battle at [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court. After seeing [[gernot|Gernot]]'s body, he fights with desperate courage. [[wolfhart|Wolfhart]] — one of Dietrich's warriors — strikes him down in close combat. His death marks the fall of the last Burgundian king, leaving only [[gunther|Gunther]] and [[hagen|Hagen]] to face [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]'s final judgment.

## Scholarly Notes

- Geiselher corresponds to the historical Gislaharius, a Burgundian prince of the 5th century, though the historical record is fragmentary.
- The repeated epithet "daz kint" (the child) has been read by scholars as an indication of his youth relative to his brothers, though by medieval standards he would have been of fighting age throughout the epic.
- Geiselher's betrothal to [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s daughter is unique to the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied tradition and absent from the Norse versions, suggesting it is a narrative invention of the German poet to heighten the tragedy of [[rudiger|Rüdiger]]'s dilemma.
