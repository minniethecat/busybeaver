# Cunning and Guile in the Nibelungenlied

_[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ (cunning, guile, craft) in the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied is the dark twin of
_[[boldness-and-daring|Kühnheit]]_. Where boldness confronts openly, cunning works in concealment.

The poem's narrative depends on acts of _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ at every turn: [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s
invisible intervention in [[gunther|Gunther]]'s wedding-night contest, [[hagen|Hagen]]'s extraction
of [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s vulnerable spot from [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]], the murder plot at the spring,
and the final manipulations that draw the Burgundians to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s court.

_[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ is the quality that makes the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied more than a simple tale
of heroic combat — it acknowledges that in a world of superhuman strength and
implacable hatred, intelligence and deceit are the most dangerous weapons.

## Definition

_[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ in Middle High German carries a semantic range from "wisdom" and "skill"
to "cunning" and "deception." The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied activates the full range.

[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ in wooing [[brunhild|Brunhild]] is presented as clever strategy for a
legitimate goal. [[hagen|Hagen]]'s _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ in extracting [[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s secret is presented
as treachery. The word _arge_ (malicious) modifies _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ in the poem's most
consequential moments (014-109, 028-242), marking where cunning becomes crime.

_[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ is thus a morally neutral instrument — its ethical valence depends
entirely on who wields it and to what end.

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

Cunning (_[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_) occupies an ambivalent position in medieval Germanic
literature. In heroic poetry, _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ is associated with the trickster figure
— Odin in Norse myth, the cunning dwarf in heroic legend — and is presented
as a complement to physical strength.

But _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ also carries connotations of treachery, especially in the courtly
context where open declaration is the aristocratic ideal. The [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied
exploits this ambivalence: the quality that enables heroic achievement also
enables murder, and the poem refuses to resolve the tension.

### In the Nibelungenlied

_[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ is the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's structural principle. The plot is a chain of
deceptions: [[siegfried|Siegfried]] deceives [[brunhild|Brunhild]], [[hagen|Hagen]] deceives [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] to learn
[[siegfried|Siegfried]]'s secret, [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]] deceives her brothers to lure them to [[etzel|Etzel]]'s
court.

Each deception generates the next. The poem's tragic momentum is inseparable
from its characters' willingness to pursue their ends through cunning rather
than open confrontation. The implicit argument: a world governed by _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_
cannot sustain itself — deceptions multiply until they consume the deceivers
along with the deceived.

## Notable Passages

> Er erwarb mit großen Listen diese herrliche Magd — Gunther won this
> magnificent maiden through great cunning. The line is brutally honest:
> Brunhild was not won by strength or merit but by invisible trickery.
> The poem never pretends otherwise. (006-076)

> Es war doch arge List — it was indeed malicious cunning. Kriemhild's
> accusation in the queens' quarrel transforms a strategic act into a
> moral crime. The line is the pivot on which the entire poem turns.
> (014-109)

> nicht Kunde von mancher argen List — no knowledge of many a malicious
> cunning. The warning acknowledges that _List_ has become the ambient
> condition: heroes who once wielded cunning now find themselves
> surrounded by it. (028-242)

## Comparative Notes

- The Old Norse _Völsunga saga_ places even greater emphasis on cunning —
  Sigurd's deceptions, Brynhild's manipulations, and [[kriemhild|Gudrun]]'s revenge are
  all framed as _vél_ (guile), closely parallel to _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_

- In the _Þiðreks saga_, [[hagen|Hagen]]'s cunning is more explicitly villainous —
  the [[nibelungen|Nibelungen]]lied's more ambivalent treatment of [[hagen|Hagen]]'s _[[betrayal-and-deception|List]]_ is one
  of its distinguishing features

- The classical parallel is Odysseus's _metis_ (cunning intelligence) —
  like [[siegfried|Siegfried]], a hero of both strength and guile, but unlike [[siegfried|Siegfried]],
  Odysseus's cunning saves rather than destroys him
