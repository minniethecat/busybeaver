## Introduction

**Dankrat** was the king of [[burgundian-court|Burgundy]] before the events of the Nibelungenlied. He is the husband of Queen [[ute|Ute]] and the father of the three Burgundian kings — [[gunther|Gunther]], [[gernot|Gernot]], and [[geiselher|Geiselher]] — as well as their sister [[kriemhild|Kriemhild]]. Dankrat is already deceased by the time the main narrative begins, and he appears only through memory and patronymic reference.

## Legacy

The poem introduces Dankrat in the opening adventure as a man of distinction: "Und Dankrat ihr Vater, der ihnen zum Gewinn / Das Erbe ließ im Tode, vordem ein starker Mann, / Der auch in seiner Jugend großer Ehren viel gewann" — he was a strong man who won great honor in his youth and left his inheritance to his children upon his death (001-026).

His reputation was such that Gunther is referred to by the patronymic "König Dankrats Sohn" (King Dankrat's son), indicating that Dankrat's name carried authority and recognition even after his passing (010-134).

After Dankrat's death, Queen Ute founded a wealthy princely abbey — the monastery at Lorsch — from their shared possessions, a foundation that the poet notes still stood with great revenues at the time of composition (019-182).

## Family

Dankrat's marriage to [[ute|Ute]] produced the royal lineage of Burgundy. His eldest son Gunther succeeded him as king, with Gernot and Geiselher as co-ruling brothers. His daughter Kriemhild became the central figure of the epic through her marriages to [[siegfried|Siegfried]] and later to King Etzel of the Huns.
