## Overview

King Etzel's court is the epic's second great political center. Where the Burgundian court embodies feudal order and courtly civilization, the Hun court represents a more ambiguous power — vast, cosmopolitan, and ultimately the instrument of Kriemhild's revenge. The court hosts exiles (Dietrich von Bern), incorporates foreign vassals (Rüdiger of Bechelaren), and draws the Burgundians eastward to their doom.

## Narrative Arc

The Hun Court progresses through three phases:

1. **Marriage and Alliance (Adventures 20–24)** : Etzel, widowed after Helche's death, seeks Kriemhild's hand. Rüdiger negotiates the marriage. Kriemhild departs Burgundy for Hunland, bringing her grievance with her (020-001).

2. **The Invitation (Adventures 24–28)** : After thirteen years, Kriemhild persuades Etzel to invite her brothers. The Burgundians journey east despite Hagen's warnings. The court's hospitality masks Kriemhild's design (024-001).

3. **Catastrophe (Adventures 29–39)** : The hall battle consumes everyone — Bloedel, Ortlieb, Iring, Rüdiger, the Dietrich heroes, and finally the Burgundian kings. Etzel survives, the last king standing in a court of corpses (038-066, 039-004).

## Thematic Significance

Etzel's court embodies the paradox of power in the Nibelungenlied. It is the most powerful political institution in the epic's world — Etzel rules from the Rhine to the East — yet the king is conspicuously powerless to prevent the disaster unfolding in his own hall. This powerlessness is a structural feature: the Hun court is a **heterogeneous polity** that incorporates exiles, foreigners, and converts, lacking the cohesive feudal bonds ([[triuwe]]) that should protect it. The court's cosmopolitanism becomes its vulnerability.
