# The Nibelungs in the Nibelungenlied

No term in the poem is more central or more elusive than "Nibelung." The word
appears in the poem's title, in the names of its characters (King Nibelung, his
sons Schilbung and Nibelung), in the designation of a land ("Nibelungenland"),
a people ("die Nibelungen"), and a treasure ("der Nibelungenhort"). By the
poem's final movement, the name has migrated from [[siegfried]]'s conquered
subjects to the Burgundians themselves, who are called "Nibelungen" as they
march toward their destruction at [[etzel]]'s court. This migration is not a
poetic inconsistency but the poem's central symbolic operation: to possess the
Nibelung treasure is to inherit the Nibelung identity, and with it the Nibelung
fate. The name "Nibelung" is, in the poem's logic, a contagion — it passes from
those who originally bore it to those who seize what was theirs, until the
distinction between conqueror and conquered collapses entirely.

## Definition

MHG _Nibelunc_ (proper noun, genitive _Nibelunges_, plural _Nibelunge_ or
_Nibelungen_) appears in the poem with at least four distinct referents: (1) a
**legendary king**, father of Schilbung and Nibelung, original possessor of the
treasure (003-175, 003-181); (2) a **people or dynasty**, the inhabitants of
Nibelungenland, ruled after the king's death by his sons and subsequently
conquered by Siegfried; (3) a **territory**, Nibelungenland, the subterranean
or otherwise remote realm where the treasure is kept; and (4) the
**Burgundians**, who after Siegfried's death and Hagen's appropriation of the
treasure are called "Nibelungen" by the Hunnish court. The name's etymology is
disputed: it may derive from a Germanic personal name (_Nibel-_) linked to
_Nebel_ (mist, fog), suggesting the Nibelungs as "sons of the mist" —
creatures of the underworld or the realm of the dead. This chthonic association
is consistent with the poem's treatment of the Nibelungs as guardians of a
treasure that brings death to all who possess it.

| [[hagen]] | The agent of the treasure's second transfer — by sinking the Nibelung treasure in the Rhine, Hagen attempts to prevent the Nibelung identity from passing to Kriemhild; but the attempt fails, as the Burgundians themselves become Nibelungen in the poem's second half |

## Thematic Significance

### Medieval Context

The figure of the Nibelungs draws on a deep stratum of Germanic legend. The Old
Norse _Völsunga saga_ and the _Poetic Edda_ preserve versions of the story in
which the Niflungar (the Old Norse cognate of Nibelungen) are a dynasty
associated with a cursed treasure — the _Andvaranaut_ ring and the hoard of the
dwarf Andvari. In these Norse sources, the treasure's curse is explicit: it
will bring death to every owner. The Nibelungenlied Christianizes and courtly-
fies this material: the curse is never stated in supernatural terms, but the
pattern is preserved — everyone who possesses the Nibelung treasure dies. The
poem's innovation is to make the name itself transferable: "Nibelung" is not
just a bloodline but a condition, the condition of possessing the treasure. This
is a profound meditation on the nature of inheritance: what you take from
others — their land, their gold, their name — takes you in return.

### In the Nibelungenlied

The poem traces the migration of the name "Nibelung" across three phases. In
the **first phase** (Adventures 1–3), the Nibelungs are Siegfried's distant
enemies — a legendary people whom he conquers in his youth, acquiring their
treasure and their land. They exist in the poem's deep past, reported rather
than shown. In the **second phase** (Adventures 4–19), the Nibelungs are
Siegfried's retainers — the thousand Nibelung knights who accompany the
treasure to Worms and serve at Siegfried's wedding. They are visible but
subordinate, their identity defined entirely by their relationship to Siegfried
and his treasure. In the **third phase** (Adventures 20–39), the name migrates
to the Burgundians: Kriemhild's Hunnish court calls the Burgundian visitors
"Nibelungen," and the designation sticks. This third migration is the poem's
darkest irony: the Burgundians, who thought they were acquiring a treasure,
have acquired an identity — and the identity is fatal. The poem's title, _Das
Nibelungenlied_ ("The Song of the Nibelungs"), is itself the final migration:
the name that once belonged to a legendary king has become the name of the poem
that tells of the destruction of an entire people.

## Notable Passages

> Die kühnen Nibelungen schlug des Helden Hand
> — "The bold Nibelungs the hero's hand struck down" — the poem's first
> mention of the Nibelungs; they are introduced as Siegfried's victims, and
> this originary violence defines their relationship (003-174)

> Der Hort König Nibelungs ward hervorgetragen
> — "King Nibelung's hoard was brought forth" — the treasure is identified
> by its original owner's name; possession of the hoard means carrying the
> name "Nibelung" with it (003-181)

> den Hort und Nibelungenland
> — "The hoard and Nibelungenland" — Siegfried's conquest is double: land
> and treasure are taken together, and the taking of one is inseparable from
> the taking of the other (003-204)

> Das Land der Nibelungen war Siegfried unterthan
> — "The land of the Nibelungs was subject to Siegfried" — the legal
> recognition of Siegfried's lordship; but the line occurs at the moment his
> death is being plotted, as if the Nibelung identity, once claimed, can only
> end in destruction (011-125)

> den erschlagen Siegfried von Nibelungenland
> — "The slain Siegfried from Nibelungenland" — in death, Siegfried is
> identified wholly with the Nibelung land; his conquest has become his
> identity, and his identity is now a dead one (017-007)

## Comparative Notes

- The Old Norse cognate _Niflungar_ in the _Völsunga saga_ and _Poetic Edda_
  designates a specific dynasty (the Gjukungs, Gunnar and Högni) associated
  with the cursed _Andvaranaut_ treasure — the Nibelungenlied expands the
  term's reference to include the original treasure-owners, Siegfried, and
  ultimately the Burgundians, making "Nibelung" a portable rather than a
  fixed identity
- The poem's transferability of the name "Nibelung" is unique among medieval
  Germanic sources — it suggests a sophisticated reflection on the nature of
  inheritance and identity that has no parallel in the Norse material
- The Nibelungenlied's treatment of the treasure as a vehicle of identity
  transfer — to possess it is to become what its previous possessor was —
  anticipates modern anthropological theories of the gift and the
  inalienability of certain objects (Mauss, Weiner)
