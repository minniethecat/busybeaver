# "I was a woman of no importance"

> I was a woman of no importance.
> (004-089)

## Context

[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] speaks this line to [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] near the end of Act IV, after refusing his [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] proposal. The full exchange is brief but devastating: she has just declared that she will not stand before God's altar and ask His blessing on "so hideous a mockery as a [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]]" between them (004-082). Illingworth, disarmed, has nothing left to offer. Her quiet statement — "I was a woman of no importance" — is the play's final word on its title.

The line is spoken at [[wrockley]], [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s modest home. She is not at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], not in his world, not on his terms. The [[the-english-country-house|setting]] reinforces the line's meaning: she is speaking from a place where his judgments carry no weight.

## Analysis

### The Power of Past Tense

The line's force lies entirely in its past tense. "I was" — not "I am." The woman who was dismissed as having no importance has become, through twenty years of maternal devotion and one act of moral refusal, the most important person in the drama. The past tense does not merely describe a former condition; it announces its overcoming.

### Restraint as Rhetoric

[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] could rage, could accuse, could deliver a speech cataloguing twenty years of suffering. She does none of these things. Instead she offers a simple declarative sentence, nine words long, that carries more moral weight than any of [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s polished [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s. The line is Wilde's demonstration that true rhetorical power does not require [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] — it requires truth. Illingworth's [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s entertain; [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s plain statement judges.

### The Title Reclaimed

The play's title is a phrase of dismissal: to be "a [[the-woman-of-no-importance|woman of no importance]]" is to be socially invisible, beneath notice. By placing this phrase in [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s mouth — and in [[secrecy-and-the-past|the past]] tense — Wilde performs an act of reappropriation. She takes the label that was used to erase her and makes it the instrument of her moral victory. The "[[the-woman-of-no-importance|woman of no importance]]" is the only person in the play whose importance endures.
