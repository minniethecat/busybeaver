# "The book of life begins with a man and a woman in a garden"

> The book of life begins with a man and a woman in a garden. It ends with Revelations.
> (002-036)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] offers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act II at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in the same exchange [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]] that produces her line about the ideal man. The remark follows [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]'s romantic musings about love and [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]]. [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]'s response recasts the entire human story — from Genesis to Revelation — as the arc of a single relationship, collapsing theology into domestic comedy.

## Analysis

The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] works on multiple levels. At the surface, it is a [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ty compression of the Biblical narrative into the story of a [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]]: Eden is the honeymoon; Revelation is the divorce. But the deeper implication is Wildean: the "book of life" that begins [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h romantic promise ends in apocalyptic dissolution — not because of cosmic forces, but because of the inherent nature of the relationship between men and women. The line is [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]'s most philosophically ambitious [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]], and it encapsulates the play's dark view of love far more economically than [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s more famous "public building" [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]].
