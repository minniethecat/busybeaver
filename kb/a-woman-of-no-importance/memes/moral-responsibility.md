# Moral Responsibility

## Overview

*A Woman of No Importance* is fundamentally a play about moral responsibility: who bears it, who evades it, and what happens when the two collide. Lord Illingworth has spent twenty years avoiding any responsibility for the woman he seduced and the child he fathered. Mrs. Arbuthnot has spent those same twenty years bearing sole responsibility for a situation she did not create alone. The play's dramatic arc forces a reckoning between these two positions — and, in doing so, asks whether a society that systematically exempts men from moral accountability can call itself just.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian moral discourse placed great emphasis on personal responsibility — the idea that individuals were accountable for their actions and their consequences. But this abstract principle was applied with radical asymmetry. Men of the upper classes, especially, operated in a social world where the consequences of their sexual behaviour could be managed, concealed, or simply ignored. Women had no such protection.

## Manifestation in the Play

Lord Illingworth's evasion of responsibility is the play's central moral fact. He has never acknowledged his son, never provided for the woman he ruined, never even — as far as the audience can tell — thought about her until circumstance forced her back into his awareness. His proposal of marriage in Act IV is not an acceptance of responsibility but a further evasion — an attempt to manage the situation on terms that cost him nothing.

Mrs. Arbuthnot's acceptance of responsibility, by contrast, has been total. She has raised Gerald alone, maintained a respectable front, and protected her son from the knowledge of his origins — all while carrying the social stigma that should properly have been shared. Her climactic refusal of Illingworth's proposal is both a rejection of his evasion and an assertion of a different kind of responsibility: the responsibility to oneself.
