# "It is perfectly monstrous the way people go about nowadays saying things against one behind one's back"

> It is perfectly monstrous the way people go about nowadays saying things against one behind one's back that are absolutely and entirely true.
> (001-031)

## Context

[[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline Pontefract]] delivers this line in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in conversation [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]. The remark is a classic example of Wilde's technique of the unconscious self-revelation — the speaker thinks she is complaining about gossip, but the qualifying clause ("that are absolutely and entirely true") reveals that her real objection is not to the act of gossiping but to the accuracy of what is said. It is the comedy of a character who cannot help but tell the truth about herself even while trying to maintain a moral posture.

## Analysis

The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]'s structure — a long, indignant build-up followed by a deflating qualification — is a Wilde signature. [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] believes she is defending propriety; she is actually exposing her own narcissism. The line encapsulates the play's larger critique of Victorian [[victorian-morality|morality]]: the people who profess the highest standards are often the most transparently self-interested. [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] is a minor character, but this line gives her a moment of pure comic illumination that says as much about the play's world as any of [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s more famous [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s.
