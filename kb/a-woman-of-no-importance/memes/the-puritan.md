# The Puritan

## Overview

The word "Puritan" appears throughout *A Woman of No Importance* as a term of both mockery and reluctant respect. For [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] and [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]], the Puritan represents everything their dandy sensibility rejects: earnestness, moral seriousness, the refusal to treat life as an aesthetic game. But Wilde gives the Puritan figure a dramatic weight that complicates any simple reading — [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]], the "Puritan in white muslin," ultimately speaks the play's most morally authoritative lines.

## Context in Victorian Society

The historical Puritans of the seventeenth century were remembered in Victorian England — when they were remembered at all — as killjoys who had closed the theatres and suppressed pleasure. By Wilde's time, "Puritanism" had become a byword for a particular kind of humourless moralism, especially in matters of sex and art. Yet the Puritan tradition also represented a genuine moral seriousness, a refusal to accept the easy compromises of worldly society. This duality — the Puritan as both comic figure and moral conscience — is what Wilde exploits in the character of Hester.

## Manifestation in the Play

The word "Puritan" is first introduced by [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] as a dismissal of Hester: "She is a Puritan besides" (001-214). Lord Illingworth takes up the term with characteristic wit: "I don't mind plain women being Puritans. It is the only excuse they have for being plain. But she is decidedly pretty" (001-215). This sets the pattern for much of the play's treatment of Puritanism as a topic for witty deflation.

But Wilde complicates this treatment. When asked by [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]] about "the punishment for the Puritan," Lord Illingworth replies that "there is no punishment for her. She will never get old. She will always be young enough to do mischief" (001-228). This is the crucial turn — the dandy's mockery of the Puritan is also an acknowledgment of her power.

The conversation about whether "Puritan women" exist — "I don't believe in the existence of Puritan women" (001-225) — establishes the play's treatment of the concept as a site of ideological contest. Hester herself later delivers a passionate defense of American Puritan values that is both moving and, in Wilde's characteristic manner, undercut by its own earnestness.

## Key Characters

- **[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]**: The "Puritan in white muslin" — her moral seriousness is both her strength and her limitation.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The dandy who mocks Puritanism while being unable to ignore its moral claims.
- **[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]**: The female dandy whose dismissal of Puritanism masks an uncomfortable recognition of its force.
