# "No woman should have a memory. A woman with a memory is a woman with a past."

> No woman should have a memory. A woman with a memory is a woman with a past.
> (002-030)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers this epigram in the Act II drawing-room conversation at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]]. The line is part of her extended verbal duel with [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]], in which the two dandies compete to produce the most outrageous and penetrating observations on gender relations.

## Analysis

This is one of the play's most quietly devastating lines. In a society where a woman's "past" — meaning her sexual history — is the single most destructive thing she can possess, the injunction to have no memory is both a piece of cynical advice and a bitter social commentary. Mrs. Allonby appears to be recommending a kind of strategic amnesia, but what she is really doing is exposing the impossible position of women in a world that punishes them for remembering what men are free to forget.

The line resonates directly with [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s situation: she has a memory, and therefore a past, and that past has defined her life in ways that the man responsible for it — [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] — has never had to confront.
