# Redemption

Redemption — the possibility of being saved from the consequences of one's past — is the theological question at the heart of *A Woman of No Importance*. The play asks not simply whether [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] can be redeemed for his treatment of [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], but more radically: who needs redemption, and what form can it take?

## Illingworth's Unredeemed State

The play's most obvious candidate for redemption is Illingworth, who has lived twenty years without acknowledging or repenting the harm he caused. His offer of marriage in Act IV — "to make the repair which every chivalrous man would make" (004-187) — is framed as an act of redemption, but Mrs. Arbuthnot's refusal exposes it as a transaction. He wants to "repair" the damage as one might repair a broken piece of furniture — quickly, conveniently, and without genuine moral cost. The play's judgment is that such redemption is not redemption at all; it is merely the purchase of a clear conscience.

## Mrs. Arbuthnot's Self-Redemption

The play's most radical theological move is to locate redemption not in the male offer of "repair" but in Mrs. Arbuthnot's self-possession. She has already redeemed herself — through twenty years of [[sacrifice]], through the creation of a life of dignity at [[wrockley|Wrockley]], through her refusal to let her past define her present. She does not need Illingworth's marriage; she does not need anyone's forgiveness. As she tells [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]: "I have no life outside of you" (004-139) — a statement that is both a confession of dependency and an assertion of moral purpose.

## Hester's Recognition

[[hester-worsley|Hester]]'s role in the redemption economy of the play is to recognize what is already true. When she kisses Mrs. Arbuthnot and says "You are the sweetest woman that ever lived" (004-204), she is not redeeming Mrs. Arbuthnot — she is acknowledging a redemption that has already occurred. This is a distinctively Protestant model of redemption: it is not conferred by an external authority (a priest, a husband, a social institution) but recognized by a community of witnesses. Hester, the [[puritanism-and-the-american-critique|Puritan]], becomes the witness who validates what Mrs. Arbuthnot has achieved alone.
