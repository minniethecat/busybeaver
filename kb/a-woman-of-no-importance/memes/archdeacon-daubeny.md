# Archdeacon Daubeny

## Character Overview

The Venerable Archdeacon Daubeny is the Church of England's representative at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]] — a Doctor of Divinity at Oxford (P03-005) and a benign, elderly clergyman whose presence sanctifies the social order [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]hout ever questioning it. His title carries intellectual prestige which, in Wilde's treatment, seems to have left him [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h little to say about the moral crises unfolding around him.

## Role in the Play

Daubeny appears briefly in Acts I and II. In Act I, he arrives late to dinner and declares himself thoroughly entertained by [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s company — "I have never enjoyed myself more" (002-048) — a remark that reveals the Church's comfortable accommodation [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h the world it is meant to judge.

He is a figure of the Establishment — respected, comfortable, and entirely disconnected from the real moral questions the play raises. His silence on matters of justice, forgiveness, and the treatment of women is itself a statement: the Church, in Wilde's portrait, has nothing to say about the [[victorian-morality|double standard]].

## Relationships

- **[[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]**: His hostess — she values his presence as a social ornament.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: A fellow guest — Daubeny neither challenges nor condemns him.

## Character Analysis

The Archdeacon is Wilde's portrait of institutional religion at its most harmless and its most useless. His theology has been reduced to good manners; his moral authority has evaporated into pleasant conversation. In a play about sin, judgment, and forgiveness, the Church's representative has nothing to contribute — and that silence is Wilde's most damning critique of the religious establishment.
