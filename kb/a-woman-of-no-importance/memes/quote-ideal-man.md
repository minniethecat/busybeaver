# "The ideal man should talk to us as if we were goddesses"

> The ideal man should talk to us as if we were goddesses, and treat us as if we were children.
> (002-034)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] during the Act II drawing-room conversation at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], responding to [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]'s sentimental views on love. It is a characteristic [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] line — worldly, self-aware, and layered [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h irony. Where [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]] deals in romantic cliché, [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] offers a knowing dissection of romantic convention that neither endorses nor condemns it, but simply exposes its contradictions.

## Analysis

The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]'s structure — two parallel clauses [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h a sharp reversal — is a Wilde signature. The first half ("talk to us as if we were goddesses") flatters female vanity; the second ("treat us as if we were children") undercuts it by suggesting that women are simultaneously elevated and patronized in [[marriage-and-courtship|courtship]]. The line is not so much a prescription as a diagnosis: it describes what the ideal man *should* do in order to navigate the contradictory demands that Victorian society placed on women.
