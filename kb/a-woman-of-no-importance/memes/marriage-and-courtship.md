# Marriage and Courtship

## Overview

Marriage in *A Woman of No Importance* is never what Victorian convention would have it be — a sacred union, a happy ending. Instead, Wilde treats marriage as an object of relentless satire, exposing it as a marketplace of social ambition, a trap for women, and, in the play's climactic proposal, "a hideous... mockery" (004-082). The traditional marriage plot, the stock-in-trade of Victorian drama, is invoked only to be refused.

## Context in Victorian Society

For Victorian women of the upper classes, marriage was the only respectable career. A woman's social existence depended on her marital status: she was a daughter, a wife, or a failure. Wilde, whose own marriage was unconventional and whose sexual life existed outside it, was acutely aware of marriage as performance. The play's drawing-room [[the-english-country-house|setting]] is itself a marriage market — a place where eligible young people are displayed and transactions negotiated under the guise of social visits.

## Manifestation in the Play

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] opens the play's treatment of marriage [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h characteristic cynicism: "So much marriage is certainly not becoming. [[quote-marriage-ruin|Twenty years of romance make a woman look like a ruin]]; but twenty years of marriage make her look like a public building" (001-186). The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] reduces marriage to architectural decay — romance beautifies, but marriage institutionalises and uglifies.

In Act II, the ladies discuss the unhappiness of modern marriages. [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] observes that "More marriages are ruined nowadays by the common sense of the husband than by anything else" (002-058). The conversation treats marital misery as a social given, a subject for [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] rather than reform.

The play's central marriage plot involves [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s proposal to [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] — not out of love but as a belated attempt to legitimise [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]. [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]], eager for social advancement, presses for the marriage: "This marriage, this necessary marriage, this marriage which is obviously the only possible solution" (004-066). But [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] refuses: "I will not stand before God's altar and ask God's blessing on so hideous a mockery as a marriage between me and [[lord-illingworth|George Harford]]" (004-082). Her refusal is the play's moral climax — a rejection of marriage as social transaction in favor of moral integrity.

## Key Characters

- **[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]**: Refuses marriage as a "mockery," choosing moral truth over social respectability.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: Views marriage as a solution to a social inconvenience, not a moral commitment.
- **[[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]]**: Pushes for the marriage that would give him legitimacy, unaware of its cost to his [[motherhood|mother]].
- **[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]**: The play's sharpest satirist of marriage, delivering Wilde's most biting [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s on the subject.
