# The Double Standard

The sexual double standard — the principle that identical behavior is judged differently depending on the gender of the actor — is the moral engine of Oscar Wilde's *A Woman of No Importance*. The entire plot turns on the asymmetry between [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s social position and [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s: he committed the same act she did, yet he is a peer of the realm while she is a social exile.

## The Asymmetry of Consequence

The double standard is established in the play's backstory and drives its present action. Twenty years before the play begins, Lord Illingworth (then George Harford) seduced and abandoned Rachel Arbuthnot, leaving her pregnant with [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]. He went on to a career of diplomatic distinction and social success. She went into hiding, raising their son alone under a false name, her entire identity consumed by the secret of her "sin" (004-163).

When Illingworth offers to marry Mrs. Arbuthnot in Act IV — "to make the repair which every chivalrous man would make" (004-187) — the offer reveals the double standard's deepest structure. Marriage is supposed to "repair" the woman's shame, but it leaves the man's twenty years of freedom untouched. Mrs. Arbuthnot's refusal — "I will not marry you" (004-195) — is the play's most radical gesture: she rejects the very framework that defines her as damaged goods in need of male reparation.

## Wilde's Critique

Wilde's critique of the double standard operates at multiple levels. At the level of plot, the double standard is the source of [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s suffering and [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s impunity. At the level of dialogue, the male characters consistently articulate the double standard as natural — Illingworth speaks of women as "a fascinatingly wilful sex" (004-168) whose transgressions are interesting but whose reputations are fragile. At the level of genre, Wilde inverts the standard "fallen woman" melodrama by having the fallen woman *refuse* the redemption offered by the man who wronged her, asserting her own moral authority over his.

The play's title itself is a reference to the double standard: a "woman of no importance" is precisely a woman whose importance has been erased by a society that measures female worth by sexual reputation. Mrs. Arbuthnot's final assertion — that she is a "woman of no importance" to the world but of supreme importance to herself — reclaims the term as a badge of independence.

## Dramatic Irony and the Next Generation

The double standard also threatens to reproduce itself across generations. [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]'s initial willingness to forgive his father while judging his mother — "My mother was a woman of no importance, you know" (004-145) — shows how deeply the double standard is internalized even by those it harms. Gerald's eventual choice to stand with his mother rather than accept his father's patronage represents a break in the cycle, though Wilde leaves it ambiguous whether Gerald's moral awakening will survive contact with the social world that produced his father.
