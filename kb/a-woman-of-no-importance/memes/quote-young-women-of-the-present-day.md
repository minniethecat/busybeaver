# "Young women of the present day make it the sole object of their lives to be always playing with fire."

> As far as I can make out, the young women of the present day seem to make it the sole object of their lives to be always playing with fire.
> (001-108)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this observation in Act I on the [[hunstanton-terrace|terrace]] at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]]. The conversation has turned to the behaviour of modern young women, a topic on which Illingworth — as on most topics — holds decided and epigrammatic views.

## Analysis

The image of "playing with fire" carries obvious sexual connotations: the modern young woman is risking her reputation, her future, her very self in pursuit of dangerous pleasures. But Illingworth's tone is not condemnatory — it is admiring, even envious. The dandy, who has spent his own life playing with fire and escaping the consequences, recognises a kindred spirit.

The line also anticipates the play's treatment of [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]], who is in some ways the opposite of the young woman Illingworth describes — earnest, moral, seemingly indifferent to the game of sexual attraction — and yet who becomes, in Act III, the object of Illingworth's own predatory attention. The fire, it turns out, is lit by the men who claim to be merely observing it.
