# "The one advantage of playing with fire is that one never gets even singed."

> The one advantage of playing with fire, Lady Caroline, is that one never gets even singed. It is the people who don't know how to play with it who get burned up.
> (001-061)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in response to [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]]'s lament that young women "seem to make it the sole object of their lives to be always playing with fire" (001-060). The exchange occurs during the opening garden-party conversation, setting up the play's central concerns with [[victorian-morality|morality]], reputation, and the gendered [[the-double-standard|double standard]].

## Analysis

Mrs. Allonby's retort is a classic Wildean inversion: the moralist's warning against "playing with fire" is reversed into a celebration of skill. The epigram functions on two levels. On the surface, it is a defense of [[dandyism|dandyism]] — the art of navigating society's dangers with elegance and impunity. On a deeper level, it exposes the [[class-and-society|class]] and gender asymmetry of Victorian moral judgment: those who "don't know how to play" — the inexperienced, the unprotected, the socially vulnerable — are the ones who suffer, while the sophisticated escape unscathed. The line anticipates the play's central tragedy: [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] was one who "got burned up."
