# Mrs. Allonby

## Character Overview

Mrs. Allonby is the play's most articulate female [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] — the counterpart to [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] in the battle of the sexes that runs through the drawing-room scenes. Her observations on [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]], men, and society are delivered [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h a worldly cynicism that is both entertaining and revealing. She is Wilde's vehicle for some of the play's sharpest social criticism, delivered in the form of comedy.

## Role in the Play

Mrs. Allonby dominates the after-dinner conversation in Act II, where the ladies discuss the shortcomings of men. Her observations are both [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ty and subversive: "More [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]]s are ruined nowadays by the common sense of the husband than by anything else" (002-058). The line gets a laugh, but it names a genuine social phenomenon — the husband's dull practicality crushing the wife's spirit.

She also delivers one of the play's most quoted lines about men and forgiveness: "Rarely, if ever, do they forgive them" (002-225). Unlike [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s, which deflect moral scrutiny, Mrs. Allonby's [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] often reveals uncomfortable truths.

## Relationships

- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: Her conversational equal — their verbal sparring is the play's most entertaining dialogue.
- **[[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]**: Her hostess — Mrs. Allonby performs her [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] for the company but seems privately weary of it.
