# The Ideal Man

## Overview

The "Ideal Man" conversation in Act II of *A Woman of No Importance* is one of Wilde's most sustained and brilliant comic set-pieces. Prompted by the earnest [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]], [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers an extended description of the Ideal Man that is simultaneously a satire of romantic fantasy, a coded portrait of [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]], and a serious commentary on the asymmetries of gender relations in Victorian society.

## Context in Victorian Society

The Victorian period produced a vast literature of idealisation: conduct books describing the ideal woman, sermons describing the ideal Christian, novels describing the ideal marriage. These ideals were almost invariably prescriptive — they told women, especially, what they should aspire to be and to want. Wilde's innovation is to turn the machinery of idealisation back on men, and to do so through a female voice that is witty, self-aware, and not at all naive about the transaction being described.

## Manifestation in the Play

When [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]] asks, with characteristic earnestness, for [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]'s "conception of the Ideal Husband," Mrs. Allonby's reply is immediate and devastating: "The Ideal Husband? There couldn't be such a thing. The institution is wrong" (002-062). But she is happy to describe "The Ideal Man, then, in his relations to us" (002-063).

What follows is a virtuosic passage of Wildean wit that functions on multiple levels. The Ideal Man, Mrs. Allonby explains, "should talk to us as if we were goddesses, and treat us as if we were children. He should refuse all our serious requests, and gratify every one of our whims. He should encourage us to have caprices, and forbid us to have missions. He should always say much more than he means, and always mean much more than he says" (002-065).

This is, on one level, a satirical description of the dandy as romantic partner — a figure who is charming, attentive, and utterly unserious. But it is also a knowing exposure of what women in Victorian society were taught to want: not equality but worship, not partnership but performance.

## Key Characters

- **[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]**: The creator of the Ideal Man portrait, whose wit masks a sharp understanding of gender politics.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The real-life referent of much of Mrs. Allonby's description — the dandy as Ideal Man.
- **[[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]**: The earnest interlocutor whose naive questions provide the occasion for Wilde's satire.
- **[[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]]**: The voice of conventional morality who wants to know "the reward of the Ideal Man."
