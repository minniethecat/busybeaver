# "Kind is a dreadful word."

> Oh, don't say that, Lady Hunstanton. Kind is a dreadful word.
> (001-074)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this brief [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], responding to [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]'s remark that he has "the most wonderful good influence over everybody" and that "there is something very kind" in his taking an interest in young [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]] (001-074). Illingworth's rejection of the word "kind" is immediate and instinctive — it is the dandy's horror of being categorized under a comfortable, bourgeois virtue.

## Analysis

This short line is one of the most revealing things Illingworth says about himself. To be called "kind" is, for him, to be domesticated — to be rendered safe, unthreatening, and therefore uninteresting. The [[dandyism|dandy]] defines himself against the moral categories of [[victorian-morality|Victorian society]]; "kindness" belongs to the world of parish clergymen and charitable ladies, not to the amoral aesthetic realm Illingworth inhabits. The line also carries dramatic irony: Illingworth's interest in Gerald is anything but kind — it is the instinct of a father drawn to a son he has never acknowledged, though Illingworth himself does not yet know this.
