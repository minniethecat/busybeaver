# "So hideous a mockery as a marriage"

> I will not stand before God's altar and ask God's blessing on so hideous a mockery as a marriage between me and George Harford.
> (004-082)

## Context

[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] speaks these words to [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] in Act IV, after he has urged her to accept [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s proposal of [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]]. It is the climactic moment of the play: the "[[the-woman-of-no-importance|woman of no importance]]" refuses the social remedy that would restore her respectability because she recognizes it as a deeper violation.

## Analysis

Every word in this line carries weight. "God's altar" invokes the sacred — [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] takes [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] more seriously than the society that offers it as a convenience. "Hideous mockery" is the judgment: this [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] would not heal a wound but deepen it. And the use of "[[lord-illingworth|George Harford]]" — [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s real name, the name of the young man who seduced and abandoned her — strips away the title and the pretense. She is not refusing a lord; she is refusing the man who wronged her.
