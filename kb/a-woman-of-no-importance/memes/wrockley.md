# Wrockley

## Overview

Wrockley is never seen on stage, but it is constantly present as [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s home — the place where she has lived for twenty years, raising [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] in obscurity while the father who abandoned her prospered in the world of [[london-in-the-play|London society]]. It represents the hidden life that the country-house world knows nothing about.

## Significance

Wrockley is the spatial embodiment of [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s social erasure. She has made a home there, a life of "always at work" (003-060), but it is a life defined by absence — absence from society, absence from recognition, absence from the world of "importance" that [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]] represents.

When [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] and [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] choose to leave [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]] at the play's end, they are choosing Wrockley — choosing obscurity and integrity over the poisoned gift of aristocratic acceptance. Wrockley, in the play's moral geography, is the place of virtue.
