# "But what world says that, Lady Stutfield? It must be the next world. This world and I are on excellent terms."

> But what world says that, Lady Stutfield? It must be the next world. This world and I are on excellent terms.
> (001-072)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this signature [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], responding to [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]'s remark that "the world says that Lord Illingworth is very, very wicked" (001-071). He sits down beside [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] as he speaks, pairing verbal audacity with physical ease. The line encapsulates Illingworth's entire philosophy of life.

## Analysis

This is arguably the most important epigram in the play, because it states Illingworth's moral position with perfect clarity. He does not deny being wicked — he denies the authority of the world that condemns him. The distinction between "this world" (the society he navigates with such skill) and "the next world" (the moral realm of judgment and consequence) is the [[dandyism|dandy]]'s fundamental creed. So long as one is successful in *this* world, the *next* world's opinion is irrelevant. The play's dramatic arc tests this philosophy to destruction: by Act IV, Illingworth discovers that "this world" includes [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], whose moral authority he cannot dismiss with a [[wit-and-epigram|witticism]].
