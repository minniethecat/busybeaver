# Dandyism

## Overview

The dandy is one of Wilde's signature character types — the man who elevates style to a philosophy, who treats life as a performance and conversation as an art form. In *A Woman of No Importance*, [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] is the dandy par excellence: [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ty, elegant, utterly self-possessed, and morally hollow. Through him, Wilde simultaneously celebrates and indicts the dandy's worldview, exposing the emptiness beneath the [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s.

## Context in Victorian Society

Dandyism emerged in the early nineteenth century as a form of aristocratic self-fashioning — Beau Brummell was its patron saint. By the fin de siècle, the dandy had evolved into a more subversive figure: the man who challenged bourgeois seriousness through [[wit-and-epigram|wit]], artifice, and the cultivation of pleasure. Wilde himself was the era's most famous dandy, and his characters — Lord Darlington, Lord Goring, [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] — are both self-portraits and self-critiques.

## Manifestation in the Play

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s dandyism is evident from his first entrance. His conversation is a cascade of [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s: "So much [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] is certainly not becoming. [[quote-marriage-ruin|Twenty years of romance make a woman look like a ruin]]; but twenty years of [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] make her look like a public building" (001-186). The [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] is undeniable — and so is the cynicism. The dandy's [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]s are a defense mechanism, a way of keeping moral seriousness at bay.

The play's judgment on dandyism is delivered through its plot. [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] cannot save him from the consequences of his past. When confronted by [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], his charm is useless; when faced [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]'s need for a father, his elegance is irrelevant. The dandy, who has lived entirely in the realm of performance, discovers that some realities cannot be out-[[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ted.

## Key Characters

- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The dandy as tragic figure — brilliant in conversation, bankrupt in moral substance.
- **[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]**: The female counterpart to the dandy, whose [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] is sharper and whose self-awareness is greater.
