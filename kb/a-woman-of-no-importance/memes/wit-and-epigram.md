# Wit and Epigram

## Overview

*A Woman of No Importance* is constructed around its epigrams. Wilde's characteristic technique — the inversion of conventional wisdom through paradox — is the play's dominant rhetorical mode. Characters do not so much converse as compete in wit, each epigram a small performance of social mastery. But Wilde also uses wit diagnostically: what a character finds funny, and how they wield humor, reveals their moral character more accurately than their stated principles. The play's epigrams are not decoration; they are its argument.

## Context in Victorian Society

The late Victorian theater was dominated by the "well-made play" — plots driven by [[secrecy-and-the-past|secret]]s, letters, and convenient coincidences. Wilde's innovation was to subordinate plot to language, making the epigram — not the revelation — the primary theatrical event. His plays are comedies of conversation in which the real action is verbal. This was both a commercial strategy (audiences came for the wit) and an artistic one (language, not plot, is where ideas live). The epigram became Wilde's signature, and *A Woman of No Importance* represents its most sustained deployment across a serious dramatic structure.

## Manifestation in the Play

### Wit as Power — Lord Illingworth

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] is the play's master of epigram, and his wit is a form of power. He enters Act I and immediately dominates: "One should never take sides in anything. Taking sides is the beginning of sincerity, and earnestness follows shortly afterwards, and the human being becomes a bore" (001-112). The statement dismisses the entire framework of moral commitment in three sentences. This is the epigram's social function — to make complexity disappear into a polished phrase — deployed by a character who has every reason to avoid moral scrutiny.

His most famous line in the play, "[[quote-forgive-after-dinner|After a good dinner one can forgive anybody]], even one's own relations" (002-119), is the epigram as deflection. It reduces forgiveness — the play's central moral question — to a matter of digestion. The joke is brilliant; the worldview it encodes is bankrupt.

### Wit as Critique — Mrs. Allonby

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]'s epigrams are often sharper than [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s because they contain more uncomfortable truth. When she observes that men "Rarely, if ever, do they forgive" (002-225), the line functions as both comedy and critique — it names the [[victorian-morality|double standard]] the play exists to condemn while maintaining the epigram's polished surface.

### Anti-Wit — Hester Worsley

The play also dramatizes the limits of wit through [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]], who refuses the epigrammatic mode entirely. Her earnestness is presented as the antithesis of Illingworth's wit — and the play's resolution vindicates her seriousness over his cleverness. Wilde, the supreme master of the epigram, wrote a play in which wit is ultimately powerless to save its wielder.

### Structural Analysis

| Act | Dominant Wit Mode | Primary Wielder | Function |
|-----|------------------|-----------------|----------|
| I | Social display | [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]], [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] | Establish characters through verbal style |
| II | Defensive humor | [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] | Wit as armor against the emerging past |
| III | Confrontation | [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]], [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] | Wit breaks; plain speech takes over |
| IV | Silence | — | The epigram has nothing left to say |

The structural trajectory is crucial: wit dominates the early acts and recedes as the moral stakes rise, until Act IV — the play's most serious act — contains almost no epigrams at all. Wilde understood that wit could conquer a drawing-room but not a conscience.

## Key Characters

- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: Wit as power and defense; his epigrams protect him from moral scrutiny — until they cannot.
- **[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]**: Wit as critique; her epigrams expose what [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s conceal. She is the only character who matches his verbal facility while wielding it more honestly.
- **[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]**: The anti-epigrammist. Her refusal to participate in the verbal game is itself a moral position — and she is vindicated.
- **[[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]**: Wit without awareness; she quotes epigrams without understanding their implications.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The epigram in *A Woman of No Importance* is inseparable from [[dandyism]] — it is the [[dandyism|dandy]]'s primary weapon. It also functions as a counterforce to [[victorian-morality|Victorian Morality]], exposing the gap between what society professes and what it practices. And it is structurally tied to [[class-and-society]]: the ability to wield epigrams is itself a class marker, available only to those with the leisure and education to cultivate it.
