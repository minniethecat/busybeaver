# "Twenty years of romance make a woman look like a ruin"

> So much marriage is certainly not becoming. Twenty years of romance make a woman look like a ruin; but twenty years of marriage make her look like a public building.
> (001-186)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] during his first extended appearance in Act I (001-181–001-198), in conversation [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] on the terrace of [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]]. It is his signature moment — the line that establishes him as the play's master of [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] and, simultaneously, reveals the cynicism at his core. The audience does not yet know his history [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], but the [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]'s casual cruelty about women's aging prefigures it.

The dramatic irony is that [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] — the woman he seduced and abandoned — has experienced precisely the "twenty years" he jokes about. His [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] is a dinner-table [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ticism; for her, it is a life sentence.

## Analysis

### The Architecture of Wit

The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] works by inverting expectations through a precise visual metaphor. Romance should beautify; Illingworth claims it produces a ruin. Marriage should provide stability; Illingworth claims it produces a public building — institutional, impersonal, ugly. The architectural contrast (ruin vs. public building) is the joke's engine: ruins are romantic, public buildings are bureaucratic. The line manages to insult both love and [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] in a single sentence, which is the [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]'s essential mode.

### Subtext as Self-Indictment

What makes the line devastating in context is not what it says but what it reveals about the speaker. Illingworth can talk about "twenty years of romance" as a joke because he has never lived [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h its consequences. The woman he ruined — [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] — has lived every one of those twenty years in obscurity and devotion, while he has ascended to diplomatic success. The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]'s [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] is genuine; its moral emptiness is absolute.

### The Epigram as Dramatic Foreshadowing

Structurally, the line functions as prophecy in reverse. It predicts exactly what the play will expose: that [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] is a mechanism for avoiding consequence, and that when consequence finally arrives — in [[quote-hideous-mockery|Mrs. Arbuthnot's refusal]] — [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] will be powerless. The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] that dominates Act I is, by Act IV, reduced to silence.
