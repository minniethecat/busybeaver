# "Women are always on the side of morality, public and private."

> The growing influence of women is the one reassuring thing in our political life, Lady Caroline. Women are always on the side of morality, public and private.
> (001-067)

## Context

[[mr-kelvil|Mr. Kelvil]] delivers this pronouncement in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in response to [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]]'s question about whether he favors women taking part in politics. The line is characteristic of Kelvil: pompous, sententious, and apparently progressive. He is the play's representative of modern political morality — a Member of Parliament who writes about "Purity" and believes in women's moral influence.

## Analysis

Wilde uses Kelvil's pronouncement to set up one of the play's central tensions: the relationship between public morality and private truth. Kelvil's faith in women's moral superiority is conventional Victorian piety, but the play's female characters complicate it at every turn. [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] openly mocks moral convention. [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] uses morality as a weapon of social control. And [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], the play's most morally serious character, is a "fallen woman" by Victorian standards. The line's dramatic function is to establish a shallow moral framework that the play's action will progressively dismantle.
