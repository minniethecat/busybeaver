# "The secret of life"

> [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]: I believe there are admirable homes where people of that kind are looked after and reformed, and I think on the whole that the secret of life is to take things very, very easily.
> (003-103)

> [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]: The secret of life is never to have an emotion that is unbecoming.
> (003-104)

> [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]: The secret of life is to appreciate the pleasure of being terribly, terribly deceived.
> (003-105)

> [[mr-kelvil|Kelvil]]: The secret of life is to resist temptation, Lady Stutfield.
> (003-106)

> [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]: There is no secret of life. Life's aim, if it has one, is simply to be always looking for temptations. There are not nearly enough. I sometimes pass a whole day without coming across a single one. It is quite dreadful. It makes one so nervous about the future.
> (003-107)

## Context

This extended [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] exchange occurs in Act III at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], during the after-dinner conversation. What begins as [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]'s innocuous comment about the need to "take things very, very easily" escalates into one of the play's most structurally elaborate [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] sequences — four characters, each offering a one-line philosophy of life that reveals their character type. [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]'s definition is blandly aristocratic; [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]'s is aesthetic and amoral; [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]'s is naively masochistic; [[mr-kelvil|Kelvil]]'s is puritanically moralistic. [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] caps them all by denying the premise entirely — there is no [[secrecy-and-the-past|secret]], only the restless pursuit of temptation.

## Analysis

The sequence is a masterclass in Wilde's technique of the cumulative [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]. Each character's definition functions as self-portraiture: [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] reveals her commitment to surface and composure, [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]] her cultivated victimhood, [[mr-kelvil|Kelvil]] his [[puritanism-and-the-american-critique|Puritan]] moralising. [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s climactic rebuttal sweeps them all aside, asserting his intellectual dominance over the room. His line is theologically significant: by denying any "[[secrecy-and-the-past|secret]]" or purpose to life, he positions himself as the play's [[dandyism|dandy]]-nihilist, for whom life is only a series of sensations and opportunities. The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] also foreshadows the play's central [[secrecy-and-the-past|secret]] — [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s past [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h Illingworth — by making "[[secrecy-and-the-past|secret]]s" the explicit topic of [[wit-and-epigram|wit]].
