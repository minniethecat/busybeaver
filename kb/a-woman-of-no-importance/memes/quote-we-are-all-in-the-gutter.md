# "We are all in the gutter, but some of us are looking at the stars."

> We are all in the gutter, but some of us are looking at the stars.
> (002-081)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this celebrated aphorism in Act II at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in the drawing-room conversation that follows the dinner party. The line arises from a discussion of ambition, aspiration, and the different ways people respond to the constraints of their circumstances.

## Analysis

This is perhaps Wilde's most famous single line from the play — an aphorism that has achieved a life far beyond its dramatic context. Its power lies in its perfect balance: the first clause ("we are all in the gutter") is a concession to democratic despair, a recognition that human life is fundamentally limited, squalid, and compromised. The second clause ("but some of us are looking at the stars") is a assertion of human transcendence — the capacity to aspire even from the lowest position.

But within the play, the line takes on a more specific and more troubling meaning. [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] considers himself one of those who is "looking at the stars" — a man of taste, wit, and vision. Yet the play demonstrates that his stargazing has been conducted at the expense of the woman he left in the gutter. The aphorism, read in dramatic context, is not an aspiration but an indictment.
