# "It is perfectly monstrous the way people go about saying things against one behind one's back that are absolutely and entirely true."

> It is perfectly monstrous the way people go about, nowadays, saying things against one behind one's back that are absolutely and entirely true.
> (001-073)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in response to [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]'s remark that "everyone I know says you are very, very wicked" (001-072). The line is a perfect example of Illingworth's conversational technique: he deflects moral criticism not by denying it but by transforming it into a joke about social convention. He does not claim to be innocent — he claims that discussing his guilt behind his back is bad manners.

## Analysis

This epigram is one of Wilde's most quoted lines because it captures the paradoxical logic of [[dandyism|dandyism]] in a single sentence. The structure is: concede the substance (the accusations are true), attack the form (saying them behind one's back). This rhetorical move disarms moral critics by making their correct observation seem petty. Illingworth's [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] is not merely decorative — it is a weapon of self-defense that protects him from the consequences of his own actions until the play's final act, when [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s truth can no longer be deflected by clever phrasing.
