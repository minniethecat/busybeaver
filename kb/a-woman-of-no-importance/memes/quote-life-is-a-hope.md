# "Nothing should be out of the reach of hope. Life is a hope."

> Nothing should be out of the reach of hope. Life is a hope.
> (001-028)

## Context

[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]] speaks this line in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]] to [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]], who has just received [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s offer of a secretaryship. Gerald says the position means "things that were out of the reach of hope before may be within hope's reach now" (001-027). Hester's reply elevates a personal moment into a philosophical principle, reflecting her [[puritanism-and-the-american-critique|American Puritan]] conviction that hope is not a luxury but a moral imperative.

## Analysis

Hester's pronouncement is one of the few moral statements in the play delivered without irony. Unlike the [[wit-and-epigram|epigrams]] of [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] or [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]], this line is meant sincerely — and its sincerity is precisely what marks Hester as an outsider in English society. The line sets up the play's central conflict between American moral idealism and English social cynicism. Hester believes in hope as a right; the English characters treat it as a privilege. The dramatic irony is that Gerald's "hope" — the secretaryship — will bring him into contact with a father who represents everything Hester's moral world condemns.
