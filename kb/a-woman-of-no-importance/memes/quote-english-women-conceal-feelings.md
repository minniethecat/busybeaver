# "English women conceal their feelings till after they are married. They show them then."

> It is not customary in England, Miss Worsley, for a young lady to speak with such enthusiasm of any person of the opposite sex. English women conceal their feelings till after they are married. They show them then.
> (001-017)

## Context

[[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], reproving [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]] for her enthusiastic praise of [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]]'s "beautiful nature" (001-016). The line is delivered as a lesson in English social decorum to the American visitor, but its Wildean twist transforms it from a rebuke into a critique of English [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]].

## Analysis

The epigram's structure is classic Wilde: a straightforward first clause sets up a conventional observation, and the second clause undermines it with a darker truth. English women *conceal* before marriage and *show* after — the implication being that marriage transforms demure performance into open emotional tyranny. Lady Caroline herself exemplifies this pattern: she spends the entire play managing [[sir-john-pontefract|Sir John]]'s mufflers, overshoes, and seating arrangements with a relentless maternal authority. The line also serves as foreshadowing: [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] has spent twenty years concealing her feelings, and when she finally reveals them in Act IV, the effect is devastating.
