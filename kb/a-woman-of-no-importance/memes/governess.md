# The Governess

## Overview

The governess is one of the most freighted figures in Victorian literature, and Wilde employs the type with characteristic sophistication in *A Woman of No Importance*. Though [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] herself never appears as a governess in the play's present action, her past as one — and the circumstances in which her employment as governess led to her seduction and ruin — make the figure central to the play's social critique. The governess was a woman of education and genteel background who was nevertheless a paid servant, and this liminal position made her uniquely vulnerable to the predatory attentions of the men in the households where she worked.

## Context in Victorian Society

The governess occupied a notoriously ambiguous position in the Victorian class system. She was typically a middle-class woman of some education — often a clergyman's daughter — who had been forced by economic necessity to earn her living. But the work she did (teaching children) placed her in a household where she was neither family member nor ordinary servant. She ate with the family but was not of the family; she was addressed with the forms of politeness but could be dismissed without notice.

This ambiguous position had well-known dangers, of which the most famous literary treatment is Charlotte Brontë's *Jane Eyre* (1847). The governess was sexually vulnerable — a woman of marriageable age and respectable background living in proximity to the men of the household, with no family to protect her and no economic independence to fall back on. The "governess seduced by the son of the house" was a recognized Victorian social type, and it is exactly this type that Wilde invokes in [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s backstory.

## Manifestation in the Play

The word "governess" appears early in the play, in a conversation that establishes both the social reality of the position and the attitude of Wilde's aristocratic characters toward it. [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] recalls that she "saw the governess" and found her "far too good-looking to be in any respectable household" (001-047). This is an exquisite comic moment — Lady Caroline's concern for respectability is shown to be a form of victim-blaming — but it also encodes the real social logic that Wilde is critiquing.

The governess in question was, of course, the young Rachel Arbuthnot, sent by Lady Pagden to Lady Caroline and then employed by the Harford family, where she met the young George Harford (now [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]). The seduction that followed is the play's originating event, and Wilde's presentation of it makes clear that the governess's vulnerability was structural — built into the very system of genteel employment that was supposed to protect her.

## Key Characters

- **[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]**: The former governess whose seduction and abandonment is the play's central injustice.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The aristocratic seducer whose casual treatment of the governess reflects the power asymmetry of class and gender.
- **[[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]]**: The voice of aristocratic social judgment whose attitude toward the governess reveals the system's cruelty.
