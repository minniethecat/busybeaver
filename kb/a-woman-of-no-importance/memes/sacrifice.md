# Sacrifice

Sacrifice — particularly the quiet, invisible sacrifice of the mother — is the moral counterweight to the play's glittering surface of [[dandyism|dandy]] [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]. Where [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] and [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] celebrate the pleasures of self-interest, [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] embodies the alternative: a life given over entirely to the welfare of another.

## Mrs. Arbuthnot's Sacrifice

The central sacrifice of the play is [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s decision, twenty years before the action, to raise her illegitimate son alone rather than seek the social protection of marriage to a man she did not love. This was not a single act but a continuous, twenty-year discipline: she has lived in obscurity at [[wrockley|Wrockley]], supported herself, and devoted her entire emotional life to [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]. "I have no life outside of you," she tells him (004-139). The line is not a complaint but a simple statement of fact.

Wilde is careful not to sentimentalize this sacrifice. Mrs. Arbuthnot is not a saint — she is bitter, wounded, and capable of searing anger. Her sacrifice has cost her everything: her social position, her name, her chance at a public identity. The play's dramatic question is whether this sacrifice will be recognized — by Gerald, by Illingworth, by the social world that has erased her.

## Hester's Sacrifice

[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]] offers a different model of sacrifice. As an American Puritan, she has been raised to value moral principle over personal happiness. Her decision to stand with Mrs. Arbuthnot — "You are the sweetest woman that ever lived" (004-204) — represents a sacrifice of her own certainties: the rigid [[puritanism-and-the-american-critique|Puritan]] moral framework that would condemn a "fallen woman" gives way to a more compassionate recognition of individual suffering. Hester's sacrifice is the sacrifice of judgment itself.

## The Economics of Sacrifice

Wilde embeds the theme of sacrifice in a broader economic metaphor that runs through the play. Mrs. Arbuthnot speaks of what Gerald "owes" her; Illingworth offers marriage as "repair"; the language of debt, payment, and value recurs throughout the final act. The point is that sacrifice creates a moral economy that the dandy's world of surfaces cannot account for. Illingworth, who has never sacrificed anything for anyone, cannot understand why his offer of marriage — a transaction that would "repair" the damage — is refused. Mrs. Arbuthnot's refusal is the play's assertion that sacrifice cannot be bought off; it must be acknowledged.
