# "Let it be said of us that we have made the world a little better for our having lived in it"

In Act II at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]] responds to the company's casual dismissal of moral seriousness [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h a declaration of purpose that stands in stark contrast to the [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]matic cynicism that surrounds her. She insists that the goal of life is not to amuse oneself — as [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] and [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] would have it — but to leave the world improved. The sentiment crystallizes her declaration that "we are trying to build up life, [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]], on a better, truer, purer basis than life rests on here" (002-103). The line is the moral heart of the play.

## Analysis

[[hester-worsley|Hester]] is often dismissed by critics as a mere foil — the Puritan American whose earnestness sets off the [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] of the English aristocrats. But this pronouncement is not merely earnest; it is structurally essential. The entire play moves toward the question of whether any character will actually *do* something moral, rather than merely talk about it. [[hester-worsley|Hester]]'s line — that we should be remembered for having made the world "a little better" — is the standard against which every other character is measured. By the play's end, [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] has met that standard; [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] has not.
