# "There are far more things forbidden to us than are forbidden to them."

> Oh, don't say that, Lady Stutfield. We have a much better time than they have. There are far more things forbidden to us than are forbidden to them.
> (001-064)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in response to [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]'s lament that "the world was made for men and not for women" (001-063). Rather than contesting the premise of male dominance, Mrs. Allonby inverts its implication: more prohibitions mean more opportunities for transgression, and transgression is where the pleasure lies.

## Analysis

This is one of Wilde's most subversive [[gender-roles|gender]] arguments, disguised as a light [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]. On its surface, it is a paradox: how can having *more* restrictions give women a "much better time"? The answer lies in the [[dandyism|dandy]]'s aesthetic: prohibition creates the conditions for transgression, and transgression is the source of excitement. Men, who can do as they please, have nothing to rebel against and are therefore bored. Women, constrained by [[victorian-morality|Victorian morality]], have the thrill of forbidden knowledge. The line is Mrs. Allonby at her most dangerous: she is not arguing for women's rights but for women's pleasures, and she locates those pleasures precisely where Victorian morality says they should not be.
