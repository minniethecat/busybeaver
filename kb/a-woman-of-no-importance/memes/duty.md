# Duty

## Overview

Duty is a word that echoes through *A Woman of No Importance* — invoked, contested, and finally redefined. For the play's more conventional characters, duty means conformity to social expectation: [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] understands duty as the obligation to maintain appearances, [[mr-kelvil|Mr. Kelvil]] as the obligation to improve the world through earnest effort. But for [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], duty undergoes a radical transformation over the course of the play — from the duty of concealment she has practised for twenty years to the duty of truth she finally embraces.

## Context in Victorian Society

Duty was one of the Victorian period's most freighted words. It appeared everywhere — in sermons, in conduct books, in political speeches — and its invocation was almost always an assertion of social control. A woman's duty was to her husband, her children, her reputation; a man's duty was to his country, his class, his name. The concept left little room for a duty to oneself.

## Manifestation in the Play

The play stages a conflict between competing conceptions of duty. [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]] feels a duty to his mother that is instinctive and fierce — his physical attack on [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] in Act III is a duty-driven act. But he also feels the pull of a duty to his own future, embodied in the career opportunity Illingworth represents.

[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s journey is from one understanding of duty to another. Her initial duty — to protect Gerald from the truth of his origins — has defined her entire adult life. But the play's climactic scene redefines duty as the obligation to be honest, to refuse complicity in the systems that have harmed her, and to claim her own moral autonomy.

## Key Characters

- **[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]**: The character whose understanding of duty is transformed.
- **[[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]]**: Torn between conflicting duties to mother and father.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The man for whom duty is an entirely optional concept.
- **[[mr-kelvil|Mr. Kelvil]]**: The earnest moralist for whom duty is a matter of public improvement.
