# "Thoroughly sensible woman, your mother, Gerald."

> Thoroughly sensible woman, your mother, Gerald. I knew she would come round in the end.
> (003-003)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] speaks this line in Act III, in the [[the-picture-gallery|picture gallery]] at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]]. He is alone with [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]], discussing [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s apparent change of heart about Gerald's appointment. The line is thick with dramatic irony: Illingworth is complimenting the woman he seduced and abandoned twenty years ago to the son who does not know his own parentage.

## Analysis

The casualness of this line is its most striking feature. Illingworth speaks of Mrs. Arbuthnot as though she were merely a sensible woman whose approval he is pleased to have secured. In fact, she is the woman whose life he destroyed, and his "thoroughly sensible woman" is the recognition of a quality — resilience — that exists only because of the necessity his actions created.

The line demonstrates Illingworth's capacity for complete moral compartmentalisation. He can speak of Mrs. Arbuthnot with apparent warmth and admiration while remaining entirely indifferent to the harm he caused her. This is the dandy's moral strategy: charm as a substitute for accountability.
