# "The soul is born old but grows young. That is the comedy of life."

> The soul is born old but grows young. That is the comedy of life.
> (002-139)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this epigram in the Act II drawing-room at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in conversation with [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]. The discussion has turned to aging and the passage of time — themes that Wilde treats with characteristic paradox.

## Analysis

This is one of Wilde's most philosophically ambitious epigrams in the play. The conventional wisdom holds that we are born innocent and accumulate experience — that the soul, like the body, ages. Illingworth inverts this: we are born weighted with ancestral wisdom (or ancestral sin) and spend our lives learning to be light, frivolous, young. The "comedy of life" is this reversal of the expected trajectory.

But the epigram is also Illingworth's personal credo. He "never intends to grow old" — a declaration he makes elsewhere in the same conversation — and the idea that the soul grows young is his justification for a life devoted to pleasure, wit, and the refusal of seriousness. The play's dramatic action tests this philosophy: can one really "grow young" when one's past is [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], waiting in a cottage at [[wrockley|Wrockley]]?
