# "Children begin by loving their parents"

> Children begin by loving their parents. After a time they judge them. Rarely, if ever, do they forgive them.
> (002-188)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] speaks these words in Act II at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], during the evening gathering. The [[wit-and-epigram|aphorism]] is presented as worldly wisdom — the observation of a man who has seen enough of life to know how family relationships evolve. The dramatic irony is that Illingworth himself is a father whose son, [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]], does not know him. [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] is still in the "loving" phase — loving the idea of a father he has never met. The "judging" and the refusal to "forgive" lie ahead, in the play's final act.

## Analysis

This [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] is Wilde's clearest statement of the play's central dynamic. The three-stage arc — love, judgment, the [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]hholding of forgiveness — exactly maps the trajectory of [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]'s relationship [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] across the four acts. In Act I, [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] is grateful and admiring. By Act III, he judges. In Act IV, he does not forgive. The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] is a prophecy that Illingworth himself delivers [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]hout understanding its application to his own life — a perfect piece of dramatic construction disguised as dinner-table conversation.
