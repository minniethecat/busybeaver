# "Curious thing, plain women are always jealous of their husbands, beautiful women never are!"

> Curious thing, plain women are always jealous of their husbands, beautiful women never are!
> (001-183)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers this observation in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]]. The line is part of the extended drawing-room conversation in which the characters dissect marriage, gender, and social relations with varying degrees of wit and insight.

## Analysis

The witticism works by inverting the expected logic: one might assume that beautiful women, having more to lose if their husbands stray, would be more jealous. But Mrs. Allonby's observation suggests that beauty confers a kind of social security — beautiful women need not be jealous because they hold the power in the transaction. Plain women, lacking this power, must police their husbands through jealousy.

The line is characteristic of [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]'s style: superficially light, structurally ruthless. It reduces marriage to a market in which women's value is determined by their appearance, and it does so with a smile that makes the reduction palatable without making it less true.
