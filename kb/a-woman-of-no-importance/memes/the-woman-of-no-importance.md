# The 'Woman of No Importance'

## Overview

The title of Wilde's play is an ironic masterstroke. The "woman of no importance" is [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] — a woman whom society has erased, whose name appears in no society column, whose existence is an embarrassment to be managed. Yet by the play's end, she has demonstrated more moral substance, more self-knowledge, and more genuine importance than any of the titled, wealthy, and socially "important" characters who surround her. The title's irony is the play's deepest argument.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian society classified women [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h brutal simplicity: there were respectable women and there were fallen women. The boundary between these categories was absolute and policed by gossip, exclusion, and the [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] market. A woman who crossed the boundary — as [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] did — became a non-person, a "woman of no importance." She might live, work, and even be spoken of kindly in private, but she could never be acknowledged in public.

## Manifestation in the Play

Wilde builds the irony of his title through accumulation. The "important" characters — [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] the diplomat, [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]] [[lady-hunstanton|the hostess]], [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline Pontefract]] the arbiter of manners — spend four acts discussing trivialities while the play's real moral drama unfolds around the woman they overlook.

[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s importance is revealed gradually. She is [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]]'s devoted [[motherhood|mother]], a woman of quiet dignity, and — the audience learns — the victim of [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s youthful seduction. Her refusal to marry him in Act IV is the play's most important act: she declines the social restoration he offers because she no longer accepts the society that would grant it. "I will not stand before God's altar and ask God's blessing on so hideous a mockery as a [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] between me and [[lord-illingworth|George Harford]]" (004-082).

## Key Characters

- **[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]**: The "woman of no importance" who becomes the most important person in the drama.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The "important" man whose moral bankruptcy is exposed by the woman he scorned.
- **[[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]**: The "important" hostess who is kind but complicit in the system of exclusion.
