# Hypocrisy

## Overview

Hypocrisy is the target of Wilde's most sustained satirical fire in *A Woman of No Importance*. The play anatomises a society in which moral pronouncement and moral behaviour bear almost no relation to each other — where [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] can pontificate about propriety while married to her fourth husband, where [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] can be received everywhere while having ruined a woman and abandoned his child, and where forgiveness is urged upon women as a duty while men are never expected to seek it.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian England maintained an elaborate code of public morality that governed everything from dress to conversation to the relations between the sexes. Yet this code was enforced with radical asymmetry: a woman who transgressed sexual norms was "fallen" — socially dead — while a man with a discreet mistress or a hidden past suffered no such penalty. This was not hypocrisy in the simple sense of individuals saying one thing and doing another; it was a structural hypocrisy built into the very fabric of respectable society, and it is this structural dimension that Wilde exposes through the machinery of his plot.

## Manifestation in the Play

The play's drawing-room conversations are a masterclass in performative morality. [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]], the hostess, dispenses conventional pieties with an air of benevolent authority that the audience quickly learns to distrust. [[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]]'s obsession with propriety — she is scandalised by bachelors, by Americans, by governesses — is rendered comic by her own marital history.

Lord Illingworth himself acknowledges the hypocrisy of the system with characteristic candour. He tells [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] that "the history of women is the history of the worst form of tyranny the world has ever known. The tyranny of the weak over the strong. It is the only tyranny that lasts" (002-010), inverting the conventional narrative while revealing the gendered machinery beneath it.

The climax of the play turns on exactly this hypocrisy: [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] must decide whether to accept the terms of a society that would forgive Lord Illingworth everything and her nothing — or to reject those terms entirely.

## Key Characters

- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: Embodies the system's hypocrisy while being its most articulate critic.
- **[[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]]**: The voice of conventional propriety whose own life belies her pronouncements.
- **[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]**: The American outsider who names English hypocrisy directly.
- **[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]**: The victim of hypocrisy who ultimately refuses to perpetuate it.
