# Puritanism and the American Critique

## Overview

[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]], the young American visitor, serves a crucial dramatic function in *A Woman of No Importance*: she is the outsider whose moral clarity exposes the compromises and hypocrisies of [[class-and-society|English society]]. Her Puritan heritage gives her a framework for judgment that the English characters lack — but Wilde also subtly critiques her absolutism, suggesting that moral rigor [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]hout human sympathy is itself a form of cruelty.

## Context in Victorian Society

Americans were a familiar presence in late Victorian fiction and drama — sometimes figures of fun (the brash heiress, the naive democrat), sometimes vehicles for social critique. Henry James and others used the "international theme" to contrast American innocence [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h European corruption. Wilde follows this tradition but complicates it: [[hester-worsley|Hester]] is morally admirable but also rigid, and the play does not fully endorse her Puritan certainties.

## Manifestation in the Play

[[hester-worsley|Hester]]'s most important intervention comes in Act III, when she declares that "there are many things [[quote-things-women-should-never-forgive|women should never forgive]]" (003-100). This statement, delivered in response to [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]'s conventional piety about feminine forgiveness, is the moral turning point of the play. [[hester-worsley|Hester]] gives [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] permission — philosophical and moral — to refuse the forgiveness society demands of her.

[[hester-worsley|Hester]] also critiques [[class-and-society|English society]] directly: "You shut out from your society the gentle and the good. You laugh at the simple and the pure" (002-103). Her American perspective allows her to name what the English characters cannot see — that their society is a closed system that values [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] over virtue.

Yet Wilde also shows the limits of Hester's Puritanism. Her rigid moral categories leave little room for the complexity of [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s actual situation. The play's wisdom lies in its synthesis: [[hester-worsley|Hester]] provides the moral clarity, [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] provides the human depth.

## Key Characters

- **[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]**: The Puritan conscience of the play — morally serious, sometimes rigid, always honest.
- **[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]**: The human reality that tests [[hester-worsley|Hester]]'s abstractions.
- **[[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]**: The voice of conventional English [[victorian-morality|morality]] that [[hester-worsley|Hester]] exposes.
