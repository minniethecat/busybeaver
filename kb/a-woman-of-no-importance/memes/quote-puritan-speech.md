# "Do you know, I don't believe in the existence of Puritan women?"

> Do you know, I don't believe in the existence of Puritan women? I don't think there is a woman in the world who would not be a little flattered if one made love to her. It is that which makes women so irresistibly adorable.
> (001-225)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this epigram in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in conversation with [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]. The topic is [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]], whom Mrs. Allonby has just dismissed as "a Puritan besides" (001-214). Illingworth, having praised Hester's beauty, dismisses the idea that any beautiful woman can be genuinely Puritan.

## Analysis

This epigram does multiple kinds of dramatic work simultaneously. It establishes Illingworth's view of women as fundamentally susceptible to male attention — a view that justifies, in his own mind, his predatory behaviour. It sets up the opposition between the dandy's worldliness and [[hester-worsley|Hester]]'s moral seriousness that will drive much of the play's conflict. And it demonstrates Wilde's characteristic technique of the epigram as thought-experiment: the proposition is outrageous, but it forces the audience to examine their own assumptions about the relationship between moral principle and human nature.

The epigram's most audacious move is its final clause: "It is that which makes women so irresistibly adorable." The quality Illingworth is praising — women's alleged susceptibility to flattery — is precisely the quality that, in his own past behaviour, he has exploited for destructive ends.
