# "You must leave my life with me. I will not accept you."

> You must leave my life with me. I will not accept you.
> (004-085)

## Context

[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] delivers this line in Act IV, in the sitting room of her cottage at [[mrs-arbuthnots-cottage|Wrockley]]. [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] has just proposed a marriage of convenience — he will marry her, regularise their son's position, and provide for them both. The proposal is, in worldly terms, everything she could want. Her refusal of it is the play's climactic moral action.

## Analysis

This line is the dramatic and moral centre of *A Woman of No Importance*. For twenty years, Mrs. Arbuthnot has lived in the shadow of her past with Illingworth — the seduction, the abandonment, the secret that has governed every choice she has made. Now, at the moment when she could finally achieve the respectability she has been denied, she refuses — not out of pride, but out of a deeper understanding of what acceptance would mean.

The phrasing is precise and devastating: "You must leave my life with me." The verb "leave" does double work — it asks him to depart, but it also demands that he stop trying to take possession of her story, her experience, her self. Her life is hers; he cannot have it. The refusal is not a rejection of happiness but an assertion of the one thing she has left: her moral autonomy.

Wilde gives this line to Mrs. Arbuthnot without irony, without epigrammatic deflection. It is one of the few moments in the play where language is used with complete sincerity, and its power derives from the contrast with the witty evasions that dominate the rest of the dialogue.
