# Lord Alfred Rufford

## Character Overview

Lord Alfred Rufford is one of Wilde's "offstage gentlemen" — a name invoked by [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]] in Act I to illustrate a social point, but a character who never appears in person. He is mentioned only once in the play, at (001-060): [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]] remarks that Lord Alfred has dined [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h her, placing him among the aristocratic set that populates the [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton]] social world. His sole dramatic function is to thicken the texture of Wilde's aristocratic milieu.

## Role in the Play

Lord Alfred does not appear on stage and speaks no lines. His existence is purely nominal — a name dropped to suggest the breadth of [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]'s social circle and the world of titled gentlemen whose lives orbit the [[the-english-country-house|country-house]] party. He is the lightest possible sketch of the aristocratic type that [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] embodies at full scale.

## Relationships

- **[[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]]**: His hostess, who mentions him as a dinner guest.
