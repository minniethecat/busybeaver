# "After a good dinner one can forgive anybody"

> It is only the intellectually lost who ever argue, and after a good dinner one can forgive anybody, even one's own relations.
> (002-119)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this line during the after-dinner conversation in Act II. The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] is a double strike: first dismissing intellectual debate as a sign of confusion, then reducing forgiveness — the play's central moral question — to a matter of physical comfort.

## Analysis

The line is a perfect example of how Wilde uses [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] to expose moral emptiness. Illingworth's version of forgiveness requires no moral effort — only a good meal. The final clause, "even one's own relations," adds the punch: family, in his view, is the hardest thing to forgive, not because family wrongs are graver but because family members are more annoying. The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] is funny precisely because it is so morally bankrupt.
