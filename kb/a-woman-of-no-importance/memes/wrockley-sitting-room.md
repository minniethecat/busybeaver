# Wrockley Sitting-Room

The sitting-room at [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s house in [[wrockley|Wrockley]] is the setting for Act IV — the play's final and most intense confrontation. As described in the stage directions, it is a "sitting-room in Mrs. Arbuthnot's house at Wrockley." This is Mrs. Arbuthnot's private space, the domain she has created over twenty years of exile, and it is here that [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] must come to her, reversing the power dynamic that has governed the entire play.

## The Private Stage

Unlike the previous three acts, which take place in the public and semi-public spaces of [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], Act IV unfolds in a genuinely private space. This is Mrs. Arbuthnot's home — the space she has built for herself and [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] in the years since Illingworth abandoned her. The setting gives her a moral authority that the public spaces of Hunstanton could not: here, she is not a guest or a supplicant but the sovereign of her own domain.

## Dramatic Function

The shift to Wrockley is the spatial equivalent of the play's moral argument. Illingworth has spent three acts dominating the social spaces of Hunstanton with his [[wit-and-epigram|wit]] and charm. In Act IV, he must enter Mrs. Arbuthnot's space on her terms. His attempt to reclaim authority — to assert the rights of a father and the privileges of a peer — founders against the reality of the room: the books, the furnishings, the accumulated evidence of a life lived without him. Mrs. Arbuthnot's sitting-room is the visible proof that she has built a world that does not require his presence, and her refusal of his marriage proposal is the verbal equivalent of that spatial fact.

The room's modesty — it is a "sitting-room," not a drawing-room — underscores the class reversal at the play's heart. The woman of no importance, in her humble sitting-room, wields more moral authority than the peer of the realm who has come to "repair" her.
