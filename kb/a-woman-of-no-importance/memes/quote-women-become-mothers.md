# "All women become like their mothers. That is their tragedy."

> People's mothers always bore me to death. All women become like their mothers. That is their tragedy.
> (002-168)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act II at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], speaking to the assembled company. The line is a casual conversational gambit — a joke meant to amuse his listeners. But its dramatic force comes from what the audience knows and Illingworth does not realize: [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], the [[motherhood|mother]] of his own son [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]], is present in the house. The remark is not merely cruel; it is an unwitting judgment on himself. He has made [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] into what she is — a woman whose entire identity has been shaped by [[motherhood|mother]]hood — and now he dismisses the result as a "tragedy" without recognizing his own authorship of it.

## Analysis

This [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] is the most concentrated example in the play of what might be called Wilde's "dramatic [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]]" — a [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ticism whose full meaning is visible only to the audience, not the speaker. On its surface, it is a Wildean inversion of sentiment: [[motherhood|mother]]s should be sacred; Illingworth finds them boring. But the deeper meaning is tragic. [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] *has* become like her [[motherhood|mother]] — a woman defined entirely by her relationship to her child — because Illingworth left her no other identity. The "tragedy" belongs to him, though he will never see it.
