# "One should never trust a woman who tells one her real age."

> One should never trust a woman who tells one her real age. A woman who would tell one that, would tell one anything.
> (002-048)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this epigram in the Act II drawing-room conversation at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]]. The line is part of his ongoing verbal sparring with [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]], in which the two match epigrams on the subjects of gender, honesty, and social performance.

## Analysis

The epigram plays on the Victorian convention that a woman's age was a closely guarded secret — a piece of information whose disclosure represented a breach of feminine decorum. Illingworth's logic is characteristically perverse: a woman who is honest about her age cannot be trusted *because* she is honest, since honesty about such a matter signals either a lack of proper social training or a dangerous indifference to the rules of the game.

Beneath the wit, the line encodes a serious point about the social construction of female identity. In a world where a woman's value is tied to her youth, revealing one's age is not honesty but self-sabotage — and a woman who commits self-sabotage is, in the dandy's logic, capable of anything.
