# The Self-Made Man

## Overview

The figure of the self-made man — the individual who rises through talent and effort rather than inheritance — is a recurring presence in Victorian literature, and Wilde gives it a characteristic twist in *A Woman of No Importance*. [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]] is poised at the threshold of just such a rise: his appointment as [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s private secretary represents an escape from obscurity into a world of influence and opportunity. But Wilde complicates this narrative by revealing that Gerald's "rise" is, in fact, being orchestrated by the father who abandoned him — and that the world of opportunity into which he is being lifted is also the world that ruined his mother.

## Context in Victorian Society

The Victorian period was an age of unprecedented social mobility, driven by industrialisation, empire, and the expansion of the professions. The ideal of the self-made man — most famously embodied in Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* (1859) — celebrated individual effort and moral character as the keys to success. But this ideal coexisted with a social order in which inherited wealth and position still conferred enormous advantages.

## Manifestation in the Play

Gerald's appointment as Illingworth's secretary is the plot mechanism that drives the entire play. It is the "wonderful opening" that [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]] celebrates and that [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] dreads, because she knows — and Gerald does not — that the man offering the opportunity is his father.

Wilde's treatment of the self-made man theme is characteristically ironic. Gerald's rise is not the result of his own merit (though he is indeed meritorious) but of Illingworth's barely-conscious acknowledgment of paternity. And the world to which Gerald aspires — the world of country houses and diplomatic appointments — is exposed by the play as morally bankrupt.

## Key Characters

- **[[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]]**: The would-be self-made man whose rise is not what it seems.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The patron whose offer of opportunity conceals a darker purpose.
- **[[mr-kelvil|Mr. Kelvil]]**: The earnest self-improver whose rhetoric of moral seriousness the play gently mocks.
