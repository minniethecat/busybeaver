# "Nowadays it is only the unreadable that occurs."

> I do. Nowadays it is only the unreadable that occurs.
> (001-195)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this line in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in response to a question about whether he reads the newspapers. The dismissive epigram is characteristic of Illingworth's attitude toward seriousness, journalism, and the earnest documentation of events.

## Analysis

This is Wilde speaking through his character with unusual directness. Wilde's own battles with the press — which he regarded as philistine, intrusive, and hostile to art — are well documented, and the line distils his position into a single elegant dismissal. "Only the unreadable that occurs" implies a world in which interesting things do not happen, or if they do, they are not reported, or if reported, they are rendered unreadable by the medium through which they are conveyed.

Within the play, the line serves to establish Illingworth's intellectual superiority to the world of ordinary affairs. He does not read newspapers because newspapers are beneath him — a position that is both genuinely aristocratic and, as the play will demonstrate, a form of wilful ignorance about the human consequences of his actions.
