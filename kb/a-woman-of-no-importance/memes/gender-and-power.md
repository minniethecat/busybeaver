# Gender and Power

## Overview

Power in *A Woman of No Importance* is distributed along gender lines with a clarity that the play's wit only partly obscures. Men have institutional power: [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] can offer careers, confer status, and ruin lives with impunity. Women have — or must cultivate — other forms of power: wit ([[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]), moral authority ([[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]), social policing ([[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]]), or the power of the outsider who can afford to walk away ([[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]).

## Manifestation in the Play

The play's central power transaction occurred twenty years before the action begins: the young George Harford used his position, his charm, and his complete impunity to seduce and abandon Rachel Arbuthnot. The entire dramatic situation is the consequence of this exercise of unchecked male power.

In the play's present, Illingworth continues to exercise power with the same casual confidence. His appointment of [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] as his secretary is an act of power — the power to confer or withhold opportunity — and it sets the entire plot in motion. His attempted kiss of Hester in Act III is another exercise of power, this time physical and sexual, and it precipitates the crisis that brings his past crashing into the present.

But the play also shows women exercising power, often through indirection. Mrs. Allonby's wit is a form of power — it allows her to compete with Illingworth on something like equal terms. Lady Caroline's social vigilance is power, however petty. And Mrs. Arbuthnot's final refusal — "I will not accept you" — is the play's most powerful assertion of the power of the powerless: the power to say no.

## Key Characters

- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The embodiment of male institutional power.
- **[[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]**: The powerless woman who discovers the power of refusal.
- **[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]**: Wit as a form of female power.
- **[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]**: The outsider whose power derives from her distance from the system.
