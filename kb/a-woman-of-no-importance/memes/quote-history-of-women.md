# "The history of women is the history of the worst form of tyranny the world has ever known"

> The history of women is the history of the worst form of tyranny the world has ever known. The tyranny of the weak over the strong. It is the only tyranny that lasts.
> (002-184)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this provocative [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act II, in conversation [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]]. It is perhaps his most intellectually ambitious line — not merely a dinner-table [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ticism but a pseudo-philosophical claim about the nature of power. Coming from the man who seduced and abandoned [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]], it is charged [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h irony: the speaker who denounces female tyranny is himself the agent of the play's deepest example of masculine exploitation.

## Analysis

The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] works through a series of reversals. The conventional Victorian view held that women were the weaker sex, subject to male tyranny. Illingworth reverses this: it is the "weak" — women — who exercise the "only tyranny that lasts." The line is Wilde's commentary on the Victorian cult of domesticity, where women's moral authority in the home was both a real form of power and a compensation for their exclusion from public life. That Illingworth speaks it — a man who has used and discarded a woman — makes the [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] not a statement of truth but an exposure of the speaker's own self-serving worldview. He blames women for the very situation he has created.
