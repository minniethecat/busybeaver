# "The Ideal Man should talk to us as if we were goddesses, and treat us as if we were children."

> The Ideal Man! Oh, the Ideal Man should talk to us as if we were goddesses, and treat us as if we were children. He should refuse all our serious requests, and gratify every one of our whims. He should encourage us to have caprices, and forbid us to have missions. He should always say much more than he means, and always mean much more than he says.
> (002-065)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers this celebrated speech in Act II at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in response to [[lady-stutfield|Lady Stutfield]]'s request for her "conception of the Ideal Husband." Having dismissed the very idea of an ideal husband ("The institution is wrong"), Mrs. Allonby pivots to the Ideal Man — the male companion in whose existence she does believe.

## Analysis

This speech is a masterpiece of Wildean double-coding. On its surface, it is a witty inversion of the conduct-book tradition that prescribed ideal behaviour for women; here, it is men who are catalogued according to their suitability as companions. But the speech also functions as a devastating exposure of what Victorian women were actually taught to want: not equality but worship, not partnership but performance, not honesty but the elaborate fiction of gallantry.

The Ideal Man's defining quality is, paradoxically, his insincerity — he "should always say much more than he means, and always mean much more than he says." This is an exact description of the dandy's conversational method, and the speech as a whole is a coded portrait of [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]], who represents both the appeal and the danger of the ideal Mrs. Allonby describes.
