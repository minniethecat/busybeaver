# "It is the problem of slavery. And we are trying to solve it by amusing the slaves."

> Quite so. It is the problem of slavery. And we are trying to solve it by amusing the slaves.
> (001-130)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this epigram in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], in response to [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]'s observation that women are subject to more prohibitions than men: "There are far more things forbidden to us than are forbidden to them" (001-129). The conversation is part of the drawing-room banter that fills Act I, but Illingworth's reply cuts deeper than the surrounding wit.

## Analysis

This is one of the play's most politically charged epigrams. By naming the condition of Victorian women as "slavery," Illingworth — the play's most accomplished cynic — articulates a feminist critique that the play's more earnest characters never quite manage. The word is not softened by irony; the observation about "amusing the slaves" — offering entertainments and distractions rather than freedom — is a precise diagnosis of the patriarchal strategy.

Yet the fact that this insight comes from Illingworth, of all people, is part of Wilde's complex dramatic design. The man who ruined [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] is also the man who can see, and name, the structural injustice of the system he benefits from. His insight does not change his behaviour — he continues to treat women as objects of conquest throughout the play — which makes the epigram a demonstration of how intelligence and moral awareness can coexist with, and even serve, moral bankruptcy.
