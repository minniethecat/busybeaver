# "One has never heard his name before in the whole course of one's life, which speaks volumes for a man, nowadays."

> He must be quite respectable. One has never heard his name before in the whole course of one's life, which speaks volumes for a man, nowadays.
> (001-010)

## Context

[[lady-caroline-pontefract|Lady Caroline]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], after [[sir-john-pontefract|Sir John]] corrects her misnaming of [[mr-kelvil|Mr. Kelvil]] as "Mr. Kettle" (001-009). The line is ostensibly about Kelvil but actually articulates a broader social principle: in an age of scandal and publicity, anonymity is the surest sign of respectability.

## Analysis

This epigram works through the same Wildean inversion as the play's more famous lines: it takes a seemingly negative attribute (obscurity) and reinterprets it as a virtue. The logic is that anyone whose name *has* been heard — in newspapers, in gossip, in scandal — has been heard for the wrong reasons. Lady Caroline's world is one in which the aristocracy's traditional assumptions are crumbling under the pressure of modern publicity; her epigram is a rearguard defense of the old order, where a gentleman's name was known only to his social equals. The line also serves as gentle self-satire: Lady Caroline herself is one of Wilde's most memorable comic creations, a woman whose every utterance announces her class position.
