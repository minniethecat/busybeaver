# "I think to elope is cowardly. It's running away from danger. And danger has become so rare in modern life."

> I think to elope is cowardly. It's running away from danger. And danger has become so rare in modern life.
> (001-050)

## Context

[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act I at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], after [[lady-hunstanton|Lady Hunstanton]] recalls the scandal of Lady Belton eloping with Lord Fethersdale from Melthorpe, a neighboring estate (001-049). The line is classic Mrs. Allonby: it takes a conventional moral judgment (elopement is wrong) and inverts it into an aesthetic complaint (elopement is boring).

## Analysis

The epigram works through a triple inversion. First, it reframes elopement not as immoral but as cowardly — a failure of nerve, not of virtue. Second, it redefines cowardice as "running away from danger," implying that the truly brave thing would be to face scandal openly. Third — and most characteristically Wildean — it complains that danger itself has become scarce in modern life. This is the [[dandyism|dandy]]'s central lament: that [[victorian-morality|Victorian society]] has made life too safe, too predictable, too *kind*. The line functions as both a joke and a serious critique of bourgeois respectability, which Wilde saw as the enemy of passion, art, and individuality.
