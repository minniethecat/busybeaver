# Love and Romance

## Overview

Wilde's treatment of love in *A Woman of No Importance* is characteristically double-edged — romantic love is both affirmed as a genuine value and exposed as a social construct that serves particular interests. The play's young lovers, [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]] and [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]], represent one pole: sincere, earnest, and somewhat naive romantic attachment. The wit-combat between [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] and [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]] represents the other: love as an elaborate game whose rules are known to all players and whose stakes are carefully calibrated.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian ideology placed romantic love at the centre of its understanding of marriage — at least in theory. The ideal of the companionate marriage, in which partners were united by mutual affection and respect, was widely celebrated. But this ideal coexisted with a marriage market in which considerations of property, status, and family alliance were never far from the surface. For women especially, "falling in love" was not merely an emotional event but an economic one — the choice of a husband determined, often irrevocably, the entire shape of a woman's future.

## Manifestation in the Play

The play opens with a love story: [[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]] has fallen in love with [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]], and her praise of his "beautiful nature" (001-020) establishes the tone of sincere romantic feeling. But this sincerity is immediately placed in tension with the sophisticated, ironic conversation of the older characters, for whom love is a subject for epigrammatic dissection rather than earnest declaration.

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s romantic history — the seduction and abandonment of the young Rachel Arbuthnot — is the play's dark counter-narrative to the courtship of Gerald and Hester. Illingworth treated love as a game with no consequences for himself and devastating consequences for his partner. The play's moral structure insists that this history cannot be buried — that the past's romantic choices reverberate into the present.

The [[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]–[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] flirtation represents yet another model of love: the sophisticated, mutually acknowledged game between equals who understand that they are playing. "Men always want to be a woman's first love," Mrs. Allonby observes. "We women have a more subtle instinct about things. What we like is to be a man's last romance" (002-020).

## Key Characters

- **[[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald Arbuthnot]]**: The young lover whose romantic idealism is tested.
- **[[hester-worsley|Hester Worsley]]**: The earnest American whose romantic feelings coexist with moral seriousness.
- **[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]**: The man for whom love was always a game — with devastating consequences.
- **[[mrs-allonby|Mrs. Allonby]]**: The female dandy who has mastered the love-game's rules.
