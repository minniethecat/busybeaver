# "Children begin by loving their parents; after a time they judge them; rarely, if ever, do they forgive them."

> Children begin by loving their parents; after a time they judge them; rarely, if ever, do they forgive them.
> (004-070)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this epigram in Act IV, in [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s [[mrs-arbuthnots-cottage|sitting room at Wrockley]]. He is attempting to persuade her to accept his marriage proposal, and the observation about children and parents is part of his argument that [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] will inevitably move beyond his attachment to his mother.

## Analysis

This is one of the play's most psychologically acute observations. The three-stage progression — love, judgment, forgiveness (or its absence) — is a compressed theory of family dynamics that has proven remarkably durable. Within the play, Illingworth deploys it as a strategic argument: Gerald will judge his mother eventually, so she might as well secure her position through marriage before that happens.

But the epigram also rebounds on Illingworth himself. Gerald's judgment, when it comes, is directed not at his mother but at his father — the parent who abandoned him. And the question of forgiveness, which the epigram declares "rarely, if ever" possible, becomes the play's central moral question: can [[gerald-arbuthnot|Gerald]] forgive [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]? Can [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]? The answers the play provides are complicated.
