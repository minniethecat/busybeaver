# "Every saint has a past, and every sinner has a future"

> The only difference between the saint and the sinner is that every saint has a past, and every sinner has a future.
> (003-066)

## Context

[[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]] delivers this [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] in Act III at [[hunstanton-chase|Hunstanton Chase]], during a conversation touching on [[victorian-morality|morality]] and social [[illegitimacy-and-reputation|reputation]]. The line functions as both a [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]ticism and a moral argument: by collapsing the boundary between "saint" and "sinner," Illingworth undermines the very basis of [[puritanism-and-the-american-critique|Puritan]] moral judgment. In the context of the play, it resonates deeply [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]h [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]]'s situation — a woman who has been condemned for a single "sin" twenty years ago, while the man responsible (Illingworth himself) occupies the position of social respectability.

## Analysis

The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] derives its force from temporal reversal. Saints are not inherently virtuous — they merely have sins that predate their [[illegitimacy-and-reputation|reputation]]. Sinners are not inherently damned — they have futures that may yet redeem them. The [[wit-and-epigram|epigram]] is Wilde's critique of [[class-and-society|Victorian morality]], which judged [[mrs-arbuthnot|Mrs. Arbuthnot]] forever by a single act while excusing [[lord-illingworth|Lord Illingworth]]'s ongoing moral carelessness. Coming from Illingworth, it is also characteristically self-serving: by universalising sin, he dissolves his own guilt. The line has become one of Wilde's most enduring [[wit-and-epigram|aphorism]]s, often quoted [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]hout its dramatic context — and [[wit-and-epigram|wit]]hout the irony that the man who speaks it is the play's true sinner, who refuses to acknowledge either past or future.
