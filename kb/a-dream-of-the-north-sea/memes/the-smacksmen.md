# The Smacksmen

The **smacksmen** (or smacksman, singular) are the deep-sea fishermen who worked the North Sea trawling fleets in the late nineteenth century. In James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea*, they are the novel's true protagonists — a community of working men whose courage, endurance, and rough dignity the author portrays with intimate knowledge and deep respect.

## Life at Sea

The smacksmen's life was one of unremitting hardship. Working from sailing smacks — small, sturdy vessels designed for beam trawling — they endured freezing gales, crushing labor, and constant proximity to death. Runciman, who knew the men personally, describes how "the smacksmen go through this all the winter long!" (005-007) — an exclamation of genuine wonder at their resilience.

The work required "consummate dexterity at any time" and continued regardless of weather. The men "must go on performing work that needs consummate dexterity" even as the sea rose and the smack pitched violently. (005-008)

## Character and Culture

Runciman saw the smacksmen as representing "the pick of the North Sea population" — men forged by adversity into something exceptional. (009-001) They were not sentimentalized: the author records their rough speech, their superstitions, and their occasional brutality alongside their courage and generosity.

A distinctive feature of smacksman culture was their attitude toward death. "By a special mercy, the smacksmen have no time for morbid brooding. They will risk their lives with the most incredible dauntlessness to save a comrade." (008-005) Yet they remembered their dead with quiet gravity, speaking "with grave pity about Jim or Jack, who were lost twenty years ago." (008-002)

## Faith and the Mission

The smacksmen's spiritual life was shaped by isolation and danger. On the North Sea, "a sailor builds his own weird faith and his own code of ethics" (010-001), and the Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen found among them both deep religious feeling and rough irreverence. Some smacksmen "substituted the experience meeting for the confessional," blending evangelical practice with the raw honesty of working-class life at sea. (010-004)

## Mortality

The human cost of the industry was staggering: "A hundred smacksmen die to-night," Runciman writes during a hurricane. (016-004) Year after year, "a score of men in the North Sea every year" perished, their names becoming part of the fleet's oral history, recalled decades later as cautionary tales and memorials to fallen shipmates. (008-002)
