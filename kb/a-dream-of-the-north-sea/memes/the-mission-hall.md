# The Mission Hall

The **mission hall** in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* is the space aboard the Mission yacht where religious services were held for the fishermen of the North Sea fleets. During the week the room served ordinary purposes, but on Sundays "the yacht was transformed into a mission hall," becoming a floating chapel in the midst of the trawling grounds. (009-006)

## Transformation

The transformation from working vessel to place of worship was both physical and symbolic. "There was a fair amount of room for a religious service, the men packed themselves into their places with admirable and silent politeness," and the ordinary space became sacred. (009-005) The fishermen, who might have been swearing and hauling nets hours before, gathered with a reverence that impressed Runciman.

## Music and Worship

The fishermen's services were notable for their musical vitality. Unlike the somber tones of shore-bound congregations, "our fishermen sing religious words to the liveliest tunes they can learn. I notice they are fonder of joyful music than of the wailing stuff." (008-006) Runciman observed that the men preferred hymns of triumph and hope to dirges — a reflection of lives already saturated with enough sorrow.

## A Sailor's Faith

The mission hall represented something unique in Victorian religious life: a space where "a sailor builds his own weird faith and his own code of ethics." (010-001) The combination of mortal danger, isolation, and the sublime scale of the sea produced a spirituality that was neither conventional nor irreverent but distinctly maritime.
