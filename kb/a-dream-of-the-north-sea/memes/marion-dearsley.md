# Marion Dearsley

**Marion Dearsley** is a central female character in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). Along with her aunt, Mrs. Walton, she joins Henry Fullerton's expedition to the North Sea trawling grounds, where her courage and composure during a violent gale mark her as far more than a passive observer.

## Character

Marion is introduced as a woman of unusual fortitude. When the ship encounters heavy weather, she declares: "Marion and I seem to take kindly to bad weather. I believe if it came on to blow a hurricane we should both of us be quite sorry for the people who were exposed to it on shore." (002-008) This is no idle boast — when the gale strikes in earnest, she proves herself equal to conditions that would terrify most landsmen.

## In the Storm

During the novel's great gale, Marion displays remarkable courage. While the stewardess cowers below, "the hideous fascination of the storm drew Marion Dearsley again and again, and she sheltered herself under the hatch" — compelled by the terrible beauty of the sea even as it threatened to sweep her overboard. (003-005) When danger comes, she acts with swift decisiveness, pointing out a man overboard and rallying the crew to the rescue.

## Relationships

Marion's presence aboard the Mission vessel creates a social dynamic unusual for the North Sea. "This delightful rough diamond called on Miss Dearsley to choose the lee side" — the fishermen, accustomed only to the company of other men, find themselves awkwardly gallant in her presence. (002-002) Her friendship with Lewis Ferrier provides the novel's emotional center, their shared experiences in danger forging a bond that transcends the social conventions of Victorian England.
