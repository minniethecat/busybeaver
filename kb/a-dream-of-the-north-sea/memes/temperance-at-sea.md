# Temperance at Sea

**Temperance at Sea** was one of the signal achievements of the [[mission-to-deep-sea-fishermen|Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen]], as chronicled in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). The campaign to eliminate alcohol from the fishing fleet was both a moral crusade and a practical reform — one that Runciman presents as transforming not only individual lives but the entire culture of the North Sea fishing community.

The novel documents the temperance movement through personal testimonies, dramatic conversions, and statistical evidence of behavioral change, framing the "drop of drink" as the gateway to wider moral and spiritual renewal.

## Drink and Degradation

Runciman portrays alcohol as a destructive force that compounded the already harsh conditions of the fishermen's lives. The testimony of Tom Barling — a converted fisherman — provides the novel's most vivid account of drink's effects:

"Out at sea I gambled and drunk when I could get the money; I made rare game of religious men, and lived as if I had never to die." (011-008) His drinking impoverished his family: "my children were barefooted" and "I'd left the wife to pick up a living how she could." (011-008)

The systemic nature of the problem is emphasized. Alcohol was not merely a personal failing but an institutionalized feature of life at sea: "the shining bottles were there, the sharp scent of the alcohol appealed to the jaded nerves of men who felt the tedium of the sea, and thus a villainous agency obtained a terrible degree of power." (026-005) Drink was the default response to boredom, isolation, and the psychological strain of the fishing life.

## Conversion and the "Drop of Drink"

The novel presents the renunciation of alcohol as the visible proof of genuine religious conversion. Tom Barling's testimony culminates in the declaration: "And now by His grace I've dropped the drink, and them fits of bad temper, and my family looks well, and I'm so quiet in my breast here like, as I can walk for hours on deck and pray quiet, and never think of no drink, nor cards, nor excitement." (011-009)

The "drop of drink" becomes a marker of transformation. When a man is described as having "dropped drink," it signals his full entry into the community of the reformed. The narrator reports with satisfaction: "No isms. And you say they've dropped drink?" — the simplicity of the formulation underscoring the practical, non-doctrinal nature of the reform. (021-012)

The Mission's approach was not prohibitionist in the abstract but evangelical in practice: conversion led naturally to temperance, and temperance confirmed the reality of conversion. A backslider who returned to drink was the subject of communal prayer: "He's a backslider just now, through that dreadful drink. Let's all pray as he may see his sin against his Saviour, and come right back to Him." (012-005)

## Practical Results

Runciman is careful to document the tangible benefits of temperance. The elimination of drink from the fleet produced measurable improvements in safety, efficiency, and social harmony.

One fisherman observes casually: "If this had happened before the drink was done away with, there would have been a funny old booze in some o' them ar smacks, just for excitement like." (025-008) The implication is clear: in the old days, a weather crisis would have been compounded by drunken disorder; now, the fleet faced emergencies sober and coordinated.

The economic argument is made explicitly: "smack-owners, for their men are more efficient and honest; the fishermen themselves are grateful, because few of them really craved after drink, and the general results are obvious to anybody who spends a month in the North Sea." (026-007) The reform was not merely morally satisfying — it was commercially advantageous.

## The Dying Drunkard's Hope

One of the novel's most poignant moments involves a wounded fisherman who, near death, reflects on his past sins. Tom Betts, gravely injured and hovering between consciousness and delirium, murmurs: "Its all right, and I'm there arter all. I've swoor, and Ive drunk, and yet arter all I'm forgiven." (004-014)

The scene captures the novel's theology of grace: even the drunkard who repents at the final hour can find forgiveness. When Ferrier administers brandy as a medical necessity, he tells the patient: "This is your first and last drink, and you may make it a good one" — a acknowledgment that medical need sometimes overrode the temperance rule, but also a firm declaration that for the reformed man, there would be no return to the old ways. (016-010)
