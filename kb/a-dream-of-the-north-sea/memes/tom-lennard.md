# Tom Lennard

**Tom Lennard** is a major character in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). Described as "by inheritance a merchant, by choice a philanthropist," Tom embodies the Victorian ideal of muscular Christianity — a man of action whose faith is expressed through physical courage and unstinting generosity. (002-001)

## A Rough Diamond

Tom is immediately established as a force of nature. Runciman describes him as a "delightful rough diamond" whose irrepressible energy and lack of social polish are more than compensated for by his warmth and integrity. When introduced to "the prim, beneficent Joseph" on a philanthropic platform, "his enthusiasm overcame him" — Tom is constitutionally incapable of restraint, whether in his affections, his philanthropy, or his response to danger. (002-002)

His physical presence is formidable: during the gale, "Marion Dearsley flung her arms round Tom Lennard, and pinned him to the mast" — the image captures both his solidity and his role as a human anchor for the others. (003-003)

## At Sea

Tom's practical skills prove as valuable as his enthusiasm. When Ferrier calls for help with the ladies during the storm, "Ferrier, Fullerton, Tom, lend a hand with the ladies" — Tom is relied upon not for his money but for his strength and steadiness. (003-008) He is a philanthropist who does not merely fund good works but participates in them bodily, sharing the fishermen's dangers and earning their respect.

## Philosophy

Tom's approach to life is summed up in his characteristic observation about the gale: "The grandeur is — ah! fahscinating, but it's rather damp grandeur. It's only grandeur fit for heroes." (003-004) The remark captures his humor, his humility, and his genuine awe at the power of the sea — qualities that make him, in Runciman's portrayal, the ideal companion for such an undertaking.
