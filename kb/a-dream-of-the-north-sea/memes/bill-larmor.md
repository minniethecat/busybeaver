# Bill Larmor

**Bill Larmor** is the skipper of the fishing smack in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). As the vessel's captain, Larmor embodies the competence, composure, and quiet authority that Runciman admired in the North Sea fishermen. He is a central figure in the novel's most dramatic storm sequences.

## Character

Larmor is portrayed as the archetype of the capable and steady-handed skipper. Described as "skilful as he was," he navigates the smack through conditions that would overwhelm a lesser seaman. (005-004) Runciman notes that Larmor was "composed, as men of his class generally are; you rarely hear them raise their voices even in moments of extreme peril." (005-005) This understated composure — the opposite of theatrical heroism — defines his character.

## In the Storm

During the novel's climactic gale, Larmor's leadership is tested to its limits. With the smack "wallowing like a buoy in a tideway" and the crew clinging to survival, he remains at his post, issuing orders with the calm that comes from decades of experience. "It couldn't be very much worse," he observes at the gale's peak — a masterpiece of understatement that captures the stoic temperament of the North Sea skipper. (006-001)

## Faith

Larmor's character also reveals the religious dimension of the fishermen's lives. When Lewis Ferrier suggests a prayer meeting during the storm, it is Larmor who organizes the gathering, and afterward "shook hands with Larmor before they once more faced the war of the night" — a gesture of solidarity that bridges social class and religious sensibility. (006-003)
