# E.J. Mather

**Ebenezer Joseph Mather** was the founder of the [[mission-to-deep-sea-fishermen|Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen]], the historical figure whose pioneering humanitarian work among North Sea fishermen forms the factual foundation of James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). Though he never appears as a character in the novel's narrative, his presence is felt throughout — in the hospital cruisers, the mission smacks, and the transformed lives of the fishermen.

## The Founder

Runciman's preface explicitly acknowledges Mather as the originator of the Mission. The work is described as having been "begun by Mr. Mather in courage and in hope, and by him carried to a unique success." (Front Matter) The novel, though cast as a "dream" of the North Sea's future, is grounded in the real achievements of Mather's organization.

The preface notes that Mather was originally to have written the introduction himself but "that gentleman, in view of the apparent references to himself throughout the tale, shrank from the task." (Front Matter) This modesty — the founder declining to introduce a book that effectively celebrated his own work — is presented as characteristic of the man.

## A Man of Practical Vision

Mather's genius, as Runciman implies, lay in his understanding that the fishermen needed care "as well for the body as the soul." (Front Matter) The Mission's dual mandate — medical care and spiritual ministry — reflected Mather's recognition that a man facing starvation or untreated injury could scarcely attend to his spiritual condition.

The novel's closing pages recount one of Mather's quiet victories: the securing of a Customs concession that "let the Mission vessels take tobacco out of bond" — a small administrative triumph that nonetheless eased the daily lives of hundreds of fishermen. (026-005) Such details reveal the founder as a man who combined large vision with meticulous attention to practical obstacles.

## The Living Legacy

Mather's most enduring achievement was institutional: he built an organization that could survive and grow beyond any single personality. By the time of Runciman's writing, the Mission operated multiple vessels, employed doctors and missionaries, and had transformed the culture of the North Sea fishing fleet.

The narrator of the novel speaks as an eyewitness to this transformation — a witness who passes "the salutes of our grimy, greasy friends" and understands that behind the changed behaviour of rough fishermen lies the quiet, persistent work of the Mission's founder.
