# England

**England** in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* is both a geographic fact and a moral community — the nation whose fishermen labored unseen, whose philanthropists sought to aid them, and whose reading public the novel aimed to educate and mobilize.

## A Maritime Nation

Victorian England was the world's preeminent maritime power, and the North Sea fishing industry was one pillar of that dominance. Yet Runciman's novel reveals a paradox: the fishermen who helped sustain England's maritime strength were among its most neglected citizens, working in conditions of extreme danger and isolation, largely invisible to the land-bound population whose tables they stocked.

## Philanthropic Culture

England in the 1870s and 1880s was also the home of a vibrant philanthropic culture, of which the Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen was a notable expression. Runciman's appeal to "give the English seamen a chance" was addressed to a national conscience that Victorian evangelicals had cultivated for generations.
