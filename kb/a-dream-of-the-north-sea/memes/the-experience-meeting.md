# The Experience Meeting

The **experience meeting** was a distinctive form of religious gathering practiced among North Sea fishermen, described in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* as a maritime adaptation of evangelical worship. In these meetings, fishermen shared personal testimonies of their faith — a practice that Runciman saw as uniquely suited to the character and circumstances of the smacksmen.

## A Maritime Confessional

Runciman explicitly contrasts the experience meeting with Catholic confession: "In Italy you may see the sailors creeping into the box while the priest listens to whispers; on the North Sea a sailor builds his own weird faith and his own code of ethics." (010-001) The smacksmen, he notes, "had, in some instances, substituted the experience meeting for the confessional" — transforming a formal, hierarchical religious practice into a communal, democratic one. (010-004)

## Form and Function

The experience meeting allowed fishermen to speak openly about their spiritual lives in their own language, without the mediation of clergy or the constraints of formal liturgy. For men who spent weeks in isolation and whose work offered constant reminders of mortality, the meetings provided both spiritual outlet and social bonding.

Runciman, who was deeply sympathetic to evangelical religion, saw the experience meeting as an authentic expression of working-class faith — rough-hewn but sincere, lacking polish but possessing the raw honesty of lives lived at the edge of survival.
