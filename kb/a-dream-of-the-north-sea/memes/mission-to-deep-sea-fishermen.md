# Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen

The **Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen** is a humanitarian organization that forms the moral backbone of James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). Founded in August 1881, the Mission dispatched hospital cruisers to the distant trawling grounds of the North Sea, providing medical care, religious services, and human companionship to fishermen who spent weeks or months at sea in dangerous isolation.

## Origins and Purpose

The Mission was "designed, in humble dependence upon the blessing of Almighty God, to carry the glad tidings of salvation to the thousands of fishermen employed in the trawling fleets of the North Sea." Its dual mandate — caring for both body and soul — reflected the Victorian era's fusion of evangelical Christianity with social reform.

The founder's achievement was described by Runciman as a singular success: it had "fallen to the lot of the Founder of the Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen to accomplish such a success. No one can now write or think or 'dream' of the trawlers on the German Ocean, without referring, and referring with high admiration and wonder, to the superb and devoted work done by that organization." (P01-008)

## Hospital Ships

A key initiative of the Mission was the deployment of hospital cruisers — ships outfitted with surgical facilities, medicine, and trained medical staff — to serve the scattered fishing fleets. Runciman appealed to his readers to give funds "to give the Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen its hospital ship, for every fleet that scours the trawling grounds, but especially a fast steam cruiser — a *Robert Cassall* — so that the wounded fisherman" might receive prompt care rather than languishing for days before reaching port. (026-003)

## Impact on Fishermen

The Mission brought fishermen "into friendly contact with much that is best among his countrymen," ending the long exiles during which they were cut off from civilization, "among thousands of the very poor, very ignorant, and very profane." (025-005) The Mission's work transformed not only individual lives but the entire culture of the North Sea fishing industry.
