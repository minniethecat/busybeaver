# Freeman

**Freeman** was the skipper of the Mission schooner yacht in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). A former fisherman who had come up through the ranks of the North Sea trade, he commanded the vessel that carried Henry Fullerton, Lewis Ferrier, Tom Lennard, and their guests among the fishing fleet. His seamanship, good humour, and intimate knowledge of the fleet's operations made him an indispensable figure in the Mission's work.

## A Fisherman at the Helm

Freeman came from the same world as the men the Mission served. "Freeman, like all of the schooner's crew, had been a fisherman, and he grinned with pleasing humour when he heard the young lady's innocent questions." (003-020) This background gave him an instinctive understanding of the fleet's movements and the fishermen's ways that no formally trained yachtsman could match.

His seamanship was of the highest order. Runciman describes how "Freeman sailed his craft and threaded the lines of the dragging trawlers with stealthy speed" — a delicate operation requiring precise judgment of wind, current, and the positions of dozens of vessels. (010-010)

During one severe gale, "Freeman shaved the smack as closely as he dared" to allow the Mission party to communicate with an injured fisherman — a manoeuvre that balanced the safety of the schooner against the urgency of the rescue. (004-008)

## The Admiral's Ally

Freeman served as the crucial link between the Mission vessel and the fishing fleet's command structure. When the fleet admiral had instructions to relay, it was Freeman who received them.

"As the boats met by the side of the carrier, there was much gossip, and many mysterious messages passed. Blair told Skipper Freeman what the Admiral wanted, and the good man grinned hard. 'Right, sir; your time's your own. I'll manage.'" (008-012) This brief exchange captures Freeman's character: competent, unflappable, and quietly amused by the complexities of fleet politics.

When the time came to signal the fleet's departure, "Freeman sent up a rocket, the schooner's foresail was let over, and she rustled away through the squadron of brown-sailed craft." (008-014) His coordination of the signal sequence — rockets answered by flares across the dark water — demonstrated his mastery of the fleet's visual communication system.

## Humour and Humanity

Runciman portrays Freeman as a man of genuine warmth and unaffected humour. His musical contributions to the shipboard atmosphere were memorable: "I cannot describe Freeman's ecstasies," the narrator writes of the skipper's enthusiastic participation in the crew's singing — a scene that blended rough sailor culture with genuine joy. (009-005)

When Blair asked him what the answering rocket meant — "What's that, Freeman?" — the skipper's reply was characteristically laconic: "You'll see, sir, presently." (008-015) He understood that some things were better witnessed than explained, and he trusted the Mission party to appreciate the spectacle he had orchestrated.
