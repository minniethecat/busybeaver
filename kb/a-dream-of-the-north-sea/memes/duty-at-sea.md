# Duty at Sea

**Duty at Sea** is a recurring moral theme in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). The concept operates at multiple levels: the [[the-smacksmen|smacksman]]'s duty to his shipmates, the skipper's duty to his vessel, the Christian's duty to "hold forth the Word of Life," and — most urgently — the wealthy [[london|Londoner]]'s duty toward the fishermen whose labour puts food on his table. Through this layered treatment, Runciman builds the ethical case for the [[mission-to-deep-sea-fishermen|Mission]].

## The Skipper's Duty

The most immediate expression of duty appears in the conduct of the smack's skipper during crisis. When a gale threatens to overwhelm the vessel, the skipper's response is professional and impersonal:

> "Very well, gentlemen. I'm not concerned for myself. My duty's done now, and I'll do my best. I advise you to take some coffee, and try to get a few hours' rest before the pinch comes." (016-010)

The skipper's duty is discharged not through heroics but through competence — the calm, methodical fulfilment of responsibility even as the sea rises. This stoic professionalism recurs throughout the novel: sailors who perform hard operations "amid air that was choking" and skippers who hold their vessels steady through conditions that would terrify landsmen.

## The Christian's Duty

The Mission's work is framed in the language of religious obligation. One Mission worker articulates the foundation:

> "For my part, while I hold tenaciously to the primary duty of 'holding forth the Word of Life' — for it is 'the entrance of Thy Word giveth light and understanding to the simple' — yet I am entirely with you in feeling that we need to cultivate the intellect of these men." (011-005)

This dual duty — spiritual and practical — defines the Mission's approach. The fishermen need both salvation and education, both prayer and medical care. The Mission vessel carries Bibles and surgical instruments, preachers and doctors, because duty to the whole man cannot be divided.

## The Rich Man's Duty

Runciman's most pointed argument concerns the duty of the wealthy. The novel's later chapters describe how [[robert-cassall|Robert Cassall]]'s philanthropic awakening "was to revolutionize rich people's conceptions of duty and necessity." (021-008) Cassall himself, once indifferent to the fishermen's plight, comes to see his wealth as a trust.

The author makes this personal in his own narrative voice:

> "I learned that I had been unwittingly neglecting a duty, and now I blame myself for remissness. It's very pleasant to blame yourself, because it gives you such a superior sense of humility, and I am enjoying the luxury to the full." (021-010)

This self-deprecating confession — Runciman admitting his own prior indifference — is a rhetorical device: if even the author was blind to his duty, how many of his readers are equally culpable? The novel's purpose, in part, is to awaken that dormant sense of obligation.

## Duty Denied

Runciman is unsparing about the consequences of neglected duty. When a fisherman is lost at sea, the fault is not the sea's alone — it belongs also to the society that failed to provide [[hospital-cruisers|hospital ships]] and rescue services. "Every duty was neglected," one passage laments, describing a fisherman's family left destitute. (010-005) The implication is clear: to know of the fishermen's peril and do nothing is itself a moral failure.

| Form of Duty | Expression in the Novel |
|-------------|------------------------|
| Professional duty | The skipper who stays at his post through the gale |
| Religious duty | "Holding forth the Word of Life" aboard the Mission smacks |
| Social duty | The rich man's obligation to fund hospital cruisers |
| Personal duty | The author's confession of his own prior neglect |
| Practical duty | Surgical care, education, and material support for fishermen |
