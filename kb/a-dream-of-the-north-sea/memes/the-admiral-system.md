# The Admiral System

**The Admiral System** was the organizational structure of the North Sea fishing fleet depicted in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). Within each fleet of fishing smacks, one vessel was designated as the "admiral" — responsible for directing fleet movements, making weather-related decisions, and coordinating with the steam-carriers that transported fish to market.

This system was not a formal naval hierarchy but a practical arrangement born of necessity: the scattered smacks required a central point of coordination to operate safely and efficiently on the dangerous North Sea grounds.

## Authority and Decision-Making

The admiral's primary function was to assess conditions and issue directives that the rest of the fleet would follow. When a gale threatened, it was the admiral who evaluated the risk and decided whether the fleet should remain on the fishing grounds or run for shelter.

During one crisis, a skipper hails the Mission yacht with the urgent message: "The admiral says we're in for another bad time. Glass falling." (004-008) The fleet then "hung around awaiting the admiral's final decision" — a moment of collective deference that underscores the gravity of the role. (004-010)

The admiral's authority was not merely advisory. As one character declares during a dispute: "I shall exert my authority as admiral" — a statement that carries weight among the fishermen, who understood that obedience to the admiral could mean the difference between safety and disaster. (008-003)

## Coordination with the Carrier

A crucial aspect of the admiral's role was liaising with the steam-carrier — the transport vessel that collected fish from the fleet and ferried it to market. The carrier was the fleet's lifeline to shore, and the admiral coordinated meeting times and locations.

Runciman describes the scene: "as the boats met by the side of the carrier, there was much gossip, and many mysterious messages passed." (008-012) The admiral relayed instructions through the carrier's captain, and the fleet's movements were organized around these rendezvous points. When "Blair told Skipper Freeman what the Admiral wanted," the skipper complied without question. (008-013)

## Signals and Communication

With no radio or electronic communication, the admiral relied on visual signals to direct the scattered fleet. Rockets, flares, and sail movements served as the fleet's communication network.

Runciman captures a striking nocturnal scene: "Freeman sent up a rocket, the schooner's foresail was let over, and she rustled away through the squadron of brown-sailed craft." (008-014) The admiral's vessel responded with its own rocket, and then "a dingy vessel broke suddenly into spots of fire; then another, then another. Flares, torches" — the fleet signaling its acknowledgment across the dark water.

The admiral's signal governed the rhythm of the fishing day. In winter, "the men awaited the Admiral's signal; the snow lay thick on the deck, and the wind bit like a million needles." (016-008) Even in the harshest conditions, the fleet's movements were synchronized through this chain of command.

## The Social Dimension

The admiral system carried a social dimension as well. The admiral was a figure of respect, addressed by title, and his vessel served as a reference point for the fleet's collective identity.

The novel notes that choosing who should serve as admiral "promised to supply a month's fun" — suggesting that the selection process, while serious in its consequences, was also a subject of good-natured rivalry and nautical jest among the skippers. (024-005) The admiral was not a remote authority figure but a fellow fisherman elevated temporarily by the consent of his peers.

When the Mission yacht prepares to depart, the admiral speaks on behalf of the entire fleet: "If you only knew, sir, what it is for *us* — *us*, you know, to have people like you among us." (008-010) The admiral's voice carries the collective sentiment of the fishermen, reinforcing his role as the fleet's representative.
