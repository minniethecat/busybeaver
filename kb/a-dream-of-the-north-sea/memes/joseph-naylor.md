# Joseph Naylor

**Joseph Naylor** was a philanthropist and supporter of the [[mission-to-deep-sea-fishermen|Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen]] in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). A minor character whose few appearances are rendered with comic precision, Naylor represents the earnest, somewhat rigid Victorian reformer — the foil to Tom Lennard's expansive, physically overwhelming benevolence.

## The Prim Philanthropist

Runciman introduces Naylor with devastating economy: "The excellent Joseph Naylor, who was never seen to smile, and who was popularly supposed to sleep in his black frock-coat and high stock." (001-025) The image is indelible — a man so committed to propriety that even his sleep seemed governed by the dress code of Victorian respectability.

The frock-coat and high stock are markers of a particular type: the serious-minded, middle-class philanthropist who brought the same formalism to charitable work that he brought to business. Naylor is benevolent, but his benevolence is administered with the gravity of a bank manager.

## The Handshake

Naylor's defining moment in the novel — and one of its finest comic set-pieces — is his introduction to Tom Lennard. Tom, overflowing with enthusiasm at meeting a fellow philanthropist, unleashes his trademark greeting:

"He brought his colossal paw down on Mr. Naylor's shoulder so that the poor man showed signs of shutting up like a concertina inside the frock-coat; he squeezed Joseph's hand so fervently that the poor victim looked like a dentist's patient." (001-026)

The description of Naylor "shutting up like a concertina" inside his immaculate frock-coat is a masterpiece of physical comedy. Tom Lennard's "colossal paw" versus Naylor's prim formality creates a collision of temperaments that defines both characters.

## The Philosopher's Response

To his credit, Naylor bears the assault with dignity. "Mr. Naylor bore the shock like a true philosopher" — but his private reflection reveals the true cost:

"My dear, our new ally, Mr. Lennard, is most friendly, most cordial, quite impressively cordial; but do you know I should not like to sign a cheque just now. His cordiality has had distinct effect on my joints, and I wish really that his left hand were lighter. Social intercourse can only be carried on with difficulty when you feel as if a large sack had fallen on you from the third floor of a warehouse." (001-027)

The passage — Naylor confiding in his wife — humanizes the prim philanthropist. His dry, precise complaint ("distinct effect on my joints") and the absurd precision of the warehouse simile reveal a man who is stuffy but not unkind, physically timid but genuinely committed to the cause. Tom Lennard may crush his hand, but he does not crush his spirit.
