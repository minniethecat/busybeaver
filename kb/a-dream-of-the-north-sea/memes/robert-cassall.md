# Robert Cassall

**Robert Cassall** is a pivotal character in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). A self-made millionaire and [[marion-dearsley|Marion Dearsley]]'s uncle, Cassall begins as a hard-headed [[mission-to-deep-sea-fishermen|Mission]] skeptic and ends as its most generous benefactor — funding a [[hospital-cruisers|hospital cruiser]] that bears his name.

His [[the-north-sea|North Sea]] philanthropy represents the novel's thesis that wealth, when allied with compassion, can transform lives.

## The Self-Made Millionaire

Cassall embodies the Victorian ideal of the self-made man. Runciman introduces him as "a man of peculiarly modern type" who "had never once acknowledged himself beaten, though he had known desperate circumstances." (017-020) From youth, he fixed his mind on one goal: "he resolved that, let who will fail, he at least would have money and plenty of it."

He achieved it through forty years of relentless application, establishing a monopoly in his wholesale drapery and hosiery business.

"He was unscrupulous in all respects so long as he could keep within the law; he had no pity, no consideration, no remorse, in business hours; and he succeeded just as any other man of ability will succeed if he gives himself up body and soul to money-making." (017-020)

Yet Cassall was no caricature of the grasping capitalist.

He was "never proud; he was only hard." To his niece Marion, "whom he passionately loved," he offered blunt advice: "Never be ashamed, my dear, to tell people that your uncle was a wholesale draper and hosier." (017-021)

His retirement wealth was colossal: "Bob Cassall's name was good for a million anywhere; and indeed it was good for two millions, and more even than that." (017-022)

Runciman's physical portrait completes the picture:

> His grim, grizzled head was stooping a little as he bent towards his niece on this soft winter day, and he himself looked almost like the human type of a hard, wholesome, not unkindly Winter. His high Roman nose, penthouse brows, quick jetty eye, square well-hung chin, and above all his sturdy, decided gait — all marked him for a Man every inch, and no manlier being walks broad England than Robert Cassall. (017-030)

## Skepticism and Persuasion

When Marion first presents the case for the Mission to Deep Sea Fishermen, Cassall's response is characteristically gruff. He wrinkles "his grim face not unpleasantly" and deflects with lawyerly skepticism: "Go on; go on. You're a lawyer, neither more nor less." (018-002)

His curiosity is piqued, however, when Marion mentions Ferrier — "this — this what's-the-name — the Doctor, that you mentioned." Marion seizes the opening, telling him how [[lewis-ferrier|Ferrier]] stuck to a sinking smack to save his patient. Cassall's resistance cracks:

> Robert Cassall muttered, "That sounds like a man's doings." (018-015)

The turning point comes as Marion weaves her full account. Runciman describes her as a "fluent, artful, mock-modest, dramatic puss" who had her uncle completely in her toils. "Robert Cassall was captured — there could not be much error about that." (018-018)

## The Hospital Ship

Cassall's conversion from skeptic to benefactor is swift once decided. He invites his physician and friend [[sir-james-roche|Sir James Roche]] to meet the Mission leaders, and together they plan the hospital cruiser that will bear Cassall's name.

Runciman frames this transformation in language both affectionate and pointed:

> So ended the enslavement of Robert Cassall, and so, I hope, began his immortality. Oh! Marion Dearsley; sweet English lady. This is what you were turning over in your maiden meditations out at sea. Demure, deep, delicious plotter. What a coup! (019-005)

The ship — christened the *Robert Cassall* — becomes a fixture of the North Sea fleet:

> Robert Cassall was at length assured by experience that his enterprise had quadrupled the power of the Mission, and he only longed to see how his little miracle would succeed in winter. (024-003)

## The Man and His Name

Cassall represents one of the novel's central arguments: that wealth, properly directed, is not incompatible with virtue.

His trajectory mirrors [[ferriers-transformation|Ferrier's Transformation]] — both men are changed by their encounter with the North Sea and its fishermen, though Cassall's conversion takes place not amid the waves but in the drawing-room, through the agency of his niece.

The final pages of the novel invoke the *Robert Cassall* as a model for future philanthropy: "for every fleet that scours the trawling grounds, but especially a fast or steam cruiser — a *Robert Cassall* — so that the wounded fisherman, in the hour of his need and his utter helplessness, may be as sure of relief as are the Wapping labourer or the Mortlake bargeman." (025-005)
