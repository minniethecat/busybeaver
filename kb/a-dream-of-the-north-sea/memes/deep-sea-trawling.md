# Deep Sea Trawling

**Deep sea trawling** was the principal commercial fishing method employed by the North Sea fleets depicted in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea*. The industry formed the economic and social context for the novel's characters — a way of life defined by ceaseless labor, constant danger, and a distinctive working-class culture.

## The Work

Trawling involved dragging a large beam trawl net along the seafloor from a sailing smack. The work was unremitting and physically devastating. In Runciman's description of a North Sea gale: "the smacksmen must go on performing work that needs consummate dexterity at any time." (005-008) The nets, heavy with fish and seawater, had to be hauled by hand in conditions that would "kill the average landsman in a week."

## The Fleets

The great North Sea trawling fleets sailed from ports such as Yarmouth and Hull, staying at sea for weeks at a time. "Fullerton's idea was to let the gallant young doctor see something of the wild work that goes on" aboard the trawlers, introducing him to a world utterly alien to landsmen. (002-005)

The trawling grounds were vast and remote — the fleets worked far from shore, in waters where a sudden gale could mean death with no hope of rescue. The industry operated on thin margins, with fishermen's families dependent on the success of each voyage.

## After the Storm

The aftermath of a gale revealed the industry's toll: "After a gale like this there are always scores of funerals." (005-012) Yet the work continued, the fleets returning to sea as soon as the weather permitted — driven by economic necessity and a fatalistic courage that Runciman admired deeply.
