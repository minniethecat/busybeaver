# The Cruiser Yacht

The **cruiser yacht** in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* was the primary vessel operated by the [[Mission to Deep-Sea Fishermen]] among the North Sea trawling fleets. More than a mode of transport, the yacht was a floating headquarters, a hospital, a chapel, and a symbol of the outside world's care for the isolated fishing communities.

## A Vessel Transformed

The yacht's versatility was its defining feature. During the week it served as a working vessel, moving between fleets to deliver supplies, treat the injured, and offer companionship. On Sundays, "the yacht was transformed into a mission hall," its ordinary spaces becoming a place of worship where fishermen gathered from across the fleet. (009-006)

The transformation from working vessel to sacred space captured the Mission's dual mandate — "as well for the body as the soul" — and the yacht embodied this unity of practical care and spiritual ministry.

## Operations at Sea

The yacht moved constantly among the scattered trawling fleets, a mobile presence in the vast emptiness of the North Sea. It was equipped to handle medical emergencies, with space for treating injured fishermen and the supplies to manage everything from broken bones to infections. Runciman describes how the yacht threaded its way through "the lines of the dragging trawlers," a nimble and purposeful presence amid the laboring smacks. (008-004)
