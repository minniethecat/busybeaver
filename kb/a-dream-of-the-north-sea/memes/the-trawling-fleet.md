# The Trawling Fleet

The **trawling fleet** in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* is the collective body of fishing vessels that worked the North Sea grounds. More than a mere aggregation of ships, the fleet functioned as a floating community — a mobile society with its own hierarchies, customs, and codes of conduct, operating far from the oversight of land-based authority.

## Composition and Organization

The great North Sea trawling fleets consisted of scores of sailing smacks, each carrying a small crew of five to seven men. The fleets sailed from ports such as Yarmouth and Hull, dispersing across the distant fishing grounds and maintaining loose contact through signal flags and the occasional passing rendezvous. "Fullerton's idea was to let the gallant young doctor see something of the wild work that goes on" among the fleet — a world that operated by rhythms entirely alien to landsmen. (002-005)

The fleet was both workplace and home. Men lived aboard their smacks for weeks at a time, their entire existence contained within the narrow confines of a wooden vessel amid the vast emptiness of the sea.

## Community at Sea

Despite the physical isolation of individual vessels, the fleet constituted a genuine community. News traveled between smacks, the Mission yacht circulated among them, and in times of crisis — a gale, an injury, a sinking — the fleet rallied. Runciman describes the aftermath of a gale when survivors searched for familiar faces: "Just to think that of all those splendid, plucky smacksmen, we haven't got one yet! I've been using the glass, and can't see a face that I know." (004-003)

The fleet embodied a paradox: men who spent their lives in one of the loneliest occupations on earth were bound together by ties as strong as any village or parish.
