# Fishermen's Songs

**Fishermen's songs** in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* refer primarily to the hymns and spiritual songs sung during services aboard the Mission vessels. The fishermen's musical culture was a distinctive blend of evangelical piety and maritime practicality — loud, joyful, and utterly unlike the restrained worship of Victorian parish churches.

## Joyful Noise

Runciman was struck by the contrast between the fishermen's singing and conventional religious music. Where shore-bound congregations "sing religious words to the most dirge-like tunes," the fishermen "sing religious words to the liveliest tunes they can learn." (008-006) The author noted that "they are fonder of joyful music than of the wailing stuff" — a preference he attributed to lives already amply furnished with sorrow.

## The Hymn in the Storm

In one memorable passage, the sound of a hymn merges with the noise of a gale: "the gale in the cordage once more, and he forgot the words of the hymn in feeling only the strong passion and yearning of the music." (009-007) The hymn becomes not just words set to melody but a direct emotional channel — the fishermen's faith expressed through sound rather than doctrine.

## Religious Feeling

The fishermen's approach to religion was experiential rather than doctrinal. Runciman observes that for the smacksmen, Christianity consisted "not in singing hymns or saying prayers, but in a personal knowledge of Jesus Christ." (010-001) Their singing was an expression of this personal faith — rough, unpolished, and entirely sincere.
