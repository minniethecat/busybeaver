# Mrs. Walton

**Mrs. Walton** is a supporting character in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876). The aunt of Marion Dearsley, she accompanies her niece on Henry Fullerton's expedition to the North Sea trawling grounds, providing a steady maternal presence amid the turbulence of the voyage.

## Character

Mrs. Walton is introduced as a woman of quiet courage and unassuming dignity. Along with her niece, she was persuaded by Fullerton and Tom Lennard to join the expedition: "I have been out before, and I persuaded Mrs. Walton and Miss Dearsley to join" — her prior experience at sea making her a reassuring presence for the younger Marion. (002-004)

Unlike the more demonstrative characters — Tom Lennard's boisterous enthusiasm, Marion's fascinated courage — Mrs. Walton provides a calm center. When Marion declares that she and her aunt "seem to take kindly to bad weather," she speaks for both of them, and Mrs. Walton's composure throughout the gale confirms the claim. (002-008)

## Relationships

The bond between Mrs. Walton and Marion is one of the novel's quiet strengths. The two women have traveled and faced danger together before, and their mutual understanding provides emotional ballast for the expedition. While Marion forms a deeper connection with Lewis Ferrier, it is Mrs. Walton's steady chaperonage that makes the social arrangement possible within Victorian conventions.
