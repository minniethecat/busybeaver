# Departure of the Fleet

The **Departure of the Fleet** is a recurring event in James Runciman's *A Dream of the North Sea* (1876) — part logistical operation, part social ritual. The gathering and sailing of the great [[the-north-sea|North Sea]] trawling fleets punctuates the novel's structure, marking the transition from the relative safety of shore to the exposed [[peril-at-sea|dangers of the deep]]. Through these departures, Runciman conveys both the scale of the fishing industry and the human cost embedded in every voyage.

## The Gathering

Before departure comes assembly. The fleet does not sail as a single body from a single port — it must be gathered from the scattered fishing grounds where individual smacks have been working. After a gale, this gathering carries a particular urgency:

> "It'll take two days to get the fleet together, and then we shall hear more." (006-012)

The skipper's words, spoken after the storm has passed, carry a grim implication: "hearing more" means learning which vessels are missing, which crews have been lost. The gathering of the fleet is also a reckoning of the dead.

When the fleet does come together, Runciman captures the peculiar energy of the moment — the "merry, eldritch exchange of salutes" as vessels recognize one another, the shared relief of survival, the unspoken knowledge that some familiar smacks will not appear:

> In the thick of the fleet, the merry, eldritch exchange of salutes began. (016-008)

## The Medical Rush

One of the most striking departure-scenes involves not sailing but surgery. Before the fleet can depart, the [[mission-to-deep-sea-fishermen|Mission]] doctors must treat the accumulated injuries of the previous voyage — a grim pre-departure ritual of its own:

> The light failed soon, and the boats durst not hang about after the fleet began to sail; but, until the last minute, one long, slow, drizzle of misery seemed to fall like a dreary litany on the surgeon's nerves. The smashed fingers alone were painful to see, but there were other accidents much worse. Every man in the fleet had been compelled to fight desperately for life, and you cannot go through such a battle without risks. (017-005)

Runciman's language here is deliberate: the fleet has been through a "battle," the injuries are "casualties" in all but name, and the surgeon's work is triage. The departure of the fleet is thus shadowed by the departure of the previous fleet — and the injuries it carried home.

## The Sailing

When the fleet finally sails, the moment is rendered with something approaching awe:

> The fleet began to sail; but, until the last minute, one long, slow, drizzle of misery seemed to fall like a dreary litany on the surgeon's nerves. (017-005)

The phrase "the fleet began to sail" is deceptively simple. In Runciman's hands, it marks the moment when men pass from the world of human care into the world of wind and wave — from a realm where a surgeon can set a broken bone to one where a man washed overboard is simply gone.

The departure is also a moment of leave-taking, particularly poignant when [[lewis-ferrier|Lewis Ferrier]] watches the yacht carrying [[marion-dearsley|Marion Dearsley]] and the other [[mission-to-deep-sea-fishermen|Mission]] visitors pull away:

> The ladies waved their farewells, for people soon grow familiar and unconventional at sea. Blair shouted, "Lennard's a born hospital nurse, but he'll overfeed your patient." Then amid falling shades and hollow moaning of winds the yacht drove slowly away with her foresail still aweather, and the fleet hung around awaiting the admiral's final decision. (015-012)

## The Larger Pattern

The departure of the fleet is not a single event but a rhythm — a pattern that repeats with every voyage, each departure carrying the same mixture of hope and hazard. Runciman uses this rhythm to structure the novel itself: departures and returns, storms and calms, injury and treatment, death and survival.

| Phase | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Gathering | Scattered smacks assemble; the missing are noted |
| Medical rush | Mission doctors treat accumulated injuries before sailing |
| Leave-taking | Farewells exchanged; the yacht departs |
| The admiral's signal | Final decision; the fleet moves as one |
| Sailing | The transition from human care to the sea's indifference |

This rhythm underlies the novel's moral argument. Each departure is another round of risk for men who have no choice but to go — and each departure is therefore another claim on the conscience of those who stay safely ashore.
