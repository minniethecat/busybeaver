# Napoleon Bonaparte

## Biography

Napoleon Bonaparte (1769–1821) rose from Corsican minor nobility to become Emperor of the French and the dominant military-political figure of early 19th-century [[europe|Europe]]. For Nietzsche, Napoleon is not merely a historical figure but a **type** — the "great human being" who appears as a sudden, inexplicable eruption of strength against the grain of modern egalitarianism and moral mediocrity.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's Napoleon is the counter-ideal to the modern "good man," the [[jesus|Christ]]ian saint, and the democratic citizen. In *Skirmishes in a War with the Age*, Nietzsche treats Napoleon as evidence that greatness remains possible even in a decadent age:

> Take the case of Napoleon. France of the Revolution, which was the very reverse of Napoleon, nevertheless produced Napoleon. And because Napoleon was something different, something *other*, he justified the Revolution. (006-976–979)

The argument is characteristic of Nietzsche's dialectical thinking: the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]], which Nietzsche despised as the triumph of *ressentiment* and egalitarian ideology, is retrospectively *redeemed* by having produced Napoleon. The Revolution's chaos and leveling created the conditions from which a figure of unified will could emerge — not as the Revolution's fulfillment, but as its overcoming.

## The "Return to Nature"

Napoleon is Nietzsche's prime example of the *return to nature* — not [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s romantic return to primitive innocence, but a return to the ruthless, instinctive, affirmative core of human power:

> To speak in a parable: Napoleon was an example of a "return to nature" ... I am thinking of the *ens realissimum* surnamed Napoleon. (006-1121–1165)

This "return to nature" (*Rückkehr zur Natur*) is not regression but **ascent** — the recovery of instincts that civilization has suppressed, now wielded with the full force of accumulated cultural power. Napoleon is, for Nietzsche, the *ens realissimum* (the most real being) — a scholastic term repurposed to designate the supreme reality of a unified, commanding will.

Goethe, whom Nietzsche places alongside Napoleon as a fellow "great human being," conceived Napoleon as the embodiment of the daemonic — a force of nature operating through a human form. Nietzsche quotes Goethe approvingly: Napoleon was "one of the most productive human beings" (006-1165, context), a man whose very existence was a creative act.
