# Ugly

## Definition

For Nietzsche, ugliness (<span lang="de">Hässlichkeit</span>) is not a property of objects but a physiological judgment — the spontaneous verdict of the organism upon what weakens or threatens it. "Nothing is ugly save the degenerating man" [006-359]: ugliness is the aesthetic correlate of decline, the felt registration of life turning against itself. Conversely, beauty is the judgment of what enhances the feeling of power. This physiological aesthetics, developed most fully in "Skirmishes of an Untimely Man" (ch06), replaces the classical question "What is beauty?" with the genealogical question "What does the judgment 'beautiful' or 'ugly' express about the judge?"

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

Nietzsche's theory of ugliness appears primarily in §19–20 of "Skirmishes" (ch06), where he outlines a "physiology of aesthetics." The core argument is that aesthetic judgments are expressions of the will to power, not disinterested contemplation. "Everything ugly weakens and depresses man. It reminds him of decay, danger, impotence" [006-362]. The judgment "ugly" is the organism's recoil from what threatens its vitality.

Crucially, this means that ugliness is not opposed to beauty in any [[plato|Plato]]nic sense — there is no Form of the Ugly. Rather, ugliness is what Nietzsche calls a "symptom" of degeneration: the aesthetic expression of physiological decay. "Every sign of exhaustion, of heaviness, of age, of weariness... impels us to the judgment 'ugly'" [006-372]. The decadent person, whose instincts are already turned against life, finds beauty in what is morbund and ugly in what is healthy.

Nietzsche extends this analysis to morality: the moral condemnation of the world — the judgment that life is ugly, cruel, meaningless — is itself a symptom of physiological decline. "A certain hatred expresses itself here; who is it that is hated here? But there is no doubt: the *decline* of his type" [006-377]. The [[ascetic-ideal|ascetic priest]] who condemns earthly life as ugly reveals not the truth about the world but the truth about his own exhausted physiology.

The concept also appears in Nietzsche's critique of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity (ch10): the [[jesus|Christ]]ian God "makes everything seem ugly" [010-2105], revealing the anti-aesthetic, life-negating core of [[jesus|Christ]]ian morality. And in "Expeditions of an Untimely Man" (ch06 §47), Nietzsche notes that the artist reveals "much that is ugly, hard and questionable in life" [006-458] — not to condemn life but to affirm it in its totality.

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's physiological aesthetics of ugliness breaks decisively with the dominant tradition of philosophical aesthetics from Baumgarten through Kant. Where Kant defines the judgment of beauty as disinterested pleasure, Nietzsche insists that all aesthetic judgments are deeply interested — they are the organism's vital self-assessment. Ugliness is not a failure of form but a signal of threat.

This theory has significant consequences for art history. Nietzsche provides a framework for understanding why certain works are initially condemned as ugly (Manet, the Impressionists, early modernism) and later celebrated as beautiful: the initial judgment registers the threat of the new, while later assimilation indicates an expansion of the organism's power. Twentieth-century art's deliberate cultivation of the ugly (from Dada to Francis Bacon) can be read as a Nietzschean testing of the limits of affirmation.

Nietzsche's concept also informs the aesthetics of the sublime. Where Kant's sublime involves the triumph of reason over sensible threat, Nietzsche's ugly involves the triumph of the will to power over the threat of formlessness. The [[tragedy|tragic art]]ist who affirms even the ugly — who "says Yes to everything questionable" — achieves a kind of [[dionysian|Dionysian]] sublimity without rationalist consolation.
