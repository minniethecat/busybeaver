# Manu

## Biography

Manu is the legendary progenitor of humanity and lawgiver in Hindu tradition, to whom the *Mānava-Dharmaśāstra* (the *Laws of Manu* or *Manusmṛti*) is attributed. Composed between the second century BCE and the second century CE, the *Law-Book of Manu* codifies the *varṇa* system — the fourfold caste hierarchy of Brahmins (priests), Kṣatriyas (warriors), Vaiśyas (merchants), and Śūdras (labourers) — along with the ritual, social, and moral obligations of each station. For Nietzsche, who encountered the text through Louis Jacolliot's French translation, Manu represents not a historical individual but a **type**: the sacerdotal lawgiver who organises society as a hieratic order, grounding every prescription in divine authority and cosmic necessity.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's engagement with Manu is among the most sustained treatments of any non-[[europe|Europe]]an figure in his corpus. In *The "Improvers" of Mankind* (ch04 of *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*), he deploys the *Law-Book of Manu* as the definitive example of one of the two fundamental modes of "improving" humanity — the **morality of breeding** (*Züchtung*), contrasted with the **morality of taming** (*Zähmung*):

> Neither Manu, nor Plato, nor Confucius, nor the teachers of Judaism and Christianity, have ever doubted their right to falsehood. They have never doubted their right to quite a number of other things... To express oneself in a formula, one might say: — all means which have been used heretofore with the object of making man moral, were through and through immoral. [004-116]

The "pia fraus" — the pious fraud — is the common instrument of all "improvers." But what distinguishes Manu from [[jesus|Christ]]ianity is the *direction* of the fraud. Manu's deception serves order, hierarchy, and the cultivation of a noble type; [[jesus|Christ]]ianity's deception serves the inversion of all hierarchy, the triumph of the weak, the revenge of the Chandala.

Nietzsche quotes Manu directly on the treatment of the Chandala — the outcaste, the product of mixed unions:

> "The Chandala are the fruit of adultery, incest, and crime... Their clothes shall consist only of the rags torn from corpses, their vessels shall be the fragments of broken pottery, their ornaments shall be made of old iron, and their religion shall be the worship of evil spirits; without rest they shall wander from place to place." [004-084]

Nietzsche's commentary on this passage is characteristically double-edged. He acknowledges the "absolutely pure and primeval humanity of the Aryans" manifested in these regulations [004-084], while also noting that "the notion 'pure blood' is the reverse of harmless" [004-084]. The law-book is at once impressive — a magnificent act of legislative will — and terrible.

The crucial distinction Nietzsche draws is between Manu's Aryan legislation and [[jesus|Christ]]ianity's Jewish-Chandala counter-movement:

> Christianity as sprung from Jewish roots... represents the counter-movement against that morality of breeding, of race and of privilege: — it is essentially an anti-Aryan religion: Christianity is the transvaluation of all Aryan values, the triumph of Chandala values. [004-084]

In *The Antichrist* (published with *Twilight*), Nietzsche returns to Manu in a more personal register:

> When I read the law-book of Manu, an incomparably superior and more intellectual work... [010-2016]

The Law-Book of Manu becomes for Nietzsche the measure against which the New Testament is weighed and found to be "an evil odour" [004-060]. The contrast is between an aristocratic legislation that *creates* values through the assertion of hierarchy, and a slave morality that *inverts* values through *ressentiment*.

> A Law-Book like that of Manu comes into being like every good law-book: it epitomises the experience, the sagacity and the ethical experimentation of long centuries; it brings things to a conclusion; it no longer creates. [010-2050]

## Key Works

- *Mānava-Dharmaśāstra* (*The Laws of Manu*, c. 2nd century BCE–2nd century CE) — The foundational Hindu legal code, prescribing the duties and organisation of the four castes
- Nietzsche discusses the text primarily through Louis Jacolliot's French translation (*Les Législateurs religieux: Manou, Moïse, Mahomet*, 1876)

## Related Figures

- [[plato]] — the philosophical lawgiver of the West, grouped with Manu as an "improver" who never doubted his right to the *pia fraus*
- [[confucius]] — the Chinese sage whose ethical legislation Nietzsche places in the same category as Manu's Brahmanical code
- [[the-priesthood]] — the sacerdotal caste that Manu's law-book empowers and sanctifies
- [[jesus]] — the anti-Moses whose gospel of the poor Nietzsche reads as the triumph of Chandala values over Aryan order
