# Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

## Biography

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) was a German polymath — philosopher, mathematician, diplomat, and librarian — who, alongside Newton, invented calculus, and alongside Spinoza, defined the horizon of early modern rationalism. His philosophical system, built around the concepts of *monads* (indivisible, immaterial substances that constitute reality), *pre-established harmony* (the divinely coordinated alignment of mind and body), and the principle of sufficient reason, represents one of the most ambitious attempts in the Western tradition to render existence rationally intelligible. His *Theodicy* (1710) coined the term itself and argued — notoriously to Voltaire's Candide — that this is "the best of all possible worlds."

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's references to Leibniz in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* place him in a specific genealogy of German intellectual obstruction. In the opening of *The Antichrist* — included in the *Twilight* volume — Nietzsche reflects on what has held [[europe|Europe]] back from genuine intellectual liberation:

> Like Luther, and like Leibniz, Kant was one brake more. [010-224]

The word "brake" (*Hemmschuh*) is crucial. Leibniz is not an irrelevance or a fool — he is an impediment. His immense intellectual power was deployed in the service of metaphysical consolation: a rational system designed to prove that the world is good, that God is just, that everything happens for a reason. This, for Nietzsche, is philosophy as narcotic — the intellect used to fabricate comfort rather than to face reality.

In the same text, Nietzsche returns to Leibniz in a list of German achievements, but the list is charged with irony:

> The Reformation, Leibniz, Kant, and the so-called German philosophy... [010-2362]

The "so-called German philosophy" (*sogenannte deutsche Philosophie*) — Nietzsche's dismissive qualifier — implicates Leibniz retroactively. What the Germans call philosophy is, in Nietzsche's diagnosis, a sequence of elaborately constructed refusals to see the world as it is. Leibniz is the first link in this chain of evasion, followed by Kant's critical apparatus and the "so-called German philosophy" of the nineteenth century.

## Philosophical Significance

Nietzsche's critique of Leibniz extends beyond the explicit references. The principle of sufficient reason — the claim that nothing happens without a reason why it should be so rather than otherwise — is the epistemological foundation of the entire rationalist project Nietzsche seeks to dismantle. Nietzsche's perspectivism, his insistence that "there are no facts, only interpretations," is a direct inversion of Leibnizian rationalism. Where Leibniz sought to ground existence in logical necessity, Nietzsche uncovers beneath every "reason" the play of instincts, the will to power, and the contingent fabrications of human need.

## Key Works

- *Monadology* (1714) — The mature statement of Leibniz's metaphysical system of indivisible substances
- *Theodicy* (1710) — The philosophical defence of divine justice that coined the term and crystallised the optimism Nietzsche found insufferable
- *Discourse on [[metaphysics|Metaphysics]]* (1686) — An early synthesis of Leibniz's rationalist principles
