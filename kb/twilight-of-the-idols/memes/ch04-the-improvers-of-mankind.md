#### THE “IMPROVERS” OF MANKIND

[004-001] You are aware of my demand upon philosophers, that they should take

[004-002] up a stand Beyond Good and Evil,—that they should have the illusion

[004-003] of the moral judgment beneath them. This demand is the result of a

[004-004] point of view which I was the first to formulate:that there are no

[004-005] such things as moral facts.Moral judgment has this in common with

[004-006] the religious one, that it believes in realities which are not real.

[004-007] Morality is only an interpretation of certain phenomena: or, more

[004-008] strictly speaking, a misinterpretation of them. Moral judgment, like

[004-009] the religious one, belongs to a stage of ignorance in which even the

[004-010] concept of reality, the distinction between real and imagined things,

[004-011] is still lacking: so that truth, at such a stage, is applied to a host

[004-012] of things which to-day we call “imaginary.” That is why the moral

[004-013] judgment must never be taken quite literally: as such it is sheer

[004-014] nonsense. As a sign code, however, it is invaluable: to him at least

[004-015] who knows, it reveals the most valuable facts concerning cultures and

[004-016] inner conditions, which did not know enough to “understand” themselves.

[004-017] Morality is merely a sign-language, simply symptomatology: one must

[004-018] already know what it is all about in order to turn it to any use.

[004-019] Let me give you one example, quite provisionally. In all ages there

[004-020] have been people who wished to “improve” mankind: this above all

[004-021] is what was called morality. But the most different tendencies

[004-022] are concealed beneath the same word. Both the taming of the beast

[004-023] man, and the rearing of a particular type of man, have been called

[004-024] “improvement”: these zoologicaltermini,alone, represent real

[004-025] things—real things of which the typical “improver,” the priest,

[004-026] naturally knows nothing, and will know nothing. To call the taming

[004-027] of an animal “improving” it, sounds to our ears almost like a joke.

[004-028] He who knows what goes on in menageries, doubts very much whether an

[004-029] animal is improved in such places. It is certainly weakened, it is

[004-030] made less dangerous, and by means of the depressing influence of fear,

[004-031] pain, wounds, and hunger, it is converted into a sick animal. And the

[004-032] same holds good of the tamed man whom the priest has “improved.” In

[004-033] the early years of the Middle Ages, during which the Church was most

[004-034] distinctly and above all a menagerie, the most beautiful examples of

[004-035] the “blond beast” were hunted down in all directions,—the noble

[004-036] Germans, for instance, were “improved.” But what did this “improved”

[004-037] German, who had been lured to the monastery look like after the

[004-038] process? He looked like a caricature of man, like an abortion: he had

[004-039] become a “sinner,” he was caged up, he had been imprisoned behind a

[004-040] host of apparling notions. He now lay there, sick, wretched, malevolent

[004-041] even toward himself: full of hate for theinstincts of life, full of

[004-042] suspicion in regard to all that is still strong and happy. In short a

[004-043] “Christian.” In physiological terms: in a fight with an animal, the

[004-044] only way of making it weak may be to make it sick. The Church undersood

[004-045] this: it ruined man, it made him weak,—but it laid claim to having

[004-046] “improved” him.

[004-047] Now let us consider the other case which is called morality, the

[004-048] case of the rearing of a particular race and species. The most

[004-049] magnificent example of this is offered by Indian morality, and is

[004-050] sanctioned religiously as the “Law of Manu.” In this book the task

[004-051] is set of rearing no less than four races at once: a priestly race,

[004-052] a warrior race, a merchant and agricultural race, and finally a race

[004-053] of servants—the Sudras. It is quite obvious that we are no longer

[004-054] in a circus watching tamers of wild animals in this book. To have

[004-055] conceived even the plan of such a breeding scheme, presupposes the

[004-056] existence of a man who is a hundred times milder and more reasonable

[004-057] than the mere lion-tamer. One breathes more freely, after stepping out

[004-058] of the Christian atmosphere of hospitals and prisons, into this more

[004-059] salubrious, loftier and more spacious world. What a wretched thing the

[004-060] New Testament is beside Manu, what an evil odour hangs around it!—But

[004-061] even this organisation found it necessary to be terrible,—not this

[004-062] time in a struggle with the animal-man, but with his opposite, the

[004-063] non-caste man, the hotch-potch man, the Chandala. And once again it

[004-064] had no other means of making him weak and harmless, than by making

[004-065] him sick,—it was the struggle with the greatest“number.” Nothing

[004-066] perhaps is more offensive to our feelings than these measures of

[004-067] security on the part of Indian morality. The third edict, for instance

[004-068] (Avadana-Sastra I.), which treats “of impure vegetables,” ordains that

[004-069] the only nourishment that the Chandala should be allowed must consist

[004-070] of garlic and onions, as the holy scriptures forbid their being given

[004-071] corn or grain-bearing fruit, water and fire. The same edict declares

[004-072] that the water which they need must be drawn neither out of rivers,

[004-073] wells or ponds, but only out of the ditches leading to swamps and out

[004-074] of the holes left by the footprints of animals. They are likewise

[004-075] forbidden to wash either their linen or themselves since the water

[004-076] which is graciously granted to them must only be used for quenching

[004-077] their thirst. Finally Sudra women are forbidden to assist Chandala

[004-078] women at their confinements, while Chandala women are also forbidden to

[004-079] assist each other at such times. The results of sanitary regulations of

[004-080] this kind could not fail to make themselves felt; deadly epidemics and

[004-081] the most ghastly venereal diseases soon appeared, and in consequence

[004-082] of these again “the Law of the Knife,”—that is to say circumcision,

[004-083] was prescribed for male children and the removal of the small labia

[004-084] from the females. Manu himself says: “the Chandala are the fruit of

[004-085] adultery, incest, and crime (—this is the necessary consequence of the

[004-086] idea of breeding). Their clothes shall consist only of the rags torn

[004-087] from corpses, their vessels shall be the fragments of broken pottery,

[004-088] their ornaments shall be made of old iron, and their religion shall be

[004-089] the worship of evil spirits; without rest they shall wander from place

[004-090] to place.They are forbidden to write from left to right or to use

[004-091] their right hand in writing: the use of the right hand and writing from

[004-092] left to right are reserved to people of virtue, to people of race.”

[004-093] These regulations are instructive enough: we can see in them the

[004-094] absolutely pure and primeval humanity of the Aryans,—we learn that

[004-095] the notion “pure blood,” is the reverse of harmless. On the other hand

[004-096] it becomes clear among which people the hatred, the Chandala hatred

[004-097] of this humanity has been immortalised, among which people it has

[004-098] become religion and genius. From this point of view the gospels are

[004-099] documents of the highest value; and the Book of Enoch is still more

[004-100] so. Christianity as sprung from Jewish roots and comprehensible only

[004-101] as grown upon this soil, represents the counter-movement against that

[004-102] morality of breeding, of race and of privilege:—it is essentially an

[004-103] anti-Aryan religion: Christianity is the transvaluation of all Aryan

[004-104] values, the triumph of Chandala values, the proclaimed gospel of the

[004-105] poor and of the low, the general insurrection of all the down-trodden,

[004-106] the wretched, the bungled and the botched, against the “race,”—the

[004-107] immortal revenge of the Chandala as thereligion of love.

[004-108] The morality of breeding and the morality of taming, in the means which

[004-109] they adopt in order to prevail, are quite worthy of each other: we may

[004-110] lay down as a leading principle that in order to create morality a

[004-111] man must have the absolute will toimmorality. This is the great and

[004-112] strange problem with which I have so long been occupied: the psychology

[004-113] of the “Improvers” of mankind. A small, and at bottom perfectly

[004-114] insignificant fact, known as the “pia fraus,” first gave me access

[004-115] to this problem: thepia fraus,the heirloom of all philosophers and

[004-116] priests who “improve” mankind. Neither Manu, nor [[plato|Plato]], nor Confucius,

[004-117] nor the teachers of Judaism and Christianity, have ever doubted their

[004-118] right to falsehood. They have never doubted their right to quite a

[004-119] number of other things To express oneself in a formula, one might

[004-120] say:—all means which have been used heretofore with the object of

[004-121] making man moral, were through and through immoral.
