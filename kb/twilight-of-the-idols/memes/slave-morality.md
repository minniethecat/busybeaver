# Slave Morality

## Definition

Slave morality (Sklavenmoral) is Nietzsche's term for the value system that arises from the resentment (ressentiment) of the weak against the strong. Unlike master morality — which affirms its own values directly — slave morality defines itself reactively: it calls "good" whatever opposes and negates the powerful. Pity, humility, and equality become virtues; pride, strength, and self-assertion become vices.

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

Nietzsche identifies [[jesus|Christ]]ianity as the paradigmatic expression of slave morality. In Chapter 6, he traces how "the movement of decadence in morality" (006-760) transformed Western values. The "improvers of mankind" (Chapter 4) are those who, through moral training, have tamed and weakened humanity.

The specific mechanism is the inversion of values: what the strong call good, the weak recode as evil. This is the "slave revolt in morality" that Nietzsche first described in *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morals* and elaborates throughout *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*.

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's genealogy of slave morality has been one of his most influential and controversial ideas, shaping 20th-century critiques of religion, democracy, and humanitarianism. Foucault's analysis of power-knowledge and Deleuze's reading of Nietzsche both extend this framework.
