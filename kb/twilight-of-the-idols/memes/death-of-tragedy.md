# Death of Tragedy

## Overview

The **Death of Tragedy** refers to the demise of Greek tragedy as a living art form in fifth-century BCE [[athens|Athens]], which Nietzsche diagnoses as the result of [[socratic-turn|Socratic rationalism]] displacing the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] [[soul|spirit]]. In *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, Nietzsche returns to this theme — first developed in his early work *The Birth of [[tragedy|Tragedy]]* (1872) — as the foundational event of his critical genealogy of Western culture.

## Historical Context

[[tragedy|Greek tragedy]] reached its zenith with Aeschylus and Sophocles in the fifth century BCE. It was born, Nietzsche argues, from the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] impulse — the ecstatic, orgiastic affirmation of life in its totality, including suffering and destruction. The tragic chorus and the music-driven structure of early tragedy gave voice to what Nietzsche calls "the psychology of orgiasm conceived as the feeling of a superabundance of vitality and strength, within the scope of which even pain acts as a stimulus" (007-175–177).

This art form did not die of natural causes. Nietzsche identifies two "murderers": **Euripides**, who brought the rationalistic [[soul|spirit]] of the Sophists onto the stage, replacing the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] music with Socratic dialectics; and **[[socrates-person|Socrates]]**, the archetype of the "theoretical man" who held that "everything must be conscious in order to be beautiful." Together, they transformed tragedy from a [[dionysian|Dionysian]] festival of life-affirmation into a moralizing vehicle — a shift Nietzsche would later characterize as the original sin of Western aesthetics.

## Nietzsche's Interpretation

In "Skirmishes in a War with the Age" (ch06), Nietzsche critiques [[schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]]'s interpretation of tragedy — and, by extension, the entire post-Socratic tradition of aesthetic theory:

> According to Schopenhauer's doctrine, the general object of art was to "free one from the Will"; and what he honoured as the great utility of tragedy, was that it "made people more resigned." — But this, as I have already shown, is a pessimistic standpoint; it is the "evil eye". (006-462–464)

Against this moralizing, "purgative" reading of tragedy — which Nietzsche traces back through Schopenhauer to [[aristotle|Aristotle]], who "regarded tragedy as a purgative" (010-146) — Nietzsche insists on tragedy's fundamentally affirmative character:

> The martial elements in our soul celebrate their Saturnalia in tragedy; he who is used to suffering, he who looks out for suffering, the heroic man, extols his existence by means of tragedy — to him alone does the tragic artist offer this cup of sweetest cruelty. (006-475–478)

This is not resignation but **triumph**. The tragic hero does not learn to accept suffering; he *wills* it. [[tragedy|Tragedy]] is the art form of the [[will-to-power|will to power]] — the affirmation of life even in its most terrible aspects.

In "Things I Owe to the Ancients" (ch07), Nietzsche returns to the question of tragedy and explicitly ties it to his own philosophical project:

> The saying of Yea to life, including even its most strange and most terrible problems, the will to life rejoicing over its own inexhaustibleness in the sacrifice of its highest types — this is what I called Dionysian, this is what I divined as the bridge leading to the psychology of the tragic poet. (007-182–186)

And crucially:

> With this I once more come into touch with the spot from which I once set out — the "Birth of Tragedy" was my first transvaluation of all values. (007-192–193)

The [[socratic-turn|death of tragedy]] is thus not merely an art-historical event; it is the originating trauma of Nietzsche's entire philosophical project. The task of *recovering* the tragic — of restoring the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] affirmation of life against two millennia of Socratic-[[jesus|Christ]]ian nihilism — defines the positive dimension of Nietzsche's thought.

## Significance

The [[socratic-turn|death of tragedy]] is significant on multiple levels within Nietzsche's philosophy:

1. **As a diagnostic event**: It marks the moment when Greek instinct turned against itself. The "murder" of tragedy by Euripidean rationalism and Socratic optimism is the paradigm case of [[decadence]] — the triumph of the rational over the instinctual, of consciousness over the body, of morality over life.

2. **As the origin of Western aesthetics**: Nietzsche traces the entire tradition of post-Aristotelian art theory — art as catharsis, as moral instruction, as escape from the will — back to this single event. The "[[socratic-turn|death of tragedy]]" inaugurates an aesthetics of weakness that Nietzsche's own philosophy aims to overcome.

3. **As Nietzsche's starting point**: Nietzsche explicitly identifies *The Birth of [[tragedy|Tragedy]]* (his first book) as his "first transvaluation of all values" (007-192–193). The [[socratic-turn|death of tragedy]] is the historical event whose interpretation launched his mature philosophical project.

4. **As counterpoint to [[jesus|Christ]]ianity**: The [[socratic-turn|death of tragedy]] prefigures the rise of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity — another "murder" of [[dionysian|Dionysian]] life-affirmation by Socratic-moralistic rationalism, culminating in what Nietzsche calls the "death of God" (see [[death-of-god-event|The Death of God]]).
