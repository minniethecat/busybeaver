# The Socratic Turn

## Overview

The **Socratic Turn** is Nietzsche's term for the decisive cultural transformation in ancient Greece when the rationalistic, dialectical method associated with [[socrates|Socrates]] displaced the [[tragedy|tragic worldview]] embodied in Aeschylean and Sophoclean tragedy. In *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, Nietzsche treats this not merely as a change in intellectual fashion but as a symptom of physiological and cultural [[decadence]] — the moment when Greek instinct began to turn against itself.

## Historical Context

The historical [[socrates-person|Socrates]] (c. 470–399 BCE) emerged in [[athens|Athens]] at a moment of cultural crisis: the Peloponnesian War, the collapse of traditional aristocratic values, and the rise of the Sophists. Against this backdrop, [[socrates-person|Socrates]] introduced a new form of intellectual practice — dialectics — which Nietzsche characterizes as a symptom of decline rather than an advance:

> What was the ultimate outcome of this philosophic eroticism of Plato's? A new art-form of the Greek Agon, dialectics. (006-432)

For Nietzsche, the "new art-form" of dialectics is not a development *within* Greek culture but a rupture that would eventually destroy it. The Socratic demand that everything be made conscious, rational, and dialectically defensible represented an attack on the instinctual basis of Greek greatness — the very instinct that had produced [[dionysian|tragedy]].

## Nietzsche's Interpretation

Nietzsche's most concentrated treatment of the Socratic turn in *Twilight* appears in his critique of Schopenhauer's interpretation of tragedy. Schopenhauer, following a line of thought Nietzsche traces back to [[socrates-person|Socrates]], valued tragedy for making people "more resigned" — as a kind of sedative (006-462–463). Against this, Nietzsche insists:

> The martial elements in our soul celebrate their Saturnalia in tragedy; he who is used to suffering, he who looks out for suffering, the heroic man, extols his existence by means of tragedy — to him alone does the tragic artist offer this cup of sweetest cruelty. (006-475–478)

The Socratic turn, then, is the moment when tragedy ceased to be an **affirmation** of existence (including its suffering) and became a **moral lesson** — a "purgative" (010-146). This reinterpretation of tragedy as moral instruction is, for Nietzsche, the original sin of Western aesthetics: the subordination of art to reason, of instinct to dialectics, of life to the "[[truth|will to truth]]."

## Significance

The Socratic turn is one of the pivotal events in Nietzsche's philosophy of history. It represents:

1. **The death of tragedy**: The [[tragedy|tragic art]] form that had been the supreme achievement of Greek culture was "murdered" by Euripidean rationalism and Socratic optimism — the belief that thought can penetrate the deepest abysses of being and not only know them but *correct* them.

2. **The birth of the theoretical man**: [[socrates-person|Socrates]] inaugurates the type of the "theoretical optimist" who believes that knowledge can make existence justified — the ancestor of the modern scientist, the scholar, and the philosopher who places truth above life.

3. **The conditions for [[jesus|Christ]]ianity**: By establishing the primacy of reason over instinct and of morality over life-affirmation, the Socratic turn prepared the ground for [[ascetic-ideal|Christianity's]] more radical life-denial.

The event thus stands at the origin of the "[[reality|true world]]" that Nietzsche will spend his career dismantling — see [[idol|the history of an error]] for the full trajectory from [[plato|Plato]] through [[jesus|Christ]]ianity to the [[nihilism|death of God]].
