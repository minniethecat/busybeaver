# France

## Overview

France occupies a complex position in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*: it is simultaneously a source of genuine cultural achievement (the classical 17th century, [[napoleon|Napoleon]]) and a case study in modern degeneration (revolutionary democracy, journalistic culture). Nietzsche, who spent considerable time in France and wrote favorably of French *esprit*, treats French culture as a diagnostic field — a place where the tensions of [[europe|Europe]]an modernity are particularly visible.

## In Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's references to France span both praise and critique. On the positive side, he admires "the seventeenth century in France" (006-1089) — the age of Corneille, Racine, and the *honnête homme* — as a high point of aristocratic taste. [[napoleon|Napoleon]] appears repeatedly as the "synthesis of the *Unmensch* and *Übermensch*" — a figure who momentarily transcended the mediocrity of his age.

On the critical side, Nietzsche associates modern France with democratic leveling:

> France of the Revolution... was being smashed to atoms (006-981)

And with a certain psychological exhaustion:

> In France almost all psychological and... (005-098)

The "sick France" (006-027) of the 19th century — a France of newspapers, democracy, and *petit bourgeois* values — serves as Nietzsche's exhibit of a great culture in decline.

## Historical Significance

France was the intellectual capital of [[europe|Europe]] for much of the 17th and 18th centuries, and Nietzsche's engagement with French culture reflects his broader critique of modernity. The [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] (see [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]) represents the political triumph of the "slave revolt in morals" Nietzsche traces to [[jesus|Christ]]ianity.
