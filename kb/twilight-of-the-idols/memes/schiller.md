# Friedrich Schiller

## Biography

Friedrich Schiller (1759–1805) was a German poet, philosopher, and playwright, best known for his dramas and his collaboration with Goethe in Weimar Classicism. For Nietzsche, Schiller represents a cultural problem: the German public's inability to distinguish between moralizing art and genuine greatness.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's treatment of Schiller in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* is brief but devastating. He places Schiller among his "Impossible People":

> Schiller, or the moral trumpeter of Säckingen. [006-002]

The epithet "moral trumpeter of Säckingen" (*Moral-Trompeter von Säckingen*) is a satirical reference — Säckingen being a small German town associated with provincial piety. The phrase captures Nietzsche's central critique: Schiller's art is essentially moralistic, a trumpet blast for virtue rather than a genuine artistic achievement.

### The "Goethe and Schiller" Problem

Nietzsche's deeper complaint concerns how Schiller's reputation distorts the German understanding of greatness. The conventional pairing "Goethe and Schiller" offends him:

> When people say "Goethe and Schiller" — I even fear that they say "Schiller and Goethe." ... Has nobody found Schiller out yet? [006-297–298]

The possibility that Schiller might be named *before* Goethe — that the moral trumpeter might rank above the genuinely great artist — is for Nietzsche a symptom of German cultural blindness. Goethe, in Nietzsche's estimation, stands alone; Schiller is a lesser figure whose moral seriousness appeals to the German taste for edification over art.

Schiller's famous line from *Jungfrau von Orleans* — "Mit der Dummheit kämpfen Götter selbst vergebens" (Against stupidity, the gods themselves struggle in vain) [010-2446–2448] — is parodied by Nietzsche, suggesting that even Schiller's most quoted lines serve as vehicles for moralizing rather than genuine insight.

### The Contrast with Goethe

The Schiller-Goethe contrast is essential to Nietzsche's argument in *Skirmishes*. Where [[goethe|Goethe]] represents the affirmative, life-embracing [[soul|spirit]] — the "return to nature" as ascent — Schiller represents the moralizing impulse that Nietzsche associates with German idealism at its worst. Goethe creates; Schiller preaches. The German public, unable to tell the difference, pairs them as equals.

Nietzsche's rhetorical question — "Has nobody found Schiller out yet?" — is a challenge to his readers: can you see what I see, that Schiller's reputation is inflated by the very moral seriousness that makes his art second-rate?

## Key Works

- *The Robbers* (1781) — his first play, a Sturm und Drang drama of rebellion
- *Don Carlos* (1787) — historical drama of political idealism
- *Wallenstein* trilogy (1799) — his major dramatic achievement
- *On the Aesthetic Education of Man* (1795) — his major philosophical work on art and morality
