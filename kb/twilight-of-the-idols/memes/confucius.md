# Confucius

## Biography

Confucius (551–479 BCE), known in Chinese as Kongzi ("Master Kong"), was a philosopher and political thinker of the State of Lu during the Eastern Zhou dynasty. After a modest career in government, he spent years travelling from state to state offering counsel to rulers, before returning to Lu to teach and edit the classical texts that would become the Confucian canon — the *Five Classics* and the *Analects* (*Lunyu*), the latter compiled by his disciples after his death. His teaching centred on *ren* (benevolence, humaneness), *li* (ritual propriety), and *xiao* (filial piety), establishing an ethical framework grounded in reciprocal obligation, self-cultivation, and the harmonious ordering of social relations. For over two millennia, Confucianism shaped the political and moral culture of East Asia as profoundly as [[jesus|Christ]]ianity shaped [[europe|Europe]].

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's two references to Confucius in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* place him in impeccable company — and it is precisely the company that constitutes the judgment. In *The "Improvers" of Mankind* (ch04), Confucius appears in a list of the great moral legislators of world history:

> Neither Manu, nor Plato, nor Confucius, nor the teachers of Judaism and Christianity, have ever doubted their right to falsehood. [004-116]

The statement is at once a condemnation and a form of recognition. To be grouped with [[manu|Manu]], [[plato|Plato]], and the founders of [[judaism|Judaism]] and [[jesus|Christ]]ianity is to be acknowledged as a world-historical force in the "improvement" of mankind. The common instrument of all these figures — the *pia fraus*, the pious fraud — is the lie told in the service of a moral order. Confucius, like his peers, "never doubted" his right to employ it.

The second reference, in *The Antichrist* (ch10), expands the genealogy:

> Confucius, to the law-book of Manu, to Muhamed, and to the Christian Church. [010-2007]

Here Confucius is placed at the head of a chain of religious-legal authorities that culminates in the [[the-church|Christian Church]]. The sequence is not chronological but typological: each represents a form of priestly legislation, the imposition of a moral interpretation upon existence.

What Nietzsche withholds is as significant as what he states. Unlike [[socrates-person|Socrates]], [[plato|Plato]], or Paul — figures he subjects to extended polemic — Confucius receives no detailed critique. The silence may reflect Nietzsche's limited knowledge of Chinese philosophy (his sources were primarily second-hand [[europe|Europe]]an accounts). But the placement of Confucius alongside [[manu|Manu]] and [[plato|Plato]] suggests that Nietzsche understood him as belonging to the same type: the aristocratic lawgiver whose legislation aims at *order*, not *truth*, and whose greatness consists in the creative power of his moral vision rather than its epistemological warrant.

## Philosophical Significance

Nietzsche's brief treatment of Confucius illuminates his broader philosophy of the "improver." For Nietzsche, all moral systems are impositions — acts of creative will that interpret existence according to a particular table of values. The question is not whether a morality is *true* (no morality is true in that sense) but what *type* of life it serves. Confucius, like [[manu|Manu]], legislates for order and hierarchy — a morality of breeding rather than taming. His inclusion in Nietzsche's pantheon of world-historical legislators is, within Nietzsche's own terms, a form of respect, even if the objects of that respect are also the targets of his critique.

## Key Works

- *The Analects* (*Lunyu*, compiled c. 5th century BCE) — The collection of sayings and dialogues attributed to Confucius and his disciples, the foundational text of Confucian ethics
- *The Five Classics* (*Wujing*) — The corpus of ancient Chinese texts edited and transmitted by Confucius, including the *Book of Documents*, *Book of Songs*, and *Spring and Autumn Annals*

## Related Figures

- [[manu]] — the Hindu lawgiver with whom Nietzsche explicitly groups Confucius as a fellow "improver" who never doubted the *pia fraus*

- [[socrates]] — the Athenian whose dialectical method, in Nietzsche's reading, inaugurated the decline of the tragic age; Confucius represents a different path — legislation without dialectics
