#### THINGS I OWE TO THE ANCIENTS

[007-001] In conclusion I will just say a word concerning that world to which I

[007-002] have sought new means of access, to which I may perhaps have found a

[007-003] new passage—the ancient world. My taste, which is perhaps the reverse

[007-004] of tolerant, is very far from saying yea through and through even to

[007-005] this world: on the whole it is not over eager to sayYea,it would

[007-006] prefer to sayNay,and better still nothing whatever.... This is true

[007-007] of whole cultures; it is true of books,—it is also true of places

[007-008] and of landscapes. Truth to tell, the number of ancient books that

[007-009] count for something in my life is but small; and the most famous are

[007-010] not of that number. My sense of style, for the epigram as style, was

[007-011] awakened almost spontaneously upon my acquaintance with Sallust. I have

[007-012] not forgotten the astonishment of my respected teacher Corssen, when

[007-013] he was forced to give his worst Latin pupil the highest marks,—at one

[007-014] stroke I had learned all there was to learn. Condensed, severe, with as

[007-015] much substance as possible in the background, and with cold but roguish

[007-016] hostility towards all “beautiful words” and “beautiful feelings”—in

[007-017] these things I found my own particular bent. In my writings up to my

[007-018] “[[zarathustra|Zarathustra]],” there will be found a very earnest ambition to attain

[007-019] to theRomanstyle, tothe “ære perennius” in style.—The same

[007-020] thing happened on my first acquaintance with Horace. Up to the present

[007-021] no poet has given me the same artistic raptures as those which from

[007-022] the first I received from an Horatian ode. In certain languages it

[007-023] would be absurd even to aspire to what is accomplished by this poet.

[007-024] This mosaic of words, in which every unit spreads its power to the

[007-025] left and to the right over the whole, by its sound, by its place in

[007-026] the sentence, and by its meaning, thisminimumin the compass and

[007-027] number of the signs, and themaximumof energy in the signs which is

[007-028] thereby achieved—all this is Roman, and, if you will believe me, noblepar excellence.By the side of this all the rest of poetry becomes

[007-029] something popular,—nothing more than senseless sentimental twaddle.

[007-030] I am not indebted to the Greeks for anything like such strong

[007-031] impressions; and, to speak frankly, they cannot be to us what the

[007-032] Romans are. One cannotlearnfrom the Greeks—their style is too

[007-033] strange, it is also too fluid, to be imperative or to have the effect

[007-034] of a classic. Who would ever have learnt writing from a Greek! Who

[007-035] would ever have learned it without the Romans!... Do not let anyone

[007-036] suggest [[plato|Plato]] to me. In regard to Plato I am a thorough sceptic, and

[007-037] have never been able to agree to the admiration of Plato theartist,which is traditional among scholars. And after all, in this matter,

[007-038] the most refined judges of taste in antiquity are on my side. In my

[007-039] opinion Plato bundles all the forms of style pell-mell together,

[007-040] in this respect he is oneof the first decadents of style: he has

[007-041] something similar on his conscience to that which the Cynics had

[007-042] who invented thesatura Menippea.For the Platonic dialogue—this

[007-043] revoltingly self-complacent and childish kind of dialectics—to

[007-044] exercise any charm over you, you must never have read any good French

[007-045] authors,—Fontenelle for instance. Plato is boring. In reality my

[007-046] distrust of Plato is fundamental. I find him so very much astray

[007-047] from all the deepest instincts of the Hellenes, so steeped in moral

[007-048] prejudices, so pre-existently Christian—the concept “good” is already

[007-049] the highest value with him,—that rather than use any other expression

[007-050] I would prefer to designate the whole phenomenon Plato with the hard

[007-051] word “superior bunkum,” or, if you would like it better, “idealism.”

[007-052] Humanity has had to pay dearly for this Athenian having gone to school

[007-053] among the Egyptians (—or among the Jews in Egypt?...) In the great

[007-054] fatality of Christianity, Plato is that double-faced fascination

[007-055] called the “ideal,” which made it possible for the more noble natures

[007-056] of antiquity to misunderstand themselves and to tread thebridgewhich led to the “cross.” And what an amount of Plato is still to be

[007-057] found in the concept “church,” and in the construction, the system

[007-058] and the practice of the church!—My recreation, my predilection, my

[007-059] cure, after all Platonism, has always been Thucydides. Thucydides and

[007-060] perhaps Machiavelli’sprincipeare most closely related to me owing

[007-061] to the absolute determination which they show of refusing to deceive

[007-062] themselves and of seeing reason inreality,—not in “rationality,”

[007-063] and still less in “morality.” There is no more radical cure than

[007-064] Thucydides for the lamentablyrose-coloured idealisation of the

[007-065] Greeks which the “classically-cultured” stripling bears with him into

[007-066] life, as a reward for his public school training. His writings must be

[007-067] carefully studied line by line, and his unuttered thoughts must be read

[007-068] as distinctly as what he actually says. There are few thinkers so rich

[007-069] in unuttered thoughts. In him the culture “of the Sophists”—that is to

[007-070] say, the culture of realism, receives its most perfect expression: this

[007-071] inestimable movement in the midst of the moral and idealistic knavery

[007-072] of the Socratic Schools which was then breaking out in all directions.

[007-073] Greek philosophy is the decadence of the Greek instinct: Thucydides is

[007-074] the great summing up, the final manifestation of that strong, severe

[007-075] positivism which lay in the instincts of the ancient Hellene. After

[007-076] all, it is courage in the face of reality that distinguishes such

[007-077] natures as Thucydides from Plato: Plato is a coward in the face of

[007-078] reality—consequently he takes refuge in the ideal: Thucydides is

[007-079] master of himself,—consequently he is able to master life.

[007-080] To rout up cases of “beautiful souls,” “golden means” and other

[007-081] perfections among the Greeks, to admire, say, their calm grandeur,

[007-082] their ideal attitude of mind, their exalted simplicity—from this

[007-083] “exalted simplicity,” which after all is a piece ofniaiserie

[007-084] allemande,I was preserved by the psychologist within me. I saw their

[007-085] strongest instinct, the [[will-to-power|Will to Power]], I saw them quivering with the

[007-086] fierce violence of this instinct,—I saw all their institutions grow

[007-087] out of measures of security calculated to preserveeach member of

[007-088] their society from the innerexplosive materialthat lay in his

[007-089] neighbour’s breast. This enormous internal tension thus discharged

[007-090] itself in terrible and reckless hostility outside the state: the

[007-091] various states mutually tore each other to bits, in order that each

[007-092] individual state could remain at peace with itself. It was then

[007-093] necessary to be strong; for danger lay close at hand,—it lurked in

[007-094] ambush everywhere. The superb suppleness of their bodies, the daring

[007-095] realism and immorality which is peculiar to the Hellenes, was a

[007-096] necessity not an inherent quality. It was a result, it had not been

[007-097] there from the beginning. Even their festivals and their arts were but

[007-098] means in producing a feeling of superiority, and of showing it: they

[007-099] are measures of self-glorification; and in certain circumstances of

[007-100] making one’s self terrible.... Fancy judging the Greeks in the German

[007-101] style, from their philosophers; fancy using the suburban respectability

[007-102] of the Socratic schools as a key to what is fundamentally Hellenic!...

[007-103] The philosophers are of course the decadents of Hellas, the

[007-104] counter-movement directed against the old and noble taste(—against the

[007-105] agonal instinct, against thePolls,against the value of the race,

[007-106] against the authority of tradition), Socratic virtues were preached to

[007-107] the Greeks,becausethe Greeks had lost virtue: irritable, cowardly,

[007-108] unsteady, and all turned to play-actors, they had more than sufficient

[007-109] reason to submit to having morality preached to them. Not that it

[007-110] helped them in any way; but great words and attitudes are so becoming

[007-111] to decadents.

[007-112] I was the first who, in order to understand the ancient, still rich and

[007-113] even superabundant Hellenic instinct, took that marvellous phenomenon,

[007-114] which bears the name of Dionysus, seriously: it can be explained only

[007-115] as a manifestation of excessive energy. Whoever had studied the Greeks,

[007-116] as that most profound of modern connoisseurs of their culture, Jakob

[007-117] Burckhardt of Bâle, had done, knew at once that something had been

[007-118] achieved by means of this interpretation. And in his “Cultur der

[007-119] Griechen” Burckhardt inserted a special chapter on the phenomenon

[007-120] in question. If you would like a glimpse of the other side, you have

[007-121] only to refer to the almost laughable poverty of instinct among German

[007-122] philologists when they approach the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] question. The celebrated

[007-123] Lobeck, especially, who with the venerable assurance of a worm dried up

[007-124] between books, crawled into this world of mysterious states, succeeded

[007-125] inconvincing himself that he was scientific, whereas he was simply

[007-126] revoltingly superficial and childish,—Lobeck, with all the pomp of

[007-127] profound erudition, gave us to understand that, as a matter of fact,

[007-128] there was nothing at all in all these curiosities. Truth to tell, the

[007-129] priests may well have communicated not a few things of value to the

[007-130] participators in such orgies; for instance, the fact that wine provokes

[007-131] desire, that man in certain circumstances lives on fruit, that plants

[007-132] bloom in the spring and fade in the autumn. As regards the astounding

[007-133] wealth of rites, symbols and myths which take their origin in the orgy,

[007-134] and with which the world of antiquityis literally smothered, Lobeck

[007-135] finds that it prompts him to a feat of even greater ingenuity than

[007-136] the foregoing phenomenon did. “The Greeks,” he says, (Aglaophamus,I. p. 672), “when they had nothing better to do, laughed, sprang and

[007-137] romped about, or, inasmuch as men also like a change at times, they

[007-138] would sit down, weep and bewail their lot. Others then came up who tried

[007-139] to discover some reason for this strange behaviour; and thus, as an

[007-140] explanation of these habits, there arose an incalculable number of

[007-141] festivals, legends, and myths. On the other hand it was believed that

[007-142] thefarcical performanceswhich then perchance began to take place

[007-143] on festival days, necessarily formed part of the celebrations, and

[007-144] they were retained as an indispensable part of the ritual.”—This is

[007-145] contemptible nonsense, and no one will take a man like Lobeck seriously

[007-146] for a moment We are very differently affected when we examine the

[007-147] notion “Hellenic,” as Winckelmann and Goethe conceived it, and find it

[007-148] incompatible with that element out of which Dionysian art springs—I

[007-149] speak of orgiasm. In reality I do not doubt that Goethe would have

[007-150] completely excluded any such thing from the potentialities of the Greek

[007-151] soul.Consequently Goethe did not understand the Greeks.For it is

[007-152] only in the Dionysian mysteries, in the psychology of the Dionysian

[007-153] state, that thefundamental factof the Hellenic instinct—its “will

[007-154] to life”—is expressed. What did the Hellene secure himself with these

[007-155] mysteries?Eternallife, the eternal recurrence of life; the future

[007-156] promised and hallowed in the past; the triumphant Yea to life despite

[007-157] death and change; real life conceived as the collective prolongation

[007-158] oflife through procreation, through the mysteries of sexuality.

[007-159] To the Greeks, the symbol of sex was the most venerated of symbols,

[007-160] the really deep significance of all the piety of antiquity. All the

[007-161] details of the act of procreation, pregnancy and birth gave rise to

[007-162] the loftiest and most solemn feelings. In the doctrine of mysteries,painwas pronounced holy: the “pains of childbirth” sanctify pain in

[007-163] general,—all becoming and all growth, everything that guarantees the

[007-164] futureinvolvespain.... In order that there may be eternal joy in

[007-165] creating, in order that the will to life may say Yea to itself in all

[007-166] eternity, the “pains of childbirth” must also be eternal. All this is

[007-167] what the word Dionysus signifies: I know of no higher symbolism than

[007-168] this Greek symbolism, this symbolism of the Dionysian phenomenon. In

[007-169] it the profoundest instinct of life, the instinct that guarantees the

[007-170] future of life and life eternal, is understood religiously,—the road

[007-171] to life itself, procreation, is pronouncedholy,... It was only

[007-172] Christianity which, with its fundamental resentment against life, made

[007-173] something impure out of sexuality: it flungfilthat the very basis,

[007-174] the very first condition of our life.

[007-175] The psychology of orgiasm conceived as the feeling of a superabundance

[007-176] of vitality and strength, within the scope of which even painacts

[007-177] as a stimulus,gave me the key to the concepttragicfeeling,

[007-178] which has been misunderstood not only by Aristotle, but also even

[007-179] more by our pessimists. Tragedy is so far from proving anything in

[007-180] regard to the pessimism ofthe Greeks, as Schopenhauer maintains,

[007-181] that it ought rather to be considered as the categorical repudiation

[007-182] andcondemnationthereof. The saying of Yea to life, including

[007-183] even its most strange and most terrible problems, the will to life

[007-184] rejoicing over its own inexhaustibleness in thesacrificeof its

[007-185] highest types—this is what I called Dionysian, this is what I

[007-186] divined as the bridge leading to the psychology of thetragicpoet.

[007-187] Not in order to escape from terror and pity, not to purify one’s self

[007-188] of a dangerous passion by discharging it with vehemence—this is how

[007-189] Aristotle understood it—but to be far beyond terror and pity and to

[007-190] be the eternal lust of Becoming itself—that lust which also involves

[007-191] thelust of destruction.And with this I once more come into touch

[007-192] with the spot from which I once set out—-the “Birth of Tragedy” was

[007-193] my first transvaluation of all values: with this I again take my stand

[007-194] upon the soil from out of which my will and my capacity spring—I, the

[007-195] last disciple of the philosopher Dionysus,—I, the prophet of eternal

[007-196] recurrence.
