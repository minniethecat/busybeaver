# Russia

## Overview

Russia appears in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* as Nietzsche's political counter-ideal to the modern [[europe|Europe]]an state. In "Skirmishes in a War with the Age" (ch06), Nietzsche singles out Russia as the sole contemporary nation possessing genuine political vitality — a stark contrast to the "decaying form of the State" he diagnoses in the [[german-empire|German Empire]] and the rest of [[europe|Western Europe]].

## In Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's most concentrated statement on Russia comes in his critique of modern democracy and the German Empire:

> If this will is present, something is founded which resembles the *imperium Romanum*; or Russia, the only great nation to-day that has some lasting power and grit in her, that can bide her time, that can still promise something. — Russia the opposite of all wretched European petty-statism and neurasthenia, which the foundation of the German Empire has brought to a crisis. (006-856–859)

This passage is remarkable for its admiration. Nietzsche, the arch-critic of nationalism, here praises Russia for possessing precisely what modern [[germany|Germany]] lacks: the "will to tradition, to authority, to responsibility for centuries to come" (006-853). Russia represents the aristocratic-political principle — long duration, accumulated power, the ability to *promise* — that democracy extinguishes.

A secondary reference, more literary in character, appears in Nietzsche's critique of Dostoiewsky: "the only psychologist from whom I had anything to learn" — a remark that "seems to have been drawn from a Russian novel" (010-935), suggesting Nietzsche's sense of Russia as a reservoir of psychological depth absent from the flattened [[europe|Europe]]an [[soul|psyche]].

## Historical Significance

Nietzsche wrote in 1888, on the eve of his mental collapse. Tsarist Russia was still an autocracy, and Nietzsche's praise must be read in context: he is not endorsing Tsarism per se but identifying Russia as the last [[europe|Europe]]an power retaining the "will to power" in its political form — the instinct for accumulation, duration, and command that the German Empire had surrendered to democratic "half-measures."
