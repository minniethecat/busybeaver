# Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart

## Biography

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756–1791) was the supreme composer of the Viennese Classical period, whose prodigious output — encompassing symphony, opera, chamber music, and sacred works — defined the musical language of the late eighteenth century. His operas, particularly *The Marriage of Figaro*, *Don Giovanni*, *Così fan tutte*, and *The Magic Flute*, remain among the most performed works in the repertoire. *The Magic Flute* (*Die Zauberflöte*, 1791), with libretto by Emanuel Schikaneder, was Mozart's final completed opera, a Singspiel blending Masonic symbolism, folk comedy, and Enlightenment idealism. Nietzsche's mature taste in music turned decisively against the German tradition — toward Bizet's *Carmen* and the Mediterranean lightness he opposed to Wagner's northern heaviness — but Mozart's ghost haunts the text of *Twilight* in a single, precisely deployed quotation.

## Nietzsche's View

Mozart enters *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* through a footnote. In *Skirmishes in a War with the Age*, Nietzsche deploys a quotation from *The Magic Flute* to skewer artistic pretension:

> "This picture is perfectly beautiful!" [006-1228/1230]

The line — drawn from Act I, Scene 3 of *The Magic Flute*, where Tamino gazes upon the portrait of Pamina — is quoted by Nietzsche's fictional "literary woman," the self-regarding artist who "listening with agonised curiosity every instant to the imperative which whispers to her from the very depths of her being: *aut liberi, aut libri*" (either children or books) [006-1228]. She is the type of the modern literary consciousness that has turned art into a mirror of its own self-satisfaction. Her exclamation — "This picture is perfectly beautiful!" — is not an aesthetic judgment but an act of self-congratulation: Tamino's innocent wonder at Pamina's portrait is repurposed as the narcissistic artist's wonder at her own creation.

The quotation is Nietzsche's most characteristic gesture: he borrows a fragment of high culture and turns it against the cultural pretensions of his age. Mozart's opera, itself a work of Masonic idealism celebrating the triumph of wisdom and love over darkness, is made to illuminate the exact opposite — the vanity, self-deception, and theatricality of the modern artistic sensibility.

The translator's note identifying the quotation — "Quotation from the Libretto of Mozart's 'Magic Flute' Act I, Sc. 3" [006-1228] — confirms that Nietzsche expected his readers to recognise the source. Mozart is not the target; he is the standard against which modern literary culture is measured and found wanting.

## Philosophical Significance

Nietzsche's indirect treatment of Mozart is significant partly for what it omits. Unlike Wagner, who dominates Nietzsche's writings on music, Mozart receives no extended critique. This silence may itself be a form of respect: Mozart's music, with its classical clarity, formal perfection, and absence of metaphysical pretension, belongs to a musical world before Wagner's "total work of art" and its attendant ideology. When Nietzsche declared that "only musicians of the very first rank can tell us anything about music" [006-1228 context], he implicitly excluded the literary appropriators of music — and Mozart, the musician's musician, stands serenely beyond the reach of such appropriation.
