# Ernest Renan

## Biography

Ernest Renan (1823–1892) was a French philologist, historian, and philosopher of religion. Trained for the priesthood before losing his faith, he became one of the most influential French intellectuals of the nineteenth century. His *Life of Jesus* (1863), which portrayed [[jesus|Christ]] as a purely human moral teacher, caused a sensation across [[europe|Europe]] and established Renan as a leading figure in the liberal secularization of religious studies.

## Philosophical Contributions

Renan's major contribution was the application of historical-critical methods to religious texts, treating the Bible as a human document subject to ordinary scholarly scrutiny. He championed an "aristocracy of intellect" — the idea that society should be guided by a scientific and intellectual elite. His vision of the future was a form of secularized religion in which science would replace dogma while preserving the moral essence of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche held Renan in deep contempt, seeing him as the embodiment of intellectual dishonesty — a man who had abandoned [[jesus|Christ]]ian faith but retained every priestly instinct. In *Skirmishes*, Nietzsche titled his Renan entry "Theology, or the corruption of reason by original sin ([[jesus|Christ]]ianity)" [006-011], diagnosing a mind that "invariably misses the point with painful regularity" [006-014]. The core of the critique is that Renan's free-[[soul|spirit]]edness was a sham: "What is the good of all free-[[soul|spirit]]edness, modernity, mockery and acrobatic suppleness, if in one's belly one is still a [[jesus|Christ]]ian, a Catholic, and even a priest!" [006-019–020]. Nietzsche compared his seductive style to that of a Jesuit confessor [006-021], and in *The Antichrist* dismissed him as "that buffoon in psychologies" [010-873].

> "What is the good of all free-spiritedness, modernity, mockery and acrobatic suppleness, if in one's belly one is still a Christian, a Catholic, and even a priest!" [006-019–020]
