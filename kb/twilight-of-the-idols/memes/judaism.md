# Judaism

## Overview

**Judaism** figures in Nietzsche's *Twilight of the [[idol|Idols]]* as the genealogical origin of the moral revolution that [[jesus|Christ]]ianity would later universalize. In Nietzsche's narrative, it was Judaism that first transformed the "[[chandala|Chandala]]" — the outcast, the low, the resentful — into the moral center of a religion: "the [[chandala|Chandala]] of Judaism, the outcasts and 'sinners'" became the protagonists of a new value system (010-837). [[jesus|Christ]]ianity, in this genealogy, is simply "the final consequence of Judaism" (010-659).

Nietzsche's relationship to Judaism in *Twilight* is complex: he respects the Old Testament as "incomparably superior" to the New, yet sees in the Jewish priesthood the original architects of the "slave revolt in morality" that devalued nobility, strength, and life-affirmation.

## Role in Nietzsche's Thought

Judaism appears across multiple chapters as a crucial link in Nietzsche's genealogy of [[jesus|Christ]]ian morality:

**The teachers of morality.** In "The Improvers of Mankind" (Chapter 4), Nietzsche groups "the teachers of Judaism and [[jesus|Christ]]ianity" among those who have "never doubted their right to lie" in the service of "improving" mankind (004-117). Like [[manu|Manu]], like [[plato|Plato]], the Jewish moralists believed that their ends justified any means — including the systematic falsification of nature.

**The origin of the concept of sin.** Nietzsche traces the [[jesus|Christ]]ian obsession with "[[sin|sin]], forgiveness, punishment, reward" directly to Judaism: "It was the Judaism of the concepts 'sin,' 'forgiveness,' '[[faith|faith]],' 'redemption through [[faith|faith]]'" that became the "preliminary history of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity" (010-1054). Judaism invented the metaphysical machinery of guilt that [[jesus|Christ]]ianity later perfected.

**Priestly revaluation.** The decisive event, for Nietzsche, was the Jewish priesthood's "revaluation of values": "Dominant Judaism, its ruling class" — the priests — transformed the natural hierarchy of noble and base into the moral hierarchy of righteous and sinful (010-1258). This was the original act of *ressentiment* that turned weakness into virtue and strength into guilt: "Thenceforward the whole of Judaism reaches its final mastership" — the triumph of the priestly over the noble (010-1427).

> It was the Judaism of the concepts "sin," "forgiveness," "faith," "redemption through faith" — this was the preliminary history of Christianity: here was that unparalleled lubricity of conception and that truly rabbinical impudence of pretension which made it possible to nail this whole casual reality to a cross. (010-1054)

**A movement independent of Judaism.** Nietzsche credits [[jesus|Christ]]ianity with one original achievement: it became "a movement independent of Judaism, a universal conflagration combining every kind of outcast" (010-2224). What began as a Jewish sect became, through [[paul-the-apostle|Paul]], a universal religion that transcended its ethnic origins while preserving its priestly moral structure.

## Historical Context

Ancient Judaism was the religion of the Israelite people, centered on the Temple in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] and the Mosaic Law. After the Babylonian Exile (6th century BCE), Judaism underwent a transformation: the [[the-priesthood|priestly class]] consolidated its authority, the Law was codified, and a distinctive moral vision — universal, monotheistic, guilt-centered — took shape.

By Nietzsche's time, the historical-critical study of the Bible (the "Higher Criticism") had begun to reconstruct this history. Nietzsche's contemporary Julius Wellhausen's *Prolegomena to the History of Israel* (1878) argued that the Law was a late, post-Exilic development, not the original Mosaic foundation — a thesis that Nietzsche's genealogy of priestly power implicitly draws upon.

Nietzsche's portrait of Judaism is not ethnographic but genealogical: he is interested in Judaism as the *structure* that made [[jesus|Christ]]ianity possible. The Jewish invention of "sin" as a metaphysical category, the priestly monopoly on interpretation, the transformation of a national deity into a universal judge — these are, for Nietzsche, the preconditions of the [[jesus|Christ]]ian moral revolution that reshaped [[europe|Europe]].

## Key Figures

- [[jesus]] — the Jewish teacher whose movement the [[the-church|Church]] transformed into a universal religion of guilt
- [[paul-the-apostle]] — the Pharisee who universalized Judaism's moral structure, detaching it from ethnic identity
- [[the-priesthood]] — the anthropological type whose will to power Judaism first organized into a ruling class
- [[moses]] — the lawgiver figure whose code Nietzsche compares to [[manu|Manu]]'s as an instrument of social ordering
