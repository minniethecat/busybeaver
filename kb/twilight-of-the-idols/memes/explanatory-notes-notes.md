#### EXPLANATORY NOTES TO “THUS SPAKE ZARATHUSTRA”

[015-001] All goals have been annihilated: valuations are turning against each

[015-002] other:

[015-003] People call him good who hearkens to the dictates of his own heart, but

[015-004] they also call him good who merely does his duty;

[015-005] People call the mild and conciliating man good, but they also call him

[015-006] good who is brave, inflexible and severe;

[015-007] People call him good who does not do violence to himself, but they also

[015-008] call the heroes of self-mastery good;

[015-009] People call the absolute friend of truth good-, but they also call him

[015-010] good who is pious and a transfigurer of things;

[015-011] People call him good who can obey his own voice, but they also call the

[015-012] devout man good;

[015-013] People call the noble and the haughty man good, but also him who does

[015-014] not despise and who does not assume condescending airs.

[015-015] People call him good who is kindhearted and who steps out of the way of

[015-016] broils, but he who thirsts for fight and triumph is also called good;

[015-017] People call him good who always wishes to be first, but they also call

[015-018] him good who does not wish to be ahead of anybody in anything.

[015-019] We possess a powerful store of moralfeelings,but we have no goal

[015-020] for them all. They mutually contradict each other: they have their

[015-021] origin in different tables of values.

[015-022] There is a wonderful amount of moral power, but there is no longer any

[015-023] goal towards which all this power can be directed.

[015-024] All goals have been annihilated, mankind must give themselves a fresh

[015-025] goal. It is an error to suppose that they had one: they gave themselves

[015-026] all the goals they ever had. But the prerequisites of all previous

[015-027] goals have been annihilated.

[015-028] Science traces the course of things but points to no goal: what it does

[015-029] give consists of the fundamental facts upon which the new goal must be

[015-030] based.

[015-031] The profound sterility of the nineteenth century. I have not

[015-032] encountered a single man who really had a new ideal to bring

[015-033] forward. The character of German music kept me hoping longest, but

[015-034] in vain. A stronger type in which all our powers are synthetically

[015-035] correlated—this constitutes my faith.

[015-036] Apparently everything is decadence. We should so direct this movement

[015-037] of decline that it may provide the strongest with a new form of

[015-038] existence.

[015-039] The dissolution of morality, in its practical consequences, leads

[015-040] to the atomistic individual, andfurther to the subdivision of the

[015-041] individual into a quantity of parts—absolute liquefaction.

[015-042] That is why a goal is now more than ever necessary; and love, but a new

[015-043] love.

[015-044] I say: “As long as your morality hung over me I breathed like one

[015-045] asphyxiated. That is why I throttled this snake. I wished to live,

[015-046] consequently it had to die.”

[015-047] As long as people are stillforced toact, that is to say as long as

[015-048] commands are given, synthesis (the suppression of the moral man) will

[015-049] not be realised To be unable to be otherwise: instincts and commanding

[015-050] reason extending beyond any immediate object: the ability to enjoy

[015-051] one’s own nature in action.

[015-052] None of them wish to bear the burden of the commander; but they will

[015-053] perform the most strenuous task if only thou commandest them.

[015-054] We must overcome the past in ourselves: we must combine the instincts

[015-055] afresh and direct the whole together to one goal:—an extremely

[015-056] difficult undertaking! It is not only the evil instincts which have to

[015-057] be overcome,—the so-called good instincts must be conquered also and

[015-058] consecrated anew!

[015-059] 10

[015-060] No leaps must be made in virtue! But everyone must be given a different

[015-061] path! Not leading to thehighest development of each! Yet everyone may

[015-062] be a bridge and an example for others.

[015-063] 11

[015-064] To help, to pity, to submit and to renounce personal attacks with a

[015-065] good will,—these things may make even insignificant and superficial

[015-066] men tolerable to the eye: such men must not be contradicted in their

[015-067] belief that this good will is “virtue in itself.”

[015-068] 12

[015-069] Man makes a deed valuable: but how might a deed make man valuable?

[015-070] 13

[015-071] Morality is the concern of those who cannot free themselves from

[015-072] it: for such people morality therefore belongs to the conditions of

[015-073] existence. It is impossible to refute conditions of existence: the only

[015-074] thing one can do is not to have them.

[015-075] 14

[015-076] If it were true that life did not deserve to be welcomed, the moral

[015-077] man, precisely on account of his self-denial and obligingness, would

[015-078] then be guilty of misusing his fellow to his own personal advantage.

[015-079] 15

[015-080] “Love thy neighbour”—this would mean first and foremost: “Let thy

[015-081] neighbour go his own way”—and it is precisely this kind of virtue that

[015-082] is the most difficult!

[015-083] 16

[015-084] The bad man as the parasite. We must not be merely feasters and

[015-085] gourmets of life: this is ignoble.

[015-086] 17

[015-087] It is a noble sense which forbids our being only feasters and gourmets

[015-088] of life—this sense revolts against hedonism—: we want to perform

[015-089] something in return!—But the fundamental feeling of the masses is that

[015-090] one must live for nothing,—that is their vulgarity.

[015-091] 18

[015-092] The converse valuations hold good for the lower among men: in their

[015-093] case therefore it is necessary to implant virtues. They must be

[015-094] elevated above their lives, by means of absolute commands and terrible

[015-095] taskmasters.

[015-096] 19

[015-097] What is required: the new law must be made practicable—and out of its

[015-098] fulfilment, the overcoming of this law, and higher law, must evolve

[015-099] [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]] defines the attitude towards law, inasmuch as he suppresses

[015-100] the law of laws which is morality.

[015-101] Laws as the backbone They must be worked at and created, by being

[015-102] fulfilled. The slavish attitude which has reigned hitherto towards law!

[015-103] 20

[015-104] The self-overcoming of Zarathustra as the prototype of mankind’s

[015-105] self-overcoming for the benefit of Superman. To this end the overcoming

[015-106] of morality is necessary.

[015-107] 21

[015-108] The type of the lawgiver, his development and his suffering. What is

[015-109] the purpose of giving laws at all?

[015-110] Zarathustra is the herald who calls forth many lawgivers.

[015-111] 22

[015-112] Individual instruments.

[015-113] 1. The Commanders, the mighty—who do not love, unless it be that they

[015-114] love the images according to which they create. The rich in vitality,

[015-115] the versatile, the free, who overcome that which is extant

[015-116] 2. The obedient, the “emancipated”—love and reverence constitute their

[015-117] happiness, they have a sense of what is higher (their deficiencies are

[015-118] made whole by the sight of the lofty).

[015-119] 3. The slaves, the order of “henchmen”—: they must be made

[015-120] comfortable, they must cultivate pity for one another.

[015-121] 23

[015-122] The giver, the creator, the teacher—these are preludes of the ruler.

[015-123] 24

[015-124] All virtue and all self-mastery has only one purpose: that of preparing

[015-125] for the rule!

[015-126] 25

[015-127] Every sacrifice that the ruler makes is rewarded a hundredfold.

[015-128] 26

[015-129] How much does not the warrior, the prince, theman who is responsible

[015-130] for himself, sacrifice!—this should be highly honoured.

[015-131] 27

[015-132] The terrible task of the ruler who educates himself:—the kind of man

[015-133] and people over which he will rule must be forecast in him: it is in

[015-134] himself therefore that he must first have become a ruler!

[015-135] 28

[015-136] The great educator like nature must elevate obstacles in order that

[015-137] these may be overcome.

[015-138] 29

[015-139] The new teachers as preparatory stages for the highest Architect (they

[015-140] must impose their type on things).

[015-141] 30

[015-142] Institutions may be regarded as the after effects of great individuals

[015-143] and the means of giving great individuals root and soil—until the

[015-144] fruit ultimately appears.

[015-145] 31

[015-146] As a matter of fact mankind is continually trying to be able to

[015-147] dispense with great individuals by means of corporations, &c. But they

[015-148] are utterly dependent upon such great individuals for their ideal.

[015-149] 32

[015-150] The eudæmonistic and social ideals lead men backwards,—it may be that

[015-151] they aim at a very useful working class,—they are creating the ideal

[015-152] slaveof the future, the lower caste which must on no account be

[015-153] lacking!

[015-154] 33

[015-155] Equal rights for all!—this is the most extraordinary form of

[015-156] injustice, for with it the highest men do not get their due.

[015-157] 34

[015-158] It is not a matter of the rights of the stronger, for strong and weak

[015-159] are alike in this, that they all extend their power as far as they can.

[015-160] 35

[015-161] A new form of estimating man: above all the question:

[015-162] How much power has he got?

[015-163] How manifold are his instincts?

[015-164] How great is his capacity for communication and assimilation?

[015-165] The ruler as the highest type.

[015-166] 36

[015-167] Zarathustra rejoices that the war of the classes is at last over,

[015-168] and that now at length the time is ripe for an order of rank among

[015-169] individuals. His hatred of the democratic system of levelling is only

[015-170] a blind; as a matter of fact he is very pleased that this has gone so

[015-171] far. Now he can perform his task.—

[015-172] Hitherto his doctrines had been directed only at the ruling caste of

[015-173] the future. These lords of the earth must now take the place of God,

[015-174] and must create for themselves the profound and absolute confidence

[015-175] of those they rule. Their new holiness, theirrenunciation of

[015-176] happiness and ease, must be their first principle. To the lowest they

[015-177] grant the heirloom of happiness, not to themselves. They deliver the

[015-178] physiologically botched by teaching them the doctrine of “swift death.”

[015-179] They offer religions and philosophical systems to each according to his

[015-180] rank.

[015-181] 37

[015-182] “The conflict in the heart of the ruler is the contest between the love

[015-183] which is in his heart for him who is most remote, and the love which he

[015-184] feels for his neighbour.”

[015-185] To be a creator and to be capable of goodness are not at all things

[015-186] which exclude one another. They are rather one and the same thing; but

[015-187] the creator is farsighted and the good man nearsighted.

[015-188] 38

[015-189] The feeling of power. The strife of all egos to discover that thought

[015-190] which will remain poised above men like a star.—The ego is aprimum

[015-191] mobile.

[015-192] 39

[015-193] The struggle for the application of the power which mankind now

[015-194] represents! Zarathustra calls to the gladiators of this struggle.

[015-195] 40

[015-196] We must make our ideals prevail:—We must strive for power in such a

[015-197] way as our ideal commands.

[015-198] 41

[015-199] The doctrine of the [[eternal-recurrence|Eternal Recurrence]] is the turning point of history.

[015-200] 42

[015-201] Suddenly the terrible chamber of truth is opened, an unconscious

[015-202] self-protectiveness, caution, ambush, defence keeps us from the gravest

[015-203] knowledge. Thus have I lived heretofore. I suppress something; but the

[015-204] restless babbling and rolling down of stones has rendered my instinct

[015-205] over-powerful. Now I am rolling my last stone, the most appalling truth

[015-206] stands close to my hand.

[015-207] Truth has been exorcised out of its grave:—we created it, we waked it:

[015-208] the highest expression of courage and of the feeling of power. Scorn of

[015-209] all pessimism that has existed hitherto!

[015-210] We fight with it,—we find out that our only means of enduring it is

[015-211] to create a creature who is able to endure it:—unless, of course, we

[015-212] voluntarily dazzle ourselves afresh and blind ourselves in regard to it

[015-213] But this we are no longer able to do!

[015-214] We it was who created the gravest thought,—let us now create a being

[015-215] unto whom it will be not only light but blessed.

[015-216] In order to be able to create we must allow ourselves greater freedom

[015-217] than has ever been vouch-safed us before; to this end we must be

[015-218] emancipated from morality, and we must be relieved by means of feasts

[015-219] (Premonitions of the future! We must celebrate the future and no longer

[015-220] the past! We must compose the myth poetry of the future! We must live

[015-221] in hopes!) Blessed moments I And then we must once again pull down the

[015-222] curtain and turn our thoughts to the next unswerving purpose.

[015-223] 43

[015-224] Mankind must set its goal above itself—not in a false world, however,

[015-225] but in one which would be a continuation of humanity.

[015-226] 44

[015-227] The half-way house is always present when the will to the future

[015-228] arises: the greatest event stands immediately before it.

[015-229] 45

[015-230] Our very essence is to create a being higher than ourselves. We must

[015-231] create beyond ourselves. That is the instinct of procreation, that is

[015-232] the instinct of action and of work.—Just as all willing presupposes a

[015-233] purpose, so does mankind presuppose a creature which is not yet formed

[015-234] but which provides the aim of life. This is the freedom of all will.

[015-235] Love, reverence, yearning for perfection, longing, all these things are

[015-236] inherent in a purpose.

[015-237] 46

[015-238] My desire: to bring forth creatures which stand sublimely above the

[015-239] whole species man: and to sacrifice “one’s neighbours” and oneself to

[015-240] this end.

[015-241] The morality which has existed hitherto was limited within the confines

[015-242] of the species: all moralities that have existed hitherto have been

[015-243] useful in the first place in order to give unconditional stability to

[015-244] this species: once this has been achieved the aim can be elevated.

[015-245] One movement is absolute; it is nothing more than the levelling down of

[015-246] mankind, great ant-organisations, &c.

[015-247] The other movement, my movement, is conversely the accentuation of all

[015-248] contrasts and gulfs, and the elimination of equality, together with the

[015-249] creation of supremely powerful creatures.

[015-250] The first movement brings forth the last man, my movement brings forth

[015-251] the Superman. It is by no means the goal to regard the latter as the

[015-252] master of the first: two races ought to exist side by side,—separated

[015-253] as far asunder as possible; the one, like the Epicurean gods, not

[015-254] concerning themselves in the least with the others.

[015-255] 47

[015-256] The opposite of the Superman is the last man: I created him

[015-257] simultaneously with the former.

[015-258] 48

[015-259] The more an individual is free and firm, the more exacting becomes his

[015-260] love: at last he yearns for Superman, because nothing else is able to

[015-261] appease his love,

[015-262] 49

[015-263] Half-way round the course Superman arises.

[015-264] 50

[015-265] Among men I was frightened: among men I desired a host of things and

[015-266] nothing satisfied me. It was then that I went into solitude and created

[015-267] Superman. And when I had created him I draped him in the great veil of

[015-268] Becoming and let the light of midday shine upon him.

[015-269] 51

[015-270] “We wish to create a Being,” we all wish to have a hand in it, to love

[015-271] it. We all want to be pregnant—and to honour and respect ourselves on

[015-272] that account.

[015-273] We must have a goal in view of which we may all love each other! All

[015-274] other goals are only fit for the scrap heap.

[015-275] 52

[015-276] The strongest in body and soul are the best—Zarathustra’s fundamental

[015-277] proposition—; from them is generated that higher morality of the

[015-278] creator. Man must be regenerated after his own image: this is what he

[015-279] wants, this is his honesty.

[015-280] 53

[015-281] Genius to Zarathustra seems like the incarnation of his thought.

[015-282] 54

[015-283] Loneliness for a certain time is necessary in order that a creature

[015-284] may become completely permeated with his own soul—cured and hard. A

[015-285] new form of community would be one in which we should assert ourselves

[015-286] martially. Otherwise the spirit becomes tame. No Epicurean “gardens”

[015-287] and mere “retirement from the masses.” War (but without powder) between

[015-288] different thoughts and the hosts who support them I

[015-289] A new nobility, the result of breeding. Feasts celebrating the

[015-290] foundation of families.

[015-291] The day divided up afresh; bodily exercise for all ages. Ἀγών

[015-292] as a principle.

[015-293] The love of the sexes as a contest around the principle in becoming and

[015-294] coming.—Ruling will be taught and practised, its hardness as well as

[015-295] its mildness. As soon as one faculty is acquired in a masterly manner

[015-296] another one must be striven after.

[015-297] We must let ourselves be taught by the evil, and allow them an

[015-298] opportunity of a contest. We must make use of the degenerate—The right

[015-299] of punishment will consist in this, that the offender may be used as

[015-300] an experimental subject (in dietetics): this is the consecration of

[015-301] punishment, that one man be used for the highest needs of a future

[015-302] being.

[015-303] We protect our new community because it is the bridge to our ideal of

[015-304] the future And for it we work and let others work.

[015-305] 55

[015-306] The measure and mean must be found in striving to attain to something

[015-307] beyond mankind: the highest and strongest kind of man must be

[015-308] discovered! The highest tendency must be represented continually

[015-309] in small things:—perfection, maturity, rosy-cheeked health, mild

[015-310] discharges of power. Just as an artist works, must we apply ourselves

[015-311] to our daily task and bring ourselves to perfection in everything we

[015-312] do. We must be honest in acknowledging our real motives to ourselves,

[015-313] as is becoming in the mighty man.

[015-314] 56

[015-315] No impatience! Superman is our next stage and to this end, to this

[015-316] limit, moderation and manliness are necessary.

[015-317] Mankind must surpass itself, as the Greeks did—and no fleshless

[015-318] fantasies must be indulged. The higher mind which is associated with a

[015-319] sickly and nervous character must be suppressed. The goal: the higher

[015-320] culture of the whole body and not only of the brain.

[015-321] 57

[015-322] “Man is something that must be surpassed”:—it is a matter of tempo:

[015-323] the Greeks were wonderful, there was no haste about them.—My

[015-324] predecessors: [[heraclitus|Heraclitus]], Empedocles, Spinoza, Goethe.

[015-325] 58

[015-326] 1. Dissatisfaction with ourselves. An antidote to repentance.

[015-327] The transformation of temperament (e.g.,by means of inorganic

[015-328] substances). Good will to this dissatisfaction. We should wait for our

[015-329] thirst and let it become great in order to discover its source.

[015-330] 2. Death must be transformed into a means of victory and triumph.

[015-331] 3. The attitude towards disease. Freedom where death is concerned.

[015-332] 4. The love of the sexes is a means to an ideal (it is the striving of

[015-333] a being to perish through his opposite). The love for a suffering deity.

[015-334] 5. Procreation is the holiest of all things. Pregnancy, the creation of

[015-335] a woman and a man, who wish to enjoy their unity, and erect a monument

[015-336] to it by means of a child.

[015-337] 6. Pity as a danger. Circumstances must be created which enable

[015-338] everyone to be able to helphimself, and which leave him to choose

[015-339] whether he would be helped.

[015-340] 7. Education must be directed at making men evil, at developing their

[015-341] inner devil.

[015-342] 8. Inner war as “development”

[015-343] 9. “The maintenance of the species,” and the thought of eternal

[015-344] recurrence.

[015-345] 59

[015-346] Principal doctrine. We must strive to make every stage one of

[015-347] perfection, and rejoice therein,—we must make no leaps!

[015-348] In the first place, the promulgation of laws. After the Superman the

[015-349] doctrine of eternal recurrence will strike us with horror: Now it is

[015-350] endurable.

[015-351] 60

[015-352] Life itself created this thought which is the most oppressive for life.

[015-353] Life wishes to get beyond its greatest obstacle I

[015-354] We must desire to perish in order to arise afresh,—from one day to

[015-355] the other. Wander through a hundred souls,—let that be thy life and

[015-356] thy fate! And then finally: desire to go through the whole process once

[015-357] more!

[015-358] 61

[015-359] The highest thing of all would be for us to be able to endure our

[015-360] immortality.

[015-361] 62

[015-362] The moment in which I begot recurrence is immortal, for the sake of

[015-363] that moment alone I will endure recurrence.

[015-364] 63

[015-365] The teaching of eternal recurrence—it is at first oppressive to the

[015-366] more noble souls and apparently a means of weeding them out,—then the

[015-367] inferior and less sensitive natures would remain over! “This doctrine

[015-368] must be suppressed and Zarathustra killed.”

[015-369] 64

[015-370] The hesitation of the disciples. “We are already able to bear with this

[015-371] doctrine, but we should destroy the many by means of it!”

[015-372] Zarathustra laughs: “Ye shall be the hammer: I laid this hammer in your

[015-373] hands.”

[015-374] 65

[015-375] I do not speak to you as I speak to the people. The highest thing for

[015-376] them would be to despise and to annihilate themselves: the next highest

[015-377] thing would be for them to despise and annihilate each other.

[015-378] 66

[015-379] “My will to do good compels me to remain silent. But my will to the

[015-380] Superman bids me speak and sacrifice even my friends.”

[015-381] “I would fain form and transform you, how could I endure things

[015-382] otherwise!”

[015-383] 67

[015-384] The history of higher man. The rearing of the better man is

[015-385] incalculably more painful. The ideal of the necessary sacrifice which

[015-386] it involves, as in the case of Zarathustra, should be demonstrated: A

[015-387] man should leave his home, his family and his nativeland. Live under

[015-388] the scorn of the prevailing morality. The anguish of experiments and

[015-389] errors. The solution of all the joys offered by the older ideals (they

[015-390] are now felt to be partly hostile and partly strange).

[015-391] 68

[015-392] What is it which gives a meaning, a value, an importance to things?

[015-393] It is the creative heart which yearns and which created out of this

[015-394] yearning. It created joy and woe. It wanted to sate itself also with

[015-395] woe. Every kind of pain that man or beast has suffered, we must take

[015-396] upon ourselves and bless, and have a goal whereby such suffering would

[015-397] acquire some meaning.

[015-398] 69

[015-399] Principal doctrine: the transfiguration of pain into a blessing, and of

[015-400] poison into food, lies in our power. The will to suffering.

[015-401] 70

[015-402] Concerning heroic greatness as the only state of pioneers. (A yearning

[015-403] for utter ruin as a means of enduring one’s existence.)

[015-404] We must not desire one state only; we must rather desire to be

[015-405] periodical creatures—like existence.

[015-406] Absolute indifference to other people’s opinions (because we know their

[015-407] weights and measures), but their opinions of themselves should be the

[015-408] subject of pity.

[015-409] 71

[015-410] Disciples must unite three qualities in themselves: they must be

[015-411] true, they must be able and willing tobe communicative, they must

[015-412] have profound insight into each other.

[015-413] 72

[015-414] All kinds of higher men and their oppression and blighting (as a case

[015-415] in point, Duhring, who was ruined by isolation)—on the whole, this

[015-416] is the fate of higher men to-day, they seem to be a species that is

[015-417] condemned to die out: this fact seems to come to Zarathustra’s ears

[015-418] like a great cry for help. All kinds of insane degenerations of higher

[015-419] natures seem to approach him (nihilism for instance).

[015-420] 73

[015-421] Higher Men who come to Zarathustra in Despair.

[015-422] Temptations to return prematurely to the world—thanks to the

[015-423] provocation of one’s sympathies.

[015-424] 1. The rolling stone, the homeless one, the wanderer:—he who has

[015-425] unlearned the love of his people because he has learned to love many

[015-426] peoples,—the good European.

[015-427] 2. The gloomy, ambitious son of the people, shy, lonely, and ready for

[015-428] anything,—who chooses rather to be alone than to be a destroyer,—he

[015-429] offers himself as an instrument.

[015-430] 3. The ugliest man, who is obliged to adorn himself (historical sense)

[015-431] and who is always in search of a new garment: he desires to make his

[015-432] appearance becoming, and finally retires into solitude in order not to

[015-433] be seen, he is ashamed of himself.

[015-434] 4. He who honours facts (“the brain of a leech”), the most subtle

[015-435] intellectual conscience, and because he has it in excess, a guilty

[015-436] conscience,—he wants to get rid of himself.

[015-437] 5. The poet, who at bottom thirsts, for savage freedom,—he chooses

[015-438] loneliness and the severity of knowledge.

[015-439] 6. The discoverer of new intoxicants,—the musician, the sorcerer, who

[015-440] finally drops on his knees before a loving heart and says: “Not to me

[015-441] do I wish to lead you but yonder to him.”

[015-442] Those who are sober to excess and who have a yearning for intoxication

[015-443] which they do not gratify. The Supersobersides.

[015-444] 7. Genius (as an attack of insanity), becoming frozen through lack of
