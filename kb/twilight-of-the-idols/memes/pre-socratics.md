# Pre-Socratics

## Overview

The **Pre-Socratics** are the early Greek thinkers who philosophized before [[socrates-person|Socrates]] (c. 470–399 BCE) — including Thales, Anaximander, [[heraclitus|Heraclitus]], Parmenides, Empedocles, and Anaxagoras. In Nietzsche's philosophical history, the Pre-Socratics represent a golden age of thought before what he diagnoses as the "decadence of the Greek instinct" brought about by [[socrates-person|Socrates]] and [[plato|Plato]] (007-073).

Nietzsche, himself a trained classical philologist, held the Pre-Socratics in unique esteem throughout his career. In *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, this admiration surfaces as a critical contrast: the Pre-Socratics embody a mode of philosophy that affirms life, engages with nature directly, and refuses the dualistic separation of "[[reality|true world]]" from apparent world that would later define the [[plato|Plato]]nic-[[jesus|Christ]]ian tradition.

## Role in Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche's treatment of the Pre-Socratics in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* is concentrated in Chapter 7 ("Things I Owe to the Ancients"), though their presence as a critical standard runs throughout the work:

**As diagnostic contrast.** Nietzsche opens his discussion of the Greeks with a striking ambivalence: "I am not indebted to the Greeks for anything like such strong impressions" as he is to the Romans (007-030). Yet this apparent dismissal is rhetorical — what Nietzsche actually rejects is the *received* image of Greek culture, the "classically-cultured" version that sanitizes and idealizes the Greeks into marble statues. Against this, Nietzsche insists on reading Greek philosophy as the expression of instinctual health or its decline.

**The Pre-Socratic standard.** For Nietzsche, "Greek philosophy is the decadence of the Greek instinct" (007-073) — a judgment that locates the *beginning* of philosophical decadence not with philosophy as such but with its Socratic turn. The Pre-Socratics, by implication, represent philosophy *before* decadence: thinkers who philosophized out of strength and fullness rather than out of the reactive need to escape from life into concepts. [[thucydides|Thucydides]], whom Nietzsche names alongside this judgment, represents the Pre-Socratic [[soul|spirit]] surviving into historiography — the "courage in the face of reality" that distinguishes pre-decadent thought.

**The [[dionysian|Dionysian]] inheritance.** The Pre-Socratics are the intellectual forebears of Nietzsche's own philosophical project. Their willingness to think *becoming* rather than *being* ([[heraclitus|Heraclitus]]), their refusal to separate reason from instinct, and their engagement with the tragic character of existence all anticipate the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] philosophy Nietzsche advocates. The "perfections among the Greeks" that Nietzsche does admire — their "calm grandeur," their "idealised naturalness" (007-081) — are Pre-Socratic perfections.

> Greek philosophy is the decadence of the Greek instinct: Thucydides is the great sum, the last revelation of that strong, severe, hard matter-of-factness which was instinctive with the more ancient Hellenes. (007-073–075)

## Historical Context

The Pre-Socratics flourished in the [[greece|Greek world]] from the 6th to the 5th centuries BCE, primarily in Ionia (Asia Minor) and Magna Graecia (southern Italy). They were the first thinkers in the Western tradition to seek naturalistic explanations of the cosmos rather than mythological ones, pioneering the modes of inquiry — cosmology, metaphysics, epistemology, ethics — that would define Western philosophy.

Their historical fate was to be largely eclipsed by the Socratic-[[plato|Plato]]nic tradition. Most Pre-Socratic writings survive only in fragments quoted by later authors, a textual condition that Nietzsche (as a philologist who worked directly with these fragments) found philosophically significant: the suppression of Pre-Socratic thought was not accidental but part of the triumph of the [[plato|Plato]]nic-[[jesus|Christ]]ian "[[reality|true world]]" that *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* undertakes to dismantle.
