#### SKIRMISHES IN A WAR WITH THE AGE

[006-001] My Impossible People.—Seneca, or the toreador of virtue—-Rousseau,

[006-002] or the return to nature,in impuris naturalibus.—Schiller, or the

[006-003] Moral-Trumpeter of Sackingen.—Dante, or the hyæna that writes poetry

[006-004] in tombs.—Kant, orcantas an intelligible character.—Victor

[006-005] Hugo, or the lighthouse on the sea of nonsense.—Liszt, or the

[006-006] school of racing—after women.—George Sand, orlactea ubertas,in plain English: the cow with plenty of beautiful milk.—Michelet,

[006-007] or enthusiasm in its shirt sleeves.—Carlyle, or Pessimism after

[006-008] undigested meals.—John Stuart Mill, or offensive lucidity.—The

[006-009] brothers Goncourt, or the two Ajaxes fighting with Homer. Music by

[006-010] Offenbach.—Zola, or the love of stinking.

[006-011] Renan.—Theology, or the corruption of reason by original sin

[006-012] (Christianity). Proof of this,—Renan who, even in those rare cases

[006-013] where he ventures to say either Yes or No on a general question,

[006-014] invariably misses the point with painful regularity. For instance,

[006-015] he would fain associate science and nobility: but surely it must be

[006-016] obvious that science is democratic. He seems to be actuated by a

[006-017] strong desire to represent an aristocracy of intellect: but, at thesame time he grovels on his knees, and not only on his knees, before

[006-018] the opposite doctrine, the gospel of the humble. What is the good of

[006-019] all free-spiritedness, modernity, mockery and acrobatic suppleness,

[006-020] if in one’s belly one is still a Christian, a Catholic, and even a

[006-021] priest! Renan’s forte, precisely like that of a Jesuit and Father

[006-022] Confessor, lies in his seductiveness. His intellectuality is not

[006-023] devoid of that unctuous complacency of a parson,—like all priests, he

[006-024] becomes dangerous only when he loves. He is second to none in the art

[006-025] of skilfully worshipping a dangerous thing. This intellect of Renan’s,

[006-026] which in its action is enervating, is one calamity the more, for poor,

[006-027] sick France with her will-power all going to pieces.

[006-028] Sainte-Beuve.—There is naught of man in him; he is full of petty

[006-029] spite towards all virile spirits. He wanders erratically; he is subtle,

[006-030] inquisitive, a little bored, for ever with his ear to key-holes,—at

[006-031] bottom a woman, with all woman’s revengefulness and sensuality. As a

[006-032] psychologist he is a genius of slander; inexhaustively rich in means

[006-033] to this end; no one understands better than he how to introduce a

[006-034] little poison into praise. In his fundamental instincts he is plebeian

[006-035] and next of kin to Rousseau’s resentful spirit: consequently he is

[006-036] a Romanticist—for beneath all romanticism Rousseau’s instinct for

[006-037] revenge grunts and frets. He is a revolutionary, but kept within

[006-038] bounds by “funk.” He is embarrassed in the face of everything that is

[006-039] strong (public opinion, the Academy, the court, even Port Royal). He

[006-040] is embittered against everything great in menand things, against

[006-041] everything that believes in itself. Enough of a poet and of a female to

[006-042] be able to feel greatness as power; he is always turning and twisting,

[006-043] because, like the proverbial worm, he constantly feels that he is

[006-044] being trodden upon. As a critic he has no standard of judgment, no

[006-045] guiding principle, no backbone. Although he possesses the tongue of

[006-046] the Cosmopolitan libertine which can chatter about a thousand things,

[006-047] he has not the courage even to acknowledge hislibertinage.As a

[006-048] historian he has no philosophy, and lacks the power of philosophical

[006-049] vision,—hence his refusal to act the part of a judge, and his adoption

[006-050] of the mask of “objectivity” in all important matters. His attitude

[006-051] is better in regard to all those things in which subtle and effete

[006-052] taste is the highest tribunal: in these things he really does have

[006-053] the courage of his own personality—he really does enjoy his own

[006-054] nature—he actually is amaster,—In some respects he is a prototype

[006-055] of Baudelaire.

[006-056] “The Imitation of Christ” is one of those books which I cannot even

[006-057] take hold of without physical loathing: it exhales a perfume of the

[006-058] eternally feminine, which to appreciate fully one must be a Frenchman

[006-059] or a Wagnerite. This saint has a way of speaking about love which

[006-060] makes even Parisiennes feel a little curious.—I am told that thatmost intelligentof Jesuits, Auguste Comte, who wished to lead his

[006-061] compatriots back to [[rome|Rome]] by the circuitous route of science, drew his

[006-062] inspiration from this book. And I believe it: “The religion of the

[006-063] heart.”

[006-064] G. Eliot.—They are rid of the Christian God and therefore

[006-065] think it all the more incumbent upon them to hold tight to Christian

[006-066] morality: this is an English way of reasoning; but let us not take it

[006-067] ill in moral femalesà laEliot. In England, every man who indulges

[006-068] in any trifling emancipation from theology, must retrieve his honour

[006-069] in the most terrifying manner by becoming a moral fanatic. That is how

[006-070] they do penance in that country.—As for us, we act differently. When

[006-071] we renounce the Christian faith, we abandon all right to Christian

[006-072] morality. This is not by any means self-evident and in defiance of

[006-073] English shallow-pates the point must be made ever more and more plain.

[006-074] Christianity is a system, a complete outlook upon the world, conceived

[006-075] as a whole. If its leading concept, the belief in God, is wrenched

[006-076] from it, the whole is destroyed; nothing vital remains in our grasp.

[006-077] Christianity presupposes that man does not and cannot know what is

[006-078] good or bad for him: the Christian believes in God who, alone, can

[006-079] know these things. Christian morality is a command, its origin is

[006-080] transcendental. It is beyond all criticism, all right to criticism;

[006-081] it is true only on condition that God is truth,—it stands or falls

[006-082] with the belief in God. If the English really believe that they know

[006-083] intuitively, and of their own accord, what is good and evil; if,

[006-084] therefore, they assert that they no longer need Christianity as a

[006-085] guarantee of morality, this in itself is simply the outcome of the

[006-086] dominion of Christian valuations, and a proof of the strength and

[006-087] profundity of this dominion. It only shows that the origin ofEnglish

[006-088] morality has been forgotten, and that its exceedingly relative right to

[006-089] exist is no longer felt. For Englishmen morality is not yet a problem.

[006-090] George Sand.—I have been reading the first “Lettres d’un

[006-091] Voyageur:” like everything that springs from Rousseau’s influence

[006-092] it is false, made-up, blown out, and exaggerated! I cannot endure

[006-093] this bright wall-paper style, any more than I can bear the vulgar

[006-094] striving after generous feelings. The worst feature about it is

[006-095] certainly the coquettish adoption of male attributes by this female,

[006-096] after the manner of ill-bred schoolboys. And how cold she must have

[006-097] been inwardly all the while, this insufferable artist! She wound

[006-098] herself up like a clock—and wrote. As cold as Hugo and Balzac, as

[006-099] cold as all Romanticists are as soon as they begin to write! And how

[006-100] self-complacently she must have lain there, this prolific ink-yielding

[006-101] cow. For she had something German in her (German in the bad sense),

[006-102] just as Rousseau, her master, had;—something which could only have

[006-103] been possible when French taste was declining!—and Renan adores her!...

[006-104] A Moral for Psychologists.Do not go in for any note-book psychology!

[006-105] Never observe for the sake of observing! Such things lead to a false

[006-106] point of view, to a squint, to something forced and exaggerated.

[006-107] To experience things on purpose—this is not a bit of good. In the

[006-108] midst of an experience a man should not turn his eyes upon himself;

[006-109] in such casesany eye becomes the “evil eye.” A born psychologist

[006-110] instinctively avoids seeing for the sake of seeing. And the same holds

[006-111] good of the born painter. Such a man never works “from nature,”—he

[006-112] leaves it to his instinct, to hiscamera obscurato sift and to

[006-113] define the “fact,” “nature,” the “experience.” The general idea,

[006-114] the conclusion, the result, is the only thing that reaches his

[006-115] consciousness. He knows nothing of that wilful process of deducing

[006-116] from particular cases. What is the result when a man sets about this

[006-117] matter differently?—when, for instance, after the manner of Parisian

[006-118] novelists, he goes in for note-book psychology on a large and small

[006-119] scale? Such a man is constantly spying on reality, and every evening

[006-120] he bears home a handful of fresh curios.... But look at the result!—a

[006-121] mass of daubs, at best a piece of mosaic, in any case something heaped

[006-122] together, restless and garish. The Goncourts are the greatest sinners

[006-123] in this respect: they cannot put three sentences together which are not

[006-124] absolutely painful to the eye—the eye of the psychologist. From an

[006-125] artistic standpoint, nature is no model. It exaggerates, distorts, and

[006-126] leaves gaps. Nature is theaccident.To study “from nature” seems to

[006-127] me a bad sign: it betrays submission, weakness, fatalism—this lying

[006-128] in the dust before trivial facts is unworthy of a thorough artist. To

[006-129] seewhat is—is the function of another order of intellects, theanti-artistic,the matter-of-fact. One must knowwhoone is.

[006-130] Concerning the psychology of the artistFor art to be possible at

[006-131] all—that is to say, in order that anæsthetic mode of action and of

[006-132] observation may exist, a certain preliminary physiological state is

[006-133] indispensableecstasy.[1]This state of ecstasy must first have

[006-134] intensified the susceptibility of the whole machine: otherwise, no art

[006-135] is possible. All kinds of ecstasy, however differently produced, have

[006-136] this power to create art, and above all the state dependent upon sexual

[006-137] excitement—this most venerable and primitive form of ecstasy. The same

[006-138] applies to that ecstasy which is the outcome of all great desires,

[006-139] all strong passions; the ecstasy of the feast, of the arena, of the

[006-140] act of bravery, of victory, of all extreme action; the ecstasy of

[006-141] cruelty; the ecstasy of destruction; the ecstasy following upon certain

[006-142] meteorological influences, as for instance that of spring-time, or upon

[006-143] the use of narcotics; and finally the ecstasy of will, that ecstasy

[006-144] which results from accumulated and surging will-power.—The essential

[006-145] feature of ecstasy is the feeling of increased strength and abundance.

[006-146] Actuated by this feeling a man gives of himself to things,he

[006-147] forcesthem to partake of his riches, he does violence to them—this

[006-148] proceeding is calledidealising.Let us rid ourselves of a prejudice

[006-149] here: idealising does not consist, as is generally believed, in a

[006-150] suppression or an elimination of detail or of unessential features.

[006-151] A stupendousaccentuationof the principal characteristics is by

[006-152] far the most decisive factor at work, and in consequence the minor

[006-153] characteristics vanish.

[006-154] In this state a man enriches everything from out his own abundance:

[006-155] what he sees, what he wills, he sees distended, compressed, strong,

[006-156] overladen with power. He transfigures things until they reflect his

[006-157] power,—until they are stamped with his perfection. This compulsion

[006-158] to transfigure into the beautiful is—Art. Everything—even that which

[006-159] he is not,—is nevertheless to such a man a means of rejoicing over

[006-160] himself; in Art man rejoices over himself as perfection.—It is

[006-161] possible to imagine a contrary state, a specifically anti-artistic

[006-162] state of the instincts,—a state in which a man impoverishes,

[006-163] attenuates, and draws the blood from everything. And, truth to tell,

[006-164] history is full of such anti-artists, of such creatures of low

[006-165] vitality who have no choice but to appropriate everything they see

[006-166] and to suck its blood and make it thinner. This is the case with the

[006-167] genuine Christian, Pascal for instance. There is no such thing as a

[006-168] Christian who is also an artist ... Let no one be so childish as to

[006-169] suggest Raphael or any homeopathic Christian of the nineteenth century

[006-170] as an objection to this statement: Raphael said Yea, RaphaeldidYea,—consequently Raphael was no Christian.

[006-172] What is the meaning of the antithetical conceptsApollonianandDionysianwhich I have introduced into the vocabulary of Æsthetic, as

[006-173] representing two distinct modes of ecstasy?—Apollonian ecstasy acts

[006-174] above all as a force stimulating the eye, so that it acquires the power

[006-175] of vision. The painter, thesculptor, the epic poet are essentially

[006-176] visionaries. In the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] state, on the other hand, the whole

[006-177] system of passions is stimulated and intensified, so that it discharges

[006-178] itself by all the means of expression at once, and vents all its power

[006-179] of representation, of imitation, of transfiguration, of transformation,

[006-180] together with every kind of mimicry and histrionic display at the same

[006-181] time. The essential feature remains the facility in transforming,

[006-182] the inability to refrain from reaction (—a similar state to that of

[006-183] certain hysterical patients, who at the slightest hint assume any

[006-184] rôle). It is impossible for the Dionysian artist not to understand any

[006-185] suggestion; no outward sign of emotion escapes him, he possesses

[006-186] the instinct of comprehension and of divination in the highest degree,

[006-187] just as he is capable of the most perfect art of communication. He

[006-188] enters into every skin, into every passion: he is continually changing

[006-189] himself. Music as we understand it to-day is likewise a general

[006-190] excitation and discharge of the emotions; but, notwithstanding this, it

[006-191] is only the remnant of a much richer world of emotional expression, a

[006-192] mere residuum of Dionysian histrionism. For music to be made possible

[006-193] as a special art, quite a number of senses, and particularly the

[006-194] muscular sense, had to be paralysed (at least relatively: for all

[006-195] rhythm still appeals to our muscles to a certain extent): and thus man

[006-196] no longer imitates and represents physically everything he feels, as

[006-197] soon as he feels it. Nevertheless that is the normal Dionysian state,

[006-198] and in any case its primitive state. Music is the slowly attained

[006-199] specialisation of this state at the cost of kindred capacities.

[006-201] The actor, the mime, the dancer, the musician, and the lyricist, are

[006-202] in their instincts fundamentally related; but they have gradually

[006-203] specialised in their particular branch, and become separated—even

[006-204] to the point of contradiction. The lyricist remained united with the

[006-205] musician for the longest period of time; and the actor with the dancer.

[006-206] The architect manifests neither a Dionysian nor an Apollonian state: In

[006-207] his case it is the great act of will, the will that moveth mountains,

[006-208] the ecstasy of the great will which aspires to art. The most powerful

[006-209] men have always inspired architects; the architect has always been

[006-210] under the suggestion of power. In the architectural structure, man’s

[006-211] pride, man’s triumph over gravitation, man’s will to power, assume

[006-212] a visible form. Architecture is a sort of oratory of power by means

[006-213] of forms. Now it is persuasive, even flattering, and at other times

[006-214] merely commanding. The highest sensation of power and security finds

[006-215] expression in grandeur of style. That power which no longer requires to

[006-216] be proved, which scorns to please; which responds only with difficulty;

[006-217] which feels no witnesses around it; which is oblivious of the fact

[006-218] that it is being opposed; which relies on itself fatalistically, and

[006-219] is a law among laws:—such power expresses itself quite naturally in

[006-220] grandeur of style.

[006-222] I have been reading the life of Thomas Carlyle, that unconscious and

[006-223] involuntary farce, that heroico-moral interpretation of dyspeptic

[006-224] moods.—Carlyle, a man of strong words and attitudes, a rhetoricianby

[006-225] necessity, who seems ever to be tormented by the desire of finding some

[006-226] kind of strong faith, and by his inability to do so (—in this respect

[006-227] a typical Romanticist!). To yearn for a strong faith is not the proof

[006-228] of a strong faith, but rather the reverse. If a man have a strong faith

[006-229] he can indulge in the luxury of scepticism; he is strong enough, firm

[006-230] enough, well-knit enough for such a luxury. Carlyle stupefies something

[006-231] in himself by means of thefortissimoof his reverence for men of a

[006-232] strong faith, and his rage over those who are less foolish: he is in

[006-233] sore need of noise. An attitude of constant and passionate dishonesty

[006-234] towards himself—this is hisproprium;by virtue of this he is and

[006-235] remains interesting.—Of course, in England he is admired precisely

[006-236] on account of his honesty. Well, that is English; and in view of the

[006-237] fact that the English are the nation of consummate cant, it is not only

[006-238] comprehensible but also very natural. At bottom, Carlyle is an English

[006-239] atheist who makes it a point of honour not to be one.

[006-256] Anti-Darwin.—As to the famous “struggle for existence,” it seems to

[006-257] me, for the present, to be more of an assumption than a fact. It does

[006-258] occur, but as an exception. The general condition of life is not one

[006-259] of want or famine, but rather of riches, of lavish luxuriance, and even

[006-260] of absurd prodigality,—where there is a struggle, it is a struggle

[006-261] for power. We should not confound Malthus with nature.—Supposing,

[006-262] however, that this struggle exists,—and it does indeed occur,—its

[006-263] result is unfortunately the very reverse of that which the Darwinian

[006-264] school seems to desire, and of that which in agreement with them we

[006-265] also might desire: that is to say, it is always to the disadvantage

[006-266] of the strong, the privileged, and the happy exceptions. Species

[006-267] do not evolve towards perfection: the weak always prevail over the

[006-268] strong—simply because they are the majority, and because they are also

[006-269] the more crafty. Darwin forgot the intellect (—that is English!), the

[006-270] weak have more intellect. In order to acquire intellect, one must be in

[006-271] need of it. One loses it when one no longer needs it. He who possesses

[006-272] strengthflings intellect to the deuce (—“let it go hence!”[2]say the Germans of the present day, “theEmpirewill remain”).

[006-273] As you perceive, intellect to me means caution, patience, craft,

[006-274] dissimulation, great self-control, and everything related to mimicry

[006-275] (what is praised nowadays as virtue is very closely related the latter).

[006-277] Casuistry of a Psychologist.—This man knows mankind: to what purpose

[006-278] does he study his fellows? He wants to derive some small or even

[006-279] great advantages from them,—he is a politician!... That man yonder

[006-280] is also well versed in human nature: and ye tell me that he wishes to

[006-281] draw no personal profit from his knowledge, that he is a thoroughly

[006-282] disinterested person? Examine him a little more closely! Maybe he

[006-283] wishes to derive a more wicked advantage from his possession; namely,

[006-284] to feel superior to men, to be able to look down upon them, no longer

[006-285] to feel one of them. This “disinterested person” is a despiser of

[006-286] mankind; and the former is of a more humane type, whatever appearances

[006-287] may seem to say to the contrary. At least he considers himself the

[006-288] equal of those about him, at least he classifies himself with them.

[006-308] Concerning “the Conscience of the Intellect” Nothing seems to me

[006-309] more uncommon to-day than genuine hypocrisy. I strongly suspect that

[006-310] this growth is unable to flourish in the mild climate of our culture.

[006-311] Hypocrisy belongs to an age of strong faith,—one in which one does

[006-312] not lose one’s own faith in spite of the fact that one has to make

[006-313] anoutward show of holding another faith. Nowadays a man gives it

[006-314] up; or, what is still more common, he acquires a second faith,—in

[006-315] any case, however, he remains honest. Without a doubt it is possible

[006-316] to have a much larger number of convictions at present, than it was

[006-317] formerly:possible—that is to say, allowable,—that is to say,harmless.From this there arises an attitude of toleration towards

[006-318] one’s self. Toleration towards one’s self allows of a greater number

[006-319] of convictions: the latter live comfortably side by side, and they

[006-320] take jolly good care, as all the world does to-day, not to compromise

[006-321] themselves. How does a man compromise himself to-day? When he is

[006-322] consistent; when he pursues a straight course; when he has anything

[006-323] less than five faces; when he is genuine.... I very greatly fear that

[006-324] modern man is much too fond of comfort for certain vices; and the

[006-325] consequence is that the latter are dying out. Everything evil which

[006-326] is the outcome of strength of will—and maybe there is nothing evil

[006-327] without the strengh of will,—degenerates, in our muggy atmosphere,

[006-328] into virtue. The few hypocrites I have known only imitated hypocrisy:

[006-329] like almost every tenth man to-day, they were actors.—

[006-331] Beautiful and Ugly:—Nothing is more relative, let us say, more

[006-332] restricted, than our sense of the beautiful. He who would try to

[006-333] divorce it from the delight man finds in his fellows, would immediately

[006-334] lose his footing. “Beauty in itself,” is simply a word, it is not even

[006-335] a concept. In the beautiful, man postulates himself as the standard of

[006-336] perfection;in exceptional cases he worships himself as that standard.

[006-337] A species has no other alternative than to say “yea” to itself alone,

[006-338] in this way. Its lowest instinct, the instinct of self-preservation and

[006-339] self-expansion, still radiates in such sublimities. Man imagines the

[006-340] world itself to be overflowing with beauty,—he forgets that he is the

[006-341] cause of it all. He alone has endowed it with beauty. Alas! and only

[006-342] with human all-too-human beauty! Truth to tell man reflects himself in

[006-343] things, he thinks everything beautiful that throws his own image back

[006-344] at him. The judgment “beautiful” is the “vanity of his species.” ...

[006-345] A little demon of suspicion may well whisper into the sceptic’s ear:

[006-346] is the world really beautified simply because man thinks it beautiful?

[006-347] He has only humanised it—that is all. But nothing, absolutely nothing

[006-348] proves to us that it is precisely man who is the proper model of

[006-349] beauty. Who knows what sort of figure he would cut in the eyes of a

[006-350] higher judge of taste? He might seem a littleoutré? perhaps even

[006-351] somewhat amusing? perhaps a trifle arbitrary? “O Dionysus, thou divine

[006-352] one, why dost thou pull mine ears?” Ariadne asks on one occasion of

[006-353] her philosophic lover, during one of those famous conversations on the

[006-354] island of Naxos. “I find a sort of humour in thine ears, Ariadne: why

[006-355] are they not a little longer?”

[006-357] Nothing is beautiful; man alone is beautiful: all æsthetic rests on

[006-358] this piece of ingenuousness, it is the first axiom of this science.

[006-359] And now let us straightway add the second to it: nothing is ugly save

[006-360] thedegenerate man,—within these two first principles the realm of

[006-361] æsthetic judgments is confined. From the physiological standpoint,

[006-362] everything ugly weakens and depresses man. It reminds him of decay,

[006-363] danger, impotence; he literally loses strength in its presence. The

[006-364] effect of ugliness may be gauged by the dynamometer. Whenever man’s

[006-365] spirits are downcast, it is a sign that he scents the proximity of

[006-1148] Goethe.—No mere German, but a European event: a magnificent attempt

[006-1149] to overcome the eighteenth century by means of a return to nature, by

[006-1150] means of an ascent to the naturalness of the Renaissance, a kind of

[006-1151] self-overcoming on the part of the century in question.—He bore the

[006-1152] strongest instincts of this century in his breast: its sentimentality,

[006-1153] and idolatry of nature, its anti-historic, idealistic, unreal, and

[006-1154] revolutionary spirit (—the latter is only a form of the unreal). He

[006-1155] enlisted history, natural science, antiquity, as well as Spinoza, and

[006-1156] above all practical activity, in his service. He drew a host of very

[006-1157] definite horizons around him; far from liberating himself from life, he

[006-1158] plunged right into it; he did not give in; he took as much as he could

[006-1159] on his own shoulders, and into his heart. That to which he aspired wastotality; he was opposed to the sundering of reason, sensuality,

[006-1160] feeling and will (as preached with most repulsive scholasticism

[006-1161] by Kant, the antipodes of Goethe); he disciplined himself into a

[006-1162] harmonious whole, hecreatedhimself. Goethe in themidst of an age

[006-1163] of unreal sentiment, was a convinced realist: he said yea to everything

[006-1164] that was like him in this regard,—there was no greater event in his

[006-1165] life than thatens realissimum, surnamed Napoleon. Goethe conceived

[006-1166] a strong, highly-cultured man, skilful in all bodily accomplishments,

[006-1167] able to keep himself in check, having a feeling of reverence for

[006-1168] himself, and so constituted as to be able to risk the full enjoyment

[006-1169] of naturalness in all its rich profusion and be strong enough for this

[006-1170] freedom; a man of tolerance, not out of weakness but out of strength,

[006-1171] because he knows how to turn to his own profit that which would ruin

[006-1172] the mediocre nature; a man unto whom nothing is any longer forbidden,

[006-1173] unless it be weakness either as a vice or as a virtue. Such a spirit,become free, appears in the middle of the universe with a feeling

[006-1174] of cheerful and confident fatalism; he believes that only individual

[006-1175] things are bad, and that as a whole the universe justifies and, affirms

[006-1176] itself—He no longer denies.... But such a faith is the highest Of

[006-1177] all faiths: I christened it with the name of Dionysus.
