# David Strauss

## Biography

David Friedrich Strauss (1808–1874) was a German Protestant theologian and writer whose *The Life of Jesus, Critically Examined* (*Das Leben Jesu*, 1835) caused a sensation across [[europe|Europe]] by treating the Gospel narratives not as divine revelation but as *myth* — the unconscious, collective poetic creation of the early [[jesus|Christ]]ian community. The book cost Strauss his academic career at Tübingen but established him as the leading figure of the German "free-[[soul|spirit]]" movement in theology. His later works included *The Old Faith and the New* (1872), an attempt to construct a post-[[jesus|Christ]]ian ethical system on the foundations of [[darwin|Darwin]]ism and modern science. Nietzsche's first published book, *David Strauss: The Confessor and the Writer* (1873) — the first of the *Untimely Meditations* — was a sustained polemic against Strauss's cultural influence.

## Nietzsche's View

In *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, Nietzsche's single reference to Strauss occurs in the chapter *Things the Germans Lack*, diagnosing the poverty of German higher education:

> leading German free-spirit, the *clever* David Strauss, into the author of a gospel for beer-house intellectuals. [005-048]

The word "clever" (*klug*) is set in italics to signal its ironic charge. Cleverness is not intelligence, not wisdom, not culture — it is the facile mental agility that contents itself with exposing the naïveté of tradition without producing anything of value to replace it. Strauss, the great demythologiser, became in Nietzsche's eyes the author of "a gospel for beer-house intellectuals" (*Bierbank-Evangelium*) — a replacement religion for the half-educated, substituting [[darwin|Darwin]]ian scientism and bourgeois comfort for the [[jesus|Christ]]ian faith he had dismantled.

This single sentence compresses the argument of Nietzsche's entire *Untimely Meditation* against Strauss. The "free-[[soul|spirit]]" who frees himself from dogma but not from the cultural mediocrity that dogma had at least disciplined, becomes a more dangerous figure than the believer: the believer at least stands within a tradition that shapes instincts; the "free-[[soul|spirit]]" of Strauss's type merely exchanges one form of intellectual laziness for another, congratulating himself on his liberation while sinking deeper into the philistinism he mistakes for enlightenment.

Nietzsche's own youthful admiration for Strauss — he "relished the work of the incomparable Strauss" as a young philologist — lends the later judgment its bitter authority: he is condemning a version of himself, the path not taken.

## Key Works

- *The Life of Jesus, Critically Examined* (1835) — The landmark work of biblical criticism that interpreted the Gospels as mythical constructions, demolishing their historical credibility
- *The Old Faith and the New* (1872) — Strauss's attempt to construct a post-[[jesus|Christ]]ian worldview on scientific foundations; the immediate target of Nietzsche's polemic in the first *Untimely Meditation*
