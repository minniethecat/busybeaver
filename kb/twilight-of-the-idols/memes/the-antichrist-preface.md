#### PREFACE

[010-001] This book belongs to the very few. Maybe not one of them is yet alive;

[010-002] unless he be of those who understand my [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]]. HowcanI

[010-003] confound myself with those who to-day already find a hearing?—Only the

[010-004] day after to-morrow belongs to me. Some are born posthumously.

[010-005] I am only too well aware of the conditions under which a man

[010-006] understands me, and thennecessarilyunderstands. He must be

[010-007] intellectually upright to the point of hardness, in order even to

[010-008] endure my seriousness and my passion. He must be used to living on

[010-009] mountain-tops,—and to feeling the wretched gabble of politics and

[010-010] national egotismbeneathhim. He must have become indifferent; he

[010-011] must never inquire whether truth is profitable or whether it may

[010-012] prove fatal.... Possessing from strength a predilection for questions

[010-013] for which no one has enough courage nowadays; the courage for theforbidden;his predestination must be the labyrinth. The experience

[010-014] of seven solitudes. New ears for new music. New eyes for the most

[010-015] remote things. A new conscience for truths which hitherto have

[010-016] remained dumb. And the will to economy on a large scale: to husband

[010-017] his strength and his enthusiasm.... He must honour himself, he must

[010-018] love himself; he must be absolutely free with regardto himself....

[010-019] Very well then! Such men alone are my readers, my proper readers,

[010-020] my preordained readers: of what account are the rest?—the rest are

[010-021] simply—humanity.—One must be superior to humanity in power, in

[010-022] loftiness of soul,—in contempt.

[010-023] FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE.

[010-024] Let us look each other in the face. We are hyperboreans,—we know

[010-025] well enough how far outside the crowd we stand. “Thou wilt find the

[010-026] way to the Hyperboreans neither by land nor by water”: Pindar already

[010-027] knew this much about us. Beyond the north, the ice, and death—our

[010-028] life, our happiness....We discovered happiness; we know the way; we

[010-029] found the way out of thousands of years of labyrinth. Whoelsewould

[010-030] have found it?—Not the modern man, surely?—“I do not know where I

[010-031] am or what I am to do; I am everything that knows not where it is or

[010-032] what to do,”—sighs the modern man. We were made quite ill bythismodernity,—with its indolent peace, its cowardly compromise, and the

[010-033] whole of the virtuous filth of its Yea and Nay. This tolerance andlargeur de cœurwhich “forgives” everything because it “understands”

[010-034] everything, is a Sirocco for us. We prefer to live amid ice than to

[010-035] be breathed upon by modern virtues and other southerly winds!... We

[010-036] were brave enough; we spared neither ourselves nor others: but we

[010-037] were very far from knowing whither to direct our bravery. We were

[010-038] becoming gloomy; people called us fatalists.Ourfate—it was the

[010-039] abundance, the tensionand the storing up of power. We thirsted for

[010-040] thunderbolts and great deeds; we kept at the most respectful distance

[010-041] from the joy of the weakling, from “resignation.” ... Thunder was in

[010-042] our air, that part of nature which we are, became overcast—for we had

[010-043] no direction.The formula of our happiness: a Yea, a Nay, a straight

[010-044] line, a goal.

[010-045] What is good? All that enhances the feeling of power, the Will to

[010-046] Power, and power itself in man. What is bad?—All that proceeds

[010-047] from weakness. What is happiness?—The feeling that power isincreasing,—that resistance has been overcome.

[010-048] Not contentment, but more power; not peace at any price, but war; not

[010-049] virtue, but efficiency[1](virtue in the Renaissance sense,virtu,free from all moralic acid). The weak and the botched shall perish:

[010-050] first principle of our humanity. And they ought even to be helped to

[010-051] perish.

[010-052] What is more harmful than any vice?—Practical sympathy with all the

[010-053] botched and the weak—Christianity.

[010-054] The problem I set in this work is not what will replace mankind in the

[010-055] order of living being! (—Man is anend—); but, what type of man

[010-056] must bereared,must bewilled,as having the higher value, as

[010-057] being the most worthy of life and the surest guarantee of the future.

[010-058] This more valuable type has appeared often enough already: but as a

[010-059] happy accident, as an exception, never aswilled.He has rather been

[010-060] precisely the most feared; hitherto he has been almost the terrible in

[010-061] itself;—and from out the very fear he provoked there arose the will

[010-062] to rear the type which has how been reared,attained:the domestic

[010-063] animal, the gregarious animal, the sick animal man,—the Christian.

[010-064] Mankind doesnotrepresent a development towards a better, stronger

[010-065] or higher type, in the sense in which this is supposed to occur to-day.

[010-066] “Progress” is merely a modern idea—that is to say, a false idea.[2]The modern European is still far below the European of the Renaissance

[010-067] in value. The process of evolution does not by any means imply

[010-068] elevation, enhancement and increasing strength.

[010-069] On the other hand isolated and individual cases are continually

[010-070] succeeding in different places on earth, as the outcome of the most

[010-071] different cultures, and in these ahigher typecertainly manifests

[010-072] itself: something which by the side of mankind in general, represents a

[010-073] kind of superman. Such lucky strokes of great success have always been

[010-074] possible and will perhaps always be possible. And even whole races,

[010-075] tribes and nations may in certain circumstances represent suchlucky

[010-076] strokes.

[010-077] We must not deck out and adorn Christianity: it has waged a deadly

[010-078] war upon thishighertype of man, it has set a ban upon all the

[010-079] fundamental instincts of this type, and has distilled evil and the

[010-080] devil himself out of these instincts:—the strong man as the typical

[010-081] pariah, the villain. Christianity has sided with everything weak, low,

[010-082] and botched; it has made an ideal out ofantagonismtowards all the

[010-083] self-preservative instincts of strong life: it has corrupted even the

[010-084] reason of the strongest intellects, by teaching that the highest values

[010-085] of intellectuality are sinful, misleading and full of temptations.

[010-086] The most lamentable example of this was the corruption of Pascal, who

[010-087] believed in the perversion of his reason through original sin, whereas

[010-088] it had only been perverted by his Christianity.

[010-089] A painful and ghastly spectacle has just risen before my eyes. I tore

[010-090] down the curtain which concealed mankind’scorruption.This word in

[010-091] my mouth is at least secure from the suspicion that it contains a moral

[010-092] charge against mankind. It is—I would fain emphasise this again—free

[010-093] from moralic acid: to such an extent is this so, that I am most

[010-094] thoroughly conscious of the corruption in question precisely in those

[010-095] quarters in which hitherto people have aspired with most determination

[010-096] to “virtue” and to “godliness.” As you have already surmised, I

[010-097] understand corruption in the sense ofdecadence.What I maintain is

[010-098] this, that all the values uponwhich mankind builds its highest hopes

[010-099] and desires aredecadentvalues.

[010-100] I call an animal, a species, an individual corrupt, when it loses its

[010-101] instincts, when it selects andprefersthat which is detrimental to

[010-102] it. A history of the “higher feelings,” of “human ideals”—and it is

[010-103] not impossible that I shall have to write it—would almost explain why

[010-104] man is so corrupt. Life itself, to my mind, is nothing more nor less

[010-105] than the instinct of growth, of permanence, of accumulating forces,

[010-106] of power: where the will to power is lacking, degeneration sets in.

[010-107] My contention is that all the highest values of mankindlackthis

[010-108] will,—that the values of decline and ofnihilismare exercising the

[010-109] sovereign power under the cover of the holiest names.

[010-110] Christianity is called the religion ofpity.—Pity is opposed to

[010-111] the tonic passions which enhance the energy of the feeling of life:

[010-112] its action is depressing. A man loses power when he pities. By

[010-113] means of pity the drain on strength which suffering itself already

[010-114] introduces into the world is multiplied a thousandfold. Through pity,

[010-115] suffering itself becomes infectious; in certain circumstances it may

[010-116] lead to a total loss of life and vital energy, which is absurdly

[010-117] put of proportion to the magnitude of the cause (—the case of the

[010-118] death of the Nazarene). This is the first standpoint; but there is a

[010-119] still more important one. Supposing one measures pity according to

[010-120] the value of the reactions it usually stimulates, its danger to life

[010-121] appears in a much more telling light On the whole, pity thwarts the

[010-122] lawof development which is the law of selection. It preserves that

[010-123] which is ripe for death, it fights in favour of the disinherited and

[010-124] the condemned of life; thanks to the multitude of abortions of all

[010-125] kinds which it maintains in life, it lends life itself a sombre and

[010-126] questionable aspect. People have dared to call pity a virtue (—in

[010-127] everynobleculture it is considered as a weakness—); people went

[010-128] still further, they exalted it tothevirtue, the root and origin

[010-129] of all virtues,—but, of course, what must never be forgotten is the

[010-130] fact that this was done from the standpoint of a philosophy which

[010-131] was nihilistic, and on whose shield the deviceThe Denial of Lifewas inscribed. Schopenhauer was right in this respect: by means of

[010-132] pity, life is denied and mademore worthy of denial,—pity is

[010-133] thepraxisof [[nihilism|Nihilism]]. I repeat, this depressing and infectious

[010-134] instinct thwarts those instincts which aim at the preservation and

[010-135] enhancement of the value life: bymultiplyingmisery quite as much

[010-136] as by preserving all that is miserable, it is the principal agent in

[010-137] promoting decadence,—pity exhorts people to nothing, tononentity!But they do not say “nonentity” they say “Beyond,” or “God,” or “the

[010-138] true life”; or Nirvana, or Salvation, or Blessedness, instead. This

[010-139] innocent rhetoric, which belongs to the realm of the religio-moral

[010-140] idiosyncrasy, immediately appears to bevery much less innocentif

[010-141] one realises what the tendency is which here tries to drape itself in

[010-142] the mantle of sublime expressions—the tendency of hostility to life.

[010-143] Schopenhauer was hostile to life: that is why he elevated pity to a

[010-144] virtue.... Aristotle, as you know, recognised in pity a morbid and

[010-145] dangerousstate, of which it was wise to rid one’s self from time to

[010-146] time by a purgative: he regarded tragedy as a purgative. For the sake

[010-147] of the instinct of life, it would certainly seem necessary to find some

[010-148] means of lancing any such morbid and dangerous accumulation of pity, as

[010-149] that which possessed Schopenhauer (and unfortunately the whole of our

[010-150] literary and artistic decadence as well, from St Petersburg to Paris,

[010-151] from Tolstoi to Wagner), if only to make itburst....Nothing is

[010-152] more unhealthy in the midst of our unhealthy modernity, than Christian

[010-153] pity. To be doctorshere,to be inexorablehere,to wield the knife

[010-154] effectivelyhere,—all this is our business, all this isourkind of love to our fellows, this is what makesusphilosophers, us

[010-155] hyperboreans!—

[010-156] It is necessary to state whom we regard as our antithesis:—the

[010-158] veins—the whole of our philosophy.... A man must have had his very

[010-160] in his own soul; he must almost have perished through it, in order

[010-161] to be unable to treat this matter lightly (—the free-spiritedness

[010-162] of our friends the naturalists and physiologists is, in my opinion,

[010-163] ajoke,—what they lack in these questions is passion, what they

[010-164] lack is having suffered from these questions—). This poisoning

[010-165] extends much further than people think: I unearthed the “arrogant”

[010-167] idealists,—wherever, thanks to superior antecedents, they claim the

[010-168] right to rise above reality and to regard it with suspicion.... Like

[010-169] the priest the idealist has every grandiloquent concept in his hand

[010-170] (—and not only in his hand!), he wields them all with kindly contempt

[010-171] against the “understanding,” the “senses,” “honours,” “decent living,”

[010-172] “science”; he regards such things asbeneathhim, as detrimental and

[010-173] seductive forces, upon the face of which, “the Spirit” moves in pure

[010-174] absoluteness:—as if humility, chastity, poverty, in a wordholiness,had not done incalculably more harm to life hitherto, than any sort of

[010-175] horror and vice.... Pure spirit is pure falsehood.... As long as the

[010-176] priest, theprofessionaldenier, calumniator and poisoner of life, is

[010-177] considered as thehighestkind of man, there can be no answer to the

[010-178] question, whatistruth? Truth has already been turned topsy-turvy,

[010-181] It is upon this theological instinct that I wage war. I find traces

[010-182] of it everywhere. Whoever has the blood of theologians in his veins,

[010-183] stands from the start in a false and dishonest position to all things.

[010-184] The pathos which grows out of this state, is calledFaith:that is

[010-185] to say, to shut one’s eyes once and for all, in order not to suffer

[010-186] at the sight of incurable falsity. People convert this faulty view of

[010-187] all things into a moral, a virtue, a thing of holiness. They endow

[010-188] their distorted vision with a good conscience,—they claim that nootherpoint ofview is any longer of value, once theirs has been

[010-189] made sacrosanct with the names “God,” “Salvation,” “Eternity.” I

[010-190] unearthed the instinct of the theologian everywhere: it is the most

[010-191] universal, and actually the most subterranean form of falsity on earth.

[010-192] That which a theologian considers true,mustof necessity be false:

[010-193] this furnishes almost the criterion of truth. It is his most profound

[010-194] self-preservative instinct which forbids reality ever to attain to

[010-195] honour in any way, or even to raise its voice. Whithersoever the

[010-196] influence of the theologian extends,valuationsare topsy-turvy,

[010-197] and the concepts “true” and “false” have necessarily changed places:

[010-198] that which is most deleterious to life, is here called “true,” that

[010-199] which enhances it, elevates it, says Yea to it, justifies it and

[010-200] renders it triumphant, is called “false.” ... If it should happen that

[010-201] theologians,viathe “conscience” either of princes or of the people,

[010-202] stretch out their hand for power, let us not be in any doubt as to

[010-203] what results therefrom each time, namely:—the will to the end, thenihilisticwill to power....
