# Eternal Recurrence

## Definition

The Eternal Recurrence (ewige Wiederkunft) is Nietzsche's most challenging idea: the proposition that every event in the universe repeats itself infinitely. It functions not as a cosmological doctrine to be proven, but as an existential test — can one affirm life so completely that one would will its eternal repetition, in every detail, without reservation?

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

The Eternal Recurrence receives its most sustained treatment in the section titled *The Eternal Recurrence* (Chapter 11). Nietzsche presents it as the ultimate counter to nihilism: if one can affirm the eternal return of all things — including suffering, failure, and loss — then one has overcome the nihilistic rejection of life.

The concept is closely tied to the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] affirmation of life that Nietzsche celebrates in his discussion of [[tragedy|Greek tragedy]] and contrasts with [[jesus|Christ]]ian world-denial.

## Critical Context

The eternal recurrence is perhaps Nietzsche's most misunderstood concept. While sometimes read as a literal cosmological theory, most interpreters (following Heidegger, Deleuze, and Klossowski) treat it as an ethical-existential test. It radicalizes Kant's categorical imperative: act as if each action would be repeated eternally.
