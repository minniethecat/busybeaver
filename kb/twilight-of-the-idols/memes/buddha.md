# Buddha (Siddhartha Gautama)

## Biography

Siddhartha Gautama (c. 563–483 BCE), the Buddha ("the awakened one"), was a prince of the Shakya clan in what is now Nepal, who renounced his royal life at the age of twenty-nine after encountering the "four sights" — an old man, a sick man, a corpse, and a wandering ascetic. After years of extreme [[ascetic-ideal|asceticism]] proved fruitless, he attained enlightenment under the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gaya, having discovered the "Middle Way" between indulgence and self-mortification. His teaching — the Four Noble [[truth|Truth]]s and the Eightfold Path — diagnosed all existence as *dukkha* (suffering, unsatisfactoriness) and prescribed the extinction of craving (*taṇhā*) as the path to liberation (*nirvāṇa*). Over the next forty-five years, he established a monastic order (*saṅgha*) that spread across the [[india|India]]n subcontinent and, in subsequent centuries, throughout Asia.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's references to [[buddhism|Buddhism]] in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* are concentrated in *The Antichrist* (ch10, included in the *Twilight* volume). His treatment is notably respectful — indeed, [[buddhism|Buddhism]] is the one religion Nietzsche discusses without the sustained polemical violence he directs at [[jesus|Christ]]ianity.

The first reference frames [[buddhism|Buddhism]] as a response to a specific physiological condition:

> A state of depression set in, which Buddha sought to combat with hygienic measures. [010-501]

The word "hygienic" (*hygienisch*) is crucial. For Nietzsche, [[buddhism|Buddhism]] is not a faith promising salvation through belief; it is a *regimen* — a set of practices designed to address a determinate set of symptoms. Where [[jesus|Christ]]ianity "is in *need* of illness" and makes people sicker, [[buddhism|Buddhism]] takes the fact of suffering as a starting point and prescribes practical measures against it.

Nietzsche sharpens the contrast with [[jesus|Christ]]ianity in a remarkable passage:

> In Buddha's doctrine egoism is a duty: the "one thing needful," the question of "how canst thou be freed from suffering" rules and determines the whole of the spiritual regimen. [010-525]

This is Buddhist "egoism" in Nietzsche's sense — not selfishness in the vulgar sense, but the disciplined focus on one's own liberation as the only path. [[buddhism|Buddhism]], unlike [[jesus|Christ]]ianity, does not demand love of the neighbour; it demands that each person address their own suffering through their own effort. The parallel with Nietzsche's own ethical individualism is unmistakable.

The third reference is more oblique. Discussing the type of the "Redeemer" in the New Testament, Nietzsche notes that Jesus "strikes us as a Buddha on a soil only very little propitious to it" [010-959]. The implication is that Jesus might have been something like a Buddhist teacher — a practitioner of gentleness, non-resistance, and detachment — but was fatally misconstrued by the Pauline church, which transformed his quietism into a militant religion of *ressentiment*.

Nietzsche's [[buddhism|Buddhism]] is, to some extent, a construction — a foil for [[jesus|Christ]]ianity that serves his polemical purposes. But the construction is coherent: [[buddhism|Buddhism]] represents a **noble pessimism**, a clear-eyed acknowledgment of suffering that responds not with the slave's fantasy of revenge but with the aristocrat's discipline of self-overcoming. The "egoism" of [[buddhism|Buddhism]] — "the 'one thing needful'" [010-525] — is not far from Nietzsche's own insistence that the highest task is the cultivation of the self.

## Key Works

- The Pāli Canon (*Tipiṭaka*) — The canonical collection of Buddhist scriptures, containing the Buddha's discourses (*Suttas*), monastic discipline (*Vinaya*), and philosophical analysis (*Abhidhamma*)
- *Dhammapada* — The most widely read collection of the Buddha's sayings, emphasising self-mastery and the path to liberation
- Nietzsche's knowledge of [[buddhism|Buddhism]] came primarily through Schopenhauer, Paul Deussen, and contemporary [[europe|Europe]]an scholarship

## Related Figures

- [[jesus]] — the "Redeemer" type whom Nietzsche reads as "a Buddha on a soil only very little propitious to it"
- [[schopenhauer]] — the [[europe|Europe]]an philosopher whose pessimism most closely approximated the Buddhist diagnosis of suffering
- [[paul-the-apostle]] — the figure who, in Nietzsche's reading, transformed the gentle quietism of Jesus into the militant anti-life religion of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity — the opposite of [[buddhism|Buddhism]]'s honest pessimism
