# Thomas Carlyle

## Biography

Thomas Carlyle (1795–1881) was a Scottish historian, essayist, and social critic. Born in Ecclefechan, Scotland, he abandoned his training for the Calvinist ministry and became one of the most influential Victorian men of letters. His major works include *Sartor Resartus*, a [[soul|spirit]]ual autobiography in fictional form; *The [[french-revolution|French Revolution]]*, a dramatic historical narrative; and *On Heroes, Hero-Worship, and the Heroic in History*, which argued that history is shaped by great men.

## Philosophical Contributions

Carlyle's thought centered on the role of the heroic individual in history, the [[soul|spirit]]ual emptiness of modern industrial society, and the necessity of strong faith. He rejected materialism and utilitarianism in favor of a vision of the universe as a divine revelation perceptible through work and duty. His "gospel of work" held that meaningful labor was the cure for the skepticism and despair of the age. His vehement prose style — marked by rhetorical excess, German-influenced syntax, and prophetic fervor — made him both celebrated and parodied.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's assessment of Carlyle was devastating. In *Skirmishes*, he called Carlyle "[[pessimism|Pessimism]] after undigested meals" [006-007] and elaborated: "I have been reading the life of Thomas Carlyle, that unconscious and involuntary farce, that heroico-moral interpretation of dyspeptic moods" [006-222]. Nietzsche diagnosed Carlyle as a Romanticist tormented by the desire for a strong faith he could never achieve: "To yearn for a strong faith is not the proof of a strong faith, but rather the reverse" [006-227–228]. He saw Carlyle's constant noise and rhetorical *fortissimo* as a method of self-stupefaction [006-231], and concluded with the sharp observation that "at bottom, Carlyle is an English atheist who makes it a point of honour not to be one" [006-238–239].
