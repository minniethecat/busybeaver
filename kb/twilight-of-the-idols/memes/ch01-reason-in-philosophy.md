#### “REASON” IN PHILOSOPHY

[001-001] You ask me what all idiosyncrasy is in philosophers? ... For instance

[001-002] their lack of the historical sense, their hatred even of the idea of

[001-003] Becoming, their Egyptianism. They imagine that they do honour to a

[001-004] thing by divorcing it from historysub specie æterni,—when they

[001-005] make a mummy of it. All the ideas that philosophers have treated for

[001-006] thousands of years, have been mummied concepts; nothing real has ever

[001-007] come out of their hands alive. These idolaters of concepts merely

[001-008] kill and stuff things when they worship,—they threaten the life of

[001-009] everything they adore. Death, change, age, as well as procreation and

[001-010] growth, are in their opinion objections,—even refutations. That which

[001-011] is cannot evolve; that which evolvesisnot. Now all of them believe,

[001-012] and even with desperation, in Being. But, as they cannot lay hold of

[001-013] it, they try to discover reasons why this privilege is withheld from

[001-014] them. “Some merely apparent quality, some deception must be the cause

[001-015] of our not being able to ascertain the nature of Being: where is the

[001-016] deceiver?” “We have him,” they cry rejoicing, “it is sensuality!” These

[001-017] senses,which in other things are so immoral,cheat us concerning the

[001-018] true world. Moral: we must get rid of the deception of the senses, of

[001-019] Becoming, of history, offalsehood.—History is nothing more than the

[001-020] belief in the senses, the belief in falsehood. Moral: we must say “no”

[001-021] to everything in which the senses believe: to all the rest of mankind:

[001-022] all that belongs to the “people.” Let us be philosophers, mummies,

[001-023] monotono-theists, grave-diggers!—And above all, away with thebody,this wretchedidée fixeof the senses, infected with all the faults

[001-024] of logic that exist, refuted, even impossible, although it be impudent

[001-025] enough to pose as if it were real!

[001-026] With a feeling of great reverence I except the name ofHeraclitus.If the rest of the philosophic gang rejected the evidences of the

[001-027] senses, because the latter revealed a state of multifariousness and

[001-028] change, he rejected the same evidence because it revealed things as if

[001-029] they possessed permanence and unity. Even [[heraclitus|Heraclitus]] did an injustice

[001-030] to the senses. The latter lie neither as the Eleatics believed them

[001-031] to lie, nor as he believed them to lie,—they do not lie at all. The

[001-032] interpretations we give to their evidence is what first introduces

[001-033] falsehood into it; for instance the lie of unity, the lie of matter,

[001-034] of substance and of permanence. Reason is the cause of our falsifying

[001-035] the evidence of the senses. In so far as the senses show us a state

[001-036] of Becoming, of transiency, and of change, they do not lie. But in

[001-037] declaring that Being was an empty illusion, Heraclitus will remain

[001-038] eternally right. The “apparent” world is the only world: the “true

[001-039] world” is no more than a false adjunct thereto.

[001-040] And what delicate instruments of observation we have in our senses!

[001-041] This human nose, for instance, of which no philosopher has yet spoken

[001-042] with reverence and gratitude, is, for the present, the most finely

[001-043] adjusted instrument at our disposal: it is able to register even such

[001-044] slight changes of movement as the spectroscope would be unable to

[001-045] record. Our scientific triumphs at the present day extend precisely

[001-046] so far as we have accepted the evidence of our senses,—as we have

[001-047] sharpened and armed them, and learned to follow them up to the

[001-048] end. What remains is abortive and not yet science—that is to say,

[001-049] metaphysics, theology, psychology, epistemology, or formal science, or

[001-050] a doctrine of symbols, like logic and its applied form mathematics.

[001-051] In all these things reality does not come into consideration at all,

[001-052] even as a problem; just as little as does the question concerning the

[001-053] general value of such a convention of symbols as logic.

[001-054] The other idiosyncrasy of philosophers is no less dangerous; it

[001-055] consists in confusing the last and the first things. They place that

[001-056] which makes its appearance last—unfortunately! for it ought not

[001-057] to appear at all!—the “highest concept,” that is to say, the most

[001-058] general, the emptiest, the last cloudy streak of evaporating reality,

[001-059] at the beginning as the beginning. This again is only their manner of

[001-060] expressing their veneration: the highest thing must not have grown out

[001-061] of the lowest, it must not have grown at all.... Moral: everything

[001-062] of the first rank must becausa sui.To have been derived from

[001-063] something else, is as good as an objection, it sets the value of a

[001-064] thing in question. All superior values are of the first rank, all the

[001-065] highest concepts—that of Being, of the Absolute, of Goodness, of

[001-066] Truth, and of Perfection; all these things cannot have been evolved,

[001-067] they must therefore becausa sui.All these things cannot however be

[001-068] unlike one another, they cannot be opposed to one another. Thus they

[001-069] attain to their stupendous concept “God.” The last, most attenuated and

[001-070] emptiest thing is postulated as the first thing, as the absolute cause,

[001-071] asens realissimum.Fancy humanity having to take the brain diseases

[001-072] of morbid cobweb-spinners seriously!—And it has paid dearly for having

[001-073] done so.

[001-074] —Against this let us set the different manner in which we (—you

[001-075] observe that I am courteous enough to say “we”) conceive the problem of

[001-076] the error and deceptiveness of things. Formerly people regarded change

[001-077] and evolution in general as the proof of appearance, as a sign of the

[001-078] fact that something must be there that leads us astray. To-day, on

[001-079] the other hand, we realise that precisely as far as the rational bias

[001-080] forces us to postulate unity, identity, permanence, substance, cause,

[001-081] materiality and being, we are in a measure involved in error, driven

[001-082] necessarily to error; however certain we may feel, as the result of a

[001-083] strict examination of the matter, that the error lies here. It is just

[001-084] the same here as with the motion of the sun: In its case it was our

[001-085] eyes that were wrong; in the matter of the concepts above mentioned it

[001-086] is our language itself that pleadsmost constantly in their favour.

[001-087] In its origin language belongs to an age of the most rudimentary

[001-088] forms of psychology: if we try to conceive of the first conditions of

[001-089] the metaphysics of language,i.e.in plain English, of reason, we

[001-090] immediately find ourselves in the midst of a system of fetichism. For

[001-091] here, the doer and his deed are seen in all circumstances, will is

[001-092] believed in as a cause in general; the ego is taken for granted, the

[001-093] ego as Being, and as substance, and the faith in the ego as substance

[001-094] is projected into all things—in this way, alone, the concept “thing”

[001-095] is created. Being is thought into and insinuated into everything as

[001-096] cause; from the concept “ego,” alone, can the concept “Being” proceed.

[001-097] At the beginning stands the tremendously fatal error of supposing the

[001-098] will to be something that actuates,—a faculty. Now we know that it

[001-099] is only a word.[1]Very much later, in a world a thousand times more

[001-100] enlightened, the assurance, the subjective certitude, in the handling

[001-101] of the categories of reason came into the minds of philosophers as a

[001-102] surprise. They concluded that these categories could not be derived

[001-103] from experience,—on the contrary, the whole of experience rather

[001-104] contradicts them.Whence do they come therefore?In India, as in

[001-105] Greece, the same mistake was made: “we must already once have lived

[001-106] in a higher world (—instead of in a much lower one, which would have

[001-107] been the truth!), we must have been divine, for we possessreason!”

[001-108] ... Nothing indeed has exercised a more simple power of persuasion

[001-109] hitherto than the error of Being, as it was formulated by the Eleatics

[001-110] for instance: in its favour are every word and every sentence that we

[001-111] utter!—Even the opponents of the Eleatics succumbed to the seductive

[001-112] powers of their concept of Being. Among others there was Democritus in

[001-113] his discovery of the atom. “Reason” in language!—oh what a deceptive

[001-114] old witch it has been! I fear we shall never be rid of God, so long as

[001-115] we still believe in grammar.

[001-116] People will feel grateful to me if I condense a point of view, which

[001-117] is at once so important and so new, into four theses: by this means

[001-118] I shall facilitate comprehension, and shall likewise challenge

[001-119] contradiction.

[001-120] Proposition One.The reasons upon which the apparent nature of “this”

[001-121] world have been based, rather tend to prove its reality,—any other

[001-122] kind of reality defies demonstration.

[001-123] Proposition Two.The characteristics with which man has endowed

[001-124] the “true Being” of things, are the characteristics of non-Being, ofnonentity.The “true world” has been erected upon a contradiction of

[001-125] the real world; and it is indeed an apparent world, seeing that it is

[001-126] merely amoralo-opticaldelusion.

[001-127] Proposition Three.There is no sense in spinning yarns about another

[001-128] world, provided, of course, that we do not possess a mighty instinct

[001-129] which urges us to slander, belittle, and cast suspicion upon this life:

[001-130] in this case we should be avenging ourselves onthis life with the

[001-131] phantasmagoria of “another,” of a “better” life.

[001-132] Proposition Four.To divide the world into a “true” and an “apparent”

[001-133] world, whether after the manner of Christianity or of Kant (after all

[001-134] a Christian in disguise), is only a sign of decadence,—a symptom ofdegeneratinglife. The fact that the artist esteems the appearance

[001-135] of a thing higher than reality, is no objection to this statement. For

[001-136] “appearance” signifies once more reality here, but in a selected,

[001-137] strengthened and corrected form. The tragic artist is no pessimist,—he

[001-138] saysYeato everything questionable and terrible, he is [[dionysian|Dionysian]].
