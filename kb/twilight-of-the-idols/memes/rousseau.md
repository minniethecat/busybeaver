# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

## Biography

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer whose political philosophy influenced the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] and whose ideas on education and human nature shaped the Romantic movement. For Nietzsche, Rousseau represents the archetype of the modern moralist whose ideals mask a deeper instinct for revenge.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's assessment of Rousseau is relentlessly critical. He places Rousseau among his "Impossible People" — figures whose moral posturing conceals something more troubling:

> Seneca, or the toreador of virtue — Rousseau, or the return to nature *in impuris naturalibus*. [006-001–002]

The phrase *in impuris naturalibus* (in impure naturalness) is a deliberate inversion of Rousseau's ideal of natural purity. For Nietzsche, Rousseau's "return to nature" is not a return to innocence but a regression into instinct dressed as virtue.

Nietzsche identifies Rousseau as the philosophical ancestor of *ressentiment* — the moralizing revenge of the weak against the strong:

> George Sand is next of kin to Rousseau's resentful spirit: consequently she is a Romanticist — for beneath all romanticism Rousseau's instinct for revenge grunts and thirsts. [006-035–036]

This passage links Rousseau to both [[george-sand|George Sand]] and the broader Romantic movement, diagnosing Romanticism itself as an expression of *ressentiment*. Everything that flows from Rousseau's influence is, in Nietzsche's view, tainted by this underlying drive.

## The "Return to Nature"

Nietzsche's most extended treatment of Rousseau appears in *Skirmishes in a War with the Age*, where he contrasts Rousseau's "return to nature" with his own conception:

> But Rousseau — whither did he want to return? Rousseau, this first modern man, idealist and *canaille* in one person. [006-1123–1124]

The term *canaille* (rabble, scum) reveals Nietzsche's contempt. Rousseau is the "first modern man" precisely because he embodies the contradiction of modern morality: high ideals combined with base instincts.

> I hate Rousseau, even in the Revolution itself: the Revolution is the world-historical expression of this duality of idealist and *canaille*. [006-1129]

Nietzsche extends his hatred of Rousseau to the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]], which he sees as Rousseau's philosophy enacted in history. The Revolution's bloody egalitarianism is, for Nietzsche, the logical consequence of Rousseau's moralizing.

He further characterizes the Revolution's legacy as "Rousseaussque morality — the so-called 'truths' of the Revolution" [006-1133], linking Rousseau's influence to the modern faith in equality and universal rights that Nietzsche regards as a symptom of decadence.

## Religious Fanaticism

In *The Antichrist*, Nietzsche groups Rousseau with other figures he sees as religious fanatics who shaped modernity:

> Savonarola, Luther, Rousseau, Robespierre, Saint-Simon — these are the fanatics. [010-1944]

This grouping is significant: Rousseau is placed in a lineage that runs from the religious reformer Savonarola through [[luther|Luther]] to the revolutionary Robespierre. For Nietzsche, Rousseau's secular moralizing is continuous with the [[jesus|Christ]]ian moral fanaticism it superficially rejected.

## Key Works

- *Discourse on the Sciences and Arts* (1750) — the work that made Rousseau famous, arguing that civilization corrupts human virtue
- *The Social Contract* (1762) — his major work of political philosophy, foundational to modern democratic theory
- *Émile, or On Education* (1762) — the work that shaped modern progressive education and expressed his philosophy of human nature
