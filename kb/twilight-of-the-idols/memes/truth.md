# Truth

## Definition

**Truth** in Nietzsche's late philosophy is not a neutral epistemic value but a *problem* — the object of a "will to truth" whose hidden motivations Nietzsche undertakes to expose. In *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, truth appears as the last and most tenacious of the idols: "I, [[plato|Plato]], *am* the truth" (001-152). The identification of truth with a person, a system, or a "[[reality|true world]]" beyond this one is, for Nietzsche, the foundational move of Western metaphysics — and its dismantling is the project of *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* as a whole.

Nietzsche does not reject truth as such — he is not a simple relativist — but he insists that the *value* of truth must itself be questioned. Why do we prefer truth to untruth? What need does the will to truth serve? And what would it mean to live without the metaphysical comfort of a "[[reality|true world]]"?

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

Nietzsche's treatment of truth weaves through the entire work:

**Chapter 1 ("Reason in Philosophy").** The philosophers' "idiosyncrasy" is to treat truth as something *found* rather than *made*, to "confound the last thing with the first" (001-054). The entire history of philosophy is the history of this error — mistaking concepts for realities, abstractions for causes, and the "[[reality|true world]]" for [[reality|the real]] one.

**Chapter 3 ("The Four Great [[error|Error]]s").** Truth is confounded with its effects: "As truth is confounded with the effects of what is believed to be true" (003-197). This is the error of [[causality|false causality]] applied to truth itself — we treat the consequences of believing something (comfort, certainty, power) as evidence of its truth.

**The "True World" fable.** Although the famous "How the 'True World' Finally Became a Fable" appears in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, its compressed narrative traces the history of "truth" from [[plato|Plato]] through [[jesus|Christ]]ianity to Kant — ending with the abolition of the [[reality|true world]] and, with it, the apparent world. "We have abolished the [[reality|true world]]: what world is left? The apparent world perhaps? But no! *With the [[reality|true world]] we have also abolished the apparent one*" (004-xxx).

> "I, Plato, am the truth." (001-152)

## Key Passages

> As truth is confounded with the effects of what is believed to be true, so the true is confounded with the believed. (003-197)

> The will to truth and to knowledge, and tragedy, one after the other — this is the history of Greek philosophy. (006-391)

> A new conscience for truths which hitherto have remained unheard. (010-015)

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's treatment of truth is one of the most contested aspects of his philosophy. Some interpreters read him as an epistemic relativist who denies truth altogether; others see him as a perspectivist who retains a robust notion of truth *within* perspectives while denying the possibility of a "view from nowhere."

*Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* supports a perspectivist reading: Nietzsche does not say "there is no truth" but rather traces the *will* to truth to its psychological sources and insists that what has been called "truth" in philosophy is often the projection of moral and ascetic needs. The target is not truth as such but the metaphysical inflation of truth into a "[[reality|true world]]" that devalues *this* world.

The closing image of *Twilight* — the hammer that "sounds out" idols — is itself an image of truth-seeking: not the truth of dogmatic assertion but the truth of diagnostic critique, the truth that reveals hollowness.
