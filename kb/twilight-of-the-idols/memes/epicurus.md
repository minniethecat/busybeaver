# Epicurus

## Biography

Epicurus (c. 341–270 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher who founded Epicureanism, one of the major schools of Hellenistic philosophy. Born on the island of Samos to Athenian parents, he established his school, the Garden, in [[athens|Athens]] around 306 BCE. Unusually for the time, the Garden admitted both women and slaves. Epicurus taught that the goal of life was *ataraxia* (tranquility) achieved through the pursuit of modest pleasures and the elimination of irrational fears — particularly the fear of death and divine punishment.

## Philosophical Contributions

Epicurus developed a materialist, atomistic physics derived from Democritus, holding that the universe consists of atoms moving through void. Ethically, he argued that pleasure — understood as the absence of pain (*aponia*) and mental disturbance (*ataraxia*) — was the highest good. His most influential doctrine was the denial of divine intervention and the rejection of immortality: the gods exist but are indifferent to human affairs, and the soul dissolves with the body. This made him the ancient world's most formidable critic of religious fear.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche regarded Epicurus with unusual admiration, calling him an "atypical decadent" — a rare figure who transcended the decadence of his age rather than succumbing to it [010-919]. In *The Antichrist*, Nietzsche presents Epicurus as the great opponent of the "subterranean cults" and the entire apparatus of guilt, punishment, and immortality that would later crystallize into [[jesus|Christ]]ianity [010-2215–2216]. Epicurus's denial of immortality was, for Nietzsche, "a genuine deliverance" [010-2219]. The tragic arc Nietzsche traces is that Epicurus triumphed in the Roman Empire — "every respectable thinker in the Roman Empire was an Epicurean" — only for St. Paul to appear and reverse the victory [010-2220].

> "Epicurus was an atypical decadent: and I was the first to recognize this." [010-919]

> "To deny immortality was at that time a genuine deliverance. — And Epicurus had triumphed, every respectable thinker in the Roman Empire was an Epicurean: *then St Paul appeared*..." [010-2219–2220]

## Key Works

- *On Nature* (37 books, mostly lost) — Comprehensive exposition of Epicurean physics and cosmology
- *Letter to Menoeceus* — Surviving ethical treatise on pleasure, death, and the good life
- *Principal Doctrines* (*Kyriai Doxai*) — Forty aphorisms summarizing Epicurean ethics
