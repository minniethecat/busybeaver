# Dante Alighieri

## Biography

Dante Alighieri (1265–1321) was an Italian poet, prose writer, and political thinker. Born in Florence, he became embroiled in the factional conflicts between the Guelphs and Ghibellines, leading to his permanent exile in 1302. His masterpiece, the *Divine Comedy* (c. 1308–1320), is an allegorical journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise that synthesized medieval theology, classical learning, and vernacular poetry into one of the foundational works of Western literature.

## Philosophical Contributions

Dante's *Divine Comedy* is a comprehensive poetic synthesis of Thomistic theology and Aristotelian philosophy. Its elaborate architecture of sin, punishment, and redemption codified the medieval [[jesus|Christ]]ian moral cosmos. The *Comedy* established the Tuscan vernacular as a literary language and demonstrated that poetry could serve as a vehicle for systematic philosophical and theological exposition. In placing figures from classical antiquity alongside [[jesus|Christ]]ian saints, Dante articulated a vision of continuity between pagan reason and [[jesus|Christ]]ian revelation.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's single reference to Dante in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* is a withering epigram. In the "My Impossible People" section of *Skirmishes of an Untimely Man*, Nietzsche lists Dante as "the hyena that writes poetry in tombs" [006-003]. The image is striking: a hyena is a scavenger that feeds on corpses, and Dante's poetry — especially the *Inferno* — dwells obsessively on the dead and their punishments. For Nietzsche, this represented the life-denying morbidity of [[jesus|Christ]]ian art, which finds its greatest aesthetic achievement in the contemplation of eternal suffering rather than the celebration of earthly vitality.

> "Dante, or the hyæna that writes poetry in tombs." [006-003]

## Key Works

- *La Divina Commedia* (*The Divine Comedy*, c. 1308–1320) — Epic poem of the afterlife, comprising *Inferno*, *Purgatorio*, and *Paradiso*
- *La Vita Nuova* (*The New Life*, 1294) — Poetic autobiography of idealized love
