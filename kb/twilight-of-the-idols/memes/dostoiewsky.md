# Fyodor Dostoevsky

## Biography

Fyodor Mikhailovich Dostoevsky (1821–1881) was a [[russia|Russia]]n novelist, short story writer, essayist, and journalist. His literary works explore human psychology in the troubled political, social, and [[soul|spirit]]ual atmosphere of 19th-century [[russia|Russia]]. In 1849 he was arrested for his involvement with the Petrashevsky Circle, a group of progressive intellectuals, and underwent a mock execution before being sentenced to four years of hard labor in a Siberian prison camp — an experience that transformed his worldview and became the basis for his semi-autobiographical novel *Memoirs of a House of the Dead* (1862) [006-1242/1244].

## Major Works

Dostoevsky's major novels include *Crime and Punishment* (1866), *The Idiot* (1869), *Demons* (1872), and *The Brothers Karamazov* (1880). His work is characterized by deep psychological penetration, existential themes, and exploration of moral and [[soul|spirit]]ual crisis. He is widely considered one of the greatest novelists in world literature and a precursor to existentialist philosophy.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche held Dostoevsky in exceptionally high regard — among the highest praise he bestowed on any modern writer. In *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, Nietzsche writes:

> "Concerning the problem before us, Dostoiewsky's testimony is of importance — Dostoiewsky who, incidentally, was the only psychologist from whom I had anything to learn: he belongs to the happiest windfalls of my life, happier even than the discovery of Stendhal." [006-1036]

Nietzsche admired Dostoevsky's insight into criminal psychology, particularly the novelist's observation that Siberian convicts were not degraded by their punishment but rather represented "the best, hardest and most valuable material that grows on [[russia|Russia]]n soil" [006-1036]. This aligned with Nietzsche's own view of the criminal as a potential expression of strength rather than mere moral failure.

Nietzsche also invoked Dostoevsky as the ideal psychological observer when discussing Jesus in *The Antichrist* (referenced in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*):

> "It is greatly to be deplored that no Dostoiewsky lived in the neighbourhood of this most interesting decadent — I mean someone who would have known how to feel the poignant charm of such a mixture of the sublime, the morbid, and the childlike." [010-949]

This passage reveals Nietzsche's wish that a psychologist of Dostoevsky's caliber had been present to capture the psychological complexity of the historical Jesus — a testament to the depth of insight Nietzsche attributed to the [[russia|Russia]]n novelist.

## Key Works

- *Memoirs of a House of the Dead* (1862) — Semi-autobiographical account of Siberian prison life, cited by Nietzsche in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* [006-1242]
- *Crime and Punishment* (1866) — Novel exploring criminal psychology, directly relevant to Nietzsche's discussion of the "criminal from strength" [006-1036]
- *The Brothers Karamazov* (1880) — His final novel, exploring faith, doubt, and morality
