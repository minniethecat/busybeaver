# Cesare Borgia

## Biography

Cesare Borgia (1475–1507) was an Italian Renaissance cardinal turned military commander, the illegitimate son of Pope Alexander VI (Rodrigo Borgia). After renouncing his cardinalate, he became Duke of Valentinois through a political alliance with [[france|France]] and embarked on a campaign to unify the Romagna region under his rule. His career was cut short by the death of his father in 1503, after which his enemies moved swiftly to dismantle his power. He died in battle in Navarre at the age of thirty-one. Machiavelli, who observed Borgia at close quarters as a Florentine diplomat, immortalized him in *The Prince* as the model of the ruthless, efficient ruler who failed only through the stroke of fortune — the very embodiment of *virtù* unconstrained by conventional morality.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche deploys Cesare Borgia as the supreme counter-symbol to [[jesus|Christ]]ian morality and modern humanitarian softness. In *Skirmishes in a War with the Age*, he recounts how his critics recoiled from his concept of the "higher man":

> Compared with us, a Cæsar Borgia was by no means to be represented as "higher man," the sort of *Superman*, which I declared him to be. [006-708]

Nietzsche's response is not to retreat from the provocation but to intensify it. He demands: *have we really become more moral?* The modern conviction of moral progress is itself evidence against it. Modern humanity, "wrapped in the thick cotton wool of our humanitarianism," is merely weaker, more senile, more fearful — not better:

> We should not dare to stand amid the conditions which prevailed at the Renaissance, we should not even dare to imagine ourselves in those conditions: our nerves could not endure that reality, not to speak of our muscles. [006-708]

The inability to endure does not constitute moral superiority; it constitutes physiological decline. If modern delicateness were stripped away, the "morality of 'humanisation' would immediately lose all value — no morality has any value *per se* — it would even fill us with scorn" [006-708].

Borgia represents the Renaissance as a peak of vital energy, and Nietzsche's provocation is to ask whether the "progress" from Borgia to the modern humanitarian is in fact a descent.

## The Superman as Anti-Christian Ideal

In *The Antichrist*, Nietzsche returns to Borgia with his most audacious image. Contrasting the New Testament with the "clean" pagan [[soul|spirit]] of Petronius, Nietzsche quotes Domenico Boccaccio's description of Borgia:

> *"è tutto festo"* — immortally healthy, immortally cheerful and well-constituted. [010-1567]

The Italian phrase — "he is all festivity" — captures the affirmative, life-celebrating quality that Nietzsche opposes to [[jesus|Christ]]ian *ressentiment*. The [[jesus|Christ]]ian, by contrast, "attacks; but everything they assail is, by that very fact alone, *distinguished*" [010-1567]. To be hated by [[jesus|Christ]]ians is an honor.

The climax of Nietzsche's Borgia fantasy comes in a passage of scorching irony:

> I see a spectacle so rich in meaning and so wonderfully paradoxical to boot, that it would be enough to make all the gods of Olympus rock with immortal laughter, — Cæsar Borgia as Pope.... [010-2348]

"*Do you understand me?*" Nietzsche asks. Borgia as Pope would have been the triumph of Life over [[jesus|Christ]]ianity — the overcoming of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity at its very headquarters. The Renaissance, Nietzsche insists, *had already overcome [[jesus|Christ]]ianity* in [[rome|Rome]]: "the old corruption, the *peccatum originale*, [[jesus|Christ]]ianity *no longer* sat upon the Papal chair! But Life! The triumph of Life! The great yea to all lofty, beautiful and daring things!" [010-2348]. And it was [[luther|Luther]] — "a German monk, with all the vindictive instincts of an abortive priest" — who "reinstated the Church; he attacked it" [010-2348].

## Key Works

- Machiavelli's *The Prince* (1532) — Borgia is the central exemplar of political *virtù*, the ability to shape events through force of will
- Nietzsche's *The Antichrist* (1895) — Borgia appears as the anti-[[jesus|Christ]]ian ideal, the figure who might have "swept [[jesus|Christ]]ianity away"
- *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* (1889), "Skirmishes in a War with the Age," §37 — Borgia is cited in Nietzsche's critique of modern humanitarian morality
