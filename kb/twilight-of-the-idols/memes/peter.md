# Saint Peter

## Biography

Simon Peter (died c. 64 CE) was one of the Twelve Apostles of Jesus and, according to Catholic tradition, the first Bishop of [[rome|Rome]]. A Galilean fisherman by trade, he became a central leader of the early [[jesus|Christ]]ian church after Jesus's death. He was martyred in [[rome|Rome]] under Emperor Nero, reportedly crucified upside down at his own request. His prominence in the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles established him as the archetypal figure of apostolic authority.

## Philosophical Contributions

Peter's significance for philosophy lies indirectly in the institution he helped found. As the traditional cornerstone of the Roman Catholic Church, Peter represents the transformation of a small Jewish sect into a universal institution whose moral framework — particularly the concepts of sin, guilt, and redemption — would become a primary target of Nietzsche's critique. The Petrine claim to authority ("upon this rock I will build my church") established a two-millennium tradition that Nietzsche regarded as the institutionalization of slave morality.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's reference to Peter in *The Antichrist* section is sharply critical. He groups Peter with Paul as undeserving recipients of the [[jesus|Christ]]ian promise of immortality, calling the extension of eternal life to ordinary individuals "the greatest, the most vicious outrage upon noble humanity that has ever been perpetrated" [010-1393]. For Nietzsche, the egalitarian promise of immortality to figures like Peter represented [[jesus|Christ]]ianity's fundamental inversion of aristocratic values — granting infinite worth to the common, the humble, and the mediocre.

> "To grant 'immortality' to every St Peter and St Paul, was the greatest, the most vicious outrage upon noble humanity that has ever been perpetrated." [010-1393]
