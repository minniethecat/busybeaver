# India

## Overview

India appears in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* primarily through two references: as the cultural origin of the **[[manu|Law-Book of Manu]]** — the Brahminical legal-moral code Nietzsche discusses extensively in "The 'Improvers' of Mankind" (ch04) — and as the birthplace of **[[buddha|Buddhism]]**, which Nietzsche contrasts favorably with [[jesus|Christ]]ianity. India functions in Nietzsche's argument as a non-[[europe|Europe]]an counterexample: a civilization that developed a sophisticated moral system without the *ressentiment*-driven structure he attributes to the Judeo-[[jesus|Christ]]ian tradition.

## In Nietzsche's Thought

Nietzsche references India in the context of moral genealogy: "In India, as in..." (001-104), drawing a parallel between Indian and Greek philosophical attitudes toward Being and Becoming. His most sustained engagement comes in his analysis of the [[manu|Law-Book of Manu]]:

> An example of this is offered by Indian morality, and is sanctioned religiously as the "Law of Manu" (004-049)

Nietzsche treats [[manu|Manu]]'s code as an aristocratic moral system — one that orders society hierarchically rather than levelling it through pity — and thus as the antithesis of [[jesus|Christ]]ian morality. He also expresses gratitude to "Indian scholars" for preserving Buddhist texts (010-481), positioning [[buddhism|Buddhism]] as a "hundred times more realistic than [[jesus|Christ]]ianity."

## Historical Significance

Ancient India (particularly the Vedic and Buddhist periods) represents one of the world's oldest continuous philosophical traditions. Nietzsche's interest in India reflects the broader 19th-century [[europe|Europe]]an Orientalist fascination, but his use of Indian sources is philosophically motivated: he seeks to demonstrate that moral systems need not be [[jesus|Christ]]ian, pity-based, or life-denying.

## Related Figures

- [[manu|Manu]] — the legendary Hindu lawgiver whose code Nietzsche analyzes as an aristocratic moral system
- [[buddha|Buddha]] — the founder of [[buddhism|Buddhism]], whom Nietzsche praises as more honest than [[jesus|Christ]]ian moralists
- [[schopenhauer|Schopenhauer]] — whose interest in Indian philosophy (particularly the Upanishads) influenced Nietzsche's early thought
