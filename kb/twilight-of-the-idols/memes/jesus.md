# Jesus of Nazareth

## Biography

Jesus of Nazareth (c. 4 BCE – c. 30 CE) was a Jewish preacher from Galilee whose teachings became the foundation of Christianity. In Nietzsche's treatment — most extensively in *The Antichrist* (included with *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* in this edition) — Jesus is understood not as a divine figure but as a **psychological type**: the embodiment of a particular mode of existence that Nietzsche characterizes as "the instinct of hatred *against* reality" and simultaneously as a "free [[soul|spirit]]" who refused all fixed doctrine (010-1001).

## Philosophical Significance

Nietzsche's Jesus is not the Christ of theology but a figure who resists the categories of "hero," "genius," and "founder." Nietzsche's reading is deliberately provocative:

> Fancy making Jesus a hero! — And what a tremendous misunderstanding is the word "genius"! (010-887–890)

For Nietzsche, Jesus represents the **abolition of all distance** between man and God: "Everybody is the child of God — Jesus does not by any means claim anything for himself alone" (010-885). This is not a doctrine of equality but a psychological practice of blessedness that renders all worldly distinctions — including morality, law, and hierarchy — irrelevant. Jesus lives in a "[[reality|true world]]" that is not a metaphysical realm but a state of inner peace, a world in which "everybody is the child of God" means that nobody is *more* the child of God than anyone else.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's engagement with Jesus in *The Antichrist* is double-edged. On one hand, he treats Jesus with a certain respect absent from his treatment of [[paul-the-apostle|Paul]] or the institutional Church. Jesus is "a free [[soul|spirit]]" (010-1001) who "did away with the whole concept of 'guilt'" and could not have desired anything beyond "the practice of his own inner life" (010-1260–1267).

On the other hand, Nietzsche sees the Jesus-type as a symptom of extreme physiological decadence:

> The instinct of hatred against reality: the consequence of an extreme capacity for suffering and irritation which can no longer endure to be "touched" at all. (010-875, context)

The true tragedy, for Nietzsche, is what **Christianity made of Jesus** — particularly through Paul, who transformed the "glad tidings" of inner peace into a doctrine of sin, judgment, and institutional power:

> What appalling Paganism! — For Jesus himself had done away with the concepts "guilt," "punishment," and "reward." (010-1289–1303)
