# Martin Luther

## Biography

Martin Luther (1483–1546) was a German Augustinian monk, theologian, and the central figure of the Protestant Reformation. His challenge to the Catholic Church's authority reshaped [[europe|Europe]]an religion and politics. For Nietzsche, Luther is a pivotal figure in the history of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity — not as a liberator, but as the man who saved [[jesus|Christ]]ianity from its natural decline at the hands of the Renaissance.

## Nietzsche's View

### The Restorer of Christianity

Nietzsche's most striking claim about Luther appears in *The Antichrist*: far from being a progressive force, Luther represents a catastrophic restoration of a dying faith:

> What happened? A German monk, Luther, came to Rome ... this monk, with all the vengeful instincts of an abortive priest, raised a rebellion in Rome *against* the Renaissance. [010-2350]

Nietzsche argues that the Renaissance was on the verge of overcoming [[jesus|Christ]]ianity — that the Church itself was becoming secularized, absorbing classical values. Luther's Reformation reversed this:

> And Luther reinstated the Church; he attacked it. The Renaissance — an event without meaning, a great *in vain*! [010-2359]

This is Nietzsche's provocative thesis: the Renaissance was the great lost opportunity to overcome [[jesus|Christ]]ianity, and Luther is the figure who sealed that loss. [[the-reformation|The Reformation]] was not liberation from Catholicism but the restoration of [[jesus|Christ]]ian seriousness at the moment it was about to dissolve.

### The Theologian's Psychology

Nietzsche diagnoses Luther's psychology with clinical precision:

> A religious man thinks only of himself. [010-2355]

This aphorism cuts to what Nietzsche sees as the core of the religious personality: apparent devotion to God is actually a form of self-absorption. Luther's famous conscience-stricken piety is, in Nietzsche's reading, a manifestation of the same will to power that drives all human action — disguised as submission to the divine.

In *The Antichrist*, Nietzsche places Luther in a lineage of theological obstructionism:

> Like Luther, and like Leibniz, Kant was one brake more upon the otherwise irreproachable mechanism of the German intellect. [010-224]

Here Luther is the first in a sequence: Luther, [[leibniz|Leibniz]], Kant — three German thinkers who, in Nietzsche's view, each added a layer of moral-theological inhibition to the German mind. The metaphor of the "brake" (*Hemmschuh*) suggests that these figures did not advance thought but retarded it.

### Luther's Legacy

Nietzsche sees Luther's influence extending beyond theology into German culture itself. Luther's language, his hymnody, and his moral seriousness shaped a specifically German form of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity:

> Luther's hymn: "Lass fahren hin" [006-1216]

This reference to Luther's famous hymn ("Let go of all that is") appears in *Skirmishes in a War with the Age*, connecting Luther's religious sensibility to the broader cultural analysis Nietzsche pursues there.

For Nietzsche, Luther's most lasting achievement was to make [[jesus|Christ]]ianity *German* — and to make German thought *[[jesus|Christ]]ian* — a fusion he regards as a hindrance to the free [[soul|spirit]]. The theologian's "cloak" of morality [010-1221] became a permanent feature of German intellectual life, from which even Nietzsche's own philosophy had to struggle to free itself.

- *Ninety-five Theses* (1517) — the document that launched the Reformation
- *On the Freedom of a [[jesus|Christ]]ian* (1520) — Luther's central theological statement
- Translation of the Bible into German (1522–1534) — shaped the German language itself
