#### THINGS THE GERMANS LACK

[005-001] Among Germans at the present day it does not suffice to have intellect;

[005-002] one is actually forced to appropriate it, to lay claim to it.

[005-003] Maybe I know the Germans, perhaps I may tell them a few home-truths.

[005-004] Modern [[germany|Germany]] represents such an enormous store of inherited and

[005-005] acquired capacity, that for some time it might spend this accumulated

[005-006] treasure even with some prodigality. It is no superior culture that has

[005-007] ultimately become prevalent with this modern tendency, nor is it by any

[005-008] means delicate taste, or noble beauty of the instincts; but rather a

[005-009] number of virtues more manly than any that other European countries can

[005-010] show. An amount of good spirits and self-respect, plenty of firmness

[005-011] in human relations and in the reciprocity of duties; much industry and

[005-012] much perseverance—and a certain inherited soberness which is much more

[005-013] in need of a spur than of a brake. Let me add that in this country

[005-014] people still obey without feeling that obedience humiliates. And no one

[005-015] despises his opponent.

[005-016] You observe that it is my desire to be fair to the Germans: and in this

[005-017] respect I should not like to be untrue to myself,—I must therefore

[005-018] also state my objections to them. It costs a good deal toattain to a

[005-019] position of power; for powerstultifies.The Germans—they were once

[005-020] called a people of thinkers: do they really think at all at present?

[005-021] Nowadays the Germans are bored by intellect, they mistrust intellect;

[005-022] politics have swallowed up all earnestness for really intellectual

[005-023] things—“Germany, Germany above all.”[1]I fear this was the death-blow

[005-024] to German philosophy. “Are there any German philosophers? Are there any

[005-025] German poets? Are there any good German books?” people ask me abroad. I

[005-026] blush; but with that pluck which is peculiar to me, even in moments of

[005-027] desperation, I reply: “Yes, Bismarck!”—Could I have dared to confess

[005-028] what booksareread to-day? Cursed instinct of mediocrity!—

[005-029] What might not German intellect have been!—who has not thought sadly

[005-030] upon this question! But this nation has deliberately stultified itself

[005-031] for almost a thousand years: nowhere else have the two great European

[005-032] narcotics, alcohol and Christianity, been so viciously abused as in

[005-033] Germany. Recently a third opiate was added to the list, one which in

[005-034] itself alone would have sufficed to complete the ruin of all subtle

[005-035] and daring intellectual animation, I speak of music, our costive and

[005-036] constipating German music. How much peevish ponderousness, paralysis,

[005-037] dampness, dressing-gown languor, and beer is there not in German

[005-038] intelligence!

[005-039] How is it really possible that young men whoconsecrate their whole

[005-040] lives to the pursuit of intellectual ends, should not feel within them

[005-041] the first instinct of intellectuality, theself-preservative instinct

[005-042] of the intellect—and should drink beer? The alcoholism of learned

[005-043] youths does not incapacitate them for becoming scholars—a man quite

[005-044] devoid of intellect may be a great scholar,—but it is a problem in

[005-045] every other respect. Where can that soft degeneracy not be found, which

[005-046] is produced in the intellect by beer! I once laid my finger upon a case

[005-047] of this sort, which became almost famous,—the degeneration of our

[005-048] leading German free-spirit, thecleverDavid Strauss, into the author

[005-049] of a suburban gospel and New Faith. Not in vain had he sung the praises

[005-050] of “the dear old brown liquor” in verse—true unto death.

[005-051] I have spoken of German intellect. I have said that it is becoming

[005-052] coarser and shallower. Is that enough?—In reality something very

[005-053] different frightens me, and that is the ever steady decline of

[005-054] German earnestness, German profundity, and German passion in things

[005-055] intellectual. Not only intellectuality, but also pathos has altered.

[005-056] From time to time I come in touch with German universities; what

[005-057] an extraordinary atmosphere prevails among their scholars! what

[005-058] barrenness! and what self-satisfied and lukewarm intellectuality! For

[005-059] any one to point to German science as an argument against me would show

[005-060] that he grossly misunderstood my meaning, while it would also prove

[005-061] that he had not read a word of my writings. For seventeenyears I

[005-062] have done little else than expose the de-intellectualising influence

[005-063] of our modern scientific studies. The severe slavery to which every

[005-064] individual nowadays is condemned by the enormous range covered by the

[005-065] sciences, is the chief reason why fuller, richer and profounder natures

[005-066] can find no education or educators that are fit for them. Nothing

[005-067] is more deleterious to this age than the superfluity of pretentious

[005-068] loafers and fragmentary human beings; our universities are really

[005-069] the involuntary forcing houses for this kind of withering-up of the

[005-070] instincts of intellectuality. And the whole of Europe is beginning

[005-071] to know this—politics on a large scale deceive no one. Germany is

[005-072] becoming ever more and more the Flat-land of Europe. I am still in

[005-073] search of a German with whom I could be serious after my own fashion.

[005-074] And how much more am I in search of one with whom I could be cheerful—The Twilight of the Idols:ah! what man to-day would be capable

[005-075] of understanding the kind of seriousness from which a philosopher is

[005-076] recovering in this work! It is our cheerfulness that people understand

[005-077] least.

[005-078] Let us examine another aspect of the question: it is not only obvious

[005-079] that German culture is declining, but adequate reasons for this decline

[005-080] are not lacking. After all, nobody can spend more than he has:—this

[005-081] is true of individuals, it is also true of nations. If you spend

[005-082] your strength in acquiring power, or in politics on a large scale,

[005-083] or in economy, or in universal commerce, or in parliamentarism, or

[005-084] in military interests—if you dissipate the modicumof reason, of

[005-085] earnestness, of will, and of self-control that constitutes your nature

[005-086] in one particular fashion, you cannot dissipate it in another. Culture

[005-087] and the state—let no one be deceived on this point—are antagonists:

[005-088] A “culture-state”[2]is merely a modern idea. The one lives upon

[005-089] the other, the one flourishes at the expense of the other. All great

[005-090] periods of culture have been periods of political decline; that which

[005-091] is great from the standpoint of culture, was always unpolitical—even

[005-092] anti-political. Goethe’s heart opened at the coming of Napoleon—it

[005-093] closed at the thought of the “Wars of Liberation.” At the very moment

[005-094] when Germany arose as a great power in the world of politics, France

[005-095] won new importance as a force in the world of culture. Even at this

[005-096] moment a large amount of fresh intellectual earnestness and passion

[005-097] has emigrated to Paris; the question of pessimism, for instance,

[005-098] and the question of Wagner; in France almost all psychological and

[005-099] artistic questions are considered with incomparably more subtlety and

[005-100] thoroughness than they are in Germany,—the Germans are even incapable

[005-101] of this kind of earnestness. In the history of European culture the

[005-102] rise of the Empire signifies, above all, a displacement of the centre

[005-103] of gravity. Everywhere people are already aware of this: in things that

[005-104] really matter—and these after all constitute culture,—the Germans

[005-105] are no longer worth considering. I ask you, can you show me one single

[005-106] man of brains who could bementioned in the same breath with other

[005-107] European thinkers, like your Goethe, your Hegel, your Heinrich Heine,

[005-108] and your Schopenhauer?—The fact that there is no longer a single

[005-109] German philosopher worth mentioning is an increasing wonder.

[005-110] Everything that matters has been lost sight of by the whole of the

[005-111] higher educational system of Germany: the end quite as much as the

[005-112] means to that end. People forget that education, the process of

[005-113] cultivation itself, is the end—and not “the Empire”—they forget that

[005-114] theeducatoris required for this end—and not the public-school

[005-115] teacher and university scholar. Educators are needed who are themselves

[005-116] educated, superior and noble intellects, who can prove that they are

[005-117] thus qualified, that they are ripe and mellow products of culture

[005-118] at every moment of their lives, in word and in gesture;—not the

[005-119] learned louts who, like “superior wet-nurses,” are now thrust upon the

[005-120] youth of the land by public schools and universities. With but rare

[005-121] exceptions, that which is lacking in Germany is the first prerequisite

[005-122] of education—that is to say, the educators; hence the decline of

[005-123] German culture. One of those rarest exceptions is my highly respected

[005-124] friend Jacob Burckhardt of Bâle: to him above all is Bâle indebted

[005-125] for its foremost position in human culture What the higher schools

[005-126] of Germany really do accomplish is this, they brutally train a vast

[005-127] crowd of young men, in the smallest amount of time possible, to become

[005-128] useful and exploitable servants of the state.“Higher education”

[005-129] and a vast crowd—these terms contradict each other from the start.

[005-130] All superior education can only concern the exception: a man must be

[005-131] privileged in order to have a right to such a great privilege. All

[005-132] great and beautiful things cannot be a common possession:pulchrum

[005-133] est paucorum hominum.—What is it that brings about the decline of

[005-134] German culture? The fact that “higher education” is no longer a special

[005-135] privilege—the democracy of a process of cultivation that has become

[005-136] “general,”common.Nor must it be forgotten that the privileges of

[005-137] the military profession by urging many too many to attend the higher

[005-138] schools, involve the downfall of the latter. In modern Germany nobody

[005-139] is at liberty to give his children a noble education: in regard to

[005-140] their teachers, their curricula, and their educational aims, our higher

[005-141] schools are one and all established upon a fundamentally doubtful

[005-142] mediocre basis. Everywhere, too, a hastiness which is unbecoming rules

[005-143] supreme; just as if something would be forfeited if the young man were

[005-144] not “finished” at the age of twenty-three, or did not know how to

[005-145] reply to the most essential question, “which calling to choose?”—The

[005-146] superior kind of man, if you please, does not like “callings,”

[005-147] precisely because he knows himself to be called. He has time, he takes

[005-148] time, he cannot possibly think of becoming “finished,”—in the matter

[005-149] of higher culture, a man of thirty years is a beginner, a child. Our

[005-150] overcrowded public-schools, our accumulation of foolishly manufactured

[005-151] public-school masters, are a scandal: maybe there are very seriousmotivesfor defending this state of affairs, as was shown quiterecently by the professors of Heidelberg; but there can be no reasons

[005-152] for doing so.

[005-153] In order to be true to my nature, which is affirmative and which

[005-154] concerns itself with contradictions and criticism only indirectly

[005-155] and with reluctance, let me state at once what the three objects

[005-156] are for which we need educators. People must learn to see; they

[005-157] must learn to think, and they must learn to speak and to write: the

[005-158] object of all three of these pursuits is a noble culture. To learn

[005-159] to see—to accustom the eye to calmness, to patience, and to allow

[005-160] things to come up to it; to defer judgment, and to acquire the habit

[005-161] of approaching and grasping an individual case from all sides. This

[005-162] is the first preparatory schooling of intellectuality. One must not

[005-163] respond immediately to a stimulus; one must acquire a command of the

[005-164] obstructing and isolating instincts. To learn to see, as I understand

[005-165] this matter, amounts almost to that which in popular language is

[005-166] called “strength of will”: its essential feature is preciselynottowishto see, to be able to postpone one’s decision. All lack of

[005-167] intellectuality, all vulgarity, arises out of the inability to resist

[005-168] a stimulus:—one must respond or react, every impulse is indulged. In

[005-169] many cases such necessary action is already a sign of morbidity, of

[005-170] decline, and a symptom of exhaustion. Almost everything that coarse

[005-171] popular language characterises as vicious, is merely that physiological

[005-172] inability to refrain from reacting.—As an instance of what it means

[005-173] to have learnt to see, let me state that a manthus trained will as a

[005-174] learner have become generally slow, suspicious, and refractory. With

[005-175] hostile calm he will first allow every kind of strange andnewthing

[005-176] to come right up to him,—he will draw back his hand at its approach.

[005-177] To stand with all the doors of one’s soul wide open, to lie slavishly

[005-178] in the dust before every trivial fact, at all times of the day to be

[005-179] strained ready for the leap, in order to deposit one’s self, to plunge

[005-180] one’s self, into other souls and other things, in short, the famous

[005-181] “objectivity” of modern times, is bad taste, it is essentially vulgar

[005-182] and cheap.

[005-183] As to learning how to think—our schools no longer have any notion

[005-184] of such a thing. Even at the universities, among the actual scholars

[005-185] in philosophy, logic as a theory, as a practical pursuit, and as a

[005-186] business, is beginning to die out. Turn to any German book: you will

[005-187] not find the remotest trace of a realisation that there is such a

[005-188] thing as a technique, a plan of study, a will to mastery, in the

[005-189] matter of thinking,—that thinking insists upon being learnt, just

[005-190] as dancing insists upon being learnt, and that thinking insists upon

[005-191] being learnt as a form of dancing. What single German can still say he

[005-192] knows from experience that delicate shudder whichlight footfallsin matters intellectual cause to pervade his whole body and limbs!

[005-193] Stiff awkwardness in intellectual attitudes, and the clumsy fist in

[005-194] grasping—these things are so essentially German, that outside Germany

[005-195] they are absolutely confounded with the German spirit. The German

[005-196] has no fingers for delicatenuances.The fact thatthe people of

[005-197] Germany have actually tolerated their philosophers, more particularly

[005-198] that most deformed cripple of ideas that has ever existed—the great

[005-199] Kant, gives one no inadequate notion of their native elegance. For,

[005-200] truth to tell, dancing in all its forms cannot be excluded from the

[005-201] curriculum of all noble education: dancing with the feet, with ideas,

[005-202] with words, and, need I add that one must also be able to dance with

[005-203] the pen—that one must learn how to write?—But at this stage I should

[005-204] become utterly enigmatical to German readers.
