# René Descartes

## Biography

René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist, widely regarded as the father of modern philosophy. Born in La Haye en Touraine, he was educated by the Jesuits and later served as a soldier before devoting himself to philosophy in the Netherlands. His *Discourse on the Method* (1637) and *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641) established the rationalist tradition. He died in Stockholm while tutoring Queen [[jesus|Christ]]ina of Sweden.

## Philosophical Contributions

Descartes' philosophical project was built on methodological doubt — the systematic rejection of all beliefs that could be called into question — arriving at the foundational certainty of *cogito, ergo sum* ("I think, therefore I am"). From this, he developed a radical dualism between mind (thinking substance) and body (extended substance). In natural philosophy, he proposed a mechanistic account of all physical phenomena, including animal behavior, which he regarded as purely automatic — beasts as *machinae*, complex machines operating without consciousness or sensation.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's engagement with Descartes appears in the context of *The Four Great [[error|Error]]s* in *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*. He acknowledged Descartes' intellectual boldness: "Descartes was the first, with really admirable daring, to venture the thought that the beast was *machina*" [010-319]. Yet Nietzsche immediately distances himself from Cartesian dualism: "Logically we do not set man apart, as Descartes did: the extent to which man is understood today goes only so far as he has been understood mechanistically" [010-322–323]. The larger context is Nietzsche's critique of the error of free will — "Formerly man was given 'free will,' as his dowry from a higher world" [010-324] — a position Descartes helped entrench by exempting the human mind from mechanistic explanation.

> "Descartes was the first, with really admirable daring, to venture the thought that the beast was *machina*, and the whole of our physiology is endeavouring to prove this proposition." [010-319–321]

## Key Works

- *Discours de la méthode* (*Discourse on the Method*, 1637) — Foundational text of modern rationalism, containing the *cogito* argument
- *Meditationes de Prima Philosophia* (*Meditations on First Philosophy*, 1641) — Systematic argument for mind-body dualism and the existence of God
