# Aristotle

## Biography

Aristotle (384–322 BCE) was a Greek philosopher and polymath, a student of [[plato|Plato]] and the tutor of Alexander the Great. He founded the Lyceum and the Peripatetic school of philosophy, authoring works that span logic, metaphysics, ethics, politics, biology, and aesthetics. His philosophical system dominated medieval scholasticism and remained the foundational framework of Western philosophy well into the early modern period. His *Poetics* established the vocabulary and categories through which tragedy has been analysed for over two millennia.

## Philosophical Contributions

Aristotle's *Poetics* introduced the concept of *catharsis* — the purgation of pity and fear through the experience of tragedy. For Aristotle, tragedy serves a therapeutic function: by arousing these emotions and then discharging them, the spectator is cleansed of dangerous affective accumulations. This model of aesthetic experience as emotional hygiene was enormously influential, shaping dramatic theory from the Renaissance through to Lessing and beyond. In ethics, his doctrine of the mean located virtue between extremes of excess and deficiency, while his political theory grounded human flourishing in the *polis* and identified man as a *zoon politikon* — a political animal whose nature is realised only in community.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche engages Aristotle at two critical junctures, and his verdict is complex. In *Things I Owe to the Ancients*, he faults Aristotle — along with "our pessimists" — for fundamentally misunderstanding the nature of tragic feeling: "[[tragedy|Tragedy]] is so far from proving anything in regard to the pessimism of the Greeks, as Schopenhauer maintains, that it ought rather to be considered as the categorical repudiation and condemnation thereof" [007-178]. The [[dionysian|Dionysian]] affirmation of life "not in order to escape from terror and pity, not to purify one's self of a dangerous passion by discharging it with vehemence — this is how Aristotle understood it — but to be far beyond terror and pity and to be the eternal lust of Becoming itself" [007-189]. Aristotle's catharsis, in Nietzsche's reading, domesticates tragedy into a mere emotional safety valve, missing its ecstatic, life-affirming core.

A more ambivalent deployment appears in *Morality as the Enemy of Nature*. Here Nietzsche recruits Aristotle against Schopenhauer's elevation of pity to the supreme moral sentiment: "Aristotle, as you know, recognised in pity a morbid and dangerous state, of which it was wise to rid one's self from time to time by a purgative: he regarded tragedy as a purgative" [010-144]. For Nietzsche's polemical purposes, Aristotle's medical model of pity is vastly preferable to Schopenhauer's sanctification of it — but the deeper criticism remains: Aristotle's entire framework, by treating powerful affects as something to be *managed* and *discharged*, reveals the same life-hostile instinct that Schopenhauer merely pushes to its logical conclusion.

> "Aristotle, as you know, recognised in pity a morbid and dangerous state, of which it was wise to rid one's self from time to time by a purgative: he regarded tragedy as a purgative." [010-144]

## Key Works

- *Poetics* (c. 335 BCE) — The foundational text of Western dramatic theory, introducing catharsis, hamartia, and the structural analysis of tragedy
- *Nicomachean Ethics* (c. 340 BCE) — Aristotle's major ethical work, articulating the doctrine of the mean and the concept of eudaimonia
- *Politics* (c. 350 BCE) — Systematic treatment of political philosophy, introducing man as a political animal

## Related Figures
