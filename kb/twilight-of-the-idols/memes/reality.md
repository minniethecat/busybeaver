# Reality

## Definition

In Nietzsche's philosophy, "reality" (<span lang="de">Wirklichkeit</span>) is not a neutral description of what exists but a historically constructed concept that functions as the privileged term in the metaphysical opposition between being and becoming. What philosophy calls "reality" is, for Nietzsche, a systematic falsification: the substitution of stable, unitary, rational fictions for the flux of becoming. The concept of reality serves to devalue the apparent world and impose moral valuations under the guise of ontology [001-051]. Nietzsche's critique aims not to replace one reality with another but to expose the genealogy of the concept itself.

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

Nietzsche's most concentrated treatment of reality appears in "How the 'True World' Finally Became a Fable," where he traces the concept through six historical stages — from [[plato|Plato]]'s equation of reality with the Forms, through [[jesus|Christ]]ianity's postponement of reality to the afterlife, Kant's unknowable noumenon, and positivism's skeptical withdrawal, to its final abolition [001-190]. At each stage, "reality" is positioned as what the world of sense-experience is not: immutable, unified, rational, good. The history of reality is thus a history of slandering the actual world.

Chapter 3, "The Four Great [[error|Error]]s," extends this critique by showing how the concept of reality becomes entangled with [[causality|false causality]]. When human beings project purposes, wills, and intentions onto events, they create "causes" that function as miniature realities behind appearances [003-103]. The most consequential of these projections is God — "the error of [[soul|spirit]] as cause confounded with reality!" [003-104]. The entire structure of moral responsibility depends on this manufactured reality of free will.

In chapter 4, Nietzsche shows that the concept of reality is inseparable from the concept of the "improver": the priest who offers access to "true reality" does so only by negating the reality of life itself. There is no innocent metaphysics, only metaphysics in the service of moral-ascetic interests [004-010].

Nietzsche also treats reality as an aesthetic problem. The artist, unlike the philosopher, does not need a "higher" reality behind appearances. The [[tragedy|tragic art]]ist "says Yes to everything questionable" [001-137] precisely because he does not require the consolation of a truer world. This aesthetic affirmation — which Nietzsche also calls [[pessimism|Dionysian pessimism]] — is the counterpoint to the metaphysical need for reality.

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's critique of reality is a key moment in what Heidegger later called "the destruction of the history of ontology." While Heidegger seeks to recover a more primordial sense of Being, Nietzsche simply abolishes the question: there is no being, only becoming; no reality, only interpretations. This is the radicality of Nietzsche's position — it is not a new theory of reality but an exit from the theory of reality altogether.

The concept has important political echoes: Marx's critique of ideology similarly exposes how claims about "reality" mask class interests, and the Frankfurt School's critique of instrumental reason extends Nietzsche's insight that "reality" is a tool of domination. In analytic philosophy, Nietzsche's position anticipates the anti-realist and pragmatist traditions (Putnam's internal realism, Rorty's ironism), which likewise deny that the concept of reality can do the work metaphysicians have asked of it.

However, Nietzsche's position differs from contemporary anti-realism in its existential dimension. The abolition of reality is not merely a cognitive achievement but a lived transformation: it inaugurates a new human type, capable of affirming life without the metaphysical props that have sustained humanity for millennia. This is the significance of [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]]'s appearance at the end of the "true world" history — the teacher of the overman arrives precisely when reality is abolished.
