# Otto von Bismarck

## Biography

Otto von Bismarck (1815–1898) was the Prussian minister-president and later Chancellor of the German Empire who unified [[germany|Germany]] through a combination of diplomacy and warfare. For Nietzsche, Bismarck is neither hero nor villain — he is a case study in the psychology of power, cited at a crucial moment to illustrate the nature of daring and deception in political action.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's most dramatic invocation of Bismarck appears not in a philosophical argument but in a personal confession. In *Skirmishes in a War with the Age*, Nietzsche asks whether he could have confessed his true thoughts in the atmosphere of the German Reich:

> In desperation, I reply: "Yes, Bismarck!" — Could I have dared to confess ... [005-027]

The reference is elliptical but charged. Bismarck here stands for the kind of daring (*Wagnis*) that Nietzsche associates with greatness — the willingness to act decisively, even ruthlessly, in defiance of conventional morality. To say "Yes, Bismarck!" in this context is to affirm the possibility of courageous action in an age of timidity.

### The Forger of the Ems Dispatch

Nietzsche also references Bismarck's most famous act of political cunning — the doctoring of the Ems Dispatch, which provoked [[france|France]] into declaring war in 1870:

> This applies apparently to Bismarck, the forger of the Ems dispatch. [010-2436–2438]

The Ems Dispatch was a telegram reporting a diplomatic exchange between the Prussian king and the French ambassador. Bismarck edited and released a version that made the exchange appear insulting to both sides, goading [[france|France]] into the Franco-Prussian War — a war Prussia won decisively, enabling German unification.

Nietzsche's annotation "the forger of the Ems dispatch" is not necessarily a condemnation. In Nietzsche's moral universe, the capacity to *forge* — to shape events through strategic deception — is not a vice but a sign of power. Bismarck understood that political action requires the will to manipulate appearances, a truth that conventional morality cannot acknowledge.

### Bismarck as Symbol

Nietzsche's references to Bismarck are too fragmentary to constitute a sustained analysis, but their placement and tone are revealing. Bismarck appears as:
- A figure of daring (*Wagnis*) in a timid age [005-027]
- A practitioner of the strategic deception that politics requires [010-2436–2438]

In both cases, Bismarck serves Nietzsche's larger argument: that greatness requires the transcendence of conventional morality. Bismarck did not unify [[germany|Germany]] by being "good" — he did it by being effective. Whether Nietzsche fully approves of Bismarck's specific aims is less important than what Bismarck demonstrates about the nature of power itself.

- Chancellor of the German Empire (1871–1890), implementing the first modern welfare state while suppressing political opposition
