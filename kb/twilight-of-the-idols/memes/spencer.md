# Herbert Spencer

## Biography

Herbert Spencer (1820–1903) was an English philosopher, biologist, and sociologist, one of the most prominent intellectual figures of the Victorian era. Largely self-taught, he developed a vast synthetic system applying evolutionary principles to biology, psychology, sociology, and ethics.

He coined the phrase "survival of the fittest," which [[darwin|Darwin]] later adopted. His *Principles of Sociology* attempted to ground social analysis in evolutionary biology. In his own time, Spencer's influence rivalled [[darwin|Darwin]]'s.

## Philosophical Contributions

Spencer proposed that all phenomena develop according to a universal law of progress from homogeneity to heterogeneity. In ethics, he advocated evolutionary utilitarianism: moral conduct conduces to the greatest length and breadth of life.

Politically, Spencer was a radical individualist who opposed state intervention and public education. He argued that evolution would naturally select the fittest individuals and institutions without governmental interference.

His concept of altruism — self-sacrificing regard for others — was presented as the highest product of social evolution. Nietzsche singled out this conclusion for specific condemnation [006-791].

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's verdict on Spencer appears in a single sentence in *Skirmishes in a War with the Age* (ch06), but it is lethal: "Our socialists are decadents: but Herbert Spencer was also a decadent — he saw something to be desired in the triumph of altruism!" [006-791].

For Nietzsche, altruism is not the pinnacle of evolutionary progress but its opposite: a symptom of declining vitality. Spencer prided himself on grounding ethics in biology, yet he failed to see that [[reality|the real]] biological lesson is not altruism but the [[will-to-power|Will to Power]].

By taking altruism as the *telos* of evolution, Spencer reveals himself as a sentimentalist who has smuggled [[jesus|Christ]]ian morality into his science. The verdict that "Herbert Spencer was also a decadent" [006-791] places him alongside the socialists — not despite his evolutionary framework but because of his use of it.

> "Our socialists are decadents: but Herbert Spencer was also a decadent — he saw something to be desired in the triumph of altruism!" [006-791]

## Key Works

- *A System of Synthetic Philosophy* (1862–1896) — Spencer's multi-volume magnum opus covering biology, psychology, sociology, and ethics
- *Principles of Sociology* (1876–1896) — The sociological component, proposing evolutionary laws of social development
- *The Data of Ethics* (1879) — Spencer's evolutionary ethics, defining the good as the pleasurable and life-enhancing
