# Instinct

## Definition

Instinct (Trieb) in Nietzsche's philosophy refers to the pre-rational, biologically rooted drives that constitute the deepest stratum of human motivation. Against the philosophical tradition that privileges reason and consciousness, Nietzsche argues that all thinking, valuing, and willing are expressions of underlying instincts.

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

Nietzsche repeatedly contrasts instinct with reason, arguing that the Socratic privileging of reason represents a pathological suppression of healthy instinct. In Chapter 2, he characterizes morality as hostile to the "instincts of life" (002-015). The "decadence of the valuing instinct" (006-868) is for Nietzsche a sign of cultural sickness.

In his discussion of the Germans, Nietzsche praises instinctual health over intellectual sophistication, arguing that German culture has lost its instinctual vitality (005-042).

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's rehabilitation of instinct anticipates Freud's concept of the drive (Trieb) and 20th-century critiques of rationalism. However, Nietzsche does not simply oppose instinct to reason — he seeks their integration in what he calls the "great reason" of the body.
