# Heinrich Heine

## Biography

Heinrich Heine (1797–1856) was a German poet, journalist, and literary critic. Born in Düsseldorf to a Jewish family, he converted to [[jesus|Christ]]ianity in 1825 to enable a professional career. Heine spent his later years in Parisian exile, where he became a bridge between German and French literary cultures. His lyric poetry, collected in the *Buch der Lieder* (1827), and his satirical travel writings made him one of the most widely read German authors of the nineteenth century.

## Philosophical Contributions

Heine's philosophical significance lies in his critique of German idealism and Romanticism from within the literary tradition. He championed the emancipation of the senses against what he saw as the life-denying tendencies of [[jesus|Christ]]ian [[soul|spirit]]uality. His essay *On the History of Religion and Philosophy in [[germany|Germany]]* (1834) provided a critical genealogy of German thought from [[luther|Luther]] to [[hegel|Hegel]] that anticipated elements of Nietzsche's own genealogical method.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche held Heine in the highest esteem among German poets. In *Things the Germans Lack*, he listed Heine alongside Goethe, [[hegel|Hegel]], and Schopenhauer as proof that [[germany|Germany]] once produced minds of [[europe|Europe]]an stature [005-107]. In *Skirmishes of an Untimely Man*, Nietzsche deepened this praise, calling Schopenhauer "a [[europe|Europe]]an event like Goethe, [[hegel|Hegel]], or Heinrich Heine, and who is not merely local, national" [006-384]. The repeated grouping of Heine with Goethe and [[hegel|Hegel]] signals that Nietzsche regarded Heine not as a minor lyricist but as a thinker of continental significance.

> "...like your Goethe, your Hegel, your Heinrich Heine, and your Schopenhauer..." [005-107]

> "...who is a European event like Goethe, Hegel, or Heinrich Heine, and who is not merely local, national..." [006-384]
