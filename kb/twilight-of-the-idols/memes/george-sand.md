# George Sand

## Biography

George Sand (1804–1876), born Amantine Lucile Aurore Dupin, was one of the most prolific and celebrated French novelists of the nineteenth century. She adopted a male pseudonym and often male attire, becoming a figure of scandal and fascination in Parisian literary society. Her works, including *[[india|India]]na*, *Lélia*, and *Consuelo*, addressed themes of love, social justice, and the condition of women, earning her a place alongside Balzac and Hugo in the French Romantic pantheon. Her salon at Nohant hosted Liszt, Chopin (with whom she had a lengthy affair), Delacroix, and Flaubert. She was also deeply influenced by the thought of [[rousseau|Rousseau]], whose vision of nature and sentiment shaped her literary sensibility.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's treatment of George Sand is a concentrated exercise in demolition. In his opening salvo of *Skirmishes in a War with the Age* — the section titled "My Impossible People" — he dispatches her in six words:

> George Sand, or *lactea ubertas*, in plain English: the cow with plenty of beautiful milk. [006-006]

The Latin phrase *lactea ubertas* — "milky abundance" — is deadly in its precision. It captures the facile productivity, the warm emotional overflow, the sheer unrestrained *quantity* of Sand's output, which Nietzsche refuses to dignify as art. She is a producer of literary milk, not a creator.

In a longer passage devoted entirely to Sand, Nietzsche elaborates his accusation with clinical specificity:

> I have been reading the first "Lettres d'un Voyageur:" like everything that springs from Rousseau's influence it is false, made-up, blown out, and exaggerated! I cannot endure this bright wall-paper style, any more than I can bear the vulgar striving after generous feelings. [006-090]

The charge is threefold. First, **falseness** — Sand's emotions are not felt but manufactured; her writing is "made-up" rhetoric dressed as spontaneous sentiment. Second, **stylistic garishness** — the "bright wall-paper style" (*Tapetenstil*) is Nietzsche's term for writing that covers a surface with decorative pattern while concealing an absence of substance. Third, **masculine posturing as feminine affectation**:

> The worst feature about it is certainly the coquettish adoption of male attributes by this female, after the manner of ill-bred schoolboys. And how cold she must have been inwardly all the while, this insufferable artist! She wound herself up like a clock — and wrote. [006-090]

The clock metaphor is the cruellest stroke. Sand's celebrated "warmth" and "generosity" are mechanical — she winds herself up like an automaton and the prose pours out, unfeeling beneath the surface of feeling. Nietzsche then delivers the verdict that links Sand to her entire literary generation:

> As cold as Hugo and Balzac, as cold as all Romanticists are as soon as they begin to write! [006-090]

The Romantics, in Nietzsche's diagnosis, are essentially *cold* — their apparent passion is a technique, not a state of being. The "prolific ink-yielding cow" [006-090] produces not nourishment but a substitute: the illusion of warmth generated by a fundamentally chilly mechanism.

## Key Works

- *Lettres d'un voyageur* (1834–1836) — The epistolary travelogue Nietzsche specifically read and attacked; a central text of Sand's early Romantic persona
- *[[india|India]]na* (1832) — Her breakthrough novel, addressing the condition of women in marriage, and an exemplar of the [[rousseau|Rousseau]]ian sensibility Nietzsche rejected
