# Auguste Comte

## Biography

Auguste Comte (1798–1857) was a French philosopher who founded the discipline of sociology and the philosophical system of positivism.

After a period as secretary to the utopian socialist Henri de Saint-Simon, Comte developed his own comprehensive doctrine, articulated in the six-volume *Cours de Philosophie Positive* (1830–1842).

His later work took a markedly religious turn. He proposed a "Religion of Humanity" with its own calendar of secular saints, rituals, and a hierarchical priesthood of scientists — an attempt to supply social cohesion on a rational, scientific basis.

## Philosophical Contributions

Comte's law of three stages held that human knowledge progresses through theological, metaphysical, and finally positive (scientific) phases. In the positive stage, explanation abandons the search for ultimate causes in favour of discovering laws governing observable phenomena.

Positivism placed empirical science at the apex of knowledge and sought to extend its method to the study of society. His later Religion of Humanity organised society around altruism rather than divine command.

Nietzsche's single recorded comment on Comte appears in *Skirmishes*, where he describes him as "that most intelligent of Jesuits, Auguste Comte, who wished to lead his compatriots back to [[rome|Rome]] by the circuitous route of science" [006-060].

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's comment occurs in a passage on *The Imitation of [[jesus|Christ]]* — a book he "cannot even take hold of without physical loathing." He reports: "I am told that that most intelligent of Jesuits, Auguste Comte, who wished to lead his compatriots back to [[rome|Rome]] by the circuitous route of science, drew his inspiration from this book. And I believe it: 'the religion of the heart'" [006-060].

The epithet "most intelligent of Jesuits" is devastating. Comte was not a Jesuit but a professed atheist and positivist, yet Nietzsche discerns the same hierarchical, dogmatic, [[rome|Rome]]-centred structure.

Comte's project is not an overcoming of [[jesus|Christ]]ianity but its continuation under another name. The positivist synthesis culminates in the "religion of the heart" [006-060] that Nietzsche identifies as the core pathology: sentimental morality substituted for intellectual honesty.

> "I am told that that most intelligent of Jesuits, Auguste Comte, who wished to lead his compatriots back to Rome by the circuitous route of science, drew his inspiration from this book. And I believe it: 'the religion of the heart.'" [006-060]
