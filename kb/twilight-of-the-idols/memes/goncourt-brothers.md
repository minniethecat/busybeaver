# Goncourt Brothers

## Biography

Edmond (1822–1896) and Jules (1830–1870) de Goncourt were French writers, art critics, and diarists who worked in a uniquely collaborative partnership until Jules's early death. Their novels — including *Germinie Lacerteux* (1865) and *Madame Gervaisais* (1869) — pioneered the transition from Romanticism to Naturalism, applying techniques of documentary observation and pathological case-study to literary fiction. Their monumental *Journal*, kept from 1851 until Edmond's death, remains one of the most vivid records of Parisian literary and artistic life in the second half of the nineteenth century. The Prix Goncourt, established by Edmond's will and first awarded in 1903, remains [[france|France]]'s most prestigious literary prize.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's judgment of the Goncourts appears in two concentrated bursts. In the opening salvo of "My Impossible People," they receive one of the most vivid images in the entire series:

> The brothers Goncourt, or the two Ajaxes fighting with Homer. [006-009]

The simile is devastating. In [[homer|Homer]]'s *Iliad*, the two Ajaxes — Ajax the Great and Ajax the Lesser — fight side by side, but their combined strength cannot overcome Hector, let alone rival [[homer|Homer]] himself. The Goncourts, for all their collaborative industry, are two minor warriors tilting at a greatness they cannot reach.

The deeper critique unfolds in Nietzsche's discussion of psychological method. The Goncourts represent for him the pathology of "note-book psychology" — the practice of accumulating observations from life and depositing them directly into fiction:

> What is the result when a man sets about this matter differently? — when, for instance, after the manner of Parisian novelists, he goes in for note-book psychology on a large and small scale? Such a man is constantly spying on reality, and every evening he bears home a handful of fresh curios.... But look at the result! — a mass of daubs, at best a piece of mosaic, in any case something heaped together, restless and garish. [006-122]

The Goncourts are, for Nietzsche, not merely practitioners of this method but its definitive failures:

> The Goncourts are the greatest sinners in this respect: they cannot put three sentences together which are not absolutely painful to the eye — the eye of the psychologist. [006-122]

The accusation is technical as well as aesthetic. The note-book method produces "mosaic" — a patchwork of observed details that never coheres into form. It is "restless and garish" because it lacks the unifying vision of the genuine artist. Nietzsche draws the larger lesson: "From an artistic standpoint, nature is no model. It exaggerates, distorts, and leaves gaps. Nature is the *accident*" [006-122]. To copy nature, to "study from nature," betrays "submission, weakness, fatalism." The Goncourts' sin is not merely stylistic; it is a failure of artistic will — the inability to impose form upon raw material, to transform observation into vision.

## Key Works

- *Germinie Lacerteux* (1865) — A novel of working-class life based on the Goncourts' discovery that their housekeeper had led a secret double life; a founding text of literary Naturalism and the prime exhibit of the "note-book" method
- *Journal des Goncourt* (published 1887–1896) — The vast diary recording decades of Parisian literary and artistic life, the ultimate monument to the Goncourts' method of continuous observation
