# The German Empire

## Overview

The **German Empire** (*Deutsches Reich*) was founded in 1871 under the leadership of [[bismarck|Otto von Bismarck]], unifying the German states after the Franco-Prussian War. Nietzsche, writing in the 1880s, treats this event not as a national triumph but as a decisive moment of cultural decline: the consolidation of political power, he argues, came at the direct expense of German intellectual and [[soul|spirit]]ual greatness.

## Historical Context

The unification of [[germany|Germany]] in 1871 transformed the political landscape of [[europe|Europe]]. After victories over Denmark (1864), Austria (1866), and [[france|France]] (1870–71), the Prussian-led North German Confederation became the German Empire, with King Wilhelm I of Prussia proclaimed German Emperor at Versailles. [[bismarck|Bismarck]], as Chancellor, dominated the new state's politics until 1890 — the year after Nietzsche's mental collapse.

For most Germans, the Empire represented the fulfillment of long-deferred national aspirations. For Nietzsche, it represented something closer to a catastrophe. He had already renounced his Prussian citizenship in 1869 and spent the subsequent decades in self-imposed exile in Switzerland and Italy, growing increasingly contemptuous of the new German state.

## Nietzsche's Interpretation

In "Things the Germans Lack" (ch05), Nietzsche delivers his most concentrated critique of post-unification [[germany|Germany]]. The core thesis is succinct:

> It costs a good deal to attain to a position of power; for power *stultifies*. The Germans — they were once called a people of thinkers: do they really think at all at present? Nowadays the Germans are bored by intellect, they mistrust intellect; politics have swallowed up all earnestness for really intellectual things — "Germany, Germany above all." I fear this was the death-blow to German philosophy. (005-019–024)

The phrase "[[germany|Germany]], [[germany|Germany]] above all" (*Deutschland, Deutschland über alles*) — the opening line of what would later become the German national anthem — is seized upon by Nietzsche as the emblem of a culture that has sacrificed depth for power. When asked abroad whether [[germany|Germany]] still produces philosophers, poets, or good books, Nietzsche's reply is devastating in its irony:

> I blush; but with that pluck which is peculiar to me, even in moments of desperation, I reply: "Yes, [[bismarck|Bismarck]]!" (005-027)

The point is not that [[bismarck|Bismarck]] is a philosopher or poet, but precisely that he is *not*: the only "great German" Nietzsche can name is a politician — which, for Nietzsche, is itself the diagnosis of the sickness.

Nietzsche extends this analysis historically, tracing German intellectual decline across a millennium:

> What might not German intellect have been! — who has not thought sadly upon this question! But this nation has deliberately stultified itself for almost a thousand years: nowhere else have the two great European narcotics, alcohol and Christianity, been so viciously abused as in Germany. Recently a third opiate was added to the list, one which in itself alone would have sufficed to complete the ruin of all subtle and daring intellectual animation, I speak of music, our costive and constipating German music. (005-029–036)

The "third opiate" is Wagnerian music — Nietzsche's former idol, now diagnosed as part of the same cultural pathology as the Empire itself.

In "Skirmishes in a War with the Age" (ch06), Nietzsche broadens the critique into a political theory: the German Empire is an instance of the "decaying form of the State" produced by modern democracy:

> Democracy has always been the death agony of the power of organisation: already in "Human All-too-Human" I pointed out that modern democracy, together with its half-measures, of which the "German Empire" is an example, was a decaying form of the State. (006-849–850)

And bluntly:

> The foundation of the German Empire has brought to a crisis... the whole of the Occident no longer possesses those instincts from which institutions grow. (006-859–860)

For Nietzsche, the German Empire is not an achievement but a symptom — the political crystallization of a broader [[europe|Europe]]an decadence.

## Significance

The German Empire figures in Nietzsche's philosophy on several registers:

1. **As evidence for the inverse relation of power and culture**: Nietzsche's thesis that political power "stultifies" is not limited to [[germany|Germany]] — it is a general claim about the psychology of nations. The German Empire is the paradigmatic modern case: the pursuit and attainment of political dominance drains the energies required for intellectual and artistic greatness.

2. **As a chapter in the "decline of German culture"**: The founding of the Empire is the political counterpart to the cultural decline Nietzsche traces throughout *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* (see [[decline-of-german-culture|Decline of German Culture]]). [[germany|Germany]]'s unification was, in Nietzsche's eyes, [[germany|Germany]]'s [[soul|spirit]]ual dissolution.

3. **As a critique of modernity**: The German Empire is a "half-measure" of democracy — a compromise formation that lacks the genuine aristocratic-warlike instincts of [[rome|Rome]] while also lacking the intellectual vitality of pre-imperial [[germany|Germany]]. It is modernity's political form par excellence: large, powerful, and [[soul|spirit]]ually empty.

4. **As autobiography**: Nietzsche's critique of the Empire is inseparable from his own position as a stateless philosopher. He wrote *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* in 1888, the final year of his productive life, as a German who had long since ceased to identify with [[germany|Germany]].
