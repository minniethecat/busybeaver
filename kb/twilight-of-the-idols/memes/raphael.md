# Raphael

## Biography

Raffaello Sanzio da Urbino (1483–1520), known as Raphael, was one of the three supreme masters of the High Renaissance alongside Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo. Working primarily in [[rome|Rome]] under the patronage of Pope Julius II, Raphael produced a body of work — including the *Stanze* frescoes in the Vatican Palace, the *Sistine Madonna*, and the *School of [[athens|Athens]]* — that defined the classical ideal of Renaissance painting: harmonious composition, grace of figure, and the serene integration of classical form with [[jesus|Christ]]ian subject matter. He died at thirty-seven, and his tomb in the Pantheon bears the epitaph: "Here lies Raphael, by whom Nature feared to be outdone while he lived, and when he died, feared she would die with him."

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's treatment of Raphael is confined to two adjacent sentences — but within those sentences he articulates a principle of enormous consequence for his entire aesthetic philosophy. In *Skirmishes in a War with the Age*, discussing the relationship between [[jesus|Christ]]ianity and art, Nietzsche states:

> Let no one be so childish as to suggest Raphael or any homeopathic Christian of the nineteenth century as an objection to this statement. [006-169]

The "statement" in question is that "there is no such thing as a [[jesus|Christ]]ian who is also an artist." Nietzsche is not arguing that [[jesus|Christ]]ians cannot paint [[jesus|Christ]]ian subjects — the whole history of Western art refutes that trivial claim. His argument is that genuine artistic creation — the act of *saying Yea* to form, to beauty, to the [[appearance|world of appearance]]s — is incompatible with the [[jesus|Christ]]ian instinct, which fundamentally *says Nay* to the world, to the body, to life itself.

The conclusion follows with the force of a syllogism:

> Raphael said Yea, Raphael *did* Yea, — consequently Raphael was no Christian. [006-170]

The verb is doubled — "said Yea, *did* Yea" (*sagte Ja, that Ja*) — to make clear that Raphael's affirmation was not a doctrinal profession but an act. His painting is the evidence. The radiance, harmony, and bodily presence of Raphael's figures, their integration into a world of order and beauty, constitutes a *yes* to existence that contradicts [[jesus|Christ]]ianity's constitutive *no*. Whatever Raphael may have believed, his art proves him a pagan.

This argument aligns Raphael with the [[apollonian|Apollonian principle]] that Nietzsche developed in *The Birth of [[tragedy|Tragedy]]*. The [[apollonian|Apollonian]] artist transfigures the world through form, measure, and the dream of beauty — not as an escape from reality but as its intensification. Raphael's "Yea" is an [[apollonian|Apollonian]] affirmation, the artist's answer to the [[dionysian|Dionysian]] ecstasy that underlies all genuine creation.

## Key Works

- *The School of [[athens|Athens]]* (1509–1511) — The fresco in the Vatican's Stanza della Segnatura that gathers the philosophers of antiquity under a single harmonious architectural vision; a supreme expression of the classical-[[apollonian|Apollonian]] ideal Nietzsche associates with Raphael's "Yea"
- *Sistine Madonna* (1512) — The altarpiece whose radiant Madonna became an icon of Western art, embodying the synthesis of celestial grace and earthly beauty that Nietzsche's reading interprets as fundamentally pagan
