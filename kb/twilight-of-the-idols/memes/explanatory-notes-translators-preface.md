#### TRANSLATOR’S PREFACE

[013-001] The notes concerning the [[eternal-recurrence|Eternal Recurrence]], in this volume, are said

[013-002] by Mrs Foerster-Nietzsche to have been the first that Nietzsche ever

[013-003] wrote on the subject of his great doctrine. This being so, they must

[013-004] have been composed towards the autumn of the year 1881.

[013-005] I have already pointed out elsewhere ([[will-to-power|Will to Power]],vol. ii.,

[013-006] Translator’s Preface) how much importance Nietzsche himself ascribed

[013-007] to this doctrine, and how, until the end, he regarded it as the

[013-008] inspiration which had led to his chief work,Thus Spake [[zarathustra|Zarathustra]].For the details relating to its inception, however, I would refer the

[013-009] reader to Mrs Foerster-Nietzsche’s Introduction to her brother’s chief

[013-010] work, which was translated for the eleventh volume of this Edition of

[013-011] the Complete Works.

[013-012] In reading these notes it would be well to refer to Nietzsche’s other

[013-013] utterances on the subject which are to be found at the end of vol.

[013-014] ii. of theWill to Power,and also, if possible, to have recourse

[013-015] to the original German text. Despite the greatest care, I confess

[013-016] that in some instances, I have felt a little doubt as to the precise

[013-017] English equivalent for the thoughts expressed under the headingEternal Recurrence;and, though I have attributed this difficulty to

[013-018] the extreme novelty of the manner in which thesubject is presented,

[013-019] it is well that the reader should be aware that such doubt has been

[013-020] entertained. For I disbelieve utterly in mere verbal translation,

[013-021] however accurate, and would question anybody’s right to convert a

[013-022] German sentence into English—even though he were so perfect in

[013-023] both languages as to be almost absolutely bilingual,—if he did not

[013-024] completely grasp the thought behind the sentence.

[013-025] The writing of the collected Explanatory Notes toThus Spake

[013-026] Zarathustra, cannot be given any exact date. Some of them consist of

[013-027] comments, written down by Nietzsche after the completion of the book,

[013-028] and kept as the nucleus of an actual commentary to Zarathustra, which

[013-029] it seems to have been his intention, one day, to write; while others

[013-030] are merely memoranda and rough sketches, probably written before the

[013-031] completion of the work, and which served the purpose of a draft of his

[013-032] original plan. The reader who knowsThus Spake Zarathustrawill be

[013-033] able to tell wherein the book ultimately differed from the plan visible

[013-034] in these preliminary notes.

[013-035] As an authoritative, though alas! all too fragmentary elucidation of a

[013-036] few of the more obscure passages of Zarathustra, some of these notes

[013-037] are of the greatest value; and, in paragraph 73, for instance, there

[013-038] is an interpretation of the Fourth and Last Part, which I myself would

[013-039] have welcomed with great enthusiasm, at the time when I was having my

[013-040] first struggles with the spirit of this great German sage’s life work.

[013-041] ANTHONY M. LUDOVICI.
