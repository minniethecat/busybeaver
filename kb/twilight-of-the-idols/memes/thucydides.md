# Thucydides

## Biography

Thucydides (c. 460–400 BCE) was an Athenian historian and general, best known for his *History of the Peloponnesian War*, which chronicled the conflict between [[athens|Athens]] and Sparta. Exiled from [[athens|Athens]] after a military failure at Amphipolis, he used his enforced detachment to produce an unprecedentedly rigorous and unsentimental account of political and military events. Unlike his predecessor Herodotus, he excluded divine causation and moralising narratives, grounding his analysis in observable facts and human nature — a methodological commitment that earned him recognition as the father of scientific history.

## Philosophical Contributions

Thucydides' principal contribution to Western thought is his articulation of political realism. His *History* presents power, fear, and self-interest as the fundamental drivers of state behaviour, most famously crystallised in the Melian Dialogue: "the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must." He rejected providential narratives and insisted that events be explained through verifiable causes and the unchanging dynamics of human nature. For this, the Sophistic movement claimed him as its supreme representative — he embodied the turn from mythos to logos, from divine explanation to empirical investigation. His method prefigures Machiavelli and Hobbes, both of whom drew directly upon his insights into power and political psychology.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's admiration for Thucydides is among the most emphatic he bestows on any figure in the entire corpus. In *Things I Owe to the Ancients* (ch07), he declares Thucydides his "recreation, my predilection, my cure, after all [[plato|Plato]]nism" [007-059]. He places Thucydides alongside Machiavelli's *Principe* as works "most closely related to me owing to the absolute determination which they show of refusing to deceive themselves and of seeing reason in *reality* — not in 'rationality,' and still less in 'morality'" [007-064]. There is, Nietzsche insists, "no more radical cure than Thucydides for the lamentably rose-coloured idealisation of the Greeks" [007-073].

The core of Nietzsche's esteem lies in the contrast with [[plato|Plato]]. Whereas [[plato|Plato]] "is a coward in the face of reality — consequently he takes refuge in the ideal," Thucydides "is master of himself — consequently he is able to master life" [007-077]. For Nietzsche, this is the fundamental distinction between the two Greek temperaments: the one flees from the harshness of existence into fabricated metaphysical comforts; the other confronts existence with unblinking clarity and self-possession. Thucydides represents "the great summing up, the final manifestation of that strong, severe positivism which lay in the instincts of the ancient Hellene" [007-078]. Greek philosophy after the Sophists is, in Nietzsche's diagnosis, a story of decline — "the decadence of the Greek instinct" — and Thucydides stands as its terminal peak before the fall.

> "My recreation, my predilection, my cure, after all Platonism, has always been Thucydides. Thucydides and perhaps Machiavelli's *Principe* are most closely related to me owing to the absolute determination which they show of refusing to deceive themselves and of seeing reason in *reality* — not in 'rationality,' and still less in 'morality.'" [007-059]

## Key Works

- *History of the Peloponnesian War* (c. 400 BCE) — An eight-book account of the war between [[athens|Athens]] and Sparta, distinguished by its empirical rigour, political realism, and exclusion of supernatural explanation
- *Pericles' Funeral Oration* (contained within the *History*) — A foundational text of democratic political philosophy, articulating Athenian self-understanding at the height of the city's power
