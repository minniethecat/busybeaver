#### THE FOUR GREAT ERRORS

[003-001] The error of the confusion of cause and effect.—There is no more

[003-002] dangerous error than to confound the effect with the cause: I call

[003-003] this error the intrinsic perversion of reason. Nevertheless this error

[003-004] is one of the most ancient and most recent habits of mankind. In one

[003-005] part of the world it has even been canonised; and it bears the name of

[003-006] “Religion” and “Morality.” Every postulate formulated by religion and

[003-007] morality contains it. Priests and the promulgators of moral laws are the

[003-008] promoters of this perversion of reason.—Let me give you an example.

[003-009] Everybody knows the book of the famous Cornaro, in which he recommends

[003-010] his slender diet as the recipe for a long, happy and also virtuous

[003-011] life. Few books have been so widely read, and to this day many thousand

[003-012] copies of it are still printed annually in England. I do not doubt that

[003-013] there is scarcely a single book (the Bible of course excepted) that

[003-014] has worked more mischief, shortened more lives, than this well-meant

[003-015] curiosity. The reason of this is the confusion of effect and cause.

[003-016] This worthy Italian saw the cause of his long life in his diet: whereas

[003-017] the prerequisites of long life, which are exceptional slowness of

[003-018] molecular change, and a low rate of expenditure in energy, were the

[003-019] cause of his meagrediet He was not at liberty to eat a small or a

[003-020] great amount. His frugality was not the result of free choice, he would

[003-021] have been ill had he eaten more. He who does not happen to be a carp,

[003-022] however, is not only wise to eat well, but is also compelled to do so.

[003-023] A scholar of the present day, with his rapid consumption of nervous

[003-024] energy, would soon go to the dogs on Cornaro’s diet.Crede experto.—

[003-025] The most general principle lying at the root of every religion and

[003-026] morality, is this: “Do this and that and avoid this and that—and

[003-027] thou wilt be happy. Otherwise——.” Every morality and every

[003-028] religion is this Imperative—I call it the great original sin of

[003-029] reason,—immortal unreason.In my mouth this principle is converted

[003-030] into its opposite—first example of my “Transvaluation of all Values”:

[003-031] a well-constituted man, a man who is one of “Nature’s lucky strokes,”mustperform certain actions and instinctively fear other actions;

[003-032] he introduces the element of order, of which he is the physiological

[003-033] manifestation, into his relations with men and things. In a formula:

[003-034] his virtue is the consequence of his good constitution. Longevity

[003-035] and plentiful offspring are not the reward of virtue, virtue itself

[003-036] is on the contrary that retardation of the metabolic process which,

[003-037] among other things, results in a long life and in plentiful offspring,

[003-038] in short inCornarism.The Church and morality say: “A race, a

[003-039] people perish through vice and luxury.” My reinstated reason says:

[003-040] when a people are going tothe dogs, when they are degenerating

[003-041] physiologically, vice and luxury (that is to say, the need of ever

[003-042] stronger and more frequent stimuli such as all exhausted natures are

[003-043] acquainted with) are bound to result. Such and such a young man grows

[003-044] pale and withered prematurely. His friends say this or that illness

[003-045] is the cause of it I say: the fact that he became ill, the fact that

[003-046] he did not resist illness, was in itself already the outcome of

[003-047] impoverished life, of hereditary exhaustion. The newspaper reader says:

[003-048] such and such a party by committing such an error will meet its death.

[003-049] My superior politics say: a party that can make such mistakes, is in

[003-050] its last agony—it no longer possesses any certainty of instinct. Every

[003-051] mistake is in every sense the sequel to degeneration of the instincts,

[003-052] to disintegration of the will. This is almost the definition of evil,

[003-053] Everything valuable is instinct—and consequently easy, necessary,

[003-054] free. Exertion is an objection, the god is characteristically different

[003-055] from the hero (in my language: light feet are the first attribute of

[003-056] divinity).

[003-057] The error of false causality.In all ages men have believed that

[003-058] they knew what a cause was: but whence did we derive this knowledge,

[003-059] or more accurately, this faith in the fact that we know? Out of the

[003-060] realm of the famous “inner facts of consciousness,” not one of which

[003-061] has yet proved itself to be a fact We believed ourselves to be causes

[003-062] even in the action of the will; we thought that in this matter at

[003-063] least we caught causality red-handed. No onedoubted that all theantecedentiaof an action were to be sought in consciousness, and

[003-064] could be discovered there—as “motive”—if only they were sought.

[003-065] Otherwise we should not be free to perform them, we should not have

[003-066] been responsible for them. Finally who would have questioned that a

[003-067] thought is caused? that the ego causes the thought? Of these three

[003-068] “facts of inner consciousness” by means of which causality seemed to

[003-069] be guaranteed, the first and most convincing is that of the will as

[003-070] cause; the conception of consciousness (“spirit”) as a cause, and

[003-071] subsequently that of the ego (the “subject”) as a cause, were merely

[003-072] born afterwards, once the causality of the will stood established

[003-073] as “given,” as a fact of experience. Meanwhile we have come to our

[003-074] senses. To-day we no longer believe a word of all this. The “inner

[003-075] world” is full of phantoms and will-o’-the-wisps: the will is one of

[003-076] these. The will no longer actuates, consequently it no longer explains

[003-077] anything—all it does is to accompany processes; it may even be absent.

[003-078] The so-called “motive” is another error. It is merely a ripple on

[003-079] the surface of consciousness, a side issue of the action, which is

[003-080] much more likely to conceal than to reveal theantecedentiaof the

[003-081] latter. And as for the ego! It has become legendary, fictional, a

[003-082] play upon words: it has ceased utterly and completely from thinking,

[003-083] feeling, and willing! What is the result of it all? There are no such

[003-084] things as spiritual causes. The whole of popular experience on this

[003-085] subject went to the devil! That is the result of it all. For we had

[003-086] blissfully abused that experience, we had built the world upon it as a

[003-087] world of causes, as a worldof will, as a world of spirit. The most

[003-088] antiquated and most traditional psychology has been at work here, it

[003-089] has done nothing else: all phenomena were deeds in the light of this

[003-090] psychology, and all deeds were the result of will; according to it the

[003-091] world was a complex mechanism of agents, an agent (a “subject”) lay

[003-092] at the root of all things. Man projected his three “inner facts of

[003-093] consciousness,” the will, the spirit, and the ego in which he believed

[003-094] most firmly, outside himself. He first deduced the concept Being out

[003-095] of the concept Ego, he supposed “things” to exist as he did himself,

[003-096] according to his notion of the ego as cause. Was it to be wondered at

[003-097] that later on he always found in things only that which he had laid

[003-098] in them?—The thing itself, I repeat, the concept thing was merely a

[003-099] reflex of the belief in the ego as cause. And even your atom, my dear

[003-100] good Mechanists and Physicists, what an amount of error, of rudimentary

[003-101] psychology still adheres to it!—Not to speak of the “thing-in-itself,”

[003-102] of thehorrendum pudendumof the metaphysicians! The error of spirit

[003-103] regarded as a cause, confounded with reality! And made the measure of

[003-104] reality! And calledGod!

[003-105] The Error of imaginary Causes.Starting out from dreamland, we

[003-106] find that to any definite sensation, like that produced by a distant

[003-107] cannon shot for instance, we are wont to ascribe a cause after the

[003-108] fact (very often quite a little romance in which the dreamer himself

[003-109] is, of course, the hero). Meanwhile the sensation becomes protracted

[003-110] like a sort ofcontinuous echo, until, as it were, the instinct of

[003-111] causality allows it to come to the front rank, no longer however as a

[003-112] chance occurrence, but as a thing which has some meaning. The cannon

[003-113] shot presents itself in acausalmanner, by means of an apparent

[003-114] reversal in the order of time. That which occurs last, the motivation,

[003-115] is experienced first, often with a hundred details which flash past

[003-116] like lightning, and the shot is theresult.What has happened? The

[003-117] ideas suggested by a particular state of our senses, are misinterpreted

[003-118] as the cause of that state. As a matter of fact we proceed in precisely

[003-119] the same manner when we are awake. The greater number of our general

[003-120] sensations—every kind of obstacle, pressure, tension, explosion in

[003-121] the interplay of the organs, and more particularly the condition of

[003-122] thenervus sympathies—stimulate our instinct of causality: we will

[003-123] have a reason which will account for our feeling thus or thus,—for

[003-124] feeling ill or well. We are never satisfied by merely ascertaining

[003-125] the fact that we feel thus or thus: we admit this fact—we become

[003-126] conscious of it—only when we have attributed it to some kind of

[003-127] motivation. Memory, which, in such circumstances unconsciously becomes

[003-128] active, adduces former conditions of a like kind, together with the

[003-129] causal interpretations with which they are associated,—but not their

[003-130] real cause. The belief that the ideas, the accompanying processes

[003-131] of consciousness, have been the causes, is certainly produced by

[003-132] the agency of memory. And in this way we becomeaccustomedto a

[003-133] particular interpretation of causes which, truth to tell, actually

[003-134] hinders and even utterly prevents the investigation of the proper

[003-135] cause.

[003-136] The Psychological Explanation of the above Fact.To trace something

[003-137] unfamiliar back to something familiar, is at once a relief, a comfort

[003-138] and a satisfaction, while it also produces a feeling of power. The

[003-139] unfamiliar involves danger, anxiety and care,—the fundamental instinct

[003-140] is to get rid of these painful circumstances. First principle: any

[003-141] explanation is better than none at all. Since, at bottom, it is

[003-142] only a question of shaking one’s self free from certain oppressive

[003-143] ideas, the means employed to this end are not selected with overmuch

[003-144] punctiliousness: the first idea by means of which the unfamiliar is

[003-145] revealed as familiar, produces a feeling of such comfort that it is

[003-146] “held to be true.” The proof of happiness (“of power”) as the criterion

[003-147] of truth. The instinct of causality is therefore conditioned and

[003-148] stimulated by the feeling of fear. Whenever possible, the question

[003-149] “why?” should not only educe the cause as cause, but rather a certain

[003-150] kind of cause—a comforting, liberating and reassuring cause. The first

[003-151] result of this need is that something known or already experienced, and

[003-152] recorded in the memory, is posited as the cause. The new factor, that

[003-153] which has not been experienced and which is unfamiliar, is excluded

[003-154] from the sphere of causes. Not only do we try to find a certain kind

[003-155] of explanation as the cause, but those kinds of explanations are

[003-156] selected and preferred which dissipate most rapidly the sensation of

[003-157] strangeness, novelty and unfamiliarity,—in fact the most ordinary

[003-158] explanations. And the result is that a certain manner of postulating

[003-159] causes tends to predominate ever more and more, becomes concentratedinto a system, and finally reigns supreme, to the complete exclusion

[003-160] of all other causes and explanations. The banker thinks immediately of

[003-161] business, the Christian of “sin,” and the girl of her love affair.

[003-162] The whole Domain of Morality and Religion may be classified under the

[003-163] Rubric “Imaginary Causes.”The “explanation” of general unpleasant

[003-164] sensations. These sensations are dependent upon certain creatures who

[003-165] are hostile to us (evil spirits: the most famous example of this—the

[003-166] mistaking of hysterical women for witches). These sensations are

[003-167] dependent upon actions which are reprehensible (the feeling of “sin,”

[003-168] “sinfulness” is a manner of accounting for a certain physiological

[003-169] disorder—people always find reasons for being dissatisfied with

[003-170] themselves). These sensations depend upon punishment, upon compensation

[003-171] for something which we ought not to have done, which we ought not

[003-172] to have been (this idea was generalised in a more impudent form by

[003-173] Schopenhauer, into that principle in which morality appears in its real

[003-174] colours,—that is to say, as a veritable poisoner and slanderer of

[003-175] life: “all great suffering, whether mental or physical, reveals what

[003-176] we deserve: for it could not visit us if we did not deserve it,” “The

[003-177] World as Will and Idea,” vol. 2, p. 666). These sensations are the

[003-178] outcome of ill-considered actions, having evil consequences, (—the

[003-179] passions, the senses, postulated as causes, as guilty. By means of

[003-180] other calamities distressing physiological conditions are interpreted

[003-181] as “merited”).—The “explanation” of pleasant sensations. These

[003-182] sensations aredependent upon a trust in God. They may depend upon

[003-183] our consciousness of having done one or two good actions (a so-called

[003-184] “good conscience” is a physiological condition, which may be the

[003-185] outcome of good digestion). They may depend upon the happy issue of

[003-186] certain undertakings (—an ingenuous mistake: the happy issue of an

[003-187] undertaking certainly does not give a hypochondriac or a Pascal any

[003-188] general sensation of pleasure). They may depend upon faith, love and

[003-189] hope,—the Christian virtues. As a matter of fact all these pretended

[003-190] explanations are but the results of certain states, and as it were

[003-191] translations of feelings of pleasure and pain into a false dialect: a

[003-192] man is in a condition of hopefulness because the dominant physiological

[003-193] sensation of his being is again one of strength and wealth; he trusts

[003-194] in God because the feeling of abundance and power gives him a peaceful

[003-195] state of mind. Morality and religion are completely and utterly parts

[003-196] of the psychology of error: in every particular case cause and effect

[003-197] are confounded; as truth is confounded with the effect of that which is

[003-198] believed to be true; or a certain state of consciousness is confounded

[003-199] with the chain of causes which brought it about.

[003-200] The Error of Free-Will.At present we no longer have any mercy upon

[003-201] the concept “free-will”: we know only too well what it is—the most

[003-202] egregious theological trick that has ever existed for the purpose of

[003-203] making mankind “responsible” in a theological manner,—that is to

[003-204] say, to make mankind dependent upon theologians. I will now explain

[003-205] to you onlythe psychology of the whole process of inculcating the

[003-206] sense of responsibility. Wherever men try to trace responsibility

[003-207] home to anyone, it is the instinct of punishment and of the desire

[003-208] to judge which is active. Becoming is robbed of its innocence when

[003-209] any particular condition of things is traced to a will, to intentions

[003-210] and to responsible actions. The doctrine of the will was invented

[003-211] principally for the purpose of punishment,—that is to say, with

[003-212] the intention of tracing guilt. The whole of ancient psychology,

[003-213] or the psychology of the will, is the outcome of the fact that its

[003-214] originators, who were the priests at the head of ancient communities,

[003-215] wanted to create for themselves a right to administer punishments—or

[003-216] the right for God to do so. Men were thought of as “free” in order that

[003-217] they might be judged and punished—in order that they might be held

[003-218] guilty: consequently every action had to be regarded as voluntary,

[003-219] and the origin of every action had to be imagined as lying in

[003-220] consciousness(—in this way the most fundamentally fraudulent character

[003-221] of psychology was established as the very principle of psychology

[003-222] itself). Now that we have entered upon the opposite movement, now that

[003-223] we immoralists are trying with all our power to eliminate the concepts

[003-224] of guilt and punishment from the world once more, and to cleanse

[003-225] psychology, history, nature and all social institutions and customs

[003-226] of all signs of those two concepts, we recognise no more radical

[003-227] opponents than the theologians, who with their notion of “a moral order

[003-228] of things,” still continue to pollute the innocence of Becoming with

[003-229] punishment and guilt Christianity is the metaphysics of the hangman.

[003-230] What then, alone, can our teaching be?—That no one gives man his

[003-231] qualities, neither God, society, his parents, his ancestors, nor

[003-232] himself (—this non-sensical idea which is at last refuted here, was

[003-233] taught as “intelligible freedom” by Kant, and perhaps even as early

[003-234] as [[plato|Plato]] himself). No one is responsible for the fact that he exists

[003-235] at all, that he is constituted as he is, and that he happens to be in

[003-236] certain circumstances and in a particular environment. The fatality of

[003-237] his being cannot be divorced from the fatality of all that which has

[003-238] been and will be. This is not the result of an individual intention,

[003-239] of a will, of an aim, there is no attempt at attaining to any “ideal

[003-240] man,” or “ideal happiness” or “ideal morality” with him,—it is absurd

[003-241] to wish him to be careering towards some sort of purpose.Weinvented

[003-242] the concept “purpose”; in reality purpose is altogether lacking. One

[003-243] is necessary, one is a piece of fate, one belongs to the whole, one is

[003-244] in the whole,—there is nothing that could judge, measure, compare,

[003-245] and condemn our existence, for that would mean judging, measuring,

[003-246] comparing and condemning the whole.But there is nothing outside the

[003-247] whole!The fact that no one shall any longer be made responsible, that

[003-248] the nature of existence may not be traced to acausa prima, that the

[003-249] world is an entity neither as a sensorium nor as a spirit—this alone

[003-250] is the great deliverance,—thus alone is the innocence of Becoming

[003-251] restored.... The concept “God” has been the greatest objection to

[003-252] existence hitherto.... We deny God, we deny responsibility in God: thus

[003-253] alone do we save the world.—
