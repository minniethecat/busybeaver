# Charles Darwin

## Biography

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) was an English naturalist whose theory of evolution by natural selection, published in *On the Origin of Species* (1859), transformed biology and challenged traditional views of human nature. Nietzsche engaged with Darwin's ideas critically, seeing in them a projection of English commercial values onto nature.

## Philosophical Contributions

Darwin's theory of natural selection proposed that organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive and reproduce, leading to the gradual evolution of species. The "struggle for existence" became a powerful metaphor that extended beyond biology into social and philosophical thought in the late 19th century.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche's critique of Darwin is concentrated in a single section of *Skirmishes in a War with the Age* titled "Anti-Darwin":

> As to the famous "struggle for existence," it seems to me to be more of an assertion than a fact. [006-256]

Nietzsche challenges Darwin's central premise. The "struggle for existence" is not, in Nietzsche's view, the fundamental reality Darwin claims it to be — it is a projection, an interpretation imposed on nature rather than discovered in it.

His deeper objection concerns what the struggle actually produces:

> The result is unfortunately the very reverse of that which the Darwinian school expects. The species do *not* grow in perfection: the weak again and again get the upper hand of the strong. [006-263]

This is Nietzsche's counter-thesis: natural selection does not produce excellence — it produces mediocrity. The weak, by virtue of their greater numbers and their cunning, survive and reproduce more successfully than the strong. Evolution is not a story of progress but of decline.

Nietzsche's most pointed critique concerns what Darwin overlooked:

> Darwin forgot the intellect (—that is English!), the weak have more intellect. [006-269]

The parenthetical "that is English!" is characteristic Nietzsche: Darwin's theory reflects English prejudices — the commercial, utilitarian values of a nation of shopkeepers. Darwin "forgot" the intellect because the English worldview cannot see that cunning, not strength, determines survival. The weak survive not because they are fitter but because they are *cleverer* — a circumstance Nietzsche regards as biologically decadent.

This brief but dense critique encapsulates Nietzsche's broader rejection of Darwinism. Where Darwin sees a mechanism of improvement, Nietzsche sees a mechanism of deterioration. Where Darwin naturalizes English commercial competition, Nietzsche insists on the primacy of the will to power — a force that cannot be reduced to mere survival.
