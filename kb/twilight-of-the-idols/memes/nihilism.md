# Nihilism

## Definition

Nihilism is, for Nietzsche, not merely a philosophical position but a historical condition: the process by which "the highest values devalue themselves." It represents the logical endpoint of Western metaphysics and [[jesus|Christ]]ian morality — when the belief in transcendent truth collapses, what remains is the absence of all meaning and value.

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

Nietzsche addresses nihilism most directly in connection with his critique of morality and truth. The death of God — the collapse of all transcendent foundations — is the precondition for nihilism. In *The Antichrist*, he describes the "nihilistic will to power" (010-203) as the self-destructive form that will to power takes when it can no longer affirm life.

Nietzsche's "philosophizing with a hammer" (008-001) is his method for confronting nihilism — not by restoring old values but by creating new ones through the revaluation of all values.

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's analysis of nihilism was profoundly influential on 20th-century thought. Heidegger interpreted it as the culmination of Western metaphysics; Deleuze saw it as the necessary passage toward active, creative forces. Nietzsche himself claimed to be the first "perfect nihilist of [[europe|Europe]]" — one who has lived through nihilism completely and emerged beyond it.
