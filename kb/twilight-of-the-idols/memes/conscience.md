# Conscience

## Definition

**Conscience** (*Gewissen*) in Nietzsche's moral psychology is not a divine voice or an innate moral faculty but a historically produced [[appearance|phenomenon]] — the internalization of cruelty and the turning of the instinct for freedom against the self. In *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s*, Nietzsche treats conscience primarily through its negative form: "bad conscience" is the sickness produced by the "improvement" of mankind by the [[the-priesthood|priestly class]], while "good conscience" is identified with a physiological condition of well-being rather than moral righteousness (003-184).

Nietzsche's analysis of conscience is continuous with his treatment in *On the [[genealogy|Genealogy]] of Morals* (1887), but *Twilight of the [[idol|Idol]]s* sharpens the critique by connecting conscience directly to the [[the-four-great-errors-concept|Four Great Errors]] — particularly the error of [[causality|false causality]] and the error of free will.

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

**Chapter 2 ("Morality as the Enemy of Nature").** Nietzsche identifies the "plump happiness of a clean conscience" as one of the seductions of the moral worldview (002-069). A "clean conscience" is not evidence of virtue but of *physiological* well-being projected onto the moral plane — the feeling of strength and health interpreted as moral approval of oneself.

**Chapter 3 ("The Four Great [[error|Error]]s").** Nietzsche provides the physiological grounding: "'Good conscience' is a physiological condition — just as 'bad conscience' is" (003-184). The feeling of moral approbation or condemnation is, at bottom, a state of the organism. The error is to treat these feelings as *perceptions* of moral facts rather than as *symptoms* of bodily states.

**Chapter 6 ("Skirmishes").** Nietzsche extends the critique to artistic conscience — "to do violence to his conscience" is what the artist risks when he breaks with convention (006-669). Conscience here functions as internalized social control, the voice of the herd within.

> A new conscience for truths which hitherto have remained unheard (010-015)

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's treatment of conscience is a radical naturalization of a concept previously owned by theology and idealist philosophy. Where Kant made conscience the voice of the moral law within, Nietzsche makes it the echo of cruelty turned inward. The "good conscience" is not the reward of virtue but the *feeling* of power — and "bad conscience" is the feeling of powerlessness, the organism's sickness at its own self-suppression.

This analysis is politically charged: if conscience is a historical product rather than a transcendental faculty, then it can be *changed*. Nietzsche's call for a "new conscience" (010-015) is a call for a conscience that affirms rather than condemns — one appropriate to [[amor-fati|amor fati]] rather than to guilt.
