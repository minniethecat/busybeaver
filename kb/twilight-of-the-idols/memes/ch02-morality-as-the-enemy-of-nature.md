#### MORALITY AS THE ENEMY OF NATURE

[002-001] There is a time when all passions are simply fatal in their action,

[002-002] when they wreck their victims with the weight of their folly,—and

[002-003] there is a later period, a very much later period, when they marry with

[002-004] the spirit, when they “spiritualise” themselves. Formerly, owing to the

[002-005] stupidity inherent in passion, men waged war against passion itself:

[002-006] men pledged themselves to annihilate it,—all ancient moral-mongers

[002-007] were unanimous on this point, “il faut tuer les passions.” The

[002-008] most famous formula for this stands in the New Testament, in that

[002-009] Sermon on the Mount, where, let it be said incidentally, things are

[002-010] by no means regardedfrom a height.It is said there, for instance,

[002-011] with an application to sexuality: “if thy eye offend thee, pluck it

[002-012] out”: fortunately no Christian acts in obedience to this precept.

[002-013] To annihilate the passions and desires, simply on account of their

[002-014] stupidity, and to obviate the unpleasant consequences of their

[002-015] stupidity, seems to us to-day merely an aggravated form of stupidity.

[002-016] We no longer admire those dentists who extract teeth simply in order

[002-017] that they may not ache again. On the other hand, it will be admitted

[002-018] with some reason, that on the soil from which Christianity grew, the

[002-019] idea ofthe “spiritualisation of passion” could not possibly have been

[002-020] conceived. The early Church, as everyone knows, certainly did wage war

[002-021] against the “intelligent,” in favour of the “poor in spirit” In these

[002-022] circumstances how could the passions be combated intelligently? The

[002-023] Church combats passion by means of excision of all kinds: its practice,

[002-024] its “remedy,” iscastration.It never inquires “how can a desire

[002-025] be spiritualised, beautified, deified?”—In all ages it has laid the

[002-026] weight of discipline in the process of extirpation (the extirpation

[002-027] of sensuality, pride, lust of dominion, lust of property, and

[002-028] revenge).—But to attack the passions at their roots, means attacking

[002-029] life itself at its source: the method of the Church is hostile to life.

[002-030] The same means, castration and extirpation, are instinctively chosen

[002-031] for waging war against a passion, by those who are too weak of will,

[002-032] too degenerate, to impose some sort of moderation upon it; by those

[002-033] natures who, to speak in metaphor (—and without metaphor), needla Trappe,or some kind of ultimatum of war, agulfset between

[002-034] themselves and a passion. Only degenerates find radical methods

[002-035] indispensable: weakness of will, or more strictly speaking, the

[002-036] inability not to react to a stimulus, is in itself simply another form

[002-037] of degeneracy. Radical and mortal hostility to sensuality, remains a

[002-038] suspicious symptom: it justifies one in being suspicious of the general

[002-039] state of one who goes to such extremes. Moreover, that hostility and

[002-040] hatred reach their height only when such natures no longer possess

[002-041] enough strength of character to adopt theradical remedy, to renounce

[002-042] their inner “Satan.” Look at the whole history of the priests, the

[002-043] philosophers, and the artists as well: the most poisonous diatribes

[002-044] against the senses have not been said by the impotent, nor by the

[002-045] ascetics; but by those impossible ascetics, by those who found it

[002-046] necessary to be ascetics.

[002-047] The spiritualisation of sensuality is calledlove:it is a great

[002-048] triumph over Christianity. Another triumph is our spiritualisation of

[002-049] hostility. It consists in the fact that we are beginning to realise

[002-050] very profoundly the value of having enemies: in short that with them

[002-051] we are forced to do and to conclude precisely the reverse of what

[002-052] we previously did and concluded. In all ages the Church wished to

[002-053] annihilate its enemies: we, the immoralists and Antichrists, see our

[002-054] advantage in the survival of the Church. Even in political life,

[002-055] hostility has now become more spiritual,—much more cautious, much

[002-056] more thoughtful, and much more moderate. Almost every party sees its

[002-057] self-preservative interests in preventing the Opposition from going

[002-058] to pieces; and the same applies to politics on a grand scale. A new

[002-059] creation, more particularly, like the new Empire, has more need

[002-060] of enemies than friends: only as a contrast does it begin to feel

[002-061] necessary, only as a contrast does itbecomenecessary. And we behave

[002-062] in precisely the same way to the “inner enemy”: in this quarter too we

[002-063] have spiritualised enmity, in this quarter too we have understood its

[002-064] value. A man is productive only in so far as he is rich in contrasted

[002-065] instincts; he can remain young only oncondition that his soul does

[002-066] not begin to take things easy and to yearn for peace. Nothing has

[002-067] grown more alien to us than that old desire—the “peace of the soul,”

[002-068] which is the aim of Christianity. Nothing could make us less envious

[002-069] than the moral cow and the plump happiness of a clean conscience. The

[002-070] man who has renounced war has renounced a grand life. In many cases,

[002-071] of course, “peace of the soul” is merely a misunderstanding,—it is

[002-072] somethingvery differentwhich has failed to find a more honest name

[002-073] for itself. Without either circumlocution or prejudice I will suggest a

[002-074] few cases. “Peace of the soul” may for instance be the sweet effulgence

[002-075] of rich animality in the realm of morality (or religion). Or the first

[002-076] presage of weariness, the first shadow that evening, every kind of

[002-077] evening, is wont to cast. Or a sign that the air is moist, and that

[002-078] winds are blowing up from the south. Or unconscious gratitude for a

[002-079] good digestion (sometimes called “brotherly love”). Or the serenity

[002-080] of the convalescent, on whose lips all things have a new taste, and

[002-081] who bides his time. Or the condition which follows upon a thorough

[002-082] gratification of our strongest passion, the well-being of unaccustomed

[002-083] satiety. Or the senility of our will, of our desires, and of our vices.

[002-084] Or laziness, coaxed by vanity into togging itself out in a moral garb.

[002-085] Or the ending of a state of long suspense and of agonising uncertainty,

[002-086] by a state of certainty, of even terrible certainty. Or the expression

[002-087] of ripeness and mastery in the midst of a task, of a creative work, of

[002-088] a production, of a thing willed, the calm breathing that denotes that

[002-089] “freedom of will” has been attained.Who knows?—maybeThe Twilight

[002-090] of the Idolsis only a sort of “peace of the soul.”

[002-091] I will formulate a principle. All naturalism in morality—that is to

[002-092] say, every sound morality is ruled by a life instinct,—any one of

[002-093] the laws of life is fulfilled by the definite canon “thou shalt,”

[002-094] “thou shalt not,” and any sort of obstacle or hostile element in the

[002-095] road of life is thus cleared away. Conversely, the morality which is

[002-096] antagonistic to nature—that is to say, almost every morality that has

[002-097] been taught, honoured and preached hitherto, is directed precisely

[002-098] against the life-instincts,—it is a condemnation, now secret, now

[002-099] blatant and impudent, of these very instincts. Inasmuch as it says “God

[002-100] sees into the heart of man,” it says Nay to the profoundest and most

[002-101] superior desires of life and takes God as the enemy of life. The saint

[002-102] in whom God is well pleased, is the ideal eunuch. Life terminates where

[002-103] the “Kingdom of God” begins.

[002-104] Admitting that you have understood the villainy of such a mutiny

[002-105] against life as that which has become almost sacrosanct in Christian

[002-106] morality, you have fortunately understood something besides; and that

[002-107] is the futility, the fictitiousness, the absurdity and the falseness

[002-108] of such a mutiny. For the condemnation of life by a living creature is

[002-109] after all but the symptom of a definite kind of life: the question as

[002-110] to whether the condemnation is justified or the reverse is not even

[002-111] raised. In order even to approach the problem of the value of life,

[002-112] a man would needto be placed outside life, and moreover know it as

[002-113] well as one, as many, as all in fact, who have lived it These are

[002-114] reasons enough to prove to us that this problem is an inaccessible one

[002-115] to us. When we speak of values, we speak under the inspiration, and

[002-116] through the optics of life: life itself urges us to determine values:

[002-117] life itself values through us when we determine values. From which it

[002-118] follows that even that morality which is antagonistic to life, and

[002-119] which conceives God as the opposite and the condemnation of life, is

[002-120] only a valuation of life—of what life? of what kind of life? But I

[002-121] have already answered this question: it is the valuation of declining,

[002-122] of enfeebled, of exhausted and of condemned life. Morality, as it has

[002-123] been understood hitherto—as it was finally formulated by Schopenhauer

[002-124] in the words “The Denial of the Will to Life,” is the instinct of

[002-125] degeneration itself, which converts itself into an imperative: it says:

[002-126] “Perish!” It is the death sentence of men who are already doomed.

[002-127] Let us at last consider how exceedingly simple it is on our part to

[002-128] say: “Man should be thus and thus!” Reality shows us a marvellous

[002-129] wealth of types, and a luxuriant variety of forms and changes: and yet

[002-130] the first wretch of a moral loafer that comes along cries “No! Man

[002-131] should be different!” He even knows what man should be like, does this

[002-132] sanctimonious prig: he draws his own face on the wall and declares:

[002-133] “ecce homo!” But even when the moralist addresses himself only to the

[002-134] individual and says “thus and thus shouldst thou be!” he still makesan ass of himself. The individual in his past and future is a piece of

[002-135] fate, one law the more, one necessity the more for all that is to come

[002-136] and is to be. To say to him “change thyself,” is tantamount to saying

[002-137] that everything should change, even backwards as well. Truly these have

[002-138] been consistent moralists, they wished man to be different,i.e.,

[002-139] virtuous; they wished him to be after their own image,—that is to say

[002-140] sanctimonious humbugs. And to this end they denied the world! No slight

[002-141] form of insanity! No modest form of immodesty! Morality, in so far it

[002-142] condemnsper se,andnotout of any aim, consideration or motive of

[002-143] life, is a specific error, for which no one should feel any mercy, a

[002-144] degenerate idiosyncrasy, that has done an unutterable amount of harm.

[002-145] We others, we immoralists, on the contrary, have opened our hearts

[002-146] wide to all kinds of comprehension, understanding and approbation.[1]We do not deny readily, we glory in saying yea to things. Our eyes

[002-147] have opened ever wider and wider to that economy which still employs

[002-148] and knows how to use to its own advantage all that which the sacred

[002-149] craziness of priests and the morbid reason in priests, rejects; to

[002-150] that economy in the law of life which draws its own advantage even out

[002-151] of the repulsive race of bigots, the priests and the virtuous,—what

[002-152] advantage?—But we ourselves, we immoralists, are the reply to this

[002-153] question.

[002-155] non tugert, negue detestari, sed intelligere”(“I have carefully

[002-156] endeavoured not to deride, or deplore, or detest human actions, but to

[002-157] understand them.”).—TR.
