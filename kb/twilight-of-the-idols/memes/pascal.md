# Blaise Pascal

## Biography

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was a French mathematician, physicist, and religious philosopher. A child prodigy in mathematics, he made foundational contributions to probability theory and hydrostatics before a profound religious experience in 1654 led him to devote himself to Jansenist theology. His unfinished apologetic work, the *Pensées*, became a classic of [[jesus|Christ]]ian philosophy.

## Philosophical Contributions

Pascal's philosophy centers on the paradoxes of human existence — man as a "thinking reed," poised between the infinite and the void. His famous "wager" argued for belief in God as a rational bet. He emphasized the limits of reason and the primacy of faith, holding that the heart has its reasons which reason cannot know. His critique of diversion and his analysis of human wretchedness without God established him as a forerunner of existentialist thought.

## Nietzsche's View

Nietzsche regarded Pascal as the paradigmatic case of [[jesus|Christ]]ian self-destruction — a great intellect corrupted by faith. He described Pascal as a "hypochondriac" whose physiological condition shaped his religious convictions [003-187]. In *Skirmishes of an Untimely Man*, Nietzsche identified Pascal as the genuine [[jesus|Christ]]ian who proves that no [[jesus|Christ]]ian can also be an artist: "There is no such thing as a [[jesus|Christ]]ian who is also an artist" [006-167]. The most severe judgment appears in *The Antichrist*: "The most lamentable example of this was the corruption of Pascal, who believed in the corruption of his reason through original sin, when it had been corrupted only by his [[jesus|Christ]]ianity" [010-086].
