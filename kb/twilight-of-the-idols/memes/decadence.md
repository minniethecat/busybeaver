# Decadence

## Definition

Decadence (décadence) in Nietzsche's philosophy refers to the physiological and psychological degeneration of the life-affirming instincts. It is characterized by the preference for that which is harmful to life — weakness over strength, pity over power, reaction over action. Nietzsche identifies decadence as the fundamental condition of modern [[europe|European civilization]] (001-134).

## In *Twilight of the Idols*

Nietzsche develops the concept of decadence most extensively in Chapter 6 (*Skirmishes in a War with the Age*). He characterizes it as "not to have one's own interests, not to have one's own will" (006-632), locating the origin of modern moral decadence in what he calls the "movement of decadence in morality" (006-760). The instinct for self-preservation gives way to the instinct for self-destruction, and the "valuing instinct" itself degenerates (006-868).

In Chapter 10 (*The Antichrist — Preface*), Nietzsche explicitly connects decadence to the absence of will to power: "where the will to power is lacking, degeneration sets in" (010-106). This frames decadence as not merely a cultural [[appearance|phenomenon]] but a fundamental biological-psychological condition.

## Key Passages

> The most general formula at the basis of every religion and morality is: "Do this and this, refrain from this and this — and thou wilt be happy! Otherwise..." Every morality, every religion, is this imperative — I call it the great original sin of reason, immortal unreason. (003-097)

> Morality, in so far as it condemns... is a specific error. (006-743)

## Critical Context

Nietzsche's concept of decadence draws on and radicalizes the late 19th-century French discourse of décadence (Baudelaire, Bourget). However, where literary decadents aestheticized decline, Nietzsche provides a philosophical diagnosis that links cultural symptoms ([[jesus|Christ]]ian morality, democratic politics, romantic art) to underlying physiological conditions. His analysis anticipates 20th-century critiques of modernity and mass culture.
