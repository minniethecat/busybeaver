#### I. ETERNAL RECURRENCE

[014-001] 1.

[014-002] The extent of universal energy is limited; it is not “infinite”: we

[014-003] should beware of such excesses in our concepts! Consequently the number

[014-004] of states, changes, combinations, and evolutions of this energy,

[014-005] although it may be enormous and practically incalculable, is at any

[014-006] rate definite and not unlimited. The time, however, in which this

[014-007] universal energy works its changes is infinite—that is to say, energy

[014-008] remains eternally the same and is eternally active:—at this moment an

[014-009] infinity has already elapsed, that is to say, every possible evolution

[014-010] must already have taken place. Consequently the present process of

[014-011] evolution must be a repetition, as was also the one before it, as will

[014-012] also be the one which will follow. And so on forwards and backwards!

[014-013] Inasmuch as the entire state of all forces continually returns,

[014-014] everything has existed an infinite number of times. Whether, apart from

[014-015] this, anything exactly like something that formerly existed has ever

[014-016] appeared, is completely beyond proof. It would seem that each complete

[014-017] state of energy forms all qualities afresh even to the smallest

[014-018] degree, so that two different complete states could have nothing in

[014-019] common.Is it to be supposed that in one and the same complete states

[014-020] two precisely similar things could appear—for instance two leaves?

[014-021] I doubt it: it would take for granted that they had both had an

[014-022] absolutely similar origin, and in that case we should have to assume

[014-023] that right back in infinity two similar things had also existed despite

[014-024] all the changes in the complete states and their creation of new

[014-025] qualities—an impossible assumption.

[014-026] Formerly it was thought that unlimited energy was a necessary corollary

[014-027] to unlimited activity in time, and that this energy could be exhausted

[014-028] by no form of consumption. Now it is thought that energy remains

[014-029] constant and docs not require to be infinite. It is eternally active

[014-030] but it is no longer able eternally to create new forms, it must repeat

[014-031] itself: that is my conclusion.

[014-032] An incalculable number of complete states of energy have existed,

[014-033] but these have not been infinitely different: for if they had been,

[014-034] unlimited energy would have been necessary. The energy of the universe

[014-035] can only have a given number of possible qualities.

[014-036] The endless evolution of new forms is a contradiction, for it would

[014-037] imply eternally increasing energy. But whence would it grow? Whence

[014-038] would it derive its nourishment and its surplus of nourishment?The

[014-039] assumption that the universe is an organism contradicts the very

[014-040] essence of the organic.

[014-041] In what principle and belief is that decisive turning point in

[014-042] philosophical thought best expressed which has come into being thanks

[014-043] to the preponderance of the scientific spirit over the religious and

[014-044] God-creating one? We insist upon the fact that the world as a sum of

[014-045] energy must not be regarded as unlimited—we forbid ourselves the

[014-046] concept infinite energy, because it seems incompatible with the concept

[014-047] energy.

[014-048] An unlimited number of new changes and states on the part of limited

[014-049] energy is a contradiction, however extensive one may imagine it to be,

[014-050] and however economical the changes may be, provided it is infinite.

[014-051] We are therefore forced to conclude: (1) either that the universe

[014-052] began its activity at a given moment of time and will end in a similar

[014-053] fashion,—but the beginning of activity is absurd; if a state of

[014-054] equilibrium had been reached it would have persisted to all eternity;

[014-055] (2) Or there is no such thing as an endless number of changes, but

[014-056] a circle consisting of a definite number of them which continually

[014-057] recurs: activity is eternal, the number of the products and states of

[014-058] energy is limited.

[014-059] If all the possible combinations and relations of forces had not

[014-060] already been exhausted, then an infinity would not yet lie behind

[014-061] us. Now sinceinfinite time must be assumed, no fresh possibility

[014-062] can exist and everything must have appeared already, and moreover an

[014-063] infinite number of times.

[014-064] The present world of forces leads back to a state of greatest

[014-065] simplicity in these forces: it likewise leads forwards to such a

[014-066] state,—cannot and must notbothstates be identical? No incalculable

[014-067] number of states can evolve out of a system of limited forces, that

[014-068] is to say, out of a given quantity of energy which may be precisely

[014-069] measured. Only when we falsely assume that space is unlimited, and that

[014-070] therefore energy gradually becomes dissipated, can the final state be

[014-071] an unproductive and lifeless one.

[014-072] First principles.—The last physical state of energy which we can

[014-073] imagine must necessarily be the first also. The absorption of energy

[014-074] in latent energy must be the cause of the production of the most vital

[014-075] energy. For a highly positive state must follow a negative state Space

[014-076] like matter is a subjective form, time is not. The notion of space

[014-077] first arose from the assumption that space could be empty. But there is

[014-078] no such thing as empty space. Everything is energy.

[014-079] We cannot think of that which moves and that which is moved together,

[014-080] but both these things constitute matter and space. We isolate.

[014-081] 10

[014-082] Concerning the resurrection of the world.—Outof two negatives, when

[014-083] they are forces, a positive arises. (Darkness comes of light opposed to

[014-084] light, cold arises from warmth opposed to warmth, &c., &c.)

[014-085] 11

[014-086] An uncertain state of equilibrium occurs just as seldom in nature as

[014-087] two absolutely equal triangles. Consequently anything like a static

[014-088] state of energy in general is impossible. If stability were possible it

[014-089] would already have been reached.

[014-090] 12

[014-091] Either complete equilibrium must in itself be an impossibility, or

[014-092] the changes of energy introduce themselves in the circular process

[014-093] before that equilibrium which is in itself possible has appeared.—But

[014-094] it would be madness to ascribe a feeling of self-preservation to

[014-095] existence! And the same applies to the conception of a contest of pain

[014-096] and pleasure among atoms.

[014-097] 13

[014-098] Physics supposes that energy may be divided up: but every one of its

[014-099] possibilities must first be adjusted to reality. There can therefore

[014-100] be no question of dividing energy into equal parts; in every one of

[014-101] its states it manifests a certain quality, and qualities cannot be

[014-102] subdivided: hence a state of equilibrium in energy is impossible.

[014-103] 14

[014-104] If energy had ever reached a stage of equilibrium that stage would

[014-105] have persisted: it has thereforenever reached such a stage. The

[014-106] present condition of things contradicts this assumption. If we assume

[014-107] that there has ever been a state absolutely like the present one this

[014-108] assumption is in no wise refuted by the present state. For, among

[014-109] all the endless possibilities, this case must already have occurred,

[014-110] as an infinity is already behind us. If equilibrium were possible it

[014-111] would already have been reached.—And if this momentary state has

[014-112] already existed then that which bore it and the previous one also would

[014-113] likewise have existed and so on backwards,—and from this it follows

[014-114] that it has already existed not only twice but three times,—just as it

[014-115] will exist again not only twice but three times,—in fact an infinite

[014-116] number of times backwards and forwards. That is to say, the whole

[014-117] process of Becoming consists of a repetition of a definite number of

[014-118] precisely similar states.—Clearly the human brain cannot be left to

[014-119] imagine the whole series of possibilities: but in any case, quite apart

[014-120] from our ability to judge or our inability to conceive the whole range

[014-121] of possibilities, the present state at least is a possible one—because

[014-122] it is a real one. We should therefore say: in the event of the number

[014-123] of possibilities not being infinite, and assuming that in the course of

[014-124] unlimited time a limited number of these must appear, all real states

[014-125] must have been preceded by similar states? Because from every given

[014-126] moment a whole infinity is to be calculated backwards? The stability of

[014-127] forces and their equilibrium is a possible alternative: but it has not

[014-128] been reached; consequently the number of possibilities is greater than

[014-129] the number of real states. The fact that nothing similar recurs couldnot be explained by appealing to accident, but only by supposing that a

[014-130] certain intention, that no similar things should recur, were actually

[014-131] inherent in the essence of energy: for, if we grant that the number of

[014-132] cases is enormous, the occurrence of like cases is more probable than

[014-133] absolute disparity.

[014-134] 15

[014-135] Let us think backwards a moment If the world had a goal, this goal

[014-136] must have been reached: if a certain (unintentional) final state

[014-137] existed for the world, this state also would have been reached. If

[014-138] it were in any way capable of a stationary or stable condition, and

[014-139] if in the whole course of its existence only one second of Being, in

[014-140] the strict sense of the word, had been possible, then there could

[014-141] no longer be such a process as evolution, and therefore no thinking

[014-142] and no observing of such a process. If on the other hand the world

[014-143] were something which continually renovated itself, it would then be

[014-144] understood to be something miraculous and free to create itself—in

[014-145] fact something divine. Eternal renovation presupposes that energy

[014-146] voluntarily increases itself, that it not only has the intention, but

[014-147] also the power, to avoid repeating itself or to avoid returning into a

[014-148] previous form, and that every instant it adjusts itself in every one of

[014-149] its movements to prevent such a contingency,—or that it was incapable

[014-150] of returning to a state it had already passed through. That would

[014-151] mean that the whole sum of energy was not constant, any more than its

[014-152] attributes were But a sum of energy which would be inconstant and which

[014-153] would fluctuate is quite unthinkable Let us not indulgeour fancy

[014-154] any longer with unthinkable things in order to fall once more before

[014-155] the concept of a Creator (multiplication out of nothing, reduction

[014-156] out of nothing, absolute arbitrariness and freedom in growth and in

[014-157] qualities):—

[014-158] 16

[014-159] He who does not believe in the circular process of the universe must

[014-160] pin his faith to an arbitrary God—thus my doctrine becomes necessary

[014-161] as opposed to all that has been said hitherto in matters of Theism.

[014-162] 17

[014-163] The hypothesis which I would oppose to that of the eternal circular

[014-164] process:—Would it be just as possible to explain the laws of the

[014-165] mechanical world as exceptions and seemingly as accidents among the

[014-166] things of the universe, as one possibility only among an incalculable

[014-167] number of possibilities? Would it be possible to regard ourselves

[014-168] as accidentally thrust into this corner of the mechanical universal

[014-169] arrangement?—That all chemical philosophy is likewise an exception

[014-170] and an accident in the world’s economy, and finally that organic life

[014-171] is a mere exception and accident in the chemical world? Should we have

[014-172] to assume as the most general form of existence a world which was

[014-173] not yet mechanical, which was outside all mechanical laws (although

[014-174] accessible to them)?—and that as a matter of fact this world would

[014-175] be the most general now and for evermore, so that the origin of the

[014-176] mechanical world would be a lawless game which would ultimately acquire

[014-177] such consistency as the organic laws seem to have nowfrom our point

[014-178] of view? So that all our mechanical laws would be not eternal, but

[014-179] evolved, and would have survived innumerable different mechanical

[014-180] laws, or that they had attained supremacy in isolated corners of the

[014-181] world and not in others?—It would seem that we need caprice, actual

[014-182] lawlessness, and only a capacity for law, a primeval state of stupidity

[014-183] which is not even able to concern itself with mechanics? The origin of

[014-184] qualities presupposes the existence of quantities, and these, for their

[014-185] part, might arise from a thousand kinds of mechanical processes.

[014-186] Is not the existence of some sort of irregularity and incomplete

[014-187] circular form in the world about us, a sufficient refutation of

[014-188] the regular circularity of everything that exists? Whence comes

[014-189] this variety within the circular process? Is not everything far too

[014-190] complicated to have been the outcome of unity? And are not the many

[014-191] chemical laws and likewise the organic species and forms inexplicable

[014-192] as the result of homogeneity? or of duality?—Supposing there were

[014-193] such a thing as a regular contracting energy in all the centres of

[014-194] force in the universe, the question would be, whence could the most

[014-195] insignificant difference spring? For then the whole world would have

[014-196] to be resolved into innumerable completely equal rings and spheres of

[014-197] existence and we should have an incalculable number of exactly equal

[014-198] worlds side by side. Is it necessary for me to assume this? Must I

[014-199] suppose that an eternal sequence of like worlds also involves eternal

[014-200] juxta-position of like worlds? But the multifariousness and disorder

[014-201] in the world which we have knownhitherto contradicts this; no such

[014-202] universal similarity has existed in evolution, for in that case even

[014-203] for our part of the cosmos a regular spherical form must have been

[014-204] formed. Should the production of qualities not be subject to any strict

[014-205] laws? Can it be possible that different things have been derived from

[014-206] “energy”? Arbitrarily? Is the conformity to law which we observe

[014-207] perhaps only a deception? Is it possible that it is not a primeval law?

[014-208] Is it possible that the multifariousness of qualities even in our part

[014-209] of the world is the result of the absolute occurrence of arbitrary

[014-210] characteristics? But that these characteristics no longer appear in our

[014-211] corner of the globe? Or that our corner of existence has adopted a rule

[014-212] which we call cause and effect when all the while it is no such thing

[014-213] (an arbitrary phenomenon become a rule, as for instance oxygen and

[014-214] hydrogen in chemistry)??? Is this rule simply a protracted kind of mood?

[014-215] 18

[014-216] If the universe had been able to become an organism it would have

[014-217] become one already.Asa whole we must try and regard it in the

[014-218] light of a thingasremoteas possiblefrom the organic. I believe

[014-220] evolved and that these appearances only occur in certain corners of

[014-230] 19

[014-231] Inorganic matter, even though in most cases it may once have

[014-232] been organic, can have stored up no experience,—it is always

[014-233] without a pastl If the reverse were the case a repetition would be

[014-236] 20

[014-237] We must guard against ascribing any aspiration or any goal to this

[014-238] circular process: Likewise we must not, from the point of view of our

[014-239] own needs, regard it as either monotonous or foolish, &c. We may grant

[014-240] that the greatest possible irrationality, as also its reverse, may be

[014-241] an essential feature of it: but we must not value it according to this

[014-242] hypothesis. Rationality or irrationality cannot stand as attributes of

[014-243] the universe.—We must not think of the law of this circular process as

[014-244] a thing evolved, by drawing false analogies with the circular motions

[014-245] occurringwithinthe circle. There was no primitive chaos followed

[014-246] gradually by a more harmonious and finally definite circular motion of

[014-247] all forces: On the contrary everything is eternal and unevolved. If

[014-248] there ever was a chaos of forces, then that chaositself was eternal

[014-249] and was repeated at its particular moment of time in the turn of the

[014-250] world wheel. The circular process is not the outcome of evolution, it

[014-251] is a primitive principle like the quantum of energy, and allows of no

[014-252] exception or violation. All Becoming takes place within the circular

[014-253] process and the quantum of energy which constitutes it: therefore we

[014-254] must not apply ephemeral processes like those for instance of heavenly

[014-255] bodies, of the ebb and flow of tides, of day and night, of the seasons,

[014-256] to the drawing of analogies for characterising the eternal circular

[014-257] process.

[014-258] 21

[014-259] The “chaos of the universe,” inasmuch as it excludes any aspiration to

[014-260] a goal, does not oppose the thought of the circular process: the latter

[014-261] is simply an irrational necessity, absolutely free from any formal

[014-262] ethical or æsthetical significance. Arbitrariness in small things as in

[014-263] great is completely lacking here.

[014-264] 22

[014-265] Let us guard against believing that the universe has a tendency to

[014-269] imperfection in the realm of mechanics.

[014-270] Everything has returned: Sirius, and the spider, and thy thoughts at

[014-273] 23

[014-277] before and thus the process goes on. If we grant eternal time we must

[014-285] 25

[014-286] The world of energy suffers no diminution: otherwise with eternal

[014-287] time it would have grown weak and finally have perished altogether.

[014-288] The world of energy suffers no stationary state, otherwise this would

[014-289] already have been reached, and the clock of the universe would be at

[014-290] a standstill. The world of energy does not therefore reach a state of

[014-291] equilibrium; for no instant in its career has it had rest; its energy

[014-292] and its movement have been the same for all time. Whatever state this

[014-293] world could have reached must ere now have been attained, and not

[014-294] only once but an incalculable number of times. This applies to this

[014-295] very moment It has already been here once before, and several times,

[014-296] and will recurin the same way, with all forces distributed as they

[014-297] are to-day: and the same holds good of the moment of time which bore

[014-298] the present and of that which shall be the child of the present.

[014-299] Fellow-man! Your whole life, like a sandglass, will always be reversed

[014-300] and will ever run out again,—a long minute of time will elapse until

[014-301] all those conditions out of which you were evolved return in the wheel

[014-302] of the cosmic process. And then you will find every pain and every

[014-303] pleasure, every friend and every enemy, every hope and every error,

[014-304] every blade of grass and every ray of sunshine once more, and the whole

[014-305] fabric of things that makes up your life. This ring in which you are

[014-306] but a grain will glitter afresh for ever. And in every one of these

[014-307] cycles of human life there will be one hour where for the first time

[014-308] one man, and then many, will perceive the mighty thought of the eternal

[014-309] recurrence of all things:—and for mankind this is always the hour of

[014-310] Noon.
