[018-001]But she, with tears in her eyes, said: "O my beloved, do not on my

[018-002]account engage in any act of violence; whatever might be the result,

[018-003]your own life would, certainly be forfeited. You have continually

[018-004]professed your great love for me; be guided now by my advice. I am

[018-005]ready to follow you wherever you go; let us then fly from this

[018-006]country, and go where we shall be safe from my persecutor."

[018-007]My new acquaintance then turned to me, and said: "You seem to have

[018-008]been a great traveller; tell us in what country we may be most in

[018-009]safety and best able to live."

[018-010]I smiled at this, and answered: "The world is wide, and there are

[018-011]plenty of countries pleasant to live in; but, after all, one's own

[018-012]country is the best; why should you banish yourselves? I think I can

[018-013]contrive some means by which you will be enabled to remain here in

[018-014]safety and comfort. Wait then a while, and if I cannot do this I will

[018-015]tell you where it will be best for you to go."

[018-016]Before we had time to say more, the young girl started up, saying: "I

[018-017]dare not stay a moment longer. I have stopped away from my mistress

[018-018]to see you, and now I hear her coming, and must join her directly. Any

[018-019]one may see the princess at this festival; I hope you will have a good

[018-020]view of her." Saying this to me, she ran off, and we both followed her

[018-021]to the place where the princess was to perform--an open stage which

[018-022]had been erected in the park.

[018-023]Presently she made her appearance, followed by a train of female

[018-024]attendants, and the moment I saw her my heart was drawn towards her. I

[018-025]almost doubted whether she were a goddess or a mortal; but when she

[018-026]began to play, I was even more captivated by her graceful movements

[018-027]than I had been by her beauty.

[018-028]First she made a low obeisance in honour of the goddess; then taking

[018-029]up the bright red ball with her slender fingers, she let it drop as

[018-030]if accidentally, and striking it as it rebounded, caught it on the

[018-031]back of her hand and sent it high into the air; then she made it rise

[018-032]and fall, at first slowly, then faster, and then very rapidly, keeping

[018-033]time to it by graceful movements of the feet. Sometimes it seemed to

[018-034]stand still, sometimes to fly up like a bird; at one time she would

[018-035]strike it alternately with her right hand and left hand; at another

[018-036]send it high into the air, dancing meanwhile to her own singing; then

[018-037]the ball would go quite away, and come back as if of itself. Thus she

[018-038]went on a long time amidst the applause of the surrounding spectators,

[018-039]performing various graceful movements, striking the ball with feet as

[018-040]well as hands, and even making it whirl round and round her so rapidly

[018-041]that she seemed to be enclosed in a fiery red cage; now with one hand

[018-042]holding up her dress or replacing her hair which had fallen down, and

[018-043]keeping the ball in motion with the other; now taking several balls

[018-044]and keeping them all in the air at once.

[018-045]At last the performance was ended; and, after again making a low

[018-046]obeisance in honour of the goddess, she walked slowly round the stage,

[018-047]leaning on the arm of her foster-sister Chandrasena, and followed by

[018-048]her maidens, casting several significant glances at me, and especially

[018-049]giving me one long lingering look as she withdrew.

[018-050]My new friend, Kosadâsa, who had stood near me all the time, invited

[018-051]me to his house, where I was most hospitably entertained.

[018-052]In the evening, Chandrasena, the lady to whom he had introduced me,

[018-053]came to see him. I said to her: "I promised to find some means of

[018-054]freeing you from the importunities of the prince; this is what I have

[018-055]thought of. I have a magic ointment, a small quantity of which applied

[018-056]to your face will make you look like a monkey in the eyes of all who

[018-057]see you. Your persecutor will certainly then be disgusted, and give

[018-058]you no more annoyance."

[018-059]"Truly I am exceedingly obliged to you," she answered, "for such a

[018-060]charming proposal. But whatever I may be in a future birth, I have no

[018-061]inclination to be turned into a monkey now. If you have nothing better

[018-062]than this to propose, we shall not esteem your wisdom very highly.

[018-063]Happily, I have thought of something much better. You have heard that,

[018-064]according to the word of Durgâ, the princess is to be allowed free

[018-065]choice of a husband. You are greatly in love with her, and she is

[018-066]favourably disposed towards you, from your appearance. My mother, of

[018-067]whom she is very fond, will do everything in her power to promote your

[018-068]interests; and no doubt she will choose you. The king and queen will

[018-069]of course give their consent; and the marriage once completed, there

[018-070]will be no further danger, since Bhimadhanwa will be subject to you,

[018-071]and you will be able easily to protect me. Wait, therefore, a few

[018-072]days, and I and my mother will do our best on your behalf. But I must

[018-073]not stay longer; my mistress will be waiting for me."

[018-074]After she was gone, Kosadâsa and I got into conversation about that

[018-075]which so greatly concerned us both; and so much interested were we,

[018-076]that we never thought of going to bed, but sat up talking all the

[018-077]night. In the morning, I went to the park, and stood for some time

[018-078]near the stage on which I had seen the princess; and in imagination

[018-079]saw her there again, in some of those graceful attitudes which she had

[018-080]displayed. While I was thus deep in thought, I was accosted by

[018-081]Bhimadhanwa, who introduced himself to me, appeared very friendly, sat

[018-082]down with me, and, after some conversation, invited me to his house.

[018-083]Having no suspicion of treachery, I accompanied him to the palace,

[018-084]where I was most hospitably entertained. After dinner, not having

[018-085]slept the night before, I lay down, and was soon fast asleep, and

[018-086]dreaming of my beloved princess. Presently, I was suddenly awakened,

[018-087]and found my arms bound with an iron chain, and Bhimadhanwa, with

[018-088]angry countenance, standing near me. "Vile wretch!" he said. "You

[018-089]fancied you could plot in safety; and little thought that all which

[018-090]that girl said was overheard, and brought to me by one of my spies,

[018-091]who heard it through the open window. My silly sister, forsooth, is in

[018-092]love with you! You are to marry her, and make me your subject; and you

[018-093]will order me to give up Chandrasena, that she may marry her lover!

[018-094]You are much mistaken. I am not so easily managed as that. We shall

[018-095]soon see how all your fine projects will end." Then calling two strong

[018-096]men, his servants, at his command they lifted me up, carried me down

[018-097]to the sea, and threw me in as I was.

[018-098]Notwithstanding the chain which confined my arms, I managed to keep

[018-099]afloat, till by a lucky chance I fell in with a piece of wood, and by

[018-100]throwing myself across it, managed to hold on, and was carried out to

[018-101]sea. After floating all night, in the morning I was seen from a ship

[018-102]sailing that way, and taken on board.

[018-103]The captain, however, who was a foreigner, had not much compassion on

[018-104]me; and only thought, as I was young and strong, how much he could get

[018-105]by selling me as a slave; and did not even release my hands. I had not

[018-106]been long on board, however, when the ship was attacked by pirates,

[018-107]who surrounded it with their boats, and poured in a shower of arrows

[018-108]and other missiles.

[018-109]Seeing that the crew of the merchant-ship were being defeated, I

[018-110]called out to the captain: "Take off my chain; set me free; and I will

[018-111]soon drive away the enemy."

[018-112]He did as I asked; and furnished me with a good bow and arrows, which

[018-113]I used so effectually, that a large number of the enemy were killed

[018-114]or wounded; and the boats began to draw off.

[018-115]Meanwhile, our ship had drifted close to the pirates' galley. I leapt

[018-116]on board, and most of the crew being disabled, took prisoner the

[018-117]captain, who turned out to be Bhimadhanwa, the very man who had so

[018-118]treacherously ill-used me. He was utterly astonished at seeing, me;

[018-119]and hung down his head ashamed, unable to answer a word, when I said

[018-120]to him: "Where are all your threats and boastings? You are now as

[018-121]completely in my power as I was in yours."

[018-122]Then the sailors, shouting for joy at the victory, bound him with the

[018-123]chain with which I had been confined; and after taking possession of

[018-124]the pirate ship, we continued the voyage; but being driven out of our

[018-125]course by a contrary wind, landed on an uninhabited island, to get

[018-126]water and wild fruits, and attend to the wounded.

[018-127]The merchant-captain and crew, delighted at my bravery, and the timely

[018-128]assistance I had rendered them, treated me with the greatest respect.

[018-129]While they were engaged, I walked about to explore the island; and

[018-130]came to a large quantity of stones which had fallen from a high rock.

[018-131]These I crossed over, and going round to the other side, found a

[018-132]gentle slope, covered with trees and flowers. Walking slowly among

[018-133]them, admiring the beautiful scenery and enjoying the cool shade, I

[018-134]arrived, almost imperceptibly and without fatigue, at the summit,

[018-135]where I found a small lake, surrounded with ruby-coloured, variegated

[018-136]rocks, and partly covered with bright lotuses. In this I bathed, and

[018-137]pulled up some of the lotus-plants, the young shoots of which were

[018-138]unusually sweet and good.

[018-139]As I came out of the water, carrying a large root on my shoulder, I

[018-140]saw standing on the bank a terrible Rakshas in human form, who called

[018-141]out, in an angry tone "Who are you? Where do you come from? What are

[018-142]you doing here, destroying my flowers?"

[018-143]Without showing any sign of fear, I walked boldly up to him, and said:

[018-144]"I am a brahman, who has just escaped many dangers. I was

[018-145]treacherously thrown into the sea, rescued by a merchant-ship, then

[018-146]attacked by pirates; and now, after conquering them, we have put into

[018-147]this island for water. I have much enjoyed my bathe, and wish you good

[018-148]morning."

[018-149]"Stop!" said he. "You will not get off so easily. You seem a bold

[018-150]fellow, however, and I will give you a chance for life. I shall ask

[018-151]you four questions. If you can answer them, you are free; if not, I

[018-152]shall devour you immediately."

[018-153]"Very good," I answered; "I am ready to hear them." Then he began:

[018-154]"What is cruel?"

[018-155]"A wicked woman's heart."

[018-156]"What is most to the advantage of a householder?"

[018-157]"Good qualities in a wife."

[018-158]"What is love?"

[018-159]"Imagination."

[018-160]"What best accomplishes difficult things?"

[018-161]"Cunning. Dhumini, Gomini, Ratnavati, and Nitambavati," I added, "are

[018-162]examples of what I have said."

[018-163]"Tell me," said he, "who they were, and how they prove the truth of

[018-164]your answers?"

[018-165]"Certainly," I replied; "you shall judge for yourself.

[018-166]"There were formerly in the country of Trigarta three brothers, all

[018-167]wealthy, having several wives, many servants and slaves, and numerous

[018-168]flocks and herds. In their time it happened that there was a great

[018-169]drought; no rain fell for several years; the streams and fountains

[018-170]ceased to flow; the pools and lakes were turned to mud, the beds of

[018-171]rivers almost dry, plants burned up, trees withered; all mirth and

[018-172]festivity were at an end; bands of thieves roamed about; the dead lay

[018-173]unburied or unburnt, and their bodies were scattered over the fields.

[018-174]At last the famine was so great that men began to devour each other.

[018-175]The three brothers, from their great wealth, were able to hold out a

[018-176]long time; but when their stores of corn and rice were all consumed,

[018-177]and their cattle all slaughtered, they, like the rest, were driven to

[018-178]cannibalism. First they killed and ate their slaves; then, even their

[018-179]wives and children, till all were gone but themselves and their three

[018-180]favourite wives. The famine still continuing, they were driven to eat

[018-181]them also, and drew lots which should be killed first. The lot fell on

[018-182]Dhumini, the wife of the youngest brother, who, unable to bear the

[018-183]thought of devouring her, escaped with her in the night. After walking

[018-184]a long way, till they were quite exhausted, they came to a large

[018-185]forest, where they found a well of water, and many fruits and roots,

[018-186]besides deer and other animals, on which they were able to live

[018-187]without difficulty; and they built a hut there.
