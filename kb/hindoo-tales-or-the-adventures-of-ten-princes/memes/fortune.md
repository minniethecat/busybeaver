# Fortune

## Definition

Fortune (Bhāgya or Sampad) represents the condition of material prosperity or adversity that shapes a character's circumstances. Unlike fate, which is the abstract power governing destiny, fortune is the tangible state of one's affairs — wealth, status, and well-being, or their absence. The tales portray fortune as inherently unstable: it can be lost through sin, misfortune, or the malice of enemies, but it can also be restored through perseverance, divine aid, and magical intervention. A broken fortune may be repaired, the text reminds us — unlike a life cut short.

## In the Narrative

Fortune and its loss are central drivers of the plot. The misfortunes that befall characters are attributed to sins committed in former existences, framing adversity within a karmic worldview (001-034). The restoration of fortune is a recurring narrative arc: a divine voice promises to restore the princes' fortunes by subduing their enemies (002-024). Personal encounters are marked by declarations of good fortune — [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] exclaims at his good fortune in meeting a companion again (003-174).

The most detailed treatment of fortune appears in Chapters 8 and 9. A character whose misfortunes are well known recounts his story, including the inheritance of a large fortune that was subsequently lost (008-153, 008-157). The loss of both fortune and wife drives a character into despair (009-134). Yet the narrative offers consolation: a broken fortune may be repaired, and the magic leather bag appears as fortune's remedy — an inexhaustible source of wealth that restores what was lost (009-142, 009-144).

Fortune also has a personal dimension. Through ill-fortune, a character is driven into the forest (004-158), while another has the good fortune to see his beloved alone (005-044). These varied uses show fortune operating at every scale, from cosmic karmic retribution to the intimate meeting of lovers.
