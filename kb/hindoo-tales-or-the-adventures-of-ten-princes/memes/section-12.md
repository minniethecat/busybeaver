[012-001]Râjavâhana, having heard this story, said "You have indeed shown

[012-002]wonderful ingenuity and courage;" then he turned to Upahâravarma, and

[012-003]said: "It is now your turn;" and he, having made due salutation, thus

[012-004]began:--

[012-005]* * * * *

[012-006]ADVENTURES OF UPAHÂRAVARMA.

[012-007]While wandering about like the others, I cams one day into the country

[012-008]of Videha. Before entering into Mithila, the capital, I stopped to

[012-009]rest at a small temple, and found there an old woman, who gave me

[012-010]water for my feet.

[012-011]Observing that she looked at me very hard, and that tears came into

[012-012]her eyes, I asked her: "O, mother, what is the cause of your grief?"

[012-013]"You bring to my mind," she answered, the remembrance of my lost

[012-014]foster-child, who, if he lives, is just about your age. But I will

[012-015]tell you how he was lost.

[012-016]"Prahâravarma was formerly king of this country. His queen was a very

[012-017]dear friend of Vasumati; wife of Râjahansa, King of Magadha, and he

[012-018]went with her and his twin sons to visit that king. How he was

[012-019]conquered and driven from his dominions by the King of Mâlwa you have

[012-020]doubtless heard. It was shortly before that invasion that the visit

[012-021]was made. In the battle which was fought, Prahâravarma assisted his

[012-022]friend, and was taken prisoner, but was subsequently liberated.

[012-023]"When returning to his own kingdom, he heard that a rebellion had

[012-024]broken out, headed by his brother's son, Vikatavarma. He therefore

[012-025]turned aside through a forest road, in the direction of Suhma, hoping

[012-026]to obtain assistance from his sister's son, the king of that country.

[012-027]On the march, he was attacked and plundered by Bheels; and I, having

[012-028]charge of one of his children, was separated from the party, and left

[012-029]behind in the forest.

[012-030]"There I was attacked, by a tiger, and dropped the child. The tiger

[012-031]was killed by an arrow; but I fainted away, and when I recovered, the

[012-032]child was gone, taken away, I suppose, by the Bheels. Having been

[012-033]found and taken care of by a compassionate cowherd, I stayed at his

[012-034]cottage till my wounds were healed.

[012-035]"Longing to get back to my friends, and to hear some tidings of my

[012-036]mistress, I was surprised one day by the appearance of my daughter,

[012-037]who had been, with me, in charge of the other child.

[012-038]"After mutual congratulations and embraces, she told me her story as

[012-039]follows: 'After we were parted, I was wounded by the robbers, lost

[012-040]the child, and was found wandering about by one of the foresters, who

[012-041]took care of me, and afterwards wished to make me his wife. I was too

[012-042]much disgusted with him and his way of life to consent; and, after

[012-043]many threats, he would at last have killed me, but for the opportune

[012-044]arrival of a young man who happened to be passing, and rescued me from

[012-045]his hands. That young man has since become my husband. We have been

[012-046]searching for you, and have now happily found you.'

[012-047]"I asked who the man was. He answered: 'I am a servant of the King of

[012-048]Mithila, to whom I am now going.' Then we all three went to Mithila,

[012-049]and told the king and queen the sad news of the loss of their

[012-050]children.

[012-051]"The war was still going on, and at last the king was overcome and

[012-052]imprisoned, together with his queen, by his wicked nephew.

[012-053]"Since then I have been living as a mendicant. My daughter, whose

[012-054]husband was killed in the war, being destitute like myself, has

[012-055]entered the service of Kalpasundari, queen of the usurper. Ah! if

[012-056]those princes had lived, they would have rescued their father from

[012-057]such degradation."

[012-058]She began then to weep and lament; but I comforted her, and said: "Do

[012-059]you not remember speaking to a certain muni, and telling him of the

[012-060]loss of the child? That boy was found by him. I am he, and I will

[012-061]contrive some means for killing that wicked usurper, and setting my

[012-062]parents free. No one can recognise me here, not even my own mother,

[012-063]were she to see me; therefore I shall be able at my leisure to

[012-064]consider what is best to be done."

[012-065]Exceedingly delighted at hearing this, she kissed me again and again,

[012-066]and said, with tears of joy: "O, darling! a glorious fortune is before

[012-067]you. Now you are here, all will be well; you will soon lift up your

[012-068]parents from the sea of sorrow which has engulfed them. Happy is Queen

[012-069]Priyamvada in having such a son!"

[012-070]Then she gave me such food as she had, and I stayed with her, and

[012-071]passed the night in that temple.

[012-072]As I lay awake, I turned over in my mind every plan that suggested

[012-073]itself to me for the accomplishment of my purpose. Knowing how

[012-074]ready-witted women are in general, and their fondness for tricks and

[012-075]intrigues, it occurred to me that my foster-sister, from her position

[012-076]near the queen, might be able to give me material assistance.

[012-077]In the morning, after worshipping the gods, I began to question the

[012-078]old woman as to her knowledge of the interior of the palace, and asked

[012-079]whether she had frequent opportunities of seeing her daughter.

[012-080]Scarcely had she begun to answer my questions when I saw some one

[012-081]coming towards us, and she exclaimed: "O, Pushkarika, behold our

[012-082]master's son; that dear child whom I so carelessly lost in the forest

[012-083]was found and preserved, and is now restored to us."

[012-084]Great was the daughter's delight at seeing me; and, when her agitation

[012-085]had subsided, her mother said to her: "I was just beginning to tell my

[012-086]dear son something of the arrangement of the palace, and the habits of

[012-087]the inmates; but you can give him the required information much better

[012-088]than I can."

[012-089]In answer to this she told me all the arrangements of the palace, and

[012-090]added: "The Queen Kalpasundari, the daughter of the sovereign of

[012-091]Kumâra, is exceedingly beautiful and accomplished. She despises her

[012-092]husband, who is exceedingly ugly; but though unkindly treated, and

[012-093]neglected, she has hitherto been faithful to him."

[012-094]Hearing this, I said to her: "Whenever you have an opportunity, dwell

[012-095]on the king's licentiousness; find out, if possible, his scandalous

[012-096]amours; make much of them; tell her how other women have behaved in

[012-097]similar circumstances; in short, do everything to stir up her

[012-098]indignation and jealousy against him; and, as soon as possible, let me

[012-099]know what she says. You may help me greatly in this affair; therefore

[012-100]be diligent and observant, and be as much as possible with your

[012-101]mistress."

[012-102]Then I said to the old woman: "You must also play your part. You can

[012-103]be introduced to the queen as a woman skilled in charms and

[012-104]fortune-telling. When you get her to listen to you, make the most of

[012-105]the opportunity, and second your daughter's endeavours."

[012-106]They both promised to do their utmost. After they were gone I took a

[012-107]small house, close to the wall of the royal gardens, and waited

[012-108]patiently for the result.

[012-109]After some days the old woman came to me, and said: "Darling, we have

[012-110]done exactly as you wished. The queen has taken a great fancy to me,

[012-111]is very indignant with her husband, and thinks herself greatly to be

[012-112]pitied. What is now to be done?"

[012-113]I then painted a portrait of myself, and said: "Show this to the

[012-114]queen; she will no doubt admire it, and say: 'Is this a portrait or a

[012-115]fancy picture?' Then do you answer: 'Suppose it should be a portrait

[012-116]of some living person; what then?' And whatever she says in reply let

[012-117]me know as soon as possible."

[012-118]The next day she came to me again, and said: "When I showed your

[012-119]portrait to the queen, she gazed at it a long time, and seemed lost in

[012-120]admiration; then she exclaimed, 'Who can have painted this? Is it

[012-121]possible that such a handsome man can exist in the world? Surely there

[012-122]is no one here like this!' I answered, 'O lady, your admiration is

[012-123]quite natural, such a handsome man is very rarely to be found, but

[012-124]still there might be such a one; and if this should be really the

[012-125]portrait of a young man, longing to see you--not only thus handsome,

[012-126]but of good birth, very learned, accomplished, and good-tempered

[012-127]--what would you say then?' 'What would I say? I say, that if he will

[012-128]be mine, all that I can give him in return, myself, my heart, my body,

[012-129]my life, will be all too little. But surely you are only deceiving me;

[012-130]there never can be such a charming person as this picture represents.'

[012-131]"In answer to this, I said: 'I am not deceiving you. There is really

[012-132]such a person, a young prince, who is staying here in disguise; he saw

[012-133]you when you were walking in the public park, at the feast of Spring,

[012-134]and immediately became a mark for the arrows of Kâma. Moved by his

[012-135]entreaties, and seeing how suited you are to each other, I have

[012-136]ventured to take this means of making his passion known to you. If you

[012-137]will but consent to see him, however difficult access to you may be,

[012-138]his courage, prudence, and ingenuity are so great, that he will

[012-139]certainly effect it; only say what your pleasure is.' Then, finding

[012-140]her quite disposed to see you, I told her your real name and birth.

[012-141]After reflecting some time, she said, 'Mother, I will not conceal from

[012-142]you a circumstance which his name brings to my memory. My father was a

[012-143]great friend of the deposed king, and their queens were very much

[012-144]attached to each other. It was settled between them, that if the one

[012-145]had a son, and the other a daughter, the two children should be

[012-146]engaged for marriage; but when the Queen Priyamvada had lost her sons,

[012-147]my father gave me in marriage to Vikatavarma. This young prince was

[012-148]really destined to be my husband, and I ought to have had him, instead

[012-149]of that ugly wretch, who is stupid, ignorant of all the arts of

[012-150]pleasing, brutal, rebellious, cruel, boastful, false, and, above all,

[012-151]most insulting in his behaviour to me; only yesterday he ill-treated

[012-152]my favourite attendant, Pushkarika, and gathered flowers from a plant

[012-153]which I had especially cherished, to give to one of his paramours, a

[012-154]low vulgar woman, who is trying to put herself on an equality with me.

[012-155]He is in every way unsuited to me, and my misery is so great, that I

[012-156]am ready to catch at any means of escape from it. It was wretched

[012-157]enough while I thought on no one else, but now that I have heard of

[012-158]this charming young man, and seen his portrait, I will endure it no

[012-159]longer, whatever the consequences may be. Therefore, let him come

[012-160]to-morrow evening to the Madhavi bower in the garden. I am impatient

[012-161]to see him; even the hearing of him has filled my heart with love.'"

[012-162]When the old nurse had given me this account, I determined to risk the

[012-163]adventure, and obtained from her a minute description of the garden,

[012-164]the direction of the road and paths, the exact situation of the

[012-165]summerhouse where I was to meet the queen, and where the guards were

[012-166]stationed.

[012-167]Having carefully impressed all these details on my memory, I waited

[012-168]impatiently for the following night, and lay down to rest. As I lay I

[012-169]thought on the difficulty of the enterprise, of the sin of seducing

[012-170]the wife of another, and of what Râjavâhana and my other friends would

[012-171]say to such conduct. On the other hand, I seemed to be justified by

[012-172]the object I had in view; the liberation of my parents.

[012-173]Perplexed with these conflicting thoughts I fell asleep, and dreamed

[012-174]that Vishnu appeared to me, and said: "Go on boldly, without

[012-175]hesitation; what you are about to do, though it may seem sinful, is

[012-176]approved of by me." Encouraged by this vision, I rose in the morning,

[012-177]fully confirmed in my purpose. The tedious day came at last to an end,

[012-178]and darkness set in.
