[020-001]"After reflecting for some time, she said Though my husband so

[020-002]neglects me, I know that he is very fond of women in general, and

[020-003]ready to be captivated by any one, especially respectable woman who

[020-004]will give him a little encouragement. Acting on this propensity, I

[020-005]think, with your help, that something may be done. There is a young

[020-006]lady, a neighbour, the daughter of a very rich man, in great favour

[020-007]with the Rajah; she is a friend of mine, and is very like me. As my

[020-008]husband hardly knows her by sight, and scarcely ever sees me, it might

[020-009]be possible to pass myself off for her. Do you, therefore, go to him

[020-010]and say that that young lady is in love with him, and that you will

[020-011]introduce him to her, only he must not give a hint that you have told

[020-012]him anything. Meanwhile I will arrange with my friend, and will be

[020-013]walking in her father's garden some evening, when you can bring him

[020-014]in.' The old woman was delighted with this contrivance, and promised

[020-015]to perform her part. She went, therefore, soon afterwards with a

[020-016]pretended message of love from the merchant's daughter to Balabhadra,

[020-017]who was delighted at having attracted the attention of such a charming

[020-018]young lady, and took care to be at the appointed time in the garden,

[020-019]where he saw his neglected wife playing at ball. As if by accident,

[020-020]she threw the ball towards him, and the old woman said: This is an

[020-021]invitation; pick up the ball, and take it to her with a pretty speech,

[020-022]and you will get acquainted with her.' In this way an intimacy began,

[020-023]and he often met his wife in the same place in the evening without in

[020-024]the least suspecting the deception. At last she gave him a hint that

[020-025]she was ready to run away with him. Madly in love, he eagerly caught

[020-026]at the proposal, and one night, having collected what money he could

[020-027]carry, he eloped with her, saying nothing to any of his friends. They

[020-028]were much astonished by his sudden disappearance; but when they found

[020-029]that Ratnavati was gone also, they readily believed the story told by

[020-030]the old woman, that he had fallen in love with his own wife; but was

[020-031]ashamed to acknowledge this after having so long neglected her, and

[020-032]was therefore gone to live in another place, where he was not known.

[020-033]Believing this story, her relations and his thought it best to take no

[020-034]steps in the matter, and abstained from making inquiry after him.

[020-035]"Meanwhile Balabhadra went to a town at some distance, and there by

[020-036]his skill and energy, though beginning with a small capital, amassed

[020-037]in a few years a considerable fortune, and was much respected in the

[020-038]place.

[020-039]"When Ratnavati eloped under another name, she engaged a woman to

[020-040]accompany her as a servant; and this woman one day having committed

[020-041]some fault, was beaten by her master, who scolded her and told her she

[020-042]was lazy, thievish, and impudent. Smarting under the punishment, she

[020-043]determined to be revenged, and going to the magistrate told him: 'This

[020-044]man, who seems to you so respectable, is a wicked wretch who has

[020-045]abandoned his own wife, and run away in the night with the daughter of

[020-046]one of his neighbours, with whom he is now living.'

[020-047]"The magistrate having heard this, and being very covetous, thought:

[020-048]'If this man is convicted, his property will be confiscated, and I

[020-049]shall get a share of it.' He therefore began to take proceedings

[020-050]against Balabhadra, who was greatly alarmed. But his wife said to him,

[020-051]'Do not be frightened; put a good face on the matter, and say: "This

[020-052]is not Kanakavati, the daughter of Niddhipatidatta; this is my own

[020-053]lawful wife, the daughter of Grihagupta, who lives at Valabhi. She was

[020-054]married to me with the proper ceremony and with the full consent of

[020-055]her parents. This woman's accusation is altogether false; but if you

[020-056]will not believe my assertion, send to Valabhi, to my wife's father,

[020-057]and hear what he will say--or send to the town where I formerly lived,

[020-058]and make inquiries there."'

[020-059]"This was done, he was admitted to bail, and a letter was written to

[020-060]the father of Ratnavati, who answered it in person, and declared that

[020-061]the lady in question was really his daughter. Thus the matter was

[020-062]settled; but the husband, thinking that the old man was deceived by

[020-063]the likeness, held to his former belief, and continued to live happily

[020-064]with his wife, without ever discovering the delusion. Therefore I say

[020-065]that love is only imagination."

[020-066]The Rakshas, though appearing to be satisfied with these stories,

[020-067]required me to relate that of Nitambavati, which I proceeded to do.

[020-068]"In a city called Madhura, there dwelt a man named Kalahakantaka, of

[020-069]great strength and vigour, ready at any time to take up the quarrel of

[020-070]a friend, famed for deeds of violence, and devoted to pleasures and

[020-071]amusements.

[020-072]"One day he saw a picture exhibited by a painter, a new-comer, and

[020-073]stopped to look at it. It was the portrait of a lady so beautiful

[020-074]that he fell in love with her at once. Desirous of finding out whom it

[020-075]represented, he praised the picture exceedingly, and having put the

[020-076]artist in good humour, got him to say who the lady was. 'Her name,'

[020-077]said he, 'is Nitambavati; she is the wife of a merchant living at

[020-078]Avanti or Oujein, and I was so struck by her beauty that I sought and

[020-079]obtained permission to paint her portrait.'

[020-080]"On hearing this, Kalahakantaka, taking another name, went to Oujein;

[020-081]and there, having disguised himself as a mendicant, got admission to

[020-082]the merchant's house, saw the lady, whose beauty exceeded even his

[020-083]expectation, and was confirmed in his wicked purpose.

[020-084]"At this time a guardian or watchman was wanted for the public

[020-085]cemetery, and he applied for and obtained the office.

[020-086]"With the clothes which he took from the bodies brought to be burnt

[020-087]there, he bribed an old woman to take a message from him. She went to

[020-088]Nitambavati, and said: 'A very handsome young man is much in love with

[020-089]you--pray let him see you if only for once.' On receiving this

[020-090]message, the merchant's wife was very indignant, and sent the old

[020-091]woman away with angry words. Kalahakantaka, however, was not

[020-092]discouraged, and said to his messenger: 'Go again, and say to the

[020-093]lady: "Do you imagine that a person like me devoted to religious

[020-094]meditation, who have passed so many years in pilgrimages to holy

[020-095]places, would wish to lead you into sin? Far from it. I had heard that

[020-096]you were childless, and wishing for children, and I know of means

[020-097]through which your wish may be accomplished; but I thought it right to

[020-098]find out first whether you were worthy of such a service, and now

[020-099]that I have ascertained you to be virtuous and true to your husband, I

[020-100]will gladly assist you."'

[020-101]"With this story the old cheat went again to the lady, who, believing

[020-102]her to be sincere, gladly accepted the offer, and she went on to say:

[020-103]'The reason of your being childless is that a spell has been laid upon

[020-104]your husband, which can only be removed by the means which I will

[020-105]indicate to you. You must go at night to a clump of trees in the park.

[020-106]I will come to you there, and will bring with me a man skilled in

[020-107]incantations. You have only to stand for a moment, putting your foot

[020-108]into his hand while he utters certain charms, then go home, and, as if

[020-109]in play, strike your husband on the breast. This will dissolve the

[020-110]spell, and by-and-by you will have children.' Anxious to have the

[020-111]spell removed from her husband, Nitambavati consented to this, and

[020-112]went at night to the appointed place. There she found Kalahakantaka

[020-113]waiting, and as the old woman had directed, put her foot into his hand

[020-114]while he knelt before her.

[020-115]"No sooner had he got hold of it than he took off her anklet, and

[020-116]slipping his hand up her leg, inflicted a slight wound above the knee,

[020-117]and ran away.

[020-118]"The poor lady, dreadfully frightened, blaming herself, and enraged

[020-119]with the old woman, who had so cruelly deceived her, got home as well

[020-120]as she could, washed and bound up the cut, and kept her bed for

[020-121]several days, having taken off the other anklet, that the loss might

[020-122]not be observed.

[020-123]"Meanwhile the rascal took the anklet he had stolen to the husband,

[020-124]saying: 'I wish to dispose of this, will you buy it?'

[020-125]"Recognising the ornament as having been his wife's, he asked: 'Where

[020-126]did you get this?'

[020-127]"The man answered: 'I will not tell you now, but if you are not

[020-128]satisfied that it is honestly mine, take me before the magistrates,

[020-129]and I will then declare how I came by it.'

[020-130]"Upon this the merchant went to his wife and said: 'Let me see your

[020-131]anklets.'

[020-132]"With some confusion and alarm, she answered: 'I have only one of

[020-133]them, the other being, as I suppose, loosely fastened, dropped off a

[020-134]few days ago when I was walking in the evening in the garden, and I

[020-135]have not been able to find it.'

[020-136]"Dissatisfied with this answer, the husband went before the

[020-137]magistrates with the man who had offered the anklet for sale, and he

[020-138]being there questioned, said: 'You know I was appointed not long ago

[020-139]to the care of the public cemetery, and as people come sometimes after

[020-140]dark to steal the clothes, or to lay a dead body on a pile prepared

[020-141]for another, and so cheat me of my fees, I have lately kept watch

[020-142]there at night.'

[020-143]"'A short time ago I saw a woman in a dark dress dragging away part of

[020-144]a half-burnt body, and ran to seize her. In the struggle her anklet

[020-145]came off, and I gave her a slight wound on the leg, but she got away,

[020-146]and I could not overtake her; this is how the ornament came into my

[020-147]possession. I leave it to you to say whether I have done wrong or no.'

[020-148]"Then the magistrates and citizens who were assembled were

[020-149]unanimously of opinion that the woman was a Sâkini.[10]

[020-150]"She was therefore divorced from her husband, and condemned to be tied

[020-151]to a stake in the cemetery, and left there.

[020-152]"In this state she was found by Kalahakantaka, who cut the cords which

[020-153]fastened her, and, falling at her feet, confessed all that he had

[020-154]done, alleging his great love for her as an excuse for his cruel

[020-155]conduct: 'And now,' said he, 'consent to be my wife, and I will carry

[020-156]you away to my own home in a distant country, where you will not be

[020-157]known. I will do everything in my power to make your life happy, and

[020-158]atone for the suffering which I have caused you.'

[020-159]"For a long time the unhappy lady refused; but at last, overcome by

[020-160]his earnest entreaties, and feeling how unjustly she had been

[020-161]disgraced and ill-treated, she consented to accompany him. Thus, by

[020-162]cunning, he gained his end, which he could not have accomplished by

[020-163]any other means. Therefore I say cunning best accomplishes difficult

[020-164]things."

[020-165]Having heard these stories, the Rakshas was much pleased, and offered

[020-166]me his assistance if I should require it. At that moment several

[020-167]pearls fell close beside us. Looking up to see whence they came, I

[020-168]perceived a Rakshas flying through the air, carrying a woman who was

[020-169]struggling with him.

[020-170]"Shall that monster carry off the lady before our eyes? O that I could

[020-171]fly to rescue her!"

[020-172]As I exclaimed thus, my new ally, without waiting to be entreated,

[020-173]sprang into the air, and calling out "Stop! stop! wicked wretch!"

[020-174]attacked and dragged down the other Rakshas. He, in defending himself,

[020-175]when only a short distance from the ground, let the lady fall, and I

[020-176]caught her with outstretched arms in such a manner that, though much

[020-177]shaken and alarmed, she was not seriously injured. I held her for a

[020-178]moment insensible in my arms, while I gazed at the combatants. Their

[020-179]flight was of short duration, for they attacked each other so

[020-180]furiously that both were killed.
