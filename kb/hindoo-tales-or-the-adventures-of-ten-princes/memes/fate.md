# Fate

## Definition

Fate (Daiva or Vidhi) is the unseen power that governs the course of human events, determining outcomes beyond mortal control. In the philosophical outlook of the tales, fate operates alongside human effort — perseverance and the will of fate together shape destiny (002-009). Fate is personified as an all-powerful agent against which no human resistance can prevail, yet the narrative also acknowledges the role of courage and action in responding to fate's decrees.

## In the Narrative

The power of fate is declared from the earliest chapters. Chapter 2 opens with a reflection on the instability of fortune: prosperity, like a wave, rises and falls by the power of fate (002-004). Yet fate does not operate in isolation — perseverance combined with the will of fate is what restores the characters to their rightful station (002-009).

Fate is often invoked to explain suffering and reversal. A prince is said to have well deserved the fate he met with (005-124), and a character bemoans his hard fate upon his couch (006-097). In Chapter 9, fate is declared an irresistible force: a character was evidently fated to die, and none can prevail over fate — lament is useless against its decree (009-076, 009-077).

The tension between fate and human agency runs through the narrative. Even when no suspicion falls upon a character, fate is all-powerful and intervenes regardless of innocence (010-113). Yet Chapter 21 offers a counterpoint: while the ways of fate are acknowledged, courage too is excellent, suggesting that the proper response to fate is not passive resignation but active virtue (021-039). In Chapter 15, a character greets a sudden turn of events with the exclamation that it is indeed the act of fate, and responds by immediately granting a boon — accepting fate's hand while acting decisively within it (015-073).
