# Knowledge

## Definition

Knowledge (Jñāna) in the world of the tales encompasses both mundane learning and supernatural cognition. On the human plane, it denotes scholarly attainment, wisdom acquired through study, and practical understanding of the world. On the divine or supernatural plane, it refers to the ability to perceive events beyond ordinary temporal bounds — knowledge of the past and future that is granted as a boon or attained through spiritual practice. The tales treat knowledge as a virtue essential to kingship: a king without knowledge is considered insufficient for rule.

## In the Narrative

Knowledge appears as a marker of distinction and admiration. In Chapter 7, a character is admired for his knowledge, drawing others to him (007-008). Chapter 8 presents knowledge in its most elevated form — supernatural cognition of past and future events, a gift that provokes wonder in those who encounter it (008-009). The same chapter speaks of wonderful knowledge as something sought after, with characters asking where such insight can be found (008-016).

The political dimension of knowledge emerges in Chapter 22 and 23. A character is described as possessed of the most marvellous knowledge, setting him apart from ordinary men (022-015). The text explicitly states that knowledge superior to that of others is essential for a king, and that one void of such knowledge is insufficient to rule — a direct statement of the political philosophy underpinning the tales (023-013, 023-015).

> "supernatural knowledge of past and future events." (008-009)

> "…sessed of the most marvellous knowledge. There is" (022-015)

> "…ient for a king; void of such knowledge, he is" (023-015)
