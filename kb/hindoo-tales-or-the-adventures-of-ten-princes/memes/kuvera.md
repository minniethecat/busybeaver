# Kuvera

Kuvera is the God of Wealth in the *Daśakumāracarita*, dwelling on the
mountain of [[alaka|Alaka]] with his attendants, the Yakshas. His divine command
directly intervenes in the mortal world to guide the fates of key characters.

## Biography

Kuvera resides on the mountain [[alaka|Alaka]], accompanied by the Yakshas — a class
of demigods and fairies who serve as his attendants and carry out his will
in the mortal realm.
(026-039) (026-164)

> "A mountain inhabited by Kuvera and the Yakshas."
> (026-039)

The character index describes Kuvera as the God of Wealth, whose attendants
were the Yakshas, establishing his divine status in the cosmological
hierarchy of the narrative.
(026-099)

When a fairy brought a child to the mortal court, she declared that it was
done by the command of Kuvera. The child was intended to enter the service
of the prince, demonstrating Kuvera's active role in shaping mortal affairs.
(002-143)

> "By command of Kuvera, I have brought this my child to you, that he may
> enter the service of your son."
> (002-143)

Kuvera's court also provided refuge to mortals. A Yaksha servant carried a
rescued infant to the god, who summoned the servant and questioned her about
the circumstances of the child's rescue.
(014-105)

> "He took it to our master, the god Kuvera, who sent for me, and asked,
> 'What induced you to bring this child?'"
> (014-105)

Though Kuvera showed compassion by permitting the infant to be saved, he
would not allow the Yaksha to keep the boy permanently. Instead, he ordered
the child to be delivered to the mortal world, where he belonged.
(014-112) (014-114)

Kuvera later oversaw the re[[marriage-in-the-tales|union]] between the child, now grown, and his foster
mother [[taravali|Târâvali]]. The narrative notes that [[taravali|Târâvali]], having received the boy
back from Kuvera, delayed in bringing him to his family — a delay attributed
to the natural hesitation of a foster mother rather than divine decree.
(015-089)

> "Târâvali, who, when she had received you again from Kuvera, did not
> bring you at once to me."
> (015-089)

The [[vasumati|Queen Vasumati]] was also visited by supernatural intervention attributed
to Kuvera's domain — a fairy appeared to her one night to deliver a child
by the god's order.
(016-082) (016-084)

## Role in the Narrative

Kuvera functions as a divine orchestrator behind several key events in the
mortal realm. Unlike gods who remain distant, Kuvera actively directs his
Yaksha servants to rescue, transport, and deliver mortal children who will
grow up to become companions of the princes.

His role is that of a protector and guardian of children destined for
greatness. When Yakshas find infants in danger, they bring them to Kuvera,
who interrogates the circumstances and then decrees their fate — always
ensuring they are placed where they can fulfill their destiny in the mortal
world.
(014-105) (014-112)

The god's mountain of [[alaka|Alaka]] serves as a waypoint between the celestial and
mortal realms, where children are temporarily sheltered before being
delivered to their earthly guardians.
(026-039)

## Relationships

Kuvera commands the **Yakshas**, demigods and fairies who serve as his
attendants and messengers in the mortal world.
(026-164)

Through his divine authority, Kuvera is connected to the household of
**[[Râjahansa]]** — children destined to become companions of Prince
**[[Râjavâhana]]** are delivered to Queen **[[Vasumati]]** by Kuvera's
servants.
(002-143) (016-082)

The Yaksha lady **[[Târâvali]]** served Kuvera and acted as the foster
mother of a child the god had rescued, later returning him to the mortal
world by the god's command.
(015-089)

Kuvera's servants also had contact with the family of **[[Dharmapâla]]**,
whose widow entered Kuvera's service and became the instrument through
which a child was brought to the [[magadha|Magadha]]n court.
(002-143)
