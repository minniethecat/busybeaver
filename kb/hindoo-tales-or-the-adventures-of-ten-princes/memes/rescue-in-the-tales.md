# Rescue in the Tales

## Summary

Rescue is one of the most morally significant event types in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes*. Characters are repeatedly saved from drowning, captivity, execution, and other deadly perils by timely intervention.

Eight distinct rescue episodes demonstrate that the princes' survival depends on the aid of others—and that their willingness to rescue in turn marks noble character.

> "Shortly after this I met with the brahman whom I had rescued, and he,..."
> (003-080)

## Context

The rescues in the tales follow a consistent narrative pattern: a character is discovered in mortal danger, a passer-by intervenes, and the rescued person reveals their story or offers service.

Some rescues are physical—pulling someone from the sea, as when one character is "treacherously thrown into the sea, rescued by a merchant-ship" (018-145). Others are social—intervening to prevent an execution or an unjust punishment.

A poignant rescue involves "a young man who happened to be passing, and rescued me from" a dire fate (012-044). The motif reaches its emotional peak when a character cries out: "fly to rescue her!" (020-171).

## Key Participants

- [[somadatta]] — rescues a brahman and receives guidance in return

- [[visruta]] — rescues an old man from the well (026-118)

- [[apahâravarma]] — involved in multiple rescue episodes

- [[nalijangha]] — the old servant rescued in the forest (024-139)

- [[ratnavati]] — a woman whose story involves being rescued from peril

## Outcome

Rescues create the social fabric of the narrative world. Each act generates a debt of gratitude that the rescued person must eventually repay.

> "Nâlijangha, the old servant whom I had rescued in the forest, met me..."
> (024-139)

This economy of rescue and repayment drives many of the tales' most intricate plot developments. The old man "whom [[visruta|Visruta]] rescued from the well" (026-118) becomes a source of wisdom.
