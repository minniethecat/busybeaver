# Asmaka

Asmaka is a kingdom depicted in the later chapters of the *Daśakumāracarita*,
ruled by a cunning and unscrupulous king who serves as one of the final
antagonists overcome by the ten princes. The kingdom is the focus of a
concerted military and diplomatic campaign.
(023-143) (024-017)

## Description

The King of Asmaka is portrayed as ambitious, cruel, and cunning — a ruler
who coveted the territories of his neighbours and employed both military force
and treacherous diplomacy to expand his domain. He had already obtained
several allies through liberal promises when the princes confronted him.
(023-143) (024-017)

> "The King of Asmaka, who had for some time coveted the country, but did not dare..."
> (023-143)

The kingdom served as a foil to the righteous rule that [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] and his
companions sought to establish. Where [[magadha|Magadha]] represented legitimate kingship,
Asmaka embodied the abuse of royal power — making the princes' eventual victory
over its king a moral as well as a military triumph.
(024-060)

> "The cunning King of Asmaka, who had gained his allies by many liberal promises..."
> (024-017)

## Significance in the Narrative

Asmaka appears primarily in the culminating chapters (23–25) as one of the
major obstacles to the princes' restoration of order. The King of Asmaka
launched a campaign against neighbouring kingdoms, and his aggression
triggered the final coalition of the ten princes against him.
(024-004) (024-012)

> "Then, thinking the time to be arrived, the King of Asmaka prepared..."
> (024-004)

The confrontation with Asmaka is resolved through a combination of military
strategy and righteous force. Overcoming the wicked King of Asmaka fulfilled
part of a prophecy and restored the country to legitimate rule.
(024-060) (024-175)

> "overcoming that wicked King of Asmaka with... the first part of the prophecy"
> (024-060) (024-175)

The princes' campaign against Asmaka also involved rallying allies, including
the King of [[anga|Anga]], demonstrating the web of political relationships that the
ten princes had cultivated throughout their adventures.
(025-026) (025-061)

## Appearances

Asmaka is concentrated in the later chapters of the narrative: Chapters 23,
24, and 25. Its story forms part of the climactic sequence in which the ten
princes unite to overcome the remaining tyrannical kings.
