[025-001]In the morning a great crowd was assembled round the temple; for

[025-002]although the secret of the queen's vision had been kept, it was

[025-003]generally understood that something wonderful was to take place there.

[025-004]Presently the queen and her attendants arrived, entered the building,

[025-005]and paid their devotions to the goddess, after which the whole temple

[025-006]was carefully searched, to make sure that no one was concealed there,

[025-007]and all having withdrawn, the doors were closed, and the people stood

[025-008]without in silence, anxiously awaiting the pleasure of the goddess.

[025-009]A band then began to play and the kettledrums were loudly struck, so

[025-010]that the sound reached me in the hiding-place. At this, which was the

[025-011]preconcerted signal, I made a great effort, moved the large stone, and

[025-012]came forth with the boy into the temple. Having changed our dresses, I

[025-013]placed the old ones in the hole, carefully refitted the stone, and

[025-014]throwing the temple door wide open, stood in front of the astonished

[025-015]multitude, holding the young prince by the hand.

[025-016]While they were gazing in bewilderment, I thus addressed them: "The

[025-017]great goddess Durgâ, who lately showed herself in a vision to the

[025-018]queen, has been pleased to restore to his longing mother this child,

[025-019]whom she, in the form of a tigress, had carried away, and she commands

[025-020]you, by my mouth, to accept him as your sovereign."

[025-021]Then turning to the queen, I said:--"Receive your child from the hands

[025-022]of Durgâ, who will henceforth protect him as her own son; and by her

[025-023]command accept me as the husband of your daughter."

[025-024]To the ministers and elders I said:--"The goddess has brought me here,

[025-025]not merely as a messenger of her will, but as a defender of your

[025-026]country from that wicked King of Asmaka, whose cruel and unscrupulous

[025-027]intrigues are well known; accept me, therefore, as your deliverer, and

[025-028]as the guardian of the young king appointed by Durgâ."

[025-029]Upon this all broke out into loud acclamations, saying: "Great is the

[025-030]power of the glorious Durgâ! happy the country of which you are the

[025-031]protector!" and I was conducted in triumph to the palace, together

[025-032]with the queen, who could now openly show her joy at the recovery of

[025-033]her son.

[025-034]So well had I managed, that no suspicion arose of the deception which

[025-035]had been practised, and all the people venerated the young king as

[025-036]being especially under the protection of the goddess, and me as the

[025-037]agent chosen by her for his restoration.

[025-038]Thus my authority was well established. I caused, in due time, the

[025-039]young prince to be formally proclaimed king, and had him carefully

[025-040]educated; and I myself received the hand of the lovely Manjuvâdini, as

[025-041]the reward of my services and in obedience to the commands of Durgâ.

[025-042]After some time, however, I began to reflect: "Though my position now

[025-043]seems quite secure, yet, after all, I am a foreigner here, and when

[025-044]the first burst of admiration is over, people may perhaps begin to

[025-045]ask, 'Who is this stranger who has come among us in such a mysterious

[025-046]manner? and what is he that he should thus lord it over us?' And it

[025-047]occurred to me that if I could make friends with an old and

[025-048]much-respected minister, named Aryaketu, so as to trust him entirely,

[025-049]he might be of great assistance to me."

[025-050]Before, however, making any overtures to him, I desired Nâlijangha to

[025-051]try him secretly and ascertain his feelings towards me.

[025-052]My agent, therefore, had many interviews with him, and tried to

[025-053]persuade him that it was not for the good of the country that a

[025-054]stranger and foreigner should occupy such an important position,

[025-055]which ought rather to be held by a native, and that it would be very

[025-056]desirable to get rid of me.

[025-057]To all this Aryaketu answered: "Do not speak against so good a man,

[025-058]and one of such wonderful ability, endowed with such great courage,

[025-059]generosity, and kindness. So many good qualities are rarely found

[025-060]united in one person. I esteem the country very fortunate in having

[025-061]such a ruler, and am convinced, that through him the King of Asmaka

[025-062]will one day be driven out, and our prince established on his father's

[025-063]throne. Nothing shall induce me to plot against such a man."

[025-064]After hearing this from Nâlijangha, I tried the old minister in

[025-065]various ways, and seeing no reason to doubt his fidelity and

[025-066]attachment, I gave him my full confidence, and found him a most useful

[025-067]friend.

[025-068]With his advice and assistance, I was able to appoint efficient

[025-069]officers in every department. I encouraged religion and punished

[025-070]heresy; I kept each of the four castes in their proper sphere, and

[025-071]without oppressing the people, I collected a large revenue, for there

[025-072]is nothing worse than weakness in a ruler, and without money he cannot

[025-073]be strong.

[025-074][Here the story breaks off abruptly.]

[025-078]My Lord, I, having a common cause with my friends of wandering, saw

[025-079]among the Suhmans, in the outer park of a city called Damalipta, a

[025-080]great festal crowd. There, in a bower of Atimukta creepers, I saw a

[025-081]certain young man amusing himself with the sound of a lute. I asked

[025-082]him "Worthy sir, what is this festival called? on what account is this

[025-083]beginning, through what cause do you stand in solitude, accompanied

[025-084](only) by your lute, as if out of spirits, not having done honour to

[025-085]the festival?"

[025-086]He replied: "The King of Suhma, called Tungadhanwa, being without

[025-087]offspring, begged from the feet of Durgâ, called Vindhyavâsiní,[11]

[025-088]dwelling in this abode, having her love for the abode in Vindhya

[025-089]forgotten, two children, and by her in a vision to him sleeping near

[025-090](her temple) direction was given: 'There shall be produced of thee one

[025-091]son, and one daughter shall be born; but he shall be in subjection to

[025-092]her husband. But let her, beginning from the seventh year till her

[025-093]marriage, propitiate me every month while the moon is in Krittika (the

[025-094]constellation of the Pleiades), with the ball-dance, for the obtaining

[025-095]an excellent husband; and whom she likes, to him she is to be given

[025-096]and let this festival be called the Ball Festival.' So she said.

[025-097]"Then in a very short time the beloved queen of the king, named

[025-098]Mediní, bore a son, and a daughter was born at the same time. That

[025-099]damsel, called Kandukavati, will to-day propitiate the goddess having

[025-100]the moon as a diadem.

[025-101]"But her friend, Chandrasena by name, her foster-sister, was beloved

[025-102]of me; and in these days she has been violently besieged by the king's

[025-103]son Bhimadhanwa. Therefore I, distressed, perplexed at heart by the

[025-104]pain of the arrow-darts of Kâma, somewhat consoling myself with the

[025-105]soft tones of the lute, occupy a solitary place."

[025-106]And at that moment there came near a certain sound of anklets, and a

[025-107]certain lady came up. He indeed having seen her, with eyes opened

[025-108]wide, having risen up, having been embraced by her, sat down; and he

[025-109]said "This is the (lady) dear as my life, separation from whom,

[025-110]burning as it were, burns me up; and by that prince the robber of

[025-111]this, my life, I am brought to a state of coldness, as if by death;

[025-112]and I shall not be able, saying he is the king's son, to practise

[025-113]loyalty towards him; therefore, having caused myself to be favourably

[025-114]regarded by her, I will abandon a life which has no remedy."

[025-115]But she, with her face full of tears, said "O beloved, do not, on my

[025-116]account, engage in violence. Thou, who having been born of a worthy

[025-117]merchant, Arthadâsa, wast called Kosadâsa by thy parents, art called

[025-118]by thy enemies Vèsadâsa (slave of a girl), from thy excessive

[025-119]attachment to me. Thou thyself being dead, I should imagine the

[025-120]popular saying would be (he was) Nrisansa-Vesa--the slave of a wicked

[025-121]one. But now take me to any place you will."

[025-122]But he said to me: "Friend, in the regions seen by you, which was

[025-123](the most) prosperous, abounding in corn, and having the greatest

[025-124]number of good men?"

[025-125]To him, having laughed a little, I said "Wide is this (world bounded

[025-126]by) ocean and sky. There is no end of pleasant regions in one place or

[025-127]another. But, indeed, if I should not be able to produce some plan

[025-128]causing you to live comfortably here, then, indeed, I will show you

[025-129]the way."

[025-130]Meanwhile, the sounds of jewel-anklets arose. Now she, in a hurry,

[025-131]said: "My lord's daughter Kandukavati is come to propitiate Durgâ with

[025-132]playing at ball; and she is of unforbidden sight in this Kanduka

[025-133](ball) festival. May the eye of you going to see her be successful; I

[025-134]must be keeping near her." So saying, she went away, and we two

[025-135]followed her.

[025-136]I first saw the red-lipped (lady) standing on the floor of a jewelled

[025-137]stage; and she, seen by me a stranger and at a distance, immediately

[025-138]settled in my heart. And I, having my mind occupied by astonishment,

[025-139]thought: "Is this Lakshmi? for the lotus is not placed in her hand;

[025-140]but in her (Lakshmi's) hand there is a lotus, and she (the goddess)

[025-141]has been all enjoyed by Vishnu, and by former kings; but in this

[025-142](lady) there is unimpaired faultless youth."

[025-143]While I was thus reflecting, she, faultless in every limb, touching

[025-144]the ground with the tips of her stretched-out fingers, having her dark

[025-145]curled locks shaken, having with agitation saluted the mighty goddess,

[025-146]took hold of the ball, resembling (in colour) the god without a body

[025-147](_i.e._ Kâma) having his eye reddened by no slight passion; and having

[025-148]dropped it with graceful languor to the ground, having struck it,

[025-149]gently rising, with her bud-like hand having the delicate fingers

[025-150]stretched out, the thumb a little bent; having thrown, it up with the

[025-151]back of the hand, she caught it observed with active grace, in the air

[025-152]as it fell like a bunch of flowers joined with a circle of bees; and

[025-153]she discharged it in middling slow and quick musical time, throwing it

[025-154]very gently; and at that moment she displayed a quick movement with

[025-155]her feet; and when it stopped, she caused it to rise up with numerous

[025-156]blows; and, contrarywise, she caused it to rest; and she made it rise

[025-157]up like a bird, striking it regularly with her left and right hands

[025-158]when it was come straight to her side, and having caught it fallen

[025-159]when it had risen to a very great height, she practised a song-step;

[025-160]and having caused it to go up in various directions, she made it come

[025-161]back again. Thus sporting sweetly in various ways, accepting the words

[025-162]of praise loudly spoken at every moment by the people with their

[025-163]feelings interested come near to the stage, she stands turned towards

[025-164]me (who was) leaning on the shoulder of Kosadaâsa, having just then

[025-165]confidence produced in me, with flushed cheek and wide expanded eye.

[025-166]Then she being caused to have a glancing look like that of Kandarpa

[025-167]when first descended to earth, corresponding therewith having her

[025-168]gracefully-curved creeper[12] eyebrows sportively playing; with the

[025-169]network of the rays of light of her lips oscillated by the waves of

[025-170]the wind of her breath, like twigs moved in sport, as if beating off

[025-171]the bees eager to catch the perfume of heir lotus-face. In the

[025-172]circular whirlings of the ball (caused) by very rapid striking,

[025-173]entering, as it were, a flowery cage, through bashfulness at sight of

[025-174]me; in the Panchavindhu movement shaking off, as if through fear, the

[025-175]five arrows of Kâma simultaneously falling (on her); in the Gomuttrika

[025-176]steps quivering like the brightness shown in the cloud imitating

[025-177]forked lightning; in the harmonious movements of her feet, having the

[025-178]time kept by the sound of the jewelled ornaments; with her lower lip

[025-179]suffused with the brightness of a furtive smile; with the mass of her

[025-180]locks put up again when fallen down; with her jewelled girdle-belt

[025-181]sounding by knocking together; with the brightness of her muslin

[025-182]dress, agitated as it rested on her gracefully prominent full hips;

[025-183]with the beautiful ball, struck by the quivering, bent, and extended

[025-184]arms; with the arms like a loop, turned downwards; with her graceful

[025-185]hair reaching to the end of the back, rolled round upwards; with the

[025-186]game continued (and) not neglected from her rapidity in putting up the

[025-187]fallen-down golden leaf of the ear-ring; with the ball whirled inwards

[025-188]and outwards by the feet and hands throwing it up repeatedly; with the

[025-189]necklace lost to sight through bending down and rising up; the pearls

[025-190]without separation in falling and rising; with the wind of the little

[025-191]branch (stuck) in (or behind) the ear engaged in drying up the paint

[025-192]of the cheek spoilt by the perspiration breaking forth; with one hand

[025-193]engaged in holding back on the surface of her bosom the falling

[025-194]muslin dress; sitting down and rising up, closing and opening her

[025-195]eyes, striking on the ground or in the air, with one ball or more than

[025-196]one, she showed various sorts of play worth looking at.
