# Satyavarma

Satyavarma was a brahman and the youngest son of [[Sitavarma]], one of the
hereditary ministers of King [[Râjahansa]] of [[Magadha]]. He renounced worldly
life to become a pilgrim, and was the father of [[Somadatta]], one of the ten
princes whose adventures form the *Daśakumāracarita*.
(001-108) (026-138)

## Biography

Satyavarma was the youngest of two sons born to [[sitavarma|Sitavarma]], one of three
hereditary ministers who had served the father of [[rajahansa|King Râjahansa]] and who
continued in service under [[rajahansa|Râjahansa]] himself. His elder brother was
[[Sumati]], who remained at court as a minister.
(001-105) (001-108)

> "Of these sons the last-mentioned renounced worldly cares and employments,
> devoted himself to religious meditation, and leaving home as a pilgrim,
> travelled into many countries in order to visit the holy places."
> (001-109)

Satyavarma turned away from the ministerial career expected of him and
embraced the life of a wandering pilgrim. His travels eventually brought
him to a distant country on the banks of the river [[kavari|Kâvari]], far from the
court of [[magadha|Magadha]] where his brother [[sumati|Sumati]] continued to serve.
(001-109) (002-157) (002-158)

> "...brahman, Satyavarma, the youngest son of Sitavarma, a minister of the
> King of Magadha, after travelling about a long time, visiting all holy
> places as a pilgrim, came to this country, and here married a Brahman's
> daughter, named Kâli."
> (002-157)

In that country Satyavarma married [[durga|Kâli]], a [[brahman|Brahman]]'s daughter, but the
marriage produced no children. He subsequently took [[durga|Kâli]]'s sister [[gauri|Gaurí]] as
his second wife, and by her he had one son.
(002-160) (002-161)

> "Having no children by her, he took as his second wife her sister Gaurí,
> and by her he had one son, this child."
> (002-160)

His first wife [[durga|Kâli]], however, was consumed by envy. She plotted to destroy
the child born to her sister [[gauri|Gaurí]]. [[gauri|Gaurí]] fled with the infant but
perished before she could reach safety. The child was rescued by a
traveller and brought to the court of King [[Râjahansa]]. Unable to
locate Satyavarma — who remained absent on his pilgrimage — the king gave
the boy the name [[Somadatta]] and entrusted him to the care of his uncle
[[sumati|Sumati]].
(002-163) (002-164) (003-012) (003-013) (003-014)

Satyavarma is recorded in the text's glossary as "son of a minister of
[[rajahansa|Râjahansa]], and father of [[somadatta|Somadatta]]," confirming his genealogical identity
despite his physical absence from much of the narrative.
(026-138)

## Role in the Narrative

Satyavarma never appears as an active character in the narrative of the
ten princes. His role is entirely referential: he is named among the sons
of [[rajahansa|Râjahansa]]'s ministers in the opening chapter, his story is recounted
by a grieving woman in the second chapter, and his absence motivates the
naming and upbringing of his son [[Somadatta]] at the royal court.
(001-108) (002-157) (003-013)

His renunciation of worldly life places him in a spiritual lineage
separate from the martial and political careers of the ten princes. Yet
his son [[somadatta|Somadatta]] became one of the most prominent companions of
[[Râjavâhana]], the hero of the epic, and was the first of the princes
to reunite with his lord after their long separation.
(003-173)

Satyavarma thus functions as a genealogical anchor: he connects the
ministerial house of [[sitavarma|Sitavarma]] to the brotherhood of princes through his
son, while his own life embodies the alternative path of religious
renunciation — a path that stands in counterpoint to the active worldly
careers cultivated at [[rajahansa|Râjahansa]]'s court.
(001-109) (026-138)

- **Nephew:** [[Pramati]], son of [[sumati|Sumati]], also one of the ten princes (026-184)
