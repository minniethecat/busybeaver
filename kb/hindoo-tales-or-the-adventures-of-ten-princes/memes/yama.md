# Yama

Yama was the God and Judge of the Infernal Regions in the mythological
world of the *Daśakumāracarita*. He appears as a divine figure associated
with death, judgment, and the afterlife, invoked both in passing references
and in direct narrative descriptions.
(003-066) (026-165)

## Description in the Text

In one passage, a character beholds Yama, the judge of the dead, seated
upon a great throne inlaid with jewels — a majestic image of divine justice.
(003-066) (003-067)

Elsewhere, the narrative describes a warrior who "was made a guest of the
city of Yama" — a euphemism for death in battle — by the god [[vishnu|Vishnu]].
(026-013) (026-014)

Yama is also listed alongside [[siva|Siva]] and other deities as a ruler of the
world.
(022-129)

When a character meets a violent end, it is said that he is "gone to the
kingdom of Yama, where he will receive the reward of his evil deeds,"
reinforcing Yama's role as the dispenser of posthumous justice.
(021-089) (021-090)

## Role in the Narrative

Yama does not appear as an active character but functions as a mythological
reference point throughout the text. His kingdom and his judgment represent
the ultimate fate of wrongdoers, and his name is invoked to underscore the
finality and justice of death. The references to Yama align with the
classical Hindu conception of the deity as the lord of the dead and the
guardian of cosmic justice.
