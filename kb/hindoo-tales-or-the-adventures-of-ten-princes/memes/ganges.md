# Ganges

The Ganges is the great sacred river of India, appearing as a recurring
geographical and spiritual landmark throughout the *Daśakumāracarita*. Its
banks are the setting for hermitages, holy bathing places, and encounters
that shape the fortunes of the wandering princes.
(007-181) (017-072)

## Description

The Ganges flows through the landscape of the narrative as both a physical
river and a spiritual boundary. On its banks, not far from the city of
[[champa|Champa]], a sage dwelt in a hermitage — one of the many ascetic
retreats that lined the sacred waters and served as way-stations for
travellers and princes in exile.
(007-181) (008-008)

> "on the banks of the Ganges, not far from Champa, who was said to have"
> (008-008)

The river was a place of ritual purification. Characters bathe in the Ganges
as an act of devotion and renewal. In one striking episode, a character made
a significant gesture while bathing in the river, using the sacred waters as
the backdrop for an act of personal transformation.
(017-072)

> "next day, while bathing in the Ganges, I made it"
> (017-072)

A holy bathing place on the bank of the Ganges served as the destination for
those seeking spiritual merit. Pilgrims and ascetics gathered at such sites,
making the river a place where worldly and religious life converged — and where
characters in the narrative often encountered one another by seeming chance.
(021-023)

> "holy place on the bank of the Ganges, with the intention"
> (021-023)

## Significance in the Narrative

The Ganges functions in the *Daśakumāracarita* as more than a geographical
feature — it is a liminal space where characters undergo transitions. The act
of bathing in the Ganges marks moments of change, purification, or revelation,
connecting the worldly adventures of the princes to the sacred geography of
India.
(017-072) (021-023)

The river also serves as a geographical anchor, linking distant locations
through its course. The city of [[champa|Champa]] stands on its banks, and the hermitages
along its shores provide settings for encounters between princes, sages, and
travellers. The Ganges thus weaves through multiple storylines, connecting the
disparate adventures into a unified geographical tapestry.
(007-181) (008-008)
