# Malaya

The Malaya mountains are a range celebrated for their sandalwood trees and
the fragrant breezes that blow from them. In the *Daśakumāracarita*, Malaya
serves as a poetic emblem of love and desire — its perfumed wind is repeatedly
invoked in the language of romance, associated with the god [[kama|Kâma]] and
the season of spring. (005-150) (026-103)

## Description

The Malaya range was famed above all for its sandal groves, from which the
south wind carried fragrance across the land. This wind was considered the
companion and ally of [[kama|Kâma]], the [[kama|god of love]]. (005-150)

> "the soft south wind, blowing, loaded with perfume, from the sandal groves
> of Malaya"
> (005-150)

In the text, the wind of Malaya is said to intensify rather than soothe the
fever of love. Lovers complain that it fans the flames of passion rather
than cooling them. (006-083)

> "They call the wind from Malaya cooling; but to me it only increases the
> fever, as if blowing up the fire of love."
> (006-083)

The mountain range also appears as a dwelling place. A Yaksha lady, the
mother of [[taravali|Târâvali]], recounts visiting a friend who lived on the Malaya
Mountains. (014-101)

> "I went to visit a friend, living on the Malaya Mountains."
> (014-101)

At the sea-shore, during the season when the south wind carries the
fragrance of Malaya's sandal groves, the king would encamp with his court
beneath shady trees to enjoy the cool sea breezes. (021-137)

## Significance in the Narrative

Malaya functions in the *Daśakumāracarita* primarily as a poetic and
atmospheric presence rather than a physical setting for action. Its
perfumed wind is invoked in passages of romantic complaint, where lovers
speak of [[kama|Kâma]]'s power over them. The wind of Malaya is personified as
[[kama|Kâma]]'s "great friend," carrying the god's influence on its fragrance.
(013-030)

> "the perfumed wind of Malaya, by your sweet breath, the notes of his
> favourite bird by your voice"
> (013-030)

The mountain's sandal groves are also invoked in descriptions of royal
leisure, where the king enjoys the season made pleasant by Malaya's
breezes. This dual association — romantic longing and courtly pleasure —
makes Malaya a versatile symbol in the text's aesthetic vocabulary.
(021-137)

## Appearances

Malaya is referenced across six chapters of the *Daśakumāracarita*:
Chapters 5 (005-150), 6 (006-083), 13 (013-030), 14 (014-101), 21
(021-137), and the proper names index in Chapter 26 (026-103).
