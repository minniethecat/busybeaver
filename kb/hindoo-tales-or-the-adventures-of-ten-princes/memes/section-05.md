[005-001]After a time, when we were all more composed, my father began to

[005-002]inquire about the king and his own relations, for during all these

[005-003]years he had heard nothing of them. I told him everything--how the

[005-004]king had been defeated, and had been living in the forest; your birth,

[005-005]and the wonderful preservation of myself and my companions; how we had

[005-006]all set out together; how we had lost you, and how I was now searching

[005-007]for you.

[005-008]As soon as my father was able to walk, I placed him and my mother

[005-009]under the care of a certain muni, not very far off, and set out again

[005-010]on my travels. Just at this time I had heard that under the ruins of

[005-011]an ancient city, overgrown by trees, a great treasure was supposed to

[005-012]be concealed; and as I possessed a magic ointment which, when applied

[005-013]to the eyes, enabled me to see through the ground, I determined to

[005-014]try to dig it up. I therefore got together some strong young men with

[005-015]the promise of good pay, went to the place, and succeeded in finding a

[005-016]large quantity of gold and silver coin. While I was thus engaged, a

[005-017]caravan of merchants came to that neighbourhood, and halted there for

[005-018]a day or two. Taking advantage of this opportunity, I purchased of

[005-019]them sacks for holding the coin, and some strong oxen to carry them. I

[005-020]then dismissed my men, well satisfied with their share, and joined the

[005-021]caravan, where I soon made friends with the leader, the son of a

[005-022]merchant at Oujein, to which place he was then going.

[005-023]On our arrival at the city, he introduced me to his father,

[005-024]Bandhupâla, by whose means I obtained permission from the King of

[005-025]Mâlwa to reside there. When I had taken a house, safely deposited the

[005-026]money, and established my parents in it, I was anxious to set out

[005-027]again in search of you.

[005-028]Bandhupâla, seeing this, said to me: "You have already spent much time

[005-029]in searching for your friend, and may spend much more in the same

[005-030]manner to no purpose, if you have no clue to guide you. Now I am

[005-031]skilled in augury and the language of birds; it is probable that I may

[005-032]obtain some indications for you; wait, therefore, patiently for the

[005-033]present. Meanwhile, my house is always open to you."

[005-034]To this I agreed, and having great pleasure in his society, was much

[005-035]with him, and soon had other attractions there, for I fell in love

[005-036]with his beautiful daughter, Bâlachandrika.

[005-037]Though I had not declared my passion, I was convinced, from her looks

[005-038]and from many things which I observed, that she was equally in love

[005-039]with me, and therefore anxiously sought an opportunity of speaking to

[005-040]her in private.

[005-041]One day, Bandhupâla, wishing to obtain information about you by

[005-042]listening to the voices of birds, went with me into a park near the

[005-043]city, and while he waited under the trees, hearing the birds, I walked

[005-044]on, and had the good fortune to see my beloved alone, in another part

[005-045]of the park.

[005-046]Although she was evidently pleased at seeing me, and did not reject my

[005-047]suit, I observed that she was distressed and dispirited, and inquired

[005-048]the cause.

[005-049]She told me, "Some time ago the old king abdicated in favour of his

[005-050]son Darpasâra, who is now gone on a pilgrimage to the Himâlaya

[005-051]Mountains, having first appointed as joint regents the two sons of his

[005-052]father's sister, Charmavarma and Dâruvarma.

[005-053]"The former of these two alone has the management of affairs; for the

[005-054]latter, given up to evil deeds, makes use of his power only for the

[005-055]indulgence of his licentious passions.

[005-056]"He has seen me during my attendance on the Princess Avantisundari,

[005-057]has endeavoured to seduce me, and I am in constant fear of his

[005-058]violence, for he hesitates at nothing in the indulgence of his wicked

[005-059]desires."

[005-060]She told me this reluctantly, and with much agitation; but I comforted

[005-061]her with the assurance of my love, and the promise of finding some

[005-062]means to free her from his annoyance.

[005-063]After some reflection, I said to her, "This is the plan which I

[005-064]propose. Your friends must give out in public that a certain Siddha

[005-065]has declared--'Bâlachandrika is guarded by a demon, who will allow no

[005-066]man to have intercourse with her without his consent. Whoever,

[005-067]therefore, wishes to marry her, must first pass one night in company

[005-068]with her and one female friend, and if he comes out uninjured, or is

[005-069]able to overcome the demon, he may then safely marry her.'

[005-070]"If Dâruvarma, on hearing this, shall be alarmed, and abstain from

[005-071]further annoyance, so much the better; if, on the other hand, he

[005-072]persists in his wicked purpose, do you appear to consent, and say, 'If

[005-073]you think you can overcome the demon, I am willing to meet you, but it

[005-074]must be openly, in your own house; and then, whatever happens, no

[005-075]blame can fall on my family.'

[005-076]"To this proposal he will be sure to agree, and you may go to his

[005-077]house without fear, for I will accompany you, disguised as a woman,

[005-078]and will manage to kill that wretch, without danger to you or myself,

[005-079]after which there will be no obstacle to our marriage; for, when I ask

[005-080]your father, he will certainly consent, seeing the great love between

[005-081]us, for he has shown great regard for me, and knows my property and

[005-082]connections. But you must tell him now what has been arranged between

[005-083]us, that he may be induced to spread abroad the report about the

[005-084]demon, and to consent to your going to Dâruvarma's house."

[005-085]Bâlachandrika was delighted with my plan, and promised to do her best

[005-086]to carry it out. She had full confidence in my courage and skill, and

[005-087]felt sure that I should succeed in what I had undertaken. Then,

[005-088]reluctantly leaving me, and looking back again and again, she walked

[005-089]slowly home.

[005-090]After quitting her I returned to her father, who was well satisfied

[005-091]with the result of his observations, and told me that he had

[005-092]ascertained that after thirty days I should meet you; and we walked

[005-093]together to his house, talking over the matter.

[005-094]After a few days, Bâlachandrika informed me that Dâruvarma, undeterred

[005-095]by the report which was now spread about the city, that she was

[005-096]haunted by a demon, had continued his importunities, and that she had

[005-097]consented to go to his house that evening.

[005-098]Meanwhile I had secretly made my preparations, and concealed in a

[005-099]lonely place everything required for my disguise. At the proper time,

[005-100]when it was quite dark, I went there, changed my dress, met the lady,

[005-101]and accompanied her to the house of the prince, who received us with

[005-102]great respect; and not having the slightest suspicion of my being

[005-103]other than what I seemed to be, sent away all his attendants, and

[005-104]conducted us to a room in a small detached building. There he seated

[005-105]her on a beautiful soft couch, inlaid with jewels, and expressing his

[005-106]great delight at seeing her, brought forth and offered to us both very

[005-107]handsome presents of dresses, ornaments, perfumes, &c. After some

[005-108]conversation--as if no longer able to restrain himself--he sat down

[005-109]beside her, and, regardless of my presence, threw his arms round her,

[005-110]and kissed her again and again.

[005-111]This was more than I could bear; suddenly seizing him by the throat, I

[005-112]threw him on the ground, and despatched him with blows of hand, foot,

[005-113]and knee, before he could call out or give an alarm.

[005-114]Then we both screamed out loudly, and I rushed forth, as if in a

[005-115]great fright, calling out, "Help! help! the horrible demon is killing

[005-116]the prince!"

[005-117]Hearing this, and seeing my apparent agitation, the attendants and

[005-118]guards hastened in great confusion to the room, where they found the

[005-119]prince dead, and the lady so agitated that she was unable to give an

[005-120]account of what had happened; the demon had of course disappeared.

[005-121]Some police were in attendance, suspicious of fraud, but even they did

[005-122]not imagine two women to be capable of such an act of violence, and

[005-123]the general opinion was that the story of the demon was founded on

[005-124]truth, and that the prince well deserved the fate he had met with.

[005-125]Bâlachandrika was therefore suffered to leave: I had already escaped

[005-126]in the first alarm and confusion, had changed my dress, and reached

[005-127]home in safety.

[005-128]No further inquiry was made, and no suspicion fell on me; I duly

[005-129]married my beloved, and as no harm happened to me, the demon was

[005-130]supposed to have been propitiated.

[005-131]The day indicated by my wife's father having arrived, I came here,

[005-132]fully expecting to see you, and now my happiness is complete.

[005-133]When Râjavâhana had heard this story, he again related his own

[005-134]adventures; after which he took leave of Somadatta, saying, "Come to

[005-135]me as soon as possible, when you have paid your devotions at Mahâkâla,

[005-136]and have taken your wife and her attendants home;" and he then

[005-137]accompanied Pushpodbhava into the city of Avanti.

[005-138]There he was hospitably received in the house of his friend, who

[005-139]introduced him by his real name to Bandhupâla, but gave out in the

[005-140]city that he was a young brahman, worthy of all honour for his

[005-141]learning and ability; and the prince remained for some time in that

[005-142]city, treated with great respect and consideration by all who became

[005-143]acquainted with him.

[005-144]* * * * *

[005-145]MARRIAGE OF AVANTISUNDARI.

[005-146]During the stay of Râjavâhana at Avanti, the season of spring arrived,

[005-147]when the great festival of Kâma is celebrated. The trees, breaking

[005-148]into flower, were filled with the song of birds and the hum of bees,

[005-149]and their branches were waved by the soft south wind, blowing, loaded

[005-150]with perfume, from the sandal groves of Malaya. The lakes and pools

[005-151]were thickly covered with lotus blossoms, among which innumerable

[005-152]water-birds were sporting, and the feelings of all were influenced by

[005-153]the charms of the season, and prepared for the worship of the god of

[005-154]love.

[005-155]On the day of the festival, the parks and gardens were crowded with

[005-156]people, some engaged in various sports, some walking about or sitting

[005-157]under the trees, looking at the players.

[005-158]Among them was the Princess Avantisundari, who was sitting on a sandy

[005-159]spot, under a large tree, attended by her women, especially by her

[005-160]dear friend Bâlachandrika, and making offerings to the god of various

[005-161]perfumes and flowers.

[005-162]The prince also walked in the park with his friend Pushpodbhava; and

[005-163]wishing to see the princess, of whose grace and beauty he had already

[005-164]heard, contrived to approach; and being encouraged by Bâlachandrika

[005-165]with a gesture of the hand, came and stood very near her.

[005-166]Then, indeed, having an opportunity of observing her, he was struck by

[005-167]her exceeding beauty. She seemed to him as if formed by the god of

[005-168]love with everything most beautiful in the world; and, as he gazed, he

[005-169]felt more and more entranced, till almost unconsciously he was deeply

[005-170]in love.

[005-171]She, indeed, seeing him beautiful as Kâma himself, was almost equally

[005-172]affected, and, pervaded by strong feeling, trembled like the branch of

[005-173]a creeping plant agitated by a gentle wind.

[005-174]Then he thought, "Never have I seen anything so lovely. She must have

[005-175]been formed by some singular accident, for there is no one like her in

[005-176]the world."

[005-177]She, indeed, ashamed to look openly at him, and half concealing

[005-178]herself among her attendants, looked at him stealthily from time to

[005-179]time, and while he had all his thoughts fixed on her, was saying to

[005-180]herself, "Who can he be? Where does he come from? Happy the maidens

[005-181]whose eyes are delighted with such beauty! happy the mother who has

[005-182]such a son! What can I do? how can I find out who he is?"
