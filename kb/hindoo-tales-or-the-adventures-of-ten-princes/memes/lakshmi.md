## Biography

Lakshmí is the Hindu goddess of fortune and beauty, the consort of the god [[vishnu|Vishnu]]. (025-139) She is traditionally depicted holding a lotus flower in her hand. (025-140) In the narrative, when a character beholds a woman of extraordinary beauty, he wonders whether she might be Lakshmí herself, noting that she is typically enjoyed by [[vishnu|Vishnu]] and by former kings. (025-139)

## Role

Lakshmí represents divine beauty and prosperity in Hindu mythology. Her mention in the text serves as a standard of comparison for mortal beauty — the narrator ponders whether the woman he sees could be the goddess herself, before concluding that the mortal lady possesses "unimpaired faultless youth" that even the goddess lacks. (025-139) (025-140)

## Relationships

- **[[vishnu|Vishnu]]**: Her divine consort. (025-139)

## Quotations

> Thought: "Is this Lakshmi? for the lotus is not placed in her hand; but in her (Lakshmi's) hand there is a lotus, and she (the goddess) has been all enjoyed by Vishnu, and by former kings; but in this (lady) there is unimpaired faultless youth." (025-139)
