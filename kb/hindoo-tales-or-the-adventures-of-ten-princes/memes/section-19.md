[019-001]"One day when the husband of Dhumini was going about in search of

[019-002]game, he found a man who had been cruelly treated by robbers; they had

[019-003]cut off his hands, feet, and nose, and left him to perish. Having

[019-004]compassion on the poor wretch, he bound up his wounds as well as he

[019-005]was able, and carried him with much difficulty to his hut. There he

[019-006]and his wife nursed him till his wounds were healed, and took care of

[019-007]him afterwards.

[019-008]"Now such is the depravity of women, that Dhumini fell in love with

[019-009]this poor mutilated wretch, and determined to have him whether he

[019-010]would or no.

[019-011]"One day her husband came home from hunting, tired and thirsty, and

[019-012]asked her for water. She answered: 'I have a very bad headache, you

[019-013]must go and draw for yourself.' Then walking softly behind him as he

[019-014]went, she waited till he stooped down over the well, and pushed him

[019-015]in.

[019-016]"Having thus, as she thought, got rid of her husband, she took the

[019-017]maimed man on her back and carried him till she reached an inhabited

[019-018]country, where there was no famine, telling those who asked her, that

[019-019]this man was her husband, and had been mutilated in that manner by a

[019-020]spiteful enemy.

[019-021]"She thus became the object of much compassion, and praise, for

[019-022]devotion to her husband, and the king of the country bestowed on her a

[019-023]small pension on which she lived in the city of Avanti. Meanwhile her

[019-024]real husband had managed to climb up from the well, and wandered about

[019-025]a long time, not knowing where his wife was gone. At last he came to

[019-026]Avanti in great distress, and was begging for food when she chanced to

[019-027]see him. Going at once to the king, she said, 'That wicked wretch who

[019-028]mutilated my husband is now here; I have seen him going about as a

[019-029]beggar.'

[019-030]"Upon this he was immediately seized, and, notwithstanding his

[019-031]protestations of innocence, condemned to death, and led away to

[019-032]execution.

[019-033]"On the way, with but faint hopes of saving his life, he said to the

[019-034]executioner, 'I have been condemned on the evidence of one witness

[019-035]only; let that man whom I am accused of injuring be questioned; if he

[019-036]says I am guilty, then indeed I deserve to die.'

[019-037]"The executioner saying, 'Perhaps he may be innocent--a few minutes'

[019-038]delay can do no harm,' took him at once to the house of his wife, and

[019-039]there the poor mutilated wretch, with many tears, declared the

[019-040]kindness with which he had been treated by the supposed criminal, and

[019-041]the wickedness of the woman who had forced him to live with her as her

[019-042]husband.

[019-043]"Thereupon the execution was stayed, and the king, having been made

[019-044]acquainted with the whole affair, ordered her to be cut in pieces and

[019-045]given to the dogs, and showed much favour and kindness to her husband.

[019-046]"I say, therefore, there is nothing so cruel as the heart of a wicked

[019-047]woman."

[019-048]The Rakshas appeared to be satisfied with this story, and said: "Go

[019-049]on, tell me about Gomini." I continued therefore:

[019-050]"There was formerly in the country of the Dravidas a young brahman of

[019-051]great wealth. Somehow he was not married when a mere boy, as is often

[019-052]the case, and when he grew up he thought to himself: 'Those who have

[019-053]no wives and those who have bad wives are equally unfortunate, I will

[019-054]not let my friends choose for me, but travel about and look out for

[019-055]myself till I find a girl who may suit me.'

[019-056]"Having formed this resolution, and changed his name, he set out alone,

[019-057]taking very little with him, but a small bag containing two or three

[019-058]pounds of rice in the husk.

[019-059]"Whenever he saw a maiden of his own caste whose appearance he liked,

[019-060]either in the houses where he was admitted or elsewhere, he would say

[019-061]to her: 'My dear, could you make me a good dinner with this rice?'

[019-062]This he did many times, but though parents in general would have been

[019-063]willing to give him their daughters, he was always laughed at, and

[019-064]often treated with contempt. One day, while sitting in a public

[019-065]place in a town which he had lately entered, he observed a young girl

[019-066]whose parents had fallen into poverty, which was shown by her scanty

[019-067]dress and slender ornaments. She passed by him accompanied by an old

[019-068]woman, and stood for a time very near him.

[019-069]"The more he looked at her the more he was pleased, and thought to

[019-070]himself: 'This is just the wife to suit me; she is neither too tall

[019-071]nor too short, too stout or too thin; her limbs are rounded and well

[019-072]knit; her back is straight, with a slight hollow; her shoulders are

[019-073]low; her arms plump and soft; the lines of her hands indicate good

[019-074]fortune; her fingers are long and slender; her nails are like polished

[019-075]gems; her neck is smooth and rounded as a slender shell; her bosom

[019-076]full and well shaped; her face has a sweet expression; her lips are

[019-077]full and red; her chin small and compact; her cheeks plump; her

[019-078]eyebrows glossy black, gracefully curved, meeting in the middle; her

[019-079]eyes are long and languishing, very black and very white; her

[019-080]forehead, adorned by beautiful curls, resembles a piece of the moon;

[019-081]her ears are delicately formed, and well set off by the ear-rings; her

[019-082]hair is glossy black, brown at the ends--long, thick, and not too much

[019-083]curled. My heart seems to be drawn towards her; if she is what she

[019-084]seems to be, I will certainly marry her; but I must not act rashly; I

[019-085]will first try her with my test. Then approaching her with a polite

[019-086]salutation, he said: 'My dear, are you clever enough to make a good

[019-087]dinner out of this bag of rice;' Without answering a word, she looked

[019-088]significantly at her old nurse, and taking the rice from his hand,

[019-089]signed him to sit down on a terrace close by; and sat down herself

[019-090]near him. Then, first spreading out the rice in the, sun that it might

[019-091]be quite dry, she rubbed it gently between her hands, so as to get off

[019-092]the husk unbroken, and giving it to the nurse, she said: 'Take this to

[019-093]some goldsmith; they use it when prepared in this way for polishing

[019-094]their gold, and you will get a few pence for it--with them buy a

[019-095]little firewood, a few cheap dishes, and an earthen pipkin, and bring

[019-096]also a wooden mortar with a long pestle.' On this errand the old woman

[019-097]departed, and soon returned, bringing the things required.

[019-098]"Then the girl put the rice into the mortar, and very gracefully

[019-099]moving the pestle up and down, separated the rice thoroughly from the

[019-100]remaining particles of husk and awns; which she carefully winnowed

[019-101]away.

[019-102]"After this she washed the rice thoroughly, and the old woman having

[019-103]meanwhile lighted a fire and placed the pipkin full of water on it,

[019-104]she threw the rice into the water as soon as it boiled, in such a

[019-105]manner that the grains lay loose and separate. When they began to

[019-106]swell and burst, she took the pot from the fire, which she raked

[019-107]together, and set it with the lid downwards near the embers, first

[019-108]carefully draining off the rice liquor, and stirring the grains

[019-109]several times with a spoon to prevent their sticking together.

[019-110]"After this she put out the fire by throwing water on it, and taking

[019-111]the charcoal, sent the old woman to sell it, and with the money to

[019-112]procure some herbs, ghee, curds, tamarind fruit, spices, salt,

[019-113]myrobalan, and sesamum oil. When these things were brought, she mixed

[019-114]the myrobalan, finely pounded, with salt, and desired the nurse to

[019-115]give it with the sesamum oil to the young brahman, and tell him to go

[019-116]and bathe and anoint himself; and he having received these things,

[019-117]went to bathe.

[019-118]"When he was returned and comfortably seated, she gave him to drink

[019-119]rice liquor, mixed with spices and cooled by fanning, and he was much

[019-120]refreshed by it; afterwards, soup made with some of the liquor, a few

[019-121]spoonfuls of rice, butter, and spices; and, lastly, the rest of the

[019-122]rice mixed with curds, buttermilk, and several condiments, and he had

[019-123]plenty, though some was left.

[019-124]"When he had finished, he asked for drink. She gave him water in a new

[019-125]cooler, sweetened and perfumed with lotus and other flowers; and it

[019-126]looked and felt so cool, gurgled so pleasantly, and tasted so sweet,

[019-127]that all his senses were gratified, and he drank eagerly again and

[019-128]again.

[019-129]"After waiting on him in this manner, as soon as the dishes and the

[019-130]remains of the meal had been removed by the old nurse, she sat down

[019-131]beside him, arranging her scanty patched dress as well as she was

[019-132]able.

[019-133]"The young brahman having thus satisfied himself of the capabilities

[019-134]of the maiden, made known his real name and position to her parents,

[019-135]and they having gladly accepted him, he married the girl in due form,

[019-136]and took her home to his own house.

[019-137]"Not very long afterwards, with very little consideration for her, he

[019-138]took to himself another wife, a woman of bad character; yet such was

[019-139]the sweetness of temper of the first, that she showed no anger at

[019-140]this, but continued to treat her husband with all due honour and

[019-141]respect, and so gained over her fellow-wife that she became her

[019-142]dearest friend. At the same time she managed the household admirably,

[019-143]keeping everything in order, yet making all the servants attached to

[019-144]her. In short, she acted in such a manner that she entirely gained the

[019-145]respect and affection of her husband, and he enjoyed great happiness,

[019-146]and trusted and consulted her in all affairs.

[019-147]"Therefore I say that the best thing for a householder is to have a

[019-148]good wife."

[019-149]Then, in illustration of the third answer, I related the story of

[019-150]Ratnavati. "There was, in a town in the country of Surat, a rich

[019-151]ship-captain who had a daughter named Ratnavati. She was married to

[019-152]Balabhadra, the son of a merchant living in another town. For some

[019-153]reason he took a sudden dislike to his bride on the very day of the

[019-154]wedding, and though she continued to live in his house, avoided her

[019-155]as much as possible, and would never speak to her, notwithstanding the

[019-156]remonstrances of his friends. The rest of the family and the servants,

[019-157]seeing this, treated her with neglect and contempt, so that she led a

[019-158]most wretched life.

[019-159]"One day, wandering about disconsolate, she met with an old woman, a

[019-160]buddhist mendicant, who, seeing her weeping and looking miserable,

[019-161]asked her the reason. She, thinking that this woman might possibly be

[019-162]possessed of some charm capable of bringing back her husband's

[019-163]affections, half unwillingly told her the cause of her grief.

[019-164]"'On the very day of our marriage my husband, from some cause or

[019-165]other, took a sudden dislike to me, and since then he has treated me

[019-166]with neglect and contempt, so that I hardly ever see his face, and

[019-167]then only by chance for a moment, for he avoids me as much as

[019-168]possible; his family also, following his example, behave to me with

[019-169]great unkindness. I have no comfort or happiness, and only wish for

[019-170]death. But you must not tell this to any one; I would not on any

[019-171]account have my misfortune talked about.'

[019-172]"The old woman answered: 'Surely this must be a punishment for some

[019-173]great sin committed in a former existence, or such a charming person

[019-174]as yourself would never be thus treated by your husband. I recommend.

[019-175]you to practise penance and prayer; perhaps the gods may be appeased,

[019-176]and a favourable change produced. Meanwhile, if there is any way in

[019-177]which I can help you, I will gladly do so. You seem very intelligent;

[019-178]cannot you think of some stratagem which may have the desired effect?'
