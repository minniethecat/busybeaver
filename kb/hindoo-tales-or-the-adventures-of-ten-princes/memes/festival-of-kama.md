# Festival of Kâma

The Festival of Kâma is the pivotal event of the *Daśakumāracarita* — the
spring celebration at [[Avanti]] where [[Râjavâhana]] first sees
[[Avantisundari]] and falls irrevocably in love. More than a narrative
device, the festival embodies the text's central themes: the power of
desire, the beauty of ritual, and the moment when destiny crosses the
threshold from the divine to the human realm.
(005-147)

## The Great Festival at Avanti

The festival is introduced at a crucial juncture in the narrative.
[[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] has arrived at [[avanti|Avanti]] after his descent into [[patala|Pâtâla]] and his
wanderings through the forests. Spring has come, and with it the annual
celebration of [[Kâma]], the [[kama|god of love]]:

> "when the great festival of Kâma is celebrated. The trees, breaking
> into blossom, seemed to offer their flowers as an offering to the god;
> the breezes, laden with perfume, seemed to whisper of love; and all
> nature appeared to join in the worship of the deity of desire."
> (005-147)

The festival transformed [[avanti|Avanti]]. Parks and gardens that ordinarily served
as retreats for the wealthy became public spaces where social hierarchies
were temporarily suspended. Citizens of all ranks gathered to celebrate,
and among them moved the royal family — including Princess [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]].
(005-155)

> "On the day of the festival, the parks and gardens were crowded with..."
> (005-155)

## The Fateful Encounter

It was in this charged atmosphere that [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] first beheld the princess:

> "Among them was the Princess Avantisundari, who was sitting on a..."
> (005-158)

The text frames this meeting as more than chance. [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]], struck by
an overwhelming sense of recognition, believes he has known her in a
former existence. The festival, with its suspension of ordinary boundaries,
becomes the sacred time in which a love spanning multiple lives can be
rekindled.
(006-011)

Spring itself is an active participant — the season of renewal mirrors
the rebirth of a love that had been dormant across incarnations. The
festival's ritual honoring of [[kama|Kâma]] becomes, in the narrative, the actual
descent of the god's power into human affairs.

## Other Festivals in the Text

### The Simanta Festival

Before the great Festival of Kâma, the text mentions another celebration —
the [[simanta|Simanta]] festival of the queen, held at [[pushpapuri|Pushpapuri]]. This festival drew
nobles from across the land, and it was during this gathering that King
[[praharavarma|Prahâravarma]] of [[Mithila]] and his family were present when calamity
struck.
(002-040)

> "to be present at the Simanta festival of the queen, and..."
> (002-040)

### The Festival at the King's Court

A later passage describes a great festival at which the king was present,
and where the dancer [[kamamanjari|Kâmamanjari]] persuaded a holy man to accompany her
to the park where festivities were held. This festival serves as the
backdrop for one of the text's comic-adventurous subplots.
(008-102) (008-107)

> "'The next day there was a great festival, at which the king was...'"
> (008-102)

### The Festival of the Ball

In a later tale, a unique festival is described — the Festival of the
Ball — instituted to commemorate a miraculous event. This festival
demonstrates how celebrations in the narrative world serve not only to
honor the gods but to memorialize human experience and weave it into
the fabric of communal memory.
(025-096)

> "and let this festival be called the Ball Festival."
> (025-096)

## The Function of Festivals in the Narrative

Festivals in the *Daśakumāracarita* serve three narrative functions:

1. **Catalyst:** They bring characters together who would not otherwise
   meet, enabling the encounters that drive the plot forward.
2. **Revelation:** In the festive atmosphere, truths that would remain
   hidden in ordinary life are disclosed — identities revealed, loves
   declared, fates sealed.
3. **Restoration:** At the epic's conclusion, a solemn festival with
   song and dance celebrates the restoration of the rightful kings,
   marking the return of cosmic order.

> "and solemn festival be made with song and dance in..."
> (022-128)
