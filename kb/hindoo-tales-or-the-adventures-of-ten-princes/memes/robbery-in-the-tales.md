# Robbery in the Tales

## Summary

Robbery in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes* appears both as a specific criminal act and as a symptom of broader social disorder. Unlike simple theft, robbery in the tales carries connotations of violence, darkness, and social disruption—an event that reveals the precariousness of civil order.

> "Robbery, equipped with a dark dress, he went out at night."
> (009-032)

The motif provides opportunities for characters to display cleverness, either as perpetrators employing disguise or as victims turning the tables on their assailants.

## Context

The most vivid robbery episode involves a character preparing for a night-time heist: "robbery, equipped with a dark dress, he went out at night" (009-032). The detail of the dark dress as robbery equipment emphasizes the theatrical, almost ritualistic nature of the act.

A subsequent episode shows the aftermath: a robber displays stolen goods to his neighbor (009-165), whose reaction drives the narrative forward. This chain—robbery → display → discovery—illustrates how robbery functions as a plot mechanism.

On a macro scale, robbery serves as an index of social decay: a kingdom where "robbery was rampant; disturbances broke out everywhere" (023-177) is a kingdom in collapse. Here robbery is not individual criminality but systemic failure, justifying the need for righteous rule.

## Key Participants

- [[apahâravarma]] — his adventures bring him into contact with robbery and thieves (009-032)
- [[dhanamittra]] — involved in the robbery narrative of Ch.009
- The citizens — suffer under rampant robbery during periods of misrule (023-177)

## Outcome

Robbery in the tales operates on two levels: as an individual event, it tests characters' resourcefulness; as a social phenomenon, it measures the health of the body politic. The restoration of order—and the consequent suppression of robbery—becomes a metric of successful kingship.
