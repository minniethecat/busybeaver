# Avanti

Avanti (also known as Oujein or Ujjayini) is one of the principal kingdoms of
ancient India depicted in the *Daśakumāracarita*. Ruled by King [[manasara|Mânasâra]], it
is the home of Princess [[Avantisundari]] and the city where [[Râjavâhana]]
encountered his destined bride during the great [[Festival of Kâma]].
(005-137)

## Geography

Avanti was situated in central India, a prosperous kingdom renowned for its
wealth, culture, and the splendour of its capital city. The text describes
the city as possessing beautiful parks and gardens, where the citizens gathered
for festivals and recreation. The silver fetter — a sacred object — was
located on the road leading to Avanti, marking it as a place of spiritual
as well as temporal significance.
(007-123)

> "accompanied Pushpodbhava into the city of Avanti."
> (005-137)

## Role in the Narrative

Avanti serves as the primary setting for the central romance of the epic. It
was here, during the season of spring, that [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] arrived after his
wanderings and participated in the great [[Festival of Kâma]]. The city's
parks and gardens, adorned for the celebration, provided the backdrop for
his first sight of [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]].
(005-146) (005-155)

> "During the stay of Râjavâhana at Avanti, the season of spring arrived,
> when the great festival of Kâma is celebrated."
> (005-146)

The festival transformed the city into a spectacle of beauty:
> "On the day of the festival, the parks and gardens were..."
> (005-155)

Princess [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]], daughter of King [[manasara|Mânasâra]], was present among the
festivities, and it was here that the fateful encounter between her and
[[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] occurred — an encounter described as having been foretold by
a swan, and charged with the memory of a former existence.
(005-158) (006-011)

> "Among them was the Princess Avantisundari, who was sitting on a..."
> (005-158)

The city also appears in the later adventures of the ten princes. It was
at Avanti that one of the companions described meeting the princess and
falling in love on the very spot where the king had once encountered her.
The city's reputation for romance and beauty is woven throughout the text.
(006-137)

In a moment of distress, the princess [[Avantisundari]] fled to Avanti,
and it was there that her story reached its resolution. The translator of
the text notably identified Avanti with the historical city of Oujein
(Ujjayini), one of the seven sacred cities of ancient India.
(019-023) (020-078)

> "Avanti or Oujein, and I was so struck..."
> (020-078)
