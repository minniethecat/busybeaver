[015-001]"Sinhaghosha, longing to be freed from restraint, caused his minister

[015-002]to be arrested, when he came the next day to the palace, as usual,

[015-003]unsuspicious of danger. This very day he will be led round the city,

[015-004]be proclaimed a traitor, and have his eyes put out.

[015-005]"I, having lost my only friend and protector, have no wish to live,

[015-006]and was fastening my sash to hang myself, when you interrupted me."

[015-007]When Purnabhadra had finished this story, I said to him, "I am that

[015-008]child who was exposed in the cemetery, and saved by the fairy. My

[015-009]coming here is indeed opportune, and with your assistance I will

[015-010]engage to deliver my father. I would boldly attack the guards as they

[015-011]lead him round the city, but fear, lest in the confusion he might be

[015-012]killed, when all my exertions would have been in vain; some other plan

[015-013]must therefore be thought of."

[015-014]While I was thus speaking to him a serpent put out his head from a

[015-015]hole near me, and, knowing how to charm serpents, I made it come

[015-016]forth, and secured it.

[015-017]Then I said to Purnabhadra: "O friend, this is just what I wanted. I

[015-018]will mix with the crowd when my father is led round, let this serpent

[015-019]fall on him as if by chance, and then run up to him and say that I am

[015-020]skilled in charms, and can save his life. No doubt they will allow me

[015-021]to try, and I will stop the effect of the poison in such a manner that

[015-022]he will not die, and yet remain insensible, as if dead. Meanwhile, do

[015-023]you go to my mother, ask to see her in private, and tell her that the

[015-024]son whom she had lost is now here. Explain to her my plan for saving

[015-025]my father, and say that when she hears of the death of her husband,

[015-026]she must go to the king as if in the greatest grief, and ask for

[015-027]permission to burn herself together with the dead body. When this

[015-028]request is granted, as no doubt it will be, she must prepare the

[015-029]funeral pile, and make ready for self-immolation, laying the

[015-030]apparently dead body on a couch in a private room till I come, when I

[015-031]will tell her what is further to be done."

[015-032]Purnabhadra, delighted with the plan which I proposed, no longer

[015-033]wished to destroy himself. He set out at once to do as I had directed

[015-034]him, and I went immediately into the city. There I saw great crowds

[015-035]already collected, and ascertained where the executioner would stand

[015-036]when the proclamation was made.

[015-037]Overhanging the place, there happened to be a large tree, with thick

[015-038]foliage. Into this I climbed, and waited patiently, listening to the

[015-039]talk of the people collected underneath.

[015-040]Presently the executioner and his men came, bringing the prisoner, and

[015-041]the proclamation was made three times.

[015-042]"Know all men that this traitor, Kâmapâla, has not only poisoned the

[015-043]late king and his eldest son, but has been convicted of plotting

[015-044]against the life of his present majesty; he endeavoured to persuade

[015-045]two of the king's faithful attendants to administer poison, but they

[015-046]have given information, and his life is justly forfeited; the king,

[015-047]however, in consideration of his being a brahman, and nearly

[015-048]connected with himself, has spared his life, and only sentenced him to

[015-049]have his eyes put out. Let all evil-doers take warning by his

[015-050]punishment."

[015-051]While this proclamation was being read, I climbed to a branch of the

[015-052]tree just over my father, and dropped on him the poisonous serpent,

[015-053]which immediately bit him. In the confusion which ensued, I slipped

[015-054]down from the tree, and, having mixed with the crowd, managed, while

[015-055]shouting out "This is a just punishment from heaven; so may all

[015-056]traitors perish," to get close to my father, and quickly applied a

[015-057]charm in such a manner that, though he fell down apparently dead, the

[015-058]effect of the poison was stopped. The executioner being also bitten;

[015-059]and his assistants, as well as the crowd of spectators, being alarmed

[015-060]and dispersed from dread of the poisonous serpent; this act of mine

[015-061]was not noticed.

[015-062]Meanwhile, my mother, who had been prepared by Purnabhadra to hear of

[015-063]her husband's death, went immediately to the king, attended by a large

[015-064]number of friends, and said; "The gods know if my husband was your

[015-065]enemy or not; I will not now attempt to defend him; but, whether he

[015-066]was innocent or guilty, your anger should cease now he is dead. I pray

[015-067]you to allow me to burn his body, and according to the custom of

[015-068]widows of my rank, to ascend the funeral pile together with him. Were

[015-069]I not to perform this duty, disgrace would fall on you and on the

[015-070]whole family, as well as on myself."

[015-071]The king, well pleased to have got rid of the obnoxious minister,

[015-072]without incurring the sin of killing him, exclaimed: "This death is

[015-073]indeed the act of fate!" And, immediately granting her request,

[015-074]permitted the body of Kâmapâla to be taken to his own house, where I

[015-075]had by that time arrived, and was ready to receive it.

[015-076]Meanwhile, my mother prepared for death, and, resisting all the

[015-077]entreaties of her friends and servants, expressed her determination to

[015-078]be burnt together with her husband.

[015-079]When everything for the funeral was arranged, she came into the

[015-080]private room, where the body had been laid, and there saw her husband

[015-081]fully recovered, and me sitting by him. Great was her delight and

[015-082]astonishment at this wonderful and sudden change; and having first

[015-083]embraced her husband, she threw her arms round me, and, with a voice

[015-084]broken by sobs of joy, said: "O, my darling son, how can I deserve

[015-085]such happiness?--I, who so cruelly abandoned you at your birth, and

[015-086]suffered you to be taken away, as if dead? but your father was not to

[015-087]blame for that; he, indeed, deserves to have been restored to life by

[015-088]you, and to have the happiness of seeing you. Cruel, indeed, was

[015-089]Târâvali, who, when she had received you again from Kuvera, did not

[015-090]bring you at once to me; but what could I expect from her? It is

[015-091]through her unkindness in leaving us that all this misfortune has

[015-092]happened; but I must not complain; I was not worthy, without previous

[015-093]suffering, to enjoy such great happiness. Come and embrace me."

[015-094]Saying this, she again threw her arms round me, and kissed me

[015-095]repeatedly, trembling with emotion, and shedding many tears of joy.

[015-096]My father's feelings were scarcely less excited. He seemed to have

[015-097]risen from the lowest depth of misery to the summit of felicity, and

[015-098]esteemed himself more fortunate than even Indra the King of the Gods.

[015-099]When we were all somewhat calmed, and I had explained to my father all

[015-100]that had occurred, I said: "There is much yet to be done; the king

[015-101]will soon find out the deception which has been practised, and send to

[015-102]arrest you again; so we must consider how we can defend ourselves."

[015-103]My father answered: "This house is a very large one; the walls are

[015-104]strong; there are many secret passages; I have a great store of

[015-105]weapons; my servants are brave and faithful, so that we could hold out

[015-106]for several days. Besides this I have many friends in the city; most

[015-107]of the authorities will favour me; many of the soldiers will be on my

[015-108]side, and there are many persons discontented and ready to rebel

[015-109]against the king. Therefore, if we act prudently, we shall have much

[015-110]assistance, and be able to cut off that tyrant."

[015-111]With this I entirely agreed, and we prepared for defence. As I had

[015-112]expected, the king, finding how he had been deceived, sent soldiers to

[015-113]take us; but, though they made many attempts, we drove them back day

[015-114]after day, with very small loss to ourselves.

[015-115]Meanwhile, fearing lest we should at last be overpowered, if something

[015-116]more were not done, I determined, if possible, to seize the person of

[015-117]the king; and, as my father's house was not far from the palace, I

[015-118]began to make an underground passage inside, in order to reach his

[015-119]sleeping-room, the exact position of which I had learnt from my

[015-120]father. After digging for some distance, I came, to my great

[015-121]astonishment, into a large, lofty, well-lighted room, occupied by a

[015-122]number of women, among whom was a young lady of surpassing beauty,

[015-123]resembling the wife of Kâma, or the tutelary goddess of the city, who

[015-124]had hidden herself here to avoid the sight of so much wickedness

[015-125]above.

[015-126]The women were equally astonished at seeing me, and ran away, alarmed,

[015-127]into other adjoining rooms. One old woman, however, remained behind,

[015-128]and, falling at my feet, said "Have pity on us poor helpless women;

[015-129]surely thou art a god, for no mortal could have thus found his way

[015-130]hither. O tell us why thou art come."

[015-131]"Calm yourself," I answered, "You have nothing to fear from me. I am

[015-132]Arthapâla, the son of the minister Kâmapâla and the Princess

[015-133]Kantimati, and have come thus unexpectedly on you while making an

[015-134]underground passage from my father's house to the palace; but tell me

[015-135]who you all are, and how you come to be living here."

[015-136]"O prince," she answered, "I had heard of your birth, but not of your

[015-137]preservation, and happy am I now to see you. Know that the young lady

[015-138]whom you have just seen is the granddaughter of your maternal

[015-139]grandfather, Chandasinha. The eldest son of that king died before his

[015-140]father, leaving his wife pregnant, and she lost her life in giving

[015-141]birth to this daughter, who was committed to my care. One day the king

[015-142]sent for me, and said: 'I intend this child when grown up to be given

[015-143]in marriage to Darpasâra, son of the King of Mâlwa; and, remembering

[015-144]the misconduct of her aunt, I am determined that nothing of the kind

[015-145]shall happen with her. I have therefore caused a spacious palace to be

[015-146]made underground, and have furnished it with provisions and all other

[015-147]necessaries for even a hundred years. I have great confidence in you;

[015-148]you will therefore go down into this subterranean dwelling, taking

[015-149]with you the princess and such attendants as you may think desirable,

[015-150]and will remain there until she is grown up, when I shall fetch you

[015-151]from below, and give her in marriage as I have intended.' So saying,

[015-152]he lifted up a small trap-door in the court-yard close to his own

[015-153]apartment, and showed me the steps leading to this place. The next day

[015-154]we all came down, and have remained here ever since. Twelve years have

[015-155]now passed, and the king seems to have forgotten us. I must tell you

[015-156]also that the princess, though destined by her grandfather for

[015-157]Darpasâra, was originally intended for you; for her mother, while the

[015-158]child was as yet unborn, promised that her daughter should become the

[015-159]wife of the son of Kantimati if he should ever return. Look on her,

[015-160]therefore, as your intended, and do what is best for us."

[015-161]Having received this account from the old woman, I told her to have no

[015-162]fear on the princess's account, but to trust entirely in me, and that

[015-163]I would soon liberate them from their long and tedious imprisonment.

[015-164]She then took a lamp and showed me the steps leading to the trap-door,

[015-165]which I forced open, and soon found my way into the king's bed-room.

[015-166]There, before he was sufficiently awake to call for help, I seized,

[015-167]gagged, and bound him, and dragging him along, as an ichneumon drags a

[015-168]serpent, past the astonished women and through the tunnel which I had

[015-169]made, I brought him, trembling with fear and bowed down by shame, to

[015-170]my father's house, and showed him to my parents, telling them how I

[015-171]had captured him, and how I had discovered the princess in the

[015-172]subterranean palace.
