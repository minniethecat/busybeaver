# Ratnavati

Ratnavati was the daughter of a rich ship-captain of [[surat|Surat]], who was
married to the merchant [[balabhadra|Balabhadra]]. Her story of elopement
and re[[marriage-in-the-tales|union]] with her husband is recounted as an illustration of feminine
cunning in the narrative of [[apaharavarma|Apahâravarma]].

## Biography

Ratnavati was born in a town in the country of [[surat|Surat]], the daughter of a
wealthy ship-captain. She was married to [[balabhadra|Balabhadra]], the son of a merchant
living in another town.
(019-150) (019-151)

> "There was, in a town in the country of Surat, a rich ship-captain who
> had a daughter named Ratnavati. She was married to Balabhadra, the son
> of a merchant living in another town."
> (019-151) (019-152)

However, on the very day of the [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]], her husband took a sudden dislike
to her. Though she continued to live in his house, he avoided her as much
as possible and would never speak to her, despite the remonstrances of his
friends.
(019-152) (019-156)

> "For some reason he took a sudden dislike to his bride on the very day
> of the wedding, and though she continued to live in his house, avoided
> her as much as possible."
> (019-153) (019-154)

The rest of the family and servants, following [[balabhadra|Balabhadra]]'s example,
treated her with neglect and contempt, so that she led a most wretched
life.
(019-156) (019-158)

One day, wandering about disconsolate, Ratnavati met an old Buddhist
mendicant woman who, seeing her weeping, asked the reason. Ratnavati
confided in her, describing her husband's unexplained aversion and the
family's unkindness.
(019-159) (019-167)

> "I have no comfort or happiness, and only wish for death. But you must
> not tell this to any one; I would not on any account have my misfortune
> talked about."
> (019-170) (019-171)

The old woman counselled her to practise penance and prayer, and offered
to help bring about a reconciliation. Through the old woman's stratagem,
Ratnavati later eloped under another name, engaging a woman to accompany
her as a servant.
(019-172) (020-039)

> "When Ratnavati eloped under another name, she engaged a woman to
> accompany her as a servant."
> (020-039)

Through her cunning and the clever arrangement, she was ultimately reunited
with her husband [[balabhadra|Balabhadra]], who had in the meantime established himself
in another town and amassed a considerable fortune through his skill and
energy.
(020-035) (020-037)

The matter was settled when the father of Ratnavati himself answered the
inquiry in person and declared that the lady was truly his daughter.
(020-060) (020-061)

## Role in the Narrative

Ratnavati is one of four women — alongside [[dhumini|Dhumini]],
[[gomini|Gomini]], and [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]] — cited as examples of
cunning in Chapter 18 of the tales.
(018-161)

> "Cunning. Dhumini, Gomini, Ratnavati, and Nitambavati are examples of
> what I have said."
> (018-161)

Her full story is recounted in Chapters 19 and 20, within the narrative
frame of [[apaharavarma|Apahâravarma]]'s adventures. Her tale illustrates
the theme of feminine wiles, as she uses deception and the help of a
female accomplice to win back her estranged husband.
