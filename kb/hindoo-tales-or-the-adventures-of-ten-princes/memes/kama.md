# Kâma

Kâma (Sanskrit: काम) appears in the *Daśakumāracarita* in three interwoven
forms: as the god of love whose annual festival drives the central romance of
the epic, as the abstract concept of desire that animates the actions of kings
and princes, and as the personal name borne by several characters including
[[kamapala|Kâmapâla]], one of the ten princes. The text draws on the rich Hindu tradition
that personifies desire as a deity while simultaneously treating it as a
fundamental force of human existence.
(005-147)

## Kâma as Deity

In Hindu mythology, Kâma is the god of love, armed with a bow of sugarcane
and arrows tipped with flowers. The *Daśakumāracarita* invokes this imagery
when it describes the great [[festival-of-kama|Festival of Kâma]] celebrated at [[Avanti]]:

> "when the great festival of Kâma is celebrated. The trees, breaking..."
> (005-147)

The text describes a prince as being "beautiful as Kâma himself," invoking
the deity as the standard of male beauty against which mortal princes are
measured. This comparison carries narrative weight — to be likened to the
god of love is to be marked as destined for romantic fulfillment.
(005-171)

> "She, indeed, seeing him beautiful as Kâma himself, was almost equally..."
> (005-171)

The festival of Kâma provided the ritual context in which mortal love could
be sanctified by divine precedent. The parks and gardens of [[avanti|Avanti]], adorned
for the celebration, became a sacred space where the boundary between divine
and human love dissolved.
(005-155)

## Kâma as Philosophical Concept

Kâma — desire or pleasure — is one of the three great aims of human life
(Trivarga) in Hindu philosophy, alongside [[virtue|Dharma]] (virtue) and [[wealth|Artha]] (wealth).
The *Daśakumāracarita* explores this triad through the actions of its
characters, showing how the pursuit of desire can lead to both fulfillment
and peril.

> "they call Kâma the god with..."
> (006-081)

The tension between Kâma and [[virtue|Dharma]] runs throughout the tales. Princes who
pursue desire recklessly face consequences; those who temper passion with
wisdom achieve their goals. The text does not condemn desire outright but
presents it as a force that must be understood and channeled — a
sophisticated ethical position consonant with classical Hindu thought.

The character of [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]] — whose very name means "protector of desire" —
embodies this ambivalence. Unlike his more studious brother, [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]]
"thought only of present pleasure, frequented the company of gamblers and
harlots, and roamed" — yet his story, too, finds its proper place in the
grand design of the narrative.
(001-113)

> "Kâmapâla was of an opposite character; he thought only of present
> pleasure..."
> (001-113)

### The Three Aims in the Text

| Aim | Sanskrit | Role in the Narrative |
|-----|----------|----------------------|
| [[virtue|Dharma]] | [[virtue|Virtue]]/duty | The moral framework against which actions are judged |
| [[wealth|Artha]] | [[wealth|Wealth]]/power | The kingdoms, treasures, and political ambitions |
| Kâma | Desire/pleasure | The loves, festivals, and personal fulfillments |

## The Festival of Kâma

The great [[festival-of-kama|Festival of Kâma]], celebrated annually at [[avanti|Avanti]] during the spring
season, is the single most consequential event in the *Daśakumāracarita*.
It is at this festival that [[Râjavâhana]] first beholds [[Avantisundari]]
and falls irrevocably in love — a moment that the text imbues with a sense
of cosmic recognition, as if the meeting had been ordained across multiple
existences.
(005-146) (006-011)

The festival transformed the city: trees burst into blossom, the parks
filled with crowds, and the barriers of ordinary social convention were
temporarily suspended. In this liminal space, the love that would shape
the destiny of kingdoms was kindled.

> "On the day of the festival, the parks and gardens were crowded with..."
> (005-155)

## Kâma in Names and Titles

The text uses Kâma-derived names to signal character traits:

- **[[kamapala|Kâmapâla]]:** One of the ten princes, son of [[dharmapala|Dharmapâla]]. His name signifies
  the tension between desire and protection. (026-089)
- **[[kamamanjari|Kâmamanjari]]:** A dancer and actress whose name means "bouquet of desire."
  She plays a pivotal role in the adventures. (008-022)
- **Kâmadattâ:** A character whose name means "given by desire."

These naming patterns are not coincidental — they form part of the text's
systematic exploration of desire in its many forms.
