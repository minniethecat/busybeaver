[007-001]In the confusion and excitement caused by the conjurer's performance,

[007-002]Râjavâhana and the princess slipped unnoticed into her apartments,

[007-003]where he was safe, for the present at least, her attendants being all

[007-004]devoted to her, and careful to keep the secret.

[007-005]He was thus able to enjoy the society of his bride without

[007-006]interruption; to give her a full account of his life and adventures,

[007-007]and to teach her many things of which she was ignorant; so that she

[007-008]became more and more attached to him, and admired his knowledge and

[007-009]eloquence as much as she had before admired his beauty.

[007-010]* * * * *

[007-011]FURTHER ADVENTURES OF RÂJAVÂHANA.

[007-012]Thus the princess, listening with delight and astonishment to the

[007-013]sweet and eloquent words of her husband, and he never tired of

[007-014]contemplating her beauty and enjoying her caresses, lived for some

[007-015]time in the greatest happiness, without care or anxiety for the

[007-016]future.

[007-017]One night, when both were sleeping, the prince had a remarkable dream.

[007-018]He seemed to see an old swan, whose legs were tied together with lotus

[007-019]fibre, approach the bedside; at that moment he awoke with a feeling of

[007-020]pressure on his feet, and found himself bound with a slender silver

[007-021]chain, bright as the rays of the moon. The princess awoke at the same

[007-022]time, and seeing her husband thus fettered, screamed out loudly in her

[007-023]fright. The attendants in the adjoining apartments, hearing the

[007-024]scream, thought something dreadful must have happened. They rushed

[007-025]into the room, added their cries to hers, and forgetting all their

[007-026]former precautions, left the doors open, so that the guards outside,

[007-027]hearing the clamour, entered and saw the prince.

[007-028]When about to seize him, they were awed by his dignity, and contented

[007-029]themselves with giving information to the regent, Chandavarma, who, on

[007-030]receiving it, came immediately to the place.

[007-031]Looking at the prince with eyes burning with the fire of anger, he

[007-032]began to recollect him, and said, "So! this is that conceited brahman

[007-033]who has been deceiving the people; making them believe that he is

[007-034]wonderfully clever; the friend of that fellow the husband of the

[007-035]wicked Bâlachandrika, the cause of my brother's death. How is it

[007-036]possible that the princess should have fallen in love with such a

[007-037]paltry wretch, overlooking a man like me? She is a disgrace to her

[007-038]family, and shall soon see her husband impaled on a stake."

[007-039]Then, with his forehead disfigured by a fearful frown, he continued to

[007-040]abuse the prince; and having tied his hands behind him, dragged him

[007-041]from the room.

[007-042]Râjavâhana, naturally brave, and encouraged by belief in that former

[007-043]existence the remembrance of which had so wonderfully arisen in his

[007-044]mind, bore all the insults with firmness, and saying to the princess,

[007-045]"Remember that speech of the swan, have patience for two months, and

[007-046]all will be well," submitted quietly to the imprisonment.

[007-047]When the ex-king and queen were informed of what had happened, they

[007-048]were greatly distressed on their daughter's account, and exerted

[007-049]themselves to save the life of their son-in-law; but the regent, in

[007-050]whom all authority was vested, resisted their entreaties; and only on

[007-051]condition of their resigning some of the few privileges which still

[007-052]remained to them did he consent to defer the execution till he had

[007-053]communicated with Darpasâra, and learned his pleasure on the subject.

[007-054]He confiscated the property of Pushpodbhava, and threw him and his

[007-055]family into prison; and being about to march against the King of Anga,

[007-056]and unwilling to leave the prince behind, lest he should be liberated

[007-057]by the old king, he caused a wooden cage to be made, in which his

[007-058]prisoner was shut up and carried with the army.

[007-059]Treated thus like some wild beast, roughly shaken and neglected,

[007-060]Râjavâhana would have suffered greatly had he not been protected by

[007-061]the magic jewel given to him in Pâtâla, and which he had contrived to

[007-062]conceal in his hair.

[007-063]Chandavarma had some time before this asked in marriage Ambâlika, the

[007-064]daughter of Sinhavarma, King of Anga, and, indignant at a refusal, was

[007-065]now marching against him, to take vengeance for the insult, and get

[007-066]possession of the princess. Advancing therefore with a large army, he

[007-067]prepared to besiege Champa, the capital city.

[007-068]Sinhavarma, being of a very impatient and impetuous disposition, would

[007-069]not wait for the arrival of the allies who had been summoned to his

[007-070]assistance, and were then on the march; but throwing open the gates,

[007-071]went forth to meet the enemy.

[007-072]A terrible battle ensued, in which both kings performed prodigies of

[007-073]valour. At last Sinhavarma was taken prisoner, and his army so

[007-074]completely defeated, that the conqueror entered and took possession of

[007-075]the city without opposition.

[007-076]Chandavarma, having now the princess in his power, determined to make

[007-077]her his wife at once: he therefore treated her father with more

[007-078]consideration than he would otherwise have done, though he put him in

[007-079]confinement, and caused it to be proclaimed throughout the city that

[007-080]the wedding would be celebrated with much splendour the next morning.

[007-081]Just then a messenger arrived from Kailâsa, bringing a letter from

[007-082]Darpasâra, in which he had written, "O fool! should there be any pity

[007-083]for the violator of the harem? If the old king, my father, now in his

[007-084]dotage, was foolish enough to favour the criminal for the sake of his

[007-085]worthless daughter, you had no need of his permission, and ought not

[007-086]to have been influenced by him. Let that vile seducer be immediately

[007-087]put to death by torture, and his paramour be shut up in prison till I

[007-088]come."

[007-089]Chandavarma, who had intended to march against the allies advancing

[007-090]for the assistance of his captive, on receiving these commands, gave

[007-091]orders to his attendants, saying, "To-morrow morning take that vile

[007-092]wretch from his cage, and set him at the palace gate. Have ready,

[007-093]also, a fierce elephant, suitably equipped, which I shall mount

[007-094]immediately after the wedding, to overtake my army in march against

[007-095]the enemy; and as I set out, I will make the elephant trample the

[007-096]life out of that criminal."

[007-097]Accordingly, the next morning, the prince was brought by the guards to

[007-098]the gate of the palace, and the elephant placed near him.

[007-099]While he stood there, calmly awaiting death, which now seemed

[007-100]inevitable, he suddenly felt his feet free, and a beautiful lady

[007-101]appeared before him.

[007-102]She humbly bowing down said: "Let my lord pardon his servant for the

[007-103]injury which she has unconsciously caused. I am an Apsaras, born from

[007-104]the rays of the moon. One day, as I was flying through the air,

[007-105]wearing a white dress, a swan, mistaking me for a lotus flower,

[007-106]attacked me. While struggling to keep off the bird, the string of my

[007-107]necklace broke, and the pearls fell on the grey head of a very holy

[007-108]rishi, bathing, in the clear water of a Himâlayan lake.

[007-109]"In his anger, he cursed me, saying: 'O wicked one, for this offence

[007-110]you are condemned to be changed into a piece of unconscious metal.'

[007-111]"When, however, I entreated forgiveness, he was so far appeased, that

[007-112]he modified the curse, and granted that I should still retain

[007-113]consciousness, and remain as a fetter on your feet for two months

[007-114]only.

[007-115]"The change took place immediately, and I fell to the ground, turned

[007-116]into a silver chain.

[007-117]"About this time, Vírasekhara, a Vidyâdhara, partly of human descent,

[007-118]had become acquainted with Darpasâra, then performing penance on the

[007-119]great mountain; and thinking he might get assistance from him in a

[007-120]feud in which he was involved, had made an alliance with him, and

[007-121]engaged to marry his sister, the Princess Avantisundari.

[007-122]"Being desirous of visiting his intended bride, he flew through the

[007-123]air to Avanti. On his way he saw the silver fetter, descended to the

[007-124]ground, picked it up, and continued his flight.

[007-125]"Having made himself invisible, he entered without difficulty the

[007-126]apartment of the princess, and was astonished and enraged on finding

[007-127]her lying in your arms. His first impulse was to kill you; but some

[007-128]irresistible influence restrained him, so that he contented himself

[007-129]with putting the silver fetter on your feet, and departed without

[007-130]otherwise disturbing you.

[007-131]"You have, in consequence, suffered all this misery. Now my

[007-132]transformation is ended, and you are so far free; tell me what I can

[007-133]do for you in atonement for the suffering which I have caused?"

[007-134]The prince, not thinking of himself, said only, "Go at once to her who

[007-135]is dearer to me than life, and comfort her with news of me."

[007-136]At that moment a great clamour was heard, and some persons, rushing

[007-137]from the interior of the palace, called out, loudly, "Help! help!

[007-138]Chandavarma is murdered! killed by an assassin, who stabbed him as he

[007-139]was about to take the hand of the princess; and that man is now moving

[007-140]about the palace, cutting down all who attempt to seize him."

[007-141]Râjavâhana, when he heard this, without losing a moment, and before

[007-142]the guards had perceived his feet to be unfettered, with a sudden

[007-143]spring leapt on the elephant intended for his destruction; and having

[007-144]thrust off the driver, urged the beast at a rapid pace, pushing aside

[007-145]the crowd right and left as he went.

[007-146]Having got into the courtyard, he shouted with a loud voice, "Who is

[007-147]the brave man that has done this great deed, hardly to be accomplished

[007-148]by a mere mortal? Let him come forth and join me; we two united are a

[007-149]match for a whole army."

[007-150]The slayer of Chandavarma hearing this, came out of the palace, and

[007-151]quickly mounting the elephant, who held down his trunk to receive him,

[007-152]placed himself behind the prince.

[007-153]Great was their mutual astonishment and joy when they recognised each

[007-154]other, the prince exclaiming, "Is it possible? Is it really you, my

[007-155]dear friend Apahâravarma, who have done this deed?" and the other

[007-156]saying, "Do I indeed see my Lord Râjavâhana?" Having thus recognised

[007-157]and embraced each other, they turned the elephant round, and passing

[007-158]through the crowd in the courtyard, went into the main street, now

[007-159]thronged by soldiers. Through these they forced their way, employing

[007-160]with good effect the weapons placed on the elephant for the use of

[007-161]Chandavarma.

[007-162]Before, however, they had gone far, they heard the noise of battle at

[007-163]a distance, and saw the soldiers in front of them scattered in all

[007-164]directions.

[007-165]Soon they saw coming towards them a very well-dressed, handsome man,

[007-166]riding on a swift elephant. On reaching them, he made obeisance to the

[007-167]prince, saying, "I am sure this is my Lord Râjavâhana;" and then

[007-168]turning to Apahâravarma, said, "I have followed your directions

[007-169]exactly, and hastened on the advancing allies. We have just now

[007-170]encountered and utterly defeated the enemy, so that there is no fear

[007-171]of any further resistance."

[007-172]Then Apahâravarma introduced the stranger to the prince, saying, "This

[007-173]is my dear friend Dhanamittra, well worthy of your respect and

[007-174]consideration; for he is as brave and clever as he is handsome. With

[007-175]your permission, he will liberate the King of Anga, and re-establish

[007-176]the former authorities; meanwhile, we will go on to a quiet place, and

[007-177]wait there for him and the princes who have come so opportunely to our

[007-178]assistance."

[007-179]Râjavâhana agreed to this. They went a little further, and dismounted

[007-180]at a pleasant cool bank, shaded by a large banian tree, and close to

[007-181]the Ganges.

[007-182]When they had been for some time seated there, Dhanamittra returned,

[007-183]accompanied by Upahâravarma, Pramati, Mitragupta, Mantragupta,

[007-184]Visruta, Prahâravarma King of Mithila, Kâmapâla lord of Benâres, and

[007-185]Sinhavarma King of Anga.

[007-186]The prince, astonished and delighted at such an unexpected meeting,

[007-187]warmly embraced his young friends, and very respectfully saluted, as a

[007-188]son, the elder men introduced by them. Many questions were asked on

[007-189]both sides. After some conversation, Râjavâhana told them his own

[007-190]adventures, and those of Somadatta and Pushpodbhava, and then begged

[007-191]his friends to relate theirs.
