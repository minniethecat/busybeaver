# Devotion

## Definition

Devotion (Bhakti) in the Hindoo world encompasses both religious dedication to deities and personal loyalty to gurus, husbands, and masters. It is expressed through outward acts — paying devotions at temples and shrines, performing rituals, and offering prayers — as well as through inward dispositions of faithfulness and commitment. Devotion is regarded as a virtue that attracts divine favour and commands social respect.

## In the Narrative

Devotion appears throughout the narrative in both its religious and personal dimensions. In Chapter 3, a holy man declares himself "pleased with your devotions," indicating that acts of religious devotion earn divine approval (003-095). Characters pause their journeys to pay devotions at sacred sites: at [[mahakala|Mahâkâla]], the great temple of [[siva|Siva]], a character is instructed to pay devotions before proceeding (005-135), and in Chapter 25, a group pays their devotions to the goddess before continuing their travels (025-005). After finishing his devotions, one narrator resumes his wanderings, suggesting that devotion is woven into the daily rhythm of religious life (014-025).

The personal dimension of devotion appears in relationships of loyalty and service. A character's "devotion to him" — to a spiritual teacher — is rewarded after many years of faithful service (014-064). A wife's "devotion to her husband" is praised as a mark of virtue (019-022). The concept also carries a cautionary note: one character is described as given to "devotion to women, or to drinking," a misplaced devotion that leads away from virtue (023-164).
