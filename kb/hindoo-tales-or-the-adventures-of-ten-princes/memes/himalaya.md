# Himâlaya

The Himâlaya mountains are the great northern range of India, serving in
the *Daśakumāracarita* as a destination for pilgrimage, a dwelling place
of holy sages, and the setting for events of supernatural consequence. The
range encompasses the peak of [[kailasa|Kailâsa]], the abode of [[kuvera|Kuvera]].
(026-083) (005-050)

## Description

The Himâlaya was a place of religious retreat and ascetic practice. King
[[darpasara|Darpasâra]], having been appointed joint regent alongside his cousins,
departed on a pilgrimage to the Himâlaya Mountains, entrusting the
administration of the kingdom to [[charmavarma|Charmavarma]] and [[dâruvarma|Dâruvarma]]. (005-050)

> "the old king abdicated in favour of his son Darpasâra, who is now gone
> on a pilgrimage to the Himâlaya Mountains."
> (005-050)

The mountains were also the site of supernatural encounters. At a
Himâlayan lake, a pearl necklace broke and its jewels fell upon the head
of a holy rishi who was bathing in the clear water. The rishi's curse
transformed the offender into unconscious metal — a pivotal event with
lasting narrative consequences. (007-108)

> "the pearls fell on the grey head of a very holy rishi, bathing, in the
> clear water of a Himâlayan lake."
> (007-108)

The range is associated with [[kailasa|Kailâsa]], a peak identified as part of the
Himâlaya chain and the dwelling place of [[kuvera]], the god of wealth.
(026-083)

## Significance in the Narrative

The Himâlaya serves as a liminal space in the *Daśakumāracarita* — a
region where worldly power recedes and divine or supernatural forces
become active. [[darpasara|Darpasâra]]'s pilgrimage there represents a retreat from
political life, while the curse at the Himâlayan lake demonstrates the
danger of offending holy figures in sacred spaces. (005-050) (007-108)

> "In his anger, he cursed me, saying: 'O wicked one, for this offence you
> are condemned to be changed into a piece of unconscious metal.'"
> (007-108)

The mountain's inclusion in the proper names index of Chapter 26
(026-083) confirms its recognized status among the significant places
of the narrative world, alongside [[kailasa|Kailâsa]], its famous peak.

## Appearances

The Himâlaya is referenced in Chapters 5 (005-050), 7 (007-108), and
the proper names index in Chapter 26 (026-083). Its role spans both
the political narrative (as a pilgrimage destination) and the
supernatural (as the site of a transformative curse).
