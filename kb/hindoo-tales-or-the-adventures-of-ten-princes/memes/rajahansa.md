# Râjahansa

Râjahansa was the King of [[magadha|Magadha]] and the father of [[Râjavâhana]], the hero of the
*Daśakumāracarita*. As the sovereign ruler, his reign and the companions he gathered
around his son set the entire narrative in motion.
(001-089)

## Biography

Râjahansa was the sovereign of the city and country of [[magadha|Magadha]], whose armies were
formidable and whose reign was prosperous.
(001-089)

> "The sovereign of this city and country was Râjahansa, whose armies..."
> (001-089)

After reigning for some years, war broke out between Râjahansa and a rival kingdom.
At last, Râjahansa was victorious and the enemy was completely defeated.
(001-119) (001-129)

Not long after, Râjahansa went to bathe at a holy place, which was a customary
practice for kings seeking spiritual merit.
(002-082)

Râjahansa was exceedingly [[pleasure|delight]]ed at seeing his son [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] grow up surrounded
by worthy companions.
(003-026)

> "Your wish is fulfilled," a sage told Râjahansa, seeing the prince surrounded
> by such companions.
> (003-031)

Râjahansa married [[vasumati|Vasumati]], a dear friend of the queen, who became the mother of
his heir.
(012-017)

## Role in the Narrative

Râjahansa serves as the anchor figure of the story. The entire narrative of the
ten princes unfolds as the adventures of his son [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] and the nine companions
gathered at his court. His reign provides the political backdrop, and his decision
to entrust his son to the care of wise ministers sets the plot in motion.

The king's court was served by ministers including [[dharmapala|Dharmapâla]] and [[padmodbhava|Padmodbhava]],
whose sons became some of the companions.
(026-075)

> "Râjahansa, king of Magadha, father of Râjavâhana, the hero of the narrative."
> (026-092)

According to the index of characters, Râjahansa was eventually conquered by
[[manasara|Mânasâra]], King of [[malwa|Mâlwa]].
(026-108)

## Relationships

Râjahansa was the father of [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]], the leader of the ten princes. His
ministers included [[dharmapala|Dharmapâla]] and [[padmodbhava|Padmodbhava]], whose sons were among the nine
companions of the prince. [[arthapala|Arthapâla]], the son of his minister [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]], was
also counted among the nine companions.
(026-047) (026-075)

He was married to [[vasumati|Vasumati]], who was a close friend of the queen.
(012-017)
