# Gomini

Gomini is one of four women — alongside [[dhumini|Dhumini]],
[[ratnavati|Ratnavati]], and [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]] — cited in
Chapter 18 as examples of cunning, whose stories illustrate feminine
wiles.

## Biography

Gomini is introduced in a list of cunning women in Chapter 18.
(018-161)

> "Cunning. Dhumini, Gomini, Ratnavati, and Nitambavati," I added, "are
> examples of what I have said."
> (018-161)

When asked to tell more about Gomini, the narrator recounted the story of a
young brahman of great wealth from the country of the [[dravidas|Dravidas]]. The
brahman, who had not married as a boy, resolved to travel about and find a
suitable wife for himself rather than letting his friends choose for him.
He set out alone, carrying a small bag of rice, and would test potential
brides by asking if they could make him a good dinner from it. After many
rejections, he eventually found a suitable girl — a maiden whose parents
had fallen into poverty — and was pleased by her appearance and qualities.
(019-049) (019-076)

## Role in the Narrative

Gomini serves as one of the illustrative examples of cunning women in the
didactic frame of Chapters 18 and 19, which explore the theme of feminine
wiles through embedded tales.
