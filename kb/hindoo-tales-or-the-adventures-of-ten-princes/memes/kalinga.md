# Kalinga

Kalinga is a kingdom in eastern India, ruled by a king whose daughter,
Kanakalekhâ, was taken as wife by Prince [[mantragupta]]. The kingdom is the
setting for one of the boldest exploits in the *Daśakumāracarita*, involving
capture, disguise, and a strategic marriage.
(021-077) (021-157)

## Description

Kalinga was a sovereign kingdom with its own royal line. Its king possessed a
daughter of marriageable age, which drew the attention of the wandering princes
seeking to establish themselves through alliance and conquest. The kingdom lay
near a public cemetery — a detail that proves crucial to [[mantragupta|Mantragupta]]'s stratagem.
(021-047)

> "Kalinga, and very near a public cemetery."
> (021-047)

The King of Kalinga became the target of [[mantragupta|Mantragupta]]'s political and military
manoeuvring. Through a combination of daring and deception, [[mantragupta|Mantragupta]]
succeeded in capturing the king — a feat that demonstrated the prince's
resourcefulness and established his power in the eastern territories.
(021-157)

> "King of Kalinga, and having taken him prisoner."
> (021-157)

The kingdom's princess, Kanakalekhâ, was brought into the narrative through
[[mantragupta|Mantragupta]]'s designs. Her marriage to the prince sealed the alliance between
the conqueror and the conquered, integrating Kalinga into the network of
kingdoms that the ten princes came to dominate.
(021-077) (026-091)

> "King of Kalinga, and bring here his daughter."
> (021-077)

## Significance in the Narrative

Kalinga represents one of the eastern kingdoms that the princes subdued or
allied with during their wanderings. Unlike [[magadha|Magadha]] or
[[malwa|Mâlwa]], which are central to the main plot, Kalinga appears as a
target of opportunity — a kingdom that [[mantragupta|Mantragupta]] identified and successfully
brought under his influence through a combination of military action and
marriage alliance.
(021-077) (021-157)

The capture of the King of Kalinga is one of the narrative's demonstrations of
princely prowess. Where other princes used diplomacy, disguise, or supernatural
aid, [[mantragupta|Mantragupta]]'s approach to Kalinga was direct and martial, showcasing the
diversity of methods by which the ten princes extended their influence across
India.
(021-157)

> "Kanakalekha, daughter of the King of Kalinga, wife of Mantragupta."
> (026-091)
