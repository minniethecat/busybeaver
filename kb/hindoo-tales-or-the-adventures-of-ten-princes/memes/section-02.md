[002-001]After a short time, more of those who had escaped joined the king; and

[002-002]when he was sufficiently recovered, the charming Vasumati, instructed

[002-003]by the ministers, said to him, "All your dominions are lost except

[002-004]this fortress; but such is the power of fate; prosperity, like a

[002-005]bubble on the water, or a flash of lightning, appears and disappears

[002-006]in a moment. Former kings, Râmachandra and others, at least as great

[002-007]as yourself, were deprived of their kingdoms, and suffered for a long

[002-008]time the hardships of adversity; yet, through patience and

[002-009]perseverance and the will of fate, they were at last restored to all

[002-010]their former splendour. Do you therefore imitate them, and, laying

[002-011]aside all anxiety, devote yourself to prayer and meditation."

[002-012]To this advice the king gave ear, and went to consult a very

[002-013]celebrated rishi, Vâmadeva, intending, under his directions, to engage

[002-014]in such penance as might lead to the accomplishment of his wishes.

[002-015]Having been well received by the holy man, he said to him: "O father,

[002-016]having heard of your great piety and wisdom, I have come hither for

[002-017]guidance and help in a great calamity. Mânasâra, King of Mâlwa, has

[002-018]overcome me, and now holds the kingdom which ought to be mine. I will

[002-019]shrink from no penance which you shall advise, if by such means I may

[002-020]obtain the favour of the gods, and be restored to my former power."

[002-021]Vâmadeva, well acquainted with all past, present, and future events,

[002-022]thus answered him: "O friend, there is no need of penance in your

[002-023]case; only wait patiently; a son will certainly be born to you who

[002-024]will crush all your enemies and restore your fortunes." Then a voice

[002-025]was heard in the air, saying, "This is true."

[002-026]The king, fully believing the prophecy of the muni, thus miraculously

[002-027]confirmed, returned to the forest, resolved to await patiently the

[002-028]fulfilment of the promise; and shortly afterwards the queen brought

[002-029]forth a son possessing all good marks,[2] to whom his father gave the

[002-030]name of Râjavâhana.

[002-031]About the same time also sons were born to his four ministers. They

[002-032]were named severally Pramati, Mitragupta, Mantragupta, and Visruta,

[002-033]and were brought up together with the young prince.

[002-034]Some time after the birth of these children, a certain muni brought a

[002-035]very beautiful boy to the king, and said: "Having gone lately into the

[002-036]forest to collect kusa-grass[3] and fuel, I met a woman, evidently in

[002-037]great distress. When I questioned her, she wiped away her tears, and

[002-038]told me, with a voice broken by sobs, that she was a servant of

[002-039]Prahâravarma, King of Mithila--that he, with his family, had gone to

[002-040]Pushpapuri, to be present at the Simanta festival of the queen, and

[002-041]had stayed there some time after the departure of the other guests;

[002-042]that at that time the King of Mâlwa, furnished with a magic weapon,

[002-043]had invaded the country; that in the battle which ensued, Prahâravarma

[002-044]had assisted his friend with the few soldiers who accompanied him, and

[002-045]had been taken prisoner, but had been liberated by the conqueror; that

[002-046]on his return he had been attacked in the forest by Bheels, and had

[002-047]repulsed them with difficulty. 'I and my daughter,' she continued,

[002-048]'who had charge of the king's twin children, were separated from the

[002-049]rest in the confusion, and lost our way in the forest. There we

[002-050]suddenly came upon a tiger. In my fright, I stumbled and fell, and

[002-051]dropped the child, which I was carrying, on the carcase of a cow with

[002-052]which the tiger had been engaged. At that moment an arrow struck and

[002-053]killed the tiger. I fainted away, and when I recovered, I found myself

[002-054]quite alone; my daughter had disappeared, and the child, as I suppose,

[002-055]was carried off by the Bheels, who shot the beast. After a time I was

[002-056]found by a compassionate cowherd, who took care of me till my wounds

[002-057]were healed; and I am now wandering about in the hope of finding the

[002-058]boy, and of hearing some tidings of my daughter and the other child.'

[002-059]After giving me this account, she went on her way again, and I,

[002-060]distressed that the son of your majesty's friend should be in such

[002-061]hands, determined to set out in search of him.

[002-062]"After some days I came to a small temple of Durgâ, where a party of

[002-063]Bheels were about to make the child an offering to the goddess, in

[002-064]the hope of obtaining success through her favour; and they were then

[002-065]deliberating in what manner they should kill him, whether by hanging

[002-066]him on the branch of a tree and cutting him to pieces with swords, or

[002-067]by partly burying him in the ground and shooting at him with arrows,

[002-068]or by worrying him with young dogs.

[002-069]"Then I went up to them very humbly, and said: 'O Kirâtas, I am an old

[002-070]brahman; having lost my way in the forest, I laid down my child whom I

[002-071]was carrying, while I went away for a moment to try to find an opening

[002-072]out of the dense thicket; when I came back he was gone. I have been

[002-073]searching for him ever since; have you seen him?' 'Is this your

[002-074]child?' said they. 'O yes!' I exclaimed. 'Take him, then,' they

[002-075]replied; 'we respect a brahman.' Thus I got possession of the boy,

[002-076]and, blessing them for their kindness, took him away as quickly as

[002-077]possible, and have now brought him here, thinking he will be best

[002-078]under your majesty's protection."

[002-079]The king, though grieved at the calamity of his friend, rejoiced that

[002-080]the child was saved from such a death; and giving him the name of

[002-081]Upahâravarma, had him brought up as his own son.

[002-082]Not long after this, Râjahansa went to bathe at a holy place, and in

[002-083]returning, as he passed by a group of Chandâlas, he observed a woman

[002-084]carrying a very beautiful boy. Being struck by the appearance of the

[002-085]child, he said "Where did you get this beautiful boy, who is like a

[002-086]king's son? Surely he is not your own child! pray tell me."

[002-087]She answered: "When the Bheels attacked and plundered the King of

[002-088]Mithila near our village, this child was picked up and brought to me

[002-089]by my husband, and I have taken care of him ever since."

[002-090]The king being convinced that this was the other child of his friend,

[002-091]the King of Mithila, by fair words and gifts induced the woman to give

[002-092]him up, and took him to the queen, giving him the name of

[002-093]Apahâravarma, and begging her to bring him up with her own son.

[002-094]Soon afterwards, a disciple of Vâmadeva brought a beautiful boy to the

[002-095]king, and said "As I was returning from a pilgrimage to Râmatirtha, I

[002-096]saw an old woman carrying this child, and asked her how she came to be

[002-097]wandering there. In answer to my questions, she told me her story,

[002-098]saying, 'I was the servant of a rich man, named Kâlagupta, living in

[002-099]the island of Kâlayavana, and I waited on his daughter Suvritta. One

[002-100]day a young merchant, named Ratnodbhava, son of a minister of the

[002-101]King of Magadha, arrived in the island, and having become acquainted

[002-102]with my master, he married his beautiful daughter.

[002-103]"'After some time, he was desirous of visiting his family, and being

[002-104]unwilling to leave behind his young wife, who was then not far from

[002-105]childbirth, he took her with him, and me as her nurse.

[002-106]"'We embarked on board a ship, and had at first a favourable voyage;

[002-107]but when approaching the land, we were overtaken by a storm, and a

[002-108]great wave broke over the ship, which went down almost immediately. I

[002-109]found myself in the water near my young mistress, and managed to

[002-110]support her till we got hold of a plank, by means of which we at last

[002-111]reached the shore. Whether my master was saved or not I do not know,

[002-112]but I fear that he perished with the rest of those on board, whom we

[002-113]never saw again.

[002-114]"'The coast where we landed appeared to be uninhabited, and the poor

[002-115]lady, being unable to walk far, after much suffering of mind and body,

[002-116]gave birth to this child under a tree in the forest. I have just left

[002-117]her, in the hope of finding some village where I may obtain

[002-118]assistance; and by her wish I have brought the child with me, since

[002-119]she is incapable of taking care of it.'

[002-120]"The woman had hardly finished speaking when a wild elephant, breaking

[002-121]through the bushes, came suddenly upon us, and she was so frightened

[002-122]that she let the child fall, and ran away.

[002-123]"I hid myself behind a tree, and saw the elephant take up the child

[002-124]with his trunk, as if about to put it into its mouth. At that moment

[002-125]he was attacked by a lion, and let the child fall. When the two beasts

[002-126]had moved from the spot, I came from my hiding-place just in time to

[002-127]see the child taken up by a monkey, who ran up a high tree. Presently

[002-128]the beast let the child drop, and as it fell on a leafy branch, I took

[002-129]it up uninjured by the fall, or the other rough treatment which it had

[002-130]received.

[002-131]"After searching for the woman some time in vain, I took the child to

[002-132]my master, the great muni Vâmadeva, and I have now brought it to you

[002-133]by his command."

[002-134]The king, astonished at the preservation of the child under such

[002-135]adverse circumstances, and hoping that Ratnodbhava might have escaped

[002-136]from the shipwreck, sent for Susruta to take charge of his brother's

[002-137]child, to whom he gave the name of Pushpodbhava.

[002-138]Some days after this the queen went up to her husband with a child in

[002-139]her arms, and told him, when he expressed his surprise "Last night I

[002-140]was suddenly awakened from sleep and saw a beautiful lady standing

[002-141]before me, holding this child. She said to me: 'O queen, I am a

[002-142]Yaksha, daughter of Manibhadra, and wife of Kâmapâla, the son of your

[002-143]husband's late minister, Dharmapâla; by command of Kuvera, I have

[002-144]brought this my child to you, that he may enter the service of your

[002-145]son, who is destined to become a mighty monarch.'

[002-146]"I was too much astonished to ask her any question, and she, having

[002-147]laid down the child near me, disappeared."

[002-148]The king, greatly surprised, especially that Kâmapâla should have

[002-149]married a Yaksha, sent for the child's uncle, Sumittra, and committed

[002-150]the boy to his care, giving him the name of Arthapâla.

[002-151]Not long after this another disciple of Vâmadeva brought a very

[002-152]beautiful child to the king, and said: "My lord, I have lately been on

[002-153]a pilgrimage to several holy places, and on my way back, happening to

[002-154]be on the bank of the river Kâvari, I saw a woman carrying this child,

[002-155]and evidently in great distress. On being questioned by me, she wiped

[002-156]away her tears, and with difficulty told me her story, saying, 'O

[002-157]brahman, Satyavarma, the youngest son of Sitavarma, a minister of the

[002-158]King of Magadha, after travelling about a long time, visiting all holy

[002-159]places as a pilgrim, came to this country, and here married a

[002-160]Brahman's daughter, named Kâli. Having no children by her, he took as

[002-161]his second wife her sister Gaurí, and by her he had one son, this

[002-162]child.

[002-163]"'Then the first wife, envious of her sister, determined to destroy

[002-164]the child; and having, with some false pretence, enticed me, when I

[002-165]was carrying the child, to the bank of the river, she pushed us in. I

[002-166]contrived to hold my charge with one hand, and to swim with the other

[002-167]till I met with an uprooted tree carried down by the rapid current. To

[002-168]this I clung, and after floating a long distance, was able at last to

[002-169]land at this place; but in getting away from the tree I disturbed a

[002-170]black serpent which had taken refuge there, and having been bitten by

[002-171]it, I now feel that I am dying.' As she spoke, the poison began to

[002-172]take greater effect, and she fell on the ground.
