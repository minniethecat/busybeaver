# Râjavâhana

Râjavâhana is the hero of the *Daśakumāracarita* and the central figure around whom
the tales of the [[Ten Princes|adventure]] revolve. Son of King [[Râjahansa]] of
[[Magadha]], he leads his nine companions through a series of adventures across
ancient India. His name — meaning "the prince whose vehicle is a king" — befits
his role as the protagonist of the narrative.
(002-030)

## Biography

Râjavâhana was born to [[rajahansa|King Râjahansa]] of [[magadha|Magadha]], a ruler renowned for his piety
and the prosperity of his kingdom. At his birth, the king gave him a name befitting
a prince "possessing all good marks," and his arrival was celebrated throughout
the realm.
(002-030)

The prince was raised alongside the sons of his father's four ministers — [[pramati|Pramati]],
[[mitragupta|Mitragupta]], [[mantragupta|Mantragupta]], and [[visruta|Visruta]] — who would become his lifelong companions.
Together with other young nobles, they formed the brotherhood of the ten princes
whose collective adventures constitute the narrative.
(003-016)

During his wanderings, Râjavâhana descended into [[patala|Pâtâla]], the netherworld, where he
was received with great honour by the brahman [[matanga|Matanga]] and his bride [[kalindi|Kalindí]]. There
he witnessed wonders beyond the mortal realm, and at his departure [[kalindi|Kalindí]] gifted
him a magic jewel that protected its bearer from hunger, thirst, fatigue, and all
bodily discomforts.
(003-154)

After emerging from [[patala|Pâtâla]], Râjavâhana found himself separated from his companions.
Wandering alone, he came to a great park outside a city where a festive crowd had
gathered — a turning point that would reunite him with his friends and lead him
to his destined bride.
(003-173)

> "Râjavâhana was treated with great respect and kindness by Matanga and his bride;
> but after seeing all the wonders of the place, his curiosity was satisfied, and
> he was desirous of returning to the upper world."
> (003-154)

## Role in the Narrative

Râjavâhana serves as both protagonist and frame narrator of the *Daśakumāracarita*.
After the princes are separated by fate, each undergoes his own adventures; it is
Râjavâhana who, after the re[[marriage-in-the-tales|union]], hears each companion's tale and in turn recounts
his own.

His martial prowess is noted early in the text — he is described as one who
"[[pleasure|delight]]ed in valour," and upon hearing [[somadatta|Somadatta]]'s story, he showed keen
appreciation for deeds of courage and skill.
(004-102)

> "Râjavâhana, who delighted in valour, having heard Somadatta's story..."
> (004-102)

At [[avanti|Avanti]], during the season of spring, Râjavâhana encountered the princess
[[Avantisundari]] at the [[festival-of-kama|great festival]] of [[Kâma]]. The meeting sparked an
immediate and profound love — Râjavâhana believed their connection must have
been forged in a former existence, and pondered whether a curse had once
separated them.
(006-011)

> "Then Râjavâhana, more deeply in love even than the princess, thought to
> himself, 'There surely must be some reason for this very sudden attraction
> which I feel towards her. She must have been my beloved wife in a former
> existence.'"
> (006-011)

To win the princess's heart, Râjavâhana employed wit and eloquence, telling
her a clever story that revealed his feelings and identity. His natural bravery
and the protection of prior blessings saw him through many dangers as he
pursued their [[marriage-in-the-tales|union]].
(006-021) (007-042)

> "Râjavâhana, naturally brave, and encouraged by belief in that former
> existence..."
> (007-042)

The conjuror [[vidyeswara|Vidyeswara]] ultimately orchestrated the royal marriage that united
Râjavâhana with [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]], sealing the central love story of the epic.
(026-160)

Throughout the narrative, Râjavâhana listens attentively to the adventures of
each companion — [[Somadatta]], [[Pushpodbhava]], [[Dhanamittra]], and others —
offering comment and praise. His role as the sympathetic audience mirrors the
reader's own journey through the tales.
(012-001)

> "Râjavâhana, having heard this story, said 'You have indeed shown...'"
> (012-001)

## Relationships

- **Father:** [[Râjahansa]], King of [[magadha|Magadha]] (026-131)
- **Wife:** [[Avantisundari]], daughter of [[manasara|Mânasâra]] (026-053)
- **Companions:** [[Somadatta]], [[Pushpodbhava]], [[Dhanamittra]], [[pramati|Pramati]],
  [[mitragupta|Mitragupta]], [[mantragupta|Mantragupta]], [[visruta|Visruta]], and others — the nine princes (026-116)
- **Guide in [[patala|Pâtâla]]:** [[matanga|Matanga]], a learned brahman (026-114)
- **Benefactor:** [[kalindi|Kalindí]], who gifted him a protective magic jewel
- **[[marriage-in-the-tales|Marriage]] facilitator:** [[vidyeswara|Vidyeswara]], the conjuror (026-160)
