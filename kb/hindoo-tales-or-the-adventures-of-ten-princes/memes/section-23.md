[023-001]"There was formerly a King of Vidarba remarkable for wisdom and

[023-002]justice, learned in the Scriptures, a protector of his subjects (by

[023-003]whom he was much beloved), a terror to his enemies, wise in political

[023-004]science, upright and honest in all his actions, kind to his

[023-005]dependents, grateful for even small services, and gracious to all.

[023-006]Having lived the full age of man, he died, leaving a prosperous

[023-007]kingdom to his son Anantavarma, a young man of great abilities, but

[023-008]caring more for the mechanical arts, music, and poetry, than for his

[023-009]duties as a ruler.

[023-010]"One day, one of his father's old counsellors in private addressed him

[023-011]thus: 'Sire, your majesty, with the advantage of royal birth, has

[023-012]almost every good quality that can be desired; your intelligence is

[023-013]very great; your knowledge superior to that of others; but all this,

[023-014]without instruction in political science and attention to public

[023-015]affairs, is insufficient for a king; void of such knowledge, he is

[023-016]despised, not only by foreigners, but by his own subjects, who,

[023-017]disregarding all laws, human and divine, at last perish miserably, and

[023-018]drag down their sovereign in their fall. A king who has not political

[023-019]wisdom, however good his eyesight may be, is regarded by the wise as a

[023-020]blind man, unable to see things as they are. I entreat you, therefore,

[023-021]to give up the pursuits to which you are so devoted, and to study the

[023-022]art of government. Your power will then be strengthened, and you may

[023-023]long reign over a happy and prosperous people.'

[023-024]"To this exhortation the young king appeared to listen attentively;

[023-025]and said: 'Such is the teaching of the wise; it ought to be followed.'

[023-026]"After dismissing the old counsellor, the king went into the women's

[023-027]apartments, and began to talk to them of the exhortation which he had

[023-028]just received. His observations were attentively listened to by one of

[023-029]his constant attendants, who determined, if possible, to turn the

[023-030]king's thoughts in another direction, and prevent him from being

[023-031]influenced by the good advice which had been given. This man had many

[023-032]accomplishments; he was skilled in dancing, music, and singing; quick

[023-033]at repartee; a good story-teller; full of fun and jokes; but devoid of

[023-034]honour and honesty; false, slanderous, a receiver of bribes, a bad man

[023-035]in every way; yet, from his wit and humour, very acceptable to the

[023-036]king, whom he now thus addressed: 'Wherever there is a person of

[023-037]exalted position, there are always clever rogues ready to prey upon

[023-038]him, and, while degrading him, to accomplish their own base purposes.

[023-039]Some, under the guise of religion, will tell him: "The happiness of

[023-040]this world is shortlived and fleeting; eternal happiness can only be

[023-041]obtained by prayer and penance;" and so they persuade him to shave

[023-042]his head, wear a dress of skins, gird himself with a rope of sacred

[023-043]grass, and, renouncing all pleasures and luxuries, to betake himself

[023-044]to fasting and penance, and give away his riches to the poor, meaning,

[023-045]of course, themselves; some of these religious impostors will even

[023-046]persuade their dupes to renounce children, wife--nay, even life

[023-047]itself.

[023-048]"'But suppose a man to have too much sense to be deluded in this way,

[023-049]they will try a different plan; to one they will say: "We can make

[023-050]gold; only furnish us with the means, and your riches shall be

[023-051]increased a thousandfold;" to another: "We can show you how to destroy

[023-052]all your enemies without a weapon;" to another: "Follow our advice,

[023-053]and, though you are nobody now, you shall soon become a great man."

[023-054]"'If their victim is a sovereign, they will say to him: "Four

[023-055]branches of study are said to be proper for kings--the vedas, the

[023-056]purânas, metaphysics, and political science;--but the first three are

[023-057]of very little advantage; they may safely be neglected, and he should

[023-058]give up his mind to the last only. Are there not the six thousand

[023-059]verses composed for the use of kings, and containing the whole

[023-060]science? Learn these by heart, and you will be prepared for all

[023-061]emergencies." So then he must set to work to learn all these crabbed

[023-062]rules. He must; according to them, distrust every one, even wife or

[023-063]son. He must rise early, take a very scanty meal, and immediately

[023-064]proceed to business.

[023-065]"'First he must go over accounts, and balance income and expenditure;

[023-066]and while his rascally ministers pretend to have everything very

[023-067]exact, they have forty thousand ways of cheating him, and take good

[023-068]care of themselves.

[023-069]"'Then he must sit in public, and be tired to death with receiving

[023-070]frivolous complaints and petitions, and will not even have the

[023-071]satisfaction of doing justice; for, whether a cause be just or not,

[023-072]his ministers will take care that the decision shall be according to

[023-073]their own interests.

[023-074]"'Then he is allowed a short time for bathing, dressing, and dining;

[023-075]if, indeed, the poor wretch can venture to dine, with the constant

[023-076]fear of poison in his mind.

[023-077]"'After this he must remain a long time in council with his ministers,

[023-078]perplexed with their conflicting arguments, and unable to understand

[023-079]even the half of them; while they, pretending to act impartially, get

[023-080]everything settled as they had previously agreed and by twisting and

[023-081]distorting the reports of spies and emissaries, manage to serve

[023-082]themselves and their friends, and to get credit for putting down

[023-083]disturbances which they themselves had excited.

[023-084]"'He is now allowed to take a little amusement, but the time for this

[023-085]is restricted to an hour and a half.

[023-086]"'Then he must review his army; hear the reports of the commander of

[023-087]his forces; give orders for peace or war; and act upon the accounts

[023-088]brought by spies and emissaries.

[023-089]"'However weary he maybe with all this, he must sit down and read

[023-090]diligently, like some poor student, for several hours. Then at last he

[023-091]may retire to rest; but before he has had half enough sleep, he will

[023-092]be awaked in the early morning; and the priests will come to him, and

[023-093]say: "There is an unfavourable conjunction of the planets; evil omens

[023-094]have appeared; there is danger impending; the gods must be

[023-095]propitiated; let a great sacrifice be made to-day. The brahmans are

[023-096]continually engaged in supplicating the gods on your behalf; your

[023-097]prosperity is dependent on their prayers; they are miserably poor, and

[023-098]have many children to support; let large donations be made." Thus the

[023-099]greedy wretches, under the pretence of religion, are continually

[023-100]robbing the king and enriching themselves.

[023-101]"'This is the sort of life which you will have to lead, if you give

[023-102]yourself up to the guidance of those greybeards; and, after all,

[023-103]though you may have studied and studied, pored over their musty

[023-104]volumes, and listened to their tedious lectures, you are not sure of

[023-105]doing right.

[023-106]"'And who are these fellows who set themselves up for wise men? Do

[023-107]they always do right? Are they not often themselves cheated by the

[023-108]unlearned? Common sense is far better than all this learning; instinct

[023-109]and feeling will guide us in the right way; even an infant without

[023-110]teaching finds out how to draw nourishment from the mother's breast.

[023-111]Cast aside, then, the rules and restrictions with which these old

[023-112]fools would bind you. Follow your natural inclinations, and enjoy life

[023-113]while you can. You possess youth, beauty, and strength. You have a

[023-114]large army, ten thousand elephants, and three hundred thousand horses;

[023-115]your treasury is full of gold and jewels, and would not be emptied in

[023-116]a thousand years. What more would you have? Life is short, and those

[023-117]who are always thinking of adding to their possessions, go on toiling

[023-118]to the last, and never really enjoy them.

[023-119]"'But why should I waste your time with needless arguments? I see you

[023-120]are already convinced. Commit, then, the cares of government to your

[023-121]ministers; spend your time with your ladies, and congenial friends

[023-122]like me; enjoy drinking, music, and dancing, and trouble yourself no

[023-123]more with affairs of state.'

[023-124]"Having thus spoken, he prostrated himself in very humble attitude at

[023-125]the feet of his master, who remained for a time silent, as if

[023-126]undecided.

[023-127]"The women, who had been listening with delight to all that was said,

[023-128]seeing his hesitation, assembled round him, and, with sweet words and

[023-129]caresses, easily persuaded him to follow his own inclination and

[023-130]theirs.

[023-131]"From that time the young king, given up entirely to pleasures and

[023-132]amusements, left the affairs of the kingdom to his ministers; and,

[023-133]while allowing them to manage as they pleased, provided they did not

[023-134]trouble him, openly treated them with insolence and neglect, and even

[023-135]took pleasure in hearing them ridiculed by the worthless parasites who

[023-136]surrounded him, so that even the wisest of his ministers, while

[023-137]lamenting the sad state of affairs, could only acknowledge their

[023-138]inability to remedy it, and wait till some great public calamity, or

[023-139]the invasion of the country by a neighbouring sovereign, who was

[023-140]gradually extending his dominions by force or cunning, should bring

[023-141]the young king to his senses.

[023-142]"Ere long, what they had expected came to pass; for the King of

[023-143]Asmaka, who had for some time coveted the country, but did not dare

[023-144]openly to invade it while it was strong and prosperous, took measures

[023-145]in secret to weaken the authority of Anantavarma, and diminish his

[023-146]resources; and, lest he should perchance see the error of his ways and

[023-147]abandon his vicious courses, he secretly gave a commission to the son

[023-148]of one of his ministers, a young man of great abilities and agreeable

[023-149]manners, an eloquent flatterer and amusing companion, who arrived at

[023-150]the court of Anantavarma, attended by a numerous retinue, as if

[023-151]travelling about for his own pleasure.

[023-152]"This man soon became intimate with the king, and took care to fall in

[023-153]with all his tastes, and to justify and praise every pursuit which he

[023-154]engaged in.

[023-155]"Thus, if he saw the king fond of hunting, he would say: 'What a fine

[023-156]manly sport this is! How it strengthens the body, braces the spirits,

[023-157]and quickens the intelligence! While roaming over hill and dale, you

[023-158]become acquainted with the country; by destroying the deer and wild

[023-159]buffaloes, you benefit the husbandmen; by killing the tigers and other

[023-160]wild beasts, you make travelling safer.' And he would go on in this

[023-161]way, without any allusion to the damage and destruction caused by the

[023-162]king's hunting expeditions.

[023-163]"If gambling was the favourite amusement, or there was excessive

[023-164]devotion to women, or to drinking, he would very ingeniously bring

[023-165]forward everything that could be said in favour of them, passing over

[023-166]their disadvantages in silence. If the king was lavish to his

[023-167]dependants, he would praise his generosity; if cruel, he would say:

[023-168]'Such severity is good; you maintain your own dignity by it; a king

[023-169]ought not to be like a patient devotee, submitting to insults, and

[023-170]ready to forgive.

[023-171]"In this manner that wicked wretch obtained great influence over the

[023-172]king, and employed it to lead him into all sorts of excesses.

[023-173]"With such an example before them, all classes gradually became

[023-174]corrupted. The magistrates neglected their duties, and thought only

[023-175]how they might enrich themselves; great criminals, who could bribe,

[023-176]escaped with impunity; the weak were oppressed by the strong; violence

[023-177]and robbery were rampant; disturbances broke out on all sides; and

[023-178]severe and indiscriminating punishments only stirred up indignation,

[023-179]without repressing crime. The revenue diminished, while expenditure

[023-180]was increasing; everywhere loud complaints were heard, and great

[023-181]distress prevailed.

[023-182]"As if all this were not sufficient, the cruel King of Asmaka sent

[023-183]emissaries in all directions to mix unsuspectedly with the inhabitants

[023-184]of Vidarba, and do as much mischief as possible.

[023-185]"Some would distribute subtle poisons in various ways; some would stir

[023-186]up quarrels between neighbouring villages, and so cause party fights;

[023-187]some contrived to let loose a furious elephant into a crowd, or get up

[023-188]an alarm by other means, and so cause a sudden panic, in which the

[023-189]people trampled down each other, and many lives were lost; others,

[023-190]disguised as hunters, promising abundance of game, would tempt men

[023-191]into some narrow valley, between high mountains, where they were

[023-192]devoured by tigers, or, unable to find their way out again, perished

[023-193]of hunger and thirst.
