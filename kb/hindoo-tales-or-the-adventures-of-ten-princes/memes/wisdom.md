# Wisdom

## Definition

Wisdom (Prajñā) is the faculty of sound judgment and prudent decision-making, highly esteemed in the ethical framework of the tales. It encompasses both intellectual discernment and practical foresight — the ability to see the consequences of one's actions before taking them. Wisdom is presented as an essential attribute of kings, ministers, and those who guide others, contrasted repeatedly with folly and rashness.

## In the Narrative

Wisdom appears throughout the tales as a marker of character quality. In Chapter 2, a visitor is drawn by reports of a character's great piety and wisdom, treating the two as companion virtues (002-016). In Chapter 8, wisdom is invoked in philosophical discourse alongside kindness, framing the wise person as one who is also compassionate (008-030).

The narrative also shows the consequences of lacking wisdom. In Chapter 9, a character is rebuked for acting without wisdom and forethought, abandoning a course of action that would have been beneficial (009-099). In Chapter 18, wisdom becomes a measure by which others judge one's conduct — failure to act wisely brings loss of esteem (018-062).

In the later chapters, wisdom is linked with governance. Chapter 22 praises a character who combines wisdom with prudence and other virtues, suggesting that sustained good rule depends on this quality (022-154). The opening of Chapter 23 introduces a king of [[vidarba|Vidarba]] who is remarkable for wisdom, setting the stage for the narrative that follows (023-001).

> "You seem to be wanting in wisdom and forethought in thus aband…" (009-099)

> "long continue to possess such wisdom and prudence, combined with w…" (022-154)
