## Biography

Dhumini is introduced in the narrative as the wife of the youngest brother. She is listed among the examples of cunning women alongside [[gomini|Gomini]], [[ratnavati|Ratnavati]], and [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]]. (018-161)

Her story is recounted as an illustration of feminine depravity. While her husband was away hunting, he discovered a man who had been cruelly treated by robbers — they had mutilated him severely. Dhumini fell in love with this poor mutilated wretch and determined to have him, demonstrating the depths of her moral corruption. (019-001) (019-008)

## Role

Dhumini serves as an example of "cunning" women in the didactic framing of the narrative. Her tale is one of several stories within Chapters 18 and 19 illustrating the theme of feminine wiles and moral depravity.
