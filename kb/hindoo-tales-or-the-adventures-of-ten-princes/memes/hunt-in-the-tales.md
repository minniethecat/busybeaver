# Hunt in the Tales

## Summary

Hunting in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes* appears in three lights: as the royal sport whose harms flatterers conceal, as the laborious outing that tests a household, and as a disguise beneath which robbers lure their victims to death.

## Context

The domestic face of the hunt is caught in a single episode: "One day her husband came home from hunting, tired and thirsty" (019-011), only to be sent out again by a wife pretending a headache. The royal face is the subject of political satire—a flatterer praises the king's love of the chase while making "no allusion to the damage and destruction caused by the king's hunting expeditions" (023-162).

> "...without any allusion to the damage and destruction caused by the king's hunting expeditions."
> (023-162)

Most ominous is the hunt as pretext. Marauders "disguised as hunters, promising abundance of game, would tempt men into some narrow valley, between high mountains, where they were devoured by tigers, or, unable to find their way out again, perished" (023-190). The sport thus shades into [[robbery-in-the-tales|Robbery]] and ambush.

## Key Participants

- The king — whose "hunting expeditions" cause harm that courtiers flatter away (023-162)
- The flattering minister — who praises the hunt as making travel safer while concealing its destruction (023-160)
- Robbers "disguised as hunters" — who lure victims to their deaths under promise of game (023-190); see [[robbery-in-the-tales|Robbery in the Tales]]
