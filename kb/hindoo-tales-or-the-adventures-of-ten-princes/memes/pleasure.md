# Pleasure

Pleasure (Sanskrit: [[kama|Kâma]], सुख) is one of the three great objects of human
pursuit — alongside virtue ([[virtue|Dharma]]) and wealth ([[wealth|Artha]]) — that form the
ethical framework of the *Daśakumāracarita*. The text explores pleasure
not as a simple indulgence but as a complex force that, when properly
understood and balanced against duty, can lead to fulfillment; when
pursued without restraint, to ruin.
(008-080)

## The Three Aims in Hindu Ethics

The *Daśakumāracarita* inherits and dramatizes the classical Hindu doctrine
of the Trivarga — the three legitimate aims of human life. A pivotal passage
in the text explicitly names and debates them:

> "wrong in reckoning virtue, wealth and pleasure as the three great..."
> (008-080)

This philosophical triad provides the ethical architecture for the entire
narrative. Each prince's adventure can be read as an exploration of how
these three aims interact: the pursuit of pleasure leads to love and marriage;
the pursuit of wealth leads to kingdoms and treasure; the pursuit of virtue
leads to honour and ultimate restoration.

> "'how far do you regard virtue as superior to...'"
> (008-082)

The text does not offer a simple answer. Rather, it dramatizes the tension
through its characters: some pursue pleasure at the expense of all else and
suffer for it; others balance the three aims and achieve lasting happiness.
This nuanced treatment elevates the tales from mere adventure stories to
a sustained ethical inquiry.

> "without virtue; but the latter is quite inde..."
> (008-087)

## Pleasure in the Narrative

### The Pleasures of Love

The central romance between [[Râjavâhana]] and [[Avantisundari]] is the
text's fullest exploration of pleasure as a motivating force. Their
encounter at the [[Festival of Kâma]] is suffused with the language of
delight: the beauty of spring, the fragrance of blossoms, the music of
celebration — all conspire to create an atmosphere in which pleasure
becomes indistinguishable from destiny.
(005-146) (006-011)

> "by equal pleasure, warmly embraced him, saying, 'O my dear friend...'"
> (003-175)

### The Pleasures of Companionship

Re[[marriage-in-the-tales|union]] is repeatedly described in terms of pleasure. When [[somadatta|Somadatta]]
recognizes [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]], both are "affected by equal pleasure," and their
embrace is the physical expression of a joy that transcends mere
happiness — it is the pleasure of restored connection, of friendship
proven true across separation and danger.
(003-174)

### The Pleasures of Place

The text lavishes descriptive attention on pleasure-gardens, parks, and
natural settings. These are not merely backdrops but active participants
in the narrative: the pleasure-grounds where lovers meet, the forests
where princes find refuge, the parks where festivals unfold. Nature
itself is portrayed as a source of legitimate pleasure.
(004-020)

> "he is staying at present near this forest to enjoy the pleasures..."
> (004-020)

## Pleasure and its Dangers

The *Daśakumāracarita* is clear-eyed about the risks of undisciplined
pleasure-seeking. [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]], whose name marks him as a devotee of desire,
is described as one who "thought only of present pleasure, frequented the
company of gamblers and harlots" — a cautionary portrait of pleasure
pursued without wisdom.
(001-113)

Yet the text is not puritanical. Pleasure, rightly ordered, is presented
as a genuine good — one of the three legitimate aims, equal in dignity
to virtue and wealth. The resolution of the epic, in which princes are
restored to their kingdoms and united with their brides, affirms pleasure
as the natural reward of virtue and the proper end of heroic endeavor.
