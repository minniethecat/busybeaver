# Poisoning in the Tales

## Summary

Poisoning is one of the most distinctive narrative events in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes*, appearing in over fifteen passages across multiple chapters. As a weapon of court intrigue, poison operates through secrecy and betrayal—the poisoner strikes from within the household, often at the king's own table.

> "As she spoke, the poison began to work, and she fell down dead."
> (002-171)

## Context

The most sustained poison plot unfolds in Chapter 15, where [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]] is accused of using poison against the king. An attendant drops "on him the poisonous serpent" (015-052), and the subsequent investigation uncovers a web of court intrigue.

> "The traitor, Kâmapâla, has not only poisoned the king, but has also employed faithful attendants to administer poison."
> (015-045)

Poison accusations carry political weight. In Chapter 14, a character is accused of having "poisoned your eldest brother" (014-155), revealing how poison serves as a tool in succession struggles. The antidote motif also appears—a character boasts "I will stop the effect of the poison in such a manner" that the victim survives (015-021).

Beyond the court, poison operates in more intimate settings. A woman falls to poison mid-speech (002-171), and a character "feels as if smeared with the poison of" love or jealousy (006-086), suggesting poison as both literal device and metaphor for emotional torment.

## Key Participants

- [[kâmapâla]] — central figure accused in the poison conspiracy of Chapter 15

- [[arthapâla]] — son of [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]], a rich man involved in the poisoning narratives

- The executioner — present at both poison and execution scenes, linking these two narrative event types

- The king — target of multiple poison plots across the tales

## Outcome

Poison plots consistently fail in their ultimate aim—the intended victims survive through antidotes, divine intervention, or the incompetence of conspirators. Yet each poison episode advances the plot by revealing hidden enmities, testing loyalties, and precipitating confrontations.

> "The effect of the poison was stopped. The executioner being also bitten..."
> (015-058)

The survival of poisoned characters reinforces the providential theme: fate protects the princes and their allies from death by treachery.
