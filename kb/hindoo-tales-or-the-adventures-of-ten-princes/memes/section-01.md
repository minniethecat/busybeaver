[001-001]HINDOO TALES

[001-002]_OR, THE ADVENTURES OF TEN PRINCES_

[001-003]FREELY TRANSLATED FROM THE SANSCRIT OF THE DASAKUMARACHARITAM

[001-004]BY P. W. JACOB

[001-005]STRAHAN & CO. 56 LUDGATE HILL, LONDON

[001-006]1873

[001-007]TRANSLATOR'S PREFACE.

[001-008]The Sanscrit work entitled "Dasakumaracharitam, or the Adventures of

[001-009]Ten Princes," though printed more than twenty-five years ago, has not,

[001-010]as far as I can ascertain, been translated into any European language.

[001-011]Many parts of it are written in such a turgid "Oriental" style, that a

[001-012]close translation would be quite unsuitable to the English reader.

[001-013]Such passages have therefore been much condensed; others, which are

[001-014]hardly decent--or, as in the speech of the parasite in the last story,

[001-015]tedious and uninteresting, have been omitted; but in general the

[001-016]original has been pretty closely adhered to, and nothing has been

[001-017]added to it.

[001-018]The exact date of the composition of the "Dasakumaracharitam" is not

[001-019]known. It is supposed to have been written about the end of the

[001-020]eleventh century, and was left unfinished by the author; but as the

[001-021]story of the last narrator is almost finished, not much could have

[001-022]been wanting to complete the work, and the reader may easily imagine

[001-023]what the conclusion would have been.

[001-024]Some of the incidents correspond with those of the "Arabian Nights,"

[001-025]but the stories on the whole are quite different from anything found

[001-026]there, and give a lively picture of Hindoo manners and morals.

[001-027]Unscrupulous deception, ready invention, extreme credulity and

[001-028]superstition, and disregard of human life, are strongly illustrated.

[001-029]The belief in the power of penance, which was supposed to confer on

[001-030]the person practising it not merely personal sanctity, but even great

[001-031]supernatural powers, was very generally entertained among the Hindoos,

[001-032]and is often alluded to here; as is also transmigration, or the birth

[001-033]of the soul after death in a new body, human or brute. Sufferings or

[001-034]misfortunes are attributed to sins committed in a former existence,

[001-035]and in more than one story two persons are supposed to recollect

[001-036]having many years before lived together as husband and wife.

[001-037]Much use also is made of the agency of supernatural beings; for

[001-038]besides numerous gods, the Hindoos believe, or at least believed, in

[001-039]the existence of innumerable beings, in some degree immortal, but

[001-040]liable to be killed even by men, swarming in the air, generally

[001-041]invisible, but sometimes assuming a human or a more terrible form;

[001-042]occasionally beneficent, but more commonly injurious to human beings.

[001-043]At the time when the original work was written, India appears to have

[001-044]been divided into a large number of small kingdoms or principalities,

[001-045]the rulers of which are here termed "Râja," a word almost adopted into

[001-046]our language, but which. I have rendered by the equivalent and more

[001-047]familiar term "King."

[001-048]The numerous uncouth names, which cannot well be shortened or

[001-049]translated, will, it is feared, cause some annoyance to the reader. As

[001-050]many as possible have been omitted, and of those which occur a list is

[001-051]given in the Appendix, together with a few terms which seemed to

[001-052]require explanation. This will save the reader the trouble of,

[001-053]referring, when a name recurs, to the place where it is first

[001-054]mentioned in order to find out to whom it belongs.

[001-055]The Appendix also contains a few pages of a very close literal

[001-056]translation, which will enable the reader to form some idea of the

[001-057]nature and style of the original, and to see how far it has been

[001-058]departed from in the preceding pages.

[001-059]P. W. J.

[001-060]GUILDFORD, _December_, 1872.

[001-061]PRONUNCIATION OF PROPER NAMES.

[001-062]The vowel _â_, is always to be pronounced as in father.

[001-063]The vowel _a_, as in America, or as u in dull, i in bird, &c.

[001-064]The vowel _e_, always as a in cake.

[001-065]The vowel _í_, as e in cede, or ee in reed.

[001-066]The vowel _i_, as in pin.

[001-067]The vowel _ú_, as in flute.

[001-068]The vowel _u_, as in bull.

[001-069]Pati is therefore pronounced putty, &c.

[001-083]LITERAL TRANSLATIONS.

[001-084]PROPER NAMES OCCURRING IN THE TALES.

[001-085]There was formerly, in the most fertile part of India, a city called

[001-086]Pushpapuri, the capital of Magadha, magnificent as a mine of jewels,

[001-087]abounding in every kind of wealth, surpassing all other cities in

[001-088]splendour and prosperity.

[001-089]The sovereign of this city and country was Râjahansa, whose armies

[001-090]were formidable with countless elephants and horses, whose glory was

[001-091]unsullied as the moon in a cloudless sky, or the plumage of the swan,

[001-092]and whose fame was sung even by celestial minstrels. Though a terror

[001-093]to his enemies, he was beloved by all his subjects, and especially by

[001-094]the learned and pious brahmans, who were continually employed in

[001-095]prayers and sacrifices to the gods, for the welfare of the king and

[001-096]his people.

[001-097]The queen Vasumati was worthy of such a husband. She was of high birth

[001-098]and of a sweet temper, and so great was her beauty that it seemed as

[001-099]if the god of love had formed her for his own special delight, by

[001-100]uniting in her single person everything that is most beautiful in the

[001-101]world.

[001-102]Among the king's counsellors were three appointed to the highest

[001-103]offices of state, men of great probity and intelligence, who had been

[001-104]long in his father's service and enjoyed his entire confidence. Their

[001-105]names were, Dharmapâla, Padmodbhava, and Sitavarma.

[001-106]The first of these had three sons, Sumantra, Sumittra, and Kâmapâla;

[001-107]the second, two, Susruta and Ratnodbhava; and the last had also two,

[001-108]Sumati and Satyavarma.

[001-109]Of these sons the last-mentioned renounced worldly cares and

[001-110]employments, devoted himself to religious meditation, and leaving home

[001-111]as a pilgrim, travelled into many countries in order to visit the holy

[001-112]places which they contained.

[001-113]Kâmapâla was of an opposite character; he thought only of present

[001-114]pleasure, frequented the company of gamblers and harlots, and roamed

[001-115]about the world seeking amusement and dissipation.

[001-116]Ratnodbhava became a merchant, and in the way of traffic made many

[001-117]long journeys by land and sea. The other sons, after their fathers'

[001-118]death, succeeded to their offices, according to the custom of the

[001-119]country. When Râjahansa had reigned some years, war broke out between

[001-120]him and the king of the adjoining country of Mâlwa, the haughty and

[001-121]ambitious Mânasâra, whom he marched to encounter with a numerous army,

[001-122]making the earth tremble with the tread of his elephants, and

[001-123]disturbing even the dwellers in the sky with the clang of kettledrums

[001-124]louder than the roar of the stormy ocean.

[001-125]Both armies were animated by equal rage, and terrible was the battle;

[001-126]the ground where they met was first turned to dust by the wheels of

[001-127]the chariots and the trampling of men and beasts, and then into mud

[001-128]through the streams of blood which flowed from the slain and wounded.

[001-129]At last Râjahansa was victorious, the enemy was completely defeated,

[001-130]their king taken prisoner, and all Mâlwa lay open to the conqueror.

[001-131]He, however, having no wish to enlarge his dominions, released his

[001-132]prisoner on very easy terms, and returning to Pushpapuri, thought only

[001-133]of governing his own kingdom in peace, not expecting after such

[001-134]generous treatment any further trouble from his ambitious neighbour.

[001-135]Though prosperous and happy in every other respect, the King of

[001-136]Magadha had one great cause of sorrow and anxiety--he had no son to

[001-137]succeed him. Therefore, at this time he made many prayers and

[001-138]offerings to Nârâyana the Creator of the World, who, having been thus

[001-139]propitiated, signified to the queen in a dream that she would bear a

[001-140]son; and not long afterwards her husband was gratified by the news of

[001-141]her pregnancy.

[001-142]When the proper time arrived the king celebrated the ceremony called

[001-143]Simanta[1] with great magnificence, and invited several of the

[001-144]neighbouring kings to be present on the occasion; among them was the

[001-145]King of Mithila, with his queen, a great friend of Vasumati--to

[001-146]congratulate whom she had accompanied her husband.

[001-147]One day after this, when the king was sitting in council with his

[001-148]ministers, he was informed that a certain venerable Yati was desirous

[001-149]to see him. On his admission the king perceived that he was one of his

[001-150]secret emissaries; dismissing, therefore, the rest of the counsellors,

[001-151]he withdrew to a private apartment, followed by one or two of his most

[001-152]confidential ministers and the supposed Yati. He, bowing down to the

[001-153]ground, said in answer to the king's inquiry, "In order the better to

[001-154]perform your Majesty's commands, I have adopted this safe disguise,

[001-155]and have resided for some time in the capital of Mâlwa, from whence I

[001-156]now bring very important news. The haughty Mânasâra, brooding over his

[001-157]defeat, unmindful of your generous forbearance, and only anxious to

[001-158]wipe off his disgrace, has been for a long time endeavouring to

[001-159]propitiate with very severe penance the mighty Siva, whose temple is

[001-160]at Mahâkâla, and he has so far succeeded that the god has given him a

[001-161]magic club, very destructive of life and conducive to victory."

[001-162]"Through this weapon, and the favour of Siva, he now thinks himself a

[001-163]match for you. He has for some time been strengthening his army, and

[001-164]will probably very soon invade this country. Your Majesty having

[001-165]received this information, will decide what ought to be done."

[001-166]On hearing this report the ministers consulted together and said to

[001-167]the king, "This enemy is coming against us favoured by the gods, and

[001-168]you cannot hope to resist him; we therefore advise that you should

[001-169]avoid fighting, and retire with your family and treasure to a strong

[001-170]fortress."

[001-171]Although they urged this advice with many reasons, it was not

[001-172]acceptable to the king, who determined to march at the head of his

[001-173]army against the invaders. When, however, the enemy had actually

[001-174]entered the country, the ministers succeeded in persuading their

[001-175]master to send away the queen and her attendants, and a part of the

[001-176]treasure, to a strong fortress in the forest of Vindhya, guarded by

[001-177]veteran soldiers.

[001-178]Presently the two armies met, the battle raged furiously, and

[001-179]Mânasâra, eagerly seeking out his former conqueror, at last

[001-180]encountered his chariot. Wielding the magic club, with one blow he

[001-181]slew the charioteer and caused the king to fall down senseless.

[001-182]The horses being freed from control, suddenly turned round, dashed off

[001-183]at full speed from the field, and never stopped till, utterly

[001-184]exhausted, they had dragged the chariot with the still insensible king

[001-185]very near to the fortress to which the queen had retreated.

[001-186]Meanwhile, some of the fugitives from the battle, having reached the

[001-187]fortress, told the queen what had happened, and she, overwhelmed by

[001-188]grief at the death of her husband, determined not to survive him.

[001-189]Perceiving her purpose, the old brahmans and faithful counsellors, who

[001-190]had accompanied her, endeavoured, to dissuade her, saying, "O

[001-191]glorious lady, we have no certain information of the king's death:

[001-192]moreover, learned astrologers have declared that the child to be born

[001-193]of you is destined to become a mighty sovereign, therefore do not act

[001-194]rashly or end so precious a life while the least hope remains."

[001-195]Apparently influenced by these reasons, eloquently urged, the queen

[001-196]remained silent, and seemed to renounce her purpose, but at midnight,

[001-197]unable to sleep, and oppressed by intolerable grief, she rose up, and

[001-198]evading her sleeping attendants and the guards outside, went into the

[001-199]forest, and there, after many passionate lamentations and prayers that

[001-200]she might rejoin her beloved husband, she formed a rope by twisting a

[001-201]part of her dress, and was preparing to hang herself with it from the

[001-202]branch of a tree, very near to the place where the chariot was

[001-203]standing concealed by the thick foliage.

[001-204]Just then the king, revived by the cool night wind, recovered

[001-205]consciousness, and hearing his wife's voice, softly called her by

[001-206]name. She, hardly believing her senses for joy, cried out loudly for

[001-207]help, and soon brought to her assistance some of the attendants, who

[001-208]carried him gently into the fort, where his wounds were dressed and

[001-209]found not to be dangerous.
