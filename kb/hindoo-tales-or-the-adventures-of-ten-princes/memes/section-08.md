[008-001]Apahâravarma spoke first.

[008-002]* * * * *

[008-003]ADVENTURES OF APAHÂRAVARMA.

[008-004]My Lord, when you had gone away with the brahman, and we were unable

[008-005]to find you, I wandered about searching for you like the rest of your

[008-006]friends.

[008-007]One day I heard by chance of a very famous muni, living in a forest on

[008-008]the banks of the Ganges, not far from Champa, who was said to have

[008-009]supernatural knowledge of past and future events.

[008-010]Hoping to obtain some information about you, I determined to seek him

[008-011]out, and accordingly came here for that purpose. Having found the way

[008-012]to his dwelling, I saw there a miserable-looking man, very unlike the

[008-013]holy devotee whom I had pictured to myself. Sitting down, however,

[008-014]beside this person, I said, "I have come a long way to consult the

[008-015]celebrated rishi Mâríchi, having heard that he is possessed of very

[008-016]wonderful knowledge. Can you tell me where to find him?"

[008-017]Deeply sighing, he answered: "There was, not long ago, such a person

[008-018]in this place; but he is changed--he is no longer what he was."

[008-019]"How can that be?" I asked.

[008-020]"One day," he replied, "while that muni was engaged in prayer and

[008-021]meditation, he was interrupted by the sudden arrival of a famous

[008-022]actress and dancer, called Kâmamanjari, who, with dishevelled hair and

[008-023]eyes full of tears, threw herself at his feet.

[008-024]"Before he had time to ask the meaning of this, a confused crowd of

[008-025]her companions came up, headed by an old woman, the mother of

[008-026]Kâmamanjari, apparently in great agitation and distress.

[008-027]"When they were all a little quieted, he asked the girl the meaning of

[008-028]her tears, and for what purpose she had come to him.

[008-029]"She answered, apparently with great respect and bashfulness, 'O

[008-030]reverend sir, I have heard of your great wisdom, and your kindness to

[008-031]those who are willing to give up the pleasures of this world for the

[008-032]sake of the next. I am tired of the disgraceful life I am leading, and

[008-033]wish to renounce it.' Upon this, her mother, with her loose grey hairs

[008-034]touching the ground, interrupted her, and said, 'Worthy sir, this

[008-035]daughter of mine would make it appear that I am to blame, but indeed I

[008-036]have done my duty, and have carefully prepared her for that profession

[008-037]for which, by birth, she was intended. From earliest childhood I have

[008-038]bestowed the greatest care upon her, doing everything in my power to

[008-039]promote her health and beauty. As soon as she was old enough, I had

[008-040]her carefully instructed in the arts of dancing, acting, playing on

[008-041]musical instruments, singing, painting, preparing perfumes and

[008-042]flowers, in writing and conversation, and even to some extent in

[008-043]grammar, logic, and philosophy. She was taught to play various games

[008-044]with skill and dexterity, and how to dress well, and show herself off

[008-045]to the greatest advantage in public; I hired persons to go about

[008-046]praising her skill and beauty, and to applaud her when she performed

[008-047]in public, and I did many other things to promote her success, and to

[008-048]secure for her liberal remuneration; yet, after all the time, trouble,

[008-049]and money which I have spent upon her, just when I was beginning to

[008-050]reap the fruit of my labours, the ungrateful girl has fallen in love

[008-051]with a stranger, a young brahman, without property, and wishes to

[008-052]marry him and give up her profession, notwithstanding all my

[008-053]entreaties, and representations of the poverty and distress to which

[008-054]all her family will be reduced if she persists in her purpose; and

[008-055]because I oppose this marriage, she declares that she will renounce

[008-056]the world, and become a devotee.'

[008-057]"The muni compassionately said to the girl: 'You will never be able to

[008-058]endure the hardships of such a life as you propose to lead--a life so

[008-059]different from that to which you have been accustomed. Heaven may be

[008-060]attained by all who duly perform the duties of their station; take my

[008-061]advice then, give up all thoughts of an undertaking which you will

[008-062]never accomplish, comply with your mother's wishes, return with her,

[008-063]and be content with that way of life in which you have been brought

[008-064]up.'

[008-065]"With many tears, she replied: 'If you will not receive me I will put

[008-066]an end to my wretched life.'

[008-067]"Finding her so determined, the muni, after some reflection, said to

[008-068]the mother and her companions: 'Go away for the present; come back

[008-069]after a few days; I will give her good advice, and you will no doubt

[008-070]find her tired of living here, and quite ready to return.'

[008-071]"Thereupon they all went away, and she was left alone with the muni.

[008-072]At first she kept at a distance from him, taking care not to interrupt

[008-073]him in his meditations, but waiting on him unobtrusively, rendering

[008-074]him many little services, watering his favourite trees, and gathering

[008-075]sacred grass, and flowers for offerings to the gods. Then, as he

[008-076]became more accustomed to her, she would amuse him with songs and

[008-077]dances, and at last began to sit near him and talk of the pleasures of

[008-078]love.

[008-079]"One day, as if in all simplicity, she said 'Surely people are very

[008-080]wrong in reckoning virtue, wealth and pleasure as the three great

[008-081]objects of life?'

[008-082]"'Tell me,' he answered, 'how far do you regard virtue as superior to

[008-083]the other two?'

[008-084]"'A very wise man like you,' she replied, 'can hardly learn anything

[008-085]from an ignorant woman like me; but since you ask, I will tell you

[008-086]what I think. There is no real acquisition of happiness or wealth

[008-087]without virtue; but the latter is quite independent of the other two.

[008-088]Without it, a man is nothing; but if he fully possesses it, he is so

[008-089]purified by it that he may indulge in pleasures occasionally, and any

[008-090]sin connected with them will no more adhere to him than dust to a

[008-091]cloud. Look at all the stories of the amours of the gods. Are they the

[008-092]less worshipped on that account? I think, therefore, that virtue is a

[008-093]hundred times superior to the other two.' With many such specious

[008-094]arguments as these, and by her winning ways, she contrived to make him

[008-095]madly in love; so that, forgetting all his religious duties and former

[008-096]austerities, he thought only how to please her.

[008-097]"When she perceived this, she said to him 'Let us stay no longer in

[008-098]the forest, but go to my house in the town, where we can have many

[008-099]more enjoyments.' Utterly infatuated, he was ready to do her

[008-100]bidding; and she, having procured a covered carriage, took him in the

[008-101]evening to her own house.

[008-102]"The next day there was a great festival, at which the king was

[008-103]accustomed to appear in public and converse familiarly with his

[008-104]subjects. On such occasions he would often be surrounded by actresses

[008-105]and dancing girls.

[008-106]"On that day Kâmamanjari persuaded the muni to put on a gay dress and

[008-107]accompany her to the park where the festival was held; and he,

[008-108]thinking only of her, and miserable if she were away from him even for

[008-109]a short time, consented to go. On their arrival there, she walked with

[008-110]him towards the king, who, seeing her, said, with a smile: 'Sit down

[008-111]here with that reverend man.' And all eyes were directed towards him.

[008-112]"Presently one of the ladies rose up, and, making a low obeisance to

[008-113]the king, said: 'My lord; I must confess myself beaten by that lady; I

[008-114]have lost my wager and must now pay the penalty.'

[008-115]"Then a great shout of laughter arose; the king congratulated

[008-116]Kâmamanjari, and presented her with handsome ornaments.

[008-117]"After this she walked away with the astonished muni, followed by a

[008-118]great crowd, shouting applause.

[008-119]"Before reaching her own house, she turned round to him with a low

[008-120]obeisance, and said: 'Reverend sir, you have favoured me with your

[008-121]company a long time; it will be well for you to attend now to your own

[008-122]affairs.'

[008-123]"Not having his eyes yet opened, he started as if thunderstruck, and

[008-124]said: 'My dear, what does all this mean? What has become of the great

[008-125]love which you professed for me?'

[008-126]"She smilingly answered: 'I will explain it all.'

[008-127]"'One day, that lady whom you saw in the park had a dispute with me as

[008-128]to which was the most attractive. At last she said: "You boast of your

[008-129]powers, forsooth; go and try them on Mâríchi. If you can persuade him

[008-130]to accompany you here, then indeed you may triumph; I will acknowledge

[008-131]myself your inferior."

[008-132]"'This was the reason of my coming to you; the trick has been

[008-133]successful; I have won my wager, and have now no further occasion for

[008-134]you.'

[008-135]"Bowed down by shame and remorse, the unhappy man slunk back to his

[008-136]hermitage, miserable and degraded, bitterly lamenting his folly and

[008-137]infatuation, but resolved to atone for it by deep repentance and

[008-138]severe penance.

[008-139]"I am that wretched man; you see, therefore, that I am now quite

[008-140]unable to assist you. But do not go away; remain in Champa. After a

[008-141]time I shall recover my former power."

[008-142]While he was telling me this sad story, the sun set, and I remained

[008-143]with him that night. The next morning, at sunrise, I took leave of

[008-144]him, and walked towards the city. On my way thither, as I passed a

[008-145]Buddhist monastery, I was struck by the appearance of a man sitting at

[008-146]the side of the road near it. He was extraordinarily ugly; his body

[008-147]naked, with the exception of a rag round his waist; and his face so

[008-148]covered with dirt, that the tears he was shedding left furrows as

[008-149]they rolled down his cheeks.

[008-150]Moved by compassion, I sat down near him, and inquired the reason of

[008-151]his distress, at the same time adding, "If it is a secret, I do not

[008-152]wish to intrude upon you."

[008-153]"'My misfortunes are well known,' he answered; 'I can have no

[008-154]objection to telling you if you wish to hear them.' Then he began:

[008-155]"My name is Vasupâlika; but from my ugliness I am generally known as

[008-156]Virúpaka,--the deformed. I am the son of a man of some importance

[008-157]here, who left me a large fortune.

[008-158]"Among my acquaintance there was a person called Sundaraka, remarkably

[008-159]handsome, but poor. Between us two some mischievous persons strove to

[008-160]excite a rivalry, pitting my money against his beauty and

[008-161]accomplishments.

[008-162]"One day, in a large assembly, having got up a dispute between us,

[008-163]they said: 'It is not beauty or wealth, but the approbation of the

[008-164]ladies, which stamps the worth of a man; therefore, let the famous

[008-165]actress, Kâmamanjari, decide between you, and agree that she shall say

[008-166]who is the best man.' To this we both assented, and she, having been

[008-167]previously prepared for the part which she was to perform, was brought

[008-168]into the room, and passing by my rival with scorn, sat down by my

[008-169]side, and, taking a garland from her own head, placed it on mine.

[008-170]"Greatly flattered and delighted by this preference, and blinded by a

[008-171]mad love for her, which I had not ventured to express, I most readily

[008-172]gave myself up to her seductions, and in a very short time she

[008-173]obtained such an influence over me that everything I possessed was at

[008-174]her disposal. Before long, she had so plundered me, and led me into

[008-175]such extravagance, that I was reduced to the most abject poverty, and

[008-176]had nothing I could call my own but this miserable rag which you now

[008-177]see me wear.

[008-178]"Cast off by her, blamed and reproached by the elder men, laughed at

[008-179]and despised by those who had been my companions in prosperity, I knew

[008-180]not where to turn; and as a last resource I entered this Buddhist

[008-181]monastery, where I obtain a bare subsistence.
