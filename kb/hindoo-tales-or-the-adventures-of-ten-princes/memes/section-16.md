[016-001]When the seizure of the king was known, those who were previously

[016-002]well-disposed to my father immediately joined us, and all opposition

[016-003]ceased.

[016-004]Soon afterwards I married the princess, who looked on me as her

[016-005]deliverer from the dungeon; Sinhaghosha was deposed; and I, having

[016-006]double claim to the throne, was acknowledged king in his stead.

[016-007]Hearing that the King of Anga, a devoted friend of your father, was at

[016-008]war, and attacked by a strong enemy, we have marched hither with an

[016-009]army to his assistance, and I have had the pleasure of helping to

[016-010]deliver him from his enemies, and the still greater happiness of

[016-011]meeting with you. I now beg of you to decide what shall be done with

[016-012]the deposed king, our prisoner, whom we have brought with us. My

[016-013]mother is very anxious to liberate him, but hitherto it has not been

[016-014]thought safe to do so.

[016-015]The prince answered: "Let that unworthy young man be freed, on

[016-016]condition of giving up all claim to the throne and leading a private

[016-017]life; and let him devote himself to pious meditation, which is the

[016-018]purifier of evil deeds." Then turning with a kind look to Pramati, he

[016-019]said: "Do you now relate your adventures," with which request he at

[016-020]once complied:--

[016-021]* * * * *

[016-022]ADVENTURES OF PRAMATI.

[016-023]My lord, while wandering like the rest of your friends in search of

[016-024]you, I found myself one evening in a large forest, far from any

[016-025]habitation. Thinking it useless to attempt to go further in an unknown

[016-026]country and in darkness, I prepared to sleep there. Having bathed in

[016-027]the water of a small lake, and made myself a bed of leaves, I lay down

[016-028]under a large tree, commending myself to the deities presiding over

[016-029]the place, and was very soon asleep.

[016-030]Presently a strange and delightful feeling came over me, gladdening my

[016-031]inmost soul; and I awoke, hardly knowing whether what I saw was a

[016-032]reality or a dream, for on looking round me I saw that I was no longer

[016-033]in the forest, but in a very large and lofty room, lying on a soft

[016-034]couch with white muslin curtains; all around me were a number of

[016-035]sleeping women. Among them my eyes were especially attracted towards a

[016-036]young lady of exceeding beauty, lying in a very graceful attitude,

[016-037]covered only by a silken petticoat, her bosom slowly rising and

[016-038]falling, and her bud-like lower lip quivering with the soft movement

[016-039]of the breath in quiet sleep.

[016-040]Lost in astonishment, I said to myself; "What has become of that great

[016-041]forest wrapt in darkness? How is my bed of leaves exchanged for this

[016-042]soft couch? Whence is this dome above me, lofty as the great temple of

[016-043]Siva? Who are all these lovely women, like a troop of Apsaras lying

[016-044]down wearied with play? And who can this beautiful lady be? She

[016-045]cannot be a goddess, for the gods do not sleep thus, nor do they

[016-046]perspire, and I see the drops breaking forth on her forehead. She must

[016-047]then be a mortal; but O how lovely! how peacefully she sleeps, as if

[016-048]she had never known the anxieties of love! My heart is drawn towards

[016-049]her."

[016-050]With these thoughts I rose up and approached the bed where she lay,

[016-051]and stood looking at her as if entranced, becoming every moment more

[016-052]enamoured, longing to touch her, but held back by the fear of

[016-053]disturbing her.

[016-054]While I was thus gazing, she gradually awoke, and raising herself into

[016-055]a sitting posture, looked at me attentively with eyes more than half

[016-056]closed. At first her lips were opened, as if she were about to cry

[016-057]out; but, apparently restrained by some secret power, she remained

[016-058]silent, trembling all over, and showing in her countenance the signs

[016-059]of mingled doubt, fear, astonishment, bashfulness, and love; till at

[016-060]last, overcome again by sleep, she slowly sank down again on the bed.

[016-061]Almost at the same time I felt myself irresistibly overcome by

[016-062]drowsiness, and was very soon fast asleep.

[016-063]When I awoke, I found myself on the bed of leaves once more, alone in

[016-064]the gloomy forest, and day was beginning to appear.

[016-065]When I was quite awake I had some difficulty in collecting my

[016-066]thoughts, and I said to myself: "Can all this of which I have such a

[016-067]vivid impression be other than a reality, or was it only a dream, a

[016-068]magical delusion? Whatever it may be, I will not quit this place till

[016-069]I find out the truth, and I will place myself under the protection of

[016-070]the deity who sent the vision."

[016-071]Having formed this resolution, I was waiting where I had slept, when I

[016-072]saw approaching me a female form faded like a flower scorched by the

[016-073]sun, with eyes red from weeping, lips parched by the hot breath of

[016-074]sighs, wearing a scanty black dress, without ornaments, and with her

[016-075]hair in a single braid, like an affectionate wife mourning for the

[016-076]absence of her husband;[6] and with all this having an air of divine

[016-077]dignity, which made me regard her with reverence, and think that she

[016-078]might be the tutelary goddess of the place, to whom I had commended

[016-079]myself; and I prostrated myself before her. But she raised me up with

[016-080]her arms, and after kissing me again and again, said, with a voice

[016-081]broken by tears and sobs, "O, my darling, surely you have heard from

[016-082]the Queen Vasumati how one night a fairy appeared to her, and placing

[016-083]the child Arthapâla[7] in her arms, told her husband's name and her

[016-084]own; and how the child was brought by order of Kuvera; and then

[016-085]disappeared. I am that fairy--your mother. Bewildered by unreasonable

[016-086]jealousy and anger, I abandoned my husband, your father, Kâmapâla; and

[016-087]for that sin I was cursed by Durgâ, who condemned me to be possessed

[016-088]by an evil spirit for a year. That year, which seemed to me like a

[016-089]thousand years, is ended; and I am now come from the great festival

[016-090]of Siva, where I have met my relations, who had assembled there, and

[016-091]have received full pardon from the goddess.

[016-092]"In my way thither, I passed by this place, saw you about to lie down,

[016-093]and heard your prayer to the local deity.

[016-094]"Being still partly under the influence of the curse, I did not

[016-095]recognise you as my son. Yet even as a stranger I felt an interest in

[016-096]you, and could not bear the thought of leaving you exposed to danger

[016-097]in such a wild place. I therefore waited till you were fast asleep;

[016-098]and having considered where I could deposit you while I was gone to

[016-099]meet the goddess, since I could not take you with me, it occurred to

[016-100]me to carry you to the palace of the King of Sravasti, and leave you

[016-101]to sleep there till my return. I therefore carried you through the

[016-102]air, and placed you in the sleeping apartment of the Princess

[016-103]Navamâlika, feeling sure that no one would disturb you there. I then

[016-104]went to the temple; and after paying due worship to Siva, and

[016-105]receiving the congratulations of my assembled friends, I was dismissed

[016-106]by the goddess, who said: 'You are forgiven; the curse is ended; go

[016-107]and be happy with your husband.' After which I returned to the palace;

[016-108]and taking you up, brought you to this place, and laid you, still

[016-109]sleeping, on your bed of leaves. Since then, I have been watching for

[016-110]your awaking; for as soon as the curse was removed, I knew you to be

[016-111]my son.

[016-112]"I must now leave you, and go to your father. I know what passed in

[016-113]the palace; how you have fallen in love with the princess, and her

[016-114]feelings towards you. Do not despond; before long you will see her

[016-115]again."

[016-116]She then warmly embraced me; and saying: "I go with reluctance,

[016-117]farewell for the present," she departed.

[016-118]Having thus found the supposed dream to be a reality, and that the

[016-119]lady whom I had seen was the Princess Navamâlika, I was confirmed in

[016-120]my love, and set out for Sravasti, determined, if possible, to see her

[016-121]again.

[016-122]On the road, I came to a village where there was a large fair and a

[016-123]great concourse of traders. Various amusements were going on; among

[016-124]others, a cock-fight, which I stopped to look at, and sat down near an

[016-125]old brahman, who was watching the fight with great interest. On seeing

[016-126]me smile, he asked the reason; and I answered: "What simpletons some

[016-127]of the breeders here must be to pit a Balâka cock against one of the

[016-128]Nârikela breed, which is sure to win."

[016-129]With a knowing look, he whispered to me: "Hush! these blockheads know

[016-130]no better. I see you are a sharp fellow; sit quiet and say nothing."

[016-131]Then he offered me betel and pawn from his box; and we got into

[016-132]conversation.

[016-133]Meanwhile, the birds fought furiously; and there was much vociferation

[016-134]on both sides; but, as I had predicted, the Balâka cock was beaten.

[016-135]The old man was delighted at the victory of the other, which was his

[016-136]own. He seemed to have taken a great liking to me, though our ages

[016-137]were so different, and invited me to his house, where I was very

[016-138]hospitably treated, and passed the night.

[016-139]The next morning he accompanied me some distance on the way to

[016-140]Sravasti; and said, at parting: "Remember, I am your friend; do not

[016-141]hesitate to apply to me if there is anything in which I can help you."

[016-142]After he had left me, I continued my journey; and arriving late and

[016-143]very tired at Sravasti, I lay down to sleep in an arbour in one part

[016-144]of the park outside the city. There I slept soundly till awakened by

[016-145]the noise of the swans and other birds in a lake not far off.

[016-146]Soon after I had risen, I heard the tinkling of anklets, and saw a

[016-147]young lady walking towards me, with a painted canvas in her hand. When

[016-148]she came near, she looked first at me, and then at the painting. This

[016-149]she did several times, and was evidently surprised and pleased at the

[016-150]comparison On casting an eye on the picture, I also was much

[016-151]surprised, finding it to be a portrait of myself.

[016-152]Feeling sure that the likeness could not be accidental, and that there

[016-153]must be some reason for her making the comparison and seeming so

[016-154]pleased at the result, I would not at first make any inquiry of her,

[016-155]but merely said: "This is a public place; we need not stand on

[016-156]ceremony; pray sit down with me." This she did; and we got into

[016-157]conversation about the news of the town.

[016-158]At last she said to me: "You seem to be quite a stranger here, and

[016-159]look as if you were travel-tired. Will you be offended if I ask you to

[016-160]come and rest at my house?"

[016-161]"Offended!" I answered. "You do me a very great favour; I shall be

[016-162]most delighted to accept your invitation." Upon this, she rose, and I

[016-163]followed her to her house, where I was most kindly entertained. When I

[016-164]was refreshed with bathing and food, she said to me: "You have been

[016-165]travelling about in various countries. Have you, in your travels, met

[016-166]with any very extraordinary adventure?"

[016-167]On hearing this question, I thought: "I have now good ground for hope.

[016-168]The picture represents that very room which I saw, with its lofty

[016-169]ceiling and white canopies--even the bed where the princess was lying.

[016-170]Instigated by love, she has doubtless painted my portrait from

[016-171]recollection; and, in the hope that I may be discovered through the

[016-172]likeness, has entrusted it to this lady who has now invited me to her

[016-173]house. She evidently thinks that I am the person; but hesitates to

[016-174]put a direct question to me. If I am right, I will soon remove her

[016-175]doubt."
