[009-001]"Distressed by the cutting off of my long hair, and by numerous

[009-002]restrictions as to eating, drinking, and sleeping, like a newly-caught

[009-003]elephant; and hearing every day abuse of those gods whom I used to

[009-004]worship; filled with remorse for my departure from the religion of my

[009-005]ancestors; I am utterly miserable and only wish for death."

[009-006]Having heard this pitiable story, I did what I could to comfort him,

[009-007]and said, "Do not despair; I have heard already of that wicked woman,

[009-008]and think I shall be able to find some means of making her restore to

[009-009]you a part at least of your property."

[009-010]After leaving him, I went into the city, and finding, from popular

[009-011]report, that it was full of rich misers, I resolved to bring them to

[009-012]their proper condition by taking away their useless wealth.

[009-013]Occupied by this thought, I went into a gaming-house, where I was much

[009-014]interested and amused by watching the players and observing their

[009-015]tricks, their sleight-of-hand, their bullying or cringing behaviour to

[009-016]each other; the reckless profusion of the winners, the muttering

[009-017]despair of those who had lost.

[009-018]While overlooking a game of chess, I smiled and made some remark about

[009-019]a bad move of one of the players, upon which his opponent, turning to

[009-020]me with a sneer, said "No doubt you think yourself very clever, but

[009-021]wait till I have finished off this stupid fellow, and I will play you

[009-022]for any stake you like."

[009-023]When the game was over, accepting his challenge, I sat down to play,

[009-024]and won altogether sixteen thousand dínars. Half of this sum I kept

[009-025]for myself, and half I divided between the gaming-house keeper and the

[009-026]players who were present. The latter were loud in praise of my

[009-027]generosity, and of the skill which I had shown in beating that

[009-028]boaster; the former asked me to dine with him, and I often went to

[009-029]his house and became very intimate with him, and obtained from him

[009-030]much information, especially such as had reference to my purpose.

[009-031]One very dark night, fully directed by him, I set out, determined on

[009-032]robbery, equipped with a dark dress, a short sword, a spade, a

[009-033]crowbar, a pair of pincers, a wooden man's head,[4] a magic candle, a

[009-034]rope and grappling-iron, a box with a bee in it,[5] and some other

[009-035]implements.

[009-036]Selecting a house where I knew there was much money, I made a hole in

[009-037]the wall, and finding all quiet, enlarged it, entered boldly, and

[009-038]carried off much booty.

[009-039]As I was returning, looking cautiously about me, I came suddenly upon

[009-040]a young woman, who was much alarmed at seeing me. Perceiving her

[009-041]agitation, I spoke to her kindly, and assured her that I would much

[009-042]rather assist than injure her.

[009-043]Encouraged by my words, she told me her story: "My name is Kulapâlika;

[009-044]I am the daughter of a rich merchant in this city, and was from

[009-045]childhood engaged to the son of another rich man, named Dhanamittra:

[009-046]he, however, being of a very generous disposition, when he had

[009-047]succeeded to his father's property was preyed on by pretended friends

[009-048]and reduced to comparative poverty. Seeing this, my father refused his

[009-049]consent to our marriage, and, in spite of my reluctance, is determined

[009-050]to give me to a rich man, called Arthapati. To escape this marriage, I

[009-051]have slipped out from home by a secret passage, rarely used, and am

[009-052]going to the house of my lover, who is expecting me and will take me

[009-053]away to some other country; pray do not detain me, but accept this."

[009-054]So saying, she put one of her ornaments into my hand. I did not refuse

[009-055]it, but walked by her side, intending to escort her to her

[009-056]destination.

[009-057]We had, however, only gone a few steps, when I saw coming towards us,

[009-058]at no great distance, a large body of the citizen guard. Without

[009-059]losing a moment, I said to the trembling girl, "Don't be alarmed; say

[009-060]that I have been bitten by a serpent, and I will manage the rest."

[009-061]By the time they reached us I had thrown myself on the ground, and lay

[009-062]as if insensible, and she stood over me, crying. On being questioned,

[009-063]she answered, with many tears, and in evident distress: "My husband

[009-064]and I, coming from the country, lost our way, and have only lately

[009-065]entered the city. Just now he was bitten by a serpent, and is all but

[009-066]dead. Is there any one among you skilled in charms who can recover

[009-067]him?"

[009-068]Among the guard there chanced to be a very conceited man, who had

[009-069]often boasted of his skill, and was now delighted to have an

[009-070]opportunity of displaying it. He stood over me while the others

[009-071]waited, and, with many gesticulations, muttered various charms

[009-072]supposed to be efficacious in such a case; but finding all of no

[009-073]avail, said at last, "Ah! it is too late; the poor man is past all

[009-074]remedies: what a pity I did not see him sooner!" Then, joining his

[009-075]companions, who were impatient to be off, he turned to the sobbing

[009-076]girl and said: "He was evidently fated to die; who can prevail over

[009-077]fate? It is useless to lament; nothing more can be done now; wait a

[009-078]little while, and when we come back we will remove the body."

[009-079]As soon as they were out of sight I rose up, took her to the house of

[009-080]Dhanamittra, and said to him: "I met this lady just now; I have

[009-081]brought her safely here, and now restore the ornament which she gave

[009-082]me in her fright; for, though I am a robber, I would not steal from

[009-083]one like her."

[009-084]Delighted at seeing her, he answered: "O, sir, you have indeed

[009-085]rendered me a great service in bringing this dear one in safety here;

[009-086]such conduct is very extraordinary in a man of your way of life, and I

[009-087]am quite unable to understand your motives for acting thus. At all

[009-088]events, I am under very great obligation to you; command my services

[009-089]in future."

[009-090]After some further talk, I asked him: "Friend, what do you now intend

[009-091]to do?"

[009-092]"It will be impossible," he answered, "for me to live here if I marry

[009-093]her without her father's consent; I propose, therefore, to leave the

[009-094]town with her this very night."

[009-095]"A clever man," I replied, "is at home in any place. Wherever he goes

[009-096]he may say this is my country. But, in travelling, many hardships must

[009-097]be endured--hunger, thirst, fatigue, and dangers from men and wild

[009-098]beasts;--how will this tender girl be able to bear them?

[009-099]"You seem to be wanting in wisdom and forethought in thus abandoning

[009-100]home and country. Take courage! be guided by me, and you shall marry

[009-101]her and live comfortably here. But first we must take her back to her

[009-102]father's house."

[009-103]To this he consented without hesitation, and we set out at once.

[009-104]Guided by her, we entered through the secret passage, carried off

[009-105]everything of value, and got away without exciting alarm.

[009-106]Having hidden our booty in some old ruins, we were going home, when we

[009-107]fell in with some of the city guard. Fortunately, there chanced to be

[009-108]an elephant tied up at the side of the road. We quickly, therefore,

[009-109]unfastened the rope, mounted him, and urged him at full speed; and

[009-110]before the watchmen could recover from their confusion, were out of

[009-111]sight. Halting the elephant close to the wall of a deserted garden, we

[009-112]got over it with the help of the trees growing there, escaped on the

[009-113]other side, and reached home undetected, where we bathed and went to

[009-114]bed.

[009-115]The next day we walked out carefully dressed, and were amused at

[009-116]hearing an exaggerated account of our adventures of the preceding

[009-117]night, which had caused much alarm and excitement in the city.

[009-118]I had hoped, by robbing the old man, to prevent the marriage of his

[009-119]daughter with Arthapati. But this hope was frustrated; for the latter

[009-120]was not only willing to take Kulapâlika without a dowry, but even made

[009-121]presents to her father; and it was settled that the marriage should

[009-122]take place at the end of a month.

[009-123]Finding this to be the case, I felt that something more must be done;

[009-124]and having hit upon a plan which I thought would be effectual, I gave

[009-125]Dhanamittra directions how to act.

[009-126]Accordingly, a few days afterwards, he went to the king, to whom he

[009-127]was previously known, and having asked for a private audience, said:

[009-128]"A very wonderful thing has happened to me, of which it seems right

[009-129]that your majesty should be informed. You have known me as

[009-130]Dhanamittra, the son of a very rich man. During my prosperity, I was

[009-131]engaged to the daughter of a wealthy merchant; but when I was reduced

[009-132]to poverty, he refused his consent to our marriage, and is now about

[009-133]to give her to another.

[009-134]"Driven to despair by the double loss of fortune and wife, I went into

[009-135]a wood near the city, intending to put an end to my wretched life.

[009-136]"There, when in the act of cutting my throat, I was stopped by a very

[009-137]aged devotee, who asked the cause of the rash act.

[009-138]"'Poverty, and contempt,' I answered.

[009-139]"'There is nothing more foolish and sinful than suicide,' he replied.

[009-140]'A man of sense will endure adversity rather than escape from it in

[009-141]such a manner. Wealth, when lost, may be regained in many ways; but

[009-142]life in none. A broken fortune may be repaired; a cut throat can never

[009-143]be joined again. But why should I preach to you thus? Here is a remedy

[009-144]for your misfortunes. This leather bag will give you abundant wealth.

[009-145]I have used it for assisting the deserving; but now I am old and

[009-146]infirm, and am not long for this world. I give it to you.

[009-147]"'Go home; if you possess anything wrongfully acquired, restore it to

[009-148]the right owner, and give away the rest of your property to brahmans

[009-149]and the poor. When this has been done, put away the purse carefully;

[009-150]and in the morning it will be found full of gold. Remember that

[009-151]whoever possesses it must comply with these conditions, and that it

[009-152]will yield its treasures only to a merchant like yourself, or to an

[009-153]actress.'

[009-154]"With these words, he handed me the purse, and immediately

[009-155]disappeared.

[009-156]"I have now brought the purse to your majesty, to know your pleasure

[009-157]concerning it."

[009-158]The king, though much astonished, believing the story, told him to

[009-159]keep and enjoy it; and in answer to his entreaty, promised that any

[009-160]one attempting to steal it should be severely punished.

[009-161]After this, Dhanamittra, making no secret of his acquisition of the

[009-162]purse, disposed of all his property somewhat ostentatiously, leaving

[009-163]himself absolutely nothing but the clothes which he wore; and in the

[009-164]morning, having filled the purse with gold--the proceeds of the

[009-165]robbery--he showed it to his neighbours, who were fully convinced of

[009-166]its magic powers.

[009-167]The fame of the purse was thus spread abroad; and we were able to

[009-168]account for our newly-acquired wealth, without incurring any suspicion

[009-169]as to the manner of obtaining it.

[009-170]At this time; for reasons which will presently appear, I induced

[009-171]Vimardaka to enter the service of Arthapati; and directed him to use

[009-172]all possible means to excite his master against Dhanamittra. In this

[009-173]he had no difficulty; for the father of Kulapâlika, hearing of his

[009-174]sudden acquisition of wealth, did not even wait to be asked, but of

[009-175]his own accord renewed the former engagement, and rejected Arthapati.

[009-176]About that time it was publicly announced that a younger sister of

[009-177]Kâmamanjari--Râgamanjari by name--would make her first appearance as a

[009-178]dancer and singer. Great expectations having been raised, a large

[009-179]number of spectators, including myself and my friend Dhanamittra, were

[009-180]present at the performance.
