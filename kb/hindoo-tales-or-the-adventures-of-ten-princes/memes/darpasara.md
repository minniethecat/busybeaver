# Darpasâra

Darpasâra was the son of the King of [[malwa|Mâlwa]], a prince who performed penance
on the great mountain while two regents governed in his stead. He appears
as both an authoritative figure and a marriage candidate in the narrative,
his decisions shaping the fates of several principal characters.
(005-050) (007-118) (015-143)

## Biography

Darpasâra was the son of the King of [[malwa|Mâlwa]]. At the time the narrative opens,
the prince had gone on a pilgrimage to the [[himalaya|Himâlaya Mountains]] to perform
penance, having first appointed as joint regents the two sons of his
father's sister, [[charmavarma|Charmavarma]] and [[dâruvarma|Dâruvarma]].
(005-050) (005-051) (005-052)

While performing penance on the great mountain, Darpasâra became acquainted
with Vírasekhara, a Vidyâdhara of partly human descent. Vírasekhara, hoping
to gain assistance in a feud, made an alliance with Darpasâra and engaged
to marry his sister, the Princess [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]].
(007-118) (007-119) (007-121)

Darpasâra exercised his authority from afar through correspondence. When
matters concerning a violation of the harem reached him, a messenger arrived
bearing a letter from the prince in which he wrote sternly:

> "O fool! should there be any pity for the violator of the harem? If
> the old king, my father, now in his dotage, was foolish enough to
> favour the criminal for the sake of his worthless daughter, you had
> no need of his permission, and ought not to have hesitated."
> (007-082) (007-084)

The prince's harsh instructions led to the confiscation of [[pushpodbhava|Pushpodbhava]]'s
property and the imprisonment of his family.
(007-053) (007-054)

Later, a marriage was arranged between a princess and Darpasâra, the son
of the King of [[malwa|Mâlwa]]. However, it was revealed that the bride had been
originally intended for another, as her mother had promised her daughter
to the son of [[kantimati|Kantimati]] before the child was born.
(015-143) (015-157) (015-159)

## Role in the Narrative

Darpasâra functions as an offstage authority figure whose decisions
reverberate through the main plot. His absence on pilgrimage creates a
power vacuum filled by regents, while his stern letters drive critical
plot developments. He also serves as a foil in a marriage plot, where
a prior betrothal complicates his intended [[marriage-in-the-tales|union]].

## Relationships

Darpasâra was the son of the King of [[malwa|Mâlwa]]. His cousins [[charmavarma|Charmavarma]] and
[[dâruvarma|Dâruvarma]] served as joint regents during his pilgrimage. He formed an
alliance with Vírasekhara, a Vidyâdhara, and was betrothed to a princess
whose mother had previously promised her to another.
(005-050) (007-118) (015-157)
