# Wedding in the Tales

## Summary

Weddings are celebrated with splendour and often serve as turning points in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes*. A [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]] may seal a political alliance, reward a hero, or introduce unexpected complications into the plot.

Three distinct [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]] episodes illustrate the range of narrative functions the ceremony can serve: from the public celebration of a royal marriage to the private tragedy of a rejected bride.

> "the wedding would be celebrated with much splendour the next morning."
> (007-080)

## Context

The most prominent [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]] in the tales is that of [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]], announced with public proclamation and "much splendour" (007-080). This [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]] is framed by political manoeuvring—immediately after the ceremony, the groom plans to "overtake my army in march against the enemy" (007-094).

Not all [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]]s are joyful. A merchant's son "took a sudden dislike to his bride on the very day of the [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]], and though she continued to live in his house, avoided her as much as possible" (019-154). This unhappy [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]] generates a subplot of estrangement and eventual resolution.

## Key Participants

- [[rajavahana]] — prince whose [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]] to [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]] is celebrated

- [[darpasara]] — sends a threatening letter on the eve of the [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]]

- [[apahâravarma]] — involved in the events surrounding the [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]]

## Outcome

Weddings in the tales are never merely ceremonial. They mark transitions of power, test loyalties, and reveal character. The public splendour of the [[marriage-in-the-tales|wedding]] ceremony contrasts with the private dramas that unfold around it.
