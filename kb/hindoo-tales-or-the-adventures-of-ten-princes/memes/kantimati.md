# Kantimati

Kantimati was a princess of the royal household of [[magadha|Magadha]], the wife of
[[Kâmapâla]] and the mother of [[Arthapâla]], one of the ten princes.

## Biography

Kantimati was the wife of [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]], a high-ranking figure in the kingdom
of [[magadha|Magadha]]. A child was born of the Princess Kantimati, marking her role
as a mother in the royal line.
(014-110)

> "The child, now born of the Princess Kantimati, was named Arthapâla."
> (014-110)

The queen of the royal household, seeing the Princess Kantimati very sad
one day, asked her the cause of her grief. This moment reveals Kantimati's
emotional state and her position within the palace hierarchy.
(014-164)

> "One day the queen, seeing the Princess Kantimati very sad, asked her
> the cause of her deep affliction."
> (014-164)

She was sought out by those who wished to see her, and she was told: "I am
not jealous, I will take you to her." This indicates the trust placed in
those who had access to her chambers.
(014-124)

Kantimati appears in the narrative when visitors come unexpectedly upon her,
revealing her presence in the unfolding events of the story.
(015-133)

> "Kantimati, and have come thus unexpectedly on you while making an
> offering to the gods."
> (015-133)

The character index records Kantimati simply as the wife of [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]] and
mother of [[arthapala|Arthapâla]].
(026-094)

## Role in the Narrative

Kantimati's primary role in the narrative is as a maternal figure. She is
the mother of **[[Arthapâla]]**, one of the ten princes, and through her
marriage to **[[Kâmapâla]]** she connects the royal household to the
wider circle of princes.
(014-110)

Her sadness, observed by the queen, suggests a personal grief or concern
that humanizes her character within the courtly setting. The queen's
attention to her indicates her importance within the palace hierarchy.
(014-164)

References to the wife of the son of Kantimati extend her significance
into the next generation, as her son [[arthapala|Arthapâla]]'s own marriage becomes
a matter of narrative concern.
(015-159)

## Relationships

Kantimati was the wife of **[[Kâmapâla]]**, a minister or noble of the
[[magadha|Magadha]]n court, and through this marriage she became part of the inner
circle of the kingdom.
(026-094)

She was the mother of **[[Arthapâla]]**, one of the ten princes whose
adventures form the central narrative of the text.
(014-110) (026-094)

She moved within the royal household of **[[magadha|Magadha]]**, where she was close
enough to the queen to share confidences.
(014-164)
