# Ceremony in the Tales

## Summary

Ceremonies in the *Tales of Ten Princes* encompass religious rituals, royal observances, and social formalities that mark significant life transitions. The most prominent is the *[[simanta|Simanta]]* ceremony — a religious rite performed for pregnant women — celebrated by the king upon receiving news of the queen's pregnancy. [[marriage-in-the-tales|Marriage]] ceremonies conducted with proper rites also feature as markers of legitimate [[marriage-in-the-tales|union]] and social recognition.

## Context

In ancient Indian society as depicted by Daṇḍin, ceremonies served as public affirmations of social and religious order. They marked transitions — birth, marriage, death — and reinforced communal bonds. The tales frequently invoke ceremonial propriety as a measure of character and legitimacy.

> [013-064] for the ceremony, and there was much talk in t…

> [020-054] married to me with the proper ceremony and with the full consent of

> [026-141] *Simanta*, a religious ceremony performed on behalf of a woman at a certain period of pregnancy.

## Key Participants

- [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] — participates in ceremonies as prince and later king
- [[simanta|Simanta]] — the pregnancy ceremony performed for the queen

## Outcome

Ceremonies in the narrative function as structural markers — they authenticate relationships, validate transitions of power, and provide occasions for gathering and revelation. The *[[simanta|Simanta]]* ceremony at the tale's opening sets the entire narrative in motion.
