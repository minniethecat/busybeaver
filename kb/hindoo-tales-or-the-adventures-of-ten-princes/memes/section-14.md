[014-001]After a short time, with the consent of my wife, I resigned the crown,

[014-002]and reinstated my parents in their former position; retaining for

[014-003]myself the dignity of heir-apparent.

[014-004]Soon afterwards, a letter arrived from Sinhavarma, an old friend of my

[014-005]father's, congratulating him on his restoration, and asking for help

[014-006]against Chandavarma, who was marching to attack him. Upon which I

[014-007]hastily equipped an army, and marched to his assistance; and have now

[014-008]had the great happiness of meeting with you, as well as of helping to

[014-009]defeat the enemy.

[014-010]Râjavâhana having heard this story, smiled, and said: "Truly, our

[014-011]friend here has committed great sins; but how can I blame him when

[014-012]his motives were so good, and he had the praiseworthy object of

[014-013]liberating from a long imprisonment those who are so dear to him, and

[014-014]of punishing the usurper and oppressor? His courage and ingenuity have

[014-015]been great; and I congratulate him on his success."

[014-016]Then turning to Arthapâla, he said: "Do you relate your adventures."

[014-017]And he immediately began his story in the following manner:--

[014-018]* * * * *

[014-019]ADVENTURES OF ARTHAPÂLA.

[014-020]My Lord, having the same object as your other friends, I wandered

[014-021]about over various countries in search of you. In the course of my

[014-022]travels, I arrived one day at the sacred city of Benâres. There I

[014-023]bathed in the pure crystal water of the river; and duly worshipped the

[014-024]mighty god, the slayer of Andhaka, at his temple outside the city.

[014-025]After finishing my devotions, I was going on my way, when I saw a

[014-026]tall, stout man, carrying an iron club, with his eyes red and swelled

[014-027]from weeping, and engaged in making a noose with his sash.

[014-028]I thought to myself: "This man has fallen into some great calamity.

[014-029]He is thinking of doing violence to himself or to others. I will see

[014-030]if I can assist him." I therefore went up to him, and said: "This

[014-031]conduct of yours seems to indicate some rash purpose. May I know the

[014-032]cause of your grief? Perhaps I may be able to help you."

[014-033]He hesitated for a moment, and looked very hard at me; but at last he

[014-034]said: "What harm can there be in telling you? You shall know my

[014-035]troubles, if you wish to learn them."

[014-036]Then we sat down together under a shady tree, and he began his tale as

[014-037]follows: "O, fortunate sir, I was once as happy as you appear to be.

[014-038]My father was in good circumstances, and brought me up carefully; but

[014-039]I preferred a wild, dissipated life, and at last became a robber. One

[014-040]night I broke into the house of a rich man in this city, was caught

[014-041]in the act, and condemned to death.

[014-042]"My hands were fettered by being passed through holes in a heavy piece

[014-043]of wood; and in this state I was led out for execution into a public

[014-044]square, where a furious elephant was brought forward to trample me to

[014-045]death. When he came near me, I shouted as loudly as possible, in order

[014-046]to frighten him; and lifting up my arms, gave him a violent blow on

[014-047]the trunk. Upon this, he turned away; and as I continued to shout out

[014-048]and abuse him, all the efforts of the driver to make him attack me

[014-049]were in vain.

[014-050]"Again and again, with much difficulty, the driver brought him in

[014-051]front of me; but each time, instead of attacking me, he turned back,

[014-052]alarmed by my menacing appearance and loud shouts; and at last ran

[014-053]right away, leaving me uninjured.

[014-054]"The courage which I had shown was observed by the king's chief

[014-055]minister, Kâmapâla, who was looking on from one of the towers of the

[014-056]palace; and he sent for me, and said: 'You seem to be a very strong,

[014-057]brave man. I did not think that elephant could have been so cowed by

[014-058]any one. It is a pity that such qualities should not be better

[014-059]employed. Are you willing, if you are pardoned, to forsake your evil

[014-060]ways, and lead an honest life? If you will give me a promise to this

[014-061]effect, I will take you into my service.'

[014-062]"I gladly gave the promise which he required; and he obtained my

[014-063]pardon, and became my protector and master; and I have served him

[014-064]faithfully ever since. After some years, seeing my devotion to him, he

[014-065]placed great confidence in me, and one day told me his own history.

[014-066]"'There was,' said he, 'formerly at Pushpapuri a very learned and

[014-067]pious man, named Dharmapâla, one of the king's ministers. His eldest

[014-068]son was like him; but I, the youngest, was of a very different

[014-069]disposition. I had no inclination for work or study; but thought only

[014-070]of amusement, and spent my time among gamblers and disreputable

[014-071]characters. My father and brother did all they could to restrain me;

[014-072]but, impatient of their control, I left my home and friends, and

[014-073]wandered about the world. One day I came to this city, Benâres, and

[014-074]not long after my arrival, I made acquaintance with the king's

[014-075]daughter, who, with her female friends, was playing at ball in a park

[014-076]outside the town. We fell in love with each other; and I contrived, by

[014-077]disguising myself as a woman, to enter her private apartments and to

[014-078]have many secret meetings with her; the result of which was the birth

[014-079]of a child.

[014-080]"'The devoted attendants kept the whole affair secret, removed the

[014-081]infant as soon as it was born, and telling the mother it was dead,

[014-082]gave it to a savari woman, who carried it to the public cemetery and

[014-083]left it there.

[014-084]"'As she was returning; she was stopped by the watchmen, and in her

[014-085]fright told them what she had done. Information was given to the king,

[014-086]and further inquiry being made, my offence was discovered, and one

[014-087]night I was arrested, while quietly sleeping unsuspicious of danger.

[014-088]Being condemned to death, I was led to execution outside the city. By

[014-089]a fortunate chance I got my hands free, and snatching the sword from

[014-090]the executioner, laid about me so vigorously that all the men fell

[014-091]back, and I made my escape to the forest. There I wandered about for

[014-092]some time, subsisting on wild fruits and roots, and sleeping in the

[014-093]trees.

[014-094]"'While living this precarious life, I was one day astonished at

[014-095]meeting a young lady, with many female attendants. She addressed me by

[014-096]my name, and desired me to sit down with her, under a large tree.

[014-097]"'When, with much surprise, I asked who she was, and how she came to

[014-098]be in that wild forest, with such a retinue, and why I was so favoured

[014-099]by her, she told me the reason of her coming, saying: My name is

[014-100]Târâvali. I am the daughter of a chief Yaksha. A short time ago I

[014-101]went to visit a friend, living on the Malaya Mountains, and while

[014-102]flying through the air on my return, as I passed over the cemetery of

[014-103]Benâres, I heard the cry of a child.

[014-104]"'Moved with compassion, I alighted on the ground, took it up and

[014-105]carried it to my father. He took it to our master, the god Kuvera, who

[014-106]sent for me, and asked, "What induced you to bring this child?" "A

[014-107]strong feeling of compassion," I answered, as if it had been my own.

[014-108]"'You are right,' he replied; 'there is good reason for what you have

[014-109]done;' and he showed me how, in a former existence, when you were

[014-110]Sudraka and I Aryadâsi, the child, now born of the Princess Kantimati,

[014-111]was ours; therefore, I am really your wife, and it was indeed a

[014-112]maternal instinct which prompted me to save the infant. Kuvera,

[014-113]however, would not allow me to keep the boy, but ordered me to take

[014-114]him to the Queen Vasumati, that he might be brought up together with

[014-115]her son, who will one day become a great monarch.

[014-116]"Having performed the command of the god, I am permitted by him to

[014-117]find you out, and relieve you from your present distress."

[014-118]"So saying, she embraced me, and afterwards took me to a fairy palace

[014-119]in the forest, furnished with all comforts and luxuries, where I

[014-120]passed some time with her in great happiness.

[014-121]"One day, when she was expressing her great love for me, I said: 'I

[014-122]have a strong desire to take some vengeance on the king who would have

[014-123]put me to death.' Upon which, with a smile, she said, 'Ah! you wish

[014-124]to see Kantimati; I am not jealous, I will take you to her.'

[014-125]"Then lifting me up, she bore me through the air to the palace, and

[014-126]without disturbing the guards, placed me at the bedside of the king.

[014-127]"Grasping a sword lying near him, I awakened him, and said: 'I am,

[014-128]your son-in-law; I took your daughter without your consent, and am now

[014-129]come to make submission and atone for my fault."

[014-130]"Seeing the drawn sword held over him he was much alarmed, and said:

[014-131]'I must have been mad to act as I did and reject such a son-in-law; I

[014-132]will now acknowledge you, and you shall duly marry my daughter.'

[014-133]"He kept his word, the next day announced the intended marriage to all

[014-134]the court, and shortly afterwards publicly gave me his daughter.

[014-135]"Târâvali remained with me, became great friends with her fellow-wife,

[014-136]told her the story which she had related to me, and how her son had

[014-137]been preserved and was taken care of by Queen Vasumati.

[014-138]"Thus I have for some years lived happily, holding, as you know, a

[014-139]very important office."

[014-140][End of the story of Kâmapâla as told to his servant.]

[014-141]* * * * *

[014-142]"Some time after this, the death of the old king occurred, and as the

[014-143]eldest son had died during his father's lifetime, of consumption

[014-144]brought on by dissipation and debauchery; my master, together with the

[014-145]other ministers, placed Sinhaghosha, a boy about five years old, on

[014-146]the throne, and had him carefully educated.

[014-147]"As the young king grew older, he was surrounded by companions nearer

[014-148]his own age, and they not liking the restraint put upon them by the

[014-149]wise and prudent Kâmapâla, endeavoured secretly to excite a prejudice

[014-150]against him, saying, 'This fellow, who sets himself up to be so wise

[014-151]and virtuous, is a wicked wretch, who first seduced the princess, and

[014-152]then, having escaped the death he so well deserved, managed to get to

[014-153]the bedside of the sleeping king, and to frighten him into compliance

[014-154]with his demands. This Kâmapâla intends to make himself king; he

[014-155]poisoned your eldest brother, and only spared you in order to obtain

[014-156]the support of the people, knowing that the real power would remain in

[014-157]his own hands. Depend on it you will not be suffered to live when you

[014-158]are old enough to shake off his authority. If you wish to be safe you

[014-159]should get rid of him at once.'

[014-160]"With these, and other similar speeches, they so prejudiced the young

[014-161]king against his guardian and minister, that he would gladly have got

[014-162]rid of him at once, but was deterred by fear of the power of his

[014-163]Yaksha wife.

[014-164]"One day the queen, seeing the Princess Kantimati very sad, asked her

[014-165]the reason of her sadness, saying, 'Tell me the truth; you cannot

[014-166]deceive me; what is the cause of this depression?' 'Did I ever deceive

[014-167]you?' she answered; 'my friend and fellow-wife, Târâvali, has taken

[014-168]offence at something done or said by our husband, and though we tried

[014-169]to soothe her, she went away, and has not returned; this is the cause

[014-170]of my distress.'

[014-171]"The queen hearing this, immediately told her husband, 'Kâmapâla has

[014-172]quarrelled with his fairy wife, and she has left him. There is nothing

[014-173]now to prevent your proceeding against him as you please.'
