# Dream

## Definition

Dreams in the Hindoo Tales occupy a liminal space between reality and divine communication. They are treated not as mere psychological phenomena but as potentially prophetic visions — vehicles through which the gods signify their will, the future is revealed, and the boundary between waking reality and supernatural experience is tested. Characters repeatedly grapple with the question: was this a dream, or was it real?

## In the Narrative

The narrative's dream motif begins with a divine communication: the queen is signified in a dream that she will bear a son (001-139). A character reassures another that an extraordinary experience is not a mere dream (003-102). The prince has a remarkable dream that shapes his subsequent actions (007-017). The most sustained dream-reality confusion spans Chapters 16 and 17, where a character repeatedly asks whether his experience was reality or a dream (016-032, 016-067), ultimately finding that the supposed dream was in fact reality (016-118). A very wonderful dream in the forest provides another instance of the motif (017-004). Dreams in these tales consistently blur rather than clarify the boundary between inner vision and outer event.

> "…signified to the queen in a dream that she would bear a…" (001-139)

> "…ay know this not to be a mere dream, a…" (003-102)

> "reality or a dream, for on looking round me I sa…" (016-032)

> "…aving thus found the supposed dream to be a reality…" (016-118)
