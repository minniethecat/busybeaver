# Mithila

Mithila (also called Videha) is a prominent kingdom in the geography of the
*Daśakumāracarita*. Ruled successively by King [[praharavarma|Prahâravarma]] and King
[[vikatavarma|Vikatavarma]], it is the homeland of several princes and a key waypoint in
the wanderings of the companions.
(026-115)

## Geography and History

Mithila was situated in the region of Videha, in what is now northern Bihar.
The text identifies it both as a city and a country, with its capital serving
as the seat of royal power. It was connected by roads to [[Avanti]], [[Magadha]],
and other major kingdoms, making it a significant node in the political geography
of the narrative.
(012-008) (026-158)

> "Before entering into Mithila, the capital, I stopped to..."
> (012-008)

King [[praharavarma|Prahâravarma]] was an early ally and friend of [[rajahansa|King Râjahansa]] of [[magadha|Magadha]].
When calamity befell the realm, [[praharavarma|Prahâravarma]] and his family were forced to
flee, and the king sought refuge near a village where one of the infant
princes was discovered and raised.
(002-039) (002-088)

> "Prahâravarma, King of Mithila — that he, with his family, had gone to..."
> (002-039)

The kingdom later passed to [[vikatavarma|Vikatavarma]], who married [[kalpasundari|Kalpasundari]]. Throughout
the narrative, Mithila maintains its character as a land of refuge, diplomacy,
and royal alliance — a counterweight to the more martial kingdoms of the south.
(026-161)

## Role in the Narrative

Mithila enters the story early, as the destination of [[vasumati|Queen Vasumati]]'s friend,
the [[praharavarma|King of Mithila]], whose alliance with [[magadha|Magadha]] was cemented by friendship
and mutual interest.
(001-145)

> "King of Mithila, with his queen, a great friend of Vasumati..."
> (001-145)

The kingdom appears as a place of both refuge and opportunity. When the princes
were scattered, one of them entered Mithila's capital incognito, observing the
city before revealing himself. The text describes Mithila as a place where
strangers could find welcome and where political fortunes could be reversed
through diplomacy and courage.
(012-008) (012-048)

> "Mithila, to whom I am now going."
> (012-048)

At the great re[[marriage-in-the-tales|union]] of the companions, the [[praharavarma|King of Mithila]] was counted among
the allies and friends of the ten princes, alongside the lords of [[benares|Benâres]] and
other kingdoms — a testament to the web of alliances that Mithila anchored
in the political landscape of the epic.
(007-184)
