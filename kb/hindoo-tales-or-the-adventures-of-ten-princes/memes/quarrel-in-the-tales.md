# Quarrel in the Tales

## Summary

A quarrel in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes* is an open breach of accord between parties—spouses, neighbours, rulers, or allies—that the narrators treat as a distinct event from battle or intrigue. It is most often the *pretext* for action rather than the action itself: the excuse seized upon, the rift that sends a spouse away, or the discord deliberately sown by a third party to weaken an opponent.

## Context

The tales distinguish the quarrel from outright combat by foregrounding the moment of rupture. When a king sends a peremptory demand for the surrender of escaped prisoners, [[manapala|Mânapâla]] receives it as a gift: he "only wanted an excuse for a quarrel" (004-070), and the insulting message supplies it. Here the quarrel is a political instrument—a desired casus belli donning the mask of injured pride.

> "Mânapâla, who only wanted an excuse for a quarrel, having heard this insulting message, his eyes red with anger, answered,--'Who is the King of Lâta, that I should bow down to him?'"
> (004-070)

The marital quarrel operates on the same logic of rupture but within the household. When [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]] "quarrelled with his fairy wife, and she has left him" (014-172), the event is immediately weaponised: the queen reports it to her husband as proof that his rival is now defenceless. A private quarrel becomes, through gossip, a political opportunity.

> "'Kâmapâla has quarrelled with his fairy wife, and she has left him. There is nothing now to prevent your proceeding against him as you please.'"
> (014-172)

Two further facets complete the motif. There is the professional quarrel-champion—the strongman [[kalahakantaka|Kalahakantaka]], "ready at any time to take up the quarrel of a friend" (020-069)—who treats another's dispute as his own calling. And there is the *engineered* quarrel, the most politically dangerous form: corrupt ministers "stir up quarrels between neighbouring villages, and so cause party fights" (023-186), while a cunning conqueror, tasked with dividing spoils among his allies, contrives "by his intrigues, to make them quarrel over the division," so that "they fought with, and so weakened each other" (024-022) and he could annex the whole.

## Key Participants

- [[manapala|Mânapâla]] — who seizes an insulting message as the desired "excuse for a quarrel" (004-070); see [[intrigue-in-the-tales|Intrigue in the Tales]]
- [[kamapala|Kâmapâla]] and his fairy wife — whose marital quarrel becomes a political vulnerability (014-172); see [[marriage-in-the-tales|Marriage in the Tales]]
- [[kalahakantaka|Kalahakantaka]] — the "ready at any time" champion of others' quarrels (020-069)
- Corrupt ministers and conquerors — who manufacture quarrels to sow factional strife (023-186, 024-022); see [[deception-in-the-tales|Deception in the Tales]]

## Outcome

The quarrel functions in the tales as the seed of escalation: it is converted—by pride, gossip, or intrigue—into war, abandonment, or the engineered ruin of allies. It stands distinct from [[battle-in-the-tales|Battle]] (open combat) and [[attack-in-the-tales|Attack]] (direct assault) by dwelling on the moment of accord's rupture, and from [[revenge-in-the-tales|Revenge]] by being provoked rather than retaliatory.
