[010-001]I was struck by her beauty the instant she appeared on the stage; but

[010-002]when I heard her sweet voice, and saw her graceful movements, I was

[010-003]perfectly enchanted, and unable to take my eyes off her for a moment.

[010-004]The performance being ended, she withdrew, followed by the longing

[010-005]eyes and loud applause of the spectators; and giving, as I fancied, a

[010-006]significant look at me.

[010-007]The next day I was anxious, restless, and unable to eat; and could do

[010-008]nothing but roam about listlessly, or lie on the couch, thinking of

[010-009]her, and making the excuse of a bad headache.

[010-010]My friend, seeing me in this state, easily guessed the reason of it,

[010-011]and said to me in private: "I know the cause of your uneasiness, and

[010-012]can give you good hopes. That girl is virtuous, whatever her mother

[010-013]and sister may be; and having watched her closely at the performance,

[010-014]I am convinced that she was much struck with you; therefore, if you

[010-015]are willing to make her your wife, there will be no great difficulties

[010-016]to overcome as far as she is concerned; for, resisting all seductions

[010-017]and the persuasions of her wicked mother and sister, she has declared:

[010-018]'No man shall have me except as a wife; and I must be won by merit,

[010-019]not by money.'

[010-020]"On the other hand, her mother and sister, fearing lest she should be

[010-021]withdrawn from the stage, have gone to the king, and obtained, through

[010-022]many tears and entreaties, a decree that if any man shall take the

[010-023]girl, either in marriage or not, without her mother's consent, he

[010-024]shall be put to death like a robber. Therefore, when you have gained

[010-025]her love, you must also obtain the mother's consent; and that can only

[010-026]be done by means of a large bribe; she will not listen to any other

[010-027]inducement."

[010-028]"I am equal to all this," I answered; "I will win the young lady, and

[010-029]find means to satisfy the old one." And I lost no time in

[010-030]accomplishing my purpose. It was first necessary to make acquaintance

[010-031]with Kâmamanjari, and to this end I found out a woman often employed

[010-032]by her as a messenger, and having gained her over by bribes, sent,

[010-033]through her, a number of small presents, till at last Kâmamanjari was

[010-034]disposed in my favour, and received me at her house. Meanwhile I

[010-035]contrived to have secret interviews with her beautiful sister, who

[010-036]consented to be my wife. As soon as this was settled, I said to

[010-037]Kâmamanjari, "I am desirous of obtaining your mother's consent to my

[010-038]marriage with your sister, who has accepted me. I know that if she

[010-039]ceases to perform, you will lose a large income; and, therefore, offer

[010-040]you in return something better and more certain. Procure for me the

[010-041]desired permission, and you shall have Dhanamittra's magic purse,

[010-042]which I will safely steal for you."

[010-043]Delighted at the thought of possessing inexhaustible wealth, she

[010-044]agreed to this; the mother's consent was formally given; and on the

[010-045]day of my marriage I secretly handed over the promised purse.

[010-046]Very soon after, Vimardaka, by my directions, in a large assembly,

[010-047]began to abuse and insult Dhanamittra, who, as if much astonished,

[010-048]said: "What does all this mean? Why should you annoy me? I am not

[010-049]aware that I have ever given you offence."

[010-050]He answered furiously: "You purse-proud wretch, do you think I will

[010-051]not take my master's part? Have you not robbed him of his intended

[010-052]wife, by bribing her father? Do you think he has no cause for anger

[010-053]against you? His interests are mine; I am ready to risk my life for

[010-054]him, and I will pay you off. Some day you shall miss that purse, the

[010-055]source of the riches with which you are so puffed up." Saying this, he

[010-056]rushed out of the place in a rage; and though nothing was done at the

[010-057]time, his words were not forgotten.

[010-058]Then Dhanamittra went to the king, and declaring that he had lost the

[010-059]purse, mentioned his suspicion of Arthapati, and the reason for it.

[010-060]He, having heard nothing of what his servant had said, when summoned

[010-061]and asked "Have you a confidential servant named Vimardaka?" answered

[010-062]without hesitation, "Certainly; he is a very trustworthy man, entirely

[010-063]devoted to my interest."

[010-064]"Bring him here to me."

[010-065]Thus commanded, he searched everywhere for his servant, but was unable

[010-066]to find him; and for a good reason, for I had furnished the man with

[010-067]money, and sent him to Oujein, to look for you.

[010-068]The supposed thief having disappeared, his master was put in prison

[010-069]till further evidence could be procured, for no one but those in the

[010-070]secret doubted that he was the instigator of the theft.

[010-071]Meanwhile Kâmamanjari, anxious to make use of the magic purse,

[010-072]proceeded to fulfil the conditions attached to its use. She went

[010-073]secretly to Virúpaka, and restored the money of which she had robbed

[010-074]him, and then gave away all her furniture, clothes, and ornaments.

[010-075]This, however, she did so incautiously, that attention was drawn to

[010-076]it; upon which Dhanamittra went again to the king, saying: "I suspect

[010-077]that the actress, Kâmamanjari, has got my purse; for though

[010-078]notoriously avaricious, she is giving away everything she possesses,

[010-079]and there must be some strong reason for such a proceeding."

[010-080]In consequence of this information, she was summoned to appear the

[010-081]next day, together with her mother; and the two women came in great

[010-082]alarm to consult me.

[010-083]I said to Kâmamanjari: "No doubt you are suspected of having the

[010-084]purse. This suspicion has arisen from your own imprudence, in giving

[010-085]away your property so openly. I much fear that you will have to give

[010-086]it up, and you will be fortunate if you escape without worse

[010-087]consequences. But you must on no account implicate me; for then I

[010-088]should be put to death, all my property would be confiscated, your

[010-089]sister would die of grief, and you would be utterly ruined."

[010-090]She answered, with many tears: "It is indeed my own fault, but you

[010-091]shall be safe. That niggardly wretch, Arthapati, is known to be

[010-092]intimate with me. I will say that I received it from him; and, as he

[010-093]is already suspected of stealing it, I shall probably be believed."

[010-094]To this I agreed, and the next day, when questioned, she at first

[010-095]denied all knowledge of the purse, then admitted having received it,

[010-096]but refused to say from whom, and at last, when threatened with

[010-097]torture, confessed, apparently with great reluctance, that Arthapati

[010-098]was the giver; and this being considered sufficient evidence against

[010-099]him, he was condemned to death.

[010-100]Then Dhanamittra interceded for him, saying. "A decree was formerly

[010-101]made by one of your ancestors, that no merchant or trader should be

[010-102]put to death for theft. I humbly entreat, therefore, that his life may

[010-103]be spared."

[010-104]To this the king consented, the poor wretch was banished, and all his

[010-105]property confiscated, a portion of it being given to Kâmamanjari, at

[010-106]the earnest entreaty of Dhanamittra, who got back his purse, and

[010-107]shortly afterwards married Kulapâlika.

[010-108]Having thus performed the promise to my friend, I increased my own

[010-109]wealth, and kept up the reputation of the purse by going on with my

[010-110]robberies, and so impoverished the rich misers, that some of them were

[010-111]glad to receive a morsel of food from the beggars to whom they had

[010-112]formerly refused help, and who were now enriched by my liberality.

[010-113]Still no suspicion fell on me; but fate is all-powerful, and it was

[010-114]decreed that I should be caught at last.

[010-115]One night, sitting with my charming wife, intoxicated, partly with

[010-116]wine and partly with her sweet caresses, I was seized with madness,

[010-117]and started up, saying: "All the wealth in the city is not too much

[010-118]for you; I will fill the house with jewels for your sake." Then, like

[010-119]a furious elephant who has broken his chain, I rushed out, in spite of

[010-120]her remonstrances, with a drawn sword, and attacked a body of police,

[010-121]who happened to be passing. Shouting out, "This is the robber!" they

[010-122]soon overpowered me, and I fell to the ground.

[010-123]The shock sobered me at once, and all the horror of the situation into

[010-124]which I had brought myself by my folly came into my mind. I thought to

[010-125]myself, my intimacy with Dhanamittra is well known; suspicion will

[010-126]fall on him; and unless I can turn it off, he, as well as my wife,

[010-127]will be arrested to-morrow; and I quickly formed a plan by which they,

[010-128]and perhaps I myself, might be saved. But no time was to be lost; and

[010-129]as they were about to take me away, I called out to my wife's nurse,

[010-130]Sringâlika, who had followed me, "Begone, old wretch! and tell that

[010-131]vile harlot your mistress, and her paramour, Dhanamittra, that she

[010-132]will never see her ornaments, nor he his magic purse again. I care not

[010-133]for life, if I am revenged on those two wretches."

[010-134]The old woman being remarkably quick-witted, at once understood my

[010-135]object in speaking thus, and very humbly accosting the police said:

[010-136]"Worthy sir, I entreat you to wait a moment, while I ask your prisoner

[010-137]where he has hid the ornaments of my mistress."

[010-138]To, this they assented, and coming to me, she said: "O, sir, your

[010-139]jealousy is without cause; whatever attentions that man may have paid

[010-140]my mistress, she is not to blame. Now that you are taken from her, she

[010-141]will have no means of support, and must go on the stage again. How

[010-142]can she do this without her ornaments? Take compassion on her, and say

[010-143]where you have hid them."

[010-144]Then, as if my anger were appeased, I answered: "Why should I, who am

[010-145]about to die, harbour resentment? Come close, and I will whisper where

[010-146]I have put them." In this manner I managed to give her a few hurried

[010-147]instructions. She went away, with many blessings on me, and thanks to

[010-148]the men for their kindness; and I was taken to the king's prison.

[010-149]At that time the governor of the prison was a very conceited young

[010-150]man, named Kantaka, who had lately succeeded to the office by the

[010-151]death of his father. When I was brought in, looking at me in a very

[010-152]contemptuous manner, he said: "So you are the thief who has committed

[010-153]so many robberies. If you do not give up the stolen property, and

[010-154]especially the magic purse, you shall suffer every possible variety of

[010-155]torture before you are put to death."

[010-156]I answered, smiling, "Even though I should give up all the other

[010-157]stolen property, I will never let the purse go back to that wretch

[010-158]Dhanamittra, my greatest enemy. You may try all your tortures; you

[010-159]will never get this secret out of me."

[010-160]Finding the fear of torture to have no effect, the next day he tried

[010-161]promises; and so went on from day to day, with alternate soothing and

[010-162]threatening.

[010-163]Meanwhile, my wounds were attended to, and I was well fed; so that I

[010-164]had regained my strength when, one day, Sringâlika made her

[010-165]appearance, well dressed, and with cheerful countenance.

[010-166]To my surprise, she was allowed to speak to me in private. She said to

[010-167]me, joyfully "Your plan has succeeded. As you directed, I went to

[010-168]Dhanamittra and told him, from you: 'You must go to the king, and say,

[010-169]"The magic purse so lately restored has again been stolen by one whom

[010-170]I regarded as a friend--a certain gambler, the husband of the actress

[010-171]Râgamanjari. He has taken it from spite, being jealous of his wife, to

[010-172]whom, from kindness, I often made presents. He is now in prison for

[010-173]other offences; and if, he is put to death immediately, as he

[010-174]deserves, I fear that I shall never recover my purse. I pray,

[010-175]therefore, that he may not be executed before he has confessed where

[010-176]it is concealed. For he admits having taken it; but declares that he

[010-177]will not give it up, unless his life is spared." Your friend,

[010-178]admiring your ingenuity, and having full confidence in your resources,

[010-179]immediately went to the king and obtained his request, so that your

[010-180]life is safe for the present.'
