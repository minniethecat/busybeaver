# Oath in the Tales

## Summary

The oath in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes* operates as a binding speech act with narrative force: once sworn, it creates obligations that characters must honor or navigate. Unlike casual promises, oaths carry the weight of solemn declaration and shape the course of subsequent events.

> "This man has sworn an oath that I should be liberated when the work was done."
> (011-074)

The oath motif appears at critical junctures—moments of captivity, obligation, and repeated entreaty—where words alone must suffice as guarantee.

## Context

Oaths in the tales function as instruments of constraint and liberation. A solemn oath binds a captor to release his prisoner: "a solemn oath that I should be liberated when the work was done" (011-068). The prisoner's subsequent reflection—"This man has sworn an oath" (011-074)—reveals the psychological weight of the sworn word; the oath is not merely received but internalized as a basis for hope and expectation.

The repetition of oaths signals their fragility as much as their force. A character "repeated his former oaths" (013-109) in response to continued skepticism, suggesting that oaths, once doubted, require reiteration to maintain their binding power. The need to repeat underscores the gap between the ideal of the oath—an unbreakable bond—and the social reality in which words can be questioned.

The oath in the tales is thus a double-edged device: it can liberate (the oath of release) or entrap (the oath that must be repeatedly sworn to skeptical ears).

## Key Participants

- Unnamed captor — swears the solemn oath of liberation (011-068)
- The prisoner — receives and reflects upon the oath as the basis for hope (011-074)
- The oath-swearer — repeats his oaths to overcome doubt (013-109)

## Outcome

Oaths in the tales reveal a world where the spoken word carries binding force, yet remains vulnerable to doubt and the need for repetition. The successful fulfillment of an oath—liberation when the work is done—affirms the providential order, while the need to repeat oaths acknowledges the imperfection of human trust.
