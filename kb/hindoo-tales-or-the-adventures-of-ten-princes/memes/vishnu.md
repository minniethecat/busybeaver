# Vishnu

Vishnu is one of the three principal deities of the Hindu pantheon who is
invoked and referenced throughout the *Daśakumāracarita*. He appears in visions,
is associated with divine justice, and is named alongside [[brahma]] and [[siva]]
as part of the divine triad.
(026-119)
(026-120)

## Biography

Vishnu is described as a powerful god who can both punish and guide. A king
suffered divine retribution after offending him:

> "by Vishnu whom he had offended, and as he had no son, I was left his heir
> and successor, and suffered great distress and perplexity."
> (003-141)
> (003-142)

Vishnu's deeds are depicted in art, including the famous scene of his victory
over the demon Hiranyakasipu:

> "After this, he represented the scene of Vishnu killing Hiranyakasipu"
> (006-165)

He appears in visions to offer guidance and encouragement:

> "that Vishnu appeared to me, and said: 'Go on boldly, without hesitation;
> what you are about to do, though it may seem sinful, is...'"
> (012-174)
> (012-175)

He is identified by the name Nârâyana:

> "_Nârâyana_, a name of Vishnu, an incarnation of the three principal gods,
> Brahma, Vishnu, Siva."
> (026-119)
> (026-120)

## Role in the Narrative

Vishnu serves as a divine force of justice and guidance. His offense against a
king results in the king losing his heir, setting in motion events that propel
the narrative forward.
(003-141)

He dispenses [[pleasure|enjoyment]] and blessings to kings:

> "has been all enjoyed by Vishnu, and by former kings"
> (025-141)

His representation in dramatic performances indicates that his mythology was
well known and formed part of the cultural fabric of the world depicted in
the *Daśakumāracarita*.
(006-165)

## Relationships

Vishnu is one of the three principal gods alongside [[brahma]] and [[siva]].
