[017-001]I asked her, therefore: "Will you allow me to examine that picture?"

[017-002]She put it into my hand; and I drew on it the princess lying as I had

[017-003]seen her; and giving it back, said: "One night, while sleeping in a

[017-004]forest, I had a very wonderful dream. I found myself lying in just

[017-005]such a room as that which is represented in this painting; and saw

[017-006]there a very beautiful young lady, such as I have painted here; could

[017-007]that have been anything more than a dream?"

[017-008]When she heard this, her face lighted up, and she answered: "That was

[017-009]no dream, but a reality; and you are indeed the person I was looking

[017-010]for." Then she told me the whole story; how the princess had seen and

[017-011]fallen in love with me; and how she had painted that picture and

[017-012]given it to her friend, that it might be the means of discovering me;

[017-013]and how delighted she would now be to hear that I was found at last.

[017-014]I begged her to assure the princess that I was even more anxious to

[017-015]see her, and had come to Sravasti solely from the hope of finding her.

[017-016]"If your friend is disposed to favour me," I continued, "beg her to

[017-017]wait patiently a few days; I will arrange a plan which will enable us

[017-018]to be together in her apartments, without danger to either of us." To

[017-019]this she agreed, and having taken leave of her, I went back to the

[017-020]village where the old brahman lived, whom I had met at the cock-fight.

[017-021]I found him at home, and delighted to see me. After I was rested and

[017-022]refreshed, he asked me, "What has brought you back so soon? is there

[017-023]anything in which you require my assistance?"

[017-024]"There is,"' I answered, "a very important affair, in which you can

[017-025]materially assist me. The King of the Sravastans, Dharmavardhana,[8]

[017-026]whose character corresponds with his name, has a very beautiful

[017-027]daughter. By an extraordinary chance, I have seen and fallen in love

[017-028]with her. I have reason to believe that she was equally struck by me,

[017-029]but know not how to contrive a meeting between us without your help;

[017-030]will you therefore assist me?"

[017-031]"What is your plan?" he asked, "and how can I be of service in

[017-032]carrying it out?"

[017-033]"My plan is this," I replied. "I will dress as a woman, and pass for

[017-034]your daughter; and you are so clever and ready-witted, that I think

[017-035]you will be able to get me into the palace as a companion to the

[017-036]princess, and even to manage so that she shall become my wife." Then I

[017-037]told him how I thought this might be accomplished; and he quite

[017-038]approved of what I proposed, entered into it with great spirit, and

[017-039]promised his ready co-operation.

[017-040]Accordingly, the first day that the king was sitting in public to

[017-041]administer justice, the old man approached, followed by me dressed as

[017-042]a woman, walking modestly behind him, and bowing down to the king, he

[017-043]said: "My lord, I have heard of your great beneficence, and how you

[017-044]are the father of all your subjects, the protector and friend of the

[017-045]helpless; I am therefore come to ask a great favour. This girl is my

[017-046]only daughter. Her mother died soon after her birth. I have brought

[017-047]her up, and she has never left me; but I am desirous now to be

[017-048]relieved of this charge and to see her well married. A long time ago,

[017-049]she was engaged to a young brahman, who went to Oujein, to study

[017-050]there, and acquire the means of supporting a wife and family. I have

[017-051]been expecting his return for some time, but have heard nothing of

[017-052]him; I am, therefore, very uneasy on my daughter's account, and

[017-053]purpose to go to Oujein, and find out whether he is alive or dead. I

[017-054]cannot leave my daughter alone, and have no friend or near relation

[017-055]with whom I can place her. Will your majesty deign to allow her to

[017-056]remain under your protection until my return?"

[017-057]To this the king graciously assented, and I was received into the

[017-058]palace, where I soon found means of letting the princess know of my

[017-059]disguise, and was taken into her apartments as one of her immediate

[017-060]attendants.

[017-061]Thus our wishes were gratified, and we enjoyed uninterrupted

[017-062]intercourse with each other. But more was yet to be done, and when the

[017-063]time was nearly arrived at which it had been arranged between me and

[017-064]the old brahman that he was to come to fetch me, I said to my darling:

[017-065]"To-morrow, as you know, there will be a procession to a certain holy

[017-066]place near the river; you and your attendants will join in it and have

[017-067]an opportunity of bathing there. While we are in the water, I will

[017-068]scream out, as if drowning, and, diving underneath the surface, will

[017-069]come up among the bushes a long way off, without being seen. Do you

[017-070]appear greatly distressed at my death; but fear nothing, I shall soon

[017-071]come to you again."

[017-072]Accordingly, the next day, while bathing in the Ganges, I made it

[017-073]appear as if I were accidentally carried out of my depth and drawn in

[017-074]by one of the eddies of the river, and screamed out loudly for help.

[017-075]My cries and screams and subsequent disappearance caused a great

[017-076]commotion, and long search was made for my body; but of course in

[017-077]vain, for I had dived under, and come to the surface unobserved among

[017-078]the thick bushes at the place which had been agreed upon. There,

[017-079]having gone on shore, I soon found the old brahman, who was waiting

[017-080]for me with a suit of men's clothes, and, putting them on, I walked

[017-081]quietly with him into the town.

[017-082]The next day, as if he had heard nothing of the loss of his pretended

[017-083]daughter, he went to the king, accompanied by me, and said "My lord, I

[017-084]have returned from Oujein, and have brought with me this young man,

[017-085]the intended husband of my daughter, with whom I am much pleased, and

[017-086]whom I can confidently recommend to your favour, for I have heard an

[017-087]exceedingly good report of him there. He is not only very learned in

[017-088]the vedas and commentaries, advanced in science and arts, well

[017-089]instructed in politics and history, clever in reciting stories and

[017-090]poetry, but is a bold and skilful rider, a good archer and swordsman.

[017-091]There is scarcely anything that a young man should know, with which he

[017-092]is not familiar; and, with all this, he is free from conceit,

[017-093]good-tempered, gentle, and kind; in short, he seems to me almost

[017-094]perfect, and more fit to marry a princess than the daughter of such a

[017-095]man as I am. When I have seen my child happily married to him, I shall

[017-096]not trouble them with my society, but withdraw from the world, and

[017-097]end my days in a hermitage. I have now come to take back my daughter,

[017-098]with the most humble and heartfelt gratitude for the gracious

[017-099]protection which you have so kindly afforded her." With these words he

[017-100]bowed himself to the ground in humble obeisance.

[017-101]On hearing this the king was greatly perplexed, and obliged to admit

[017-102]that the girl had been drowned while bathing, and that her body had

[017-103]not been found.

[017-104]Then the old man began to tear his hair, beat his breast, and show

[017-105]signs of the most extravagant grief, calling on the king to restore

[017-106]his dear daughter, and reproaching him with having caused her death.

[017-107]In vain did the king make him large offers of compensation; he refused

[017-108]them all, declaring it to be his firm intention to put himself to

[017-109]death at the gate of the palace, and so cause the sin to fall on the

[017-110]king's head.[9]

[017-111]He, despairing of finding any other way of appeasing the old man,

[017-112]after some consideration and consultation with his ministers, said to

[017-113]him: "You have told me that your intended son-in-law is a young man of

[017-114]rare abilities, and more fit to be the husband of a princess than of

[017-115]your daughter, and his appearance is very prepossessing; I offer him

[017-116]then my daughter in the place of yours. Will this satisfy you?" Then

[017-117]at last the old man professed to be contented; I was treated with much

[017-118]honour, in due time became the husband of the princess, and reached

[017-119]the summit of my wishes.

[017-120]After a time, an army was sent by my father-in-law to the assistance

[017-121]of the King of Anga, and, thinking of the possibility of meeting you

[017-122]here, I solicited and obtained the command of it, and my hopes have

[017-123]been fulfilled, since I have now the great pleasure of seeing you.

[017-124]Having heard this story, the prince remarked: "You have done no deeds

[017-125]of blood, but have gained your ends by gentleness and ingenuity. This

[017-126]is the way approved of by the wise." Then turning to Mitragupta, he

[017-127]said "It is now your turn," and he immediately began his story thus:--

[017-128]* * * * *

[017-129]ADVENTURES OF MITRAGUPTA.

[017-130]My lord, I set out on my travels in search of you, like the rest, and

[017-131]arriving one day at Damalipta, I saw a great crowd collected in a

[017-132]large park outside the city. While looking about me to find some one

[017-133]of whom I might inquire what this festival was, I espied a young man,

[017-134]sitting alone in an arbour, amusing himself with playing on a lute.

[017-135]Going up to him, I asked "What is this concourse of people? Why do you

[017-136]sit here alone, away from the others?"

[017-137]He answered: "A long time ago, the king of this country, having no

[017-138]children, made many prayers and offerings to the goddess Durgâ, in

[017-139]the hope of propitiating her. At last she appeared to him in a dream,

[017-140]and said: 'Your prayer is granted; your wife shall bear twins--a

[017-141]daughter who must be your successor, and a son who must be subject to

[017-142]her and to her husband when she marries. Further, it is my will and

[017-143]pleasure that, beginning from her seventh year, you shall make, every

[017-144]month when the moon is in the constellation Krittika (or the

[017-145]Pleiades), a great festival, to be called the Festival of the Ball

[017-146]Dance, at which she shall publicly exhibit her skill before the

[017-147]people. I also will, that in reference to a husband, she shall have

[017-148]free choice without any pressure on your part, and that he whom she

[017-149]marries shall have equal power with her, and reign after your death.'

[017-150]"The promise given in the dream was fulfilled. The queen bore

[017-151]twins--a son and a daughter. The king has duly obeyed the commands of

[017-152]the goddess, and to-day the princess, whose name is Kandukavati, will

[017-153]again perform the ball dance for the propitiation of Durgâ in the

[017-154]sight of the people here assembled.

[017-155]"You asked me also why I am sitting here alone. I will tell you. The

[017-156]Princess Kandukavati has a dear friend and foster-sister, who is

[017-157]engaged to me.

[017-158]"Of late, Bhimadhanwa, the brother of the princess, has cast his eyes

[017-159]on her, and persecuted her with his importunities. Knowing his

[017-160]character, I have great fear lest some day he should use violence

[017-161]towards her. This is why I am so anxious and uneasy, and have no

[017-162]inclination to join in the festivities."

[017-163]Just then I heard the tinkling of anklets, and a young lady came to

[017-164]the place where we were sitting.

[017-165]On seeing her, my companion started up with great delight, and, taking

[017-166]her by the hand, introduced her to me, saying: "This is the lady whom

[017-167]I have told you of, dearer to me than life, the thought of separation

[017-168]from whom, through the wickedness of that wretch, burns me like fire,

[017-169]and causes me to suffer misery greater than death. I have no loyalty

[017-170]or respect towards him, and will lose my life rather than suffer him

[017-171]to accomplish his wicked purpose."
