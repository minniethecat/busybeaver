# Feast in the Tales

## Summary

Feasts and festivals punctuate the social world of the *Adventures of the Ten Princes*. These public celebrations bring characters together in ways that advance the plot, enabling encounters that would not occur in ordinary settings.

The Feast of Spring is a particularly significant occasion, drawing characters into the public park where fateful meetings take place.

> "he saw you when you were walking in the public park, at the feast of Spring, and immediately became a mark for the arrows of Kâma."
> (012-133)

## Context

The Feast of Spring (012-133) serves as the backdrop for a romantic encounter: a young prince in disguise sees a woman in the public park and is struck by desire. The festival setting dissolves social barriers and enables the meeting.

In a darker register, the text alludes to a ghoul who goes out at "night to feast on dead bodies" (026-193), a folkloric motif that contrasts with the celebratory feasts of the court.

## Key Participants

- [[kama]] — the [[kama|god of love]] whose arrows strike during the Feast of Spring

- [[festival-of-kama]] — the festival that shares thematic kinship with the spring feast

## Outcome

Feasts in the tales function as narrative thresholds. The public, celebratory space of the feast temporarily suspends ordinary social rules, allowing encounters, attractions, and intrigues to unfold. The contrast between courtly feasts and darker folkloric feast-imagery reflects the work's range of tone.
