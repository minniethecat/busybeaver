# Marriage in the Tales of Ten Princes

Marriage is the culminating event type in the *Daśakumāracarita*, serving as
the narrative resolution for nearly every prince's adventure. More than a
personal union, each marriage in the text functions simultaneously as a
romantic fulfillment, a political alliance, and a marker of restored cosmic
order. The text presents marriage as the institution through which the
scattered energies of desire, ambition, and duty are gathered into harmony.
(004-013)

## Marriage as Narrative Structure

The *Daśakumāracarita* employs marriage as its fundamental plot mechanism.
Each prince's tale follows a pattern: separation from the brotherhood,
wandering, adventure, encounter with a princess, obstacles overcome, and
union in marriage. This recurring structure gives the sprawling narrative
coherence — marriage is the destination toward which every adventure tends.
(005-079)

> "after which there will be no obstacle to our marriage; for, when I ask..."
> (005-079)

The text explicitly treats marriage as something that must be earned through
cleverness, courage, and the navigation of social obstacles. It is not
bestowed freely but won through the demonstration of worthiness — a narrative
logic that aligns marriage with the heroic ethos of the tales.

> "the marriage can be celebrated with greater magnificence in his..."
> (004-017)

## Key Marriages in the Text

### Râjavâhana and Avantisundari

The central marriage of the epic unites the hero [[Râjavâhana]] with
[[Avantisundari]], princess of [[Avanti]]. Their union is orchestrated by
the conjuror [[vidyeswara|Vidyeswara]], who transforms a chance encounter at the
[[Festival of Kâma]] into a royal wedding. This marriage is framed as
destined — the fulfillment of a connection that spans multiple existences.
(006-169) (006-177) (026-160)

> "therefore, to represent a royal marriage, and one of my people will..."
> (006-169)

> "the conjurer, being a brahman, was able to complete the marriage with..."
> (006-177)

### Somadatta and Vâmalochana

[[Somadatta]] married Vâmalochana, the daughter of [[viraketu|Víraketu]], cementing
his position within the web of alliances that structure the narrative's
political geography.
(026-154)

### Other Princely Marriages

The text recounts numerous other unions: the marriage of a sovereign's
daughter sought and refused (004-013); the marriage that one prince
opposes but cannot prevent (008-055); the arranged betrothal from which
a clever heroine escapes (009-049); and the marriage that a scheming
suitor attempts to usurp.
(009-050) (009-121)

> "consent to our marriage, and, in spite of my reluctance, is determined..."
> (009-049)

### Dhanamittra's Marriage

Among the most dramatic marriage plots is that of [[Dhanamittra]], whose
engagement to a rich man's daughter is threatened by a rival. The story
involves secret handovers, accusations before the king, and the eventual
triumph of true love secured through wit and the magic purse.
(009-132) (010-045) (010-023)

> "the day of my marriage I secretly handed over the..."
> (010-045)

## Marriage as Political Alliance

Throughout the text, marriage functions as diplomacy. The union of
[[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]] with [[avantisundari|Avantisundari]] binds [[Magadha]] to [[Avanti]]; the marriages
of other princes create a network of allied kingdoms that, by the epic's
end, encompasses much of the known world. This political dimension elevates
marriage from personal romance to the mechanism of cosmic restoration —
when the rightful kings are married and enthroned, dharma is restored.
