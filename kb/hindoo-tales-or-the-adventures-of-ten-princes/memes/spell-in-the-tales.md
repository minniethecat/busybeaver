# Spell in the Tales

## Summary

The spell motif in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes* appears as a concentrated supernatural device within a single chapter, governing the central conflict of [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]]'s narrative. The laying and removal of a spell determines the fate of a marriage and drives the plot toward its resolution.

> "The reason of your being childless is that a spell has been laid upon your husband."
> (020-103)

Unlike the broader motifs of [[magic-in-the-tales|magic]] or [[curse-in-the-tales|curses]], the spell here is a specific, removable enchantment with clear cause and remedy.

## Context

The spell appears in Chapter 20, where [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]] learns the cause of her childlessness. The revelation comes as a direct statement: a spell has been laid upon her husband (020-103). The remedy is presented as a process—"by-and-by you will have children" (020-110)—suggesting that spells in this world are not permanent but can be undone through proper intervention.

The removal of the spell requires [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]]'s consent and participation: "Anxious to have the spell removed from her husband, [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]] consented to this" (020-111). This frames the spell not merely as external magic but as a narrative mechanism that tests character and demands agency.

Unlike the broader magical events in the tales—visions, divine interventions, miraculous transformations—the spell operates as a targeted affliction whose mechanics are understood and discussed by the characters.

## Key Participants

- [[nitambavati]] — wife of the afflicted husband, whose consent is required for the spell's removal (020-111)
- [[nitambavati|Nitambavati]]'s husband — the unnamed target of the spell, rendered childless by its effect (020-103)

## Outcome

The spell narrative reinforces a pattern in the tales: supernatural afflictions are temporary obstacles that can be overcome through knowledge and proper action. The spell's removal opens the path to the desired outcome—children and marital fulfillment—demonstrating the providential framework within which even magic operates.
