# Wealth

## Definition

Wealth (Artha) is one of the three great aims of human life in Hindu philosophy, alongside [[virtue|Virtue]] ([[virtue|Dharma]]) and [[pleasure|Pleasure]] ([[kama|Kama]]). It represents material prosperity, economic resources, and the means to support oneself and one's dependents in the world. In the narrative, wealth appears both as a philosophical ideal debated among the learned and as a practical force driving the plot forward.

## In the Narrative

Wealth features prominently in two distinct registers. In Chapter 8, it is the subject of philosophical debate — compared against virtue and pleasure as an aim of life, with arguments made for its necessity: without wealth, neither virtue nor pleasure can be sustained. The debaters ultimately acknowledge that wealth, properly acquired and used, is a legitimate human aim.

In Chapters 9 and 10, wealth takes narrative centre stage through the story of the magic purse — an inexhaustible source of gold that transforms the fortunes of its possessor. The sudden acquisition of wealth brings with it the problem of explaining its origin without incurring suspicion, illustrating the social dimension of prosperity in the world of the tales.

> "wrong in reckoning virtue, wealth and pleasure as the three great…" (008-080)

> "It is not beauty or wealth, but the approbation of the…" (008-163)

> "…er bag will give you abundant wealth." (009-144)
