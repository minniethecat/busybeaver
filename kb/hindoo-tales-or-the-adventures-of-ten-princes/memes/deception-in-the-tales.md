# Deception in the Tales

## Summary

Deception pervades the *Adventures of the Ten Princes* as a practical art. The translator singles it out as a defining trait of the world depicted: "Unscrupulous deception, ready invention, extreme credulity and superstition, and disregard of human life, are strongly illustrated" (001-027). It runs from the lone confidence trickster to the systematic cheating of kings by their own ministers.

## Context

The tales' most developed portrait of deception is the "old cheat" of the twentieth chapter, who preys upon a childless lady with a fabricated tale. "With this story the old cheat went again to the lady, who, believing her to be sincere, gladly accepted the offer" (020-101), never suspecting the deception being practised on her (020-024). The trick depends entirely upon the victim's "extreme credulity" (001-027).

> "With this story the old cheat went again to the lady, who, believing her to be sincere, gladly accepted the offer."
> (020-101)

The motif scales upward into governance. While pretending to keep exact accounts, corrupt ministers "have forty thousand ways of cheating him, and take good care of themselves" (023-067). Deception thus appears as a social constant—practised by the lone trickster and the minister of state alike.

## Key Participants

- The "old cheat" of the twentieth chapter — a confidence trickster who fabricates a story to defraud a childless lady (020-101)
- Corrupt ministers — who cheat the king under cover of exact accounts (023-067); see [[intrigue-in-the-tales|Intrigue in the Tales]]
- The credulous lady — whose trust enables the deception (020-101)
