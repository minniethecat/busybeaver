# Torture in the Tales

## Summary

Torture appears in nine passages across the *Adventures of the Ten Princes* as an instrument of judicial coercion and punishment. Victims face torture to extract confessions, as threatened punishment, or as a prelude to execution—and the response to torture becomes a test of character.

> "Put to death by torture, and his paramour be shut up..."
> (007-087)

## Context

Torture in the tales is embedded in the judicial apparatus of ancient Indian kingdoms. When suspects refuse to confess, they are "compelled by torture" to reveal hidden wealth or crimes (003-059). The threat of torture is deployed strategically—officials warn prisoners that they will face "torture to-morrow, unless we" obtain what we seek (004-055).

> "Finding the fear of torture to have no effect, the next day..."
> (010-160)

The most revealing torture episode spans Chapter 10, where a character faces a classic interrogation. The officials "torture" him and he "confessed, apparently with great reluctance" (010-097). Yet when threatened with further torture—"you shall be put to the torture before you are put to death" (010-155)—another character responds with defiance: "You may try all your tortures; you cannot shake my resolution" (010-158).

> "But to put him to torture. No doubt the king will give..."
> (011-054)

The metaphysical dimension of torture appears in Chapter 3, where a character journeys to the afterlife and witnesses "the tortures of the wicked" (003-071), linking earthly judicial torture to cosmic punishment.

## Key Participants

- [[apahâravarma]] — threatened with death by torture (007-087)

- [[somadatta]] — witnesses the tortures of the wicked in the afterlife (003-071)

- The king — authorizes torture as judicial procedure (011-054)

## Outcome

Torture in the tales functions as a narrative test of character. Those who withstand torture without breaking demonstrate virtue and fortitude; those who confess under duress reveal weakness. The pattern reinforces the moral calculus of the work: suffering endured with courage earns narrative reward.

The cosmic dimension—torture as both earthly punishment and afterlife consequence—connects the judicial practice to the tales' broader theological framework of karma and retribution.
