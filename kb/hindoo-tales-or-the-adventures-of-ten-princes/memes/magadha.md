# Magadha

Magadha is the central kingdom of the *Daśakumāracarita*, ruled by King
[[râjahansa]] and later restored by his son [[râjavâhana]] and the ten
princes. Its capital, [[pushpapuri|Pushpapuri]] (also identified with [[pushpapuri|Pâtaliputra]]), is
described as magnificent as a mine of jewels.
(001-086) (026-100)

## Description

Magadha was the premier kingdom of the narrative world — wealthy, cultured,
and politically central. Its capital [[pushpapuri|Pushpapuri]] was renowned for its splendour
and served as the seat of the royal dynasty. The kingdom's prosperity, however,
was shadowed by one great sorrow: [[rajahansa|King Râjahansa]] had no son to succeed him
until the miraculous birth of [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]].
(001-086) (001-136)

> "Pushpapuri, the capital of Magadha, magnificent as a mine of jewels"
> (001-086)

[[rajahansa|King Râjahansa]] maintained alliances with neighbouring kingdoms, including
[[mithila|Mithila]], and the queen [[vasumati|Vasumati]] was a great friend of the [[praharavarma|King of Mithila]].
These webs of diplomacy and friendship defined Magadha's position in the
political landscape.

> "Magadha had one great cause of sorrow and anxiety — he had no son"
> (001-136)

After the conquest by [[manasara|Mânasâra]] of [[malwa|Mâlwa]], [[rajahansa|Râjahansa]] became a wanderer, visiting
holy places across India. His eventual return and the restoration of Magadha
form the resolution of the epic.
(002-158) (004-130)

## Significance in the Narrative

Magadha is the birthplace and rightful inheritance of [[rajavahana|Râjavâhana]], the first
and foremost of the ten princes. The kingdom's fall to [[manasara|Mânasâra]] of [[malwa|Mâlwa]] is
the inciting catastrophe that scatters the infant princes across India.
(001-130) (026-131)

> "Râjahansa, king of Magadha, father of Râjavâhana, the hero of the..."
> (026-131)

Throughout the narrative, Magadha serves as the symbolic centre of rightful
rule. The ten princes' individual adventures all lead back toward the
restoration of [[rajahansa|Râjahansa]] to his throne. The kingdom represents legitimacy,
order, and the proper seat of dharma in the epic's political geography.
(002-101)

> "King of Magadha, arrived in the island, and having become acquainted..."
> (002-101)

## Appearances

Magadha is referenced across Chapters 1, 2, 4, 7, 8, 12, 16, 24, and the
proper names index in Chapter 26. It is the most frequently mentioned kingdom
in the narrative, appearing both as a geographical location and as the
political anchor of the entire epic.
