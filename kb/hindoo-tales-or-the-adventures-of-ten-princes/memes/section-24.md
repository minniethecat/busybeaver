[024-001]"By these and many other devices, they succeeded in destroying life

[024-002]and weakening the country, so that less resistance might be offered to

[024-003]the invader.

[024-004]"Then, thinking the time to be arrived, the King of Asmaka prepared

[024-005]for war. Meanwhile, his emissary was leading on the foolish young king

[024-006]to destruction; and at this very time, as if in perfect security, he

[024-007]was amusing himself with the performances of a celebrated actress and

[024-008]dancer, having, at the instigation of his treacherous friend,

[024-009]persuaded her, by large donations, to leave the King of Kuntala, with

[024-010]whom she was a great favourite.

[024-011]"Indignant at such an insult, that king was easily persuaded to join

[024-012]the King of Asmaka, who had already obtained several other allies

[024-013]eager to have a share in the expected conquest and plunder.

[024-014]"Thus, when the country was actually invaded, no effectual resistance

[024-015]was made; Anantavarma was easily defeated, and fell into the power of

[024-016]his cruel enemy.

[024-017]"The cunning King of Asmaka, who had gained his allies by many liberal

[024-018]promises, had no intention of sharing the conquered country with any

[024-019]one; he professed, however, great disinterestedness; declared that he

[024-020]should be contented with a very small part; and, having desired his

[024-021]allies to arrange between themselves what each should take, contrived,

[024-022]by his intrigues, to make them quarrel over the division. The result

[024-023]was that they fought with, and so weakened each other, that he was

[024-024]able to disregard their claims, and to annex the whole of the

[024-025]conquered country to his own dominions.

[024-026]"After the defeat and death of Anantavarma, an old and faithful

[024-027]minister escaped with the queen and her two children, this boy and his

[024-028]elder sister Manjuvâdini, together with a few faithful followers,

[024-029]including myself; and though the old minister was taken ill and died

[024-030]on the road, the rest arrived safely at Mahishmati, where the queen

[024-031]was well received by the king Amittravarma, a half-brother of her

[024-032]husband, and where she devoted herself to the education of her son,

[024-033]hoping that he might one day recover his father's kingdom.

[024-034]"After a time, however, that king sought to marry his brother's widow;

[024-035]and, having been rejected by her, determined to take revenge by

[024-036]killing her son.

[024-037]"The queen, having discovered his intentions, sent for me, and said:

[024-038]'My life is wrapped up in this boy; I can endure any thing, so long as

[024-039]he is safe; take him and make your escape at once; I know not where to

[024-040]send you, but if you can find a safe refuge, let me know, and I will

[024-041]come to you, if possible.'

[024-042]"In obedience to her commands, I took the boy, succeeded in escaping

[024-043]with him, and reached a shepherd's hut on the borders of this forest.

[024-044]There we stayed a few days till I saw a man whom I suspected to be

[024-045]searching for us. Fearing discovery, I left the cottage, and entered

[024-046]the forest. Here, while trying to get water to quench the poor child's

[024-047]burning thirst, I slipped into the well, where I should have perished

[024-048]but for your timely assistance; and now, having done us this kindness,

[024-049]will you add to it by protecting the boy, and helping us to reach a

[024-050]place of safety?"

[024-051]"Who was his mother," I asked. "Of what family was she?"

[024-052]"She is the daughter of the King of Oude," he answered, "and her

[024-053]mother was Sagaradatta, daughter of Vaisravana, a merchant of

[024-054]Pâtaliputra."

[024-055]"If so," I replied, "she and my father are cousins by the mother's

[024-056]side; this boy is therefore my relation, and has a right to my

[024-057]protection."

[024-058]The old man was much pleased at hearing this, and I promised not only

[024-059]to protect the boy, but to contrive some means for reinstating him in

[024-060]his proper position, and overcoming that wicked King of Asmaka with

[024-061]cunning equal to his own.

[024-062]For the present, however, the most needful thing was to procure food.

[024-063]While I was considering how to obtain this, two deer passed, pursued

[024-064]by a forester, who shot three arrows and missed them, and, in despair,

[024-065]let fall his bow and two remaining arrows. Hastily snatching up these,

[024-066]I discharged the arrows in rapid succession, and killed both the deer;

[024-067]one of them I gave to the hunter, the other I prepared, and roasted a

[024-068]part of it for ourselves.

[024-069]The forester was astonished by my skill, and delighted at the

[024-070]acquisition of so much food; and it occurred to me that I might get

[024-071]some information from him. I asked him therefore: "Do you know

[024-072]anything of what is going on at Mahishmati?"

[024-073]"I was there early this morning," he answered, "for I had a tiger skin

[024-074]and other skins to sell, and great festivities were in preparation;

[024-075]the Prince Prachandavarma, the king's younger brother, is about to

[024-076]marry the Princess Manjuvâdini, and the rejoicings are on this

[024-077]account."

[024-078]After the forester was gone, I said to the old man (whose name was

[024-079]Nâlijangha): "That wretch Amittravarma is trying to make it up with

[024-080]his sister-in-law by promoting a good marriage for her daughter; no

[024-081]doubt he thinks to persuade her to recall her son, that he may have

[024-082]him in his power. Do you therefore leave the boy with me, and go back

[024-083]at once to his mother. Tell her how you have met with me, and that the

[024-084]child is quite safe under my protection; but give out in public that

[024-085]he has been carried off and devoured by a tiger. I shall come to the

[024-086]city disguised as a beggar; do you wait for me near the cemetery."

[024-087]All this he promised to do, and set off immediately, having first

[024-088]received further directions for the guidance of the queen.

[024-089]After some days, it was generally understood at Mahishmati that the

[024-090]boy who had escaped into the forest had been killed by a tiger; and

[024-091]the king, secretly rejoicing, went to condole with the mother. She

[024-092]appeared as if greatly distressed by the news, and said to him: "I

[024-093]look upon the death of my son as a judgment upon me for not complying

[024-094]with your wishes, and am therefore now ready to become your wife."

[024-095]The old wretch was delighted at her compliance, and preparations were

[024-096]made for the marriage.

[024-097]On the appointed day, in the presence of a numerous assembly, she took

[024-098]a small leafy branch, and dipping it in what appeared to be water, but

[024-099]which really contained a deadly poison, struck him gently with it on

[024-100]the face, saying: "If you are acting right, this will not injure you;

[024-101]if you are sinning in taking me, your brother's wife, and I am

[024-102]faithful to my husband, may this be like the blow of a sword to you."

[024-103]Such was the strength of the poison that he fell dead almost

[024-104]instantaneously. Then dipping the same branch into other water

[024-105]containing an antidote, she struck her daughter in a similar manner;

[024-106]and, as no injury followed, the spectators were fully convinced that

[024-107]the death of Amittravarma was a punishment from heaven.

[024-108]Soon after this (by my directions, and in order to throw him off his

[024-109]guard), she said to Prachandavarma: "The throne is now vacant; you

[024-110]should occupy it at once, and make my daughter your queen."

[024-111]He listened to the suggestion; and, as the young boy, the nephew of

[024-112]the late king, was supposed to be dead, no opposition was made by the

[024-113]people.

[024-114]Then the Queen Vasundhara (also by my directions) sent for some of the

[024-115]late king's ministers, and of the elders of the city, whom she knew to

[024-116]be ill-affected towards Prachandavarma, and said to them: "Last night

[024-117]the goddess Durgâ appeared to me in a vision, and said: 'Your child is

[024-118]safe; I myself, in the form of a tigress, carried him away, to save

[024-119]him from his enemies. In four days from this time Prachandavarma will

[024-120]suddenly die; on the fifth day let all the authorities assemble round

[024-121]my temple on the bank of the river, and close the doors, after having

[024-122]ascertained that no one is concealed inside. After waiting one hour,

[024-123]the door will open and a young brahman will come forth, holding your

[024-124]son by the hand. That boy will become King of Vidarba, and that

[024-125]brahman is to marry your daughter.'"

[024-126]After the divine manifestation in favour of the queen when

[024-127]Amittravarma was struck dead, this account of the vision was readily

[024-128]believed by her hearers, who promised to keep the secret and to be

[024-129]guided by her directions.

[024-130]When the fourth day arrived I entered the city, disguised as a beggar,

[024-131]and brought the boy to his delighted mother, who introduced me to her

[024-132]daughter, whom I greatly admired, and she, though agitated, was

[024-133]evidently pleased with me, even under such a disguise.

[024-134]I did not venture to stay long, and after receiving an alms and

[024-135]assuring the queen that the imagined dream would prove true, I went

[024-136]away, taking the boy with me, and at parting, in order to deceive her

[024-137]attendants, she said aloud: "Your application shall not have been in

[024-138]vain; I will take care to protect your boy."

[024-139]Nâlijangha, the old servant whom I had rescued in the forest, met me

[024-140]on my arrival, and was waiting at the place which I had appointed. I

[024-141]went to him there and asked him for information as to the movements

[024-142]and occupations of the new king. "That doomed man," he answered,

[024-143]"thinking all obstacles removed, and rejoicing at his accession to

[024-144]power, is now amusing himself in the palace gardens, with a number of

[024-145]actors, tumblers, and dancing girls."

[024-146]"I could not have a better opportunity," I replied; "do you therefore

[024-147]stay here with the boy, and wait for me in this old ruin. I shall not

[024-148]be long gone."

[024-149]I then dressed myself in the clothes of a tumbler, which I had brought

[024-150]with me for the purpose, went boldly into the garden, presented myself

[024-151]to the king, and asked for permission to exhibit my skill before him.

[024-152]This was readily granted; an opportunity was soon given me of showing

[024-153]what I could do, and I obtained much applause from the spectators.

[024-154]After a time I begged some of those present to lend me their knives,

[024-155]and I caused much astonishment by the way in which I appeared to

[024-156]balance myself on the points. Then, still, holding one of the knives,

[024-157]I imitated the pouncing of a hawk and an eagle, and having by degrees

[024-158]got near the king, I threw the knife with such good aim, that it

[024-159]pierced him to the heart, and I shouted out at the same time, "Long

[024-160]live Vasantabhânu!" that it might be supposed I had been sent by him.

[024-161]After this, dashing by the guards, who tried to stop me, I suddenly

[024-162]leaped over the wall, and before any of my pursuers could cross it, I

[024-163]had run a long way on the other side. Doubling back, I got behind a

[024-164]great heap of bricks, and from thence, concealed by the trees,

[024-165]succeeded in reaching the ruins unobserved. Here I changed my clothes

[024-166]and went back to the city, as if nothing had happened.

[024-167]In order to have everything ready for my intended concealment, I had

[024-168]gone secretly the day before to the Temple of Durgâ, and had there

[024-169]made an underground chamber, communicating with the interior through

[024-170]an opening in the wall, which was carefully closed with a large stone,

[024-171]and now, taking the boy with me, I entered the hiding place, having

[024-172]been furnished with suitable dresses and ornaments, sent by the queen,

[024-173]through Nâlijangha.

[024-174]The assassination of Prachandavarma was universally attributed to his

[024-175]enemy, the King of Asmaka, and the first part of the prophecy of

[024-176]Durgâ, as told by the queen, being thus accomplished, there was no

[024-177]doubt, on the part of those who were in the secret, as to the

[024-178]fulfilment of the remainder.
