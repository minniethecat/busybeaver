[006-001]Meanwhile Bâlachandrika, quick in discrimination, perceived the

[006-002]impression they had made on each other; and not thinking it desirable

[006-003]to declare his name and rank before the other attendants, or in such a

[006-004]public place, introduced him to the princess, saying, "This is a very

[006-005]learned and clever young brahman, a friend of my husband, worthy of

[006-006]your notice. Allow me to recommend him to your favourable

[006-007]consideration."

[006-008]The princess, delighted at heart, but concealing her feelings,

[006-009]motioned to the prince to sit down near her, and gave him betel,

[006-010]flowers, perfumes, &c., through one of her attendants.

[006-011]Then Râjavâhana, more deeply in love even than the princess, thought

[006-012]to himself, "There surely must be some reason for this very sudden

[006-013]attraction which I feel towards her. She must have been my beloved

[006-014]wife in a former existence. Perhaps a curse was laid upon us; and now

[006-015]that is removed. If so, the recognition ought to be mutual; at all

[006-016]events I will try what I can do to produce the same feeling in her

[006-017]which exists in my mind."

[006-018]While he was considering how this might be accomplished, a swan

[006-019]approached the princess, as if expecting to be fed or caressed; and in

[006-020]sport, she desired Bâlachandrika to catch it.

[006-021]Inspired by this circumstance with a happy thought, Râjavâhana said to

[006-022]the princess, "Will you allow me to tell you a short story? There was

[006-023]formerly a king called Samba. When walking one day together with his

[006-024]beloved wife at the side of a small lake in the pleasure-grounds, he

[006-025]saw a swan asleep, just under the bank. Having caught it, he tied its

[006-026]legs together, put it down again on the ground, and saying to his

[006-027]wife, 'This bird sits as quiet as a muni; let him go where he likes,'

[006-028]amused himself with laughing at its awkward attempts to walk. Then the

[006-029]swan suddenly spoke: 'O king, though in the form of a swan, I am a

[006-030]devout brahman; and since you have thus, without cause, ill-treated me

[006-031]while sitting quiet here, engaged in meditation, I lay my curse upon

[006-032]you, and you shall endure the pain of separation from your beloved

[006-033]wife.'

[006-034]"Hearing this, the king, alarmed and distressed, bowed respectfully to

[006-035]the ground, and said, 'O mighty sage, forgive an act done through

[006-036]ignorance.'

[006-037]"Then that holy person, having his anger appeased, answered, 'My words

[006-038]cannot be made of no effect. I will, however, so far modify the curse

[006-039]that it will not take place during your present existence; but in a

[006-040]future birth, when you are united to the same lady in another body,

[006-041]you must endure the misery of separation from her for two months,

[006-042]though you will afterwards enjoy very great happiness with her; and I

[006-043]will also confer on you both the power of recognising each other in

[006-044]your next existence,'--I beg of you therefore not to tie this bird

[006-045]which you were wishing to catch."

[006-046]The princess, hearing this story, was quite ready to believe it; and

[006-047]from her own feelings was convinced that it really referred to a

[006-048]previous existence of herself, now brought to her recollection; and

[006-049]that the love which she felt springing up in her heart was directed

[006-050]towards one who had formerly been her husband. With a sweet smile, she

[006-051]answered: "Doubtless Samba tied the bird in that way on purpose to

[006-052]obtain the power of recognition in another birth; and it was very

[006-053]cleverly managed by him."

[006-054]From that moment they seemed perfectly to understand each other, and

[006-055]sat without speaking, their hearts full of happiness.

[006-056]Presently the mother of the princess--the queen of the ex-king

[006-057]Mânasâra, who had also come with her attendants into the park, joined

[006-058]her daughter; and Bâlachandrika having seen her approaching, made a

[006-059]sign to the prince, upon which he and his friend slipped on one side,

[006-060]and hid themselves behind some leafy bushes.

[006-061]After the queen had stayed a short time talking to her daughter and

[006-062]looking at the games, she set out to return, and the princess

[006-063]accompanied her.

[006-064]Before going, she turned round, as if addressing the swan, but

[006-065]intending the speech for the prince, who was anxiously watching her

[006-066]from his hiding-place, "Though you came near me so lovingly just now,

[006-067]I may not stay longer with you: I must leave you and follow my mother:

[006-068]do not forget me or imagine that I neglect you, for I am still fond of

[006-069]you."

[006-070]With these words she walked slowly away, looking with longing eyes in

[006-071]the direction of her lover.

[006-072]On their return to the palace, the princess heard from Bâlachandrika a

[006-073]full account of Râjavâhana and his adventures, through which she was

[006-074]even more in love than before; and having no opportunity of seeing him

[006-075]again, became listless and indifferent to her usual occupations, lost

[006-076]her appetite, wasted away, and at last lay on her bed, burning with

[006-077]fever.

[006-078]In vain did her devoted attendants use all their efforts to diminish

[006-079]the heat by means of cold water, fanning, and other remedies; and she,

[006-080]seeing their distress, said to her faithful Bâlachandrika: "Ah, dear

[006-081]friend, all you can do is to no purpose; they call Kâma the god with

[006-082]five arrows; but surely this is a wrong name, for I feel as if pierced

[006-083]by him with hundreds of arrows. They call the wind from Malaya

[006-084]cooling; but to me it only increases the fever, as if blowing up the

[006-085]fire which consumes me: my own necklace, the contact of which was

[006-086]formerly agreeable, now feels as if smeared with the poison of

[006-087]serpents. Give up your exertions; the prince is the only physician who

[006-088]can cure me; and how can he come to me here?"

[006-089]Then Bâlachandrika thought to herself: "Something must be done, and

[006-090]that without delay, or this violent passion of love will surely cause

[006-091]her death. I will at least see the prince, and try if it is possible

[006-092]to bring about a meeting."

[006-093]Having thus resolved, she begged the princess to write a few lines to

[006-094]her lover; and committing her to the care of the other attendants, she

[006-095]went to the house of her husband. There she found Râjavâhana almost in

[006-096]the same state as the princess, burning with fever, throwing himself

[006-097]about restlessly on his couch, and bemoaning his hard fate to his

[006-098]friend.

[006-099]On seeing Bâlachandrika, he started up, saying, "Oh, how welcome is

[006-100]the sight of you! I am sure you must be the bearer of good news. Sit

[006-101]down here and tell me about my darling."

[006-102]She answered: "The princess is suffering like yourself, longing to see

[006-103]you; and has now sent me with this letter."

[006-104]Eagerly opening it, he read--

[006-105]"Beloved--Having seen your beauty, delicate as a flower, faultless,

[006-106]unrivalled in the world, my heart is full of longing. Do you likewise

[006-107]make your heart soft."

[006-108]Having read this, he said: "Your coming here is refreshing to me as

[006-109]water to a withered plant; you are the wife of my very dear friend,

[006-110]Pushpodbhava, and I know how attached you are to my darling, therefore

[006-111]I can speak freely to you. Tell her that when she left the grove that

[006-112]day she carried off my heart with her, and that I long to see her even

[006-113]more than she longs for me; tell her only not to despond; the entrance

[006-114]to her apartments is indeed difficult, but I will contrive to see her

[006-115]by some means or other. Come back soon, and, having thought over the

[006-116]matter, I will tell you what is to be done." With this message,

[006-117]Bâlachandrika went to rejoice her friend; and the prince, though much

[006-118]comforted, could not remain quiet, but walked to the park, to have the

[006-119]pleasure of seeing at least the place where he had first met his

[006-120]charmer. There he stayed a long time together with his friend, looking

[006-121]at her footsteps in the sand, the withered flowers which she had

[006-122]gathered and thrown down, the place where she had sat, and the shrubs

[006-123]from which he had watched her, and listening to the murmur of the wind

[006-124]among the leaves, the hum of the bees and the song of the birds.

[006-125]Presently, they saw approaching them a brahman, splendidly dressed,

[006-126]followed by a servant. He, coming up to the prince, saluted him; and

[006-127]the prince, returning the salute, asked who he was. He answered "My

[006-128]name is Vidyeswara. I am a famous conjurer, and travel about

[006-129]exhibiting my skill for the amusement of kings and nobles. I have now

[006-130]come to Oujein, to show off my skill before the king." Then, with a

[006-131]knowing smile, he added, "But what makes you look so pale?"

[006-132]Pushpodbhava, thinking to himself this is just the man to help us,

[006-133]answered, "There is something in your appearance which induces me to

[006-134]look on you as a friend, and you know how sometimes intimate

[006-135]friendship arises from a very short acquaintance; I will therefore

[006-136]tell you why my friend is thus sad. Not long ago, he, the son of a

[006-137]king, met the Princess Avantisundari on this very spot, and they fell

[006-138]in love with each other. From the impossibility of meeting, both are

[006-139]suffering, and the prince is brought into this condition which you

[006-140]see."

[006-141]Vidyeswara, in reply, looking at the prince, said, with a smile, "To

[006-142]such as you, with me for an ally, nothing is impossible. I will,

[006-143]through my skill, contrive that you shall marry the princess in the

[006-144]presence of her father and his court; but you must follow my

[006-145]directions exactly, and she must be informed of her part in the affair

[006-146]through some trusty female friend."

[006-147]Then, having given the necessary directions, the conjurer went his

[006-148]way. Râjavâhana also returned to the house, and when he had given

[006-149]Bâlachandrika, who came again in the evening, the directions received

[006-150]from the conjurer, and a loving message of encouragement for the

[006-151]princess, he anxiously awaited the morrow, unable to sleep from the

[006-152]thought of the expected happiness, and fluctuating between alternate

[006-153]hopes and fears. In the morning, Vidyeswara, having collected a large

[006-154]troop of followers, went to the palace and announced himself to the

[006-155]doorkeeper, saying, "Tell the king the great conjurer is arrived."

[006-156]Mânasâra, who had heard of his great skill, and was desirous of seeing

[006-157]it, ordered him to be immediately admitted, and, after the usual

[006-158]salutations, the performance began.

[006-159]First, while the band was playing, peacocks' tails were waving, and

[006-160]singers imitating the plaintive notes of birds, to excite the feelings

[006-161]and distract the attention of the hearers, the conjurer turned round

[006-162]violently several times, with his eyes half-closed, and caused great

[006-163]hooded serpents to appear and vultures to come down from the sky to

[006-164]seize them.

[006-165]After this, he represented the scene of Vishnu killing Hiranyakasipu,

[006-166]chief of the Asuras, to the great astonishment of the spectators;

[006-167]then, turning to the king, he said, "It is desirable that the

[006-168]performance should end with something auspicious; I propose,

[006-169]therefore, to represent a royal marriage, and one of my people will

[006-170]act as your daughter, another as a prince, endowed with all good

[006-171]qualities. But first I must apply to your eyes this ointment, which

[006-172]will give you preternatural clearness of vision." To all this the king

[006-173]consented.

[006-174]Meanwhile, the princess had contrived to slip out unobserved, and

[006-175]stood among the conjurer's people. Râjavâhana also stood ready, and

[006-176]the performance began. Thus, under the disguise of a piece of acting,

[006-177]the conjurer, being a brahman, was able to complete the marriage with

[006-178]all proper rites and ceremonies without any suspicion on the part of

[006-179]the king that it was his own daughter whom he saw before him; and the

[006-180]others, also unsuspecting, only admired the skill of the conjurer in

[006-181]making the actress so like the lady whom she represented. When the

[006-182]performance was ended, the conjurer, having been liberally rewarded by

[006-183]the king, dismissed his hired attendants and departed.
