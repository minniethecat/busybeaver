[004-001]Exhausted by fatigue and the scorching heat of the sun, I went into a

[004-002]small temple to rest, and saw there a brahman with a number of

[004-003]children, all looking wretched and half-starved. He seemed to regard

[004-004]me as a possible benefactor, and when questioned, readily told me his

[004-005]story; how his wife had died, leaving him with the care of all these

[004-006]children, and how, having no means of subsistence, he had wandered

[004-007]about in the hope of obtaining some employment; but had got nothing

[004-008]better than the charge of this small temple, where the offerings were

[004-009]not sufficient to support him and his family.

[004-010]I asked him--"What is that camp which I see at some distance?"

[004-011]He answered--"The Lord of Lâta, Mattakâla by name, hearing again and

[004-012]again of the great beauty of Vâmalochana, daughter of Víraketu,

[004-013]sovereign of this country, asked her in marriage, and was refused.

[004-014]Being determined to obtain her, he raised an army and besieged Pâtali,

[004-015]the capital city. Víraketu finding himself unable to resist the enemy,

[004-016]purchased peace by giving up his daughter, and Mattakâla, thinking

[004-017]that the marriage can be celebrated with greater magnificence in his

[004-018]own country, has deferred it till his return. He is now on his way

[004-019]home with a small part of his army, the rest having been dismissed;

[004-020]and he is staying at present near this forest to enjoy the pleasures

[004-021]of the chase. The princess is not with her intended husband, but under

[004-022]the care of Mânapâla, one of her father's officers, who is said to be

[004-023]very indignant at the surrender of the lady; you may see his camp at

[004-024]no great distance from the other."

[004-025]While thanking the poor man for his information, a thought came into

[004-026]my mind--here is a very poor and deserving man, I will give him the

[004-027]jewel which I have found; and I did so.

[004-028]He received the gift with profuse thanks, and set out immediately to

[004-029]try to dispose of it; while I lay down there to sleep.

[004-030]After a time I was awakened by a great clamour, and saw the brahman

[004-031]coming towards me with his hands tied behind him, driven along, with

[004-032]blows of a whip and much abuse, by a party of soldiers.

[004-033]On seeing me, he called out, "There is the thief; that is the man who

[004-034]gave me the jewel."

[004-035]Upon this the soldiers let him go, and, seizing me, refused to listen

[004-036]to my remonstrances, or to my account of the manner in which I had

[004-037]found the ruby. They dragged me along with them, and having put

[004-038]fetters on my feet, thrust me into a dungeon, saying, "There are your

[004-039]companions," pointing at the same time to some other prisoners

[004-040]confined in that place.

[004-041]When I recovered my senses--for I was half stunned by the violence

[004-042]with which I had been pushed in--I said to my fellow-prisoners, "Who

[004-043]are you, and what did the soldiers mean by calling you my companions?

[004-044]for you are quite strangers to me."

[004-045]Those prisoners then told me the story of the King of Lâta, which I

[004-046]had already heard from the brahman, and further said, "We were sent by

[004-047]Mânapâla to assassinate that king, and broke into the place where we

[004-048]supposed him to be. Not finding him, we were unwilling to come away

[004-049]empty-handed; we therefore carried off everything of value within our

[004-050]reach and made our escape to the forest. The next morning there was an

[004-051]active pursuit, our hiding-place was discovered, we were all captured,

[004-052]and the stolen property taken from us, with the exception of one ruby

[004-053]of great value, which had disappeared. The king is exceedingly angry

[004-054]that this cannot be found; our assertion that we have lost it is

[004-055]disbelieved, and we are threatened with torture to-morrow, unless we

[004-056]say where it is hidden."

[004-057]Having heard the robbers' story, I was convinced that the ruby in

[004-058]question was the one which I had found and given to the brahman, and I

[004-059]now understood why these men were supposed to be my accomplices.

[004-060]I told them who I was, how I had found the jewel, and had been

[004-061]unjustly arrested on account of it, and exhorted them to take courage

[004-062]and join me in an attempt to escape that night. To this they agreed,

[004-063]and at midnight we managed to overpower the jailors and knock off our

[004-064]fetters; and having armed ourselves with weapons which we found in the

[004-065]prison, we cut our way through the guards, and reached Mânapâla's camp

[004-066]in safety. The next day, men sent by the King of Lâta came to

[004-067]Mânapâla, and said--"Some robbers, who were caught after breaking into

[004-068]the king's dwelling, have made their escape, and are known to have

[004-069]come here; give them up immediately, or it will be the worse for you."

[004-070]Mânapâla, who only wanted an excuse for a quarrel, having heard this

[004-071]insulting message, his eyes red with anger, answered,--"Who is the

[004-072]King of Lâta, that I should bow down to him? What have I to do with

[004-073]that low fellow? Begone!"

[004-074]When the men returned to their master and told him the reception they

[004-075]had met with, he was in a furious rage, and, disregarding the

[004-076]smallness of the force which was with him, marched out at once to

[004-077]attack Mânapâla, who was quite prepared to meet him.

[004-078]When I entered the camp, after my escape, Mânapâla, who received from

[004-079]his servants an exaggerated account of my coolness, dexterity, and

[004-080]courage, had treated me with great honour, and now I offered my

[004-081]services in the approaching fight. They were gladly accepted, and I

[004-082]was furnished with an excellent chariot and horses guided by a skilful

[004-083]charioteer, a strong coat of mail, a bow and two quivers full of

[004-084]arrows, as well as with other weapons.

[004-085]Thus equipped, I went forth to meet the enemy, and seeking out the

[004-086]leader, soon found myself near him. First confusing him with arrows

[004-087]poured upon him in rapid succession, I brought my chariot close to

[004-088]his, and suddenly springing into it, cut off his head at a blow.

[004-089]Seeing the king fall, his soldiers were discouraged, and fled; the

[004-090]camp was taken, much booty gained, and the princess led back, to her

[004-091]father. He having received an account of the victory, and of my share

[004-092]in it, through a messenger sent from Mânapâla, came forth to meet us

[004-093]when we entered the city, and received me with great honour. After a

[004-094]time, as I continued daily to increase in favour with him, he bestowed

[004-095]on me the hand of his daughter, and declared me his successor.

[004-096]Being thus arrived at the height of prosperity and happiness, I had

[004-097]but one cause of sorrow--my absence from you. I am on my way to

[004-098]Mahâkâla, to worship Siva there. I have stopped at this place, hoping,

[004-099]at a festival so much frequented, I might at least hear some tidings

[004-100]of you, and now the god has favoured his worshipper, and through this

[004-101]happy meeting all my wishes are fulfilled.

[004-102]Râjavâhana, who delighted in valour, having heard Somadatta's story,

[004-103]while expressing his sorrow for his undeserved imprisonment,

[004-104]congratulated him on the happy result of it, and told him his own

[004-105]adventures.

[004-106]He had scarcely finished the relation of them when a third person came

[004-107]up, and the prince, warmly greeting him, exclaimed, "O, Somadatta,

[004-108]here is Pushpodbhava." Then there were mutual embracings and

[004-109]rejoicings, after which they all three sat down again, and Râjavâhana

[004-110]said: "Somadatta has told me his adventures, but I know nothing of the

[004-111]rest of my friends. What did you do when you missed me that morning in

[004-112]the forest?" Then Pushpodbhava respectfully spoke as follows:--

[004-113]* * * * *

[004-114]ADVENTURES OF PUSHPODBHAVA.

[004-115]My lord, your friends being convinced that you had gone on some

[004-116]expedition with the brahman, and knowing nothing of the direction

[004-117]which you had taken, were greatly perplexed. At last we agreed to

[004-118]separate, each going a different way, and I, like the rest, set out by

[004-119]myself. One day, being unable to bear the heat of the noonday sun, I

[004-120]sat down in the shade of a tree at the bottom of a mountain. Happening

[004-121]to look up, I saw a man falling from the rock above, and he came to

[004-122]the ground very near me.

[004-123]On going up to him, I found that he was still alive, and having

[004-124]revived him by throwing cold water over him, and by other means, I

[004-125]found that he had no bone broken, and did not appear to have received

[004-126]any serious injury.

[004-127]When he was sufficiently recovered, I asked him who he was and how he

[004-128]came to fall from the precipice. With tears in his eyes, and a feeble

[004-129]voice, he said: "My name is Ratnodbhava; I am the son of a minister of

[004-130]the King of Magadha; travelling about as a merchant, I came, many

[004-131]years ago, to the island of Kâlayavana. There I married a merchant's

[004-132]daughter, and going with her by sea to visit my relations, was

[004-133]overtaken by a violent storm, during which the ship sank, and I was

[004-134]the only person saved.

[004-135]"After reaching the shore, I wandered about for some time in a strange

[004-136]country, and, unable to bear my misery, was about to put an end to my

[004-137]life, when I was stopped by a Siddha, who assured me that after

[004-138]sixteen years I should find my wife. Trusting to this promise, I have

[004-139]endured life through all these years; but the appointed time having

[004-140]passed without any sign of the fulfilment of the prophecy, I could

[004-141]hold out no longer, and threw myself from the top of this precipice."

[004-142]At that moment the voice of a woman in distress was heard not far off,

[004-143]and saying to him whom I recognised as my father, "Take courage, I

[004-144]have good news for you; only wait a moment," I ran off in the

[004-145]direction of the place whence the voice had proceeded, and soon came

[004-146]in sight of a large fire and two women near it, the one trying to

[004-147]throw herself into the flames, the other struggling to prevent her.

[004-148]Going to the help of the latter, I soon got the lady away, and

[004-149]brought her and her companion to the place where my father was lying.

[004-150]I then said to the old woman, "Pray tell me what all this means? How

[004-151]came you to be in such a place, and why did the lady wish to destroy

[004-152]herself?"

[004-153]With a voice broken by sobs, she answered me: "This lady, whose name

[004-154]is Suvritta, is the daughter of a merchant in the island of

[004-155]Kâlayavana, and the wife of Ratnodbhava. While crossing the sea with

[004-156]her husband, there was a great storm, the ship sank, and this lady and

[004-157]I, her nurse, were the only persons saved. A few days afterwards she

[004-158]gave birth to a son in the forest; but through my ill-fortune the

[004-159]child was lost, having been seized by a wild elephant. Afterwards we

[004-160]two wandered about in great misery, and she would have put an end to

[004-161]her life had we not met with a holy man, who comforted her with the

[004-162]assurance that after sixteen years she would be reunited with her

[004-163]husband and son. Relying on this prophecy, she consented to wait, and

[004-164]we have spent all these years living near his hermitage; but the

[004-165]sixteen years were ended some time ago, and having lost all hope, she

[004-166]was about to end her wretched life by throwing herself into a fire

[004-167]which she had made, when you so opportunely came to my assistance."

[004-168]Hearing this story, my father was unable to speak from astonishment. I

[004-169]made him known to my mother, and myself to both of them, to their very

[004-170]great joy; and my mother seemed as if she would never weary of kissing

[004-171]and embracing me.
