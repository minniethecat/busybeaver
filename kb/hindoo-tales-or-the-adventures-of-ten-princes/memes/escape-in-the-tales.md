# Escape in the Tales

## Summary

Escape—whether from captivity, danger, or unwanted obligation—is one of the most recurrent narrative events in the *Adventures of the Ten Princes*. Characters repeatedly flee imprisonment, pursuit, forced marriages, and deadly peril.

Over twenty distinct escape episodes reflect the adventurous structure of the frame narrative.

> "After a short time, more of those who had escaped joined the king; and..."
> (002-001)

## Context

The ten princes, separated from their king and from one another, each wander through a world of danger. Their narratives follow a pattern of misfortunes followed by narrow escapes.

Escapes take several forms. Some are tactical withdrawals from military defeat. Others are flights from captivity under cover of darkness.

> "...the king's dwelling, have made their escape, and are known to have..."
> (004-068)

Still others are escapes from social obligation, as when a character flees to avoid an arranged marriage: "To escape this marriage, I..." (009-050). The forest serves as a recurring refuge beyond the city walls.

The moral dimension of escape is also explored.

> "A man of sense will endure adversity rather than escape from it in..."
> (009-140)

## Key Participants

- [[rajavahana]] — narrator-prince who recounts many escape episodes

- [[apahâravarma]] — escapes captivity and rejoins the king

- [[upahâravarma]] — escapes and informs [[sinhaghosha|Sinhaghosha]] of his situation (011-154)

- [[somadatta]] — escapes to the forest after various adventures

- [[arthapâla]] — a rich man from whom a character escapes an arranged marriage

- [[mânapâla]] — receives a companion after his escape from the enemy camp

## Outcome

The escape motif establishes the princes as resourceful survivors. Each escape reinforces their ability to navigate a hostile world through wit, timing, and allies.

After escaping to the forest, characters encounter brahmans, merchants, and mysterious women who set them on new quests.

> "There I wandered about for..."
> (014-091)

The pattern of peril, escape, encounter, and new adventure drives the narrative engine of the entire work.
