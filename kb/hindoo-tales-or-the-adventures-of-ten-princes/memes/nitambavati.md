# Nitambavati

Nitambavati was the wife of a merchant living at [[avanti|Avanti]] ([[avanti|Oujein]]). Her beauty captivated [[kalahakantaka|Kalahakantaka]], and her subsequent actions became one of the illustrative examples of feminine cunning recounted in the tales.

## Biography

Nitambavati lived in [[avanti|Avanti]] as the wife of a wealthy merchant. [[kalahakantaka|Kalahakantaka]], who had fallen in love with her portrait, sought her out and was struck by her beauty in person.

> "This," said he, "is Nitambavati; she is the wife of a merchant living at Avanti or Oujein, and I was so struck by her beauty that I sought and obtained an introduction to her." (020-077–078)

An old woman acted as intermediary, telling Nitambavati that a very handsome young man was deeply in love with her and pleading with her to grant him an audience:

> The old woman went to Nitambavati, and said: "A very handsome young man is much in love with you — pray let him see you if only for once." (020-088–089)

Nitambavati initially resisted, but after a spell was removed from her husband — presumably an enchantment that kept him possessive or suspicious — she consented and went at night to the appointed place, where she met [[kalahakantaka|Kalahakantaka]] (020-111–112).

## Role in the Tales

Nitambavati is explicitly listed among the examples of "cunning" women, alongside [[dhumini|Dhumini]], [[gomini|Gomini]], and [[ratnavati|Ratnavati]]. She represents the theme of feminine cleverness and resourcefulness that runs through several of the tales:

> "Cunning. Dhumini, Gomini, Ratnavati, and Nitambavati," I added, "are examples of what I have said." (018-161)
