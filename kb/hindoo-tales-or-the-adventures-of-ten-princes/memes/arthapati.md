# Arthapati

Arthapati was a wealthy merchant of Champâ who appears in the ninth chapter
of the narrative. He is described as a niggardly man who sought to marry
Kulapâlikâ against her will, and was later implicated in the affair of the
stolen magic purse.
(009-050) (010-091) (026-048)

## Biography

Arthapati was a rich merchant of Champâ, known for his wealth and his
miserly character. He became involved in the affairs of Kulapâlikâ when
her father determined to give her to him in marriage, despite her own
reluctance.
(009-050) (026-048)

> "I am determined to give me to a rich man, called Arthapati. To escape
> this marriage, I have slipped out from home by a secret passage."
> (009-050)

Kulapâlikâ's father had hoped to marry his daughter to Arthapati, but this
hope was frustrated when the young woman renewed a former engagement with
another suitor and rejected Arthapati entirely.
(009-119) (009-175)

[[vimardaka|Vimardaka]] was directed to enter the service of Arthapati and use every
effort to render himself necessary to him, as part of a larger scheme
involving the merchant.
(009-171)

> "That niggardly wretch, Arthapati, is known to be possessed of great
> wealth."
> (010-091)

When the magic purse went missing, suspicion fell upon Arthapati. Under
torture, he confessed to having taken the purse, and his property was
confiscated as a consequence.
(010-059) (010-097)

A portion of the confiscated goods was given as reward to others involved
in the recovery of the purse, while Arthapati suffered the loss of his
fortune.
(010-105)

## Role in the Narrative

Arthapati serves as an antagonist and foil in the ninth and tenth chapters.
His attempted marriage to Kulapâlikâ sets in motion her flight and the
subsequent chain of events. His miserly reputation and ultimate downfall
illustrate the narrative's treatment of avarice as a vice that leads to ruin.
(009-050) (010-097)

## Relationships

Arthapati sought to marry Kulapâlikâ, the daughter of a man who favoured
the match, though she did not return his interest. He employed [[vimardaka|Vimardaka]]
in his service, who was in fact an agent planted to deceive him. The
prince who lost the magic purse also pursued Arthapati as the prime suspect
in its theft.
(026-048) (010-091)
