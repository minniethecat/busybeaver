# Pushpapuri

Pushpapuri (also known as Pâtaliputra) is the capital city of [[magadha|Magadha]]
and the seat of King [[râjahansa]] in the *Daśakumāracarita*. It is the birthplace
of the hero [[râjavâhana]] and the centre of the kingdom whose conquest by
[[mânasâra]] of [[malwa|Mâlwa]] sets the entire narrative in motion.
(001-086) (001-132)

## Description

Pushpapuri was the magnificent capital of [[magadha|Magadha]], described as a city of great
splendour and learning. It was the residence of [[rajahansa|King Râjahansa]] and the site of
important royal ceremonies, including the [[simanta|Simanta]] ritual performed for the
queen during her pregnancy — an event that drew nobles and dignitaries from
across the kingdom.
(001-086) (002-040)

> "Pushpapuri, the capital of Magadha, magnified"
> (001-086)

The city was renowned as a seat of scholarship. A very learned and
accomplished man was said to have formerly resided at Pushpapuri, indicating
its status as a centre of intellectual and cultural life in addition to its
political importance.
(014-066)

> "there was,' said he, 'formerly at Pushpapuri a very learned and"
> (014-066)

After [[manasara|Mânasâra]]'s conquest of [[magadha|Magadha]], [[rajahansa|Râjahansa]] was driven from Pushpapuri.
The city fell under the dominion of the conqueror, and the infant princes were
scattered — an event that the narrative returns to repeatedly as the origin of
all their subsequent adventures.
(001-132)

> "easy terms, and returning to Pushpapuri, thought only"
> (001-132)

## Significance in the Narrative

Pushpapuri is the geographical and symbolic heart of the *Daśakumāracarita*.
It is the seat of legitimate rule — the throne that [[rajahansa|Râjahansa]] lost and that the
ten princes ultimately seek to restore. Every prince's story begins with the
fall of Pushpapuri, and the city's fate is intertwined with the destiny of
the entire royal house.
(001-086) (001-132)

The [[simanta|Simanta]] ceremony held at Pushpapuri marks one of the earliest events in
the timeline, bringing together the key figures of the kingdom before the
catastrophe of [[manasara|Mânasâra]]'s invasion. This gathering at the capital foreshadows
the unity that the princes will later restore.
(002-040)

> "Pushpapuri, to be present at the Simanta ceremony."
> (002-040)

The city is also known by its historical name Pâtaliputra, one of the great
cities of ancient India. This dual identity — Pushpapuri in the Sanskrit
narrative and Pâtaliputra in historical geography — anchors the tale in the
real landscape of classical Indian civilization.
(026-129)

> "Pushpapuri, the capital of Magadha."
> (026-129)

before the conquest, in Chapter 14 as a former centre of learning, and in the
proper names index of Chapter 26.
