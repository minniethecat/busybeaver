# Champa

Champa is a fortified capital city situated on the banks of the [[ganges|Ganges]] in the
*Daśakumāracarita*. It appears in the narratives of several princes, most
notably as the target of a siege by [[chandavarma]] and as the home of the
merchant [[arthapati|Arthapati]], who wished to marry Kulapâlikâ.
(007-067) (011-165)

## Description

Champa was a walled city, strategically located near the [[ganges|Ganges]], making it both
a commercial hub and a military objective. Its fortifications were significant
enough that [[chandavarma|Chandavarma]] undertook a formal siege to capture it, an event
remembered in the narrative as a notable feat of arms.
(007-067)

> "prepared to besiege Champa, the capital city."
> (007-067)

The city lay not far from a hermitage on the banks of the [[ganges|Ganges]], where a sage
dwelt and where travellers would stop on their journeys. This proximity to both
the holy river and the wilderness gave Champa a distinctive character as a place
where urban and ascetic life intersected.
(008-008)

> "on the banks of the Ganges, not far from Champa, who was said to have"
> (008-008)

Within the city, the merchant [[arthapati|Arthapati]] made his home. He became entangled in
the affair of Kulapâlikâ, seeking her hand in marriage — a subplot that
connected Champa to the broader web of relationships in the tale.
(026-048)

> "Arthapati, a merchant at Champa, who wished to marry Kulapâlika."
> (026-048)

## Significance in the Narrative

Champa enters the story through two distinct threads. In [[apaharavarma|Apahâravarma]]'s
narrative, the siege of Champa by [[chandavarma|Chandavarma]] is recalled as part of the
political turmoil that followed the scattering of the princes. The conquest
of the city was one of the military actions that reshaped the regional
balance of power.
(007-067) (011-165)

> "know how Chandavarma besieged Champa, and how Sinhavarma was"
> (011-165)

The city also features as the residence of [[arthapati|Arthapati]] the merchant, whose
romantic ambition to marry Kulapâlikâ draws Champa into the intricate love
plots that run through the later chapters. After certain events, characters
were urged to remain in Champa, suggesting it served as a place of refuge and
resettlement.
(008-140)

its aftermath, and in the proper names index of Chapter 26 where [[arthapati|Arthapati]] is
listed as a merchant of the city.
