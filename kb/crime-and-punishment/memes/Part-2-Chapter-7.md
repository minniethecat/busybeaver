[014-001] An elegant carriage stood in the middle of the road with a pair of

[014-002] spirited grey horses; there was no one in it, and the coachman had got

[014-003] off his box and stood by; the horses were being held by the bridle....

[014-004] A mass of people had gathered round, the police standing in front. One

[014-005] of them held a lighted lantern which he was turning on something lying

[014-006] close to the wheels. Everyone was talking, shouting, exclaiming; the

[014-007] coachman seemed at a loss and kept repeating:

[014-008] “What a misfortune! Good Lord, what a misfortune!”

[014-009] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] pushed his way in as far as he could, and succeeded at last

[014-010] in seeing the object of the commotion and interest. On the ground a

[014-011] man who had been run over lay apparently unconscious, and covered with

[014-012] blood; he was very badly dressed, but not like a workman. Blood was

[014-013] flowing from his head and face; his face was crushed, mutilated and

[014-014] disfigured. He was evidently badly injured.

[014-015] “Merciful heaven!” wailed the coachman, “what more could I do? If I’d

[014-016] been driving fast or had not shouted to him, but I was going quietly,

[014-017] not in a hurry. Everyone could see I was going along just like everybody

[014-018] else. A drunken man can’t walk straight, we all know.... I saw him

[014-019] crossing the street, staggering and almost falling. I shouted again

[014-020] and a second and a third time, then I held the horses in, but he fell

[014-021] straight under their feet! Either he did it on purpose or he was very

[014-022] tipsy.... The horses are young and ready to take fright... they started,

[014-023] he screamed... that made them worse. That’s how it happened!”

[014-024] “That’s just how it was,” a voice in the crowd confirmed.

[014-025] “He shouted, that’s true, he shouted three times,” another voice

[014-026] declared.

[014-027] “Three times it was, we all heard it,” shouted a third.

[014-028] But the coachman was not very much distressed and frightened. It was

[014-029] evident that the carriage belonged to a rich and important person who

[014-030] was awaiting it somewhere; the police, of course, were in no little

[014-031] anxiety to avoid upsetting his arrangements. All they had to do was to

[014-032] take the injured man to the police station and the hospital. No one knew

[014-033] his name.

[014-034] Meanwhile Raskolnikov had squeezed in and stooped closer over him. The

[014-035] lantern suddenly lighted up the unfortunate man’s face. He recognised

[014-036] him.

[014-037] “I know him! I know him!” he shouted, pushing to the front. “It’s a

[014-038] government clerk retired from the service, [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]. He lives close

[014-039] by in Kozel’s house.... Make haste for a doctor! I will pay, see?” He

[014-040] pulled money out of his pocket and showed it to the policeman. He was in

[014-041] violent agitation.

[014-042] The police were glad that they had found out who the man was.

[014-043] Raskolnikov gave his own name and address, and, as earnestly as if it

[014-044] had been his father, he besought the police to carry the unconscious

[014-045] Marmeladov to his lodging at once.

[014-046] “Just here, three houses away,” he said eagerly, “the house belongs to

[014-047] Kozel, a rich German. He was going home, no doubt drunk. I know him,

[014-048] he is a drunkard. He has a family there, a wife, children, he has one

[014-049] daughter.... It will take time to take him to the hospital, and there is

[014-050] sure to be a doctor in the house. I’ll pay, I’ll pay! At least he will

[014-051] be looked after at home... they will help him at once. But he’ll die

[014-052] before you get him to the hospital.” He managed to slip something

[014-053] unseen into the policeman’s hand. But the thing was straightforward

[014-054] and legitimate, and in any case help was closer here. They raised the

[014-055] injured man; people volunteered to help.

[014-056] Kozel’s house was thirty yards away. Raskolnikov walked behind,

[014-057] carefully holding Marmeladov’s head and showing the way.

[014-058] “This way, this way! We must take him upstairs head foremost. Turn

[014-059] round! I’ll pay, I’ll make it worth your while,” he muttered.

[014-060] [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] had just begun, as she always did at every free

[014-061] moment, walking to and fro in her little room from window to stove and

[014-062] back again, with her arms folded across her chest, talking to herself

[014-063] and coughing. Of late she had begun to talk more than ever to her eldest

[014-064] girl, Polenka, a child of ten, who, though there was much she did not

[014-065] understand, understood very well that her mother needed her, and so

[014-066] always watched her with her big clever eyes and strove her utmost

[014-067] to appear to understand. This time Polenka was undressing her little

[014-068] brother, who had been unwell all day and was going to bed. The boy was

[014-069] waiting for her to take off his shirt, which had to be washed at night.

[014-070] He was sitting straight and motionless on a chair, with a silent,

[014-071] serious face, with his legs stretched out straight before him--heels

[014-072] together and toes turned out.

[014-073] He was listening to what his mother was saying to his sister, sitting

[014-074] perfectly still with pouting lips and wide-open eyes, just as all good

[014-075] little boys have to sit when they are undressed to go to bed. A little

[014-076] girl, still younger, dressed literally in rags, stood at the screen,

[014-077] waiting for her turn. The door on to the [[staircases|stairs]] was open to relieve

[014-078] them a little from the clouds of tobacco smoke which floated in from the

[014-079] other rooms and brought on long terrible fits of coughing in the poor,

[014-080] consumptive woman. Katerina Ivanovna seemed to have grown even thinner

[014-081] during that week and the hectic flush on her face was brighter than

[014-082] ever.

[014-083] “You wouldn’t believe, you can’t imagine, Polenka,” she said, walking

[014-084] about the room, “what a happy luxurious life we had in my papa’s house

[014-085] and how this drunkard has brought me, and will bring you all, to ruin!

[014-086] Papa was a civil colonel and only a step from being a governor; so that

[014-087] everyone who came to see him said, ‘We look upon you, Ivan Mihailovitch,

[014-088] as our governor!’ When I... when...” she coughed violently, “oh, cursed

[014-089] life,” she cried, clearing her throat and pressing her hands to her

[014-090] breast, “when I... when at the last ball... at the marshal’s...

[014-091] Princess Bezzemelny saw me--who gave me the blessing when your father

[014-093] girl who danced the shawl dance at the breaking-up?’ (You must mend

[014-094] that tear, you must take your needle and darn it as I showed you, or

[014-095] to-morrow--cough, cough, cough--he will make the hole bigger,” she

[014-096] articulated with effort.) “Prince Schegolskoy, a kammerjunker, had just

[014-097] come from [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] then... he danced the mazurka with me and wanted to

[014-098] make me an offer next day; but I thanked him in flattering expressions

[014-101] the shirt, and the stockings! Lida,” said she to the youngest one, “you

[014-104] doesn’t come in? He has worn his shirt till it looks like a dish-clout,

[014-105] he has torn it to rags! I’d do it all together, so as not to have to

[014-106] work two nights running! Oh, dear! (Cough, cough, cough, cough!) Again!

[014-107] What’s this?” she cried, noticing a crowd in the passage and the men,

[014-108] who were pushing into her room, carrying a burden. “What is it? What are

[014-109] they bringing? Mercy on us!”

[014-110] “Where are we to put him?” asked the policeman, looking round when

[014-111] Marmeladov, unconscious and covered with blood, had been carried in.

[014-112] “On the sofa! Put him straight on the sofa, with his head this way,”

[014-113] Raskolnikov showed him.

[014-114] “Run over in the road! Drunk!” someone shouted in the passage.

[014-115] Katerina Ivanovna stood, turning white and gasping for breath. The

[014-116] children were terrified. Little Lida screamed, rushed to Polenka and

[014-117] clutched at her, trembling all over.

[014-118] Having laid Marmeladov down, Raskolnikov flew to Katerina Ivanovna.

[014-119] “For God’s sake be calm, don’t be frightened!” he said, speaking

[014-120] quickly, “he was crossing the road and was run over by a carriage, don’t

[014-121] be frightened, he will come to, I told them bring him here... I’ve been

[014-122] here already, you remember? He will come to; I’ll pay!”

[014-123] “He’s done it this time!” Katerina Ivanovna cried despairingly and she

[014-124] rushed to her husband.

[014-125] Raskolnikov noticed at once that she was not one of those women who

[014-126] swoon easily. She instantly placed under the luckless man’s head a

[014-127] pillow, which no one had thought of and began undressing and examining

[014-128] him. She kept her head, forgetting herself, biting her trembling lips

[014-129] and stifling the screams which were ready to break from her.

[014-130] Raskolnikov meanwhile induced someone to run for a doctor. There was a

[014-131] doctor, it appeared, next door but one.

[014-132] “I’ve sent for a doctor,” he kept assuring Katerina Ivanovna, “don’t be

[014-133] uneasy, I’ll pay. Haven’t you water?... and give me a napkin or a towel,

[014-134] anything, as quick as you can.... He is injured, but not killed, believe

[014-135] me.... We shall see what the doctor says!”

[014-136] Katerina Ivanovna ran to the window; there, on a broken chair in the

[014-137] corner, a large earthenware basin full of water had been stood, in

[014-138] readiness for washing her children’s and husband’s linen that night.

[014-139] This washing was done by Katerina Ivanovna at night at least twice a

[014-140] week, if not oftener. For the family had come to such a pass that they

[014-141] were practically without change of linen, and Katerina Ivanovna could

[014-142] not endure uncleanliness and, rather than see dirt in the house, she

[014-143] preferred to wear herself out at night, working beyond her strength when

[014-144] the rest were asleep, so as to get the wet linen hung on a line and dry

[014-145] by the morning. She took up the basin of water at Raskolnikov’s request,

[014-146] but almost fell down with her burden. But the latter had already

[014-147] succeeded in finding a towel, wetted it and began washing the blood off

[014-148] Marmeladov’s face.

[014-149] Katerina Ivanovna stood by, breathing painfully and pressing her hands

[014-150] to her breast. She was in need of attention herself. Raskolnikov began

[014-151] to realise that he might have made a mistake in having the injured man

[014-152] brought here. The policeman, too, stood in hesitation.

[014-153] “Polenka,” cried Katerina Ivanovna, “run to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], make haste. If you

[014-154] don’t find her at home, leave word that her father has been run over

[014-155] and that she is to come here at once... when she comes in. Run, Polenka!

[014-156] there, put on the shawl.”

[014-157] “Run your fastest!” cried the little boy on the chair suddenly, after

[014-158] which he relapsed into the same dumb rigidity, with round eyes, his

[014-159] heels thrust forward and his toes spread out.

[014-160] Meanwhile the room had become so full of people that you couldn’t have

[014-161] dropped a pin. The policemen left, all except one, who remained for a

[014-162] time, trying to drive out the people who came in from the stairs. Almost

[014-163] all Madame Lippevechsel’s lodgers had streamed in from the inner rooms

[014-164] of the flat; at first they were squeezed together in the doorway, but

[014-165] afterwards they overflowed into the room. Katerina Ivanovna flew into a

[014-166] fury.

[014-167] “You might let him die in peace, at least,” she shouted at the crowd,

[014-168] “is it a spectacle for you to gape at? With cigarettes! (Cough, cough,

[014-169] cough!) You might as well keep your hats on.... And there is one in his

[014-170] hat!... Get away! You should respect the dead, at least!”

[014-171] Her cough choked her--but her reproaches were not without result. They

[014-175] accident, even in those nearest and dearest to the victim, from which

[014-176] no living man is exempt, even in spite of the sincerest sympathy and

[014-177] [[pity-and-compassion|compassion]].

[014-178] Voices outside were heard, however, speaking of the hospital and saying

[014-179] that they’d no business to make a disturbance here.

[014-180] “No business to die!” cried Katerina Ivanovna, and she was rushing to

[014-182] face with Madame Lippevechsel who had only just heard of the accident

[014-184] irresponsible German.

[014-185] “Ah, my God!” she cried, clasping her hands, “your husband drunken

[014-186] horses have trampled! To the hospital with him! I am [[praskovya-pavlovna|the landlady]]!”

[014-187] “[[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Ludwigovna]], I beg you to recollect what you are saying,”

[014-188] Katerina Ivanovna began haughtily (she always took a haughty tone with

[014-189] the landlady that she might “remember her place” and even now could not

[014-190] deny herself this satisfaction). “Amalia Ludwigovna...”

[014-191] “I have you once before told that you to call me Amalia Ludwigovna may

[014-193] “You are not Amalia Ivanovna, but Amalia Ludwigovna, and as I am not

[014-218] “My God! His whole chest is crushed! How he is bleeding,” she said

[014-219] in despair. “We must take off his clothes. Turn a little, Semyon

[014-220] Zaharovitch, if you can,” she cried to him.

[014-221] Marmeladov recognised her.

[014-222] “A priest,” he articulated huskily.

[014-223] Katerina Ivanovna walked to the window, laid her head against the window

[014-224] frame and exclaimed in despair:

[014-225] “Oh, cursed life!”

[014-226] “A priest,” the dying man said again after a moment’s silence.

[014-227] “They’ve gone for him,” Katerina Ivanovna shouted to him, he obeyed her

[014-228] shout and was silent. With sad and timid eyes he looked for her; she

[014-229] returned and stood by his pillow. He seemed a little easier but not for

[014-230] long.

[014-231] Soon his eyes rested on little Lida, his favourite, who was shaking in

[014-232] the corner, as though she were in a fit, and staring at him with her

[014-233] wondering childish eyes.

[014-234] “A-ah,” he signed towards her uneasily. He wanted to say something.

[014-235] “What now?” cried Katerina Ivanovna.

[014-236] “Barefoot, barefoot!” he muttered, indicating with frenzied eyes the

[014-237] child’s bare feet.

[014-238] “Be silent,” Katerina Ivanovna cried irritably, “you know why she is

[014-240] “Thank God, the doctor,” exclaimed Raskolnikov, relieved.

[014-246] On the left side, just over the heart, was a large, sinister-looking

[014-251] whispered softly to Raskolnikov.

[014-253] “He will die immediately.”

[014-255] “Not the faintest! He is at the last gasp.... His head is badly injured,

[014-256] too... Hm... I could bleed him if you like, but... it would be useless.

[014-259] “If you like.... But I warn you it will be perfectly useless.”

[014-264] Raskolnikov begged the doctor to remain a little while. He shrugged his

[014-265] shoulders and remained.
