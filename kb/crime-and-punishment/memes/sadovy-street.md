# Sadovy Street

Sadovaya Street (Садовая улица, Garden Street) is one of the major thoroughfares radiating from the [[hay-market]] in central [[st-petersburg]]. It appears at two critical junctures in *Crime and Punishment*, both times in the context of Raskolnikov's agitated walking circuit through the Haymarket district. The street's appearances bookend Raskolnikov's psychological movement toward confession.

## Overview

Sadovaya Street is one of St. Petersburg's principal east–west arteries, running from the Field of Mars in the west to the area near the Haymarket in the east. In the 1860s, it was a commercial thoroughfare lined with shops, tenement buildings, and government offices. The street intersects with Gorokhovaya Street, Voznesensky Prospect, and other major routes, making it a natural axis for foot traffic through the city center. Dostoevsky, who lived at various addresses in this quarter of Petersburg during the 1860s, knew its geography intimately.

## In the Novel

Sadovaya Street is mentioned at two distinct moments in the novel, both during Raskolnikov's wanderings around the Haymarket area.

### First Appearance (Part 2, Chapter 6)

Raskolnikov, having just left Razumihin's gathering and still in a state of feverish agitation, walks from the Haymarket toward Sadovaya Street:

> "He walked from the market-place to Sadovy Street." [013-078]

This brief mention occurs during one of Raskolnikov's compulsive, aimless rambles — the walking that functions throughout the novel as an externalization of his unquiet mind. His path from the Haymarket to Sadovaya traces a real route through the district that Dostoevsky renders with documentary precision.

### Second Appearance (Part 2, Chapter 6)

Later in the same chapter, Raskolnikov has just witnessed a woman — later identified as a seamstress — attempting suicide by jumping into the Catherine Canal. The shock of this spectacle pushes him toward a decisive act:

> "He came to the corner and turned into Sadovy Street." [013-481]

The turn into Sadovy Street is narratively significant: he is headed toward the police station to confess. "I'll go and fall on my knees and tell everything," he says to himself [013-478]. The turn represents a motion toward accountability — and immediately after, "he stopped" [013-482], hesitates, and turns away toward Voznesensky Prospect instead. The geography of confession is mapped onto the geography of the city: every street is a possible route to the police station, and every turn away is a deferral.

The sequence is paradigmatic of what Bakhtin calls Dostoevsky's "threshold" chronotope: the character is in motion, nearing a decisive point, then recoiling from it. The street names are not arbitrary — they constitute a moral cartography in which the distance between Sadovaya Street and the police station is measured not in versts but in the space between intention and action.

## The Neighbourhood

Sadovaya Street runs east–west through the commercial heart of Dostoevsky's Petersburg, passing close to the Haymarket and intersecting with Gorokhovaya Street and Voznesensky Prospect. The street's commercial character — lined with shops, markets, and tenements — makes it part of the bustling, oppressive urban fabric that the novel renders in such visceral detail. The Haymarket district through which Sadovaya passes was one of the poorest and most crowded quarters of the capital, home to the flophouses, taverns, and brothels that form the novel's moral landscape.

## Raskolnikov's Walking Routes

Raskolnikov's walking through the novel is compulsive, obsessive, and geographically precise. His routes trace a circuit centered on the Haymarket: down Sadovaya Street, along the Catherine Canal, up Voznesensky Prospect, past the police station, and back toward his garret on Stolyarny Lane. Each walk is a negotiation with the impulse to confess — the geography of the city becomes the external form of his internal struggle.

The precision of Dostoevsky's street names — Sadovaya, Voznesensky, Stolyarny, Gorokhovaya, and others — is not mere local color. It is a literary technique that anchors the novel's philosophical and psychological drama in the physical reality of the modern city. Raskolnikov is not Everyman wandering an allegorical landscape; he is a specific man walking specific streets in a specific district of St. Petersburg in July 1865. The tension between the universality of the questions the novel raises and the particularity of its urban setting is one of the sources of its enduring power.

## Significance

As a named street with minimal description, Sadovaya functions differently from richly rendered spaces like the [[hay-market]] or [[raskolnikovs-garret]]. Its purpose is cartographic rather than symbolic — it grounds Raskolnikov's walks in the real geography of St. Petersburg. The reader familiar with the city can trace his route: from the Haymarket down Sadovaya, turning toward the canal, approaching the police station, veering away. The specificity of the street names — even when only briefly mentioned — gives the novel its documentary texture.

Yet the street's placement at the moment of Raskolnikov's near-confession elevates it beyond mere cartography. The turn into Sadovy Street is a turn *toward* something — accountability, exposure, the end of isolation — and his halt on that street is the last pause before the long journey toward confession that will occupy the remainder of the novel.
