# Alcohol and Intoxication

Alcohol in *Crime and Punishment* functions as a social anaesthetic and a mechanism of self-destruction, most vividly embodied in the figure of Marmeladov, whose drunken confession to Raskolnikov opens the novel's exploration of poverty, shame, and moral collapse.

## Definition

Intoxication in Dostoevsky's novel is not merely a personal vice but a symptom of systemic social degradation. Drunkenness represents the desperate attempt of marginalized individuals to escape the unbearable weight of their circumstances — poverty, humiliation, and moral failure. As Marmeladov himself articulates, drinking becomes a form of deliberate self-punishment: "I drink because I wish to suffer twice as much!" (001-068).

## Expression in the Novel

The novel opens with Raskolnikov walking through a part of town filled with "drunken men" (001-043), establishing intoxication as part of the Petersburg atmosphere. Marmeladov's extended tavern monologue in Part 1, Chapter 2 forms the most concentrated exploration of alcoholism: he is "very drunk" yet speaks "boldly, only occasionally losing the thread" (002-068). He articulates the paradox of the drunkard: "drunkenness is not a virtue, and this is even more true" (002-072), yet he cannot stop. The tavern itself is populated by "a party of revellers already drunk" (002-312) and an artisan "drunk, but not extremely so" (001-270). Marmeladov's confession that he lay "drunk" while Sonia was forced into prostitution (002-252) crystallizes the moral dimension of his drinking — it is both escape from guilt and source of further guilt. Raskolnikov himself walks "like a drunken man, regardless of the passers-by" (001-243), linking intoxication to the psychological disorientation that pervades the novel.

## Key Passages

> "I drink because I wish to suffer twice as much!" (001-068)

> "He was very drunk: and had" (002-067)

> "I was lying drunk and I heard my Sonia speaking" (002-252)
