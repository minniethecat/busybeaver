[032-001] A strange period began for [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]: it was as though a fog had

[032-002] fallen upon him and wrapped him in a dreary solitude from which there

[032-003] was no escape. Recalling that period long after, he believed that his

[032-004] mind had been clouded at times, and that it had continued so, with

[032-005] intervals, till the final catastrophe. He was convinced that he had been

[032-006] mistaken about many things at that time, for instance as to the date

[032-007] of certain events. Anyway, when he tried later on to piece his

[032-008] recollections together, he learnt a great deal about himself from what

[032-009] other people told him. He had mixed up incidents and had explained

[032-010] events as due to circumstances which existed only in his imagination. At

[032-011] times he was a prey to agonies of morbid uneasiness, amounting sometimes

[032-012] to panic. But he remembered, too, moments, hours, perhaps whole days,

[032-013] of complete apathy, which came upon him as a reaction from his previous

[032-014] terror and might be compared with the abnormal insensibility, sometimes

[032-015] seen in the dying. He seemed to be trying in that latter stage to escape

[032-016] from a full and clear understanding of his position. Certain essential

[032-017] facts which required immediate consideration were particularly irksome

[032-018] to him. How glad he would have been to be free from some cares, the

[032-019] neglect of which would have threatened him with complete, inevitable

[032-020] ruin.

[032-021] He was particularly worried about Svidrigaïlov, he might be said to be

[032-022] permanently thinking of Svidrigaïlov. From the time of Svidrigaïlov’s

[032-023] too menacing and unmistakable words in [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]’s room at the moment of

[032-024] [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]’s death, the normal working of his mind seemed to

[032-025] break down. But although this new fact caused him extreme uneasiness,

[032-026] Raskolnikov was in no hurry for an explanation of it. At times, finding

[032-027] himself in a solitary and remote part of the town, in some wretched

[032-028] eating-house, sitting alone lost in thought, hardly knowing how he had

[032-029] come there, he suddenly thought of Svidrigaïlov. He recognised

[032-030] suddenly, clearly, and with dismay that he ought at once to come to an

[032-031] understanding with that man and to make what terms he could. Walking

[032-032] outside the city gates one day, he positively fancied that they had

[032-033] fixed a meeting there, that he was waiting for Svidrigaïlov. Another

[032-034] time he woke up before daybreak lying on the ground under some bushes

[032-035] and could not at first understand how he had come there.

[032-036] But during the two or three days after Katerina Ivanovna’s death, he

[032-037] had two or three times met Svidrigaïlov at Sonia’s lodging, where he

[032-038] had gone aimlessly for a moment. They exchanged a few words and made no

[032-039] reference to the vital subject, as though they were tacitly agreed not

[032-040] to speak of it for a time.

[032-041] Katerina Ivanovna’s body was still lying in the coffin, Svidrigaïlov was

[032-042] busy making arrangements for the funeral. Sonia too was very busy. At

[032-043] their last meeting Svidrigaïlov informed Raskolnikov that he had made

[032-044] an arrangement, and a very satisfactory one, for Katerina Ivanovna’s

[032-045] children; that he had, through certain connections, succeeded in getting

[032-046] hold of certain personages by whose help the three orphans could be at

[032-047] once placed in very suitable institutions; that the money he had settled

[032-048] on them had been of great assistance, as it is much easier to place

[032-049] orphans with some property than destitute ones. He said something

[032-050] too about Sonia and promised to come himself in a day or two to see

[032-051] Raskolnikov, mentioning that “he would like to consult with him, that

[032-052] there were things they must talk over....”

[032-053] This conversation took place in the passage on the [[staircases|stairs]]. Svidrigaïlov

[032-054] looked intently at Raskolnikov and suddenly, after a brief pause,

[032-055] dropping his voice, asked: “But how is it, Rodion Romanovitch; you

[032-056] don’t seem yourself? You look and you listen, but you don’t seem to

[032-057] understand. Cheer up! We’ll talk things over; I am only sorry, I’ve

[032-058] so much to do of my own business and other people’s. Ah, Rodion

[032-059] Romanovitch,” he added suddenly, “what all men need is fresh air, fresh

[032-060] air... more than anything!”

[032-061] He moved to one side to make way for the priest and server, who

[032-062] were coming up the stairs. They had come for the requiem service. By

[032-063] Svidrigaïlov’s orders it was sung twice a day punctually. Svidrigaïlov

[032-064] went his way. Raskolnikov stood still a moment, thought, and followed

[032-065] the priest into Sonia’s room. He stood at the door. They began quietly,

[032-066] slowly and mournfully singing the service. From his childhood the

[032-067] thought of death and the presence of death had something oppressive

[032-068] and mysteriously awful; and it was long since he had heard the requiem

[032-069] service. And there was something else here as well, too awful and

[032-070] disturbing. He looked at the children: they were all kneeling by the

[032-071] coffin; Polenka was weeping. Behind them Sonia prayed, softly and, as it

[032-072] were, timidly weeping.

[032-073] “These last two days she hasn’t said a word to me, she hasn’t glanced at

[032-074] me,” Raskolnikov thought suddenly. The sunlight was bright in the room;

[032-075] the incense rose in clouds; the priest read, “Give rest, oh Lord....”

[032-076] Raskolnikov stayed all through the service. As he blessed them and

[032-077] took his leave, the priest looked round strangely. After the service,

[032-079] head sink on his shoulder. This slight friendly gesture bewildered

[032-080] Raskolnikov. It seemed strange to him that there was no trace of

[032-081] repugnance, no trace of disgust, no tremor in her hand. It was the

[032-082] furthest limit of self-abnegation, at least so he interpreted it.

[032-084] very miserable. If it had been possible to escape to some solitude, he

[032-085] would have thought himself lucky, even if he had to spend his whole life

[032-086] there. But although he had almost always been by himself of late, he had

[032-087] never been able to feel alone. Sometimes he walked out of the town on to

[032-093] easier and even more solitary. One day at dusk he sat for an hour

[032-095] enjoyed it. But at last he had suddenly felt the same uneasiness again,

[032-102] challenge again... some attack. Yes, yes!” he thought. He went out of

[032-107] arrived. After some hours’ sleep the fever left him, but he woke up

[032-108] late, two o’clock in the afternoon.

[032-109] He remembered that Katerina Ivanovna’s funeral had been fixed for that

[032-111] some food; he ate and drank with appetite, almost with greediness. His

[032-112] head was fresher and he was calmer than he had been for the last three

[032-113] days. He even felt a passing wonder at his previous attacks of panic.
