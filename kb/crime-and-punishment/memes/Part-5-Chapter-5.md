[031-001] Lebeziatnikov looked perturbed.

[031-002] “I’ve come to you, Sofya Semyonovna,” he began. “Excuse me... I thought

[031-003] I should find you,” he said, addressing [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] suddenly, “that is,

[031-004] I didn’t mean anything... of that sort... But I just thought... Katerina

[031-005] Ivanovna has gone out of her mind,” he blurted out suddenly, turning

[031-006] from Raskolnikov to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]].

[031-007] Sonia screamed.

[031-008] “At least it seems so. But... we don’t know what to do, you see! She

[031-009] came back--she seems to have been turned out somewhere, perhaps

[031-010] beaten.... So it seems at least.... She had run to your father’s former

[031-011] chief, she didn’t find him at home: he was dining at some other

[031-012] general’s.... Only fancy, she rushed off there, to the other general’s,

[031-013] and, imagine, she was so persistent that she managed to get the chief to

[031-014] see her, had him fetched out from dinner, it seems. You can imagine what

[031-015] happened. She was turned out, of course; but, according to her own

[031-016] story, she abused him and threw something at him. One may well believe

[031-017] it.... How it is she wasn’t taken up, I can’t understand! Now she is

[031-018] telling everyone, including [[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Ivanovna]]; but it’s difficult to

[031-019] understand her, she is screaming and flinging herself about.... Oh yes,

[031-020] she shouts that since everyone has abandoned her, she will take the

[031-021] children and go into the street with a barrel-organ, and the children

[031-022] will sing and dance, and she too, and collect money, and will go every

[031-023] day under the general’s window... ‘to let everyone see well-born

[031-024] children, whose father was an official, begging in the street.’ She

[031-025] keeps beating the children and they are all crying. She is teaching Lida

[031-026] to sing ‘My Village,’ the boy to dance, Polenka the same. She is tearing

[031-027] up all the clothes, and making them little caps like actors; she means

[031-028] to carry a tin basin and make it tinkle, instead of music.... She won’t

[031-029] listen to anything.... Imagine the state of things! It’s beyond

[031-030] anything!”

[031-031] Lebeziatnikov would have gone on, but Sonia, who had heard him almost

[031-032] breathless, snatched up her cloak and hat, and ran out of the room,

[031-033] putting on her things as she went. Raskolnikov followed her and

[031-034] Lebeziatnikov came after him.

[031-035] “She has certainly gone mad!” he said to Raskolnikov, as they went out

[031-036] into the street. “I didn’t want to frighten Sofya Semyonovna, so I said

[031-037] ‘it seemed like it,’ but there isn’t a doubt of it. They say that in

[031-038] consumption the tubercles sometimes occur in the brain; it’s a pity I

[031-039] know nothing of medicine. I did try to persuade her, but she wouldn’t

[031-040] listen.”

[031-041] “Did you talk to her about the tubercles?”

[031-042] “Not precisely of the tubercles. Besides, she wouldn’t have understood!

[031-043] But what I say is, that if you convince a person logically that he

[031-044] has nothing to cry about, he’ll stop crying. That’s clear. Is it your

[031-045] conviction that he won’t?”

[031-046] “Life would be too easy if it were so,” answered Raskolnikov.

[031-047] “Excuse me, excuse me; of course it would be rather difficult for

[031-048] [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] to understand, but do you know that in Paris they have

[031-049] been conducting serious experiments as to the possibility of curing the

[031-050] insane, simply by logical argument? One professor there, a scientific

[031-051] man of standing, lately dead, believed in the possibility of such

[031-052] treatment. His idea was that there’s nothing really wrong with the

[031-053] physical organism of the insane, and that insanity is, so to say, a

[031-054] logical mistake, an error of judgment, an incorrect view of things. He

[031-055] gradually showed the madman his error and, would you believe it, they

[031-056] say he was successful? But as he made use of douches too, how far

[031-057] success was due to that treatment remains uncertain.... So it seems at

[031-058] least.”

[031-059] Raskolnikov had long ceased to listen. Reaching the house where he

[031-060] lived, he nodded to Lebeziatnikov and went in at the gate. Lebeziatnikov

[031-061] woke up with a start, looked about him and hurried on.

[031-062] Raskolnikov went into his little room and stood still in the middle

[031-063] of it. Why had he come back here? He looked at the yellow and tattered

[031-064] paper, at the dust, at his sofa.... From the yard came a loud continuous

[031-065] knocking; someone seemed to be hammering... He went to the window, rose

[031-066] on tiptoe and looked out into the yard for a long time with an air of

[031-067] absorbed attention. But the yard was empty and he could not see who was

[031-068] hammering. In the house on the left he saw some open windows; on the

[031-069] window-sills were pots of sickly-looking geraniums. Linen was hung out

[031-070] of the windows... He knew it all by heart. He turned away and sat down

[031-071] on the sofa.

[031-072] Never, never had he felt himself so fearfully alone!

[031-073] Yes, he felt once more that he would perhaps come to hate Sonia, now

[031-074] that he had made her more miserable.

[031-075] “Why had he gone to her to beg for her tears? What need had he to poison

[031-076] her life? Oh, the meanness of it!”

[031-077] “I will remain alone,” he said resolutely, “and she shall not come to

[031-078] the prison!”

[031-079] Five minutes later he raised his head with a strange smile. That was a

[031-080] strange thought.

[031-081] “Perhaps it really would be better in Siberia,” he thought suddenly.

[031-082] He could not have said how long he sat there with vague thoughts surging

[031-083] through his mind. All at once the door opened and [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] came in. At

[031-084] first she stood still and looked at him from the doorway, just as he

[031-085] had done at Sonia; then she came in and sat down in the same place

[031-086] as yesterday, on the chair facing him. He looked silently and almost

[031-087] vacantly at her.

[031-088] “Don’t be angry, brother; I’ve only come for one minute,” said Dounia.

[031-089] Her face looked thoughtful but not stern. Her eyes were bright and soft.

[031-090] He saw that she too had come to him with love.

[031-091] “Brother, now I know all, _all_. Dmitri Prokofitch has explained and

[031-092] told me everything. They are worrying and persecuting you through a

[031-093] stupid and contemptible suspicion.... Dmitri Prokofitch told me that

[031-094] there is no danger, and that you are wrong in looking upon it with such

[031-095] horror. I don’t think so, and I fully understand how indignant you must

[031-096] be, and that that indignation may have a permanent effect on you. That’s

[031-097] what I am afraid of. As for your cutting yourself off from us, I don’t

[031-098] judge you, I don’t venture to judge you, and forgive me for having

[031-099] blamed you for it. I feel that I too, if I had so great a trouble,

[031-100] should keep away from everyone. I shall tell mother nothing _of this_,

[031-101] but I shall talk about you continually and shall tell her from you that

[031-102] you will come very soon. Don’t worry about her; _I_ will set her mind at

[031-103] rest; but don’t you try her too much--come once at least; remember that

[031-104] she is your mother. And now I have come simply to say” (Dounia began

[031-105] to get up) “that if you should need me or should need... all my life or

[031-106] anything... call me, and I’ll come. Good-bye!”

[031-107] She turned abruptly and went towards the door.

[031-108] “Dounia!” Raskolnikov stopped her and went towards her. “That [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]],

[031-109] Dmitri Prokofitch, is a very good fellow.”

[031-110] Dounia flushed slightly.

[031-111] “Well?” she asked, waiting a moment.

[031-112] “He is competent, hardworking, honest and capable of real love....

[031-113] Good-bye, Dounia.”

[031-114] Dounia flushed crimson, then suddenly she took alarm.

[031-115] “But what does it mean, brother? Are we really parting for ever that

[031-116] you... give me such a parting message?”

[031-117] “Never mind.... Good-bye.”

[031-118] He turned away, and walked to the window. She stood a moment, looked at

[031-119] him uneasily, and went out troubled.

[031-120] No, he was not cold to her. There was an instant (the very last one)

[031-121] when he had longed to take her in his arms and _say good-bye_ to her,

[031-122] and even _to tell_ her, but he had not dared even to touch her hand.

[031-123] “Afterwards she may shudder when she remembers that I embraced her, and

[031-124] will feel that I stole her kiss.”

[031-125] “And would _she_ stand that test?” he went on a few minutes later to

[031-126] himself. “No, she wouldn’t; girls like that can’t stand things! They

[031-127] never do.”

[031-128] And he thought of Sonia.

[031-129] There was a breath of fresh air from the window. The daylight was

[031-130] fading. He took up his cap and went out.

[031-131] He could not, of course, and would not consider how ill he was. But all

[031-132] this continual anxiety and agony of mind could not but affect him. And

[031-133] if he were not lying in high [[illness-and-delirium|fever]] it was perhaps just because this

[031-134] continual inner strain helped to keep him on his legs and in possession

[031-135] of his faculties. But this artificial excitement could not last long.

[031-136] He wandered aimlessly. The sun was setting. A special form of misery had

[031-137] begun to oppress him of late. There was nothing poignant, nothing acute

[031-138] about it; but there was a feeling of permanence, of eternity about it;

[031-139] it brought a foretaste of hopeless years of this cold leaden misery, a

[031-140] foretaste of an eternity “on a square yard of space.” Towards evening

[031-141] this sensation usually began to weigh on him more heavily.

[031-142] “With this idiotic, purely physical weakness, depending on the sunset or

[031-143] something, one can’t help doing something stupid! You’ll go to Dounia,

[031-144] as well as to Sonia,” he muttered bitterly.

[031-145] He heard his name called. He looked round. Lebeziatnikov rushed up to

[031-146] him.

[031-147] “Only fancy, I’ve been to your room looking for you. Only fancy, she’s

[031-148] carried out her plan, and taken away the children. Sofya Semyonovna and

[031-149] I have had a job to find them. She is rapping on a frying-pan and making

[031-150] the children dance. The children are crying. They keep stopping at the

[031-151] cross-roads and in front of shops; there’s a crowd of fools running

[031-152] after them. Come along!”

[031-153] “And Sonia?” Raskolnikov asked anxiously, hurrying after Lebeziatnikov.

[031-154] “Simply frantic. That is, it’s not Sofya Semyonovna’s frantic, but

[031-155] Katerina Ivanovna, though Sofya Semyonova’s frantic too. But Katerina

[031-156] Ivanovna is absolutely frantic. I tell you she is quite mad. They’ll be

[031-157] taken to the police. You can fancy what an effect that will have....

[031-158] They are on [[catherine-canal|the canal]] bank, near the bridge now, not far from Sofya

[031-159] Semyonovna’s, quite close.”

[031-176] one may say aristocratic, house” had been brought to. If she heard

[031-177] laughter or jeering in the crowd, she would rush at once at the scoffers

[031-184] the singing, but broke down at the second note with a fearful cough,

[031-186] furious was the weeping and terror of Kolya and Lida. Some effort had

[031-188] boy had on a turban made of something red and white to look like a Turk.

[031-189] There had been no costume for Lida; she simply had a red knitted cap,

[031-190] or rather a night cap that had belonged to [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]], decorated with

[031-191] a broken piece of white ostrich feather, which had been Katerina

[031-192] Ivanovna’s grandmother’s and had been preserved as a family possession.
