# Paranoia and Surveillance

**Paranoia and surveillance** form a central psychological dynamic in Dostoevsky's *Crime and Punishment*, tracing [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s descent into a state of near-constant dread following the murder. The fear of being watched — by the police, by strangers in the street, by the very walls of his garret — becomes both a manifestation of guilt and a mechanism of psychological unraveling.

## The Fear of Discovery

From the moment after the murder, Raskolnikov is consumed by the terror of a police search. "A search — there will be a search at once," he thinks, frantically checking his clothing for traces of blood (008-604). This fear is not irrational — [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] will later confirm that a search was indeed conducted — but it metastasizes into a pervasive state of hypervigilance.

> "And what if there has been a search already? What if I find them in my room?" (009-001)

## The Tradesman's Accusation

The paranoid state crystallizes in one of the novel's most striking episodes: a mysterious tradesman points at Raskolnikov in the street and whispers "Murderer!" (Part 3, Chapter 6). Though the man later apologizes — he had only heard rumors — the moment shatters Raskolnikov's fragile composure and marks a turning point in his psychological deterioration. The episode blurs the line between genuine surveillance and guilt-induced [[dreams-and-hallucinations|hallucination]].

## Porfiry's Psychological Game

[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s method of investigation is itself a form of psychological surveillance. Rather than gather physical evidence, he observes Raskolnikov's behavior, engages him in philosophical conversation, and constructs an atmosphere of gentle but inexorable scrutiny. His famous "cat and mouse" technique turns Raskolnikov's own paranoia into the primary instrument of his undoing.

## Moral Implication

Dostoevsky uses paranoia and surveillance to dramatize a theological insight: the murderer is never truly unobserved, because conscience itself is an internal watcher. Raskolnikov's eventual [[confession|confession]] is less a response to external pressure than to the unbearable weight of being seen — by Porfiry, by Sonia, and ultimately by himself.
