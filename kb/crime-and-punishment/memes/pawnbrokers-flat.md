# The Pawnbroker's Flat

The pawnbroker's flat is the fourth-floor apartment in a building on S. Lane where [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] lives and conducts her money-lending business, and where [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] commits the double murder that is the novel's inciting crime. "The little room into which the young man walked, with yellow paper on the walls, geraniums in the windows, and muslin curtains" (001-180) becomes the novel's most densely charged space: site of the murder, scene of near-discovery, and the origin point of the physical evidence — the stolen trinkets and purse hidden under a stone — that links Raskolnikov to his crime.

## Description

The flat consists of a small entryway, a main room where Alyona conducts business, and interior rooms where she keeps her strongbox. The main room is decorated with "yellow paper on the walls, geraniums in the windows, and muslin curtains" (001-180) — a description of suffocating domestic respectability. The flat is secured by a door that Alyona keeps locked from inside with a hook during the day (007-270), a detail that [[koch|Koch]] notes as suspicious when he arrives and finds it locked from inside. The flat's layout — two rooms with a kitchen near the entrance — determines the murder's spatial logic: Raskolnikov kills Alyona in the main room, then must kill [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] when she enters through the kitchen.

## Location & Geography

The flat is on the fourth floor of a tenement building in S. Lane, near the Hay Market, within walking distance of Raskolnikov's [[raskolnikovs-garret|garret]] on S. Place. Its elevated position — up four flights of stairs — is crucial to the novel's suspense architecture: Raskolnikov must descend the stairs undetected after the murder, a sequence that becomes the novel's first extended suspense set piece.

## Key Scenes

- **Raskolnikov's rehearsal visit** (Part 1, Chapter 1): Raskolnikov pawns his father's watch and memorizes the flat's layout, noting where Alyona keeps her keys and money-box.
- **The murder** (Part 1, Chapter 7): Raskolnikov enters with a fake "cigarette case" pledge, strikes Alyona with the axe, then kills the returning Lizaveta. The scene is rendered in claustrophobic detail.
- **Koch and Pestryakov's discovery** (Part 1, Chapter 7): Koch and [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]] arrive for appointments, find the door locked, and eventually discover the bodies after Raskolnikov's escape.

## Symbolic & Atmospheric Significance

The pawnbroker's flat is the novel's architectural symbol of the closed, calculable space that Raskolnikov's rational theory requires — and the scene of that theory's catastrophic failure. Its domestic details (geraniums, muslin curtains, yellow wallpaper) insistently remind the reader that this is a home, not an abstraction, and that the woman Raskolnikov kills is a person who waters plants and draws curtains. The flat's two-room structure also encodes the novel's moral geometry: the murder of Alyona in the inner room corresponds to the "planned" crime, while the murder of Lizaveta in the outer room is the "unplanned" crime that undoes the theory.
