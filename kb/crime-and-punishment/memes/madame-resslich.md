# Madame Resslich

Madame Resslich is a minor but atmospherically significant figure in *Crime and Punishment* — a German landlady in [[st-petersburg]] whose proximity to [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] and shadowy involvement in his affairs contribute to the novel's portrait of the city's moral underworld.

## Overview

Madame Resslich appears only at the margins of the narrative, yet her presence casts a long shadow. She is Svidrigailov's landlady and neighbor — he lodges "at Madame Resslich's, the other side of the wall" (031-450) — and is described, ominously, as "an old and devoted friend" of his (031-451). Her association with Svidrigailov, combined with her role in the urban geography of the novel (her flat is adjacent to the Kapernaumovs', where [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] lives), positions her at the intersection of St. Petersburg's respectable and criminal spheres.

## Role in the Novel

Madame Resslich's most significant narrative function is her connection to Svidrigailov's backstory. It is through her that Svidrigailov reportedly had access to a vulnerable young girl — a deaf-mute teenager who, according to rumor, committed suicide after Svidrigailov "insulted her cruelly" (035-013). [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] alludes to this incident when she confronts Svidrigailov, noting that Madame Resslich "seems in a way to have arranged" the girl's fate (035-049).

This backstory — never directly narrated, only reported through other characters' speech — transforms Madame Resslich from a mere landlady into a symbol of the city's capacity to facilitate predation. She is the kind of figure who exists in the interstices of respectability: a property owner, a member of the German merchant community, yet implicated in the exploitation of those without protection.

## Key Relationships

- **[[arkady-svidrigailov|Arkady Svidrigailov]]**: Her tenant and, by her own admission, a "devoted friend." Their relationship is one of mutual convenience — she provides him lodging and, it is implied, access; he provides her with money and the aura of aristocratic connection.

- **[[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia Raskolnikov]]**: Indirectly linked — Dounia's knowledge of the deaf-mute girl's fate (facilitated by Resslich) fuels her determination to resist Svidrigailov's advances, culminating in the climactic confrontation at [[dounia-confronts-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov's rented room]].

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Madame Resslich belongs to Dostoevsky's gallery of Petersburg landladies — figures like [[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Lippevechsel]] and [[praskovya-pavlovna|Praskovya Pavlovna]] — who collectively represent the city's economic infrastructure of exploitation. They are the gatekeepers of rooms, the holders of leases, the witnesses to comings and goings. In a novel obsessively concerned with thresholds and enclosed spaces, landladies are the guardians of spatial boundaries — and in Resslich's case, the guardian is complicit in what happens behind the walls she owns.

Her German name — like Lippevechsel's — also situates her within the substantial German population of 19th-century St. Petersburg, an ethnic community that Dostoevsky often portrayed with ambivalence: industrious and orderly, yet capable of a peculiarly bureaucratic form of moral indifference.

## Key Quotes

> "I lodge here at Madame Resslich's, the other side of the wall. Here is Kapernaumov, and there lives Madame Resslich, an old and devoted friend of mine" (031-450) — Svidrigailov's casual introduction of Resslich, the word "devoted" carrying an ironic, sinister undertone.
