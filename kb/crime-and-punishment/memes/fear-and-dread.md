# Fear and Dread

Fear in *Crime and Punishment* is not a passing emotion but a structural condition — the psychological atmosphere that permeates every scene, from Raskolnikov's "dread of meeting anyone" before the crime to the "dreadful stillness" of the murder scene to the paranoia that pursues him through Petersburg's streets [001-015].

## Definition

Dostoevsky distinguishes between ordinary *fear* (the anticipation of a specific threat) and existential *dread* (a diffuse, objectless terror that saturates consciousness). Raskolnikov experiences both: the sharp fear of being discovered by the police, and the deeper dread that his entire philosophical framework is collapsing. The novel's innovation is to treat this dread not as a mere symptom of guilt but as the experiential proof that Raskolnikov's "extraordinary man" theory is false — the extraordinary man, by definition, would not feel dread [001-026].

## Expression in the Novel

Fear operates on every level of the narrative. Structurally, Dostoevsky builds suspense through Raskolnikov's constant awareness of being watched — by the landlady, by strangers in the street, by Porfiry's knowing smile. Atmospherically, Petersburg itself is rendered as a city of fear: cramped rooms, suffocating heat, "dreadful stillness" in stairwells and corridors [020-309]. Psychologically, Raskolnikov's fear manifests somatically — his "hands were fearfully weak," he is seized by chills and fever, he faints at the police station [007-053].

The novel traces fear's evolution across Raskolnikov's arc. Before the crime, fear is anticipatory — "aware of his fears," he oscillates between resolve and paralysis [001-026]. During the crime, fear gives way to "simple horror and loathing" — a more primitive response that bypasses his rational defenses [007-176]. After the crime, fear metastasizes into paranoia: every glance is suspicion, every conversation a trap. Only in the Epilogue, after confession and the beginning of genuine remorse, does fear begin to dissipate — replaced by the quieter suffering of love and acceptance.

A crucial counterpoint is Sonia, whose fear is of a different order. "She was always shy and dreaded conversations," the narrator notes — her fear is not of punishment but of human connection, born from shame rather than guilt [010-031]. Her courage in the Lazarus reading scene — facing her dread to reach Raskolnikov — models the transformation of fear through faith that Raskolnikov cannot yet achieve.

## Key Passages

> "He had a peculiar dread of meeting anyone" — the novel's first description of Raskolnikov's psychological state, establishing dread as his baseline condition before the crime [001-015].

> "He felt he had cast off that fearful burden that had so long been weighing upon him" — the brief, illusory relief after deciding to act, a calm that the novel immediately undermines [005-270].

> "Raskolnikov had a fearful dream. He dreamt he was back in his childhood" — the horse-beating dream, where childhood terror and moral horror fuse into the novel's most visceral scene [005-069].

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Fear is both his torment and his truth-teller. His rational mind insists that the extraordinary man is beyond fear, but his body — in fainting, trembling, fever — testifies that he is not extraordinary. His dread is the voice of conscience speaking in a language his philosophy cannot refute.

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Experiences fear as social shame and religious awe. Her "dread of conversations" stems from her position as a fallen woman, but her fear before God — trembling as she reads the Gospel — is the reverent fear that leads to faith, not paralysis.

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: Does not feel fear but studies it. His psychological method depends on fear as the criminal's inevitable response: "if he's guilty, he's sure to come" [033-136]. Porfiry weaponizes Raskolnikov's fear, using silences and ambiguous remarks to let dread do the work of interrogation.
