# St. Petersburg

Dostoevsky's St. Petersburg is not a backdrop but an active participant in the novel's moral drama — a city of "insufferable stench from the pot-houses" (001-042) and "poverty-stricken appearance" (003-004), where the heat, the crowds, and the architecture itself seem to conspire toward [[murder-of-pawnbroker|the crime]]. The city's geography — its [[hay-market|squares]], [[raskolnikovs-garret|garrets]], bridges, and [[police-station|police stations]] — maps [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s psychological disintegration with cartographic precision.

## Geography and Atmosphere

The novel maps specific locations across the city with obsessive precision. In July, the city is described with "insufferable" heat (008-194) that compounds the characters' physical and psychological distress. Dostoevsky's St. Petersburg is the anti-Imperial capital — not the city of palaces and prospects, but of cramped tenements, stifling back staircases, and public squares where "the insufferable stench from the pot-houses, which are particularly numerous in that part of the city, and the drunken men whom he met continually, although it was a working day, completed the revolting misery of the scene" (001-042).

## The Hay Market

The [[hay-market|Hay Market]] (Sennaya Ploshchad) is the novel's moral crossroads. Raskolnikov overhears the crucial conversation about the pawnbroker there (001-080), and it is there — at [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s instruction — that he kneels to kiss the earth and publicly [[confession-at-hay-market|confess]]. The square is the city's exposed conscience, where private intentions are subjected to the judgment of the crowd.

## Bridges and Thresholds

The novel's bridges — K. Bridge, the Lesser Neva crossing (005-035–005-036) — function as thresholds between states of being. Raskolnikov repeatedly stands on bridges contemplating suicide, and it is on a bridge that he receives the lash from a coachman's whip (005-184–005-185), a moment of public humiliation that foreshadows his confession.

## Dostoevsky's Petersburg

Dostoevsky's rendering of the city draws on and transforms the "Petersburg text" of Russian literature — Pushkin's elegant imperial capital and Gogol's phantasmagoric labyrinth. Dostoevsky's contribution is the psychological city: a St. Petersburg whose geography is interiorized, where every street and staircase is both a physical location and a state of mind. The critic Nikolai Antsiferov called it the "city of the soul" — a place where "there can be no architecture without a human soul."
