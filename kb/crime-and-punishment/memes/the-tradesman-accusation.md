# The Tradesman's Accusation

One of the most unsettling moments in *Crime and Punishment* occurs when an anonymous tradesman — a man [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has never seen before — approaches him in the street and delivers a single, devastating accusation. The brief encounter, in Part 3, Chapter 6, functions as the novel's first external confirmation that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s secret is known.

## The Encounter

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is walking near his lodgings when the porter of his building points him out to a stranger — a short man "who looked like an artisan, wearing a long coat and a waistcoat, and looking at a distance remarkably like a woman. He stooped, and his head in a greasy cap hung forward." The man stares at [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] with unsettling intensity, then follows him.

When [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] turns to face him, the stranger speaks a single sentence:

> "_You_ are a murderer," the man answered still more articulately and emphatically, with a smile of triumphant hatred, and again he looked straight into Raskolnikov's pale face and stricken eyes. [021-045]

Then — without explanation, without demanding anything, without denouncing him to the authorities — the man turns and walks away. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], already feverish and unravelling, is shattered.

## Who Is the Tradesman?

The novel never fully reveals the tradesman's identity. Later, during [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]]'s investigation, the tradesman reappears — as a witness who saw [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] return to the pawnbroker's building after the murder and ring the bell, asking strange questions. But at [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s door, the tradesman suddenly recants his accusation, claiming he was mistaken.

[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]], with characteristic ambiguity, suggests the man was frightened off — but by whom, or what, remains unclear. The episode is one of several in *Crime and Punishment* where the machinery of detection and justice is shadowed by inexplicable events that resist rational explanation.

## Psychological Impact

The tradesman's accusation is a psychological turning point for [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]. Before this moment, he could believe his crime was hidden behind a wall of rational precaution. The anonymous stranger — who has no apparent motive, who does not go to the police, who simply _knows_ — shatters that illusion. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] realises that his guilt is not a secret file locked in a drawer but a visible stain, perceptible to strangers on the street.

> "He knows... he knows everything," Raskolnikov thought, frozen. "How does he know? Who could have told him?"

The encounter accelerates [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s psychological disintegration and drives him closer to the confession that will come, first to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and ultimately to the authorities.

## Narrative Function

The tradesman's accusation is one of Dostoevsky's most characteristic devices: an intrusion of the inexplicable into a narrative that otherwise follows the logic of crime and detection. The stranger is both a real person — a witness — and something more: an embodiment of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s guilt, a voice from the collective unconscious of [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]] that has already judged him. In a novel that pits rationalism (the extraordinary man theory, the utilitarian calculus, [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s forensic logic) against forces that exceed reason, the anonymous tradesman belongs to the latter camp.
