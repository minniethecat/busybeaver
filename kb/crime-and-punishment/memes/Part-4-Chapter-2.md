[022-001] It was nearly eight o’clock. The two young men hurried to Bakaleyev’s,

[022-002] to arrive before Luzhin.

[022-003] “Why, who was that?” asked [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]], as soon as they were in the

[022-004] street.

[022-005] “It was Svidrigaïlov, that landowner in whose house my sister was

[022-006] insulted when she was their governess. Through his persecuting her with

[022-007] his attentions, she was turned out by his wife, [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]]. This

[022-008] Marfa Petrovna begged [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]’s forgiveness afterwards, and she’s just

[022-009] died suddenly. It was of her we were talking this morning. I don’t

[022-010] know why I’m afraid of that man. He came here at once after his wife’s

[022-011] funeral. He is very strange, and is determined on doing something.... We

[022-012] must guard Dounia from him... that’s what I wanted to tell you, do you

[022-013] hear?”

[022-014] “Guard her! What can he do to harm Avdotya Romanovna? Thank you, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodya]],

[022-015] for speaking to me like that.... We will, we will guard her. Where does

[022-016] he live?”

[022-017] “I don’t know.”

[022-018] “Why didn’t you ask? What a pity! I’ll find out, though.”

[022-019] “Did you see him?” asked Raskolnikov after a pause.

[022-020] “Yes, I noticed him, I noticed him well.”

[022-021] “You did really see him? You saw him clearly?” Raskolnikov insisted.

[022-022] “Yes, I remember him perfectly, I should know him in a thousand; I have

[022-023] a good memory for faces.”

[022-024] They were silent again.

[022-025] “Hm!... that’s all right,” muttered Raskolnikov. “Do you know, I

[022-026] fancied... I keep thinking that it may have been an hallucination.”

[022-027] “What do you mean? I don’t understand you.”

[022-028] “Well, you all say,” Raskolnikov went on, twisting his mouth into a

[022-029] smile, “that I am mad. I thought just now that perhaps I really am mad,

[022-030] and have only seen a phantom.”

[022-031] “What do you mean?”

[022-032] “Why, who can tell? Perhaps I am really mad, and perhaps everything that

[022-033] happened all these days may be only imagination.”

[022-034] “Ach, Rodya, you have been upset again!... But what did he say, what did

[022-035] he come for?”

[022-036] Raskolnikov did not answer. Razumihin thought a minute.

[022-037] “Now let me tell you my story,” he began, “I came to you, you were

[022-038] asleep. Then we had dinner and then I went to [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]’s, [[zametov|Zametov]] was

[022-039] still with him. I tried to begin, but it was no use. I couldn’t speak in

[022-040] the right way. They don’t seem to understand and can’t understand, but

[022-041] are not a bit ashamed. I drew Porfiry to the window, and began talking

[022-042] to him, but it was still no use. He looked away and I looked away. At

[022-043] last I shook my fist in his ugly face, and told him as a cousin I’d

[022-044] brain him. He merely looked at me, I cursed and came away. That was

[022-045] all. It was very stupid. To Zametov I didn’t say a word. But, you see, I

[022-046] thought I’d made a mess of it, but as I went downstairs a brilliant idea

[022-047] struck me: why should we trouble? Of course if you were in any danger

[022-048] or anything, but why need you care? You needn’t care a hang for them. We

[022-049] shall have a laugh at them afterwards, and if I were in your place I’d

[022-050] mystify them more than ever. How ashamed they’ll be afterwards! Hang

[022-051] them! We can thrash them afterwards, but let’s laugh at them now!”

[022-052] “To be sure,” answered Raskolnikov. “But what will you say to-morrow?”

[022-053] he thought to himself. Strange to say, till that moment it had never

[022-054] occurred to him to wonder what Razumihin would think when he knew. As he

[022-055] thought it, Raskolnikov looked at him. Razumihin’s account of his visit

[022-056] to Porfiry had very little interest for him, so much had come and gone

[022-057] since then.

[022-058] In the corridor they came upon Luzhin; he had arrived punctually

[022-059] at eight, and was looking for the number, so that all three went in

[022-060] together without greeting or looking at one another. The young men

[022-061] walked in first, while Pyotr Petrovitch, for good manners, lingered a

[022-062] little in the passage, taking off his coat. [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] came

[022-063] forward at once to greet him in the doorway, Dounia was welcoming her

[022-064] brother. Pyotr Petrovitch walked in and quite amiably, though with

[022-065] redoubled dignity, bowed to the ladies. He looked, however, as though

[022-066] he were a little put out and could not yet recover himself. Pulcheria

[022-067] Alexandrovna, who seemed also a little embarrassed, hastened to make

[022-068] them all sit down at the round table where a samovar was boiling. Dounia

[022-069] and Luzhin were facing one another on opposite sides of the table.

[022-070] Razumihin and Raskolnikov were facing Pulcheria Alexandrovna, Razumihin

[022-071] was next to Luzhin and Raskolnikov was beside his sister.

[022-072] A moment’s silence followed. Pyotr Petrovitch deliberately drew out a

[022-073] cambric handkerchief reeking of scent and blew his nose with an air of

[022-074] a benevolent man who felt himself slighted, and was firmly resolved to

[022-075] insist on an explanation. In the passage the idea had occurred to him to

[022-076] keep on his overcoat and walk away, and so give the two ladies a sharp

[022-077] and emphatic lesson and make them feel the gravity of the position.

[022-078] But he could not bring himself to do this. Besides, he could not endure

[022-079] uncertainty, and he wanted an explanation: if his request had been so

[022-080] openly disobeyed, there was something behind it, and in that case it was

[022-081] better to find it out beforehand; it rested with him to punish them and

[022-082] there would always be time for that.

[022-083] “I trust you had a favourable journey,” he inquired officially of

[022-084] Pulcheria Alexandrovna.

[022-085] “Oh, very, Pyotr Petrovitch.”

[022-086] “I am gratified to hear it. And Avdotya Romanovna is not over-fatigued

[022-087] either?”

[022-088] “I am young and strong, I don’t get tired, but it was a great strain for

[022-089] mother,” answered Dounia.

[022-090] “That’s unavoidable! our national railways are of terrible length.

[022-091] ‘Mother Russia,’ as they say, is a vast country.... In spite of all my

[022-092] desire to do so, I was unable to meet you yesterday. But I trust all

[022-093] passed off without inconvenience?”

[022-094] “Oh, no, Pyotr Petrovitch, it was all terribly disheartening,” Pulcheria

[022-095] Alexandrovna hastened to declare with peculiar intonation, “and if

[022-096] Dmitri Prokofitch had not been sent us, I really believe by God Himself,

[022-097] we should have been utterly lost. Here, he is! Dmitri Prokofitch

[022-098] Razumihin,” she added, introducing him to Luzhin.

[022-099] “I had the pleasure... yesterday,” muttered Pyotr Petrovitch with a

[022-100] hostile glance sidelong at Razumihin; then he scowled and was silent.

[022-103] directly they are crossed in anything, are completely disconcerted, and

[022-104] become more like sacks of flour than elegant and lively men of society.

[022-105] Again all was silent; Raskolnikov was obstinately mute, Avdotya

[022-106] Romanovna was unwilling to open the conversation too soon. Razumihin had

[022-107] nothing to say, so Pulcheria Alexandrovna was anxious again.

[022-108] “Marfa Petrovna is dead, have you heard?” she began having recourse to

[022-109] her leading item of conversation.

[022-110] “To be sure, I heard so. I was immediately informed, and I have come to

[022-113] at least I have excellent authority for believing.”

[022-114] “To Petersburg? here?” Dounia asked in alarm and looked at her mother.

[022-115] “Yes, indeed, and doubtless not without some design, having in view the

[022-116] rapidity of his departure, and all the circumstances preceding it.”

[022-117] “Good heavens! won’t he leave Dounia in peace even here?” cried

[022-118] Pulcheria Alexandrovna.

[022-119] “I imagine that neither you nor Avdotya Romanovna have any grounds for

[022-120] uneasiness, unless, of course, you are yourselves desirous of getting

[022-121] into communication with him. For my part I am on my guard, and am now

[022-122] discovering where he is lodging.”

[022-123] “Oh, Pyotr Petrovitch, you would not believe what a fright you have

[022-124] given me,” Pulcheria Alexandrovna went on: “I’ve only seen him twice,

[022-125] but I thought him terrible, terrible! I am convinced that he was the

[022-126] cause of Marfa Petrovna’s death.”

[022-127] “It’s impossible to be certain about that. I have precise information. I

[022-128] do not dispute that he may have contributed to accelerate the course of

[022-129] events by the moral influence, so to say, of the affront; but as to the

[022-141] Siberia, was hushed up. That’s the sort of man he is, if you care to

[022-142] know.”

[022-167] “Quite so, but what drove him, or rather perhaps disposed him,

[022-169] Svidrigaïlov.”

[022-185] Marfa Petrovna had not the slightest intention of settling anything

[022-190] “Pyotr Petrovitch, I beg you,” said Dounia, “say no more of Mr.

[022-191] Svidrigaïlov. It makes me miserable.”

[022-192] “He has just been to see me,” said Raskolnikov, breaking his silence for

[022-193] the first time.

[022-213] “Yes.”
