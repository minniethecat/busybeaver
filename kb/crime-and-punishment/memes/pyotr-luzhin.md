# Pyotr Luzhin

Pyotr Petrovich Luzhin is a successful Petersburg lawyer who becomes engaged to [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] — not out of love, but from a calculated desire for a wife "of poor family who has known great misfortune" and who "will look upon him as her savior" (020-023). He is the novel's most fully realized portrait of the ethical vacuity underlying progressive self-interest doctrines, and his attempt to frame [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] for theft represents one of the novel's most dramatically charged confrontations.

## Character and Philosophy

Luzhin's intellectual creed is a vulgarized utilitarianism he calls "rational egoism." He argues that if every person pursues their own material self-interest, the aggregate effect will be general prosperity — "acquire for yourself alone, and you will thereby give to your neighbor" (020-152). Dostoevsky uses Luzhin to expose the moral consequences of this doctrine: it provides ideological cover for exploitation while dressing predation as economic rationality.

Luzhin's vanity is his defining trait. He arrives in Petersburg "preening himself like a cock before a mirror" (020-080) and takes excessive pride in his rise from poverty. His speech is laced with self-congratulation about his "new apartment" and "rising career." This vanity makes him vulnerable — when Raskolnikov exposes his mistreatment of Dounia, Luzhin's fragile self-image shatters, and he retaliates with the cruelty of wounded pride.

## Key Scenes

### Arrival and First Meeting (Part 2, Chapter 5)

Luzhin visits Raskolnikov's garret shortly after arriving in Petersburg. The meeting establishes the immediate antipathy between the two men. Luzhin pontificates about the benefits of "rational egoism" while Raskolnikov, ill and irritable, sees through him immediately: "it turns out, according to you, that you can kill" (020-167). Raskolnikov recognizes in Luzhin a degraded mirror of his own theory — both men justify transgression, but Luzhin's version is merely cheap self-interest dressed as principle.

### The Frame-Up (Part 5, Chapter 3)

At [[marmeladovs-memorial-dinner|Marmeladov's memorial dinner]], Luzhin attempts to frame Sonia for stealing a hundred-ruble note. He slips the note into her pocket, then publicly accuses her of theft before the assembled guests. The scheme is exposed by [[andrey-lebeziatnikov|Lebeziatnikov]], who witnessed the act. Luzhin's motive is revenge: by discrediting Sonia, he hopes to damage Raskolnikov's standing with his mother and sister, and thereby salvage his engagement to Dounia. The scene reveals Luzhin's essential character — his rational egoism, stripped of its intellectual veneer, is nothing more than the willingness to destroy an innocent person for personal gain.

### The Confrontation with Dounia (Part 5, Chapter 5)

After the frame-up fails, Dounia confronts Luzhin and breaks off the engagement. She recognizes that his "generosity" was always conditional on her submission: "I offended you, Pyotr Petrovich, I remember. But you have just offended me far more gravely" (025-298). Luzhin leaves defeated, his pride wounded beyond repair. His exit from the novel is marked by spite — he sends a venomous letter to Pulcheria Alexandrovna blaming Raskolnikov for everything.

## Symbolic Function

Luzhin functions as a thematic counterpoint to Raskolnikov. Both men transgress moral boundaries under the cover of theory, but where Raskolnikov's Nietzschean "extraordinary man" doctrine at least has tragic grandeur, Luzhin's "rational egoism" is merely selfishness with footnotes. Dostoevsky uses this contrast to suggest that the two positions are points on a single continuum — the rejection of Christian ethics in favor of human-devised moral systems leads, in the end, to Luzhin's pettiness no less than to Raskolnikov's violence.
