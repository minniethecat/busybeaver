# Family Duty

Beneath the novel's philosophical architecture runs a network of family relationships — mother to son, sister to brother, father to children — each freighted with duty, sacrifice, and guilt. Raskolnikov's crime is not only against the pawnbroker but against his own family, whom his actions threaten with ruin.

## Definition

Family duty in the novel is the unwritten moral code that binds relatives to one another's welfare. It manifests as Pulcheria Alexandrovna's desperate love for her son, Dounia's willingness to sacrifice herself for her brother's future, and Raskolnikov's own submerged guilt at failing his familial obligations. Marmeladov articulates the failure of family duty from the other side — the father who has "not touched this" money because he knows it belongs to his children (PN 002-211), yet who drinks anyway. For Dostoevsky, family is both the primary locus of moral obligation and the place where human selfishness is most starkly exposed.

## Expression in the Novel

Dounia's contemplated marriage to Luzhin is the novel's central case study in family duty perverted: she agrees to marry a man she does not love in order to secure her brother's future. Raskolnikov's outrage — "I won't have your sacrifice, Dounia" (PN 004-153) — is his most morally lucid moment before the murder, the point at which his instinctive love for his sister breaks through his ideological rationalism.

Pulcheria Alexandrovna's letters and visits reveal the intensity and blindness of maternal love. She worships Rodya, cannot see his deterioration for what it is, and dies without knowing her son is a murderer — spared by those around her from the truth. Her death from "brain fever" (PN 006-623) is one of the novel's quiet tragedies: a mother destroyed by love for a son she cannot know.

The Marmeladovs provide the novel's portrait of family as catastrophe: Marmeladov's drinking, Katerina Ivanovna's consumption and pride, Sonia's prostitution, the children's hunger. Family duty here is both absolute and absolutely failed. Yet it is within this ruined family that Sonia — the novel's moral center — emerges, suggesting that even failed families can produce saints.

Raskolnikov's relationship with his own family deteriorates after the crime. He can barely speak to his mother and sister; the guilt creates an unbridgeable distance. He entrusts them to Razumihin, effectively abdicating his family role — and it is Razumihin who proves worthy of the trust, marrying Dounia and caring for Pulcheria Alexandrovna until her death.

## Key Passages

> "I won't have your sacrifice, Dounia, I won't have it so long as I am living" — Raskolnikov's defense of his sister (PN 004-153)

> "She has of her own free will deigned to accept this man" — the language of duty masking coercion (PN 003-256)

> "It is her duty to make her husband happy who had been so good to her" — the perversion of duty into justification for exploitation (PN 003-293)

## Character Perspectives

- **Dounia Raskolnikov**: The dutiful daughter and sister whose trajectory moves from self-sacrifice to self-possession. Her shooting of Svidrigailov and refusal of Luzhin mark her emancipation from duty-as-bondage into duty freely chosen.
- **Pulcheria Alexandrovna**: Maternal love as both devotion and blindness. Her death from "brain fever" is one of the novel's quiet tragedies — a mother destroyed by love for a son she cannot know, yet surrounded by a new family (Razumihin and Dounia) who protects her to the end.
- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His failure of family duty is both cause and symptom of his spiritual sickness. He loves his mother and sister but cannot bear their presence after the murder — the crime has severed the bonds family duty would preserve.
- **Marmeladov**: The novel's most articulate spokesman for family duty in failure. He knows exactly what he owes his wife and children and cannot pay it, turning his guilt into eloquence and his eloquence into further drinking.

## Historical & Philosophical Context

The 1860s saw the "woman question" and the "family question" debated intensely in Russian journals. Radicals like Chernyshevsky advocated companionate marriage and women's economic independence; conservatives defended traditional patriarchal structures. Dostoevsky's treatment of family duty navigates between these positions: he critiques the sacrifice of women to family (Dounia, Sonia) while affirming family as an irreducible moral community that no amount of rationalist theory can dissolve.
