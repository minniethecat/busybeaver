# Poverty and Degradation

Poverty permeates every page of *Crime and Punishment* — not as mere background but as an active force that shapes character, constrains choice, and tests moral limits. Raskolnikov is "crushed by poverty" (001-016); the Marmeladov family is reduced to "hopeless poverty" (002-199); the pawnbroker's clients are "reduced to poverty" and selling their "household goods" (006-004).

## Definition

Marmeladov's tavern speech establishes the novel's key distinction: "poverty is not a vice, that's a true saying. Yet... destitution is a vice. In poverty you may still retain your innate nobility of soul; in destitution you never can" (002-071, 002-074). Poverty is material lack that permits moral dignity; destitution is the condition in which material need compels moral compromise.

## Expression in the Novel

The novel maps poverty across St. Petersburg's spatial and social terrain: Raskolnikov's "garret" (001-016), the Marmeladovs' room of "extreme poverty in their belongings" (016-186), the Hay Market with its "poverty-stricken appearance" (003-004). Poverty is the motive force of the plot: Sonia is driven to prostitution by the family's need; Dounia contemplates marriage to Luzhin to rescue her brother from poverty; Raskolnikov's theory is partly a response to the material degradation he sees and experiences. Luzhin's doctrine — to "raise a wife from poverty, so that you may have complete" control (012-319) — reveals how poverty enables exploitation.

## Key Passages

> "Poverty is not a vice, that's a true saying. Yet... destitution is a vice. In poverty you may still retain your innate nobility of soul; in destitution you never can." (002-071, 002-074)

> "He was crushed by poverty, but the anxieties of his position had of late ceased to weigh upon him." (001-016)

## Character Perspectives

- **Marmeladov**: The philosopher of poverty, who articulates its spiritual dimension even as he is destroyed by it. His drunkenness is self-medication: "I drink so that I may suffer twice as much" (002-165).

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Poverty's most visible victim — forced into prostitution to feed her step-siblings. Yet she retains "innate nobility of soul" that Marmeladov described.

- **Katerina Ivanovna**: Poverty combined with fallen social status drives her to madness and death. Her "poor man's pride" (028-009) prevents her from accepting help.
