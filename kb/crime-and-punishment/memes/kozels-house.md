## Overview

Kozel's House is a tenement building in [[st-petersburg]] where the Marmeladov family occupies a corner room. It is one of the novel's most significant sites of suffering, being the location of [[semyon-marmeladov]]'s death, [[katerina-ivanovna]]'s final illness and death, and the space where [[rodion-raskolnikov]] performs his first sustained act of compassion.

## Description

The building is identified by its owner, Kozel:

> The house of Kozel, the cabinet-maker... a German, a rich man. [002-485]

It stands approximately thirty yards from the Haymarket in a crowded district of tenements:

> Kozel's house was thirty yards away. [014-056]

The Marmeladovs live at the top of the building, in a corner room accessible only by a steep staircase:

> They went in from the yard and up to the fourth storey. The staircase grew darker and darker. [002-487–490]

At the very top, the door stands ajar, revealing the family's squalid quarters: smoke, noise, washing hung across the room, and the dying Katerina Ivanovna coughing on a makeshift bed. The spatial degradation — the dark stairs, the crowded room at the summit of a tenement — mirrors the family's social descent.

## Key Scenes

### Marmeladov's Death

After Marmeladov is crushed by a carriage in the street, Raskolnikov leads the crowd carrying his broken body to Kozel's House. The scene is one of the novel's most detailed renderings of a Petersburg interior:

> They carried him up to the fourth storey. Katerina Ivanovna was pacing up and down her little room. [002-500]

The room fills with neighbours, a priest, and the children, while Marmeladov dies on the bed and Sonia arrives in her gaudy street dress — a collision of private grief and public spectacle that Dostoevsky renders with documentary precision.

### Katerina Ivanovna's Death

Days later, Katerina Ivanovna, driven mad by poverty and consumption, runs out into the street with her children and collapses near the Haymarket. She is carried back to Kozel's House:

> She was carried to Kozel's house and laid on the bed. [014-039–056]

She dies in the same room as her husband, raving about her noble ancestry, leaving the children orphaned. The room thus witnesses both of the Marmeladov parents' deaths.

## Significance

Kozel's House is the spatial centre of the Marmeladov subplot — a counterpoint to [[raskolnikovs-garret]]. Where Raskolnikov's room is the site of intellectual isolation and the planning of transgression, Kozel's House is the site of communal suffering and the first stirrings of Raskolnikov's compassion. It is here that he gives the Marmeladovs his last money, supports the dying Semyon, and meets [[sonia-marmeladov]] for the first time. The building thus functions as the space where Raskolnikov's humanity, suppressed by the murder, first reasserts itself.
