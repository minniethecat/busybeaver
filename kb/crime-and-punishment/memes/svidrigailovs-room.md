## Overview

Svidrigailov's room is a cramped, miserable hotel lodging on the outskirts of [[st-petersburg]] where [[arkady-svidrigailov]] spends the night before his suicide in Part 6, Chapter 6 of *Crime and Punishment*. The room is the physical expression of Svidrigailov's spiritual state: squalid, claustrophobic, and permeated by the damp cold of a Petersburg night.

## Location and Description

The hotel stands on Y. Prospect, in a remote and shabby part of the city:

> He went towards Y. Prospect. He walked along that endless Prospect for a long while; at last he reached the place. There was a hotel there towards the end, a miserable, dirty place with a bar. [037-156–162]

The building is notably conspicuous: "the hotel was so conspicuous in that God-forsaken place that it was impossible not to recognise it." The room itself is described in terms that emphasise its oppressive narrowness:

> It was a very small room, a narrow, cramped little room, such as one might find in any poor provincial hotel. [037-170]

Dostoevsky lingers on the details of the room's discomfort: the wallpaper is peeling, the furniture is shabby, and the air is stale. The window looks out onto a blank wall or a dark courtyard, reinforcing the sense of entrapment.

## The Final Night

Svidrigailov spends his last night in this room tormented by a series of nightmares that reveal his guilt and psychological disintegration. The dreams escalate in horror:

1. **The dead girl**: He sees a young suicide victim laid out in a coffin, surrounded by flowers. [037-190–210]
2. **The drowned woman**: A second dream-recollection of a fourteen-year-old girl who drowned herself after his abuse.
3. **The child**: The most disturbing vision — a five-year-old girl in the corner of the room who becomes a knowing temptress. [037-240–280]

Each nightmare drives Svidrigailov deeper into despair until he is compelled to leave the room and walk into the damp Petersburg morning to shoot himself.

## Significance

The room functions as the spatial counterpart to [[raskolnikovs-garret]]: both are cramped, impoverished spaces where their occupants confront the consequences of their transgressions. But while Raskolnikov's garret is the site of feverish intellectual justification before the murder, Svidrigailov's room is the site of existential reckoning after a life of moral corruption. The room embodies the novel's recurrent spatial motif — the "square yard of space" that Raskolnikov contemplates in his own narrower moments of despair.

Svidrigailov's final words to the sentry ("Say I've gone to America") are a sardonic farewell delivered as he steps out of this room, marking the finality of his exit from both the space and life itself.
