Raskolnikov is a master of self-deception, and the novel's probing of his rationalizations constitutes one of the earliest and most sophisticated explorations of the psychology of self-justification in modern fiction. From the moment he first contemplates the murder, Raskolnikov constructs an elaborate intellectual framework to justify what is, at its root, an act of pure will — the desire to prove himself a "Napoleon," a man beyond conventional morality. The gap between his stated motive (utilitarian calculus: one death for a hundred lives) and his actual motive (existential arrogance: the need to know whether he belongs to the "extraordinary" category) is the novel's central psychological drama.

## The Layers of Rationalization

Raskolnikov's self-deception operates in layers. The outermost layer is the utilitarian argument: Alyona Ivanovna is a "louse," a parasite who exploits the poor, and her death would liberate resources for the deserving (PN 005-187). Beneath this is the extraordinary-man theory: certain individuals have the right to "step over" obstacles, including moral laws, in pursuit of a higher purpose (PN 018-101). And beneath even this is the raw, pre-conceptual desire to be exceptional — the same desire that the nameless student in the tavern articulates when he asks, "Wouldn't thousands of good deeds make up for one tiny little crime?" (PN 006-274). Each layer serves to disguise the one beneath it, and the novel peels them away one by one.

## The Article "On Crime"

Raskolnikov's article "On Crime," which Porfiry Petrovich has read and which becomes the basis for their extended cat-and-mouse dialogue (Part 3, Chapter 5), is the most formal expression of his self-deception. The article divides humanity into the "ordinary" — those who live in obedience and are morally bound by law — and the "extraordinary" — those who have the right to transgress in the service of new truth (PN 018-101). That Raskolnikov published this argument *before* the murder is crucial: the theory is not a retrospective justification but a prospective self-authorization, a philosophical permission slip written by a man who already knows what he intends to do.

## Porfiry as Unmasker

Porfiry Petrovich functions in the novel as the external voice of unmasking — the detective who sees through Raskolnikov's rationalizations not because he has evidence, but because he understands the psychology of guilt. Porfiry never accuses Raskolnikov directly; instead, he engages him in philosophical conversation, inviting him to elaborate his theories while a "little idea" flickers in his eyes (PN 025-047). Porfiry's method is to let Raskolnikov's self-deception collapse under its own weight, a process that culminates in the extraordinary scene where Porfiry tells Raskolnikov, with almost pastoral gentleness, that he knows he is the murderer and that "suffering is a great thing" (PN 035-184).

## The Collapse of Self-Deception

The novel traces the progressive collapse of Raskolnikov's self-deception through a series of crises. The first is the murder itself, which does not go as planned — the unexpected killing of Lizaveta shatters the clean intellectual structure of the utilitarian argument. The second is the growing recognition that he is not a Napoleon: "I wanted to become a Napoleon, that is why I killed her" (PN 027-159). The third is the confession to Sonia, in which he admits that he killed "for myself, for myself alone" (PN 030-439). And the final collapse comes in the epilogue, when a dream of a rationalist plague sweeping the world (PN 039-262) reveals to him the ultimate destination of the logic he has been following.

## Self-Deception and Narrative Form

Dostoevsky's choice of a third-person narrator who is nevertheless closely fused with Raskolnikov's consciousness is integral to the novel's treatment of self-deception. The narrator does not tell us that Raskolnikov is deceiving himself; instead, the narrative voice inhabits Raskolnikov's perspective so thoroughly that we experience the rationalizations from the inside, only gradually recognizing their inadequacy. This technique makes *Crime and Punishment* not merely a novel *about* self-deception but a novel that enacts the experience of self-deception in the reader.
