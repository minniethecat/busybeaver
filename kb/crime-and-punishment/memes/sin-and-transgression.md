# Sin and Transgression

In *Crime and Punishment*, Dostoevsky draws a crucial distinction between *crime* — a legal category defined by positive law — and *sin* — a spiritual condition defined by violation of divine moral order. The novel's Russian title, *Prestuplenie i nakazanie*, begins with the word *prestuplenie*, literally "stepping over" — the transgression of a moral boundary. This linguistic choice signals that the novel's central concern is not criminal justice but the metaphysics of sin.

## The Crime-Sin Distinction

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s initial rationalization of the murder rests on the collapse of sin into crime. If morality is merely a social convention — if there is no transcendent law — then the only question is whether one can escape legal punishment. His [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary man theory]], articulated in his published article, is an attempt to philosophically justify "stepping over" moral boundaries: certain individuals, he argues, have the right to transgress conventional morality for the advancement of humanity [019-330].

The novel systematically dismantles this distinction. Raskolnikov escapes legal detection — Porfiry has no direct evidence — but cannot escape the reality of sin. His fever, delirium, paranoia, and compulsive return to the murder scene are the body's testimony against his rational philosophy. As [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] observes, the guilty man "will suffer for his mistake" regardless of whether he is caught [019-552].

## Orthodox Framework

Dostoevsky's understanding of sin draws on the Russian Orthodox tradition, in which sin (*grekh*) is understood not primarily as a violation of rules but as a sickness of the soul — a separation from God that can only be healed through *pokayanie* (repentance), not merely punished. This framework informs [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s response to Raskolnikov's confession: she does not speak of prison or punishment but of suffering and redemption: "Go now, this minute, stand in the cross-roads, bow down, first kiss the earth which you have defiled, and then bow down to all the world and say to all men aloud, 'I am a murderer!' Then God will send you life again" [026-031].

Sonia's instruction shifts the register from criminal justice to spiritual restoration. Her insistence on public confession — kissing the earth, bowing to "all the world" — is not about legal process but about the communal nature of sin and the necessity of re-entering the human community from which transgression has separated the sinner.

## False Confession and True Repentance

The novel explores the sin-crime distinction through the parallel of [[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay Dementyev]], the house-painter who falsely confesses to the murders. Nikolay's confession is legally false but spiritually meaningful — he is a religious sectarian who seeks suffering as a path to purification. "He wants to suffer for his sins," Porfiry explains [032-287]. Nikolay's false confession contrasts with Raskolnikov's inability to genuinely confess: Raskolnikov technically admits the act but struggles to accept responsibility as sin rather than as a failed philosophical experiment.

## Transgression as Self-Destruction

A central paradox of the novel is that transgression, far from liberating the transgressor, destroys him. Raskolnikov's murder of [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] is meant to be an assertion of will — a Napoleon-like stepping over — but it results in a more profound enslavement: to fear, to isolation, to the very conscience he tried to silence. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] represents the terminal point of this logic: a man who has "stepped over" so many boundaries that existence itself has become meaningless, leading to his suicide. "If you are convinced that you must not listen to the door, then you may act on your conviction — that's all," Svidrigailov tells Raskolnikov, articulating the nihilist position the novel refutes [030-365].

## Redemptive Suffering

Dostoevsky's resolution — anticipated throughout but only fully realized in the Epilogue — is that suffering accepted as consequence of sin becomes the vehicle of spiritual renewal. Raskolnikov's Siberian imprisonment is not merely punitive but transformative: "He did not know that the new life would not be given him for nothing, that he would have to pay dearly for it, that it would cost him great striving, great suffering" [039-828]. The novel's final image — Raskolnikov with the New Testament under his pillow, the "dawn of a new future" — affirms the Orthodox conviction that no sin is beyond redemption, provided the sinner accepts suffering not as external punishment but as internal transformation.
