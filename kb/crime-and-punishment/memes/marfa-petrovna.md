# Marfa Petrovna

Marfa Petrovna is the late wife of [[arkady-svidrigailov|Arkady Svidrigailov]], whose death — officially from a stroke, but widely suspected to have been hastened by her husband — haunts the novel's backstory. Never seen alive by the reader, she is known entirely through the letters and recollections of other characters, particularly through the long letter from [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] in Part 1, Chapter 3. Her "fine and generous behaviour" (003-146) toward [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia Raskolnikov]], whom she first employed as governess and then slandered after suspecting an affair with Svidrigailov, establishes her as a figure of contradictory impulses: jealousy and magnanimity, cruelty and reparation.

## Overview

Marfa Petrovna is a provincial landowner of considerable wealth who "ransomed [Svidrigailov] from the debtor's prison for a large sum of money" (003-150) and married him. She employed Dounia as a governess at her country estate, where Svidrigailov became obsessed with the young woman. After discovering her husband's advances, Marfa Petrovna blamed Dounia, "slandered [her] to everyone in the town" (003-196), and caused a scandal that nearly ruined the Raskolnikov family's reputation. Later discovering Dounia's innocence through a letter Svidrigailov had forced her to write, Marfa Petrovna undertook a campaign of public vindication, "going round to every house in the town... showing Dounia's letter" (003-207–003-214), before dying suddenly — a death that leaves ambiguous the question of Svidrigailov's culpability.

## Background

Marfa Petrovna belonged to the provincial gentry, with an estate in the —sky district. She was substantially older than Svidrigailov and financially dominant in their marriage. Her will left Dounia three thousand roubles as reparation, a bequest that shapes the novel's marriage plot by giving Dounia the financial independence to resist [[pyotr-luzhin|Luzhin]]'s demands. Pulcheria Alexandrovna's letter describes her as impetuous, theatrical, and capable of both "flagrantly unjust" cruelty and "fine and generous" restitution.

## Role in the Novel

Marfa Petrovna's influence operates entirely through absence and memory. Her letter of vindication for Dounia, her death, and her will are all reported in Pulcheria's letter in Part 1 — events that occurred before the novel's present action. She reappears as a ghostly presence in Svidrigailov's narration: he claims that "Marfa Petrovna visits me... three times" as an apparition (024-116–024-130), a confession that links her death to his guilt and foreshadows his eventual [[svidrigailovs-suicide|suicide]]. Her bequest to Dounia also provides the financial basis for Dounia's refusal of Luzhin and her eventual marriage to Razumihin, making Marfa Petrovna an unseen but structurally decisive benefactor.

## Key Relationships

- **[[arkady-svidrigailov|Arkady Svidrigailov]]**: Her husband, whose financial and moral debts to her form the backstory of his corruption. He may have caused her death — "he gave her a beating, it seems" — and her ghost haunts him.
- **[[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia Raskolnikov]]**: Her governess, first slandered and then vindicated. Marfa Petrovna's bequest to Dounia is a material act of reparation that shapes the novel's plot.

## Psychological Profile

Marfa Petrovna's psychology, reconstructed from secondhand reports, reveals a woman of extreme emotional oscillation: capable of vicious public slander and elaborate public apology, of possessive jealousy and posthumous generosity. Her relationship with Svidrigailov — she "ransomed" him, she controlled him financially, and she may have been killed by him — suggests a marriage founded on power and dependence rather than affection. Her ghostly visitations to Svidrigailov suggest either his guilty conscience or, in the novel's supernatural register, an unresolved claim of justice from beyond the grave.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Marfa Petrovna embodies the theme of reparation — the possibility and limits of making amends for harm done. Her public vindication of Dounia and her three-thousand-rouble bequest are genuine acts of restitution, but they cannot undo the original slander or the exposure to Svidrigailov's predation. Her ghost, visiting Svidrigailov three times, represents the return of the repressed — the crimes that cannot be buried, the victims who will not stay dead. In this sense she is a female counterpart to the murdered pawnbroker in Svidrigailov's psyche, a figure of [[guilt|guilt]] that demands acknowledgment.
