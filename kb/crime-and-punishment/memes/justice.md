# Justice

The novel poses a persistent question: what is justice, and where can it be found? Raskolnikov's theory claims that extraordinary men create their own justice. Porfiry represents the reformed Russian legal system. Katerina Ivanovna, dying in the street, cries out: "Is there no justice on earth?" (029-472). The novel's answer is complex — legal justice is necessary but insufficient; true justice requires conscience, confession, and suffering.

## Definition

Justice operates on three levels in the novel: (1) the utilitarian "justice" of Raskolnikov's theory that killing a "louse" serves the greater good (006-117); (2) legal justice, represented by Porfiry's investigation and the eventual trial; and (3) moral or divine justice, which Porfiry invokes: "There is justice. You must fulfil the demands of justice" (033-398–033-399).

## Expression in the Novel

The student in the tavern argues for the "justice" of killing the pawnbroker: "I was only arguing the justice of it" (006-117), to which the officer replies: "If you do not do it yourself, there's no justice about it" (006-119) — justice requires action, not just theory. Katerina Ivanovna's death scene is the novel's most visceral cry for justice: "Is there no justice on earth? There is law and justice on earth, there is, I will find justice!" (029-469–029-472). She is told "she had run off somewhere 'to seek justice'" (030-041) — the irony being that she dies before finding any. Porfiry's final appeal to Raskolnikov invokes justice as moral imperative: "There is justice. You must fulfil the demands of justice. I know that you don't believe in it, but life will bring you to it" (033-398–033-399).

## Key Passages

> "I was only arguing the justice of it.... It's nothing to do with me." (006-117)

> "Is there no justice on earth? There is law and justice on earth, there is, I will find justice on earth!" (029-469–029-472)

> "There is justice. You must fulfil the demands of justice. I know that you don't believe in it, but life will bring you to it." (033-398–033-399)

## Character Perspectives

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: The novel's most complex voice on justice — he represents the law but recognizes its limits. His prediction that Raskolnikov will confess is based not on evidence but on the psychology of conscience: justice as internal necessity.

- **Katerina Ivanovna**: Her demand for "justice on earth" is the cry of the humiliated. Her death embodies the gap between the demand for justice and its earthly absence.
