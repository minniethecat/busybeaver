# Murder of the Pawnbroker

The murder of [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] and her half-sister [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] occurs in Part 1, Chapter 7 — the novel's structural and moral axis. Everything before leads to it; everything after follows from it. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] kills the pawnbroker with an axe, then kills [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] when she unexpectedly returns, in what he had planned as a rational act — a test of his [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary-man theory]] — and what becomes, instantly, an irrevocable catastrophe that inaugurates his journey toward [[confession-and-redemption|confession and redemption]].

## The Act

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] enters the pawnbroker's apartment in [[st-petersburg]] with a fake pledge wrapped in paper, inside which is concealed an axe. He strikes [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] on the head from behind with the blunt side, then again as she falls. As he searches her body for keys and valuables, [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] returns unexpectedly. He kills her too — "the blow fell on the skull with the sharp edge" (007-100). The second murder is unplanned, destroying the [[utilitarian-rationalism|utilitarian calculus]] that justified the first: [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] was the innocent one, the meek one whom [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] had no theoretical reason to harm.

## The Victims

- **[[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]]**: The pawnbroker — "a diminutive, withered old woman of sixty, with sharp malignant eyes and a sharp little nose" (001-022). She is portrayed as exploitative, miserly, and cruel to her half-sister [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s theoretical justification for killing her rests on her supposed uselessness and harmfulness: "a useless, loathsome, harmful louse."

- **[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]]**: Alyona's half-sister — "tall, clumsy, timid, and meek, almost an idiot" (001-137). She is pregnant at the time of her murder. Her death is the novel's starkest refutation of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s theory: an innocent killed not by calculation but by the uncontrollable consequences of the initial act. [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] was a friend of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s — a connection that tightens the moral web around [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]].

## Aftermath

The murder scene is followed by [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s panicked flight, his near-discovery by [[koch|Koch]] and [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]] knocking at the door, his narrow escape, and the beginning of his psychological disintegration — fever, delirium, and the progressive breakdown of his capacity to function. The murder weapon, the axe, is returned to its place in the porter's shed, but the act cannot be undone. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s subsequent behavior — fainting at the [[police-station]] when the murder is mentioned, his compulsive return to the murder scene, his inability to dispose of the stolen goods — is the novel's demonstration that crime carries its own punishment within the criminal's psyche.
