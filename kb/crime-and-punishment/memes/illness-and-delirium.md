# Illness and Delirium

Physical illness pervades *Crime and Punishment* not as incidental detail but as structural motif. Raskolnikov moves through the novel in a state of fever, delirium, and collapse that is neither purely somatic nor purely psychological — his body enacts the crisis his mind cannot fully face. Other characters, too — Katerina Ivanovna's consumption, Pulcheria Alexandrovna's decline, Marmeladov's alcoholism — wear their spiritual conditions as physical disease.

## Raskolnikov's Fever as Moral Symptom

From the novel's opening pages, Raskolnikov is described in terms of nervous exhaustion and incipient illness (001-001). The oppressive [[heat-and-atmosphere|July heat]] amplifies his condition: "in spite of the heat he felt cold" (005-029), a paradoxical somatic response that signals the onset of serious nervous disorder. After the murder, his condition intensifies into full delirium: "He lost consciousness" (008-057). He lies in a fever for days, tended by [[nastasya|Nastasya]] and [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]], surfacing only to make incriminating remarks that tighten [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s suspicions.

The fever is not merely medical — it is the body's response to a moral wound the mind has refused to register. When Raskolnikov asks, "Did I say anything in delirium?" (010-282), the question carries existential terror: the fever may have betrayed what consciousness concealed. The illness functions as an involuntary [[confession-and-redemption|confession]] mechanism — what the will refuses to admit, the body declares.

Dostoevsky's own epilepsy and his familiarity with nervous disorders inform the depiction. The illness is both realistic (contemporary medical discourse recognized "brain fever" and nervous collapse) and symbolic — Raskolnikov's body revolts against what his intellect has authorized. His physical recovery parallels his moral awakening, but the two trajectories are not synchronized: he can be physically well and spiritually dead, or feverish and closer to truth.

## Illness as Social Condition

The motif extends beyond Raskolnikov. Katerina Ivanovna's tuberculosis is inseparable from her poverty and wounded pride — "driven to distraction by her illness and the crying of the hungry children" (002-260). Her death in the street, raving and coughing blood ([[katerina-ivanovnas-death|Katerina Ivanovna's Death]]), is the novel's most visceral fusion of social degradation and physical collapse. Pulcheria Alexandrovna's decline into "a sort of fever" (003-342) upon learning of her son's crime literalizes maternal anguish — her body cannot survive the knowledge of what her son has become. Illness in the novel is never merely biological; it is the language the body speaks when social and moral conditions become unbearable.

## The Fever-Dream Continuum

Raskolnikov's delirium blurs into the novel's dream sequences. The [[raskolnikovs-dream-of-the-beaten-horse|dream of the beaten horse]] (Part 1, Chapter 5) occurs while Raskolnikov lies down in a "state of feverish agitation" before the murder. His post-murder delirium generates paranoid visions: he imagines [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] winking at him, hears mocking laughter, and cannot distinguish waking perception from fever-induced hallucination. This blurring of illness and dream is thematically precise — both are states in which the unconscious speaks, bypassing the rationalizing intellect that constructed the [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary man theory]].

## Key Passages

> "He lost consciousness" — the narrator on Raskolnikov's post-murder collapse (008-057)

> "Did I say anything in delirium?" — Raskolnikov, terrified his fever has betrayed him (010-282)

> "Driven to distraction by her illness and the crying of the hungry children" — Katerina Ivanovna's consumption as social-moral symptom (002-260)

> "In spite of the heat he felt cold" — Raskolnikov's paradoxical somatic state before the murder (005-029)
