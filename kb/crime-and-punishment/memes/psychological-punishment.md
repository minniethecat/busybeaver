# Psychological Punishment

Dostoevsky's most original contribution to the crime narrative is the idea that punishment is primarily psychological. Raskolnikov is never in danger of being caught by the police — Porfiry lacks evidence throughout — but he is tormented from the moment of the murder by fever, delirium, and the unbearable pressure of his own conscience. The novel's title, *Crime and Punishment*, refers not to two sequential events but to a single experience: the crime contains its punishment from the first moment.

## Definition

Psychological punishment operates through what Dostoevsky calls "conscience" — not merely guilt as an emotion, but conscience as an active, tormenting force. Before the murder, Raskolnikov asks: "Has she a secret conscience-prick at sacrificing her daughter?" (004-038), already framing conscience as a hidden tribunal. The student in the tavern dismisses conscience: "duty, conscience — I don't want to say anything against conscience" (006-110), but the novel insists that conscience cannot be dismissed. After the murder, Raskolnikov is "tormented by a" fever and dread (001-250), "worried and tormented himself trying to remember" (010-015). His mother observes that "his face showed intense suffering" (014-356), and he has "undergone some terrible physical suffering" (017-009) — physical manifestations of a psychological state.

## Expression in the Novel

The novel traces the arc of psychological punishment through Raskolnikov's physical and mental deterioration. His illness after the murder is not incidental — it is the body's expression of what the mind refuses to acknowledge. Porfiry understands this perfectly: he never presents hard evidence because he knows Raskolnikov's own conscience will drive him to confess. His method is to "torment" Raskolnikov psychologically (012-356), applying pressure that accelerates the internal process already underway.

The structural genius of the novel is that the legal punishment — Siberian exile — occupies only the Epilogue, and even there it is secondary to the psychological and spiritual transformation. The real punishment has already happened by the time Raskolnikov confesses.

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His body betrays what his mind denies. Fever, fainting, and nightmares externalize the torment of a conscience he refuses to acknowledge.

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: Represents the externalization of psychological punishment — he mirrors and amplifies Raskolnikov's internal torment, saying "I am not afraid of you" (012-356) because he knows the real judge is within.
