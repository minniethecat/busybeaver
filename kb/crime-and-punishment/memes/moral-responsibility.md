# Moral Responsibility

Moral responsibility in *Crime and Punishment* is the central ethical question that the novel's plot is designed to test: can an individual overstep moral law through sheer force of intellect, or is conscience an inescapable feature of human nature that will reassert itself regardless of rational justification? The novel's answer — dramatized through Raskolnikov's physical and psychological collapse — is that moral responsibility cannot be argued away, because it operates at a level deeper than intellect.

## Definition

Moral responsibility, as Dostoevsky frames it, is not a matter of legal guilt but of conscience — the internal tribunal that operates independently of external judgment. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] articulates this when he asks of the hypothetical murderer: "If he has a conscience he will suffer for his mistake" (019-552). The concept is tied to suffering: moral responsibility manifests not as a decision but as an experience, one that Raskolnikov cannot escape despite his intellectual conviction that his crime was justified (019-428).

Crucially, Dostoevsky distinguishes between two registers of responsibility. The first is the legal-juridical register — whether an act violates the criminal code and deserves punishment by the state. The second, and more important, is the moral-spiritual register — whether an act violates the law of conscience and requires expiation through suffering. Raskolnikov evades legal responsibility for most of the novel (Porfiry cannot arrest him without evidence), but he cannot evade moral responsibility for a single moment after the axe falls. This doubling of responsibility — the gap between what the law can prove and what the soul knows — is the engine of the novel's suspense.

## Expression in the Novel

### The Article and Its Flaw

The novel traces Raskolnikov's failed attempt to exempt himself from moral responsibility through philosophical argument. His article on crime advances the theory that "extraordinary" individuals have "the right to decide in his own conscience to overstep certain obstacles" (019-383), seeking "within himself, in his conscience, a sanction for wading through blood" (019-428). The theory posits a sovereign conscience — one that grants itself permission rather than submitting to external moral law.

Yet [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] immediately identifies the fatal flaw: committing murder "in the name of conscience" is "more terrible than if it were done with official permission" (019-520). Why? Because when one acts "in the name of conscience," one corrupts the very faculty that would otherwise recognize the crime as wrong. The conscience, rather than serving as a check on action, becomes its enabler — and once conscience is co-opted by theory, there is nothing left to sound the alarm. Raskolnikov's subsequent illness and delirium are the body's protest against what the intellect has sanctioned.

### The Tavern Student and Unwitting Prophecy

Raskolnikov's student interlocutor in the tavern unwittingly frames the question that the entire novel will answer: "conscience — I don't want to say anything against conscience" (006-111). The student's hesitancy about conscience is precisely Raskolnikov's error: he treats conscience as an optional consideration that can be bracketed for the sake of argument. The novel demonstrates that conscience cannot be silenced by argument, that it returns with the force of physiological illness and psychological disintegration.

### Sonia and the Alternative Model

[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] represents the alternative — moral responsibility accepted through confession and suffering rather than evaded through theory. Where Raskolnikov tries to reason his way out of guilt, Sonia accepts her situation without rationalization and bears its weight. Her reading of the Lazarus story (004-123) offers the novel's theological counterpoint to Raskolnikov's philosophical argument: resurrection — whether of Lazarus or of a murderer's soul — requires not intellectual assent but the acknowledgment of death and the willingness to be called forth.

## Conscience as Embodied Knowledge

One of the novel's most distinctive contributions to the philosophy of moral responsibility is its insistence that conscience is embodied. Raskolnikov does not decide to feel guilty; his body decides for him — through fever, fainting fits, and the compulsion to return to the scene of the crime. The body remembers what the intellect denies. This is why Porfiry is so confident that Raskolnikov will eventually confess: "He will come of himself" (019-552), because the pressure of conscience, operating through the body, is stronger than the will to conceal.
