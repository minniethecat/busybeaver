# Raskolnikov Receives His Mother's Letter

Early in Part 1 of *Crime and Punishment*, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] — already sunk in poverty, isolation, and obsessive thought about the murder he has not yet committed — receives a long, thick letter from his mother, [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]]. The letter, arriving after months of silence, delivers a cascade of devastating news: his sister [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s engagement to the wealthy but repellent [[pyotr-luzhin|Pyotr Luzhin]], the [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] scandal that nearly destroyed [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s reputation, and the family's plan to move to [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]]. Far from comforting [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], the letter ignites a rage that pushes him closer to the act he has been contemplating.

## The Arrival

The letter arrives at [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s garret on a day when he has been drifting through [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]] in a state of feverish dissociation. The servant [[nastasya|Nastasya]] brings it to him:

> A minute later the letter was brought him. That was it: from his mother, from the province of R——. He turned pale as he took it. It was a long while since he had received a letter, but another feeling also suddenly clutched at his heart. [003-045]

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] waits until he is alone, then opens the letter with trembling hands. It is thick — "weighing over two ounces, two large sheets covered with small handwriting" — and carries the familiar scent of home. He kisses it before reading.

## The Letter's Contents

[[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]]'s letter is a masterpiece of maternal anxiety disguised as cheerful reportage. She begins with apologies for the long silence, explains that [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] has been working as a governess for the [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] family, and then — reluctantly, with many digressions — reveals the central news: [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] is engaged to marry Pyotr Petrovich Luzhin, a man of forty-five, a lawyer with "a government post" and "capital."

The letter betrays, between its lines, the desperate calculus behind the engagement. Luzhin is not a romantic match; he is a financial and social lifeline. Pulcheria writes that Luzhin "shares my opinion that [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] will be far happier with him than in her present situation" — a formulation that reveals more than it intends about the family's circumstances.

Worse still, the letter recounts the [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] scandal: [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]], [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s wife, caught her husband pursuing [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] and, in a fit of jealous rage, publicly slandered [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] throughout the provincial town. Only the discovery of a letter from [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] herself — rebuffing [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] with "great heat and indignation" — restored [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s reputation. But the damage, and the humiliation, linger on every page.

## Raskolnikov's Reaction

The letter produces an effect opposite to what Pulcheria intended. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] does not see a family plan for survival; he sees his sister being sold — "sacrificed," in his word — to a man he instantly despises. The parallel between [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s situation and [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]]'s — both women driven to self-sacrifice by economic desperation — strikes him with particular force:

> "Sonia! Sonia Marmeladov! Eternal Sonia, while the world lasts! But have you measured the sacrifice, the sacrifice, both of you, fully? Is it so? Can it be borne? Is it any use? Is it wise?" [003-090]

The letter does not deter [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] from his murder plan; it accelerates it. His rage at Luzhin, his guilt at his own helplessness, and his terror of watching his family degrade themselves for his sake all feed the feverish logic that drives him toward the pawnbroker's door. The murder, in this reading, is not merely an intellectual experiment but a frantic, misdirected attempt to rescue his family by force.

## Narrative Function

The mother's letter serves as the novel's primary inciting incident. Before the letter, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is contemplating murder in the abstract — a "casuistry" of the extraordinary man theory. After the letter, the abstract acquires urgency: there is a specific villain (Luzhin), a specific victim to be saved ([[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]), and a specific crime that could fund the rescue (the pawnbroker's murder). The psychological machinery of the novel is set in motion by this thick, tear-stained, desperately cheerful communication from the provinces.
