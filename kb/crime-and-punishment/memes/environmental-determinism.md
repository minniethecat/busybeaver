# Environmental Determinism

**Environmental determinism** is the sociological doctrine that criminal behavior results from social conditions — poverty, inequality, and corrupting environments — rather than from individual moral choice. In Dostoevsky's *Crime and Punishment*, the theory serves as a foil: the novel systematically tests and ultimately rejects the claim that "environment accounts for everything."

## The Socialist Argument

The doctrine is articulated most clearly in a conversation between [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] and [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]], where Razumihin parodies the socialist position: crime is entirely explained by "the influence of environment, and nothing else" (019-268). The socialists, he notes, argue that a "normal social organisation" would eliminate crime at its root by removing its environmental causes.

> "Crime is a protest against the abnormality of the social organisation and nothing more." (019-261)

## Dostoevsky's Critique

Dostoevsky uses the novel to challenge this determinism. Razumihin objects: "No, brother, you are wrong, environment accounts for a great deal, but not everything" (019-298). The character of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] embodies the counter-argument — his crime is driven not by poverty alone but by intellectual pride, [[rational-egoism|rational egoism]], and a philosophical theory that divides humanity into ordinary and extraordinary.

The novel's psychological depth undermines the simplistic environmental model by demonstrating that even in identical material conditions, moral choice remains operative. Raskolnikov's eventual confession and [[spiritual-resurrection|spiritual regeneration]] affirm the Dostoevskian principle that conscience transcends environment.
