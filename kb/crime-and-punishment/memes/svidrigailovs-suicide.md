# Svidrigailov's Suicide

After [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s absolute rejection — she shoots at him and misses, then drops the revolver — [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] spends his final night wandering [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]]. He visits a hotel, endures a night of nightmares (the mouse, the drowned girl, the five-year-old seductress), and at dawn walks out into the rain. He finds a watchman near a fire tower, tells him "I am going to America" (039-280), and shoots himself.

## The Last Night

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s final dreams are Dostoevsky's most concentrated exploration of guilt. Each dream peels back another layer: the dead girl who drowned herself after he abused her, the child who transforms before his eyes into a knowing seductress. These are not dreams of repentance but of exhausted recognition — he sees what he is and can no longer sustain it.

## America as Death

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s parting words — "I am going to America" — are Dostoevsky's bitter joke: America is not a place of new beginnings but a euphemism for suicide. The watchman, a military veteran, understands and is horrified. The scene is the novel's structural counterpoint to [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s confession: one chooses death, the other chooses suffering and the possibility of rebirth.
