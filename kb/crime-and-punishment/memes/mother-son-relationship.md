# Mother-Son Relationship

The relationship between Raskolnikov and his mother Pulcheria Alexandrovna forms one of the novel's deepest emotional undercurrents — a bond of unconditional maternal love that becomes unbearable to Raskolnikov after his crime.

## Definition

The mother-son relationship in *Crime and Punishment* is characterized by Pulcheria's devoted, almost worshipful love for her son and Raskolnikov's growing inability to receive that love after the murder. Dostoevsky uses this relationship to explore how crime severs not only the criminal's connection to society but also his most intimate human bonds. The mother's letter — a document of love, sacrifice, and impending family crisis — becomes the catalyst for Raskolnikov's final decision to act (003-102).

## Expression in the Novel

Pulcheria's letter to Raskolnikov (003-102 through 003-418) is the novel's central document of maternal love. In it, she reveals Dounia's sacrifice in agreeing to marry Luzhin, her own financial struggles, and her unwavering faith in Raskolnikov's future greatness. The letter tortures Raskolnikov (004-001) — not because of its content alone, but because hearing of his mother's love and sacrifice makes his planned murder unimaginable, forcing him toward his fateful resolution: "No, mother, no, Dounia, you won't deceive me" (004-007). After the murder, Raskolnikov physically recoils from his mother's embrace (015-019), unable to bear the love of someone whose moral world he has violated.

## Key Passages

> "My dear Rodya," wrote his mother — "it's two months since I last talked to you by letter, a fact which has distressed me and kept me awake at nights" (003-102)

> His mother's letter had been a torture to him (004-001)

> "How, how can I thank you!" Pulcheria Alexandrovna was beginning, overcome by the joy of seeing her son after three years (015-019)

## Character Perspectives

- **Pulcheria Alexandrovna**: Her love for Raskolnikov is absolute and blind — she reveres him as a future scholar and great man, remaining ignorant of his crime until her death. Her maternal devotion is so pure that Raskolnikov cannot face it after the murder
- **Raskolnikov**: For him, his mother represents the moral world he is leaving behind. Receiving her letter fills him not with comfort but with torment (004-001), and after the murder he treats her coldly — not from lack of love but from the impossibility of being loved by someone whose moral universe he has shattered

## Historical & Philosophical Context

The mother-son bond in 19th-century Russian literature — from Gogol to Turgenev — often serves as a moral touchstone. Dostoevsky intensifies this tradition by making Pulcheria's love the emotional counterweight to Raskolnikov's theory: her love is everything his rational egoism cannot account for. Her eventual death, caused by grief over his absence, completes the tragic arc — the crime's collateral damage extends to the most innocent.
