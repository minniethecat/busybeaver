[002-001] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] was not used to crowds, and, as we said before, he avoided

[002-002] society of every sort, more especially of late. But now all at once he

[002-003] felt a desire to be with other people. Something new seemed to be taking

[002-004] place within him, and with it he felt a sort of thirst for company. He

[002-005] was so weary after a whole month of concentrated wretchedness and gloomy

[002-006] excitement that he longed to rest, if only for a moment, in some other

[002-007] world, whatever it might be; and, in spite of the filthiness of the

[002-008] surroundings, he was glad now to stay in the tavern.

[002-009] The master of the establishment was in another room, but he frequently

[002-010] came down some steps into the main room, his jaunty, tarred boots with

[002-011] red turn-over tops coming into view each time before the rest of his

[002-012] person. He wore a full coat and a horribly greasy black satin waistcoat,

[002-013] with no cravat, and his whole face seemed smeared with oil like an

[002-014] iron lock. At the counter stood a boy of about fourteen, and there was

[002-015] another boy somewhat younger who handed whatever was wanted. On the

[002-016] counter lay some sliced cucumber, some pieces of dried black bread, and

[002-017] some fish, chopped up small, all smelling very bad. It was insufferably

[002-018] close, and so heavy with the fumes of spirits that five minutes in such

[002-019] an atmosphere might well make a man drunk.

[002-020] There are [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] meetings with strangers that interest us from the

[002-021] first moment, before a word is spoken. Such was the impression made on

[002-022] Raskolnikov by the person sitting a little distance from him, who looked

[002-023] like a retired clerk. The young man often recalled this impression

[002-024] afterwards, and even ascribed it to presentiment. He looked repeatedly

[002-025] at the clerk, partly no doubt because the latter was staring

[002-026] persistently at him, obviously anxious to enter into conversation. At

[002-027] the other persons in the room, including the tavern-keeper, the clerk

[002-028] looked as though he were used to their company, and weary of it, showing

[002-029] a shade of condescending contempt for them as persons of station and

[002-030] culture inferior to his own, with whom it would be useless for him to

[002-031] converse. He was a man over fifty, bald and grizzled, of medium height,

[002-032] and stoutly built. His face, bloated from continual drinking, was of

[002-033] a yellow, even greenish, tinge, with swollen eyelids out of which keen

[002-034] reddish eyes gleamed like little chinks. But there was something very

[002-035] strange in him; there was a light in his eyes as though of intense

[002-036] feeling--perhaps there were even thought and intelligence, but at the

[002-037] same time there was a gleam of something like madness. He was wearing an

[002-038] old and hopelessly ragged black dress coat, with all its buttons missing

[002-039] except one, and that one he had buttoned, evidently clinging to this

[002-040] last trace of respectability. A crumpled shirt front, covered with spots

[002-041] and stains, protruded from his canvas waistcoat. Like a clerk, he wore

[002-042] no beard, nor moustache, but had been so long unshaven that his chin

[002-043] looked like a stiff greyish brush. And there was something respectable

[002-044] and like an official about his manner too. But he was restless; he

[002-045] ruffled up his hair and from time to time let his head drop into his

[002-046] hands dejectedly resting his ragged elbows on the stained and sticky

[002-047] table. At last he looked straight at Raskolnikov, and said loudly and

[002-048] resolutely:

[002-049] “May I venture, honoured sir, to engage you in polite conversation?

[002-050] Forasmuch as, though your exterior would not command respect, my

[002-051] experience admonishes me that you are a man of education and not

[002-052] accustomed to drinking. I have always respected education when in

[002-053] conjunction with genuine sentiments, and I am besides a titular

[002-054] counsellor in rank. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]--such is my name; titular counsellor. I

[002-055] make bold to inquire--have you been in the service?”

[002-056] “No, I am studying,” answered the young man, somewhat surprised at

[002-057] the grandiloquent style of the speaker and also at being so directly

[002-058] addressed. In spite of the momentary desire he had just been feeling for

[002-059] company of any sort, on being actually spoken to he felt immediately his

[002-060] habitual irritable and uneasy aversion for any stranger who approached

[002-061] or attempted to approach him.

[002-062] “A student then, or formerly a student,” cried the clerk. “Just what

[002-063] I thought! I’m a man of experience, immense experience, sir,” and he

[002-064] tapped his forehead with his fingers in self-approval. “You’ve been a

[002-065] student or have attended some learned institution!... But allow me....”

[002-066] He got up, staggered, took up his jug and glass, and sat down beside

[002-067] the young man, facing him a little sideways. He was drunk, but spoke

[002-068] fluently and boldly, only occasionally losing the thread of his

[002-069] sentences and drawling his words. He pounced upon Raskolnikov as

[002-070] greedily as though he too had not spoken to a soul for a month.

[002-071] “Honoured sir,” he began almost with solemnity, “poverty is not a vice,

[002-072] that’s a true saying. Yet I know too that drunkenness is not a virtue,

[002-073] and that that’s even truer. But beggary, honoured sir, beggary is a

[002-074] vice. In poverty you may still retain your innate nobility of soul, but

[002-075] in beggary--never--no one. For beggary a man is not chased out of human

[002-076] society with a stick, he is swept out with a broom, so as to make it as

[002-077] humiliating as possible; and quite right, too, forasmuch as in beggary

[002-078] I am ready to be the first to humiliate myself. Hence the pot-house!

[002-079] Honoured sir, a month ago Mr. Lebeziatnikov gave my wife a beating, and

[002-080] my wife is a very different matter from me! Do you understand? Allow me

[002-081] to ask you another question out of simple curiosity: have you ever spent

[002-082] a night on a hay barge, on [[neva-river|the Neva]]?”

[002-083] “No, I have not happened to,” answered Raskolnikov. “What do you mean?”

[002-084] “Well, I’ve just come from one and it’s the fifth night I’ve slept

[002-085] so....” He filled his glass, emptied it and paused. Bits of hay were in

[002-086] fact clinging to his clothes and sticking to his hair. It seemed quite

[002-087] probable that he had not undressed or washed for the last five days.

[002-088] His hands, particularly, were filthy. They were fat and red, with black

[002-089] nails.

[002-090] His conversation seemed to excite a general though languid interest. The

[002-091] boys at the counter fell to sniggering. The innkeeper came down from the

[002-092] upper room, apparently on purpose to listen to the “funny fellow”

[002-093] and sat down at a little distance, yawning lazily, but with dignity.

[002-094] Evidently Marmeladov was a familiar figure here, and he had most

[002-095] likely acquired his weakness for high-flown speeches from the habit of

[002-096] frequently entering into conversation with strangers of all sorts in

[002-097] the tavern. This habit develops into a necessity in some drunkards, and

[002-098] especially in those who are looked after sharply and kept in order

[002-099] at home. Hence in the company of other drinkers they try to justify

[002-100] themselves and even if possible obtain consideration.

[002-101] “Funny fellow!” pronounced the innkeeper. “And why don’t you work, why

[002-102] aren’t you at your duty, if you are in the service?”

[002-103] “Why am I not at my duty, honoured sir,” Marmeladov went on, addressing

[002-104] himself exclusively to Raskolnikov, as though it had been he who put

[002-105] that question to him. “Why am I not at my duty? Does not my heart ache

[002-106] to think what a useless worm I am? A month ago when Mr. Lebeziatnikov

[002-107] beat my wife with his own hands, and I lay drunk, didn’t I suffer?

[002-108] Excuse me, young man, has it ever happened to you... hm... well, to

[002-109] petition hopelessly for a loan?”

[002-110] “Yes, it has. But what do you mean by hopelessly?”

[002-111] “Hopelessly in the fullest sense, when you know beforehand that you

[002-112] will get nothing by it. You know, for instance, beforehand with positive

[002-113] certainty that this man, this most reputable and exemplary citizen, will

[002-114] on no consideration give you money; and indeed I ask you why should he?

[002-115] For he knows of course that I shan’t pay it back. From [[pity-and-compassion|compassion]]? But

[002-116] Mr. Lebeziatnikov who keeps up with modern ideas explained the other day

[002-117] that compassion is forbidden nowadays by science itself, and that that’s

[002-118] what is done now in England, where there is political economy. Why, I

[002-119] ask you, should he give it to me? And yet though I know beforehand that

[002-120] he won’t, I set off to him and...”

[002-121] “Why do you go?” put in Raskolnikov.

[002-122] “Well, when one has no one, nowhere else one can go! For every man must

[002-123] have somewhere to go. Since there are times when one absolutely must

[002-124] go somewhere! When my own daughter first went out with a yellow ticket,

[002-125] then I had to go... (for my daughter has a yellow passport),” he added

[002-126] in parenthesis, looking with a certain uneasiness at the young man.

[002-127] “No matter, sir, no matter!” he went on hurriedly and with apparent

[002-128] composure when both the boys at the counter guffawed and even the

[002-129] innkeeper smiled--“No matter, I am not confounded by the wagging of

[002-130] their heads; for everyone knows everything about it already, and all

[002-131] that is secret is made open. And I accept it all, not with contempt, but

[002-132] with humility. So be it! So be it! ‘Behold the man!’ Excuse me, young

[002-133] man, can you.... No, to put it more strongly and more distinctly; not

[002-134] _can_ you but _dare_ you, looking upon me, assert that I am not a pig?”

[002-135] The young man did not answer a word.

[002-136] “Well,” the orator began again stolidly and with even increased dignity,

[002-137] after waiting for the laughter in the room to subside. “Well, so be

[002-138] it, I am a pig, but she is a lady! I have the semblance of a beast, but

[002-139] [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]], my spouse, is a person of education and an officer’s

[002-140] daughter. Granted, granted, I am a scoundrel, but she is a woman of a

[002-141] noble heart, full of sentiments, refined by education. And yet... oh,

[002-142] if only she felt for me! Honoured sir, honoured sir, you know every man

[002-143] ought to have at least one place where people feel for him! But Katerina

[002-144] Ivanovna, though she is magnanimous, she is unjust.... And yet, although

[002-145] I realise that when she pulls my hair she only does it out of pity--for

[002-146] I repeat without being ashamed, she pulls my hair, young man,” he

[002-147] declared with redoubled dignity, hearing the sniggering again--“but, my

[002-148] God, if she would but once.... But no, no! It’s all in vain and it’s no

[002-149] use talking! No use talking! For more than once, my wish did come true

[002-150] and more than once she has felt for me but... such is my fate and I am a

[002-151] beast by nature!”

[002-152] “Rather!” assented the innkeeper yawning. Marmeladov struck his fist

[002-153] resolutely on the table.

[002-154] “Such is my fate! Do you know, sir, do you know, I have sold her very

[002-155] stockings for drink? Not her shoes--that would be more or less in the

[002-156] order of things, but her stockings, her stockings I have sold for drink!

[002-157] Her mohair shawl I sold for drink, a present to her long ago, her own

[002-158] property, not mine; and we live in a cold room and she caught cold this

[002-159] winter and has begun coughing and spitting blood too. We have three

[002-160] little children and Katerina Ivanovna is at work from morning till

[002-161] night; she is scrubbing and cleaning and washing the children, for she’s

[002-162] been used to cleanliness from a child. But her chest is weak and she has

[002-163] a tendency to consumption and I feel it! Do you suppose I don’t feel it?

[002-164] And the more I drink the more I feel it. That’s why I drink too. I try

[002-165] to find sympathy and feeling in drink.... I drink so that I may suffer

[002-166] twice as much!” And as though in despair he laid his head down on the

[002-167] table.

[002-168] “Young man,” he went on, raising his head again, “in your face I seem to

[002-169] read some trouble of mind. When you came in I read it, and that was why

[002-170] I addressed you at once. For in unfolding to you the story of my life, I

[002-171] do not wish to make myself a laughing-stock before these idle listeners,

[002-172] who indeed know all about it already, but I am looking for a man

[002-173] of feeling and education. Know then that my wife was educated in a

[002-174] high-class school for the daughters of noblemen, and on leaving she

[002-175] danced the shawl dance before the governor and other personages for

[002-176] which she was presented with a gold medal and a certificate of merit.

[002-177] The medal... well, the medal of course was sold--long ago, hm... but the

[002-178] certificate of merit is in her trunk still and not long ago she showed

[002-179] it to our landlady. And although she is most continually on bad terms

[002-180] with [[praskovya-pavlovna|the landlady]], yet she wanted to tell someone or other of her past

[002-181] honours and of the happy days that are gone. I don’t condemn her for

[002-182] it, I don’t blame her, for the one thing left her is recollection of

[002-183] the past, and all the rest is dust and ashes. Yes, yes, she is a lady

[002-184] of spirit, proud and determined. She scrubs the floors herself and has

[002-185] nothing but black bread to eat, but won’t allow herself to be treated

[002-186] with disrespect. That’s why she would not overlook Mr. Lebeziatnikov’s

[002-187] rudeness to her, and so when he gave her a beating for it, she took to

[002-188] her bed more from the hurt to her feelings than from the blows. She was

[002-189] a widow when I married her, with three children, one smaller than the

[002-190] other. She married her first husband, an infantry officer, for love, and

[002-191] ran away with him from her father’s house. She was exceedingly fond of

[002-192] her husband; but he gave way to cards, got into trouble and with that he

[002-193] died. He used to beat her at the end: and although she paid him back, of

[002-194] which I have authentic documentary evidence, to this day she speaks of

[002-195] him with tears and she throws him up to me; and I am glad, I am glad

[002-196] that, though only in imagination, she should think of herself as having

[002-197] once been happy.... And she was left at his death with three children in

[002-198] a wild and remote district where I happened to be at the time; and she

[002-199] was left in such hopeless poverty that, although I have seen many ups

[002-200] and downs of all sort, I don’t feel equal to describing it even. Her

[002-201] relations had all thrown her off. And she was proud, too, excessively

[002-202] proud.... And then, honoured sir, and then, I, being at the time a

[002-203] widower, with a daughter of fourteen left me by my first wife, offered

[002-204] her my hand, for I could not bear the sight of such suffering. You can

[002-205] judge the extremity of her calamities, that she, a woman of education

[002-206] and culture and distinguished family, should have consented to be my

[002-207] wife. But she did! Weeping and sobbing and wringing her hands, she

[002-208] married me! For she had nowhere to turn! Do you understand, sir, do you

[002-209] understand what it means when you have absolutely nowhere to turn? No,

[002-210] that you don’t understand yet.... And for a whole year, I performed

[002-211] my duties conscientiously and faithfully, and did not touch this” (he

[002-212] tapped the jug with his finger), “for I have feelings. But even so, I

[002-213] could not please her; and then I lost my place too, and that through no

[002-214] fault of mine but through changes in the office; and then I did touch

[002-215] it!... It will be a year and a half ago soon since we found ourselves at

[002-216] last after many wanderings and numerous calamities in this magnificent

[002-217] capital, adorned with innumerable monuments. Here I obtained a

[002-218] situation.... I obtained it and I lost it again. Do you understand? This

[002-219] time it was through my own fault I lost it: for my weakness had come

[002-220] out.... We have now part of a room at [[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Fyodorovna]] Lippevechsel’s;

[002-221] and what we live upon and what we pay our rent with, I could not say.

[002-222] There are a lot of people living there besides ourselves. Dirt and

[002-223] disorder, a perfect Bedlam... hm... yes... And meanwhile my daughter by

[002-224] my first wife has grown up; and what my daughter has had to put up with

[002-225] from her step-mother whilst she was growing up, I won’t speak of. For,

[002-226] though Katerina Ivanovna is full of generous feelings, she is a spirited

[002-227] lady, irritable and short-tempered.... Yes. But it’s no use going over

[002-228] that! [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], as you may well fancy, has had no education. I did make an

[002-229] effort four years ago to give her a course of geography and universal

[002-230] history, but as I was not very well up in those subjects myself and we

[002-231] had no suitable books, and what books we had... hm, anyway we have not

[002-232] even those now, so all our instruction came to an end. We stopped at

[002-233] Cyrus of Persia. Since she has attained years of maturity, she has read

[002-234] other books of romantic tendency and of late she had read with great

[002-235] interest a book she got through Mr. Lebeziatnikov, Lewes’ Physiology--do

[002-236] you know it?--and even recounted extracts from it to us: and that’s the

[002-237] whole of her education. And now may I venture to address you, honoured

[002-238] sir, on my own account with a private question. Do you suppose that

[002-239] a respectable poor girl can earn much by honest work? Not fifteen

[002-240] farthings a day can she earn, if she is respectable and has no special

[002-241] talent and that without putting her work down for an instant! And what’s

[002-242] more, Ivan Ivanitch Klopstock the civil counsellor--have you heard of

[002-243] him?--has not to this day paid her for the half-dozen linen shirts she

[002-244] made him and drove her roughly away, stamping and reviling her, on the

[002-245] pretext that the shirt collars were not made like the pattern and were

[002-246] put in askew. And there are the little ones hungry.... And Katerina

[002-247] Ivanovna walking up and down and wringing her hands, her cheeks flushed

[002-248] red, as they always are in that disease: ‘Here you live with us,’ says

[002-249] she, ‘you eat and drink and are kept warm and you do nothing to help.’

[002-250] And much she gets to eat and drink when there is not a crust for the

[002-251] little ones for three days! I was lying at the time... well, what of

[002-252] it! I was lying drunk and I heard my Sonia speaking (she is a gentle

[002-253] creature with a soft little voice... fair hair and such a pale, thin

[002-254] little face). She said: ‘Katerina Ivanovna, am I really to do a thing

[002-255] like that?’ And Darya Frantsovna, a woman of evil character and very

[002-256] well known to the police, had two or three times tried to get at her

[002-257] through the landlady. ‘And why not?’ said Katerina Ivanovna with a jeer,

[002-258] ‘you are something mighty precious to be so careful of!’ But don’t blame

[002-259] her, don’t blame her, honoured sir, don’t blame her! She was not herself

[002-260] when she spoke, but driven to distraction by her illness and the crying

[002-261] of the hungry children; and it was said more to wound her than anything

[002-262] else.... For that’s Katerina Ivanovna’s character, and when children

[002-263] cry, even from hunger, she falls to beating them at once. At six o’clock

[002-264] I saw Sonia get up, put on her kerchief and her cape, and go out of the

[002-265] room and about nine o’clock she came back. She walked straight up to

[002-266] Katerina Ivanovna and she laid thirty roubles on the table before her

[002-267] in silence. She did not utter a word, she did not even look at her, she

[002-268] simply picked up our big green _drap de dames_ shawl (we have a shawl,

[002-269] made of _drap de dames_), put it over her head and face and lay down

[002-270] on the bed with her face to the wall; only her little shoulders and her

[002-271] body kept shuddering.... And I went on lying there, just as before....

[002-272] And then I saw, young man, I saw Katerina Ivanovna, in the same silence

[002-273] go up to Sonia’s little bed; she was on her knees all the evening

[002-274] kissing Sonia’s feet, and would not get up, and then they both fell

[002-275] asleep in each other’s arms... together, together... yes... and I... lay

[002-276] drunk.”

[002-277] Marmeladov stopped short, as though his voice had failed him. Then he

[002-278] hurriedly filled his glass, drank, and cleared his throat.

[002-279] “Since then, sir,” he went on after a brief pause--“Since then, owing

[002-280] to an unfortunate occurrence and through information given by

[002-281] evil-intentioned persons--in all which Darya Frantsovna took a

[002-282] leading part on the pretext that she had been treated with want of

[002-283] respect--since then my daughter Sofya Semyonovna has been forced to take

[002-284] a yellow ticket, and owing to that she is unable to go on living with

[002-285] us. For our landlady, Amalia Fyodorovna would not hear of it (though

[002-286] she had backed up Darya Frantsovna before) and Mr. Lebeziatnikov too...

[002-287] hm.... All the trouble between him and Katerina Ivanovna was on Sonia’s

[002-288] account. At first he was for making up to Sonia himself and then all of

[002-289] a sudden he stood on his dignity: ‘how,’ said he, ‘can a highly educated

[002-290] man like me live in the same rooms with a girl like that?’ And Katerina

[002-291] Ivanovna would not let it pass, she stood up for her... and so that’s

[002-292] how it happened. And Sonia comes to us now, mostly after dark; she

[002-293] comforts Katerina Ivanovna and gives her all she can.... She has a room

[002-294] at the Kapernaumovs’ the tailors, she lodges with them; Kapernaumov is

[002-295] a lame man with a cleft palate and all of his numerous family have cleft

[002-296] palates too. And his wife, too, has a cleft palate. They all live in one

[002-297] room, but Sonia has her own, partitioned off.... Hm... yes... very poor

[002-298] people and all with cleft palates... yes. Then I got up in the morning,

[002-299] and put on my rags, lifted up my hands to heaven and set off to his

[002-300] excellency Ivan Afanasyvitch. His excellency Ivan Afanasyvitch, do you

[002-301] know him? No? Well, then, it’s a man of God you don’t know. He is wax...

[002-302] wax before the face of the Lord; even as wax melteth!... His eyes were

[002-303] dim when he heard my story. ‘Marmeladov, once already you have

[002-304] deceived my expectations... I’ll take you once more on my own

[002-305] responsibility’--that’s what he said, ‘remember,’ he said, ‘and now you

[002-306] can go.’ I kissed the dust at his feet--in thought only, for in reality

[002-307] he would not have allowed me to do it, being a statesman and a man of

[002-308] modern political and enlightened ideas. I returned home, and when I

[002-309] announced that I’d been taken back into the service and should receive a

[002-310] salary, heavens, what a to-do there was!...”

[002-311] Marmeladov stopped again in violent excitement. At that moment a whole

[002-312] party of revellers already drunk came in from the street, and the sounds

[002-313] of a hired concertina and the cracked piping voice of a child of seven

[002-314] singing “The Hamlet” were heard in the entry. The room was filled with

[002-315] noise. The tavern-keeper and the boys were busy with the new-comers.

[002-316] Marmeladov paying no attention to the new arrivals continued his story.

[002-317] He appeared by now to be extremely weak, but as he became more and more

[002-318] drunk, he became more and more talkative. The recollection of his

[002-319] recent success in getting the situation seemed to revive him, and was

[002-320] positively reflected in a sort of radiance on his face. Raskolnikov

[002-321] listened attentively.

[002-322] “That was five weeks ago, sir. Yes.... As soon as Katerina Ivanovna

[002-323] and Sonia heard of it, mercy on us, it was as though I stepped into the

[002-324] kingdom of Heaven. It used to be: you can lie like a beast, nothing but

[002-325] abuse. Now they were walking on tiptoe, hushing the children. ‘Semyon

[002-326] Zaharovitch is tired with his work at the office, he is resting, shh!’

[002-327] They made me coffee before I went to work and boiled cream for me! They

[002-328] began to get real cream for me, do you hear that? And how they managed

[002-329] to get together the money for a decent outfit--eleven roubles, fifty

[002-330] copecks, I can’t guess. Boots, cotton shirt-fronts--most magnificent,

[002-331] a uniform, they got up all in splendid style, for eleven roubles and

[002-332] a half. The first morning I came back from the office I found Katerina

[002-333] Ivanovna had cooked two courses for dinner--soup and salt meat with

[002-334] horse radish--which we had never dreamed of till then. She had not any

[002-335] dresses... none at all, but she got herself up as though she were going

[002-336] on a visit; and not that she’d anything to do it with, she smartened

[002-337] herself up with nothing at all, she’d done her hair nicely, put on a

[002-338] clean collar of some sort, cuffs, and there she was, quite a different

[002-339] person, she was younger and better looking. Sonia, my little darling,

[002-340] had only helped with money ‘for the time,’ she said, ‘it won’t do for me
