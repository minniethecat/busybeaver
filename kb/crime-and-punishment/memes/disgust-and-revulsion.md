# Disgust and Revulsion

Disgust in *Crime and Punishment* operates as a visceral, bodily register of moral transgression — Raskolnikov's physical revulsion toward the world and toward himself is the body's way of acknowledging a guilt that his intellect refuses to accept.

## Definition

Disgust and revulsion in Dostoevsky's novel are not simple emotions but complex psycho-somatic responses that blur the boundary between physical sensation and moral judgment. From the novel's opening pages, "disgust gleamed for a moment in the young man's refined face" (001-046) as he surveys the squalor of Petersburg. This initial disgust at his environment soon turns inward: "disgusting, loathsome, loathsome!" (001-237) — Raskolnikov's self-directed outburst before the murder. After the crime, the sensation intensifies into "a feeling of intense repulsion, which had begun to oppress and torture his heart" (001-239), suggesting that physical disgust has become the psyche's primary mechanism for registering moral horror.

## Expression in the Novel

The trajectory of disgust in the novel maps Raskolnikov's psychological deterioration. Before the murder, disgust is projected outward — onto the pawnbroker ("a sickening impression," 002-513), onto the city's drunkards and beggars. But after the axe falls, disgust recoils upon himself. He experiences "complete revulsion of feeling" (004-335) at key moments, and his habitual response to strangers becomes one of "irritable and uneasy aversion" (002-060). Marmeladov articulates the social dimension of this emotion: if there can be "no respect either, if on the contrary there is aversion, contempt, repulsion, what then?" (004-141, 004-142). The novel suggests that disgust is both a symptom of moral sickness and, paradoxically, the first sign of a conscience still alive enough to be revolted by its own corruption.
