# Napoleon Complex

"I wanted to become a Napoleon, that is why I killed her" (030-312). Raskolnikov's confession to Sonia reduces his elaborate theory to its psychological core: the desire to test whether he belongs among history's great transgressors. The Napoleon figure haunts the novel as both aspiration and accusation.

## Definition

The Napoleon complex, as it operates in the novel, is not the popular psychological syndrome but a specific literary and philosophical fixation: the belief that exceptional individuals — "Lycurgus, Solon, Mahomet, Napoleon" (019-396) — are "all without exception criminals" who created new moral orders through transgression, and that one might test one's own greatness by attempting the same.

## Expression in the Novel

Raskolnikov's article invoked Napoleon as the exemplar of the extraordinary man. Porfiry, with characteristic irony, asks whether "it was one of these future Napoleons who did for Alyona Ivanovna" (019-595). After the murder, Raskolnikov's internal monologue becomes a trial of his Napoleonic ambitions: "Napoleon, the pyramids, Waterloo — and a wretched, skinny old woman" (020-223). The disparity between the grand historical precedent and the squalid reality of his crime becomes a source of self-torment. The thought "almost made him laugh. Napoleon, the pyramids" (020-223) — the bathos is the point. As Porfiry later observes, had Raskolnikov truly been a Napoleon, he would have "taken him prisoner" (025-318), but instead, "I shouldn't have been a Napoleon, but I might have been a" lesser figure (025-325). The final verdict comes from Raskolnikov himself: "Napoleon, for instance, had happened to" commit great deeds without hesitation, but Raskolnikov could not (030-319).

## Key Passages

> "I wanted to become a Napoleon, that is why I killed her." (030-312)

> "Napoleon, the pyramids, Waterloo — and a wretched, skinny old woman, a pawnbroker with a red trunk." (020-223)

> "All great men, or even men who are a little out of the common — Lycurgus, Solon, Mahomet, Napoleon — were all without exception criminals." (019-396)

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His self-assessment oscillates between Napoleonic grandiosity and crushing self-contempt. The gap between the historical Napoleon and his own crime becomes the measure of his failure.

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: Uses the Napoleon reference as a diagnostic tool. His questions about "future Napoleons" (019-595) are designed to make Raskolnikov confront the absurdity of his own pretensions.
