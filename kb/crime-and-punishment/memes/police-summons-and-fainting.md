# Police Summons and Fainting

The police summons and subsequent fainting occur in Part 2, Chapter 1 — the first major event after the [[murder-of-pawnbroker|murder of the pawnbroker]]. Still in a feverish state from the psychological shock of the crime, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] receives an ordinary summons to the district police station over an unpaid debt. What begins as a bureaucratic triviality becomes a crucible of terror when the officers begin discussing the murder, and [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] faints — an involuntary confession that marks the beginning of his psychological disintegration and places him under official suspicion for the first time.

## Summary

On the morning after the murder, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] — still feverish and half-delirious — receives a police summons from the district station (008-130). Believing he is about to be arrested for the murder, he dresses in terror, clutching evidence from the crime. At the police office, he learns the summons is merely about an unpaid debt to his landlady. Relief turns to crisis when officers Ilya Petrovitch and Nikodim Fomitch begin discussing the pawnbroker's murder. Overwhelmed by the stifling atmosphere and the psychological pressure, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] faints — an event that the astute officers note with suspicion (008-581).

## Context & Buildup

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has just committed the double murder of [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] and [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] in [[pawnbrokers-flat|the pawnbroker's flat]]. He returns to his garret in a state of psychological collapse — feverish, hallucinatory, unable to dispose of evidence or function normally. The porter delivers a grey folded paper: "A summons to the police office, of course. You know which office" (008-130). [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s immediate assumption is that his crime has been discovered.

In his delirium, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has been clutching the shreds of his bloodstained trousers — evidence that [[nastasya|Nastasya]], his servant, notices: "Look at the rags he's collected and sleeps with them, as though he has got hold of a treasure" (008-147). Yet even in his fever, he reasons: if they were coming to arrest him, they would not send an ordinary summons. The cognitive dissonance between his rational assessment and his visceral terror defines the entire scene.

## Detailed Account

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] arrives at the police station in squalid dress, contrasting with the "rather better dressed" crowd in the inner rooms (008-239). He presents his summons to the head clerk, a young, fashionably dressed man with pomaded hair and rings, who tells him to wait while he attends to another case — a German woman named [[luise-ivanovna|Luise Ivanovna]] who is being charged with disorderly conduct (008-265).

The assistant superintendent, Ilya Petrovitch, enters "very jauntily, with a peculiar swing of his shoulders at each step" and immediately asserts his authority (008-276). [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is called to answer for his debt to his landlady, and signs a statement. The matter appears resolved.

Then the conversation shifts. Officers Nikodim Fomitch and Ilya Petrovitch begin discussing the murder of the pawnbroker — specifically the timing and how the murderer must have been in the apartment when [[koch|Koch]] and [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]] knocked at the door. They debate whether the murderer locked himself in with [[alyona-ivanovna|the old woman]] (008-541–577). [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], standing in the stifling room, listens as the officers reconstruct his crime in real time.

The head clerk notices [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s condition: "Are you ill?" (008-527). [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] signs the paper — "He could hardly hold his pen when he was signing" (008-577) — and then, overcome by "a terrible inner turmoil" in the stifling air, he faints: "the sick man when he fainted" (008-581). When he recovers and leaves, Ilya Petrovitch looks at him "rather peculiarly" (008-596).

## Immediate Aftermath

In the street, "his faintness passed off completely" (008-603) — the physical symptom vanishes the moment he leaves the police environment, suggesting its psychological origin. But the damage is done. The officers have witnessed his extreme reaction to the murder discussion. Ilya Petrovitch's "peculiar" look signals the beginning of official suspicion.

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s fainting becomes a recurring reference point. In Part 2, Chapter 4, [[zametov|Zametov]] reminds [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]: "the day before you fainted at the police office while they were talking about it" (011-108). The event has entered the novel's web of circumstantial evidence against him.

## Long-Term Consequences

The fainting episode establishes a pattern that recurs throughout the novel: [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s body betrays what his intellect denies. His physiological reactions — fainting, fever, delirium — become markers of guilt that the detective [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] later exploits with his psychological method of investigation.

The event also creates a paper trail. The police now have a record of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s presence, his debt, and his suspicious behavior. When [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] later questions him, the fainting becomes a key datum: "at the police-office, you remember, when I fainted" (013-247). What [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] intended as a perfect, rational crime has already begun to unravel through involuntary self-betrayal.

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The police summons scene dramatizes the novel's central thesis: that crime carries its psychological punishment within the criminal's psyche. The summons is a false alarm — the police have no suspicion of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] — yet his own guilt transforms a bureaucratic errand into an ordeal. Dostoevsky uses the contrast between the mundane (an unpaid debt) and the catastrophic (murder) to show how guilt reorganizes reality: everything becomes evidence, every glance becomes accusation.

The scene also introduces the police apparatus that will eventually close in on [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]. Ilya Petrovitch ("Gunpowder") is established as a volatile personality; his later role in [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s confession at the [[hay-market|Hay Market]] police station creates a structural parallel between this early false confession (the fainting) and the final true one.

## Characters Involved

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]** — The protagonist, summoned over a debt but consumed by terror that his murder has been discovered. His fainting is an involuntary betrayal of guilt (008-581).
- **Ilya Petrovitch ("Gunpowder")** — The assistant superintendent, volatile and officious. His "peculiar" look at [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s fainting represents the first official suspicion (008-596).
- **Nikodim Fomitch** — The district superintendent, who discusses the murder case with Ilya Petrovitch in [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s presence, inadvertently triggering the crisis (008-521).
- **[[nastasya|Nastasya]]** — [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s servant, who notices his feverish clutching of the bloody rags and tries to prevent him from going to the station (008-135).
