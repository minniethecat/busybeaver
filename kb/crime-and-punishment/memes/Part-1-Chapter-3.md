[003-001] He waked up late next day after a broken sleep. But his sleep had not

[003-002] refreshed him; he waked up bilious, irritable, ill-tempered, and looked

[003-003] with hatred at his room. It was a tiny cupboard of a room about six

[003-004] paces in length. It had a poverty-stricken appearance with its dusty

[003-005] yellow paper peeling off the walls, and it was so low-pitched that a man

[003-006] of more than average height was ill at ease in it and felt every moment

[003-007] that he would knock his head against the ceiling. The furniture was in

[003-008] keeping with the room: there were three old chairs, rather rickety; a

[003-009] painted table in the corner on which lay a few manuscripts and books;

[003-010] the dust that lay thick upon them showed that they had been long

[003-011] untouched. A big clumsy sofa occupied almost the whole of one wall and

[003-012] half the floor space of the room; it was once covered with chintz, but

[003-013] was now in rags and served [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] as a bed. Often he went to sleep

[003-014] on it, as he was, without undressing, without sheets, wrapped in his old

[003-015] student’s overcoat, with his head on one little pillow, under which he

[003-016] heaped up all the linen he had, clean and dirty, by way of a bolster. A

[003-017] little table stood in front of the sofa.

[003-018] It would have been difficult to sink to a lower ebb of disorder, but to

[003-019] Raskolnikov in his present state of mind this was positively agreeable.

[003-020] He had got completely away from everyone, like a tortoise in its shell,

[003-021] and even the sight of a servant girl who had to wait upon him and looked

[003-022] sometimes into his room made him writhe with nervous irritation. He was

[003-023] in the condition that overtakes some monomaniacs entirely concentrated

[003-024] upon one thing. His landlady had for the last fortnight given up sending

[003-025] him in meals, and he had not yet thought of expostulating with her,

[003-026] though he went without his dinner. [[nastasya|Nastasya]], the cook and only servant,

[003-027] was rather pleased at the lodger’s mood and had entirely given up

[003-028] sweeping and doing his room, only once a week or so she would stray into

[003-029] his room with a broom. She waked him up that day.

[003-030] “Get up, why are you asleep?” she called to him. “It’s past nine, I have

[003-031] brought you some tea; will you have a cup? I should think you’re fairly

[003-032] starving?”

[003-033] Raskolnikov opened his eyes, started and recognised Nastasya.

[003-034] “From [[praskovya-pavlovna|the landlady]], eh?” he asked, slowly and with a sickly face sitting

[003-035] up on the sofa.

[003-036] “From the landlady, indeed!”

[003-037] She set before him her own cracked teapot full of weak and stale tea and

[003-038] laid two yellow lumps of sugar by the side of it.

[003-039] “Here, Nastasya, take it please,” he said, fumbling in his pocket (for

[003-040] he had slept in his clothes) and taking out a handful of coppers--“run

[003-041] and buy me a loaf. And get me a little sausage, the cheapest, at the

[003-042] pork-butcher’s.”

[003-043] “The loaf I’ll fetch you this very minute, but wouldn’t you rather have

[003-044] some cabbage soup instead of sausage? It’s capital soup, yesterday’s. I

[003-045] saved it for you yesterday, but you came in late. It’s fine soup.”

[003-046] When the soup had been brought, and he had begun upon it, Nastasya

[003-047] sat down beside him on the sofa and began chatting. She was a country

[003-048] peasant-woman and a very talkative one.

[003-049] “Praskovya Pavlovna means to complain to the police about you,” she

[003-050] said.

[003-051] He scowled.

[003-052] “To the police? What does she want?”

[003-053] “You don’t pay her money and you won’t turn out of the room. That’s what

[003-054] she wants, to be sure.”

[003-055] “The devil, that’s the last straw,” he muttered, grinding his teeth,

[003-056] “no, that would not suit me... just now. She is a fool,” he added aloud.

[003-057] “I’ll go and talk to her to-day.”

[003-058] “Fool she is and no mistake, just as I am. But why, if you are so

[003-059] clever, do you lie here like a sack and have nothing to show for it? One

[003-060] time you used to go out, you say, to teach children. But why is it you

[003-061] do nothing now?”

[003-062] “I am doing...” Raskolnikov began sullenly and reluctantly.

[003-063] “What are you doing?”

[003-064] “Work...”

[003-065] “What sort of work?”

[003-066] “I am thinking,” he answered seriously after a pause.

[003-067] Nastasya was overcome with a fit of laughter. She was given to laughter

[003-068] and when anything amused her, she laughed inaudibly, quivering and

[003-069] shaking all over till she felt ill.

[003-070] “And have you made much money by your thinking?” she managed to

[003-071] articulate at last.

[003-072] “One can’t go out to give lessons without boots. And I’m sick of it.”

[003-073] “Don’t quarrel with your bread and butter.”

[003-074] “They pay so little for lessons. What’s the use of a few coppers?” he

[003-075] answered, reluctantly, as though replying to his own thought.

[003-076] “And you want to get a fortune all at once?”

[003-077] He looked at her strangely.

[003-078] “Yes, I want a fortune,” he answered firmly, after a brief pause.

[003-079] “Don’t be in such a hurry, you quite frighten me! Shall I get you the

[003-080] loaf or not?”

[003-081] “As you please.”

[003-082] “Ah, I forgot! A letter came for you yesterday when you were out.”

[003-083] “A letter? for me! from whom?”

[003-084] “I can’t say. I gave three copecks of my own to the postman for it. Will

[003-085] you pay me back?”

[003-086] “Then bring it to me, for God’s sake, bring it,” cried Raskolnikov

[003-087] greatly excited--“good God!”

[003-091] suddenly stabbed his heart.

[003-093] copecks, but for goodness’ sake, make haste and go!”
