[018-001] At that moment the door was softly opened, and a young girl walked into

[018-002] the room, looking timidly about her. Everyone turned towards her with

[018-003] surprise and curiosity. At first sight, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] did not recognise

[018-004] her. It was [[sonia-marmeladov|Sofya Semyonovna Marmeladov]]. He had seen her yesterday for

[018-005] the first time, but at such a moment, in such surroundings and in such

[018-006] a dress, that his memory retained a very different image of her. Now she

[018-007] was a modestly and poorly-dressed young girl, very young, indeed,

[018-008] almost like a child, with a modest and refined manner, with a candid but

[018-009] somewhat frightened-looking face. She was wearing a very plain indoor

[018-010] dress, and had on a shabby old-fashioned hat, but she still carried a

[018-011] parasol. Unexpectedly finding the room full of people, she was not so

[018-012] much embarrassed as completely overwhelmed with shyness, like a

[018-013] little child. She was even about to retreat. “Oh... it’s you!” said

[018-014] Raskolnikov, extremely astonished, and he, too, was confused. He at once

[018-015] recollected that his mother and sister knew through Luzhin’s letter

[018-016] of “some young woman of notorious behaviour.” He had only just been

[018-017] protesting against Luzhin’s calumny and declaring that he had seen the

[018-018] girl last night for the first time, and suddenly she had walked in. He

[018-019] remembered, too, that he had not protested against the expression “of

[018-020] notorious behaviour.” All this passed vaguely and fleetingly through

[018-021] his brain, but looking at her more intently, he saw that the humiliated

[018-022] creature was so humiliated that he felt suddenly sorry for her. When she

[018-023] made a movement to retreat in terror, it sent a pang to his heart.

[018-024] “I did not expect you,” he said, hurriedly, with a look that made her

[018-025] stop. “Please sit down. You come, no doubt, from [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]].

[018-026] Allow me--not there. Sit here....”

[018-027] At Sonia’s entrance, [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]], who had been sitting on one of

[018-028] Raskolnikov’s three chairs, close to the door, got up to allow her to

[018-029] enter. Raskolnikov had at first shown her the place on the sofa where

[018-030] [[zossimov|Zossimov]] had been sitting, but feeling that the sofa which served him

[018-031] as a bed, was too _familiar_ a place, he hurriedly motioned her to

[018-032] Razumihin’s chair.

[018-033] “You sit here,” he said to Razumihin, putting him on the sofa.

[018-034] Sonia sat down, almost shaking with terror, and looked timidly at the

[018-035] two ladies. It was evidently almost inconceivable to herself that she

[018-036] could sit down beside them. At the thought of it, she was so frightened

[018-037] that she hurriedly got up again, and in utter confusion addressed

[018-038] Raskolnikov.

[018-039] “I... I... have come for one minute. Forgive me for disturbing you,” she

[018-040] began falteringly. “I come from Katerina Ivanovna, and she had no one to

[018-041] send. Katerina Ivanovna told me to beg you... to be at the service... in

[018-042] the morning... at Mitrofanievsky... and then... to us... to her...

[018-043] to do her the honour... she told me to beg you...” Sonia stammered and

[018-044] ceased speaking.

[018-045] “I will try, certainly, most certainly,” answered Raskolnikov. He,

[018-046] too, stood up, and he, too, faltered and could not finish his sentence.

[018-047] “Please sit down,” he said, suddenly. “I want to talk to you. You are

[018-048] perhaps in a hurry, but please, be so kind, spare me two minutes,” and

[018-049] he drew up a chair for her.

[018-050] Sonia sat down again, and again timidly she took a hurried, frightened

[018-051] look at the two ladies, and dropped her eyes. Raskolnikov’s pale face

[018-052] flushed, a shudder passed over him, his eyes glowed.

[018-053] “Mother,” he said, firmly and insistently, “this is Sofya Semyonovna

[018-054] [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]], the daughter of that unfortunate Mr. Marmeladov, who was run

[018-055] over yesterday before my eyes, and of whom I was just telling you.”

[018-056] [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] glanced at Sonia, and slightly screwed up

[018-057] her eyes. In spite of her embarrassment before Rodya’s urgent and

[018-058] challenging look, she could not deny herself that satisfaction. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]

[018-059] gazed gravely and intently into the poor girl’s face, and scrutinised

[018-060] her with perplexity. Sonia, hearing herself introduced, tried to raise

[018-061] her eyes again, but was more embarrassed than ever.

[018-062] “I wanted to ask you,” said Raskolnikov, hastily, “how things were

[018-063] arranged yesterday. You were not worried by the police, for instance?”

[018-064] “No, that was all right... it was too evident, the cause of death...

[018-065] they did not worry us... only the lodgers are angry.”

[018-066] “Why?”

[018-067] “At the body’s remaining so long. You see it is hot now. So that,

[018-068] to-day, they will carry it to the cemetery, into the chapel, until

[018-069] to-morrow. At first Katerina Ivanovna was unwilling, but now she sees

[018-070] herself that it’s necessary...”

[018-071] “To-day, then?”

[018-072] “She begs you to do us the honour to be in the church to-morrow for the

[018-073] service, and then to be present at the funeral lunch.”

[018-074] “She is giving a funeral lunch?”

[018-075] “Yes... just a little.... She told me to thank you very much for helping

[018-076] us yesterday. But for you, we should have had nothing for the funeral.”

[018-077] All at once her lips and chin began trembling, but, with an effort, she

[018-078] controlled herself, looking down again.

[018-080] thin, very thin, pale little face, rather irregular and angular, with a

[018-081] sharp little nose and chin. She could not have been called pretty, but

[018-082] her blue eyes were so clear, and when they lighted up, there was such

[018-083] a kindliness and simplicity in her expression that one could not help

[018-084] being attracted. Her face, and her whole figure indeed, had another

[018-085] peculiar characteristic. In spite of her eighteen years, she looked

[018-086] almost a little girl--almost a child. And in some of her gestures, this

[018-087] childishness seemed almost absurd.

[018-089] Does she even mean to have a funeral lunch?” Raskolnikov asked,

[018-090] persistently keeping up the conversation.

[018-091] “The coffin will be plain, of course... and everything will be plain, so

[018-094] it should be so. You know one can’t... it’s a comfort to her... she is

[018-095] like that, you know....”

[018-096] “I understand, I understand... of course... why do you look at my room

[018-097] like that? My mother has just said it is like a tomb.”

[018-105] course. Come, Dounia.... And you, Rodya, had better go for a little

[018-106] walk, and then rest and lie down before you come to see us.... I am

[018-107] afraid we have exhausted you....”

[018-109] something to see to.”

[018-110] “But surely you will have dinner together?” cried Razumihin, looking in

[018-111] surprise at Raskolnikov. “What do you mean?”

[018-112] “Yes, yes, I am coming... of course, of course! And you stay a minute.

[018-113] You do not want him just now, do you, mother? Or perhaps I am taking him

[018-114] from you?”

[018-115] “Oh, no, no. And will you, Dmitri Prokofitch, do us the favour of dining

[018-116] with us?”

[018-117] “Please do,” added Dounia.

[018-118] Razumihin bowed, positively radiant. For one moment, they were all

[018-119] strangely embarrassed.

[018-120] “Good-bye, Rodya, that is till we meet. I do not like saying good-bye.

[018-121] Good-bye, [[nastasya|Nastasya]]. Ah, I have said good-bye again.”

[018-122] Pulcheria Alexandrovna meant to greet Sonia, too; but it somehow failed

[018-123] to come off, and she went in a flutter out of the room.

[018-124] But Avdotya Romanovna seemed to await her turn, and following her mother

[018-125] out, gave Sonia an attentive, courteous bow. Sonia, in confusion, gave

[018-126] a hurried, frightened curtsy. There was a look of poignant discomfort

[018-127] in her face, as though Avdotya Romanovna’s courtesy and attention were

[018-128] oppressive and painful to her.

[018-129] “Dounia, good-bye,” called Raskolnikov, in the passage. “Give me your

[018-130] hand.”

[018-131] “Why, I did give it to you. Have you forgotten?” said Dounia, turning

[018-132] warmly and awkwardly to him.

[018-133] “Never mind, give it to me again.” And he squeezed her fingers warmly.

[018-134] Dounia smiled, flushed, pulled her hand away, and went off quite happy.

[018-135] “Come, that’s capital,” he said to Sonia, going back and looking

[018-136] brightly at her. “God give peace to the dead, the living have still to

[018-137] live. That is right, isn’t it?”

[018-138] Sonia looked surprised at the sudden brightness of his face. He looked

[018-139] at her for some moments in silence. The whole history of the dead father

[018-140] floated before his memory in those moments....
