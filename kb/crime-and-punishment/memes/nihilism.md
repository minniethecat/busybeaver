# Nihilism

Though the word "nihilism" rarely appears in *Crime and Punishment*, the novel is one of literature's most sustained examinations of its consequences. The intellectual atmosphere of 1860s St. Petersburg — with its "socialist" doctrines (019-259), its theories of "environment" as the sole determinant of human behavior (019-268), and its "people with new ideas" (019-492) — forms the milieu in which Raskolnikov's extraordinary man theory could take root.

## Definition

Russian nihilism of the 1860s was not merely the denial of meaning but a specific intellectual program: the rejection of all authorities (religious, political, familial) not justified by reason; the embrace of scientific materialism and utilitarian ethics; and the belief that human behavior is exclusively determined by "environment" (019-268). Dostoevsky saw in nihilism the logical endpoint of Enlightenment rationalism unmoored from Orthodox Christian ethics — reason without conscience, freedom without responsibility.

## Expression in the Novel

The novel embeds nihilist ideas in multiple characters and conversations. Razumihin, though personally opposed to nihilism, describes its ascendancy: "It began with the socialist doctrine" (019-259). Lebeziatnikov, Luzhin's young roommate, parrots the environmental-determinist line that crime is purely "the influence of environment" (019-268), a theory that would absolve the criminal of moral responsibility. Luzhin himself represents nihilism's fusion with bourgeois self-interest — his "rational egoism" reduces all human relations, including marriage, to economic calculation. Raskolnikov's theory, though more sophisticated than Lebeziatnikov's parroting or Luzhin's vulgarity, shares nihilism's core premise: that traditional morality has no binding authority, and that a sufficiently rational individual may construct his own moral law. The novel's plot demonstrates where this premise leads: to the axe.

## Key Passages

> "It began with the socialist doctrine. The doctrine is well known: crime is a protest against the abnormality of the social organisation — nothing more, and nothing else. No other causes are admitted." (019-259–019-268)

> "People with new ideas, people with the faintest capacity for saying something *new* — are very few in number, extraordinarily few in fact." (019-492)

> "Environment accounts for a great deal in crime, I can assure you." (019-298)

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Embodies nihilism's highest and most dangerous form — the brilliant individual who takes rationalist premises to their homicidal conclusion. His trajectory is Dostoevsky's argument that nihilism, pursued honestly, ends in either self-destruction or faith.

- **Dmitri Razumihin**: Represents the healthy alternative — reason grounded in human decency. He "abuses the socialists" (020-235) not from conservative reflex but because he recognizes the gap between their theories and the complexity of actual human beings.

- **Pyotr Luzhin**: The novel's satire of nihilism-as-self-interest — a "progressive" who uses the language of rational egoism to justify exploitation. His doctrine that a man should love himself alone because "everything in this world is based on self-interest" reveals the moral vacuum at nihilism's core.
