# Thresholds and Staircases

Staircases, doorways, corridors, and thresholds recur obsessively in *Crime and Punishment*. Characters meet on stairs, hesitate at thresholds, listen at doors, creep down darkened staircases "like a cat" [001-023]. These are not neutral architectural details but charged liminal spaces — sites of transition, concealment, and exposure — that externalize Raskolnikov's psychological condition. Mikhail Bakhtin identified the threshold as Dostoevsky's defining chronotope: the point where crisis crystallizes and a character stands between two incompatible states of being.

## Staircases as Moral Architecture

Raskolnikov's life is organized around staircases. He dreads being "stopped on the stairs" [001-020] by his landlady — an encounter that would force engagement with the mundane world he has abandoned. After committing the murder, "as he went down the stairs, he even stopped short" [001-231], his body registering what his mind cannot: that he has crossed a line from which there is no return. The staircase is the space between the crime and the world, between his room (isolation, theory) and the street (society, consequence).

The pawnbroker's building is itself a labyrinth of thresholds: "the door at the very top of the stairs stood ajar" [002-491], an ominous openness that Raskolnikov interprets as fate. When he later returns to the scene, the same staircase triggers an uncanny repetition — the architecture remembers what he has done. "He went up the staircase" of the old woman's building a second time, drawn by a compulsion he cannot explain, and "the same paint, the same yellow walls, the same sharp, unpleasant smell" [016-010] greet him. The staircase is unchanged; only he has been altered by what transpired there.

The staircase to Raskolnikov's garret is described as "dark and narrow" with "thirteen steps" [001-019] — a number freighted with ominous suggestion. Each ascent is an ordeal: "he had to pass the landlady's kitchen, the door of which almost always stood wide open. And each time he passed, he experienced some morbid, cowardly sensation which made him wince with shame" [001-020]. The stairwell is not merely the route to his room but the architecture of his shame.

## The Threshold as Moral Crisis

Porfiry's final visit brings Raskolnikov to a literal threshold: the detective "stood on the threshold" [028-426], scanning the room before entering. The moment is not merely dramatic — Porfiry pauses at the boundary of Raskolnikov's private space, and his presence on the threshold enacts the moral pressure that will push Raskolnikov toward confession. To cross a threshold in this novel is always to commit to a course of action; to hesitate at one is to occupy the space of unresolved moral crisis.

The threshold motif reaches its symbolic apex at Raskolnikov's confession to Sonia. He "stood in the doorway" of her room, "not daring to go in" [025-120]. The doorway frames the moment of decision: to enter is to begin the passage from isolation toward human connection, from concealment toward confession. Sonia herself occupies a threshold position throughout the novel — she lives in a room accessed through the Kapernaumovs' apartment, a space that is not quite private and not quite public, appropriate to her liminal social status as both sinner and saint.

Raskolnikov's final act before confessing to the police is to pause at "the threshold" of the station [038-002]. He turns back, walks to the Haymarket, and performs a public bow — then returns and crosses the threshold. The physical motion of stepping across a doorway becomes, in this climactic sequence, the outward sign of an inward transformation.

## Doorways and Eavesdropping

Doorways in the novel also serve as sites of surreptitious knowledge. Raskolnikov first learns of the murder plot by overhearing the student and officer talking in a tavern [005-150] — a public threshold between speech and silence. Svidrigailov overhears Raskolnikov's confession to Sonia through "the thin partition wall" of the adjoining room [030-180], a spatial arrangement that collapses the boundary between private and public. The doorway, half-open, becomes the aperture through which secrets leak and fates intersect.

## Bakhtin's Threshold Chronotope

Mikhail Bakhtin, in *Problems of Dostoevsky's Poetics*, identified the threshold as the dominant chronotope of Dostoevsky's fiction. Unlike the expansive "road" chronotope of Gogol or the biographical time of Tolstoy, Dostoevsky's narratives concentrate crisis onto points of spatial threshold: "The staircase, the front hall, the corridor... and also the squares and streets that are their continuation — these are the main places of action." In Bakhtin's reading, Dostoevsky "leaps over" all biographical time — childhood, education, gradual development — and lands directly on the moment of crisis, which is always rendered spatially as a threshold.

## Key Passages

> "To be stopped on the stairs, to be forced to listen to her" — Raskolnikov's dread of the staircase encounter [001-020]

> "He would creep down the stairs like a cat" — the spatial language of concealment and stealth [001-023]

> "As he went down the stairs, he even stopped short" — Raskolnikov's body registering the moral crossing [001-231]

> "He stood on the threshold. He stood scanning the party" — Porfiry's liminal entrance [028-426]

> "He stood in the doorway, not daring to go in" — Raskolnikov at Sonia's door [025-120]

> "For the last time he cast a rapid glance, took another step, and then *went in*" — the decisive threshold-crossing at the police station [038-005]

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: The character most defined by thresholds. His psychological state is externalized in his relationship to staircases and doorways — dread, hesitation, compulsive return, and finally the decisive crossing that initiates confession.

- **Arkady Svidrigailov**: The eavesdropper who exploits the novel's porous spatial boundaries. His overhearing of Raskolnikov's confession through a thin wall dramatizes the absence of true privacy in Dostoevsky's Petersburg.

- **Sonia Marmeladova**: Her room, accessed through a shared passage in the Kapernaumov apartment, reflects her threshold social position — between chastity and prostitution, between damnation and redemption.
