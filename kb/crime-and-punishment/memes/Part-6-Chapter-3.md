[034-001] He hurried to Svidrigaïlov’s. What he had to hope from that man he

[034-002] did not know. But that man had some hidden power over him. Having once

[034-003] recognised this, he could not rest, and now the time had come.

[034-004] On the way, one question particularly worried him: had Svidrigaïlov been

[034-005] to [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]’s?

[034-006] As far as he could judge, he would swear to it, that he had not. He

[034-007] pondered again and again, went over Porfiry’s visit; no, he hadn’t been,

[034-008] of course he hadn’t.

[034-009] But if he had not been yet, would he go? Meanwhile, for the present he

[034-010] fancied he couldn’t. Why? He could not have explained, but if he could,

[034-011] he would not have wasted much thought over it at the moment. It all

[034-012] worried him and at the same time he could not attend to it. Strange to

[034-013] say, none would have believed it perhaps, but he only felt a faint vague

[034-014] anxiety about his immediate future. Another, much more important anxiety

[034-015] tormented him--it concerned himself, but in a different, more vital way.

[034-016] Moreover, he was conscious of immense moral fatigue, though his mind was

[034-017] working better that morning than it had done of late.

[034-018] And was it worth while, after all that had happened, to contend with

[034-019] these new trivial difficulties? Was it worth while, for instance, to

[034-020] manoeuvre that Svidrigaïlov should not go to Porfiry’s? Was it worth

[034-021] while to investigate, to ascertain the facts, to waste time over anyone

[034-022] like Svidrigaïlov?

[034-023] Oh, how sick he was of it all!

[034-024] And yet he was hastening to Svidrigaïlov; could he be expecting

[034-025] something _new_ from him, information, or means of escape? Men will

[034-026] catch at straws! Was it [[fate-and-coincidence|destiny]] or some instinct bringing them together?

[034-027] Perhaps it was only fatigue, despair; perhaps it was not Svidrigaïlov

[034-028] but some other whom he needed, and Svidrigaïlov had simply presented

[034-029] himself by chance. [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]? But what should he go to Sonia for now? To beg

[034-030] her tears again? He was afraid of Sonia, too. Sonia stood before him as

[034-031] an irrevocable sentence. He must go his own way or hers. At that moment

[034-032] especially he did not feel equal to seeing her. No, would it not be

[034-033] better to try Svidrigaïlov? And he could not help inwardly owning that

[034-034] he had long felt that he must see him for some reason.

[034-035] But what could they have in common? Their very evil-doing could not

[034-036] be of the same kind. The man, moreover, was very unpleasant, evidently

[034-037] depraved, undoubtedly cunning and deceitful, possibly malignant. Such

[034-038] stories were told about him. It is true he was befriending Katerina

[034-039] Ivanovna’s children, but who could tell with what motive and what it

[034-040] meant? The man always had some design, some project.

[034-041] There was another thought which had been continually hovering of late

[034-042] about [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]’s mind, and causing him great uneasiness. It was so

[034-043] painful that he made distinct efforts to get rid of it. He sometimes

[034-044] thought that Svidrigaïlov was dogging his footsteps. Svidrigaïlov had

[034-045] found out his secret and had had designs on [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]. What if he had them

[034-046] still? Wasn’t it practically certain that he had? And what if, having

[034-047] learnt his secret and so having gained power over him, he were to use it

[034-048] as a weapon against Dounia?

[034-049] This idea sometimes even tormented his dreams, but it had never

[034-050] presented itself so vividly to him as on his way to Svidrigaïlov.

[034-051] The very thought moved him to gloomy rage. To begin with, this would

[034-052] transform everything, even his own position; he would have at once to

[034-053] confess his secret to Dounia. Would he have to give himself up perhaps

[034-054] to prevent Dounia from taking some rash step? The letter? This morning

[034-055] Dounia had received a letter. From whom could she get letters in

[034-056] [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]]? Luzhin, perhaps? It’s true [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] was there to protect

[034-057] her, but Razumihin knew nothing of the position. Perhaps it was his duty

[034-058] to tell Razumihin? He thought of it with repugnance.

[034-059] In any case he must see Svidrigaïlov as soon as possible, he decided

[034-060] finally. Thank God, the details of the interview were of little

[034-061] consequence, if only he could get at the root of the matter; but

[034-062] if Svidrigaïlov were capable... if he were intriguing against

[034-063] Dounia--then...

[034-064] Raskolnikov was so exhausted by what he had passed through that month

[034-065] that he could only decide such questions in one way; “then I shall kill

[034-066] him,” he thought in cold despair.

[034-067] A sudden anguish oppressed his heart, he stood still in the middle of

[034-068] the street and began looking about to see where he was and which way he

[034-069] was going. He found himself in X. Prospect, thirty or forty paces from

[034-070] the [[hay-market|Hay Market]], through which he had come. The whole second storey of

[034-071] the house on the left was used as a tavern. All the windows were wide

[034-072] open; judging from the figures moving at the windows, the rooms were

[034-073] full to overflowing. There were sounds of singing, of clarionet and

[034-074] violin, and the boom of a Turkish drum. He could hear women shrieking.

[034-075] He was about to turn back wondering why he had come to the X. Prospect,

[034-076] when suddenly at one of the end windows he saw Svidrigaïlov, sitting

[034-077] at a tea-table right in the open window with a pipe in his mouth.

[034-078] Raskolnikov was dreadfully taken aback, almost terrified. Svidrigaïlov

[034-079] was silently watching and scrutinising him and, what struck Raskolnikov

[034-080] at once, seemed to be meaning to get up and slip away unobserved.

[034-081] Raskolnikov at once pretended not to have seen him, but to be looking

[034-082] absent-mindedly away, while he watched him out of the corner of his eye.

[034-084] did not want to be seen. He took the pipe out of his mouth and was on

[034-085] the point of concealing himself, but as he got up and moved back his

[034-086] chair, he seemed to have become suddenly aware that Raskolnikov had seen

[034-087] him, and was watching him. What had passed between them was much the

[034-089] sly smile came into Svidrigaïlov’s face and grew broader and

[034-090] broader. Each knew that he was seen and watched by the other. At last

[034-091] Svidrigaïlov broke into a loud laugh.

[034-092] “Well, well, come in if you want me; I am here!” he shouted from the

[034-093] window.

[034-094] Raskolnikov went up into the tavern. He found Svidrigaïlov in a tiny

[034-095] back room, adjoining the saloon in which merchants, clerks and numbers

[034-096] of people of all sorts were drinking tea at twenty little tables to the

[034-101] of eighteen, wearing a tucked-up striped skirt, and a Tyrolese hat with

[034-102] ribbons. In spite of the chorus in the other room, she was singing some

[034-103] servants’ hall song in a rather husky contralto, to the accompaniment of

[034-104] the organ.

[034-105] “Come, that’s enough,” Svidrigaïlov stopped her at Raskolnikov’s

[034-106] entrance. The girl at once broke off and stood waiting respectfully.

[034-107] She had sung her guttural rhymes, too, with a serious and respectful

[034-108] expression in her face.

[034-109] “Hey, Philip, a glass!” shouted Svidrigaïlov.

[034-110] “I won’t drink anything,” said Raskolnikov.

[034-111] “As you like, I didn’t mean it for you. Drink, Katia! I don’t want

[034-112] anything more to-day, you can go.” He poured her out a full glass, and

[034-113] laid down a yellow note.

[034-114] Katia drank off her glass of wine, as women do, without putting it down,

[034-115] in twenty gulps, took the note and kissed Svidrigaïlov’s hand, which he

[034-116] allowed quite seriously. She went out of the room and the boy trailed

[034-117] after her with the organ. Both had been brought in from the street.

[034-118] Svidrigaïlov had not been a week in Petersburg, but everything about him

[034-119] was already, so to speak, on a patriarchal footing; the waiter, Philip,

[034-120] was by now an old friend and very obsequious.

[034-121] The door leading to the saloon had a lock on it. Svidrigaïlov was at

[034-122] home in this room and perhaps spent whole days in it. The tavern was

[034-123] dirty and wretched, not even second-rate.

[034-124] “I was going to see you and looking for you,” Raskolnikov began, “but

[034-125] I don’t know what made me turn from the Hay Market into the X. Prospect

[034-126] just now. I never take this turning. I turn to the right from the Hay

[034-127] Market. And this isn’t the way to you. I simply turned and here you are.

[034-128] It is strange!”

[034-129] “Why don’t you say at once ‘it’s a miracle’?”

[034-130] “Because it may be only chance.”

[034-131] “Oh, that’s the way with all you folk,” laughed Svidrigaïlov. “You won’t

[034-132] admit it, even if you do inwardly believe it a miracle! Here you say

[034-133] that it may be only chance. And what cowards they all are here, about

[034-134] having an opinion of their own, you can’t fancy, Rodion Romanovitch. I

[034-135] don’t mean you, you have an opinion of your own and are not afraid to

[034-136] have it. That’s how it was you attracted my curiosity.”

[034-137] “Nothing else?”

[034-138] “Well, that’s enough, you know,” Svidrigaïlov was obviously exhilarated,

[034-139] but only slightly so, he had not had more than half a glass of wine.

[034-140] “I fancy you came to see me before you knew that I was capable of having

[034-141] what you call an opinion of my own,” observed Raskolnikov.

[034-142] “Oh, well, it was a different matter. Everyone has his own plans. And

[034-143] apropos of the miracle let me tell you that I think you have been asleep

[034-144] for the last two or three days. I told you of this tavern myself, there

[034-145] is no miracle in your coming straight here. I explained the way myself,

[034-146] told you where it was, and the hours you could find me here. Do you

[034-147] remember?”

[034-148] “I don’t remember,” answered Raskolnikov with surprise.

[034-149] “I believe you. I told you twice. The address has been stamped

[034-150] mechanically on your memory. You turned this way mechanically and yet

[034-151] precisely according to the direction, though you are not aware of

[034-152] it. When I told you then, I hardly hoped you understood me. You give

[034-153] yourself away too much, Rodion Romanovitch. And another thing, I’m

[034-161] be reflected on the whole country. But that is neither here nor there

[034-166] talking to yourself, and sometimes you wave one hand and declaim, and at

[034-171] “Do you know that I am being followed?” asked Raskolnikov, looking

[034-172] inquisitively at him.

[034-173] “No, I know nothing about it,” said Svidrigaïlov, seeming surprised.

[034-174] “Well, then, let us leave me alone,” Raskolnikov muttered, frowning.

[034-175] “Very good, let us leave you alone.”

[034-176] “You had better tell me, if you come here to drink, and directed me

[034-177] twice to come here to you, why did you hide, and try to get away just

[034-178] now when I looked at the window from the street? I saw it.”

[034-179] “He-he! And why was it you lay on your sofa with closed eyes and

[034-180] pretended to be asleep, though you were wide awake while I stood in your

[034-181] doorway? I saw it.”

[034-182] “I may have had... reasons. You know that yourself.”

[034-183] “And I may have had my reasons, though you don’t know them.”

[034-186] full minute he scrutinised his face, which had impressed him before. It
