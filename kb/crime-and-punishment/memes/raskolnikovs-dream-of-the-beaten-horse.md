# Raskolnikov's Dream of the Beaten Horse

Shortly before the murder of the pawnbroker, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] falls into a feverish sleep on [[petrovsky-island|Petrovsky Island]] and dreams himself back into his childhood. In the dream, the seven-year-old [[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodya]] walks with his father through his provincial hometown when they come upon a horrifying scene: a crowd of drunken peasants, led by a young carter named [[mikolka|Mikolka]], beating an emaciated mare to death. The dream is one of the most famous and psychologically dense passages in Dostoevsky's novel, functioning simultaneously as prophecy, repressed memory, and moral allegory.

## The Dream Scene

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] dreams himself a child again, walking with his father past the town tavern on a holiday. A crowd has gathered around a heavy cart harnessed to a "thin little sorrel beast, one of those peasants' nags" — a mare far too weak to pull such a load:

> "Why, Mikolka, are you crazy to put a nag like that in such a cart?" [005-126]

[[mikolka|Mikolka]], a young thick-necked peasant, insists the mare will gallop and invites the jeering crowd to pile into the cart. Six men, then more, climb aboard — joined by a fat, laughing woman cracking nuts. The absurdity is deliberate: the mare cannot possibly move this weight, and everyone knows it.

With a cry of "now," the mare strains forward but can scarcely move. The three whips descend "like hail," and the laughter in the crowd only redoubles. [[mikolka|Mikolka]], enraged by the mare's failure to perform the impossible, escalates the violence with each failed attempt:

> "Get in, all get in," cried Mikolka, "she will draw you all. I'll beat her to death!" And he thrashed and thrashed at the mare, beside himself with fury. [005-155]

## The Child's Response

The seven-year-old [[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodya]], watching from the crowd, is convulsed with pity. He cries out to his father, who tries to pull him away, but the boy tears himself free and runs toward the horse:

> "Father, father," he cried, "father, what are they doing? Father, they are beating the poor horse!" [005-157]

The child's compassion is visceral and total — he runs beside the mare, sees her "being whipped across the eyes, right in the eyes," weeps, feels himself choking. When a bystander strikes him with a whip, he does not feel it. His entire consciousness is absorbed by the suffering animal.

The mare tries to kick feebly, and the crowd finds even this struggle amusing. "To think of a wretched little beast like that trying to kick!" The suffering is doubled by the spectators' laughter.

## The Killing

[[mikolka|Mikolka]] grows ever more frenzied. He discards the whip for a long, thick shaft from the cart:

> "It's my property," shouted Mikolka and brought the shaft down with a swinging blow. There was a sound of a heavy thud. [005-194]

The shaft falls again — a second time, then a third, then a fourth, "with heavy measured blows." When even this fails to kill the mare outright, [[mikolka|Mikolka]] seizes an iron crowbar and deals "a stunning blow." The boy [[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodya]], screaming, pushes through the crowd to embrace the dying animal's bloodied head.

He wakes from the dream drenched in sweat, his hair soaked, gasping for breath — and immediately asks himself the question that links the dream to the act he is about to commit: "Can it be that I shall really take an axe, that I shall strike her on the head, split her skull open?"

## Narrative Function: The Dream as Foreshadowing

The dream operates on multiple psychological registers:

**As prophecy**: The mare's brutal destruction by [[mikolka|Mikolka]] with axe-like implements (a shaft, a crowbar) prefigures [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s murder of the pawnbroker with an axe. The dream's graphic detail of blows to the head echoes the murder scene that follows.

**As moral allegory**: The innocent child [[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodya]], weeping with compassion for the suffering creature, stands in judgment over the man [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is about to become. The dream externalizes [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s own suppressed horror at the act he contemplates.

**As critique of rationalism**: [[mikolka|Mikolka]]'s refrain — "It's my property, I'll do what I choose" — is the logic of ownership stripped of moral restraint. The dream suggests that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s own "extraordinary man" theory, which treats human beings as material for great men to use, is the same logic [[mikolka|Mikolka]] applies to the mare.

**As repressed memory**: Dostoevsky scholars have noted that such scenes of peasant cruelty to animals were common in 19th-century Russia. The dream draws on genuine childhood experience, lending it the quality of a recovered traumatic memory rather than a mere symbol.

## Relation to the Murder

Upon waking, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] explicitly connects the dream to his murder plan, asking in horror:

> "Can it be," he thought, "can it be that I shall really take an axe, that I shall strike her on the head, split her skull open... that I shall tread in the sticky warm blood, break the lock, steal and tremble; hide, all spattered in the blood... with the axe...?" [005-200]

The dream thus serves as what [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] himself recognizes — but cannot heed — as a final warning from his own conscience. He will go on to commit the murder, but the dream's imagery of senseless cruelty and the innocent child's helpless compassion haunts the entire novel.
