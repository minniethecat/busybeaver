# Dounia Confronts Svidrigailov

In Part 6, Chapter 5 of *Crime and Punishment*, Avdotya Romanovna ([[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]) [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] confronts [[arkady-svidrigailov|Arkady Svidrigailov]] in his rented room. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] has lured her there under the pretence of revealing her brother [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s secret — that he is the murderer of the pawnbroker. What follows is one of the novel's most intense scenes of psychological and physical struggle, in which [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s moral scruple — her inability to kill even her would-be rapist — becomes the catalyst for [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s final reckoning with himself.

## The Trap

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] has orchestrated the meeting with calculated precision. He sends [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] a note claiming he can prove [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s guilt through a conversation he overheard between [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] and [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]], desperate to save her brother, agrees to meet him. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] locks the door and pockets the key, revealing his darker intentions while keeping up a veneer of civility. He admits his brotherly claims were a pretext — what he wants is [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] herself, and he threatens to denounce [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] unless she submits.

But [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] has come prepared.

## The Revolver

In a sudden reversal, [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] draws a concealed weapon:

> Suddenly she pulled out of her pocket a revolver, cocked it and laid it in her hand on the table. Svidrigailov jumped up. [036-384]

The revolver is an heirloom of [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s own household — it belonged to his late wife, [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]], whom many suspect he poisoned. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], momentarily disconcerted, tries to disarm the situation with banter:

> "Why, it's my revolver, an old friend! And how I've hunted for it! The shooting lessons I've given you in the country have not been in vain!" [036-389]

[[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s response is cold and precise:

> "It's not your revolver, it belonged to Marfa Petrovna, whom you killed, wretch! There was nothing of yours in her house. I took it when I began to suspect what you were capable of." [036-392]

This exchange does more than establish the weapon's provenance. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s invocation of [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]] — the wife [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] is widely believed to have murdered — frames the coming confrontation as a moral settling of accounts.

## The Shot

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], testing [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s resolve, advances toward her. She is "deadly pale," measuring the distance between them:

> Dounia raised the revolver, and deadly pale, gazed at him, measuring the distance and awaiting the first movement on his part. Her lower lip was white and trembling. [036-416]

He takes a step forward, and she fires. The bullet grazes his hair and embeds in the wall behind him. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], unnerved but alive, takes another step. A second shot clicks — the revolver misfires. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] has only one barrel remaining.

The scene's extraordinary tension emerges from the asymmetry of their positions: [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], unarmed and physically vulnerable, advancing on a woman holding a loaded gun. Yet the power dynamic is far from simple. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s strange composure — the "pang of anguish" he feels at the sight of her beauty even as she aims at his heart — reveals a man already half in love with his own destruction.

## The Refusal to Kill

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], now standing directly before her, tells her the third barrel is loaded and that he will step aside if she lowers the weapon. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]], trembling, raises the revolver once more. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] does not flinch. She holds the barrel inches from his face for a long moment — and then throws the revolver away:

> Suddenly she flung away the revolver. "I can't..." she said, almost inaudibly. [036-445]

This moment is the scene's moral centre. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] cannot kill. Her refusal is not weakness but a moral limit — the same limit that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s "extraordinary man" theory denies exists. Where [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] rationalised murder as the prerogative of superior beings, [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] discovers through direct experience that the act is impossible for her.

## Narrative Function: Svidrigailov's Turning Point

The confrontation is a turning point for [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s refusal to kill him — and her unambiguous rejection of everything he represents — strips away his last illusions. Immediately afterwards, he releases her, and his thoughts turn toward suicide. He sets out into the wet [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]] night, wandering toward [[petrovsky-island|Petrovsky Island]] and, ultimately, to his death.

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s line as he picks up the discarded revolver is quietly devastating:

> The revolver which Dounia had flung away lay near the door. He picked it up. It was a little pocket three-barrel revolver of old-fashioned construction. There were two charges and one cap left in it. It could be fired again. He thought a little, put the revolver in his pocket, took his hat and went out. [036-481]

The revolver — [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]]'s revolver, which [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] could not use to kill him — will be the weapon with which [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] takes his own life before the night is over.
