# Extraordinary Man Theory

Raskolnikov's philosophical theory, articulated in his article "On Crime" published in the *Periodical Review*, divides humanity into two categories — the "ordinary" who must live in submission, and the "extraordinary" who possess the right to transgress moral law in service of a "new word" that advances humanity. The theory serves as the intellectual framework through which [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] rationalizes the [[murder-of-pawnbroker|murder of Alyona Ivanovna]] (019-367), and its collapse under the weight of [[conscience-and-suffering|conscience]] forms the novel's central philosophical drama.

## Definition

The theory posits that "men are divided into 'ordinary' and 'extraordinary'" (019-367). Ordinary men "have a right to" live "in submission" and "must be" only ordinary; "they are ordinary" (019-369). But "extraordinary men have a right to" commit crimes — "that is not an official right, but an inner right to decide in his own conscience to overstep... certain obstacles" (019-382–019-383). The extraordinary man possesses "the gift or the talent to utter *a new word*" (019-416) and "must from their very nature be criminals — more or less" (019-405).

## Historical and Philosophical Context

Raskolnikov's theory synthesizes several 19th-century intellectual currents. The utilitarian calculus — that one death might enable a thousand good deeds — echoes the radical rationalism of the 1860s Russian intelligentsia (006-079). The invocation of historical figures who "were all without exception criminals" — "Lycurgus, Solon, Mahomet, Napoleon" (019-396) — draws on the Napoleonic legend pervasive in 19th-century Russian literature and the emerging cult of the great individual. The concept of the extraordinary man who creates new moral tables anticipates, and may have influenced, Nietzsche's later formulation of the Übermensch, though Dostoevsky treats it as a pathology to be dissected rather than celebrated (019-574).

## Expression in the Novel

Raskolnikov first publishes the theory six months before the novel's action in an article "On Crime" for the *Periodical Review* (019-330–019-331). [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]], the examining magistrate, has read the article and uses it as the centerpiece of his psychological investigation, drawing Raskolnikov into a philosophical discussion that becomes an implicit confession (019-326). The theory is tested against reality throughout the novel: Raskolnikov's inability to "overstep" without crippling guilt — "if he has a conscience he will suffer for his mistake" (019-552) — demonstrates the inadequacy of purely rational moral frameworks. The murder of Lizaveta, the unintended second victim, further exposes the gap between theoretical justification and the messy reality of violence (019-520).

## Theory's Logical Structure

| Component | Description | Key Assertion |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Division of humanity | Two classes: ordinary vs. extraordinary | "Men are divided into 'ordinary' and 'extraordinary'" (019-367) |
| Ordinary function | Live in submission, reproduce the species | They "have no right to transgress the law" (019-371) |
| Extraordinary right | Inner moral right to overstep obstacles | "An inner right to decide in his own conscience" (019-382) |
| Historical warrant | All great lawgivers were criminals | "Lycurgus, Solon, Mahomet, Napoleon" (019-396) |
| Utilitarian calculus | One death for a thousand lives | Newton "duty-bound to eliminate the dozen" (019-428) |
| Conscience test | Guilt proves ordinariness | "If he has a conscience he will suffer" (019-552) |

## Key Passages

> "Men are divided into 'ordinary' and 'extraordinary.' Ordinary men have to live in submission and have no right to transgress the law, because, don't you see, they are ordinary. But extraordinary men have a right to commit any crime and to transgress the law in any way, just because they are extraordinary." (019-367, 019-369, 019-371)

> "The 'extraordinary' man has the right... that is not an official right, but an inner right to decide in his own conscience to overstep... certain obstacles, and only in case it is essential for the practical fulfilment of his idea (sometimes, perhaps, of benefit to the whole of humanity)." (019-382–019-383)

> "I maintain that if the discoveries of Kepler and Newton could not have been made known except by sacrificing the lives of one or a dozen or more people, Newton would have had the right, would indeed have been duty-bound... to *eliminate* the dozen or the hundred men." (019-428)

> "If he has a conscience he will suffer for his mistake. That will be his punishment — as well as the prison." (019-552)

## Character Perspectives

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]**: The theory's author and primary test subject. He genuinely believes in the intellectual framework but discovers, through the psychological torment following the murder, that he is not among the "extraordinary" who can transgress without conscience (019-574). His eventual confession to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and to the authorities represents the theory's collapse as a lived philosophy.

- **[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]]**: The examining magistrate who treats the article as the key to Raskolnikov's psychology. Porfiry's method is to draw Raskolnikov into philosophical debate, asking pointed questions — "how do you distinguish those extraordinary people from the ordinary?" (019-458) — that expose the theory's internal contradictions. He predicts Raskolnikov's eventual confession by the logic of the theory itself: a man with conscience *must* suffer for his crime (019-549–019-552).

- **[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]]**: Though she never directly engages with the theory, Sonia represents its antithesis: [[christian-faith|Christian faith]], self-sacrifice, and the sanctity of every human life. Her influence draws Raskolnikov toward confession and redemption, the path that the theory's rational calculus cannot provide.

## Critical Interpretations

Literary critics have read the Extraordinary Man Theory as Dostoevsky's rebuttal to several contemporary ideologies. Joseph Frank interprets it as a direct attack on the utilitarian rationalism of Chernyshevsky and the radical *What Is to Be Done?* generation. Mikhail Bakhtin saw the theory's articulation and dissolution as exemplary of Dostoevsky's polyphonic method — the idea is given full voice and then tested to destruction through dialogue with other consciousnesses. The theory also prefigures 20th-century critiques of ideological violence: the notion that abstract ideas can justify concrete murder anticipates the totalitarian rationalizations Dostoevsky would later dissect in *Demons* and *The Brothers Karamazov*.
