[030-001] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] had been a vigorous and active champion of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] against

[030-002] Luzhin, although he had such a load of horror and anguish in his own

[030-003] heart. But having gone through so much in the morning, he found a sort

[030-004] of relief in a change of sensations, apart from the strong personal

[030-005] feeling which impelled him to defend Sonia. He was agitated too,

[030-006] especially at some moments, by the thought of his approaching interview

[030-007] with Sonia: he _had_ to tell her who had killed [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]. He knew the

[030-008] terrible suffering it would be to him and, as it were, brushed away the

[030-009] thought of it. So when he cried as he left [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]’s, “Well,

[030-010] Sofya Semyonovna, we shall see what you’ll say now!” he was still

[030-011] superficially excited, still vigorous and defiant from his triumph over

[030-012] Luzhin. But, strange to say, by the time he reached Sonia’s lodging, he

[030-013] felt a sudden impotence and [[fear-and-dread|fear]]. He stood still in hesitation at the

[030-014] door, asking himself the strange question: “Must he tell her who killed

[030-015] Lizaveta?” It was a strange question because he felt at the very time

[030-016] not only that he could not help telling her, but also that he could

[030-017] not put off the telling. He did not yet know why it must be so, he

[030-018] only _felt_ it, and the agonising sense of his impotence before

[030-019] the inevitable almost crushed him. To cut short his hesitation and

[030-020] suffering, he quickly opened the door and looked at Sonia from the

[030-021] doorway. She was sitting with her elbows on the table and her face in

[030-022] her hands, but seeing Raskolnikov she got up at once and came to meet

[030-023] him as though she were expecting him.

[030-024] “What would have become of me but for you?” she said quickly, meeting

[030-025] him in the middle of the room.

[030-026] Evidently she was in haste to say this to him. It was what she had been

[030-027] waiting for.

[030-028] Raskolnikov went to the table and sat down on the chair from which she

[030-029] had only just risen. She stood facing him, two steps away, just as she

[030-030] had done the day before.

[030-031] “Well, Sonia?” he said, and felt that his voice was trembling, “it was

[030-032] all due to ‘your social position and the habits associated with it.’ Did

[030-033] you understand that just now?”

[030-034] Her face showed her distress.

[030-035] “Only don’t talk to me as you did yesterday,” she interrupted him.

[030-036] “Please don’t begin it. There is misery enough without that.”

[030-037] She made haste to smile, afraid that he might not like the reproach.

[030-038] “I was silly to come away from there. What is happening there now? I

[030-039] wanted to go back directly, but I kept thinking that... you would come.”

[030-040] He told her that [[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Ivanovna]] was turning them out of their lodging

[030-041] and that Katerina Ivanovna had run off somewhere “to seek justice.”

[030-042] “My God!” cried Sonia, “let’s go at once....”

[030-043] And she snatched up her cape.

[030-044] “It’s everlastingly the same thing!” said Raskolnikov, irritably.

[030-045] “You’ve no thought except for them! Stay a little with me.”

[030-046] “But... Katerina Ivanovna?”

[030-047] “You won’t lose Katerina Ivanovna, you may be sure, she’ll come to you

[030-048] herself since she has run out,” he added peevishly. “If she doesn’t find

[030-049] you here, you’ll be blamed for it....”

[030-050] Sonia sat down in painful suspense. Raskolnikov was silent, gazing at

[030-051] the floor and deliberating.

[030-052] “This time Luzhin did not want to prosecute you,” he began, not looking

[030-053] at Sonia, “but if he had wanted to, if it had suited his plans, he would

[030-054] have sent you to prison if it had not been for Lebeziatnikov and me.

[030-055] Ah?”

[030-056] “Yes,” she assented in a faint voice. “Yes,” she repeated, preoccupied

[030-057] and distressed.

[030-058] “But I might easily not have been there. And it was quite an accident

[030-059] Lebeziatnikov’s turning up.”

[030-060] Sonia was silent.

[030-061] “And if you’d gone to prison, what then? Do you remember what I said

[030-062] yesterday?”

[030-063] Again she did not answer. He waited.

[030-064] “I thought you would cry out again ‘don’t speak of it, leave off.’”

[030-065] Raskolnikov gave a laugh, but rather a forced one. “What, silence

[030-066] again?” he asked a minute later. “We must talk about something, you

[030-067] know. It would be interesting for me to know how you would decide a

[030-068] certain ‘problem’ as Lebeziatnikov would say.” (He was beginning to lose

[030-069] the thread.) “No, really, I am serious. Imagine, Sonia, that you had

[030-070] known all Luzhin’s intentions beforehand. Known, that is, for a fact,

[030-071] that they would be the ruin of Katerina Ivanovna and the children and

[030-072] yourself thrown in--since you don’t count yourself for anything--Polenka

[030-073] too... for she’ll go the same way. Well, if suddenly it all depended on

[030-074] your decision whether he or they should go on living, that is whether

[030-075] Luzhin should go on living and doing wicked things, or Katerina Ivanovna

[030-076] should die? How would you decide which of them was to die? I ask you?”

[030-077] Sonia looked uneasily at him. There was something peculiar in this

[030-078] hesitating question, which seemed approaching something in a roundabout

[030-079] way.

[030-080] “I felt that you were going to ask some question like that,” she said,

[030-081] looking inquisitively at him.

[030-082] “I dare say you did. But how is it to be answered?”

[030-083] “Why do you ask about what could not happen?” said Sonia reluctantly.

[030-084] “Then it would be better for Luzhin to go on living and doing wicked

[030-085] things? You haven’t dared to decide even that!”

[030-086] “But I can’t know the Divine Providence.... And why do you ask what

[030-087] can’t be answered? What’s the use of such foolish questions? How could

[030-088] it happen that it should depend on my decision--who has made me a judge

[030-089] to decide who is to live and who is not to live?”

[030-090] “Oh, if the Divine Providence is to be mixed up in it, there is no doing

[030-091] anything,” Raskolnikov grumbled morosely.

[030-092] “You’d better say straight out what you want!” Sonia cried in distress.

[030-093] “You are leading up to something again.... Can you have come simply to

[030-094] torture me?”

[030-095] She could not control herself and began crying bitterly. He looked at

[030-096] her in gloomy misery. Five minutes passed.

[030-097] “Of course you’re right, Sonia,” he said softly at last. He was suddenly

[030-098] changed. His tone of assumed arrogance and helpless defiance was gone.

[030-099] Even his voice was suddenly weak. “I told you yesterday that I was not

[030-100] coming to ask forgiveness and almost the first thing I’ve said is to ask

[030-101] forgiveness.... I said that about Luzhin and Providence for my own sake.

[030-102] I was asking forgiveness, Sonia....”

[030-103] He tried to smile, but there was something helpless and incomplete in

[030-104] his pale smile. He bowed his head and hid his face in his hands.

[030-105] And suddenly a strange, surprising sensation of a sort of bitter hatred

[030-106] for Sonia passed through his heart. As it were wondering and frightened

[030-107] of this sensation, he raised his head and looked intently at her; but he

[030-108] met her uneasy and painfully anxious eyes fixed on him; there was

[030-109] love in them; his hatred vanished like a phantom. It was not the real

[030-110] feeling; he had taken the one feeling for the other. It only meant that

[030-111] _that_ minute had come.

[030-112] He hid his face in his hands again and bowed his head. Suddenly he

[030-113] turned pale, got up from his chair, looked at Sonia, and without

[030-114] uttering a word sat down mechanically on her bed.

[030-115] His sensations that moment were terribly like the moment when he had

[030-116] stood over [[alyona-ivanovna|the old woman]] with the axe in his hand and felt that “he must

[030-117] not lose another minute.”

[030-118] “What’s the matter?” asked Sonia, dreadfully frightened.

[030-119] He could not utter a word. This was not at all, not at all the way he

[030-120] had intended to “tell” and he did not understand what was happening to

[030-121] him now. She went up to him, softly, sat down on the bed beside him and

[030-122] waited, not taking her eyes off him. Her heart throbbed and sank. It

[030-123] was unendurable; he turned his deadly pale face to her. His lips worked,

[030-124] helplessly struggling to utter something. A pang of terror passed

[030-125] through Sonia’s heart.

[030-126] “What’s the matter?” she repeated, drawing a little away from him.

[030-127] “Nothing, Sonia, don’t be frightened.... It’s nonsense. It really is

[030-128] nonsense, if you think of it,” he muttered, like a man in delirium. “Why

[030-129] have I come to torture you?” he added suddenly, looking at her. “Why,

[030-130] really? I keep asking myself that question, Sonia....”

[030-131] He had perhaps been asking himself that question a quarter of an hour

[030-132] before, but now he spoke helplessly, hardly knowing what he said and

[030-133] feeling a continual tremor all over.

[030-134] “Oh, how you are suffering!” she muttered in distress, looking intently

[030-135] at him.

[030-136] “It’s all nonsense.... Listen, Sonia.” He suddenly smiled, a pale

[030-137] helpless smile for two seconds. “You remember what I meant to tell you

[030-138] yesterday?”

[030-139] Sonia waited uneasily.

[030-140] “I said as I went away that perhaps I was saying good-bye for ever, but

[030-141] that if I came to-day I would tell you who... who killed Lizaveta.”

[030-142] She began trembling all over.

[030-143] “Well, here I’ve come to tell you.”

[030-144] “Then you really meant it yesterday?” she whispered with difficulty.

[030-145] “How do you know?” she asked quickly, as though suddenly regaining her

[030-146] reason.

[030-147] Sonia’s face grew paler and paler, and she breathed painfully.

[030-148] “I know.”

[030-149] She paused a minute.

[030-150] “Have they found him?” she asked timidly.

[030-151] “No.”

[030-152] “Then how do you know about _it_?” she asked again, hardly audibly and

[030-153] again after a minute’s pause.

[030-154] He turned to her and looked very intently at her.

[030-155] “Guess,” he said, with the same distorted helpless smile.

[030-156] A shudder passed over her.

[030-157] “But you... why do you frighten me like this?” she said, smiling like a

[030-158] child.

[030-159] “I must be a great friend of _his_... since I know,” Raskolnikov went

[030-160] on, still gazing into her face, as though he could not turn his eyes

[030-161] away. “He... did not mean to kill that Lizaveta... he... killed her

[030-162] accidentally.... He meant to kill the old woman when she was alone and

[030-163] he went there... and then Lizaveta came in... he killed her too.”

[030-164] Another awful moment passed. Both still gazed at one another.

[030-165] “You can’t guess, then?” he asked suddenly, feeling as though he were

[030-166] flinging himself down from a steeple.

[030-167] “N-no...” whispered Sonia.

[030-168] “Take a good look.”

[030-169] As soon as he had said this again, the same familiar sensation froze his

[030-170] heart. He looked at her and all at once seemed to see in her face the

[030-176] holding out their little hands on the point of crying. Almost the same

[030-181] even more immovably on him. Her terror infected him. The same fear

[030-182] showed itself on his face. In the same way he stared at her and almost

[030-183] with the same _childish_ smile.

[030-184] “Have you guessed?” he whispered at last.

[030-185] “Good God!” broke in an awful wail from her bosom.

[030-186] She sank helplessly on the bed with her face in the pillows, but a

[030-187] moment later she got up, moved quickly to him, seized both his hands

[030-188] and, gripping them tight in her thin fingers, began looking into his

[030-189] face again with the same intent stare. In this last desperate look she

[030-190] tried to look into him and catch some last hope. But there was no hope;

[030-191] there was no doubt remaining; it was all true! Later on, indeed, when

[030-192] she recalled that moment, she thought it strange and wondered why she

[030-193] had seen at once that there was no doubt. She could not have said, for

[030-194] instance, that she had foreseen something of the sort--and yet now, as

[030-195] soon as he told her, she suddenly fancied that she had really foreseen

[030-196] this very thing.

[030-197] “Stop, Sonia, enough! don’t torture me,” he begged her miserably.

[030-198] It was not at all, not at all like this he had thought of telling her,

[030-199] but this is how it happened.

[030-200] She jumped up, seeming not to know what she was doing, and, wringing her

[030-201] hands, walked into the middle of the room; but quickly went back and sat

[030-202] down again beside him, her shoulder almost touching his. All of a sudden

[030-203] she started as though she had been stabbed, uttered a cry and fell on

[030-204] her knees before him, she did not know why.

[030-205] “What have you done--what have you done to yourself?” she said in

[030-206] despair, and, jumping up, she flung herself on his neck, threw her arms

[030-207] round him, and held him tightly.

[030-208] Raskolnikov drew back and looked at her with a mournful smile.

[030-209] “You are a strange girl, Sonia--you kiss me and hug me when I tell you

[030-210] about that.... You don’t think what you are doing.”

[030-211] “There is no one--no one in the whole world now so unhappy as you!” she

[030-212] cried in a frenzy, not hearing what he said, and she suddenly broke into

[030-213] violent hysterical weeping.

[030-214] A feeling long unfamiliar to him flooded his heart and softened it at

[030-215] once. He did not struggle against it. Two tears started into his eyes

[030-216] and hung on his eyelashes.

[030-217] “Then you won’t leave me, Sonia?” he said, looking at her almost with

[030-218] hope.

[030-219] “No, no, never, nowhere!” cried Sonia. “I will follow you, I will follow

[030-220] you everywhere. Oh, my God! Oh, how miserable I am!... Why, why didn’t I

[030-222] “Here I have come.”

[030-223] “Yes, now! What’s to be done now?... Together, together!” she repeated

[030-224] as it were unconsciously, and she hugged him again. “I’ll follow you to

[030-225] Siberia!”

[030-226] He recoiled at this, and the same hostile, almost haughty smile came to

[030-227] his lips.

[030-228] “Perhaps I don’t want to go to Siberia yet, Sonia,” he said.

[030-229] Sonia looked at him quickly.

[030-239] it mean?”

[030-240] “Oh, well--to plunder. Leave off, Sonia,” he answered wearily, almost

[030-241] with vexation.

[030-242] Sonia stood as though struck dumb, but suddenly she cried:

[030-243] “You were hungry! It was... to help your mother? Yes?”

[030-244] “No, Sonia, no,” he muttered, turning away and hanging his head. “I was

[030-245] not so hungry.... I certainly did want to help my mother, but... that’s

[030-246] not the real thing either.... Don’t torture me, Sonia.”

[030-247] Sonia clasped her hands.

[030-248] “Could it, could it all be true? Good God, what a truth! Who could

[030-281] “I asked you to go with me yesterday because you are all I have left.”

[030-283] “Not to steal and not to murder, don’t be anxious,” he smiled bitterly.

[030-288] Sonia?”

[030-289] She squeezed his hand.

[030-290] “And why, why did I tell her? Why did I let her know?” he cried a minute

[030-298] “But aren’t you suffering, too?” cried Sonia.

[030-299] Again a wave of the same feeling surged into his heart, and again for an

[030-308] “No, no, it was a good thing you came,” cried Sonia. “It’s better I

[030-338] “You had better tell me straight out... without examples,” she begged,

[030-366] one... no, that’s not right, not right.”

[030-367] “You see yourself that it’s not right. But I’ve spoken truly, it’s the

[030-369] “As though that could be the truth! Good God!”
