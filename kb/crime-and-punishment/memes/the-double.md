# The Double

A structural and psychological device in which secondary characters function as externalized versions of the protagonist's competing selves — each double embodying a path Raskolnikov might take, a dimension of his psyche made visible and autonomous.

## Definition

The double (Russian: *dvoinik*) is a recurring Dostoevskian technique by which a character encounters figures who represent aspects of their own personality made external and independent. Unlike simple foils, doubles are uncanny — they mirror, distort, and challenge the protagonist, often producing intense attraction and revulsion simultaneously. In *Crime and Punishment*, Raskolnikov is surrounded by doubles who each embody a possible resolution of his internal conflict. The device allows Dostoevsky to dramatize internal psychological struggle as external encounter, giving narrative form to Raskolnikov's divided consciousness.

## Expression in the Novel

Svidrigailov is the most striking double: he has committed crimes (or is rumored to have) without apparent guilt, lives entirely for sensual gratification, and regards Raskolnikov's torment with knowing amusement — he is Raskolnikov's theory lived out without conscience. Their conversations carry an eerie intimacy, as if each recognizes something of himself in the other. When Svidrigailov tells Raskolnikov "we are birds of a feather" (PN 004-121), he identifies the essential kinship: both have crossed a moral boundary, and the question is only whether conscience can be silenced or must be heard.

Razumihin is the healthy double, representing the life of work, friendship, and ordinary decency that Raskolnikov abandoned. His practical energy, his loyalty to Dounia and Pulcheria Alexandrovna, and his unashamed enjoyment of human connection make him the living refutation of Raskolnikov's isolation. Notably, Raskolnikov repeatedly seeks Razumihin out even as he pushes him away — drawn to what he has lost.

Luzhin is the degraded double — the rational egoism Raskolnikov theorized, reduced to petty financial calculation. Raskolnikov's visceral hatred of Luzhin is partly hatred of his own ideas made vulgar and visible. And Sonia, though rarely discussed as a double, functions as the redemptive possibility — the self Raskolnikov cannot yet become, the one who accepts suffering and remains in community.

## Key Passages

> "We are birds of a feather" — Svidrigailov to Raskolnikov, recognizing their kinship (PN 004-121)

> "There was something in him that resembled Raskolnikov" — the narrator's observation of the uncanny similarity (PN 004-098)

> "In spite of all the fastidiousness of youth, he minded his rags least of all" — Raskolnikov's perverse identification with degradation, mirrored in Svidrigailov's amorality (PN 001-065)

## Character Perspectives

- **Arkady Svidrigailov**: The dark double. He has crossed the line Raskolnikov only approached, and his suicide is the novel's warning of where Raskolnikov's path leads without confession. His final dreams — of a mouse, a drowned girl, a corrupted child — represent the terminus of a consciousness that has extinguished moral feeling.
- **Dmitri Razumihin**: The healthy double. His name (*razum* = reason) suggests what Raskolnikov lacks: reason grounded in life rather than abstraction. He is the novel's demonstration that intelligence and practicality need not entail isolation.
- **Pyotr Luzhin**: The debased double. Raskolnikov despises him because Luzhin's petty rationalism is a vulgar mirror of his own intellectual pride. Luzhin's exposure at the memorial dinner (framing Sonia for theft) is the novel's judgment on rational egoism in practice.
- **Sonia Marmeladov**: The redemptive double. Where Raskolnikov isolates himself through transgression, Sonia bears shame and remains in love. She is the self he cannot yet be — and his gradual movement toward her is the novel's moral arc.

## Historical & Philosophical Context

Dostoevsky had explored the double motif earlier in his 1846 novella *The Double*, where the protagonist Golyadkin encounters his exact duplicate. In *Crime and Punishment*, the technique is refined: the doubles are not literal duplicates but thematic mirrors, each illuminating a different facet of the protagonist's moral crisis. The device influenced later psychological fiction and anticipates Freud's concept of the *Unheimliche* (the uncanny) — the familiar made strange and threatening through slight distortion.
