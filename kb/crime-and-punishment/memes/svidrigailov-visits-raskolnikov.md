# Svidrigailov's First Visit to Raskolnikov

In Part 4, Chapter 1, [[arkady-svidrigailov|Arkady Ivanovitch Svidrigailov]] appears unannounced in [[raskolnikovs-garret|Raskolnikov's garret]], materializing like a figure from a dream — a moment that marks the novel's introduction of Raskolnikov's most disturbing double and sets in motion the subplot that will culminate in Svidrigailov's suicide.

## Summary

The visit occurs shortly after [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s tense meeting with his mother and sister. Having just dismissed [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]], Raskolnikov is alone when his door opens and a stranger enters — a man "whom he had never seen stood in the doorway watching him intently" (020-348). Raskolnikov initially believes he is still dreaming. The stranger identifies himself as [[arkady-svidrigailov|Arkady Ivanovitch Svidrigailov]] (020-369) and calmly takes a seat, launching into a monologue that oscillates between concern for [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s welfare, memories of his late wife [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]], and unsettling philosophical digressions.

## Context & Buildup

The visit is preceded by several key developments. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has just received his mother's letter (Part 1, Chapter 3) describing Svidrigailov's harassment of [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] while she worked as a governess in his household (004-020). [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]]'s recent death under suspicious circumstances — and Svidrigailov's subsequent arrival in [[st-petersburg]] — adds an ominous cast to his sudden appearance. Raskolnikov already harbors "an almost physical loathing" for the man, having never met him but knowing intimately what he represents: the predatory, amoral sensuality that his own theory of the extraordinary man might, in its darkest form, justify.

The chapter opens with Raskolnikov in a state of psychological exhaustion after the emotional confrontations with his mother, [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]], and [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]]. He is lying on his sofa, half-dreaming, when the door opens — blurring the boundary between dream and reality in a way that characterizes the entire encounter (020-336).

## The Encounter

Svidrigailov enters with an almost theatrical composure. He steps "cautiously into the room, carefully closing the door after him" (020-355), and introduces himself with a calm laugh (020-369). Raskolnikov's initial reaction — "Can this be still a dream?" (021-001) — is never fully resolved; Dostoevsky maintains a dreamlike atmosphere throughout the scene.

The conversation covers several topics in Svidrigailov's characteristically meandering style:

- **Marfa Petrovna's death**: Svidrigailov dismisses accusations of foul play with unnerving calm, noting that Marfa Petrovna "was very likely pleased to die" after a three-day drinking binge (021-064). He mentions that [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]] has appeared to him as a ghost — three times (022-363).

- **Dounia**: Svidrigailov declares his intention to prevent [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s marriage to [[pyotr-luzhin|Luzhin]], offering her ten thousand roubles as compensation for the harm he caused her — a gesture that mingles apparent generosity with unsettling possessiveness (022-111).

- **Philosophical musings**: Svidrigailov speaks of St. Petersburg as "a town of half-crazy people" (021-140) and of his own capacity for boredom and sensation-seeking, hinting at the nihilistic ennui that drives him.

## Immediate Aftermath

After Svidrigailov's departure, Raskolnikov is shaken. The visit confirms the arrival of a figure who mirrors his own transgressions — a man who has crossed moral boundaries without the theoretical justification Raskolnikov claims. Svidrigailov's calm indifference to his wife's death, his ghostly visitations, and his predatory interest in [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] all establish him as Raskolnikov's dark double: what Raskolnikov might become if his [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary-man theory]] were pursued to its logical, amoral conclusion.

## Long-Term Consequences

This visit initiates the Svidrigailov subplot that runs through the remainder of the novel. Svidrigailov will later eavesdrop on Raskolnikov's [[raskolnikov-confesses-to-sonia|confession to Sonia]], use this knowledge to manipulate [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]], and ultimately — after Dounia definitively rejects him — take his own life in [[svidrigailovs-suicide|a dawn suicide]]. The visit also plants the first explicit suggestion that Raskolnikov's secret is known to others, intensifying the psychological pressure that drives him toward confession.

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

Svidrigailov's entry into the novel marks the introduction of the [[the-double|double]] motif in its most explicit form. Where [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] pursues Raskolnikov through legal and psychological means, Svidrigailov operates outside all frameworks — legal, moral, and even metaphysical. He is Raskolnikov's possible future: a man who has committed terrible acts and feels no need to justify them through theory. The dreamlike quality of their first meeting underscores the uncanny nature of the double — Svidrigailov seems less a separate person than an emanation of Raskolnikov's own guilt, made flesh.

- **[[arkady-svidrigailov|Arkady Svidrigailov]]** — the unexpected visitor, calm, unsettling, bearing news and veiled threats (020-369)
