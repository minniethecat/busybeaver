# Luzhin Frames Sonia

The frame-up of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]] by [[luzhin|Pyotr Petrovitch Luzhin]] occurs at the [[marmeladovs-memorial-dinner|Marmeladov memorial dinner]] in Part 5, Chapter 3. It is one of the novel's most dramatically charged scenes: Luzhin, the calculating suitor whose engagement to [[dounia|Dounia]] has been broken, attempts to destroy [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s reputation by planting a hundred-rouble note in her pocket and publicly accusing her of theft. The scheme is exposed by [[andrey-lebeziatnikov|Andrey Lebeziatnikov]], who witnessed Luzhin slip the note into [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s pocket. The episode reveals Luzhin's moral bankruptcy while paradoxically strengthening the bond between [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], who immediately recognizes the frame-up as an attack on himself through the woman he has come to trust above all others.

## Summary

After being ejected from [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s apartment and losing his engagement to [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]], Luzhin seeks revenge by discrediting [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] — knowing [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s attachment to her. At the chaotic memorial dinner for [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]], Luzhin quietly slips a folded hundred-rouble note into [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s pocket, then publicly accuses her of stealing it. [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] furiously defends [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], snatching the ten-rouble donation note from [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s hand and flinging it into Luzhin's face (029-128). Just as [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] appears doomed, Lebeziatnikov steps forward to testify that he watched Luzhin secrete the hundred-rouble note into [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s pocket with his own hands (029-249). [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], who has been observing silently, delivers the definitive explanation of Luzhin's motive: the frame-up was designed to discredit [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] in order to wound [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] and regain leverage over [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] and [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]].

## Context & Buildup

Luzhin arrives in [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] as [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s wealthy suitor, embodying a philosophy of rational self-interest: "love yourself before all men, for everything in the world rests on self-interest" (004-099). His engagement to [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] unravels when [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] exposes Luzhin's manipulative character — particularly his attempt to slander [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and his insulting assumption that [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] should be grateful for his wealth.

After the engagement is broken, Luzhin nurses his wounded pride. He knows that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has given money to the [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] family and has become [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s protector. By publicly branding [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] a thief, Luzhin aims to prove that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s charitable impulses are misplaced — that the "fallen woman" he defends is precisely the criminal type Luzhin's worldview predicts. The memorial dinner provides the perfect stage: a crowded room of marginal characters, a grieving widow [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] already at the breaking point, and [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], the meek prostitute who is the family's sole financial support.

## Detailed Account

The scene unfolds at [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]'s funeral dinner for [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]. Luzhin arrives late, after the dinner has already descended into chaos — drunken guests, simmering class resentments, and [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]'s desperate attempts to maintain dignity (028-425). Luzhin makes his entrance with calculated solemnity.

He announces that a hundred-rouble note has disappeared from his room — "immediately after your visit I found that a hundred-rouble note was missing from my table" (029-034). He specifically names [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], whom he had summoned earlier to give a ten-rouble donation. Luzhin recounts the details: he had counted out three thousand roubles that morning, placed five hundred on the table including three hundred-rouble notes, and after [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s visit, one hundred-rouble note was gone (029-060–081).

[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] stands "deadly pale, staring at Luzhin and unable to say a word" (029-042). When Luzhin asks directly "And the hundred roubles you do not confess to taking?" (029-104), she can only protest her innocence. [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] erupts, snatching the ten-rouble note from [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s hand and flinging it into Luzhin's face, crying "Give me the ten roubles at once — here!" (029-126–128).

Luzhin calls for the police and demands that [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s pockets be searched. The assembled guests, primed by the chaotic atmosphere and their own social prejudices, begin to turn against [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] — the prostitute who "must" be a thief. Then Lebeziatnikov steps forward.

Lebeziatnikov, Luzhin's former ward and a self-styled progressive, delivers the decisive testimony: "I saw, I saw it!... I saw how he himself, with his own hands gave Sofya Semyonovna that hundred-rouble note — I saw it, I was a witness, I'll take my oath!" (029-249). He describes watching Luzhin take up the hundred-rouble note from the table, pretend to give [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] a ten-rouble donation, and secretly slip the hundred-rouble note into her pocket: "the other, the left, you slipped the note into her pocket" (029-260). The hundred-rouble note, folded in eight, is found in [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s pocket exactly as Lebeziatnikov described (029-170).

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], who has been observing quietly, now speaks. He explains Luzhin's motive: Luzhin wanted to prove that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s defense of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] was naive, to "quarrel me with my mother and sister" by showing that "the person whom I defend is a thief" (030-052). The frame-up was not about [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] at all — she was merely the instrument in Luzhin's campaign against [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]].

## Immediate Aftermath

Luzhin, exposed and humiliated, smiles contemptuously and retreats — "The thief has been more than unmasked" (029-424). He is driven from the room by the crowd's fury. [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], vindicated, collapses in relief and terror. [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]], her last reserves of dignity exhausted, takes her children and flees into the streets — an act of desperation that leads directly to her own death in the following chapter.

For [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], the event has a transformative effect. Watching [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s unjust persecution mirrors his own hidden guilt: she is publicly accused of a crime she did not commit while he remains unaccused of a crime he did. His defense of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] becomes a surrogate confession — a declaration, through her, of the injustice of false accusation that implicates his own unacknowledged truth.

## Long-Term Consequences

The frame-up episode solidifies [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s bond with [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]. After Luzhin's departure, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] goes to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and reveals that he knows Luzhin's true intentions. This conversation leads to a deeper intimacy between them — [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is drawn to the strength [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] displays under false accusation, a strength he lacks in the face of his real guilt.

The episode also marks the final destruction of Luzhin as a character of any moral standing. His exposure is total: even Lebeziatnikov, his ideological disciple, repudiates him. Luzhin disappears from the novel shortly after — his worldview of rational self-interest having been exposed as mere cruelty dressed in philosophical language. "Pyotr Petrovitch Luzhin, with all his virtues, was not worth Sofya Semyonovna's little finger" — the crowd's verdict stands as the narrative's own (029-368).

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The frame-up scene functions as a moral inversion of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s crime. Luzhin commits a calculated, bloodless crime — a lie designed to destroy a person's reputation — and is exposed through the testimony of a witness. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has committed a far greater crime — a double murder — and remains unexposed. The parallel is deliberate: Dostoevsky uses Luzhin's petty villainy to illuminate the magnitude of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s crime by contrast.

The scene also dramatizes the novel's recurring theme of false accusation and true guilt. [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], the innocent, is publicly branded a thief; [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], the murderer, watches in silence. This inversion — the innocent suffering for the guilty, the guilty watching the innocent suffer — anticipates the novel's Christian framework of redemptive suffering and [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s eventual confession.

Lebeziatnikov's role is also significant: the "progressive" ideologue, whose theories [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has mocked, performs the novel's single most morally decisive act — stepping forward to tell the truth and save an innocent person from ruin. His testimony demonstrates that moral action does not depend on correct ideology.

## Characters Involved

- **[[luzhin|Pyotr Petrovitch Luzhin]]** — The antagonist who frames [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] to destroy [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s credibility and regain [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]. His method — planting the note while pretending charity — reveals the cruelty beneath his philosophy of rational self-interest (029-034).
- **[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]]** — The victim of the frame-up. Her silent dignity under false accusation contrasts with [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s inability to endure even a passing mention of the murder (029-042).
- **[[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]** — [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s stepmother, who passionately defends her — snatching the note and flinging it at Luzhin's face — in one of the novel's most vivid acts of loyalty (029-127).
- **[[andrey-lebeziatnikov|Andrey Lebeziatnikov]]** — Luzhin's former ward, whose eyewitness testimony exposes the frame-up. His moral courage redeems his otherwise comical philosophizing (029-249).
- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]** — The silent observer who explains Luzhin's motive. His defense of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] is psychologically complex: he defends the innocent while himself being the guilty (030-052).
