[038-001] The same day, about seven o’clock in the evening, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] was on

[038-002] his way to his mother’s and sister’s lodging--the lodging in Bakaleyev’s

[038-003] house which [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] had found for them. The [[staircases|stairs]] went up from

[038-004] the street. Raskolnikov walked with lagging steps, as though still

[038-005] hesitating whether to go or not. But nothing would have turned him back:

[038-006] his decision was taken.

[038-007] “Besides, it doesn’t matter, they still know nothing,” he thought, “and

[038-008] they are used to thinking of me as eccentric.”

[038-009] He was appallingly dressed: his clothes torn and dirty, soaked with a

[038-010] night’s rain. His face was almost distorted from fatigue, exposure, the

[038-011] inward conflict that had lasted for twenty-four hours. He had spent all

[038-012] the previous night alone, God knows where. But anyway he had reached a

[038-013] decision.

[038-014] He knocked at the door which was opened by his mother. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] was not

[038-015] at home. Even the servant happened to be out. At first Pulcheria

[038-016] Alexandrovna was speechless with joy and surprise; then she took him by

[038-017] the hand and drew him into the room.

[038-018] “Here you are!” she began, faltering with joy. “Don’t be angry with

[038-019] me, Rodya, for welcoming you so foolishly with tears: I am laughing, not

[038-020] crying. Did you think I was crying? No, I am delighted, but I’ve got

[038-021] into such a stupid habit of shedding tears. I’ve been like that ever

[038-022] since your father’s death. I cry for anything. Sit down, dear boy, you

[038-023] must be tired; I see you are. Ah, how muddy you are.”

[038-024] “I was in the rain yesterday, mother....” Raskolnikov began.

[038-025] “No, no,” [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] hurriedly interrupted, “you thought I

[038-026] was going to cross-question you in the womanish way I used to; don’t be

[038-027] anxious, I understand, I understand it all: now I’ve learned the ways

[038-028] here and truly I see for myself that they are better. I’ve made up my

[038-029] mind once for all: how could I understand your plans and expect you to

[038-030] give an account of them? God knows what concerns and plans you may have,

[038-031] or what ideas you are hatching; so it’s not for me to keep nudging your

[038-032] elbow, asking you what you are thinking about? But, my goodness! why

[038-033] am I running to and fro as though I were crazy...? I am reading your

[038-034] article in the magazine for the third time, Rodya. Dmitri Prokofitch

[038-035] brought it to me. Directly I saw it I cried out to myself: ‘There,

[038-036] foolish one,’ I thought, ‘that’s what he is busy about; that’s the

[038-037] solution of the mystery! Learned people are always like that. He may

[038-038] have some new ideas in his head just now; he is thinking them over and I

[038-039] worry him and upset him.’ I read it, my dear, and of course there was a

[038-040] great deal I did not understand; but that’s only natural--how should I?”

[038-041] “Show me, mother.”

[038-042] Raskolnikov took the magazine and glanced at his article. Incongruous

[038-043] as it was with his mood and his circumstances, he felt that strange and

[038-044] bitter sweet sensation that every author experiences the first time he

[038-045] sees himself in print; besides, he was only twenty-three. It lasted only

[038-046] a moment. After reading a few lines he frowned and his heart throbbed

[038-047] with anguish. He recalled all the inward conflict of the preceding

[038-048] months. He flung the article on the table with disgust and anger.

[038-049] “But, however foolish I may be, Rodya, I can see for myself that you

[038-050] will very soon be one of the leading--if not the leading man--in the

[038-051] world of Russian thought. And they dared to think you were mad! You

[038-052] don’t know, but they really thought that. Ah, the despicable creatures,

[038-053] how could they understand genius! And Dounia, Dounia was all but

[038-054] believing it--what do you say to that? Your father sent twice to

[038-055] magazines--the first time poems (I’ve got the manuscript and will show

[038-056] you) and the second time a whole novel (I begged him to let me copy it

[038-057] out) and how we prayed that they should be taken--they weren’t! I was

[038-058] breaking my heart, Rodya, six or seven days ago over your food and your

[038-059] clothes and the way you are living. But now I see again how foolish

[038-060] I was, for you can attain any position you like by your intellect and

[038-061] talent. No doubt you don’t care about that for the present and you are

[038-062] occupied with much more important matters....”

[038-063] “Dounia’s not at home, mother?”

[038-064] “No, Rodya. I often don’t see her; she leaves me alone. Dmitri

[038-065] Prokofitch comes to see me, it’s so good of him, and he always talks

[038-066] about you. He loves you and respects you, my dear. I don’t say that

[038-067] Dounia is very wanting in consideration. I am not complaining. She has

[038-068] her ways and I have mine; she seems to have got some secrets of late and

[038-069] I never have any secrets from you two. Of course, I am sure that Dounia

[038-070] has far too much sense, and besides she loves you and me... but I don’t

[038-071] know what it will all lead to. You’ve made me so happy by coming now,

[038-072] Rodya, but she has missed you by going out; when she comes in I’ll tell

[038-073] her: ‘Your brother came in while you were out. Where have you been all

[038-074] this time?’ You mustn’t spoil me, Rodya, you know; come when you can,

[038-075] but if you can’t, it doesn’t matter, I can wait. I shall know, anyway,

[038-076] that you are fond of me, that will be enough for me. I shall read what

[038-077] you write, I shall hear about you from everyone, and sometimes you’ll

[038-078] come yourself to see me. What could be better? Here you’ve come now to

[038-079] comfort your mother, I see that.”

[038-080] Here Pulcheria Alexandrovna began to cry.

[038-081] “Here I am again! Don’t mind my foolishness. My goodness, why am I

[038-082] sitting here?” she cried, jumping up. “There is coffee and I don’t offer

[038-083] you any. Ah, that’s the selfishness of old age. I’ll get it at once!”

[038-084] “Mother, don’t trouble, I am going at once. I haven’t come for that.

[038-085] Please listen to me.”

[038-086] Pulcheria Alexandrovna went up to him timidly.

[038-087] “Mother, whatever happens, whatever you hear about me, whatever you are

[038-088] told about me, will you always love me as you do now?” he asked suddenly

[038-089] from the fullness of his heart, as though not thinking of his words and

[038-090] not weighing them.

[038-091] “Rodya, Rodya, what is the matter? How can you ask me such a question?

[038-092] Why, who will tell me anything about you? Besides, I shouldn’t believe

[038-093] anyone, I should refuse to listen.”

[038-094] “I’ve come to assure you that I’ve always loved you and I am glad

[038-095] that we are alone, even glad Dounia is out,” he went on with the same

[038-096] impulse. “I have come to tell you that though you will be unhappy, you

[038-097] must believe that your son loves you now more than himself, and that all

[038-098] you thought about me, that I was cruel and didn’t care about you, was

[038-099] all a mistake. I shall never cease to love you.... Well, that’s enough:

[038-100] I thought I must do this and begin with this....”

[038-101] Pulcheria Alexandrovna embraced him in silence, pressing him to her

[038-102] bosom and weeping gently.

[038-103] “I don’t know what is wrong with you, Rodya,” she said at last. “I’ve

[038-104] been thinking all this time that we were simply boring you and now I see

[038-105] that there is a great sorrow in store for you, and that’s why you are

[038-106] miserable. I’ve foreseen it a long time, Rodya. Forgive me for speaking

[038-107] about it. I keep thinking about it and lie awake at nights. Your sister

[038-108] lay talking in her sleep all last night, talking of nothing but you. I

[038-109] caught something, but I couldn’t make it out. I felt all the morning

[038-110] as though I were going to be hanged, waiting for something, expecting

[038-111] something, and now it has come! Rodya, Rodya, where are you going? You

[038-112] are going away somewhere?”

[038-113] “Yes.”

[038-114] “That’s what I thought! I can come with you, you know, if you need

[038-115] me. And Dounia, too; she loves you, she loves you dearly--and Sofya

[038-116] Semyonovna may come with us if you like. You see, I am glad to look upon

[038-117] her as a daughter even... Dmitri Prokofitch will help us to go together.

[038-118] But... where... are you going?”

[038-119] “Good-bye, mother.”

[038-120] “What, to-day?” she cried, as though losing him for ever.

[038-121] “I can’t stay, I must go now....”

[038-122] “And can’t I come with you?”

[038-123] “No, but kneel down and pray to God for me. Your prayer perhaps will

[038-124] reach Him.”

[038-125] “Let me bless you and sign you with the cross. That’s right, that’s

[038-126] right. Oh, God, what are we doing?”

[038-127] Yes, he was glad, he was very glad that there was no one there, that

[038-128] he was alone with his mother. For the first time after all those awful

[038-129] months his heart was softened. He fell down before her, he kissed her

[038-130] feet and both wept, embracing. And she was not surprised and did not

[038-131] question him this time. For some days she had realised that something

[038-132] awful was happening to her son and that now some terrible minute had

[038-133] come for him.

[038-134] “Rodya, my darling, my first born,” she said sobbing, “now you are just

[038-135] as when you were little. You would run like this to me and hug me and

[038-136] kiss me. When your father was living and we were poor, you comforted us

[038-137] simply by being with us and when I buried your father, how often we

[038-138] wept together at his grave and embraced, as now. And if I’ve been crying

[038-139] lately, it’s that my mother’s heart had a foreboding of trouble. The

[038-140] first time I saw you, that evening, you remember, as soon as we arrived

[038-141] here, I guessed simply from your eyes. My heart sank at once, and to-day

[038-142] when I opened the door and looked at you, I thought the fatal hour had

[038-143] come. Rodya, Rodya, you are not going away to-day?”

[038-144] “No!”

[038-145] “You’ll come again?”

[038-146] “Yes... I’ll come.”

[038-147] “Rodya, don’t be angry, I don’t dare to question you. I know I mustn’t.

[038-148] Only say two words to me--is it far where you are going?”

[038-149] “Very far.”

[038-150] “What is awaiting you there? Some post or career for you?”

[038-151] “What God sends... only pray for me.” Raskolnikov went to the door, but

[038-152] she clutched him and gazed despairingly into his eyes. Her face worked

[038-153] with terror.

[038-154] “Enough, mother,” said Raskolnikov, deeply regretting that he had come.

[038-155] “Not for ever, it’s not yet for ever? You’ll come, you’ll come

[038-156] to-morrow?”

[038-157] “I will, I will, good-bye.” He tore himself away at last.

[038-158] It was a warm, fresh, bright evening; it had cleared up in the morning.

[038-159] Raskolnikov went to his lodgings; he made haste. He wanted to finish all

[038-160] before sunset. He did not want to meet anyone till then. Going up the

[038-161] stairs he noticed that [[nastasya|Nastasya]] rushed from the samovar to watch him

[038-162] intently. “Can anyone have come to see me?” he wondered. He had a

[038-163] disgusted vision of [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]. But opening his door he saw Dounia. She

[038-164] was sitting alone, plunged in deep thought, and looked as though she had

[038-165] been waiting a long time. He stopped short in the doorway. She rose from

[038-166] the sofa in dismay and stood up facing him. Her eyes, fixed upon him,

[038-167] betrayed horror and infinite grief. And from those eyes alone he saw at

[038-168] once that she knew.

[038-169] “Am I to come in or go away?” he asked uncertainly.

[038-170] “I’ve been all day with Sofya Semyonovna. We were both waiting for you.

[038-171] We thought that you would be sure to come there.”

[038-172] Raskolnikov went into the room and sank exhausted on a chair.

[038-173] “I feel weak, Dounia, I am very tired; and I should have liked at this

[038-174] moment to be able to control myself.”

[038-175] He glanced at her mistrustfully.

[038-176] “Where were you all night?”

[038-177] “I don’t remember clearly. You see, sister, I wanted to make up my mind

[038-178] once for all, and several times I walked by [[neva-river|the Neva]], I remember that

[038-179] I wanted to end it all there, but... I couldn’t make up my mind,” he

[038-180] whispered, looking at her mistrustfully again.

[038-181] “Thank God! That was just what we were afraid of, Sofya Semyonovna and

[038-182] I. Then you still have faith in life? Thank God, thank God!”

[038-183] Raskolnikov smiled bitterly.

[038-184] “I haven’t faith, but I have just been weeping in mother’s arms; I

[038-185] haven’t faith, but I have just asked her to pray for me. I don’t know

[038-186] how it is, Dounia, I don’t understand it.”

[038-187] “Have you been at mother’s? Have you told her?” cried Dounia,

[038-188] horror-stricken. “Surely you haven’t done that?”

[038-189] “No, I didn’t tell her... in words; but she understood a great deal.

[038-190] She heard you talking in your sleep. I am sure she half understands it

[038-191] already. Perhaps I did wrong in going to see her. I don’t know why I did

[038-192] go. I am a contemptible person, Dounia.”

[038-193] “A contemptible person, but ready to face suffering! You are, aren’t

[038-194] you?”

[038-195] “Yes, I am going. At once. Yes, to escape the disgrace I thought of

[038-196] drowning myself, Dounia, but as I looked into the water, I thought that

[038-197] if I had considered myself strong till now I’d better not be afraid of

[038-198] disgrace,” he said, hurrying on. “It’s pride, Dounia.”

[038-199] “Pride, Rodya.”

[038-200] There was a gleam of fire in his lustreless eyes; he seemed to be glad

[038-201] to think that he was still proud.

[038-202] “You don’t think, sister, that I was simply afraid of the water?” he

[038-203] asked, looking into her face with a sinister smile.

[038-204] “Oh, Rodya, hush!” cried Dounia bitterly. Silence lasted for two

[038-205] minutes. He sat with his eyes fixed on the floor; Dounia stood at the

[038-206] other end of the table and looked at him with anguish. Suddenly he got

[038-207] up.

[038-208] “It’s late, it’s time to go! I am going at once to give myself up. But I

[038-209] don’t know why I am going to give myself up.”

[038-210] Big tears fell down her cheeks.

[038-211] “You are crying, sister, but can you hold out your hand to me?”

[038-212] “You doubted it?”

[038-213] She threw her arms round him.

[038-214] “Aren’t you half expiating your crime by facing the suffering?” she

[038-215] cried, holding him close and kissing him.

[038-216] “Crime? What crime?” he cried in sudden fury. “That I killed a vile

[038-217] noxious insect, an old pawnbroker woman, of use to no one!... Killing

[038-218] her was atonement for forty sins. She was sucking the life out of poor

[038-219] people. Was that a crime? I am not thinking of it and I am not thinking

[038-220] of expiating it, and why are you all rubbing it in on all sides? ‘A

[038-221] crime! a crime!’ Only now I see clearly the imbecility of my cowardice,

[038-222] now that I have decided to face this superfluous disgrace. It’s simply

[038-223] because I am contemptible and have nothing in me that I have decided to,

[038-224] perhaps too for my advantage, as that... Porfiry... suggested!”

[038-225] “Brother, brother, what are you saying? Why, you have shed blood?” cried

[038-226] Dounia in despair.

[038-227] “Which all men shed,” he put in almost frantically, “which flows and has

[038-228] always flowed in streams, which is spilt like champagne, and for which

[038-229] men are crowned in the Capitol and are called afterwards benefactors of

[038-230] mankind. Look into it more carefully and understand it! I too wanted to

[038-231] do good to men and would have done hundreds, thousands of good deeds

[038-232] to make up for that one piece of stupidity, not stupidity even, simply

[038-233] clumsiness, for the idea was by no means so stupid as it seems now

[038-234] that it has failed.... (Everything seems stupid when it fails.) By that

[038-235] stupidity I only wanted to put myself into an independent position, to

[038-236] take the first step, to obtain means, and then everything would have

[038-237] been smoothed over by benefits immeasurable in comparison.... But I...

[038-238] I couldn’t carry out even the first step, because I am contemptible,

[038-239] that’s what’s the matter! And yet I won’t look at it as you do. If I had

[038-240] succeeded I should have been crowned with glory, but now I’m trapped.”

[038-241] “But that’s not so, not so! Brother, what are you saying?”

[038-242] “Ah, it’s not picturesque, not æsthetically attractive! I fail to

[038-243] understand why bombarding people by regular siege is more honourable.

[038-244] The [[fear-and-dread|fear]] of appearances is the first symptom of impotence. I’ve never,

[038-245] never recognised this more clearly than now, and I am further than ever

[038-246] from seeing that what I did was a crime. I’ve never, never been stronger

[038-247] and more convinced than now.”

[038-248] The colour had rushed into his pale exhausted face, but as he uttered

[038-249] his last explanation, he happened to meet Dounia’s eyes and he saw such

[038-250] anguish in them that he could not help being checked. He felt that he

[038-251] had, anyway, made these two poor women miserable, that he was, anyway,

[038-252] the cause...

[038-253] “Dounia darling, if I am guilty forgive me (though I cannot be forgiven

[038-254] if I am guilty). Good-bye! We won’t dispute. It’s time, high time to go.

[038-255] Don’t follow me, I beseech you, I have somewhere else to go.... But you

[038-256] go at once and sit with mother. I entreat you to! It’s my last request

[038-257] of you. Don’t leave her at all; I left her in a state of anxiety, that

[038-258] she is not fit to bear; she will die or go out of her mind. Be with

[038-259] her! Razumihin will be with you. I’ve been talking to him.... Don’t cry

[038-260] about me: I’ll try to be honest and manly all my life, even if I am a

[038-261] murderer. Perhaps I shall some day make a name. I won’t disgrace you,

[038-262] you will see; I’ll still show.... Now good-bye for the present,” he

[038-263] concluded hurriedly, noticing again a strange expression in Dounia’s

[038-264] eyes at his last words and promises. “Why are you crying? Don’t cry,

[038-265] don’t cry: we are not parting for ever! Ah, yes! Wait a minute, I’d

[038-266] forgotten!”

[038-267] He went to the table, took up a thick dusty book, opened it and took

[038-268] from between the pages a little water-colour portrait on ivory. It was

[038-269] the portrait of his landlady’s daughter, who had died of [[illness-and-delirium|fever]], that

[038-270] strange girl who had wanted to be a nun. For a minute he gazed at the

[038-271] delicate expressive face of his betrothed, kissed the portrait and gave

[038-272] it to Dounia.

[038-273] “I used to talk a great deal about it to her, only to her,” he said

[038-274] thoughtfully. “To her heart I confided much of what has since been so

[038-275] hideously realised. Don’t be uneasy,” he returned to Dounia, “she was

[038-276] as much opposed to it as you, and I am glad that she is gone. The great

[038-277] point is that everything now is going to be different, is going to

[038-278] be broken in two,” he cried, suddenly returning to his dejection.

[038-279] “Everything, everything, and am I prepared for it? Do I want it myself?

[038-280] They say it is necessary for me to suffer! What’s the object of these

[038-281] senseless sufferings? shall I know any better what they are for, when I

[038-282] am crushed by hardships and idiocy, and weak as an old man after twenty

[038-283] years’ penal servitude? And what shall I have to live for then? Why am I

[038-284] consenting to that life now? Oh, I knew I was contemptible when I stood

[038-285] looking at the Neva at daybreak to-day!”

[038-286] At last they both went out. It was hard for Dounia, but she loved him.

[038-287] She walked away, but after going fifty paces she turned round to look

[038-288] at him again. He was still in sight. At the corner he too turned and for

[038-289] the last time their eyes met; but noticing that she was looking at him,

[038-290] he motioned her away with impatience and even vexation, and turned the

[038-291] corner abruptly.

[038-292] “I am wicked, I see that,” he thought to himself, feeling ashamed a

[038-293] moment later of his angry gesture to Dounia. “But why are they so fond

[038-294] of me if I don’t deserve it? Oh, if only I were alone and no one loved

[038-295] me and I too had never loved anyone! _Nothing of all this would have

[038-296] happened._ But I wonder shall I in those fifteen or twenty years grow so

[038-297] meek that I shall humble myself before people and whimper at every word

[038-298] that I am a criminal? Yes, that’s it, that’s it, that’s what they are

[038-299] sending me there for, that’s what they want. Look at them running to and

[038-300] fro about the streets, every one of them a scoundrel and a criminal at

[038-301] heart and, worse still, an idiot. But try to get me off and they’d be

[038-302] wild with righteous indignation. Oh, how I hate them all!”

[038-303] He fell to musing by what process it could come to pass, that he could

[038-304] be humbled before all of them, indiscriminately--humbled by conviction.

[038-305] And yet why not? It must be so. Would not twenty years of continual

[038-306] bondage crush him utterly? Water wears out a stone. And why, why should

[038-307] he live after that? Why should he go now when he knew that it would be

[038-308] so? It was the hundredth time perhaps that he had asked himself that

[038-309] question since the previous evening, but still he went.
