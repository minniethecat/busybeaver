## Overview

The Yusupov Garden (Юсуповский сад) is a real public garden in St. Petersburg, located between the Haymarket and the Fontanka Canal. In *Crime and Punishment*, it appears in several pivotal moments, including the backstory of [[marfa-petrovna]]'s persecution of [[dounia-raskolnikov]], Raskolnikov's walking reveries, and [[arkady-svidrigailov]]'s meandering final evening.

## The Garden in the Novel

### The Dounia Incident

The garden is the site of the confrontation between Marfa Petrovna and Dounia that precipitates Dounia's departure from the Svidrigailov household:

> Marfa Petrovna came upon her in the garden and slapped her face. [003-175]

This scene, recounted by Pulcheria Alexandrovna in a letter to Raskolnikov, establishes the garden as a space of exposure and humiliation. Following the public accusation, Marfa Petrovna drives around the town denouncing Dounia — a campaign of shame that Raskolnikov's family hears about in their provincial town.

### Marfa Petrovna's Letter

Later, when Marfa Petrovna discovers Dounia's innocence, she publicly recants in the same space:

> She read the letter in the garden to all the town. [003-210]

The garden thus becomes the stage for both accusation and exoneration, functions that underline the public character of the space.

### Raskolnikov's Wanderings

Raskolnikov passes through gardens during his fevered walks across Petersburg. He reflects on the contrast between the squalid reality of the city and the ideal of public spaces:

> There are no gardens nor fountains; where there is dirt and stench and all sorts of vileness. [006-364]

This reflection occurs as he contemplates the Mihailovsky Palace and its gardens, contrasting the aesthetic ideal with Petersburg's actual condition.

### Svidrigailov's Final Evening

Svidrigailov's last hours include a visit to the Yusupov Garden:

> He was going to the Yusupov garden and then to the Palais de Cristal. [011-033]

Later, he returns to a garden-like setting — a "kitchen garden" — where he sits through a thunderstorm before taking shelter in the [[crystal-palace-restaurant]].

## Significance

The Yusupov Garden belongs to the network of public spaces in Dostoevsky's [[st-petersburg]] where private moral dramas become public spectacles. Like the [[hay-market]], it functions as a space of exposure, but its association with leisure and beauty creates a sharper ironic contrast when it hosts scenes of violence, shame, and despair. The garden motif extends into Svidrigailov's final dream of a "charming flower garden" — a vision of paradise immediately undercut by the nightmare of the dead child that follows [037-290].
