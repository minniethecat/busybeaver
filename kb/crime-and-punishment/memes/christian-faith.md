# Christian Faith

Dostoevsky's Christianity is not doctrinal but existential — faith as the only answer to the nihilism and rational egoism the novel dissects. Sonia's simple, unwavering faith stands as the moral counterforce to Raskolnikov's theory, and the novel's most charged scene — the reading of the raising of Lazarus — places the Gospel at the narrative's center. The novel's entire arc, from murder through confession to the promise of resurrection, traces the shape of a Christian penitential journey.

## Definition

Faith in the novel operates on multiple levels: as Sonia's lived conviction that "there must be righteousness" (024-119); as the content of the Lazarus story (John 11), which Sonia reads aloud at Raskolnikov's demand; and as the condition of spiritual renewal that Raskolnikov can access only after confession and suffering. Faith is presented not as intellectual assent but as a way of being — Sonia "has such faith, such faith" (024-148) that it transforms those around her.

Crucially, Dostoevsky never presents faith as easy. Sonia's faith coexists with her degradation as a prostitute; she clings to God while the world calls her sinner. This paradox — the saintly prostitute, the believing sinner — is at the heart of Dostoevsky's Orthodox vision, where grace penetrates the most fallen circumstances.

## Expression in the Novel

### The Lazarus Reading

The Lazarus reading (Part 4, Chapter 4) is the novel's theological center. Raskolnikov demands that Sonia read him "the raising of Lazarus" (024-354). She reads, trembling, through the entire passage: "Jesus said unto her, I am the resurrection and the life: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live" (024-425). The scene functions as both challenge and prophecy — Raskolnikov, spiritually dead, is being offered resurrection.

The physical book Sonia reads from is itself charged with meaning: it is a Russian translation of the New Testament given to her by [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]], the woman Raskolnikov murdered. "It was given to me by Lizaveta, the one who was murdered," Sonia whispers (024-300). The Gospel thus becomes a material link between murderer and victim, between crime and the possibility of redemption.

### The Cypress Cross

After the confession, Sonia gives Raskolnikov a cypress cross that belonged to Lizaveta (030-156). She keeps Lizaveta's copper cross for herself and gives him the cypress one. This exchange of crosses — Sonia wearing the murdered woman's cross, Raskolnikov wearing the other — is a profound symbolic act. It binds the three of them in a shared economy of sin and grace, and it marks the moment Raskolnikov begins to accept, however reluctantly, the burden of Christian repentance: "Take it, it is my cross. We will bear it together" (030-158).

### The Epilogue: Resurrection

In the Epilogue, Sonia follows Raskolnikov to Siberia. During his illness, "the raising of Lazarus" is read to him again (039-854), and at the novel's close, Raskolnikov reaches for the same New Testament from under his pillow: "Can her beliefs not be my beliefs now?" (039-874). The novel closes with the promise of "a full resurrection into a new life" (039-828) — a statement that is both narrative resolution (Raskolnikov will return to life after his prison term) and theological claim (spiritual rebirth is possible for anyone).

## The Marmeladov Confession

Sonia's faith is prefigured in her father's drunken confession at the tavern (Part 1, Chapter 2). Marmeladov, despite his degradation, insists that God will forgive even him: "And He will judge and forgive all, the good and the evil, the wise and the humble.... And He will hold out His arms to us and we shall fall at His feet and weep" (002-430). This vision of universal forgiveness, spoken by a drunkard in a filthy tavern, establishes the novel's theological horizon — mercy is not reserved for the righteous.

## Character Perspectives

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Faith as identity — her religious conviction is inseparable from her capacity for love and sacrifice. She does not argue for faith; she embodies it. Her simple question to Raskolnikov — "What would I be without God?" (024-120) — is the novel's most succinct theological statement.

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His demand that Sonia read Lazarus reveals an unconscious desire for the very faith his intellect rejects. His conversion in the Epilogue is the novel's answer to his own theory: the extraordinary man cannot save himself; only grace can.

- **Semyon Marmeladov**: The drunken clerk whose vision of universal forgiveness (002-430) provides the novel's most radical theological moment — God's mercy extends even to those who have utterly failed.

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: The detective who, in urging Raskolnikov to "find faith, and life will be given back to you" (028-312), speaks with the voice of Dostoevsky's own conviction that law alone cannot heal.
