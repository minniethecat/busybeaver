The motif of resurrection runs through *Crime and Punishment* as both a theological promise and a narrative structure. The novel moves from death (the murder) through a prolonged period of spiritual entombment (Raskolnikov's isolation, fever, and torment) to a tentative rebirth (the epilogue's "gradual renewal"). This arc is explicitly framed by the story of Lazarus, whom Jesus raised from the dead after four days in the tomb — a story that Sonia reads aloud to Raskolnikov at his request, in one of the novel's most charged scenes (PN 027-039).

## The Lazarus Reading

The reading of the Lazarus story (John 11:1–44) in Part 4, Chapter 4 is the novel's symbolic center. Raskolnikov, who has been spiritually dead since the murder, asks Sonia to read him "the raising of Lazarus" (PN 027-032). The choice is not arbitrary: Lazarus was in the tomb for four days, and Raskolnikov has been in his moral tomb for a comparable period. Sonia's reading, her voice trembling with conviction that the miracle is real, creates a moment of shared anticipation — will there be a resurrection for Raskolnikov as well? "The candle-end was flickering out in the battered candlestick, dimly lighting up in the poverty-stricken room the murderer and the harlot who had so strangely come together over the reading of the eternal book" (PN 027-092).

## Death and Rebirth Symbolism

Beyond the explicit Lazarus reference, the novel is saturated with death-and-rebirth imagery. Raskolnikov's garret is repeatedly described as a coffin or tomb (PN 001-003). His periods of fever and unconsciousness function as symbolic deaths from which he awakens into altered states. The cross that Sonia gives him — a cypress-wood cross that had belonged to Lizaveta (PN 030-498) — is simultaneously an instrument of death and a promise of resurrection. Even the novel's physical movement, from the cramped, airless rooms of St. Petersburg to the "wide, open space" of Siberia in the epilogue (PN 039-028), maps the passage from entombment to liberation.

## The Epilogue's "Gradual Renewal"

The novel's final pages describe Raskolnikov's conversion not as a dramatic event but as a gradual process — "the gradual renewal of a man, the story of his gradual regeneration, his gradual transition from one world to another" (PN 039-397). This gradualism is theologically significant: Dostoevsky rejects the idea of instantaneous conversion in favor of an Orthodox understanding of *theosis* — the slow, painful process of becoming holy through suffering. Raskolnikov's resurrection is not the cancellation of his past but its transfiguration through love.

## Resurrection and the City

The motif of resurrection is spatial as well as temporal. The novel's St. Petersburg is a city of the dead — a place of coffins, cramped rooms, and suffocating heat. The epilogue's Siberian setting, with its "wide landscape" and "bright sunshine" (PN 039-042), represents a geographical resurrection: the passage from the tomb-city to the living land. When Raskolnikov falls at Sonia's feet on the riverbank and realizes that "life had stepped into the place of theory" (PN 039-639), the novel's spatial and spiritual arcs converge.
