[037-001] He spent that evening till ten o’clock going from one low haunt to

[037-002] another. Katia too turned up and sang another gutter song, how a certain

[037-003] “villain and tyrant”

[037-004] “began kissing Katia.”

[037-005] Svidrigaïlov treated Katia and the organ-grinder and some singers and

[037-006] the waiters and two little clerks. He was particularly drawn to these

[037-007] clerks by the fact that they both had crooked noses, one bent to the

[037-008] left and the other to the right. They took him finally to a pleasure

[037-009] garden, where he paid for their entrance. There was one lanky

[037-010] three-year-old pine-tree and three bushes in the garden, besides a

[037-011] “Vauxhall,” which was in reality a drinking-bar where tea too was

[037-012] served, and there were a few green tables and chairs standing round it.

[037-013] A chorus of wretched singers and a drunken but exceedingly depressed

[037-014] German clown from Munich with a red nose entertained the public. The

[037-015] clerks quarrelled with some other clerks and a fight seemed imminent.

[037-016] Svidrigaïlov was chosen to decide the dispute. He listened to them for

[037-017] a quarter of an hour, but they shouted so loud that there was no

[037-018] possibility of understanding them. The only fact that seemed certain was

[037-019] that one of them had stolen something and had even succeeded in

[037-020] selling it on the spot to a Jew, but would not share the spoil with his

[037-021] companion. Finally it appeared that the stolen object was a teaspoon

[037-022] belonging to the Vauxhall. It was missed and the affair began to seem

[037-023] troublesome. Svidrigaïlov paid for the spoon, got up, and walked out of

[037-024] the garden. It was about six o’clock. He had not drunk a drop of wine

[037-025] all this time and had ordered tea more for the sake of appearances than

[037-026] anything.

[037-027] It was a dark and stifling evening. Threatening storm-clouds came over

[037-028] the sky about ten o’clock. There was a clap of thunder, and the rain

[037-029] came down like a waterfall. The water fell not in drops, but beat on the

[037-030] earth in streams. There were flashes of lightning every minute and each

[037-031] flash lasted while one could count five.

[037-032] Drenched to the skin, he went home, locked himself in, opened the

[037-033] bureau, took out all his money and tore up two or three papers. Then,

[037-034] putting the money in his pocket, he was about to change his clothes,

[037-035] but, looking out of the window and listening to the thunder and the

[037-036] rain, he gave up the idea, took up his hat and went out of the room

[037-037] without locking the door. He went straight to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]. She was at home.

[037-038] She was not alone: the four Kapernaumov children were with her. She

[037-039] was giving them tea. She received Svidrigaïlov in respectful silence,

[037-040] looking wonderingly at his soaking clothes. The children all ran away at

[037-041] once in indescribable terror.

[037-042] Svidrigaïlov sat down at the table and asked Sonia to sit beside him.

[037-043] She timidly prepared to listen.

[037-045] as I am probably seeing you for the last time, I have come to make some

[037-046] arrangements. Well, did you see the lady to-day? I know what she said to

[037-047] you, you need not tell me.” (Sonia made a movement and blushed.) “Those

[037-048] people have their own way of doing things. As to your sisters and your

[037-049] brother, they are really provided for and the money assigned to them

[037-050] I’ve put into safe keeping and have received acknowledgments. You had

[037-051] better take charge of the receipts, in case anything happens. Here, take

[037-052] them! Well now, that’s settled. Here are three 5-per-cent bonds to the

[037-053] value of three thousand roubles. Take those for yourself, entirely for

[037-054] yourself, and let that be strictly between ourselves, so that no one

[037-055] knows of it, whatever you hear. You will need the money, for to go on

[037-056] living in the old way, Sofya Semyonovna, is bad, and besides there is no

[037-087] She longed to speak, to ask a question, but for the first moments she

[037-088] did not dare and did not know how to begin.

[037-164] blackened wooden building, and in spite of the late hour there were

[037-165] lights in the windows and signs of life within. He went in and asked

[037-166] a ragged fellow who met him in the corridor for a room. The latter,

[037-167] scanning Svidrigaïlov, pulled himself together and led him at once to a

[037-170] looked inquiringly.

[037-172] “Yes, sir.”

[037-173] “What else is there?”

[037-174] “Veal, vodka, savouries.”

[037-175] “Bring me tea and veal.”

[037-176] “And you want nothing else?” he asked with apparent surprise.

[037-177] “Nothing, nothing.”

[037-178] The ragged man went away, completely disillusioned.

[037-195] the moment he entered the room. He listened: someone was upbraiding and
