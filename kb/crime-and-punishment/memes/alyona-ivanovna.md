# Alyona Ivanovna

Alyona Ivanovna is the elderly pawnbroker of [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]] whose murder by [[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]] — together with the unintended killing of her half-sister [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]] — is the novel's central crime. Described by Raskolnikov as "a useless, loathsome, harmful louse" (030-370), she functions less as a fully realized character than as a symbolic test case for Raskolnikov's [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary-man theory]] and a catalyst for the novel's exploration of [[guilt|guilt]], [[conscience-and-suffering|conscience]], and the moral limits of rational calculation.

## Overview

Alyona Ivanovna is "a diminutive, withered old woman of about sixty, with sharp malignant eyes and a sharp little nose" (001-178). She lives alone in a fourth-floor flat with her half-sister Lizaveta, whom she treats "completely as a servant" (005-316). Her profession — money-lending at usurious rates — places her at the economic margins of St. Petersburg society, where she exploits the desperation of impoverished students and clerks. Raskolnikov first visits her apartment six weeks before the murder, pawning his father's watch and noting every detail of her flat's layout and the keys she wears around her neck (001-184–001-229).

## Background

Little is revealed of Alyona's personal history beyond her relationship with Lizaveta. The two are half-sisters, and Alyona is reputed to have "a tidy sum of money" — enough that she has made a will leaving everything to a monastery rather than to her sister (005-320). This rumored wealth is what attracts Raskolnikov's attention when he overhears a student in a tavern declaring that "a hundred, a thousand good deeds could be done with that old woman's money" (006-050–006-056). Alyona's existence is defined entirely by her economic function; Dostoevsky gives her no interiority, no backstory, no redeeming features — she is the dehumanized object of a utilitarian calculus.

## Role in the Novel

Alyona Ivanovna appears directly in only two scenes — Raskolnikov's rehearsal visit (Part 1, Chapter 1) and the murder itself (Part 1, Chapter 7) — but her presence reverberates throughout the novel. On the day of the murder, Raskolnikov visits her apartment with a fake "cigarette case" pledge, and as she turns away to untie the packet, he strikes her on the crown of the head with an axe-butt (007-085–007-155). Her death is instantaneous, but the crime expands beyond calculation when Lizaveta unexpectedly returns home, forcing a second murder that transforms Raskolnikov's "rational" crime into a double killing.

In the novel's second half, Alyona survives only as memory and evidence. Porfiry Petrovich's psychological pursuit of Raskolnikov relies on details of the murder scene that only the killer could know, and the discovery of her stolen goods — hidden under a stone — becomes the physical evidence of the crime. Her rumored wealth, which motivated the murder in the first place, turns out to have been largely illusory: "the old woman's money" yields only a few trinkets and a purse, and "almost all that was got out of it went to the expenses of the funeral" — Lizaveta's, not Alyona's.

## Key Relationships

- **[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]]**: Alyona's younger half-sister, whom she treats "completely as a servant" (005-316), beating her and keeping her in a state of fearful subjugation. Lizaveta's unexpected return during the murder makes her the novel's second victim.
- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]**: Her murderer, who visits her twice — once for reconnaissance and once to kill. Raskolnikov's progressive dehumanization of Alyona ("a louse") is the novel's most direct critique of utilitarian rationalism.

## Psychological Profile

Alyona's psychology is notable only for its absence. Dostoevsky deliberately withholds interior access to her, presenting her solely through external description: her "sharp malignant eyes," her "coughing and groaning" behind a closed door, her paranoid suspicion of visitors. This narrative strategy reinforces her thematic function — she is not a person to be understood but an object to be used, the logical endpoint of the utilitarian calculus that Raskolnikov applies to human life. Her flat, crammed with pawned goods and locked with multiple doors, externalizes her psychological state: hoarding, mistrust, isolation.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Alyona Ivanovna embodies the novel's central question: can one human life be weighed against the good of many? Raskolnikov's argument — that she is "a useless, loathsome, harmful louse" whose death would benefit humanity — is Dostoevsky's vehicle for testing and ultimately rejecting [[utilitarian-rationalism|utilitarian rationalism]]. The fact that her murder requires the additional killing of the innocent Lizaveta drives home the impossibility of a "clean" rational crime. Alyona's wealth, hoarded for a monastery, symbolizes the sterility of wealth without compassion, a counterpoint to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia's]] sacrificial poverty.
