# The Lazarus Reading

In Part 4, Chapter 4, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] visits [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] in her room and, after a series of probing, cruel questions about her faith and future, demands that she read him the story of Lazarus from the Gospel of John. "Where is the story of Lazarus? Find it for me, [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]" (024-351–024-354). The scene that follows is the novel's theological and emotional center — the moment the crime narrative pivots toward the possibility of resurrection.

## The Scene

The scene is charged with multiple levels of meaning. Raskolnikov, spiritually dead from his crime and increasingly isolated from all human connection, asks to hear the story of a man raised from the dead. Sonia, the prostitute whom society has condemned but whose faith remains intact, reads the Gospel given to her by [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] — the very woman Raskolnikov murdered in the pawnbroker's flat. This chain of connections (murderer → victim → Gospel → prostitute-saint → resurrection story) makes the reading a node where all the novel's themes converge.

Sonia hesitates. "What for? You don't believe, do you?" (024-356). But Raskolnikov insists. She begins to read, her voice shaking. As she reaches the climactic verse — "He cried with a loud voice, Lazarus, come forth. And he that was dead came forth" (024-474–024-485) — her voice breaks: "She was trembling all over, as if in a fever. She had indeed expected it" (024-474).

The candle gutters in the crooked candlestick. The room, with its "poverty-stricken appearance," is rendered sacred by the act of reading. "It is all about the raising of Lazarus," Sonia whispers afterward (024-485). The murderer and the prostitute are alone together, and the Gospel has spoken.

## The Gospel Book

The physical book Sonia reads from carries immense symbolic weight. It is a Russian translation of the New Testament, given to Sonia by Lizaveta Ivanovna, the pawnbroker's meek sister whom Raskolnikov killed with an axe. Lizaveta and Sonia had exchanged crosses — a Russian Orthodox gesture of spiritual sisterhood — and after Lizaveta's death, Sonia kept her book. "It was given to me by Lizaveta, the one who was murdered" (024-300).

This material connection means that when Sonia reads the Gospel to Raskolnikov, she is reading from the book of a woman he killed. The act is both accusation and offer of grace — the victim's book becomes the vehicle for the murderer's potential salvation.

## Narrative Function

The Lazarus reading marks the novel's pivot from the logic of crime and detection to the logic of confession and redemption. Before this scene, the dominant question is "Will Raskolnikov be caught?" After it, the question becomes "Can Raskolnikov be saved?" [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] reinforces this shift when he later tells Raskolnikov that he sees him as a man who "will come to believe" — the detective, too, understands that the real drama is spiritual, not legal.

The scene also solidifies Sonia's role as Raskolnikov's spiritual guide. She is no longer merely a sympathetic figure; she becomes the bearer of the word that might recall him from spiritual death. Her courage in reading — knowing that Raskolnikov is the murderer and that the book was Lizaveta's — transforms her from victim into agent of grace.

## Connection to the Epilogue

The Lazarus story returns in the Epilogue. During Raskolnikov's illness in the prison hospital, Sonia reads the Gospel to him again (039-854). And at the novel's close, Raskolnikov reaches for the same New Testament from under his pillow: "Can her beliefs not be my beliefs now?" (039-874). The Lazarus reading in Part 4 is thus the seed whose fruit only ripens in Siberia — the offer of resurrection, made in a candlelit room, is finally accepted in a prison barrack.
