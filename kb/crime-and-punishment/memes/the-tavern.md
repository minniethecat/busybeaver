# The Tavern

The tavern in S. Place is the site of one of the novel's most consequential coincidences: [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], sitting alone, overhears a student and a young officer discussing [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] in utilitarian terms — "A hundred thousand good deeds could be done with that old woman's money" — an argument that crystallizes his own half-formed plan and gives it the sanction of apparently independent reason. The tavern is Dostoevsky's dramatic embodiment of the novel's [[fate-and-coincidence|theme of providential coincidence]]: the random overheard conversation that feels like destiny.

## Description

The tavern is located near S. Place, in the Hay Market district. Raskolnikov enters it after his rehearsal visit to the pawnbroker, feeling "giddy" and uncharacteristically drawn to public company: "he had never been into a tavern, but now he felt giddy and was tormented by a burning thirst" (001-250). The tavern is described as containing "drunken men" and a "tavern-keeper" who serves behind the counter (001-266–002-027). It is a low, working-class establishment — not the fashionable Crystal Palace restaurant of later chapters but a place where laborers and clerks drink and talk loudly, their conversation spilling across tables to unintended listeners.

## Location & Geography

The tavern is situated near S. Place, within walking distance of the pawnbroker's flat and Raskolnikov's garret. Its proximity to the Hay Market places it in the novel's geography of public, lower-class Petersburg — the streets and squares where chance encounters between strangers carry fatal weight.

## Key Scenes

- **Raskolnikov overhears the murder argument** (Part 1, Chapter 6): A student, speaking to a young officer, lays out the utilitarian case for killing the pawnbroker: "Kill her, take her money, and with its help devote yourself to the service of humanity... One death and a hundred lives in exchange" (006-054). The argument closely mirrors Raskolnikov's own unpublished article, and he experiences it as an uncanny external confirmation: "as though there really were something preordained in it, some guiding hint" (006-057).
- **Marmeladov's confession** (Part 1, Chapter 2): Raskolnikov first meets the drunken [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] in this or a similar tavern, where the former official delivers his long, desperate monologue about his family's suffering and his daughter [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s prostitution.

## Symbolic & Atmospheric Significance

The tavern embodies the novel's logic of [[fate-and-coincidence|fateful coincidence]]. Raskolnikov's presence there — his first visit to a tavern, driven by an uncharacteristic impulse — places him in the path of a conversation that seems to authorize his crime. Dostoevsky uses the tavern's porous public space to dramatize the idea that ideas circulate in the social atmosphere, and that individual decisions are shaped by the intellectual currents one happens to overhear.
