# Hay Market

The Hay Market (Sennaya Ploshchad) is the novel's geographical and moral center — the place where private intentions meet public reality. It is here that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] overhears Lizaveta's conversation revealing the pawnbroker will be alone (001-080), here that he returns after [[murder-of-pawnbroker|the murder]] in a fever of remorse, and here that [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] instructs him to kneel and confess: "Go to the cross-roads, bow down to the people, kiss the earth, and say aloud: 'I have sinned before the whole world'" (030-415).

## Key Events at the Hay Market

| Event | Chapter | Significance |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| Overheard conversation | Part 1, Ch. 1 | Raskolnikov learns Lizaveta will be away — "the fatal accident" that makes the murder possible |
| Post-murder return | Part 2, Ch. 1 | Raskolnikov is drawn back to the scene, trembling and paranoid |
| First confession attempt | Part 6, Ch. 8 | Raskolnikov kneels but cannot speak; the crowd laughs |
| [[confession-at-hay-market|Successful confession]] | Part 6, Ch. 8 | Kneels, kisses the earth "with bliss and rapture" (039-060) |

## Role in the Novel

Raskolnikov is drawn to the Hay Market compulsively. "It was about nine o'clock when he crossed the Hay Market" (005-297) — the phrase recurs, marking the square as the point where his isolation is breached. The market's crowds — prostitutes, drunkards, merchants, police — represent the collective life from which his crime has excluded him. In Dostoevsky's symbolic geography, the Hay Market is the antithesis of Raskolnikov's [[raskolnikovs-garret|garret]]: open where the garret is closed, crowded where the garret is solitary, public where the garret is secret.

## The Confession

Sonia's instruction to confess at the cross-roads identifies the Hay Market as sacred space — not a church but the profane city made holy by the act of public acknowledgment. The cross-roads (*perekryostok*) carries both literal and symbolic weight in Russian folk tradition: it is where one meets fate, where deals with the devil are struck, and where the earth can be kissed in ritual penance. Raskolnikov's confession there is aborted on his first attempt (he cannot speak), but on the second, he succeeds, kneeling in the dust as a voice in the crowd says, "He's gone to Jerusalem" — the profane crowd recognizing, despite itself, the sacred nature of the act.

## The Crowd as Moral Witness

The Hay Market crowd functions as a collective character throughout the novel. They laugh at Raskolnikov's first kneeling attempt, mistaking him for a drunk. But they also witness — and their witness matters. Dostoevsky's point is that confession requires an audience: it is not enough to acknowledge one's crime in private (to Sonia) or in legal form (at the [[police-station]]); the acknowledgement must be made before the *narod*, the people, whose judgment is more forgiving — and more final — than any court's.
