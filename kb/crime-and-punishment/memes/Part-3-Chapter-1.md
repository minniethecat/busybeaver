[015-001] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] got up, and sat down on the sofa. He waved his hand weakly

[015-002] to [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] to cut short the flow of warm and incoherent consolations

[015-003] he was addressing to his mother and sister, took them both by the hand

[015-004] and for a minute or two gazed from one to the other without speaking.

[015-005] His mother was alarmed by his expression. It revealed an emotion

[015-006] agonisingly poignant, and at the same time something immovable, almost

[015-007] insane. [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] began to cry.

[015-008] Avdotya Romanovna was pale; her hand trembled in her brother’s.

[015-009] “Go home... with him,” he said in a broken voice, pointing to Razumihin,

[015-010] “good-bye till to-morrow; to-morrow everything... Is it long since you

[015-011] arrived?”

[015-012] “This evening, Rodya,” answered Pulcheria Alexandrovna, “the train was

[015-013] awfully late. But, Rodya, nothing would induce me to leave you now! I

[015-014] will spend the night here, near you...”

[015-015] “Don’t torture me!” he said with a gesture of irritation.

[015-016] “I will stay with him,” cried Razumihin, “I won’t leave him for a

[015-017] moment. Bother all my visitors! Let them rage to their hearts’ content!

[015-018] My uncle is presiding there.”

[015-019] “How, how can I thank you!” Pulcheria Alexandrovna was beginning, once

[015-020] more pressing Razumihin’s hands, but Raskolnikov interrupted her again.

[015-021] “I can’t have it! I can’t have it!” he repeated irritably, “don’t worry

[015-022] me! Enough, go away... I can’t stand it!”

[015-023] “Come, mamma, come out of the room at least for a minute,” [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]

[015-024] whispered in dismay; “we are distressing him, that’s evident.”

[015-025] “Mayn’t I look at him after three years?” wept Pulcheria Alexandrovna.

[015-026] “Stay,” he stopped them again, “you keep interrupting me, and my ideas

[015-027] get muddled.... Have you seen Luzhin?”

[015-028] “No, Rodya, but he knows already of our arrival. We have heard, Rodya,

[015-029] that Pyotr Petrovitch was so kind as to visit you today,” Pulcheria

[015-030] Alexandrovna added somewhat timidly.

[015-031] “Yes... he was so kind... Dounia, I promised Luzhin I’d throw him

[015-032] downstairs and told him to go to hell....”

[015-033] “Rodya, what are you saying! Surely, you don’t mean to tell us...”

[015-034] Pulcheria Alexandrovna began in alarm, but she stopped, looking at

[015-035] Dounia.

[015-036] Avdotya Romanovna was looking attentively at her brother, waiting

[015-037] for what would come next. Both of them had heard of the quarrel from

[015-038] [[nastasya|Nastasya]], so far as she had succeeded in understanding and reporting it,

[015-039] and were in painful perplexity and suspense.

[015-040] “Dounia,” Raskolnikov continued with an effort, “I don’t want that

[015-041] marriage, so at the first opportunity to-morrow you must refuse Luzhin,

[015-042] so that we may never hear his name again.”

[015-043] “Good Heavens!” cried Pulcheria Alexandrovna.

[015-044] “Brother, think what you are saying!” Avdotya Romanovna began

[015-045] impetuously, but immediately checked herself. “You are not fit to talk

[015-046] now, perhaps; you are tired,” she added gently.

[015-047] “You think I am delirious? No... You are marrying Luzhin for _my_

[015-048] sake. But I won’t accept the sacrifice. And so write a letter before

[015-049] to-morrow, to refuse him... Let me read it in the morning and that will

[015-050] be the end of it!”

[015-051] “That I can’t do!” the girl cried, offended, “what right have you...”

[015-052] “Dounia, you are hasty, too, be quiet, to-morrow... Don’t you see...”

[015-053] the mother interposed in dismay. “Better come away!”

[015-054] “He is raving,” Razumihin cried tipsily, “or how would he dare!

[015-055] To-morrow all this nonsense will be over... to-day he certainly did

[015-056] drive him away. That was so. And Luzhin got angry, too.... He made

[015-057] speeches here, wanted to show off his learning and he went out

[015-058] crest-fallen....”

[015-059] “Then it’s true?” cried Pulcheria Alexandrovna.

[015-060] “Good-bye till to-morrow, brother,” said Dounia compassionately--“let us

[015-061] go, mother... Good-bye, Rodya.”

[015-062] “Do you hear, sister,” he repeated after them, making a last effort,

[015-063] “I am not delirious; this marriage is--an infamy. Let me act like

[015-064] a scoundrel, but you mustn’t... one is enough... and though I am a

[015-065] scoundrel, I wouldn’t own such a sister. It’s me or Luzhin! Go now....”

[015-066] “But you’re out of your mind! Despot!” roared Razumihin; but Raskolnikov

[015-067] did not and perhaps could not answer. He lay down on the sofa, and

[015-068] turned to the wall, utterly exhausted. Avdotya Romanovna looked with

[015-069] interest at Razumihin; her black eyes flashed; Razumihin positively

[015-070] started at her glance.

[015-071] Pulcheria Alexandrovna stood overwhelmed.

[015-072] “Nothing would induce me to go,” she whispered in despair to Razumihin.

[015-073] “I will stay somewhere here... escort Dounia home.”

[015-074] “You’ll spoil everything,” Razumihin answered in the same whisper,

[015-075] losing patience--“come out on to the [[staircases|stairs]], anyway. Nastasya, show a

[015-076] light! I assure you,” he went on in a half whisper on the stairs--“that

[015-077] he was almost beating the doctor and me this afternoon! Do you

[015-078] understand? The doctor himself! Even he gave way and left him, so as not

[015-079] to irritate him. I remained downstairs on guard, but he dressed at once

[015-080] and slipped off. And he will slip off again if you irritate him, at this

[015-081] time of night, and will do himself some mischief....”

[015-082] “What are you saying?”

[015-083] “And Avdotya Romanovna can’t possibly be left in those lodgings without

[015-084] you. Just think where you are staying! That blackguard Pyotr Petrovitch

[015-085] couldn’t find you better lodgings... But you know I’ve had a little to

[015-086] drink, and that’s what makes me... swear; don’t mind it....”

[015-087] “But I’ll go to [[praskovya-pavlovna|the landlady]] here,” Pulcheria Alexandrovna insisted,

[015-088] “I’ll beseech her to find some corner for Dounia and me for the night. I

[015-089] can’t leave him like that, I cannot!”

[015-090] This conversation took place on the landing just before the landlady’s

[015-091] door. Nastasya lighted them from a step below. Razumihin was in

[015-092] extraordinary excitement. Half an hour earlier, while he was bringing

[015-093] Raskolnikov home, he had indeed talked too freely, but he was aware of

[015-094] it himself, and his head was clear in spite of the vast quantities he

[015-095] had imbibed. Now he was in a state bordering on ecstasy, and all that he

[015-096] had drunk seemed to fly to his head with redoubled effect. He stood with

[015-097] the two ladies, seizing both by their hands, persuading them, and giving

[015-098] them reasons with astonishing plainness of speech, and at almost every

[015-099] word he uttered, probably to emphasise his arguments, he squeezed their

[015-100] hands painfully as in a vise. He stared at Avdotya Romanovna without the

[015-101] least regard for good manners. They sometimes pulled their hands out of

[015-102] his huge bony paws, but far from noticing what was the matter, he drew

[015-103] them all the closer to him. If they’d told him to jump head foremost

[015-104] from the staircase, he would have done it without thought or hesitation

[015-105] in their service. Though Pulcheria Alexandrovna felt that the young man

[015-106] was really too eccentric and pinched her hand too much, in her anxiety

[015-107] over her Rodya she looked on his presence as providential, and was

[015-108] unwilling to notice all his peculiarities. But though Avdotya Romanovna

[015-109] shared her anxiety, and was not of timorous disposition, she could not

[015-110] see the glowing light in his eyes without wonder and almost alarm. It

[015-111] was only the unbounded confidence inspired by Nastasya’s account of her

[015-112] brother’s queer friend, which prevented her from trying to run away from

[015-113] him, and to persuade her mother to do the same. She realised, too,

[015-114] that even running away was perhaps impossible now. Ten minutes later,

[015-115] however, she was considerably reassured; it was characteristic of

[015-116] Razumihin that he showed his true nature at once, whatever mood he might

[015-117] be in, so that people quickly saw the sort of man they had to deal with.

[015-118] “You can’t go to the landlady, that’s perfect nonsense!” he cried. “If

[015-119] you stay, though you are his mother, you’ll drive him to a frenzy, and

[015-120] then goodness knows what will happen! Listen, I’ll tell you what I’ll

[015-121] do: Nastasya will stay with him now, and I’ll conduct you both home, you

[015-122] can’t be in the streets alone; [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] is an awful place in that

[015-123] way.... But no matter! Then I’ll run straight back here and a quarter of

[015-124] an hour later, on my word of honour, I’ll bring you news how he is,

[015-125] whether he is asleep, and all that. Then, listen! Then I’ll run home in

[015-126] a twinkling--I’ve a lot of friends there, all drunk--I’ll fetch

[015-127] [[zossimov|Zossimov]]--that’s the doctor who is looking after him, he is there, too,

[015-128] but he is not drunk; he is not drunk, he is never drunk! I’ll drag him

[015-129] to Rodya, and then to you, so that you’ll get two reports in the

[015-130] hour--from the doctor, you understand, from the doctor himself, that’s a

[015-131] very different thing from my account of him! If there’s anything wrong,

[015-132] I swear I’ll bring you here myself, but, if it’s all right, you go to

[015-133] bed. And I’ll spend the night here, in the passage, he won’t hear me,

[015-134] and I’ll tell Zossimov to sleep at the landlady’s, to be at hand. Which

[015-135] is better for him: you or the doctor? So come home then! But the

[015-136] landlady is out of the question; it’s all right for me, but it’s out of

[015-137] the question for you: she wouldn’t take you, for she’s... for she’s a

[015-138] fool... She’d be jealous on my account of Avdotya Romanovna and of you,

[015-139] too, if you want to know... of Avdotya Romanovna certainly. She is an

[015-140] absolutely, absolutely unaccountable character! But I am a fool, too!...

[015-141] No matter! Come along! Do you trust me? Come, do you trust me or not?”

[015-142] “Let us go, mother,” said Avdotya Romanovna, “he will certainly do what

[015-143] he has promised. He has saved Rodya already, and if the doctor really

[015-144] will consent to spend the night here, what could be better?”

[015-145] “You see, you... you... understand me, because you are an angel!”

[015-146] Razumihin cried in ecstasy, “let us go! Nastasya! Fly upstairs and sit

[015-147] with him with a light; I’ll come in a quarter of an hour.”

[015-148] Though Pulcheria Alexandrovna was not perfectly convinced, she made no

[015-149] further resistance. Razumihin gave an arm to each and drew them down

[015-150] the stairs. He still made her uneasy, as though he was competent and

[015-151] good-natured, was he capable of carrying out his promise? He seemed in

[015-152] such a condition....

[015-153] “Ah, I see you think I am in such a condition!” Razumihin broke in upon

[015-154] her thoughts, guessing them, as he strolled along the pavement with huge

[015-155] steps, so that the two ladies could hardly keep up with him, a fact he

[015-156] did not observe, however. “Nonsense! That is... I am drunk like a fool,

[015-157] but that’s not it; I am not drunk from wine. It’s seeing you has turned

[015-158] my head... But don’t mind me! Don’t take any notice: I am talking

[015-159] nonsense, I am not worthy of you.... I am utterly unworthy of you! The

[015-160] minute I’ve taken you home, I’ll pour a couple of pailfuls of water over

[015-161] my head in the gutter here, and then I shall be all right.... If only

[015-162] you knew how I love you both! Don’t laugh, and don’t be angry! You may

[015-163] be angry with anyone, but not with me! I am his friend, and therefore I

[015-164] am your friend, too, I want to be... I had a presentiment... Last year

[015-165] there was a moment... though it wasn’t a presentiment really, for

[015-166] you seem to have fallen from heaven. And I expect I shan’t sleep all

[015-167] night... Zossimov was afraid a little time ago that he would go mad...

[015-168] that’s why he mustn’t be irritated.”

[015-169] “What do you say?” cried the mother.

[015-170] “Did the doctor really say that?” asked Avdotya Romanovna, alarmed.

[015-171] “Yes, but it’s not so, not a bit of it. He gave him some medicine, a

[015-172] powder, I saw it, and then your coming here.... Ah! It would have been

[015-173] better if you had come to-morrow. It’s a good thing we went away. And in

[015-174] an hour Zossimov himself will report to you about everything. He is not

[015-175] drunk! And I shan’t be drunk.... And what made me get so tight? Because

[015-176] they got me into an argument, damn them! I’ve sworn never to argue! They

[015-177] talk such trash! I almost came to blows! I’ve left my uncle to preside.

[015-178] Would you believe, they insist on complete absence of individualism

[015-179] and that’s just what they relish! Not to be themselves, to be as unlike

[015-180] themselves as they can. That’s what they regard as the highest point of

[015-181] progress. If only their nonsense were their own, but as it is...”

[015-182] “Listen!” Pulcheria Alexandrovna interrupted timidly, but it only added

[015-183] fuel to the flames.

[015-184] “What do you think?” shouted Razumihin, louder than ever, “you think I

[015-185] am attacking them for talking nonsense? Not a bit! I like them to talk

[015-186] nonsense. That’s man’s one privilege over all creation. Through error

[015-187] you come to the truth! I am a man because I err! You never reach any

[015-188] truth without making fourteen mistakes and very likely a hundred and

[015-189] fourteen. And a fine thing, too, in its way; but we can’t even make

[015-190] mistakes on our own account! Talk nonsense, but talk your own nonsense,

[015-191] and I’ll kiss you for it. To go wrong in one’s own way is better than

[015-192] to go right in someone else’s. In the first case you are a man, in the

[015-193] second you’re no better than a bird. Truth won’t escape you, but life

[015-194] can be cramped. There have been examples. And what are we doing now?

[015-195] In science, development, thought, invention, ideals, aims, liberalism,

[015-196] judgment, experience and everything, everything, everything, we are

[015-197] still in the preparatory class at school. We prefer to live on other

[015-198] people’s ideas, it’s what we are used to! Am I right, am I right?” cried

[015-199] Razumihin, pressing and shaking the two ladies’ hands.

[015-200] “Oh, [[pity-and-compassion|mercy]], I do not know,” cried poor Pulcheria Alexandrovna.

[015-201] “Yes, yes... though I don’t agree with you in everything,” added Avdotya

[015-202] Romanovna earnestly and at once uttered a cry, for he squeezed her hand

[015-203] so painfully.

[015-204] “Yes, you say yes... well after that you... you...” he cried in

[015-205] a transport, “you are a fount of goodness, purity, sense... and

[015-206] perfection. Give me your hand... you give me yours, too! I want to kiss

[015-207] your hands here at once, on my knees...” and he fell on his knees on the

[015-208] pavement, fortunately at that time deserted.

[015-209] “Leave off, I entreat you, what are you doing?” Pulcheria Alexandrovna

[015-210] cried, greatly distressed.

[015-211] “Get up, get up!” said Dounia laughing, though she, too, was upset.

[015-212] “Not for anything till you let me kiss your hands! That’s it! Enough! I

[015-213] get up and we’ll go on! I am a luckless fool, I am unworthy of you and

[015-214] drunk... and I am ashamed.... I am not worthy to love you, but to do

[015-215] homage to you is the duty of every man who is not a perfect beast! And

[015-216] I’ve done homage.... Here are your lodgings, and for that alone Rodya

[015-221] scoundrel.”

[015-223] was beginning.

[015-224] “Yes, yes, you are right, I did forget myself, I am ashamed of it,”

[015-226] me for speaking so! For I speak sincerely and not because... hm, hm!

[015-227] That would be disgraceful; in fact not because I’m in... hm! Well,

[015-230] curled at the barber’s, not because he was in such a hurry to show his

[015-238] is not on the right path. Though I’ve been calling them all sorts of

[015-241] is an honest man and knows his work. But enough, it’s all said and

[015-242] forgiven. Is it forgiven? Well, then, let’s go on. I know this corridor,

[015-243] I’ve been here, there was a scandal here at Number 3.... Where are you

[015-244] here? Which number? eight? Well, lock yourselves in for the night, then.

[015-245] Don’t let anybody in. In a quarter of an hour I’ll come back with news,

[015-246] and half an hour later I’ll bring Zossimov, you’ll see! Good-bye, I’ll

[015-249] Alexandrovna, addressing her daughter with anxiety and dismay.

[015-250] “Don’t worry yourself, mother,” said Dounia, taking off her hat and

[015-251] cape. “God has sent this gentleman to our aid, though he has come from a

[015-252] drinking party. We can depend on him, I assure you. And all that he has

[015-253] done for Rodya....”

[015-255] myself to leave Rodya?... And how different, how different I had fancied

[015-256] our meeting! How sullen he was, as though not pleased to see us....”

[015-257] Tears came into her eyes.

[015-258] “No, it’s not that, mother. You didn’t see, you were crying all the

[015-259] time. He is quite unhinged by serious illness--that’s the reason.”

[015-260] “Ah, that illness! What will happen, what will happen? And how he talked

[015-277] for Avdotya Romanovna. Yet apart from his eccentric condition, many

[015-278] people would have thought it justified if they had seen Avdotya

[015-279] Romanovna, especially at that moment when she was walking to and

[015-285] might be described as really beautiful. Her hair was dark brown, a

[015-287] black eyes and yet at times a look of extraordinary kindness. She was

[015-288] pale, but it was a healthy pallor; her face was radiant with freshness

[015-289] and vigour. Her mouth was rather small; the full red lower lip projected

[015-290] a little as did her chin; it was the only irregularity in her beautiful

[015-291] face, but it gave it a peculiarly individual and almost haughty

[015-292] expression. Her face was always more serious and thoughtful than gay;

[015-293] but how well smiles, how well youthful, lighthearted, irresponsible,

[015-305] Avdotya Romanovna on his account. Although Pulcheria Alexandrovna was

[015-306] forty-three, her face still retained traces of her former beauty; she

[015-307] looked much younger than her age, indeed, which is almost always the

[015-311] hair had begun to grow grey and thin, there had long been little crow’s

[015-313] anxiety and grief, and yet it was a handsome face. She was Dounia

[015-314] over again, twenty years older, but without the projecting underlip.

[015-315] Pulcheria Alexandrovna was emotional, but not sentimental, timid and

[015-316] yielding, but only to a certain point. She could give way and accept a

[015-317] great deal even of what was contrary to her convictions, but there was a

[015-318] certain barrier fixed by honesty, principle and the deepest convictions

[015-319] which nothing would induce her to cross.

[015-325] came. Now I am fetching Zossimov, he will report to you and then you’d

[015-327] And he ran off down the corridor.

[015-328] “What a very competent and... devoted young man!” cried Pulcheria

[015-329] Alexandrovna exceedingly delighted.

[015-330] “He seems a splendid person!” Avdotya Romanovna replied with some

[015-331] warmth, resuming her walk up and down the room.

[015-333] and another knock at the door. Both women waited this time completely

[015-334] relying on Razumihin’s promise; he actually had succeeded in bringing

[015-335] Zossimov. Zossimov had agreed at once to desert the drinking party to

[015-336] go to Raskolnikov’s, but he came reluctantly and with the greatest
