# Guilt

The central psychological force in *Crime and Punishment* — not merely a legal fact but an all-consuming inner state that unravels Raskolnikov's rational defenses and propels him toward confession.

## Definition

In Dostoevsky's novel, guilt operates on two distinct levels. The first is *legal guilt* — the objective fact of having committed murder. The second, far more significant, is *psychological guilt* — the inward torment that Raskolnikov experiences regardless of whether he is discovered. Porfiry Petrovich articulates this duality when he observes that guilt is not simply a matter of evidence but a psychological condition: "if a man is guilty, you must be able to get at him psychologically" [033-061]. The novel's central drama is not the detective's pursuit of the criminal but guilt's pursuit of the criminal's conscience.

## Expression in the Novel

Guilt manifests in Raskolnikov not as remorse for the murders themselves but as a progressive disintegration of his mental and physical state. From the moment after the crime, he is seized by fever, delirium, and a compulsion to revisit the scene of the murder — behaviors that baffle him rationally but reveal the operation of conscience beneath his intellectual defenses [011-221]. His body betrays what his mind denies: "he hadn't felt guilty" is the rational self-assessment, yet every involuntary action — fainting at the police station, returning to the pawnbroker's flat, confessing to Sonia — demonstrates guilt's grip [011-315].

Dostoevsky stages guilt as a force that demands expression. Porfiry understands this intuitively: "if he's guilty, he's sure to come. Another man might not, but he will" [033-136]. This is not a deduction from evidence but a psychological law — guilt cannot remain silent. Raskolnikov's eventual confession in the Hay Market, kneeling and kissing the ground, is the culmination of guilt's relentless pressure against the dam of his rationalizations.

## Key Passages

> "But what if I am not guilty after all? What if I am not the sort of man who has the right?" Raskolnikov's guilt is entangled with his theory — if the extraordinary man is beyond guilt, then feeling guilt proves he is not extraordinary [019-403].

> "I am guilty! Mine is the sin! I am the murderer!" Raskolnikov's confession to Sonia in her room is the first moment guilt breaks through his defenses in words, though he immediately retreats into theory again [026-031].

> "Dounia darling, if I am guilty forgive me (though I cannot be forgiven if I am guilty)" — even in his farewell note, Raskolnikov cannot fully inhabit his guilt, oscillating between confession and denial [038-253].

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Experiences guilt as a physiological and psychological illness while denying its moral basis. His guilt proves that he is not the "extraordinary man" he theorized himself to be — the very existence of his conscience refutes his philosophy. His trajectory is from denial ("I am not guilty in any real sense") through ambivalence to confession [033-335].

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Understands guilt not psychologically but theologically — as sin requiring repentance. She tells Raskolnikov to "accept suffering and be redeemed by it," framing guilt as the gateway to resurrection rather than a terminal condition [026-031].

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: Treats guilt as a forensic-psychological tool. His method is to let guilt do the work of prosecution: the guilty man will betray himself through behavior, contradictions, and ultimately confession. "Nikolay was guilty, after what had passed between us" — Porfiry is willing to accept a false confession because it fits his psychological narrative [032-287].

- **Nikolay Dementyev**: The house-painter who falsely confesses to the murder represents a perversion of guilt — religious guilt that seeks suffering for its own sake, detached from actual wrongdoing. His false confession contrasts with Raskolnikov's inability to confess truly [026-059].

## Historical and Philosophical Context

Dostoevsky's treatment of guilt draws on the Russian Orthodox understanding of sin as a condition of the soul rather than merely a violation of law. In this tradition, guilt is not resolved by punishment alone but by *pokayanie* (penitence) — a transformative suffering that restores the sinner to community. This stands in opposition to the Western rationalist-legal tradition, represented in the novel by Luzhin and the utilitarian calculus, where guilt is a matter of evidence and proportionate punishment.

The novel also engages with the emerging psychological discourse of the 1860s. Raskolnikov's guilt-induced illness — fever, paranoia, compulsion — reflects Dostoevsky's interest in the physiological basis of moral states, a theme explored in contemporary Russian medical literature and his own epilepsy.
