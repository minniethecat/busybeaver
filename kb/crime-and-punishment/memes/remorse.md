# Remorse

The emotional and moral experience of genuine regret for wrongdoing — distinct from guilt (awareness of having transgressed) and from confession (the act of disclosure). In *Crime and Punishment*, remorse is not automatic: Raskolnikov confesses long before he feels it, and the novel's dramatic arc traces the painful distance between acknowledgment of an act and genuine sorrow for it.

## Raskolnikov's Absence of Remorse

For most of the novel, Raskolnikov does not feel remorse. He feels fear of discovery, disgust at his own weakness, and a kind of vertiginous isolation — but not moral regret. When he confesses to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], his language remains defiant: "I've only killed a louse, Sonia, a useless, loathsome, harmful louse" (030-370). He is ashamed of having failed, not of having killed.

The examining magistrate [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] understands this psychological state with clinical precision. He does not urge Raskolnikov to confess out of procedural necessity alone; he understands that confession without remorse is hollow, and he waits — patiently, almost tenderly — for the younger man to arrive at genuine moral recognition.

## The Turning Point

Raskolnikov's [[raskolnikov-confesses-to-sonia|confession to Sonia]] is a crucial turning point, not because it produces remorse immediately but because it creates the conditions in which remorse becomes possible. By telling Sonia, Raskolnikov lets another human being into the sealed chamber of his guilt. Sonia's response — "You have murdered yourself" (033-385) — reframes the crime in moral rather than intellectual terms. She does not debate the [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary man theory]]; she simply grieves for the soul that has destroyed itself.

## The Gap Between Confession and Remorse

A central irony of the novel is that Raskolnikov confesses long before he feels remorse. Even after the [[confession-at-hay-market|public confession at the Hay Market]] — where he kneels, kisses the ground, but is laughed at by the crowd (030-415) — even after being sentenced to Siberia, he remains hardened: "His conscience was at rest" (038-233). He is ashamed of his failure, not of his crime.

What finally produces remorse is not argument or punishment but love — Sonia's faithful presence in Siberia, her daily visits to the prison, her refusal to abandon him. In the novel's closing scene, Raskolnikov falls at her feet, weeping, and "in those sick and pale faces there already gleamed the dawn of a renewed future" (039-239). Remorse arrives not as a philosophical conclusion but as a gift of grace, mediated through human love.

## Remorse as Spiritual Prerequisite

For Dostoevsky, remorse is the necessary precondition for any genuine [[resurrection-through-love|moral regeneration]]. [[suffering-as-redemption|Suffering]] without remorse is merely pain; confession without remorse is merely information. The novel insists that the movement toward redemption must pass through the narrow gate of genuine sorrow for the harm one has done. Raskolnikov's tears at Sonia's feet in the Epilogue are the first authentic emotion he has felt since before the murder — and they mark the beginning of his restoration to humanity.
