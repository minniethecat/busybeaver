# The Catherine Canal

The Catherine Canal (Ekaterininsky Canal) is a waterway in [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]] that features in two crucial episodes: Raskolnikov's attempt to dispose of the stolen trinkets by throwing them into the water, and [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s final night wandering along its banks before his [[svidrigailovs-suicide|suicide]]. The canal functions as the novel's spatial image of moral and psychological threshold — a boundary between the order of the city and the dark water that promises to swallow evidence, guilt, and finally life itself.

## Description

The canal is described as a "dirty" waterway lined with embankments and crossed by bridges. Raskolnikov approaches it at dusk, intending to throw the pawnbroker's purse and trinkets into the water, but finds that "there was not a spot to be seen where he could throw anything into the water unnoticed" — the canal's banks are too exposed, the city too watchful. Svidrigailov, in his final hours, wanders along the canal in a cold rain, passing "the dirty entrance of a house" and a watchman who sees him but does not intervene.

## Key Scenes

- **Raskolnikov's attempted disposal** (Part 2, Chapter 2): Raskolnikov tries to throw the stolen goods into the canal but finds no private spot, eventually hiding them under a stone instead.
- **Svidrigailov's final night** (Part 6, Chapter 6): Svidrigailov walks along the canal in the rain, passing through the city for the last time before his suicide.

## Symbolic & Atmospheric Significance

The canal embodies the novel's spatial logic of liminality — it is neither land nor water, neither concealment nor exposure. Raskolnikov's inability to throw the evidence into it represents the failure of the "clean" disposal his rational plan required; the city refuses to be an accomplice. For Svidrigailov, the canal is the last landscape he sees — a cold, indifferent waterway that mirrors his own spiritual emptiness.
