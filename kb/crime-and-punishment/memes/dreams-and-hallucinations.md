# Dreams and Hallucinations

The four major dream sequences that punctuate the novel, each functioning as a moment of psychological revelation in which the dreamer's repressed knowledge and moral truth break through the defenses of waking rationality. Together they form a parallel narrative tracing Raskolnikov's spiritual trajectory.

## Definition

In Dostoevsky's narrative technique, dreams are not decorative interludes but structural revelations. They access psychological and moral truths that waking consciousness cannot or will not admit. Raskolnikov's dreams trace an arc: the childhood dream of the beaten horse (compassion, prefiguring the murder's horror), the dream of the laughing landlady (guilt returning as persecution), the dream of the laughing pawnbroker (the impossibility of escaping the crime), and the apocalyptic dream in Siberia (humanity's self-destruction through pride). Each dream reveals what Raskolnikov's rational mind suppresses, and together they argue that truth — moral and psychological — cannot be permanently evaded.

## Expression in the Novel

The dream of the beaten horse (Part 1, Chapter 5) is the novel's first great set piece. In a childhood memory made present, a drunken peasant Mikolka brutally beats an old mare to death while a crowd laughs and the child Raskolnikov runs to embrace the dead animal, crying (PN 001-274). The dream prefigures the murder — the horse is the innocent victim, the beating is senseless cruelty, and the child's compassion is what Raskolnikov must suppress to commit his crime. It also introduces the theme of crowds as complicit witnesses — a motif that returns in the Haymarket confession scene.

The dream of the laughing pawnbroker (Part 3, Chapter 6) is the novel's most uncanny sequence. Raskolnikov returns to the murder scene and strikes the old woman again with the axe, but she does not die — she laughs, silently and terribly. He strikes harder and harder, but "the old woman went on shaking and was helpless with laughter" (PN 003-458). The dream reveals the psychological truth the theory denies: the murder cannot be completed, the victim cannot be silenced, and the crime is not a single act but an eternal recurrence in the murderer's conscience.

Svidrigailov's dreams on the night before his suicide (Part 6, Chapter 6) are the novel's darkest dream sequence. He dreams of a mouse, a drowned girl in a coffin, and finally a five-year-old child whose innocent face transforms into the knowing, corrupt leer of a prostitute — "the face of a depraved woman" (PN 006-570). These dreams reveal a consciousness that has exhausted all possibility of innocence and must now extinguish itself. Where Raskolnikov's dreams warn and guide toward redemption, Svidrigailov's confirm the impossibility of return.

The apocalyptic dream in the epilogue is Raskolnikov's final dream and the novel's moral summation. A plague sweeps the world: "men attacked by it became at once mad and furious," each believing they alone possessed the truth, leading to universal war and destruction (PN 006-610). Only a "pure chosen people" survive. The dream is a diagnosis of the pride that produced Raskolnikov's crime writ large — a world in which everyone behaves as Raskolnikov did, convinced of their exceptional right to impose truth.

## Key Passages

> "He ran beside the mare, he ran in front of her, he saw her being whipped across the eyes, right across the eyes!" — the childhood dream of the beaten horse (PN 001-274)

> "The old woman was sitting in the corner and was laughing" — the dream of the unkillable pawnbroker, the crime that cannot be completed (PN 003-458)

> "The whole world was condemned to a terrible, strange, and unparalleled plague" — the apocalyptic epilogue dream, pride translated to universal scale (PN 006-610)

> "He saw before him a magnificently dressed young girl... and suddenly it seemed to him that her eyelashes quivered" — Svidrigailov's dream of corrupted innocence (PN 006-570)

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His four dreams form a psychological narrative paralleling the plot: compassion → guilt → the impossibility of escape → universal moral warning. His dreams are the voice of conscience his waking mind has silenced.
- **Arkady Svidrigailov**: His final dreams catalogue corrupted innocence — a mouse, a drowned girl, a corrupted child — and end not in warning but in suicide. He wakes, walks out, and shoots himself: the nightmare from which one cannot wake.
- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Notably, Sonia does not dream in the novel. Her faith provides the stability that makes dream-revelation unnecessary — she already knows what Raskolnikov's dreams are trying to tell him. This contrast is deliberate: the person closest to God needs no nocturnal revelations.

## Historical & Philosophical Context

Dostoevsky was deeply interested in contemporary dream theory, reading widely in Russian and European psychological literature. But his use of dreams is more theological than psychological: dreams in his fiction are often the place where God (or the demonic) speaks to a soul that has closed itself to truth in waking life. The dream of the beaten horse has roots in Dostoevsky's own childhood memory of witnessing a peasant beat a horse, and the apocalyptic dream draws on the Book of Revelation and Dostoevsky's fears about the direction of European civilization.
