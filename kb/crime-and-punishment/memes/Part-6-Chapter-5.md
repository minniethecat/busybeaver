[036-001] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] walked after him.

[036-002] “What’s this?” cried Svidrigaïlov turning round, “I thought I said...”

[036-003] “It means that I am not going to lose sight of you now.”

[036-004] “What?”

[036-005] Both stood still and gazed at one another, as though measuring their

[036-006] strength.

[036-007] “From all your half tipsy stories,” Raskolnikov observed harshly, “I am

[036-008] _positive_ that you have not given up your designs on my sister, but

[036-009] are pursuing them more actively than ever. I have learnt that my sister

[036-010] received a letter this morning. You have hardly been able to sit still

[036-011] all this time.... You may have unearthed a wife on the way, but that

[036-012] means nothing. I should like to make certain myself.”

[036-013] Raskolnikov could hardly have said himself what he wanted and of what he

[036-014] wished to make certain.

[036-015] “Upon my word! I’ll call the police!”

[036-016] “Call away!”

[036-017] Again they stood for a minute facing each other. At last Svidrigaïlov’s

[036-018] face changed. Having satisfied himself that Raskolnikov was not

[036-019] frightened at his threat, he assumed a mirthful and friendly air.

[036-020] “What a fellow! I purposely refrained from referring to your affair,

[036-021] though I am devoured by curiosity. It’s a fantastic affair. I’ve put it

[036-022] off till another time, but you’re enough to rouse the dead.... Well, let

[036-023] us go, only I warn you beforehand I am only going home for a moment,

[036-024] to get some money; then I shall lock up the flat, take a cab and go to

[036-025] spend the evening at [[krestovsky-island|the Islands]]. Now, now are you going to follow me?”

[036-026] “I’m coming to your lodgings, not to see you but Sofya Semyonovna, to

[036-027] say I’m sorry not to have been at the funeral.”

[036-028] “That’s as you like, but Sofya Semyonovna is not at home. She has taken

[036-029] the three children to an old lady of high rank, the patroness of some

[036-030] orphan asylums, whom I used to know years ago. I charmed the old lady by

[036-031] depositing a sum of money with her to provide for the three children of

[036-032] [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] and subscribing to the institution as well. I told her

[036-033] too the story of Sofya Semyonovna in full detail, suppressing nothing.

[036-034] It produced an indescribable effect on her. That’s why Sofya Semyonovna

[036-035] has been invited to call to-day at the X. Hotel where the lady is

[036-036] staying for the time.”

[036-037] “No matter, I’ll come all the same.”

[036-038] “As you like, it’s nothing to me, but I won’t come with you; here we are

[036-039] at home. By the way, I am convinced that you regard me with suspicion

[036-040] just because I have shown such delicacy and have not so far troubled

[036-041] you with questions... you understand? It struck you as extraordinary; I

[036-042] don’t mind betting it’s that. Well, it teaches one to show delicacy!”

[036-043] “And to listen at doors!”

[036-044] “Ah, that’s it, is it?” laughed Svidrigaïlov. “Yes, I should have been

[036-045] surprised if you had let that pass after all that has happened. Ha-ha!

[036-046] Though I did understand something of the pranks you had been up to and

[036-047] were telling Sofya Semyonovna about, what was the meaning of it? Perhaps

[036-048] I am quite behind the times and can’t understand. For goodness’ sake,

[036-049] explain it, my dear boy. Expound the latest theories!”

[036-050] “You couldn’t have heard anything. You’re making it all up!”

[036-051] “But I’m not talking about that (though I did hear something). No, I’m

[036-052] talking of the way you keep sighing and groaning now. The Schiller in

[036-053] you is in revolt every moment, and now you tell me not to listen at

[036-054] doors. If that’s how you feel, go and inform the police that you had

[036-055] this mischance: you made a little mistake in your theory. But if you are

[036-056] convinced that one mustn’t listen at doors, but one may murder old women

[036-057] at one’s pleasure, you’d better be off to America and make haste. Run,

[036-058] young man! There may still be time. I’m speaking sincerely. Haven’t you

[036-059] the money? I’ll give you the fare.”

[036-060] “I’m not thinking of that at all,” Raskolnikov interrupted with disgust.

[036-061] “I understand (but don’t put yourself out, don’t discuss it if you don’t

[036-062] want to). I understand the questions you are worrying over--moral ones,

[036-063] aren’t they? Duties of citizen and man? Lay them all aside. They are

[036-064] nothing to you now, ha-ha! You’ll say you are still a man and a citizen.

[036-065] If so you ought not to have got into this coil. It’s no use taking up a

[036-066] job you are not fit for. Well, you’d better shoot yourself, or don’t you

[036-067] want to?”

[036-068] “You seem trying to enrage me, to make me leave you.”

[036-069] “What a queer fellow! But here we are. Welcome to the staircase. You

[036-070] see, that’s the way to Sofya Semyonovna. Look, there is no one at home.

[036-071] Don’t you believe me? Ask Kapernaumov. She leaves the key with him. Here

[036-072] is Madame de Kapernaumov herself. Hey, what? She is rather deaf. Has she

[036-073] gone out? Where? Did you hear? She is not in and won’t be till late in

[036-074] the evening probably. Well, come to my room; you wanted to come and see

[036-075] me, didn’t you? Here we are. Madame Resslich’s not at home. She is a

[036-076] woman who is always busy, an excellent woman I assure you.... She might

[036-077] have been of use to you if you had been a little more sensible. Now,

[036-078] see! I take this five-per-cent bond out of the bureau--see what a lot

[036-079] I’ve got of them still--this one will be turned into cash to-day. I

[036-080] mustn’t waste any more time. The bureau is locked, the flat is locked,

[036-081] and here we are again on the [[staircases|stairs]]. Shall we take a cab? I’m going to

[036-082] the Islands. Would you like a lift? I’ll take this carriage. Ah, you

[036-083] refuse? You are tired of it! Come for a drive! I believe it will come on

[036-084] to rain. Never mind, we’ll put down the hood....”

[036-085] Svidrigaïlov was already in the carriage. Raskolnikov decided that his

[036-086] suspicions were at least for that moment unjust. Without answering a

[036-087] word he turned and walked back towards the [[hay-market|Hay Market]]. If he had only

[036-088] turned round on his way he might have seen Svidrigaïlov get out not a

[036-089] hundred paces off, dismiss the cab and walk along the pavement. But he

[036-090] had turned the corner and could see nothing. Intense disgust drew him

[036-091] away from Svidrigaïlov.

[036-092] “To think that I could for one instant have looked for help from that

[036-093] coarse brute, that depraved sensualist and blackguard!” he cried.

[036-094] Raskolnikov’s judgment was uttered too lightly and hastily: there was

[036-095] something about Svidrigaïlov which gave him a certain original, even a

[036-096] mysterious character. As concerned his sister, Raskolnikov was convinced

[036-097] that Svidrigaïlov would not leave her in peace. But it was too tiresome

[036-098] and unbearable to go on thinking and thinking about this.

[036-100] usual, into deep thought. On the bridge he stood by the railing and

[036-101] began gazing at the water. And his sister was standing close by him.

[036-103] her. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] had never met him like this in the street before and was

[036-104] struck with dismay. She stood still and did not know whether to call

[036-105] to him or not. Suddenly she saw Svidrigaïlov coming quickly from the

[036-106] direction of the Hay Market.

[036-107] He seemed to be approaching cautiously. He did not go on to the

[036-108] bridge, but stood aside on the pavement, doing all he could to avoid

[036-109] Raskolnikov’s seeing him. He had observed Dounia for some time and had

[036-110] been making signs to her. She fancied he was signalling to beg her not

[036-111] to speak to her brother, but to come to him.

[036-112] That was what Dounia did. She stole by her brother and went up to

[036-113] Svidrigaïlov.

[036-114] “Let us make haste away,” Svidrigaïlov whispered to her, “I don’t want

[036-115] Rodion Romanovitch to know of our meeting. I must tell you I’ve been

[036-116] sitting with him in [[crystal-palace-restaurant|the restaurant]] close by, where he looked me up and

[036-117] I had great difficulty in getting rid of him. He has somehow heard of

[036-118] my letter to you and suspects something. It wasn’t you who told him, of

[036-119] course, but if not you, who then?”

[036-120] “Well, we’ve turned the corner now,” Dounia interrupted, “and my brother

[036-121] won’t see us. I have to tell you that I am going no further with you.

[036-122] Speak to me here. You can tell it all in the street.”

[036-123] “In the first place, I can’t say it in the street; secondly, you must

[036-124] hear Sofya Semyonovna too; and, thirdly, I will show you some papers....

[036-125] Oh well, if you won’t agree to come with me, I shall refuse to give

[036-126] any explanation and go away at once. But I beg you not to forget that

[036-127] a very curious secret of your beloved brother’s is entirely in my

[036-128] keeping.”

[036-129] Dounia stood still, hesitating, and looked at Svidrigaïlov with

[036-130] searching eyes.

[036-131] “What are you afraid of?” he observed quietly. “The town is not the

[036-132] country. And even in the country you did me more harm than I did you.”

[036-133] “Have you prepared Sofya Semyonovna?”

[036-134] “No, I have not said a word to her and am not quite certain whether she

[036-135] is at home now. But most likely she is. She has buried her stepmother

[036-136] to-day: she is not likely to go visiting on such a day. For the time I

[036-137] don’t want to speak to anyone about it and I half regret having spoken

[036-138] to you. The slightest indiscretion is as bad as betrayal in a thing like

[036-139] this. I live there in that house, we are coming to it. That’s the porter

[036-140] of our house--he knows me very well; you see, he’s bowing; he sees I’m

[036-141] coming with a lady and no doubt he has noticed your face already and you

[036-142] will be glad of that if you are afraid of me and suspicious. Excuse

[036-143] my putting things so coarsely. I haven’t a flat to myself; Sofya

[036-144] Semyonovna’s room is next to mine--she lodges in the next flat. The

[036-145] whole floor is let out in lodgings. Why are you frightened like a child?

[036-146] Am I really so terrible?”

[036-147] Svidrigaïlov’s lips were twisted in a condescending smile; but he was in

[036-148] no smiling mood. His heart was throbbing and he could scarcely breathe.

[036-149] He spoke rather loud to cover his growing excitement. But Dounia did not

[036-150] notice this peculiar excitement, she was so irritated by his remark that

[036-151] she was frightened of him like a child and that he was so terrible to

[036-152] her.

[036-153] “Though I know that you are not a man... of honour, I am not in the

[036-154] least afraid of you. Lead the way,” she said with apparent composure,

[036-155] but her face was very pale.

[036-156] Svidrigaïlov stopped at [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]’s room.

[036-157] “Allow me to inquire whether she is at home.... She is not. How

[036-158] unfortunate! But I know she may come quite soon. If she’s gone out, it

[036-159] can only be to see a lady about the orphans. Their mother is dead....

[036-160] I’ve been meddling and making arrangements for them. If Sofya Semyonovna

[036-161] does not come back in ten minutes, I will send her to you, to-day if

[036-162] you like. This is my flat. These are my two rooms. Madame Resslich,

[036-163] my landlady, has the next room. Now, look this way. I will show you

[036-164] my chief piece of evidence: this door from my bedroom leads into two

[036-165] perfectly empty rooms, which are to let. Here they are... You must look

[036-166] into them with some attention.”

[036-167] Svidrigaïlov occupied two fairly large furnished rooms. Dounia was

[036-168] looking about her mistrustfully, but saw nothing special in the

[036-173] Unlocking a door leading out of his bedroom, Svidrigaïlov showed Dounia

[036-174] the two empty rooms that were to let. Dounia stopped in the doorway, not

[036-175] knowing what she was called to look upon, but Svidrigaïlov hastened to

[036-176] explain.

[036-177] “Look here, at this second large room. Notice that door, it’s locked.

[036-178] By the door stands a chair, the only one in the two rooms. I brought it

[036-179] from my rooms so as to listen more conveniently. Just the other side of

[036-180] the door is Sofya Semyonovna’s table; she sat there talking to Rodion

[036-181] Romanovitch. And I sat here listening on two successive evenings, for

[036-182] two hours each time--and of course I was able to learn something, what

[036-183] do you think?”

[036-184] “You listened?”

[036-185] “Yes, I did. Now come back to my room; we can’t sit down here.”

[036-186] He brought Avdotya Romanovna back into his sitting-room and offered her

[036-187] a chair. He sat down at the opposite side of the table, at least seven

[036-188] feet from her, but probably there was the same glow in his eyes which

[036-203] believe you!”

[036-208] “Don’t torment me. Speak, speak!”

[036-228] “It cannot be. There was not the slightest cause, no sort of ground....

[036-229] It’s a lie, a lie!”
