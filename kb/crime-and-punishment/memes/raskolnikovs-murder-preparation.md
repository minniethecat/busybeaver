# Raskolnikov's Murder Preparation

Part 1, Chapter 6 of *Crime and Punishment* depicts the final hours before the murder of the pawnbroker, as [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] transforms abstract theory into practical criminality. The chapter is a study in the collision between intellectual justification and physical reality: the loop sewn into the coat, the stolen axe, the rehearsed timing. Dostoevsky renders the preparation in excruciating detail, emphasizing the gap between [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s Napoleonic self-image and the sordid mechanics of the act he is about to commit.

## Summary

Having received his mother's letter confirming [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s engagement to [[luzhin|Luzhin]], [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] spends the day in feverish agitation. The letter, paradoxically, crystallizes his resolve to commit the murder — the sacrifice of his sister for money mirrors [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s sacrifice, and he cannot bear to be impotent in the face of it. By evening, he moves from deliberation to execution: he sews a loop inside his overcoat to conceal an axe, plans his timing to coincide with [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s absence, and steals the axe from the porter's kitchen. Each practical step brings the "abstract" crime closer to reality, and [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s own consciousness registers the widening gap between theory and action.

## Context & Buildup

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has been nursing the idea of murder for weeks — "for the last month he had been thinking about it" (006-001). His visit to the pawnbroker's apartment earlier in Part 1 was a "rehearsal," a reconnaissance of the physical space and routine. The theory behind the crime — the [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary man theory]] — has been fully articulated in his article, published before the events of the novel.

The immediate catalyst is his mother's letter (Part 1, Chapter 3), which reveals [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s engagement to Luzhin and her impending move to [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]]. The letter confronts [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] with his own impotence: his sister is making a sacrificial marriage to secure his future, exactly as [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] prostituted herself for her family. The parallel is unbearable. In his feverish state, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] transforms the moral outrage into a resolution: "It must be done" (005-249).

The chapter opens with an apparently accidental discovery: on his walk through the [[hay-market|Hay Market]], [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] overhears that [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]], the pawnbroker's sister, will be away from the flat at exactly seven o'clock the next evening: "To-morrow at seven o'clock [[alyona-ivanovna|the old woman]] would be alone" (005-345). This [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] piece of intelligence transforms possibility into plan. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] feels "as though some invisible hand had arranged everything for him."

## Detailed Account

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s preparation unfolds in three mechanical stages. First, he sews the loop: "he had long ago prepared the thing that was needed for this — a narrow strip of something like tape, about an inch wide" (006-180). He sews it inside the left armpit of his overcoat, creating a sling that will hold the axe head concealed, the handle hanging down inside the coat. The sewing is done "automatically, almost without thinking" — as though his body is acting independently of his will.

Second, he confronts the axe problem. He had "decided long ago" that the murder "must be done with an axe" (006-238). He owns a small hatchet, but his plan requires the large axe from the kitchen. Timing becomes everything: he must obtain the axe without being seen, and he must return it before its absence is noticed. The plan calls for him to walk to the kitchen, take the axe, and an hour later — "when everything would be over" — return it (006-257).

Third, the actual theft of the axe becomes a crisis. When [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] enters the passageway heading for the kitchen, he is confronted with an unforeseen obstacle: "[[nastasya|Nastasya]] was not only at home in the kitchen at that hour, but she was busy there, taking out linen from a basket" (006-315). The plan collapses — and then, with the suddenness of [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] that the novel repeatedly deploys, a door catches his eye. The porter's shed was "wide open" and within it stood a gleam: "He dashed to the axe (it was an axe) and pulled it from under the bench" (006-336).

The theft of the axe marks the point of no return. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s internal state is one of strange detachment: "he felt that there was something almost mechanical about his movements, as though he were not walking on his own legs but being pushed from behind" (006-360). The philosophical murder has become a physical one, and even [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s own body seems to be following a script written by forces beyond his control.

## Immediate Aftermath

With the axe concealed in the loop, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] makes his way to the pawnbroker's building. The chapter ends with his arrival at [[alyona-ivanovna|the old woman]]'s door, where he rings the bell with a "firm, unhurried" hand. The careful preparation immediately begins to unravel in the actual commission — the second, unplanned murder of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]], the panicked fumbling with the lock, the narrow escape from the painters — but the chapter's focus is the psychological threshold: the moment when a man who has been thinking about murder for a month becomes, in fact, a murderer.

## Long-Term Consequences

The preparation scene establishes a pattern that persists throughout the novel: [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s careful plans are repeatedly undone by [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]]. The "invisible hand" of fate that seems to arrange the murder's timing ([[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s absence) is later complemented by its opposite — the unpredictable arrival of [[koch|Koch]] and [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]] at the door, forcing [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] to hide in the empty flat.

The axe itself becomes a symbolic object in the novel's moral economy. It is a tool of labor (chopping wood in the kitchen) transformed into an instrument of murder — a material emblem of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s transgression of ordinary morality. Its theft from the porter's shed, a space of communal domesticity, underscores the crime's violation of social order.

The preparation also exposes the contradiction at the heart of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s theory: if he is an extraordinary man acting beyond moral categories, why does the act require such elaborate concealment? The sling, the stolen axe, the rehearsed timing — these are the mechanics of common criminality, not the gestures of a Napoleon.

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

Dostoevsky's detailed rendering of the murder preparation serves a specific narrative purpose: it demystifies the crime. The novel's philosophical argument against utilitarianism and nihilism operates not through abstract rebuttal but through concrete detail. By showing the gap between theory (the extraordinary man who may "step over" moral law) and practice (the need to sew a loop, steal an axe, time one's movements), the preparation chapter performs a critique of all ideology that claims to transcend material reality.

The chapter also establishes the novel's treatment of [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] and fate. The overheard conversation about [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s schedule, the open porter's door — these are "providential" coincidences that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] interprets as signs. But the same pattern of [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] will later work against him (the painters, the tradesman's accusation), suggesting that what looks like fate is merely contingency — and that the criminal who depends on luck is gambling everything on randomness.

## Characters Involved

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]** — The protagonist, moving from abstract theorizing to the mechanical details of murder: sewing the loop, stealing the axe, timing the act.
- **[[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]]** — The pawnbroker, absent from the scene but present as the intended victim, her daily routine (and [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s) the object of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s calculation.
- **[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]]** — The pawnbroker's half-sister, whose scheduled absence at seven o'clock becomes the crucial timing variable that sets the murder plan in motion (005-345).
- **[[nastasya|Nastasya]]** — The servant whose unexpected presence in the kitchen forces [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] to improvise, leading to the theft of the axe from the porter's shed instead.
