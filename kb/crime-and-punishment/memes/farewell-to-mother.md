# Farewell to Mother

The farewell to his mother occurs in Part 6, Chapter 7 — the evening before [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] surrenders himself to the authorities. After his final conversation with [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], who has urged him to confess, and his sister [[dounia|Dounia]]'s silent acknowledgment of his guilt, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] goes to his mother's lodging to say goodbye. The scene is one of the novel's most emotionally devastating passages: [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], on the verge of confession, speaks to his mother in veiled language — telling her he must go away, that she will be unhappy, that she must believe her son loves her "now more than himself" (038-097). [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]], who has sensed the impending catastrophe for weeks, blesses him and lets him go, never learning the truth of what her son has done.

## Summary

At about seven o'clock in the evening, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] arrives at his mother and sister's lodging in Bakaleyev's house, "appallingly dressed," his clothes torn and dirty from the previous night's storm, his face "almost distorted from fatigue, exposure, the inward conflict" (038-010). [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] is not home — she has been keeping secrets from her mother — and [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] finds himself alone with [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] in what will be their final meeting. He tells her he must go away; asks her whether she will always love him "whatever you hear about me, whatever you are told about me" (038-087–088); and receives her blessing. She, sensing the gravity of the moment but unable to name it, weeps and clings to him, comparing his presence at her side to the days of his childhood (038-134). He tears himself away, instructing her to pray for him, and leaves without seeing [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] — a deliberate choice, since [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] already knows or suspects the truth.

## Context & Buildup

Since arriving in [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]], [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] has watched her son deteriorate without understanding why. She read his article "On Crime" — published pseudonymously — and recognized his intellectual brilliance, but could not connect it to the "fatal hour" she sensed approaching (038-142). Throughout the novel, she has been the character most willfully blind to [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s condition: her love for him is so absolute that she cannot entertain the possibility of his moral ruin.

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has just spent the night wandering the streets in the rain after [[svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s suicide. He has seen [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], who gave him her cross and urged him to confess at the crossroads. He has seen [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]], who looked at him with a "look of anguish" that told him she knew. Now he comes to his mother — not to confess (he cannot bring himself to tell her the truth), but to say goodbye forever.

## Detailed Account

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] arrives at his mother's lodging. [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] opens the door, and her joy at seeing him is immediate and overwhelming: she is "speechless with joy and surprise," laughing through her tears (038-018–019). She immediately tries to manage his expectations — "Don't be angry with me, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodya]], for welcoming you so foolishly with tears" — and assures him that she has learned not to cross-question him "in the womanish way" she used to (038-026).

She shows him his published article — she has been reading it for the third time — and tells him she sees his future as one of the "leading men" in the world of Russian thought (038-050). The irony is devastating: [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] holds in his hands the intellectual justification for the murder he committed, while his mother beams with pride at her son's philosophical achievement. He flings the article on the table "with disgust and anger" (038-048).

Then the tone shifts. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] asks whether [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] is home — she is not — and tells his mother he must leave. [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] begins to cry. He asks her, "Mother, whatever happens, whatever you hear about me, whatever you are told about me, will you always love me as you do now?" (038-087–088). She, in her innocence, cannot understand the question: "Why, who will tell me anything about you?" (038-092).

He tells her: "I have come to tell you that though you will be unhappy, you must believe that your son loves you now more than himself, and that all you thought about me, that I was cruel and didn't care about you, was wrong" (038-096–098). [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] embraces him in silence, pressing him to her bosom. She tells him she has sensed "a great sorrow in store for you" (038-105) — that she lies awake at night thinking of it, that [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] talks in her sleep about him.

He asks her to kneel and pray to God for him. She blesses him and signs him with the cross. In their final moments together, she recalls his childhood: "now you are just as when you were little. You would run like this to me and hug me and kiss me" (038-134–135). He tears himself away at last — "Good-bye, mother" — and she clutches him, gazing despairingly into his eyes, asking "is it far where you are going?" (038-148). His answer: "What God sends... only pray for me" (038-151).

## Immediate Aftermath

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] leaves without seeing [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] — a deliberate choice, for she knows too much and would demand the truth. [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] is left alone with her foreboding and her love. She will never see her son again, and she will never learn the truth of what he has done: Dostoevsky spares her this knowledge, allowing her to die believing in her son's essential goodness — a mercy that the novel's Christian framework presents as both tragic and redemptive.

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] proceeds to the [[hay-market|Hay Market]], where he will kneel, kiss the ground, and try to confess — the gesture [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] demanded of him. But the crowd laughs, and he cannot say the words aloud. He goes instead to the police station, where he will finally make his formal confession.

## Long-Term Consequences

[[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]], unlike [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] or [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], is never told of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s crime. She falls ill after his departure, her health deteriorating from the strain of not knowing what has happened to her son. She dies — "her reason was affected... she fell into a fever and died delirious" — without ever learning that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is a murderer. Her death is one of the novel's most quietly devastating consequences: the collateral damage of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s crime extends to the person who loved him most purely.

For [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], the farewell to his mother is the last human connection he severs before his confession. He has said goodbye to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] (who knows the truth), to [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] (who suspects it), and now to his mother (who cannot be allowed to know). Each farewell strips away another layer of his former identity. By the time he kneels in the [[hay-market|Hay Market]], he is almost nothing — a man without a mother, without a future, waiting only for the formal acknowledgment of what he has become.

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The farewell to mother is the novel's purest expression of unconditional love — the love that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s extraordinary-man theory cannot account for. [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] asks nothing of her son; she requires no confession, no justification, no explanation. Her love exists outside the moral calculus that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has tried (and failed) to impose on human existence.

The scene also dramatizes the cost of concealment. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has spent the novel hiding his crime from everyone — but hiding it from his mother requires a different order of deception. He cannot tell her the truth, not because she would reject him (she would not), but because the truth would destroy her. The paradox is cruel: his love for her is what prevents him from being honest with her. The farewell is thus a kind of confession in negative — everything said except the thing that matters, every gesture toward truth stopping short of disclosure.

[[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]]'s death, too, has structural significance. Her love — so pure, so blind, so all-forgiving — cannot survive in the moral universe [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has created. Her death is the novel's final judgment on his crime: not the legal judgment of the state, but the human judgment of a mother's broken heart.

## Characters Involved

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]** — The protagonist, who visits his mother to say goodbye without revealing his crime. His question "will you always love me?" (038-087) is the emotional climax of his relationship with his mother, expressing both love and the terror of being known.
- **[[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]]** — [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s mother, whose unconditional love for her son is one of the novel's moral constants. She senses the catastrophe approaching but never learns its nature. Her blessing is the last gesture of maternal love [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] receives before his confession (038-125).
