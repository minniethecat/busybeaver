[021-001] “Can this be still a dream?” [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] thought once more.

[021-002] He looked carefully and suspiciously at the unexpected visitor.

[021-003] “Svidrigaïlov! What nonsense! It can’t be!” he said at last aloud in

[021-004] bewilderment.

[021-005] His visitor did not seem at all surprised at this exclamation.

[021-006] “I’ve come to you for two reasons. In the first place, I wanted to make

[021-007] your personal acquaintance, as I have already heard a great deal about

[021-008] you that is interesting and flattering; secondly, I cherish the hope

[021-009] that you may not refuse to assist me in a matter directly concerning the

[021-010] welfare of your sister, Avdotya Romanovna. For without your support she

[021-011] might not let me come near her now, for she is prejudiced against me,

[021-012] but with your assistance I reckon on...”

[021-013] “You reckon wrongly,” interrupted Raskolnikov.

[021-014] “They only arrived yesterday, may I ask you?”

[021-015] Raskolnikov made no reply.

[021-016] “It was yesterday, I know. I only arrived myself the day before. Well,

[021-017] let me tell you this, Rodion Romanovitch, I don’t consider it necessary

[021-018] to justify myself, but kindly tell me what was there particularly

[021-019] criminal on my part in all this business, speaking without prejudice,

[021-020] with common sense?”

[021-021] Raskolnikov continued to look at him in silence.

[021-022] “That in my own house I persecuted a defenceless girl and ‘insulted her

[021-023] with my infamous proposals’--is that it? (I am anticipating you.) But

[021-024] you’ve only to assume that I, too, am a man _et nihil humanum_... in a

[021-025] word, that I am capable of being attracted and falling in love (which

[021-026] does not depend on our will), then everything can be explained in the

[021-027] most natural manner. The question is, am I a monster, or am I myself

[021-028] a victim? And what if I am a victim? In proposing to the object of my

[021-029] passion to elope with me to America or Switzerland, I may have cherished

[021-030] the deepest respect for her and may have thought that I was promoting

[021-031] our mutual happiness! Reason is the slave of passion, you know; why,

[021-032] probably, I was doing more harm to myself than anyone!”

[021-033] “But that’s not the point,” Raskolnikov interrupted with disgust. “It’s

[021-034] simply that whether you are right or wrong, we dislike you. We don’t

[021-035] want to have anything to do with you. We show you the door. Go out!”

[021-036] Svidrigaïlov broke into a sudden laugh.

[021-037] “But you’re... but there’s no getting round you,” he said, laughing in

[021-038] the frankest way. “I hoped to get round you, but you took up the right

[021-039] line at once!”

[021-040] “But you are trying to get round me still!”

[021-041] “What of it? What of it?” cried Svidrigaïlov, laughing openly. “But this

[021-042] is what the French call _bonne guerre_, and the most innocent form of

[021-043] deception!... But still you have interrupted me; one way or another, I

[021-044] repeat again: there would never have been any unpleasantness except for

[021-045] what happened in the garden. [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]]...”

[021-046] “You have got rid of Marfa Petrovna, too, so they say?” Raskolnikov

[021-047] interrupted rudely.

[021-048] “Oh, you’ve heard that, too, then? You’d be sure to, though.... But

[021-049] as for your question, I really don’t know what to say, though my own

[021-050] conscience is quite at rest on that score. Don’t suppose that I am in

[021-051] any apprehension about it. All was regular and in order; the medical

[021-052] inquiry diagnosed apoplexy due to bathing immediately after a heavy

[021-053] dinner and a bottle of wine, and indeed it could have proved nothing

[021-054] else. But I’ll tell you what I have been thinking to myself of late, on

[021-055] my way here in the train, especially: didn’t I contribute to all that...

[021-056] calamity, morally, in a way, by irritation or something of the

[021-057] sort. But I came to the conclusion that that, too, was quite out of the

[021-058] question.”

[021-059] Raskolnikov laughed.

[021-060] “I wonder you trouble yourself about it!”

[021-061] “But what are you laughing at? Only consider, I struck her just twice

[021-062] with a switch--there were no marks even... don’t regard me as a cynic,

[021-063] please; I am perfectly aware how atrocious it was of me and all that;

[021-064] but I know for certain, too, that Marfa Petrovna was very likely pleased

[021-065] at my, so to say, warmth. The story of your sister had been wrung out to

[021-066] the last drop; for the last three days Marfa Petrovna had been forced to

[021-067] sit at home; she had nothing to show herself with in the town. Besides,

[021-068] she had bored them so with that letter (you heard about her reading the

[021-069] letter). And all of a sudden those two switches fell from heaven! Her

[021-070] first act was to order the carriage to be got out.... Not to speak

[021-071] of the fact that there are cases when women are very, very glad to be

[021-072] insulted in spite of all their show of indignation. There are instances

[021-073] of it with everyone; human beings in general, indeed, greatly love to

[021-074] be insulted, have you noticed that? But it’s particularly so with women.

[021-075] One might even say it’s their only amusement.”

[021-076] At one time Raskolnikov thought of getting up and walking out and so

[021-077] finishing the interview. But some curiosity and even a sort of prudence

[021-078] made him linger for a moment.

[021-079] “You are fond of fighting?” he asked carelessly.

[021-080] “No, not very,” Svidrigaïlov answered, calmly. “And Marfa Petrovna and

[021-081] I scarcely ever fought. We lived very harmoniously, and she was always

[021-082] pleased with me. I only used the whip twice in all our seven years (not

[021-083] counting a third occasion of a very ambiguous character). The first

[021-084] time, two months after our marriage, immediately after we arrived in the

[021-085] country, and the last time was that of which we are speaking. Did you

[021-086] suppose I was such a monster, such a reactionary, such a slave driver?

[021-087] Ha, ha! By the way, do you remember, Rodion Romanovitch, how a few years

[021-088] ago, in those days of beneficent publicity, a nobleman, I’ve forgotten

[021-089] his name, was put to shame everywhere, in all the papers, for having

[021-090] thrashed a German woman in the railway train. You remember? It was in

[021-091] those days, that very year I believe, the ‘disgraceful action of the

[021-092] _Age_’ took place (you know, ‘The Egyptian Nights,’ that public reading,

[021-093] you remember? The dark eyes, you know! Ah, the golden days of our youth,

[021-094] where are they?). Well, as for the gentleman who thrashed the German,

[021-095] I feel no sympathy with him, because after all what need is there

[021-096] for sympathy? But I must say that there are sometimes such provoking

[021-097] ‘Germans’ that I don’t believe there is a progressive who could quite

[021-098] answer for himself. No one looked at the subject from that point of view

[021-099] then, but that’s the truly humane point of view, I assure you.”

[021-100] After saying this, Svidrigaïlov broke into a sudden laugh again.

[021-101] Raskolnikov saw clearly that this was a man with a firm purpose in his

[021-102] mind and able to keep it to himself.

[021-103] “I expect you’ve not talked to anyone for some days?” he asked.

[021-104] “Scarcely anyone. I suppose you are wondering at my being such an

[021-105] adaptable man?”

[021-106] “No, I am only wondering at your being too adaptable a man.”

[021-107] “Because I am not offended at the rudeness of your questions? Is that

[021-108] it? But why take offence? As you asked, so I answered,” he replied,

[021-109] with a surprising expression of simplicity. “You know, there’s

[021-110] hardly anything I take interest in,” he went on, as it were dreamily,

[021-111] “especially now, I’ve nothing to do.... You are quite at liberty to

[021-112] imagine though that I am making up to you with a motive, particularly as

[021-113] I told you I want to see your sister about something. But I’ll confess

[021-115] delighted to see you.... Don’t be angry, Rodion Romanovitch, but you

[021-116] seem to be somehow awfully strange yourself. Say what you like, there’s

[021-117] something wrong with you, and now, too... not this very minute, I mean,

[021-118] but now, generally.... Well, well, I won’t, I won’t, don’t scowl! I am

[021-119] not such a bear, you know, as you think.”

[021-120] Raskolnikov looked gloomily at him.

[021-121] “You are not a bear, perhaps, at all,” he said. “I fancy indeed that

[021-122] you are a man of very good breeding, or at least know how on occasion to

[021-123] behave like one.”

[021-124] “I am not particularly interested in anyone’s opinion,” Svidrigaïlov

[021-125] answered, dryly and even with a shade of haughtiness, “and therefore why

[021-126] not be vulgar at times when vulgarity is such a convenient cloak for our

[021-127] climate... and especially if one has a natural propensity that way,” he

[021-128] added, laughing again.

[021-129] “But I’ve heard you have many friends here. You are, as they say, ‘not

[021-130] without connections.’ What can you want with me, then, unless you’ve

[021-131] some special object?”

[021-132] “That’s true that I have friends here,” Svidrigaïlov admitted, not

[021-133] replying to the chief point. “I’ve met some already. I’ve been lounging

[021-134] about for the last three days, and I’ve seen them, or they’ve seen me.

[021-135] That’s a matter of course. I am well dressed and reckoned not a poor

[021-136] man; the emancipation of the serfs hasn’t affected me; my property

[021-137] consists chiefly of forests and water meadows. The revenue has not

[021-139] ago. I’ve been here three days and have called on no one.... What a town

[021-140] it is! How has it come into existence among us, tell me that? A town of

[021-141] officials and students of all sorts. Yes, there’s a great deal I didn’t

[021-142] notice when I was here eight years ago, kicking up my heels.... My only

[021-143] hope now is in anatomy, by Jove, it is!”

[021-144] “Anatomy?”

[021-145] “But as for these clubs, Dussauts, parades, or progress, indeed,

[021-147] noticing the question. “Besides, who wants to be a card-sharper?”

[021-149] “How could I help being? There was a regular set of us, men of the best

[021-152] society the best manners are found among those who’ve been thrashed,

[021-155] Marfa Petrovna turned up; she bargained with him and bought me off for

[021-156] thirty thousand silver pieces (I owed seventy thousand). We were united

[021-157] in lawful wedlock and she bore me off into the country like a treasure.

[021-158] You know she was five years older than I. She was very fond of me. For

[021-159] seven years I never left the country. And, take note, that all my life

[021-160] she held a document over me, the IOU for thirty thousand roubles, so

[021-161] if I were to elect to be restive about anything I should be trapped at

[021-164] “If it hadn’t been for that, would you have given her the slip?”

[021-165] “I don’t know what to say. It was scarcely the document restrained me. I

[021-171] blames others for everything and excuses oneself. I should have gone

[021-172] perhaps on an expedition to the North Pole, because _j’ai le vin

[021-173] mauvais_ and hate drinking, and there’s nothing left but wine. I have

[021-174] tried it. But, I say, I’ve been told Berg is going up in a great balloon

[021-175] next Sunday from the [[yusupov-garden|Yusupov Garden]] and will take up passengers at a

[021-176] fee. Is it true?”

[021-177] “Why, would you go up?”

[021-178] “I... No, oh, no,” muttered Svidrigaïlov really seeming to be deep in

[021-179] thought.

[021-181] “No, the document didn’t restrain me,” Svidrigaïlov went on,

[021-182] meditatively. “It was my own doing, not leaving the country, and nearly

[021-183] a year ago Marfa Petrovna gave me back the document on my name-day

[021-185] fortune, you know. ‘You see how I trust you, Arkady Ivanovitch’--that

[021-186] was actually her expression. You don’t believe she used it? But do

[021-187] you know I managed the estate quite decently, they know me in the

[021-188] neighbourhood. I ordered books, too. Marfa Petrovna at first approved,

[021-189] but afterwards she was afraid of my over-studying.”

[021-190] “You seem to be missing Marfa Petrovna very much?”

[021-191] “Missing her? Perhaps. Really, perhaps I am. And, by the way, do you

[021-192] believe in ghosts?”

[021-193] “What ghosts?”

[021-194] “Why, ordinary ghosts.”

[021-195] “Do you believe in them?”

[021-196] “Perhaps not, _pour vous plaire_.... I wouldn’t say no exactly.”

[021-197] “Do you see them, then?”

[021-198] Svidrigaïlov looked at him rather oddly.

[021-199] “Marfa Petrovna is pleased to visit me,” he said, twisting his mouth

[021-200] into a strange smile.

[021-202] “She has been three times. I saw her first on the very day of the

[021-203] funeral, an hour after she was buried. It was the day before I left to

[021-208] “Quite awake. I was wide awake every time. She comes, speaks to me for

[021-211] “What made me think that something of the sort must be happening to

[021-212] you?” Raskolnikov said suddenly.

[021-214] excited.

[021-216] really? Didn’t I say that there was something in common between us, eh?”

[021-218] “Didn’t I?”

[021-219] “No!”

[021-220] “I thought I did. When I came in and saw you lying with your eyes shut,

[021-222] “What do you mean by ‘the man?’ What are you talking about?” cried

[021-223] Raskolnikov.

[021-224] “What do I mean? I really don’t know....” Svidrigaïlov muttered

[021-225] ingenuously, as though he, too, were puzzled.
