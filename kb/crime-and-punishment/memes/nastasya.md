# Nastasya

Nastasya is the cook and only servant at the lodging-house where [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] rents his [[raskolnikovs-garret|garret]]. "The cook and only servant" of the landlady [[praskovya-pavlovna|Praskovya Pavlovna]] (003-026), she is one of the few characters who has regular, unguarded contact with Raskolnikov during the period of his illness and isolation after the murder. Her practical kindness — bringing him food on credit, expressing simple concern for his health — contrasts with the psychological intensity of Raskolnikov's relationships with [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]], and his family.

## Overview

Nastasya first appears in the novel's opening pages, where Raskolnikov "went without his dinner" because "Nastasya, the cook and only servant, was rather pleased at the lodger's mood" (003-026). She is characterized by her unpolished speech, her willingness to extend credit for meals, and her genuine if unsophisticated concern for Raskolnikov's well-being. When she finds him ill, she exclaims: "Are you ill, eh?" and brings him food on a "battered tray" (006-139–006-147). In a novel where every relationship is charged with philosophical meaning or psychological tension, Nastasya represents the ordinary human decency that Raskolnikov's intellectual pride has cut him off from.

## Background

Nastasya is a peasant woman employed as a servant by Praskovya Pavlovna, the landlady of Raskolnikov's building. She is uneducated, superstitious, and speaks with the colloquial directness of her class. Her world is entirely practical — food, cleaning, rent — and she has no intellectual or spiritual dimension.

## Role in the Novel

Nastasya appears in the novel's early chapters as a recurring presence in Raskolnikov's daily life. She brings him tea and cabbage soup, observes his strange behavior, and reports on the landlady's complaints about unpaid rent. She is the first person to notice that something is wrong with Raskolnikov — "You are raving, aren't you?" (003-092) — though she attributes his condition to illness rather than crime. Her function is to anchor Raskolnikov's psychological drama in the physical realities of food, sleep, and rent — the bodily needs that persist even as his mind spirals toward confession.

## Key Relationships

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]**: Her lodger, to whom she extends the simple kindness of food brought on credit. Their interactions are the novel's most ordinary human exchanges — free of the philosophical and moral intensity that characterizes Raskolnikov's other relationships.
- **[[praskovya-pavlovna|Praskovya Pavlovna]]**: Her employer, the landlady who threatens to complain to the police about Raskolnikov's unpaid rent.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Nastasya represents the mundane world of bodily needs and ordinary kindness that Raskolnikov's crime has severed him from. Her concern — "Are you ill, eh?" — is the novel's most basic expression of human care, and Raskolnikov's inability to receive it without suspicion or irritation measures his spiritual isolation. In Dostoevsky's symbolic economy, Nastasya is the anti-Raskolnikov: practical where he is theoretical, content with her station where he is ambitious, connected to others through service where he is isolated by pride.
