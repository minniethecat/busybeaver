# Svidrigailov's Dreams

The sequence of dreams [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] experiences on his last night — in a squalid hotel room on the eve of his suicide — is one of the most concentrated passages of psychological horror in all of Dostoevsky. Occurring in Part 6, Chapter 6, the dreams function as a moral judgment delivered by the unconscious. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], who has lived a life of cynical hedonism and moral indifference, encounters in three visions everything his philosophy has suppressed: the innocent girl he drove to suicide, the idea of a spiritual purity he has violated, and the corruption of childhood innocence into depravity. Each dream strips away another layer of his worldly armor, leaving him with nothing but the revolver.

## Summary

Having settled his affairs — providing for [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and the orphans, giving money to his young fiancée's family — [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] rents a tiny, squalid room in a cheap hotel on the outskirts of [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] during a stormy night. Unable to sleep, he drifts through three nightmares. The first is of a mouse scratching — a "nasty, grey" creature that he chases around the room in futile pursuit (037-272). The second vision is of a drowned fourteen-year-old girl laid out in a coffin among flowers in a beautiful country house — a suicide he recognizes (037-304–318). The third and most devastating: he rescues a five-year-old girl from the corridor, undresses her damp clothes, puts her to bed — and then watches in horror as her innocent sleeping face transforms into the "shameless face of a French harlot," winking at him with knowing depravity (037-379–399). He wakes, goes out into the mist, and shoots himself before the eyes of a Jewish soldier in an Achilles helmet.

## Context & Buildup

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] has spent the day making final arrangements. He has visited [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], providing money for her to follow [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] to Siberia, and arranged for the Kapernaumov orphans to be placed in institutions. He has visited his young fiancée's family, giving them fifteen thousand roubles. These acts of apparent generosity are not repentance but finality — [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] is settling accounts before departure (037-001–100).

The atmosphere is apocalyptic: thunder, rain, and a rising river. The flood signals from the Peter and Paul Fortress — cannon shots every hour — punctuate the night (037-329). [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] chooses a cheap hotel "towards the end" of a wooden street, a "God-forsaken place" (037-160). The room is "close and tiny," "under the [[staircases|stairs]]," smelling of mice and damp (037-168–222).

Before the dreams begin, [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s state of mind is one of exhausted indifference. He has accepted his suicide as inevitable — he told [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] earlier that he had "two alternatives: a bullet in the brain or Siberia" (037-067) — and now there is nothing left but to wait for dawn. His interest in the "fantastic" — the dreams — is noted by the narrator: "Perhaps the cold, or the dampness, or the dark, or the wind that howled under the window and tossed the trees roused a sort of persistent craving for the fantastic" (037-287–289).

## Detailed Account

### The First Dream: The Mouse

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] drifts into drowsiness and becomes fixated on a mouse "scratching in the corner" (037-222). The mouse-sounds transform into a full hallucination: "He shook the blanket and suddenly a mouse jumped out on the sheet. He tried to catch it, but the mouse ran to and fro in zigzags" (037-272–273). The dream is absurd and degrading — a man who has committed terrible deeds reduced to chasing a mouse on his bed. The mouse represents the squalor of his existence, the vermin-like quality of a life without moral purpose.

### The Second Dream: The Drowned Girl

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] sinks deeper and enters a vision of extraordinary beauty: a flower garden, "a bright, warm, almost hot day — Trinity day," a "fine, sumptuous country cottage in the English taste overgrown with fragrant flowers" (037-291–292). He ascends a "light, cool staircase, carpeted with rich rugs," noting "nosegays of tender, white, heavily fragrant narcissus" (037-294–296).

In the drawing room, on a table "covered with a white satin shroud, stood a coffin" (037-304). "Among the flowers lay a girl in a white muslin dress," her "loose fair hair was wet; there was a wreath of roses on her head" (037-306–309). The smile on her "pale lips was full of an immense unchildish misery and sorrowful appeal" (037-311). "Svidrigaïlov knew that girl; there was no holy image, no burning candle beside the coffin; no sound of prayers: the girl had drowned herself. She was only fourteen, but her heart was broken. And she had destroyed herself, crushed by an insult that had appalled and amazed that childish soul" (037-312–316).

This is the first of [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s crimes to surface in the dreams. The drowned girl — a suicide caused by his predation — appears not as an accuser but as a silent victim, her unchildish misery the only judgment. The absence of religious imagery (no icon, no candle, no prayers) suggests the spiritual void in which her death occurred: [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s world has no framework for atonement.

### The Third Dream: The Five-Year-Old Prostitute

The most harrowing vision begins innocently. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] finds a "girl, not more than five years old, shivering and crying, with her clothes as wet as a soaking house-flannel" in the corridor (037-350–351). She has run away from her mother, a drunken cook, after breaking a cup. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] — in what seems an act of humanity — "took her in his arms, went back to his room, sat her on the bed, and began undressing her" (037-367–368). He wraps her in the blanket and she "fell asleep at once" (037-372).

Then comes the transformation. When he checks on her, "her crimson lips were hot and glowing; but what was this? He suddenly fancied that her long black eyelashes were quivering, as though the lids were opening and a sly crafty eye peeped out with an unchildlike wink" (037-383–386). The child's face transforms: "her lips parted in a smile... now it was a grin, a broad grin; there was something shameless, provocative in that quite unchildish face; it was depravity, it was the face of a harlot, the shameless face of a French harlot" (037-388–392).

The horror culminates in [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s recognition: "What, at five years old?" (037-396). He raises his hand to strike her — and wakes. The dream is the novel's most concentrated image of corrupted innocence, and it functions as the final refutation of [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s libertine philosophy. The child who becomes a prostitute in his unconscious is the logical endpoint of his worldview: if nothing is sacred, then nothing — not even childhood — remains uncorrupted.

## Immediate Aftermath

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] wakes "in the same bed, still wrapped in the blanket" at dawn (037-400). "I've had nightmare all night!" He gets up "angrily, feeling utterly shattered; his bones ached" (037-402). There is "a thick mist outside" — the same miasma that pervades [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] throughout the novel, associated with moral and spiritual confusion.

With mechanical calm, he checks his revolver, writes a note in his notebook — "his death, he wrote, had nothing to do with his illness" (037-406–408) — and goes out. He encounters the Jewish soldier (Achilles) at the fire tower and engages in the novel's strangest dialogue before death: "I am going to foreign parts, brother." "To foreign parts?" "To America" (037-447–450). "America" is [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s private code for suicide — annihilation as emigration. After a brief, absurd exchange about whether "this is not the place for such jokes," [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] "put the revolver to his right temple" and "pulled the trigger" (037-458–461).

## Long-Term Consequences

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s suicide has profound consequences for the novel's resolution. His death eliminates [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s tormentor and removes the threat that has driven much of Part 6's suspense. More importantly, it establishes a moral contrast with [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]: [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] chooses death over confession, while [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] (partly through seeing [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s fate) chooses confession over death.

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s generous bequests — the money for [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], for the orphans, for his fiancée — create a paradox that challenges the novel's moral calculus. A man capable of genuine kindness (and his provision for the orphans is genuine) is also capable of driving a fourteen-year-old girl to suicide. The coexistence of generosity and predation in the same person is more disturbing than pure evil would be — it suggests that moral corruption is compatible with ordinary human decency, that the line between good and evil runs through every human heart.

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The dream sequence is the novel's most explicit treatment of the unconscious as a moral agent. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], who has built his identity on cynical detachment, cannot escape judgment in sleep. The dreams are not random — they are a systematic indictment. The mouse: the squalor of a degraded life. The drowned girl: the consequence of his predation. The corrupted child: the logical endpoint of a philosophy that treats innocence as an illusion. Each dream is more devastating than the last, and together they make suicide not a choice but an inevitability.

The sequence also completes the novel's implicit argument about confession. [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]], who has committed crimes as serious as [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s, never confesses — not to the law, not to himself. His "confession" takes the form of nightmares that his waking consciousness cannot process. The contrast with [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s eventual confession to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and to the police is deliberate: confession (to another person, to the community) is the only path to reintegration; silence leads to annihilation.

The "America" code — suicide as emigration — resonates with the novel's geography of redemption. Alaska (the Russian far east, Siberia) is [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s destination — a place of suffering that leads to rebirth. America is [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s destination — a place of absolute otherness, of disappearance. The two "emigrations" represent the novel's two possible endings: suffering that leads to life, or escape that leads to nothing.

## Characters Involved

- **[[svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]** — The libertine landowner whose final night strips away every defense of cynicism, culminating in suicide.
- **The Drowned Girl** — A fourteen-year-old suicide victim, "crushed by an insult" — one of [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s victims, appearing in the second dream as silent accusation (037-312–316).
- **The Five-Year-Old Girl** — An abandoned child whose innocent face transforms into the face of a harlot in [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s most harrowing dream (037-379–399).
- **The Soldier (Achilles)** — The Jewish fire-watchman in a copper helmet who witnesses [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]'s suicide and protests that "this is not the place" (037-435–460).
