# Sensuality and Depravity

Sensuality and depravity, embodied in the character of Arkady Ivanovich Svidrigaïlov, represent the pursuit of sensual gratification unconstrained by moral law. While Raskolnikov transgresses through an intellectual theory, Svidrigaïlov transgresses through appetite. Together they form the novel's dual exploration of what happens when a person steps beyond conventional morality.

## Definition

Svidrigaïlov defines his own condition succinctly: "Through idleness and depravity" (021-345). The pairing is significant: depravity is not presented as a conscious philosophical position but as the natural consequence of a life without purpose or moral restraint. His sensuality is not the passion of a tempestuous nature but something colder — the systematic pursuit of pleasure by a man who has ceased to believe in anything.

## Expression in the Novel

Svidrigaïlov arrives in Petersburg as a figure from Dunia's past — the former employer who sexually harassed her. His pursuit of Dunia is the novel's most direct depiction of sensual predation, but his character is more complex than a simple villain. His philanthropic acts (providing for the Marmeladov children after Katerina Ivanovna's death, arranging Sonia's financial security) coexist with his predatory behavior. He tells Raskolnikov of his visits to the "depravity which obscures the mind" (024-299), describing a descent into sensation that deadens rather than excites.

The novel distinguishes between external and internal corruption. Of Dunia, it is said that "not one drop of real depravity had penetrated to her heart" (024-296) despite Svidrigaïlov's attempts. The face of a child prostitute is described as "depravity, it was the face of a harlot" (037-391), showing how depravity marks its victims. Svidrigaïlov's ultimate fate — suicide — suggests that sensuality without meaning leads to self-annihilation.

## Character Perspectives

- **Arkady Svidrigaïlov**: The primary embodiment. His sensuality is inseparable from his nihilism — he pursues pleasure precisely because he believes in nothing. His attempted seduction of Dunia and his final dream of a child-prostitute reveal the emptiness at the core of the sensual life.

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Recognizes in Svidrigaïlov a distorted mirror of himself. Both have stepped outside moral law, but Svidrigaïlov's path is one of appetite rather than theory, and Raskolnikov is repulsed.
