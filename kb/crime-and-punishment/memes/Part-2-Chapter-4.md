[011-001] [[zossimov|Zossimov]] was a tall, fat man with a puffy, colourless, clean-shaven face

[011-002] and straight flaxen hair. He wore spectacles, and a big gold ring on

[011-003] his fat finger. He was twenty-seven. He had on a light grey fashionable

[011-004] loose coat, light summer trousers, and everything about him loose,

[011-005] fashionable and spick and span; his linen was irreproachable, his

[011-006] watch-chain was massive. In manner he was slow and, as it were,

[011-007] nonchalant, and at the same time studiously free and easy; he made

[011-008] efforts to conceal his self-importance, but it was apparent at every

[011-009] instant. All his acquaintances found him tedious, but said he was clever

[011-010] at his work.

[011-011] “I’ve been to you twice to-day, brother. You see, he’s come to himself,”

[011-012] cried [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]].

[011-013] “I see, I see; and how do we feel now, eh?” said Zossimov to

[011-014] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], watching him carefully and, sitting down at the foot of the

[011-015] sofa, he settled himself as comfortably as he could.

[011-016] “He is still depressed,” Razumihin went on. “We’ve just changed his

[011-017] linen and he almost cried.”

[011-018] “That’s very natural; you might have put it off if he did not wish

[011-019] it.... His pulse is first-rate. Is your head still aching, eh?”

[011-020] “I am well, I am perfectly well!” Raskolnikov declared positively

[011-021] and irritably. He raised himself on the sofa and looked at them with

[011-022] glittering eyes, but sank back on to the pillow at once and turned to

[011-023] the wall. Zossimov watched him intently.

[011-024] “Very good.... Going on all right,” he said lazily. “Has he eaten

[011-025] anything?”

[011-026] They told him, and asked what he might have.

[011-027] “He may have anything... soup, tea... mushrooms and cucumbers, of

[011-028] course, you must not give him; he’d better not have meat either, and...

[011-029] but no need to tell you that!” Razumihin and he looked at each

[011-030] other. “No more medicine or anything. I’ll look at him again to-morrow.

[011-031] Perhaps, to-day even... but never mind...”

[011-032] “To-morrow evening I shall take him for a walk,” said Razumihin. “We are

[011-033] going to the Yusupov garden and then to the Palais de Cristal.”

[011-034] “I would not disturb him to-morrow at all, but I don’t know... a little,

[011-035] maybe... but we’ll see.”

[011-036] “Ach, what a nuisance! I’ve got a house-warming party to-night; it’s

[011-037] only a step from here. Couldn’t he come? He could lie on the sofa. You

[011-038] are coming?” Razumihin said to Zossimov. “Don’t forget, you promised.”

[011-039] “All right, only rather later. What are you going to do?”

[011-040] “Oh, nothing--tea, vodka, herrings. There will be a pie... just our

[011-041] friends.”

[011-042] “And who?”

[011-043] “All neighbours here, almost all new friends, except my old uncle, and

[011-044] he is new too--he only arrived in [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] yesterday to see to some

[011-045] business of his. We meet once in five years.”

[011-046] “What is he?”

[011-047] “He’s been stagnating all his life as a district postmaster; gets a

[011-048] little pension. He is sixty-five--not worth talking about.... But I

[011-049] am fond of him. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] Petrovitch, the head of the Investigation

[011-050] Department here... But you know him.”

[011-051] “Is he a relation of yours, too?”

[011-052] “A very distant one. But why are you scowling? Because you quarrelled

[011-053] once, won’t you come then?”

[011-054] “I don’t care a damn for him.”

[011-055] “So much the better. Well, there will be some students, a teacher, a

[011-056] government clerk, a musician, an officer and [[zametov|Zametov]].”

[011-057] “Do tell me, please, what you or he”--Zossimov nodded at

[011-058] Raskolnikov--“can have in common with this Zametov?”

[011-059] “Oh, you particular gentleman! Principles! You are worked by principles,

[011-060] as it were by springs; you won’t venture to turn round on your own

[011-061] account. If a man is a nice fellow, that’s the only principle I go upon.

[011-062] Zametov is a delightful person.”

[011-063] “Though he does take bribes.”

[011-064] “Well, he does! and what of it? I don’t care if he does take bribes,”

[011-065] Razumihin cried with unnatural irritability. “I don’t praise him for

[011-066] taking bribes. I only say he is a nice man in his own way! But if one

[011-067] looks at men in all ways--are there many good ones left? Why, I am sure

[011-068] I shouldn’t be worth a baked onion myself... perhaps with you thrown

[011-069] in.”

[011-070] “That’s too little; I’d give two for you.”

[011-071] “And I wouldn’t give more than one for you. No more of your jokes!

[011-072] Zametov is no more than a boy. I can pull his hair and one must draw him

[011-073] not repel him. You’ll never improve a man by repelling him, especially

[011-074] a boy. One has to be twice as careful with a boy. Oh, you progressive

[011-075] dullards! You don’t understand. You harm yourselves running another man

[011-076] down.... But if you want to know, we really have something in common.”

[011-077] “I should like to know what.”

[011-078] “Why, it’s all about a house-painter.... We are getting him out of

[011-079] a mess! Though indeed there’s nothing to [[fear-and-dread|fear]] now. The matter is

[011-080] absolutely self-evident. We only put on steam.”

[011-081] “A painter?”

[011-082] “Why, haven’t I told you about it? I only told you the beginning then

[011-083] about the murder of [[alyona-ivanovna|the old pawnbroker]]-woman. Well, the painter is mixed

[011-084] up in it...”

[011-085] “Oh, I heard about that murder before and was rather interested in it...

[011-086] partly... for one reason.... I read about it in the papers, too....”

[011-087] “[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] was murdered, too,” [[nastasya|Nastasya]] blurted out, suddenly addressing

[011-088] Raskolnikov. She remained in the room all the time, standing by the door

[011-089] listening.

[011-090] “Lizaveta,” murmured Raskolnikov hardly audibly.

[011-091] “Lizaveta, who sold old clothes. Didn’t you know her? She used to come

[011-092] here. She mended a shirt for you, too.”

[011-093] Raskolnikov turned to the wall where in the dirty, yellow paper he

[011-094] picked out one clumsy, white flower with brown lines on it and began

[011-095] examining how many petals there were in it, how many scallops in the

[011-096] petals and how many lines on them. He felt his arms and legs as lifeless

[011-097] as though they had been cut off. He did not attempt to move, but stared

[011-098] obstinately at the flower.

[011-099] “But what about the painter?” Zossimov interrupted Nastasya’s chatter

[011-100] with marked displeasure. She sighed and was silent.

[011-101] “Why, he was accused of the murder,” Razumihin went on hotly.

[011-102] “Was there evidence against him then?”

[011-103] “Evidence, indeed! Evidence that was no evidence, and that’s what we

[011-104] have to prove. It was just as they pitched on those fellows, [[koch|Koch]] and

[011-105] [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]], at first. Foo! how stupidly it’s all done, it makes one

[011-106] sick, though it’s not one’s business! Pestryakov may be coming

[011-107] to-night.... By the way, Rodya, you’ve heard about the business already;

[011-108] it happened before you were ill, the day before you fainted at the

[011-109] police office while they were talking about it.”

[011-110] Zossimov looked curiously at Raskolnikov. He did not stir.

[011-111] “But I say, Razumihin, I wonder at you. What a busybody you are!”

[011-112] Zossimov observed.

[011-113] “Maybe I am, but we will get him off anyway,” shouted Razumihin,

[011-114] bringing his fist down on the table. “What’s the most offensive is not

[011-115] their lying--one can always forgive lying--lying is a delightful thing,

[011-116] for it leads to truth--what is offensive is that they lie and worship

[011-117] their own lying.... I respect Porfiry, but... What threw them out at

[011-118] first? The door was locked, and when they came back with the porter

[011-119] it was open. So it followed that Koch and Pestryakov were the

[011-120] murderers--that was their logic!”

[011-121] “But don’t excite yourself; they simply detained them, they could not

[011-122] help that.... And, by the way, I’ve met that man Koch. He used to buy

[011-123] unredeemed pledges from the old woman? Eh?”

[011-124] “Yes, he is a swindler. He buys up bad debts, too. He makes a profession

[011-125] of it. But enough of him! Do you know what makes me angry? It’s their

[011-126] sickening rotten, petrified routine.... And this case might be the means

[011-127] of introducing a new method. One can show from the psychological data

[011-128] alone how to get on the track of the real man. ‘We have facts,’ they

[011-129] say. But facts are not everything--at least half the business lies in

[011-130] how you interpret them!”

[011-131] “Can you interpret them, then?”

[011-132] “Anyway, one can’t hold one’s tongue when one has a feeling, a tangible

[011-133] feeling, that one might be a help if only.... Eh! Do you know the

[011-134] details of the case?”

[011-135] “I am waiting to hear about the painter.”

[011-136] “Oh, yes! Well, here’s the story. Early on the third day after the

[011-137] murder, when they were still dandling Koch and Pestryakov--though they

[011-138] accounted for every step they took and it was as plain as a pikestaff--an

[011-139] unexpected fact turned up. A peasant called Dushkin, who keeps a

[011-140] dram-shop facing the house, brought to the police office a jeweller’s

[011-141] case containing some gold ear-rings, and told a long rigamarole. ‘The

[011-142] day before yesterday, just after eight o’clock’--mark the day and the

[011-143] hour!--‘a journeyman house-painter, [[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay]], who had been in to see me

[011-144] already that day, brought me this box of gold ear-rings and stones, and

[011-145] asked me to give him two roubles for them. When I asked him where he got

[011-146] them, he said that he picked them up in the street. I did not ask him

[011-147] anything more.’ I am telling you Dushkin’s story. ‘I gave him a note’--a

[011-148] rouble that is--‘for I thought if he did not pawn it with me he would

[011-149] with another. It would all come to the same thing--he’d spend it on

[011-150] drink, so the thing had better be with me. The further you hide it

[011-151] the quicker you will find it, and if anything turns up, if I hear any

[011-152] rumours, I’ll take it to the police.’ Of course, that’s all taradiddle;

[011-153] he lies like a horse, for I know this Dushkin, he is a pawnbroker and

[011-154] a receiver of stolen goods, and he did not cheat Nikolay out of a

[011-155] thirty-rouble trinket in order to give it to the police. He was simply

[011-156] afraid. But no matter, to return to Dushkin’s story. ‘I’ve known

[011-157] this peasant, Nikolay Dementyev, from a child; he comes from the same

[011-158] province and district of Zaraïsk, we are both Ryazan men. And though

[011-159] Nikolay is not a drunkard, he drinks, and I knew he had a job in that

[011-160] house, painting work with Dmitri, who comes from the same village, too.

[011-161] As soon as he got the rouble he changed it, had a couple of glasses,

[011-162] took his change and went out. But I did not see Dmitri with him then.

[011-163] And the next day I heard that someone had murdered Alyona Ivanovna and

[011-164] her sister, Lizaveta Ivanovna, with an axe. I knew them, and I felt

[011-165] suspicious about the ear-rings at once, for I knew the murdered woman

[011-166] lent money on pledges. I went to the house, and began to make careful

[011-167] inquiries without saying a word to anyone. First of all I asked, “Is

[011-168] Nikolay here?” Dmitri told me that Nikolay had gone off on the spree; he

[011-169] had come home at daybreak drunk, stayed in the house about ten minutes,

[011-170] and went out again. Dmitri didn’t see him again and is finishing the

[011-171] job alone. And their job is on the same staircase as the murder, on

[011-172] the second floor. When I heard all that I did not say a word to

[011-173] anyone’--that’s Dushkin’s tale--‘but I found out what I could about

[011-174] the murder, and went home feeling as suspicious as ever. And at eight

[011-175] o’clock this morning’--that was the third day, you understand--‘I saw

[011-176] Nikolay coming in, not sober, though not to say very drunk--he could

[011-177] understand what was said to him. He sat down on the bench and did not

[011-178] speak. There was only one stranger in the bar and a man I knew asleep

[011-179] on a bench and our two boys. “Have you seen Dmitri?” said I. “No, I

[011-180] haven’t,” said he. “And you’ve not been here either?” “Not since the day

[011-181] before yesterday,” said he. “And where did you sleep last night?”

[011-182] “In Peski, with the Kolomensky men.” “And where did you get those

[011-183] ear-rings?” I asked. “I found them in the street,” and the way he said

[011-184] it was a bit queer; he did not look at me. “Did you hear what happened

[011-185] that very evening, at that very hour, on that same staircase?” said I.

[011-186] “No,” said he, “I had not heard,” and all the while he was listening,

[011-187] his eyes were staring out of his head and he turned as white as chalk. I

[011-188] told him all about it and he took his hat and began getting up. I wanted

[011-189] to keep him. “Wait a bit, Nikolay,” said I, “won’t you have a drink?”

[011-190] And I signed to the boy to hold the door, and I came out from behind the

[011-191] bar; but he darted out and down the street to the turning at a run.

[011-192] I have not seen him since. Then my doubts were at an end--it was his

[011-193] doing, as clear as could be....’”

[011-194] “I should think so,” said Zossimov.

[011-195] “Wait! Hear the end. Of course they sought high and low for Nikolay;

[011-196] they detained Dushkin and searched his house; Dmitri, too, was arrested;

[011-197] the Kolomensky men also were turned inside out. And the day before

[011-199] had gone there, taken the silver cross off his neck and asked for a dram

[011-201] to the cowshed, and through a crack in the wall she saw in the stable

[011-202] adjoining he had made a noose of his sash from the beam, stood on a

[011-203] block of wood, and was trying to put his neck in the noose. The woman

[011-204] screeched her hardest; people ran in. ‘So that’s what you are up to!’

[011-205] ‘Take me,’ he says, ‘to such-and-such a police officer; I’ll confess

[011-206] everything.’ Well, they took him to that police station--that is

[011-207] here--with a suitable escort. So they asked him this and that, how old

[011-208] he is, ‘twenty-two,’ and so on. At the question, ‘When you were working

[011-209] with Dmitri, didn’t you see anyone on the staircase at such-and-such a

[011-210] time?’--answer: ‘To be sure folks may have gone up and down, but I did

[011-211] not notice them.’ ‘And didn’t you hear anything, any noise, and so on?’

[011-212] ‘We heard nothing special.’ ‘And did you hear, Nikolay, that on the same

[011-215] Pavlovitch the day before yesterday.’ ‘And where did you find the

[011-216] ear-rings?’ ‘I found them on the pavement.’ ‘Why didn’t you go to work

[011-217] with Dmitri the other day?’ ‘Because I was drinking.’ ‘And where were

[011-218] you drinking?’ ‘Oh, in such-and-such a place.’ ‘Why did you run away

[011-219] from Dushkin’s?’ ‘Because I was awfully frightened.’ ‘What were

[011-220] you frightened of?’ ‘That I should be accused.’ ‘How could you be

[011-221] frightened, if you felt free from guilt?’ Now, Zossimov, you may not

[011-222] believe me, that question was put literally in those words. I know it

[011-224] “Well, anyway, there’s the evidence.”

[011-225] “I am not talking of the evidence now, I am talking about that question,

[011-226] of their own idea of themselves. Well, so they squeezed and squeezed

[011-227] him and he confessed: ‘I did not find it in the street, but in the flat

[011-228] where I was painting with Dmitri.’ ‘And how was that?’ ‘Why, Dmitri and

[011-229] I were painting there all day, and we were just getting ready to go, and

[011-230] Dmitri took a brush and painted my face, and he ran off and I after him.

[011-232] ran right against the porter and some gentlemen--and how many gentlemen

[011-233] were there I don’t remember. And the porter swore at me, and the other

[011-234] porter swore, too, and the porter’s wife came out, and swore at us, too;

[011-235] and a gentleman came into the entry with a lady, and he swore at us,

[011-236] too, for Dmitri and I lay right across the way. I got hold of Dmitri’s

[011-241] to the flat alone; I had to clear up my things. I began putting them

[011-242] together, expecting Dmitri to come, and there in the passage, in the

[011-243] corner by the door, I stepped on the box. I saw it lying there wrapped

[011-244] up in paper. I took off the paper, saw some little hooks, undid them,

[011-245] and in the box were the ear-rings....’”

[011-246] “Behind the door? Lying behind the door? Behind the door?” Raskolnikov

[011-247] cried suddenly, staring with a blank look of terror at Razumihin, and he

[011-248] slowly sat up on the sofa, leaning on his hand.

[011-249] “Yes... why? What’s the matter? What’s wrong?” Razumihin, too, got up

[011-250] from his seat.

[011-251] “Nothing,” Raskolnikov answered faintly, turning to the wall. All were

[011-252] silent for a while.

[011-253] “He must have waked from a dream,” Razumihin said at last, looking

[011-254] inquiringly at Zossimov. The latter slightly shook his head.

[011-255] “Well, go on,” said Zossimov. “What next?”

[011-257] everything, he took up his cap and ran to Dushkin and, as we know, got

[011-258] a rouble from him. He told a lie saying he found them in the street, and

[011-260] ‘I know nothing of it, never heard of it till the day before yesterday.’

[011-261] ‘And why didn’t you come to the police till now?’ ‘I was frightened.’

[011-262] ‘And why did you try to hang yourself?’ ‘From anxiety.’ ‘What anxiety?’

[011-263] ‘That I should be accused of it.’ Well, that’s the whole story. And now

[011-264] what do you suppose they deduced from that?”

[011-265] “Why, there’s no supposing. There’s a clue, such as it is, a fact. You

[011-266] wouldn’t have your painter set free?”

[011-267] “Now they’ve simply taken him for the murderer. They haven’t a shadow of

[011-268] doubt.”

[011-269] “That’s nonsense. You are excited. But what about the ear-rings? You

[011-273] “How did they get there? How did they get there?” cried Razumihin.

[011-274] “How can you, a doctor, whose duty it is to study man and who has more

[011-279] on the box and picked it up.”

[011-280] “The holy truth! But didn’t he own himself that he told a lie at first?”

[011-316] understand!”

[011-317] “Oh, I see you are excited! Wait a bit. I forgot to ask you; what proof

[011-318] is there that the box came from the old woman?”

[011-319] “That’s been proved,” said Razumihin with apparent reluctance, frowning.

[011-320] “Koch recognised the jewel-case and gave the name of the owner, who

[011-322] “That’s bad. Now another point. Did anyone see Nikolay at the time

[011-334] “How do I explain them? What is there to explain? It’s clear. At any

[011-336] the jewel-case points to it. The real murderer dropped those ear-rings.

[011-338] at the door. Koch, like an ass, did not stay at the door; so the
