# Melancholy and Hypochondria

From the novel's first chapter, Raskolnikov is introduced as being in a state "verging on hypochondria" (001-014) — a condition that Dostoevsky uses to explore the porous boundary between physical illness, psychological disorder, and spiritual crisis.

## Definition

Hypochondria in the 19th-century sense was not merely the modern fear of illness but a broader condition of morbid self-observation, nervous exhaustion, and withdrawal from social life — what earlier ages called "melancholy" or "spleen." Dostoevsky draws on this medical-psychological vocabulary to characterize Raskolnikov's state as one in which the mind turns obsessively inward, feeding on its own fears and theories. The condition is simultaneously physical (Raskolnikov is often feverish and bedridden) and philosophical (his isolation breeds the extraordinary-man theory). As Porfiry observes clinically: "I know one case in which a hypochondriac, a man of forty, cut the throat" (016-091) — linking hypochondria directly to the potential for violence.

## Expression in the Novel

Raskolnikov's hypochondria manifests as a pattern of withdrawal and obsessive rumination. He walks "with folded arms, pensive and melancholy" (015-280), and even his mother notices he has become "suddenly thoughtful and melancholy" (013-264). His "spleen rose within him" (009-166) at moments of frustration, a term directly borrowed from the vocabulary of melancholic illness. The condition is contagious within the novel's world: Pulcheria Alexandrovna agrees "with a dejected air" (016-247), and Marmeladov too sits "with his head sunk on his hands dejectedly" (002-046). What distinguishes Raskolnikov's case is that his melancholy is not passive — it fuels the intellectual arrogance that leads him to murder. As Porfiry notes, the hypochondriac is "frantic... with his morbid exceptional vanity" (016-095), making melancholy not just a symptom but a contributing cause of his crime.

## Key Passages

> "He was in a state verging on hypochondria." (001-014)

> "He walked to and fro with folded arms, pensive and melancholy." (015-280)

> "I know one case in which a hypochondriac, a man of forty, cut the throat of a friend." (016-091)
