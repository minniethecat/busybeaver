# Sacrifice

The novel's moral architecture is built on a series of sacrifices — mostly by women — that stand in stark opposition to Raskolnikov's philosophy of transgression. Where the extraordinary man takes lives for an idea, Sonia and Dounia give their own lives (metaphorically and nearly literally) for others.

## Definition

Sacrifice in the novel operates on two registers: the Christian ideal of self-giving love (Sonia), and the worldly manifestation of that ideal as women sacrificing their bodies, futures, and happiness for family (Dounia, Sonia, Katerina Ivanovna). Raskolnikov himself frames the question: "For such a son who would not sacrifice such a daughter!" (004-134) — the exclamation mark marking both outrage and recognition that such sacrifice is expected.

## Expression in the Novel

Sonia's prostitution is the novel's central sacrifice: she "sacrifices everything, everything" (035-282) for her step-siblings. Dounia contemplates marrying Luzhin — "I won't have your sacrifice, Dounia" (004-153) — in what Raskolnikov correctly identifies as a parallel to Sonia's position. Katerina Ivanovna's death is a kind of sacrifice to pride and circumstance. The novel refuses to sentimentalize these sacrifices: Sonia's is degrading, Dounia's nearly entraps her, and only through the rejection of false sacrifice (Dounia's refusal of Luzhin; Raskolnikov's eventual confession) does sacrifice become redemptive rather than merely destructive. Marmeladov's description of Sonia's first night — "she sacrificed everything" — and the family's response ("they all lay down, and Sonia went too") renders sacrifice as both sacred and unbearable.

## Key Passages

> "For such a son who would not sacrifice such a daughter!" (004-134)

> "I won't have your sacrifice, Dounia." (004-153)

> "Self-sacrifice, it's simply work, honourable work." (027-333)

> "She sacrificed everything, everything, and they all lay down, and Sonia went too." (035-282)

## Character Perspectives

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Sacrifice personified. Her prostitution is a daily martyrdom, yet she retains her faith and moral clarity. She becomes the instrument of Raskolnikov's redemption precisely because she understands sacrifice not as humiliation but as love.

- **Dounia Raskolnikov**: Her trajectory from contemplated sacrifice (marrying Luzhin) to refusal and self-possession (shooting Svidrigailov) shows sacrifice transformed from passive suffering to active moral choice.
