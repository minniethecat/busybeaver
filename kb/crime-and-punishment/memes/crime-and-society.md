*Crime and Punishment* is set in the slums and tenements of 1860s St. Petersburg, and the material conditions of its characters are never incidental to the moral drama. Dostoevsky presents a world in which poverty, exploitation, and urban alienation create the conditions in which crime becomes thinkable — and yet the novel resists the reduction of crime to mere social product. The tension between social determinism and individual moral responsibility is one of the novel's central intellectual preoccupations.

## The Environmental Argument

The novel provides abundant evidence for what would now be called a sociological explanation of crime. Raskolnikov's garret — a "coffin" of a room, low-ceilinged and oppressive (PN 001-003) — is itself a kind of incubator of desperate thoughts. Marmeladov's alcoholism, which destroys his family, is inseparable from the degradation of the low-ranking civil servant, a man who "has nowhere to go" (PN 001-243). Sonia is driven to prostitution not by vice but by the literal starvation of Katerina Ivanovna's children (PN 001-266). The novel catalogues, with unflinching precision, the social machinery that grinds individuals into criminality and despair.

## Dostoevsky's Anti-Determinism

And yet the novel is equally insistent that social conditions do not *determine* human action. Raskolnikov is surrounded by people who are as poor as he is — Razumihin, Sonia, the Marmeladov children — and who do not commit murder. The environmental-determinist position is given its most articulate expression by Lebeziatnikov, who argues that "environment is everything and crime is a protest against the abnormal structure of society" (PN 021-048). But Lebeziatnikov is a figure of satire, and his mechanical progressivism is implicitly contrasted with the novel's insistence on the irreducible reality of individual conscience.

## The Statistical View of Humanity

The novel's critique of social determinism is part of a broader polemic against what Dostoevsky saw as the dehumanizing tendency of utilitarian social science. The "statistical" view — that human behavior can be predicted, managed, and explained by reference to environmental variables — is the intellectual framework that makes Raskolnikov's crime conceivable as a rational act. If the old pawnbroker is "a louse" whose death benefits hundreds, then murder becomes a problem of arithmetic. The novel's entire architecture is a refutation of this premise: crime is not a social equation but a spiritual catastrophe, and the murderer is not a social problem to be solved but a soul to be redeemed.

## Poverty as Moral Landscape

Poverty in the novel is never merely an economic condition; it is a moral and spiritual state. The "crushed by poverty" (PN 001-016) that opens the novel describes Raskolnikov's external circumstances, but the word "crushed" resonates through the entire narrative as a description of the soul under the weight of transgression. The Marmeladov family's destitution is both material fact and moral metaphor — the "nowhere to go" that Marmeladov laments is as much existential as geographical.
