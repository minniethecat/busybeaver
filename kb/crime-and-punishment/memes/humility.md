# Humility

If intellectual pride is Raskolnikov's tragic flaw, humility is the novel's answer to it. Sonia embodies this virtue, but it also appears in unexpected places — in the meek prisoner of Porfiry's parable, in Raskolnikov's final prostration before Sonia, and in the Epilogue's vision of redemption through love.

## Definition

Humility in Dostoevsky's Orthodox framework is not weakness or self-abasement but the recognition of one's dependence on God and connection to others. It is the opposite of Raskolnikov's isolation and self-sufficiency. Marmeladov's drunken prayer — "Behold the man! Permit me to ask, 'Who has pity on all men?'" (002-132) — expresses humility as the acknowledgment of shared human frailty. The novel presents humility not as a passive state but as an active spiritual posture that enables genuine connection and redemption.

## Expression in the Novel

Sonia is the novel's living icon of humility. Her meekness is noted by others: Luzhin's accusation relies on the assumption of her passivity — "You reckoned on her meekness! You relied upon that!" (029-149). But her meekness is not weakness; it conceals immense spiritual strength. She reads the story of Lazarus to Raskolnikov, follows him to Siberia, and waits patiently for his spiritual transformation — all without asserting her own will.

Porfiry tells Raskolnikov of "a very meek and mild prisoner" (033-223), offering this as a model of the humility Raskolnikov lacks. The parable suggests that humility, not intellect or will, is what enables a person to endure suffering and find meaning in it.

Marmeladov's vision of the Last Judgment imagines Christ receiving "the wise and the meek" (002-443) — those who did not consider themselves righteous but who acknowledged their sinfulness. This is precisely the posture Raskolnikov must learn.

## Character Perspectives

- **Sonia Marmeladova**: Humility is her defining virtue. She bears her cross — prostitution, poverty, social contempt — without resentment and becomes the instrument of Raskolnikov's redemption precisely through her refusal to judge or assert herself.

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Must learn humility through suffering. His intellectual pride prevented him from seeing his connection to others; humility restores it. His prostration in the Haymarket and his eventual embrace of Sonia in Siberia are acts of humility.
