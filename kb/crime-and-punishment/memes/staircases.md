## Overview

Staircases are among the most potent spatial motifs in *Crime and Punishment*. Nearly every significant interior space in the novel is accessed by a staircase, and the act of ascending or descending stairs becomes a barometer of psychological state. The staircases of [[st-petersburg]] — narrow, dark, malodorous, and crowded — function simultaneously as physical passageways and as symbols of the boundary between private and public, intention and action, salvation and damnation.

## The Pawnbroker's Staircase

The most narratively significant staircase is the one leading to [[alyona-ivanovna]]'s flat in [[pawnbrokers-flat]]. It is here that the murder plot unfolds in real time, with the staircase serving as a stage for Raskolnikov's narrow escapes:

> He would creep down the stairs like a cat and slip away unseen. [001-023]

The staircase is also where Raskolnikov nearly runs into the porter on his way out after the rehearsal visit, and where the crucial scene with Koch and Pestryakov unfolds on the day of the murder:

> Someone else was approaching from outside. They were on the very stairs now. [007-264]

The sequence of arrivals and departures on this staircase — the workmen painting, Koch knocking, Pestryakov arriving — constitutes a temporal architecture of near-discovery that propels the novel's suspense.

## Raskolnikov's Garret Staircase

The stairs leading to [[raskolnikovs-garret]] are described as steep, dark, and narrow. Raskolnikov's movement up and down these stairs maps his psychological oscillations:

> As he went down the stairs, he even stopped short, two or three times. [001-231]

The staircase is a space where Raskolnikov is vulnerable to interruption — by his landlady, by strangers, by the demands of a world he is trying to flee. His descent from the garret into the street parallels his descent from intellectual isolation into the chaos of lived reality.

## The Marmeladov Staircase

The staircase in the Marmeladov tenement is where Raskolnikov first encounters the family and carries the dying Marmeladov up to his room [002-490]. This ascent — carrying the broken body of the man he has just seen crushed by a carriage — marks Raskolnikov's first compassionate act in the novel and prefigures his eventual redemption through [[sonia-marmeladov]].

## Significance

The staircase is Dostoevsky's spatial correlative for the moral threshold. Every ascent or descent in the novel carries symbolic weight: descending into the street is entering the moral chaos of the world; ascending is approaching a decisive encounter (murder, confrontation, confession). The darkness and narrowness of Petersburg staircases intensify the claustrophobia of the novel's interiors and make every step a physical risk — tripping, being overheard, being seen.

The motif connects to Raskolnikov's obsession with the "square yard of space" that would constitute his eternity: the staircase is the compressed, transitional space between the two kinds of confinement — the garret above and the street below.
