# Porfiry's Interrogation

The interrogation of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] by the examining magistrate [[porfiry-petrovitch|Porfiry Petrovitch]] spans two major encounters — in Part 3, Chapter 5 and Part 4, Chapter 5 — and constitutes the novel's central intellectual duel. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]], who has no material evidence against [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], deploys a "psychological method" of indirect probing, philosophical entrapment, and deliberate ambiguity. The interrogations dramatize the clash between [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary-man theory]] and the inexorable machinery of conscience, law, and human connection. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] does not arrest [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] because he wants him to confess freely — believing that confession, not capture, is the path to moral regeneration.

## Summary

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] visits [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s apartment in the company of [[razumihin|Razumihin]], ostensibly to recover items pawned at the murdered [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]]'s flat. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] — who already suspects [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] — turns the visit into an interrogation disguised as intellectual conversation. He raises [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s published article "On Crime," which divides humanity into "ordinary" and "extraordinary" beings, the latter having an inner right to transgress moral law (019-367). In the second encounter at the police bureau, [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] tightens the psychological net, revealing his knowledge of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s return to the murder scene and his awareness of the tradesman's accusation — until the sudden false confession of [[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay Dementyev]] momentarily disrupts his trap.

## Context & Buildup

By Part 3, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is under [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s suspicion but has not been formally accused. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] has learned of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] through multiple sources: his article "On Crime" published months earlier (019-337), his fainting at the [[police-summons-and-fainting|police station]] when the murder was discussed (011-108), and his suspicious visit to the pawnbroker's apartment to ask about bloodstains. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s method is rooted in his belief that "the criminal is a psychological fact" — that a guilty man will eventually betray himself through intellectual and emotional contradictions rather than physical evidence.

## Detailed Account: First Encounter (Part 3, Chapter 5)

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] and [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] arrive at [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s flat, where [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] greets them with exaggerated gaiety — laughing, shaking hands, filling the room with smoke and chatter (019-022). His manner is deliberately disorienting: "[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] Petrovitch laughed and was ready to go on laughing" (019-029). [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] immediately raises the topic of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s article, published pseudonymously in a periodical. He claims he read it "with interest," particularly its concluding idea that "all men are divided into 'ordinary' and 'extraordinary'" (019-367).

[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] presses [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] to clarify: "Extraordinary men have a right to commit any crime and to transgress the law in any way, just because they are extraordinary" (019-370–371). [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] clarifies his position — that the extraordinary person has "an inner right to decide in his own conscience to overstep... certain obstacles" (019-383) when necessary for the realization of an idea beneficial to humanity. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] repeatedly returns to the question of conscience and its aftermath, probing whether a guilty man might "not be able to endure it" and "begin to give himself away" (019-427).

[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] then springs a trap: he asks whether [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], during his visit to [[pawnbrokers-flat|the old woman's apartment]], asked about bloodstains and whether painters were working on the day of the murder — questions that only the murderer would ask. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] realizes that [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] has been collecting circumstantial evidence while maintaining a facade of intellectual curiosity.

## Detailed Account: Second Encounter (Part 4, Chapter 5)

The second interrogation takes place at [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s office in the police bureau. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] now abandons some of his earlier joviality and speaks more directly. He reveals that he has a "little surprise" — a tradesman who saw [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] at the pawnbroker's apartment and accused him of the murder. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] describes how the tradesman came forward with the accusation, then dramatically reveals that the tradesman has been proven unreliable — the trap built on his testimony has collapsed.

[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] openly tells [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] that he knows he is the murderer, that he has no physical evidence, but that he is waiting for [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] to confess. He offers [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] a deal: confess, and he will arrange a reduced sentence; refuse, and the machinery of the law will be harsher. The interrogation reaches its climax when [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] declares: "I consider it my duty to explain matters to you, and what's more, I consider it my duty to prove my conviction to you... I've come to you with an open and direct proposal — to make a confession" (033-344).

Just as [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s psychological net tightens, [[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay Dementyev]], a young painter arrested on false suspicion, bursts into the room and loudly confesses to the murder — an act of religious self-sacrifice by a man seeking suffering as spiritual purification. The false confession temporarily disrupts [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s trap, buying [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] more time but also deepening his existential crisis: even a stranger's self-sacrifice implicates his own refusal to confess.

## Immediate Aftermath

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] leaves both encounters in states of heightened agitation. After the first interrogation, he debates with [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] about whether [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] has real evidence or is purely toying with him. After the second, the shock of [[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay]]'s false confession — a man who chooses suffering freely — intensifies [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s guilt. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s method proves effective not in extracting a confession on the spot, but in accelerating [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s psychological deterioration toward his eventual confession to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] and, finally, at the [[hay-market|Hay Market]].

## Long-Term Consequences

[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s interrogations establish that the state's suspicion has solidified around [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] — not as a formal accusation, but as a psychological certainty awaiting confession. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s refusal to arrest without confession reflects his philosophy of justice: punishment must be accompanied by moral acknowledgment. This sets up the novel's final act, in which [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s confession is not coerced by evidence but catalyzed by the cumulative pressure of conscience, human connection ([[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]), and social exposure.

[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s "psychological method" also serves as a structural counterpoint to [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s intellectual arrogance. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] defeats [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] not with superior logic but with superior understanding of human psychology — the very domain in which [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s theory, with its disdain for ordinary human weakness, is most inadequate.

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] interrogations are the novel's most sustained dramatization of its core philosophical conflict. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s extraordinary-man theory — that certain great individuals have the right to transgress moral law for a higher purpose — is tested not in the abstract but against the concrete fact of a brutal double murder. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] does not argue against the theory in philosophical terms; he uses it as a mirror, forcing [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] to see the gap between his intellectual justification and the squalid reality of the crime.

The interrogations also advance the novel's exploration of conscience as an involuntary mechanism. [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s method works because guilt is not merely a legal construct but a psychological reality — the criminal's "inner right" (019-383) turns out to be no right at all, but a delusion that crumbles under the pressure of human connection and self-knowledge.

## Characters Involved

- **[[porfiry-petrovitch|Porfiry Petrovitch]]** — The examining magistrate, a master of psychological manipulation who suspects [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] from the beginning. His laughing, indirect manner masks a penetrating intelligence that dissects [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s theory while building a case without physical evidence (019-022).
- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]** — The protagonist, whose extraordinary-man theory becomes the central subject of the interrogation. His intellectual arrogance is systematically dismantled by [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s psychological probing (019-386).
- **[[razumihin|Razumihin]]** — [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s loyal friend, who accompanies him to the first interrogation and later serves as a foil: his honest, straightforward nature contrasts with both [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s deviousness and [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s corruption (018-394).
- **[[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay Dementyev]]** — The young painter whose false confession in Part 4, Chapter 5 disrupts [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]]'s trap. [[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay]]'s act of religious self-sacrifice — confessing to a crime he did not commit to embrace suffering — becomes a moral reproach to [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]].
