# Ordinary vs. Extraordinary

The division of humanity into "ordinary" and "extraordinary" individuals — articulated in [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s article "On Crime," published six months before the novel's action — is the philosophical engine of *Crime and Punishment*. The theory provides Raskolnikov with an intellectual justification for murder, and the novel's dramatic arc is, in large part, the systematic demolition of this justification by the reality of conscience.

## The Article

The reader first learns of Raskolnikov's theory through [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]], the examining magistrate, who has read the article in the *Periodical Review* and recognized its author. Porfiry summarizes its argument during their first interview [019-330]:

> "The point is that men are in general divided by a law of nature into two categories: inferior (ordinary), that is, so to say, material serving only for the propagation of their species; and men who have the gift or the talent to utter a *new word*." [019-330]

The "ordinary" are "conservative by nature, staid, they live in obedience and like it." The "extraordinary" — legislators, prophets, conquerors — "all transgress the law, are destroyers or are disposed to it, in accordance with their capacities." The extraordinary man, Raskolnikov argues, "has the right — not an official right, but an inner right — to decide in his own conscience to overstep... certain obstacles" for the fulfillment of his idea, if that idea benefits all humanity [019-331].

Raskolnikov further specifies that the ordinary exist solely to "multiply the human species" while the extraordinary "move the world and lead it to its goal" [019-331]. The extraordinary man "if necessary, may give himself the inner permission to step over blood — depending, however, on the idea and the scale of it" [019-332]. This "inner permission" is the theory's most dangerous move: it locates moral authority not in law, tradition, or God but in the individual's subjective judgment of his own greatness.

> "In my opinion, if Kepler's and Newton's discoveries could not have been known to the world except through the sacrifice of the lives of one, or ten, or a hundred or more people who hindered it... then Newton would have the right, and would even be obliged... to *remove* those ten or a hundred people." [019-333]

## Intellectual Genealogy

The theory synthesizes several currents of 1860s Russian thought. The utilitarian calculus — kill one "useless" old woman to save thousands — echoes the radical rationalism of Chernyshevsky and the Russian nihilists, who argued that morality was merely a function of maximizing social utility. The figure of the "great man" who transcends moral law draws on the Napoleonic myth that fascinated Dostoevsky's generation — Napoleon as the archetype of the individual who remakes the world by sheer force of will. The Hegelian concept of world-historical individuals who serve the unfolding of Spirit, indifferent to conventional morality, provides a philosophical framework within which such transgressions appear noble rather than criminal.

Dostoevsky, writing from his post-Siberian Christian perspective, saw these ideas as not merely mistaken but demonic — for they licensed the destruction of actual human beings in the name of abstractions. The article's publication six months before the murder suggests that the theory *preceded* and *enabled* the act: Raskolnikov killed the pawnbroker not despite his philosophy but because of it.

## Porfiry's Challenge

Porfiry's interrogation of Raskolnikov focuses not on evidence but on the theory itself, because he understands that the theory *is* the crime. His questions are devastating in their simplicity:

> "What if some man or youth imagines that he is a Lycurgus or Mahomet — a future one of course — and suppose he begins to 'remove all obstacles'...? You see, you permit the shedding of blood *in conscience* — and, excuse me, that's more terrible than an official legal permission" [019-385].

Porfiry identifies the theory's fatal flaw: once moral law is made subjective — once each person decides for themselves whether they are "ordinary" or "extraordinary" — the distinction collapses into pure will. Anyone with sufficient arrogance can declare themselves a Napoleon. The magistrate further probes Raskolnikov's own self-conception, asking pointedly whether he "also, when writing the article, considered yourself — well, even just a little — a man *uttering a new word*" [019-340]. Raskolnikov's angry response — "very possibly" — betrays the personal investment beneath the theoretical apparatus.

Porfiry's method is to draw Raskolnikov into defending his theory, because every defense tightens the noose: the more Raskolnikov argues for the extraordinary man's right to transgress, the more he incriminates himself. His intellectual pride becomes his interrogator's instrument.

## Razumihin's Critique

[[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]], who has also read the article, delivers a less strategic but equally penetrating critique. In conversation with Porfiry and [[zametov|Zametov]], Razumihin identifies what he considers the article's "truly original" but "terrible" implication: "You permit bloodshed *in conscience* — and that, let me tell you, is more terrible than if it were officially, legally permitted" [019-390]. Razumihin's objection is moral rather than legal: the theory does not simply permit murder but elevates it to a *duty* when the transgression serves a sufficiently grand idea. This, Razumihin senses, is a doctrine that transforms moral horror into intellectual obligation.

## Self-Refutation

The novel demonstrates the theory's failure through Raskolnikov's own experience. His inability to "step over" without psychological collapse proves, paradoxically, that he belongs to the "ordinary" he despises. The extraordinary man, by Raskolnikov's own definition, would feel no guilt — would be "a bronze image, or a little tin god" [019-403]. Raskolnikov's torment — the very guilt and remorse that drive him to confession — is the evidence that he is not extraordinary. His body and conscience refuse to obey his philosophy.

After the murder, Raskolnikov's internal monologue repeatedly returns to this self-diagnosis. He did not kill a human being, he tells himself — "I killed a principle, but I didn't manage to step over — I remained on this side" [027-050]. The formulation is precise: he crossed the legal and moral line but could not inhabit the position on the other side. He remained, in his own terms, an ordinary man who committed an extraordinary transgression — and the gap between the two is precisely his suffering.

[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] provides the novel's alternative vision: moral law is not a human convention that the strong may override but a divine reality grounded in the sacredness of every human life. "You turned away from God," she tells Raskolnikov, "and God has smitten you, has given you over to the devil" [026-031]. The novel's resolution affirms that there is no category of person for whom murder is not sin — the theory, however intellectually elegant, collapses before the irreducible reality of the human soul.

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: The theory's author and primary test case. His progressive disillusionment with his own argument — from intellectual pride through psychological collapse to confession — constitutes the novel's central dramatic arc [019-330 to 027-050].

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: The theory's most devastating critic. His interrogation strategy weaponizes Raskolnikov's intellectual vanity, forcing him to defend positions that simultaneously incriminate him [019-330 to 019-403].

- **Dmitri Razumihin**: The moral critic who identifies the theory's dangerous implication — that bloodshed "in conscience" is more terrifying than bloodshed by law, because it elevates murder to a personal obligation [019-390].

- **Sonia Marmeladova**: The spiritual counter-force. Where Raskolnikov's theory divides humanity by capacity, Sonia's Christian vision affirms the equal and infinite value of every soul [026-031].

## Legacy

The ordinary-vs-extraordinary theory has become one of the most discussed aspects of *Crime and Punishment* in literary criticism and philosophy. It prefigures Nietzsche's concept of the *Übermensch* — Nietzsche admired Dostoevsky — and has been read as a prophetic critique of twentieth-century totalitarian ideologies that justified mass murder in the name of historical progress. Raskolnikov's article functions as a warning: the intellectual frameworks that rationalize violence are always more dangerous than the violence itself, because they transform moral horror into philosophical duty.
