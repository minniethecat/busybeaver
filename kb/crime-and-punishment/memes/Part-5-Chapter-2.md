[028-001] It would be difficult to explain exactly what could have originated the

[028-002] idea of that senseless dinner in [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]’s disordered brain.

[028-003] Nearly ten of the twenty roubles, given by [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] for [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]’s

[028-004] funeral, were wasted upon it. Possibly Katerina Ivanovna felt obliged to

[028-005] honour the memory of the deceased “suitably,” that all the lodgers,

[028-006] and still more [[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Ivanovna]], might know “that he was in no way their

[028-007] inferior, and perhaps very much their superior,” and that no one had the

[028-008] right “to turn up his nose at him.” Perhaps the chief element was that

[028-009] peculiar “poor man’s pride,” which compels many poor people to spend

[028-010] their last savings on some traditional social ceremony, simply in order

[028-011] to do “like other people,” and not to “be looked down upon.” It is very

[028-012] probable, too, that Katerina Ivanovna longed on this occasion, at

[028-013] the moment when she seemed to be abandoned by everyone, to show those

[028-014] “wretched contemptible lodgers” that she knew “how to do things, how

[028-015] to entertain” and that she had been brought up “in a genteel, she might

[028-016] almost say aristocratic colonel’s family” and had not been meant for

[028-017] sweeping floors and washing the children’s rags at night. Even the

[028-018] poorest and most broken-spirited people are sometimes liable to these

[028-019] paroxysms of pride and vanity which take the form of an irresistible

[028-020] nervous craving. And Katerina Ivanovna was not broken-spirited; she

[028-021] might have been killed by circumstance, but her spirit could not have

[028-022] been broken, that is, she could not have been intimidated, her will

[028-023] could not be crushed. Moreover [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] had said with good reason that her

[028-024] mind was unhinged. She could not be said to be insane, but for a year

[028-025] past she had been so harassed that her mind might well be overstrained.

[028-026] The later stages of consumption are apt, doctors tell us, to affect the

[028-027] intellect.

[028-028] There was no great variety of wines, nor was there Madeira; but wine

[028-029] there was. There was vodka, rum and Lisbon wine, all of the poorest

[028-030] quality but in sufficient quantity. Besides the traditional rice and

[028-031] honey, there were three or four dishes, one of which consisted of

[028-032] pancakes, all prepared in Amalia Ivanovna’s kitchen. Two samovars were

[028-033] boiling, that tea and punch might be offered after dinner. Katerina

[028-034] Ivanovna had herself seen to purchasing the provisions, with the help

[028-035] of one of the lodgers, an unfortunate little Pole who had somehow been

[028-036] stranded at Madame Lippevechsel’s. He promptly put himself at Katerina

[028-037] Ivanovna’s disposal and had been all that morning and all the day before

[028-038] running about as fast as his legs could carry him, and very anxious

[028-039] that everyone should be aware of it. For every trifle he ran to Katerina

[028-040] Ivanovna, even hunting her out at the bazaar, at every instant called

[028-041] her “_Pani_.” She was heartily sick of him before the end, though

[028-042] she had declared at first that she could not have got on without this

[028-043] “serviceable and magnanimous man.” It was one of Katerina Ivanovna’s

[028-044] characteristics to paint everyone she met in the most glowing colours.

[028-045] Her praises were so exaggerated as sometimes to be embarrassing; she

[028-046] would invent various circumstances to the credit of her new acquaintance

[028-047] and quite genuinely believe in their reality. Then all of a sudden she

[028-048] would be disillusioned and would rudely and contemptuously repulse the

[028-049] person she had only a few hours before been literally adoring. She

[028-050] was naturally of a gay, lively and peace-loving disposition, but from

[028-051] continual failures and misfortunes she had come to desire so _keenly_

[028-052] that all should live in peace and joy and should not _dare_ to break the

[028-053] peace, that the slightest jar, the smallest disaster reduced her almost

[028-054] to frenzy, and she would pass in an instant from the brightest hopes and

[028-055] fancies to cursing her fate and raving, and knocking her head against

[028-056] the wall.

[028-057] Amalia Ivanovna, too, suddenly acquired extraordinary importance in

[028-058] Katerina Ivanovna’s eyes and was treated by her with extraordinary

[028-059] respect, probably only because Amalia Ivanovna had thrown herself heart

[028-060] and soul into the preparations. She had undertaken to lay the table,

[028-061] to provide the linen, crockery, etc., and to cook the dishes in her

[028-062] kitchen, and Katerina Ivanovna had left it all in her hands and gone

[028-063] herself to the cemetery. Everything had been well done. Even the

[028-064] table-cloth was nearly clean; the crockery, knives, forks and glasses

[028-065] were, of course, of all shapes and patterns, lent by different lodgers,

[028-066] but the table was properly laid at the time fixed, and Amalia Ivanovna,

[028-067] feeling she had done her work well, had put on a black silk dress and

[028-068] a cap with new mourning ribbons and met the returning party with some

[028-069] pride. This pride, though justifiable, displeased Katerina Ivanovna for

[028-070] some reason: “as though the table could not have been laid except by

[028-071] Amalia Ivanovna!” She disliked the cap with new ribbons, too. “Could she

[028-072] be stuck up, the stupid German, because she was mistress of the house,

[028-073] and had consented as a favour to help her poor lodgers! As a favour!

[028-074] Fancy that! Katerina Ivanovna’s father who had been a colonel and almost

[028-075] a governor had sometimes had the table set for forty persons, and then

[028-076] anyone like Amalia Ivanovna, or rather Ludwigovna, would not have been

[028-077] allowed into the kitchen.”

[028-078] Katerina Ivanovna, however, put off expressing her feelings for the

[028-079] time and contented herself with treating her coldly, though she decided

[028-080] inwardly that she would certainly have to put Amalia Ivanovna down

[028-081] and set her in her proper place, for goodness only knew what she was

[028-082] fancying herself. Katerina Ivanovna was irritated too by the fact that

[028-083] hardly any of the lodgers invited had come to the funeral, except

[028-084] the Pole who had just managed to run into the cemetery, while to the

[028-085] memorial dinner the poorest and most insignificant of them had turned

[028-086] up, the wretched creatures, many of them not quite sober. The older

[028-087] and more respectable of them all, as if by common consent, stayed away.

[028-088] Pyotr Petrovitch Luzhin, for instance, who might be said to be the most

[028-089] respectable of all the lodgers, did not appear, though Katerina Ivanovna

[028-090] had the evening before told all the world, that is Amalia Ivanovna,

[028-091] Polenka, Sonia and the Pole, that he was the most generous,

[028-092] noble-hearted man with a large property and vast connections, who had

[028-093] been a friend of her first husband’s, and a guest in her father’s

[028-094] house, and that he had promised to use all his influence to secure her

[028-095] a considerable pension. It must be noted that when Katerina Ivanovna

[028-096] exalted anyone’s connections and fortune, it was without any ulterior

[028-097] motive, quite disinterestedly, for the mere pleasure of adding to

[028-098] the consequence of the person praised. Probably “taking his cue” from

[028-099] Luzhin, “that contemptible wretch Lebeziatnikov had not turned up

[028-100] either. What did he fancy himself? He was only asked out of kindness

[028-101] and because he was sharing the same room with Pyotr Petrovitch and was a

[028-102] friend of his, so that it would have been awkward not to invite him.”

[028-103] Among those who failed to appear were “the genteel lady and her

[028-104] old-maidish daughter,” who had only been lodgers in the house for the

[028-105] last fortnight, but had several times complained of the noise and uproar

[028-106] in Katerina Ivanovna’s room, especially when Marmeladov had come

[028-107] back drunk. Katerina Ivanovna heard this from Amalia Ivanovna who,

[028-108] quarrelling with Katerina Ivanovna, and threatening to turn the whole

[028-109] family out of doors, had shouted at her that they “were not worth the

[028-110] foot” of the honourable lodgers whom they were disturbing. Katerina

[028-111] Ivanovna determined now to invite this lady and her daughter, “whose

[028-112] foot she was not worth,” and who had turned away haughtily when she

[028-113] casually met them, so that they might know that “she was more noble in

[028-114] her thoughts and feelings and did not harbour malice,” and might see

[028-115] that she was not accustomed to her way of living. She had proposed to

[028-116] make this clear to them at dinner with allusions to her late father’s

[028-117] governorship, and also at the same time to hint that it was exceedingly

[028-118] stupid of them to turn away on meeting her. The fat colonel-major (he

[028-119] was really a discharged officer of low rank) was also absent, but it

[028-120] appeared that he had been “not himself” for the last two days. The party

[028-121] consisted of the Pole, a wretched looking clerk with a spotty face and

[028-122] a greasy coat, who had not a word to say for himself, and smelt

[028-123] abominably, a deaf and almost blind old man who had once been in the

[028-124] post office and who had been from immemorial ages maintained by someone

[028-125] at Amalia Ivanovna’s.

[028-126] A retired clerk of the commissariat department came, too; he was

[028-127] drunk, had a loud and most unseemly laugh and only fancy--was without

[028-128] a waistcoat! One of the visitors sat straight down to the table without

[028-129] even greeting Katerina Ivanovna. Finally one person having no suit

[028-130] appeared in his dressing-gown, but this was too much, and the efforts of

[028-131] Amalia Ivanovna and the Pole succeeded in removing him. The Pole brought

[028-132] with him, however, two other Poles who did not live at Amalia Ivanovna’s

[028-133] and whom no one had seen here before. All this irritated Katerina

[028-134] Ivanovna intensely. “For whom had they made all these preparations

[028-135] then?” To make room for the visitors the children had not even been laid

[028-136] for at the table; but the two little ones were sitting on a bench in the

[028-137] furthest corner with their dinner laid on a box, while Polenka as a big

[028-138] girl had to look after them, feed them, and keep their noses wiped like

[028-139] well-bred children’s.

[028-140] Katerina Ivanovna, in fact, could hardly help meeting her guests with

[028-141] increased dignity, and even haughtiness. She stared at some of them with

[028-142] special severity, and loftily invited them to take their seats. Rushing

[028-143] to the conclusion that Amalia Ivanovna must be responsible for those who

[028-144] were absent, she began treating her with extreme nonchalance, which the

[028-145] latter promptly observed and resented. Such a beginning was no good omen

[028-146] for the end. All were seated at last.

[028-147] Raskolnikov came in almost at the moment of their return from the

[028-148] cemetery. Katerina Ivanovna was greatly delighted to see him, in the

[028-149] first place, because he was the one “educated visitor, and, as everyone

[028-150] knew, was in two years to take a professorship in the university,” and

[028-151] secondly because he immediately and respectfully apologised for having

[028-152] been unable to be at the funeral. She positively pounced upon him, and

[028-153] made him sit on her left hand (Amalia Ivanovna was on her right). In

[028-154] spite of her continual anxiety that the dishes should be passed round

[028-155] correctly and that everyone should taste them, in spite of the agonising

[028-156] cough which interrupted her every minute and seemed to have grown worse

[028-157] during the last few days, she hastened to pour out in a half whisper to

[028-158] Raskolnikov all her suppressed feelings and her just indignation at

[028-159] the failure of the dinner, interspersing her remarks with lively and

[028-160] uncontrollable laughter at the expense of her visitors and especially of

[028-161] her landlady.

[028-162] “It’s all that cuckoo’s fault! You know whom I mean? Her, her!” Katerina

[028-163] Ivanovna nodded towards [[praskovya-pavlovna|the landlady]]. “Look at her, she’s making round

[028-164] eyes, she feels that we are talking about her and can’t understand.

[028-165] Pfoo, the owl! Ha-ha! (Cough-cough-cough.) And what does she put on that

[028-166] cap for? (Cough-cough-cough.) Have you noticed that she wants everyone

[028-167] to consider that she is patronising me and doing me an honour by being

[028-168] here? I asked her like a sensible woman to invite people, especially

[028-169] those who knew my late husband, and look at the set of fools she has

[028-170] brought! The sweeps! Look at that one with the spotty face. And those

[028-171] wretched Poles, ha-ha-ha! (Cough-cough-cough.) Not one of them has ever

[028-172] poked his nose in here, I’ve never set eyes on them. What have they come

[028-173] here for, I ask you? There they sit in a row. Hey, _pan_!” she cried

[028-174] suddenly to one of them, “have you tasted the pancakes? Take some more!

[028-175] Have some beer! Won’t you have some vodka? Look, he’s jumped up and is

[028-176] making his bows, they must be quite starved, poor things. Never mind,

[028-177] let them eat! They don’t make a noise, anyway, though I’m really afraid

[028-178] for our landlady’s silver spoons... Amalia Ivanovna!” she addressed her

[028-179] suddenly, almost aloud, “if your spoons should happen to be stolen,

[028-180] I won’t be responsible, I warn you! Ha-ha-ha!” She laughed turning to

[028-181] Raskolnikov, and again nodding towards the landlady, in high glee at her

[028-182] sally. “She didn’t understand, she didn’t understand again! Look how

[028-183] she sits with her mouth open! An owl, a real owl! An owl in new ribbons,

[028-184] ha-ha-ha!”

[028-185] Here her laugh turned again to an insufferable fit of coughing that

[028-186] lasted five minutes. Drops of perspiration stood out on her forehead

[028-187] and her handkerchief was stained with blood. She showed Raskolnikov

[028-188] the blood in silence, and as soon as she could get her breath began

[028-189] whispering to him again with extreme animation and a hectic flush on her

[028-190] cheeks.

[028-191] “Do you know, I gave her the most delicate instructions, so to speak,

[028-192] for inviting that lady and her daughter, you understand of whom I am

[028-193] speaking? It needed the utmost delicacy, the greatest nicety, but she

[028-194] has managed things so that that fool, that conceited baggage, that

[028-195] provincial nonentity, simply because she is the widow of a major, and

[028-196] has come to try and get a pension and to fray out her skirts in the

[028-197] government offices, because at fifty she paints her face (everybody

[028-198] knows it)... a creature like that did not think fit to come, and has

[028-199] not even answered the invitation, which the most ordinary good manners

[028-200] required! I can’t understand why Pyotr Petrovitch has not come? But

[028-201] where’s Sonia? Where has she gone? Ah, there she is at last! what is it,

[028-202] Sonia, where have you been? It’s odd that even at your father’s funeral

[028-203] you should be so unpunctual. Rodion Romanovitch, make room for her

[028-204] beside you. That’s your place, Sonia... take what you like. Have some of

[028-205] the cold entrée with jelly, that’s the best. They’ll bring the pancakes

[028-206] directly. Have they given the children some? Polenka, have you got

[028-207] everything? (Cough-cough-cough.) That’s all right. Be a good girl, Lida,

[028-208] and, Kolya, don’t fidget with your feet; sit like a little gentleman.

[028-209] What are you saying, Sonia?”

[028-210] Sonia hastened to give her Pyotr Petrovitch’s apologies, trying to

[028-211] speak loud enough for everyone to hear and carefully choosing the most

[028-212] respectful phrases which she attributed to Pyotr Petrovitch. She added

[028-213] that Pyotr Petrovitch had particularly told her to say that, as soon as

[028-214] he possibly could, he would come immediately to discuss _business_ alone

[028-215] with her and to consider what could be done for her, etc., etc.

[028-216] Sonia knew that this would comfort Katerina Ivanovna, would flatter her

[028-217] and gratify her pride. She sat down beside Raskolnikov; she made him a

[028-218] hurried bow, glancing curiously at him. But for the rest of the time

[028-219] she seemed to avoid looking at him or speaking to him. She seemed

[028-220] absent-minded, though she kept looking at Katerina Ivanovna, trying

[028-221] to please her. Neither she nor Katerina Ivanovna had been able to get

[028-222] mourning; Sonia was wearing dark brown, and Katerina Ivanovna had on her

[028-223] only dress, a dark striped cotton one.

[028-224] The message from Pyotr Petrovitch was very successful. Listening to

[028-225] Sonia with dignity, Katerina Ivanovna inquired with equal dignity how

[028-226] Pyotr Petrovitch was, then at once whispered almost aloud to

[028-227] Raskolnikov that it certainly would have been strange for a man of

[028-228] Pyotr Petrovitch’s position and standing to find himself in such

[028-229] “extraordinary company,” in spite of his devotion to her family and his

[028-230] old friendship with her father.

[028-231] “That’s why I am so grateful to you, Rodion Romanovitch, that you have

[028-232] not disdained my hospitality, even in such surroundings,” she added

[028-233] almost aloud. “But I am sure that it was only your special affection for

[028-234] my poor husband that has made you keep your promise.”

[028-235] Then once more with pride and dignity she scanned her visitors, and

[028-236] suddenly inquired aloud across the table of the deaf man: “Wouldn’t he

[028-237] have some more meat, and had he been given some wine?” The old man made

[028-238] no answer and for a long while could not understand what he was asked,

[028-239] though his neighbours amused themselves by poking and shaking him. He

[028-240] simply gazed about him with his mouth open, which only increased the

[028-241] general mirth.

[028-242] “What an imbecile! Look, look! Why was he brought? But as to Pyotr

[028-243] Petrovitch, I always had confidence in him,” Katerina Ivanovna

[028-244] continued, “and, of course, he is not like...” with an extremely stern

[028-245] face she addressed Amalia Ivanovna so sharply and loudly that the latter

[028-246] was quite disconcerted, “not like your dressed up draggletails whom

[028-247] my father would not have taken as cooks into his kitchen, and my late

[028-248] husband would have done them honour if he had invited them in the

[028-249] goodness of his heart.”

[028-250] “Yes, he was fond of drink, he was fond of it, he did drink!” cried the

[028-251] commissariat clerk, gulping down his twelfth glass of vodka.

[028-252] “My late husband certainly had that weakness, and everyone knows

[028-253] it,” Katerina Ivanovna attacked him at once, “but he was a kind and

[028-254] honourable man, who loved and respected his family. The worst of it was

[028-255] his good nature made him trust all sorts of disreputable people, and he

[028-256] drank with fellows who were not worth the sole of his shoe. Would you

[028-257] believe it, Rodion Romanovitch, they found a gingerbread cock in his

[028-258] pocket; he was dead drunk, but he did not forget the children!”

[028-259] “A cock? Did you say a cock?” shouted the commissariat clerk.

[028-260] Katerina Ivanovna did not vouchsafe a reply. She sighed, lost in

[028-261] thought.

[028-262] “No doubt you think, like everyone, that I was too severe with him,” she

[028-263] went on, addressing Raskolnikov. “But that’s not so! He respected me, he

[028-264] respected me very much! He was a kind-hearted man! And how sorry I was

[028-265] for him sometimes! He would sit in a corner and look at me, I used to

[028-266] feel so sorry for him, I used to want to be kind to him and then would

[028-267] think to myself: ‘Be kind to him and he will drink again,’ it was only

[028-268] by severity that you could keep him within bounds.”

[028-269] “Yes, he used to get his hair pulled pretty often,” roared the

[028-270] commissariat clerk again, swallowing another glass of vodka.

[028-271] “Some fools would be the better for a good drubbing, as well as having

[028-272] their hair pulled. I am not talking of my late husband now!” Katerina

[028-273] Ivanovna snapped at him.

[028-274] The flush on her cheeks grew more and more marked, her chest heaved. In

[028-275] another minute she would have been ready to make a scene. Many of the

[028-276] visitors were sniggering, evidently delighted. They began poking the

[028-277] commissariat clerk and whispering something to him. They were evidently

[028-278] trying to egg him on.

[028-279] “Allow me to ask what are you alluding to,” began the clerk, “that is

[028-280] to say, whose... about whom... did you say just now... But I don’t care!

[028-281] That’s nonsense! Widow! I forgive you.... Pass!”

[028-282] And he took another drink of vodka.

[028-283] Raskolnikov sat in silence, listening with disgust. He only ate from

[028-284] politeness, just tasting the food that Katerina Ivanovna was continually

[028-285] putting on his plate, to avoid hurting her feelings. He watched Sonia

[028-286] intently. But Sonia became more and more anxious and distressed; she,

[028-287] too, foresaw that the dinner would not end peaceably, and saw with

[028-288] terror Katerina Ivanovna’s growing irritation. She knew that she, Sonia,

[028-289] was the chief reason for the ‘genteel’ ladies’ contemptuous treatment of

[028-290] Katerina Ivanovna’s invitation. She had heard from Amalia Ivanovna that

[028-291] the mother was positively offended at the invitation and had asked the

[028-292] question: “How could she let her daughter sit down beside _that young

[028-293] person_?” Sonia had a feeling that Katerina Ivanovna had already heard

[028-294] this and an insult to Sonia meant more to Katerina Ivanovna than an

[028-295] insult to herself, her children, or her father. Sonia knew that

[028-296] Katerina Ivanovna would not be satisfied now, “till she had shown those

[028-297] draggletails that they were both...” To make matters worse someone

[028-298] passed Sonia, from the other end of the table, a plate with two hearts

[028-299] pierced with an arrow, cut out of black bread. Katerina Ivanovna flushed

[028-300] crimson and at once said aloud across the table that the man who sent it

[028-301] was “a drunken ass!”

[028-302] Amalia Ivanovna was foreseeing something amiss, and at the same time

[028-303] deeply wounded by Katerina Ivanovna’s haughtiness, and to restore the

[028-304] good-humour of the company and raise herself in their esteem she began,

[028-305] apropos of nothing, telling a story about an acquaintance of hers “Karl

[028-306] from the chemist’s,” who was driving one night in a cab, and that “the

[028-307] cabman wanted him to kill, and Karl very much begged him not to kill,

[028-308] and wept and clasped hands, and frightened and from [[fear-and-dread|fear]] pierced his

[028-309] heart.” Though Katerina Ivanovna smiled, she observed at once that

[028-310] Amalia Ivanovna ought not to tell anecdotes in Russian; the latter was

[028-311] still more offended, and she retorted that her “_Vater aus Berlin_ was a

[028-312] very important man, and always went with his hands in pockets.” Katerina

[028-313] Ivanovna could not restrain herself and laughed so much that Amalia

[028-314] Ivanovna lost patience and could scarcely control herself.

[028-315] “Listen to the owl!” Katerina Ivanovna whispered at once, her

[028-316] good-humour almost restored, “she meant to say he kept his hands in

[028-317] his pockets, but she said he put his hands in people’s pockets.

[028-318] (Cough-cough.) And have you noticed, Rodion Romanovitch, that all these

[028-319] [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] foreigners, the Germans especially, are all stupider than

[028-320] we! Can you fancy anyone of us telling how ‘Karl from the chemist’s’

[028-321] ‘pierced his heart from fear’ and that the idiot, instead of punishing

[028-322] the cabman, ‘clasped his hands and wept, and much begged.’ Ah, the fool!

[028-323] And you know she fancies it’s very touching and does not suspect how

[028-324] stupid she is! To my thinking that drunken commissariat clerk is a great

[028-325] deal cleverer, anyway one can see that he has addled his brains with

[028-326] drink, but you know, these foreigners are always so well behaved

[028-327] and serious.... Look how she sits glaring! She is angry, ha-ha!

[028-328] (Cough-cough-cough.)”

[028-329] Regaining her good-humour, Katerina Ivanovna began at once telling

[028-330] Raskolnikov that when she had obtained her pension, she intended to open

[028-331] a school for the daughters of gentlemen in her native town T----.

[028-332] This was the first time she had spoken to him of the project, and she

[028-333] launched out into the most alluring details. It suddenly appeared that

[028-334] Katerina Ivanovna had in her hands the very certificate of honour of

[028-335] which Marmeladov had spoken to Raskolnikov in the tavern, when he told

[028-336] him that Katerina Ivanovna, his wife, had danced the shawl dance

[028-337] before the governor and other great personages on leaving school. This

[028-338] certificate of honour was obviously intended now to prove Katerina

[028-339] Ivanovna’s right to open a boarding-school; but she had armed herself

[028-340] with it chiefly with the object of overwhelming “those two stuck-up

[028-341] draggletails” if they came to the dinner, and proving incontestably

[028-342] that Katerina Ivanovna was of the most noble, “she might even say

[028-343] aristocratic family, a colonel’s daughter and was far superior to

[028-344] certain adventuresses who have been so much to the fore of late.” The

[028-345] certificate of honour immediately passed into the hands of the drunken

[028-346] guests, and Katerina Ivanovna did not try to retain it, for it actually

[028-347] contained the statement _en toutes lettres_, that her father was of the

[028-348] rank of a major, and also a companion of an order, so that she really

[028-349] was almost the daughter of a colonel.

[028-350] Warming up, Katerina Ivanovna proceeded to enlarge on the peaceful and
