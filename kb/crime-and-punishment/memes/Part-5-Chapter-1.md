[027-001] The morning that followed the fateful interview with [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] and

[027-002] her mother brought sobering influences to bear on Pyotr Petrovitch.

[027-003] Intensely unpleasant as it was, he was forced little by little to accept

[027-004] as a fact beyond recall what had seemed to him only the day before

[027-005] fantastic and incredible. The black snake of wounded vanity had been

[027-006] gnawing at his heart all night. When he got out of bed, Pyotr Petrovitch

[027-007] immediately looked in the looking-glass. He was afraid that he had

[027-008] jaundice. However his health seemed unimpaired so far, and looking at

[027-009] his noble, clear-skinned countenance which had grown fattish of

[027-010] late, Pyotr Petrovitch for an instant was positively comforted in the

[027-011] conviction that he would find another bride and, perhaps, even a better

[027-012] one. But coming back to the sense of his present position, he turned

[027-013] aside and spat vigorously, which excited a sarcastic smile in Andrey

[027-014] Semyonovitch Lebeziatnikov, the young friend with whom he was staying.

[027-015] That smile Pyotr Petrovitch noticed, and at once set it down against his

[027-016] young friend’s account. He had set down a good many points against him

[027-017] of late. His anger was redoubled when he reflected that he ought not to

[027-018] have told Andrey Semyonovitch about the result of yesterday’s interview.

[027-019] That was the second mistake he had made in temper, through impulsiveness

[027-020] and irritability.... Moreover, all that morning one unpleasantness

[027-021] followed another. He even found a hitch awaiting him in his legal case

[027-022] in the senate. He was particularly irritated by the owner of the flat

[027-023] which had been taken in view of his approaching marriage and was being

[027-024] redecorated at his own expense; the owner, a rich German tradesman,

[027-025] would not entertain the idea of breaking the contract which had just

[027-026] been signed and insisted on the full forfeit money, though Pyotr

[027-027] Petrovitch would be giving him back the flat practically redecorated. In

[027-028] the same way the upholsterers refused to return a single rouble of the

[027-029] instalment paid for the furniture purchased but not yet removed to the

[027-030] flat.

[027-031] “Am I to get married simply for the sake of the furniture?” Pyotr

[027-032] Petrovitch ground his teeth and at the same time once more he had a

[027-033] gleam of desperate hope. “Can all that be really so irrevocably over?

[027-034] Is it no use to make another effort?” The thought of Dounia sent a

[027-035] voluptuous pang through his heart. He endured anguish at that moment,

[027-036] and if it had been possible to slay [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] instantly by wishing it,

[027-037] Pyotr Petrovitch would promptly have uttered the wish.

[027-038] “It was my mistake, too, not to have given them money,” he thought, as

[027-039] he returned dejectedly to Lebeziatnikov’s room, “and why on earth was I

[027-040] such a Jew? It was false economy! I meant to keep them without a penny

[027-041] so that they should turn to me as their [[fate-and-coincidence|providence]], and look at them!

[027-042] foo! If I’d spent some fifteen hundred roubles on them for the trousseau

[027-043] and presents, on knick-knacks, dressing-cases, jewellery, materials, and

[027-044] all that sort of trash from Knopp’s and the English shop, my position

[027-045] would have been better and... stronger! They could not have refused me

[027-046] so easily! They are the sort of people that would feel bound to return

[027-047] money and presents if they broke it off; and they would find it hard to

[027-048] do it! And their conscience would prick them: how can we dismiss a man

[027-049] who has hitherto been so generous and delicate?.... H’m! I’ve made a

[027-050] blunder.”

[027-051] And grinding his teeth again, Pyotr Petrovitch called himself a

[027-052] fool--but not aloud, of course.

[027-053] He returned home, twice as irritated and angry as before. The

[027-054] preparations for the funeral dinner at [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]’s excited

[027-055] his curiosity as he passed. He had heard about it the day before; he

[027-056] fancied, indeed, that he had been invited, but absorbed in his own cares

[027-057] he had paid no attention. Inquiring of Madame Lippevechsel who was busy

[027-058] laying the table while Katerina Ivanovna was away at the cemetery, he

[027-059] heard that the entertainment was to be a great affair, that all the

[027-060] lodgers had been invited, among them some who had not known the dead

[027-061] man, that even Andrey Semyonovitch Lebeziatnikov was invited in spite of

[027-062] his previous quarrel with Katerina Ivanovna, that he, Pyotr Petrovitch,

[027-063] was not only invited, but was eagerly expected as he was the most

[027-064] important of the lodgers. [[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Ivanovna]] herself had been invited with

[027-065] great ceremony in spite of the recent unpleasantness, and so she was

[027-066] very busy with preparations and was taking a positive pleasure in them;

[027-067] she was moreover dressed up to the nines, all in new black silk, and she

[027-068] was proud of it. All this suggested an idea to Pyotr Petrovitch and he

[027-069] went into his room, or rather Lebeziatnikov’s, somewhat thoughtful. He

[027-070] had learnt that Raskolnikov was to be one of the guests.

[027-071] Andrey Semyonovitch had been at home all the morning. The attitude of

[027-072] Pyotr Petrovitch to this gentleman was strange, though perhaps natural.

[027-073] Pyotr Petrovitch had despised and hated him from the day he came to stay

[027-074] with him and at the same time he seemed somewhat afraid of him. He

[027-075] had not come to stay with him on his arrival in [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] simply from

[027-076] parsimony, though that had been perhaps his chief object. He had heard

[027-077] of Andrey Semyonovitch, who had once been his ward, as a leading young

[027-078] progressive who was taking an important part in certain interesting

[027-079] circles, the doings of which were a legend in the provinces. It had

[027-080] impressed Pyotr Petrovitch. These powerful omniscient circles who

[027-081] despised everyone and showed everyone up had long inspired in him a

[027-082] peculiar but quite vague alarm. He had not, of course, been able to form

[027-083] even an approximate notion of what they meant. He, like everyone, had

[027-084] heard that there were, especially in Petersburg, progressives of some

[027-085] sort, nihilists and so on, and, like many people, he exaggerated and

[027-086] distorted the significance of those words to an absurd degree. What for

[027-087] many years past he had feared more than anything was _being shown

[027-088] up_ and this was the chief ground for his continual uneasiness at the

[027-089] thought of transferring his business to Petersburg. He was afraid of

[027-090] this as little children are sometimes panic-stricken. Some years before,

[027-091] when he was just entering on his own career, he had come upon two cases

[027-092] in which rather important personages in the province, patrons of his,

[027-093] had been cruelly shown up. One instance had ended in great scandal

[027-094] for the person attacked and the other had very nearly ended in serious

[027-095] trouble. For this reason Pyotr Petrovitch intended to go into the

[027-096] subject as soon as he reached Petersburg and, if necessary, to

[027-097] anticipate contingencies by seeking the favour of “our younger

[027-098] generation.” He relied on Andrey Semyonovitch for this and before

[027-099] his visit to Raskolnikov he had succeeded in picking up some current

[027-100] phrases. He soon discovered that Andrey Semyonovitch was a commonplace

[027-101] simpleton, but that by no means reassured Pyotr Petrovitch. Even if he

[027-102] had been certain that all the progressives were fools like him, it

[027-103] would not have allayed his uneasiness. All the doctrines, the ideas, the

[027-104] systems, with which Andrey Semyonovitch pestered him had no interest for

[027-105] him. He had his own object--he simply wanted to find out at once what

[027-106] was happening _here_. Had these people any power or not? Had he anything

[027-107] to [[fear-and-dread|fear]] from them? Would they expose any enterprise of his? And what

[027-108] precisely was now the object of their attacks? Could he somehow make up

[027-109] to them and get round them if they really were powerful? Was this the

[027-110] thing to do or not? Couldn’t he gain something through them? In fact

[027-111] hundreds of questions presented themselves.

[027-112] Andrey Semyonovitch was an anæmic, scrofulous little man, with strangely

[027-113] flaxen mutton-chop whiskers of which he was very proud. He was a clerk

[027-114] and had almost always something wrong with his eyes. He was rather

[027-115] soft-hearted, but self-confident and sometimes extremely conceited in

[027-116] speech, which had an absurd effect, incongruous with his little figure.

[027-117] He was one of the lodgers most respected by Amalia Ivanovna, for he did

[027-118] not get drunk and paid regularly for his lodgings. Andrey Semyonovitch

[027-119] really was rather stupid; he attached himself to the cause of progress

[027-120] and “our younger generation” from enthusiasm. He was one of the numerous

[027-121] and varied legion of dullards, of half-animate abortions, conceited,

[027-122] half-educated coxcombs, who attach themselves to the idea most in

[027-123] fashion only to vulgarise it and who caricature every cause they serve,

[027-124] however sincerely.

[027-125] Though Lebeziatnikov was so good-natured, he, too, was beginning to

[027-126] dislike Pyotr Petrovitch. This happened on both sides unconsciously.

[027-127] However simple Andrey Semyonovitch might be, he began to see that Pyotr

[027-128] Petrovitch was duping him and secretly despising him, and that “he was

[027-129] not the right sort of man.” He had tried expounding to him the system of

[027-130] Fourier and the Darwinian theory, but of late Pyotr Petrovitch began to

[027-131] listen too sarcastically and even to be rude. The fact was he had begun

[027-132] instinctively to guess that Lebeziatnikov was not merely a commonplace

[027-133] simpleton, but, perhaps, a liar, too, and that he had no connections of

[027-134] any consequence even in his own circle, but had simply picked things up

[027-135] third-hand; and that very likely he did not even know much about his own

[027-136] work of propaganda, for he was in too great a muddle. A fine person he

[027-137] would be to show anyone up! It must be noted, by the way, that Pyotr

[027-138] Petrovitch had during those ten days eagerly accepted the strangest

[027-139] praise from Andrey Semyonovitch; he had not protested, for instance,

[027-140] when Andrey Semyonovitch belauded him for being ready to contribute to

[027-141] the establishment of the new “commune,” or to abstain from christening

[027-142] his future children, or to acquiesce if Dounia were to take a lover a

[027-143] month after marriage, and so on. Pyotr Petrovitch so enjoyed hearing

[027-144] his own praises that he did not disdain even such virtues when they were

[027-145] attributed to him.

[027-146] Pyotr Petrovitch had had occasion that morning to realise some

[027-147] five-per-cent bonds and now he sat down to the table and counted over

[027-148] bundles of notes. Andrey Semyonovitch who hardly ever had any money

[027-151] Pyotr Petrovitch that Andrey Semyonovitch could really look on the money

[027-153] Petrovitch was capable of entertaining such an idea about him and

[027-154] was, perhaps, glad of the opportunity of teasing his young friend by

[027-155] reminding him of his inferiority and the great difference between them.

[027-156] He found him incredibly inattentive and irritable, though he, Andrey

[027-157] Semyonovitch, began enlarging on his favourite subject, the foundation

[027-159] Petrovitch between the clicking of the beads on the reckoning frame

[027-162] with Dounia and he was burning with impatience to discourse on that

[027-163] theme. He had something progressive to say on the subject which

[027-164] might console his worthy friend and “could not fail” to promote his

[027-165] development.

[027-167] widow’s, isn’t there?” Pyotr Petrovitch asked suddenly, interrupting

[027-168] Andrey Semyonovitch at the most interesting passage.

[027-171] talking to her yesterday...”

[027-172] “I should never have expected that beggarly fool would have spent on

[027-176] Pyotr Petrovitch, who seemed to have some object in pursuing the

[027-178] remember. But I shan’t go. Why should I? I only said a word to her in

[027-180] a destitute widow of a government clerk. I suppose she has invited me on

[027-181] that account, hasn’t she? He-he-he!”

[027-182] “I don’t intend to go either,” said Lebeziatnikov.

[027-183] “I should think not, after giving her a thrashing! You might well

[027-184] hesitate, he-he!”

[027-185] “Who thrashed? Whom?” cried Lebeziatnikov, flustered and blushing.
