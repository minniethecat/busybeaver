## Overview

Vassilyevsky Ostrov (Васильевский остров) is one of the central island districts of [[st-petersburg]], situated between the Bolshaya Neva and Malaya Neva rivers. In *Crime and Punishment*, it is best known as the neighbourhood where [[dmitri-razumihin]] lives, and it appears repeatedly as a destination in Raskolnikov's meandering walks across the city.

## Geography

The island is the largest in the Neva delta and was, in Dostoevsky's time, a district of mixed character: government buildings at the eastern end near the Strelka, and poorer residential tenements further west. To reach Vassilyevsky Ostrov from the Haymarket area, one walks north across one of the bridges spanning the [[neva-river]].

The district is defined by its grid of straight prospects (called "lines" in St. Petersburg) intersecting at right angles — a layout that distinguishes it from the organic, winding streets of the central districts. Vassilyevsky Prospect runs the length of the island:

> walking along Vassilyevsky Prospect, as though he had some business there. [003-441]

## Razumihin's Home

Razumihin lives on Vassilyevsky Ostrov, and it becomes Raskolnikov's refuge at several points in the novel. After his feverish wandering following the murder, Raskolnikov instinctively heads towards the island:

> He walked right across Vassilyevsky Ostrov, came out on to the Lesser Neva, crossed the bridge and turned towards the Islands. [005-035]

Later, when Raskolnikov needs help, Razumihin's address on the island is a recurring practical reference point:

> I must go to Vassilyevsky Ostrov, to Razumihin. That's what I'll do, let them see. [004-377]

The distance between Raskolnikov's garret near the Haymarket and Razumihin's rooms on Vassilyevsky Ostrov becomes a measure of Raskolnikov's isolation and his sporadic impulses to reconnect.

## Raskolnikov's Wanderings

Vassilyevsky Ostrov appears in the novel primarily as a destination in Raskolnikov's walking routes. He crosses it in states of fever, indecision, and aimlessness — the island's grid of long, straight streets mirroring the relentless linearity of his obsessive thoughts:

> Why, he lives here, in that house over there, by the bridge to Vassilyevsky Ostrov. [009-136]

This line, from Raskolnikov's internal monologue, captures the way Vassilyevsky Ostrov functions as a mental landmark in the city's geography of despair and possibility.

## Significance

Unlike the [[hay-market]] or the [[crystal-palace-restaurant]], Vassilyevsky Ostrov is not a site of dramatic action but a space of transit and refuge. It represents the alternative life that Raskolnikov might choose — represented by Razumihin's practical energy, his work, his capacity for friendship. That Raskolnikov repeatedly turns towards the island but cannot stay there encodes his inability to accept the help that Razumihin consistently offers. The bridge crossings that connect the island to the central districts become symbols of the crossing Raskolnikov must make from isolation to community.
