# Cross Symbolism

The cross appears at critical moments in *Crime and Punishment* as the supreme symbol of Christian suffering and redemption. Sonia gives Raskolnikov a cypress cross, and Lizaveta — the innocent woman Raskolnikov killed — had given Sonia her own cross. The exchange of crosses between Sonia and Raskolnikov represents the novel's central spiritual transaction: Raskolnikov must take up the cross of suffering in order to find redemption.

## Definition

In Russian Orthodox tradition, the cross is not merely a symbol of Christ's sacrifice but a personal object of devotion, worn on the body and exchanged as a sign of spiritual kinship. The cypress wood cross that Sonia gives Raskolnikov carries a double meaning: cypress is associated with death and mourning, but also with incorruptibility. The cross is both the weight of suffering and the promise of resurrection.

## Expression in the Novel

Marmeladov's deathbed cry — "Crucify me, O Judge, crucify me!" (002-427) — introduces the cross as a plea for punishment-as-redemption. His reference to his daughter who "gave herself for her cross" (002-435) links Sonia's sacrifice (prostitution for her family) to Christ's Passion. When Sonia finally gives Raskolnikov her cypress cross, she tells him: "Here, take this one, of cypress wood. I have another, of copper, that belonged to Lizaveta" (030-563). The doubled crosses — one from Sonia, one from the murdered Lizaveta — bind Raskolnikov to both the woman who loves him and the innocent woman he killed.

In the Epilogue, Raskolnikov keeps "two crosses, one of cypress wood and one of copper" (039-055) under his pillow, though he has not yet fully accepted their meaning. The cross remains a physical object awaiting spiritual activation — much like Raskolnikov himself awaiting genuine repentance.

## Character Perspectives

- **Sonia Marmeladova**: The bearer of the cross in both literal and spiritual senses. She gives Raskolnikov her cypress cross as an act of faith that he will one day accept its meaning.

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Receives the cross but cannot yet "take it up." His acceptance of the cross in the Epilogue coincides with his spiritual rebirth.

- **Lizaveta Ivanovna**: The innocent victim whose copper cross passes to Raskolnikov through Sonia — a silent reminder of the murder that demands atonement.
