# Moral Paralysis

The psycho-moral condition in which the murderer finds himself unable to act, choose, or connect after his crime — a state the novel presents as the automatic consequence of violating the moral law, independent of external detection.

## Definition

Moral paralysis is the progressive dissolution of volition that follows Raskolnikov's murders. Before the crime, he is capable of detailed planning and decisive action. Afterward, he can barely manage to answer questions, cross a room, or decide what to do next. He wanders the streets in a fever, lies in his garret for days, and when Porfiry interrogates him, he cannot sustain even a simple deception — not because he feels guilt consciously, but because the act of murder has damaged the psychological mechanism of will itself (PN 001-050, PN 002-457). The novel's deep insight is that the punishment for crime is built into the act.

## Expression in the Novel

The paralysis manifests in multiple registers. Physically, Raskolnikov collapses repeatedly, succumbs to fever and delirium (PN 002-457). Socially, he cannot endure the presence of his mother or sister, flinching from touch (PN 003-092). Intellectually, he begins to forget what he has said and done, contradicting himself — a man who prided himself on superior reason now cannot keep his story straight. Most tellingly, when the tradesman accuses him publicly, Raskolnikov cannot even muster a denial: "he could not help asking himself" a question that unmakes his entire theory (PN 001-111).

## Key Passages

> "He felt that he had, as it were, cut himself off from everyone and everything at that moment" — the moment of paralysis as separation (PN 002-548)

> "He walked along not observing what was about him and not caring to observe it" — the collapse of attention and agency after the murder (PN 001-050)

> "He was sitting apart, now and then sipping from his pot and looking about with an air of indifference" — the post-crime drift state that defines much of Parts 2–6 (PN 001-285)

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: The primary case. His paralysis is not cowardice — he faces danger repeatedly. It is a deeper inability to will in the absence of moral framework. His theory said murder was permissible; his body and psyche say otherwise.
- **Porfiry Petrovich**: Understands moral paralysis as an investigative tool. His interrogation strategy is designed to exacerbate it: he waits, circles, and lets Raskolnikov's own paralysis do the work of confession.
- **Sonia Marmeladov**: The antidote figure. Where Raskolnikov is paralyzed, Sonia acts — she follows him to Siberia, she maintains faith, she performs the small, daily acts of love that restore his capacity for will.

## Historical & Philosophical Context

Dostoevsky's depiction of moral paralysis draws on the Russian Orthodox understanding of sin as self-wounding: the sinner damages himself, not merely an external law. It also anticipates 20th-century existential psychology — Raskolnikov's condition resembles what later thinkers would call "bad faith" or "inauthenticity." The novel's counterintuitive argument — that the murderer is the one who loses freedom — is a direct rebuke to the Nietzschean/Chernyshevskian claim that freedom lies in casting off moral constraints.
