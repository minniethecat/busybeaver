# Children and Suffering

The suffering of children runs through *Crime and Punishment* as an unanswerable moral challenge. Dostoevsky, who would later develop the theme into Ivan Karamazov's famous "rebellion" in *The Brothers Karamazov*, seeds the novel with images of children in distress — hungry, beaten, exploited, abandoned. These images resist every attempt to rationalize suffering as the price of a greater good. A child's hunger, a child's tears, a child's terror: these are not compensable by any future utopia.

## The Marmeladov Children

The most sustained depiction of childhood suffering is the Marmeladov household. Katerina Ivanovna's consumption is exacerbated by "the crying of the hungry children" (PN 002-261). The children — Polenka, Lyonya, and Kolya — appear throughout the novel as silent, watching presences, bearers of suffering they did not choose and cannot escape. When Marmeladov is dying, the children's terror at their father's bloody body is rendered with brutal particularity. Their suffering has no redemptive purpose; it simply is, and its very pointlessness is Dostoevsky's argument against any ethics that would trade present misery for future happiness.

## The Beaten Horse Dream

Raskolnikov's childhood dream of the beaten horse (Part 1, Chapter 5) is the novel's most concentrated image of innocent suffering. A drunken crowd, "shouting, laughter and abuse" (PN 005-079), beats an overburdened mare to death while the child Raskolnikov watches in horror, then runs to kiss the dead animal's muzzle. The dream connects childhood perception (unmediated, moral, horrified) with adult reality (the murder Raskolnikov is about to commit). The mare's suffering is gratuitous, public, and met with laughter — the same laughter that greets Marmeladov in the tavern. The dream's placement immediately before the murder frames the old pawnbroker's killing not as the act of a superior man but as participation in the world's cruelty toward the weak.

## Sonia and the Children

Sonia's sacrifice — prostitution to feed the Marmeladov children — places her in the double position of victim and protector. She is herself barely more than a child, and her degradation is explicitly linked to the children's survival: she feeds them with her body. When Raskolnikov forces her to confront the logic of her situation ("Polenka will go the same way"), he uses the children as a lever of despair — a use Dostoevsky implicitly condemns.

## Key Passages

> "The crying of the hungry children" — the Marmeladov household's defining sound (PN 002-261)

> "Shouting, laughter and abuse" — the crowd at the beaten horse, a microcosm of cruelty toward the defenseless (PN 005-079)

> "Three children, one smaller than the other" — Katerina Ivanovna's burden, enumerated by Marmeladov (PN 002-189)
