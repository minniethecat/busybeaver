[039-001] When he went into [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]’s room, it was already getting dark. All day

[039-002] Sonia had been waiting for him in terrible anxiety. [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] had been

[039-003] waiting with her. She had come to her that morning, remembering

[039-004] Svidrigaïlov’s words that Sonia knew. We will not describe the

[039-005] conversation and tears of the two girls, and how friendly they became.

[039-006] Dounia gained one comfort at least from that interview, that her

[039-007] brother would not be alone. He had gone to her, Sonia, first with his

[039-008] confession; he had gone to her for human fellowship when he needed it;

[039-009] she would go with him wherever fate might send him. Dounia did not ask,

[039-010] but she knew it was so. She looked at Sonia almost with reverence and

[039-011] at first almost embarrassed her by it. Sonia was almost on the point

[039-012] of tears. She felt herself, on the contrary, hardly worthy to look at

[039-013] Dounia. Dounia’s gracious image when she had bowed to her so attentively

[039-014] and respectfully at their first meeting in [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]’s room had

[039-015] remained in her mind as one of the fairest [[dreams-and-hallucinations|visions]] of her life.

[039-016] Dounia at last became impatient and, leaving Sonia, went to her

[039-017] brother’s room to await him there; she kept thinking that he would come

[039-018] there first. When she had gone, Sonia began to be tortured by the [[fear-and-dread|dread]]

[039-019] of his committing suicide, and Dounia too feared it. But they had spent

[039-020] the day trying to persuade each other that that could not be, and both

[039-021] were less anxious while they were together. As soon as they parted, each

[039-022] thought of nothing else. Sonia remembered how Svidrigaïlov had said to

[039-023] her the day before that Raskolnikov had two alternatives--Siberia or...

[039-024] Besides she knew his vanity, his pride and his lack of faith.

[039-025] “Is it possible that he has nothing but cowardice and fear of death to

[039-026] make him live?” she thought at last in despair.

[039-027] Meanwhile the sun was setting. Sonia was standing in dejection, looking

[039-028] intently out of the window, but from it she could see nothing but the

[039-029] unwhitewashed blank wall of the next house. At last when she began to

[039-030] feel sure of his death--he walked into the room.

[039-031] She gave a cry of joy, but looking carefully into his face she turned

[039-032] pale.

[039-033] “Yes,” said Raskolnikov, smiling. “I have come for your cross, Sonia. It

[039-034] was you told me to go to the cross-roads; why is it you are frightened

[039-035] now it’s come to that?”

[039-036] Sonia gazed at him astonished. His tone seemed strange to her; a cold

[039-037] shiver ran over her, but in a moment she guessed that the tone and the

[039-038] words were a mask. He spoke to her looking away, as though to avoid

[039-039] meeting her eyes.

[039-040] “You see, Sonia, I’ve decided that it will be better so. There is one

[039-041] fact.... But it’s a long story and there’s no need to discuss it. But

[039-042] do you know what angers me? It annoys me that all those stupid brutish

[039-043] faces will be gaping at me directly, pestering me with their stupid

[039-044] questions, which I shall have to answer--they’ll point their fingers at

[039-045] me.... Tfoo! You know I am not going to [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]], I am sick of him. I’d

[039-046] rather go to my friend, the Explosive Lieutenant; how I shall surprise

[039-047] him, what a sensation I shall make! But I must be cooler; I’ve become

[039-048] too irritable of late. You know I was nearly shaking my fist at my

[039-049] sister just now, because she turned to take a last look at me. It’s

[039-050] a brutal state to be in! Ah! what am I coming to! Well, where are the

[039-051] crosses?”

[039-052] He seemed hardly to know what he was doing. He could not stay still or

[039-053] concentrate his attention on anything; his ideas seemed to gallop after

[039-054] one another, he talked incoherently, his hands trembled slightly.

[039-055] Without a word Sonia took out of the drawer two crosses, one of cypress

[039-056] wood and one of copper. She made the sign of the cross over herself and

[039-057] over him, and put the wooden cross on his neck.

[039-058] “It’s the symbol of my taking up the cross,” he laughed. “As though I

[039-059] had not suffered much till now! The wooden cross, that is the peasant

[039-060] one; the copper one, that is [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]’s--you will wear yourself, show

[039-061] me! So she had it on... at that moment? I remember two things like

[039-062] these too, a silver one and a little ikon. I threw them back on the old

[039-063] woman’s neck. Those would be appropriate now, really, those are what I

[039-064] ought to put on now.... But I am talking nonsense and forgetting what

[039-065] matters; I’m somehow forgetful.... You see I have come to warn you,

[039-066] Sonia, so that you might know... that’s all--that’s all I came for. But

[039-067] I thought I had more to say. You wanted me to go yourself. Well, now I

[039-068] am going to prison and you’ll have your wish. Well, what are you crying

[039-069] for? You too? Don’t. Leave off! Oh, how I hate it all!”

[039-070] But his feeling was stirred; his heart ached, as he looked at her. “Why

[039-071] is she grieving too?” he thought to himself. “What am I to her? Why does

[039-072] she weep? Why is she looking after me, like my mother or Dounia? She’ll

[039-073] be my nurse.”

[039-074] “Cross yourself, say at least one prayer,” Sonia begged in a timid

[039-075] broken voice.

[039-076] “Oh certainly, as much as you like! And sincerely, Sonia, sincerely....”

[039-077] But he wanted to say something quite different.

[039-078] He crossed himself several times. Sonia took up her shawl and put

[039-079] it over her head. It was the green _drap de dames_ shawl of which

[039-080] [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] had spoken, “the family shawl.” Raskolnikov thought of that

[039-081] looking at it, but he did not ask. He began to feel himself that he

[039-082] was certainly forgetting things and was disgustingly agitated. He was

[039-083] frightened at this. He was suddenly struck too by the thought that Sonia

[039-084] meant to go with him.

[039-085] “What are you doing? Where are you going? Stay here, stay! I’ll go

[039-086] alone,” he cried in cowardly vexation, and almost resentful, he moved

[039-087] towards the door. “What’s the use of going in procession?” he muttered

[039-088] going out.

[039-089] Sonia remained standing in the middle of the room. He had not even said

[039-090] good-bye to her; he had forgotten her. A poignant and rebellious doubt

[039-091] surged in his heart.

[039-092] “Was it right, was it right, all this?” he thought again as he went down

[039-093] the [[staircases|stairs]]. “Couldn’t he stop and retract it all... and not go?”

[039-094] But still he went. He felt suddenly once for all that he mustn’t ask

[039-095] himself questions. As he turned into the street he remembered that he

[039-096] had not said good-bye to Sonia, that he had left her in the middle of

[039-097] the room in her green shawl, not daring to stir after he had shouted

[039-098] at her, and he stopped short for a moment. At the same instant, another

[039-099] thought dawned upon him, as though it had been lying in wait to strike

[039-100] him then.

[039-101] “Why, with what object did I go to her just now? I told her--on

[039-102] business; on what business? I had no sort of business! To tell her I was

[039-103] _going_; but where was the need? Do I love her? No, no, I drove her away

[039-104] just now like a dog. Did I want her crosses? Oh, how low I’ve sunk! No,

[039-105] I wanted her tears, I wanted to see her terror, to see how her heart

[039-106] ached! I had to have something to cling to, something to delay me, some

[039-107] friendly face to see! And I dared to believe in myself, to dream of what

[039-108] I would do! I am a beggarly contemptible wretch, contemptible!”

[039-109] He walked along [[catherine-canal|the canal]] bank, and he had not much further to go. But

[039-110] on reaching the bridge he stopped and turning out of his way along it

[039-111] went to the [[hay-market|Hay Market]].

[039-112] He looked eagerly to right and left, gazed intently at every object and

[039-113] could not fix his attention on anything; everything slipped away. “In

[039-114] another week, another month I shall be driven in a prison van over this

[039-115] bridge, how shall I look at the canal then? I should like to remember

[039-116] this!” slipped into his mind. “Look at this sign! How shall I read those

[039-117] letters then? It’s written here ‘Campany,’ that’s a thing to remember,

[039-118] that letter _a_, and to look at it again in a month--how shall I look

[039-119] at it then? What shall I be feeling and thinking then?... How trivial

[039-120] it all must be, what I am fretting about now! Of course it must all be

[039-121] interesting... in its way... (Ha-ha-ha! What am I thinking about?) I am

[039-122] becoming a baby, I am showing off to myself; why am I ashamed? Foo! how

[039-123] people shove! that fat man--a German he must be--who pushed against

[039-124] me, does he know whom he pushed? There’s a peasant woman with a baby,

[039-125] begging. It’s curious that she thinks me happier than she is. I might

[039-126] give her something, for the incongruity of it. Here’s a five copeck

[039-127] piece left in my pocket, where did I get it? Here, here... take it, my

[039-128] good woman!”

[039-129] “God bless you,” the beggar chanted in a lachrymose voice.

[039-130] He went into the Hay Market. It was distasteful, very distasteful to be

[039-131] in a crowd, but he walked just where he saw most people. He would have

[039-132] given anything in the world to be alone; but he knew himself that he

[039-133] would not have remained alone for a moment. There was a man drunk and

[039-134] disorderly in the crowd; he kept trying to dance and falling down. There

[039-135] was a ring round him. Raskolnikov squeezed his way through the crowd,

[039-136] stared for some minutes at the drunken man and suddenly gave a short

[039-137] jerky laugh. A minute later he had forgotten him and did not see him,

[039-138] though he still stared. He moved away at last, not remembering where he

[039-139] was; but when he got into the middle of the square an emotion suddenly

[039-140] came over him, overwhelming him body and mind.

[039-141] He suddenly recalled Sonia’s words, “Go to the cross-roads, bow down to

[039-142] the people, kiss the earth, for you have sinned against it too, and say

[039-143] aloud to the whole world, ‘I am a murderer.’” He trembled, remembering

[039-144] that. And the hopeless misery and anxiety of all that time, especially

[039-145] of the last hours, had weighed so heavily upon him that he positively

[039-146] clutched at the [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] of this new unmixed, complete sensation. It came

[039-147] over him like a fit; it was like a single spark kindled in his soul and

[039-148] spreading fire through him. Everything in him softened at once and the

[039-149] tears started into his eyes. He fell to the earth on the spot....

[039-150] He knelt down in the middle of the square, bowed down to the earth, and

[039-151] kissed that filthy earth with bliss and rapture. He got up and bowed

[039-152] down a second time.

[039-153] “He’s boozed,” a youth near him observed.

[039-154] There was a roar of laughter.

[039-155] “He’s going to Jerusalem, brothers, and saying good-bye to his children

[039-156] and his country. He’s bowing down to all the world and kissing the great

[039-157] city of [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]] and its pavement,” added a workman who was a

[039-158] little drunk.

[039-159] “Quite a young man, too!” observed a third.

[039-160] “And a gentleman,” someone observed soberly.

[039-161] “There’s no knowing who’s a gentleman and who isn’t nowadays.”

[039-162] These exclamations and remarks checked Raskolnikov, and the words, “I am

[039-163] a murderer,” which were perhaps on the point of dropping from his lips,

[039-164] died away. He bore these remarks quietly, however, and, without looking

[039-165] round, he turned down a street leading to the police office. He had a

[039-166] glimpse of something on the way which did not surprise him; he had felt

[039-167] that it must be so. The second time he bowed down in the Hay Market he

[039-168] saw, standing fifty paces from him on the left, Sonia. She was hiding

[039-169] from him behind one of the wooden shanties in the market-place. She had

[039-170] followed him then on his painful way! Raskolnikov at that moment felt

[039-171] and knew once for all that Sonia was with him for ever and would follow

[039-172] him to the ends of the earth, wherever fate might take him. It wrung his

[039-173] heart... but he was just reaching the fatal place.

[039-174] He went into the yard fairly resolutely. He had to mount to the third

[039-175] storey. “I shall be some time going up,” he thought. He felt as though

[039-176] the fateful moment was still far off, as though he had plenty of time

[039-177] left for consideration.

[039-178] Again the same rubbish, the same eggshells lying about on the spiral

[039-179] stairs, again the open doors of the flats, again the same kitchens and

[039-180] the same fumes and stench coming from them. Raskolnikov had not been

[039-181] here since that day. His legs were numb and gave way under him, but

[039-182] still they moved forward. He stopped for a moment to take breath, to

[039-183] collect himself, so as to enter _like a man_. “But why? what for?” he

[039-184] wondered, reflecting. “If I must drink the cup what difference does it

[039-185] make? The more revolting the better.” He imagined for an instant the

[039-186] figure of the “explosive lieutenant,” Ilya Petrovitch. Was he actually

[039-187] going to him? Couldn’t he go to someone else? To Nikodim Fomitch?

[039-188] Couldn’t he turn back and go straight to Nikodim Fomitch’s lodgings?

[039-189] At least then it would be done privately.... No, no! To the “explosive

[039-190] lieutenant”! If he must drink it, drink it off at once.

[039-191] Turning cold and hardly conscious, he opened the door of the office.

[039-192] There were very few people in it this time--only a house porter and a

[039-193] peasant. The doorkeeper did not even peep out from behind his screen.

[039-194] Raskolnikov walked into the next room. “Perhaps I still need not speak,”

[039-195] passed through his mind. Some sort of clerk not wearing a uniform was

[039-196] settling himself at a bureau to write. In a corner another clerk was

[039-197] seating himself. [[zametov|Zametov]] was not there, nor, of course, Nikodim Fomitch.

[039-198] “No one in?” Raskolnikov asked, addressing the person at the bureau.

[039-199] “Whom do you want?”

[039-200] “A-ah! Not a sound was heard, not a sight was seen, but I scent the

[039-201] Russian... how does it go on in the fairy tale... I’ve forgotten! ‘At

[039-202] your service!’” a familiar voice cried suddenly.

[039-203] Raskolnikov shuddered. The Explosive Lieutenant stood before him. He

[039-204] had just come in from the third room. “It is the hand of fate,” thought

[039-205] Raskolnikov. “Why is he here?”

[039-206] “You’ve come to see us? What about?” cried Ilya Petrovitch. He

[039-207] was obviously in an exceedingly good humour and perhaps a trifle

[039-208] exhilarated. “If it’s on business you are rather early.[*] It’s only a

[039-209] chance that I am here... however I’ll do what I can. I must admit, I...

[039-210] what is it, what is it? Excuse me....”

[039-211] [*] Dostoevsky appears to have forgotten that it is after

[039-212] sunset, and that the last time Raskolnikov visited the

[039-213] police office at two in the afternoon he was reproached for

[039-214] coming too late.--TRANSLATOR.

[039-215] “Raskolnikov.”

[039-216] “Of course, Raskolnikov. You didn’t imagine I’d forgotten? Don’t think I

[039-217] am like that... Rodion Ro--Ro--Rodionovitch, that’s it, isn’t it?”

[039-218] “Rodion Romanovitch.”

[039-219] “Yes, yes, of course, Rodion Romanovitch! I was just getting at it. I

[039-220] made many inquiries about you. I assure you I’ve been genuinely grieved

[039-221] since that... since I behaved like that... it was explained to me

[039-222] afterwards that you were a literary man... and a learned one too... and

[039-223] so to say the first steps... Mercy on us! What literary or scientific

[039-224] man does not begin by some originality of conduct! My wife and I have

[039-225] the greatest respect for literature, in my wife it’s a genuine passion!

[039-226] Literature and art! If only a man is a gentleman, all the rest can be

[039-227] gained by talents, learning, good sense, genius. As for a hat--well,

[039-228] what does a hat matter? I can buy a hat as easily as I can a bun; but

[039-229] what’s under the hat, what the hat covers, I can’t buy that! I was even

[039-230] meaning to come and apologise to you, but thought maybe you’d... But I

[039-231] am forgetting to ask you, is there anything you want really? I hear your

[039-232] family have come?”

[039-233] “Yes, my mother and sister.”

[039-234] “I’ve even had the honour and happiness of meeting your sister--a highly

[039-235] cultivated and charming person. I confess I was sorry I got so hot with

[039-236] you. There it is! But as for my looking suspiciously at your fainting

[039-237] fit--that affair has been cleared up splendidly! Bigotry and fanaticism!

[039-238] I understand your indignation. Perhaps you are changing your lodging on

[039-239] account of your family’s arriving?”

[039-240] “No, I only looked in... I came to ask... I thought that I should find

[039-241] Zametov here.”

[039-242] “Oh, yes! Of course, you’ve made friends, I heard. Well, no, Zametov is

[039-243] not here. Yes, we’ve lost Zametov. He’s not been here since yesterday...

[039-244] he quarrelled with everyone on leaving... in the rudest way. He is a

[039-245] feather-headed youngster, that’s all; one might have expected something

[039-246] from him, but there, you know what they are, our brilliant young men.

[039-247] He wanted to go in for some examination, but it’s only to talk and

[039-248] boast about it, it will go no further than that. Of course it’s a very

[039-249] different matter with you or Mr. [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] there, your friend. Your

[039-250] career is an intellectual one and you won’t be deterred by failure. For

[039-251] you, one may say, all the attractions of life _nihil est_--you are an

[039-252] ascetic, a monk, a hermit!... A book, a pen behind your ear, a learned

[039-253] research--that’s where your spirit soars! I am the same way myself....

[039-254] Have you read Livingstone’s Travels?”

[039-255] “No.”

[039-256] “Oh, I have. There are a great many Nihilists about nowadays, you know,

[039-257] and indeed it is not to be wondered at. What sort of days are they? I

[039-259] openly, openly!”

[039-260] “N-no...”

[039-261] “Believe me, you can speak openly to me as you would to yourself!

[039-268] reputation, over a glass of champagne... that’s all your Zametov is good

[039-270] feelings, and besides I have rank, consequence, a post! I am married and

[039-271] have children, I fulfil the duties of a man and a citizen, but who is

[039-273] these midwives, too, have become extraordinarily numerous.”

[039-275] Petrovitch, who had obviously been dining, were for the most part a

[039-276] stream of empty sounds for him. But some of them he understood. He

[039-277] looked at him inquiringly, not knowing how it would end.

[039-280] one, ha-ha! They go to the Academy, study anatomy. If I fall ill, am

[039-282] Petrovitch laughed, quite pleased with his own wit. “It’s an immoderate

[039-283] zeal for education, but once you’re educated, that’s enough. Why abuse

[039-284] it? Why insult honourable people, as that scoundrel Zametov does? Why

[039-285] did he insult me, I ask you? Look at these suicides, too, how common

[039-287] themselves, boys and girls and old people. Only this morning we heard

[039-309] “Oh, not at all, as often as you like. It’s a pleasure to see you and I

[039-311] Ilya Petrovitch held out his hand.

[039-316] what he was doing. He began going down the stairs, supporting himself

[039-317] with his right hand against the wall. He fancied that a porter pushed

[039-318] past him on his way upstairs to the police office, that a dog in

[039-319] the lower storey kept up a shrill barking and that a woman flung a

[039-320] rolling-pin at it and shouted. He went down and out into the yard.

[039-322] She looked wildly at him. He stood still before her. There was a look of

[039-323] poignant agony, of despair, in her face. She clasped her hands. His lips

[039-324] worked in an ugly, meaningless smile. He stood still a minute, grinned

[039-325] and went back to the police office.

[039-327] him stood the same peasant who had pushed by on the stairs.

[039-344] EPILOGUE

[039-345] I

[039-346] Siberia. On the banks of a broad solitary river stands a town, one of

[039-347] the administrative centres of Russia; in the town there is a fortress,

[039-348] in the fortress there is a prison. In the prison the second-class

[039-349] convict Rodion Raskolnikov has been confined for nine months. Almost a

[039-350] year and a half has passed since his crime.

[039-351] There had been little difficulty about his trial. The criminal adhered

[039-352] exactly, firmly, and clearly to his statement. He did not confuse nor

[039-353] misrepresent the facts, nor soften them in his own interest, nor omit

[039-354] the smallest detail. He explained every incident of the murder, the

[039-355] secret of _the pledge_ (the piece of wood with a strip of metal) which

[039-357] had taken her keys, what they were like, as well as the chest and its

[039-359] [[koch|Koch]] and, after him, the student knocked, and repeated all they had said

[039-361] and Dmitri shouting; how he had hidden in the empty flat and afterwards

[039-366] had hidden the trinkets and the purse under a stone, without making

[039-369] never opened the purse and did not even know how much was in it seemed

[039-371] seventeen roubles and sixty copecks. From being so long under the stone,

[039-373] damp. They were a long while trying to discover why the accused man

[039-374] should tell a lie about this, when about everything else he had made

[039-375] a truthful and straightforward confession. Finally some of the lawyers

[039-376] more versed in psychology admitted that it was possible he had really

[039-377] not looked into the purse, and so didn’t know what was in it when he

[039-378] hid it under the stone. But they immediately drew the deduction that

[039-379] the crime could only have been committed through temporary mental

[039-380] derangement, through homicidal mania, without object or the pursuit of

[039-381] gain. This fell in with the most recent fashionable theory of temporary

[039-382] insanity, so often applied in our days in criminal cases. Moreover

[039-383] Raskolnikov’s hypochondriacal condition was proved by many witnesses, by

[039-384] Dr. [[zossimov|Zossimov]], his former fellow students, his landlady and her servant.

[039-386] quite like an ordinary murderer and robber, but that there was another

[039-387] element in the case.

[039-396] privation and failure. To the question what led him to confess, he

[039-398] coarse....

[039-608] He was ill a long time. But it was not the horrors of prison life, not

[039-609] the hard labour, the bad food, the shaven head, or the patched clothes

[039-610] that crushed him. What did he care for all those trials and hardships!

[039-611] he was even glad of the hard work. Physically exhausted, he could at

[039-612] least reckon on a few hours of quiet sleep. And what was the food to

[039-613] him--the thin cabbage soup with beetles floating in it? In the past as a

[039-614] student he had often not had even that. His clothes were warm and suited

[039-615] to his manner of life. He did not even feel the fetters. Was he ashamed

[039-616] of his shaven head and parti-coloured coat? Before whom? Before Sonia?

[039-617] Sonia was afraid of him, how could he be ashamed before her? And yet he

[039-618] was ashamed even before Sonia, whom he tortured because of it with

[039-619] his contemptuous rough manner. But it was not his shaven head and his

[039-620] fetters he was ashamed of: his pride had been stung to the quick. It was

[039-621] wounded pride that made him ill. Oh, how happy he would have been if he

[039-622] could have blamed himself! He could have borne anything then, even

[039-623] shame and disgrace. But he judged himself severely, and his exasperated

[039-624] conscience found no particularly terrible fault in his past, except

[039-625] a simple _blunder_ which might happen to anyone. He was ashamed just

[039-626] because he, Raskolnikov, had so hopelessly, stupidly come to grief

[039-627] through some decree of blind fate, and must humble himself and submit to

[039-628] “the idiocy” of a sentence, if he were anyhow to be at peace.

[039-629] Vague and objectless anxiety in the present, and in the future a

[039-630] continual sacrifice leading to nothing--that was all that lay before

[039-631] him. And what comfort was it to him that at the end of eight years he

[039-632] would only be thirty-two and able to begin a new life! What had he to

[039-633] live for? What had he to look forward to? Why should he strive? To live

[039-634] in order to exist? Why, he had been ready a thousand times before to

[039-635] give up existence for the sake of an idea, for a hope, even for a fancy.

[039-636] Mere existence had always been too little for him; he had always wanted

[039-637] more. Perhaps it was just because of the strength of his desires that he

[039-638] had thought himself a man to whom more was permissible than to others.

[039-639] And if only fate would have sent him repentance--burning repentance that

[039-640] would have torn his heart and robbed him of sleep, that repentance, the

[039-641] awful agony of which brings visions of hanging or drowning! Oh, he would

[039-642] have been glad of it! Tears and agonies would at least have been life.

[039-643] But he did not repent of his crime.

[039-644] At least he might have found relief in raging at his stupidity, as he

[039-645] had raged at the grotesque blunders that had brought him to prison.

[039-646] But now in prison, _in freedom_, he thought over and criticised all his

[039-647] actions again and by no means found them so blundering and so grotesque

[039-648] as they had seemed at the fatal time.

[039-649] “In what way,” he asked himself, “was my theory stupider than others

[039-650] that have swarmed and clashed from the beginning of the world? One has

[039-651] only to look at the thing quite independently, broadly, and uninfluenced

[039-652] by commonplace ideas, and my idea will by no means seem so... strange.

[039-653] Oh, sceptics and halfpenny philosophers, why do you halt half-way!

[039-654] “Why does my action strike them as so horrible?” he said to himself. “Is

[039-655] it because it was a crime? What is meant by crime? My conscience is at

[039-656] rest. Of course, it was a legal crime, of course, the letter of the law

[039-657] was broken and blood was shed. Well, punish me for the letter of the

[039-658] law... and that’s enough. Of course, in that case many of the

[039-659] benefactors of mankind who snatched power for themselves instead of

[039-660] inheriting it ought to have been punished at their first steps. But

[039-661] those men succeeded and so _they were right_, and I didn’t, and so I

[039-662] had no right to have taken that step.”

[039-663] It was only in that that he recognised his criminality, only in the fact

[039-664] that he had been unsuccessful and had confessed it.

[039-665] He suffered too from the question: why had he not killed himself? Why

[039-666] had he stood looking at the river and preferred to confess? Was the

[039-667] desire to live so strong and was it so hard to overcome it? Had not

[039-668] Svidrigaïlov overcome it, although he was afraid of death?

[039-671] perhaps been dimly conscious of the fundamental falsity in himself and

[039-673] the promise of a future crisis, of a new view of life and of his future

[039-676] could not step over, again through weakness and meanness. He looked at

[039-722] particular services. Only once at Christmas she sent them all presents

[039-723] of pies and rolls. But by degrees closer relations sprang up between

[039-724] them and Sonia. She would write and post letters for them to their

[039-725] relations. Relations of the prisoners who visited the town, at their

[039-726] instructions, left with Sonia presents and money for them. Their wives

[039-727] and sweethearts knew her and used to visit her. And when she visited

[039-728] Raskolnikov at work, or met a party of the prisoners on the road, they

[039-729] all took off their hats to her. “Little mother Sofya Semyonovna, you

[039-730] are our dear, good little mother,” coarse branded criminals said to that

[039-731] frail little creature. She would smile and bow to them and everyone was

[039-732] delighted when she smiled. They even admired her gait and turned round

[039-733] to watch her walking; they admired her too for being so little, and, in

[039-734] fact, did not know what to admire her most for. They even came to her

[039-735] for help in their illnesses.

[039-736] He was in the hospital from the middle of Lent till after Easter. When

[039-737] he was better, he remembered the dreams he had had while he was feverish

[039-738] and delirious. He dreamt that the whole world was condemned to a

[039-739] terrible new strange plague that had come to Europe from the depths of

[039-740] Asia. All were to be destroyed except a very few chosen. Some new sorts

[039-741] of microbes were attacking the bodies of men, but these microbes were

[039-742] endowed with intelligence and will. Men attacked by them became at once

[039-743] mad and furious. But never had men considered themselves so intellectual

[039-744] and so completely in possession of the truth as these sufferers, never

[039-745] had they considered their decisions, their scientific conclusions, their

[039-746] moral convictions so infallible. Whole villages, whole towns and peoples

[039-747] went mad from the infection. All were excited and did not understand

[039-748] one another. Each thought that he alone had the truth and was wretched

[039-749] looking at the others, beat himself on the breast, wept, and wrung

[039-750] his hands. They did not know how to judge and could not agree what to

[039-751] consider evil and what good; they did not know whom to blame, whom

[039-752] to justify. Men killed each other in a sort of senseless spite. They

[039-753] gathered together in armies against one another, but even on the march

[039-754] the armies would begin attacking each other, the ranks would be broken

[039-755] and the soldiers would fall on each other, stabbing and cutting, biting

[039-756] and devouring each other. The alarm bell was ringing all day long in

[039-757] the towns; men rushed together, but why they were summoned and who was

[039-758] summoning them no one knew. The most ordinary trades were abandoned,

[039-759] because everyone proposed his own ideas, his own improvements, and they

[039-760] could not agree. The land too was abandoned. Men met in groups, agreed

[039-761] on something, swore to keep together, but at once began on something

[039-762] quite different from what they had proposed. They accused one another,

[039-763] fought and killed each other. There were conflagrations and famine. All

[039-764] men and all things were involved in destruction. The plague spread and

[039-765] moved further and further. Only a few men could be saved in the whole

[039-766] world. They were a pure chosen people, destined to found a new race and

[039-767] a new life, to renew and purify the earth, but no one had seen these

[039-768] men, no one had heard their words and their voices.

[039-769] Raskolnikov was worried that this senseless dream haunted his memory so

[039-770] miserably, the impression of this feverish delirium persisted so long.

[039-771] The second week after Easter had come. There were warm bright spring

[039-772] days; in the prison ward the grating windows under which the sentinel

[039-773] paced were opened. Sonia had only been able to visit him twice during

[039-774] his illness; each time she had to obtain permission, and it was

[039-775] difficult. But she often used to come to the hospital yard, especially

[039-776] in the evening, sometimes only to stand a minute and look up at the

[039-777] windows of the ward.

[039-778] One evening, when he was almost well again, Raskolnikov fell asleep. On

[039-779] waking up he chanced to go to the window, and at once saw Sonia in the

[039-780] distance at the hospital gate. She seemed to be waiting for someone.

[039-781] Something stabbed him to the heart at that minute. He shuddered and

[039-782] moved away from the window. Next day Sonia did not come, nor the day

[039-783] after; he noticed that he was expecting her uneasily. At last he was

[039-784] discharged. On reaching the prison he learnt from the convicts that

[039-785] Sofya Semyonovna was lying ill at home and was unable to go out.

[039-786] He was very uneasy and sent to inquire after her; he soon learnt that

[039-787] her illness was not dangerous. Hearing that he was anxious about her,

[039-788] Sonia sent him a pencilled note, telling him that she was much better,

[039-789] that she had a slight cold and that she would soon, very soon come and

[039-790] see him at his work. His heart throbbed painfully as he read it.

[039-791] Again it was a warm bright day. Early in the morning, at six o’clock, he

[039-792] went off to work on the river bank, where they used to pound alabaster

[039-793] and where there was a kiln for baking it in a shed. There were only

[039-794] three of them sent. One of the convicts went with the guard to the

[039-795] fortress to fetch a tool; the other began getting the wood ready and

[039-796] laying it in the kiln. Raskolnikov came out of the shed on to the river

[039-797] bank, sat down on a heap of logs by the shed and began gazing at the

[039-798] wide deserted river. From the high bank a broad landscape opened before

[039-799] him, the sound of singing floated faintly audible from the other bank.

[039-800] In the vast steppe, bathed in sunshine, he could just see, like black

[039-801] specks, the nomads’ tents. There there was freedom, there other men were

[039-802] living, utterly unlike those here; there time itself seemed to stand

[039-803] still, as though the age of Abraham and his flocks had not passed.

[039-804] Raskolnikov sat gazing, his thoughts passed into day-dreams, into

[039-805] contemplation; he thought of nothing, but a vague restlessness excited

[039-806] and troubled him. Suddenly he found Sonia beside him; she had come up

[039-807] noiselessly and sat down at his side. It was still quite early; the

[039-808] morning chill was still keen. She wore her poor old burnous and the

[039-809] green shawl; her face still showed signs of illness, it was thinner and

[039-810] paler. She gave him a joyful smile of welcome, but held out her hand

[039-811] with her usual timidity. She was always timid of holding out her hand

[039-812] to him and sometimes did not offer it at all, as though afraid he would

[039-813] repel it. He always took her hand as though with repugnance, always

[039-814] seemed vexed to meet her and was sometimes obstinately silent throughout

[039-815] her visit. Sometimes she trembled before him and went away deeply

[039-816] grieved. But now their hands did not part. He stole a rapid glance

[039-817] at her and dropped his eyes on the ground without speaking. They were

[039-818] alone, no one had seen them. The guard had turned away for the time.

[039-819] How it happened he did not know. But all at once something seemed to

[039-820] seize him and fling him at her feet. He wept and threw his arms round

[039-821] her knees. For the first instant she was terribly frightened and she

[039-822] turned pale. She jumped up and looked at him trembling. But at the same

[039-823] moment she understood, and a light of infinite happiness came into her

[039-824] eyes. She knew and had no doubt that he loved her beyond everything and

[039-825] that at last the moment had come....

[039-826] They wanted to speak, but could not; tears stood in their eyes. They

[039-827] were both pale and thin; but those sick pale faces were bright with the

[039-828] dawn of a new future, of a full resurrection into a new life. They were

[039-829] renewed by love; the heart of each held infinite sources of life for the

[039-830] heart of the other.

[039-831] They resolved to wait and be patient. They had another seven years to

[039-832] wait, and what terrible suffering and what infinite happiness before

[039-833] them! But he had risen again and he knew it and felt it in all his

[039-834] being, while she--she only lived in his life.

[039-835] On the evening of the same day, when the barracks were locked,

[039-836] Raskolnikov lay on his plank bed and thought of her. He had even fancied

[039-837] that day that all the convicts who had been his enemies looked at him

[039-838] differently; he had even entered into talk with them and they answered

[039-839] him in a friendly way. He remembered that now, and thought it was bound

[039-840] to be so. Wasn’t everything now bound to be changed?

[039-841] He thought of her. He remembered how continually he had tormented her

[039-842] and wounded her heart. He remembered her pale and thin little face.

[039-843] But these recollections scarcely troubled him now; he knew with what

[039-844] infinite love he would now repay all her sufferings. And what were all,

[039-845] _all_ the agonies of the past! Everything, even his crime, his sentence

[039-846] and imprisonment, seemed to him now in the first rush of feeling an

[039-847] external, strange fact with which he had no concern. But he could not

[039-848] think for long together of anything that evening, and he could not have

[039-849] analysed anything consciously; he was simply feeling. Life had stepped

[039-850] into the place of theory and something quite different would work itself

[039-851] out in his mind.

[039-852] Under his pillow lay the New Testament. He took it up mechanically.

[039-853] The book belonged to Sonia; it was the one from which she had read the

[039-854] raising of Lazarus to him. At first he was afraid that she would worry

[039-855] him about religion, would talk about the gospel and pester him with

[039-856] books. But to his great surprise she had not once approached the subject

[039-857] and had not even offered him the Testament. He had asked her for it

[039-858] himself not long before his illness and she brought him the book without

[039-859] a word. Till now he had not opened it.

[039-860] He did not open it now, but one thought passed through his mind: “Can

[039-861] her convictions not be mine now? Her feelings, her aspirations at

[039-862] least....”

[039-863] She too had been greatly agitated that day, and at night she was taken

[039-864] ill again. But she was so happy--and so unexpectedly happy--that she was

[039-865] almost frightened of her happiness. Seven years, _only_ seven years! At

[039-866] the beginning of their happiness at some moments they were both ready

[039-867] to look on those seven years as though they were seven days. He did not

[039-868] know that the new life would not be given him for nothing, that he would

[039-869] have to pay dearly for it, that it would cost him great striving, great

[039-870] suffering.

[039-871] But that is the beginning of a new story--the story of the gradual

[039-872] renewal of a man, the story of his gradual regeneration, of his passing

[039-873] from one world into another, of his initiation into a new unknown life.

[039-874] That might be the subject of a new story, but our present story is

[039-875] ended.
