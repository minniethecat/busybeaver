# Social Class and Status

Social class in *Crime and Punishment* is not merely background but a driving force of the plot — every character's actions, motivations, and moral possibilities are shaped by their position in the rigid hierarchy of 1860s St. Petersburg, from the destitute student to the calculating aristocrat.

## Definition

"Class" in Dostoevsky's novel refers to the system of social stratification that governed life in 19th-century Russia — a hierarchy based on birth rank (the *chin* or Table of Ranks), wealth, education, and family name. The "working class population crowded in these streets and alleys" (001-061) forms the backdrop of the novel's Petersburg — a city where poverty and rank dictate not only economic circumstance but moral standing and personal dignity. As Marmeladov declares: "poverty is not a vice, that's a true saying.... In poverty you may still retain your innate nobility of soul, but in beggary — never — no one" (002-071, 002-074).

## Expression in the Novel

The novel's social spectrum is deliberately drawn. At the bottom are the Marmeladovs — a former "counsellor in rank" (002-054) reduced to beggary, his consumptive wife Katerina Ivanovna clinging to memories of her "noble" upbringing in a "high-class school for the daughters of noblemen" (002-174). Raskolnikov occupies an ambiguous middle position: "a student, or formerly a student" (002-062), crushed by poverty (001-016) yet belonging to the educated class, which fuels his Napoleonic theory that extraordinary men transcend ordinary morality — a theory inseparable from class resentment. At the top sit figures like Luzhin, the self-made man obsessed with climbing the social ladder, and Svidrigailov, the landed gentry whose wealth grants him impunity. The novel maps Petersburg as a city where social distance is physical: the Haymarket with its working-class crowds, the cramped garrets of students, and the drawing rooms of the aristocracy.

## Key Passages

> "Honoured sir, poverty is not a vice, that's a true saying. I know too that drunkenness is not a virtue, and that is even truer. But beggary, honoured sir, beggary is a vice. In poverty you may still retain your innate nobility of soul, but in beggary — never — no one." (002-071, 002-074)

> "He was crushed by poverty, but the anxieties of his position had of late ceased to weigh upon him." (001-016)

> "A student then, or formerly a student," cried the clerk. "Just what I thought! A student or have attended some learned institution!" (002-062, 002-065)
