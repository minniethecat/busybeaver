# Pride and Humiliation

Dostoevsky's St. Petersburg is a theater of humiliation in which every character performs a version of the same drama: the assertion of dignity against overwhelming evidence of degradation. Pride is the defensive response; humiliation is the inevitable outcome. Katerina Ivanovna's "poor man's pride" (028-009) is the novel's paradigmatic case.

## Definition

"Poor man's pride" is the compulsion "which compels many poor people to spend their last savings on some traditional social ceremony, simply in order to do 'like other people' and not to be 'criticised'" (028-009). It is pride weaponized against oneself — the refusal to accept help that makes suffering worse. More broadly, the novel traces pride across the social spectrum: Marmeladov's drunken self-abasement, Raskolnikov's "haughty pride and reserve" (004-387), Luzhin's vanity, and the "paroxysms of pride and vanity" (028-019) that afflict the humiliated.

## Expression in the Novel

Katerina Ivanovna's funeral dinner is the novel's great set piece of pride and humiliation. She spends money the family doesn't have on a funeral feast, driven by the need to demonstrate that she is "of noble birth" despite her circumstances. The dinner collapses into farce: wrong guests arrive, Luzhin accuses Sonia of theft, and Katerina Ivanovna is evicted, dying in the street still proclaiming her dignity. Raskolnikov's pride operates differently — intellectual rather than social. His "haughty pride and reserve" (004-387) isolates him from Razumihin's friendship and Sonia's compassion. The novel's resolution requires the surrender of pride: Raskolnikov must kneel in the Hay Market and confess before he can begin to heal.

## Key Passages

> "There is a peculiar 'poor man's pride' which compels many poor people to spend their last savings on some traditional social ceremony, simply in order to do 'like other people.'" (028-009)

> "Paroxysms of pride and vanity which take the form of a peculiar irritability." (028-019)

> "There was a sort of haughty pride and reserve about him, as though he were hiding something." (004-387)

## Character Perspectives

- **Katerina Ivanovna**: Pride as pathology — her insistence on her noble origins and proper ceremonies destroys what remains of her family's security. She embodies the tragic dimension of pride: admirable in its refusal to accept degradation, destructive in its refusal to accept help.

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His intellectual pride is the barrier to confession and redemption. Each step toward Sonia is a surrender of pride, culminating in the public confession at the Hay Market.
