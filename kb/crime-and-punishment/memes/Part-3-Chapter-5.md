[019-001] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] was already entering the room. He came in looking as though

[019-002] he had the utmost difficulty not to burst out laughing again. Behind him

[019-003] [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] strode in gawky and awkward, shamefaced and red as a peony,

[019-004] with an utterly crestfallen and ferocious expression. His face and

[019-005] whole figure really were ridiculous at that moment and amply justified

[019-006] Raskolnikov’s laughter. Raskolnikov, not waiting for an introduction,

[019-008] looking inquiringly at them. He held out his hand and shook hands, still

[019-009] apparently making desperate efforts to subdue his mirth and utter a few

[019-011] a serious air and muttering something when he suddenly glanced again as

[019-012] though accidentally at Razumihin, and could no longer control himself:

[019-013] his stifled laughter broke out the more irresistibly the more he tried

[019-014] to restrain it. The extraordinary ferocity with which Razumihin received

[019-015] this “spontaneous” mirth gave the whole scene the appearance of most

[019-016] genuine fun and naturalness. Razumihin strengthened this impression as

[019-021] “But why break chairs, gentlemen? You know it’s a loss to the Crown,”

[019-022] Porfiry Petrovitch quoted gaily.

[019-035] unpleasantly.

[019-043] that was all, I think!”
