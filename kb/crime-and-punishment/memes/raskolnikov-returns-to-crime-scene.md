# Raskolnikov Returns to the Crime Scene

In one of the novel's most psychologically acute scenes, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] returns to the pawnbroker's apartment days after the murder, driven by an inexplicable compulsion to ring the doorbell and re-experience the sound he first heard when casing the crime. This act — witnessed by workmen and later reported to [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] — becomes a critical thread in the detective's psychological net.

## Summary

Shortly after the murder but before his illness fully subsides, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] finds himself walking toward the pawnbroker's building in [[st-petersburg]]. He enters "the dark and narrow staircase" and ascends to the second floor (024-005). Finding the apartment being redecorated by workmen — the traces of the crime erased — he asks about the blood. When the workmen are puzzled, he rings the bell repeatedly, fixated on the sound: "The same bell, the same cracked note" (013-607). The workmen grow suspicious, and Raskolnikov's strange behavior is later reported to the police, giving [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] a crucial piece of psychological evidence.

## Context & Buildup

The return occurs during the period of Raskolnikov's psychological disintegration following the double murder. He has been alternating between delirium and lucidity, alienated from [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]]'s care and [[zossimov|Zossimov]]'s medical attention. The impulse to return to the scene arises without conscious planning — Dostoevsky emphasizes its compulsive, almost involuntary nature. Earlier, Raskolnikov had "forgotten the note of that bell" but found himself "remembering it with peculiar, startling distinctness" (001-120), foreshadowing the centrality of auditory sensation to his guilt.

The scene also follows the police summons (Part 2, Chapter 1), where Raskolnikov fainted upon hearing the murder discussed — establishing his physiological inability to control his response to the crime. The return to the crime scene is the second instance of this involuntary self-betrayal.

## The Return

Ascending the familiar staircase, Raskolnikov enters the apartment to find it stripped of furniture and occupied by two workmen hanging new wallpaper (024-027). The transformation disorients him — the crime has been physically erased, but its psychological reality intensifies.

He asks the workmen about the blood: "The blood? What blood?" they reply, bewildered (025-414). His question draws their attention. Then, in the scene's most charged moment, he walks to the door and rings the bell:

> "He slowly put out his hand to the bell and rang. The same bell, the same cracked note. He rang a second, a third time; he listened and remembered" (013-607, expanded at 006-406).

This repetition is Dostoevsky's masterstroke — Raskolnikov re-enacts the moment before the murder (when he first rang that bell with the fake pledge) to recapture the sensation of crossing the threshold. The bell's "cracked note" becomes an auditory symbol of his fractured conscience.

The workmen, now alarmed, challenge him. One follows him down the stairs, demanding to know who he is and why he came. Raskolnikov's hostile, evasive response — "Come to the police station then, if you want" (013-647) — only deepens their suspicion.

## Immediate Aftermath

The workmen report the incident, and it reaches [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]]. In their later interrogation (Part 4, Chapter 5), Porfiry uses the bell-ringing as a devastating psychological weapon:

> "Why did you ring the bell at nearly eleven at night at the murdered woman's flat? And why did you ask about the blood?" (025-511)

Porfiry interprets the act not as a rational investigation but as a compulsion — the criminal's need to return, to test, to taunt fate. He calls it "going to ring bells at night and asking about blood" (025-446), framing it as the behavior of a guilty man who wants to be caught: "to work upon your nerves, to go ringing bells at night" (025-449).

## Long-Term Consequences

The bell-ringing incident becomes Porfiry's most potent piece of non-material evidence. Unlike physical clues, it cannot be explained away — it is a window into Raskolnikov's psychology. Throughout the novel, the memory of the bell haunts Raskolnikov:

> "He wanted to feel the cold shiver over again" (033-255).

The scene also foreshadows Raskolnikov's eventual confession — it demonstrates that his psyche cannot sustain the burden of concealment and will, through compulsive acts, betray him. The bell-ringing is the first of many such self-betrayals, culminating in his [[confession-at-hay-market|confession at the Hay Market]].

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The return to the crime scene dramatizes Dostoevsky's central psychological insight: that crime carries its own punishment within the criminal's mind. Raskolnikov is not pursued externally (at first) but internally — his own psyche drives him back to the scene, compels him to ring the bell, and forces him to ask about the blood. The scene is a refutation of the [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary-man theory]]: the "extraordinary man" who kills without remorse does not exist; the human psyche cannot transcend its own conscience.

The auditory motif — the "cracked note" of the bell — is also thematically significant. Sound, unlike sight, is inescapable; it penetrates consciousness without the mediation of interpretation. The bell's note is the voice of the crime itself, calling Raskolnikov back.
