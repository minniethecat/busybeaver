# Raskolnikov Meets Marmeladov

In Part 1, Chapter 2, immediately after his visit to the pawnbroker's flat, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] wanders into a tavern for the first time in his life and encounters Semyon [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]], a drunken ex-titular councillor who delivers one of the novel's most devastating monologues. The meeting is not a [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] encounter in narrative terms — it is a carefully placed moral mirror. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s confession of degradation, his daughter [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia's]] sacrifice, and his paradoxical self-loathing pride anticipate every major theme of the novel: suffering, sacrifice, poverty, and the possibility of redemption through acknowledgment of guilt.

## Summary

Disturbed and hungry after his rehearsal visit to the pawnbroker's apartment, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] enters a tavern on his way home — his first time entering such an establishment (001-250). There a man "over fifty, bald, medium height, stoutly built" who "looked like a retired government clerk" approaches him and launches into a long, unprompted confession (002-054). [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] recounts his family's descent into destitution, his daughter [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s entry into prostitution to feed his stepchildren, and his own self-destructive drinking that feeds on the very guilt of what he has done to his family. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], initially dismissive, becomes an absorbed listener, and the encounter ends with [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] escorting the drunken [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] home — where he witnesses the family's squalor firsthand and silently leaves a handful of money on the windowsill.

## Context & Buildup

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is in a state of psychological turmoil after his visit to [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]], the pawnbroker — what he terms a "rehearsal" for the murder he is contemplating. He is "in a state of overstrained irritability, akin to hypochondria" (001-007). On his way home from the pawnbroker's, he stops at a tavern for a glass of beer — "he had never been into a tavern, but now he felt giddy and was tormented by a burning thirst" (001-250).

The tavern is almost empty, "besides the two drunken men at the door with a pot of beer, a whole group of five men and a girl with a concertina had gone out just before" (001-266–269). Into this space [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] enters, "looking round at the company with an air of disdain, or rather of haughty pride," and fixes his attention on [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] "as though he had been waiting for an opportunity" (002-055).

[[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s opening words establish the confessional frame: "May I venture, honoured sir, to engage you in polite conversation? For though your exterior would not command respect, my experience distinguishes a man of education and one accustomed to good society" (002-054).

## Detailed Account

[[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s monologue is the longest continuous speech in Part 1 of the novel, running through nearly the entire chapter. He begins with a startling self-definition: "I am a titular councillor, [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] — such is my name; a titular councillor" (002-054). The precision of rank contradicts the degradation of his appearance — a man clinging to the dignity of a title that can no longer support him.

He introduces his wife, [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]], as "a person of education and an officer's daughter" (002-139), widowed with three small children and married to [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] in desperation. "She is full of generous feelings," [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] explains, but "hot-tempered and irritable" from relentless poverty (002-226). The family lives in a corner of a room at Mrs. Lippewechsel's lodging, where [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] "is at work from morning till night, scrubbing and cleaning and washing the children" (002-160).

Then comes the central revelation. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] describes how, after losing his position through drink, his fourteen-year-old daughter [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] — "a gentle creature with a soft little voice" — was driven to prostitution by [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]'s accusation that the family were "a burden on her" (002-228–254). [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] recounts:

> "Sonia got up, put on her kerchief and her cape, and went out of the room. She came back at nine o'clock. She went straight to Katerina Ivanovna and laid thirty roubles on the table before her in silence." (002-264)

The image of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] returning home, laying down the money without a word, and lying face down on her bed sobbing, is one of the novel's most compressed moments of moral shock. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] adds: "I was lying drunk and I heard my [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] speaking (she is a gentle creature with a soft little voice...)" (002-252–254).

[[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s speech then takes a philosophical turn. He declares: "For everyone must have somewhere to go" (002-371) — a line that reverberates through the novel. He insists on the paradox of his suffering: "I drink so that I may suffer twice as much!" (002-380). He describes his recent reinstatement and immediate fall: after five days of "paradise" restored to his post, he stole his wife's last savings, drank for five days, and sold his uniform.

The confession builds to its climax: "And the more I drink, the more I feel it. That is why I drink — to find compassion and feeling in drink. I drink because I wish to suffer twice as much!" (002-380). Evidently, [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] was a familiar figure at the tavern — "his habit develops into a need for public confession" (002-097).

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], who has listened through most of this in silence, escorts [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] home. At the [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] family's squalid lodgings, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] witnesses [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]'s fury, the ragged children, and the consumptive wife coughing blood. Without a word, he leaves a handful of copper money — his last remaining kopecks — on the windowsill as he departs.

## Immediate Aftermath

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] leaves the [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] apartment with a sense of confused emotion: "he was full of the conviction that his intentions were now fully formed" (002-550). The encounter has crystallized something in him — though what exactly remains ambiguous. He reflects: "Well, that's settled. But it must be done quickly" — a line that bridges directly to the murder plot.

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s silent donation of money connects him to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s sacrifice with money earned through degradation, even before he knows her. The encounter also introduces the [[hay-market|Hay Market]] neighborhood and the geography of poverty that will dominate the novel's physical and moral landscape.

## Long-Term Consequences

The [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] meeting establishes the novel's central moral calibration: suffering acknowledged versus suffering denied. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s public confession — grotesque, self-pitying, yet genuine in its self-accusation — stands as a counterpoint to [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s silence about his own crime. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] drinks to punish himself; [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] murders to prove himself beyond punishment.

The encounter also introduces [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] as a moral presence before she appears as a character. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s description of her sacrifice — without self-justification, without resentment — prefigures her role as the novel's conscience. When [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] later [[raskolnikov-confesses-to-sonia|confesses to Sonia]], the reader already understands why he chose her: she is the one who bears suffering without complaint.

Most practically, the meeting ensures [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] knows the [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]] family, setting up his future involvement in [[marmeladovs-death|Marmeladov's death]] (Part 2, Chapter 7) and [[marmeladovs-memorial-dinner|the memorial dinner]] (Part 5).

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The tavern scene is structurally the novel's first embedded confession — a microcosm of the entire moral architecture of *Crime and Punishment*. [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s monologue performs a triple function. First, it introduces the theme of confession as a psychological necessity: the drunk's compulsion to tell his story parallels [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s eventual compulsion to confess. Second, it establishes a moral spectrum with [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] at one end (self-sacrifice) and [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary man theory]] at the other (self-exemption). Third, it presents suffering as a form of moral knowledge — [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]], in his degradation, understands something about human need that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s intellectual arrogance cannot grasp.

Dostoevsky compresses the novel's entire thesis into [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s declaration: "For everyone must have somewhere to go" (002-371). The line resonates through every subsequent scene of desperation — from [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s prostitution to [[svidrigailov|Svidrigailov's]] suicide — as the novel's ultimate question: where does the isolated self find a home?

## Characters Involved

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]** — The protagonist, still deliberating the murder. His encounter with [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s confession of degradation provides an unexpected moral mirror on the eve of his crime.
- **[[semyon-marmeladov]]** — A drunken ex-titular councillor whose self-lacerating confession about his family's suffering and his daughter's prostitution constitutes one of the novel's most powerful monologues (002-054–550).
- **[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]]** — Not present in the scene but introduced through her father's devastating account of her sacrifice — the novel's moral center established before she appears.
- **[[katerina-ivanovna]]** — [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s consumptive wife, a proud officer's daughter reduced to scrubbing floors, whose fury at her husband animates the domestic tragedy.
