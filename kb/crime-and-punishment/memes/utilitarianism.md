# Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism — the philosophical doctrine that the moral worth of an action is determined by its contribution to the greatest happiness of the greatest number — pervades the intellectual atmosphere of *Crime and Punishment*. It is the ambient ideology against which [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] develops his [[extraordinary-man-theory|extraordinary man theory]], and its logic of calculation and commensuration echoes through the novel's central moral crisis.

## The Doctrine

The core utilitarian principle holds that actions are right insofar as they promote happiness and wrong insofar as they produce suffering. As formulated by Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832), the moral calculus weighs the pleasure and pain produced by any action, treating all human beings as equal units in the calculation. John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) refined the doctrine, distinguishing higher and lower pleasures and emphasizing that "it is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied."

In the Russian intellectual context of the 1860s, utilitarian ideas fused with nihilism and radical social criticism. Thinkers like Nikolai Chernyshevsky (author of *What Is to Be Done?*, 1863) popularized "rational egoism" — the argument that pursuing one's own rational self-interest naturally aligns with the common good. This is the intellectual milieu in which Raskolnikov's article "On Crime" was written.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Raskolnikov's Calculus

Raskolnikov's justification for murder draws directly on utilitarian reasoning. In a tavern, he overhears a student arguing that [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]]'s death would be a net benefit:

> "Kill her, take her money and with the help of it devote oneself to the service of humanity and the good of all. What do you think, would not one tiny crime be wiped out by thousands of good deeds? For one life thousands would be saved from corruption and decay. One death, and a hundred lives in exchange — it's simple arithmetic!" (001-084)

This is utilitarian logic in its starkest form — the commensuration of one human life against many, reduced to "simple arithmetic." Raskolnikov later echoes this reasoning himself (030-312), though the novel systematically undermines it through the reality of [[conscience-and-suffering|conscience]].

### Luzhin's Utilitarian Egoism

[[pyotr-luzhin|Pyotr Luzhin]] represents a debased, self-serving version of utilitarian logic. He argues that if everyone pursues their own interest, the general prosperity is served — a vulgarization of rational egoism that he uses to justify his exploitation of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]].

### Lebezyatnikov's "Progressive" Utilitarianism

[[lebezyatnikov|Andrei Lebezyatnikov]] spouts the fashionable radical ideas of the day, including utilitarian and Fourierist doctrines, but his understanding is shallow and comical. His attempted defense of Sonia against Luzhin's accusation is one of the few moments where these ideas produce a positive outcome — but the novel treats his progressive posturing with irony.

## Critique in the Novel

Dostoevsky's critique of utilitarianism operates on multiple levels. At the narrative level, Raskolnikov's utilitarian calculus fails catastrophically — the "one death" does not lead to "a hundred lives" saved, but to a second, unintended murder (that of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]) and Raskolnikov's own psychological disintegration. At the philosophical level, the novel questions whether human beings can be legitimately treated as units in a calculation — [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s irreducible personhood stands as a living refutation of commensuration. At the theological level, the novel suggests that suffering is not a debit to be minimized but a path to [[suffering-as-redemption|redemption]].
