[006-001] Later on [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] happened to find out why the huckster and his

[006-002] wife had invited [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]. It was a very ordinary matter and there was

[006-003] nothing exceptional about it. A family who had come to the town and been

[006-004] reduced to poverty were selling their household goods and clothes, all

[006-005] women’s things. As the things would have fetched little in the market,

[006-006] they were looking for a dealer. This was Lizaveta’s business. She

[006-007] undertook such jobs and was frequently employed, as she was very honest

[006-008] and always fixed a fair price and stuck to it. She spoke as a rule

[006-009] little and, as we have said already, she was very submissive and timid.

[006-010] But Raskolnikov had become superstitious of late. The traces of

[006-011] superstition remained in him long after, and were almost ineradicable.

[006-012] And in all this he was always afterwards disposed to see something

[006-013] strange and mysterious, as it were, the presence of some peculiar

[006-014] influences and coincidences. In the previous winter a student he knew

[006-015] called Pokorev, who had left for Harkov, had chanced in conversation to

[006-016] give him the address of [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]], the old pawnbroker, in case he

[006-017] might want to pawn anything. For a long while he did not go to her, for

[006-018] he had lessons and managed to get along somehow. Six weeks ago he had

[006-019] remembered the address; he had two articles that could be pawned: his

[006-020] father’s old silver watch and a little gold ring with three red stones,

[006-021] a present from his sister at parting. He decided to take the ring. When

[006-022] he found the old woman he had felt an insurmountable repulsion for her

[006-023] at the first glance, though he knew nothing special about her. He got

[006-024] two roubles from her and went into a miserable little tavern on his way

[006-025] home. He asked for tea, sat down and sank into deep thought. A strange

[006-026] idea was pecking at his brain like a chicken in the egg, and very, very

[006-027] much absorbed him.

[006-029] did not know and had never seen, and with him a young officer. They had

[006-030] played a game of billiards and began drinking tea. All at once he heard

[006-033] had just come from her and here at once he heard her name. Of course

[006-034] it was a [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]], but he could not shake off a very extraordinary

[006-035] impression, and here someone seemed to be speaking expressly for him;

[006-036] the student began telling his friend various details about Alyona

[006-037] Ivanovna.

[006-038] “She is first-rate,” he said. “You can always get money from her. She is

[006-039] as rich as a Jew, she can give you five thousand roubles at a time and

[006-040] she is not above taking a pledge for a rouble. Lots of our fellows have

[006-085] living for herself, and who will die in a day or two in any case. You

[006-088] companion attentively.

[006-115] “You are talking and speechifying away, but tell me, would you kill the

[006-116] old woman _yourself_?”

[006-117] “Of course not! I was only arguing the justice of it.... It’s nothing to

[006-118] do with me....”

[006-119] “But I think, if you would not do it yourself, there’s no justice about

[006-120] it.... Let us have another game.”

[006-121] Raskolnikov was violently agitated. Of course, it was all ordinary

[006-122] youthful talk and thought, such as he had often heard before in

[006-123] different forms and on different themes. But why had he happened to hear

[006-124] such a discussion and such ideas at the very moment when his own brain

[006-128] coincidence always seemed strange to him. This trivial talk in a tavern

[006-129] had an immense influence on him in his later action; as though there had

[006-130] really been in it something preordained, some guiding hint....
