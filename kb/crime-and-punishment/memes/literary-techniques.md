An index of wiki pages exploring the literary and narrative techniques that distinguish *Crime and Punishment* as a landmark of modern fiction. Dostoevsky's narrative method — the third-person narrator who is nevertheless intimately fused with Raskolnikov's consciousness, the use of dreams as psychological revelation, the doubling of characters, the strategic deployment of coincidence — represents a quantum leap in the development of the psychological novel. This page gathers entries tagged with literary-technique labels, documenting the formal innovations that give the novel its distinctive power.

## About this index

The entries in this index document specific narrative techniques and their effects in the novel. Topics include structural features (the doubling of Raskolnikov and Svidrigailov, the role of coincidences in the plot), stylistic devices (the fusion of narrator and character consciousness, the use of free indirect discourse), and compositional strategies (dream sequences as psychological exposition, the epilogue's shift in narrative register). Each entry traces a technique through its appearances in the text, with PN-cited examples and analysis of its contribution to the novel's meaning. Quality ratings indicate analytical depth; `basic` entries are candidates for future enrichment with additional textual evidence.
