# Marmeladov's Memorial Dinner

Following [[marmeladov|Marmeladov]]'s death, his widow [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] spends nearly all the money [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] gave her on a memorial dinner (*pominki*), determined to honour her late husband with a proper funeral feast despite their desperate poverty. The event, spanning Part 5, Chapters 2 and 3, collapses into social humiliation, false accusation, and public chaos — a quintessential Dostoevskian set-piece in which poverty, pride, and cruelty collide in a cramped tenement room.

## The Dinner

[[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]'s preparations are driven by a desperate need to demonstrate that she and her family are "of good birth" — a refrain she repeats throughout the novel. She invites the better-class tenants of the building, but most decline. Those who do attend are the poorest and least respectable: obscure government clerks, a drunken Pole, a deaf old man who says nothing. The contrast between [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]'s proud talk of her aristocratic upbringing and the squalid reality of the gathering is excruciating:

> …he would be in time for the memorial dinner, and he arrived at the very moment when the party, consisting of the poorest and most insignificant of the lodgers, was leaving the table after the meal. [026-170]

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] himself attends, and the scene unfolds with mounting tension. [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]], already consumptive and feverish, alternates between grandiosity and hysterical outbursts against her landlady, [[amalia-lippevechsel|Amalia Ivanovna]], whom she despises.

## Luzhin's Accusation

The dinner's central crisis arrives with [[pyotr-luzhin|Pyotr Luzhin]], [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]'s former fiancé, who has been humiliated by his break with the [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] family and seeks revenge. He enters the room and, in front of all the guests, accuses [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]] of stealing a hundred-rouble note from him.

Luzhin's performance is calculated: he speaks with studied politeness, presents himself as a benefactor who merely wanted to help the poor family, and produces the "evidence" — the hundred-rouble note that [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] has supposedly hidden. [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], trembling and confused, can only protest her innocence:

> "What! You accuse her of stealing? Sonia? Ah, the low, mean creatures!" Katerina Ivanovna rushed at Luzhin. [028-087]

The accusation's cruelty is compounded by [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s position: she is already a social outcast, a prostitute by necessity, and the easiest target for slander. Luzhin, counting on the prejudices of the assembled company, expects the accusation to stick.

## Lebezyatnikov's Intervention

The scene is rescued — unexpectedly — by Andrey Lebezyatnikov, Luzhin's young room-mate and a follower of the "new ideas" of nihilism and Fourierism. Lebezyatnikov has witnessed Luzhin secretly slipping the hundred-rouble note into [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s pocket earlier, and now, overcoming his own timidity, he denounces Luzhin publicly.

The tables turn. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], who has been watching silently, explains Luzhin's motive: he wanted to discredit [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] in order to drive a wedge between [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] and his mother and sister, hoping to regain control over [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]]. Luzhin retreats in disgrace, and the dinner dissolves into chaos:

> "Do you see? Do you see? What a memorial dinner for you!" cried Katerina Ivanovna. [029-192]

## Aftermath

The memorial dinner marks a point of no return for [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]. The humiliation, combined with her advanced consumption, drives her from the apartment. Within hours, she will be wandering the streets with her children, forcing them to sing and dance for alms — and by the chapter's end, she will be dead, coughing blood on the pavement.

For [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], the episode is a trial by ordeal that deepens her bond with [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], who defends her against Luzhin's accusation. The communal witnessing of her innocence — and Luzhin's exposure — strengthens the spiritual authority she accumulates as the novel's moral centre.

## Narrative Significance

The memorial dinner is Dostoevsky's study in social cruelty. Every element — the absent guests of higher status, the "revolting convention" of the memorial feast observed by people who cannot afford bread, the ease with which a rich man's lie almost destroys a poor woman's reputation — exposes the structures of a society in which dignity is a commodity few can afford. [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]'s desperate quest to perform respectability, and her inevitable failure, is one of the novel's most devastating sequences.
