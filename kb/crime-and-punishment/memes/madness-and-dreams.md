# Madness and Dreams

*Crime and Punishment* is a novel of consciousness under extreme pressure, and much of its action occurs not in the material world but in the fevered interior of Raskolnikov's mind. Delirium, fever, and dreams function simultaneously as realistic symptoms of psychological collapse and as Dostoevsky's narrative devices for accessing truths the conscious mind refuses.

## Definition

In the novel's psychological economy, "delirium" (007-326) and "fever" (006-136) are the body's testimony against the mind's rationalizations. Dostoevsky explicitly theorizes the dream as revelatory: "in a morbid condition of the brain, dreams often have a singular clarity, vividness, and extraordinary semblance of reality" (005-061–005-062). The dream state produces "a picture so artistically consistent" that "the dreamer, were he an artist like Pushkin or Turgenev, could never have invented it in the waking state" (005-065–005-067). Dreams, in this view, are not escapes from reality but intensifications of it.

## Expression in the Novel

Raskolnikov's fever begins before the murder and intensifies after it. His delirium after the crime (008-057) is both physical illness and moral symptom — the body's refusal to accommodate what the mind has done. The novel's most famous dream — the beating of the mare (005-069) — occurs before the murder and functions as both prophecy and moral warning. In the dream, young Raskolnikov witnesses a peasant brutally beating an old mare to death; he runs to kiss the dead horse's muzzle. The dream reveals the horror his conscious mind is suppressing. After the murder, Raskolnikov repeatedly asks, "Did I say anything in delirium?" (010-282), recognizing that delirium may have betrayed what consciousness conceals. Svidrigailov's dreams before his suicide — the mouse, the drowned girl, the five-year-old seductress — provide the novel's other dream sequence, a gallery of guilt and corruption that leads to his final act.

## Key Passages

> "In a morbid condition of the brain, dreams often have a singular clarity, vividness, and extraordinary semblance of reality." (005-061–005-062)

> "Such sick dreams always remain long in the memory and make a powerful impression on the overwrought and deranged nervous system." (005-067)

> "Raskolnikov had a fearful dream. He dreamt he was back in his childhood in the little town of his birth." (005-069)

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His dreams and fevers chart his moral trajectory — from the warning dream of the mare through post-murder delirium to the regenerative dream of the plague in the Epilogue. His psychological arc is narrated through bodily symptoms.

- **Svidrigailov**: His dreams on the night of his suicide offer the novel's other dream vision — a gallery of corruption that reveals the inner life of a character who otherwise maintains perfect external composure. His dreams lead to death; Raskolnikov's, ultimately, to renewal.
