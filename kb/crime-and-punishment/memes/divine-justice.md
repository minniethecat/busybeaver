# Divine Justice

The concept of divine justice in *Crime and Punishment* refers to a moral order that transcends human legal systems — a form of accountability rooted in conscience, suffering, and spiritual redemption rather than the penal code.

## Definition

Divine justice stands in opposition to the earthly justice administered by Porfiry Petrovich and the police. While the legal system seeks to establish facts and assign punishment through evidence and "psychology," divine justice operates through the inner moral law — the inescapable voice of conscience that Sonia insists Raskolnikov must heed. Porfiry himself invokes this higher framework, urging Raskolnikov to "fulfil the demands of justice" not merely as a legal obligation but as a spiritual necessity (033-398).

## Expression in the Novel

The tension between human and divine justice runs throughout the novel. Raskolnikov's initial theory of the "extraordinary man" explicitly rejects divine justice in favor of utilitarian calculation — the pawnbroker's death is justified by the greater good. Yet after the murder, he finds that human justice (Porfiry's investigation) and divine justice (his own deteriorating conscience) converge: "I am guilty! Mine is the sin!" (026-031). Katerina Ivanovna's desperate cry — "Is there no justice on earth?" (029-467) — echoes this theme, answered ultimately by Sonia's insistence that confession before God and the people is the only path to peace.

## Key Passages

> "There is law and justice on earth, there is, I will find justice!" — Katerina Ivanovna (029-469)

> "You must fulfil the demands of justice. I know that you don't believe it, but, by God, life will carry you along" — Porfiry to Raskolnikov (033-398)

> "I am guilty! Mine is the sin!" — Raskolnikov (026-031)

## Character Perspectives

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Represents divine justice in its purest form — she urges Raskolnikov to confess publicly, kiss the earth, and accept suffering as the path to resurrection, framing justice as reconciliation with God rather than punishment by the state
- **Porfiry Petrovich**: Though an agent of human justice, he invokes divine justice as the deeper ground of moral accountability, telling Raskolnikov that "life will carry you along" once he accepts the demands of justice (033-398)
- **Raskolnikov**: Initially dismissive of divine justice, he argues the "justice of it" in rational terms (006-117), but his confession at Sonia's urging marks his movement from legal evasion toward spiritual accountability

## Historical & Philosophical Context

Dostoevsky's vision of divine justice reflects the Russian Orthodox emphasis on *pokayanie* (repentance) as a transformative act rather than mere regret. This stands in contrast to the Western legal-rational tradition represented by the Napoleonic Code and the utilitarian ethics Dostoevsky critiques throughout the novel. The Siberian prison camp settings of the Epilogue reinforce the idea that earthly punishment alone cannot produce moral transformation.
