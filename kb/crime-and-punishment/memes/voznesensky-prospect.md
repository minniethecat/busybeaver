## Overview

Voznesensky Prospect (Вознесенский проспект, Ascension Prospect) is one of the three major radial avenues that converge on the Admiralty in [[st-petersburg]], alongside Nevsky Prospect and Gorokhovaya Street. In *Crime and Punishment*, it appears as "V---- Prospect," a thoroughfare that Raskolnikov traverses in his agitated walks between the [[hay-market]], the [[neva-river]], and the police station.

## In the Novel

### Route to the Police Station

Voznesensky Prospect is the route Raskolnikov takes toward the police station, his anxiety mounting with every step:

> He walked along V---- Prospect towards the Neva. [009-045]

The prospect serves as a spatial corridor of mounting dread. As he approaches the square, he sees the entrance to the police station, and the prospect becomes the approach to his potential unmasking.

### The Haymarket Connection

Voznesensky Prospect runs directly from the Haymarket area toward the Neva, making it a natural axis for Raskolnikov's walking routes. The junction where the prospect meets the square is where the drama of the confession repeatedly almost happens:

> Coming out of V---- Prospect towards the square, he saw on the left the entrance to the yard. [009-051]

### Sonia's Neighbourhood

Later in the novel, Voznesensky Prospect is referenced as the vicinity of [[sonia-marmeladov]]'s dwelling:

> They are all there now, in the yard off the V---- Prospect. [030-262]

This connects the prospect to the network of spaces around Sonia — the tenement courtyards, the Kapernaumov apartment, and the proximity to the canal.

### Epilogue Reference

The prospect appears in the Epilogue as a remembered space of the crime:

> The purse and the trinkets under a stone in a yard off Voznesensky Prospect. [039-363]

The prospect thus frames the crime's geography from the initial approach to the police station through to the final accounting.

## Significance

Voznesensky Prospect is one of the novel's "projected spaces" — a real Petersburg street charged with psychological pressure. Its function is less architectural than moral: it is the road to the police station, the road of reckoning. Each time Raskolnikov walks along it, he is moving toward or fleeing from confession. As the third of the great radial prospects after Nevsky, its inclusion in the novel anchors the fiction in the real cartography of the city.
