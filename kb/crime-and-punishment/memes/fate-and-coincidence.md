# Fate and Coincidence

Dostoevsky structures *Crime and Punishment* around a series of extraordinary coincidences that Raskolnikov himself cannot dismiss as mere chance. Each coincidence — the overheard conversation in the Hay Market, the meeting with Marmeladov, the timing of Lizaveta's absence — functions simultaneously as plot mechanism and as an insistent suggestion that events are governed by something beyond rational causation [006-014].

## Definition

The novel presents coincidence not as lazy plotting but as a philosophical problem. Raskolnikov, the rationalist, is confronted by a chain of events that seem too perfectly aligned to be accidental — yet his ideology permits no explanation beyond material causation. The tension between viewing these coincidences as "mere chance" and sensing in them the hand of fate or providence mirrors the novel's larger conflict between rationalism and faith. Dostoevsky leaves the question deliberately unresolved: the coincidences are objectively present in the narrative, but their meaning is for the reader — and Raskolnikov — to interpret [006-128].

## Expression in the Novel

The novel's plot is propelled by a cascade of coincidences, each one closing off Raskolnikov's alternatives and narrowing his path toward the crime. He happens to overhear a student in a tavern discussing the pawnbroker's death — the exact idea he has been brooding on himself [006-014]. He happens to learn, while lying ill and half-conscious, that Lizaveta will be absent from the flat at a specific hour on a specific day. He happens to arrive at the pawnbroker's door at precisely the moment when no one else is on the staircase. Each coincidence increases his conviction that the murder is "fated" — that the universe itself is conspiring to make it possible.

After the crime, coincidence becomes the instrument of his unmasking. He happens to overhear discussions of the murder in a tavern. He happens to encounter Sonya's family. He happens to enter the Crystal Palace at the moment his name is mentioned. The same force that seemed to authorize the crime now works to expose it. Marmeladov's drunken fall under a carriage — the coincidence that connects Raskolnikov to Sonia's family — becomes the turning point of his moral trajectory: "Such is my fate!" Marmeladov cries, and the phrase echoes across the novel [002-154].

## Key Passages

> "Strange influences and coincidences" — the narrator's explicit acknowledgment that the plot is driven by forces beyond character intention, inviting the reader to consider whether these are accidents or something more [006-014].

> "This coincidence always seemed strange to him. This man, who seemed to have sprung out of the earth, had said at once all that was in his mind" — Raskolnikov reflecting on a chance meeting, unable to dismiss its significance [006-128].

> "Such is my fate!" — Marmeladov's refrain, spoken in drunken self-pity but resonant with the novel's larger theme: are we the agents of our actions or the subjects of fate? [002-154]

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Torn between rational dismissal of coincidence and a creeping sense that "fate" is directing him. His article on crime argues that great men override moral law through will, yet he experiences himself as acted upon rather than acting — a contradiction he never fully resolves.

- **Semyon Marmeladov**: The novel's embodiment of fatalism. His repeated cry of "Such is my fate!" rationalizes his degradation as inevitable, a self-serving determinism that mirrors Raskolnikov's own evasions in a degraded key [002-150].

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Experiences coincidence as providence. Her encounter with Raskolnikov — mediated through her father's death — becomes, in her understanding, a divine appointment, not an accident. Her faith transforms the novel's coincidences from plot mechanics into signs of grace.
