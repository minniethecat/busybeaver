# Conscience and Suffering

In *Crime and Punishment*, conscience functions not as a quiet moral guide but as an active, tormenting force — an "inner right to decide in his own conscience to overstep... certain obstacles" (019-383) that, once violated, turns against the transgressor. The novel's title encodes this mechanism: punishment begins long before any legal sentence, operating through the conscience as psychological suffering.

## Definition

Conscience in Dostoevsky's novel is the internal moral faculty that judges action and inflicts suffering for transgression. Porfiry articulates the mechanism with precision: "If he has a conscience he will suffer for his mistake. That will be his punishment — as well as the prison" (019-552). The sanction of "bloodshed *by conscience*" is, Porfiry argues, "more terrible than the official sanction" (019-520) — it is punishment administered by the self upon the self.

## Expression in the Novel

The student in the tavern articulates the utilitarian challenge to conscience: killing the pawnbroker would not cause a "faintest conscience-prick" because her death would fund a hundred good deeds (006-079). Raskolnikov tests this proposition and discovers its falsehood. From the moment of the murder, conscience manifests as physical illness, fever, fainting spells, and paranoid behavior — the body betraying what the mind attempts to rationalize (010-256). His eventual confession to Sonia and then to the authorities represents conscience's final victory over ideological justification. In the Epilogue, conscience transforms from tormentor to path of renewal: through Sonia's love, suffering becomes redemptive rather than merely punitive.

## Key Passages

> "The 'extraordinary' man has the right... that is not an official right, but an inner right to decide in his own conscience to overstep... certain obstacles, and only in case it is essential for the practical fulfilment of his idea." (019-382–019-383)

> "If he has a conscience he will suffer for his mistake. That will be his punishment — as well as the prison." (019-552)

> "The sanction of bloodshed *by conscience* is to my mind... more terrible than the official sanction." (019-520)

## Character Perspectives

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Attempts to suppress conscience through rational theory but finds it inescapable. His physical collapse after the murder — fever, delirium, fainting — demonstrates conscience operating beneath conscious control. His final confession to Sonya (039-008) is conscience's ultimate triumph.

- **Sonia Marmeladov**: Embodies conscience purified through faith. She has transgressed (prostitution) but retains moral clarity. She tells Raskolnikov to "accept suffering and achieve atonement through it," offering the Christian model of conscience as path to redemption rather than mere punishment.

- **Porfiry Petrovich**: The theorist of conscience. His interrogation strategy is built on the premise that conscience will force confession — the law need only wait. He predicts Raskolnikov's trajectory with clinical accuracy (025-468).
