# Arkady Svidrigailov

Arkady Ivanovich Svidrigailov is the novel's most disturbing presence — a wealthy landowner who has passed beyond good and evil into moral exhaustion. He is Raskolnikov's dark double: the extraordinary man theory lived out to its horrifying conclusion.

## Overview

Svidrigailov arrives in St. Petersburg in Part 4 with uncanny knowledge — he has overheard Raskolnikov's confession to Sonia. He uses this knowledge not to blackmail but to demonstrate the emptiness of Raskolnikov's pretensions. He tells Raskolnikov: "There is something about you like me" (025-150).

## Background

A former cavalry officer imprisoned for debt, Svidrigailov was bought out by Marfa Petrovna, whom he married and whose death under suspicious circumstances freed him. He employed Dounia as governess and became obsessed with her. His past includes rumored abuse and deaths, yet he also performs genuine charity: providing for the Marmeladov orphans and arranging the children's future.

## Role in the Novel

Svidrigailov functions as Raskolnikov's moral mirror. Both have transgressed; both are isolated. But where Raskolnikov is tormented by conscience, Svidrigailov is merely bored. His pursuit of Dounia culminates when she shoots at him twice — grazing his temple — before he releases her, his one act of genuine renunciation. He spends his final night wandering St. Petersburg in a fog of dreams before shooting himself at dawn. His vision of eternity as "a little room, something like a bathhouse, with spiders in every corner" (025-380) is the novel's darkest image of the afterlife.

## Key Relationships

- **Dounia Raskolnikov**: His obsession. Her absolute rejection prompts his sole moment of moral agency — releasing her.
- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: His philosophical double. Svidrigailov sees through Raskolnikov's rationalizations.
- **Marfa Petrovna**: His late wife, whose ghost appears to him.

## Psychological Profile

Svidrigailov has exhausted all moral categories. His dreams on his final night — the mouse, the drowned girl, the five-year-old seductress — are the novel's most compressed gallery of guilt. He is too exhausted even for evil.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Svidrigailov represents the endpoint of Raskolnikov's theory: the extraordinary man who overstepped all barriers and found nothing. His suicide is the theory's final refutation.
