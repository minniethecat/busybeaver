# Blood Symbolism

Blood in *Crime and Punishment* functions as the central symbolic motif linking physical violence to psychological guilt, transforming from literal gore at the murder scene into a metaphysical stain on Raskolnikov's conscience. The motif traces a complete arc through the novel: from prophetic dreams, through the horror of enactment, to obsessive concealment, and finally to the spiritual reckoning that confession demands.

## Definition

Blood symbolism in Dostoevsky's novel operates on two intertwined levels: the literal blood of the murder victims and the figurative blood that haunts Raskolnikov's psyche. The motif represents the irreversible nature of transgression — once blood is spilled, it cannot be unseen or undone (007-070). Beyond this, blood signifies the embodied nature of moral violation: the crime is not an abstract philosophical experiment but a physical act that leaves tangible traces, which in turn become the inescapable evidence of guilt. Raskolnikov's own article had spoken of "wading through blood" as a theoretical possibility (019-428), but the reality of sticky, warm, gushing blood proves far more overwhelming than any intellectual formulation could anticipate.

## Expression in the Novel

### The Dream of the Mare

Blood first appears in Raskolnikov's dream of the beaten mare, where the peasant Mikolka, "with bloodshot eyes," beats the animal to death with a crowbar, foreshadowing the murder to come (005-224). The dream's violence is saturated with blood imagery — the mare's bleeding flanks, the bloodthirsty laughter of the crowd — establishing the motif before any actual crime occurs. This dream functions as a prophetic warning that Raskolnikov's conscious mind cannot hear, though his body responds with fever and dread.

### The Murder Scene

The murder itself is rendered in terms of blood. In his feverish anticipation, Raskolnikov imagines himself treading "in the sticky warm blood," breaking the lock with his axe (005-250). The reality exceeds even this grim foresight: "The blood gushed as from an overturned glass" (007-070) when he strikes Alyona Ivanovna, and he finds his hands "sticky with blood" (007-184). His immediate instinct is concealment — he notes that blood on the red brocade "will be less noticeable" (007-129) — yet the very act of cleaning becomes a futile ritual, as he obsessively checks for "spots of blood" on his clothing (007-189). The physical fact of blood transforms Raskolnikov from philosopher to criminal in an instant; theory dissolves in the presence of the real.

### The Stain of Conscience

After the murder, blood becomes a psychological as much as a physical reality. Raskolnikov imagines bloodstains where none exist; he fears every glance will reveal his secret. When the tradesman accuses him — "You have blood on you!" — the statement, though metaphorical, strikes with the force of physical revelation (022-353). Dostoevsky uses blood to literalize the Christian concept of the stain of sin: it cannot be washed away by water alone, but only by confession and suffering. Raskolnikov's futile scrubbing of his clothes mirrors his futile attempts to scrub his conscience through rational argument.
