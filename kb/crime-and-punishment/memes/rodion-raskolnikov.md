# Rodion Raskolnikov

Rodion Romanovich Raskolnikov is the protagonist of *Crime and Punishment* — a former law student "crushed by poverty" (001-016) in [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]], whose intellectual pride and [[utilitarian-rationalism|utilitarian rationalism]] drive him to murder [[murder-of-pawnbroker|Alyona Ivanovna, a pawnbroker]], as a test of his [[extraordinary-man-theory|theory that certain extraordinary individuals have the right to transgress moral law]] for the greater good.

## Overview

Raskolnikov is Dostoevsky's most fully realized portrait of the divided modern consciousness — brilliant and self-destructive, compassionate and cruel, capable of both the murder of an innocent woman and the spontaneous charity of giving his last money to the [[marmeladov|Marmeladovs]]. He is "exceptionally handsome, above the average in height, slim, well-built, with beautiful dark eyes and dark brown hair" (001-005), but his physical beauty contrasts with his spiritual condition. His name derives from the Russian *raskol*, meaning "schism" or "split" — he is a man at war with himself.

## Background

A former law student at the University of St. Petersburg, Raskolnikov was forced to abandon his studies due to poverty. He lives in a tiny [[raskolnikovs-garret|"garret" under the roof of a five-story building]] in S. Place (001-002), isolated from his family and former classmates. His mother [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] and sister [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] live in the provinces, making sacrifices to support him. Before the novel's action, he published an article "On Crime" in the *Periodical Review* (019-330–019-331) arguing that extraordinary individuals have the right to transgress moral law — an article that [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]], the examining magistrate, has read and recognized.

## Role in the Novel

Raskolnikov's arc spans the novel's six parts and Epilogue. He murders Alyona Ivanovna and her half-sister Lizaveta with an axe (001-100) in what he believes will be a decisive, rational act. Instead, the murder triggers a psychological collapse: fever, delirium, paranoid behavior, and the progressive breakdown of his ability to function in the world. Porfiry Petrovich investigates without direct evidence, using psychological pressure — the knowledge that "if he has a conscience he will suffer for his mistake" (019-552). Raskolnikov confesses first to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]], who tells him to "accept suffering and achieve atonement through it" (030-423), then publicly at the [[hay-market|Hay Market]]. In the Epilogue, exiled to Siberia, he experiences the first stirrings of spiritual renewal through Sonia's love: "the dawn of a new future, of a full resurrection into a new life" (039-828).

## Key Relationships

- **[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]]**: The prostitute-turned-savior who receives Raskolnikov's confession and follows him to Siberia. Their relationship is the novel's moral center — faith meeting nihilism, love meeting isolation.
- **[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]]**: The examining magistrate who psychologically pursues Raskolnikov without evidence. Their three interviews (Parts 3, 4, and 6) are the novel's intellectual and psychological core.
- **[[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia Raskolnikov]]**: His sister, whose contemplated marriage to [[pyotr-luzhin|Luzhin]] mirrors Sonia's sacrifice and whose strength (shooting [[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]]) contrasts with his weakness.
- **[[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]]**: His mother, whose love is blind and unconditional — she dies without knowing her son is a murderer.
- **[[dmitri-razumihin|Dmitri Razumihin]]**: His loyal friend, whose practical decency and warmth represent the life Raskolnikov might have lived.

## Psychological Profile

Raskolnikov's psychology is the novel's subject. He oscillates between grandiosity ("I wanted to become a Napoleon" — 030-312) and self-loathing ("I am an æsthetic louse and nothing more" — 020-243). His "haughty pride and reserve" (004-387) isolate him from those who would help him. Dostoevsky renders his consciousness through free indirect discourse, fever dreams, and interior monologue, creating what Mikhail Bakhtin would later call a "polyphonic" character — one whose voice exists in unresolved dialogue with the other characters and with the reader.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Raskolnikov's name (*raskol* = schism) encodes his thematic function: he is the divided self, torn between intellect and conscience, theory and reality, isolation and community. He embodies the novel's central argument: that rationalism unmoored from [[christian-faith|faith]] and [[conscience-and-suffering|conscience]] leads not to liberation but to self-destruction. His trajectory — crime, suffering, confession, rebirth — maps the Orthodox Christian understanding of sin and redemption onto the modern psychological novel.
