# Luise Ivanovna

Luise Ivanovna is a "lady of easy virtue" who appears at the police station during Raskolnikov's summons the day after the murder (Part 2, Chapter 1). Her elaborate curtsies and exaggerated deference to the police lieutenant provide one of the novel's few moments of pure comedy — albeit comedy shadowed by the desperation of a woman who must flatter state power to survive. She is observed by [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] while he waits to give his statement, her theatrical submissiveness a distorted mirror of his own performance of normality.

## Overview

Luise Ivanovna is brought into the police station for some unspecified infraction and immediately begins curtsying "with hurried deference" (008-420) to Lieutenant Ilya Petrovich, who responds with patronizing gallantry: "Luise Ivanovna, you can sit down" (008-265). The scene is simultaneously farcical and grim — Luise's performance of deference is a survival strategy, and the lieutenant's mock-chivalry masks the raw power he holds over her. Raskolnikov watches this exchange with the heightened perception of a man who has just committed murder and is himself performing innocence for the same audience.

## Role in the Novel

Luise appears only in this single scene, but it is one of the novel's most compressed studies of power and performance. Her curtsies mirror Raskolnikov's own strategic self-presentation at the police station — both are performing submission to authority while hiding their true selves. The lieutenant's treatment of her also foreshadows the police's differential treatment of the powerful and the powerless, a theme that reaches its full development in Raskolnikov's interviews with [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]].

## Key Quotes

> "Luise Ivanovna, you can sit down." — Police lieutenant Ilya Petrovich (008-265)
