## Overview

Yellow is the dominant color in [[crime-and-punishment|Crime and Punishment]], appearing in descriptions of faces, rooms, wallpaper, furniture, and even liquids. Dostoevsky uses yellow to create an atmosphere of sickness, decay, and moral unease. Unlike the red of blood or the white of purity, yellow occupies an intermediate symbolic space — associated with both physical illness and spiritual corruption.

## Yellow in the Physical Environment

The pawnbroker's flat is decorated with yellow wallpaper and furnished with "yellow wood" [001-158]. Raskolnikov's own garret features "yellow paper peeling off the walls" [003-005]. The police station where he faints is a "little yellow room" [003-437]. These yellow interiors create a claustrophobic, jaundiced visual world that mirrors Raskolnikov's feverish mental state.

Dostoevsky repeatedly draws attention to yellow as the color of decay:
> "…all very old and of yellow wood, consisted of a sofa with…" [001-158]

> "yellow paper peeling off the walls" [003-005]

## Yellow Faces and Bodies

Human complexion in the novel is frequently described as yellow, suggesting illness and spiritual malaise. Raskolnikov's face has "a yellow, even greenish, tinge, with sunken eyes" [002-033]. Porfiry Petrovich is described with a "yellowish glass filled with yellow water" on his desk [008-573], and Raskolnikov's "wasted, pale and yellow face" [013-022] externalizes his inner turmoil.

The yellow face is not merely a symptom of physical illness — it is a marker of the moral sickness that pervades the novel's world. Characters who are spiritually compromised or physically degraded take on this jaundiced hue.

## Sonia's Yellow Ticket

The most socially charged use of yellow is Sonia's "yellow ticket" (or "yellow passport") — the official license permitting her to work as a prostitute [002-124][002-125][002-284]. Here yellow becomes the color of social shame, institutionalized exploitation, and the moral compromises that poverty forces upon the vulnerable. The yellow ticket marks Sonia as an outcast even as it is the very thing that sustains her family.

## Yellow in the Wider Symbolic Scheme

Yellow functions as Dostoevsky's visual shorthand for a world out of joint. It is neither the red of passionate violence nor the black of nihilistic despair, but something more insidious — the color of slow corruption, of things turning bad from within. The yellow wallpaper, yellow faces, and yellow tickets form a chromatic chain linking environment, body, and social condition in a single oppressive atmosphere.
