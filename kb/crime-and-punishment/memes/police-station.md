# Police Station

The police station on Sadovaya Street is the novel's theater of law — where Raskolnikov is first summoned for a trivial debt matter (and nearly faints when the murder is mentioned), where Porfiry conducts his three psychological interrogations, and where the formal confession ultimately occurs.

## The Summons

Raskolnikov's first visit to the station (Part 2, Chapter 1) is a masterpiece of psychological tension: summoned for an unpaid debt, he overhears the police discussing the murder and nearly collapses. The station's bureaucratic mundanity — clerks, papers, trivial complaints — contrasts vertiginously with the murder that Raskolnikov carries in his consciousness.

## Porfiry's Office

Porfiry's room within the station is the setting for the novel's three great dialogues. It is described as a comfortable, almost domestic space — Porfiry's laughter, his cigarettes, his apparent casualness — that becomes, under his management, more threatening than any interrogation cell.

## Key Passages
