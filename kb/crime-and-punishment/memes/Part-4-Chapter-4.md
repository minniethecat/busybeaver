[024-001] [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] went straight to the house on [[catherine-canal|the canal]] bank where [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]

[024-002] lived. It was an old green house of three storeys. He found the

[024-003] porter and obtained from him vague directions as to the whereabouts of

[024-004] Kapernaumov, the tailor. Having found in the corner of the courtyard

[024-005] the entrance to the dark and narrow staircase, he mounted to the second

[024-006] floor and came out into a gallery that ran round the whole second storey

[024-007] over the yard. While he was wandering in the darkness, uncertain where

[024-008] to turn for Kapernaumov’s door, a door opened three paces from him; he

[024-009] mechanically took hold of it.

[024-010] “Who is there?” a woman’s voice asked uneasily.

[024-011] “It’s I... come to see you,” answered Raskolnikov, and he walked into

[024-012] the tiny entry.

[024-013] On a broken chair stood a candle in a battered copper candlestick.

[024-014] “It’s you! Good heavens!” cried Sonia weakly, and she stood rooted to

[024-015] the spot.

[024-016] “Which is your room? This way?” and Raskolnikov, trying not to look at

[024-017] her, hastened in.

[024-018] A minute later Sonia, too, came in with the candle, set down the

[024-019] candlestick and, completely disconcerted, stood before him inexpressibly

[024-020] agitated and apparently frightened by his unexpected visit. The colour

[024-021] rushed suddenly to her pale face and tears came into her eyes... She

[024-022] felt sick and ashamed and happy, too.... Raskolnikov turned away quickly

[024-023] and sat on a chair by the table. He scanned the room in a rapid glance.

[024-024] It was a large but exceedingly low-pitched room, the only one let by the

[024-025] Kapernaumovs, to whose rooms a closed door led in the wall on the left.

[024-026] In the opposite side on the right hand wall was another door, always

[024-027] kept locked. That led to the next flat, which formed a separate lodging.

[024-028] Sonia’s room looked like a barn; it was a very irregular quadrangle and

[024-029] this gave it a grotesque appearance. A wall with three windows looking

[024-030] out on to the canal ran aslant so that one corner formed a very acute

[024-031] angle, and it was difficult to see in it without very strong light.

[024-032] The other corner was disproportionately obtuse. There was scarcely any

[024-033] furniture in the big room: in the corner on the right was a bedstead,

[024-034] beside it, nearest the door, a chair. A plain, deal table covered by a

[024-035] blue cloth stood against the same wall, close to the door into the other

[024-036] flat. Two rush-bottom chairs stood by the table. On the opposite

[024-037] wall near the acute angle stood a small plain wooden chest of drawers

[024-038] looking, as it were, lost in a desert. That was all there was in the

[024-039] room. The yellow, scratched and shabby wall-paper was black in the

[024-040] corners. It must have been damp and full of fumes in the winter. There

[024-041] was every sign of poverty; even the bedstead had no curtain.

[024-042] Sonia looked in silence at her visitor, who was so attentively and

[024-043] unceremoniously scrutinising her room, and even began at last to tremble

[024-044] with terror, as though she was standing before her judge and the arbiter

[024-045] of her destinies.

[024-046] “I am late.... It’s eleven, isn’t it?” he asked, still not lifting his

[024-047] eyes.

[024-048] “Yes,” muttered Sonia, “oh yes, it is,” she added, hastily, as though in

[024-049] that lay her means of escape. “My landlady’s clock has just struck... I

[024-050] heard it myself....”

[024-051] “I’ve come to you for the last time,” Raskolnikov went on gloomily,

[024-052] although this was the first time. “I may perhaps not see you again...”

[024-053] “Are you... going away?”

[024-054] “I don’t know... to-morrow....”

[024-055] “Then you are not coming to [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]] to-morrow?” Sonia’s voice

[024-056] shook.

[024-057] “I don’t know. I shall know to-morrow morning.... Never mind that: I’ve

[024-058] come to say one word....”

[024-059] He raised his brooding eyes to her and suddenly noticed that he was

[024-060] sitting down while she was all the while standing before him.

[024-061] “Why are you standing? Sit down,” he said in a changed voice, gentle and

[024-062] friendly.

[024-063] She sat down. He looked kindly and almost compassionately at her.

[024-064] “How thin you are! What a hand! Quite transparent, like a dead hand.”

[024-065] He took her hand. Sonia smiled faintly.

[024-066] “I have always been like that,” she said.

[024-067] “Even when you lived at home?”

[024-068] “Yes.”

[024-069] “Of course, you were,” he added abruptly and the expression of his face

[024-070] and the sound of his voice changed again suddenly.

[024-071] He looked round him once more.

[024-072] “You rent this room from the Kapernaumovs?”

[024-073] “Yes....”

[024-074] “They live there, through that door?”

[024-075] “Yes.... They have another room like this.”

[024-076] “All in one room?”

[024-077] “Yes.”

[024-078] “I should be afraid in your room at night,” he observed gloomily.

[024-079] “They are very good people, very kind,” answered Sonia, who still seemed

[024-080] bewildered, “and all the furniture, everything... everything is theirs.

[024-081] And they are very kind and the children, too, often come to see me.”

[024-082] “They all stammer, don’t they?”

[024-083] “Yes.... He stammers and he’s lame. And his wife, too.... It’s not

[024-084] exactly that she stammers, but she can’t speak plainly. She is a very

[024-085] kind woman. And he used to be a house serf. And there are seven

[024-086] children... and it’s only the eldest one that stammers and the others

[024-087] are simply ill... but they don’t stammer.... But where did you hear

[024-088] about them?” she added with some surprise.

[024-089] “Your father told me, then. He told me all about you.... And how you

[024-090] went out at six o’clock and came back at nine and how Katerina Ivanovna

[024-091] knelt down by your bed.”

[024-092] Sonia was confused.

[024-093] “I fancied I saw him to-day,” she whispered hesitatingly.

[024-094] “Whom?”

[024-095] “Father. I was walking in the street, out there at the corner, about ten

[024-096] o’clock and he seemed to be walking in front. It looked just like him. I

[024-097] wanted to go to Katerina Ivanovna....”

[024-098] “You were walking in the streets?”

[024-099] “Yes,” Sonia whispered abruptly, again overcome with confusion and

[024-100] looking down.

[024-101] “Katerina Ivanovna used to beat you, I dare say?”

[024-102] “Oh no, what are you saying? No!” Sonia looked at him almost with

[024-103] dismay.

[024-104] “You love her, then?”

[024-105] “Love her? Of course!” said Sonia with plaintive emphasis, and she

[024-106] clasped her hands in distress. “Ah, you don’t.... If you only knew!

[024-107] You see, she is quite like a child.... Her mind is quite unhinged, you

[024-108] see... from sorrow. And how clever she used to be... how generous... how

[024-109] kind! Ah, you don’t understand, you don’t understand!”

[024-110] Sonia said this as though in despair, wringing her hands in excitement

[024-111] and distress. Her pale cheeks flushed, there was a look of anguish in

[024-112] her eyes. It was clear that she was stirred to the very depths, that

[024-113] she was longing to speak, to champion, to express something. A sort

[024-114] of _insatiable_ [[pity-and-compassion|compassion]], if one may so express it, was reflected in

[024-115] every feature of her face.

[024-116] “Beat me! how can you? Good heavens, beat me! And if she did beat me,

[024-117] what then? What of it? You know nothing, nothing about it.... She is so

[024-118] unhappy... ah, how unhappy! And ill.... She is seeking righteousness,

[024-119] she is pure. She has such faith that there must be righteousness

[024-120] everywhere and she expects it.... And if you were to torture her, she

[024-121] wouldn’t do wrong. She doesn’t see that it’s impossible for people to

[024-122] be righteous and she is angry at it. Like a child, like a child. She is

[024-123] good!”

[024-124] “And what will happen to you?”

[024-125] Sonia looked at him inquiringly.

[024-126] “They are left on your hands, you see. They were all on your hands

[024-127] before, though.... And your father came to you to beg for drink. Well,

[024-128] how will it be now?”

[024-129] “I don’t know,” Sonia articulated mournfully.

[024-130] “Will they stay there?”

[024-131] “I don’t know.... They are in debt for the lodging, but [[praskovya-pavlovna|the landlady]],

[024-132] I hear, said to-day that she wanted to get rid of them, and Katerina

[024-133] Ivanovna says that she won’t stay another minute.”

[024-134] “How is it she is so bold? She relies upon you?”

[024-135] “Oh, no, don’t talk like that.... We are one, we live like one.” Sonia

[024-136] was agitated again and even angry, as though a canary or some other

[024-137] little bird were to be angry. “And what could she do? What, what could

[024-138] she do?” she persisted, getting hot and excited. “And how she cried

[024-139] to-day! Her mind is unhinged, haven’t you noticed it? At one minute she

[024-140] is worrying like a child that everything should be right to-morrow, the

[024-141] lunch and all that.... Then she is wringing her hands, spitting blood,

[024-142] weeping, and all at once she will begin knocking her head against the

[024-143] wall, in despair. Then she will be comforted again. She builds all her

[024-144] hopes on you; she says that you will help her now and that she will

[024-145] borrow a little money somewhere and go to her native town with me and

[024-146] set up a boarding school for the daughters of gentlemen and take me to

[024-147] superintend it, and we will begin a new splendid life. And she kisses

[024-148] and hugs me, comforts me, and you know she has such faith, such faith in

[024-149] her fancies! One can’t contradict her. And all the day long she has been

[024-150] washing, cleaning, mending. She dragged the wash tub into the room with

[024-151] her feeble hands and sank on the bed, gasping for breath. We went this

[024-152] morning to the shops to buy shoes for Polenka and Lida for theirs are

[024-153] quite worn out. Only the money we’d reckoned wasn’t enough, not nearly

[024-154] enough. And she picked out such dear little boots, for she has taste,

[024-155] you don’t know. And there in the shop she burst out crying before the

[024-156] shopmen because she hadn’t enough.... Ah, it was sad to see her....”

[024-157] “Well, after that I can understand your living like this,” Raskolnikov

[024-158] said with a bitter smile.

[024-159] “And aren’t you sorry for them? Aren’t you sorry?” Sonia flew at him

[024-160] again. “Why, I know, you gave your last penny yourself, though you’d

[024-161] seen nothing of it, and if you’d seen everything, oh dear! And how

[024-162] often, how often I’ve brought her to tears! Only last week! Yes, I! Only

[024-163] a week before his death. I was cruel! And how often I’ve done it! Ah,

[024-164] I’ve been wretched at the thought of it all day!”

[024-165] Sonia wrung her hands as she spoke at the pain of remembering it.

[024-166] “You were cruel?”

[024-167] “Yes, I--I. I went to see them,” she went on, weeping, “and father said,

[024-168] ‘read me something, Sonia, my head aches, read to me, here’s a book.’ He

[024-169] had a book he had got from Andrey Semyonovitch Lebeziatnikov, he lives

[024-170] there, he always used to get hold of such funny books. And I said, ‘I

[024-171] can’t stay,’ as I didn’t want to read, and I’d gone in chiefly to show

[024-172] Katerina Ivanovna some collars. [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]], the pedlar, sold me some

[024-173] collars and cuffs cheap, pretty, new, embroidered ones. Katerina

[024-174] Ivanovna liked them very much; she put them on and looked at herself

[024-175] in the glass and was delighted with them. ‘Make me a present of them,

[024-176] Sonia,’ she said, ‘please do.’ ‘_Please do_,’ she said, she wanted them

[024-177] so much. And when could she wear them? They just reminded her of her old

[024-178] happy days. She looked at herself in the glass, admired herself, and she

[024-179] has no clothes at all, no things of her own, hasn’t had all these years!

[024-180] And she never asks anyone for anything; she is proud, she’d sooner give

[024-181] away everything. And these she asked for, she liked them so much. And I

[024-182] was sorry to give them. ‘What use are they to you, Katerina Ivanovna?’ I

[024-183] said. I spoke like that to her, I ought not to have said that! She gave

[024-184] me such a look. And she was so grieved, so grieved at my refusing her.

[024-185] And it was so sad to see.... And she was not grieved for the collars,

[024-186] but for my refusing, I saw that. Ah, if only I could bring it all back,

[024-187] change it, take back those words! Ah, if I... but it’s nothing to you!”

[024-188] “Did you know Lizaveta, the pedlar?”

[024-189] “Yes.... Did you know her?” Sonia asked with some surprise.

[024-190] “Katerina Ivanovna is in consumption, rapid consumption; she will soon

[024-191] die,” said Raskolnikov after a pause, without answering her question.

[024-192] “Oh, no, no, no!”

[024-193] And Sonia unconsciously clutched both his hands, as though imploring

[024-194] that she should not.

[024-195] “But it will be better if she does die.”

[024-196] “No, not better, not at all better!” Sonia unconsciously repeated in

[024-197] dismay.

[024-198] “And the children? What can you do except take them to live with you?”

[024-199] “Oh, I don’t know,” cried Sonia, almost in despair, and she put her

[024-200] hands to her head.

[024-201] It was evident that that idea had very often occurred to her before and

[024-202] he had only roused it again.

[024-203] “And, what, if even now, while Katerina Ivanovna is alive, you get ill

[024-204] and are taken to the hospital, what will happen then?” he persisted

[024-205] pitilessly.

[024-206] “How can you? That cannot be!”

[024-207] And Sonia’s face worked with awful terror.

[024-208] “Cannot be?” Raskolnikov went on with a harsh smile. “You are not

[024-209] insured against it, are you? What will happen to them then? They will

[024-210] be in the street, all of them, she will cough and beg and knock her head

[024-211] against some wall, as she did to-day, and the children will cry....

[024-212] Then she will fall down, be taken to the police station and to the

[024-213] hospital, she will die, and the children...”

[024-214] “Oh, no.... God will not let it be!” broke at last from Sonia’s

[024-215] overburdened bosom.

[024-216] She listened, looking imploringly at him, clasping her hands in dumb

[024-217] entreaty, as though it all depended upon him.

[024-218] Raskolnikov got up and began to walk about the room. A minute passed.

[024-219] Sonia was standing with her hands and her head hanging in terrible

[024-220] dejection.

[024-221] “And can’t you save? Put by for a rainy day?” he asked, stopping

[024-222] suddenly before her.

[024-223] “No,” whispered Sonia.

[024-224] “Of course not. Have you tried?” he added almost ironically.

[024-225] “Yes.”

[024-226] “And it didn’t come off! Of course not! No need to ask.”

[024-227] And again he paced the room. Another minute passed.

[024-228] “You don’t get money every day?”

[024-229] Sonia was more confused than ever and colour rushed into her face again.

[024-230] “No,” she whispered with a painful effort.

[024-231] “It will be the same with Polenka, no doubt,” he said suddenly.

[024-232] “No, no! It can’t be, no!” Sonia cried aloud in desperation, as though

[024-233] she had been stabbed. “God would not allow anything so awful!”

[024-234] “He lets others come to it.”

[024-235] “No, no! God will protect her, God!” she repeated beside herself.

[024-236] “But, perhaps, there is no God at all,” Raskolnikov answered with a sort

[024-237] of malignance, laughed and looked at her.

[024-238] Sonia’s face suddenly changed; a tremor passed over it. She looked at

[024-239] him with unutterable reproach, tried to say something, but could not

[024-240] speak and broke into bitter, bitter sobs, hiding her face in her hands.

[024-241] “You say Katerina Ivanovna’s mind is unhinged; your own mind is

[024-242] unhinged,” he said after a brief silence.

[024-243] Five minutes passed. He still paced up and down the room in silence, not

[024-244] looking at her. At last he went up to her; his eyes glittered. He put

[024-245] his two hands on her shoulders and looked straight into her tearful

[024-246] face. His eyes were hard, feverish and piercing, his lips were

[024-247] twitching. All at once he bent down quickly and dropping to the

[024-249] certainly he looked like a madman.

[024-250] “What are you doing to me?” she muttered, turning pale, and a sudden

[024-251] anguish clutched at her heart.

[024-252] He stood up at once.

[024-253] “I did not bow down to you, I bowed down to all the suffering of

[024-254] humanity,” he said wildly and walked away to the window. “Listen,” he

[024-255] added, turning to her a minute later. “I said just now to an insolent

[024-256] man that he was not worth your little finger... and that I did my sister

[024-258] “Ach, you said that to them! And in her presence?” cried Sonia,

[024-317] better than any other. He began looking more intently at her.

[024-318] “So you pray to God a great deal, Sonia?” he asked her.

[024-320] “What should I be without God?” she whispered rapidly, forcibly,

[024-321] glancing at him with suddenly flashing eyes, and squeezing his hand.

[024-322] “Ah, so that is it!” he thought.

[024-323] “And what does God do for you?” he asked, probing her further.

[024-326] “Be silent! Don’t ask! You don’t deserve!” she cried suddenly, looking

[024-327] sternly and wrathfully at him.

[024-328] “That’s it, that’s it,” he repeated to himself.

[024-343] “It was brought me,” she answered, as it were unwillingly, not looking

[024-344] at him.

[024-345] “Who brought it?”

[024-346] “Lizaveta, I asked her for it.”

[024-347] “Lizaveta! strange!” he thought.

[024-352] Sonia looked obstinately at the ground and would not answer. She was

[024-353] standing sideways to the table.

[024-355] She stole a glance at him.

[024-356] “You are not looking in the right place.... It’s in the fourth gospel,”

[024-363] Sonia heard Raskolnikov’s request distrustfully and moved hesitatingly

[024-364] to the table. She took the book however.

[024-365] “Haven’t you read it?” she asked, looking up at him across the table.

[024-366] Her voice became sterner and sterner.

[024-367] “Long ago.... When I was at school. Read!”

[024-368] “And haven’t you heard it in church?”

[024-369] “I... haven’t been. Do you often go?”

[024-370] “N-no,” whispered Sonia.

[024-371] Raskolnikov smiled.

[024-372] “I understand.... And you won’t go to your father’s funeral to-morrow?”

[024-373] “Yes, I shall. I was at church last week, too... I had a requiem
