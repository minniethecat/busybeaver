## Overview

[[crime-and-punishment|Crime and Punishment]] is structured around a series of confrontations between rebellion and authority. Raskolnikov's crime is at once a rebellion against moral law, social order, and divine authority. His subsequent psychological disintegration is Dostoevsky's argument that such rebellion cannot be sustained — the human soul is not built to live without submitting to something larger than itself.

## Confrontations with Porfiry

The three interviews between Raskolnikov and [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] form the novel's central drama of authority. Porfiry never directly accuses; instead, he exerts a kind of psychological authority through patience, intelligence, and an uncanny understanding of Raskolnikov's mind. ## Rebellion Against the Mother

Raskolnikov's relationship with his mother [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]] is a quieter but equally significant form of rebellion. Her unconditional love is a kind of authority he cannot bear — it demands a response he is incapable of giving in his post-murder state. His coldness toward her is a rebellion against the bonds of family that his theory has severed.

## The Napoloenic Rebellion

Raskolnikov's [[extraordinary-man-theory|theory of the extraordinary man]] is explicitly framed as a doctrine of rebellion: the great man is defined by his capacity to overstep moral law. Napoleon — who "paved his way" over corpses — is the model. Raskolnikov's tragedy is that he discovers he is not Napoleon; he cannot sustain the rebellion his theory demands. The [[napoleon-complex|Napoleonic ambition]] collapses under the weight of conscience.

## Svidrigailov: Rebellion Without Return

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] represents rebellion pushed to its logical extreme. He has transgressed every moral boundary — possibly including murder — and feels no guilt. His suicide is the ultimate statement: a life of absolute rebellion against moral authority ends not in freedom but in annihilation. Svidrigailov is Raskolnikov's dark double, showing what Raskolnikov would become if his rebellion succeeded.

## Surrender and Authority Regained

The novel's resolution requires Raskolnikov to surrender — to confess to the police, to accept punishment, and ultimately to submit to Sonia's love and faith. This surrender is not defeat but the precondition for reclaiming his humanity. Dostoevsky's argument is that true freedom lies not in rebellion but in willing submission to a moral order that precedes and transcends the individual.
