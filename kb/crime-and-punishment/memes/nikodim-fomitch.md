# Nikodim Fomitch

Nikodim Fomitch is the police superintendent at the district station — a calm, pragmatic bureaucrat who serves as a counterweight to his volatile subordinate [[ilya-petrovitch|Ilya Petrovitch]]. He is present at two of the novel's pivotal scenes involving [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] and the law.

## Character

Unlike the "Explosive Lieutenant" Ilya Petrovitch, Nikodim Fomitch is characterized by a measured, conciliatory temperament. When Ilya Petrovitch berates [[luise-ivanovna|Luise Ivanovna]], Nikodim Fomitch intervenes with diplomatic restraint: "You go too far yourself" (008-441). He possesses the bureaucratic instinct to smooth over conflicts rather than inflame them.

His interactions with Raskolnikov are marked by a paternal, almost kindly tone — he addresses Raskolnikov affably (008-441) and, when Raskolnikov faints, his reaction is one of genuine concern: "What's this? Are you ill?" (008-574–008-576). This humanity, unremarkable in itself, stands in subtle contrast to the intellectual cruelty of [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]]'s psychological games.

## Role in Key Scenes

### The Police Station (Part 2, Chapter 1)

Nikodim Fomitch is introduced during Raskolnikov's summons to the police station over the landlady's debt complaint. While Ilya Petrovitch handles the immediate proceedings with bluster, Nikodim Fomitch provides the institutional calm — a quiet authority that keeps the station functioning. He is present when the conversation turns to the [[murder-of-pawnbroker|murder of the pawnbroker]] and witnesses Raskolnikov's sudden collapse (008-540).

### The Confession

Nikodim Fomitch is present at the police station when Raskolnikov returns to confess. His role is minor but significant — he represents the institutional reality that Raskolnikov must finally confront and submit to, the machinery of justice that has been closing around him since the murder.

## Significance

If Ilya Petrovitch is the temper of the law and [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]] is its intellect, Nikodim Fomitch is its everyday face — bureaucratic, reasonable, and ultimately humane. His presence reminds the reader that the legal system, for all its flaws, is staffed by ordinary men doing their jobs. This ordinariness is itself thematically significant: Raskolnikov's crime is not against an abstraction but against a real social order maintained by unremarkable men like Nikodim Fomitch.
