Poverty is the atmospheric condition of *Crime and Punishment* — the element in which every character moves and the pressure that shapes every decision. The novel opens with Raskolnikov "crushed by poverty" (PN 001-016), and this crushing is literal as well as figurative: his garret is impossibly small, his clothes are rags, he owes back rent to his landlady, and he has pawned his last possessions — his father's watch and his sister's ring — to the old pawnbroker. The murder is born, in part, from economic desperation, but the novel's treatment of poverty is far more complex than a simple causal chain from deprivation to crime.

## The Marmeladov Family

The Marmeladov family serves as the novel's most extended study of the degradations of poverty. Semyon Marmeladov, the former titular councillor, has drunk his family into destitution. His consumptive wife Katerina Ivanovna, once a provincial governor's daughter, now lives in a single room with three small children and no furniture. And Sonia, Marmeladov's daughter by his first marriage, has been driven to prostitution — not through moral failing but through the brute arithmetic of survival: "the little ones are hungry" (PN 001-266). The Marmeladovs' poverty is depicted in excruciating detail: the "ragged" appearance of the children, the lack of "even a separate corner" in the room, the public humiliation of Katerina Ivanovna at her husband's funeral dinner (PN 032-098).

## Poverty and Moral Choice

The novel does not sentimentalize poverty as inherently ennobling. Some of its poorest characters — Sonia, Lizaveta — are among its most virtuous, but poverty also breeds the desperation that produces the pawnbroker's usury, Marmeladov's alcoholism, and Luzhin's rational egoism. Luzhin, who has pulled himself out of poverty, argues that poverty is a moral failing remedied by self-interest: "if you acquire solely and exclusively for yourself... you are thereby benefiting the community" (PN 016-211). His "economic" view of morality is the mirror image of Raskolnikov's utilitarian calculus — both reduce human worth to a balance sheet.

## The Garret as Economic Symbol

Raskolnikov's room — "more like a cupboard than a room" (PN 001-003) — is the spatial embodiment of poverty. It is not merely small; it is coffin-like, oppressive, a physical reduction of human possibility to the minimum. The room is both a cause of Raskolnikov's feverish state and a symbol of the constriction that poverty imposes on the spirit. That his most radical thoughts — the "extraordinary man" theory, the murder plan — are hatched in this space is not coincidental: the garret is the laboratory in which intellectual abstraction, unmoored from the ordinary textures of human life, becomes toxic.

## Economic Transactions as Moral Barometer

The novel is dense with economic transactions that function as moral tests. Raskolnikov gives his last twenty copecks to the Marmeladov family after Marmeladov's accident — an impulsive act of charity that reveals the generosity his theories cannot extinguish (PN 012-049). Luzhin's attempt to frame Sonia for theft by planting a hundred-rouble note in her pocket (PN 031-012) is the novel's most damning portrait of money as moral corruption. And the pawnbroker Alyona Ivanovna, whose economic function is to convert desperation into profit, is the novel's symbol of the dehumanizing potential of economic rationality taken to its extreme.
