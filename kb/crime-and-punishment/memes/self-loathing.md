# Self-Loathing

**Self-loathing** is a central psychological motif in Dostoevsky's *Crime and Punishment*, expressed primarily through [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s recurring self-characterization as a "louse" (Russian: вошь). The metaphor traces his trajectory from intellectual arrogance before the murder, through psychological disintegration afterward, to eventual moral reckoning.

## The "Louse" Metaphor

Raskolnikov first invokes the louse metaphor before the murder, rationalizing that killing the pawnbroker would be "no more than the life of a louse" (006-101). After the murder, the metaphor turns inward — he repeatedly calls himself an "aesthetic louse" (020-243), recognizing that his failure was not moral but aesthetic: he proved unable to carry out his own theory cleanly.

> "Ech, I am an aesthetic louse and nothing more." (020-243)

## Self-Disgust and Moral Failure

Raskolnikov's self-loathing intensifies through his interactions with [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], to whom he confesses: "I've only killed a louse, Sonia, a useless, loathsome, harmful creature" (030-370). Sonia's reply — "A human being — a louse!" (030-372) — forces him to confront the moral reality he has been evading. The louse metaphor thus serves as both a psychological defense mechanism and the instrument of its own destruction.

## Resolution

In the novel's final movement, Raskolnikov begins to move beyond self-loathing. By the Epilogue, he acknowledges: "After all I am a man and not a louse" (030-524). This shift marks the beginning of his [[spiritual-resurrection|spiritual resurrection]] — the recognition that self-hatred cannot substitute for genuine moral reckoning.
