# Rational Egoism

The philosophical doctrine — dominant among Russian radicals in the 1860s — that all human action is motivated by rational self-interest, and that society can be perfected by aligning individual interests with collective good. The novel presents this doctrine as both false and dangerous, showing through Raskolnikov's crime and Luzhin's schemes that reducing morality to calculation produces not utopia but destruction.

## Definition

Rational egoism, as developed by Nikolai Chernyshevsky in *What Is to Be Done?* (1863), held that humans always act in their perceived self-interest. The task of social reform was therefore to educate people to see that their true self-interest aligned with the common good — at which point selfishness and altruism would converge. Dostoevsky regarded this as a monstrous simplification of human psychology, one that denied conscience, free will, and the irrational depths of the soul. Raskolnikov's theory is a dark inversion of Chernyshevsky's: if self-interest governs all, then the extraordinary man has the right to pursue his interest beyond conventional morality.

## Expression in the Novel

Pyotr Luzhin is the novel's living embodiment of rational egoism. He preaches that if everyone pursues their own advantage, universal prosperity results — a doctrine he uses to justify his attempt to dominate Dounia while appearing philanthropic. Raskolnikov's more sophisticated version argues that extraordinary men may transgress moral law for the greater eventual good. Both versions are shown to fail: Luzhin is exposed as a petty schemer, and Raskolnikov discovers that murder, far from being a rational calculation, produces psychological devastation that no theory can contain.

## Key Passages

> "Love yourself before all men, for everything in the world rests on self-interest" — Luzhin's creed (PN 003-257)

> "Trifles that always ruin everything" — Raskolnikov's recognition that irrational, small impulses undo rational plans (PN 001-086)

## Character Perspectives

- **Pyotr Luzhin**: The vulgar advocate of rational egoism. He reduces all human relations to economic calculation, offering Dounia a "rational" marriage that is transparently exploitative. His exposure as a petty thief is the novel's verdict on his philosophy.
- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: The intellectual heir of rational egoism who takes it to its logical extreme — and is destroyed by it. His recovery begins only when he abandons rational self-justification.
- **Andrey Lebezyatnikov**: A comic figure who parrots progressive ideas without understanding them, but who redeems himself by exposing Luzhin's scheme — the novel's suggestion that even misguided idealism can produce moral action.
