# Criminal Psychology

The systematic examination of the criminal's mental state before, during, and after the act of murder, as portrayed through Raskolnikov's psychological deterioration in *Crime and Punishment*.

## Definition

Criminal psychology in *Crime and Punishment* refers to the complex interplay of rational calculation, moral confusion, and psychological breakdown that characterizes Raskolnikov's experience of murder. Dostoevsky depicts crime not merely as a legal transgression but as a profound psychological event that reshapes the perpetrator's consciousness (019-352).

## Expression in the Novel

The novel traces criminal psychology through three distinct phases. Before the murder, Raskolnikov experiences feverish obsession and rationalization, convincing himself that killing the pawnbroker is an intellectual exercise in utilitarian justice (005-028). During the immediate aftermath, he is overcome by physical illness and mental fragmentation — the "fever that had begun long before" the murder intensifies into a state of near-delirium (008-015). After confessing, Raskolnikov's psychology evolves from guilt-denial toward the possibility of remorse, though Porfiry explicitly questions whether he feels any "moral feeling" at all (036-273).

## Key Passages

> "They have a psychology of their own, brother. But it's nothing like what's in the books." — Porfiry to Raskolnikov (020-027)

> "A case of morbid psychology," Porfiry went on quickly. "A man lies, and he thinks he's telling the truth" (025-436)

> "But remorse? You deny him any moral feeling?" (036-273)

## Character Perspectives

- **Raskolnikov**: Embodies the "morbid psychology" of the criminal — he oscillates between defiant rationalization ("I am guilty! Mine is the sin!" at 026-031) and physical collapse, illustrating Dostoevsky's thesis that crime carries its own psychological punishment
- **Porfiry Petrovich**: Functions as the novel's psychological investigator, deploying "psychology" as an interrogation technique — he repeatedly invokes "morbid psychology" (025-436, 025-447) to dismantle Raskolnikov's defenses, though he also acknowledges that psychology "cuts both ways" as evidence (026-216, 033-304)

## Historical & Philosophical Context

Dostoevsky's exploration of criminal psychology emerged from his own experience as a political prisoner in Siberia, where he observed criminals firsthand. The novel's psychological realism challenged the prevailing utilitarian theories of Chernyshevsky, which treated crime as a social disease curable by environmental reform. Dostoevsky instead insisted on the irreducible complexity of the criminal psyche — the coexistence of rational justification and irrational torment (032-285).
