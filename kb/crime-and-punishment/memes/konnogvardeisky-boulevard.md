## Overview

The Konnogvardeisky Boulevard (Конногвардейский бульвар, Horse-Guards Boulevard) is a St. Petersburg boulevard that appears in Parts 1 and 4 of *Crime and Punishment* simply as "the boulevard." It is the site of a crucial scene in the novel's backstory: [[dounia-raskolnikov]]'s harassment by [[arkady-svidrigailov]], recounted in Pulcheria Alexandrovna's letter to [[rodion-raskolnikov]].

## The Dounia Incident

Pulcheria Alexandrovna's letter describes a sequence of escalating encounters on the boulevard. Svidrigailov, having engineered the firing of Dounia's governess position, begins accosting her in public:

> This boulevard was never much frequented, and in the evening it was still more deserted. [004-232]

The boulevard's emptiness at dusk makes it a space of vulnerability. Svidrigailov positions himself where Dounia must pass:

> He sat down on a seat on the further side of the boulevard, about fifteen paces from the path. [004-234]

He waits there deliberately — a spatial predator who has studied her route. When Dounia tries to avoid him, the geometry of the boulevard works against her:

> She only just reached the seat before he was beside her. [004-305]

The scene establishes Svidrigailov's method: using the public-but-empty spaces of [[st-petersburg]] to threaten without witnesses, to insinuate without evidence.

## Spatial Architecture of Menace

The boulevard functions in the novel as what modern criticism would call a space of gendered surveillance. Unlike the bustling [[hay-market]] or the cramped [[raskolnikovs-garret]], the boulevard is a publicly accessible space whose very emptiness enables predation. It is a liminal zone — neither the private interior where violence is hidden nor the crowded street where witnesses provide protection.

The boulevard's design — straight, lined with benches, designed for leisure — is perverted by Svidrigailov into a hunting ground. He studies Dounia from a bench, waits at the corner, follows at a calibrated distance.

## Later Appearances

The boulevard appears again as a point of reference when Svidrigailov's later movements are narrated, though by this point Dounia has left for Petersburg. The boulevard remains associated in the novel's spatial memory with the predatory pattern that follows Svidrigailov throughout the narrative.

## Significance

The Konnogvardeisky Boulevard illustrates Dostoevsky's technique of charging real Petersburg spaces with psychological and moral significance. A tree-lined promenade — a symbol of civilised urban leisure — becomes, through Svidrigailov's occupation of it, a stage for the coercion that operates just beneath the surface of social respectability. The boulevard is the spatial correlative of Svidrigailov's own character: outwardly charming and well-appointed, inwardly corrupt and predatory.
