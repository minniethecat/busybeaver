## Overview

Light and darkness form a fundamental symbolic axis in [[crime-and-punishment|Crime and Punishment]]. Dostoevsky deploys this opposition not as simple moral allegory — light equals good, darkness equals evil — but as a fluid register of psychological and spiritual condition. Characters move between illumination and shadow, and the quality of light in a scene often signals its deeper meaning.

## Raskolnikov's Garret: The Coffin in Twilight

Raskolnikov's tiny room is repeatedly described as dark, cramped, and coffin-like. He lies in semi-darkness for days, drifting between feverish sleep and wakefulness. The garret's perpetual twilight externalizes his moral paralysis — he is neither fully in the dark of unconscious guilt nor in the light of confession and redemption:

> "…stifled in the little yellow room that was…" [003-437]

The darkness of the garret is not merely atmospheric; it is the visible form of Raskolnikov's spiritual state before his eventual turn toward the light.

## Candlelight and the Lazarus Reading

The most significant use of light in the novel occurs during Sonia's reading of the Lazarus story. A candle burns between them as she reads — a fragile point of light in the darkness of her room, symbolizing both the possibility of resurrection and the precariousness of faith:

The candlelit scene echoes the Gospel narrative itself: Lazarus emerging from the tomb into light. Sonia's candle becomes the novel's central image of spiritual illumination breaking through darkness.

## Svidrigailov and the Darkness

[[arkady-svidrigailov|Svidrigailov]] inhabits a world of deepening darkness. His final night is spent wandering through a city lashed by rain, culminating in his suicide at dawn — a perverse anti-resurrection. Where Raskolnikov moves toward light, Svidrigailov is consumed by shadow. His dreams are nightmares, and even the dawn he greets is grey and indifferent.

## Dawn and Epilogue Transformation

The novel's resolution comes at dawn. In the Epilogue, Raskolnikov sits by the river in Siberia as the sun rises, and his transformation begins. Dostoevsky uses this natural light — as opposed to the artificial yellow interiors of St. Petersburg — to mark the shift from psychological torment to spiritual renewal. The dawn on the Siberian river is the novel's answer to the twilight of the garret.
