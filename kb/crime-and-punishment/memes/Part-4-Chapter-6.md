[026-001] When he remembered the scene afterwards, this is how [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] saw it.

[026-002] The noise behind the door increased, and suddenly the door was opened a

[026-003] little.

[026-004] “What is it?” cried [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] Petrovitch, annoyed. “Why, I gave orders...”

[026-005] For an instant there was no answer, but it was evident that there were

[026-006] several persons at the door, and that they were apparently pushing

[026-007] somebody back.

[026-008] “What is it?” Porfiry Petrovitch repeated, uneasily.

[026-009] “The prisoner [[nikolay-dementyev|Nikolay]] has been brought,” someone answered.

[026-010] “He is not wanted! Take him away! Let him wait! What’s he doing here?

[026-011] How irregular!” cried Porfiry, rushing to the door.

[026-012] “But he...” began the same voice, and suddenly ceased.

[026-013] Two seconds, not more, were spent in actual struggle, then someone gave

[026-014] a violent shove, and then a man, very pale, strode into the room.

[026-015] This man’s appearance was at first sight very strange. He stared

[026-016] straight before him, as though seeing nothing. There was a determined

[026-017] gleam in his eyes; at the same time there was a deathly pallor in his

[026-018] face, as though he were being led to the scaffold. His white lips were

[026-019] faintly twitching.

[026-020] He was dressed like a workman and was of medium height, very young,

[026-021] slim, his hair cut in round crop, with thin spare features. The man whom

[026-022] he had thrust back followed him into the room and succeeded in seizing

[026-023] him by the shoulder; he was a warder; but Nikolay pulled his arm away.

[026-024] Several persons crowded inquisitively into the doorway. Some of them

[026-025] tried to get in. All this took place almost instantaneously.

[026-026] “Go away, it’s too soon! Wait till you are sent for!... Why have you

[026-027] brought him so soon?” Porfiry Petrovitch muttered, extremely annoyed,

[026-028] and as it were thrown out of his reckoning.

[026-029] But Nikolay suddenly knelt down.

[026-030] “What’s the matter?” cried Porfiry, surprised.

[026-031] “I am guilty! Mine is the sin! I am the murderer,” Nikolay articulated

[026-032] suddenly, rather breathless, but speaking fairly loudly.

[026-033] For ten seconds there was silence as though all had been struck dumb;

[026-034] even the warder stepped back, mechanically retreated to the door, and

[026-035] stood immovable.

[026-036] “What is it?” cried Porfiry Petrovitch, recovering from his momentary

[026-037] stupefaction.

[026-038] “I... am the murderer,” repeated Nikolay, after a brief pause.

[026-039] “What... you... what... whom did you kill?” Porfiry Petrovitch was

[026-040] obviously bewildered.

[026-041] Nikolay again was silent for a moment.

[026-042] “[[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] and her sister [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]], I... killed... with

[026-043] an axe. Darkness came over me,” he added suddenly, and was again silent.

[026-044] He still remained on his knees. Porfiry Petrovitch stood for some

[026-045] moments as though meditating, but suddenly roused himself and waved back

[026-046] the uninvited spectators. They instantly vanished and closed the door.

[026-047] Then he looked towards Raskolnikov, who was standing in the corner,

[026-048] staring wildly at Nikolay and moved towards him, but stopped short,

[026-049] looked from Nikolay to Raskolnikov and then again at Nikolay, and

[026-050] seeming unable to restrain himself darted at the latter.

[026-051] “You’re in too great a hurry,” he shouted at him, almost angrily. “I

[026-052] didn’t ask you what came over you.... Speak, did you kill them?”

[026-053] “I am the murderer.... I want to give evidence,” Nikolay pronounced.

[026-054] “Ach! What did you kill them with?”

[026-055] “An axe. I had it ready.”

[026-056] “Ach, he is in a hurry! Alone?”

[026-057] Nikolay did not understand the question.

[026-058] “Did you do it alone?”

[026-059] “Yes, alone. And Mitka is not guilty and had no share in it.”

[026-060] “Don’t be in a hurry about Mitka! A-ach! How was it you ran downstairs

[026-061] like that at the time? The porters met you both!”

[026-062] “It was to put them off the scent... I ran after Mitka,” Nikolay replied

[026-063] hurriedly, as though he had prepared the answer.

[026-064] “I knew it!” cried Porfiry, with vexation. “It’s not his own tale he is

[026-065] telling,” he muttered as though to himself, and suddenly his eyes rested

[026-066] on Raskolnikov again.

[026-067] He was apparently so taken up with Nikolay that for a moment he had

[026-068] forgotten Raskolnikov. He was a little taken aback.

[026-069] “My dear Rodion Romanovitch, excuse me!” he flew up to him, “this won’t

[026-070] do; I’m afraid you must go... it’s no good your staying... I will...

[026-071] you see, what a surprise!... Good-bye!”

[026-072] And taking him by the arm, he showed him to the door.

[026-073] “I suppose you didn’t expect it?” said Raskolnikov who, though he had

[026-074] not yet fully grasped the situation, had regained his courage.

[026-075] “You did not expect it either, my friend. See how your hand is

[026-076] trembling! He-he!”

[026-077] “You’re trembling, too, Porfiry Petrovitch!”

[026-078] “Yes, I am; I didn’t expect it.”

[026-079] They were already at the door; Porfiry was impatient for Raskolnikov to

[026-080] be gone.

[026-081] “And your little surprise, aren’t you going to show it to me?”

[026-082] Raskolnikov said, sarcastically.

[026-083] “Why, his teeth are chattering as he asks, he-he! You are an ironical

[026-084] person! Come, till we meet!”

[026-085] “I believe we can say _good-bye_!”

[026-086] “That’s in God’s hands,” muttered Porfiry, with an unnatural smile.

[026-087] As he walked through the office, Raskolnikov noticed that many people

[026-088] were looking at him. Among them he saw the two porters from _the_ house,

[026-089] whom he had invited that night to the police station. They stood there

[026-090] waiting. But he was no sooner on the [[staircases|stairs]] than he heard the voice of

[026-091] Porfiry Petrovitch behind him. Turning round, he saw the latter running

[026-092] after him, out of breath.

[026-093] “One word, Rodion Romanovitch; as to all the rest, it’s in God’s hands,

[026-094] but as a matter of form there are some questions I shall have to ask

[026-095] you... so we shall meet again, shan’t we?”

[026-096] And Porfiry stood still, facing him with a smile.

[026-097] “Shan’t we?” he added again.

[026-098] He seemed to want to say something more, but could not speak out.

[026-099] “You must forgive me, Porfiry Petrovitch, for what has just passed... I

[026-100] lost my temper,” began Raskolnikov, who had so far regained his courage

[026-101] that he felt irresistibly inclined to display his coolness.

[026-102] “Don’t mention it, don’t mention it,” Porfiry replied, almost gleefully.

[026-103] “I myself, too... I have a wicked temper, I admit it! But we shall meet

[026-104] again. If it’s God’s will, we may see a great deal of one another.”

[026-105] “And will get to know each other through and through?” added

[026-106] Raskolnikov.

[026-107] “Yes; know each other through and through,” assented Porfiry Petrovitch,

[026-108] and he screwed up his eyes, looking earnestly at Raskolnikov. “Now

[026-109] you’re going to a birthday party?”

[026-110] “To a funeral.”

[026-111] “Of course, the funeral! Take care of yourself, and get well.”

[026-112] “I don’t know what to wish you,” said Raskolnikov, who had begun to

[026-113] descend the stairs, but looked back again. “I should like to wish you

[026-114] success, but your office is such a comical one.”

[026-115] “Why comical?” Porfiry Petrovitch had turned to go, but he seemed to

[026-116] prick up his ears at this.

[026-117] “Why, how you must have been torturing and harassing that poor Nikolay

[026-118] psychologically, after your fashion, till he confessed! You must have

[026-119] been at him day and night, proving to him that he was the murderer, and

[026-120] now that he has confessed, you’ll begin vivisecting him again. ‘You are

[026-121] lying,’ you’ll say. ‘You are not the murderer! You can’t be! It’s not

[026-122] your own tale you are telling!’ You must admit it’s a comical business!”

[026-123] “He-he-he! You noticed then that I said to Nikolay just now that it was

[026-124] not his own tale he was telling?”

[026-125] “How could I help noticing it!”

[026-126] “He-he! You are quick-witted. You notice everything! You’ve really a

[026-127] playful mind! And you always fasten on the comic side... he-he! They say

[026-128] that was the marked characteristic of Gogol, among the writers.”

[026-129] “Yes, of Gogol.”

[026-130] “Yes, of Gogol.... I shall look forward to meeting you.”

[026-131] “So shall I.”

[026-132] Raskolnikov walked straight home. He was so muddled and bewildered that

[026-133] on getting home he sat for a quarter of an hour on the sofa, trying to

[026-134] collect his thoughts. He did not attempt to think about Nikolay; he

[026-135] was stupefied; he felt that his confession was something inexplicable,

[026-136] amazing--something beyond his understanding. But Nikolay’s confession

[026-137] was an actual fact. The consequences of this fact were clear to him at

[026-138] once, its falsehood could not fail to be discovered, and then they

[026-139] would be after him again. Till then, at least, he was free and must do

[026-140] something for himself, for the danger was imminent.

[026-141] But how imminent? His position gradually became clear to him.

[026-142] Remembering, sketchily, the main outlines of his recent scene with

[026-143] Porfiry, he could not help shuddering again with horror. Of course,

[026-144] he did not yet know all Porfiry’s aims, he could not see into all his

[026-145] calculations. But he had already partly shown his hand, and no one knew

[026-146] better than Raskolnikov how terrible Porfiry’s “lead” had been for

[026-147] him. A little more and he _might_ have given himself away completely,

[026-148] circumstantially. Knowing his nervous temperament and from the first

[026-149] glance seeing through him, Porfiry, though playing a bold game, was

[026-150] bound to win. There’s no denying that Raskolnikov had compromised

[026-151] himself seriously, but no _facts_ had come to light as yet; there was

[026-152] nothing positive. But was he taking a true view of the position? Wasn’t

[026-153] he mistaken? What had Porfiry been trying to get at? Had he really some

[026-154] surprise prepared for him? And what was it? Had he really been expecting

[026-155] something or not? How would they have parted if it had not been for the

[026-156] unexpected appearance of Nikolay?

[026-157] Porfiry had shown almost all his cards--of course, he had risked

[026-158] something in showing them--and if he had really had anything up his

[026-159] sleeve (Raskolnikov reflected), he would have shown that, too. What was

[026-160] that “surprise”? Was it a joke? Had it meant anything? Could it have

[026-161] concealed anything like a fact, a piece of positive evidence? His

[026-162] yesterday’s visitor? What had become of him? Where was he to-day? If

[026-163] Porfiry really had any evidence, it must be connected with him....

[026-164] He sat on the sofa with his elbows on his knees and his face hidden in

[026-165] his hands. He was still shivering nervously. At last he got up, took his

[026-166] cap, thought a minute, and went to the door.

[026-167] He had a sort of presentiment that for to-day, at least, he might

[026-168] consider himself out of danger. He had a sudden sense almost of joy; he

[026-169] wanted to make haste to [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]]’s. He would be too late for

[026-170] the funeral, of course, but he would be in time for the memorial dinner,

[026-171] and there at once he would see [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]].

[026-172] He stood still, thought a moment, and a suffering smile came for a

[026-173] moment on to his lips.

[026-174] “To-day! To-day,” he repeated to himself. “Yes, to-day! So it must

[026-175] be....”

[026-176] But as he was about to open the door, it began opening of itself. He

[026-177] started and moved back. The door opened gently and slowly, and there

[026-178] suddenly appeared a figure--yesterday’s visitor _from underground_.

[026-179] The man stood in the doorway, looked at Raskolnikov without speaking,

[026-180] and took a step forward into the room. He was exactly the same as

[026-181] yesterday; the same figure, the same dress, but there was a great change

[026-182] in his face; he looked dejected and sighed deeply. If he had only put

[026-183] his hand up to his cheek and leaned his head on one side he would have

[026-184] looked exactly like a peasant woman.

[026-185] “What do you want?” asked Raskolnikov, numb with terror. The man was

[026-186] still silent, but suddenly he bowed down almost to the ground, touching

[026-187] it with his finger.

[026-188] “What is it?” cried Raskolnikov.

[026-189] “I have sinned,” the man articulated softly.

[026-190] “How?”

[026-191] “By evil thoughts.”

[026-192] They looked at one another.

[026-193] “I was vexed. When you came, perhaps in drink, and bade the porters go

[026-194] to the police station and asked about the blood, I was vexed that they

[026-195] let you go and took you for drunken. I was so vexed that I lost my

[026-196] sleep. And remembering the address we came here yesterday and asked for

[026-197] you....”

[026-198] “Who came?” Raskolnikov interrupted, instantly beginning to recollect.

[026-199] “I did, I’ve wronged you.”

[026-200] “Then you come from that house?”

[026-201] “I was standing at the gate with them... don’t you remember? We have

[026-202] carried on our trade in that house for years past. We cure and prepare

[026-203] hides, we take work home... most of all I was vexed....”

[026-204] And the whole scene of the day before yesterday in the gateway came

[026-205] clearly before Raskolnikov’s mind; he recollected that there had

[026-206] been several people there besides the porters, women among them.

[026-207] He remembered one voice had suggested taking him straight to the

[026-208] police-station. He could not recall the face of the speaker, and even

[026-209] now he did not recognise it, but he remembered that he had turned round

[026-210] and made him some answer....

[026-211] So this was the solution of yesterday’s horror. The most awful thought

[026-212] was that he had been actually almost lost, had almost done for himself

[026-213] on account of such a _trivial_ circumstance. So this man could tell

[026-214] nothing except his asking about the flat and the blood stains. So

[026-215] Porfiry, too, had nothing but that _delirium_, no facts but this

[026-216] _psychology_ which _cuts both ways_, nothing positive. So if no more

[026-217] facts come to light (and they must not, they must not!) then... then

[026-218] what can they do to him? How can they convict him, even if they arrest

[026-219] him? And Porfiry then had only just heard about the flat and had not

[026-220] known about it before.

[026-221] “Was it you who told Porfiry... that I’d been there?” he cried, struck

[026-222] by a sudden idea.

[026-223] “What Porfiry?”

[026-224] “The head of the detective department?”

[026-225] “Yes. The porters did not go there, but I went.”

[026-226] “To-day?”

[026-227] “I got there two minutes before you. And I heard, I heard it all, how he

[026-228] worried you.”

[026-229] “Where? What? When?”

[026-230] “Why, in the next room. I was sitting there all the time.”

[026-231] “What? Why, then you were the surprise? But how could it happen? Upon my

[026-232] word!”

[026-233] “I saw that the porters did not want to do what I said,” began the man;

[026-234] “for it’s too late, said they, and maybe he’ll be angry that we did not

[026-235] come at the time. I was vexed and I lost my sleep, and I began making

[026-236] inquiries. And finding out yesterday where to go, I went to-day. The

[026-237] first time I went he wasn’t there, when I came an hour later he couldn’t

[026-238] see me. I went the third time, and they showed me in. I informed him of

[026-239] everything, just as it happened, and he began skipping about the room

[026-240] and punching himself on the chest. ‘What do you scoundrels mean by it?

[026-241] If I’d known about it I should have arrested him!’ Then he ran out,

[026-242] called somebody and began talking to him in the corner, then he turned

[026-243] to me, scolding and questioning me. He scolded me a great deal; and I

[026-244] told him everything, and I told him that you didn’t dare to say a word

[026-245] in answer to me yesterday and that you didn’t recognise me. And he

[026-246] fell to running about again and kept hitting himself on the chest, and

[026-247] getting angry and running about, and when you were announced he told

[026-248] me to go into the next room. ‘Sit there a bit,’ he said. ‘Don’t move,

[026-249] whatever you may hear.’ And he set a chair there for me and locked

[026-250] me in. ‘Perhaps,’ he said, ‘I may call you.’ And when Nikolay’d been

[026-251] brought he let me out as soon as you were gone. ‘I shall send for you

[026-252] again and question you,’ he said.”

[026-253] “And did he question Nikolay while you were there?”

[026-254] “He got rid of me as he did of you, before he spoke to Nikolay.”

[026-255] The man stood still, and again suddenly bowed down, touching the ground

[026-256] with his finger.

[026-257] “Forgive me for my evil thoughts, and my slander.”

[026-258] “May God forgive you,” answered Raskolnikov.

[026-259] And as he said this, the man bowed down again, but not to the ground,

[026-260] turned slowly and went out of the room.

[026-261] “It all cuts both ways, now it all cuts both ways,” repeated

[026-262] Raskolnikov, and he went out more confident than ever.

[026-263] “Now we’ll make a fight for it,” he said, with a malicious smile, as he

[026-264] went down the stairs. His malice was aimed at himself; with shame and

[026-265] contempt he recollected his “cowardice.”
