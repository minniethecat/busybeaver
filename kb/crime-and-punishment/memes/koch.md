# Koch

Koch is the man who, together with the student [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]], discovers the bodies of [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] and [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]] in the pawnbroker's flat. His arrival at the apartment door — ringing the bell just as [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is trying to escape — creates one of the novel's most suspenseful sequences, a prolonged moment of near-discovery in which Raskolnikov hides behind the door while Koch and then Pestryakov attempt to gain entry to the flat.

## Overview

Koch appears in only one chapter (Part 1, Chapter 7), but his role is structurally crucial. He is the first person to arrive at the pawnbroker's flat after the murder, and his persistent ringing of the bell — combined with the fact that the door is only held shut by a hook that Raskolnikov has placed — generates the novel's first extended scene of suspense. When Koch goes downstairs to fetch the porter, Raskolnikov slips out, leaving Koch and Pestryakov to discover the bodies upon their return. He is never seen again in the novel.

## Role in the Novel

Koch arrives at the old woman's flat to keep an appointment, apparently for some kind of business transaction. Finding the door locked from inside — "she never locks the door from inside during the day" (007-270) — he becomes suspicious and rings repeatedly. He is joined by Pestryakov, and the two men speculate about what has happened before going to fetch the porter. Their departure creates the narrow window through which Raskolnikov escapes, hiding in an empty flat on the floor below.

Koch's testimony later becomes part of the police investigation. Razumihin mentions him as one of the people who were at the scene, and his description of events — the locked door, the bell, the absence of the old woman — forms part of the circumstantial case that Porfiry builds.

## Key Relationships

- **[[pestryakov|Pestryakov]]**: Koch's companion in the discovery of the bodies. Together they form a pair of ordinary citizens whose mundane business appointment collides with the extraordinary fact of murder.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Koch represents the intrusion of ordinary reality into Raskolnikov's "extraordinary" act. His ringing of the bell is the first external check on Raskolnikov's theory — the first sign that the world does not conform to the rational plan the murderer has constructed. In the novel's symbolic logic, Koch is one of many characters who, without knowing it, function as agents of [[fate-and-coincidence|fate and coincidence]], the web of chance encounters and near-misses that Dostoevsky uses to suggest a providential order beneath the surface of events.
