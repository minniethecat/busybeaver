[001-001] On an exceptionally hot evening early in July a young man came out of

[001-002] the garret in which he lodged in S. Place and walked slowly, as though

[001-003] in hesitation, towards K. bridge.

[001-004] He had successfully avoided meeting his landlady on the staircase. His

[001-005] garret was under the roof of a high, five-storied house and was more

[001-006] like a cupboard than a room. The landlady who provided him with garret,

[001-007] dinners, and attendance, lived on the floor below, and every time

[001-008] he went out he was obliged to pass her kitchen, the door of which

[001-009] invariably stood open. And each time he passed, the young man had a

[001-010] sick, frightened feeling, which made him scowl and feel ashamed. He was

[001-011] hopelessly in debt to his landlady, and was afraid of meeting her.

[001-012] This was not because he was cowardly and abject, quite the contrary; but

[001-013] for some time past he had been in an overstrained irritable condition,

[001-014] verging on hypochondria. He had become so completely absorbed in

[001-015] himself, and isolated from his fellows that he dreaded meeting, not

[001-016] only his landlady, but anyone at all. He was crushed by poverty, but the

[001-017] anxieties of his position had of late ceased to weigh upon him. He had

[001-018] given up attending to matters of practical importance; he had lost all

[001-019] desire to do so. Nothing that any landlady could do had a real terror

[001-020] for him. But to be stopped on the [[staircases|stairs]], to be forced to listen to her

[001-021] trivial, irrelevant gossip, to pestering demands for payment, threats

[001-022] and complaints, and to rack his brains for excuses, to prevaricate, to

[001-023] lie--no, rather than that, he would creep down the stairs like a cat and

[001-024] slip out unseen.

[001-025] This evening, however, on coming out into the street, he became acutely

[001-026] aware of his fears.

[001-027] “I want to attempt a thing _like that_ and am frightened by these

[001-028] trifles,” he thought, with an odd smile. “Hm... yes, all is in a man’s

[001-029] hands and he lets it all slip from cowardice, that’s an axiom. It would

[001-030] be interesting to know what it is men are most afraid of. Taking a new

[001-031] step, uttering a new word is what they [[fear-and-dread|fear]] most.... But I am talking

[001-032] too much. It’s because I chatter that I do nothing. Or perhaps it is

[001-033] that I chatter because I do nothing. I’ve learned to chatter this

[001-034] last month, lying for days together in my den thinking... of Jack the

[001-035] Giant-killer. Why am I going there now? Am I capable of _that_? Is

[001-036] _that_ serious? It is not serious at all. It’s simply a fantasy to amuse

[001-037] myself; a plaything! Yes, maybe it is a plaything.”

[001-038] The heat in the street was terrible: and the airlessness, the bustle

[001-039] and the plaster, scaffolding, bricks, and dust all about him, and that

[001-040] special [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]] stench, so familiar to all who are unable to get out

[001-041] of town in summer--all worked painfully upon the young man’s already

[001-042] overwrought nerves. The insufferable stench from the pot-houses, which

[001-043] are particularly numerous in that part of the town, and the drunken men

[001-044] whom he met continually, although it was a working day, completed

[001-045] the revolting misery of the picture. An expression of the profoundest

[001-046] disgust gleamed for a moment in the young man’s refined face. He was,

[001-047] by the way, exceptionally handsome, above the average in height, slim,

[001-048] well-built, with beautiful dark eyes and dark brown hair. Soon he sank

[001-049] into deep thought, or more accurately speaking into a complete blankness

[001-050] of mind; he walked along not observing what was about him and not caring

[001-051] to observe it. From time to time, he would mutter something, from the

[001-052] habit of talking to himself, to which he had just confessed. At these

[001-053] moments he would become conscious that his ideas were sometimes in a

[001-054] tangle and that he was very weak; for two days he had scarcely tasted

[001-055] food.

[001-056] He was so badly dressed that even a man accustomed to shabbiness would

[001-057] have been ashamed to be seen in the street in such rags. In that quarter

[001-058] of the town, however, scarcely any shortcoming in dress would have

[001-059] created surprise. Owing to the proximity of the [[hay-market|Hay Market]], the number

[001-060] of establishments of bad character, the preponderance of the trading

[001-061] and working class population crowded in these streets and alleys in the

[001-062] heart of Petersburg, types so various were to be seen in the streets

[001-063] that no figure, however queer, would have caused surprise. But there was

[001-064] such accumulated bitterness and contempt in the young man’s heart, that,

[001-065] in spite of all the fastidiousness of youth, he minded his rags least

[001-066] of all in the street. It was a different matter when he met with

[001-067] acquaintances or with former fellow students, whom, indeed, he disliked

[001-068] meeting at any time. And yet when a drunken man who, for some unknown

[001-069] reason, was being taken somewhere in a huge waggon dragged by a heavy

[001-070] dray horse, suddenly shouted at him as he drove past: “Hey there, German

[001-071] hatter” bawling at the top of his voice and pointing at him--the young

[001-072] man stopped suddenly and clutched tremulously at his hat. It was a tall

[001-073] round hat from Zimmerman’s, but completely worn out, rusty with age, all

[001-074] torn and bespattered, brimless and bent on one side in a most unseemly

[001-075] fashion. Not shame, however, but quite another feeling akin to terror

[001-076] had overtaken him.

[001-077] “I knew it,” he muttered in confusion, “I thought so! That’s the worst

[001-078] of all! Why, a stupid thing like this, the most trivial detail might

[001-079] spoil the whole plan. Yes, my hat is too noticeable.... It looks absurd

[001-080] and that makes it noticeable.... With my rags I ought to wear a cap, any

[001-081] sort of old pancake, but not this grotesque thing. Nobody wears such

[001-082] a hat, it would be noticed a mile off, it would be remembered.... What

[001-083] matters is that people would remember it, and that would give them

[001-084] a clue. For this business one should be as little conspicuous as

[001-085] possible.... Trifles, trifles are what matter! Why, it’s just such

[001-086] trifles that always ruin everything....”

[001-087] He had not far to go; he knew indeed how many steps it was from the gate

[001-088] of his lodging house: exactly seven hundred and thirty. He had counted

[001-089] them once when he had been lost in dreams. At the time he had put no

[001-090] faith in those dreams and was only tantalising himself by their hideous

[001-091] but daring recklessness. Now, a month later, he had begun to look upon

[001-092] them differently, and, in spite of the monologues in which he jeered at

[001-093] his own impotence and indecision, he had involuntarily come to regard

[001-094] this “hideous” dream as an exploit to be attempted, although he

[001-095] still did not realise this himself. He was positively going now for a

[001-096] “rehearsal” of his project, and at every step his excitement grew more

[001-097] and more violent.

[001-098] With a sinking heart and a nervous tremor, he went up to a huge house

[001-099] which on one side looked on to [[catherine-canal|the canal]], and on the other into the

[001-100] street. This house was let out in tiny tenements and was inhabited by

[001-101] working people of all kinds--tailors, locksmiths, cooks, Germans of

[001-102] sorts, girls picking up a living as best they could, petty clerks, etc.

[001-103] There was a continual coming and going through the two gates and in the

[001-104] two courtyards of the house. Three or four door-keepers were employed on

[001-105] the building. The young man was very glad to meet none of them, and

[001-106] at once slipped unnoticed through the door on the right, and up the

[001-107] staircase. It was a back staircase, dark and narrow, but he was familiar

[001-108] with it already, and knew his way, and he liked all these surroundings:

[001-109] in such darkness even the most inquisitive eyes were not to be dreaded.

[001-110] “If I am so scared now, what would it be if it somehow came to pass that

[001-111] I were really going to do it?” he could not help asking himself as he

[001-112] reached the fourth storey. There his progress was barred by some porters

[001-113] who were engaged in moving furniture out of a flat. He knew that the

[001-114] flat had been occupied by a German clerk in the civil service, and his

[001-115] family. This German was moving out then, and so the fourth floor on this

[001-116] staircase would be untenanted except by [[alyona-ivanovna|the old woman]]. “That’s a good

[001-117] thing anyway,” he thought to himself, as he rang the bell of the old

[001-118] woman’s flat. The bell gave a faint tinkle as though it were made of

[001-119] tin and not of copper. The little flats in such houses always have bells

[001-120] that ring like that. He had forgotten the note of that bell, and now

[001-121] its peculiar tinkle seemed to remind him of something and to bring it

[001-122] clearly before him.... He started, his nerves were terribly overstrained

[001-123] by now. In a little while, the door was opened a tiny crack: the old

[001-124] woman eyed her visitor with evident distrust through the crack, and

[001-125] nothing could be seen but her little eyes, glittering in the darkness.

[001-126] But, seeing a number of people on the landing, she grew bolder, and

[001-127] opened the door wide. The young man stepped into the dark entry, which

[001-128] was partitioned off from the tiny kitchen. The old woman stood facing

[001-129] him in silence and looking inquiringly at him. She was a diminutive,

[001-130] withered up old woman of sixty, with sharp malignant eyes and a sharp

[001-131] little nose. Her colourless, somewhat grizzled hair was thickly smeared

[001-132] with oil, and she wore no kerchief over it. Round her thin long neck,

[001-133] which looked like a hen’s leg, was knotted some sort of flannel rag,

[001-134] and, in spite of the heat, there hung flapping on her shoulders, a mangy

[001-135] fur cape, yellow with age. The old woman coughed and groaned at every

[001-136] instant. The young man must have looked at her with a rather peculiar

[001-137] expression, for a gleam of mistrust came into her eyes again.

[001-138] “[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], a student, I came here a month ago,” the young man made

[001-139] haste to mutter, with a half bow, remembering that he ought to be more

[001-140] polite.

[001-141] “I remember, my good sir, I remember quite well your coming here,” the

[001-142] old woman said distinctly, still keeping her inquiring eyes on his face.

[001-143] “And here... I am again on the same errand,” Raskolnikov continued, a

[001-144] little disconcerted and surprised at the old woman’s mistrust. “Perhaps

[001-145] she is always like that though, only I did not notice it the other

[001-146] time,” he thought with an uneasy feeling.

[001-147] The old woman paused, as though hesitating; then stepped on one side,

[001-148] and pointing to the door of the room, she said, letting her visitor pass

[001-149] in front of her:

[001-150] “Step in, my good sir.”

[001-151] The little room into which the young man walked, with yellow paper on

[001-152] the walls, geraniums and muslin curtains in the windows, was brightly

[001-153] lighted up at that moment by the setting sun.

[001-154] “So the sun will shine like this _then_ too!” flashed as it were by

[001-155] [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] through Raskolnikov’s mind, and with a rapid glance he scanned

[001-156] everything in the room, trying as far as possible to notice and

[001-157] remember its arrangement. But there was nothing special in the room. The

[001-158] furniture, all very old and of yellow wood, consisted of a sofa with

[001-159] a huge bent wooden back, an oval table in front of the sofa, a

[001-160] dressing-table with a looking-glass fixed on it between the windows,

[001-161] chairs along the walls and two or three half-penny prints in yellow

[001-162] frames, representing German damsels with birds in their hands--that was

[001-163] all. In the corner a light was burning before a small ikon. Everything

[001-164] was very clean; the floor and the furniture were brightly polished;

[001-165] everything shone.

[001-166] “[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]’s work,” thought the young man. There was not a speck of dust

[001-167] to be seen in the whole flat.

[001-168] “It’s in the houses of spiteful old widows that one finds such

[001-169] cleanliness,” Raskolnikov thought again, and he stole a curious glance

[001-170] at the cotton curtain over the door leading into another tiny room, in

[001-171] which stood the old woman’s bed and chest of drawers and into which he

[001-172] had never looked before. These two rooms made up the whole flat.

[001-173] “What do you want?” the old woman said severely, coming into the room

[001-174] and, as before, standing in front of him so as to look him straight in

[001-175] the face.

[001-176] “I’ve brought something to pawn here,” and he drew out of his pocket

[001-177] an old-fashioned flat silver watch, on the back of which was engraved a

[001-178] globe; the chain was of steel.

[001-179] “But the time is up for your last pledge. The month was up the day

[001-180] before yesterday.”

[001-181] “I will bring you the interest for another month; wait a little.”

[001-182] “But that’s for me to do as I please, my good sir, to wait or to sell

[001-183] your pledge at once.”

[001-184] “How much will you give me for the watch, Alyona Ivanovna?”

[001-185] “You come with such trifles, my good sir, it’s scarcely worth anything.

[001-186] I gave you two roubles last time for your ring and one could buy it

[001-187] quite new at a jeweler’s for a rouble and a half.”

[001-188] “Give me four roubles for it, I shall redeem it, it was my father’s. I

[001-189] shall be getting some money soon.”

[001-190] “A rouble and a half, and interest in advance, if you like!”

[001-191] “A rouble and a half!” cried the young man.

[001-192] “Please yourself”--and the old woman handed him back the watch. The

[001-193] young man took it, and was so angry that he was on the point of going

[001-194] away; but checked himself at once, remembering that there was nowhere

[001-195] else he could go, and that he had had another object also in coming.

[001-196] “Hand it over,” he said roughly.

[001-197] The old woman fumbled in her pocket for her keys, and disappeared behind

[001-198] the curtain into the other room. The young man, left standing alone in

[001-199] the middle of the room, listened inquisitively, thinking. He could hear

[001-200] her unlocking the chest of drawers.

[001-201] “It must be the top drawer,” he reflected. “So she carries the keys in

[001-202] a pocket on the right. All in one bunch on a steel ring.... And there’s

[001-203] one key there, three times as big as all the others, with deep notches;

[001-204] that can’t be the key of the chest of drawers... then there must be some

[001-205] other chest or strong-box... that’s worth knowing. Strong-boxes always

[001-206] have keys like that... but how degrading it all is.”

[001-207] The old woman came back.

[001-208] “Here, sir: as we say ten copecks the rouble a month, so I must take

[001-209] fifteen copecks from a rouble and a half for the month in advance. But

[001-210] for the two roubles I lent you before, you owe me now twenty copecks

[001-211] on the same reckoning in advance. That makes thirty-five copecks

[001-212] altogether. So I must give you a rouble and fifteen copecks for the

[001-213] watch. Here it is.”

[001-214] “What! only a rouble and fifteen copecks now!”

[001-215] “Just so.”

[001-216] The young man did not dispute it and took the money. He looked at the

[001-217] old woman, and was in no hurry to get away, as though there was still

[001-218] something he wanted to say or to do, but he did not himself quite know

[001-219] what.

[001-220] “I may be bringing you something else in a day or two, Alyona

[001-221] Ivanovna--a valuable thing--silver--a cigarette-box, as soon as I get it

[001-222] back from a friend...” he broke off in confusion.

[001-223] “Well, we will talk about it then, sir.”

[001-224] “Good-bye--are you always at home alone, your sister is not here with

[001-225] you?” He asked her as casually as possible as he went out into the

[001-226] passage.

[001-227] “What business is she of yours, my good sir?”

[001-228] “Oh, nothing particular, I simply asked. You are too quick.... Good-day,

[001-229] Alyona Ivanovna.”

[001-230] Raskolnikov went out in complete confusion. This confusion became more

[001-231] and more intense. As he went down the stairs, he even stopped short, two

[001-232] or three times, as though suddenly struck by some thought. When he was

[001-233] in the street he cried out, “Oh, God, how loathsome it all is! and

[001-234] can I, can I possibly.... No, it’s nonsense, it’s rubbish!” he added

[001-235] resolutely. “And how could such an atrocious thing come into my head?

[001-236] What filthy things my heart is capable of. Yes, filthy above all,

[001-237] disgusting, loathsome, loathsome!--and for a whole month I’ve been....”

[001-238] But no words, no exclamations, could express his agitation. The feeling

[001-239] of intense repulsion, which had begun to oppress and torture his heart

[001-240] while he was on his way to the old woman, had by now reached such a

[001-241] pitch and had taken such a definite form that he did not know what to

[001-242] do with himself to escape from his wretchedness. He walked along the

[001-243] pavement like a drunken man, regardless of the passers-by, and jostling

[001-244] against them, and only came to his senses when he was in the next

[001-245] street. Looking round, he noticed that he was standing close to a tavern

[001-246] which was entered by steps leading from the pavement to the basement.

[001-247] At that instant two drunken men came out at the door, and abusing and

[001-248] supporting one another, they mounted the steps. Without stopping to

[001-249] think, Raskolnikov went down the steps at once. Till that moment he had

[001-250] never been into a tavern, but now he felt giddy and was tormented by a

[001-251] burning thirst. He longed for a drink of cold beer, and attributed his

[001-252] sudden weakness to the want of food. He sat down at a sticky little

[001-253] table in a dark and dirty corner; ordered some beer, and eagerly drank

[001-254] off the first glassful. At once he felt easier; and his thoughts became

[001-255] clear.

[001-256] “All that’s nonsense,” he said hopefully, “and there is nothing in it

[001-257] all to worry about! It’s simply physical derangement. Just a glass of

[001-258] beer, a piece of dry bread--and in one moment the brain is stronger,

[001-259] the mind is clearer and the will is firm! Phew, how utterly petty it all

[001-260] is!”

[001-261] But in spite of this scornful reflection, he was by now looking cheerful

[001-262] as though he were suddenly set free from a terrible burden: and he gazed

[001-263] round in a friendly way at the people in the room. But even at that

[001-264] moment he had a dim foreboding that this happier frame of mind was also

[001-265] not normal.

[001-266] There were few people at the time in the tavern. Besides the two drunken

[001-267] men he had met on the steps, a group consisting of about five men and

[001-268] a girl with a concertina had gone out at the same time. Their departure

[001-269] left the room quiet and rather empty. The persons still in the tavern

[001-270] were a man who appeared to be an artisan, drunk, but not extremely so,

[001-271] sitting before a pot of beer, and his companion, a huge, stout man with

[001-272] a grey beard, in a short full-skirted coat. He was very drunk: and had

[001-273] dropped asleep on the bench; every now and then, he began as though in

[001-274] his sleep, cracking his fingers, with his arms wide apart and the upper

[001-275] part of his body bounding about on the bench, while he hummed some

[001-276] meaningless refrain, trying to recall some such lines as these:

[001-277] “His wife a year he fondly loved

[001-278] His wife a--a year he--fondly loved.”

[001-279] Or suddenly waking up again:

[001-280] “Walking along the crowded row

[001-281] He met the one he used to know.”

[001-282] But no one shared his enjoyment: his silent companion looked with

[001-283] positive hostility and mistrust at all these manifestations. There was

[001-284] another man in the room who looked somewhat like a retired government

[001-285] clerk. He was sitting apart, now and then sipping from his pot and

[001-286] looking round at the company. He, too, appeared to be in some agitation.
