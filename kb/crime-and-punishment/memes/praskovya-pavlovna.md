# Praskovya Pavlovna

Praskovya Pavlovna is [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s landlady at the lodging-house in S. Place. Though she never appears directly in the novel, her presence is felt through [[nastasya|Nastasya]]'s reports of her complaints about unpaid rent and through Raskolnikov's fear that she will "complain to the police" (003-049) about his debts. She represents the economic pressures that bear down on Raskolnikov even before the murder — the material reality that his intellectual preoccupations cannot escape.

## Overview

Praskovya Pavlovna owns the building where Raskolnikov rents his tiny [[raskolnikovs-garret|garret]]. Her cook Nastasya serves as intermediary between landlady and lodger, reporting that "Praskovya Pavlovna means to complain to the police" about Raskolnikov's unpaid rent (003-049). In the novel's opening pages, Raskolnikov is acutely aware of this threat, and his debt to her is one of the first details that establishes his economic desperation.

## Role in the Novel

Praskovya Pavlovna exists entirely offstage. Her function is to embody the impersonal economic relations that govern Raskolnikov's life — the rent that must be paid, the threats that must be evaded. She is the system, not a character, and her absence from the novel's action reflects Raskolnikov's isolation: he has no personal relationship even with the woman whose roof he sleeps under.

## Key Relationships

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]**: Her lodger, deeply in arrears on rent
- **[[nastasya|Nastasya]]**: Her cook and servant, who communicates the landlady's threats

## Key Quotes

> "Praskovya Pavlovna means to complain to the police." — Nastasya, relaying the landlady's threat (003-049)
