# Raskolnikov's Garret

Raskolnikov's room is more coffin than dwelling — "a tiny cupboard of a room about six paces in length" with "a poverty-stricken appearance" (003-004), located under the roof of a five-story building. The room's dimensions mirror Raskolnikov's psychological state: cramped, airless, cut off from the life of the city below. It is the novel's most important interior space — the site where theory becomes crime, where illness incubates, and where confession is finally made possible.

## Description

The garret is described as "more like a cupboard than a room" (001-003), with yellow, dusty wallpaper peeling from the walls, a sofa that takes up half the floor space, a low ceiling that forces even someone of average height to stoop, and three old chairs — one of which has a broken leg. The furnishings are "three old chairs, not quite sound, a painted table in the corner, and a large, clumsy sofa that occupied almost the whole of one wall and half the floor-space of the room" (001-004).

The room's most striking feature is its suffocating smallness. "He was crushed by poverty" (001-016), and the room crushes him in turn. The low ceiling — "you could strike your head against it on standing up" (020-008) — is a constant physical reminder of constriction. Dostoevsky uses the garret to literalize Raskolnikov's mental state: his ideas, like his body, have no room to breathe.

## The Room as Psychological Incubator

The garret is where Raskolnikov's theory gestates. It is here that he first overhears the student and officer discussing the pawnbroker, here that he "lay for whole days in his corner, thinking" (001-030), here that his "dream of the horse" visits him with its premonitory horror, and here that he rehearses the murder in his mind until the idea becomes action. The room's isolation from the street below — "he had got into the habit of avoiding his landlady" (001-001) — makes it a sealed chamber for the incubation of violence.

After the murder, the garret becomes a fever-room. Raskolnikov collapses there and descends into days of delirium (Part 2, Chapters 1–3), during which he nearly confesses to [[zametov|Zametov]] and raves about blood. The room is now a prison as much as a refuge: he is "afraid of being alone" yet unable to leave.

## Key Scenes in the Garret

### Razumihin's Visit (Part 2, Chapter 3)

[[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] finds Raskolnikov in the grip of fever and becomes his caretaker, bringing food, a new set of clothes, and the doctor [[zossimov]]. Razumihin's practical decency — he sits with Raskolnikov through his illness, untangles his financial affairs, and never asks uncomfortable questions — provides the novel's most sustained portrait of fraternal loyalty. The garret, under Razumihin's care, briefly becomes a space of nurture rather than isolation.

### Sonia's Visit (Part 4, Chapter 4)

[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] visits the garret before the Lazarus reading, and the contrast between her presence and the room's usual atmosphere is striking. "She looked almost like a young girl, much younger than her age" (024-002), and her gentleness makes the garret feel, for a moment, less like a trap. The meeting in the garret sets the terms for all that follows: Raskolnikov alternately torments and confides in her, testing whether she will condemn or absolve him.

### Porfiry's Interrogation (Part 4, Chapter 5)

Although the formal interrogations take place at the police station, [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry]] makes a crucial visit to the garret in Part 6, Chapter 2. Here, in Raskolnikov's own space, the detective delivers his psychological masterstroke — telling Raskolnikov he knows he is the murderer, that he has no evidence, and that he is giving him "a day or two more" to confess voluntarily (033-039). The garret, which has been Raskolnikov's fortress of isolation, becomes the space where his psychological defenses are dismantled.

### The Confession to Sonia (Part 5, Chapter 4)

Raskolnikov confesses the murders to Sonia not in his garret but in her room. The movement between these two interior spaces — from his garret of isolation to her room of faith — maps the novel's moral geography.

## Symbolic Meaning

The garret is the architectural expression of Raskolnikov's intellectual pride. He chose this isolation; he "avoided everyone" (001-002) and cultivated his theory in solitude. The room is simultaneously a womb (where the idea gestates), a tomb (where his humanity is buried), and a chrysalis (from which a transformed self might emerge). Its position under the roof, above the life of the city, parallels Raskolnikov's self-positioning above ordinary morality.

Dostoevsky contrasts the garret with other spaces: [[sonias-room|Sonia's room]], equally poor but open to others; the [[crystal-palace-restaurant]], where ideas are debated openly rather than nurtured in isolation; and the Siberian prison, where Raskolnikov finally finds the community his garret denied him.
