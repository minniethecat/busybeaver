[007-001] The door was as before opened a tiny crack, and again two sharp and

[007-002] suspicious eyes stared at him out of the darkness. Then [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] lost

[007-003] his head and nearly made a great mistake.

[007-004] Fearing [[alyona-ivanovna|the old woman]] would be frightened by their being alone, and not

[007-005] hoping that the sight of him would disarm her suspicions, he took

[007-006] hold of the door and drew it towards him to prevent the old woman from

[007-007] attempting to shut it again. Seeing this she did not pull the door back,

[007-009] it on to the [[staircases|stairs]]. Seeing that she was standing in the doorway not

[007-010] allowing him to pass, he advanced straight upon her. She stepped back

[007-011] in alarm, tried to say something, but seemed unable to speak and stared

[007-012] with open eyes at him.

[007-014] his voice would not obey him, it broke and shook. “I have come... I have

[007-015] brought something... but we’d better come in... to the light....”

[007-016] And leaving her, he passed straight into the room uninvited. The old

[007-017] woman ran after him; her tongue was unloosed.

[007-020] the pledge I promised the other day...” And he held out the pledge.

[007-023] mistrustfully. A minute passed; he even fancied something like a sneer

[007-025] he was losing his head, that he was almost frightened, so frightened

[007-379] At last he reached the turning. He turned down it more dead than alive.

[007-380] Here he was half way to safety, and he understood it; it was less risky

[007-381] because there was a great crowd of people, and he was lost in it like a

[007-382] grain of sand. But all he had suffered had so weakened him that he could

[007-383] scarcely move. Perspiration ran down him in drops, his neck was all wet.

[007-384] “My word, he has been going it!” someone shouted at him when he came out

[007-385] on [[catherine-canal|the canal]] bank.

[007-386] He was only dimly conscious of himself now, and the farther he went the

[007-387] worse it was. He remembered however, that on coming out on to the canal

[007-388] bank, he was alarmed at finding few people there and so being more

[007-389] conspicuous, and he had thought of turning back. Though he was almost

[007-390] falling from fatigue, he went a long way round so as to get home from

[007-391] quite a different direction.

[007-392] He was not fully conscious when he passed through the gateway of his

[007-393] house! He was already on the staircase before he recollected the axe.

[007-394] And yet he had a very grave problem before him, to put it back and to

[007-395] escape observation as far as possible in doing so. He was of course

[007-396] incapable of reflecting that it might perhaps be far better not to

[007-397] restore the axe at all, but to drop it later on in somebody’s yard. But

[007-398] it all happened fortunately, the door of the porter’s room was closed

[007-399] but not locked, so that it seemed most likely that the porter was at

[007-400] home. But he had so completely lost all power of reflection that he

[007-401] walked straight to the door and opened it. If the porter had asked him,

[007-402] “What do you want?” he would perhaps have simply handed him the axe. But

[007-403] again the porter was not at home, and he succeeded in putting the axe

[007-404] back under the bench, and even covering it with the chunk of wood as

[007-405] before. He met no one, not a soul, afterwards on the way to his room;

[007-406] [[praskovya-pavlovna|the landlady]]’s door was shut. When he was in his room, he flung himself

[007-407] on the sofa just as he was--he did not sleep, but sank into blank

[007-408] forgetfulness. If anyone had come into his room then, he would have

[007-410] simply swarming in his brain, but he could not catch at one, he could

[007-411] not rest on one, in spite of all his efforts....
