# Utilitarian Rationalism

The novel opens with a utilitarian argument — overheard by Raskolnikov in a tavern — that killing the pawnbroker would be morally justified because her wealth, redistributed, would generate net social good. This "simple arithmetic" (006-099) is the ideology Raskolnikov attempts to live by, and whose collapse constitutes the novel's moral argument.

## Definition

Utilitarian rationalism, as presented in the novel, is the belief that moral decisions can be reduced to a calculus of costs and benefits: "one death, and a hundred lives in exchange — it's simple arithmetic!" (006-099). The pawnbroker is "a louse" whose life has "no more than the life of a louse, of a black-beetle, less in fact" (006-101) compared to the good her money could do. This ideology strips moral reasoning of its psychological and spiritual dimensions, treating human lives as commensurable units in a utilitarian equation.

## Expression in the Novel

The tavern conversation (Part 1, Chapter 6) presents the utilitarian argument in its purest form: a student argues that Alyona Ivanovna's death would benefit society, that her money could fund hundreds of charitable acts, and that there is therefore no genuine moral objection to killing her. Raskolnikov overhears this conversation at the very moment he is contemplating the murder himself — the external voice giving form to his internal rationalization. After the murder, Raskolnikov's anguished self-assessment — "I am an æsthetic louse and nothing more" (020-243), "utterly a louse" (020-255), "more loathsome than the louse I killed" (020-256) — turns the utilitarian metaphor against itself. The arithmetic fails because it cannot account for conscience. As he tells Sonia: "I've only killed a louse, Sonia, a useless, loathsome, harmful louse" (030-370), to which she responds with horror: "A human being — a louse!" (030-372). This exchange crystallizes the novel's rejection of utilitarian calculus: human beings are not commensurable units, and the attempt to treat them as such destroys the calculator as much as the calculated.
