# Dounia Raskolnikov

Avdotya (Dounia) Romanovna Raskolnikov is Raskolnikov's younger sister — beautiful, intelligent, proud, and, as the novel gradually reveals, far stronger than her brother. Her trajectory from contemplated self-sacrifice through disillusionment to self-possession forms one of the novel's most compelling arcs.

## Overview

Dounia is described as "remarkably good-looking — tall, strikingly so, strong and self-confident" (015-230). She has her brother's intensity without his morbidity, his pride without his self-destructiveness. Where Raskolnikov theorizes about transgression, Dounia acts decisively — most dramatically when she shoots Svidrigailov, refusing to be his victim.

## Background

Dounia worked as a governess in the Svidrigailov household in the provinces, where Marfa Petrovna, Svidrigailov's wife, spread malicious rumors about her. Svidrigailov's obsession with her — and her rejection of him — forms a crucial subplot. She agrees to marry Pyotr Luzhin, a wealthy lawyer, to secure her brother's future — a decision Raskolnikov vehemently opposes.

## Role in the Novel

Dounia arrives in St. Petersburg with her mother, intending to marry Luzhin. Raskolnikov exposes Luzhin's true character — petty, controlling, and cruel — and Dounia breaks the engagement. She then faces Svidrigailov, who has followed her to Petersburg. In their final confrontation, she shoots at him twice, grazing his temple, before dropping the revolver. Svidrigailov, recognizing the finality of her rejection, releases her and commits suicide. Dounia's courage in this scene — facing a man who holds her entirely in his power — is the novel's purest example of moral action unmediated by theory.

## Key Relationships

- **Rodion Raskolnikov**: Her brother, whom she loves and for whom she was willing to sacrifice herself. Her strength exposes his weakness.
- **Pyotr Luzhin**: Her would-be fiancé, whose pettiness she recognizes and rejects.
- **Arkady Svidrigailov**: Her former employer who pursues her obsessively. Their final confrontation is the novel's most suspenseful scene.
- **Pulcheria Alexandrovna**: Her mother, with whom she shares the burden of family care.

## Psychological Profile

Dounia's psychology is characterized by pride, intelligence, and moral clarity. She shares Raskolnikov's "haughty pride" (004-387) but channels it into self-respect rather than self-destruction. Her decisions — to break with Luzhin, to shoot Svidrigailov — are made quickly and without the agonized self-examination that paralyzes her brother.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Dounia represents the possibility of strong moral agency without the corrupting influence of ideology. She embodies the novel's argument that moral clarity is accessible through action and love, not theory.

## Key Quotes

> "I won't have your sacrifice, Dounia, I won't have it so long as I am living." — Raskolnikov to Dounia (004-153)

> "She has of her own free will deigned to accept this man." (016-256)
