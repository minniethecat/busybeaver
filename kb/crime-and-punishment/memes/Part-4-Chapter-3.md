[023-001] The fact was that up to the last moment he had never expected such an

[023-002] ending; he had been overbearing to the last degree, never dreaming that

[023-003] two destitute and defenceless women could escape from his control. This

[023-004] conviction was strengthened by his vanity and conceit, a conceit to

[023-005] the point of fatuity. Pyotr Petrovitch, who had made his way up from

[023-006] insignificance, was morbidly given to self-admiration, had the highest

[023-007] opinion of his intelligence and capacities, and sometimes even gloated

[023-008] in solitude over his image in the glass. But what he loved and valued

[023-009] above all was the money he had amassed by his labour, and by all sorts

[023-010] of devices: that money made him the equal of all who had been his

[023-011] superiors.

[023-012] When he had bitterly reminded [[dounia-raskolnikov|Dounia]] that he had decided to take her in

[023-013] spite of evil report, Pyotr Petrovitch had spoken with perfect sincerity

[023-014] and had, indeed, felt genuinely indignant at such “black ingratitude.”

[023-015] And yet, when he made Dounia his offer, he was fully aware of the

[023-016] groundlessness of all the gossip. The story had been everywhere

[023-017] contradicted by [[marfa-petrovna|Marfa Petrovna]], and was by then disbelieved by all the

[023-018] townspeople, who were warm in Dounia’s defence. And he would not have

[023-019] denied that he knew all that at the time. Yet he still thought highly

[023-020] of his own resolution in lifting Dounia to his level and regarded it as

[023-021] something heroic. In speaking of it to Dounia, he had let out the secret

[023-022] feeling he cherished and admired, and he could not understand that

[023-023] others should fail to admire it too. He had called on [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] with

[023-024] the feelings of a benefactor who is about to reap the fruits of his good

[023-025] deeds and to hear agreeable flattery. And as he went downstairs now, he

[023-026] considered himself most undeservedly injured and unrecognised.

[023-027] Dounia was simply essential to him; to do without her was unthinkable.

[023-028] For many years he had had voluptuous dreams of marriage, but he had

[023-029] gone on waiting and amassing money. He brooded with relish, in profound

[023-030] secret, over the image of a girl--virtuous, poor (she must be poor),

[023-031] very young, very pretty, of good birth and education, very timid, one

[023-032] who had suffered much, and was completely humbled before him, one who

[023-033] would all her life look on him as her saviour, worship him, admire him

[023-034] and only him. How many scenes, how many amorous episodes he had imagined

[023-035] on this seductive and playful theme, when his work was over! And,

[023-036] behold, the dream of so many years was all but realised; the beauty and

[023-037] education of Avdotya Romanovna had impressed him; her helpless position

[023-038] had been a great allurement; in her he had found even more than he

[023-039] dreamed of. Here was a girl of pride, character, virtue, of education

[023-040] and breeding superior to his own (he felt that), and this creature would

[023-041] be slavishly grateful all her life for his heroic condescension, and

[023-042] would humble herself in the dust before him, and he would have absolute,

[023-043] unbounded power over her!... Not long before, he had, too, after long

[023-044] reflection and hesitation, made an important change in his career and

[023-045] was now entering on a wider circle of business. With this change his

[023-046] cherished dreams of rising into a higher class of society seemed likely

[023-047] to be realised.... He was, in fact, determined to try his fortune

[023-048] in [[st-petersburg|Petersburg]]. He knew that women could do a very great deal. The

[023-049] fascination of a charming, virtuous, highly educated woman might make

[023-050] his way easier, might do wonders in attracting people to him, throwing

[023-051] an aureole round him, and now everything was in ruins! This sudden

[023-052] horrible rupture affected him like a clap of thunder; it was like a

[023-053] hideous joke, an absurdity. He had only been a tiny bit masterful,

[023-054] had not even time to speak out, had simply made a joke, been carried

[023-055] away--and it had ended so seriously. And, of course, too, he did love

[023-056] Dounia in his own way; he already possessed her in his dreams--and all

[023-057] at once! No! The next day, the very next day, it must all be set right,

[023-058] smoothed over, settled. Above all he must crush that conceited milksop

[023-059] who was the cause of it all. With a sick feeling he could not help

[023-060] recalling [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] too, but, he soon reassured himself on that score;

[023-061] as though a fellow like that could be put on a level with him! The man

[023-062] he really dreaded in earnest was Svidrigaïlov.... He had, in short, a

[023-063] great deal to attend to....

[023-065] “No, I, I am more to blame than anyone!” said Dounia, kissing and

[023-066] embracing her mother. “I was tempted by his money, but on my honour,

[023-067] brother, I had no idea he was such a base man. If I had seen through him

[023-068] before, nothing would have tempted me! Don’t blame me, brother!”

[023-069] “God has delivered us! God has delivered us!” [[pulcheria-alexandrovna|Pulcheria Alexandrovna]]

[023-070] muttered, but half consciously, as though scarcely able to realise what

[023-071] had happened.

[023-072] They were all relieved, and in five minutes they were laughing. Only now

[023-073] and then Dounia turned white and frowned, remembering what had passed.

[023-074] Pulcheria Alexandrovna was surprised to find that she, too, was glad:

[023-075] she had only that morning thought rupture with Luzhin a terrible

[023-076] misfortune. Razumihin was delighted. He did not yet dare to express his

[023-077] joy fully, but he was in a [[illness-and-delirium|fever]] of excitement as though a ton-weight

[023-078] had fallen off his heart. Now he had the right to devote his life to

[023-079] them, to serve them.... Anything might happen now! But he felt afraid to

[023-080] think of further possibilities and dared not let his imagination

[023-081] range. But Raskolnikov sat still in the same place, almost sullen and

[023-082] indifferent. Though he had been the most insistent on getting rid of

[023-083] Luzhin, he seemed now the least concerned at what had happened. Dounia

[023-084] could not help thinking that he was still angry with her, and Pulcheria

[023-085] Alexandrovna watched him timidly.

[023-086] “What did Svidrigaïlov say to you?” said Dounia, approaching him.

[023-087] “Yes, yes!” cried Pulcheria Alexandrovna.

[023-088] Raskolnikov raised his head.

[023-089] “He wants to make you a present of ten thousand roubles and he desires

[023-090] to see you once in my presence.”

[023-091] “See her! On no account!” cried Pulcheria Alexandrovna. “And how dare he

[023-092] offer her money!”

[023-093] Then Raskolnikov repeated (rather dryly) his conversation with

[023-094] Svidrigaïlov, omitting his account of the ghostly visitations of Marfa

[023-095] Petrovna, wishing to avoid all unnecessary talk.

[023-096] “What answer did you give him?” asked Dounia.

[023-097] “At first I said I would not take any message to you. Then he said that

[023-098] he would do his utmost to obtain an interview with you without my help.

[023-099] He assured me that his passion for you was a passing infatuation, now he

[023-100] has no feeling for you. He doesn’t want you to marry Luzhin.... His talk

[023-101] was altogether rather muddled.”

[023-102] “How do you explain him to yourself, Rodya? How did he strike you?”

[023-103] “I must confess I don’t quite understand him. He offers you ten

[023-104] thousand, and yet says he is not well off. He says he is going away, and

[023-105] in ten minutes he forgets he has said it. Then he says he is going to be

[023-106] married and has already fixed on the girl.... No doubt he has a motive,

[023-107] and probably a bad one. But it’s odd that he should be so clumsy about

[023-108] it if he had any designs against you.... Of course, I refused this

[023-109] money on your account, once for all. Altogether, I thought him very

[023-110] strange.... One might almost think he was mad. But I may be mistaken;

[023-111] that may only be the part he assumes. The death of Marfa Petrovna seems

[023-112] to have made a great impression on him.”

[023-113] “God rest her soul,” exclaimed Pulcheria Alexandrovna. “I shall always,

[023-114] always pray for her! Where should we be now, Dounia, without this three

[023-115] thousand! It’s as though it had fallen from heaven! Why, Rodya, this

[023-116] morning we had only three roubles in our pocket and Dounia and I were

[023-117] just planning to pawn her watch, so as to avoid borrowing from that man

[023-118] until he offered help.”

[023-119] Dounia seemed strangely impressed by Svidrigaïlov’s offer. She still

[023-120] stood meditating.

[023-121] “He has got some terrible plan,” she said in a half whisper to herself,

[023-122] almost shuddering.

[023-123] Raskolnikov noticed this disproportionate terror.

[023-124] “I fancy I shall have to see him more than once again,” he said to

[023-125] Dounia.

[023-126] “We will watch him! I will track him out!” cried Razumihin, vigorously.

[023-127] “I won’t lose sight of him. Rodya has given me leave. He said to me

[023-128] himself just now. ‘Take care of my sister.’ Will you give me leave, too,

[023-129] Avdotya Romanovna?”

[023-130] Dounia smiled and held out her hand, but the look of anxiety did not

[023-131] leave her face. Pulcheria Alexandrovna gazed at her timidly, but the

[023-132] three thousand roubles had obviously a soothing effect on her.

[023-133] A quarter of an hour later, they were all engaged in a lively

[023-134] conversation. Even Raskolnikov listened attentively for some time,

[023-135] though he did not talk. Razumihin was the speaker.

[023-136] “And why, why should you go away?” he flowed on ecstatically. “And what

[023-137] are you to do in a little town? The great thing is, you are all here

[023-138] together and you need one another--you do need one another, believe me.

[023-139] For a time, anyway.... Take me into partnership, and I assure you we’ll

[023-140] plan a capital enterprise. Listen! I’ll explain it all in detail to

[023-141] you, the whole project! It all flashed into my head this morning,

[023-142] before anything had happened... I tell you what; I have an uncle, I must

[023-143] introduce him to you (a most accommodating and respectable old man).

[023-144] This uncle has got a capital of a thousand roubles, and he lives on his

[023-145] pension and has no need of that money. For the last two years he has

[023-146] been bothering me to borrow it from him and pay him six per cent

[023-147] interest. I know what that means; he simply wants to help me. Last year

[023-148] I had no need of it, but this year I resolved to borrow it as soon as

[023-149] he arrived. Then you lend me another thousand of your three and we have

[023-150] enough for a start, so we’ll go into partnership, and what are we going

[023-151] to do?”

[023-152] Then Razumihin began to unfold his project, and he explained at length

[023-153] that almost all our publishers and booksellers know nothing at all

[023-154] of what they are selling, and for that reason they are usually bad

[023-155] publishers, and that any decent publications pay as a rule and give

[023-156] a profit, sometimes a considerable one. Razumihin had, indeed, been

[023-157] dreaming of setting up as a publisher. For the last two years he had

[023-158] been working in publishers’ offices, and knew three European languages

[023-159] well, though he had told Raskolnikov six days before that he was

[023-160] “schwach” in German with an object of persuading him to take half his

[023-161] translation and half the payment for it. He had told a lie then, and

[023-162] Raskolnikov knew he was lying.

[023-163] “Why, why should we let our [[fate-and-coincidence|chance]] slip when we have one of the chief

[023-164] means of success--money of our own!” cried Razumihin warmly. “Of course

[023-165] there will be a lot of work, but we will work, you, Avdotya Romanovna,

[023-166] I, Rodion.... You get a splendid profit on some books nowadays! And

[023-167] the great point of the business is that we shall know just what wants

[023-168] translating, and we shall be translating, publishing, learning all at

[023-169] once. I can be of use because I have experience. For nearly two years

[023-170] I’ve been scuttling about among the publishers, and now I know every

[023-171] detail of their business. You need not be a saint to make pots, believe

[023-172] me! And why, why should we let our chance slip! Why, I know--and I kept

[023-173] the secret--two or three books which one might get a hundred roubles

[023-174] simply for thinking of translating and publishing. Indeed, and I would

[023-175] not take five hundred for the very idea of one of them. And what do you

[023-176] think? If I were to tell a publisher, I dare say he’d hesitate--they are

[023-177] such blockheads! And as for the business side, printing, paper, selling,

[023-178] you trust to me, I know my way about. We’ll begin in a small way and go

[023-179] on to a large. In any case it will get us our living and we shall get

[023-180] back our capital.”

[023-181] Dounia’s eyes shone.

[023-182] “I like what you are saying, Dmitri Prokofitch!” she said.

[023-183] “I know nothing about it, of course,” put in Pulcheria Alexandrovna,

[023-184] “it may be a good idea, but again God knows. It’s new and untried. Of

[023-185] course, we must remain here at least for a time.” She looked at Rodya.

[023-186] “What do you think, brother?” said Dounia.

[023-187] “I think he’s got a very good idea,” he answered. “Of course, it’s too

[023-188] soon to dream of a publishing firm, but we certainly might bring out

[023-189] five or six books and be sure of success. I know of one book myself

[023-190] which would be sure to go well. And as for his being able to manage it,

[023-191] there’s no doubt about that either. He knows the business.... But we can

[023-192] talk it over later....”

[023-193] “Hurrah!” cried Razumihin. “Now, stay, there’s a flat here in this

[023-194] house, belonging to the same owner. It’s a special flat apart, not

[023-195] communicating with these lodgings. It’s furnished, rent moderate,

[023-196] three rooms. Suppose you take them to begin with. I’ll pawn your watch

[023-197] to-morrow and bring you the money, and everything can be arranged then.

[023-198] You can all three live together, and Rodya will be with you. But where

[023-199] are you off to, Rodya?”

[023-200] “What, Rodya, you are going already?” Pulcheria Alexandrovna asked in

[023-201] dismay.

[023-202] “At such a minute?” cried Razumihin.

[023-203] Dounia looked at her brother with incredulous wonder. He held his cap in

[023-204] his hand, he was preparing to leave them.

[023-205] “One would think you were burying me or saying good-bye for ever,” he

[023-206] said somewhat oddly. He attempted to smile, but it did not turn out a

[023-207] smile. “But who knows, perhaps it is the last time we shall see each

[023-208] other...” he let slip accidentally. It was what he was thinking, and it

[023-209] somehow was uttered aloud.

[023-210] “What is the matter with you?” cried his mother.

[023-211] “Where are you going, Rodya?” asked Dounia rather strangely.

[023-212] “Oh, I’m quite obliged to...” he answered vaguely, as though hesitating

[023-213] what he would say. But there was a look of sharp determination in his

[023-214] white face.

[023-215] “I meant to say... as I was coming here... I meant to tell you, mother,

[023-216] and you, Dounia, that it would be better for us to part for a time. I

[023-217] feel ill, I am not at peace.... I will come afterwards, I will come of

[023-218] myself... when it’s possible. I remember you and love you.... Leave me,

[023-219] leave me alone. I decided this even before... I’m absolutely resolved on

[023-220] it. Whatever may come to me, whether I come to ruin or not, I want to be

[023-221] alone. Forget me altogether, it’s better. Don’t inquire about me. When

[023-222] I can, I’ll come of myself or... I’ll send for you. Perhaps it will all

[023-223] come back, but now if you love me, give me up... else I shall begin to

[023-224] hate you, I feel it.... Good-bye!”

[023-225] “Good God!” cried Pulcheria Alexandrovna. Both his mother and his sister

[023-226] were terribly alarmed. Razumihin was also.

[023-227] “Rodya, Rodya, be reconciled with us! Let us be as before!” cried his

[023-228] poor mother.

[023-229] He turned slowly to the door and slowly went out of the room. Dounia

[023-230] overtook him.

[023-231] “Brother, what are you doing to mother?” she whispered, her eyes

[023-232] flashing with indignation.

[023-233] He looked dully at her.

[023-234] “No matter, I shall come.... I’m coming,” he muttered in an undertone,

[023-235] as though not fully conscious of what he was saying, and he went out of

[023-236] the room.

[023-237] “Wicked, heartless egoist!” cried Dounia.

[023-238] “He is insane, but not heartless. He is mad! Don’t you see it? You’re

[023-239] heartless after that!” Razumihin whispered in her ear, squeezing

[023-240] her hand tightly. “I shall be back directly,” he shouted to the

[023-241] horror-stricken mother, and he ran out of the room.

[023-242] Raskolnikov was waiting for him at the end of the passage.

[023-243] “I knew you would run after me,” he said. “Go back to them--be with

[023-244] them... be with them to-morrow and always.... I... perhaps I shall

[023-245] come... if I can. Good-bye.”

[023-246] And without holding out his hand he walked away.

[023-247] “But where are you going? What are you doing? What’s the matter with

[023-248] you? How can you go on like this?” Razumihin muttered, at his wits’ end.

[023-249] Raskolnikov stopped once more.

[023-250] “Once for all, never ask me about anything. I have nothing to tell you.

[023-251] Don’t come to see me. Maybe I’ll come here.... Leave me, but _don’t

[023-252] leave_ them. Do you understand me?”

[023-253] It was dark in the corridor, they were standing near the lamp. For a

[023-254] minute they were looking at one another in silence. Razumihin remembered

[023-255] that minute all his life. Raskolnikov’s burning and intent eyes

[023-256] grew more penetrating every moment, piercing into his soul, into his

[023-257] consciousness. Suddenly Razumihin started. Something strange, as it

[023-258] were, passed between them.... Some idea, some hint, as it were, slipped,

[023-259] something awful, hideous, and suddenly understood on both sides....

[023-260] Razumihin turned pale.

[023-261] “Do you understand now?” said Raskolnikov, his face twitching nervously.

[023-262] “Go back, go to them,” he said suddenly, and turning quickly, he went

[023-263] out of the house.

[023-264] I will not attempt to describe how Razumihin went back to the ladies,

[023-265] how he soothed them, how he protested that Rodya needed rest in his

[023-266] illness, protested that Rodya was sure to come, that he would come every

[023-267] day, that he was very, very much upset, that he must not be irritated,

[023-268] that he, Razumihin, would watch over him, would get him a doctor, the

[023-269] best doctor, a consultation.... In fact from that evening Razumihin took

[023-270] his place with them as a son and a brother.
