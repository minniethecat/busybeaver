# Petrovsky Island

Petrovsky Island (*Petrovsky Ostrov*) is a wooded island in the Little Neva River in St. Petersburg. In *Crime and Punishment*, it serves as a liminal refuge for characters in states of psychological extremity — first Raskolnikov, in the exhausted haze following the murder, and later Svidrigailov, on the night he takes his own life.

## Raskolnikov's Exhaustion

Shortly after the murder of the pawnbroker, Raskolnikov wanders the city in a dissociated state. Reaching Petrovsky Island, his body gives out entirely:

> …reaching Petrovsky Ostrov he stopped completely exhausted, turned off the road into the bushes, sank down upon the grass and instantly fell asleep. [005-059]

This sleep — deep, dream-filled, and pathological — marks the beginning of Raskolnikov's prolonged physical and mental collapse following the crime. The island, with its bushes and grass, offers an anonymous, non-human respite that the crowded tenements and streets of St. Petersburg cannot.

## Svidrigailov's Final Night

Petrovsky Island is most strongly associated with Arkady Svidrigailov, who wanders there on the night before his suicide. The setting is rendered with a wet, cold, almost tactile bleakness characteristic of Dostoevsky's late style:

> …the waters of the Little Neva swollen in the night, Petrovsky Island, the wet paths, the wet grass, the wet trees and bushes and at last the bush… [037-418]

Svidrigailov's internal monologue as he approaches the island reveals a character treating his own death with ironic detachment. He recalls having passed Petrovsky Park earlier and shivered at the bridge over the Little Neva:

> Petrovsky Park just now. This reminded him of the bridge over the Little Neva and he felt cold again as he had when standing there. "I never have liked water," he thought, "even in a landscape." [037-228]

He mocks his own hesitation with dark humour:

> I ought to have gone into the Petrovsky Park! I suppose it seemed dark, cold, ha-ha! As though I were seeking pleasant sensations! [037-234]

Yet, momentarily, the thought of Petrovsky Island seems to offer a reprieve from his suicidal intent — a place to wait out the storm:

> "Bah!" he shouted, "here is a place. Why should it be Petrovsky? It will be in the presence of an official witness anyway…" [037-430]

He turns away from the island and into the street, heading instead toward the apartment where he will spend his final hours.

## Symbolic Function

Petrovsky Island operates as a threshold space in the novel's symbolic geography. Unlike the crowded Hay Market or the oppressive staircases of tenement buildings, the island is open, vegetal, and bounded by water — elements that in Dostoevsky often signal a character's proximity to spiritual crisis or transformation. That both Raskolnikov and Svidrigailov are drawn here at their lowest points — one toward unconscious oblivion, the other toward conscious self-destruction — makes the island a silent witness to the novel's deepest psychological extremes.
