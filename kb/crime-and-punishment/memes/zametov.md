# Alexandr Grigorievitch Zametov

Alexandr Grigorievitch Zametov is the head clerk at the police bureau where [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is summoned the day after the murder. Young, fashion-conscious, and ambitious, Zametov is one of several figures who circle Raskolnikov with suspicion in the novel's middle sections. His most significant scene — a strange, semi-confessional conversation with Raskolnikov at the Crystal Palace restaurant — functions as a dress rehearsal for Raskolnikov's later interviews with [[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]], establishing the pattern of cat-and-mouse psychological games that drives the novel's detective plot.

## Overview

Zametov is introduced at the police station on the morning after the murder, where Raskolnikov observes "a very young man with dark, mobile eyes, dressed in a fashionable, well-cut coat" (010-194). As head clerk, Zametov occupies a position of minor but real authority — he processes reports, hears gossip, and forms opinions about cases. His suspicion of Raskolnikov is intuitive rather than methodical: he notices Raskolnikov's strange behavior, his fainting fit at the mention of the murder, and his compulsive return to the subject of the crime.

## Background

Zametov is a young civil servant on the make, interested in fashionable clothes and social advancement. His friend [[dmitri-razumihin|Razumihin]] describes him as "a delightful person" (011-062) but also warns Raskolnikov that Zametov has noticed his suspicious behavior. Unlike Porfiry, who is a trained investigator with a philosophical cast of mind, Zametov is a sharp but conventional clerk whose suspicions arise from street-level observation and police-station gossip rather than psychological penetration.

## Role in the Novel

Zametov's most important scene occurs at the Crystal Palace restaurant (Part 2, Chapter 6), where Raskolnikov, in a state of feverish agitation, virtually confesses to the murder in the form of a hypothetical: "What if it were I who murdered the old woman and Lizaveta?" (010-330). Zametov refuses to take the confession seriously, dismissing it as delirium, but the encounter leaves both men unsettled. This scene establishes the pattern of "confession-through-denial" that reaches its full development in Raskolnikov's interviews with Porfiry — the idea that the murderer's guilt compels him toward self-exposure even as he tries to conceal the crime.

Later, Razumihin reports to Raskolnikov that Zametov has discussed the case with Porfiry and that "your name has... been mentioned" among the suspects. Zametov thus functions as a conduit between the street-level gossip of the police station and the formal investigation, a minor but essential link in the chain of suspicion that tightens around Raskolnikov.

## Key Relationships

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]**: The object of Zametov's suspicion. Their restaurant conversation is an early example of the psychological duel that defines Raskolnikov's interactions with authority.
- **[[porfiry-petrovich|Porfiry Petrovich]]**: Zametov's superior in the investigation, to whom he passes information gathered from the police station. Zametov is Porfiry's informal intelligence network.
- **[[dmitri-razumihin|Dmitri Razumihin]]**: A social acquaintance who vouches for Raskolnikov while also inadvertently confirming Zametov's suspicions.

## Psychological Profile

Zametov is not a deep psychological study — he is defined by his professional ambition, his fashionable self-presentation, and his sharp but limited intelligence. His suspicion of Raskolnikov is the suspicion of the capable bureaucrat: he notices anomalies, draws inferences, and reports them upward. Crucially, he lacks the philosophical depth to understand what he is seeing; when Raskolnikov all but confesses, Zametov dismisses it as fever-talk. This limitation makes him an interesting foil to Porfiry, who would never have let such a confession slip past him.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Zametov represents the institutional machinery of justice — competent, conventional, and ultimately unable to penetrate the psychological complexity of Raskolnikov's crime. His presence establishes that Raskolnikov is under suspicion not just from the brilliant Porfiry but from the entire apparatus of the state, from court clerks to examining magistrates. The Crystal Palace restaurant scene, in which Raskolnikov is simultaneously confessing and mocking Zametov's inability to recognize the confession, dramatizes the novel's theme of [[guilt|guilt]] as a force that demands expression regardless of rational self-interest.
