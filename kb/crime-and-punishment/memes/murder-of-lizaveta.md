# Murder of Lizaveta

The murder of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]] is the second, unplanned killing in Part 1, Chapter 7 of *Crime and Punishment*, and in many ways it is more significant than the first. While [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] had spent weeks rationalizing the murder of [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] — a "louse," a "useless" usurer — the killing of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] is spontaneous, panicked, and entirely outside the theoretical framework he had prepared. It is the moment the novel's philosophical argument collapses into brute reality: the extraordinary man, who was supposed to transcend moral law through superior consciousness, becomes a common murderer of an innocent.

## Summary

After striking [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] with the axe and a second blow to "split her skull open," [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] begins searching the body for keys and loot. He is in a state of "overwhelming horror" — not from the act itself but from the [[fear-and-dread|fear]] of discovery. As he fumbles with the keys and blood, he hears footsteps. Turning, he sees [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] standing "in the middle of the room with a big bundle in her arms," staring "as white as a sheet" at her murdered sister (007-148). Without time to think, in "a frenzy of [[fear-and-dread|fear]]," [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] brings the axe down on [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s head. She falls without a cry. Unlike Alyona, [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] makes no sound — "she only raised her hand to her head" — and dies in silence. The second murder transforms what [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] had conceived as an execution into a massacre, and its psychological consequences haunt him far more than the first.

## Context & Buildup

[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]] is introduced earlier in Part 1 as [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]]'s half-sister and virtual servant: she "was of lower rank than her sister, unmarried, and awfully clumsy, remarkably tall, with long, flat feet, but gentle and timid" (006-063). The pawnbroker treats her brutally — "the other day she bit [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s finger out of spite" (006-103) — and [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] endures it all with "patient submission."

The novel goes to considerable lengths to establish [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s innocence. She is "continually with child" (006-055), a seamstress and trader who works to support her abusive sister. The overheard conversation at the [[hay-market|Hay Market]] that determines the murder's timing — she will be "away from the flat at exactly seven o'clock" (005-345) — centers entirely on [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s business as a dealer in secondhand goods.

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s plan explicitly depends on [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s absence. He knows she will be out at the appointed hour, and the entire theory of the crime — one death, one victim, one utilitarian calculation — rests on this premise. When [[koch|Koch]] and [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]]'s knock sends [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] into hiding, it is the unexpected return of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] that shatters every assumption of his plan.

## Detailed Account

The scene unfolds with devastating economy. [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has just killed [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]] with two blows of the axe. He is in a state of "overwhelming horror and panic" (007-080), fumbling through the dead woman's pockets for keys, his hands bloodied, the axe still in his grip. He has taken the keys from [[alyona-ivanovna|the old woman]]'s neck and is preparing to open the chest of drawers when he hears "footsteps" (007-130).

He turns: "Lo and behold — in the middle of the room stood [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] with a big bundle in her arms" (007-148). She is staring at Alyona, "white as a sheet," paralyzed. She has walked in on the scene through the door that [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]], in his panic, left open. Her bundle — the goods she was trading — is still in her arms.

The text emphasizes [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s helplessness: "She seemed unable even to cry out" (007-148). [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] rushes at her with the axe "in a perfect frenzy." The blow is not calculated — it is instinctual, driven by "horror" and "despair." He strikes her "straight on the skull with the blunt side of the axe" and "split her head open at one blow from the crown to the forehead." She falls without a sound.

The contrast between the two murders is stark and deliberate. Alyona's killing was premeditated, rehearsed, and — in [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s mind — justified. [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s killing is unplanned, reflexive, and utterly unjustifiable. She is pregnant, gentle, and "patient in submission" — the very type of the innocent sufferer that the novel elevates as its highest moral category. Her death is the novel's most powerful refutation of the theory that one can kill a "louse" and walk away clean.

## Immediate Aftermath

[[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] is "more and more overcome by [[fear-and-dread|fear]]" after the second murder. "The more he killed, the more he was afraid" (007-155). His panic escalates: he washes the blood from his hands and the axe, but cannot clean the blood from his boot or the purse he has stolen. He struggles with the locked chest, missing his opportunity for substantial loot. The second murder has transformed a planned robbery into a chaotic bloodbath.

When [[koch|Koch]] and [[pestryakov|Pestryakov]] knock at the door, [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] hides behind the door — still holding the axe. He experiences "sensations of a man condemned to death" (007-260). The "minute of paroxysm" when the two men leave and he himself must slip out — past the painters who are descending the [[staircases|stairs]] — is the novel's most sustained passage of pure suspense.

The second murder alters the physical evidence. There are now two bodies, twice the blood, twice the traces. But more importantly, it alters the moral evidence: [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has killed not just a "useless louse" but an innocent woman — one who was, by his own knowledge, "gentle and timid," exploited by her sister, and pregnant.

## Long-Term Consequences

[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s murder haunts [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] in ways Alyona's never does. In his subsequent deliriums and confessions, it is [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] who appears — her "pale face," her silence, her raised hand. When he eventually [[raskolnikov-confesses-to-sonia|confesses to Sonia]], one of his first admissions is: "I killed [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] too... I killed her accidentally" (025-185). The qualifier "accidentally" is damning — it acknowledges that [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s death was outside his plan, that the crime exceeded his theory.

[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s death also creates an unexpected web of connection. [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] was a friend of [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] — they had exchanged crosses, and [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] had given [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] aNew Testament, the very book [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] reads to [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] during the [[lazarus-reading|Lazarus reading scene]]. The novel constructs a hidden chain of innocence: [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s gift to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] becomes the instrument of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s eventual spiritual awakening. The woman [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] killed without thought becomes, through her friendship with [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], the silent agent of his redemption.

## Narrative & Thematic Significance

The murder of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] is the novel's pivot from intellectual crime to moral catastrophe. Dostoevsky's argument against utilitarianism and rational egoism is conducted not through philosophical rebuttal but through the brute fact of [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s body on the floor. You cannot kill one "louse" without also killing her innocent sister. You cannot commit a "rational" murder because murder, by its nature, escapes rational control.

The scene also dramatizes the novel's theology of innocent suffering. [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]], like [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]], is a "holy fool" — a simple, good, exploited person whose suffering has no explanation and offers no utility. Her death is pure waste, pure evil. By making [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] pregnant (implied through "continually with child"), Dostoevsky compounds the horror: [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]] has killed not just a woman but a mother and her unborn child. The axe that was meant to liberate humanity from a "louse" has instead extinguished the most innocent possible life.

[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s silence in death — she "seemed unable even to cry out" — becomes a recurring motif. Unlike Alyona, who "shrieked" and struggled, [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] simply falls. Her silence is the silence of the innocent throughout the novel: [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]]'s silent suffering, the silent endurance of poverty. It is the silence that ultimately speaks louder than [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s theories.

## Characters Involved

- **[[rodion-raskolnikov|Rodion Raskolnikov]]** — The murderer, whose carefully rationalized crime collapses into panicked, indiscriminate killing the moment reality deviates from his plan.
- **[[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta Ivanovna]]** — The pawnbroker's half-sister, a "gentle and timid" woman, constantly pregnant, exploited and abused by her sister. Her unexpected return triggers the second, unplanned murder (007-148).
- **[[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]]** — The first victim, already dead on the floor when [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] enters — the "planned" murder that made the second one inevitable.
- **[[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia Marmeladov]]** — Not present but spiritually connected: [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]] and [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] exchanged crosses and were friends; [[lizaveta-ivanovna|Lizaveta]]'s gift of the New Testament to [[sonia-marmeladov|Sonia]] becomes the instrument of [[rodion-raskolnikov|Raskolnikov]]'s eventual awakening.
