## Overview

Krestovsky Island (Крестовский остров) is one of the northernmost islands in the Neva delta, known in Dostoevsky's time as a semi-rural outskirt of [[st-petersburg]] with open fields, sparse woodland, and dachas. In *Crime and Punishment*, it appears in a single, haunting reference as the place where [[arkady-svidrigailov]] spends the night before his suicide.

## Svidrigailov's Last Night

After [[dounia-raskolnikov]] shoots at him and flees, Svidrigailov drifts through Petersburg in a state of despair. His wandering takes him to Krestovsky Island, where he seeks shelter:

> In the morning he was found among some bushes on Krestovsky Island, trembling and soaked through. [032-105]

The island lies beyond the [[neva-river]], across the Tuchkov Bridge and past the [[vassilyevsky-ostrov]] district, continuing the outward trajectory that leads Svidrigailov away from the city's centre and toward its margins. His route — from the Haymarket, across the islands, and back to the hotel on Y. Prospect — maps a spiral toward death.

## Spatial Symbolism

Krestovsky Island represents the limit of Petersburg's reach. In the novel's spatial logic, movement outward from the centre (the [[hay-market]], the crowded tenements) towards the islands maps a psychological movement from society toward isolation, from connection toward dissolution. Raskolnikov, too, is drawn to the islands during his fevered walks:

> He walked right across Vassilyevsky Ostrov, came out on to the Lesser Neva, crossed the bridge and turned towards the Islands. The greenness and freshness pleased his weary eyes. [005-035–037]

But where Raskolnikov finds a temporary refreshment in the islands' green expanse, Svidrigailov finds only exposure. The trembling in the bushes is the physical correlative of his spiritual nakedness — stripped of his charm, his money, his power over Dounia, he is reduced to a body shivering in a thicket at the edge of the city.

## Significance

Krestovsky Island, despite its minimal page-time, is significant in the novel's geography of despair. It occupies the farthest edge of the spatial trajectory that defines Svidrigailov's final chapter: from the centre (the [[crystal-palace-restaurant]], the [[yusupov-garden]]) outward to the islands and back to the grim hotel on Y. Prospect where he kills himself. The island is not a destination but a way-station in a deathward journey.
