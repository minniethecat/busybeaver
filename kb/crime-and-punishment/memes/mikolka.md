# Mikolka

Mikolka is the peasant who, in Raskolnikov's childhood dream (Part 1, Chapter 5), beats an old mare to death with a crowbar while a crowd of onlookers cheers. Though he exists only in Raskolnikov's dream-memory, he is one of the novel's most vivid symbolic figures — a distillation of the irrational cruelty that the dream's child-witness (the young Raskolnikov) recoils from in horror and that the adult Raskolnikov will himself enact in the murder of [[alyona-ivanovna|Alyona Ivanovna]].

## Overview

The Mikolka dream is the novel's first major set piece and its most concentrated symbolic foreshadowing. In the dream, the seven-year-old Raskolnikov witnesses a drunken peasant named Mikolka invite a crowd to climb into his cart, only to begin beating the "wretched, lean old mare" with increasing fury when she cannot pull the load. Mikolka escalates from whip to shaft to crowbar, shouting "My property! I'll kill her!" while the crowd eggs him on. The child Raskolnikov runs to embrace the dying horse, weeping, and his father leads him away — but the image of Mikolka's crowbar descending will return at the moment Raskolnikov raises his own axe against the pawnbroker.

## Symbolic & Thematic Significance

Mikolka is the novel's primal image of violence without rational justification — the opposite of Raskolnikov's [[extraordinary-man-theory|theory]] that killing can be logical and purposive. His cry "My property!" links violence to ownership, prefiguring Raskolnikov's own reduction of Alyona to a "louse" whose wealth can be appropriated. But the dream's horror is that Mikolka's violence is *enjoyable* — he is "in a perfect frenzy" — suggesting that beneath Raskolnikov's rationalizations lies something darker: not the calculated act of a Napoleon but the blind cruelty of a drunk peasant beating a horse.

## Key Quotes

> "My property! I'll do what I like with her! I'll kill her!" — Mikolka, beating the mare (from Raskolnikov's dream, Part 1, Chapter 5)

> "He was in a perfect frenzy." — Narration of Mikolka's escalating violence
