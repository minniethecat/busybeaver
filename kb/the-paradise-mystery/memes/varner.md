# Varner

Varner is a stonemason employed at [[wrychester-cathedral]] whose eyewitness testimony places him at the very centre of the [[paradise|Paradise]] mystery. He is the first person to see [[john-braden]] fall — or, as he insists, be thrown — from the gallery of [[st-wrythas-stair|St. Wrytha's Stair]], and his unshakeable account shapes the entire course of the subsequent [[criminal-investigation|investigation]].

## Role in the Story

Varner is working in [[wrychester-cathedral|the Cathedral]] when the fatal incident occurs. He is positioned in the nave, watching a man — later identified as [[john-braden|John Braden]] — who is looking around the sacred building, "gazing about him as a stranger would" (003-039). Varner's attention is drawn to the stranger, and he becomes the sole witness to the moment of death.

When the incident occurs, Varner rushes to [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]]'s surgery to raise the alarm. Bursting in with the words "What is it, Varner?" — a question that [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] asks with practiced calm (003-032) — Varner delivers the shocking news: a man has fallen — "or rather — flung!" — from the gallery (003-037). [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] seizes Varner's arm and shakes him, demanding clarity (003-035), but Varner's account is unwavering: he saw it happen, and he is adamant that the man did not simply fall.

## Key Appearances

### Chapter III: St. Wrytha's Stair

Varner provides the crucial first account of the death. He testifies that he was looking at the stranger from the nave — "I was more for watching him than the doorway" — when he saw a man's hand emerge from behind the stranger and push him (003-039)(003-041). Varner is clear on the timing: he watched for "five or six minutes" before the incident, and he rushed immediately to the fallen man (003-043).

He leads [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] and others back to [[wrychester-cathedral|the Cathedral]], where the stranger lies dead in the gallery below (003-046)(003-047). Varner guides the party to the scene, showing them the clerestory gallery where the man fell from (003-060)(003-062).

### Chapter IV-VI: The Inquest

Varner becomes the central witness at the coroner's inquest. His testimony is unequivocal: he saw [[john-braden|the dead man]] thrown by an unseen hand (004-021)(005-017). Under cross-examination, he sticks stoutly to his account, refusing to be shaken even when his certainty is questioned (006-041).

The reliability of Varner's testimony becomes a point of contention. [[pemberton-bryce]] is initially skeptical, noting that the events "could all happen so quickly that Varner might easily be mistaken" (005-026). But Varner's persistence — the town notes that "Varner persists in sticking to what he saw" — ensures that the death is treated as suspicious rather than accidental (007-002).

### The Gallery Route

Varner explains the layout of [[wrychester-cathedral|the Cathedral]] with professional familiarity: the gallery runs all round the nave, and the door to it is "always open" — or at least had been open for some time (003-058)(003-060). This architectural knowledge, coming from a man who works daily in the building, gives weight to his account of where the assailant must have been positioned.

## Relationships

- **[[pemberton-bryce]]**: Called upon by Varner immediately after the death, [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] follows him to [[wrychester-cathedral|the Cathedral]] and becomes the first medical man on the scene (003-032)(003-051).
- **[[mark-ransford]]**: Ransford watches Varner with "a set, concentrated look" during [[coroners-inquest|the inquest]], a detail that [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] notes with interest (004-022).
- **[[john-braden]]**: Varner was the last person to see [[john-braden|Braden]] alive and the first to find him dead — a proximity that makes his testimony indispensable (003-039)(004-021).

## Significance to the Mystery

Varner is the linchpin of the case for murder. Without his eyewitness account, the death of [[john-braden|John Braden]] would almost certainly have been recorded as an accident — a tourist's fatal misstep in an unfamiliar gallery. His insistence on what he saw — the hand, the push, the fall — transforms the incident from a tragedy into a crime, setting in motion the entire [[criminal-investigation|investigation]] that follows.

Varner represents the value of the ordinary observer. He is not a detective, a doctor, or a gentleman — he is a working mason going about his daily labour. Yet his attentive watching and his refusal to have his testimony dismissed by those who would prefer a simpler explanation make him, in many ways, the novel's most important witness. The entire [[paradise|Paradise]] mystery turns on his word.

> the jackdaws were making such a to-do up there by the roof I glanced up at them. And I saw this man thrown through that door—fairly flung through it! God!—do you think I could mistake my own eyes?
>
> (003-039)
