# Paradise

Paradise is the ironically named location within the [[cathedral-close|Cathedral Close]] of [[wrychester|Wrychester]] where the central event of the novel occurs: the discovery of [[john-braden|John Braden]]'s dying body at the foot of St. Wrytha's Stair. The name's contrast with the violence that occurs there establishes the novel's governing theme — that tranquil surfaces conceal dark truths.

## Description

Paradise is a green space within the Cathedral precincts, part of the "great green sward, fringed by tall elms and giant beeches" that surrounds the Cathedral (001-001). It is a place of "old-world peace" in appearance — a garden close where one would expect nothing more dramatic than afternoon strolls and the circling of rooks.

The location's key feature is St. Wrytha's Stair, an ancient stone stairway leading up the side of the Cathedral. It is from this stair — "forty feet above" the ground — that John Braden fell to his death (003-052)(004-004). The stair thus transforms Paradise from a place of peace to a crime scene.

> "It's an enclosure—between the south porch and the transept," he said. "Full of old tombs and trees—a sort of wilderness—why called Paradise I don't know. There's a shortcut across it to the Deanery and that part of the Close—through that archway you see over there."
>
> (003-015)

## In the Novel

The body is discovered in Paradise by a verger making his morning rounds. [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]], passing through the Close, is among the first to reach the dying man. The location's name becomes bitterly significant: Paradise, the novel's version of Eden, is where death enters the seemingly perfect world of Wrychester's ecclesiastical community.

The discovery at Paradise triggers the entire subsequent investigation. The scrap of paper found on Braden's body (003-054) passes from Paradise through [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]]'s pocket to [[mr-harker|Harker]]'s hands, becoming the thread that unravels the mystery. Paradise is thus both the scene of the crime and the point of origin for the novel's entire narrative movement.

## Symbolism

The name "Paradise" is neither accidental nor merely descriptive. It signals the novel's engagement with themes of innocence and fall, concealment and revelation. The Cathedral — a symbol of divine order — overlooks a garden whose name promises perfection but whose reality contains violence. The irony is explicit: in Paradise, a man dies, and the search for truth begins.
