# Barthorpe

**Barthorpe** is an ancient market-town of two thousand inhabitants in the north of Leicestershire, notable in the Paradise mystery as the destination to which the trail of a single antiquarian book leads [[Pemberton Bryce|Bryce]] in his investigation of [[Death of John Braden|John Braden's]] true identity (007-025). It is in Barthorpe and the nearby village of Braden Medworth that Bryce uncovers the crucial link between the names Ransford, Brake, and Bewery — a discovery that reshapes the entire investigation (008-021).

## Description

> Barthorpe was an ancient market-town of two thousand inhabitants in the north of Leicestershire, famous for nothing except that it had been the scene of a battle at the time of the Wars of the Roses, and that its trade was mainly in agriculture and stocking-making — evidently a slow, sleepy old place. (007-025)

The town is characterized by its old-world quality: "a quaint old-world little market-town, in which some of even the principal houses still wore roofs of thatch, and wherein the old custom of ringing the curfew bell was kept up" (007-032). Its marketplace is dominated by the parish church, under whose shadow stands the Peacock Hotel — an old-fashioned establishment with an oak-panelled dining-room "hung about with portraits of masters of foxhounds and queer old prints of sporting and coaching days" (007-032).

The Peacock's smoking-room is described as even older and quainter, "a room of nooks and corners, with a great open fireplace, and old furniture and old pictures and curiosities — the sort of place to which the old-fashioned tradesmen of the small provincial towns still resort of an evening rather than patronize the modern political clubs" (008-015).

Barthorpe possesses its own railway station on the Midland line, a stop between Leicester and the northern towns — the conductor's cry of "Barthorpe next stop! — next stop Barthorpe!" heralds Bryce's arrival (007-029). The town also maintains public records including parish registers, burgess rolls, and directories, which Bryce systematically searches during his investigation (008-016).


## Role in the Story

Barthorpe enters the narrative through a single physical object: the *History of Barthorpe*, an antiquarian book found among [[Death of John Braden|John Braden's]] effects at [[The Mitre Hotel]] (004-061).

### The Book as Clue

When [[Inspector Mitchington]] empties Braden's suitcase following his death, the only personal effect of note is "this old book — what is it — *History of Barthorpe*" (004-061). The book is described as having "a curious old binding and queer old copper-plates," bought by Braden from a London book-barrow for one-and-six (004-063). [[Pemberton Bryce|Bryce]] examines the title page and makes "a mental note of the fact that Barthorpe was a market-town in the Midlands" (004-063).

### Campany's Theory

The book's significance is first articulated by [[Ambrose Campany]], the Cathedral librarian:

> "Here's a man in possession of an old history of Barthorpe. Barthorpe is a small market-town in the Midlands — Leicestershire, I believe, of no particular importance that I know of, but doubtless with a story of its own. Why should anyone but a Barthorpe man, past or present, be interested in that story so far as to carry an old account of it with him? Therefore, I conclude this stranger was a Barthorpe man. And it's at Barthorpe that I should make inquiries about him." (006-004)

Campany's logic — that a person wouldn't buy an obscure local history without personal connection to the place — becomes the guiding hypothesis that sends Bryce to Leicestershire (007-024).

### Police Inquiry and Its Failure

At the inquest into Braden's death, [[Inspector Mitchington|Mitchington]] reports that the Wrychester police telegraphed the Barthorpe authorities:

> "In consequence of finding the book about Barthorpe in the suitcase, we sent a long telegram yesterday to the police there, telling them what had happened, and asking them to make the most careful inquiries at once about any townsman of theirs of the name of John Braden, and to wire us the result of such inquiries this morning. This is their reply, received by us an hour ago. Nothing whatever is known at Barthorpe — which is a very small town — of any person of that name." (006-024)

This official dead end does not deter Bryce; he has already concluded that "Braden" was an assumed name, and he resolves to conduct his own investigation using "better methods than that of finding things out" (007-024).

### Bryce's Journey

After consulting a gazetteer at the Free Library to learn Barthorpe's particulars, Bryce packs a bag and takes an early train to London, arriving in Leicestershire by evening (007-025). On the train approaching Barthorpe, he overhears two fellow passengers discussing the Paradise case:

> "Barthorpe next stop! — next stop Barthorpe!"
> "Barthorpe? That's the place that was mentioned in connection with that very queer affair at Wrychester..." (007-027)

One passenger mentions knowing the Barthorpe district and notes a curious coincidence: "Now close to Barthorpe — a mile or two outside it, there's a village of that name — Braden Medworth" (007-030). This unwitting tip from a stranger gives Bryce his most promising lead — a direct link between the dead man's assumed surname and a nearby village (007-032).

Bryce spends his first full day in Barthorpe systematically searching public records — parish registers, burgess rolls, lists of voters, directories — without once finding the name Braden (008-016). His methodical approach confirms his hypothesis that "Braden" was never a genuine Barthorpe surname.

### The Braden Medworth Discovery

On his second day, Bryce walks the two miles from Barthorpe to [[Braden Medworth]], a "very small, quiet, and picturesque place, with an old church on the banks of a river" (008-016). At the vicarage, he inspects the parish registers under the pretext of researching a book on English surnames (008-016). There, in the carefully compiled index, he finds what he seeks:

> Bewery! A name so uncommon that here — here, in this out-of-the-way Midland village! — there must be some connection with the object of his search. There the name stood out before him, to the exclusion of all others — Bewery — with just one entry of figures against it. He turned to page 387 with a sense of sure discovery. (008-021)

This discovery links the dead man to the Bewery family — and through them, to [[Mark Ransford|Ransford]] and the late [[John Brake]] — revealing the triangular relationship at the heart of the mystery.

### The Barthorpe Police

The Barthorpe police, under the local superintendent, play a supporting role. Unknown to Bryce, [[mr-harker|Simpson Harker]] has also travelled to Barthorpe and is working with the superintendent, who assigns a plainclothes officer to shadow Bryce "wherever he goes" (008-011). The police provide Harker with reports of Bryce's movements throughout his stay, though Bryce remains "all unconscious that anyone was interested in his movements" (008-015).

## Location Context

Barthorpe lies in northern Leicestershire, in the Midlands of England. It is approximately:

- **Two miles** from the village of [[Braden Medworth]] — a pleasant walk Bryce makes on his second day of investigation (008-016)
- A train journey from **London**, with an express connection via Leicester (007-025)
- The nearest "reg'lar doctor" for the inhabitants of Braden Medworth (008-046)

The town's historical significance is limited to "the scene of a battle at the time of the Wars of the Roses" (007-025); its economy rests on agriculture and stocking-making. Unlike [[Wrychester]], Barthorpe has no cathedral or ecclesiastical prestige — it is deliberately characterized as a "slow, sleepy old place" whose obscurity makes the *History of Barthorpe* clue all the more telling (007-025).
