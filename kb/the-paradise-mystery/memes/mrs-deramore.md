Mrs. Deramore is a resident of the [[cathedral-close|Wrychester Close]] who lives next door to [[mark-ransford|Dr. Mark Ransford]]. Her eyewitness report — that she saw [[john-braden|John Braden]] crossing [[cathedral-close|the Close]] from the direction of Ransford's garden on the morning of the accident — sparks the rumour that begins to cast [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] on [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]], setting in motion several key subplots.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Deramore functions as the novel's primary source of gossip and circumstantial witness. Though she appears only indirectly — through the reports of other characters — her observation from an upper window is the seed from which grows the persistent [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] that Ransford knew more about the dead stranger than he admitted (007-019). Her role illustrates how innocent observation can be weaponised by those with ulterior motives.

## Key Appearances

- **The sighting**: On the morning of [[john-braden|John Braden]]'s fatal fall, Mrs. Deramore happened to look out of an upstairs window and saw a man — matching the description of the "mysterious stranger" — crossing [[cathedral-close|the Close]] to[[guardianship|ward]]s [[wrychester-cathedral|the Cathedral]], moving in what she believed to be a straight line from the direction of [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]]'s garden (007-019).
- **Spreading the rumour**: She reported her observation to [[mrs-folliot|Mrs. Folliot]], who in turn relayed it to [[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]], providing him with ammunition to fuel [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] against Ransford (007-019).
- **At the [[mr-folliot|Folliot]] house**: Her remarks were discussed at the [[mr-folliot|Folliot]] home in the presence of [[sackville-bonham|Sackville Bonham]] and [[mr-folliot|Mr. Folliot]], with [[sackville-bonham|Sackville]] confirming he had never seen the stranger visit Ransford's house (010-055).
- **[[mr-folliot|Mr. Folliot]] warns [[mary-bewery|Mary]]**: Concerned about the rumour's potential impact on Ransford, [[mr-folliot|Folliot]] informed [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]] of what Mrs. Deramore had been saying, advising her to warn Ransford that the gossip originated with his neighbour, not with the [[mr-folliot|Folliot]] household (010-050).
- **[[dick-bewery|Dick Bewery]]'s version**: [[dick-bewery|Dick Bewery]] reported to Ransford that Jim Deramore (presumably Mrs. Deramore's son) had relayed his mother's claim that she saw the stranger leave the house and cross [[cathedral-close|the Close]] shortly before ten o'clock (011-013).
- **The error identified**: [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] later explained to [[mitchington|Detective-Sergeant Mitchington]] that Mrs. Deramore had "made two and two into five" — she saw [[john-braden|Braden]] crossing [[cathedral-close|the Close]] and assumed he had been visiting Ransford, when in fact Ransford had already left the house and only [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] was present (011-052).

## Relationships

- **[[mark-ransford|Dr. Mark Ransford]]** — Neighbour. Her sighting inadvertently fuels [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] of Ransford.
- **[[mrs-folliot|Mrs. Folliot]]** — Confidante. Deramore shared her observation with [[mrs-folliot|Mrs. Folliot]], through whom the rumour spread.
- **Jim Deramore** — Son. He relayed his mother's claims to [[dick-bewery|Dick Bewery]].
- **[[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]]** — Indirect beneficiary. [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] used the rumour she started to advance his own agenda against Ransford.
- **[[mr-folliot|Mr. Folliot]]** — Aware of her gossip and attempted to inoculate Ransford against it (010-050).

## Significance to the Mystery

Mrs. Deramore is a catalyst figure — she does not drive the plot directly, but her innocent observation provides the initial spark for the cloud of [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] that hangs over [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]] throughout the novel. Her mistake (seeing [[john-braden|Braden]] crossing [[cathedral-close|the Close]] and inferring he had visited Ransford, when Ransford was absent) exemplifies one of the novel's themes: how circumstantial evidence, magnified by gossip, can create dangerous false impressions.
