# Braden Medworth

**Braden Medworth** is a small village situated about two miles from [[Barthorpe]] in Leicestershire, whose parish register holds the key to the Paradise mystery (008-016). It is in the vicarage of this quiet, picturesque place that [[Pemberton Bryce|Bryce]] discovers the marriage entry of John Brake to Mary Bewery — witnessed by [[Mark Ransford]] — unravelling the hidden triangular relationship between the dead man, his wife, and the doctor who now cares for her daughter (008-021).

## Description

> He found Braden Medworth a very small, quiet, and picturesque place, with an old church on the banks of a river which promised good sport to anglers. (008-016)

The village features an old parish church on the riverbank, a vicarage, a handful of houses between the inn and the bridge, and a small shop bearing the name Charles Claybourne — the village cobbler and parish clerk (008-021). The river's angling prospects made Braden Medworth a destination for fishing trips: it was this recreational appeal that first brought [[John Brake]] and [[Mark Ransford]] as young men from London to Leicestershire (009-014).

A watercolour drawing of Braden Medworth hangs over the mantelpiece of Mr. Gilwaters — the village's former vicar — in his retirement home in Bayswater, London (009-001).

## Role in the Story

Braden Medworth's significance to the narrative is entirely genealogical: its parish register provides the documentary proof that unlocks the hidden history behind the [[Death of John Braden|death in Paradise]].

### The Train Hint

Bryce first learns of Braden Medworth's existence from a fellow passenger on the train approaching Barthorpe:

> "This strange man's name was given in the papers as John Braden. Now close to Barthorpe — a mile or two outside it, there's a village of that name — Braden Medworth. That's a curious coincidence — and taken in conjunction with the man's possession of an old book about Barthorpe — why, perhaps there's something in it — possibly more than I thought for at first." (007-030)

Bryce thanks his "good luck" and resolves to visit the village, recognizing that "the coincidence was too striking to be neglected" (007-032).

### The Marriage Register Discovery

On his second day of investigation, Bryce walks the two miles from Barthorpe to Braden Medworth and visits the vicarage, requesting to inspect the parish registers under the pretext of researching a book on English surnames (008-016). As he scans the carefully compiled index, "On the third page he saw the name Bewery":

> Bewery! A name so uncommon that here — here, in this out-of-the-way Midland village! — there must be some connection with the object of his search. There the name stood out before him, to the exclusion of all others — Bewery — with just one entry of figures against it. He turned to page 387 with a sense of sure discovery. (008-021)

The entry reads:

> June 19th, 1891. John Brake, bachelor, of the parish of St. Pancras, London, to Mary Bewery, spinster, of this parish, by the Vicar. Witnesses, Charles Claybourne, Selina Womersley, Mark Ransford. (008-025)

This single entry reveals the entire hidden architecture of the mystery: John Brake (alias Braden) married Mary Bewery; Mark Ransford was his best man; and the couple's daughter — the younger [[Mary Bewery]] now living in [[Wrychester]] under Ransford's guardianship — is the dead man's child (008-025).

### Claybourne's Testimony

Before returning to the vicarage, Bryce notices a little shop with "Charles Claybourne" painted above its window. The old, cheery-faced cobbler — one of the witnesses to the marriage — provides further confirmation (008-021). Through Claybourne, Bryce learns that the two young men (Brake and Ransford) used to come to Braden Medworth for fishing, that Ransford was the local doctor's substitute when needed, and that after the marriage, "the two gentlemen went away" and "never saw neither of 'em since" (008-046).

### Gilwaters's Account

The trail from Braden Medworth leads Bryce to London, where he interviews the elderly Mr. Gilwaters, the former vicar who performed the marriage ceremony (009-001). Gilwaters provides the complete backstory:

> "Mary Bewery was our governess at Braden Medworth. She came to us when she was nineteen — she was married four years later. She was a girl who had no friends or relatives — she had been educated at a school in the North... Brake and Ransford were two young men from London, who used to come fishing in Leicestershire... They were pleasant young fellows, and I used to ask them to the vicarage. Eventually, Mary Bewery and John Brake became engaged to be married. My wife and I were a good deal surprised — we had believed, somehow, that the favoured man would be Ransford." (009-014)

This revelation — that everyone expected Mary Bewery to marry Ransford, not Brake — becomes the emotional core of the mystery, suggesting a long-standing romantic rivalry that may have culminated in murder (009-014).

## Location Context

Braden Medworth is situated:

- **Two miles** from [[Barthorpe]] — a pleasant walk Bryce makes on his second morning in the district (008-016)
- In **Leicestershire**, in the Midlands of England
- Near a river that provides good angling — the recreational draw that first brought Brake and Ransford from London (008-016)
- Its parish church is old, standing on the riverbank (008-016)

The village's obscurity — an "out-of-the-way Midland village" (008-021) — is a narrative asset: the extreme rarity of the name "Bewery" in this isolated place makes the discovery all the more conclusive. The coincidence of the dead man's assumed surname ("Braden") matching the village name ("Braden Medworth") provides the first thread that Bryce follows to unravel the entire mystery.
