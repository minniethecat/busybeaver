# Mrs. Partingley

**Mrs. Partingley** is the landlady of the Mitre Inn, the "quaint old-world inn which filled almost one side of [[monday-market|the little square]] known as [[monday-market|Monday Market]]" in [[wrychester|Wrychester]] (004-030). As the person who registered [[john-braden|John Braden]] upon his arrival and observed his movements on the morning of his death, she is one of the most important early witnesses in the [[criminal-investigation|investigation]] — her testimony establishes the basic chronology of [[john-braden|Braden]]'s final hours and introduces the puzzling figure of [[dellingham|Christopher Dellingham]], [[john-braden|Braden]]'s travelling companion.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Partingley appears in the immediate aftermath of [[death-of-john-braden|Braden's death]], when Inspector [[mitchington|Mitchington]] and [[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]] come to the Mitre to examine [[john-braden|the dead man]]'s room and gather information. Her role is that of the reliable, observant witness — her visitor's book and her recollections provide the factual scaffolding upon which [[coroners-inquest|the inquest]] is built.

[[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]]'s first impression captures her character succinctly: "[[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] saw at once that she had heard the news" (004-030). She is distressed but composed, her "business faculties" intact — "she made haste to produce a big visitors' book and to spread it open before her callers" (004-030).

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter IV**: Mrs. Partingley is found "looking out of the bow window which had served as an outer bar in the coaching days" of the Mitre (004-030). She "uttered another exclamation of distress and astonishment, lifting her plump hands in horror" upon learning the full details of [[death-of-john-braden|Braden's death]] (004-030).

- **Chapter IV**: Mrs. Partingley provides the key testimony about [[john-braden|Braden]]'s arrival with [[dellingham|Christopher Dellingham]]: "They'd evidently come in by the London train — that gets in at six-forty, as you know. They came here together, and they'd dinner together, and spent the evening together" (004-030).

- **Chapter IV**: She recounts the movements of both men on the morning of the death: [[dellingham|Dellingham]] "asked me the way to the old Manor Mill, and he went off there" while [[john-braden|Braden]] "hung about a bit, studying a local directory I'd lent him, and after a while he asked me if he could hire a trap to take him out to [[saxonsteade|Saxonsteade]] in the afternoon, as he wished to see the Duke" (004-030).

- **Chapter IV**: Mrs. Partingley leads [[mitchington|Mitchington]] and [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] to [[john-braden|Braden]]'s room. [[mitchington|Mitchington]] says, "We'd better examine his room, Mrs. Partingley" (004-030), and she complies.

- **Chapter VI**: At [[coroners-inquest|the inquest]], "Mrs. Partingley told how he had arrived at the Mitre, registered in her book as Mr. [[john-braden|John Braden]], and had next morning asked if he could get a conveyance for [[saxonsteade|Saxonsteade]] in the afternoon, as he wished to see the Duke" (006-023). Her testimony is straightfor[[guardianship|ward]] and factual, establishing the timeline without embellishment.

- **Chapter VI**: Her testimony also establishes a key negative fact — that she (like everyone else) could provide no information about who [[john-braden|Braden]] really was: "this apparently mysterious stranger, who, for anything that Mrs. Partingley or anybody else could say to the contrary, might have had an appointment and business with him" (006-023).

- **Chapter XI**: Her testimony is referenced later when [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] puzzles over the relationship between [[john-braden|Braden]] and [[dellingham|Dellingham]]: "I thought we learned from Mrs. Partingley that he and the other man, [[dellingham|Dellingham]], spent the evening together?" (011-069).

## Relationships

- **[[john-braden|John Braden]]** — Her guest, who registered under that name and whose death brought the police to her establishment. She noted his interest in a local directory and his plan to visit [[saxonsteade|Saxonsteade]].

- **[[dellingham|Christopher Dellingham]]** — [[john-braden|Braden]]'s travelling companion, who arrived with him on the London train and shared dinner and the evening with him before departing separately the next morning.

- **[[mitchington|Detective-Sergeant Mitchington]]** — The police officer who first interviews her at the Mitre and examines [[john-braden|Braden]]'s room.

- **[[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]]** — Present at the initial examination of [[john-braden|Braden]]'s room and a close observer of her testimony.

## Significance to the Mystery

Mrs. Partingley's role in the mystery is that of the reliable chronicler. Her visitor's book — a meticulous record of arrivals and departures — and her clear recollections provide the [[criminal-investigation|investigation]] with its factual foundation. Several key facts emerge solely from her testimony:

1. [[john-braden|Braden]] and [[dellingham|Dellingham]] arrived together, suggesting a connection between them that proves significant later.
2. [[john-braden|Braden]] intended to visit the [[duke-of-saxonsteade|Duke of Saxonsteade]], establishing a potential motive for his presence in [[wrychester|Wrychester]].
3. [[john-braden|Braden]] studied a local directory before setting out, suggesting he was orienting himself in an unfamiliar town.
4. The two men departed separately on the morning of the death — [[dellingham|Dellingham]] to the Manor Mill, [[john-braden|Braden]] to [[wrychester-cathedral|the Cathedral]].

Without Mrs. Partingley's orderly record-keeping and clear testimony, the [[criminal-investigation|investigation]] would lack the chronological framework needed to reconstruct [[john-braden|Braden]]'s final hours. She exemplifies the novel's method of building its mystery from the accumulated observations of ordinary, credible witnesses.
