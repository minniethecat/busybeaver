# St. Wrytha's Stair

The ancient stone stairway built into the south side of [[wrychester-cathedral|Wrychester Cathedral]] — the site of the novel's inciting death and the physical pivot around which the entire mystery turns.

## Location and Structure

St. Wrytha's Stair rises within the Cathedral's south gallery, leading from the nave level up to the clerestory. At its head is "an open doorway in the clerestory" (003-052). The stair's foot opens onto [[paradise|Paradise]], the ancient garden-close where [[john-braden|John Braden]]'s body was discovered.

The stair itself had a dangerous feature: "the flooring of the clerestory gallery is worn so smooth that it's like a piece of glass — and it slopes!" (005-026). The master-mason confirmed "the same decided conviction — that the real truth was that the pavement of the gallery had at that particular place become so smooth, and was inclined towards" the edge (006-041). This smooth, sloping floor became central to the inquest's finding of accidental death — and equally central to [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]]'s suspicion that the "accident" was staged.

> "Just at that doorway, at the head of St. Wrytha's Stair, the flooring of the clerestory gallery is worn so smooth that it's like a piece of glass—and it slopes! Slopes at a very steep angle, too, to the doorway itself. A stranger walking along there might easily slip, and if the door was open, as it was, he'd be shot out and into space before he knew what was happening."
>
> (005-026)

## The Fatal Fall

The novel opens with the discovery of [[john-braden|John Braden]] dying at the foot of the stair. [[dick-bewery|Dick Bewery]], who had been playing [[paradise|in the close]], ran to fetch [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]]: "There's been an accident in Paradise — man fallen from that door at the head of St. Wrytha's Stair" (004-004).

Braden's own dying words located the scene: "Foot of St. Wrytha's Stair there, doctor" (003-034). He had fallen "forty feet above" the ground (003-052). The critical question — "Thrown from an open doorway in the clerestory over St. Wrytha's Stair?" (003-052) — was whether he fell, jumped, or was pushed.

## The View from Above

A crucial piece of evidence emerged from the location itself. From the gallery doorway at the head of the stair, there was "an uninterrupted view of the gallery on the south side of the nave, and of the arched doorway at the head of St. Wrytha's Stair" (020-054). This sightline became important when [[collishaw|Collishaw]], working nearby clearing timber, was identified as a potential witness — someone who might have seen more than his killers realised.

## Role in the Investigation

The stair is the central site in the controversy over [[john-braden|Braden]]'s death. The official inquest — relying on the testimony of the master-mason about the worn, sloping floor — returned a verdict of accidental death. But [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] held a "firm opinion that it was Mark Ransford who flung John Brake down St. Wrytha's Stair and killed him" (010-001), a suspicion he pursued relentlessly.

The stair thus represents the novel's central ambiguity: was Braden's death an accident facilitated by ancient, worn stone — or a calculated murder that exploited the stair's dangerous design as cover?

## Significance

As the physical locus of the novel's central event, St. Wrytha's Stair is more than a setting — it is a structural element of the plot. The stair connects the sacred space of the Cathedral (above) with the earthly garden of [[paradise|Paradise]] (below), mirroring the novel's thematic concern with hidden sin concealed beneath respectable surfaces. Every investigator — [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]], [[mitchington|Mitchington]], [[mr-harker|Harker]] — must reckon with what happened at this spot, and the answer to "accident or murder?" at the head of St. Wrytha's Stair unlocks the entire mystery.
