# Mrs. Batts

**Mrs. Batts** is the next-door neighbour of the Collishaw family in Wrychester, whose observations during Dr. Ransford's visit to the sick Mrs. Collishaw became a critical piece of evidence in the investigation of Collishaw's poisoning.

## Testimony

When Collishaw's wife fell ill, it was Mrs. Batts's eldest lad who was sent to fetch Dr. Ransford (012-041). Mrs. Batts was "doing a thing or two about the house" (012-045) while Ransford attended to Mrs. Collishaw upstairs. According to her account, [[collishaw|Collishaw]] was putting up his dinner to take to his work (012-041).

At Ransford's request, Mrs. Batts went next door to fetch "some small matter which the Collishaws hadn't got and she had" (012-045). During her brief absence, Ransford was left alone with Collishaw's prepared dinner — the meal that would shortly afterward prove to contain hydrocyanic acid. This window of opportunity, reported by Mrs. Batts to [[mitchington|Inspector Mitchington]], became one of the most damaging pieces of circumstantial evidence against Ransford.

Mitchington, recounting her testimony to [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]], was "obviously much upset" by its implications (012-041).

## Significance

Mrs. Batts is a purely functional character: she exists in the narrative to provide Mitchington — and through him, the reader — with a specific piece of information that deepens the suspicion against Ransford. Her evidence establishes the *means* component of the circumstantial case: Ransford had both access and opportunity to tamper with Collishaw's food. She has no further role in the story beyond this single interview.

## Key Passages

> …the next-door neighbour, Mrs. Batts. Mrs. Batts says that when Ransford — who'd been fetched by her eldest lad to Mrs. Collishaw, who was then sick — had finished with his patient, Collishaw was putting up his dinner, which he was going to take with him to his work. And Ransford, said Mrs. Batts, asked her to step next door and fetch him some small matter which the Collishaws hadn't got and she had. So that Ransford was left alone with the dinner.
>
> (012-041)
