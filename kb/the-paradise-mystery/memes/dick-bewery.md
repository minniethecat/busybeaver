# Dick Bewery

A seventeen-year-old schoolboy, ward of [[mark-ransford|Dr. Mark Ransford]], whose boyish energy and sharp eyes make him the novel's catalyst — it is Dick who discovers [[john-braden|John Braden]] dying at the foot of [[st-wrythas-stair|St. Wrytha's Stair]] and who later plays an unexpected role in the surveillance that cracks the case.

## Role in the Story

Dick Bewery lives at [[ransfords-house|Dr. Ransford's house]] as the doctor's [[guardianship|ward]], alongside his sister [[mary-bewery|Mary]]. The morning of the fateful discovery, he bolts through the household with schoolboy haste:

> Dick Bewery, without a word, bolted half his coffee, snatched up his book, grabbed at a cap which lay with more books on a chair close by, and vanished through the open window.
>
> (001-008)

> Dick Bewery came round a corner from the Deanery Walk, evidently keenly excited. With him was a girl of about his own age — a certain characterful young lady whom Bryce knew as Betty Campany, daughter of the librarian to the Dean and Chapter.
>
> (005-036)

It is this very energy — his habit of roaming [[paradise|Paradise]] — that places him at the scene of the crime.

## Discovery of the Body

Dick Bewery was playing in [[paradise|the Cathedral close]] when he found [[john-braden|John Braden]] lying at the foot of [[st-wrythas-stair|St. Wrytha's Stair]], and ran immediately to fetch [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]]: "Dick Bewery came hurriedly in" (027-002) with his urgent news. This discovery makes Dick the first witness in the chain of events that becomes [[the-paradise-mystery|The Paradise Mystery]].

His schoolboy curiosity about the case is evident: when [[mark-ransford|Ransford]] returned from examining the body, Dick was "almost irritable when Dick Bewery, schoolboy-like, asked him concerning professional details" (007-001). He presses for information with the impatience of youth, unaware of how close to home the mystery would strike.

## Coming of Age

A significant subplot involves Dick's relationship with [[betty-campany|Betty Campany]]:

> Dick Bewery and Betty Campany had of late progressed out of the schoolboy and schoolgirl hail-fellow-well-met stage to the first dawnings of love, and in spite of their frequent meetings had begun a romantic correspondence between each other, the joy and mystery of which was increased a hundredfold by a secret method of exchange of these missives.
>
> (016-002)

This emerging romance is not merely decorative — it represents the approach of adulthood, which threatens the [[secrecy-and-concealment|secrecy]] of [[mark-ransford|Ransford]]'s household. When Dick comes of age, the legal arrangements of his [[guardianship|guardianship]] change, potentially exposing the carefully guarded identity secrets at the heart of the mystery.

## Surveillance and Investigation

Dick Bewery becomes an active, if unofficial, participant in the investigation. [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] and [[mr-harker|Harker]] enlist him in covert surveillance: "He led Harker back to Paradise and to the place where he had left Dick Bewery, whom they approached so quietly that Bryce was by the lad's side before Dick knew he was there" (016-040). Together they "secretly watched from behind the screen of cypress trees" (016-043) the suspicious movements of their quarry. When Dick "moved and began to slip off, Bryce reached out a hand and took him by the shoulder" (016-047) — the boy was willing but needed restraint.

He also served as a messenger and observer for [[mitchington|Mitchington]]: "the sound of footsteps behind him gave warning of the return of Dick Bewery, who, in another minute, hurried through the bushes, followed by Mitchington" (012-015).

## Relationships

- **[[mark-ransford]]** — Dick's guardian; the doctor's secrets place Dick's future at the centre of the guardianship arrangement.
- **[[mary-bewery]]** — Dick's sister, also a ward of Ransford, with whom he shares the doctor's household.
- **[[betty-campany]]** — Dick's emerging love interest; their relationship signals his transition to adulthood.
- **[[pemberton-bryce]]** — Enlisted Dick as an observer during covert surveillance in Paradise.
- **[[mr-harker]]** — Participated with Dick in the stakeout behind the cypress trees.
- **[[mitchington]]** — The detective-sergeant who worked with Dick on the scene.

## Home and Daily Life

Dick's daily routine — school, lunch at home, roaming Paradise — establishes the settled rhythm of [[wrychester|Wrychester]] life that the murder disrupts. He attends school during the day: "Dick Bewery would be at school" (022-024), and can be found "going leisurely home to lunch" (012-010), glancing at passersby with "inquisitive" schoolboy curiosity.

## Significance to the Mystery

Dick Bewery is the novel's innocent eye — the character whose accidental presence at the scene of death pulls the whole mystery into motion. His approaching adulthood creates a temporal pressure on the plot: the guardianship that shelters [[mark-ransford|Ransford]]'s household secrets will dissolve when Dick comes of age, making the resolution of [[john-braden|John Braden]]'s identity and death urgent not just for justice but for the survival of the household itself. In a story about [[secrecy-and-concealment|secrets]] and their corrosive power, Dick represents the future that will not wait.
