# John Brake

**John Brake** (c. 1858 – May 1891), also known as **John Braden**, is the figure whose hidden history drives the entire Paradise mystery. A bank manager convicted of embezzlement, Brake served ten years' penal servitude and emerged fixated on a single purpose: to find the two men who had defrauded him and left him to bear the punishment alone. His return to Wrychester under an assumed name set in motion the chain of events — death, concealment, and investigation — that forms the narrative of *The Paradise Mystery*.

## Identity and Aliases

Throughout the novel, Brake appears under two names that reflect the split in his life:

- **John Brake** — his true name, used before and during his trial (009-032). Known to Gilwaters, the former vicar of Braden Medworth; to Mark Ransford; and ultimately to the Wrychester police.
- **John Braden** — the alias under which he travels to Wrychester after his release from prison (014-024). The name "Braden" is taken from Braden Medworth, the Leicestershire village where he married Mary Bewery — "there's a village of that name — Braden Medworth. That's a curious coincidence" (007-030).

The revelation of his true identity is a pivotal moment in the investigation. Harker eventually establishes "the solid, hard, positive fact" that "John Braden was, in reality, one John Brake" (019-014).

## Early Life and Marriage

Brake was a young Londoner who, with his friend Mark Ransford, made fishing trips to Leicestershire. The two "used to come here for the fishing, now and then," the parish clerk Charles Claybourne recalled, coming "two or three times a year" and putting up at the village inn (008-046). Brake was "a banker, that one," Claybourne said; Ransford was a medical student (008-046).

On June 19, 1891, at the parish church of Braden Medworth, John Brake married Mary Bewery, the governess at the vicarage (008-024). The marriage register records him as "John Brake, bachelor, of the parish of St. Pancras, London" (008-024). Mark Ransford signed as a witness alongside Charles Claybourne and Selina Womersley (008-024).

The marriage was not without its private tensions. Gilwaters, the vicar who performed the ceremony, later recalled: "My wife and I were a good deal surprised — we had believed, somehow, that the favoured man would be Ransford" (009-014). The triangular relationship between Brake, Mary Bewery, and Ransford would haunt all three lives for decades afterward.

## The Bank Fraud and Conviction

At some point after his marriage, Brake became manager of the Upper Tooting branch of the London & Home Counties Bank. He fell into dealings with two men — one a former Barthorpe estate agent — who persuaded him to lend them bank funds for short-term financial transactions. When the two men absconded, Brake was left to face the consequences alone (009-020).

The *Times* reported the trial at the Central Criminal Court:

> John Brake, thirty-three, formerly manager of the Upper Tooting branch of the London & Home Counties Bank, Ltd., pleaded *guilty* to embezzling certain sums, the property of his employers. (009-032)

Brake's counsel, Mr. Walkinshaw, Q.C., offered "curious and mysterious hints as to what might have been said if it had been of any use or advantage to say it" (009-032). But Brake pleaded guilty and was sentenced to ten years' penal servitude.

Gilwaters visited Brake in prison after the trial. He found a man "sullen and morose" (009-020), who refused to explain the full truth. When Gilwaters asked about his wife and two children — "one, a mere infant" — Brake could not or would not answer (009-020).

## After Prison: The Search for Vengeance

Upon his release, Brake dedicated himself to finding the two men who had ruined him. The Barthorpe police superintendent later told Harker:

> "After his time was up, Brake came down here, and for a time he lived a solitary sort of life in a lonely cottage that I could show you, on the outskirts of the town. He came here, I am sure, to search for those two men. He was always searching for them. He found the one who had been the Barthorpe man — found out that he'd gone abroad and made money, and had come back and was living somewhere — but where, he couldn't ascertain." (025-031)

The two men were Falkiner Wraye (the Barthorpe estate agent, now living in Wrychester as "Stephen Folliot") and a man named Flood (024-016). Brake eventually traced Wraye to Wrychester — and there, under the name John Braden, he came to confront his past.

## Return to Wrychester and Death

Brake arrived in Wrychester with Christopher Dellingham, a fellow London tourist he had met on the train (004-037). They took rooms at the Mitre Hotel, registered under the name "John Braden" (004-039). Brake carried with him an old copy of the *History of Barthorpe* — a clue, as Ambrose Campany later deduced, to his true origins (006-004).

On the morning after his arrival, Brake entered Wrychester Cathedral. He was seen by the verger, Varner, ascending St. Wrytha's Stair toward the gallery (005-005). Moments later, he was found dead at the foot of the stair, his neck broken (005-006). The official inquest returned a verdict of accidental death, citing the worn, sloping stone floor of the ancient stair (006-023).

But Pemberton Bryce held "a firm opinion that it was Mark Ransford who flung John Brake down St. Wrytha's Stair and killed him" (010-001). The question of whether Brake's death was accident or murder is the central mystery of the novel — a question that is not fully resolved until the final chapters.

## Family and Legacy

Brake and Mary Bewery had two children:

- **Mary Bewery** (the younger) — raised by Mark Ransford as his ward in Wrychester. Gilwaters, seeing her photograph, exclaimed: "Without a doubt those are Brake's children! ... the very nose and eyes and mouth of the Mary Bewery I knew" (009-046).
- **Dick Bewery** — the infant son, also raised by Ransford. A schoolboy at the time of the novel's events (001-004).

After Brake's conviction, his wife disappeared "under extraordinarily mysterious circumstances" (009-020). Rumours circulated that "the amount of the defalcations was much larger than had been allowed to appear" and that Mrs. Brake had "gone off somewhere — Australia, or Canada, or some other far-off region — to await his release" (009-020). Gilwaters never believed these speculations, but the truth of Mary Bewery's fate remained unknown.

Ransford became the legal guardian of both children, raising them in the Close at Wrychester (001-001). Throughout the novel, he conceals their parentage from them — a protective silence that becomes the object of Bryce's suspicions and the emotional core of the mystery. The truth is finally revealed by Ransford himself in the novel's climactic scene:

> "The man who was killed, or who accidentally met his death, in Paradise, was, in reality, John Brake — your father!" (027-025)
