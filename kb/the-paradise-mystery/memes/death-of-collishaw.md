# Death of Collishaw

The **death of Collishaw** — a mason's labourer employed at [[Wrychester Cathedral]], found poisoned at his workplace — is the second violent death in *The Paradise Mystery* and the event that transforms a shadow of suspicion into evidence of murder. Collishaw had hinted that he possessed knowledge about the [[death-of-john-braden|Paradise death]], and his sudden, violent end — from poison introduced into his tin bottle while he worked — leaves no doubt that someone in [[Wrychester]] is willing to kill a second time to protect a secret. The investigation of Collishaw's murder runs parallel to the [[criminal-investigation|inquiry into Braden's death]], and the two deaths, once connected, lead inexorably toward the truth that [[Mark Ransford]] has been concealing.

> Bryce turned the body over and recognized the face of the labourer who had been working on the Cathedral masonry. "Good Lord!" he gasped. "It's Collishaw!" (012-016)

## Background: A Man Who Knew Something

Collishaw is introduced obliquely, as a shadow on the edge of the Paradise investigation. A townsman employed as a mason's labourer about the Cathedral, he is described as "usually a very sober and respectable man" (011-062). But Collishaw had let slip something — a hint, a suggestion — that he knew more about the circumstances of John Braden's death than he had told the police.

> There was a man named Collishaw, a townsman, who had been employed as a mason's labourer about the Cathedral of late. He had dropped a significant hint — that there were things he could tell about the Paradise affair if he chose. (011-058)

The nature of Collishaw's knowledge is never stated explicitly, but its existence is enough. Word spreads through the Close. Someone hears it. And someone acts on it.

## The Death

Collishaw is found dead at his workplace within the Cathedral grounds. The circumstances immediately suggest poisoning. His dinner, packed in the usual manner, sits beside him — along with his tin bottle, the kind "in which labouring men carry their tea" (012-007). The bottle, when examined, yields evidence of a toxic substance.

### The Tin Bottle

The tin bottle becomes the central piece of physical evidence in the murder investigation. Bryce, who tastes the contents at the scene, immediately detects the bitterness of poison (013-009). The bottle is secured by the police and becomes the focal point of the forensic inquiry.

> Mitchington picked up the tin bottle, made a cup of his hand, poured a little of the liquid into it, and tasted gingerly. The bitterness was unmistakable. (012-025)

The question that drives the investigation is not only *what* poison was used but *when* it was introduced — and by whom. Collishaw had been working alone, but the Close is a public place. Anyone might have passed by.

### The Medicine Box

A second piece of evidence emerges: a box of medicine found in Collishaw's waistcoat pocket, bearing the inscription "One after meals — Mr. Collishaw" (013-020). The box, when examined, contains a substance that does not match the prescription. The implication is clear: the poison was administered under the guise of medicine — a deception that required both familiarity with Collishaw's habits and access to his person.

## The Investigation

The investigation of Collishaw's death unfolds alongside the continuing inquiry into Braden's. [[Detective-Sergeant Mitchington]] leads the official police work, but it is [[Pemberton Bryce]] — already deep into his private investigation of the first death — who most aggressively pursues the connection between the two.

Bryce's theory is direct: Collishaw was killed because he knew something about the Paradise death. And the person with the strongest motive to silence a witness is [[Mark Ransford]] — the man who recognized the dead stranger and said nothing.

> "Those rumours about what Collishaw hinted he could say had got to somebody's ears," Bryce said. "And that somebody made sure Collishaw would never say them." (012-052)

[[Mr. Harker]], the retired detective, brings his professional experience to bear on the question. Unlike Bryce, whose investigation is entangled with personal motives, Harker proceeds methodically — examining the evidence, interviewing witnesses, and tracing the poison to its source. His involvement raises the quality of the investigation from amateur speculation to professional detection.

### The Inquest

A second coroner's inquest is convened — "that very morning before a coroner and his jury" (015-055) — and the public interest is intense. Two deaths in quick succession, both in the Cathedral Close, both suspicious: the town is alive with rumour and speculation. The inquest hears evidence about the tin bottle, the medicine box, and Collishaw's known habits, but the deeper connection to Braden's death remains just beyond the reach of official testimony.

## Significance

The death of Collishaw transforms the novel's moral landscape. Braden's death *could* have been an accident. Collishaw's death cannot be. The presence of poison, the medicine box switched, the timing so soon after Collishaw's hints became known — these facts eliminate the possibility of misadventure. Someone in Wrychester is a murderer.

The second death also raises the stakes for every character involved in the [[secrecy-and-concealment|concealment]] of the truth. If a man can be poisoned for what he *might* say, what danger awaits those who *know*? Ransford, Bryce, [[Mary Bewery]], and [[Mr. Folliot]] are all drawn deeper into the web — and each must reckon with the possibility that silence, once chosen, may no longer be a protection but a liability.

The connection between the two deaths — Braden and Collishaw — is the hinge on which the novel's plot turns. Once Bryce and Harker establish that link, the investigation narrows. The question is no longer *what happened in Paradise?* but *who is killing to keep the answer hidden?* And that question, pursued to its end, leads to the old well house and the final revelation.
