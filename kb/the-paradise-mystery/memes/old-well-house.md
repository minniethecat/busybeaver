# The Old Well House

**The Old Well House** is an ancient stone building situated in a secluded corner of [[Stephen Folliot]]'s large garden within the [[Cathedral Close]] of [[Wrychester]]. Converted by Folliot into a private "snuggery," it serves as the setting for the novel's climactic confrontation in which Folliot, exposed as the fugitive Falkiner Wraye, shoots [[Pemberton Bryce]] and then takes his own life by poison. Its hidden well, over a hundred feet deep, is both a physical curiosity and a symbol of the secrets buried at the heart of the mystery.

## Description

The well-house is a "square building of old stone, its walls unlined, unplastered; its floor paved with much worn flags of limestone, evidently set down in a long dead age and now polished to marble-like smoothness" (025-003). Covered with ivy and standing among high trees in the farthest corner of Folliot's garden, the building is inconspicuous — "a bit of a fancy of mine" as Folliot described it (025-003).

In the centre of the ground floor, set flush with the flagged floor, is a trapdoor furnished with a heavy iron ring. Beneath it lies the "deepest well in all Wrychester" — over a hundred feet deep, though dry for years. Folliot remarked that "some people would have pulled this old well-house down — but not me! I did better — I turned it to good account" (025-003).

The upper floor, accessed by a flight of steps in one corner, has been transformed into a small but luxurious apartment. A modern ceiling of strong oak timbers was installed, and the room was "arranged and furnished in something closely approaching to luxury" (025-003). The walls are hung with thick fabrics, the carpeting is equally thick, and the room contains pictures, books, curiosities, and deep armchairs. Two windows command "pleasant views of the Cathedral towers on one side and of the Close on the other" (025-003). A modern fire-grate and a cabinet stocked with spirits and cigars complete the room's amenities — Folliot described it as "cool in summer — warm in winter" and a "nice little place to be alone in" (025-003).

> "Deepest well in all Wrychester under that," he remarked. "You'd never think it—it's a hundred feet deep—and more! Dry now—water gave out some years ago. Some people would have pulled this old well-house down—but not me! I did better—I turned it to good account." He raised a hand and pointed upward to an obviously modern ceiling of strong oak timbers. "Had that put in," he continued, "and turned the top of the building into a little snuggery. Come up!"
>
> (025-006)

## Role in the Story

**Bryce's Confrontation with Folliot.** The well-house is the setting for the pivotal scene in which [[Pemberton Bryce]] confronts Folliot with his accumulated knowledge. Summoned by Bryce, who had just seen [[Glassdale]] leaving Folliot's garden, the two men retreat to the well-house for a private conversation. There, Bryce reveals that he has traced Folliot's true identity: he is Falkiner Wraye, the man who — together with an accomplice named Flood — defrauded [[John Braden]] (then John Brake) of bank funds years ago, leading to Brake's conviction and ten-year imprisonment (025-012).

Folliot, cornered by Bryce's revelations but maintaining outward calm, denies being the hand that threw Brake from the Cathedral doorway, stating emphatically: "Not mine, my lad! That's a fact! … I'd naught to do with it!" (025-042). He implies that Flood — his former accomplice — was the killer, repeating emphatically: "I'd naught to do with it!" (025-043).

**The Final Tragedy.** The well-house becomes the scene of the novel's violent conclusion. When the police, led by [[Mitchington]], surround Folliot's garden, Bryce — who is inside the well-house with Folliot — is ordered to let them in. As the officers "crowded up the stairway into Folliot's snuggery," Folliot "brought his concealed hand sharply round and presenting a revolver" (026-061) and fired. The bullet struck Bryce, who fell, and in the same moment Folliot "drew his other hand from his pocket, slipped something into his mouth and sat down" (026-062) — taking poison. The well-house, Folliot's private retreat, became the place of his final, fatal act.

## Location Context

The well-house stands in the farthest corner of Folliot's garden, a large property within the Cathedral Close of Wrychester. It is reached by a side-path through Folliot's rose garden — a "still more retired region" (025-001) well away from the main house and from public view. A doorway secured with a lock ensures privacy. The building's elevation gives its upper windows views of both the Cathedral towers and the Close, placing it in direct visual relationship with the other key locations of the novel's geography.
