# Mrs. Folliot

**Mrs. Folliot** is the wife of [[mr-folliot|Mr. Folliot]] (later revealed as the fugitive swindler [[falkiner-wraye|Falkiner Wraye]]) and mother of [[sackville-bonham|Sackville Bonham]]. A physically imposing and socially formidable woman, she "dominated certain circles of [[wrychester|Wrychester]] society in several senses" (007-010). Her insatiable curiosity and readiness to spread [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] make her the principal engine of the rumour that [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]] had some prior connection to the mysterious dead stranger, [[john-braden|John Braden]] — a rumour that drives much of the novel's social tension.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Folliot is introduced in detail through [[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]]'s encounter with her, where she is described with memorable physicality:

> "Mrs. Folliot was one of those women who have been gifted by nature with capacity — she was conspicuous in many ways. Her voice was masculine; she stood nearly six feet in her stoutly-soled shoes; her breadth corresponded to her height; her eyes were piercing, her nose Roman; there was not a curate in Wrychester who was not under her thumb, and if the Dean himself saw her coming, he turned hastily into the nearest shop" (007-010).

Her narrative function is threefold. First, she is a comedic figure — her formidable physical presence and relentless inquisitiveness provide moments of social satire. Second, she is a vector of gossip: it is Mrs. Folliot who seizes upon [[mrs-deramore|Mrs. Deramore]]'s observation of [[john-braden|Braden]] crossing [[cathedral-close|the Close]] and transforms it into a theory of Ransford's guilt (007-010). Third, she is a foil to Ransford's reserve — where Ransford keeps silent about his past, Mrs. Folliot fills the vacuum with speculation.

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter I**: [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]] mentions that [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] "mentioned it again — yesterday — at Mrs. Folliot's garden-party" (001-004), establishing Mrs. Folliot as a social hub before she appears in person.

- **Chapter II**: [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] warns Ransford that "Mrs. Folliot, mother of yonder callow youth, who's the apple of her eye, is one of the inquisitive ladies" (002-070) and will investigate [[mary-bewery|Mary]]'s origins if [[sackville-bonham|Sackville]] pursues her.

- **Chapter VII**: [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] encounters Mrs. Folliot "at the corner of a back street down which she was about to sail on one of her charitable missions, to the terror of any of the women who happened to be caught gossiping" (007-010). She immediately interrogates him about Ransford's wreath: "What, now, should make [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]] cause flowers to be laid on the grave of a total stranger? A sentimental feeling? Fiddle-de-dee! There must be some reason" (007-010).

- **Chapter VII**: Mrs. Folliot relays [[mrs-deramore|Mrs. Deramore]]'s observation — that [[john-braden|Braden]] was seen "crossing [[cathedral-close|the Close]] to[[guardianship|ward]]s [[wrychester-cathedral|the Cathedral]] in... a dead straight line from [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]]'s garden — as if he had been there" (007-010) — and suggests the Coroner should have questioned Ransford directly.

- **Chapter VII**: [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] leaves the encounter "convinced that Mrs. Folliot's inquisitiveness had been aroused, and that her tongue would not be idle: Mrs. Folliot, left to herself, had the gift of creating an atmosphere" (007-010).

- **Chapter X**: [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]] visits the [[mr-folliot|Folliot]] garden and is warned by [[mr-folliot|Mr. Folliot]] that "Ladies that are — getting on a bit in years, you know — like my wife, are apt to let their tongues wag" (010-005) — a gentle but telling characterization from her own husband.

- **Chapter XV**: It is revealed that Mrs. Folliot "wants her son to marry [[mary-bewery|Miss Bewery]]" and "doesn't wish any [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] to hang over the family" (015-037) — explaining the [[mr-folliot|Folliot]]s' offer of a re[[guardianship|ward]] for information about [[death-of-john-braden|Braden's death]].

- **Chapter XXVI**: At the novel's climax, "Mrs. Folliot was out" — absent from the house when the police and [[glassdale|Glassdale]] arrive to confront her husband (026-049).

## Relationships

- **[[mr-folliot|Mr. Folliot]]** — Her husband, a wealthy rose-grower of [[cathedral-close|the Close]]. Mrs. Folliot is unaware of his true identity as [[falkiner-wraye|Falkiner Wraye]]. [[mr-folliot|Mr. Folliot]] privately characterizes her as one of those "ladies that are — getting on a bit in years" whose tongues wag (010-005).

- **[[sackville-bonham|Sackville Bonham]]** — Her son from a previous marriage, "the apple of her eye" (002-070). She actively promotes his suit for [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]].

- **[[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]]** — The young woman Mrs. Folliot wishes her son to marry, and the [[guardianship|ward]] of [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]], whom Mrs. Folliot suspects.

- **[[mark-ransford|Dr. Mark Ransford]]** — The object of her [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]]. Mrs. Folliot is the primary spreader of the rumour that Ransford knew [[john-braden|John Braden]].

- **[[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]]** — An ally in gossip. [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] deliberately encourages her [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]]s, "wondering if it would be wise to instil some minute drop of poison into the lady's mind, there to increase in potency and in due course to spread" (007-010).

## Significance to the Mystery

Mrs. Folliot is not directly involved in [[death-of-john-braden|Braden's death]], but she is instrumental in creating the atmosphere of [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] that surrounds [[mark-ransford|Dr. Ransford]] throughout the novel. Her readiness to transform [[mrs-deramore|Mrs. Deramore]]'s casual observation into a theory of criminal conspiracy exemplifies the way gossip functions as a narrative force in [[wrychester|Wrychester]] — a "hotbed of gossip" where "once gossip was started there, it spread, widening in circle after circle" (011-002).

Her character also serves as dramatic irony. While she busies herself with [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] of Ransford, the real criminal lives under her own roof — her husband, whom she has unknowingly assisted in establishing a respectable facade in [[wrychester|Wrychester]] society. Her social dominance and relentless inquisitiveness are, in the end, blind to the truth closest to her.
