# Charles Claybourne

**Charles Claybourne** is the sexton and shoemaker of [[braden-medworth|Braden Medworth]], a small Leicestershire village. His role in the narrative is that of a crucial informant: it is Claybourne who provides [[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]] with the marriage registry entry linking [[john-braden|John Brake]], [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]], and [[mark-ransford|Mark Ransford]], and who supplies the oral history that allows [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] to reconstruct the tangled relationships among the three figures from twenty years earlier.

## Role in the Story

When [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] travels to [[braden-medworth|Braden Medworth]] in pursuit of the clue suggested by [[ambrose-campany|Ambrose Campany]] — that the [[barthorpe|Barthorpe]] history book found in [[john-braden|Braden]]'s luggage might point to [[john-braden|the dead man]]'s origin — he discovers the village church register containing the marriage entry for [[john-braden|John Brake]] and [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]], witnessed by Charles Claybourne and [[mark-ransford|Mark Ransford]] (008-001).

[[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] then seeks out Claybourne at his combined shoemaking shop and sexton's office. Claybourne proves to be a willing informant, "evidently anxious to give his visitor good value for his half-crown" (008-001). Through Claybourne's recollections, [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] learns the essential backstory: that [[john-braden|John Brake]] and [[mark-ransford|Mark Ransford]] had been close friends who vacationed together in [[braden-medworth|Braden Medworth]], that both had been attached to [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]] (the vicarage governess), and that Ransford was "a doctor" while [[john-braden|Brake]] was "a banker" (008-001).

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter VIII**: [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] discovers Claybourne's name as a witness in the marriage register: "June 19th, 1891. [[john-braden|John Brake]], bachelor, of the parish of St. Pancras, London, to [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]], spinster, of this parish, by the Vicar. Witnesses, Charles Claybourne, Selina Womersley, [[mark-ransford|Mark Ransford]]" (008-001).

- **Chapter VIII**: [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] visits Claybourne's shop: "At the end of the few houses between the inn and the bridge he saw a little shop with the name Charles Claybourne painted over it — evidently the establishment of a shoemaker who combined cobbling with parish-clerking" (008-001). [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] asks directly: "Are you the Charles Claybourne mentioned there?" (008-001).

- **Chapter VIII**: Claybourne describes the young [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]] as a "Nice, sweet young lady" who was "Governess at the vicarage" (008-001).

- **Chapter VIII**: Claybourne reveals the history of [[john-braden|Brake]] and Ransford's visits: "[[john-braden|Brake]] had come here for three years before they were married — him and his friend Mr. Ransford... They used to put up at the inn there... They came two or three times a year" (008-001).

- **Chapter VIII**: Most significantly, Claybourne reveals the romantic triangle: "at one time it was considered it was going to be her and the other young gentleman — the doctor — Mr. Ransford" (008-001). This information proves vital to [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]]'s developing theory about Ransford's connection to [[john-braden|Braden]].

- **Chapter VIII**: Claybourne identifies Ransford's profession ("He was a doctor") and [[john-braden|Brake]]'s ("He was a banker") (008-001), and directs [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] to the former vicar, the [[gilwaters|Reverend Thomas Gilwaters]], for further information.

- **Chapter IX**: [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]], visiting Gilwaters, mentions Claybourne as his reference: "both he, and the sexton there, Claybourne, whom you, of course, remember, thought you would be able to give me some information" (009-001).

## Relationships

- **[[john-braden|John Brake (Braden)]]** — Claybourne witnessed [[john-braden|Brake]]'s marriage and remembered him as a regular fishing visitor to the village (008-001).

- **[[mark-ransford|Mark Ransford]]** — Claybourne witnessed Ransford's role as best man at the wedding and recalled him as the "other young gentleman" who might have married [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]] (008-001).

- **[[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]]** — The vicarage governess whom Claybourne remembered as a "nice, sweet young lady" (008-001).

- **[[gilwaters|Reverend Thomas Gilwaters]]** — The former vicar of [[braden-medworth|Braden Medworth]] to whom Claybourne directs [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] for further information (008-001).

- **[[pemberton-bryce|Pemberton Bryce]]** — The investigator who extracts crucial historical information from Claybourne in exchange for a half-crown (008-001).

## Significance to the Mystery

Charles Claybourne is a classic example of the "small-world informant" in detective fiction — an apparently minor provincial figure whose detailed local knowledge unlocks a crucial piece of the puzzle. Without Claybourne's testimony, [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]] would not have learned that Ransford had been a rival for [[mary-bewery|Mary Bewery]]'s affections before she married [[john-braden|John Brake]], nor that Ransford was present at the [[braden-medworth|Braden Medworth]] wedding as a witness. This information forms the foundation of [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]]'s theory that Ransford had a personal connection to [[john-braden|the dead man]] — a theory that, while largely misdirected in [[pemberton-bryce|Bryce]]'s hands, contains the germ of the truth about the intertwined fates of [[john-braden|Brake]], Ransford, and [[falkiner-wraye|Falkiner Wraye]].

Claybourne's role also illustrates the novel's method of [[criminal-investigation|investigation]]: the truth is not uncovered through deductive brilliance but through the patient accumulation of oral testimony from a network of provincial informants, each of whom holds one piece of a larger mosaic.
