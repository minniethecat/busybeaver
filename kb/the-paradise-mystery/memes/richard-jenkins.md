## 生卒与身份

Richard Jenkins (also spelled Jenkinson) was a Welshman who settled in Wrychester during the 17th century. He died on 8 March 1715 and was buried in Paradise, the ancient enclosure within Wrychester Cathedral Close, on 10 March 1715. (006-014)

Little is recorded of his life within the novel's narrative, though it is known that he left money to St. Hedwige's Church, a church situated outside the city walls. (012-004) His tomb, however, became the linchpin of the central mystery in "The Paradise Mystery."

## The Tomb in Paradise

Richard Jenkins's tomb is located in the corner of Paradise, near the east wall of the south porch of Wrychester Cathedral. (012-004) Though the original inscription had worn away by the time of the novel's events, the tomb remained identifiable by its distinctive coat of arms — a shield bearing three birds, intended either as crows or ravens. (012-004)

> No doubt at all. In that particular case, there's no doubt that a certain tomb out there in the corner of Paradise, near the east wall of the south porch, is that of one Richard Jenkins, because it bears his coat-of-arms, which, as you see, bore these birds — intended either as crows or ravens.
>
> (012-004)

The tomb escaped documentation in a burial chart of Paradise prepared in 1850, precisely because the inscription had already become illegible by that date. (006-015) This omission made locating it a significant challenge for investigators.

## Role in the Paradise Mystery

Richard Jenkins's tomb is at the heart of the scrap-of-paper mystery — the central puzzle of the novel. John Braden, before his death at St. Wrytha's Stair, carried in his purse a scrap of paper containing a cryptic Latin direction:

> In Paradise, at Wrychester, next to, or near, the tomb of Richard Jenkins — or, possibly, Jenkinson — from, or behind, the head, twenty-three, fifteen.
>
> (005-005)

This clue indicated that something was hidden at the tomb of Richard Jenkins: twenty-three inches from, or behind, the head of the tomb, at a depth of fifteen inches. (005-005)

Pemberton Bryce, having obtained the scrap of paper from Braden's body, made it his mission to locate the tomb and discover what was concealed there. (012-001) After consulting Ambrose Campany, the Cathedral librarian, Bryce confirmed the tomb's identity through the coat of arms. (012-004)

The scrap of paper ultimately led to the hiding place of the Saxonsteade jewels — a famous collection of diamonds stolen years earlier from the Duchess of Saxonsteade. (016-022) The tomb thus served as the secret cache for the proceeds of the historic jewel robbery, and its discovery was the key to unraveling the entire mystery.

Bryce's nocturnal visit to the tomb, and the events that unfolded around it, brought the Paradise mystery to its climax (016-001) — transforming the forgotten grave of a 17th-century Welshman into the pivot upon which the fates of Dr. Ransford, John Braden, and Falkiner Wraye all turned.
