# Wrychester Police

The **Wrychester Police** are the official law-enforcement body responsible for investigating the [[death-of-john-braden|Paradise death]] and the subsequent [[death-of-collishaw|poisoning of Collishaw]]. Led by [[Detective-Sergeant Mitchington]], "a well-known police official" (003-054), the force represents institutional competence within the novel — thorough, procedurally correct, but constrained by its dependence on the evidence and testimony that witnesses choose to provide.

> "Don't you mind them — go straight to the police. Bring them back — I'll stay here." (003-051)

## Role in the Novel

The Wrychester Police are the first official body to respond to the discovery of a dying man at the foot of [[St. Wrytha's Stair]]. Within hours of the alarm being raised, Detective-Sergeant Mitchington is on the scene, directing the examination of the body and the collection of evidence. A stretcher is fetched, the body is removed (004-027), and the machinery of official investigation begins to turn.

The police-station becomes a focal point of public attention. "At the police-station doors a crowd had gathered" (004-064) as news of the death spreads through the town. The police manage the flow of information to the public — and to the press — while conducting witness interviews behind closed doors.

### Constraints of Official Investigation

The Wrychester Police operate within the bounds of official procedure, and it is precisely these bounds that limit their effectiveness in the Paradise case. Mitchington cannot compel [[Mark Ransford]] to share what he knows about the dead man's identity. He cannot search [[Pemberton Bryce]]'s pockets for the scrap of paper Bryce has pocketed. The police see only what witnesses are willing to show them — and in Wrychester, where protective [[secrecy-and-concealment|secrecy]] and strategic silence are the norm, the official view is necessarily partial.

### The Second Death

When [[Collishaw]], a mason's labourer, is found poisoned in the Cathedral Close, the police investigation intensifies. Mitchington personally secures the tin bottle and the remains of Collishaw's dinner (012-034). The poison is identified. The medicine box — bearing the inscription "One after meals — Mr. Collishaw" (013-020) — is recovered from Collishaw's waistcoat pocket. But the connection between the two deaths — the Paradise death and the labourer's poisoning — requires an insight that official procedure alone cannot provide.

## Key Personnel

**[[Detective-Sergeant Mitchington]]** is the novel's primary representative of the Wrychester Police. He is competent, methodical, and professionally courteous — a man who knows his job and does it within its defined limits. When the coroner's inquest reaches a provisional verdict, Mitchington accepts it as the legal determination it is, even while the deeper truth remains unexposed. His limitation is not personal but institutional: the police can only investigate what is reported to them.

## Limitations

The Wrychester Police embody both the strengths and the weaknesses of official investigation. They are systematic where amateurs are haphazard; they follow procedure where private inquiry follows instinct. But procedure cannot penetrate [[secrecy-and-concealment|protective silence]], and official authority cannot compel truth from those who have chosen concealment. The novel's deepest truths are uncovered not by the police but by [[Mr. Harker]], a retired detective working privately, and by [[Pemberton Bryce]], whose motives are far from official.
