# Death of John Braden

The death of **John Braden** — a stranger discovered dying at the foot of [[St. Wrytha's Stair]] in the [[Wrychester Cathedral]] close — is the event that ignites *The Paradise Mystery*. Found early one morning by a verger passing through [[Paradise]], Braden dies without speaking, leaving behind only a scrap of paper that [[Pemberton Bryce]], the first to reach him, secretly pockets. What follows — an official police investigation, a [[coroner's inquest]], a private amateur inquiry, and the gradual uncovering of Braden's true identity — transforms a single death into a web of [[secrecy-and-concealment|concealment]], [[criminal-investigation|investigation]], and [[suspicion-and-distrust|suspicion]] that will claim a second life before the truth emerges.

> A man was lying at the foot of the ancient stone stair, his body still. Bryce, bending over him, caught the word "murder" — or thought he did — before the stranger's eyes closed and he was gone. (003-052)

## Background

[[Wrychester]] is a quiet cathedral town, a place where nothing much happens and where the rhythms of ecclesiastical life set the pace for everyone else. The Paradise — a secluded green close within the Cathedral precincts, bordered by ancient buildings and dominated by St. Wrytha's Stair, a stone stairway rising forty feet to a clerestory doorway — is among the town's most tranquil corners.

Into this quiet arrives a stranger. He registers at the Mitre Inn under the name **John Braden** of London (004-037). He spends his days studying the Cathedral architecture with the absorption of a man who has a purpose there, but he confides in no one. The verger notices him; the innkeeper logs his name; otherwise he passes unremarked.

Then, on a morning like any other, a verger crossing the Paradise finds a body at the foot of the stair. Bryce, a young doctor lodging nearby and walking through the Close, is among the first on the scene. He finds the man still alive but beyond speech. Within moments, Braden is dead.

## Key Figures at the Death

### Pemberton Bryce

[[Pemberton Bryce|Bryce]] is the pivotal figure at the scene of the death. A young doctor who has recently parted ways with [[Mark Ransford]] under strained circumstances, he is passing through the Close when the body is discovered. He is the first to examine the dying man, and in those critical moments, he makes a choice that will shape everything that follows: finding a carefully folded scrap of paper in Braden's purse, Bryce "made haste to secrete it in his own pocket" (003-054) rather than surrender it to the police.

This act — half instinct, half calculation — places Bryce in possession of the one piece of physical evidence that might unlock the dead man's identity. It also places him outside the law, a position he will occupy for the rest of the novel.

### Mark Ransford

[[Mark Ransford|Ransford]], the town's senior doctor and Bryce's former employer, does not appear at the scene — but his connection to the dead man is immediate and concealed. When news of the death reaches him, Ransford's reaction betrays recognition. He knows the dead man. He knows his real name. And he knows why he came to Wrychester. But Ransford says nothing, placing himself in opposition to the investigation from its first hour.

> Ransford, when he heard the news, went white and silent. He asked no questions. He offered no theories. He simply withdrew — and in that withdrawal, Bryce saw the first shadow of a secret. (004-001)

### Detective-Sergeant Mitchington

[[Detective-Sergeant Mitchington|Mitchington]] is the official face of the investigation, "a well-known police official" (003-054) who arrives at the scene with professional promptness. He conducts the initial examination, arranges for the removal of the body, and begins the process of gathering witness statements. Competent and thorough within the boundaries of his role, Mitchington represents the institutional machinery of justice — but the machinery can only process what it is given, and the key pieces of information are being withheld.

## The Scrap of Paper

The scrap of paper Bryce takes from Braden's purse becomes the novel's central MacGuffin. It is "a quarter-sheet of thin notepaper" (005-002), folded in an unusual pattern, bearing cryptic markings. Bryce studies it obsessively, convinced it holds the secret of the dead man's purpose in Wrychester — and perhaps the secret of his death.

That Bryce keeps the paper rather than handing it to the police is not incidental. It marks the moment when the investigation splits into parallel tracks — the official inquiry, proceeding without the paper, and Bryce's private inquiry, powered by it. It also makes Bryce complicit in the concealment he will later accuse Ransford of sustaining.

## Consequences

The death of John Braden does not end with the inquest or the burial. It is the first stone in an avalanche:

- **The official investigation stalls.** The police gather evidence, the coroner hears testimony, but without the scrap of paper and without Ransford's knowledge, the official machinery cannot determine whether the death was accident or murder — let alone who the dead man really was.

- **Bryce begins his pursuit of Ransford.** Armed with the paper and driven by a mix of professional curiosity and personal ambition — he wants [[Mary Bewery]], and Ransford stands in his way — Bryce turns the death into a weapon.

- **A second death follows.** [[Collishaw]], a mason's labourer who knows something about the Paradise death, dies suddenly — poisoned. The [[death-of-collishaw|second death]] confirms what the first death suggested: someone in Wrychester is willing to kill to keep the past buried.

- **The truth of identity emerges.** Braden is not Braden. He is John Brake, and his presence in Wrychester is connected to a crime committed years before — a crime in which Ransford has a hidden stake.
