# Dr. Ransford's House

**Dr. Ransford's House** is a residence and medical practice situated within the [[Cathedral Close]] of [[Wrychester]]. It is the home of Dr. [[Mark Ransford]] and his two wards, [[Mary Bewery]] and [[Dick Bewery]], and the site of the surgery where [[Pemberton Bryce]] served as Ransford's assistant for nearly two years. The house plays a pivotal role in the novel's opening events: it is here that the mysterious stranger [[John Braden]] first calls seeking Ransford on the morning of his death.

## Description

The house includes a surgery at its side, accessed by a "long stonewalled passage which led to his surgery at the side of the house" (001-058). The surgery has a side entrance that is "screened from the Close by a shrubbery" (004-006), making callers at that door largely unobserved. Inside, the surgery contains Ransford's desk, drug preparation areas, and a side window overlooking the garden.

The garden itself is visible from the surgery side window, and it is here that Mary Bewery could sometimes be seen walking with visitors (002-070). The house is described as part of the Ransford "ménage," a term used by the gossiping society of the Close — which was "greatly exercised over the position of the Ransford ménage" (005-012) — to describe the unconventional domestic arrangement of a bachelor doctor living with two young wards.

The house was one of the residences in the southeast corner of the Close, accessible via the pathways through [[Paradise]]. A pathway "led towards the houses in the southeast corner of the Close" (003-029), situating Ransford's residence among the cluster of dwellings occupied by the professional class of the cathedral city.

> The society of the Close was greatly exercised over the position of the Ransford ménage. Ransford, a bachelor, a well-preserved, active, alert man who was certainly of no more than middle age and did not look his years, had come to Wrychester only a few years previously, and had never shown any signs of forsaking his single state. No one had ever heard him mention his family or relations; then, suddenly, without warning, he had brought into his house Mary Bewery, a handsome young woman of nineteen, who was said to have only just left school, and her brother Richard, then a boy of sixteen.
>
> (005-012)

## Role in the Story

**The Stranger's Call.** On the morning of the death of John Braden, the stranger — later identified as Braden, also known as John Brake — came to the surgery door seeking Dr. Ransford. He asked: "Is this — is Dr. Ransford within? I was told this is his house" (003-006). Bryce, who had just been dismissed from his position, directed the stranger towards the Deanery, where he claimed Ransford had gone. The stranger "came, shyly and furtively" (003-046), his manner suggesting secrecy and urgency.

**Bryce's Dismissal.** Earlier that same morning, Ransford dismissed Bryce from his position as assistant, writing him a cheque and telling him he must leave within a month. The dismissal was prompted by Bryce's persistent unwanted attentions towards Mary Bewery, whom Ransford was determined to protect. The tense conversation took place in the surgery, where Bryce — who "always walked into a room as if its occupant were asleep" (002-001) — had worked for nearly two years.

**Ransford's Return.** After the discovery of the body in Paradise, Bryce ran back to the house to observe Ransford's reaction. He found Ransford in the surgery, "the very picture of despair, stood just within, his face convulsed, beating one hand upon the other" (003-065). This moment deepened Bryce's suspicion that Ransford was implicated in the stranger's death.

**The Society's Speculation.** The house and its domestic arrangements were the subject of considerable gossip among the Close's tea-table circles. "The society of the Close was greatly exercised over the position of the Ransford ménage" (005-012). Ransford, a bachelor of no more than middle age, had come to Wrychester only a few years previously and had suddenly brought Mary Bewery, a handsome young woman of nineteen, and her brother Richard into his house as his wards — without explanation. The presence of the young and attractive Mary Bewery, living without a chaperon under the roof of a bachelor doctor, was a source of endless speculation and disapproval.

## Location Context

The house is situated within the Cathedral Close of Wrychester, a walled ecclesiastical precinct entered through a medieval gateway. It lies in the southeast corner of the Close, near the pathways leading through Paradise — the ancient burial ground that serves as a shortcut between the Cathedral, the Close residences, and the outer parts of the precinct.

The house's side entrance, screened by shrubbery, opens towards the Close, while its garden and windows face the interior of the property. Its proximity to the Cathedral and Paradise places it at the geographical heart of the novel's mystery: the surgery door where Braden called, the west porch from which Ransford emerged in agitation, and the spot beneath St. Wrytha's Stair where Braden's body was found all lie within a few hundred yards of one another.
