# Indiscretions of Archie (the Novel)

*Indiscretions of Archie* is a comic novel by [[p-g-wodehouse|P. G. Wodehouse]], first published in book form in [[1921]] by the [[george-h-doran-company|George H. Doran Company]] of [[new-york-city|New York]], following serialization in *[[cosmopolitan-magazine|Cosmopolitan]]* magazine in 1920. It tells the story of Archibald "[[archie-moffam|Archie]]" Moffam, an amiable but perpetually broke [[gentlemanly-conduct|English gentleman]] navigating marriage, [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]] troubles, and the general chaos of life in Prohibition-era [[new-york-city|New York]].

## Overview

[[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]] wrote *Indiscretions of Archie* during a period of intense productivity in the years following [[world-war-i|World War I]]. The novel is structured as a series of loosely connected comic episodes rather than a single sustained plot — a format that suited both its magazine serialization and [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]]'s gift for the self-contained comic set-piece.

The novel introduces [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]], one of [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]]'s characteristic innocents abroad: well-meaning, perpetually optimistic, and possessed of a genius for turning simple situations into cascading disasters. Unlike the more famous Bertie Wooster (who would debut later), [[archie-moffam|Archie]] is married — to [[lucille-brewster|Lucille Brewster]], daughter of hotel magnate [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]] — and the novel derives much of its comic energy from the triangular tension between [[archie-moffam|Archie]], his adoring wife, and his perpetually exasperated [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]].

## Publication History

The novel's publication details appear in the front matter:

> "NEW YORK GEORGE H. DORAN COMPANY COPYRIGHT, 1921, BY GEORGE H. DORAN COMPANY" (001-044)

> "COPYRIGHT, 1920, BY INTERNATIONAL MAGAZINE COMPANY (COSMOPOLITAN MAGAZINE)" (001-045)

The book was thus first serialized in *[[cosmopolitan-magazine|Cosmopolitan]]* magazine in 1920, with the book edition following in [[1921]] from [[george-h-doran-company|George H. Doran]], a major [[new-york-city|New York]] publisher of the era. The dual copyright reflects the standard publishing practice of the time: magazine serialization rights held by one entity, book rights by another.

### Literary Context

*Indiscretions of Archie* occupies an interesting position in [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]]'s bibliography. Published in [[1921]], it falls between his early school stories and the mature Jeeves-and-Wooster series that would define his reputation. The novel shows [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]] experimenting with an American setting and a married protagonist — both dep[[art-and-collecting|art]]ures from the formula that would later make him famous. While not as celebrated as the Jeeves novels, *Indiscretions of Archie* contains many of the hallmarks of [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]] at his best: precisely engineered comic [[conversation-and-dialogue|dialogue]], a deep affection for his characters' foibles, and an unshakeable faith that everything will turn out all right in the end.

The novel is dedicated to B. W. King-Hall, "My dear Buddy," a friend of eighteen years under whose roof [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]] wrote several of his books (001-001).
