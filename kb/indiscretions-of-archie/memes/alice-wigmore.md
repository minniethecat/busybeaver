# Alice Wigmore

Alice Wigmore is the fiancée of magazine illustrator [[j-b-wheeler|J. B. Wheeler]] and the painter of "The Wigmore Venus," an oil painting of a comfortably plump young woman intended to represent the goddess Venus. She has since given up painting after Wheeler taught her golf (034-172).

## Character and Background

Alice is introduced late in the novel as Wheeler's fiancée — a former painter who has abandoned her art for golf, much to Wheeler's relief. Wheeler describes her as having artistic talent that he found inconvenient: "She's given it up now. I taught her golf" (034-172). The implication is that Wheeler, a successful magazine illustrator, preferred a non-competing partner.

Her artistic legacy survives in a single work: the Wigmore Venus, a portrait of an unnamed model — "a comfortably plump young woman who, from her rather weak-minded simper and the fact that she wore absolutely nothing except a small dove on her left shoulder, was plainly intended to be the goddess Venus" (034-004–006).

## The Wigmore Venus

The painting becomes the central object of the novel's final chapters. Archie encounters it in Wheeler's studio and, through a series of comic misunderstandings, the painting ends up in [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]]'s possession at the Cosmopolis Hotel — where Brewster, an art collector of more enthusiasm than discernment, hangs it prominently in his private quarters.

The painting's effect on viewers is consistently strong and frequently unfortunate. Brewster finds his gaze drawn irresistibly to it: "his eyes, try as he might to wrench them away, insisted on climbing up the wall and resting on the Wigmore Venus" (035-049). For Archie, the painting "hit him behind the ear like a stuffed eel-skin" (035-081) — a Wodehousian description of its unsettling impact. By the novel's end, the Venus seems to smile knowingly at Archie: "The Wigmore Venus smiled up at Archie — it seemed to him with a sort of derisive mockery" (036-150).
