## Section 33

[033-001] “What do you want?” he demanded.

[033-002] “Hallo, old thing!” said [[archie-moffam|Archie]]. “Come and join the party!”

[033-003] “Don’t call me old thing!”

[033-004] “Right-o, old companion, just as you say. I say, I was just going to

[033-005] suggest to [[mr-connolly|Mr. Connolly]] that we should all go up to my suite and [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]]

[033-006] this business over quietly.”

[033-007] “He says he’s the manager of your new hotel,” said Mr. Connolly. “Is

[033-008] that right?”

[033-009] “I suppose so,” said [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]], gloomily.

[033-010] “Then I’m doing you a kindness,” said Mr. Connolly, “in not letting it

[033-011] be built.”

[033-012] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] dabbed at his forehead with his handkerchief. The moments were

[033-013] flying, and it began to seem impossible to shift these two men. Mr.

[033-014] Connolly was as firmly settled in his chair as some primeval rock. As

[033-015] for Mr. Brewster, he, too, had seated himself, and was gazing at Archie

[033-016] with a weary repulsion. Mr. Brewster’s glance always made Archie feel

[033-017] as though there were soup on his shirt-front.

[033-018] And suddenly from the orchestra at the other end of the room there came

[033-019] a familiar sound, the prelude of “Mother’s Knee.”

[033-020] “So you’ve started a cabaret, Dan?” said Mr. Connolly, in a satisfied

[033-021] voice. “I always told you you were behind the times here!”

[033-022] Mr. Brewster jumped.

[033-023] “Cabaret!”

[033-024] He stared unbelievingly at the white-robed figure which had just

[033-025] mounted the orchestra dais, and then concentrated his gaze on Archie.

[033-026] Archie would not have looked at his [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]] at this juncture if

[033-027] he had had a free and untrammelled choice; but Mr. Brewster’s eye drew

[033-028] his with something of the fascination which a snake’s has for a rabbit.

[033-029] Mr. Brewster’s eye was fiery and intimidating. A basilisk might have

[033-030] gone to him with advantage for a course of lessons. His gaze went right

[033-031] through Archie till the latter seemed to feel his back-hair curling

[033-032] crisply in the flames.

[033-033] “Is this one of your fool-tricks?”

[033-034] Even in this tense moment Archie found time almost unconsciously to

[033-035] admire his father-in-law’s penetration and intuition. He seemed to have

[033-036] a sort of sixth sense. No doubt this was how great fortunes were made.

[033-037] “Well, as a matter of fact—to be absolutely accurate—it was like this—”

[033-038] “Say, cut it out!” said Mr. Connolly. “Can the chatter! I want to

[033-039] listen.”

[033-040] Archie was only too ready to oblige him. Conversation at the moment was

[033-041] the last thing he himself desired. He managed with a strong effort to

[033-042] disengage himself from Mr. Brewster’s eye, and turned to the orchestra

[033-043] dais, where Miss [[spectatia-huskisson|Spectatia Huskisson]] was now beginning the first verse

[033-044] of [[wilson-hymack|Wilson Hymack]]’s masterpiece.

[033-045] Miss Huskisson, like so many of the female denizens of the Middle West,

[033-046] was tall and blonde and constructed on substantial lines. She was a

[033-047] girl whose [[appearance-and-first-impressions|appearance]] suggested the old homestead and fried pancakes

[033-048] and pop coming home to [[dining-and-social-ritual|dinner]] after the morning’s ploughing. Even her

[033-049] bobbed hair did not altogether destroy this impression. She looked big

[033-050] and strong and healthy, and her lungs were obviously good. She attacked

[033-051] the verse of the song with something of the vigour and breadth of

[033-052] treatment with which in other days she had reasoned with refractory

[033-053] mules. Her diction was the diction of one trained to call the cattle

[033-054] home in the teeth of Western hurricanes. Whether you wanted to or not,

[033-055] you heard every word.

[033-056] The subdued clatter of knives and forks had ceased. The diners, unused

[033-057] to this sort of thing at [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]], were trying to adjust their

[033-058] faculties to cope with the outburst. Waiters stood transfixed, frozen,

[033-059] in attitudes of service. In the momentary lull between verse and

[033-060] refrain Archie could hear the deep breathing of Mr. Brewster.

[033-061] Involuntarily he turned to gaze at him once more, as refugees from

[033-062] Pompeii may have turned to gaze upon Vesuvius; and, as he did so, he

[033-063] caught sight of Mr. Connolly, and paused in astonishment.

[033-064] Mr. Connolly was an altered man. His whole personality had undergone a

[033-065] subtle change. His face still looked as though hewn from the living

[033-066] rock, but into his eyes had crept an expression which in another man

[033-067] might almost have been called sentimental. Incredible as it seemed to

[033-068] Archie, Mr. Connolly’s eyes were dreamy. There was even in them a

[033-069] suggestion of unshed tears. And when with a vast culmination of sound

[033-070] Miss Huskisson reached the high note at the end of the refrain and,

[033-071] after holding it as some storming-party, spent but victorious, holds

[033-072] the summit of a hard-won redoubt, broke off suddenly, in the stillness

[033-073] which followed there proceeded from Mr. Connolly a deep sigh.

[033-074] Miss Huskisson began the second verse. And Mr. Brewster, seeming to

[033-075] recover from some kind of a trance, leaped to his feet.

[033-076] “Great Godfrey!”

[033-077] “Sit down!” said Mr. Connolly, in a broken voice. “Sit down, Dan!”

[033-078] “He went back to his mother on the train that very day:

[033-079] He knew there was no other who could make him bright and gay:

[033-080] He kissed her on the forehead and he whispered, ‘I’ve come home!’

[033-081] He told her he was never going any more to roam.

[033-082] And onward through the happy years, till he grew old and grey,

[033-083] He never once regretted those brave words he once did say:

[033-084] It’s a long way back to mother’s knee—”

[033-085] The last high note screeched across the room like a shell, and the

[033-086] applause that followed was like a shell’s bursting. One could hardly

[033-087] have recognised the refined interior of [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]] dining-room.

[033-088] Fair women were waving napkins; brave men were hammering on the tables

[033-089] with the butt-end of knives, for all the world as if they imagined

[033-090] themselves to be in one of those distressing midnight-revue places.

[033-091] Miss Huskisson bowed, retired, returned, bowed, and retired again, the

[033-092] tears streaming down her ample face. Over in a corner Archie could see

[033-093] his brother-in-law clapping strenuously. A waiter, with a display of

[033-094] manly emotion that did him credit, dropped an order of new peas.

[033-095] “Thirty years ago last October,” said Mr. Connolly, in a shaking voice,

[033-096] “I—”

[033-097] Mr. Brewster interrupted him violently.

[033-098] “I’ll fire that orchestra-leader! He goes to-morrow! I’ll fire—” He

[033-099] turned on Archie. “What the devil do you mean by it, you—you—”

[033-100] “Thirty years ago,” said Mr. Connolly, wiping away a tear with his

[033-101] napkin, “I left me dear old home in the old country—”

[033-102] “_My_ hotel a bear-garden!”

[033-103] “Frightfully sorry and all that, old companion—”

[033-104] “Thirty years ago last October! ’Twas a fine autumn evening the finest

[033-105] ye’d ever wish to see. Me old mother, she came to the station to see me

[033-106] off.”

[033-107] Mr. Brewster, who was not deeply interested in Mr. Connolly’s old

[033-108] mother, continued to splutter inarticulately, like a firework trying to

[033-109] go off.

[033-110] “‘Ye’ll always be a good boy, Aloysius?’ she said to me,” said Mr.

[033-111] Connolly, proceeding with, his autobiography. “And I said: ‘Yes,

[033-112] Mother, I will!’” Mr. Connolly sighed and applied the napkin again.

[033-113] “’Twas a liar I was!” he observed, remorsefully. “Many’s the dirty I’ve

[033-114] played since then. ‘It’s a long way back to Mother’s knee.’ ’Tis a true

[033-115] word!” He turned impulsively to Mr. Brewster. “Dan, there’s a deal of

[033-116] trouble in this world without me going out of me way to make more. The

[033-117] strike is over! I’ll send the men back tomorrow! There’s me hand on

[033-118] it!”

[033-119] Mr. Brewster, who had just managed to co-ordinate his views on the

[033-120] situation and was about to express them with the generous strength

[033-121] which was ever his custom when dealing with his son-in-law, checked

[033-122] himself abruptly. He stared at his old friend and business enemy,

[033-123] wondering if he could have heard aright. Hope began to creep back into

[033-124] Mr. Brewster’s heart, like a shamefaced dog that has been away from

[033-125] home hunting for a day or two.

[033-126] “You’ll what!”

[033-127] “I’ll send the men back to-morrow! That song was sent to guide me, Dan!

[033-128] It was meant! Thirty years ago last October me dear old mother—”

[033-129] Mr. Brewster bent forward attentively. His views on Mr. Connolly’s dear

[033-130] old mother had changed. He wanted to hear all about her.

[033-131] “’Twas that last note that girl sang brought it all back to me as if

[033-132] ’twas yesterday. As we waited on the platform, me old mother and I, out

[033-133] comes the train from the tunnel, and the engine lets off a screech the

[033-134] way ye’d hear it ten miles away. ’Twas thirty years ago—”

[033-135] Archie stole softly from the table. He felt that his presence, if it

[033-136] had ever been required, was required no longer. Looking back, he could

[033-137] see his father-in-law patting Mr. Connolly affectionately on the

[033-138] shoulder.

[033-139] Archie and [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] lingered over their coffee. [[maxie-blumenthal|Mr. Blumenthal]] was out

[033-140] in the telephone-box settling the business end with Wilson Hymack. The

[033-141] music-publisher had been unstinted in his praise of “Mother’s Knee.” It

[033-142] was sure-fire, he said. The words, stated Mr. Blumenthal, were gooey

[033-143] enough to hurt, and the tune reminded him of every other song-hit he

[033-144] had ever heard. There was, in Mr. Blumenthal’s opinion, nothing to stop

[033-145] this thing selling a million copies.

[033-146] Archie smoked contentedly.

[033-147] “Not a bad evening’s work, old thing,” he said. “Talk about birds with

[033-148] one stone!” He looked at Lucille reproachfully. “You don’t seem

[033-149] bubbling over with joy.”

[033-150] “Oh, I am, precious!” Lucille sighed. “I was only thinking about Bill.”

[033-151] “What about Bill?”

[033-152] “Well, it’s rather awful to think of him tied for life to that-that

[033-153] steam-siren.”

[033-154] “Oh, we mustn’t look on the jolly old dark side. Perhaps—Hallo, Bill,

[033-155] old top! We were just talking about you.”

[033-156] “Were you?” said [[bill-brewster|Bill Brewster]], in a dispirited voice.

[033-157] “I take it that you want congratulations, what?”

[033-158] “I want sympathy!”

[033-159] “Sympathy?”

[033-160] “Sympathy! And lots of it! She’s gone!”

[033-161] “Gone! Who?”

[033-162] “Spectatia!”

[033-163] “How do you mean, gone?”

[033-164] Bill glowered at the tablecloth.

[033-165] “Gone home. I’ve just seen her off in a cab. She’s gone back to

[033-166] Washington Square to pack. She’s catching the ten o’clock train back to

[033-167] Snake Bite. It was that damned song!” muttered Bill, in a stricken

[033-168] voice. “She says she never realised before she sang it to-night how

[033-169] hollow [[new-york-city|New York]] was. She said it suddenly came over her. She says she’s

[033-170] going to give up her career and go back to her mother. What the deuce

[033-171] are you twiddling your fingers for?” he broke off, irritably.

[033-172] “Sorry, old man. I was just counting.”

[033-173] “Counting? Counting what?”

[033-174] “Birds, old thing. Only birds!” said Archie.

[033-175] CHAPTER XXV.

[033-176] THE WIGMORE VENUS

[033-177] The morning was so brilliantly fine; the populace popped to and fro in

[033-178] so active and cheery a manner; and everybody appeared to be so

[033-179] absolutely in the pink, that a casual observer of the city of New York

[033-180] would have said that it was one of those happy days. Yet Archie Moffam,

[033-181] as he turned out of the sun-bathed street into the ramshackle building

[033-182] on the third floor of which was the studio belonging to his artist

[033-183] friend, James B. [[j-b-wheeler|Wheeler]], was faintly oppressed with a sort of a kind

[033-184] of feeling that something was wrong. He would not have gone so far as

[033-185] to say that he had the pip—it was more a vague sense of discomfort.

[033-186] And, searching for first causes as he made his way upstairs, he came to

[033-187] the conclusion that the person responsible for this nebulous depression

[033-188] was his wife, Lucille. It seemed to Archie that at breakfast that

[033-189] morning Lucille’s manner had been subtly rummy. Nothing you could put

[033-190] your finger on, still—rummy.

[033-191] Musing thus, he reached the studio, and found the door open and the

[033-192] room empty. It had the air of a room whose owner has dashed in to fetch

[033-193] his [[leisure-and-sports|golf]]-[[clubs-and-social-institutions|clubs]] and biffed off, after the casual fashion of the artist

[033-194] temperament, without bothering to close up behind him. And such,

[033-195] indeed, was the case. The studio had seen the last of J. B. Wheeler for

[033-196] that day: but Archie, not realising this and feeling that a chat with

[033-197] Mr. Wheeler, who was a light-hearted bird, was what he needed this

[033-198] morning, sat down to wait. After a few moments, his gaze, straying over

[033-199] the room, encountered a handsomely framed picture, and he went across

[033-200] to take a look at it.
