# The Iliad

*The Iliad* is the ancient Greek epic poem attributed to Homer, composed circa the 8th century BCE. In [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]], the narrator name-drops Homer in a moment of mock-epic humility — a brief flash of classical erudition in Wodehouse's otherwise relentlessly modern New York.

## In the Novel

The reference appears in Chapter XXX, during a verse passage in which the narrator describes a pie-eating contest between two rivals. Confronted with the challenge of doing justice to so titanic a gastronomic struggle, the narrator invokes the ultimate benchmark:

> We freely own we'd give a leg if we could borrow, steal, or beg the skill old Homer used to show. (He wrote the *Iliad*, you know.) Old Homer swung a wicked pen, but we are ordinary men, and cannot even start to dream of doing justice to our theme. (030-014–030-017)

The parenthetical "(He wrote the *Iliad*, you know.)" — with its faux-helpful, almost Wodehousian tone — is the joke. The narrator pretends his audience might need a reminder of who Homer was, then admits he himself cannot match the epic poet's descriptive powers. The passage is a miniature of Wodehouse's comic method: the collision of high-cultural reference with low-stakes subject matter (a pie-eating contest) produces the humor.

## Literary Function

The *Iliad* reference belongs to a tradition of mock-epic that stretches back to Pope and Fielding. By invoking Homer in the context of a hotel dining-room eating competition, Wodehouse both elevates and deflates the scene — the grandeur of the allusion makes the triviality of the event funnier, not more solemn. The technique is similar to the narrator's reference to [[the-adventures-of-sherlock-holmes|Sherlock Holmes]] in Chapter VIII: real-world literary touchstones that root the fictional world while inviting the reader to smile at the incongruity.

## Associated Characters

The reference is purely narratorial and involves no diegetic character. The narrator himself is the speaker of the verse passage.

## Real-World Context

*The Iliad*, set during the Trojan War, is one of the foundational works of Western literature. By 1921, when Wodehouse published *Indiscretions of Archie*, Homer's epics were standard educational fare for classically educated Englishmen — and a reliable source of comic allusion for writers who could count on their readers recognizing the name even if they had never read the Greek.
