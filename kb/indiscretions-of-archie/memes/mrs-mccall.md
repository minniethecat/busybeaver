## Overview

Mrs. Cora Bates McCall is an eminent lecturer on "Rational Eating" and the formidable matriarch of the McCall household. She practises as well as preaches her dietary doctrines, subjecting her husband Mr. McCall and son Washington "Washy" McCall to a regime of Health Bread, nut butter, and an "peculiarly uninviting cereal, which looked and tasted like an old straw hat that had been run through a meat chopper" [029-079]. Her authority is shattered when she discovers that Washy has secretly become a pie-eating champion.

## In the Novel

### The Breakfast Table Tyrant

Mrs. McCall presides over the family breakfast with the same platform authority she brings to her public lectures. She monitors Washy's appetite as a vindication of her methods: "I shudder when I think of the growing boys who are permitted by irresponsible people to devour meat, candy, pie" [029-108]. Her husband, a "little hunted-looking man" with semicircular glasses, responds to her pronouncements with nervous monosyllables [029-079].

Her mood each day depends on whether the newspaper has adequately reported her activities. When a report is satisfactory, "Mrs. McCall's mood for the next twelve hours would be as uniformly sunny as it was possible for it to be" [029-145].

### The Pie Revelation

Mrs. McCall's world collapses when she reads a newspaper report revealing that her son Washy has won the Great Pie Championship. "Mrs. McCall gave a sharp shriek, and the paper fluttered from her hand" [029-158]. She reads on "with burning eyes" as "a bright wave of colour had flowed over her masterful features" [029-158].

Confronting Archie, who took Washy to the championship, her reaction escalates from icy politeness to outrage: "'I think,' said Mrs. McCall, icily, 'that you must have mistaken your profession'" [030-069]. When faced with the full truth, she can only shudder: "'Do you mean this—this—' Mrs. McCall shuddered. 'Are you referring to my son?'" [030-101].

### Loss of Authority

Mrs. McCall's dietary regime collapses when Mr. McCall, emboldened by Washy's thriving on pie, finally rebels: "I don't want to hurt your feelings, Cora, but Washington and I have drunk our last cup of anti-caffeine!" [030-154–030-158]. The narrator notes her origins as "Miss Cora Bates" and describes how "with eloquence the world she weans from chops and steaks and pork and beans" [030], a mission now undone within her own household.

## Character

Mrs. McCall is one of Wodehouse's formidable female figures — a woman whose intellectual convictions have hardened into domestic tyranny. Her lectures on "The Nation's Menace—Pie" become bitterly ironic when her own son proves to be the nation's premier pie consumer. She is a satirical portrait of the early twentieth-century food-reform movement, and her defeat at the hands of pie represents the novel's broader allegiance to pleasure over puritanism.
