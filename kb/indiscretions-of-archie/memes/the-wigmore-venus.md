# The Wigmore Venus

An oil painting by the artist Alice Wigmore, fiancee of the magazine illustrator J. B. Wheeler, depicting the artist herself as the goddess Venus. The painting — admired by almost no one except Archie Moffam and its creator — passes through multiple owners across the novel's final chapters, serving as the comic MacGuffin that ultimately reconciles Archie with his father-in-law.

## Description

The painting depicts "the goddess Venus" (034-007), rendered as a comfortably plump young woman wearing only a small dove on her left shoulder. Archie, who admits he is no art expert, nevertheless responds to it instinctively: he "knew enough about Art to recognise Venus when" he saw it (034-008). "The Venus caught his eye" (034-040) and he finds it compelling in a way he cannot articulate.

The painting is "handsomely framed" and of sufficient size to require consideration of "the wall as suitable for the Venus" (034-142) when being hung.

## In the Novel

The Wigmore Venus enters the narrative when Archie visits J. B. Wheeler's studio and encounters the painting. Despite — or perhaps because of — its questionable artistic merit, Archie becomes fixated on it. The painting sets off a chain of transactions:

Archie acquires the Venus, intending it as a wedding anniversary gift for his wife Lucille. Lucille, in turn, regifts it to her father Daniel Brewster for his birthday — a practical decision driven more by a desire to remove the picture from her own suite than by genuine generosity.

Mr. Brewster's reaction is one of horror. "His admiration for the Wigmore Venus, which had faltered" (034-185) upon closer inspection, turns to active loathing. Upon the "discovery of the Wigmore Venus in his artistically furnished" suite (035-049), suspended on his wall, he confronts it with mounting fury. "He stared at the Venus dumbly. Unlike most hotel-pro[prietors]" (035-076), Brewster is a connoisseur — and the Venus offends every artistic sensibility he possesses. The impact is visceral: "the Wigmore Venus hit him behind the ear like a" physical blow (035-081).

In the novel's climactic resolution, Archie destroys the painting — an act that unexpectedly earns Mr. Brewster's respect. In a final, almost tender moment, "The Wigmore Venus smiled up at Archie — it seemed" (036-150), as if the painting itself acknowledges the comic rightness of its destruction.
