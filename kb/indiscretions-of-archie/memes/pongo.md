# Pongo

Pongo is a small antique china figurine of a warrior in armour advancing with a raised spear, dating to the Ming Dynasty. It stands on the mantelpiece in Daniel Brewster's suite at the Hotel Cosmopolis and becomes the centre of a comic subplot involving art authentication, auctions, and family reconciliation.

## Description

The figurine represents a warrior "of pre-khaki days advancing with a spear upon some adversary who, judging from the contented expression on the warrior's face, was smaller than himself" (PN: 001-192). Archie Moffam calls it a "rummy-looking what-not" and whimsically names it Pongo, admitting he doesn't "know what else you could call him" (PN: 009-134). It is one of a pair — the companion figure is identical in design — and together the pair is worth "several thousand dollars" according to Parker the valet (PN: 009-147).

## In the Novel

Pongo is first introduced in the opening chapter when Professor Binstead examines it on Brewster's mantelpiece, calling it "a small china figure of delicate workmanship" (PN: 001-192). Parker, Brewster's valet, later explains to Archie that the figure alone is not especially valuable, but the pair together would be worth a considerable sum (PN: 009-099–009-147).

The "revival of Pongo" occurs several weeks later during the summer at Brookport (PN: 010-001). The companion figure appears at an art auction, prompting Archie to attempt to purchase it in order to curry favour with his father-in-law. The comic negotiation with the auction house, involving the "dog-faced" assistant Willie and an elaborate bidding war, forms the core of the Pongo subplot (PN: 011-036–012-037).

The subplot resolves when Archie secures the companion figure, hoping to present it to Brewster and "break the news with the other" about various domestic complications (PN: 017-045).

## Real-World Context

The figurine is described as dating to the [[ming-dynasty]] (PN: 012-020), a real Chinese imperial dynasty (1368–1644) known for its porcelain and ceramic artistry. Antique Ming Dynasty china figures are indeed highly valued by collectors.
