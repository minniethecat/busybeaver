# The Sausage Chappie

The "Sausage Chappie" (revealed to be named John Smith) is a First World War veteran who suffers from amnesia, encountered by Archie Moffam on a Broadway street. Having shared half a sausage with a starving Archie at the Battle of St. Mihiel, he is taken under Archie's protection, employed as a waiter at the Cosmopolis Hotel, and eventually catapulted into a movie career after demonstrating his peerless pie-throwing abilities. His character arc spans three named chapters ("The Sausage Chappie," "The-Sausage-Chappie-Clicks," and "The Growing Boy") and forms one of the novel's most elaborate comic subplots.

## Role in the Novel

The Sausage Chappie appears in four chapters as a major subplot character whose trajectory — from homeless amnesiac to Hollywood prospect — mirrors the novel's thematic concern with postwar dislocation and the restorative power of loyalty. Archie's dogged protection of him, driven by gratitude for the wartime sausage, drives conflict with Daniel Brewster and ultimately produces an unlikely happy ending. (PN 022-074, 027-110)

His subplot intersects with multiple major characters: Archie (as benefactor), Daniel Brewster (as reluctant employer), James Gossett (as unwitting career-launcher), and his unnamed wife (as emotional anchor). The pie-throwing climax in the Cosmopolis grill-room is one of the novel's most elaborate set-pieces. (PN 027-003–027-089)

## Biography / Backstory

The Sausage Chappie's pre-amnesia identity is slowly revealed: he was born in Springfield, Ohio, and worked as an actor before the war. A shrapnel wound to the head caused memory loss — he can remember everything after emerging from hospital, but nothing before. He spent Armistice night wandering the streets, having signed the pledge earlier that evening. (PN 023-035–023-041, 026-103–026-104, 027-056–027-057)

The defining moment of his past is the wartime encounter at St. Mihiel, France, where he encountered a starving Archie Moffam at the end of a five-hour hike and selflessly shared half a sausage. Archie never forgot this act of generosity, and the phrase "he gave me a bit of sausage" becomes a refrain throughout the novel. (PN 022-145–022-149, 023-089–023-094, 026-068–026-070)

After the war, homeless and penniless with no memory of his identity, he was reduced to sleeping on Park benches before Archie recognized him on Broadway. (PN 023-044–023-045)

## Personality and Traits

His most distinguishing physical characteristic is his face — scarred, homely, and memorably "funny." A shrapnel wound left a scar running from the corner of his mouth halfway across his cheek. When he smiles, his expression is so odd that "odd became a mild adjective." Yet the face is "unquestionably genial" with "a quality of humorous appeal." A child in the grill-room fixates on him, repeatedly asking his mother why the man has "such a funny face." (PN 022-124–022-131, 026-079–026-091)

Despite his disconcerting appearance, he possesses a cheerful, uncomplaining disposition. He "chuckles amusedly" at Archie's remarks, "beams" when praised, and approaches his work as a waiter with brisk attentiveness, doing it "as if he liked it." He is also notably absent-minded — when his memory begins returning, he remembers his name as Smith with an almost comic ordinariness after running through a roster of more exotic possibilities. (PN 022-167, 026-065–026-066, 026-107–026-118, 027-055)

His sartorial choices are memorably eccentric: a green scarf, an evening-dress coat, tweed trousers built for a larger man, a straw hat, and brown shoes — a combination so jarring that it physically staggers the fashion-sensitive Reggie van Tuyl. (PN 022-108–022-112)

As a pie-thrower, he demonstrates what Archie calls "without a peer" skill — his accuracy, speed, and control are compared favorably to Fatty Arbuckle and Charlie Chaplin. He has always wanted to go into the movies, and before the war he was an actor — a detail he remembers only after the pie-throwing incident. (PN 027-044–027-057)

## Key Relationships

- **[[archie-moffam]]** — Archie's unwavering benefactor, driven by gratitude for the wartime sausage. Archie shelters him at the Cosmopolis, secures him a waiter's job, and acts as his "personal representative" in negotiating the movie contract with Gossett. (PN 022-145–022-149, 023-089–023-094, 027-036–027-075)

- **[[daniel-brewster]]** — The reluctant employer. Brewster is outraged to find a "tramp comedian" in his bedroom trying on his clothes, but is eventually coerced into giving the Sausage Chappie a job as a waiter. (PN 023-062–023-134)

- **James Gossett** — The movie magnate who becomes the Sausage Chappie's unlikely career-launcher after having a pie thrown in his face. Gossett initially threatens arrest but is won over by Archie's salesmanship and the "voice of the Public" — a child demanding more pies. (PN 027-008–027-089)

- **His Wife** — The unnamed woman and child in the grill-room turn out to be his wife and son. She cries "John!" upon recognizing him, and he realizes his name is John — "And I thought it was Lancelot!" Their reunion after his presumed death brings the subplot to its emotional conclusion. (PN 027-001–027-007)

- **[[reggie-van-tuyl]]** — Reggie is physically staggered by the Sausage Chappie's outfit and makes a hasty exit from the reunion, "a broken man." (PN 022-092–022-161)

## Real-World Context

Fictional character. The Sausage Chappie represents the postwar "lost man" archetype — the soldier whose identity was fractured by war trauma — treated through Wodehouse's characteristic comic lens. His trajectory from battlefield camaraderie to amnesia to recovery and Hollywood success mirrors American postwar anxieties about identity and reinvention.
