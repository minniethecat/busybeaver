# The Telegram

The telegram is a pivotal plot device in the opening chapters of *Indiscretions of Archie*. It arrives at the Hotel Cosmopolis bearing news that transforms Daniel Brewster's irritation at a complaining guest into the novel's central familial conflict: his daughter Lucille has married the very Englishman who has just insulted his hotel.

## The Event

The telegram arrives shortly after Archie's disastrous first encounter with Brewster in the hotel lobby. A bell-boy "returned with a telegram" (001-214), delivering it to Brewster's suite. The message is from Lucille, Brewster's daughter, informing her father that she has married Archie Moffam — the "long, thin, string-bean of an Englishman" whom Brewster has already resolved to eject from his hotel.

The timing is exquisite: Brewster has just declared war on Archie for calling the Cosmopolis "a bally rotten hotel," and now he learns that the same man is his son-in-law. The telegram collapses the distance between professional insult and personal injury, making the conflict inescapable.

## The Revelation

Brewster's opening of the telegram is one of the novel's finest comic set-pieces. He treats it casually — "It can wait. I get them all day long. I expect it's from Lucille, saying what train she's making" (001-224–225) — and continues eating his lunch. When he finally opens the envelope, the effect is instantaneous:

> He sat staring at the telegram, his mouth open. (002-005)

The message itself is a masterpiece of Wodehousian compression. Lucille's breezy announcement — "'Returning New York to-day with darling Archie. Lots of love from us both. Lucille.'" (002-012–013) — reduces marriage to the dimensions of a travel itinerary. Professor Binstead, reading over Brewster's shoulder, can only ask the obvious question: "Who is Archie?" (002-013), to which Brewster can only echo: "Who is Archie? That's just what I would like to know." (002-015–016)

The narrative voice supplies the devastating gloss:

> When you send your only daughter on a visit to Miami minus any entanglements and she mentions in a telegram that she has acquired a darling Archie, you are naturally startled. (002-019–022)

Brewster, now in full panic, recalls that a letter from Lucille arrived earlier in the week and was set aside unread — "Lucille was a dear girl, he had felt, but her letters when on a vacation seldom contained anything that couldn't wait a few days for a reading" (002-027–029). He springs for his desk to find the neglected letter, already sensing that his entire world has been rearranged by a few words on a telegraph slip.

## Aftermath

Brewster's reaction is one of stunned disbelief. "He sat staring at the telegram, his mouth open" (002-005), rendered speechless by the convergence of calamities. Professor Binstead, Brewster's friend, observes the scene: "the professor, musing over the telegram" (002-017) recognizes its implications even before Brewster fully absorbs them.

Lucille's message reveals that she "has acquired a darling" (002-021) — a characteristically breezy description of marriage that only compounds Brewster's horror. The telegram thus functions as both revelation and comic understatement, reducing the momentous fact of marriage to a few telegraphic words that shatter Brewster's world.
