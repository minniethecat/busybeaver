# The Girl Friend

The unnamed girlfriend of New York Giants pitcher Gus "Looney" Biddle, whose emotional volatility directly influences Biddle's performance on the mound. Her quarrels with Gus — and Archie Moffam's attempts to mediate them — drive the baseball subplot across chapters 18 through 20.

## In the Novel

Archie first encounters the Girl Friend when he realizes that "the red-haired female before him was the Girl Friend, in" the flesh (018-044), the woman whose romantic disputes hold the fate of the Giants' season in balance. She is forthright and opinionated, quick to pass judgment on celebrities: "Bryant Washburn was a pill! A pill!" she declares with "penetrating" conviction (018-055).

Her quarrel with Gus Biddle stems from a movie date gone wrong — Gus insulted the film actor Bryant Washburn, triggering an argument that sent the Girl Friend fleeing. Archie pursues her through New York in an attempt to broker a reconciliation. When Archie suggests she make up with Gus, she responds with characteristic directness: "You acted right, dearie" (018-065). Yet she is also capable of regret — "No," she says "regretfully. I don't believe" (018-070) when pressed on the possibility of reconciliation.

The Girl Friend's steadfastness is evident in her physical demeanor: she is described "walking with the firm and de[termined]" step (018-074) of someone who knows her own mind. Even when Archie pleads Gus's case for "the first five minutes" (018-098) of their encounter, she remains unmoved.

The emotional climax comes when Gus himself appears: "Gus! screamed the Girl Friend, bounding forward" (019-027), her earlier resolve dissolving at the sight of her wounded lover. Moments later, "releasing her wounded lover" (019-049), she becomes part of the resolution rather than the obstacle. Archie later reflects on the absurdity of the whole chase: "Why had he trailed the Girl Friend, dash[ing]" (020-017) through the city.

### Narrative Function

The Girl Friend functions as the human linchpin of the Looney Biddle subplot. Biddle's pitching — and by extension the Giants' fortunes — depends entirely on his romantic status. When he and the Girl Friend are quarreling, he collapses on the mound; when reconciled, he is unbeatable. In this sense, the Girl Friend wields more influence over New York baseball than any manager or owner.

Her anonymity — she is never given a proper name — emphasizes her function as a narrative device: the volatile love interest whose moods determine the fate of larger forces. Wodehouse uses her to satirize the disproportion between private romantic squabbles and public sporting spectacle.
