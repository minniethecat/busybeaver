## Section 12

[012-001] “One thousand is bid!” he carolled. “Now, gentlemen, I don’t want to

[012-002] hurry you over this. You are all connoisseurs here, and you don’t want

[012-003] to see a priceless china figure of the Ming Dynasty get away from you

[012-004] at a sacrifice price. Perhaps you can’t all see the figure where it is.

[012-005] Willie, take it round and show it to ’em. We’ll take a little

[012-006] intermission while you look carefully at this wonderful figure. Get a

[012-007] move on, Willie! Pick up your feet!”

[012-008] [[archie-moffam|Archie]], sitting dazedly, was aware that [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]] had finished

[012-009] his beauty sleep and was addressing the young man in the seat in front.

[012-010] “Why, hallo,” said Reggie. “I didn’t know you were back. You remember

[012-011] me, don’t you? Reggie van Tuyl. I know your sister very well. [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]],

[012-012] old man, I want you to meet my friend, Bill [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]]. Why, dash it!” He

[012-013] chuckled sleepily. “I was forgetting. Of course! He’s your—”

[012-014] “How are you?” said the young man. “Talking of my sister,” he said to

[012-015] Reggie, “I suppose you haven’t met her husband by any chance? I suppose

[012-016] you know she married some awful chump?”

[012-017] “Me,” said Archie.

[012-018] “How’s that?”

[012-019] “I married your sister. My name’s Moffam.”

[012-020] The young man seemed a trifle taken aback.

[012-021] “Sorry,” he said.

[012-022] “Not at all,” said Archie.

[012-023] “I was only going by what my father said in his [[letters-and-correspondence|letters]],” he explained,

[012-024] in extenuation.

[012-025] Archie nodded.

[012-026] “I’m afraid your jolly old father doesn’t appreciate me. But I’m hoping

[012-027] for the best. If I can rope in that rummy-looking little china thing

[012-028] that Jo-Jo the dog-faced boy is showing the customers, he will be all

[012-029] over me. I mean to say, you know, he’s got another like it, and, if he

[012-030] can get a full house, as it were, I’m given to understand he’ll be

[012-031] bucked, cheered, and even braced.”

[012-032] The young man stared.

[012-033] “Are _you_ the fellow who’s been bidding against me?”

[012-034] “Eh, what? Were you bidding against _me?_”

[012-035] “I wanted to buy the thing for my father. I’ve a special reason for

[012-036] wanting to get in right with him just now. Are you buying it for him,

[012-037] too?”

[012-038] “Absolutely. As a surprise. It was [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]]’s idea. His valet, a chappie

[012-039] named [[parker|Parker]], tipped us off that the thing was to be sold.”

[012-040] “Parker? Great Scot! It was Parker who tipped _me_ off. I met him on

[012-041] Broadway, and he told me about it.”

[012-042] “Rummy he never mentioned it in his letter to me. Why, dash it, we

[012-043] could have got the thing for about two dollars if we had pooled our

[012-044] bids.”

[012-045] “Well, we’d better pool them now, and extinguish that pill at the back

[012-046] there. I can’t go above eleven hundred. That’s all I’ve got.”

[012-047] “I can’t go above eleven hundred myself.”

[012-048] “There’s just one thing. I wish you’d let me be the one to hand the

[012-049] thing over to Father. I’ve a special reason for wanting to make a hit

[012-050] with him.”

[012-051] “Absolutely!” said Archie, magnanimously. “It’s all the same to me. I

[012-052] only wanted to get him generally braced, as it were, if you know what I

[012-053] mean.”

[012-054] “That’s awfully good of you.”

[012-055] “Not a bit, laddie, no, no, and far from it. Only too glad.”

[012-056] Willie had returned from his rambles among the connoisseurs, and

[012-057] Pongo’s brother was back on his pedestal. The high-priest cleared his

[012-058] throat and resumed his discourse.

[012-059] “Now that you have all seen this superb figure we will—I was offered

[012-060] one thousand—one thousand-one-one-one-one—eleven hundred. Thank you,

[012-061] sir. Eleven hundred I am offered.”

[012-062] The high-priest was now exuberant. You could see him doing figures in

[012-063] his head.

[012-064] “You do the bidding,” said [[bill-brewster|Brother Bill]].

[012-065] “Right-o!” said Archie.

[012-066] He waved a defiant hand.

[012-067] “Thirteen,” said the man at the back.

[012-068] “Fourteen, dash it!”

[012-069] “Fifteen!”

[012-070] “Sixteen!”

[012-071] “Seventeen!”

[012-072] “Eighteen!”

[012-073] “Nineteen!”

[012-074] “Two thousand!”

[012-075] The high-priest did everything but sing. He radiated good will and

[012-076] bonhomie.

[012-077] “Two thousand I am offered. Is there any advance on two thousand? Come,

[012-078] gentlemen, I don’t want to give this superb figure away. Twenty-one

[012-079] hundred. Twenty-one-one-one-one. This is more the sort of thing I have

[012-080] been accustomed to. When I was at Sotheby’s Rooms in London, this kind

[012-081] of bidding was a common-place. Twenty-two-two-two-two-two. One hardly

[012-082] noticed it. Three-three-three. Twenty-three-three-three. Twenty-three

[012-083] hundred dollars I am offered.”

[012-084] He gazed expectantly at Archie, as a man gazes at some favourite dog

[012-085] whom he calls upon to perform a trick. But Archie had reached the end

[012-086] of his tether. The hand that had twiddled so often and so bravely lay

[012-087] inert beside his trouser-leg, twitching feebly. Archie was through.

[012-088] “Twenty-three hundred,” said the high-priest, ingratiatingly.

[012-089] Archie made no movement. There was a tense pause. The high-priest gave

[012-090] a little sigh, like one waking from a beautiful dream.

[012-091] “Twenty-three hundred,” he said. “Once twenty-three. Twice

[012-092] twenty-three. Third, last, and final call, twenty-three. Sold at

[012-093] twenty-three hundred. I congratulate you, sir, on a genuine bargain!”

[012-094] Reggie van Tuyl had dozed off again. Archie tapped his brother-in-law

[012-095] on the shoulder.

[012-096] “May as well be popping, what?”

[012-097] They threaded their way sadly together through the crowd, and made for

[012-098] the street. They passed into [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] without breaking the silence.

[012-099] “Bally nuisance,” said Archie, at last.

[012-100] “Rotten!”

[012-101] “Wonder who that chappie was?”

[012-102] “Some collector, probably.”

[012-103] “Well, it can’t be helped,” said Archie.

[012-104] Brother Bill attached himself to Archie’s arm, and became

[012-105] communicative.

[012-106] “I didn’t want to mention it in front of van Tuyl,” he said, “because

[012-107] he’s such a talking-machine, and it would have been all over [[new-york-city|New York]]

[012-108] before [[dining-and-social-ritual|dinner]]-time. But you’re one of the family, and you can keep a

[012-109] secret.”

[012-110] “Absolutely! Silent tomb and what not.”

[012-111] “The reason I wanted that darned thing was because I’ve just got

[012-112] engaged to a girl over in [[england|England]], and I thought that, if I could hand

[012-113] my father that china figure-thing with one hand and break the news with

[012-114] the other, it might help a bit. She’s the most wonderful girl!”

[012-115] “I’ll bet she is,” said Archie, cordially.

[012-116] “The trouble is she’s in the chorus of one of the revues over there,

[012-117] and Father is apt to kick. So I thought—oh, well, it’s no good worrying

[012-118] now. Come along where it’s quiet, and I’ll tell you all about her.”

[012-119] “That’ll be jolly,” said Archie.

[012-120] CHAPTER XI.

[012-121] SALVATORE CHOOSES THE WRONG MOMENT

[012-122] Archie reclaimed the family jewellery from its temporary home next

[012-123] morning; and, having done so, sauntered back to [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]]. He was

[012-124] surprised, on entering the lobby, to meet his [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]]. More

[012-125] surprising still, Mr. Brewster was manifestly in a mood of

[012-126] extraordinary geniality. Archie could hardly believe his eyes when the

[012-127] other waved cheerily to him—nor his ears a moment later when Mr.

[012-128] Brewster, addressing him as “my boy,” asked him how he was and

[012-129] mentioned that the day was a warm one.

[012-130] Obviously this jovial frame of mind must be taken advantage of; and

[012-131] Archie’s first thought was of the downtrodden [[salvatore|Salvatore]], to the tale of

[012-132] whose wrongs he had listened so sympathetically on the previous day.

[012-133] Now was plainly the moment for the waiter to submit his grievance,

[012-134] before some ebb-tide caused the milk of human kindness to flow out of

[012-135] Daniel Brewster. With a swift “Cheerio!” in his father-in-law’s

[012-136] direction, Archie bounded into the grill-room. Salvatore, the hour for

[012-137] luncheon being imminent but not yet having arrived, was standing

[012-138] against the far wall in an attitude of thought.

[012-139] “Laddie!” cried Archie.

[012-140] “Sare?”

[012-141] “A most extraordinary thing has happened. Good old Brewster has

[012-142] suddenly popped up through a trap and is out in the lobby now. And

[012-143] what’s still more weird, he’s apparently bucked.”

[012-144] “Sare?”

[012-145] “Braced, you know. In the pink. Pleased about something. If you go to

[012-146] him now with that yarn of yours, you can’t fail. He’ll kiss you on both

[012-147] cheeks and give you his bank-roll and collar-stud. Charge along and ask

[012-148] the head-waiter if you can have ten minutes off.”

[012-149] Salvatore vanished in search of the potentate named, and Archie

[012-150] returned to the lobby to bask in the unwonted sunshine.

[012-151] “Well, well, well, what!” he said. “I thought you were at Brookport.”

[012-152] “I came up this morning to meet a friend of mine,” replied Mr. Brewster

[012-153] genially. “[[professor-binstead|Professor Binstead]].”

[012-154] “Don’t think I know him.”

[012-155] “Very interesting man,” said Mr. Brewster, still with the same uncanny

[012-156] amiability. “He’s a dabbler in a good many things—science, phrenology,

[012-157] [[art-and-collecting|antiques]]. I asked him to bid for me at a sale yesterday. There was a

[012-158] little china figure—”

[012-159] Archie’s jaw fell.

[012-160] “China figure?” he stammered feebly.

[012-161] “Yes. The companion to one you may have noticed on my mantelpiece

[012-162] upstairs. I have been trying to get the pair of them for years. I

[012-163] should never have heard of this one if it had not been for that valet

[012-164] of mine, Parker. Very good of him to let me know of it, considering I

[012-165] had fired him. Ah, here is Binstead.”—He moved to greet the small,

[012-166] middle-aged man with the tortoiseshell-rimmed spectacles who was

[012-167] bustling across the lobby.—“Well, Binstead, so you got it?”

[012-168] “Yes.”

[012-169] “I suppose the price wasn’t particularly stiff?”

[012-170] “Twenty-three hundred.”

[012-171] “Twenty-three hundred!” Mr. Brewster seemed to reel in his tracks.

[012-172] “Twenty-three _hundred!_”

[012-173] “You gave me carte blanche.”

[012-174] “Yes, but twenty-three hundred!”

[012-175] “I could have got it for a few dollars, but unfortunately I was a

[012-176] little late, and, when I arrived, some young fool had bid it up to a

[012-177] thousand, and he stuck to me till I finally shook him off at

[012-178] twenty-three hundred. Why, this is the very man! Is he a friend of

[012-179] yours?”

[012-180] Archie coughed.

[012-181] “More a relation than a friend, what? Son-in-law, don’t you know!”

[012-182] Mr. Brewster’s amiability had vanished.

[012-183] “What damned foolery have you been up to _now?_” he demanded. “Can’t I

[012-184] move a step without stubbing my toe on you? Why the devil did you bid?”

[012-185] “We thought it would be rather a fruity scheme. We talked it over and

[012-186] came to the conclusion that it was an egg. Wanted to get hold of the

[012-187] rummy little object, don’t you know, and surprise you.”

[012-188] “Who’s we?”

[012-189] “Lucille and I.”

[012-190] “But how did you hear of it at all?”

[012-191] “Parker, the valet-chappie, you know, wrote me a letter about it.”

[012-192] “Parker! Didn’t he tell you that he had told me the figure was to be

[012-193] sold?”

[012-194] “Absolutely not!” A sudden suspicion came to Archie. He was normally a

[012-195] guileless young man, but even to him the extreme fishiness of the part

[012-196] played by Herbert Parker had become apparent. “I say, you know, it

[012-197] [[appearance-and-first-impressions|looks]] to me as if friend Parker had been having us all on a bit, what?

[012-198] I mean to say it was jolly old Herb, who tipped your son off—Bill, you

[012-199] know—to go and bid for the thing.”

[012-200] “Bill! Was Bill there?”

[012-201] “Absolutely in person! We were bidding against each other like the

[012-202] dickens till we managed to get together and get acquainted. And then

[012-203] this bird—this [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]—sailed in and started to slip it across us.”

[012-204] Professor Binstead chuckled—the care-free chuckle of a man who sees all

[012-205] those around him smitten in the pocket, while he himself remains

[012-206] untouched.

[012-207] “A very ingenious rogue, this Parker of yours, Brewster. His method

[012-208] seems to have been simple but masterly. I have no doubt that either he

[012-209] or a confederate obtained the figure and placed it with the auctioneer,

[012-210] and then he ensured a good price for it by getting us all to bid

[012-211] against each other. Very ingenious!”

[012-212] Mr. Brewster struggled with his feelings. Then he seemed to overcome

[012-213] them and to force himself to look on the bright side.

[012-214] “Well, anyway,” he said. “I’ve got the pair of figures, and that’s what

[012-215] I wanted. Is that it in that parcel?”

[012-216] “This is it. I wouldn’t trust an express company to deliver it. Suppose

[012-217] we go up to your room and see how the two look side by side.”

[012-218] They crossed the lobby to the lift.-The cloud was still on Mr.

[012-219] Brewster’s brow as they stepped out and made their way to his suite.

[012-220] Like most men who have risen from [[wealth-and-money|poverty]] to wealth by their own

[012-221] exertions, Mr. Brewster objected to parting with his money

[012-222] unnecessarily, and it was plain that that twenty-three hundred dollars

[012-223] still rankled.
