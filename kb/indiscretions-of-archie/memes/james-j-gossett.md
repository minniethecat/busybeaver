# James J. Gossett

A New York motion-picture executive who appears briefly in Sections 26 and 27 during the climactic restaurant scene, where he is identified to Archie as "a motion-picture man" and promptly becomes collateral damage in the Sausage Chappie's outburst.

## Role in the Novel

Gossett appears only in the restaurant scene at the Hotel Cosmopolis. He is pointed out to Archie by one of the hotel regulars: "That's a man named Gossett. James J. Gossett. He's a motion-picture man. You must have seen his name around" (PN: 026-185). Moments later, the Sausage Chappie — an amnesiac war veteran who has just recognised his wife dining with another man — takes up a fruit salad and "decanted it over Mr. Gossett's bald head" (PN: 026-219). Gossett endures this indignity with remarkable composure, drying himself "sketchily with a napkin" (PN: 026-225) before Archie's father-in-law Daniel Brewster attempts to engage him in "business-talk" while he is "gradually emerging from the fruit-salad" (PN: 027-023–027-028).

## Personality and Traits

Despite being the victim of a random act of fruit-based aggression, Gossett displays a stoic, businesslike demeanour. Even while "speaking thickly through the fruit-salad" (PN: 027-008), he maintains his composure and is quickly drawn into business discussions with Daniel Brewster. His bald head is noted twice, suggesting a middle-aged, prosperous appearance befitting a motion-picture magnate.

## In the Eyes of Others

James J. Gossett's brief appearance is rich with comic meaning, and the various perspectives on him — and on his fruit-salad drenching — reveal the novel's attitudes toward business, dignity, and the randomness of urban life.

### Through Archie's Eyes

To [[archie-moffam]], Gossett is initially just another face in the Cosmopolis dining room — a motion-picture man whose name "you must have seen around" (PN: 026-185). Archie's interest in him is the idle curiosity of a diner scanning a restaurant. When the fruit salad lands, Archie becomes a spectator to one of the novel's most purely comic moments, his perspective that of a man who is grateful, for once, that the chaos is happening to someone else. Archie's detachment from Gossett's predicament is part of the joke: in a novel where Archie is perpetually on the receiving end of social catastrophe, watching a stranger get a fruit salad poured on his head is a rare moment of uncomplicated amusement.

### Through Daniel Brewster's Eyes

For [[daniel-brewster]], Gossett is a business opportunity dressed in fruit salad. Even as Gossett is "gradually emerging from the fruit-salad" (PN: 027-023), Brewster sees past the indignity to the motion-picture magnate beneath, seizing the moment to have "a little business-talk" (PN: 027-023). Brewster's response captures his defining quality: the relentless pursuit of commercial advantage, a man for whom no social situation — however absurd — is an excuse to stop doing business. His perspective on Gossett is purely instrumental; the fruit salad is incidental, the motion-picture deals are what matter.

### Through the Narrator's Eyes

The narrator presents Gossett with the same poker-faced delight in absurdity that characterises the novel's finest comic set-pieces. The verb "decanted" (PN: 026-219) — a word associated with wine service — elevates a crude act of food-throwing into something almost ceremonial, a perfect Wodehousian touch. Gossett's response — drying himself "sketchily with a napkin" (PN: 026-225) and carrying on with business talk — is relayed without editorial comment, the narrator trusting the reader to appreciate the magnificent incongruity of a man discussing motion-picture deals while still wearing his dessert.

## Key Relationships

- **[[daniel-brewster]]** — After the fruit salad incident, Brewster seizes the opportunity to have "a little business-talk with Mr. Gossett" (PN: 027-023).
- **[[sausage-chappie]]** — The amnesiac war veteran who impulsively throws a fruit salad over Gossett's head during his emotional crisis (PN: 026-219).
