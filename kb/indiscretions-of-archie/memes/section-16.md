## Section 16

[016-001] “I did think that for once my guardian angel had gone back to work and

[016-002] was doing something for me. ‘[[stage-star-and-midnight-marauder|Stage Star and Midnight Marauder]],’”

[016-003] murmured [[miss-silverton|Miss Silverton]], wistfully. “‘Footlight Favourite Foils

[016-004] Felon.’”

[016-005] “Bit thick!” agreed [[archie-moffam|Archie]], sympathetically. “Well, you’ll probably be

[016-006] wanting to get to bed and all that sort of rot, so I may as well be

[016-007] popping, what! Cheerio!”

[016-008] A sudden gleam came into Miss Silverton’s compelling eyes.

[016-009] “Wait!”

[016-010] “Eh?”

[016-011] “Wait! I’ve got an idea!” The wistful sadness had gone from her manner.

[016-012] She was bright and alert. “Sit down!”

[016-013] “Sit down?”

[016-014] “Sure. Sit down and take the chill off the arm-chair. I’ve thought of

[016-015] something.”

[016-016] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] sat down as directed. At his elbow the bulldog eyed him gravely

[016-017] from the basket.

[016-018] “Do they know you in this hotel?”

[016-019] “Know me? Well, I’ve been here about a week.”

[016-020] “I mean, do they know who you are? Do they know you’re a good citizen?”

[016-021] “Well, if it comes to that, I suppose they don’t. But—”

[016-022] “Fine!” said Miss Silverton, appreciatively. “Then it’s all right. We

[016-023] can carry on!”

[016-024] “Carry on!”

[016-025] “Why, sure! All I want is to get the thing into the papers. It doesn’t

[016-026] matter to me if it turns out later that there was a mistake and that

[016-027] you weren’t a burglar trying for my jewels after all. It makes just as

[016-028] good a story either way. I can’t think why that never struck me before.

[016-029] Here have I been kicking because you weren’t a real burglar, when it

[016-030] doesn’t amount to a hill of beans whether you are or not. All I’ve got

[016-031] to do is to rush out and yell and rouse the hotel, and they come in and

[016-032] pinch you, and I give the story to the papers, and everything’s fine!”

[016-033] Archie leaped from his chair.

[016-034] “I say! What!”

[016-035] “What’s on your mind?” enquired Miss Silverton, considerately. “Don’t

[016-036] you think it’s a nifty scheme?”

[016-037] “Nifty! My dear old soul! It’s frightful!”

[016-038] “Can’t see what’s wrong with it,” grumbled Miss Silverton. “After I’ve

[016-039] had someone get [[new-york-city|New York]] on the long-distance ’phone and give the story

[016-040] to the papers you can explain, and they’ll let you out. Surely to

[016-041] goodness you don’t object, as a personal favour to me, to spending an

[016-042] hour or two in a cell? Why, probably they haven’t got a prison at all

[016-043] out in these parts, and you’ll simply be locked in a room. A child of

[016-044] ten could do it on his head,” said Miss Silverton. “A child of six,”

[016-045] she emended.

[016-046] “But, dash it—I mean—what I mean to say—I’m married!”

[016-047] “Yes?” said Miss Silverton, with the politeness of faint interest.

[016-048] “I’ve been married myself. I wouldn’t say it’s altogether a bad thing,

[016-049] mind you, for those that like it, but a little of it goes a long way.

[016-050] My first husband,” she proceeded, reminiscently, “was a travelling man.

[016-051] I gave him a two-weeks’ try-out, and then I told him to go on

[016-052] travelling. My second husband—now, _he_ wasn’t a [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] in any sense

[016-053] of the word. I remember once—”

[016-054] “You don’t grasp the point. The jolly old point! You fail to grasp it.

[016-055] If this bally thing comes out, my wife will be most frightfully sick!”

[016-056] Miss Silverton regarded him with pained surprise.

[016-057] “Do you mean to say you would let a little thing like that stand in the

[016-058] way of my getting on the front page of all the papers—_with_

[016-059] photographs? Where’s your chivalry?”

[016-060] “Never mind my dashed chivalry!”

[016-061] “Besides, what does it matter if she does get a little sore? She’ll

[016-062] soon get over it. You can put that right. Buy her a box of candy. Not

[016-063] that I’m strong for candy myself. What I always say is, it may taste

[016-064] good, but look what it does to your hips! I give you my honest word

[016-065] that, when I gave up eating candy, I lost eleven ounces the first week.

[016-066] My second husband—no, I’m a liar, it was my third—my third husband

[016-067] said—Say, what’s the big idea? Where are you going?”

[016-068] “Out!” said Archie, firmly. “Bally out!”

[016-069] A dangerous light flickered in Miss Silverton’s eyes.

[016-070] “That’ll be all of that!” she said, raising the pistol. “You stay right

[016-071] where you are, or I’ll fire!”

[016-072] “Right-o!”

[016-073] “I mean it!”

[016-074] “My dear old soul,” said Archie, “in the recent unpleasantness in

[016-075] France I had chappies popping off things like that at me all day and

[016-076] every day for close on five years, and here I am, what! I mean to say,

[016-077] if I’ve got to choose between staying here and being pinched in your

[016-078] room by the local constabulary and having the dashed thing get into the

[016-079] papers and all sorts of trouble happening, and my wife getting the wind

[016-080] up and—I say, if I’ve got to choose—”

[016-081] “Suck a lozenge and start again!” said Miss Silverton.

[016-082] “Well, what I mean to say is, I’d much rather take a chance of getting

[016-083] a bullet in the [[old-bean|old bean]] than that. So loose it off and the best o’

[016-084] luck!”

[016-085] Miss Silverton lowered the pistol, sank into a chair, and burst into

[016-086] tears.

[016-087] “I think you’re the meanest man I ever met!” she sobbed. “You know

[016-088] perfectly well the bang would send me into a fit!”

[016-089] “In that case,” said Archie, relieved, “[[pip-pip-cheerio|cheerio]], good luck, pip-pip,

[016-090] toodle-oo, and good-bye-ee! I’ll be shifting!”

[016-091] “Yes, you will!” cried Miss Silverton, energetically, recovering with

[016-092] amazing swiftness from her collapse. “Yes, you will, I by no means

[016-093] suppose! You think, just because I’m no champion with a pistol, I’m

[016-094] helpless. You wait! Percy!”

[016-095] “My name is not Percy.”

[016-096] “I never said it was. Percy! Percy, come to muzzer!”

[016-097] There was a creaking rustle from behind the arm-chair. A heavy body

[016-098] flopped on the carpet. Out into the room, heaving himself along as

[016-099] though sleep had stiffened his joints, and breathing stertorously

[016-100] through his tilted nose, moved the fine bulldog. Seen in the open, he

[016-101] looked even more formidable than he had done in his basket.

[016-102] “Guard him, Percy! Good dog, guard him! Oh, heavens! What’s the matter

[016-103] with him?”

[016-104] And with these words the emotional woman, uttering a wail of anguish,

[016-105] flung herself on the floor beside the animal.

[016-106] Percy was, indeed, in manifestly bad shape. He seemed quite unable to

[016-107] drag his limbs across the room. There was a curious arch in his back,

[016-108] and, as his mistress touched him, he cried out plaintively,

[016-109] “Percy! Oh, what _is_ the matter with him? His nose is burning!”

[016-110] Now was the time, with both sections of the enemy’s forces occupied,

[016-111] for Archie to have departed softly from the room. But never, since the

[016-112] day when at the age of eleven he had carried a large, damp, and muddy

[016-113] terrier with a sore foot three miles and deposited him on the best sofa

[016-114] in his mother’s drawing-room, had he been able to ignore the spectacle

[016-115] of a dog in trouble.

[016-116] “He does look bad, what!”

[016-117] “He’s dying! Oh, he’s dying! Is it distemper? He’s never had

[016-118] distemper.”

[016-119] Archie regarded the sufferer with the grave eye of the expert. He shook

[016-120] his head.

[016-121] “It’s not that,” he said. “Dogs with distemper make a sort of snifting

[016-122] noise.”

[016-123] “But he _is_ making a snifting noise!”

[016-124] “No, he’s making a snuffling noise. Great difference between snuffling

[016-125] and snifting. Not the same thing at all. I mean to say, when they snift

[016-126] they snift, and when they snuffle they—as it were—snuffle. That’s how

[016-127] you can tell. If you ask _me_”—he passed his hand over the dog’s back.

[016-128] Percy uttered another cry. “I know what’s the matter with him.”

[016-129] “A brute of a man kicked him at rehearsal. Do you think he’s injured

[016-130] internally?”

[016-131] “It’s rheumatism,” said Archie. “Jolly old rheumatism. That’s all

[016-132] that’s the trouble.”

[016-133] “Are you sure?”

[016-134] “Absolutely!”

[016-135] “But what can I do?”

[016-136] “Give him a good hot bath, and mind and dry him well. He’ll have a good

[016-137] sleep then, and won’t have any pain. Then, first thing to-morrow, you

[016-138] want to give him salicylate of soda.”

[016-139] “I’ll never remember that.”—“I’ll write it down for you. You ought to

[016-140] give him from ten to twenty grains three times a day in an ounce of

[016-141] water. And rub him with any good embrocation.”

[016-142] “And he won’t die?”

[016-143] “Die! He’ll live to be as old as you are!-I mean to say—”

[016-144] “I could kiss you!” said Miss Silverton, emotionally.

[016-145] Archie backed hastily.

[016-146] “No, no, absolutely not! Nothing like that required, really!”

[016-147] “You’re a darling!”

[016-148] “Yes. I mean no. No, no, really!”

[016-149] “I don’t know what to say. What can I say?”

[016-150] “Good night,” said Archie.

[016-151] “I wish there was something I could do! If you hadn’t been here, I

[016-152] should have gone off my head!”

[016-153] A great idea flashed across Archie’s brain.

[016-154] “Do you really want to do something?”

[016-155] “Anything!”

[016-156] “Then I do wish, like a dear sweet soul, you would pop straight back to

[016-157] New York to-morrow and go on with those rehearsals.”

[016-158] Miss Silverton shook her head.

[016-159] “I can’t do that!”

[016-160] “Oh, right-o! But it isn’t much to ask, what!”

[016-161] “Not much to ask! I’ll never forgive that man for kicking Percy!”

[016-162] “Now listen, dear old soul. You’ve got the story all wrong. As a matter

[016-163] of fact, jolly old [[george-benham|Benham]] told me himself that he has the greatest

[016-164] esteem and respect for Percy, and wouldn’t have kicked him for the

[016-165] world. And, you know it was more a sort of push than a kick. You might

[016-166] almost call it a light shove. The fact is, it was beastly dark in the

[016-167] theatre, and he was legging it sideways for some reason or other, no

[016-168] doubt with the best motives, and unfortunately he happened to stub his

[016-169] toe on the poor old bean.”

[016-170] “Then why didn’t he say so?”

[016-171] “As far as I could make out, you didn’t give him a chance.”

[016-172] Miss Silverton wavered.

[016-173] “I always hate going back after I’ve walked out on a show,” she said.

[016-174] “It seems so weak!”

[016-175] “Not a bit of it! They’ll give three hearty cheers and think you a

[016-176] topper. Besides, you’ve got to go to New York in any case. To take

[016-177] Percy to a vet., you know, what!”

[016-178] “Of course. How right you always are!” Miss Silverton hesitated again.

[016-179] “Would you really be glad if I went back to the show?”

[016-180] “I’d go singing about the hotel! Great pal of mine, Benham. A

[016-181] thoroughly cheery old bean, and very cut up about the whole affair.

[016-182] Besides, think of all the coves thrown out of work—the thingummabobs

[016-183] and the poor what-d’you-call-’ems!”

[016-184] “Very well.”

[016-185] “You’ll do it?”

[016-186] “Yes.”

[016-187] “I say, you really are one of the best! Absolutely like mother made!

[016-188] That’s fine! Well, I think I’ll be saying good night.”

[016-189] “Good night. And thank you so much!”

[016-190] “Oh, no, rather not!”

[016-191] Archie moved to the door.

[016-192] “Oh, by the way.”

[016-193] “Yes?”

[016-194] “If I were you, I think I should catch the very first train you can get

[016-195] to New York. You see—er—you ought to take Percy to the vet. as soon as

[016-196] ever you can.”

[016-197] “You really do think of everything,” said Miss Silverton.

[016-198] “Yes,” said Archie, meditatively.

[016-199] CHAPTER XIV.

[016-200] THE SAD CASE OF LOONEY BIDDLE

[016-201] Archie was a simple soul, and, as is the case with most simple souls,

[016-202] gratitude came easily to him. He appreciated kind treatment. And when,

[016-203] on the following day, [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] returned to the Hermitage, all smiles and

[016-204] affection, and made no further reference to Beauty’s Eyes and the flies

[016-205] that got into them, he was conscious of a keen desire to show some

[016-206] solid recognition of this magnanimity. Few wives, he was aware, could

[016-207] have had the nobility and what not to refrain from occasionally turning

[016-208] the [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]] in the direction of the above-mentioned topics. It had

[016-209] not needed this behaviour on her part to convince him that Lucille was

[016-210] a topper and a corker and one of the very best, for he had been

[016-211] cognisant of these facts since the first moment he had met her: but

[016-212] what he did feel was that she deserved to be rewarded in no uncertain

[016-213] manner. And it seemed a happy coincidence to him that her birthday

[016-214] should be coming along in the next week or so. Surely, felt Archie, he

[016-215] could whack up some sort of a not unjuicy gift for that

[016-216] occasion—something pretty ripe that would make a substantial hit with

[016-217] the dear girl. Surely something would come along to relieve his chronic

[016-218] impecuniosity for just sufficient length of time to enable him to

[016-219] spread himself on this great occasion.
