# "Old Bean"

A catchphrase that runs through [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]] — [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]'s characteristic term of casual endearment, appearing in nearly every chapter and serving as both a marker of his Englishness and a barometer of his relationships.

## The Quote

> "My dear old bean," he said. (036-112)

> "My dear old bean," repeated Mr. Brewster firmly, "I'm the happiest man alive." (036-114)

## Context

"Old bean" is [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]'s most distinctive speech habit — a casual term of endearment he deploys with everyone from desk clerks to millionaire fathers-in-law. It appears throughout [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]]: "Well, there, old bean" (002-193), "Honestly, old bean" (002-089), "Awfully good of you, old bean" (030-172), "Sorry, old bean" (007-110), "Reflect, dear old bean" (027-039), "Look on the bright side, old bean" (027-144).

The phrase functions as [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s verbal signature. Whether he is greeting a friend, apologizing, or asking for a favor, the "old bean" is never far from his lips. It is p[[art-and-collecting|art]] of the linguistic armor he brings from [[england|England]] — a token of the public-school, club-land world he has left behind but never quite abandoned.

The catchphrase reaches its dramatic climax in Section 36. After a novel's worth of conflict with his [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]] [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]], [[archie-moffam|Archie]] finally wins him over — and [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]]'s acceptance is signaled by his adoption of [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s own idiom: "My dear old bean" (036-112, 036-114). It is a moment of full reconciliation, conveyed through the simple act of borrowing another man's catchphrase.

## Speaker

- **[[archie-moffam]]** — The novel's protagonist, whose "old bean" is his most reliable verbal tic. Used with everyone from his wife [[lucille-brewster]] to his [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]] [[daniel-brewster]] to hotel staff and strangers.

## Significance

The catchphrase serves several narrative functions. First, it marks [[archie-moffam|Archie]] as unmistakably English — a linguistic outsider in America, where "old bean" sounds charmingly (or infuriatingly) foreign. Second, it signals [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s essential good nature: the phrase is never hostile, always friendly, even when delivered in moments of tension. Third, its adoption by [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]] at [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]]'s climax provides one of the most satisfying moments of character development — the stern American businessman has, in the end, been won over to [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s worldview, and the catchphrase is the proof.

[[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]] uses the repetition of "old bean" to create a rhythmic comic effect. Its ubiquity becomes itself a joke — the reader comes to expect it, and [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]] rarely disappoints.
