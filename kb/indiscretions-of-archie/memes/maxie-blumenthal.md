# Maxie Blumenthal

A cheerful, heavyset businessman whom Daniel Brewster and Archie Moffam entertain at lunch in the later chapters of the novel. Brewster describes him as someone being taken on a "six weeks' tour" of entertainment, and his business dealings form part of the novel's comic financial subplots. Blumenthal functions as a catalyst for tension between Archie and his father-in-law, his presence at the lunch table forcing the two men into an uneasy alliance of hospitality.

## Role in the Novel

Blumenthal appears as a business contact whom Daniel Brewster is cultivating — described by Brewster as "a bird of the name of Blumenthal" (031-093) whom he intends to woo over a six-week campaign of dinners, theatre outings, and general entertainment. Archie participates in the lunch, and the conversation ranges from working-man themes to theatrical matters. Blumenthal's jovial, somewhat simple personality provides comic contrast to Brewster's intense business focus. (PN: 031-093, 032-075–032-150, 033-139–033-144)

The lunch scene forms one of the novel's set-piece comic meals. With Brewster straining to impress his guest and Archie struggling to navigate the social currents, Blumenthal sits serenely at the centre, "mystified" by the undercurrents but thoroughly enjoying his meal. His obliviousness to the tensions around him is both a comic device and a subtle commentary on the businessman as a creature of simple appetites.

## Personality and Traits

Blumenthal is characterized as "jovial old Blumenthal," a man of hearty appetite and simple pleasures. He is described as having strong opinions on working-class matters — "the working man, he contended" — and is easily mystified by social subtleties. He repeats himself for emphasis, being "a man who liked to drive his points home." (PN: 032-080, 032-119, 032-138–032-150)

Physically, Blumenthal is implied to be heavyset — a man of substance in both girth and business standing. His appetite is a running motif: he eats with gusto, speaks with enthusiasm, and approaches both food and conversation with the same undiscriminating relish. This physicality aligns him with a familiar Wodehouse type: the prosperous businessman whose relationship with the material world is uncomplicated and satisfying.

His taste in entertainment runs to the simple and direct. Brewster's strategy of "six weeks' tour" suggests a man who can be won over through sustained hospitality — a figure whose favour is purchased not through clever argument but through the steady application of good food, good drink, and good company.

## Key Relationships

- **Daniel Brewster** — Brewster is entertaining Blumenthal as a business prospect, planning a "six weeks' tour" to win his investment or partnership. Their relationship is purely transactional, with Brewster's formidable business energy directed entirely at securing Blumenthal's goodwill. (PN: 031-093, 032-075–032-080)
- **Archie Moffam** — Archie attends the lunch with Blumenthal and Brewster; Blumenthal's presence adds to the comic tension of the meal. Archie's role is that of reluctant co-host, pressed into service by his father-in-law to help charm the guest. (PN: 032-075)
- **Lucille Brewster** — Lucille's puzzled question — "Who on earth is Maxie Blumenthal?" (032-076) — establishes Blumenthal as a figure outside the novel's central domestic circle, a creature of her father's business world rather than the Moffams' social one.

## As Seen by Others

Blumenthal, for all his centrality to the lunch scene, is defined less by his own words than by how the other characters perceive and react to him. Each perspective reveals as much about the observer as about the observed.

**Brewster's Calculation.** To Daniel Brewster, Blumenthal is not a person but a project — "a bird" to be captured through six weeks of strategic hospitality (031-093). Brewster's language is that of the hunter or the angler: Blumenthal is prey, to be lured with dinners and theatres until he makes "all the difference" (032-080) to Brewster's business. This instrumental view strips Blumenthal of any human particularity — Brewster cares nothing for the man's opinions on the working classes or his taste in entertainments except insofar as they can be leveraged. The "six weeks' tour" is a campaign timetable, and Blumenthal its objective.

**Archie's Discomfort.** Archie experiences Blumenthal as a social burden. Pressed into co-hosting duties by his father-in-law, Archie must feign interest in a man whose conversation — revolving around working-man themes and theatrical investments — lies well outside his natural repertoire. Blumenthal represents for Archie the disagreeable side of married life: the obligation to be civil to his father-in-law's business acquaintances, to laugh at jokes he does not find funny, and to maintain an appearance of conviviality while inwardly counting the minutes until escape.

**Lucille's Detachment.** Lucille's question — "Who on earth is Maxie Blumenthal?" (032-076) — is the most revealing of all. She has never heard of him because he belongs to her father's world, not hers. Her phrasing — "on earth" — carries a hint of amused incredulity, as if the very existence of someone called Maxie Blumenthal were a mild absurdity. Through Lucille's eyes, Blumenthal is reduced to a name, a curiosity, a footnote in the larger drama of the Brewster-Moffam household — and in this reduction lies Wodehouse's implicit judgment on the ultimate insignificance of the business "bird."

## Comic Significance

Blumenthal belongs to a gallery of Wodehouse "business birds" — men whose commercial importance is belied by their personal simplicity. He is important enough for Brewster to invest six weeks of hospitality in courting him, yet so unsophisticated that he can be won over by repeated dinners. The gap between his financial significance and his personal guilelessness is the source of the comedy.

His function in the narrative is primarily structural: he provides the occasion for a set-piece lunch scene in which Archie and Brewster must cooperate despite their mutual friction. As a catalyst for this enforced camaraderie, Blumenthal — entirely unknowingly — drives the novel's central father-in-law/son-in-law dynamic into new configurations.
