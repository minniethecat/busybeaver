# Friendship

Friendship is the novel's social currency. Archie Moffam navigates the hostile terrain of his father-in-law's New York through an instinctive talent for making friends. His "friendly soul" (003-029) and "open-hearted hospitality" (003-031) disarm adversaries and recruit allies, transforming potential enemies into members of an ever-expanding social circle.

## Overview

The novel's social world is built on bonds of friendship that cross class, nationality, and temperament. Archie's defining characteristic is not intelligence or industry but amiability — a Wodehousian virtue that proves more powerful than wealth or status. His friendship with his father-in-law's associates, hotel staff, and random strangers repeatedly rescues him from the consequences of his own blunders.

Brewster, by contrast, represents the limitations of a life without friendship. His eighteen-year friendship with Professor Binstead (001-052) suggests a capacity for loyalty, but his suspicious nature and obsession with his hotel leave him isolated. The novel's arc is, in part, the story of Brewster learning to value the friendships that Archie so effortlessly creates.

## In the Novel

Archie's talent for friendship is established in his first appearance. He is "a friendly soul, a mixer" who "got on splendidly with Americans" (003-028). His "open-hearted hospitality" (003-031) creates alliances that repeatedly prove decisive. When Brewster's business associates become Archie's friends, the father-in-law's power to isolate Archie evaporates.

The friendship between Archie and Brewster's old friend is particularly significant. Their eighteen-year bond (001-052) becomes a bridge between the hostile generations, with the older friend mediating between Archie's charm and Brewster's fury.

## Key Passages

> "He was a friendly soul, a mixer. He liked America and got on splendidly with Americans." (003-028)

> "We have been friends for eighteen years. A considerable time." (001-052)
