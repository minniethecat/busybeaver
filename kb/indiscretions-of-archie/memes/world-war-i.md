# World War I

The Great War of 1914–1918 — the defining event of the generation that populates *Indiscretions of Archie*, forming the recent backstory against which the novel's comedy unfolds.

## Overview

World War I ended only three years before the novel's publication. For Wodehouse's original readers, the war was not history but memory — the defining trauma of their time. *Indiscretions of Archie* is not a war novel, but the war is everywhere in its background: in [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]'s military service, in the friendships forged in France, in the sense of a world that had survived cataclysm and was now determined to enjoy itself.

## In the Novel

The novel's references to the war are characteristically understated. Archie served as a second lieutenant — "in the early days of the war, before he had got his commission" (018-190) — and the experience of military service informs his easy camaraderie with men of all classes. When he encounters a fellow veteran who says "We met outside St Mihiel in the war" (022-145), the shared experience of combat provides an instant bond.

Archie's valet Parker is also a veteran: "since the war he had been less reserved" (010-132), suggesting that the shared experience of military service had begun to loosen some of England's rigid class structures. The war, in Wodehouse's treatment, is never dwelt upon — but its shadow falls across the novel's sunlit surface, a reminder of what these characters have been through and what they are now trying to enjoy.

A French acquaintance is described as "a bird I met in France during the war" (023-089), and another exchange includes the characteristic inquiry: "What did you do before the war?" (023-008) — the casual question that, for men of Archie's generation, carried the weight of a world that had been divided forever into "before" and "after."

## Historical Context

The war's key dates relative to the novel:

- **1914–1918**: The conflict itself — Archie would have served in France
- **Battle of St. Mihiel** (September 1918): Mentioned in the novel (022-145), an American-led offensive in which Archie apparently encountered a fellow soldier
- **1919**: The Treaty of Versailles — the war's formal end
- **1920–1921**: The novel's present — the post-war world of Prohibition, economic boom, and determined gaiety

Wodehouse himself did not serve in the war (he was in his mid-thirties and living in America when it began), but he understood the generation that did. Archie Moffam's good nature and indestructible cheerfulness read differently when one remembers that he is a veteran — a man who has seen real disaster and emerged determined never to take minor ones too seriously.
