# Miss Vera Silverton

Miss Vera Silverton is an American stage actress and singer — "the spectacular Miss Silverton" (013-186) — who appears prominently across four consecutive sections of the novel (Sections 13 through 16). She is the leading lady in George Benham's play and a figure from New York's theatrical world, characterized by her "compelling eyes" (016-008), her "dubious hair" (013-187) and "criticised eyes" (013-188), and a temperament that oscillates between dramatic intensity and comic petulance.

## Role in the Novel

Vera Silverton appears in Sections 13 through 16 as a disruptive and vivid presence. She is the leading lady in George Benham's play but has abandoned the New York rehearsals to stay at the resort, much to the playwright's distress. Her unexpected presence drives a subplot involving Archie's attempts to manage the situation, a bulldog that ends up in Archie's room, and a series of misunderstandings that threaten to embroil Archie in scandal. (PN: 013-151–015-169)

## Appearances

### Section 13: Introduction

Miss Silverton is first mentioned by name — "Her name's Vera Silverton" (013-151) — in conversation among the novel's characters. She is described with the striking epithet "the spectacular Miss Silverton" (013-186), immediately establishing her as a vivid and memorable presence. The adjective "spectacular" suggests both physical attractiveness and a theatrical quality that sets her apart from the novel's more conventional female characters. George Benham, her playwright, is introduced here as the man whose play she has abandoned.

### Section 14: At the Summer Resort

Miss Silverton is seen at the summer hotel, "talking to somebody on a settee in the corner" (014-051). The episode revolves around a characteristically trivial mishap: "There's something in my eye" (014-051). Archie Moffam, ever the gentleman, assists her — an act of gallantry that immediately becomes, in his anxious imagination, a potential source of scandal. "Oh, was that you on the settee, talking to Miss Silverton?" (014-074) confirms that proximity to a "spectacular" actress at a summer resort attracts notice.

She remarks with comic self-awareness: "Such a nuisance getting things in your eye... I'm always doing it!" (014-074–014-098), a line that captures her blend of glamour and haplessness. George Benham complains bitterly that no playwright could succeed "if his leading woman had been anyone like Vera Silverton" (014-081), his exasperation illuminating the uneasy dependence of the creative artist on the capricious performer.

### Section 15: The Pleading

The narrative tension around Miss Silverton intensifies when a male character contemplates approaching her: "To seek Miss Silverton out and plead with her — even if..." (015-018). The hesitation is palpable: "His whole being shrank from extending to Miss Silverton that inch which..." (015-023). The scene moves to "the room belonging to Miss Vera Silverton" (015-100), a private space that heightens the stakes.

The emotional intensity reaches a comic peak when the thought of violence — "kill Miss Silverton — if possible, painfully" (015-134) — flashes through a character's mind, immediately deflated by Miss Silverton's understated spoken response: "For the love of Mike!" (015-153). The contrast between violent fantasy and mundane dialogue is pure Wodehouse.

### Section 16: The Dramatic Confrontation

Miss Silverton's longest and most vivid appearance occupies Section 16. Here her personality fills the narrative space: she "murmur[s] wistfully" about a headline — "'Footlight Favourite Foils...'" (016-003); her eyes flash with theatrical energy ("A sudden gleam came into Miss Silverton's compelling eyes" 016-008); and she deploys professional expertise with casual authority: "Can't see what's wrong with it" (016-038), "ten could do it on his head... A child of six" (016-044).

A crucial dramatic moment pivots on a pistol: "Miss Silverton lowered the pistol, sank into a chair, and burst into tears" (016-085), followed by an equally sudden recovery — she "crie[s] energetically, recovering with..." (016-091). Her emotional oscillations are comic in their speed: from tears to energetic recovery, from "pained surprise" (016-056) to appreciative agreement (016-022), from wavering indecision (016-172) to emotional declarations (016-144).

The section closes with a moment of genuine warmth: "You really do think of everything" (016-197), suggesting that beneath the theatrical surface, Miss Silverton is capable of sincere appreciation.

## Personality and Traits

The narrator regards Vera Silverton with amused detachment. The epithet "spectacular" is not quite a compliment — it carries a note of ironic overstatement, as though the narrator is reporting the word with invisible quotation marks. Observations about her "dubious hair" (013-187) and "criticised eyes" (013-188) frame her as a figure who projects glamour while inviting scepticism — a type the Wodehousian narrator knows intimately and treats with fond, deflating precision.

Her greatest comic attribute is the gap between her sense of herself as a Serious Artist and the triviality of the mishaps that befall her. She gets things in her eye, abandons rehearsals for a resort holiday, threatens with pistols and then bursts into tears — all while maintaining the impermeable self-regard of the stage professional. She is, in short, one of Wodehouse's stage-world characters — a type he knew well from his own experience in musical theatre.

## In the Eyes of Others

### Through Archie's Eyes

To Archie Moffam, Vera Silverton represents a walking social hazard. When he first encounters her, it is with the wary politeness of a married man who knows that proximity to a "spectacular" actress at a summer resort invites domestic trouble. His careful assistance in removing something from her eye (014-001–014-051) — an act of simple gallantry — becomes, in his imagination, a source of potential scandal. Archie does not dislike Vera; he fears the appearance of an association with her, which speaks more to his anxieties about his marriage to Lucille than to any quality of Vera's own.

### Through George Benham's Eyes

For George Benham, Vera Silverton is a professional nightmare. She is the leading lady of his play but has abandoned rehearsals to idle at a summer resort. His complaint that no playwright could succeed with "anyone like Vera Silverton" (014-081) is the exasperated cry of an artist whose work is held hostage by the caprices of a performer. His perspective illuminates the uneasy relationship between creative artist and commercial performer in early-twentieth-century theatre.
