# Lord Seacliff

Lord Seacliff, known to Archie Moffam as "Squiffy," is a young English aristocrat and old school friend of Archie's. His uncle, General Mannister, brings him to America hoping that Prohibition will cure his chronic drinking habit.

## Role in the Novel

Seacliff appears in three chapters as a comedic subplot character. His arrival at the Cosmopolis Hotel creates complications for Archie (who is tasked with keeping him sober) and for Daniel Brewster (who discovers Seacliff is a friend of Archie's and wants him ejected). His accidental encounter with Peter the snake inadvertently triggers his decision to reform. (PN 007-043, 008-082, 009-049)

Seacliff's subplot serves as a vehicle for Wodehouse's satire of Prohibition-era America — the irony being that an English aristocrat is sent to America specifically because alcohol is illegal there, only to find that determined young men can still obtain it. (PN 007-083)

## Biography / Backstory

Seacliff is the nephew of General Mannister and presumably holds a title in the English peerage. He attended Eton and Oxford with Archie Moffam, and Archie often visited him at his family home during the Long Vacation. (PN 007-043, 007-044)

During the First World War, Seacliff sustained a shrapnel wound to his foot and was sent home. The enforced inaction after his injury led to a worsening of what General Mannister euphemistically calls "a family failing" — heavy drinking. (PN 007-062, 007-064)

General Mannister, attached to the British Legation in Washington, induced Seacliff to accompany him to America, reasoning that Prohibition made it "the ideal place for a young man of his views." Seacliff refused to go to Washington (claiming the thought gave him "the pip") and insisted on staying in New York instead. (PN 007-077, 007-083)

At some point before the novel's events, both Seacliff and Archie went through bankruptcy court together — Archie notes the "rummy coincidence" that neither had any assets. (PN 008-096)

## Personality and Traits

Seacliff is described as a "strong, silent" type. Archie characterizes him to Daniel Brewster: "He never says much, but it's whispered round the clubs that he thinks a lot. It was rumoured in the spring of nineteen-thirteen that Squiffy was on the point of making a bright remark, but it never came to anything." (PN 008-087, 008-089)

His defining trait is his alcoholism — referred to by Archie in slang terms as "mopping it up," "shifting it," and "looking on the jolly old stuff when it was red." When Archie met him in Paris during the war, Seacliff was "quite tolerably blotto." (PN 007-066, 007-072)

Despite his drinking problem, Seacliff is portrayed sympathetically. When he believes he has seen a snake (actually Peter, the real snake from Madame Brudowska's room), he is so frightened that he pours out all his alcohol and resolves to reform. Archie, recognizing that this is a fortunate outcome, wisely chooses not to tell him the snake was real. (PN 009-052, 009-062)

## Key Relationships

- **[[archie-moffam]]** — Close friend from Eton and Oxford days. Archie is entrusted by General Mannister to look after Seacliff in New York. (PN 007-087, 007-043)

- **General Mannister** — Seacliff's uncle, who brings him to America hoping Prohibition will cure his drinking. Attached to the British Legation in Washington. (PN 007-043, 007-075)

- **[[daniel-brewster]]** — Seacliff's brief presence at the Cosmopolis Hotel angers Brewster, who upon learning Seacliff is a friend of Archie's, resolves to eject him. (PN 008-082, 008-099)
