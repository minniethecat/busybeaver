# China Warrior Figurine

A small china figure of a warrior — one of a pair of antique Ming Dynasty figurines — owned by the hotel magnate and art collector Daniel Brewster. The figurine and the search for its missing counterpart form a recurring motif across the novel's early chapters, illustrating Brewster's connoisseurship and the value placed on rare collectibles in his social circle.

## Description

The figurine is "a small china figure of delicate" workmanship representing "a warrior of pre-khaki days advancing with a spear" (001-192–001-193). The warrior's face is notably small — "was smaller than himself" (001-195) — and in a later description it appears as "the figure of a warrior in a suit of armour" (011-100). The piece is identified as "a priceless china figure of the Ming Dynasty" (012-003), establishing its considerable monetary and artistic value.

The figurine is displayed "on a pedestal" (011-099) among Brewster's collection, and its physical delicacy is emphasized when a character handles it — "balanced it on the palm of" their hand (013-020).

## In the Novel

The china figure first appears when Brewster acquires it through his art agent Mawson, who discovered it in a shop on the east side of New York (001-192). The figure is part of a pair, and Mawson is tasked with locating the missing counterpart.

The figurine's value is confirmed by an expert who declares, "Undoubtedly, sir. These antique china figures are in great demand" (009-157). A visitor to the Cosmopolis expresses keen interest — "interested in a small china figure. To assist your memory, the f[igure]" (010-039) — suggesting word of Brewster's acquisition has spread among collectors.

The plot thickens when a confrontation arises over the figurine. One character laments seeing "a priceless china figure of the Ming Dynasty get away" (012-003), and there is a threat involving the figure — a character is described as having the capacity to "take my father that china figure-thing with one hand and break" it (012-113). This establishes the figurine as a point of tension and potential collateral damage within the domestic comedy.
