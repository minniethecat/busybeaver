# Greenwich Village

A neighborhood in lower Manhattan known in the 1920s as New York's bohemian quarter, used in the novel to represent a self-consciously "artistic" lifestyle that attracts imitators from the provinces.

## In the Novel

Greenwich Village appears in connection with a young woman from the [[middle-west|Middle West]] whom [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s acquaintance is visiting. The woman is described as having read "magazine stories about Greenwich Village" and having "modelled herself" on its perceived bohemian style (031-146). Wodehouse uses the Village as a cultural signifier: it represents the cosmopolitan, unconventional New York that provincial newcomers aspire to emulate, often with unintentionally comic results. The reference comes during a conversation about a girl from the Middle West who is "trying to be" a Village bohemian (031-143), an affectation treated with gentle mockery.

## Description

The novel does not provide a physical description of the Village itself. Instead, it functions metonymically—the name "Greenwich Village" stands for a whole complex of associations: tea-drinking, artistic pretension, unconventional behavior, and the striving of small-town girls to appear sophisticated. The reference to having "tea with a girl down in Greenwich Village" (031-109) captures the neighborhood's reputation as a destination for social and cultural experimentation.

## Associated Characters

- **[[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]** — Mentioned in conversation regarding an acquaintance visiting the Village (031-109)
- An unnamed young woman from the Middle West, described as modelling herself after magazine depictions of Village life (031-143)

## Real-World Context

Greenwich Village in the 1920s was at the height of its Bohemian period, home to artists, writers, political radicals, and experimental theaters. The neighborhood had become nationally famous through magazine articles and novels depicting its free-spirited culture. Wodehouse, himself a sophisticated observer of New York society, captures the era's phenomenon of middle-American "wannabe" bohemians who aped Village mannerisms without understanding them—a comic type still recognizable today.
