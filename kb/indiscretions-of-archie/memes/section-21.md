## Section 21

[021-001] “He has thought of a way of getting rid of [[salvatore|Salvatore]] and his shop.”

[021-002] For the first time [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]]’s interest in the [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]] seemed

[021-003] to stir. He looked sharply at his son-in-law.

[021-004] “He has, has he?” he said.

[021-005] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] balanced a roll on a fork and inserted a plate underneath. The

[021-006] roll bounded away into a corner.

[021-007] “Sorry!” said [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]]. “My fault, absolutely! I owe you a roll. I’ll

[021-008] sign a bill for it. Oh, about this sportsman Salvatore, Well, it’s like

[021-009] this, you know. He and I are great [[friendship|pals]]. I’ve known him for years and

[021-010] years. At least, it seems like years and years. Lu was suggesting that

[021-011] I seek him out in his lair and ensnare him with my diplomatic manner

[021-012] and superior brain power and what not.”

[021-013] “It was your idea, precious,” said [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]].

[021-014] Mr. Brewster was silent.—Much as it went against the grain to have to

[021-015] admit it, there seemed to be something in this.

[021-016] “What do you propose to do?”

[021-017] “Become a jolly old ambassador. How much did you offer the chappie?”

[021-018] “Three thousand dollars. Twice as much as the place is worth. He’s

[021-019] holding out on me for revenge.”

[021-020] “Ah, but how did you offer it to him, what? I mean to say, I bet you

[021-021] got your lawyer to write him a letter full of whereases, peradventures,

[021-022] and parties of the first part, and so forth. No good, old companion!”

[021-023] “Don’t call me old companion!”

[021-024] “All wrong, laddie! Nothing like it, dear heart! No good at all, friend

[021-025] of my youth! Take it from your Uncle Archibald! I’m a student of human

[021-026] nature, and I know a thing or two.”

[021-027] “That’s not much,” growled Mr. Brewster, who was finding his

[021-028] son-in-law’s superior manner a little trying.

[021-029] “Now, don’t interrupt, father,” said Lucille, severely. “Can’t you see

[021-030] that Archie is going to be tremendously clever in a minute?”

[021-031] “He’s got to show me!”

[021-032] “What you ought to do,” said Archie, “is to let me go and see him,

[021-033] taking the stuff in crackling bills. I’ll roll them about on the table

[021-034] in front of him. That’ll fetch him!” He prodded Mr. Brewster

[021-035] encouragingly with a roll. “I’ll tell you what to do. Give me three

[021-036] thousand of the best and crispest, and I’ll undertake to buy that shop.

[021-037] It can’t fail, laddie!”

[021-038] “Don’t call me laddie!” Mr. Brewster pondered. “Very well,” he said at

[021-039] last. “I didn’t know you had so much sense,” he added grudgingly.

[021-040] “Oh, positively!” said Archie. “Beneath a rugged exterior I hide a

[021-041] brain like a buzz-saw. Sense? I exude it, laddie; I drip with it.”

[021-042] There were moments during the ensuing days when Mr. Brewster permitted

[021-043] himself to [[hope-and-optimism|hope]]; but more frequent were the moments when he told

[021-044] himself that a pronounced chump like his son-in-law could not fail

[021-045] somehow to make a mess of the negotiations. His relief, therefore, when

[021-046] Archie curveted into his private room and announced that he had

[021-047] succeeded was great.

[021-048] “You really managed to make that wop sell out?”

[021-049] Archie brushed some papers off the desk with a careless gesture, and

[021-050] seated himself on the vacant spot.

[021-051] “Absolutely! I spoke to him as one old friend to another, sprayed the

[021-052] bills all over the place; and he sang a few bars from ‘Rigoletto,’ and

[021-053] signed on the dotted line.”

[021-054] “You’re not such a fool as you look,” owned Mr. Brewster.

[021-055] Archie scratched a match on the desk and lit a cigarette.

[021-056] “It’s a jolly little shop,” he said. “I took quite a fancy to it. Full

[021-057] of newspapers, don’t you know, and cheap novels, and some weird-looking

[021-058] sort of chocolates, and cigars with the most fearfully attractive

[021-059] labels. I think I’ll make a success of it. It’s bang in the middle of a

[021-060] dashed good neighbourhood. One of these days somebody will be building

[021-061] a big hotel round about there, and that’ll help [[business-and-enterprise|trade]] a lot. I look

[021-062] forward to ending my days on the other side of the counter with a full

[021-063] set of white whiskers and a skull-cap, beloved by everybody.

[021-064] Everybody’ll say, ‘Oh, you _must_ patronise that quaint, delightful old

[021-065] blighter! He’s quite a character.’”

[021-066] Mr. Brewster’s air of grim satisfaction had given way to a look of

[021-067] discomfort, almost of alarm. He presumed his son-in-law was merely

[021-068] indulging in _badinage;_ but even so, his words were not soothing.

[021-069] “Well, I’m much obliged,” he said. “That infernal shop was holding up

[021-070] everything. Now I can start building right away.”

[021-071] Archie raised his eyebrows.

[021-072] “But, my dear old top, I’m sorry to spoil your daydreams and stop you

[021-073] chasing rainbows, and all that, but aren’t you forgetting that the shop

[021-074] belongs to me? I don’t at all know that I want to sell, either!”

[021-075] “I gave you the [[wealth-and-money|money]] to buy that shop!”

[021-076] “And dashed generous of you it was, too!” admitted Archie,

[021-077] unreservedly. “It was the first money you ever gave me, and I shall

[021-078] always tell interviewers that it was you who founded my fortunes. Some

[021-079] day, when I’m the Newspaper-and-Tobacco-Shop King, I’ll tell the world

[021-080] all about it in my autobiography.”

[021-081] Mr. Brewster rose dangerously from his seat.

[021-082] “Do you think you can hold me up, you—you worm?”

[021-083] “Well,” said Archie, “the way I look at it is this. Ever since we met,

[021-084] you’ve been after me to become one of the world’s workers, and earn a

[021-085] living for myself, and what not; and now I see a way to repay you for

[021-086] your confidence and encouragement. You’ll look me up sometimes at the

[021-087] good old shop, won’t you?” He slid off the table and moved towards the

[021-088] door. “There won’t be any formalities where you are concerned. You can

[021-089] sign bills for any reasonable amount any time you want a cigar or a

[021-090] stick of chocolate. Well, [[pip-pip-cheerio|toodle-oo]]!”

[021-091] “Stop!”

[021-092] “Now what?”

[021-093] “How much do you want for that damned shop?”

[021-094] “I don’t want money.-I want a job.-If you are going to take my

[021-095] life-work away from me, you ought to give me something else to do.”

[021-096] “What job?”

[021-097] “You suggested it yourself the other day. I want to manage your new

[021-098] hotel.”

[021-099] “Don’t be a fool! What do you know about managing an hotel?”

[021-100] “Nothing. It will be your pleasing task to teach me the business while

[021-101] the shanty is being run up.”

[021-102] There was a pause, while Mr. Brewster chewed three inches off a

[021-103] pen-holder.

[021-104] “Very well,” he said at last.

[021-105] “Topping!” said Archie. “I knew you’d see it. I’ll study your methods,

[021-106] what! Adding some of my own, of course. You know, I’ve thought of one

[021-107] improvement on [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] already.”

[021-108] “Improvement on [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]]!” cried Mr. Brewster, gashed in his

[021-109] finest feelings.

[021-110] “Yes. There’s one point where the old Cosmop slips up badly, and I’m

[021-111] going to see that it’s corrected at my little shack. Customers will be

[021-112] entreated to leave their boots outside their doors at night, and

[021-113] they’ll find them cleaned in the morning. Well, pip, pip! I must be

[021-114] popping. Time is money, you know, with us business men.”

[021-115] CHAPTER XVII.

[021-116] BROTHER BILL’S ROMANCE

[021-117] “Her eyes,” said [[bill-brewster|Bill Brewster]], “are like—like—what’s the word I want?”

[021-118] He looked across at Lucille and Archie. Lucille was leaning forward

[021-119] with an eager and interested face; Archie was leaning back with his

[021-120] finger-tips together and his eyes closed. This was not the first time

[021-121] since their meeting in Beale’s Auction Rooms that his brother-in-law

[021-122] had touched on the subject of the girl he had become engaged to marry

[021-123] during his trip to [[england|England]]. Indeed, Brother Bill had touched on very

[021-124] little else: and Archie, though of a sympathetic nature and fond of his

[021-125] young relative, was beginning to feel that he had heard all he wished

[021-126] to hear about Mabel Winchester. Lucille, on the other hand, was

[021-127] absorbed. Her brother’s recital had thrilled her.

[021-128] “Like—” said Bill. “Like—”

[021-129] “Stars?” suggested Lucille.

[021-130] “Stars,” said Bill gratefully. “Exactly the word. Twin stars shining in

[021-131] a clear sky on a summer night. Her teeth are like—what shall I say?”

[021-132] “Pearls?”

[021-133] “Pearls. And her hair is a lovely brown, like leaves in autumn. In

[021-134] fact,” concluded Bill, slipping down from the heights with something of

[021-135] a jerk, “she’s a corker. Isn’t she, Archie?”

[021-136] Archie opened his eyes.

[021-137] “Quite right, old top!” he said. “It was the only thing to do.”

[021-138] “What the devil are you talking about?” demanded Bill coldly. He had

[021-139] been suspicious all along of Archie’s statement that he could listen

[021-140] better with his eyes shut.

[021-141] “Eh? Oh, sorry! Thinking of something else.”

[021-142] “You were asleep.”

[021-143] “No, no, positively and distinctly not. Frightfully interested and rapt

[021-144] and all that, only I didn’t quite get what you said.”

[021-145] “I said that Mabel was a corker.”

[021-146] “Oh, absolutely in every respect.”

[021-147] “There!” Bill turned to Lucille triumphantly. “You hear that? And

[021-148] Archie has only seen her photograph. Wait till he sees her in the

[021-149] flesh.”

[021-150] “My dear old chap!” said Archie, shocked. “Ladies present! I mean to

[021-151] say, what!”

[021-152] “I’m afraid that father will be the one you’ll find it hard to

[021-153] convince.”

[021-154] “Yes,” admitted her brother gloomily.

[021-155] “Your Mabel sounds perfectly charming, but—well, you know what father

[021-156] is. It _is_ a pity she sings in the chorus.”

[021-157] “She hasn’t much of a voice,”—argued Bill—in extenuation.

[021-158] “All the same—”

[021-159] Archie, the conversation having reached a topic on which he considered

[021-160] himself one of the greatest living authorities—to wit, the unlovable

[021-161] disposition of his [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]]—addressed the meeting as one who has a

[021-162] right to be heard.

[021-163] “Lucille’s absolutely right, old thing.—Absolutely correct-o! Your

[021-164] esteemed progenitor is a pretty tough nut, and it’s no good trying to

[021-165] get away from it.-And I’m sorry to have to say it, old bird, but, if

[021-166] you come bounding in with part of the personnel of the ensemble on your

[021-167] arm and try to dig a father’s blessing out of him, he’s extremely apt

[021-168] to stab you in the gizzard.”

[021-169] “I wish,” said Bill, annoyed, “you wouldn’t talk as though Mabel were

[021-170] the ordinary kind of chorus-girl. She’s only on the stage because her

[021-171] mother’s hard-up and she wants to educate her little brother.”

[021-172] “I say,” said Archie, concerned. “Take my tip, old top. In chatting the

[021-173] matter over with the pater, don’t dwell too much on that aspect of the

[021-174] affair.—I’ve been watching him closely, and it’s about all he can

[021-175] stick, having to support _me_. If you ring in a mother and a little

[021-176] brother on him, he’ll crack under the strain.”

[021-177] “Well, I’ve got to do something about it. Mabel will be over here in a

[021-178] week.”

[021-179] “Great Scot! You never told us that.”

[021-180] “Yes. She’s going to be in the new Billington show. And, naturally, she

[021-181] will expect to meet my family. I’ve told her all about you.”

[021-182] “Did you explain father to her?” asked Lucille.

[021-183] “Well, I just said she mustn’t mind him, as his bark was worse than his

[021-184] bite.”

[021-185] “Well,” said Archie, thoughtfully, “he hasn’t bitten me yet, so you may

[021-186] be right. But you’ve got to admit that he’s a bit of a barker.”

[021-187] Lucille considered.

[021-188] “Really, Bill, I think your best plan would be to go straight to father

[021-189] and tell him the whole thing.—You don’t want him to hear about it in a

[021-190] roundabout way.”

[021-191] “The trouble is that, whenever I’m with father, I can’t think of

[021-192] anything to say.”

[021-193] Archie found himself envying his father-in-law this merciful

[021-194] dispensation of Providence; for, where he himself was concerned, there

[021-195] had been no lack of eloquence on Bill’s part. In the brief period in

[021-196] which he had known him, Bill had talked all the time and always on the

[021-197] one topic. As unpromising a subject as the tariff laws was easily

[021-198] diverted by him into a discussion of the absent Mabel.

[021-199] “When I’m with father,” said Bill, “I sort of lose my nerve, and

[021-200] yammer.”

[021-201] “Dashed awkward,” said Archie, politely. He sat up suddenly. “I say! By

[021-202] Jove! I know what you want, old friend! Just thought of it!”
