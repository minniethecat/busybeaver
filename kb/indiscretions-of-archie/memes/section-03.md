## Section 3

[003-001] “Well, I suppose I’ve got to support you. There seems no way out of it.

[003-002] I’ll tell you exactly what I propose to do. You think my hotel is a

[003-003] pretty poor hotel, eh? Well, you’ll have plenty of opportunity of

[003-004] judging, because you’re coming to live here. I’ll let you have a suite

[003-005] and I’ll let you have your [[dining-and-social-ritual|meals]], but outside of that—nothing doing!

[003-006] Nothing doing! Do you understand what I mean?”

[003-007] “Absolutely! You mean, ‘Napoo!’”

[003-008] “You can sign bills for a reasonable amount in my restaurant, and the

[003-009] hotel will look after your laundry. But not a cent do you get out of

[003-010] me. And, if you want your shoes shined, you can pay for it yourself in

[003-011] the basement. If you leave them outside your door, I’ll instruct the

[003-012] floor-waiter to throw them down the air-shaft. Do you understand? Good!

[003-013] Now, is there anything more you want to ask?”

[003-014] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] smiled a propitiatory smile.

[003-015] “Well, as a matter of fact, I was going to ask if you would stagger

[003-016] along and have a bite with us in the grill-room?”

[003-017] “I will not!”

[003-018] “I’ll sign the bill,” said [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], ingratiatingly. “You don’t think

[003-019] much of it? Oh, right-o!”

[003-020] CHAPTER IV.

[003-021] WORK WANTED

[003-022] It seemed to Archie, as he surveyed his position at the end of the

[003-023] first month of his married life, that all was for the best in the best

[003-024] of all possible worlds. In their attitude towards America, visiting

[003-025] Englishmen almost invariably incline to extremes, either detesting all

[003-026] that therein is or else becoming enthusiasts on the subject of the

[003-027] country, its climate, and its institutions. Archie belonged to the

[003-028] second class. He liked America and got on splendidly with Americans

[003-029] from the start. He was a friendly soul, a mixer; and in [[new-york-city|New York]], that

[003-030] city of mixers, he found himself at home. The atmosphere of

[003-031] good-[[friendship|fellowship]] and the open-hearted hospitality of everybody he met

[003-032] appealed to him. There were moments when it seemed to him as though New

[003-033] York had simply been waiting for him to arrive before giving the word

[003-034] to let the revels commence.

[003-035] Nothing, of course, in this world is perfect; and, rosy as were the

[003-036] glasses through which Archie looked on his new surroundings, he had to

[003-037] admit that there was one flaw, one fly in the ointment, one individual

[003-038] caterpillar in the salad. Mr. [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]], his [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]],

[003-039] remained consistently unfriendly. Indeed, his manner towards his new

[003-040] relative became daily more and more a manner which would have caused

[003-041] gossip on the plantation if Simon Legree had exhibited it in his

[003-042] relations with Uncle Tom. And this in spite of the fact that Archie, as

[003-043] early as the third morning of his stay, had gone to him and in the most

[003-044] frank and manly way, had withdrawn his criticism of the Hotel

[003-045] Cosmopolis, giving it as his considered opinion that the Hotel

[003-046] Cosmopolis on closer inspection appeared to be a good egg, one of the

[003-047] best and brightest, and a bit of all right.

[003-048] “A credit to you, old thing,” said Archie cordially.

[003-049] “Don’t call me old thing!” growled Mr. Brewster.

[003-050] “Right-o, old companion!” said Archie amiably.

[003-051] Archie, a true philosopher, bore this hostility with fortitude, but it

[003-052] worried [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]].

[003-053] “I do wish father understood you better,” was her wistful comment when

[003-054] Archie had related the [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]].

[003-055] “Well, you know,” said Archie, “I’m open for being understood any time

[003-056] he cares to take a stab at it.”

[003-057] “You must try and make him fond of you.”

[003-058] “But how? I smile winsomely at him and what not, but he doesn’t

[003-059] respond.”

[003-060] “Well, we shall have to think of something. I want him to realise what

[003-061] an angel you are. You _are_ an angel, you know.”

[003-062] “No, really?”

[003-063] “Of course you are.”

[003-064] “It’s a rummy thing,” said Archie, pursuing a train of thought which

[003-065] was constantly with him, “the more I see of you, the more I wonder how

[003-066] you can have a father like—I mean to say, what I mean to say is, I wish

[003-067] I had known your mother; she must have been frightfully attractive.”

[003-068] “What would really please him, I know,” said Lucille, “would be if you

[003-069] got some work to do. He loves people who work.”

[003-070] “Yes?” said Archie doubtfully. “Well, you know, I heard him

[003-071] interviewing that chappie behind the desk this morning, who works like

[003-072] the dickens from early morn to dewy eve, on the subject of a mistake in

[003-073] his figures; and, if he loved him, he dissembled it all right. Of

[003-074] course, I admit that so far I haven’t been one of the toilers, but the

[003-075] dashed difficult thing is to know how to start. I’m nosing round, but

[003-076] the openings for a bright young man seem so scarce.”

[003-077] “Well, keep on trying. I feel sure that, if you could only find

[003-078] something to do, it doesn’t matter what, father would be quite

[003-079] different.”

[003-080] It was possibly the dazzling prospect of making Mr. Brewster quite

[003-081] different that stimulated Archie. He was strongly of the opinion that

[003-082] any change in his father-in-law must inevitably be for the better. A

[003-083] chance meeting with James B. [[j-b-wheeler|Wheeler]], the artist, at the Pen-and-Ink

[003-084] Club seemed to open the way.

[003-085] To a visitor to New York who has the ability to make himself liked it

[003-086] almost appears as though the leading industry in that city was the

[003-087] issuing of two-weeks’ invitation-cards to [[clubs-and-social-institutions|clubs]]. Archie since his

[003-088] arrival had been showered with these pleasant evidences of his

[003-089] popularity; and he was now an honorary member of so many clubs of

[003-090] various kinds that he had not time to go to them all. There were the

[003-091] fashionable clubs along [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] to which his friend [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van

[003-092] [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]], son of his Florida hostess, had introduced him. There were the

[003-093] businessmen’s clubs of which he was made free by more solid citizens.

[003-094] And, best of all, there were the Lambs’, the Players’, the Friars’, the

[003-095] Coffee-House, the Pen-and-Ink,—and the other resorts of the artist, the

[003-096] author, the actor, and the Bohemian. It was in these that Archie spent

[003-097] most of his time, and it was here that he made the acquaintance of J.

[003-098] B. Wheeler, the popular illustrator.

[003-099] To Mr. Wheeler, over a friendly lunch, Archie had been confiding some

[003-100] of his ambitions to qualify as the hero of one of the

[003-101] Get-on-or-get-out-young-man-step-lively-books.

[003-102] “You want a job?” said Mr. Wheeler.

[003-103] “I want a job,” said Archie.

[003-104] Mr. Wheeler consumed eight fried potatoes in quick succession. He was

[003-105] an able trencherman.

[003-106] “I always looked on you as one of our leading lilies of the field,” he

[003-107] said. “Why this anxiety to toil and spin?”

[003-108] “Well, my wife, you know, seems to think it might put me one-up with

[003-109] the jolly old dad if I did something.”

[003-110] “And you’re not particular what you do, so long as it has the outer

[003-111] aspect of work?”

[003-112] “Anything in the world, laddie, anything in the world.”

[003-113] “Then come and pose for a picture I’m doing,” said J. B. Wheeler. “It’s

[003-114] for a magazine cover. You’re just the model I want, and I’ll pay you at

[003-115] the usual rates. Is it a go?”

[003-116] “Pose?”

[003-117] “You’ve only got to stand still and look like a chunk of wood. You can

[003-118] do that, surely?”

[003-119] “I can do that,” said Archie.

[003-120] “Then come along down to my studio to-morrow.”

[003-121] “Right-o!” said Archie.

[003-122] CHAPTER V.

[003-123] STRANGE EXPERIENCES OF AN ARTIST’S MODEL

[003-124] “I say, old thing!”

[003-125] Archie spoke plaintively. Already he was looking back ruefully to the

[003-126] time when he had supposed that an artist’s model had a soft job. In the

[003-127] first five minutes muscles which he had not been aware that he

[003-128] possessed had started to ache like neglected teeth. His respect for the

[003-129] toughness and durability of artists’ models was now solid. How they

[003-130] acquired the stamina to go through this sort of thing all day and then

[003-131] bound off to Bohemian revels at night was more than he could

[003-132] understand.

[003-133] “Don’t wobble, confound you!” snorted Mr. Wheeler.

[003-134] “Yes, but, my dear old artist,” said Archie, “what you don’t seem to

[003-135] grasp—what you appear not to realise—is that I’m getting a crick in the

[003-136] back.”

[003-137] “You weakling! You miserable, invertebrate worm. Move an inch and I’ll

[003-138] murder you, and come and dance on your grave every Wednesday and

[003-139] Saturday. I’m just getting it.”

[003-140] “It’s in the spine that it seems to catch me principally.”

[003-141] “Be a man, you faint-hearted string-bean!” urged J. B. Wheeler. “You

[003-142] ought to be ashamed of yourself. Why, a girl who was posing for me last

[003-143] week stood for a solid hour on one leg, holding a tennis racket over

[003-144] her head and smiling brightly withal.”

[003-145] “The female of the species is more india-rubbery than the male,” argued

[003-146] Archie.

[003-147] “Well, I’ll be through in a few minutes. Don’t weaken. Think how proud

[003-148] you’ll be when you see yourself on all the bookstalls.”

[003-149] Archie sighed, and braced himself to the task once more. He wished he

[003-150] had never taken on this binge. In addition to his physical discomfort,

[003-151] he was feeling a most awful chump. The cover on which Mr. Wheeler was

[003-152] engaged was for the August number of the magazine, and it had been

[003-153] necessary for Archie to drape his reluctant form in a two-piece bathing

[003-154] suit of a vivid lemon colour; for he was supposed to be representing

[003-155] one of those jolly dogs belonging to the best families who dive off

[003-156] floats at exclusive seashore resorts. J. B. Wheeler, a stickler for

[003-157] accuracy, had wanted him to remove his socks and shoes; but there

[003-158] Archie had stood firm. He was willing to make an ass of himself, but

[003-159] not a silly ass.

[003-160] “All right,” said J. B. Wheeler, laying down his brush. “That will do

[003-161] for to-day. Though, speaking without prejudice and with no wish to be

[003-162] offensive, if I had had a model who wasn’t a weak-kneed,

[003-163] jelly-backboned son of Belial, I could have got the darned thing

[003-164] finished without having to have another sitting.”

[003-165] “I wonder why you chappies call this sort of thing ‘sitting,’” said

[003-166] Archie, pensively, as he conducted tentative experiments in osteopathy

[003-167] on his aching back. “I say, old thing, I could do with a restorative,

[003-168] if you have one handy. But, of course, you haven’t, I suppose,” he

[003-169] added, resignedly. Abstemious as a rule, there were moments when Archie

[003-170] found the Eighteenth Amendment somewhat trying.

[003-171] J. B. Wheeler shook his head.

[003-172] “You’re a little previous,” he said. “But come round in another day or

[003-173] so, and I may be able to do something for you.” He moved with a certain

[003-174] conspirator-like caution to a corner of the room, and, lifting to one

[003-175] side a pile of canvases, revealed a stout barrel, which he regarded

[003-176] with a fatherly and benignant eye. “I don’t mind telling you that, in

[003-177] the fullness of time, I believe this is going to spread a good deal of

[003-178] sweetness and light.”

[003-179] “Oh, ah,” said Archie, interested. “Home-brew, what?”

[003-180] “Made with these hands. I added a few more raisins yesterday, to speed

[003-181] things up a bit. There is much virtue in your raisin. And, talking of

[003-182] speeding things up, for goodness’ sake try to be a bit more punctual

[003-183] to-morrow. We lost an hour of good daylight to-day.”

[003-184] “I like that! I was here on the absolute minute. I had to hang about on

[003-185] the landing waiting for you.”

[003-186] “Well, well, that doesn’t matter,” said J. B. Wheeler, impatiently, for

[003-187] the artist soul is always annoyed by petty details. “The point is that

[003-188] we were an hour late in getting to work. Mind you’re here to-morrow at

[003-189] eleven sharp.”

[003-190] It was, therefore, with a feeling of guilt and trepidation that Archie

[003-191] mounted the stairs on the following morning; for in spite of his good

[003-192] resolutions he was half an hour behind time. He was relieved to find

[003-193] that his friend had also lagged by the wayside. The door of the studio

[003-194] was ajar, and he went in, to discover the place occupied by a lady of

[003-195] mature years, who was scrubbing the floor with a mop. He went into the

[003-196] bedroom and donned his bathing suit. When he emerged, ten minutes

[003-197] later, the charwoman had gone, but J. B. Wheeler was still absent.

[003-198] Rather glad of the respite, he sat down to kill time by reading the

[003-199] morning paper, whose sporting page alone he had managed to master at

[003-200] the breakfast table.
