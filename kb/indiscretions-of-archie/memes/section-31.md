## Section 31

[031-001] “What are you doing these days?” enquired [[wilson-hymack|Wilson Hymack]].

[031-002] “Me?” said [[archie-moffam|Archie]]. “Well, as a matter of fact, there is what you might

[031-003] call a sort of species of lull in my activities at the moment. But my

[031-004] jolly old [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]] is bustling about, running up a new hotel a bit

[031-005] farther down-town, and the scheme is for me to be manager when it’s

[031-006] finished. From what I have seen in this place, it’s a simple sort of

[031-007] job, and I fancy I shall be somewhat hot stuff. How are you filling in

[031-008] the long hours?”

[031-009] “I’m in my uncle’s office, darn it!”

[031-010] “Starting at the bottom and learning the business and all that? A noble

[031-011] pursuit, no doubt, but I’m bound to say it would give me the pip in no

[031-012] uncertain manner.”

[031-013] “It gives me,” said Wilson Hymack, “a pain in the thorax. I want to be

[031-014] a composer.”

[031-015] “A composer, eh?”

[031-016] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] felt that he should have guessed this. The chappie had a

[031-017] distinctly artistic look. He wore a bow-tie and all that sort of thing.

[031-018] His trousers bagged at the knees, and his hair, which during the

[031-019] martial epoch of his career had been pruned to the roots, fell about

[031-020] his ears in luxuriant disarray.

[031-021] “Say! Do you want to hear the best thing I’ve ever done?”

[031-022] “Indubitably,” said Archie, politely. “Carry on, old bird!”

[031-023] “I wrote the lyric as well as the melody,” said Wilson Hymack, who had

[031-024] already seated himself at the piano. “It’s got the greatest title you

[031-025] ever heard. It’s a lallapaloosa! It’s called ‘It’s a Long Way Back to

[031-026] Mother’s Knee.’ How’s that? Poor, eh?”

[031-027] Archie expelled a smoke-ring doubtfully.

[031-028] “Isn’t it a little stale?”

[031-029] “Stale? What do you mean, stale? There’s always room for another song

[031-030] boosting Mother.”

[031-031] “Oh, is it boosting Mother?” Archie’s face cleared. “I thought it was a

[031-032] hit at the short skirts. Why, of course, that makes all the difference.

[031-033] In that case, I see no reason why it should not be ripe, fruity, and

[031-034] pretty well all to the mustard. Let’s have it.”

[031-035] Wilson Hymack pushed as much of his hair out of his eyes as he could

[031-036] reach with one hand, cleared his throat, looked dreamily over the top

[031-037] of the piano at a photograph of Archie’s [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]], Mr. Daniel

[031-038] Brewster, played a prelude, and began to sing in a weak, high,

[031-039] composer’s voice. All composers sing exactly alike, and they have to be

[031-040] heard to be believed.

[031-041] “One night a young man wandered through the glitter of Broadway:

[031-042] His [[wealth-and-money|money]] he had squandered. For a meal he couldn’t pay.”

[031-043] “Tough luck!” murmured Archie, sympathetically.

[031-044] “He thought about the village where his boyhood he had spent,

[031-045] And yearned for all the simple joys with which he’d been content.”

[031-046] “The right spirit!” said Archie, with approval. “I’m beginning to like

[031-047] this chappie!”

[031-048] “Don’t interrupt!”

[031-049] “Oh, right-o! Carried away and all that!”

[031-050] “He looked upon the city, so frivolous and gay; And,

[031-051] as he heaved a weary sigh, these words he then did say:

[031-052] It’s a long way back to Mother’s knee,

[031-053] Mother’s knee,

[031-054] Mother’s knee:

[031-055] It’s a long way back to Mother’s knee,

[031-056] Where I used to stand and prattle

[031-057] With my teddy-bear and rattle:

[031-058] Oh, those childhood days in Tennessee,

[031-059] They sure look good to me!

[031-060] It’s a long, long way, but I’m gonna start to-day!

[031-061] I’m going back,

[031-062] Believe me, oh!

[031-063] I’m going back

[031-064] (I want to go!)

[031-065] I’m going back—back—on the seven-three

[031-066] To the dear old shack where I used to be!

[031-067] I’m going back to Mother’s knee!”

[031-068] Wilson Hymack’s voice cracked on the final high note, which was of an

[031-069] altitude beyond his powers. He turned with a modest cough.

[031-070] “That’ll give you an idea of it!”

[031-071] “It has, old thing, it has!”

[031-072] “Is it or is it not a ball of fire?”

[031-073] “It has many of the earmarks of a sound egg,” admitted Archie. “Of

[031-074] course—”

[031-075] “Of course, it wants singing.”

[031-076] “Just what I was going to suggest.”

[031-077] “It wants a woman to sing it. A woman who could reach out for that last

[031-078] high note and teach it to take a joke. The whole refrain is working up

[031-079] to that. You need Tetrazzini or someone who would just pick that note

[031-080] off the roof and hold it till the janitor came round to lock up the

[031-081] building for the night.”

[031-082] “I must buy a copy for my wife. Where can I get it?”

[031-083] “You can’t get it! It isn’t published. Writing music’s the darndest

[031-084] job!” Wilson Hymack snorted fiercely. It was plain that the man was

[031-085] pouring out the pent-up emotion of many days. “You write the biggest

[031-086] thing in years and you go round trying to get someone to sing it, and

[031-087] they say you’re a genius and then shove the song away in a drawer and

[031-088] forget about it.”

[031-089] Archie lit another cigarette.

[031-090] “I’m a jolly old child in these matters, old lad,” he said, “but why

[031-091] don’t you take it direct to a publisher? As a matter of fact, if it

[031-092] would be any use to you, I was foregathering with a music-publisher

[031-093] only the other day. A bird of the name of [[maxie-blumenthal|Blumenthal]]. He was lunching

[031-094] in here with a pal of mine, and we got tolerably matey. Why not let me

[031-095] tool you round to the office to-morrow and play it to him?”

[031-096] “No, thanks. Much obliged, but I’m not going to play that melody in any

[031-097] publisher’s office with his hired gang of Tin-Pan Alley composers

[031-098] listening at the keyhole and taking notes. I’ll have to wait till I can

[031-099] find somebody to sing it. Well, I must be going along. Glad to have

[031-100] seen you again. Sooner or later I’ll take you to hear that high note

[031-101] sung by someone in a way that’ll make your spine tie itself in knots

[031-102] round the back of your neck.”

[031-103] “I’ll count the days,” said Archie, courteously. “Pip-pip!”

[031-104] Hardly had the door closed behind the composer when it opened again to

[031-105] admit [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]].

[031-106] “Hallo, light of my soul!” said Archie, rising and embracing his wife.

[031-107] “Where have you been all the afternoon? I was expecting you this many

[031-108] an hour past. I wanted you to meet—”

[031-109] “I’ve been having tea with a girl down in Greenwich Village. I couldn’t

[031-110] get away before. Who was that who went out just as I came along the

[031-111] passage?”

[031-112] “Chappie of the name of Hymack. I met him in France. A composer and

[031-113] what not.”

[031-114] “We seem to have been moving in artistic circles this afternoon. The

[031-115] girl I went to see is a singer. At least, she wants to sing, but gets

[031-116] no encouragement.”

[031-117] “Precisely the same with my bird. He wants to get his music sung but

[031-118] nobody’ll sing it. But I didn’t know you knew any Greenwich Village

[031-119] warblers, sunshine of my home. How did you meet this female?”

[031-120] Lucille sat down and gazed forlornly at him with her big grey eyes. She

[031-121] was registering something, but Archie could not gather what it was.

[031-122] “Archie, darling, when you married me you undertook to share my

[031-123] sorrows, didn’t you?”

[031-124] “Absolutely! It’s all in the book of words. For better or for worse, in

[031-125] sickness and in health, all-down-set-’em-up-in-the-other-alley. Regular

[031-126] iron-clad contract!”

[031-127] “Then share ’em!” said Lucille. “Bill’s in love again!”

[031-128] Archie blinked.

[031-129] “Bill? When you say Bill, do you mean Bill? Your brother Bill? My

[031-130] brother-in-law Bill? Jolly old William, the son and heir of the

[031-131] Brewsters?”

[031-132] “I do.”

[031-133] “You say he’s in love? Cupid’s dart?”

[031-134] “Even so!”

[031-135] “But, I say! Isn’t this rather—What I mean to say is, the lad’s an

[031-136] absolute scourge! The Great Lover, what! Also ran, Brigham Young, and

[031-137] all that sort of thing! Why, it’s only a few weeks ago that he was

[031-138] moaning brokenly about that vermilion-haired female who subsequently

[031-139] hooked on to old [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]]!”

[031-140] “She’s a little better than that girl, thank goodness. All the same, I

[031-141] don’t think Father will approve.”

[031-142] “Of what calibre is the latest exhibit?”

[031-143] “Well, she comes from the Middle West, and seems to be trying to be

[031-144] twice as Bohemian as the rest of the girls down in Greenwich Village.

[031-145] She wears her hair bobbed and goes about in a kimono. She’s probably

[031-146] read magazine stories about Greenwich Village, and has modelled herself

[031-147] on them. It’s so silly, when you can see Hicks Corners sticking out of

[031-148] her all the time.”

[031-149] “That one got past me before I could grab it. What did you say she had

[031-150] sticking out of her?”

[031-151] “I meant that anybody could see that she came from somewhere out in the

[031-152] wilds. As a matter of fact, Bill tells me that she was brought up in

[031-153] Snake Bite, Michigan.”

[031-154] “Snake Bite? What rummy names you have in America! Still, I’ll admit

[031-155] there’s a village in [[england|England]] called Nether Wallop, so who am I to cast

[031-156] the first stone? How is old Bill? Pretty feverish?”

[031-157] “He says this time it is the real thing.”

[031-158] “That’s what they all say! I wish I had a dollar for every

[031-159] time—Forgotten what I was going to say!” broke off Archie, prudently.

[031-160] “So you think,” he went on, after a pause, “that William’s latest is

[031-161] going to be one more shock for the old dad?”

[031-162] “I can’t imagine Father approving of her.”

[031-163] “I’ve studied your merry old progenitor pretty closely,” said Archie,

[031-164] “and, between you and me, I can’t imagine him approving of anybody!”

[031-165] “I can’t understand why it is that Bill goes out of his way to pick

[031-166] these horrors. I know at least twenty delightful girls, all pretty and

[031-167] with lots of money, who would be just the thing for him; but he sneaks

[031-168] away and goes falling in love with someone impossible. And the worst of

[031-169] it is that one always feels one’s got to do one’s best to see him

[031-170] through.”

[031-171] “Absolutely! One doesn’t want to throw a spanner into the works of

[031-172] Love’s young dream. It behoves us to rally round. Have you heard this

[031-173] girl sing?”

[031-174] “Yes. She sang this afternoon.”

[031-175] “What sort of a voice has she got?”

[031-176] “Well, it’s—loud!”

[031-177] “Could she pick a high note off the roof and hold it till the janitor

[031-178] came round to lock up the building for the night?”

[031-179] “What on earth do you mean?”

[031-180] “Answer me this, woman, frankly. How is her high note? Pretty lofty?”

[031-181] “Why, yes.”

[031-182] “Then say no more,” said Archie. “Leave this to me, my dear old better

[031-183] four-fifths! Hand the whole thing over to Archibald, the man who never

[031-184] lets you down. I have a scheme!”

[031-185] As Archie approached his suite on the following afternoon he heard

[031-186] through the closed door the drone of a gruff male voice; and, going in,

[031-187] discovered Lucille in the company of his brother-in-law. Lucille,

[031-188] Archie thought, was looking a trifle fatigued. Bill, on the other hand,

[031-189] was in great shape. His eyes were shining, and his face looked so like

[031-190] that of a stuffed frog that Archie had no difficulty in gathering that

[031-191] he had been lecturing on the subject of his latest enslaver.

[031-192] “Hallo, Bill, old crumpet!” he said.

[031-193] “Hallo, Archie!”

[031-194] “I’m so glad you’ve come,” said Lucille. “Bill is telling me all about

[031-195] Spectatia.”

[031-196] “Who?”

[031-197] “Spectatia. The girl, you know. Her name is [[spectatia-huskisson|Spectatia Huskisson]].”

[031-198] “It can’t be!” said Archie, incredulously.

[031-199] “Why not?” growled Bill.

[031-200] “Well, how could it?” said Archie, appealing to him as a reasonable

[031-201] man. “I mean to say! Spectatia Huskisson! I gravely doubt whether there

[031-202] is such a name.”

[031-203] “What’s wrong with it?” demanded the incensed Bill. “It’s a darned

[031-204] sight better name than Archibald Moffam.”

[031-205] “Don’t fight, you two children!” intervened Lucille, firmly. “It’s a

[031-206] good old Middle West name. Everybody knows the Huskissons of Snake

[031-207] Bite, Michigan. Besides, Bill calls her Tootles.”

[031-208] “Pootles,” corrected Bill, austerely.

[031-209] “Oh, yes, Pootles. He calls her Pootles.”

[031-210] “Young blood! Young blood!” sighed Archie.

[031-211] “I wish you wouldn’t [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] as if you were my grandfather.”
