# Rational Eating

A dietary philosophy and lecture-circuit topic associated with Cora Bates McCall ([[mrs-mccall|Mrs. McCall]]), who travels America delivering addresses on the principles of scientifically informed food consumption.

## Overview

Rational Eating is a health movement of the early twentieth century, presented in the novel with Wodehouse's characteristic comic treatment of progressive social causes. Mrs. McCall is described as an "eminent lecturer on Rational Eating" (029-071), suggesting she has achieved a degree of celebrity for her dietary evangelism. The philosophy advocates for disciplined, scientifically informed food choices—a principle that becomes richly ironic when contrasted with Mrs. McCall's own behavior.

## In the Novel

Rational Eating serves primarily as a comic foil for Mrs. McCall's characterization. The irony is established immediately: McCall, the crusading apostle of dietary restraint, is introduced while "eating a slice of pie" (029-079). When her son [[washburn-mccall|Washburn]] attempts to inform her of a serious matter, she dismisses him with her "platform voice" and proceeds to discourse on the evils of indulgence while herself indulging (029-120). The scene at the restaurant on [[sixth-avenue|Sixth Avenue]] where Archie Moffam meets the McCalls further exploits the gap between Rational Eating's high-minded principles and its practitioner's appetites (028-113).

The concept also appears in newspaper coverage: an article about Rational Eating draws attention to Mrs. McCall's public profile (028-185). The philosophy is treated by Wodehouse as part of the broader landscape of American progressive fads that his English protagonist encounters—earnest, vaguely ridiculous, and undermined by the all-too-human weaknesses of their proponents.

## Key Passages

> "Pie," proceeded Mrs. McCall, in her platform voice. She seemed to address an invisible audience. (029-120)

> "I'll have to look up the papers, and it was about Rational Eating, and her picture was in them." (028-185)

## Associated Characters

- **[[mrs-mccall|Mrs. McCall]]** — Cora Bates McCall, the "eminent lecturer on Rational Eating" whose own eating habits satirize her philosophy (029-071)
- **[[washburn-mccall|Washburn McCall]]** — Mrs. McCall's son, long-suffering audience to his mother's dietary pronouncements (029-091)
- **[[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]** — Encounters the McCalls at a Sixth Avenue restaurant; provides the outsider's perspective on the absurdity (028-131)

## Historical / Cultural Context

The early twentieth century saw a flourishing of dietary reform movements in America, from Horace Fletcher's "Fletcherism" (thorough chewing) to John Harvey Kellogg's vegetarian sanitarium at Battle Creek. Wodehouse gently satirizes this landscape through the figure of Mrs. McCall—a type familiar to his readers: the self-appointed expert who lectures the public on health while privately ignoring her own prescriptions. The humor relies on the gap between progressive rhetoric and ordinary human appetite, a theme that recurs throughout Wodehouse's American fiction.
