## Section 14

[014-001] “Thanks so much!” said [[miss-silverton|Miss Silverton]].

[014-002] “Oh no, rather not,” said [[archie-moffam|Archie]].

[014-003] “Such a nuisance getting things in your eye.”

[014-004] “Absolutely!”

[014-005] “I’m always doing it!”

[014-006] “Rotten luck!”

[014-007] “But I don’t often find anyone as clever as you to help me.”

[014-008] [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] felt called upon to break in on this feast of reason and flow

[014-009] of soul.

[014-010] “[[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]],” she said, “if you go and get your [[clubs-and-social-institutions|clubs]] now, I shall just

[014-011] have time to walk round with you before my train goes.”

[014-012] “Oh, ah!” said Archie, perceiving her for the first time. “Oh, ah, yes,

[014-013] right-o, yes, yes, yes!”

[014-014] On the way to the first tee it seemed to Archie that Lucille was

[014-015] distrait and abstracted in her manner; and it occurred to him, not for

[014-016] the first time in his life, what a poor support a clear conscience is

[014-017] in moments of crisis. [[dash-it|Dash it all]], he didn’t see what else he could

[014-018] have done. Couldn’t leave the poor female staggering about the place

[014-019] with squads of flies wedged in her eyeball. Nevertheless—

[014-020] “Rotten thing getting a fly in your eye,” he hazarded at length.

[014-021] “Dashed awkward, I mean.”

[014-022] “Or convenient.”

[014-023] “Eh?”

[014-024] “Well, it’s a very good way of dispensing with an introduction.”

[014-025] “Oh, I say! You don’t mean you think—”

[014-026] “She’s a horrid woman!”

[014-027] “Absolutely! Can’t think what people see in her.”

[014-028] “Well, you seemed to enjoy fussing over her!”

[014-029] “No, no! Nothing of the kind! She inspired me with absolute

[014-030] what-d’you-call-it—the sort of thing chappies do get inspired with, you

[014-031] know.”

[014-032] “You were beaming all over your face.”

[014-033] “I wasn’t. I was just screwing up my face because the sun was in my

[014-034] eye.”

[014-035] “All sorts of things seem to be in people’s eyes this morning!”

[014-036] Archie was saddened. That this sort of misunderstanding should have

[014-037] occurred on such a topping day and at a moment when they were to be

[014-038] torn asunder for about thirty-six hours made him feel—well, it gave him

[014-039] the pip. He had an idea that there were words which would have

[014-040] straightened everything out, but he was not an eloquent young man and

[014-041] could not find them. He felt aggrieved. Lucille, he considered, ought

[014-042] to have known that he was immune as regarded females with flashing eyes

[014-043] and experimentally-coloured hair. Why, dash it, he could have extracted

[014-044] flies from the eyes of Cleopatra with one hand and Helen of Troy with

[014-045] the other, simultaneously, without giving them a second thought. It was

[014-046] in depressed mood that he played a listless nine holes; nor had life

[014-047] brightened for him when he came back to the hotel two hours later,

[014-048] after seeing Lucille off in the train to [[new-york-city|New York]]. Never till now had

[014-049] they had anything remotely resembling a quarrel. Life, Archie felt, was

[014-050] a bit of a wash-out. He was disturbed and jumpy, and the sight of Miss

[014-051] Silverton, talking to somebody on a settee in the corner of the hotel

[014-052] lobby, sent him shooting off at right angles and brought him up with a

[014-053] bump against the desk behind which the room-clerk sat.

[014-054] The room-clerk, always of a chatty disposition, was saying something to

[014-055] him, but Archie did not listen. He nodded mechanically. It was

[014-056] something about his room. He caught the word “satisfactory.”

[014-057] “Oh, rather, quite!” said Archie.

[014-058] A fussy devil, the room-clerk! He knew perfectly well that Archie found

[014-059] his room satisfactory. These chappies gassed on like this so as to try

[014-060] to make you feel that the management took a personal interest in you.

[014-061] It was part of their job. Archie beamed absently and went in to [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]].

[014-062] Lucille’s empty seat stared at him mournfully, increasing his sense of

[014-063] desolation.

[014-064] He was half-way through his lunch, when the chair opposite ceased to be

[014-065] vacant. Archie, transferring his gaze from the scenery outside the

[014-066] window, perceived that his friend, [[george-benham|George Benham]], the playwright, had

[014-067] materialised from nowhere and was now in his midst.

[014-068] “Hallo!” he said.

[014-069] George Benham was a grave young man whose spectacles gave him the look

[014-070] of a mournful owl. He seemed to have something on his mind besides the

[014-071] artistically straggling mop of black hair which swept down over his

[014-072] brow. He sighed wearily, and ordered fish-pie.

[014-073] “I thought I saw you come through the lobby just now,” he said.

[014-074] “Oh, was that you on the settee, talking to Miss Silverton?”

[014-075] “She was talking to _me_,” said the playwright, moodily.

[014-076] “What are you doing here?” asked Archie. He could have wished Mr.

[014-077] Benham elsewhere, for he intruded on his gloom, but, the chappie being

[014-078] amongst those present, it was only civil to [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] to him. “I thought you

[014-079] were in New York, watching the rehearsals of your jolly old drama.”

[014-080] “The rehearsals are hung up. And it [[appearance-and-first-impressions|looks]] as though there wasn’t going

[014-081] to be any drama. Good Lord!” cried George Benham, with honest warmth,

[014-082] “with opportunities opening out before one on every side—with life

[014-083] extending prizes to one with both hands—when you see coal-heavers

[014-084] making fifty dollars a week and the fellows who clean out the sewers

[014-085] going happy and singing about their work—why does a man deliberately

[014-086] choose a job like writing plays? Job was the only man that ever lived

[014-087] who was really qualified to write a play, and he would have found it

[014-088] pretty tough going if his leading woman had been anyone like Vera

[014-089] Silverton!”

[014-090] Archie—and it was this fact, no doubt, which accounted for his

[014-091] possession of such a large and varied circle of friends—was always able

[014-092] to shelve his own troubles in order to listen to other people’s

[014-093] hard-luck stories.

[014-094] “Tell me all, laddie,” he said. “Release the film! Has she walked out

[014-095] on you?”

[014-096] “Left us flat! How did you hear about it? Oh, she told you, of course?”

[014-097] Archie hastened to try to dispel the idea that he was on any such terms

[014-098] of intimacy with Miss Silverton.

[014-099] “No, no! My wife said she thought it must be something of that nature

[014-100] or order when we saw her come in to breakfast. I mean to say,” said

[014-101] Archie, reasoning closely, “woman can’t come into breakfast here and be

[014-102] rehearsing in New York at the same time. Why did she administer the

[014-103] raspberry, old friend?”

[014-104] Mr. Benham helped himself to fish-pie, and spoke dully through the

[014-105] steam.

[014-106] “Well, what happened was this. Knowing her as intimately as you do—”

[014-107] “I _don’t_ know her!”

[014-108] “Well, anyway, it was like this. As you know, she has a dog—”

[014-109] “I didn’t know she had a dog,” protested Archie. It seemed to him that

[014-110] the world was in conspiracy to link him with this woman.

[014-111] “Well, she has a dog. A beastly great whacking brute of a bulldog. And

[014-112] she brings it to rehearsal.” Mr. Benham’s eyes filled with tears, as in

[014-113] his emotion he swallowed a mouthful of fish-pie some eighty-three

[014-114] degrees Fahrenheit hotter than it looked. In the intermission caused by

[014-115] this disaster his agile mind skipped a few chapters of the story, and,

[014-116] when he was able to speak again, he said, “So then there was a lot of

[014-117] trouble. Everything broke loose!”

[014-118] “Why?” Archie was puzzled. “Did the management object to her bringing

[014-119] the dog to rehearsal?”

[014-120] “A lot of good that would have done! She does what she likes in the

[014-121] theatre.”

[014-122] “Then why was there trouble?”

[014-123] “You weren’t listening,” said Mr. Benham, reproachfully. “I told you.

[014-124] This dog came snuffling up to where I was sitting—it was quite dark in

[014-125] the body of the theatre, you know—and I got up to say something about

[014-126] something that was happening on the stage, and somehow I must have

[014-127] given it a push with my foot.”

[014-128] “I see,” said Archie, beginning to get the run of the plot. “You kicked

[014-129] her dog.”

[014-130] “Pushed it. Accidentally. With my foot.”

[014-131] “I understand. And when you brought off this kick—”

[014-132] “Push,” said Mr. Benham, austerely.

[014-133] “This kick or push. When you administered this kick or push—”

[014-134] “It was more a sort of light shove.”

[014-135] “Well, when you did whatever you did, the trouble started?”

[014-136] Mr. Benham gave a slight shiver.

[014-137] “She talked for a while, and then walked out, taking the dog with her.

[014-138] You see, this wasn’t the first time it had happened.”

[014-139] “Good Lord! Do you spend your whole time doing that sort of thing?”

[014-140] “It wasn’t me the first time. It was the stage-manager. He didn’t know

[014-141] whose dog it was, and it came waddling on to the stage, and he gave it

[014-142] a sort of pat, a kind of flick—”

[014-143] “A slosh?”

[014-144] “_Not_ a slosh,” corrected Mr. Benham, firmly. “You might call it a

[014-145] tap—with the promptscript. Well, we had a lot of difficulty smoothing

[014-146] her over that time. Still, we managed to do it, but she said that if

[014-147] anything of the sort occurred again she would chuck up her part.”

[014-148] “She must be fond of the dog,” said Archie, for the first time feeling

[014-149] a touch of goodwill and sympathy towards the lady.

[014-150] “She’s crazy about it. That’s what made it so awkward when I

[014-151] happened—quite inadvertently—to give it this sort of accidental shove.

[014-152] Well, we spent the rest of the day trying to get her on the ’phone at

[014-153] her apartment, and finally we heard that she had come here. So I took

[014-154] the next train, and tried to persuade her to come back. She wouldn’t

[014-155] listen. And that’s how matters stand.”

[014-156] “Pretty rotten!” said Archie, sympathetically.

[014-157] “You can bet it’s pretty rotten—for me. There’s nobody else who can

[014-158] play the part. Like a chump, I wrote the thing specially for her. It

[014-159] means the play won’t be produced at all, if she doesn’t do it. So

[014-160] you’re my last [[hope-and-optimism|hope]]!”

[014-161] Archie, who was lighting a cigarette, nearly swallowed it.

[014-162] “_I_ am?”

[014-163] “I thought you might persuade her. Point out to her what a lot hangs on

[014-164] her coming back. Jolly her along, _you_ know the sort of thing!”

[014-165] “But, my dear old friend, I tell you I don’t know her!”

[014-166] Mr. Benham’s eyes opened behind their zareba of glass.

[014-167] “Well, she knows _you_. When you came through the lobby just now she

[014-168] said that you were the only real human being she had ever met.”

[014-169] “Well, as a matter of fact, I did take a fly out of her eye. But—”

[014-170] “You did? Well, then, the whole thing’s simple. All you have to do is

[014-171] to ask her how her eye is, and tell her she has the most beautiful eyes

[014-172] you ever saw, and coo a bit.”

[014-173] “But, my dear old son!” The frightful programme which his friend had

[014-174] mapped out stunned Archie. “I simply can’t! Anything to oblige and all

[014-175] that sort of thing, but when it comes to cooing, distinctly Napoo!”

[014-176] “Nonsense! It isn’t hard to coo.”

[014-177] “You don’t understand, laddie. You’re not a married man. I mean to say,

[014-178] whatever you say for or against marriage—personally I’m all for it and

[014-179] consider it a ripe egg—the fact remains that it practically makes a

[014-180] chappie a spent force as a cooer. I don’t want to dish you in any way,

[014-181] [[old-bean|old bean]], but I must firmly and resolutely decline to coo.”

[014-182] Mr. Benham rose and looked at his watch.

[014-183] “I’ll have to be moving,” he said. “I’ve got to get back to New York

[014-184] and report. I’ll tell them that I haven’t been able to do anything

[014-185] myself, but that I’ve left the matter in good hands. I know you will do

[014-186] your best.”

[014-187] “But, laddie!”

[014-188] “Think,” said Mr. Benham, solemnly, “of all that depends on it! The

[014-189] other actors! The small-part people thrown out of a job! Myself—but no!

[014-190] Perhaps you had better touch very lightly or not at all on my

[014-191] connection with the thing. Well, you know how to handle it. I feel I

[014-192] can leave it to you. Pitch it strong! Good-bye, my dear old man, and a

[014-193] thousand thanks. I’ll do the same for you another time.” He moved

[014-194] towards the door, leaving Archie transfixed. Half-way there he turned

[014-195] and came back. “Oh, by the way,” he said, “my lunch. Have it put on

[014-196] your bill, will you? I haven’t time to stay and settle. Good-bye!

[014-197] Good-bye!”
