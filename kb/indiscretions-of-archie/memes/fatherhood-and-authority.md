# Fatherhood and Authority

The central comic tension of *Indiscretions of Archie* is generational: [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]], the self-made millionaire and father of [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]], confronts [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]], the penniless Englishman who has married his daughter without permission. Wodehouse uses this father–son-in-law dynamic to explore authority, inheritance, and the transfer of patriarchal power in 1920s America. The novel's central question — can a self-made man accept a son-in-law who has made nothing at all? — generates comedy from a genuinely painful social situation.

## Overview

Mr. Brewster's identity as a father is inseparable from his identity as a proprietor. He is introduced as Archie's "father-in-law" (001-034), a role he neither sought nor welcomes. The novel opens with the consequences of Archie and Lucille's elopement already in motion — Brewster has been presented with a fait accompli, and his authority, the authority on which his entire self-conception rests, has been bypassed entirely.

The father-in-law relationship becomes the novel's primary axis of conflict. Brewster's question — "What sort of a man _is_ your father?" (002-105) — reflects his anxious need to categorize and control. From the moment he learns of the marriage, Brewster is a man struggling to reassert a dominance that has been taken from him.

## In the Novel

### The Son-in-Law Problem

Wodehouse returns repeatedly to the son-in-law as a comic type. Brewster, "having acquired Archie for a son-in-law" (002-117), finds himself burdened with a young man who "combined in almost equal proportions the qualities of imbecile and crook" (002-122). The son-in-law is an intrusion into the patriarchal order — a stranger with claims on the daughter and, by extension, on the father's resources and affection.

Brewster's lament — "inflicted this—_this_ on me for a son-in-law?" (002-148) — is the cry of a man whose entire system of values has been violated. Archie is not merely a disappointment; he is a refutation of everything Brewster believes about merit, industry, and reward.

### Generations and Authority

The fatherhood theme operates on multiple levels. At the surface, it drives the plot: Brewster's disapproval motivates Archie's attempts to prove himself. At a deeper level, it explores the transition of authority from one generation to the next. Brewster must learn to accept that his daughter has chosen a husband he would never have selected — and that her choice, not his, is the one that matters.

Lucille occupies the pivotal position between the two men. Her introduction of Archie — "Archie, darling, this is father" (002-133) — is the novel's key bridging moment, an attempt to connect two incompatible worlds through the bond of her affection for both. Her subsequent management of her father's emotions demonstrates that true authority in the novel belongs not to the patriarch but to the daughter who navigates between patriarch and husband.

The son-in-law relationship evolves across the novel. By later chapters, Archie has embraced his status as "jolly old Brewster's son-in-law" (032-187) as a kind of credential — a transformation that mirrors Brewster's own grudging acceptance. Brewster, who "had suffered much from his son-in-law" (027-119), ultimately finds that suffering has produced something like affection.
