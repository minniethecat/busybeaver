## Section 28

[028-001] Suddenly he became aware that he was not the only person present who

[028-002] was deriving enjoyment from the scent of the meal. Standing beside the

[028-003] waiter and gazing wistfully at the foodstuffs was a long, thin boy of

[028-004] about sixteen. He was one of those boys who seem all legs and knuckles.

[028-005] He had pale red hair, sandy eyelashes, and a long neck; and his eyes,

[028-006] as he removed them from the-table and raised them to [[archie-moffam|Archie]]’s, had a

[028-007] hungry look. He reminded [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] of a half-grown, half-starved hound.

[028-008] “That smells good!” said the long boy. He inhaled deeply. “Yes, sir,”

[028-009] he continued, as one whose mind is definitely made up, “that smells

[028-010] good!”

[028-011] Before Archie could reply, the telephone bell rang. It was [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]],

[028-012] confirming her prophecy that the pest Jane would insist on her staying

[028-013] to dine.

[028-014] “Jane,” said Archie, into the telephone, “is a pot of poison. The

[028-015] waiter is here now, setting out a rich banquet, and I shall have to eat

[028-016] two of everything by myself.”

[028-017] He hung up the receiver, and, turning, met the pale eye of the long

[028-018] boy, who had propped himself up in the doorway.

[028-019] “Were you expecting somebody to [[dining-and-social-ritual|dinner]]?” asked the boy.

[028-020] “Why, yes, old friend, I was.”

[028-021] “I wish—”

[028-022] “Yes?”

[028-023] “Oh, nothing.”

[028-024] The waiter left. The long boy hitched his back more firmly against the

[028-025] doorpost, and returned to his original theme.

[028-026] “That surely does smell good!” He basked a moment in the aroma. “Yes,

[028-027] sir! I’ll tell the world it does!”

[028-028] Archie was not an abnormally rapid thinker, but he began at this point

[028-029] to get a clearly defined impression that this lad, if invited, would

[028-030] waive the formalities and consent to join his meal. Indeed, the idea

[028-031] Archie got was that, if he were not invited pretty soon, he would

[028-032] invite himself.

[028-033] “Yes,” he agreed. “It doesn’t smell bad, what!”

[028-034] “It smells _good!_” said the boy. “Oh, doesn’t it! Wake me up in the

[028-035] night and ask me if it doesn’t!”

[028-036] “_Poulet en casserole_,” said Archie.

[028-037] “Golly!” said the boy, reverently.

[028-038] There was a pause. The situation began to seem to Archie a trifle

[028-039] difficult. He wanted to start his meal, but it began to appear that he

[028-040] must either do so under the penetrating gaze of his new friend or else

[028-041] eject the latter forcibly. The boy showed no signs of ever wanting to

[028-042] leave the doorway.

[028-043] “You’ve dined, I suppose, what?” said Archie.

[028-044] “I never dine.”

[028-045] “What!”

[028-046] “Not really dine, I mean. I only get vegetables and nuts and things.”

[028-047] “Dieting?”

[028-048] “Mother is.”

[028-049] “I don’t absolutely catch the drift, [[old-bean|old bean]],” said Archie. The boy

[028-050] sniffed with half-closed eyes as a wave of perfume from the _poulet en

[028-051] casserole_ floated past him. He seemed to be anxious to intercept as

[028-052] much of it as possible before it got through the door.

[028-053] “Mother’s a food-reformer,” he vouchsafed. “She lectures on it. She

[028-054] makes Pop and me live on vegetables and nuts and things.”

[028-055] Archie was shocked. It was like listening to a tale from the abyss.

[028-056] “My dear old chap, you must suffer agonies—absolute shooting pains!” He

[028-057] had no hesitation now. Common humanity pointed out his course. “Would

[028-058] you care to join me in a bite now?”

[028-059] “Would I!” The boy smiled a wan smile. “Would I! Just stop me on the

[028-060] street and ask me!”

[028-061] “Come on in, then,” said Archie, rightly taking this peculiar phrase

[028-062] for a formal acceptance. “And close the door. The fatted calf is

[028-063] getting cold.”

[028-064] Archie was not a man with a wide visiting-list among people with

[028-065] families, and it was so long since he had seen a growing boy in action

[028-066] at the table that he had forgotten what sixteen is capable of doing

[028-067] with a knife and fork, when it really squares its elbows, takes a deep

[028-068] breath, and gets going. The spectacle which he witnessed was

[028-069] consequently at first a little unnerving. The long boy’s idea of

[028-070] trifling with a meal appeared to be to swallow it whole and reach out

[028-071] for more. He ate like a starving Eskimo. Archie, in the time he had

[028-072] spent in the trenches making the world safe for the working-man to

[028-073] strike in, had occasionally been quite peckish, but he sat dazed before

[028-074] this majestic hunger. This was real eating.

[028-075] There was little [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]]. The growing boy evidently did not

[028-076] believe in table-talk when he could use his mouth for more practical

[028-077] purposes. It was not until the final roll had been devoured to its last

[028-078] crumb that the guest found [[leisure-and-sports|leisure]] to address his host. Then he leaned

[028-079] back with a contented sigh.

[028-080] “Mother,” said the human python, “says you ought to chew every mouthful

[028-081] thirty-three times....”

[028-082] “Yes, sir! Thirty-three times!” He sighed again, “I haven’t ever had a

[028-083] meal like that.”

[028-084] “All right, was it, what?”

[028-085] “Was it! Was it! Call me up on the ’phone and ask me!-Yes,

[028-086] sir!-Mother’s tipped off these darned waiters not to serve me anything

[028-087] but vegetables and nuts and things, darn it!”

[028-088] “The mater seems to have drastic ideas about the good old feed-bag,

[028-089] what!”

[028-090] “I’ll say she has! Pop hates it as much as me, but he’s scared to kick.

[028-091] Mother says vegetables contain all the proteins you want. Mother says,

[028-092] if you eat meat, your blood-pressure goes all blooey. Do you think it

[028-093] does?”

[028-094] “Mine seems pretty well in the pink.”

[028-095] “She’s great on talking,” conceded the boy. “She’s out to-night

[028-096] somewhere, giving a lecture on Rational Eating to some ginks. I’ll have

[028-097] to be slipping up to our suite before she gets back.” He rose,

[028-098] sluggishly. “That isn’t a bit of roll under that napkin, is it?” he

[028-099] asked, anxiously.

[028-100] Archie raised the napkin.

[028-101] “No. Nothing of that species.”

[028-102] “Oh, well!” said the boy, resignedly. “Then I believe I’ll be going.

[028-103] Thanks very much for the dinner.”

[028-104] “Not a bit, old top. Come again if you’re ever trickling round in this

[028-105] direction.”

[028-106] The long boy removed himself slowly, loath to leave. At the door he

[028-107] cast an affectionate glance back at the table.

[028-108] “Some meal!” he said, devoutly. “Considerable meal!”

[028-109] Archie lit a cigarette. He felt like a Boy Scout who has done his day’s

[028-110] Act of Kindness.

[028-111] On the following morning it chanced that Archie needed a fresh supply

[028-112] of tobacco. It was his custom, when this happened, to repair to a small

[028-113] shop on Sixth Avenue which he had discovered accidentally in the course

[028-114] of his rambles about the great city. His relations with Jno. Blake, the

[028-115] proprietor, were friendly and intimate. The discovery that Mr. Blake

[028-116] was English and had, indeed, until a few years back maintained an

[028-117] establishment only a dozen doors or so from Archie’s London club, had

[028-118] served as a bond.

[028-119] To-day he found Mr. Blake in a depressed mood. The tobacconist was a

[028-120] hearty, red-faced man, who looked like an English sporting publican—the

[028-121] kind of man who wears a fawn-coloured top-coat and drives to the Derby

[028-122] in a dog-cart; and usually there seemed to be nothing on his mind

[028-123] except the vagaries of the weather, concerning which he was a great

[028-124] conversationalist. But now moodiness had claimed him for its own. After

[028-125] a short and melancholy “Good morning,” he turned to the task of

[028-126] measuring out the tobacco in silence.

[028-127] Archie’s sympathetic nature was perturbed.—“What’s the matter, laddie?”

[028-128] he enquired. “You would seem to be feeling a bit of an onion this

[028-129] bright morning, what, yes, no? I can see it with the naked eye.”

[028-130] Mr. Blake grunted sorrowfully.

[028-131] “I’ve had a knock, Mr. Moffam.”

[028-132] “Tell me all, friend of my youth.”

[028-133] Mr. Blake, with a jerk of his thumb, indicated a poster which hung on

[028-134] the wall behind the counter. Archie had noticed it as he came in, for

[028-135] it was designed to attract the eye. It was printed in black [[letters-and-correspondence|letters]] on

[028-136] a yellow ground, and ran as follows:

[028-137] CLOVER-LEAF SOCIAL AND OUTING CLUB

[028-138] GRAND CONTEST

[028-139] PIE-EATING CHAMPIONSHIP OF THE WEST SIDE

[028-140] SPIKE O’DOWD

[028-141] (Champion)

[028-142] _v_.

[028-143] BLAKE’S UNKNOWN

[028-144] FOR A PURSE OF $50 AND SIDE-BET

[028-145] Archie examined this document gravely. It conveyed nothing to him

[028-146] except—what he had long suspected—that his sporting-looking friend had

[028-147] sporting blood as well as that kind of exterior. He expressed a kindly

[028-148] [[hope-and-optimism|hope]] that the other’s Unknown would bring home the bacon.

[028-149] Mr. Blake laughed one of those hollow, mirthless laughs.

[028-150] “There ain’t any blooming Unknown,” he said, bitterly. This man had

[028-151] plainly suffered. “Yesterday, yes, but not now.”

[028-152] Archie sighed.

[028-153] “In the midst of life—Dead?” he enquired, delicately.

[028-154] “As good as,” replied the stricken tobacconist. He cast aside his

[028-155] artificial restraint and became voluble. Archie was one of those

[028-156] sympathetic souls in whom even strangers readily confided their most

[028-157] intimate troubles. He was to those in travail of spirit very much what

[028-158] catnip is to a cat. “It’s ’ard, sir, it’s blooming ’ard! I’d got the

[028-159] event all sewed up in a parcel, and now this young feller-me-lad ’as to

[028-160] give me the knock. This lad of mine—sort of cousin ’e is; comes from

[028-161] London, like you and me—’as always ’ad, ever since he landed in this

[028-162] country, a most amazing knack of stowing away grub. ’E’d been a bit

[028-163] underfed these last two or three years over in the old country, what

[028-164] with food restrictions and all, and ’e took to the food over ’ere

[028-165] amazing. I’d ’ave backed ’im against a ruddy orstridge! Orstridge! I’d

[028-166] ’ave backed ’im against ’arff a dozen orstridges—take ’em on one after

[028-167] the other in the same ring on the same evening—and given ’em a

[028-168] handicap, too! ’E was a jewel, that boy. I’ve seen him polish off four

[028-169] pounds of steak and mealy potatoes and then look round kind of wolfish,

[028-170] as much as to ask when dinner was going to begin! That’s the kind of a

[028-171] lad ’e was till this very morning. ’E would have out-swallowed this

[028-172] ’ere O’Dowd without turning a hair, as a relish before ’is tea! I’d got

[028-173] a couple of ’undred dollars on ’im, and thought myself lucky to get the

[028-174] odds. And now—”

[028-175] Mr. Blake relapsed into a tortured silence.

[028-176] “But what’s the matter with the blighter? Why can’t he go over the top?

[028-177] Has he got indigestion?”

[028-178] “Indigestion?” Mr. Blaife laughed another of his hollow laughs. “You

[028-179] couldn’t give that boy indigestion if you fed ’im in on safety-razor

[028-180] blades. Religion’s more like what ’e’s got.”

[028-181] “Religion?”

[028-182] “Well, you can call it that. Seems last night, instead of goin’ and

[028-183] resting ’is mind at a picture-palace like I told him to, ’e sneaked off

[028-184] to some sort of a lecture down on Eighth Avenue. ’E said ’e’d seen a

[028-185] piece about it in the papers, and it was about Rational Eating, and

[028-186] that kind of attracted ’im. ’E sort of thought ’e might pick up a few

[028-187] hints, like. ’E didn’t know what rational eating was, but it sounded to

[028-188] ’im as if it must be something to do with food, and ’e didn’t want to

[028-189] miss it. ’E came in here just now,” said Mr. Blake, dully, “and ’e was

[028-190] a changed lad! Scared to death ’e was! Said the way ’e’d been goin’ on

[028-191] in the past, it was a wonder ’e’d got any stummick left! It was a lady

[028-192] that give the lecture, and this boy said it was amazing what she told

[028-193] ’em about blood-pressure and things ’e didn’t even know ’e ’ad. She

[028-194] showed ’em [[art-and-collecting|pictures]], coloured pictures, of what ’appens inside the

[028-195] injudicious eater’s stummick who doesn’t chew his food, and it was like

[028-196] a battlefield! ’E said ’e would no more think of eatin’ a lot of pie

[028-197] than ’e would of shootin’ ’imself, and anyhow eating pie would be a

[028-198] quicker death. I reasoned with ’im, Mr. Moffam, with tears in my eyes.

[028-199] I asked ’im was he goin’ to chuck away fame and wealth just because a

[028-200] woman who didn’t know what she was talking about had shown him a lot of

[028-201] faked pictures. But there wasn’t any doin’ anything with him. ’E give

[028-202] me the knock and ’opped it down the street to buy nuts.” Mr. Blake

[028-203] moaned. “Two ’undred dollars and more gone pop, not to talk of the

[028-204] fifty dollars ’e would have won and me to get twenty-five of!”
