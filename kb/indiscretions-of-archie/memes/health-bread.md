# Health Bread

**Health Bread** is a recurring comic motif in *Indiscretions of Archie*, associated with [[mrs-mccall|Mrs. Cora Bates McCall]] and her "Rational Eating" movement. Served with nut butter at the McCall family breakfast table, the bread embodies the dietary-reform philosophy that Mrs. McCall preaches — and that her family endures with varying degrees of enthusiasm.

## Rational Eating

Mrs. McCall is described as "the eminent lecturer on Rational Eating" (029-071), and she both practises and preaches its doctrines: "Mrs. McCall herself was eating a slice of Health Bread and nut butter. For she practised as well as preached the doctrines" (029-079–080). The bread functions as a physical manifestation of an ideology — wholesome, principled, and, for those subjected to it at the breakfast table, distinctly unappealing.

## Comic Function

Wodehouse deploys Health Bread for character comedy. The McCall men approach it with barely concealed dread. Washington McCall "had been reaching out his hand for a slice of Health Bread, but now he withdrew it rather hurriedly and sat back breathing hard" (029-116–118) — the bread becoming almost a test of filial endurance. When Archie visits the McCall breakfast, he "took an uninvited seat, eyed the Health Bread on the breakfast table" (030-079), his reaction contributing to the broader comic portrait of American dietary faddism as seen through English eyes.

## In Simple Terms

Health Bread serves as Wodehouse's shorthand for an entire worldview — and his critique of it. In the novel's symbolic economy, the bread stands for everything earnest, improving, and joyless about Progressive-era dietary reform. It is not merely a foodstuff but a test of character: to eat Health Bread is to submit to Mrs. McCall's authority; to recoil from it, as Washington McCall does, is to assert — however timidly — the claims of ordinary human appetite against the tyranny of self-improvement.

The pairing of Health Bread with nut butter is itself a comic detail. The nut butter — bland, pasty, devoid of the rich pleasure of dairy butter — doubles down on the bread's message of renunciation. Together they constitute a breakfast of ideological compliance: every mouthful a reaffirmation of the Rational Eating creed, every swallowed bite a small defeat for the palate.

For Wodehouse's original readers, "Health Bread" would have conjured the figure of the food reformer — a familiar type in early-twentieth-century America, where movements like Grahamism (the whole-wheat crusade of Sylvester Graham) and Kellogg's Battle Creek sanitarium had made dietary reform a subject of both earnest advocacy and easy satire. Wodehouse, writing for a readership that included both adherents and sceptics of such movements, deploys the bread as a neutral comic prop that each reader can interpret according to their own sympathies — though the narrative's clear preference for pleasure over principle leaves little doubt about where its own loyalties lie.
