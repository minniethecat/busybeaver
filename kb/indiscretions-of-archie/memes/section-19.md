## Section 19

[019-001] “I’ve had my eye on you!” said the young man.

[019-002] He still had his eye on him. It was a hot, gimlet-like eye, and it

[019-003] pierced the recesses of [[archie-moffam|Archie]]’s soul. He backed a little farther

[019-004] against the wall.

[019-005] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] was frankly disturbed. He was no poltroon, and had proved the

[019-006] fact on many occasions during the days when the entire German army

[019-007] seemed to be picking on him personally, but he hated and shrank from

[019-008] anything in the nature of a bally public scene.

[019-009] “What,” enquired the young man, still bearing the burden of the

[019-010] [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]], and shifting his left hand a little farther behind his

[019-011] back, “do you mean by following this young lady?”

[019-012] Archie was glad he had asked him. This was precisely what he wanted to

[019-013] explain.

[019-014] “My dear old lad—” he began.

[019-015] In spite of the fact that he had asked a question and presumably

[019-016] desired a reply, the sound of Archie’s voice seemed to be more than the

[019-017] young man could endure. It deprived him of the last vestige of

[019-018] restraint. With a rasping snarl he brought his left fist round in a

[019-019] sweeping semicircle in the direction of Archie’s head.

[019-020] Archie was no novice in the [[art-and-collecting|art]] of self-defence. Since his early days

[019-021] at school he had learned much from leather-faced professors of the

[019-022] science. He had been watching this unpleasant young man’s eyes with

[019-023] close attention, and the latter could not have indicated his scheme of

[019-024] action more clearly if he had sent him a formal note. Archie saw the

[019-025] swing all the way. He stepped nimbly aside, and the fist crashed

[019-026] against the wall. The young man fell back with a yelp of anguish.

[019-027] “Gus!” screamed the Girl Friend, bounding forward.

[019-028] She flung her arms round the injured man, who was ruefully examining a

[019-029] hand which, always of an out-size, was now swelling to still further

[019-030] dimensions.

[019-031] “Gus, darling!”

[019-032] A sudden chill gripped Archie. So engrossed had he been with his

[019-033] mission that it had never occurred to him that the love-lorn pitcher

[019-034] might have taken it into his head to follow the girl as well in the

[019-035] [[hope-and-optimism|hope]] of putting in a word for himself. Yet such apparently had been the

[019-036] case. Well, this had definitely torn it. Two loving hearts were united

[019-037] again in complete reconciliation, but a fat lot of good that was. It

[019-038] would be days before the misguided Looney Biddle would be able to pitch

[019-039] with a hand like that. It looked like a ham already, and was still

[019-040] swelling. Probably the wrist was sprained. For at least a week the

[019-041] greatest left-handed pitcher of his time would be about as much use to

[019-042] [[new-york-giants|the Giants]] in any professional capacity as a cold in the head. And on

[019-043] that crippled hand depended the fate of all the [[wealth-and-money|money]] Archie had in the

[019-044] world. He wished now that he had not thwarted the fellow’s simple

[019-045] enthusiasm. To have had his head knocked forcibly through a brick wall

[019-046] would not have been pleasant, but the ultimate outcome would not have

[019-047] been as unpleasant as this. With a heavy heart Archie prepared to

[019-048] withdraw, to be alone with his sorrow.

[019-049] At this moment, however, the Girl Friend, releasing her wounded lover,

[019-050] made a sudden dash for him, with the plainest intention of blotting him

[019-051] from the earth.

[019-052] “No, I say! Really!” said Archie, bounding backwards. “I mean to say!”

[019-053] In a series of events, all of which had been a bit thick, this, in his

[019-054] opinion, achieved the maximum of thickness. It was the extreme ragged,

[019-055] outside edge of the limit. To brawl with a fellow-man in a public

[019-056] street had been bad, but to be brawled with by a girl—the shot was not

[019-057] on the board. Absolutely not on the board. There was only one thing to

[019-058] be done. It was dashed undignified, no doubt, for a fellow to pick up

[019-059] the old waukeesis and leg it in the face of the enemy, but there was no

[019-060] other course. Archie started to run; and, as he did so, one of the

[019-061] loafers made the mistake of gripping him by the collar of his coat.

[019-062] “I got him!” observed the loafer.

[019-063] There is a time for all things. This was essentially not the time for

[019-064] anyone of the male sex to grip the collar of Archie’s coat. If a

[019-065] syndicate of Dempsey, Carpentier, and one of the Zoo gorillas had

[019-066] endeavoured to stay his progress at that moment, they would have had

[019-067] reason to consider it a rash move. Archie wanted to be elsewhere, and

[019-068] the blood of generations of Moffams, many of whom had swung a wicked

[019-069] axe in the free-for-all mix-ups of the Middle Ages, boiled within him

[019-070] at any attempt to revise his plans. There was a good deal of the

[019-071] loafer, but it was all soft. Releasing his hold when Archie’s heel took

[019-072] him shrewdly on the shin, he received a nasty punch in what would have

[019-073] been the middle of his waistcoat if he had worn one, uttered a gurgling

[019-074] bleat like a wounded sheep, and collapsed against the wall. Archie,

[019-075] with a torn coat, rounded the corner, and sprinted down Ninth Avenue.

[019-076] The suddenness of the move gave him an initial advantage. He was

[019-077] halfway down the first block before the vanguard of the pursuit poured

[019-078] out of the side street. Continuing to travel well, he skimmed past a

[019-079] large dray which had pulled up across the road, and moved on. The noise

[019-080] of those who pursued was loud and clamorous in the rear, but the dray

[019-081] hid him momentarily from their sight, and it was this fact which led

[019-082] Archie, the old campaigner, to take his next step.

[019-083] It was perfectly obvious—he was aware of this even in the novel

[019-084] excitement of the chase—that a chappie couldn’t hoof it at twenty-five

[019-085] miles an hour indefinitely along a main thoroughfare of a great city

[019-086] without exciting remark. He must take cover. Cover! That was the

[019-087] wheeze. He looked about him for cover.

[019-088] “You want a nice suit?”

[019-089] It takes a great deal to startle your commercial New Yorker. The small

[019-090] tailor, standing in his doorway, seemed in no way surprised at the

[019-091] spectacle of Archie, whom he had seen pass at a conventional walk some

[019-092] five minutes before, returning like this at top speed. He assumed that

[019-093] Archie had suddenly remembered that he wanted to buy something.

[019-094] This was exactly what Archie had done. More than anything else in the

[019-095] world, what he wanted to do now was to get into that shop and have a

[019-096] long talk about gents’ clothing. Pulling himself up abruptly, he shot

[019-097] past the small tailor into the dim interior. A confused aroma of cheap

[019-098] clothing greeted him. Except for a small oasis behind a grubby counter,

[019-099] practically all the available space was occupied by suits. Stiff suits,

[019-100] looking like the body when discovered by the police, hung from hooks.

[019-101] Limp suits, with the [[appearance-and-first-impressions|appearance]] of having swooned from exhaustion, lay

[019-102] about on chairs and boxes. The place was a cloth morgue, a Sargasso Sea

[019-103] of serge.

[019-104] Archie would not have had it otherwise. In these quiet groves of

[019-105] clothing a regiment could have lain hid.

[019-106] “Something nifty in tweeds?” enquired the business-like proprietor of

[019-107] this haven, following him amiably into the shop, “Or, maybe, yes, a

[019-108] nice serge? Say, mister, I got a sweet thing in blue serge that’ll fit

[019-109] you like the paper on the wall!”

[019-110] Archie wanted to talk about clothes, but not yet.

[019-111] “I say, laddie,” he said, hurriedly. “Lend me your ear for half a

[019-112] jiffy!” Outside the baying of the pack had become imminent. “Stow me

[019-113] away for a moment in the undergrowth, and I’ll buy anything you want.”

[019-114] He withdrew into the jungle. The noise outside grew in volume. The

[019-115] pursuit had been delayed for a priceless few instants by the arrival of

[019-116] another dray, moving northwards, which had drawn level with the first

[019-117] dray and dexterously bottled up the fairway. This obstacle had now been

[019-118] overcome, and the original searchers, their ranks swelled by a few

[019-119] dozen more of the leisured classes, were hot on the trail again.

[019-120] “You done a murder?” enquired the voice of the proprietor, mildly

[019-121] interested, filtering through a wall of cloth. “Well, boys will be

[019-122] boys!” he said, philosophically. “See anything there that you like?

[019-123] There some sweet things there!”

[019-124] “I’m inspecting them narrowly,” replied Archie. “If you don’t let those

[019-125] chappies find me, I shouldn’t be surprised if I bought one.”

[019-126] “One?” said the proprietor, with a touch of austerity.

[019-127] “Two,” said Archie, quickly. “Or possibly three or six.”

[019-128] The proprietor’s cordiality returned.

[019-129] “You can’t have too many nice suits,” he said, approvingly, “not a

[019-130] young feller like you that wants to look nice. All the nice girls like

[019-131] a young feller that dresses nice. When you go out of here in a suit I

[019-132] got hanging up there at the back, the girls’ll be all over you like

[019-133] flies round a honey-pot.”

[019-134] “Would you mind,” said Archie, “would you mind, as a personal favour to

[019-135] me, old companion, not mentioning that word ‘girls’?”

[019-136] He broke off. A heavy foot had crossed the threshold of the shop.

[019-137] “Say, uncle,” said a deep voice, one of those beastly voices that only

[019-138] the most poisonous blighters have, “you seen a young feller run past

[019-139] here?”

[019-140] “Young feller?” The proprietor appeared to reflect. “Do you mean a

[019-141] young feller in blue, with a Homburg hat?”

[019-142] “That’s the duck! We lost him. Where did he go?”

[019-143] “Him! Why, he come running past, quick as he could go. I wondered what

[019-144] he was running for, a hot day like this. He went round the corner at

[019-145] the bottom of the block.”

[019-146] There was a silence.

[019-147] “Well, I guess he’s got away,” said the voice, regretfully.

[019-148] “The way he was travelling,” agreed the proprietor, “I wouldn’t be

[019-149] surprised if he was in Europe by this. You want a nice suit?”

[019-150] The other, curtly expressing a wish that the proprietor would go to

[019-151] eternal perdition and take his entire stock with him, stumped out.

[019-152] “This,” said the proprietor, tranquilly, burrowing his way to where

[019-153] Archie stood and exhibiting a saffron-coloured outrage, which appeared

[019-154] to be a poor relation of the flannel family, “would put you back fifty

[019-155] dollars. And cheap!”

[019-156] “Fifty dollars!”

[019-157] “Sixty, I said. I don’t speak always distinct.”

[019-158] Archie regarded the distressing garment with a shuddering horror. A

[019-159] young man with an educated taste in clothes, it got right in among his

[019-160] nerve centres.

[019-161] “But, honestly, old soul, I don’t want to hurt your feelings, but that

[019-162] isn’t a suit, it’s just a regrettable incident!”

[019-163] The proprietor turned to the door in a listening attitude.

[019-164] “I believe I hear that feller coming back,” he said.

[019-165] Archie gulped.

[019-166] “How about trying it on?” he said. “I’m not sure, after all, it isn’t

[019-167] fairly ripe.”

[019-168] “That’s the way to talk,” said the proprietor, cordially. “You try it

[019-169] on. You can’t judge a suit, not a real nice suit like this, by looking

[019-170] at it. You want to put it on. There!” He led the way to a dusty mirror

[019-171] at the back of the shop. “Isn’t that a bargain at seventy

[019-172] dollars?...Why, say, your mother would be proud if she could see her

[019-173] boy now!”

[019-174] A quarter of an hour later, the proprietor, lovingly kneading a little

[019-175] sheaf of currency bills, eyed with a fond look the heap of clothes

[019-176] which lay on the counter.

[019-177] “As nice a little lot as I’ve ever had in my shop!” Archie did not deny

[019-178] this. It was, he thought, probably only too true.

[019-179] “I only wish I could see you walking up [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] in them!”

[019-180] rhapsodised the proprietor. “You’ll give ’em a treat! What you going to

[019-181] do with ’em? Carry ’em under your arm?” Archie shuddered strongly.

[019-182] “Well, then, I can send ’em for you anywhere you like. It’s all the

[019-183] same to me. Where’ll I send ’em?”
