# The Personality That Wins

*The Personality That Wins* is a self-help book that promises to teach its readers the secret of personal magnetism and social confidence. In [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]], it serves as a comic prop — the kind of earnest, formulaic advice manual whose lessons, when applied, produce results that are anything but winning.

## Overview

The book is introduced in Chapter XXII, where [[archie-moffam|Archie]] purchases it at a New York bookstore for the sum of two dollars — and considerable embarrassment:

> _The Personality That Wins_ cost Archie two dollars in cash and a lot of embarrassment when he asked for it at the store. To buy a treatise of that name would automatically seem to argue that you haven't a winning personality already, and Archie was at some pains to explain to the girl behind the counter that he wanted it for a friend. (022-075–022-079)

The "friend" in question is his brother-in-law [[bill-brewster]], whom Archie is attempting to coach for a confrontation with [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]]. The very title of the book becomes a running joke: anyone seen acting stiffly, nervously, or with exaggerated confidence is presumed to have been reading it.

## In the Novel

The book's influence surfaces at three key moments:

1. **The Purchase (Chapter XXII)** — [[archie-moffam|Archie]], ever the improvisational problem-solver, acquires the book as a coaching aid. His mortification at the counter — having to explain he is buying it for someone else — is pure [[archie-moffam|Archie]]: well-intentioned, deeply awkward, and entirely self-aware.

2. **Bill's Transformation (Chapter XXIV)** — When [[bill-brewster]] enters "like a cat on hot bricks" and stands frozen with nervousness, [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] delivers the sardonic verdict: "that was just the Personality That Wins!" (024-011–024-012). The joke is that Bill's "winning personality" manifests as visible terror.

3. **Reggie's Metamorphosis (Chapter XXV)** — [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie van Tuyl]], normally "a somnolent slouch," appears bounding with energy, chest expanded and smile bright — "for all the world as if he had been reading the hints in _The Personality That Wins_" (025-184–025-185). The narrator uses the book as shorthand for any sudden, suspicious improvement in demeanor.

## Associated Characters

- **[[archie-moffam]]** — Purchases the book and deploys its teachings (PN 022-075)
- **[[bill-brewster]]** — The intended beneficiary, whose attempts at a winning personality produce comically rigid results (PN 024-011)
- **[[lucille-brewster]]** — Delivers the novel's sharpest verdict on the book's effectiveness (PN 024-012)
- **[[reggie-van-tuyl]]** — His uncharacteristic animation is likened to someone who has absorbed the book's lessons (PN 025-184)
