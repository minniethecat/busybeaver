# Miss Silverton's Pistol

A small automatic pistol owned by Miss Silverton, the actress performing at the Hermitage resort. The weapon features prominently in a farcical confrontation between Miss Silverton and the theatre manager Benham. It is one of Wodehouse's finest comic props — an object that promises violence and delivers only tears.

## Description

Miss Silverton's pistol is a compact automatic firearm, small enough for the actress to carry and brandish. Its exact make is not specified in the text, but its size and Miss Silverton's admitted lack of skill with it suggest a small-calibre weapon. The pistol is notable less for its physical attributes than for the comedic threat it represents — and for how quickly that threat evaporates.

The weapon's anonymity is itself a comic choice. In a genre novel (detective fiction, thriller) the make and calibre of a pistol would be specified with forensic precision. Wodehouse gives us none of that: the pistol is simply "the pistol," a generic stage property whose vagueness underlines its purely theatrical function. It is not a weapon; it is a prop that looks like one.

## Role in the Plot

The pistol appears during a heated exchange at the Hermitage resort theatre. When Benham, the theatre manager, offends Miss Silverton by mistreating her bulldog Percy during a rehearsal, she produces the pistol in a dramatic gesture of indignation. Raising the weapon, she confronts Benham with theatrical menace — a gesture drawn straight from the melodramatic repertoire she performs on stage.

> "she said, raising the pistol. 'You stay right—'" (016-070)

The confrontation quickly deflates into farce. Miss Silverton, by her own admission, is terrified of the weapon:

> "Miss Silverton lowered the pistol, sank into a chair, and burst into tears." (016-085)

> "You think, just because I'm no champion with a pistol, I'm—" (016-093)

The sequence from menace to tears follows a precise comic arc. Miss Silverton raises the pistol — the room holds its breath — and then she lowers it, sits down, and cries. The pistol, which a moment earlier symbolized power and righteous fury, becomes merely a heavy object in the hand of a frightened woman. Her admission that "the bang would send me into a fit" completes the deflation: the weapon that was to punish Benham turns out to be more terrifying to its wielder than to its target.

The pistol thus serves as a classic Wodehouse prop — an instrument of mock-menace that collapses into absurdity, revealing the gap between the character's theatrical posturing and her genuine timidity.

- **[[george-benham]]** — theatre manager at the Hermitage; the target of Miss Silverton's pistol-waving. His mistreatment of Percy the bulldog — the casus belli — reveals his own comic blindness to the forces he is provoking. (016-070)
- **[[percy-the-bulldog]]** — Miss Silverton's bulldog, whose mistreatment by Benham triggers the pistol incident. Percy is the unwitting prime mover of the entire sequence: a dog who, by simply being a dog, sets in motion a chain of events culminating in an armed confrontation. (chapter 016)

## Comic Significance

Wodehouse deploys the pistol as an instrument of comic reversal: the weapon that should convey power instead exposes vulnerability. Miss Silverton's admission that "the bang would send me into a fit" transforms a potentially threatening situation into pure farce, exemplifying the author's technique of deflating melodrama through self-revelation.

The pistol scene also functions as a commentary on the gap between theatrical and real emotion. Miss Silverton, an actress by profession, reaches for the most theatrical response available — brandishing a weapon — and discovers that real-life melodrama is considerably harder to sustain than the stage variety. On stage, the pistol would fire (or not) according to the script; in the Hermitage, it fires not at all, and its wielder dissolves into tears.

The scene's structure — threat, deflation, tears, reconciliation — is essentially the structure of farce itself. The pistol is the mechanism that enables this arc, a physical token of the aggression that the genre requires and then systematically dismantles.
