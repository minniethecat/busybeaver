## Section 29

[029-001] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] took his tobacco and walked pensively back to the hotel. He was

[029-002] fond of Jno. Blake, and grieved for the trouble that had come upon him.

[029-003] It was odd, he felt, how things seemed to link themselves up together.

[029-004] The woman who had delivered the fateful lecture to injudicious eaters

[029-005] could not be other than the mother of his young guest of last night. An

[029-006] uncomfortable woman! Not content with starving her own family—[[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]]

[029-007] stopped in his tracks. A pedestrian, walking behind him, charged into

[029-008] his back, but Archie paid no attention. He had had one of those sudden,

[029-009] luminous ideas, which help a man who does not do much thinking as a

[029-010] rule to restore his average. He stood there for a moment, almost dizzy

[029-011] at the brilliance of his thoughts; then hurried on. Napoleon, he mused

[029-012] as he walked, must have felt rather like this after thinking up a hot

[029-013] one to spring on the enemy.

[029-014] As if Destiny were suiting her plans to his, one of the first persons

[029-015] he saw as he entered the lobby of [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] was the long boy. He

[029-016] was standing at the bookstall, reading as much of a morning paper as

[029-017] could be read free under the vigilant eyes of the presiding girl. Both

[029-018] he and she were observing the unwritten rules which govern these

[029-019] affairs—to wit, that you may read without interference as much as can

[029-020] be read without touching the paper. If you touch the paper, you lose,

[029-021] and have to buy.

[029-022] “Well, well, well!” said Archie. “Here we are again, what!” He prodded

[029-023] the boy amiably in the lower ribs. “You’re just the chap I was looking

[029-024] for. Got anything on for the time being?”

[029-025] The boy said he had no engagements.

[029-026] “Then I want you to stagger round with me to a chappie I know on Sixth

[029-027] Avenue. It’s only a couple of blocks away. I think I can do you a bit

[029-028] of good. Put you on to something tolerably ripe, if you know what I

[029-029] mean. Trickle along, laddie. You don’t need a hat.”

[029-030] They found Mr. Blake brooding over his troubles in an empty shop.

[029-031] “Cheer up, old thing!” said Archie. “The relief expedition has

[029-032] arrived.” He directed his companion’s gaze to the poster. “Cast your

[029-033] eye over that. How does that strike you?”

[029-034] The long boy scanned the poster. A gleam appeared in his rather dull

[029-035] eye.

[029-036] “Well?”

[029-037] “Some people have all the luck!” said the long boy, feelingly.

[029-038] “Would you like to compete, what?”

[029-039] The boy smiled a sad smile.

[029-040] “Would I! Would I! Say!...”

[029-041] “I know,” interrupted Archie. “Wake you up in the night and ask you! I

[029-042] knew I could rely on you, old thing.” He turned to Mr. Blake. “Here’s

[029-043] the fellow you’ve been wanting to meet. The finest left-and-right-hand

[029-044] eater east of the Rockies! He’ll fight the good fight for you.”

[029-045] Mr. Blake’s English training had not been wholly overcome by residence

[029-046] in [[new-york-city|New York]]. He still retained a nice eye for the distinctions of

[029-047] class.

[029-048] “But this young [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]’s a young gentleman,” he urged, doubtfully,

[029-049] yet with [[hope-and-optimism|hope]] shining in his eye. “He wouldn’t do it.”

[029-050] “Of course, he would. Don’t be ridic, old thing.”

[029-051] “Wouldn’t do what?” asked the boy.

[029-052] “Why save the old homestead by taking on the champion. Dashed sad case,

[029-053] between ourselves! This poor egg’s nominee has given him the raspberry

[029-054] at the eleventh hour, and only you can save him. And you owe it to him

[029-055] to do something you know, because it was your jolly old mater’s lecture

[029-056] last night that made the nominee quit. You must charge in and take his

[029-057] place. Sort of poetic justice, don’t you know, and what not!” He turned

[029-058] to Mr. Blake. “When is the conflict supposed to start? Two-thirty? You

[029-059] haven’t any important engagement for two-thirty, have you?”

[029-060] “No. Mother’s lunching at some ladies’ club, and giving a lecture

[029-061] afterwards. I can slip away.”

[029-062] Archie patted his head.

[029-063] “Then leg it where glory waits you, [[old-bean|old bean]]!”

[029-064] The long boy was gazing earnestly at the poster. It seemed to fascinate

[029-065] him.

[029-066] “Pie!” he said in a hushed voice.

[029-067] The word was like a battle-cry.

[029-068] CHAPTER XXII.

[029-069] WASHY STEPS INTO THE HALL OF FAME

[029-070] At about nine o’clock next morning, in a suite at the [[hotel-cosmopolis|Hotel Cosmopolis]],

[029-071] [[mrs-mccall|Mrs. Cora Bates McCall]], the eminent lecturer on Rational Eating, was

[029-072] seated at [[dining-and-social-ritual|breakfast]] with her family. Before her sat [[mr-mccall|Mr. McCall]], a

[029-073] little hunted-looking man, the natural peculiarities of whose face were

[029-074] accentuated by a pair of glasses of semicircular shape, like half-moons

[029-075] with the horns turned up. Behind these, Mr. McCall’s eyes played a

[029-076] perpetual game of peekaboo, now peering over them, anon ducking down

[029-077] and hiding behind them. He was sipping a cup of anti-caffeine. On his

[029-078] right, toying listlessly with a plateful of cereal, sat his son,

[029-079] Washington. Mrs. McCall herself was eating a slice of Health Bread and

[029-080] nut butter. For she practised as well as preached the doctrines which

[029-081] she had striven for so many years to inculcate in an unthinking

[029-082] populace. Her day always began with a light but nutritious breakfast,

[029-083] at which a peculiarly uninviting cereal, which looked and tasted like

[029-084] an old straw hat that had been run through a meat chopper, competed for

[029-085] first place in the dislike of her husband and son with a more than

[029-086] usually offensive brand of imitation coffee. Mr. McCall was inclined to

[029-087] think that he loathed the imitation coffee rather more than the cereal,

[029-088] but Washington held strong views on the latter’s superior ghastliness.

[029-089] Both Washington and his father, however, would have been fair-minded

[029-090] enough to admit that it was a close thing.

[029-091] Mrs. McCall regarded her offspring with grave approval.

[029-092] “I am glad to see, Lindsay,” she said to her husband, whose eyes sprang

[029-093] dutifully over the glass fence as he heard his name, “that [[washburn-mccall|Washy]] has

[029-094] recovered his appetite. When he refused his dinner last night, I was

[029-095] afraid that he might be sickening for something. Especially as he had

[029-096] quite a flushed look. You noticed his flushed look?”

[029-097] “He did look flushed.”

[029-098] “Very flushed. And his breathing was almost stertorous. And, when he

[029-099] said that he had no appetite, I am bound to say that I was anxious. But

[029-100] he is evidently perfectly well this morning. You do feel perfectly well

[029-101] this morning, Washy?”

[029-102] The heir of the McCall’s looked up from his cereal. He was a long, thin

[029-103] boy of about sixteen, with pale red hair, sandy eyelashes, and a long

[029-104] neck.

[029-105] “Uh-huh,” he said.

[029-106] Mrs. McCall nodded.

[029-107] “Surely now you will agree, Lindsay, that a careful and rational diet

[029-108] is what a boy needs? Washy’s constitution is superb. He has a

[029-109] remarkable stamina, and I attribute it entirely to my careful

[029-110] supervision of his food. I shudder when I think of the growing boys who

[029-111] are permitted by irresponsible people to devour meat, candy, pie—” She

[029-112] broke off. “What is the matter, Washy?”

[029-113] It seemed that the habit of shuddering at the thought of pie ran in the

[029-114] McCall family, for at the mention of the word a kind of internal shimmy

[029-115] had convulsed Washington’s lean frame, and over his face there had come

[029-116] an expression that was almost one of pain. He had been reaching out his

[029-117] hand for a slice of Health Bread, but now he withdrew it rather

[029-118] hurriedly and sat back breathing hard.

[029-119] “I’m all right,” he said, huskily.

[029-120] “Pie,” proceeded Mrs. McCall, in her platform voice. She stopped again

[029-121] abruptly. “Whatever is the matter, Washington? You are making me feel

[029-122] nervous.”

[029-123] “I’m all right.”

[029-124] Mrs. McCall had lost the thread of her remarks. Moreover, having now

[029-125] finished her breakfast, she was inclined for a little light reading.

[029-126] One of the subjects allied to the matter of dietary on which she felt

[029-127] deeply was the question of reading at meals. She was of the opinion

[029-128] that the strain on the eye, coinciding with the strain on the

[029-129] digestion, could not fail to give the latter the short end of the

[029-130] contest; and it was a rule at her table that the morning paper should

[029-131] not even be glanced at till the conclusion of the meal. She said that

[029-132] it was upsetting to begin the day by reading the paper, and events were

[029-133] to prove that she was occasionally right.

[029-134] All through breakfast the _New York Chronicle_ had been lying neatly

[029-135] folded beside her plate. She now opened it, and, with a remark about

[029-136] looking for the report of her yesterday’s lecture at the Butterfly

[029-137] Club, directed her gaze at the front page, on which she hoped that an

[029-138] editor with the best interests of the public at heart had decided to

[029-139] place her.

[029-140] Mr. McCall, jumping up and down behind his glasses, scrutinised her

[029-141] face closely as she began to read. He always did this on these

[029-142] occasions, for none knew better than he that his comfort for the day

[029-143] depended largely on some unknown reporter whom he had never met. If

[029-144] this unseen individual had done his work properly and as befitted the

[029-145] importance of his subject, Mrs. McCall’s mood for the next twelve hours

[029-146] would be as uniformly sunny as it was possible for it to be. But

[029-147] sometimes the fellows scamped their job disgracefully; and once, on a

[029-148] day which lived in Mr. McCall’s memory, they had failed to make a

[029-149] report at all.

[029-150] To-day, he noted with relief, all seemed to be well. The report

[029-151] actually was on the front page, an honour rarely accorded to his wife’s

[029-152] utterances. Moreover, judging from the time it took her to read the

[029-153] thing, she had evidently been reported at length.

[029-154] “Good, my dear?” he ventured. “Satisfactory?”

[029-155] “Eh?” Mrs. McCall smiled meditatively. “Oh, yes, excellent. They have

[029-156] used my photograph, too. Not at all badly reproduced.”

[029-157] “Splendid!” said Mr. McCall.

[029-158] Mrs. McCall gave a sharp shriek, and the paper fluttered from her hand.

[029-159] “My dear!” said Mr. McCall, with concern.

[029-160] His wife had recovered the paper, and was reading with burning eyes. A

[029-161] bright wave of colour had flowed over her masterful features. She was

[029-162] breathing as stertorously as ever her son Washington had done on the

[029-163] previous night.

[029-164] “Washington!”

[029-165] A basilisk glare shot across the table and turned the long boy to

[029-166] stone—all except his mouth, which opened feebly.

[029-167] “Washington! Is this true?”

[029-168] Washy closed his mouth, then let it slowly open again.

[029-169] “My dear!” Mr. McCall’s voice was alarmed. “What is it?” His eyes had

[029-170] climbed up over his glasses and remained there. “What is the matter? Is

[029-171] anything wrong?”

[029-172] “Wrong! Read for yourself!”

[029-173] Mr. McCall was completely mystified. He could not even formulate a

[029-174] guess at the cause of the trouble. That it appeared to concern his son

[029-175] Washington seemed to be the one solid fact at his disposal, and that

[029-176] only made the matter still more puzzling. Where, Mr. McCall asked

[029-177] himself, did Washington come in?

[029-178] He looked at the paper, and received immediate enlightenment. Headlines

[029-179] met his eyes:

[029-180] GOOD STUFF IN THIS BOY.

[029-181] ABOUT A TON OF IT.

[029-182] SON OF CORA BATES McCALL

[029-183] FAMOUS FOOD-REFORM LECTURER

[029-184] WINS PIE-EATING CHAMPIONSHIP OF WEST SIDE.

[029-185] There followed a lyrical outburst. So uplifted had the reporter

[029-186] evidently felt by the importance of his news that he had been unable to

[029-187] confine himself to prose:—

[029-188] My children, if you fail to shine or triumph in your special line; if,

[029-189] let us say, your hopes are bent on some day being President, and folks

[029-190] ignore your proper worth, and say you’ve not a chance on earth—Cheer

[029-191] up! for in these stirring days Fame may be won in many ways. Consider,

[029-192] when your spirits fall, the case of Washington McCall.

[029-193] Yes, cast your eye on Washy, please! He [[appearance-and-first-impressions|looks]] just like a piece of

[029-194] cheese: he’s not a brilliant sort of chap: he has a dull and vacant

[029-195] map: his eyes are blank, his face is red, his ears stick out beside his

[029-196] head. In fact, to end these compliments, he would be dear at thirty

[029-197] cents. Yet Fame has welcomed to her Hall this self-same Washington

[029-198] McCall.
