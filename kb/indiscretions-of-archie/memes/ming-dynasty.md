# Ming Dynasty

The **Ming Dynasty** (1368–1644) is referenced in *Indiscretions of Archie* in connection with a valuable Chinese porcelain figurine that becomes central to the novel's plot. The reference serves to establish the exceptional rarity and value of the object, which is described as dating back to this celebrated period of Chinese ceramic art.

## In the Novel

The Ming Dynasty is invoked during a negotiation over a china figure. A dealer, presenting the piece, emphasizes its provenance with deliberate gravity:

> "This," he said, with a shake in his voice, "is something very special. China figure, said to date back to the Ming Dynasty. Unique. Nothing like it on either side of the Atlantic." (011-111–113)

The reference draws on the widely recognized association between the Ming Dynasty and the pinnacle of Chinese porcelain craftsmanship. By attributing the figurine to this period, the dealer frames it as an irreplaceable treasure — an assertion that underpins the subsequent negotiations, theft, and recovery that form a major subplot of the novel.

The Ming reference recurs when the figurine's disappearance is lamented: the loss of a "priceless china figure of the Ming Dynasty" (012-003) is framed not merely as a financial setback but as a cultural calamity — the "priceless" modifier elevating the theft from petty crime to minor tragedy.

## Historical Significance

The Ming Dynasty is historically renowned for its ceramic production, particularly the blue-and-white porcelain that reached its technical and artistic zenith during this era. Ming porcelain was already highly prized by Western collectors at the time of the novel's setting (early twentieth century), making the reference both period-appropriate and culturally resonant for Wodehouse's contemporary readership.

## Why Ming?

Wodehouse's choice of the Ming Dynasty for the figurine's provenance is neither accidental nor arbitrary. By the 1920s, "Ming" had become, in the Western imagination, a byword for Chinese artistic excellence — a single syllable that could confer instant value and authenticity upon any piece of Chinese porcelain. The dealer's appeal to Ming provenance in the novel would have needed no explanation for contemporary readers; it functioned much as "Stradivarius" does for violins or "Tiffany" for glassware — a proper noun that doubles as a certificate of quality.

The Ming attribution also serves a narrative purpose. A "priceless" Ming figurine raises the stakes of the theft subplot far beyond what a merely "valuable" object would achieve. The word "priceless" (012-003) is carefully chosen: it suggests not only high monetary value but uniqueness, irreplaceability — the kind of object whose loss cannot be compensated by insurance money. This escalation transforms the figurine's disappearance from a property crime into something approaching sacrilege, and its recovery into a matter of moral as well as financial urgency.

The dealer's performance — "with a shake in his voice" (011-111) — is a miniature set-piece of salesmanship. The trembling voice, the strategic deployment of "Ming Dynasty," the claim of uniqueness "on either side of the Atlantic" — these are the tools of a man who understands that in the art market, provenance is half the price. Wodehouse, ever alert to the mechanics of persuasion, lets us see both the performance and its intended effect: the figurine becomes, in the space of a few sentences, not merely old but ancient, not merely rare but unique, not merely valuable but priceless.
