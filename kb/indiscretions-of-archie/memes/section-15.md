## Section 15

[015-001] CHAPTER XIII.

[015-002] RALLYING ROUND PERCY

[015-003] It amazed [[archie-moffam|Archie]] through the whole of a long afternoon to reflect how

[015-004] swiftly and unexpectedly the blue and brilliant sky of life can cloud

[015-005] over and with what abruptness a man who fancies that his feet are on

[015-006] solid ground can find himself immersed in Fate’s gumbo. He recalled,

[015-007] with the bitterness with which one does recall such things, that that

[015-008] morning he had risen from his bed without a care in the world, his

[015-009] happiness unruffled even by the thought that [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] would be leaving

[015-010] him for a short space. He had sung in his bath. Yes, he had chirruped

[015-011] like a bally linnet. And now—

[015-012] Some men would have dismissed the unfortunate affairs of Mr. George

[015-013] [[george-benham|Benham]] from their mind as having nothing to do with themselves, but

[015-014] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] had never been made of this stern stuff. The fact that Mr.

[015-015] Benham, apart from being an agreeable companion with whom he had

[015-016] lunched occasionally in [[new-york-city|New York]], had no claims upon him affected him

[015-017] little. He hated to see his fellowman in trouble. On the other hand,

[015-018] what could he do? To seek [[miss-silverton|Miss Silverton]] out and plead with her—even if

[015-019] he did it without cooing—would undoubtedly establish an intimacy

[015-020] between them which, instinct told him, might tinge her manner after

[015-021] Lucille’s return with just that suggestion of Auld Lang Syne which

[015-022] makes things so awkward.

[015-023] His whole being shrank from extending to Miss Silverton that inch which

[015-024] the female artistic temperament is so apt to turn into an ell; and

[015-025] when, just as he was about to go in to [[dining-and-social-ritual|dinner]], he met her in the lobby

[015-026] and she smiled brightly at him and informed him that her eye was now

[015-027] completely recovered, he shied away like a startled mustang of the

[015-028] prairie, and, abandoning his intention of worrying the table d’hote in

[015-029] the same room with the amiable creature, tottered off to the

[015-030] smoking-room, where he did the best he could with sandwiches and

[015-031] coffee.

[015-032] Having got through the time as best he could till eleven o’clock, he

[015-033] went up to bed.

[015-034] The room to which he and Lucille had been assigned by the management

[015-035] was on the second floor, pleasantly sunny by day and at night filled

[015-036] with cool and heartening fragrance of the pines. Hitherto Archie had

[015-037] always enjoyed taking a final smoke on the balcony overlooking the

[015-038] woods, but, to-night such was his mental stress that he prepared to go

[015-039] to bed directly he had closed the door. He turned to the cupboard to

[015-040] get his pyjamas.

[015-041] His first thought, when even after a second scrutiny no pyjamas were

[015-042] visible, was that this was merely another of those things which happen

[015-043] on days when life goes wrong. He raked the cupboard for a third time

[015-044] with an annoyed eye. From every hook hung various garments of

[015-045] Lucille’s, but no pyjamas. He was breathing a soft malediction

[015-046] preparatory to embarking on a point-to-point hunt for his missing

[015-047] property, when something in the cupboard caught his eye and held him

[015-048] for a moment puzzled.

[015-049] He could have sworn that Lucille did not possess a mauve _négligé_.

[015-050] Why, she had told him a dozen times that mauve was a colour which she

[015-051] did not like. He frowned perplexedly; and as he did so, from near the

[015-052] window came a soft cough.

[015-053] Archie spun round and subjected the room to as close a scrutiny as that

[015-054] which he had bestowed upon the cupboard. Nothing was visible. The

[015-055] window opening on to the balcony gaped wide. The balcony was manifestly

[015-056] empty.

[015-057] “_Urrf!_”

[015-058] This time there was no possibility of error. The cough had come from

[015-059] the immediate neighbourhood of the window.

[015-060] Archie was conscious of a pringly sensation about the roots of his

[015-061] closely-cropped back-hair, as he moved cautiously across the room. The

[015-062] affair was becoming uncanny; and, as he tip-toed towards the window,

[015-063] old ghost stories, read in lighter moments before cheerful fires with

[015-064] plenty of light in the room, flitted through his mind. He had the

[015-065] feeling—precisely as every chappie in those stories had had—that he was

[015-066] not alone.

[015-067] Nor was he. In a basket behind an arm-chair, curled up, with his

[015-068] massive chin resting on the edge of the wicker-work, lay a fine

[015-069] bulldog.

[015-070] “Urrf!” said the bulldog.

[015-071] “Good God!” said Archie.

[015-072] There was a lengthy pause in which the bulldog looked earnestly at

[015-073] Archie and Archie looked earnestly at the bulldog.

[015-074] Normally, Archie was a dog-lover. His hurry was never so great as to

[015-075] prevent him stopping, when in the street, and introducing himself to

[015-076] any dog he met. In a strange house, his first act was to assemble the

[015-077] canine population, roll it on its back or backs, and punch it in the

[015-078] ribs. As a boy, his earliest ambition had been to become a veterinary

[015-079] surgeon; and, though the years had cheated him of his career, he knew

[015-080] all about dogs, their points, their manners, their customs, and their

[015-081] treatment in sickness and in health. In short, he loved dogs, and, had

[015-082] they met under happier conditions, he would undoubtedly have been on

[015-083] excellent terms with this one within the space of a minute. But, as

[015-084] things were, he abstained from fraternising and continued to goggle

[015-085] dumbly.

[015-086] And then his eye, wandering aside, collided with the following objects:

[015-087] a fluffy pink dressing-gown, hung over the back of a chair, an entirely

[015-088] strange suit-case, and, on the bureau, a photograph in a silver frame

[015-089] of a stout [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] in evening-[[appearance-and-first-impressions|dress]] whom he had never seen before in

[015-090] his life.

[015-091] Much has been written of the emotions of the wanderer who, returning to

[015-092] his childhood home, finds it altered out of all recognition; but poets

[015-093] have neglected the theme—far more poignant—of the man who goes up to

[015-094] his room in an hotel and finds it full of somebody else’s

[015-095] dressing-gowns and bulldogs.

[015-096] Bulldogs! Archie’s heart jumped sideways and upwards with a wiggling

[015-097] movement, turning two somersaults, and stopped beating. The hideous

[015-098] truth, working its way slowly through the concrete, had at last

[015-099] penetrated to his brain. He was not only in somebody else’s room, and a

[015-100] woman’s at that. He was in the room belonging to Miss Vera Silverton.

[015-101] He could not understand it. He would have been prepared to stake the

[015-102] last cent he could borrow from his [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]] on the fact that he

[015-103] had made no error in the number over the door. Yet, nevertheless, such

[015-104] was the case, and, below par though his faculties were at the moment,

[015-105] he was sufficiently alert to perceive that it behoved him to withdraw.

[015-106] He leaped to the door, and, as he did so, the handle began to turn.

[015-107] The cloud which had settled on Archie’s mind lifted abruptly. For an

[015-108] instant he was enabled to think about a hundred times more quickly than

[015-109] was his leisurely wont. Good [[wealth-and-money|fortune]] had brought him to within easy

[015-110] reach of the electric-light switch. He snapped it back, and was in

[015-111] darkness. Then, diving silently and swiftly to the floor, he wriggled

[015-112] under the bed. The thud of his head against what appeared to be some

[015-113] sort of joist or support, unless it had been placed there by the maker

[015-114] as a practical joke, on the chance of this kind of thing happening some

[015-115] day, coincided with the creak of the opening door. Then the light was

[015-116] switched on again, and the bulldog in the corner gave a welcoming

[015-117] woofle.

[015-118] “And how is mamma’s precious angel?”

[015-119] Rightly concluding that the remark had not been addressed to himself

[015-120] and that no social obligation demanded that he reply, Archie pressed

[015-121] his cheek against the boards and said nothing. The question was not

[015-122] repeated, but from the other side of the room came the sound of a

[015-123] patted dog.

[015-124] “Did he think his muzzer had fallen down dead and was never coming up?”

[015-125] The beautiful picture which these words conjured up filled Archie with

[015-126] that yearning for the might-have-been which is always so painful. He

[015-127] was finding his position physically as well as mentally distressing. It

[015-128] was cramped under the bed, and the boards were harder than anything he

[015-129] had ever encountered. Also, it appeared to be the practice of the

[015-130] housemaids at the [[the-hermitage|Hotel Hermitage]] to use the space below the beds as a

[015-131] depository for all the dust which they swept off the carpet, and much

[015-132] of this was insinuating itself into his nose and mouth. The two things

[015-133] which Archie would have liked most to do at that moment were first to

[015-134] kill Miss Silverton—if possible, painfully—and then to spend the

[015-135] remainder of his life sneezing.

[015-136] After a prolonged period he heard a drawer open, and noted the fact as

[015-137] promising. As the old married man, he presumed that it signified the

[015-138] putting away of hair-pins. [[About]] now the dashed woman would be looking

[015-139] at herself in the glass with her hair down. Then she would brush it.

[015-140] Then she would twiddle it up into thingummies. Say, ten minutes for

[015-141] this. And after that she would go to bed and turn out the light, and he

[015-142] would be able, after giving her a bit of time to go to sleep, to creep

[015-143] out and leg it. Allowing at a conservative estimate three-quarters of—

[015-144] “Come out!”

[015-145] Archie stiffened. For an instant a feeble [[hope-and-optimism|hope]] came to him that this

[015-146] remark, like the others, might be addressed to the dog.

[015-147] “Come out from under that bed!” said a stern voice. “And mind how you

[015-148] come! I’ve got a pistol!”

[015-149] “Well, I mean to say, you know,” said Archie, in a propitiatory voice,

[015-150] emerging from his lair like a tortoise and smiling as winningly as a

[015-151] man can who has just bumped his head against the leg of a bed, “I

[015-152] suppose all this seems fairly rummy, but—”

[015-153] “For the love of Mike!” said Miss Silverton.

[015-154] The point seemed to Archie well taken and the comment on the situation

[015-155] neatly expressed.

[015-156] “What are you doing in my room?”

[015-157] “Well, if it comes to that, you know—shouldn’t have mentioned it if you

[015-158] hadn’t brought the subject up in the course of general chit-[[conversation-and-dialogue|chat]]—what

[015-159] are you doing in mine?”

[015-160] “Yours?”

[015-161] “Well, apparently there’s been a bloomer of some species somewhere, but

[015-162] this was the room I had last night,” said Archie.

[015-163] “But the desk-clerk said that he had asked you if it would be quite

[015-164] satisfactory to you giving it up to me, and you said yes. I come here

[015-165] every summer, when I’m not working, and I always have this room.”

[015-166] “By Jove! I remember now. The chappie did say something to me about the

[015-167] room, but I was thinking of something else and it rather went over the

[015-168] top. So that’s what he was talking about, was it?”

[015-169] Miss Silverton was frowning. A moving-picture director, scanning her

[015-170] face, would have perceived that she was registering disappointment.

[015-171] “Nothing breaks right for me in this darned world,” she said,

[015-172] regretfully. “When I caught sight of your leg sticking out from under

[015-173] the bed, I did think that everything was all lined up for a real find

[015-174] and, at last, I could close my eyes and see the thing in the papers. On

[015-175] the front page, with photographs: ‘[[plucky-actress-captures-burglar|Plucky Actress Captures Burglar]].’

[015-176] Darn it!”

[015-177] “Fearfully sorry, you know!”

[015-178] “I just needed something like that. I’ve got a Press-agent, and I will

[015-179] say for him that he eats well and sleeps well and has just enough

[015-180] intelligence to cash his monthly cheque without forgetting what he went

[015-181] into the bank for, but outside of that you can take it from me he’s not

[015-182] one of the world’s workers! He’s about as much solid use to a girl with

[015-183] aspirations as a pain in the lower ribs. It’s three weeks since he got

[015-184] me into print at all, and then the brightest thing he could think up

[015-185] was that my favourite breakfast-fruit was an apple. Well, I ask you!”

[015-186] “Rotten!” said Archie.
