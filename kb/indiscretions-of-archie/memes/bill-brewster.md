## Overview

Bill Brewster is the brother of Lucille Brewster and brother-in-law of Archie Moffam. He appears throughout the novel, most prominently in Chapter XVII ("Brother Bill's Romance"), where he is engaged to Mabel Winchester, a chorus girl. His relationship faces obstacles from the Brewster family's social expectations, and Archie becomes involved in schemes to help him.

## In the Novel

### Introduction

Bill Brewster first appears when Archie and Reggie van Tuyl encounter him. Reggie, who knows Bill's sister, introduces them — only to realize and comically "forget" that Bill is Archie's brother-in-law [012-012].

### Brother Bill's Romance

Chapter XVII is dedicated to Bill's romance. He is engaged to Mabel Winchester, a girl in the chorus, and spends much of his time rhapsodizing about her eyes: "Her eyes," said Bill Brewster, "are like—like—what's the word I want?" [021-117]. Lucille listens with eager interest while Archie, having heard this many times before, leans back with his eyes closed [021-117].

The central conflict is that Daniel Brewster, Lucille and Bill's father, would not approve of Mabel: "You know what father is. He isn't like other fathers" [017]. Bill's plan involves getting Mabel a part in a comedy, hoping this would elevate her social standing enough for his father to accept her.

### The Sausage Chappie Episode

During a luncheon where Reggie van Tuyl accidentally reveals that he too knows Mabel Winchester, Bill Brewster leans forward "with bulging eyes and soaring eyebrows" and demands, "Are you engaged to Mabel Winchester?" [026-008]. After the misunderstanding is resolved, "rendered restless by relief, Bill Brewster did not linger long at the luncheon-table" and goes for a walk to calm his "excited mind" [026-049].

### Later Appearances

Bill makes a brief, dispirited appearance near the novel's end when Mabel has gone away: "I want sympathy!" he tells Archie, "And lots of it! She's gone!" [033-156].

## Character

Bill Brewster is characterized by his romantic enthusiasm and youthful anxiety about his father's approval. He shares Lucille's struggle against Daniel Brewster's rigid expectations, though his approach is less confrontational than his sister's. His devotion to Mabel Winchester — despite her chorus-girl status — mirrors the novel's broader theme of love triumphing over social convention.
