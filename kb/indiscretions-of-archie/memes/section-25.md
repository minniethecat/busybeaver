## Section 25

[025-001] [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] beamed with relief. He felt just as he had felt on that other

[025-002] occasion at the moment when a taxi-cab had rolled up and enabled him to

[025-003] hide his spatless leg from the public gaze.

[025-004] “Oh, I see!” he said. “Why, delighted, old man, quite delighted!”

[025-005] “Any small part would do. Isn’t there a maid or something in your

[025-006] bob’s-worth of refined entertainment who drifts about saying, ‘Yes,

[025-007] madam,’ and all that sort of thing? Well, then that’s just the thing.

[025-008] Topping! I knew I could rely on you, old bird. I’ll get [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] to ship

[025-009] her round to your address when she arrives. I fancy she’s due to totter

[025-010] in somewhere in the next few days. Well, I must be popping. Toodle-oo!”

[025-011] “Pip-pip!” said Reggie.

[025-012] It was about a week later that Lucille came into the suite at the Hotel

[025-013] Cosmopolis that was her home, and found [[archie-moffam|Archie]] lying on the couch,

[025-014] smoking a refreshing pipe after the labours of the day. It seemed to

[025-015] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] that his wife was not in her usual cheerful frame of mind. He

[025-016] kissed her, and, having relieved her of her parasol, endeavoured

[025-017] without success to balance it on his chin. Having picked it up from the

[025-018] floor and placed it on the table, he became aware that Lucille was

[025-019] looking at him in a despondent sort of way. Her grey eyes were clouded.

[025-020] “[[what-ho|Halloa]], old thing,” said Archie. “What’s up?”

[025-021] Lucille sighed wearily.

[025-022] “Archie, darling, do you know any really good swear-words?”

[025-023] “Well,” said Archie, reflectively, “let me see. I did pick up a few

[025-024] tolerably ripe and breezy expressions out in France. All through my

[025-025] military career there was something about me—some subtle magnetism,

[025-026] don’t you know, and that sort of thing—that seemed to make colonels and

[025-027] blighters of that order rather inventive. I sort of inspired them,

[025-028] don’t you know. I remember one brass-hat addressing me for quite ten

[025-029] minutes, saying something new all the time. And even then he seemed to

[025-030] think he had only touched the fringe of the subject. As a matter of

[025-031] fact, he said straight out in the most frank and confiding way that

[025-032] mere words couldn’t do justice to me. But why?”

[025-033] “Because I want to relieve my feelings.”

[025-034] “Anything wrong?”

[025-035] “Everything’s wrong. I’ve just been having tea with Bill and his

[025-036] Mabel.”

[025-037] “Oh, ah!” said Archie, interested. “And what’s the verdict?”

[025-038] “Guilty!” said Lucille. “And the sentence, if I had anything to do with

[025-039] it, would be transportation for life.” She peeled off her gloves

[025-040] irritably. “What fools men are! Not you, precious! You’re the only man

[025-041] in the world that isn’t, it seems to me. You did marry a nice girl,

[025-042] didn’t you? _You_ didn’t go running round after females with crimson

[025-043] hair, goggling at them with your eyes popping out of your head like a

[025-044] bulldog waiting for a bone.”

[025-045] “Oh, I say! Does old Bill look like that?”

[025-046] “Worse!”

[025-047] Archie rose to a point of order.

[025-048] “But one moment, old lady. You speak of crimson hair. Surely old

[025-049] Bill—in the extremely jolly monologues he used to deliver whenever I

[025-050] didn’t see him coming and he got me alone—used to allude to her hair as

[025-051] brown.”

[025-052] “It isn’t brown now. It’s bright scarlet. Good gracious, I ought to

[025-053] know. I’ve been looking at it all the afternoon. It dazzled me. If I’ve

[025-054] got to meet her again, I mean to go to the oculist’s and get a pair of

[025-055] those smoked glasses you wear at Palm Beach.” Lucille brooded silently

[025-056] for a while over the tragedy. “I don’t want to say anything against

[025-057] her, of course.”

[025-058] “No, no, of course not.”

[025-059] “But of all the awful, second-rate girls I ever met, she’s the worst!

[025-060] She has vermilion hair and an imitation Oxford manner. She’s so

[025-061] horribly refined that it’s dreadful to listen to her. She’s a sly,

[025-062] creepy, slinky, made-up, insincere vampire! She’s common! She’s awful!

[025-063] She’s a cat!”

[025-064] “You’re quite right not to say anything against her,” said Archie,

[025-065] approvingly. “It begins to look,” he went on, “as if the good old pater

[025-066] was about due for another shock. He has a hard life!”

[025-067] “If Bill _dares_ to introduce that girl to father, he’s taking his life

[025-068] in his hands.”

[025-069] “But surely that was the idea—the scheme—the wheeze, wasn’t it? Or do

[025-070] you think there’s any chance of his weakening?”

[025-071] “Weakening! You should have seen him looking at her! It was like a

[025-072] small boy flattening his nose against the window of a candy-store.”

[025-073] “Bit thick!”

[025-074] Lucille kicked the leg of the table.

[025-075] “And to think,” she said, “that, when I was a little girl, I used to

[025-076] look up to Bill as a monument of wisdom. I used to hug his knees and

[025-077] gaze into his face and wonder how anyone could be so magnificent.” She

[025-078] gave the unoffending table another kick. “If I could have looked into

[025-079] the future,” she said, with feeling, “I’d have bitten him in the

[025-080] ankle!”

[025-081] In the days which followed, Archie found himself a little out of touch

[025-082] with Bill and his [[love-and-marriage|romance]]. Lucille referred to the matter only when he

[025-083] brought the subject up, and made it plain that the topic of her future

[025-084] sister-in-law was not one which she enjoyed discussing. [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]],

[025-085] senior, when Archie, by way of delicately preparing his mind for what

[025-086] was about to befall, asked him if he liked red hair, called him a fool,

[025-087] and told him to go away and bother someone else when they were busy.

[025-088] The only person who could have kept him thoroughly abreast of the trend

[025-089] of affairs was Bill himself; and experience had made Archie wary in the

[025-090] matter of meeting Bill. The position of confidant to a young man in the

[025-091] early stages of love is no sinecure, and it made Archie sleepy even to

[025-092] think of having to [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] to his brother-in-law. He sedulously avoided

[025-093] his love-lorn relative, and it was with a sinking feeling one day that,

[025-094] looking over his shoulder as he sat in [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] grill-room

[025-095] preparatory to ordering [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]], he perceived Bill bearing down upon him,

[025-096] obviously resolved upon joining his meal.

[025-097] To his surprise, however, Bill did not instantly embark upon his usual

[025-098] monologue. Indeed, he hardly spoke at all. He champed a chop, and

[025-099] seemed to Archie to avoid his eye. It was not till lunch was over and

[025-100] they were smoking that he unburdened himself.

[025-101] “Archie!” he said.

[025-102] “Hallo, old thing!” said Archie. “Still there? I thought you’d died or

[025-103] something. Talk about our old [[friendship|pals]], Tongue-tied Thomas and Silent

[025-104] Sammy! You could beat ’em both on the same evening.”

[025-105] “It’s enough to make me silent.”

[025-106] “What is?”

[025-107] Bill had relapsed into a sort of waking dream. He sat frowning

[025-108] sombrely, lost to the world. Archie, having waited what seemed to him a

[025-109] sufficient length of time for an answer to his question, bent forward

[025-110] and touched his brother-in-law’s hand gently with the lighted end of

[025-111] his cigar. Bill came to himself with a howl.

[025-112] “What is?” said Archie.

[025-113] “What is what?” said Bill.

[025-114] “Now listen, old thing,” protested Archie. “Life is short and time is

[025-115] flying. Suppose we cut out the cross-talk. You hinted there was

[025-116] something on your mind—something worrying the [[old-bean|old bean]]—and I’m waiting

[025-117] to hear what it is.”

[025-118] Bill fiddled a moment with his coffee-spoon.

[025-119] “I’m in an awful hole,” he said at last.

[025-120] “What’s the trouble?”

[025-121] “It’s about that darned girl!”

[025-122] Archie blinked.

[025-123] “What!”

[025-124] “That darned girl!”

[025-125] Archie could scarcely credit his senses. He had been prepared—indeed,

[025-126] he had steeled himself—to hear Bill allude to his affinity in a number

[025-127] of ways. But “that darned girl” was not one of them.

[025-128] “Companion of my riper years,” he said, “let’s get this thing straight.

[025-129] When you say ‘that darned girl,’ do you by any possibility allude to—?”

[025-130] “Of course I do!”

[025-131] “But, William, old bird—”

[025-132] “Oh, I know, I know, I know!” said Bill, irritably. “You’re surprised

[025-133] to hear me talk like that about her?”

[025-134] “A trifle, yes. Possibly a trifle. When last heard from, laddie, you

[025-135] must recollect, you were speaking of the lady as your soul-mate, and at

[025-136] least once—if I remember rightly—you alluded to her as your little

[025-137] dusky-haired lamb.”

[025-138] A sharp howl escaped Bill.

[025-139] “Don’t!” A strong shudder convulsed his frame. “Don’t remind me of it!”

[025-140] “There’s been a species of slump, then, in dusky-haired lambs?”

[025-141] “How,” demanded Bill, savagely, “can a girl be a dusky-haired lamb when

[025-142] her hair’s bright scarlet?”

[025-143] “Dashed difficult!” admitted Archie.

[025-144] “I suppose Lucille told you about that?”

[025-145] “She did touch on it. Lightly, as it were. With a sort of gossamer

[025-146] touch, so to speak.”

[025-147] Bill threw off the last fragments of reserve.

[025-148] “Archie, I’m in the devil of a fix. I don’t know why it was, but

[025-149] directly I saw her—things seemed so different over in [[england|England]]—I mean.”

[025-150] He swallowed ice-water in gulps. “I suppose it was seeing her with

[025-151] Lucille. Old Lu is such a thoroughbred. Seemed to kind of show her up.

[025-152] Like seeing imitation pearls by the side of real pearls. And that

[025-153] crimson hair! It sort of put the lid on it.” Bill brooded morosely. “It

[025-154] ought to be a criminal offence for women to dye their hair. Especially

[025-155] red. What the devil do women do that sort of thing for?”

[025-156] “Don’t blame me, old thing. It’s not my fault.”

[025-157] Bill looked furtive and harassed.

[025-158] “It makes me feel such a cad. Here am I, feeling that I would give all

[025-159] I’ve got in the world to get out of the darned thing, and all the time

[025-160] the poor girl seems to be getting fonder of me than ever.”

[025-161] “How do you know?” Archie surveyed his brother-in-law critically.

[025-162] “Perhaps her feelings have changed too. Very possibly she may not like

[025-163] the colour of _your_ hair. I don’t myself. Now if you were to dye

[025-164] yourself crimson—”

[025-165] “Oh, shut up! Of course a man knows when a girl’s fond of him.”

[025-166] “By no means, laddie. When you’re my age—”

[025-167] “I _am_ your age.”

[025-168] “So you are! I forgot that. Well, now, approaching the matter from

[025-169] another angle, let us suppose, old son, that Miss What’s-Her-Name—the

[025-170] party of the second part—”

[025-171] “Stop it!” said Bill suddenly. “Here comes Reggie!”

[025-172] “Eh?”

[025-173] “Here comes Reggie van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]]. I don’t want him to hear us talking about

[025-174] the darned thing.”

[025-175] Archie looked over his shoulder and perceived that it was indeed so.

[025-176] Reggie was threading his way among the tables.

[025-177] “Well, _he_ [[appearance-and-first-impressions|looks]] pleased with things, anyway,” said Bill, enviously.

[025-178] “Glad somebody’s happy.”

[025-179] He was right. Reggie van Tuyl’s usual mode of progress through a

[025-180] restaurant was a somnolent slouch. Now he was positively bounding

[025-181] along. Furthermore, the usual expression on Reggie’s face was a sleepy

[025-182] sadness. Now he smiled brightly and with animation. He curveted towards

[025-183] their table, beaming and erect, his head up, his gaze level, and his

[025-184] chest expanded, for all the world as if he had been reading the hints

[025-185] in _The Personality That Wins_.

[025-186] Archie was puzzled. Something had plainly happened to Reggie. But what?

[025-187] It was idle to suppose that somebody had left him [[wealth-and-money|money]], for he had

[025-188] been left practically all the money there was a matter of ten years

[025-189] before.

[025-190] “Hallo, old bean,” he said, as the new-comer, radiating good will and

[025-191] bonhomie, arrived at the table and hung over it like a noon-day sun.

[025-192] “We’ve finished. But rally round and we’ll watch you eat. Dashed

[025-193] interesting, watching old Reggie eat. Why go to the Zoo?”

[025-194] Reggie shook his head.

[025-195] “Sorry, old man. Can’t. Just on my way to the Ritz. Stepped in because

[025-196] I thought you might be here. I wanted you to be the first to hear the

[025-197] news.”

[025-198] “News?”

[025-199] “I’m the happiest man alive!”

[025-200] “You look it, darn you!” growled Bill, on whose mood of grey gloom this

[025-201] human sunbeam was jarring heavily.

[025-202] “I’m engaged to be married!”

[025-203] “Congratulations, old egg!” Archie shook his hand cordially. “Dash it,

[025-204] don’t you know, as an old married man I like to see you young fellows

[025-205] settling down.”
