# 1921

The year in which *Indiscretions of Archie* was published — and the approximate present of the novel's narrative, set in a Prohibition-era New York that was simultaneously modern and in transition.

## Overview

Nineteen twenty-one placed Wodehouse's novel at the threshold of the Jazz Age. The Great War was three years in the past; Prohibition, enacted in 1920, was in its second year; and American culture was undergoing the rapid transformation that would define the decade. *Indiscretions of Archie* captures this moment with Wodehouse's characteristic lightness of touch — the novel is not a historical document, but it is saturated with the atmosphere of its time.

## In the Novel

The novel's copyright page establishes the date precisely: "COPYRIGHT, 1921, BY GEORGE H. DORAN COMPANY" (001-044). The novel's events unfold in what contemporary readers would have recognized as the immediate present — the world of [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|grand New York hotels]], [[new-york-giants|professional baseball]], [[art-and-collecting|the art market]], and the social rituals of the American wealthy class in the early 1920s.

Prohibition, though never a major plot element, forms part of the novel's background. Archie's world is one of hotel bars that serve something called "ginger ale" and a general air of circumvention that Wodehouse treats with his usual comic lightness. The cultural clash between Archie's Edwardian Englishness and America's roaring twenties modernity provides a steady undertone of comic tension.

## Historical Context

The year 1921 saw several developments relevant to the novel's world:

- **Prohibition** was the law of the land, reshaping American social life and creating the speakeasy culture that would define the decade
- **Professional baseball**, recovering from the Black Sox scandal of 1919, was entering a new era under Commissioner Kenesaw Mountain Landis — the [[new-york-giants]] would win the World Series in 1921
- **The art market** was booming, with American industrialists like [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]] building collections that would eventually form the cores of major museums
- **Transatlantic travel** was resuming after the disruptions of World War I, bringing waves of English visitors (like Archie) to American shores
