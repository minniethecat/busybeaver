# Dash It

> "But I wanted them cleaned, dash it!" (001-132)

## The Quote

"Dash it" (and its intensified variant "dash it all") is [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]'s go-to exclamation — a mild, characteristically British expression of frustration, annoyance, or indignation. It appears throughout [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]] whenever [[archie-moffam|Archie]] encounters the incomprehensible customs, unreasonable demands, or general absurdities of American life. As with other [[archie-moffam|Archie]]-isms, "dash it" marks [[archie-moffam|Archie]] as an English outsider — a [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]ly protest that never quite rises to genuine anger.

Key occurrences:
- "But I wanted them cleaned, dash it!" — complaining about [[hotel-cosmopolis|Hotel Cosmopolis]]'s shoe-shining policy (001-132)
- "Dash it all, he didn't see what else he could" — a moment of sudden realization or dismay (014-017)
- "Why, dash it!" — upon introducing Bill [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]] to a friend (012-012)
- "Fourteen, dash it!" — during the auction bidding war (012-068)
- "But, dash it—I mean—what I mean to say—I'm…" — flustered and cornered (016-046)
- "Well, dash it all, I don't…" — expressing moral outrage (020-120)
- "But, dash it, old scream, if she's going to…" — trying to reason with an unreasonable situation (022-067)
- "So, dash it, I must look…" — resigned determination (023-093)

## Context

"Dash it" is one of the most frequently recurring catchphrases in *[[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Indiscretions of Archie]]*, appearing at least ten times across [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]] (001-132, 014-017, 012-012, 012-042, 012-068, 014-043, 016-046, 020-120, 022-067, 023-093). The phrase is always spoken by [[archie-moffam|Archie]] and serves as a verbal punctuation mark for moments of comic exasperation. Its first [[appearance-and-first-impressions|appearance]] — [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s protest about his unpolished boots at the [[hotel-cosmopolis|Hotel Cosmopolis]] — immediately establishes the character's English sensibility clashing with American hotel customs (001-132).

[[archie-moffam|Archie]] uses "dash it" in the auction scene when the bidding for the china warrior figurine escalates beyond his expectations — "Fourteen, dash it!" (012-068) — capturing the blend of competitiveness and [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]ly restraint that defines his character. When cornered by [[miss-silverton|Miss Silverton]] with her pistol, his flustered "But, dash it—I mean—what I mean to say—I'm…" (016-046) reveals his fundamental decency even under duress by an armed actress.

Unlike his other catchphrases ("What ho!", "Old bean"), "dash it" carries a note of genuine feeling — [[archie-moffam|Archie]] is truly exasperated, not merely cheerful. It is the verbal tic that reveals [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s limits: when the world's absurdity exceeds even his considerable tolerance, "dash it" is the safety valve.

## Speaker

- **[[archie-moffam]]** — The protagonist's English public-school background supplies the vocabulary of understated protest. "Dash it" is the verbal equivalent of [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s perpetual mild bewilderment — never crude, never truly angry, always decent. It is the exclamation of a man who expects reasonable behavior from the world and is perpetually surprised when it fails to deliver.

## Significance

"Dash it" belongs to the distinctive linguistic palette that P.G. [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]] uses to define [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s Englishness against his American surroundings. Where an American character might say "damn it" or worse, [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s "dash it" signals class, nationality, and temperament in two syllables. It is the catchphrase of a specific social type — the well-bred young Englishman abroad, disarmed by American informality but maintaining his linguistic decorum even in extremis.

The phrase's comic effect derives from the gap between [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s mild language and the genuinely frustrating situations he faces — financial ruin, [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]] fury, armed confrontation. That his strongest exclamation is "dash it" is both the joke and the character study.
