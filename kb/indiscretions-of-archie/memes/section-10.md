## Section 10

[010-001] “I know. But, light of my soul, have you any idea how dashed difficult

[010-002] it is to get beaten by your festive parent at [[leisure-and-sports|golf]]?”

[010-003] “Oh, well!” [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] sighed. “It can’t be helped, I suppose.” She felt

[010-004] in the pocket of her sweater. “Oh, there’s a letter for you. I’ve just

[010-005] been to fetch the mail. I don’t know who it can be from. The

[010-006] handwriting [[appearance-and-first-impressions|looks]] like a vampire’s. Kind of scrawly.”

[010-007] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] inspected the envelope. It provided no solution.

[010-008] “That’s rummy! Who could be writing to me?”

[010-009] “Open it and see.”

[010-010] “Dashed bright scheme! I will, Herbert [[parker|Parker]]. Who the deuce is Herbert

[010-011] Parker?”

[010-012] “Parker? Father’s valet’s name was Parker. The one he dismissed when he

[010-013] found he was wearing his shirts.”

[010-014] “Do you mean to say any reasonable chappie would willingly wear the

[010-015] sort of shirts your father—? I mean to say, there must have been some

[010-016] mistake.”

[010-017] “Do read the letter. I expect he wants to use your influence with

[010-018] father to have him taken back.”

[010-019] “_My_ influence? With your _father_? Well, I’m dashed. Sanguine sort of

[010-020] Johnny, if he does. Well, here’s what he says. Of course, I remember

[010-021] jolly old Parker now—great pal of mine.”

[010-022] Dear Sir,—It is some time since the undersigned had the honour of

[010-023] conversing with you, but I am respectfully trusting that you may recall

[010-024] me to mind when I mention that until recently I served [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]],

[010-025] your [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]], in the capacity of valet. Owing to an unfortunate

[010-026] misunderstanding, I was dismissed from that position and am now

[010-027] temporarily out of a job. “How [[art-and-collecting|art]] thou fallen from Heaven, O Lucifer,

[010-028] son of the morning!” (Isaiah xiv. 12.)

[010-029] “You know,” said [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], admiringly, “this bird is hot stuff! I mean to

[010-030] say he writes dashed well.”

[010-031] It is not, however, with my own affairs that I desire to trouble you,

[010-032] dear sir. I have little doubt that all will be well with me and that I

[010-033] shall not fall like a sparrow to the ground. “I have been young and now

[010-034] am old; yet have I not seen the righteous forsaken, nor his seed

[010-035] begging bread” (Psalms xxxvii. 25). My object in writing to you is as

[010-036] follows. You may recall that I had the pleasure of meeting you one

[010-037] morning in Mr. Brewster’s suite, when we had an interesting [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] on the

[010-038] subject of Mr. B.’s _objets d’art_. You may recall being particularly

[010-039] interested in a small china figure. To assist your memory, the figure

[010-040] to which I allude is the one which you whimsically referred to as

[010-041] Pongo. I informed you, if you remember, that, could the accompanying

[010-042] figure be secured, the pair would be extremely valuable.

[010-043] I am glad to say, dear sir, that this has now transpired, and is on

[010-044] view at Beale’s Art Galleries on West Forty-Fifth Street, where it

[010-045] will be sold to-morrow at auction, the sale commencing at

[010-046] two-thirty sharp. If Mr. Brewster cares to attend, he will, I

[010-047] fancy, have little trouble in securing it at a reasonable price. I

[010-048] confess that I had thought of refraining from apprising my late

[010-049] employer of this matter, but more Christian feelings have

[010-050] prevailed. “If thine enemy hunger, feed him; if he thirst, give him

[010-051] drink; for in so doing thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head”

[010-052] (Romans xii. 20). Nor, I must confess, am I altogether uninfluenced

[010-053] by the thought that my action in this matter may conceivably lead

[010-054] to Mr. B. consenting to forget the past and to reinstate me in my

[010-055] former position. However, I am confident that I can leave this to

[010-056] his good feeling.

[010-057] I remain, respectfully yours,

[010-058] Herbert Parker.

[010-059] Lucille clapped her hands.

[010-060] “How splendid! Father _will_ be pleased!”

[010-061] “Yes. Friend Parker has certainly found a way to make the old dad fond

[010-062] of him. Wish _I_ could!”

[010-063] “But you can, silly! He’ll be delighted when you show him that letter.”

[010-064] “Yes, with Parker. Old Herb. Parker’s is the neck he’ll fall on—not

[010-065] mine.”

[010-066] Lucille reflected.

[010-067] “I wish—” she began. She stopped. Her eyes lit up. “Oh, Archie,

[010-068] darling, I’ve got an idea!”

[010-069] “Decant it.”

[010-070] “Why don’t you slip up to [[new-york-city|New York]] to-morrow and buy the thing, and

[010-071] give it to father as a surprise?”

[010-072] Archie patted her hand kindly. He hated to spoil her girlish

[010-073] day-dreams.

[010-074] “Yes,” he said. “But reflect, queen of my heart! I have at the moment

[010-075] of going to press just two dollars fifty in specie, which I took off

[010-076] your father this after-noon. We were playing twenty-five cents a hole.

[010-077] He coughed it up without enthusiasm—in fact, with a nasty hacking

[010-078] sound—but I’ve got it. But that’s all I have got.”

[010-079] “That’s all right. You can pawn that ring and that bracelet of mine.”

[010-080] “Oh, I say, what! Pop the family jewels?”

[010-081] “Only for a day or two. Of course, once you’ve got the thing, father

[010-082] will pay us back. He would give you all the [[wealth-and-money|money]] we asked him for, if

[010-083] he knew what it was for. But I want to surprise him. And if you were to

[010-084] go to him and ask him for a thousand dollars without telling him what

[010-085] it was for, he might refuse.”

[010-086] “He might!” said Archie. “He might!”

[010-087] “It all works out splendidly. To-morrow’s the Invitation Handicap, and

[010-088] father’s been looking forward to it for weeks. He’d hate to have to go

[010-089] up to town himself and not play in it. But you can slip up and slip

[010-090] back without his knowing anything about it.”

[010-091] Archie pondered.

[010-092] “It sounds a ripe scheme. Yes, it has all the ear-marks of a somewhat

[010-093] fruity wheeze! By Jove, it _is_ a fruity wheeze! It’s an egg!”

[010-094] “An egg?”

[010-095] “Good egg, you know. [[what-ho|Halloa]], here’s a postscript. I didn’t see it.”

[010-096] P.S.—I should be glad if you would convey my most cordial respects to

[010-097] Mrs. Moffam. Will you also inform her that I chanced to meet Mr.

[010-098] William this morning on Broadway, just off the boat. He desired me to

[010-099] send his regards and to say that he would be joining you at Brookport

[010-100] in the course of a day or so. Mr. B. will be pleased to have him back.

[010-101] “A wise son maketh a glad father” (Proverbs x. 1).

[010-102] “Who’s Mr. William?” asked Archie.

[010-103] “My brother Bill, of course. I’ve told you all about him.”

[010-104] “Oh yes, of course. Your brother Bill. Rummy to think I’ve got a

[010-105] brother-in-law I’ve never seen.”

[010-106] “You see, we married so suddenly. When we married, Bill was in Yale.”

[010-107] “Good God! What for?”

[010-108] “Not jail, silly. Yale. The university.”

[010-109] “Oh, ah, yes.”

[010-110] “Then he went over to Europe for a trip to broaden his mind. You must

[010-111] look him up to-morrow when you get back to New York. He’s sure to be at

[010-112] his club.”

[010-113] “I’ll make a point of it. Well, vote of thanks to good old Parker! This

[010-114] really does begin to look like the point in my career where I start to

[010-115] have your forbidding old parent eating out of my hand.”

[010-116] “Yes, it’s an egg, isn’t it!”

[010-117] “Queen of my soul,” said Archie enthusiastically, “it’s an omelette!”

[010-118] The business negotiations in connection with [[lucilles-bracelet|the bracelet]] and the ring

[010-119] occupied Archie on his arrival in New York to an extent which made it

[010-120] impossible for him to call on [[bill-brewster|Brother Bill]] before [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]]. He decided to

[010-121] postpone the affecting meeting of brothers-in-law to a more convenient

[010-122] season, and made his way to his favourite table at [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]]

[010-123] grill-room for a bite of lunch preliminary to the fatigues of the sale.

[010-124] He found [[salvatore|Salvatore]] hovering about as usual, and instructed him to come

[010-125] to the rescue with a minute steak.

[010-126] Salvatore was the dark, sinister-looking waiter who attended, among

[010-127] other tables, to the one at the far end of the grill-room at which

[010-128] Archie usually sat. For several weeks Archie’s conversations with the

[010-129] other had dealt exclusively with the bill of fare and its contents; but

[010-130] gradually he had found himself becoming more personal. Even before the

[010-131] war and its democratising influences, Archie had always lacked that

[010-132] reserve which characterises many Britons; and since [[world-war-i|the war]] he had

[010-133] looked on nearly everyone he met as a brother. Long since, through the

[010-134] medium of a series of friendly chats, he had heard all about

[010-135] Salvatore’s home in Italy, the little newspaper and tobacco shop which

[010-136] his mother owned down on Seventh Avenue, and a hundred other personal

[010-137] details. Archie had an insatiable curiosity about his fellow-man.

[010-138] “Well done,” said Archie.

[010-139] “Sare?”

[010-140] “The steak. Not too rare, you know.”

[010-141] “Very good, sare.”

[010-142] Archie looked at the waiter closely. His tone had been subdued and sad.

[010-143] Of course, you don’t expect a waiter to beam all over his face and give

[010-144] three rousing cheers simply because you have asked him to bring you a

[010-145] minute steak, but still there was something about Salvatore’s manner

[010-146] that disturbed Archie. The man appeared to have the pip. Whether he was

[010-147] merely homesick and brooding on the lost delights of his sunny native

[010-148] land, or whether his trouble was more definite, could only be

[010-149] ascertained by enquiry. So Archie enquired.

[010-150] “What’s the matter, laddie?” he said sympathetically. “Something on

[010-151] your mind?”

[010-152] “Sare?”

[010-153] “I say, there seems to be something on your mind. What’s the trouble?”

[010-154] The waiter shrugged his shoulders, as if indicating an unwillingness to

[010-155] inflict his grievances on one of the tipping classes.

[010-156] “Come on!” persisted Archie encouragingly. “All [[friendship|pals]] here. Barge along,

[010-157] old thing, and let’s have it.”

[010-158] Salvatore, thus admonished, proceeded in a hurried undertone—with one

[010-159] eye on the headwaiter—to lay bare his soul. What he said was not very

[010-160] coherent, but Archie could make out enough of it to gather that it was

[010-161] a sad story of excessive hours and insufficient pay. He mused awhile.

[010-162] The waiter’s hard case touched him.

[010-163] “I’ll tell you what,” he said at last. “When jolly old Brewster comes

[010-164] back to town—he’s away just now—I’ll take you along to him and we’ll

[010-165] beard the old boy in his den. I’ll introduce you, and you get that

[010-166] extract from Italian opera off your chest which you’ve just been

[010-167] singing to me, and you’ll find it’ll be all right. He isn’t what you

[010-168] might call one of my greatest admirers, but everybody says he’s a

[010-169] square sort of cove and he’ll see you aren’t snootered. And now,

[010-170] laddie, touching the matter of that steak.”

[010-171] The waiter disappeared, greatly cheered, and Archie, turning, perceived

[010-172] that his friend [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]] was entering the room. He waved to him

[010-173] to join his table. He liked Reggie, and it also occurred to him that a

[010-174] man of the world like the heir of the van Tuyls, who had been popping

[010-175] about New York for years, might be able to give him some much-needed

[010-176] information on the procedure at an auction sale, a matter on which he

[010-177] himself was profoundly ignorant.

[010-178] CHAPTER X.

[010-179] DOING FATHER A BIT OF GOOD

[010-180] Reggie Van Tuyl approached the table languidly, and sank down into a

[010-181] chair. He was a long youth with a rather subdued and deflated look, as

[010-182] though the burden of the van Tuyl millions was more than his frail

[010-183] strength could support. Most things tired him.

[010-184] “I say, Reggie, old top,” said Archie, “you’re just the lad I wanted to

[010-185] see. I require the assistance of a blighter of ripe intellect. Tell me,

[010-186] laddie, do you know anything about sales?”

[010-187] Reggie eyed him sleepily.

[010-188] “Sales?”

[010-189] “Auction sales.”

[010-190] Reggie considered.

[010-191] “Well, they’re sales, you know.” He checked a yawn. “Auction sales, you

[010-192] understand.”

[010-193] “Yes,” said Archie encouragingly. “Something—the name or

[010-194] something—seemed to tell me that.”

[010-195] “Fellows put things up for sale you know, and other fellows—other

[010-196] fellows go in and—and buy ’em, if you follow me.”

[010-197] “Yes, but what’s the procedure? I mean, what do I do? That’s what I’m

[010-198] after. I’ve got to buy something at Beale’s this afternoon. How do I

[010-199] set about it?”

[010-200] “Well,” said Reggie, drowsily, “there are several ways of bidding, you

[010-201] know. You can shout, or you can nod, or you can twiddle your fingers—”

[010-202] The effort of concentration was too much for him. He leaned back limply

[010-203] in his chair. “I’ll tell you what. I’ve nothing to do this afternoon.

[010-204] I’ll come with you and show you.”
