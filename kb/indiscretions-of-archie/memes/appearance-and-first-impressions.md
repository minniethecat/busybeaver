# Appearance and First Impressions

In the lobby of the Hotel Cosmopolis, appearance is everything. Characters are sized up at a glance: Archie's English tailoring, Brewster's proprietary bearing, the various guests and visitors whose looks announce their social position before they speak. Wodehouse uses physical description with economy and precision, creating vivid impressions that are both essential to the comedy and — as the novel demonstrates — frequently misleading.

## Overview

The novel is set in a world of visual judgment. The hotel lobby is a theater of appearances, where clothes signal class, posture signals character, and first impressions determine the course of relationships. Archie, despite his poverty, looks like a gentleman — and in Wodehouse's universe, looking like a gentleman is half the battle.

But the novel also undermines the reliability of appearances. Brewster looks like a successful man and is one, but his appearance conceals the vulnerability of his pride. Archie looks like a feckless aristocrat and is one, but his appearance conceals the genuine warmth that is his greatest asset. The comedy depends on the gap between how characters appear and who they actually are.

## In the Novel

First impressions drive much of the plot. Archie's initial appearance at the Cosmopolis — well-dressed, well-mannered, English — determines how he is received. Brewster's first impression of Archie as an idle fortune-hunter is both accurate (Archie is idle and fortune-less) and misleading (Archie is not a hunter). The novel's episodic structure allows these first impressions to be repeatedly tested and revised.

The theme of appearance extends beyond individuals to the hotel itself. The Cosmopolis is designed to impress — its tapestries, paintings, and antique furniture selected to create an impression of taste and permanence. Yet this carefully curated appearance is also vulnerable, as Archie's casual criticism demonstrates.
