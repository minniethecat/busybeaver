# The Baseball Bet

The Baseball Bet is a four-chapter subplot in *Indiscretions of Archie* in which
Archie Moffam wagers $1000 on a baseball game and discovers that the outcome
depends on the romantic fortunes of the star pitcher.

## The Wager

Reggie van Tuyl "assured Archie that the New York Giants would win the opening
game of the series" against the Pittsburg baseball team [017-009]. Archie,
"contrived to double his capital" by betting on the outcome [017-010].
As a "whole-hearted supporter of the Giants," Archie's confidence was
initially high [017-071].

## The Stakes

The bet's true stakes are established before a single pitch is thrown. Archie's starting capital comes from an unexpected windfall — "an almost forgotten aunt in England suddenly, out of an absolutely blue sky, shot no less a sum than five hundred dollars across the ocean" (017-001–003) — which he promptly doubles on the opening game. The thousand dollars is not his to spend: it is "earmarked for Lucille's birthday present" (017-115–116), specifically a bracelet he has already selected at a Broadway jeweller's.

When Reggie proposes doubling the wager, Archie hesitates:

> "That thousand dollars of his was earmarked for Lucille's birthday present, and he doubted whether he ought to risk it. Then the thought that the honour of New York was in his hands decided him. Besides, the risk was negligible. Betting on Looney Biddle was like betting on the probable rise of the sun in the east." (017-115–119)

Archie's self-reassurance is characteristically sunny — "Nothing was too good for Lucille on her birthday" (017-126) — and he imagines upgrading to an even finer bracelet after collecting his winnings, transforming a reckless gamble into what he persuades himself is "a rather unusually sound and conservative investment" (017-120).

## Looney Biddle

The Giants' star pitcher, Gus "Looney" Biddle, is central to the bet's outcome.
So-called "by an affectionately admiring public" [017-083], Biddle had been
"plugging the holes in the dam" of the Giants' pitching staff [017-089].
Betting on Biddle "was like betting on the rising of the sun" [017-118] —
ordinarily a sure thing. However, Biddle's performance was entirely contingent
on his romantic relationship: when his girlfriend left him, his pitching arm
became useless and he was unable to pitch [019-038].

## The Chase

Discovering that Biddle's estranged girlfriend was the key to winning his bet,
Archie pursued her through New York. Her interest in baseball was notably
indifferent [017-179], but Archie's persistence — and the fate of his $1000 —
depended on reconciling the couple before game time. The chase narrative spans
chapters 18 and 19 as Archie frantically attempts to locate the girl.

## Resolution

The bet is resolved in chapter 20. Archie's intervention succeeds in
reuniting Biddle with his girlfriend, and Biddle's left hand — "that priceless
left hand" [020-019] — is restored to form. The Giants win, and Archie
collects his winnings.

## Significance

The Baseball Bet subplot is notable for:
- Introducing baseball as a motif in a novel otherwise focused on hotel life
- Showcasing Archie's resourcefulness and willingness to go to extreme lengths
- Providing one of the novel's most sustained comic chase sequences
- Demonstrating the Wodehousian plot device of cascading consequences from a
  single impulsive decision
