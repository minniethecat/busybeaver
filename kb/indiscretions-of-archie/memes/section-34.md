## Section 34

[034-001] [[j-b-wheeler|J. B. Wheeler]] was an artist who made a large annual income as an

[034-002] illustrator for the magazines, and it was a surprise to [[archie-moffam|Archie]] to find

[034-003] that he also went in for this kind of thing. For [[the-wheeler-painting|the picture]], dashingly

[034-004] painted in oils, represented a comfortably plump young woman who, from

[034-005] her rather weak-minded simper and the fact that she wore absolutely

[034-006] nothing except a small dove on her left shoulder, was plainly intended

[034-007] to be the goddess Venus. [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] was not much of a lad around the

[034-008] picture-galleries, but he knew enough about Art to recognise Venus when

[034-009] he saw her; though once or twice, it is true, artists had

[034-010] double-crossed him by ringing in some such title as “Day Dreams,” or

[034-011] “When the Heart is Young.”

[034-012] He inspected this picture for awhile, then, returning to his seat, lit

[034-013] a cigarette and began to meditate on [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] once more. “Yes, the dear

[034-014] girl had been rummy at [[dining-and-social-ritual|breakfast]]. She had not exactly said anything or

[034-015] done anything out of the ordinary; but—well, you know how it is. We

[034-016] husbands, we lads of the for-better-or-for-worse brigade, we learn to

[034-017] pierce the mask. There had been in Lucille’s manner that curious,

[034-018] strained sweetness which comes to women whose husbands have failed to

[034-019] match the piece of silk or forgotten to post an important letter. If

[034-020] his conscience had not been as clear as crystal, Archie would have said

[034-021] that that was what must have been the matter. But, when Lucille wrote

[034-022] [[letters-and-correspondence|letters]], she just stepped out of the suite and dropped them in the

[034-023] mail-chute attached to the elevator. It couldn’t be that. And he

[034-024] couldn’t have forgotten anything else, because—”

[034-025] “Oh my sainted aunt!”

[034-026] Archie’s cigarette smouldered, neglected, between his fingers. His jaw

[034-027] had fallen and his eyes were staring glassily before him. He was

[034-028] appalled. His memory was weak, he knew; but never before had it let him

[034-029] down so scurvily as this. This was a record. It stood in a class by

[034-030] itself, printed in red ink and marked with a star, as the bloomer of a

[034-031] lifetime. For a man may forget many things: he may forget his name, his

[034-032] umbrella, his nationality, his spats, and the friends of his youth: but

[034-033] there is one thing which your married man, your

[034-034] in-sickness-and-in-health lizard must not forget: and that is the

[034-035] anniversary of his wedding-day.

[034-036] Remorse swept over Archie like a wave. His heart bled for Lucille. No

[034-037] wonder the poor girl had been rummy at breakfast. What girl wouldn’t be

[034-038] rummy at breakfast, tied for life to a ghastly outsider like himself?

[034-039] He groaned hollowly, and sagged forlornly in his chair: and, as he did

[034-040] so, [[the-wigmore-venus|the Venus]] caught his eye. For it was an eye-catching picture. You

[034-041] might like it or dislike it, but you could not ignore it.

[034-042] As a strong swimmer shoots to the surface after a high dive, Archie’s

[034-043] soul rose suddenly from the depths to which it had descended. He did

[034-044] not often get inspirations, but he got one now. Hope dawned with a

[034-045] jerk. The one way out had presented itself to him. A rich present! That

[034-046] was the wheeze. If he returned to her bearing a rich present, he might,

[034-047] with the help of Heaven and a face of brass, succeed in making her

[034-048] believe that he had merely pretended to forget the vital date in order

[034-049] to enhance the surprise.

[034-050] It was a scheme. Like some great general forming his plan of campaign

[034-051] on the eve of battle, Archie had the whole binge neatly worked out

[034-052] inside a minute. He scribbled a note to Mr. Wheeler, explaining the

[034-053] situation and promising reasonable payment on the instalment system;

[034-054] then, placing the note in a conspicuous position on the easel, he

[034-055] leaped to the telephone: and presently found himself connected with

[034-056] Lucille’s room at [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]].

[034-057] “Hullo, darling,” he cooed.

[034-058] There was a slight pause at the other end of the wire.

[034-059] “Oh, hullo, Archie!”

[034-060] Lucille’s voice was dull and listless, and Archie’s experienced ear

[034-061] could detect that she had been crying. He raised his right foot, and

[034-062] kicked himself indignantly on the left ankle.

[034-063] “Many happy returns of the day, old thing!”

[034-064] A muffled sob floated over the wire.

[034-065] “Have you only just remembered?” said Lucille in a small voice.

[034-066] Archie, bracing himself up, cackled gleefully into the receiver.

[034-067] “Did I take you in, light of my home? Do you mean to say you really

[034-068] thought I had forgotten? For Heaven’s sake!”

[034-069] “You didn’t say a word at breakfast.”

[034-070] “Ah, but that was all part of the devilish cunning. I hadn’t got a

[034-071] present for you then. At least, I didn’t know whether it was ready.”

[034-072] “Oh, Archie, you darling!” Lucille’s voice had lost its crushed

[034-073] melancholy. She trilled like a thrush, or a linnet, or any bird that

[034-074] goes in largely for trilling. “Have you really got me a present?”

[034-075] “It’s here now. The dickens of a fruity picture. One of J. B. Wheeler’s

[034-076] things. You’ll like it.”

[034-077] “Oh, I know I shall. I love his work. You are an angel. We’ll hang it

[034-078] over the piano.”

[034-079] “I’ll be round with it in something under three ticks, star of my soul.

[034-080] I’ll take a taxi.”

[034-081] “Yes, do hurry! I want to hug you!”

[034-082] “Right-o!” said Archie. “I’ll take two taxis.”

[034-083] It is not far from Washington Square to the [[hotel-cosmopolis|Hotel Cosmopolis]], and

[034-084] Archie made the journey without mishap. There was a little

[034-085] unpleasantness with the cabman before starting—he, on the prudish plea

[034-086] that he was a married man with a local reputation to keep up, declining

[034-087] at first to be seen in company with the masterpiece. But, on Archie

[034-088] giving a promise to keep the front of the picture away from the public

[034-089] gaze, he consented to take the job on; and, some ten minutes later,

[034-090] having made his way blushfully through the hotel lobby and endured the

[034-091] frank curiosity of the boy who worked the elevator, Archie entered his

[034-092] suite, the picture under his arm.

[034-093] He placed it carefully against the wall in order to leave himself more

[034-094] scope for embracing Lucille, and when the joyful reunion—or the sacred

[034-095] scene, if you prefer so to call it, was concluded, he stepped forward

[034-096] to turn it round and exhibit it.

[034-097] “Why, it’s enormous,” said Lucille. “I didn’t know Mr. Wheeler ever

[034-098] painted pictures that size. When you said it was one of his, I thought

[034-099] it must be the original of a magazine drawing or something like—Oh!”

[034-100] Archie had moved back and given her an uninterrupted view of the work

[034-101] of [[art-and-collecting|art]], and she had started as if some unkindly disposed person had

[034-102] driven a bradawl into her.

[034-103] “Pretty ripe, what?” said Archie enthusiastically.

[034-104] Lucille did not speak for a moment. It may have been sudden joy that

[034-105] kept her silent. Or, on the other hand, it may not. She stood looking

[034-106] at the picture with wide eyes and parted lips.

[034-107] “A bird, eh?” said Archie.

[034-108] “Y—yes,” said Lucille.

[034-109] “I knew you’d like it,” proceeded Archie with animation, “You see?

[034-110] you’re by way of being a picture-hound—know all about the things, and

[034-111] what not—inherit it from the dear old dad, I shouldn’t wonder.

[034-112] Personally, I can’t tell one picture from another as a rule, but I’m

[034-113] bound to say, the moment I set eyes on this, I said to myself ‘What

[034-114] ho!’ or words to that effect, I rather think this will add a touch of

[034-115] distinction to the home, yes, no? I’ll hang it up, shall I? ’Phone down

[034-116] to the office, light of my soul, and tell them to send up a nail, a bit

[034-117] of string, and the hotel hammer.”

[034-118] “One moment, darling. I’m not quite sure.”

[034-119] “Eh?”

[034-120] “Where it ought to hang, I mean. You see—”

[034-121] “Over the piano, you said. The jolly old piano.”

[034-122] “Yes, but I hadn’t seen it then.”

[034-123] A monstrous suspicion flitted for an instant into Archie’s mind.

[034-124] “I say, you _do_ like it, don’t you?” he said anxiously.

[034-125] “Oh, Archie, darling! Of _course_ I do! And it was so sweet of you to

[034-126] give it to me. But, what I was trying to say was that this picture is

[034-127] so—so striking that I feel that we ought to wait a little while and

[034-128] decide where it would have the best effect. The light over the piano is

[034-129] rather strong.”

[034-130] “You think it ought to hang in a dimmish light, what?”

[034-131] “Yes, yes. The dimmer the—I mean, yes, in a dim light. Suppose we leave

[034-132] it in the corner for the moment—over there—behind the sofa, and—and

[034-133] I’ll think it over. It wants a lot of thought, you know.”

[034-134] “Right-o! Here?”

[034-135] “Yes, that will do splendidly. Oh, and, Archie.”

[034-136] “Hullo?”

[034-137] “I think perhaps... Just turn its face to the wall, will you?” Lucille

[034-138] gave a little gulp. “It will prevent it getting dusty.”

[034-139] It perplexed Archie a little during the next few days to notice in

[034-140] Lucille, whom he had always looked on as pre-eminently a girl who knew

[034-141] her own mind, a curious streak of vacillation. Quite half a dozen times

[034-142] he suggested various spots on the wall as suitable for the Venus, but

[034-143] Lucille seemed unable to decide. Archie wished that she would settle on

[034-144] something definite, for he wanted to invite J. B. Wheeler to the suite

[034-145] to see the thing. He had heard nothing from the artist since the day he

[034-146] had removed the picture, and one morning, encountering him on Broadway,

[034-147] he expressed his appreciation of the very decent manner in which the

[034-148] other had taken the whole affair.

[034-149] “Oh, that!” said J. B. Wheeler. “My dear fellow, you’re welcome.” He

[034-150] paused for a moment. “More than welcome,” he added. “You aren’t much of

[034-151] an expert on pictures, are you?”

[034-152] “Well,” said Archie, “I don’t know that you’d call me an absolute nib,

[034-153] don’t you know, but of course I know enough to see that this particular

[034-154] exhibit is not a little fruity. Absolutely one of the best things

[034-155] you’ve ever done, laddie.”

[034-156] A slight purple tinge manifested itself in Mr. Wheeler’s round and rosy

[034-157] face. His eyes bulged.

[034-158] “What are you talking about, you Tishbite? You misguided son of Belial,

[034-159] are you under the impression that _I_ painted that thing?”

[034-160] “Didn’t you?”

[034-161] Mr. Wheeler swallowed a little convulsively.

[034-162] “My fiancée painted it,” he said shortly.

[034-163] “Your fiancée? My dear old lad, I didn’t know you were engaged. Who is

[034-164] she? Do I know her?”

[034-165] “Her name is [[alice-wigmore|Alice Wigmore]]. You don’t know her.”

[034-166] “And she painted that picture?” Archie was perturbed. “But, I say!

[034-167] Won’t she be apt to wonder where the thing has got to?”

[034-168] “I told her it had been stolen. She thought it a great compliment, and

[034-169] was tickled to death. So that’s all right.”

[034-170] “And, of course, she’ll paint you another.”

[034-171] “Not while I have my strength she won’t,” said J. B. Wheeler firmly.

[034-172] “She’s given up painting since I taught her [[leisure-and-sports|golf]], thank goodness, and

[034-173] my best efforts shall be employed in seeing that she doesn’t have a

[034-174] relapse.”

[034-175] “But, laddie,” said Archie, puzzled, “you [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] as though there were

[034-176] something wrong with the picture. I thought it dashed hot stuff.”

[034-177] “God bless you!” said J. B. Wheeler.

[034-178] Archie proceeded on his way, still mystified. Then he reflected that

[034-179] artists as a class were all pretty weird and rummy and talked more or

[034-180] less consistently through their hats. You couldn’t ever take an

[034-181] artist’s opinion on a picture. Nine out of ten of them had views on Art

[034-182] which would have admitted them to any looney-bin, and no questions

[034-183] asked. He had met several of the species who absolutely raved over

[034-184] things which any reasonable chappie would decline to be found dead in a

[034-185] ditch with. His admiration for the Wigmore Venus, which had faltered

[034-186] for a moment during his conversation with J. B. Wheeler, returned in

[034-187] all its pristine vigour. Absolute rot, he meant to say, to try to make

[034-188] out that it wasn’t one of the ones and just like mother used to make.

[034-189] Look how Lucille had liked it!
