# Wealth and Money

Money — its possession, its absence, and its pursuit — is the invisible hand that moves nearly every character in *Indiscretions of Archie*. Archie arrives in New York with "neither money nor occupation" (001-035), a condition that defines his precarious position in his father-in-law's world. Daniel Brewster, by contrast, is a self-made millionaire whose wealth is embodied in the Hotel Cosmopolis itself.

## Overview

The novel's financial landscape is sharply stratified. Brewster represents earned wealth: the hotel proprietor who has built his fortune through industry and vigilance. Archie represents inherited gentility without capital — the English gentleman adrift in a culture that measures worth in dollars. This tension is the novel's economic engine: Archie must prove he can earn money, not merely marry into it.

Wodehouse uses money as a comic device with remarkable consistency. Business negotiations go awry, sums are wildly miscalculated, and the pursuit of wealth leads characters into ever more elaborate misunderstandings. Yet beneath the comedy lies a clear-eyed observation of the social power of money in Jazz Age America.

## In the Novel

Archie's poverty is established in the opening pages. Brewster's objection to his son-in-law is not merely personal but economic: Archie is "a young man who had neither money nor occupation" (001-035). Throughout the novel, Archie's attempts to rectify this condition — through various business schemes, art deals, and entrepreneurial ventures — generate much of the plot.

Brewster's relationship with money is equally revealing. Having risen "from poverty to wealth by his own efforts" (012-220), he "objected to parting with his money" (012-221) and views financial prudence as a moral virtue. The novel's comic climaxes often involve the collision between Archie's casual attitude toward money and Brewster's fierce protectiveness of it.

## Key Passages
