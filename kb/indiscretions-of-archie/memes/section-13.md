## Section 13

[013-001] [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]] unlocked the door and crossed the room. Then, suddenly, he

[013-002] halted, stared, and stared again. He sprang to the bell and pressed it,

[013-003] then stood gurgling wordlessly.

[013-004] “Anything wrong, [[old-bean|old bean]]?” queried [[archie-moffam|Archie]], solicitously.

[013-005] “Wrong! Wrong! It’s gone!”

[013-006] “Gone?”

[013-007] “The figure!”

[013-008] The floor-waiter had manifested himself silently in answer to the bell,

[013-009] and was standing in the doorway.

[013-010] “Simmons!” Mr. Brewster turned to him wildly. “Has anyone been in this

[013-011] suite since I went away?”

[013-012] “No, sir.”

[013-013] “Nobody?”

[013-014] “Nobody except your valet, sir—[[parker|Parker]]. He said he had come to fetch

[013-015] some things away. I supposed he had come from you, sir, with

[013-016] instructions.”

[013-017] “Get out!”

[013-018] [[professor-binstead|Professor Binstead]] had unwrapped his parcel, and had placed the Pongo

[013-019] on the table. There was a weighty silence. [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] picked up the little

[013-020] china figure and balanced it on the palm of his hand. It was a small

[013-021] thing, he reflected philosophically, but it had made quite a stir in

[013-022] the world.

[013-023] Mr. Brewster fermented for a while without speaking.

[013-024] “So,” he said, at last, in a voice trembling with self-pity, “I have

[013-025] been to all this trouble—”

[013-026] “And expense,” put in Professor Binstead, gently.

[013-027] “Merely to buy back something which had been stolen from me! And, owing

[013-028] to your damned officiousness,” he cried, turning on Archie, “I have had

[013-029] to pay twenty-three hundred dollars for it! I don’t know why they make

[013-030] such a fuss about Job. Job never had anything like you around!”

[013-031] “Of course,” argued Archie, “he had one or two boils.”

[013-032] “Boils! What are boils?”

[013-033] “Dashed sorry,” murmured Archie. “Acted for the best. Meant well. And

[013-034] all that sort of rot!”

[013-035] Professor Binstead’s mind seemed occupied to the exclusion of all other

[013-036] aspects of the affair, with the ingenuity of the absent Parker.

[013-037] “A cunning scheme!” he said. “A very cunning scheme! This man Parker

[013-038] must have a brain of no low order. I should like to feel his bumps!”

[013-039] “I should like to give him some!” said the stricken Mr. Brewster. He

[013-040] breathed a deep breath. “Oh, well,” he said, “situated as I am, with a

[013-041] crook valet and an imbecile son-in-law, I suppose I ought to be

[013-042] thankful that I’ve still got my own property, even if I have had to pay

[013-043] twenty-three hundred dollars for the privilege of keeping it.” He

[013-044] rounded on Archie, who was in a reverie. The thought of the unfortunate

[013-045] Bill had just crossed Archie’s mind. It would be many moons, many weary

[013-046] moons, before Mr. Brewster would be in a suitable mood to listen

[013-047] sympathetically to the story of love’s young dream. “Give me that

[013-048] figure!”

[013-049] Archie continued to toy absently with Pongo. He was wondering now how

[013-050] best to break this sad occurrence to [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]]. It would be a

[013-051] disappointment for the poor girl.

[013-052] “_Give me that figure!_”

[013-053] Archie started violently. There was an instant in which Pongo seemed to

[013-054] hang suspended, like Mohammed’s coffin, between heaven and earth, then

[013-055] the force of gravity asserted itself. Pongo fell with a sharp crack and

[013-056] disintegrated. And as it did so there was a knock at the door, and in

[013-057] walked a dark, furtive person, who to the inflamed vision of Mr. Daniel

[013-058] Brewster looked like something connected with the executive staff of

[013-059] the Black Hand. With all time at his disposal, the unfortunate

[013-060] [[salvatore|Salvatore]] had selected this moment for stating his case.

[013-061] “Get out!” bellowed Mr. Brewster. “I didn’t ring for a waiter.”

[013-062] Archie, his mind reeling beneath the catastrophe, recovered himself

[013-063] sufficiently to do the honours. It was at his instigation that

[013-064] Salvatore was there, and, greatly as he wished that he could have seen

[013-065] fit to choose a more auspicious moment for his business [[conversation-and-dialogue|chat]], he felt

[013-066] compelled to do his best to see him through.

[013-067] “Oh, I say, half a second,” he said. “You don’t quite understand. As a

[013-068] matter of fact, this chappie is by way of being downtrodden and

[013-069] oppressed and what not, and I suggested that he should get hold of you

[013-070] and speak a few well-chosen words. Of course, if you’d rather—some

[013-071] other time—”

[013-072] But Mr. Brewster was not permitted to postpone the interview. Before he

[013-073] could get his breath, Salvatore had begun to talk. He was a strong,

[013-074] ambidextrous talker, whom it was hard to interrupt; and it was not for

[013-075] some moments that Mr. Brewster succeeded in getting a word in. When he

[013-076] did, he spoke to the point. Though not a linguist, he had been able to

[013-077] follow the discourse closely enough to realise that the waiter was

[013-078] dissatisfied with conditions in his hotel; and Mr. Brewster, as has

[013-079] been indicated, had a short way with people who criticised the

[013-080] Cosmopolis.

[013-081] “You’re fired!” said Mr. Brewster.

[013-082] “Oh, I say!” protested Archie.

[013-083] Salvatore muttered what sounded like a passage from Dante.

[013-084] “Fired!” repeated Mr. Brewster resolutely. “And I wish to heaven,” he

[013-085] added, eyeing his son-in-law malignantly, “I could fire _you!_”

[013-086] “Well,” said Professor Binstead cheerfully, breaking the grim silence

[013-087] which followed this outburst, “if you will give me your cheque,

[013-088] Brewster, I think I will be going. Two thousand three hundred dollars.

[013-089] Make it open, if you will, and then I can run round the corner and cash

[013-090] it before [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]]. That will be capital!”

[013-091] CHAPTER XII.

[013-092] BRIGHT EYES—AND A FLY

[013-093] [[the-hermitage|The Hermitage]] (unrivalled scenery, superb cuisine, Daniel Brewster,

[013-094] proprietor) was a picturesque summer hotel in the green heart of the

[013-095] mountains, built by Archie’s [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]] shortly after he assumed

[013-096] control of [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]]. Mr. Brewster himself seldom went there,

[013-097] preferring to concentrate his attention on his [[new-york-city|New York]] establishment;

[013-098] and Archie and Lucille, breakfasting in the airy dining-room some ten

[013-099] days after the incidents recorded in the last chapter, had consequently

[013-100] to be content with two out of the three advertised attractions of the

[013-101] place. Through the window at their side quite a slab of the unrivalled

[013-102] scenery was visible; some of the superb cuisine was already on the

[013-103] table; and the fact that the eye searched in vain for Daniel Brewster,

[013-104] proprietor, filled Archie, at any rate, with no sense of aching loss.

[013-105] He bore it with equanimity and even with positive enthusiasm. In

[013-106] Archie’s opinion, practically all a place needed to make it an earthly

[013-107] Paradise was for Mr. Daniel Brewster to be about forty-seven miles away

[013-108] from it.

[013-109] It was at Lucille’s suggestion that they had come to the Hermitage.

[013-110] Never a human sunbeam, Mr. Brewster had shown such a bleak front to the

[013-111] world, and particularly to his son-in-law, in the days following the

[013-112] Pongo incident, that Lucille had thought that he and Archie would for a

[013-113] time at least be better apart—a view with which her husband cordially

[013-114] agreed. He had enjoyed his stay at the Hermitage, and now he regarded

[013-115] the eternal hills with the comfortable affection of a healthy man who

[013-116] is breakfasting well.

[013-117] “It’s going to be another perfectly topping day,” he observed, eyeing

[013-118] the shimmering landscape, from which the morning mists were swiftly

[013-119] shredding away like faint puffs of smoke. “Just the day you ought to

[013-120] have been here.”

[013-121] “Yes, it’s too bad I’ve got to go. New York will be like an oven.”

[013-122] “Put it off.”

[013-123] “I can’t, I’m afraid. I’ve a fitting.”

[013-124] Archie argued no further. He was a married man of old enough standing

[013-125] to know the importance of fittings.

[013-126] “Besides,” said Lucille, “I want to see father.” Archie repressed an

[013-127] exclamation of astonishment. “I’ll be back to-morrow evening. You will

[013-128] be perfectly happy.”

[013-129] “Queen of my soul, you know I can’t be happy with you away. You know—”

[013-130] “Yes?” murmured Lucille, appreciatively. She never tired of hearing

[013-131] Archie say this sort of thing.

[013-132] Archie’s voice had trailed off. He was looking across the room.

[013-133] “By Jove!” he exclaimed. “What an awfully pretty woman!”

[013-134] “Where?”

[013-135] “Over there. Just coming in, I say, what wonderful eyes! I don’t think

[013-136] I ever saw such eyes. Did you notice her eyes? Sort of flashing!

[013-137] Awfully pretty woman!”

[013-138] Warm though the morning was, a suspicion of chill descended upon the

[013-139] breakfast-table. A certain coldness seemed to come into Lucille’s face.

[013-140] She could not always share Archie’s fresh young enthusiasms.

[013-141] “Do you think so?”

[013-142] “Wonderful figure, too!”

[013-143] “Yes?”

[013-144] “Well, what I mean to say, fair to medium,” said Archie, recovering a

[013-145] certain amount of that intelligence which raises man above the level of

[013-146] the beasts of the field. “Not the sort of type I admire myself, of

[013-147] course.”

[013-148] “You know her, don’t you?”

[013-149] “Absolutely not and far from it,” said Archie, hastily. “Never met her

[013-150] in my life.”

[013-151] “You’ve seen her on the stage. Her name’s Vera Silverton. We saw her

[013-152] in—”

[013-153] “Of course, yes. So we did. I say, I wonder what she’s doing here? She

[013-154] ought to be in New York, rehearsing. I remember meeting

[013-155] what’s-his-name—you know—chappie who writes plays and what not—George

[013-156] [[george-benham|Benham]]—I remember meeting George Benham, and he told me she was

[013-157] rehearsing in a piece of his called—I forget the name, but I know it

[013-158] was called something or other. Well, why isn’t she?”

[013-159] “She probably lost her temper and broke her contract and came away.

[013-160] She’s always doing that sort of thing. She’s known for it. She must be

[013-161] a horrid woman.”

[013-162] “Yes.”

[013-163] “I don’t want to talk about her. She used to be married to someone, and

[013-164] she divorced him. And then she was married to someone else, and he

[013-165] divorced her. And I’m certain her hair wasn’t that colour two years

[013-166] ago, and I don’t think a woman ought to make up like that, and her

[013-167] [[appearance-and-first-impressions|dress]] is all wrong for the country, and those pearls can’t be genuine,

[013-168] and I hate the way she rolls her eyes about, and pink doesn’t suit her

[013-169] a bit. I think she’s an awful woman, and I wish you wouldn’t keep on

[013-170] talking about her.”

[013-171] “Right-o!” said Archie, dutifully.

[013-172] They finished breakfast, and Lucille went up to pack her bag. Archie

[013-173] strolled out on to the terrace outside the hotel, where he smoked,

[013-174] communed with nature, and thought of Lucille. He always thought of

[013-175] Lucille when he was alone, especially when he chanced to find himself

[013-176] in poetic surroundings like those provided by the unrivalled scenery

[013-177] encircling the Hotel Hermitage. The longer he was married to her the

[013-178] more did the sacred institution seem to him a good egg. Mr. Brewster

[013-179] might regard their marriage as one of the world’s most unfortunate

[013-180] incidents, but to Archie it was, and always had been, a bit of all

[013-181] right. The more he thought of it the more did he marvel that a girl

[013-182] like Lucille should have been content to link her lot with that of a

[013-183] Class C specimen like himself. His meditations were, in fact, precisely

[013-184] what a happily-married man’s meditations ought to be.

[013-185] He was roused from them by a species of exclamation or cry almost at

[013-186] his elbow, and turned to find that the spectacular [[miss-silverton|Miss Silverton]] was

[013-187] standing beside him. Her dubious hair gleamed in the sunlight, and one

[013-188] of the criticised eyes was screwed up. The other gazed at Archie with

[013-189] an expression of appeal.

[013-190] “There’s something in my eye,” she said.

[013-191] “No, really!”

[013-192] “I wonder if you would mind? It would be so kind of you!”

[013-193] Archie would have preferred to remove himself, but no man worthy of the

[013-194] name can decline to come to the rescue of womanhood in distress. To

[013-195] twist the lady’s upper lid back and peer into it and jab at it with the

[013-196] corner of his handkerchief was the only course open to him. His conduct

[013-197] may be classed as not merely blameless but definitely praiseworthy.

[013-198] King Arthur’s knights used to do this sort of thing all the time, and

[013-199] look what people think of them. Lucille, therefore, coming out of the

[013-200] hotel just as the operation was concluded, ought not to have felt the

[013-201] annoyance she did. But, of course, there is a certain superficial

[013-202] intimacy about the attitude of a man who is taking a fly out of a

[013-203] woman’s eye which may excusably jar upon the sensibilities of his wife.

[013-204] It is an attitude which suggests a sort of _rapprochement_ or

[013-205] _[[friendship|camaraderie]]_ or, as Archie would have put it, what not.
