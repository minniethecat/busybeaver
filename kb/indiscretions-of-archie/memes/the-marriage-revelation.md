# The Marriage Revelation

The marriage revelation is the novel's emotional climax — the moment when [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]] learns that the young Englishman who called his hotel "bally rotten" is now his son-in-law. The scene, set in Brewster's rooms at the [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|Hotel Cosmopolis]], transforms the comic misunderstanding of Section 1 into a family crisis of genuine weight, handled with the lightness that is Wodehouse's signature.

## The Telegram

The revelation arrives in the form of a telegram from [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]]: "Returning New York to-day with darling Archie" (002-012). Brewster, reading it alongside his old friend [[professor-binstead|Professor Binstead]], is baffled. "Who is Archie?" he asks (002-015) — a question that, in the novel's comic logic, he will spend the next thirty chapters trying to answer.

Binstead, ever the scholar, examines the text with academic precision: "'Darling Archie,' murmured the professor, musing over the telegram. 'Returning to-day with darling Archie.' Strange!" (002-017). His detached curiosity provides comic counterpoint to Brewster's mounting dread.

## The Letter

The telegram is followed by Lucille's letter — "a long letter, and there was silence in the room as Mr. Brewster read it" (002-031). Wodehouse wisely keeps the letter's contents offstage, conveying its impact through Brewster's reactions: the silence, the stillness, the moment when he puts the letter down and the world has changed.

When Brewster finally speaks, his question is characteristically practical: "Inflicted this—_this_ on me for a son-in-law?" (002-148). He is a man of business confronting an irreversible transaction — a deal he never approved, a merger he never sanctioned.

## The Introduction

The scene's emotional centerpiece is Lucille's introduction of Archie to her father: "Archie, darling, this is father" (002-133). The seven words bridge the novel's two worlds — the world of English charm and the world of American authority — and Lucille, in speaking them, occupies the position she will hold throughout the novel: mediator, translator, and the only person capable of managing both men.

The confrontation that follows is a masterpiece of comic awkwardness. Brewster, "having acquired Archie for a son-in-law" (002-117), finds himself face to face with a young man who "combined in almost equal proportions the qualities of imbecile and crook" (002-122). Archie, for his part, does his best to be agreeable — and succeeds only in deepening the offense.

## Aftermath

The revelation transforms the novel's stakes. Before Section 2, Archie's criticism of the Cosmopolis was a social offense; after Section 2, it is a family wound. Every subsequent attempt Archie makes to placate Brewster is shadowed by the knowledge that he began their relationship with two unforgivable acts: marrying without permission and criticizing the hotel that was Brewster's life's work.
