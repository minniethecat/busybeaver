## Section 23

[023-001] “Nowhere just at present. I thought of taking one of those

[023-002] self-contained Park benches.”

[023-003] “Are you broke?”

[023-004] “Am I!”

[023-005] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] was concerned.

[023-006] “You ought to get a job.”

[023-007] “I ought. But somehow I don’t seem able to.”

[023-008] “What did you do before [[world-war-i|the war]]?”

[023-009] “I’ve forgotten.”

[023-010] “Forgotten!”

[023-011] “Forgotten.”

[023-012] “How do you mean—forgotten? You can’t mean—_forgotten?_”

[023-013] “Yes. It’s quite gone.”

[023-014] “But I mean to say. You can’t have forgotten a thing like that.”

[023-015] “Can’t I! I’ve forgotten all sorts of things. Where I was born. How old

[023-016] I am. Whether I’m married or single. What my name is—”

[023-017] “Well, I’m dashed!” said [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], staggered. “But you remembered about

[023-018] giving me a bit of sausage outside St. Mihiel?”

[023-019] “No, I didn’t. I’m taking your word for it. For all I know you may be

[023-020] luring me into some den to rob me of my straw hat. I don’t know you

[023-021] from Adam. But I like your [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]]—especially the part about

[023-022] eating—and I’m taking a chance.”

[023-023] Archie was concerned.

[023-024] “Listen, [[old-bean|old bean]]. Make an effort. You must remember that sausage

[023-025] episode? It was just outside St. Mihiel, about five in the evening.

[023-026] Your little lot were lying next to my little lot, and we happened to

[023-027] meet, and I said ‘What ho!’ and you said ‘[[what-ho|Halloa]]!’ and I said ‘What ho!

[023-028] What ho!’ and you said ‘Have a bit of sausage?’ and I said ‘What ho!

[023-029] What ho! What _ho!_’”

[023-030] “The dialogue seems to have been darned sparkling but I don’t remember

[023-031] it. It must have been after that that I stopped one. I don’t seem quite

[023-032] to have caught up with myself since I got hit.”

[023-033] “Oh! That’s how you got that scar?”

[023-034] “No. I got that jumping through a plate-glass window in London on

[023-035] Armistice night.”

[023-036] “What on earth did you do that for?”

[023-037] “Oh, I don’t know. It seemed a good idea at the time.”

[023-038] “But if you can remember a thing like that, why can’t you remember your

[023-039] name?”

[023-040] “I remember everything that happened after I came out of hospital. It’s

[023-041] the part before that’s gone.”

[023-042] Archie patted him on the shoulder.

[023-043] “I know just what you want. You need a bit of quiet and repose, to

[023-044] think things over and so forth. You mustn’t go sleeping on Park

[023-045] benches. Won’t do at all. Not a bit like it. You must shift to the

[023-046] Cosmopolis. It isn’t half a bad spot, the old Cosmop. I didn’t like it

[023-047] much the first night I was there, because there was a dashed tap that

[023-048] went drip-drip-drip all night and kept me awake, but the place has its

[023-049] points.”

[023-050] “Is [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] giving free board and lodging these days?”

[023-051] “Rather! That’ll be all right. Well, this is the spot. We’ll start by

[023-052] trickling up to the old boy’s suite and looking over his

[023-053] reach-me-downs. I know the waiter on his floor. A very sound chappie.

[023-054] He’ll let us in with his pass-key.”

[023-055] And so it came about that Mr. [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]], returning to his suite

[023-056] in the middle of [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]] in order to find a paper dealing with the

[023-057] subject he was discussing with his guest, the architect of his new

[023-058] hotel, was aware of a murmur of voices behind the closed door of his

[023-059] bedroom. Recognising the accents of his son-in-law, he breathed an oath

[023-060] and charged in. He objected to Archie wandering at large about his

[023-061] suite.

[023-062] The sight that met his eyes when he opened the door did nothing to

[023-063] soothe him. The floor was a sea of clothes. There were coats on the

[023-064] chairs, trousers on the bed, shirts on the bookshelf. And in the middle

[023-065] of his welter stood Archie, with a man who, to Mr. Brewster’s heated

[023-066] eye, looked like a tramp comedian out of a burlesque show.

[023-067] “Great Godfrey!” ejaculated Mr. Brewster.

[023-068] Archie looked up with a friendly smile.

[023-069] “Oh, halloa-halloa!” he said, affably, “We were just glancing through

[023-070] your spare scenery to see if we couldn’t find something for my pal

[023-071] here. This is Mr. Brewster, my [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]], old man.”

[023-072] Archie scanned his relative’s twisted features. Something in his

[023-073] expression seemed not altogether encouraging. He decided that the

[023-074] negotiations had better be conducted in private. “One moment, old lad,”

[023-075] he said to his new friend. “I just want to have a little talk with my

[023-076] father-in-law in the other room. Just a little friendly business chat.

[023-077] You stay here.”

[023-078] In the other room Mr. Brewster turned on Archie like a wounded lion of

[023-079] the desert.

[023-080] “What the—!”

[023-081] Archie secured one of his coat-buttons and began to massage it

[023-082] affectionately.

[023-083] “Ought to have explained!” said Archie, “only didn’t want to interrupt

[023-084] your lunch. The sportsman on the horizon is a dear old pal of mine—”

[023-085] Mr. Brewster wrenched himself free.

[023-086] “What the devil do you mean, you worm, by bringing tramps into my

[023-087] bedroom and messing about with my clothes?”

[023-088] “That’s just what I’m trying to explain, if you’ll only listen. This

[023-089] bird is a bird I met in France during the war. He gave me a bit of

[023-090] sausage outside St. Mihiel—”

[023-091] “Damn you and him and the sausage!”

[023-092] “Absolutely. But listen. He can’t remember who he is or where he was

[023-093] born or what his name is, and he’s broke; so, dash it, I must look

[023-094] after him. You see, he gave me a bit of sausage.”

[023-095] Mr. Brewster’s frenzy gave way to an ominous calm.

[023-096] “I’ll give him two seconds to clear out of here. If he isn’t gone by

[023-097] then I’ll have him thrown out.”

[023-098] Archie was shocked.

[023-099] “You don’t mean that?”

[023-100] “I do mean that.”

[023-101] “But where is he to go?”

[023-102] “Outside.”

[023-103] “But you don’t understand. This chappie has lost his memory because he

[023-104] was wounded in the war. Keep that fact firmly fixed in the old bean. He

[023-105] fought for you. Fought and bled for you. Bled profusely, by Jove. _And_

[023-106] he saved my life!”

[023-107] “If I’d got nothing else against him, that would be enough.”

[023-108] “But you can’t sling a chappie out into the cold hard world who bled in

[023-109] gallons to make the world safe for the [[hotel-cosmopolis|Hotel Cosmopolis]].”

[023-110] Mr. Brewster looked ostentatiously at his watch.

[023-111] “Two seconds!” he said.

[023-112] There was a silence. Archie appeared to be thinking. “Right-o!” he said

[023-113] at last. “No need to get the wind up. I know where he can go. It’s just

[023-114] occurred to me I’ll put him up at my little shop.”

[023-115] The purple ebbed from Mr. Brewster’s face. Such was his emotion that he

[023-116] had forgotten that infernal shop. He sat down. There was more silence.

[023-117] “Oh, gosh!” said Mr. Brewster.

[023-118] “I knew you would be reasonable about it,” said Archie, approvingly.

[023-119] “Now, honestly, as man to man, how do we go?”

[023-120] “What do you want me to do?” growled Mr. Brewster.

[023-121] “I thought you might put the chappie up for a while, and give him a

[023-122] chance to look round and nose about a bit.”

[023-123] “I absolutely refuse to give any more loafers free board and lodging.”

[023-124] “Any _more?_”

[023-125] “Well, he would be the second, wouldn’t he?”

[023-126] Archie looked pained.

[023-127] “It’s true,” he said, “that when I first came here I was temporarily

[023-128] resting, so to speak; but didn’t I go right out and grab the

[023-129] managership of your new hotel? Positively!”

[023-130] “I will _not_ adopt this tramp.”

[023-131] “Well, find him a job, then.”

[023-132] “What sort of a job?”

[023-133] “Oh, any old sort.”

[023-134] “He can be a waiter if he likes.”

[023-135] “All right; I’ll put the matter before him.”

[023-136] He returned to the bedroom. [[sausage-chappie|The Sausage Chappie]] was gazing fondly into

[023-137] the mirror with a spotted tie draped round his neck.

[023-138] “I say, old top,” said Archie, apologetically, “the Emperor of the

[023-139] Blighters out yonder says you can have a job here as waiter, and he

[023-140] won’t do another dashed thing for you. How about it?”

[023-141] “Do waiters eat?”

[023-142] “I suppose so. Though, by Jove, come to think of it, I’ve never seen

[023-143] one at it.”

[023-144] “That’s good enough for me!” said the Sausage Chappie. “When do I

[023-145] begin?”

[023-146] CHAPTER XIX.

[023-147] REGGIE COMES TO LIFE

[023-148] The advantage of having plenty of time on one’s hands is that one has

[023-149] [[leisure-and-sports|leisure]] to attend to the affairs of all one’s circle of friends; and

[023-150] Archie, assiduously as he watched over the destinies of the Sausage

[023-151] Chappie, did not neglect the romantic needs of his brother-in-law Bill.

[023-152] A few days later, [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]], returning one morning to their mutual suite,

[023-153] found her husband seated in an upright chair at the table, an unusually

[023-154] stern expression on his amiable face. A large cigar was in the corner

[023-155] of his mouth. The fingers of one hand rested in the armhole of his

[023-156] waistcoat: with the other hand he tapped menacingly on the table.

[023-157] As she gazed upon him, wondering what could be the matter with him,

[023-158] Lucille was suddenly aware of Bill’s presence. He had emerged sharply

[023-159] from the bedroom and was walking briskly across the floor. He came to a

[023-160] halt in front of the table.

[023-161] “Father!” said Bill.

[023-162] Archie looked up sharply, frowning heavily over his cigar.

[023-163] “Well, my boy,” he said in a strange, rasping voice. “What is it? Speak

[023-164] up, my boy, speak up! Why the devil can’t you speak up? This is my busy

[023-165] day!”

[023-166] “What on earth are you doing?” asked Lucille.

[023-167] Archie waved her away with the large gesture of a man of blood and iron

[023-168] interrupted while concentrating.

[023-169] “Leave us, woman! We would be alone! Retire into the jolly old

[023-170] background and amuse yourself for a bit. Read a book. Do acrostics.

[023-171] Charge ahead, laddie.”

[023-172] “Father!” said Bill, again.

[023-173] “Yes, my boy, yes? What is it?”

[023-174] “Father!”

[023-175] Archie picked up the red-covered volume that lay on the table.

[023-176] “Half a mo’, old son. Sorry to stop you, but I knew there was

[023-177] something. I’ve just remembered. Your walk. All wrong!”

[023-178] “All wrong?”

[023-179] “All wrong! Where’s the chapter on the Art. of Walking? Here we are.

[023-180] Listen, dear old soul. Drink this in. ‘In walking, one should strive to

[023-181] acquire that swinging, easy movement from the hips. The

[023-182] correctly-poised walker seems to float along, as it were.’ Now, old

[023-183] bean, you didn’t float a dam’ bit. You just galloped in like a chappie

[023-184] charging into a railway restaurant for a bowl of soup when his train

[023-185] leaves in two minutes. Dashed important, this walking business, you

[023-186] know. Get started wrong, and where are you? Try it again.... Much

[023-187] better.” He turned to Lucille. “Notice him float along that time?

[023-188] Absolutely skimmed, what?”

[023-189] Lucille had taken a seat,-and was waiting for enlightenment.

[023-190] “Are you and Bill going into vaudeville?” she asked.

[023-191] Archie, scrutinising-his-brother-in-law closely, had further criticism

[023-192] to make.

[023-193] “‘The man of self-respect and self-confidence,’” he read, “‘stands

[023-194] erect in an easy, natural, graceful attitude. Heels not too far apart,

[023-195] head erect, eyes to the front with a level gaze’—get your gaze level,

[023-196] old thing!—‘shoulders thrown back, arms hanging naturally at the sides

[023-197] when not otherwise employed’—that means that, if he tries to hit you,

[023-198] it’s all right to guard—‘chest expanded naturally, and abdomen’—this is

[023-199] no place for you, Lucille. Leg it out of earshot—‘ab—what I said

[023-200] before—drawn in somewhat and above all not protruded.’ Now, have you

[023-201] got all that? Yes, you look all right. Carry on, laddie, carry on.

[023-202] Let’s have two-penn’orth of the Dynamic Voice and the Tone of

[023-203] Authority—some of the full, rich, round stuff we hear so much about!”

[023-204] Bill fastened a gimlet eye upon his brother-in-law and drew a deep

[023-205] breath.

[023-206] “Father!” he said. “Father!”

[023-207] “You’ll have to brighten up Bill’s dialogue a lot,” said Lucille,

[023-208] critically, “or you will never get bookings.”

[023-209] “Father!”

[023-210] “I mean, it’s all right as far as it goes, but it’s sort of monotonous.

[023-211] Besides, one of you ought to be asking questions and the other

[023-212] answering. Bill ought to be saying, ‘Who was that lady I saw you coming

[023-213] down the street with?’ so that you would be able to say, ‘That wasn’t a

[023-214] lady. That was my wife.’ I _know!_ I’ve been to lots of vaudeville

[023-215] shows.”
