# Archie's Lemon-Coloured Bathing Suit

A two-piece bathing suit of vivid lemon colour, worn by Archie Moffam during the early chapters of his stay at the Hotel Cosmopolis. The garment becomes a running comic motif, serving — in Archie's own words — as "the pivot of the whole" affair. Few items of clothing in the Wodehouse canon achieve the narrative prominence of this suit: it is simultaneously a costume, a plot engine, and a running joke.

## Description

The suit is described as a two-piece garment of a "vivid lemon colour" (003-154). Archie regards it with acute discomfort, referring to it as a "lemon-coloured contrivance" (006-003). Its garish hue and Archie's self-consciousness in wearing it combine to produce a series of comic predicaments. The suit's function is to render its wearer ridiculous — a purpose it fulfills with unwavering efficiency across three chapters.

> "necessary for Archie to drape his reluctant form in a two-piece bathing suit of a vivid lemon colour; for he was supposed to be representing—" (003-153–154)

The word "drape" is characteristic: Archie does not "wear" the suit so much as submit to it, his body becoming a passive support for a garment that dominates him. The verb "reluctant" applied to "form" rather than "Archie" extends the comedy from the man to his very physique — even his body, it seems, resists the suit.

## Role in the Plot

The bathing suit is the catalyst for one of Archie's earliest embarrassments at the Hotel Cosmopolis. Required to wear it for an activity at the hotel — modeling for a magazine cover illustration — Archie dons the suit and ventures out into the public spaces of the establishment, acutely aware of his ridiculous appearance.

> "here he was, out in the world, alone, in a lemon-coloured—" (004-040)

His appearance in the suit outside a fellow guest's door leads to a comic misunderstanding. Archie is discovered singing "song-hits in a lemon-coloured bathing suit" (004-091) outside J. B. Wheeler's room — an image of absurdity that sets the tone for his subsequent misadventures. The scene crystallizes the novel's comic method: take a man of essentially conventional dignity, dress him in absurd attire, and place him in a situation that demands gravitas.

Archie himself later reflects on the suit's narrative importance with characteristic hyperbole:

> "The bathing suit's what you might call the good old pivot of the whole thing" (006-019)

The word "pivot" is a joke within a joke. Archie, attempting to explain his predicament to an unsympathetic listener, elevates a piece of beachwear to the status of narrative linchpin — and in doing so, accidentally tells the truth. The suit really is the pivot: without it, the chain of misunderstandings that drives the early chapters would have no first link.

The garment recurs as a comic refrain through chapters 3, 4, and 6, with each mention compounding the absurdity of Archie's situation. Wodehouse never lets the reader forget the suit; it lurks in the background of every scene, a yellow spectre waiting to be invoked.

Archie's relationship with the suit is one of reluctant partnership: he needs it (for the magazine cover), resents it (for its colour), and cannot escape it (for narrative reasons). ## Comic Significance

The lemon-coloured bathing suit exemplifies Wodehouse's gift for elevating a mundane object into a comic engine. The suit's garishness contrasts with Archie's gentlemanly self-image, creating a visual gag that recurs across multiple chapters. Archie's retrospective framing of the suit as "the pivot of the whole thing" (006-019) is itself a joke — elevating a trivial garment to mock-heroic narrative significance.

The suit belongs to a distinguished tradition in Wodehouse: the comic object that takes on a life of its own. Like the pig in *Summer Lightning* or the cow-creamer in *The Code of the Woosters*, Archie's bathing suit transcends its material nature to become a narrative force. Unlike those objects, however, the suit is worn — it cannot be stolen, hidden, or swapped; it must be inhabited. This corporeal dimension adds a layer of physical comedy absent from the portable-object plots: Archie cannot simply put the suit down and walk away; wherever he goes, the suit goes with him.
