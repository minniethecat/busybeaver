## Section 36

[036-001] There are certain moods in which one loses count of time, and [[archie-moffam|Archie]]

[036-002] could not have said how long he had been sitting in the deep arm-chair

[036-003] near the window when he first became aware that he was not alone in the

[036-004] room. He had closed his eyes, the better to meditate, so had not seen

[036-005] anyone enter. Nor had he heard the door open. The first intimation he

[036-006] had that somebody had come in was when some hard substance knocked

[036-007] against some other hard object, producing a sharp sound which brought

[036-008] him back to earth with a jerk.

[036-009] He sat up silently. The fact that the room was still in darkness made

[036-010] it obvious that something nefarious was afoot. Plainly there was dirty

[036-011] work in preparation at the cross-roads. He stared into the blackness,

[036-012] and, as his eyes grew accustomed to it, was presently able to see an

[036-013] indistinct form bending over something on the floor. The sound of

[036-014] rather stertorous breathing came to him.

[036-015] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] had many defects which prevented him being the perfect man, but

[036-016] lack of courage was not one of them. His somewhat rudimentary

[036-017] intelligence had occasionally led his superior officers during [[world-war-i|the war]]

[036-018] to thank God that Great Britain had a Navy, but even these stern

[036-019] critics had found nothing to complain of in the manner in which he

[036-020] bounded over the top. Some of us are thinkers, others men of action.

[036-021] Archie was a man of action, and he was out of his chair and sailing in

[036-022] the direction of the back of the intruder’s neck before a wiser man

[036-023] would have completed his plan of campaign. The miscreant collapsed

[036-024] under him with a squashy sound, like the wind going out of a pair of

[036-025] bellows, and Archie, taking a firm seat on his spine, rubbed the

[036-026] other’s face in the carpet and awaited the progress of events.

[036-027] At the end of half a minute it became apparent that there was going to

[036-028] be no counter-attack. The dashing swiftness of the assault had

[036-029] apparently had the effect of depriving the marauder of his entire stock

[036-030] of breath. He was gurgling to himself in a pained sort of way and

[036-031] making no effort to rise. Archie, feeling that it would be safe to get

[036-032] up and switch on the light, did so, and, turning after completing this

[036-033] manoeuvre, was greeted by the spectacle of his [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]], seated on

[036-034] the floor in a breathless and dishevelled condition, blinking at the

[036-035] sudden illumination. On the carpet beside Mr. Brewster lay a long

[036-036] knife, and beside the knife lay the handsomely framed masterpiece of J.

[036-037] B. [[j-b-wheeler|Wheeler]]’s fiancée, Miss [[alice-wigmore|Alice Wigmore]]. Archie stared at this

[036-038] collection dumbly.

[036-039] “Oh, [[what-ho]]!” he observed at length, feebly.

[036-040] A distinct chill manifested itself in the region of Archie’s spine.

[036-041] This could mean only one thing. His fears had been realised. The strain

[036-042] of modern life, with all its hustle and excitement, had at last proved

[036-043] too much for Mr. Brewster. Crushed by the thousand and one anxieties

[036-044] and worries of a millionaire’s existence, Daniel Brewster had gone off

[036-045] his onion.

[036-046] Archie was nonplussed. This was his first experience of this kind of

[036-047] thing. What, he asked himself, was the proper procedure in a situation

[036-048] of this sort? What was the local rule? Where, in a word, did he go from

[036-049] here? He was still musing in an embarrassed and baffled way, having

[036-050] taken the precaution of kicking the knife under the sofa, when Mr.

[036-051] Brewster spoke. And there was in, both the words and the method of

[036-052] their delivery so much of his old familiar self that Archie felt quite

[036-053] relieved.

[036-054] “So it’s you, is it, you wretched blight, you miserable weed!” said Mr.

[036-055] Brewster, having recovered enough breath to be going on with. He

[036-056] glowered at his son-in-law despondently. “I might have expected it! If

[036-057] I was at the North Pole, I could count on you butting in!”

[036-058] “Shall I get you a drink of water?” said Archie.

[036-059] “What the devil,” demanded Mr. Brewster, “do you imagine I want with a

[036-060] drink of water?”

[036-061] “Well—” Archie hesitated delicately. “I had a sort of idea that you had

[036-062] been feeling the strain a bit. I mean to say, rush of modern life and

[036-063] all that sort of thing—”

[036-064] “What are you doing in my room?” said Mr. Brewster, changing the

[036-065] subject.

[036-066] “Well, I came to tell you something, and I came in here and was waiting

[036-067] for you, and I saw some chappie biffing about in the dark, and I

[036-068] thought it was a burglar or something after some of your things, so,

[036-069] thinking it over, I got the idea that it would be a fairly juicy scheme

[036-070] to land on him with both feet. No idea it was you, old thing!

[036-071] Frightfully sorry and all that. Meant well!”

[036-072] Mr. Brewster sighed deeply. He was a just man, and he could not but

[036-073] realise that, in the circumstances, Archie had behaved not unnaturally.

[036-074] “Oh, well!” he said. “I might have known something would go wrong.”

[036-075] “Awfully sorry!”

[036-076] “It can’t be helped. What was it you wanted to tell me?” He eyed his

[036-077] son-in-law piercingly. “Not a cent over twenty dollars!” he said

[036-078] coldly.

[036-079] Archie hastened to dispel the pardonable error.

[036-080] “Oh, it wasn’t anything like that,” he said. “As a matter of fact, I

[036-081] think it’s a good egg. It has bucked me up to no inconsiderable degree.

[036-082] I was [[dining-and-social-ritual|dining]] with [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] just now, and, as we dallied with the

[036-083] food-stuffs, she told me something which—well, I’m bound to say, it

[036-084] made me feel considerably braced. She told me to trot along and ask you

[036-085] if you would mind—”

[036-086] “I gave Lucille a hundred dollars only last Tuesday.”

[036-087] Archie was pained.

[036-088] “Adjust this sordid outlook, old thing!” he urged. “You simply aren’t

[036-089] anywhere near it. Right off the target, absolutely! What Lucille told

[036-090] me to ask you was if you would mind—at some tolerably near date—being a

[036-091] grandfather! Rotten thing to be, of course,” proceeded Archie

[036-092] commiseratingly, “for a chappie of your age, but there it is!”

[036-093] Mr. Brewster gulped.

[036-094] “Do you mean to say—?”

[036-095] “I mean, apt to make a fellow feel a bit of a [[fatherhood-and-authority|patriarch]]. Snowy hair and

[036-096] what not. And, of course, for a chappie in the prime of life like you—”

[036-097] “Do you mean to tell me—? Is this true?”

[036-098] “Absolutely! Of course, speaking for myself, I’m all for it. I don’t

[036-099] know when I’ve felt more bucked. I sang as I came up here—absolutely

[036-100] warbled in the elevator. But you—”

[036-101] A curious change had come over Mr. Brewster. He was one of those men

[036-102] who have the [[appearance-and-first-impressions|appearance]] of having been hewn out of the solid rock, but

[036-103] now in some indescribable way he seemed to have melted. For a moment he

[036-104] gazed at Archie, then, moving quickly forward, he grasped his hand in

[036-105] an iron grip.

[036-106] “This is the best news I’ve ever had!” he mumbled.

[036-107] “Awfully good of you to take it like this,” said Archie cordially. “I

[036-108] mean, being a grandfather—”

[036-109] Mr. Brewster smiled. Of a man of his appearance one could hardly say

[036-110] that he smiled playfully; but there was something in his expression

[036-111] that remotely suggested playfulness.

[036-112] “My [[old-bean|dear old bean]],” he said.

[036-113] Archie started.

[036-114] “My dear old bean,” repeated Mr. Brewster firmly, “I’m the happiest man

[036-115] in America!” His eye fell on [[the-wheeler-painting|the picture]] which lay on the floor. He

[036-116] gave a slight shudder, but recovered himself immediately. “After this,”

[036-117] he said, “I can reconcile myself to living with that thing for the rest

[036-118] of my life. I feel it doesn’t matter.”

[036-119] “I say,” said Archie, “how about that? Wouldn’t have brought the thing

[036-120] up if you hadn’t introduced the topic, but, speaking as man to man,

[036-121] what the dickens WERE you up to when I landed on your spine just now?”

[036-122] “I suppose you thought I had gone off my head?”

[036-123] “Well, I’m bound to say—”

[036-124] Mr. Brewster cast an unfriendly glance at the picture.

[036-125] “Well, I had every excuse, after living with that infernal thing for a

[036-126] week!”

[036-127] Archie looked at him, astonished.

[036-128] “I say, old thing, I don’t know if I have got your meaning exactly, but

[036-129] you somehow give me the impression that you don’t like that jolly old

[036-130] work of Art.”

[036-131] “Like it!” cried Mr. Brewster. “It’s nearly driven me mad! Every time

[036-132] it caught my eye, it gave me a pain in the neck. To-night I felt as if

[036-133] I couldn’t stand it any longer. I didn’t want to hurt Lucille’s

[036-134] feelings, by telling her, so I made up my mind I would cut the damned

[036-135] thing out of its frame and tell her it had been stolen.”

[036-136] “What an extraordinary thing! Why, that’s exactly what old Wheeler

[036-137] did.”

[036-138] “Who is old Wheeler?”

[036-139] “Artist chappie. Pal of mine. His fiancée painted the thing, and, when

[036-140] I lifted it off him, he told her it had been stolen. _He_ didn’t seem

[036-141] frightfully keen on it, either.”

[036-142] “Your friend Wheeler has evidently good taste.”

[036-143] Archie was thinking.

[036-144] “Well, all this rather gets past me,” he said. “Personally, I’ve always

[036-145] admired the thing. Dashed ripe bit of work, I’ve always considered.

[036-146] Still, of course, if you feel that way—”

[036-147] “You may take it from me that I do!”

[036-148] “Well, then, in that case—You know what a clumsy devil I am—You can

[036-149] tell Lucille it was all my fault—”

[036-150] [[the-wigmore-venus|The Wigmore Venus]] smiled up at Archie—it seemed to Archie with a

[036-151] pathetic, pleading smile. For a moment he was conscious of a feeling of

[036-152] guilt; then, closing his eyes and hardening his heart, he sprang

[036-153] lightly in the air and descended with both feet on the picture. There

[036-154] was a sound of rending canvas, and the Venus ceased to smile.

[036-155] “Golly!” said Archie, regarding the wreckage remorsefully.

[036-156] Mr. Brewster did not share his remorse. For the second time that night

[036-157] he gripped him by the hand.

[036-158] “My boy!” he quavered. He stared at Archie as if he were seeing him

[036-159] with new eyes. “My dear boy, you were through the war, were you not?”

[036-160] “Eh? Oh yes! Right through the jolly old war.”

[036-161] “What was your rank?”

[036-162] “Oh, second lieutenant.”

[036-163] “You ought to have been a general!” Mr. Brewster clasped his hand once

[036-164] more in a vigorous embrace. “I only [[hope-and-optimism|hope]],” he added “that your son will

[036-165] be like you!”

[036-166] There are certain compliments, or compliments coming from certain

[036-167] sources, before which modesty reels, stunned. Archie’s did.

[036-168] He swallowed convulsively. He had never thought to hear these words

[036-169] from Daniel Brewster.

[036-170] “How would it be, old thing,” he said almost brokenly, “if you and I

[036-171] trickled down to the bar and had a spot of sherbet?”
