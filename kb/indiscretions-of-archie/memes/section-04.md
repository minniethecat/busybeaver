## Section 4

[004-001] There was not a great deal in the paper to interest him. The usual

[004-002] bond-robbery had taken place on the previous day, and the police were

[004-003] reported hot on the trail of the Master-Mind who was alleged to be at

[004-004] the back of these financial operations. A messenger named Henry Babcock

[004-005] had been arrested and was expected to become confidential. To one who,

[004-006] like [[archie-moffam|Archie]], had never owned a bond, the story made little appeal. He

[004-007] turned with more interest to a cheery half-column on the activities of

[004-008] a [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] in Minnesota who, with what seemed to [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], as he thought

[004-009] of Mr. [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]], a good deal of resource and public spirit, had

[004-010] recently beaned his [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]] with the family meat-axe. It was only

[004-011] after he had read this through twice in a spirit of gentle approval

[004-012] that it occurred to him that [[j-b-wheeler|J. B. Wheeler]] was uncommonly late at the

[004-013] tryst. He looked at his watch, and found that he had been in the studio

[004-014] three-quarters of an hour.

[004-015] Archie became restless. Long-suffering [[old-bean|old bean]] though he was, he

[004-016] considered this a bit thick. He got up and went out on to the landing,

[004-017] to see if there were any signs of the blighter. There were none. He

[004-018] began to understand now what had happened. For some reason or other the

[004-019] bally artist was not coming to the studio at all that day. Probably he

[004-020] had called up the hotel and left a message to this effect, and Archie

[004-021] had just missed it. Another man might have waited to make certain that

[004-022] his message had reached its destination, but not woollen-headed

[004-023] Wheeler, the most casual individual in [[new-york-city|New York]].

[004-024] Thoroughly aggrieved, Archie turned back to the studio to [[appearance-and-first-impressions|dress]] and go

[004-025] away.

[004-026] His progress was stayed by a solid, forbidding slab of oak. Somehow or

[004-027] other, since he had left the room, the door had managed to get itself

[004-028] shut.

[004-029] “Oh, dash it!” said Archie.

[004-030] The mildness of the expletive was proof that the full horror of the

[004-031] situation had not immediately come home to him. His mind in the first

[004-032] few moments was occupied with the problem of how the door had got that

[004-033] way. He could not remember shutting it. Probably he had done it

[004-034] unconsciously. As a child, he had been taught by sedulous elders that

[004-035] the little gentleman always closed doors behind him, and presumably his

[004-036] subconscious self was still under the influence. And then, suddenly, he

[004-037] realised that this infernal, officious ass of a subconscious self had

[004-038] deposited him right in the gumbo. Behind that closed door, unattainable

[004-039] as youthful ambition, lay his gent’s heather-mixture with the green

[004-040] twill, and here he was, out in the world, alone, in a lemon-coloured

[004-041] bathing suit.

[004-042] In all crises of human affairs there are two broad courses open to a

[004-043] man. He can stay where he is or he can go elsewhere. Archie, leaning on

[004-044] the banisters, examined these alternatives narrowly. If he stayed where

[004-045] he was he would have to spend the night on this dashed landing. If he

[004-046] legged it, in this kit, he would be gathered up by the constabulary

[004-047] before he had gone a hundred yards. He was no pessimist, but he was

[004-048] reluctantly forced to the conclusion that he was up against it.

[004-049] It was while he was musing with a certain tenseness on these things

[004-050] that the sound of footsteps came to him from below. But almost in the

[004-051] first instant the [[hope-and-optimism|hope]] that this might be J. B. Wheeler, the curse of

[004-052] the human race, died away. Whoever was coming up the stairs was

[004-053] running, and J. B. Wheeler never ran upstairs. He was not one of your

[004-054] lean, haggard, spiritual-looking geniuses. He made a large income with

[004-055] his brush and pencil, and spent most of it in creature comforts. This

[004-056] couldn’t be J. B. Wheeler.

[004-057] It was not. It was a tall, thin man whom he had never seen before. He

[004-058] appeared to be in a considerable hurry. He let himself into the studio

[004-059] on the floor below, and vanished without even waiting to shut the door.

[004-060] He had come and disappeared in almost record time, but, brief though

[004-061] his passing had been, it had been long enough to bring consolation to

[004-062] Archie. A sudden bright light had been vouchsafed to Archie, and he now

[004-063] saw an admirably ripe and fruity scheme for ending his troubles. What

[004-064] could be simpler than to toddle down one flight of stairs and in an

[004-065] easy and debonair manner ask the chappie’s permission to use his

[004-066] telephone? And what could be simpler, once he was at the ’phone, than

[004-067] to get in touch with somebody at [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] who would send down a

[004-068] few trousers and what not in a kit bag. It was a priceless solution,

[004-069] thought Archie, as he made his way downstairs. Not even embarrassing,

[004-070] he meant to say. This chappie, living in a place like this, wouldn’t

[004-071] bat an eyelid at the spectacle of a fellow trickling about the place in

[004-072] a bathing suit. They would have a good laugh about the whole thing.

[004-073] “I say, I hate to bother you—dare say you’re busy and all that sort of

[004-074] thing—but would you mind if I popped in for half a second and used your

[004-075] ’phone?”

[004-076] That was the speech, the extremely gentlemanly and well-phrased speech

[004-077] which Archie had prepared to deliver the moment the man appeared. The

[004-078] reason he did not deliver it was that the man did not appear. He

[004-079] knocked, but nothing stirred.

[004-080] “I say!”

[004-081] Archie now perceived that the door was ajar, and that on an envelope

[004-082] attached with a tack to one of the panels was the name “[[elmer-m-moon|Elmer M. Moon]]”

[004-083] He pushed the door a little farther open and tried again.

[004-084] “Oh, Mr. Moon! Mr. Moon!” He waited a moment. “Oh, Mr. Moon! Mr. Moon!

[004-085] Are you there, Mr. Moon?”

[004-086] He blushed hotly. To his sensitive ear the words had sounded exactly

[004-087] like the opening line of the refrain of a vaudeville song-hit. He

[004-088] decided to waste no further speech on a man with such an unfortunate

[004-089] surname until he could see him face to face and get a chance of

[004-090] lowering his voice a bit. Absolutely absurd to stand outside a

[004-091] chappie’s door singing song-hits in a [[archies-lemon-coloured-bathing-suit|lemon-coloured bathing suit]]. He

[004-092] pushed the door open and walked in; and his subconscious self, always

[004-093] the gentleman, closed it gently behind him.

[004-094] “Up!” said a low, sinister, harsh, unfriendly, and unpleasant voice.

[004-095] “Eh?” said Archie, revolving sharply on his axis.

[004-096] He found himself confronting the hurried gentleman who had run

[004-097] upstairs. This sprinter had produced an [[miss-silvertons-pistol|automatic pistol]], and was

[004-098] pointing it in a truculent manner at his head. Archie stared at his

[004-099] host, and his host stared at him.

[004-100] “Put your hands up,” he said.

[004-101] “Oh, right-o! Absolutely!” said Archie. “But I mean to say—”

[004-102] The other was drinking him in with considerable astonishment. Archie’s

[004-103] costume seemed to have made a powerful impression upon him.

[004-104] “Who the devil are you?” he enquired.

[004-105] “Me? Oh, my name’s—”

[004-106] “Never mind your name. What are you doing here?”

[004-107] “Well, as a matter of fact, I popped in to ask if I might use your

[004-108] ’phone. You see—”

[004-109] A certain relief seemed to temper the austerity of the other’s gaze. As

[004-110] a visitor, Archie, though surprising, seemed to be better than he had

[004-111] expected.

[004-112] “I don’t know what to do with you,” he said, meditatively.

[004-113] “If you’d just let me toddle to the ’phone—”

[004-114] “Likely!” said the man. He appeared to reach a decision. “Here, go into

[004-115] that room.”

[004-116] He indicated with a jerk of his head the open door of what was

[004-117] apparently a bedroom at the farther end of the studio.

[004-118] “I take it,” said Archie, chattily, “that all this may seem to you not

[004-119] a little rummy.”

[004-120] “Get on!”

[004-121] “I was only saying—”

[004-122] “Well, I haven’t time to listen. Get a move on!”

[004-123] The bedroom was in a state of untidiness which eclipsed anything which

[004-124] Archie had ever witnessed. The other appeared to be moving house. Bed,

[004-125] furniture, and floor were covered with articles of clothing. A silk

[004-126] shirt wreathed itself about Archie’s ankles as he stood gaping, and, as

[004-127] he moved farther into the room, his path was paved with ties and

[004-128] collars.

[004-129] “Sit down!” said Elmer M. Moon, abruptly.

[004-130] “Right-o! Thanks,” said Archie, “I suppose you wouldn’t like me to

[004-131] explain, and what not, what?”

[004-132] “No!” said Mr. Moon. “I haven’t got your spare time. Put your hands

[004-133] behind that chair.”

[004-134] Archie did so, and found them immediately secured by what felt like a

[004-135] silk tie. His assiduous host then proceeded to fasten his ankles in a

[004-136] like manner. This done, he seemed to feel that he had done all that was

[004-137] required of him, and he returned to the packing of a large suitcase

[004-138] which stood by the window.

[004-139] “I say!” said Archie.

[004-140] Mr. Moon, with the air of a man who has remembered something which he

[004-141] had overlooked, shoved a sock in his guest’s mouth and resumed his

[004-142] packing. He was what might be called an impressionist packer. His aim

[004-143] appeared to be speed rather than neatness. He bundled his belongings

[004-144] in, closed the bag with some difficulty, and, stepping to the window,

[004-145] opened it. Then he climbed out on to the fire-escape, dragged the

[004-146] suit-case after him, and was gone.

[004-147] Archie, left alone, addressed himself to the task of freeing his

[004-148] prisoned limbs. The job proved much easier than he had expected. Mr.

[004-149] Moon, that hustler, had wrought for the moment, not for all time. A

[004-150] practical man, he had been content to keep his visitor shackled merely

[004-151] for such a period as would permit him to make his escape unhindered. In

[004-152] less than ten minutes Archie, after a good deal of snake-like writhing,

[004-153] was pleased to discover that the thingummy attached to his wrists had

[004-154] loosened sufficiently to enable him to use his hands. He untied himself

[004-155] and got up.

[004-156] He now began to tell himself that out of evil cometh good. His

[004-157] encounter with the elusive Mr. Moon had not been an agreeable one, but

[004-158] it had had this solid advantage, that it had left him right in the

[004-159] middle of a great many clothes. And Mr. Moon, whatever his moral

[004-160] defects, had the one excellent quality of taking about the same size as

[004-161] himself. Archie, casting a covetous eye upon a tweed suit which lay on

[004-162] the bed, was on the point of climbing into the trousers when on the

[004-163] outer door of the studio there sounded a forceful knocking.

[004-164] “Open up here!”

[004-165] CHAPTER VI.

[004-166] THE BOMB

[004-167] Archie bounded silently out into the other room and stood listening

[004-168] tensely. He was not a naturally querulous man, but he did feel at this

[004-169] point that Fate was picking on him with a somewhat undue severity.

[004-170] “In th’ name av th’ Law!”

[004-171] There are times when the best of us lose our heads. At this juncture

[004-172] Archie should undoubtedly have gone to the door, opened it, explained

[004-173] his presence in a few well-chosen words, and generally have passed the

[004-174] whole thing off with ready tact. But the thought of confronting a posse

[004-175] of police in his present costume caused him to look earnestly about him

[004-176] for a hiding-place.

[004-177] Up against the farther wall was a settee with a high, arching back,

[004-178] which might have been put there for that special purpose. He inserted

[004-179] himself behind this, just as a splintering crash announced that the

[004-180] Law, having gone through the formality of knocking with its knuckles,

[004-181] was now getting busy with an axe. A moment later the door had given

[004-182] way, and the room was full of trampling feet. Archie wedged himself

[004-183] against the wall with the quiet concentration of a clam nestling in its

[004-184] shell, and hoped for the best.

[004-185] It seemed to him that his immediate future depended for better or for

[004-186] worse entirely on the native intelligence of the Force. If they were

[004-187] the bright, alert men he hoped they were, they would see all that junk

[004-188] in the bedroom and, deducing from it that their quarry had stood not

[004-189] upon the order of his going but had hopped it, would not waste time in

[004-190] searching a presumably empty apartment. If, on the other hand, they

[004-191] were the obtuse, flat-footed persons who occasionally find their way

[004-192] into the ranks of even the most enlightened constabularies, they would

[004-193] undoubtedly shift the settee and drag him into a publicity from which

[004-194] his modest soul shrank. He was enchanted, therefore, a few moments

[004-195] later, to hear a gruff voice state that th’ mutt had beaten it down th’

[004-196] fire-escape. His opinion of the detective abilities of the New York

[004-197] police force rose with a bound.
