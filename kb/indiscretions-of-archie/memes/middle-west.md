# The Middle West

The broad central region of the United States, depicted in the novel as a cultural counterpoint to New York sophistication—a place from which earnest, unsophisticated characters emerge to encounter the complexities of metropolitan life.

## In the Novel

The Middle West appears as a signifier of provincial origin and small-town earnestness. In chapter 31, a young woman is described as coming "from the Middle West" and "trying to be" a [[greenwich-village|Greenwich Village]] bohemian after reading magazine stories about New York's artistic quarter (031-143). A character remarks approvingly on a "good old Middle West name" (031-206), suggesting the region connotes authenticity and straightforwardness. In chapter 33, reference is made to "the female denizens of the Middle West" (033-045), reinforcing the region's association with a particular type—provincial, aspiring, slightly comic in their attempts at cosmopolitanism.

Wodehouse's treatment is characteristically gentle: the Middle Westerner is not viciously satirized but affectionately observed, their foibles understood as the natural consequence of moving from a small pond to a large one. The region functions in the novel's geography of values much as the English provinces do in Wodehouse's Blandings stories—as a foil to metropolitan sophistication.

## Description

No physical description of the Middle West is provided in the novel. The region exists in the text as a cultural construct rather than a geographic reality—"the Middle West" is shorthand for a set of perceived traits: earnestness, naivete, ambition, and a slightly comic eagerness to shed provincial ways.

## Associated Characters

- Various unnamed young women described as "female denizens of the Middle West" (033-045)

## Real-World Context

In the 1920s, the American Middle West was undergoing rapid cultural transformation. Magazine culture, radio, and the movies had begun to erode regional isolation, creating a new type: the small-town striver who absorbed cosmopolitan tastes through mass media and attempted to perform them, often imperfectly. Wodehouse captures this moment with precision—the girl who "reads magazine stories" about Greenwich Village and models herself accordingly is a recognizable product of the era. The comic gap between the earnest Middle Westerner's aspirations and her execution is part of the novel's broader sympathetic treatment of American striving.
