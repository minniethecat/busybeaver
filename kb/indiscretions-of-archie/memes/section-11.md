## Section 11

[011-001] When he entered the Art Galleries a few minutes later, [[archie-moffam|Archie]] was glad

[011-002] of the moral support of even such a wobbly reed as [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]].

[011-003] There is something about an auction room which weighs heavily upon the

[011-004] novice. The hushed interior was bathed in a dim, religious light; and

[011-005] the congregation, seated on small wooden chairs, gazed in reverent

[011-006] silence at the pulpit, where a [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] of commanding presence and

[011-007] sparkling pince-nez was delivering a species of chant. Behind a gold

[011-008] curtain at the end of the room mysterious forms flitted to and fro.

[011-009] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], who had been expecting something on the lines of the [[new-york-city|New York]]

[011-010] Stock Exchange, which he had once been privileged to visit when it was

[011-011] in a more than usually feverish mood, found the atmosphere oppressively

[011-012] ecclesiastical. He sat down and looked about him. The presiding priest

[011-013] went on with his chant.

[011-014] “Sixteen-sixteen-sixteen-sixteen-sixteen—worth three

[011-015] hundred—sixteen-sixteen-sixteen-sixteen-sixteen—ought to bring five

[011-016] hundred—sixteen-sixteen-seventeen-seventeen-eighteen-eighteen

[011-017] nineteen-nineteen-nineteen.”

[011-018] He stopped and eyed the worshippers with a glittering and reproachful

[011-019] eye. They had, it seemed, disappointed him. His lips curled, and he

[011-020] waved a hand towards a grimly uncomfortable-looking chair with insecure

[011-021] legs and a good deal of gold paint about it. “Gentlemen! Ladies and

[011-022] gentlemen! You are not here to waste my time; I am not here to waste

[011-023] yours. Am I seriously offered nineteen dollars for this

[011-024] eighteenth-century chair, acknowledged to be the finest piece sold in

[011-025] New York for months and months? Am I—twenty? I thank you.

[011-026] Twenty-twenty-twenty-twenty. _Your_ opportunity! Priceless. Very few

[011-027] extant. Twenty-five-five-five-five-thirty-thirty. Just what you are

[011-028] looking for. The only one in the City of New York.

[011-029] Thirty-five-five-five-five. Forty-forty-forty-forty-forty. Look at

[011-030] those legs! Back it into the light, Willie. Let the light fall on those

[011-031] legs!”

[011-032] Willie, a sort of acolyte, manœuvred the chair as directed. Reggie van

[011-033] Tuyl, who had been yawning in a hopeless sort of way, showed his first

[011-034] flicker of interest.

[011-035] “Willie,” he observed, eyeing that youth more with pity than reproach,

[011-036] “has a face like Jo-Jo the dog-faced boy, don’t you think so?”

[011-037] Archie nodded briefly. Precisely the same criticism had occurred to

[011-038] him.

[011-039] “Forty-five-five-five-five-five,” chanted the high-priest. “Once

[011-040] forty-five. Twice forty-five. Third and last call, forty-five. Sold at

[011-041] forty-five. Gentleman in the fifth row.”

[011-042] Archie looked up and down the row with a keen eye. He was anxious to

[011-043] see who had been chump enough to give forty-five dollars for such a

[011-044] frightful object. He became aware of the dog-faced Willie leaning

[011-045] towards him.

[011-046] “Name, please?” said the canine one.

[011-047] “Eh, what?” said Archie. “Oh, my name’s Moffam, don’t you know.” The

[011-048] eyes of the multitude made him feel a little nervous “Er—glad to meet

[011-049] you and all that sort of rot.”

[011-050] “Ten dollars deposit, please,” said Willie.

[011-051] “I don’t absolutely follow you, [[old-bean|old bean]]. What is the big thought at

[011-052] the back of all this?”

[011-053] “Ten dollars deposit on the chair.”

[011-054] “What chair?”

[011-055] “You bid forty-five dollars for the chair.”

[011-056] “Me?”

[011-057] “You nodded,” said Willie, accusingly. “If,” he went on, reasoning

[011-058] closely, “you didn’t want to bid, why did you nod?”

[011-059] Archie was embarrassed. He could, of course, have pointed out that he

[011-060] had merely nodded in adhesion to the statement that the other had a

[011-061] face like Jo-Jo the dog-faced boy; but something seemed to tell him

[011-062] that a purist might consider the excuse deficient in tact. He hesitated

[011-063] a moment, then handed over a ten-dollar bill, the price of Willie’s

[011-064] feelings. Willie withdrew like a tiger slinking from the body of its

[011-065] victim.

[011-066] “I say, old thing,” said Archie to Reggie, “this is a bit thick, you

[011-067] know. No purse will stand this drain.”

[011-068] Reggie considered the matter. His face seemed drawn under the mental

[011-069] strain.

[011-070] “Don’t nod again,” he advised. “If you aren’t careful, you get into the

[011-071] habit of it. When you want to bid, just twiddle your fingers. Yes,

[011-072] that’s the thing. Twiddle!”

[011-073] He sighed drowsily. The atmosphere of the auction room was close; you

[011-074] weren’t allowed to smoke; and altogether he was beginning to regret

[011-075] that he had come. The service continued. Objects of varying

[011-076] unattractiveness came and went, eulogised by the officiating priest,

[011-077] but coldly received by the congregation. Relations between the former

[011-078] and the latter were growing more and more distant. The congregation

[011-079] seemed to suspect the priest of having an ulterior motive in his

[011-080] eulogies, and the priest seemed to suspect the congregation of a

[011-081] frivolous desire to waste his time. He had begun to speculate openly as

[011-082] to why they were there at all. Once, when a particularly repellent

[011-083] statuette of a nude female with an unwholesome green skin had been

[011-084] offered at two dollars and had found no bidders—the congregation

[011-085] appearing silently grateful for his statement that it was the only

[011-086] specimen of its kind on the continent—he had specifically accused them

[011-087] of having come into the auction room merely with the purpose of sitting

[011-088] down and taking the weight off their feet.

[011-089] “If your thing—your whatever-it-is, doesn’t come up soon, Archie,” said

[011-090] Reggie, fighting off with an effort the mists of sleep, “I rather think

[011-091] I shall be toddling along. What was it you came to get?”

[011-092] “It’s rather difficult to describe. It’s a rummy-looking sort of

[011-093] what-not, made of china or something. I call it Pongo. At least, this

[011-094] one isn’t Pongo, don’t you know—it’s his little brother, but presumably

[011-095] equally foul in every respect. It’s all rather complicated, I know,

[011-096] but—hallo!” He pointed excitedly. “By Jove! We’re off! There it is!

[011-097] Look! Willie’s unleashing it now!”

[011-098] Willie, who had disappeared through the gold curtain, had now returned,

[011-099] and was placing on a pedestal a small china figure of delicate

[011-100] workmanship. It was the figure of a warrior in a suit of armour

[011-101] advancing with raised spear upon an adversary. A thrill permeated

[011-102] Archie’s frame. [[parker|Parker]] had not been mistaken. This was undoubtedly the

[011-103] companion-figure to the redoubtable Pongo. The two were identical. Even

[011-104] from where he sat Archie could detect on the features of the figure on

[011-105] the pedestal the same expression of insufferable complacency which had

[011-106] alienated his sympathies from the original Pongo.

[011-107] The high-priest, undaunted by previous rebuffs, regarded the figure

[011-108] with a gloating enthusiasm wholly unshared by the congregation, who

[011-109] were plainly looking upon Pongo’s little brother as just another of

[011-110] those things.

[011-111] “This,” he said, with a shake in his voice, “is something very special.

[011-112] China figure, said to date back to the Ming Dynasty. Unique. Nothing

[011-113] like it on either side of the Atlantic. If I were selling this at

[011-114] Christie’s in London, where people,” he said, nastily, “have an

[011-115] educated appreciation of the beautiful, the rare, and the exquisite, I

[011-116] should start the bidding at a thousand dollars. This afternoon’s

[011-117] experience has taught me that that might possibly be too high.” His

[011-118] pince-nez sparkled militantly, as he gazed upon the stolid throng.

[011-119] “Will anyone offer me a dollar for this unique figure?”

[011-120] “Leap at it, old top,” said Reggie van Tuyl. “Twiddle, dear boy,

[011-121] twiddle! A dollar’s reasonable.”

[011-122] Archie twiddled.

[011-123] “One dollar I am offered,” said the high-priest, bitterly. “One

[011-124] gentleman here is not afraid to take a chance. One gentleman here knows

[011-125] a good thing when he sees one.” He abandoned the gently sarcastic

[011-126] manner for one of crisp and direct reproach. “Come, come, gentlemen, we

[011-127] are not here to waste time. Will anyone offer me one hundred dollars

[011-128] for this superb piece of—” He broke off, and seemed for a moment almost

[011-129] unnerved. He stared at someone in one of the seats in front of Archie.

[011-130] “Thank you,” he said, with a sort of gulp. “One hundred dollars I am

[011-131] offered! One hundred—one hundred—one hundred—”

[011-132] Archie was startled. This sudden, tremendous jump, this wholly

[011-133] unforeseen boom in Pongos, if one might so describe it, was more than a

[011-134] little disturbing. He could not see who his rival was, but it was

[011-135] evident that at least one among those present did not intend to allow

[011-136] Pongo’s brother to slip by without a fight. He looked helplessly at

[011-137] Reggie for counsel, but Reggie had now definitely given up the

[011-138] struggle. Exhausted nature had done its utmost, and now he was leaning

[011-139] back with closed eyes, breathing softly through his nose. Thrown on his

[011-140] own resources, Archie could think of no better course than to twiddle

[011-141] his fingers again. He did so, and the high-priest’s chant took on a

[011-142] note of positive exuberance.

[011-143] “Two hundred I am offered. Much better! Turn the pedestal round,

[011-144] Willie, and let them look at it. Slowly! Slowly! You aren’t spinning a

[011-145] roulette-wheel. Two hundred. Two-two-two-two-two.” He became suddenly

[011-146] lyrical. “Two-two-two—There was a young lady named Lou, who was

[011-147] catching a train at two-two. Said the porter, ‘Don’t worry or hurry or

[011-148] scurry. It’s a minute or two to two-two!’ Two-two-two-two-two!”

[011-149] Archie’s concern increased. He seemed to be twiddling at this voluble

[011-150] man across seas of misunderstanding. Nothing is harder to interpret to

[011-151] a nicety than a twiddle, and Archie’s idea of the language of twiddles

[011-152] and the high-priest’s idea did not coincide by a mile. The high-priest

[011-153] appeared to consider that, when Archie twiddled, it was his intention

[011-154] to bid in hundreds, whereas in fact Archie had meant to signify that he

[011-155] raised the previous bid by just one dollar. Archie felt that, if given

[011-156] time, he could make this clear to the high-priest, but the latter gave

[011-157] him no time. He had got his audience, so to speak, on the run, and he

[011-158] proposed to hustle them before they could rally.

[011-159] “Two hundred—two hundred—two—three—thank you,

[011-160] sir—three-three-three-four-four-five-five-six-six-seven-seven-seven—”

[011-161] Archie sat limply in his wooden chair. He was conscious of a feeling

[011-162] which he had only experienced twice in his life—once when he had taken

[011-163] his first lesson in driving a motor and had trodden on the accelerator

[011-164] instead of the brake; the second time more recently, when he had made

[011-165] his first down-trip on an express lift. He had now precisely the same

[011-166] sensation of being run away with by an uncontrollable machine, and of

[011-167] having left most of his internal organs at some little distance from

[011-168] the rest of his body. Emerging from this welter of emotion, stood out

[011-169] the one clear fact that, be the opposition bidding what it might, he

[011-170] must nevertheless secure the prize. [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] had sent him to New York

[011-171] expressly to do so. She had sacrificed her jewellery for the cause. She

[011-172] relied on him. The enterprise had become for Archie something almost

[011-173] sacred. He felt dimly like a knight of old hot on the track of the Holy

[011-174] Grail.

[011-175] He twiddled again. The ring and [[lucilles-bracelet|the bracelet]] had fetched nearly twelve

[011-176] hundred dollars. Up to that figure his hat was in the ring.

[011-177] “Eight hundred I am offered. Eight hundred. Eight-eight-eight-eight—”

[011-178] A voice spoke from somewhere at the back of the room. A quiet, cold,

[011-179] nasty, determined voice.

[011-180] “Nine!”

[011-181] Archie rose from his seat and spun round. This mean attack from the

[011-182] rear stung his fighting spirit. As he rose, a young man sitting

[011-183] immediately in front of him rose too and stared likewise. He was a

[011-184] square-built resolute-looking young man, who reminded Archie vaguely of

[011-185] somebody he had seen before. But Archie was too busy trying to locate

[011-186] the man at the back to pay much attention to him. He detected him at

[011-187] last, owing to the fact that the eyes of everybody in that part of the

[011-188] room were fixed upon him. He was a small man of middle age, with

[011-189] tortoise-shell-rimmed spectacles. He might have been a professor or

[011-190] something of the kind. Whatever he was, he was obviously a man to be

[011-191] reckoned with. He had a rich sort of look, and his demeanour was the

[011-192] demeanour of a man who is prepared to fight it out on these lines if it

[011-193] takes all the summer.

[011-194] “Nine hundred I am offered. Nine-nine-nine-nine—”

[011-195] Archie glared defiantly at the spectacled man.

[011-196] “A thousand!” he cried.

[011-197] The irruption of high [[wealth-and-money|finance]] into the placid course of the afternoon’s

[011-198] proceedings had stirred the congregation out of its lethargy. There

[011-199] were excited murmurs. Necks were craned, feet shuffled. As for the

[011-200] high-priest, his [[hope-and-optimism|cheerfulness]] was now more than restored, and his faith

[011-201] in his fellow-man had soared from the depths to a very lofty altitude.

[011-202] He beamed with approval. Despite the warmth of his praise he would have

[011-203] been quite satisfied to see Pongo’s little brother go at twenty

[011-204] dollars, and the reflection that the bidding had already reached one

[011-205] thousand and that his commission was twenty per cent, had engendered a

[011-206] mood of sunny happiness.
