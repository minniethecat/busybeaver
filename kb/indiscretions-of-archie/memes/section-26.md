## Section 26

[026-001] “I don’t know how to thank you enough, [[archie-moffam|Archie]], old man,” said [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]],

[026-002] fervently.

[026-003] “Thank me?”

[026-004] “It was through you that I met her. Don’t you remember the girl you

[026-005] sent to me? You wanted me to get her a small part—”

[026-006] He stopped, puzzled. [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] had uttered a sound that was half gasp and

[026-007] half gurgle, but it was swallowed up in the extraordinary noise from

[026-008] the other side of the table. Bill [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]] was leaning forward with

[026-009] bulging eyes and soaring eyebrows.

[026-010] “Are you engaged to Mabel Winchester?”

[026-011] “Why, by George!” said Reggie. “Do you know her?”

[026-012] Archie recovered himself.

[026-013] “Slightly,” he said. “Slightly. Old Bill knows her slightly, as it

[026-014] were. Not very well, don’t you know, but—how shall I put it?”

[026-015] “Slightly,” suggested Bill.

[026-016] “Just the word. Slightly.”

[026-017] “Splendid!” said Reggie van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]]. “Why don’t you come along to the Ritz

[026-018] and meet her now?”

[026-019] Bill stammered. Archie came to the rescue again.

[026-020] “Bill can’t come now. He’s got a date.”

[026-021] “A date?” said Bill.

[026-022] “A date,” said Archie. “An appointment, don’t you know. A—a—in fact, a

[026-023] date.”

[026-024] “But—er—wish her happiness from me,” said Bill, cordially.

[026-025] “Thanks very much, old man,” said Reggie.

[026-026] “And say I’m delighted, will you?”

[026-027] “Certainly.”

[026-028] “You won’t forget the word, will you? Delighted.”

[026-029] “Delighted.”

[026-030] “That’s right. Delighted.”

[026-031] Reggie looked at his watch.

[026-032] “[[what-ho|Halloa]]! I must rush!”

[026-033] Bill and Archie watched him as he bounded out of the restaurant.

[026-034] “Poor old Reggie!” said Bill, with a fleeting compunction.

[026-035] “Not necessarily,” said Archie. “What I mean to say is, tastes differ,

[026-036] don’t you know. One man’s peach is another man’s poison, and vice

[026-037] versa.”

[026-038] “There’s something in that.”

[026-039] “Absolutely! Well,” said Archie, judicially, “this would appear to be,

[026-040] as it were, the maddest, merriest day in all the glad New Year, yes,

[026-041] no?”

[026-042] Bill drew a deep breath.

[026-043] “You bet your sorrowful existence it is!” he said. “I’d like to do

[026-044] something to celebrate it.”

[026-045] “The right spirit!” said Archie. “Absolutely the right spirit! Begin by

[026-046] paying for my [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]]!”

[026-047] CHAPTER XX.

[026-048] THE-SAUSAGE-CHAPPIE-CLICKS

[026-049] Rendered restless by relief, [[bill-brewster|Bill Brewster]] did not linger long at the

[026-050] luncheon-table. Shortly after Reggie van Tuyl had retired, he got up

[026-051] and announced his intention of going for a bit of a walk to calm his

[026-052] excited mind. Archie dismissed him with a courteous wave of the hand;

[026-053] and, beckoning to the Sausage Chappie, who in his role of waiter was

[026-054] hovering near, requested him to bring the best cigar the hotel could

[026-055] supply. The padded seat in which he sat was comfortable; he had no

[026-056] engagements; and it seemed to him that a pleasant half-hour could be

[026-057] passed in smoking dreamily and watching his fellow-men eat.

[026-058] The grill-room had filled up. [[sausage-chappie|The Sausage Chappie]], having brought

[026-059] Archie his cigar, was attending to a table close by, at which a woman

[026-060] with a small boy in a sailor suit had seated themselves. The woman was

[026-061] engrossed with the bill of fare, but the child’s attention seemed

[026-062] riveted upon the Sausage Chappie. He was drinking him in with wide

[026-063] eyes. He seemed to be brooding on him.

[026-064] Archie, too, was brooding on the Sausage Chappie, The latter made an

[026-065] excellent waiter: he was brisk and attentive, and did the work as if he

[026-066] liked it; but Archie was not satisfied. Something seemed to tell him

[026-067] that the man was fitted for higher things. Archie was a grateful soul.

[026-068] That sausage, coming at the end of a five-hour hike, had made a deep

[026-069] impression on his plastic nature. Reason told him that only an

[026-070] exceptional man could have parted with half a sausage at such a moment;

[026-071] and he could not feel that a job as waiter at a [[new-york-city|New York]] hotel was an

[026-072] adequate job for an exceptional man. Of course, the root of the trouble

[026-073] lay in the fact that the fellow could not remember what his real

[026-074] life-work had been before [[world-war-i|the war]]. It was exasperating to reflect, as

[026-075] the other moved away to take his order to the kitchen, that there, for

[026-076] all one knew, went the dickens of a lawyer or doctor or architect or

[026-077] what not.

[026-078] His meditations were broken by the voice of the child.

[026-079] “Mummie,” asked the child interestedly, following the Sausage Chappie

[026-080] with his eyes as the latter disappeared towards the kitchen, “why has

[026-081] that man got such a funny face?”

[026-082] “Hush, darling.”

[026-083] “Yes, but why HAS he?”

[026-084] “I don’t know, darling.”

[026-085] The child’s faith in the maternal omniscience seemed to have received a

[026-086] shock. He had the air of a seeker after truth who has been baffled. His

[026-087] eyes roamed the room discontentedly.

[026-088] “He’s got a funnier face than that man there,” he said, pointing to

[026-089] Archie.

[026-090] “Hush, darling!”

[026-091] “But he has. Much funnier.”

[026-092] In a way it was a sort of compliment, but Archie felt embarrassed. He

[026-093] withdrew coyly into the cushioned recess. Presently the Sausage Chappie

[026-094] returned, attended to the needs of the woman and the child, and came

[026-095] over to Archie. His homely face was beaming.

[026-096] “Say, I had a big night last night,” he said, leaning on the table.

[026-097] “Yes?” said Archie. “Party or something?”

[026-098] “No, I mean I suddenly began to remember things. Something seems to

[026-099] have happened to the works.”

[026-100] Archie sat up excitedly. This was great news.

[026-101] “No, really? My dear old lad, this is absolutely topping. This is

[026-102] priceless.”

[026-103] “Yessir! First thing I remembered was that I was born at Springfield,

[026-104] Ohio. It was like a mist starting to lift. Springfield, Ohio. That was

[026-105] it. It suddenly came back to me.”

[026-106] “Splendid! Anything else?”

[026-107] “Yessir! Just before I went to sleep I remembered my name as well.”

[026-108] Archie was stirred to his depths.

[026-109] “Why, the thing’s a walk-over!” he exclaimed. “Now you’ve once got

[026-110] started, nothing can stop you. What is your name?”

[026-111] “Why, it’s—That’s funny! It’s gone again. I have an idea it began with

[026-112] an S. What was it? Skeffington? Skillington?”

[026-113] “Sanderson?”

[026-114] “No; I’ll get it in a moment. Cunningham? Carrington? Wilberforce?

[026-115] Debenham?”

[026-116] “Dennison?” suggested Archie, helpfully.—“No, no, no. It’s on the tip

[026-117] of my tongue. Barrington? Montgomery? Hepplethwaite? I’ve got it!

[026-118] Smith!”

[026-119] “By Jove! Really?”

[026-120] “Certain of it.”

[026-121] “What’s the first name?”

[026-122] An anxious expression came into the man’s eyes. He hesitated. He

[026-123] lowered his voice.

[026-124] “I have a horrible feeling that it’s Lancelot!”

[026-125] “Good God!” said Archie.

[026-126] “It couldn’t really be that, could it?”

[026-127] Archie looked grave. He hated to give pain, but he felt he must be

[026-128] honest.

[026-129] “It might,” he said. “People give their children all sorts of rummy

[026-130] names. My second name’s Tracy. And I have a pal in [[england|England]] who was

[026-131] christened Cuthbert de la Hay Horace. Fortunately everyone calls him

[026-132] Stinker.”

[026-133] The head-waiter began to drift up like a bank of fog, and the Sausage

[026-134] Chappie returned to his professional duties. When he came back, he was

[026-135] beaming again.

[026-136] “Something else I remembered,” he said, removing the cover. “I’m

[026-137] married!”

[026-138] “Good Lord!”

[026-139] “At least I was before the war. She had blue eyes and brown hair and a

[026-140] Pekingese dog.”

[026-141] “What was her name?”

[026-142] “I don’t know.”

[026-143] “Well, you’re coming on,” said Archie. “I’ll admit that. You’ve still

[026-144] got a bit of a way to go before you become like one of those blighters

[026-145] who take the Memory Training Courses in the magazine advertisements—I

[026-146] mean to say, you know, the lads who meet a fellow once for five

[026-147] minutes, and then come across him again ten years later and grasp him

[026-148] by the hand and say, ‘Surely this is Mr. Watkins of Seattle?’ Still,

[026-149] you’re doing fine. You only need patience. Everything comes to him who

[026-150] waits.” Archie sat up, electrified. “I say, by Jove, that’s rather

[026-151] good, what! Everything comes to him who waits, and you’re a waiter,

[026-152] what, what. I mean to say, what!”

[026-153] “Mummie,” said the child at the other table, still speculative, “do you

[026-154] think something trod on his face?”

[026-155] “Hush, darling.”

[026-156] “Perhaps it was bitten by something?”

[026-157] “Eat your nice fish, darling,” said the mother, who seemed to be one of

[026-158] those dull-witted persons whom it is impossible to interest in a

[026-159] discussion on first causes.

[026-160] Archie felt stimulated. Not even the advent of his [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]], who

[026-161] came in a few moments later and sat down at the other end of the room,

[026-162] could depress his spirits.

[026-163] The Sausage Chappie came to his table again.

[026-164] “It’s a funny thing,” he said. “Like waking up after you’ve been

[026-165] asleep. Everything seems to be getting clearer. The dog’s name was

[026-166] Marie. My wife’s dog, you know. And she had a mole on her chin.”

[026-167] “The dog?”

[026-168] “No. My wife. Little beast! She bit me in the leg once.”

[026-169] “Your wife?”

[026-170] “No. The dog. Good Lord!” said the Sausage Chappie.

[026-171] Archie looked up and followed his gaze.

[026-172] A couple of tables away, next to a sideboard on which the management

[026-173] exposed for view the cold meats and puddings and pies mentioned in

[026-174] volume two of the bill of fare (“Buffet Froid”), a man and a girl had

[026-175] just seated themselves. The man was stout and middle-aged. He bulged in

[026-176] practically every place in which a man can bulge, and his head was

[026-177] almost entirely free from hair. The girl was young and pretty. Her eyes

[026-178] were blue. Her hair was brown. She had a rather attractive little mole

[026-179] on the left side of her chin.

[026-180] “Good Lord!” said the Sausage Chappie.

[026-181] “Now what?” said Archie.

[026-182] “Who’s that? Over at the table there?”

[026-183] Archie, through long attendance at [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] Grill, knew most of

[026-184] the habitues by sight.

[026-185] “That’s a man named Gossett. James J. Gossett. He’s a motion-picture

[026-186] man. You must have seen his name around.”

[026-187] “I don’t mean him. Who’s the girl?”

[026-188] “I’ve never seen her before.”

[026-189] “It’s my wife!” said the Sausage Chappie.

[026-190] “Your wife!”

[026-191] “Yes!”

[026-192] “Are you sure?”

[026-193] “Of course I’m sure!”

[026-194] “Well, well, well!” said Archie. “Many happy returns of the day!”

[026-195] At the other table, the girl, unconscious of the drama which was about

[026-196] to enter her life, was engrossed in [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]] with the stout man.

[026-197] And at this moment the stout man leaned forward and patted her on the

[026-198] cheek.

[026-199] It was a paternal pat, the pat which a genial uncle might bestow on a

[026-200] favourite niece, but it did not strike the Sausage Chappie in that

[026-201] light. He had been advancing on the table at a fairly rapid pace, and

[026-202] now, stirred to his depths, he bounded forward with a hoarse cry.

[026-203] Archie was at some pains to explain to his father-in-law later that, if

[026-204] the management left cold pies and things about all over the place, this

[026-205] sort of thing was bound to happen sooner or later. He urged that it was

[026-206] putting temptation in people’s way, and that Mr. Brewster had only

[026-207] himself to blame. Whatever the rights of the case, the Buffet Froid

[026-208] undoubtedly came in remarkably handy at this crisis in the Sausage

[026-209] Chappie’s life. He had almost reached the sideboard when the stout man

[026-210] patted the girl’s cheek, and to seize a huckleberry pie was with him

[026-211] the work of a moment. The next instant the pie had whizzed past the

[026-212] other’s head and burst like a shell against the wall.

[026-213] There are, no doubt, restaurants where this sort of thing would have

[026-214] excited little comment, but [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]] was not one of them.

[026-215] Everybody had something to say, but the only one among those present

[026-216] who had anything sensible to say was the child in the sailor suit.

[026-217] “Do it again!” said the child, cordially.

[026-218] The Sausage Chappie did it again. He took up a fruit salad, poised it

[026-219] for a moment, then decanted it over Mr. Gossett’s bald head. The

[026-220] child’s happy laughter rang over the restaurant. Whatever anybody else

[026-221] might think of the affair, this child liked it and was prepared to go

[026-222] on record to that effect.

[026-223] Epic events have a stunning quality. They paralyse the faculties. For a

[026-224] moment there was a pause. The world stood still. Mr. Brewster bubbled

[026-225] inarticulately. Mr. Gossett dried himself sketchily with a napkin. The

[026-226] Sausage Chappie snorted.
