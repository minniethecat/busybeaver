# Gentlemanly Conduct

Archie Moffam's defining characteristic is not intelligence, wealth, or competence — it is that he is a gentleman. In Wodehouse's comic universe, gentlemanliness is a specific moral-aesthetic quality: a combination of good nature, refusal to take offense, and an unshakeable belief that things will work out. It is this quality, more than any practical skill, that enables Archie to survive and ultimately thrive in the hostile environment of his father-in-law's New York.

## Overview

The figure of the English gentleman was already an object of affectionate satire when Wodehouse began writing. Archie represents the gentleman reduced to his essence: no money, no occupation, no particular talents — only an inexhaustible fund of good humor and "open-hearted hospitality" (003-031). The novel's comic project is to test whether this essence is sufficient in the modern world.

Remarkably, it proves to be. Archie's gentlemanliness disarms enemies, wins allies, and resolves conflicts that money and authority cannot touch. The novel suggests, with characteristic lightness, that being a gentleman — in the Wodehousian sense of being decent, cheerful, and fundamentally well-meaning — is a genuine form of social power.

## In the Novel

Archie's conduct is established in contrast to the American characters who surround him. Where Brewster is suspicious and proprietary, Archie is open and trusting. Where business acquaintances are calculating, Archie is spontaneous. This contrast generates the novel's comedy but also its moral structure: Archie's gentlemanly values are repeatedly vindicated by events.

The novel does not, however, treat gentlemanliness uncritically. Archie's refusal to take anything seriously — including his own poverty — is both his greatest charm and his greatest liability. The tension between gentlemanly ease and the demands of modern life is never fully resolved, only continually renegotiated through the episodic structure.
