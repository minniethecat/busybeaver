## Section 17

[017-001] And, as if in direct answer to prayer, an almost forgotten aunt in

[017-002] [[england|England]] suddenly, out of an absolutely blue sky, shot no less a sum

[017-003] than five hundred dollars across the ocean. The present was so lavish

[017-004] and unexpected that [[archie-moffam|Archie]] had the awed feeling of one who participates

[017-005] in a miracle. He felt, like Herbert [[parker|Parker]], that the righteous was not

[017-006] forsaken. It was the sort of thing that restored a fellow’s faith in

[017-007] human nature. For nearly a week he went about in a happy trance: and

[017-008] when, by thrift and enterprise—that is to say, by betting [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van

[017-009] [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]] that the [[new-york-city|New York]] [[new-york-giants|Giants]] would win the opening game of the series

[017-010] against the [[pittsburg-pirates|Pittsburg]] [[leisure-and-sports|baseball]] team—he contrived to double his capital,

[017-011] what it amounted to was simply that life had nothing more to offer. He

[017-012] was actually in a position to go to a thousand dollars for [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]]’s

[017-013] birthday present. He gathered in Mr. van Tuyl, of whose taste in these

[017-014] matters he had a high opinion, and dragged him off to a jeweller’s on

[017-015] Broadway.

[017-016] The jeweller, a stout, comfortable man, leaned on the counter and

[017-017] fingered lovingly [[lucilles-bracelet|the bracelet]] which he had lifted out of its nest of

[017-018] blue plush. [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], leaning on the other side of the counter, inspected

[017-019] the bracelet searchingly, wishing that he knew more about these things;

[017-020] for he had rather a sort of idea that the merchant was scheming to do

[017-021] him in the eyeball. In a chair by his side, Reggie van Tuyl, half

[017-022] asleep as usual, yawned despondently. He had permitted Archie to lug

[017-023] him into this shop; and he wanted to buy something and go. Any form of

[017-024] sustained concentration fatigued Reggie.

[017-025] “Now this,” said the jeweller, “I could do at eight hundred and fifty

[017-026] dollars.”

[017-027] “Grab it!” murmured Mr. van Tuyl.

[017-028] The jeweller eyed him approvingly, a man after his own heart; but

[017-029] Archie looked doubtful. It was all very well for Reggie to tell him to

[017-030] grab it in that careless way. Reggie was a dashed millionaire, and no

[017-031] doubt bought bracelets by the pound or the gross or what not; but he

[017-032] himself was in an entirely different position.

[017-033] “Eight hundred and fifty dollars!” he said, hesitating.

[017-034] “Worth it,” mumbled Reggie van Tuyl.

[017-035] “More than worth it,” amended the jeweller. “I can assure you that it

[017-036] is better value than you could get anywhere on [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]].”

[017-037] “Yes?” said Archie. He took the bracelet and twiddled it thoughtfully.

[017-038] “Well, my dear old jeweller, one can’t say fairer than that, can one—or

[017-039] two, as the case may be!” He frowned. “Oh, well, all right! But it’s

[017-040] rummy that women are so fearfully keen on these little thingummies,

[017-041] isn’t it? I mean to say, can’t see what they see in them. Stones, and

[017-042] all that. Still, there it is, of course!”

[017-043] “There,” said the jeweller, “as you say, it is, sir.”

[017-044] “Yes, there it is!”

[017-045] “Yes, there it is,” said the jeweller, “fortunately for people in my

[017-046] line of business. Will you take it with you, sir?”

[017-047] Archie reflected.

[017-048] “No. No, not take it with me. The fact is, you know, my wife’s coming

[017-049] back from the country to-night, and it’s her birthday to-morrow, and

[017-050] the thing’s for her, and, if it was popping about the place to-night,

[017-051] she might see it, and it would sort of spoil the surprise. I mean to

[017-052] say, she doesn’t know I’m giving it her, and all that!”

[017-053] “Besides,” said Reggie, achieving a certain animation now that the

[017-054] tedious business interview was concluded, “going to the ball-game this

[017-055] afternoon—might get pocket picked—yes, better have it sent.”

[017-056] “Where shall I send it, sir?”

[017-057] “Eh? Oh, shoot it along to Mrs. Archibald Moffam, at [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]].

[017-058] Not to-day, you know. Buzz it in first thing to-morrow.”

[017-059] Having completed the satisfactory deal, the jeweller threw off the

[017-060] business manner and became chatty.

[017-061] “So you are going to the ball-game? It should be an interesting

[017-062] contest.”

[017-063] Reggie van Tuyl, now—by his own standards—completely awake, took

[017-064] exception to this remark.

[017-065] “Not a bit of it!” he said, decidedly. “No contest! Can’t call it a

[017-066] contest! Walkover for the Pirates!”

[017-067] Archie was stung to the quick. There is that about baseball which

[017-068] arouses enthusiasm and the partisan spirit in the unlikeliest bosoms.

[017-069] It is almost impossible for a man to live in America and not become

[017-070] gripped by the game; and Archie had long been one of its warmest

[017-071] adherents. He was a whole-hearted supporter of the Giants, and his only

[017-072] grievance against Reggie, in other respects an estimable young man, was

[017-073] that the latter, whose [[wealth-and-money|money]] had been inherited from steel-mills in

[017-074] that city, had an absurd regard for the Pirates of Pittsburg.

[017-075] “What absolute bally rot!” he exclaimed. “Look what the Giants did to

[017-076] them yesterday!”

[017-077] “Yesterday isn’t to-day,” said Reggie.

[017-078] “No, it’ll be a jolly sight worse,” said Archie. “Looney Biddle’ll be

[017-079] pitching for the Giants to-day.”

[017-080] “That’s just what I mean. The Pirates have got him rattled. Look what

[017-081] happened last time.”

[017-082] Archie understood, and his generous nature chafed at the innuendo.

[017-083] Looney Biddle—so-called by an affectionately admiring public as the

[017-084] result of certain marked eccentricities—was beyond dispute the greatest

[017-085] left-handed pitcher New York had possessed in the last decade. But

[017-086] there was one blot on Mr. Biddle’s otherwise stainless scutcheon. Five

[017-087] weeks before, on the occasion of the Giants’ invasion of Pittsburg, he

[017-088] had gone mysteriously to pieces. Few native-born partisans, brought up

[017-089] to baseball from the cradle, had been plunged into a profounder gloom

[017-090] on that occasion than Archie; but his soul revolted at the thought that

[017-091] that sort of thing could ever happen again.

[017-092] “I’m not saying,” continued Reggie, “that Biddle isn’t a very fair

[017-093] pitcher, but it’s cruel to send him against the Pirates, and somebody

[017-094] ought to stop it. His best friends should interfere. Once a team gets a

[017-095] pitcher rattled, he’s never any good against them again. He loses his

[017-096] nerve.”

[017-097] The jeweller nodded approval of this sentiment.

[017-098] “They never come back,” he said, sententiously.

[017-099] The fighting blood of the Moffams was now thoroughly stirred. Archie

[017-100] eyed his friend sternly. Reggie was a good chap—in many respects an

[017-101] extremely sound egg—but he must not be allowed to [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] rot of this

[017-102] description about the greatest left-handed pitcher of the age.

[017-103] “It seems to me, old companion,” he said, “that a small bet is

[017-104] indicated at this juncture. How about it?”

[017-105] “Don’t want to take your money.”

[017-106] “You won’t have to! In the cool twilight of the merry old summer

[017-107] evening I, friend of my youth and companion of my riper years, shall be

[017-108] trousering yours.”

[017-109] Reggie yawned. The day was very hot, and this argument was making him

[017-110] feel sleepy again.

[017-111] “Well, just as you like, of course. Double or quits on yesterday’s bet,

[017-112] if that suits you.”

[017-113] For a moment Archie hesitated. Firm as his faith was in Mr. Biddle’s

[017-114] stout left arm, he had not intended to do the thing on quite this

[017-115] scale. That thousand dollars of his was earmarked for Lucille’s

[017-116] birthday present, and he doubted whether he ought to risk it. Then the

[017-117] thought that the honour of New York was in his hands decided him.

[017-118] Besides, the risk was negligible. Betting on Looney Biddle was like

[017-119] betting on the probable rise of the sun in the east. The thing began to

[017-120] seem to Archie a rather unusually sound and conservative investment. He

[017-121] remembered that the jeweller, until he drew him firmly but kindly to

[017-122] earth and urged him to curb his exuberance and talk business on a

[017-123] reasonable plane, had started brandishing bracelets that cost about two

[017-124] thousand. There would be time to pop in at the shop this evening after

[017-125] the game and change the one he had selected for one of those. Nothing

[017-126] was too good for Lucille on her birthday.

[017-127] “Right-o!” he said. “Make it so, old friend!”

[017-128] Archie walked back to [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]]. No misgivings came to mar his

[017-129] perfect contentment. He felt no qualms about separating Reggie from

[017-130] another thousand dollars. Except for a little small change in the

[017-131] possession of the Messrs. Rockefeller and Vincent Astor, Reggie had all

[017-132] the money in the world and could afford to lose. He hummed a gay air as

[017-133] he entered the lobby and crossed to the cigar-stand to buy a few

[017-134] cigarettes to see him through the afternoon.

[017-135] The girl behind the cigar counter welcomed him with a bright smile.

[017-136] Archie was popular with all the employés of the Cosmopolis.

[017-137] “’S a great day, Mr. Moffam!”

[017-138] “One of the brightest and best,” agreed Archie. “Could you dig me out

[017-139] two, or possibly three, cigarettes of the usual description? I shall

[017-140] want something to smoke at the ball-game.”

[017-141] “You going to the ball-game?”

[017-142] “Rather! Wouldn’t miss it for a fortune.”

[017-143] “No?”

[017-144] “Absolutely no! Not with jolly old Biddle pitching.”

[017-145] The cigar-stand girl laughed amusedly.

[017-146] “Is he pitching this afternoon? Say, that feller’s a nut? D’you know

[017-147] him?”

[017-148] “Know him? Well, I’ve seen him pitch and so forth.”

[017-149] “I’ve got a girl friend who’s engaged to him!”

[017-150] Archie looked at her with positive respect. It would have been more

[017-151] dramatic, of course, if she had been engaged to the great man herself,

[017-152] but still the mere fact that she had a girl friend in that astounding

[017-153] position gave her a sort of halo.

[017-154] “No, really!” he said. “I say, by Jove, really! Fancy that!”

[017-155] “Yes, she’s engaged to him all right. Been engaged close on a coupla

[017-156] months now.”

[017-157] “I say! That’s frightfully interesting! Fearfully interesting, really!”

[017-158] “It’s funny about that guy,” said [[cigar-stand-girl|the cigar-stand girl]]. “He’s a nut!

[017-159] The fellow who said there’s plenty of room at the top must have been

[017-160] thinking of Gus Biddle’s head! He’s crazy about m’ girl friend, y’

[017-161] know, and, whenever they have a fuss, it seems like he sort of flies

[017-162] right off the handle.”

[017-163] “Goes in off the deep end, eh?”

[017-164] “Yes, _sir!_ Loses what little sense he’s got. Why, the last time him

[017-165] and m’ girl friend got to scrapping was when he was going on to

[017-166] Pittsburg to play, about a month ago. He’d been out with her the day he

[017-167] left for there, and he had a grouch or something, and he started making

[017-168] low, sneaky cracks about her Uncle Sigsbee. Well, m’ girl friend’s got

[017-169] a nice disposition, but she c’n get mad, and she just left him flat and

[017-170] told him all was over. And he went off to Pittsburg, and, when he

[017-171] started in to pitch the opening game, he just couldn’t keep his mind on

[017-172] his job, and look what them assassins done to him! Five runs in the

[017-173] first innings! Yessir, he’s a nut all right!”

[017-174] Archie was deeply concerned. So this was the explanation of that

[017-175] mysterious disaster, that weird tragedy which had puzzled the sporting

[017-176] press from coast to coast.

[017-177] “Good God! Is he often taken like that?”

[017-178] “Oh, he’s all right when he hasn’t had a fuss with m’ girl friend,”

[017-179] said the cigar-stand girl, indifferently. Her interest in baseball was

[017-180] tepid. Women are too often like this—mere butterflies, with no concern

[017-181] for the deeper side of life.

[017-182] “Yes, but I say! What I mean to say, you know! Are they pretty pally

[017-183] now? The good old Dove of Peace flapping its little wings fairly

[017-184] briskly and all that?”

[017-185] “Oh, I guess everything’s nice and smooth just now. I seen m’ girl

[017-186] friend yesterday, and Gus was taking her to the movies last night, so I

[017-187] guess everything’s nice and smooth.”

[017-188] Archie breathed a sigh of relief.

[017-189] “Took her to the movies, did he? Stout fellow!”

[017-190] “I was at the funniest picture last week,” said the cigar-stand girl.

[017-191] “Honest, it was a scream! It was like this—”

[017-192] Archie listened politely; then went in to get a bite of [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]]. His

[017-193] equanimity, shaken by the discovery of the rift in the peerless one’s

[017-194] armour, was restored. Good old Biddle had taken the girl to the movies

[017-195] last night. Probably he had squeezed her hand a goodish bit in the

[017-196] dark. With what result? Why, the fellow would be feeling like one of

[017-197] those chappies who used to joust for the smiles of females in the

[017-198] Middle Ages. What he meant to say, presumably the girl would be at the

[017-199] game this afternoon, whooping him on, and good old Biddle would be so

[017-200] full of beans and buck that there would be no holding him.
