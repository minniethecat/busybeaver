# Daniel Brewster

Daniel Brewster is the novel's antagonist and its most fully realized American character. A self-made millionaire who built the [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|Hotel Cosmopolis]] through his own industry, Brewster embodies the American values of hard work, personal supervision, and financial prudence. His world is upended by the arrival of [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]] — a young Englishman who marries his daughter [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] without permission and then has the temerity to criticize Brewster's beloved hotel (001-135). The resulting conflict between American business sense and English aristocratic insouciance is the engine of the novel's comedy.

## Role in the Novel

Brewster is both antagonist and comic foil. His fury at Archie is entirely reasonable — Archie has, after all, eloped with his daughter and insulted his life's work — yet Wodehouse renders this fury in terms that are themselves comic. Brewster is a serious man in a novel that refuses to take anything seriously, and this mismatch is the source of much of the humor.

His narrative function is to provide the resistance against which Archie's charm must operate. Without Brewster's opposition, Archie would have nothing to overcome; without Brewster's eventual (grudging) acceptance, the novel would have no resolution. He is the immovable object against which Archie's irresistible amiability must contend — and in the Wodehousian universe, amiability always wins, though not before Brewster has been thoroughly tormented.

## Biography

Brewster is a self-made man who "had risen from poverty to wealth by his own efforts" (012-220). He built the Hotel Cosmopolis as a monument to his success, personally selecting every tapestry and piece of furniture (001-165). As proprietor, he "insisted" on standards that made the Cosmopolis one of New York's premier establishments (001-071). His pride in his achievement is absolute — and absolutely vulnerable to criticism.

His relationship with his daughter Lucille is affectionate but complicated by his inability to accept her choice of husband. His eighteen-year friendship with [[professor-binstead|Professor Binstead]] (001-052) suggests a capacity for loyalty that his suspicious nature often obscures. When Binstead addresses him simply as "Brewster" (001-176), the easy familiarity reveals a relationship that predates the hotel and its attendant anxieties.

## Personality and Traits

Brewster's defining trait is pride — pride in his hotel, pride in his achievement, pride in his judgment. This pride makes him suspicious of Archie from the first meeting, when he "was bristling for combat" (001-086). His speech is direct, American, and businesslike — a sharp contrast to Archie's elaborately casual English manner. When Archie criticizes the Cosmopolis, Brewster's "compact frame quivered" (001-136) with barely contained rage — a physical manifestation of the insult to his life's work.

Beneath the bluster, Brewster is not unsympathetic. His objection to Archie is based on genuine concern for his daughter's welfare, and his eventual acceptance of the marriage represents genuine emotional growth. He is, in Wodehouse's hands, a type — the self-made American businessman — rendered with sufficient particularity to transcend the type. His brow "clouded" (001-200) at bad news, and he "sat down again with a thud" (002-040) when overwhelmed — these small physical details humanize what might otherwise be a purely functional antagonist.

As a hotel proprietor, Brewster's attention to detail is legendary. He personally supervised every aspect of the Cosmopolis, from the tapestries on the walls to the honesty of his employees: "My employés are honest," he declared with characteristic certainty (001-131). This hands-on approach is both his strength and his vulnerability — it means that any criticism of the hotel is a criticism of Brewster himself.

## Key Relationships

- **[[archie-moffam]]** — Son-in-law and nemesis; the relationship that defines the novel's conflict (001-034). Archie "attached himself to the top button of Mr. Brewster's coat" (001-100) in a futile attempt at conciliation, and Brewster "disentangled himself" (001-112) — a physical metaphor for their entire relationship.
- **[[lucille-brewster]]** — Daughter; Lucille is the one person who can manage Brewster's temper (002-133). Her elopement — announced by the telegram "Returning to-day with darling Archie" (002-012) — is the shock that sets the novel in motion.
- **[[professor-binstead]]** — Old friend of eighteen years (001-052); their friendship provides the novel's most durable relationship.
