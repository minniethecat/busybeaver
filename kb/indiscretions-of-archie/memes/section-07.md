## Section 7

[007-001] “He doesn’t bite, I suppose, or sting or what-not?”

[007-002] “He may what-not occasionally. It depends on the weather. But, outside

[007-003] of that, he’s as harmless as a canary.”

[007-004] “Dashed dangerous things, canaries,” said [[archie-moffam|Archie]], thoughtfully. “They

[007-005] peck at you.”

[007-006] “Don’t weaken!” pleaded the Press-agent

[007-007] “Oh, all right. I’ll take him. By the way, touching the matter of

[007-008] browsing and sluicing. What do I feed him on?”

[007-009] “Oh, anything. Bread-and-milk or fruit or soft-boiled egg or

[007-010] dog-biscuit or ants’-eggs. You know—anything you have yourself. Well,

[007-011] I’m much obliged for your hospitality. I’ll do the same for you another

[007-012] time. Now I must be getting along to see to the practical end of the

[007-013] thing. By the way, Her Nibs lives at [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]], too. Very

[007-014] convenient. Well, so long. See you later.”

[007-015] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], left alone, began for the first time to have serious doubts. He

[007-016] had allowed himself to be swayed by Mr. [[roscoe-sherriff|Sherriff]]’s magnetic

[007-017] personality, but now that the other had removed himself he began to

[007-018] wonder if he had been entirely wise to lend his sympathy and

[007-019] co-operation to the scheme. He had never had intimate [[business-and-enterprise|dealings]] with a

[007-020] snake before, but he had kept silkworms as a child, and there had been

[007-021] the deuce of a lot of fuss and unpleasantness over them. Getting into

[007-022] the salad and what-not. Something seemed to tell him that he was asking

[007-023] for trouble with a loud voice, but he had given his word and he

[007-024] supposed he would have to go through with it.

[007-025] He lit another cigarette and wandered out into [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]. His

[007-026] usually smooth brow was ruffled with care. Despite the eulogies which

[007-027] Sherriff had uttered concerning Peter, he found his doubts increasing.

[007-028] Peter might, as the Press-agent had stated, be a great scout, but was

[007-029] his little Garden of Eden on the fifth floor of [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]] Hotel

[007-030] likely to be improved by the advent of even the most amiable and

[007-031] winsome of serpents? However—

[007-032] “Moffam! My dear fellow!”

[007-033] The voice, speaking suddenly in his ear from behind, roused Archie from

[007-034] his reflections. Indeed, it roused him so effectually that he jumped a

[007-035] clear inch off the ground and bit his tongue. Revolving on his axis, he

[007-036] found himself confronting a middle-aged man with a face like a horse.

[007-037] The man was dressed in something of an old-world style. His clothes had

[007-038] an English cut. He had a drooping grey moustache. He also wore a grey

[007-039] bowler hat flattened at the crown—but who are we to judge him?

[007-040] “Archie Moffam! I have been trying to find you all the morning.”

[007-041] Archie had placed him now. He had not seen General Mannister for

[007-042] several years—not, indeed, since the days when he used to meet him at

[007-043] the home of [[lord-seacliff|young Lord Seacliff]], his nephew. Archie had been at Eton

[007-044] and Oxford with Seacliff, and had often visited him in the Long

[007-045] Vacation.

[007-046] “[[what-ho|Halloa]], General! What ho, what ho! What on earth are you doing over

[007-047] here?”

[007-048] “Let’s get out of this crush, my boy.” General Mannister steered Archie

[007-049] into a side-street, “That’s better.” He cleared his throat once or

[007-050] twice, as if embarrassed. “I’ve brought Seacliff over,” he said,

[007-051] finally.

[007-052] “Dear old Squiffy here? Oh, I say! Great work!”

[007-053] General Mannister did not seem to share his enthusiasm. He looked like

[007-054] a horse with a secret sorrow. He coughed three times, like a horse who,

[007-055] in addition to a secret sorrow, had contracted asthma.

[007-056] “You will find Seacliff changed,” he said. “Let me see, how long is it

[007-057] since you and he met?”

[007-058] Archie reflected.

[007-059] “I was demobbed just about a year ago. I saw him in Paris about a year

[007-060] before that. The old egg got a bit of shrapnel in his foot or

[007-061] something, didn’t he? Anyhow, I remember he was sent home.”

[007-062] “His foot is perfectly well again now. But, unfortunately, the enforced

[007-063] inaction led to disastrous results. You recollect, no doubt, that

[007-064] Seacliff always had a—a tendency;—a—a weakness—it was a family

[007-065] failing—”

[007-066] “Mopping it up, do you mean? Shifting it? Looking on the jolly old

[007-067] stuff when it was red and what not, what?”

[007-068] “Exactly.”

[007-069] Archie nodded.

[007-070] “Dear old Squiffy was always rather a lad for the wassail-bowl. When I

[007-071] met him in Paris, I remember, he was quite tolerably blotto.”

[007-072] “Precisely. And the failing has, I regret to say, grown on him since he

[007-073] returned from [[world-war-i|the war]]. My poor sister was extremely worried. In fact,

[007-074] to cut a long story short, I induced him to accompany me to America. I

[007-075] am attached to the British Legation in Washington now, you know.”

[007-076] “Oh, really?”

[007-077] “I wished Seacliff to come with me to Washington, but he insists on

[007-078] remaining in [[new-york-city|New York]]. He stated specifically that the thought of

[007-079] living in Washington gave him the—what was the expression he used?”

[007-080] “The pip?”

[007-081] “The pip. Precisely.”

[007-082] “But what was the idea of bringing him to America?”

[007-083] “This admirable Prohibition enactment has rendered America—to my

[007-084] mind—the ideal place for a young man of his views.” The General looked

[007-085] at his watch. “It is most fortunate that I happened to run into you, my

[007-086] dear fellow. My train for Washington leaves in another hour, and I have

[007-087] packing to do. I want to leave poor Seacliff in your charge while I am

[007-088] gone.”

[007-089] “Oh, I say! What!”

[007-090] “You can look after him. I am credibly informed that even now there are

[007-091] places in New York where a determined young man may obtain

[007-092] the—er—stuff, and I should be infinitely obliged—and my poor sister

[007-093] would be infinitely grateful—if you would keep an eye on him.” He

[007-094] hailed a taxi-cab. “I am sending Seacliff round to the Cosmopolis

[007-095] to-night. I am sure you will do everything you can. Good-bye, my boy,

[007-096] good-bye.”

[007-097] Archie continued his walk. This, he felt, was beginning to be a bit

[007-098] thick. He smiled a bitter, mirthless smile as he recalled the fact that

[007-099] less than half an hour had elapsed since he had expressed a regret that

[007-100] he did not belong to the ranks of those who do things. Fate since then

[007-101] had certainly supplied him with jobs with a lavish hand. By bed-time he

[007-102] would be an active accomplice to a theft, valet and companion to a

[007-103] snake he had never met, and—as far as could gather the scope of his

[007-104] duties—a combination of nursemaid and private detective to dear old

[007-105] Squiffy.

[007-106] It was past four o’clock when he returned to the Cosmopolis. Roscoe

[007-107] Sherriff was pacing the lobby of the hotel nervously, carrying a small

[007-108] hand-bag.

[007-109] “Here you are at last! Good heavens, man, I’ve been waiting two hours.”

[007-110] “Sorry, [[old-bean|old bean]]. I was musing a bit and lost track of the time.”

[007-111] The Press-agent looked cautiously round. There was nobody within

[007-112] earshot.

[007-113] “Here he is!” he said.

[007-114] “Who?”

[007-115] “Peter.”

[007-116] “Where?” said Archie, staring blankly.

[007-117] “In this bag. Did you expect to find him strolling arm-in-arm with me

[007-118] round the lobby? Here you are! Take him!”

[007-119] He was gone. And Archie, holding the bag, made his way to the lift. The

[007-120] bag squirmed gently in his grip.

[007-121] The only other occupant of the lift was a striking-looking woman of

[007-122] foreign [[appearance-and-first-impressions|appearance]], dressed in a way that made Archie feel that she

[007-123] must be somebody or she couldn’t look like that. Her face, too, seemed

[007-124] vaguely familiar. She entered the lift at the second floor where the

[007-125] tea-room is, and she had the contented expression of one who had tea’d

[007-126] to her satisfaction. She got off at the same floor as Archie, and

[007-127] walked swiftly, in a lithe, pantherish way, round the bend in the

[007-128] corridor. Archie followed more slowly. When he reached the door of his

[007-129] room, the passage was empty. He inserted the key in his door, turned

[007-130] it, pushed the door open, and pocketed the key. He was about to enter

[007-131] when the bag again squirmed gently in his grip.

[007-132] From the days of Pandora, through the epoch of Bluebeard’s wife, down

[007-133] to the present time, one of the chief failings of humanity has been the

[007-134] disposition to open things that were better closed. It would have been

[007-135] simple for Archie to have taken another step and put a door between

[007-136] himself and the world, but there came to him the irresistible desire to

[007-137] peep into the bag now—not three seconds later, but now. All the way up

[007-138] in the lift he had been battling with the temptation, and now he

[007-139] succumbed.

[007-140] The bag was one of those simple bags with a thingummy which you press.

[007-141] Archie pressed it. And, as it opened, out popped the head of Peter. His

[007-142] eyes met Archie’s. Over his head there seemed to be an invisible mark

[007-143] of interrogation. His gaze was curious, but kindly. He appeared to be

[007-144] saying to himself, “Have I found a friend?”

[007-145] Serpents, or Snakes, says the Encyclopaedia, are reptiles of the

[007-146] saurian class Ophidia, characterised by an elongated, cylindrical,

[007-147] limbless, scaly form, and distinguished from lizards by the fact that

[007-148] the halves (_rami_) of the lower jaw are not solidly united at the

[007-149] chin, but movably connected by an elastic ligament. The vertebra are

[007-150] very numerous, gastrocentrous, and procoelous. And, of course, when

[007-151] they put it like that, you can see at once that a man might spend hours

[007-152] with combined entertainment and profit just looking at a snake.

[007-153] Archie would no doubt have done this; but long before he had time

[007-154] really to inspect the halves (_rami_) of his new friend’s lower jaw and

[007-155] to admire its elastic fittings, and long before the gastrocentrous and

[007-156] procoelous character of the other’s vertebrae had made any real

[007-157] impression on him, a piercing scream almost at his elbow—startled him

[007-158] out of his scientific reverie. A door opposite had opened, and the

[007-159] woman of the elevator was standing staring at him with an expression of

[007-160] horror and fury that went through, him like a knife. It was the

[007-161] expression which, more than anything else, had made Mme. Brudowska what

[007-162] she was professionally. Combined with a deep voice and a sinuous walk,

[007-163] it enabled her to draw down a matter of a thousand dollars per week.

[007-164] Indeed, though the fact gave him little pleasure, Archie, as a matter

[007-165] of fact, was at this moment getting about—including war-tax—two dollars

[007-166] and seventy-five cents worth of the great emotional star for nothing.

[007-167] For, having treated him gratis to the look of horror and fury, she now

[007-168] moved towards him with the sinuous walk and spoke in the tone which she

[007-169] seldom permitted herself to use before the curtain of act two, unless

[007-170] there was a whale of a situation that called for it in act one.

[007-171] “Thief!”

[007-172] It was the way she said it.

[007-173] Archie staggered backwards as though he had been hit between the eyes,

[007-174] fell through the open door of his room, kicked it to with a flying

[007-175] foot, and collapsed on the bed. Peter, the snake, who had fallen on the

[007-176] floor with a squashy sound, looked surprised and pained for a moment;

[007-177] then, being a philosopher at heart, cheered up and began hunting for

[007-178] flies under the bureau.

[007-179] CHAPTER VIII.

[007-180] A DISTURBED NIGHT FOR DEAR OLD SQUIFFY

[007-181] Peril sharpens the intellect. Archie’s mind as a rule worked in rather

[007-182] a languid and restful sort of way, but now it got going with a rush and

[007-183] a whir. He glared round the room. He had never seen a room so devoid of

[007-184] satisfactory cover. And then there came to him a scheme, a ruse. It

[007-185] offered a chance of escape. It was, indeed, a bit of all right.

[007-186] Peter, the snake, loafing contentedly about the carpet, found himself

[007-187] seized by what the Encyclopaedia calls the “distensible gullet” and

[007-188] looked up reproachfully. The next moment he was in his bag again; and

[007-189] Archie, bounding silently into the bathroom, was tearing the cord off

[007-190] his dressing-gown.

[007-191] There came a banging at the door. A voice spoke sternly. A masculine

[007-192] voice this time.

[007-193] “Say! Open this door!”
