# Summer Storms

**Summer Storms** is the title of Chapter XV of *Indiscretions of Archie*, and the name given to a romantic subplot in which [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]] attempts to reconcile an estranged couple — a young woman known as [[the-girl-friend|the Girl Friend]] and a baseball pitcher — before an important game between the [[new-york-giants|New York Giants]] and the [[pittsburg-pirates|Pittsburg Pirates]].

## The Problem

The chapter opens with Archie realising the urgency of his mission. The metaphorical "summer storms" are emotional: a lovers' quarrel threatens to derail the pitching performance of a key player. As the narrative puts it:

> What he wanted to do was to point out to the injured girl all that hung on her. He wished to touch her heart, to plead with her, to desire her to restate her war-aims, and to persuade her — before three o'clock when that stricken gentleman would be stepping into the pitcher's box to loose off the first ball against the Pittsburg Pirates — to let bygones be bygones and forgive Augustus Biddle. (018-087–094)

## The Girl Friend's Grievance

Before Archie can mediate, the reader is given the Girl Friend's own account of the quarrel — delivered in a vibrant vernacular monologue to the hotel cigar-stand girl. The dispute, it transpires, began at the movies:

> "It was a Bryant Washburn film, and somehow, whenever I see him on the screen, nothing else seems to matter. I just get that goo-ey feeling, and couldn't start a fight if you asked me to. So we go off to have a soda, and I said to him, 'That sure was a lovely film, Gus!' and would you believe me, he says straight out that he didn't think it was such a much, and he thought Bryant Washburn was a pill! A pill!" (018-049–055)

The Girl Friend's outrage — delivered "with emotion" that her companion receives with shocked sympathy — is rooted in a perceived slight against her romantic taste. She walked out mid-soda: "I wasn't more than half-way through my vanilla and maple, but I got up without a word and left him" (018-058–059). Though she admits she will eventually forgive Gus, she sets a firm timeline — "it won't be for a week" (018-068) — a resolution that, as the cigar-stand girl notes, could do lasting damage to the Giants' pennant hopes.

## Archie as Mediator

The chapter follows Archie's comic struggles to find the right moment to intervene. His difficulty is both logistical and social: "he couldn't yell at the girl in a crowded street-car; and, if he let go of his strap and bent over her, somebody would step on his neck" (018-095–097). The scene develops into one of the novel's characteristic set-pieces, with Archie navigating the fraught emotional terrain of someone else's romance while the clock ticks toward game time.

## Narrative Function

"Summer Storms" connects the novel's baseball subplot (the bet on the Giants–Pirates game) with its romantic comedy elements. Archie's self-appointed role as peacemaker reflects his broader pattern of well-meaning but chaotic intervention in other people's affairs — a quality that defines his character throughout the novel.
