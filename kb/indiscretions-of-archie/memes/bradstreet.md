# Bradstreet

Bradstreet is a credit reference publication referenced in an early exchange between [[daniel-brewster|Daniel Brewster]] and [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]] — a fleeting but telling allusion that reveals [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]]'s suspicions about his new [[fatherhood-and-authority|son-in-law]]'s motives.

## Overview

Bradstreet (formally, Bradstreet's) was a well-known credit rating agency and reference book in early twentieth-century America. Published as a commercial reference, it listed businesses and individuals with their estimated creditworthiness — an essential tool for merchants, bankers, and anyone extending credit in an era before computerized credit checks.

In its mention in [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]], Bradstreet functions as a pointed accusation disguised as a joke. When [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]] suggests [[archie-moffam|Archie]] consulted Bradstreet before proposing to [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]], he is implying that [[archie-moffam|Archie]] married for [[wealth-and-money|money]] — that his courtship was, in effect, a commercial transaction vetted by the standard reference work of American [[business-and-enterprise|commerce]].

## In the Novel

> "I suppose you looked me up in Bradstreet before committing yourself?" (002-184)

[[daniel-brewster|Brewster]] delivers this line with characteristic sarcasm. The implication is clear: [[archie-moffam|Archie]], perpetually broke, must have verified that his bride's father was a man of substance before "committing himself" to the marriage. The line is a blow aimed at [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s honor — and [[archie-moffam|Archie]], to his credit, is genuinely dismayed by it: "I never looked at it like that before!" he responds.

The reference encapsulates [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]]'s central tension between love and [[wealth-and-money|money]], sincerity and calculation. [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]], a businessman who sees the world through the lens of commercial transactions, can conceive of marriage only as a kind of merger — one for which due diligence (checking Bradstreet) would be a natural prerequisite.

## Real-World Context

The Bradstreet Company was founded in 1849 by John Bradstreet and became one of America's first credit reporting agencies. It later merged with R.G. Dun & Company to form Dun & Bradstreet (D&B), which remains active today. The reference in [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]]'s [[1921]] novel would have been instantly recognizable to contemporary readers as shorthand for commercial due diligence — making [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]]'s accusation all the more pointed.
