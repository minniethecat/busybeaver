## Section 18

[018-001] Encouraged by these thoughts, [[archie-moffam|Archie]] lunched with an untroubled mind.

[018-002] Luncheon concluded, he proceeded to the lobby to buy back his hat and

[018-003] stick from the boy brigand with whom he had left them. It was while he

[018-004] was conducting this financial operation that he observed that at the

[018-005] cigar-stand, which adjoined the coat-and-hat alcove, his friend behind

[018-006] the counter had become engaged in [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]] with another girl.

[018-007] This was a determined looking young woman in a blue [[appearance-and-first-impressions|dress]] and a large

[018-008] hat of a bold and flowery species. [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] happening to attract her

[018-009] attention, she gave him a glance out of a pair of fine brown eyes,

[018-010] then, as if she did not think much of him, turned to her companion and

[018-011] resumed their conversation—which, being of an essentially private and

[018-012] intimate nature, she conducted, after the manner of her kind, in a

[018-013] ringing soprano which penetrated into every corner of the lobby.

[018-014] Archie, waiting while the brigand reluctantly made change for a dollar

[018-015] bill, was privileged to hear every word.

[018-016] “Right from the start I seen he was in a ugly mood. _You_ know how he

[018-017] gets, dearie! Chewing his upper lip and looking at you as if you were

[018-018] so much dirt beneath his feet! How was _I_ to know he’d lost fifteen

[018-019] dollars fifty-five playing poker, and anyway, I don’t see where he gets

[018-020] a licence to work off his grouches on me. And I told him so. I said to

[018-021] him, ‘Gus,’ I said, ‘if you can’t be bright and smiling and cheerful

[018-022] when you take me out, why do you come round at all? Was I wrong or

[018-023] right, dearie?”

[018-024] The girl behind the counter heartily endorsed her conduct. “Once you

[018-025] let a man think he could use you as a door-mat, where were you?”

[018-026] “What happened then, honey?”

[018-027] “Well, after that we went to the movies.”

[018-028] Archie started convulsively. The change from his dollar-bill leaped in

[018-029] his hand. Some of it sprang overboard and tinkled across the floor,

[018-030] with the brigand in pursuit. A monstrous suspicion had begun to take

[018-031] root in his mind.

[018-032] “Well, we got good seats, but—well, you know how it is, once things

[018-033] start going wrong. You know that hat of mine, the one with the daisies

[018-034] and cherries and the feather—I’d taken it off and given it him to hold

[018-035] when we went in, and what do you think that fell’r’d done? Put it on

[018-036] the floor and crammed it under the seat, just to save himself the

[018-037] trouble of holding it on his lap! And, when I showed him I was upset,

[018-038] all he said was that he was a pitcher and not a hatstand!”

[018-039] Archie was paralysed. He paid no attention to the hat-check boy, who

[018-040] was trying to induce him to accept treasure-trove to the amount of

[018-041] forty-five cents. His whole being was concentrated on this frightful

[018-042] tragedy which had burst upon him like a tidal wave. No possible room

[018-043] for doubt remained. “Gus” was the only Gus in [[new-york-city|New York]] that mattered,

[018-044] and this resolute and injured female before him was the Girl Friend, in

[018-045] whose slim hands rested the happiness of New York’s [[leisure-and-sports|baseball]] followers,

[018-046] the destiny of the unconscious [[new-york-giants|Giants]], and the fate of his thousand

[018-047] dollars. A strangled croak proceeded from his parched lips.

[018-048] “Well, I didn’t say anything at the moment. It just shows how them

[018-049] movies can work on a girl’s feelings. It was a Bryant [[washburn-mccall|Washburn]] film,

[018-050] and somehow, whenever I see him on the screen, nothing else seems to

[018-051] matter. I just get that goo-ey feeling, and couldn’t start a fight if

[018-052] you asked me to. So we go off to have a soda, and I said to him, ‘That

[018-053] sure was a lovely film, Gus!’ and would you believe me, he says

[018-054] straight out that he didn’t think it was such a much, and he thought

[018-055] Bryant Washburn was a pill! A pill!” [[the-girl-friend|The Girl Friend]]’s penetrating

[018-056] voice shook with emotion.

[018-057] “He never!” exclaimed the shocked cigar-stand girl.

[018-058] “He did, if I die the next moment! I wasn’t more than half-way through

[018-059] my vanilla and maple, but I got up without a word and left him. And I

[018-060] ain’t seen a sight of him since. So there you are, dearie! Was I right

[018-061] or wrong?”

[018-062] The cigar-stand girl gave unqualified approval. What men like Gus

[018-063] Biddle needed for the salvation of their souls was an occasional good

[018-064] jolt right where it would do most good.

[018-065] “I’m glad you think I acted right, dearie,” said the Girl Friend. “I

[018-066] guess I’ve been too weak with Gus, and he’s took advantage of it. I

[018-067] s’pose I’ll have to forgive him one of these old days, but, believe me,

[018-068] it won’t be for a week.”

[018-069] The cigar-stand girl was in favour of a fortnight.

[018-070] “No,” said the Girl Friend, regretfully. “I don’t believe I could hold

[018-071] out that long. But, if I speak to him inside a week, well—! Well, I

[018-072] gotta be going. Goodbye, honey.”

[018-073] The cigar-stand girl turned to attend to an impatient customer, and the

[018-074] Girl Friend, walking with the firm and decisive steps which indicate

[018-075] character, made for the swing-door leading to the street. And as she

[018-076] went, the paralysis which had pipped Archie released its hold. Still

[018-077] ignoring the forty-five cents which the boy continued to proffer, he

[018-078] leaped in her wake like a panther and came upon her just as she was

[018-079] stepping into a car. The car was full, but not too full for Archie. He

[018-080] dropped his five cents into the box and reached for a vacant strap. He

[018-081] looked down upon the flowered hat. There she was. And there he was.

[018-082] Archie rested his left ear against the forearm of a long,

[018-083] strongly-built young man in a grey suit who had followed him into the

[018-084] car and was sharing his strap, and pondered.

[018-085] CHAPTER XV.

[018-086] SUMMER STORMS

[018-087] Of course, in a way, the thing was simple. The wheeze was, in a sense,

[018-088] straightforward and uncomplicated. What he wanted to do was to point

[018-089] out to the injured girl all that hung on her. He wished to touch her

[018-090] heart, to plead with her, to desire her to restate her war-aims, and to

[018-091] persuade her—before three o’clock when that stricken [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] would be

[018-092] stepping into the pitcher’s box to loose off the first ball against the

[018-093] [[pittsburg-pirates|Pittsburg Pirates]]—to let bygones be bygones and forgive Augustus

[018-094] Biddle. But the blighted problem was, how the deuce to find the

[018-095] opportunity to start. He couldn’t yell at the girl in a crowded

[018-096] street-car; and, if he let go of his strap and bent over her, somebody

[018-097] would step on his neck.

[018-098] The Girl Friend, who for the first five minutes had remained entirely

[018-099] concealed beneath her hat, now sought diversion by looking up and

[018-100] examining the faces of the upper strata of passengers. Her eye caught

[018-101] Archie’s in a glance of recognition, and he smiled feebly, endeavouring

[018-102] to register bonhomie and good-will. He was surprised to see a startled

[018-103] expression come into her brown eyes. Her face turned pink. At least, it

[018-104] was pink already, but it turned pinker. The next moment, the car having

[018-105] stopped to pick up more passengers, she jumped off and started to hurry

[018-106] across the street.

[018-107] Archie was momentarily taken aback. When embarking on this business he

[018-108] had never intended it to become a blend of otter-hunting and a

[018-109] moving-picture chase. He followed her off the car with a sense that his

[018-110] grip on the affair was slipping. Preoccupied with these thoughts, he

[018-111] did not perceive that the long young man who had shared his strap had

[018-112] alighted too. His eyes were fixed on the vanishing figure of the Girl

[018-113] Friend, who, having buzzed at a smart pace into Sixth Avenue, was now

[018-114] legging it in the direction of the staircase leading to one of the

[018-115] stations of the Elevated Railroad. Dashing up the stairs after her, he

[018-116] shortly afterwards found himself suspended as before from a strap,

[018-117] gazing upon the now familiar flowers on top of her hat. From another

[018-118] strap farther down the carriage swayed the long young man in the grey

[018-119] suit.

[018-120] The train rattled on. Once or twice, when it stopped, the girl seemed

[018-121] undecided whether to leave or remain. She half rose, then sank back

[018-122] again. Finally she walked resolutely out of the car, and Archie,

[018-123] following, found himself in a part of New York strange to him. The

[018-124] inhabitants of this district appeared to eke out a precarious

[018-125] existence, not by taking in one another’s washing, but by selling one

[018-126] another second-hand clothes.

[018-127] Archie glanced at his watch. He had lunched early, but so crowded with

[018-128] emotions had been the period following [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]] that he was surprised to

[018-129] find that the hour was only just two. The discovery was a pleasant one.

[018-130] With a full hour before the scheduled start of the game, much might be

[018-131] achieved. He hurried after the girl, and came up with her just as she

[018-132] turned the corner into one of those forlorn New York side-streets which

[018-133] are populated chiefly by children, cats, desultory loafers, and empty

[018-134] meat-tins.

[018-135] The girl stopped and turned. Archie smiled a winning smile.

[018-136] “I say, my dear sweet creature!” he said. “I say, my dear old thing,

[018-137] one moment!”

[018-138] “Is that so?” said the Girl Friend.

[018-139] “I beg your pardon?”

[018-140] “Is that so?”

[018-141] Archie began to feel certain tremors. Her eyes were gleaming, and her

[018-142] determined mouth had become a perfectly straight line of scarlet. It

[018-143] was going to be difficult to be chatty to this girl. She was going to

[018-144] be a hard audience. Would mere words be able to touch her heart? The

[018-145] thought suggested itself that, properly speaking, one would need to use

[018-146] a pick-axe.

[018-147] “If you could spare me a couple of minutes of your valuable time—”

[018-148] “Say!” The lady drew herself up menacingly. “You tie a can to yourself

[018-149] and disappear! Fade away, or I’ll call a cop!”

[018-150] Archie was horrified at this misinterpretation of his motives. One or

[018-151] two children, playing close at hand, and a loafer who was trying to

[018-152] keep the wall from falling down, seemed pleased. Theirs was a

[018-153] colourless existence and to the rare purple moments which had enlivened

[018-154] it in the past the calling of a cop had been the unfailing preliminary.

[018-155] The loafer nudged a fellow-loafer, sunning himself against the same

[018-156] wall. The children, abandoning the meat-tin round which their game had

[018-157] centred, drew closer.

[018-158] “My dear old soul!” said Archie. “You don’t understand!”

[018-159] “Don’t I! I know your sort, you trailing arbutus!”

[018-160] “No, no! My dear old thing, believe me! I wouldn’t dream!”

[018-161] “Are you going or aren’t you?”

[018-162] Eleven more children joined the ring of spectators. The loafers stared

[018-163] silently, like awakened crocodiles.

[018-164] “But, I say, listen! I only wanted—”

[018-165] At this point another voice spoke.

[018-166] “Say!”

[018-167] The word “Say!” more almost than any word in the American language, is

[018-168] capable of a variety of shades of expression. It can be genial, it can

[018-169] be jovial, it can be appealing. It can also be truculent. The “Say!”

[018-170] which at this juncture smote upon Archie’s ear-drum with a suddenness

[018-171] which made him leap in the air was truculent; and the two loafers and

[018-172] twenty-seven children who now formed the audience were well satisfied

[018-173] with the dramatic development of the performance. To their experienced

[018-174] ears the word had the right ring.

[018-175] Archie spun round. At his elbow stood a long, strongly-built young man

[018-176] in a grey suit.

[018-177] “Well!” said the young man, nastily. And he extended a large, freckled

[018-178] face toward Archie’s. It seemed to the latter, as he backed against the

[018-179] wall, that the young man’s neck must be composed of india-rubber. It

[018-180] appeared to be growing longer every moment. His face, besides being

[018-181] freckled, was a dull brick-red in colour; his lips curled back in an

[018-182] unpleasant snarl, showing a gold tooth; and beside him, swaying in an

[018-183] ominous sort of way, hung two clenched red hands about the size of two

[018-184] young legs of mutton. Archie eyed him with a growing apprehension.

[018-185] There are moments in life when, passing idly on our way, we see a

[018-186] strange face, look into strange eyes, and with a sudden glow of human

[018-187] warmth say to ourselves, “We have found a friend!” This was not one of

[018-188] those moments. The only person Archie had ever seen in his life who

[018-189] looked less friendly was the sergeant-major who had trained him in the

[018-190] early days of [[world-war-i|the war]], before he had got his commission.
