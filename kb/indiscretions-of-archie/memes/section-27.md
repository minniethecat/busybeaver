## Section 27

[027-001] The girl had risen to her feet and was staring wildly.

[027-002] “[[sausage-chappie|John]]!” she cried.

[027-003] Even at this moment of crisis the Sausage Chappie was able to look

[027-004] relieved.

[027-005] “So it is!” he said. “And I thought it was Lancelot!”

[027-006] “I thought you were dead!”

[027-007] “I’m not!” said the Sausage Chappie.

[027-008] Mr. Gossett, speaking thickly through the fruit-salad, was understood

[027-009] to say that he regretted this. And then confusion broke loose again.

[027-010] Everybody began to [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] at once.

[027-011] “I say!” said [[archie-moffam|Archie]]. “I say! One moment!”

[027-012] Of the first stages of this interesting episode [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] had been a

[027-013] paralysed spectator. The thing had numbed him. And then—

[027-014] Sudden a thought came, like a full-blown rose.

[027-015] Flushing his brow.

[027-016] When he reached the gesticulating group, he was calm and business-like.

[027-017] He had a constructive policy to suggest.

[027-018] “I say,” he said. “I’ve got an idea!”

[027-019] “Go away!” said [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]]. “This is bad enough without you butting

[027-020] in.”

[027-021] Archie quelled him with a gesture.

[027-022] “Leave us,” he said. “We would be alone. I want to have a little

[027-023] business-talk with Mr. Gossett.” He turned to the movie-magnate, who

[027-024] was gradually emerging from the fruit-salad rather after the manner of

[027-025] a stout Venus rising from the sea. “Can you spare me a moment of your

[027-026] valuable time?”

[027-027] “I’ll have him arrested!”

[027-028] “Don’t you do it, laddie. Listen!”

[027-029] “The man’s mad. Throwing pies!”

[027-030] Archie attached himself to his coat-button.

[027-031] “Be calm, laddie. Calm and reasonable!”

[027-032] For the first time Mr. Gossett seemed to become aware that what he had

[027-033] been looking on as a vague annoyance was really an individual.

[027-034] “Who the devil are you?”

[027-035] Archie drew himself up with dignity.

[027-036] “I am this [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]’s representative,” he replied, indicating the

[027-037] Sausage Chappie with a motion of the hand. “His jolly old personal

[027-038] representative. I act for him. And on his behalf I have a pretty ripe

[027-039] proposition to lay before you. Reflect, [[old-bean|dear old bean]],” he proceeded

[027-040] earnestly. “Are you going to let this chance slip? The opportunity of a

[027-041] lifetime which will not occur again. By Jove, you ought to rise up and

[027-042] embrace this bird. You ought to clasp the chappie to your bosom! He has

[027-043] thrown pies at you, hasn’t he? Very well. You are a movie-magnate. Your

[027-044] whole [[wealth-and-money|fortune]] is founded on chappies who throw pies. You probably scour

[027-045] the world for chappies who throw pies. Yet, when one comes right to you

[027-046] without any fuss or trouble and demonstrates before your very eyes the

[027-047] fact that he is without a peer as a pie-propeller, you get the wind up

[027-048] and talk about having him arrested. Consider! (There’s a bit of cherry

[027-049] just behind your left ear.) Be sensible. Why let your personal feeling

[027-050] stand in the way of doing yourself a bit of good? Give this chappie a

[027-051] job and give it him quick, or we go elsewhere. Did you ever see Fatty

[027-052] Arbuckle handle pastry with a surer touch? Has Charlie Chaplin got this

[027-053] fellow’s speed and control. Absolutely not. I tell you, old friend,

[027-054] you’re in danger of throwing away a good thing!”

[027-055] He paused. The Sausage Chappie beamed.

[027-056] “I’ve aways wanted to go into the movies,” he said. “I was an actor

[027-057] before [[world-war-i|the war]]. Just remembered.”

[027-058] Mr. Brewster attempted to speak. Archie waved him down.

[027-059] “How many times have I got to tell you not to butt in?” he said,

[027-060] severely.

[027-061] Mr. Gossett’s militant demeanour had become a trifle modified during

[027-062] Archie’s harangue. First and foremost a man of business, Mr. Gossett

[027-063] was not insensible to the arguments which had been put forward. He

[027-064] brushed a slice of orange from the back of his neck, and mused awhile.

[027-065] “How do I know this fellow would screen well?” he said, at length.

[027-066] “Screen well!” cried Archie. “Of course he’ll screen well. Look at his

[027-067] face. I ask you! The map! I call your attention to it.” He turned

[027-068] apologetically to the Sausage Chappie. “Awfully sorry, old lad, for

[027-069] dwelling on this, but it’s business, you know.” He turned to Mr.

[027-070] Gossett. “Did you ever see a face like that? Of course not. Why should

[027-071] I, as this gentleman’s personal representative, let a face like that go

[027-072] to waste? There’s a fortune in it. By Jove, I’ll give you two minutes

[027-073] to think the thing over, and, if you don’t talk business then, I’ll

[027-074] jolly well take my man straight round to Mack Sennett or someone. We

[027-075] don’t have to ask for jobs. We consider offers.”

[027-076] There was a silence. And then the clear voice of the child in the

[027-077] sailor suit made itself heard again.

[027-078] “Mummie!”

[027-079] “Yes, darling?”

[027-080] “Is the man with the funny face going to throw any more pies?”

[027-081] “No, darling.”

[027-082] The child uttered a scream of disappointed fury.

[027-083] “I want the funny man to throw some more pies! I want the funny man to

[027-084] throw some more pies!”

[027-085] A look almost of awe came into Mr. Gossett’s face. He had heard the

[027-086] voice of the Public. He had felt the beating of the Public’s pulse.

[027-087] “Out of the mouths of babes and sucklings,” he said, picking a piece of

[027-088] banana off his right eyebrow, “Out of the mouths of babes and

[027-089] sucklings. Come round to my office!”

[027-090] CHAPTER XXI.

[027-091] THE GROWING BOY

[027-092] The lobby of [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] Hotel was a favourite stamping-ground of

[027-093] Mr. Daniel Brewster, its proprietor. He liked to wander about there,

[027-094] keeping a paternal eye on things, rather in the manner of the Jolly

[027-095] Innkeeper (hereinafter to be referred to as Mine Host) of the

[027-096] old-fashioned novel. Customers who, hurrying in to [[dining-and-social-ritual|dinner]], tripped over

[027-097] Mr. Brewster, were apt to mistake him for the hotel detective—for his

[027-098] eye was keen and his aspect a trifle austere—but, nevertheless, he was

[027-099] being as jolly an innkeeper as he knew how. His presence in the lobby

[027-100] supplied a personal touch to [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]] which other [[new-york-city|New York]] hotels

[027-101] lacked, and it undeniably made the girl at the book-stall

[027-102] extraordinarily civil to her clients, which was all to the good.

[027-103] Most of the time Mr. Brewster stood in one spot and just looked

[027-104] thoughtful; but now and again he would wander to the marble slab behind

[027-105] which he kept the desk-clerk and run his eye over the register, to see

[027-106] who had booked rooms—like a child examining the stocking on Christmas

[027-107] morning to ascertain what Santa Claus had brought him.

[027-108] As a rule, Mr. Brewster concluded this performance by shoving the book

[027-109] back across the marble slab and resuming his meditations. But one night

[027-110] a week or two after the Sausage Chappie’s sudden restoration to the

[027-111] normal, he varied this procedure by starting rather violently, turning

[027-112] purple, and uttering an exclamation which was manifestly an exclamation

[027-113] of chagrin. He turned abruptly and cannoned into Archie, who, in

[027-114] company with [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]], happened to be crossing the lobby at the moment

[027-115] on his way to dine in their suite.

[027-116] Mr. Brewster apologised gruffly; then, recognising his victim, seemed

[027-117] to regret having done so.

[027-118] “Oh, it’s you! Why can’t you look where you’re going?” he demanded. He

[027-119] had suffered much from his [[fatherhood-and-authority|son-in-law]].

[027-120] “Frightfully sorry,” said Archie, amiably. “Never thought you were

[027-121] going to fox-trot backwards all over the fairway.”

[027-122] “You mustn’t bully Archie,” said Lucille, severely, attaching herself

[027-123] to her father’s back hair and giving it a punitive tug, “because he’s

[027-124] an angel, and I love him, and you must learn to love him, too.”

[027-125] “Give you lessons at a reasonable rate,” murmured Archie.

[027-126] Mr. Brewster regarded his young relative with a lowering eye.

[027-127] “What’s the matter, father darling?” asked Lucille. “You seem upset.”

[027-128] “I am upset!” Mr. Brewster snorted. “Some people have got a nerve!” He

[027-129] glowered forbiddingly at an inoffensive young man in a light overcoat

[027-130] who had just entered, and the young man, though his conscience was

[027-131] quite clear and Mr. Brewster an entire stranger to him, stopped dead,

[027-132] blushed, and went out again—to dine elsewhere. “Some people have got

[027-133] the nerve of an army mule!”

[027-134] “Why, what’s happened?”

[027-135] “Those darned McCalls have registered here!”

[027-136] “No!”

[027-137] “Bit beyond me, this,” said Archie, insinuating himself into the

[027-138] conversation. “Deep waters and what not! Who are the McCalls?”

[027-139] “Some people father dislikes,” said Lucille. “And they’ve chosen his

[027-140] hotel to stop at. But, father dear, you mustn’t mind. It’s really a

[027-141] compliment. They’ve come because they know it’s the best hotel in New

[027-142] York.”

[027-143] “Absolutely!” said Archie. “Good accommodation for man and beast! All

[027-144] the comforts of home! Look on the bright side, old bean. No good

[027-145] getting the wind up. Cherrio, old companion!”

[027-146] “Don’t call me old companion!”

[027-147] “Eh, what? Oh, right-o!”

[027-148] Lucille steered her husband out of the danger zone, and they entered

[027-149] the lift.

[027-150] “Poor father!” she said, as they went to their suite, “it’s a shame.

[027-151] They must have done it to annoy him. This man McCall has a place next

[027-152] to some property father bought in Westchester, and he’s bringing a

[027-153] law-suit against father about a bit of land which he claims belongs to

[027-154] him. He might have had the tact to go to another hotel. But, after all,

[027-155] I don’t suppose it was the poor little fellow’s fault. He does whatever

[027-156] his wife tells him to.”

[027-157] “We all do that,” said Archie the married man.

[027-158] Lucille eyed him fondly.

[027-159] “Isn’t it a shame, precious, that all husbands haven’t nice wives like

[027-160] me?”

[027-161] “When I think of you, by Jove,” said Archie, fervently, “I want to

[027-162] babble, absolutely babble!”

[027-163] “Oh, I was telling you about the McCalls. [[mr-mccall|Mr. McCall]] is one of those

[027-164] little, meek men, and his wife’s one of those big, bullying women. It

[027-165] was she who started all the trouble with father. Father and Mr. McCall

[027-166] were very fond of each other till she made him begin the suit. I feel

[027-167] sure she made him come to this hotel just to annoy father. Still,

[027-168] they’ve probably taken the most expensive suite in the place, which is

[027-169] something.”

[027-170] Archie was at the telephone. His mood was now one of quiet peace. Of

[027-171] all the happenings which went to make up existence in New York, he

[027-172] liked best the cosy _tête-à-tête_ dinners with Lucille in their suite,

[027-173] which, owing to their engagements—for Lucille was a popular girl, with

[027-174] many friends—occurred all too seldom.

[027-175] “Touching now the question of browsing and sluicing,” he said. “I’ll be

[027-176] getting them to send along a waiter.”

[027-177] “Oh, good gracious!”

[027-178] “What’s the matter?”

[027-179] “I’ve just remembered. I promised faithfully I would go and see Jane

[027-180] Murchison to-day. And I clean forgot. I must rush.”

[027-181] “But light of my soul, we are about to eat. Pop around and see her

[027-182] after dinner.”

[027-183] “I can’t. She’s going to a theatre to-night.”

[027-184] “Give her the jolly old miss-in-baulk, then, for the nonce, and spring

[027-185] round to-morrow.”

[027-186] “She’s sailing for [[england|England]] to-morrow morning, early. No, I must go and

[027-187] see her now. What a shame! She’s sure to make me stop to dinner, I tell

[027-188] you what. Order something for me, and, if I’m not back in half an hour,

[027-189] start.”

[027-190] “Jane Murchison,” said Archie, “is a bally nuisance.”

[027-191] “Yes. But I’ve known her since she was eight.”

[027-192] “If her parents had had any proper feeling,” said Archie, “they would

[027-193] have drowned her long before that.”

[027-194] He unhooked the receiver, and asked despondently to be connected with

[027-195] Room Service. He thought bitterly of the exigent Jane, whom he

[027-196] recollected dimly as a tall female with teeth. He half thought of going

[027-197] down to the grill-room on the chance of finding a friend there, but the

[027-198] waiter was on his way to the room. He decided that he might as well

[027-199] stay where he was.

[027-200] The waiter arrived, booked the order, and departed. Archie had just

[027-201] completed his toilet after a shower-bath when a musical clinking

[027-202] without announced the advent of the meal. He opened the door. The

[027-203] waiter was there with a table congested with things under covers, from

[027-204] which escaped a savoury and appetising odour. In spite of his

[027-205] depression, Archie’s soul perked up a trifle.
