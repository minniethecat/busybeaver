## Section 8

[008-001] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] rapidly attached the dressing-gown cord to the handle of the

[008-002] bag, leaped to the window, opened it, tied the cord to a projecting

[008-003] piece of iron on the sill, lowered Peter and the bag into the depths,

[008-004] and closed the window again. The whole affair took but a few seconds.

[008-005] Generals have received the thanks of their nations for displaying less

[008-006] resource on the field of battle.

[008-007] He opened the door. Outside stood the bereaved woman, and beside her a

[008-008] bullet-headed [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] with a bowler hat on the back of his head, in

[008-009] whom [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] recognised the hotel detective.

[008-010] The hotel detective also recognised Archie, and the stern cast of his

[008-011] features relaxed. He even smiled a rusty but propitiatory smile. He

[008-012] imagined—erroneously—that Archie, being the son-in-law of the owner of

[008-013] the hotel, had a pull with that gentleman; and he resolved to proceed

[008-014] warily lest he jeopardise his job.

[008-015] “Why, Mr. Moffam!” he said, apologetically. “I didn’t know it was you I

[008-016] was disturbing.”

[008-017] “Always glad to have a [[conversation-and-dialogue|chat]],” said Archie, cordially. “What seems to be

[008-018] the trouble?”

[008-019] “My snake!” cried the queen of tragedy. “Where is my snake?”

[008-020] Archie, looked at the detective. The detective looked at Archie.

[008-021] “This lady,” said the detective, with a dry little cough, “thinks her

[008-022] snake is in your room, Mr. Moffam.”

[008-023] “Snake?”

[008-024] “Snake’s what the lady said.”

[008-025] “My snake! My Peter!” Mme. Brudowska’s voice shook with emotion. “He is

[008-026] here—here in this room.”

[008-027] Archie shook his head.

[008-028] “No snakes here! Absolutely not! I remember noticing when I came in.”

[008-029] “The snake is here—here in this room. This man had it in a bag! I saw

[008-030] him! He is a thief!”

[008-031] “Easy, ma’am!” protested the detective. “Go easy! This gentleman is the

[008-032] boss’s son-in-law.”

[008-033] “I care not who he is! He has my snake! Here—here in this room!”

[008-034] “Mr. Moffam wouldn’t go round stealing snakes.”

[008-035] “Rather not,” said Archie. “Never stole a snake in my life. None of the

[008-036] Moffams have ever gone about stealing snakes. Regular family tradition!

[008-037] Though I once had an uncle who kept gold-fish.”

[008-038] “Here he is! Here! My Peter!”

[008-039] Archie looked at the detective. The detective looked at Archie. “We

[008-040] must humour her!” their glances said.

[008-041] “Of course,” said Archie, “if you’d like to search the room, what? What

[008-042] I mean to say is, this is Liberty Hall. Everybody welcome! Bring the

[008-043] kiddies!”

[008-044] “I will search the room!” said Mme. Brudowska.

[008-045] The detective glanced apologetically at Archie.

[008-046] “Don’t blame me for this, Mr. Moffam,” he urged.

[008-047] “Rather not! Only too glad you’ve dropped in!”

[008-048] He took up an easy attitude against the window, and watched the empress

[008-049] of the emotional drama explore. Presently she desisted, baffled. For an

[008-050] instant she paused, as though about to speak, then swept from the room.

[008-051] A moment later a door banged across the passage.

[008-052] “How do they get that way?” queried the detective, “Well, g’bye, Mr.

[008-053] Moffam. Sorry to have butted in.”

[008-054] The door closed. Archie waited a few moments, then went to the window

[008-055] and hauled in the slack. Presently the bag appeared over the edge of

[008-056] the window-sill.

[008-057] “Good God!” said Archie.

[008-058] In the rush and swirl of recent events he must have omitted to see that

[008-059] the clasp that fastened the bag was properly closed; for the bag, as it

[008-060] jumped on to the window-sill, gaped at him like a yawning face. And

[008-061] inside it there was nothing.

[008-062] Archie leaned as far out of the window as he could manage without

[008-063] committing suicide. Far below him, the traffic took its usual course

[008-064] and the pedestrians moved to and fro upon the pavements. There was no

[008-065] crowding, no excitement. Yet only a few moments before a long green

[008-066] snake with three hundred ribs, a distensible gullet, and gastrocentrous

[008-067] vertebras must have descended on that street like the gentle rain from

[008-068] Heaven upon the place beneath. And nobody seemed even interested. Not

[008-069] for the first time since he had arrived in America, Archie marvelled at

[008-070] the cynical detachment of the New Yorker, who permits himself to be

[008-071] surprised at nothing.

[008-072] He shut the window and moved away with a heavy heart. He had not had

[008-073] the pleasure of an extended acquaintanceship with Peter, but he had

[008-074] seen enough of him to realise his sterling qualities. Somewhere beneath

[008-075] Peter’s three hundred ribs there had lain a heart of gold, and Archie

[008-076] mourned for his loss.

[008-077] Archie had a [[dining-and-social-ritual|dinner]] and theatre engagement that night, and it was late

[008-078] when he returned to the hotel. He found his [[daniel-brewster|father-in-law]] prowling

[008-079] restlessly about the lobby. There seemed to be something on Mr.

[008-080] Brewster’s mind. He came up to Archie with a brooding frown on his

[008-081] square face.

[008-082] “Who’s this man Seacliff?” he demanded, without preamble. “I hear he’s

[008-083] a friend of yours.”

[008-084] “Oh, you’ve met him, what?” said Archie. “Had a nice little chat

[008-085] together, yes? Talked of this and that, no!”

[008-086] “We have not said a word to each other.”

[008-087] “Really? Oh, well, dear old [[lord-seacliff|Squiffy]] is one of those strong, silent

[008-088] fellers you know. You mustn’t mind if he’s a bit dumb. He never says

[008-089] much, but it’s whispered round the [[clubs-and-social-institutions|clubs]] that he thinks a lot. It was

[008-090] rumoured in the spring of nineteen-thirteen that Squiffy was on the

[008-091] point of making a bright remark, but it never came to anything.”

[008-092] Mr. Brewster struggled with his feelings.

[008-093] “Who _is_ he? You seem to know him.”

[008-094] “Oh yes. Great pal of mine, Squiffy. We went through Eton, Oxford, and

[008-095] the Bankruptcy Court together. And here’s a rummy coincidence. When

[008-096] they examined _me_, I had no assets. And, when they examined Squiffy,

[008-097] _he_ had no assets! Rather extraordinary, what?”

[008-098] Mr. Brewster seemed to be in no mood for discussing coincidences.

[008-099] “I might have known he was a friend of yours!” he said, bitterly.

[008-100] “Well, if you want to see him, you’ll have to do it outside my hotel.”

[008-101] “Why, I thought he was stopping here.”

[008-102] “He is—to-night. To-morrow he can look for some other hotel to break

[008-103] up.”

[008-104] “Great Scot! Has dear old Squiffy been breaking the place up?”

[008-105] Mr. Brewster snorted.

[008-106] “I am informed that this precious friend of yours entered my grill-room

[008-107] at eight o’clock. He must have been completely intoxicated, though the

[008-108] head waiter tells me he noticed nothing at the time.”

[008-109] Archie nodded approvingly.

[008-110] “Dear old Squiffy was always like that. It’s a gift. However woozled he

[008-111] might be, it was impossible to detect it with the naked eye. I’ve seen

[008-112] the dear old chap many a time whiffled to the eyebrows, and looking as

[008-113] sober as a bishop. Soberer! When did it begin to dawn on the lads in

[008-114] the grill-room that the old egg had been pushing the boat out?”

[008-115] “The head waiter,” said Mr. Brewster, with cold fury, “tells me that he

[008-116] got a hint of the man’s condition when he suddenly got up from his

[008-117] table and went the round of the room, pulling off all the table-cloths,

[008-118] and breaking everything that was on them. He then threw a number of

[008-119] rolls at the diners, and left. He seems to have gone straight to bed.”

[008-120] “Dashed sensible of him, what? Sound, practical chap, Squiffy. But

[008-121] where on earth did he get the—er—materials?”

[008-122] “From his room. I made enquiries. He has six large cases in his room.”

[008-123] “Squiffy always was a chap of infinite resource! Well, I’m dashed sorry

[008-124] this should have happened, don’t you know.”

[008-125] “If it hadn’t been for you, the man would never have come here.” Mr.

[008-126] Brewster brooded coldly. “I don’t know why it is, but ever since you

[008-127] came to this hotel I’ve had nothing but trouble.”

[008-128] “Dashed sorry!” said Archie, sympathetically.

[008-129] “Grrh!” said Mr. Brewster.

[008-130] Archie made his way meditatively to the lift. The injustice of his

[008-131] [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]]’s attitude pained him. It was absolutely rotten and all

[008-132] that to be blamed for everything that went wrong in the Hotel

[008-133] Cosmopolis.

[008-134] While this conversation was in progress, Lord Seacliff was enjoying a

[008-135] refreshing sleep in his room on the fourth floor. Two hours passed. The

[008-136] noise of the traffic in the street below faded away. Only the rattle of

[008-137] an occasional belated cab broke the silence. In the hotel all was

[008-138] still. Mr. Brewster had gone to bed. Archie, in his room, smoked

[008-139] meditatively. Peace may have been said to reign.

[008-140] At half-past two Lord Seacliff awoke. His hours of slumber were always

[008-141] irregular. He sat up in bed and switched the light on. He was a

[008-142] shock-headed young man with a red face and a hot brown eye. He yawned

[008-143] and stretched himself. His head was aching a little. The room seemed to

[008-144] him a trifle close. He got out of bed and threw open the window. Then,

[008-145] returning to bed, he picked up a book and began to read. He was

[008-146] conscious of feeling a little jumpy, and reading generally sent him to

[008-147] sleep.

[008-148] Much has been written on the subject of bed-books. The general

[008-149] consensus of opinion is that a gentle, slow-moving story makes the best

[008-150] opiate. If this be so, dear old Squiffy’s choice of literature had been

[008-151] rather injudicious. His book was _The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes_,

[008-152] and the particular story which he selected for perusal was the one

[008-153] entitled, “The Speckled Band.” He was not a great reader, but, when he

[008-154] read, he liked something with a bit of zip to it.

[008-155] Squiffy became absorbed. He had read the story before, but a long time

[008-156] back, and its complications were fresh to him. The tale, it may be

[008-157] remembered, deals with the activities of an ingenious gentleman who

[008-158] kept a snake, and used to loose it into people’s bedrooms as a

[008-159] preliminary to [[art-and-collecting|collecting]] on their insurance. It gave Squiffy pleasant

[008-160] thrills, for he had always had a particular horror of snakes. As a

[008-161] child, he had shrunk from visiting the serpent house at the Zoo; and,

[008-162] later, when he had come to man’s estate and had put off childish

[008-163] things, and settled down in real earnest to his self-appointed mission

[008-164] of drinking up all the alcoholic fluid in [[england|England]], the distaste for

[008-165] Ophidia had lingered. To a dislike for real snakes had been added a

[008-166] maturer shrinking from those which existed only in his imagination. He

[008-167] could still recall his emotions on the occasion, scarcely three months

[008-168] before, when he had seen a long, green serpent which a majority of his

[008-169] contemporaries had assured him wasn’t there.

[008-170] Squiffy read on:—

[008-171] “Suddenly another sound became audible—a very gentle, soothing sound,

[008-172] like that of a small jet of steam escaping continuously from a kettle.”

[008-173] Lord Seacliff looked up from his book with a start. Imagination was

[008-174] beginning to play him tricks. He could have sworn that he had actually

[008-175] heard that identical sound. It had seemed to come from the window. He

[008-176] listened again. No! All was still. He returned to his book and went on

[008-177] reading.

[008-178] “It was a singular sight that met our eyes. Beside the table, on a

[008-179] wooden chair, sat Doctor Grimesby Rylott, clad in a long dressing-gown.

[008-180] His chin was cocked upward and his eyes were fixed in a dreadful, rigid

[008-181] stare at the corner of the ceiling. Round his brow he had a peculiar

[008-182] yellow band, with brownish speckles, which seemed to be bound tightly

[008-183] round his head.”

[008-184] “I took a step forward. In an instant his strange head-gear began

[008-185] to move, and there reared itself from among his hair the squat,

[008-186] diamond-shaped head and puffed neck of a loathsome serpent...”

[008-187] “Ugh!” said Squiffy.

[008-188] He closed the book and put it down. His head was aching worse than

[008-189] ever. He wished now that he had read something else. No fellow could

[008-190] read himself to sleep with this sort of thing. People ought not to

[008-191] write this sort of thing.

[008-192] His heart gave a bound. There it was again, that hissing sound. And

[008-193] this time he was sure it came from the window.

[008-194] He looked at the window, and remained staring, frozen. Over the sill,

[008-195] with a graceful, leisurely movement, a green snake was crawling. As it

[008-196] crawled, it raised its head and peered from side to side, like a

[008-197] shortsighted man looking for his spectacles. It hesitated a moment on

[008-198] the edge of the sill, then wriggled to the floor and began to cross the

[008-199] room. Squiffy stared on.
