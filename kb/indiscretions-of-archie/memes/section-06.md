## Section 6

[006-001] “Well, it was this bathing suit, you know,” he said.

[006-002] “What bathing suit?”

[006-003] “Mine, don’t you know. A lemon-coloured contrivance. Rather bright and

[006-004] so forth, but in its proper place not altogether a bad egg. Well, the

[006-005] whole thing started, you know, with my standing on a bally pedestal

[006-006] sort of arrangement in a diving attitude—for the cover, you know. I

[006-007] don’t know if you have ever done anything of that kind yourself, but it

[006-008] gives you a most fearful crick in the spine. However, that’s rather

[006-009] beside the point, I suppose—don’t know why I mentioned it. Well, this

[006-010] morning he was dashed late, so I went out—”

[006-011] “What the devil are you talking about?”

[006-012] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] looked at him, surprised.

[006-013] “Aren’t I making it clear?”

[006-014] “No.”

[006-015] “Well, you understand about [[archies-lemon-coloured-bathing-suit|the bathing suit]], don’t you? The jolly old

[006-016] bathing suit, you’ve grasped that, what?”

[006-017] “No.”

[006-018] “Oh, I say,” said [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]]. “That’s rather a nuisance. I mean to say, the

[006-019] bathing suit’s what you might call the good old pivot of the whole

[006-020] dashed affair, you see. Well, you understand about the cover, what?

[006-021] You’re pretty clear on the subject of the cover?”

[006-022] “What cover?”

[006-023] “Why, for the magazine.”

[006-024] “What magazine?”

[006-025] “Now there you rather have me. One of these bright little periodicals,

[006-026] you know, that you see popping to and fro on the bookstalls.”

[006-027] “I don’t know what you’re talking about,” said the captain. He looked

[006-028] at Archie with an expression of distrust and hostility. “And I’ll tell

[006-029] you straight out I don’t like the [[appearance-and-first-impressions|looks]] of you. I believe you’re a pal

[006-030] of his.”

[006-031] “No longer,” said Archie, firmly. “I mean to say, a chappie who makes

[006-032] you stand on a bally pedestal sort of arrangement and get a crick in

[006-033] the spine, and then doesn’t turn up and leaves you biffing all over the

[006-034] countryside in a bathing suit—”

[006-035] The reintroduction of the bathing suit motive seemed to have the worst

[006-036] effect on the captain. He flushed darkly.

[006-037] “Are you trying to josh me? I’ve a mind to soak you!”

[006-038] “If ye plaze, sorr,” cried [[officer-donahue|Officer Donahue]] and [[officer-cassidy|Officer Cassidy]] in

[006-039] chorus. In the course of their professional career they did not often

[006-040] hear their superior make many suggestions with which they saw eye to

[006-041] eye, but he had certainly, in their opinion, spoken a mouthful now.

[006-042] “No, honestly, my dear old thing, nothing was farther from my

[006-043] thoughts—”

[006-044] He would have spoken further, but at this moment the world came to an

[006-045] end. At least, that was how it sounded. Somewhere in the immediate

[006-046] neighbourhood something went off with a vast explosion, shattering the

[006-047] glass in the window, peeling the plaster from the ceiling, and sending

[006-048] him staggering into the inhospitable arms of Officer Donahue.

[006-049] The three guardians of the Law stared at one another.

[006-050] “If ye plaze, sorr,” said Officer Cassidy, saluting.

[006-051] “Well?”

[006-052] “May I spake, sorr?”

[006-053] “Well?”

[006-054] “Something’s exploded, sorr!”

[006-055] The information, kindly meant though it was, seemed to annoy the

[006-056] captain.

[006-057] “What the devil did you think I thought had happened?” he demanded,

[006-058] with not a little irritation, “It was a bomb!”

[006-059] Archie could have corrected this diagnosis, for already a faint but

[006-060] appealing aroma of an alcoholic nature was creeping into the room

[006-061] through a hole in the ceiling, and there had risen before his eyes the

[006-062] picture of [[j-b-wheeler|J. B. Wheeler]] affectionately regarding that barrel of his on

[006-063] the previous morning in the studio upstairs. J. B. Wheeler had wanted

[006-064] quick results, and he had got them. Archie had long since ceased to

[006-065] regard J. B. Wheeler as anything but a tumour on the social system, but

[006-066] he was bound to admit that he had certainly done him a good turn now.

[006-067] Already these honest men, diverted by the superior attraction of this

[006-068] latest happening, appeared to have forgotten his existence.

[006-069] “Sorr!” said Officer Donahue.

[006-070] “Well?”

[006-071] “It came from upstairs, sorr.”

[006-072] “Of course it came from upstairs. Cassidy!”

[006-073] “Sorr?”

[006-074] “Get down into the street, call up the reserves, and stand at the front

[006-075] entrance to keep the crowd back. We’ll have the whole city here in five

[006-076] minutes.”

[006-077] “Right, sorr.”

[006-078] “Don’t let anyone in.”

[006-079] “No, sorr.”

[006-080] “Well, see that you don’t. Come along, Donahue, now. Look slippy.”

[006-081] “On the spot, sorr!” said Officer Donahue.

[006-082] A moment later Archie had the studio to himself. Two minutes later he

[006-083] was picking his way cautiously down the fire-escape after the manner of

[006-084] the recent [[elmer-m-moon|Mr. Moon]]. Archie had not seen much of Mr. Moon, but he had

[006-085] seen enough to know that in certain crises his methods were sound and

[006-086] should be followed. Elmer Moon was not a good man; his ethics were poor

[006-087] and his moral code shaky; but in the matter of legging it away from a

[006-088] situation of peril and discomfort he had no superior.

[006-089] CHAPTER VII.

[006-090] MR. ROSCOE SHERRIFF HAS AN IDEA

[006-091] Archie inserted a fresh cigarette in his long holder and began to smoke

[006-092] a little moodily. It was about a week after his disturbing adventures

[006-093] in J. B. Wheeler’s studio, and life had ceased for the moment to be a

[006-094] thing of careless enjoyment. Mr. Wheeler, mourning over his lost

[006-095] home-brew and refusing, like Niobe, to be comforted, has suspended the

[006-096] sittings for the magazine cover, thus robbing Archie of his life-work.

[006-097] [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]] had not been in genial mood of late. And, in addition to

[006-098] all this, [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] was away on a visit to a school-friend. And when

[006-099] Lucille went away, she took with her the sunshine. Archie was not

[006-100] surprised at her being popular and in demand among her friends, but

[006-101] that did not help him to become reconciled to her absence.

[006-102] He gazed rather wistfully across the table at his friend, Roscoe

[006-103] [[roscoe-sherriff|Sherriff]], the Press-agent, another of his Pen-and-Ink Club

[006-104] acquaintances. They had just finished [[dining-and-social-ritual|lunch]], and during the meal

[006-105] Sherriff, who, like most men of action, was fond of hearing the sound

[006-106] of his own voice and liked exercising it on the subject of himself, had

[006-107] been telling Archie a few anecdotes about his professional past. From

[006-108] these the latter had conceived a picture of Roscoe Sherriff’s life as a

[006-109] prismatic thing of energy and adventure and well-paid withal—just the

[006-110] sort of life, in fact, which he would have enjoyed leading himself. He

[006-111] wished that he, too, like the Press-agent, could go about the place

[006-112] “slipping things over” and “putting things across.” Daniel Brewster, he

[006-113] felt, would have beamed upon a son-in-law like Roscoe Sherriff.

[006-114] “The more I see of America,” sighed Archie, “the more it amazes me. All

[006-115] you birds seem to have been doing things from the cradle upwards. I

[006-116] wish I could do things!”

[006-117] “Well, why don’t you?”

[006-118] Archie flicked the ash from his cigarette into the finger-bowl.

[006-119] “Oh, I don’t know, you know,” he said, “Somehow, none of our family

[006-120] ever have. I don’t know why it is, but whenever a Moffam starts out to

[006-121] do things he infallibly makes a bloomer. There was a Moffam in the

[006-122] Middle Ages who had a sudden spasm of energy and set out to make a

[006-123] pilgrimage to Jerusalem, dressed as a wandering friar. Rum ideas they

[006-124] had in those days.”

[006-125] “Did he get there?”

[006-126] “Absolutely not! Just as he was leaving the front door his favourite

[006-127] hound mistook him for a tramp—or a varlet, or a scurvy knave, or

[006-128] whatever they used to call them at that time—and bit him in the fleshy

[006-129] part of the leg.”

[006-130] “Well, at least he started.”

[006-131] “Enough to make a chappie start, what?”

[006-132] Roscoe Sherriff sipped his coffee thoughtfully. He was an apostle of

[006-133] Energy, and it seemed to him that he could make a convert of Archie and

[006-134] incidentally do himself a bit of good. For several days he had been,

[006-135] looking for someone like Archie to help him in a small matter which he

[006-136] had in mind.

[006-137] “If you’re really keen on doing things,” he said, “there’s something

[006-138] you can do for me right away.”

[006-139] Archie beamed. Action was what his soul demanded.

[006-140] “Anything, dear boy, anything! State your case!”

[006-141] “Would you have any objection to putting up a snake for me?”

[006-142] “Putting up a snake?”

[006-143] “Just for a day or two.”

[006-144] “But how do you mean, old soul? Put him up where?”

[006-145] “Wherever you live. Where do you live? The Cosmopolis, isn’t it? Of

[006-146] course! You married old Brewster’s daughter. I remember reading about

[006-147] it.”

[006-148] “But, I say, laddie, I don’t want to spoil your day and disappoint you

[006-149] and so forth, but my jolly old [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]] would never let me keep a

[006-150] snake. Why, it’s as much as I can do to make him let me stop on in the

[006-151] place.”

[006-152] “He wouldn’t know.”

[006-153] “There’s not much that goes on in the hotel that he doesn’t know,” said

[006-154] Archie, doubtfully.

[006-155] “He mustn’t know. The whole point of the thing is that it must be a

[006-156] dead secret.”

[006-157] Archie flicked some more ash into the finger-bowl.

[006-158] “I don’t seem absolutely to have grasped the affair in all its aspects,

[006-159] if you know what I mean,” he said. “I mean to say—in the first

[006-160] place—why would it brighten your young existence if I entertained this

[006-161] snake of yours?”

[006-162] “It’s not mine. It belongs to Mme. Brudowska. You’ve heard of her, of

[006-163] course?”

[006-164] “Oh yes. She’s some sort of performing snake female in vaudeville or

[006-165] something, isn’t she, or something of that species or order?”

[006-166] “You’re near it, but not quite right. She is the leading exponent of

[006-167] high-brow tragedy on any stage in the civilized world.”

[006-168] “Absolutely! I remember now. My wife lugged me to see her perform one

[006-169] night. It all comes back to me. She had me wedged in an orchestra-stall

[006-170] before I knew what I was up against, and then it was too late. I

[006-171] remember reading in some journal or other that she had a pet snake,

[006-172] given her by some Russian prince or other, what?”

[006-173] “That,” said Sherriff, “was the impression I intended to convey when I

[006-174] sent the story to the papers. I’m her Press-agent. As a matter of fact,

[006-175] I bought Peter-its name’s Peter-myself down on the East Side. I always

[006-176] believe in animals for Press-agent stunts. I’ve nearly always had good

[006-177] results. But with Her Nibs I’m handicapped. Shackled, so to speak. You

[006-178] might almost say my genius is stifled. Or strangled, if you prefer it.”

[006-179] “Anything you say,” agreed Archie, courteously, “But how? Why is your

[006-180] what-d’you-call-it what’s-its-named?”

[006-181] “She keeps me on a leash. She won’t let me do anything with a kick in

[006-182] it. If I’ve suggested one rip-snorting stunt, I’ve suggested twenty,

[006-183] and every time she turns them down on the ground that that sort of

[006-184] thing is beneath the dignity of an artist in her position. It doesn’t

[006-185] give a fellow a chance. So now I’ve made up my mind to do her good by

[006-186] stealth. I’m going to steal her snake.”

[006-187] “Steal it? Pinch it, as it were?”

[006-188] “Yes. Big story for the papers, you see. She’s grown very much attached

[006-189] to Peter. He’s her mascot. I believe she’s practically kidded herself

[006-190] into believing that Russian prince story. If I can sneak it away and

[006-191] keep it away for a day or two, she’ll do the rest. She’ll make such a

[006-192] fuss that the papers will be full of it.”

[006-193] “I see.”

[006-194] “Wow, any ordinary woman would work in with me. But not Her Nibs. She

[006-195] would call it cheap and degrading and a lot of other things. It’s got

[006-196] to be a genuine steal, and, if I’m caught at it, I lose my job. So

[006-197] that’s where you come in.”

[006-198] “But where am I to keep the jolly old reptile?”

[006-199] “Oh, anywhere. Punch a few holes in a hat-box, and make it up a

[006-200] shakedown inside. It’ll be company for you.”

[006-201] “Something in that. My wife’s away just now and it’s a bit lonely in

[006-202] the evenings.”

[006-203] “You’ll never be lonely with Peter around. He’s a great scout. Always

[006-204] merry and bright.”
