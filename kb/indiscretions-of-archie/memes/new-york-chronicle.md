# New York Chronicle

The *New York Chronicle* is a fictional New York morning newspaper that appears in Chapter XXIX of [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]]. It serves as a small but pointed vehicle for domestic comedy at the McCall breakfast table.

## In the Novel

The *Chronicle* enters the narrative during a tense morning at the McCall household:

> All through breakfast the *New York Chronicle* had been lying neatly folded beside her plate. She now opened it, and, with a remark about looking for the report of her yesterday's lecture at the Butterfly Club, directed her gaze at the front page, on which she hoped that an editor with the best interests of the public at heart had decided to place her. (029-134–029-139)

[[mrs-mccall|Mrs. McCall]], the food-reformer and lecturer on [[rational-eating]], scans the front page hoping to see her own name in print. The moment is watched closely by her husband:

> Mr. McCall, jumping up and down behind his glasses, scrutinised her face closely as she began to read. He always did this on these occasions, for none knew better than he that his comfort for the day depended largely on the tone of the morning paper. (029-140–029-142)

The *Chronicle* thus functions as a barometer of the McCall household's emotional weather: whether Mrs. McCall finds favorable coverage determines whether her husband's day will be peaceful or stormy.

## Role in the Novel

The newspaper appears only briefly, but its narrative role is precise. It crystallizes Mrs. McCall's vanity and professional self-regard — her assumption that a lecture to a women's club merits front-page coverage — and Mr. McCall's well-practiced vigilance in monitoring her moods. Like many of Wodehouse's small props, the folded newspaper on the breakfast table opens a window into a marriage.
