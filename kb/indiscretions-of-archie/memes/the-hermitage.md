# The Hermitage

The Hermitage is a summer resort hotel that serves as the setting for a four-chapter sequence in *Indiscretions of Archie*, chapters 13 through 16. Advertised with "unrivalled scenery, superb cuisine" (013-093), it provides the backdrop for the Percy-the-bulldog incident and its theatrical consequences. The hotel functions as a self-contained comic world — a pastoral pressure cooker in which actors, guests, staff, and a disruptive bulldog collide with escalating absurdity.

## Description

The Hotel Hermitage is situated in a rural resort setting, "encircling" its grounds with natural beauty (013-177). As a summer destination, it attracts both theatrical companies in rehearsal and vacationing hotel guests from the Cosmopolis circle, including Lucille Brewster. The hotel's self-presentation as an idyllic retreat — "unrivalled scenery, superb cuisine" — is undercut at every turn by the chaos that unfolds within its walls.

Its physical layout — a central hotel building surrounded by grounds, with rehearsal spaces shared between the theatrical company and the regular guests — creates the conditions for the comic collisions that define the Hermitage sequence. The enclosed nature of a resort, where guests cannot easily escape one another's company, amplifies every misunderstanding and confrontation.

## Role in the Novel

George Benham's theatrical production, starring Miss Silverton, is in rehearsal at the Hermitage. The juxtaposition of a working theatrical company with vacationing hotel guests creates a fertile ground for farce. Lucille Brewster stays at the hotel during the summer season and "returned to the Hermitage, all smiles" after a day in the city (016-203). The housemaids at the Hotel Hermitage were known to "use the space below the bed" for storage (015-130), a detail that plays into the comic chaos of the bulldog incident.

The Hermitage chapters form a distinct narrative unit within the novel — a summer interlude that takes the characters out of their usual New York habitat and places them in a more compressed, more volatile environment. The resort setting strips away the protective layers of urban routine, leaving the characters exposed to one another's eccentricities in ways that the Hotel Cosmopolis never quite permits.

## The Percy Incident

Miss Silverton's bulldog, Percy, is a central agent of chaos at the Hermitage. The dog's presence in the hotel disrupts rehearsals and leads to a series of comedic misunderstandings. When Benham, the theatre manager, mistreats Percy during a rehearsal, Miss Silverton retaliates by brandishing a pistol — an act that escalates the already farcical situation to operatic heights.

The enclosed hotel environment amplifies the disorder, as the theatrical company, hotel guests, and staff collide in the confined resort setting. The housemaids' habit of storing items under the bed (015-130) becomes a plot point when the search for Percy (or objects related to him) sends characters rummaging through the hotel's hidden spaces. Each new discovery under a bed or behind a door compounds the comic confusion.

## Guests and Visitors

- **Lucille Brewster** — Stays at the Hermitage during the summer season, bridging the novel's New York and resort settings (016-203)
- **Miss Silverton** — The actress whose theatrical company is in rehearsal at the hotel; her bulldog Percy and her pistol are the engines of Hermitage chaos
- **George Benham** — The theatrical producer running rehearsals at the Hermitage; his mistreatment of Percy triggers the central confrontation

## Comparison with Hotel Cosmopolis

Unlike the urban Hotel Cosmopolis in New York, the Hermitage represents a pastoral escape — though one that proves no less chaotic. Where the Cosmopolis is defined by business and social maneuvering, the Hermitage is defined by leisure, art, and the absurdities of summer resort life.

The two hotels form a complementary pair in the novel's geography: the Cosmopolis is vertical, urban, and driven by the rhythms of commerce; the Hermitage is horizontal, rural, and governed by the looser logic of vacation. Yet both prove equally susceptible to the disorder that follows Archie and his circle. The shift from one to the other — from city hotel to country resort — does not so much change the nature of the comedy as transpose it into a different key.
