# Pen-and-Ink Club

A New York coffee-house and social club that serves as a gathering place for the city's artistic and literary set. Archie Moffam is introduced to this Bohemian circle through his acquaintance with J. B. Wheeler the artist, and it becomes one of his regular haunts.

## In the Novel

The Pen-and-Ink Club is first referenced as the venue where Archie meets J. B. Wheeler, "the artist, at the Pen-and-Ink Club" (PN: 003-083). It is grouped among "the other resorts of the artist, the author, the actor, and the Bohemian" (PN: 003-095) — the places where Archie feels most at home in New York, preferring them to more conventional social venues.

The club's membership includes Roscoe Sherriff, "the Press-agent, another of his Pen-and-Ink Club acquaintances" (PN: 006-103), who becomes a significant figure in Archie's life. Through the Pen-and-Ink, Archie gains access to a network of creative professionals who provide him with odd jobs, artistic opportunities, and social connections that help him navigate New York society.

## Key Members

- **[[j-b-wheeler]]** — The artist Archie meets at the club, who hires him as a magazine cover model
- **[[roscoe-sherriff]]** — A press agent and fellow club member who becomes Archie's friend
- **[[archie-moffam]]** — A regular patron who finds the club more congenial than his father-in-law's business circles

## Cultural Significance

The Pen-and-Ink represents the Bohemian counterpoint to the commercial world of Daniel Brewster's hotel business. Where the Hotel Cosmopolis embodies corporate America, the Pen-and-Ink embodies artistic New York — a space of creative freedom and casual camaraderie that aligns with Archie's temperament and values.
