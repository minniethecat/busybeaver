## Overview

"Pip-pip," "cheerio," and "toodle-oo" form a cluster of period British farewell expressions used by [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]] in *[[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Indiscretions of Archie]]*. The most memorable deployment occurs when [[archie-moffam|Archie]] strings all three — plus "good-bye-ee" — together in a single rapid-fire exit line, showcasing [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]]'s ear for the rhythmic absurdity of period public-school slang.

## In the Novel

The expressions appear most notably in Chapter XIII ("Rallying Round Percy"), when [[archie-moffam|Archie]] attempts to make a swift exit from an awkward encounter with [[miss-silverton|Miss Silverton]]:

> "In that case," said Archie, relieved, "cheerio, good luck, pip-pip, toodle-oo, and good-bye-ee! I'll be shifting!" (016-089–016-090)

The cumulative effect of four farewell expressions in a single breath is characteristic of [[archie-moffam|Archie]]'s verbal style — an accumulation of slang that is both ingratiating and absurd. The scene is immediately undercut as [[miss-silverton|Miss Silverton]] refuses to let him leave:

> "Yes, you will!" cried Miss Silverton, energetically. (016-091)

"Toodle-oo" reappears independently later when [[archie-moffam|Archie]] dismisses a business matter with characteristic breeziness:

> sign bills for any reasonable amount any time you want a cigar or a stick of chocolate. Well, toodle-oo!" (021-090)

## Cultural Context

These expressions were current in British public-school and upper-class slang of the early 20th century. "Pip-pip" was a conventional informal farewell; "cheerio" combined farewell with good wishes; "toodle-oo" (sometimes spelled "toodle-pip") was a more playful variant. [[p-g-wodehouse|Wodehouse]]'s comic effect comes from piling all of them together, turning a simple goodbye into a verbal performance piece.
