# Boy Scouts of America

The Boy Scouts of America appear twice in the novel as a comic reference point for selfless action. Archie Moffam, in his moments of earnest (if clumsy) altruism, reaches for the Boy Scout as the cultural archetype of the good-deed-doer — a comparison that the narrative deploys with characteristic Wodehousian irony.

## In the Novel

The first reference comes when Archie, having performed some small service, lights a cigarette and reflects: "He felt like a Boy Scout who has done his day's good deed" (028-109). The simile is affectionate rather than mocking — Archie genuinely wishes to be helpful, and the Boy Scout provides the vocabulary for that aspiration.

The second reference is more pointed. A character observes approvingly: "This is the sort of thing Boy Scouts do!" (035-012) — again using the organization as shorthand for praiseworthy conduct. In both instances, the Boy Scouts function as a cultural touchstone accessible to both British and American readers, a shared reference in the transatlantic world the novel inhabits.

## Key Passages

> "Archie lit a cigarette. He felt like a Boy Scout who has done his day's good deed." (028-109)

> "This is the sort of thing Boy Scouts do! Well, of course, it is." (035-012)

## Real-World Context

The Boy Scouts of America was founded in 1910, modelled on Robert Baden-Powell's Boy Scout movement established in Britain in 1907. By the early 1920s, when *Indiscretions of Archie* was written and published, the organization was firmly established in American culture. The "daily good turn" was — and remains — a core tenet of Scout philosophy, making it the natural reference point for a character measuring his own altruistic impulses.
