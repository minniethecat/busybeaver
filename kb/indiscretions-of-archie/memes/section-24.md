## Section 24

[024-001] Bill relaxed his attitude. He deflated his chest, spread his heels, and

[024-002] ceased to draw in his abdomen.

[024-003] “We’d better try this another time, when we’re alone,” he said,

[024-004] frigidly. “I can’t do myself justice.”

[024-005] “Why do you want to do yourself justice?” asked [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]].

[024-006] “Right-o!” said [[archie-moffam|Archie]], affably, casting off his forbidding expression

[024-007] like a garment. “Rehearsal postponed. I was just putting old Bill

[024-008] through it,” he explained, “with a view to getting him into mid-season

[024-009] form for the jolly old pater.”

[024-010] “Oh!” Lucille’s voice was the voice of one who sees light in darkness.

[024-011] “When Bill walked in like a cat on hot bricks and stood there looking

[024-012] stuffed, that was just the Personality That Wins!”

[024-013] “That was it.”

[024-014] “Well, you couldn’t blame me for not recognising it, could you?”

[024-015] [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]] patted her head paternally.

[024-016] “A little less of the caustic critic stuff,” he said. “Bill will be all

[024-017] right on the night. If you hadn’t come in then and put him off his

[024-018] stroke, he’d have shot out some amazing stuff, full of authority and

[024-019] dynamic accents and what not. I tell you, light of my soul, old Bill is

[024-020] all right! He’s got the winning personality up a tree, ready whenever

[024-021] he wants to go and get it. Speaking as his backer and trainer, I think

[024-022] he’ll twist your father round his little finger. Absolutely! It

[024-023] wouldn’t surprise me if at the end of five minutes the good old dad

[024-024] started jumping through hoops and sitting up for lumps of sugar.”

[024-025] “It would surprise _me_.”

[024-026] “Ah, that’s because you haven’t seen old Bill in action. You crabbed

[024-027] his act before he had begun to spread himself.”

[024-028] “It isn’t that at all. The reason why I think that Bill, however

[024-029] winning his personality may be, won’t persuade father to let him marry

[024-030] a girl in the chorus is something that happened last night.”

[024-031] “Last night?”

[024-032] “Well, at three o’clock this morning. It’s on the front page of the

[024-033] early editions of the evening papers. I brought one in for you to see,

[024-034] only you were so busy. Look! There it is!”

[024-035] Archie seized the paper.

[024-036] “Oh, Great Scot!”

[024-037] “What is it?” asked Bill, irritably. “Don’t stand goggling there! What

[024-038] the devil is it?”

[024-039] “Listen to this, old thing!”

[024-040] REVELRY BY NIGHT.

[024-041] SPIRITED BATTLE ROYAL AT HOTEL

[024-042] COSMOPOLIS.

[024-043] THE HOTEL DETECTIVE HAD A GOOD HEART

[024-044] BUT PAULINE PACKED THE PUNCH.

[024-045] The logical contender for Jack Dempsey’s championship honours has been

[024-046] discovered; and, in an age where women are stealing men’s jobs all the

[024-047] time, it will not come as a surprise to our readers to learn that she

[024-048] belongs to the sex that is more deadly than the male. Her name is Miss

[024-049] [[pauline-preston|Pauline Preston]], and her wallop is vouched for under oath—under many

[024-050] oaths—by Mr. Timothy O’Neill, known to his intimates as Pie-Face, who

[024-051] holds down the arduous job of detective at the [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|Hotel Cosmopolis]].

[024-052] At three o’clock this morning, Mr. O’Neill was advised by the

[024-053] night-clerk that the occupants of every room within earshot of number

[024-054] 618 had ’phoned the desk to complain of a disturbance, a noise, a vocal

[024-055] uproar proceeding from the room mentioned. Thither, therefore, marched

[024-056] Mr. O’Neill, his face full of cheese-sandwich, (for he had been

[024-057] indulging in an early [[dining-and-social-ritual|breakfast]] or a late supper) and his heart of

[024-058] devotion to duty. He found there the Misses Pauline Preston and

[024-059] “Bobbie” St. Clair, of the personnel of the chorus of the Frivolities,

[024-060] entertaining a few friends of either sex. A pleasant time was being had

[024-061] by all, and at the moment of Mr. O’Neill’s entry the entire strength of

[024-062] the company was rendering with considerable emphasis that touching

[024-063] ballad, “There’s a Place For Me In Heaven, For My Baby-Boy Is There.”

[024-064] The able and efficient officer at once suggested that there was a place

[024-065] for them in the street and the patrol-wagon was there; and, being a man

[024-066] of action as well as words, proceeded to gather up an armful of

[024-067] assorted guests as a preliminary to a personally-conducted tour onto

[024-068] the cold night. It was at this point that Miss Preston stepped into the

[024-069] limelight. Mr. O’Neill contends that she hit him with a brick, an iron

[024-070] casing, and the Singer Building. Be that as it may, her efforts were

[024-071] sufficiently able to induce him to retire for reinforcements, which,

[024-072] arriving, arrested the supper-party regardless of age or sex.

[024-073] At the police-court this morning Miss Preston maintained that she and

[024-074] her friends were merely having a quiet home-evening and that Mr.

[024-075] O’Neill was no [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]. The male guests gave their names respectively

[024-076] as Woodrow Wilson, David Lloyd-George, and William J. Bryan. These,

[024-077] however, are believed to be incorrect. But the moral is, if you want

[024-078] excitement rather than sleep, stay at the [[hotel-cosmopolis|Hotel Cosmopolis]].

[024-079] Bill may have quaked inwardly as he listened to this epic but outwardly

[024-080] he was unmoved.

[024-081] “Well,” he said, “what about it?”

[024-082] “What about it!” said Lucille.

[024-083] “What about it!” said Archie. “Why, my dear old friend, it simply means

[024-084] that all the time we’ve been putting in making your personality winning

[024-085] has been chucked away. Absolutely a dead loss! We might just as well

[024-086] have read a manual on how to knit sweaters.”

[024-087] “I don’t see it,” maintained Bill, stoutly.

[024-088] Lucille turned apologetically to her husband.

[024-089] “You mustn’t judge me by him, Archie, darling. This sort of thing

[024-090] doesn’t run in the family.-We are supposed to be rather bright on the

[024-091] whole. But poor Bill was dropped by his nurse when he was a baby, and

[024-092] fell on his head.”

[024-093] “I suppose what you’re driving at,” said the goaded Bill, “is that what

[024-094] has happened will make father pretty sore against girls who happen to

[024-095] be in the chorus?”

[024-096] “That’s absolutely it, old thing, I’m sorry to say. The next person who

[024-097] mentions the word chorus-girl in the jolly old governor’s presence is

[024-098] going to take his life in his hands. I tell you, as one man to another,

[024-099] that I’d much rather be back in France hopping over the top than do it

[024-100] myself.”

[024-101] “What darned nonsense! Mabel may be in the chorus, but she isn’t like

[024-102] those girls.”

[024-103] “Poor old Bill!” said Lucille. “I’m awfully sorry, but it’s no use not

[024-104] facing facts. You know perfectly well that the reputation of the hotel

[024-105] is the thing father cares more about than anything else in the world,

[024-106] and that this is going to make him furious with all the chorus-girls in

[024-107] creation. It’s no good trying to explain to him that your Mabel is in

[024-108] the chorus but not of the chorus, so to speak.”

[024-109] “Deuced well put!” said Archie, approvingly. “You’re absolutely right.

[024-110] A chorus-girl by the river’s brim, so to speak, a simple chorus-girl is

[024-111] to him, as it were, and she is nothing more, if you know what I mean.”

[024-112] “So now,” said Lucille, “having shown you that the imbecile scheme

[024-113] which you concocted with my poor well-meaning husband is no good at

[024-114] all, I will bring you words of cheer. Your own original plan—of getting

[024-115] your Mabel a part in a comedy—was always the best one. And you can do

[024-116] it. I wouldn’t have broken the bad news so abruptly if I hadn’t had

[024-117] some consolation to give you afterwards. I met [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]] just

[024-118] now, wandering about as if the cares of the world were on his

[024-119] shoulders, and he told me that he was putting up most of the [[wealth-and-money|money]] for

[024-120] a new play that’s going into rehearsal right away. Reggie’s an old

[024-121] friend of yours. All you have to do is to go to him and ask him to use

[024-122] his influence to get your Mabel a small part. There’s sure to be a maid

[024-123] or something with only a line or two that won’t matter.”

[024-124] “A ripe scheme!” said Archie. “Very sound and fruity!”

[024-125] The cloud did not lift from Bill’s corrugated brow.

[024-126] “That’s all very well,” he said. “But you know what a talker Reggie is.

[024-127] He’s an obliging sort of chump, but his tongue’s fastened on at the

[024-128] middle and waggles at both ends. I don’t want the whole of [[new-york-city|New York]] to

[024-129] know about my engagement, and have somebody spilling the news to

[024-130] father, before I’m ready.”

[024-131] “That’s all right,” said Lucille. “Archie can speak to him. There’s no

[024-132] need for him to mention your name at all. He can just say there’s a

[024-133] girl he wants to get a part for. You would do it, wouldn’t you,

[024-134] angel-face?”

[024-135] “Like a bird, queen of my soul.”

[024-136] “Then that’s splendid. You’d better give Archie that photograph of

[024-137] Mabel to give to Reggie, Bill.”

[024-138] “Photograph?” said Bill. “Which photograph? I have twenty-four!”

[024-139] Archie found Reggie van Tuyl brooding in a window of his club that

[024-140] looked over [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]. Reggie was a rather melancholy young man who

[024-141] suffered from elephantiasis of the bank-roll and the other evils that

[024-142] arise from that complaint. Gentle and sentimental by nature, his

[024-143] sensibilities had been much wounded by contact with a sordid world; and

[024-144] the thing that had first endeared Archie to him was the fact that the

[024-145] latter, though chronically hard-up, had never made any attempt to

[024-146] borrow money from him. Reggie would have parted with it on demand, but

[024-147] it had delighted him to find that Archie seemed to take a pleasure in

[024-148] his society without having any ulterior motives. He was fond of Archie,

[024-149] and also of Lucille; and their happy marriage was a constant source of

[024-150] gratification to him.

[024-151] For Reggie was a sentimentalist. He would have liked to live in a world

[024-152] of ideally united couples, himself ideally united to some charming and

[024-153] affectionate girl. But, as a matter of cold fact, he was a bachelor,

[024-154] and most of the couples he knew were veterans of several divorces. In

[024-155] Reggie’s circle, therefore, the home-life of Archie and Lucille shone

[024-156] like a good deed in a naughty world. It inspired him. In moments of

[024-157] depression it restored his waning faith in human nature.

[024-158] Consequently, when Archie, having greeted him and slipped into a chair

[024-159] at his side, suddenly produced from his inside pocket the photograph of

[024-160] an extremely pretty girl and asked him to get her a small part in the

[024-161] play which he was financing, he was shocked and disappointed. He was in

[024-162] a more than usually sentimental mood that afternoon, and had, indeed,

[024-163] at the moment of Archie’s arrival, been dreaming wistfully of soft arms

[024-164] clasped snugly about his collar and the patter of little feet and all

[024-165] that sort of thing.-He gazed reproachfully at Archie.

[024-166] “Archie!” his voice quivered with emotion. “Is it worth it?, is it

[024-167] worth it, old man?-Think of the poor little woman at home!”

[024-168] Archie was puzzled.

[024-169] “Eh, old top? Which poor little woman?”

[024-170] “Think of her trust in you, her faith—“.

[024-171] “I don’t absolutely get you, [[old-bean|old bean]].”

[024-172] “What would Lucille say if she knew about this?”

[024-173] “Oh, she does. She knows all about it.”

[024-174] “Good heavens!” cried Reggie. He was shocked to the core of his

[024-175] being. One of the articles of his faith was that the union of Lucille

[024-176] and Archie was different from those loose partnerships which were the

[024-177] custom in his world. He had not been conscious of such a poignant

[024-178] feeling that the foundations of the universe were cracked and tottering

[024-179] and that there was no light and sweetness in life since the morning,

[024-180] eighteen months back, when a negligent valet had sent him out into

[024-181] Fifth Avenue with only one spat on.

[024-182] “It was Lucille’s idea,” explained Archie. He was about to mention his

[024-183] brother-in-law’s connection with the matter, but checked himself in

[024-184] time, remembering Bill’s specific objection to having his secret

[024-185] revealed to Reggie. “It’s like this, old thing, I’ve never met this

[024-186] female, but she’s a pal of Lucille’s”—he comforted his conscience by

[024-187] the reflection that, if she wasn’t now, she would be in a few days-“and

[024-188] Lucille wants to do her a bit of good. She’s been on the stage in

[024-189] [[england|England]], you know, supporting a jolly old widowed mother and educating

[024-190] a little brother and all that kind and species of rot, you understand,

[024-191] and now she’s coming over to America, and Lucille wants you to rally

[024-192] round and shove her into your show and generally keep the home fires

[024-193] burning and so forth. How do we go?”
