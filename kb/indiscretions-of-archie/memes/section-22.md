## Section 22

[022-001] “That busy brain is never still,” explained [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]].

[022-002] “Saw it in the paper this morning. An advertisement of a book, don’t

[022-003] you know.”

[022-004] “I’ve no time for reading.”

[022-005] “You’ve time for reading this one, laddie, for you can’t afford to miss

[022-006] it. It’s a what-d’you-call-it book. What I mean to say is, if you read

[022-007] it and take its tips to heart, it guarantees to make you a convincing

[022-008] talker. The advertisement says so. The advertisement’s all about a

[022-009] chappie whose name I forget, whom everybody loved because he talked so

[022-010] well. And, mark you, before he got hold of this book—_The Personality

[022-011] That Wins_ was the name of it, if I remember rightly—he was known to

[022-012] all the lads in the office as Silent Samuel or something. Or it may

[022-013] have been Tongue-Tied Thomas. Well, one day he happened by good luck to

[022-014] blow in the necessary for the good old P. that W.’s, and now, whenever

[022-015] they want someone to go and [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] Rockefeller or someone into lending

[022-016] them a million or so, they send for Samuel. Only now they call him

[022-017] Sammy the Spell-Binder and fawn upon him pretty copiously and all that.

[022-018] How about it, old son? How do we go?”

[022-019] “What perfect nonsense,” said Lucille.

[022-020] “I don’t know,” said Bill, plainly impressed. “There might be something

[022-021] in it.”

[022-022] “Absolutely!” said [[archie-moffam|Archie]]. “I remember it said, ‘Talk convincingly, and

[022-023] no man will ever treat you with cold, unresponsive indifference.’ Well,

[022-024] cold, unresponsive indifference is just what you don’t want the pater

[022-025] to treat you with, isn’t it, or is it, or isn’t it, what? I mean,

[022-026] what?”

[022-027] “It sounds all right,” said Bill.

[022-028] “It _is_ all right,” said [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]]. “It’s a scheme! I’ll go farther. It’s

[022-029] an egg!”

[022-030] “The idea I had,” said Bill, “was to see if I couldn’t get Mabel a job

[022-031] in some straight comedy. That would take the curse off the thing a bit.

[022-032] Then I wouldn’t have to dwell on the chorus end of the business, you

[022-033] see.”

[022-034] “Much more sensible,” said Lucille.

[022-035] “But what a-deuce of a sweat”—argued Archie. “I mean to say, having to

[022-036] pop round and nose about and all that.”

[022-037] “Aren’t you willing to take a little trouble for your stricken

[022-038] brother-in-law, worm?” said Lucille severely.

[022-039] “Oh, absolutely! My idea was to get this book and coach the dear old

[022-040] chap. Rehearse him, don’t you know. He could bone up the early chapters

[022-041] a bit and then drift round and try his convincing talk on me.”

[022-042] “It might be a good idea,” said Bill reflectively.

[022-043] “Well, I’ll tell you what _I’m_ going to do,” said Lucille. “I’m going

[022-044] to get Bill to introduce me to his Mabel, and, if she’s as nice as he

[022-045] says she is, _I’ll_ go to father and talk convincingly to him.”

[022-046] “You’re an ace!” said Bill.

[022-047] “Absolutely!” agreed Archie cordially. “_My_ partner, what! All the

[022-048] same, we ought to keep the book as a second string, you know. I mean to

[022-049] say, you are a young and delicately nurtured girl—full of sensibility

[022-050] and shrinking what’s-its-name and all that—and you know what the jolly

[022-051] old pater is. He might bark at you and put you out of action in the

[022-052] first round. Well, then, if anything like that happened, don’t you see,

[022-053] we could unleash old Bill, the trained silver-tongued expert, and let

[022-054] him have a shot. Personally, I’m all for the P. that W.’s.”—“Me, too,”

[022-055] said Bill.

[022-056] Lucille looked at her watch.

[022-057] “Good gracious! It’s nearly one o’clock!”

[022-058] “No!” Archie heaved himself up from his chair. “Well, it’s a shame to

[022-059] break up this feast of reason and flow of soul and all that, but, if we

[022-060] don’t leg it with some speed, we shall be late.”

[022-061] “We’re lunching at the Nicholson’s!” explained Lucille to her brother.

[022-062] “I wish you were coming too.”

[022-063] “Lunch!” Bill shook his head with a kind of tolerant scorn. “Lunch

[022-064] means nothing to me these days. I’ve other things to think of besides

[022-065] [[dining-and-social-ritual|food]].” He looked as spiritual as his rugged features would permit. “I

[022-066] haven’t written to Her yet to-day.”

[022-067] “But, dash it, old scream, if she’s going to be over here in a week,

[022-068] what’s the good of writing? The letter would cross her.”

[022-069] “I’m not mailing my [[letters-and-correspondence|letters]] to [[england|England]],” said Bill. “I’m keeping them

[022-070] for her to read when she arrives.”

[022-071] “My sainted aunt!” said Archie.

[022-072] Devotion like this was something beyond his outlook.

[022-073] CHAPTER XVIII.

[022-074] THE SAUSAGE CHAPPIE

[022-075] _The Personality That Wins_ cost Archie two dollars in cash and a lot

[022-076] of embarrassment when he asked for it at the store. To buy a treatise

[022-077] of that name would automatically seem to argue that you haven’t a

[022-078] winning personality already, and Archie was at some pains to explain to

[022-079] the girl behind the counter that he wanted it for a friend. The girl

[022-080] seemed more interested in his English accent than in his explanation,

[022-081] and Archie was uncomfortably aware, as he receded, that she was

[022-082] practising it in an undertone for the benefit of her colleagues and

[022-083] fellow-workers. However, what is a little discomfort, if endured in

[022-084] friendship’s name?

[022-085] He was proceeding up Broadway after leaving the store when he

[022-086] encountered [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]], who was drifting along in somnambulistic

[022-087] fashion near Thirty-Ninth Street.

[022-088] “Hullo, Reggie old thing!” said Archie.

[022-089] “Hullo!” said Reggie, a man of few words.

[022-090] “I’ve just been buying a book for Bill [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]],” went on Archie. “It

[022-091] appears that old Bill—What’s the matter?”

[022-092] He broke off his recital abruptly. A sort of spasm had passed across

[022-093] his companion’s features. The hand holding Archie’s arm had tightened

[022-094] convulsively. One would have said that Reginald had received a shock.

[022-095] “It’s nothing,” said Reggie. “I’m all right now. I caught sight of that

[022-096] fellow’s clothes rather suddenly. They shook me a bit. I’m all right

[022-097] now,” he said, bravely.

[022-098] Archie, following his friend’s gaze, understood. Reggie van Tuyl was

[022-099] never at his strongest in the morning, and he had a sensitive eye for

[022-100] clothes. He had been known to resign from [[clubs-and-social-institutions|clubs]] because members

[022-101] exceeded the bounds in the matter of soft shirts with dinner-jackets.

[022-102] And the short, thick-set man who was standing just in front of them in

[022-103] attitude of restful immobility was certainly no dandy. His best friend

[022-104] could not have called him dapper. Take him for all in all and on the

[022-105] hoof, he might have been posing as a model for a sketch of What the

[022-106] Well-Dressed Man Should Not Wear.

[022-107] In costume, as in most other things, it is best to take a definite line

[022-108] and stick to it. This man had obviously vacillated. His neck was

[022-109] swathed in a green scarf; he wore an evening-[[appearance-and-first-impressions|dress]] coat; and his lower

[022-110] limbs were draped in a pair of tweed trousers built for a larger man.

[022-111] To the north he was bounded by a straw hat, to the south by brown

[022-112] shoes.

[022-113] Archie surveyed the man’s back carefully.

[022-114] “Bit thick!” he said, sympathetically. “But of course Broadway isn’t

[022-115] [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]. What I mean to say is, Bohemian licence and what not.

[022-116] Broadway’s crammed with deuced brainy devils who don’t care how they

[022-117] look. Probably this bird is a master-mind of some species.”

[022-118] “All the same, man’s no right to wear evening-dress coat with tweed

[022-119] trousers.”

[022-120] “Absolutely not! I see what you mean.”

[022-121] At this point the sartorial offender turned. Seen from the front, he

[022-122] was even more unnerving. He appeared to possess no shirt, though this

[022-123] defect was offset by the fact that the tweed trousers fitted snugly

[022-124] under the arms. He was not a handsome man. At his best he could never

[022-125] have been that, and in the recent past he had managed to acquire a scar

[022-126] that ran from the corner of his mouth half-way across his cheek. Even

[022-127] when his face was in repose he had an odd expression; and when, as he

[022-128] chanced to do now, he smiled, odd became a mild adjective, quite

[022-129] inadequate for purposes of description. It was not an unpleasant face,

[022-130] however. Unquestionably genial, indeed. There was something in it that

[022-131] had a quality of humorous appeal.

[022-132] Archie started. He stared at the man, Memory stirred.

[022-133] “Great Scot!” he cried. “It’s the Sausage Chappie!”

[022-134] Reginald van Tuyl gave a little moan. He was not used to this sort of

[022-135] thing. A sensitive young man as regarded scenes, Archie’s behaviour

[022-136] unmanned him. For Archie, releasing his arm, had bounded forward and

[022-137] was shaking the other’s hand warmly.

[022-138] “Well, well, well! My dear old chap! You must remember me, what? No?

[022-139] Yes?”

[022-140] The man with the scar seemed puzzled. He shuffled the brown shoes,

[022-141] patted the straw hat, and eyed Archie questioningly.

[022-142] “I don’t seem to place you,” he said.

[022-143] Archie slapped the back of the evening-dress coat. He linked his arm

[022-144] affectionately with that of the dress-reformer.

[022-145] “We met outside St Mihiel in [[world-war-i|the war]]. You gave me a bit of sausage. One

[022-146] of the most sporting events in history. Nobody but a real sportsman

[022-147] would have parted with a bit of sausage at that moment to a stranger.

[022-148] Never forgotten it, by Jove. Saved my life, absolutely. Hadn’t chewed a

[022-149] morsel for eight hours. Well, have you got anything on? I mean to say,

[022-150] you aren’t booked for lunch or any rot of that species, are you? Fine!

[022-151] Then I move we all toddle off and get a bite somewhere.” He squeezed

[022-152] the other’s arm fondly. “Fancy meeting you again like this! I’ve often

[022-153] wondered what became of you. But, by Jove, I was forgetting. Dashed

[022-154] rude of me. My friend, Mr. van Tuyl.”

[022-155] Reggie gulped. The longer he looked at it, the harder this man’s

[022-156] costume was to bear. His eye passed shudderingly from the brown shoes

[022-157] to the tweed trousers, to the green scarf, from the green scarf to the

[022-158] straw hat.

[022-159] “Sorry,” he mumbled. “Just remembered. Important date. Late already.

[022-160] Er—see you some time—”

[022-161] He melted away, a broken man. Archie was not sorry to see him go.

[022-162] Reggie was a good chap, but he would undoubtedly have been _de trop_ at

[022-163] this reunion.

[022-164] “I vote we go to [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]],” he said, steering his newly-found

[022-165] friend through the crowd. “The browsing and sluicing isn’t bad there,

[022-166] and I can sign the bill which is no small consideration nowadays.”

[022-167] [[sausage-chappie|The Sausage Chappie]] chuckled amusedly.

[022-168] “I can’t go to a place like [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]] looking like this.”

[022-169] Archie, was a little embarrassed.

[022-170] “Oh, I don’t know, you know, don’t you know!” he said. “Still, since

[022-171] you have brought the topic up, you _did_ get the good old wardrobe a

[022-172] bit mixed this morning what? I mean to say, you seem absent-mindedly,

[022-173] as it were, to have got hold of samples from a good number of your

[022-174] various suitings.”

[022-175] “Suitings? How do you mean, suitings? I haven’t any suitings! Who do

[022-176] you think I am? Vincent Astor? All I have is what I stand up in.”

[022-177] Archie was shocked. This tragedy touched him. He himself had never had

[022-178] any [[wealth-and-money|money]] in his life, but somehow he had always seemed to manage to

[022-179] have plenty of clothes. How this was he could not say. He had always

[022-180] had a vague sort of idea that tailors were kindly birds who never

[022-181] failed to have a pair of trousers or something up their sleeve to

[022-182] present to the deserving. There was the drawback, of course, that once

[022-183] they had given you things they were apt to write you rather a lot of

[022-184] letters about it; but you soon managed to recognise their handwriting,

[022-185] and then it was a simple task to extract their communications from your

[022-186] morning mail and drop them in the waste-paper basket. This was the

[022-187] first case he had encountered of a man who was really short of clothes.

[022-188] “My dear old lad,” he said, briskly, “this must be remedied! Oh,

[022-189] positively! This must be remedied at once! I suppose my things wouldn’t

[022-190] fit you? No. Well, I tell you what. We’ll wangle something from my

[022-191] [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]]. Old Brewster, you know, the fellow who runs the

[022-192] Cosmopolis. His’ll fit you like the paper on the wall, because he’s a

[022-193] tubby little blighter, too. What I mean to say is, he’s also one of

[022-194] those sturdy, square, fine-looking chappies of about the middle height.

[022-195] By the way, where are you stopping these days?”
