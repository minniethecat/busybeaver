# Sixth Avenue

A major Manhattan thoroughfare that appears in several episodes of the novel as a setting for commercial encounters and transit.

## In the Novel

Sixth Avenue features in three separate chapters as a backdrop to the novel's action. In chapter 18, a taxicab carrying the Girl Friend "buzzed at a smart pace into Sixth Avenue" as Archie trails her on his mission of reconciliation (018-113). In chapter 28, Archie has discovered a restaurant on Sixth Avenue where he encounters [[mrs-mccall|Mrs. McCall]] and her son [[washburn-mccall|Washburn]] (028-113). In chapter 30, reference is made to a tobacco shop on Sixth Avenue whose proprietor has fallen into difficulties (030-107). Together, these references establish Sixth Avenue as part of the working commercial fabric of the novel's New York—a street of small businesses, restaurants, and traffic, distinct from the luxury of [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]].

## Description

Wodehouse provides little physical description of Sixth Avenue proper, using it rather as a geographic waypoint that grounds the action in a recognizable Manhattan. The contrast with Fifth Avenue is implicit: where Fifth Avenue is the avenue of jewelers and high-end commerce (017-036), Sixth Avenue is the realm of modest restaurants and tobacco shops—the New York of ordinary working life rather than the city of wealth and display.

## Associated Characters

- **[[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]** — Discovers a restaurant here and encounters the McCalls (028-113)
- **[[mrs-mccall|Mrs. McCall]]** — Dines at the Sixth Avenue restaurant where Archie meets her (028-131)
- **[[the-girl-friend|The Girl Friend]]** — Her taxicab travels up Sixth Avenue (018-113)

## Real-World Context

Sixth Avenue (officially Avenue of the Americas since 1945, though New Yorkers rarely use the name) is a major north-south artery running through Manhattan. In the 1920s, its character varied by neighborhood: commercial and industrial downtown, passing through the shopping and entertainment districts of Midtown. Wodehouse, who lived and worked in New York, uses the street name to add authentic texture to his fictionalized Manhattan, much as he uses street names in his London stories to establish class and neighborhood associations.
