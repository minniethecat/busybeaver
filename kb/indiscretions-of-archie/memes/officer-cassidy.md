# Officer Cassidy

A New York City police officer who appears in Chapters V and VI, partnering with Officer Donahue. He arrests Archie Moffam in the streets of New York and later testifies about the incident. Cassidy is the first representative of law enforcement encountered in the novel, and his interactions with Archie set the pattern for the protagonist's comic dealings with authority.

## Role in the Novel

Officer Cassidy serves as a comic minor character in the early chapters. He and his partner Donahue encounter Archie on the street, leading to Archie's arrest. The scene is set in motion when Archie, hiding from a previous predicament, is discovered by Cassidy in a stranger's apartment. The policeman enters via the fire-escape, "swinging a substantial club in a negligent sort of way," and fixes Archie with "a total absence of bonhomie" (005-026–027). Cassidy's subsequent interactions with his captain reveal a meek, somewhat simple-minded Irish policeman who is easily intimidated by authority. (PN: 005-036–005-193, 006-038–006-084)

The arrest itself unfolds through a series of comic misunderstandings. Archie attempts to explain his presence in the apartment by referencing a convoluted sequence of events involving a bathing suit, a magazine cover, and an illustrator — a narrative that leaves Cassidy and his superiors bewildered. Cassidy's role is that of the straight man in a comic duo, his granite-faced incomprehension providing the foil for Archie's rambling explanations.

## Personality and Traits

Cassidy speaks with a pronounced Irish accent, using expressions like "sorr," "ye plaze," and "plaze, sorr." He is described as having a face of "granite" impassivity, yet is easily flustered when confronted by his superior officer. He takes a naive pride in his detective work, modestly explaining how an idea "sorta came to me." (PN: 005-087, 005-091, 005-106, 005-129, 005-187)

Beneath his imposing uniform and club, Cassidy possesses a fundamentally unthreatening nature. When his captain fixes him with a stern glare, Cassidy is "frozen" — reduced to monosyllabic, deferential responses that contrast sharply with his earlier authoritative demeanour. This deflation from menace to meekness is characteristic of Wodehouse's treatment of authority figures: the uniform suggests power, but the man inside it dissolves under scrutiny.

Cassidy is also revealed to be literal-minded and somewhat slow on the uptake. When Archie tries to explain the convoluted chain of events that led to his presence in the apartment — involving bathing suits, magazine covers, and illustrators — Cassidy makes no attempt to follow the thread, content to simply present the facts (as he understands them) to his captain.

## Key Relationships

- **Officer Donahue** — Cassidy's partner and "old college chum," the two patrolmen work together as a comic duo, appearing in tandem throughout their scenes. Donahue shares Cassidy's Irish background and deferential attitude toward their captain. (PN: 005-138, 005-193)
- **Archie Moffam** — Cassidy arrests Archie, setting off the chain of events in the early chapters. Their dynamic is one of mutual incomprehension: Cassidy cannot follow Archie's explanations, and Archie cannot penetrate Cassidy's granite-faced officiality. (PN: 005-036–005-078)
- **The Police Captain** — Cassidy is "frozen" by his captain's glare and reduced to meek, monosyllabic responses when questioned. The hierarchical relationship between the patrolman and his superior is played for broad comedy. (PN: 005-142–005-187)

## As Seen by Others

Cassidy's character is refracted through the perspectives of those who encounter him, each revealing a different facet of the man behind the uniform.

**Archie's View.** To Archie, Cassidy is first and foremost a threat — the "substantial club" swinging "in a negligent sort of way" (005-026) conveys casual menace. Yet as their encounter progresses, Archie seems to sense the vulnerability beneath the granite exterior. His rambling explanations, far from being terrified gibberish, suggest a man who intuits that the policeman before him can be talked past, if not quite talked around. Archie does not so much fear Cassidy as register him as an obstacle to be navigated with words — a tactic that, while unsuccessful in preventing the arrest, establishes the comic rhythm of their pairing.

**Donahue's Camaraderie.** Officer Donahue's relationship with Cassidy is one of easy, unspoken partnership. They move in tandem, share glances of mutual understanding, and present a united front of official bafflement when confronted with Archie's explanations. Donahue appears to regard Cassidy as a competent if unambitious colleague — the kind of partner with whom one can patrol a beat in comfortable silence, the "old college chum" (005-138) dynamic suggesting a bond forged in shared routine rather than dramatic incident.

**The Captain's Assessment.** The police captain sees Cassidy as a subordinate to be managed — a functionary whose reports are to be extracted, interrogated, and dismissed with the merest flexing of hierarchical muscle. When the captain fixes him with a glare, Cassidy is "frozen" (005-187), his earlier swagger evaporating instantly. The captain's view is the novel's implicit judgment: Cassidy is a man whose authority is entirely borrowed from his uniform, and who reverts to anxious deference the moment genuine authority — in the person of the captain — enters the room.

## Comic Significance

Cassidy exemplifies a recurring Wodehouse type: the authority figure whose imposing exterior conceals a pliable, easily intimidated interior. The "granite" face and swinging club promise menace, but the moment the captain's eye falls upon him, Cassidy crumbles into servile monosyllables. This pattern — the puncturing of official dignity — is one Wodehouse deploys throughout the novel, and Cassidy is its first and purest embodiment.

His partnership with Donahue functions as a classic comic double-act: two supposedly formidable policemen who, when faced with their superior, become as meek as schoolboys. Their shared Irishness adds a layer of ethnic colour to the comedy, with Wodehouse mining their accents and speech patterns for humorous effect without descending into caricature.
