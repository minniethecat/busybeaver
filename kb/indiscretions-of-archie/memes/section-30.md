## Section 30

[030-001] His mother (nee Miss [[mrs-mccall|Cora Bates]]) is one who frequently orates upon the

[030-002] proper kind of [[dining-and-social-ritual|food]] which every menu should include. With eloquence the

[030-003] world she weans from chops and steaks and pork and beans. Such horrid

[030-004] things she’d like to crush, and make us live on milk and mush. But oh!

[030-005] the thing that makes her sigh is when she sees us eating pie. (We heard

[030-006] her lecture last July upon “The Nation’s Menace—Pie.”) Alas, the hit it

[030-007] made was small with Master [[washburn-mccall|Washington McCall]].

[030-008] For yesterday we took a trip to see the great Pie Championship, where

[030-009] men with bulging cheeks and eyes consume vast quantities of pies. A

[030-010] fashionable West Side crowd beheld the champion, Spike O’Dowd,

[030-011] endeavour to defend his throne against an upstart, Blake’s Unknown. He

[030-012] wasn’t an Unknown at all. He was young Washington McCall.

[030-013] We freely own we’d give a leg if we could borrow, steal, or beg the

[030-014] skill old Homer used to show. (He wrote the _Iliad_, you know.) Old

[030-015] Homer swung a wicked pen, but we are ordinary men, and cannot even

[030-016] start to dream of doing justice to our theme. The subject of that great

[030-017] repast is too magnificent and vast. We can’t describe (or even try) the

[030-018] way those rivals wolfed their pie. Enough to say that, when for hours

[030-019] each had extended all his pow’rs, toward the quiet evenfall O’Dowd

[030-020] succumbed to young McCall.

[030-021] The champion was a willing lad. He gave the public all he had. His was

[030-022] a genuine fighting soul. He’d lots of speed and much control. No yellow

[030-023] streak did he evince. He tackled apple-pie and mince. This was the

[030-024] motto on his shield—“O’Dowds may burst. They never yield.” His eyes

[030-025] began to start and roll. He eased his belt another hole. Poor fellow!

[030-026] With a single glance one saw that he had not a chance. A python would

[030-027] have had to crawl and own defeat from young McCall.

[030-028] At last, long last, the finish came. His features overcast with shame,

[030-029] O’Dowd, who’d faltered once or twice, declined to eat another slice. He

[030-030] tottered off, and kindly men rallied around with oxygen. But Washy,

[030-031] Cora Bates’s son, seemed disappointed it was done. He somehow made

[030-032] those present feel he’d barely started on his meal. We ask him, “Aren’t

[030-033] you feeling bad?” “Me!” said the lion-hearted lad. “Lead me”—he started

[030-034] for the street—“where I can get a bite to eat!” Oh, what a lesson does

[030-035] it teach to all of us, that splendid speech! How better can the curtain

[030-036] fall on Master Washington McCall!

[030-037] [[mr-mccall|Mr. McCall]] read this epic through, then he looked at his son. He first

[030-038] looked at him over his glasses, then through his glasses, then over his

[030-039] glasses again, then through his glasses once more. A curious expression

[030-040] was in his eyes. If such a thing had not been so impossible, one would

[030-041] have said that his gaze had in it something of respect, of admiration,

[030-042] even of reverence.

[030-043] “But how did they find out your name?” he asked, at length.

[030-044] Mrs. McCall exclaimed impatiently.

[030-045] “Is _that_ all you have to say?”

[030-046] “No, no, my dear, of course not, quite so. But the point struck me as

[030-047] curious.”

[030-048] “Wretched boy,” cried Mrs. McCall, “were you insane enough to reveal

[030-049] your name?”

[030-050] Washington wriggled uneasily. Unable to endure the piercing stare of

[030-051] his mother, he had withdrawn to the window, and was looking out with

[030-052] his back turned. But even there he could feel her eyes on the back of

[030-053] his neck.

[030-054] “I didn’t think it ’ud matter,” he mumbled. “A fellow with

[030-055] tortoiseshell-rimmed specs asked me, so I told him. How was I to know—”

[030-056] His stumbling defence was cut short by the opening of the door.

[030-057] “Hallo-allo-allo! What ho! What ho!”

[030-058] [[archie-moffam|Archie]] was standing in the doorway, beaming ingratiatingly on the

[030-059] family.

[030-060] The apparition of an entire stranger served to divert the lightning of

[030-061] Mrs. McCall’s gaze from the unfortunate Washy. [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], catching it

[030-062] between the eyes, blinked and held on to the wall. He had begun to

[030-063] regret that he had yielded so weakly to [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]]’s entreaty that he

[030-064] should look in on the McCalls and use the magnetism of his personality

[030-065] upon them in the [[hope-and-optimism|hope]] of inducing them to settle the lawsuit. He

[030-066] wished, too, if the visit had to be paid that he had postponed it till

[030-067] after lunch, for he was never at his strongest in the morning. But

[030-068] Lucille had urged him to go now and get it over, and here he was.

[030-069] “I think,” said Mrs. McCall, icily, “that you must have mistaken your

[030-070] room.”

[030-071] Archie rallied his shaken forces.

[030-072] “Oh, no. Rather not. Better introduce myself, what? My name’s Moffam,

[030-073] you know. I’m old [[daniel-brewster|Brewster]]’s son-in-law, and all that sort of rot, if

[030-074] you know what I mean.” He gulped and continued. “I’ve come about this

[030-075] jolly old lawsuit, don’t you know.”

[030-076] Mr. McCall seemed about to speak, but his wife anticipated him.

[030-077] “Mr. Brewster’s attorneys are in [[letters-and-correspondence|communication]] with ours. We do not

[030-078] wish to discuss the matter.”

[030-079] Archie took an uninvited seat, eyed the Health Bread on the breakfast

[030-080] table for a moment with frank curiosity, and resumed his discourse.

[030-081] “No, but I say, you know! I’ll tell you what happened. I hate to totter

[030-082] in where I’m not wanted and all that, but my wife made such a point of

[030-083] it. Rightly or wrongly she regards me as a bit of a hound in the

[030-084] diplomacy line, and she begged me to look you up and see whether we

[030-085] couldn’t do something about settling the jolly old thing. I mean to

[030-086] say, you know, the old bird—old Brewster, you know—is considerably

[030-087] perturbed about the affair—hates the thought of being in a posish where

[030-088] he has either got to bite his old pal McCall in the neck or be bitten

[030-089] by him—and—well, and so forth, don’t you know! How about it?” He broke

[030-090] off. “Great Scot! I say, what!”

[030-091] So engrossed had he been in his appeal that he had not observed the

[030-092] presence of the pie-eating champion, between whom and himself a large

[030-093] potted plant intervened. But now Washington, hearing the familiar

[030-094] voice, had moved from the window and was confronting him with an

[030-095] accusing stare.

[030-096] “_He_ made me do it!” said Washy, with the stern joy a sixteen-year-old

[030-097] boy feels when he sees somebody on to whose shoulders he can shift

[030-098] trouble from his own. “That’s the fellow who took me to the place!”

[030-099] “What are you talking about, Washington?”

[030-100] “I’m telling you! He got me into the thing.”

[030-101] “Do you mean this—this—” Mrs. McCall shuddered. “Are you referring to

[030-102] this pie-eating contest?”

[030-103] “You bet I am!”

[030-104] “Is this true?” Mrs. McCall glared stonily at Archie, “Was it you who

[030-105] lured my poor boy into that—that—”

[030-106] “Oh, absolutely. The fact is, don’t you know, a dear old pal of mine

[030-107] who runs a tobacco shop on Sixth Avenue was rather in the soup. He had

[030-108] backed a chappie against the champion, and the chappie was converted by

[030-109] one of your lectures and swore off pie at the eleventh hour. Dashed

[030-110] hard luck on the poor chap, don’t you know! And then I got the idea

[030-111] that our little friend here was the one to step in and save the

[030-112] situash, so I broached the matter to him. And I’ll tell you one thing,”

[030-113] said Archie, handsomely, “I don’t know what sort of a capacity the

[030-114] original chappie had, but I’ll bet he wasn’t in your son’s class. Your

[030-115] son has to be seen to be believed! Absolutely! You ought to be proud of

[030-116] him!” He turned in friendly fashion to Washy. “Rummy we should meet

[030-117] again like this! Never dreamed I should find you here. And, by Jove,

[030-118] it’s absolutely marvellous how fit you look after yesterday. I had a

[030-119] sort of idea you would be groaning on a bed of sickness and all that.”

[030-120] There was a strange gurgling sound in the background. It resembled

[030-121] something getting up steam. And this, curiously enough, is precisely

[030-122] what it was. The thing that was getting up steam was Mr. Lindsay

[030-123] McCall.

[030-124] The first effect of the Washy revelations on Mr. McCall had been merely

[030-125] to stun him. It was not until the arrival of Archie that he had had

[030-126] [[leisure-and-sports|leisure]] to think; but since Archie’s entrance he had been thinking

[030-127] rapidly and deeply.

[030-128] For many years Mr. McCall had been in a state of suppressed revolution.

[030-129] He had smouldered, but had not dared to blaze. But this startling

[030-130] upheaval of his fellow-sufferer, Washy, had acted upon him like a high

[030-131] explosive. There was a strange gleam in his eye, a gleam of

[030-132] determination. He was breathing hard.

[030-133] “Washy!”

[030-134] His voice had lost its deprecating mildness. It rang strong and clear.

[030-135] “Yes, pop?”

[030-136] “How many pies did you eat yesterday?”

[030-137] Washy considered.

[030-138] “A good few.”

[030-139] “How many? Twenty?”

[030-140] “More than that. I lost count. A good few.”

[030-141] “And you feel as well as ever?”

[030-142] “I feel fine.”

[030-143] Mr. McCall dropped his glasses. He glowered for a moment at the

[030-144] breakfast table. His eye took in the Health Bread, the imitation

[030-145] coffee-pot, the cereal, the nut-butter. Then with a swift movement he

[030-146] seized the cloth, jerked it forcibly, and brought the entire contents

[030-147] rattling and crashing to the floor.

[030-148] “Lindsay!”

[030-149] Mr. McCall met his wife’s eye with quiet determination. It was plain

[030-150] that something had happened in the hinterland of Mr. McCall’s soul.

[030-151] “Cora,” he said, resolutely, “I have come to a decision. I’ve been

[030-152] letting you run things your own way a little too long in this family.

[030-153] I’m going to assert myself. For one thing, I’ve had all I want of this

[030-154] food-reform foolery. Look at Washy! Yesterday that boy seems to have

[030-155] consumed anything from a couple of hundredweight to a ton of pie, and

[030-156] he has thriven on it! Thriven! I don’t want to hurt your feelings,

[030-157] Cora, but Washington and I have drunk our last cup of anti-caffeine! If

[030-158] you care to go on with the stuff, that’s your look-out. But Washy and I

[030-159] are through.”

[030-160] He silenced his wife with a masterful gesture and turned to Archie.

[030-161] “And there’s another thing. I never liked the idea of that lawsuit, but

[030-162] I let you [[conversation-and-dialogue|talk]] me into it. Now I’m going to do things my way. Mr.

[030-163] Moffam, I’m glad you looked in this morning. I’ll do just what you

[030-164] want. Take me to Dan Brewster now, and let’s call the thing off, and

[030-165] shake hands on it.”

[030-166] “Are you mad, Lindsay?”

[030-167] It was Cora Bates McCall’s last shot. Mr. McCall paid no attention to

[030-168] it. He was shaking hands with Archie.

[030-169] “I consider you, Mr. Moffam,” he said, “the most sensible young man I

[030-170] have ever met!”

[030-171] Archie blushed modestly.

[030-172] “Awfully good of you, [[old-bean|old bean]],” he said. “I wonder if you’d mind

[030-173] telling my jolly old [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]] that? It’ll be a bit of news for

[030-174] him!”

[030-175] CHAPTER XXIII.

[030-176] MOTHER’S KNEE

[030-177] Archie Moffam’s connection with that devastatingly popular ballad,

[030-178] “Mother’s Knee,” was one to which he always looked back later with a

[030-179] certain pride. “Mother’s Knee,” it will be remembered, went through the

[030-180] world like a pestilence. Scots elders hummed it on their way to kirk;

[030-181] cannibals crooned it to their offspring in the jungles of Borneo; it

[030-182] was a best-seller among the Bolshevists. In the United States alone

[030-183] three million copies were disposed of. For a man who has not

[030-184] accomplished anything outstandingly great in his life, it is something

[030-185] to have been in a sense responsible for a song like that; and, though

[030-186] there were moments when Archie experienced some of the emotions of a

[030-187] man who has punched a hole in the dam of one of the larger reservoirs,

[030-188] he never really regretted his share in the launching of the thing.

[030-189] It seems almost bizarre now to think that there was a time when even

[030-190] one person in the world had not heard “Mother’s Knee”; but it came

[030-191] fresh to Archie one afternoon some weeks after the episode of Washy, in

[030-192] his suite at the [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|Hotel Cosmopolis]], where he was cementing with

[030-193] cigarettes and pleasant conversation his renewed friendship with Wilson

[030-194] [[wilson-hymack|Hymack]], whom he had first met in the neighbourhood of Armentières

[030-195] during [[world-war-i|the war]].
