# Elopement of Archie and Lucille

The elopement is the novel's foundational event, occurring before the narrative begins but determining everything that follows. Archie and Lucille, "both so much in love that they simply had to slip away and get married at once" (002-046), wed without consulting Lucille's father. The marriage certificate (002-086) becomes the novel's most significant document.

## Summary

The elopement creates the novel's central dramatic situation: Archie is now Daniel Brewster's son-in-law, but Brewster does not yet know it. The revelation of this fact — delayed, managed, and ultimately explosive — provides the narrative engine for the novel's opening chapters. The marriage itself is presented as happy and stable; it is its social consequences that generate the comedy.
