# Elmer M. Moon

A New York clothing merchant and tailor whose name appears on the panel of Archie Moffam's hotel room furnishings. Moon supplies Archie's extensive wardrobe and is characterized as "a hustler" with dubious ethics, though his taste in clothing is praised.

## Role in the Novel

Moon appears in an early chapter when Archie attempts to contact him at his shop on the ground floor of the Hotel Cosmopolis building. Their encounter is marked by Moon's brusque, businesslike manner — he tells Archie to "Sit down!" [004-129] with an abruptness that the Englishman finds disconcerting. When Archie attempts pleasantries, Moon cuts him off: "No! I haven't got your spare time" [004-132], establishing himself as a man for whom commerce trumps courtesy.

The exchange reveals a fundamental cultural contrast. Archie approaches the interaction with English expectations of polite preliminaries; Moon treats it as a pure transaction. Neither man is wrong, but their styles are incompatible — a microcosm of the Anglo-American tensions that run through the novel.

Moon is later referenced as the supplier of Archie's hotel wardrobe [005-019] and is described in moral terms as "not a good man" [006-086]. The narrative voice is unusually direct on this point: "Elmer Moon was not a good man; his ethics were not above suspicion" [006-086]. This moral clarity is striking in a novel that generally avoids such judgments, suggesting that Moon represents something the narrative finds genuinely objectionable — not merely cultural difference but commercial dishonesty.

## Personality and Traits

Moon is portrayed as an aggressive businessman — "that hustler" [004-149] — who works quickly and efficiently but whose "moral" character is suspect. He is "elusive" [004-159] and hard to pin down, as Archie discovers when trying to reach him. Despite his questionable ethics, his taste in clothing design is credited as excellent [005-019], creating a tension between his aesthetic competence and his moral deficiency.

This tension is characteristic of Wodehouse's treatment of American commerce. Moon has talent and energy — the virtues of the American businessman — but lacks the ethical restraint that would make those virtues admirable. He represents a type that the novel treats with wary respect: capable, efficient, and not to be trusted.

## Moon as Comic Type

Moon belongs to a recognizable Wodehousian category: the American comic businessman. Like other figures in this tradition, he is defined by a single dominant trait (aggressive hustle), speaks in short, declarative sentences, and treats social niceties as a waste of time. His function in the narrative is to provide friction — Archie's encounter with him is frustrating, and this frustration is played for comedy. But Moon also serves a thematic purpose: he embodies the commercial world that Archie, as an English gentleman of leisure, finds both baffling and necessary.

## Key Relationships

- **Archie Moffam** — Archie wears clothing designed by Moon and attempts to contact him at his shop. Their encounter is one of the novel's early demonstrations of the Anglo-American cultural clash — Archie's English politeness meets Moon's American hustle and comes off second-best. ## Quotes

> "Sit down!" said Elmer M. Moon, abruptly. [004-129]

> "No!" said Mr. Moon. "I haven't got your spare time." [004-132]

> "...Elmer Moon was not a good man; his ethics were not above suspicion." [006-086]

The three quotes trace a clear character arc across only a few sections: from brusque command to open rudeness to definitive moral judgment. Wodehouse's economy in establishing Moon is notable — the character appears in only a handful of paragraphs yet leaves a lasting impression as the novel's representative of sharp American commercial practice.
