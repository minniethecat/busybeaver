# Poulet en Casserole

*Poulet en casserole* is a classic French dish of chicken braised slowly in a covered casserole with vegetables, herbs, and wine. In [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|the novel]], it appears as the centerpiece of an extravagant room-service dinner that becomes the occasion for a comic encounter between [[archie-moffam|Archie]] and the famished [[washburn-mccall|Washington McCall]].

## In the Novel

The dish arrives in Chapter XXVIII, after Archie has been abandoned to dine alone at the [[hotel-cosmopolis|Hotel Cosmopolis]]. His wife [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] has been detained by the "pest Jane," leaving Archie with "a rich banquet" for two — and a solitary appetite (028-015–028-016). When the hotel waiter brings the meal, the aroma draws the attention of young Washington McCall, who appears in the doorway:

> "Were you expecting somebody to dinner?" asked the boy.
>
> "Why, yes, old friend, I was."
>
> "I wish—" (028-019–028-021)

The boy's unfinished sentence is the prelude to what becomes a shared meal. When Archie names the dish — "*Poulet en casserole*" — the boy responds with a reverent "Golly!" (028-036–028-037). The moment is charged with comic pathos: Washington, whose mother [[mrs-mccall|Mrs. McCall]] is a food-reformer who "makes Pop and me live on vegetables and nuts and things" (028-054), has been starved of proper food. The wafting perfume of the *poulet en casserole* represents everything his mother's [[rational-eating]] philosophy has denied him.

> The boy sniffed with half-closed eyes as a wave of perfume from the *poulet en casserole* floated past him. He seemed to be anxious to intercept as much of it as possible before it got through the door. (028-050–028-052)

## Narrative Significance

The dish functions as more than set-dressing. It is the sensory trigger that bridges Archie's solitude and the boy's hunger, transforming an abandoned dinner into an act of hospitality. Through the *poulet en casserole*, Wodehouse dramatizes the central conflict of the McCall household — between gustatory pleasure and [[rational-eating|dietary reform]] — without a word of explicit commentary. The aroma says everything.

## Culinary Context

In classical French cooking, *poulet en casserole* (also known as *poulet cocotte* or *poulet poêlé*) involves browning a whole chicken in butter, then slow-cooking it in a covered pot with aromatic vegetables, stock, and often wine. The method produces tender meat and a rich, concentrated sauce. By the early twentieth century, French dishes like this were staples of upscale hotel dining rooms in New York — exactly the kind of "rich banquet" a Cosmopolis waiter would deliver to a guest's room.
