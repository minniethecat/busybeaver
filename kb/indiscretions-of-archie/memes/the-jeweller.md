# The Jeweller

The Jeweller is a minor character — a "stout, comfortable man" (017-016) who runs a jewellery shop near the [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|Hotel Cosmopolis]]. He conducts business with [[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]] during the novel's jewellery-related subplot, his approving manner making him one of the more agreeable commercial figures Archie encounters.

## Role in the Novel

The jeweller appears in Chapter XVII when Archie visits his shop to negotiate the sale of jewellery. He conducts the transaction with professional approval — "the jeweller eyed him approvingly, a man after his own heart" (017-028) — and offers a price of eight hundred dollars (017-025). The negotiation is conducted with mutual satisfaction: Archie gets his price, and the jeweller secures his profit. His shop is again referenced, though he does not appear in person, in connection with its location "near the Hotel Cosmopolis" (020-058).

## Personality and Traits

The jeweller is characterized by his comfortable, approving manner. He "leaned" with professional interest and "nodded approval" (017-097) of Archie's business sense. Unlike the more predatory commercial figures in the novel, the jeweller conducts business with straightforward geniality — he is "a man after his own heart" (017-028), recognizing in Archie a fellow spirit of reasonable dealing. After the transaction is complete, he "threw off the" formal manner of business (017-059), suggesting a man who separates professional and personal identities cleanly.

When Archie returns under different circumstances, the jeweller "recognised in the" voice of his customer even before seeing him (020-061), demonstrating professional memory and attention to his clientele.

## In the Eyes of Others

The jeweller, though a minor figure, occupies a significant symbolic position in the novel's treatment of commerce — a rare instance of a commercial character who is treated with genuine warmth rather than satirical deflation.

### Through Archie's Eyes

For [[archie-moffam]], the jeweller is a revelation: a man of business who is also a man of reasonable good humour. Archie's foray into jewellery-selling is born of domestic desperation — he needs money and has a family heirloom to sell — and the jeweller's approving manner transforms what could have been a humiliating transaction into something approaching a pleasant encounter. The jeweller recognises Archie "a man after his own heart" (PN: 017-028), a phrase that confers a kind of commercial fellowship Archie rarely experiences. Their dealings have none of the predatory tension that characterises Archie's encounters with figures like the art-shop assistant. Instead, the jeweller offers Archie the novel experience of conducting business with someone who treats him as an equal.

### Through the Narrator's Eyes

The narrator treats the jeweller with unusual warmth for a commercial character. The description "stout, comfortable man" (PN: 017-016) carries none of the satirical edge that accompanies, say, the "dog-faced" Willie or the various predatory hotel functionaries. The jeweller is presented as a professional who takes pride in his work and satisfaction in a fair transaction — a type the Wodehousian narrator, for all his mockery of the business world, can genuinely respect. The detail that he "threw off" his business manner after the transaction (PN: 017-059) is a small but significant touch: it suggests a man whose commercial persona is a role well played rather than his entire identity.
