# Business and Enterprise

Business is the arena in which Archie must prove himself worthy of his American father-in-law. The novel's many commercial episodes — from art deals to baseball negotiations — form a running commentary on the collision between English amateurism and American enterprise. Wodehouse mines this collision for its richest comic ore.

## Overview

Daniel Brewster's world is defined by business. As a self-made hotel proprietor, he views the world through the lens of commerce: value, profit, and the "modern spirit of efficiency" (005-208). Into this world comes Archie, whose notion of work is vague at best and whose business instincts are catastrophically optimistic. The resulting frictions produce the novel's most sustained comic sequences.

Archie's approach to business is characteristically English: good-natured, improvisational, and almost entirely ineffective. Yet his very ineptitude becomes a strange kind of asset — his transparent honesty and refusal to be discouraged eventually win over even the hardened businessmen he encounters.

## In the Novel

Archie's first taste of American business comes through his father-in-law's demand that he find occupation. Various episodes follow: art negotiations with collectors (section-09), baseball team dealings (section-17), and assorted entrepreneurial schemes. Each episode follows a Wodehousian pattern: Archie's good intentions lead to chaos, which is resolved not by business acumen but by personal charm.

The "business negotiations" (010-118) around a painting transaction exemplify the pattern. Archie, acting as intermediary, manages to misunderstand every detail of the deal while somehow keeping all parties hopeful. The "tedious business interview" (017-054) with a baseball promoter similarly showcases the gap between commercial seriousness and Archie's irrepressible sociability.
