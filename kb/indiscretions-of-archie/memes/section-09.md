## Section 9

[009-001] It would have pained Peter deeply, for he was a snake of great

[009-002] sensibility, if he had known how much his entrance had disturbed the

[009-003] occupant of the room. He himself had no feeling but gratitude for the

[009-004] man who had opened the window and so enabled him to get in out of the

[009-005] rather nippy night air. Ever since the bag had swung open and shot him

[009-006] out onto the sill of the window below [[archie-moffam|Archie]]’s, he had been waiting

[009-007] patiently for something of the kind to happen. He was a snake who took

[009-008] things as they came, and was prepared to rough it a bit if necessary;

[009-009] but for the last hour or two he had been hoping that somebody would do

[009-010] something practical in the way of getting him in out of the cold. When

[009-011] at home, he had an eiderdown quilt to sleep on, and the stone of the

[009-012] window-sill was a little trying to a snake of regular habits. He

[009-013] crawled thankfully across the floor under [[lord-seacliff|Squiffy]]’s bed. There was a

[009-014] pair of trousers there, for his host had undressed when not in a frame

[009-015] of mind to fold his clothes neatly and place them upon a chair. Peter

[009-016] looked the trousers over. They were not an eiderdown quilt, but they

[009-017] would serve. He curled up in them and went to sleep. He had had an

[009-018] exciting day, and was glad to turn in.

[009-019] After about ten minutes, the tension of Squiffy’s attitude relaxed. His

[009-020] heart, which had seemed to suspend its operations, began beating again.

[009-021] Reason reasserted itself. He peeped cautiously under the bed. He could

[009-022] see nothing.

[009-023] Squiffy was convinced. He told himself that he had never really

[009-024] believed in Peter as a living thing. It stood to reason that there

[009-025] couldn’t really be a snake in his room. The window looked out on

[009-026] emptiness. His room was several stories above the ground. There was a

[009-027] stern, set expression on Squiffy’s face as he climbed out of bed. It

[009-028] was the expression of a man who is turning over a new leaf, starting a

[009-029] new life. He looked about the room for some implement which would carry

[009-030] out the deed he had to do, and finally pulled out one of the

[009-031] curtain-rods. Using this as a lever, he broke open the topmost of the

[009-032] six cases which stood in the corner. The soft wood cracked and split.

[009-033] Squiffy drew out a straw-covered bottle. For a moment he stood looking

[009-034] at it, as a man might gaze at a friend on the point of death. Then,

[009-035] with a sudden determination, he went into the bathroom. There was a

[009-036] crash of glass and a gurgling sound.

[009-037] Half an hour later the telephone in [[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]]’s room rang. “I say, Archie,

[009-038] old top,” said the voice of Squiffy.

[009-039] “[[what-ho|Halloa]], [[old-bean|old bean]]! Is that you?”

[009-040] “I say, could you pop down here for a second? I’m rather upset.”

[009-041] “Absolutely! Which room?”

[009-042] “Four-forty-one.”

[009-043] “I’ll be with you eftsoons or right speedily.”

[009-044] “Thanks, old man.”

[009-045] “What appears to be the difficulty?”

[009-046] “Well, as a matter of fact, I thought I saw a snake!”

[009-047] “A snake!”

[009-048] “I’ll tell you all about it when you come down.”

[009-049] Archie found Lord Seacliff seated on his bed. An arresting aroma of

[009-050] mixed drinks pervaded the atmosphere.

[009-051] “I say! What?” said Archie, inhaling.

[009-052] “That’s all right. I’ve been pouring my stock away. Just finished the

[009-053] last bottle.”

[009-054] “But why?”

[009-055] “I told you. I thought I saw a snake!”

[009-056] “Green?”

[009-057] Squiffy shivered slightly.

[009-058] “Frightfully green!”

[009-059] Archie hesitated. He perceived that there are moments when silence is

[009-060] the best policy. He had been worrying himself over the unfortunate case

[009-061] of his friend, and now that Fate seemed to have provided a solution, it

[009-062] would be rash to interfere merely to ease the old bean’s mind. If

[009-063] Squiffy was going to reform because he thought he had seen an imaginary

[009-064] snake, better not to let him know that the snake was a real one.

[009-065] “Dashed serious!” he said.

[009-066] “Bally dashed serious!” agreed Squiffy. “I’m going to cut it out!”

[009-067] “Great scheme!”

[009-068] “You don’t think,” asked Squiffy, with a touch of hopefulness, “that it

[009-069] could have been a real snake?”

[009-070] “Never heard of the management supplying them.”

[009-071] “I thought it went under the bed.”

[009-072] “Well, take a look.”

[009-073] Squiffy shuddered.

[009-074] “Not me! I say, old top, you know, I simply can’t sleep in this room

[009-075] now. I was wondering if you could give me a doss somewhere in yours.”

[009-076] “Rather! I’m in five-forty-one. Just above. Trot along up. Here’s the

[009-077] key. I’ll tidy up a bit here, and join you in a minute.”

[009-078] Squiffy put on a dressing-gown and disappeared. Archie looked under the

[009-079] bed. From the trousers the head of Peter popped up with its usual

[009-080] expression of amiable enquiry. Archie nodded pleasantly, and sat down

[009-081] on the bed. The problem of his little friend’s immediate future wanted

[009-082] thinking over.

[009-083] He lit a cigarette and remained for a while in thought. Then he rose.

[009-084] An admirable solution had presented itself. He picked Peter up and

[009-085] placed him in the pocket of his dressing-gown. Then, leaving the room,

[009-086] he mounted the stairs till he reached the seventh floor. Outside a room

[009-087] half-way down the corridor he paused.

[009-088] From within, through the open transom, came the rhythmical snoring of a

[009-089] good man taking his rest after the labours of the day. [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]] was

[009-090] always a heavy sleeper.

[009-091] “There’s always a way,” thought Archie, philosophically, “if a chappie

[009-092] only thinks of it.”

[009-093] His [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]]’s snoring took on a deeper note. Archie extracted

[009-094] Peter from his pocket and dropped him gently through the transom.

[009-095] CHAPTER IX.

[009-096] A LETTER FROM PARKER

[009-097] As the days went by and he settled down at the [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|Hotel Cosmopolis]],

[009-098] Archie, looking about him and revising earlier judgments, was inclined

[009-099] to think that of all his immediate circle he most admired [[parker|Parker]], the

[009-100] lean, grave valet of Mr. Daniel Brewster. Here was a man who, living in

[009-101] the closest contact with one of the most difficult persons in [[new-york-city|New York]],

[009-102] contrived all the while to maintain an unbowed head, and, as far as one

[009-103] could gather from appearances, a tolerably cheerful disposition. A

[009-104] great man, judge him by what standard you pleased. Anxious as he was to

[009-105] earn an honest living, Archie would not have changed places with Parker

[009-106] for the salary of a movie-star.

[009-107] It was Parker who first directed Archie’s attention to the hidden

[009-108] merits of Pongo. Archie had drifted into his father-in-law’s suite one

[009-109] morning, as he sometimes did in the effort to establish more amicable

[009-110] relations, and had found it occupied only by the valet, who was dusting

[009-111] the furniture and bric-a-brac with a feather broom rather in the style

[009-112] of a man-servant at the rise of the curtain of an old-fashioned farce.

[009-113] After a courteous exchange of greetings, Archie sat down and lit a

[009-114] cigarette. Parker went on dusting.

[009-115] “The guv’nor,” said Parker, breaking the silence, “has some nice little

[009-116] objay dar, sir.”

[009-117] “Little what?”

[009-118] “Objay dar, sir.”

[009-119] Light dawned upon Archie.

[009-120] “Of course, yes. French for junk. I see what you mean now. Dare say

[009-121] you’re right, old friend. Don’t know much about these things myself.”

[009-122] Parker gave an appreciative flick at a vase on the mantelpiece.

[009-123] “Very valuable, some of the guv’nor’s things.” He had picked up the

[009-124] small china figure of [[china-warrior-figurine|the warrior with the spear]], and was grooming it

[009-125] with the ostentatious care of one brushing flies off a sleeping Venus.

[009-126] He regarded this figure with a look of affectionate esteem which seemed

[009-127] to Archie absolutely uncalled-for. Archie’s taste in Art was not

[009-128] precious. To his untutored eye the thing was only one degree less foul

[009-129] than his father-in-law’s Japanese prints, which he had always observed

[009-130] with silent loathing. “This one, now,” continued Parker. “Worth a lot

[009-131] of [[wealth-and-money|money]]. Oh, a lot of money.”

[009-132] “What, Pongo?” said Archie incredulously.

[009-133] “Sir?”

[009-134] “I always call that rummy-looking what-not Pongo. Don’t know what else

[009-135] you could call him, what!”

[009-136] The valet seemed to disapprove of this levity. He shook his head and

[009-137] replaced the figure on the mantelpiece.

[009-138] “Worth a lot of money,” he repeated. “Not by itself, no.”

[009-139] “Oh, not by itself?”

[009-140] “No, sir. Things like this come in pairs. Somewhere or other there’s

[009-141] the companion-piece to this here, and if the guv’nor could get hold of

[009-142] it, he’d have something worth having. Something that connoozers would

[009-143] give a lot of money for. But one’s no good without the other. You have

[009-144] to have both, if you understand my meaning, sir.”

[009-145] “I see. Like filling a straight flush, what?”

[009-146] “Precisely, sir.”

[009-147] Archie gazed at Pongo again, with the dim [[hope-and-optimism|hope]] of discovering virtues

[009-148] not immediately apparent to the casual observer. But without success.

[009-149] Pongo left him cold—even chilly. He would not have taken Pongo as a

[009-150] gift, to oblige a dying friend.

[009-151] “How much would the pair be worth?” he asked. “Ten dollars?”

[009-152] Parker smiled a gravely superior smile. “A leetle more than that, sir.

[009-153] Several thousand dollars, more like it.”

[009-154] “Do you mean to say,” said Archie, with honest amazement, “that there

[009-155] are chumps going about loose—absolutely loose—who would pay that for a

[009-156] weird little object like Pongo?”

[009-157] “Undoubtedly, sir. These antique china figures are in great demand

[009-158] among [[art-and-collecting|collectors]].”

[009-159] Archie looked at Pongo once more, and shook his head.

[009-160] “Well, well, well! It takes all sorts to make a world, what!”

[009-161] What might be called the revival of Pongo, the restoration of Pongo to

[009-162] the ranks of the things that matter, took place several weeks later,

[009-163] when Archie was making holiday at the house which his father-in-law had

[009-164] taken for the summer at Brookport. The curtain of the second act may be

[009-165] said to rise on Archie strolling back from the [[leisure-and-sports|golf]]-links in the cool

[009-166] of an August evening. From time to time he sang slightly, and wondered

[009-167] idly if [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] would put the finishing touch upon the all-rightness of

[009-168] everything by coming to meet him and sharing his homeward walk.

[009-169] She came in view at this moment, a trim little figure in a white skirt

[009-170] and a pale blue sweater. She waved to Archie; and Archie, as always at

[009-171] the sight of her, was conscious of that jumpy, fluttering sensation

[009-172] about the heart, which, translated into words, would have formed the

[009-173] question, “What on earth could have made a girl like that fall in love

[009-174] with a chump like me?” It was a question which he was continually

[009-175] asking himself, and one which was perpetually in the mind also of Mr.

[009-176] Brewster, his father-in-law. The matter of Archie’s unworthiness to be

[009-177] the husband of Lucille was practically the only one on which the two

[009-178] men saw eye to eye.

[009-179] “Hallo—allo—allo!” said Archie. “Here we are, what! I was just hoping

[009-180] you would drift over the horizon.”

[009-181] Lucille kissed him.

[009-182] “You’re a darling,” she said. “And you look like a Greek god in that

[009-183] suit.”

[009-184] “Glad you like it.” Archie squinted with some complacency down his

[009-185] chest. “I always say it doesn’t matter what you pay for a suit, so long

[009-186] as it’s _right_. I hope your jolly old father will feel that way when

[009-187] he settles up for it.”

[009-188] “Where is father? Why didn’t he come back with you?”

[009-189] “Well, as a matter of fact, he didn’t seem any too keen on my company.

[009-190] I left him in the locker-room chewing a cigar. Gave me the impression

[009-191] of having something on his mind.”

[009-192] “Oh, Archie! You didn’t beat him _again?_”

[009-193] Archie looked uncomfortable. He gazed out to sea with something of

[009-194] embarrassment.

[009-195] “Well, as a matter of fact, old thing, to be absolutely frank, I, as it

[009-196] were, did!”

[009-197] “Not badly?”

[009-198] “Well, yes! I rather fancy I put it across him with some vim and not a

[009-199] little emphasis. To be perfectly accurate, I licked him by ten and

[009-200] eight.”

[009-201] “But you promised me you would let him beat you to-day. You know how

[009-202] pleased it would have made him.”
