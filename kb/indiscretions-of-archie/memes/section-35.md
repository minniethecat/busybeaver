## Section 35

[035-001] At [[dining-and-social-ritual|breakfast]] next morning, [[archie-moffam|Archie]] once more brought up the question of

[035-002] the hanging of [[the-wheeler-painting|the picture]]. It was absurd to let a thing like that go

[035-003] on wasting its sweetness behind a sofa with its face to the wall.

[035-004] “Touching the jolly old masterpiece,” he said, “how about it? I think

[035-005] it’s time we hoisted it up somewhere.”

[035-006] [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]] fiddled pensively with her coffee-spoon.

[035-007] “[[indiscretions-of-archie-the-novel|Archie]], dear,” she said, “I’ve been thinking.”

[035-008] “And a very good thing to do,” said Archie. “I’ve often meant to do it

[035-009] myself when I got a bit of time.”

[035-010] “[[About]] that picture, I mean. Did you know it was father’s birthday

[035-011] to-morrow?”

[035-012] “Why no, old thing, I didn’t, to be absolutely honest. Your revered

[035-013] parent doesn’t confide in me much these days, as a matter of fact.”

[035-014] “Well, it is. And I think we ought to give him a present.”

[035-015] “Absolutely. But how? I’m all for spreading sweetness and light, and

[035-016] cheering up the jolly old pater’s sorrowful existence, but I haven’t a

[035-017] bean. And, what is more, things have come to such a pass that I scan

[035-018] the horizon without seeing a single soul I can touch. I suppose I could

[035-019] get into [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie]] van [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]]’s ribs for a bit, but—I don’t know—touching

[035-020] poor old Reggie always seems to me rather like potting a sitting bird.”

[035-021] “Of course, I don’t want you to do anything like that. I was

[035-022] thinking—Archie, darling, would you be very hurt if I gave father the

[035-023] picture?”

[035-024] “Oh, I say!”

[035-025] “Well, I can’t think of anything else.”

[035-026] “But wouldn’t you miss it most frightfully?”

[035-027] “Oh, of course I should. But you see—father’s birthday—”

[035-028] Archie had always thought Lucille the dearest and most unselfish angel

[035-029] in the world, but never had the fact come home to him so forcibly as

[035-030] now. He kissed her fondly.

[035-031] “By Jove!” he exclaimed. “You really are, you know! This is the biggest

[035-032] thing since jolly old Sir Philip What’s-his-name gave the drink of

[035-033] water to the poor blighter whose need was greater than his, if you

[035-034] recall the incident. I had to sweat it up at school, I remember. Sir

[035-035] Philip, poor [[old-bean|old bean]], had a most ghastly thirst on, and he was just

[035-036] going to have one on the house, so to speak, when... but it’s all in

[035-037] the history-books. This is the sort of thing [[boy-scouts-of-america|Boy Scouts]] do! Well, of

[035-038] course, it’s up to you, queen of my soul. If you feel like making the

[035-039] sacrifice, right-o! Shall I bring the pater up here and show him the

[035-040] picture?”

[035-041] “No, I shouldn’t do that. Do you think you could get into his suite

[035-042] to-morrow morning and hang it up somewhere? You see, if he had the

[035-043] chance of—what I mean is, if—yes, I think it would be best to hang it

[035-044] up and let him discover it there.”

[035-045] “It would give him a surprise, you mean, what?”

[035-046] “Yes.”

[035-047] Lucille sighed inaudibly. She was a girl with a conscience, and that

[035-048] conscience was troubling her a little. She agreed with Archie that the

[035-049] discovery of the [[the-wigmore-venus|Wigmore Venus]] in his artistically furnished suite

[035-050] would give [[daniel-brewster|Mr. Brewster]] a surprise. Surprise, indeed, was perhaps an

[035-051] inadequate word. She was sorry for her father, but the instinct of

[035-052] self-preservation is stronger than any other emotion.

[035-053] Archie whistled merrily on the following morning as, having driven a

[035-054] nail into his [[fatherhood-and-authority|father-in-law]]’s wallpaper, he adjusted the cord from

[035-055] which the Wigmore Venus was suspended. He was a kind-hearted young man,

[035-056] and, though Mr. Daniel Brewster had on many occasions treated him with

[035-057] a good deal of austerity, his simple soul was pleased at the thought of

[035-058] doing him a good turn, He had just completed his work and was stepping

[035-059] cautiously down, when a voice behind him nearly caused him to

[035-060] overbalance.

[035-061] “What the devil?”

[035-062] Archie turned beamingly.

[035-063] “Hullo, old thing! Many happy returns of the day!”

[035-064] Mr. Brewster was standing in a frozen attitude. His strong face was

[035-065] slightly flushed.

[035-066] “What—what—?” he gurgled.

[035-067] Mr. Brewster was not in one of his sunniest moods that morning. The

[035-068] proprietor of a large hotel has many things to disturb him, and to-day

[035-069] things had been going wrong. He had come up to his suite with the idea

[035-070] of restoring his shaken nerve system with a quiet cigar, and the sight

[035-071] of his son-in-law had, as so frequently happened, made him feel worse

[035-072] than ever. But, when Archie had descended from the chair and moved

[035-073] aside to allow him an uninterrupted view of the picture, Mr. Brewster

[035-074] realised that a worse thing had befallen him than a mere visit from one

[035-075] who always made him feel that the world was a bleak place.

[035-076] He stared at the Venus dumbly. Unlike most hotel-proprietors, Daniel

[035-077] Brewster was a connoisseur of Art. Connoisseuring was, in fact, his

[035-078] hobby. Even the public rooms of [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|the Cosmopolis]] were decorated with

[035-079] taste, and his own private suite was a shrine of all that was best and

[035-080] most artistic. His tastes were quiet and restrained, and it is not too

[035-081] much to say that the Wigmore Venus hit him behind the ear like a

[035-082] stuffed eel-skin.

[035-083] So great was the shock that for some moments it kept him silent, and

[035-084] before he could recover speech Archie had explained.

[035-085] “It’s a birthday present from Lucille, don’t you know.”

[035-086] Mr. Brewster crushed down the breezy speech he had intended to utter.

[035-087] “Lucille gave me—that?” he muttered.

[035-088] He swallowed pathetically. He was suffering, but the iron courage of

[035-089] the Brewsters stood him in good stead. This man was no weakling.

[035-090] Presently the rigidity of his face relaxed. He was himself again. Of

[035-091] all things in the world he loved his daughter most, and if, in whatever

[035-092] mood of temporary insanity, she had brought herself to suppose that

[035-093] this beastly daub was the sort of thing he would like for a birthday

[035-094] present, he must accept the situation like a man. He would on the whole

[035-095] have preferred death to a life lived in the society of the Wigmore

[035-096] Venus, but even that torment must be endured if the alternative was the

[035-097] hurting of Lucille’s feelings.

[035-098] “I think I’ve chosen a pretty likely spot to hang the thing, what?”

[035-099] said Archie cheerfully. “It [[appearance-and-first-impressions|looks]] well alongside those Japanese prints,

[035-100] don’t you think? Sort of stands out.”

[035-101] Mr. Brewster licked his dry lips and grinned a ghastly grin.

[035-102] “It does stand out!” he agreed.

[035-103] CHAPTER XXVI.

[035-104] A TALE OF A GRANDFATHER

[035-105] Archie was not a man who readily allowed himself to become worried,

[035-106] especially about people who were not in his own immediate circle of

[035-107] friends, but in the course of the next week he was bound to admit that

[035-108] he was not altogether easy in his mind about his father-in-law’s mental

[035-109] condition. He had read all sorts of things in the Sunday papers and

[035-110] elsewhere about the constant strain to which captains of industry are

[035-111] subjected, a strain which sooner or later is only too apt to make the

[035-112] victim go all blooey, and it seemed to him that Mr. Brewster was

[035-113] beginning to find the going a trifle too tough for his stamina.

[035-114] Undeniably he was behaving in an odd manner, and Archie, though no

[035-115] physician, was aware that, when the American business-man, that

[035-116] restless, ever-active human machine, starts behaving in an odd manner,

[035-117] the next thing you know is that two strong men, one attached to each

[035-118] arm, are hurrying him into the cab bound for Bloomingdale.

[035-119] He did not confide his misgivings to Lucille, not wishing to cause her

[035-120] anxiety. He hunted up Reggie van Tuyl at the club, and sought advice

[035-121] from him.

[035-122] “I say, Reggie, old thing—present company excepted—have there been any

[035-123] loonies in your family?”

[035-124] Reggie stirred in the slumber which always gripped him in the early

[035-125] afternoon.

[035-126] “Loonies?” he mumbled, sleepily. “Rather! My uncle Edgar thought he was

[035-127] twins.”

[035-128] “Twins, eh?”

[035-129] “Yes. Silly idea! I mean, you’d have thought one of my uncle Edgar

[035-130] would have been enough for any man.”

[035-131] “How did the thing start?” asked Archie.

[035-132] “Start? Well, the first thing we noticed was when he began wanting two

[035-133] of everything. Had to set two places for him at dinner and so on.

[035-134] Always wanted two seats at the theatre. Ran into [[wealth-and-money|money]], I can tell

[035-135] you.”

[035-136] “He didn’t behave rummily up till then? I mean to say, wasn’t sort of

[035-137] jumpy and all that?”

[035-138] “Not that I remember. Why?”

[035-139] Archie’s tone became grave.

[035-140] “Well, I’ll tell you, old man, though I don’t want it to go any

[035-141] farther, that I’m a bit worried about my jolly old father-in-law. I

[035-142] believe he’s about to go in off the deep-end. I think he’s cracking

[035-143] under the strain. Dashed weird his behaviour has been the last few

[035-144] days.”

[035-145] “Such as?” murmured Mr. van Tuyl.

[035-146] “Well, the other morning I happened to be in his suite—incidentally he

[035-147] wouldn’t go above ten dollars, and I wanted twenty-five-and he suddenly

[035-148] picked up a whacking big paper-weight and bunged it for all he was

[035-149] worth.”

[035-150] “At you?”

[035-151] “Not at me. That was the rummy part of it. At a mosquito on the wall,

[035-152] he said. Well, I mean to say, do chappies bung paper-weights at

[035-153] mosquitoes? I mean, is it done?”

[035-154] “Smash anything?”

[035-155] “Curiously enough, no. But he only just missed a rather decent picture

[035-156] which Lucille had given him for his birthday. Another foot to the left

[035-157] and it would have been a goner.”

[035-158] “Sounds queer.”

[035-159] “And, talking of that picture, I looked in on him about a couple of

[035-160] afternoons later, and he’d taken it down from the wall and laid it on

[035-161] the floor and was staring at it in a dashed marked sort of manner. That

[035-162] was peculiar, what?”

[035-163] “On the floor?”

[035-164] “On the jolly old carpet. When I came in, he was goggling at it in a

[035-165] sort of glassy way. Absolutely rapt, don’t you know. My coming in gave

[035-166] him a start—seemed to rouse him from a kind of trance, you know—and he

[035-167] jumped like an antelope; and, if I hadn’t happened to grab him, he

[035-168] would have trampled bang on the thing. It was deuced unpleasant, you

[035-169] know. His manner was rummy. He seemed to be brooding on something. What

[035-170] ought I to do about it, do you think? It’s not my affair, of course,

[035-171] but it seems to me that, if he goes on like this, one of these days

[035-172] he’ll be stabbing someone with a pickle-fork.”

[035-173] To Archie’s relief, his father-in-law’s symptoms showed no signs of

[035-174] development. In fact, his manner reverted to the normal once more, and

[035-175] a few days later, meeting Archie in the lobby of the hotel, he seemed

[035-176] quite cheerful. It was not often that he wasted his time talking to his

[035-177] son-in-law, but on this occasion he chatted with him for several

[035-178] minutes about the big picture-robbery which had formed the chief item

[035-179] of news on the front pages of the morning papers that day. It was Mr.

[035-180] Brewster’s opinion that the outrage had been the work of a gang and

[035-181] that nobody was safe.

[035-182] Daniel Brewster had spoken of this matter with strange earnestness, but

[035-183] his words had slipped from Archie’s mind when he made his way that

[035-184] night to his father-in-law’s suite. Archie was in an exalted mood. In

[035-185] the course of dinner he had had a bit of good news which was occupying

[035-186] his thoughts to the exclusion of all other matters. It had left him in

[035-187] a comfortable, if rather dizzy, condition of benevolence to all created

[035-188] things. He had smiled at the room-clerk as he crossed the lobby, and if

[035-189] he had had a dollar, he would have given it to the boy who took him up

[035-190] in the elevator.

[035-191] He found the door of the Brewster suite unlocked, which at any other

[035-192] time would have struck him as unusual; but to-night he was in no frame

[035-193] of mind to notice these trivialities. He went in, and, finding the room

[035-194] dark and no one at home, sat down, too absorbed in his thoughts to

[035-195] switch on the lights, and gave himself up to dreamy meditation.
