[001-066] CHAPTER I.

[001-067] DISTRESSING SCENE

[001-068] “I say, laddie!” said Archie.

[001-069] “Sir?” replied the desk-clerk alertly. All the employes of the Hotel

[001-070] Cosmopolis were alert. It was one of the things on which Mr. Daniel

[001-071] Brewster, the proprietor, insisted. And as he was always wandering

[001-072] about the lobby of the hotel keeping a personal eye on affairs, it was

[001-073] never safe to relax.

[001-074] “I want to see the manager.”

[001-075] “Is there anything I could do, sir?”

[001-076] Archie looked at him doubtfully.

[001-077] “Well, as a matter of fact, my dear old desk-clerk,” he said, “I want

[001-078] to kick up a fearful row, and it hardly seems fair to lug you into it.

[001-079] Why you, I mean to say? The blighter whose head I want on a charger is

[001-080] the bally manager.”

[001-081] At this point a massive, grey-haired man, who had been standing close

[001-082] by, gazing on the lobby with an air of restrained severity, as if

[001-083] daring it to start anything, joined in the [[conversation-and-dialogue|conversation]].

[001-084] “I am the manager,” he said.

[001-085] His eye was cold and hostile. Others, it seemed to say, might like

[001-086] Archie Moffam, but not he. Daniel Brewster was bristling for combat.

[001-087] What he had overheard had shocked him to the core of his being. The

[001-088] [[hotel-cosmopolis-place|Hotel Cosmopolis]] was his own private, personal property, and the thing

[001-089] dearest to him in the world, after his daughter Lucille. He prided

[001-090] himself on the fact that his hotel was not like other [[new-york-city|New York]] hotels,

[001-091] which were run by impersonal companies and shareholders and boards of

[001-092] directors, and consequently lacked the paternal touch which made the

[001-093] Cosmopolis what it was. At other hotels things went wrong, and clients

[001-094] complained. At [[hotel-cosmopolis|the Cosmopolis]] things never went wrong, because he was

[001-095] on the spot to see that they didn’t, and as a result clients never

[001-096] complained. Yet here was this long, thin, string-bean of an Englishman

[001-097] actually registering annoyance and dissatisfaction before his very

[001-098] eyes.

[001-099] “What is your complaint?” he enquired frigidly.

[001-100] Archie attached himself to the top button of Mr. Brewster’s coat, and

[001-101] was immediately dislodged by an irritable jerk of the other’s

[001-102] substantial body.

[001-103] “Listen, old thing! I came over to this country to nose about in search

[001-104] of a job, because there doesn’t seem what you might call a general

[001-105] demand for my services in [[england|England]]. Directly I was demobbed, the family

[001-106] started talking about the Land of Opportunity and shot me on to a

[001-107] liner. The idea was that I might get hold of something in America—”

[001-108] He got hold of Mr. Brewster’s coat-button, and was again shaken off.

[001-109] “Between ourselves, I’ve never done anything much in England, and I

[001-110] fancy the family were getting a bit fed. At any rate, they sent me over

[001-111] here—”

[001-112] Mr. Brewster disentangled himself for the third time.

[001-113] “I would prefer to postpone the story of your life,” he said coldly,

[001-114] “and be informed what is your specific complaint against the Hotel

[001-115] Cosmopolis.”

[001-116] “Of course, yes. The jolly old hotel. I’m coming to that. Well, it was

[001-117] like this. A chappie on the boat told me that this was the best place

[001-118] to stop at in New York—”

[001-119] “He was quite right,” said Mr. Brewster.

[001-120] “Was he, by Jove! Well, all I can say, then, is that the other New York

[001-121] hotels must be pretty mouldy, if this is the best of the lot! I took a

[001-122] room here last night,” said Archie quivering with self-pity, “and there

[001-123] was a beastly tap outside somewhere which went drip-drip-drip all night

[001-124] and kept me awake.”

[001-125] Mr. Brewster’s annoyance deepened. He felt that a chink had been found

[001-126] in his armour. Not even the most paternal hotel-proprietor can keep an

[001-127] eye on every tap in his establishment.

[001-128] “Drip-drip-drip!” repeated Archie firmly. “And I put my boots outside

[001-129] the door when I went to bed, and this morning they hadn’t been touched.

[001-130] I give you my solemn word! Not touched.”

[001-131] “Naturally,” said Mr. Brewster. “My employés are honest.”

[001-132] “But I wanted them cleaned, dash it!”

[001-133] “There is a shoe-shining parlour in the basement. At the Cosmopolis

[001-134] shoes left outside bedroom doors are not cleaned.”

[001-135] “Then I think the Cosmopolis is a bally rotten hotel!”

[001-136] Mr. Brewster’s compact frame quivered. The unforgivable insult had been

[001-137] offered. Question the legitimacy of Mr. Brewster’s parentage, knock Mr.

[001-138] Brewster down and walk on his face with spiked shoes, and you did not

[001-139] irremediably close all avenues to a peaceful settlement. But make a

[001-140] remark like that about his hotel, and war was definitely declared.

[001-141] “In that case,” he said, stiffening, “I must ask you to give up your

[001-142] room.”

[001-143] “I’m going to give it up! I wouldn’t stay in the bally place another

[001-144] minute.”

[001-145] Mr. Brewster walked away, and Archie charged round to the cashier’s

[001-146] desk to get his bill. It had been his intention in any case, though for

[001-147] dramatic purposes he concealed it from his adversary, to leave the

[001-148] hotel that morning. One of the [[letters-and-correspondence|letters]] of introduction which he had

[001-149] brought over from England had resulted in an invitation from a Mrs. van

[001-150] [[mr-tuyl|Tuyl]] to her house-party at [[miami|Miami]], and he had decided to go there at

[001-151] once.

[001-152] “Well,” mused Archie, on his way to the station, “one thing’s certain.

[001-153] I’ll never set foot in _that_ bally place again!”

[001-154] But nothing in this world is certain.

[001-155] CHAPTER II.

[001-156] A SHOCK FOR MR. BREWSTER

[001-157] Mr. Daniel Brewster sat in his luxurious suite at the Cosmopolis,

[001-158] smoking one of his admirable cigars and chatting with his old friend,

[001-159] [[professor-binstead|Professor Binstead]]. A stranger who had only encountered Mr. Brewster in

[001-160] the lobby of the hotel would have been surprised at the [[appearance-and-first-impressions|appearance]] of

[001-161] his sitting-room, for it had none of the rugged simplicity which was

[001-162] the keynote of its owner’s personal appearance. Daniel Brewster was a

[001-163] man with a hobby. He was what [[parker|Parker]], his valet, termed a connoozer.

[001-164] His educated taste in Art was one of the things which went to make the

[001-165] Cosmopolis different from and superior to other New York hotels. He had

[001-166] personally selected the tapestries in the [[dining-and-social-ritual|dining]]-room and the various

[001-167] [[art-and-collecting|paintings]] throughout the building. And in his private capacity he was

[001-168] an enthusiastic collector of things which Professor Binstead, whose

[001-169] tastes lay in the same direction, would have stolen without a twinge of

[001-170] conscience if he could have got the chance.

[001-171] The professor, a small man of middle age who wore tortoiseshell-rimmed

[001-172] spectacles, flitted covetously about the room, inspecting its treasures

[001-173] with a glistening eye. In a corner, Parker, a grave, lean individual,

[001-174] bent over the chafing-dish, in which he was preparing for his employer

[001-175] and his guest their simple lunch.

[001-176] “Brewster,” said Professor Binstead, pausing at the mantelpiece.

[001-177] Mr. Brewster looked up amiably. He was in placid mood to-day. Two weeks

[001-178] and more had passed since the meeting with Archie recorded in the

[001-179] previous chapter, and he had been able to dismiss that disturbing

[001-180] affair from his mind. Since then, everything had gone splendidly with

[001-181] Daniel Brewster, for he had just accomplished his ambition of the

[001-182] moment by completing the negotiations for the purchase of a site

[001-183] further down-town, on which he proposed to erect a new hotel. He liked

[001-184] building hotels. He had the Cosmopolis, his first-born, a summer hotel

[001-185] in the mountains, purchased in the previous year, and he was toying

[001-186] with the idea of running over to England and putting up another in

[001-187] London, That, however, would have to wait. Meanwhile, he would

[001-188] concentrate on this new one down-town. It had kept him busy and

[001-189] worried, arranging for securing the site; but his troubles were over

[001-190] now.

[001-191] “Yes?” he said.

[001-192] Professor Binstead had picked up a small china figure of delicate

[001-193] workmanship. It represented a warrior of pre-khaki days advancing with

[001-194] a spear upon some adversary who, judging from the contented expression

[001-195] on the warrior’s face, was smaller than himself.

[001-196] “Where did you get this?”

[001-197] “That? [[mawson|Mawson]], my agent, found it in a little shop on the east side.”

[001-198] “Where’s the other? There ought to be another. These things go in

[001-199] pairs. They’re valueless alone.”

[001-200] Mr. Brewster’s brow clouded.

[001-201] “I know that,” he said shortly. “Mawson’s looking for the other one

[001-202] everywhere. If you happen across it, I give you _carte blanche_ to buy

[001-203] it for me.”

[001-204] “It must be somewhere.”

[001-205] “Yes. If you find it, don’t worry about the expense. I’ll settle up, no

[001-206] matter what it is.”

[001-207] “I’ll bear it in mind,” said Professor Binstead. “It may cost you a lot

[001-208] of money. I suppose you know that.”

[001-209] “I told you I don’t care what it costs.”

[001-210] “It’s nice to be a millionaire,” sighed Professor Binstead.

[001-211] “Luncheon is served, sir,” said Parker.

[001-212] He had stationed himself in a statutesque pose behind Mr. Brewster’s

[001-213] chair, when there was a knock at the door. He went to the door, and

[001-214] returned with a telegram.

[001-215] “Telegram for you, sir.”

[001-216] Mr. Brewster nodded carelessly. The contents of the chafing-dish had

[001-217] justified the advance advertising of their odour, and he was too busy

[001-218] to be interrupted.

[001-219] “Put it down. And you needn’t wait, Parker.”

[001-220] “Very good, sir.”

[001-221] The valet withdrew, and Mr. Brewster resumed his lunch.

[001-222] “Aren’t you going to open it?” asked Professor Binstead, to whom a

[001-223] telegram was a telegram.

[001-224] “It can wait. I get them all day long. I expect it’s from Lucille,

[001-225] saying what train she’s making.”
