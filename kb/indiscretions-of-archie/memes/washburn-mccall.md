## Overview

Washington "Washy" McCall is the teenage son of Mr. Lindsay McCall and Mrs. Cora Bates McCall. A long, thin boy of about sixteen with pale red hair and sandy eyelashes, Washy is raised on his mother's strict "Rational Eating" diet — until he secretly enters a pie-eating championship and becomes an overnight sensation, winning the title in Chapter XXII ("Washy Steps Into the Hall of Fame").

## In the Novel

### The Rational Eating Regimen

Washy first appears at the McCall family breakfast table, where his mother monitors his diet with scientific precision. Mrs. McCall attributes her son's "superb" constitution and "remarkable stamina" entirely to her careful supervision of his food, shuddering at the thought of "growing boys who are permitted by irresponsible people to devour meat, candy, pie" [029-101–029-108].

The irony becomes apparent when Washy abruptly closes his mouth at the mention of pie — an internal reaction that suggests he has already sampled forbidden food [029-112].

### Pie-Eating Champion

The central episode of Washy's arc occurs when Archie Moffam takes him to the Great Pie Championship, where men "with bulging cheeks and eyes consume vast quantities of pies" [030-007]. Competing as "Blake's Unknown," Washy defeats the defending champion, O'Dowd, who "declined to eat another slice" and needed oxygen after his effort. Washy, by contrast, "seemed disappointed it was done. He somehow made those present feel he'd barely started on his meal" [030-030].

When his mother discovers the truth, Washy deploys the classic teenage defense: "'_He_ made me do it!' said Washy, with the stern joy a sixteen-year-old boy feels when he sees somebody on to whose shoulders he can shift trouble from his own" — pointing the finger squarely at Archie [030-096].

### Catalyst for Family Rebellion

Washy's pie-eating success has consequences beyond his own fame. His father, Mr. McCall, witnessing the "Washy revelations" and seeing how his son has "thriven" on pie, finally rebels against his wife's dietary regime: "I don't want to hurt your feelings, Cora, but Washington and I have drunk our last cup of anti-caffeine! If you care to go on with the stuff, that's your look-out. But Washy and I are through" [030-154–030-158].

Washy thus inadvertently becomes the catalyst that breaks the McCall household free from Mrs. McCall's food-reform tyranny.

## Character and Appearance

The narrator describes Washy with characteristic Wodehousian candor: "He looks just like a piece of cheese: he's not a brilliant sort of chap: he has a dull and vacant map: his eyes are blank, his face is red, his ears stick out beside his head" [029-193]. Despite this unflattering portrait, Washy achieves an unlikely kind of greatness, proving that "in these stirring days Fame may be won in many ways" [029-192].
