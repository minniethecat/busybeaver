# The Sad Case of Looney Biddle

A disastrous chain of events in which Archie Moffam, attempting to reconcile Looney Biddle with his girlfriend, accidentally causes the star pitcher to break his left hand—the very hand upon which Archie's bet (and his entire thousand dollars) depends.

## Summary

Archie, having doubled his capital to a thousand dollars by betting on the New York Giants, plans to buy a birthday bracelet for his wife [[lucille-brewster|Lucille]]. His confidence in the Giants rests entirely on their star left-handed pitcher, [[looney-biddle|Looney Biddle]] (017-078). After purchasing the bracelet at a jeweller's on [[broadway|Broadway]], Archie spots the [[the-girl-friend|Girl Friend]]—Biddle's sweetheart—and decides to follow her in an attempt to persuade her to reconcile with Biddle, whose pitching form has been suffering due to romantic troubles (018-044). This well-intentioned intervention backfires catastrophically: Biddle, also trailing the girl, mistakes Archie for a rival and throws a punch. Archie dodges, and Biddle's fist smashes against a brick wall, grievously injuring his priceless left hand (019-027).

## Background / Lead-Up

The episode is set in motion by two concurrent circumstances. First, Archie has received an unexpected five-hundred-dollar windfall from an English aunt, which he doubles by betting on the Giants against the [[pittsburg-pirates|Pittsburg Pirates]] (017-005). Second, Looney Biddle's pitching—and therefore the Giants' chances—has been compromised by a quarrel with his girlfriend, who has walked out after an argument at the movies (018-016). Archie, seeing the Girl Friend at the Hotel Cosmopolis, resolves to track her down and plead Biddle's case, believing that a happy pitcher is a winning pitcher (018-044).

## Timeline

1. **Archie buys the bracelet** — At a jeweller's shop, Archie purchases an eight-hundred-and-fifty-dollar bracelet for Lucille's birthday, accompanied by [[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie van Tuyl]] (017-035)
2. **Archie spots the Girl Friend** — In the hotel lobby, Archie overhears the Girl Friend complaining to the cigar-stand girl about her quarrel with Biddle (018-016)
3. **Archie follows her** — He trails the Girl Friend through the streets, determined to talk her into reconciling with the pitcher (018-044)
4. **Biddle attacks** — Looney Biddle, also following the girl, confronts Archie on the street and swings at him (019-019)
5. **The injury** — Archie dodges; Biddle's fist crashes into the wall. His left hand—the foundation of the Giants' pitching staff—is wrecked (019-038)
6. **The aftermath** — Archie walks despondently down Ninth Avenue, realizing that his thousand dollars is gone and Lucille will receive no birthday present (020-009)

## Characters Involved

- **[[looney-biddle|Looney Biddle]]** — Star left-handed pitcher for the New York Giants; his injured hand dooms Archie's bet (017-083)
- **[[archie-moffam|Archie Moffam]]** — Well-meaning protagonist whose intervention triggers the disaster (019-019)
- **[[the-girl-friend|The Girl Friend]]** — Biddle's unnamed sweetheart; the object of both Archie's pursuit and Biddle's jealousy (018-044)
- **[[reggie-van-tuyl|Reggie van Tuyl]]** — Archie's friend, present at the bracelet purchase (017-034)

## Outcome / Consequences

Biddle's left hand, described as "that priceless left hand before which opposing batters quailed and wilted," is "out of action, resting in a sling, careened like a damaged battleship" (020-019). The Giants' chances of beating the Pirates evaporate, and with them Archie's entire thousand dollars. Archie is left alone, penniless, on [[ninth-avenue|Ninth Avenue]], contemplating the ruin of Lucille's birthday. The episode illustrates Wodehouse's signature pattern of well-meaning actions snowballing into catastrophe—Archie's attempt to help Biddle's romance destroys Biddle's pitching hand and Archie's finances in one stroke.

## Comic Elements

The episode is a classic Wodehouse farce built on comic irony: Archie follows the Girl Friend to help Biddle win her back, but Biddle mistakenly believes Archie is a romantic rival. The physical comedy of the punch—Archie's nimble dodge and Biddle's hand meeting the wall—is pure slapstick. The narrative voice heightens the absurdity by treating Biddle's left hand with mock-epic reverence, framing a baseball pitcher's hand as a priceless national treasure. The rising stakes (Archie's marriage, his finances, a baseball pennant race) all pivot on one missed punch, exemplifying Wodehouse's gift for making cosmic consequences hinge on trivial mishaps.
