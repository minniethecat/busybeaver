## Overview

The **Dragon of Alca** (*le dragon d'Alca*) is the monster whose depredations drive the central crisis of Livre II. Its reign of terror over the Penguin countryside is ended only when the virgin [[orberose|Orberose]] confronts and subdues it, establishing her sainthood and becoming the foundational miracle of the Penguin national cult.

## In the Text

The dragon first appears as a rumor sweeping through the coastal villages:

> On apprit partout à la fois qu'un dragon affreux avait ravagé deux fermes dans la baie des Plongeons. (016-020)

Witnesses are summoned and their testimonies collected. The descriptions vary wildly — the dragon has "une tête de dragon, des griffes de lion, une queue de poisson" (016-047); one farmer reports seeing it remove its head in a barn to give a kiss (016-056); another, asked how large it is, can only reply "Il est couleur de dragon" (016-075).

The narrator frames the episode with a characteristic note of skepticism, citing Casanova's memoirs as evidence that belief in dragons was widespread (016-004–009). The beast continues its ravages through the farms of Les Dalles and Les Dombes (017-002), prompting a delegation of Penguins to seek [[saint-mael|Saint Maël]]'s aid.

## Defeat by Orberose

Maël, having studied the history and habits of dragons, declares that only a virgin can overcome the beast (019-044). The young Orberose, recently returned from her mysterious disappearance, is selected for the task. She confronts and subdues the dragon, an act that becomes the founding miracle of her sainthood and the basis of the cult of [[orberose|Sainte Orberose]].

## Literary Significance

France's treatment of the dragon is deeply ironic. The conflicting eyewitness accounts parody judicial testimony; the insistence on a virgin as the only remedy mocks hagiographic convention; and the entire episode, framed by Casanova's skeptical memoirs, undermines the miraculous from within. The dragon is simultaneously a real threat in the narrative and an obvious fabrication in the satire — France never resolves this tension, leaving the reader to navigate between credulity and cynicism.
