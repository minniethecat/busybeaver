## Overview

**Queen Glamorgane** is the wife of King [[roi-brian|Brian the Pious]] and a central figure in the Oddoul affair, one of the earliest hagiographic episodes in the Penguin chronicles. Her story — inflamed with illicit desire, rejected, and turning to false accusation — forms a satirical commentary on courtly corruption and the malleability of feminine virtue in medieval hagiography.

## Biography

Glamorgane appears in Livre III, Chapitre I ("Brian le Pieux et la Reine Glamorgane"), during the early Christian period of Penguin history. She accompanies the king daily to the wooden monastery (moustier) built near the palace for the monks of Yvern, attending religious ceremonies and singing hymns (025-021).

The Devil, having failed to tempt the virtuous young monk [[oddoul|Oddoul]], turns his attention to the queen:

> Par une nuit d'été, il s'approcha de la reine endormie sur sa couche, lui représenta l'image du jeune religieux qu'elle voyait tous les jours dans le moustier de bois, et il mit un charme sur cette image. Aussitôt l'amour entra comme un poison subtil dans les veines de Glamorgane. (025-034–037)

Consumed by desire, Glamorgane repeatedly seeks Oddoul's company under the pretext of having him instruct her children (025-041). When Oddoul refuses her advances out of religious scruple, her desires turn to irritation:

> Ce refus irrita les désirs de Glamorgane. Un jour, elle fit venir Oddoul dans sa chambre... (025-046)

She attempts to pull him to her bed, but Oddoul flees, reproaching her immodesty (025-057). Fearing exposure, Glamorgane fabricates evidence of assault:

> Glamorgane, lui montrant ses cheveux épars, ses yeux luisants de larmes, accusa Oddoul d'avoir attenté à son honneur... (025-067)

She tears her garments and scratches herself to simulate struggle. King Brian, enraged by the accusation, orders Oddoul burned alive before the palace (025-075).

The episode ends with Glamorgane's scheme exposed — but not through human justice. An angel intervenes to save Oddoul from the flames, and the truth of Glamorgane's false accusation is revealed through divine intervention. France notes that while Oddoul's innocence prevails, Glamorgane herself faces no earthly punishment:

> ...tandis que Glamorgane a résisté (025-082)

## Literary Significance

Glamorgane embodies the hagiographic archetype of the lustful queen — a recurring figure in medieval saints' lives where feminine desire functions as a trial for male sanctity. Anatole France inverts the convention by emphasizing the queen's agency and the social mechanics of false accusation: Glamorgane's tearing of her garments is depicted not as madness but as calculated performance.

The episode satirizes the credulity of royal authority: King Brian accepts the accusation without investigation, illustrating how power and piety fail to produce justice. France's ironic treatment — the Devil enters the story not through the saint but through the queen's sleeping body — suggests that the real threat to religious institutions comes not from external enemies but from the passions of those closest to power.
