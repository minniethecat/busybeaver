# M. du Clos des Lunes

**M. du Clos des Lunes** is a scholarly authority cited in the historical apparatus of Anatole France's *Penguin Island*. He appears exclusively in Livre III as one of the fictitious erudite sources that the narrator deploys to lend the parody of medieval chronicle its texture of academic authenticity.

## Scholarly Citations

M. du Clos des Lunes is invoked twice, both times as a source of textual and historical authority. His first appearance credits him with the publication of a Latin document central to the Penguin historical record:

> « …latin, a été publiée par M. du Clos des Lunes. On la trouvera ici… » [030-005]

The phrase "On la trouvera ici" — "it will be found here" — mimics the citation conventions of genuine scholarship, positioning du Clos des Lunes within an apparatus of footnotes and bibliographic references that is, of course, entirely invented.

His second citation is more emphatic:

> « Clos des Lunes l'a solidement établie. » [030-360]

The adverb *solidement* — solidly — is the satirical pivot. In genuine scholarship, a claim "solidly established" by a respected authority would close the matter. Here, the solidity is conferred by a scholar who does not exist, on a history that never happened, in a chronicle that is itself a parody. The very gesture of citation becomes the object of satire.

## Satirical Function

Du Clos des Lunes belongs to a class of characters in *Penguin Island* who never appear in person but whose names are deployed to construct the novel's elaborate pretense of historicity. Like the fictitious archives, lost manuscripts, and apocryphal chronicles that litter the narrator's footnotes, M. du Clos des Lunes exists only as a textual effect — a name that confers legitimacy by the mere fact of being cited in the proper format.

His name itself — "du Clos des Lunes," literally "of the Enclosure of Moons" — carries the faintly absurd, vaguely poetic quality characteristic of Anatole France's invented erudition. It sounds plausible enough to pass unchallenged, yet strange enough to reward a reader who pauses to consider it.
