## Overview

Prince Adélestan des Boscénos is one of the most active royalist conspirators in the plot to overthrow the Republic of Penguinia and restore the Draconid monarchy. Descended from an ancient noble family dating back to [[roi-brian|Brian the Pious]], he joins forces with [[pere-agaric|Father Agaric]] to organize the royalist conspiracy, advocating for direct action — bribery, corruption, and kidnapping — over legal means.

## Lineage and Background

The house of Boscénos traced its origins to Brian the Pious and had held the highest offices under the Draconids (039-005). Their coat of arms bore a silver warming-pan, a gift from Queen Crucha (039-011). Their family motto — «Ainsi qu'on se trouve» — originated in the 16th century when Duke Jean des Boscénos was caught by the king with his hand under the Duchesse de Skull's skirt; the king merely remarked, "So one finds oneself" (039-015).

Prince Adélestan, faithful to Draconid blood, had seen his personal fortune ruined under the republic and longed for the restoration of [[prince-crucho|Prince Crucho]], which he saw as the return of his own prosperity (039-021).

## Role in the Conspiracy

At the secret meeting convened by Agaric at the Duke d'Ampoule's country house, the prince opposed [[monsieur-de-la-trumelle|M. de la Trumelle]]'s advocacy for legal action, arguing that just causes need force as much as, or more than, unjust ones (039-034). He proposed three means of action: "embaucher les garçons bouchers, corrompre les ministres et enlever le président Formose" (039-038).

During the insurrection, the prince des Boscénos fought actively, losing his fortune in the struggle. He was seen on the palace walls waving an immense flag and singing the august canticle as the republic fell (042-121). When the republic was restored, he was among the conspirators tried and punished.

## Legacy

The prince represents the fusion of aristocratic privilege, reactionary politics, and cynical violence that France satirizes throughout the novel. His family history — a litany of treason, sexual scandal, and self-serving alliance — mirrors the real French aristocracy's claim to ancient legitimacy. His proposal to kidnap President Formose while another royalist calls Formose an ally encapsulates the incoherence of reactionary politics.
