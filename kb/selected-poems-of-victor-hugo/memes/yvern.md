# Yvern

**Yvern** (also known as Beargarden) is the great abbey on the coast of [[pingouinie|Pingouinie]] that serves as the spiritual and intellectual cradle of Penguin civilization. Founded before the baptism of the Penguins, it is the institution where [[saint-mael|Saint Maël]] received his education, rose to become abbot, and from which he launched the missionary voyage that would accidentally create the Penguin nation. As a physical place, Yvern encompasses the monastery buildings, a school, an infirmary, a guest house, and the coastal landing where boats from the island of Gad arrived — a complex that embodied both sanctity and, in France's ironic treatment, the persistent vulnerability of holy places to human frailty.

## Origins and Architecture

Yvern was already an established monastic foundation when the young Maël arrived there at the age of nine, sent by his royal Cambrian family to study sacred and profane letters (002-002). Under the blessed Abbot Gal, the abbey flourished. When Gal died, Maël succeeded him and expanded the physical plant:

> « Il y établit une école, une infirmerie, une maison des hôtes. » [002-010]

These additions — a school, an infirmary, and a guest house — transformed Yvern from a contemplative retreat into a hub of learning, healing, and hospitality, prefiguring its role as the civilizational seed of Pingouinie.

## Coastal Setting

The abbey's location on the coast of Cambrie made it a point of contact between the monastic world and the wider sea. Boats from the island of Gad frequently landed on "la côte d'Yvern" (003-083), bringing nuns whose visits, while intended as pious, became a source of scandalous disorder. This coastal setting — simultaneously a place of spiritual retreat and worldly temptation — encapsulates France's satirical vision of religious institutions: holy in design, perpetually compromised by human nature.

## Role in Penguin History

After Maël's return from baptizing the Penguins, Yvern became the command center from which the Penguin mission was directed. The monks of Yvern supplied the newly humanized Penguins with clothing — the monk Magis personally dressed [[orberose|Orberose]] (015-016) — and with ecclesiastical law. The monastery's intellectual tradition produced the chronicler Bulloch (013-023) and the historian Johannès Talpa, whose writings form the purported source material for France's "history."

When pagan raiders later sacked Yvern and drove out its monks (025-018), the event marked a turning point: the spiritual center of Penguin civilization was displaced, and power shifted decisively toward the secular realm of [[alca|Alca]].
