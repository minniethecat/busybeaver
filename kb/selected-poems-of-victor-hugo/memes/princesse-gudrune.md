# Princesse Gudrune des Boscénos

**Princesse Gudrune des Boscénos** is the wife of [[prince-adelestan|Prince Adélestan des Boscénos]] and a figure in the social and political life of modern Alca in Anatole France's *Penguin Island*. Appearing alongside her husband in Livre V through Livre VII, she moves from the ballroom to the courtroom tribune, her trajectory mirroring the declining fortunes of the old aristocracy.

## Name and Historical Echo

The name "Gudrune" carries a historical resonance within the Penguin chronicles. An earlier Gudrune appears in the medieval era of Livre III, where she is associated with liberating a prisoner and with a scene involving the figure Oddoul (025-088, 025-092). Whether Anatole France intended the modern Princesse Gudrune as a deliberate echo of this earlier namesake — or whether the recurrence is merely a satirical flourish on the recycling of noble names — remains open to interpretation. The medieval Gudrune belongs to the legendary past; the modern Princesse Gudrune belongs to a world where nobility has become decorative.

## Social Life

The Princesse first appears at court, where she and the Prince drink together as a married couple (038-054). Her presence at social functions is emblematic of the aristocratic round:

> « On dansa. Après le bal, la princesse Gudrune déchira sa robe verte. » [038-081]

This detail — the deliberate tearing of a green dress after a ball — suggests a gesture at once wasteful and theatrical, befitting a class for whom display and destruction are two faces of the same coin. It is one of the few moments in the novel where the Princesse acts independently, rather than as an appendage to her husband.

## The Affair and Its Aftermath

As [[the-affair|the Affair of the Eighty Thousand Bales of Hay]] unfolds, the Princesse des Boscénos follows the trajectory of her class from prominence to debasement. She appears in the tribunes during the trial, observing the proceedings alongside other noble figures (053-023). Her presence there signals the aristocracy's investment in the outcome — and their determination to be seen investing.

The most striking image of the Princesse comes at the height of the crisis:

> « Prosternée à ses pieds, la princesse des Boscénos lui tenait les cuisses. » [052-072]

Prostrate, clutching at thighs — the posture is one of desperate supplication. The Princesse who once danced and tore silk gowns now kneels in abjection. Anatole France offers no commentary on this transformation; the image speaks for itself as a satirical portrait of nobility brought low.

In the closing chapters of the Affair cycle, the Prince and Princesse continue to circulate in Alcan society, listed alongside vicomtes and other titled figures as though nothing has changed (058-003). The persistence of their social presence, despite humiliation and financial ruin, is perhaps the novel's final irony on the subject of aristocratic resilience.
