# Chatillon

**Chatillon** is the central figure of Livre V (*Les Temps Modernes: Chatillon*), a naval commander whose rise to celebrity status serves as Anatole France's satirical anatomy of modern political propaganda, media manipulation, and the manufacture of public heroes. Handsome, fortunate, and entirely thoughtless — "il était beau; il était heureux; il ne pensait à rien" (040-028) — Chatillon is the perfect empty vessel into which a nation pours its adoration.

## The Admiral and His Fleet

Chatillon first appears as the commander of a Penguin naval fleet:

> « Cette flotte était commandée par le fameux Chatillon qui portait le... » [040-020]

His reputation precedes him, though the basis for his fame remains conspicuously vague. France's description of Chatillon's qualities is deliberately thin: beauty, happiness, and an absence of thought. The admiral is not a strategist or a hero in any meaningful sense — he is simply *visible*, and visibility in the modern political order is its own form of power.

## The Manufacture of Celebrity

The machinery of Chatillon's fame is set in motion by interested parties. A financier, seeing opportunity, approaches the admiral with a large sum of money, and soon a crowd of hired enthusiasts runs behind Chatillon's horse shouting "Vive l'émiral!" (040-035). From this manufactured beginning, the enthusiasm becomes self-sustaining:

> « Chatillon ne pouvait désormais faire un pas sans être acclamé. » [040-036]

His portrait is sold on the boulevards of Alca; his bust is hawked at every bridge (041-004, 041-006). Every evening he appears on his balcony while pathetic crowds sing "C'est Chatillon qu'il nous faut" (041-010). The feedback loop of celebrity — visibility begets more visibility — operates with perfect indifference to the admiral's actual merits.

## Political Instrumentalization

Chatillon's popularity, once established, becomes a resource to be harvested. Political factions compete to claim him. He is hailed as "Chatillon, héros des Pingouins! Chatillon défenseur de la gloire et de..." (041-036), titles that mean everything and nothing. A minister treats him with calculated roughness (041-031), while others salute him with grand reverence as "connétable" (040-111).

The admiral himself remains passive throughout. When confronted with the political uses of his image, his characteristic response is mild demurral: "Mais non, répondait Chatillon" (041-041). He is not a participant in his own legend but its hostage.

## Literary Significance

Chatillon represents the modern phenomenon of the manufactured hero — the individual whose fame is a product not of achievement but of media amplification. His trajectory parallels and inverts that of [[greatauk|Greatauk]]: where Greatauk is a figure of active, violent authority from the novel's medieval origins, Chatillon is the passive, ornamental authority of the modern age. Greatauk *does*; Chatillon is *seen*.

France, writing in the era of mass newspapers and the cult of personality surrounding the Dreyfus Affair, understood that in modern politics, visibility is fungible with legitimacy. Chatillon is the pure case: a man who becomes indispensable to his nation not despite having done nothing, but precisely because his emptiness makes him available for every projection.
