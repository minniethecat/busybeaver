# Greatauk

**Greatauk** is one of the most enduring figures in Anatole France's *Penguin Island*, appearing first in the medieval origins of Pingouinie as the primal feudal lord and resurfacing in modern times as the corrupt Minister of War who fabricates the case against [[pyrot|Pyrot]]. Across the novel's vast historical span, Greatauk embodies the continuity of violent authority — the same "grand pingouin" who clubs a rival to death with a tree trunk in the age of [[saint-mael|Saint Maël]] becomes, centuries later, the general who declares guilt without evidence and demands proof be manufactured to fit his predetermined conclusion.

## Origins: The First Feudal Lord

Greatauk enters the narrative in the earliest days of Penguin society, shortly after the baptism. While the monk Bulloch discourses on the principles of civilization, a "grand pingouin à la peau blanche, au poil roux" descends into the valley carrying a tree trunk on his shoulder. Approaching a smaller penguin tending his lettuces, he declares simply:

> « Ton champ est à moi! » [013-103]

When the smaller penguin resists, the grand pingouin strikes him dead with a single blow of his club. This act — the first violent land seizure in Penguin history — goes unpunished. Instead, Bulloch approaches the murderer and, bowing to the ground, addresses him with the deference due to a sovereign:

> « Seigneur Greatauk, prince très redouté, lui dit-il, je viens vous rendre hommage, comme au fondateur d'une puissance légitime et d'une richesse héréditaire. » [013-103–104]

The monk sanctifies the crime, declaring that the skull of the slain penguin buried in the field will "attestera à jamais les droits sacrés de votre postérité sur cette terre anoblie par vous" (013-106–107). Greatauk's sons will be dukes of Skull, dominating the island of Alca. Bulloch then turns to Saint Maël and pronounces:

> « Mon père, bénissez Greatauk. Car toute puissance vient de Dieu. » [013-111]

Maël remains silent, eyes raised to heaven — an ambiguous witness to the consecration of violence into law.

In the chapter that follows, Greatauk is chosen "comme le plus noble" to lead the assembly of Penguin notables (014-017). His hand on the sacred sword, he presides over the codification of the earliest Penguin laws — the same laws that Bulloch has just used to legitimize his act of murder.

## Modern Incarnation: The General and the Affair

Centuries later, Greatauk reappears as **Général Greatauk, duc du Skull**, chief of the Penguin army. His personal animosity toward [[pyrot|Pyrot]] — a junior officer distinguished by zeal, a hooked nose, vanity, studiousness, thick lips, and exemplary conduct — is the spark that ignites the great [[the-affair|Affair of the Eighty Thousand Bundles of Hay]].

When informed that eighty thousand bundles of hay have gone missing, Greatauk does not investigate: he *knows*:

> « Ce doit être Pyrot qui les a volées! » [045-012]

This spontaneous conviction — unsupported by any fact — becomes the immovable premise of the entire judicial apparatus. Greatauk's certainty precedes and displaces evidence; proof is not something discovered but something produced to satisfy a conclusion already reached.

When the trial proceeds and the machinery of justice grinds forward, Greatauk articulates his philosophy of evidence with devastating clarity:

> « Des preuves, murmura Greatauk, des preuves, qu'est-ce que c'est que ça? » [046-059]

For Greatauk, "proof" is an obstacle to justice rather than its foundation. The trial of Pyrot is conducted in secret, conviction is a foregone conclusion, and the logic of confession is inverted: denial itself becomes proof of guilt (046-075).

Greatauk's own corruption is an open secret. He has "tripoté dans les fournitures" — taken kickbacks from a supplier named Maloury, the smallness of the bribe making it, if anything, more contemptible (045-047–049). The man who demands Pyrot's conviction for theft is himself a proven embezzler of military funds — a hypocrisy that [[the-affair|the Affair]] systematically refuses to recognize.

## Literary Significance

Greatauk serves a double satirical function. In the medieval chapters, his story anatomizes the origins of feudal legitimacy: power is born in violence, and law is invented afterward to sanctify what force has already seized. Bulloch's obeisance to the murderer is France's compressed allegory of all political philosophy that derives authority from *fait accompli*.

In the modern chapters, Greatauk embodies the military's role in the Dreyfus Affair — the historical scandal that France dissects through the lens of Pingouinie. Greatauk is the composite of every général who, confronted with inconvenient facts, preferred the comfort of prejudice to the labor of investigation. His trajectory from club-wielding brute to decorated minister traces a straight line: the instruments change, but the will to power that wields them does not.
