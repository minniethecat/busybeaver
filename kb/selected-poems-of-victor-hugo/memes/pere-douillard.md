## Overview

The Reverend Father Douillard is a Benedictine preacher whose retreats draw the fashionable society of Alca and whose spiritual direction of [[clarence|Madame Clarence]] places him at the center of «l'affaire des quatre-vingt mille bottes de foin.» A master of moral theology whose expertise is deployed in the service of ecclesiastical wealth accumulation, Douillard represents the commercialization of religion and the clerical exploitation of feminine piety.

## Ministry and Preaching

Douillard first appears as the preacher at a retreat where "devait monter le révérend père Douillard, de l'ordre de Saint-Benoît" (053-013). His retreats, held every Friday, attracted the elite of Penguin society. His preaching was noted for its "emportement apostolique" — apostolic fervor — which moved his audiences deeply (058-012).

His sermon text, *Deposuit potentes de sede* ("He hath put down the mighty from their seat"), from the Magnificat (053-032), was deployed with ironic effect: Douillard used it not to advocate for the poor but to consolidate ecclesiastical power.

## The Affair of the Eighty Thousand Trusses of Hay

Douillard became the spiritual director of [[clarence|Madame Clarence]], the wife of the Minister of Commerce. When Clarence sought guidance on how to handle her husband's involvement in the hay speculation scheme, Douillard provided theological and practical counsel. His direction — prioritizing the Church's financial interests over moral clarity — exemplified the institutional corruption France satirizes throughout the novel.

Douillard's "excellence en théologie morale" (062-068) was consistently deployed to rationalize transactions that benefited the religious establishment. The narrative underscores the irony: a moral theologian whose casuistry serves to dissolve moral obligations rather than clarify them.

## Character

The chronicle portrays Douillard as a master of religious performance — eloquent in the pulpit, solemn in the confessional, and effective in private counsel. His physical description as "un petit vieillard" (a small old man) belies his enormous institutional influence. Like [[pere-agaric|Father Agaric]], he represents the fusion of religious authority and political-economic power that France saw as characteristic of the Church's role in the Third Republic.

## Literary Significance

Douillard and Agaric form a complementary pair in France's clerical satire: Agaric is the political schemer, Douillard the spiritual-technical expert whose moral theology provides cover for institutional rapacity. The juxtaposition of sublime sermon texts (*Deposuit potentes*) with mundane financial schemes creates the ironic dissonance characteristic of the novel's treatment of religion.
