# Prince Adélestan des Boscénos

**Prince Adélestan des Boscénos** is a minor nobleman in the modern era of Anatole France's *Penguin Island*, appearing primarily in Livre V and Livre VI. A descendant of the ancient Penguin king [[roi-brian|Brian the Pious]], the Prince embodies the fossilized arrogance and political impotence of the old Alcan aristocracy. Frequently referred to simply as "le prince des Boscénos," he serves as a recurring figure in the satirical portrayal of the Penguin ruling class — placid, irreconcilable, and ultimately humiliated.

## Lineage

The House of Boscénos traced its origins to Brian the Pious (Brian le Pieux), one of the earliest kings of the Penguins (039-005). The family had occupied prominent offices under the ancient Alcan monarchy, and their heraldic distinction was confirmed by the gift of a silver warming-pan (*bassinoire d'argent*) bestowed upon them in recognition of their service (039-011). The family motto, reduced to four words, distilled the essence of noble pretension (039-018):

> « Ces quatre mots devinrent la devise des Boscénos. » [039-018]

Anatole France notes with characteristic irony that Prince Adélestan was not unworthy of this lineage:

> « Le prince Adélestan n'était point dégénéré de ses aïeux. » [039-019]

## Political Role

Throughout Livre V, the Prince des Boscénos appears as a figure of obstinate conservatism. He expresses opinions contrary to prevailing sentiment (039-034) and maintains his proposals with persistence even when they fail to gain traction (039-051). His political style is one of immovable placidity:

> « Le prince des Boscénos était placide à son ordinaire. » [039-124]

This placidity masks a deeper intransigence. During the escalating political tensions, he is described as "le plus irréconciliable" — the most irreconcilable of all (045-035). Yet his fury, when it does surface, proves fragile and collapses as quickly as it rises (045-079).

## Personal Character

The Prince is portrayed as a man of leisure and indulgence. After political skirmishes, he is found calmly smoking a cigar (043-021), and in moments of crisis he waits "indulgent et serein" in the darkness (041-136). He is not immune to mockery: a popular refrain circulates — "Boscénos est un gros cochon" (039-112) — which the Prince notably dislikes hearing (039-117).

His financial situation deteriorates over the course of the narrative; by Livre VI he has lost his fortune (042-059). Despite this, he continues to participate in political life, standing on palace walls waving immense flags (042-130, 042-136).

## The Affair of the Eighty Thousand Bales of Hay

The Prince des Boscénos plays a supporting role in [[the-affair|the Affair of the Eighty Thousand Bales of Hay]] (Livre VI). When the scandal begins to threaten the established order, it appears urgent to him that the agitation be curbed (050-008). He is charged with delivering the official response (050-072), emerging from council chambers to face public scrutiny (050-095).

Anatole France describes him in this moment as belonging to a type of "ancient soul" — a man whose convictions cannot be shaken by evidence or reason (050-107). The affair concludes with his physical humiliation: he is beaten with ox sinews (*nerfs de boeufs*) alongside his associates (050-126), a satirical denouement that underscores the collapse of aristocratic prestige in modern Penguin society.
