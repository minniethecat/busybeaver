## Overview

Oddoul is a young monk at the abbey of [[yvern|Yvern]] during the reign of [[roi-brian|King Brian the Pious]], appearing in the early Christian period of Penguin history. Known for his learning and virtue, his story — the attempted seduction by Queen [[glamorgane|Glamorgane]], false accusation, and miraculous deliverance — constitutes one of the earliest hagiographic episodes in the chronicle, satirizing medieval saints' lives.

## Life

Oddoul lived at the wooden monastery built by King Brian near his palace (025-023). A youth of exceptional piety and learning, he resisted multiple temptations sent by the Devil, who appeared to him in various forms: a warhorse, a maiden, a cup of mead, and a pair of dice (025-026). Having repelled each temptation through the sign of the cross, Oddoul became the target of a more subtle trap.

The Devil inflamed Queen Glamorgane with desire for the young monk. She repeatedly sought his company under the pretext of having him instruct her children (025-041). When Oddoul refused her advances out of religious scruple, she summoned him to her chamber and attempted to pull him to her bed (025-057). Oddoul fled, reproaching her immodesty.

Fearing exposure, Glamorgane accused Oddoul of attempted rape, tearing her own garments and scratching herself to fabricate evidence (025-069). King Brian, enraged, ordered Oddoul burned alive before the palace (025-075).

An angel, disguised as a servant named Gudrune, freed Oddoul from prison and led him to safety. When the angel discovered that Oddoul had in fact resisted the queen's advances, the angel's response was blunt: "Alors, qu'est-ce que tu fiches ici, espèce d'andouille?" (025-103). Oddoul was pushed into the street, where a chamber pot was emptied on his head, prompting him to reflect: "Tes desseins sont mystérieux, Seigneur, et tes voies impénétrables" (025-111).

## Literary Significance

The Oddoul episode exemplifies Anatole France's satirical treatment of hagiographic conventions. The story follows the classic template of the falsely accused saint (compare Joseph and Potiphar's wife), complete with angelic intervention, but subverts the genre through the angel's colloquial dismissal and the pot-de-chambre anticlimax. The chronicler's pedantic footnote about the Latin phrase *species inductilis* (025-105) further mocks ecclesiastical textual apparatus. Oddoul's final reflection — mistaking random misfortune for divine providence — encapsulates the book's critique of religious interpretation.
