## Overview

The Reverend Father Agaric is the mastermind behind the royalist conspiracy that forms the central plot of Livre V, «Les Temps modernes: Chatillon.» Tall, gaunt, and perpetually scheming, he runs a school for the sons of impoverished noble families in a quiet suburb of Alca. His political genius — and his ruthless pragmatism — drive the attempted restoration of the Draconid monarchy, making him one of the novel's most fully developed satirical portraits of clerical ambition.

## Life and Character

Under the principate of [[formose|Théodore Formose]], Agaric lived in a peaceful suburb of Alca, where he instructed the youth of ancient families in piety, fencing, and riding. He married them off to the daughters of the wealthy but despised financier caste (037-028). Tall, thin, dark, Agaric walked ceaselessly with his breviary in hand, his brow furrowed with cares that extended far beyond pedagogy (037-034).

The "thought of his thought, the work of his work" was to overthrow the republic. He was not motivated by personal interest, but by the conviction that democratic government was contrary to the holy society to which he belonged body and soul (037-041).

Agaric's political acumen showed in his interpretation of events. When he heard of a republican thrown into a well by cavalry officers shouting "Vive la chose publique!", he immediately took it as a sign of a great aristocratic ferment and concluded the moment to act had arrived (037-058).

## The Conspiracy

Agaric began by visiting [[pere-cornemuse|Father Cornemuse]] at the Conils wood, seeking financial backing (037-068). He then traveled to Marsouinia, disguised as a cattle merchant, to meet the exiled [[prince-crucho|Prince Crucho]] (038-001). Riding in Crucho's automobile with two young women, Agaric outlined the conspiracy while balancing a demoiselle on his knees, adjuring the prince with "sublime hauteur d'âme" to be faithful to his royal duties (038-031).

Back in Penguinia, Agaric convened a secret meeting of royalist nobles at the [[duc-dampoule|Duke d'Ampoule]]'s country house, including [[prince-des-boscenos|Prince des Boscénos]], [[monsieur-de-la-trumelle|M. de la Trumelle]], and [[vicomte-olive|the Vicomte Olive]] (039-027).

The conspiracy unraveled, and Agaric was eventually tried, convicted, and exiled. He took the road of exile "abattu" (defeated), his great designs brought to nothing — though the narrative suggests this was merely one episode in an eternal cycle of clerical-political ambition.

## Literary Significance

Agaric embodies the clerical-political type that France relentlessly satirized throughout his career — the churchman whose religious vocation is entirely subordinated to political machination. His name (agaric = a type of fungus) suggests a parasitic relationship to the body politic. The contrast between his pious exterior (breviary, priestly garb) and his ruthless realpolitik — bribing butchers, corrupting ministers, plotting kidnapping — illustrates the gulf between religious pretense and clerical ambition.

The conspiracy's absurdities — Agaric outlining revolution while sitting with a woman on his knees, the conspirators unable to agree whether Formose is enemy or ally — embody France's view of reactionary politics as simultaneously dangerous and farcical.
