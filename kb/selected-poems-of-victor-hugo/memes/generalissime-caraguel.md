# Généralissime Caraguel

**Généralissime Caraguel** is the supreme commander of the Penguin national army (*généralissime des armées pingouines*) during the turbulent modern era of Anatole France's *Penguin Island*. Appearing in Livre V and Livre VI, Caraguel embodies the military dimension of the political crisis that engulfs Alca — a figure whose authority is simultaneously invoked, courted, and menaced by the competing factions of the [[the-affair|Affair of the Eighty Thousand Bales of Hay]].

## Military Command

The office of generalissimo is presented in the novel as the apex of military ambition. In a satirical passage enumerating the rewards of political loyalty, the position is dangled as the ultimate prize — "vous seriez émiralissime et généralissime, commandant" (040-078) — a hyperbolic formulation that conflates naval and army supreme command into a single, absurd title. The generalissimo is, in the novel's cynical arithmetic, less a strategic mastermind than a piece on the chessboard of factional politics: "Pour qu'on les offre au généralissime qui les acceptera, lui!" (040-104).

Caraguel first appears by title alone, as "le généralissime de l'armée nationale" arriving at a social function (041-080). His physical presence is deferred — he is defined by his rank before he is defined by his name. When the crisis deepens, "le généralissime des armées pingouines s'y rendit" (043-007) — the impersonal construction suggesting that the office, more than the man, is what matters to the narrative.

## Role in the Affair

The most vivid image of Caraguel places him in physical intimacy with one of the Affair's central figures:

> « Douillard et le généralissime Caraguel, en se tenant embrassés. » [052-107]

This embrace between the generalissimo and the [[pere-douillard|Révérend Père Douillard]], the influential Capuchin preacher, is a tableau of the alliance between military power and religious authority. That they are "embrased" — holding each other — suggests not merely political alignment but a mutual dependency, two pillars of the old order clinging together as the ground shifts beneath them.

On the fifteenth of June, Caraguel takes his place in the unfolding drama (053-008). The specific date lends the narrative the texture of historical chronicle, even as the events it recounts are pure satire. Shortly thereafter, a direct warning is issued to him:

> « Écoutez; je vous en préviens, généralissime des armées pingouines. » [053-064]

The speaker and the content of the warning are left ambiguous in the narrative, but the formality of the address — the full title intoned as though in a proclamation — underscores the weight of the moment. The generalissimo of the Penguin armies is being put on notice.

## Satirical Function

Caraguel exemplifies the novel's treatment of military authority as a function of political convenience rather than martial prowess. He commands no battles, devises no strategies, and leads no troops in the field. Instead, he is embraced, warned, and positioned — an object moved by forces he does not control. His rank, far from signifying competence, signifies only the importance of the interests that claim his allegiance.
