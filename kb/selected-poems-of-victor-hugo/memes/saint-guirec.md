## Overview

**Saint Guirec** is a Breton saint invoked in Livre I of *L'Île des Pingouins* as part of the hagiographic tradition that provides the model for [[saint-mael|Saint Maël]]'s miraculous voyage. He belongs to the legendary age of Celtic evangelization.

## In the Text

When Maël arrives at the northern coast and sees a stone trough floating like a boat upon the waters, the narrator situates this marvel within a well-established tradition:

> C'était dans une cuve semblable que saint Guirec, le grand saint Colomban et tant de religieux d'Écosse et d'Irlande étaient allés évangéliser l'Armorique. (003-004–006)

Saint Guirec is listed alongside Saint Colomban and other Irish and Scottish monks who crossed the sea in stone vessels — a motif drawn from Breton folklore and hagiography. The passage continues with Sainte Avoye (who traveled in a pink granite mortar) and Saint Vouga (who crossed from Hibernia on a rock), establishing the miraculous stone-boat as a recognized form of saintly transport.

## Literary Significance

Guirec functions as a narrative authentication device: by placing Maël's stone-trough voyage in a lineage of Breton saints, France grounds his satire in authentic hagiographic convention. The accumulation of saints with improbable conveyances — troughs, mortars, rocks — simultaneously establishes and undermines the miraculous, a characteristic gesture of France's irony.
