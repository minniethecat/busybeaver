# Alca

## Overview

**Alca** is the capital city of the penguin nation in Anatole France's *L'Île des Pingouins*. As the political, cultural, and economic center of Penguinia, Alca serves as a transparent satirical stand-in for Paris — the city where all the absurdities of French civilization are concentrated and magnified.

## In the Novel

Alca appears throughout the novel as the stage for penguin history. Its streets, boulevards, and institutions mirror those of Belle Époque Paris. In the later books, Alca becomes the epicenter of political scandals, mob violence, and the Dreyfus-affair parody (050-001 through 050-005).

The city's geography — with its river, its quarters, its monuments — maps recognizably onto Parisian topography, allowing France to critique contemporary French society while maintaining the protective veneer of penguin fiction.

> The streets of Alca were the scene of daily violence against the pyrots: "Chaque jour, dans les rues d'Alca, on en assommait deux ou trois" (050-002) — each day in Alca's streets, two or three were beaten.

## Literary Function

As a fictionalized Paris, Alca enables France's satirical method: by renaming the familiar, he makes its absurdities visible. The city is simultaneously *nowhere* (a penguin capital) and *everywhere* (a recognizable modern metropolis), embodying the universalizing ambition of the satire.
