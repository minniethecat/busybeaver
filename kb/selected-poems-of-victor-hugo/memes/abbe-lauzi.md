## Overview

**Abbé Lauzi** is an eighteenth-century scholarly critic mentioned in passing in Livre III, Chapitre V of *L'Île des Pingouins*. He represents the aesthetic judgments of the Enlightenment era, which Anatole France treats with characteristic irony.

## In the Text

Lauzi appears in the chapter describing the Penguin painter Margaritone and the vicissitudes of artistic reputation:

> Un savant critique du XVIIIe siècle, l'abbé Lauzi, a traité les ouvrages de Margaritone avec un profond dédain. « Ce ne sont, a-t-il dit, que de grossiers barbouillages. En ces temps infortunés, on ne savait ni dessiner ni peindre. » (029-056–059)

His dismissal of Margaritone's works as "crude daubings" is later ironically reversed when the nineteenth century rediscovers and celebrates the "primitives." France uses Lauzi as a stand-in for the confident ignorance of one age judging another.

## Literary Significance

Lauzi serves as a narrative device in France's satire of art criticism. His verdict — delivered with the authority of the *savant* — is shown to be merely the prejudice of his time. The episode forms part of France's broader argument that aesthetic judgment is historically contingent, a theme he develops throughout the Margaritone passages.
