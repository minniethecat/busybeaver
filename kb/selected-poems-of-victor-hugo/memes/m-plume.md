# M. de Plume

**M. de Plume** is a minor figure in the household of the [[prince-adelestan|Prince des Boscénos]], serving as his squire (*écuyer*) during the modern era of Anatole France's *Penguin Island*. His appearances are brief and functional — he departs on errands and retrieves objects — but his presence helps to sketch the domestic apparatus that surrounds the aristocratic protagonist of Livre V.

## Role in the Boscénos Household

The title *écuyer* (squire) places M. de Plume in the traditional hierarchy of a noble household: not a servant in the modern sense, but a gentleman-attendant whose duties combine practical service with the symbolic function of reinforcing the prince's status. His first recorded action is a departure:

> « M. de Plume, écuyer du prince, partit le soir même. » [038-085]

The laconic formulation — he "departed that very evening" — leaves his mission unspecified. The urgency implied by "le soir même" suggests that whatever errand dispatched him was of consequence, though the narrative does not elaborate.

In his second appearance, M. de Plume is associated with the retrieval of domestic items — scarves and slippers (042-004). The juxtaposition of a gentleman-squire handling such quotidian objects is characteristic of Anatole France's satirical method: the grandeur of noble titles is undercut by the banality of the tasks they actually perform.

## Satirical Function

The name "de Plume" — literally "of Feather" — participates in the novel's repertoire of absurd aristocratic nomenclature. Like "des Boscénos" (of the Boscénos), "de Plume" sounds plausibly noble while carrying a faintly ridiculous charge: a squire named after a feather, attending a prince whose own name evokes thickets and woods. The cumulative effect of these names is to hollow out the dignity they purport to confer.

Within the narrative economy, M. de Plume is purely instrumental — he exists to be sent, to find, to depart. He has no independent agency, no speech, no interiority. This is not a defect of characterization but a deliberate choice: the squire is furniture in the house of Boscénos, and the novel treats him as such.
