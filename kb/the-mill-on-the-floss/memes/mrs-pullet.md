# Mrs. Pullet

Mrs. Pullet is the most extravagantly emotional of the Dodson sisters. Her elaborate displays of grief, her fascination with illness and medicine, and her conviction that every family occasion requires tears make her one of Eliot's finest comic portraits of provincial sentimentality.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Pullet appears primarily in the family gathering scenes, where her theatrical mourning and medical preoccupations provide comic relief while also revealing the emotional poverty of Dodson culture. Her sentiment is genuine but entirely [[custom|convention]]al; she feels what the occasion requires her to feel.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Pullet's defining trait is her performative grief. She weeps at every family occasion with a relish that suggests enjoyment as much as sorrow. Her obsession with her health and her medicine cabinet is a running joke. Yet Eliot's comedy is never mere mockery: Mrs. Pullet's tears, however [[custom|convention]]al, express a real need for emotional expression that the Dodson code otherwise suppresses. She is the family's designated mourner, the one permitted—even expected—to feel publicly.
