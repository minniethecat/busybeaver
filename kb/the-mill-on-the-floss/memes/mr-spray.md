# Mr. Spray

Mr. Spray is the Independent minister in St Ogg's, a Dissenting preacher whose venture into political controversy introduces one of the novel's characteristic notes: the collision between established provincial order and the disruptive forces of the wider world. Though he appears only in a single narrative aside, his presence registers the religious diversity beneath St Ogg's Anglican surface and the political currents — Catholic Emancipation — that agitated early nineteenth-century England.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Spray enters the narrative during the description of the Glegg household and its place in St Ogg's society. "But with the Catholic Question had come a slight wind of controversy to break the calm: the elderly rector had become occasionally historical and argumentative; and Mr Spray, the Independent minister, had begun to preach political sermons, in which he distinguished with much subtlety between his fervent belief in the right of the Catholics to the franchise and his fervent belief in their eternal perdition" [013-004]. The sentence is a masterpiece of Eliot's irony: Mr. Spray's "subtlety" — distinguishing political rights from theological condemnation — is precisely what his congregation cannot follow. "Most of Mr Spray's hearers," the narrator notes dryly, "were incapable of following his subtleties, and many old-fashioned Dissenters were much pained by his 'siding with the Catholics'; while others thought he had better let politics alone" [013-004]. The minister who tries to think carefully about a complex question pleases no one — a small parable of the intellectual's fate in provincial society.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Spray occupies a distinctive position in the novel's religious landscape. He is neither Anglican (like the elderly rector) nor Evangelical (like the rising Dr. Kenn); he represents the older Dissenting tradition — Independent, nonconformist, grounded in communities that had existed since the seventeenth century. His willingness to engage the Catholic Question from the pulpit marks him as a man of some intellectual ambition, yet the narrator's epithet — "much subtlety" — is double-edged. It praises his intelligence while noting its futility. In a town where "old-fashioned Dissenters" are pained by any suggestion of Catholic rights, subtlety is wasted breath.

The brief portrait glances at Eliot's own complicated relationship with Dissent. Brought up in an evangelical Anglican household, she had by the time of writing *The Mill on the Floss* moved far from orthodoxy, yet she retained a novelist's interest in the varieties of religious experience. Mr. Spray is neither saint nor fool; he is a man trying to think, and the town has no apparatus for receiving thought.
