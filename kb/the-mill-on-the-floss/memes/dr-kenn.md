# Dr. Kenn

Dr. Kenn is the minister of [[st-oggs-town|St. Ogg's]], a figure of genuine Christian [[sympathy|compassion]] who stands against the communal judgment that condemns [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] after her near-elopement. He appears late in the novel but provides one of its few examples of institutional religion practiced with wisdom and mercy.

## Role in the Novel

> Dr. Kenn was a man who, by his very presence, seemed to diffuse a sense of possible goodness. [058-007]

Dr. Kenn functions as a moral counterweight to the communal hypocrisy that destroys [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]. While [[mudport|the town]]speople of [[st-oggs-town|St. Ogg's]] treat her as fallen, Dr. Kenn recognizes her moral seriousness and offers her practical help. His inability to fully protect her from communal condemnation is not a failure of his character but a commentary on the limits of individual goodness against collective prejudice.

## Character Analysis

Dr. Kenn is Eliot's portrait of what genuine Christian ministry might look like. Unlike [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]]'s [[custom|convention]]al religiosity, Dr. Kenn's [[religion|faith]] expresses itself in practical [[sympathy|compassion]]. He is wise without being preachy, kind without being sentimental. His recognition of [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s moral worth—his refusal to condemn her for an act she did not complete—makes him one of the few characters who sees her clearly.

## Key Scenes

- **Offering refuge** (Book 7, Chapter 2): Dr. Kenn provides [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] with a place to stay when the community rejects her.
- **Ministering to [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]**: His [[nature|pastoral]] care as an example of religion practiced with sympathy.
- **The limits of goodness**: Dr. Kenn cannot save [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] from the community's judgment, revealing the power of collective condemnation.
