# Bob Jakin's Flood Rescue

Bob Jakin's flood rescue is the novel's final act of human agency before the waters claim their victims. As the Floss rises in the night, Bob — the peddler, the loyal friend, the unpretentious figure who has always seen Maggie clearly — rows out through the inundated streets, his boat the last hope for the siblings he loves.

## Summary

When the flood waters rise in Book 7, Chapter 5, Bob Jakin does not hesitate. He takes his boat — the same boat he has used in his trade along the river — and rows toward [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]], where Tom is trapped. He finds Maggie already in the water, also rowing toward the mill in a smaller boat. Together, they reach Tom at the mill, where the waters are rising fast. In a moment of fragile reunion, brother and sister embrace in the boat. But the current is too strong; the boat is overwhelmed, and Maggie and Tom drown together, "in an embrace never to be parted" (058-045). Bob survives — the witness who lives to tell of their final moments.

## Context

Bob Jakin has been Maggie's steadfast friend since childhood, when she showed him kindness that others denied him. His presence in the final scene is thematically crucial: Bob represents the loyal, unjudging love that respectable society — personified by the Dodsons — has never offered Maggie. His boat, the humble vessel of trade, becomes an ark of salvation that cannot quite save. Eliot's choice to have Bob as the rescuer underscores the novel's critique of social hierarchy: it is the peddler, not the gentleman, who risks his life for love (058-044).

## Aftermath and Consequences

The flood rescue ends in tragedy. Maggie and Tom die together, their childhood bond restored in the moment of death. Bob survives as the sole witness, the keeper of their story. The flood — the river that has been the novel's constant presence — proves to be both destroyer and uniter, erasing the divisions that separated brother and sister in life.

## Literary Significance

The flood rescue is Eliot's most operatic set-piece, a scene of extraordinary narrative compression and emotional force. Bob Jakin's role as rescuer is one of the novel's most pointed social ironies: the uneducated peddler, dismissed by the Dodsons as beneath notice, performs the novel's most heroic action. The scene also completes the novel's symbolic architecture: the Floss, which has flowed through every chapter, becomes the agent of both destruction and reunion, a natural force indifferent to human morality but mysteriously aligned with human love (058-043).
