# Mr. Gore

Mr. Gore is the attorney in [[st-oggs|St Ogg's]] who represents [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] in the aftermath of his lost lawsuit against [[mr-pivart|Mr. Pivart]]. As the bearer of catastrophic news — that [[lawyer-wakem|Wakem]] has secretly acquired the mortgage on [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]] — he functions as the pivot point from hope to ruin in the Tulliver family's financial collapse.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Gore serves as a functional minor character whose professional actions have disproportionate narrative consequences. He is the conduit through which impersonal financial and legal realities enter the Tulliver family's domestic world — a professional intermediary who delivers information rather than judgment, yet whose efficient communication triggers the family's collapse [024-007].

After the lawsuit against [[mr-pivart|Mr. Pivart]] is decided against Tulliver, it is to Mr. Gore that Tulliver turns for advice and strategy:

> "It was no wonder his face was flushed when he came away from his talk with his attorney, Mr Gore, and mounted his horse to ride home from Lindum." [024-001]

Mr. Gore is tasked with sounding out Furley, the mortgage-holder, about purchasing the estate and keeping Tulliver on as tenant — a scheme that might have preserved the family at [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]] [024-005].

## Character Analysis

Mr. Gore is defined entirely by his professional role. He appears only indirectly — through his letter, through Tulliver's recollections, through the financial aftermath — and the novel grants him no interiority or personal detail. This absence of characterisation is itself significant: he represents the faceless machinery of law and finance that governs provincial life, indifferent to the human suffering it administers.

His role as a creditor after the bankruptcy — "there would remain the friendly bill of Mr Gore" [030-006] — adds a quiet irony to his professional function. The attorney who represents Tulliver is also one of the creditors whose claims make the shortfall "not more than ten or twelve shillings in the pound" [030-006], a detail that captures the entanglement of professional duty and personal interest in the provincial economy.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]** — Mr. Gore's client, whom he represents in the aftermath of the lost lawsuit. The trauma of Mr. Gore's letter is so profound that, during Tulliver's subsequent illness, "the impression on his mind that it was but yesterday when he received the letter from Mr Gore was so continually implied in his talk" that even [[mr-turnbull|Mr. Turnbull]] despaired of preparing him for reality [031-001].
- **[[lawyer-wakem|Lawyer Wakem]]** — The antagonist whose acquisition of the Tulliver mortgage Mr. Gore discovers and reports. The two attorneys occupy opposite sides of the legal machinery that grinds the Tullivers down.
- **[[mr-pivart|Mr. Pivart]]** — The victor in the lawsuit, whose triumph sets in motion the chain of events that brings Tulliver to Mr. Gore's office.

## Key Scenes

- **The Consultation at Lindum** ([024-001]): Tulliver returns flushed from his talk with Mr. Gore, his attorney's advice having stirred hope for a scheme to retain the mill — a hope that will shortly be shattered.
- **The Fateful Letter** ([024-007]): Before Tulliver can meet with Mr. Gore in person, a clerk intercepts him on the road with a letter containing the news that Furley has sold the mortgage to Wakem. This short, factual communication functions as the novel's pivot point from hope to ruin.
- **The Financial Settlement** ([030-006]): Mr. Gore's own bill as a creditor contributes to the shortfall that makes bankruptcy inevitable, a detail that quietly implicates even the most neutral-seeming professional relationships in the Tullivers' downfall.
