# Death of Mr. Tulliver

Mr. Tulliver's death in Book 3, Chapter 9 is one of the novel's most violent and emotionally complex scenes. After months of decline following his bankruptcy, he encounters Lawyer Wakem on the road, attacks him with his horsewhip, and dies shortly afterward—passing his vendetta to Tom with his final breath.

## Background

> In half an hour after this Mr Tulliver's own wagoner found him lying by the roadside insensible, with an open letter near him, and his gray horse snuffing uneasily about him. [024-008]

Since losing the lawsuit, Mr. Tulliver has been a broken man: stripped of his mill, dependent on his son, consumed by hatred of Wakem. His physical and mental decline has been traced through Book 3 with unsparing clarity.

## The Event

The death scene is shocking in its violence. Mr. Tulliver, seeing Wakem on the road, attacks him with a horsewhip in a blind fury. The act is both a release of long-suppressed rage and a final, desperate assertion of agency. After the attack, Mr. Tulliver is carried home, where he dies—but not before making Tom swear to continue the vendetta.

## Consequences

Mr. Tulliver's dying demand that Tom pursue revenge against Wakem binds the son to the father's hatred. Tom internalizes this obligation completely, and it shapes his treatment of Philip Wakem and ultimately his judgment of Maggie. The death scene thus reaches forward through the entire novel, its consequences only fully understood in the final tragedy.

## Key Passages

- **The attack on Wakem** (Book 3, Chapter 9): Mr. Tulliver's final act of violence.
- **The deathbed vow**: Tom swears to carry on his father's revenge.
- **The passing of obligation**: One generation's hatred becomes the next generation's duty.
