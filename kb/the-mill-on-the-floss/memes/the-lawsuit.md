# The Lawsuit

Mr. Tulliver's lawsuit against Lawyer Wakem over water rights on the River Floss is the catalytic event of the novel. Driven by pride rather than prudence, the lawsuit results in Mr. Tulliver's defeat, the family's bankruptcy, and a vow of revenge that shapes Tom's life.

## Background

Mr. Tulliver has long resented Lawyer Wakem's influence in the district. The water-rights dispute provides an occasion for their antagonism to become open conflict. Despite the warnings of his wife and the Dodsons—who recognize the imprudence of suing a lawyer—Mr. Tulliver's pride will not permit him to back down. He insists that "niver run i' my family" to lose in court (003-019), a claim that turns out to be tragically mistaken.

## The Event

The lawsuit's outcome is devastating. Mr. Tulliver loses the case and is ordered to pay costs, forcing the sale of Dorlcote Mill to Wakem himself. The loss is not merely financial but existential: Mr. Tulliver loses his livelihood, his home, and his sense of self. His subsequent decline and death can be traced directly to this defeat.

## Consequences

The lawsuit's consequences ripple through the entire novel. Tom assumes the burden of repaying the family's debts and regaining the mill. The feud between the Tullivers and Wakems makes Maggie's relationship with Philip Wakem forbidden. Mr. Tulliver's vow of revenge—inscribed in the family Bible—binds Tom to a narrowing sense of duty that ultimately contributes to the sibling tragedy.

## Key Passages

- **Mr. Tulliver's pride** (003-019): His insistence that bankruptcy has never run in his family.
- **The Dodsons' warnings** (008-033): "It must go out o' your own family"—the property creed that the lawsuit violates.
- **The aftermath** (Book 3): The family's financial ruin and Mr. Tulliver's decline.
