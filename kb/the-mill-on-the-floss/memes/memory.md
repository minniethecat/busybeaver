# Memory

**Memory** in *The Mill on the Floss* functions as both theme and narrative method. The novel is saturated with the past: characters are defined by what they remember, haunted by what they cannot forget, and driven by vows made in childhood that memory will not release.

## Definition

Eliot treats memory not as passive recollection but as an active force that shapes present experience. The narrator's famous opening meditation—looking back at the remembered landscape of the Floss—establishes memory as the novel's governing consciousness. For characters, memory is often burdensome: Mr. Tulliver's memory of his defeat by Wakem becomes an obsession he passes to Tom, while Maggie's memories of childhood happiness with Tom make his adult rejections more painful.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's structure itself enacts the work of memory. The narrator begins in the present, looking back, and the entire narrative can be read as an act of remembrance. Mr. Tulliver's demand that Tom inscribe his vow of revenge in the family Bible literalizes the connection between memory and moral obligation: to remember is to remain bound.

For Maggie, memory is both solace and torment. Her childhood at the mill—the freedom of the river, the intensity of her bond with Tom—provides memories that sustain her through adult hardship, but also sets a standard of happiness that adult life cannot meet. The novel's ending, in which Maggie and Tom die embraced in the flood, suggests that memory's deepest claim is the return to origins.

- **The narrator's opening memory** (002-001): The novel begins with an act of memory, the narrator looking back at the Floss landscape.
- **Maggie's childhood in memory** (005-006): The intensity of childhood experience, preserved in memory.
