# The Flood

The flood of the River Floss is the climactic event of *The Mill on the Floss*. Rising waters inundate Dorlcote Mill, and Maggie — rowing alone through the flood in a small boat — rescues Tom. They are swept away together, dying embraced, in an ending that is both tragic and redemptive (065-056).

## Summary

In the second week of September, Maggie is alone in her lodging by the river, struggling with Stephen's letter urging her to come to him (065-001). Past midnight, as rain beats against the window, she feels water at her feet — the Floss has risen and is flooding the house (065-019). Without panic, she wakes Bob Jakin, and together they find two boats still moored outside (065-023). Maggie plunges through the rising water to reach them (065-024).

A sudden current sweeps both boats apart, and Maggie finds herself alone on the wide water (065-029). Disoriented in the darkness, she realizes she must reach the mill to save Tom and her mother (065-032). Spotting St. Ogg's in the distance, she navigates toward Dorlcote Mill (065-035), struggling against debris and the river's force (065-038).

She reaches the mill and calls out. Tom, alone in the attic, is astonished to find Maggie has come for him by boat, alone (065-046). "God has taken care of me, to bring me to you," she tells him (065-047). Tom takes the oars, but as they row toward safety, huge floating fragments of wreckage bear down upon them (065-054). "It is coming, Maggie!" Tom cries, loosing the oars and clasping her (065-056). The boat is overturned; brother and sister go down together "in an embrace never to be parted" (065-059).

## Context

Throughout the novel, the River Floss has been a double presence: beautiful and dangerous, nurturing and threatening. The mill depends on its current for power; Maggie's childhood freedom unfolds along its banks (005-006). Yet the river's destructive potential has always been latent — the flood that was "almost a disaster" in Mr. Tulliver's memory, the ever-present risk to the low-lying mill.

The immediate context is Maggie's social destruction. Shunned by St. Ogg's after her return (063-001), separated from Stephen whom she still loves, she sits alone reading his letter when the waters rise (065-010). The flood arrives at her moment of deepest isolation, transforming her from passive sufferer to active rescuer. Her solitary boat journey is the novel's most dramatic assertion of her moral agency: the woman whom society has condemned is the one who rows through darkness to save her brother.

## Key Participants

- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]**: Alone in her lodging when the flood begins, she rows through the darkness to rescue Tom — her final act of love and courage.
- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]**: Trapped in the attic of the flooded mill. His recognition of what Maggie has done — coming for him, alone, through the flood — overwhelms him with understanding (065-049).
- **[[bob-jakin|Bob Jakin]]**: Woken by Maggie, he helps launch the boats but is swept away by a separate current. His rescue attempt arrives too late.

## Aftermath and Consequences

Brother and sister drown together, and the epilogue records their burial: "Near that brick grave there was a tomb erected, very soon after the flood, for two bodies that were found in close embrace" (066-005). The tomb bears the inscription: "In their death they were not divided" (066-009).

The flood resolves the novel's central tension — the conflict between individual desire and family loyalty — through an act of nature that is neither reward nor punishment. Eliot refuses to moralize: "Nature repairs her ravages — repairs them with her sunshine, and with human labour" (066-001). Dorlcote Mill is rebuilt, but the human cost is permanent. The ending has provoked critical debate since publication: is it tragic inevitability, transcendent reunion, or an evasion of the social problems the novel has raised?

Two men visit the grave at different moments: one (Philip) "was always solitary," finding companionship among the trees of the Red Deeps (066-007); the other (Stephen) visited "again with a sweet face beside him — but that was years after" (066-006), suggesting he eventually married Lucy.

## Literary Significance

The flood ending is among the most debated in Victorian fiction. Henry James called it a "lamentable ending" that evaded the novel's social questions. Later critics, particularly feminist scholars, have read the flood as the logical culmination of the novel's symbolic logic: Maggie, identified with the river throughout, is reclaimed by it. The ending refuses the conventional marriage plot — Maggie cannot marry either Philip or Stephen without violating her moral integrity — and instead offers a resolution through nature, death, and the restoration of the sibling bond. The biblical echo of David's lament for Saul and Jonathan ("in death they were not divided") elevates the sibling relationship to tragic dignity, asserting that the deepest love in the novel is between brother and sister, not romantic partners.
