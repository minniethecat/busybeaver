# The Miss Guests

The Miss Guests — Miss Guest and Miss Laura — are [[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]]'s fashionable sisters, who move in the highest circles of St Ogg's society. As leaders of fashion and taste, they serve as a measure of the town's social standards and prejudices, their treatment of [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]] illustrating the mechanisms — gossip, exclusion, condescension — through which a provincial community enforces its norms and punishes transgression.

## Role in the Novel

As a collective character, the Miss Guests embody the snobbery and moral certitude of provincial high society. They are described as leaders of fashion:

> "The Miss Guests, who associated chiefly on terms of condescension with the families of St Ogg's, and were the glass of fashion there" [051-001]

Their social standing gives them the authority to host the town's most important gatherings. One notable event is "one of the Miss Guests' thoroughly condescending parties, including no member of any aristocracy higher than that of St Ogg's" [055-001], where [[lucy-deane|Lucy Deane]] is presented as "the acknowledged queen of the occasion" [055-001].

After [[the-near-elopement|Maggie and Stephen's near-elopement]], the Miss Guests become active agents in the social response. They take [[lucy-deane|Lucy]] to the coast for her recovery and scheme to reunite Stephen with Lucy, adding "the momentum" of their genuine "pity and indignation on behalf of the gentle forsaken girl" to their desire that Stephen return [064-007]. They also convey gossip about Maggie and [[dr-kenn|Dr. Kenn]] to Stephen, helping to precipitate the novel's tragic conclusion.

## Character Analysis

The Miss Guests are defined by the tension between their social polish and their moral narrowness. They are "much too well-bred to have any of the grimaces and affected tones that belong to pretentious vulgarity" [054-001] — yet at the charity bazaar they are juxtaposed unfavourably with Maggie:

> "Their stall being next to the one where Maggie sat, it seemed newly obvious to-day that Miss Guest held her chin too high, and that Miss Laura spoke and moved continually with a view to effect." [054-001]

Their attitude toward Maggie is a blend of condescension and suspicion. They take "some exception to Maggie's manners" [051-001], finding her unwillingness to assent to fashionable opinions a mark of *gaucherie*. They are uncomfortably conscious of her connection to the less socially elevated Dodson relations:

> "It was only a wonder that there was no tinge of vulgarity about her, considering what the rest of poor Lucy's relations were — an allusion which always made the Miss Guests shudder a little." [051-001]

After the near-elopement, their judgment hardens. They are "not among those who disbelieved their brother's letter" [064-007] but remain suspicious: "They had always thought her disagreeable; they now thought her artful and proud" [064-007].

## Relationships

- **[[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]]** — Their brother, whose near-elopement with Maggie provokes their protective indignation and scheming to reunite him with Lucy.
- **[[lucy-deane|Lucy Deane]]** — Their social protégée and Stephen's intended, whom they champion after the near-elopement, taking her to the coast for recovery.
- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]** — The object of their condescension and, after the near-elopement, their active hostility. Their gossip about her and Dr. Kenn helps precipitate the novel's tragic conclusion.
- **[[mr-guest|Mr. Guest]]** — Their father, head of the Guest family and its commercial interests.

## Key Scenes

- **The Condescending Party** ([055-001]): The Miss Guests host a party that includes "no member of any aristocracy higher than that of St Ogg's," where Lucy Deane is the acknowledged queen and Maggie's social position is subtly diminished.
- **The Charity Bazaar** ([054-001]): At the bazaar, the Miss Guests' studied elegance is juxtaposed unfavourably with Maggie's natural dignity — Miss Guest holds her chin too high, Miss Laura moves for effect, and Maggie's quiet presence exposes what is performative in their refinement.
- **After the Elopement** ([064-007]): The Miss Guests take Lucy to the coast, scheme to reunite Stephen with her, and convey gossip about Maggie and Dr. Kenn to Stephen — their "pity and indignation" becoming active forces in the novel's tragic resolution.
