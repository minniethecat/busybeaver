# Luke

Luke is the head miller at [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]], a figure of quiet loyalty and unassuming competence. His presence throughout the novel's early books provides a stable counterpoint to the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] family's turbulence, and his solicitude for the ruined [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] is one of the novel's most touching examples of cross-class sympathy.

## Role in the Novel

Luke appears primarily in the novel's first half, where he serves as a witness to and commentator on the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] family's affairs. His genuine grief at [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s [[justice|bankruptcy]]—"what wi' the rust on the wheat" (032-007)—reveals an emotional investment in the mill that transcends mere employment.

## Character Analysis

Luke represents Eliot's ideal of working-class virtue: competent, loyal, and emotionally present without being intrusive. His "soothing sympathy" for [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] after the [[justice|bankruptcy]] (032-007) is offered without condescension or self-interest. Unlike the Dodson code of proper behavior, Luke's goodness is instinctive and warm. He is one of the novel's small but significant demonstrations that moral worth is not correlated with social rank.
