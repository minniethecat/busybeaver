# Duty

**Duty** in *The Mill on the Floss* operates as a powerful moral imperative that shapes character decisions and drives the novel's tragic arc. For the Dodson family, duty is synonymous with social propriety and family obligation; for Maggie Tulliver, it becomes an internalized form of renunciation that conflicts with her passionate nature.

## Definition

In the world of the novel, duty is understood primarily as obligation to family and social expectation rather than as abstract ethical principle. Mr. Stelling invokes duty as a pedagogical tool, expecting his pupils to feel its weight without questioning its content (016-003). The Dodson code treats duty as adherence to traditional norms—what one owes to family name, property, and reputation. For Maggie, however, duty becomes a deeply personal struggle: a call to deny her own desires in favor of others' claims upon her (038-021).

## Context in the Novel

Duty drives the plot at critical junctures. Tom Tulliver's assumption of financial responsibility after his father's bankruptcy is framed as filial duty—a son's obligation to restore the family's fortunes (042-065). His rigid understanding of duty leaves no room for Maggie's more complex moral vision, creating the central sibling conflict.

Maggie's relationship with duty is the novel's most psychologically rich thread. She internalizes Thomas à Kempis's doctrine of renunciation, attempting to "think away all my duty" by suppressing desire entirely (038-021). But Eliot's narrator suggests this is a form of self-deception: duty untempered by sympathy becomes cruelty, as when Tom tells Maggie her "duty was clear enough" in rejecting Philip Wakem (042-059).

## Key Passages

- **Mr. Stelling on duty** (016-003): The clergyman-schoolmaster treats duty as an external standard to be imposed, not questioned—illustrating the era's moral education.
- **Duty as renunciation** (038-021): Maggie confesses she tried to "think away all my duty," revealing the psychological cost of suppressing natural feeling.
- **Tom's absolute judgment** (042-059): Tom declares Maggie's "duty was clear enough," demonstrating how duty becomes an instrument of moral absolutism.
- **Filial duty** (042-065): Tom resolves to "do the duty of a son and a brother," embodying the Victorian ideal of masculine familial obligation.
