# Dorlcote Mill

Dorlcote Mill is the Tulliver family home and the spatial center of *The Mill on the Floss*. Powered by the River Floss, it represents both livelihood and identity: to be a Tulliver is to belong to the mill, and losing the mill is losing the self.

## Description

The mill stands on the River Floss, its wheel turning with the current that both sustains and threatens it. Eliot's opening description establishes the mill within a landscape of "wide plain" and "broadening Floss" (002-001). The mill is both workplace and home, its rhythms shaping the Tulliver family's daily life. Maggie "loved to linger in the great spaces of the mill" (005-006), finding in its physical vastness a counterpart to her own expansive nature.

## Significance

The mill is the novel's governing symbol of rootedness and its loss. Mr. Tulliver's identity is inseparable from it; his bankruptcy and the sale of the mill to Lawyer Wakem constitute a kind of death before death. Tom's determination to regain the mill drives the economic plot, and his eventual success represents a restoration of family honor. Yet the mill's return does not bring happiness—Eliot refuses the easy equation of property with fulfillment.

## Key Events at This Location

- **The novel's opening** (002-001): The narrator's gaze settles on Dorlcote Mill, establishing it as the narrative's point of origin.
- **Maggie's childhood freedom** (005-006): The mill's great spaces as the arena of Maggie's early life.
- **The family's financial ruin**: Bankruptcy transforms the mill from home to lost inheritance.
- **The flood** (Book 7, Chapter 5): The Floss rises and reclaims the mill, returning Maggie to Tom in death.
