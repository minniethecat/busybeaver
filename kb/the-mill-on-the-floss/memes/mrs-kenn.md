# Mrs. Kenn

Mrs. Kenn is the wife of [[dr-kenn|Dr. Kenn]], the clergyman of St Ogg's. Though deceased before the novel's present action, her death has significant consequences for both her husband and, ultimately, for [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]. As an absent presence — a wife whose death creates the opening through which Maggie finds employment and the scandal that drives her from it — Mrs. Kenn illustrates how the dead continue to shape the lives of the living in the closed world of provincial society.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Kenn appears only indirectly — through the charitable work she can no longer perform, through the children she leaves behind, and through the gossip her death provokes. Her function in the narrative is to enable the plot mechanism by which [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] finds respectable employment as governess to the Kenn children, while simultaneously providing fuel for the speculation about Dr. Kenn's attentions to Maggie that ultimately helps drive her from St Ogg's.

During her lifetime, Mrs. Kenn is part of the charitable life of the town. She is responsible for organising contributions to the charity bazaar, a task she delegates when her health fails: her "delicate state of health, threatening to become confirmed illness through an attack of bronchitis" obliges her "to resign her functions at the coming bazaar into the hands of other ladies, of whom she wished [[lucy-deane|Lucy]] to be one" [051-006]. [[lucy-deane|Lucy Deane]] takes charge of "certain large plain articles" that Mrs. Kenn had undertaken to provide [054-002].

After her death between Book 6 and Book 7, the town gossips seize upon Dr. Kenn's attention to Maggie. The speculation about "the dreadful possibility of her some time or other becoming the Rector's wife" [064-006] is made scandalous precisely by the recency of Mrs. Kenn's death:

> "It was enough to make poor Mrs Kenn turn in her grave, that they should be put under the care of this girl only a few weeks after her death." [064-006]

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Kenn is a figure defined entirely by her absence — she is a wife, a mother, and a charitable organiser, and the novel's interest in her is entirely in the consequences of her no longer being any of these things. This narrative strategy is characteristic of Eliot's approach to minor characters: the dead shape the world of the living as powerfully as the present do, and a death opens a space that the community rushes to fill with judgment.

Her role in the charity bazaar episode is telling: even in illness, she exercises social influence, choosing Lucy as her delegate and thus advancing the social integration of the Deane family into St Ogg's highest circles. Her choice has consequences beyond the bazaar itself, shaping the social landscape that Maggie must later navigate.

## Relationships

- **[[dr-kenn|Dr. Kenn]]** — Her husband, the clergyman of St Ogg's, whose status as a widower makes his attention to Maggie the subject of scandalous speculation.
- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]** — Who becomes governess to the Kenn children after Mrs. Kenn's death, a position that is both a lifeline and, through the gossip it provokes, a source of further suffering.
- **[[lucy-deane|Lucy Deane]]** — Whom Mrs. Kenn chooses as her delegate for the charity bazaar, a mark of social favour that advances Lucy's standing in St Ogg's society.

## Key Scenes

- **The Charity Bazaar Delegation** ([051-006]): Mrs. Kenn, in failing health, delegates her bazaar responsibilities to other ladies, wishing Lucy to be among them — a small act of social patronage that shapes the novel's subsequent social dynamics.
- **The Posthumous Gossip** ([064-006]): After Mrs. Kenn's death, the town speculates about the "dreadful possibility" of Maggie becoming the Rector's wife — the dead woman's memory enlisted in the campaign of social judgment against Maggie.
