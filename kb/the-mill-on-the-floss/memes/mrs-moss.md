# Mrs. Moss

Mrs. Moss, born Gritty Tulliver, is [[Mr. Tulliver]]'s only sister. She married [[Mr. Moss]], a struggling farmer, and lives a life of quiet hardship at [[basset|Basset]] with her eight [[childhood|children]], bearing her poverty with patience and [[pride|dignity]].

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Moss appears in one extended scene in Book 1 Chapter 9, when [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] visits [[basset|Basset]] to ask her husband to repay a loan of three hundred pounds. The visit reveals the contrast between the two siblings' fortunes: while [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] owns [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]] and considers himself prosperous, his sister struggles to feed and clothe her large family. Mrs. Moss's function in the novel is to humanize [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] — her poverty and her patient [[pride|dignity]] soften his anger about the unpaid [[property|debt]] — and to introduce the theme of family [[duty|obligation]] that runs through the novel [009-009] [009-013] [009-046].

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Moss embodies a strain of [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] generosity that runs counter to Dodson prudence. "A large, handsome, blonde woman" in her [[childhood|youth]], her features are echoed years later in [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] [008-037]. Years of childbearing and farm labor have worn her down: she is described with a "worn, tired look" and speaks with a humility bordering on self-abasement [009-016] [009-013].

Yet beneath her weariness there is spirit. When her brother suggests that eight [[childhood|children]] would be "a poor bargain," she defends her family with a flash of indignation, arguing that her [[childhood|children]]'s love is worth more than gold [009-023] [009-025]. This moment of resistance — brief but genuine — places Mrs. Moss among the novel's morally sympathetic figures. Her tears at her brother's forgiveness of the [[property|debt]] are tears of relief and gratitude, not manipulation [009-049] [009-051].

## Relationships

- **[[Mr. Tulliver]]** — Her brother. Despite the financial strain between them, their bond is genuine. [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s visit to [[basset|Basset]] reveals an [[love|affection]] that survives across the economic gulf separating them [009-013] [009-051].
- **[[Mr. Moss]]** — Her husband, a poor farmer whose financial difficulties are a constant worry [009-034] [009-036].
- **[[Maggie Tulliver]]** — Her niece; [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] is said to resemble her aunt in appearance, being "the picture of her aunt [[mr-moss|Moss]]" [008-037].
- **[[Tom Tulliver]]** — Her nephew; [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] and [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] are both said to love visiting their aunt and uncle [[mr-moss|Moss]] [007-007].

## Key Scenes

- **The Visit to [[basset|Basset]]** — [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] rides to [[basset|Basset]] to demand repayment of a three-hundred-pound loan from [[mr-moss|Mr. Moss]]. Mrs. Moss greets him with anxious hospitality, and her evident poverty and patient endurance move [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] to forgive the [[property|debt]] — an act of generosity that has serious financial consequences [009-006] through [009-051].
