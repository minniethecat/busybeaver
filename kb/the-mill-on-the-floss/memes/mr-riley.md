# Mr. Riley

Mr. Riley is the local auctioneer whose advice to [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] about [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s education sets one of the novel's key plot lines in motion. Though he appears only briefly, his recommendation of [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]] as a tutor has lasting consequences for [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s development.

## Role in the Novel

Riley appears in the early chapters as a man of superficial intelligence whose opinions [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] trusts because they are delivered with confidence. His advice that [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] should be sent to a clergyman-educator rather than to a practical school reflects the class aspirations and educational assumptions that Eliot's novel critiques.

## Character Analysis

Riley is a man of modest talents and immodest self-regard. He presents himself as [[education|knowledge]]able about education despite having no particular expertise. His recommendation of [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]] is based on social connection rather than educational judgment. Eliot's treatment of Riley is gently satirical: he is not malicious, merely mediocre, but his mediocrity has consequences for others that he will never know or care about.

## Key Scenes

- **Riley's advice** (Book 1, Chapter 3): Mr. Riley recommends [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]], setting [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s educational path.
- **The limits of Riley's [[education|knowledge]]**: His confident ignorance as a critique of provincial expertise.
