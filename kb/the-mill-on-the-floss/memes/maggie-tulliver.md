# Maggie Tulliver

Maggie Tulliver is the central consciousness of *The Mill on the Floss* and one of Victorian fiction's most psychologically complex heroines. From childhood to young womanhood [003-027], she struggles to reconcile her intense emotional nature and fierce intelligence with a world that has no place for such qualities in a woman.

## Role in the Novel

Maggie is the novel's moral and emotional center. The narrative follows her from age nine to approximately nineteen, tracing her development from impulsive, loving child to self-denying young woman to tragic heroine. Her defining trait is the intensity of her feeling—she "loved to linger in the great space" of the mill [005-006] and poured affection onto her brother [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] with desperate hunger for reciprocation. Eliot uses Maggie to explore the central tension of the novel: the conflict between individual desire and social duty, between the claims of the heart and the demands of convention.

## Character Analysis

Maggie's character is defined by excess: she feels too much, thinks too much, wants too much. In a culture that values female self-effacement, her very vitality becomes a liability. Eliot describes her as "a creature full of eager, passionate longings for all that was beautiful and glad" [003-027], yet this abundance of spirit finds no acceptable outlet in provincial St. Ogg's.

Her discovery of Thomas à Kempis's *The Imitation of Christ* offers a path toward self-mastery through renunciation—she attempts to "think away all my duty towards God and man" by suppressing desire [038-021]. Eliot presents this as an incomplete solution: Maggie's renunciation is less spiritual transcendence than self-punishment, a hunger strike of the soul.

### Maggie and Sympathy

Maggie embodies the novel's central moral value: [[sympathy]]. Her capacity to imagine others' feelings—even those who have wronged her—distinguishes her from every other character. When she returns from her near-elopement with [[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]], she endures public condemnation rather than justify herself at [[lucy-deane|Lucy]]'s expense. This sympathetic imagination is Eliot's answer to the narrow moral codes that dominate St. Ogg's: true morality is not rule-following but the capacity to feel with others.

### The Divided Self

Eliot presents Maggie as inwardly divided—between the "dark-eyed" passionate child and the self-denying ascetic, between her love for [[philip-wakem|Philip]] and her attraction to [[stephen-guest|Stephen]], between duty and [[love|desire]]. This division is not mere character flaw but a condition of modern consciousness. Maggie's tragedy is that her finest qualities—her capacity for [[sympathy]], her moral seriousness, her refusal to take the easy path—lead directly to her suffering.

## Relationships

- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]**: Maggie's beloved older brother, whose conditional affection shapes her emotional life. She loves him with an intensity he cannot return, creating the novel's central relational dynamic. Their childhood bond [005-016] and final embrace in the flood represent the novel's deepest emotional arc.
- **[[philip-wakem|Philip Wakem]]**: The hunchbacked son of her father's enemy, Philip offers Maggie the intellectual companionship and sympathetic understanding that Tom cannot provide. Their meetings at the Red Deeps [Book 5] are the novel's most sustained exploration of intellectual and emotional intimacy.
- **[[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]]**: Lucy Deane's suitor, whose passionate attraction to Maggie precipitates the novel's climactic moral crisis in Book 6. The boat scene [Book 6, Chapter 14] dramatizes the conflict between passion and duty that defines Maggie's adult life.
- **[[lucy-deane|Lucy Deane]]**: Maggie's cousin and foil—the conventionally feminine, charming woman Maggie can never be. Their relationship embodies the novel's critique of Victorian femininity.
- **[[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]**: Maggie's father, whose financial ruin and vow of revenge against [[lawyer-wakem|Wakem]] shape her circumstances. Maggie inherits his passionate nature but not his bitterness.

## Key Scenes

- **Childhood by the Floss** [005-006]: Maggie lingers in the mill's great spaces, establishing her love of freedom and sensory intensity. "She loved to linger in the great space of the mill."
- **Tom withdraws love** [006-038]: Tom tells Maggie he doesn't love her—a childhood wound that prefigures adult rejections. Maggie's desperate need for love is established here.
- **The attic and the fetish** [004-011]: Maggie retreats to the attic with a wooden doll she punishes—an early image of her turn toward self-punishment and asceticism.
- **Reading à Kempis** [036-037]: Maggie discovers the doctrine of renunciation that will shape her young adulthood. She attempts to discipline her passionate nature through religious self-denial.
- **Meeting Philip at the Red Deeps** [Book 5]: Maggie's secret meetings with Philip offer intellectual companionship and awaken her to the possibility of a life beyond duty.
- **The boat with Stephen** [Book 6, Chapter 14]: Maggie's near-elopement—the novel's central moral crisis. She drifts past the point of return and must choose between passion and duty.
- **The return to St. Ogg's** [Book 7]: Maggie returns unmarried, endures social ostracism, and refuses Stephen's offer of marriage—choosing integrity over happiness.
- **The flood** [Book 7, Chapter 5]: Maggie rows alone through the flood to rescue Tom. They die embraced, the sibling bond restored in death: "In the deathly embrace, they were not divided."

## Critical Perspectives

Maggie Tulliver has been read as a proto-feminist heroine, a study in repressed female desire, and an autobiographical projection of Eliot herself. Contemporary critics note that Maggie's fate—death by drowning, literally overwhelmed by forces beyond her control—mirrors the fate of the intelligent Victorian woman: society offers no viable path for her gifts. Her embrace of renunciation anticipates later feminist critiques of self-sacrifice as a form of internalized oppression. Yet Eliot's treatment is more complex than simple critique: Maggie's moral seriousness is genuine, and her refusal to harm others even at the cost of her own happiness commands respect.
