# The Auction

The auction of the Tullivers' household goods is the most devastating public consequence of Mr. Tulliver's bankruptcy. Mrs. Tulliver's "teraphim, or household gods"—her linen, her china, her carefully accumulated possessions—are sold to the highest bidder in a scene that transforms private grief into public spectacle.

## Background

> "I wish it could be managed so as my teapot and chany and the best castors needn't be put up for sale," said poor Mrs Tulliver, beseechingly, "and the sugar-tongs the first things ever I bought." [026-021]

Following his defeat in the lawsuit, Mr. Tulliver is declared bankrupt. His assets, including the family's household goods, must be sold to satisfy creditors. For Mrs. Tulliver, the auction is not merely a financial catastrophe but an existential one: her identity is invested in her possessions, and losing them is losing herself.

## The Event

The auction is described with painful specificity. Neighbors and strangers examine the Tullivers' possessions, appraising what was once private. Mrs. Tulliver's grief at seeing her linen and china sold is treated by Eliot with unexpected tenderness—this is, for her, the real tragedy, more immediate than the loss of the mill. Mrs. Glegg's practical intervention (she buys some items to keep them in the family) provides the only relief.

## Consequences

The auction completes the Tullivers' fall from respectability. The family is now publicly poor, stripped not only of property but of the appearance of property that Dodson culture equates with moral worth. The experience hardens Tom's determination to repay the debts and reclaim the family's honor.

## Key Passages

- **Mrs. Tulliver's teraphim** (Book 3, Chapter 2): Her lament for her household goods.
- **The auction scene**: The public sale of private life.
- **Mrs. Glegg's intervention**: An unexpected act of family loyalty.
