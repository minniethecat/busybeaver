# Woman

The position of **woman** in the world of *The Mill on the Floss* is one of profound constraint. Maggie Tulliver's intelligence, passion, and moral seriousness are consistently thwarted by a society that offers women no outlet for such qualities beyond domestic affection and self-sacrifice.

## Definition

Eliot presents womanhood not as a fixed essence but as a social construction enforced through education, economic dependence, and moral expectation. The Dodson women police each other's conformity to feminine norms, while the narrator's interventions repeatedly highlight the waste of female potential in a world that values women only as daughters, wives, and mothers.

## Context in the Novel

Maggie's entire life is shaped by the gap between what she is capable of and what she is permitted to do. Denied the classical education given to Tom, she educates herself haphazardly. Denied economic independence, she depends first on her father, then on Tom. Denied the freedom to act on her desires, she turns to renunciation. Each pathway toward fulfillment is blocked by the simple fact of her sex.

The novel's other female characters illustrate the range of responses available to women: Mrs. Tulliver's helpless domesticity, Mrs. Glegg's matriarchal authority exercised through family networks, Lucy Deane's conventional charm, and Maggie's doomed attempt to forge a different path. The tragedy of Maggie is not merely personal but representative—the tragedy of a woman whose nature exceeds her culture's capacity to accommodate it.

## Key Passages

- **Maggie's love of space and freedom** (005-006): Maggie "loved to linger in the great space" of the mill, a desire for expansiveness that her gendered life cannot satisfy.
- **Tom's conditional love as gendered power** (006-038): Tom withdraws affection as discipline—a pattern that prefigures the gendered dynamics of adult relationships.
- **Maggie's intellectual hunger** (005-016): "Tom's not fond of reading. I love Tom so dearly"—Maggie's reading contrasts with Tom's indifference to ideas.
- **The renunciation of desire** (038-021): Maggie's turn to asceticism as the only culturally available path for female self-mastery.
