# Mrs. Tulliver

Bessy Tulliver, née Dodson, is [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] and [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s mother—a woman whose identity is entirely defined by her household goods and her family connections. Her helplessness in the face of financial disaster is both comic and genuinely touching, and her inability to understand [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s [[love|passion]]ate nature contributes to her daughter's isolation.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Tulliver embodies a form of [[woman|femininity]] that the novel both satirizes and pities. Her "teraphim, or household gods"—her linen, her china, her carefully preserved [[property|possessions]] (Book 3, Chapter 2)—represent the only domain in which she exercises authority. When [[justice|bankruptcy]] strips her of these things, she is stripped of herself.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Tulliver is not stupid but profoundly limited. Her Dodson upbringing has given her a code of conduct centered on domestic [[custom|propriety]], and she cannot comprehend any values beyond it. Her grief at losing her [[property|possessions]] is genuine, and Eliot refuses to mock it, even as she shows its inadequacy as a response to genuine tragedy. Mrs. Tulliver's relationship with [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] is particularly painful: she loves her daughter but cannot understand her, and her constant criticism of [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s un[[custom|convention]]ality reinforces the message that [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] is somehow wrong.

## Relationships

- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]**: Mrs. Tulliver's daughter, whose intensity bewilders and frustrates her.
- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]**: Her son, whom she adores and depends upon after [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s decline.
- **[[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]**: Her husband, whose impracticality she has spent her marriage managing.
- **The Dodson sisters**: [[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]], [[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]], and [[mrs-deane|Mrs. Deane]], whose judgments shape Mrs. Tulliver's sense of self.

## Key Scenes

- **The family council** (007-001): Mrs. Tulliver's helplessness is on display as the Dodsons discuss the family's crisis.
- **Mrs. Tulliver's teraphim** (Book 3, Chapter 2): Her lament over her household goods reveals the depth of her identification with domestic objects.
- **The aftermath of [[justice|bankruptcy]]**: Mrs. Tulliver's grief for her lost [[property|possessions]], treated by Eliot with unexpected tenderness.
