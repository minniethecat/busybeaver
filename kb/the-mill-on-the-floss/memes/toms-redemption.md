# Tom's Redemption

Tom Tulliver's steady, grinding campaign to repay his father's debts and regain Dorlcote Mill is the novel's economic counter-plot—a story of discipline and determination that parallels Maggie's story of passion and renunciation. It is a genuine achievement and a moral limitation.

## Background

After Mr. Tulliver's bankruptcy and death, Tom is determined to "do the duty of a son and a brother" (042-065) by repaying every creditor in full and buying back the mill. He works for Guest and Company under his uncle Deane's supervision, saving obsessively and investing wisely with Bob Jakin's help.

## The Event

Tom's redemption is not a single event but a process—years of patient labor, deferred gratification, and single-minded focus. His eventual success is the fulfillment of the Victorian masculine ideal: the son who restores the family's fortunes through hard work and moral discipline. The scene in which he repays the creditors is a moment of genuine triumph.

## Consequences

Tom's success is real but narrow. The qualities that enable his economic triumph—rigidity, absolutism, emotional constriction—are the same qualities that make him incapable of understanding Maggie. His redemption of the family's finances coincides with his rejection of his sister. Eliot thus qualifies the celebration: Tom has done everything right, and it is not enough.

## Key Passages

- **Tom's vow** (042-065): His determination to "do the duty of a son and a brother."
- **The repayment**: Tom pays the creditors in full, restoring the family's honor.
- **The cost of triumph**: Tom's economic success and emotional failure.
