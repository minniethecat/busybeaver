# Sally

**Sally** is the maid at [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]], serving the [[tulliver-family|Tulliver family]]. She appears in the early chapters of Book 1, providing a domestic backdrop to the childhood adventures of [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] and [[tom-tulliver|Tom]].

## Role in the Household

Sally is an unnamed servant — known only by her first name — who performs household duties at [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]]. Her interactions with the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] [[childhood|children]] reveal the informal, familiar relationship between the family and their domestic staff in the early nineteenth-century rural household.

## Key Appearances

### The Jam Puff Incident

Sally plays a memorable role in [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s childhood misadventure. When [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] has been sent to her aunt [[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]]'s and is expected to behave, Sally inadvertently provides a dramatic entrance:

> "Sally's bringing the old mat and the haircloth seats to put in the best parlour" (010-013)

Later, Sally becomes the bearer of alarming news:

> "instead of the tea-tray, Sally introduced an object so startling that Mrs Tulliver gave a scream" (010-083) — the object being a disheveled Maggie, who has run away from her aunt's.

### Guarding the Premises

After [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s misbehavior, Sally receives elaborate instructions:

> "Sally, as you are aware, lost no time in presenting Lucy with the large sweet cake" (011-022)

When [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] cuts off her own hair in a fit of remorse, Sally is at the kitchen door, "making a wry face" at the mud tracked in (011-020), and is ordered to "keep her at the door, Sally! Don't bring her off the oil-cloth!" (011-023).

## Character

Sally is practical, somewhat long-suffering, and occupies the familiar role of a household servant who is both exasperated by and fond of the [[childhood|children]] she serves. She is neither idealized nor sentimentalized — she reacts to events with a servant's pragmatic concern for cleanliness and order:

> "they're such mud as that?" said Sally, making a wry face, as she stood at the kitchen door (011-020)

## Significance

Sally's presence in the novel serves several functions:

1. **Domestic authenticity**: She represents the everyday life of a rural mill household, with its practical realities of cleaning, cooking, and managing [[childhood|children]].

2. **Class presence**: As a servant, Sally belongs to a social class distinct from both the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]]s and the Dodsons. Her inclusion in the narrative ac[[education|knowledge]]s the full social ecosystem of the household.

3. **Comic relief**: Her exasperated reactions to the [[childhood|children]]'s chaos provide light humor in the early chapters.
