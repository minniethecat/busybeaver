# Sympathy

**Sympathy** is the moral heart of George Eliot's ethical vision in *The Mill on the Floss*. Against the rigid claims of duty, social convention, and self-interest, Eliot repeatedly affirms the capacity to enter into another's feelings as the truest source of moral action.

## Definition

Sympathy in Eliot's usage goes beyond mere pity. It is an active imaginative effort to understand another person's inner life, to "feel with" rather than merely "feel for." The narrator describes it as emerging from "growing insight and sympathy" (062-029), linking moral development to the enlargement of emotional understanding. Unlike the Dodson code—which judges actions by external conformity to family norms—sympathy requires an inward movement toward the other.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's tragic structure turns on failures of sympathy. Tom Tulliver, for all his dutifulness, cannot extend sympathy to Maggie's emotional needs; his "face showed little" when their "dispositions were not in sympathy" (039-003). Philip Wakem, by contrast, offers Maggie the sympathy Tom withholds: he "could give her sympathy; he could give her help" precisely because he understands suffering from within (038-066).

The narrator explicitly frames the novel's moral argument in terms of sympathy, warning against "maiming the faith and sympathy that were the best organs of" moral perception (058-003). Luke's "soothing sympathy" for the ruined Mr. Tulliver (032-007) and Bob Jakin's practical kindness demonstrate that sympathy is not confined to the educated or refined.

## Key Passages

- **Sympathy as moral knowledge** (062-029): The narrator defines the "man of maxims" as one who lacks "growing insight and sympathy," opposing rule-based morality to empathetic understanding.
- **Philip's gift of sympathy** (038-066): Philip "could give her sympathy; he could give her help"—sympathy as the foundation of genuine care.
- **The cost of sympathy's absence** (058-003): The narrator laments "maiming the faith and sympathy" that enable moral perception.
- **Tom's failure of sympathy** (039-003): Tom's face "showed little" when dispositions were not in sympathy, foreshadowing the sibling tragedy.
- **Luke's simple sympathy** (032-007): Luke offers "soothing sympathy" to the ruined Mr. Tulliver, showing sympathy across class lines.
