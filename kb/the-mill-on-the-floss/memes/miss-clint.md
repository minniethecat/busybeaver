# Miss Clint

Miss Clint is known in the novel only as the woman who became [[lawyer-wakem|Mrs. Wakem]]. Her significance lies entirely in the fact of her marriage — a fact that [[mrs-tulliver|Mrs. Tulliver]] seizes upon as evidence that the formidable Lawyer Wakem was once simply a young man who married a local woman, and might therefore be amenable to reason.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Clint appears only in Mrs. Tulliver's internal reasoning as she prepares to plead with Wakem to spare [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]]. Mrs. Tulliver reasons that Wakem cannot be entirely monstrous because:

> "He had married a Miss Clint, and at the time Mrs Tulliver had heard of that marriage, the summer when she wore her blue satin spencer, and had not yet any thoughts of Mr Tulliver, she knew no harm of Wakem." [030-008]

This memory — Wakem marrying Miss Clint in the summer of the blue satin spencer — functions as Mrs. Tulliver's psychological bridge from terror to negotiation. If Wakem married a Miss Clint, he belongs to the same provincial social world as the Dodsons; he can be appealed to through shared social memory [030-008].

Mrs. Tulliver reinforces this connection in her direct plea: "I remember your wedding as if it was yesterday; Mrs Wakem was a Miss Clint, I know *that*" [030-032]. By naming the bride, Mrs. Tulliver asserts familiarity — a claim that she and Wakem inhabit the same moral community.

## Character Analysis

Miss Clint is an absence that serves a function. We learn nothing of her person, her character, or her fate — only that she married Wakem and that her maiden name was Clint. Yet this thin biographical fact carries enormous weight in Mrs. Tulliver's imagination: it is the one piece of evidence that Wakem might be "a very reasonable man." The irony is that Mrs. Tulliver's faith in this logic proves misplaced — Wakem's marriage to Miss Clint does not, in the end, make him merciful.
