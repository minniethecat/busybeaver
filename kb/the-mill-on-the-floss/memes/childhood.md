# Childhood

**Childhood** in *The Mill on the Floss* is not merely a prelude to adult life but the novel's emotional and psychological foundation. The first two books, covering Maggie and Tom's childhood at Dorlcote Mill, establish patterns of love, conflict, and temperament that determine the course of the entire narrative.

## Definition

Eliot presents childhood as the period when character is formed through the interplay of innate temperament and early experience. Maggie's passionate, impulsive nature and Tom's rigid sense of fairness are already fully present in their childhood selves. The narrator's attention to childhood consciousness—its intensities, its injustices, its small tragedies—reflects Eliot's conviction that adult psychology is rooted in early emotional life.

## Context in the Novel

The childhood books (Books 1 and 2) are among the most celebrated in Victorian fiction. Maggie's desperate love for Tom, her sense of being misunderstood, her hunger for approval—all are rendered with psychological precision. Key childhood episodes—the dead rabbit incident, the jam puff, Maggie's flight to the gypsies, Tom's schooling—establish themes that resonate throughout the novel.

Tom's childhood is dominated by the expectation that he will be "made a scholarship of," while Maggie's is shaped by the constant message that she is too impulsive, too clever, too much. The asymmetry of their childhood experiences—Tom's confident entitlement, Maggie's anxious need for love—foreshadows their adult tragedy.

## Key Passages

- **Maggie's love of space** (005-006): Maggie "loved to linger in the great space" of the mill, a childhood freedom that adult life will close off.
- **The intensity of sibling love** (005-016): "I love Tom so dearly, Luke,—better than anybody"—Maggie's childhood declaration of the love that will define her life.
- **Tom's conditional affection** (006-038): Tom weaponizes love—"I don't love you, Maggie"—teaching Maggie that affection must be earned.
- **Forgiveness and love** (006-041): Maggie's promise of unconditional love—"I'd forgive you and love you"—contrasts with Tom's transactional affection.
