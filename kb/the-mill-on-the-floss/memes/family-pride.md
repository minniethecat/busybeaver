# Family Pride

**Family pride** is a central thematic force in *The Mill on the Floss*, shaping the motivations, conflicts, and ultimate fates of both the [[tulliver-family|Tulliver]] and [[dodson-family|Dodson]] families.

## In the Tulliver Family

For [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]], family pride becomes the engine of his young adulthood. After his father's bankruptcy, "Tom felt intensely that common cause with his father which springs from family pride, and was bent on being irreproachable as a son" [039-003]. This pride channels his "strong will" into the disciplined pursuit of commercial success — he "bound together his integrity, his pride, his family regrets, and his personal ambition, and made them one force" [039-002].

[[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] embodies family pride in an older, more destructive register. His determination to "go to law" against [[lawyer-wakem|Wakem]] over water rights is driven less by economic calculation than by the conviction that a Tulliver must not be bested. This pride, when wounded, curdles into the oath inscribed in the family Bible — a curse on Wakem that Maggie must later struggle against.

## In the Dodson Family

The [[dodson-family|Dodson family]] code is itself a form of family pride — an elaborate system of propriety, thrift, and mutual judgment that defines the Dodson sisters' identity. Mrs. Glegg's pride in her Dodson lineage, Mrs. Pullet's careful curation of family possessions, and Mrs. Deane's social ascent all express the same conviction: that to be a Dodson is to occupy a particular and superior place in the provincial social order.

## Tragic Dimensions

Family pride in the novel is double-edged. It gives Tom the discipline to repay his father's debts, but it also hardens him against [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie's]] more expansive emotional needs — his "face showed little radiance" during home hours [039-003]. Mr. Tulliver's pride leads him to the lawsuit that ruins the family; Tom's pride leads him to reject Maggie when she most needs a brother's forgiveness. The narrator suggests that pride, untempered by [[sympathy]], becomes a form of moral blindness.
