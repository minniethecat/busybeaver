# Mr. Guest

**Mr. Guest** is the senior partner of Guest & Co, a prosperous shipping and trading firm in [[st-oggs|St Ogg's]], and the father of [[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]]. He represents the established commercial class that has risen to prominence in the provincial town.

## Business Position

Mr. Guest is a leading businessman in St Ogg's, with his firm handling shipping, trading, and various commercial ventures. His position is indicated by his involvement in significant business transactions:

> "Guest & Co. were going to bid for it. Mr Guest and Mr Wakem were on friendly terms" (030-045)

He works alongside [[lawyer-wakem|Mr. Wakem]] on business matters, showing his integration into the commercial network of [[mudport|the town]]. The relationship between Guest and [[lawyer-wakem|Wakem]] represents the alliance between commerce and [[justice|law]] that characterizes St Ogg's economic life.

## Relationship with the Tullivers

After the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] family's financial ruin, Mr. Guest becomes relevant to their fortunes through his firm. [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]] works to establish himself and repay his father's [[property|debt]]s, and his path intersects with Guest & Co:

> "Mr Guest and I see clear enough" (050-010) — Mr. Deane, discussing the business outlook with Tom

Mr. Guest, along with [[mr-deane|Mr. Deane]] (his manager), represents the world of commercial opportunity that [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] aspires to join. The firm of Guest & Co becomes a symbol of the financial recovery that [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] pursues with single-minded determination.

## Family

Mr. Guest is the father of [[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]], the handsome and socially prominent young man who becomes entangled with [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]. [[stephen-guest|Stephen]]'s position as "the son of Mr. Guest" is repeatedly invoked to emphasize his social standing:

> "I feel sure he and Mr Guest are expecting that" (047-005) — Stephen, speaking of his father's expectations

Mr. Guest's social position means that [[stephen-guest|Stephen]]'s romantic entanglement with [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] carries serious consequences — not just personal but familial and commercial.

## Character

Mr. Guest appears only indirectly in the novel, through references by other characters. He is portrayed as a practical, commercially minded man whose primary concern is the business. His daughters — "the Miss Guests" — are mentioned as fashionable young women who move in St Ogg's best society:

> "The Miss Guests, who associated chiefly on terms of condescension" (051-001)

## Significance

Mr. Guest represents the established commercial order of St Ogg's. His firm is the engine of [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]'s eventual financial rise, but his family's social position is also what makes the [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]-[[stephen-guest|Stephen]] scandal so destructive. The Guest family embodies the interconnection of commerce, social standing, and moral reputation in the provincial world of the novel.
