# Sir John Crake

Sir John Crake, master of the harriers, is a figure of provincial gentility whom the young Tom Tulliver admires from a distance — a man whose appeal lies not in learning but in a kind of natural, unlettered authority that Tom instinctively respects. He appears only as a name invoked in conversation, yet his brief mention captures the social aspirations and values of the rural world in which Tom is being raised.

## Role in the Novel

Sir John Crake enters the narrative during Tom's education at King's Lorton, when the question of the utility of Latin arises. Tom, struggling with the classical curriculum, asks Philip Wakem a pointed question: "do you think Sir John Crake, the master of the harriers, knows Latin?" [018-034]. The question is not rhetorical but diagnostic — Tom has "often thought he should like to resemble Sir John Crake," and if Sir John does not need Latin, then perhaps Tom need not master it either [018-034]. Philip's reply — that Sir John "had a grinder" who did his Latin for him — allows Tom to conclude with "serious satisfaction" that "as far as Latin was concerned, there was no hindrance to his resembling Sir John Crake" [018-036]. The moment is quietly devastating: Tom's highest ambition is to resemble a country gentleman whose classical attainments are borrowed, and even this modest goal is undermined by the education supposedly designed to elevate him.

## Character Analysis

Sir John Crake is never seen, never heard, and occupies barely three sentences of the novel. Yet his function is precise. He embodies the rural masculine ideal as Tom understands it — a man of action, not of books; a master of hounds, not of declensions. The gap between this ideal and the gentleman that Mr. Stelling's classical education is meant to produce is the novel's quiet satire of the English educational system. Tom, it turns out, is right: the world he will enter — the world of Guest & Co., of commerce and credit — requires no Latin. Sir John Crake, for all his remoteness, represents a truth that Mr. Stelling cannot teach.

## Key Scenes

- **Tom's question to Philip** [018-034]: Tom asks whether Sir John Crake knows Latin, revealing his unspoken aspiration to resemble the master of the harriers rather than any classical scholar.
- **Philip's answer and Tom's relief** [018-036]: Philip explains that Sir John had a "grinder" to do his Latin, and Tom draws the self-serving but not wholly incorrect conclusion that Latin is not an obstacle to becoming the kind of man he wishes to be.
