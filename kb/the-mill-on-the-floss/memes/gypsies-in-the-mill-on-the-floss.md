# Gypsies in *The Mill on the Floss*

The gypsy camp on the common outside St Ogg's serves as both a literal presence and a potent symbol in the novel — representing for the young Maggie Tulliver an imagined realm of freedom from the constraints of Dodson respectability, and for the adult reader a complex figure of social otherness through which Eliot explores the costs of belonging.

## Definition

In the world of the novel, "the gypsies" occupy a liminal space — physically on the common at the edge of the cultivated world, and socially beyond the pale of respectable provincial society. For Maggie, repeatedly told she is "like a gypsy" for her dark hair, dark eyes, and unconventional behaviour [008-094], the gypsies represent both a threat (the accusation of wildness) and a promise (the possibility of a world where her difference would be accepted). The gypsy episode in Book I, Chapter XI is one of the novel's most revealing early scenes: a childhood fantasy of escape that teaches Maggie, through comic disappointment, the impossibility of simply stepping outside one's social world.

## Context in the Novel

### Maggie's Identification with the Gypsies

Maggie's association with gypsies is established early by her Dodson aunts, who use the comparison as a criticism: "She's more like a gypsy nor ever" [008-094]. But Maggie internalises this identification differently — less as shame than as a recognition that she does not, and perhaps cannot, fit the Dodson mould. When family tensions become unbearable, she decides to "run away and go to the gypsies" [012-001], reasoning that a people who look like her might accept her as the Dodsons do not.

### The Gypsy Episode (Book I, Chapter XI)

Maggie's flight to the gypsy camp unfolds as a comedy of disappointed expectations. She arrives imagining herself as a kind of queen who will "teach the gypsies to use a washing-basin" and elevate them through her presence [012-007]. Instead, the gypsies treat her as a potential source of ransom money, and she is returned — humbled and hungry — to her father. The episode is both a gentle satire of Maggie's childish grandiosity and a serious meditation on the limits of escape. The gypsies do not represent the alternative world Maggie imagines; they represent the reality that there is no alternative world.

### Symbolic Significance

The gypsy episode functions as a miniature of the novel's larger tragic structure: Maggie's desire to escape the constraints of her social world repeatedly encounters the hard fact that there is nowhere to escape to. The gypsies, as a community that exists outside but adjacent to provincial society, embody the fantasy of an "outside" — but the episode's comic deflation of Maggie's expectations suggests that this outside is itself a projection, not a real alternative.

## Key Passages

- **Aunt Pullet's Accusation** ([008-094]): "She's more like a gypsy nor ever" — the Dodson aunts' racialised critique of Maggie's appearance and behaviour, establishing the gypsy comparison as a marker of Maggie's difference.
- **The Decision to Run Away** ([012-001]): Maggie, "so often told she was like a gypsy, and 'half wild,'" resolves to "run away and go to the gypsies" — the logic of a child who believes that those who are called outsiders must belong together.
- **Arrival at the Camp** ([012-004]): "The gypsies saw at once that she was a little lady" — the comic reversal in which Maggie's fantasy of gypsy solidarity collapses against the reality of class difference.
- **The Plan to Civilise** ([012-007]): Maggie imagines "how charming when she had taught the gypsies to use a washing-basin" — the colonising impulse of the well-brought-up child, rendered both comic and poignant.
- **Prejudice in Favour of Property** ([012-012]): Maggie concludes "the gypsies had a strong prejudice in favour of property" — a deflating recognition that even outsiders operate within economic reality.
