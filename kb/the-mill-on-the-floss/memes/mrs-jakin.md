# Mrs. Jakin

**Mrs. Jakin** is the mother of [[bob-jakin|Bob Jakin]] and a widow living in [[st-oggs|St Ogg's]]. She keeps lodgings and provides a refuge for [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]] during her final crisis.

## Character

Mrs. Jakin is depicted as a humble, hardworking woman who runs a lodging house in St Ogg's. She is deferential but warm, treating [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] with kindness and respect when [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] comes to stay.

When [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] arrives at [[bob-jakin|Bob Jakin]]'s lodgings, Mrs. Jakin welcomes her warmly:

> "I hope Mumps is friendly with Mrs Jakin, Bob," said [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]], smiling. (049-015)

Mrs. Jakin is attentive and discreet, going about her duties without prying:

> "...sitting down, Miss," said Mrs Jakin, rubbing her apron over a perfectly clean chair (049-005)

She "disappears" promptly when her presence is not needed, showing a respectful awareness of boundaries (049-007).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Jakin appears in two key passages:

| Scene | PN Range | Role |
|-------|----------|------|
| [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s first stay at [[bob-jakin|Bob]]'s lodgings | 049-005–015 | Provides hospitality, respects [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s privacy |
| [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s final refuge | 061-032 | Makes room for [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] and [[mrs-tulliver|Mrs. Tulliver]] after the [[shame|disgrace]] |

In the final crisis, after [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s reputation is ruined, both "Mrs Jakin the larger and Mrs Jakin the smaller" (suggesting [[bob-jakin|Bob]]'s mother and possibly wife) provide vacant lodgings for [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] and her mother (061-032).

## Connection to Bob Jakin

Mrs. Jakin's son, [[bob-jakin|Bob Jakin]], is a packman and former childhood acquaintance of [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]. [[bob-jakin|Bob]]'s loyalty to [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] and later to [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] makes the Jakin household an important place of refuge. The Jakin family, though humble, provides the practical kindness and shelter that [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s own social circle denies her after her [[shame|disgrace]].

## Significance

Mrs. Jakin represents a recurring theme in [[the-mill-on-the-floss|The Mill on the Floss]]: genuine human kindness existing outside the rigid social structures of St Ogg's. Unlike the [[dodson-family|Dodsons]] and the respectable society that judges [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]], the Jakins offer unconditional hospitality. Mrs. Jakin's simple kindness — rubbing a clean chair, disappearing when not needed, making room in a crisis — stands in quiet contrast to the moral condemnation [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] faces from her social superiors.
