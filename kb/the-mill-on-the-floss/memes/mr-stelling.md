# Mr. Stelling

The Reverend Mr. Stelling is the clergyman-educator to whom [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]] is sent to be "made a scholarship of." His ineffective pedagogy and blindness to [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s actual needs make him one of Eliot's sharpest satirical portraits of institutional education.

## Role in the Novel

Stelling appears primarily in Book 2, where [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] is placed under his instruction. His function is to demonstrate the inadequacy of the classical education system for [[childhood|children]] like [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]—practical, unbookish boys who need training relevant to their future lives rather than Latin grammar.

## Character Analysis

Stelling is not malicious but profoundly limited. He teaches "in the ordinary way" (016-009) because it has never occurred to him that any other way might exist. His "duty was to teach the lad in the ordinary way" (016-009)—a formula that absolves him of [[duty|responsibility]] for the outcome. Eliot's satire is gentle but devastating: Stelling represents an entire educational philosophy that values form over substance, [[custom|convention]] over adaptation to individual need.

## Relationships

- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]**: Stelling's pupil, whose practical intelligence the schoolmaster completely fails to recognize or develop.
- **[[philip-wakem|Philip Wakem]]**: Another pupil, whose physical disability and artistic sensibility Stelling handles with more sympathy than he shows [[tom-tulliver|Tom]].

## Key Scenes

- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s arrival at Stelling's** (Book 2, Chapter 1): The beginning of [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s unhappy [[education|schooling]].
- **Stelling on duty** (016-003): Stelling's philosophy of education revealed in his treatment of [[tom-tulliver|Tom]].
- **[[philip-wakem|Philip]]'s introduction** (Book 2, Chapter 3): Stelling's household becomes the setting for [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] and [[philip-wakem|Philip]]'s first meeting.
