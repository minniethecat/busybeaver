# Nature

**Nature** in *The Mill on the Floss* is far more than scenic backdrop. The River Floss functions as the novel's governing symbol: it sustains the mill that gives the Tullivers their livelihood, serves as the site of Maggie's childhood freedom, and ultimately becomes the instrument of the novel's tragic conclusion.

## Definition

Eliot presents nature as both beautiful and indifferent to human concerns. The opening description of the Floss—"How lovely the little river is" (002-001)—establishes a pastoral register that the novel repeatedly complicates. Nature is not a moral agent; it neither punishes nor rewards. But it provides the material conditions of life (the mill, the fields, the changing seasons) and supplies the metaphors through which characters understand their experience.

## Context in the Novel

The mill at Dorlcote, powered by the Floss, represents the Tulliver family's rootedness in place and tradition. Mr. Tulliver's identity is inseparable from the mill; losing it is losing himself. For Maggie, the river is associated with freedom and escape—she runs to it in moments of crisis, and her childhood fishing expeditions with Tom are her happiest memories.

The novel's ending transforms the river from pastoral setting to tragic agent. The flood that reunites Maggie and Tom in death is both natural disaster and symbolic resolution: the river that sustained their childhood now claims them both. Eliot refuses to moralize this ending, presenting nature's indifference as the ultimate context for human striving.

## Key Passages

- **The lovely Floss** (002-001): The novel opens with a celebration of the river's beauty, establishing nature as primary presence.
- **The narrator's love of place** (002-002): "I am in love with moistness"—the narrator's attachment to the natural world as emotional foundation.
- **Maggie in the landscape** (005-006): Maggie "loved to linger in the great space" of the mill's natural surroundings.
- **The flood** (066-001): The climactic flood that returns Maggie to the river and to Tom.
