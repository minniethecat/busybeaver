# Miss Firniss

Miss Firniss keeps a boarding school for young ladies in the ancient town of Laceham on the Floss, where Maggie Tulliver and her cousin Lucy Deane are sent during the years of Tom's education at King's Lorton. Though mentioned only in passing, Miss Firniss's establishment represents the conventional feminine education of the era — an education sharply contrasted with the passionate, unconventional intelligence of her most notable pupil.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Firniss's school is introduced during the narrative's account of Tom's extended stay at King's Lorton: "while Maggie was growing with a rapidity which her aunts considered highly reprehensible, at Miss Firniss's boarding-school in the ancient town of Laceham on the Floss, with cousin Lucy for her companion" [022-001]. The pairing of Maggie with Lucy — the novel's wild and conventional heroines — under Miss Firniss's roof is characteristic of Eliot's method: the school becomes the site where Maggie's difference from ordinary girls becomes visible to herself and others.

The school reappears at a moment of family crisis. When Mr. Tulliver, overwhelmed by the excitement of his lawsuit and desperate for Maggie's presence, follows the Laceham coach to the coach-office, he has a letter written demanding Maggie's immediate return: "he wanted the letter to be given to the coachman to deliver at Miss Firniss's school in the morning" [024-003]. The summons from Miss Firniss's school marks the end of Maggie's formal education and the beginning of her constrained adult life.

## Character Analysis

Miss Firniss herself remains an absence — a name without a face, a schoolmistress without a quoted word. But her function in the narrative is structural: she presides over the kind of education deemed suitable for provincial young ladies, an education in accomplishments rather than understanding. That Maggie's aunts consider her "rapid growth" at this school "highly reprehensible" is darkly comic — what they object to is not the curriculum but Maggie's physical development, her body's refusal to remain childlike and containable [022-001].

## Key Scenes

- **Maggie's school years** [022-001]: The narrator notes that Maggie spends her adolescence at Miss Firniss's boarding school with Lucy, a period during which her separation from Tom and Philip reshapes her emotional world.
- **The summons home** [024-003]: Mr. Tulliver, in the grip of financial ruin, sends a letter to Miss Firniss's school demanding Maggie's immediate return — the abrupt end of her formal education.
