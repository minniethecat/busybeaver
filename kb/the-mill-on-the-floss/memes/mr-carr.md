# Mr. Carr

Mr. Carr is a figure known to the Dodson family only through the cautionary tale of his decline. He appears in the novel solely as a warning — invoked by [[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]] during the family council after [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s catastrophic illness.

## Role in the Novel

When Mr. Tulliver lies insensible after his collapse, Mrs. Pullet offers her prognosis by analogy:

> "It's much if he ever gets up again; and if he does, he'll most like be childish, as Mr Carr was, poor man! They fed him with a spoon as if he'd been a babby for three year. He'd quite lost the use of his limbs; but then he'd got a Bath chair, and somebody to draw him; and that's what you won't have, I doubt, Bessy." [026-016]

Mrs. Pullet's sisterly "comfort" — Mr. Carr's fate as a warning of what might befall Mr. Tulliver — is characteristically Dodson: truth-telling without tenderness, practical observation without emotional support. The mention of the Bath chair that the Tullivers "won't have" underscores the financial gulf between the families: Mr. Carr's people could afford a chair and an attendant; the Tullivers cannot.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Carr is significant not as a character but as a narrative device. His name appears only to be immediately dismissed as irrelevant — "Mr Carr was none of our blood, nor noways connected with us, as I've ever heared" [026-017] — yet the Dodson sisters cannot resist measuring themselves against his misfortune. He represents the outer boundary of Dodson consciousness: close enough to be invoked as an example, distant enough to be disclaimed. His function in the family council scene is to darken the atmosphere, to make the worst outcome feel proximate and real.
