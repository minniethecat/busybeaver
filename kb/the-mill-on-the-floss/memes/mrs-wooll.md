# Mrs. Wooll

Mrs. Wooll of [[st-oggs|St Ogg's]] is a minor figure in the novel's provincial social landscape — a lace-buyer whose habits serve as a foil for the Dodson sisters' particular brand of respectability. She appears only in the discourse of other characters, yet her name encapsulates a whole system of social comparison, consumption, and moral judgment.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Wooll functions as a social benchmark. When [[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]] wishes to establish the superiority of her own domestic management, she measures herself against Mrs. Wooll:

> "When Mrs Glegg died, it would be found that she had better lace laid by in the right-hand drawer of her wardrobe in the Spotted Chamber than ever Mrs Wooll of St Ogg's had bought in her life, although Mrs Wooll wore her lace before it was paid for." [008-001]

This single sentence captures the essence of Dodson morality: the virtue lies not in abstaining from fine things but in acquiring them prudently — paying before wearing, storing before displaying. Mrs. Wooll's transgression is not her taste for lace but her impatience: she wears it "before it was paid for," violating the Dodson code of financial rectitude.

Much later in the novel, when [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]'s reputation is under attack, Mrs. Glegg's defiant loyalty takes the form of daring "Mrs Wooll, or any one else" to come to her with false tales — once again invoking Mrs. Wooll as the representative bearer of provincial gossip [063-001].

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Wooll is barely a character — she is a symbol, a name that stands for a type. Her repeated invocation across the novel (from Book 1 to Book 7) demonstrates the persistence of provincial social hierarchies: even a woman who never speaks a line of dialogue can serve as a measuring-rod for Dodson respectability across decades. The fact that Mrs. Glegg is still measuring herself against Mrs. Wooll by the novel's end suggests both the narrowness of the Dodson social world and the durability of its judgments.
