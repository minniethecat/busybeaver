# The Miss Dodsons

"The Miss Dodsons" is the collective name by which the four Dodson sisters — [[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]], [[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]], [[mrs-deane|Mrs. Deane]], and [[mrs-tulliver|Mrs. Tulliver]] — were known before their marriages. As a collective entity, they represent the Dodson family's formidable social identity: a clan of women governed by shared traditions, rigid propriety, and an unshakeable conviction in their own respectability.

## Role in the Novel

The Miss Dodsons embody the Dodson ethos before it was dispersed into four separate households. As George Eliot describes:

> "The Miss Dodsons had always been thought to hold up their heads very high, and no one was surprised the two eldest had married so well, — not at an early age, for that was not the practice of the Dodson family." [007-009]

The collective identity of "the Miss Dodsons" continues to operate even after their marriages — when they gather for family councils, their shared Dodson training reasserts itself, overriding their individual circumstances and temperaments. Mrs. Tulliver invokes her identity as "a Miss Dodson" when appealing to [[lawyer-wakem|Wakem]], as if the name itself carried legal weight [030-008].

Even in their physical variety, the sisters remain a recognizable set: "It was quite unaccountable that Mrs Deane, the thinnest and sallowest of all the Miss Dodsons, should have had this child, who might have been taken for Mrs Tulliver's any day" [008-037]. The frame of "the Miss Dodsons" persists long after the name has legally changed.

## Character Analysis

The Miss Dodsons as a collective entity represent the power of shared upbringing and class identity. What makes them formidable is not individual talent but their unanimity of judgment — they share "particular ways of doing everything": bleaching linen, making cowslip wine, curing hams, and above all, pronouncing on the conduct of families "ungoverned by the Dodson tradition" [007-009]. Their collective identity is both their strength and their limitation: it gives them social standing but leaves little room for individual divergence, as [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]] would discover across the novel.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]]** — The eldest Miss Dodson, chosen by Mr. Glegg as "a handsome embodiment of female prudence and thrift" [013-007].
- **[[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]]** — One of the four sisters, known for her emotional sensibility and her marriage to a gentleman farmer.
- **[[mrs-deane|Mrs. Deane]]** — "The thinnest and sallowest of all the Miss Dodsons" [008-037], who made what was initially considered the worst match but whose husband's success would prove otherwise.
- **[[mrs-tulliver|Mrs. Tulliver]]** — The youngest Miss Dodson, whose marriage to Mr. Tulliver placed her outside the Dodson orbit of financial prudence, to her lasting regret.
- **[[dodson-family]]** — The family system within which the Miss Dodsons were formed and to which they remained loyal.
