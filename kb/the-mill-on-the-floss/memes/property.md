# Property

**Property** in *The Mill on the Floss* is never merely economic. It is the foundation of family identity, the measure of social standing, and the instrument through which characters exercise power over one another. The mill at Dorlcote is more than a business—it is the Tulliver name made tangible.

## Definition

Property in the novel operates on multiple levels: as livelihood (the mill that feeds the family), as inheritance (what passes from parent to child), as debt (the obligation that binds the future), and as identity (who one is, defined by what one owns). The Dodsons' religion of property—"it must go out o' your own family" (008-033)—reveals how completely economic and moral categories are fused in provincial thinking.

## Context in the Novel

The plot of *The Mill on the Floss* is driven by property transactions: Mr. Tulliver's lawsuit over water rights, the family's bankruptcy, the sale of the mill to Lawyer Wakem, and Tom's eventual repurchase of it. Each of these events is not merely economic but emotional and moral. Mr. Tulliver loses the mill and loses himself; Tom regains the mill and believes he has restored the family's honor.

For the women of the novel, property is something they depend on but rarely control. Mrs. Tulliver's "teraphim, or household gods"—her linen, her china, her household goods—represent a feminine relationship to property that is both intimate and precarious (Book 3, Chapter 2). Maggie, as a woman, can neither inherit the mill nor earn the money to reclaim it; her economic dependence is a form of structural powerlessness.

## Key Passages

- **Property must stay in the family** (008-033): The Dodson creed—property is moral as well as material.
- **The mill as identity** (003-008): The mill that defines the Tullivers, its care woven into daily life.
- **Mrs. Tulliver's household gods** (Book 3, Chapter 2): The feminine relationship to property as domestic and precarious.
- **Tom's economic restoration** (042-065): Tom's vow to "do the duty of a son and a brother" through financial redemption.
