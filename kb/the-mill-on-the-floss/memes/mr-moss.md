# Mr. Moss

**Mr. Moss** is a farmer at [[basset|Basset]], married to [[mrs-moss|Gritty Moss]] (née [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]]), [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s sister. He appears primarily in Book 1 Chapter 9, when [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] visits [[basset|Basset]] to ask for repayment of a loan.

## Family and Social Position

Mr. Moss married Miss [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]], making him [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s brother-in-[[justice|law]]. The marriage is described as a step down in social standing for the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] family:

> "Mr Moss, who, when he married Miss Tulliver, had been thought to have made a very poor match" (009-034)

The Moss family lives at [[basset|Basset]], a rural area where they farm on a modest scale. They have several [[childhood|children]], whom [[mrs-moss|Mrs. Moss]] refers to collectively as "the small Mosses" (009-019).

## Character and Circumstances

Mr. Moss is portrayed as a hardworking but perpetually struggling farmer, constantly short of money. When [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] visits to ask for repayment of a three-hundred-pound loan, Mr. Moss's financial difficulties become apparent:

> "I was so unlucky wi' the cows, and the sheep were taken bad" (009-036)

He is deferential and apologetic toward [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]], aware of his dependency on his brother-in-[[justice|law]]'s forbearance. He makes excuses about bad harvests and sick livestock, speaking "deprecatingly" and "looking blankly before him" (009-038, 009-040).

## Relationship with Mr. Tulliver

The dynamic between Mr. Moss and [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] reveals the latter's generosity despite his own financial pressures. Although [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] needs the money back, he does not press Mr. Moss harshly:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Loan amount | Three hundred pounds |
| Reason for visit | [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] seeks repayment |
| Moss's situation | Failed crops, sick livestock |
| Outcome | [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]] does not force repayment |

[[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s visit to [[basset|Basset]] (009-006) also shows his [[love|affection]] for his sister and her family, despite the Mosses' lowly circumstances.

## Significance

Mr. Moss serves as a minor character who illustrates two important themes in *[[the-mill-on-the-floss|The Mill on the Floss]]*:

1. **[[family|Family]] [[duty|obligation]]**: The [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] family's sense of duty toward poorer relations. [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s forbearance toward Mr. Moss contrasts with the [[dodson-family|Dodson family]]'s more rigid, judgmental attitudes toward financial failure.

2. **Social decline through marriage**: The marriage of Miss [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] to Mr. Moss represents a downward social trajectory, a source of some [[shame|embarrassment]] to the Dodson side of the family. [[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]], in particular, views such matches with disapproval.
