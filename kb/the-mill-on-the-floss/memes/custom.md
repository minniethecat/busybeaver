# Custom

**Custom** in *The Mill on the Floss* is the invisible force that governs life in St. Ogg's. More powerful than law and more pervasive than religion, custom dictates how one dresses, marries, mourns, and judges one's neighbors. The Dodson family is its high priesthood.

## Definition

Custom, as the Dodsons understand it, is the accumulated wisdom of generations—the way things have always been done, and therefore the way they should be done. It encompasses everything from the proper way to arrange a funeral to the correct attitude toward debt. To violate custom is not merely to make a mistake but to commit a moral offense against the community itself.

## Context in the Novel

The Dodson sisters are the novel's great portrait of custom made flesh. Mrs. Glegg in particular judges every action by its conformity to traditional standards. The family gatherings—the parties, the consultations, the funerals—are rituals of custom, occasions for the display and reinforcement of collective norms.

Maggie's tragedy is partly the tragedy of a person who cannot live within custom's constraints. Her intelligence, her passion, her moral seriousness—all exceed what custom can accommodate. When she runs away with Stephen Guest (even though she returns before the act is consummated), custom condemns her absolutely. The community that once tolerated her eccentricity now treats her as fallen. Eliot does not simply condemn custom—she recognizes its role in sustaining social order—but she shows its cruelty when applied without sympathy.

## Key Passages

- **Custom as moral code** (003-005): Mrs. Glegg treats any departure from family custom as a moral failing.
- **The family party as ritual** (007-001): The Dodson gathering as a performance of custom and mutual surveillance.
- **Custom's kindness and cruelty** (034-004): The Dodsons' "simple, semi-pagan" customs provide both comfort and constraint.
- **The weight of tradition** (008-004): "It used to be the way in our family"—custom invoked as unanswerable authority.
