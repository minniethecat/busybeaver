# Mr. Poulter

Mr. Poulter is the village schoolmaster at King's Lorton, an old Peninsular War veteran employed to drill [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]] during his time at [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]]'s school. His reminiscences of military life and the sword he loans to Tom are central to the events of Book 2, where his combination of martial vanity, diminished dignity, and conscientious scruple makes him one of Eliot's most vivid minor characters.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Poulter appears in a brief but consequential narrative window during Tom's education at King's Lorton. He combines his role as village schoolmaster with drilling lessons for [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]] — an arrangement that was "a source of high mutual pleasure" for both teacher and pupil [019-007]. His function in the narrative is threefold:

1. **Comic relief through military vanity** — his self-aggrandising war stories and contempt for rival heroes like General Wolfe provide gentle satire of martial pride.
2. **Foreshadowing of danger** — the loan of the sword, an adult weapon placed in a boy's hands, creates dramatic tension.
3. **A catalyst for character development** — Tom's manipulation of Mr. Poulter with money shows his emerging pragmatism, while the resulting injury deepens the bond between Tom and Maggie.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Poulter is described with a mixture of martial dignity and present decline:

> "He had rather a shrunken appearance, and was tremulous in the mornings, not from age, but from the extreme perversity of the King's Lorton boys, which nothing but gin could enable him to sustain with any firmness." [019-007]

Despite his diminished state, he maintains a soldierly bearing:

> "Still, he carried himself with martial erectness, had his clothes scrupulously brushed, and his trousers tightly strapped." [019-007]

He is intensely proud of his military past and was reported to have been "a conspicuous figure at Talavera" [019-007], claiming his regiment inspired peculiar terror in the enemy. His vanity is gently satirised by the narrator:

> "Mr Poulter was a host in himself; that is to say, he admired himself more than a whole army of spectators could have admired him." [019-035]

The drilling lessons were "always protracted by episodes of warlike narrative, much more interesting to Tom than Philip's stories out of the Iliad" [019-007]. Mr. Poulter held dismissive views of other military heroes, declaring of General Wolfe: "'Nothing o' the sort!' said Mr Poulter, contemptuously. 'He did nothing but die of his wound; that's a poor haction, I consider. Any other man 'ud have died o' the wounds I've had.'" [019-009]

## Relationships

- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]** — Mr. Poulter's pupil at King's Lorton. Tom persuades him with a five-shilling piece to lend his cavalry sword, showing an early talent for pragmatic manipulation. The sword accident that follows deepens Tom's bond with Maggie.
- **[[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]]** — Mr. Poulter's employer at King's Lorton, who engages him to drill Tom as part of a gentleman's education.
- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]** — Witness to Tom's sword accident; her distress at Tom's injury reveals the depth of their sibling attachment.
- **[[philip-wakem|Philip Wakem]]** — Tom's schoolfellow, whom Mr. Poulter dismisses contemptuously, his martial values standing in stark contrast to Philip's literary and artistic sensibilities.

## Key Scenes

- **The Drilling Lessons** ([019-007]–[019-009]): Mr. Poulter drills Tom and tells war stories, his martial vanity on full display as he dismisses General Wolfe's heroism and elevates his own.
- **The Loan of the Sword** ([019-041]–[019-043]): Tom offers a five-shilling piece for the loan of Mr. Poulter's cavalry sword. Mr. Poulter, attempting to act conscientiously, demonstrates how to draw and sheathe the sword safely, feeling he had "acted with scrupulous conscientiousness" [019-043].
- **Tom's Injury** ([020-027]): The sword falls while Tom performs the sword exercise for Maggie, cutting his foot and causing him to faint. The accident is a direct consequence of Mr. Poulter's well-meaning but imprudent loan.
