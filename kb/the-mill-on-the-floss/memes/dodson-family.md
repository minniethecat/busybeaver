# The Dodson Family

The Dodson family is the novel's central social institution — a matriarchal clan defined not by wealth alone but by a rigorous code of conduct, propriety, and domestic economy that constitutes what Eliot calls "the religion of the Dodsons."

## Definition

The Dodsons are a St. Ogg's family of "very respectable" standing, distinguished less by material wealth than by an exacting standard of behaviour that elevates prudence, cleanliness, and correct domestic management to the level of moral principle [007-009]. For a Dodson, to be "respectable" means to conduct one's affairs — financial, domestic, and social — with a correctness that leaves no room for reproach. The family's code operates as an unwritten law, enforced through the authority of the Dodson sisters: Mrs. Glegg, Mrs. Pullet, Mrs. Deane, and Mrs. Tulliver.

## Context in the Novel

The Dodson ethos provides the moral yardstick against which the Tullivers are measured — and found wanting. Mr. Tulliver's impulsive litigation against Pivart is the antithesis of Dodson prudence, and his bankruptcy is, in Dodson eyes, a moral failure as much as a financial one [008-036][008-037].

The Dodson blood runs imperfectly in the Tulliver children. Tom inherits the Dodson capacity for rigid principle and determination, though without the softening domestic virtues [007-010]. Maggie inherits none of it — her passionate, impulsive nature is entirely at odds with Dodson self-command, making her the family's perennial problem.

The Dodson code is both the novel's satire and its tragedy. Its insistence on appearances, its horror of debt and disgrace, and its equation of moral worth with material correctness create the social machinery that expels Maggie. Yet the code is not presented as simply villainous; it is the Dodsons' genuine religion, their way of making sense of a precarious world through the discipline of correctness.

## Key Passages

- **The Dodson creed** ([007-009]): The narrator's exposition of the Dodson family's defining values — respectability, propriety, and the conviction that "to be honest and poor was never a Dodson failing."
- **The Dodson sisters' response to Tulliver's bankruptcy** ([008-001]): The family council scene where the Dodson aunts debate their duty to their ruined sister, revealing the tension between family obligation and the Dodson horror of financial imprudence.
- **Mrs. Glegg's pronouncement on mixed blood** ([008-037]): Mrs. Glegg's verdict that "the Tulliver blood" does not mix well with Dodson blood — a theory of inherited character that hangs over the entire novel.
- **Tom Tulliver as imperfect Dodson** ([007-010]): The observation that "the true Dodson was partly latent in Tom" — the Dodson capacity for rigid adherence to principle, but without the warm domestic virtues.

## Critical Interpretation

The Dodsons represent what Eliot regarded as the ambiguous legacy of provincial English dissent: a sturdy, principled independence that had hardened, by the early nineteenth century, into a narrow and unforgiving moral code. They are not villains but products of a system; their tragedy — and Maggie's — is that they cannot see past their code to the human being it crushes.
