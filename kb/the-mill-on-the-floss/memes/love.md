# Love

**Love** in *The Mill on the Floss* appears in multiple, often conflicting forms: the fierce childhood bond between Maggie and Tom, the forbidden romantic attraction to Philip Wakem, the dangerous passion for Stephen Guest, and the narrator's own elegiac love for the remembered landscape of the Floss.

## Definition

Eliot presents love not as a simple virtue but as a complex psychological force that can sustain or destroy. Maggie's love for Tom is the novel's emotional foundation—"I love Tom so dearly, Luke,—better than anybody" (005-016)—yet this same love becomes a source of pain when Tom withdraws his affection as punishment. Romantic love, when it enters, threatens to undo the very self Maggie has struggled to construct.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's opening chapters establish love as Maggie's defining characteristic: she "loved to linger in the great space" of the mill (005-006) and pours affection onto her brother with desperate intensity. Tom's conditional love—"I don't love you, Maggie. You sha'n't go fishing with me tomorrow" (006-038)—teaches Maggie early that love can be withdrawn as discipline.

Romantic love complicates this picture. Philip Wakem offers a love based on shared sensibility and mutual understanding, but family enmity makes it impossible. Stephen Guest's attraction represents passionate love untethered from moral consideration, and Maggie's near-escape with him down the river becomes the novel's central moral crisis. The novel's ending—Maggie and Tom drowned together in the Floss—returns sibling love to primacy, suggesting that this earliest bond is also the deepest (067-005).

## Key Passages

- **Maggie's childhood love for Tom** (005-016): Maggie declares she "love[s] Tom so dearly," establishing the novel's emotional center.
- **Tom's conditional affection** (006-038): Tom weaponizes love, withdrawing it to punish Maggie.
- **Love and forgiveness** (006-041): Maggie promises "I'd forgive you and love you," contrasting her unconditional love with Tom's conditional version.
- **Love in the landscape** (002-002): The narrator confesses "I am in love with moistness," linking love to the novel's sense of place.
