# Mrs. Deane

Mrs. Deane is the youngest and most socially elevated of the Dodson sisters. Married to the prosperous [[mr-deane|Mr. Deane]], a manager at Guest and Company, she represents the Dodson ideal successfully realized: respectability combined with material comfort.

## Role in the Novel

> I wonder you don't take pattern by your sister Deane; she's far more sensible. [008-008]

Mrs. Deane appears primarily as a point of social comparison. Her daughter [[lucy-deane|Lucy]]'s [[custom|convention]]al charm and social success make her the implicit standard against which [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] is measured and found wanting. Mrs. Deane's own quiet satisfaction with her position embodies the Dodson aspiration to rise through prudent marriage and careful management.

## Character Analysis

Unlike [[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]]'s aggressive correctness or [[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]]'s theatrical sentimentality, Mrs. Deane's Dodson character manifests as a placid sense of having done everything right. She has married well, raised an accomplished daughter, and secured her family's place in [[st-oggs-town|St. Ogg's]] society. Eliot's treatment of her is restrained but pointed: Mrs. Deane's very success reveals the narrowness of the Dodson vision of the good life.

## Relationships

- **[[lucy-deane|Lucy Deane]]**: Mrs. Deane's daughter, the novel's model of [[custom|convention]]al feminine achievement.
- **[[mr-deane|Mr. Deane]]**: Her husband, whose commercial success enables the family's social rise.
- **[[mrs-glegg|Mrs. Glegg]] and [[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]]**: Her sisters, whose foibles she observes with quiet superiority.
- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]**: The niece whose un[[custom|convention]]ality troubles the Deane family's social surface.
