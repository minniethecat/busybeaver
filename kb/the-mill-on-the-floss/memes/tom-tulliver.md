# Tom Tulliver

Tom Tulliver is [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s older brother and the novel's second protagonist. Where Maggie is passionate and introspective, Tom is practical and conventional—a boy who grows into a man defined by duty, pride, and an unshakeable but narrow sense of right and wrong.

## Role in the Novel

Tom's role is structural as well as thematic: his assumption of the family's financial responsibility after [[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]'s bankruptcy drives the plot of Books 3 through 6. He resolves to "do the duty of a son and a brother" [042-065], and his relentless pursuit of this goal—repaying the creditors, regaining the mill—represents the Victorian ideal of masculine honor. Yet Tom's virtues are also his limitations: the same rigidity that enables him to succeed economically makes him incapable of understanding his sister.

## Character Analysis

Tom is not a villain but a tragedy of limited vision. His virtues are real: he is hardworking, loyal to his family, and committed to justice as he understands it. But his understanding is fatally narrow. He cannot extend [[sympathy]] to those who deviate from his moral code, and his judgment of Maggie after her near-elopement—declaring "you have disgraced us all" [Book 7, Chapter 1]—reveals the cruelty latent in his rectitude.

Eliot traces Tom's limitations to both nature and nurture. His unimaginative temperament is reinforced by an education that teaches him nothing useful and a culture that equates masculinity with stoicism and control. As a child, Tom's limitations manifest as simple boyishness—he cares more for rabbits and fishing than for Maggie's emotional needs [005-011]. As an adult, these same qualities harden into a punitive moral rigidity.

### Tom and the Dodson Ethic

Tom inherits more from his mother's Dodson side than he would ever admit. The Dodson creed—"to take care of your own property and not to meddle with other people's" [008-065]—finds its fullest expression in Tom. He shares the Dodson conviction that what is right is self-evident and needs no sympathetic imagination to discern. This is the source of both his strength and his blindness.

### The Limits of Duty

Tom's tragedy mirrors Maggie's: each is trapped by a version of duty that leaves no room for the other's needs. Where Maggie's duty demands self-sacrifice, Tom's demands the sacrifice of others—specifically, the sacrifice of his sister's happiness to his sense of family honor. Eliot does not condemn Tom's sense of duty but shows its insufficiency as a moral framework. Duty without [[sympathy]], she suggests, becomes cruelty.

## Relationships

- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]**: Tom's sister, whom he loves but cannot understand. His conditional affection shapes Maggie's emotional life; his final rejection of her precipitates the novel's tragic climax. Their reconciliation in the flood suggests a love that life could never fully express.
- **[[mr-tulliver|Mr. Tulliver]]**: Tom's father, whose financial ruin and vow of revenge against [[lawyer-wakem|Wakem]] Tom inherits as sacred obligation. Tom writes the vow in the family Bible [022-032], making it a kind of religious duty.
- **[[mrs-tulliver|Mrs. Tulliver]]**: Tom is his mother's favorite, and her Dodson values shape his moral world more than he realizes.
- **[[philip-wakem|Philip Wakem]]**: Tom's schoolfellow at [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]]'s and later rival for Maggie's affection; Tom's hostility to Philip is driven by family loyalty but also by a cruelty that Eliot does not excuse.
- **[[bob-jakin|Bob Jakin]]**: Tom's childhood friend who becomes his business partner, representing a warmer, more flexible masculinity. Bob's success as a trader suggests an alternative model of male competence.

## Key Scenes

- **The rabbits** [005-011]: Young Tom dismisses Maggie's grief over his dead rabbits, establishing his emotional limitations and Maggie's desperate need for his approval.
- **Tom at Mr. Stelling's** [016-003]: Tom's unsuitable education reveals the gap between institutional schooling and real capability. He learns Latin but not how to think.
- **Writing the vow** [022-032]: Tom inscribes his father's curse against Wakem in the family Bible, transforming private bitterness into sacred duty.
- **Assuming responsibility** [042-065]: Tom resolves to repay the family debts, embodying filial duty at its most admirable.
- **Forbidding Philip** [Book 5]: Tom discovers Maggie's secret meetings with Philip and forbids further contact, asserting patriarchal authority over his sister.
- **Judging Maggie** [Book 7, Chapter 1]: After Maggie's return, Tom refuses to receive her, declaring she has "disgraced us all." This is the emotional climax of their relationship.
- **The flood** [Book 7, Chapter 5]: Tom's final embrace of Maggie in death—"the living and the dead were together"—suggests the love that his moral rigidity could not express in life.

## Critical Perspectives

Tom Tulliver has divided critics since the novel's publication. Some read him as Eliot's critique of patriarchal moralism—a figure whose rigidity destroys what he most loves. Others see him more sympathetically, as a boy forced into adult responsibility too young, whose "narrowness" is the survival mechanism of a damaged child. Feminist critics note that Tom's authority over Maggie is never questioned within the novel's world, even as Eliot's narration subtly undermines it. His tragedy is that he never learns what Maggie knows instinctively: that justice without [[sympathy]] is merely another form of cruelty.
