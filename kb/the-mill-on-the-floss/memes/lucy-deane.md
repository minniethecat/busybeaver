# Lucy Deane

Lucy Deane is the daughter of [[mrs-deane|Mrs Deane]] and niece of [[mrs-tulliver|Mrs Tulliver]], a fair-haired, sweet-tempered girl whose beauty and docility embody the Victorian feminine ideal against which her cousin [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]] is repeatedly measured — and found wanting. Lucy's central role in the novel's romantic crisis makes her both an agent of happiness and an unwitting casualty of [[love|passion]]s she barely understands.

## Role in the Novel

Lucy functions as [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s foil: where [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] is dark, impulsive, and intellectually restless, Lucy is fair, placid, and content with the domestic sphere. From their earliest childhood juxtaposition — "there's Lucy Deane's such a good child" [007-006] — Lucy is held up as the standard [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] cannot meet. Yet Eliot does not make Lucy a merely negative contrast; she is genuinely good, and her goodness is precisely what makes the betrayal so painful.

In the novel's second half, Lucy becomes the pivot of the central romantic entanglement. She loves [[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]] with innocent devotion; she draws [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] into her social circle out of [[love|affection]]; and she is repaid with the near-elopement that shatters her happiness. Her forgiveness of both [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] and [[stephen-guest|Stephen]] — achieved not easily but earnestly — marks one of the novel's most morally complex resolutions.

## Character Analysis

Lucy's defining quality is a sweetness that the novel presents as both admirable and inadequate. She is "of course Lucy Deane" [046-001] — the epithet "of course" signalling that her perfection is a cultural given, a template rather than an achievement. Her goodness is unreflective; she has never needed to struggle for it, unlike [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]], whose goodness is forged through [[religion|renunciation]] and suffering.

This contrast sharpens the novel's moral argument. Lucy's virtue is real but untested; [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s is battered but authentic. When the crisis comes, Lucy's response is characteristically generous: she visits [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]], she forgives, she writes letters of reconciliation. Yet the forgiveness itself underscores her position — she is the one who can afford to forgive, having lost [[stephen-guest|Stephen]] not through her own fault but through a catastrophe she had no part in creating.

> "For shame!" said Lucy, adjusting her little rose-coloured cap. [046-014]

This moment — Lucy's playful rebuke, concerned with [[custom|propriety]] and appearance — captures the distance between her world and [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s. Where [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] wrestles with [[renunciation|renunciation]] and [[duty|duty]], Lucy navigates by social grace.

## Relationships

- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]**: The novel's central female relationship. Lucy loves [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] without reservation, bringing her into the society of [[st-oggs-town|St Ogg's]] and into proximity with [[stephen-guest|Stephen]]. Her trust is absolute, which makes the betrayal absolute. Her eventual forgiveness is one of the novel's few moments of unambiguous grace.

- **[[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]]**: Lucy's betrothed. She loves him with a placid, [[custom|convention]]al devotion. The shock of his attraction to [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] is a revelation not only of his weakness but of the shallowness of the social surface on which her own happiness rested.

- **[[mrs-deane|Mrs Deane]]**: Lucy's mother, who shares her daughter's fair prettiness and social ease. The Deane household represents the prosperous, respectable middle ground from which the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]]s have fallen.

- **[[philip-wakem|Philip Wakem]]**: A peripheral relationship, but significant: [[philip-wakem|Philip]], who loves [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]], observes Lucy's social circle and [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s place within it with the clarity of an outsider.

## Key Scenes

- **[[childhood|Childhood]] Comparison** — Mrs [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] unfavourably contrasts [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] with Lucy, establishing the lifelong pattern: "there's Lucy Deane's such a good child" [007-006].
- **The St Ogg's Society Scenes** — Lucy introduces [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] to social life, unwittingly placing her in [[stephen-guest|Stephen]]'s orbit. The narrator's description of Lucy's busy fingers identifies her as a figure of domestic industry and charm [046-001].
- **The Discovery** — Lucy learns of [[stephen-guest|Stephen]]'s [[love|passion]] for [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]], a scene of quiet devastation that the novel handles with characteristic restraint.
- **The Forgiveness** — Lucy visits [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] and offers reconciliation, a scene in which her goodness finally achieves moral weight through the difficulty of the act.

## Critical Reception

Lucy Deane has often been read as an embodiment of the Victorian "Angel in the House" — the domestic ideal that Eliot simultaneously honours and interrogates. Feminist critics have noted that Lucy's very perfection is a form of limitation: she is what [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] can never be, but also what [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] should not want to be. The novel's genius is to make us care for Lucy even as we recognise that her world is too narrow to contain the full range of human [[love|passion]] and moral struggle.
