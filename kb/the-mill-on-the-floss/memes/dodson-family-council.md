# The Dodson Family Council

The family council that convenes at [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]] after Mr. Tulliver's collapse is one of the novel's great set-pieces: a scene in which the Dodson aunts and uncles deliberate the bankrupt Tullivers' fate with a mixture of genuine concern, procedural punctilio, and devastating condescension. It is comedy and tragedy at once — the Dodson code of respectability laid bare in all its rigidity and, paradoxically, its reliability.

## Summary

With Mr. Tulliver incapacitated and the household goods facing auction, the Dodson aunts and uncles gather to decide what is to be done for "poor Bessy and her children" [026-012]. Mrs. Glegg, Mrs. Pullet, and Mrs. Deane arrive at the mill in due order of precedence, each bringing her own mixture of moral certainty and family feeling. The deliberation is conducted according to strict Dodson protocol: the aunts pronounce, the uncles temporise, and Mrs. Tulliver — "a thorough Dodson, though a mild one" — receives their judgments with bewildered submission. The upshot is a qualified rescue: the family will help, but not so generously as to remove the moral lesson. Mr. Tulliver must be made to feel his humiliation; it is, after all, the consequence of his "hot-tempered crotchets" [026-045]. The children — Maggie and Tom — are expected to understand that their family's fall is a judgment, and that the Dodson way is the only way back to respectability.

## Context

The family council occurs at the novel's midpoint, when the Tulliver family's fortunes have reached their nadir. The lawsuit against Wakem is lost; Mr. Tulliver lies in a paralytic stupor; the furniture is being sold. The Dodson aunts, who have always regarded Mr. Tulliver with suspicion, now find their worst predictions confirmed. Mrs Glegg considered that Mr Tulliver "must be made to feel, when he came to his right mind, that he could never humble himself enough" [026-018]. The council scene crystallises the Dodson worldview — "to live respected, and have the proper bearers at your funeral" — and demonstrates both its strengths (the family does not abandon its own) and its limitations (the help comes wrapped in moral condemnation).

The key participants define the council's dynamics. [[mrs-glegg]], the eldest and sternest Dodson sister, upholds tradition and correct feeling. [[mrs-pullet]] is sentimental and tearful but no less certain of family proprieties. [[mrs-deane]], the most prosperous Dodson sister, arrives in her handsome gig. [[mr-glegg]], the retired wool-stapler, is cautious with money but not unkind. [[mrs-tulliver]] — "poor Bessy" — is bewildered by ruin but comforted by the presence of her sisters. [[tom-tulliver]], at sixteen, is already assuming responsibility, while [[maggie-tulliver]] listens and learns the hard lessons of family duty.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The family council's decisions shape the Tullivers' immediate future: Tom will go to work, Mr. Tulliver will be placed under Wakem as manager of the mill he once owned, and Maggie will learn the limits of family generosity. More profoundly, the scene installs in both children a lasting awareness of the Dodson code — its rigid expectations, its conditional support, its fusion of love and judgment. Tom absorbs the code as duty; Maggie internalises it as guilt. The council is thus not merely a practical deliberation but a moral education, and its lessons echo through everything that follows.

## Literary Significance

The family council is Eliot at her finest as a social anatomist. The scene is saturated with the Dodson ethic: "the religion of the Dodsons consisted in revering whatever was customary and respectable." Eliot's narrator observes the proceedings with irony sharp enough to expose absurdity but warm enough to acknowledge genuine virtue. The Dodsons are not villains; they are limited, provincial, and self-satisfied, but they are also — in their own terms — faithful. The council scene balances this double vision perfectly, making it one of the novel's most achieved passages of social comedy.
