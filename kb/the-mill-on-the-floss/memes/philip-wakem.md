# Philip Wakem

Philip Wakem is the novel's most sympathetic male character: the hunchbacked son of [[lawyer-wakem|Lawyer Wakem]], whose physical deformity has given him a depth of feeling and understanding that the physically robust characters lack. His love for [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]] represents the possibility of a relationship based on mutual recognition rather than social convention.

## Role in the Novel

Philip serves as both foil and counterweight to [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]. Where Tom is rigid and unimaginative, Philip is flexible and perceptive. He "could give her sympathy; he could give her help" [038-066]—precisely what Tom cannot offer Maggie. His role as the son of Mr. Tulliver's enemy makes him the embodiment of the conflict between love and family loyalty. Philip is also the novel's artist figure: his sketching and his sensibility align him with the aesthetic and the contemplative, in contrast to Tom's world of commerce and action.

## Character Analysis

Philip's physical deformity—his hunchback—is both a social liability and, in Eliot's treatment, a source of moral insight. His suffering has taught him the [[sympathy]] that Tom's conventional life has not. Eliot describes him as having "the delicate, indrawn look of the great portraits of the young St. John" [017-003], associating him with spiritual and artistic sensitivity. He reads widely, draws skillfully, and offers Maggie the intellectual companionship she craves.

### Deformity and Insight

Eliot uses Philip's physical condition to develop one of the novel's central arguments: that suffering can be a moral education. Philip tells Maggie, "I think the artists are better than the uncultivated; they have more feeling" [039-064]. His hunchback has forced him inward, developing the reflective capacity that Tom—strong, handsome, and unthinking—never acquires. Yet Eliot avoids sentimentality: Philip's goodness is genuine but not effortless, and his love for Maggie contains an element of neediness that complicates any simple reading of him as victim.

### The Artist in a Commercial World

Philip is the novel's representative of the aesthetic life. His sketches, his reading, his sensitivity to landscape—especially at the Red Deeps [Book 5]—mark him as belonging to a world of feeling and perception that St. Ogg's commercial culture has no language for. His father [[lawyer-wakem|Lawyer Wakem]] values him but does not understand him; his love of art is, in the novel's moral economy, a sign of his humanity rather than a mere accomplishment.

## Relationships

- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]**: The love of Philip's life, discovered when both are adolescents. Their relationship is forbidden by the feud between their fathers. Philip offers Maggie the intellectual and emotional recognition that no one else provides—but his love is also possessive, and his claim on her becomes another form of constraint.
- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]**: Philip's schoolfellow at [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling]]'s, whose initial cruelty (kicking Philip on their first meeting) gives way to a complex, uneasy dynamic. Tom's hostility is driven by family loyalty, but also by a visceral distaste for Philip's difference.
- **[[lawyer-wakem|Lawyer Wakem]]**: Philip's father, whose professional victory over Mr. Tulliver poisons Philip's chance at happiness with Maggie. Their relationship is one of the novel's few functional parent-child bonds—Lawyer Wakem's tenderness toward his son complicates his role as villain.

## Key Scenes

- **The new schoolfellow** [Book 2, Chapter 3]: Philip's introduction at Mr. Stelling's. Tom kicks him, establishing the dynamic of physical cruelty and moral superiority that will define their relationship.
- **Philip offers sympathy** [038-066]: Philip tells Maggie he "could give her sympathy; he could give her help"—the first time anyone has offered her emotional recognition without condition.
- **The Red Deeps meetings** [Book 5]: Philip and Maggie's secret meetings in the wooded hollow. These scenes are the novel's most sustained exploration of intellectual and emotional intimacy between equals.
- **The declaration** [Book 5, Chapter 4]: Philip declares his love for Maggie in the Red Deeps. Maggie's conflicted response—she loves him but cannot commit—sets up the novel's central romantic tension.
- **Philip's loss** [Book 6, Chapter 9]: Philip confronts Maggie about Stephen Guest, recognizing with painful clarity that he has lost her. His dignity in this scene is one of the novel's most poignant moments.

## Critical Perspectives

Philip Wakem has generated significant critical discussion around disability representation in Victorian fiction. Some readers see him as a positive portrayal—a disabled character whose physical difference is linked to moral depth rather than villainy, in contrast to figures like Richard III. Others note that Eliot still uses his disability instrumentally, as a signifier of sensitivity and as a plot device (his deformity makes him an "unsuitable" match for Maggie in the eyes of her family). Philip's role as the one who waits—patient, understanding, ultimately surpassed by the more virile Stephen—also reflects Victorian assumptions about masculinity and desirability.
