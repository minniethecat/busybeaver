# Justice

**Justice** in *The Mill on the Floss* operates on two registers that rarely align: the legal system that adjudicates property disputes, and the moral sense that demands fairness in human relations. Mr. Tulliver's lawsuit against Lawyer Wakem demonstrates how the pursuit of legal justice can destroy what it seeks to protect.

## Definition

Legal justice in the novel is represented by the courts that decide Mr. Tulliver's case—an impersonal machinery that reduces human conflict to property claims. Moral justice, by contrast, is the inner sense of right that characters invoke when legal outcomes feel wrong. Mr. Tulliver's demand that Tom "make him and his feel" the consequences of Wakem's victory (008-030) reveals how easily moral justice collapses into revenge.

## Context in the Novel

The lawsuit over water rights on the Floss is the event that sets the novel's tragic machinery in motion. Mr. Tulliver sues Wakem not merely for financial reasons but out of a sense of injured pride—his feeling that justice demands he prevail against a man he despises. When he loses, the family is plunged into bankruptcy, and the legal outcome generates a moral wound that festers for the remainder of the novel.

Tom internalizes this wound, dedicating himself to repaying the family debts and eventually regaining the mill—a form of justice that satisfies honor but leaves no room for mercy. His treatment of Maggie after her near-elopement with Stephen Guest shows justice untempered by sympathy: he judges her by an absolute standard and casts her out. The novel's ending, in which Tom and Maggie die together, can be read as a final, terrible form of justice—or as its transcendence.

## Key Passages

- **Mr. Tulliver's vow of revenge** (008-030): The demand that Wakem "shall feel" the consequences of the lawsuit—justice collapsed into personal vengeance.
- **The family's financial fall** (008-033): The insistence that property "must go out o' your own family" frames bankruptcy as moral as well as economic catastrophe.
- **Tom's filial duty as justice** (042-065): Tom resolves to repay the debts, embodying a narrow but genuine conception of justice.
- **Tom's harsh judgment** (042-059): Tom declares Maggie's "duty was clear enough"—justice without sympathy.
