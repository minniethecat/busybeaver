# The Tulliver Family

The Tullivers of Dorlcote Mill — Jeremy Tulliver, his wife Elizabeth (née Dodson), and their children Tom and Maggie — form the novel's central domestic unit, a family suspended between the Dodson clan's rigid respectability and the impulsive, emotionally-driven temperament of the Tulliver line. Their fortunes, and the tensions within and around them, drive the entire narrative arc of *The Mill on the Floss*.

## Definition

The Tulliver family represents a precarious social formation in the provincial world of St Ogg's. Unlike the Dodsons, whose identity is grounded in generational continuity, fixed habits, and material prudence [007-009], the Tullivers are defined by Mr. Tulliver's emotional impulsiveness and Mrs. Tulliver's anxious attachment to domestic propriety. The family is riven by competing temperaments: the father's "un-Dodson-like" tendency to act on feeling rather than calculation; the mother's fretful Dodson inheritance of valuing appearances; Tom's rigid, practical sense of justice; and Maggie's passionate, yearning nature that finds no adequate outlet in the family's narrow world [035-002]. As a unit, the Tullivers command "sincere pity" from the community after their ruin — yet Eliot's narrator makes clear that pity does not equal understanding [030-004].

## Context in the Novel

The family is established in the novel's opening chapters. Mr. Tulliver, the miller of Dorlcote Mill, is introduced as a man of strong feeling and limited foresight — "what I want is to give Tom a good education" becomes the fateful declaration that sets much of the plot in motion [003-002]. Mrs. Tulliver, born a Dodson, brings to the marriage a concern for linen, china, and the opinion of her sisters that contrasts sharply with her husband's indifference to social convention.

The family's internal dynamics are defined by a generational split. Mr. and Mrs. Tulliver, though deeply attached to their children, are "not apt to manifest their feelings" in ways the children can recognise [022-005]. Tom inherits his father's pride — "which springs from family pride" — and is "bent on being irreproachable" [039-003]. Maggie inherits her father's emotional intensity but without the practical channel that the mill provides for him.

Mr. Tulliver's bankruptcy is the family's defining catastrophe. The loss of Dorlcote Mill strips the Tullivers not only of their livelihood but of their social place. The children are thrust prematurely into adult responsibilities: Tom into the world of business, Maggie into the constrained existence of a fallen family's daughter. The aunts and uncles — Gleggs, Deanes, Pullets — gather to discuss the family's fate in scenes that reveal the Dodson code in operation: duty is owed, but never without calculation [009-023].

Eliot draws a sharp contrast between the Tulliver and Dodson temperaments. Where the Dodsons are "respectable for many generations" and governed by established habit [013-005], the Tullivers are governed by impulse — Mr. Tulliver's lawsuit against Wakem, his oath of vengeance sworn on the family Bible, Maggie's elopement — all represent a refusal or inability to calculate consequences that the Dodson mind would find incomprehensible. This temperament is the family's tragedy and its distinction.

## Key Passages

- **The Family as Object of Pity** ([030-004]): The community feels "sincere pity for the Tulliver family" — a phrase that captures both the genuineness of the sympathy and the social distance it implies.
- **The Dodson Contrast** ([007-009]): "It was not the practice of the Dodson family" to do things in particular ways — establishing the normative framework against which the Tullivers are measured and found wanting.
- **Unexpressed Affection** ([022-005]): Tom is not surprised that "his father and mother were not apt to manifest their feelings" — a quiet observation about the emotional economy of the Victorian provincial family.
- **Family Pride** ([039-003]): Tom's motivation is "family pride" — the same pride that animates his father's lawsuit and his oath, passed down and intensified across generations.
- **Generational Irksomeness** ([035-002]): "The peculiarities of his father and mother were very irksome to him" — Tom's adult perspective on the parents whose limitations shaped his own.

## Critical Interpretation

The Tulliver family has been read as Eliot's most sustained exploration of the tension between individual desire and familial obligation — a theme she would develop further in *Middlemarch*. Unlike the Dodsons, who present a united front of provincial respectability, the Tullivers enact the nineteenth-century family's internal contradictions: the gap between emotional need and material provision, between inherited identity and self-fashioning, between the claims of kin and the claims of conscience. The family's trajectory — from modest prosperity through catastrophic loss to partial, tragic restoration — mirrors the larger social transformations of Eliot's era, in which old certainties of class and occupation were being eroded by new economic forces.
