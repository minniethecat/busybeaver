# Park House

The impressive residence of the Deane family, situated on high ground above St. Ogg's, serving as the principal venue for the novel's social gatherings and a material symbol of the Dodson ideal of worldly success.

## Description

Park House is a "great place" of substantial size and grandeur, standing "high up out of the flood" on elevated ground — a detail that proves significant during the novel's climactic inundation [065-053][047-005]. The house features rooms opening into each other, decorated with "brilliant" lighting for evening entertainments [055-001]. Its late dinner hour marks the Deane household as following fashionable metropolitan custom [051-013].

## Social Significance

Park House is the centre of St. Ogg's social world as experienced by the aspiring mercantile class. Lucy Deane, raised in the house, is described as its natural ornament, and invitations to Park House are a marker of social acceptance [051-001]. The house is the setting for the dance at which Stephen Guest and Maggie Tulliver's mutual attraction becomes publicly visible — a turning point in the novel's romantic plot [055-001].

For the Dodson family, Park House represents their highest ideal: a prosperous household managed with exacting standards of respectability. Mrs. Deane's position as its mistress places her at the apex of the Dodson sisterhood's social hierarchy.

## Key Events at This Location

- Maggie Tulliver's visits to Park House with Lucy, where her shabby appearance is noted and contrasted with Lucy's elegance [047-102]
- The dinner party at Park House, attended by Mr. Stephen Guest, marking his entry into the Deane social circle [051-013]
- The dance at Park House where Stephen Guest's attraction to Maggie becomes evident to observers [054-062][055-001]
- Park House's elevated position saves it from the flood — a geographic detail that mirrors the Deane family's insulation from the Tullivers' tragedy [065-053]
