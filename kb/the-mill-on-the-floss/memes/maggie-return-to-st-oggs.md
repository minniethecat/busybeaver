# Maggie's Return to St Ogg's

Maggie Tulliver's return to St Ogg's after her boat trip with [[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]] is the novel's most painful passage — a study in social cruelty and moral courage. Having refused Stephen's proposal and returned alone, Maggie expects to explain herself and be understood. Instead, she finds that the community has already condemned her, and her own brother turns her from his door.

## Summary

After five days on the river with Stephen Guest, Maggie returns to St Ogg's alone — having refused both his marriage proposal and his entreaties to stay. She arrives at Tom's lodgings hoping for understanding, but Tom, interpreting her absence as proof of moral ruin, refuses to receive her. "You have disgraced us all," he tells her (054-018), and sends her to their mother. Maggie takes a lodging by the river, where only [[dr-kenn|Dr Kenn]] and [[bob-jakin|Bob Jakin]] show her kindness. The town's judgment is swift and merciless: respectable society closes its doors, and Maggie becomes the subject of gossip, speculation, and outright condemnation. The chapter "St Ogg's Passes Judgment" anatomises this communal verdict with devastating irony, exposing the gap between Maggie's actual innocence and the world's certainty of her guilt.

## Context

Maggie's return is the immediate consequence of the boat trip with Stephen Guest, her cousin Lucy's acknowledged suitor. Although Maggie has not consummated any relationship with Stephen — she refused him, and returned as soon as she could — the appearance of impropriety is all that St Ogg's requires. The episode dramatises the central tension of Maggie's life: her inner moral integrity, which no one can see, against the external judgment of a society that reads all actions through the harshest lens. Eliot's narrator anatomises this "world's wife" — the collective voice of respectable condemnation — with an irony that exposes its cruelty while acknowledging its implacable power.

- [[bob-jakin]] — the loyal friend who visits Maggie and brings news of the world outside
- [[mrs-tulliver]] — Maggie's mother, bewildered and unable to comprehend her daughter's disgrace

## Aftermath and Consequences

Maggie's ostracism is the prelude to the novel's final catastrophe. Her isolation by the river leaves her vulnerable to the flood that will reunite her with Tom in death. The social death she experiences — cut off from family, friends, and community — makes the physical death by drowning almost a release. Maggie returned "without a trousseau, without a husband," and "the world's wife" saw at once that her conduct "had been of the most aggravated kind" (055-021). Eliot's grim irony is that Maggie's return to St Ogg's, which she has chosen as the morally right path, is the very thing that destroys her.

## Literary Significance

The return to St Ogg's is the novel's most sustained examination of social judgment and gender double standards. Maggie is condemned — as Stephen is not — because she is a woman, and the community's verdict reflects the harsh moral economy of Victorian provincial life. Eliot's treatment of this episode refuses both sentimentality and cynicism: Maggie is neither a perfect martyr nor a fallen woman, but a complex human being crushed by forces she cannot control and a social code she has internalized even as it destroys her.
