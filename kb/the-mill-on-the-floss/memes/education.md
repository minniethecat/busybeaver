# Education

**Education** in *The Mill on the Floss* appears as a site of social reproduction and gender inequality. Tom Tulliver receives a classical education intended to raise his social standing, while Maggie's intellectual hunger is fed haphazardly through self-directed reading—a disparity that shapes both their fates.

## Definition

Eliot distinguishes between formal education (schooling, Latin grammar, the markers of gentlemanly status) and genuine learning (the cultivation of sympathy, insight, and moral understanding). Mr. Stelling's pedagogy exemplifies the former: he teaches Tom Latin and geometry without ever understanding the boy's mind or needs (016-003). Maggie's autodidacticism represents the latter—fragmentary but driven by real intellectual passion.

## Context in the Novel

Tom's education at Mr. Stelling's is one of the novel's most sustained social critiques. Mr. Tulliver sends Tom to be "made a scholarship of" in the belief that education will raise the family's status, but the education Tom receives is irrelevant to his actual life. Mr. Stelling's "duty was to teach the lad in the ordinary way" (016-009)—a formula that serves the institution, not the student.

Maggie, denied formal schooling because of her gender, reads everything she can find, from devotional works to novels. Her intellectual isolation mirrors her emotional isolation: she has no one with whom to share what she learns. The contrast between Tom's expensive, useless education and Maggie's hungry, unschooled intelligence is one of Eliot's sharpest feminist observations.

## Key Passages

- **Mr. Stelling's limited pedagogy** (016-003): Education reduced to routine, with no attention to the individual child.
- **Education as social performance** (016-009): Mr. Stelling's duty to "teach the lad in the ordinary way" prioritizes form over substance.
- **Maggie's love of reading** (005-006): Maggie "loved to linger" with books, showing her natural intellectual curiosity.
- **Gender and education** (016-015): The narrator observes the gap between what boys and girls are taught to value.
