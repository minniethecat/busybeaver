# Religion

**Religion** in *The Mill on the Floss* appears not as institutional doctrine but as a varied cultural force: the Dodsons' "simple, semi-pagan" traditionalism, Maggie's intense private spirituality, and the worldly pragmatism of the Anglican clergy.

## Definition

Eliot presents religion as a spectrum. At one end lies the Dodson family's unreflective traditionalism: "one sees little trace of religion, still less of a distinctively devotional or doctrinal" quality in their lives (034-002). Their "religion was of a simple, semi-pagan kind" (034-004)—a matter of custom rather than conviction. At the other end is Maggie's discovery of Thomas à Kempis's *The Imitation of Christ*, which offers her a doctrine of renunciation that transforms her suffering into spiritual discipline (036-037).

## Context in the Novel

Mr. Stelling, the clergyman-educator, represents religion as social institution: "he thought religion was a very excellent thing" (016-009) but treats it as one subject among others, subordinate to gentlemanly education. His failure to recognize Tom's emotional needs reveals the limits of institutional religion.

Maggie's religious awakening is the novel's most detailed treatment of spiritual experience. Reading à Kempis, she finds that "passion and dependence becomes religion" (036-037)—a vision of self-denial that speaks directly to her hunger for meaning. Yet Eliot is careful to show that this ascetic religion can become another form of self-deception, a way of avoiding rather than resolving life's difficulties.

The novel's ultimate religious vision, articulated through the narrator, is one where genuine religion includes rather than excludes natural feeling: a "high religion which excludes the striving" of human affection is incomplete (064-001).

## Key Passages

- **The Dodsons' semi-pagan religion** (034-004): The narrator characterizes Dodson piety as "simple, semi-pagan," rooted in custom rather than doctrine.
- **Maggie's spiritual awakening** (036-037): Reading à Kempis, Maggie finds that "passion and dependence becomes religion."
- **Religion as social form** (016-009): Mr. Stelling treats religion as "a very excellent thing" but subordinates it to social convention.
- **True religion includes feeling** (064-001): The narrator criticizes any "high religion which excludes the striving" of human affection.
