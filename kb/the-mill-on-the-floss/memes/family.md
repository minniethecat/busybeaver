# Family

The concept of **family** in *The Mill on the Floss* functions as the primary social structure through which characters define their identity, obligations, and moral worth. It is both a source of belonging and a site of conflict, as the competing claims of the Tulliver and Dodson family traditions pull characters—especially Maggie—in opposing directions.

## Definition

In the world of St. Ogg's, family is not merely a private bond but a public identity. The Dodsons in particular treat family as a standard of conduct: to act "as becomes a Dodson" is to uphold a code of prudence, propriety, and mutual obligation. Mrs. Glegg invokes family reputation as a moral absolute, judging every action by whether it reflects well or poorly on the clan (003-005). The Tullivers, by contrast, value emotional warmth over social performance, creating a tension that Maggie inherits and never fully resolves.

## Context in the Novel

Family shapes the novel's central conflicts at every level. The Tulliver family's financial downfall—Mr. Tulliver's lawsuit and bankruptcy—drives the plot of Books 3 and 4, transforming Tom from a schoolboy into the family's economic savior (008-033). The Dodson sisters (Mrs. Glegg, Mrs. Pullet, Mrs. Deane, and Mrs. Tulliver) embody different attitudes toward family obligation, from Mrs. Glegg's rigid formalism to Mrs. Tulliver's helpless domesticity (007-001).

Maggie's tragedy is fundamentally a family tragedy: she loves her brother Tom with an intensity that he cannot reciprocate on equal terms, and her desire for Philip Wakem—son of her father's enemy—places romantic attachment in direct conflict with family loyalty (008-030). Eliot's narrator traces how "family intercourse" can become a "painful difficulty" when individual temperament collides with collective expectation (005-006).

## Key Passages

- **Family as moral code** (003-005): Mrs. Glegg rebukes any suggestion of disrespect toward the family name, establishing the Dodson equation of family with moral standing.
- **The Dodson family gathering** (007-001): A family party becomes the stage for exposing tensions between the Dodson sisters, revealing family as both bond and battlefield.
- **Family pride and identity** (008-001): The narrator observes that the Dodsons "were certainly a handsome family," linking physical appearance to family pride and social position.
- **Family duty vs. individual desire** (008-030): The demand that property "must go out o' your own family" illustrates how family obligation overrides personal preference.
- **The pain of family conflict** (005-006): Eliot describes "a painful difficulty in their family intercourse," capturing how family love and family friction coexist.

## Critical Interpretation

> Family in *The Mill on the Floss* has been read by critics as Eliot's critique of provincial social structures that subordinate individual flourishing to collective identity. The Dodson code—with its emphasis on respectability, thrift, and keeping up appearances—represents a kind of secular religion that the narrator both satirizes and respects. Maggie's inability to reconcile her passionate nature with family expectations prefigures later Victorian novels' treatment of the "woman question."
