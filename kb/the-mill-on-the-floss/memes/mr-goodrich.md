# Mr. Goodrich

**Mr. Goodrich** is the drawing master at King's Lorton, engaged to teach [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]] drawing during his time at [[mr-stelling|Mr. Stelling's]] school.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Goodrich is introduced as "quite at the head of his profession within a circuit of twelve miles round King's Lorton" [019-002]. His appointment comes at the suggestion of [[mr-riley|Mr. Riley]], who advises Mr. Tulliver that Tom should learn drawing to prepare for a business career involving plans and maps [019-002].

The narrator uses Goodrich's teaching to satirise the educational standards of the period. Tom, whose "feeling for the picturesque in landscape was at present quite latent," finds that "Mr. Goodrich's productions seemed to him an uninteresting form of art" — brooks, rustic bridges, and ruins rendered with "a general softness of black-lead surface" rather than the dogs and donkeys Tom would have preferred to draw [019-002]. Through this detail, Eliot contrasts Tom's practical, detail-oriented mind with the genteel but irrelevant accomplishments offered by provincial education.
