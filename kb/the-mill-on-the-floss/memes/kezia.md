# Kezia

Kezia is the housemaid of the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] household at [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]], a frank, warm-hearted servant whose rough kindness and habitual scolding provide a steady domestic backdrop to the turbulent lives of [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] and [[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]. Though a minor character, she appears at pivotal moments across the narrative, bridging the downstairs world of household labour and the intimate crises of the family she serves.

## Role in the Novel

Kezia functions as a domestic constant — present from the novel's early chapters through to the aftermath of Mr [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]]'s financial ruin. She is introduced in Book 1 as part of the household scenery: her name first surfaces when "Kezia’s got 'em hanging at the fire" in the bustle over the best bed [003-010]. But her role deepens as she becomes an intermediary in the emotional life of the [[childhood|children]]. She is at once scolding and protective, the voice of practical necessity cutting through the [[childhood|children]]'s [[love|passion]]s. When [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] locks herself in the attic in a rage after cutting her hair, it is Kezia who is sent to coax her down [008-074] [008-075] [008-076]. Kezia's blend of gruffness and fondness makes her one of the novel's most credible minor figures — a servant who knows her place yet speaks her mind.

George Eliot gives Kezia the memorable epithet "the good-hearted, bad-tempered Kezia" [029-002], a compressed characterisation that captures the servant's dual nature. She is good-hearted enough to stay with the ruined family, returning at three o'clock to prepare the house for the family's arrival after the [[justice|bankruptcy]]; she is bad-tempered enough to grumble while doing it [029-002] [029-005].

## Character Analysis

Kezia's character is built on a foundation of loyalty that outlasts prosperity. Before the [[justice|bankruptcy]], she is the bustling domestic who "found the heat of the fire a great deal too much for her constitution" [007-001], a detail that humanises her with a note of comic self-awareness. After the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]]s lose [[dorlcote-mill|the mill]], however, Kezia remains — marking her as one of the few characters whose attachment to the family is not contingent on their worldly standing.

Her interactions with the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] [[childhood|children]] reveal the texture of her relationship to the family. When [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]], in a fit of self-[[shame|mortification]] after the hair-cutting incident, refuses to come down to dinner, Kezia's response is a mixture of exasperation and tenderness:

> "Well, Miss, you'll do as you like," said Kezia, going out again. But she had not betrayed the reason of Maggie's refusal to the family [008-078] [008-087].

This quiet discretion — choosing not to expose the child's shame to the dinner table — shows a delicacy of feeling that the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]]s' relatives often lack. Later, when Mrs [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] is incapacitated by grief after the [[justice|bankruptcy]], it is Kezia who is called upon: "Ask Kezia to fetch her down, and keep her out of harm's way" [031-003]. And the narrator adds, with a note of dry approval, "Kezia was equal to the task" [031-004].

## Relationships

Kezia's most significant relationships are with the members of the [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] household:

- **[[maggie-tulliver|Maggie Tulliver]]**: Kezia is one of the few adults who deals directly with [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s emotional extremes. She scolds, cajoles, and protects, often all at once. After [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] cuts her hair, Kezia finds her and delivers the maternal rebuke that [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]]'s own mother is too weak to voice [008-074] [008-075] [008-076]. When Mr [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] jokingly asks "Why, what's the matter wi' the little wench? It's nought but a mouse you've picked up in the road, Kezia?" [008-089], the servant's silence preserves the child's [[pride|dignity]].

- **[[tom-tulliver|Tom Tulliver]]**: Kezia defers to [[tom-tulliver|Tom]] as "Master [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]" but is not afraid to speak plainly. After the [[justice|bankruptcy]], she objects to leaving the parlour at [[tom-tulliver|Tom]]'s request, a small assertion of her own judgment [029-005].

- **[[mrs-tulliver|Mrs Tulliver]]**: As the mistress she serves, Mrs [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] issues orders and Kezia executes them, but in moments of crisis the dependence reverses — it is Mrs [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] who needs Kezia to manage what she cannot [031-003].

- **[[mr-tulliver|Mr Tulliver]]**: The relationship is one of easy familiarity within hierarchy. Mr [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]] notices Kezia's absences and moods with the indifferent attention of a man who has always had servants.

## Key Scenes

- **The Best Bed** — Kezia is first mentioned in the domestic flurry of preparing the best bedroom for visitors [003-010].
- **The Hair-Cutting Aftermath** — In the novel's most sustained Kezia sequence, she confronts [[maggie-tulliver|Maggie]] after the hair-cutting, urges her to come to dinner, and chooses not to betray the child's shame to the family [008-074] [008-075] [008-076] [008-077] [008-078] [008-087] [008-089]. This is her longest and most revealing appearance.
- **The Return to [[dorlcote-mill|the Mill]] After Bankruptcy** — Kezia is already at work at three o'clock, preparing the house for the [[shame|disgrace]]d family's return. She grumbles but does the work, and her entrance "with very black looks" [029-039] registers the emotional temperature of the household [029-002] [029-005].
- **Managing Mrs [[mr-tulliver|Tulliver]]** — After the ruin, Kezia is asked to "fetch her down, and keep her out of harm's way," a charge she handles with characteristic competence [031-003] [031-004].

## Critical Reception

Kezia belongs to the long [[custom|tradition]] of the loyal family servant in Victorian fiction — a type that includes Mrs Dean in *Wuthering Heights* and Mrs Wilson in *Mary Barton*. What distinguishes Kezia is Eliot's refusal to sentimentalise her. She is not a saintly figure of lower-class wisdom but a recognisable human being, irritable and devoted in roughly equal measure. The epithet "good-hearted, bad-tempered" [029-002] is a miniature example of Eliot's commitment to psychological realism even in her minor characters.
