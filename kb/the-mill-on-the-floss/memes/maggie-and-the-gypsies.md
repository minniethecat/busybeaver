# Maggie Runs Away to the Gypsies

The episode in which **Maggie Tulliver** runs away to the [[gypsies-in-the-mill-on-the-floss|gypsies]] is one of the novel's most vivid childhood scenes — a rebellion at once comic and poignant that introduces the central tension of Maggie's character: her longing for a world larger than the one she inhabits, and her painful discovery that escape is never as simple as imagination promises.

## Summary

Angry at her family and convinced that no one values her, the young Maggie decides to run away to the gypsies, whom she imagines as a free and noble people who will welcome her as their queen. She walks to the common where the gypsies are camped, expecting to be recognised immediately as "a little lady" (012-004). Instead, she finds herself ignored, uncomfortable, and increasingly frightened. The gypsies are indifferent to her pretensions; her attempts to instruct them in the use of a washing-basin are met with bewilderment (012-007). Within hours, her romantic fantasy collapses into the grim reality of hunger, cold, and the realisation that "Tom must be right about the gypsies; they must certainly be thieves" (012-032). Tom finds her and brings her home, and the episode passes into family lore as "that business of her running away to the gypsies" (016-088).

## Context

The gypsy episode occurs when Maggie is still a child, living at [[dorlcote-mill|Dorlcote Mill]] under the indifferent affection of her family. Tom has gone to school, leaving Maggie without her emotional anchor. Her father's affection is unreliable; her mother and aunts value only conventional feminine behaviour, which Maggie cannot produce. The gypsies represent everything the Dodsons are not: wildness, freedom, the romance of the outsider.

The episode is both a piece of realistic childhood psychology — Maggie's ideas about gypsies "had undergone a rapid modification" on contact with reality (012-036) — and a miniature of her later story. The pattern of imagination leading to escape, escape leading to disillusionment, and disillusionment leading to return will be replayed on a tragic scale in her adult relationship with [[stephen-guest|Stephen Guest]].

## Key Passages

- **The resolution to flee** (012-001): Maggie decides she "would run away and go to the gypsies, and Tom should never see her any more" — the child's ultimate threat given narrative form.
- **Arrival among the gypsies** (012-004): "Just what Maggie expected; the gypsies saw at once that she was a little lady" — the comic irony of Maggie's self-deception, framed by the narrator's gentle mockery.
- **The washing-basin instruction** (012-007): Maggie "instructing the gypsies to use a washing-basin" — the Dodson impulse to domestic improvement meeting the resistance of an alien world.
- **The fantasy collapses** (012-032): Maggie's realisation that "Tom must be right about the gypsies" — the return to her brother's moral framework as the safe harbour she tried to flee.
- **Family memory** (016-088): The episode is recalled in adulthood as "that business of her running away to the gypsies" — diminished by time into anecdote, yet resonant with the themes of her life.
