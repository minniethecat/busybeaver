# Mr. Jacobs

Mr. Jacobs — familiarly known to his pupils as "Old Goggles" for his habit of wearing spectacles — is Tom Tulliver's first schoolmaster, running a modest academy that represents the limited educational opportunities available to provincial children in the novel's world. Though never appearing directly in the narrative, his reputation and the contrast between his school and Mr. Stelling's more ambitious establishment at King's Lorton illuminates the novel's critique of early nineteenth-century education.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Jacobs serves as a narrative foil to the Rev. Walter Stelling. Where Mr. Jacobs's academy offered Tom a world in which "life had not presented itself to him as a difficult problem" — a place of play, fighting, and easy precedence — Mr. Stelling's classical curriculum plunges Tom into confusion and humiliation [016-001]. Mr. Tulliver's conviction that "Jacobs at th' academy's no parson, and he's done very bad by the boy" becomes the motivation for sending Tom to a clergyman-schoolmaster, a decision shaped by the era's assumption that clergymen made the best educators [008-108]. The contrast between the two schoolmasters — one a "snuffy old hypocrite" who can "spout 'my name is Norval' without bungling," the other a learned but unimaginative clergyman — suggests that neither model of education truly serves the child.

## Character Analysis

Eliot sketches Mr. Jacobs through Tom's retrospective comparison. At the academy, Tom enjoyed the kind of rough-and-tumble hierarchy governed by physical prowess rather than Latin declensions: "Tom being good at all active games,—fighting especially,—had that precedence among them which appeared to him inseparable from the personality of Tom Tulliver" [016-001]. Mr. Jacobs himself "imposed no painful awe" — a sharp contrast to the intimidating Mr. Stelling, whose very dinner-table questions reduce Tom to terrified monosyllables.

The narrator's wry description of Mr. Jacobs as a "snuffy old hypocrite" who writes "like copperplate" and surrounds his signature "with arabesques" captures the pretensions of the petty provincial schoolmaster — a type familiar to Victorian readers. Tom's naive conclusion that he "was not going to be a snuffy schoolmaster" but "a substantial man, like his father" is one of the illusions that King's Lorton will cruelly dissipate [016-001].

## Key Scenes

- **Mr. Tulliver's assessment** [008-108]: Mr. Tulliver declares Jacobs "no parson" and blames him for Tom's inadequate education, setting in motion the decision to send Tom to Mr. Stelling at Midsummer.
- **Tom's recollection of the academy** [016-001]: The narrator contrasts Tom's easy life at Jacobs's academy — where he was top of the boys' hierarchy — with the misery of his first quarter at King's Lorton, establishing the novel's critique of classical education.
