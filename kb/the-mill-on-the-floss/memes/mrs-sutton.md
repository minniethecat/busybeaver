# Mrs. Sutton

Mrs. Sutton is a recently deceased resident of St Ogg's parish, the wealthiest person in the neighbourhood. Though she never appears in the novel's present action, her death, will, and fortune provide a window into the social and moral preoccupations of the [[dodson-family|Dodson family]] — a world in which property, inheritance, and the medical drama of decline and death are subjects of inexhaustible interest.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Sutton exists entirely as a figure of memory and conversation. Her death and testamentary arrangements are discussed in two contexts: first, in [[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]]'s characteristically tearful meditation on mortality, and second, as a benchmark of medical attention and social consequence. The conversation about her will reveals the tension in provincial society between family obligation and testamentary freedom — a tension that resonates with the larger theme of inheritance and debt that shapes the [[tulliver-family|Tulliver family]]'s fate.

Mrs. Sutton is introduced through Mrs. Pullet's lamentation over the universality of death, in which she identifies Mrs. Sutton's fatal dropsy as a particularly dreadful example:

> "I know as it's old Mrs Sutton o' the Twentylands. ... there isn't another such a dropsy in the parish." [008-025]

[[mr-pullet|Mr. Pullet]] supplies the material facts, noting her exceptional wealth: "Mrs Sutton didn't die without making her will. ... Ours is a rich parish, but they say there's nobody else to leave as many thousands behind 'em as Mrs Sutton" [008-032].

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Sutton is not a character in any conventional sense — she is a narrative absence that generates social meaning. Her will is the key detail: she "left no leggicies to speak on,—left it all in a lump to her husband's nevvy" [008-032]. This nephew, as Mrs. Pullet reports, is "troubled with the asthmy, and goes to bed every night at eight o'clock" — an invalid who is "quite a gentleman sort o' man" [008-034]. Mrs. Pullet's account of her conversation with the nephew — he told her, "Mrs Pullet, I can feel for you" [008-034] — reveals the Dodsons' characteristic blending of medical preoccupation with social curiosity, grief with gossip.

After her death, Mrs. Sutton becomes a measure of medical consequence. [[mr-pullet|Mr. Pullet]] consoles his wife by noting, "Doctor Turnbull hasn't got such another patient as you i' this parish, now old Mrs Sutton's gone" [010-064], effectively elevating [[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]] to Mrs. Sutton's recently vacated position as the parish's most important invalid. This is Dodson logic at its purest: status is measured not in achievements but in the quality of one's complaints and the prominence of one's medical attendants.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-pullet|Mrs. Pullet]]** — The principal narrator of Mrs. Sutton's story, whose tearful account of the death blends genuine feeling with social performance.
- **[[mr-pullet|Mr. Pullet]]** — Who supplies the legal details of the will and later uses Mrs. Sutton's death to console his wife about her own medical importance.
- **[[mr-turnbull|Mr. Turnbull]]** — The doctor who attended both Mrs. Sutton and Mrs. Pullet, his professional status serving as a measure of his patients' social consequence.

## Key Scenes

- **The Announcement of Death** ([008-025]–[008-034]): Mrs. Pullet, in the midst of a general lamentation on mortality, identifies Mrs. Sutton's dropsy as the parish's most distinguished death. The conversation pivots from grief to the details of the will, revealing the Dodson talent for transforming all subjects — even death — into occasions for social and financial calculation.
- **The Consolation of Mr. Pullet** ([010-064]): Mr. Pullet comforts his wife after Maggie's hair-cutting incident by assuring her that Dr. Turnbull now has no patient more important than her in the parish "now old Mrs Sutton's gone" — a compliment whose terms capture the Dodson conflation of medical attention with social standing.
