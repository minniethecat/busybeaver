# Renunciation

**Renunciation** is the ascetic doctrine that Maggie Tulliver embraces as a way to master her passionate nature. Derived from Thomas à Kempis's *The Imitation of Christ*, it offers Maggie a vision of self-conquest through the denial of desire—but Eliot's treatment reveals its psychological ambiguity as both genuine moral growth and a form of emotional evasion.

## Definition

Renunciation, as Maggie practices it, is the deliberate suppression of personal desire in favor of duty to others. Unlike the Dodson code of external propriety, Maggie's renunciation is inward and spiritual: she seeks not merely to act correctly but to extinguish the very wishes that lead to transgression. The narrator traces how "passion and dependence becomes religion" for Maggie (036-037), suggesting that renunciation channels rather than eliminates her intense emotional nature.

## Context in the Novel

Maggie discovers à Kempis during the family's financial ruin, when her world has collapsed and she desperately needs a framework for making sense of suffering. The doctrine gives her a way to be "good" that doesn't depend on Tom's approval or social acceptance. She attempts to "think away all my duty" by suppressing her natural desires entirely (038-021).

But Eliot is clear-eyed about renunciation's limits. Philip Wakem recognizes it as unsustainable—a denial of Maggie's essential nature. The novel's later books show Maggie struggling with the return of desires she thought she had conquered, culminating in her near-escape with Stephen Guest. Renunciation, Eliot suggests, cannot substitute for the harder work of integrating desire and duty.

## Key Passages

- **Maggie's spiritual discovery** (036-037): Reading à Kempis, Maggie transforms "passion and dependence" into religion.
- **The attempt to renounce** (038-021): Maggie confesses she tried to "think away all my duty," revealing renunciation's psychological cost.
- **Tom's narrow judgment** (042-059): Tom declares Maggie's "duty was clear enough," opposing his external standard to her internal struggle.
- **The limits of self-denial** (058-003): The narrator warns against "maiming the faith and sympathy" that renunciation can damage.
