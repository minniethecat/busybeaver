# Jefferson's Hundred-Dollar Reward

[[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]]'s startlingly generous gift of one hundred dollars to each of the Hardy boys during their first face-to-face meeting, revealing his true character and deepening the mystery of the letter.

## Summary

When the boys finally meet [[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]] in person, he is "advancing toward them, his hand outstretched" (005-003) — a warm welcome that contrasts sharply with the cold tone of the permission letter. [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] "referred to a reward" (005-009) and then surprises the boys by handing each of them "a hundred-dollar bill" (005-012). The boys receive the money "with surprise" — the amount is staggeringly generous for the era, demonstrating both [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s wealth and his genuine appreciation for the boys' spirit. The reward cements [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] as a benefactor figure and raises the stakes of the mystery: someone this generous deserves to have his stamp collection returned.
