# Permission Granted

The meeting between the Hardy boys and [[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]] at his [[shore-road|Shore Road]] residence transforms the cryptic [[strange-note|note]] into a concrete mission. [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] not only grants the boys permission to camp on [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]] but also offers a reward that raises the stakes of the mystery.

## The Meeting

On the afternoon specified in the note, "the Hardy boys set out for the handsome Jefferson home on the Shore Road" (004-040). They are greeted by a servant and shown into a library where [[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]], an elderly antique dealer, awaits them.

The atmosphere is formal but not intimidating. [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] reveals that he knows of the boys through their earlier detective success: "we recovered his automobile for him from the Shore Road thieves" (004-003). The boys' reputation precedes them.

## The Proposition

[[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s business is direct. He owns [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]], a lonely, uninhabited island in [[cabin-cove|Cabin Cove]], but a mysterious man named [[hanleigh|Hanleigh]] has been pressuring him to sell. [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] has refused — "not for sale at any price, Mr. Hanleigh, and that settles it" (004-083) — but [[hanleigh|Hanleigh]]'s persistence worries him.

The proposition is straightforward: the boys may use [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]] for their winter camping trip, and if they can discover why [[hanleigh|Hanleigh]] is so determined to acquire the island, [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] will pay them a hundred dollars:

> "I am prepared to pay a hundred dollars to anyone who can solve the mystery of Cabin Island." (005-007)

## The Significance of the Reward

The hundred-dollar reward — a substantial sum in 1929 — transforms what might have been a casual camping trip into a professional engagement. The boys are not merely vacationing; they are employed, in effect, as private investigators. The reward also establishes [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] as a serious patron who values the boys' skills enough to pay for them.

Moreover, the reward creates a formal obligation. The boys now have a client — and with a client comes the responsibility to deliver results. This early experience of professional detective work, with its blend of adventure and accountability, foreshadows the careers that [[frank-hardy|Frank]] and [[joe-hardy|Joe]] will eventually pursue.

## Permission as a Weapon

[[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s permission serves a strategic purpose he may not fully intend. By granting the boys legal right to be on [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]], he arms them against [[hanleigh|Hanleigh]]'s intimidation. When [[hanleigh|Hanleigh]] later confronts them, [[frank-hardy|Frank]] can respond with the authority of the island's owner: "You have no right to order us off this island" (008-031). The permission is not just a courtesy — it is a legal shield.
