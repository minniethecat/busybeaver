# The Fox Hunt

During their stay on [[Cabin Island]], [[Frank Hardy]] and [[Joe Hardy]] organized a foxhunt to eliminate the foxes that had been raiding [[Amos Grice]]'s henhouse, [[theft|stealing]] his chickens. The successful hunt cemented their friendship with the storekeeper, whose knowledge of the island and its mysteries proved invaluable to their [[detective-work|investigation]].

## Summary

While visiting [[Amos Grice]]'s store after recovering their stolen supplies, the Hardy boys learned that foxes had been raiding the storekeeper's henhouse at night, making off with seven of his chickens (015-041). Grice was distraught over the losses, and [[frank-hardy|Frank]] and [[joe-hardy|Joe]] saw an opportunity to repay his kindness. They proposed a foxhunt, an idea that Grice welcomed enthusiastically, even providing [[chet-morton|Chet]] with a rifle (015-055). The boys tracked the foxes through the deep snow near the island's woods, with [[biff-hooper|Biff Hooper]]'s marksmanship proving decisive: he shot the lead fox, while [[joe-hardy|Joe]] brought down a second (015-094). They returned to Grice's store with the two fox carcasses, earning his profound gratitude and the assurance that his remaining chickens were safe.

## Participants

- [[Frank Hardy]]: Planned the hunt and tracked the foxes through the snow
- [[Joe Hardy]]: Shot the second fox as it fled through the underbrush (015-097)
- [[Biff Hooper]]: Fired the decisive shot that brought down the lead fox (015-094)
- [[Chet Morton]]: Accompanied the party and was provided a rifle by [[amos-grice|Amos Grice]], though his role was limited (015-055)
- [[Amos Grice]]: The storekeeper whose chickens were being stolen; provided rifles and gratefully received the foxes (015-054)

## Location

The foxhunt took place in the woods and snow-covered terrain surrounding [[Amos Grice]]'s store, near [[Cabin Island]] (015-062). The boys followed the foxes' trail from the henhouse out into the deeper brush.

## Narrative Significance

The foxhunt served a dual purpose in the narrative. Functionally, it eliminated a nuisance that was threatening Grice's livelihood, earning the boys a grateful and loyal local informant. Strategically, it deepened their connection to the island community — Grice would later prove crucial to understanding the mystery of the stolen [[Bender Stamp Collection|stamp collection]] and the history of [[Cabin Island]]. The hunt also demonstrated the boys' resourcefulness and marksmanship, reinforcing their capability as young investigators operating in a harsh winter environment.

## Lead-up

When the boys visited [[Amos Grice]]'s store to report that they had recovered their stolen supplies, Grice told them of a more pressing concern: foxes had been raiding his henhouse. "Stole a lot of my chickens," he lamented. "Seven fine fat chickens I lost" (015-041–042). He explained that he was not the first to lose chickens that winter (015-047). The boys, eager to repay Grice for his earlier help with their supplies, immediately offered to hunt the foxes.

> "And if you can drill them two foxes I'll be mighty grateful to you," said Amos Grice (015-054).

## Aftermath

The boys returned to Grice's store with the two dead foxes, and the storekeeper was overjoyed. "When he saw the two foxes lying in the snow, he rubbed his hands together with satisfaction" (015-112). Grice declared that he could now sleep peacefully knowing his chickens were safe (015-115). The boys cut the brushes from the foxes as trophies before returning to [[Cabin Island]] (015-122). The goodwill generated by this act would later prove valuable when Grice shared crucial information about the island's mysterious history.
