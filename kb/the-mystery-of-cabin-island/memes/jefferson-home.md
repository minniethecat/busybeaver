The Jefferson Home is the residence of [[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]], the wealthy antique dealer and stamp collector whose stolen [[bender-stamp-collection|stamp collection]] lies at the heart of the [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]] mystery. Located on [[shore-road|Shore Road]] in [[bayport|Bayport]], the mansion is the setting for the boys' crucial first meeting with [[elroy-jefferson|Mr. Jefferson]] — an interview that sets the entire [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]] expedition in motion.

## Description

The Hardy boys' first impression of the Jefferson home emphasizes its imposing, somber character:

> The place was not far away, and as they trudged along through the crisp, crunching snow, they speculated upon the mysterious note they had received. (004-040)

The house itself is described as "a large, gloomy mansion, set back some distance from the road" (004-069). Its size and architecture mark [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] as a man of considerable wealth, while its "gloomy" aspect hints at the sadness that hangs over its owner — the loss of his treasured [[bender-stamp-collection|stamp collection]] years before.

The interior includes at least a library where [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] receives visitors, and the grounds include a driveway and approach from [[shore-road|Shore Road]].

## Key Events

### The Mysterious Note

The boys' first connection to the Jefferson home comes through a letter: "Messrs. Frank and Joseph Hardy, Bayport. Dear Sirs: If it is convenient for you to call upon me at my home on Shore Road this afternoon, I should like to discuss a matter of business with you" (004-001). The formal, vaguely legal tone puzzles the boys — they have no idea what business [[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]] could have with them.

### The First Interview

[[frank-hardy|Frank]] and [[joe-hardy|Joe]] visit the Jefferson home on a crisp winter afternoon (004-040). During this interview, [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]] reveals both his permission for the boys to camp on [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]] and his offer of a [[jefferson-reward|hundred-dollar reward]] for the recovery of his stolen stamps [005-039] [005-045].

[[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s questions about the boys' detective father, [[fenton-hardy|Fenton Hardy]], suggest that he sought them out specifically because of their family's investigative reputation. The interview transforms what had been [[chet-morton|Chet]]'s casual suggestion of a camping trip into a formal expedition with a hidden agenda.

### Connection to the Stamp Theft

The Jefferson home is the origin point of the central mystery: it was from this house that the [[bender-stamp-collection|stamp collection]] was stolen years before by [[john-sparewell|John Sparewell]], a servant in [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s employ [011-055]. The theft from this "large, gloomy mansion" set in motion the chain of events that would culminate in the discovery of the [[rosewood-box|rosewood box]] hidden in [[the-chimney|the chimney]] on [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]].

### Return of the Stamps

At the story's conclusion, the stamps are returned to their rightful owner at the Jefferson home. [[elroy-jefferson|Jefferson]]'s joy at recovering his collection — worth "a small fortune" (024-063) — brings the mansion's gloomy atmosphere to a brighter resolution.

## Significance

The Jefferson Home represents the starting point and final destination of the novel's mystery plot. The stamps were stolen from this house, the boys' mission is authorized from this house, and the stamps ultimately return to this house. Its physical description — large, gloomy, set back from the road — mirrors [[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]]'s own character: wealthy but isolated, impressive but marked by loss.
