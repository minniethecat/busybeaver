The chimney is the central architectural feature of the old cabin on [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]] and the climactic hiding place of the [[rosewood-box|rosewood box]] containing [[elroy-jefferson|Elroy Jefferson]]'s stolen [[bender-stamp-collection|stamp collection]]. It serves as both a narrative focal point and the ultimate objective of the Hardy boys' [[detective-work|investigation]].

## Description

The chimney is described as a "great stone chimney" [009-014] and "the big chimney" [019-051], built of mortared stone and brick. It is a massive structure that dominates the cabin's main room, with a fireplace opening large enough for a person to climb into [018-075]. The chimney rises through the roof of the cabin and is tall enough to be referenced as a landmark visible from outside.

Despite its imposing construction, the chimney is old and has weathered years of exposure to the elements. This structural vulnerability becomes a critical plot point when a hurricane-lashed tree strikes it, causing a partial collapse [023-002] [023-005].

## Role in the Mystery

### The Cipher's Target

The chimney becomes the focus of the mystery when [[the-cipher|the cipher message]] left by [[hanleigh|Hanleigh]] is decoded. The message reads: "Cabin Island Chimney Left Front Nine Feet High" [018-031]. [[frank-hardy|Frank]] immediately recognizes its significance: "Don't you understand? This message refers to the chimney right in this very cabin. All we have to do now is examine a part of the chimney on the left-hand side, about nine feet up, somewhere at the front" [018-034].

### Initial Searches

The boys' early attempts to investigate the chimney are thorough but fruitless. [[frank-hardy|Frank]] climbs up inside the chimney, covering himself in soot while examining the interior with a flashlight [018-075] [018-076]. [[joe-hardy|Joe]] also makes an attempt, getting wedged in the narrow passage — "He reached up and seized one of Joe's feet. He tugged, but Joe was evidently firmly wedged in the chimney" [019-006]. Their failure to find anything initially leads to doubt about whether they had interpreted the cipher correctly [019-051].

### The Storm and Collapse

During a violent hurricane that strikes [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]], a tree blown over by the wind strikes the chimney. [[frank-hardy|Frank]] shouts the alarm: "The chimney is caving in!" [023-002]. The collapse is dramatic — stones and mortar crash down into the fireplace and living area, filling the main room with debris [023-005]. The boys narrowly escape injury by retreating to the kitchen.

### The Discovery

The destruction of the chimney proves to be the key to solving the mystery. After the storm passes, the boys clear the debris from the fireplace [024-005]. [[frank-hardy|Frank]] climbs up into the damaged chimney and finds that the collapse had dislodged a stone that had been mortared in place years before. Behind it was a hollow containing the [[rosewood-box|rosewood box]]:

> "I found it right at the place mentioned in the cipher. We didn't discover it before, because the box had been hidden in a hollow right in the middle of one of the stones, and it had been mortared up when they were building the chimney. The shaking-up the chimney got a little while ago had broken the mortar and dislodged the stone." [024-028]

## The Hiding Method

The method of concealment was ingenious: the [[rosewood-box|rosewood box]] was placed in a hollow carved into one of the chimney stones, then covered with mortar during the chimney's original construction. This made it essentially invisible to anyone examining the chimney from the outside or inside — only the destruction of the stonework itself could reveal it. The cipher's reference to "nine feet high" and "left front" provided the approximate location, but the box could not be found until the collapse broke the mortar seal [024-028].

## Significance

The chimney represents the final barrier between the Hardy boys and the solution to the [[cabin-island|Cabin Island]] mystery. Its physical stubbornness — yielding only to a force of nature rather than to careful searching — underscores a recurring theme in the Hardy Boys series: that thorough detective work must sometimes be supplemented by patience and circumstance. The chimney's collapse is the catalyst that transforms the boys from frustrated searchers into triumphant discoverers.
