# Operation Hildegarde

Operation Hildegarde was the [[chartered-uller-company|Chartered Uller Company]]'s emergency nuclear weapons development program, conducted during the siege of the [[konkrook|Konkrook]] Reservation. Faced with the discovery that King Orgzild of [[keegark|Keegark]] possessed operational fission weapons — and lacking any nuclear arsenal of its own on [[uller|Uller]] — the Company initiated a crash program to design, construct, and deploy counter-strike weapons before the Keegarkans could bring their bombs to bear.

## The Intelligence Crisis

The operation was triggered by the revelation that the Keegarkans had succeeded where no native power should have succeeded: they had built working nuclear weapons. The intelligence was gathered from multiple sources, including Gorkrink's [[niflheim|Niflheim]] connection — the Keegarkan prince had spent a year working alongside Dr. Murillo at the polar mines, where he had absorbed technical information about nuclear devices through Lingua Terra documents and conversations (013-006)(013-012).

The Company had no nuclear weapons on Uller. The Federation Navy maintained nuclear stockpiles, but the nearest arsenal was off-world — too distant to be delivered before the Keegarkans could launch their own strike. The Company's only option was to build its own bombs, immediately, using whatever materials and knowledge were available on the planet.

## The Romance Novel Source

The most extraordinary aspect of Operation Hildegarde was its primary technical source. Lacking classified nuclear weapons design documents — which would have required Federation authorization and a hyperwave transmission that the Keegarkans might intercept — the engineers turned to an unlikely alternative: a historical romance novel from Earth's atomic era that happened to include a detailed description of early fission bomb design (014-027)(014-030).

> "Separating the nuclear physics from the pornography." — an engineer describing the task of extracting bomb designs from a romance novel (014-038)

The novel, a work of popular fiction from a period when nuclear physics had captured the public imagination, contained enough accurate technical detail — interleaved with romantic plot elements — to serve as a design document. The line was grimly humorous but literally accurate: the team had to extract genuine engineering specifications from a work of fiction, cross-referencing each detail against their own technical knowledge to distinguish fact from authorial invention.

## Construction and Assembly

### Engineering Challenges

| Challenge | Solution | Source |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| No official bomb designs | Reverse-engineer from historical romance novel | 014-027, 014-030 |
| No specialized casing | Repurpose liquid-oxygen tank | 014-065 |
| No weapons-grade fissile material | Repurpose Company industrial plutonium stocks | — |
| No nuclear engineers on staff | General engineers work from first principles | — |
| Time pressure (active siege) | Crash program, parallel work streams | — |

The assembly process pushed the limits of the Company's engineering workforce. Nuclear weapons design is a specialized discipline requiring expertise in high-explosive lensing, neutron physics, and critical mass calculations — none of which were represented in the Company's standard engineering corps. The Hildegarde team worked from first principles, the romance novel, and desperation. That they succeeded at all is a testament to the quality of Terran technical education; that they succeeded in time is a testament to luck.

## Deployment

The bombs assembled under Operation Hildegarde were deployed in the bombing of Keegark, carried by the cutter *Elmoran* under [[carlos-von-schlichten|von Schlichten]]'s personal command, with [[paula-quinton|Paula Quinton]] falling in behind him as he boarded (015-011). Three devices were dropped over the Keegarkan capital, producing the nuclear detonations that killed approximately 150,000 Ullerans and ended the [[uller-uprising|Uller Uprising]] (015-034).

## Legacy

Operation Hildegarde occupies an ambiguous place in military history. Technically, it was a triumph of improvisational engineering — perhaps the most successful crash weapons program ever conducted outside of a major power's formal military-industrial complex. Morally, it was the program that made the bombing of Keegark possible, and therefore the program that enabled the most destructive single act of the Uller Uprising.

The operation also represents the ultimate convergence of the novel's themes: colonial improvisation, the Faustian bargain of technological transfer, and the thin line between civilization and barbarism. The same Company that scattered plutonium "like confetti" across Uller (013-016) found itself building nuclear weapons from a romance novel to destroy a city it had itself armed with the knowledge to threaten it. The circularity is the novel's darkest irony.
