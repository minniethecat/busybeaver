# Bwork

Bwork was a city on the eastern shore of [[uller|Uller]], one of the Free Cities in the [[takkad-sea|Takkad Sea]] region, and the site of a Chartered Uller Company Residency and trading station. During the [[uller-uprising|Uller Uprising]], Bwork fell to native forces early in the conflict, and its name later appeared in General [[carlos-von-schlichten|Carlos von Schlichten]]'s plans for nuclear escalation — initially as the site of a demonstration bombardment before the fateful decision to strike [[keegark|Keegark]] instead.

## Geography & Location

Bwork was situated on the eastern shore of Uller's equatorial zone, within the broader Takkad Sea cultural and political region. Its position on the eastern side of the planet made it a gateway for trade and communication with the interior territories. The city's location was referenced as a navigational waypoint during fleet operations — the *Sky-Spy* tracked the *Oom Paul Kruger* approaching "over her horizon to the east, from the direction of Bwork" (015-043), indicating the city's role as a known geographic reference point on the eastern approaches.

## The Bwork Residency

Like other Company outposts across Uller, Bwork hosted a Residency — a fortified trading post and administrative center staffed by Terran personnel. The Residency at Bwork fell during the early stages of the coordinated uprising. The Boer-class freighter *Oom Paul Kruger* "had last been reported at Bwork, and there was little chance that she had slipped into Keegark since the uprising had started" (014-040), and the text notes that the ship was last seen "before the Bwork Residency fell" — confirming that the native forces overwhelmed the Company presence there.

The fall of Bwork, along with trading stations on the eastern shore of the Takkad Sea, was part of the pattern of coordinated attacks that von Schlichten struggled to interpret in the uprising's opening hours. Some trading stations in the region resisted the rebellion — notably the south shore station whose native population refused to rise and instead "crucified five of Rakkeed's disciples" (015-043) — but Bwork was not among the holdouts.

## The Demonstration Bomb Plan

In the moral aftermath of the Keegark bombing, von Schlichten reflected on an alternative he had briefly considered: a demonstration strike on the uninhabited mountains behind Bwork. He recalled "considering just where, against the mountains back of Bwork, he would drop a demonstration bomb as a prelude to a surrender demand" (015-046). The thought was quickly dismissed as self-deception — a "drunkard's morning-after resolution" — since after the first nuclear weapon was used, "there is no turning back, any more than there had been after Hiroshima, four-hundred-and-fifty-odd years ago" (015-046).

The Bwork demonstration plan represented the path not taken: a show of force that might have compelled surrender without mass civilian casualties. But von Schlichten's ultimate conclusion was that such gradualism was a fantasy — nuclear weapons, once deployed, could not be calibrated for moral effect. The mountains behind Bwork remained unbombed, a monument to a fleeting moment of restraint that did not survive the strategic calculus of the uprising.
