# Company Rule

Company rule on Uller is a system of privatized colonial governance. The Chartered Uller Company exercises sovereign powers — maintaining armed forces, conducting foreign relations with native kingdoms, administering justice — while operating as a profit-seeking commercial enterprise accountable to stockholders and the Banking Cartel.

## The Chartered Company Model

The Company was originally formed to exploit Uller alone, but the Federation Government required it to also manage Niflheim operations as a condition of its charter. The Federation Space Navy had discovered and explored both planets, making them public domain under Federation law. The Company received an exclusive charter for economic development in exchange for assuming the costs and risks of colonial administration (004-025).

This public-private hybrid structure creates competing incentives. The Company must maintain enough order to sustain profitable operations while minimizing the overhead costs of military occupation and civil administration. Every soldier, every Administrator, every Residency building is a cost to the stockholders — and every cost reduction that weakens colonial control increases the risk of the uprising that eventually erupts.

## The Sovereignty Gap

Company Governors-General like Harrington and von Schlichten exercise powers that in any domestic Terran context would belong to elected governments: they command armed forces, negotiate treaties with foreign powers (the native kingdoms), and make life-and-death decisions affecting hundreds of thousands of subjects. Yet their formal accountability is to a corporate board of directors and, indirectly, to a Federation Government six months away by hyperdrive.

The Banking Cartel's influence over Company policy adds another layer of distortion. When the uprising threatens Company assets, "the losses the Banking Cartel are taking on this deal" become a factor in strategic calculations (010-013). Financial interests in Terra — investors who have never seen Uller or met an Ulleran — shape the decisions of commanders fighting for their lives on a planet 21 light-years from the stock exchange.

## Governance Through Native Intermediaries

The Company does not directly administer the native population. Instead, it governs through the existing political structure of native kingdoms — the Free Cities of the Konk Isthmus, each ruled by a king. The Company's Residents at each city function as a combination of ambassador, trade representative, and political officer, managing relationships with the local monarch.

This indirect rule model is cost-effective but fragile. It depends on the cooperation of native kings who have their own political ambitions. When King Orgzild of Keegark decides that the time is right to expel the Terrans, the Company's indirect governance structure provides no direct levers of control over his population — only the threat of military force.

## The Post-War Settlement

Von Schlichten's closing vision for Uller represents a fundamental revision of Company rule. The old model of indirect governance through native kings has failed catastrophically. His proposed alternative includes permanent orbital surveillance stations, dispersal of Residency locations to avoid concentration of Terran personnel, and the elevation of the Kragans from mercenary auxiliaries to proconsular administrators (015-046).

This vision keeps the Company's commercial operations intact while militarizing the political framework. It is Company rule 2.0 — still privatized, still profit-driven, but backed by overwhelming force and a network of native allies who have proven their loyalty in combat.

## Narrative Significance

Company rule is the novel's central political theme. Piper uses the corporate-colonial framework to explore questions of power without legitimacy, profit without responsibility, and governance without consent. The Company is not a villain — it is a system, one whose internal logic (minimize costs, maximize extraction) produces outcomes (brutal labor practices, fragile indirect rule, nuclear war) that no individual Company officer would have chosen.
