# Geek

A ubiquitous Terran racial slur for Ullerans, "geek" pervades Company discourse from enlisted men to senior officers, encapsulating the casual contempt that defines the colonial relationship between the Chartered Uller Company and the native population (L197, L222, L248).

## Etymology and Usage

The term "geek" is applied universally to Ullerans regardless of their social station, ethnicity, or political allegiance. Company personnel use it to refer to Konkrook's King Jaikark, his Prime Minister Gurgurk, common laborers, rebel soldiers, and even allied Kragan troops (L256, L296, L475). The word functions as a linguistic leveler that reduces the complex Ulleran civilization — with its multiple kingdoms, distinct ethnic groups, technological achievements, and sophisticated political structures — to a single contemptible category.

Von Schlichten himself, despite fifteen years on Uller and extensive interaction with native peoples, routinely uses "geek" in both private speech and direct address. In combat, he shouts at an Ulleran attacker: "*Suddabit* yourself, you geek bastard!" (L272). Governor-General Harrington uses it casually in policy discussions: "The more you do for these damned geeks, the more they expect from you" (L222). Even Paula Quinton, the sociographic researcher ostensibly studying Ulleran culture, adopts the term in crisis: "those geeks grabbed you" (L360).

The term is so embedded in Company culture that it appears in official chapter titling — Chapter III is titled "Four-and-Twenty Geek Heads" (L484, L488) — and in the naming of Company equipment, most notably the "geek-speaker" (enunciator, Ulleran).

## Social and Political Implications

The pervasive use of "geek" reflects and reinforces the power asymmetry between Terrans and Ullerans. Company personnel never use Ulleran self-designations or ethnic identifiers; the term erases the distinctions between Konkrookans, Keegarkans, Kragans, Zirks, Jeels, Skilkans, and the numerous other Ulleran peoples. This linguistic erasure parallels the Company's political strategy of treating all Ullerans as interchangeable subjects rather than recognizing their separate sovereignties and interests.

The term's casualness is particularly revealing. It is not a weapon wielded in anger but a default descriptor, used even in moments of cooperation or grudging respect. A Terran soldier can describe an Ulleran as "a damn good worker" in one breath and call him a geek in the next (L197). This contradiction — functional respect for individual capability combined with categorical contempt for species identity — captures the fundamental instability of the Company's colonial project.

The Ullerans themselves are keenly aware of the term's meaning. While the novel rarely provides direct Ulleran perspective on Terran language, the political exploitation of anti-Terran sentiment by figures like Rakkeed demonstrates that the native population understands precisely how the Company regards them. The slur "geek" is the linguistic counterpart to the material realities of forced labor, economic exploitation, and military subjugation.

## Narrative Significance

"Geek" operates as a constant tonal undercurrent throughout *Uller Uprising*, signaling to the reader that the Terrans are not benevolent administrators but colonial occupiers who hold the native population in contempt. The term's ubiquity undermines any reading of the Company as a civilizing force and casts the Ulleran uprising not as a betrayal of grateful subjects but as a predictable response to systematic degradation.

The novel's narrative sympathy lies with von Schlichten, yet his unreflective use of "geek" complicates that sympathy. His casual racism is not an aberration but the norm; the reader is forced to recognize that the protagonist shares the prejudices of the system he serves. This moral complexity — a hero who is also a bigot — is one of the novel's more sophisticated narrative achievements.

The chapter title "Four-and-Twenty Geek Heads" (L484) crystallizes the term's function: Ulleran lives are reduced to trophies, their deaths counted and displayed as tokens of Terran power. The number — four and twenty — echoes historical punitive displays, connecting the Company's rule on Uller to older traditions of colonial violence.
