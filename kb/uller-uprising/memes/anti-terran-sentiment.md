# Anti-Terran Sentiment

A pervasive current of hostility toward the Terran colonial presence on Uller, expressed through religious revivalism, economic resentment, and organized political conspiracy — most visibly channeled through the messianic preacher Rakkeed and his rallying cry, "*znidd suddabit*" ("death to the Terrans") (002-029, 004-022).

## Sources of Resentment

Anti-Terran sentiment draws from multiple sources. The northern merchants resent the Company for "spoiling the caravan-trade" and undercutting "their local business, because the land-owning barons, who used to deal with them, are now dealing directly with us" (004-022). This economic displacement creates a natural constituency for anti-Company agitation. The urban bourgeoisie in the equatorial kingdoms similarly chafe under Company dominance, though their grievances are more political than commercial.

The feudal nobility have their own reasons for discontent. King Firkked of Skilk "was afraid his feudal nobility is going to try to force a Runnymede on him, so he's been currying favor with the urban merchants; that makes him as pro-Rakkeed and as anti-Terran as they are" (004-022). Thus anti-Terranism becomes a tool of internal Ulleran politics, with kings and nobles maneuvering for advantage by positioning themselves relative to the Company.

## Rakkeed and Religious Mobilization

Rakkeed, a messianic preacher, transformed diffuse anti-Terran grievance into an organized movement with the battle cry "*znidd suddabit*" (004-022). His disciples included figures like Keeluk, and his influence reached from Konkrook to the Arctic mining regions. The movement's religious character gave it reach beyond political factions: "Everywhere we turn, Rakkeed's at the bottom of whatever happens to be wrong" (002-029).

Von Schlichten's intelligence network tracked "Skilkans known to be Rakkeed disciples and rabidly anti-Terran" (003-011), linking the religious movement to specific individuals and networks. The letters carried by Keeluk's contacts contained gossip about "friends and parishioners which might have arbitrary code-meanings" (003-011), suggesting an organized communication network operating under the cover of religious correspondence.

## Gurgurk's Exploitation

The Konkrookan Prime Minister Gurgurk saw anti-Terran sentiment as a political weapon rather than an ideological commitment. Blount's analysis was that "Gurgurk's plan is something like this: Rakkeed will stir up anti-Terran sentiment here in Konkrook, and direct it against our puppet, Jaikark, as well as against us. When the outbreak comes, Jaikark will be killed, and then Gurgurk will step in, seize the Palace, and use the Royal army to put down the revolt that he's incited in the first place" (002-029).

This cynical manipulation — inciting anti-Terran violence to eliminate a rival and then suppressing it to claim the role of the Company's friend — illustrates how anti-Terranism functioned as a currency in Ulleran power politics, valuable precisely because it could be spent and then disavowed.

## Narrative Significance

Anti-Terran sentiment is the fuel of the novel's central conflict — the Uller uprising. It is neither irrational xenophobia nor a simple morality play of colonized versus colonizer. The Company's economic disruption of traditional Ulleran trade creates real grievances; Ulleran elites exploit those grievances for power; religious figures like Rakkeed give them transcendent meaning. The result is a political ecosystem in which violence against Terrans is simultaneously a genuine expression of native resistance and a tool manipulated by native princes who care little for the cause itself.

Von Schlichten's response to the uprising — culminating in nuclear bombing of Keegark — is haunted by his knowledge that the sentiment driving it is partly of the Company's own making: "the next geek potentate who tries to organize an anti-Terran conspiracy, or the next crazy caravan-driver who preached *znidd suddabit*, will be lynched on the spot" (015-058). Brutality becomes a deliberate deterrence strategy, not merely retaliation.
