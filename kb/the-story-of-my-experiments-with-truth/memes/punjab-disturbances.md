# Punjab Disturbances of 1919

The Punjab disturbances of 1919 encompassed the cycle of protest, repression, and investigation that followed the Rowlatt Act agitation. When Gandhi was barred from entering the Punjab, the province descended into a spiral of popular unrest and brutal British retaliation: martial law was declared, leaders were imprisoned without trial, and atrocities — including the Jallianwala Bagh massacre and the infamous crawling order — were perpetrated against civilians. The Congress responded with an independent inquiry committee, whose report Gandhi drafted, documenting "what inhumanities and barbarities" the British Government was "capable of perpetrating in order to maintain its power" (257-000).

## Background

The Rowlatt Act *hartal* of April 6, 1919, was observed with remarkable unity across India. In the Punjab, where Drs. Satyapal and Kitchlew had built a strong local movement, the response was particularly intense. The British authorities, under Lieutenant-Governor Sir Michael O'Dwyer, responded with escalating repression. Delhi had already witnessed police firing on a *hartal* procession, "causing a number of casualties, and the reign of repression commenced" (251-000).

## Timeline

### Gandhi Barred from the Punjab

Gandhi left Bombay for Delhi and Amritsar on the night of April 7, 1919. At Mathura, Acharya Gidwani met him with news of his probable arrest. Before reaching Palwal station, Gandhi was served with an order prohibiting his entry into the Punjab. He refused to comply and was removed from the train, held in police custody, and eventually escorted back to Bombay (251-000). Sir Michael O'Dwyer "held me responsible for all that had happened in the Punjab" (254-000).

### Martial Law and Repression

With Gandhi excluded and local leaders arrested, the situation spiraled. In Ahmedabad, Gandhi found the city under martial law, the people "terror-stricken," and acts of violence being "made to pay for them with interest" (252-000). He met with Commissioner Pratt, expressed regret for the disturbances, suggested martial law was unnecessary, and offered full cooperation to restore peace. He addressed a public meeting at the Sabarmati Ashram on April 13 — the same day as the Jallianwala Bagh massacre — and martial law was withdrawn in Ahmedabad the following day (252-000).

But in the Punjab, repression intensified: "Government's policy of lawless repression was in full career... Leaders were put under arrest, martial law, which in other words meant no law, was proclaimed, special tribunals were set up. These tribunals were not courts of justice but instruments for carrying out the arbitrary will of an autocrat" (252-000). Gandhi was more disturbed by the systemic degradation — "In Amritsar innocent men and women were made to crawl like worms on their bellies" — than by the Jallianwala Bagh massacre itself, which nonetheless "principally attracted the attention of the people of India and of the world" (252-000).

### The Congress Inquiry

When the British Government appointed the Hunter Committee to investigate, the Congress decided to boycott it and appoint its own independent inquiry. The committee comprised Pandit Motilal Nehru, C. R. Das, Abbas Tyabji, M. R. Jayakar, and Gandhi himself. Gandhi bore primary responsibility for organizing the inquiry and conducted investigations in the largest number of places, giving him "a rare opportunity of observing at close quarters the people of the Punjab and the Punjab villages" (256-000).

Gandhi also drafted the committee's report. He later wrote: "I would recommend a perusal of this report to anyone who wants to have an idea of the kind of atrocities that were perpetrated on the Punjab people. There is not a single conscious exaggeration in it anywhere, and every statement made in it is substantiated by evidence... Not a single statement, regarding the validity of which there was the slightest room for doubt, was permitted to appear in the report" (257-000). He asserted that "not a single statement made in this report has ever been disproved" (257-000).

### The Amritsar Congress

The Amritsar Congress that winter was Gandhi's "real entrance into the Congress politics" (258-000). The Punjab prisoners were released, and leaders like the Ali brothers arrived "straight from jail." Gandhi was appointed a trustee for the Jallianwala Bagh memorial fund, and his work on the Punjab inquiry earned him the confidence of senior leaders including Lokamanya Tilak, Deshbandhu Das, and Motilal Nehru.

## Gandhi's Role

The Punjab disturbances marked a pivotal transition in Gandhi's political career. He was not merely a protest leader but had become an investigator, a draftsman, and a fundraiser — three roles that would define his Congress work. The Punjab inquiry report demonstrated his capacity for meticulous documentation of state violence. His simultaneous response — calling for nonviolent discipline while documenting atrocities — established the template for all his subsequent campaigns.

## Historical Significance

The Punjab disturbances and their documentation in the Congress inquiry report marked the first systematic effort by Indian nationalists to hold the colonial state accountable through independent investigation. The report itself became a landmark document of anti-colonial evidence-gathering. The disturbances also directly catalyzed the Non-cooperation Movement, which Gandhi launched with the demand for redress of both the "Punjab wrong" and the "Khilafat wrong."

## Key Passages

> Government's policy of lawless repression was in full career on the other, and was manifesting itself in the Punjab in all its nakedness. Leaders were put under arrest, martial law, which in other words meant no law, was proclaimed, special tribunals were set up. These tribunals were not courts of justice but instruments for carrying out the arbitrary will of an autocrat. (252-000)

> This report, prepared as it was solely with a view to bringing out the truth and nothing but the truth, will enable the reader to see to what lengths the British Government is capable of going, and what inhumanities and barbarities it is capable of perpetrating in order to maintain its power. (257-000)
