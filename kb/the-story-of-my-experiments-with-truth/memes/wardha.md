# Wardha

Wardha is a town in present-day Maharashtra, India, best known in the context of Gandhi's life as the location near which he established his Sevagram ashram. Several of Gandhi's close associates had connections to the Wardha area.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi's connection to Wardha developed through his network of coworkers. Keshavrao Deshpande, a contemporary and close friend of Gandhi's from his student days in England, had connections to the region. Deshpande had conducted a school called "Ganganath Vidyalaya" in the Baroda State, and several of the teachers there — including Kakasaheb Kalelkar — later became Gandhi's devoted coworkers ([1915-P01]).

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

Wardha's significance in Gandhi's narrative is primarily through the personal ties that linked the region to his movement. The network of educators from the Ganganath Vidyalaya — Deshpande ("Saheb"), Kalelkar ("Kaka"), and others — maintained their "spiritual relationship" and assumed family names long after the school dissolved, eventually becoming Gandhi's coworkers "one after another" ([1915-P01]).

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Wardha area became symbolically important as the site of Sevagram, Gandhi's ashram established in 1936, which served as his base for rural reconstruction work and the 1942 Quit India Movement. The region represented Gandhi's commitment to village-centered development and his belief that India's soul lived in its villages.

## Historical Context

Wardha is located in the Vidarbha region of Maharashtra and was historically part of the Central Provinces during the British colonial period. The town gained national prominence during the Indian independence movement when Gandhi chose nearby Segaon village (renamed Sevagram, meaning "village of service") as his residence from 1936 onward. Sevagram became a center of the national movement and hosted key Congress Working Committee meetings.
