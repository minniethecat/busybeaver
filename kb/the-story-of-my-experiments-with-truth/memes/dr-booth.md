# Dr. Joseph Booth

Dr. Joseph Booth was an English clergyman in South Africa who befriended Gandhi during his early years in Durban. A devout Christian with an open mind, Booth engaged Gandhi in sustained theological dialogue that helped shape Gandhi's understanding of Christianity and reinforced his commitment to his own Hindu tradition. When Gandhi's evolving conception of ahimsa made him uncomfortable with the militaristic lyrics of the British national anthem, Booth endorsed his concerns and helped compose an alternative.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Booth was one of the first Christian clergymen Gandhi encountered who approached Hinduism with respect rather than a conversion agenda. Their friendship was grounded in shared ethical commitments and mutual intellectual respect. When Gandhi shared his discomfort with the lines of the national anthem that referred to "knavish" enemies, Booth "entirely endorsed my sentiments" and helped compose a new anthem that reflected Gandhian values (051-007). This episode was significant: it showed Gandhi that Christian faith and Gandhian ethics could be compatible, and that honest interfaith dialogue was possible.

## Biography

### Role in the Narrative

Booth appears in the narrative around the time of the Boer War, serving as a supportive interlocutor during a period when Gandhi was intensively developing his ideas about nonviolence. His willingness to take Gandhi's ethical concerns seriously — even when they challenged his own cultural traditions — modeled the kind of cross-cultural moral dialogue Gandhi would champion throughout his life.
