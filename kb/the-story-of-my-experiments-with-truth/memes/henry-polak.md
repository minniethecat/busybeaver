# Henry Polak

Henry Polak was a British-born journalist, Theosophist, and one of Gandhi's closest associates during the South African years. As sub-editor of *Indian Opinion* and a devoted friend, he played an essential supporting role in the early satyagraha campaigns. His questioning of Gandhi's participation in World War I, given Gandhi's commitment to ahimsa, prompted one of the most revealing exchanges about the tensions in Gandhi's philosophy.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Polak met Gandhi in South Africa through Theosophical circles and quickly became one of his most trusted collaborators. He worked alongside Gandhi at *Indian Opinion*, the newspaper that served as the voice of the South African Indian community and the platform for Gandhi's developing ideas. Polak shared Gandhi's dietary experiments and his commitment to simple living. Their friendship was deep enough that Polak felt able to challenge Gandhi directly — most notably when he cabled Gandhi during World War I to question "the consistency of my action with my profession of ahimsa" (116-001), referring to Gandhi's decision to support the British war effort.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in England in 1882, Polak was drawn to Theosophy and social reform. He moved to South Africa, where he became involved with the Indian community's struggles and met Gandhi. Trained as a journalist, he brought professional skills to the running of *Indian Opinion*, which under Gandhi's direction became a unique journal of political and spiritual education.

### Role in the Narrative

Polak appears in the autobiography primarily in the context of the South African satyagraha and the running of *Indian Opinion*. He was a reliable lieutenant — someone who could be trusted to maintain the newspaper and the movement during Gandhi's absences. His respectful challenge to Gandhi's wartime stance (116-001) prompted Gandhi to articulate his nuanced position: that while participation in war "could never be consistent with ahimsa" (116-003), the duty of a votary of truth was not always clear, and one must sometimes "grope in the dark."

## Historical Significance

Polak represents the circle of dedicated European and Indian associates who made Gandhi's South African work possible. His biography of Gandhi, published in 1918, was one of the earliest serious studies of Gandhi's life and thought.

## Key Passages

> As soon as the news reached South Africa that I along with other Indians had offered my services in the war, I received two cables. One of these was from Mr. Polak who questioned the consistency of my action with my profession of ahimsa. (116-001)
