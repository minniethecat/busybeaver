# Passive Resistance in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

"Passive resistance" was the term Gandhi and his South African compatriots initially used to describe their movement. But Gandhi soon found the English phrase inadequate — too narrow, too suggestive of weakness, too compatible with hatred and potential violence. His decision to replace it with the Gujarati neologism *Satyagraha* marks one of the most consequential acts of conceptual clarification in modern political history.

## From "Passive Resistance" to "Satyagraha"

Gandhi describes the moment of reckoning:

> The principle called satyagraha came into being before that name was invented. Indeed when it was born, I myself could not say what it was. In Gujarati also we used the English phrase "passive resistance" to describe it. When in a meeting of Europeans I found that the term "passive resistance" was too narrowly construed, that it was supposed to be a weapon of the weak, that it could be characterized by hatred, and that it could finally manifest itself as violence, I had to demur to all these statements and explain the real nature of the Indian movement. It was clear that a new word must be coined by the Indians to designate their struggle.
>
> (136-001)

The European misunderstanding revealed a deeper truth: "passive resistance" carried connotations that were fundamentally at odds with what Gandhi was actually practising. It sounded like the weapon of those too weak to fight — whereas Gandhi insisted his method required greater courage than violence. It sounded like it could coexist with hatred — whereas Gandhi's method required love for the opponent. And it sounded like it might boil over into violence — whereas Gandhi's method was a discipline of self-suffering.

## The Coinage of "Satyagraha"

Maganlal Gandhi, Gandhi's cousin and close associate, won a prize offered through *Indian Opinion* for coining "Sadagraha" (from *sat* = truth, *agraha* = firmness). Gandhi refined this to "Satyagraha":

> But in order to make it clearer I changed the word to "Satyagraha" which has since become current in Gujarati as a designation for the struggle.
>
> (136-002)

The shift from "passive resistance" to "Satyagraha" — literally "truth-force" or "holding firmly to truth" — was not merely semantic. It reoriented the movement around a positive principle (truth) rather than a negative one (passivity).

## Roots in the Sermon on the Mount

Gandhi's earliest encounter with the principle underlying passive resistance came not through politics but through scripture. Reading the New Testament in England, the Sermon on the Mount struck him with the force of recognition:

> The verses, "But I say unto ye, that ye resist not evil: but whosoever shall smite thee on thy right cheek, turn to him the other also. And if any man take away thy coat let him have thy cloak too," delighted me beyond measure.
>
> (030-014)

The teaching to "resist not evil" — which Gandhi connected to the Gita's doctrine of non-attachment and the Buddha's compassion — planted the seed that would decades later flower into Satyagraha. But where the Sermon on the Mount spoke of non-resistance, Gandhi's genius was to transform this into *active* nonviolent resistance — a force, not a surrender.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[satyagraha]]**: The Gujarati term that replaced "passive resistance" — truth-force as a positive principle.
- **[[nonviolence]]**: What "passive" failed to capture — the active, disciplined refusal to harm.
- **[[truth]]**: The core of Satyagraha — the satyagrahi holds firmly to truth, not merely refuses to act.
- **[[ahimsa]]**: The ethical foundation of nonviolent resistance.
- **[[civil-disobedience]]**: The specific political application of Satyagraha to unjust laws.
