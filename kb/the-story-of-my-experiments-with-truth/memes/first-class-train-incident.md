# First Class Train Incident

On a bitterly cold winter night in 1893, Mohandas Gandhi — then a young barrister traveling to Pretoria on his first legal assignment — was thrown off a train at Maritzburg (Pietermaritzburg) station because he was "coloured" and dared to occupy a first-class compartment. The night he spent shivering in the unlit waiting room, wrestling with whether to fight or flee, is widely recognized as the founding moment of Gandhi's philosophy of nonviolent resistance and his lifelong commitment to fighting racial injustice.

## Background

Gandhi had arrived in Durban only a week earlier, engaged by Dada Abdulla & Company to assist in a commercial lawsuit. Abdulla Sheth had booked him a first-class seat and urged him to purchase a bedding ticket, but Gandhi — out of "obstinacy and pride and with a view to saving five shillings" (048-000) — declined. Abdulla Sheth warned him: "This is a different country from India. Thank God, we have enough and to spare. Please do not stint yourself in anything that you may need" (048-000).

## Timeline

### The Journey Begins

Gandhi departed Durban by train. At about 9 p.m., the train reached Maritzburg, the capital of Natal. A railway servant offered bedding, which Gandhi again declined. But then a white passenger entered, "looked me up and down" and, seeing a "coloured" man, went to fetch officials. An official ordered Gandhi to move to the van compartment (048-000).

"But I have a first class ticket," Gandhi protested.

"That doesn't matter. I tell you, you must go to the van compartment."

Gandhi refused. A constable was summoned. "He took me by the hand and pushed me out. My luggage was also taken out. I refused to go to the other compartment and the train steamed away" (048-000).

### The Night in the Waiting Room

It was winter, and Maritzburg sits at high altitude: "the cold was extremely bitter. My overcoat was in my luggage, but I did not dare to ask for it lest I should be insulted again, so I sat and shivered. There was no light in the room" (048-000). A passenger came in at midnight but Gandhi was in no mood to talk.

During that long, cold night, Gandhi confronted the defining question of his life:

> "Should I fight for my rights or go back to India, or should I go on to Pretoria without minding the insults, and return to India after finishing the case? It would be cowardice to run back to India without fulfilling my obligation. The hardship to which I was subjected was superficial — only a symptom of the deep disease of colour prejudice. I should try, if possible, to root out the disease and suffer hardships in the process." (048-000)

### Resolution and Aftermath

By morning, Gandhi had resolved to continue to Pretoria. He sent a long telegram to the General Manager of the Railway and informed Abdulla Sheth, who immediately intervened. The General Manager instructed the Station Master to ensure Gandhi reached his destination safely — though he also justified the railway authorities' conduct. Indian merchants came to comfort Gandhi, "narrating their own hardships and explaining that what had happened to me was nothing unusual" (048-000). That evening, Gandhi boarded the next train — this time with a bedding ticket — and continued his journey.

## Gandhi's Role

The Maritzburg incident was not yet an act of political resistance — it was a raw, personal experience of humiliation. But the decision Gandhi made that night — not to flee, not to seek merely personal redress, but to "root out the disease" of colour prejudice itself — marked the birth of the moral framework that would later become satyagraha. Gandhi was 24 years old at the time, unknown, and alone in a foreign country. The incident is the archetypal "experiment with truth" that gives the autobiography its name.

## Historical Significance

The Maritzburg incident is widely regarded as the origin moment of Gandhi's political consciousness. It transformed him from a diffident young lawyer into a determined fighter against racial injustice. The decision he made in that unlit waiting room — to stay and fight rather than return to the safety of India — set in motion the events that would lead to the birth of satyagraha and, ultimately, to the Indian independence movement. The station at Pietermaritzburg now houses a memorial to the event.

## Key Passages

> I began to think of my duty. Should I fight for my rights or go back to India, or should I go on to Pretoria without minding the insults, and return to India after finishing the case? It would be cowardice to run back to India without fulfilling my obligation. The hardship to which I was subjected was superficial — only a symptom of the deep disease of colour prejudice. I should try, if possible, to root out the disease and suffer hardships in the process. (048-000)

> So I decided to take the next available train to Pretoria. (048-000)
