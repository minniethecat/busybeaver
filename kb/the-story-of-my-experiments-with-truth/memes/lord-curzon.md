# Lord Curzon

George Nathaniel Curzon (1859–1925), 1st Marquess Curzon of Kedleston, served as Viceroy of India from 1899 to 1905. His controversial partition of Bengal in 1905 catalyzed the Swadeshi movement and marked a turning point in Indian nationalism. In Gandhi's autobiography, Curzon appears as the embodiment of imperial pomp whose durbar exposed the subservience of Indian princes to British authority.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi had no personal relationship with Curzon. He observed Curzon from the periphery of colonial power — as an Indian lawyer and aspiring public figure navigating the stratified world of British India. The durbar Curzon held became, in Gandhi's telling, a morality play about the psychological degradation of colonial rule, symbolized by Indian princes who exchanged their traditional dhotis for "trousers befitting khansamas."

## Biography

### Early Life

Born into the British aristocracy in 1859 and educated at Eton and Oxford, Curzon entered Parliament and served as Under-Secretary of State for India before being appointed Viceroy at the age of thirty-nine. His viceroyalty was marked by ambitious administrative reforms, the controversial partition of Bengal, and an assertive imperial vision encapsulated in his belief that "the British Empire is under Providence the greatest instrument for good that the world has seen."

### Role in the Narrative

Curzon's durbar — a ceremonial gathering of Indian princes and notables — became the occasion for one of Gandhi's sharpest observations on colonial psychology. Gandhi, a member of a Calcutta club frequented by Rajas and Maharajas, noticed that these princes who normally wore "fine Bengali dhotis and shirts and scarves" appeared at Curzon's durbar in Western trousers and shining boots [070-003].

When Gandhi inquired about the change, one prince replied with devastating self-awareness: "They are our khansamas, we are Lord Curzon's khansamas. If I were to absent myself from the levee, I should have to suffer the consequences. If I were to attend it in my usual dress, it would be an offence" [070-006]. This exchange crystallized for Gandhi the degradation inherent in colonial rule — the reduction of India's traditional rulers to liveried servants of an alien power.

## Historical Significance

Lord Curzon's viceroyalty was a watershed in the history of Indian nationalism. The partition of Bengal (1905) — an administrative measure widely perceived as a "divide and rule" stratagem — provoked the Swadeshi movement, boycotts of British goods, and a new phase of mass political mobilization. Though Curzon left India in 1905, the forces his policies unleashed transformed the Indian National Congress from an elite debating society into a mass movement. For Gandhi, the episode illustrated how colonial humiliation could awaken political consciousness — the princes in their khansama trousers were living symbols of India's subjugation.
