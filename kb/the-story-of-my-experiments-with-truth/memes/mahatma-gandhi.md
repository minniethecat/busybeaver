# Mahatma Gandhi

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi (1869–1948), honored as Mahatma ("Great Soul"), was the author of *The Story of My Experiments with Truth* and the central figure of this wiki. The autobiography traces his life from childhood in Porbandar through his student years in London, his two decades of work in South Africa, and his emergence as the leader of India's nonviolent independence movement up to the early 1920s.

## Relationship to the Autobiography

Gandhi is simultaneously the narrator, the subject, and the moral center of the autobiography. The work is structured not as a conventional chronological memoir but as a series of "experiments" — deliberate tests of ethical principles — with truth as the governing ideal. Gandhi writes with remarkable candor about his failures and weaknesses: his childhood thefts, his adolescent dietary transgressions, his struggles with lust and anger. This confessional mode is integral to his conception of the autobiography as a "story of experiments" rather than a record of achievements.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, a coastal town in Kathiawar, Gandhi was the youngest child of Karamchand Gandhi, the diwan (chief minister) of Porbandar, and Putlibai, a deeply religious woman whose vows and fasts left a lasting impression. Married at thirteen to Kasturba, he struggled academically and socially, barely passing his matriculation examination. His father's death during Gandhi's adolescent years was a source of lasting guilt — he had been nursing his father when he left his bedside for marital relations, and his father died minutes later.

### Education in London (1888–1891)

At eighteen, despite the opposition of his caste elders, Gandhi sailed to London to study law. His three years in England were transformative: he adopted Western dress, took dancing and elocution lessons in an awkward attempt to become an English gentleman, and — most consequentially — embraced vegetarianism as a matter of principle rather than mere obedience to his mother's vow. His involvement with the Vegetarian Society and encounters with Theosophists introduced him to the Bhagavad Gita through Sir Edwin Arnold's translation, planting seeds that would bear fruit decades later.

### South Africa (1893–1914)

Gandhi's arrival in Durban in 1893, engaged by Dada Abdulla and Co. for a commercial lawsuit, began a twenty-one-year sojourn that transformed him. The famous incident at Pietermaritzburg — being thrown off a first-class railway carriage despite holding a valid ticket — crystallized his resolve to fight racial discrimination. In South Africa, Gandhi developed the technique of satyagraha ("truth-force"), led campaigns against discriminatory laws, founded the Phoenix Settlement and Tolstoy Farm, and published *Indian Opinion*. He returned to India in 1915 as a proven leader with an international reputation.

### Return to India and Mass Movements (1915–1920s)

Back in India, Gandhi established the Sabarmati Ashram near Ahmedabad and, under Gokhale's mentorship, spent a year traveling and observing before entering active politics. His first major interventions — Champaran (1917), Ahmedabad textile strike (1918), and Kheda (1918) — established satyagraha as a potent tool on Indian soil. The Rowlatt Act (1919) provoked his first nationwide satyagraha, and the Jallianwala Bagh massacre radicalized his critique of British rule. By the early 1920s, Gandhi had emerged as the preeminent leader of the Indian National Congress, launching the Non-Cooperation Movement.

## Historical Significance

Gandhi's legacy extends far beyond Indian independence. His method of nonviolent civil resistance influenced movements from the American civil rights struggle to the anti-apartheid campaign in South Africa to the Velvet Revolution in Czechoslovakia. His insistence on the unity of means and ends — that violent means cannot produce peaceful ends — remains a challenging proposition in an age of asymmetric warfare and terrorism. His economic philosophy, emphasizing self-reliance (swadeshi) and the dignity of manual labor, anticipates contemporary debates about sustainable development. And his lifelong experiment with truth — the attempt to align speech, action, and belief into a coherent whole — stands as one of modernity's most ambitious ethical projects.
