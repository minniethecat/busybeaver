# Self-Sacrifice in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Self-sacrifice runs as a quiet current throughout Gandhi's autobiography. It is not a dramatic ideal of martyrdom but a daily discipline of putting others before oneself — learned first from his mother, tested in public life, and finally understood as the engine of soul-force.

## The Spirit of Self-Sacrifice

Gandhi acknowledged the tension within himself between the desire for security and the impulse to give everything away. Looking back on his early career in South Africa, he wrote:

> Up to now there had been in me a mixed desire. The spirit of self-sacrifice was tempered by the desire to lay by something for the future.
>
> (085-002)

This "mixed desire" captures the human reality behind the ideal — Gandhi was not a born renouncer but someone who struggled between prudence and the spirit of sacrifice, gradually letting the latter win.

## Sacrifice and Service

For Gandhi, self-sacrifice was not about self-hatred or the denial of legitimate needs. It was about the willingness to inconvenience oneself for the sake of others — as he described the woman who modelled this quality:

> Her courage was equal to her sacrifice. She is one of the few women I have been privileged to come across, with a character as clear as crystal and courage that would shame a warrior.
>
> (089-013)

The pairing of "courage" and "sacrifice" is significant. Self-sacrifice requires courage — the courage to go without, to risk disapproval, to put one's own comfort last.

## Sacrifice as the Currency of Soul-Force

Gandhi came to understand self-sacrifice — what he called "self-suffering" — as the mechanism by which soul-force operates on the world. While brute force tries to make others suffer, soul-force accepts suffering upon oneself, and in doing so transforms both the sufferer and the oppressor. Self-sacrifice was therefore not an end in itself but a means of moral persuasion.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[sacrifice]]**: Self-sacrifice is the personal dimension of sacrifice — what one gives up of oneself rather than what one offers to a deity.
- **[[renunciation]]**: Self-sacrifice and renunciation are linked — both require the discipline of saying no to desire.
- **[[service]]**: Self-sacrifice finds its purpose in service; without service, sacrifice is mere asceticism.
