# Vow

The vow occupied a central place in Gandhi's moral psychology. He discovered that a formal, publicly declared commitment — particularly a vow of self-restraint — was not a burden but a liberation: "Before the vow I had been open to being overcome by temptation at any moment. Now the vow was a sure shield against temptation" (062-002). This insight — that binding oneself could be freeing — was one of Gandhi's most distinctive contributions to ethical practice.

## Definition

Gandhi distinguished a true vow from a mere resolution. A resolution was private and revocable; a vow was public and binding, made before God or conscience. The public character of the vow was essential: it enlisted one's reputation and honor in support of one's will. A vow also transformed the moral calculus of temptation — when every lapse was a broken vow rather than a mere slip, the stakes were higher. Gandhi's major vows included brahmacharya (celibacy), vegetarianism, and various fasting commitments, but the principle extended to the satyagraha pledge, which committed resisters to nonviolence and acceptance of punishment.

## Origins and Influences

The concept of *vrata* (religious vow) has deep roots in Indian tradition. Hindu, Jain, and Buddhist practitioners all observed various vows — of fasting, pilgrimage, celibacy, or truthfulness. Gandhi's mother Putlibai was known for her rigorous observance of religious vows, and this early example shaped his understanding. However, Gandhi transformed the traditional concept by making the vow a tool of conscious ethical experimentation rather than mere ritual observance. He was also influenced by the Western tradition of oaths and pledges, particularly the temperance pledge movement, which demonstrated the psychological power of public commitment.

## Gandhi's Practice

### The Brahmacharya Vow (1906)

Gandhi's most consequential personal vow was brahmacharya, taken in 1906 at age thirty-seven. "The final resolution could only be made as late as 1906... The work set me furiously thinking in the direction of self-control" (061-005). The vow transformed his life: "The freedom and joy that came to me after taking the vow had never been experienced before 1906" (062-002).

### The Vegetarian Vow

Gandhi's earliest conscious vow was his promise to his mother before leaving for London: to abstain from meat, alcohol, and women. "I blessed the day on which I had taken the vow before my mother. The vow saved me" (014-009). This experience — of a vow functioning as protection rather than restriction — became a template for his later practice.

### The Satyagraha Pledge

Gandhi extended the vow from personal discipline to collective political action. Participants in satyagraha campaigns signed formal pledges committing themselves to nonviolence and to accepting whatever punishment the law imposed. This transformed civil disobedience from an ad hoc act into a solemn moral commitment.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

The vow is the mechanism that converts aspiration into discipline. It connects the inner life of self-control to the outer life of political action. Without the vow, brahmacharya is a wish; with the vow, it becomes a force. The vow also embodies Gandhi's conviction that means determine ends: if the means of moral struggle include public commitment and self-suffering, they must themselves be undertaken with the solemnity of a vow.
