# Mansukhlal Naazar

Mansukhlal Naazar was the editor of *Indian Opinion*, the newspaper that became the voice of the Indian community in South Africa and the primary vehicle for Gandhi's ideas during the satyagraha years. Though less well-known than Gandhi's European associates like [[henry-polak]] or [[hermann-kallenbach]], Naazar was a crucial figure in the infrastructure of the movement — the steady editorial hand that kept the paper running while Gandhi was in the field.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Naazar's relationship to Gandhi was professional rather than intimate — he was an editor and a colleague, not a spiritual companion. But the nature of their collaboration reveals an important dimension of Gandhi's leadership: his willingness to trust competent people with essential work.

When Gandhi decided to move *Indian Opinion* to the Phoenix Settlement and reorganize it around the principle of manual labour, Naazar did not join the experiment. He remained in Durban, where a branch office continued to operate:

> The editor was still Mansukhlal Naazar. He had not accepted the new scheme and was directing the paper from Durban where there was a branch office for Indian Opinion.
>
> (081-005)

## Biography

Little personal detail about Naazar survives in the autobiography. He was presumably a Gujarati journalist recruited by Gandhi to edit the newspaper. His competence is attested by the fact that Gandhi entrusted him with the editorship during the critical early years of the paper.

## Role in the Narrative

Naazar's role in the autobiography is brief but significant. He represents the division of labour within Gandhi's movement — the recognition that not everyone needed to live at Phoenix or practice manual labour. Naazar's choice to remain in Durban and direct the paper from the city was accepted by Gandhi without criticism. The movement needed both the settlers at Phoenix and the professionals in town.

The Phoenix experiment itself — where "the idea was for every member of the Settlement to learn typesetting" (081-005) — was made possible by Naazar's continued editorial work from Durban. While Gandhi and the settlers learned to set type and work the land, Naazar ensured that the paper maintained its quality and its voice.

## Historical Significance

Mansukhlal Naazar represents the many unsung Indian professionals — lawyers, journalists, clerks, merchants — who sustained Gandhi's movement in South Africa. Unlike the more famous European associates, figures like Naazar have been largely forgotten, in part because Gandhi himself wrote sparingly about them. But his presence in the narrative reminds us that the satyagraha movement was not a one-man show but depended on a network of capable, committed individuals.
