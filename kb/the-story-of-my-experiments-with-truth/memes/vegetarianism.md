# Vegetarianism

Vegetarianism was Gandhi's earliest conscious ethical commitment and served as a foundation for much of his later philosophy. His vow to abstain from meat, made to his mother before leaving for England, became the seed from which his systematic discipline of self-restraint grew. "My faith in vegetarianism grew on me from day to day" (015-001), he wrote of his London years, when the practice shifted from a matter of cultural obligation to one of intellectual and moral conviction.

## Definition

For Gandhi, vegetarianism was not simply a dietary preference but a moral imperative rooted in the principle of nonviolence. He came to see the consumption of meat as inconsistent with ahimsa, since it required the taking of animal life for human pleasure. His vegetarianism extended beyond diet to encompass an ethical stance against the unnecessary exploitation of living beings. In his later years, he also abstained from milk on ethical grounds, though he eventually resumed its use on medical advice.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi was born into a Vaishnava Hindu family in Gujarat, where vegetarianism was the norm. His mother Putlibai was devoutly religious and observed strict dietary rules. As a youth, however, Gandhi briefly experimented with meat-eating under the influence of a friend who argued that meat consumption would make Indians strong enough to resist British rule. He abandoned the experiment out of guilt over deceiving his parents.

When Gandhi traveled to London in 1888 to study law, he promised his mother he would abstain from meat, alcohol, and women. The vegetarian vow became a lifeline: finding a vegetarian restaurant in London led him to the vegetarian movement and its literature (014-008). "I blessed the day on which I had taken the vow before my mother... The vow saved me" (014-009). He joined the London Vegetarian Society, wrote articles for its journal, and "saw that the writers on vegetarianism had examined the question very minutely, reasoning it out from the ethical, physical, and economic points of view" (017-001). This intellectual conversion transformed a cultural habit into a reasoned conviction.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Early Experiments

In London, Gandhi began systematic dietary experiments. He "launched out in search of a vegetarian restaurant" (014-008) and soon discovered a community of ethical vegetarians, including figures like Dr. Allinson (018-002) and Henry Salt. He began reading vegetarian literature extensively — so much so that his friend feared "lest these studies should result in my completely eschewing all social relations" (015-002).

### Dietetic Science

Gandhi approached vegetarianism scientifically, studying the nutritional properties of foods. His investigation showed that "even in vegetarian restaurants many courses used eggs, and that the English vegetarian was not necessarily opposed to eggs" (017-006), leading him to refine his own practice. He experimented with raw foods, fruit diets, and various fasting regimens, always seeking the diet that would best support both health and spiritual practice.

### Moral Dimension

Over time, Gandhi connected vegetarianism to his broader ethical framework. The practice of controlling the palate was training for controlling other desires. It was also an expression of ahimsa — nonviolence extended to all living beings. "The writers on vegetarianism had examined the question very minutely, reasoning it out from the ethical, physical, and economic points of view" (017-001). This multi-dimensional analysis — ethical, health-related, and practical — became characteristic of Gandhi's approach to all questions of conduct.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Vegetarianism occupies an important though often understated place in Gandhi's thought. It was his earliest laboratory for self-discipline, teaching him that a vow could be a source of strength rather than constraint. It connected diet to ethics (ahimsa), self-control (brahmacharya), and truth-seeking. The dietary experiments of his London years — reading, testing, refining — foreshadowed the experimental method he would apply to all of life. "I may claim to have become a vegetarian by choice" (014-009): this claim of conscious, reasoned commitment is the essence of Gandhi's approach to all moral questions.
