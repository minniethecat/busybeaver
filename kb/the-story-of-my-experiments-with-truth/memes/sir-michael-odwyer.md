# Sir Michael O'Dwyer

Sir Michael Francis O'Dwyer (1864–1940) was the Lieutenant Governor of the Punjab during one of the most traumatic episodes in British Indian history — the Jallianwala Bagh massacre of 1919 and the subsequent imposition of martial law. In Gandhi's narrative, O'Dwyer appears as the official who perceived Gandhi's presence as a threat to public order and barred his entry into the Punjab.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi had no direct personal meeting with O'Dwyer; their conflict was structural and political rather than personal. O'Dwyer's administration viewed Gandhi's civil disobedience campaign as a threat to order in the Punjab and took the extraordinary step of removing Gandhi from a train at Palwal, placing him under police custody, and forcibly returning him toward Bombay. O'Dwyer later held Gandhi responsible for all that had transpired in the Punjab.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in Ireland in 1864, Michael O'Dwyer joined the Indian Civil Service in 1885 and served in various administrative posts across the Punjab and North-West Frontier Province. He rose to become Lieutenant Governor of the Punjab in 1913, a position he held throughout World War I and the turbulent period that followed.

### Role in the Narrative

In 1919, as Gandhi attempted to travel to the Punjab in the aftermath of the Rowlatt Act protests, he was intercepted at Palwal railway station. The police officer escorting him "commenced a long panegyric of Sir Michael O'Dwyer," explaining that while O'Dwyer had "nothing against me personally," he "apprehended a disturbance of the peace if I entered the Punjab" [155-012]. Gandhi was removed from the train, held overnight, and sent back toward Bombay.

O'Dwyer subsequently held Gandhi responsible for the Punjab disturbances. As Gandhi recorded: "Sir Michael O'Dwyer held me responsible for all that had happened in the Punjab, and some irate young Punjabis held me responsible for the martial law" [159-001]. Some young Punjabis went so far as to threaten Gandhi with assassination should he enter the Punjab — a tragic prefiguring of O'Dwyer's own fate: he was assassinated in London in 1940 by Udham Singh, a survivor of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre.

## Historical Significance

Sir Michael O'Dwyer embodies the authoritarian strain in late colonial governance — the conviction that order must be maintained at any cost and that those who challenged it bore responsibility for the consequences. His decision to bar Gandhi from the Punjab and his retrospective attribution of blame to Gandhi for the violence that followed the Rowlatt Act protests illustrate the irreconcilable worldviews of the colonial administrator and the satyagrahi. O'Dwyer's assassination in 1940 by Udham Singh — who explicitly stated he was avenging Jallianwala Bagh — marked a violent coda to the events set in motion during O'Dwyer's Punjab tenure.

## Key Passages

They asserted that, if only I had not suspended civil disobedience, there would have been no Jallianwala Bagh massacre. Some of them even went to the length of threatening me with assassination if I went to the Punjab.
