# Hermann Kallenbach

Hermann Kallenbach was a German-Jewish architect who became one of Gandhi's most intimate friends and devoted supporters in South Africa. A successful professional who embraced simple living under Gandhi's influence, Kallenbach purchased the land for Tolstoy Farm and co-founded the communal settlement that became the headquarters of the satyagraha movement. Gandhi described their friendship as a "marriage of souls."

## Relationship to Gandhi

Kallenbach met Gandhi in Johannesburg and was quickly drawn into his orbit. Despite his wealth and comfort, Kallenbach embraced radical simplicity — reducing his possessions, adopting a vegetarian diet, and dedicating his resources to the satyagraha cause. He purchased a farm of over 1,100 acres near Johannesburg and gave it to the movement; Gandhi named it Tolstoy Farm. Their friendship was so close that Kallenbach planned to accompany Gandhi to India in 1914, but was prevented by the outbreak of World War I (as a German national, he was not permitted to travel). The two maintained a correspondence for decades.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in Germany in 1871, Kallenbach trained as an architect and moved to South Africa, where he established a successful practice in Johannesburg. He was prosperous, athletic, and fond of the good life — until his encounter with Gandhi transformed him.

### Role in the Narrative

Kallenbach appears in the autobiography as the ideal friend and comrade — someone who not only supported Gandhi financially and practically but embraced his ideals as his own. Tolstoy Farm, their joint creation, became the model for Gandhi's later ashrams in India. Kallenbach's story illustrates Gandhi's power to inspire radical personal transformation in those close to him.

## Historical Significance

Kallenbach represents the multi-ethnic, multi-faith community that formed around Gandhi in South Africa — a German Jew dedicating his life and fortune to the cause of Indian rights. His story challenges reductive views of Gandhi's movement as narrowly Indian or Hindu.
