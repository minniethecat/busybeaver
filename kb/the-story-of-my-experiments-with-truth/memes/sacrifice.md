## Overview

Sacrifice was a recurring theme throughout Mahatma Gandhi's autobiography, appearing both as a personal practice and as a quality he admired in communities and individuals. For Gandhi, sacrifice was not merely abstention but an active orientation of one's life toward service. He saw it in the illiterate laborers of South Africa, in his educated coworkers, and in his own evolving experiments with self-denial.

## Personal Practice

Gandhi's understanding of sacrifice began in his family life. As a youth devoted to his parents, he came to recognize — sometimes through painful experience — that "all happiness and pleasure should be sacrificed in devoted service to my parents" [003-009]. This early lesson in filial sacrifice laid groundwork for his later, broader practice of self-denial in service to community and country.

The development of this spirit in Gandhi was gradual and self-aware. Reflecting on his career in South Africa, he wrote: "Up to now there had been in me a mixed desire. The spirit of self-sacrifice was tempered by the desire to lay by something for the future" [090-009]. This honest admission reveals that even for Gandhi, the impulse toward sacrifice was not a single moment of renunciation but an ongoing negotiation between self-interest and service.

## Acts of Radical Sacrifice

Some of the most striking examples of sacrifice in the autobiography involve Gandhi's willingness to place coworkers in harm's way for a greater cause. During the Black Plague outbreak in Johannesburg, he had four young Indian clerks working in his office. He made a deliberate decision "to sacrifice all four — call them clerks, coworkers or sons." Their response was immediate and unhesitating: "Where you are, we will also be" [101-017]. This episode illustrates how Gandhi's own willingness to sacrifice created a culture in which others, too, were prepared to risk their lives.

Among his closest associates, Miss Sonja Schlesin stood out for her sacrifice. "For a considerable period she did not draw more than £6, and refused ever to receive more than £10 a month. When I urged her to take more, she would give me a scolding and say, 'I am not here to draw a salary from you. I am here because I like to work with you and I like your ideals'" [098-029]. Gandhi noted that her "courage was equal to her sacrifice. She is one of the few women I have been privileged to come across, with a character as clear as crystal and courage that would shame a warrior" [098-031].

Maganlal Gandhi similarly "left his business for good to cast in his lot with me, and by ability, sacrifice and devotion stands foremost among my original coworkers in my ethical experiments" [105-021]. This was the pattern Gandhi both inspired and depended upon: individuals who surrendered material security for shared ideals.

## Sacrifice in Community

Throughout his South African years, Gandhi witnessed and documented the sacrifices of ordinary Indians. He recalled with deep feeling the "faith, perseverance and selfless sacrifice" of Tamil and Telugu compatriots in South Africa, noting that they were "mostly illiterate" yet demonstrated extraordinary dedication to the community's cause [049-008]. This recognition of sacrifice across class and education lines became a hallmark of his social philosophy.

During the Champaran campaign, Gandhi encountered Principal Kripalani, who had resigned his professorship at the Government College to live a life of simplicity and service. Dr. Choithram had told Gandhi "of his great sacrifice, of his simple life" [147-009] — evidence that the spirit of sacrifice Gandhi cultivated in South Africa found ready echoes among Indians engaged in their own nation's struggles.

## Spiritual Dimension

For Gandhi, sacrifice was inseparable from spiritual growth. His dietary experiments, his vows of renunciation, and his embrace of simplicity all reflected a conviction that voluntary sacrifice purified the soul and prepared one for higher service. He connected this not only to Hindu traditions but also found resonance with Christian ideals of self-giving. Though he resisted the doctrine that Jesus was uniquely the son of God — believing instead that all humans share equally in the divine — he could "accept Jesus as a martyr, an embodiment of sacrifice, and a divine teacher." He went further, observing: "From the point of view of sacrifice, it seemed to me that the Hindus greatly surpassed the Christians" [040-007].

The connection between sacrifice and self-restraint appears throughout Gandhi's experiments. Reflecting on fasting, he noted that "an atmosphere of self-restraint naturally sprang up on the Farm. All the Farm inmates now began to join us in keeping partial and complete fasts, which, I am sure, was entirely to the good" [117-017]. Sacrifice, in this framing, was not an isolated act of heroism but a daily discipline cultivated in community.

## The Limits of Sacrifice

Gandhi was honest about his own failures. In a moment of striking self-criticism, he recorded how during a severe illness he compromised his vow to abstain from milk by accepting goat's milk: "The will to live proved stronger than the devotion to truth, and for once the votary of truth compromised his sacred ideal by his eagerness to take up the satyagraha fight. The memory of this action even now rankles in my breast and fills me with remorse" [163-021]. This admission reveals that sacrifice, for Gandhi, was never a completed achievement but a perpetual struggle — and that even the most dedicated practitioners could fall short.
