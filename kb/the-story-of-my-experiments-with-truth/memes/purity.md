## Overview

Purity — of heart, conduct, and diet — is a recurring ideal in Gandhi's autobiography. It functions not as a static state to be attained but as a standard against which to measure one's progress. From his youthful decision to abjure meat "out of the purity of my desire not to lie to my parents" [007-008] to his mature reflections on the path of self-purification, purity represents the alignment of inner intention with outer action.

## Purity of Heart

For Gandhi, the ultimate purity is that of the heart. He quotes with approval the idea that "if we achieve that purity of the heart when it is 'emptied of all' but love" [021-008], spiritual transformation follows. This interior purity is the source from which all other forms of purity derive their meaning — dietary restrictions, vows, and disciplines are means to purify the heart, not ends in themselves.

Gandhi saw this ideal embodied in others. Of Miss Sonja Schlesin, he wrote approvingly of Gokhale's assessment: "I have rarely met with the sacrifice, the purity and the fearlessness I have seen in Miss Schlesin" [089-015]. And in observations on character, he noted that "true beauty after all consists in purity of heart" [099-010], elevating inward purity above all external qualities.

This ideal of purity extended to his professional life. Addressing a gathering of merchants in Pretoria, Gandhi "strongly contested" the notion that "pure truth... is out of the question in business, one can speak it only so far as is suitable" [044-013]. For him, purity of conduct in commercial affairs was an extension of purity of heart — one could not be compartmentalized without the other.

## Purity and Truth

Purity and truth are intimately connected in Gandhi's framework. The pristine quality of truth — its uncompromised character — requires purity to sustain. He insisted that "without properly kept accounts it is impossible to maintain truth in its pristine purity" [051-029], grounding an abstract spiritual ideal in the concrete practice of meticulous bookkeeping. The struggle for purity is therefore the struggle to keep one's conduct aligned with truth despite inevitable pressures to dilute or distort it.

## Purity in Practice

Gandhi's dietary experiments — vegetarianism, fasting, restrictions on spices and stimulants — were practical expressions of the quest for purity. His initial abstention from meat was, significantly, not for health or philosophy but "out of the purity of my desire not to lie to my parents" [007-008]. This pattern — an external discipline motivated by an internal commitment to truthfulness — characterizes all of Gandhi's experiments with purity.

In his public work, too, Gandhi insisted on the connection between purity and effectiveness. During the sanitary reform campaign in Durban, he reflected: "the Indian community learnt to recognize more or less the necessity for keeping their houses and environments clean. I gained the esteem of the authorities. They saw that, though I had made it my business to ventilate grievances and press for rights, I was no less keen and insistent upon self-purification" [073-011]. This dual commitment — to external reform and internal purification — became a hallmark of his satyagraha method.

## Self-Purification as Political Method

Perhaps Gandhi's most distinctive contribution was his insistence on self-purification as the essential precondition for political action. When proposing the all-India *hartal* against the Rowlatt Bills, he framed it in these terms: "Satyagraha is a process of self-purification, and ours is a sacred fight, and it seems to me to be in the fitness of things that it should be commenced with an act of self-purification" [164-025]. The political strike was thus reinterpreted as a spiritual exercise — fasting and prayer as the necessary foundation for nonviolent resistance.

In the closing passage of his autobiography, Gandhi crystallized this understanding: "Identification with everything that lives is impossible without self-purification; without self-purification the observance of the law of Ahimsa must remain an empty dream. God can never be realized by one who is not pure of heart. Self-purification therefore must mean purification in all the walks of life" [178-015]. Yet he closed with characteristic honesty, acknowledging that the path "is hard and steep. To attain to perfect purity one has to become absolutely passion-free in thought, speech and action; to rise above the opposing currents of love and hatred, attachment and repulsion. I know that I have not in me as yet that triple purity, in spite of constant ceaseless striving for it" [178-017].
