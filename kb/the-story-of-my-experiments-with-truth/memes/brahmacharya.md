# Brahmacharya

Brahmacharya (literally "conduct leading to Brahman") is the discipline of self-restraint — particularly sexual continence — that Gandhi came to regard as indispensable for anyone seeking to realize truth and practice satyagraha. He took a formal vow of brahmacharya in 1906 at the age of thirty-seven, and reflected that "the freedom and joy that came to me after taking the vow had never been experienced before" (062-002).

## Definition

In traditional Hindu thought, brahmacharya is the first of the four life stages (ashramas) — the celibate student phase. Gandhi radicalized and extended this concept, making it a lifelong commitment rather than a temporary stage. For him, brahmacharya meant comprehensive control of all senses, not merely sexual restraint: it encompassed control of the palate (diet), speech, and thought. He came to believe that "a perfect observance of brahmacharya means realization of brahmacharya" — that is, the practice itself was the path to the goal (062-003).

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's engagement with brahmacharya was sparked by his reflections during the Zulu Rebellion of 1906, when his service with an ambulance corps "set me furiously thinking in the direction of self-control" (061-005). He had been married since age thirteen and was the father of four sons. The idea of taking a formal vow came to him gradually. He consulted his wife Kasturba, who consented, and took the vow in 1906. His reading of Hindu scriptures, particularly the Gita, reinforced his conviction that self-control was the foundation of spiritual power. He was also influenced by the Jain emphasis on asceticism and by Tolstoy's ideals of chastity.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Taking the Vow (1906)

The decision to take the brahmacharya vow marked a turning point in Gandhi's life. He later wrote that before the vow, "I had been open to being overcome by temptation at any moment. Now the vow was a sure shield against temptation" (062-002). The vow gave him a sense of liberation rather than constraint, and "the great potentiality of brahmacharya daily became more and more patent to me" (062-002).

### Supporting Practices

Gandhi recognized that brahmacharya could not be practiced in isolation. He identified several essential supports:
- **Fasting**: "As an external aid to brahmacharya, fasting is as necessary as selection and restriction in diet" (062-007)
- **Dietary control**: Restriction of stimulating foods, particularly spices and rich foods
- **Physical labor**: Manual work as a means of channeling energy
- **Prayer and meditation**: Spiritual disciplines to strengthen resolve

### Evolution

Over the decades following his vow, Gandhi's understanding deepened. He came to see brahmacharya not as a negative prohibition but as a positive force — a source of energy that could be redirected toward public service. "Without the observance of brahmacharya, service of the family would be inconsistent" with service of the community (102-004). He acknowledged the difficulty of the ideal, observing that "such brahmacharya was impossible to attain by mere human effort" (062-009), yet insisted on its necessity for those who would "desire to observe brahmacharya with a view to realizing God" (062-010).

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Brahmacharya occupies a crucial mediating position in Gandhi's thought. It is the personal discipline that prepares the individual for public action. Without self-control, the satyagrahi lacks the moral authority and spiritual power to confront injustice nonviolently. It connects dietary practice (vegetarianism, fasting) to political action (satyagraha) by training the will. In Gandhi's framework, brahmacharya, ahimsa, and truth form an interdependent triad: self-control enables nonviolence, which enables the pursuit of truth.
