# Indian Opinion

*Indian Opinion* was the multilingual weekly newspaper that Gandhi founded in [[durban]] in 1903 and edited for over a decade. More than a journalistic venture, it was the organizing instrument of the Indian community in [[south-africa]], the platform from which Gandhi articulated his evolving philosophy of [[satyagraha]], and the practical laboratory in which he tested his ideas about simplicity, manual labor, and the moral responsibilities of the press.

## Founding

The newspaper grew out of a crisis. In 1903, the Indian community of Natal faced a flood of hostile legislation and even more hostile press coverage. The white-owned newspapers of Durban and [[johannesburg]] routinely depicted Indians as a "coolie menace" — dirty, disease-ridden, and economically threatening. Gandhi, by then a well-known advocate and the secretary of the [[natal-indian-congress]], recognized that the community needed its own voice.

Madanjit Vyavaharik, a former schoolteacher and Gandhi's associate, proposed starting a press. The first issue of *Indian Opinion* appeared on June 4, 1903, printed in four languages: English, Gujarati, Hindi, and Tamil. The multilingual format was deliberate — it was meant to reach not only the English-educated elite but also the indentured laborers and trader classes in their own tongues.

## Gandhi's Editorship

Gandhi's approach to editing *Indian Opinion* was inseparable from his broader ethical commitments:

| Principle | Practice |
|-----------|---------|
| No advertisements for harmful products | Refused patent medicine and liquor ads |
| Truth above sensationalism | Refused to publish unverified allegations |
| Education, not agitation | Prioritized explanatory journalism over inflammatory rhetoric |
| Service, not profit | Set subscription prices to cover costs only |

As he wrote in the autobiography: "The newspaper became for me a training ground in self-restraint and a means of studying human nature" (037-012).

## From Durban to Phoenix

In 1904, *Indian Opinion* faced a financial crisis. The printing costs in Durban were unsustainable, and the newspaper was on the verge of folding. [[henry-polak]], Gandhi's closest European associate in South Africa, handed him a copy of John Ruskin's *Unto This Last* at the Johannesburg railway station — a book that Gandhi later said "brought about an instantaneous and practical transformation in my life" (077-001).

Ruskin's argument — that the good of the individual is contained in the good of all, that all work has equal dignity, and that a life of labor is the only life worth living — inspired Gandhi to move the newspaper's operations to a rural commune. He purchased a hundred-acre farm near Durban, named it the [[phoenix-settlement]], and relocated the press there.

At Phoenix, the printing was done by hand on a treadle-operated press. Every resident — Gandhi included — took turns at manual labor. The paper was distributed on foot to nearby Indian settlements. Gandhi described the Phoenix years as among the happiest and most instructive of his life.

## Content and Influence

*Indian Opinion* served multiple functions:

- **Legal education**: Gandhi serialized explanations of Natal laws affecting Indians, translated into Gujarati
- **Community mobilization**: Reports on satyagraha campaigns, fund-raising appeals, lists of volunteers
- **Moral instruction**: Gandhi's own editorial essays on diet, hygiene, sanitation, and religious tolerance
- **Translation of world thought**: Selections from [[leo-tolstoy]], Thoreau, Ruskin, and the Gita in Gujarati translation

The newspaper's readership was never large — a few thousand copies per issue at its peak — but its influence far exceeded its circulation. British officials in Natal and the Colonial Office in London read *Indian Opinion* carefully. The South African press reprinted (and attacked) its arguments. And the newspaper created, for the first time, a shared public discourse for Indians scattered across the four South African colonies.

## Legacy

When Gandhi left South Africa in 1914, *Indian Opinion* continued under the editorship of his son Manilal Gandhi and later others. The newspaper survived until 1961 — a remarkable fifty-eight-year run for a community press.

For Gandhi himself, *Indian Opinion* was the prototype for his later journalistic ventures in India — *Navajivan* (Gujarati) and *Young India* (English) — which served similar mobilizing and educational functions during the Indian freedom movement. The lesson of Phoenix — that a newspaper could be produced not by paid employees in a commercial office but by a community of volunteer-workers dedicated to service — remained central to Gandhi's thinking about media for the rest of his life.
