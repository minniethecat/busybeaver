# Portsmouth

Portsmouth was the setting for one of the most intimate moral trials in Gandhi's autobiography. During his final year as a law student in England, he attended a Vegetarian Conference in this naval seaport and was lodged — unbeknownst to the organizers — in a house whose female residents tested the limits of his brahmacharya vow.

## Gandhi's Connection

In 1890, during his last year in England, Gandhi and an Indian friend were invited to a Vegetarian Conference at Portsmouth. The Reception Committee, unaware of the character of the lodging, placed them in a house frequented by "women of ill fame, women not actually prostitutes, but at the same time, not very scrupulous about their morals." Portsmouth's identity as a seaport with a large naval population meant that such establishments were commonplace. (650-001)

## In the Narrative

### The Night of Temptation

Gandhi records that this was "the first occasion on which a woman, other than my wife, moved me to lust." He spent the night sleepless, assailed by conflicting thoughts: "Should I leave this house? Should I run away from the place? Where was I? What would happen to me if I had not my wits about me?" The experience left him shaken but resolute. (656-001)

### Resolution

Gandhi decided on a course of extreme caution: he would not leave the house precipitously, which might cause a scene, but would leave Portsmouth as soon as possible. The Conference lasted only two more days, and he departed the next evening, while his companion remained longer. This early victory over temptation reinforced his conviction that moral vigilance was essential to the spiritual life. (656-001)

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Portsmouth episode previewed the central role that brahmacharya would later play in Gandhi's spiritual discipline. Though he had not yet taken the formal vow — that would come in 1906 in South Africa — the experience demonstrated both the reality of temptation and the possibility of resisting it through deliberate vigilance. The memory of that sleepless night became one of the foundation stones of his later teaching on self-restraint. (656-001)

## Key Passages

> I recall this as the first occasion on which a woman, other than my wife, moved me to lust. I passed that night sleeplessly, all kinds of thoughts assailing me. (656-001)

> I decided to act thenceforth with great caution; not to leave the house, but somehow leave Portsmouth. (656-001)
