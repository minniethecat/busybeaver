The death of Karamchand Gandhi in 1885 was a pivotal event in young Gandhi's life, one that he later described with deep remorse as "My Father's Death and My Double Shame." At the time, the sixteen-year-old Gandhi served as one of his father's principal attendants during a prolonged illness.

## The Father's Illness

Karamchand Gandhi was bedridden, suffering from a fistula. Gandhi, his mother, and an old family servant served as the primary caregivers. Gandhi's duties included dressing the wound, administering medicine, compounding drugs, and massaging his father's legs every night (260-263). He recalled: "I loved to do this service. I do not remember ever having neglected it" (261-263). His time was divided between school and attending on his father, with evening walks permitted only when his father was feeling well.

Various medical treatments were attempted. Ayurvedic physicians, Hakims, and local practitioners all tried their remedies without success. An English surgeon recommended an operation, but the family physician objected on grounds of Karamchand's advanced age. The operation was abandoned, and Karamchand returned from Bombay with surgical equipment that would go unused. Gandhi believed that if the physician had permitted the surgery, "the wound would have been easily healed" (262-265).

## The "Double Shame"

Gandhi described two sources of shame associated with this period. First, his wife Kasturba was pregnant, which Gandhi later viewed as evidence of unrestrained carnal desire while he was still a student. Second, and more devastatingly, he was with his wife in the bedroom at the very moment his father died.

On the fateful night, Gandhi was massaging his father's legs when his uncle offered to relieve him. Relieved, Gandhi went to his bedroom where his wife was asleep. "In five or six minutes, however, the servant knocked at the door" (263-266). The servant delivered the news: "Father is no more." Gandhi rushed to his father's room, consumed with shame.

He reflected: "if animal passion had not blinded me, I should have been spared the torture of separation from my father during his last moments. I should have been massaging him, and he would have died in my arms" (263-267). Instead, it was his uncle who performed the final services for Karamchand.

## Lasting Impact

Gandhi regarded his absence at his father's deathbed as "a blot I have never been able to efface or forget" (264-267). He concluded that although his devotion to his parents knew no bounds, "it was weighed and found unpardonably wanting because my mind was at the same moment in the grip of lust." This event profoundly shaped Gandhi's later reflections on self-discipline and his eventual vow of brahmacharya (celibacy).

A child born to Kasturba shortly after these events survived only three or four days (264-268).
