# Narayan Hemchandra

Narayan Hemchandra was a Gujarati writer who crossed paths with Gandhi in London and left an impression disproportionate to his literary fame. With his "queer" dress, complete disregard for English grammar, and cheerful poverty, Hemchandra embodied a form of simplicity that was both comical and profound — and that resonated deeply with Gandhi's own emerging ideals.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi met Narayan Hemchandra at the house of Miss Manning of the National Indian Association. He had heard of him as a writer. Their first meeting was awkward — Hemchandra knew no English, and Gandhi was "tongue-tied" at social gatherings — but they quickly discovered a deep kinship:

> We met daily. There was a considerable amount of similarity between our thoughts and actions. Both of us were vegetarians. We would often have our lunch together.
>
> (017-004)

## Biography

### Early Life

Narayan Hemchandra was a Gujarati author who had traveled widely despite having no money. Gandhi describes his first impression:

> His dress was queer — a clumsy pair of trousers, a wrinkled, dirty, brown coat after the Parsi fashion, no necktie or collar, and a tasselled woollen cap. He grew a long beard.
>
> (017-002)

The description is affectionate but unsparing. Hemchandra was not trying to be eccentric; he simply had no interest in conventions of dress or appearance.

### Role in the Narrative

Hemchandra's role in the autobiography is to model a radical simplicity that Gandhi was only beginning to explore. Hemchandra traveled the world with virtually no money, living on the minimum of food and clothing:

> The minimum amount of food and the minimum amount of clothing suffice for me. And for this what little I get out of my books and from my friends is enough. I always travel third class. While going to America also I shall travel on deck.
>
> (017-003)

Gandhi adds: "Narayan Hemchandra's simplicity was all his own, and his frankness was on a par with it. Of pride he had not the slightest trace" (017-004).

**The Cardinal Manning episode**: The most memorable anecdote about Hemchandra is his determination to meet Cardinal Manning, the Catholic prelate famous for mediating the London dock strike. Upon hearing of Disraeli's tribute to the Cardinal's simplicity, Hemchandra declared: "Then I must see the sage" (017-006). When Gandhi pointed out the practical difficulties, Hemchandra replied with characteristic directness: "I must get you to write to him in my name. Tell him I am an author."

**Language and grammar**: Hemchandra knew no English grammar, and his attempts at the language were a source of affectionate comedy:

> Narayan Hemchandra was innocent of grammar. "Horse" was a verb with him and "run" a noun. I remember many such funny instances. But he was not to be baffled by his ignorance. My little knowledge of grammar could make no impression on him. Certainly he never regarded his ignorance of grammar as a matter for shame.
>
> (017-010)

## Historical Significance

Narayan Hemchandra represents a type that Gandhi deeply admired: the person who lives entirely according to inner conviction, untroubled by the opinions of others. His fearlessness, simplicity, and total absence of pride made him, in Gandhi's eyes, a kind of secular saint — not through ascetic discipline but through natural indifference to worldly concerns.

The friendship also highlights Gandhi's attraction to unconventional personalities. Throughout the autobiography, Gandhi is drawn to people who live by their own rules — [[raychandbhai]], [[leo-tolstoy]], Narayan Hemchandra — and each offers a different model of the authentic life that Gandhi himself was seeking.

## Key Passages

> Narayan Hemchandra's simplicity was all his own, and his frankness was on a par with it. Of pride he had not the slightest trace, excepting, of course, a rather undue regard for his own capacity as a writer.
>
> (017-004)
