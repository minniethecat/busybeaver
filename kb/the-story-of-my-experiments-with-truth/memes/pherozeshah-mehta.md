# Pherozeshah Mehta

Sir Pherozeshah Mehta (1845–1915) was one of the founding leaders of the [[indian-national-congress]] and the most formidable lawyer in [[bombay]]. To the young Gandhi — fresh from his failures in the Bombay High Court and the political intrigues of [[rajkot]] — Pherozeshah Mehta represented the pinnacle of Indian professional and political achievement. Their two brief encounters in 1896 shaped Gandhi's understanding of public leadership and political responsibility.

## Gandhi's First Impressions

Gandhi's awareness of Pherozeshah Mehta began in [[england]], where fellow Indian students spoke of him in almost legendary terms. Studying for the bar examination, Gandhi heard that "Pherozeshah Mehta's acumen, memory and ability" were extraordinary — but his English friend Mr. Pincutt reassured him that such gifts "were not essential to the making of a successful lawyer; honesty and industry were enough" (025-006).

When Gandhi sought help after being rebuffed by a British Political Agent in Rajkot, he turned to Pherozeshah Mehta, who happened to be visiting the city. This was Gandhi's first direct encounter with the "Lion of Bombay":

> I prepared to be awed by his presence. I had heard of the popular titles he had won, and it was not without some trepidation that I went into his room. But the awe gave place to surprise when I met him. He greeted me as an old friend.
>
> (051-010)

Pherozeshah listened carefully to Gandhi's appeal, but advised him not to pursue the matter against the Political Agent — the political costs would outweigh any personal vindication. It was a lesson in the limits of legal remedies and the realities of power.

## The Bombay Public Meeting

The second encounter proved decisive. In 1896, Gandhi returned to India to mobilize public opinion about the plight of Indians in [[south-africa]]. Armed with introductions from Justice Ranade and Justice Tyabji, he approached Pherozeshah Mehta for help organizing a public meeting in Bombay.

Despite his imposing reputation, Pherozeshah was quick to act. After barely two minutes of introduction: "'Gandhi,' said he, 'I see that I must help you. I must call a public meeting here.'" (051-011). He turned to his secretary and instructed him to arrange everything.

The meeting, held at the Framji Cowasji Institute, was Gandhi's first major public address in India. Pherozeshah presided — and then did something that taught Gandhi a lasting lesson about public leadership:

> To my surprise, at about 11 p.m. Sir Pherozeshah, who had been sitting through the meeting with the greatest patience, went away without saying anything. I was pained. But I was told afterwards that this was Sir Pherozeshah's habit. He was not able to sit beyond a certain hour, and he did not think it necessary to wait for the end of the meeting.
>
> (052-002)

## Lessons for the Young Gandhi

The two encounters with Pherozeshah Mehta taught Gandhi several enduring lessons:

| Lesson | Source |
|--------|--------|
| Professional eminence does not require extraordinary gifts — honesty and diligence suffice | Pincutt's advice, which Gandhi found confirmed in practice (025-006) |
| A great leader can make quick decisions and act on them immediately | Pherozeshah's instant commitment to the Bombay meeting (051-011) |
| Public leaders have their own rhythms and limitations — one should not expect what they cannot give | Pherozeshah's early departure from the meeting (052-002) |
| Political prudence sometimes outweighs personal justice | Pherozeshah's advice not to pursue the Rajkot Political Agent |

## Place in Indian Nationalism

Pherozeshah Mehta was part of the Moderate faction of the Congress — men like Dadabhai Naoroji, Gokhale, and Surendranath Banerjea who believed in constitutional agitation and gradual reform. Gandhi, whose own politics would later evolve toward mass [[satyagraha]], never adopted Pherozeshah's constitutionalism. But he always acknowledged his debt to the senior leader who, at a critical moment, chose to help a young unknown with a cause that hardly seemed to concern Bombay's elite.
