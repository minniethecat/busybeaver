# Benares

Benares (now Varanasi) is the ancient pilgrimage city on the banks of the Ganges where Gandhi stopped during his third-class tour of India in 1901. His visit to Benares exposed him to the darker side of organized religion — the exploitation of innocent pilgrims by greedy priests — and deepened his resolve to reform religious practice from within.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi arrived in Benares during a planned tour from Calcutta to Rajkot, traveling third class to "acquaint himself with the hardships of the third class passengers" (020-003). He halted at Benares for one day, along with Agra, Jaipur, and Palanpur, staying in dharmsalas or with pandas like an ordinary pilgrim (020-004). His teacher Gokhale had initially ridiculed the idea of such a tour, but Gandhi persisted.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

Upon arriving at Benares station in the morning, Gandhi was surrounded by numerous Brahmans competing to serve as his guide. He selected one who appeared "comparatively cleaner and better" than the rest — but this proved a mistake. The panda subjected him to the standard exploitation of pilgrims: demands for donations, pressure to purchase ritual items, and general harassment (020-010). Gandhi departed with a reinforced conviction that religious reform must address the corruption of priestly intermediaries.

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Benares episode crystallized a theme that runs throughout the autobiography: Gandhi's critique of hollow ritualism and priestly exploitation. His own deep religiosity was always counterbalanced by a fierce rejection of what he saw as the commercialization of faith. Benares, the holiest city of Hinduism, became in his narrative a symbol of how far religious practice had drifted from spiritual truth.

## Key Passages

> But I shall leave the third class passengers at that, and come to my experiences in Benares. I arrived there in the morning. I had decided to put up with a panda. Numerous Brahmans surrounded me, as soon as I got out of the train, and I selected one who struck me to be comparatively cleaner and better than the rest.
>
> (020-010)
