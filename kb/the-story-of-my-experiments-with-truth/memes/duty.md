## Overview

Duty (*dharma* in the Hindu tradition) is a central concept in Gandhi's autobiography, representing the moral obligation to act righteously regardless of personal cost. Gandhi frames many of his life decisions — from confessing his youthful transgressions to fighting for Indian rights in South Africa — through the lens of duty. Unlike a mere legal obligation, duty for Gandhi is intimately tied to truth and morality.

## Duty and Truthfulness

Gandhi presents the telling of truth as a painful but necessary duty. When recording his child marriage at thirteen, he writes: "It is my painful duty to have to record here my marriage at the age of thirteen" [003-001]. This framing — duty as something that compels honest self-disclosure even when uncomfortable — recurs throughout the autobiography. He sees the duty to truth as the foundation of all other duties.

The relationship between duty and truth is further refined in his professional life. In South Africa, Gandhi gave his first public speech on "observing truthfulness in business," rejecting the merchants' claim "that truth was not possible in business" [037-004]. For Gandhi, duty in professional conduct could not be separated from truthfulness.

## Duty, Desire, and Self-Restraint

A recurring tension in the autobiography is between duty and personal desire. As a young student, Gandhi recalls how "carnal lust got the better of what I regarded as my duty to study, and of what was even a greater duty, my devotion to my parents" [009-002]. This confession illustrates Gandhi's evolving understanding that duty requires the subordination of passion and impulse.

Early in his marriage, Gandhi also reflects on how he misconstrued mutual obligation: "Her duty was easily converted into my right to exact faithfulness from her" [004-002]. This self-critical observation marks his maturation from possessive love to a genuine ethics of reciprocal duty.

## Duty in Public and Professional Life

Gandhi's sense of duty extended beyond the personal to the civic sphere. Confronted with racial discrimination on a train in South Africa, he deliberated: "Should I fight for my rights or go back to India...? It would be cowardice to run back to India without fulfilling my obligation" [033-023]. Here duty becomes the decisive factor — his obligation to complete his legal case overrode the humiliation he suffered.

Duty also shaped his understanding of nonviolence and social action. During the Indian struggle in South Africa, he reported that "over ten thousand suffering imprisonment" [046-013] willingly accepted hardship as a duty in the satyagraha campaign, transforming suffering from passive endurance into active moral witness.
