# Hardvar

Hardvar (also known as Haridwar) is an ancient pilgrimage city on the banks of the Ganges River in present-day Uttarakhand, India. Gandhi visited Hardvar in 1915 to attend the Kumbh Mela, a massive Hindu pilgrimage fair held every twelve years, and his experiences there profoundly shaped his approach to public service and personal discipline in India.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi visited Hardvar in 1915, the year of the Kumbha fair, shortly after his return from South Africa. Though he was "by no means eager to attend the fair," his primary motivation was to meet Mahatma Munshiramji at his Gurukul. Gokhale's Servants of India Society had sent a volunteer corps to serve at the Kumbha, and Gandhi was invited to send the Phoenix party to assist them ([1915-P02]). Maganlal Gandhi had already preceded him, and Gandhi joined the band upon returning from Rangoon.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

The Hardvar visit proved to be a watershed moment for Gandhi's understanding of his role in India. He was struck by the massive gathering — "seventeen lakhs of men" reported to be there — and spent his days "mostly keeping sitting in the tent giving *darshan* and holding religious and other discussions with numerous pilgrims" ([1915-P07]). He was followed everywhere, "even to the bathing ghat by these darshan-seekers," and they did not leave him alone during meals ([1915-P07]).

The Phoenix party took on the scavenger's work, covering latrine pits with earth — work that Gandhi's stay in Shantiniketan had taught them would be "our special function in India" ([1915-P07]). Meanwhile, Gandhi grappled with the moral contradictions of the pilgrimage: the juxtaposition of genuine piety with the "hypocrisy" and "iniquity" he witnessed around him ([1915-P15]).

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Hardvar experience crystallized two major personal resolutions. Troubled by the lavish entertainment he had received from hosts in Calcutta and Rangoon, Gandhi pledged "never whilst in India to take more than five articles in twenty-four hours, and never to eat after dark" — a vow of dietary simplicity with "no exception on any account whatsoever" ([1915-P17]). This was characteristic of Gandhi's practice of imposing "disciplinary resolutions" on himself during moments of moral reckoning.

The visit also helped Gandhi decide where to settle in India. As he later reflected, "The Hardvar experiences proved for me to be of inestimable value. They helped me in no small way to decide where I was to live and what I was to do" ([1915-P63]). The pilgrimage to the Kumbha fair marked his second visit to the city ([1915-P71]).

## Historical Context

The Kumbh Mela is one of the world's largest religious gatherings, rotating between four sacred river sites: Hardvar (Haridwar), Prayag (Allahabad), Nashik, and Ujjain. The Hardvar Kumbh of 1915 drew an estimated 1.7 million pilgrims. Gandhi arrived at a time when orthodox Hindu practices regarding caste purity and water consumption were strictly observed — he noted that pilgrims suffering from thirst on the journey "could not persuade orthodox Hindus to take water, if it was 'Mussalmani'" ([1915-P05]). The tensions between traditional orthodoxy and Gandhi's evolving philosophy of religious equality and sanitation reform were sharply highlighted during this visit.
