## Overview

Self-restraint (_samyama_ in Sanskrit) was a cornerstone of Mahatma Gandhi's ethical and spiritual practice. More than mere abstention, he understood it as an active, positive discipline that strengthened the will and purified the character.

## Jail as Teacher

Gandhi's first experience of imprisonment in 1908 proved unexpectedly instructive for his practice of self-restraint. He observed that certain prison regulations — such as finishing the last meal before sunset — were precisely the kind of rules "that should be voluntarily observed by a brahmachari, that is, one desiring to practise self-restraint" [106-001]. Upon release, he voluntarily imposed these same rules on himself, noting that "inhibitions imposed from without rarely succeed, but when they are self-imposed, they have a decidedly salutary effect" [106-002].

## Dietary Discipline

Food became a primary arena for Gandhi's experiments in self-restraint. He adopted and maintained a saltless and pulseless diet for extended periods, finding that "the experiment stimulated the inclination for self-restraint" and that his senses became "more subdued than ever" [106-010]. He extended these dietary practices to willing coworkers in South Africa with what he described as good results. For Gandhi, the moral dimension was clear: "The diet of a man of self-restraint must be different from that of a man of pleasure, just as their ways of life are different" [106-011].

## Brahmacharya and Beyond

Self-restraint found its fullest expression in Gandhi's lifelong vow of _brahmacharya_ (celibacy). He acknowledged frankly the difficulty of this path: "I must confess that I had not then fully realized the magnitude and immensity of the task I undertook. The difficulties are even today staring me in the face" [102-009]. Yet he persisted, regarding a life without this discipline as "insipid and animal-like." Self-restraint, in Gandhi's framework, was not repression but liberation — a way of channeling energy from sensual gratification toward spiritual and social service.
