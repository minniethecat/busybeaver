# Sir Edwin Arnold

Sir Edwin Arnold (1832–1904) was an English poet, journalist, and orientalist best known for his epic poem *The Light of Asia* (1879) and his English translation of the Bhagavad Gita, *The Song Celestial* (1885). For Gandhi, Arnold was both a personal acquaintance in London and, more importantly, the translator whose work opened the door to the Gita's spiritual wisdom and kindled his lifelong devotion to the text.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi met Arnold in London during his student years (1888–1891). Arnold lived in Bayswater, the same London locality where Gandhi, newly converted to vegetarianism, founded a vegetarian club. Gandhi invited Arnold to serve as the club's Vice-President [017-010]. Their connection, however, was more intellectual than personal — Arnold's translations of Indian sacred texts reached Gandhi at a formative moment and shaped his religious and philosophical outlook.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in Gravesend, Kent, in 1832, Edwin Arnold was educated at King's College, London, and University College, Oxford. After a distinguished academic career, he served as principal of the Government Sanskrit College in Poona (Pune), India, during the 1850s — an experience that gave him deep exposure to Indian languages and religious literature. He later became a prominent journalist, serving as editor of the *Daily Telegraph* in London.

### Role in the Narrative

Arnold enters Gandhi's story at two significant points. First, as a neighbor and the vice-president of Gandhi's short-lived vegetarian club in Bayswater — a modest but formative experience in organizing and leading institutions [017-010]. Second, and far more consequentially, through his translations. When two Theosophist brothers introduced Gandhi to the Bhagavad Gita, they were reading Arnold's *The Song Celestial* [020-001]. Gandhi, who had never read the Gita in Sanskrit or Gujarati, was moved by the text and later came to regard Arnold's translation as the finest English rendering: "He has been faithful to the text, and yet it does not read like a translation" [020-002].

The brothers also recommended *The Light of Asia*, Arnold's celebrated poetic account of the Buddha's life. Gandhi read it "with even greater interest than...the Bhagavad Gita" and found that, once begun, he "could not leave off" [020-003]. These two works — Arnold's Gita and his life of the Buddha — combined with the Sermon on the Mount to form the triad of sacred texts that Gandhi sought to harmonize in his youthful spiritual explorations [020-005].

## Historical Significance

Sir Edwin Arnold occupies a pivotal place in modern inter-religious literary history. *The Light of Asia* was a publishing phenomenon in the late Victorian era, introducing millions of Western readers to the life and teachings of the Buddha. His Gita translation, *The Song Celestial*, became the standard English version for generations and was the edition read by figures including Gandhi and, later, Swami Vivekananda. Arnold's work represents one of the earliest and most influential bridges between Indian spiritual literature and the English-speaking world — a bridge that, in Gandhi's case, directly contributed to the formation of the philosophy of nonviolence.
