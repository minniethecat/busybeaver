# Renunciation in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Renunciation was, for Gandhi, the practical discipline through which spiritual freedom was won. He distinguished sharply between external renunciation — the giving up of objects — and internal renunciation — the letting go of desires. Only the latter, he came to believe, was genuine.

## Renunciation of Objects vs. Renunciation of Desires

The distinction was crystallized for Gandhi in a verse by the Gujarati poet Nishkulanand, which he quotes at a moment of intense self-reproach:

> "Renunciation of objects, without the renunciation of desires, is short-lived, however hard you may try."
>
> (005-004)

Gandhi returns to this verse twice more in the autobiography — once in the context of vows (140-003) and again in the context of *brahmacharya* (140-005). Each time, the lesson is the same: merely abstaining from a thing without extinguishing the desire for it is not renunciation but suppression, and suppression always fails.

## Renunciation as the Highest Religion

Gandhi's first encounter with renunciation as a spiritual ideal came through comparative reading of the Gita, the *Light of Asia*, and the Sermon on the Mount:

> My young mind tried to unify the teaching of the Gita, The Light of Asia and the Sermon on the Mount. That renunciation was the highest form of religion appealed to me greatly.
>
> (030-014)

The Sermon on the Mount's teaching of non-resistance to evil — "whosoever shall smite thee on thy right cheek, turn to him the other also" — struck Gandhi as the Christian expression of the same principle the Gita taught as *anasakti* (non-attachment) and the Buddha taught as *vairagya*.

## Vows as Instruments of Renunciation

Gandhi's most original contribution to the practice of renunciation was his understanding of vows. Far from being a restriction on freedom, a vow was "like a passage from libertinism to a real monogamous marriage" (140-002). The vow fixed the will, eliminating the daily struggle of indecision:

> Where the desire is gone, a vow of renunciation is the natural and inevitable fruit.
>
> (140-006)

This insight was forged in the Zulu "Rebellion" of 1906, during which Gandhi took the vow of *brahmacharya* (celibacy). The six weeks of ambulance service clarified for him that "mere effort" without a binding vow "meant ignorance of the certain fact that the serpent is bound to kill me" (140-002).

## Renunciation and Brahmacharya

Gandhi understood *brahmacharya* — control of the senses in thought, word, and deed — as the fullest expression of renunciation. But he was honest about its difficulty:

> There is no limit to the possibilities of renunciation even as there is none to those of brahmacharya. Such brahmacharya is impossible of attainment by limited effort.
>
> (140-007)

The practice of renunciation was, like all Gandhi's experiments, asymptotic — one approached the ideal without ever fully attaining it.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[truth]]**: Renunciation clears the ground for truth by removing the attachments that distort perception.
- **[[brahmacharya]]**: The most demanding form of renunciation — control of the senses at every level.
- **[[vows]]**: The instruments that make renunciation binding and effective.
- **[[vegetarianism]]**: The everyday practice of renunciation — abstaining from meat as a discipline of desire.
- **[[desire]]**: The obstacle that renunciation seeks to overcome.
