## Overview

The Bhagavad Gita was the most important spiritual text in Mahatma Gandhi's life. He first encountered it during his student years in England through two Theosophist brothers, who invited him to read the original Sanskrit alongside Sir Edwin Arnold's English translation, _The Song Celestial_ [020-001]. Despite feeling ashamed that he had not previously read this divine poem in either Sanskrit or Gujarati, Gandhi began studying it and was profoundly moved.

## Gandhi's Spiritual Anchor

Gandhi regarded the Gita as "the book par excellence for the knowledge of Truth" and credited it with providing "invaluable help in my moments of gloom" [020-002]. The scripture became his constant companion and reference point for ethical decision-making. He later formed reading groups to study the Gita with friends and colleagues, including a group that read it together in Johannesburg in 1903 [082-002].

## Influence on Gandhi's Philosophy

The Gita shaped several core aspects of Gandhi's thinking. Its teaching on non-attachment reinforced his commitment to selfless action. The second chapter's emphasis on self-restraint and equanimity deeply influenced his practice of self-discipline [108-011]. When discussing spiritual paths with Christian interlocutors, Gandhi invoked the Gita's _bhakti-marga_ (path of devotion) as his chosen approach to the divine [072-006]. He also found resonance between the Gita's teachings and the Sermon on the Mount in the New Testament, particularly regarding non-resistance to evil [020-005].
