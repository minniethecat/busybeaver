# Truth

Truth (Satya) is the sovereign principle of Gandhi's entire philosophy. He famously declared that "Truth is God" rather than the conventional "God is Truth," making truth the ultimate reality to which even the divine must conform. His autobiography is explicitly framed as "My Experiments with Truth" (P05-005), emphasizing that truth is not a doctrine to be asserted but a reality to be discovered through systematic experimentation. "Truth became my sole objective. It began to grow in magnitude" (010-013).

## Definition

Gandhi equated Truth with God but inverted the traditional formulation. "But for me, truth is the sovereign principle, which includes numerous other principles" (P05-005). By placing Truth above God, he made the pursuit accessible to atheists and theists alike: one did not need to believe in a deity to commit to truthfulness. Truth for Gandhi was not merely factual accuracy but a comprehensive moral and metaphysical reality. "Truth is like a vast tree, which yields more and more fruit the more it is nourished" (065-005). Every act of truth-telling, every honest experiment, nourished the tree.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's devotion to truth was shaped by multiple sources. His mother's religious piety, the Jain emphasis on truthfulness as a cardinal virtue, the Bhagavad Gita's teachings on dharma, and Tolstoy's Christian anarchism all contributed. But Gandhi's distinctive contribution was to make truth an experimental discipline rather than a revealed doctrine. He absorbed the Indian tradition of *satya* (truth as cosmic order) but insisted that it must be verified in personal experience. His reading of Ruskin's *Unto This Last* and Thoreau's *Civil Disobedience* reinforced his conviction that truth was not merely a private virtue but a public force.

## Gandhi's Practice

### The Autobiography as Experiment

Gandhi's autobiography is his most sustained experiment with truth. "I claim to be a worshipper of Truth. It is my painful duty to have to record my setbacks in the pursuit of Truth" (003-001). By narrating his failures as well as his successes, he demonstrated that truth-seeking required honesty about one's own shortcomings. The autobiography is not a hagiography but a laboratory notebook.

### Truth in Public Life

Truth was also Gandhi's standard for public conduct. "A devotee of Truth may not do anything in deference to mere convention. He must always hold himself open to correction" (116-009). This meant that political positions must be revisable in light of new evidence, and that satyagraha must always remain open to the possibility that one's opponent might be partially right. Truth required humility, not dogmatism.

### The Unity of Truth and Nonviolence

Gandhi insisted that Truth could only be pursued through nonviolent means. Violence distorted perception and made truth unreachable. "In the march towards Truth, anger, selfishness, hatred, etc., naturally give way, for otherwise Truth would be impossible to attain" (114-011). Ahimsa was not merely compatible with the search for truth — it was the only method by which truth could be approached.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Truth is the sun around which all other Gandhian concepts orbit. Ahimsa is the means of pursuing truth; Satyagraha is truth applied to social conflict; Brahmacharya is self-purification for truth-seeking; Swaraj is the social condition in which truth can flourish. "Truth is like a vast tree" (065-005) — every other principle is a branch growing from this root. Gandhi's genius was to make truth not just a philosophical abstraction but a practical, testable, daily discipline.
