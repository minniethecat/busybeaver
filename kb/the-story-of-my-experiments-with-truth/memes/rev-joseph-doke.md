# Rev. Joseph Doke

Rev. Joseph J. Doke (1861–1913) was an English Baptist minister in Johannesburg who became one of Gandhi's closest European friends in South Africa. He authored the first biography of Gandhi, *M. K. Gandhi: An Indian Patriot in South Africa* (1909), and his family sheltered Gandhi during a period of danger.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Doke entered Gandhi's life during the crisis of the Black Plague outbreak in Johannesburg. Gandhi's strong letter to the press holding the Municipality responsible for the outbreak "secured me Mr. Henry Polak, and was partly responsible for the friendship of the late Rev. Joseph Doke" [093-007]. Doke's friendship with Gandhi transcended racial and religious boundaries — a white Baptist minister and an Indian Hindu lawyer finding common ground in their shared commitment to justice and service.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in England in 1861, Joseph Doke was ordained as a Baptist minister and served in Johannesburg, South Africa, during the tumultuous years of the early twentieth century. His congregation included both Europeans and, unusually for the time, non-Europeans.

### Role in the Narrative

Doke's most significant contribution to Gandhi's story lies outside the autobiography proper — during the satyagraha campaign of 1908–1909, when Gandhi was assaulted by Pathan opponents, Doke and his family sheltered the injured Gandhi in their home. Doke also accompanied Gandhi to London in 1909 as part of the Transvaal Indian deputation, and his biography, published that same year, helped introduce Gandhi to the wider world.

Within the autobiography, Doke appears as one of the "new friends" Gandhi made during the satyagraha years, alongside Henry Polak and Hermann Kallenbach — Europeans who saw in Gandhi's cause something worthy of their devotion.

## Historical Significance

Joseph Doke's biography of Gandhi holds a unique place in Gandhian literature as the first book-length account of Gandhi's life and work, written by a close associate who witnessed the development of satyagraha firsthand. Doke represented a current within Protestant Christianity — the social gospel — that found natural affinity with Gandhi's spiritually grounded activism. His friendship demonstrated that the racial barriers of colonial South Africa could be transcended through shared moral commitment.
