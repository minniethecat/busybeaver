## Overview

The Satyagraha Ashram was founded on May 25, 1915, shortly after Gandhi's return from South Africa. After considering locations including Hardvar (urged by Shraddhanandji), Vaidyanathadham (recommended by Calcutta friends), and Rajkot, Gandhi chose Ahmedabad — drawn by its status as a Gujarati cultural center, a historic handloom weaving hub, and the prospect of financial support from its wealthy citizens (4269-1).

## The Choice of Ahmedabad

Gandhi had a predilection for Ahmedabad. As a Gujarati, he believed he could render the greatest service to the country through the Gujarati language. The city was an ancient centre of handloom weaving, making it likely the most favourable field for reviving the cottage industry of hand-spinning. As the capital of Gujarat, monetary help from its wealthy citizens would be more available there than elsewhere (4269-2).

The question of untouchability was naturally among the subjects discussed with the Ahmedabad friends. Gandhi made it clear from the start that he would take the first opportunity of admitting an untouchable candidate to the Ashram if otherwise worthy. A Vaishnava friend responded self-complacently: "Where is the untouchable who will satisfy your condition?" (4269-3)

For accommodation, Sjt. Jivanlal Desai, a barrister in Ahmedabad, was the principal helper. He offered to let, and they decided to hire, his Kochrab bungalow (4269-4).

## The Name

Selecting the name for the new community required careful deliberation. Among the suggestions were "Sevashram" (the abode of service) and "Tapovan" (the abode of austerities). Gandhi liked the name "Sevashram" but felt it lacked emphasis on the method of service. "Tapovan" seemed pretentious — though tapas (austerities) was dear to them, they could not presume to be tapasvins. Their creed was devotion to truth, and their business was the search for and insistence on truth. Gandhi wanted to acquaint India with the method he had tried in South Africa and test in India the extent to which its application might be possible. The companions selected the name "Satyagraha Ashram" as conveying both their goal and their method of service (4270-1).

Sir Gurudas Banerji, reviewing the draft code of rules, suggested adding humility as an observance, believing the younger generation sadly lacked it. Gandhi, while noticing the fault, resisted making humility a formal vow:

> "The true connotation of humility is self-effacement. Self-effacement is moksha (salvation), and whilst it cannot, by itself, be an observance, there may be other observances necessary for its attainment. Service without humility is selfishness and egotism." (4271-1)

## The Founding Community

The Ashram community at founding consisted of about twenty-five men and women. Five Tamil youngsters had accompanied Gandhi from South Africa, and the rest came from different parts of the country. There were about thirteen Tamilians in total. All had their meals in a common kitchen and strove to live as one family (4270-2).

### The Untouchability Test

Within months, the Ashram faced its defining test. Amritlal Thakkar wrote to ask whether an untouchable family might be admitted. Gandhi and his companions agreed without hesitation, and Dudabhai, his wife Danibehn, and their daughter Lakshmi were accepted into the Ashram. The decision provoked an immediate and severe crisis (4272-1).

The man operating the well — drawing water for the entire Ashram — objected to the untouchable family using the same water. His hostility created a tense atmosphere. More devastatingly, all monetary support was cut off. The wealthy patrons who had promised to fund the Ashram withdrew their assistance in protest against the admission of untouchables (4272-2).

When funds ran out entirely, Maganlal Gandhi came to Gandhi with a warning: there was nothing for the next month. Gandhi's response was unflinching:

> "Then we shall go to the untouchables' quarter." (4273-1)

This was not a bluff or a negotiating tactic — Gandhi was prepared to abandon the physical Ashram rather than abandon the principle. Help arrived at the last moment: an anonymous Sheth drove up to the Ashram and offered thirteen thousand rupees. This timely assistance allowed the Ashram to survive its first test of principle. The unknown benefactor, Gandhi later reflected, was in his view a visible sign of divine assistance. The test had purified the Ashram and confirmed its commitment to the abolition of untouchability as a living practice, not merely a theoretical position (4273-2).

## Significance

The founding of the Satyagraha Ashram represented the transplantation of Gandhi's South African method to Indian soil. The Ashram was not merely a residence but a living laboratory — a place where the vows of truth, non-possession, celibacy, control of the palate, non-stealing, and fearlessness could be tested in community. The admission of an untouchable family, and the community's willingness to face destitution rather than compromise, established the Ashram's moral credibility from the outset and demonstrated that the practice of satyagraha extended beyond political campaigns into the intimate fabric of daily life.
