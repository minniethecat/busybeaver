# Surat

Surat is a major port city in Gujarat, appearing in Gandhi's autobiography as a transit point during his arrest and deportation in April 1919. Gandhi was on his way to the Punjab — where martial law had been imposed and Sir Michael O'Dwyer ruled with a heavy hand after the protests against the Rowlatt Act — when he was served with an order prohibiting entry into the province. Arrested at Palwal, shuttled between police barracks at Mathura, then transported in a goods train towards Bombay, Gandhi was "made over to the charge of another police officer" at Surat [155-013]. From here he was taken to Bombay, where a vast, excited crowd awaited him — and where a mounted police charge would produce what he called "a most dreadful spectacle" [155-018].

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi reached Surat not as a free traveler but as a prisoner being shuttled across India by colonial authorities uncertain what to do with him. He had been arrested at Palwal railway station while traveling to the Punjab in response to "a pressing invitation, not to foment unrest, but to allay it" [155-008]. Rather than comply with the ban and turn back, Gandhi chose to disobey the order — an early deliberate act of civil disobedience in India — and "suffer the penalty for disobeying it" [155-009].

The authorities, caught off guard by his refusal, scrambled to handle him. They woke him at 4 a.m., put him in a goods train, then later upgraded him to a first-class compartment after Inspector Bowring arrived. At Surat, the chain of custody changed hands, and the final leg of the journey brought him to Bombay — where he was told he was "now free" [155-013].

## In the Narrative

### A Prisoner in Transit

The journey through Surat was part of a bewildering sequence: arrested, prohibited from entering the Punjab, refused to comply, taken to police barracks at Mathura, roused at 4 a.m., put in a goods wagon, transferred at Sawai Madhopur to a first-class compartment, handed over at Surat, and finally released — sort of — in Bombay. Inspector Bowring had tried to persuade him to return voluntarily; Gandhi refused. "Whereupon the officer, seeing no other course, told me that he would have to enforce the law against me" [155-012]. But even the officers seemed unsure what that meant in practice.

### The Aftermath in Bombay

What greeted Gandhi upon his arrival revealed the consequences of his arrest. His friend told him "that the news of my arrest had incensed the people and roused them to a pitch of mad frenzy. 'An outbreak is apprehended every minute near Pydhuni'" [155-013]. Umar Sobani and Anasuyabehn arrived immediately, begging him to come and pacify the crowd. The procession that formed when the people saw him was met by mounted police who charged with lances, and Gandhi witnessed firsthand the violence that colonial authorities were willing to inflict on unarmed crowds.

## Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Surat itself was simply a handover point — a police jurisdiction boundary. But the episode of which Surat was part marked a critical escalation in Gandhi's confrontation with the Raj. His willingness to be arrested rather than comply with a ban on entering the Punjab was an early rehearsal of the satyagraha method on Indian soil. And the scenes in Bombay that followed — the mounted police charge that Gandhi witnessed immediately after passing through Surat — gave him searing firsthand evidence of the violence that the British state would deploy against peaceful protest.
