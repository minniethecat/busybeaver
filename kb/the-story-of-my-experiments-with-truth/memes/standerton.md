# Standerton

Standerton is a small village in the Transvaal region of South Africa (now Mpumalanga province) where Gandhi's stagecoach halted for the night during his first journey from [[durban]] to [[pretoria]] in 1893. The stop at Standerton marked a turning point in what had been a terrifying journey: here Gandhi was met by Indian residents who had been alerted by telegram, providing him with both physical shelter and emotional solidarity after he had been assaulted by a white passenger on the coach.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi reached Standerton after enduring a harrowing coach journey from [[charlestown]]. In those days, "there was no railway, in those days, between Charlestown and Johannesburg, but only a stagecoach, which halted at Standerton for the night en route" [034-001]. The journey was made worse by the aggression of the coach leader — the same man who had earlier forced Gandhi to sit on a footboard. Throughout the journey, the man "cast an angry look at me now and then and, pointing his finger at me, growled: 'Take care, let me once get to Standerton and I shall show you what I do'" [034-005].

The threat was terrifying enough that Gandhi "sat speechless and prayed to God to help me" [034-005]. When the coach finally arrived after dark, Gandhi "heaved a sigh of relief on seeing some Indian faces" [034-006]. The Indian community — having received a telegram from [[dada-abdulla]] — had come to receive him and escort him to Isa Sheth's shop, where they "gathered round me. I told them all that I had gone through. They were very sorry to hear it and comforted me by relating to me their own bitter experiences" [034-006].

## In the Narrative

### The Complaint to the Coach Company

That evening, Gandhi wrote to the agent of the Coach Company, "narrating everything that had happened, and drawing his attention to the threat his man had held out." He also requested "an assurance that he would accommodate me with the other passengers inside the coach when we started the next morning" [034-007].

The agent's reply brought some relief: "From Standerton we have a bigger coach with different men in charge. The man complained of will not be there tomorrow, and you will have a seat with the other passengers" [034-007]. As Gandhi wrote, this "somewhat relieved me. I had, of course, no intention of proceeding against the man who had assaulted me, and so the chapter of the assault closed there."

### From Standerton to Johannesburg

The contrast between Standerton and the next destination was stark: "Standerton is a small village and Johannesburg a big city" [034-009]. Gandhi's arrival in Johannesburg brought a fresh set of humiliations — most notably being turned away from the Grand National Hotel with the polite fiction that it was "full up" — but also the warm welcome of [[abdul-gani-sheth]], who "had a hearty laugh over the story of my experience at the hotel" and asked the pointed question: "How ever did you expect to be admitted to a hotel?" [034-009].

## Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Standerton exemplified the protective network of Indian merchants that sustained Gandhi through his early South African travels. As in [[maritzburg]] and [[charlestown]], the Indian trading community — connected by telegraph and bound by ties of kinship and commerce — provided crucial support that allowed Gandhi to survive and eventually challenge the system of racial discrimination he was encountering.

The Standerton experience also highlighted Gandhi's characteristic response to injustice at this stage of his life: he complained formally through proper channels (writing to the coach agent) but declined to pursue legal action against his abuser. This pattern — assertive complaint without retribution — would later evolve, as Gandhi developed the philosophy of [[satyagraha]] that channelled grievance into non-violent resistance rather than personal vengeance.
