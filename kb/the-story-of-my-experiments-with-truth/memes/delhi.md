# Delhi

Delhi, as the seat of British imperial authority, appears at several pivotal moments in Gandhi's autobiography. It was the site of the Viceroy's War Conference in 1918, the dramatic observance of the 1919 hartal against the Rowlatt Act, and a frequent transit point in Gandhi's ceaseless travels across India.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi's most significant engagement with Delhi came in 1918, when Viceroy Lord Chelmsford invited various Indian leaders to a war conference in the capital. Gandhi attended despite deep reservations — principally the exclusion of leaders like the Ali brothers, who were then in jail (150-002). Upon reaching Delhi, Dinabandhu Andrews raised a further moral objection: how could Gandhi participate if Britain had entered into secret treaties with other European powers? Gandhi knew nothing of the treaties but accepted Andrews's word as sufficient ground for concern (150-009).

Nevertheless, Gandhi spoke a single sentence at the conference in support of the war resolution — a decision he later described as having "considerable significance" and one that burdened his conscience (151-003). While in Delhi, he wrote a detailed letter to the Viceroy explaining his position and stating what the people expected from the Government.

The Khilafat question had not yet assumed the shape it would later take, but Gandhi had already intended to submit the Muslim case to the Viceroy during this Delhi visit (150-008).

## In the Narrative

Delhi reappears dramatically in connection with the 1919 hartal. The city observed the protest on March 30, before the nationwide date of April 6. Under the leadership of Swami Shraddhanandji and Hakim Ajmal Khan Saheb, Hindus and Muslims "seemed united like one man" — a display of unity the authorities could not tolerate (155-002). The Delhi hartal became a precursor to the wider unrest that would culminate in the Punjab crisis and the Jallianwala Bagh massacre.

## Key Passages

> In response to the invitation I went to Delhi. I had, however, objections to taking part in the conference, the principal one being the exclusion from it of leaders like the Ali brothers. They were then in jail. (150-002)

> Delhi had never witnessed a hartal like that before. Hindus and Mussalmans seemed united like one man. Swami Shraddhanandji was invited to deliver a speech in the Jumma Masjid which he did. (155-002)

> There was one undertaking that I had to fulfil while yet in Delhi. I had to write a letter to the Viceroy. I felt it my duty both in the interests of the Government and of the people to explain how and why I attended the conference. (151-003)
