# Champaran Satyagraha

The Champaran Satyagraha of 1917 was Gandhi's first major application of satyagraha in India. At the invitation of local peasants, he investigated the tinkathia system — a colonial law that forced indigo farmers to grow indigo on a portion of their land and sell it at fixed prices to British planters. The campaign's success established Gandhi's method on Indian soil and is widely regarded as the dawn of the mass phase of India's independence movement.

## Background

Indigo cultivation in Champaran had been declining for decades due to German synthetic dyes, but British planters continued to extract rents and forced labor from peasants under the tinkathia system. Peasants had petitioned for relief for years without success. Local leaders, including Raj Kumar Shukla, traveled to Lucknow to persuade Gandhi to take up their cause.

## Timeline

Gandhi arrived in Champaran in April 1917. The British district administration immediately ordered him to leave. Gandhi refused — his first act of civil disobedience in India — and was arrested and tried at Motihari. Faced with popular support and Gandhi's willingness to accept punishment, the government backed down and appointed him to an official inquiry committee. The committee's report vindicated the peasants, and the tinkathia system was abolished.

## Gandhi's Role

Gandhi described Champaran as "a bold experiment with Truth and Ahimsa" (140-001). He approached the campaign not as a lawyer but as an investigator and mediator, recording thousands of peasant testimonies and building a case so thorough that the government could not refute it.

## Historical Significance

Champaran demonstrated that satyagraha could work in India as it had in South Africa. It established Gandhi as a leader who could deliver concrete results for ordinary Indians, and it inaugurated the method — investigation, negotiation, nonviolent pressure — that would characterize all his subsequent campaigns.
