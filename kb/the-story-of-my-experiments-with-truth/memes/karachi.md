# Karachi

Karachi is the port city in Sindh (now in Pakistan) where Gandhi stopped in 1896 during his India-wide tour to raise awareness about the plight of Indians in South Africa. His public meetings in Karachi drew "unbounded enthusiasm," a reception that surprised Gandhi himself and demonstrated the resonance of the South African Indian cause across the subcontinent.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi visited Karachi in 1896 as part of a speaking tour that covered major Indian cities including Calcutta, Madras, Bombay, and Poona. He was on a mission to publicize the discriminatory treatment of Indians in South Africa, particularly in Natal and the Transvaal. The Karachi visit was one of the earliest stops on a tour that confirmed Gandhi's emerging gifts as a public mobilizer.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

Gandhi's reception in Karachi exceeded expectations. He recorded that there were "fine meetings everywhere, and there was unbounded enthusiasm" (135-009). The gatherings in Karachi demonstrated that the South African Indian cause resonated with the broader Indian public, even in cities far from the centers of political activity. Gandhi himself noted: "I had not expected anything like it when the agitation was launched" (135-009), suggesting that the Karachi response was a turning point in his understanding of mass politics.

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Karachi visit was part of Gandhi's first major experience with large-scale public mobilization in India. The enthusiasm he encountered — echoed in Calcutta where he wrote "Just as in Karachi, here also there was unbounded enthusiasm" (135-015) — must have reinforced his conviction that his work in South Africa had relevance for India itself. This tour prefigured the kind of mass campaigns Gandhi would later lead across the subcontinent.
