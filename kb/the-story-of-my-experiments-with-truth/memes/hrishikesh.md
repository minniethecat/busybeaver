# Hrishikesh

Hrishikesh was a pilgrimage town on the Ganges that Gandhi visited after attending the Hardvar Kumbh Mela in 1915. His journey on foot to the Lakshman Jhula — a hanging bridge some distance from the town — became the occasion for encounters with sannyasis and for one of his earliest recorded reflections on the meaning and limits of religious symbols.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi traveled to Hrishikesh at the urging of friends who pressed him to see the Lakshman Jhula during his stay at Hardvar. He undertook the pilgrimage on foot in two stages, accompanied by members of the Phoenix Settlement party who had followed him from South Africa to India. The natural beauty of the region charmed him, and he "bowed his head in reverence to our ancestors for their sense of the beautiful in Nature." (4233-001) (4255-001)

## In the Narrative

### Encounters with Sannyasis

Many sannyasis called on Gandhi at Hrishikesh, and one in particular was drawn to him. The Swami engaged Gandhi in discussion about religious practice, but disagreed sharply on the question of wearing the sacred thread. Where Gandhi argued against it as a fetish that encouraged religious superiority, the Swami maintained that outward symbols were necessary so long as different religions existed. (4235-001) (4251-001)

### The Sacred Thread Debate

Gandhi's position on the sacred thread crystallized at Hrishikesh and remained essentially unchanged thereafter. He acknowledged that every religion might need "some outward distinctive symbol," but insisted that "when the symbol is made into a fetish and an instrument of proving the superiority of one's religion over others', it is fit only to be discarded." The sacred thread, he concluded, did not appear to him to be "a means of uplifting Hinduism." (4251-001)

### The Desecration of Holy Places

The beauty of Hrishikesh was marred for Gandhi by the same disregard for cleanliness he had observed at Hardvar. He was filled with agony to see people "performing natural functions on the thoroughfares and river banks, when they could easily have gone a little farther away from public haunts." This recurring observation reinforced his belief that the uplift of Indian society required not merely political change but a transformation of daily habits. (4257-001)

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Hrishikesh deepened Gandhi's engagement with three themes that would define his mature thought: the relationship between inner spirituality and outward symbols, the imperative of sanitation as a moral duty, and the recognition that India's sacred geography was being degraded by the very pilgrims who came to honor it. (4255-001)

## Key Passages

> I was charmed with the natural scenery about Hrishikesh and the Lakshman Jhula, and bowed my head in reverence to our ancestors for their sense of the beautiful in Nature, and for their foresight in investing beautiful manifestations of Nature with a religious significance. (4255-001)
