# Faith in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Faith for Gandhi was not a static creed but an evolving experiment. His autobiography traces the journey from a boy who "had no living faith in God" to a man for whom faith became the bedrock of public action.

## The Seed of Faith

Gandhi's earliest encounter with faith came not from scripture or priest but from his nurse Rambha, who taught him to repeat *Ramanama* as a cure for his childhood fear of ghosts:

> I had more faith in her than in her remedy, and so at a tender age I began repeating Ramanama to cure my fear of ghosts and spirits. This was of course, short-lived, but the good seed sown in childhood was not sown in vain. I think it is due to the seed sown by that good woman Rambha that today Ramanama is an infallible remedy for me.
>
> (010-004)

Faith began as trust in a person — the nurse he loved — before it became trust in a practice. The trajectory from "faith in her" to "faith in Ramanama" would prove paradigmatic: Gandhi's faith always remained personal and relational rather than doctrinal.

## Faith Tested and Deepened

Gandhi's adolescence brought a crisis of belief. Despite being born in the Vaishnava faith and regularly visiting the *Haveli* temple, the "glitter and pomp" of temple worship left him cold, and rumours of immorality practised there "lost all interest in it" (009-010). Exposure to his father's Muslim and Parsi friends, combined with reading Manusmriti, pushed him toward a tolerant but faithless agnosticism.

What restored faith was lived experience. In England, faced with persistent pressure to eat meat, Gandhi found himself protected by "faith that was at work — faith of which the seed had been sown by the good nurse Rambha" (021-002). And after being saved from a brothel visit, he recorded: "The incident increased my faith in God and taught me, to a certain extent, to cast off false shame" (047-016).

## Faith and Toleration

Gandhi's father modelled a distinctive kind of faith — one that listened respectfully to Muslim and Parsi friends discussing their own religions, and that "inculcated in me a toleration for all faiths" (010-010). This early exposure meant that Gandhi's own faith never required him to diminish the faith of others. His later encounter with Raychandbhai, the Jain jeweller-poet whose "passion to see God face to face" coexisted with a thriving commercial life, provided a model of faith integrated with worldly responsibility (053-005).

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[truth]]**: Faith is trust that truth exists and can be approached — Gandhi's experiments required faith that the next step would be revealed.
- **[[prayer]]**: Faith expresses itself in prayer; Ramanama was Gandhi's daily anchor.
- **[[courage]]**: Faith and courage are linked — Gandhi prayed for courage in the conviction that God would grant it.
- **[[religion]]**: Faith is the living core of religion, distinguished from ritual and institutional form.
