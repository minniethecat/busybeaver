# Veraval

Veraval is a port town on the Saurashtra coast of Gujarat, significant in Gandhi's autobiography as one of the sites where his early legal career in Kathiawad was tested. It was here that Gandhi attended an appeal hearing before the peripatetic court of the Judicial Assistant to the Political Agent, at a time when plague was devastating the town. His willingness to endure the dangers of the plague-ridden location, and his success in the cases at both [[jamnagar]] and Veraval, convinced his mentor Kevalram Mavji Dave that he was ready for Bombay.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi came to Veraval during a period when he was practising as a barrister in [[rajkot]], handling briefs arranged by his old well-wisher Kevalram Dave. The Judicial Assistant's court was peripatetic — it moved from town to town, and vakils and their clients had to follow. "The appeal of which I am talking was to be heard at Veraval where plague was raging," Gandhi recalled. "I have a recollection that there were as many as fifty cases daily in the place with a population of 5,500" [075-007]. The town was practically deserted, and Gandhi put up in a deserted *dharmsala* at some distance from the town.

His overriding concern was not for his own safety but for his clients. "But where were the clients to stay? If they were poor, they had simply to trust themselves to God's mercy," he wrote [075-007]. This solicitude for the vulnerable — the poor litigants who had to follow the court wherever it moved — was characteristic of Gandhi's early professional ethics.

## In the Narrative

### The Plague and the Application

When a friend who also had cases before the court wired Gandhi to apply for the camp to be moved to another station because of the plague, Gandhi submitted the application. The English sahib's response revealed the gulf between colonial officialdom and Indian reality: "Are you afraid?" the sahib asked [075-008].

Gandhi answered: "It is not a question of my being afraid. I think I can shift for myself, but what about the clients?" [075-008]. The sahib's reply was dismissive: "The plague has come to stay in India. Why fear it? The climate of Veraval is lovely" [075-010]. Gandhi noted drily that the sahib "lived far away from the town in a palatial tent pitched on the seashore" [075-010], insulated from the suffering of ordinary Indians.

### The Insensitivity of Colonial Rule

This encounter at Veraval crystallized for Gandhi a broader observation about the structural blindness of British rule. "How could the man have an idea of the hardships of poor India?" he asked. "How was he to understand the needs, habits, idiosyncrasies and customs of the people? How was one, accustomed to measuring things in gold sovereigns, all at once to make calculations in tiny bits of copper?" [075-012]. The metaphor that followed was searing: "As the elephant is powerless to think in the terms of the ant, in spite of the best intentions in the world, even so is the Englishman powerless to think in the terms of, or legislate for, the Indian" [075-012].

### A Proving Ground for Bombay

Despite the harsh conditions, Veraval formed part of Gandhi's growing reputation. When Kevalram Dave later urged him to settle in [[bombay]], he cited Gandhi's performance in Veraval as proof of his readiness: "You have proved your worth in Jamnagar and Veraval, and I have therefore not the least anxiety about you. You are destined to do public work, and we will not allow you to be buried in Kathiawad" [075-015]. Veraval — along with Jamnagar — thus became the professional credential that launched Gandhi's Bombay phase and, ultimately, his journey to South Africa and the wider stage of public life.

## Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Veraval represents a formative moment in Gandhi's understanding of colonial governance. The experience taught him that even well-intentioned English officials could be structurally incapable of comprehending Indian realities — a lesson that would inform his later critique of imperial rule. His concern for the poor clients — asking not about his own comfort but about where they would stay — also prefigured the ethic of service that would define his public work.
