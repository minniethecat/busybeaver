# Baroda

Baroda (now Vadodara) was a princely state in present-day Gujarat, India. It appears in Gandhi's autobiography through two significant connections: an experimental school run by his friend Keshavrao Deshpande, and the rediscovery of the traditional spinning wheel that would become central to Gandhi's swadeshi program.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi had an indirect connection to Baroda through his friend Keshavrao Deshpande, a contemporary from their student days in England. Deshpande conducted a school in the Baroda State called "Ganganath Vidyalaya," which operated with a distinctive family atmosphere — teachers were given family names like "Kaka" (paternal uncle) and "Mama" (maternal uncle) ([1915-P01]).

Later, during Gandhi's campaign to revive hand-spinning, Baroda proved crucial. After "no end of wandering in Gujarat," Gangabehn — a widow who became one of Gandhi's earliest khadi workers — finally found the spinning wheel "in Vijapur in the Baroda State," where many homes had wheels "long since consigned to the lofts as useless lumber" ([1921-P01]).

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

The Ganganath Vidyalaya was a formative institution for several of Gandhi's future coworkers. Kakasaheb Kalelkar, Phadke ("Mama"), Harihar Sharma ("Anna"), and others all received their family names there. These relationships proved remarkably durable — when the Vidyalaya dissolved, "the family also broke up, but they never gave up their spiritual relationship or their assumed names" ([1915-P01]). In time, these teachers became Gandhi's coworkers "one after another."

The rediscovery of the spinning wheel in Vijapur within Baroda State was a breakthrough for Gandhi's khadi movement. Gangabehn communicated "the joyful news" to Gandhi, and the families there expressed "their readiness to resume spinning, if someone promised to provide them with a regular supply of slivers, and to buy the yarn spun by them" ([1921-P01]).

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Baroda's contribution to Gandhi's work was twofold: it produced key personnel (the Ganganath Vidyalaya teachers who became his devoted followers) and it provided the material means (the spinning wheel) for his most iconic economic program. The khadi movement, which made the spinning wheel a symbol of Indian self-reliance, had its practical origins in the forgotten charkhas stored in the lofts of Baroda State.

## Historical Context

Baroda was one of the largest and most prosperous princely states under British paramountcy, ruled by the Gaekwad dynasty. It was known for its progressive administration, educational reforms, and patronage of the arts. The state's relatively liberal policies may have contributed to the existence of experimental schools like Ganganath Vidyalaya. Vijapur, a town within Baroda State, was a site where traditional hand-spinning had survived into the early twentieth century, making it a natural starting point for Gandhi's efforts to revive the practice nationally.
