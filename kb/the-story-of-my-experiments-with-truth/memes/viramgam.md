# Viramgam

Viramgam was a railway town in Gujarat whose oppressive customs cordon became the target of Gandhi's first successful political intervention after returning to India. The campaign to remove the Viramgam customs — a petty harassment of third-class railway passengers — demonstrated how localized grievances could be addressed through direct appeal to authority, and marked what Gandhi considered "the advent of satyagraha in India."

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi first learned of the Viramgam customs hardships in 1915, when he was medically inspected there while traveling third-class from Bombay to Rajkot. The tailor Motilal of Wadhwan, "a noted public worker," met him at the station and described the systematic harassment railway passengers endured. Though feverish at the time, Gandhi filed the information away and later investigated thoroughly. (4077-001) (4079-001)

## In the Narrative

### The Customs Cordon Campaign

Upon settling in Ahmedabad, Gandhi found that complaints about the Viramgam customs followed him throughout Kathiawad. He collected all available literature on the subject, convinced himself the complaints were well-founded, and opened correspondence with the Bombay Government. Lord Willingdon expressed sympathy but shifted responsibility to Delhi, and the Government of India initially offered only "an acknowledgment." (4097-001) (4099-001)

### Resolution Through Direct Appeal

The deadlock broke when Gandhi had occasion to meet Lord Chelmsford, the Viceroy. Placing the facts before him, Gandhi found the Viceroy astonished — "He had known nothing of the matter." Chelmsford gave a patient hearing, telephoned immediately for papers, and promised to remove the cordon if authorities had no defense. Within days, the Viramgam customs cordon was abolished. (4099-001)

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Gandhi regarded this episode as "the advent of satyagraha in India." The Bombay Government's Secretary had expressed disapproval of Gandhi using the word "satyagraha" in a speech at Bagasra, but the successful outcome validated Gandhi's method: careful fact-finding, patient correspondence, and direct personal appeal to the highest accessible authority. (4101-001)

## Key Passages

> I regarded this event as the advent of satyagraha in India. (4101-001)
