## Overview

Fearlessness (_abhaya_ in Sanskrit) was one of the central virtues in Mahatma Gandhi's ethical system. It formed part of the eleven vows observed at his ashrams, and he regarded it as essential for both personal integrity and effective political action. Crucially, Gandhi distinguished fearlessness from belligerence: true courage, in his view, was always tempered by civility and compassion.

## In Gandhi's Coworkers

Gandhi often remarked on fearlessness in those he worked alongside. He was particularly struck by the "indefatigable zeal and fearlessness" displayed by the young Indian volunteers in South Africa during the Black Plague outbreak. "One could understand the bravery of Dr. Godfrey and of an experienced man like Sjt. Madanjit," he wrote, "but the spirit of these callow youths!" filled him with joy beyond measure [092-009].

Sjt. Madanjit himself, the canvasser for _Indian Opinion_ who first alerted Gandhi to the plague outbreak, was described as "a remarkably fearless man." Upon seeing victims of the scourge in the Johannesburg location, "his heart wept," yet he immediately sent a pencil-note summoning Gandhi to take prompt measures [101-011].

Gopal Krishna Gokhale, whom Gandhi deeply respected, gave the highest praise to Miss Sonja Schlesin among all of Gandhi's coworkers — both Indian and European. Gokhale declared: "I have rarely met with the sacrifice, the purity and the fearlessness I have seen in Miss Schlesin. Amongst your coworkers, she takes the first place in my estimation" [089-015].

## In Gandhi's Own Conduct

Gandhi's own fearlessness expressed itself not in dramatic confrontations but in quiet, principled candor. When a client of his, Parsi Rustomji, became entangled in a customs violation, Gandhi "brought to bear on this case all my powers of persuasion. I met the Customs Officer and fearlessly apprised him of the whole affair" [133-035]. Rather than concealing the truth or exploiting legal technicalities, he chose the path of complete disclosure — a choice that required the moral courage to risk his client's anger and his professional reputation.

During a sea voyage to India, when a violent storm struck the ship, Gandhi noted that he "had little fear of the storm, for I had had experience of similar ones. I am a good sailor and do not get seasick. So I could fearlessly move amongst the passengers, bringing them comfort and good cheer" [063-019]. This episode illustrates how fearlessness, for Gandhi, was not the absence of danger but the capacity to act calmly and usefully in its presence.

## Fearlessness and Civility

In his work with the peasants of Champaran, Gandhi sought to help the agriculturists overcome their fear of colonial officials. He believed the central task was "ridding the agriculturists of their fear by making them realize that the officials were not the masters but the servants of the people." At the same time, he emphasized the importance of "combining civility with fearlessness" — a formulation that captured his belief that true courage need not be aggressive [148-005].

He elaborated on this tension: "Once they had shed the fear of the officials, how could they be stopped from returning their insults? And yet if they resorted to incivility it would spoil their satyagraha, like a drop of arsenic in milk." Gandhi reflected candidly that he "realized later that they had less fully learnt the lesson of civility than I had expected. Experience has taught me that civility is the most difficult part of satyagraha. Civility does not here mean the mere outward gentleness of speech cultivated for the occasion, but an inborn gentleness and desire to do the opponent good" [148-005].

## Fearlessness in the Community

Beyond individuals, Gandhi recognized fearlessness as a communal virtue. During the Durban agitation against Indian immigrants, he noted how both Sheth Abdul Karim Haji Adam and the advocate Mr. Laughton were "capable and fearless" men who guided and defended the Indian community against mob threats [064-011]. Mahadev Govind Ranade, Gokhale's mentor, also exemplified this quality: "Although he was a judge, he fearlessly attended the Congress" [079-017] — a reminder that institutional position need not constrain moral courage.
