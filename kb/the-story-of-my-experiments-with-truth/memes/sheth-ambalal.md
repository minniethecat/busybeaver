# Sheth Ambalal Sarabhai

Sheth Ambalal Sarabhai was a prominent Ahmedabad textile mill-owner and the principal figure behind the mill-owners' resistance during Gandhi's 1918 Ahmedabad textile strike. Despite their adversarial positions, Ambalal earned Gandhi's deep respect for his resolute will and transparent sincerity. His wife Sarladevi became attached to Gandhi with the affection of a blood-sister, and the Sarabhai family cared for Gandhi during a near-fatal illness.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi's relationship with Ambalal Sarabhai was paradoxical — they were opponents in the labour dispute yet personally close. Ambalal's sister, Anasuyabai Sarabhai (Anasuyaben), fought on the side of the striking mill-hands against her own brother [144-009]. The strain of Gandhi's fast, undertaken to break the mill-owners' resistance, caused Gandhi genuine anguish because of his personal regard for Ambalal and his family. After the strike concluded, the Sarabhai family became devoted caregivers during Gandhi's severe illness in 1918–1919.

## Biography

### Early Life

Ambalal Sarabhai belonged to the influential Sarabhai family of Ahmedabad, one of the leading industrial families of western India. The family's textile mills were central to Ahmedabad's economy, and Ambalal emerged as the spokesman for the mill-owners' interests during the labour disputes of the late 1910s.

### Role in the Narrative

In the Ahmedabad textile strike of 1918, Ambalal Sarabhai "led the fray on behalf of the mill-owners" while his sister Anasuyaben battled on the workers' side [144-009]. Gandhi described Ambalal's "resolute will and transparent sincerity" as "wonderful" and confessed that "it was a pleasure to be pitched against him" [146-009].

The turning point came when Gandhi undertook a fast to pressure the mill-owners. The fast's effect on Ambalal, as head of the opposition, cut Gandhi "to the quick," especially because of Sarladevi's anguish [146-009]. After the strike settled, the sweets for the workers were distributed at Ambalal's Mirzapur bungalow [146-014].

When Gandhi fell dangerously ill in 1918–1919, Ambalal and Sarladevi came to Nadiad, conferred with Gandhi's coworkers, and removed him "with the greatest care" to their Mirzapur bungalow. Gandhi acknowledged he "could not receive more loving and selfless service" than he received under their roof [152-005].

## Historical Significance

Ambalal Sarabhai represents the unique capacity of Gandhi's method to humanize industrial conflict. The mill-owner who was Gandhi's opponent became his host and caregiver — not through surrender but through the mutual recognition of each other's sincerity. The Sarabhai family went on to become one of India's most distinguished industrial and philanthropic dynasties, with Ambalal's descendants founding institutions including the Indian Institute of Management, Ahmedabad.
