# Detachment in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

For Gandhi, detachment was not the cold distance of indifference. It was the warm discipline of loving the world without clutching at it — of serving others without needing their gratitude, of pursuing truth without demanding to be right at every step.

## The Cook Who Taught Detachment

One of the most unexpected teachers in Gandhi's autobiography was a cook — a man whose arrival in his household coincided with a turn toward simpler living:

> But for the new cook I should never have discovered the truth and, being under the influence of the companion, I should probably have been unable to lead the life of detachment that I then began.
>
> (051-005)

The cook, by taking over domestic duties, freed Gandhi from dependence on a manipulative companion. Detachment here is practical — the removal of entanglements that cloud judgment.

## Detachment and Renunciation

Gandhi distinguished between outward renunciation and inward detachment. He quoted the Gujarati poet Nishkulanand to make this point:

> Renunciation of objects, without the renunciation of desires, is short-lived, however hard you may try.
>
> (002-006)

Detachment in Gandhi's sense was not about giving up possessions but about loosening the grip of desire. A person could own nothing and still be attached; another could own much and hold it as a trustee.

## Living with Detachment

Gandhi practised detachment most concretely in his relationships. Looking back on an episode of jealousy with his wife, he wrote:

> Today I am in a position to narrate the incident with some detachment, as it belongs to a period out of which I have fortunately emerged.
>
> (088-007)

Detachment here enables truthful narration — a man can look at his own past follies without being consumed by shame or defensiveness. This emotional detachment was the precondition for the radical honesty of the autobiography itself.

## Living Among All with Detachment

Gandhi extended the principle of detachment to his social life, believing it was essential for those who wished to serve all without partiality:

> I hold that believers who have to see the same God in others that they see in themselves, must be able to live amongst all with sufficient detachment.
>
> (088-014)

This is the paradox at the heart of Gandhi's detachment: to see God in everyone, one must be attached enough to serve them and detached enough to serve them all equally.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[renunciation]]**: Detachment is the inner dimension of renunciation — renunciation of desire, not merely of objects.
- **[[non-possession]]**: The practice of non-possession is the outward expression of inward detachment.
- **[[self-control]]**: Detachment requires the discipline of self-control — particularly control of the senses and the mind.
