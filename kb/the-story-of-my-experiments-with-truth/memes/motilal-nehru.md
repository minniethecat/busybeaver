## Summary

Pandit Motilal Nehru (1861–1931) was one of India's most distinguished lawyers and a prominent leader of the Indian National Congress. Father of Jawaharlal Nehru, he appears in the autobiography primarily in connection with the noncooperation movement of 1920, where he was among the first established leaders to support Gandhi's resolution at the special Congress session in Calcutta.

## Role During the Punjab Crisis

During the aftermath of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre and the martial law regime in Punjab, Motilal Nehru took an active part in the Congress response. Gandhi noted that with the principal Punjab leaders imprisoned, "their place, I found, had been properly taken up by Pandit Malaviyaji, Pandit Motilalji and the late Swami Shraddhanandji" [159-007].

When the Congress decided to boycott the official Hunter Committee inquiry and appoint a non-official committee to hold a parallel inquiry, Pandit Motilal Nehru was among those appointed to conduct this investigation on behalf of the Congress [159-009].

## Support for Noncooperation

Motilal Nehru played a pivotal role in Gandhi's noncooperation movement. At the special Congress session in Calcutta in September 1920, where Gandhi moved the noncooperation resolution, Motilalji was among the first established leaders to join the movement.

Gandhi recalled the moment with warmth: "Motilalji was the first to join the movement. I still remember the sweet discussion that I had with him on the resolution. He suggested some changes in its phraseology which I adopted. He undertook to move the resolution on the noncooperation in the subjects session" [166-009].

This support was significant, as Motilal Nehru represented the established Congress leadership — seasoned lawyers and politicians who had built the national movement over decades. His endorsement lent crucial legitimacy to Gandhi's radical proposal.

## Relationship with Other Leaders

Gandhi placed Motilal Nehru among the "seasoned, well-tried and universally revered leaders" alongside figures like Lokamanya Tilak and Lala Lajpat Rai [161-007] [166-007]. While Gandhi sometimes found himself differing from these senior leaders, he acknowledged the "sweet discussion" he had with Motilalji, suggesting a relationship of mutual respect even in disagreement.

## Legacy

Motilal Nehru's decision to cast his lot with Gandhi's noncooperation movement marked a turning point in the Indian freedom struggle. It bridged the gap between the constitutional methods of the pre-war Congress and the mass movement that Gandhi was building. As the patriarch of the Nehru family, his support for Gandhi also paved the way for his son Jawaharlal's deep involvement in the national movement under Gandhi's leadership.
