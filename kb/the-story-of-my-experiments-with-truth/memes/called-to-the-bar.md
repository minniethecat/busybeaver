## Overview

Gandhi was formally called to the Bar on June 10, 1891, and enrolled in the High Court the following day. He sailed for India on June 12, two days after being called. The occasion marked the fulfillment of the purpose for which he had gone to England three years earlier, though he left with deep misgivings about his ability to practise law [760-1].

## Keeping Terms

Before being called, a student had to fulfill two conditions: keeping twelve terms — equivalent to about three years — and passing examinations. "Keeping terms" meant eating one's terms, that is, attending at least six out of about twenty-four dinners per term at the Inn of Court. "Eating" did not mean actually partaking of the dinner — it meant reporting oneself at the fixed hours and remaining present throughout the dinner. Usually, everyone ate and drank the good commons and choice wines provided [758-1].

A dinner cost from two shillings and sixpence to three shillings and sixpence, which was considered moderate since the same amount would be spent on wines alone at a hotel. To Indians like Gandhi, it was a matter of surprise that the cost of drink should exceed the cost of food. "The first revelation gave me a great shock," he recalled, "and I wondered how people had the heart to throw away so much money on drink." Later, he came to understand [758-2].

Gandhi often ate nothing at these dinners, as the only things he might eat were bread, boiled potato, and cabbage. In the beginning he did not eat these because he did not like them; later, when he began to relish them, he also gained the courage to ask for other dishes. The dinner provided for the benchers used to be better than that for the students. A Parsi vegetarian student and Gandhi applied, in the interests of vegetarianism, for the vegetarian courses served to the benchers. The application was granted, and they began to get fruits and other vegetables from the benchers' table [758-3].

Two bottles of wine were allotted to every group of four students. As a teetotaler and vegetarian, Gandhi was always in demand to form a quartet so that three others might drink his share. On the "grand night" in each term, when extra wines like champagne in addition to port and sherry were served, he was specially requested to attend and was in great demand [758-4].

Gandhi observed that the institution had "gradually lost all its meaning, but conservative England retained it nevertheless." In earlier times, only a few students attended the dinners and there were opportunities for talks between students and benchers, with speeches that helped develop knowledge of the world, polish, refinement, and speaking ability. By Gandhi's time, the benchers had a table all to themselves and no such interaction was possible [759-1].

## Study of Law

The curriculum of study was easy, and barristers were humorously known as "dinner barristers." Everyone knew the examinations had practically no value. There were two examinations: one in Roman Law and the other in Common Law. Regular textbooks were prescribed, but scarcely anyone read them. Gandhi knew many who passed the Roman Law examination by cramming notes in a couple of weeks, and the Common Law examination by reading notes over two or three months. Question papers were easy and examiners generous. The pass rate for Roman Law was 95 to 99 per cent; for the final examination it was 75 per cent or more. Examinations were held four times a year, so there was little fear of being "plucked" [761-1].

Gandhi, however, resolved differently. "I felt that I should read all the textbooks," he wrote. "It was a fraud, I thought, not to read these books." He invested considerable money in them and decided to read Roman Law in Latin — the Latin he had acquired for the London Matriculation stood him in good stead. The reading of Justinian helped him a great deal in understanding South African law, where Roman-Dutch is the common law [761-2].

He spent nine months of fairly hard labour reading through the Common Law of England. Broom's *Common Law*, a big but interesting volume, took up a good deal of time. Snell's *Equity* was full of interest but hard to understand. White and Tudor's *Leading Cases*, from which certain cases were prescribed, was full of interest and instruction. He also read Williams and Edwards's *Real Property* and Goodeve's *Personal Property* with pleasure. Williams' book, he noted, "read like a novel." The one book he remembered reading on his return to India with the same unflagging interest was Mayne's *Hindu Law* [761-3].

## Called and Enrolled

Gandhi passed his examinations, was called to the Bar on June 10, 1891, and enrolled in the High Court on June 11. On June 12 he sailed for home [760-1]. The summary progression — called, enrolled, and departed within three days — reflected the urgency with which he returned to India.

## Self-Doubt and the Question of Helplessness

Despite his diligence, Gandhi was overcome by a sense of helplessness. "It was easy to be called," he reflected, "but it was difficult to practise at the bar" [762-1]. He had read the laws but had not learned how to practise them. He had read "Legal Maxims" with great interest but did not know how to apply them in practice. One maxim — "*Sic utere tuo ut alienum non laedas*" (Use your property in such a way as not to damage that of others) — he knew well, and had read all the leading cases on it, but he was at a loss to know how one could employ this maxim for the benefit of one's client [762-2].

His practical knowledge was lacking in every way. He had not the slightest idea of Hindu and Mahomedan law, had never learnt how to draft a plaint, and felt completely at sea. He had heard of Sir Pherozeshah Mehta as one who "roared like a lion in law courts" and wondered how Mehta could have learnt the art in England. It seemed out of the question for Gandhi ever to acquire such legal acumen, and he had serious misgivings as to whether he would be able even to earn a living by the profession [762-3].

### Seeking Guidance

Gandhi confided his difficulties to friends. One suggested that he seek Dadabhai Naoroji's advice. Gandhi had a note of introduction to Dadabhai from his time in India but had availed himself of it very late, thinking he had no right to trouble such a great man. He would attend Dadabhai's addresses, "feast his eyes and ears" from a corner of the hall, and go away. Dadabhai had founded an association for close contact with Indian students, and Gandhi attended its meetings. In time, he mustered up courage to present the note, and Dadabhai replied: "You can come and have my advice whenever you like." But Gandhi never availed himself of the offer, thinking it wrong to trouble him without the most pressing necessity [764-1].

Another friend recommended Mr. Frederick Pincutt, a Conservative whose affection for Indian students was "pure and unselfish." Gandhi sought and received an appointment. Pincutt greeted him as a friend and "laughed away his pessimism":

> "Do you think that everyone must be a Pherozeshah Mehta? Pherozeshahs and Badruddins are rare. Rest assured it takes no unusual skill to be an ordinary lawyer. Common honesty and industry are enough to enable him to make a living. All cases are not complicated." [765-1]

When Pincutt asked about his general reading and learned of Gandhi's meagre stock, he was visibly disappointed — but only for a moment. His face beamed with a pleasing smile:

> "I understand your trouble. Your general reading is meagre. You have no knowledge of the world, a sine qua non for a vakil. You have not even read the history of India. A vakil should know human nature. He should be able to read a man's character from his face. And every Indian ought to know Indian history." [765-2]

Pincutt recommended Kaye and Malleson's history of the Mutiny of 1857, and also Lavator's and Shemmelpennick's books on physiognomy. Gandhi purchased Lavator's book the next day (Shemmelpennick's was unavailable) and found it "more difficult than Snell's *Equity* and scarcely interesting." He studied Shakespeare's physiognomy but "did not acquire the knack of finding out the Shakespeares walking up and down the streets of London" [766-1].

Pincutt's advice did little direct service, but his kindness proved invaluable. "His smiling open face stayed in my memory," Gandhi wrote, "and I trusted his advice that Pherozeshah Mehta's acumen, memory and ability were not essential to the making of a successful lawyer; honesty and industry were enough." As Gandhi felt he had a fair share of these last, he was somewhat reassured [766-2].

## Significance

Gandhi's professional anxiety would prove well-founded — his early attempts at legal practice in Bombay failed, and it was not until his work in South Africa that he found his footing. Yet the discipline of reading the law texts thoroughly, especially Roman Law in Latin, later proved valuable when he encountered Roman-Dutch law in South Africa. His habit of rigorous self-study, developed during these years of keeping terms, became a hallmark of his approach to every challenge he faced thereafter. Pincutt's assurance — that ordinary honesty and industry were sufficient for the ordinary lawyer — proved to be a prophetic description of Gandhi's own professional path.
