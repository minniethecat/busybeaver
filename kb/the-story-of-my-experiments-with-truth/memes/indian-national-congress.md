# Indian National Congress

The Indian National Congress was the primary vehicle of India's independence movement. Founded in 1885 as a forum for educated Indians to express their aspirations to the British government, it was transformed by Gandhi into a mass organization capable of mobilizing millions. The Congress became synonymous with the struggle for independence.

## Gandhi's Relationship

Gandhi first attended Congress sessions in 1901 (Calcutta) and 1902, where he met Gokhale and presented his resolution on South Africa. Over the following decades, he reshaped the organization — introducing mass membership, requiring delegates to wear khadi and spin, establishing regional committees in villages, and making satyagraha the Congress's primary method. "The Congress was the attainment of swaraj within the British Empire" (167-003).

## Role in the Narrative

The Congress appears throughout the autobiography's later chapters as the institutional framework for Gandhi's political work. The Congress sessions, the passing of resolutions on untouchability and khadi (167-004), and the evolution from moderate petitioning to mass non-cooperation all reflect Gandhi's growing influence.

## Key Passages

> The Congress was the attainment of swaraj within the British Empire if possible, and outside if necessary. (167-003)
