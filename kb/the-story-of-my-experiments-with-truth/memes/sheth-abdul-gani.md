# Sheth Abdul Gani

Sheth Abdul Gani was a Johannesburg-based Indian merchant associated with Muhammad Kasam Kamruddin's firm. He received Gandhi upon his arrival in Johannesburg in 1893, witnessed his first encounter with racial discrimination at a hotel, and supported his determination to travel first-class to Pretoria — an early assertion of dignity that foreshadowed Gandhi's lifelong campaign against racial discrimination.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Abdul Gani was one of Gandhi's earliest contacts in the Transvaal and played a supporting role in Gandhi's first confrontation with South African racial barriers. His warm reception and genuine concern for Gandhi's safety contrasted with the cold formality of white establishments. Gandhi described him with affection — the man who "had a hearty laugh over the story of my experience at the hotel."

## Biography

### Early Life

Little is recorded of Abdul Gani's early life beyond his identity as an Indian Muslim merchant in the Transvaal. He was connected to the firm of Muhammad Kasam Kamruddin, part of the network of Indian trading houses that spanned Natal and the Transvaal in the 1890s.

### Role in the Narrative

When Gandhi arrived in Johannesburg in 1893, he was unknown and unrecognized. The agent sent to meet him at the stagecoach failed to identify him, and Gandhi, rebuffed by the Grand National Hotel, made his way to Muhammad Kasam Kamruddin's shop. There "I found Abdul Gani Sheth expecting me, and he gave me a cordial greeting. He had a hearty laugh over the story of my experience at the hotel. 'How ever did you expect to be admitted to a hotel?' he said" [034-009].

When Gandhi resolved to travel first-class to Pretoria to assert his right as a barrister — an audacious act for an Indian in 1893 South Africa — Abdul Gani counseled prudence but supported the decision: "Sheth Abdul Gani drew my attention to the extra time and money this would mean, but agreed to my proposal to travel first" [034-018].

The following morning, Abdul Gani came to the station to see Gandhi off. The successful first-class journey — a small but significant victory over the racial order — "gave him an agreeable surprise, but he warned me saying: 'I shall be thankful if you reach Pretoria all right. I am afraid the guard will not leave you in peace in the first class, and even if he does, the passengers will not'" [034-023].

## Historical Significance

Abdul Gani represents the network of Indian merchants and traders whose practical support enabled Gandhi's early South African work. Without the hospitality, introductions, and material assistance of men like Abdul Gani, Abdul Karim Jhaveri, and Dada Abdulla, Gandhi's South African experiment would have been impossible. These relationships also shaped Gandhi's understanding of the Indian diaspora's vulnerabilities and strengths.
