# Lucknow

Lucknow, the capital of the United Provinces (now Uttar Pradesh), was the site of the 1916 Indian National Congress session and the place where [[mahatma-gandhi]]'s historic involvement with the [[champaran]] Satyagraha began. It was at Lucknow that a peasant named Rajkumar Shukla first approached Gandhi, setting in motion a chain of events that would define Gandhi's method of non-violent resistance in India.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi travelled to Lucknow in 1916 to attend the annual Congress session. His stay there proved consequential not for what happened within the Congress pavilion but for what happened outside it. It was at Lucknow that he was approached by Rajkumar Shukla, a tenant farmer from Champaran in Bihar, who pleaded for his intervention against the exploitation of indigo cultivators by European planters [136-004].

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

The pivotal event at Lucknow was Shukla's first approach to Gandhi. Gandhi records: "This man caught hold of me at Lucknow, where I had gone for the Congress of 1916" [136-004]. Shukla introduced him to Babu Brajkishore Prasad, who would become Gandhi's esteemed coworker in Champaran. Initially, Gandhi was unimpressed: "Brajkishore Babu failed then to make an impression on me. I took it that he must be some vakil exploiting the simple agriculturists" [136-004].

Shukla's persistence was extraordinary. When Gandhi left Lucknow for Cawnpore, Shukla followed him there, insisting: "Champaran is very near here. Please give a day" [136-006]. Gandhi committed himself further, promising: "Pray excuse me this time. But I promise that I will come" [136-006].

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Lucknow meeting was the seed of Gandhi's first major Satyagraha campaign on Indian soil. The Champaran agitation that followed would establish the pattern for all his subsequent campaigns: investigation of facts, non-violent resistance, and constructive work. Later, when Gandhi met Brajkishore Prasad again in Bihar, he saw a transformed man: "Brajkishorebabu was not the Babu Brajkishore Prasad I had met in Lucknow. He impressed me this time with his humility, simplicity, goodness and extraordinary faith" [137-007].

Lucknow thus marks the beginning of Gandhi's rural awakening in India — the moment when the problems of India's peasants ceased to be abstract political concerns and became a personal calling.
