# Putlibai Gandhi

Gandhi's mother, Putlibai, was the most formative spiritual influence of his childhood. Her saintliness — expressed through rigorous fasting, daily temple worship, and unwavering religious devotion — provided the young Gandhi with his first model of a life organized around spiritual discipline. Though she left no writings of her own, her character permeates the autobiography as the quiet moral foundation on which much of Gandhi's later spirituality was built.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Putlibai was Kaba Gandhi's fourth wife and the mother of Gandhi, his sister Raliatbehn, and two other brothers. Kaba Gandhi was over forty when he married her, and Gandhi was their youngest child. She bore three sons — Laxmidas, Karsandas, and Mohandas — and one daughter (002-002).

Gandhi's description of his mother centers on a single quality: saintliness. He writes:

> The outstanding impression my mother has left on my memory is that of saintliness. She was deeply religious. She would not think of taking her meals without her daily prayers. Going to Haveli — the Vaishnava temple — was one of her daily duties.
>
> (002-005)

## Biography

### Early Life

Little is known of Putlibai's early life from the autobiography. She was from a traditional background, likely from the Porbandar or Junagadh region. Her illiteracy was typical of women of her station, but Gandhi emphasizes her "strong common sense" and that she was "well informed about all matters of State" — the ladies of the court "thought highly of her intelligence" (002-006).

### Role in the Narrative

Putlibai's most significant role in the autobiography is as a moral anchor. Three episodes illustrate her influence:

**The fasting discipline**: Gandhi recounts his mother's extraordinary commitment to religious vows. She would observe the Chaturmas (four-month rainy season penance), sometimes fasting on alternate days, sometimes vowing not to eat without seeing the sun:

> During another Chaturmas she vowed not to have food without seeing the sun. We children on those days would stand, staring at the sky, waiting to announce the appearance of the sun to our mother... And I remember days when, at his sudden appearance, we would rush and announce it to her. She would run out to see with her own eyes, but by that time the fugitive sun would be gone, thus depriving her of her meal. "That does not matter," she would say cheerfully, "God did not want me to eat today."
>
> (002-005)

This cheerful acceptance of hardship as God's will left a profound impression on Gandhi, who would later adopt fasting as a central spiritual practice.

**Permission to go to England**: When Gandhi sought to go to England for legal studies, it was Putlibai's consent — obtained only after he took a vow before a Jain monk to abstain from wine, women, and meat — that made the journey possible. Her concern for his moral welfare in a foreign land, and her insistence on the vow, revealed both maternal love and moral seriousness (011-003).

**Warning about bad company**: Putlibai warned Gandhi about a friend who was leading him toward meat-eating and other indulgences. Gandhi "dared not go against the opinion of my mother," though he rationalized his continued association as an attempt to reform the friend (005-003). The episode shows Putlibai's moral discernment even when her son was too proud to heed it.

## Historical Significance

Putlibai Gandhi belongs to that company of historical mothers — like Monica, the mother of St. Augustine — whose religious devotion shaped a child who would change the world. Though she could not read or write, her lived example of saintliness, discipline, and cheerful self-denial provided the emotional and spiritual template for Gandhi's later experiments with fasting, [[celibacy]], and the pursuit of [[truth]].

Gandhi acknowledged this debt indirectly throughout the autobiography. His statement that "the instruments for the quest of truth are as simple as they are difficult" (001-003) could well describe his mother's fasting practice. And his conviction that religion was not doctrine but lived discipline was learned not from books but from watching Putlibai's daily rituals.
