## Overview

Gandhi's second voyage to South Africa in December 1896 differed markedly from his first. He now travelled as a married man with his wife Kasturba, their two sons, and the only son of his widowed sister — a family party rather than a solitary student. Dada Abdulla and Company, having just purchased the steamship *Courland*, offered the family free passage (1882-1).

## Family and "Civilization"

This was Gandhi's first voyage with his wife and children, and he took it upon himself to direct every detail of their presentation. Believing that European standards of dress and manners were necessary for influence, he had his wife adopt the Parsi sari and the boys wear Parsi coats and trousers with shoes and stockings. They were also required to use knives and forks. Gandhi later reflected that this infatuation with the outward signs of civilization was a passing phase — "I can see today that we feel all the freer and lighter for having cast off the tinsel of 'civilization'" (1883-1).

## The Storm

Four days from Natal, the ship was caught in a violent and prolonged gale. Passengers of all faiths — Mussalmans, Hindus, Christians — "forgot their differences and began to think of the one and only God" (1884-1). The captain reassured them that a well-built ship could withstand almost any weather, but "every minute were heard sounds and crashes which foreboded breaches and leaks." Gandhi, a seasoned sailor who did not get seasick, moved among the passengers offering comfort and relaying the captain's reports. The friendships formed during this crisis "stood me, as we shall see, in very good stead" (1885-1).

## Arrival and Foreboding

The ship cast anchor at Durban on December 18 or 19. That same day, the *Naderi* also reached port. The storm at sea had passed, but as Gandhi noted with grim foresight: "The real storm was still to come" (1886-1). He was returning to a South Africa increasingly hostile to Indian immigration.
