# Karamchand Gandhi

Karamchand Gandhi (1822–1885), also known as Kaba Gandhi, was the father of Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi and served as the diwan (chief minister) of several princely states in Kathiawar, including Porbandar, Rajkot, and Vankaner.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Karamchand Gandhi was the fifth of six sons of Ota Gandhi and rose to become Prime Minister in Porbandar. Gandhi described his father as "a lover of his clan, truthful, brave and generous, but short-tempered," and noted that "to a certain extent he might have been given to carnal pleasures." Despite his limited formal education — "he had no education, save that of experience" — Karamchand was skilled at managing state affairs. His death when Gandhi was sixteen, occurring minutes after Gandhi had left his bedside for marital relations, became a source of lifelong guilt and reflection.

## Biography

Born around 1822, Karamchand Gandhi belonged to the Modh Bania caste and, despite receiving little formal schooling, rose through ability and practical wisdom to become diwan of Porbandar and later of Rajkot and Vankaner [001-002]. He was a member of the Rajasthanik Court, an influential body for settling disputes between princely clans.

His third and fourth marriages produced children. His fourth wife, Putlibai, was the mother of Mohandas — the youngest of her four children. Karamchand suffered a fistula in his later years, and the young Mohandas nursed him devotedly. When Karamchand finally died, Mohandas was not at his bedside — a circumstance the son would recall with painful honesty in his autobiography.

## Historical Significance

Karamchand Gandhi represents a transitional figure in Indian history — a man of traditional upbringing and limited education who nonetheless navigated the complex world of princely state politics with integrity and skill. His practical wisdom and reputation for incorruptibility, combined with Putlibai's religious devotion, provided the ethical foundation on which their youngest son would build a revolutionary philosophy. Gandhi's own commitment to public service, his indifference to material wealth, and his insistence that means must be as pure as ends all carry echoes of the father he lost too young.
