# South Africa in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

South Africa was the crucible in which Gandhi's philosophy was forged. He arrived in 1893 as a twenty-three-year-old lawyer with a one-year contract; he left in 1914 as a forty-four-year-old [[mahatma-gandhi]] whose method of [[satyagraha]] had been tested and refined through two decades of struggle. More than a third of the autobiography is set in South Africa — a testament to how profoundly this land shaped the man and his message.

## Arrival and Awakening

Gandhi landed in [[durban]] in May 1893. His host, [[abdulla-sheth]], met him at the quay. Gandhi's first observation was of the contempt with which Indians were treated:

> I observed that the Indians were not held in much respect. I could not fail to notice a sort of snobbishness about the manner of those who knew Abdulla Sheth, and it yet stung me.
>
> (032-001)

Within a week, Gandhi would experience racial humiliation firsthand. On the train journey from Durban to [[pretoria]], he was ordered out of his first-class compartment at Maritzburg station — despite holding a valid ticket — because he was "colored." The cold night he spent in the station waiting room became a turning point:

> I began to think of my duty. Should I fight for my rights, or go back to India? My active nonviolence began from that date.
>
> (034-008)

## The Geography of Resistance

South Africa in Gandhi's time comprised four distinct political entities, each with its own Indian community and its own pattern of discrimination:

| Territory | Gandhi's Base | Key Events |
|-----------|--------------|------------|
| [[natal]] | Durban | Founding of [[natal-indian-congress]] (1894) |
| [[transvaal]] | Johannesburg, Pretoria | Asiatic Registration Act satyagraha (1906–1911) |
| Orange Free State | (Indians barred from residence) | Legal restrictions on Indian entry |
| Cape Colony | Cape Town (transit) | Franchise struggles |

### Natal: The First Campaign

Gandhi's political career began in Natal. Within a year of his arrival, he had founded the [[natal-indian-congress]] to fight the disenfranchisement of Indians. In 1896, he returned to [[india]] to publicize the plight of Natal Indians — his first venture as a public spokesman.

The 1897 return to Natal nearly cost him his life. A mob of white settlers, enraged by his allegedly inflammatory statements in India, attacked him on the Durban docks. He was saved only by the intervention of the police superintendent's wife, Mrs. Alexander (046-007).

### Transvaal: The Birth of Satyagraha

The [[transvaal]] was where satyagraha was born. In 1906, the Transvaal government proposed the Asiatic Registration Act — the "Black Act" — requiring all Indians to register with fingerprints and carry passes. Gandhi organized the first mass civil disobedience campaign, and at the Empire Theatre meeting in [[johannesburg]], the community took a solemn vow of resistance (084-005).

The movement evolved through multiple phases:
- The 1908 compromise and Gandhi's first imprisonment in [[johannesburg]] jail
- General Smuts's broken promise and the bonfire of registration certificates
- The 1913 Great March — 2,000 Indians crossing into the Transvaal without permits
- The miners' strike in Newcastle and the final settlement through the Gandhi-Smuts Agreement

### Tolstoy Farm and Phoenix Settlement

South Africa was also where Gandhi built his first intentional communities. [[phoenix-settlement]] (founded 1904) near Durban became the headquarters of *Indian Opinion* and a laboratory for simple living. [[tolstoy-farm]] (founded 1910) near Johannesburg was the communal home of satyagrahi families during the final phase of the struggle. Both embodied Gandhi's vision of self-reliant communities grounded in manual labor, simple diet, and spiritual discipline.

## The Legacy

When Gandhi finally left South Africa in July 1914, he left behind a transformed Indian community — politically organized, economically more secure, and conscious of its dignity. He took with him to [[india]] a tested method of nonviolent resistance, a network of trusted colleagues ([[henry-polak]], [[hermann-kallenbach]], [[cf-andrews]]), and the conviction that moral force could prevail against the might of an empire.

The autobiography's South African chapters are not merely historical narrative — they are the recorded experiments from which Gandhi's entire philosophy emerged. Without South Africa, there would be no [[satyagraha]], no [[brahmacharya]], no [[sabarmati-ashram]] — and no Mahatma.
