# Madras

Madras (now Chennai) was a key center of support for [[mahatma-gandhi]] during his 1896 return to India to publicize the grievances of South African Indians. The city responded with particular enthusiasm, influenced by the story of Balasundaram, a Tamil indentured labourer whose case Gandhi had taken up in Natal.

## The 1896 Advocacy Tour

After his initial years in South Africa, Gandhi returned to India in 1896 to mobilize public opinion on behalf of the Indian community in Natal. Madras was a major stop on this tour:

> "I next proceeded to Madras. It was wild with enthusiasm. The Balasundaram incident made a profound impression on the meeting." [028-005]

The Balasundaram case — an indentured labourer from Madras Presidency whose mistreatment Gandhi had exposed in [[durban]] — resonated deeply with the local population. "The echoes of Balasundaram's case were heard in far off Madras. Labourers from different parts of the province, who went to Natal on indenture, came to know of this case through their indentured brethren" [020-005].

## The Green Pamphlet

At the Madras meeting, Gandhi distributed his "Green Pamphlet" (later known as the "Grievances of the British Indians in South Africa"), which summarized the disabilities faced by Indians in Natal. The response was overwhelming:

> "I brought out a second and revised edition of 10,000 copies. They sold like hot cakes, but I saw that it was not necessary to print such a large number. In my enthusiasm I had overcalculated." [028-005]

## Press Support

Gandhi received significant assistance from Madras journalists. [[g-parameshvaran-pillay]], editor of *The Madras Standard*, "placed the columns of *The Madras Standard* entirely at my disposal, and I freely availed myself of the offer" [028-005]. G. Subrahmaniam of *The Hindu* and Dr. Subrahmaniam were also sympathetic to the cause.

## Later Connections

Even after returning to South Africa, Gandhi maintained connections with Madras. The Tamil community in Natal, many of whom originated from the Madras Presidency, became an important constituency for his work. While traveling back to South Africa, Gandhi began studying Tamil using a *Tamil Self-Teacher* given to him by a ship's doctor, recognizing that knowledge of Tamil would help him "get into closer touch with the Madras Indians" [024-005].

## Role in the Congress Movement

Madras later became an important center of Congress activity. During the aftermath of the Rowlatt Act agitation, when Gandhi was too weak to stand and address meetings, he still accepted an invitation from Madras "to take the risk of the long journey" [030-001], demonstrating the city's continuing importance in the national movement.
