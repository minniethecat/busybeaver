# Wadhwan

Wadhwan is a town in the Kathiawad region of Gujarat, significant in Gandhi's autobiography as the place where two threads of his early Indian work converged: his campaign against the oppressive Viramgam customs cordon, and his commitment to third-class travel as a discipline of solidarity with the poor. It was at Wadhwan (or perhaps Viramgam — he couldn't remember which) that he was medically inspected during a plague outbreak [127-003], and it was here that the tailor Motilal, "a noted public worker of the place," met him at the station and first raised the Viramgam issue [127-004].

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi passed through Wadhwan while traveling third-class from [[poona]] to [[rajkot]] and [[porbandar]], wearing the simple Kathiawadi dress he had adopted during the South African satyagraha — shirt, dhoti, and an inexpensive Kashmiri cap. "One dressed in that fashion was sure to pass muster as a poor man," he observed [127-002]. He was running a slight fever, and when the medical inspector noted his temperature, Gandhi was told to report to the Medical Officer at Rajkot [127-003].

This episode became one of Gandhi's object lessons in the indignities of third-class travel and the principle that those who lead must share the conditions of the poorest.

## In the Narrative

### The Tailor Who Led

Someone had sent word that Gandhi was passing through, for "the tailor Motilal, a noted public worker of the place, met me at the station" [127-004]. Motilal told him about the Viramgam customs — a cordon that subjected rail passengers to humiliating searches — and the hardships it caused. Ill with fever, Gandhi replied with a terse challenge: "Are you prepared to go to jail?" [127-006].

Gandhi had taken Motilal for an impetuous youth who spoke before thinking. He was wrong. Motilal replied "with firm deliberation": "We will certainly go to jail, provided you lead us. As Kathiawadis, we have the first right on you" [127-007]. Motilal asked Gandhi to promise to halt at Wadhwan on his return, assuring him: "You will be delighted to see the work and the spirit of our youths" [127-007].

### A Life Cut Short

Later, Gandhi came to know Motilal intimately. "He made a point of spending some days in the then newly started Ashram every month to teach the children tailoring and to do some of the tailoring of the Ashram himself" [127-010]. Motilal was a master tailor who earned his modest living — fifteen rupees a month — in a single hour of work each day, giving the rest of his time to public service. "He would talk to me every day of Viramgam, and the hardships of the passengers, which had become absolutely unbearable for him" [127-010].

Motilal's life ended tragically early. "He was cut off in the prime of youth by a sudden illness, and public life at Wadhwan suffered without him" [127-010]. Gandhi's eulogy in a single sentence captured both the value of Motilal's service and the irreplaceable nature of such local leadership.

### The Viramgam Campaign

The meeting at Wadhwan was the seed of Gandhi's campaign against the Viramgam customs cordon. He collected literature on the subject, corresponded with the Bombay Government, and eventually secured a meeting with Lord Chelmsford, the Viceroy. "He gave me a patient hearing, telephoned that very moment for papers about Viramgam, and promised to remove the cordon" [127-014]. Within days, the cordon was removed. Gandhi regarded this as "the advent of satyagraha in India" [127-014].

## Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Wadhwan demonstrates how Gandhi built his Indian satyagraha movement from the ground up, one local grievance at a time. The tailor Motilal — earning his living in an hour a day, working the rest for his community — embodied the ideal of the self-sufficient, public-spirited citizen that Gandhi sought to multiply across India. And the Viramgam campaign, sparked by that feverish meeting on the station platform, proved that even imperial customs cordons could be dismantled by methodical, nonviolent persuasion backed by the willingness to suffer imprisonment.
