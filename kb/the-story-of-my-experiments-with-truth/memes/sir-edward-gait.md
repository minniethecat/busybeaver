# Sir Edward Gait

Sir Edward Gait (1863–1950) was the Lieutenant Governor of Bihar and Orissa during Gandhi's Champaran Satyagraha of 1917. Unlike many colonial officials who opposed Gandhi's interventions, Gait proved to be a fair-minded administrator who appointed the official inquiry committee on Gandhi's terms and used his authority to secure lasting agrarian reform.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi's relationship with Sir Edward Gait was marked by mutual respect despite their adversarial positions in the Champaran struggle. Gait summoned Gandhi, listened to his demands, and appointed an inquiry committee that included Gandhi as a member representing the indigo ryots (peasants). Gandhi titled the chapter recounting this interaction "When a Governor Is Good," signaling his appreciation for Gait's sincerity.

## Biography

### Early Life

Edward Albert Gait was born in 1863 and joined the Indian Civil Service in 1882. He served in various administrative capacities in Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa, eventually rising to the position of Lieutenant Governor of Bihar and Orissa (1915–1920). A scholar as well as an administrator, Gait authored works on the history and ethnography of Assam and Bengal.

### Role in the Narrative

Gait entered Gandhi's story during the Champaran Satyagraha in 1917. Summoning Gandhi to his office, Gait expressed willingness to appoint an official inquiry and invited Gandhi to join the Committee. Gandhi accepted on conditions: freedom to consult his coworkers, recognition that his Committee membership did not nullify his role as the ryots' advocate, and the right to guide the ryots should the inquiry prove unsatisfactory [143-004].

Gait accepted these conditions as "just and proper" and announced the inquiry, appointing Sir Frank Sly as Chairman [143-005]. More importantly, Gait played a decisive role in converting the Committee's findings into law. The European planters wielded extraordinary power and offered strenuous opposition, but Gait "remained firm up to the last and fully carried out the recommendations of the Committee," producing a unanimous report and securing passage of the Champaran Agrarian Act [143-007].

## Historical Significance

Sir Edward Gait represents a rare example of constructive colonial governance in Gandhi's narrative. His willingness to accommodate Gandhi's conditions — unprecedented for a British official dealing with an agitator — set a precedent for official recognition of peasant grievances. The Champaran Agrarian Act became the legislative fruit of Gandhi's first major satyagraha on Indian soil, and Gait's role in securing it demonstrated that reform was possible within the colonial framework when officials acted with integrity.

## Key Passages

> Sir Edward Gait had a large share in getting the Committee to make a unanimous report and in getting the Agrarian Bill passed in accordance with the Committee's recommendations. Had he not adopted a firm attitude, and had he not brought all his tact to bear on the subject, the report would not have been unanimous, and the Agrarian Act would not have been passed.
>
> [143-007]
