## Overview

Gandhi's first departure for England in 1888 marked his initial journey beyond India. At eighteen years of age, he sailed from Bombay on September 4 aboard a steamer bound for London, accompanied by Tryambakrai Mazmudar, a Junagadh vakil. The voyage followed intense family negotiations, a caste outcaste declaration, and his own determination to become a barrister. The journey was the fulfillment of a plan proposed by an old family adviser who argued that an English legal qualification would position Gandhi far better than a local Indian degree.

## Origins of the Plan

The idea of sending Gandhi to England was proposed by Mavji Dave (affectionately called "Joshiji"), a shrewd and learned Brahman who was an old family friend and adviser. During a visit while Gandhi was on vacation from Samaldas College, Joshiji told his mother and elder brother: "The times are changed. And none of you can expect to succeed to your father's gadi without having had a proper education." He argued that a B.A. degree would at best qualify Gandhi for a sixty-rupee post — not for a Diwanship. Sending him to England to become a barrister, Joshiji said, would take only three years and cost four to five thousand rupees [335-1].

Gandhi, struggling with his studies at Samaldas College where he found himself "entirely at sea" and unable to follow the professors' lectures, eagerly accepted the proposal. "Nothing could have been more welcome to me," he recalled [335-3].

## The Three Vows

Gandhi's mother was the hardest to convince. She was told that young men were lost in England, that they took to meat, and that they could not live there without liquor. When she confronted Gandhi, he swore an oath that he would touch none of these things. But she was still not satisfied [414-1].

She turned to Becharji Swami, a Jain monk who was another family adviser. The Swami proposed a solution: "I shall get the boy solemnly to take the three vows, and then he can be allowed to go." He administered the oath, and Gandhi took a solemn vow before his mother to abstain from wine, women, and meat. With this, his mother gave her permission [414-2].

## The Caste Confrontation

While Gandhi waited in Bombay — his original departure having been delayed when his brother heard reports of rough seas — the Modh Bania caste became agitated. No Modh Bania had ever gone to England, and a general meeting of the caste was called [411-1].

Gandhi was summoned before the meeting. The Sheth, the headman of the community who was distantly related to Gandhi and had been on good terms with his father, declared that the voyage abroad was against their religion. He warned that it was not possible to live in England without compromising one's religion and that one was obliged to eat and drink with Europeans. When Gandhi replied that he had taken solemn vows and that the caste should not interfere, the Sheth pronounced his order: "This boy shall be treated as an outcaste from today. Whoever helps him or goes to see him off at the dock shall be punishable with a fine of one rupee four annas" [411-2].

The order had no effect on Gandhi. "I took my leave of the Sheth," he wrote. "I wondered how my brother would take it." Fortunately his brother remained firm and wrote to assure Gandhi that he had permission to go, the Sheth's order notwithstanding [411-3].

## The Outcaste Declaration and Final Arrangements

The caste meeting's pronouncement created a real obstacle. Gandhi's brother-in-law, who had been entrusted with the money for the journey, now refused to release it. He cited the Sheth's order and said he could not afford to lose caste. The situation appeared desperate — Gandhi had passage money but nothing more. He then appealed to a family friend who not only provided the funds for his passage and sundries but also cheered him up in the moment of crisis [413-1].

Another friend with experience of travel helped him acquire the necessary wardrobe and equipment. The experience was both novel and uncomfortable. "The necktie, which I delighted in wearing later, I then abhorred," Gandhi recalled. "The short jacket I looked upon as immodest. But this dislike was nothing before the desire to go to England, which was uppermost in me" [413-2].

Friends also arranged a berth for Gandhi in the same cabin as Sjt. Tryambakrai Mazmudar, the Junagadh vakil, to whom they commended him. Mazmudar was "an experienced man of mature age" who knew the world, while Gandhi was "yet a stripling of eighteen without any experience of the world." Mazmudar told Gandhi's friends not to worry about him [412-1].

The high school in Rajkot had a send-off in his honour. An incident at this ceremony revealed Gandhi's crippling shyness in its full measure. He had written out a few words of thanks on paper but, when he stood to deliver them, his head reeled and his whole frame shook. He could scarcely stammer the words out — a foreshadowing of the public-speaking difficulty that would haunt him throughout his early career [414-3].

## The Voyage

Gandhi sailed at last from Bombay on September 4, 1888. Once at sea, his shyness became acute. He was quite unaccustomed to speaking English, and except for Mazmudar, all the other passengers in the second saloon were English. He hid in his cabin throughout the day, subsisting mainly on the sweets and fruits he had brought with him, too bashful to inquire which dishes on the menu were free of meat or to attempt using knives and forks at the dining table [415-1].

An older English passenger befriended him. When they were in the Red Sea, the passenger warned Gandhi that he would have to abandon his vegetarianism in the cold of England: "It is all very well so far but you will have to revise your decision in the Bay of Biscay. And it is so cold in England that one cannot possibly live there without meat" [415-2].

Gandhi replied firmly: "I have solemnly promised to my mother not to touch meat, and therefore I cannot think of taking it. If it be found impossible to get on without it, I will far rather go back to India than eat meat in order to remain there." The Englishman later gave him a certificate testifying to his abstinence from meat, which Gandhi treasured until he realized the certificate was meaningless — and that his word alone should suffice [415-3].

## Arrival in England

The steamer reached Southampton in late September. Gandhi had worn a white flannel suit, thinking white clothes would suit him better when he stepped ashore. To his horror, he found he was the only person wearing white — "the shame of being the only person in white clothes was already too much for me" [420-1].

He and Mazmudar went to the Victoria Hotel in London, where Gandhi committed his first breach of European etiquette. Dr. P. J. Mehta, to whom Gandhi had wired, called at eight o'clock that evening. As they talked, Gandhi casually picked up Dr. Mehta's top-hat and, trying to see how smooth it was, passed his hand over it the wrong way and disturbed the fur. Dr. Mehta looked somewhat angrily at what Gandhi was doing and stopped him, but the mischief had been done [420-2].

Dr. Mehta, with a kindly sense of humour, then initiated Gandhi into the essentials of European conduct:

> "Do not touch other people's things. Do not ask questions as we usually do in India on first acquaintance; do not talk loudly; never address people 'sir' whilst speaking to them as we do in India; only servants and subordinates address their masters that way." [420-3]

That night and in the days that followed, Gandhi was consumed by homesickness. "I would continually think of my home and country. My mother's love always haunted me. At night the tears would stream down my cheeks, and home memories of all sorts made sleep out of the question." But he steeled himself: "Now that I had come, I must finish the three years, said the inner voice" [420-4].
