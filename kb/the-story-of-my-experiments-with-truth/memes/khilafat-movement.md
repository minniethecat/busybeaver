# The Khilafat Movement

The Khilafat Movement was the first national campaign in which Gandhi collaborated closely with Muslim religious leadership — and the testing ground for his conviction that Hindu-Muslim unity was essential to [[swaraj]]. By championing a cause that was ostensibly about the Ottoman Caliphate, thousands of miles from [[india]], Gandhi demonstrated his strategic genius for finding common ground across religious communities. The movement's eventual collapse, and the communal violence that followed its abrupt end, marked one of the most painful chapters in Gandhi's public life.

## Origins

The movement arose from the Treaty of Sèvres (1920), which dismembered the Ottoman Empire after its defeat in World War I. To India's Muslims, the Ottoman Sultan was also the Caliph (Khalifa) — the symbolic head of the global Muslim community, or *ummah*. The British government's threat to abolish the Caliphate and partition Ottoman territories — including the holy places of Islam — provoked outrage across the Muslim world.

In India, two brothers — Maulana Muhammad Ali and Maulana Shaukat Ali (the "Ali Brothers") — mobilized a mass campaign demanding that the British government:
1. Preserve the Ottoman Sultan's authority as Caliph
2. Maintain Ottoman control over the holy places of Islam
3. Restore pre-war Ottoman territories

## Gandhi's Intervention

Gandhi saw in the Khilafat agitation an unprecedented opportunity. For years, he had argued that Hindu-Muslim unity was a prerequisite for national freedom — and that unity had proved elusive. Here was a cause that mattered deeply to Muslims, and Gandhi believed Hindus should support it as an act of solidarity.

His reasoning was characteristically moral:

> I am not less a Hindu for sympathizing with the Muhammadans in their sorrow. I have said that I regard the Khilafat as a just cause. I regard it as an integral part of non-cooperation.
>
> (128-010)

At the All-India Khilafat Conference in Delhi (November 1919), Gandhi was the only non-Muslim on the platform. He advised the delegates to adopt nonviolent non-cooperation as their method — the same approach that was about to become the defining strategy of the [[indian-national-congress]].

## The Alliance

The Khilafat-Non-cooperation alliance transformed Indian politics:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Mass mobilization | Combined Khilafat and Congress networks brought millions into the streets |
| Hindu-Muslim unity | Joint processions, shared platforms, interfaith pledges |
| Boycott program | British goods, courts, schools, titles — a comprehensive withdrawal |
| Fund-raising | The Tilak Swaraj Fund raised over one crore rupees |
| Leadership | Gandhi, the Ali Brothers, Motilal Nehru, Lala Lajpat Rai — a united front |

Gandhi described this period as one of "intoxication" — the country seemed on the verge of achieving Swaraj within a year.

## Collapse and Aftermath

The movement was shattered by the Chauri Chaura incident in February 1922, when a mob of protesters — some calling themselves Khilafat volunteers — burned a police station in Gorakhpur district, killing twenty-two policemen. Gandhi, who had been preparing mass civil disobedience in Bardoli, immediately suspended the entire movement, declaring that the country was not yet ready for nonviolent resistance.

The decision was deeply unpopular. Many Congress leaders — including Motilal Nehru and C. R. Das — protested. But the Ali Brothers were among those who accepted Gandhi's judgment.

The final blow came in 1924, when Mustafa Kemal Atatürk's new Turkish Republic abolished the Caliphate itself — making the Khilafat demand irrelevant. The movement dissolved, and the bonds of Hindu-Muslim unity it had forged began to fray. The communal riots of the mid-1920s, culminating in the Kohat tragedy, broke Gandhi's heart.

## Lessons for Gandhi

| Lesson | Source |
|--------|--------|
| Hindu-Muslim unity is achievable through shared causes | The mass mobilization of 1920–21 |
| Religious passion, once awakened, is hard to control | Post-movement communal violence |
| Nonviolence must be absolute — one outbreak can undo years of work | Chauri Chaura |
| Leadership means taking unpopular decisions when principle demands | Gandhi's suspension of the movement |
| Transnational solidarity has limits — the Caliphate's abolition rendered the movement moot | Atatürk's reforms, 1924 |
