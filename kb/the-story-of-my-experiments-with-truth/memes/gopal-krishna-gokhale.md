# Gopal Krishna Gokhale

Gopal Krishna Gokhale was the Indian political leader whom Gandhi acknowledged as his "political guru." A moderate nationalist, scholar, and founder of the Servants of India Society, Gokhale guided Gandhi's entry into Indian public life after his return from South Africa. Gandhi met him in 1901 in Calcutta and later in Pune, encounters that shaped his understanding of Indian politics.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi first met Gokhale in Calcutta in 1901, describing the meeting as one of "faith" (046-013). Their connection deepened when Gandhi visited Gokhale in Pune, finding him at the Fergusson College residence (053-004). Gokhale became Gandhi's most trusted political advisor in India. When Gokhale visited South Africa, Gandhi served him personally, even washing his clothes — an act of devotion that embarrassed the elder statesman (063-008). Gokhale, in turn, supported Gandhi's South African work and facilitated his introduction to the Indian National Congress.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in 1866 in the Bombay Presidency, Gokhale was educated at Elphinstone College in Bombay and became a professor of history and political economy at Fergusson College in Pune. He entered politics through the Indian National Congress, becoming one of its most respected moderate leaders.

### Role in the Narrative

Gokhale introduced Gandhi to the workings of the Indian National Congress. "Gokhale took me to the Committee meeting" (069-003) of the Congress, where Gandhi was to present a resolution on South Africa. When Gandhi hesitated to speak, Gokhale urged him forward: "You have been waiting long" (069-006). Gokhale supported Gandhi's resolution (069-013) and mentored him through the political process. He was also a frequent presence at the Club where Gandhi's South African work was discussed (070-001). Gandhi observed and learned from "stalwarts like Gokhale and Surendranath" (068-013), modeling his own political conduct on Gokhale's example.

## Historical Significance

Gokhale represented the moderate wing of Indian nationalism — committed to constitutional methods, dialogue with the British, and gradual reform. He founded the Servants of India Society in 1905 to train Indians for public service. Though he died in 1915, shortly after Gandhi's return to India, his influence on Gandhi was profound and lasting. Gandhi's emphasis on constructive work, his respect for political opponents, and his willingness to engage with the British all owed something to Gokhale's example.

## Key Passages

> When Gokhale came to South Africa, he had with him a scarf which he greatly prized. He was much pleased at my washing it. "I can undertake any service," I said, "but not as a washerman." "What if you should soil it?" said Gokhale. (063-008)
