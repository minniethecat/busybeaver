# Fasting

Fasting was for Gandhi far more than a dietary practice — it was a spiritual discipline, a support for brahmacharya, and ultimately a form of satyagraha itself. He came to believe that "fasting could be made as powerful a weapon of indulgence as of restraint" (104-005), and his public fasts became among the most dramatic and controversial expressions of his philosophy.

## Definition

Gandhi distinguished between fasting as self-purification and fasting as moral pressure on others. The first was a purely personal discipline; the second was a form of satyagraha directed at those with whom one shared a bond of love. He insisted that "a fasting which is merely a carrying of the earth and water and fasting treatment" had no spiritual value (099-004). True fasting required a pure motive and was inseparable from prayer and self-examination. He also distinguished fasting from mere dieting, emphasizing that its purpose was spiritual rather than physical.

## Origins and Influences

Fasting has ancient roots in Indian religious practice — Hindus, Jains, and Buddhists all observed various forms of fasting. Gandhi's mother Putlibai was known for her rigorous observance of religious fasts, and this early exposure shaped his understanding. However, Gandhi transformed the practice by connecting it to his political method. He was also influenced by the Western naturopathic tradition, which used fasting as a therapeutic tool, but he subordinated this medical dimension to the spiritual and moral.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Fasting and Brahmacharya

Gandhi's earliest systematic use of fasting was as an aid to brahmacharya: "As an external aid to brahmacharya, fasting is as necessary as selection and restriction in diet" (062-007). He found that controlling the palate supported control of other senses, and that the discipline of regular fasting strengthened the will. He practiced various forms: complete fasts, restricted diets (fruit-only, single meal), and periodic abstention from specific foods.

### Fasting as Moral Action

As Gandhi's philosophy matured, he began to see fasting as more than personal discipline. "It dawned upon me that fasting could be made as powerful a weapon of indulgence as of restraint" (104-005). This insight opened the door to the public fast — fasting undertaken not for one's own purification alone but to move others to moral action. His fasts in Ahmedabad (1918) during the mill-workers' strike, and later his many fasts for communal harmony, became models of this practice. He insisted that fasting be undertaken only against those with whom one had a bond of love — never against strangers or enemies.

### Limitations and Dangers

Gandhi was aware of the coercive potential of fasting and warned against its misuse. "A dirty fast" — one undertaken with impure motives or to coerce rather than persuade — was worse than no fast at all. He acknowledged that "those who go in for a fruit diet, and those who observe all the rules of dietetic restrictions and fasting are as much in error as those who indulge in regular items" if they lacked the right spirit (107-010). The efficacy of fasting depended entirely on the moral condition of the faster.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Fasting sits at the intersection of Gandhi's personal and public disciplines. It supports brahmacharya (personal self-control), expresses ahimsa (nonviolent pressure rather than violent coercion), and serves satyagraha (truth-force applied through self-suffering rather than inflicting suffering on others). In this sense, fasting is the most concentrated form of satyagraha — the satyagrahi turns suffering entirely inward.
