# Burdwan

Burdwan (now Bardhaman) is a railway town in West Bengal, situated on the route between [[shantiniketan]] and northern India. It appears in [[mahatma-gandhi]]'s autobiography as the site of a significant conversation about the future of satyagraha and as a place where Gandhi experienced firsthand the indignities of third-class railway travel.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi passed through Burdwan in early 1915, travelling from Shantiniketan after the death of [[gopal-krishna-gokhale]]. He was accompanied as far as Burdwan by Charles Freer Andrews, the English missionary and friend of India. The journey by third-class carriage tested Gandhi's principles of simplicity against the harsh realities of Indian railway travel.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

At Burdwan, Andrews posed a question of historic significance: "Do you think that a time will come for satyagraha in India? And if so, have you any idea when it will come?" [128-012]. Gandhi replied that he could not say, and that he was not thinking of satyagraha for the moment. This exchange captures Gandhi's state of mind in early 1915 — still absorbing the lessons of South Africa, not yet ready to launch the movement that would define Indian nationalism.

The practical hardships of Burdwan were equally instructive. Gandhi recorded that at Burdwan "we came face to face with the hardships that a third-class passenger has to go through even in securing his ticket" [129-001]. When told that third-class tickets were not booked early, Gandhi sought out the Station Master, only to receive the same reply. When the booking window finally opened, he found that "might was right, and passengers, who were forward and indifferent to others, coming one after another, continued to push us out" [129-001].

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Burdwan crystallized Gandhi's commitment to travelling third class as a discipline of solidarity with India's poor. The indignity of being pushed aside at the booking window was not an isolated inconvenience but a revelation of how ordinary Indians were treated. The railway experience at Burdwan — and the subsequent difficulty in obtaining a refund for the excess fare between Burdwan and Mogalsarai [129-007] — reinforced his conviction that personal hardship was inseparable from political truth.
