# Self-Control in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Self-control was for Gandhi not a peripheral virtue but the central discipline of the spiritual life. His autobiography is, in large part, the story of his struggle to master his senses — palate, passion, and possessiveness — and his conviction that without this inner mastery, no outward pursuit of [[truth]] could succeed.

## Gandhi's Definition

For Gandhi, self-control was the distinguishing mark of the human. Without it, human life was indistinguishable from animal existence:

> The brute by nature knows no self-restraint. Man is man because he is capable of, and only in so far as he exercises, self-restraint.
>
> (084-015)

This is a radical anthropological claim: what makes us human is not reason or speech but the capacity to say no to our impulses. Self-control is not repression for Gandhi but the condition of genuine freedom — the ability to choose one's actions rather than being driven by appetite.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's understanding of self-control drew from multiple sources. The Hindu and Jain traditions provided the conceptual framework of indriya-nigraha (sense-control), while the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali offered a systematic path. But Gandhi's practice was shaped as much by personal experience as by doctrine.

His reading of [[leo-tolstoy]] was crucial: Tolstoy's advocacy of "internal efforts as opposed to outward means" — of self-control rather than contraception — had a "far greater effect" on Gandhi than any other teaching on the subject (050-004). The conviction that moral problems required internal solutions, not technical ones, became a cornerstone of Gandhi's ethics.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Early Struggles

Gandhi is unusually candid about his early failures with self-control. He describes himself as "devoted to the passions that flesh is heir to" (001-007), and his early marriage brought the challenge of sensual desire to the center of his life. His jealousy of his wife Kasturba, his attempt to control her movements, and the suffering this caused them both were, by his own reckoning, failures of self-control:

> The canker of suspicion was rooted out only when I understood Ahimsa in all its bearings.
>
> (009-004)

### The Path to Brahmacharya

Self-control found its fullest expression in Gandhi's vow of [[brahmacharya]] (celibacy), taken in 1906. He describes this as the culmination of years of effort:

> Even after my conscience had been roused in the matter, I failed twice. I failed because the motive that actuated the effort was none the highest.
>
> (050-003)

The key insight was that self-control could not be achieved through negative effort alone — through the mere suppression of desire. It required a positive reorientation of the entire person toward Truth. When the motive became the highest — service to the community and the pursuit of self-realization — self-control became possible:

> I had to break up my household at Johannesburg to be able to serve during the "Rebellion." Within one month of offering my services, I had to give up the house I had so carefully furnished. I took my wife and children to Phoenix and led the Indian ambulance corps attached to the Natal forces. During the difficult marches that had then to be performed, the idea flashed upon me that, if I wanted to devote myself to the service of the community in this manner, I must relinquish the desire for children and wealth and live the life of a vanaprastha — of one retired from household cares.
>
> (050-005)

### Diet and Self-Control

Gandhi understood diet as inseparable from self-control. The palate was the gateway to the senses, and mastery of food was the beginning of mastery of desire:

> As an external aid to brahmacharya, fasting is as necessary as selection and restriction in diet. So overpowering are the senses that they can be kept under control only when they are completely hedged in on all sides, from above and from beneath.
>
> (053-004)

But Gandhi was careful to note that mechanical fasting — abstaining from food while feasting the mind on imagined delicacies — was worse than useless. Fasting was effective "when mind cooperates with starving body, that is to say, when it cultivates a distaste for the objects that are denied to the body" (053-004).

### The Joy of Self-Restraint

Perhaps the most striking feature of Gandhi's account of self-control is his insistence that it brought not deprivation but joy:

> As I look back upon the twenty years of the vow, I am filled with pleasure and wonderment. The more or less successful practice of self-control had been going on since 1901. But the freedom and joy that came to me after taking the vow had never been experienced before 1906. Before the vow I had been open to being overcome by temptation at any moment. Now the vow was a sure shield against temptation.
>
> (052-013)

The vow was not a burden but a "sure shield" — a liberation from the constant vulnerability to temptation. This paradox — that self-restraint brings freedom — is central to Gandhi's ethics.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Self-control is the inner foundation of Gandhi's entire ethical system:

- **[[brahmacharya]]**: The vow of celibacy is the most demanding form of self-control, and Gandhi considered it essential for the pursuit of Truth.
- **[[truth]]**: Self-control is necessary for truthfulness — the uncontrolled person cannot be trusted to speak or act truthfully under pressure of desire.
- **[[ahimsa]]**: Outward nonviolence requires inward self-control; the person who cannot master their own anger and impulse cannot practice genuine nonviolence.
- **[[fasting]]**: Voluntary fasting is both a tool for and an expression of self-control.
- **[[vow]]**: The formal vow is the instrument through which self-control is stabilized and made irrevocable.
- **[[celibacy]]**: A specific dimension of self-control related to sexual restraint.
