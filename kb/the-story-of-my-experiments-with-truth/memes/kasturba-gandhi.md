# Kasturba Gandhi

Kasturba Gandhi (affectionately called "Ba") was Gandhi's wife, married to him in an arranged child marriage when both were thirteen. Over six decades, she was not merely a witness to Gandhi's experiments but a participant in them — enduring the same hardships, sharing his imprisonments, and at times resisting his more extreme demands. Their relationship was a laboratory for Gandhi's evolving ideas about marriage, gender, and self-control.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Kasturba was betrothed to Gandhi at age seven and married at thirteen. Their early relationship followed traditional patterns: Gandhi was a jealous and controlling husband during his early years in India and South Africa. Over time, however, the relationship transformed. Kasturba became a partner in his public work, joining him in South Africa, participating in satyagraha campaigns, and enduring imprisonment. She also became the primary target of Gandhi's experiments with brahmacharya — his 1906 vow of celibacy was a decision that affected her profoundly. Despite tensions, she remained loyal to him and his cause until her death in prison in 1944.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in 1869 in Porbandar, Kasturba Kapadia was illiterate when she married. Gandhi himself taught her to read and write, though not without friction. She bore four sons — Harilal, Manilal, Ramdas, and Devdas — and managed the household through years of Gandhi's absences and experiments.

### Role in the Narrative

Kasturba appears throughout the autobiography not as a central character but as a constant presence — the partner who shared Gandhi's dietary experiments, his vows, his moves between continents, and his imprisonments. She participated in the satyagraha campaigns in South Africa and India, often leading women protesters. Her quiet endurance and occasional resistance to Gandhi's decisions provide a counterpoint to his narrative of spiritual progress.

## Historical Significance

Kasturba Gandhi was more than "Gandhi's wife." She was a political activist in her own right, leading women's protests during the Salt Satyagraha and the Quit India Movement. Her death in 1944, while imprisoned at the Aga Khan Palace in Pune, was a devastating blow to Gandhi. She represents the often-unacknowledged role of women in the Indian independence movement.
