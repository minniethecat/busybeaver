# The Inner Voice in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Throughout his autobiography, Gandhi returns to a quiet but decisive phenomenon: the inner voice. It was not a metaphor but a real experience — a source of guidance that he trusted more than public opinion, more than the advice of friends, and sometimes even more than his own reasoning.

## The First Awakening

Gandhi's earliest recorded experience of the inner voice came in London, when homesickness threatened to overwhelm him:

> England I could not bear, but to return to India was not to be thought of. Now that I had come, I must finish the three years, said the inner voice.
>
> (013-017)

The inner voice here is not mystical but almost practical — it is the voice that cuts through paralysis and points to duty. An eighteen-year-old boy, alone in a foreign country, discovers an internal compass.

## Surrender to the Voice

By the time he encountered Christian missionaries in South Africa, Gandhi had learned to trust this internal guidance as a matter of principle:

> I had long since taught myself to follow the inner voice. I delighted in submitting to it. To act against it would be difficult and painful to me.
>
> (040-008)

The phrase "I delighted in submitting to it" is striking. Submission to the inner voice was not a burden but a joy — the relief of knowing what one must do and simply doing it.

## Conscience Above Law

Gandhi's most famous invocation of the inner voice came during his trial for sedition, when he explained his disobedience as obedience to a higher law:

> I have disregarded the order served upon me not for want of respect for lawful authority, but in obedience to the higher law of our being, the voice of conscience.
>
> (137-013)

This is the inner voice at its most challenging: not a private feeling but a public justification for civil disobedience. Gandhi was willing to accept the penalty of law because he answered to a law beyond it.

## Cultivating the Inner Voice

Gandhi did not believe the inner voice was available on demand. It had to be cultivated through silence, prayer, and the discipline of truth-telling. Only a mind stilled by these practices could distinguish the genuine inner voice from the clamour of ego and desire.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[truth]]**: The inner voice is the channel through which truth speaks to the individual conscience.
- **[[prayer]]**: Prayer quiets the mind and creates the conditions in which the inner voice can be heard.
- **[[silence]]**: Gandhi practised weekly silence (mauna) partly to sharpen his sensitivity to the inner voice.
