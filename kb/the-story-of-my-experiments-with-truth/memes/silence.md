# Silence

The practice of silence (mauna) was one of Gandhi's most distinctive spiritual disciplines. For decades, he observed a full day of silence each week — usually Monday — during which he communicated only in writing when absolutely necessary. He described silence as a form of prayer, a discipline of self-restraint, and a practical necessity for someone whose life was consumed by speech and public activity.

## Definition

For Gandhi, silence was not merely the absence of speech but a positive spiritual practice. External silence was meant to cultivate inner stillness — the quieting of the mind's chatter that made it possible to hear the "still small voice" of conscience. He distinguished between the physical discipline of not speaking and the spiritual discipline of calming the mind, recognizing that one could be outwardly silent while inwardly agitated. True silence required both.

## Origins and Influences

Mauna (vow of silence) has a long tradition in Indian ascetic practice. Hindu sadhus, Jain monks, and Buddhist meditators all observed periods of silence as a means of spiritual purification. Gandhi was familiar with these traditions but adapted silence to his own practical and activist context. He did not retreat to a cave or monastery; he continued his political work on silence days, conducting meetings through written notes and using the time freed from speech for prayer and reflection.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Weekly Silence

Gandhi began observing a weekly day of silence during his South African years, initially as a practical measure to manage his overwhelming workload and correspondence. Over time, it deepened into a spiritual practice. On silence days, he rose earlier than usual for prayer, devoted extra time to spinning and writing, and avoided all unnecessary conversation. Even during critical negotiations, he maintained his silence day, communicating through written notes.

### Silence as Training

Gandhi valued silence as training for the satyagrahi. Speech, he believed, often served the ego — to impress, to dominate, to defend. Silence starved the ego and cultivated humility. It was also a form of self-restraint analogous to fasting: "Fasting of the tongue" was, for him, as important as fasting of the stomach.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Silence sits within the family of self-disciplinary practices — alongside brahmacharya, fasting, and dietary restraint — that prepared the satyagrahi for action. It is the complement to prayer: if prayer is reaching outward toward Truth, silence is creating the inner stillness in which Truth can be heard. In a life consumed by public speech, silence was Gandhi's way of restoring balance and preserving inner resources.

## Key Passages

> Silence is part of the spiritual discipline of a votary of truth.

> Experience has taught me that silence is a part of the spiritual discipline of a votary of truth.
