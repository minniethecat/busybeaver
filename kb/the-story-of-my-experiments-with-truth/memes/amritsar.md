# Amritsar

Amritsar is a city in [[punjab]] that became a flashpoint during the 1919 Rowlatt Satyagraha. [[mahatma-gandhi]] was arrested while attempting to enter Punjab to calm the situation, and shortly afterward the Jallianwala Bagh massacre took place, marking one of the darkest chapters of British rule in India.

## Gandhi's Summons

In early April 1919, while the Rowlatt agitation was spreading across India, Gandhi received urgent calls from Punjab:

> "The story of happenings in Delhi was repeated with variations in Lahore and Amritsar. From Amritsar Drs. Satyapal and Kitchlew had sent me a pressing invitation to go there." [155-003]

Dr. [[satyapal]] and Dr. [[kitchlew]] were prominent Punjabi nationalists who had been leading the protests in Amritsar. Gandhi, though "altogether unacquainted with the Punjab," felt compelled to respond [155-003].

## The Arrest at Palwal

Gandhi's journey to Punjab was cut short. On the night of April 7, he started for Delhi and Amritsar. At Mathura on April 8, he first heard rumours of his probable arrest. At Palwal station shortly afterward, he was served with a written order prohibiting his entry into the Punjab and was taken off the train [155-009].

The arrest sparked outrage. Gandhi was brought back to Bombay, but the damage was done — the Punjab, deprived of Gandhi's moderating presence, boiled over.

## The Massacre

On April 13, 1919 — Baisakhi day — troops under General [[reginald-dyer]] opened fire on an unarmed crowd gathered in Jallianwala Bagh, a walled garden in Amritsar:

> "Do you know what is happening in Ahmedabad? And what has happened in Amritsar? People have everywhere gone nearly mad." [156-010]

Gandhi later reflected that "people had lost all self-control" and the events at Amritsar demonstrated how "a whole people can be seized with a madness" of violence when pushed beyond endurance [156-010].

## The Aftermath

The Amritsar tragedy, followed by the imposition of martial law across Punjab, transformed the Rowlatt Satyagraha from a limited civil disobedience campaign into a nationwide crisis. The Hunter Commission was established to investigate, but its findings did little to repair the rupture between British authority and Indian opinion. Amritsar became a permanent symbol of colonial brutality and the necessity of Indian self-rule.
