## Overview

The Durban Farewell Meeting was a gathering held at Sydenham in 1894, hosted by Dada Abdulla Sheth to bid farewell to Mohandas Gandhi, who was preparing to return to India after completing his legal case in Pretoria. During the event, a casual glance at a newspaper transformed a social occasion into the genesis of a political movement. "Thus God laid the foundations of my life in South Africa and sowed the seed of the fight for national self-respect," Gandhi later wrote [897-1].

## The Gathering

Abdulla Sheth, Gandhi's client and host, was not the man to let him sail without a send-off. He organized a farewell party at Sydenham, intending to spend the whole day there. Gandhi's legal case in Pretoria had concluded, and he had returned to Durban to make preparations for his return to India. There appeared to be no reason to stay [892-1].

## The Accidental Discovery

While idly turning over the sheets of newspapers at the party, Gandhi chanced upon a paragraph in a corner of one of them under the caption "Indian Franchise." It referred to a Bill before the Natal House of Legislature that sought to deprive Indians of their right to elect members of the Legislative Assembly. Gandhi was ignorant of the Bill, and so were the rest of the guests assembled there [892-2].

When Gandhi inquired, Abdulla Sheth responded with a revealing admission of the Indian community's political helplessness:

> "What can we understand in these matters? We can only understand things that affect our trade. As you know all our trade in the Orange Free State has been swept away. We agitated about it, but in vain. We are after all lame men, being unlettered. We generally take in newspapers simply to ascertain the daily market rates, etc. What can we know of legislation? Our eyes and ears are the European attorneys here." [893-1]

## The Young Christian Indians

Gandhi then asked about the young Indians born and educated in Natal. "Do not they help you?" he asked. Abdulla Sheth responded in despair: "They never care to come to us, and to tell you the truth, we care less to recognize them. Being Christians, they are under the thumb of the white clergymen, who in their turn are subject to the Government" [893-2].

This revelation opened Gandhi's eyes. He felt deeply that this class of educated, Christian Indians should be "claimed as our own." The question arose in his mind: "Was this the meaning of Christianity? Did they cease to be Indians because they had become Christians?" [893-3]

## The Franchise Crisis

Gandhi immediately recognized the gravity of the situation. He told the assembled guests: "This Bill, if it passes into law, will make our lot extremely difficult. It is the first nail into our coffin. It strikes at the root of our self-respect" [894-1].

Abdulla Sheth then revealed the genesis of the franchise question. The community knew nothing about their voting rights until Mr. Escombe, one of the best-known attorneys in Natal and a combative political figure, put the idea into their heads. Escombe feared that the Wharf Engineer, with whom he was feuding, might deprive him of his votes and defeat him at the election. So he encouraged the Indians to register as voters — and they voted for him. Abdulla explained that the franchise did not hold the profound value for them that Gandhi attached to it [894-2].

## The Decision to Stay

The conversation had transformed the mood of the gathering. One of the guests spoke up: "Shall I tell you what should be done? You cancel your passage by this boat, stay here a month longer, and we will fight as you direct us." All the others joined in: "Indeed, indeed. Abdulla Sheth, you must detain Gandhibhai" [895-1].

Abdulla Sheth, a shrewd man, raised a practical point: "But you should remember that he is a barrister. What about his fees?" The mention of fees pained Gandhi, who broke in: "Abdulla Sheth, fees are out of the question. There can be no fees for public work. I can stay, if at all, as a servant" [895-2].

Gandhi, however, raised the practical need for funds — for telegrams, printed literature, touring, consultations with local attorneys, and law-books. A chorus of voices responded: "Allah is great and merciful. Money will come in. Men there are, as many as you may need. You please consent to stay, and all will be well" [895-3].

The farewell party was thus turned into a working committee. Gandhi suggested finishing dinner quickly and returning home. That evening he worked out in his own mind an outline of the campaign, ascertained the names of those on the voters' list, and made up his mind to stay on for a month [896-1].

## The Natal Indian Congress: A Permanent Organization

The despatch of the petition regarding the disfranchising bill was not sufficient in itself. Sustained agitation was essential for making an impression on the Secretary of State for the Colonies. Gandhi consulted Abdulla Sheth and other friends, and they all decided to bring into being a permanent organization. On May 22, the Natal Indian Congress came into being [1020-1].

The choice of name was itself a political statement. Gandhi knew the name "Congress" was in bad odour with Conservatives in England, and yet the Congress was the very life of India. He wanted to popularize it in Natal. "It savoured of cowardice to hesitate to adopt the name," he wrote. With full explanation of his reasons, he recommended the name, and it was adopted [1020-2].

Dada Abdulla's spacious room was packed to the full on that day. The Congress received enthusiastic approval. Its constitution was simple but its subscription was deliberately set high — only those who paid five shillings monthly could be members. Abdulla Sheth headed the subscription list with £2 per month. Gandhi, though it was "no small amount" for him, put down £1 per month [1020-3].

### Financial Discipline and Self-Reliance

Gandhi established strict financial practices from the outset. He had learnt at the outset not to carry on public work with borrowed money. "One could rely on people's promises in most matters except in respect of money," he observed. The Natal Indian Congress never ran into debt. Every pie was accounted for — Gandhi did not even have receipt books printed in the beginning, but used a cyclostyle machine in his office to take copies of receipts [1021-1].

He insisted that every contributor receive a receipt, even though people never cared to have them. The account books for 1894 could be found intact even decades later. Gandhi believed that "carefully kept accounts are a sine qua non for any organization. Without them it falls into disrepute. Without properly kept accounts it is impossible to maintain truth in its pristine purity" [1021-2].

### Civic Education Through Meetings

Meetings were held once a month or even once a week if required. People had no experience of taking part in public discussions or speaking briefly and to the point. Everyone hesitated to stand up. Gandhi explained the rules of procedure, and they respected them. Many who had never spoken before an audience "soon acquired the habit of thinking and speaking publicly about matters of public interest" [1021-3].

### Outreach to Colonial-Born Indians

Another feature of the Congress was service of the Colonial-born educated Indians. The Colonial-born Indian Educational Association was founded under the auspices of the Congress. It served to ventilate their needs and grievances, stimulate thought, bring them into touch with Indian merchants, and afford them scope for community service. It functioned as a debating society with regular meetings and a small library [1022-1].

## Significance

This impromptu political awakening at a social gathering marked the genesis of Gandhi's twenty-year struggle for Indian rights in South Africa. It led directly to the formation of the Natal Indian Congress, the first major political organization representing Indian interests in South Africa. The encounter revealed to Gandhi the depth of political powerlessness among the Indian merchant class — wealthy in trade but "lame men, being unlettered" in matters of legislation — and the moral failure of a system that severed educated Christian Indians from their community of origin. The methods of petition, public meeting, organized collective action, and meticulous financial accounting that Gandhi pioneered in the ensuing campaign became the foundation upon which the method of Satyagraha was later built.
