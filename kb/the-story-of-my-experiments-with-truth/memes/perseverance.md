## Overview

Perseverance was a quality that Mahatma Gandhi repeatedly emphasized and exemplified in his autobiography. He saw it as essential to both spiritual growth and practical achievement, and he noted it as a defining characteristic of those he most admired. For Gandhi, perseverance was not a matter of mere stubbornness but a disciplined commitment to truth and duty.

## In Gandhi's Own Practice

Gandhi's perseverance manifested in small daily disciplines as well as large undertakings. During his early days in South Africa, he carefully and persistently taught himself the tune of the British national anthem, believing that loyalty to the throne required such demonstrations [051-002]. This same quality drove his language studies: while traveling by ship, he and an English friend hired an Urdu teacher from among the deck passengers and made "excellent progress" despite Gandhi finding it harder to memorize vocabulary than his companion. He observed wryly: "The officer had a better memory than I. He would never forget a word after once he had seen it; I often found it difficult to decipher Urdu letters. I brought more perseverance to bear, but could never overtake the officer" [049-005].

Gopal Krishna Gokhale, during the month Gandhi spent living with him in Calcutta, was "deeply impressed with my habit of fending for myself, my personal cleanliness, perseverance and regularity, and would often overwhelm me with praise" [079-007]. This recognition from a man Gandhi revered as his political guru reinforced the value he placed on steady, methodical effort.

## Perseverance in the Face of Opposition

Gandhi's perseverance was often tested by those who disagreed with his choices. In his early London days, a friend who disapproved of his vegetarianism would "continually reason with me to eat meat," arguing that Gandhi's persistence in keeping a vow made to his mother was "pure superstition." The friend declared bluntly: "I tell you this persistence will not help you to gain anything here" [020-009]. Gandhi's refusal to yield, despite social pressure and the practical inconvenience of his diet, foreshadowed the moral steadfastness that would characterize his later satyagraha campaigns.

In his legal practice, perseverance combined with frankness became a powerful tool. A client once remarked to him: "I see you will never take a no for an answer" [133-043] — an admission that Gandhi's patient persistence could wear down even the most resistant parties. This quality served him equally in the pursuit of out-of-court settlements, where he believed persistence in revealing the full truth to both sides would ultimately make litigation unnecessary.

## Perseverance in Others

Gandhi frequently acknowledged perseverance in those around him. He recalled with admiration the "faith, perseverance and selfless sacrifice" of the Dravidian workers in South Africa, whose dedication he witnessed firsthand. "And they were mostly illiterate, the men no less than the women. The fight in South Africa was for such, and it was fought by illiterate soldiers; it was for the poor, and the poor took their full share in it" [049-008]. He similarly praised Mr. and Mrs. Spencer Walton of the South Africa General Mission for their "humility, perseverance and devotion to work" [047-002].

At the Phoenix Settlement in Shantiniketan, he observed how Maganlal Gandhi, "by dint of his love, knowledge and perseverance," had made the ashram community flourish [128-004]. Later, reflecting on his choice of Adamji Miyakhan as secretary of the Natal Indian Congress, Gandhi noted with satisfaction that Miyakhan "satisfied all with his perseverance, liberality, amiability and courtesy, and proved to everyone that the secretary's work did not require a man with a barrister's degree or high English education" [056-009].

## Perseverance and Truth

For Gandhi, perseverance was inseparable from his experiments with truth. Writing the autobiography itself was an act of perseverance — one he undertook only because coworkers "persisted in their suggestion" for him to do so [097-021]. The quality that allowed him to sustain dietary experiments, vows, and political campaigns over decades was the same quality he observed and celebrated in the ordinary people around him: a quiet, determined refusal to abandon what conscience and duty required.
