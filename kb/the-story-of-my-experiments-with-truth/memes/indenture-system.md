# The Indenture System

The indenture system — known as "girmit" among the laborers themselves — was the colonial labor regime that brought over 150,000 Indians to South Africa between 1860 and 1911. These indentured workers, recruited primarily from the Madras and Calcutta Presidencies, worked the sugar cane fields, coal mines, and railways of [[natal]] under five-year contracts that often became lifetime servitude. The system created the Indian population whose rights Gandhi fought for — and the conditions of degradation that radicalized his politics.

## Origins and Operation

Indian indenture emerged after the abolition of slavery in the British Empire (1833), when colonial planters demanded a replacement labor supply. The system was presented as free contract migration, but the reality was starkly different:

| Aspect | Official Description | Reality |
|--------|---------------------|---------|
| Recruitment | Voluntary migration | Many were deceived about destination and terms |
| Contract | 5-year indenture, then free return passage | Renewal pressure, debt traps, lost papers |
| Working conditions | "Free" laborers with legal rights | Near-slave conditions, corporal punishment |
| Wages | Fixed minimum wage | Systematically underpaid |
| Post-indenture | Free settlement rights | £3 annual tax to remain in Natal |

Gandhi arrived in [[durban]] in 1893 — just two years before the £3 tax on "free" Indians (ex-indentured laborers who chose to remain in Natal) was imposed. This tax, designed to force Indians to "repatriate" or re-indenture, became one of Gandhi's earliest and most sustained campaigns.

## Gandhi's Engagement

Gandhi's relationship with the indentured community was initially distant. He was a barrister serving wealthy Muslim merchants like [[abdulla-sheth]] — men who carefully distinguished themselves from "coolies," the derogatory term for indentured laborers. Gandhi notes his hosts' own prejudices: Abdulla Sheth's slighting reference to the "waiter" class (032-012) reflected the class divisions within the Indian diaspora.

Yet Gandhi's politics steadily drew him toward the indentured. Several factors converged:

1. **The £3 Tax Campaign**: Gandhi recognized that the tax was designed to destroy the free Indian population of Natal — and that its abolition was essential to the community's survival.

2. **The 1913 Satyagraha**: The culminating campaign of Gandhi's South African years was explicitly on behalf of indentured laborers. When the Cape Supreme Court ruled that only Christian marriages were valid — effectively bastardizing the children of Hindu and Muslim marriages — the indentured community was the most vulnerable. Gandhi's satyagraha expanded to include a strike of Newcastle coal miners and the Great March of 2,000 Indians crossing into the Transvaal.

3. **Moral Education**: Living among indentured communities during the satyagraha campaigns, Gandhi came to see them not as objects of charity but as fellow satyagrahis — moral agents capable of the same self-sacrifice and discipline as educated nationalists.

## The System's End

The Indian indenture system to Natal was formally terminated in 1911, though existing contracts continued to run their course. The abolition was the result of sustained agitation by the Indian National Congress in India, the Natal Indian Congress in South Africa, and liberal opinion in Britain — a transnational alliance that Gandhi helped forge.

The broader significance of the indenture system for Gandhi's thought cannot be overstated:

| Dimension | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Political | Taught Gandhi to organize across class lines — merchants and laborers in one movement |
| Moral | Forced him to confront the limits of constitutional petitioning and the need for mass action |
| Spiritual | Made him see satyagraha not as elite politics but as the weapon of the poor |
| Strategic | The 1913 satyagraha proved that indentured laborers could sustain disciplined nonviolence |
