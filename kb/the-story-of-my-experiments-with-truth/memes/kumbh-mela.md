# Kumbh Mela

The Kumbh Mela of 1915 at Hardvar (Haridwar) was the first great Indian religious gathering Gandhi witnessed after his return from South Africa. The fair, held once every twelve years, drew an estimated seventeen lakh (1.7 million) pilgrims. For Gandhi, the experience was less a pilgrimage than a profound confrontation with both the faith and hypocrisy of popular Hinduism — a confrontation that led him to adopt two of his lifelong vows.

## Background

Gandhi had returned to India only months earlier, after a decade in South Africa. He had not gone to Hardvar as a pilgrim: "I have never thought of frequenting places of pilgrimage in search of piety" (207-000). Rather, he went to meet Mahatma Munshiramji at his Gurukul and to join the volunteer corps that Gokhale's Servants of India Society had sent for service at the Kumbh. Maganlal Gandhi and the Phoenix party had already preceded him.

## Timeline

### The Journey

Gandhi traveled from Rangoon to Calcutta, then to Hardvar. The journey was arduous: "From Saharanpur we were huddled into carriages for goods or cattle. These had no roofs, and what with the blazing midday sun overhead and the scorching iron floor beneath, we were all but roasted" (207-000). He noted with irony that orthodox Hindus who refused water marked as "Mussalmani" would not hesitate to accept wine or beef tea from a doctor during illness (207-000).

### Service at the Fair

At Hardvar, the Phoenix party took up scavenger's work — covering latrine pits with earth — a practice they had first adopted at Shantiniketan. Gandhi himself, however, found his time consumed by crowds of *darshan*-seekers: "This left me not a minute which I could call my own. I was followed even to the bathing ghat by these darshan-seekers, nor did they leave me alone whilst I was having my meals" (207-000). It was here that he first realized "what a deep impression my humble services in South Africa had made throughout the whole of India" (207-000).

### The Night of Decision

On the day of the fair, Gandhi was troubled by the contrast between the genuine piety of countless pilgrims and the hypocrisy and iniquity he also observed. He spent the entire night in deep thought: "There were those pious souls in the midst of the hypocrisy that surrounded them. They would be free of guilt before their Maker" (207-000). Rather than leave in protest, he chose to impose self-discipline upon himself. He resolved on two lifelong vows: never to take more than five articles of food in twenty-four hours, and never to eat after dark.

## Gandhi's Role

Gandhi was not an organizer of the Kumbh but an acute observer. The experience crystallized his understanding of how to engage with India's popular religious culture — not by rejecting mass pilgrimages as superstitious, but by using them as occasions for self-purification and service. His dietary vows, adopted that night at Hardvar, became permanent features of his disciplined life. He later reflected: "I have been under these vows for now thirteen years. They have subjected me to a severe test, but I am able to testify that they have also served as my shield" (207-000).

## Historical Significance

The Kumbh Mela experience cemented Gandhi's practice of responding to moral difficulty with personal vows of self-denial — a method he would repeatedly deploy throughout his public life. It also deepened his connection with the masses of India on their own religious terms, even as he maintained his critical distance from popular superstition. The Hardvar experience directly preceded the founding of the Satyagraha Ashram at Ahmedabad on May 25, 1915.
