# Zulu Rebellion

The Zulu Rebellion (or Bambatha Rebellion) of 1906 was a revolt against British colonial rule in Natal. Gandhi, still professing loyalty to the Empire, organized a small Indian ambulance corps to tend wounded Zulus — who were being neglected by official medical services. The experience of witnessing the brutality of colonial warfare "set me furiously thinking in the direction of self-control" (061-005) and directly led to his brahmacharya vow.

## Background

The rebellion was triggered by a poll tax imposed on Zulu men. British forces suppressed it with extreme violence. Gandhi's ambulance corps served in difficult conditions, tending to Zulu casualties whom white medical personnel ignored.

## Gandhi's Role

Gandhi's experience during the rebellion was transformative. The sight of colonial violence, combined with the discipline of ambulance work, convinced him that a life of public service required radical self-purification. Shortly after, he took the vow of brahmacharya.

## Historical Significance

The Zulu Rebellion was, for Gandhi, the crucible in which his commitment to self-discipline was forged. Without it, the brahmacharya vow — and the ascetic discipline that defined his later public life — might not have taken the form it did.
