# Broach

Broach (now Bharuch) is a historic port city on the banks of the Narmada River in Gujarat, significant in Gandhi's autobiography as the place where the practical revival of the spinning wheel — the *charkha* — found its most important early champion. It was at the Broach Educational Conference in 1917 that Gandhi "discovered that remarkable lady Gangabehn Majmundar" [163-005], whose enterprising search across Gujarat would eventually locate the long-abandoned spinning wheels that launched the khadi movement.

## Gandhi's Connection

By 1917, Gandhi had become increasingly impatient about the spinning wheel. He had written in *Hind Swaraj* (1908) that the charkha was the "panacea for the growing pauperism of India," yet he had still never actually seen one. The [[sabarmati-ashram]] had introduced handlooms, but no one in the Ashram knew how to spin. "We could get neither a spinning wheel nor a spinner to teach us how to spin," Gandhi recalled [163-004]. "I plied every chance visitor to the Ashram who was likely to possess some information about hand spinning with questions about the art. But the art being confined to women and having been all but exterminated, if there was some stray spinner still surviving in some obscure corner, only a member of that sex was likely to find out her whereabouts" [163-004].

It was in this state of frustrated searching that Gandhi was taken by his Gujarati friends to preside at the Broach Educational Conference.

## In the Narrative

### The Discovery of Gangabehn Majmundar

At Broach, Gandhi met Gangabehn Majmundar, a widow whose "enterprising spirit knew no bounds" [163-005]. Her education, Gandhi noted, "in the accepted sense of the term, was not much. But in courage and common sense she easily surpassed the general run of our educated women" [163-005]. She had already rejected untouchability and "fearlessly moved among and served the suppressed classes" [163-005]. She was independent — she "had means of her own, and her needs were few" [163-005] — and possessed a physical courage that set her apart: "She had a well seasoned constitution, and went about everywhere without an escort. She felt quite at home on horseback" [163-005].

### From Broach to Godhra

Gandhi came to know Gangabehn more intimately at the Godhra Conference. To her he "poured out my grief about the charkha, and she lightened my burden by a promise to prosecute an earnest and incessant search for the spinning wheel" [163-005]. This promise — made at Broach and deepened at Godhra — would prove to be the turning point in the practical revival of hand-spinning in India.

Gangabehn's search eventually led her to Vijapur in Baroda State, where she found families that still had spinning wheels stored away as "useless lumber" and were willing to resume spinning if provided with slivers and a market for their yarn. This discovery, rooted in the meeting at Broach, was the seed from which the entire khadi movement grew.

## Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Broach represents the way Gandhi's programme of national regeneration depended on finding the right people — often unlikely people — in unlikely places. Gangabehn Majmundar was not a Congress leader or an educated reformer; she was a widow with courage, common sense, and a willingness to travel alone through Gujarat on horseback in search of a nearly extinct technology. The Broach Educational Conference, which brought Gandhi to the city, was thus the occasion for a discovery that would shape the economic dimension of India's freedom struggle far more than any political resolution passed at a Congress session.
