# Sabarmati

Sabarmati is the site of [[mahatma-gandhi]]'s Satyagraha Ashram, established on the banks of the Sabarmati River near [[ahmedabad]] in 1917. The Ashram became Gandhi's principal residence in India and the nerve centre of his political campaigns, including the 1918 [[kheda]] Satyagraha and the 1930 Salt March to [[dandi]].

## Founding of the Ashram

After returning from South Africa, Gandhi was determined to establish an Ashram in [[gujarat]] where he could settle with his Phoenix family. With the support of [[gopal-krishna-gokhale]], he chose a site on the banks of the Sabarmati River, just outside Ahmedabad. The Ashram was deliberately kept simple, emphasizing manual labour, self-sufficiency, and communal living.

The Ashram's location was strategic: close enough to Ahmedabad for access to mills and funding, yet far enough to maintain the simplicity of rural life. It became known as the Satyagraha Ashram, embodying the principle of truth-force that Gandhi was developing into a political methodology.

## The Kheda Campaign

During the 1918 Kheda Satyagraha, the Ashram served as the organizational base from which Gandhi and his coworkers, including [[vallabhbhai-patel]], coordinated the no-revenue campaign. Volunteers were trained at the Ashram before being dispatched to villages throughout Kheda. Even when Gandhi's health failed — he once walked "with very great difficulty to the Sabarmati station, a distance of only ten furlongs" — the Ashram remained the anchor point [152-003].

## The Rowlatt Agitation

In 1919, during the Rowlatt Satyagraha, the Ashram became a centre of political ferment. Gandhi wrote that news of the [[amritsar]] massacre reached the Ashram through travellers and newspapers, deepening the community's commitment to non-cooperation. The Ashram's discipline of non-violence was severely tested as "people had everywhere gone nearly mad" with anger at British repression [156-010].

## A Laboratory for Living

More than a political headquarters, the Ashram was a laboratory for Gandhi's ideas about simple living. Residents spun their own cloth, grew their own food, and observed vows of truth, non-violence, celibacy, non-possession, and manual labour. The Ashram's daily routine — prayer, spinning, community work — was designed to demonstrate that political freedom must be built on personal discipline.

## The Salt March

In 1930, Gandhi began his famous Salt March from the Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi on the Gujarat coast, a journey of 240 miles. The march, which began with 78 Ashram inmates, grew into a nationwide movement of civil disobedience against the salt tax. Before departing, Gandhi vowed not to return to the Ashram until India achieved independence — a vow he kept.
