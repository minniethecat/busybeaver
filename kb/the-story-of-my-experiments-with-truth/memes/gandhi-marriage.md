Gandhi's child marriage at the age of thirteen was a defining event of his early years, one he later described as a "bitter draught" he had to record in his pursuit of Truth (102-079). The event was part of a triple wedding arranged by his family in Porbandar.

## The Marriage Ceremony

Gandhi was married, not merely betrothed, in a formal Hindu ceremony. He noted that in Kathiawad there were two distinct rites — betrothal and marriage — with betrothal being a non-binding preliminary agreement between parents (102-079). Gandhi recalled having been betrothed three times without his knowledge, as two girls chosen for him had died before the final marriage arrangement was made (103-080).

The decision to marry Gandhi, his second brother, and a cousin simultaneously was driven by considerations of convenience and economy rather than the children's welfare. As Gandhi wrote: "In doing so there was no thought of our welfare, much less our wishes. It was purely a question of their own convenience and economy" (103-080). The elders reasoned that a single triple wedding would mean less expense and greater ceremony.

## Family Circumstances

Gandhi's father, Karamchand Gandhi, who served as Diwan (prime minister) of Porbandar, faced difficulties during the wedding preparations. The Thakore Saheb would not release him until the last moment, and although special stage coaches were arranged to reduce the five-day cart journey to three days, the coach overturned at the third stage, leaving Karamchand severely injured and arriving "bandaged all over" (104-081). Despite his injuries, he put on a brave face and participated fully in the wedding ceremony.

## Gandhi's Reflection

At the time, the young Gandhi saw the wedding only in terms of "good clothes to wear, drum beating, marriage processions, rich dinners and a strange girl to play with" (103-080). He candidly admitted that "carnal desire came later." Looking back, he reflected that "two innocent children all unwittingly hurled themselves into the ocean of life" (105-081).

The experience left a lasting impression. In later years, Gandhi would become a vocal critic of child marriage and would cite his own experience as evidence of its harm. He noted that whenever he heard the Nishkulanand verse "Renunciation of objects, without the renunciation of desires, is short-lived," the memory of an incident from his wedding days would fill him with shame (104-081).
