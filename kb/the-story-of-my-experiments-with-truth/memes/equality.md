# Equality in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Equality for Gandhi was both a spiritual discipline and a social commitment. His autobiography traces the evolution of equal regard from a personal insight about marriage to a political principle that challenged racial hierarchy and caste discrimination.

## Equal Partnership in Marriage

Gandhi's earliest and most personal encounter with the idea of equality came through his relationship with his wife Kasturbai. Reflecting on his early jealousy and possessiveness, he came to a transformative insight:

> I saw then the glory of brahmacharya and realized that the wife is not the husband's bondslave, but his companion and his helpmate, and an equal partner in all his joys and sorrows — as free as the husband to choose her own path.
>
> (007-012)

This recognition of marital equality was a radical departure from the patriarchal norms of his time. It was born not from reading but from the painful experience of his own "lustful cruelty" and suspicion.

## The Unequal Duel in South Africa

Gandhi's political consciousness of inequality was forged in Durban, where he witnessed the structural inequality of colonial racism:

> Thus Durban had become the scene of an unequal duel. On one side there was a handful of poor Indians and a few of their English friends, and on the other were ranged the white men, strong in arms, in numbers, in education and in wealth. They had also the backing of the State, for the Natal Government openly helped them.
>
> (056-004)

This vivid image of the "unequal duel" captures the asymmetry that Gandhi would spend his life trying to redress — not through arms but through soul-force, which required no material equality to be effective.

## Samabhava: The Gita's Equal Vision

Gandhi found the deepest source of his egalitarianism in the Bhagavad Gita's teaching of *samabhava* — equal regard for all beings. The ideal of treating pleasure and pain, praise and blame, friend and foe with equal steadiness became the spiritual foundation for his social egalitarianism. This was not a political doctrine of equal distribution but a spiritual discipline of equal vision: to see the same God in every creature.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[non-possession]]**: Equality and non-possession are linked — to see all equally is to regard nothing as exclusively one's own.
- **[[untouchability]]**: The logical extreme of Gandhi's equality principle led him to challenge untouchability.
- **[[truth]]**: Equality before truth — the Absolute Truth is equally accessible to all, regardless of status.
