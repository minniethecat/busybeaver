# Shantiniketan

Shantiniketan (lit. "abode of peace") was the ashram and school founded by the poet [[rabindranath-tagore]] in rural Bengal. Gandhi visited Shantiniketan in early 1915, shortly after returning from South Africa and meeting with [[gopal-krishna-gokhale]] in Poona. The visit became a pivotal moment in Gandhi's Indian years, as it reunited him with his Phoenix Ashram family and planted early seeds for his experiments in communal living and manual labour.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi arrived at Shantiniketan from Rajkot, after his meeting with Gokhale in Poona. He had only learned upon landing in Bombay that the Phoenix party from South Africa had been sent to Shantiniketan, and was therefore "impatient to meet them as soon as I could" [125-004]. On the eve of his departure from Poona, a visibly ailing Gokhale — who would die within a week — insisted on holding a farewell party for Gandhi. Gokhale "came, but fainted and had to be carried away," yet upon recovering "sent word that we must go on with the party" [126-014].

The reception at Shantiniketan overwhelmed Gandhi: "The teachers and students overwhelmed me with affection. The reception was a beautiful combination of simplicity, art and love" [128-001]. It was here that Gandhi met [[kakasaheb-kalelkar]] for the first time, along with Chintaman Shastri, both of whom helped in teaching Sanskrit.

## In the Narrative

### The Phoenix Family at Shantiniketan

The Phoenix party had been assigned separate quarters at Shantiniketan under the leadership of [[maganlal-gandhi]]. Gandhi observed with satisfaction that Maganlal "had made it his business to see that all the rules of the Phoenix Ashram should be scrupulously observed," and noted that "by dint of his love, knowledge and perseverance, he had made his fragrance felt in the whole of Shantiniketan" [128-004].

### Experiments in Self-Help

Gandhi's arrival catalysed a wave of reforms. The Phoenix party's kitchen operated on the simplest principles: "Condiments were eschewed. Rice, dal, vegetables and even wheat flour were all cooked at one and the same time in a steam cooker" [128-009]. Shantiniketan students soon started a similar kitchen. More significantly, Gandhi introduced manual scavenging to the campus, an experiment that "became a busy hive" of activity [128-008].

The English teacher Pearson embraced these reforms with characteristic enthusiasm: "One would always find him with his smiling face doing something or other in or about the kitchen. He had taken upon himself the cleaning of the bigger utensils" [128-008]. Students played sitar before the cleaning party "in order to beguile the tedium of the operation."

## Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Shantiniketan was a formative experience for Gandhi's Ashram experiments. The lesson he carried forward was that "the scavenger's work would be our special function in India" [131-008]. This conviction, born during his brief week at Shantiniketan, later became a defining practice at the [[sabarmati]] Ashram and throughout his public life.

Gandhi had intended to stay at Shantiniketan for some time, "but fate willed otherwise. I had hardly been there a week when I received from Poona a telegram announcing Gokhale's death" [128-011]. The community was "immersed in grief," and a special meeting was called in the Ashram temple to mourn the national loss. Gandhi left the same day for Poona with his wife [[kasturba-gandhi]] and Maganlal, while the rest of the Phoenix party remained.

## Historical Context

Founded by [[rabindranath-tagore]]'s father Debendranath Tagore in 1863 as a meditation retreat, Shantiniketan was transformed by the younger Tagore into a unique educational institution — Visva-Bharati University — that blended Indian spiritual traditions with modern pedagogy. Gandhi's 1915 visit came at a moment of transition for both men: Gandhi was beginning his work in India, and Tagore would receive the Nobel Prize for Literature later that year. Their relationship would evolve into one of the most intellectually fertile exchanges of the Indian independence movement, marked by both deep mutual respect and sharp public disagreement.
