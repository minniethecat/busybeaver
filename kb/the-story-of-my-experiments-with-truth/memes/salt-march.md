# Salt March

The Salt March (Dandi March) of 1930 was Gandhi's most iconic act of civil disobedience. He led a 240-mile march from Sabarmati Ashram to the coastal village of Dandi, where he picked up a lump of natural salt — an act that defied the British monopoly on salt production and taxation. The march electrified India and the world, demonstrating the power of symbolic nonviolent action.

## Background

The British salt tax was a universal burden — every Indian, rich or poor, paid it. By choosing salt as the target of civil disobedience, Gandhi selected an issue that affected all Indians equally and dramatized the injustice of colonial rule. The march was launched after the British rebuffed Gandhi's offer of negotiation.

## Timeline

On March 12, 1930, Gandhi and 78 followers set out from Sabarmati Ashram. Over 24 days, they walked through Gujarat villages, growing to thousands by the time they reached Dandi on April 6. Gandhi picked up salt from the shore, and the act was replicated across India — millions made or bought illegal salt, courting arrest.

## Gandhi's Role

Gandhi conceived the march as a masterstroke of political theater — a simple, universally understood act that made the abstract injustice of colonialism concrete. He was arrested shortly after and imprisoned, but the movement continued without him.

## Historical Significance

The Salt March attracted worldwide attention and is widely credited with bringing the Indian independence movement to international consciousness. It inspired Martin Luther King Jr. and civil rights movements globally.

## Key Passages

> The Salt March was an object lesson in the power of nonviolent direct action.
