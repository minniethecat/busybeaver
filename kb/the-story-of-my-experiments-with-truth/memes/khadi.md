# Khadi

Khadi — hand-spun and hand-woven cloth — was far more than fabric in Gandhi's vision. It was the material expression of swadeshi, a tool of economic resistance against British colonialism, a source of livelihood for millions of impoverished Indians, and a spiritual discipline for those who spun it. Gandhi elevated the simple act of spinning thread into a sacrament of the independence movement.

## Definition

Khadi is cloth made from hand-spun yarn woven on a handloom. Gandhi distinguished it from mill-made cloth (which he opposed as a symbol of industrialization and exploitation) and even from hand-woven cloth made from mill-spun yarn. The crucial element was hand-spinning: the act of producing yarn oneself, which Gandhi saw as a meditative practice, a form of labor that connected the spinner to the village economy and to the poorest Indians. Every Congress member was expected to spin daily and to wear khadi.

## Origins and Influences

The idea of using homespun cloth as a political weapon emerged during the Swadeshi movement of 1905, when Indian nationalists called for a boycott of British textiles. Gandhi, returning to India in 1915, adopted and radicalized this idea. He was influenced by John Ruskin's idealization of handicraft labor and by his own experience of the poverty of Indian villages, where spinning had once been a universal source of supplementary income. The charkha (spinning wheel) became his symbol of economic liberation.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Spinning as Spiritual Discipline

Gandhi spun daily and required the same of ashram inmates and Congress workers. He saw spinning as a form of prayer — a repetitive, meditative activity that cultivated patience, concentration, and identification with the poor. The rhythm of the wheel was, for him, a physical mantra.

### Khadi and the Constructive Programme

Khadi was the centerpiece of Gandhi's constructive programme — the alternative to both British rule and Western-style industrialization. The All-India Spinners' Association organized production, set standards, and marketed khadi. By wearing khadi, Indians made a visible commitment to swadeshi and a visible break from colonial dependence.

### Political Symbolism

The bonfires of foreign cloth that marked the non-cooperation movement (1920–22) dramatized khadi's role. Wealthy Indians publicly burned their imported clothing and adopted the simple khadi loincloth and shawl. Gandhi himself reduced his clothing to a minimal khadi loincloth, identifying with the poorest Indians.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Khadi is the physical embodiment of swadeshi and the economic foundation of swaraj. It connects the personal discipline of the spinner to the political liberation of the nation. For Gandhi, every yard of khadi was an act of nonviolent resistance — a refusal to participate in the economic exploitation that British rule represented.

## Key Passages

> The spinning wheel is a symbol of nonviolence. It stands for the dignity of labour.
