# Jallianwala Bagh Massacre

The Jallianwala Bagh massacre of April 13, 1919, in which British troops under General Reginald Dyer opened fire on an unarmed gathering of Indians in a walled garden in Amritsar, was one of the darkest episodes of colonial rule in India. For Gandhi, who was barred from entering the Punjab at the time, the massacre and its aftermath — the imposition of martial law, the crawling order, the summary tribunals — represented a fundamental break in the relationship between ruler and ruled.

## Background

The massacre occurred against the backdrop of the Rowlatt Act agitation and the nationwide *hartal* of April 6, 1919. The Punjab had seen particularly intense unrest. Gandhi had been prohibited from entering the province — served with a written order at Palwal railway station on April 8 — precisely because the authorities feared his presence would intensify the agitation. Drs. Satyapal and Kitchlew, local leaders in Amritsar, had been arrested. A crowd gathered at Jallianwala Bagh on April 13, Baisakhi day, to protest the arrests and to hear speeches. General Dyer ordered his troops to fire without warning into the enclosed space, killing hundreds and wounding over a thousand.

## Timeline

### Gandhi's Prohibition from the Punjab

Gandhi had been invited to the Punjab by Drs. Satyapal and Kitchlew to help calm the situation. En route, at Mathura, Acharya Gidwani warned him of his probable arrest. At Palwal station, Gandhi was served a written order: he was prohibited from entering the Punjab boundary as his presence was "likely to result in a disturbance of the peace" (251-000). Gandhi refused to comply voluntarily: "I want to go to the Punjab in response to a pressing invitation, not to foment unrest, but to allay it" (251-000). He was removed from the train and placed under police custody.

### The Massacre and Its Aftermath

While Gandhi was being escorted back to Bombay, the massacre occurred. News reached him in fragments — telegraph wires had been cut. When he finally learned the full scale of the atrocity, he was horrified but also felt vindicated in his insistence on nonviolence: "Had not the Punjab Government prevented my entry into the Punjab, I should have been considerably helpful in keeping the peace there" (251-000).

### Gandhi's Response

Gandhi's response was characteristically complex. While he condemned the massacre absolutely, he was even more disturbed by the systemic repression that followed — the martial law regime, the crawling order, the special tribunals. He wrote: "In Amritsar innocent men and women were made to crawl like worms on their bellies. Before this outrage the Jallianwala Bagh tragedy paled into insignificance in my eyes, though it was this massacre principally that attracted the attention of the people of India and of the world" (252-000).

At the Amritsar Congress later that year, Gandhi was appointed a trustee for the Jallianwala Bagh memorial fund. The Congress resolved to build a memorial on the ground "to sanctify which, Hindus, Mussalmans and Sikhs mingled their blood" (258-000). The fundraising campaign, in which Gandhi discovered his "capacity in this direction," collected about five lakhs of rupees.

## Gandhi's Role

Gandhi was not present at the massacre itself but was intimately involved in its political and moral aftermath. He headed the memorial fundraising, served on the Congress inquiry into "Dyerism in the Punjab," and was publicly held responsible — by both the British (Sir Michael O'Dwyer) and some young Punjabis — for the tragedy. Some Punjabis even "went to the length of threatening me with assassination if I went to the Punjab," arguing that had he not suspended civil disobedience, there would have been no massacre (254-000).

## Historical Significance

Jallianwala Bagh marked a moral rupture in British-Indian relations. It transformed the Rowlatt agitation from a political campaign into a moral crisis of empire. The massacre, combined with the humiliating martial law regime, convinced many Indians — including those who had previously been loyal to the Crown — that British rule was fundamentally unjust. It was a key catalyst for the Non-cooperation Movement that would follow.
