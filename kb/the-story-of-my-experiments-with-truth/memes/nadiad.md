# Nadiad

Nadiad is a town in the [[kheda]] district of [[gujarat]] that served as [[mahatma-gandhi]]'s headquarters during two critical campaigns: the 1918 Kheda Satyagraha and the subsequent war recruitment drive. The town's Anathashram (orphanage) became the operational base for Gandhi and his coworkers.

## Headquarters of the Kheda Satyagraha

During the 1918 Kheda Satyagraha against revenue collection, Gandhi established his base at Nadiad:

> "We fixed up our headquarters at the Nadiad Anathashram, no other place being available which would have been large enough to accommodate all of us." [147-007]

The Anathashram provided space for Gandhi's growing team of volunteers who were documenting peasant grievances and organizing the no-revenue campaign across Kheda's villages.

## The End of the Campaign

After months of resistance, the Kheda Satyagraha reached a turning point:

> "The campaign came to an unexpected end. It was clear that the people were exhausted, and I hesitated to let the unbending be driven to utter ruin. I was casting about for some graceful way of terminating the struggle." [149-001]

The government issued instructions directing officers to collect revenue only from those who could pay — a compromise that partially met the peasants' demands but did not constitute full victory. Nevertheless, the campaign marked "the beginning of an awakening among the peasants of Gujarat, the beginning of their true political education" [025-007].

## The Recruitment Campaign

Later in 1918, Gandhi returned to Nadiad for a very different purpose: recruiting soldiers for the British war effort. This controversial decision stemmed from Gandhi's belief that Indians must demonstrate their capacity for self-defence before claiming the rights of citizenship:

> "The other part of my obligation consisted in raising recruits. Where could I make a beginning except in Kheda? And whom could I invite to be the first recruits except my own coworkers?" [152-003]

Gandhi walked through Kheda villages, often with great physical difficulty. On one occasion he "walked with very great difficulty to the Sabarmati station, a distance of only ten furlongs" due to illness, with [[vallabhbhai-patel]] supporting him [152-005]. They reached Nadiad at about ten o'clock, where "the Hindu Anathashram where we had our headquarters was only half a mile from the station; but it was as good as ten" given Gandhi's weakened condition [152-005].

## Role in Gandhi's Work

Nadiad represents the grassroots, village-level character of Gandhi's political method. Unlike [[delhi]] or [[calcutta]], which featured in set-piece Congress events, Nadiad was where Gandhi and his colleagues lived among the peasants, walked from village to village, and conducted the unglamorous daily work of satyagraha organization.
