# England

England was the destination of Gandhi's first voyage abroad. At the age of eighteen, he traveled to London to study law, a decision that would shape his professional career and expose him to Western society for the first time. The three years he spent there (1888–1891) were a period of personal transformation, during which he navigated the challenges of maintaining his religious vows while adapting to English life.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi's decision to go to England was prompted by a family friend, Mavji Dave, who advised that becoming a barrister was the surest path to succeeding to his father's position as Diwan. Despite his mother's concerns about the moral dangers of English society — including meat-eating, liquor, and religious compromise — Gandhi swore an oath before a Jain monk to abstain from meat, alcohol, and women while abroad (011-020). His uncle refused to directly endorse the voyage, regarding it as an irreligious act (011-016).

The separation from his wife Kasturbai during the England years saved Gandhi, by his own account, from the "disasters of lustful love" that might have consumed him in an early marriage (004-007). His time in England also made him conscious of his own shortcomings: he later regretted his neglect of handwriting, a failing that became apparent when he saw the polished script of South African-born lawyers (005-007).

## In the Narrative

Gandhi's departure for England was a dramatic break with his community. The Modh Bania caste to which he belonged declared his voyage an excommunicable offense, yet he persisted. In London, he undertook the serious study of English law, sat for the bar examination, and enrolled in the High Court. The experience of navigating a foreign culture while keeping his vows laid the foundation for the discipline he would later bring to the satyagraha movement. Upon returning to India in 1891, he found that his mother had died while he was away — news his brother had withheld to spare him grief.

## Key Passages

> At the age of eighteen I went to England, and this meant a long and healthy spell of separation. Even after my return from England, we hardly stayed together for more than six months. (004-007)

> "Would you not rather go to England than study here?" Nothing could have been more welcome to me. I was fighting shy of my difficult studies. So I jumped at the proposal. (011-004)

> "The times are changed. And none of you can expect to succeed to your father's gadi without having had a proper education. It is the wisest thing therefore to become a barrister." (011-003)
