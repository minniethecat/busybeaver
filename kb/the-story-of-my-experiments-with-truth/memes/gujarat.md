# Gujarat

Gujarat is a region in western India and the homeland of [[mahatma-gandhi]], who was born in Porbandar and later established his base at [[ahmedabad]]. As a Gujarati, Gandhi believed he could best serve India through serving Gujarat, and the region became the laboratory for his earliest Indian political experiments, including the [[kheda]] Satyagraha of 1918.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi's ties to Gujarat were both personal and political. His family belonged to the Vaishnava tradition, and Jainism — strong in Gujarat — influenced his ethical formation [001-006]. Upon returning from South Africa, Gandhi deliberately chose to settle in Gujarat. He informed [[gopal-krishna-gokhale]] of his intention to establish an Ashram "preferably somewhere in Gujarat, as, being a Gujarati, I thought I was best fitted to serve the country through serving Gujarat" [010-006].

Gandhi chose [[ahmedabad]] as his base for several reasons: as a Gujarati he could reach the people through the Gujarati language; Ahmedabad was an ancient centre of handloom weaving, favourable for reviving hand-spinning; and as the capital of Gujarat, wealthy men there were likely to contribute funds [010-014].

## Political Awakening

Gandhi served as President of the Gujarat Sabha, which sent petitions and telegrams to the Government during the Kheda crisis [020-001]. The Kheda Satyagraha of 1918 marked a watershed moment for the region:

> "The Kheda Satyagraha marks the beginning of an awakening among the peasants of Gujarat, the beginning of their true political education." [025-007]

Dr. Besant's Home Rule agitation had touched the peasants, but it was the Kheda campaign that "compelled the educated public workers to establish contact with the actual life of the peasants" [025-007]. Through this campaign, workers like [[vallabhbhai-patel]] found their vocation and capacity for sacrifice grew.

## Role in the Rowlatt Satyagraha

During the 1919 Rowlatt agitation, the Gujarat region was deeply involved. Government Commissioners held conferences on the Delhi model across divisions, including one in Gujarat where Gandhi and his coworkers were invited [155-003]. Gandhi spoke at length but felt ill at ease in "this atmosphere of servile submission" [155-003].

## Cultural Context

Gujarat's religious culture — combining Vaishnava Hinduism with strong Jain influence — shaped Gandhi's early ethical development. The Jain emphasis on non-violence (*ahimsa*) and the Vaishnava tradition of devotion were both formative influences that later crystallized into Gandhi's philosophy of [[satyagraha]].
