## Summary

Parsi Rustomji (Rustomji Jivanji Ghorkhodu) was a prominent Parsi merchant in Durban and one of Gandhi's earliest and most trusted associates in South Africa. He features extensively in the autobiography, appearing from the early days of Gandhi's work in Natal through to the establishment of the Phoenix Settlement. Their relationship encompassed professional legal counsel, political collaboration in the Natal Indian Congress, and deeply personal moral guidance, most notably when Gandhi helped Rustomji navigate a crisis involving customs smuggling.

## Role in the Natal Indian Congress

Parsi Rustomji was among the founding members of the Natal Indian Congress. When Gandhi organized the campaign to petition against the disenfranchisement of Indians in Natal, Rustomji was among the leading merchants who signed. Alongside Sheth Dawud Muhammad, Adamji Miyakhan, and other prominent traders, Rustomji helped lend the weight of the commercial class to the political movement [042-003]. Gandhi later remarked on the remarkable unity among the diverse Indian merchants: "Ten thousand signatures were obtained in the course of a fortnight" [042-010].

## Gandhi's Trusted Coworker

When Gandhi briefly returned to India in 1896, he needed someone to take charge of the Congress work and the Education Society in his absence. Rustomji was one of only two men Gandhi could think of for the task, alongside Adamji Miyakhan. Gandhi described him as foremost "among those who could fulfil the duties of the secretary by regular work" [049-002].

After Gandhi's return to Durban in early 1897, when a hostile white mob threatened his life at the docks, it was to Rustomji's house that Gandhi's wife and children were safely driven. "Mr. Rustomji's house was about two miles from the dock," and Gandhi rejoined his family there after a tense confrontation at the police station [057-001] [057-003] [057-010].

## Charitable Work

Rustomji's financial support enabled significant humanitarian initiatives. With his charitable contributions, Gandhi was able to establish a small charitable hospital under Dr. Joseph Booth's charge at St. Aidan's Mission, which treated patients free of charge [060-002]. Gandhi noted, "The late Mr. Rustomji always supported me in such enterprises," including the construction of the Phoenix Settlement, to which Rustomji donated secondhand corrugated iron sheets and other building materials [096-006].

## The Customs Smuggling Episode

The most vivid narrative involving Parsi Rustomji concerns a moral crisis around customs smuggling. Rustomji had been smuggling goods through Durban customs for years. When his deception was discovered, he came to Gandhi in tears: "Bhai, I have deceived you. My guilt has been discovered today. I have smuggled and I have lied to the Customs Officer" [124-003].

Gandhi advised Rustomji to make a full confession to the Customs Officer rather than attempt a legal defense. Rustomji's regular counsel was skeptical, but Rustomji insisted: "I should like to be guided by Mr. Gandhi's advice in this case. He knows me intimately" [124-008] [124-010].

Gandhi personally met with the Customs Officer, apprised him of the whole affair, and told him "how penitent Parsi Rustomji was feeling" [124-015]. The case was compromised: Rustomji paid a penalty equal to twice the amount he had confessed to smuggling. As a permanent reminder to himself, Rustomji "reduced to writing the facts of the whole case, got the paper framed and hung it up in his office to serve as a perpetual reminder to his heirs and fellow merchants" [124-020].

## Material Renunciation

Another significant episode involved a gift of valuable items — gold, silver, and diamond ornaments — that the Indian community had presented to Gandhi and his wife. Gandhi, feeling he could not keep these things, drafted a letter creating a trust in favor of the community and appointed Parsi Rustomji among the trustees [066-011].

## Legacy

Rustomji's relationship with Gandhi demonstrates the deep trust that developed between the young lawyer and the established Indian merchants of Durban. When warned by other friends that Rustomji's charitable contributions might be temporary, Rustomji himself responded: "What would be my fate if I deceived you?" [124-021]. He appears in the autobiography as both a benefactor and a beneficiary of Gandhi's moral guidance, embodying the complex interplay between commerce, community leadership, and personal ethics.
