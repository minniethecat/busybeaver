# Punjab

The Punjab occupies a dramatic and painful chapter in Gandhi's autobiography. In 1919, as the Rowlatt Act agitation swept India, the province became the epicenter of imperial repression. Gandhi was barred from entering the Punjab, martial law was proclaimed, and the Jallianwala Bagh massacre at Amritsar shocked the nation — all of which pushed Gandhi toward the Non-Cooperation Movement.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi had never visited the Punjab before 1919, but its events transformed his political trajectory. En route to the province in response to an invitation from local leaders, he was served with a written order at Palwal railway station prohibiting his entry, on the grounds that his presence was "likely to result in a disturbance of the peace" (155-010). He was taken off the train, placed under police custody, and forcibly returned to Bombay.

Sir Michael O'Dwyer, the Lieutenant-Governor of the Punjab, held Gandhi responsible for the unrest. Meanwhile, some young Punjabis blamed Gandhi for the martial law itself, arguing that had he not suspended civil disobedience, the Jallianwala Bagh massacre might have been averted. Some even threatened him with assassination if he entered the Punjab (159-001).

## In the Narrative

The Punjab crisis unfolds across several chapters. Gandhi describes the "lawless repression" under martial law, the special tribunals that functioned as "instruments for carrying out the arbitrary will of an autocrat," and the suppression of the *Bombay Chronicle* after its editor, Mr. Horniman, was deported (158-001). It was in this atmosphere that Gandhi took up the editorship of *Young India*, converting it into a biweekly organ to expound the meaning of satyagraha to the public (158-006).

## Key Passages

> I was prohibited from entering the boundary of the Punjab, as my presence there was likely to result in a disturbance of the peace. I refused to get down from the train saying, "I want to go to the Punjab in response to a pressing invitation, not to foment unrest, but to allay it." (155-010)

> Government's policy of lawless repression was in full career, and was manifesting itself in the Punjab in all its nakedness. Leaders were put under arrest, martial law, which in other words meant no law, was proclaimed. (158-001)

> Had not the Punjab Government prevented my entry into the Punjab, I should have been considerably helpful in keeping the peace there. By preventing me they gave the people unnecessary provocation. (156-011)
