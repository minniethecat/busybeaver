# Religious Tolerance in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Religious tolerance for Gandhi was not an abstract liberal principle but a lived inheritance. His autobiography traces its roots to his father's household, where Jain monks, Muslim scholars, and Parsi friends were all received with equal respect.

## An Early Grounding in Toleration

Gandhi's most formative education in tolerance came not from books but from his father's example:

> In Rajkot, however, I got an early grounding in toleration for all branches of Hinduism and sister religions. For my father and mother would visit the Haveli as also Shiva's and Rama's temples, and would take or send us youngsters there. Jain monks also would pay frequent visits to my father, and would even go out of their way to accept food from us — non-Jains. They would have talks with my father on subjects religious and mundane.
>
> (010-005)

This was not an abstract teaching but a daily practice of hospitality across religious boundaries.

## Listening Across Faiths

What distinguished his father's tolerance was that it was active, not passive. Karamchand Gandhi did not merely coexist with followers of other faiths — he listened to them:

> He had, besides, Mussalman and Parsi friends, who would talk to him about their own faiths, and he would listen to them always with respect, and often with interest. Being his nurse, I often had a chance to be present at these talks. These many things combined to inculcate in me a toleration for all faiths.
>
> (010-006)

The child Gandhi absorbed this as a nurse at his father's bedside — watching a man who listened to other faiths "always with respect, and often with interest."

## Tolerance as a Political Principle

In Gandhi's mature politics, religious tolerance became inseparable from satyagraha itself. As he wrote to Gokhale about his insistence on his own principles:

> Gokhale believed that, in spite of my insistence on my own principles, I was equally ready and able to tolerate theirs.
>
> (126-009)

This captures the difficult balance Gandhi sought: firmness in one's own convictions combined with genuine tolerance for the convictions of others. The two were not contradictory but complementary — tolerance required the strength of conviction, not its absence.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[faith]]**: Tolerance made Gandhi's faith capacious rather than exclusive — he could learn from every tradition without abandoning his own.
- **[[truth]]**: If Truth is one but its manifestations are many, then tolerance is the natural response to religious diversity.
- **[[ahimsa]]**: Religious intolerance is a form of violence; tolerance is its nonviolent antidote.
