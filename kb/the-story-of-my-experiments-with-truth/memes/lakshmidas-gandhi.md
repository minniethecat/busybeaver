# Lakshmidas Gandhi

Lakshmidas (also spelled Laxmidas) was Gandhi's elder brother, one of three brothers born to [[karamchand-gandhi]] and [[putlibai]]. His life intersects with the autobiography at several key moments — as a fellow victim of the "reform" friend's influence, as a source of financial pressure during Gandhi's London years, and as the disappointed recipient of Gandhi's letter renouncing all future financial support for the family.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Lakshmidas was the eldest of the three brothers who survived to adulthood. He was already married when the family decided to marry Gandhi, his second brother Karsandas, and a cousin simultaneously:

> The elders decided to marry my second brother, who was two or three years my senior, a cousin, possibly a year older, and me, all at the same time.
>
> (003-006)

The triple wedding was an exercise in economy and convenience — the brothers' wishes were not consulted.

## Biography

### Early Life

Lakshmidas and his brothers grew up in the household of Kaba Gandhi in Rajkot. Like Gandhi, he was educated at the local high school, but marriage disrupted his studies more severely than Gandhi's:

> Marriage resulted in both of us wasting a year. Indeed the result was even worse for my brother, for he gave up studies altogether.
>
> (003-016)

### Role in the Narrative

**The "reform" friend**: Lakshmidas was the first to fall under the influence of the friend who would later lead Gandhi toward meat-eating. Gandhi notes that his "elder brother had already fallen" (005-019), meaning that Lakshmidas had already accepted the friend's arguments about the necessity of meat-eating for strength. The brother "supported my friend's argument" in attempting to persuade Gandhi (005-019).

**The gold armlet theft**: One of the most shameful episodes in Gandhi's autobiography involves stealing gold from Lakshmidas:

> I stole a bit of gold out of my meat-eating brother's armlet. This brother had run into a debt of about twenty-five rupees. He had on his arm an armlet of solid gold. It was not difficult to clip a bit out of it.
>
> (008-003)

Gandhi confessed this theft to his father in the famous letter that moved Kaba Gandhi to tears of forgiveness — a transformative moment in Gandhi's understanding of [[truthfulness]] and confession. But the theft itself reveals something about Lakshmidas: he had run into debt, a pattern that would continue.

**Financial pressures**: During Gandhi's London years, Lakshmidas was the brother who "nobly responded to my regular calls for monetary help" (015-005). The awareness of his brother's financial struggle was one of the factors driving Gandhi to simplify his own life. Later, when Gandhi decided to renounce personal wealth and devote all future earnings to the community, it was Lakshmidas who received the painful letter:

> I could not easily make my brother understand this. In stern language he explained to me my duty towards him. I should not, he said, aspire to be wiser than our father. I must support the family as he did.
>
> (072-004)

Gandhi's response — that he was "doing exactly what our father had done" by simply widening the meaning of "family" to include the community — must have seemed cold comfort to a brother who had supported him through law school.

## Historical Significance

Lakshmidas Gandhi represents the personal cost of Gandhi's radical ethics. For every spiritual breakthrough Gandhi achieved, there was often a family member who bore the collateral damage. Lakshmidas is not a villain in the autobiography — he is a loyal brother who cannot understand why his younger sibling has abandoned his traditional duties. The tension between Gandhi's widening circle of obligation and his narrowing circle of personal attachment is one of the autobiography's deepest unresolved conflicts.
