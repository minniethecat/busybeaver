# Balasundaram

Balasundaram was a Tamil indentured labourer in Natal whose appearance at Gandhi's door — bleeding, trembling, and with two broken teeth — became one of the turning points of Gandhi's South African years. In serving Balasundaram, Gandhi discovered his vocation: not the practice of law but the service of the poorest and most vulnerable members of the Indian community.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Balasundaram was not a friend, colleague, or political associate of Gandhi. He was a client in the deepest sense — a person in desperate need who came to Gandhi seeking justice. Gandhi was then a young barrister, barely three or four months into his practice, and the Natal Indian Congress was still in its infancy. Balasundaram's case was the first that brought Gandhi into direct contact with the indentured labourers who formed the poorest stratum of the Indian community in South Africa:

> I had put in scarcely three or four months' practice, and the Congress also was still in its infancy, when a Tamil man in tattered clothes, headgear in hand, two front teeth broken and his mouth bleeding, stood before me trembling and weeping. He had been heavily belaboured by his master.
>
> (038-008)

## Biography

Nothing is known of Balasundaram's life before or after this episode. The autobiography preserves only this single moment of crisis. He was serving his indenture under a well-known European resident of Durban. His master, in a fit of anger, had lost self-control and beaten him severely, breaking two of his teeth (038-008).

## Role in the Narrative

Balasundaram's significance in the autobiography is that he crystallized Gandhi's commitment to serving the poor. Gandhi took practical action — sending him to a doctor, securing a medical certificate, and taking the case to the magistrate:

> The magistrate was indignant when he read it, and issued a summons against the employer.
>
> (038-009)

But Gandhi's goal was not punishment but liberation. He studied the law of indentured labour and discovered its quasi-criminal character — unlike ordinary servants, indentured labourers could be imprisoned for leaving their masters:

> It was far from my desire to get the employer punished. I simply wanted Balasundaram to be released from him. I read the law about indentured labour... That is why Sir William Hunter called the indenture system almost as bad as slavery. Like the slave the indentured labourer was the property of his master.
>
> (038-009)

Gandhi succeeded in having Balasundaram transferred to another employer. The episode was a practical education in the structural injustice of the indenture system and a personal revelation of the power of legal advocacy when placed in the service of the powerless.

## Historical Significance

Balasundaram himself left no mark on history beyond his single appearance in Gandhi's autobiography. But in that appearance, he represents the millions of indentured Indian labourers whose suffering galvanized Gandhi's political consciousness. The chapter titled "Balasundaram" (038) marks the moment when Gandhi's abstract sympathy for the poor became concrete — when "service of the poor" ceased to be an ideal and became a face, a name, and a legal case.

Gandhi would later write:

> Service of the poor has been my heart's desire, and it has always thrown me amongst the poor and enabled me to identify myself with them.
>
> (038-014)

Balasundaram was the first of those poor — the first to throw Gandhi into the work that would define his life.
