# Satyagraha

Satyagraha (literally "holding firmly to truth") is Gandhi's doctrine of nonviolent resistance, developed in South Africa and applied throughout India's independence movement. Gandhi described it as "an absolutely nonviolent weapon" (127-017) and distinguished it sharply from passive resistance, emphasizing its active moral force rooted in the pursuit of truth.

## Definition

For Gandhi, satyagraha was not merely a political tactic but a spiritual discipline. He coined the term to distinguish his method from "passive resistance," which he saw as a weapon of the weak that could include violence as a last resort. Satyagraha, by contrast, excluded violence absolutely and required the practitioner to actively seek truth rather than merely endure suffering (127-017). The term combines *satya* (truth) and *agraha* (insistence or holding firmly), reflecting Gandhi's conviction that truth and nonviolence were inseparable.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi developed satyagraha not from any preconceived plan but organically through his experiences. He later wrote that "Satyagraha had not been a preconceived plan. It came on spontaneously, without my having willed it" (062-002). The concept crystallized in South Africa in 1906, during the struggle against discriminatory laws targeting Indians in the Transvaal. Gandhi's reading of Tolstoy, Ruskin, and the Bhagavad Gita, combined with his personal experiments with truth and self-control, created the intellectual soil from which satyagraha grew (061-005).

## Gandhi's Practice

### First Experiments

The first satyagraha campaign began in South Africa in 1906, when the Transvaal government proposed legislation requiring Indians to register and carry passes. Gandhi organized a mass meeting at the Empire Theatre in Johannesburg, where Indians pledged to defy the law nonviolently. This marked the birth of organized satyagraha. A detailed account of these early campaigns appears in Gandhi's *History of Satyagraha in South Africa* (038-001).

### Evolution

Through his work as editor of *Indian Opinion*, Gandhi systematically expounded and refined the principles of satyagraha. "Week after week I poured out my soul in its columns, and expounded the principles and practice of satyagraha as I understood it" (090-004). The journal served as both a laboratory for his ideas and a means of educating the Indian community in the discipline of nonviolent action.

Upon returning to India in 1915, Gandhi founded the Satyagraha Ashram in Ahmedabad on May 25, 1915 (133-002). After consulting with friends about the name, he chose "Satyagraha Ashram" because it conveyed both the goal (truth) and the method (nonviolent insistence), rejecting alternatives like "Sevashram" (abode of service) and "Tapovan" (abode of austerities) as less precise (133-008).

### Key Applications

**Champaran (1917)**: Gandhi's first major satyagraha in India, addressing the exploitation of indigo farmers in Bihar. He described it as "a bold experiment with Truth and Ahimsa" (140-001).

**Kheda (1918)**: When crops failed in the Kheda district of Gujarat, Gandhi led peasants in a campaign of nonpayment of land revenue. "The Kheda Satyagraha marks the beginning of an awakening among the peasants of Gujarat, the beginning of their true political education" (149-004). Gandhi plunged into this struggle immediately after the Ahmedabad mill-hands' strike (147-001).

**Rowlatt Bills (1919)**: In response to repressive wartime legislation, Gandhi established the Satyagraha Sabha in Bombay to coordinate nationwide nonviolent resistance. "A separate body called the Satyagraha Sabha was established at my instance" (153-014), and thousands signed the satyagraha pledge.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Satyagraha occupies the central position in Gandhi's ethical and political thought, connecting his metaphysical commitment to Truth with his practical method of nonviolence (Ahimsa). For Gandhi, truth was God, and satyagraha was the only means worthy of a seeker after truth. It is the active, applied dimension of his philosophy — the bridge between personal spiritual discipline (Brahmacharya, fasting, dietary restraint) and collective political action (civil disobedience, non-cooperation, Swaraj). Without satyagraha, Gandhi's other concepts remain abstract; with it, they become a coherent program for individual and social transformation.
