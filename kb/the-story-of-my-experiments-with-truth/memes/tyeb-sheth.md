## Summary

Tyeb Sheth (Tyeb Haji Khan Muhammad) was a leading Indian merchant in Pretoria. As a near relative of Abdulla Sheth (Gandhi's client in the Dada Abdulla vs. Tyeb Sheth case), Tyeb was the opposing party in the landmark legal dispute that first brought Gandhi to South Africa in 1893. His presence in the autobiography illustrates the close-knit nature of the Indian merchant community in the Transvaal and the role of arbitration in resolving disputes within it.

## The Dada Abdulla Case

Tyeb Sheth was the defendant in the commercial dispute that prompted Dada Abdulla to engage Gandhi as his legal representative in South Africa [033-009]. When Gandhi arrived in Durban, he was housed by Abdulla Sheth. Learning of the close family ties between the litigants — Tyeb Sheth being a near relative of Abdulla — Gandhi immediately saw the potential for settlement [033-011].

Gandhi proposed an out-of-court settlement to Abdulla, suggesting he could approach Tyeb Sheth about arbitration. Abdulla was skeptical: "Tyeb Sheth is not a man to consent to a settlement easily. With the slightest nudge he flares up" [033-011]. Nevertheless, Gandhi confidently offered to speak with Tyeb Sheth directly [033-012].

## Position in Pretoria

Sheth Tyeb Haji Khan Muhammad held a position in Pretoria comparable to Dada Abdulla's in Natal. As Gandhi described: "There was no public movement that could be conducted without him" [037-002]. Tyeb Sheth provided Gandhi with papers detailing how Indians were cruelly driven out from the Orange Free State, which Gandhi used to understand the broader plight of Indians in South Africa [037-012].

## The Arbitration

Gandhi eventually approached Tyeb Sheth with his proposal for arbitration, advising him to see his counsel. "I recommended him to go to arbitration" [039-008]. The arbitration was successful — the award came down in Dada Abdulla's favor. However, Gandhi faced a new dilemma: immediate execution of the award would have been impossible for Tyeb Sheth to meet in full, and there was "an unwritten law among the Indian merchants that no one should go to court for recovery of a debt" [039-009].

Gandhi arranged for the payment to be made in installments, preserving both the legal victory and the community relationship. This outcome foreshadowed Gandhi's lifelong commitment to resolving disputes through reconciliation rather than adversarial litigation.

## Defender of Gandhi's Role

When a colonial official in Ceylon challenged Gandhi's right to represent the Indian community, it was Tyeb Sheth who defended Gandhi's standing: "He is our adviser, and he has come here at our request" [079-006]. When the official further questioned why the community needed Gandhi when they had the official himself, Tyeb Sheth replied pointedly: "Of course you are there. But Gandhi is our man. He knows our language and understands us" [079-008].

Tyeb Sheth also voiced the community's solidarity with Gandhi when he was insulted: "Does not an insult to you amount to an insult to the community? How can we forget that you are our representative?" [080-005]. When Gandhi urged restraint, Tyeb Sheth argued: "Come what may, why should we swallow a fresh insult? Nothing worse can possibly happen to us. Have we many rights to lose?" [080-007].

## Legacy

Tyeb Sheth represents the generation of Indian merchants in South Africa who became Gandhi's clients, supporters, and collaborators. Though their relationship began in commercial litigation, Tyeb Sheth became one of Gandhi's staunchest defenders, demonstrating how the young lawyer's integrity and commitment to the community transcended the adversarial roles of his early legal practice.
