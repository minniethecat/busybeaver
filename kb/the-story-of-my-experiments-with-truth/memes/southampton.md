# Southampton

Southampton is a historic port city on the south coast of England that served as the gateway to Britain for [[mahatma-gandhi]] on two significant occasions: his arrival as a young law student in 1888 and his passage through the English Channel in 1914 at the outbreak of the First World War.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi first arrived at Southampton in late September 1888, a nineteen-year-old student embarking on his legal studies in London. The experience was marked by cultural disorientation and a memorable lesson in European etiquette. A quarter-century later, in 1914, Southampton was again his point of entry to England — this time under the shadow of war, as his ship navigated through submarine mines laid throughout the English Channel.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

Gandhi's first arrival at Southampton in 1888 was a study in cultural awkwardness. He had chosen to wear white flannel clothes for his landing, believing they would suit him better, but discovered he was "the only person wearing such clothes" in the late September weather [013-007]. He left all his luggage, including his keys, with an agent of Grindlay and Company, following the example of other passengers — a decision born of inexperience that left a "bitter taste of poverty" [013-007].

That same evening, Dr. Pranjivan Mehta, to whom Gandhi had wired from Southampton, called upon him. During their conversation, Gandhi casually picked up Dr. Mehta's top-hat and "passed my hand over it the wrong way and disturbed the fur," prompting an angry look from Dr. Mehta [013-009]. This became, as Gandhi ruefully noted, his "first lesson in European etiquette" [013-009].

In 1914, the return journey was far more perilous. The ship received news of the outbreak of the Great War while entering the English Channel and "it took about two days to reach Southampton" as the vessel was carefully towed through submarine mines [114-014].

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Southampton bookends Gandhi's English experience: the naive student of 1888 arriving in white flannels and the seasoned leader of 1914 navigating a world at war. The gap between these two arrivals measures the transformation of a shy, uncertain young man into a figure of moral and political consequence.

>
