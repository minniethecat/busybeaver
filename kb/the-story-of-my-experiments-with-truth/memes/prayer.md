# Prayer

Prayer was Gandhi's most intimate spiritual practice — the daily discipline through which he sought communion with Truth and drew strength for public service. He described prayer as "the key of the morning and the bolt of the evening" and insisted that it was not a petition for favors but a discipline of self-purification. For Gandhi, Ramanama — the repetition of the name of Rama — was both a specifically Hindu practice and a universal spiritual tool, accessible to anyone regardless of religious affiliation.

## Definition

Gandhi defined prayer as a heartfelt reaching toward the divine — or, for the non-theist, toward the highest ideal of Truth. It was not a mechanical ritual or a petition for material benefits. "Prayer has saved my life," he wrote. "Without it I should have been a lunatic long ago." He distinguished between vocal prayer (communal or private recitation) and the inner prayer of the heart, which could be continuous throughout the day. Prayer was for him inseparable from self-examination and moral inventory — a daily audit of one's conduct against the standard of Truth.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's prayer practice was shaped by the Vaishnava devotionalism of his mother, who regularly visited the temple and observed religious fasts. Ramanama — the repetition of Rama's name — was taught to him in childhood as a remedy for fear, and he returned to it throughout his life, particularly in moments of crisis. He was also influenced by the multi-religious prayer meetings at his ashrams, which included readings from Hindu, Muslim, Christian, Buddhist, and other scriptures. His approach to prayer was deliberately inclusive: he wanted it to be accessible to atheists and theists alike as a form of moral self-examination.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Daily Discipline

Gandhi's day began and ended with prayer. The ashram prayer meetings, held at dawn and dusk, were central to community life. They included recitations from multiple scriptures, the singing of bhajans (devotional songs), and periods of silence. Gandhi composed some of the ashram prayers himself, and the spinning wheel often accompanied prayer as a form of active meditation.

### Prayer in Crisis

In moments of extreme trial — during fasts, in prison, amid communal violence — Gandhi intensified his prayer practice. His last fast, undertaken in January 1948 for communal harmony in Delhi, was sustained by continuous Ramanama. He described the repetition of Rama's name as his "staff of life" and credited it with giving him the strength to endure suffering without hatred.

### Universalizing Prayer

Gandhi worked to make prayer accessible across religious boundaries. He rejected the idea that prayer required belief in a personal God, arguing that the earnest striving toward an ideal was itself prayer. This universalized understanding allowed him to present prayer as a tool for all — including rationalists and atheists — who sought moral clarity and inner strength.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Prayer is the inward dimension of Gandhi's outward activism. If satyagraha is truth-force applied to social conflict, prayer is truth-force applied to the self. It is the daily practice that sustains the satyagrahi, providing the inner resources needed for nonviolent resistance. Without prayer, Gandhi believed, the activist would burn out or become corrupted by power.
