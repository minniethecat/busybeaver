# Sheth Abdul Karim Jhaveri

Sheth Abdul Karim Jhaveri was a partner in the firm of Dada Abdulla and Company, the Porbandar-based trading firm that engaged the young barrister M. K. Gandhi for legal work in South Africa in 1893. It was through his introduction — arranged by Gandhi's brother — that Gandhi embarked on the journey that would transform him from an obscure lawyer into a political leader.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Abdul Karim Jhaveri was Gandhi's first professional client and the gateway to his South African career. Gandhi's brother Lakshmidas introduced the twenty-three-year-old barrister to the Sheth, who assured Gandhi the work would not be difficult and promised introductions to "big Europeans" and assistance with English correspondence. The relationship, mediated through the firm, gave Gandhi his initial foothold in Natal's Indian community.

## Biography

### Early Life

Abdul Karim Jhaveri was a Memon merchant from Porbandar, part of the trading diaspora that connected the Kathiawar ports of western India with the Indian Ocean world. As a partner in Dada Abdulla and Co., he was a significant figure in the commercial network that linked Porbandar, Durban, and Natal's Indian merchant community.

### Role in the Narrative

When Gandhi found himself unable to establish a legal practice in India and was seeking opportunities abroad, his brother Lakshmidas arranged the introduction: "My brother introduced me to the late Sheth Abdul Karim Jhaveri, a partner of Dada Abdulla and Co." [030-011]. The Sheth offered Gandhi a year's engagement in South Africa to assist with a commercial lawsuit involving £40,000 — a substantial sum.

The Sheth's assurances were modest and practical: "It won't be a difficult job," he told Gandhi. "We have big Europeans as our friends, whose acquaintance you will make. You can be useful to us in our shop. Much of our correspondence is in English and you can help us with that too. You will, of course, be our guest and hence will have no expense whatever" [030-011].

## Historical Significance

In the chain of historical causation, Abdul Karim Jhaveri occupies a small but pivotal place. The legal engagement that seemed routine to him — a commercial dispute between Indian traders — brought Gandhi to South Africa, where the experience of racial discrimination, the challenge of organizing a diaspora community, and two decades of experimentation would forge the philosophy and technique of satyagraha. The Sheth could not have foreseen that his young barrister would return to India as a Mahatma.
