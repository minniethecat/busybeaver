# Ahmedabad Mill Strike

The Ahmedabad Mill Strike of 1918 was Gandhi's first labor intervention in India. When textile workers demanded a bonus to offset wartime inflation and mill owners refused, Gandhi mediated — and when the owners locked out the workers, he undertook his first public fast in India to pressure both sides toward a just settlement.

## Background

Ahmedabad's textile industry was booming from wartime demand, but workers' wages had not kept pace with inflation. Gandhi, with his ashram located nearby, was drawn into the dispute. He urged workers to demand a 35% bonus increase and to remain nonviolent.

## Gandhi's Role

When negotiations stalled, Gandhi undertook a fast — not against the mill owners but as a moral appeal to both sides. The fast broke the deadlock: the owners agreed to arbitration, and the workers received a substantial portion of their demand. This was the first time Gandhi used fasting as a public moral tool.

## Historical Significance

The Ahmedabad strike demonstrated the applicability of satyagraha to labor disputes and established fasting as a tool of moral pressure. It also led to the formation of the Textile Labour Association, one of India's oldest unions.

## Key Passages

> It dawned upon me that fasting could be made as powerful a weapon of indulgence as of restraint. (104-005)
