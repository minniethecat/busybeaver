# Rowlatt Act

The Rowlatt Act (also known as the Anarchical and Revolutionary Crimes Act, 1919) was a repressive colonial law that extended the wartime Defence of India Act's emergency powers into peacetime. It authorized the British government to imprison suspected revolutionaries without trial and to try political cases without juries. For Gandhi, the Act was the catalyst that transformed him from a loyal ally of the Empire into a leader of mass civil disobedience — "the idea of leading a campaign against the Rowlatt Act had possessed me" (143-000).

## Background

The Rowlatt Committee was appointed in 1917 under Justice Sidney Rowlatt to investigate revolutionary conspiracies in India. Its report recommended continuing wartime emergency powers indefinitely. Gandhi, still recovering from severe dysentery at the time, read the committee's recommendations in the papers while convalescing and was "startled" by them (143-000). He wrote: "The recommendations of the Rowlatt Committee seemed to me to be altogether unwarranted by the evidence published in its report, and were, I felt, such that no self-respecting people could submit to them" (143-000).

## Timeline

### Prelude: The Committee Report

Gandhi had hardly begun to recover from illness when he read the Rowlatt Committee's published report. Alarmed, he discussed it with Shankarlal Banker, Umar Sobani, and Vallabhbhai Patel, who urged him to take action. Gandhi proposed that if even a handful of men would sign a pledge of resistance, they should offer satyagraha the moment the Bill passed: "If I was not laid up like this, I should give battle against it all alone" (143-000).

### The Satyagraha Pledge

A small meeting — barely twenty people — was called at the Sabarmati Ashram. Among those present were Vallabhbhai Patel, Sarojini Naidu, Mr. Horniman, Umar Sobani, Shankarlal Banker, and Anasuyabehn. The satyagraha pledge was drafted and signed by all present (143-000). To coordinate the campaign, Gandhi established the Satyagraha Sabha with its headquarters in Bombay. "Intending covenanters began to sign the satyagraha pledge in large numbers, bulletins were issued, and popular meetings began to be held everywhere" (143-000).

### The Bill Becomes Law

Despite widespread agitation and even a rare parliamentary speech by Shastriji — which Gandhi described as so moving that "the Viceroy seemed to be listening spellbound, his eyes riveted on Shastriji" — the Government was "anxious only to go through the farce of legal formality" (144-000). The Bill passed and was published as an Act.

### The Dream and the Hartal

That night, Gandhi fell asleep wrestling with how to oppose the Act. "Towards the small hours of the morning I woke up somewhat earlier than usual. I was still in that twilight condition between sleep and consciousness when suddenly the idea broke upon me — it was as if in a dream" (144-000). The idea was a nationwide *hartal* — a day of fasting, prayer, and suspension of business as an act of self-purification. Gandhi immediately shared it with Rajagopalachari, who was "at once taken up with" the suggestion (144-000). The hartal was set for April 6, 1919.

## Gandhi's Role

Gandhi's role was not as a conventional political organizer but as a moral leader who transformed a legislative grievance into a spiritual call to action. He framed the response to the Rowlatt Act as an act of self-purification, not merely protest: "Satyagraha is a process of self-purification, and ours is a sacred fight" (144-000). The Rowlatt agitation was also the moment when Gandhi first discovered the potential of nationwide mass mobilization — a capacity that would define the next three decades of India's freedom movement.

## Historical Significance

The Rowlatt Act galvanized the first nationwide satyagraha in India. The April 6 hartal was observed with remarkable unity across Bombay, Madras, Bihar, and Sind. However, the colonial government's response — culminating in the Jallianwala Bagh massacre — pushed the confrontation to a tragic extreme and permanently altered the character of the Indian independence movement. The Act itself was never repealed during the colonial period, but the agitation it sparked made Gandhi the undisputed leader of Indian nationalism.

## Key Passages

> The idea of leading a campaign against the Rowlatt Act had possessed me. And with it grew the desire to live. (143-000)
