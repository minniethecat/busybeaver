# Truthfulness in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

For Gandhi, truthfulness in word was not a separate virtue but an aspect of the sovereign principle of Truth — the discipline of aligning one's speech absolutely with reality. From his childhood fascination with the story of Harishchandra to his mature understanding that "Truth is God," the practice of verbal honesty was the foundation on which all his other experiments rested.

## Gandhi's Definition

In the introduction to his autobiography, Gandhi distinguishes between truthfulness in word and the larger Truth that is God:

> This truth is not only truthfulness in word, but truthfulness in thought also, and not only the relative truth of our conception, but the Absolute Truth, the Eternal Principle, that is God.
>
> (001-003)

Truthfulness in word is thus the first rung on a ladder that ascends toward the Absolute. Without verbal honesty, Gandhi believed, no progress toward the larger Truth was possible — "even my Himalayan blunders have seemed trifling to me because I have kept strictly to this path" (001-003).

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi traced his passion for truthfulness to a single childhood experience: watching a play about the legendary King Harishchandra, who sacrificed everything — kingdom, family, freedom — rather than tell a single lie:

> This play — *Harishchandra* — captured my heart. I could never be tired of seeing it... "Why should not all be truthful like Harishchandra?" was the question I asked myself day and night. To follow truth and to go through all the ordeals Harishchandra went through was the one ideal it inspired in me.
>
> (002-015)

This story "haunted" Gandhi throughout his life. Harishchandra and Shravana became "living realities" for him — not historical figures but enduring moral exemplars whose example shaped his entire ethical framework.

His father Kaba Gandhi also modeled truthfulness. Described as "truthful, brave and generous," his father's refusal to apologize for speaking truth to power — even when detained — provided an early model of truthful courage (002-003).

## Gandhi's Practice

### Childhood Experiments

Gandhi's earliest experiments with truthfulness were not always successful. As a schoolboy, he lied to avoid punishment for a minor infraction:

> I had not done my homework. The teacher was on the round of inspection, and when he came to me, I had to pretend... The teacher caught me and I had to confess. The shame of it stayed with me for years.
>
> (002-007)

These early failures were formative. Each lapse in truthfulness taught Gandhi the cost of dishonesty — not external punishment but the internal "shame" that corroded self-respect.

### The Meat-Eating Deception

One of the most painful episodes in Gandhi's autobiography involves the systematic lies he told his mother to conceal his experiment with meat-eating:

> Whenever I had occasion to indulge in these surreptitious feasts, dinner at home was out of the question. My mother would naturally ask me to come and take my food and want to know the reason why I did not wish to eat. I would say to her, "I have no appetite today; there is something wrong with my digestion." It was not without compunction that I devised these pretexts. I knew I was lying, and lying to my mother.
>
> (006-010)

The "compunction" — the gnawing of conscience — was, for Gandhi, evidence that truthfulness was not an external rule but an internal demand. Lying to his mother was worse than lying to a stranger because it violated a relationship of trust.

### Truthfulness as Harmonization

When Gandhi simplified his life in London, he experienced truthfulness not as constraint but as liberation:

> The change harmonized my inward and outward life. It was also more in keeping with the means of my family. My life was certainly more truthful and my soul knew no bounds of joy.
>
> (015-007)

This passage is significant: truthfulness for Gandhi was not merely about avoiding false statements. It was about bringing one's entire life into alignment — making the outward life match the inward conviction. A person living beyond their means, he believed, was living untruthfully.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Truthfulness is the practical foundation of Gandhi's entire ethical system:

- **[[truth]]**: Truthfulness in word is the entry point to the larger pursuit of Absolute Truth. Gandhi held that one could not approach God — who is Truth — while speaking falsehoods.
- **[[vow]]**: The formal vow was the instrument for stabilizing truthfulness — by publicly committing to truthful speech, the practitioner makes it harder to retreat.
- **[[nonviolence]]**: Truthfulness and nonviolence are inseparable — violence often begins with a lie, and lying is itself a form of violence against the hearer.
- **[[simplicity]]**: A simple life is a more truthful life because it requires fewer pretenses and deceptions.
- **[[brahmacharya]]**: The discipline of self-control includes control of speech — the tongue must be as restrained as the other senses.
