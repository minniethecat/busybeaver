## Overview

Morality is, for Gandhi, the foundation of all things and the essence of religion itself. In the Introduction to his autobiography, he declares: "the essence of religion is morality" [P05-003]. This conviction — that ethical conduct is not merely a byproduct of religious belief but its very core — animates the entire narrative. Morality for Gandhi is not abstract principle but lived practice, tested and refined through his "experiments with truth."

## Morality as the Basis of Things

As a schoolboy, Gandhi formed a conviction that "took deep root in me — the conviction that morality is the basis of things, and that truth is the substance of all morality" [010-013]. This early insight, developed while reading didactic Gujarati poetry, became the philosophical anchor of his life. It separated Gandhi from those who treated morality as a domain separate from politics, business, or daily conduct.

This conviction was reinforced by his encounter with Tolstoy's *The Kingdom of God Is Within You*, which "overwhelmed" him with its "independent thinking, profound morality, and the truthfulness" [040-012]. Tolstoy's insistence that Christian ethics demanded nonviolence and resistance to state coercion provided Gandhi with a framework for connecting personal morality to political action.

## Morality and Truth

In Gandhi's thought, morality and truth are inseparable: "truth is the substance of all morality" [010-013]. A moral act is one aligned with truth; an immoral act is a departure from truth. This unity has practical consequences — there can be no moral shortcuts, no "white lies" in service of a supposedly good end. The means must be as pure as the end.

## Morality and Religious Practice

Gandhi consistently subordinates ritual and doctrine to morality. He was skeptical of religious practices that did not produce ethical conduct. During his student days in England, he "heard rumours of immorality being practised" [010-002] at a theosophical lodge and lost interest. His vegetarianism, initially a matter of a vow to his mother, evolved from dietary rule to moral principle: he came to see it "not of any system of morality" [018-002] narrowly but as part of a broader ethical commitment to nonviolence.
