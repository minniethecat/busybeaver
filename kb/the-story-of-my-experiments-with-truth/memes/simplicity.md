# Simplicity in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Simplicity was not merely a lifestyle preference for Gandhi but a moral discipline intimately connected to his search for Truth. From his early efforts to reduce expenses in London to the radical simplification of his life in South Africa, the shedding of material excess became for him both a spiritual practice and a political statement — a way of identifying with the poor and stripping away everything that stood between him and self-realization.

## Gandhi's Understanding of Simplicity

For Gandhi, simplicity was inseparable from truthfulness. He came to see that a life cluttered with possessions and comforts was fundamentally at odds with the pursuit of Truth:

> The extreme forms in which my passion for self-help and simplicity ultimately expressed itself will be described in their proper place. The seed had been long sown. It only needed watering to take root, to flower and to fructify, and the watering came in due course.
>
> (045-011)

The metaphor of the seed is significant: simplicity was not an external imposition but something organic that grew naturally from Gandhi's evolving convictions. It was, in his understanding, the outward expression of an inward harmonization.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's turn toward simplicity had both personal and literary sources. His most direct inspiration came from observing the lives of poor students in London:

> I saw that most of those who were spending from eight to fifteen pounds monthly had the advantage of scholarships. I had before me examples of much simpler living. I came across a fair number of poor students living more humbly than I. One of them was staying in the slums in a room at two shillings a week and living on two pence worth of cocoa and bread per meal from Lockhart's cheap Cocoa Rooms.
>
> (015-005)

The example of these poor students stirred Gandhi's conscience. He was also influenced by books on simple living, and by the example of his struggling brother in India, who continued to send money despite his own difficulties. The sense of financial responsibility toward his family became a moral imperative for simplification (015-005).

Beyond personal circumstances, [[leo-tolstoy]]'s writings on simple living and John Ruskin's *Unto This Last* reinforced Gandhi's conviction that a life of manual labor and reduced wants was not only economically virtuous but spiritually necessary.

## Gandhi's Practice

### First Experiments

Gandhi's first deliberate experiment with simplicity came during his student days in London. Upon realizing that his way of living was extravagant relative to his family's means, he took decisive action:

> With my preparation for another trial, I made an effort to simplify my life still further. I felt that my way of living did not yet befit the modest means of my family. The thought of my struggling brother, who nobly responded to my regular calls for monetary help, deeply pained me.
>
> (015-005)

He gave up his suite of rooms, rented a single room, invested in a stove, and began cooking his own breakfast. His daily expense dropped to a shilling and three pence. This was not experienced as deprivation but as liberation:

> Let not the reader think that this living made my life by any means a dreary affair. On the contrary the change harmonized my inward and outward life. It was also more in keeping with the means of my family. My life was certainly more truthful and my soul knew no bounds of joy.
>
> (015-007)

### Evolution in South Africa

In South Africa, Gandhi's passion for simplicity continued to deepen:

> As the ideals of sacrifice and simplicity were becoming more and more realized, and the religious consciousness was becoming more and more quickened in my daily life, the passion for vegetarianism as a mission went on increasing.
>
> (059-001)

He linked simplicity directly to religious growth. The practice of reducing material wants was not separate from his pursuit of God but integral to it. In Durban, he furnished a house but quickly found that even modest comfort held no real attraction for him. He taught himself to wash his own clothes, cutting the washerman's bill and turning a chore into a "pleasure" through the novelty of self-reliance.

### The Washing Experiment

The story of Gandhi's first self-washed collar captures both his commitment and his imperviousness to social ridicule:

> I shall never forget the first collar that I washed myself. I had used more starch than necessary, the iron had not been made hot enough, and for fear of burning the collar I had not pressed it sufficiently. The result was that, though the collar was fairly stiff, the superfluous starch continually dropped off it. I went to court with the collar on, thus inviting the ridicule of brother barristers, but even in those days I could be impervious to ridicule.
>
> (045-010)

This small anecdote illustrates a larger principle in Gandhi's practice: the willingness to endure embarrassment for the sake of a self-discipline that he believed was right.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Simplicity occupies a foundational position in Gandhi's ethical system. It is the practical correlate of several of his core concepts:

- **Truth**: A complicated life is necessarily a less truthful one, because possessions and status require pretense. "My life was certainly more truthful" after simplifying (015-007).
- **[[non-possession]]**: Simplicity is the daily practice of non-possession — the voluntary reduction of wants rather than the mere renunciation of excess.
- **[[bread-labor]]**: Manual work (cooking, washing, cleaning) was Gandhi's way of embodying simplicity and honoring the dignity of labor.
- **[[swadeshi]]**: Material simplicity extended to economic choices — buying local, wearing homespun [[khadi]], rejecting imported luxuries.
- **Service**: Gandhi explicitly connected simplicity with the spirit of service, arguing that his sons were better served by a simple upbringing than by formal education that would have taught them "artificial ways of living" (045-003).
