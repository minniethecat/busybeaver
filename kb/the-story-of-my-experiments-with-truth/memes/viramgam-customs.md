## Background

Upon his return to India from South Africa, Gandhi travelled through Kathiawad and encountered the Viramgam customs cordon. At the time, owing to plague concerns, third-class railway passengers were being subjected to medical inspections at Viramgam or Wadhwan. Gandhi himself, having a slight fever during one journey, had his temperature checked and his name noted by the inspector [065-005].

## The Customs Hardship

At Wadhwan station, a local tailor and noted public worker named Motilal met Gandhi and told him about the Viramgam customs and the hardships railway passengers suffered on account of it. Though Gandhi had little inclination to talk at the time due to his fever, he later came into close contact with Motilal, who made a point of spending days each month at Gandhi's newly started Ashram. Motilal would speak to Gandhi every day of Viramgam and the hardships of the passengers, which had become absolutely unbearable for him [066-001].

Wherever Gandhi went in Kathiawad, he heard complaints about the Viramgam customs. Convinced that the complaints were well-founded, he decided to make use of an offer from Lord Willingdon, the Governor of Bombay. He collected and read all available literature on the subject and opened correspondence with the Bombay Government [066-003].

## Gandhi's Campaign

Gandhi called on the Private Secretary to Lord Willingdon and waited on His Excellency as well. Lord Willingdon expressed sympathy but shifted the responsibility to Delhi, suggesting Gandhi approach the Government of India. Gandhi communicated with the Government of India but received no reply beyond an acknowledgment [066-004].

The breakthrough came when Gandhi had an occasion to meet Lord Chelmsford, the Viceroy. When he placed the facts before him, Lord Chelmsford expressed astonishment — he had known nothing of the matter. He gave Gandhi a patient hearing, telephoned that very moment for papers about Viramgam, and promised to remove the cordon if the authorities had no explanation or defense to offer [066-005].

## Resolution

Within a few days of this interview, Gandhi read in the papers that the Viramgam customs cordon had been removed. The campaign had succeeded through patient persistence and direct appeal to the highest authorities [066-005]. The reform was one of Gandhi's early successes in addressing specific administrative grievances in India after his return from South Africa.
