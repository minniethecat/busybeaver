# God in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

From his earliest religious impressions in Porbandar to his mature synthesis in South Africa, Gandhi's understanding of God underwent a profound transformation — from the ritual deity of his mother's Vaishnava devotion to an immanent moral presence he identified with Truth itself. God, for Gandhi, was not a distant metaphysical entity but the living force that "saved" him at critical junctures and powered his pursuit of self-realization.

## Gandhi's Definition

Gandhi defined God not in theological terms but in experiential and moral ones. He came to identify God with Truth — a conviction he articulated as his life's central purpose:

> What I want to achieve — what I have been striving and pining to achieve these thirty years — is self-realization, to see God face to face, to attain moksha. I live and move and have my being in pursuit of this goal. All that I do by way of speaking and writing, and all my ventures in the political field, are directed to this same end.
>
> (001-015)

This passage is perhaps the most direct statement of Gandhi's spiritual ambition in the entire autobiography. It places God at the center of his life's work and frames even his political action as spiritual pursuit.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's earliest exposure to God came through his mother Putlibai, whose saintliness left a deep impression. She would not eat without daily prayers, and her visits to the Haveli (Vaishnava temple) were a fixed ritual (001-007). Yet this early religiosity did not translate into a "living faith in God" for the young Gandhi:

> I happened, about this time, to come across Manusmriti which was amongst my father's collection. The story of the creation and similar things in it did not impress me very much, but on the contrary made me somewhat inclined towards atheism.
>
> (010-010)

Gandhi's journey toward faith was therefore not a straight line from childhood piety. It was forged through doubt, comparative religious study in [[london]], and the influence of several key figures.

Three moderns left a particularly deep impression on Gandhi's religious thinking: [[raychandbhai]] through living contact; [[leo-tolstoy]] through his book *The Kingdom of God Is Within You*; and John Ruskin through *Unto This Last* (026-009). Each contributed a different dimension — Raychandbhai offered a living model of Jain-influenced devotion; Tolstoy convinced Gandhi that the Kingdom of God was an inner reality, not a future event; Ruskin taught that individual morality and social justice were inseparable.

Gandhi's exposure to Christianity in South Africa also shaped his thinking. He attended prayer meetings led by Mr. Baker, read the Bible seriously, and was deeply moved by the Sermon on the Mount. Yet he could not accept the exclusivist doctrine that "Jesus was the only incarnate son of God" — for Gandhi, "if God could have sons, all of us were His sons" (040-007).

## Gandhi's Practice

### First Experiments

Gandhi's earliest conscious reliance on God came during his moral struggles in [[england]]. When a well-meaning friend persistently argued that he should eat meat and abandon his vow of vegetarianism, Gandhi found a resource beyond rational argument:

> Day in and day out the friend would argue, but I had an eternal negative to face him with. The more he argued, the more uncompromising I became. Daily I would pray for God's protection and get it. Not that I had any idea of God. It was faith that was at work — faith of which the seed was sown by the good nurse Rambha.
>
> (014-004)

This is a remarkable confession: Gandhi admits he had no clear idea of God at this stage, yet his faith in some protective power was already functioning. He traces this seed of faith to his childhood nurse Rambha, who had taught him to repeat the name of Rama as a protection against fear (014-003).

### Evolution Through Trials

Gandhi narrates several episodes where he experienced what he understood as divine intervention. Each trial deepened his conviction that God worked through conscience:

| Trial | Location | PN | Nature of Intervention |
|-------|----------|-----|----------------------|
| Brothel visit | Rajkot | 007-009 | "Saved by the skin of my teeth" — inner revulsion |
| Meat-eating pressure | London | 014-004 | Prayer brought protection |
| Platonic relationship | London | 021-004–007 | Inner restraint at critical moment |
| Shipboard temptation | At sea | 031-011–012 | Refusal born of inner conviction |
| Train assault | South Africa | 034-005 | Composure under physical threat |

After the incident on the ship, Gandhi reflected that he "could claim no credit for having come out unscathed" — the credit belonged to God's protection (031-012). Similarly, after narrowly avoiding an improper relationship in England, he wrote:

> I did not then know the essence of religion or of God, and how He works in us. Only vaguely I understood that God had saved me on that occasion. On all occasions of trial He has saved me.
>
> (021-007)

### God as Truth

The culmination of Gandhi's evolving theology was his identification of God with [[truth]]. While the autobiography does not use the exact phrase "God is Truth" in its final form, Gandhi's repeated equation of the two concepts is unmistakable. He described his life's goal as both "self-realization" and "to see God face to face" (001-015), and he equated the moral law with divine will.

This synthesis resolved for Gandhi the tension between different religious traditions — if God is Truth, then every sincere religious seeker approaches the same God, regardless of the name they use.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

God occupies the apex of Gandhi's conceptual framework. Every other major Gandhian concept flows from this center:

| Concept | Relationship to God |
|---------|-------------------|
| [[truth]] | God *is* Truth; Truth is the face of God |
| [[ahimsa]] | The means by which one approaches God; God is love |
| [[satyagraha]] | The method of clinging to Truth (= God) |
| [[prayer]] | Direct communion with God, "the food of the soul" |
| [[brahmacharya]] | Self-purification necessary to see God |

Gandhi's father [[karamchand-gandhi]] reinforced this connection through his own example. On his deathbed, he had "an abiding faith in prayer" and a "firm conviction that God could not but listen to prayer fervently offered" (040-003).
