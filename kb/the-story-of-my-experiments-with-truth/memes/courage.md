# Courage in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Gandhi was, by his own admission, a coward as a child. His autobiography is remarkable for how frankly it documents this fact — and for how it traces the slow, uneven cultivation of courage through a lifetime of experiments with truth.

## Confronting Childhood Fear

The young Gandhi was haunted by fear of "thieves, ghosts, and serpents." He could not sleep without a light and dared not stir outdoors at night. What made this particularly painful was the contrast with his young wife:

> I knew that she had more courage than I, and I felt ashamed of myself. She knew no fear of serpents and ghosts. She could go out anywhere in the dark.
>
> (007-009)

This shame at his own cowardice was, paradoxically, the seed of future courage. Gandhi's honesty about his fear was itself a form of courage — the refusal to pretend to a bravery he did not possess.

## Courage Through Truth-Telling

The turning point in Gandhi's relationship with courage came through the discipline of truth. In England, having concealed his marriage and lived under false pretences, he found the courage to confess to the elderly widow whose family had befriended him:

> I am glad God has now given me the courage to speak out the truth. Will you forgive me?
>
> (028-016)

This was, in Gandhi's telling, not his own achievement but a gift — "God has now given me the courage." The act of truth-telling and the cultivation of courage were inextricable; each act of honesty strengthened the capacity for the next.

## Public Courage

Gandhi's most famous statement linking courage and forgiveness came in response to a question about how he would face mob violence in Durban:

> "I hope God will give me the courage and the sense to forgive them and to refrain from bringing them to law. I have no anger against them. I am only sorry for their ignorance and their narrowness."
>
> (071-012)

Courage here is not the courage to fight but the courage *not* to fight — the fortitude to absorb violence without returning it. This was the courage that [[satyagraha]] would demand of its practitioners.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

- **[[truth]]**: Courage is the strength required to speak and live truthfully; each truthful act builds courage.
- **[[faith]]**: Faith undergirds courage — Gandhi's prayer for courage presupposes a God who grants it.
- **[[forgiveness]]**: True forgiveness requires courage; Gandhi explicitly linked the two.
- **[[satyagraha]]**: Courage is the internal prerequisite for the nonviolent confrontation of injustice.
- **[[ahimsa]]**: Ahimsa without courage is passivity; with courage, it becomes a force.
