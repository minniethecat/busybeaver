# C. F. Andrews

Charles Freer Andrews was an Anglican priest and missionary whose commitment to social justice led him from conventional Christian mission work to deep involvement in India's independence movement. He became one of Gandhi's most trusted friends, a mediator between Gandhi and the British government, and an advocate for Indian indentured laborers worldwide. Gandhi called him "Deenabandhu" — friend of the poor.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Andrews met Gandhi in South Africa and was immediately drawn to his philosophy. Unlike many Christian missionaries who sought to convert Indians, Andrews respected Hinduism and sought common ethical ground. He became an intermediary between Gandhi and British authorities, using his position as an English clergyman to advocate for Indian rights. Gandhi trusted him deeply, and Andrews was one of the few people permitted to be present during Gandhi's fasts. Their friendship transcended religious and cultural difference, embodying Gandhi's ideal of interfaith solidarity.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in England in 1871, Andrews was ordained as an Anglican priest and traveled to India as a missionary with the Cambridge Mission to Delhi. His encounters with Indian poverty and racism transformed him from a conventional missionary into a social reformer and anti-colonial activist.

### Role in the Narrative

Andrews appears in the autobiography as the exemplary Christian ally — someone who understood Christianity not as a tool of colonial domination but as a call to serve the oppressed. His work on behalf of Indian indentured laborers in Fiji and other British colonies aligned with Gandhi's own campaigns.

## Historical Significance

Andrews represents the possibility of cross-cultural moral solidarity — an English priest who became an Indian patriot without ceasing to be a Christian. His life challenges narratives of inevitable civilizational clash.

## Key Passages

> Andrews was a true friend of India. He gave his life in her service.
