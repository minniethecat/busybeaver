# Swadeshi

Swadeshi (literally "of one's own country") is Gandhi's principle of economic self-reliance. It began as a boycott of British goods during the Swadeshi movement of 1905 but was transformed by Gandhi into a comprehensive philosophy: individuals and communities should produce and consume locally, minimizing dependence on distant and exploitative systems. Swadeshi was for Gandhi not mere protectionism but a spiritual discipline — a way of honoring one's neighbors and respecting the limits of nature.

## Definition

Gandhi defined swadeshi as the duty to use locally produced goods, particularly hand-spun and hand-woven cloth (khadi), even if they were of lower quality or higher price than imported alternatives. This was not economic irrationality but moral logic: buying foreign cloth enriched distant mill-owners while impoverishing one's own neighbors. Swadeshi extended beyond cloth to all aspects of life — food, education, law, medicine — and pointed toward a decentralized, village-centered economy. At its heart was the conviction that political freedom (Swaraj) was impossible without economic self-reliance.

## Origins and Influences

The Swadeshi movement emerged in Bengal in 1905 as a response to Lord Curzon's partition of the province. Nationalist leaders called for a boycott of British goods and the promotion of Indian-made products. Gandhi, then in South Africa, observed these developments and began to develop his own, more radical interpretation. He was influenced by John Ruskin's *Unto This Last*, which argued that economic life should be organized around human welfare rather than profit, and by Tolstoy's celebration of peasant labor. The spinning wheel (charkha) became Gandhi's symbol of swadeshi — a tool that every Indian could use to produce cloth and resist economic imperialism.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Khadi as Swadeshi in Action

Khadi — hand-spun and hand-woven cloth — was the centerpiece of Gandhi's swadeshi programme. He required Congress members to spin daily and wear khadi. The All-India Spinners' Association was formed to organize production and distribution. For Gandhi, spinning was not merely economic activity but a form of prayer and meditation.

### Constructive Programme

Swadeshi was the economic dimension of Gandhi's constructive programme. Alongside khadi, it included village sanitation, basic education in the vernacular, the promotion of indigenous medicine, and the revival of village crafts. The goal was to make Indian villages self-sufficient — "village republics" that could resist both British colonialism and the industrial exploitation Gandhi feared would follow independence.

### Boycott and Non-Cooperation

Swadeshi provided the economic content for political non-cooperation. The boycott of foreign cloth during the non-cooperation movement (1920–22) was swadeshi in action: millions of Indians burned their foreign clothes and committed to wearing khadi. This was simultaneously an economic act (depriving Britain of revenue), a political act (challenging colonial authority), and a spiritual act (purifying oneself of attachment to foreign goods).

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Swadeshi connects Gandhi's personal ethics to his economic and political vision. It embodies ahimsa (local production minimizes the violence of long supply chains), swaraj (self-reliance is the economic foundation of self-rule), and satyagraha (the boycott is a form of nonviolent resistance). It is also the practical bridge between individual discipline and social transformation — the spinning wheel in every home.

## Key Passages

> Swadeshi is that spirit in us which restricts us to the use and service of our immediate surroundings to the exclusion of the more remote. (Gandhi, Hind Swaraj)
