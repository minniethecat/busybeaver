# Sabarmati Ashram

The Sabarmati Ashram (originally Satyagraha Ashram) was Gandhi's primary residence and community in India from 1915 to 1930. Located on the banks of the Sabarmati River outside Ahmedabad, it was the nerve center of India's independence movement — the place from which the Salt March was launched in 1930.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi founded the ashram on May 25, 1915, shortly after his return from South Africa (133-002). After consulting friends about the name, he chose "Satyagraha Ashram" to emphasize the method of truth-force (133-008). The ashram's residents — including Gandhi's family, political associates, and a diverse community of seekers — observed strict disciplines: bread labor, nonviolence, dietary restraint, prayer, and the rejection of untouchability (134-017). The ashram served as a model for the decentralized, self-sufficient village communities Gandhi envisioned for India.

## In the Narrative

The ashram figures prominently in the autobiography's later chapters. Key events include its founding and naming, the crisis over admitting a Dalit family (which cost the ashram its funding until an anonymous donor intervened), and the daily disciplines that shaped its residents. The ashram was disbanded in 1933 when Gandhi vowed not to return until India achieved independence.
