# Patna

Patna, the capital of [[bihar]] province, was [[mahatma-gandhi]]'s gateway to the [[champaran]] district during his first major Indian campaign in 1917. It was here that Gandhi first encountered the deep social divisions of Bihari society and formed the relationships with Bihari lawyers that would sustain the Champaran Satyagraha.

## Arrival and First Impressions

Gandhi arrived in Patna en route to Champaran in April 1917, at the invitation of [[rajkumar-shukla]], the indigo peasant who had pursued him across India. In Patna, Gandhi was hosted by [[rajendra-prasad]], then a successful lawyer who would later become independent India's first President.

Professor [[kripalani]], who met Gandhi in Patna, "spoke to me about the desperate condition of Bihar, particularly of the Tirhut division, and gave me an idea of the difficulty of my task" [136-009]. Kripalani had already established close contact with the Biharis and had prepared the ground for Gandhi's mission.

## Social Realities

Gandhi's stay in Patna exposed him to the entrenched social hierarchies of Bihar:

> "There was strict untouchability in Bihar. I might not draw water at the well whilst the servants were using it, lest drops of water from my bucket might pollute them, the servants not knowing to what caste I belonged." [137-008]

The servants directed him to outdoor latrines, not out of malice but because "the servants were doing the duty which they thought Rajendra Babu would wish them to do" [137-008]. Gandhi accepted all this without irritation, being "inured to such things" [137-008].

## Forming the Legal Team

It was in Patna that Gandhi met the key figures who would form his legal team in Champaran. [[brajkishore-prasad]] arrived from Darbhanga, and Gandhi's impression of him was transformed from their brief Lucknow meeting:

> "He impressed me this time with his humility, simplicity, goodness and extraordinary faith, so characteristic of the Biharis, and my heart was joyous over it." [137-011]

Gandhi soon felt himself "becoming bound to this circle of friends in lifelong friendship" [137-011]. [[brajkishore-prasad]] acquainted him with the facts of the tenants' cases, and Gandhi formed his strategy: the courts were useless where "the ryots are so crushed and fear-stricken" — the real remedy was "to be free from fear" [137-003].
