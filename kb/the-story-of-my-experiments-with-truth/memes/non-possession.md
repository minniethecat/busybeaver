# Non-Possession in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Non-possession — aparigraha in Sanskrit — was for Gandhi one of the most radical of his ethical commitments. It went beyond [[simplicity]] (reducing wants) to the conviction that one should own nothing at all, holding whatever one controlled as a trustee for the community. The seed of this conviction was planted by the Bhagavad Gita and nurtured through Gandhi's encounter with Ruskin and his own experiments with renunciation.

## Gandhi's Understanding of Non-Possession

Gandhi's conception of non-possession crystallized during his intensive study of the Bhagavad Gita, which he called his "dictionary of daily reference." The Gita's teaching on aparigraha struck him with particular force:

> Words like *aparigraha* (non-possession) and *samabhava* (equability) gripped me. How to cultivate and preserve that equability was the question... How was one to divest oneself of all possessions? Was not the body itself possession enough? Were not wife and children possessions? Was I to destroy all the cupboards of books I had? Was I to give up all I had and follow Him? Straight came the answer: I could not follow Him unless I gave up all I had.
>
> (072-001)

The questions Gandhi asks are not rhetorical — they are the real, agonized questions of a man confronting a teaching that demands everything. Is the body itself a possession? Are wife and children? The "straight" answer — "I could not follow Him unless I gave up all I had" — left no room for compromise.

## Origins and Influences

The Bhagavad Gita was the primary source, but Gandhi's understanding of non-possession was deepened by his legal training. The English law of equity, particularly the concept of the trustee, provided a practical framework:

> My study of English law came to my help. Snell's discussion of the maxims of Equity came to my memory. I understood more clearly in the light of the Gita teaching the implication of the word "trustee" ... I understood the Gita teaching of non-possession to mean that those who desired salvation should act like the trustee who, though having control over great possessions, regards not an iota of them as his own.
>
> (072-002)

This was Gandhi's great innovation: non-possession did not require literal poverty. It required a change of attitude — the recognition that everything one controlled was held in trust for the community, not owned for personal benefit. The wealthy could practice non-possession if they regarded themselves as trustees rather than owners.

## Gandhi's Practice

### The Insurance Policy

The first practical test of Gandhi's commitment to non-possession was his decision to cancel his life insurance policy. He wrote to his brother, who had acted as a father to him, explaining that "henceforth he should expect nothing from me, for future savings, if any, would be utilized for the benefit of the community" (072-003).

His brother's response was predictable — he accused Gandhi of aspiring to be wiser than their father and reminded him of his duty to support the family. Gandhi's reply was telling:

> I pointed out to him that I was doing exactly what our father had done. The meaning of "family" had but to be slightly widened and the wisdom of my step would become clear.
>
> (072-004)

The widening of "family" to include the entire community — and ultimately all of humanity — was Gandhi's ethical genius. Non-possession was not the abandonment of responsibility but its radical expansion.

### Returning Gifts

Gandhi's commitment to non-possession was tested when he received valuable gifts from the Indian community in recognition of his public service. He convinced his wife Kasturba — with difficulty — to return jewelry and other valuables:

> The gifts received in 1896 and 1901 were all returned. A trust-deed was prepared, and they were deposited with a bank, to be used for the service of the community, according to my wishes or to those of the trustees.
>
> (056-007)

The trust-deed was the practical expression of the trustee concept: Gandhi did not throw the gifts away but placed them under formal legal control so that they could benefit the community rather than himself or his family.

### Non-Possession Among the Cardinal Virtues

Gandhi ranked non-possession alongside nonviolence and celibacy as a "cardinal virtue" requiring continuous striving:

> I cannot claim this as a special virtue, as it is in my very nature, rather than a result of an effort on my part, whereas in the case of ahimsa (nonviolence), brahmacharya (celibacy), aparigraha (non-possession) and other cardinal virtues, I am fully conscious of a continuous striving for their cultivation.
>
> (074-008)

This distinction is significant: some virtues came naturally to Gandhi (his instinctive refusal to distinguish between relatives and strangers, for instance), but non-possession required "continuous striving." It was not a settled achievement but an ongoing discipline.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Non-possession is the economic dimension of Gandhi's comprehensive ethical vision:

- **[[simplicity]]**: Non-possession is simplicity taken to its logical conclusion — not just reducing wants but renouncing ownership itself.
- **[[truth]]**: Gandhi could not follow Truth unless he gave up all he had. Non-possession is thus a precondition of the spiritual life.
- **[[bread-labor]]**: Manual labor is the economic expression of non-possession — earning one's living by bodily work rather than accumulation.
- **[[swadeshi]]**: Economic self-reliance in harmony with non-possession — producing what one needs locally, not accumulating excess.
- **[[service]]**: Non-possession frees one for service. The trustee serves the community with what they hold; the owner serves only themselves.
- **[[brahmacharya]]**: Both brahmacharya and aparigraha require the renunciation of desire — the former of sexual desire, the latter of the desire to possess.
