# Forgiveness in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Forgiveness was, for Gandhi, not merely a virtue but a practical necessity of the nonviolent life. His autobiography traces forgiveness along three axes: the forgiveness he sought from others, his own struggle to forgive himself, and his public practice of forgiving those who wronged him.

## Gandhi's Understanding

Gandhi understood forgiveness as inseparable from truth-telling. A clean confession, he believed, made forgiveness possible — and forgiveness, once given, strengthened the bond between the forgiver and the forgiven. This understanding was first forged in a childhood episode: having stolen a bit of gold from his brother, the young Gandhi wrote out a confession and submitted it to his father, expecting anger but receiving tears instead:

> This sort of sublime forgiveness was not natural to my father. I had thought that he would be angry, say hard things, and strike his forehead. But he was so wonderfully peaceful, and I believe this was due to my clean confession.
>
> (008-008)

The experience taught Gandhi that "a clean confession, combined with a promise never to commit the sin again, when offered before one who has the right to receive it, is the purest type of repentance" (008-008).

## Self-Forgiveness and Its Limits

Gandhi was often harder on himself than on others. He records with pain his failure to forgive himself for suspecting his wife Kasturba at the instigation of a jealous friend:

> I never could doubt his veracity. And I have never forgiven myself the violence of which I have been guilty in often having pained my wife by acting on his information.
>
> (007-011)

The inability to forgive himself for what he regarded as betrayals of trust — toward his wife, toward his dying father — remained a persistent theme throughout the autobiography. Gandhi's high standard for self-conduct meant that self-forgiveness was the hardest form to practice.

## Forgiveness and Nonviolence

Gandhi's most mature expression of forgiveness came in the context of [[satyagraha]]. Confronted with the threat of mob violence upon his return to Durban in 1897, he was asked how he would reconcile self-defense with nonviolence:

> "I hope God will give me the courage and the sense to forgive them and to refrain from bringing them to law. I have no anger against them. I am only sorry for their ignorance and their narrowness."
>
> (071-012)

This was forgiveness not as passive acceptance but as an active discipline of refusing to retaliate — the very core of [[satyagraha]].

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Forgiveness functions as the connective tissue between Gandhi's other core commitments:

- **[[truth]]**: A clean confession precedes true forgiveness; forgiveness in turn restores the relationship that untruth has damaged.
- **[[ahimsa]]**: Forgiveness is ahimsa in the face of injury — the refusal to return harm for harm.
- **[[courage]]**: Forgiveness requires courage; Gandhi explicitly linked the two, praying for "the courage and the sense to forgive."
- **[[compassion]]**: Forgiveness flows from compassion — the recognition that the wrongdoer acts from ignorance rather than malice.
