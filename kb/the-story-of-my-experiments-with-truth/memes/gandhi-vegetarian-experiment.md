Gandhi's commitment to vegetarianism during his student years in London (1888–1891) was one of the formative experiments of his early life. What began as a vow made to his mother became a deeply held conviction, influencing his lifelong philosophy of self-discipline and dietetic reform.

## The Vegetarian Vow

Before departing for England, Gandhi had taken a vow before his mother to abstain from meat. The vow created practical difficulties in London, where he found English food tasteless and unsatisfying as a vegetarian. He described himself as "between Scylla and Charybdis" — unable to bear England but unwilling to return to India before completing his studies (443-418).

His discovery of a vegetarian restaurant on Farringdon Street proved transformative. At the entrance, he noticed Salt's *Plea for Vegetarianism* displayed in a glass case. He purchased the book for a shilling and enjoyed his "first hearty meal since my arrival in England" (465-419). Gandhi later declared: "God had come to my aid."

## A Vegetarian by Choice

Reading Salt's book from cover to cover profoundly changed Gandhi's relationship to vegetarianism. "From the date of reading this book, I may claim to have become a vegetarian by choice" (467-420). Previously he had abstained from meat only because of the vow and the principle of truthfulness, while secretly wishing every Indian would be a meat-eater and hoping to become one himself someday. Now, he wrote, "The choice was now made in favour of vegetarianism, the spread of which henceforward became my mission" (467-420).

His commitment deepened through extensive reading. He studied Howard Williams's *The Ethics of Diet*, Dr. Anna Kingsford's *The Perfect Way in Diet*, and Dr. Allinson's writings on health and hygiene. A vegetarian himself, Allinson prescribed a strictly vegetarian diet for his patients and advocated a curative system based on dietary regulation. The cumulative effect of this reading was that "dietetic experiments came to take an important place in my life" (475-421). Initially driven by health considerations, religion later became the supreme motive.

## The Vegetarian Society

Gandhi subscribed to the Vegetarian Society's weekly journal, joined the Society, and soon found himself on its Executive Committee. Here he came into contact with the leading figures of the vegetarian movement. He encountered three different definitions of "meat" among English vegetarians: some permitted fish but not flesh, others permitted eggs but not fish, and the strictest definition excluded all animal products including milk and eggs. Gandhi determined that his mother's definition — excluding eggs — was the one binding on him. Though this caused practical difficulties in restaurants, "the strict observance of the vow produced an inward relish distinctly more healthy, delicate and permanent" (531-425).

## Deeper Implications

The vegetarian studies led Gandhi to broader reflections. The ethical arguments of vegetarian writers convinced him "that man's supremacy over the lower animals meant not that the former should prey upon the latter, but that the higher should protect the lower, and that there should be mutual aid between the two as between man and man" (521-423). These ideas foreshadowed his later development of *ahimsa* (non-violence) as a comprehensive moral philosophy.
