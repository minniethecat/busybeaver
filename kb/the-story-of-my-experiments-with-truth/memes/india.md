# India in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

India is the gravitational center of Gandhi's autobiography. It is the land he departed for [[england]] as a nervous teenager, the country he returned to as a struggling lawyer, and the nation whose freedom became his life's work. In the autobiography, India is never merely a geographical setting — it is a moral landscape, a field of experiments, and the community whose purification Gandhi sought through his own.

## Gandhi's India

The India of Gandhi's childhood was a mosaic of princely states and British-administered provinces. His own [[porbandar]] and [[rajkot]] were small Kathiawad states in what is now Gujarat — worlds apart from the cosmopolitan ferment of [[bombay]] or the imperial grandeur of [[calcutta]]. Gandhi notes the contrast himself when describing his journey to England:

> I was quite unaccustomed to talking English, and except for Sjt. Mazmudar all the other passengers in the second saloon were English. I could not speak to them.
>
> (013-001)

This shyness was not just linguistic — it marked the cultural distance between provincial Kathiawad and the wider world Gandhi was about to enter.

## The Return

When Gandhi returned from South Africa in 1896, India was not the same country he had left. The [[indian-national-congress]] had grown in stature, and nationalist sentiment was rising. Yet Gandhi was struck by how little professional opportunity awaited him. He was "completely at sea" in Indian law, having studied only English law in London (025-002). His attempts to practice in Bombay ended in humiliation when he could not muster the courage to cross-examine a witness in his first case.

This professional failure drove him back to Rajkot, then to South Africa — but not before his encounters with leaders like [[pherozeshah-mehta]], Justice Ranade, and other luminaries of Bombay gave him his first taste of Indian public life.

## India as Political Stage

When Gandhi finally returned to India permanently in 1915, the country was on the cusp of a new political era. The autobiography traces this transformation through key campaigns:

- **[[champaran-satyagraha]]** (1917) — Gandhi's first major intervention in Indian peasant life, securing relief for indigo cultivators in Bihar
- **[[kheda-satyagraha]]** (1918) — Peasant resistance to revenue collection during famine in Gujarat
- **[[ahmedabad-mill-strike]]** (1918) — Gandhi's first labor intervention and first public fast
- **[[rowlatt-satyagraha]]** (1919) — The first nationwide satyagraha campaign
- **[[non-cooperation-movement]]** (1920–1922) — Mass civil disobedience that transformed the Congress

Each of these campaigns deepened Gandhi's relationship with India's villages and peasants. The India that emerges from the autobiography is not the India of the cities — Bombay, [[calcutta]], [[madras]] — but the India of the countryside, where most Indians lived and where Gandhi believed true Swaraj must be rooted.

## Gandhi's Vision

By the end of the autobiography, India had become for Gandhi something more than a nation — it was the arena of his spiritual experiments, the community whose moral purification through [[satyagraha]] and [[swaraj]] animated every public act. "I have found," he wrote, "that it is our first duty to serve our immediate neighbours" — and for Gandhi, India's 300 million were those neighbors.
