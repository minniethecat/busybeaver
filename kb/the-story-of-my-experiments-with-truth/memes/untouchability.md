# Untouchability

Gandhi considered the abolition of untouchability to be as essential to India's moral and political regeneration as the achievement of independence. "If untouchability could be a part of Hinduism, it could but be a rotten part" (040-009). He devoted decades to campaigning against the practice, risking the wrath of orthodox Hindus who considered his views heretical, and making the removal of untouchability a central plank of his constructive programme.

## Definition

Untouchability, in the Indian context, was the practice of treating certain caste groups — then called "untouchables," now referred to as Dalits — as ritually polluting and socially excluded. They were barred from temples, denied access to public wells, restricted to the most degrading occupations, and subjected to severe social and economic discrimination. Gandhi rejected the religious basis for this exclusion while initially defending the fourfold *varna* system (the theoretical division of society into priests, warriors, merchants, and laborers) as a form of social organization distinct from the practice of untouchability. He coined the term "Harijan" (children of God) as a respectful alternative to "untouchable."

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's commitment to fighting untouchability had multiple roots. In South Africa, he experienced racial discrimination firsthand and recognized parallels with caste discrimination in India. His reading of Hindu scriptures convinced him that untouchability had no sanction in authentic Hinduism — it was a "rotten part" that had grown up as a corruption. Christian missionary critiques of caste also influenced him, though he resisted conversion. His contact with Dalit leaders and communities, particularly in Champaran and later at the Sabarmati Ashram, gave his campaign practical urgency.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Personal Example

Gandhi practiced what he preached. He adopted a Dalit child, lived in Dalit quarters during his travels, and insisted that his ashrams admit Dalits on equal terms. "Those who wanted to help the Ashram would have to do so without any distinction of caste" (134-017). This caused recurring crises when orthodox donors withdrew support, but Gandhi refused to compromise.

### Political Campaigns

The removal of untouchability was one of the pillars of Gandhi's constructive programme, alongside Hindu-Muslim unity and the promotion of khadi. He fasted in 1932 against the British proposal for separate electorates for Dalits, arguing that this would permanently divide Hindu society. His fast secured the Poona Pact, which provided for reserved seats within a joint electorate. He toured India extensively for the Harijan cause, raising funds and consciousness.

### Tensions and Limitations

Gandhi's approach to untouchability had significant limitations. His defense of the *varna* system, even as he condemned untouchability, put him at odds with Dalit leaders like B.R. Ambedkar, who argued that the entire caste system was irredeemable. Gandhi's paternalistic language and his emphasis on the reform of Hinduism from within were criticized as insufficiently radical. These debates continue to shape assessments of Gandhi's legacy.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Untouchability was, for Gandhi, the test case for his philosophy of reform through nonviolent persuasion. He believed that caste discrimination could be eliminated not through law alone but through a change of heart, spurred by satyagraha. The campaign against untouchability embodied his conviction that social reform and political independence were inseparable — an India that tolerated untouchability did not deserve swaraj.
