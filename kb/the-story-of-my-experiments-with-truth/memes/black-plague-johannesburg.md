## Background

In early 1904, the Indian community in Johannesburg was living in an insanitary location. Having ceased to be proprietors of their dwellings and become tenants of the Municipality, their surroundings had become worse than before, as the Municipality did not enforce the same standards of cleanliness that fear of the law had previously compelled. The number of tenants increased, and with them the squalor and the disorder [092-001].

## The Outbreak

While the Indians were dealing with these conditions, there was a sudden outbreak of the black plague — also called pneumonic plague — more terrible and fatal than the bubonic variety. The outbreak originated not in the Indian location but in one of the gold mines near Johannesburg, where African workers labored under conditions for which their white employers were solely responsible. Twenty-three Indians working in connection with the mine suddenly caught the infection and returned one evening to their quarters in the location with an acute attack of the plague [092-003].

Sjt. Madanjit, who was then canvassing subscribers for *Indian Opinion*, happened to be in the location. A remarkably fearless man, he immediately sent a pencil-note to Gandhi:

> "There has been a sudden outbreak of the black plague. You must come immediately and take prompt measures, otherwise we must be prepared for dire consequences. Please come immediately." [092-002]

Madanjit bravely broke open the lock of a vacant house and put all the patients there. Gandhi cycled to the location and wrote to the Town Clerk to inform him of the circumstances in which they had taken possession of the house [092-004].

## Gandhi's Response

Gandhi responded without hesitation and went to the location with Dr. William Godfrey, who was practicing in Johannesburg and ran to the rescue as soon as he got the news. But twenty-three patients were more than the three of them could cope with [092-005].

At the time, Gandhi had four young Indians working in his office — Kalyandas, Maneklal, Gunvantrai Desai, and another whose name he could not later recall. Kalyandas had been entrusted to Gandhi by his father. In South Africa, Gandhi declared that he had rarely come across anyone more obliging and willing to render implicit obedience than Kalyandas. Fortunately he was unmarried then, and Gandhi did not hesitate to impose on him duties involving great risks. Maneklal, also unmarried as far as Gandhi could remember, had been secured in Johannesburg. Gandhi decided to sacrifice all four — calling them clerks, coworkers, or sons. All four volunteered without hesitation [092-008].

> "Where you are, we will also be," was their short and sweet reply. [092-010]

Mr. Ritch had a large family and was ready to take the plunge, but Gandhi prevented him, not having the heart to expose him to the risk. He attended to the work outside the danger zone instead [093-001].

It was a terrible night of vigil and nursing. Gandhi had nursed patients before, but never any attacked by the black plague. Dr. Godfrey's courage proved infectious. The indefatigable zeal and fearlessness with which the young volunteers worked rejoiced Gandhi beyond measure. One could understand the bravery of Dr. Godfrey and of an experienced man like Madanjit, but the spirit of these callow youths was remarkable [092-012]. As far as Gandhi could recollect, they pulled all the patients through that night [092-013].

## Medical Response and Earth Treatment

The Town Clerk expressed his gratitude to Gandhi and frankly confessed that the Town Council had no immediate means to cope with such an emergency. Once awakened to a sense of their duty, the Municipality made no delay in taking prompt measures [093-002].

The next day they placed a vacant godown at Gandhi's disposal for housing the patients, though the building was unkempt and unclean. They cleaned it themselves, raised beds and other necessaries through charitable Indians, and improvised a temporary hospital [093-001].

The Municipality lent the services of a nurse who came with brandy and other hospital equipment. Dr. Godfrey remained in charge. The nurse was a kindly lady and would have attended to the patients, but the volunteers rarely allowed her to touch them lest she should catch the contagion. The instructions were to give patients frequent doses of brandy, and the nurse even asked the volunteers to take it for precaution as she was doing herself. But none of them would touch it. Gandhi had no faith in its beneficial effect [093-003].

With Dr. Godfrey's permission, Gandhi put three patients who were prepared to do without brandy under earth treatment, applying wet earth bandages to their heads and chests. Two of these were saved. The other twenty died in the godown. This experience enhanced Gandhi's faith in earth treatment, as well as his scepticism of the efficacy of brandy even as a medicine. He acknowledged that neither the faith nor the scepticism was based on solid grounds, but the impression he received remained with him [093-004].

There was a lazaretto for contagious diseases about seven miles from Johannesburg. The two surviving patients were removed to tents near the lazaretto, and arrangements were made for sending any fresh cases there. Gandhi and his coworkers were thus relieved of their work [094-001].

In the course of a few days, the volunteers learned that the good nurse had had an attack of the plague and immediately succumbed to it. It was impossible to say how the two patients were saved and how the others remained immune [093-005].

## Evacuation and the Location Fire

The Municipality was wide awake where the health of its white citizens was concerned. Despite the many sins of omission and commission against the Indians that Gandhi had laid at the door of the Municipality, he could not help commending its solicitude for the white citizens, and he rendered it as much help as he could. He had an impression that if he had withheld his cooperation, the task would have been more difficult for the Municipality, and that it would not have hesitated to use armed force and do its worst. But all that was averted. The Municipal authorities were pleased at the Indians' behaviour [103-001].

The location was put under a strong guard, with passage in and out made impossible without permission. Gandhi and his coworkers had free permits of entry and exit. The decision was made to vacate the entire location population and have them live under canvas for three weeks in an open plain about thirteen miles from Johannesburg, after which the location would be set on fire [103-002].

The people were in a terrible fright, but Gandhi's constant presence was a consolation to them. Many of the poor people used to hoard their scanty savings underground. This had to be unearthed, and Gandhi became their banker. Streams of money poured into his office. Nearly sixty thousand pounds were deposited through him. The bank manager accommodated him in every way, and it was decided to disinfect all the money before sending it to the bank. Gandhi advised those who had enough money to place it as fixed deposit, and they accepted the advice. The result was that some of them became accustomed to investing their money in banks [103-003].

The location residents were removed by special train to Klipspruit Farm near Johannesburg, where they were supplied with provisions by the Municipality at public expense. This city under canvas looked like a military camp. Gandhi used to cycle out to them daily. Within twenty-four hours of their stay, the people forgot all their misery and began to live merrily. Whenever Gandhi went there he found them enjoying themselves with song and mirth. Three weeks' stay in the open air evidently improved their health [103-004].

The location was put to the flames on the very next day after its evacuation. The Municipality showed not the slightest inclination to save anything from the conflagration. About this very time, and for the same reason, the Municipality burnt down all its timber in the market and sustained a loss of some ten thousand pounds. The reason for this drastic step was the discovery of some dead rats in the market [103-005].

## New Relationships Forged

On the outbreak of the plague, Gandhi had addressed a strong letter to the press, holding the Municipality guilty of negligence after the location came into its possession and responsible for the outbreak of the plague itself. This letter secured him the acquaintance of Mr. Henry Polak and was partly responsible for the friendship of the late Rev. Joseph Doke [093-006].

Mr. Albert West, whom Gandhi used to meet at a vegetarian restaurant every evening, read the letter in the press about the outbreak. Not finding Gandhi in the restaurant for a day or two, he knocked at Gandhi's door early one morning. "I did not find you in the restaurant and was really afraid lest something should have happened to you," West said. "I am ready to help in nursing the patients. You know that I have no one depending on me" [093-008].

Gandhi expressed his gratitude and instead offered West the charge of the *Indian Opinion* press at Durban, since Madanjit was likely to be engaged in Johannesburg. A salary of £10 per month and a part of the profits, if any, was fixed. The very next day Mr. West left for Durban by the evening mail. West belonged to a peasant family in Louth (Lincolnshire), had had an ordinary school education, but had learnt a good deal in the school of experience and by dint of self-help. Gandhi always knew him to be a pure, sober, God-fearing, humane Englishman. From that day until Gandhi left the shores of South Africa, West remained a partner of his joys and sorrows [093-009].

## Aftermath

Though Gandhi and his coworkers were eventually relieved of the charge of the patients, many consequences of the black plague remained to be dealt with [094-001]. The black plague enhanced his influence with the poor Indians and increased both his community work and his responsibilities. Some of the new contacts with Europeans that arose from the crisis added considerably to his moral obligations [095-001].

The Municipality had to incur heavy expenditure, but it successfully arrested the further progress of the plague, and Johannesburg once more breathed freely [103-006].
