# Swaraj

Swaraj (self-rule) was for Gandhi far more than political independence from British rule. It signified the capacity of individuals and communities to govern themselves — morally, economically, and spiritually. He insisted that swaraj must begin with the individual: "Even in 1915, when I returned to India, I had not attained swaraj myself" (163-001). Political swaraj without personal swaraj would be, in his view, an empty shell.

## Definition

Gandhi distinguished between swaraj as political independence and swaraj as self-mastery. The latter was primary: a people incapable of governing their own passions could not genuinely govern their country. He believed that "the same process" of individual self-discipline would "also establish swaraj" at the national level (163-001). This meant that the means of achieving swaraj — satyagraha, nonviolence, constructive work — had to embody the values of swaraj itself. One could not achieve self-rule through methods that relied on hatred or violence.

## Origins and Influences

The term swaraj has deep roots in Indian political thought. Dadabhai Naoroji and Bal Gangadhar Tilak had popularized it as a demand for self-government within the British Empire. Gandhi adopted the term but deepened its meaning, drawing on the Bhagavad Gita's teaching of self-mastery and on Thoreau's essay on civil disobedience, which argued that the best government is that which governs least. His 1909 work *Hind Swaraj* (Indian Home Rule) articulated his distinctive vision: true self-rule meant not merely Indians ruling Indians but each Indian ruling himself or herself.

## Gandhi's Practice

### Personal Swaraj

Gandhi consistently presented himself as a fellow-seeker rather than an accomplished master of swaraj. He acknowledged his own ongoing struggle for self-mastery through brahmacharya, dietary discipline, and the practice of silence. His ashrams — Phoenix Settlement, Tolstoy Farm, Satyagraha Ashram — were laboratories for swaraj, communities where Indians could learn to govern themselves through manual labor, simple living, and collective decision-making.

### Political Swaraj

Gandhi transformed the Indian National Congress from an elite debating society into a mass movement oriented toward swaraj. He argued that "the absence of swaraj was the biggest wrong" facing India (166-008), subsuming all particular grievances into this overarching demand. The non-cooperation movement (1920–22) and civil disobedience campaigns were designed not merely to pressure the British but to build the Indian people's capacity for self-governance.

### Constructive Programme

Gandhi's constructive programme — promoting khadi (hand-spun cloth), village sanitation, Hindu-Muslim unity, and the abolition of untouchability — was as important to swaraj as political agitation. He believed that Indians needed to build the institutions of self-rule before they could fully inherit political power. The spinning wheel (charkha) became a symbol of this constructive swaraj.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Swaraj is the end toward which Gandhi's entire philosophical apparatus is directed. Truth is the ultimate reality, ahimsa is the means, and swaraj is the concrete social and political expression of their union. In this framework, "the experiment contains the key to swaraj" (128-006) — meaning that every act of satyagraha, every personal discipline, and every constructive effort is a microcosm of the self-rule being built.

## Key Passages
