Gandhi's adoption of the brahmacharya (celibacy) vow in 1906 at the Phoenix Settlement in South Africa was a landmark event in his spiritual evolution. He came to regard the vow as foundational to his development of satyagraha, and he reflected on it extensively in his autobiography.

## The Road to the Vow

Gandhi traced the seeds of his thinking on brahmacharya to his early conversations with Raychandbhai (Rajchandra), the Jain poet and philosopher whose influence he had described in earlier chapters. Raychandbhai challenged Gandhi's understanding of devotion by posing a revealing question: "Which of the two do you prize more — the love of Mrs. Gladstone for her husband as his wife, or her devoted service irrespective of her relation to Mr. Gladstone?" (449-2062). This question prompted Gandhi to examine the nature of his own marital relations.

He had long been "wedded to a monogamous ideal" and considered faithfulness to his wife as part of his commitment to truth. But in South Africa he "came to realize the importance of observing brahmacharya even with respect to my wife" (449-2062). The key question he asked himself: "Did my faithfulness consist in making my wife the instrument of my lust? So long as I was the slave of lust, my faithfulness was worth nothing" (449-2066).

## Taking the Vow

The formal vow was taken in 1906 at the Phoenix Settlement. Gandhi described the experience as transformative. Before the vow, he had been "open to being overcome by temptation at any moment. Now the vow was a sure shield against temptation. The great potentiality of brahmacharya daily became more and more patent to me" (452-2086).

Remarkably, within a month of his return to Johannesburg after taking the vow, "the foundation of satyagraha was laid." Gandhi reflected: "As though unknown to me, the brahmacharya vow had been preparing me for it. Satyagraha had not been a preconceived plan. It came on spontaneously, without my having willed it. But I could see that all my previous steps had led up to that goal" (452-2086).

## The Meaning and Practice

Gandhi defined brahmacharya broadly as "control of the senses in thought, word and deed" (455-2098). He emphasized that the observance extended far beyond physical abstinence. The distinction between a brahmachari and one who is not "ought to be clear as daylight. Both use their eyesight, but whereas the brahmachari uses it to see the glories of God, the other uses it to see the frivolity around him" (455-2097).

He acknowledged that perfect brahmacharya was an ideal that might remain out of reach for many: "Such brahmacharya is impossible of attainment by limited effort. For many it must remain only as an ideal. An aspirant after brahmacharya will always be conscious of his shortcomings, will seek out the passions lingering in the innermost recesses of his heart and will incessantly strive to get rid of them" (455-2098).

## Diet and Discipline

Gandhi observed that diet played a crucial role in the observance of brahmacharya. He noted that during his time in South Africa, when he lived on fruits and nuts alone, "brahmacharya needed no effort on my part." After resuming milk consumption, it became "a matter of very great effort" (455-2094). He was careful not to generalize this observation, noting that "the effect on brahmacharya of different kinds of food can be determined only after numerous experiments" (455-2094).

Fasting, he found, was also an indispensable aid, but only when the mind cooperated with the body: "Fasting is useful, when mind cooperates with starving body, that is to say, when it cultivates a distaste for the objects that are denied to the body. Mind is at the root of all sensuality" (455-2096).

## Legacy

Looking back on twenty years of the vow, Gandhi expressed "pleasure and wonderment." The practice of self-control had been gradually developing since 1901, but "the freedom and joy that came to me after taking the vow had never been experienced before 1906" (452-2086). He considered the vow's connection to satyagraha as providential, evidence that the spiritual discipline of brahmacharya had prepared the ground for his most significant contribution to political philosophy.
