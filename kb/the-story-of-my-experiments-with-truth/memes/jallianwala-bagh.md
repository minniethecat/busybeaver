# Jallianwala Bagh

Jallianwala Bagh is the public garden in Amritsar where British troops under General Dyer opened fire on an unarmed crowd on 13 April 1919, killing hundreds. For Gandhi, the massacre was a defining trauma of the British Raj — though he was personally blamed by some Punjabis for the martial law that preceded it — and he later led the Congress effort to build a national memorial at the site.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi was not present at the massacre, but its aftermath shaped his political trajectory. Sir Michael O'Dwyer, the Lieutenant Governor of the Punjab, held Gandhi responsible for the unrest that preceded the tragedy. At the same time, some young Punjabis blamed Gandhi for suspending civil disobedience, arguing that his decision had left the people defenseless and led to the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (035-004). Some even threatened him with assassination over this perceived failure.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

In the autobiography's account, Gandhi frames the Jallianwala Bagh massacre within the larger context of government repression during the Rowlatt Satyagraha. Leaders were arrested, martial law was declared, and what he called the "policy of lawless repression" manifested itself in the Punjab "in all its nakedness" (034-004). The massacre itself, which Gandhi notes "paled into insignificance" before the broader pattern of Punjab atrocities in his eyes, nevertheless became the symbolic center of national grief and outrage.

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Gandhi took personal responsibility for the Jallianwala Bagh memorial project. The Congress passed a resolution for it amid great enthusiasm, and a fund of approximately five lakhs was established. Gandhi was appointed one of the trustees, and the main responsibility for fundraising fell to him (038-006). This role — transforming a site of trauma into a memorial of national remembrance — reflected Gandhi's philosophy of converting suffering into constructive purpose.
