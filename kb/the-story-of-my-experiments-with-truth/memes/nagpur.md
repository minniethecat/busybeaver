# Nagpur

Nagpur is the city where the Indian National Congress held its annual session in 1920, at which Gandhi's resolution on non-cooperation was confirmed and the Congress constitution was revised. It marked a turning point in India's freedom struggle — the moment Gandhi's program of nonviolent resistance became the official policy of the Congress.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi attended the Nagpur Congress session in December 1920, having secured the endorsement of key leaders including Motilal Nehru and Deshbandhu Chittaranjan Das. The resolutions adopted at the Calcutta special session were brought to Nagpur for final confirmation, amid an unprecedented rush of visitors and delegates (043-003). The session cemented Gandhi's leadership of the Congress and formalized the transition from elite petitioning to mass mobilization.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

The Nagpur session confirmed two historic resolutions: the non-cooperation program — which included the triple boycott of law courts, legislative councils, and educational institutions — and the revision of the Congress constitution (043-004). The constitutional reforms, drafted by a subcommittee and first presented at the Calcutta special session, received thorough debate before final adoption at Nagpur (043-005). Sjt. C. Vijayaraghavachari served as President of this transformative session.

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Nagpur represents the moment Gandhi's vision for the Congress as a mass organization became reality. The constitutional changes he championed democratized the party's structure and opened it to ordinary Indians. For Gandhi personally, the session validated his philosophy of nonviolent non-cooperation as a viable political strategy on a national scale.

## Key Passages

> The resolutions adopted at the Calcutta special session of the Congress were to be confirmed at its annual session at Nagpur. Here again, as at Calcutta, there was a great rush of visitors and delegates. The number of delegates in the Congress had not been limited yet.
