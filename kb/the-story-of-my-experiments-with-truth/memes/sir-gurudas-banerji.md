# Sir Gurudas Banerji

Sir Gurudas Banerji (1844–1918) was a distinguished Indian judge of the Calcutta High Court and the first Indian Vice-Chancellor of the University of Calcutta. In Gandhi's narrative, he appears briefly but memorably — as a respected elder whose suggestion about humility led Gandhi to articulate one of his most important philosophical insights.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi sought Banerji's help for his work in South Africa during a visit to Calcutta [072-007]. Later, when Gandhi circulated the draft code of rules for the newly founded Sabarmati Ashram, Banerji responded with a suggestion that stayed in Gandhi's memory: he "suggested that humility should be added as one of the observances, as he believed that the younger generation sadly lacked humility" [133-009].

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in 1844 in Calcutta, Gurudas Banerji was educated at Presidency College and became one of the first Indians to achieve distinction in the legal profession under British rule. He served as a judge of the Calcutta High Court from 1888 to 1904 and was knighted for his services. He also served as the first Indian Vice-Chancellor of the University of Calcutta (1890–1892).

### Role in the Narrative

Banerji appears at two points. First, during Gandhi's Calcutta visit while lobbying for the South African Indian cause: "I met Justice Mitter and Sir Gurudas Banerji, whose help I wanted in my work in South Africa" [072-007].

His more significant contribution came through his response to the draft Ashram rules. Banerji's suggestion to include humility as an observance prompted Gandhi to a characteristically nuanced reflection: "I feared humility would cease to be humility the moment it became a matter of vow. The true connotation of humility is self-effacement. Self-effacement is moksha (salvation), and whilst it cannot, by itself, be an observance, there may be other observances necessary for its attainment" [133-009].

## Historical Significance

The exchange between Banerji and Gandhi captures a generational transmission of values from an elder of the Bengal Renaissance to the emerging leader of Indian nationalism. Banerji's concern about the "younger generation" lacking humility and Gandhi's dialectical response — that enforced humility is not humility at all — illuminate the tension between institutional discipline and spiritual authenticity that ran through Gandhi's ashram experiment.
