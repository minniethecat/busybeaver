# Lord Chelmsford

Frederic John Napier Thesiger, 1st Viscount Chelmsford (1868–1933), served as Viceroy of India from 1916 to 1921 — a period encompassing World War I, the Rowlatt Act protests, the Jallianwala Bagh massacre, and the launch of Gandhi's first all-India satyagraha. Despite being on opposite sides of the political divide, Chelmsford maintained personally cordial relations with Gandhi and proved responsive to individual grievances.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi described his relations with Lord Chelmsford as "cordial" [150-001]. Unlike some officials who kept Gandhi at arm's length, Chelmsford granted interviews, listened patiently, and on occasion acted decisively to remedy injustices. Their interactions ranged from the specific — removing an arbitrary customs cordon at Viramgam — to the profound — a prolonged discussion about the morality of Indian participation in World War I. Gandhi's respect for Chelmsford's personal sincerity persisted even as their political positions diverged sharply after the Rowlatt Act.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in 1868, Frederic Thesiger was educated at Winchester and Magdalen College, Oxford. He served as Governor of Queensland (1905–1909) and Governor of New South Wales (1909–1913) before being appointed Viceroy of India in 1916. His tenure coincided with some of the most turbulent years in the history of British India.

### Role in the Narrative

Chelmsford appears in Gandhi's autobiography at several critical junctures. When Gandhi discovered that an arbitrary customs cordon at Viramgam was subjecting travelers to harassment, he raised the matter with Chelmsford, who expressed astonishment — "He had known nothing of the matter" — and telephoned for the papers immediately. Within days, the Viramgam customs cordon was removed [127-014].

During the agitation for abolition of indentured emigration, Chelmsford initially refused permission for a bill introduced by Pandit Malaviyaji [135-005]. However, when Gandhi sought an interview, Chelmsford granted it immediately and "promised to be helpful" [135-006].

The most philosophically significant exchange occurred during World War I, when Gandhi hesitated to attend a war conference after learning of secret treaties between Britain and European powers. Chelmsford invited Gandhi to discuss the matter and engaged in a "prolonged discussion" [150-009]. His argument — that the Viceroy could not know everything done by the Cabinet, that the Empire had on the whole been a force for good, and that moral questions could be raised after the war — persuaded Gandhi to participate.

## Historical Significance

Lord Chelmsford's viceroyalty illustrates the complex interplay between reformist and repressive impulses in late colonial governance. He oversaw the Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (1919) that expanded Indian participation in government, yet his administration also passed the repressive Rowlatt Act that provoked Gandhi's first nationwide satyagraha. The Jallianwala Bagh massacre occurred on his watch. Chelmsford's cordial personal dealings with Gandhi stand in tension with the structural violence of the system he represented — a contradiction Gandhi himself navigated with strategic pragmatism.
