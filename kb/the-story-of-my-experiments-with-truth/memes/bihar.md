# Bihar

Bihar is a province in eastern India where [[mahatma-gandhi]] conducted his first major civil disobedience campaign in India — the [[champaran]] Satyagraha of 1917. The campaign against the oppressive *tinkathia* system of indigo cultivation established Gandhi's method of [[satyagraha]] on Indian soil and introduced him to the profound poverty and social rigidity of rural India.

## The Call to Champaran

Gandhi's connection to Bihar began at the Lucknow Congress of 1916, where a peasant named [[rajkumar-shukla]] approached him:

> "This man caught hold of me at Lucknow, where I had gone for the Congress of 1916. 'Vakil Babu will tell you everything about our distress,' he said, and urged me to go to Champaran." [136-004]

"Vakil Babu" was [[brajkishore-prasad]], who became Gandhi's esteemed coworker and whom Gandhi later described as "the soul of public work in Bihar" [136-004]. Despite initial hesitation, Gandhi was persuaded to investigate the plight of the indigo cultivators.

## Social Conditions

Bihar presented Gandhi with stark social realities. Untouchability was practiced with particular strictness:

> "There was strict untouchability in Bihar. I might not draw water at the well whilst the servants were using it, lest drops of water from my bucket might pollute them, the servants not knowing to what caste I belonged." [137-008]

Gandhi noted the irony of [[rajendra-prasad]]'s servants directing him to outdoor latrines, observing that "the servants were doing the duty which they thought Rajendra Babu would wish them to do" [137-008].

## The Legal Campaign

The Bihar vakils (lawyers) became crucial allies. [[brajkishore-prasad]] acquainted Gandhi with the facts of the tenants' cases. Gandhi soon concluded that litigation was futile:

> "Taking such cases to the courts does little good. Where the ryots are so crushed and fear-stricken, law courts are useless. The real relief for them is to be free from fear. We cannot sit still until we have driven *tinkathia* out of Bihar." [137-003]

Gandhi, who had initially thought the work might take two days, now realized "the work might take even two years" and declared himself prepared to give that time [137-003].

## Village Education

As Gandhi's experience of Bihar deepened, he recognized that permanent change required education:

> "I became convinced that work of a permanent nature was impossible without proper village education. The ryots' ignorance was pathetic." [140-003]

Children either roamed about or toiled on indigo plantations from morning to night — a male labourer earned no more than ten pice a day, a female six, and a child three [140-003]. This led Gandhi to establish primary schools in Champaran villages, the beginning of his lifelong commitment to basic education.

## The Bihari Character

Gandhi developed a deep affection for the Bihari people. Upon meeting [[brajkishore-prasad]] again in Champaran, he was struck by "his humility, simplicity, goodness and extraordinary faith, so characteristic of the Biharis" [137-011]. He soon felt "bound to this circle of friends in lifelong friendship" [137-011].
