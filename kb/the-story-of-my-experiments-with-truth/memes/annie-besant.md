# Annie Besant

Annie Besant (1847–1933) was a British socialist, theosophist, women's rights activist, and Indian nationalist. By the time Gandhi encountered her, she had journeyed from atheism to theosophy and from Fabian socialism to the leadership of India's Home Rule movement. She was, Gandhi noted, "very much in the limelight" in the London of his student days, and later became a significant figure in the Indian political landscape he navigated upon his return.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi's awareness of Annie Besant spanned three decades. As a law student in London, he followed with "great interest the controversy about her conversion" to Theosophy [020-003]. Her intellectual journey from atheism to theism "strengthened my aversion to atheism," and he read her book *How I Became a Theosophist* [020-008]. In India, Gandhi visited her in Benares when she was ill, out of respect [074-020]. Politically, he considered her exclusion from the Delhi War Conference a "grave blunder" and described her as among "the most powerful leaders of public opinion" [151-040]. At the Calcutta Congress session, she stood among the "imposing phalanx of veteran warriors" [166-007].

## Biography

### Early Life

Born Annie Wood in London in 1847, she married clergyman Frank Besant at twenty, separated from him over religious differences, and embarked on a remarkable public career. She was a prominent secularist and birth control advocate (prosecuted alongside Charles Bradlaugh), a Fabian socialist, and a labor organizer (co-leading the 1888 Matchgirls' Strike) before her conversion to Theosophy in 1889 under the influence of Madame Blavatsky.

### Role in the Narrative

Besant first enters Gandhi's story obliquely — as a public figure whose conversion from atheism to Theosophy registered in his young mind as evidence that spiritual hunger was real and legitimate [020-008]. When Gandhi visited the Blavatsky Lodge in London, Besant had "just then joined the Theosophical Society" [020-003].

Twenty-five years later, in India, their paths intersected more directly. Gandhi visited her in Benares to pay his respects, acknowledging her delicate health [074-020]. During World War I, when the Viceroy convened a War Conference without inviting Besant, Tilak, and the Ali brothers, Gandhi considered this exclusion a "grave blunder" — a mark of his respect for her political standing [151-040].

Her Home Rule agitation "had certainly touched the peasants" [149-005], contributing to the political awakening that Gandhi's own campaigns in Champaran and Kheda would deepen. At the 1917 Calcutta Congress, she stood as a veteran among veterans [166-007].

## Historical Significance

Annie Besant's trajectory — from secularist firebrand to Theosophist mystic to Indian nationalist leader — embodies the fluid intellectual currents of the late Victorian and Edwardian eras. Her election as President of the Indian National Congress in 1917 made her the first woman to hold that position and underscored the role of Theosophy as a bridge between Western spiritual seekers and Indian religious traditions. For Gandhi, Besant represented a model of the intellectually serious, spiritually committed public figure — a woman whose convictions led her across continents, ideologies, and faiths.
