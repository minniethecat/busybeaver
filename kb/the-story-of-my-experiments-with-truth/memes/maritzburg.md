# Maritzburg

Maritzburg (Pietermaritzburg) is a city in Natal, South Africa, where [[mahatma-gandhi]] experienced the defining incident of his early life — being thrown off a train at the railway station in 1893 for refusing to leave the first-class compartment despite holding a valid ticket. This moment of racial humiliation became the catalytic event that launched his career of resistance against injustice.

## The Train Incident

In 1893, the twenty-four-year-old Gandhi, newly arrived in South Africa, was travelling from [[durban]] to Pretoria on a first-class ticket. At Maritzburg station, a white passenger objected to the presence of a "coloured" man in the first-class compartment. When Gandhi refused to move to the van compartment, a railway official summoned a police constable, and Gandhi was "pushed out" of the train with his luggage [033-015].

> "It was winter, and winter in the higher regions of South Africa is severely cold. Maritzburg being at a high altitude, the cold was extremely bitter. My overcoat was in my luggage, but I did not dare to ask for it lest I should be insulted again, so I sat and shivered." [033-022]

## The Night of Decision

The night Gandhi spent in the cold waiting room of Maritzburg station became the turning point of his life:

> "I began to think of my duty. Should I fight for my rights, or go back to India, or go on to Pretoria without minding the insults, and return to India after finishing the case? It would be cowardice to run back to India without fulfilling my obligation." [033-025]

Gandhi resolved to stay and fight. "The hardship to which I was subjected was superficial — only a symptom of the deep disease of colour prejudice. I should try, if possible, to root out the disease and suffer hardships in the process" [034-001]. This decision — to confront injustice at its root rather than flee from its symptoms — became the foundation of [[satyagraha]].

## Historical Significance

The Maritzburg incident is often identified as the birthplace of Gandhi's political consciousness. Before Maritzburg, Gandhi was a young barrister concerned primarily with his professional career. After Maritzburg, he became an activist committed to fighting racial discrimination. The journey that began on that cold platform would eventually lead to the development of satyagraha as a method of non-violent resistance, applied first in South Africa and then in India.
