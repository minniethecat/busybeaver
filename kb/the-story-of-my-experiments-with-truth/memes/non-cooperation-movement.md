# Non-cooperation Movement

The Non-cooperation Movement (1920–22) was Gandhi's first nationwide campaign for Indian independence. Millions of Indians boycotted British institutions — schools, courts, legislatures, foreign cloth — in a coordinated campaign of nonviolent resistance. The movement transformed the Indian National Congress from an elite organization into a mass movement.

## Background

The movement was launched in response to the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (1919) and the perceived betrayal of the Khilafat cause. Gandhi persuaded the Congress to adopt non-cooperation as its strategy, arguing that British rule depended on Indian cooperation and could be undone by its withdrawal.

## Gandhi's Role

Gandhi designed the campaign's strategy and personally toured India mobilizing support. When violence broke out at Chauri Chaura in 1922, he suspended the entire movement — a controversial decision that demonstrated his absolute commitment to nonviolence over political expediency.

## Historical Significance

The Non-cooperation Movement marked India's transition from elite petitioning to mass politics. Though suspended, it established the pattern of waves of satyagraha that ultimately achieved independence.

## Key Passages

> Non-cooperation is a protest against an unwitting and unwilling cooperation with evil.
