# Ahimsa

Ahimsa (nonviolence or non-harming) is the foundational ethical principle of Gandhi's philosophy — the active refusal to harm any living being in thought, word, or deed. Gandhi considered it inseparable from the search for truth: "This ahimsa is the basis of the search for truth. I am realizing every day that the search is vain unless it is founded on ahimsa as the basis" (086-013).

## Definition

For Gandhi, ahimsa was not merely the absence of physical violence but a comprehensive and active principle. "Ahimsa is a comprehensive principle. We are helpless mortals caught in the conflagration of himsa. The saying that life lives on life has a deep meaning in it" (116-004). He recognized that perfect nonviolence was impossible for embodied beings — the very acts of eating, drinking, and moving involve some destruction of life — but insisted that the votary of ahimsa must constantly strive to minimize harm. The principle extended beyond physical action to encompass thought and speech; Gandhi became increasingly vigilant about both as his understanding matured (051-006).

## Origins and Influences

Ahimsa has deep roots in Indian religious traditions, particularly Jainism, Buddhism, and Hinduism. Gandhi was exposed to these influences from childhood in Gujarat, where Jain traditions of nonviolence were strong. However, he transformed ahimsa from a primarily personal spiritual discipline into a powerful political method. His early experiences with vegetarianism in London, his reading of Tolstoy's Christian pacifism, and Ruskin's social ethics all contributed to his developing understanding. The concept crystallized through his practical experiments in South Africa, where he discovered that nonviolence could serve as a form of collective resistance.

## Gandhi's Practice

### First Experiments

Gandhi's earliest conscious applications of ahimsa were in the domestic sphere. In South Africa, he began to connect the personal ethic with political action. His participation in the Boer War and later World War I created tensions with his commitment to ahimsa, prompting deep reflection. When challenged about his wartime service, he acknowledged that "participation in war could never be consistent with ahimsa. But it is not always given to one to be equally clear about one's duty" (116-003).

### Evolution

As Gandhi's conception of ahimsa matured, it became the defining lens through which he evaluated all actions. He recognized that ahimsa was not passive but an "attribute of ahimsa" to resist systems while respecting persons: "It is quite proper to resist and attack a system, but to resist and attack its author is tantamount to resisting and attacking oneself" (086-013). This insight — separating the sin from the sinner — became a hallmark of satyagraha campaigns.

### Key Applications

Gandhi applied ahimsa across multiple domains:
- **Diet**: Vegetarianism and dietary restraint as expressions of nonviolence
- **Speech**: Vigilance against violent or hostile language, even in patriotic anthems (051-007)
- **Politics**: Satyagraha campaigns as the practical application of ahimsa to collective grievances
- **War**: Wrestling with the tension between nonviolence and perceived duty during the Boer War and WWI (116-001)

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Ahimsa is the means by which Gandhi pursued Truth. While Truth (Satya) was the goal, ahimsa was "the basis of the search for truth" (086-013). The two are inseparable: violence clouds perception and makes truth unreachable, while nonviolence clears the path. Ahimsa connects Gandhi's personal disciplines (Brahmacharya, fasting, dietary control) to his political method (Satyagraha) and his ultimate goal (Swaraj). His love for the people was, in his own assessment, "nothing but an expression of my unshakable faith in ahimsa" (138-013).
