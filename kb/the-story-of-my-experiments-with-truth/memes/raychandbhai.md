# Raychandbhai

Raychandbhai (Rajchandra Ravjibhai Mehta) was a Jain poet, diamond merchant, and spiritual seeker whom Gandhi acknowledged as one of the three most important influences on his life. Gandhi met him in Bombay upon returning from England in 1891 and was deeply impressed by his spiritual depth, poetic genius, and encyclopedic knowledge of religious traditions. Though Raychandbhai died young at thirty-four, his influence on Gandhi's spiritual development was lasting.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi met Raychandbhai shortly after returning from London, during a period of religious questioning. He was immediately drawn to Raychandbhai's combination of spiritual depth and worldly competence — a successful businessman who was also a poet and a seeker. Their conversations covered religion, ethics, and the nature of the soul. When Gandhi faced religious doubts during his years in South Africa, he wrote to Raychandbhai for guidance. Gandhi later said that Raychandbhai's influence, along with Tolstoy's *The Kingdom of God Is Within You* and Ruskin's *Unto This Last*, were the three most important intellectual and spiritual influences on his life.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born in 1867 in Vavania, Gujarat, into a Jain family, Rajchandra showed extraordinary intellectual and spiritual gifts from childhood. He composed poetry from an early age, earning the title "Kavi" (poet). Despite his spiritual inclinations, he successfully ran the family jewelry business in Bombay.

### Role in the Narrative

Raychandbhai appears in the autobiography primarily in the early chapters dealing with Gandhi's return to India and his religious questioning. His combination of Jain philosophy, spiritual practice, and practical engagement with the world provided Gandhi with a model quite different from the ascetic renouncer — a model of spirituality integrated into active life.

## Historical Significance

Raychandbhai's influence on Gandhi represents the often-overlooked Jain contribution to Gandhian thought. The Jain emphasis on *ahimsa* (nonviolence) as the supreme religious duty, *anekantavada* (the many-sidedness of truth), and the integration of spiritual practice with worldly life all entered Gandhi's philosophy partly through his relationship with Raychandbhai.

## Key Passages

> I have often said that three persons have influenced me deeply: Tolstoy, Ruskin, and Raychandbhai.
