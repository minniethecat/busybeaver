# Junagadh

Junagadh is a princely state in the Kathiawad region of Gujarat that figures in Gandhi's family history and his early life. It was to Junagadh that Gandhi's grandfather, Uttamchand Gandhi, fled when state intrigues forced him from his Diwan post in Porbandar. Years later, a Junagadh vakil (lawyer) named Tryambakrai Mazmudar became young Gandhi's traveling companion on his first voyage to England.

## Gandhi's Connection

Junagadh is connected to Gandhi through two generations. His grandfather Uttamchand Gandhi — "a man of principle" — served as Diwan there after political intrigues compelled him to leave Porbandar (001-001). A generation later, in 1888, the eighteen-year-old Gandhi found passage to England in the company of Sjt. Tryambakrai Mazmudar, "the Junagadh vakil" who was traveling to be called to the bar (012-012).

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

**The Grandfather's Exile**: Uttamchand Gandhi, Gandhi's grandfather, was Diwan of Porbandar before state intrigues forced him to seek refuge in Junagadh. There, in a gesture that became family legend, he saluted the Nawab of Junagadh with his left hand. When asked about the apparent discourtesy, he explained: "The right hand is already pledged to Porbandar" (001-001). The story, which opens the autobiography, was meant to illustrate the family's tradition of principled loyalty.

**The Traveling Companion**: In 1888, as Gandhi anxiously prepared for his journey to England to study law, he learned that "a Junagadh vakil was going to England, to be called to the bar there" (012-012). His family's friends secured him a berth in the same cabin as Sjt. Tryambakrai Mazmudar and commended the young Gandhi to his care. Mazmudar, "an experienced man of mature age" who "knew the world," reassured Gandhi's friends not to worry about the "stripling of eighteen" (012-012).

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Junagadh represents generational continuity in the Gandhi family's tradition of state service. The grandfather's principled stand — remaining loyal to Porbandar even while serving Junagadh — prefigured Gandhi's own insistence on truth and loyalty above convenience. The Junagadh vakil's companionship on Gandhi's first voyage to England also gave the young man a crucial anchor of respectability and experience, smoothing the path that would transform him from a Kathiawad youth into a London-trained barrister.
