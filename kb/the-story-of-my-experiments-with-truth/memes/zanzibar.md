# Zanzibar

Zanzibar is an island port in East Africa (now part of Tanzania) where Gandhi's ship from [[bombay]] made an extended stopover of eight to ten days during his first voyage to [[south-africa]] in 1893. The long halt at Zanzibar proved unexpectedly consequential: it was here that Gandhi first visited a colonial court and realized how unprepared he was for the legal work awaiting him, prompting a crash course in bookkeeping that would prove essential to his first case.

## Gandhi's Connection

After calling at Lamu and Mombasa, Gandhi's ship reached Zanzibar where "the halt here was a long one — eight or ten days — and we then changed to another boat" [031-010]. Taking advantage of the extended stop, Gandhi "took rooms in the town and saw a good deal by wandering about the neighbourhood" [031-013].

The lush vegetation of the island amazed him. He wrote: "Only Malabar can give any idea of the luxuriant vegetation of Zanzibar. I was amazed at the gigantic trees and the size of the fruits" [031-013]. For a young man from Gujarat who had already spent three years in London, Zanzibar offered a first encounter with the tropical abundance of the East African coast.

## In the Narrative

### The Courtroom Awakening

During his days in Zanzibar, Gandhi decided to visit the local court to observe the legal work. What he saw there triggered a crisis of confidence: "A Parsi lawyer was examining a witness and asking him questions regarding credit and debit entries in account books. It was all Greek to me" [033-004].

Gandhi realized with alarm that he lacked the most basic professional knowledge required for the case awaiting him in South Africa: "Bookkeeping I had learnt neither at school nor during my stay in England. And the case for which I had come to South Africa was mainly about accounts. Only one who knew accounts could understand and explain it" [033-004].

### The Crash Course

The visit to the Zanzibar court proved a crucial wake-up call. The clerk from [[dada-abdulla]]'s firm, who was travelling with Gandhi, began explaining the case — but his talk of debits and credits only deepened Gandhi's confusion: "I did not know what a P. Note meant. I failed to find the word in the dictionary. I revealed my ignorance to the clerk, and learned from him that a P. Note meant a promissory note" [033-004].

Gandhi responded with characteristic diligence. He "purchased a book on bookkeeping and studied it. That gave me some confidence. I understood the case" [033-004]. He emerged from the Zanzibar interlude prepared enough to tell Abdulla Sheth, upon arriving in Durban, that he "was prepared to go to Pretoria" to handle the case.

### Later Connections

Years later, on his way back to South Africa from India, Gandhi met one of the Tyabji family members at Zanzibar, who "also promised to come and help me, but never came" [052-012]. Gandhi wryly noted that "Mr. Abbas Tyabji is atoning for that offence" — a reference to Tyabji's later dedication to the [[khadi]] movement.

## Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Zanzibar represents a recurring pattern in Gandhi's life: a moment of humbling self-awareness followed by disciplined self-education. Just as his London shyness forced him to develop inner resources, his Zanzibar courtroom shock pushed him to acquire the practical knowledge — bookkeeping — that would allow him to handle [[dada-abdulla]]'s case successfully. That case, in turn, brought him to [[pretoria]] and set in motion the chain of events that would transform him from a struggling lawyer into a political leader.
