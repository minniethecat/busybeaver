Gandhi's first meeting with Gopal Krishna Gokhale at Fergusson College grounds in Poona was one of the most significant events of his early political career. Gokhale, already a towering figure in Indian public life, became Gandhi's political mentor and one of his most deeply respected associates.

## The First Meeting

Gandhi met Gokhale on the Fergusson College grounds in Poona during his visit to India to mobilize support for the Indian cause in South Africa. The meeting made an immediate and lasting impression. Gokhale "gave me an affectionate welcome, and his manner immediately won my heart" (403-1844). Though it was their first meeting, Gandhi felt "as though we were renewing an old friendship."

In a memorable comparison, Gandhi characterized the major political figures of the time: "Sir Pherozeshah had seemed to me like the Himalaya, the Lokamanya like the ocean. But Gokhale was as the Ganges. One could have a refreshing bath in the holy river" (403-1844). The Himalaya was unscalable and the ocean intimidating, but "the Ganges invited one to its bosom."

Gokhale examined Gandhi's credentials thoroughly, "as a schoolmaster would examine a candidate seeking admission to a school" (403-1844). He guided Gandhi on whom to approach and reviewed his speech. Gandhi left the meeting "exultantly happy," and reflected that in politics, "the place that Gokhale occupied in my heart during his lifetime and occupies even now was and is absolutely unique" (403-1844).

## Living with Gokhale

During a later stay at Gokhale's home, Gandhi experienced Gokhale's way of life firsthand. From the very first day, Gokhale treated Gandhi "as though I were his younger brother," attending to all his needs and expressing admiration for Gandhi's habits of self-help, personal cleanliness, perseverance, and regularity (517-2393).

Gokhale introduced Gandhi to prominent figures, including Dr. P. C. Ray, whom he described as a scientist who "having a monthly salary of Rs. 800, keeps just Rs. 40 for himself and devotes the balance to public purposes" and who refused to marry (517-2393).

Watching Gokhale at work was both a joy and an education. "He never wasted a minute. His private relations and friendships were all for public good. All his talks had reference only to the good of the country and were absolutely free from any trace of untruth or insincerity" (517-2394). India's poverty and subjection were Gokhale's constant and intense concern. To those who sought his involvement in various causes, his consistent reply was: "What I want is freedom for my country. After that is won, we can think of other things" (517-2395).

## Returning to India

After Gandhi's return to India in 1915, Gokhale immediately sought to support him. Gokhale assured Gandhi that he would cover the expenses of establishing an Ashram in Gujarat: "You must look to me for the expenses of the Ashram, which I will regard as my own" (535-4051). Gokhale was also keen that Gandhi join the Servants of India Society, though the other members were hesitant due to differences in ideals and methods.

On the eve of Gandhi's departure for Shantiniketan, Gokhale arranged a farewell gathering despite being too ill to walk. He insisted on attending, but fainted and had to be carried away — a "fainting fit" that Gandhi noted "was to be no common event in my life" (536-4053), foreshadowing Gokhale's death shortly thereafter.
