# London

London was the city where the young Gandhi first encountered Western civilization on its own ground. He arrived in 1888 at age eighteen to study law at the Inner Temple, and his three years there transformed him from a provincial Indian youth into a cosmopolitan thinker. It was in London that vegetarianism became a conscious ethical choice rather than a cultural habit, and where Gandhi began his experiments with religious and philosophical inquiry.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi arrived in London with a vow to his mother to abstain from meat, alcohol, and women. The vegetarian vow, which he initially found burdensome, became a lifeline. Finding a vegetarian restaurant led him to the London Vegetarian Society, where he met ethical vegetarians like Henry Salt and Dr. Allinson. He read widely — the Bhagavad Gita in English translation, the Bible, theosophical literature — and began the comparative religious study that would define his later philosophy. He qualified as a barrister in 1891 and returned to India.

## In the Narrative

London occupies several chapters in the autobiography's first part. Gandhi's struggles with English customs, his experiments with dancing and elocution lessons, his encounter with vegetarianism as an ethical movement, and his religious explorations all took place in London. The city was his first laboratory for cross-cultural adaptation and moral experimentation.
