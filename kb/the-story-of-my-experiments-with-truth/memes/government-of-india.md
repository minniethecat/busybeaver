# The Government of India

The Government of India — the British colonial administration that ruled the subcontinent from 1858 to 1947 — is the omnipresent institutional actor in Gandhi's autobiography. It was simultaneously his adversary in political campaigns, the law-giver whose statutes he challenged through [[satyagraha]], and the administrative machinery within which he sometimes sought reform rather than abolition. Understanding Gandhi's relationship with the colonial state is essential to grasping the strategic logic of his nonviolence.

## Structure and Power

The Government of India was a vast bureaucracy headed by a Viceroy appointed by the British Crown. Below the Viceroy were provincial governors, district collectors, and an elaborate hierarchy of civil servants — the famed Indian Civil Service (ICS). The system rested on three pillars:

| Pillar | Function | Gandhi's Target |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| Executive (Viceroy, Governors) | Policy-making and administration | Petitions, deputations, civil disobedience |
| Legislative (Imperial Council) | Law-making with limited Indian representation | Boycott of councils during non-cooperation |
| Judiciary (High Courts, district courts) | Rule of law enforcement | Court boycotts, parallel arbitration |

Gandhi's own career began within this system. He studied law at the Inner Temple in [[london]], practiced briefly at the Bombay High Court, and for much of his early life sought justice through colonial legal institutions. His shift from reformer to resister was gradual — and his relationship with the Raj remained more complex than simple opposition.

## Gandhi's Engagement

### Phase 1: The Loyal Subject (1893–1919)

In [[south-africa]], Gandhi consistently appealed to the British government — in Natal, in London, and in India — as a loyal subject demanding that imperial ideals of justice be honored. He organized ambulance corps during the [[boer-war]] (1899–1902) and the Zulu Rebellion (1906), believing that Indian loyalty would earn equal citizenship. He accepted British war medals and addressed Viceroys with deference.

Even after returning to India in 1915, Gandhi initially sought cooperation. His [[champaran-satyagraha]] was directed not against the Government of India but against European indigo planters — and the Government, under pressure, eventually appointed Gandhi to the official inquiry committee.

### Phase 2: The Rebel (1919–1922)

The [[rowlatt-satyagraha]] (1919) marked the turning point. The Rowlatt Act, which extended wartime emergency powers into peacetime, convinced Gandhi that the Government of India was structurally unjust — that it would never voluntarily relinquish power. The subsequent [[non-cooperation-movement]] (1920–1922) was Gandhi's first all-India campaign of systematic non-cooperation with colonial institutions.

### Phase 3: The Negotiator (1922–1931)

After suspending non-cooperation following the Chauri Chaura violence, Gandhi entered a period of political withdrawal and constructive work. But he re-emerged as the Congress's chief negotiator during the [[round-table-conference]] in London, where he represented the Congress before the British Government. These encounters taught him that the Empire would negotiate endlessly but concede nothing substantial without mass pressure.

## The Administration as Foil

The Government of India's responses to Gandhi's campaigns form a pattern in the autobiography:

| Campaign | Government Response | Gandhi's Counter |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| [[champaran-satyagraha]] | Ordered Gandhi to leave the district | Disobeyed order; Government backed down |
| [[rowlatt-satyagraha]] | Enacted repressive law; Jallianwala Bagh massacre | Nationwide hartal; eventually suspended |
| [[non-cooperation-movement]] | Arrests of thousands | Mass jail-going; suspended after Chauri Chaura |
| Salt Satyagraha | Arrest of Gandhi and Congress leadership | Dandi March; 60,000 arrests nationwide |

Through each confrontation, Gandhi refined his understanding of the colonial state — not as a malevolent force but as a system whose legitimacy rested on Indian cooperation. Withdraw that cooperation, he argued, and the Raj would crumble.
