# Abdulla Sheth

Abdulla Sheth (also known as Dada Abdulla) was the Gujarati merchant whose commercial lawsuit first brought [[mahatma-gandhi]] to [[south-africa]] in 1893. A wealthy, self-made Memon trader in [[durban]], he was the central figure in the case of *Dada Abdulla & Co. vs. Tyeb Sheth* — a dispute over £40,000 that introduced Gandhi to the legal and racial complexities of Natal. More than a client, Abdulla Sheth became Gandhi's first host, cultural mediator, and early supporter in a land where Indians faced systematic discrimination.

## Role in Gandhi's South Africa Journey

### The Fateful Invitation

In 1893, Gandhi — then a struggling young lawyer in [[bombay]] and [[rajkot]] — was approached through his brother by Sheth Abdul Karim Jhaveri, a partner of Dada Abdulla & Co. The firm needed an Indian barrister to assist their English counsel in a major commercial case in Pretoria. The terms: £105 in compensation, first-class return fare, and all expenses paid — a handsome offer for a fledgling lawyer with few prospects (030-011).

Gandhi accepted the one-year engagement, seeing it less as a legal opportunity than as a chance to see a new country. He could not have known that it would become a twenty-one-year sojourn.

### First Impressions of Durban

When Gandhi's ship arrived at [[durban]], Abdulla Sheth was at the quay to receive him. Gandhi immediately noticed how Indians were treated:

> As the ship arrived at the quay and I watched the people coming on board to meet their friends, I observed that the Indians were not held in much respect. I could not fail to notice a sort of snobbishness about the manner of those who knew Abdulla Sheth, and it yet stung me.
>
> (032-001)

Abdulla Sheth himself was puzzled by the young barrister — dressed in Western clothes but culturally Indian, fluent in English but a vegetarian, a lawyer but seemingly inexperienced. "He thought his brother had sent him a white elephant," Gandhi recalled (032-002). Yet he quickly recognized Gandhi's value.

### Cultural Mediation and Practical Wisdom

Though "practically unlettered," Abdulla Sheth "had a rich fund of experience" and "an acute intellect" (032-003). He had learned just enough English to conduct all his business — banking, legal correspondence, trade with European merchants — through sheer practical intelligence. He became Gandhi's first guide to South African racial etiquette.

When Gandhi wanted to wear an English hat instead of his Indian turban after the famous Durban court incident, Abdulla Sheth dissuaded him with characteristic pragmatism:

> "If you do anything of the kind, it will have a very bad effect. You will compromise those insisting on wearing Indian turbans. And an Indian turban sits well on your head. If you wear an English hat, you will pass for a waiter."
>
> (032-011)

Gandhi noted the "practical wisdom, patriotism and a little bit of narrowness in this advice" (032-012). It was a formative moment — Abdulla Sheth taught Gandhi that personal choices carried political meaning for the entire Indian community.

## The Legal Case

The commercial dispute between Dada Abdulla and his relative Tyeb Sheth (033-009) involved complex account books and a claim of approximately £40,000. It required Gandhi to travel from Durban to [[pretoria]], the seat of the Transvaal Supreme Court.

After the infamous train incident at Pietermaritzburg station — where Gandhi was thrown off the train for being "colored" in a first-class compartment — Abdulla Sheth immediately intervened. He met with the General Manager of the Railways to protest the treatment, securing assurances that Gandhi would not be further harassed (033-025). This was Gandhi's first experience of organized Indian resistance to racial discrimination — and Abdulla Sheth was its agent.

## Legacy

The case was ultimately settled out of court through Gandhi's mediation, saving both parties the ruinous costs of prolonged litigation. Abdulla Sheth, impressed by Gandhi's ability and integrity, became one of his earliest supporters. When Gandhi's year-long contract ended and he prepared to return to India, it was the Indian merchants of Natal — led by Abdulla Sheth — who persuaded him to stay and take up the cause of the Indian community.
