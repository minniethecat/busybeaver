# Leo Tolstoy

Leo Tolstoy was the Russian novelist and Christian philosopher whose later writings on nonviolence, civil disobedience, and the moral illegitimacy of the state had a profound influence on Gandhi. Gandhi acknowledged Tolstoy as one of his three most important influences, alongside Ruskin and Raychandbhai. The two corresponded in the final year of Tolstoy's life, and Gandhi named his second South African ashram "Tolstoy Farm" in his honor.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi encountered Tolstoy's writings during his formative years in South Africa. Tolstoy's *The Kingdom of God Is Within You* — an argument for nonviolent resistance to evil based on Jesus's teachings in the Sermon on the Mount — provided philosophical grounding for the satyagraha Gandhi was already practicing. They exchanged letters in 1909–1910, with Tolstoy expressing admiration for Gandhi's work in South Africa. Tolstoy Farm, established near Johannesburg in 1910, was Gandhi's tribute to the Russian sage.

## Biography

### Early Life

Born into Russian aristocracy in 1828, Tolstoy achieved worldwide fame as the author of *War and Peace* and *Anna Karenina*. In his later years, he underwent a profound spiritual crisis that led him to embrace a radical Christian anarchism, rejecting the state, private property, and organized religion while insisting on nonviolent resistance to evil.

### Role in the Narrative

Tolstoy appears in the autobiography as a philosophical mentor rather than a personal acquaintance. His ideas — particularly the rejection of violence, the dignity of manual labor, and the corrupting nature of the state — shaped Gandhi's evolving philosophy. The letter exchange between the two men, occurring near the end of Tolstoy's life, represents a passing of the torch from the elder prophet of nonviolence to the younger.

## Historical Significance

Tolstoy's influence on Gandhi represents one of the most consequential intellectual transmissions in modern history — the Christian anarchist tradition passing to the Indian independence movement. Through Gandhi, Tolstoy's ideas about nonviolent resistance reached Martin Luther King Jr., Nelson Mandela, and movements for nonviolent change worldwide.
