# Compassion in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Compassion was for Gandhi not a sentiment but a discipline — the active extension of [[ahimsa]] from principle to practice. His autobiography reveals a man who was acutely aware of his own failures of compassion and who measured spiritual progress by the capacity to feel the suffering of others as one's own.

## Gandhi's Definition

Gandhi's understanding of compassion is most clearly revealed in a conversation about the comparative merits of Jesus and the Buddha. He argued that the Buddha's compassion surpassed even Christ's in its universality:

> "Look at Gautama's compassion!" said I. "It was not confined to mankind, it was extended to all living beings. Does not one's heart overflow with love to think of the lamb joyously perched on his shoulders? One fails to notice this love for all living beings in the life of Jesus."
>
> (040-009)

This passage, drawn from Gandhi's reading of Arnold's *Light of Asia*, reveals his conviction that true compassion must be universal — it cannot stop at the boundary of the human species. The image of the Buddha carrying a lamb on his shoulders was, for Gandhi, the perfect symbol of a love that embraced all sentient life.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's compassion was nurtured by the Jain traditions of his native Gujarat, where the principle of non-harm to all living beings was a living practice. His mother Putlibai's saintliness included an instinctive care for all creatures, and the Vaishnava temples she visited taught reverence for life.

But Gandhi's compassion was also tested and refined through his own moral failures. The most striking example is his participation in secret meat-eating as a boy — an act that required him to suppress his natural compassion:

> I got over my dislike for bread, forswore my compassion for the goats, and became a relisher of meat-dishes, if not of meat itself.
>
> (006-006)

The phrase "forswore my compassion" is remarkable: Gandhi understood that eating meat required not merely a change of diet but a willful suppression of his natural sympathy for animals. The recovery of that suppressed compassion would later become one of the driving forces behind his commitment to [[vegetarianism]].

## Gandhi's Practice

### Failures of Compassion

Gandhi is remarkably honest about his failures of compassion, particularly in his relationship with his wife Kasturba. In one painful episode, he describes how anger caused his compassion to fail entirely:

> She shouted back: "Keep your house to yourself and let me go." I forgot myself, and the spring of compassion dried up in me. I caught her by the hand, dragged the helpless woman to the gate, which was just opposite the ladder, and proceeded to open it with the intention of pushing her out. The tears were running down her cheeks in torrents.
>
> (074-005)

This confession is devastating in its honesty. The "spring of compassion dried up" — Gandhi acknowledges that his capacity for compassion was not a permanent possession but a resource that could be depleted by anger and self-righteousness. The image of Kasturba weeping at the gate stands as a permanent rebuke to any claim of perfected virtue.

### Compassion and Vegetarianism

Gandhi's vegetarianism was the most consistent outward expression of his compassion. Unlike those who abstained from meat for health or custom, Gandhi's vegetarianism was rooted in the conviction that animals were beings worthy of moral consideration:

> The conviction that it was wrong to take animal life for the satisfaction of the human palate grew upon me.
>
> (014-001)

This conviction deepened over time, eventually extending to a refusal to take milk — on the grounds that the treatment of cows and buffaloes in Indian dairies was itself a form of violence.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Compassion is the emotional foundation without which the other virtues become cold abstractions:

- **[[ahimsa]]**: Compassion is the positive content of ahimsa — not merely refraining from harm but actively loving and protecting the vulnerable.
- **[[nonviolence]]**: Without compassion, nonviolence becomes mere political technique; with compassion, it becomes a way of being.
- **[[service]]**: Compassion drives service — the sight of suffering compels action. Gandhi's service to indentured labourers was born of compassion for their condition.
- **[[vegetarianism]]**: The most consistent daily practice of compassion toward animals.
- **[[truth]]**: Compassion and truth are linked — Gandhi believed that cruelty often began with self-deception.
