# Charlestown

Charlestown was the railway terminus in northern Natal where Gandhi arrived on the morning after the infamous Maritzburg incident. It was here that he encountered a second act of discrimination — the stagecoach agent refused him passage to Johannesburg — deepening his resolve to challenge racial injustice in South Africa.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi reached Charlestown by train on the morning after being thrown off at Pietermaritzburg station. In those days, there was no railway line between Charlestown and Johannesburg; the journey continued by stagecoach, which halted overnight at Standerton (009-004). Gandhi held a valid ticket for the coach, but the agent used a pretext to deny him a seat.

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

The train reached Charlestown in the morning. Gandhi possessed a ticket for the connecting stagecoach, which had not been cancelled despite his interrupted journey at Maritzburg. However, the coach agent, "only needing a pretext for putting me off," discovered that Gandhi had been delayed and used this as grounds for refusing him passage (009-004). This compounding of indignities — the train ejection followed by the coach refusal — exemplified the systematic nature of racial discrimination in colonial South Africa.

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

Charlestown represents the cumulative nature of Gandhi's early South African experiences. Each incident of discrimination reinforced the previous one, building toward his eventual determination to resist. The sequence — Durban → Maritzburg → Charlestown → Standerton → Johannesburg — traces the geography of Gandhi's political awakening.

## Key Passages

> The train reached Charlestown in the morning. There was no railway, in those days, between Charlestown and Johannesburg, but only a stagecoach, which halted at Standerton for the night en route.
