# Paris

Paris was the first continental European city Gandhi visited, during a seven-day trip from London in 1890. The visit left a lasting impression — not for the Great Exhibition's technological marvels, but for the ancient cathedrals whose "grandeur and peacefulness" revealed to him a dimension of Christian devotion he had not encountered in England.

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi traveled to Paris in 1890, during his final year as a law student in London, motivated by a keen desire to see both the Great Exhibition and the Eiffel Tower — then a controversial structure, "constructed entirely of iron, and nearly 1,000 feet high," whose very possibility had been doubted by engineers. He managed the journey economically, doing most of his sightseeing on foot with the help of maps, and stayed at a vegetarian restaurant. (740-001)

## In the Narrative

### The Churches and the Eiffel Tower

The ancient churches of Paris made a deeper impression on Gandhi than the Exhibition's technological displays. He recalled Notre Dame's "wonderful construction" and "elaborate decoration of the interior with its beautiful sculptures," and reflected that "those who expended millions on such divine cathedrals could not but have the love of God in their hearts." The contrast between the churches' peace and the streets' frivolity became for him evidence of a genuine spiritual hunger beneath the surface of modern life. (746-001) (748-001)

### A Lesson in Religious Devotion

Watching worshippers kneel before the Virgin Mary transformed Gandhi's understanding of Christian practice. He concluded that "all this kneeling and prayer could not be mere superstition; the devout souls kneeling before the Virgin could not be worshipping mere marble." This insight — that image worship could express genuine devotion to the divine through its symbol — later informed his defense of Hindu murti-puja against Christian missionary criticism. (748-001)

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Paris visit contributed to Gandhi's growing appreciation of religious pluralism. His observation that Christian worshippers "were fired with genuine devotion and they worshipped not stone, but the divinity of which it was symbolic" prefigured his mature teaching that all religions were paths to the same truth. (748-001)

## Key Passages

> The ancient churches of Paris are still in my memory. Their grandeur and their peacefulness are unforgettable. The wonderful construction of Notre Dame and the elaborate decoration of the interior with its beautiful sculptures cannot be forgotten. (746-001)
