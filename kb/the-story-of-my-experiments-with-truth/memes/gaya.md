# Gaya

Gaya is a city in Bihar, significant in Gandhi's autobiography as the home of "Gaya Babu" — a well-known local vakil (lawyer) who hosted Gandhi during his 1917 Champaran mission. After arriving at Muzaffarpur and staying briefly at Professor Malkani's quarters, Gandhi was persuaded to move to Gaya Babu's residence, where he and his family "showered all their affection" on their guest (137-006).

## Gandhi's Connection

Gandhi's connection to Gaya came through the network of local supporters who assisted his Champaran inquiry. After word spread of his arrival at Muzaffarpur, local vakils (lawyers) approached him, expressing both their fear of Government reprisal and their desire to help. They insisted that Professor Malkani's quarters were unsuitable for Gandhi's work and urged him to move to Gaya Babu's house (137-005).

## In the Narrative

### Key Events at This Location

**The Invitation**: Shortly after Gandhi's midnight arrival at Muzaffarpur, a local emissary approached him at Professor Malkani's quarters. The messenger acknowledged the political sensitivity of Gandhi's presence: "I confess we are all afraid of Government, but we shall render what help we can" (137-005). He confirmed that most of Rajkumar Shukla's account of peasant exploitation was true and reported that telegrams had been sent to Babu Brajkishore Prasad and Babu Rajendra Prasad — two influential Bihar leaders — to join the effort.

**The Host's Generosity**: Gandhi initially hesitated, "for fear of embarrassing Gaya Babu" (137-006), aware that associating with him carried risk. But Gaya Babu's warmth overcame Gandhi's reluctance. Gandhi recorded simply: "He put me at ease, and so I went over to stay with him. He and his people showered all their affection on me" (137-006).

### Significance to Gandhi's Journey

The Gaya episode illustrates the quiet courage of provincial professionals during the early satyagraha period. Men like Gaya Babu — established lawyers with reputations to protect — nevertheless risked Government displeasure to support Gandhi's investigation of indigo planters. Their moral support was as crucial to the Champaran campaign as the testimony of the peasants themselves. It also shows Gandhi's growing ability to attract local allies who could provide infrastructure — lodging, introductions, local knowledge — for his inquiries.
