## Background

While the Kheda Satyagraha campaign was still in progress, the deadly war in Europe continued. A critical moment arrived when the Viceroy, Lord Chelmsford, invited various Indian leaders to a war conference in Delhi. Gandhi, who had cordial relations with the Viceroy, was urged to attend [079-001].

## The Moral Dilemma

Before the conference, Gandhi faced a moral challenge. His friend Charles (Dinabandhu) Andrews raised a question about the morality of participating in the war conference. Andrews told Gandhi of a controversy in the British press regarding secret treaties between England and Italy, asking how Gandhi could participate if England had entered into secret treaties with another European power [079-003].

Taking Andrews's word seriously, Gandhi addressed a letter to Lord Chelmsford explaining his hesitation. The Viceroy invited him to discuss the question personally.

## The Viceroy's Argument

In a prolonged discussion with Lord Chelmsford and his Private Secretary Mr. Maffey, Gandhi was persuaded to attend. The Viceroy's argument was pragmatic:

> "Surely you do not believe that the Viceroy knows everything done by the British Cabinet. I do not claim, no one claims, that the British Government is infallible. But if you agree that the Empire has been, on the whole, a power for good, if you believe that India has, on the whole, benefited by the British connection, would you not admit that it is the duty of every Indian citizen to help the Empire in the hour of its need?" [079-005]

The Viceroy assured Gandhi that he knew nothing beyond what the papers said about the secret treaties, and invited Gandhi to raise whatever moral issues he liked after the war.

## At the Conference

At the conference, Gandhi took the position that India should offer her support to the Empire while simultaneously pursuing Home Rule. He argued that India's loyalty during the crisis would accelerate the journey toward self-government. In a letter drafted at the conference, he wrote that the desire for Home Rule had widely penetrated even the rural masses, and that nothing less than a definite vision of Home Rule to be realized in the shortest possible time would satisfy the Indian people [079-019].

He further argued for the rights of Mohammedan States, emphasizing that just treatment of Muslim sentiment regarding their places of worship and India's claim to Home Rule were essential to the safety of the Empire [079-021].

## Aftermath

Following the conference, Gandhi undertook a recruiting campaign in Kheda to encourage Indians to enlist in the British Army. This decision was controversial among his followers, as it appeared to contradict his commitment to nonviolence. Gandhi defended his action by arguing that if India desired the rights and privileges of the Empire, she must also share in its burdens. The recruiting campaign, however, took a severe toll on Gandhi's health [081-001].
