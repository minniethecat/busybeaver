# Civil Disobedience

Civil disobedience is the deliberate, public, and nonviolent violation of laws deemed unjust, undertaken as a form of moral protest and political pressure. For Gandhi, it was not an isolated tactic but one expression of the broader satyagraha philosophy. He distinguished it from merely criminal lawbreaking by insisting on its openness (no secrecy), its nonviolence (no harm to persons), and its willingness to accept punishment (no resistance to arrest).

## Definition

Gandhi understood civil disobedience as a moral dialogue with the state. By openly breaking an unjust law and submitting to the legal penalty, the civil resister demonstrated both the injustice of the law and their own respect for the rule of law in general. The goal was not anarchy but moral persuasion — to make the law's injustice visible and unsustainable. Civil disobedience required strict discipline: resisters must be trained in nonviolence, must not resist arrest, and must not react with anger to provocation.

## Origins and Influences

Henry David Thoreau's 1849 essay "Civil Disobedience" provided the name and conceptual foundation. Thoreau argued that individuals have a moral duty to resist unjust laws, and that conscience must take precedence over legal obligation. Gandhi encountered Thoreau's work during his South African years and found in it a philosophical justification for what he was already practicing. He adapted Thoreau's individualistic approach into a collective, mass-based method. Leo Tolstoy's writings on nonviolent resistance to evil also shaped Gandhi's understanding.

## Gandhi's Practice

### South African Origins

Gandhi's first organized civil disobedience campaign was the 1906–1914 satyagraha in South Africa, where Indians defied registration laws, immigration restrictions, and a tax on former indentured laborers. Thousands courted imprisonment, and the campaign ultimately secured significant concessions. These years were the laboratory in which the techniques of civil disobedience were refined.

### Indian Campaigns

**Rowlatt Satyagraha (1919)** — Gandhi's first nationwide civil disobedience campaign in India, protesting wartime emergency laws that continued into peacetime. The campaign included hartals (general strikes) and the public sale of prohibited literature.

**Salt Satyagraha (1930)** — The most iconic civil disobedience action: Gandhi's 240-mile march to the sea to make salt in defiance of the British salt monopoly. This simple act dramatized the injustice of colonial rule and inspired millions.

**Quit India (1942)** — The most confrontational civil disobedience campaign, demanding an end to British rule. Gandhi and the entire Congress leadership were imprisoned, and the movement was suppressed with massive force.

### Discipline and Limits

Gandhi insisted that civil disobedience required the same discipline as military service. Participants took pledges, underwent training, and accepted a strict code of conduct. He was also willing to suspend campaigns if they turned violent, as he did after the Chauri Chaura incident in 1922.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Civil disobedience sits within the broader satyagraha framework as one of its most visible expressions. It is not the whole of satyagraha — constructive work, fasting, and negotiation are also components — but it is the sharp edge. It embodies the Gandhian principle that means determine ends: a just society can only be built through just methods, and civil disobedience is just means applied to unjust laws.
