# General Jan Smuts

General Jan Smuts was the South African statesman who, as Colonial Secretary and later Prime Minister, was Gandhi's primary adversary during the satyagraha campaigns of 1906–1914. Their relationship evolved from confrontation to mutual respect, embodying Gandhi's principle of separating the system from the person. Smuts later acknowledged Gandhi's greatness, saying that his "saintly" opponent had been right.

## Relationship to Gandhi

Gandhi and Smuts faced each other across the negotiating table multiple times during the South African satyagraha. Gandhi consistently distinguished between Smuts the person — whom he treated with respect — and the unjust laws Smuts administered. This approach exemplified satyagraha's core principle: "It is quite proper to resist and attack a system, but to resist and attack its author is tantamount to resisting and attacking oneself" (086-013). After the satyagraha achieved its goals, Smuts and Gandhi parted with mutual respect. Smuts later presented Gandhi with a pair of sandals he had made — a gesture Gandhi treasured.

## Biography

### Early Life

Smuts was born in the Cape Colony in 1870, educated at Cambridge, and rose to become one of South Africa's most powerful political figures. A philosopher-statesman, he coined the term "holism" and was a signatory to the Treaty of Versailles.

### Role in the Narrative

Smuts appears as the worthy opponent — intelligent, principled within his own framework, and ultimately capable of being moved by moral force. The satyagraha campaigns against his government demonstrated that nonviolent pressure could succeed even against a determined adversary.

## Historical Significance

The Gandhi-Smuts relationship is a case study in satyagraha's claim that nonviolent resistance can transform opponents rather than merely defeat them. Smuts's later acknowledgment of Gandhi's moral stature vindicated the method.
