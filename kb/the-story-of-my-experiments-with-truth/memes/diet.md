# Diet in Gandhi's Experiments with Truth

Diet was never merely a matter of nutrition for Gandhi. It was a moral discipline, a spiritual practice, and one of the most sustained fields of experimentation in his life. From the fateful vow of vegetarianism made to his mother before departing for [[england]], through his involvement with the London Vegetarian Society, to his radical simplifications in [[south-africa]], Gandhi treated every meal as an opportunity for self-mastery.

## Gandhi's Definition of Diet

For Gandhi, diet was inseparable from ethics. He did not see vegetarianism as merely a health choice but as a moral imperative grounded in [[ahimsa]]. His dietary philosophy rested on three pillars: nonviolence toward animals, self-restraint in appetite, and simplicity as a spiritual discipline.

## Origins and Influences

Gandhi's dietary journey began with a vow. Before leaving for England, his mother extracted a promise that he would not touch meat, wine, or women (011-014). At this stage, the vow was a concession to maternal piety — he was "a convinced meat-eater" intellectually, believing that meat-eating was necessary for India's physical strength (007-006).

The intellectual conversion came in London through reading. Henry Salt's *A Plea for Vegetarianism* was the decisive text:

> I read Salt's book from cover to cover and was very much impressed by it. From the date of reading this book, I may claim to have become a vegetarian by choice. I blessed the day on which I had taken the vow before my mother. I had all along abstained from meat in the interests of truth and of the vow I had taken, but had wished at the same time that every Indian should be a meat-eater, and had looked forward to being one myself freely and openly some day, and to enlisting others in the cause. The choice was now made in favour of vegetarianism, the spread of which henceforward became my mission.
>
> (014-009)

This passage marks a turning point — Gandhi moved from reluctant compliance with a vow to enthusiastic advocacy. Vegetarianism became "his mission."

## Gandhi's Practice

### Early Experiments in England

After reading Salt, Gandhi's "appetite for dietetic studies" expanded rapidly. He read Howard Williams's *The Ethics of Diet*, Anna Kingsford's *The Perfect Way in Diet*, and Dr. Allinson's writings on health (015-001). He joined the Vegetarian Society, was elected to its Executive Committee, and founded a short-lived vegetarian club in his own neighborhood of Bayswater (017-010).

His experiments grew increasingly systematic:

| Experiment | Period | Outcome |
|------------|--------|---------|
| Starchy foods elimination | London period | Brief trial, abandoned |
| Bread and fruit only | London period | Brief trial |
| Cheese, milk, and eggs | London period | Failed within two weeks — advocated by a milk reformer who "could not produce an unbroken egg" (017-005) |
| Strict vegetarianism | From London onward | Permanent — gave up eggs entirely per his vow |
| Saltless diet | South Africa | Temporary health experiment |
| Uncooked food / fruits only | Later years | Intermittent |
| One meal a day | Later years | Practiced at times |

### Diet as Moral Discipline

Gandhi saw a direct link between diet and self-control. When he undertook his experiments in [[brahmacharya]], he recognized that "control of the palate" was the first and most essential step:

> I saw that the writers on vegetarianism had examined the question very minutely, attacking it from the religious, scientific, practical, and medical points of view. Taking a purely ethical standpoint, they argued that man's supremacy over the lower animals meant not that the former should prey upon the latter, but that the higher should protect the lower.
>
> (017-001)

### Simplification and Economy

Diet also became a tool of simplicity and non-possession. In London, Gandhi reduced his living expenses by moving to a single room, cooking his own meals, and walking miles to save on fares. His diet shifted to "porridge and cocoa" for breakfast, home-made bread and soup for lunch and dinner (016-006). This was not asceticism for its own sake — he was deeply conscious of his family's financial sacrifice and wanted to minimize the burden.

## Place in Gandhi's Philosophy

Diet connects to multiple strands of Gandhi's thought:

| Concept | Dietary Connection |
|---------|-------------------|
| [[vegetarianism]] | The specific moral principle; diet as nonviolence applied to eating |
| [[brahmacharya]] | Control of the palate as the gateway to control of all senses |
| [[vow]] | His mother's vow gave diet its binding moral character |
| [[simplicity]] | Simple food as an expression of non-possession |
| [[ahimsa]] | Vegetarianism as the most everyday practice of nonviolence |

Gandhi's father's deathbed experience also shaped his dietary views. When his father was ill, a nurse suggested that a meat broth might have saved him, but the family's vegetarian convictions made this impossible. Gandhi later wrote that he never regretted this choice (009-003).
