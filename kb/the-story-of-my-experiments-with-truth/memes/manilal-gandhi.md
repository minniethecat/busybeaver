## Summary

Manilal Gandhi (1892–1956) was the second son of Mahatma Gandhi and Kasturba Gandhi. In the autobiography, he appears most prominently in an extended narrative about a serious childhood illness and Gandhi's decision to treat him through natural healing methods — hydropathy and careful dietary nursing — rather than conventional medicine. This episode served as a practical test of Gandhi's principles regarding health, diet, and parental responsibility.

## Childhood Illness and Natural Healing

Shortly after the Gandhi family returned to India and settled in Girgaum, Bombay, Manilal, then ten years old, fell seriously ill with an obstinate fever [076-001]. A Parsi doctor recommended that Manilal be given chicken broth and eggs for strength, but Gandhi refused on vegetarian grounds: "I told him that we were all vegetarians and that I could not possibly give either of the two things to my son" [076-003].

When the doctor's treatment proved ineffective, Gandhi took over Manilal's care personally, applying the natural healing methods he had learned from Adolf Just and Louis Kuhne. He gave Manilal hip baths, "never keeping him in the tub for more than three minutes" [076-009], and eventually decided to try a wet sheet pack.

The critical moment came one night when Gandhi, lying beside Manilal, made the decision to administer the wet sheet pack. He describes the process in detail: "I got up, wetted a sheet, wrung the water out of it and wrapped it about Manilal, keeping only his head out, and then covered him with two blankets" [076-012]. Exhausted, Gandhi went for a walk on Chaupati beach to refresh himself [076-013].

## Recovery

When Gandhi returned, Manilal was already showing signs of improvement: "You have returned, Bapu?" [076-014]. Gandhi continued the treatment, telling him, "Manilal, your fever is sure to go now. A little more perspiration and then I will take you out" [076-020].

Manilal's fever subsided substantially by the next morning. He continued on "diluted milk and fruit juices" for forty days [076-023]. Gandhi reflected on the outcome with characteristic humility: "Today Manilal is the healthiest of my boys. Who can say whether his recovery was due to God's grace, or to hydropathy, or to careful dietary and nursing? Let everyone decide according to his own faith" [076-024].

## At Phoenix Settlement

The recovery of Manilal's health coincided with the family's move away from the damp Girgaum house. Gandhi decided to relocate to better-ventilated quarters in consultation with Shri Revashankar Jagjivan [077-001]. Manilal later lived at the Phoenix Settlement in South Africa, where he would become one of its long-term residents and custodians.

## Legacy

Manilal Gandhi spent much of his adult life in South Africa, where he edited the *Indian Opinion* and continued his father's work at the Phoenix Settlement. The childhood illness narrative in the autobiography serves as a vivid illustration of Gandhi's faith in nature cure and his willingness to apply his principles even — or especially — when they concerned those he loved most.
