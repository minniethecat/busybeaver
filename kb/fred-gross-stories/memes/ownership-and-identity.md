# Ownership and Identity

For Fred Gross, owning things is not merely a matter of possession but of identity. He is what he owns — or rather, he is the gap between what he owns and what he wants to own. The bungalow, the car, the club membership: these are not just assets but components of selfhood, and Fred's relationship to them reveals his understanding of who he is and who he wants to be.

## The Owned Self

Fred's identity is constructed through ownership. He is a homeowner, a car owner, a club member — each possessive defines an aspect of his social being. The move from renting to owning is, in Fred's understanding, a transformation of self as much as of circumstance. "Our own home" is not just a place to live but an assertion of who Fred Gross is.

## The Anxiety of Possession

Ownership generates anxiety as well as identity. What is owned can be lost — to creditors, to foreclosure, to the endless expenses of maintenance and upkeep. Fred's possessions are also obligations, and the weight of these obligations grows as his possessions accumulate. The man who owns things is also owned by them.

## The Limits of Ownership

Lardner quietly demonstrates the limits of Fred's understanding. The things Fred owns do not, in fact, transform him into the man he wants to be. The bungalow does not make him secure; the car does not make him free; the club membership does not make him belong. Ownership promises transformation and delivers only possession. The gap between promise and reality is the space in which the stories' comedy — and their pathos — operates.
