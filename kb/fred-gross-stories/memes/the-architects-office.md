# The Architect's Office

The architect's office is where Fred Gross's bungalow takes shape on paper — a professional space that represents expertise, authority, and, in Fred's experience, the art of charging for services not fully rendered. It is the site of decisions that will determine the cost and quality of Fred's home, and Fred enters it as a client who is unsure what clients are entitled to expect.

## The Professional Setting

An architect's office in 1915 was a place of drawings, specifications, and professional vocabulary — a space designed to impress upon the client the value of the expertise being purchased. Fred, who has never hired an architect before, navigates this environment without a clear sense of what $100 should buy him. The office is the stage on which the architect performs competence, whether or not the competence is real.

## The Negotiations

The substantive interactions between Fred and his architect occur in this office — discussions of plans, selections of materials, approval of changes. Each meeting is a negotiation in which Fred is at an informational disadvantage. The architect knows what things should cost; Fred knows only what he is being charged. The office is a site of asymmetry.

## The Telephone Extension

As the project progresses, the architect's office extends into Fred's life through the telephone. The daily calls that drive Fred to distraction are, in effect, the office reaching into his home — the professional space colonizing the domestic. Fred cannot escape the architect even when he is not in the office, and this intrusion is part of what makes the experience so exhausting.
