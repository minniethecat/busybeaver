# Social Status

Social status is the silent subject of every Fred Gross letter, the invisible hierarchy that Fred simultaneously denies and desperately seeks to climb, and the force that drives every major decision in the stories.

## Overview

Fred Gross never uses the term "social status" or "class." His vocabulary is the vocabulary of common sense: "nice people," "the right kind," "swell," "a good address." But beneath this everyday language, the stories document a man consumed by status anxiety. Every decision—where to live, what club to join, how to spend money, whom to befriend—is shaped by Fred's need to establish and maintain social position.

Lardner's genius is to make status visible through Fred's unwitting self-revelation. Fred tells Charley about his new lot in Allison, his country club membership, his golf lessons, and in the telling, he reveals the status calculus driving each choice. The reader perceives what Fred cannot: that his pursuit of status is itself the marker of the status he lacks.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The move to Allison is a status move. Fred's disgust with the "rotten L servus" (001-004) and apartment living is as much about what these things say about him as about their practical inconveniences. The bungalow he plans to build is a status object—a visible marker of having arrived. The real estate man's sales pitch exploits this: "some body else was libel to see it & cop it out" (001-011).

> The other day the Walters come to see us & stayed all p.m. & little Ed & the baby both of them hollered all the wile they was there & when the Walters had went I says to Grace its a bout time you lerned them babys to shut there mouth when we got Co. & says I can't do nothing with them because there both of them teethin and theys 1 consolation the babys woant trouble us much longer because we are going to move out of this rotten L servus as soon as our lease is up.
>
> (001-004)

> I had $100 a long with me when we went out & when I seen what a bargun we was gettin & the real estate man says we better hurry if we wanted it because some body else was libel to see it & cop it out so I give the real estate man the $100 to bind the bargun & he is going to get the papers fixed up to day & when they are fixed up I will pay him the rest.
>
> (001-011)

### Across All Stories

Status anxiety surfaces in every story. Fred's sensitivity to slights, his obsessive accounting of costs, his careful cataloguing of who has what—all reveal a man for whom status is a constant preoccupation. The practical jokes that drive the plots are status contests by other means: who can humiliate whom, who has the last laugh.

## Historical Context

Early 20th-century America was a society in which class boundaries were both rigid and permeable. The suburban expansion created new communities where status had to be negotiated from scratch, without the established hierarchies of older urban neighborhoods. The country club, the bungalow, the automobile—these were all instruments of status differentiation in communities where everyone was, in some sense, starting over.

## Literary Significance

Lardner's treatment of social status is notable for its indirection. He never names what he is describing. Instead, he lets Fred's own words reveal the status system that structures his world. This technique—showing the invisible through the visible, the structural through the personal—is one of Lardner's most important contributions to American literary realism.
