# Thrift

Thrift—the discipline of saving, economizing, and calculating costs—is the constant background music of the Fred Gross stories, shaping every decision Fred makes and revealing the economic precarity beneath his middle-class performance.

## Overview

Fred Gross is an accountant of his own life. His letters to Charley are filled with numbers: $800 in combined savings, $1,500 for the lot, $1,200 for the bungalow, $25 per week for construction payments, $25 per week to live on, 6% interest per annum. These figures are not incidental details but the substance of Fred's consciousness. He thinks in costs, dreams in budgets, measures his progress in dollars saved and debts retired.

Thrift in Fred's world is both practical necessity and moral virtue. To be thrifty is to be responsible, foresighted, respectable—the opposite of the fecklessness Fred associates with apartment-dwellers and spendthrifts. But thrift is also a burden: the constant vigilance, the deferred gratifications, the "cut down on cloths & nickle shows & groserys" (001-005) that make the difference between solvency and debt.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The first story is a case study in thrift. Fred and Grace pool their savings—"I had got out my bank book & seen where I had a bout $300 & with her $500 that makes a bout $800" (001-005)—and commit to an aggressive savings plan: "we can save $30 per wk. in sted of $25 if we live pretty clost" (001-017). The phrase "live pretty clost" encapsulates the thrifty life: not poverty, but constant constraint.

The interest calculation—"6% int. per annum" (001-016) on the lot note—is a detail Fred records with precision. Money has a cost, and Fred is acutely aware of it. Every dollar spent on interest is a dollar not available for the bungalow.

### Across Other Stories

Thrift shapes Fred's social life. The country club bar tab that shocks him is a violation of thrifty principles: money spent on drinks that could have gone to the house fund. His careful accounting of golf expenses, of automobile show admission, of every outlay—this is the mentality of a man for whom money is never abundant enough to be taken for granted.

## Historical Context

Thrift was a central value of the American middle class in the early 20th century. Savings accounts, building and loan associations, and the ethos of "paying as you go" were promoted by banks, churches, and civic organizations. The $800 that Fred and Grace have saved represented months of disciplined scrimping on what was likely a clerk's salary of $30–$40 per week.

## Literary Significance

Lardner's treatment of thrift is characteristically double-edged. Fred's thrift is admirable—he is trying to do the right thing for his family—but it is also comic, even tragic. The relentless calculation of costs reveals a life lived in perpetual constraint, where every pleasure must be weighed against the house fund. Fred's thrift is the sign of his responsibility and the measure of his limitation.
