# Business and Employment

Fred Gross has a job — he goes to an office, earns a salary, and supports his family — but the nature of his employment remains deliberately vague. Lardner's decision to keep Fred's professional life in soft focus is a narrative choice with significant implications: Fred's identity is built not on what he does for a living but on what he owns, where he lives, and who he knows.

## The Invisible Office

Fred mentions going to the office, dealing with business matters, and interacting with colleagues, but the specifics are almost never provided. His work is in Chicago, necessitating the train commute, but what happens there remains offstage. This absence is characteristic of Lardner's technique — the domestic and social spheres are where character is revealed; the office is merely where Fred goes when he is not being interesting.

## Business as Social Metaphor

Fred frequently uses business language to describe social situations. Deals are made, bargains struck, accounts settled. This rhetorical habit reveals Fred's worldview: social life is transactional, and every interaction has a balance sheet. The real estate deal, the bank loan, the architect's contract — these are the templates through which Fred understands all human exchange.

Freds commute shapes his professional identity:

> I want to play a couple times to get the hang of it so as I can talk with the other fellows going in on the train because that seems to be about all they talk
>
> (P03-008)

## The Economic Context

Fred's employment situates him in the expanding white-collar class of early twentieth-century America. He is neither working class nor wealthy — a man who must save $30 a week, calculate the cost of everything, and worry about whether his salary will cover his ambitions. His economic position is precarious in ways he only partly acknowledges, and his consumption (the bungalow, the car, the club membership) consistently runs ahead of his means.
