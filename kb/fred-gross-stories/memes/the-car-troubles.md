# The Car Troubles

The automobile, for all its promise of freedom and status, brings Fred Gross a new category of problems. Mechanical failures, repair bills, the unreliability of early automotive technology — these are the hidden costs of car ownership that Fred discovers only after purchase.

## The Unreliable Machine

Early automobiles like the Swift Six were temperamental machines requiring constant attention. Tires went flat, engines misfired, electrical systems failed. Fred, who is not mechanically inclined, finds himself at the mercy of a technology he owns but does not understand. The car that was supposed to liberate him becomes another source of frustration and expense.

> But she finely turned over and I got in and drove about 3 blocks and the car begin to shake and shimmy and I says to Chas something must of come loose but I guess it will work its way back in place.
>
> (006-084)

## The Repair Economy

Automobile repair in the 1910s was a nascent industry. Garages were few, mechanics were of variable quality, and parts were not always available. When Fred's car breaks down, he enters a repair economy that mirrors the construction economy of the first story — a world of uncertain expertise, unexpected costs, and the nagging suspicion that he is being overcharged.

## The Metaphor

The car troubles function as a metaphor for Fred's broader experience with modernity. The new technology promises freedom and delivers dependence. The purchase that was supposed to simplify life complicates it. The machine that was supposed to be reliable proves unreliable. In each case, Fred's response is the same: surprise, indignation, and the determination to keep going.
