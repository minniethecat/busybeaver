# Epistolary Form

The epistolary form—stories told through a series of dated letters—is the defining narrative technique of the Fred Gross stories, enabling Ring Lardner's characteristic ironic distance between the naive narrator and the knowing reader.

## Overview

Each Fred Gross story is presented as a series of letters from Fred Gross to his brother Charley, dated and signed. The letters are written in Fred's distinctive vernacular—a semi-literate dialect full of misspellings, malapropisms, and grammatical errors that Lardner rendered with painstaking phonetic precision. This form creates a double perspective: the reader sees both what Fred intends to communicate (his earnest self-presentation as a successful suburban householder) and what he inadvertently reveals (his anxieties, pretensions, and limitations).

The letter format is not merely decorative but constitutive of the stories' meaning. Fred is writing to Charley, and this shapes everything he says: he is performing for an audience, boasting, complaining, seeking approval. The reader occupies the position of an eavesdropper on a one-sided conversation, privy to Fred's unguarded self-revelation.

## In the Stories

### Across All Stories

Every story in the collection uses the epistolary form. Fred's letters are dated with specific days in 1915–1917, and each story covers a sequence of letters spanning days or weeks. Fred periodically acknowledges the letter-writing frame: "the last time I wrote you a letter I have took 2 lessons from the golf teacher" (P03-028), or "the last time I wrote you a letter and they aint nothing much to" (002-035) report.

The letters create their own temporality. Events happen, Fred writes about them, and then subsequent letters revisit and reinterpret earlier events. This recursive structure allows Lardner to show how Fred's understanding of his own life evolves—or fails to evolve—over time.

The physical act of writing is occasionally foregrounded. Fred describes "the table where I am writeing this letter" (P03-055), and his letters sometimes reference the practical difficulties of correspondence: he "set down & tride to write a letter to them peopl asking them to" (002-045) respond, and worries when they "send me no anser to the letter I wrote them" (002-050).

## Historical Context

The epistolary novel has a long tradition in English literature, from Samuel Richardson's "Pamela" (1740) through the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. By the early twentieth century, the form was less common in serious fiction, but it persisted in popular and humorous writing. Lardner's innovation was to combine the epistolary form with first-person dialect narration, creating a voice that was simultaneously authentic and unreliable.

The letters in the Fred Gross stories reflect actual early 20th-century correspondence practices. Letters were the primary means of long-distance communication in 1915, and the rhythm of writing and waiting for replies shaped social relationships in ways that would later be transformed by the telephone.

## Literary Significance

Lardner's use of the epistolary form achieves several effects simultaneously. It provides a natural motivation for Fred's detailed self-narration (he is writing to his brother, not to us). It creates ironic distance (we see what Fred does not). It fragments the narrative into discrete episodes, each shaped by Fred's mood and circumstances at the moment of writing. And it foregrounds the act of narration itself—Fred is not just living his life but constantly telling the story of his life, and the gap between the two is where Lardner's satire operates.

## A Letter to Charley

> **Dear Charley**. Well Charley they aint nothing much come off since the last time I wrote you a letter and they aint nothing much to write a bout & I wouldent be writeing only I was figureing on takeing a bath tonight and I cant take no bath so Im writeing you a letter insted and why I cant take no bath is because the waters shut off all over town & the water co. says may be it wont be turned on for 2 or 3 days.
>
> (002-035)
