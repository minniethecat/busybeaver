# South Side, Chicago

The working-class neighborhood on Chicago's South Side where Fred and Grace Gross rented a flat before their move to Allison — a place Fred recalls with a mixture of nostalgia and defensive disparagement, and the geographic counterpoint that defines the suburban experiment.

## Overview

The South Side flat is less a depicted location than an invoked memory, but its presence in the stories is constant. It is where Fred and Grace began their married life, where their children were born, and from which the move to Allison represents both escape and loss. Fred characterizes the flat as cramped and undesirable compared to the spacious bungalow, but he also admits — usually in moments of financial stress — that life was easier there: "we was not never so hard up when we was liveing in the flat that we had to ask him to pay our rent" (002-002).

The flat anchors a persistent tension in the stories: the gap between the status of ownership and the comfort of renting. Fred's bungalow confers social standing but brings financial anxiety; the flat lacked prestige but provided security. This ambivalence runs beneath all six stories and surfaces whenever the costs of suburban life become overwhelming.

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The South Side appears indirectly through the logistics of the move. Fred and Grace sub-let their flat to another family from March 1 to May 1, expecting to be in the bungalow by then. When construction delays drag on, they face the prospect of homelessness: "I & Grace will half to stay at some hotel or some wheres from the 1 of march till the house is finnished" (001-070). The flat — their old secure home — has been given away, and the new house is not ready.

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The South Side emerges as a point of comparison and, implicitly, regret. When Grace's father has to pay their interest, Fred reflects: "I bet he wishes I & Grace had of stuck in our flat over on the s. side insted of building" (002-002). The flat represents the road not taken — the path of renting and urban living that Fred has rejected but cannot quite stop measuring himself against.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Though the flat is no longer Fred's home, the South Side as a social concept lingers. The neighborly density Fred experienced there — people living close together, sharing walls and streets — is replaced by Allison's more dispersed but more intense social surveillance. Fred's wistfulness about urban anonymity ("her old man couldent keep popping in on us evry time I got ready to hold her hand," 003-004) suggests what has been lost.

## Historical & Geographic Context

In the 1910s, Chicago's South Side was a vast, rapidly growing working-class and middle-class residential district. The area south of the Loop — especially neighborhoods like Bridgeport, Bronzeville, and the stockyards district — housed the city's industrial workforce and its aspiring clerical class. Fred's reference to "the L" (elevated railroad, 001-010) suggests he lived near one of the South Side elevated lines, which connected the residential neighborhoods to the Loop in roughly 35 minutes.

The South Side flat Fred describes would have been a unit in a two- or three-flat building, the characteristic Chicago housing type of the era — a brick courtyard building or frame structure with one apartment per floor. These flats, unlike the suburban bungalow, offered no yard, no basement storage, and no status, but they provided proximity to workplaces, established ethnic communities, and the anonymity of urban density that Fred both disparages and misses.
