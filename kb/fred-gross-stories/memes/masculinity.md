# Masculinity

Fred Gross is constantly performing masculinity — or rather, his version of it — and he is never quite sure he is doing it right. Golf, business, drinking, card-playing, homeownership: each is a stage on which Fred attempts to enact the role of American manhood as he understands it, and each attempt reveals the gap between the performance and the reality.

## Masculine Domains

The stories organize masculinity into distinct domains. The office is one — productive, serious, the source of income. The country club is another — competitive, convivial, the arena of male bonding. The home is a third — Fred is the provider, the decision-maker, the head of household, at least in theory. In practice, each domain generates its own anxieties, and Fred moves between them with more confidence than competence.

## Competition and Status

Masculinity in Fred's world is inherently competitive. Golf handicaps, business success, the size of one's house — all are measurable, and measurement invites comparison. Fred's sense of his own masculinity is constantly under negotiation, threatened by men who are better golfers, wealthier businessmen, or more established members of the community.

## Masculinity and Emotion

Fred's emotional range is constrained by his understanding of manhood. He can express anger, indignation, and competitive drive. He cannot express vulnerability, tenderness, or fear — at least not directly. These emotions emerge indirectly, through his complaints about money, his physical symptoms, his grievances against neighbors who have wronged him. The feelings are there; the vocabulary for expressing them is not.
