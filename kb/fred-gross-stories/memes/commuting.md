# Commuting

Commuting by train defines the daily rhythm of Fred Gross's life, linking his suburban home in Allison, Illinois to his workplace in Chicago and serving as the backdrop for social encounters that drive the stories' plots.

## Overview

Fred Gross is a commuter. He travels daily between Allison—"16 mi. west of the Loop on the c d & x" (001-010)—and downtown Chicago. The commute is not merely a logistical detail but a structuring element of Fred's life: it determines where he can afford to live, shapes his social interactions, and provides a recurring setting for the chance encounters and observations that fill his letters to brother Charley.

Fred's comparison of his old and new commutes—"it dont only take 40 min. for the trains to come in to town & it takes me 35 min. now to go in on the L" (001-010)—reveals the calculus of suburban life: a slightly longer commute in exchange for homeownership, space, and respectability. The commuter railroad makes Allison viable for a clerk like Fred, and his acceptance of the trade-off is the foundational act of the stories.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The commute is central to Story 1's premise. Fred's decision to buy a lot in Allison hinges on the train connection: the suburb is "16 mi. west of the Loop" and the train takes "only 40 min." (001-010). His disdain for the "rotten L servus" (001-004)—Chicago's elevated railway—provides the negative motivation for the move, while the commuter railroad provides the positive solution.

The train also serves as a social space. Other commuters become figures in Fred's narrative: "the other fellows going in on the train" (P03-008) who discuss stock market tips, or the encounters that lead to business opportunities and social connections.

### The Swift Six (Story 6)

In the final story, Fred's commute intersects with the automobile theme when he disparages "them suburban trains where your lucky to" (006-027) find a seat, contrasting the discomfort of rail commuting with the freedom promised by automobile ownership—a freedom he cannot yet afford.

> It would be pretty soft to drive to town and back every day in your car and not half to patternize them suburban trains where your lucky to find rm. enough for your ft. to stand on let alone rm. enough to set down unlest you starve your self for a few months 1st.
>
> (006-027)

### Across All Stories

Throughout the stories, train schedules and the rhythms of commuting structure Fred's days. The late train forces rushed decisions (P03-132), while shared commutes create opportunities for the casual interactions—business tips, gossip, confrontations—that drive Lardner's plots.

## Historical Context

Chicago's commuter rail network was among the most extensive in the United States by 1915. The Chicago and Northwestern Railway, the Chicago, Burlington and Quincy, and the Illinois Central all operated suburban services. The "c d & x" line Fred mentions likely refers to one of these carriers. Fares were affordable for clerks and skilled workers, making suburbs like Allison (likely a fictionalized version of actual Chicago suburbs like Oak Park or Evanston) accessible to the middle class.

The expansion of commuter rail in this period directly enabled the suburban building boom that Fred participates in. Without reliable train service, a 16-mile commute would have been impossible for a daily worker.

## Literary Significance

The commute serves Lardner as both setting and symbol. The train car is a liminal space where social boundaries blur—where Fred can rub shoulders with businessmen and stock traders, overhear tips, and imagine himself part of a world he only brushes against. The commute embodies the promise and limitation of Fred's life: it connects him to the city's economic opportunities while also marking the distance—physical and social—between his suburban bungalow and the centers of power.
