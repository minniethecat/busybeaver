# Mr. Hamilton

Mr. Hamilton is Fred Gross's neighbor and the primary antagonist of the middle stories, a figure whose automobile, social status, and willingness to escalate neighborly disputes make him both the object of Fred's envy and the engine of narrative conflict.

## Role & Overview

The Hamiltons arrive as new neighbors in "Welcome to Our City" (Story 2), and Fred's relationship with Mr. Hamilton quickly becomes the dominant subject of the subsequent stories. What begins as neighborly welcome — Grace introduces herself and Fred to the newcomers — escalates through practical jokes, perceived slights, and competitive consumption into the open warfare of "Uncivil War" (Story 4).

Hamilton occupies the social position Fred aspires to: he owns an automobile — "Hamiltons got a automobile and they been running it all" (004-040) — and moves through suburban society with a confidence Fred cannot match. The conflict between the two men is partly personal and partly structural: they are competitors for the same social territory, and only one can have the last laugh.

## Character Profile

### Background

Hamilton is a fellow suburbanite in Allison, Illinois, with a wife and sufficient means to own an automobile — a significant status marker in 1915. His occupation is not specified, but his lifestyle suggests greater affluence than Fred's.

### Personality & Traits

Hamilton is Fred's equal in pettiness and his superior in social confidence. He participates enthusiastically in the practical joke economy of suburban life but, unlike Fred, he seems to understand the rules — when to escalate, when to withdraw, how to maintain appearances while pursuing a feud. Fred's reports of Hamilton's behavior are filtered through resentment and envy, making it difficult to assess Hamilton's character independently. But even through Fred's hostile narration, Hamilton emerges as someone who knows how to play the suburban social game — and plays it to win.

## Key Appearances

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The Hamiltons arrive in Allison and are immediately subjected to the community's ritual of practical joking. Fred and Grace participate in the welcome, but the seeds of future conflict are already present.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

The practical joke competition escalates. Fred reports confrontations and retaliations, the humor becoming increasingly weaponized. (003-019)

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

The conflict reaches its peak. Fred distinguishes between acceptable and unacceptable humor: "theys a big diffrunts between a joke and not a joke" (004-051). Hamilton's automobile becomes a symbol of the status differential: Fred fears being seen "on acct. of the automobile bean lit up" (004-041), the illuminated car exposing what Fred would prefer to hide.

## Literary Significance

Hamilton is the mirror in which Fred sees his own inadequacies. He has what Fred wants — automobile, social ease, the last laugh — and his presence in the stories forces Fred to confront the limits of his own social competence. Lardner uses the Fred-Hamilton conflict to explore how class competition operates in the intimate theater of suburban life, where neighbors cannot escape each other and every slight must be answered.

Fred learns his neighbors identity:
