# Femininity and Gender Roles

The women of the Fred Gross stories — Grace Gross most prominently, but also Mrs. Hamilton, Mrs. Martin, and the other wives of Allison — inhabit a world defined by the domestic sphere, social management, and the expectations of early twentieth-century femininity. Their lives are no less shaped by gender performance than Fred's, but the terms are different.

## Grace Gross

Grace is Fred's wife and his most important social partner. She manages the household, raises the children, and navigates the complex social terrain of Allison with more skill than Fred realizes. Her desires — for a nicer home, a better social position, the right clothes and furnishings — drive much of the family's consumption, and Fred's narration both acknowledges and resents this.

## The Work of Social Management

In Allison's gender economy, women do the work of social management. They organize the dinner invitations, maintain the calling calendar, monitor the neighborhood's emotional temperature, and enforce its norms. When Fred undertakes social action — the prank on the Hamiltons, the decision to host a party — he does so in consultation with Grace, whose judgment about social matters is generally more acute than his own.

## Women's Voices

Lardner gives us women's voices only through Fred's mediation — we hear what Fred reports Grace saying, never Grace's own words directly. This narrative choice is significant: the women of the stories are as real as the men, but they are rendered entirely through the filter of Fred's perception, which is unreliable in ways Fred cannot recognize. What Grace actually thinks and feels remains, to some degree, a mystery.
