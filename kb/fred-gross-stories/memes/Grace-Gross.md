# Grace Gross

Grace Gross, Fred's wife, is the unseen engine of the Gross household — the partner whose savings exceed Fred's, whose social ambitions drive the family's move to the suburbs, and whose practical judgment consistently exceeds her husband's, though Fred's narration only intermittently acknowledges this.

## Role & Overview

Grace appears in the stories only through Fred's reports of her words and actions, but she emerges as the more clear-sighted and strategically minded partner. It is Grace who proposes buying a lot and building a house: "she says and besides I got $500 in the bank that you can take & how much you got in the bank your self" (001-005). Her savings of $500 — significantly more than Fred's $300 — make the suburban project financially possible.

Grace's social ambitions drive Fred's country club membership: Fred admits he joined "mostly on acct. of the Mrs." (P03-004). She manages the family's social life, initiates neighborly visits, and navigates the complex social terrain of Allison with a competence that Fred's narration simultaneously reveals and understates.

## Character Profile

### Background

Grace is a homemaker caring for at least two young children — "little Ed & the baby" (001-004). She manages the household budget, contributes to savings, and makes key financial decisions jointly with Fred. Her background is not explicitly detailed, but her $500 in savings suggests either prior employment, an inheritance, or more disciplined saving than Fred.

### Personality & Traits

Grace is practical where Fred is impulsive. When the building loan falls through, it is Grace who absorbs the disappointment and recalibrates: "when I come home & told Grace I thot shed cry her eyesout but finely we seen they wasent no use in that" (001-017). She is socially astute, understanding the unspoken rules of suburban society better than Fred, and she manages the family's public presentation with strategic awareness.

## Key Appearances

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

Grace initiates the suburban project and provides the financial foundation. Her argument for the move is practical: "I dont see why is it we cant find a place out in the country some wheres & live there & it wouldent take you no longer to get to work" (001-004). She suggests building rather than buying: "lets buy a lot some wheres & build our own house" (001-005).

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

Grace takes the lead in neighborly outreach, visiting the newly arrived Hamiltons and establishing the social connections that will drive the subsequent stories' plots.

### Across All Stories

Grace's presence is felt even when she is not quoted directly. Fred's decisions are shaped by what Grace will think, what Grace will say, how Grace will react. She is the audience for whom Fred's performance as a successful householder is ultimately staged.

## Literary Significance

Grace Gross exemplifies Lardner's skill at creating characters who exist in the gap between the narrator's perception and the reader's understanding. Fred presents Grace as his partner, but the reader gradually perceives that she is his superior — smarter, more practical, more socially adept. This dual portrait, achieved entirely through Fred's unreliable narration, is a technical achievement of considerable sophistication.

Fred describes Grace as his social compass:

> Grace is tickeled to death because a lady ast her if she wouldent be on the entertainment comity of the country club & thats the kind of thing that suits Grace to a T.
>
> (P03-048)
