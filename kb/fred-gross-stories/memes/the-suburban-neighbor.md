# The Suburban Neighbor

The neighbor is the central figure in Fred Gross's social universe — more present than family, more significant than coworkers, more consequential than distant friends. The neighbor is simultaneously companion and competitor, ally and adversary, and Fred's relationships with his neighbors define the emotional landscape of the stories.

## The Neighbor as Social Unit

In Allison, the neighbor is the basic unit of social life. Neighbors host dinners, attend parties, share information, and monitor each other's behavior. The proximity is physical — houses are close — but it is also social, creating an intensity of relationship that urban apartment living did not provide. Fred's neighbors are unavoidable in a way that city neighbors were not.

## The Neighbor as Competitor

Every neighbor is also a benchmark. The Carpenters entertain better. The Hamiltons have higher status. The Curtises are more established. Fred measures himself against his neighbors continuously, and the measurements generate the anxieties that fuel the stories. The neighbor is both model (this is how one should live) and rival (this is who one must surpass).

## The Neighbor as Audience

Fred performs for his neighbors as he performs for Charley — constructing a self that will be admired, respected, envied. The bungalow, the party, the club membership, the car — each is a performance directed partly at the neighbors, a statement about who Fred Gross is and where he stands. The neighbors are the audience that validates the performance or, by their indifference, denies it.
