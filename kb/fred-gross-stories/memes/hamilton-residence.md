# Hamilton Residence

The Allison home of Mr. and Mrs. Hamilton — a house that functions simultaneously as a private residence, an informal gambling parlor for the women of Allison society, and the physical stage on which the Gross-Hamilton feud reaches its most theatrical moments.

## Overview

The Hamilton residence is never described in architectural detail, but its social function is vividly rendered. It is the place where Mrs. Hamilton hosts the card parties that draw Allison's women — Grace Gross among them — into a world of bridge whist, punch, and whispered gossip. It is also the target of Fred Gross's most elaborate pranks: a valentine, a fake quarantine sign, and a telephone harassment campaign that Fred conducts from his own window while watching Hamilton eat breakfast.

The house's most dramatic moment comes in Story 3, when Deputy Parker — drunk, gun drawn — pounds on the front door and declares the place "pinched" for being a gambling house. Mrs. Hamilton's composure during this crisis ("she looked the gat right in the face") reveals her as a far more formidable figure than Fred's dismissive descriptions suggest.

## In the Stories

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

The Hamilton residence is the scene of the botched gambling raid that Fred engineers. Deputy Parker, fortified with "a few glassis of currage," arrives at the house and "went up & pound it on Hamiltons front door & when the higher girl opened up the door he pulled his gun and she shreeked & pretty near fainted" (003-054). Mrs. Hamilton rushes to the door, faces down the deputy's gun, demands to see a warrant, and then calmly calls her husband, who contacts the sheriff's office. The raid collapses into farce, but the event cements the Hamilton house as the symbolic center of Allison's social tensions.

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

The residence becomes the target of Fred's prank campaign. Martin suggests hanging a quarantine sign on Hamilton's porch: "if I could get a hold of 1 of them sines and hang it under the electrick light on Hamiltons porch the night of the party the guests would all of them see it and be scared to go in the house" (004-015). The plan — scaring away Hamilton's party guests with a fake health department notice — captures the house's role as a site of social performance and vulnerability. The valentine exchange that opens the feud (004-023) and the telephone calls Fred makes while watching Hamilton eat breakfast (004-064) further establish the residence as both target and stage.

## Historical & Geographic Context

The Hamilton residence, like Fred's bungalow, is a product of Allison's early-20th-century suburban development. Its role as an informal gambling house reflects the era's ambiguous legal and social status of home-based card games. In Illinois in the 1910s, gambling was nominally illegal, but enforcement was selective and often corrupt — precisely the dynamic the story exploits when Deputy Parker's raid is revealed to be a setup orchestrated by Sheriff Bob Barnes at Fred's request.

The house's front door — the site of Parker's dramatic entrance — was the threshold that separated private family life from public social performance in early suburban culture. The "higher girl" (hired girl) who opens the door, the "electric light" on the porch that would illuminate the quarantine sign, and the telephone that connects the residence to the sheriff's office and to Fred's harassing calls are all markers of the modern domestic infrastructure that was rapidly transforming American suburban life in the 1910s.

Fred's description of the Hamiltons' house mingles admiration with egalitarian resentment:

> The Hamiltons has got a swell big house build out of stones or concrete blocks & they probly got a bbl. of money but you know the old saying Charley all men was crated free and wear just as good is them even if we got all my pay for the next 30 yrs. spent all ready.
>
> (002-019)
