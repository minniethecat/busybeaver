# Gambling and Card Games

Card games are the organizing ritual of Allison's social life, and they provide the occasion for some of the most consequential events in the Fred Gross stories. From auction bridge to the gambling raid on the Hamilton residence, the card table is where social alliances are formed, tested, and broken.

## Auction Bridge and Social Standing

Auction bridge — the dominant card game of the era — functions as a social sorting mechanism. Those who play well are welcome in the best homes; those who do not are excluded. Fred's participation in these games is part of his broader project of social integration, but the stakes are never purely recreational. The card table is where reputations are made.

Fred's own card-playing is a mixture of genuine enjoyment and social obligation. Like golf, card games are something he feels he must do to belong, and like golf, they come with costs — time, money, and the ever-present risk of embarrassment.


Fred describes his bewildering first encounter with the game:

> Well they told us it was going to be a card party so I and Grace thot of corse they would play cinch or rummy or may be whist but when we got over there they sprung this here game they call auction bridge whist.
>
> (003-011)

## The Hamilton Gambling Raid

The most dramatic event associated with card games in the stories is Fred's orchestration of a gambling raid on the Hamilton residence. This is card-playing weaponized — the social ritual turned against itself. Fred, who participates in the same card games he reports, uses the specter of illegal gambling as a tool of revenge. The hypocrisy is, for Lardner, the point.

## Gambling and Morality

The early twentieth century saw widespread moral opposition to gambling, even as card games were a staple of middle-class entertainment. The ambiguity of this position — gambling is wrong, but everyone does it — creates the opening that Fred exploits in the Hamilton raid. The law can be invoked selectively, and Fred invokes it not out of principle but out of spite.
