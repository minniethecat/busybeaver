# The Bridge Tournament

The auction bridge tournament organizes an evening's entertainment into a structure of winners and losers — a microcosm of the social competition that pervades Allison life. For Fred, it is another arena in which to prove himself, with results as mixed as his golf.

## The Stakes

Bridge in 1915 was not merely a card game but a social institution. Auction bridge, the dominant variant of the era, required skill, memory, and partnership — qualities that translated easily into social capital. Winning at bridge was winning at society; losing was a small but real diminishment of status.

## Fred at the Table

Fred approaches the bridge table as he approaches the golf course: with more confidence than competence. He wants to win, expects to win, and is genuinely surprised when he does not. His card-playing is adequate rather than expert, and his narration of bridge evenings reveals his characteristic blend of competitiveness and self-justification.

> Well they told us it was going to be a card party so I and Grace thot of corse they would play cinch or rummy or may be whist but when we got over there they sprung this here game they call auction bridge whist.
>
> (003-011)

## The Social Function

The bridge tournament is also a social sorting mechanism. Partners are chosen, tables are arranged, and the evening's geometry maps onto the town's social hierarchy. Who plays with whom, who wins and who loses — these are not trivial details but data points in Allison's ongoing social calculus.
