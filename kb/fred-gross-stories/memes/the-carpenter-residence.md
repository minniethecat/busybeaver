# The Carpenter Residence

The Carpenters' home is one of the reference points in Fred Gross's mental map of Allison's social geography. It is the house of an established family — people who were in Allison before the Grosses arrived, who know the town's unwritten rules, whose social position is secure in ways Fred's is not.

## The House as Benchmark

Fred measures his own bungalow against the homes of families like the Carpenters. Is his house as nice? His entertaining as polished? His family as well-regarded? The Carpenter residence is a standard Fred aspires to meet, and his awareness of the gap between their domestic establishment and his own is a quiet source of anxiety.

## The Social Center

The Carpenter home functions as a social center — a place where people gather, where hospitality is practiced at a level Fred struggles to match. When the Carpenters entertain, they set a standard that the Grosses must then strive to meet when it is their turn to host. The house is not merely a dwelling but a stage, and the Carpenters are accomplished performers.

## The Unseen Interior

Fred describes the Carpenter residence from the outside — from the perspective of a guest, a neighbor, a social competitor. The interior life of the household, its private tensions and satisfactions, remains invisible to him. As with all the houses in Allison, Fred sees the surface and infers the depth, and his inferences say as much about him as about the Carpenters.
