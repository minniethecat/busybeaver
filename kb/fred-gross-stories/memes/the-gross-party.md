# The Gross Party

The evening entertainment Fred and Grace Gross host in "The Last Laugh" — a social milestone that marks the family's full entry into Allison's party-giving circuit and inadvertently triggers the competitive escalation that will explode into open warfare in "Uncivil War."

## Overview

Having attended parties at the Curtises' and Carpenters' homes, Fred and Grace feel obligated to reciprocate: "the Carrys and the Curtisis thats had us to there house we will half to return the complement" (003-024). The party they host becomes the centerpiece of "The Last Laugh" — a social event that Fred reports with characteristic pride, documenting its success in the local paper while missing the resentment it stirs in the Hamiltons. The party operates on multiple levels: as a marker of social arrival, as the fulfillment of reciprocal obligation, and as a provocation that sets the stage for the Valentine's Day feud.

## Background

The obligation to host a party has been accumulating since Fred and Grace began attending neighbors' entertainments. Grace is particularly conscious of the social ledger: "we will half to return the complement" (003-024). The party also represents an opportunity to establish the Grosses as legitimate social players in Allison — to demonstrate that they can entertain at the standard expected by the Curtises, Carpenters, and other established families.

The party is planned despite Fred's financial reservations: "I says we aint got no money to give no party" (003-024). Grace overrules him, and the expense becomes another entry in Fred's running ledger of aspirational costs.

## Key Events

- **The planning**: Grace insists on hosting despite Fred's financial protests. The party is scheduled and preparations begin — food, drinks, and entertainment. (003-024)
- **The Hamilton anxiety**: News of the Gross party visibly unsettles the Hamiltons. Fred reports that "Mrs. Hamilton come runing to the door" (003-054) upon hearing of the event — a sign that every gathering in Allison is monitored by social rivals.
- **The entertainment**: The party features the fox trot — a dance that was still relatively new and fashionable in 1915–1916. Fred boasts of "doing the foxey trot out in our dinning room" (003-040), proud that his home can accommodate such modern entertainments.
- **The newspaper notice**: The party is reported in the local paper, a milestone of social recognition that Fred reports with evident pride. The public acknowledgment validates the Grosses' social standing — but also ensures that the Hamiltons' exclusion is a matter of public record.
- **The refreshments**: Fred notes the refreshments served, including drinks — a detail that carries weight in a community where hospitality is measured partly by the quality and quantity of food and drink provided.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The Gross party has two immediate consequences. First, it establishes the Grosses as full participants in Allison's social economy — they are now givers as well as receivers of hospitality. Second, and more fatefully, it deepens the Hamilton rivalry. By hosting a successful party that excludes the Hamiltons (whether by oversight or design), the Grosses have issued a social challenge. The Hamiltons' Valentine's Day party in "Uncivil War" — which pointedly excludes the Grosses — is a direct retaliation. The party-giving cycle has become a cycle of competitive exclusion.

## Literary Significance

The Gross party exemplifies Lardner's technique of using social events as narrative engines. The party is simultaneously a genuine source of pleasure for Fred and Grace, a marker of their social progress, and a tactical move in a rivalry they only dimly perceive. Fred's narration — proud, detailed, oblivious to the subtext — creates the characteristic Lardner irony: the reader understands the social dynamics better than the narrator does.

The newspaper notice is a particularly rich detail. For Fred, it is pure validation — proof that the Grosses have arrived. For the reader, it is evidence of the small-town social surveillance that makes Allison's party circuit a pressure cooker rather than a pleasure.

Fred's breathless account of the Allison party circuit captures his delight at being included:

> Well Charley we was to a party the night before last & wear going to an other party next wk. & it takes most of my time geting my shoes shined & they talk about Chicago being a live place well Charley its like a grave yd. come pared with this place & some thing doing evry minut when your a good dresser & the peopl likes you.
>
> (003-009)
