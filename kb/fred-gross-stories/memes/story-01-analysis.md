# Own Your Own Home

The inaugural Fred Gross story in which Fred and Grace decide to escape apartment life for suburban homeownership — a decision that entangles them in bank loans, contractor disputes, and the financial anxieties that will define the entire series, all rendered in Fred's distinctive dialect epistolary voice.

**Author:** Ring Lardner (as Fred Gross)
**Published:** 1915, in *The Saturday Evening Post*
**Story Number:** 1 of the Fred Gross series
**Epistolary Dates:** May 3 – mid-1915
**Word Count:** ~8,000

## Synopsis

Fred Gross writes to his brother Charley announcing that he and Grace have decided to buy a lot in the suburbs and build a bungalow. After touring Allison, Illinois — 16 miles west of Chicago's Loop — they purchase a lot for $1,500, putting down $100 immediately and financing the remainder. Fred's attempt to secure a building loan from his bank fails because the lot is not yet fully paid off. The family commits to an aggressive savings plan to clear the lot debt, with construction deferred to the following spring. Throughout, Fred's narration oscillates between boosterish enthusiasm for the suburban dream and dawning unease at the financial complexity he has stumbled into.

## Plot Summary

### May 3 — The Decision

Fred announces the suburban plan to Charley with characteristic enthusiasm, opening the entire series with an invitation: "I bet when you here what I & Grace has made up our minds you & Mary will wisht you was doing the same thing" (001-002). The immediate catalyst is the Walters' recent visit, during which the Gross children misbehaved in the cramped apartment — a domestic crisis that crystallizes the need for more space. Grace proposes buying a lot and building: "Grace says lets buy a lot & build a little house some wheres in the subbirds." (001-003)

The financial calculus begins immediately. Grace has saved $500; Fred has additional savings. She suggests borrowing the rest: "you can borry the money & pay it back a little to a time." (001-005) The letter oscillates between dream ("our own littel home") and practical anxiety ("I says to her what would we use for the payments").

### May 5 — The Lot

Fred and Grace tour Allison and immediately settle on a property: "100 ft. frontidge & 150 ft. deep & only $1,500" (001-010). The real estate man applies pressure: "we better hurry if we wanted it because some body else was libel to see it & cop it out" (001-011). The lot purchase introduces Fred to the rhythms of speculation and urgency that characterize suburban land markets. Fred puts down $100 to bind the bargain, and the pattern is set: impulsive commitment followed by belated reckoning.

### May 7 — The Bank

Fred approaches his bank for a building loan and receives a harsh education. The banker requires the lot to be paid off before any construction loan can be considered. Fred is baffled by the complexity of deeds, title guarantees, and interest calculations — "a lot of red tape" (001-016) — and by the revelation that "we dont do no busness that way" (001-017). This scene is the story's thematic core: the institutional machinery of American finance as experienced by a man who has never before encountered it.

### The Savings Plan

With the construction loan conditional on clearing the lot debt, the family commits to a rigorous savings regimen. Grace will bank money from her millinery work while Fred covers living expenses. The plan is concrete and quantified — characteristic of Fred's documentary instinct — yet the reader senses, in Fred's defensive tone, that he is trying to convince himself as much as Charley: "we can do it all right if we dont have no bad luck." (001-033)

### The Architect and Contractor

Fred's encounters with the building trades follow the same pattern as his banking education: optimistic initial engagement followed by the discovery of hidden costs. The contractor's price eventually reaches $2,000, bringing the all-in cost to roughly $3,700: "thats a lot of money but look what wear geting a new house" (001-023). The architeck (architect) mediates between Fred and the contractor, but Fred suspects collusion. By the story's end, the house is under construction — but Fred's tone has shifted from exuberance to a kind of weary accounting, the emotional template for all subsequent stories.

## Style & Form

"Own Your Own Home" establishes the formal template for the entire series. The epistolary structure — dated entries, direct address to "Brother Charley," formulaic closings ("Your aff. Brother, Fred") — creates an illusion of documentary authenticity that Lardner exploits for satiric effect. Fred's dialect voice is fully realized from the first letter: the comic misspellings ("bungello," "subbirds," "archateck"), the malapropisms, the characteristic rhythm of complaint and self-justification.

The story also introduces a distinctive structural feature: the financial ledger as narrative device. Fred's letters function like account books, tracking dollar amounts, interest rates, and payment schedules with obsessive specificity. This financial documentation serves a dual purpose — it grounds the story in material reality while revealing Fred's inability to distinguish between economic and emotional accounting. He tallies costs as if the sums will eventually add up to vindication.

> "I says to her what would we use for the payments & she says because you can borry the money & pay it back a little to a time & besides I got $500 in the bank." (001-005)

## Historical Context

The 1915 setting places the story at a pivotal moment in American suburban history. Chicago's commuter railroads had opened the western suburbs to middle-class settlement; the bungalow was the era's iconic house type, promoted by pattern books, magazines, and the federal government's "Own Your Own Home" campaign. Fred's $3,700 house-and-lot package was a realistic figure for a working-class or lower-middle-class family — roughly three to four years of a clerk's salary.

The banking practices Fred encounters were also historically accurate: savings banks in the 1910s were conservative lenders, typically requiring 40–50% down payments on home construction loans. The requirement to clear the lot before borrowing for construction was standard practice, not the imposition of a particularly difficult banker.

The title phrase "Own Your Own Home" was itself a period slogan, promoted by the National Association of Real Estate Boards and the U.S. Department of Labor as part of a campaign to increase American homeownership rates. Fred's story is, in this sense, a case study in how national ideology played out in individual lives — with all the friction, confusion, and unintended comedy that attended the translation of policy into practice.
