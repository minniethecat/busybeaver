# The Golf Caddie

The caddie who carries Fred Gross's clubs at the Allison Country Club is a silent presence in the stories — a figure whose labor is essential to Fred's golf but whose personhood is invisible to Fred's narration. The caddie embodies the class dynamics that the stories document but do not foreground.

## The Caddie's Role

In 1915, country club caddies were typically young working-class boys or men who carried members' clubs, located errant shots, tended the flag, and provided local knowledge of the course. They were paid modestly, tipped irregularly, and treated with the casual condescension that marked class relations in the era. The caddie was essential to the game but invisible to the social world that the game served.

> So they give me 3 balls and a boy to carry my bag and chase up the balls I knocked and then they showed me where to start at.
>
> (P03-017)

## Fred and the Caddie

Fred rarely mentions his caddie, and this omission is telling. The caddie is part of the equipment of golf — present, useful, taken for granted. Fred's preoccupation with his own performance and his own status leaves little attention for the working-class figures who make that performance possible.

## Class and Service

The caddie represents the broader class of service workers whose labor sustains the suburban middle class: servants, tradesmen, deliverymen, railway workers, postal carriers. These figures are everywhere in the stories but rarely named, rarely described, rarely acknowledged. Their invisibility is a structural feature of Fred's narrative perspective — and a structural feature of the class system he inhabits.
