# The Telephone in Allison

The telephone was still a relatively new technology in 1915, and its presence in the Fred Gross stories marks the arrival of modern communications in suburban life. The architect's daily calls, the social arrangements made by phone, the instrument's power to intrude and connect — all reflect a world in the midst of a communications revolution.

## The Instrument

A telephone in 1915 was typically a wooden box mounted on the wall, with a separate earpiece and a mouthpiece fixed to the box. Party lines — shared among multiple households — were common in smaller communities. The telephone was a substantial investment, and its presence in the Gross household is another marker of middle-class status.

## The Intrusion

The telephone's capacity for intrusion is dramatized in the architect's daily calls — "ringing me up on the phone all the wile a bout 5 times a day" (001-020). The phone brings the outside world into the home unbidden, and Fred's frustration with the architect is amplified by the medium through which the intrusion occurs. The telephone makes escape impossible.

## The Connection

The telephone also enables connection. Social arrangements — dinner invitations, party planning, club communications — are accelerated and simplified by the ability to speak directly rather than write letters. The telephone compresses social time, making the rapid exchanges of Allison's social economy possible.
