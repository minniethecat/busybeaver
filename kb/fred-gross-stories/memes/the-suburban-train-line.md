# The Suburban Train Line

The railroad line between Allison and Chicago is the geographic spine of the Fred Gross stories — the physical connection that makes Fred's divided life possible. Without the train line, there is no commute, no suburb, no Allison as we know it. The line is infrastructure so fundamental that it disappears into the background of daily life.

## The Corridor

The railroad corridor connecting Chicago to its suburbs was one of the great engineering achievements of the nineteenth century, and by 1915 it supported a complex system of commuter service. Towns like Allison existed because the railroad made them accessible — close enough to Chicago for daily commuting, far enough to feel like a separate world.

## The Schedule

The train schedule organizes Fred's day. The morning train determines when he must leave the house; the evening train determines when he can return. The schedule is inflexible — missing a train means waiting for the next, and the intervals between trains structure the possibilities of daily life. Fred's time is partly his own and partly the railroad's.

## The View

The view from the train window — the transition from urban density to suburban space, from factories to houses to fields — is the visual experience of the commute. Fred sees the landscape change daily, and the changing view marks his transition between identities. The railroad line is not just a route but a narrative: the story of leaving the city and coming home.
