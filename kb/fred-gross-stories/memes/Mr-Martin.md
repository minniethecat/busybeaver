# Mr. Martin

Mr. Martin is a neighbor of the Gross family in Allison whose arrival as a new resident and subsequent interactions with Fred contribute to the complex social dynamics of the suburban community Lardner depicts.

## Role & Overview

Mr. Martin and his wife move in next door to the Grosses: "this here Martin that moved in next door to us" (003-055). As newcomers, they become part of the neighborhood's social network and, inevitably, its conflicts. Fred's reports of his interactions with Martin reveal the ongoing negotiation of neighborly relations in the new suburb.

## Character Profile

### Background

Martin and his wife are new residents of Allison, arriving after the Grosses. The timing of their arrival places them in the position of newcomers — a status Fred once occupied — and Fred's treatment of them reflects both the empathy of shared experience and the competitiveness of established residents toward new arrivals.

### Personality & Traits

Fred's limited reports suggest Martin is a man of similar social standing to Fred — a neighbor, not a superior like Thomas or a rival like Hamilton. Their interactions are characterized by the ordinary rhythms of suburban acquaintance: shared train rides, casual conversations, the low-stakes social exchanges that constitute neighborhood life.

## Key Appearances

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Martin appears in the context of the practical joke narrative, his presence adding another layer to the neighborhood's social dynamics. Fred mentions him in the aftermath of a key encounter "on the same train to gather" (003-029).

Fred confronts his noisy neighbor — and flashes his badge:

> I slipped my over cote and sox and shoes on over my night gown and went over and rapped at the door and Mr. Martin come to the door and I says you better lay off on some of the rackit and he says what will you do if we dont and then I flashed my star on him and I says cut the noise out or youll get the worst of it.
>
> (003-029)

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

Martin and his wife are described as neighbors "on 1 side of you that you could" (004-002) get along with, in contrast to the Hamiltons on the other side. This explicit comparison establishes Martin as the "good neighbor" against which Hamilton's bad behavior is measured.

## Literary Significance

Martin serves as a structural counterweight to Hamilton. His presence demonstrates that neighborly conflict is not inevitable — that it is possible to live side by side without warfare — and thus makes Hamilton's antagonism a matter of character rather than circumstance.
