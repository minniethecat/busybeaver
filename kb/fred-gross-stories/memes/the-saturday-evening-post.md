# The Saturday Evening Post

The *Saturday Evening Post* was the primary venue for Ring Lardner's Fred Gross stories, which appeared in its pages between 1915 and 1919. The magazine was one of the most influential publications in American history — a weekly that reached millions of households and helped define the literary tastes of the middle class.

## The Magazine

Founded in 1821 and revitalized under editor George Horace Lorimer, the *Post* combined fiction, journalism, cartoons, and advertising into a package that appealed to the broad American middle class. Its covers — many by Norman Rockwell — became iconic. Its fiction pages published writers from F. Scott Fitzgerald to William Faulkner alongside popular humorists like Lardner.

The Fred Gross stories were originally published in the Saturday Evening Post:

> *Fred Gross Stories* was compiled from short stories published between 1915 and 1917 by Ring Lardner.
>
> (007-002–004)

## Lardner and the Post

Lardner's relationship with the *Post* was central to his career. The magazine's audience — middle-class, aspirational, hungry for entertainment that reflected their own experiences — was the ideal readership for the Fred Gross stories. Fred's voice, rendered in vernacular dialect and focused on the comic anxieties of suburban life, spoke directly to *Post* readers who recognized themselves in his struggles.

## Historical Significance

The *Post* represents a specific moment in American literary culture — a time when mass-circulation magazines served as the primary distribution channel for serious fiction. The Fred Gross stories were not published as literature but as entertainment, and their literary reputation grew later. The *Post* was the medium through which Lardner reached his audience, and understanding that medium is essential to understanding the stories.
