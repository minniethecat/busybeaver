# Mary Gross

Mary Gross, wife of Charley Gross and sister-in-law to Fred, exists entirely in the margins of the Fred Gross stories — invoked in every letter's closing salutation, never seen, yet an essential part of the domestic frame that gives the letters their intimate character.

## Role & Overview

Every letter Fred writes to his brother Charley ends with some variation of "Kindest to Mary" (001-007). Mary is never described, never quoted, never given independent existence — and yet her presence is constant. She is the silent partner in the fraternal correspondence, the implied reader of every word Fred writes, the domestic counterpart to Grace in the parallel household Fred imagines his brother maintaining.

Mary's invisibility is itself significant: it reflects the epistolary form's limitations (Fred writes to Charley, not to Mary) and the gender conventions of early 20th-century correspondence, where a husband's letter to his brother was implicitly also a communication to the entire household.

> Kindest to Mary and next time she comes to see you tell her to come and see us and me and Grace will try and show her a good time and she will find things a hole lot diffrunt then what they was in Chi because out here a person can feel like the night air was worth $1.00 per lb.
>
> (P03-010)

## Character Profile

### Background

Mary is married to Charley Gross and lives with him in an unspecified city. She and Charley presumably have children, making their household parallel to Fred and Grace's — a parallel Fred explicitly draws when he urges Charley to "get in to the game" (001-006) of suburban homeownership.

### Personality & Traits

Mary's personality can only be inferred from Fred's consistent inclusion of her in his closings — a gesture that suggests genuine affection rather than mere formula. The regularity of "Kindest to Mary" across all six stories suggests a stable, enduring family relationship.

## Literary Significance

Mary Gross exemplifies Lardner's ability to create presence through absence. She is never described, yet she is felt in every letter. Her invisibility is both a constraint of the epistolary form and a resource: the reader fills Mary in, imagines her receiving Fred's letters through Charley, and in doing so becomes an active participant in the story's domestic world.
