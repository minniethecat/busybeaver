# Servants and Domestic Help

The question of servants haunts the margins of the Fred Gross stories. A family of the Grosses' aspirations would, in 1915, typically employ at least a part-time domestic — a maid, a cook, or a cleaning woman. The Grosses' relationship to domestic help reveals the gap between their aspirations and their finances, and the broader class dynamics of early twentieth-century suburban life.

## The Servant Question

For middle-class families of the era, employing a servant was both practical necessity and social signaling. A maid demonstrated that the lady of the house was not required to perform manual labor; a cook demonstrated that the family could afford specialized help. The absence of servants was, conversely, a quiet admission of financial limitation.

## The Grosses' Position

Fred rarely mentions servants directly, and the omission is telling. The Grosses likely cannot afford regular domestic help, or can afford only occasional assistance. Grace manages the household herself — cooking, cleaning, caring for the children — and this fact places the family below the social tier they aspire to occupy. The absence of servants is one of the invisible constraints that define the Grosses' social position.

## Class and Domestic Labor

Lardner's treatment of domestic labor reflects the broader class structure of early twentieth-century America. The families who employ servants are the ones who have already arrived; the families who do not are still striving. The Grosses are strivers, and their relationship to domestic help — wanting it, perhaps needing it, but unable comfortably to afford it — encapsulates their position in the social hierarchy.
