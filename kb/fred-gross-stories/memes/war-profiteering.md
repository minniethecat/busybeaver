# War Profiteering

War profiteering—what Fred Gross calls "war bribes"—provides the thematic center of the fifth story, exposing the intersection of patriotism, business, and corruption in America during the First World War.

## Overview

In "War Bribes," Fred Gross becomes an enthusiastic participant in the wartime economy, investing in defense-related stocks and marveling at the profits being made. His characteristic moral blindness is on full display: he uses the language of patriotism to describe what is essentially profiteering, genuinely unable to perceive the ethical ambiguity of his investments.

Lardner uses the war profiteering theme to satirize American business culture more broadly. The men Fred encounters—factory owners, stock promoters, self-proclaimed patriots—are all profiting from the war while congratulating themselves on their public-spiritedness. Fred, as always, is both participant and dupe, eager to join the money-making while dimly sensing that something is not quite right.

## In the Stories

### War Bribes (Story 5)

The fifth story is built around the theme of war profiteering. Fred explains the term himself: "what they call the war bribes and the reason they call them" (005-029) is explained with characteristic Fred-logic. He observes that "some of the automobile plants and boat factorys" (005-030) are benefiting from wartime contracts, and he wants a share.

Fred's awareness of the ethical issues is characteristically selective. He notes that "the factorys keeps there self garded pretty" (005-031) close, implying that something is being hidden. Yet his solution is not to avoid these investments but to find a way in: "I got an" (005-042) inside track on one of the war contracts.

The specific investment that captures Fred's imagination involves "1 of these here war bribes Charley and its a big factory down in Ind. some wheres" (005-046). Fred's geography is vague, his understanding of the business even vaguer, but his enthusiasm is unbounded. The story traces his journey from eager investor to disillusioned participant, though his disillusionment—characteristically—never quite reaches self-awareness.

### Fore! (Preface)

The theme is introduced earlier in the Fore! section, where Fred notes that "prices was up on acct. of the war like every body tells you about" (P03-037). This casual observation establishes the wartime economy as background before it moves to the foreground in Story 5.

## Historical Context

The United States remained officially neutral in World War I until April 1917, but American industry was deeply involved in the war economy from 1914 onward. Manufacturers supplied both the Allied powers and, to a lesser extent, the Central Powers with everything from munitions to foodstuffs. This period saw massive profits for connected industrialists and widespread accusations of war profiteering.

The term "war bride" (which Fred renders as "war bribe") was common slang of the period, referring to companies and individuals who profited from defense contracts. Congressional investigations into war profiteering began even before American entry into the war, and the issue remained politically charged throughout the conflict.

## Literary Significance

Lardner's treatment of war profiteering is satirically precise. Fred Gross is the perfect vehicle for the theme: he is too naive to be genuinely corrupt, but too eager for profit to be innocent. His dialect narration transforms "war brides" (or "war bribes," as he has it) from a political issue into a personal adventure. The reader sees the ethical dimensions that Fred cannot, and the gap between the two perspectives is where Lardner's critique operates.

The story also functions as a period piece, capturing the mood of a nation poised between neutrality and engagement, where war was simultaneously a tragedy overseas and a business opportunity at home.
