# Golf

Golf in the Fred Gross stories functions as the primary vehicle for Ring Lardner's satire of middle-class social climbing, with Fred's fumbling attempts to master the game at the Allison Country Club exposing his earnest desire for respectability and his comic incomprehension of upper-class rituals.

## Overview

When Fred Gross moves to Allison, Illinois, one of his first acts is to join the Allison Country Club, where he takes up golf. Fred's relationship with golf is characteristically contradictory: he dismisses it as "a baby game" (P03-008) while simultaneously investing time and money in lessons and equipment. This tension between contempt and aspiration is central to Lardner's portrait of the American middle class—people who both resent and crave the markers of elite status.

Fred's golf education becomes a narrative thread through the Fore! section, as he progresses from complete novice to someone who can at least complete a round, though never with distinction. The golf course serves as a social theater where Fred navigates the unspoken rules of class, gender, and etiquette that he only dimly perceives.

## In the Stories

### Fore! (Preface)

The Fore! section—named after the golf warning cry—contains the richest concentration of golf material. Fred announces his membership in the Allison Country Club, clarifying that "it isent on acct. of playing golf that I have joined the club but mostly on acct. of the Mrs." (P03-004), revealing that his wife Grace's social ambitions drive the decision. When club member Mr. Ewing asks if he plays golf, Fred admits "I told him no, but I never seen no game yet I couldent pick up if I put my mind to it" (P03-006), displaying the blithe confidence that will soon be humbled.

Fred's first experience on the course confirms golf's difficulty. He reports that "I have broke in to the golf game and its a hole lot different game than I expected" (P03-015). His practice sessions involve "being back and 4th between the golf club and back" (P03-016), and he eventually takes "2 lessons from the golf teacher out to the club" (P03-028). The expense mounts: Fred's wife objects that he should "spend all your time out to the golf links" (P03-038), and Fred notes the rising cost of equipment because "theys a big demand for golf instruments now because the prices was up on acct. of the war" (P03-037).

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

Golf is not directly mentioned in Story 1, but the country club membership that Fred describes in Fore! establishes the social context for his suburban aspirations. The Allison Country Club represents the community he is buying into—literally and figuratively—with his lot purchase.

## Historical Context

Golf in America experienced its first major boom in the 1910s. The number of golf courses in the United States grew from approximately 1,000 in 1900 to over 4,000 by 1920. Country clubs proliferated in the new commuter suburbs ringing cities like Chicago, serving as social hubs for the aspiring middle class. Club membership fees, equipment costs, and lesson fees made golf a significant financial commitment—equivalent to weeks of a clerk's wages—making Fred's investment in the game a notable statement of social ambition.

## Literary Significance

Lardner uses golf as a satirical device of extraordinary precision. The game's elaborate etiquette, specialized vocabulary, and rigid class associations make it the perfect mirror for Fred's social pretensions. Fred's dialect narration—describing golf as "a baby game" while spending heavily on it—creates the ironic distance that defines Lardner's style. The reader sees what Fred cannot: that his desperate desire to fit in at the country club is itself the source of his exclusion.

Fred's characteristic ambivalence toward golf — contempt masking aspiration — is captured in his own words:

> I dont expect to get no excitement out of a baby game like golf but I dont half to play much and the main thing is being a member of the club.
>
> (P03-008)
