# Friendship and Sociability

Friendship in the Fred Gross stories is a complex phenomenon — part genuine affection, part social obligation, part strategic alliance. Fred values his friends, needs his friends, and is frequently disappointed by them, often for reasons he misidentifies. The social fabric of Allison is woven from these ambivalent bonds.

## The Currency of Friendship

Fred's friendships operate within an economy of reciprocity. Dinners are exchanged for dinners, favors for favors, invitations for invitations. When the Martins fail to respond to the Grosses' dinner invitation, the slight is felt as a debt unpaid — a violation of the implicit contract that governs neighborly relations. Friendship, in this world, is something you keep track of.

## Fred as Friend

Fred is a better friend in his own estimation than he likely is in practice. He values loyalty, expects it from others, and feels its absence acutely. But his own loyalty is conditional — contingent on reciprocity, vulnerable to resentment, easily redirected into grievance. His friendships are real but fragile, sustained by proximity and threatened by the constant friction of social comparison.

## Male Friendship

Fred's friendships with other men — Curtis, Martin, the members of the country club — are structured by the conventions of masculine sociability: golf, cards, drinks, mutual complaint about wives and work. These bonds are real but shallow, providing the pleasures of company without the demands of intimacy. Lardner's portrait of male friendship is unsentimental and, for that reason, convincing.
