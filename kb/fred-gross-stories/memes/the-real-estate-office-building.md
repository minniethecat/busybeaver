# The Real Estate Office

The real estate office is where Fred Gross's suburban dream begins — the place where he signs the papers that commit him to the lot in Allison and set the stories in motion. It is a space of persuasion, paperwork, and promises whose full implications Fred will not understand until long after he leaves.

## The Office Environment

A real estate office in 1915 was a place of desks, maps, contracts, and the reassuring presence of professional authority. The agent who shows Fred the lot operates from this office, and the office's atmosphere — businesslike, confident, established — lends credibility to the agent's claims. Fred enters as a customer and leaves as a debtor, the transition smoothed by the office's professional demeanor.

## The Transaction

The office is where the lot purchase becomes official — papers signed, deposit paid, obligations assumed. The physical act of signing transforms Fred's intentions into legal commitments, and the office's function is to make this transformation feel natural, even inevitable. Fred signs with enthusiasm, not realizing that his signature has committed him to more than he knows.

## The After-the-Fact

Fred returns to the real estate office only in retrospect, when the tax bill arrives and the agent's promises about "pretty near nothing" in taxes (001-022) are revealed as optimistic. The office, like the bank, is a place where Fred's memory of what was said differs from what was written, and the written version always prevails.
