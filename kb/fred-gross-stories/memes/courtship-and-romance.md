# Courtship and Romance

The Fred Gross stories are not love stories, but romance and courtship flicker at their edges — in the dances at the country club, the flirtations at parties, the remembered attractions of youth. The Grosses are already married, but the world around them is full of people who are not, and the rituals of courtship are part of the social landscape Fred navigates.

## Courtship Rituals

Dances, parties, outings — these are the venues where young people meet and pair. The Allison dance is an event where social skills are tested and romantic possibilities are explored. Fred, as a married observer, watches these rituals with a mixture of nostalgia and bemusement, aware that this phase of life is behind him.

## Marriage as Destination

In the world of the stories, courtship leads inexorably to marriage — the expected, indeed the only legitimate outcome. Romance is not an end in itself but a path to domesticity. The single people in the stories are understood to be in transition, moving toward the married state that the Grosses already occupy.

## Love in the Gross Marriage

Fred rarely speaks of love directly — it is not in his emotional vocabulary. But his partnership with Grace, for all its tensions and negotiations, contains evidence of genuine affection. They plan together, worry together, celebrate together. The love is there, even if Fred lacks the language to articulate it.
