# Alcohol

Drinking permeates the social world of the Fred Gross stories, from the country club bar to the neighbor's kitchen, serving as both lubricant for social interaction and a recurring source of Fred's financial and marital troubles.

## Overview

Fred Gross drinks. He drinks at the Allison Country Club, at home with neighbors, and in Chicago bars after work. His drinking is not portrayed as pathological but as normative—the expected behavior of a middle-class American man in 1915. Yet alcohol consistently leads Fred into trouble: unexpected bills, marital friction, professional embarrassment, and the hazy boundary between camaraderie and exploitation.

The stories' treatment of alcohol is characteristically Lardneresque: never moralistic, always observant. Fred's drinking is presented matter-of-factly, through his own unreliable narration, and the reader is left to draw conclusions that Fred himself never reaches.

## In the Stories

### Fore! (Preface)

The country club is the primary theater of Fred's drinking. In the Fore! section, Fred's interactions at the club bar establish the rituals of masculine sociability. When a fellow member asks what he wants, "I says a high ball just jokeing. But sure enough in a few minutes" (P03-057) the drink arrives—the joke becoming reality, the social obligation entangled with genuine desire.

The financial cost of club drinking emerges as a recurring anxiety. Fred signs for drinks, assuming they are complimentary, only to discover that "all that wine and them other drinks I signed for wasent free after all" (P03-116). The bill arrives as a shock: "they just as good as told me the drinks was free. What will you do about it" (P03-118). Fred's indignation at being charged reveals his ongoing naivety about how the club economy works.

Beyond the club, Fred recounts evenings where "I had a hole lot to drink and when I got up yesterday" (P03-084) felt the consequences. Social drinking extends to his marriage, as when Grace's country club rival is described as someone who might "set down with me and have a drink" (P03-085). Even the champagne flows freely: "all the champagne you want to drink by just signing your name" (P03-086)—a promise Fred learns is too good to be true.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

In Story 3, drinking intersects with the practical joke theme. Fred's hospitality includes beer for guests: "if they want to drink our beer the girls bow can pay for it" (003-004), and he prides himself on providing "my frends enough to eat and drink when they come to see me" (003-010).

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

In the Hamilton conflict, Fred and Grace discover that "I and Grace can drink the beer when wear thirsty" (002-039) from a shared source—a domestic detail that humanizes the characters while also revealing the centrality of alcohol to their domestic life.

## Historical Context

The Fred Gross stories are set in 1915–1917, on the eve of national Prohibition (the 18th Amendment was ratified in 1919, taking effect in 1920). The temperance movement was at its height, and many states and localities had already enacted dry laws. Illinois, however, remained largely wet, and Chicago's drinking culture was famously robust.

Country clubs of this era derived significant revenue from bar sales, and the practice of members signing for drinks (rather than paying cash) was standard. This system, which Fred finds so confusing and expensive, was designed to encourage consumption by deferring the awareness of cost—a dynamic that Lardner captures with characteristic precision.

## Literary Significance

Alcohol in Lardner's hands is never mere background detail. It is a social solvent that dissolves pretension and reveals character. Fred's drinking exposes his vulnerability: his desire to fit in, his susceptibility to social pressure, his inability to calculate the true cost of his choices. The bar tab that shocks him is both a literal financial shock and a metaphor for the hidden costs of social aspiration.

Lardner's treatment of alcohol also reflects the journalistic realism that was his trademark. As a sportswriter and columnist, Lardner spent years observing American drinking culture at close range, and his depiction of Fred's relationship with alcohol has the texture of lived experience.
