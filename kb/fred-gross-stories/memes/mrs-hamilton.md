# Mrs. Hamilton

A conspicuous presence in Allison's social landscape, Mrs. Hamilton serves as Grace Gross's principal rival for neighborhood status — a woman whose party invitations, social calls, and perceived slights drive key conflicts across three of Fred Gross's letters home.

## Role & Overview

Mrs. Hamilton appears as a supporting figure in three Fred Gross stories, her influence growing from minor mention in "Welcome to Our City" to central antagonist in "Uncivil War." She is never directly quoted — readers encounter her entirely through Fred's resentful narration and Grace's reported grievances — yet her offstage actions propel the series' most elaborate social conflict. The epistolary filter makes it impossible to know whether Mrs. Hamilton's offenses are real or imagined, which is precisely Lardner's satiric point about the petty preoccupations of suburban life. (002-025) (004-006)

## Character Profile

### Background

Mrs. Hamilton is married to [[Mr-Hamilton]], and the couple resides in Allison, Illinois, where they maintain a household that includes a "higher girl" (a live-in maid). Their economic standing appears comfortable: Mr. Hamilton's unspecified business role affords them the ability to host parties and maintain a servant, placing them at or slightly above the Gross family in Allison's finely calibrated social hierarchy. (004-069) (004-070)

### Personality & Traits

Fred's portrait of Mrs. Hamilton is consistently unflattering, filtered through Grace's rivalry with her. She is depicted as status-conscious, deliberate in her social maneuvers — particularly in the matter of paying (or withholding) social calls — and willing to weaponize party guest lists as instruments of exclusion. The key incidents suggest a woman who understands and exploits the unwritten rules of small-town social politics:

- **The unpaid call**: Grace is "kind of sore on Mrs. Hamilton for not comeing over to see us after we moved in our new house" — a breach of neighborly etiquette that festers into active resentment. (002-037)
- **The cold shoulder**: When Grace passes Mrs. Hamilton and Mrs. Carpenter on the street, "they dident some to recugnize her" — a calculated public snub. (003-019)
- **The party wars**: Mrs. Hamilton's Valentine's party becomes the battleground on which the Gross-Hamilton feud is fought, with Fred orchestrating a noise campaign to disrupt it. (004-014)

## Key Appearances

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

Mrs. Hamilton makes her first appearance when Grace greets her with forced cordiality: "Grace says how do you do Mrs. Hamilton I was afrade youd be out." Fred immediately undercuts the pleasantry by noting that neither he nor Mrs. Hamilton has actually done anything to the other — "nether I or Mrs. Hamilton have did any thing against the other" — establishing the dynamic of imagined grievance that will escalate in later stories. (002-025) (002-026)

- Grace feels slighted when Mrs. Hamilton fails to pay a welcoming call after the Grosses move into their new house. (002-037)
- The tension is initially played for minor comedy — Fred blames Mrs. Hamilton's rudeness on indigestion: "Mrs. Hamilton must of ett to much dinner." (002-027)

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Mrs. Hamilton appears at the periphery of the social whirl surrounding Fred and Grace's party:

- Grace reports that the Carpenters and Mrs. Hamilton snubbed her on the street. (003-019)
- Fred learns, via gossip passed through a "little girl," that Mrs. Hamilton belongs to an auction bridge club — a marker of social aspiration that Fred mocks. (003-048)
- When news of the Gross party spreads, Mrs. Hamilton "come runing to the door" in apparent alarm at the social competition. (003-054)

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

Mrs. Hamilton's most significant role. The story revolves around a feud sparked when the Hamiltons are not invited to a party at the Gross house:

- Fred admits visiting the Hamiltons' house at 1 p.m. "when Mrs. Hamilton was giveing a party" — an uninvited drop-in that may have caused embarrassment. (004-006)
- Grace learns that "Mrs. Hamilton is going to give a party the day after tomorrow night" — a Valentine's Day event pointedly scheduled without inviting the Grosses. (004-014)
- Fred and Grace retaliate by planning to disrupt the Hamilton party with noise, then "beat it home." (004-017)
- In the aftermath, Grace concedes that "it probly wasent Mrs. Hamiltons falt but just Hamilton him self" — a rare moment of qualified fairness toward her rival. (004-057)
- Mr. Hamilton later tells Fred that "both him and Mrs. Hamilton and there higher girl thot the party was a big success" — the maid's opinion being solicited underscores the Hamilton's social aspirations. (004-069)
- The root cause of the feud is traced to an unpaid social call: "Mrs. Hamilton hadent payed no call on Mrs. Gross after the Grosses moved" — a breach of 1910s suburban etiquette serious enough to justify (in Grace's view) social warfare. (004-070)

## Relationships

- **[[Grace-Gross]]** — Chief social rival. The two women compete for status through party-giving, social calls, and neighborhood influence. The rivalry is conducted entirely through indirect means: guest lists, street snubs, and third-party gossip.
- **[[Mr-Hamilton]]** — Husband. Mrs. Hamilton's social ambitions appear to be a joint project; Mr. Hamilton defends his wife's reputation and reports her opinions to Fred.
- **[[Fred-Gross]]** — Narrator and indirect antagonist. Fred never reports a direct conversation with Mrs. Hamilton, yet he orchestrates the disruption of her Valentine's party and shapes the reader's entire perception of her.
- **[[Mr-Carpenter]]** / [[Carry-Carpenter]] — Social allies. The Carpenters and Hamiltons appear to move as a social bloc, jointly snubbing Grace and jointly being snubbed in return.

## Literary Significance

Mrs. Hamilton is a masterful example of Lardner's technique of the absent antagonist — a character whose power derives precisely from never being heard in her own voice. Everything the reader knows about her comes through Fred, whose resentment is transparently petty and whose account is demonstrably unreliable. The feud between Grace Gross and Mrs. Hamilton — fought over party invitations, social calls, and perceived slights — is Lardner's satire of the suburban social order, where status is measured in the smallest currencies imaginable.

The epistolary form compounds the irony: Fred writes to Charley as if these neighborhood skirmishes were events of genuine importance, never suspecting that his earnest reporting reveals more about his own small-mindedness than about Mrs. Hamilton's character. The reader is left to wonder whether Mrs. Hamilton is a genuine social manipulator or simply a woman going about her life while the Grosses project their insecurities onto her — and the stories are richer for the ambiguity.

Grace meets Mrs. Hamilton for the first time:

> A womman come to the door & Grace says how do you do Mrs. Hamilton I was afrade youd be out & the womman says who did you want to see & Grace says you or Mr. Hamilton or the both of them.
>
> (002-025)
