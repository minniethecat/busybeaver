# The Allison Dance

The dance held at the Allison opera house — a social event from "Welcome to Our City" (story-02) that represents both the allure and the financial burden of suburban social life for the Gross family.

## Overview

In June 1916, Fred and Grace Gross received an invitation to a dance at the Allison opera house. The event was a highlight of Allison's social calendar, but for Fred it represented yet another expense — dancing lessons, new clothes, babysitting arrangements — in a life already stretched thin by the costs of homeownership. (002-057)

## Background

Social life in Allison revolved around a circuit of entertainments: card parties, dinners, and dances. For newcomers like the Grosses, attending these events was both a pleasure and an obligation — a way to meet neighbors and establish social standing. Fred, however, was acutely aware of the financial implications. (002-058)

Grace, eager to participate in Allison's social scene, was determined to attend. Fred's resistance was characteristically financial: he calculated the cost of tickets, dancing lessons, and the inevitable new dress, and worried aloud to Charley. (002-060)

## Key Events

- **The invitation**: Fred received an invitation to a dance scheduled for the 17th at the Allison opera house. The timing was inconvenient — Fred was still recovering from the expenses of the move and the sidewalk assessment. (002-057) (002-058)

Fred's report of the invitation, misaddressed to the Hamiltons, is classic Gross logic:

> A little boy come to the house after supper tonight & give me a letter only it wasent no letter but a invatation to a dance here in Allison the 17 of this mo. & the dance is being gave for the benifit of the Bellgiums that got killed in the war. This here invatation was addressed to the Hamiltons but if we get theres they got ourn so whats the diffrunts...
>
> (002-057)

- **Dancing lessons**: Grace insisted that Fred needed dancing lessons before the event. Fred grumbled about the cost but ultimately capitulated. (002-060) (002-061)

- **The babysitting problem**: Finding someone to watch the children during the dance proved difficult. Fred suggested asking Mrs. Carry, but Grace worried about imposing. (002-066) (002-070)

- **The dance itself**: Fred attended the dance, spent money he didn't have, and watched Grace perform all the latest steps. The evening was, by Fred's own account, a success — but the expense lingered in his mind as another cost of the suburban life he had not fully anticipated. (002-065)

## Aftermath & Consequences

The Allison dance was a social success but a financial irritant — a pattern that repeated throughout the Fred Gross stories. Fred's letter to Charley about the dance is typical of his narrative voice: proud of Grace's social triumph, resentful of the cost, and vaguely suspicious that the whole enterprise was designed to separate him from his money.

## Literary Significance

Lardner uses the dance episode to illustrate the gap between Fred's pragmatic, cost-conscious worldview and the social expectations of suburban life. The dance is never described in romantic or celebratory terms — it appears in Fred's letter only as a line item in his running budget of grievances.
