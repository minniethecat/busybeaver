# The Bank Lobby

The bank lobby is the architectural expression of institutional finance — a space designed to communicate solidity, permanence, and authority. Fred Gross enters this space as a customer and is transformed, by its architecture, into a supplicant.

## The Architecture of Authority

Bank lobbies in the early twentieth century were designed to inspire confidence and enforce deference. High ceilings, marble surfaces, the hushed acoustics of serious transactions — every element communicated that this was a place of consequence, where important decisions were made by important men. Fred, whose own architectural experience is limited to his bungalow and his flat, is overmatched by the space before he speaks a word.

## The Counter

The counter is the physical interface between customer and institution — a barrier that separates Fred from the bank's operations. Transactions are conducted across this barrier, and its height, its solidity, its material — typically marble or polished wood — communicate distance even as they enable exchange. Fred approaches the counter as a petitioner, not a partner.

## The Real Estate Department

The real estate department, where Fred negotiates his building loan, is a space within the larger space of the bank — set apart, specialized, perhaps less grand but no less institutional. It is here that Fred's assumptions about credit and worthiness collide with the bank's institutional logic, and the physical setting amplifies the asymmetry of the encounter.
