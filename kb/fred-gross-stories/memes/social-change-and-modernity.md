# Social Change and Modernity

The Fred Gross stories unfold at a moment of accelerated transformation. The automobile is replacing the horse, the suburb is replacing the urban neighborhood, consumer credit is replacing saving, and a world war is reshaping the global order. Fred Gross lives in the midst of these changes, experiencing them not as historical forces but as the texture of daily life.

## Technology and Daily Life

The technologies that define modern life — the automobile, the telephone, the electric light, the mass-circulation newspaper — are all present in the stories, mostly as background. Fred takes them for granted, as people do with new technologies once they have become familiar. But Lardner's attention to these details captures a world in transition, when the infrastructure of modern life was still new enough to be noticed.

## Economic Transformation

The credit economy, the consumer marketplace, the professionalization of work — these are the economic dimensions of modernity that Fred navigates with incomplete success. He is a modern economic actor (borrower, consumer, commuter) who still thinks in older economic categories (thrift, self-sufficiency, the value of a bargain). The tension between his economic behavior and his economic understanding is one of the stories' recurring themes.

## Social Transformation

The social world Fred inhabits — the suburb, the country club, the commuting marriage — is itself a product of modernity. These institutions did not exist a generation earlier; they were created by the same forces that created Fred. His struggles to navigate them are, in this sense, the struggles of a man learning the rules of a game that was invented while he was learning to play it.
