# Mr. Carlton

Mr. Carlton is a member of the Allison Country Club and part of Fred Gross's regular golf foursome, appearing alongside Mr. Thomas and Mr. Carpenter in the social circle that defines Fred's experience of the club.

## Role & Overview

Carlton appears as one of the foursome Fred plays golf with: "the 4 was I and Mr. Thomas and Mr. Carlton and Mr. Carpenter" (P03-049). Beyond this mention, Carlton is given no individual characterization — he is defined entirely by his membership in the group. This is itself significant: Carlton represents the anonymous social mass of the country club, the men whose presence constitutes the institutional environment Fred is trying to join.

> It seems like there trying to build a new club house so they been makeing a play for new members to get there money and thats how I come to join because I wouldent of never thought of it only for them comeing around to the house and soliciting me. It was Mr. Carlton the president of the village and Mr. Ewing thats in the coal business down town that ast me to join.
>
> (P03-005)

## Character Profile

Carlton's background, personality, and role are not individually specified. He exists as a name in a list — a member of the club, a partner in a golf round, a node in the social network. His anonymity reflects the reality of club life: not every member is a Thomas or an Ewing. Most are Carltons — present, participating, unremarkable.

## Literary Significance

Carlton's near-invisibility is his literary function. He represents the social background against which the more vividly drawn characters — Thomas, Carpenter, Hamilton — stand out. His presence in Fred's golf foursome establishes the normality of club life: four men, eighteen holes, the ordinary rhythms of masculine sociability into which Fred is being initiated.
