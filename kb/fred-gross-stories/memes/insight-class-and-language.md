# Class and Language in the Fred Gross Stories

Fred Gross's language is a class marker of extraordinary precision. Every phonetic spelling, every nonstandard verb form, every colloquial idiom locates Fred in a specific position in the American class structure — not quite working class, not quite middle class, aspiring to a linguistic standard he cannot consistently maintain.

## The Sociolect of Aspiration

Fred's language is not the language of any real demographic but a constructed sociolect — Lardner's invention, designed to signal specific social information. The phonetic spellings ("bungello," "onct," "ast") signal limited formal education. The grammatical patterns (double negatives, nonstandard past tenses) signal vernacular speech patterns. The vocabulary (business terms, club terminology, consumer goods) signals Fred's zones of cultural competence.

## What Fred's Language Reveals

Fred's language reveals what he cannot directly say: his educational level, his social origins, his cultural aspirations. When Fred uses a word he has only heard spoken, his phonetic spelling betrays his unfamiliarity with its written form. When he deploys business jargon, his slight misuse of terms signals that he is performing a competence he does not fully possess. The language is a continuous, involuntary self-portrait.

## Language and Power

The standard English that Fred cannot consistently produce is the language of power in his world — the language of the bank officer, the architect, the real estate agent. Fred's linguistic "errors" place him at a disadvantage in every institutional encounter, and his awareness of this disadvantage — never explicitly stated but palpable in his defensiveness — adds another layer to his social anxiety.
