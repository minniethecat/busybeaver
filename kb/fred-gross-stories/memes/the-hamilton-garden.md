# The Hamilton Garden

The Hamiltons' garden is a contested space — a boundary zone between two households whose relationship oscillates between cordiality and open warfare. It is where the quarantine sign is planted, where the noise campaign is conducted, where the symbolic border between the Gross and Hamilton domains is negotiated.

## The Garden as Boundary

In the close-quartered geography of suburban Allison, gardens define the boundaries between households. The Hamilton garden abuts or faces the Gross property, making it a zone of mutual visibility and potential friction. What happens in the garden is visible to the neighbors, and visibility in Allison is always socially significant.

> Well Charley this is a funny World where a man has neighbors on 1 side of you that you couldent fine no better peopl no wheres and on the other side peopl that they aint nothing to mean and small for them to do and allways trying to make you missable.
>
> (004-002)

## The Stage of Conflict

When Fred and Mr. Martin plan their prank — placing a quarantine sign on the Hamilton porch — the garden is the approach route and the sightline. The garden becomes a theater of operations in the neighborly cold war. Its flowers and shrubs, planted for beauty, become cover for hostile action.

## The Symbolic Landscape

Gardens in the stories are never merely decorative. They are statements of domestic competence, assertions of property rights, performances of middle-class respectability. The Hamilton garden, whatever its horticultural merits, is primarily a social artifact — a claim about who the Hamiltons are and where they stand in Allison's hierarchy.
