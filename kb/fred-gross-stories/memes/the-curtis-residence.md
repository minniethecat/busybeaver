# The Curtis Residence

The Curtis home occupies a prominent position in Fred Gross's mental map of Allison — a house that represents the standard of established suburban domesticity that Fred aspires to match. Like the Carpenter residence, it is a benchmark, a point of comparison, and a quiet reminder of how far Fred still has to go.

## The Established Neighbor

The Curtises are among Allison's established families — people who were there before the Grosses, whose social position is secure, whose entertaining sets the standard Fred struggles to meet. Their home reflects this security: it is not showy but substantial, not anxious but assured. For Fred, who experiences his own home as a work in progress, the Curtis residence represents arrival.

## The Social Venue

When the Curtises entertain, their home becomes a theater of social performance in which Fred is a player rather than the director. He is guest, not host; judged, not judging. His accounts of evenings at the Curtis home reveal both his pleasure in being included and his discomfort at being on display.

> The party last night was over to Curtis house the wood and coal man & no wonder his wife ast us to the party because there geting a bout $20.00 dollars per mo. out of us for 2 & ½ ton of coal & it wouldent hurt them none to give us a party onct a mo. & have champane wine but they dident give us no wine only beer & not hardley enough of that to wet your tungue.
>
> (003-010)

## The Comparative Gaze

Fred's descriptions of the Curtis residence are filtered through comparison with his own bungalow. He notices what they have that he lacks, what they do that he cannot yet do. The house is not merely a dwelling but a datum in Fred's ongoing calculus of social standing, and its value in this calculus is measured by its distance from his own.
