# Education and Culture

Fred Gross's relationship to education and culture is characteristically ambivalent. He respects formal learning without possessing it, values refinement without understanding it, and measures himself against standards he only partially grasps. His insecurity about his own education colors his interactions with those he perceives as more cultured than himself.

## Fred's Education

Fred's formal education is limited — his phonetic spelling and nonstandard grammar are Lardner's clearest signals of a man whose schooling ended early. But Fred is not unintelligent; he is undereducated, and he is aware of the deficit even as he compensates with native shrewdness. His letters to Charley are, in their way, an ongoing education — an effort to make sense of a world that is more complex than his schooling prepared him for.

## Culture as Social Marker

In Allison, culture is performed rather than pursued. The piano in the parlor signals refinement even if no one plays it well. Attendance at lectures, concerts, or theater in Chicago is a mark of distinction. Fred participates in these performances without entirely believing in them — he wants the status that culture confers without the effort that culture requires.

## The Arnoldian Gap

Lardner's treatment of culture recalls Matthew Arnold's conception of culture as "the best that has been thought and said" — but seen from below, from the perspective of a man who knows such things exist but cannot quite reach them. Fred's relationship to culture is aspirational but incomplete, like his relationship to everything else.
