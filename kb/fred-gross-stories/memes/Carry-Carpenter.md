# Carry Carpenter

The Carrys (Carry Carpenter and his wife) are neighbors of the Gross family in Allison, Illinois, whose household serves as a social hub, childcare resource, and site of neighborly interaction in the Fred Gross stories.

## Role & Overview

The Carrys appear primarily in "Welcome to Our City" (Story 2), where they function as established community members — the kind of neighbors who know everyone, host gatherings, and provide the social infrastructure of suburban life. Grace Gross leaves the children "over to the Carrys wile she was makeing the calls" (002-038), and the Carrys' home is a gathering place for the neighborhood.

The Carrys represent the established social order into which newcomers like the Hamiltons must be integrated. Their centrality to neighborhood life makes them arbiters of community acceptance — and their judgments about Fred and Grace matter.

## Character Profile

### Background

Carry Carpenter and his wife live in Allison with what Fred describes as "a sistern" (002-036) — likely a cistern, indicating a well-established property with improvements. They are among the longer-established residents of the community.

### Personality & Traits

Mrs. Carry is portrayed as socially active and connected. She knows the neighborhood, makes judgments about its residents, and her opinions carry weight. Fred reports that Grace consulted Mrs. Carry about the Hamiltons and that Mrs. Carry's assessment was not entirely favorable: "the Carrys wasent no good any way" (002-070).

## Key Appearances

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The Carrys are central to the social network described in Story 2. Grace uses their home as a base for her social calls, leaving the children there while she visits the newly arrived Hamiltons. The Carrys host an evening gathering: "The Carrys is comeing over tonight to play" (002-053). Mrs. Carry provides the social intelligence that guides Grace's approach to the new neighbors.

Grace Gross visits the Carpenters during her inaugural round of neighborly calls:

> Grace went to the Carpenters and rung the door bell and Mr. Carpenter him self come to the door and she give him 1 of the cards and he says well whats the charge and she seen he was makeing the same miss take Mr. Hamilton made so she went a long with it.
>
> (002-028)

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Carry Carpenter appears in the context of the ongoing practical joke competition, with the Carrys' social position making them important witnesses to — and judges of — the escalating neighborly conflicts.

## Literary Significance

The Carrys exemplify Lardner's skill at populating a social world through brief but telling details. They appear in only a few scenes, but their presence establishes the density of suburban social life — the network of mutual observation, judgment, and obligation that constrains and defines Fred's world.
