# The First Golf Round

Fred's first golf round is a rite of passage executed badly — a public display of incompetence that he recounts with characteristic mixture of self-deprecation and wounded pride. It is one of the most richly comic sequences in the Fore! section and establishes the pattern of golf as an arena of masculine inadequacy.

## The Build-Up

Fred approaches his first round with the confidence of a man who has taken two lessons and believes this is sufficient preparation. "I have took 2 lessons from the golf teacher out to the club" (P03-028), he tells Charley, as if this modest investment should have transformed him into a competent player. The gap between Fred's expectations and reality is the engine of the comedy that follows.

## The Round

The actual golf is a disaster. Fred "broke in to the golf game and its a hole lot different game than what you would think from just takeing lessons" (P03-037). The ball does not cooperate. His swings fail to connect. "I was so sick I couldent hardly see and I mist the ball entirely" (P03-101) — the humiliation is physical, visceral, public. Mr. Thomas, the club member who agreed to play with him, witnesses Fred's incompetence at close range.

> Fredericks shot 1st and his ball went about 150 yds. and then it was my turn and I missed the ball the 1st time and Fredericks says I tried to hit it too hard. Just go easy at 1st he says. So I hit at it easy and missed it again on acct. of hitting the ground behind it and then I swang easy again and hit it this time but it only went 5 or 6 ft.
>
> (P03-018)

## The Aftermath

Fred's response to failure is characteristic: he blames the conditions, the clubs, the unfairness of the handicapping system — anything but his own lack of skill. Yet he also resolves to continue. "I am not no yellow dog," he insists (P03-092), and he will keep trying. Fred's persistence in the face of humiliation is both admirable and ridiculous — the quality that makes him more than a mere comic foil.

## Significance

The first golf round is a microcosm of Fred's broader social experience. He enters a new arena believing that wanting to belong is the same as belonging. He discovers that the rules are more complex than he imagined. He fails publicly. And he resolves to try again. The pattern repeats across every domain of his life.
