# Banking

Banking institutions stand between Fred Gross and his dream of homeownership, their policies and procedures exposing the gap between middle-class aspiration and the financial system's indifference to individual circumstance.

## Overview

Fred Gross's encounter with the banking system is a central episode in "Own Your Own Home." Having purchased a lot in Allison with $800 in savings and a $700 promissory note, Fred approaches his bank's real estate department seeking a building loan. His confidence—based on the simple logic that he owns a lot, therefore he should be able to borrow against it—collides with the bank's institutional procedures, and the collision is the emotional core of the first story.

The bank functions in Lardner's narrative as an impersonal force, neither malicious nor benevolent, simply indifferent. Its rules exist independent of Fred's needs or character. The gap between Fred's understanding of how the world should work and the bank's actual procedures is the gap that Lardner's satire inhabits.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The banking sequence unfolds across several letters. Fred begins with confidence: "I had got out my bank book & seen where I had a bout $300 & with her $500 that makes a bout $800" (001-005). The pooled savings of husband and wife represent their foothold in the financial system—modest but real.

After purchasing the lot, Fred goes "a round to the bank where I had my saveings at & went to the real estate dept." (001-017). He asks "did they make building lones & the man says yes." But when Fred explains his situation—lot purchased with a balance still owed—the banker's response is definitive: "we dont do no busness that way & 1st you got to pay off what you owe on the lot & then may be we can fix it up a bout a building lone but I wont promus nothing" (001-017).

Fred argues but "nothing doing." The bank's logic is sound from an institutional perspective, but from Fred's perspective it is baffling and cruel. He already owns the lot (he has the deed and title guarantee), so why can't he borrow to build? The answer—that the bank requires clear title before extending construction credit—is a distinction Fred cannot fully grasp.

### Across Other Stories

Banking themes recur in more peripheral ways. In "War Bribes," Fred's involvement with "automobile stock" (005-081) represents a different kind of financial participation—speculation rather than savings. And throughout the stories, Fred's constant calculation of costs, savings rates, and payment schedules reveals a mind shaped by the discipline of household finance.

## Historical Context

American banking in the 1910s was undergoing significant change. The Federal Reserve System was established in 1913, and consumer credit was expanding beyond traditional mortgage lending. However, building loans for individual homeowners remained relatively uncommon—most construction was financed by builders or through savings and loan associations, not commercial banks.

The savings account that Fred and Grace maintain was typical of working-class and lower-middle-class financial practice in this period. Banks paid modest interest on savings deposits, and the passbook (Fred's "bank book") was a physical token of financial respectability. The $800 that Fred and Grace have accumulated represents significant discipline given Fred's likely salary of $1,500–$2,000 annually.

## Literary Significance

The bank scene in Story 1 is a miniature masterpiece of Lardner's technique. Fred narrates his encounter with the banker in his characteristic dialect, and the clash between Fred's vernacular and the bank's institutional language exposes the class barrier that Fred cannot see. The banker is not cruel or dishonest—he simply operates in a different linguistic and conceptual world. Fred's inability to bridge that gap is both comic and poignant.
