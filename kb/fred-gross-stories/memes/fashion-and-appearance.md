# Fashion and Appearance

In the world of the Fred Gross stories, what you wear is who you are — or at least who you want to be seen as. Clothing is the most visible form of social signaling, and Fred's attention to what people wear reveals his anxious navigation of Allison's class hierarchy.

## Dressing for the Club

The Allison Country Club demands a certain standard of dress, and Fred is acutely aware of the wardrobe required. Golf clothes, club attire, the right hat — these are not optional accessories but tickets to belonging. Fred's investment in his golf wardrobe parallels his investment in the bungalow: both are bets on a future self, a version of Fred Gross who belongs unambiguously in the suburban middle class.

## Women's Fashion

Grace's clothing receives Fred's characteristic blend of admiration and cost-consciousness. Dresses for parties, hats for church, the right outfit for calling on neighbors — each purchase is a negotiation between Grace's social ambitions and Fred's financial anxiety. The women of Allison judge each other by what they wear, and Grace cannot afford to fall behind.

## The Hat as Symbol

The hat deserves special attention in Lardner's world. A man's hat signals his occupation, his class, his aspirations. Fred's hats — the golf cap, the business hat, the Sunday-best hat — mark his movement between social spheres. Removing the hat indoors, tipping it to a lady, wearing it at the correct angle — these small rituals encode the social knowledge that Fred is still learning.
