# Pride and Resentment

Fred Gross is a man of considerable pride and even greater resentment. The two emotions are intertwined: every wound to his pride generates a resentment, and every resentment is experienced as a legitimate grievance against someone who has failed to recognize his worth.

## The Sources of Pride

Fred's pride is rooted in his sense of himself as a man who has earned his place. He works, he saves, he provides for his family. He has bought a lot, built a house, joined a club. These are not merely possessions but certificates of worth, and when others fail to acknowledge them, Fred's pride curdles into indignation.

## Resentment as Fuel

Resentment is the emotional engine of many of Fred's actions. The prank on the Hamiltons is fueled by resentment at their perceived social superiority. His complaints about the architect are fueled by resentment at being treated as someone whose opinion does not matter. His account of the bank loan negotiation is structured around resentment at a system that refuses to recognize his creditworthiness.

## The Comic Dimension

Lardner's treatment of Fred's pride is characteristically double-edged. Fred's sense of injury is genuine, and the reader can sympathize with his frustration even while recognizing that his expectations are unrealistic. The comedy arises from the gap between the magnitude of Fred's grievances and the triviality of their causes — a gap that Fred himself never perceives.
