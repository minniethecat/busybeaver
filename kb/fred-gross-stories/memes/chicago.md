# Chicago

The city whose Loop serves as Fred Gross's workplace, whose Police Department provides a benchmark of official authority, and whose distance from Allison — 16 miles by commuter railroad — defines the spatial rhythm of the Fred Gross stories.

## Overview

Chicago appears in the stories not as a directly depicted setting but as an implied presence: the place Fred goes to work, the metropolitan center against which Allison's suburban smallness is measured, and the source of the economic infrastructure (banks, railroads, real estate markets) that shapes the Gross family's life. Every letter Fred writes is datelined either "Chicago" (from his office) or "Allison, Ill." (from home), and the alternation maps the geographic division of his existence — work in the city, life in the suburbs.

The city's presence is most tangible in the datelines that structure the first story: "Chicago, May 3," "Chicago, May 5," "Chicago, May 7" (001-001, 001-009, 001-015). Fred writes from his Chicago office, where the bank, the real estate department, and the professional world are all located.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The Chicago datelines frame the entire narrative. Fred conducts his financial business — bank visits, real estate transactions, architect consultations — from his Chicago workplace. The spatial pattern is established: decisions are made in the city, but their consequences play out in Allison. "I went a round to the bank where I had my saveings at & went to the real estate dept." (001-017) — these are Chicago institutions, not Allison ones.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Chicago appears as a point of comparison: "they talk about Chicago being a live place well Charley they ought to see Allison" (003-009). Fred's boast is characteristically ironic — Allison's "liveliness" consists of card parties and social feuds, not urban excitement. The comparison reveals Fred's provincialism even as he intends to demonstrate sophistication.

### The Swift Six (Story 6)

The automobile Fred purchases is offered "fob Chicago" (006-079) — the city as commercial hub, the origin point for consumer goods that flow outward to the suburbs. The phrase also echoes the railroad terminology (Free On Board) that structures Fred's geographic world.

## Historical & Geographic Context

In 1915, Chicago was America's second-largest city (population ~2.2 million), a manufacturing and railroad hub whose growth had been explosive since the Great Fire of 1871. The Loop — the central business district encircled by elevated railroad tracks — was one of the densest concentrations of commercial activity in the world. Commuter railroads radiated in every direction, carrying white-collar workers between downtown offices and suburban homes in a pattern that Fred Gross exemplifies.

The 16-mile distance between the Loop and Allison was a realistic commute for the era: steam-hauled commuter trains on the CB&Q and C&NW lines made the trip in roughly 30–40 minutes. The "c d & x" railroad Fred mentions appears to be a fictional composite of these real lines.

Fred describes Allison's geographic relationship to Chicago:

> We got our lot all ready & its out to Allison 16 mi. west of the Loop on the c d & x & it dont only take 40 min. for the trains to come in to town & it takes me 35 min. now to go in on the L.
>
> (001-010)
