# Money and Finance

The Fred Gross stories are, at their economic core, a ledger of middle-class aspiration in 1915 America — every letter bristles with dollar amounts, loan terms, interest rates, and the gap between what a man earns and what his wife believes they deserve.

## Overview

Money is the most frequently invoked concrete subject in the Fred Gross corpus, appearing in some form in every story. Fred's letters meticulously document the family's financial position: savings ($500 in the bank at the series' opening), debts (the $1,500 lot purchase, the $2,000 construction budget), income ("makeing good money now"), and expenditures (club dues, party costs, automobile prices). This financial specificity serves a dual literary purpose: it grounds Fred's narrative in documentary reality while exposing the precariousness of his middle-class position — a man perpetually one contractor's bill or social obligation away from embarrassment. ## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The entire plot is a financial transaction. Fred and Grace decide to buy a lot in Allison for $1,500, putting $100 down immediately. Grace has $500 in savings; Fred has additional funds. The plan is to borrow the construction cost from Fred's bank. The story unfolds as a series of financial revelations:

- The banker requires the lot to be paid in full before any construction loan is considered: "we dont do no busness that way." (001-017)
- The family commits to an aggressive savings plan: Grace will bank money from her millinery work while Fred covers living expenses. (001-004)
- Fred discovers the layered costs of homebuilding — architect's fees, insurance, the bank's commission, contractor payments — each revealed like a fresh wound: "if it wasent to late Id tell the bank to go to." (001-035)
- The total construction budget is approximately $2,000, bringing the all-in cost to roughly $3,700: "thats a lot of money but look what wear geting a new house." (001-023)

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

Financial themes recede somewhat as social conflicts take the foreground, but the economic infrastructure remains visible: the house that money built is now the stage on which social dramas play out. The street paving dispute — who pays for improvements, and at what cost — extends the financial theme into municipal politics. (002-011)

### The Swift Six (Story 6)

The automobile purchase brings financial anxiety roaring back. Fred buys a used Swift Six for $200, only to discover that the car requires endless additional investment — repairs, tires, a garage. The transaction becomes another case study in Fred's pattern of optimistic under-budgeting followed by resentful cost-accounting:

> "I says to him what do you mean by a car & he says that there Swift of mine will be a car when I get it paid for." (006-036)

## Historical Context

The financial world Fred navigates reflects the real economic landscape of 1915 America:

| Institution | Role in the Stories | Historical Note |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Savings banks | Fred's $500 savings; mortgage lending | 1910s banks were conservative lenders; home mortgages typically required 40–50% down |
| Real estate developers | The Allison lot sale ($1,500 / 100 × 150 ft) | Chicago's suburban land boom was fueled by rail expansion and developer salesmanship |
| Building contractors | The $2,000 construction budget and payment disputes | Small contractors operated on thin margins; disputes over change orders were common |
| Country clubs | "Because you payed your money just the same" | Club membership signified arrival in the middle class; dues were a recurring anxiety |
| Automobile dealers | The $200 Swift Six | Used cars were emerging as an affordable entry point to automobile ownership |

The dollar amounts Fred cites are illuminating: $3,700 for a new bungalow on a suburban lot represents roughly 3–4 years of a clerk's salary — a serious but achievable commitment for a white-collar family willing to save aggressively.

## Literary Significance

Lardner's use of money in the Fred Gross stories operates on three levels:

**As documentary realism**: The precise dollar amounts, bank procedures, and contract terms give the stories the texture of authentic reportage. Fred writes about money the way a man writes about something that genuinely preoccupies him — with specificity born of anxiety.

**As character revelation**: Fred's financial narration reveals him as a man caught between aspiration and competence. He signs contracts he doesn't fully understand, trusts contractors who overcharge him, and then rehearses his grievances at length to Charley — always the victim, never the negotiator. His financial decisions follow a pattern: impulsive commitment, belated discovery of the true cost, resentful compliance.

**As social satire**: The financial content exposes the gap between the Gross family's means and their aspirations. Grace wants the country club, the new house, the automobile, the ability to host parties — and Fred, despite his grumbling, wants to be the man who can provide them. The tension between "makeing good money now" and the endless drain of suburban respectability is the engine that drives the entire series.
