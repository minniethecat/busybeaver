# The Allison Druggist

The drugstore in a town like Allison was more than a pharmacy — it was a general merchant, a soda fountain, a social gathering place, and a node in the community's information network. The druggist was a familiar figure, dispensing medicine and conversation in equal measure.

## The Drugstore in 1915

The American drugstore of the 1910s was a hybrid institution. It sold prescription medicines compounded on site, patent remedies of dubious efficacy, toiletries, cigarettes, candy, and — at the soda fountain — ice cream and soft drinks. It was one of the few businesses that served both medical and social functions, and its hours were often longer than other shops.

## The Druggist's Role

The druggist occupied a distinctive position in the community — not quite a doctor, not quite a shopkeeper, but someone trusted with intimate knowledge of families' health and habits. He knew who was sick, who was recovering, what remedies were being purchased. In a town like Allison, the druggist was a repository of community knowledge.

## Fred and the Drugstore

Fred's visits to the drugstore are part of the routine of suburban life — picking up medicine when someone is ill, buying sundries, perhaps stopping at the soda fountain. The drugstore is another institution in the network of Allison commerce, and like the hardware store and the bank, it functions in the background of Fred's letters.
