# The Real Estate Office

The real estate brokerage that facilitates Fred and Grace Gross's entry into suburban homeownership — an institution that embodies the salesmanship, legal machinery, and strategic ambiguity of the 1915 Chicago suburban land market.

## Overview

The real estate office appears throughout the first Fred Gross story as the intermediary between urban aspiration and suburban reality. Its representative — "the real estate man" — shows Fred and Grace available lots, applies sales pressure ("we better hurry if we wanted it because some body else was libel to see it & cop it out"), handles the legal paperwork ("the real estate man dident have no papers fixed up yet"), and provides intelligence about neighbors ("thats what the real estate man told us when we got the lot"). (001-010) (001-011) (001-016) (002-017)

The office serves a dual function in the narrative: it is simultaneously a genuine service provider (without it, Fred could not navigate the legal complexity of land purchase) and a source of the strategic misinformation that fuels Fred's later grievances (the real estate man's assurances about the Hamiltons prove unreliable).

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The real estate office is central to the plot. Fred and Grace are shown lots by the real estate man, who steers them to available properties and facilitates the purchase. Fred visits "the real estate dept." (001-017) as part of his financial rounds, encountering the institutional layering — real estate, banking, architecture — that makes home buying a multi-office ordeal.

The real estate man also provides the first hint of municipal obligations: "the real estate man told me that taxes was pretty near nothing in Allison" (001-057) — an assurance that will prove misleading when the village orders Fred to install a new sidewalk.

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The real estate man reappears when Fred seeks information about his new neighbors: "I seen the real estate man today that sold us the place & ast him what kind of people the Hamiltons was" (002-091). The response — characteristically hedged — becomes another entry in Fred's catalog of grievances against the institutional world.

## Historical Context

The real estate brokerage depicted in the stories reflects the actual structure of the 1910s suburban land market. Developers would buy farmland along new or expanded railroad lines, subdivide it into residential lots, and market them through local real estate offices. The sales techniques Fred encounters — pressure to buy quickly, vague promises about taxes and neighbors, incremental revelation of costs — were standard practice in an era before consumer protection laws regulated real estate transactions.

The "real estate dept." Fred visits at his bank (001-017) suggests an institutional connection between banking and land sales that was common in the period: banks often maintained real estate departments to handle foreclosed properties and to facilitate the mortgage lending that made suburban expansion possible.
