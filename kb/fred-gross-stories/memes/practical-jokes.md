# Practical Jokes

Practical jokes serve as the primary engine of conflict in the Fred Gross stories, functioning simultaneously as social currency, weapons of class warfare, and the mechanism through which Lardner exposes the cruelty beneath middle-class neighborliness.

## Overview

From the very first story, practical jokes drive the narrative. Fred and his neighbors engage in an escalating series of pranks that blur the line between humor and hostility. Lardner uses these jokes to expose the competitive, anxious social world of the new suburbs, where residents are simultaneously desperate to fit in and eager to assert superiority over one another.

Fred's relationship with practical jokes is characteristically ambivalent: he participates enthusiastically when he is the perpetrator but takes deep offense when he becomes the target. This double standard reveals the fragility of his social position—he can dish it out but cannot take it.

## In the Stories

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The second story opens with the Hamiltons, Fred's new neighbors, arriving at their purchased home only to find a prank waiting. Grace explains "the joke & told him what come off over" (002-028), and the neighbors gather to "come pareing nots a bout the joke because she herd them laughing" (002-029). The joke serves as an initiation ritual—a test of whether the new neighbors can take the humor that defines the community's social interactions.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Story 3 centers on the practical joke as a central plot element. Fred "pertend it like it was a joke" (003-019) but reveals his true feelings of humiliation. The story's title itself—"The Last Laugh"—announces the competitive nature of the humor, where the goal is to have the final, definitive laugh at another's expense.

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

In Story 4, the practical joke escalates into open conflict. Fred reflects on the difference between acceptable and unacceptable humor: "theys a big diffrunts between a joke and not a joke" (004-051). He distinguishes between jokes sent "for a joke" and those meant to "inselt some body" (004-027), though the boundary between the two is precisely what the stories call into question.

### War Bribes (Story 5)

Even in the business-themed fifth story, humor persists: "we was laughing and jokeing a bout the smart aleck" (003-037) and Fred's workplace interactions are tinged with competitive humor.

## Historical Context

The practical joke occupied a distinctive place in early 20th-century American culture. Fraternal organizations, social clubs, and neighborhood life all featured forms of ritualized humor that served to establish hierarchies and test social bonds. The suburban communities that Fred inhabits were new social formations, with few established norms—practical jokes became a way of negotiating the unwritten rules of neighborly behavior.

The literary tradition of American humor—from Mark Twain through George Ade to Ring Lardner himself—frequently used practical jokes as narrative devices. The prank reveals character: who laughs, who sulks, who retaliates, and who walks away.

## Literary Significance

Lardner's treatment of the practical joke is characteristically double-edged. The jokes are genuinely funny—Fred's dialect narration makes them so—but they are also cruel. The reader laughs at the jokes while also recognizing their meanness. This duality is the essence of Lardner's satire: he implicates the reader in the very behavior he critiques.

The practical joke also serves as a narrative engine. Where other writers might use coincidence or melodrama, Lardner uses the prank to set plots in motion. A joke goes too far, a victim retaliates, and the story drives forward on the energy of social conflict.
