# The Commuter Rail Car

The railroad car that carries Fred Gross between Allison and Chicago is a space of suspension — neither here nor there, neither working nor at leisure, neither fully public nor fully private. It is where Fred spends some of his most unobserved hours, and it may be where he composes, in his mind if not on paper, the letters to Charley that constitute the stories.

## The Car

A commuter railroad car in 1915 was a functional space — rows of seats, overhead racks for hats and parcels, windows looking out on the passing landscape. Cars might be divided by class (first class and coach) or by function (smoking cars for men, general cars for families). The car was a microcosm of the commuting public, stratified by ticket price and social convention.

## The Journey

The ride between Allison and Chicago occupied a significant portion of Fred's day — perhaps thirty to forty-five minutes each way. This time was not productive in the economic sense but was rich in the mental sense: time to think, to worry, to plan, to compose. The railroad car was Fred's study, his refuge, the one space in which he was neither employee nor husband but simply a man in transit.

Fred describes the conversational currency of the commuter train:

> I want to play a couple times to get the hang of it so as I can talk with the other fellows going in on the train because that seems to be about all they talk
>
> (P03-008)

## The Community of Commuters

The regular commuters on the Allison line formed a community of recognition — not friends but familiar faces, people whose presence marked the rhythm of the working day. Fred's fellow passengers were the supporting cast of his daily drama, and the railroad car was the stage on which their wordless, repetitive performance unfolded.
