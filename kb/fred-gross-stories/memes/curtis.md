# Curtis

The coal and wood dealer of Allison, Illinois — a solidly middle-class businessman whose household serves as a recurring venue for the town's social gatherings and a yardstick against which Fred Gross measures his own social standing.

## Role & Overview

Curtis appears across three stories as a background figure in Allison's social economy. His occupation places him in the practical, unglamorous stratum of small-town commerce — "the wood & coal busness" — yet his household hosts card parties that attract the town's aspiring middle class. Fred mentions him without the active resentment he directs at the Hamiltons; Curtis seems to represent the baseline of respectable Allison society against which the Grosses calibrate their own position. (002-097) (003-010)

## Character Profile

### Background

Curtis operates a coal and wood business in Allison — an essential trade in an era when homes relied on coal furnaces for heating. The business appears successful enough to support a household that can host parties. He is married to Mrs. Curtis, who participates actively in the town's social life and introduces Fred to auction bridge whist. The couple lives in a house suitable for entertaining. (002-097) (003-011)

### Personality & Traits

Fred's portrait of Curtis is thinner than his treatment of the Hamiltons or Carpenters, but certain traits emerge from the reported incidents:

- **A competent host**: The Curtis party in "The Last Laugh" is well-attended and features cards and refreshments. Curtis himself serves the drinks, and Fred notes his hospitality: "Curtis says it taste it like more so come a head." (003-037)
- **Socially connected but not dominant**: Unlike the Hamiltons, who actively compete for social primacy, the Curtises seem to occupy a neutral zone — they host parties, attend others' gatherings, and maintain relationships across Allison's social spectrum. They appear on guest lists for both the Gross and Hamilton factions. (004-045) (004-053)
- **A figure of mild comedy**: When Curtis attempts to distinguish guests by social rank ("them whose who"), Fred reports that "they wanted Curtis to but when they seen him lay down they all layed down" — a moment of social awkwardness that Fred relates with characteristic amusement. (003-038)

## Key Appearances

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

Curtis receives only a passing mention — a brief identification as "a man & his wife name Curtis thats in the wood & coal busness" — situating him as part of the Allison backdrop that Fred is still learning to navigate. (002-097)

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Curtis plays his most prominent role as host of the card party that becomes a key episode in the story:

- Fred and Grace attend "a party last night was over to Curtis house the wood and coal man." (003-010)
- Mrs. Curtis introduces Fred to auction bridge whist — a game he mistakes for regular whist, leading to comic confusion: "Mrs. Curtis says if I played whist I would have to come over to there house some afternoon when the ladies played." (003-011)
- After the party, Fred and Grace discuss the social obligations they've incurred: "the Carrys and the Curtisis thats had us to there house we will half to return the complement." (003-024)
- At refreshment time, Curtis serves drinks: "Curtis says it taste it like more so come a head." (003-037)
- The party makes the local paper, though Fred is miffed that Curtis's name is misspelled or omitted: "they dident say nothing in the papers a bout Curtis." (003-041)

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

Curtis and his wife appear as members of the broader social circle caught in the crossfire of the Gross-Hamilton feud:

- When Mr. Hamilton tries to assemble allies, he gathers "the Martins and the Curtis and Carrys and some other nice people." (004-045)
- Grace later calls "the Curtis and the Carrys" to warn them away from the Hamilton party — dragging the neutral Curtises into the feud. (004-054)

## Relationships

- **Mrs. Curtis** — Wife and social partner. She introduces Fred to auction bridge and participates actively in Allison's social calendar.
- **[[Carry-Carpenter]]** / [[Mr-Carpenter]] — Social allies. The Curtises and Carpenters are frequently mentioned as a pair, moving through the same social circuits.
- **[[Mr-Martin]]** — Fellow guest and social peer. Both families appear on Hamilton's list of "nice people."
- **[[Fred-Gross]]** — Narrator. Fred's attitude toward Curtis is notably less hostile than toward the Hamiltons — perhaps because Curtis, as a tradesman rather than a white-collar aspirant, poses less of a status threat.

## Literary Significance

Curtis represents the neutral ground of Allison's social ecosystem — the merchant class that hosts gatherings, serves as audience for the town's social dramas, and maintains relationships across factional lines. His presence in the stories provides texture and scale: Allison is not merely the Grosses versus the Hamiltons, but a web of households (Curtises, Carpenters, Martins, Thomases) whose interconnected social obligations create the density of small-town life that Lardner satirizes.

The coal business itself is a period detail worth noting: in 1915, coal was the primary heating fuel for American homes, and a local coal dealer was an essential community figure. Curtis's trade roots him in the physical reality of Allison — unlike the white-collar abstraction of Fred's bank job — and perhaps accounts for his relatively unpretentious social style.

Fred describes his new neighbors:

> That wasent the Carrys that rung the door bell but a man & his wife name Curtis thats in the wood & coal busness out here & as nice peopl is youd want to meet.
>
> (002-097)
