# The Letter as Performance

Fred Gross's letters to his brother Charley are not transparent records of his life but performances of a self he is constructing for an audience. The epistolary form is not neutral; it shapes what Fred says, how he says it, and what he leaves out. Reading the letters as performance reveals a Fred who is always, even in his most intimate communications, on stage.

## The Audience of One

Charley is Fred's ideal reader — a brother who will sympathize, admire, and not judge too harshly. Fred writes for this audience, emphasizing his triumphs, justifying his failures, presenting himself as the hero of his own story. The Charley we infer from the letters — silent, receptive, perhaps slightly more successful — is a creation of Fred's narrative needs.

## The Performed Self

The Fred Gross who emerges from the letters is not the "real" Fred Gross (whatever that would mean) but the Fred Gross that Fred wants Charley to see. He is more competent, more wronged, more sympathetic than the Fred the reader infers from between the lines. The gap between performed Fred and inferred Fred is the gap in which Lardner's irony operates.

## What the Letters Exclude

The letters exclude as much as they include. Fred rarely writes about his work, his physical intimacy with Grace, his fears about the future, his moments of genuine despair. These omissions are not concealment so much as the natural boundary of what a man like Fred can say to a brother like Charley. The epistolary form respects these boundaries, and the reader respects them too — understanding that what is not said is often as significant as what is.
