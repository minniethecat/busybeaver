# Music and Performance

Music appears in the Fred Gross stories primarily through the domestic piano — an instrument that was, in 1915 America, both a symbol of middle-class respectability and a practical source of home entertainment. The piano purchase that figures in "The Swift Six" crystallizes the tension between Fred's financial anxieties and his family's social aspirations.

## The Piano as Domestic Symbol

In the early twentieth century, a piano in the home signaled cultivation. For a family like the Grosses, acquiring one was a statement of arrival — proof that they were not merely surviving but thriving, that their home was a place of culture as well as shelter. But for Fred, the piano is also a financial burden, another item on the long list of things he must pay for.

Fred's characteristic ambivalence surfaces in his response to the family's musical ambitions: "I says if you got to have a music instermunt in a famly where there is nobody that can play them" (006-018). The practical objection masks deeper resentment — the piano represents a refinement Fred does not possess and a expense he did not choose.

## Music and Social Gatherings

At parties, music structures the evening. At the Hamilton gathering, "finely the music beggin & they all started dancing" (002-079). The moment when the music begins marks the transition from awkward milling to structured sociability. Those who dance and those who do not are sorted into visible categories.

When the Grosses host their own party without music — "of corse we dident have no music" (003-037) — the absence is conspicuous, and Curtis must pretend not to notice. The lack of music becomes another small failure in Fred's ongoing struggle to match the social standards of his neighbors.

## Performance and Gender

Musical performance in these stories is gendered. It falls to the women — Grace, the daughters — to provide music, while the men listen, drink, and judge. The piano is part of the domestic sphere that women are expected to furnish with culture, even as men like Fred control the purse strings and resent the cost.
