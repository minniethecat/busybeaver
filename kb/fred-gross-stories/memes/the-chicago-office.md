# The Chicago Office

Fred Gross's office in Chicago is the great unseen space of the stories — the place where he spends his working hours, earns his salary, and inhabits an identity that is almost entirely absent from his letters to Charley. The office is mentioned but never described, and this absence is itself significant.

## The Unseen Workplace

Lardner's decision to keep Fred's office offstage is a deliberate narrative choice. The stories are about domestic and social life — the office is neither domestic nor social in the sense that interests Lardner. By excluding the workplace, Lardner focuses attention on the arena where Fred's character is most fully revealed: the home, the club, the neighborhood.

## What the Office Represents

Though unseen, the office is the economic foundation of everything Fred does. It is the source of the salary that pays for the bungalow, the car, the club membership, the drinks. It is the reason Fred commutes, the justification for his social ambitions, the invisible engine of his visible life. The office is necessity; Allison is choice.

## The Divided Life

Fred's life is divided between two geographies and two identities: the Chicago worker and the Allison householder. The office represents one half of this division, and its invisibility in the stories reflects Fred's own preference — he would rather talk about his house than his job, his golf than his work. The stories honor this preference by keeping the office in the background where Fred himself keeps it.
