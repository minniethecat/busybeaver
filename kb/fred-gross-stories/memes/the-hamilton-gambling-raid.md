# The Hamilton Gambling Raid

The deputy sheriff's raid on the Hamilton residence on suspicion of running a gambling house — a pivotal event in "Uncivil War" (story-04) that Fred Gross secretly orchestrates to retaliate against his neighbors, exposing the pettiness of suburban feuds in early 20th-century America.

## Overview

In July 1916, a deputy sheriff raided the Hamilton residence in Allison, Illinois, acting on a tip that the Hamiltons were operating a gambling house. The raid was secretly instigated by Fred Gross as payback for the Hamiltons' tire-slashing prank. Mrs. Hamilton was arrested, the gambling operation was exposed, and the incident deepened the already bitter feud between the Gross and Hamilton families. (004-006)

## Background

The Gross-Hamilton feud had been simmering since the Hamiltons moved in next door. Tensions escalated when the Hamiltons acquired an automobile and began flaunting their wealth. The immediate trigger came when someone — widely suspected to be a Hamilton — slashed the tires on Fred's car while it was parked by the curb. (004-040) Fred, convinced of the Hamiltons' guilt, resolved to strike back. (004-006)

The Hamiltons had been running regular card games at their house — auction bridge whist — that drew players from across Allison. These gatherings straddled the line between social entertainment and illicit gambling, providing Fred with the leverage he needed. (003-048)

## Key Events

- **The tip-off**: Fred contacted the sheriff's office and reported the Hamilton residence as a gambling house. He arranged for a deputy to be sent at 1 p.m. on a Thursday, when the regular bridge club met. (004-006) Fred took care to hide his involvement, knowing the Hamiltons would retaliate if they discovered the source. (004-007)

- **The raid**: Deputy Parker arrived at the Hamilton residence while the bridge game was in progress. He entered the house and confronted Mrs. Hamilton, informing her she was under arrest for running a gambling house. (003-054) The players — among them some of Allison's most respectable women — were caught mid-game. (003-049)

> He pored a few glassis of currage down his throte before he went on the job & when he got ready to work he was all lit up like a church & went up & pound it on Hamiltons front door & when the higher girl opened up the door he pulled his gun and she shreeked & pretty near fainted.
>
> (003-054)

- **The arrest**: Mrs. Hamilton was taken into custody, protesting that her bridge club was a social gathering, not a commercial gambling operation. The distinction did not impress the deputy. (004-006) The arrest caused an immediate scandal in Allison's social circles. (003-054)

- **Fred's satisfaction**: Fred watched the proceedings from a safe distance, pleased that his plan had worked. He remarked to his friend Charley that the Hamiltons had finally gotten what they deserved after the tire-slashing incident. (004-041)

## Aftermath & Consequences

The raid had immediate and lasting consequences. Mrs. Hamilton's arrest humiliated the family and became the talk of Allison. (003-054) The Hamiltons suspected Fred of orchestrating the raid but could not prove it, intensifying the cold war between the two households. (004-007) The incident also had a chilling effect on Allison's social card-playing circles — the women who had been present at the raid were reluctant to host bridge games for some time afterward. (004-045)

In the longer term, the raid cemented Fred's reputation as someone willing to use underhanded tactics against his enemies. It also demonstrated the porous boundary between respectable social recreation and illicit gambling in a small town where everyone knew everyone else's business.

## Literary Significance

The Hamilton gambling raid serves as the climax of "Uncivil War" and represents the peak of suburban pettiness in the Fred Gross stories. Ring Lardner uses the episode to satirize the moral hypocrisy of small-town respectability: the Hamiltons' bridge club is both a genteel social institution and, when Fred chooses to weaponize it, a criminal enterprise. The raid also showcases Fred's narrative reliability — he presents himself as the wronged party throughout, even as he engineers a police raid on his neighbors out of spite. Lardner's epistolary form allows the reader to see both Fred's self-justification and the obvious pettiness of the entire affair.
