# Mr. Thomas

Mr. Thomas, described by Fred Gross as "the richest man in Allison" (P03-048), is a country club member whose wealth, golf game, and social position make him a figure of both admiration and anxiety in Fred's letters.

## Role & Overview

Mr. Thomas appears primarily in the Fore! section, where Fred plays golf with him and navigates the social dynamics of proximity to wealth. Fred's relationship with Thomas is characteristically ambivalent: he is proud to be associated with a man of Thomas's standing but also made acutely aware of his own inferior position. "Mr. Thomas, Charley, the richest man in" (P03-048) Allison — the appositive tells the reader everything about why Thomas matters to Fred.

## Character Profile

### Background

Thomas is a wealthy resident of Allison and a member of the country club. His specific business is not identified, but his wealth is sufficient to make him the acknowledged richest man in town. He is married to Mrs. Thomas, and Grace Gross is "excited about me playing Mr. Thomas and she meeting Mrs. Thomas" (P03-050).

### Personality & Traits

Thomas is portrayed as a competent golfer and a figure of casual authority at the club. He is grouped with "Mr. Carlton and Mr. Carpenter" (P03-049) as part of the club's inner circle. Fred's reports suggest Thomas is neither particularly warm nor particularly cold — simply a man secure in his position, unconscious of the social anxieties he triggers in others.

## Key Appearances

Fred plays golf with Thomas and reports the experience to Charley with his usual mixture of pride and self-deprecation. Thomas's wealth and status provide Fred with both vicarious satisfaction and uncomfortable self-awareness. Grace's excitement at the prospect of meeting Mrs. Thomas reveals the social calculus at work in the Gross household.

Fred introduces his golf opponent to his brother Charley:

> Mr. Thomas, Charley, the richest man in Allison and his wife is the high monkey monk in society here and Grace is tickeled to death because here is her chance to get acquainted with Mrs. Thomas and then she will be in right.
>
> (P03-048)

## Literary Significance

Mr. Thomas embodies the distance between Fred's social position and his social aspirations. He is everything Fred would like to be — wealthy, respected, at ease — and his casual presence at the club is a constant reminder of what Fred is not. Lardner uses Thomas sparingly but effectively: a few mentions are enough to establish the social hierarchy that Fred is trying to climb.
