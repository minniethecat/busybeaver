# The Sidewalk Assessment

The village of Allison's order requiring Fred Gross to install a cement sidewalk and driveway at his newly built bungalow — an unexpected municipal assessment that exemplifies the hidden costs and bureaucratic intrusions of suburban homeownership in early 20th-century America.

## Overview

In the spring of 1915, shortly after Fred Gross purchased his lot and began building his bungalow, the village of Allison ordered him to install a cement sidewalk and driveway at his own expense. The assessment — costing approximately $250 ($150 for the driveway of cement and gravel, plus $100 for the cement walk) — came as an unwelcome financial shock to Fred, who was already stretching his budget to afford the house itself. (001-094) (001-095) The cracked, substandard result added insult to injury. (001-102)

## Background

Fred Gross's decision to buy a lot and build a house in Allison was driven by a conviction that homeownership would be cheaper and more dignified than renting in Chicago. He had carefully budgeted for the land, the construction, and the basic furnishings. What he had not anticipated were the municipal assessments — costs imposed by the village for infrastructure improvements that homeowners were required to finance. (001-094)

The village of Allison, like many growing suburbs of the era, used special assessments to fund sidewalks, roads, and other public improvements. Homeowners had little choice but to pay — the alternative was a lien on the property. For new homeowners like Fred, who had stretched to the limit of their financial resources, these assessments could be devastating. (001-094)

## Key Events

- **The village order**: Fred received notice that the village of Allison had ordered him to install a new cement sidewalk in front of his property. The order was non-negotiable — the sidewalk was required by municipal code, and the cost was the homeowner's responsibility. (001-094)

- **The driveway requirement**: In addition to the sidewalk, Fred was required to install a cement and gravel driveway. The combined estimate came to $150 for the driveway alone. Fred, who had not budgeted for either expense, was dismayed. (001-095)

- **The installation**: Fred hired a contractor — "Frackter," as he called him — to do the work. The contractor installed both the sidewalk and the driveway, but the quality was poor. (001-102)

- **The cracked result**: By the time Fred moved into the bungalow, the cement walk was already cracking. The $100 sidewalk, barely finished, was visibly deteriorating. Fred complained bitterly to Charley about the shoddy workmanship and the injustice of paying for something that was already falling apart. (001-102)

## Aftermath & Consequences

The sidewalk assessment was an early and painful lesson in the hidden costs of homeownership. Financially, it added roughly $250 to Fred's already tight budget — equivalent to a significant fraction of his annual salary. The cracked walk served as a daily reminder of the transaction's injustice. (001-102)

Psychologically, the assessment reinforced Fred's suspicion that the institutions of suburban life — the village government, the contractors, the whole system — were rigged against the ordinary working man. The episode foreshadowed a recurring pattern in the Fred Gross stories: Fred's expectations of suburban life colliding with its realities. He had imagined homeownership as a path to independence and dignity; the sidewalk assessment taught him that owning property meant being perpetually at the mercy of municipal bureaucracies and unreliable tradesmen.

## Literary Significance

The sidewalk assessment is one of Ring Lardner's most economical pieces of social commentary. In a few paragraphs of Fred's letter to Charley, Lardner captures the structural exploitation built into early 20th-century suburban development: the village mandates improvements, the homeowner pays, the contractor does shoddy work, and the homeowner is left holding a cracked sidewalk and a depleted bank account. It is a small, specific injustice that stands in for a much larger critique of the American dream of homeownership.
