# The C. D. & X. Railroad

The commuter railroad line that connects Allison, Illinois, to Chicago's Loop, 16 miles to the east — the invisible infrastructure on which Fred Gross's suburban dream depends, and a recurring background presence in the stories' geography.

## Overview

The C. D. & X. Railroad is mentioned explicitly only once in the Fred Gross stories — "its out to Allison 16 mi. west of the Loop on the c d & x" (001-010) — but its presence is felt throughout. Every letter Fred writes from his Chicago office implies a train journey that morning; every letter from Allison implies a train journey the evening before. The railroad is the physical link between Fred's two worlds: the urban workplace where money is earned and the suburban home where it is spent.

> Brother Charley. I dident think I would be writeing you this news so quick but we got our lot all ready & its out to Allison 16 mi. west of the Loop on the c d & x & it dont only take 40 min. for the trains to come in to town & it takes me 35 min. now & when we get in the new house it will only take me a half hr. because its nearer the station.
>
> (001-010)

The railroad's initials appear to be a fictional rendering — no actual Chicago railroad used the "C. D. & X." designation — but the line it describes was real enough: the commuter corridors that radiated from Chicago's Union Station and North Western Station, carrying white-collar workers between downtown offices and the growing suburbs of the early 20th century.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The railroad appears in the foundational description of Allison's location: "16 mi. west of the Loop on the c d & x" (001-010). This single reference establishes the spatial framework for the entire series — Allison is defined by its distance from Chicago and its connection to the city by rail.

The railroad is also implied in Fred's commuting pattern. His letters shift between Chicago and Allison datelines, the alternation mapping the daily rhythm of train travel. The commute itself is never described in detail — Fred is not interested in the journey, only in the destinations — but it structures his existence.

### The Swift Six (Story 6)

The automobile purchase represents a potential liberation from railroad dependence. When Fred buys the Swift Six, he acquires the ability to travel between Allison and Chicago on his own schedule — a freedom that proves, characteristically, to be more expensive and complicated than anticipated. The car competes with the train as a mode of transport, and Fred's ambivalence about both reflects his larger pattern of aspiration followed by disillusionment.

## Historical Context

Chicago's commuter railroad network was one of the most extensive in the world by 1915. The Chicago, Burlington & Quincy (CB&Q) and the Chicago & North Western (C&NW) operated frequent steam-hauled commuter service to western suburbs including Berwyn, La Grange, Hinsdale, and Naperville — towns that correspond roughly to the fictional Allison's location. A 16-mile trip would have taken approximately 30–40 minutes on a local train.

The commuter railroad was the essential technology of early 20th-century suburbanization. Before widespread automobile ownership (which would not arrive until the 1920s), the railroad determined where suburbs could develop: land within walking distance of a station was valuable; land beyond that radius was farmland. Allison's very existence as a commuter suburb is a product of the C. D. & X. line.
