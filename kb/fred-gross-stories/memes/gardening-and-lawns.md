# Gardening and Lawns

The garden and lawn are essential components of the suburban dream that Fred Gross pursues. A house with a yard — space for flowers, a vegetable garden, perhaps chickens — represents the self-sufficiency and connection to the land that urban apartment living cannot provide. Fred's vision of his bungalow includes the surrounding grounds as an integral part of the domestic ideal.

## The Dream of Cultivation

Fred imagines the finished bungalow with "room for the kids to play a round all day" and space to "raise chickens & have a garden. I guess thats better than paying rent onct a mo." The garden is both practical (food) and symbolic (stewardship, permanence). It connects Fred to an older American ideal of the yeoman householder even as he participates in the thoroughly modern economy of suburban consumption.

> may be raise chickens & have a garden. I guess thats better than paying $25 per mo. rent & being cooped up in a 4 room flat eh Charley.

## The Lawn as Status Symbol

The suburban lawn in early twentieth-century America was an innovation — a space that was neither agricultural nor wild, devoted entirely to aesthetic display. For families like the Grosses, maintaining a proper lawn signaled respectability, industry, and conformity to community standards. The unkempt lawn was a social liability; the well-tended one, a quiet boast.

## The Grading Episode

The house grading incident is, in part, about the lawn. The grading was necessary to prepare the ground for grass seed, and the $60 expense was an unplanned cost of the lawn Fred had imagined. "We can get busy right a way & fix up the lawn & plant the grass seeds" becomes an obligation deferred — another piece of the suburban dream that costs more and takes longer than expected.
