# Tobacco and Smoking

Smoking in the Fred Gross stories is primarily a male activity and a marker of masculine sociability. Cigars at the club, a pipe at home, cigarettes among friends — tobacco use maps onto the geography of Fred's social world and signals his participation in the rituals of American manhood.

## Smoking and the Club

The country club is the primary theater of Fred's smoking. Cigars are offered, accepted, and smoked in an economy of masculine exchange — a gift that creates obligation, a gesture that signals membership. When Fred reports on club interactions, smoking is part of the background texture of male sociability.

> In the 1st place Im going to cut down on smokeing because I figgure where I smoke to much and when a man smokes as much is I been in the habit you get up in the a.m. feeling like a tramp and hawk and cough like you was going to loose a lunge.
>
> (006-005)

## Tobacco and Class

The type of tobacco one consumes signals class position. Cigars are for men of substance; pipes are for reflective domesticity; cigarettes are modern, urban, perhaps slightly suspect. Fred's tobacco consumption locates him in the middle tier of the smoking hierarchy — aspiring to cigars but comfortable with whatever is offered.

## The Economics of Smoke

Tobacco, like alcohol, is an expense Fred tracks with characteristic ambivalence. It is necessary — a man must smoke — but it is also costly, and Fred's accounting mind registers the expense even as his social self requires the consumption.
