# Allison, Illinois

The suburban village that serves as the primary setting for all six Fred Gross stories — a commuter town 16 miles west of Chicago's Loop on the C. D. & X. Railroad, where Fred and Grace Gross pursue the dream of homeownership amid a web of neighborly rivalries and social obligations.

## Overview

Allison is introduced in the first story as the destination of Fred and Grace's suburban aspiration: "its out to Allison 16 mi. west of the Loop on the c d & x" (001-010). The town is a product of the early 20th-century commuter railroad boom that opened Chicago's western hinterlands to middle-class settlement. For Fred, Allison represents both the fulfillment of the American Dream (property ownership, fresh air, escape from apartment living) and the source of his most persistent anxieties (neighbors, social competition, municipal regulations).

Fred's relationship with Allison evolves across the six stories from boosterish enthusiasm to wary accommodation. The town's physical features — its lots, streets, country club, and railroad station — are the infrastructure on which the stories' social dramas are staged.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

Allison is introduced as a place of promise. Fred and Grace tour lots with a real estate agent and select a 100 × 150 foot property for $1,500: "100 ft. frontidge & 150 ft. deep & only $1,500" (001-010). The town's affordability and accessibility (a 16-mile train ride from Fred's Chicago office) make it feasible for a clerk's family. But Allison also imposes obligations: "The village of Allison has ordered us to put in a new side walk" (001-094) — municipal regulations that add unexpected costs to the homeownership dream.

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

With the Grosses now resident, Allison's social geography comes into focus. The arrival of the Hamiltons as neighbors introduces the town's central dynamic: proximity without community, the physical closeness of suburban life intensifying social friction rather than relieving it. The real estate man's earlier promise that "the Hamiltons was all right" proves unreliable.

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

Allison's small scale makes the Gross-Hamilton feud inescapable — there is nowhere to go, no anonymity in which to hide. The automobile headlights that expose Fred's nighttime movements symbolize the town's social surveillance: everyone sees everything. Yet the same scale also enables the reconciliation: Mr. Hamilton can simply walk over to talk things through.

## Historical & Geographic Context

Allison is a fictional town, but its characteristics closely match the real western suburbs of Chicago that experienced explosive growth in the 1900–1920 period. Towns like Oak Park, Maywood, and La Grange — all served by commuter railroads radiating from Chicago's Loop — provided the model. The "16 miles west of the Loop" location places Allison roughly in the zone of what is now Chicago's near western suburbs.

The "c d & x" railroad Fred mentions appears to be a fictionalized version of the Chicago, Burlington & Quincy (CB&Q) or the Chicago & North Western (C&NW), both of which operated commuter service to western suburbs in the 1910s. The suburban land boom of this era was directly fueled by railroad expansion, with developers buying farmland along new rail lines and subdividing it into residential lots.
