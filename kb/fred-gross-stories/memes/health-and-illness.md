# Health and Illness

Illness enters the Fred Gross stories as a practical disruption, a social event, and — in the shadow of the 1918 influenza pandemic — an unspoken historical presence. Fred's body, like his finances, is something he cannot fully control, and his encounters with sickness reveal his characteristic blend of self-pity and resilience.

## Fred's Body

Fred is not a robust man. At the country club, he complains of the physical toll of golf: "all the sleep they have gave me if I aint sick" (P03-043). The sleeplessness he attributes to golf handicaps is characteristic — Fred experiences his body as something that happens to him, another system whose rules he does not understand.

> Of corse I aint so good yet that they isent some of the young fellows in the club that could give me a trimming but they give a man a handicap in these here tournaments and believe me Charley I will win with the handicap they have gave me if I aint sick.
>
> (P03-043)

His golf nerves produce physical symptoms: "I was so sick I couldent hardly see and I mist the ball entirely" (P03-101). The word "sick" blurs the boundary between physical illness and emotional distress, and Fred's body registers what his conscious mind cannot acknowledge — the pressure of performance, the fear of humiliation.

> You beat me all right, I says to Thomas, but you know what a fat chance you would of had only for that ketchup I had for lunch being spoiled and made me sick.
>
> (P03-101)

## Grace's Health

Grace's health is mentioned with Fred's characteristic mix of concern and self-interest. When she is described as "sick and tired of the people she had to associate with" (P03-007) in their old neighborhood, the idiom captures both genuine fatigue and social aspiration — Grace's "sickness" is as much about where she lives as how she feels.

## Historical Context

The Fred Gross stories were published between 1915 and 1919, on the eve of and during the 1918 influenza pandemic that killed millions worldwide. While Lardner does not foreground the pandemic in the stories, the historical moment lends weight to every mention of illness. The casual references to feeling "sick" take on darker resonance when read against the backdrop of a world in which sickness had become catastrophe.

## Illness as Metaphor

In the stories, illness often functions as a social metaphor. Characters become "sick" of situations, "tired" of people, "sick and tired" of their circumstances. The language of physical ailment bleeds into the language of social and emotional discontent, reflecting a world in which the body and the social self are not easily separated.
