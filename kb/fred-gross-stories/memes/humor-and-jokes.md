# Humor and Jokes

Jokes are never innocent in the Fred Gross stories. They are weapons of social aggression, masks for genuine hostility, and — in Lardner's hands — the primary vehicle through which Fred's character reveals itself. Fred is both perpetrator and target of jokes, and his narration of them exposes the cruelty beneath the laughter.

## Jokes as Social Weapons

The practical joke is the dominant mode of humor in the stories, and it is almost never harmless. When Fred and Mr. Martin plan a prank on the Hamiltons — placing a quarantine sign on their porch — the ostensible humor masks genuine animosity. Fred recounts:

> she says thats a grate joke & Mr. Hamilton says may be it is and may be it aint
>
> (002-027)

The ambiguity of whether something is "a grate joke" or not hovers over every prank: the boundary between jest and malice is deliberately blurred.

## Fred as Joker and Fool

Fred's own attempts at humor often misfire. He tells Grace about the prank and "pertend it like it was a joke but I would like to get a good look at their face when they seen it" (003-019). His pretense that cruelty is mere comedy is characteristic — Fred uses the alibi of humor to justify behavior he cannot acknowledge as hostile.

At the country club bar, Fred's relationship with humor is more social: "I says a high ball just jokeing" (P03-057). The "just jokeing" is a defense mechanism, a way of testing social waters without committing. When the drink actually arrives, the joke becomes reality — and, as so often with Fred, the costs are not yet visible.

## Lardner's Comic Technique

Lardner's humor operates on two levels simultaneously. On the surface, Fred's malapropisms, phonetic spelling, and earnest self-justification are comic. Beneath the surface, the gap between what Fred says and what the reader understands creates a darker comedy of self-exposure. The joke, ultimately, is on Fred — and Fred never gets it.
