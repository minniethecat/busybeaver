# Bungalow Architecture

The bungalow that Fred Gross builds in Allison was not just any house — it was a specific architectural type that swept across America in the early twentieth century, carrying with it a ideology of simplicity, efficiency, and middle-class aspiration. Fred's "bungello" is both a physical structure and a cultural statement.

## The Bungalow Type

The American bungalow of the 1910s was typically a one or one-and-a-half story house with a low-pitched roof, wide eaves, a front porch, and an open floor plan. Derived from the California bungalow popularized by architects like Greene and Greene, the style was adapted for mass production and sold through pattern books and kit-house catalogs. It was the default choice for the aspiring suburban homeowner.

## The Appeal

The bungalow's appeal was ideological as well as practical. It promised efficiency (no wasted space), modernity (open plans, built-in furniture), and respectability (a proper single-family home on its own lot). For Fred, building a bungalow was an act of cultural citizenship — a claim to membership in the great American middle class.

## Fred's Bungalow

Fred's specific bungalow — $2,000, ivory bathtub, grading extra — is a modest example of the type. It is not a showpiece but a starter home, the first rung on the property ladder. Its modesty is part of its meaning: it represents what was achievable for a man of Fred's income, and its achievement is a genuine accomplishment even amid the comic frustrations of its construction.
