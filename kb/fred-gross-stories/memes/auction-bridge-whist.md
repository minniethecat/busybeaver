# Auction Bridge Whist Games

The auction bridge whist card games held at Carry Curtis's house and the Hamilton residence — social rituals that serve as a stage for class anxiety, marital tension, and Fred Gross's perpetual discomfort with Allison's middle-class entertainments.

## Overview

In the summer of 1916, Fred Gross found himself drawn into the world of auction bridge whist, a fashionable card game that dominated Allison's social scene. First introduced to the game at Carry Curtis's house, Fred struggled with its rules, its social codes, and its cost — both financial and marital. The bridge games became a recurring source of tension between Fred and his wife Grace, culminating in a near-disastrous incident at the Hamilton bridge club involving a deputy sheriff. (003-011) (003-018)

## Background

Auction bridge whist was the game of the moment in Allison's middle-class circles. Mrs. Hamilton belonged to a bridge club that met every Thursday, and the game was a fixture at social gatherings throughout the town. (003-048) For Fred Gross — a former city-dweller who preferred baseball and poker — the game represented everything alienating about suburban respectability: it was fussy, expensive, and demanded a kind of social performance he found exhausting. (003-011)

Grace Gross, by contrast, saw the bridge games as an opportunity for social advancement. She encouraged Fred to learn the game so they could participate in Allison's better circles. (003-007)

## Key Events

- **Learning the game at Carry's**: Fred attended his first auction bridge game at Carry Curtis's house. Mrs. Curtis explained the rules, but Fred found the game baffling and the stakes uncomfortably high. He played poorly and felt humiliated. (003-011) (003-018)

Fred's first encounter with auction bridge captures his bewilderment at the game's complexity:

> ...they sprung this here game they call auction bridge whist. Mrs. Curtis says if I played whist I wouldent have no trubble lerning this here game but in this game you bid back & 4th like pitch only you half to say what you are biding on & they got a lot of funny sines that means some thing & a mans got to go threw collige to lern all them sines...
>
> (003-011)

- **The cost of playing**: Fred was shocked by the financial stakes of the bridge games. He worried that a single bad night could cost more than a week's wages. His anxiety about money — a running theme throughout the stories — surfaced acutely at the card table. (003-037)

- **The Thursday club at the Hamiltons**: Mrs. Hamilton's bridge club was the most prestigious regular game in Allison. The women who attended included the town's social elite, and an invitation was a marker of status. (003-048) Grace wanted in; Fred dreaded the prospect.

- **The deputy's interruption**: During one Thursday game at the Hamiltons, a deputy sheriff walked in, claiming the gathering was an illegal gambling house. The raid — secretly instigated by Fred — shattered the social veneer of the bridge club and exposed the thin line between respectable recreation and criminal gambling. (003-049) (003-054)

## Aftermath & Consequences

The auction bridge games had lasting effects on Fred's social standing and marriage. His poor performance at the card table embarrassed Grace and reinforced her perception that Fred was holding back her social ambitions. (003-018) The deputy raid on the Hamilton bridge club, while satisfying Fred's desire for revenge, also made the Grosses social pariahs among the bridge-playing set — a consequence Fred had not fully anticipated. (004-045)

The episode also marked a turning point in Fred's relationship with Allison's social institutions. Having burned his bridges (literally and figuratively) with the bridge club, Fred increasingly retreated from organized social activities, preferring the company of his family and a few trusted friends.

## Literary Significance

The auction bridge episodes showcase Ring Lardner's skill at using a simple social ritual as a lens for class analysis. Through Fred's bewildered narration, Lardner exposes the class anxieties that animated early 20th-century suburban life. The bridge games are never just about cards: they are about money, status, gender roles, and the performance of respectability. Fred's inability to master the game mirrors his broader failure to assimilate into Allison's middle class — he is, and always will be, the rough-edged city man pretending to belong.
