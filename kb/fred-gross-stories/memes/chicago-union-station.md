# Chicago Union Station

The train station is the hinge between Fred's two worlds: Chicago, where he works, and Allison, where he lives. Every day, Fred passes through its gates, and the station represents the daily ritual of transition between professional and domestic identities.

## The Commuter's Portal

For the early twentieth-century suburban commuter, the train station was both practical necessity and symbolic threshold. Entering the station in the morning meant leaving home and becoming a worker; departing in the evening meant leaving work and becoming a husband and father. The station organized the day and, in a sense, the self.

## The Architecture

Chicago's train stations in the 1910s were among the busiest and most architecturally ambitious in the world. Union Station, rebuilt in the 1920s, was preceded by earlier terminals that served the burgeoning commuter railroads. For Fred, the station was not a destination but a passage — a space defined by movement rather than arrival.

## The Daily Rhythm

Fred's commute structures his days and his consciousness. The train ride is time suspended between obligations — moments to think, to worry, perhaps to compose the letters to Charley that constitute the stories themselves. The station is where this suspended time begins and ends, the physical location of Fred's divided life.
