# Debt and Credit

Debt is the unspoken subject of the Fred Gross stories — the financial obligation that makes the suburban dream possible and simultaneously threatens to undo it. Fred's relationship to debt is complex: he fears it, depends on it, resents it, and cannot escape it. The bungalow, the car, the club membership — all are purchased on credit, and the payments form the bass line beneath the melody of Fred's daily life.

## The Mortgage

The building loan that the bank initially refuses is, in essence, a mortgage — debt secured by property. When Fred finally secures financing and begins construction, he enters into a long-term obligation that will shape his finances for years. The mortgage is both achievement (proof that a bank considers him creditworthy) and burden (a monthly payment that constrains every other choice).

## The Culture of Credit

The early twentieth century saw the expansion of consumer credit — installment plans, store credit, bank loans — that made middle-class consumption possible on middle-class incomes. Fred participates in this credit economy without fully understanding it. He signs for drinks at the club, buys on credit at the store, borrows from the bank. Each transaction is a small bet on future income, and the bets accumulate.

## Debt and Anxiety

Fred's financial anxiety is rooted in debt. He lies awake calculating payments, worries about interest, fears the consequences of default. Yet he continues to borrow — because the alternative is to stop aspiring, and stopping is not an option Fred can entertain. Debt is the price of the dream, and Fred pays it.
