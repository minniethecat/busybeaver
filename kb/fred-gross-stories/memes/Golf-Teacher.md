# Golf Teacher

The golf teacher at the Allison Country Club — unnamed, briefly mentioned, yet structurally significant — represents the service class whose labor makes the leisure of club members possible.

## Role & Overview

Fred mentions the golf teacher in passing: "I have took 2 lessons from the golf teacher out to the club and now" (P03-028). This single reference is the character's entire presence in the stories, but it opens a window onto the class structure of the country club that Fred's narration otherwise ignores. The golf teacher is not a member — he is an employee, someone whose expertise is purchased by the hour. Fred's relationship with him mirrors his relationship with the club itself: he pays for access to a world in which he is never quite at home.

> Well Charley since the last time I wrote you a letter I have took 2 lessons from the golf teacher out to the club and now I guess I am about ready to give them all an argument that is when I have had a little more practice. It is a mistake for a beginner to not take no lessons because theys a few little things that a man would never think of himself and if the teacher tells you them once you got them for good.
>
> (P03-028)

## Character Profile

Almost nothing is known about the golf teacher as an individual. He teaches at the Allison Country Club, gives lessons to members, and presumably possesses the golf expertise that Fred lacks. His anonymity is itself meaningful: service workers in Fred's narrative are rarely named, their individuality subsumed into their function.

## Literary Significance

The golf teacher exemplifies Lardner's sotto voce social criticism. Fred never reflects on the teacher's status; the class distinction between member and employee passes without comment. But the detail is there for the reader who notices: the club is not a democracy but a hierarchy, and the golf teacher occupies a rung that Fred — for all his anxieties — is comfortably above.
