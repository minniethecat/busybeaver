# Aging and Mortality

Death and aging are rarely foregrounded in the Fred Gross stories, but they are present — in the influenza outbreak, in the passage of time marked by the children's growth, in Fred's own awareness that he is no longer young. The stories' relentless focus on the present tense of social competition is shadowed by an awareness that time is passing and that what time takes cannot be recovered.

## Middle Age

Fred Gross is a man in middle age — old enough to have children, young enough to still be building his life. He is past the adventures of youth but not yet settled into the wisdom (or resignation) of age. His anxieties about money, status, and social standing are the anxieties of a man who feels that time is limited and that what he has not yet achieved may never be achieved.

## The Shadow of Mortality

The influenza outbreak brings mortality close — not as an abstract concern but as a practical reality. People get sick; some die. Fred's characteristic response is not reflection but action: he worries about practical consequences rather than existential meanings. But the shadow is there, and the reader feels it even if Fred does not name it.

## Time and the Stories

The stories themselves mark the passage of time — from the first letter about buying a lot through the building of the house, the settling into suburban life, the war years, to the final story. Fred ages across the sequence, and the cumulative effect is a portrait of a life in motion, moving through its middle passage toward an unstated but inevitable end.
