# Ring Lardner

Ring Lardner (Ringgold Wilmer Lardner, 1885–1933) was an American journalist, sports columnist, and short story writer whose creation of the Fred Gross character established him as one of the most distinctive and influential voices in early 20th-century American literature.

## Role & Overview

Lardner is the author of all six Fred Gross stories, originally published between 1915 and 1919 in *The Saturday Evening Post* and later collected in various editions.

> *Fred Gross Stories* was compiled from short stories published between 1915 and 1917 by Ring Lardner. (007-002–004)

While Lardner does not appear as a character in the stories, his presence is felt in every word: the dialect narration, the ironic distance between Fred's voice and the reader's understanding, and the meticulous construction of Fred's social world are Lardner's achievements.

Lardner's own background as a sportswriter and journalist shaped his literary technique. His ear for American vernacular speech — developed through years of reporting on athletes, fans, and the sporting public — enabled him to create Fred Gross's voice with a precision that no American writer had achieved before him.

Lardner's vernacular brilliance is evident from the opening lines of the first story:

> Dear Bro. Chas: Well I guess you will begin to think that I have forgot all about you but I haven't. I have been busier than a 1 armed paper hanger ever since we come back from our trip to Allison and I bet you will be surprised when I tell you that we have went and bought a lot out there and are going to build a bungello. (P03-002–004)

## Character Profile

### Background

Born in Niles, Michigan, Lardner began his career as a newspaper reporter and sports columnist. His baseball reporting for the *Chicago Tribune* and other papers earned him a national reputation before he turned to fiction. The Fred Gross stories, beginning with "Own Your Own Home," marked his emergence as a major literary figure.

### Literary Style

Lardner's signature technique — first-person vernacular narration by a naive or limited narrator — influenced an entire generation of American writers. Ernest Hemingway acknowledged Lardner's influence, and his technique of showing character through dialect and self-revelation anticipated the narrative experiments of high modernism while remaining accessible to a mass audience.

The deliberately unpolished prose, rendered in Fred's phonetically spelled voice, creates a double consciousness in the reader — we hear what Fred says, but we understand what Fred does not:

> I have been out to the club every day and have been trying to learn to play golf so as I can get in the game when the season opens and not make a fool out of myself in front of all the members because they are all pretty near experts and I would feel pretty cheap if I went out there and couldn't hit the ball. (P03-037–038)

## Key Works

The six Fred Gross stories — "Own Your Own Home," "Welcome to Our City," "The Last Laugh," "Uncivil War," "War Bribes," and "The Swift Six" — represent Lardner's most sustained work of epistolary fiction.

They were collected posthumously and have been reprinted in various editions, including the Standard Ebooks edition used as the corpus for this wiki.

## Literary Significance

Lardner occupies a unique position in American literary history: a popular journalist who became a writer's writer, a humorist whose comedy masked profound social criticism, and a vernacular stylist whose influence extended from Hemingway to Salinger to the present day.

The Fred Gross stories remain his most accessible and enduring achievement — a portrait of American middle-class life that is simultaneously comic, cruel, and compassionate.
