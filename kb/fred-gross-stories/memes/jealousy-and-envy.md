# Jealousy and Envy

Fred Gross measures himself constantly against others, and the measurement rarely favors him. His neighbors have bigger houses, better parties, higher social standing. His country club rivals have lower golf handicaps. His awareness of these differentials is acute, and his response oscillates between admiration, resentment, and the determination to close the gap.

## The Objects of Envy

Fred envies what his neighbors possess: the Hamiltons' social confidence, the Carpenters' entertaining style, the Curtises' ease at the card table. These are not merely material advantages but qualities of being — ways of inhabiting the suburban world that Fred has not yet mastered. His envy is not greed but aspiration frustrated by its own limitations.

## Jealousy and Grace

Fred's jealousy also operates within his marriage. When other men pay attention to Grace, or when Grace admires other men's accomplishments, Fred registers the slight with characteristic sensitivity. His jealousy is less about romantic suspicion than about status anxiety — the fear that Grace might compare him unfavorably to other husbands.

## The Unspoken Emotion

Jealousy is one of the emotions Fred cannot directly acknowledge. He frames his grievances in terms of fairness, propriety, principle — never in terms of envy. But the envy is visible to the reader nonetheless, seeping through the cracks in Fred's self-justifications. Lardner's achievement is to make Fred's envy legible without ever letting Fred name it.
