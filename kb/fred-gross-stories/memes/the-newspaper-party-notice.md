# The Newspaper Party Notice

When the local Allison newspaper prints a notice of the Grosses' party, Fred experiences one of his purest moments of satisfaction. The notice — a few lines in the society column — represents something Fred has been pursuing since the first story: public acknowledgment that the Gross family matters.

## The Notice

Local newspapers in early twentieth-century America routinely printed social notices: who hosted whom, who visited from out of town, what was served, who attended. For families like the Grosses, appearing in these columns was not vanity but validation — proof that one's social life was significant enough to be recorded.

## Fred's Response

Fred's narration of the notice is characteristically transparent. He is thrilled — not because the party was a success (it was not, by most measures) but because the newspaper said it was. The notice transforms the party from a private event with mixed results into a public success recorded for posterity. Fred does not distinguish between being in the newspaper and being important; for him, they are the same thing.

## The Irony

Lardner's irony is operating at full strength here. The reader understands that the newspaper prints notices of everyone's parties — that appearing in the society column is a function of living in a small town, not of social distinction. Fred's joy at the notice is genuine, but it is the joy of a man who mistakes the form of status for its substance.
