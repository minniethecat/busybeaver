# Golf Equipment

Golf equipment in the Fred Gross stories is both practical necessity and status marker — the tools of the game and the symbols of belonging to the class of people who play it. Fred's acquisition of clubs, balls, and accessories is another chapter in his ongoing education in the hidden costs of middle-class recreation.

## The Cost of Entry

Golf in 1915 required a significant investment in equipment. Clubs were expensive — Fred reports that "the clubs was $2.50 a peace" (P03-037) — and balls, which were easily lost, were an ongoing expense. The initial outlay for a set of clubs, a bag, balls, and appropriate clothing represented a meaningful commitment for a man of Fred's means.

> The balls come to 65 cents a peace and the clubs was $2.50 a peace and hows that for a skin game Charley. But they wasent no use kicking because they would tell you the prices was up on acct. of the war like every body tells you about every thing these days.
>
> (P03-037)

## The Equipment Economy

Golf equipment created its own economy of desire and distinction. Better clubs promised better play. Newer balls promised greater distance. The equipment catalog was a consumer catalog, and Fred, who was susceptible to consumer logic in all domains, was susceptible here too. His equipment purchases reflect his broader pattern: wanting the best, settling for what he can afford, and wondering whether the difference matters.

## Equipment and Competence

Fred's relationship with his equipment is characteristically externalized: he attributes his poor play to his clubs rather than his swing, his lost balls to bad luck rather than bad shots. The equipment serves as a screen on which Fred projects his frustrations, a material explanation for immaterial failures. If only he had better clubs, he seems to believe, he would be a better golfer.
