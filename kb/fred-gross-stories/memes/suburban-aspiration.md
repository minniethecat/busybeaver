# Suburban Aspiration

The move from a Chicago flat to a bungalow in Allison is the central project of the Fred Gross stories — not merely a change of address but an assertion of identity, a bet on the future, and the concrete expression of Fred's understanding of the American dream. Suburban aspiration is the gravitational center around which all of Fred's other concerns orbit.

## The Dream of Ownership

For Fred, owning a home is not just a financial decision but a moral one. Renters are dependent; owners are free. The flat in Chicago represents everything Fred wants to escape: crowdedness, transience, the lack of space for children to play. The bungalow in Allison represents everything he wants to become: established, respectable, permanent. "Our own home" — the phrase recurs throughout the first story — is both practical goal and spiritual aspiration.

## The Cost of the Dream

Lardner never lets the reader forget what the dream costs. The $2,000 construction price, the $700 lot balance, the $100 architect's fee, the $60 grading, the $90 taxes, the $25 here and $30 there for upgrades — every step toward the dream generates new expenses. Fred's meticulous accounting is a record of aspiration priced out in dollars. The dream is real, but so are the bills.

## Suburbia as Character

Allison is not merely a setting but an active force in the stories. Its social dynamics, its institutions, its physical layout, its seasonal rhythms — all shape Fred's experience and constrain his choices. The suburb is the stage on which Fred performs his aspirations, and the stage directions are written by a culture he only partly understands.
