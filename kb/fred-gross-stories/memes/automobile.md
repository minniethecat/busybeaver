# Automobile

The automobile in the Fred Gross stories functions as both a practical mode of transportation and a potent symbol of social aspiration, marking the boundaries between middle-class respectability and the wealth of the newly rich in early 20th-century suburban America.

## Overview

In the years 1915–1917, when Ring Lardner's Fred Gross letters are set, the automobile was transitioning from a luxury item to a middle-class necessity. Fred Gross himself does not own an automobile—he commutes by train from his suburban home in Allison, Illinois to Chicago—but automobiles appear throughout the stories as markers of social status, objects of desire, and vehicles for satire of American consumer culture.

Fred's relationship with the automobile is characteristically ambivalent: he both admires those who own cars and resents the social advantages they confer. The automobile becomes a lens through which Lardner critiques the aspirational materialism of the American middle class, using Fred's distinctive dialect voice to expose the gap between appearance and reality.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

In the first story, Fred contemplates the purchase of a suburban lot in Allison, Illinois, a decision shaped by transportation considerations. The automobile is implicitly present in Fred's comparison of his current commute—"35 min. now to go in on the L" (001-010)—with the train from Allison that "dont only take 40 min." The story establishes the transportation infrastructure that makes suburban life possible for a clerk like Fred, even as automobile ownership itself remains beyond his means.

Fred's reflection that he wishes he "had of kept a hold of my automobile as it would come in handy driving" (P03-016) reveals his past encounter with car ownership—a brief, perhaps ill-fated experiment that he now regrets abandoning. This admission, made in the Fore! section, casts Fred as someone who has tasted automotive mobility but could not sustain it financially.

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

The automobile takes center stage in "Uncivil War" through the character of Mr. Hamilton, who has acquired a car. Fred reports with a mixture of envy and disapproval that "Hamiltons got a automobile and they been running it all" (004-040), and notes that he feared being seen "on acct. of the automobile bean lit up" (004-041). The illuminated automobile becomes a beacon of conspicuous consumption that Fred simultaneously desires and resents.

### War Bribes (Story 5)

In "War Bribes," the automobile appears in an industrial context: Fred mentions "some of the automobile plants and boat factorys" (005-030) that are benefiting from wartime contracts, linking car manufacturing to the broader theme of war profiteering. Later, Fred invests in "a automobile stock" (005-081), participating—however naively—in the speculative economy surrounding the automotive industry.

### The Swift Six (Story 6)

The final story contains the most concentrated treatment of the automobile, as Fred attends the Chicago automobile show. He reports being "up to the automobile show yesterday and they certainly" (006-026) have impressive vehicles. Fred's observation that he "would rather lay around" (006-020) attending the show than working reveals his fascination. His suspicion of "automobile agts" (006-048) (agents/salesmen) and his reference to Grace's denial that she was "springing no automobile story" (006-080) show how thoroughly the automobile has permeated the Gross family's consciousness and conversations.

## Historical Context

The period 1915–1917 marked a pivotal moment in American automotive history. Henry Ford's Model T, introduced in 1908, had brought automobile ownership within reach of the middle class, while the 1914 introduction of the moving assembly line dramatically reduced production costs. By 1915, annual U.S. automobile production exceeded 800,000 units.

However, car ownership remained a significant financial undertaking for a clerk like Fred Gross. A new Model T touring car cost approximately $440 in 1915—roughly equivalent to several months of a clerk's salary. The automobile show Fred attends in Chicago (Story 6) was likely the famous Chicago Auto Show, first held in 1901, which by 1915 had become a major event showcasing the latest models from dozens of manufacturers.

The suburban development Fred participates in—buying a lot in Allison and planning a bungalow—was itself enabled by the automobile. While Fred commutes by train, the broader suburbanization of America in this period was increasingly automobile-driven, as cars freed commuters from the fixed routes of streetcars and interurban rail lines.

## Literary Significance

Lardner uses the automobile as a satirical device to expose Fred Gross's class anxieties. Fred's envy of Hamilton's car, his regret over selling his own automobile, and his attendance at the automobile show all reveal a man caught between aspiration and limitation. The automobile represents what Fred wants but cannot quite afford—a perfect vehicle for Lardner's satire of middle-class American life.

The epistolary form amplifies this effect: Fred's letters to his brother Charley are performative, and his comments about automobiles are calibrated to impress. When Fred reports on Hamilton's car or the automobile show, he is not merely describing—he is positioning himself within a social hierarchy that Lardner invites the reader to see through.

Fred's longing for a car is palpable in his account of visiting the Chicago automobile show:

> I was up to the automobile show yesterday and they certainly got a grand show and I couldent help from wishing I was in a position to buy a car and a man can get a good 1 now for the next to nothing.
>
> (006-026)
