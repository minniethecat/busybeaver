# Allison Train Station

The commuter railroad stop on the C. D. & X. line that links Allison to Chicago — a place of daily ritual, chance encounters, and the threshold between the suburban domestic sphere and the urban world of work.

## Overview

The Allison train station is the hinge between the two halves of Fred Gross's existence. Every morning he walks or drives from his bungalow to the station; every evening the train deposits him back. The station's rhythm — the 40-minute trip to the Loop, the 40-minute trip home — structures the temporal experience of suburban life in the stories. It is at the station that Fred encounters neighbors (Martin, on the morning after the Hamilton raid), reads newspapers (the story of the gambling raid he himself orchestrated), and processes the events of Allison life during the interstitial minutes between departure and arrival.

The station is not described in architectural detail — Fred is not a man who notices architecture — but its functional importance is absolute. Without the station, Allison would be an isolated rural village rather than a commuter suburb. The 40-minute train ride is the price and the promise of the suburban experiment.

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The station is established as part of the commuting infrastructure that makes Allison viable. Fred's assessment of the lot includes the crucial fact that "it dont only take 40 min. for the trains to come in to town" (001-010). The station is the unstated midpoint in this measurement — the place where the 40 minutes begin and end.

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The station's role as social crossroads emerges when Fred waits for the Walters to arrive: "may be they would come on the train that gets here at 1 a clock & she says my dinner will all be spoiled & finely after the 1 a clock train come & no body showed up I had to tell her the truth" (002-050). The station is where guests arrive and where their failure to appear is first confirmed — a place of anticipation and disappointment.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

The station becomes a site of male camaraderie and conspiracy. Fred and Martin ride the morning train together, share the newspaper story about the Hamilton raid, and laugh: "he was laughing so loud that he drownded out the train" (003-055). The station is the place where the male world of work and the female world of domestic social competition briefly intersect, and where information — gossip, news, plots — passes between neighbors.

## Historical & Geographic Context

In the 1910s, the commuter railroad station was the essential infrastructure of American suburbanization. Towns like Allison (modeled on real western suburbs such as Oak Park, Maywood, and La Grange) owed their existence to the railroad lines that radiated from Chicago's Loop. A typical suburban station of the era was a modest wooden or brick structure with a platform, a waiting room, and a ticket office — often the first building a visitor saw and the symbolic gateway to the community.

The 40-minute commute Fred describes was realistic for steam-hauled commuter service in the 1910s. The C. D. & X. — apparently a fictionalized version of the Chicago, Burlington & Quincy (CB&Q) or the Chicago & North Western (C&NW) — would have operated multiple daily trains, with service concentrated at morning and evening rush hours. The station's role as social hub was typical: in an era before universal automobile ownership, the station was where suburban residents encountered one another daily, creating the kind of casual acquaintanceship that Fred's friendship with Martin exemplifies.
