# Allison Village Government

The municipal government of Allison, Illinois — an institution that appears in Fred Gross's letters primarily as a source of unexpected assessments, mandatory improvements, and the kind of bureaucratic intrusion that he had hoped to escape by leaving Chicago.

## Overview

The village of Allison exercises its authority over Fred Gross primarily through infrastructure assessments — requiring homeowners to install and pay for cement sidewalks, driveways, and other improvements. Fred encounters the village government as an impersonal force that issues orders without consultation and expects compliance without negotiation. (001-094)

Fred's correspondence preserves his indignation at the village's demands:

> The village of Allison has ordered us to put in a new cement sidewalk & we got to have a side walk up in to the yard up to the house as well as a long the st. & its going to cost a even $100.
>
> (001-094)

## Role in the Stories

- **The sidewalk assessment**: The village's most consequential intervention in Fred's life came when it ordered him to install a cement sidewalk and driveway at his newly built bungalow. The combined cost — approximately $250 — was an unbudgeted shock that Fred resented deeply. (001-094) (001-095)

- **Police protection**: When Fred sought police protection for the neighborhood — motivated by his feud with the Hamiltons — the village's response was less than satisfactory. The deputy sheriff system proved both unreliable and susceptible to manipulation, as Fred himself demonstrated when he arranged the Hamilton gambling raid. (002-077) (003-002)

- **Infrastructure standards**: The village's building and infrastructure codes governed everything from sidewalk materials to heating systems. Fred, who had imagined suburban life as an escape from regulation, discovered that the village was in some ways more intrusive than the city. (001-094)

## Significance

The Allison village government embodies one of Lardner's central ironies: the suburban dream of independence and self-determination is undermined by the very municipal structures that make suburban life possible. Fred moves to Allison to escape Chicago's crowds and constraints, only to find himself subject to a new set of rules, fees, and officials. The village is the suburban face of the institutional frustration that pervades the Fred Gross stories.
