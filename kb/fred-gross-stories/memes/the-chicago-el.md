# The Chicago Elevated Railway

The Chicago "El" — the elevated railway that loops through the city's downtown — is part of the transportation infrastructure of Fred Gross's world. It is the urban counterpart to the commuter train: where the train connects city to suburb, the El connects neighborhood to neighborhood within the city.

## The Loop

The Loop — Chicago's central business district, defined by the elevated tracks that encircle it — is the gravitational center of Fred's working life. His office is in or near the Loop, and his daily commute terminates at a Loop station. The El defines the geography of downtown Chicago as the commuter train defines the geography of the suburbs.

## Urban Transit

The El in 1915 was a relatively new technology — the first elevated line opened in 1892 — and it was central to Chicago's identity as a modern city. For Fred, the El is part of the texture of urban life: the noise of trains overhead, the stairs to the platform, the crowds of fellow passengers. It is infrastructure so familiar it requires no description.

## The Two Railways

The El and the commuter train represent two modes of mobility: the El is urban, frequent, workaday; the commuter train is suburban, scheduled, transitional. Fred moves between these two systems daily, and the transition from one to the other marks the transition between his two identities.
