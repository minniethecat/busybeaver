# Real Estate

Real estate transactions drive the central plot of the Fred Gross stories, with Fred's purchase of a suburban lot in Allison serving as the foundational act of his middle-class aspirations and the source of much of the financial anxiety and social comedy that follows.

## Overview

Fred Gross's decision to buy a vacant lot in Allison, Illinois, and build a bungalow is the defining financial commitment of his life. The transaction occupies much of Story 1 ("Own Your Own Home") and casts a shadow over the subsequent stories as Fred navigates mortgages, deeds, building loans, and the gap between his expectations and the realities of property ownership.

The real estate man—a figure who appears repeatedly in Fred's letters—embodies the ambiguous role of the agent in early 20th-century suburban development: part facilitator, part hustler. Fred's interactions with him reveal both Fred's naivety and the structural inequalities of the property market, where a clerk's savings are no match for the complexity of real estate law and finance.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The real estate narrative unfolds across Story 1's letters. Fred and Grace tour Allison with "the real estate man & he showed us some swell lots" (001-010), and they quickly settle on a property: "its 100 ft. frontidge & 150 ft. deep & only $1,500 & we can pay for it when ever we get ready provideing its in side of 2 yrs." (001-010). The real estate man pressures them with the classic sales tactic: "we better hurry if we wanted it because some body else was libel to see it & cop it out" (001-011).

Fred's education in property law begins immediately. "I dident know how they done them things but found out that theys a lot of red tape" (001-016). He acquires "the warranty deed or what ever it is they call it" and pays "$25 to get what they call a garantee polisy from the title & trust Co." (001-016). The gap between Fred's enthusiastic leap and the sobering complexity of property transactions is pure Lardner.

The transaction continues to unfold across subsequent letters: "our lease is till the 1 of May" (001-022) intersects with building plans, and the financing complications mount as Fred learns that banks will not lend for construction until the lot is fully paid off.

## Historical Context

The 1910s saw a massive expansion of suburban real estate development around American cities. The combination of commuter railroads, rising wages for white-collar workers, and the cultural ideal of homeownership drove demand for suburban lots. Real estate agents—a relatively new profession—served as crucial intermediaries, often using high-pressure sales tactics.

The typical suburban lot in the Chicago area in 1915 cost between $500 and $3,000 depending on location and size. Fred's $1,500 lot with 100-foot frontage was a significant but achievable purchase for a clerk earning perhaps $1,500–$2,000 annually. Title insurance, which Fred purchases for $25, was a relatively recent innovation (the first title insurance company was founded in 1876).

## Literary Significance

The real estate transaction serves Lardner as a framework for exploring Fred's character. Fred's impulsive $100 deposit, his confusion about deeds and warranties, and his gradual realization that property ownership is more complex than he imagined all reveal the gap between middle-class aspiration and financial literacy. Lardner does not mock Fred's desire for homeownership—the desire is treated as reasonable—but he relentlessly exposes the institutional barriers that make the realization of that desire so difficult for someone of Fred's class and education.

Fred's impulsive leap into property ownership, pressured by the real estate man's sales pitch:
