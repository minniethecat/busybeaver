# Mr. Walter

The Walter family — Gus Walter, his wife, and their children — are neighbors of the Grosses whose chaotic visit to the Gross apartment becomes the catalyst for Fred and Grace's decision to seek a suburban home.

## Role & Overview

The Walters' visit in the opening of "Own Your Own Home" serves as the narrative's inciting incident. "The Walters come to see us & stayed all p.m. & little Ed & the baby both of them hollered all the wile they was there" (001-004). The children's misbehavior during the visit — and Fred and Grace's inability to manage it in their cramped apartment — provides the emotional force behind the suburban project.

Grace later receives intelligence from "her brothers wife Gus Walters" (001-022) about bungalow trends, and the Walters visit again later: "The Walters was out to see us today" (001-113), suggesting an ongoing relationship.

> The Walters come to see us & stayed all p.m. & little Ed & the baby both of them gets pretty mad a bout 5 p.m. in the afternoon
>
> (001-004)

## Character Profile

### Background

Gus Walter is connected to Grace's family through marriage. The Walters have children, like the Grosses, making them peers in the shared experience of urban family life. Their visit dramatizes the difficulties of apartment living for families with young children.

### Personality & Traits

The Walters are portrayed as ordinary friends whose visit inadvertently exposes the inadequacy of the Grosses' living situation. They are not antagonists but catalysts — their presence makes visible what Fred and Grace already feel: that the flat is too small, the children need space, and suburban homeownership is the solution.

## Key Appearances

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The Walters' afternoon visit provides the emotional turning point of the story's opening. After they leave, Grace's frustration boils over: "I says to Grace its a bout time you lerned them babys to shut there mouth when we got Co." (001-004). This exchange leads directly to the decision to buy property.

## Literary Significance

The Walters serve a crucial narrative function: their visit transforms Fred and Grace's vague dissatisfaction with apartment life into a concrete plan. Lardner uses this brief, seemingly incidental episode to motivate the entire suburban project that drives the stories.
