# The Gross Flat

The Chicago flat that the Gross family inhabits before moving to Allison is mentioned primarily as a negative reference point — the place they are escaping. It represents everything the bungalow is not: rented rather than owned, urban rather than suburban, provisional rather than permanent.

## The Space

Fred's descriptions of the flat emphasize its limitations. There is no yard; the children must play in the street. There is no garden; no space for chickens. It is a place of transience and constraint — the kind of dwelling that Fred, in his new identity as a suburban property owner, is eager to disown.

## The Contrast

The flat's most important function in the stories is as contrast to the bungalow. Every virtue Fred attributes to the new house — space, safety, permanence, ownership — is defined against the flat's corresponding deficiency. The move to Allison is not just a change of address but a moral improvement, and the flat is the "before" in Fred's narrative of self-transformation.

## What Remains

Despite Fred's eagerness to leave the flat behind, certain continuities persist. The financial habits formed during the renting years — the careful accounting, the anxiety about expenses — follow the Grosses to Allison. The flat is a place they have left, but the mindset it cultivated proves harder to escape.
