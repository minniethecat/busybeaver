# Neighbor Relations

Relations between neighbors form the primary social fabric of the Fred Gross stories, with Fred's interactions with the Hamiltons, the Walters, and other Allison residents driving much of the narrative conflict and comedy.

## Overview

In the new suburban community of Allison, Illinois, neighbors are both indispensable and insufferable. Fred Gross navigates a social world where the boundaries between friendship and rivalry, hospitality and intrusion, are constantly negotiated. Lardner uses neighbor relations to expose the fragility of middle-class respectability—the way a dispute over a practical joke or a perceived slight can escalate into open warfare, while the need to maintain appearances prevents either party from simply walking away.

The neighbor relationship is inherently ambiguous in Fred's world: neighbors are the audience before whom Fred performs his middle-class identity, but they are also competitors for status. Their proximity makes them witnesses to everything Fred would prefer to hide.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The Walters' visit to the Gross flat establishes the baseline of neighborly interaction. "The Walters come to see us & stayed all p.m. & little Ed & the baby both of them hollered all the wile" (from story 1). This early scene establishes the tension between the obligations of hospitality and the irritations of proximity that will define neighbor relations throughout the stories.

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The arrival of the Hamiltons as new neighbors provides the occasion for the stories' most elaborate neighborly performance. Grace introduces herself and Fred to the newcomers, explaining that "she was just paying him a" (002-027) neighborly visit. The practical joke played on the Hamiltons serves as both welcome and test—a ritual of inclusion that also establishes hierarchy.

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

Neighbor relations reach their breaking point in "Uncivil War," where Fred reflects on the absurdity of the situation: "its a funny World where a man has neighbors on 1 side of you that you could" (004-002) get along with and others who make life impossible. The story explores the peculiar torment of being unable to escape someone who lives next door—the enforced intimacy of suburban life.

Throughout the conflict, Fred worries about appearances: "if we was on friendly turns all the wile the neighbors would get suspicious" (P03-041). The neighbors' gaze is a constant presence, a surveillance that constrains behavior and amplifies every conflict.

## Historical Context

The early 20th-century suburb was a new kind of social environment. Unlike the dense urban neighborhoods where residents had long-established community ties, the new commuter suburbs like Allison were populated by strangers thrown together by real estate transactions. Social norms were still being established, and neighbor relations were characterized by a distinctive combination of formality and forced intimacy.

The suburban ideal promised privacy—a detached house on its own lot—but the reality was a community where everyone knew everyone else's business. This tension between the promise of autonomy and the reality of surveillance is central to Lardner's portrait of suburban life.

## Literary Significance

Lardner's treatment of neighbor relations is both comic and clinical. He documents the unwritten rules of suburban social life with the precision of an anthropologist, while exploiting the gap between those rules and actual human behavior for comic effect. Fred's earnest attempts to be a good neighbor while simultaneously resenting and competing with his neighbors create the ironic distance that defines Lardner's narrative voice.
