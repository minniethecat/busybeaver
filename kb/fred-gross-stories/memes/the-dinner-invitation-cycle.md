# The Dinner Invitation Cycle

The dinner party is the basic unit of Allison's social economy. Families invite each other to dinner according to an unwritten calendar of reciprocity: the Curtises host, so the Grosses must host; the Carpenters invite, so they must be invited back. Fred Gross navigates this cycle with a ledger-keeper's attention to debts and credits.

## The Rules of Reciprocity

The dinner invitation cycle operates on a simple principle: an invitation received creates an obligation to invite in return. Failure to reciprocate is a social debt unpaid — a statement, whether intended or not, about the value one places on the relationship. Fred is acutely aware of his obligations and anxious about meeting them.

## The Grosses as Hosts

Hosting a dinner party is expensive, labor-intensive, and risky. The food must be adequate, the house presentable, the company congenial. For the Grosses, whose finances and domestic skills are stretched, hosting is a challenge they meet with more determination than grace. Fred's account of their efforts is both comic and sympathetic.

## The Social Calendar

The dinner cycle organizes the social season. Fall brings a new round of invitations; winter sees the peak of entertaining; spring winds down the social calendar. Fred's letters mark the passing seasons through the rhythm of dinners given and received, and the cycle becomes a measure of the family's social standing.
