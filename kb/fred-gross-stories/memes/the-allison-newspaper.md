# The Allison Newspaper

The local newspaper is one of the most important institutions in Allison — the recorder of births and deaths, the publisher of society notices, the arbiter of what counts as news in a small suburban town. For Fred Gross, the newspaper's society column is a mirror in which he sees his social self reflected and, he hopes, validated.

## The Paper's Role

In a town like Allison in 1915, the local newspaper performed functions that have since been distributed across multiple media. It was the social register, the business chronicle, the police blotter, and the community bulletin board. Its columns documented the life of the town with a granularity that larger urban papers could not match.

## The Society Column

The society column — where the Gross party notice appears — was a fixture of small-town newspapers. It recorded who entertained whom, who visited from out of town, who was ill and who was recovering. For families like the Grosses, appearing in this column was a form of social recognition — proof that one's doings were significant enough to be printed.

## Fred and the Paper

Fred's relationship with the newspaper is characteristically self-interested. He reads it for evidence of his own social standing, clipping or at least noting the items that mention him and his family. The paper is not a source of news in the conventional sense but a scoreboard for the social competition he is perpetually engaged in.
