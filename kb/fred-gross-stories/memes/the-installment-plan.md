# The Installment Plan

The installment plan — buying goods on time, with a small down payment and regular subsequent payments — was transforming American consumer culture in the 1910s, and Fred Gross is both beneficiary and victim of this transformation. The plan makes his purchases possible; the payments make his life precarious.

## The Credit Revolution

Before the installment plan, major purchases required saving the full price in advance — a discipline that limited consumption to what could be afforded. The installment plan inverted this logic: buy now, pay later. Furniture, automobiles, appliances, even houses could be purchased on time, and the result was a consumer boom that reshaped the American economy.

## Fred and the Installment Logic

Fred's entire financial life is structured around installment logic. The lot: $100 down, $700 to follow. The bungalow: built with borrowed money, paid back "a mo. at a time" (001-015). The car: purchased on credit. Each commitment is manageable in isolation; together, they form a structure of obligation that leaves Fred perpetually exposed.

## The Psychology of Installment

The installment plan changes the psychology of consumption. The down payment feels manageable; the ongoing payments fade into the background of monthly obligations. Fred, who is skilled at focusing on the immediate expense while ignoring the cumulative burden, is an ideal installment customer. The system is designed for people like him, and his participation in it is both rational and self-defeating.
