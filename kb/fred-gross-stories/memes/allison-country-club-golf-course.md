# Allison Country Club Golf Course

The golf course at the Allison Country Club is where Fred Gross learns to play golf, meets his fellow members, and experiences some of his most acute moments of social exposure. It is a landscape shaped by privilege and exclusion, and Fred's navigation of it mirrors his broader negotiation of Allison's social terrain.

## The Physical Course

The course is described through Fred's experience of playing it — the long fairways that punish his weak drives, the rough that swallows his errant shots, the greens whose subtle breaks defeat his putting. It is not a championship course but a respectable suburban layout, the kind of course that served the aspiring middle class of early twentieth-century America.

Fred describes his first journey out to the course:

> I layed off yesterday p.m. and came out home and hired a rig to take me out to the country club. I wish now I had of kept a hold of my automobile as it would come in handy driveing back and 4th between the golf club and back.
>
> (P03-016)

## The Social Landscape

More important than the physical layout is the course's social geography. The first tee is a stage on which golfers perform for an audience of fellow members. The clubhouse veranda overlooks the eighteenth green, ensuring that every round ends under observation. For Fred, who is acutely conscious of being watched, the course is a theater of potential humiliation.

## Fred's Education

Fred's golf education unfolds on this course. His lessons with the golf teacher, his practice sessions, his disastrous first round with Mr. Thomas — each is a chapter in Fred's ongoing tutorial in the unwritten rules of club life. The course teaches Fred what the club's membership committee did not: that belonging is not a status you achieve but a performance you must sustain.
