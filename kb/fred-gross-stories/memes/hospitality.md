# Hospitality

Hospitality—the rituals of inviting, hosting, feeding, and entertaining—structures the social world of the Fred Gross stories, serving as both the glue that holds the community together and the arena in which social competition plays out.

## Overview

The Gross household is constantly engaged in hospitality: the Walters visit and stay all afternoon, neighbors drop in, Fred and Grace host gatherings that require food and drink. These are not optional social graces but obligatory performances—failures of hospitality have consequences, as Fred discovers when his shortcomings as a host become topics of neighborhood gossip.

Hospitality in Fred's world is governed by unspoken rules that Fred only partially understands. How much food to provide, what kind of drink to serve, how long to let guests stay—these are matters of anxious calculation. Fred wants to be a generous host, both because he genuinely values sociability and because he understands that hospitality is a form of status display. But the costs—financial, temporal, emotional—are a constant strain.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The Walters' visit establishes the hospitality baseline: "The Walters come to see us & stayed all p.m." (from story 1). The children's misbehavior during the visit—"little Ed & the baby both of them hollered all the wile"—creates the domestic crisis that motivates the move to the suburbs. In the new bungalow, Fred imagines, there will be room to entertain properly.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Hospitality takes center stage in the third story. Fred prides himself on providing "my frends enough to eat and drink when they come to see me" (003-010), and the practical joke competition is embedded in gatherings where food and drink flow. The line between hospitality and humiliation is thin—a joke that goes too far transforms the host into a victim.

> The party last night was over to Curtis house the wood and coal man & no wonder his wife ast us to the party because there geting a bout $20.00 dollars per mo. out of us for 2 & ½ ton of coal & it wouldent hurt them none to give us a party onct a mo. & have champane wine but they dident give us no wine & I says to Grace I could have got just as drunk on there wine as I can on my own wine.
>
> (003-010)

### Across All Stories

The country club extends hospitality into an institutional setting. Fred's bar tab is, in essence, a hospitality expense—the cost of being sociable. His shock at the bill reflects the gap between the hospitable impulse ("have a drink") and its institutional cost.

## Historical Context

Domestic hospitality was a central social institution in early 20th-century America. Without television, radio (still in its infancy), or automobiles (for most families), the home was the primary site of social life. The bungalow that Fred plans to build was designed with hospitality in mind—open floor plans, generous living areas, and porches for entertaining.

## Literary Significance

Hospitality in Lardner's hands becomes a diagnostic tool. How characters host, what they serve, how they treat guests—these details reveal character more reliably than anything they say about themselves. Fred's hospitality is generous but anxious, warm but calculating, and the tension between these qualities is what makes him so fully realized as a character.
