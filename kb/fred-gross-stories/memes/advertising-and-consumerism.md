# Advertising and Consumerism

The Fred Gross stories unfold in the early years of modern consumer culture — a world of newspaper advertisements, mail-order catalogs, brand-name goods, and the emerging ideology that the right purchases can transform a life. Fred is both an eager participant in and an unwitting victim of this new consumer order.

## The Consumer Dream

Fred's bungalow is the ultimate consumer purchase — a branded vision of the good life, complete with ivory bathtub and garden plot. The real estate agent's promises, the architect's upselling, the contractor's change orders — every step of the process involves someone selling Fred something he is not sure he needs at a price he is not sure he can afford. The bungalow is a product as much as a home.

## Advertising's Reach

While the stories do not dwell on specific advertisements, the language of advertising permeates Fred's narration. He has internalized the consumer logic of his era: that more is better, that new is superior to old, that the right purchase will solve the right problem. His letters to Charley are full of product descriptions and cost accounting that read like an unwitting parody of consumer catalogs.

Fred's account of Grace's encounter with a piano advertisement illustrates how consumer bargains drive the stories' domestic economy:

> ...she had been down on Wabash ave. with Mrs. Dutton and walked passed a piano store and seen a piano in the window that was advertised to sell for $110.00 and the reglar price of that kind of a piano was $250.00. But they was selling this 1 cheap because some body had ordered it and made a paymunt and had it sent out to there house and then desided they dident want it and sent it back.
>
> (006-015)

## The Price of Everything

Fred's obsessive cost-tracking — the $2,000 bungalow, the $100 architect's fee, the $60 grading expense, the $90 tax bill — reflects a world in which everything has been commodified. Even social relationships are priced: the drinks he buys at the club, the party he hosts, the favors he owes. Fred lives in a market economy and thinks in market terms.
