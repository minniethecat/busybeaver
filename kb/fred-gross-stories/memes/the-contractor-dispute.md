# The Contractor Dispute

The building contractor who constructs Fred's bungalow is simultaneously essential and adversarial — the man who makes the dream real and the man who makes it expensive. Fred's relationship with the contractor follows the same pattern as his dealings with the bank, the architect, and the real estate agent: initial trust, progressive disillusionment, and final resignation.

## The Contract

The construction contract is supposed to protect Fred. It specifies what will be built, for what price, by what date. But Fred discovers that contracts are porous — full of exceptions, subject to interpretation, only as strong as the willingness to enforce them. The contractor, experienced in these matters, knows how to work the contract; Fred, inexperienced, learns only through being worked.

Fred learns the economics of the construction business:

> the contracter under bid on the contrack & seen he couldnt build no house like mine for no $2,000 & thats why he wanted to quit
>
> (001-068)

## The Subcontractors

The general contractor hires subcontractors for specialized work — excavation, framing, plumbing, electrical. Each subcontractor answers to the contractor, not to Fred, and the chain of accountability is longer than Fred anticipated. When something goes wrong — the foundation dug "the rong way" — responsibility diffuses across the chain until it settles, inevitably, on Fred's wallet.

## The Resolution

As with all of Fred's construction disputes, the contractor issue is resolved not by victory but by exhaustion. Fred pays what he must, complains to Charley, and moves on. He has learned something about the construction industry, but the lesson costs more than the education is worth.
