# Home Construction

The dream of building a bungalow drives the opening story of the Fred Gross series, representing the material realization of middle-class aspiration and the point at which that aspiration collides with financial reality.

## Overview

Fred Gross does not want to buy an existing house—he wants to build one. "Lets buy a lot some wheres & build our own house," Grace proposes, and Fred embraces the vision of a custom bungalow designed to their specifications. The construction project—from lot selection through foundation-laying to completion—is the organizing ambition of the stories' opening, the thing Fred returns to in letter after letter as both goal and torment.

The construction project reveals everything about Fred: his optimism, his naivety, his inability to navigate institutions, his genuine desire to provide for his family, and his susceptibility to the promises of salesmen and contractors. The house that exists in Fred's imagination—roomy, modern, a visible marker of success—contrasts with the obstacles that prevent its realization.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

Construction is the story's central preoccupation. Fred and Grace settle on a bungalow design: "a bungello say costing $1,200 and have it all payed for in 1 yr. by paying $25 per wk." (001-005). The financial planning is meticulous and optimistic: they will "live on $25 per wk. & cut down on cloths & nickle shows & groserys" while paying off the construction loan.

> So I says well when our lease runs out in Oct. I will look a round in some of them subburbs & see can we rent a house reasonible or may be we can buy a little 1 on payments. Then Grace says lets buy a lot some wheres & build our own house & I says what would it cost & Grace says I dont know may be we can get 1 on easy terms.
>
> (001-005)

But the plan collapses when Fred approaches the bank for a building loan and is told he must first pay off the lot. The construction timetable shifts: "we can be all ready to start building & get the foundashun layed before real cold weather & then the carpenters can finnish up dureing the winter & we can be in the house next spring" (001-017). The house exists in a perpetual future tense, always about to be started, never quite begun.

> Well after this was fixed up I went a round to the bank where I had my saveings at & went to the real estate dept. & seen the man there and asked him did they make building lones & the man says yes. Well I says I want to borry $1,200 to build a bungello.
>
> (001-017)

## Historical Context

The bungalow was the iconic American house type of the 1910s. Pattern books and kit-home companies (most famously Sears, Roebuck) offered standardized designs that could be built for $1,000–$3,000. The bungalow's open floor plan, built-in cabinetry, and generous porch represented a modern ideal of efficient, comfortable family living.

Construction financing was a significant barrier for middle-class families. Building loans typically required clear title to the lot, a substantial down payment, and proof of stable income—requirements that a clerk like Fred could meet only with difficulty.

## Literary Significance

The house that Fred plans to build is a potent symbol. It is the American Dream rendered in lumber and shingles—a material promise that hard work and thrift will be rewarded. That the house remains unbuilt at the end of Fred's letters is Lardner's quiet commentary on the gap between promise and reality in American life.
