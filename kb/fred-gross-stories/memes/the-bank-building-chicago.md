# The Bank Building (Chicago)

The Chicago bank building where Fred Gross negotiates his building loan is more than a physical location — it is an institutional space that embodies the power asymmetry between the individual borrower and the financial system. Fred enters the building as a customer and is treated as a supplicant.

## The Architecture of Finance

Early twentieth-century bank buildings were designed to convey solidity, permanence, and authority. Marble floors, high ceilings, brass fixtures, the hushed acoustics of serious transactions — every element communicated that money was a serious matter conducted by serious men in serious spaces. Fred, entering this environment, is already at a disadvantage: the building itself tells him that he does not belong.

## The Real Estate Department

The bank's real estate department is where mortgages are made and denied. For Fred, it is the site of one of his formative humiliations — the moment when "the man there" tells him that his plan to borrow $1,200 will not work because he still owes $700 on the lot. The department is a bureaucratic space where decisions are made according to rules Fred does not fully understand.

## The Counter

Fred's interactions at the bank counter — depositing savings, making payments, inquiring about loans — are transactions that reduce his complex financial life to numbers on a ledger. The counter is the interface between Fred's personal economy and the institutional economy, and it is an interface designed for the institution's convenience, not Fred's.
