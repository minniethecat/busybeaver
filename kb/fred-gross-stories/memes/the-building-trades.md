# The Building Trades

The bungalow that Fred Gross dreams of is built by men whose names he rarely records — carpenters, masons, plumbers, electricians, graders, painters. The building trades are the manual infrastructure of Fred's suburban dream, and their presence in the stories is simultaneously essential and invisible.

## The Workforce

The construction of a house in 1915 involved a hierarchy of trades. The general contractor coordinated the work; the excavators dug the foundation; the masons laid the brick; the carpenters framed and finished; the plumbers and electricians installed the systems; the painters and paperhangers completed the interior. Each trade had its own skills, its own wages, its own place in the construction sequence.

## Fred and the Tradesmen

Fred's interactions with the tradesmen are mediated through the contractor and architect. He rarely speaks to them directly, and his letters record their work only when it goes wrong — the foundation dug "the rong way," the grading that costs extra. The tradesmen are present in the stories as a collective force: necessary, expensive, and not entirely under Fred's control.

## The Class Dynamic

Fred occupies an ambiguous position relative to the building trades. He is their employer — he pays the bills — but he is not their social superior in any obvious way. The tradesmen earn wages Fred can only afford by borrowing. They possess skills Fred lacks. The class dynamic between homeowner and tradesman is more complex than simple hierarchy, and Fred navigates it with characteristic uncertainty.
