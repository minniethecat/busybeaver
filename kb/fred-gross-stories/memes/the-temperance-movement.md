# The Temperance Movement

The Fred Gross stories were written and set on the eve of national Prohibition, and the temperance movement that would soon triumph is an unspoken presence in the stories' abundant drinking. Fred's world of country club bars, highballs, and champagne is a world whose days are numbered, and Lardner's portrait of that world carries the weight of impending change.

## The Movement

The temperance movement had been active in America since the early nineteenth century, but it gained decisive momentum in the 1910s. The Anti-Saloon League, the Women's Christian Temperance Union, and allied organizations built a political coalition that would achieve the ratification of the Eighteenth Amendment in 1919, prohibiting the manufacture and sale of alcoholic beverages nationwide.

## Drinking Under the Shadow

Fred Gross drinks as though Prohibition is not coming — which, from his perspective in 1915, it may not have seemed to be. The stories capture American drinking culture at a moment of transition, when the old norms of masculine sociability through alcohol were still intact but the forces that would soon overturn them were gathering strength.

## Lardner's Neutrality

Lardner's treatment of drinking is characteristically neutral. He neither celebrates alcohol nor condemns it; he simply records its presence in Fred's world with journalistic accuracy. This neutrality is itself a quiet argument — against both the temperance crusaders who saw alcohol as moral poison and the boosters who saw it as harmless fun. For Lardner, drinking is simply what people do, and what it reveals about them is the interesting thing.
