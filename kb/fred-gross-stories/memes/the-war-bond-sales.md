# The War Bond Sales

The war bond drive in "War Bribes" places Fred at the intersection of patriotism, social pressure, and personal finance — a position he navigates with his usual blend of good intentions and self-interested calculation. The episode reflects the real-world Liberty Bond campaigns that swept America during World War I.

## The Patriotic Imperative

By 1917–1918, war bond sales had become a national obsession — a way for civilians to participate in the war effort and a test of patriotic commitment. Fred, as a man of business and a suburban householder, is expected to do his part. The social pressure to buy bonds is intense and inescapable.

## Fred's Dilemma

For Fred, the war bond campaign presents a familiar conflict: the obligation to spend money he would rather keep. His characteristic ambivalence — wanting to be seen as a good citizen while resenting the cost — colors his account of the bond drive. He participates, but his heart is not entirely in it, and his narration reveals the gap between public patriotism and private calculation.

## Historical Context

The Liberty Bond campaigns of 1917–1918 raised billions of dollars for the American war effort. Local committees, often led by prominent businessmen, organized drives that combined patriotic appeals with subtle (and sometimes not-so-subtle) social coercion. The Fred Gross stories capture this dynamic from the perspective of the small-scale participant — the man who buys bonds not out of conviction but out of an inability to say no.
