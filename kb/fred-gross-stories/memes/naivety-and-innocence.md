# Naivety and Innocence

Fred Gross is not innocent in the moral sense — he lies, schemes, and takes petty revenge like everyone else. But he possesses a deeper innocence: an inability to see through the surfaces of social life, to recognize when he is being manipulated, to understand that wanting something to be true does not make it true.

## Innocence as Narrative Condition

Fred's innocence is the precondition for Lardner's dramatic irony. Because Fred cannot see what is happening to him, the reader must see it instead. The real estate agent's manipulation, the architect's evasion, the neighbor's condescension — all are visible to the reader through the transparent medium of Fred's narration, even as Fred himself remains oblivious.

## The Price of Innocence

Fred's naivety costs him. He pays more than he should for the lot, the house, the grading, the bathtub, the drinks. He trusts people who do not deserve his trust and is surprised when that trust is not rewarded. Yet his naivety is also protective — it shields him from the full recognition of how often he has been taken advantage of.

## Innocence and the American Character

Lardner's portrait of Fred's innocence participates in a long American literary tradition — the innocent abroad, the naif who encounters a corrupt world and fails to recognize corruption. But Lardner's version is distinctly modern: Fred's innocence is not rural simplicity but suburban aspiration, the faith that the system works for people like him.
