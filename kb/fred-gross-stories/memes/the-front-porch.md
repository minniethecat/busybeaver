# The Front Porch

The front porch of Fred's bungalow is a liminal space — neither fully inside nor fully outside, neither fully private nor fully public. It is where the Gross family sits on summer evenings, where neighbors are greeted, where the boundary between domestic life and community life is negotiated.

## The Bungalow Porch

The front porch was an essential feature of bungalow architecture — a wide, roofed platform that extended the living space of the house into the outdoors. It was a place for sitting, visiting, watching the street, and being seen by passersby. The porch was both amenity and statement — a declaration that the occupants of the house had leisure time to enjoy and social connections to maintain.

## The Social Threshold

The porch mediates between the private world of the home and the public world of the neighborhood. A visitor approaches the porch, is greeted, is invited inside — or not. The porch is where social calls begin and end, where casual encounters with neighbors occur, where the family presents itself to the community. It is the stage for the performance of domestic respectability.

## Fred's Porch

Fred's porch is where he sits after work, where he and Grace receive visitors, where the children play on summer afternoons. It is a space of relaxation and display, and Fred's relationship to it is characteristically double: he enjoys the porch as a amenity of homeownership and worries about it as a marker of status. Is his porch as nice as the Carpenters'? As welcoming as the Curtises'? The porch, like everything Fred owns, is subject to the calculus of social comparison.
