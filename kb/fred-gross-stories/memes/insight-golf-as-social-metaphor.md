# Golf as Social Metaphor

Golf in the Fred Gross stories is never just golf. It is a metaphor for everything Fred Gross is trying and failing to do: compete with his social superiors, master an arcane system of rules, perform competence in public, and maintain dignity in the face of repeated failure.

## The Rules You Cannot Master

Golf's elaborate rulebook — handicaps, penalties, etiquette, the endless minutiae of the game — mirrors the unwritten rules of Allison society. Fred cannot master either set of rules, and his frustration with golf's complexities is indistinguishable from his frustration with social complexities. In both domains, he feels that the rules were designed by people who already know them, to the disadvantage of people who are still learning.

## The Public Performance

Golf is played in public, before an audience of fellow members who watch from the clubhouse veranda and judge from the first tee. Fred cannot practice in private and perform when ready; every stroke is witnessed, every failure is visible. Social life in Allison operates on the same principle: there is no rehearsal, only performance, and every performance is evaluated.

## The Unwinnable Game

Golf, for Fred, is unwinnable in principle. Even as he improves, the handicap system adjusts, and the better players remain out of reach. The game is designed so that most players lose most of the time, and Fred's experience of golf — perpetual striving, occasional small successes, inevitable frustration — is his experience of life in Allison.
