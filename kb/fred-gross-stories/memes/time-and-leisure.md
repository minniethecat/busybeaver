# Time and Leisure

Leisure in the Fred Gross stories is never simply free time — it is time that must be filled with the right activities, in the right company, for the right reasons. Golf, cards, parties, club functions: these are not relaxations but obligations, arenas in which social capital is earned and spent.

## The Structure of Leisure

Leisure in Allison follows a predictable calendar. Weekends are for golf (in season) and social calls. Evenings are for cards or club functions. Sunday is for church and formal dinner. The schedule is not explicitly enforced but universally observed, and deviation from it is noticed. Fred's leisure time is as structured as his work time, just by different rules.

## Leisure and Class

The content of leisure signals class position. Golf is for the middle class; boxing or baseball spectating might be for the working class. The country club is for members; the public park is for everyone. Fred's leisure choices — golf, cards, club functions — place him firmly in the aspiring middle class, and his anxiety about performing these activities correctly reflects his awareness of what is at stake.

## Work and Leisure

For Fred, leisure is defined in opposition to work — it is what he does when he is not at the office. But the distinction is not clean. His social activities generate expenses that require more work to pay. His leisure time is colonized by the same anxieties that occupy his working hours. The boundary between work and leisure is permeable, and Fred's mind rarely rests.
