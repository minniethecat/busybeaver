# First National Bank

The First National Bank of Chicago — Fred Gross's employer and the institution where he navigates the bewildering world of mortgage applications, title guarantees, and construction loans. A recurring presence in the Fred Gross stories, the bank embodies the institutional bureaucracy that stands between Fred and the American Dream of homeownership.

## Overview

The First National Bank serves a dual role in Fred Gross's life: it is both his employer — the source of his salary and professional identity — and the financial gatekeeper that controls his access to the credit needed for suburban homeownership. When Fred approaches the bank for a construction loan, he confronts a system of rules, paperwork, and institutional logic that seems designed to frustrate the ordinary working man. (001-017)

## Role in the Stories

- **Fred's employer**: Fred works at the First National Bank, where his office is located "way up on pretty near the top" — a detail he reports with characteristic pride. The bank provides Fred with a stable white-collar salary that makes the suburban experiment financially conceivable. (001-022)

- **The loan application**: When Fred attempts to secure a construction loan, the bank informs him that the lot must be fully paid off before any building loan can be considered. The logic — the bank won't lend against unencumbered land — is sound from an institutional perspective, but it comes as a shock to Fred, who had assumed his good standing as an employee would smooth the process. (001-017)

Fred's encounter with the bank's real estate department is a masterpiece of institutional frustration:

> Well after this was fixed up I went a round to the bank where I had my saveings at & went to the real estate dept. & seen the man there and asked him did they make building lones & the man says yes. Well I says I want to borry $1,200 to build a bungello out to Allison & he says do you own a lot & I says yes...he says o no you wont because we dont do no busness that way & 1st you got to pay off what you owe on the lot & then may be we can fix it up a bout a building lone but I wont promus nothing.
>
> (001-017)

- **Title and deed complications**: The bank's requirements expose Fred to the arcane world of title guarantees, warranty deeds, and interest calculations — a world in which he is thoroughly out of his depth. His letters to Charley are filled with both bewilderment and resentment at the complexity of the process. (001-016) (001-017)

- **The savings plan**: The bank's refusal to lend against the lot forces the Gross family into an aggressive savings plan — banking all of Grace's millinery earnings while living on Fred's salary. The plan is a testament to the family's determination, but also a reminder of how precarious their financial position truly is. (001-011)

## Significance

The First National Bank is more than a setting — it is an antagonist in Fred's narrative, representing the institutional forces that constrain and frustrate individual ambition. Lardner uses the bank to critique the gap between the rhetoric of homeownership (accessible to all hard-working Americans) and the reality (a bureaucratic maze designed by and for institutions).
