# The Price Tag

Fred Gross's letters to Charley are punctuated by prices — $2,000 for the bungalow, $100 for the architect, $60 for the grading, $25 for the bathtub upgrade, $90 for the taxes. These numbers are not incidental details but the building blocks of Fred's consciousness. He thinks in prices, and his narration is a running ledger of costs incurred and anticipated.

## The Habit of Accounting

Fred's price-tagging is a habit of mind — the reflex of a man whose finances are perpetually stretched. Every experience has a cost, and the cost is the first thing Fred records. The habit is partly practical (he needs to track his obligations) and partly psychological (naming the price is a way of asserting control over expenses that feel uncontrollable).

## The Rhetoric of Cost

Fred's invocation of prices serves a rhetorical function in his letters to Charley. The price tag is evidence — of the bargains he has found, of the injustices he has suffered, of the sacrifices he has made. "They stuck me up for $60" is a moral claim as much as a financial report. The price tag is a device of persuasion, and Fred wields it with unconscious skill.

## What Prices Conceal

Fred's focus on individual prices conceals the cumulative picture. He records each expense separately but never totals them, never calculates what the bungalow truly cost including every overage, upgrade, and unexpected bill. The focus on the individual price tag is a way of avoiding the aggregate — a financial version of the self-deception that characterizes Fred's thinking in every domain.
