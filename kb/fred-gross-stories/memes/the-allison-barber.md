# The Allison Barber

The barber shop in Allison is more than a place to get a haircut — it is a male social space, a node in the community's information network, and a site where the rituals of masculine grooming intersect with the rituals of masculine sociability.

## The Shop

A barber shop in 1915 was a distinctive space — the striped pole outside, the chairs inside, the mirrors, the combs and scissors and straight razors, the smell of tonic and talc. It was a place of transformation, where men emerged looking more respectable than when they entered. For Fred, a visit to the barber was part of the routine of self-maintenance — a small investment in the appearance that mattered in Allison's social economy.

## The Barber

The barber was a figure of community knowledge — someone who heard the talk of the town while cutting the hair of its citizens. He knew who was prospering and who was struggling, who was feuding and who was reconciled. The barber chair was a confessional, and the barber was a secular priest, absolving no one but listening to everyone.

## The Male Space

The barber shop was one of the few exclusively male spaces in the town — no women, no children (except boys getting their first haircuts), just men in the company of men. The conversation was masculine in idiom and content: business, sports, politics, the mild complaints about wives and work that constituted male bonding. For Fred, the barber shop was another arena of masculine performance, less formal than the club but no less socially significant.
