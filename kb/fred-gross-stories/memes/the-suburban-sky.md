# The Suburban Sky

The sky over Allison is different from the sky over Chicago — more visible, less obstructed, a reminder of the openness that Fred sought when he moved his family to the suburbs. It is the negative space of the suburban landscape, the emptiness that defines the fullness of the domestic world below.

## The Visible Sky

In Chicago, the sky is fragmented by buildings — visible in slices between structures, reflected in windows, present but mediated. In Allison, the sky is open — a dome over the bungalow, the garden, the golf course. This openness is part of what Fred paid for when he bought the lot: not just a house but a relationship to the natural world that the city could not provide.

## Weather and the Sky

The sky is the source of weather — the rain that delays construction, the sun that enables grading, the cold that threatens the foundation. Fred's attention to the sky is practical rather than aesthetic: he watches it for what it portends, not for what it is. The sky is a utilitarian phenomenon, part of the equipment of homeownership.

## The Unnoticed Sublime

Fred rarely describes the sky in aesthetic terms — that is not his idiom. But the sky is there, in the background of every outdoor scene, and the reader can supply what Fred does not articulate: the beauty of the open Midwestern sky, the changing light of the seasons, the quiet sublimity of the natural world surrounding the small dramas of suburban life.
