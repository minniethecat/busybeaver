# Fred Joins the Allison Country Club

Fred Gross's decision to join the Allison Country Club in May 1916 — a social milestone driven more by his wife Mary's ambitions than his own enthusiasm, and the beginning of his comically disastrous relationship with the game of golf.

## Overview

In May 1916, shortly after moving to Allison, Fred Gross joined the Allison Country Club. The decision was motivated primarily by his wife Mary's desire for social acceptance — she believed club membership would open doors to Allison's better circles. For Fred, the club became the setting for a series of humiliating encounters with golf, a game he neither understood nor enjoyed. (P03-003) (P03-004)

## Background

The Gross family's move from Chicago to Allison represented both an escape from city congestion and an entry into a more socially stratified community. Mary Gross quickly realized that social life in Allison revolved around institutions like the country club, and that membership was effectively mandatory for anyone who wanted to be "somebody" in town. (P03-007)

Fred, meanwhile, was preoccupied with the practical and financial challenges of homeownership — the mortgage, the cement sidewalk, the furnishing of the bungalow. The country club, with its fees and its dress codes and its arcane games, struck him as an unnecessary expense. But Mary was insistent, and Fred rarely denied Mary anything. (P03-004)

## Key Events

- **The decision to join**: Fred announced his decision to join the Allison Country Club in a letter to his friend Charley, dated May 1. He admitted that he had joined "mostly on acct. of the Mrs." and that he had little interest in the club's primary activity — golf. (P03-003) (P03-004)

Fred's announcement of his membership to Charley mixes bravado with barely concealed reluctance:

> I have joined the Allison country club Charley and the first day I can get off I am going out and take a whirl at golf and if I cant make some of these high brows around here look like a sucker at that game I will kill myself or something.
>
> (P03-003)

- **Mr. Ewing and the first golf lesson**: A fellow club member named Mr. Ewing asked Fred if he played golf. Fred replied that he did not but was confident he could pick it up quickly — a confidence that proved entirely unfounded. (P03-006)

- **The humiliations of learning golf**: Fred's attempts to learn golf became a running source of comedy. He found the game far harder than it looked, took lessons from the club's golf teacher, and struggled with the physical and social demands of the sport. (P03-015) (P03-028)

- **Mary's social campaign**: While Fred flailed on the links, Mary pursued her own agenda at the club — attending dances, card parties, and social gatherings designed to integrate the Gross family into Allison's social fabric. (P03-004) (P03-007)

## Aftermath & Consequences

Fred's country club membership had mixed results. On the one hand, it did give Mary the social entrée she craved — she attended parties and made connections that elevated the Gross family's standing in Allison. On the other hand, Fred's golf struggles became a recurring source of frustration and self-deprecating humor in his letters. The club membership also imposed a financial burden that Fred, already anxious about money, resented. (P03-016)

The country club would become a recurring setting in the Fred Gross stories — the site of social triumphs and humiliations, card games and confrontations, and Fred's ongoing battle with a game he could never quite master.

## Literary Significance

Fred's country club membership crystallizes the central tension of the Fred Gross stories: the gap between Fred's rough-edged, practical, city-bred sensibility and the genteel pretensions of suburban respectability. Lardner uses golf — a game Fred cannot play, does not understand, and secretly despises — as a metaphor for everything alienating about his new life. The country club is not merely a setting; it is a theater of class performance, and Fred, as always, is miscast.
