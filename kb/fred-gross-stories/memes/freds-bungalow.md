# Fred's Bungalow

The two-story house Fred Gross builds on his 100-by-150-foot Allison lot — a physical manifestation of his middle-class aspirations and the stage on which all the domestic comedies of the six stories play out, from construction calamities to card parties, feuds, and family crises.

## Overview

Fred's bungalow is more than a house: it is the project around which the first story revolves and the fixed point from which all subsequent stories radiate. The lot is purchased for $1,500 — "100 ft. frontidge & 150 ft. deep & only $1,500" (001-010) — and the house itself is built for $2,000 under the supervision of a young architect whose office is "way up on pretty near the top flr. of the Jackson bldg." (001-017). The construction process, chronicled in exhaustive and exasperated detail across Story 1, becomes a tutorial in the gap between the dream of homeownership and its reality: contractors quit, costs overrun, the sidewalk cracks immediately, and the move-in date keeps slipping.

Once occupied, the bungalow becomes the container for Fred and Grace's social life. Card parties are hosted in its rooms, neighbors drop by uninvited, and Fred conducts his telephone harassment campaign against Hamilton from its windows. The house's physical features — the fireplace Grace keeps burning even in hot weather because "the fire looks so pretty" (002-011), the basement storing leftover lumber, the front porch that offers a stage for neighborhood surveillance — are woven into the narrative fabric of every story.

## In the Stories

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

The entire story is structured around the bungalow's creation. Fred selects the lot, negotiates with the bank's real estate department, hires the architect, battles the contractor, and endures a cascade of hidden costs: "$60 for gradeing but that gradeing dident take so I payed $150 more for the same job the 2nd time" (001-094). The house that was supposed to cost $2,000 ends up costing far more, and the move-in is delayed from March to sometime later — Fred and Grace are forced to board at a hotel and store their furniture in the interim. The bungalow is both the object of Fred's pride and the source of his financial precariousness.

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

Now resident, Fred discovers that owning the bungalow does not mean controlling it. The village of Allison orders a new sidewalk. The fireplace consumes the leftover construction lumber while Grace admires the flames. And the arrival of new neighbors — the Hamiltons — transforms the bungalow from a private retreat into a node in a social network Fred cannot opt out of. "Graces old man come acrost with the money so as I could pay the int." (002-002) — the bungalow's mortgage is a recurring anxiety that links the domestic space to obligations extending far beyond Allison.

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

The bungalow becomes a vantage point for surveillance and a base of operations in the Gross-Hamilton feud. Fred can see Hamilton's house from his window: "I can see out of the window when he sets down to breakfast and then I call him up" (004-064). Martin, the next-door neighbor, crosses the property line for card games that escalate into the central conflict of the story. The bungalow's physical proximity to the Hamilton residence — no fences, no anonymity — makes the feud inescapable.

## Historical & Geographic Context

The bungalow Fred builds represents a specific moment in American architectural and social history. The Craftsman bungalow — a modest, efficient single-family house popularized by Gustav Stickley's magazine *The Craftsman* (1901–1916) — was the dominant middle-class housing type of the 1910s. These houses typically featured low-pitched roofs, wide front porches, and open floor plans, and they were marketed through pattern books and kit-home catalogs (Sears, Aladdin) that made architectural design accessible to people of Fred's means.

The $2,000 construction cost Fred quotes was realistic for a modest suburban house in 1915 (roughly $55,000 in 2025 dollars). The architect's 5% fee ($100) and the contractor troubles Fred experiences were standard features of the speculative building economy of the era, when small-scale contractors routinely walked off jobs that proved unprofitable. The bungalow's location in Allison — 16 miles west of Chicago's Loop — placed it in the path of the early 20th century's most dramatic suburban expansion, fueled by commuter railroads and a cultural imperative toward homeownership that Fred articulates with poignant (and comic) earnestness.

Fred's eagerness to escape apartment life and build the bungalow is palpable:

> I cant hardly wait till we got our lot & started to build our bungello because a man aint realy liveing when he is liveing in a flat & espeshaly when youve got childern.
>
> (001-006)
