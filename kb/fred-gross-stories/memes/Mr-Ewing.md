# Mr. Ewing

Mr. Ewing, "president of the village" (P03-005) and a businessman "in the coal business down town" (P03-005), occupies the apex of Allison's social hierarchy — a position that makes him both a gatekeeper Fred must impress and a figure of institutional authority Fred can never quite satisfy.

## Role & Overview

Ewing appears in the Fore! section as the man who invites Fred to join the Allison Country Club and thereby sets in motion the entire country club plotline. His invitation — "Mr. Ewing thats in the coal business down town that ast me to join" (P03-005) — is both a social opportunity and a financial obligation, as Fred discovers when the "president's cup thats put up by Mr. Ewing the president of the club" (P03-042) comes with costs Fred did not anticipate.

Ewing's dual role as village president and club president makes him the institutional embodiment of Allison. To join the club is to enter Ewing's domain; to play in the president's cup is to accept his authority; to fail at the club's financial obligations is to fall short in his eyes.

## Character Profile

### Background

Ewing is in the coal business — a stable, profitable industry in 1915 Chicago — and serves as president of both the village of Allison and the country club. This concentration of civic and social authority in one person reflects the reality of small suburban communities where business leaders doubled as civic and social leaders.

### Personality & Traits

Ewing is portrayed through Fred's anxious perspective. He is neither warm nor hostile — simply the man in charge, whose judgment matters. He asks Fred "did I play golf. I told him no, but I never seen no game yet I couldent pick up" (P03-006) — and Fred's bluster in response to this simple question reveals everything about the power differential between them.

## Key Appearances

Ewing recruits Fred for the club, sponsors the president's cup tournament, and presides over the institution that will become the theater of Fred's social ambitions and humiliations. He appears only briefly but his institutional presence shadows every club scene.

## Literary Significance

Ewing represents institutional authority in Lardner's social world. He is not a character so much as a position — the person who decides who belongs and who does not. Fred's relationship with Ewing is entirely aspirational: he wants Ewing's approval but can never be certain he has it, and this uncertainty defines his experience of the club.

Mr. Ewing asks Fred the crucial question:

> Mr. Ewing ast me did I play golf. I told him no, but I never seen the game yet I couldent pick up in a short time and master it.
>
> (P03-006)
