# The Family Budget

When the bank refuses Fred's building loan application, the Gross family's response is not despair but determination: a six-month austerity plan designed to save $30 per week instead of $25. The budget is Fred's characteristic solution to financial difficulty — more work, less spending, deferred gratification.

## The Arithmetic

Fred calculates that if they "live pretty clost" they can save $30 per week, paying off the $700 lot balance in less than six months. The arithmetic is optimistic — it assumes no unexpected expenses, no emergencies, no deviations from the plan. Fred's budgets always assume the best case, and reality always delivers something worse.

> I figure the hole thing includeing the lot will amt. to a bout $3,700 & thats a lot of money but look what wear geting a nice home of our own where decent people lives & a big yd. where we can let the kids run a raund & may be raise chickens & have a garden. I guess thats better than paying $25 per mo. rent & being cooped up in a 4 room flat eh Charley.
>
> (001-023)

## The Sacrifice

Living "pretty clost" means sacrifices that Fred does not detail but that the reader can infer: fewer entertainments, simpler meals, postponed purchases, the constant pressure of saying no to things the family wants. Grace bears much of this burden, managing a household on a constrained budget while maintaining the appearance of middle-class respectability.

## The Discipline

Fred takes pride in the discipline the budget represents. Saving, in his understanding, is a moral act — proof of character, evidence of deservingness. The budget is not merely a financial plan but a statement of values, and Fred's adherence to it is a point of honor. That the discipline is imposed by necessity rather than chosen by virtue is a distinction Fred blurs in his own telling.
