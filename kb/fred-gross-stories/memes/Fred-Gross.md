# Fred Gross

Fred A. Gross — Chicago clerk, suburban aspirant, country club member, and compulsive letter-writer — is the narrator and central figure of Ring Lardner's six-story epistolary series, through whose distinctive dialect voice the reader experiences the comedy and pathos of early 20th-century American middle-class life.

## Role & Overview

Fred Gross is the first-person narrator of all six stories, each presented as a series of dated letters to his brother Charley. Fred is a clerk in Chicago, married to Grace, with at least two young children. His letters document his family's move from a city apartment to a suburban lot in Allison, Illinois, his entanglements with neighbors and country club members, and his perpetual negotiation between aspiration and limitation.

Fred's narrative voice—a semi-literate dialect rendered with painstaking phonetic precision by Lardner—is the defining feature of the stories. Fred misspells, mangles grammar, and misuses words, but his voice is never merely comic: it conveys genuine emotion, sharp observation, and a fundamental decency that survives every social humiliation Lardner subjects him to. (P03-025), (001-008), (001-014)

## Character Profile

### Background

Fred works as a clerk in Chicago's business district, commuting daily from his home. His salary—never specified but sufficient to support a family of four and save modest amounts—places him in the lower middle class. He and Grace have "a bout $800" in combined savings: Fred has saved $300, Grace $500. (001-005)

### Personality & Traits

Fred is earnest, optimistic, and easily impressed. He wants to do the right thing—buy a home, join the club, be a good neighbor—but his understanding of how the world works consistently falls short of what his ambitions require. He is both boastful and insecure, generous and calculating, good-natured and quick to take offense.

> "I bet when you here what I & Grace has made up our minds you & Mary will wisht you was doing the same thing" (001-002)

His defining trait is his unreliability as a narrator. Fred reports events faithfully but interprets them in ways that reveal more than he intends. When Fred complains about the "rotten L servus" (001-004) or boasts of his golf progress, the reader sees the gap between Fred's self-image and reality—and that gap is where Lardner's satire lives.

## Key Appearances

### Own Your Own Home (Story 1)

Fred and Grace decide to buy a lot in Allison and build a bungalow. Fred navigates real estate agents, banks, and building loans with characteristic enthusiasm and limited success. He commits to an aggressive savings plan: "save $30 per wk. in sted of $25 if we live pretty clost" (001-017).

### Welcome to Our City (Story 2)

The arrival of the Hamiltons as new neighbors provides Fred with an audience for his suburban performance and a target for the practical jokes that drive the plot. Fred reports his letter-writing difficulties: "I set down & tride to write a letter to them peopl asking them to" (002-045) respond.

### Uncivil War (Story 4)

Fred's conflict with Hamilton escalates into open neighborly warfare. His reflection on the absurdity of the situation—"its a funny World where a man has neighbors on 1 side of you that you could" (004-002) get along with—reveals both his humor and his helplessness.

### The Last Laugh (Story 3)

Fred navigates the accelerated social calendar of suburban Allison, attending a series of parties where his discomfort with auction bridge and the rituals of middle-class entertaining exposes both his social ambitions and his limitations. (003-003), (003-011), (003-012)

### War Bribes (Story 5)

Fred becomes an enthusiastic investor in war-related stocks, his financial ambitions running ahead of his understanding. (005-030), (005-081)

### The Swift Six (Story 6)

Fred attends the Chicago automobile show and dreams of car ownership, his suburban aspirations extending to the ultimate status symbol. (006-026)

## Literary Significance

Fred Gross is one of Lardner's most fully realized creations. His dialect voice—comic but never condescended to—established a template for American vernacular narration that influenced everyone from Hemingway to Salinger. Fred is the archetype of the American naif: well-meaning, perpetually out of his depth, and utterly unaware of how much he reveals in every word he writes.

> "they talk about Chicago being a live place well Charley its like a grave yd. come pared with this place & some thing doing evry minut when your a good dresser & the peopl likes you" (003-009)
