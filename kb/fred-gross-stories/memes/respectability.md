# Respectability

Respectability is the invisible currency of Allison — more valuable than money, more fragile than reputation, more urgently sought than happiness. Fred Gross organizes his life around the pursuit of respectability, and his successes and failures in this pursuit define the emotional arc of the stories.

## The Components of Respectability

Respectability in Allison has specific components: homeownership, church attendance, club membership, the right kind of entertaining, the right kind of car, the right kind of wife and children. Each component is necessary; none is sufficient. Respectability is a composite, and its acquisition requires performance across multiple domains simultaneously.

## The Performance

Respectability is not a state but a performance — something you do rather than something you are. Fred understands this intuitively, which is why his pursuit of respectability takes the form of visible action: buying the lot, joining the club, hosting the party. He performs respectability in the hope that performance will become reality, that doing the right things will make him the right kind of person.

## The Anxiety of Respectability

The pursuit of respectability generates its own anxiety. The standards are high, the judges are many, and the consequences of failure — social exclusion, diminished status, the quiet judgment of neighbors — are real. Fred's anxiety about money, status, and belonging is, at root, anxiety about respectability: the fear that he will be found out, that his performance will fail, that he will be revealed as not quite the man he pretends to be.
