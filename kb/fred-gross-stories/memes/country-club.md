# Country Club

The Allison Country Club serves as the primary social institution of the Fred Gross stories, a space where Fred navigates the rituals of middle-class respectability with characteristic earnestness and incomprehension.

## Overview

When Fred Gross moves to Allison, Illinois, one of his very first acts is to join the Allison Country Club. His membership is not motivated by golf—"it isent on acct. of playing golf that I have joined the club but mostly on acct. of the Mrs." (P03-004)—but by social ambition, specifically his wife Grace's determination that the Gross family should belong to the community's premier institution.

The country club functions in the stories as a microcosm of the class system Fred is trying to navigate. Its rules—written and unwritten—govern everything from golf etiquette to bar tabs, and Fred's repeated failures to understand these rules are Lardner's vehicle for satirizing both the institution and the aspirant.

## In the Stories

### Fore! (Preface)

The Fore! section is set largely at the country club. Fred describes joining "the Allison country club" (P03-003) and his immediate discovery that membership brings obligations: "they was trying to build a new club house so they been makeing a" (P03-005) special assessment on new members. The financial surprises begin immediately—Fred is "elected all ready and could pay my fees when ever I felt like" (P03-007), discovering that the initial membership fee is only the beginning.

The club bar becomes Fred's social theater. Fellow members buy him drinks; he reciprocates; the bills accumulate. Fred's shock at discovering that "all that wine and them other drinks I signed for wasent free" (P03-116) is a perfect Lardner moment—the naif's encounter with institutional reality.

## Historical Context

Country clubs proliferated in American suburbs during the 1900s–1920s. They served multiple functions: golf and sports facilities, social hubs for the business class, and gatekeepers of community respectability. Membership fees, assessments, and bar charges made clubs significant financial commitments. The Allison Country Club, with its new clubhouse under construction and its special assessments on new members, reflects this era of institutional expansion.

## Literary Significance

The country club is Lardner's laboratory for studying class performance. Fred's dialect narration transforms the club's rituals—golf, drinking, dining, the subtle hierarchies of membership—into comic material while also revealing their cruelty. The club promises inclusion but delivers a running tally of Fred's inadequacies, and Fred's inability to perceive this contradiction is the essence of Lardner's satire.

Fred announces his country club membership with characteristic bravado:

> I have joined the Allison country club Charley and the first day I can get off I am going out and take a whirl at golf and if I cant make some of these high brows around here look like a sucker at that game I will kill myself or something.
>
> (P03-003)
