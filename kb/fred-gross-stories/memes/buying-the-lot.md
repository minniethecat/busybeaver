# Buying the Lot and Building the Bungalow

The series of real estate and construction transactions that form the central narrative of "Own Your Own Home" — Fred and Grace Gross's purchase of a suburban lot, their encounter with banking bureaucracy, and the construction of the bungalow that becomes the physical stage for all subsequent stories.

## Overview

The lot purchase and house construction occupy nearly the entirety of the first Fred Gross story, and their consequences reverberate through the entire series. Fred and Grace decide to escape apartment living for suburban homeownership, buying a 100 × 150 foot lot in Allison for $1,500 with a $100 down payment. Fred's attempt to secure a construction loan from his bank reveals the complexity of mortgage finance — the lot must be fully paid before any building loan is approved. The family commits to an aggressive savings plan, and construction eventually proceeds, but not before Fred has been educated in the hidden costs, contractual ambiguities, and institutional obstacles that attend the American Dream of homeownership.

## Background

The immediate catalyst is domestic: the Walters' chaotic visit exposes the inadequacy of the Gross family's apartment. Grace proposes the suburban solution: "Grace says lets buy a lot & build a little house some wheres in the subbirds" (001-003). Grace has $500 in savings; Fred has additional funds. The plan is to borrow the balance from Fred's bank.

The broader context is the 1915 suburban land boom around Chicago, fueled by commuter railroads, rising white-collar wages, and a cultural campaign promoting homeownership as the foundation of American citizenship and middle-class respectability. Fred and Grace are not individual actors but participants in a mass social movement.

## Key Events

- **The lot tour**: Fred and Grace visit Allison with a real estate agent who shows them "some swell lots only they was to high" before settling on the 100 × 150 foot property at $1,500 (001-010).
- **The $100 binder**: Fred puts down $100 to secure the lot, urged by the real estate man's warning that "some body else was libel to see it & cop it out" (001-011) — the classic pressure tactic of speculative land sales.
- **The bank meeting**: Fred approaches his bank for a construction loan and learns that "we dont do no busness that way" (001-017) — the lot must be fully paid off before any building loan can be considered. Fred is baffled by deeds, title guarantees, and interest calculations.
- **The savings commitment**: The family commits to banking all of Grace's millinery earnings while living on Fred's salary — a plan that Fred describes with pride and the reader recognizes as precarious.
- **The architect and contractor**: Fred hires an architect ("archateck") who recommends a contractor. The construction budget eventually reaches $2,000, bringing the all-in cost to roughly $3,700 — a figure Fred repeats with a mixture of pride and alarm: "thats a lot of money but look what wear geting a new house" (001-023).
- **The sidewalk order**: "The village of Allison has ordered us to put in a new side walk" (001-094) — municipal regulation adding unexpected costs and reminding Fred that property ownership entails obligations as well as pleasures.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The lot purchase and construction set in motion the entire series. The bungalow becomes the stage for all subsequent social dramas — the Hamiltons' arrival, the party-giving cycle, the neighbor feud, the automobile purchase. Financially, the commitment shapes Fred's behavior throughout: every subsequent expenditure (country club dues, party costs, the Swift Six) is measured against the mortgage. The "Own Your Own Home" dream, once achieved, proves to be not an ending but a beginning — the entry point into a world of obligations Fred had not anticipated.

## Literary Significance

The lot purchase and construction sequence is Lardner's most sustained treatment of the intersection between personal aspiration and institutional reality. Fred's journey from enthusiastic consumer to wary debtor is rendered through the accumulation of financial detail — dollar amounts, interest rates, contractual clauses — that Lardner uses to ground the comedy in documentary realism. The gap between Fred's initial confidence ("I bet when you here what I & Grace has made up our minds") and his later weariness ("if it wasent to late Id tell the bank to go to") is the emotional arc of the entire series in miniature.

Fred's encounter with the bank reveals the institutional barriers to his dream of homeownership:

> I went a round to the bank where I had my saveings at & went to the real estate dept. & seen the man there and asked him did they make building lones & the man says yes. Well I says I want to borry $1,200 to build a bungello out to Allison & he says do you own a lot & I says yes & he says let me take a look at your papers so I show him my papers & he says yes you own a lot all but paying $700 more on it.
>
> (001-017)
