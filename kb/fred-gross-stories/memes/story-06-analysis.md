# The Swift Six

The final Fred Gross story brings the series full circle, as Fred attends the Chicago automobile show and the car — the ultimate symbol of the status he has pursued throughout the letters — takes center stage.

**Author:** Ring Lardner (as Fred Gross)
**Published:** 1919
**Story Number:** 6

## Synopsis

Fred attends the Chicago automobile show and reports his impressions to Charley with characteristic enthusiasm. The automobile — the status symbol that has haunted the series from the beginning — becomes the story's subject, as Fred dreams of ownership while remaining firmly in the spectator class.

## Plot Summary

Fred reports "going up to the Coliseum to the automobile show and Id rather lay around" (006-020) than miss it. His visit "up to the automobile show yesterday and they certainly" (006-026) have impressive vehicles confirms his long-standing automobile fascination. His disparagement of "them suburban trains where your lucky to" (006-027) find a seat contrasts with the freedom promised by car ownership. His suspicion of "automobile agts" (006-048) and Grace's denial of "springing no automobile story" (006-080) show the automobile's penetration of the Gross household's consciousness.

> It would be pretty soft to drive to town and back every day in your car and not half to patternize them suburban trains where your lucky to find rm. enough for your ft. to stand on let alone rm. enough to set down unlest you starve your self for a few months 1st.
>
> (006-027)

## Themes

- **Automobile Culture** — the car as symbol and substance of American consumer desire
- **Aspiration vs. Limitation** — Fred's enduring inability to close the gap
- **Technology & Progress** — the automobile as modernity's promise

## Style & Form

The final story functions as a coda to the series, returning to the automobile theme that has threaded through the letters since the Fore! section. Fred ends the series as he began: watching, wanting, writing to Charley.
