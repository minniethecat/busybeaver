## Definition

Counterfeiting in *As You Like It* refers to the deliberate performance of a false emotion or state, distinct from the physical disguise that dominates the play's plot. Where [[disguise]] alters external appearance — [[rosalind|Rosalind]] becoming [[ganimed|Ganymede]], [[celia|Celia]] becoming [[aliena|Aliena]] — counterfeiting alters performed affect: the swoon, the blush, the lover's sigh. The word recurs most densely in Act 4 Scene 3, where [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s fainting at the sight of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s bloodied napkin becomes a site of meta-theatrical play about what is real and what is performed, a question that radiates outward to implicate the boy actor, the stage, and the very nature of theatrical representation.

## In As You Like It

The motif surfaces early when [[rosalind|Rosalind]], still in the court, coaches [[celia|Celia]] on dissembling cheerfulness: "Now counterfeit to swound, why now fall downe" (015-019). The line collapses performance and instruction — [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is already rehearsing the swoon she will later perform in earnest. This rehearsal quality pervades the play's treatment of counterfeiting, positioning identity itself as something practiced rather than possessed.

The motif's richest exploration comes in Act 4 Scene 3, when [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — still disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] — faints upon seeing [[orlando|Orlando]]'s bloodied napkin, delivered by [[oliver|Oliver]]. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s observation is direct: "a body would thinke this was well counterfeited" (018-171). [[rosalind|Rosalind]] immediately seizes the word, insisting "I pray you tell your brother how well I counterfeited" (018-172), reframing a genuine physiological response as a calculated performance. [[oliver|Oliver]] resists the reframe: "This was not counterfeit, there is too great testimony" (018-174). Yet [[rosalind|Rosalind]] persists: "Counterfeit, I assure you" (018-176).

This exchange opens a vertiginous meta-theatrical space. A boy actor plays a woman ([[rosalind|Rosalind]]) who is disguised as a boy ([[ganimed|Ganymede]]) who feigns a woman's swoon at the sight of blood — and then claims the swoon was faked. The audience watches a performed faint that the performer insists was performance, while the other character observes that it looked real. The scene asks whether any emotion performed on stage is more or less "counterfeit" than any emotion felt off it, and whether the distinction itself holds.

The word echoes once more as the scene closes: "my counterfeiting to him" (018-186), as [[rosalind|Rosalind]] promises to continue the performance. Counterfeiting, the play suggests, is not an occasional deception but a continuous practice — a mode of being in which identity is always in quotation marks.
