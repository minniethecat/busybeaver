# Oliver

## Overview

Oliver de Boys is the eldest son of Sir Rowland de Boys and the play's domestic antagonist. In Act 1, he denies [[orlando|Orlando]] his inheritance, withholds his education, and plots his death — first through [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler, then through arson. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s villainy is rooted in envy: "for my soule (yet I know not why) hates nothing more then he" (001-049). His transformation is the play's most dramatic: saved from a lioness by [[orlando|Orlando]], he converts from fratricide to fraternal love, and his sudden love for [[celia|Celia]] completes the fourth marriage. Oliver embodies the play's argument that even the most hardened villain can be redeemed by mercy and love.

## Role in the Play

Oliver opens the play as its most immediately compelling villain. His mistreatment of [[orlando|Orlando]] — keeping him "rustically at home" (001-002), denying him education, and calling him "villaine" — is rendered with psychological precision. His confession to [[charles|Charles]] reveals an irrational hatred: "I hope I shall see an end of him; for my soule (yet I know not why) hates nothing more then he" (001-049).

After [[orlando|Orlando]]'s escape, Oliver pursues him to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. His near-death to the lioness and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s mercy in saving him produce the play's most significant conversion. [[orlando|Orlando]] describes the encounter to [[rosalind|Rosalind]], who faints at the sight of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s blood-stained handkerchief — her swoon, mistaken for a performance, reveals the depth of her love.

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s love for [[celia|Celia]] is instantaneous and transformative. [[celia|Celia]] describes it to [[rosalind|Rosalind]]: "your brother and my sister no sooner met, but they look'd: no sooner look'd, but they lou'd" (020-017) — the swiftest love in the play. Oliver's conversion from fratricide to bridegroom completes the play's pattern: evil is not punished but redeemed.

## The Conversion

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s transformation is the most structurally compressed conversion in the play. He enters [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] intending to capture or kill [[orlando|Orlando]]: he is [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[hunting|hunter]]. But the [[hunting|hunter]] becomes the hunted when a lioness attacks him as he sleeps. [[orlando|Orlando]], who has every reason to let his brother die, instead risks his own life to save him — an act of mercy that costs [[orlando|Orlando]] a wound to his arm.

The conversion is reported rather than staged. Oliver tells the story to [[ganimed|Ganymede]] ([[rosalind|Rosalind]] in disguise) and [[celia|Celia]] in Act 4, Scene 3, and the telling itself becomes the means of his redemption: his description of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s heroism is his first act of fraternal generosity. The conversion is sealed by his decision to estate his father's house and all its revenue upon [[orlando|Orlando]] (020-002), reversing the inheritance crime that opened the play. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s arc is the play's most complete — from denying his brother his birthright to freely giving it, from would-be murderer to loving husband.

## Key Scenes

### The Confession (Act 1, Scene 1)

> Oli. I hope I shall see an end of him; for my soule (yet I know not why) hates nothing more then he: yet hee's gentle, neuer school'd, and yet learned, full of noble deuise (001-049)

### The Arson Plot (Act 2, Scene 3)

[[adam|Adam]] warns [[orlando|Orlando]] that Oliver "this night he meanes, To burne the lodging where you vse to lye, And you within it" (006-025). This is [[oliver|Oliver]]'s second murder plot, more direct than the wrestling scheme.

### The Lioness (Act 4, Scene 3)

Oliver describes how [[orlando|Orlando]] saved him from the lioness — the act of mercy that transforms him.

### Love for Celia (Act 5, Scene 2)

Oliver and [[celia|Celia]] fall in love at first sight, completing the play's fourth romantic couple. Oliver declares he will "liue and die a Shepherd" in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] (020-002).

## Relationships

- [[orlando|Orlando]] — Younger brother; his victim, then his redeemer. Oliver seeks his death, then receives his mercy.
- [[celia|Celia]] — Sudden romantic love; they marry in the final scene, reconciling the de Boys brothers.
- [[charles|Charles]] — His co-conspirator in the plot against [[orlando|Orlando]]'s life.
- [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] — Fellow antagonist, parallel conversion arc; both are redeemed at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge.
- [[adam|Adam]] — The loyal servant of Sir Rowland who warns [[orlando|Orlando]] of [[oliver|Oliver]]'s murderous intent.
