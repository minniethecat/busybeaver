# [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Banishment

## Summary

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]] in Act 1, Scene 3 is the event that propels the play into [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], having already banished his brother [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], now turns on [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — his daughter [[celia|Celia]]'s beloved companion. His decree is absolute: "Mistris, dispatch you with your safest haste, And get you from our Court" (003-024), with death as the penalty for disobedience. [[celia|Celia]]'s response — choosing voluntary [[freedom|exile]] with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — transforms a punishment into a freely chosen adventure.

## Dramatic Significance

The banishment serves multiple dramatic functions. It completes the play's pattern of [[freedom|exile]] ([[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] first, now [[rosalind|Rosalind]]), demonstrates the arbitrary nature of tyrannical power, and triggers [[celia|Celia]]'s act of voluntary loyalty that defines her character. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s decision to disguise herself as a man for the journey — "mans apparell" — is a practical response to danger that becomes the source of her comic power in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]].

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s stated reason for the [[exile|banishment]] reveals his paranoia. He claims [[rosalind|Rosalind]] "is too subtile for thee, and her smoothnesse, Her verie silence, and her patience, Speake to the people, and they pittie her" (003-065). Her virtue is perceived as a political threat, and her popularity with the people makes her dangerous. [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s tyranny thus consumes even his daughter's happiness.
