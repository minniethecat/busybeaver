# Corin

## Character Overview

Corin is an old shepherd in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] who embodies pastoral authenticity. Unlike the exiled courtiers who play at rural life or the lovelorn [[silvius|Silvius]] who performs Petrarchan suffering, Corin actually works the land. He is a hired man — "I am shepheard to another man, / And do not sheere the Fleeces that I graze" (007-067) — yet he speaks with a dignity that makes him one of the play's most quietly authoritative figures. His exchange with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] in Act 3, Scene 2 is the play's most concentrated exploration of the tension between court [[wit]] and pastoral simplicity, and Corin emerges from it not only unscathed but victorious.

Harold Bloom described Corin as representing "the dignity of labor without ideology," and the character's refusal to romanticize his existence stands in pointed contrast to the pastoral conventions that surround him. Where [[silvius|Silvius]] sighs and [[phebe|Phebe]] scorns, Corin simply works — and in that simplicity lies a wisdom that [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s elaborate [[wit]] cannot penetrate.

## Role in the Plot

Corin serves three essential dramatic functions. First, he is the gatekeeper of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]: his encounter with the exhausted refugees [[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[celia|Celia]], and [[touchstone|Touchstone]] in Act 2, Scene 4 provides the practical entry point into pastoral life. Though he cannot offer hospitality directly — his master is "of churlish disposition" — he directs them to [[rosalinds-cottage|the cottage]] that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] purchases, becoming "your very faithfull Feeder" (007-089).

Second, he is the enabler of the romantic comedy. By selling his cottage (as proxy) to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]], he provides the physical space in which the play's central love plot unfolds. His report of [[silvius|Silvius]]'s suffering in Act 3, Scene 4 brings [[rosalind|Rosalind]] to witness the [[phebe|Phebe]]-[[silvius|Silvius]] dynamic, catalyzing her intervention and the complex courtship plot that follows.

Third, and most importantly, he is the philosophical counterweight to [[touchstone|Touchstone]]. Their debate is the play's most direct confrontation between court and country, [[wit]] and wisdom, sophistication and simplicity — and it is Corin, not [[touchstone|Touchstone]], who has the last meaningful word.

## Philosophy

[[corin|Corin]]'s philosophy is stated directly and memorably:

> Sir, I am a true labourer, I earne that I eate, get that I weare; owe no man hate, enuie no mans happinesse: glad of other mens good, content with my harme, and the greatest of my pride is, to see my Ewes graze, and my Lambes sucke. (012-034)

This is not naive simplicity but a coherent ethical stance: self-sufficiency, contentment, [[freedom]] from envy, and pride in productive work. It echoes classical ideals of the good life — the Stoic emphasis on what is within one's control, the Horatian praise of rural contentment — without any of the literary self-consciousness that marks the pastoral tradition. Corin does not know he is being philosophical; that is precisely what makes his philosophy genuine.

When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] asks "Has't any Philosophie in thee shepheard?" [[corin|Corin]]'s reply — a string of deliberately obvious truisms — is a masterful comic maneuver:

> No more, but that I know the more one sickens, the worse at ease he is: and that hee that wants money, meanes, and content, is without three good frends. That the propertie of raine is to wet, and fire to burne: That good pasture makes fat sheepe: and that a great cause of the night, is lacke of the Sunne. (012-014)

The truisms are a defense mechanism: by offering only what is indisputably true, Corin makes himself invulnerable to [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s satiric method. Wit operates by exposing pretension, and Corin has none to expose. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] can call him "shallow" and "raw," but he cannot land a blow.

## Relationship with Touchstone

[[corin|Corin]]'s encounter with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] in Act 3, Scene 2 is structured as a comic debate in which court [[wit]] meets pastoral simplicity — and loses. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] begins with a set-piece that anatomizes [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s life through elaborate antitheses: "in respect of it selfe, it is a good life; but in respect that it is a shepheards life, it is naught" (012-014). This is a bravura performance of rhetorical sophistication, but it collapses upon contact with Corin's plainness.

When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] declares that Corin is damned for never having been at court, Corin responds with devastating logic:

> Not a whit Touchstone, those that are good maners at the Court, are as ridiculous in the Countrey, as the behauiour of the Countrie is most mockeable at the Court. (012-023)

This is cultural relativism a century before the term existed. Corin argues that manners are context-dependent — that courtly courtesy (hand-kissing) would be "vncleanlie if Courtiers were shepheards" because "we are still handling our Ewes, and their Fels you know are greasie" (012-025). [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s attempts to refute this — claiming mutton grease is as wholesome as human sweat, that tar is baser than civet — grow increasingly strained. Corin finally terminates the contest:

> You haue too Courtly a wit, for me, Ile rest. (012-032)

The line is a tactical retreat that functions as a victory. By refusing to continue the game, Corin reveals that [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit]] requires a willing opponent — and that silence can be the most effective answer to sophistry.

In Act 5, Scene 1, Corin reappears as a messenger, summoning [[touchstone|Touchstone]] to "Our Master and Mistresse" (019-038). The relationship has settled into a functional working arrangement: the old shepherd and the court fool now serve the same household, their philosophical differences absorbed into the practical business of pastoral life.

## Key Scenes

### First Encounter: The Weary Travelers (Act 2, Scene 4)

Corin enters mid-conversation with [[silvius|Silvius]], already established as the voice of experience counseling the young lover: "That is the way to make her scorne you still" (007-016). When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] ask for help, [[corin|Corin]]'s response reveals both his decency and his limitations:

> Faire Sir, I pittie her, / And wish for her sake more then for mine owne, / My fortunes were more able to releeue her: / But I am shepheard to another man, / And do not sheere the Fleeces that I graze. (007-064)

His honesty about his subordinate position — combined with his willingness to help within his means — establishes him immediately as a figure of integrity.

### The Philosophical Shepherd (Act 3, Scene 2)

The extended debate with [[touchstone|Touchstone]], covering the merits of court vs. country, the nature of good manners, and the definition of philosophy. This is [[corin|Corin]]'s longest and richest scene, culminating in his credo of the "true labourer."

### The Messenger of Love (Act 3, Scene 4)

Corin reports [[silvius|Silvius]]'s love-suffering to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]]:

> If you will see a pageant truely plaid / Betweene the pale complexion of true Loue, / And the red glowe of scorne and prowd disdaine, / Goe hence a little, and I shall conduct you / If you will marke it. (014-036)

His language here is notably more elevated than in other scenes — he rises to the occasion, describing the [[silvius|Silvius]]-[[phebe|Phebe]] dynamic in almost theatrical terms. It is Corin the enabler, facilitating the romantic comedy.

### The Final Errand (Act 5, Scene 1)

Corin delivers the brief summons that draws [[touchstone|Touchstone]] away from bullying [[william|William]]: "Our Master and Mistresse seekes you: come away, away" (019-038). A small appearance that confirms his integration into the household economy of the final act.

## Relationships

- **[[touchstone|Touchstone]]** — Court [[wit]] meets pastoral simplicity; [[corin|Corin]]'s plainness withstands [[touchstone|the fool]]'s elaborate satire. Their debate in Act 3, Scene 2 is the play's most direct examination of the court-versus-country polarity.
- **[[silvius|Silvius]]** — Fellow shepherd and confidant. Corin listens to [[silvius|Silvius]]'s lovesick complaints with the patience of [[youth|age]], and later reports on his suffering to [[rosalind|Rosalind]], facilitating the romantic plot.
- **[[rosalind|Rosalind]]** — Corin sells his cottage to her (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) and becomes her faithful servant. She values his practical help and quiet good sense.
- **[[celia|Celia]]** — [[corin|Corin]]'s hospitality extends to both exiled ladies; she appreciates the refuge he helps provide.
- **[[phebe|Phebe]]** — The disdainful shepherdess whose cruelty to [[silvius|Silvius]] Corin witnesses and reports.
- **[[shepherd]]** — As a concept, Corin embodies the realistic [[shepherd|shepherd archetype]] in contrast to [[silvius|Silvius]]'s Petrarchan lover.
