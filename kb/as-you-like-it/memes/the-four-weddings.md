# The Four Weddings

## Summary

The four weddings in Act 5, Scene 4 constitute the comedic resolution of [[as-you-like-it|As You Like It]]. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] sheds her [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise, reveals herself to her father [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and [[orlando|Orlando]], and with the god [[hymen|Hymen]]'s blessing, four couples are united: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]], [[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]], [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]], and [[silvius|Silvius]] and [[phebe|Phebe]]. The ceremony restores not only the lovers but the political order: [[duke-fredericks-conversion|[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s conversion]] is announced by [[jaques|Jaques de Boys]], [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom is returned, and the fractures of the play's opening are healed. [[jaques|Jaques]] alone refuses the celebration, choosing continued [[freedom|exile]] and philosophical contemplation over the communal dance -- a reminder that comedy's happy endings are selective, not universal.

| [[celia|Celia]] + [[oliver|Oliver]] | [[love|Love]] at first sight | Swiftest union; redeems the fraternal villain |

## The Four Couples

### Rosalind and Orlando: The Tested Courtship

The union of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]] is the culmination of the play's central [[love]] plot, which began at the [[wrestling-match|wrestling match]] in Act 1 and unfolded through the long pastoral education of the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]. [[rosalind|Rosalind]], as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], has spent the play teaching [[orlando|Orlando]] to love a real woman rather than a Petrarchan ideal -- to see "[[rosalind|Rosalind]]" as a person with moods, demands, and complexities rather than as a distant goddess. In the final scene, she extracts his vow before [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]: "You say, if I bring in your Rosalinde, You wil bestow her on [[orlando|Orlando]] heere?" (022-008). His reply, "[t]hat would I, had I kingdoms to giue with hir" (022-010), confirms the generational and political dimensions of the union.

When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] finally sheds [[disguise-plan|her disguise]], her declaration is both simple and absolute:

> To you I giue my selfe, for I am yours. (022-069)

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s recognition -- "[i]f there be truth in sight, you are my [[rosalind|Rosalind]]" (022-072) -- closes the gap between appearance and reality that [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] created. Their marriage is the play's most earned union: a love that has been tested, mocked, educated, and deepened through sustained [[wit]] and mutual vulnerability.

### Celia and Oliver: Love at First Sight

The union of [[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]] is the swiftest and most compressed romance in the play. Where [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s courtship spans five acts, [[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]] fall in love in a single scene. [[celia|Celia]] describes the encounter to [[rosalind|Rosalind]]: "your brother and my sister no sooner met, but they look'd: no sooner look'd, but they lou'd". The compression is thematically pointed: [[oliver|Oliver]]'s transformation from fratricidal villain to loving bridegroom is the play's most dramatic instance of [[transformation|regeneration]] through the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]'s green-world magic.

The pairing completes the de Boys brothers' redemption. Where [[orlando|Orlando]] won [[rosalind|Rosalind]] through [[wrestling-match|courage]] and [[loyalty-and-service|constancy]], [[oliver|Oliver]] wins [[celia|Celia]] through the [[orlando|mercy of the brother]] he tried to kill. His love for [[celia|Celia]] is the visible sign of his conversion -- a [[transformation]] that parallels and reinforces the political conversion of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], also announced in this scene.

### Touchstone and Audrey: Pragmatic Desire

The union of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]] provides the play's anti-romantic counterpoint. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s motives are explicitly carnal: "as hornes are odious, they are necessarie" (013-031). He introduces himself to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] as one of the "Country copulatiues" who comes "to sweare, and to forsweare, according as mariage binds and blood breakes" (022-050). His description of [[audrey|Audrey]] -- "a poore virgin sir, an il-fauor'd thing sir, but mine owne" (022-050) -- is deliberately unromantic, exposing the economic and appetitive foundations that courtly [[love]] convention obscures.

Yet [[hymen|Hymen]] blesses this union alongside the others. The god of [[marriage]] makes no distinction between romantic idealism and pragmatic desire, between the courtly lovers and the "strange beasts" (022-040) that [[jaques|Jaques]] mocks. Shakespeare's comic inclusiveness grants blessing even to [[touchstone|the fool]]'s earthy accommodation -- suggesting that [[marriage]], in all its varieties, participates in the restorative pattern of comedy.

### Silvius and Phebe: Comic Correction

The union of [[silvius|Silvius]] and [[phebe|Phebe]] is the play's most explicitly comic resolution, achieved through [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s manipulation of [[phebe|Phebe]]'s rash promise. [[phebe|Phebe]], who has scorned [[silvius|Silvius]] throughout the play and fallen in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]] instead, is trapped by her own words. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] extracts the terms in public:

> Ros. You say, you'l marrie me, if I be willing
> Phe. That will I, should I die the houre after
> Ros. But if you do refuse to marrie me,
> You'l giue your selfe to this most faithfull Shepheard
> Phe. So is the bargaine (022-013 -- 022-017)

When [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is revealed as [[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[phebe|Phebe]]'s love for the "[[youth]]" becomes impossible. Her capitulation -- "[i]f sight & shape be true, why then my [[love|loue]] adieu" (022-073) -- is comic in its bewilderment but not cruel. Her final acceptance of [[silvius|Silvius]] -- "[i] wil not eate my word, now thou art mine, Thy faith, my fancie to thee doth combine" (022-102) -- completes the pastoral love plot with a note of genuine, if enforced, commitment.

## [[hymen|Hymen]]'s Role

[[hymen|Hymen]], the Roman god of [[marriage]], appears only in this scene, but his function is essential: he transforms [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s carefully orchestrated comic machinery into a sacred ceremony. He enters with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]], announced by still music, and his first words establish the cosmic frame:

> Then is there mirth in heauen,
> When earthly things made eauen
> attone together. (022-061 -- 022-063)

[[hymen|Hymen]]'s authority supersedes even [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s. Where she has managed the lovers' contracts through [[wit]] and disguise, [[hymen|Hymen]] imposes order through divine fiat: "Peace hoa: I barre confusion, / 'Tis I must make conclusion / Of these most strange euents" (022-077 -- 022-079). He counts the couples -- "Here's eight that must take hands, / To ioyne in Hymens bands" (022-080) -- and pairs them with the impersonal authority of ritual: "You and you, no crosse shall part; / You and you, are hart in hart" (022-083 -- 022-084).

His wedding song universalizes the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]'s celebration:

> Wedding is great Iunos crowne,
> O blessed bond of boord and bed:
> 'Tis Hymen peoples euerie towne,
> High wedlock then be honored (022-094 -- 022-097)

The presence of a god on stage is audacious even by Shakespearean standards. [[hymen|Hymen]]'s descent to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] links the pastoral comedy to mythic patterns of death and [[regeneration|rebirth]], linking the personal unions of the lovers to the cosmic order that comedy affirms.

## Comic Resolution Pattern

The four weddings follow a carefully structured pattern that parallels and resolves the play's three plot lines:

**[[duke-fredericks-court|The Court]] Plot (Political)**. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s usurpation of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom, established in Act 1, is reversed through conversion rather than battle. [[jaques|Jaques de Boys]] (the second brother of [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[oliver|Oliver]]) arrives to announce:

> After some question with him, was conuerted
> Both from his enterprize, and from the world (022-115 -- 022-116)

[[duke-frederick|Frederick]] bequeaths his crown to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and restores the lands of the exiled lords. The political order is healed not through revenge but through the same pattern of [[transformation]] and conversion that governs the love plots.

**The [[love|Love]] Plot (Romantic)**. All four couples are united simultaneously, from the central pairing of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]] to the comic pairing of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]]. The structure is cumulative and inclusive -- no couple is privileged above the others in [[hymen|Hymen]]'s blessing, and the range of [[love]] represented (tested, sudden, pragmatic, enforced) suggests that comic resolution embraces love in all its varieties.

**The Forest Plot ([[pastoralism|Pastoral]])**. The [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]'s green-world magic, which has enabled disguise, education, and [[transformation]], now yields to ceremony. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] acknowledges the transition: "First, in this Forrest, let vs do those ends / That heere were well begun, and wel begot" (022-125 -- 022-126). The [[green-world|forest]] has served its purpose; the [[persons|characters]] will return to court, enriched by what they learned in [[freedom|exile]].

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s refusal to join the celebration is structurally essential. He alone declines the communal dance, choosing instead to follow [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] into religious contemplation:

> out of these conuertites,
> There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd (022-139)

His departure reminds the audience that comedy's resolutions are partial. Not everyone is included, not every wound is healed, and the melancholy philosopher who has anatomized human folly throughout the play cannot himself be assimilated into the happy ending he has helped to define.

## Thematic Significance

The four weddings crystallize the play's central [[concepts|themes]]: the relationship between [[nature|Nature]] and [[fortune|Fortune]], the educative power of [[love]], the necessity of [[transformation]] for social [[regeneration|renewal]], and the inclusiveness of comic vision.

**[[love|Love]] as Education**. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s "cure" of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[love]] has been the play's central pedagogical project. The wedding is both the end of the lesson and its fulfillment: [[orlando|Orlando]] has learned to love a real woman, and his reward is the real [[rosalind|Rosalind]], no longer disguised.

**[[transformation|Transformation]] and [[regeneration|Regeneration]]**. Every major character undergoes [[transformation]] in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]: [[orlando|Orlando]] from dispossessed [[youth]] to worthy husband, [[oliver|Oliver]] from fratricide to bridegroom, [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] from tyrant to hermit, even [[touchstone|Touchstone]] from satiric observer to married participant. The weddings are the visible sign that these [[transformation|transformations]] are complete and recognized.

**Comic Inclusiveness**. The blessing of all four couples -- romantic, sudden, pragmatic, and enforced -- by the same god, in the same ceremony, asserts a vision of [[marriage]] that is radically inclusive. [[hymen|Hymen]] does not discriminate among the varieties of [[love]]; all are worthy of divine sanction. This inclusiveness extends to the political: [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s invitation that "euery of this happie number / That haue endur'd shrew'd daies, and nights with vs, / Shal share the good of our returned fortune" (022-127 -- 022-129) distributes the benefits of [[regeneration|restoration]] across the entire community.

**The Limits of Comedy**. [[jaques|Jaques]]'s departure and [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s earthy dismissal of romantic convention remind us that comedy's happy endings are always partial, always selective. The "dancing measures" (022-134) exclude as well as include, and the philosopher who sees too clearly cannot join the dance. Shakespeare gives [[jaques|Jaques]] the scene's most generous speech -- blessing each couple in turn (022-139) -- even as he exits the celebration. Comedy accommodates even the refusal of comedy.
