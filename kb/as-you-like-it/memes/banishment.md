## Definition

Banishment is the political mechanism that sets the entire plot of As You Like It in motion. Three distinct acts of [[exile|banishment]] or forced [[freedom|exile]] structure the play's movement: [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s usurpation and [[exile|banishment]] by his younger brother [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s summary [[exile|expulsion]] from the court on pain of death, and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s flight from [[oliver|Oliver]]'s murderous intentions. Unlike physical violence or execution, [[exile|banishment]] removes the victim from the political space without shedding blood — a gesture of ostensible mercy that masks tyranny, and a device that channels all the play's major [[persons|characters]] from the [[duke-fredericks-court|court]] into the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]].

## In As You Like It

The play opens with [[exile|banishment]] already accomplished. [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler reports that "the [[duke-senior|old Duke]] is banished by his yonger brother" (001-041), establishing the usurpation as the pre-history against which all subsequent action unfolds. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s [[exile|banishment]] creates the alternative society in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — a community of loyal followers who "fleet the time carelesly as they did in the [[green-world|golden world]]" (001-043).

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]] is the play's most dramatically immediate act of [[exile|expulsion]]. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] summons her and delivers the decree without appeal:

> Mistris, dispatch you with your safest haste,
> And get you from our Court
>
> If thou out-stay the time, vpon mine honor,
> And in the greatnes of my word, you die. (003-076)

When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] protests that she is no traitor, [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s reply reveals [[exile|banishment]]'s true nature as an instrument of absolute power: "The reason is, I haue past vpon her, she is banish'd" (003-071). [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] needs no justification — the sovereign's word is law. [[celia|Celia]]'s plea that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is her "cosin" and companion since infancy is met with cold finality: the Duke "hath banish'd me his daughter" (003-084), as [[celia|Celia]] frames her own solidarity with [[rosalind|Rosalind]].

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] transforms the meaning of [[exile|banishment]] through language, reframing her punishment as liberation: "To libertie, and not to [[exile|banishment]]" (003-129). This rhetorical move — converting political suffering into pastoral [[freedom]] — is one of the play's essential gestures, and it enables the entire [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] comedy to unfold as a space of possibility rather than punishment.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s experience of [[exile|banishment]] is less formal but no less real. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s plot against his life forces [[orlando|Orlando]] into flight with [[adam|Adam]], and [[orlando|Orlando]] explicitly frames this as [[exile|banishment]]: "thy brother hath banish'd you" (004-031), as [[adam|Adam]] warns. Unlike [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], [[orlando|Orlando]] is banished not by decree but by fraternal hatred — a domestic tyranny that mirrors the political one.

The play's resolution reverses all its banishments. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s sudden conversion at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge — "His crowne bequeathing to his banish'd Brother" (022-117) — restores the political order, while the quadruple wedding reunites all the exiles in a community that transcends both [[court-vs-forest|court and forest]].
