# Timeline of Events

*As You Like It* unfolds across five acts that trace a complete dramatic arc: from oppression at court, through [[freedom|exile]] and transformation in the pastoral [[forest-of-arden|green world]], to resolution and [[regeneration|restoration]]. This timeline traces the chronological sequence of major events, situating each within the play's broader narrative architecture.

---

## Act 1 -- The Court (Setup)

The play opens in the corrupt world of the court, where two parallel tyrannies -- [[oliver|Oliver]]'s domestic cruelty and [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s political usurpation -- drive the protagonists toward [[freedom|exile]].

### [Act 1, Scene 1] — [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Grievance Against [[oliver|Oliver]]

The play begins with [[orlando|Orlando]] confiding his desperation to [[adam|Adam]], the family's aged servant. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s elder brother [[oliver|Oliver]], charged by their late father Sir Rowland de Boys to "breed mee well," has instead kept [[orlando|Orlando]] "rustically at home [...] vnkept" (001-002), denying him the education and gentlemanly training that befit his birth. [[orlando|Orlando]] confronts [[oliver|Oliver]] directly, seizing him by the throat and demanding either proper treatment or his inheritance of "a thousand Crownes" (001-002).

[[oliver|Oliver]] pretends compliance but secretly plots his brother's destruction. Summoning [[charles|Charles]], [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s champion wrestler, [[oliver|Oliver]] falsely portrays [[orlando|Orlando]] as "a secret & villanous contriuer against mee his naturall brother" (001-047) and encourages [[charles|Charles]] to break his neck in the next day's match rather than merely his finger.

In the same scene, [[charles|Charles]] delivers crucial exposition: the "[[duke-senior|old Duke]]" ([[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]) has been banished by his younger brother ([[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]) and now lives "like the old Robin Hood of England" (001-043) in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], where "many yong Gentlemen flocke to him euery day, and fleet the time carelesly as they did in the [[green-world|golden world]]" (001-043). This passage establishes the [[green-world|golden world]] / court antithesis that structures the entire play.

### [Act 1, Scene 2] — The Wrestling Match and the Birth of Love

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] enter in conversation, their banter revealing the depth of their friendship and their shared situation as daughters of warring brothers. The entrance of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] the fool introduces the play's [[wit|comic intelligence]]. When the courtier [[le-beau|Le Beau]] arrives with news of [[charles|Charles]]'s wrestling victories -- in which he "broke three of his ribbes, that there is little hope of life in him" (002-065) -- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] decide to watch the spectacle.

The [[wrestling-match|wrestling match]] is the play's inciting event. Despite [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]]'s pleas -- and [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s own attempt to dissuade him -- [[orlando|Orlando]] insists on fighting: "in it I haue nothing" (002-095). He defeats [[charles|Charles]] decisively, knocking the champion unconscious.

Three consequences ripple outward from this single action:

1. **[[love|Love]]**: [[rosalind|Rosalind]], moved by [[orlando|Orlando]]'s courage, gives him her chain: "Weare this for me: one out of suites with fortune" (002-147). [[orlando|Orlando]], struck speechless, finds himself "ouerthrowne" (002-161) by love even as he has overthrown [[charles|Charles]].

2. **Political enmity**: When [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] learns that [[orlando|Orlando]] is the son of Sir Rowland de Boys -- his former enemy -- he turns hostile: "I would thou hadst beene son to some man else" (002-122). Le Beau warns [[orlando|Orlando]] that [[duke-frederick|the Duke]] "is humorous" (002-171) and that his malice "Will sodainly breake forth" (002-188).

3. **Fratricidal rage**: [[oliver|Oliver]]'s hatred intensifies from neglect to murderous intent, preparing the ground for the crisis of Act 2.

### [Act 1, Scene 3] — [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Banishment and the Disguise Plan

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s tyranny now consumes his own household. He banishes [[rosalind|Rosalind]] on pain of death, citing her popularity with the people as his reason: "Her verie silence, and her patience, Speake to the people, and they pittie her" (003-065). His decree is absolute: within ten days, if she is found within twenty miles of court, "Thou diest for it" (003-030).

[[celia|Celia]], in her most defining moment, protests her father's judgment and declares she will share [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[freedom|exile]]: "I cannot liue out of her companie" (003-073). She proposes they flee to the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] to seek [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]. [[rosalind|Rosalind]], being "more then common tall" (003-106), devises the [[disguise|disguise]] plan: she will dress as a young man named [[ganimed|Ganymede]] -- "Ioues owne Page" (003-116) -- and [[celia|Celia]] as [[aliena|Aliena]], a name that references her estranged state. They recruit [[touchstone|Touchstone]] the fool to accompany them as "a comfort to our trauaile" (003-122).

The scene closes with the two women preparing their escape "To libertie, and not to [[exile|banishment]]" (003-129), transforming [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s punishment into a freely chosen journey.

## Act 2 -- Into Arden (Rising Action)

The action shifts to the pastoral world as all three persecuted parties -- [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s exiled court, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]], and [[orlando|Orlando]] with [[adam|Adam]] -- converge on the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]].

### [Act 2, Scene 1] — [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s Exiled Court

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and his loyal lords, living as foresters in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], articulate the play's pastoral philosophy. In his celebrated opening speech, [[duke-senior|the Duke]] finds "tongues in trees, bookes in the running brookes, Sermons in stones, and good in euery thing" (004-018), praising "the uses of aduersitie" (004-014) over the "painted pompe" (004-005) of courtly life.

The scene introduces [[jaques|Jaques]], the play's melancholic philosopher, through the report of a lord who observed him moralizing over a wounded stag. The deer's suffering becomes, in [[jaques|Jaques]]'s interpretation, an emblem of human ingratitude: weeping into a stream that needs no more water, abandoned by his "veluet friend[s]" (004-054), ignored by a "carelesse Heard" (004-056) of well-fed companions. [[jaques|Jaques]] extends his critique from the deer to "the body of Countrie, Citie, Court" (004-063), establishing himself as the play's satiric conscience.

### [Act 2, Scene 3] — Adam Warns Orlando

The court world's corruption reaches its climax. [[adam|Adam]], the eighty-year-old servant, intercepts [[orlando|Orlando]] before he enters the house, revealing [[oliver|Oliver]]'s plot: "this night he meanes, To burne the lodging where you vse to lye, And you within it" (006-024). [[adam|Adam]]'s warning carries theological weight: [[orlando|Orlando]]'s very virtues -- his "gentle[ness], strong, and valiant" (006-007) nature -- have become "sanctified and holy traitors" (006-014) to him in a world where excellence provokes envy.

When [[orlando|Orlando]] hesitates, refusing to become a highwayman -- "What, would'st thou haue me go & beg my food, Or with a base and boistrous Sword enforce A theeuish liuing on the common rode?" (006-033) -- [[adam|Adam]] offers his life savings of "fiue hundred Crownes" (006-040), the money he saved across a lifetime of thrifty service under Sir Rowland, and pledges his own aged body to the journey: "let me be your seruant" (006-048). [[orlando|Orlando]]'s response -- "Oh good old man, how well in thee appeares The constant [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]] of the antique world, When [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]] sweate for dutie, not for meede" (006-058) -- frames [[adam|Adam]]'s sacrifice as the moral standard against which the play measures all other human relationships.

### [Act 2, Scene 6] — [[adam|Adam]]'s Collapse in Arden

The journey's cost is made visible. Deep in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], [[adam|Adam]] collapses from hunger: "Deere Master, I can go no further: O I die for food. Heere lie I downe, And measure out my graue" (009-003). The image of the old man lying down to die literalizes the mortal price of loyalty.

[[orlando|Orlando]] carries [[adam|Adam]] on his back to find shelter, promising that "If this vncouth Forrest yeeld any thing sauage, I wil either be food for it, or bring it for foode to thee" (009-007). This moment establishes the pattern of protective care that will later govern [[orlando|Orlando]]'s rescue of [[oliver|Oliver]] -- and it prepares the moral ground for his desperate intrusion into [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s banquet.

### [Act 2, Scene 7] — [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Arrival at [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s Camp

Starving and desperate, [[orlando|Orlando]] bursts into [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s encampment with drawn sword, commanding the nobles to "Forbeare, and eate no more" (010-092). [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s response -- "Your gentlenesse shall force, more then your force Moue vs to gentlenesse" (010-108) -- transforms what could have been a violent confrontation into a scene of mutual recognition. When [[orlando|Orlando]] explains he must first fetch "an old poore man, Who after me, hath many a weary steppe Limpt in pure [[love|loue]]" (010-135), the Duke orders him to go and "we will nothing waste till you returne" (010-141).

While [[orlando|Orlando]] retrieves [[adam|Adam]], [[jaques|Jaques]] delivers the play's most famous speech: "All the world's a stage, And all the men and women, meerely Players" (010-147). His [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] of man -- from "the Infant, Mewling, and puking" (010-152) to "second childishnesse, and meere obliuion, Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing" (010-173) -- frames the human life cycle as a relentless decline into nothingness. Yet [[adam|Adam]], who enters immediately after this speech, presents a living counter-argument: at "almost fourescore" (006-073), he has lost his teeth "in your [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]]" (001-029) but is anything but oblivion. He is alert, courageous, and now received with courtly tenderness by the Duke: "Welcome: set downe your venerable burthen, and let him feede" (010-176).

In the scene's final movement, [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] recognizes [[orlando|Orlando]] as the son of his beloved Sir Rowland, welcomes him to the exiled community, and the community celebrates with [[amiens|Amiens]]' song "Blow, blow, thou winter winde" (010-185) -- a song whose refrain reminds the company that "Most frendship, is fayning; most Louing, meere folly" (010-190), yet whose communal performance enacts the very loyalty it questions.

## Act 3 -- Complications (Climax)

The pastoral comedy unfolds as love, [[wit]], and mistaken identities generate the play's richest complications. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s male [[disguise|disguise]] as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] becomes the engine of the central courtship plot.

### [Act 3, Scene 2] — [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Love Poems and the Meeting with Ganymede

[[orlando|Orlando]] enters the [[forest-of-arden-place|forest]] scattering the first of his [[love-poetry|love poems]], hanging them on trees: "Hang there my verse, in witnesse of my [[love|loue]]" (012-002). His verses -- "From the east to westerne Inde, no iewel is like Rosalinde" (012-039) -- are sincerely felt but metrically clumsy, and both [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[touchstone|Touchstone]] mock them mercilessly. [[celia|Celia]], having spotted [[orlando|Orlando]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], teases [[rosalind|Rosalind]] with a protracted reveal before confirming that the poet is indeed "yong [[orlando|Orlando]], that tript vp the Wrastlers heeles, and your heart, both in an instant" (012-079).

The scene's pivotal encounter occurs when [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) approaches [[orlando|Orlando]]. She challenges his lovesick condition, offering to "cure" him of love through a program of role-play: she will impersonate [[rosalind|Rosalind]] while [[orlando|Orlando]] woos her daily. This arrangement -- "I would cure you, if you would but call me [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and come euerie day to my Coat, and woe me" (012-182) -- creates the play's central dramatic irony: the woman [[orlando|Orlando]] loves is standing before him, teaching him how to love her, while pretending to be a boy pretending to be a woman. The love-cure plot will drive the action through Act 4.

Meanwhile, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[silvius|the shepherd]] [[corin|Corin]] debate the relative merits of court and country life, with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] exposing the contradictions in his own snobbery while [[corin|Corin]] articulates a quiet dignity rooted in honest labor: "I am a true Labourer, I earne that I eate: get that I weare; owe no man hate, enuie no mans happinesse" (012-034).

### [Act 3, Scene 3] — Touchstone Courts Audrey

[[touchstone|Touchstone]], the court fool transplanted to the pastoral world, transfers his affections to [[audrey|Audrey]], a simple country wench. Their courtship is a comic parody of the romantic idealism that governs [[orlando|Orlando]]'s wooing: where [[orlando|Orlando]] litters trees with verse, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] negotiates marriage in blunt prose. His logic is characteristic: "As the Oxe hath his bow sir, the horse his curb, and the Falcon her bels, so man hath his desires, and as Pigeons bill, so wedlocke would be nibling" (013-038).

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] attempts to marry [[audrey|Audrey]] under a tree with Sir [[oliver|Oliver]] Martext, a hedge-priest of dubious qualifications. [[jaques|Jaques]], observing the scene, intervenes: "And wil you (being a man of your breeding) be married vnder a bush like a begger? Get you to church, and haue a good Priest" (013-040). [[touchstone|Touchstone]] reluctantly agrees, postponing the wedding -- a delay that will ultimately elevate the marriage from rustic farce to participation in the play's climactic quadruple wedding.

### [Act 3, Scene 5] — Silvius, Phebe, and the Pastoral Love Triangle

The pastoral love plot intensifies. [[silvius|Silvius]], a young shepherd, pines desperately for [[phebe|Phebe]], a shepherdess who scorns him with calculated cruelty. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]), witnessing [[phebe|Phebe]]'s mistreatment of [[silvius|Silvius]], delivers a blistering rebuke: "what though you haue no beauty ... Must you be therefore prowd and pittilesse?" (015-039). She urges [[phebe|Phebe]] to recognize her good fortune: "Sell when you can, you are not for all markets" (015-064).

[[phebe|Phebe]]'s response is the play's most ironic twist: she falls in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]] -- that is, with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] in male disguise. Her confession -- "Dead Shepheard, now I find thy saw of might, Who euer lov'd, that lou'd not at first sight?" (015-085) -- quotes Christopher Marlowe's *Hero and Leander*, linking the pastoral subplot to the wider literary tradition while creating a tangle that only [[rosalind|Rosalind]] can unravel. [[silvius|Silvius]], ever faithful, agrees to carry [[phebe|Phebe]]'s love letter to [[ganimed|Ganymede]], becoming the unwitting messenger of his beloved's infatuation with another.

## Act 4 -- Healing (Falling Action)

The fourth act turns from complication toward resolution. The play's most significant conversion -- [[oliver|Oliver]]'s transformation from fratricidal villain to penitent brother -- provides the moral climax.

### [Act 4, Scene 3] — [[oliver|Oliver]]'s Conversion and [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Faint

The scene opens with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] awaiting [[orlando|Orlando]]'s return for another love-cure session. [[silvius|Silvius]] arrives instead, bearing [[phebe|Phebe]]'s love letter to [[ganimed|Ganymede]]. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reads the letter aloud -- a declaration that "Whiles you chid me, I did [[love|loue]]" (018-060) -- and dismisses the love-struck [[silvius|Silvius]] with instructions to deliver her own message: "if she [[love|loue]] me, I charge her to [[love|loue]] thee" (018-072).

[[oliver|Oliver]], transformed and unrecognizable, enters seeking [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s cottage. He introduces himself as [[orlando|Orlando]]'s brother and delivers to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] a "bloudy napkin" (018-093) -- a handkerchief stained with [[orlando|Orlando]]'s blood. Pressed to explain, [[oliver|Oliver]] recounts the extraordinary events:

Sent by [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] to hunt down [[orlando|Orlando]], [[oliver|Oliver]] had fallen asleep under an old oak in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]. [[orlando|Orlando]], passing by on his way to meet [[rosalind|Rosalind]], discovered the sleeping man with "A greene and guilded snake" (018-109) coiled about his neck and a ravenous lioness crouching nearby. [[orlando|Orlando]] recognized his brother yet "Twice did he turne his backe, and purpos'd so" (018-129) to leave him to his fate. But "kindnesse, nobler euer then reuenge, And [[nature|Nature]] stronger then his iust occasion, Made him giue battell to the Lyonnesse" (018-130). [[orlando|Orlando]] killed the lioness but suffered a wound to his arm in the fight.

The rescue transforms [[oliver|Oliver]] entirely. His confession -- "'Twas I: but 'tis not I: I doe not shame To tell you what I was, since my conuersion So sweetly tastes, being the thing I am" (018-138) -- marks his [[regeneration|regeneration]] from murderer to brother. The brothers reconcile in tears, and [[orlando|Orlando]] leads [[oliver|Oliver]] to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], who provides "fresh aray, and entertainment" (018-147).

Hearing the account -- and seeing [[orlando|Orlando]]'s blood -- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) faints dead away. Her collapse is the closest [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] comes to breaking; she recovers and claims it was merely "counterfeit" (018-176), but [[oliver|Oliver]] observes the truth: "there is too great testimony in your complexion, that it was a passion of earnest" (018-174). The moment reveals the depth of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s love even as it strains the [[disguise|disguise]] to its limit.

In the scene's aftermath, [[oliver|Oliver]] and [[celia|Celia]] (as [[aliena|Aliena]]) fall in love at first sight, providing the fourth couple for the play's climactic resolution.

## Act 5 -- Resolution (Denouement)

The fifth act gathers all threads toward the quadruple wedding, the political [[regeneration|restoration]], and the play's complex leave-taking.

### [Act 5, Scene 1] — Touchstone Dismisses William

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] resolves the question of his courtship by disposing of his rustic rival. [[william|William]], a country [[youth]] with a prior claim on [[audrey|Audrey]], appears and is subjected to [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s finest comic intimidation. After establishing that [[william|William]] is neither learned nor possessed of the rhetorical "figure in Rhetoricke" (019-032) that [[touchstone|Touchstone]] himself deploys, the fool delivers his ultimatum: "I will kill thee a hundred and fifty wayes" (019-033). [[william|William]], sensibly, withdraws. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]] proceed toward the climactic gathering.

### [Act 5, Scene 4] — The Four Weddings, Conversion, and Restoration

The play's final scene gathers the entire company. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) extracts promises from all parties: [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] will give his daughter to [[orlando|Orlando]]; [[orlando|Orlando]] will wed [[rosalind|Rosalind]]; [[phebe|Phebe]] will marry [[ganimed|Ganymede]] or, if refusing, wed [[silvius|Silvius]]; and [[silvius|Silvius]] will take [[phebe|Phebe]] if she is refused. Having bound them all, she exits with [[celia|Celia]].

She returns not as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] but as herself, accompanied by [[hymen|Hymen]], the god of marriage. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reveals her identity to her father and to [[orlando|Orlando]]: "To you I giue my selfe, for I am yours" (022-069). [[hymen|Hymen]] presides over the quadruple union:

| Couple | Type of [[love|Love]] | Significance |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| [[rosalind|Rosalind]] + [[orlando|Orlando]] | Tested romantic love | Central courtship; love "cured" through [[wit]] |
| [[celia|Celia]] + [[oliver|Oliver]] | [[love|Love]] at first sight | Swiftest union; redeems the villain |
| [[touchstone|Touchstone]] + [[audrey|Audrey]] | Pragmatic desire | Parodies romantic idealism; grounds love in the body |
| [[silvius|Silvius]] + [[phebe|Phebe]] | Comic correction | [[phebe|Phebe]]'s rash promise to [[ganimed|Ganymede]] enforced |

[[hymen|Hymen]]'s blessing -- "[[marriage|Wedding]] is great Iunos crowne, O blessed bond of boord and bed" (022-094) -- elevates the weddings from social ritual to cosmic event, linking the pastoral comedy to the mythic pattern of death and [[regeneration|rebirth]].

Immediately after the weddings, [[jaques|Jaques]] de Boys -- the second son of Sir Rowland, absent throughout the play -- arrives with extraordinary news. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], marching "a mightie power" (022-110) toward [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] to capture and kill [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], was stopped at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge by "an old Religious man" (022-114). After conversation with this hermit, [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] "was conuerted both from his enterprize, and from the world" (022-115). He renounces his usurped dukedom, bequeaths the crown to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], restores the lands of all exiled lords, and withdraws to a religious life.

The conversion resolves the play's political conflict not by battle but by spiritual transformation -- a *deus ex machina* that affirms [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s regenerative power over even the tyrannical will. The [[forest-of-arden|green world]] that sheltered exiles now actively converts the tyrant.

[[jaques|Jaques]], the melancholic philosopher, recognizes the significance immediately. He declines to join the communal celebration and instead announces his intention to follow [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]: "Out of these conuertites, There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd" (022-139). His departure -- the only character who refuses the comic resolution -- is a reminder that comedy's happy endings are selective, not universal. Some souls are drawn not to marriage feasts but to monastic contemplation.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] invites the company to "fall into our Rusticke Reuelrie" (022-132) and then return to court, where "euery of this happie number ... Shal share the good of our returned fortune" (022-129). The movement from court to [[green-world|forest]] and back again is complete -- but the return is transformed, the restored court enriched by the pastoral wisdom gained in [[freedom|exile]].

## Dramatic Architecture

The five-act structure traces a symmetrical arc:

| Act | Phase | Key Events |
|-----|-------|------------|
| **Act 1** | Setup (Court) | [[oliver|Oliver]]'s tyranny, [[wrestling-match|Wrestling Match]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]], [[disguise-plan|Disguise Plan]] |
| **Act 2** | Rising Action (Journey) | [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s pastoral court, [[adam|Adam]] warns [[orlando|Orlando]], flight to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], welcome at the Duke's table |
| **Act 3** | Climax (Complications) | [[love|Love]] poems, [[ganimed|Ganymede]] meets [[orlando|Orlando]], [[touchstone-and-audrey|Touchstone courts Audrey]], [[phebe|Phebe]] falls for [[ganimed|Ganymede]] |
| **Act 4** | Falling Action (Healing) | [[olivers-conversion|[[oliver|Oliver]]'s Conversion]] -- saved from lion, fraternal reconciliation, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] faints |
| **Act 5** | Denouement (Resolution) | [[william|William]] dismissed, [[the-four-weddings|Four Weddings]] with [[hymen|Hymen]], [[duke-fredericks-conversion|[[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s Conversion]], [[jaques|Jaques]]'s departure |

The play's central structural principle is the antithesis between court and [[forest-of-arden|forest]], oppression and [[freedom]], artifice and [[nature|Nature]]. The events of the timeline trace a double movement: [[exile|expulsion]] from the corrupt court into the regenerative [[green-world|green world]], and eventual return -- transformed -- to a restored political order. What begins with [[oliver|Oliver]]'s abuse of [[orlando|Orlando]] (Act 1, Scene 1) and ends with [[hymen|Hymen]]'s blessing of the four weddings (Act 5, Scene 4) is a journey from discord to harmony, sustained by the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]'s mysterious power to heal what the court has broken.
