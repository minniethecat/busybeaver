## Definition

Rites and ceremonies in *As You Like It* designate the formal, public, and often sacred actions through which personal commitments — marriages, reconciliations, returns from [[freedom|exile]] — are ratified and rendered binding upon the community. The play distinguishes carefully between the private contract of love and the public ceremony of [[marriage]], between the solitary vow and the witnessed rite. This distinction culminates in Act 5's extraordinary wedding masque, in which the god [[hymen|Hymen]] himself appears to solemnize four marriages, converting the improvised courtships of [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] into a social order that can survive the return to court.

## In As You Like It

The distinction between private contract and public ceremony is established early. [[touchstone|Touchstone]], discoursing on marriage to [[audrey|Audrey]] with characteristic legalism, observes that a marriage "is not lawfull" without being "giuen" (013-033) — that is, solemnized before witnesses. He elaborates that "a Priest" must tell "what marriage is," distinguishing the officiant's declarative role from the mere contractual "ioyne" (013-040). Even the fool understands that a rite transforms a private agreement into a publicly acknowledged bond.

The language of ceremony accrues throughout the play. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] speaks of "the contract of her marriage, and the day it is solemnizd" (012-150), marking the temporal gap between betrothal and ritual completion. The metaphor is extended socially: [[celia|Celia]] describes her bond with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] in ceremonial terms as a "wedlocke" (013-039), suggesting that ceremonies of commitment are not limited to romantic love but structure all significant human relationships.

The play's ritual architecture reaches its apex in Act 5 Scene 4 with the entrance of [[hymen|Hymen]], the god of marriage. His appearance is abrupt and hieratic: "Enter [[hymen|Hymen]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and [[celia|Celia]]" (022-059), accompanied by music. [[hymen|Hymen]] speaks in a register entirely distinct from the play's prevailing comic prose and pastoral verse — his language is incantatory, oracular:

> Hymen from Heauen brought her,
> Yea brought her hither (022-065)

The god's presence transforms the scene from private resolution into public rite. He presides over not one but four marriages — [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]], [[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]], [[phebe|Phebe]] and [[silvius|Silvius]], [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]] — uniting the play's disparate erotic plots into a single ceremonial event. His closing benediction — "High [[marriage|wedlock]] then be honored" (022-097) — and the repeated invocations "To [[hymen|Hymen]], God of euerie Towne" (022-099) and "'Tis [[hymen|Hymen]] peoples euerie towne" (022-096) frame marriage not merely as a private choice but as a civilizational act, the mechanism by which communities are "peopled" and perpetuated.

[[hymen|Hymen]]'s theophany is also a theatrical event. The god's entrance realizes what [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] has been gesturing toward all along: the transformation of lived experience into ritual form, of chance encounters into ordained unions. The play understands ceremony not as empty formality but as the necessary translation of private feeling into public meaning — the rite that makes the contract real.
