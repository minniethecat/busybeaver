## Definition

The fool in Shakespearean drama is a licensed truth-teller: a figure permitted to mock, critique, and expose folly without reprisal. [[touchstone|The fool]]'s [[wit]] functions as a mirror held up to society, revealing contradictions that more "serious" [[persons|characters]] cannot or will not acknowledge. In *As You Like It*, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] embodies this archetype, though his role departs from the "wise fool" model of later plays like *King Lear* or *Twelfth Night* — [[touchstone|Touchstone]] is more satirist than sage, more court [[wit]] than holy fool.

## In As You Like It

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] enters the play as a court fool who follows [[celia|Celia]] and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] into [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], bringing courtly [[wit]] into the pastoral setting. His presence creates a critical perspective on both worlds: he punctures pastoral idealism with urban cynicism, yet his own court-bred cleverness is repeatedly shown to be hollow. The play uses "fool" and "foolery" language (15 occurrences) to probe the boundary between wisdom and folly — a theme made explicit when [[touchstone|Touchstone]] declares that "the more pittie that fooles may not speak wisely, what wise men do foolishly" (002-038).

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s most extended fool-performance is his dissection of the seven degrees of the lie in Act 5 — a virtuoso display of [[wit]] that simultaneously demonstrates and critiques [[touchstone|the fool]]'s art:

> "O sir, we quarrel in print, by the booke: as you have bookes for good manners: I will name you the degrees. The first, the Retort courteous: the second, the Quip-modest..." (022-057)

The set-piece is both brilliant and absurd, showing how [[touchstone|the fool]]'s verbal facility can become an end in itself. [[rosalind|Rosalind]], herself the play's sharpest [[wit]], recognizes this limitation: "Peace foole, he's not thy kinsman" (007-053) — she deploys the label "fool" to draw the line between productive [[wit]] and empty display.

## Key Passages

### Touchstone Defends Fool's Speech

> "The more pittie that fooles may not speak wisely, what wise men do foolishly." (002-038)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s self-defense establishes [[touchstone|the fool]]'s license: the fool's apparent folly exposes the real folly of those who consider themselves wise.

### Jaques on Touchstone

> "Heere shall he see, grosse fooles as he, / And if he will, as you may see." (008-035)

[[jaques|Jaques]], who envies [[touchstone|the fool]]'s [[freedom]], reveals how the fool archetype becomes aspirational in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — [[jaques|the melancholy Jaques]] wishes he could wear motley and speak truth to power.

### [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Rebuke

> "Peace foole, he's not thy kinsman." (007-053)

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] silences [[touchstone|Touchstone]] when his [[wit]] turns merely obstructive. This moment illustrates [[touchstone|the fool]]'s limitation: [[wit]] without purpose becomes noise. The play's wisest character polices the boundary.

### Touchstone on Foolish Runaways

> "now am I in Arden, the more foole I, when I was at home I was in a better place." (007-010)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s self-designation as "fool" for leaving court life inverts the pastoral ideal: the fool sees through the romanticization of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]].

### [[orlando|Orlando]]'s "Fool" Insult

> "Thou art a foole, she robs thee of thy name." (003-067)

Here "fool" is used as a blunt insult by [[orlando|Orlando]] against [[jaques|Jaques]], showing the term's double life in the play — it is both a professional identity ([[touchstone|Touchstone]]) and a general term of abuse for anyone acting against reason, particularly in matters of love.

## Related Characters

- **[[touchstone]]** — The play's named fool, whose professional role is to jest and critique. His journey from court to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] tests [[touchstone|the fool]]'s art against pastoral simplicity.
- **[[rosalind]]** — Though not a fool, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is the play's most effective satirist. She outperforms [[touchstone|Touchstone]] at his own game, using [[wit]] for genuine discovery rather than mere display.
- **[[jaques]]** — The melancholy [[jaques|Jaques]] envies [[touchstone|the fool]]'s license: he wishes he could "cleanse the foul body of the infected world" through satiric speech, yet the play treats his ambition as itself a form of folly.
- **[[celia]]** — [[celia|Celia]]'s exasperated "The more pittie that fooles may not speak wisely" (002-038) voices the audience's mixed feelings about [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s verbal excess.
