# Love

## Definition

Romantic love in As You Like It is treated as a form of delightful folly — a "madness" that afflicts nearly every major character and drives the play's action from court to [[green-world|forest]]. Shakespeare presents love through multiple couples ([[rosalind|Rosalind]]-[[orlando|Orlando]], [[silvius|Silvius]]-[[phebe|Phebe]], [[touchstone|Touchstone]]-[[audrey|Audrey]], [[oliver|Oliver]]-[[celia|Celia]]), each representing a different attitude toward romantic passion: idealized [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]], Petrarchan posturing, rustic pragmatism, and sudden conversion. The [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] serves as the transformative space where love's "cure" is administered and its follies resolved. No other Shakespearean comedy presents love with such comprehensive variety — the four couples constitute a virtual taxonomy of romantic possibility.

## Love's Four Couples: A Taxonomy

### Rosalind and Orlando: The Educative Love

The central couple models a love that is simultaneously passionate and intelligent. [[orlando|Orlando]] begins in the Petrarchan mode — hanging poems on trees, sighing, unable to speak — but [[rosalind|Rosalind]], through her [[disguise-plan|disguise]] as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], educates him out of it. Her "cure" is not a cure for love but a cure for idealized love: she teaches [[orlando|Orlando]] that women are not Petrarchan goddesses but mortal, changeable, and full of contradictions (012-180). The education works because [[rosalind|Rosalind]] herself is as deep in love as [[orlando|Orlando]] — "my affection hath an vnknowne bottome, like the Bay of Portugall" (016-091) — so her teaching comes from experience, not detachment. Their love represents Shakespeare's ideal: passion tempered by intelligence, romance grounded in reality.

### Silvius and Phebe: The Petrarchan Parody

[[silvius|Silvius]] and [[phebe|Phebe]] enact the Petrarchan love script in its purest form: the devoted shepherd-lover, the disdainful beloved, and the endless suffering of unrequited passion. [[silvius|Silvius]]'s credo — "Thou hast not lou'd. O [[phebe|Phebe]], [[phebe|Phebe]], [[phebe|Phebe]]" (007-020) — is simultaneously ridiculous and touching. Shakespeare's treatment is characteristically double: he parodies the Petrarchan convention while acknowledging the genuine pain it encodes. [[phebe|Phebe]]'s sudden conversion to love of [[ganimed|Ganymede]] — who is [[rosalind|Rosalind]] in disguise — adds a layer of comic gender confusion. When [[phebe|Phebe]] declares "Dead Shepheard, now I find thy saw of might, Who euer lov'd, that lou'd not at first sight?" (015-085), she quotes Marlowe's "Hero and Leander" while loving a woman she believes to be a boy. The Petrarchan position is both exposed as artificial and granted its moment of truth.

### Touchstone and Audrey: The Anti-Romantic Union

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]] represent love stripped of all idealization. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s reasons for marrying are pragmatic, even cynical: "As hornes are odious, they are necessarie" (013-031). There is no [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] in their courtship, no sighing, no trees hung with verses — only [[touchstone|the fool]]'s practical accommodation of desire. Yet Shakespeare grants this union the same blessing he gives the romantic couples. [[hymen|Hymen]] presides over [[touchstone-and-audrey|Touchstone's marriage]] as surely as over [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s. The implication is radical: romantic love is not the only valid basis for marriage; pragmatic coupling, free of illusion, has its own legitimacy.

### Oliver and Celia: The Conversion Love

[[oliver|Oliver]] and [[celia|Celia]]'s love-at-first-sight conversion is the play's most compressed romantic plot. [[oliver|Oliver]], the villainous brother who tried to have [[orlando|Orlando]] killed, is transformed by love into a figure of generosity: he gives his estate to [[orlando|Orlando]] and marries [[celia|Celia]] within moments of meeting her. [[celia|Celia]], who earlier teased [[rosalind|Rosalind]] about love's folly, falls with equal suddenness: her reply to [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s confession — "Or rather bottomlesse, that as fast as you poure affection in, it runs out" (016-092) — is remembered as [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[wit]], but it is [[celia|Celia]] who speaks it, already half-smitten herself. Their love represents love's irrational power to transform character — a power the play celebrates even as it renders its operation mysterious. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s conversion, like [[duke-fredericks-conversion|[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s]], occurs offstage, as if to emphasize that such transformations cannot be explained, only reported.

## Love as Madness, Love as Cure

The play's governing metaphor for love is sickness. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] describes love as "a madnesse" and claims to have cured a lover by her "counsel" (012-180). Her "cure" involves role-playing: she has [[orlando|Orlando]] woo her as if she were [[rosalind|Rosalind]], forcing him to confront the reality of the woman rather than the Petrarchan ideal. The cure succeeds not by eliminating love but by transforming it — from a love of fantasy to a love of reality, from Petrarchan worship to mutual recognition.

The love-as-sickness metaphor has deep roots in Renaissance medical theory, which treated love as a humoral imbalance requiring purgation. Shakespeare borrows the medical framework but subverts it: [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s cure does not purge love but educates it. The play's comic resolution — four marriages — confirms that love, properly understood, is not a sickness to be cured but a condition to be lived with.

## Gender and Love

The gender dynamics of love in As You Like It are uniquely complex because of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[disguise-plan|disguise]] as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]. The boy-actor playing [[rosalind|Rosalind]] disguises herself as a boy who then impersonates a woman for [[orlando|Orlando]]'s benefit. This recursive gender performance allows Shakespeare to explore love's relationship to gender identity: [[orlando|Orlando]] learns to love [[rosalind|Rosalind]] by practicing on [[ganimed|Ganymede]]; [[phebe|Phebe]] falls in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]] thinking he is male; and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] herself experiences the [[freedom]] of speaking about love from a male position.

The homoerotic dimension of [[phebe|Phebe]]'s love for [[ganimed|Ganymede]] — and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s intimacy with [[ganimed|Ganymede]] — hovers at the edge of the play's consciousness. Shakespeare never resolves these tensions; he simply resolves the plot through marriage, leaving the gender complexities to resonate in the audience's memory. The play's treatment of love is thus inseparable from its treatment of gender: love in As You Like It is always love across the instabilities of identity that [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] enables.

## Pastoral Love Conventions

The play's love plots are shaped by the pastoral tradition, which idealized shepherds and shepherdesses engaged in intricate courtship rituals. Shakespeare inherits this tradition and both uses and subverts it. [[silvius|Silvius]] is the perfect [[silvius|pastoral lover]] — faithful, suffering, eloquent in his [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] — but the play treats him with affectionate mockery. His Petrarchan [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] to [[phebe|Phebe]] is presented as a form of self-indulgence rather than genuine love: [[silvius|Silvius]] loves the posture of loving more than he loves [[phebe|Phebe]] herself.

The pastoral convention is further subverted by [[corin|Corin]], the genuine shepherd whose wisdom has nothing to do with love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]], and by [[touchstone|Touchstone]], whose anti-romantic pragmatism exposes pastoral idealization as a courtly fantasy. The [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] is not a real pastoral landscape but a theatrical space where love conventions can be tested, parodied, and ultimately renewed.

## Love and Social Order

The four marriages at the play's end restore social order after the disruption of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s usurpation. Love, in the comedic tradition, is the mechanism through which society regenerates itself: the marriages heal the breach between [[court-vs-forest|court and forest]], between the exiled and the usurpers. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s marriage to [[celia|Celia]] reconciles the two brothers; [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s marriage to [[orlando|Orlando]] reunites the houses; even [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s marriage to [[audrey|Audrey]] crosses the class boundary between court and country. Love in As You Like It is not merely a personal emotion but a social force — the force that enables the comedic resolution and the return to court.

## Key Passages

### Orlando Struck Dumb (Act 1, Scene 2)

After defeating [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler and meeting [[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[orlando|Orlando]] finds himself paralyzed by love:

> What passion hangs these waights vpon my toong? I cannot speake to her, yet shee vrg'd conference. O poore Orlando! thou art ouerthrowne (002-161)

Love's first effect is the loss of speech — the lover's tongue is weighed down by passion that exceeds language.

### The Love Cure (Act 3, Scene 2)

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) explains her method for curing love:

> Hee was to imagine me his Loue, his Mistris: and I set him euerie day to woe me. At which time would I, being but a moonish youth, greeue, be effeminate, changeable, longing, and liking, proud, fantasticall, apish, shallow, inconstant, full of teares, full of smiles (012-180)

The list of adjectives — changeable, proud, fantastic, apish, shallow, inconstant — is [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s anti-Petrarchan manifesto: women are not fixed ideals but fluctuating, contradictory beings.

### [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Confession (Act 4, Scene 1)

> O coz, coz, coz: my pretty little coz, that thou didst know how many fathome deepe I am in loue: but it cannot bee sounded: my affection hath an vnknowne bottome, like the Bay of Portugall (016-091)

[[celia|Celia]]'s reply captures the play's attitude toward love as endless overflow:

> Or rather bottomlesse, that as fast as you poure affection in, it runs out (016-092)

### [[silvius|Silvius]]'s Pastoral Devotion (Act 2, Scene 4)

[[silvius|Silvius]] presents the Petrarchan lover's credo to the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]]:

> Though in thy youth thou wast as true a louer / As euer sigh'd vpon a midnight pillow (007-020)

### The True Lover's Clock (Act 3, Scene 2)

> Then there is no true Louer in the Forrest, else sighing euerie minute, and groaning euerie houre wold detect the lazie foot of time, as wel as a clocke (012-148)

Love creates its own temporality — the lover's sighs become the measure of time itself.

### [[phebe|Phebe]]'s Conversion (Act 3, Scene 5)

> Dead Shepheard, now I find thy saw of might, Who euer lov'd, that lou'd not at first sight? (015-085)

[[phebe|Phebe]] quotes Marlowe while falling in love with a woman she believes to be a boy — a perfect Shakespearean knot of literary allusion, gender confusion, and romantic suddenness.
