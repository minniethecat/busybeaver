# Touchstone

## Overview

Touchstone is the licensed fool of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s court who accompanies [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] into [[freedom|exile]] in the [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]]. His name is significant: a touchstone was used to test the purity of gold and silver by the streak left upon the stone, and [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s function in the play is to test the authenticity of [[persons|characters]] and conventions through [[wit]]. As "the cutter off of natures witte" (002-018), he deflates pastoral idealism, romantic excess, courtly ceremony, and philosophical pretension with equal vigor. His courtship of the rustic [[audrey|Audrey]] provides the play's most anti-romantic love plot, and his philosophy of accommodation — making the best of imperfect circumstances — offers a pragmatic counterpoint to both [[jaques|Jaques]]'s melancholy and the lovers' idealism. Touchstone is the most intelligent fool in Shakespeare — a character whose [[wit]] exposes the follies of every other character while revealing its own limits.

## The Name: Touchstone as Social Assay

The name "Touchstone" was not Shakespeare's invention — it was a common term for a dark siliceous stone used by goldsmiths to test the purity of precious metals by rubbing them against the stone's surface and observing the streak. The metaphor was already current in Renaissance discourse: a "touchstone" was anything that revealed the true nature of what it tested.

Shakespeare makes the metaphor structural. Touchstone tests every convention he encounters: pastoral idealism (through his dialogue with [[corin|Corin]]), courtly [[love]] (through his parody of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s verses), philosophical melancholy (through his encounter with [[jaques|Jaques]]), and the institution of [[marriage]] itself (through his pragmatic courtship of [[audrey|Audrey]]). Nothing escapes his assay, and nothing tests entirely pure. Yet [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s own position is never tested — he remains the assayer, never the assayed, a structural privilege that makes him complicit in the follies he exposes.

## The Fool Tradition

Touchstone belongs to a distinguished lineage of Shakespearean fools that includes Feste in Twelfth Night, the Fool in King Lear, and Lavatch in All's Well That Ends Well. The licensed fool occupied a unique position in Renaissance court culture: permitted to speak truths that would be dangerous from any other mouth, [[touchstone|the fool]]'s "motley" was both a costume and a form of epistemological immunity.

Touchstone exploits this license fully. His parody of courtly rhetoric in the "seven degrees of the lie" speech (022-054) is simultaneously an exposure of courtly artifice and a dazzling performance of that same artifice. The fool who reveals that all social behavior is performed does so through an equally performed [[wit]] — a recursion that Shakespeare handles with characteristic lightness. Unlike [[jaques|Jaques]], who yearns for [[touchstone|the fool]]'s license ("I must have [[freedom|liberty]]" to "Cleanse the foule bodie of th' infected world" (010-063)), Touchstone already possesses it and uses it with casual mastery.

## Role in the Play

### The Fortune-Nature Debate (Act 1, Scene 2)

Touchstone enters the [[fortune|Fortune]]-[[nature|Nature]] debate as an embodied argument. [[celia|Celia]] describes him as "[[fortune|Fortune]]'s foole" (002-018) — an agent of chance sent to disrupt philosophical certainty — while [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reinterprets him as [[nature|Nature]]'s gift: "when fortune makes natures naturall, the cutter off of natures witte" (002-018). Touchstone himself deflects the philosophical framing, turning everything into jest. His refusal to take the debate seriously is itself a philosophical position: [[wit]] trumps wisdom, performance trumps sincerity.

### Satirist of Pastoral Convention

In [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], Touchstone becomes the play's satiric conscience. His parody of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] — improvising doggerel verses in "the verie false gallop of Verses" that make [[rosalind|Rosalind]] laugh (012-042) — deflates the Petrarchan tradition without dismissing love itself. His most sustained satiric performance is his encounter with [[corin|Corin]] [[silvius|the shepherd]], in which Touchstone deploys all his courtly [[wit]] against [[corin|Corin]]'s rustic simplicity and loses. [[corin|Corin]]'s simple wisdom — "I am a true labourer, I earne that I eate" (012-034) — withstands [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit]] because it makes no claim that [[wit]] can expose. The pastoral idealists ([[silvius|Silvius]], the Petrarchan lover) are vulnerable to Touchstone's satire; the genuinely rustic ([[corin|Corin]]) are not. Shakespeare's point is subtle: pastoralism as literary convention is hollow, but pastoral life as lived experience has an integrity that satire cannot touch.

### The Seven Degrees of the Lie (Act 5, Scene 4)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s extended meditation on the degrees of the lie — the "Lie seuen times remoued" (022-054) — is a tour de force of comic rhetoric, delivered to [[jaques|Jaques]] in the final act. He anatomizes courtly manners with the precision of a scholastic philosopher: the Retort Courteous, the Quip Modest, the Reply Churlish, the Reproof Valiant, the Countercheck Quarrelsome, the Lie Circumstantial, and the Lie Direct. Each degree is a further retreat from honest speech, and Touchstone's taxonomy exposes the elaborate architecture of evasion that constitutes courtly discourse. The speech is both a joke and a serious critique: all social behavior is governed by rules as arbitrary and elaborate as the dueling code Touchstone parodies.

### The Anti-Romantic Marriage

His marriage to [[audrey|Audrey]] is the play's earthiest union. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s reasons for marrying are deliberately anti-heroic: "As hornes are odious, they are necessarie" (013-031) — cuckoldry is the price of [[marriage|matrimony]], and he accepts it with comic resignation. Unlike [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Petrarchan [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] or [[silvius|Silvius]]'s pastoral suffering, Touchstone's approach to marriage is pragmatic, even cynical: "We must be married, or we must live in bawdry" (013-031). Yet his marriage, like the others, is blessed by [[hymen|Hymen]], suggesting that even [[touchstone|the fool]]'s practical accommodation of desire finds its place in the comedic resolution. Shakespeare grants Touchstone's anti-romantic pragmatism the same blessing he gives to romantic love — a remarkably inclusive comic vision.

### Dismissing William (Act 5, Scene 1)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s confrontation with [[william|William]], his rustic rival for [[audrey|Audrey]], is comedy of social dominance. Touchstone deploys his full rhetorical arsenal against a man who cannot possibly comprehend it, threatening [[william|William]] with elaborate vocabulary of destruction (019-033). The scene is hilarious but disturbing: the fool who exposes pretension is himself a bully when facing someone without [[wit]] to defend himself. Shakespeare does not let Touchstone off easily — his [[wit]], so liberating when turned against courtly artifice, becomes an instrument of oppression against the genuinely powerless.

## Wit and Wisdom: [[touchstone|The Fool]]'s Philosophy

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s philosophy is a form of radical pragmatism. Where [[jaques|Jaques]] seeks "matter" — the deep substance of melancholy reflection — Touchstone seeks accommodation. His [[wit]] is not an instrument of truth-seeking but of survival: he makes the best of imperfect circumstances because circumstances are always imperfect. This philosophy has its limitations — it offers no vision of transformation, no "[[forest-of-arden|green world]]" of [[regeneration|renewal]] — but it has the virtue of honesty. Touchstone never pretends that [[wit]] can change the world; he only claims that [[wit]] can make the world bearable.

The contrast with [[jaques|Jaques]] is instructive. Both [[persons|characters]] are satirists, both anatomize human folly, but their temperaments are opposed. [[jaques|Jaques]]'s satire is moral — it aims at reform, even as it despairs of achieving it. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s satire is amoral — it aims only at exposure, and takes pleasure in the exposure itself. [[jaques|Jaques]] envies Touchstone's license while Touchstone probably finds [[jaques|Jaques]]'s moral seriousness faintly ridiculous. Their final encounter, in which Touchstone performs the anatomy of the lie for [[jaques|Jaques]]'s delighted appreciation, is the play's most concentrated exploration of the varieties of satiric intelligence.

## Key Scenes

### Enter the Fool (Act 1, Scene 2)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s entrance provokes the [[fortune|Fortune]]-[[nature|Nature]] debate:

> Cel. No; when Nature hath made a faire creature, may she not by Fortune fall into the fire? though nature hath giuen vs wit to flout at Fortune, hath not Fortune sent in this foole to cut off the argument? (002-018)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s presence immediately disrupts philosophical discourse, reducing abstract debate to comic performance.

### The Seven Degrees of the Lie (Act 5, Scene 4)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s comic anatomy of courtly lying, beginning with "the Retort courteous" and ending with "the Lie direct" (022-054). The speech is a masterpiece of comic rhetoric that simultaneously parodies and exemplifies the courtly artifice it describes.

### Rustic Marriage (Act 3, Scene 3)

Touchstone negotiates marriage to [[audrey|Audrey]] with Sir [[oliver|Oliver]] Martext:

> Truly she must be giuen, or the marriage is not lawfull (013-033)

His insistence on a proper ceremony, despite his otherwise anti-romantic stance, reveals an unexpected respect for social forms — or perhaps simply a practical concern for legality over romance.

### [[corin|Corin]]'s Wisdom (Act 3, Scene 2)

Touchstone questions [[corin|Corin]] about pastoral life, and [[corin|Corin]]'s simple answers prove unexpectedly resistant to [[touchstone|the fool]]'s [[wit]]. Touchstone asks whether [[corin|Corin]] has ever been at court; [[corin|Corin]] answers "No truly" (012-017) with the unshakeable calm of a man who has never needed to be. The scene is Shakespeare's quietest defense of the pastoral against its courtly detractors.

### Dismissing William (Act 5, Scene 1)

Touchstone dismisses [[william|William]], his rival for [[audrey|Audrey]], with comic menace and the triumphant assertion of courtly superiority. The scene reveals the dark side of [[wit]]: when deployed against those without the means to defend themselves, it becomes indistinguishable from bullying.

### Philosophy of Accommodation

Throughout the play, Touchstone models a philosophy of making do: "Wast euer in Court?" (012-016) he asks [[corin|Corin]], concluding that "Thou art in a parlous state shepheard" (012-022). His [[wit]] is not for changing the world but for surviving it — a philosophy that proves remarkably durable.

## Critical Reception

Touchstone has been celebrated as Shakespeare's most intellectually sophisticated fool. Harold Bloom called him "the most intelligent character in the play," noting that [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit]] "exceeds even [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s in its verbal dexterity." Feminist critics have noted the gendered dimension of Touchstone's license — his [[freedom]] to speak truth is a male privilege that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] must achieve through disguise — while Marxist readings have emphasized Touchstone's role in exposing the class dynamics that pastoral convention obscures.

## Relationships

- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — Values his [[wit]]; he serves as her companion and comic foil. Their shared intelligence makes them the play's most formidable satiric team
- [[celia|Celia]] — Brought him to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]; appreciates his satiric intelligence as a relief from pastoral earnestness
- [[jaques|Jaques]] — Fellow satirist, but of a different temper: [[wit]] vs melancholy. Their encounter in Act 5 is a summit meeting of the play's two great intelligences
- [[audrey|Audrey]] — His rustic bride; their marriage is practical rather than romantic, a union of [[wit]] with simplicity
- [[corin|Corin]] — The old shepherd whose simplicity withstands [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit]]; their encounter is the play's most nuanced treatment of court vs country
- [[william|William]] — [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s rustic rival, whom he intellectually bullies into submission
- [[orlando|Orlando]] — Touchstone parodies his love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]], exposing Petrarchan convention to comic deflation
