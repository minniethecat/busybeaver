# [[oliver|Oliver]]'s Conversion in Arden

## Overview

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s Conversion is the pivotal Act 4 event of *As You Like It* — the moment when the fratricidal elder brother is transformed through his younger brother's grace. Sent by [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] to hunt down [[orlando|Orlando]], [[oliver|Oliver]] instead finds himself rescued by [[orlando|Orlando]] from a ravenous lioness. The near-death experience and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Christ-like mercy trigger [[oliver|Oliver]]'s moral regeneration: he abandons his murderous mission, reconciles with his brother, and falls in love with [[celia|Celia]] (as [[aliena|Aliena]]). [[oliver|Oliver]] tells this story himself to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] in a remarkable narrative of conversion.

## Dramatic Function

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s conversion is the moral climax of the play's fraternal plot. It parallels and completes the pattern established by [[duke-frederick|[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s]] usurpation of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]: where one fraternal relationship is corrupted by envy, another is healed by sacrifice. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s rescue of the brother who sought to kill him is the play's supreme act of [[regeneration|regeneration]], proving that [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s transformative power extends even to the play's most hardened villain.

The event also drives the romantic plot to its conclusion: [[oliver|Oliver]]'s sudden love for [[celia|Celia]] ([[aliena|Aliena]]) provides the fourth couple for the play's climactic weddings.

## Key Passages

### [[oliver|Oliver]]'s Account (Act 4, Scene 3)

[[oliver|Oliver]] narrates his rescue by [[orlando|Orlando]]:

> When from the first to last betwixt vs two, / Teares our recountments had most kindely bath'd, / As how I came into that Desert place. / In briefe, he led me to the gentle Duke, / Who gaue me fresh aray, and entertainment, / Committing me vnto my brothers loue, / Who led me instantly vnto his Caue (018-146)

### The Lioness Wound (Act 4, Scene 3)

[[oliver|Oliver]] describes [[orlando|Orlando]]'s wound, received while saving him:

> There stript himselfe, and heere vpon his arme / The Lyonnesse had torne some flesh away, / Which all this while had bled; and now he fainted, / And cride in fainting vpon Rosalinde (018-149)

### [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Reaction (Act 4, Scene 3)

The news of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s wound causes [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) to faint — a moment that nearly blows her male disguise:

> Briefe, I recouer'd him, bound vp his wound, / And after some small space, being strong at heart, / He sent me hither, stranger as I am / To tell this story (018-151)

## Participants

- [[orlando|Orlando]] — The rescuer, who twice chose not to kill his sleeping brother and fought the lioness to save him
- [[oliver|Oliver]] — The rescued brother, whose near-death experience catalyzes his moral transformation
- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — Faints upon hearing of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s wound, revealing her love beneath the [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise
- [[celia|Celia]] — Witnesses the story; will become [[oliver|Oliver]]'s bride
