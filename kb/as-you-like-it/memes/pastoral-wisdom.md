## Definition

[[pastoralism|Pastoral]] wisdom in *As You Like It* designates a mode of knowledge acquired not through books, formal education, or courtly refinement but through immersion in the natural world and the rhythms of rural life. It is articulated most memorably in [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s meditation on the "sweet uses of adversity" at the opening of Act 2, and embodied in [[silvius|the shepherd]] [[corin|Corin]]'s plainspoken philosophy. Unlike the [[wit]] of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — which deconstructs through parody — or the melancholy of [[jaques|Jaques]] — which withdraws into critique — pastoral wisdom is affirmative: it finds meaning and instruction in what the natural world already offers, without needing to re-make it in language.

## In As You Like It

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s speech at the opening of Act 2 Scene 1 is the play's definitive statement of pastoral philosophy. Banished to the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], he reframes [[freedom|exile]] as education:

> Sweet are the vses of aduersitie
> Which like the toad, ougly and venemous,
> Weares yet a precious iewell in his head:
> And this our life exempt from publike haunt,
> Findes tongues in trees, bookes in the running brookes,
> Sermons in stones, and good in euery thing [004-014–019]

The speech performs the very transformation it describes. The "painted pompe" (004-005) of court is rejected not for being false — the comparison is "sweete" vs "painted," pleasantness vs mere appearance — but for being less nourishing than the adversities that teach. [[nature|Nature]], in this vision, is a library: trees speak, brooks are books, stones preach. The metaphor is not decorative but epistemological. It proposes that knowledge inheres in the world, available to anyone who learns to read it, irrespective of social rank or formal education.

The "counsellors" of [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] — "the churlish chiding of the winters winde" (004-009), the biting cold — are contrasted explicitly with flattering courtiers: "This is no flattery: these are counsellors / That feelingly perswade me what I am" [004-012–013]. Courtly knowledge is distorted by the desire to please; natural knowledge tells the truth because it is indifferent to human vanity. The wind that "bites and blows upon my body" (004-011) does not lie.

[[corin|Corin]] [[silvius|the shepherd]] provides the comic, practical complement to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s philosophical elevation. When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] asks "how like you this shepherds life," [[corin|Corin]]'s reply is so matter-of-fact as to become a different kind of wisdom: [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s life is what it is — its virtues and hardships are inseparable, and it needs no courtly critic to validate or dismiss it (012-014). [[corin|Corin]]'s famous observation that "the property of raine is to wet, and fire to burne" — a line of such apparent simplicity that it has become a touchstone for pastoral philosophy — asserts that nature's truths are self-evident and require no interpreter. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s elaborate court [[wit]], for all its brilliance, cannot improve upon what the world already plainly shows.

The play does not settle the question of which wisdom is superior. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s satirical demolition of pastoral cliché — "the diuell himselfe will haue no shepherds" (012-035) — coexists with [[corin|Corin]]'s plain virtue, and [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s high philosophy coexists with his eventual return to court. [[pastoralism|Pastoral]] wisdom, the play suggests, is not a final answer but a necessary corrective — a reminder that there are truths the court cannot teach and that "good in euery thing" is a posture of attention, not a claim of fact.
