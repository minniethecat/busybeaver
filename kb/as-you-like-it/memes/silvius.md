# Silvius

## Character Overview

Silvius is the archetypal pastoral lover of As You Like It -- a young shepherd whose all-consuming [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] to the disdainful [[phebe|Phebe]] exemplifies the Petrarchan love tradition in its most extreme form. He sighs, writes verses, and endures contempt with masochistic dedication, providing both a comic mirror for [[orlando|Orlando]]'s more successful courtship and a vehicle for Shakespeare's gentle satire of literary love conventions.

Unlike [[orlando|Orlando]], whose love for [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is educated and refined through [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[ganimed|Ganymede]] persona, Silvius remains stubbornly faithful to his hyperbolic suffering throughout the play. He is the lover who has perfected the art of loving without being loved in return. His eventual union with [[phebe|Phebe]] -- achieved not through his own agency but through [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s manipulation in the final scene -- completes the play's comic resolution, joining him to the pastoral comedy's four concluding marriages at [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]].

## The Petrarchan Lover

Silvius is the play's most deliberately exaggerated embodiment of the Petrarchan lover: a figure defined by abjection, hyperbole, and the conviction that no one has ever loved so deeply. The Petrarchan tradition, originating in the [[verse-and-poetry|sonnets]] of Petrarch, established a set of conventions in which the male lover worships an idealized, unattainable beloved who remains cold and indifferent. Shakespeare draws on these conventions throughout [[silvius|Silvius]]'s dialogue, pushing them to the edge of self-parody.

His very first words announce the Petrarchan posture:

> **Silvius**: Oh [[corin|Corin]], that thou knew'st how I do [[love|loue]] her (007-017)

The complaint to a sympathetic listener, the insistence on the uniqueness of his suffering, the conviction that no older generation could understand -- these are all standard elements of the Petrarchan lover's repertoire. When [[corin|Corin]] claims to have loved himself, Silvius dismisses him:

> Though in thy [[youth]] thou wast as true a louer / As euer sigh'd vpon a midnight pillow (007-020)

The midnight pillow -- a classic image of the solitary, sleepless lover -- signals the literary nature of [[silvius|Silvius]]'s suffering. He is not merely a young man in love; he is a shepherd who has read (or somehow absorbed) the conventions of [[love-poetry|love poetry]] and performs them with unwavering [[loyalty-and-service|fidelity]].

The repeated refrain "Thou hast not lou'd" (007-030, 007-033, 007-036) turns his complaint into a litany, a rhetorical structure that reinforces the absoluteness of his self-conception. No one else's experience can compare; his love is the measure against which all others fail.

## Relationship with Phebe

[[silvius|Silvius]]'s relationship with [[phebe|Phebe]] defines his dramatic function. He is the devoted suitor; she is the disdainful beloved. The dynamic is rendered with comic excess in Act 3, Scene 5, where Silvius pleads with [[phebe|Phebe]] even as she openly scorns him:

> **Silvius**: Sweet Phebe doe not scorne me, do not Phebe / Say that you [[love|loue]] me not, but say not so / In bitternesse (015-002)

The plea captures the essence of [[silvius|Silvius]]'s love: he does not demand affection, only that rejection be delivered without cruelty. He compares his suffering to that of a condemned man facing execution, begging the executioner ([[phebe|Phebe]]) for a gentler death. This extravagant self-abasement is the Petrarchan lover's stock-in-trade, and Shakespeare renders it with a mixture of sympathy and amusement.

[[phebe|Phebe]]'s response is coldly rational: she denies that her eyes can wound him, dismissing his poetic claims as literal falsehoods. [[silvius|Silvius]]'s reply reveals the philosophical core of his position:

> **Silvius**: O deere Phebe, / If euer (as that euer may be neere) / You meet in some fresh cheeke the power of fancie, / Then shall you know the wounds inuisible / That Loues keene arrows make (015-030)

The invisible wounds of love -- Silvius insists that love's reality is not empirically demonstrable but subjectively undeniable. This claim, however, is undercut by the immediate dramatic context: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] enters and berates [[phebe|Phebe]], causing [[phebe|Phebe]] to fall instantly in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]]. [[silvius|Silvius]]'s abstract philosophy of love is answered by love's actual irrationality.

At the scene's conclusion, when [[phebe|Phebe]] begins using Silvius as a messenger to [[ganimed|Ganymede]], [[silvius|Silvius]]'s abjection reaches its limit:

> **Silvius**: So holy, and so perfect is my loue, / And I in such a pouerty of grace, / That I shall thinke it a most plenteous crop / To gleane the broken eares after the man / That the maine haruest reapes (015-106)

He accepts the role of gleaner -- content to gather whatever scraps of attention [[phebe|Phebe]] deigns to offer, even as she pursues another. The metaphor is agricultural and self-denigrating, and its very excess becomes comic: there is such a thing as loving too humbly.

## Comic Function

Silvius serves a triple comic function in the play. First, he is a satirical mirror for [[orlando|Orlando]]: both are young men helplessly in love, but where [[orlando|Orlando]] grows through [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s tutelage, Silvius remains frozen in his Petrarchan posture. The contrast implies that love, to be successful, must be tempered by [[wit]] and self-knowledge -- qualities Silvius conspicuously lacks.

Second, he participates in the play's extended parody of pastoral conventions. In the tradition of pastoral romance, shepherds are noble philosophers who speak in elegant verse; Silvius is a shepherd who speaks like a sonnet, but his eloquence produces no results. The gap between literary convention and dramatic outcome is the source of comedy.

Third, his love-lament in Act 5, Scene 2 provides the play's most formalized anatomy of love. When [[phebe|Phebe]] asks him to "tell this [[youth]] what 'tis to [[love|loue]]" (020-031), Silvius delivers a structured definition:

> **Silvius**: It is to be all made of sighes and teares, / And so am I for Phebe (020-032)

> It is to be all made of faith and [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]], / And so am I for Phebe (020-037)

> It is to be all made of fantasie, / All made of passion, and all made of wishes, / All adoration, dutie, and obseruance, / All humblenesse, all patience, and impatience, / All puritie, all triall, all obseruance: / And so am I for Phebe (020-042)

This passage is the play's definitive statement of the Petrarchan love formula. The anaphoric "all made of" structure reduces love to a catalogue of affective states, each one a component in a self-annihilating [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]]. The litany is echoed by [[phebe|Phebe]] ("And so am I for [[ganimed|Ganimed]]") and [[orlando|Orlando]] ("And so am I for [[rosalind|Rosalind]]"), creating a choral structure that universalizes the complaint even as it exposes its conventionality. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s deflating response -- "And so am I for no woman" -- punctures the rhetorical inflation with characteristic [[wit]].

In the final scene, [[silvius|Silvius]]'s willingness to marry [[phebe|Phebe]] "though to haue her and death, were both one thing" (022-019) reiterates his comic function: he is the lover who never stops being a lover, even at the altar. His union with [[phebe|Phebe]] at [[the-four-weddings]] is comic precisely because nothing in his character has changed -- he simply gets what he has always wanted, whether he deserves it or not.

## Key Scenes

### The Faithful Lover (Act 2, Scene 4)

[[silvius|Silvius]]'s first appearance establishes the Petrarchan dynamic. His complaint to [[corin|Corin]] -- "Oh [[corin|Corin]], that thou knew'st how I do [[love|loue]] her" (007-017) -- and his dismissal of [[corin|Corin]]'s own experience of love set the pattern of hyperbolic [[pride|self-regard]]. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) observes and recognizes her own love in his: "Ioue, Ioue, this Shepherds passion, / Is much vpon my fashion" (007-045).

### [[phebe|Phebe]]'s Scorn (Act 3, Scene 5)

The play's fullest treatment of [[silvius|Silvius]] and [[phebe|Phebe]]. Silvius pleads ("Sweet [[phebe|Phebe]] doe not scorne me" (015-002)), [[phebe|Phebe]] dismisses, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] intervenes, and [[phebe|Phebe]] falls in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]]. Silvius is reduced to delivering [[phebe|Phebe]]'s love-letters to his rival, accepting "the broken eares after the man / That the maine haruest reapes" (015-106).

### The Messenger (Act 4, Scene 3)

Silvius delivers [[phebe|Phebe]]'s letter to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]), protesting his ignorance of its contents: "I am but as a guiltlesse messenger" (018-014). [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reads the letter aloud, exposing [[phebe|Phebe]]'s infatuation while Silvius -- incredulous -- asks "Call you this railing?" (018-047) and "Call you this chiding?" (018-070).

### The Love Litany (Act 5, Scene 2)

Silvius delivers the play's most formal definition of love: "It is to be all made of faith and [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]]" (020-037), joined by [[phebe|Phebe]] and [[orlando|Orlando]] in a choral structure that simultaneously celebrates and satirizes the Petrarchan tradition.

### The Wedding (Act 5, Scene 4)

Silvius agrees to marry [[phebe|Phebe]] "though to haue her and death, were both one thing" (022-019). His final union with the shepherdess who has scorned him throughout the play completes the pastoral comedy at [[the-four-weddings]].

## Relationships

- [[phebe|Phebe]] -- Suitor: his disdainful beloved; he endures her contempt with abject [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]], accepting even the role of messenger to his rival [[ganimed|Ganymede]]
- [[corin|Corin]] -- Fellow-shepherd: the older shepherd who witnesses [[silvius|Silvius]]'s love-lament and counsels him, though Silvius dismisses his experience as insufficient
- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] -- Aided-by: as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], she attempts to help him win [[phebe|Phebe]] but complicates his love; ultimately she is the architect of his marriage
