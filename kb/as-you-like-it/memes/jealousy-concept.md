# Jealousy

## Varieties of Jealousy

Jealousy appears in As You Like It in multiple forms: romantic jealousy,
fraternal envy, and the possessiveness that attends love. Unlike the tragic
jealousy of Othello or The Winter's Tale, the jealousy in As You Like It is
treated comically — it creates dramatic complication but is ultimately resolved
without destruction.

## Romantic Jealousy: Phebe and the Love Triangle

The play's most explicit jealousy plot involves [[phebe|Phebe]], the disdainful
shepherdess. She scorns the devoted [[silvius|Silvius]] — "I had as liefe be woo'd of a
Snayle" (015-083) — only to fall instantly in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]] (the disguised
[[rosalind|Rosalind]]). When [[gender-performance|Rosalind-as-Ganymede]] rebukes her, [[phebe|Phebe]] redirects her
frustration toward [[silvius|Silvius]]: "Tell this [[youth]] what tis to [[love|loue]]" (015-098).

The triangle ([[silvius|Silvius]] loves [[phebe|Phebe]], [[phebe|Phebe]] loves [[ganimed|Ganymede]], [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is [[rosalind|Rosalind]],
[[rosalind|Rosalind]] loves [[orlando|Orlando]]) becomes a comic knot that only the removal of disguise
can untie. [[phebe|Phebe]]'s jealousy is treated as foolish but not malicious — she is a
comic victim of her own misdirected passion.

## Fraternal Jealousy: Oliver and Orlando

The play opens with fraternal jealousy. [[orlando|Orlando]] complains that [[oliver|Oliver]] "barres
mee the place of a brother" and "mines my [[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]] with my education"
(001-002). [[oliver|Oliver]]'s jealousy of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s natural virtues — his "gentlenesse"
and the love he inspires — drives the initial conflict that sends [[orlando|Orlando]] into
[[freedom|exile]].

Unlike the romantic jealousy plots, fraternal jealousy in As You Like It is
treated more seriously because it has real consequences: attempted murder
([[oliver|Oliver]]'s plot with [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler), [[freedom|exile]], and the threat of continued
violence. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s conversion in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] effects a reconciliation that resolves
this jealousy through supernatural transformation rather than psychological
development.

## [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Comic Management of Jealousy

[[rosalind|Rosalind]], though disguised, experiences jealousy when she learns that [[phebe|Phebe]]
has written a love letter to [[ganimed|Ganymede]]. Her response — "I saw her hand, she has
a leatherne hand" (017-011) — is comic but reveals genuine possessiveness.
[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s ability to manage her jealousy through [[wit]] and disguise
distinguishes her from the play's other jealous [[persons|characters]] and demonstrates
her control over the comic narrative.

## Comic Resolution

The play's resolution dissolves jealousy through the mechanism of recognition.
When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reveals her true identity, [[phebe|Phebe]]'s love for [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is revealed
as a category error — she loved a woman in disguise. Her quick pivot to [[silvius|Silvius]]
("I will not eate my word, now thou art mine, Thy faith, my fancie to thee doth
combine" — 021-016) is a comic acknowledgment that jealousy, in this play, is
never more than a temporary misdirection of desire.

> As You Like It treats jealousy as a byproduct of misrecognition. Once
> identities are properly revealed and matched, jealousy dissolves — a
> characteristically comic treatment that distinguishes it from the tragic
> tradition where jealousy proves fatal.
