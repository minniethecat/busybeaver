## Definition

Gender performance in *As You Like It* refers to the play's sustained exploration of gender as a set of
behaviours that can be adopted, performed, and discarded, rather than an essential identity. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s
assumption of the male persona "[[ganimed|Ganymede]]" drives the central romantic plot of Acts 3 through 5 and
culminates in the Epilogue's metatheatrical revelation that the character is a boy-actor playing a woman
playing a boy. The device draws on the Renaissance convention of the "disguised heroine" while pushing
it to an unprecedented level of sophistication: [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s performance of masculinity becomes a tool for
teaching [[orlando|Orlando]] how to love, a screen for mocking Petrarchan convention, and ultimately a meditation
on the instability of identity itself.

## In As You Like It

The gender performance begins as a practical survival strategy — "I could finde in my heart to disgrace
my mans apparell, and to cry like a woman: but I must comfort the weaker vessell, as doublet and hose ought
to show it selfe coragious to petty-coate" (007-006) — but quickly exceeds its pragmatic function. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s
words here encode the performance's central irony: the "courage" she performs in male clothing is itself a
constructed masculinity, one visible to the audience but invisible to the other [[persons|characters]].

The comedy of [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] reaches its peak in the courtship lessons, where [[rosalind|Rosalind]]-[[ganimed|Ganymede]] offers to
"cure" [[orlando|Orlando]] of love by role-playing his mistress. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s own awareness of the performance's
instability surfaces in moments of near-exposure: "Do you not know I am a woman, when I thinke" (012-101);
"I should haue beene a woman" (018-179). These slips transform what could be mere farce into a sustained
inquiry into the relationship between inner self and outward presentation.

The Epilogue shatters the theatrical frame entirely. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — now explicitly the boy-actor who played
her — addresses the audience directly: "If I were a Woman, I would kisse as many of you as had beards
that pleas'd me" (023-001). This final turn of the screw reminds the audience that every gender
performance they have watched — [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s femininity, [[ganimed|Ganymede]]'s masculinity, [[orlando|Orlando]]'s chivalric
manhood — has been a theatrical construction from the start.
