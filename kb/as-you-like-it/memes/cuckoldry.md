## Definition

Cuckoldry — the fear or reality of a wife's infidelity, symbolised by the wearing of invisible horns —
is one of early modern comedy's most persistent [[concepts|motifs]]. In *As You Like It*, the horn joke operates on
multiple levels: it is a bawdy comic staple, a marker of the anxieties attending marriage, and, through
the hunting [[song-and-music|songs]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], a means of naturalising human sexual suspicion within the rhythms of
pastoral life. Shakespeare deploys the motif most extensively through [[touchstone|Touchstone]], whose prose reflections
on horns transform a crude jest into a miniature philosophy of marriage.

## In As You Like It

The horn motif surfaces most prominently in [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s extended prose meditation in Act 3. Anticipating
his own marriage to [[audrey|Audrey]], he reflects: "As hornes are odious, they are necessarie. It is said, many a man
knowes no end of his goods; right: Many a man has good Hornes, and knows no end of them. Well, that is the
dowrie of his wife, 'tis none of his owne getting" (013-031). The self-aware fatalism of this passage — horns
are both repellent and unavoidable — encapsulates [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s role as the play's comic realist. His earlier
observation that "no assembly but horne-beasts" populates [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] literalises the metaphor: [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s deer
carry visible horns, while its men carry invisible ones.

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] deploys the horn jest against [[orlando|Orlando]] with characteristic [[wit]]: when he protests that "Vertue is
no horne-maker: and my [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is virtuous," she retorts "Why hornes: w such as you are faine to be
beholding" (016-022) (016-025), implying that even virtuous wives cannot save their husbands from the
condition. The exchange occurs within the [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise, layering the jest with gender-performance
irony — a boy-actor playing a woman playing a boy warns a man about the inescapability of cuckoldry.

The motif culminates in the deer-hunting song of Act 4: "Take thou no scorne to weare the horne, / It was a
crest ere thou wast borne" (017-010). The song — "The horne, the horne, the lusty horne" (017-014) —
naturalises cuckoldry as an ancient inheritance rather than a personal shame, converting masculine anxiety
into communal ritual. [[jaques|Jaques]]' proposal to "set the Deares horns vpon his head, for a branch of victory"
(017-004) completes the transformation: horns shift from mark of shame to trophy, from fear to farce.
