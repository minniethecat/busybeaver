# Exile

## Definition

Exile — the forced or voluntary departure from one's home and rightful place — is the primary plot engine of As You Like It. Nearly every major character experiences some form of [[freedom|exile]]: [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] is banished by his usurping brother; [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is subsequently banished by the same Duke; [[orlando|Orlando]] flees to escape murder; and even [[oliver|Oliver]] is exiled from his estate by the play's moral logic. The concept draws on pastoral, philosophical, and political traditions that understand [[freedom|exile]] as both a loss and a potential source of wisdom. Shakespeare's innovation is to make [[freedom|exile]] not merely the condition that precedes pastoral retreat but the mechanism through which [[persons|characters]] discover their truest selves.

## Exile as Narrative Engine

The play's action is set in motion before it begins, with the senior Duke's banishment by his younger brother [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]. This backstory is delivered in Act 1, Scene 1: "the olde Duke is banished by his yonger brother the new Duke" (001-041). The exiled duke has retreated to [[forest-of-arden|the Forest of Arden]], establishing the pattern that governs the entire play: [[freedom|exile]] leads to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], and [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] leads to transformation. [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler's report frames the [[freedom|exile]] in romantic terms — the Duke and his lords "fleet the time carelesly as they did in the [[green-world|golden world]]" (001-043) — but this pastoral idealization is immediately complicated by the play's subsequent action.

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s banishment follows immediately, accelerating the plot. Despite being the new Duke's niece and [[celia|Celia]]'s beloved companion, she is expelled on pain of death (003-024). Her crime is being her father's daughter — "thou art thy Fathers daughter, there's enough" (003-045). The arbitrariness of [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s decree exposes the nature of tyranny: it punishes identity rather than action, punishing [[rosalind|Rosalind]] not for what she has done but for what she is.

[[celia|Celia]]'s decision to join [[rosalind|Rosalind]] in [[freedom|exile]] transforms banishment into a voluntary act of loyalty: "To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]" (003-097). [[celia|Celia]]'s choice is the play's most explicit statement about the moral value of [[freedom|exile]]: it is a test of loyalty, and those who pass it ([[celia|Celia]], the Duke's lords) demonstrate their virtue through voluntary suffering.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[freedom|exile]] is different in kind: it is flight from murderous fraternal hatred rather than ducal decree. [[adam|Adam]] warns him that [[oliver|Oliver]] intends to burn him in his lodging (006-025), forcing [[orlando|Orlando]] to flee to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] as well. His journey, with the faithful [[adam|Adam]], literalizes the hardships of [[freedom|exile]] — hunger, exhaustion, and the threat of death in the wilderness (009-003). Where the Duke's [[freedom|exile]] is philosophical (finding "sermons in stones") and [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s is strategic (planning a disguise), [[orlando|Orlando]]'s is raw survival.

## Voluntary vs Forced Exile

The play carefully distinguishes between forced and voluntary [[freedom|exile]], assigning moral weight to the distinction. [[duke-senior|The Duke]] and his lords are exiles by force who transform their condition into something approaching choice — they "would not change it" (004-020). [[celia|Celia]] chooses [[freedom|exile]] out of love. [[orlandos-flight|Orlando flees]] to survive. And [[jaques|Jaques]], at the play's end, chooses continued [[freedom|exile]] — a voluntary refusal of the [[regeneration|restoration]] that everyone else welcomes.

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s choice is the play's most unsettling treatment of [[freedom|exile]]. While everyone else returns to court, [[jaques|Jaques]] joins the converted [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] in religious retirement: "out of these conuertites, There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd" (022-139). His [[freedom|exile]] is no longer forced but chosen — a philosophical retreat rather than a political punishment. The play neither endorses nor condemns this choice; it simply records it as a limit case of what [[freedom|exile]] can mean.

## Exile as Liberation

The play's most radical argument is that [[freedom|exile]] can be a form of liberation. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s opening speech in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] transforms adversity into sweetness: "Hath not old custome made this life more sweete / Then that of painted pompe?" (004-004). The [[green-world|forest]]'s elements become "counsellors / That feelingly perswade me what I am" (004-012). Exile strips away the "painted pomp" of court — the flattery, the ceremony, the artificial hierarchy — and reveals what is essential.

This argument has deep roots in pastoral and philosophical traditions. The Stoic philosophers taught that [[freedom|exile]] was an indifferent — neither good nor evil in itself, but made good or evil by one's response to it. The pastoral tradition imagined rural retreat as an escape from courtly corruption. Shakespeare combines these traditions in the Duke's philosophy: [[freedom|exile]] is an occasion for virtue, an opportunity to discover that happiness does not depend on [[fortune|Fortune]]'s gifts.

Yet the play does not naively celebrate [[freedom|exile]] as liberation. The physical hardships are real: [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[adam|Adam]] nearly starve; [[rosalind|Rosalind]] collapses from exhaustion ("I could finde in my heart to disgrace my mans apparell, and to cry like a woman" (007-006)); and [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] has dangers (the lioness that attacks [[oliver|Oliver]] (018-115), the green and gilded snake that threatens him (018-109)). Exile liberates, but it also threatens. The play holds both truths in suspension.

## The Pastoral Exile Tradition

Shakespeare's treatment of [[freedom|exile]] engages with a rich literary tradition. Ovid's *Metamorphoses* — the playwright's favorite classical source — is full of exiles transformed by their suffering. The pastoral romances of Sidney and Lodge used [[freedom|exile]] as a plot device to transport [[persons|characters]] from court to countryside. And the political reality of Elizabethan England, with its anxieties about succession and usurpation, gave the theme of the exiled rightful ruler a contemporary resonance that Shakespeare exploits throughout his career.

What distinguishes As You Like It's treatment of [[freedom|exile]] from these sources is its systematic quality. Exile is not merely the backstory for one character but the condition shared by virtually everyone. The [[green-world|forest]] becomes a community of exiles — Duke, lords, princess, fool, and outcast alike — and the play's comic resolution depends on their collective ability to transform [[freedom|exile]] from a punishment into an opportunity.

## The Reversal of Exile

The play's resolution reverses the pattern of [[freedom|exile]]. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], marching to destroy [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], encounters "an old religious man" at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge and is converted, abandoning his usurped power (022-114). The banisher becomes the banished — [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] renounces the world, effectively exiling himself from the political order he usurped. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] is restored, and all exiles return — except [[jaques|Jaques]].

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s refusal to return is the play's final word on [[freedom|exile]]. The philosopher who has made [[freedom|exile]] his identity cannot accept [[regeneration|restoration]], for [[regeneration|restoration]] would mean the loss of the perspective that [[freedom|exile]] enabled. His choice of continued [[freedom|exile]] over communal celebration marks the limit of comedy's power to assimilate all experience. Some exiles, the play suggests, are permanent — not because return is impossible, but because the [[freedom|exile]] has become someone who cannot return.

## Key Passages

### [[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s Banishment (Act 1, Scene 1)

> the olde Duke is banished by his yonger brother the new Duke, and three or foure louing Lords haue put themselues into voluntary exile with him (001-041)

[[charles|Charles]]'s report establishes both the political situation and the moral response: the lords' voluntary [[freedom|exile]] testifies to the Duke's worth.

### Rosalind Banished (Act 1, Scene 3)

> Duk. Mistris, dispatch you with your safest haste, / And get you from our Court (003-024)

> Ros. I am more then your daughter, am I not your childe? / Duk. Thou art thy Fathers daughter, there's enough (003-045)

[[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s decree reduces [[rosalind|Rosalind]] to a single identity — her father's daughter — that is also the identity she most values. The irony is characteristic of Shakespeare's treatment of tyranny: it punishes what is most worthy of reward.

### [[celia|Celia]]'s Loyal Exile (Act 1, Scene 3)

> Cel. To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of Arden (003-097)

[[celia|Celia]]'s choice — to abandon her father's court for her uncle's [[freedom|exile]] — is the play's supreme act of loyalty, transforming banishment from a punishment into an act of love.

### [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s Exile Wisdom (Act 2, Scene 1)

> Hath not old custome made this life more sweete / Then that of painted pompe? (004-004)

[[duke-senior|The Duke]] reframes [[freedom|exile]] as liberation from courtly artifice. "Custom" — the habit of living in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — has made [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] sweeter than the court, a striking claim that "custom" can improve nature rather than corrupt it.

### [[adam|Adam]]'s Hunger (Act 2, Scene 6)

> O I die for food. Heere lie I downe (009-003)

The physical cost of [[freedom|exile]], endured by [[orlando|Orlando]]'s aged servant. [[adam|Adam]]'s near-death from hunger is the play's most direct acknowledgment that [[freedom|exile]] involves real suffering, not merely philosophical opportunity.

### [[jaques|Jaques]]'s Chosen Exile (Act 5, Scene 4)

> out of these conuertites, There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd (022-139)

The play's final word on [[freedom|exile]]: [[jaques|Jaques]] chooses to remain outside the restored order, pursuing "matter" rather than celebration.

- [[adam|Adam]] — His willingness to follow [[orlando|Orlando]] into [[freedom|exile]] and his near-death from hunger literalize [[freedom|exile]]'s physical cost
