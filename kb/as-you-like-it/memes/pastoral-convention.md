# Pastoral Convention

## Definition and Tradition

The pastoral convention is a literary mode dating back to Theocritus and Virgil,
in which shepherds and rural life are idealized as virtuous, simple, and free
from the corruptions of court and city life (001-002). In the Renaissance, the
pastoral was revived through works such as Sannazaro's *Arcadia* and Sidney's
*Arcadia*, which established the conventions Shakespeare both uses and parodies:
the lovesick shepherd, the disdainful shepherdess, the [[song-and-music|singing]] contests, and
the golden-[[youth|age]] nostalgia for a lost rural simplicity.

## Pastoral Elements in As You Like It

Shakespeare deploys the full pastoral toolkit in As You Like It: the [[freedom|exile]] from
court, the retreat to a natural setting, shepherds and shepherdesses, love
complaints sung to nature, and the ultimate [[regeneration|restoration]] of harmony.

The [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] functions as the pastoral [[forest-of-arden|green world]], where [[persons|characters]]
fleeing the corrupt court of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] find transformation (012-014).
[[corin|Corin]] [[silvius|the shepherd]] represents the honest countryman, while [[phebe-falls-for-ganymede|Silvius and Phebe]]
embody the conventional pastoral lovers — the lovesick shepherd pursuing a
disdainful shepherdess (007-013).

The pastoral [[song-and-music|songs]] punctuate the action: [[amiens|Amiens]]'s "Vnder the greene wood tree"
(008-003) invites listeners to pastoral ease, while the foresters' hunting song
(017-004) celebrates the active life of the woodland. These [[song-and-music|songs]] are not mere
decoration; they establish the tonal register of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] as a place apart.

## The Court-Country Contrast

The pastoral convention depends on the opposition between court and country.
[[touchstone|Touchstone]], the court fool, provides the sharpest commentary on this contrast.
When [[corin|Corin]] describes [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s life, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] responds with a witty
deconstruction: "in respect that it is a shepheards life, it is naught"
(012-014). He goes on to mock the notion that country life is inherently
superior, observing how "vncleanlie" it would be "if Courtiers were shepheards"
(012-023).

[[corin|Corin]]'s reply — that he is "shepheard to another man" and does not own the
flocks he tends (007-067) — introduces a real economic dimension that undercuts
the pastoral ideal. The pastoral tradition typically elides the material
conditions of rural labour; [[corin|Corin]]'s frank acknowledgement of his status as a
hireling punctures the convention's central fantasy of shepherdly independence.

## Shakespeare's Irony and Subversion

Shakespeare does not simply adopt the pastoral convention; he ironizes it.
[[rosalind|Rosalind]], disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], mocks the Petrarchan love conventions that
[[silvius|Silvius]] embodies. The play acknowledges the artificiality of pastoral retreat
even as it uses the [[forest-of-arden|green world]] to enable transformation and [[regeneration|renewal]].

[[silvius|Silvius]]'s exaggerated [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] — "Oh [[corin|Corin]], that thou knew'st how I do [[love|loue]]
her" (007-017) — and [[phebe|Phebe]]'s [[scorn-and-disdain|disdain]] serve as a comic mirror of the pastoral
love complaint tradition. Meanwhile, the genuine shepherds ([[corin|Corin]]) are not
idealized but presented with pragmatic realism.

[[jaques|Jaques]] adds a further layer of ironic commentary. His melancholy meditation
on the wounded deer — "the poore dapled fooles / Being natiue Burgers of this
desert City" (004-024) — moralizes the pastoral landscape in ways that the
convention does not anticipate. Where pastoral convention celebrates harmony
between human and natural worlds, [[jaques|Jaques]] sees only suffering and injustice.

## The Golden World Allusion

[[charles|Charles]] the wrestler's early description of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s exiled court frames
[[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] through a specific classical lens: the duke and his men "fleet the time
carelesly as they did in the [[green-world|golden world]]" (001-043). This allusion to the
[[the-golden-world|Golden Age]] myth — drawn from Ovid's *Metamorphoses* — establishes the pastoral
as a nostalgic return to a pre-lapsarian condition. Yet the play immediately
complicates this idealisation: the same speech notes that the exiled lords
live "like the old Robin Hood of England," a reference that grounds the
pastoral in English folk tradition rather than classical myth.

## The Pastoral's Function

The pastoral setting serves as a space of transformation. Characters enter
[[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] in crisis and emerge changed: [[orlando|Orlando]] learns to moderate his passion,
[[rosalind|Rosalind]] orchestrates her own marriage while testing [[orlando|Orlando]]'s love, [[oliver|Oliver]]
undergoes moral conversion, and even [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] finds religion at the
[[green-world|forest]]'s edge. The pastoral [[forest-of-arden|green world]] does not offer simple escape but a
liminal space where identities can be renegotiated (020-029).

Crucially, the pastoral transformation is temporary. The marriages of Act 5
signal a return to the social order — and [[jaques|Jaques]]'s decision to remain in the
[[green-world|forest]] rather than join the wedding festivities marks his refusal of the
pastoral's conventional happy ending. His departure to join [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]
in religious seclusion represents a different kind of retreat, one that
the pastoral convention cannot accommodate.

> The pastoral convention in As You Like It is not a naive celebration of rural
> life but a sophisticated dramatic tool: Shakespeare deploys the convention's
> familiar motifs only to subject them to comic scrutiny, ultimately affirming
> the regenerative power of the green world while refusing to pretend it is
> anything other than a theatrical construct.
