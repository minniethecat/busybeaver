# Rosalind

## Overview

Rosalind is the daughter of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and the protagonist of As You Like It. Banished from [[duke-fredericks-court|[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s court]], she disguises herself as a young man named [[ganimed|Ganymede]] and flees to the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] with her cousin [[celia|Celia]]. There, encountering the love-struck [[orlando|Orlando]], she uses her male disguise to "cure" him of love while simultaneously deepening their romantic bond. Rosalind is Shakespeare's most intellectually agile heroine — witty, pragmatic, and emotionally sophisticated — and she speaks more lines than any other female character in the canon. Her disguise enables her to move freely between gender roles, exposing the conventions that govern both love and identity in the process.

## Role in the Play

Rosalind serves as the play's dramatic engineer. From the moment she enters, she seizes narrative agency: proposing that she and [[celia|Celia]] "deuise sports" including "falling in Loue" (002-007), she frames the romantic comedy as a conscious game. Her [[exile|banishment]] by [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] is the play's inciting crisis, and her flight to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] sets the pastoral plot in motion.

In [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], Rosalind becomes the tutor of love. She has [[orlando|Orlando]] woo her as if she were Rosalind, creating a layered performance — a boy actor playing a woman playing a boy playing a woman — that exposes the performative nature of gender and courtship alike. Her declaration, "I say I am a Magitian" (020-023), acknowledges the quasi-magical power of her verbal and theatrical art as she prepares to orchestrate the play's resolution.

She orchestrates the play's resolution: extracting [[orlando|Orlando]]'s vow, schooling [[phebe|Phebe]] in humility, arranging the revelation of her true identity, and — with [[hymen|Hymen]]'s aid — conducting [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]] that restore order. Her final speech, the Epilogue, acknowledges her liminal status as both female character and boy actor, directly addressing the audience in a moment of meta-theatrical intimacy.

## Key Scenes

### Her Father's Banishment (Act 1, Scene 2)

Rosalind enters grieving her exiled father. [[celia|Celia]] urges her to be merry:

> Ros. Deere Cellia; I show more mirth then I am mistresse of, and would you yet were merrier (002-003)

### Falling in Love (Act 1, Scene 2)

Witnessing [[orlando|[[orlando|Orlando]]'s]] [[wrestling-match|wrestling victory]], Rosalind is struck by love. She gives him her chain: "Weare this for me: one out of suites with fortune" (002-147). After he leaves, she confesses her passion to [[celia|Celia]].

### Banishment (Act 1, Scene 3)

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] banishes Rosalind on pain of death: "Mistris, dispatch you with your safest haste, And get you from our Court" (003-024). She protests that "Treason is not inherited my Lord" (003-048), claiming an identity beyond [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s definition.

### The Disguise (Act 1, Scene 3)

Rosalind resolves to disguise herself as a man for safety: she will wear "mans apparell" (007-006) and carry "a gallant curtelax vpon my thigh" (003-108). The plan is both practical and generative — [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] will become her instrument of comic power.

### The Love Cure (Act 3, Scene 2)

Discovering [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[verse-and-poetry|love poems]] on the trees of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], Rosalind (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) proposes to cure him of love by having him woo her daily:

> Hee was to imagine me his Loue, his Mistris: and I set him euerie day to woe me (012-180)

### The Confession (Act 4, Scene 1)

To [[celia|Celia]], she reveals the depth of her feeling:

> O coz, coz, coz: my pretty little coz, that thou didst know how many fathome deepe I am in loue: but it cannot bee sounded: my affection hath an vnknowne bottome, like the Bay of Portugall (016-091)

### The Revelation (Act 5, Scene 4)

Rosalind sheds her disguise and reveals herself to her father and [[orlando|Orlando]], enabling the quadruple wedding that closes the play (022-037).

### The Epilogue

Rosalind steps out of character to address the audience directly, acknowledging the actor behind the role and soliciting applause.

## Relationships

- [[celia|Celia]] — Beloved cousin and companion; their bond is the play's deepest emotional attachment
- [[orlando|Orlando]] — Romantic love; she tests, teaches, and finally marries him
- [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] — Her exiled father; their reunion in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] restores the family
- [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] — Her uncle and banisher; his conversion enables her return
- [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — Fellow traveler to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]; the fool and the [[wit]] share a complementary intelligence
- [[jaques|Jaques]] — A contrasting intelligence; she and [[jaques|Jaques]] represent [[wit]] and melancholy as competing responses to experience
