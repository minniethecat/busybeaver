## Definition

Pastoralism is a literary mode that idealizes rural life, contrasting the simplicity and authenticity of shepherds and country-dwellers with the corruption and artifice of court and city life. In As You Like It, Shakespeare engages the pastoral tradition by moving his [[persons|characters]] from [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s tyrannical court into [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — a [[green-world|green world]] where social hierarchies loosen, identities become fluid, and love triumphs. Yet Shakespeare characteristically subverts pastoral idealism: [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] contains hardship as well as [[freedom]], and [[persons|characters]] like [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[jaques|Jaques]] serve as anti-pastoral voices that puncture rural sentimentality.

## In As You Like It

The pastoral framework is established through the play's geography: the court is a place of tyranny and fraternal hatred, while [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is introduced by [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] as a place of natural [[freedom|liberty]]. His opening speech in Act 2 celebrates [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s [[freedom]] from courtly corruption:

> Are not these woods more free from perill then the enuious Court? (004-006)

Pastoral conventions are embodied in [[silvius|the shepherd]] [[persons|characters]] — [[corin|Corin]], the practical shepherd; [[silvius|Silvius]], the lovelorn [[silvius|pastoral lover]]; and [[phebe|Phebe]], the disdainful shepherdess. [[silvius|Silvius]]' exaggerated [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] parodies pastoral love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]], while [[phebe|Phebe]]'s instant infatuation with [[ganimed|Ganimed]] subverts the convention of the chaste shepherdess.

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] functions as the play's anti-pastoral voice. His mockery of shepherd life — "the truest [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] is the most faining" — and his cynical commentary on court vs country puncture the idealized pastoral vision. When [[corin|Corin]] compares court and country manners, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] systematically dismantles pastoral pretensions while simultaneously revealing his own courtly corruption.

The play ultimately reconciles pastoral and courtly values through marriage: the four weddings in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] unite court [[persons|characters]] ([[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[orlando|Orlando]], [[oliver|Oliver]]) with the pastoral setting, suggesting a temporary synthesis rather than a permanent rejection of civilization.

## The Court/Country Debate: Touchstone vs. Corin

The most sustained interrogation of pastoral assumptions occurs in Act 3's debate between [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[corin|Corin]] [012-014–012-034]. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] opens with a deliberately paradoxical verdict on shepherd life:

> Truely Shepheard, in respect of it selfe, it is a good life; but in respect that it is a shepheards life, it is naught. (012-014)

The double judgment — good in itself, bad because it is itself — is pure sophistry, but it contains a real critique: pastoral life can only be celebrated from outside. Those who live it ([[corin|Corin]]) do not idealize it; those who idealize it (court exiles) do not live it permanently.

When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] asks whether [[corin|Corin]] has ever been to court and, on hearing "No truly" (012-017), declares "Then thou art damn'd" (012-018), he deploys a mock-syllogism: no court → no good manners → wickedness → sin → damnation. [[corin|Corin]]'s reply is the play's definitive pastoral counter-argument:

> those that are good maners at the Court, are as ridiculous in the Countrey, as the behauiour of the Countrie is most mockeable at the Court. (012-023)

Manners are relative, not absolute. [[duke-fredericks-court|The court]]'s refinement is as absurd in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] as [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s rusticity is at court. This is more than a defense of country life — it is a critique of the very standard by which [[touchstone|Touchstone]] judges. Value is contextual, not hierarchical.

[[corin|Corin]]'s self-description provides the ethical counterweight to [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s sophistry:

> Sir, I am a true Labourer, I earne that I eate: get that I weare; owe no man hate, enuie no mans happinesse: glad of other mens good content with my harme: and the greatest of my pride, is to see my Ewes graze, and my Lambes sucke. (012-034)

The catalogue of virtues — self-sufficiency, [[freedom]] from envy, contentment, pride in husbandry — is a working man's philosophy, practical rather than theoretical. [[corin|Corin]] does not argue for the superiority of pastoral life; he simply lives it, and that living is its own justification. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s response — a bawdy joke about copulating cattle (012-035) — concedes the argument by changing the subject. Wit cannot answer integrity.

## Subverting Pastoral Conventions

Shakespeare's treatment of pastoralism is notable for what it refuses to sentimentalize. The [[green-world|forest]] is cold, and [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] acknowledges "the icy phang / And churlish chiding of the winters winde" (004-008–004-009). [[adam|Adam]] nearly starves. The deer are hunted and wounded. [[jaques|Jaques]] moralizes over the "poore dapled fooles" (004-024) bleeding into the stream, transforming the pastoral landscape from idealized retreat into a site of real suffering.

The play's pastoral love-plot is equally deflationary. [[silvius|Silvius]] performs the conventional role of the lovelorn shepherd with such excess that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] diagnoses him as suffering from "a moonish [[youth]]" who "grieued, dispair'd, and tooke on" (016-009). [[phebe|Phebe]]'s instant infatuation with [[ganimed|Ganimed]] inverts the pastoral convention of the disdainful shepherdess by making her the active pursuer rather than the pursued. And [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s courtship of [[audrey|Audrey]] is a deliberate anti-pastoral parody — the fool choosing the ugliest woman available and justifying the match through cynical logic rather than romantic passion.

These subversions do not destroy the pastoral mode but complicate it. The play's ultimate synthesis — weddings that include both courtly and pastoral elements, presided over by [[hymen|Hymen]], the god of marriage — acknowledges that pastoralism is a fiction even as it grants the fiction its moment of triumph.
