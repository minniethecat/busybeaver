# The Wedding Site in Arden

The wedding site in the Forest of Arden is the culminating setting of *As You Like It*, where Act 5, Scene 4 brings together all the play's characters for the quadruple wedding. It is a space of convergence — where court and forest, disguise and identity, conflict and resolution meet, and where the pastoral world delivers its promised transformations.

## The Sacred Grove

The wedding site is presented as a natural sacred space within the forest, presided over by [[hymen|Hymen]], the god of marriage. The presence of a deity confirms that this is no ordinary woodland clearing but a consecrated site where the play's comic logic achieves its fullest expression:

> "Then is there mirth in heaven / When earthly things made even / Atone together."

Hymen's song establishes the wedding site as a place where cosmic and earthly harmony align — where the divisions that have structured the entire play are healed through marriage and recognition.

## The Gathering

The wedding site draws together every surviving character from the play's multiple plot lines. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and his exiled lords represent the forest court. [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[oliver|Oliver]] arrive with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]], the latter pair still in their male disguises as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] and [[aliena|Aliena]]. [[silvius|Silvius]] and [[phebe|Phebe]] bring the pastoral love plot. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]] represent the comic low plot. Even [[jaques-de-boys|Jaques de Boys]] arrives as an unexpected messenger.

The gathering at one site of characters who have pursued separate narrative threads is a hallmark of Shakespearean comic structure. The wedding site functions as the spatial correlative of the play's drive toward unity and resolution.

## The Unveiling

It is at the wedding site that Rosalind finally sheds her Ganymede disguise and appears as herself. The theatricality of this moment is heightened by the forest setting — she enters not in elaborate court dress but transformed through the magic of recognition:

> "I have, since I was three year old, conversed with a magician... I can do strange things."

Rosalind's claim to magical power is playful but truthful in the play's symbolic logic. The wedding site, as a liminal space between the natural and the supernatural, is where "strange things" — transformations of identity, reversals of fortune, and redemptions of character — become possible.

## Hymen's Blessing

The entry of Hymen with Rosalind marks the wedding site as ritual ground. Hymen's song — "Wedding is great Juno's crown" — invokes classical authority and natural order, blessing the four unions:

> "Here's eight that must take hands / To join in Hymen's bands."

The four marriages performed simultaneously — Rosalind to Orlando, Celia to Oliver, Phebe to Silvius, and Touchstone to Audrey — transform the woodland clearing into a sacred theater of social renewal.

## The Final Exits

The wedding site witnesses not only unions but departures. [[jaques|Jaques]] declines to join the celebratory return to court, choosing instead to seek out the converted [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] in his religious retreat. His exit — the only character to refuse the comic resolution — preserves a note of melancholy that distinguishes *As You Like It* from more uniformly celebratory comedies.

The wedding site thus becomes the threshold between the pastoral interlude and the restored political order. From this point, the characters disperse — some to the court, others to contemplation — but all transformed by their time in Arden.
