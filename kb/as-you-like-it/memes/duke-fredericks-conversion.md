## Summary

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s conversion is the final plot resolution of *As You Like It* — the event that restores political order and dissolves the tyranny that set the play in motion. [[duke-frederick|Frederick]], who had usurped his elder brother [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom and later banished [[rosalind|Rosalind]], marches an army toward [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] intending to capture and kill his brother. On [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s outskirts, however, he encounters "an old Religious man" whose counsel converts him entirely: [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] abandons his military enterprise, renounces worldly power, bequeaths the crown back to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], restores the lands of all exiled lords, and withdraws to a religious life.

## Dramatic Significance

The conversion performs three essential dramatic functions. First, it provides the *deus ex machina* resolution to the play's political conflict — the usurpation that frames Act 1 is undone not by battle but by spiritual transformation. Second, it affirms [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s regenerative power: the [[forest-of-arden|green world]] does not merely shelter exiles but actively transforms tyranny into renunciation. Third, it enables the play's comic resolution — without [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s conversion, the weddings and reunions of Act 5 Scene 4 would be shadowed by the approaching army. The conversion converts tragedy into comedy.

The event is reported rather than staged — [[jaques|Jaques]] de Boys, the second son of Sir Rowland, arrives as messenger. This narrative indirection keeps the play's focus on the festive community rather than on [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] himself, and it allows Shakespeare to present the conversion as an accomplished fact rather than a psychological journey.

## Context

The conversion must be understood against the play's opening situation. In Act 1, [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] has usurped [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom (see [[duke-senior]] and [[duke-frederick]]) and later banishes [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (see [[rosalinds-banishment]]). Throughout the play, the exiled court in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] exists in a state of suspended injustice — the usurper holds power while the rightful duke lives in [[exile|banishment]]. Frederick's conversion resolves this dramatic debt in a single stroke, restoring all that was taken.

The conversion also establishes a thematic parallel with [[oliver|Oliver]]'s conversion (see [[olivers-conversion]]): both antagonists are transformed upon entering [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]. Where [[oliver|Oliver]] is saved by [[orlando|Orlando]]'s mercy and [[brotherhood|brotherly love]], [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] is converted by religious counsel — two modes of redemption that together suggest [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s comprehensive moral efficacy.

## Key Quotations

> "Duke Frederick hearing how that euerie day men of great worth resorted to this forrest, addrest a mightie power... and to the skirts of this wilde Wood he came; where, meeting with an old Religious man, after some question with him, was conuerted both from his enterprize, and from the world" (022-108)

The compression of this passage is remarkable: an entire military campaign, a spiritual crisis, and a life-transforming conversion are dispatched in a handful of lines. The phrase "both from his enterprize, and from the world" marks the totality of [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s transformation — he renounces not only this specific attack but worldly ambition itself.

> "His crowne bequeathing to his banish'd Brother, and all their Lands restor'd to him againe that were with him exil'd" (022-117)

The bequest of the crown — phrased as a voluntary gift rather than a compelled surrender — transforms the political resolution from conquest into grace. The [[regeneration|restoration]] extends beyond [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] to all the exiled lords, making the conversion a collective liberation.

> "The Duke hath put on a Religious life, and throwne into neglect the pompous Court" (022-136)

[[jaques|Jaques]], the play's melancholic philosopher, immediately recognises the significance: Frederick's conversion offers a model of renunciation that appeals to [[jaques|Jaques]]'s own [[scorn-and-disdain|disdain]] for courtly artifice. His decision to join [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] — "Out of these conuertites, there is much matter to be heard, and learn'd" — extends the conversion's dramatic reach beyond political resolution into philosophical territory.
