# Song and Music

## Definition

Song in As You Like It functions as more than entertainment — it is a vehicle for thematic commentary, a marker of the pastoral mode, and a medium through which [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] world articulates its philosophy. Four major songs punctuate the action ("Under the Greenwood Tree," "Blow, Blow, Thou Winter Wind," "What Shall He Have That Killed the Deer," and "It Was a Lover and His Lass"), each reflecting on love, nature, ingratitude, and the passing of time. The songs are performed by [[amiens|Amiens]] and [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] lords, establishing music as a communal activity of the exiled court-in-[[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]].

## In As You Like It

Music enters the play immediately upon the [[persons|characters]]' arrival in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. [[amiens|Amiens]], one of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s attendant lords, serves as [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s principal singer. His first song, "Under the Greenwood Tree" (008-003), establishes the pastoral invitation: "Come hither, come hither, come hither: Heere shall he see no enemie, But Winter and rough weather." The song offers [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] as a refuge from human hostility, where only the seasons pose threat.

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s response to the music is characteristically melancholy: "I can sucke melancholly out of a song, As a Weasel suckes egges" (008-007). His request for more singing — "More, more, I prethee more" (008-012) — reveals the melancholic's hunger for material to feed his disposition, even as he recognizes the artifice.

The hunting song ("What shall he haue that kild the Deare," 014-033) introduces the ritual of the [[hunting|deer hunt]], complete with horn and ceremonial presentation of the [[cuckoldry|horns]]. This song bridges pastoral convention and courtly ritual, showing how the exiled lords have adapted their ceremonies to [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]].

"Blow, Blow, Thou Winter Wind" (010-184) delivers the play's most bitter commentary on human ingratitude, contrasting the honest harshness of nature with the hypocrisy of friendship: "Most friendship is fayning, most Louing meere folly."

The wedding song ("It Was a Lover and His Lass") closes the play's musical arc, aligning song with the comedic resolution in marriage. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] dismisses it: "I count it but time lost to heare such a foolish song" (021-036) — [[touchstone|the fool]]'s final deflation of pastoral convention.

## Key Passages

### Under the Greenwood Tree (Act 2, Scene 5)

> Vnder the greene wood tree, who loues to lye with mee, And turne his merrie Note, vnto the sweet Birds throte: Come hither, come hither, come hither: Heere shall he see no enemie, But Winter and rough weather. (008-003)

### Jaques and Melancholy (Act 2, Scene 5)

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s response to [[amiens|Amiens]]'s singing:

> Iaq. I can sucke melancholly out of a song, As a Weasel suckes egges (008-007)

> Iaq. More, I prethee more. (008-006)

### Blow, Blow, Thou Winter Wind (Act 2, Scene 7)

> Blow, blow, thou winter winde, Thou art not so vnkinde, as mans ingratitude: Thy tooth is not so keene, because thou art not seene, although thy breath be rude. Heigh ho, sing heigh ho, vnto the greene holly, Most frendship, is fayning; most Louing, meere folly: The heigh ho, the holly, This Life is most iolly. (010-185)

### The Deer Hunt Song (Act 4, Scene 2)

Praising the [[hunting|hunter]] who killed the deer, with ceremonial horn presentation (014-033).

### [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s Dismissal (Act 5, Scene 3)

> Clo. I count it but time lost to heare such a foolish song (021-036)
