## Definition

Social degree — the gradation of rank, status, and precedence — is one of the structuring principles of the court world that [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] partially suspends. In Elizabethan England, "degree" was a cosmological as much as a social concept: the Great Chain of Being ordered all creation from God down to inanimate matter, and social hierarchy was understood as a microcosm of divine order. *As You Like It* subjects this ideology to comic scrutiny, most explicitly in [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s virtuosic catalogue of "the degrees of the lie" in Act 5, which parodies the very idea of systematic gradation by applying it to the most trivial of social offenses — the quarrel.

## In As You Like It

The play's most sustained meditation on degree comes in Act 5 Scene 4, when [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — the former court fool now living in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] — recounts a quarrel conducted entirely by the book. [[jaques|Jaques]] introduces him as "the Motley-minded Gentleman, that I haue so often met in the Forrest: he hath bin a Courtier he sweares" (022-042). [[touchstone|Touchstone]] rises to the validation: "If any man doubt that, let him put mee to my purgation, I haue trod a measure, I haue flattred a Lady, I haue bin politicke with my friend, smooth with mine enemie, I haue vndone three Tailors" (022-043). Courtiership, in this catalogue, is not a matter of birth but of acquired behaviors — dancing, flattery, political cunning, financial ruin — all performed competences that anyone with [[wit]] might master.

The quarrel speech itself is a comic taxonomy of calibrated offense. Asked by [[jaques|Jaques]] to "nominate in order now, the degrees of the lye" (022-056), [[touchstone|Touchstone]] obliges with mock-scholarly precision: "The first, the Retort courteous: the second, the Quip-modest: the third, the reply Churlish: the fourth, the Reproofe valiant: the fift, the Counterchecke quarrelsome: the sixt, the Lye with circumstance: the seuenth, the Lye direct" (022-057). The quarrel escalated through seven named stages, each representing a precisely calibrated increase in aggression — and each governed by an invisible code of conduct no less rigid than the sumptuary laws that regulated Elizabethan dress by social rank.

The speech's comic force lies in the disproportion between its content (a trivial dispute over a courtier's beard-cut) and its form (a systematic, seven-tiered taxonomy delivered with the gravity of legal or scholastic discourse). The joke cuts both ways: the quarrel's absurdity mocks the pretensions of courtly [[honor]] culture, but the existence of the code — "we quarrel in print, by the booke" (022-057) — also reveals that hierarchy penetrates even the most apparently spontaneous human interactions. Even insult, the play suggests, has a social grammar.

Beyond this set piece, the play explores degree through spatial contrast. The [[duke-fredericks-court|court]] is a world of rigid rank — [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s power to banish on a whim, [[oliver|Oliver]]'s authority over [[orlando|Orlando]] as eldest brother, the elaborate protocols of the wrestling match. The [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], by contrast, suspends these hierarchies: a duke dines with his exiled lords "like the old Robin Hood of England" (001-043), a shepherd debates philosophy with a court fool, and a "yong Gallant" can woo a princess while disguised as a peasant boy. Yet degree is never fully dissolved — [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] remains "the Duke" even in [[freedom|exile]], and the play's resolution restores him to his throne "in no lesse degree" (022-101).
