## Definition

Verse in *As You Like It* is a formal medium and a thematic subject. Shakespeare famously alternates between verse and prose across the play: blank verse (unrhymed iambic pentameter) is the language of courtly passion, philosophical meditation, and ritual — spoken by [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], [[jaques|Jaques]], [[orlando|Orlando]] (in love), and the goddess-like [[hymen|Hymen]]. Prose is the language of fools, shepherds, comic courtship, and self-aware performance — spoken by [[touchstone|Touchstone]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]), [[corin|Corin]], and [[audrey|Audrey]]. This alternation is not merely decorative; it is a structural principle that maps the play's central tensions: court versus country, idealism versus realism, performance versus sincerity, poetry versus the mockery of poetry.

The play also treats poetry itself as a subject. [[orlando|Orlando]] hangs his [[love-poetry|love poems]] on the trees of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — "Hang there my verse, in witnesse of my [[love|loue]]" (012-002) — enacting the pastoral convention of the tree-carving lover. Shakespeare immediately subjects this convention to comic scrutiny: [[touchstone|Touchstone]] parodies the verses (012-040–042), [[rosalind|Rosalind]] critiques their lame metrical feet (012-054–058), and even the trees are said to yield "bad fruite" (012-043). The play never denies poetry's power to express love; it only insists that poetry, like love itself, must survive mockery to prove genuine.

## Verse vs. Prose Distribution

The formal distribution of verse and prose in *As You Like It* is one of the most deliberately patterned in the Shakespearean canon. Roughly 55% of the play is in prose — an unusually high proportion for a romantic comedy — and the choices are almost never arbitrary.

**Court [[persons|characters]]** speak blank verse in moments of elevation. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s opening speech in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — "Now my Coe-mates, and brothers in [[freedom|exile]]" — is in blank verse, as is his famous meditation "Sweet are the vses of aduersitie" (004-014). [[jaques|Jaques]]' "All the world's a stage" monologue (010-147–174) is blank verse at its most incantatory. [[orlando|Orlando]] drops into verse when he enters the lover's role: his tree-hanging poems are in rhymed pentameter and tetrameter; his later wooing of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] under the name of [[ganimed|Ganymede]], however, is largely in prose — because [[gender-performance|Rosalind-as-Ganymede]] keeps him on the ground.

**Fools and rustics** speak prose. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] never speaks verse — his [[wit]] is grounded in colloquial rhythms, and his parody of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s rhymes shows that he can command the lover's medium but chooses not to. [[corin|Corin]], [[silvius|the shepherd]], speaks prose; his wisdom is practical, not lyrical. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] in her own person speaks verse, but as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] she speaks almost exclusively prose — [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] strips away not only her gender but her formal register. The prose of [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is the play's dominant mode: witty, rapid, analytical, and endlessly self-aware.

**The [[song-and-music|songs]]** occupy a third register. Sung verse (rhymed, stanzaic, set to music) punctuates the action: [[amiens|Amiens]]' "Vnder the greene wood tree" (008-003) and "Blow, blow, thou winter winde" (010-185), the anonymous "What 'tis to [[love|loue]]?" song, and the two pages' "It was a Louer, and his Lasse" (021-016–021). Sung verse is communal where spoken verse is individual; it invites the audience into [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s atmosphere rather than advancing argument or character.

**The epilogue** returns to prose — but it is [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s prose, not [[ganimed|Ganymede]]'s. The boy actor, having shed his female disguise, steps forward to address the audience directly. The shift from the verse ritual of [[hymen|Hymen]]'s wedding masque to the prose intimacy of the epilogue enacts the play's final movement: from the [[forest-of-arden|green world]] back to the real one.

## The "All the World's a Stage" Speech

[[jaques|Jaques]]' Seven Ages monologue (010-147–174) is the play's most sustained piece of blank verse and its most famous passage. Delivered in response to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s observation that "This wide and vniuersall Theater / Presents more wofull Pageants then the Sceane / Wherein we play in" (010-144–146), the speech transforms the Duke's metaphor into a complete philosophy.

> "All the world's a stage, / And all the men and women, meerely Players" (010-147–148)

The verse form is crucial to the speech's effect. The unrhymed iambic pentameter moves with a relentless, processional inevitability — each of the [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] is given exactly the metrical space it requires and no more. The compression is the art: Shakespeare reduces a human lifespan to 28 lines without making it feel rushed. The famous closing line — "Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing" (010-174) — achieves its hammering finality through metrical accumulation: four stressed "sans" phrases, each stripping away a faculty, until nothing remains.

The speech's verse is deliberately impersonal. Unlike [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[love-poetry|love poems]] (which announce their speaker's identity) or [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s epilogue (which foregrounds the actor's body), [[jaques|Jaques]]' blank verse aspires to the condition of universal truth. It is the voice of the moralist — detached, diagnostic, and, as the play gently demonstrates, insufficient. The blank-verse form that conveys [[jaques|Jaques]]' pessimism with such authority is the same form that conveys [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s charitable welcome to [[orlando|Orlando]] only moments earlier. The play does not critique the verse medium; it critiques the speaker who mistakes the medium's authority for his own.

For a full treatment of the speech's structure and context, see [[seven-ages-of-man]].

## [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Love Poems

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s poetic output in Act 3, Scene 2 is central to the play's treatment of verse as both pastoral convention and comic target. Banished to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], he adopts the role of [[silvius|pastoral lover]] with theatrical zeal. His first poem, a sestet in rhymed iambic pentameter, invokes the classical apparatus of Diana and declares his intention:

> "Hang there my verse, in witnesse of my loue, / And thou thrice crowned Queene of night suruey / With thy chaste eye, from thy pale spheare aboue / Thy Huntresse name, that my full life doth sway. / O Rosalind, these Trees shall be my Bookes, / And in their barkes my thoughts Ile charracter" (012-002–007)

The poem is technically competent — Shakespeare is careful to make it a plausible lover's effusion, not a parody — but its very competence is the point. [[orlando|Orlando]] is not a great poet; he is a lover performing the role of poet, and the play treats his verses with affectionate irony. The closing couplet — "The faire, the chaste, and vnexpressiue shee" (012-011) — is genuinely beautiful, but the word "vnexpressiue" (meaning both inexpressible and inexpressive) introduces a productive ambiguity: is [[rosalind|Rosalind]] beyond expression, or does she simply not express? The poem cannot decide, and the play exploits the uncertainty.

The second, longer poem — "Why should this Desert bee, for it is vnpeopled?" (012-049) — is more explicitly self-conscious. [[orlando|Orlando]] envisions a [[green-world|forest]] transformed into a library of love: "Tonges Ile hang on euerie tree, that shall ciuill sayings show" and "Heauen would in little show" (012-049). The ambition is touching and absurd in equal measure. The catalogue of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s virtues — Helen's cheek, Cleopatra's majesty, Atalanta's better part, Lucrece's modesty — is the Renaissance blazon in miniature, the lover's art of praising his mistress by enumerating her parts. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s response — "O most gentle Iupiter, what tedious homilie of Loue" (012-050) — deflates the convention without quite denying its charm.

## [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s Parody and the Critique of Verse

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s response to [[orlando|Orlando]]'s verses is the play's sharpest exercise in literary criticism. Finding the poems, he immediately grasps the convention and subverts it:

> "Ile rime you so, eight yeares together; dinners, and suppers, and sleeping hours excepted: it is the right Butter-womens ranke to Market" (012-040–042)

The court fool reduces the Petrarchan mode to a mechanical exercise — "the right Butter-womans ranke to Market" — the rhythmic chant of a woman selling butter. His own parody — "If a Hart doe lacke a Hinde, / Let him seeke out Rosalinde" (012-042) — exposes the formula underlying [[orlando|Orlando]]'s earnest effusions. The verse form is shown to be reproducible by anyone with a rudimentary sense of rhythm and a willingness to rhyme "Rosalinde" with anything ending in "-inde."

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) extends the critique with clinical precision. When [[celia|Celia]] asks "Didst thou heare these verses?" (012-053), [[rosalind|Rosalind]] replies:

> "O yes, I heard them all, and more too, for some of them had in them more feete then the Verses would beare" (012-054–056)

The pun on "feete" — metrical feet and physical feet — is characteristic of the play's meta-literary [[wit]]. [[celia|Celia]] extends the joke: "the feet might beare the verses" (012-057), and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] completes it: "the feet were lame, and could not beare themselues without the verse, and therefore stood lamely in the verse" (012-058). This is literary criticism as comedy: the verses are technically defective, but the defect does not diminish their affective power. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] critiques what she also treasures — she has been "neuer so berim'd since Pythagoras time" (012-059), a confession disguised as a complaint.

## Pastoral Poetry Conventions

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s verses belong to a specific tradition: the pastoral love poem in which the lover carves his mistress's name on trees. The convention descends from classical pastoral — Virgil's Eclogue X, in which Gallus declares "I will carve my loves on the young trees" — through Renaissance imitations by Sannazaro, Tasso, Spenser, and Sidney. By the time Shakespeare wrote *As You Like It*, the tree-carving lover was a stock figure, instantly recognizable and ripe for parody.

Shakespeare's treatment is characteristically double-edged. He gives [[orlando|Orlando]] genuine poetic moments — "the faire, the chaste, and vnexpressiue shee" (012-011) is a beautiful line — while simultaneously exposing the convention's absurdity. The "vnexpressiue shee" who inspires the poems is, in the play's reality, a disguised woman reading the poems aloud to her cousin and laughing. The gap between pastoral ideal and theatrical reality could hardly be wider — or more productive of comedy.

The play also deploys the related convention of the pastoral [[song-and-music|singing]] contest. The two pages' "It was a Louer, and his Lasse" (021-016–021) is a carpe diem song in the classical tradition ("Sweet Louers [[love|loue]] the spring" (021-019)), while [[amiens|Amiens]]' [[song-and-music|songs]] establish the tonal register of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] as a place of deliberate leisure. Each song foregrounds its own conventionality — the pages' "hey nonino" refrain is self-consciously rustic — and the play treats them as theatrical resources rather than authentic folk expression. See also [[pastoral-convention]] for the broader literary mode.

## Song and Lyric

The five [[song-and-music|songs]] of *As You Like It* constitute a distinct lyric register within the play's verse system. They are:

1. **"Vnder the greene wood tree"** (008-003) — [[amiens|Amiens]]' invitation to pastoral ease, sung at [[jaques|Jaques]]' insistence. [[jaques|Jaques]]' response — "I can sucke melancholly out of a song, / As a Weazel suckes egges" (008-007–008) — immediately ironizes the pastoral invitation. The song offers escape; [[jaques|Jaques]] weaponizes it as occasion for melancholy performance.

2. **"Blow, blow, thou winter winde"** (010-185–196) — Sung at [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s request after the meal shared with [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[adam|Adam]]. The song's thesis — that the winter wind is less unkind than "mans ingratitude" (010-186) and "benefitts forgot" (010-194) — echoes the Duke's earlier meditation on "the churlish chiding of the winters winde" (004-009) but gives it lyric form. The refrain — "Most frendship, is fayning; most Louing, meere folly" (010-190) — is the play's darkest utterance, and it is sung, not spoken, as if the truth about human inconstancy can only be admitted through the distancing medium of song.

3. **"What 'tis to [[love|loue]]?"** — [[silvius|Silvius]]'s love complaint in Act 2, Scene 4 (not given in the Folio but implied by stage direction). The conventional [[silvius|pastoral lover]] sings his despair, and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] immediately recognizes the convention: "Alas poore Shepheard... I haue by hard aduenture found mine owne" (007-039–040).

4. **"It was a Louer, and his Lasse"** (021-016–021) — The pages' carpe diem song in Act 5, Scene 3. "Sweet Louers [[love|loue]] the spring" (021-019) and "How that a life was but a Flower" (021-030) condense the carpe diem tradition into its simplest form. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s critique — "there was no great matter in the dittie, yet y note was very vntunable" (021-032–033) — is [[touchstone|the fool]]'s last word on lyric poetry: dismissive, technically specific, and beside the point.

5. **"[[marriage|Wedding]] is great Iunos crowne"** (022-094–099) — The wedding hymn sung as [[hymen|Hymen]] presides over the quadruple marriage in Act 5, Scene 4. This is ritual verse, not personal expression: it invokes Juno, goddess of marriage, and [[hymen|Hymen]], god of weddings, in a ceremonial register that belongs to masque rather than drama. The shift from the naturalistic [[song-and-music|songs]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] to the hieratic verse of the wedding masque marks the play's movement from pastoral comedy toward ritual resolution.

The [[song-and-music|songs]] share a formal property: they are all in rhymed, stanzaic verse set to music, distinct from both the blank verse of courtly speech and the prose of comic exchange. They function as tonal punctuation — each song marks a pause in the dramatic action, an invitation to the audience to inhabit [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s atmosphere rather than follow its plot.

## The Epilogue: [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Prose Address

The play's final speech is in prose — and this is a deliberate formal choice with significant implications. After the verse ritual of [[hymen|Hymen]]'s wedding masque, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (now out of her [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise) steps forward to address the audience:

> "It is not the fashion to see the Ladie the Epilogue: but it is no more vnhandsome, then to see the Lord the Prologue" (023-001)

The epilogue works through a series of formal transgressions. First, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] acknowledges the convention (epilogues are spoken by men) and violates it (she speaks as a woman — or rather, as a boy actor who has just played a woman who played a boy). Second, she abandons verse for prose, completing the play's movement from the ritual elevation of the wedding masque back to the colloquial intimacy of direct address. Third, she explicitly invokes the actor's body: "If I were a Woman, I would kisse as many of you as had beards that pleas'd me" (023-001). The conditional "If I were a Woman" — spoken by a boy actor whose female character has just been restored — collapses the distinction between character, actor, and person in a single, breathtaking gesture.

The prose epilogue is the formal counterpart to the play's critique of verse. If blank verse aspires to universality — [[jaques|Jaques]]' "All the world's a stage" — prose insists on particularity: this actor, this stage, this audience, this moment. The play that began with [[orlando|Orlando]]'s earnest verse declarations ends with [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s prose invitation to applause. The movement from verse to prose mirrors the play's larger movement from idealizing pastoral convention to comic realism, from the [[forest-of-arden|green world]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] back to the wooden O of the Globe. Poetry cannot contain the experience; only the direct, provisional, and self-aware address of prose can bring the play to its proper close.
