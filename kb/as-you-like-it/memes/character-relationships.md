# Character Relationships

## Overview

The relationship network of As You Like It is among the most symmetrically structured in the Shakespearean canon. The play organizes its [[persons|characters]] into a system of paired opposites, parallel arcs, and choral echoes that map the movement from discord to harmony across five distinct relational domains. The network's architecture is governed by a single large-scale transformation: the migration of nearly every major character from the [[duke-fredericks-court|court]] to the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], where rigid hierarchies dissolve and relationships reconstitute themselves under the pressure of [[freedom|pastoral liberty]]. This article traces that architecture across five dimensions — family bonds, romantic pairings, friendship, the court-to-[[green-world|forest]] realignment, and servant-master dynamics — showing how each contributes to the play's culminating harmony at [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]].

Shakespeare draws on multiple relationship paradigms: the fraternal rivalry of the Cain and Abel story, the Petrarchan dyad of suffering lover and disdainful beloved, the classical ideal of friendship as a single soul in two bodies, and the feudal bond of loyal service. His achievement is to braid these traditions into a single, coherent network in which every character's transformation depends on another's — a web of mutual redemption that comedy alone can sustain.

## Family Bonds

Family relationships in As You Like It are overwhelmingly fraternal and overwhelmingly hostile at the outset. The play presents two pairs of brothers and one pair of cousins, and in each case the darker principle — envy, usurpation, the will to dominate — threatens to destroy the natural bond. That all three fractured family bonds are healed by the final scene is the play's deepest structural argument: comedy can repair what tragedy would leave broken.

### The Two Dukes: Brothers in Opposition

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] are the play's foundational fraternal pair, and their relationship establishes the political stakes that motivate the entire plot. [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] has usurped his elder brother's dukedom, forcing Senior into [[exile]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] "with a many merry men with him; and there they liue like the old Robin Hood of England" (001-043). The usurpation is the play's originating wound, and its consequences ripple outward: [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s precarious position at court, [[celia|Celia]]'s divided loyalty, [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s atmosphere of fear and surveillance.

[[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s tyranny is sustained by paranoia. His [[exile|banishment]] of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — "She is too subtile for thee, and her smoothnesse, Her verie silence, and her patience, Speake to the people, and they pittie her" (003-065) — reveals a ruler who perceives virtue itself as a political threat. The brothers never share a scene. Their reconciliation occurs entirely offstage: [[duke-frederick|Frederick]], marching to destroy [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], encounters "an old religious man" at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge and "After some question with him, was conuerted" (022-115). The instantaneous conversion is the play's most compressed transformation — no dramatic struggle, no negotiation — and it reflects comedy's grace-model of change, in which evil is not defeated but simply dissolved.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], by contrast, models a different form of brotherhood. His opening address — "Now my Co-mates, and brothers in [[freedom|exile]]" (004-001) — redefines brotherhood as voluntary community rather than biological inheritance. His lords are "brothers" by choice, not blood, and [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] community he leads is an alternative family constituted through shared adversity. Where [[duke-fredericks-court|[[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s court]] is organized around fear and coercion, Senior's [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is organized around fellowship and consent.

### Rosalind and Duke Senior: The Father-Daughter Bond

The relationship between [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] is one of the play's quiet emotional centers. Separated by [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s usurpation, they do not share the stage until the final scene. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s grief for her exiled father is the first emotion she expresses — "I show more [[joy-and-celebration|mirth]] then I am mistresse of" (002-003) — and her disguise as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] allows her to approach him incognito in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], a dramatic irony that stretches across three acts.

Their reunion at [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]] is the moment that restores the play's generational order. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reveals herself to her father with the words "To you I giue my selfe, for I am yours" (022-079), and the Duke's blessing authorizes her marriage to [[orlando|Orlando]]. The daughter who fled the court as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] returns as herself, and the father who transformed adversity into philosophy ("Findes tongues in trees, bookes in the running brookes, Sermons in stones" (004-018)) sees his wisdom vindicated in his daughter's [[regeneration|restoration]].

### Celia and Duke Frederick: The Abandoned Father

[[celia|Celia]]'s relationship with [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] is the play's most asymmetrical parent-child bond. [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] loves his daughter — his [[exile|banishment]] of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is partly motivated by jealousy of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s bond with [[celia|Celia]] — but his love is possessive and controlling. [[celia|Celia]]'s response to her father's tyranny is not rebellion but withdrawal: she chooses voluntary [[exile]] over comfortable complicity. "To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]" (003-097) is her declaration of independence, and the act transforms her from [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s daughter to her own moral agent. [[duke-fredericks-conversion|Frederick's conversion]] at the play's end repairs the political order but does not restore [[celia|Celia]] to him — she has already chosen a new family in her marriage to [[oliver|Oliver]], and the play's resolution does not require her return.

### Orlando and Oliver: The Cain and Abel Echo

The relationship between [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[oliver|Oliver]] is the most extensively developed fraternal bond in the play and the one that most explicitly echoes the biblical story of Cain and Abel. Shakespeare signals the allusion through [[orlando|Orlando]]'s opening complaint: [[oliver|Oliver]] keeps him "rustically at home" (001-002), denies him education, and treats him as less than a brother. The motivation is envy — "for my soule (yet I know not why) hates nothing more then he: yet hee's gentle, neuer school'd, and yet learned, full of noble deuise" (001-049) — a hatred that is irrational, self-aware, and therefore all the more dangerous.

[[oliver|Oliver]] plots [[orlando|Orlando]]'s death twice: first through the [[wrestling-match|wrestling match]] with [[charles|Charles]], then through arson. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s escape to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] with [[adam|Adam]] is a flight from fratricide. The brothers' reunion in Act 4, Scene 3 is the play's ethical climax. [[orlando|Orlando]], discovering [[oliver|Oliver]] sleeping beneath a tree and threatened by a lioness, twice turns away — "kindnesse, nobler euer then reuenge" — and twice returns to save him. The act of mercy transforms [[oliver|Oliver]] from fratricide to brother: "I do not shame to tell you what I was, Since my conuersion so sweetly tastes, Being the thing I am" (018-109). The language of conversion — the same word used for [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s transformation — links [[oliver|Oliver]]'s domestic redemption to the play's larger pattern of regenerated relationships.

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s love for [[celia|Celia]] follows immediately, as if the capacity to love his brother has unlocked the capacity to love at all. His conversion and marriage complete the fraternal plot: the brother who would have been Cain becomes Abel's restored companion, and the play's most fractured family bond produces its most surprising romantic union.

### Adam: The Surrogate Father

In the absence of his biological father (Sir Rowland de Boys, dead before the play begins), [[orlando|Orlando]] finds a surrogate in [[adam|Adam]], the aged servant who has served the de Boys household through two [[youth|generations]]. [[adam|Adam]]'s offering of his life savings — "I haue fiue hundred Crownes, The thriftie hire I saued vnder your Father" (006-040) — is the play's most luminous moment of selflessness, and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s care for the starving [[adam|Adam]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] ("O I die for food. Heere lie I downe, And measure out my graue" (009-003)) is [[orlando|Orlando]]'s finest moral moment. The servant who is not a father performs the father's office, funding the son's escape and following him into [[freedom|exile]] — a relationship in which the feudal bond of service is elevated to the voluntary bond of love.

## Romantic Pairings

The romantic network of As You Like It is organized around four couples whose unions constitute [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]] of Act 5, Scene 4. Each couple represents a distinct mode of love, and their juxtaposition creates a taxonomy of romantic experience that ranges from the ideal to the absurd.

### Rosalind and Orlando: The Central Romance

The courtship of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]] is the play's structural spine. It begins at the [[wrestling-match|wrestling match]], where [[rosalind|Rosalind]] gives [[orlando|Orlando]] her chain — "Weare this for me: one out of suites with fortune" (002-147) — and [[orlando|Orlando]] is struck speechless: "What passion hangs these waights vpon my toong? I cannot speake to her" (002-161). This initial encounter establishes the asymmetry that defines their relationship: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] recognizes and names love; [[orlando|Orlando]] experiences it but cannot articulate it.

Their courtship in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is conducted through the medium of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise. The disguise enables a pedagogy of love: as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]] teaches [[orlando|Orlando]] to see women as they are rather than as Petrarchan ideals. She challenges his pastoral posturing ("men haue died from time to time, and wormes haue eaten them, but not for [[love|loue]]" (013-083)), demands constancy ("woo me, woo mee: for now I am in a holy-day humor" (016-024)), and exposes the gap between romantic rhetoric and emotional reality. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s willingness to participate — to woo a boy who claims to be [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — demonstrates a capacity for imaginative play that distinguishes him from the rigid [[silvius|Silvius]].

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s confession to [[celia|Celia]] — "O coz, coz, coz: my pretty little coz, that thou didst know how many fathome deepe I am in [[love|loue]]: but it cannot bee sounded: my affection hath an vnknowne bottome, like the Bay of Portugall" (016-091) — reveals that her pedagogical coolness conceals a passion as deep as [[orlando|Orlando]]'s. The relationship is genuinely reciprocal: she teaches him to love wisely; he teaches her to love vulnerably. Their union at the play's end is the fruit of mutual education.

### Celia and Oliver: Sudden Conversion Love

The romance of [[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]] is the play's most compressed love plot — a courtship that occupies roughly a single scene. [[oliver|Oliver]], freshly converted from fratricide to brotherhood, encounters [[celia|Celia]] (as [[aliena|Aliena]]) and falls in love at first sight. [[celia|Celia]] reciprocates with equal suddenness. The speed of their union is deliberately jarring: where [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s love required five acts of testing and education, [[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]]'s requires only a shared glance.

This compressed courtship serves a specific structural function. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s capacity to love [[celia|Celia]] proves the sincerity of his conversion — the man who hated his brother without reason now loves a stranger without hesitation. [[celia|Celia]]'s capacity to love [[oliver|Oliver]] proves the play's argument that [[virtue-and-gentility|virtue]], once awakened, is immediately recognizable. Their marriage at [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]] is the swiftest and most surprising of the four unions, and it completes the pattern: the villain whose hatred set the plot in motion becomes a bridegroom whose love helps resolve it.

### Touchstone and Audrey: The Comic-Earthy Union

The courtship of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]] is the play's deliberate anti-romance — a relationship grounded in pragmatism, physical appetite, and comic resignation. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s proposal is couched in the language of cuckoldry: "As hornes are odious, they are necessarie" (013-031). [[audrey|Audrey]]'s acceptance is couched in monosyllables: "I am not a slut, though I thanke the Goddes I am foule" (013-026). Where [[orlando|Orlando]] pins verses on trees and [[silvius|Silvius]] suffers in Petrarchan agony, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] negotiates marriage as a practical transaction.

Yet Shakespeare grants this anti-romantic union the same blessing he grants the heroic ones. [[hymen|Hymen]] includes [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]] among the married couples at [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]], and his blessing — "[[marriage|Wedding]] is great Iunos crowne" (022-094) — extends to the fool and the goatherd as fully as to [[duke-senior|the duke]]'s daughter and the gentleman. The inclusion is theologically significant: comedy's grace is not conditional on eloquence, beauty, or romantic passion. The earthy union of [[wit]] and simplicity finds its place alongside the elevated union of [[wit]] and courage.

### Phebe and Silvius: The Petrarchan Parody

The relationship between [[phebe|Phebe]] and [[silvius|Silvius]] is the play's most sustained satire of Petrarchan love convention. [[silvius|Silvius]] is the archetypal suffering lover: he sighs, he writes, he endures contempt with masochistic dedication. His litany — "It is to be all made of sighes and teares... It is to be all made of faith and [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]]... All adoration, dutie, and obseruance, All humblenesse, all patience, and impatience" (020-037) — is the play's definitive anatomy of the Petrarchan formula, and its rhetorical excesses expose the [[pride|self-regard]] that underlies the pose of self-abnegation.

[[phebe|Phebe]] is the archetypal disdainful beloved — cold, cruel, and rhetorically sophisticated in her cruelty. Her demolition of [[silvius|Silvius]]'s conceit — "Thou tellst me there is murder in mine eye, 'Tis pretty sure, and very probable, That eyes that are the frailst, and softest things, Who shut their coward gates on atomyes, Should be called tyrants, butchers, murtherers" (015-012) — is intellectually formidable, and her refusal of pity ("But till that time Come not thou neere me" (015-035)) is morally decisive.

The comic reversal is immediate and devastating. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) berates [[phebe|Phebe]] for her cruelty, and [[phebe|Phebe]] falls instantly in love with the berater: "Sweet [[youth]], I pray you chide a yere together, I had rather here you chide, then this man wooe" (015-068). The cruel mistress becomes the abject supplicant, and her journey from [[scorn-and-disdain|disdain]] to humiliation is completed by her borrowed Marlowe couplet: "Dead Shepheard, now I find thy saw of might, Who euer lov'd, that lou'd not at first sight?" (015-085).

[[phebe|Phebe]]'s eventual marriage to [[silvius|Silvius]] is not a romantic triumph but a comic correction. She is trapped by her own rash promise — "That will I, should I die the houre after" (022-014) — and her capitulation ("I wil not eate my word, now thou art mine, Thy faith, my fancie to thee doth combine" (022-102)) is the acceptance of a contractual obligation, not the consummation of a passion. Shakespeare does not ask us to believe in their romantic happiness; he asks us to accept that comedy's machinery rewards the faithful and humbles the proud, whether or not love follows.

### The Four Weddings as Network Resolution

[[the-four-weddings|The four weddings]] of Act 5, Scene 4 are the point at which the play's entire relationship network converges into a single ceremonial moment. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] sheds her disguise, four couples are united, [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom is restored, and [[duke-fredericks-conversion|[[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s conversion]] is announced. The ceremony is presided over by [[hymen|Hymen]], the god of marriage, whose presence elevates the social ritual to cosmic event. The four couples represent the full range of the play's attitudes toward love — tested, sudden, pragmatic, and coerced — and their simultaneous blessing affirms that comedy's inclusiveness extends to every mode of human attachment.

## Friendship

The friendship bonds in As You Like It constitute a third relational domain — neither familial nor romantic — that provides the play's emotional infrastructure. Friendships enable the plot ([[touchstone|Touchstone]] accompanies the cousins to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]), sustain the [[persons|characters]] through adversity ([[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s lords follow him into [[freedom|exile]]), and offer moral counterpoint to the excesses of romance.

### Rosalind and Celia: "Dearer Than the Natural Bond of Sisters"

The friendship between [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] is the play's deepest emotional attachment — a bond that the text repeatedly emphasizes exceeds the claims of both blood and romance. [[celia|Celia]]'s offer to share her inheritance — "thou shalt be his heire; for what hee hath taken away from thy father perforce, I will render thee againe in affection" (002-006) — is the play's most radical statement of chosen loyalty over inherited obligation. When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is banished, [[celia|Celia]] does not hesitate: "To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]" (003-097). She abandons her father, her inheritance, and her safety — not for romantic love (that comes later, with [[oliver|Oliver]]) but for friendship.

The intimacy of their bond is established through the play's richest pattern of endearments. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s "O coz, coz, coz: my pretty little coz" (016-091) and [[celia|Celia]]'s affectionate exasperation — "I would sing my song without a burthen, thou bring'st me out of tune" (012-099) — reveal a relationship of mutual intelligence, shared humor, and the [[freedom]] to criticize without threatening the bond. Their friendship survives [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s absorption in romantic love: [[celia|Celia]] tolerates being relegated to audience and confidante, her occasional sharpness never hardening into resentment.

The relationship has been read by many critics as the play's true love story — a reading supported by the text's insistence that [[celia|Celia]]'s bond with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is "dearer than the natural bond of sisters." Whether read as friendship, sisterhood, or something more, the [[rosalind|Rosalind]]-[[celia|Celia]] bond is the play's emotional anchor, the relationship whose constancy makes all other relationships possible.

### Duke Senior and His Lords: The Fellowship of Exile

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s relationship with his attendant lords — particularly [[amiens|Amiens]] and [[jaques|Jaques]] — models a form of masculine fellowship grounded in shared adversity and philosophical conversation. The lords have "put themselues into voluntary [[freedom|exile]] with him" (001-041), and their loyalty is freely chosen rather than compelled. [[amiens|Amiens]]'s [[song-and-music|songs]] — "Under the greene wood tree" (005-001), "Blow, blow, thou winter winde" (010-111) — provide the soundtrack to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s philosophical life, transforming hardship into art.

[[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s relationship with [[jaques|Jaques]] is the fellowship's most complex element. [[jaques|Jaques]] challenges the Duke's pastoral optimism at every turn, and the Duke values the challenge: "I [[love|loue]] to cope him in these sullen fits, For then he's full of matter" (004-034). This is friendship as intellectual friction — the ruler who surrounds himself with dissent because he understands that wisdom requires it. [[jaques|Jaques]]'s departure at the play's end — choosing continued [[freedom|exile]] over restored prosperity — is a reminder that even the healthiest fellowship cannot accommodate every temperament. Comedy's inclusiveness, like [[jaques|Jaques]] himself, has limits.

### Touchstone as Companion

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s role as companion to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] differs from both the [[rosalind|Rosalind]]-[[celia|Celia]] bond and the Duke's fellowship with his lords. He is a court dependent — brought to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] because [[celia|Celia]] values his [[wit]] — and his relationship with the cousins is asymmetrical in both status and intimacy. Yet he provides a necessary service: his satiric intelligence deflates the earnestness that pastoral convention invites, and his presence reminds the audience that even the most idealistic comedy requires a fool to keep it honest.

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit]] complements [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s. Where [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s intelligence is directed toward love — testing, teaching, orchestrating — [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s is directed toward exposure — deflating pretension wherever he finds it. Together they form the play's most formidable satiric team, two intelligences whose shared delight in verbal play creates an alliance of [[wit]] that crosses the boundaries of rank and gender. That [[touchstone|Touchstone]] accompanies the cousins into [[freedom|exile]] and remains with them through the play's duration is a structural marker of his importance: the fool is not merely entertainment but an essential component of the community that comedy assembles.

## Court vs. Forest Realignment

The migration from [[duke-fredericks-court|court]] to [[forest-of-arden-place|forest]] is the play's single most important structural device, and its effect on the relationship network is transformative. The court is a space of hierarchy, fear, and fixed identities; [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] is a space of fluidity, [[freedom|liberty]], and experimental selfhood. Characters who move from one to the other find their relationships reconstituted under new conditions.

### The Court: Relationships Under Tyranny

At the court, relationships are governed by power. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] rules through decree and [[exile|banishment]]; his relationship with every other character is defined by the threat of violence. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s relationship with [[orlando|Orlando]] is defined by economic control and physical menace. Even the bond between [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] is shadowed by the knowledge that [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s favor is conditional. The court is a place where relationship is indistinguishable from subjection — where the natural bonds of brotherhood, friendship, and love are distorted by the artificial bonds of power.

The turning point comes with [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]]. [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s decree — "Mistris, dispatch you with your safest haste, And get you from our Court" (003-024) — is the act that triggers the mass exodus to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. Three [[persons|characters]] flee immediately ([[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[celia|Celia]], [[touchstone|Touchstone]]); [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[adam|Adam]] follow shortly after; [[oliver|Oliver]] pursues them; even [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] eventually marches toward [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], only to be converted at its edge. The court empties itself into [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], and the relationship network reconstitutes itself in a new space governed by different laws.

### The Forest: Relationships Reborn

In [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], relationships undergo a systematic transformation. The [[green-world|forest]]'s [[freedom|liberty]] permits the experiments that [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s rigidity forbade: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] can adopt a male disguise and tutor [[orlando|Orlando]] in love; [[orlando|Orlando]] can hang poems on trees; [[touchstone|Touchstone]] can court a goatherd; [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] can philosophize with his lords; even [[oliver|Oliver]] can be saved from a lioness and converted from villain to brother. The [[green-world|forest]] is the space of what critic C.L. Barber called "release" — a temporary suspension of social constraint that enables the [[regeneration|renewal]] of social bonds.

The transformation is not merely spatial but relational. Characters who were enemies at court become allies in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] ([[oliver|Oliver]] saves [[orlando|Orlando]]; [[orlando|Orlando]] saves [[oliver|Oliver]]). Characters who were strangers become lovers ([[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]]). Characters who were silent become eloquent ([[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s disguise liberates her [[wit]]). The [[green-world|forest]] does not erase relationships; it remakes them by stripping away the external markers of identity — rank, gender, wealth — and revealing the underlying dispositions that those markers obscure.

The symmetry of the realignment is one of the play's most striking formal features. Every major character who begins at the court ends in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], and every character who enters [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] is changed by it. The only exception is [[jaques|Jaques]], who leaves the restored court for deeper seclusion — a reminder that transformation, however comprehensive, is never universal.

## Servant-Master Relationships

The servant-master relationships in As You Like It constitute the network's most quietly radical dimension. In a play obsessed with hierarchy — who rules, who inherits, who marries whom — the servant figures consistently invert the expected order, offering models of loyalty, wisdom, and moral authority that exceed those of their social superiors.

### Adam and Orlando: The Servant as Father

[[adam|Adam]]'s relationship with [[orlando|Orlando]] is the play's most morally luminous bond across any social divide. The aged servant who offers his life savings to the young master — "Take that, and he that doth the Rauens feede, Yea prouidently caters for the Sparrow, Be comfort to my [[youth|age]]: here is the gold, All this I giue you, let me be your seruant" (006-040) — inverts the expected economic relationship: the servant funds the master. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s response — carrying the starving [[adam|Adam]] on his back into the Duke's camp and demanding food at sword-point — repays loyalty with loyalty, service with care. The feudal bond between master and servant is transformed into a mutual covenant of protection, and the old man who was dismissed as an "olde dogge" (001-029) by [[oliver|Oliver]] becomes the moral standard against which every other relationship in the play is measured.

### Corin and His Unseen Master

[[corin|Corin]]'s relationship with his absent master — "I am shepheard to another man, And do not sheere the Fleeces that I graze" (007-067) — introduces a note of economic realism into the pastoral idyll. [[corin|Corin]] is a hired laborer, not a landowner, and his inability to offer hospitality directly ("My master is of churlish disposition" (007-071)) reminds the audience that [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] is not, in fact, free from the hierarchies that structure the court. Yet [[corin|Corin]]'s response to his subordinate position is dignity, not resentment. His credo — "Sir, I am a true labourer, I earne that I eate, get that I weare; owe no man hate, enuie no mans happinesse: glad of other mens good, content with my harme" (012-034) — is a philosophy of self-sufficiency that requires no master's approval. [[corin|Corin]] serves, but he serves on terms that preserve his moral autonomy, and his simple wisdom withstands [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s elaborate [[wit]] precisely because it makes no claim that [[wit]] can expose.

### Touchstone and the Cousins: The Fool as Servant

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s relationship with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] occupies an ambiguous position in the servant-master spectrum. He is a court dependent, brought to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] at [[celia|Celia]]'s pleasure, and his function is to amuse. Yet his [[wit]] grants him a license that the ordinary servant lacks — [[touchstone|the fool]]'s motley is a form of epistemological immunity, permitting truths that would be dangerous from any other mouth. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] serves the cousins, but his service includes the [[freedom]] to satirize them, their lovers, and the pastoral conventions they inhabit. The fool who is a servant is also, paradoxically, the character most free to speak — a paradox that Shakespeare exploits throughout the play.

## The Network as Dramatic Argument

The relationship network of As You Like It is not merely a collection of character interactions but a dramatic argument about the nature of human connection. The play proposes that relationships are not fixed by birth, rank, or initial disposition — that they can be transformed by chosen loyalty ([[celia|Celia]]), mercy ([[orlando|Orlando]]), education ([[rosalind|Rosalind]]), and grace (the conversions of [[oliver|Oliver]] and [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]). The movement from the fractured court to the reconciled [[green-world|forest]] is a movement from relationship-as-power to relationship-as-gift — from bonds that constrain to bonds that liberate.

The four weddings that close the play are the network's visible resolution, but the deeper resolution is the transformation of the conditions that make relationship possible. The court that began the play as a space of envy, usurpation, and fear becomes — through the [[persons|characters]]' sojourn in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] and their return — a space of restored legitimacy and authentic connection. Shakespeare's comedy does not abolish hierarchy; it renews it by subjecting every relationship, from the ducal to the rustic, to [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s alchemy of transformation.
