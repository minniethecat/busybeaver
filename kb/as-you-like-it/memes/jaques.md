# Jaques

## Overview

Jaques (pronounced "JAY-kweez") is the melancholy philosopher of As You Like It. Unlike every other character in the play, he resists the pastoral cure and the comedic resolution: he weeps for wounded deer, anatomizes human folly, and finally chooses continued [[freedom|exile]] over the [[regeneration|restoration]] of courtly order. His "All the world's a stage" speech, mapping the [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] of human life from infancy to oblivion, is the play's most famous passage. Jaques represents a mode of intelligence — skeptical, detached, morally serious — that comedy cannot assimilate. His refusal to join the final celebration marks the limit of pastoral comedy's power to transform, and his choice of religious seclusion over communal [[joy-and-celebration|festivity]] makes him the most unsettling presence in a play otherwise devoted to harmony.

## The Melancholy Tradition

Jaques belongs to a well-established Renaissance type: the melancholy man. In the Galenic humoral theory that Shakespeare inherited, melancholy was associated with black bile, introspection, and exceptional intellectual capacity — it was the humor of scholars, philosophers, and poets. Robert Burton's *Anatomy of [[melancholy|Melancholy]]* (1621) would later codify the type, but Shakespeare had already explored melancholy figures in Don John (Much Ado [[About]] Nothing) and would return to it in Hamlet.

What distinguishes Jaques from other Shakespearean melancholics is the self-conscious theatricality of his condition. He does not merely suffer melancholy — he performs it, cultivates it, and extracts intellectual pleasure from it. His boast that he "can sucke melancholly out of a song, As a Weasel suckes egges" (008-007) reveals a gourmand's relationship to sorrow. [[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s description captures the paradox precisely: [[jaques|Jaques]]'s company is sought precisely for the "matter" his "sullen fits" produce (004-072). [[melancholy|Melancholy]], in Jaques's hands, becomes a mode of perception — a lens that reveals truths invisible to happier temperaments.

## Role in the Play

### Introduction Through Report (Act 2, Scene 1)

Jaques is introduced through a report rather than direct appearance — a technique Shakespeare uses to establish his distinctive voice before the audience meets him. The First Lord describes finding him weeping over a wounded stag:

> A poore sequestred Stag, That from the Hunters aime had tane a hurt, Did come to languish (004-037)

This tableau — the melancholy philosopher moralizing over animal suffering — establishes Jaques as the play's dissenting voice. Where the Duke finds "sermons in stones" (004-018), Jaques finds only matter for lamentation. The stag becomes a mirror for human suffering, and [[jaques|Jaques]]'s tears — whether genuine or performed — mark him as the character who refuses to look away from pain while others seek pleasure.

### The Philosophical Antagonist to Duke Senior

His relationship with [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] is one of affectionate antagonism. [[duke-senior|The Duke]] enjoys [[jaques|Jaques]]'s company precisely because of their disagreement: "I [[love|loue]] to cope him in these sullen fits, For then he's full of matter" (004-072). Their debates provide the play's philosophical substance, contrasting the Duke's optimistic pastoralism with Jaques's skeptical realism. [[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s "sweet are the uses of adversity" philosophy meets its counter-argument in Jaques's insistence that adversity produces not sweetness but continued suffering.

### The Seven Ages Speech (Act 2, Scene 7)

The "Seven Ages" speech is [[jaques|Jaques]]'s most sustained performance and the play's most famous passage. Delivered in response to [[adam|Adam]]'s suffering and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s desperation, it universalizes human life into a deterministic sequence: "All the world's a stage, And all the men and women, meerely Players; They haue their Exits and their Entrances, And one man in his time playes many parts, His Acts being seuen ages" (010-151).

The speech maps the human life cycle through seven archetypal roles: the infant "mewling, and puking in the Nurses armes," the "whining Schoole-boy," the "Louer, Sighing like Furnace," the "Soldier, Full of strange oaths," the "Iustice, In faire round belly," the "lean and slipper'd Pantaloone," and finally "second childishnesse, and meere obliuion, Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing" (010-151).

The speech is simultaneously profound and reductive. It offers genuine wisdom about the universality of human decline while denying individual agency — every life follows the same script, and every actor exits to the same oblivion. This pessimism is [[jaques|Jaques]]'s signature: where comedy multiplies possibilities, Jaques reduces them to a single, melancholy trajectory. The speech's rhetorical power — its relentless accumulation, its Latinate finality ("sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing") — makes it unforgettable, but its philosophical implications are deeply troubling for a comedy.

### Encounter with Touchstone (Act 5, Scene 4)

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s encounter with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] is a meeting of kindred but incompatible spirits. When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] delivers his anatomy of the "Lie seuen times remoued" (022-054) — the seven degrees from the Retort Courteous to the Lie Direct — Jaques is delighted: "Is not this a rare fellow my Lord? hee's as good at any thing, and yet a foole" (022-058). He recognizes [[touchstone|the fool]]'s intelligence but cannot share his comic detachment. Where [[touchstone|Touchstone]] uses [[wit]] to accommodate himself to the world's absurdities, Jaques uses intelligence to distance himself from them entirely. [[touchstone|The fool]]'s laughter and the philosopher's lament are opposed responses to the same perception of human folly.

### The Final Refusal

At the play's end, Jaques alone refuses the [[regeneration|restoration]]. While the four couples marry and the exiled court prepares to return, Jaques announces he will join the converted [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] in religious retirement: "out of these conuertites, There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd" (022-139). His final word — "matter" — is exquisitely chosen. It means both "subject matter" for continued philosophical reflection and "significance" that comedy has not provided. Jaques seeks not joy but understanding, not [[marriage|union]] but continued inquiry. His departure is the play's most honest acknowledgment that some temperaments are not healed by comedy.

## Jaques as the Unassimilated Outsider

Jaques functions as what critic C.L. Barber called a "release" figure — a character who expresses what the comic form must exclude to achieve its happy resolution. His melancholy, his moral seriousness, and his refusal to participate in the final celebration represent everything that pastoral comedy promises to cure but cannot. Shakespeare's genius is to give Jaques the last word before [[the-four-weddings|[[hymen|Hymen]]'s blessing]]: the philosopher exits to seek "matter" while the lovers exit to marriage. Neither perspective is privileged; the play holds both in suspension.

This structural position makes Jaques essential to the play's tonal complexity. Without him, As You Like It would risk sentimentality; with him, it earns its joy by acknowledging what that joy excludes. His presence — and his departure — are what make the final celebration feel earned rather than merely conventional.

## Key Scenes

### The Wounded Stag (Act 2, Scene 1)

The First Lord reports [[jaques|Jaques]]'s response to the [[death-and-mortality|dying]] deer:

> A poore sequestred Stag, That from the Hunters aime had tane a hurt, Did come to languish (004-037)

Jaques moralizes the stag's suffering as an indictment of human cruelty, finding in the animal's tears a mirror for the world's injustice. Through the First Lord's report, Jaques charges that the Duke and his men "Are meere vsurpers, tyrants, and whats worse" (004-065), turning the hunt into an indictment of all human predation.

### Melancholy and Music (Act 2, Scene 5)

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s response to [[amiens|Amiens]]'s song reveals his method of extracting sorrow from every source:

> Iaq. I can sucke melancholly out of a song, As a Weasel suckes egges (008-007)

His demand for "More, I prethee more" (008-006) is not a request for pleasure but for raw material — more "matter" for his melancholy to work upon. The song, which celebrates the pastoral life of "under the greene-wood tree," becomes in [[jaques|Jaques]]'s hearing an occasion for sadness rather than joy.

### Seven Ages of Man (Act 2, Scene 7)

> All the world's a stage, And all the men and women, meerely Players; They haue their Exits and their Entrances, And one man in his time playes many parts, His Acts being seuen ages. (010-151)

The [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] unfurl with relentless determinism: infant, schoolboy, lover, soldier, justice, pantaloon, and finally "second childishnesse, and meere obliuion, Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing." The speech is theatre's most famous memento mori.

### Pride and Satire (Act 2, Scene 7)

Before the Seven Ages speech, Jaques delivers a blistering critique of [[pride]]:

> Iaq. Why who cries out on pride, that can therein taxe any priuate party: doth it not flow as hugely as the Sea (010-073)

Jaques claims [[touchstone|the fool]]'s license — "I must haue [[freedom|liberty]]" — to "Cleanse the foule bodie of th' infected world" (010-063). [[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s objection, that Jaques himself has been "a Libertine, As sensuall as the brutish sting" (010-068), complicates the satire: the satirist is implicated in the vices he condemns.

### Meeting the Fool (Act 5, Scene 4)

Jaques delights in [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s anatomy of the lie: "Is not this a rare fellow my Lord?" (022-058). The moment reveals [[jaques|Jaques]]'s genuine appreciation for intelligence, even when it takes forms he cannot share. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s comic taxonomy of lies mirrors Jaques's own taxonomic impulse in the Seven Ages speech — both [[persons|characters]] anatomize human behavior, one through laughter and one through lament.

### Choosing Exile (Act 5, Scene 4)

> Out of these conuertites, There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd (022-139)

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s final speech is a refusal of the comedic resolution. He will not dance at weddings; he will seek "matter" — the raw substance of continued reflection — in religious solitude. The play's final image of Jaques is of a man walking away from celebration toward inquiry.

## Critical Reception

Jaques has attracted sustained critical attention as one of Shakespeare's most enigmatic creations. [[william|William]] Hazlitt praised him as "the only purely contemplative character in Shakespeare," while Harold Bloom argued that Jaques "represents something in Shakespeare that comedy cannot accommodate." Contemporary feminist criticism has noted that [[jaques|Jaques]]'s melancholy is a form of privileged masculine withdrawal — he can afford to refuse the social because others (particularly [[rosalind|Rosalind]]) have done the work of [[regeneration|restoration]].

## Relationships

- [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] — His patron and friendly antagonist; their debates provide the play's philosophical substance
- [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — Fellow satirist; Jaques envies [[touchstone|the fool]]'s license but cannot share his laughter
- [[orlando|Orlando]] — Jaques mocks his love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] and resists his charm; their mutual antipathy is the most direct in the play
- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — Their wits clash; she dismisses his melancholy as self-indulgence, recognizing it as a pose that serves male privilege
- [[amiens|Amiens]] — Jaques feeds on his [[song-and-music|songs]] as raw material for melancholy; "More, I prethee more"
- [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] — The converted usurper whom Jaques joins in religious retirement; their pairing at the end is one of the play's strangest ironies
