(003-001)Enter [[celia|Celia]] and [[rosalind|Rosaline]].

(003-002)Cel. Why Cosen, why Rosaline: Cupid haue mercie,

(003-003)Not a word?

(003-004)Ros. Not one to throw at a dog

(003-005)Cel. No, thy words are too precious to be cast away vpon curs, throw some of them at me; come lame mee with reasons

(003-006)Ros. Then there were two Cosens laid vp, when the one should be lam'd with reasons, and the other mad without any

(003-007)Cel. But is all this for your Father?

(003-008)Ros. No, some of it is for my childes Father: Oh

(003-009)how full of briers is this working day world

(003-010)Cel. They are but burs, Cosen, throwne vpon thee in holiday foolerie, if we walke not in the trodden paths our very petty-coates will catch them

(003-011)Ros. I could shake them off my coate, these burs are in my heart

(003-012)Cel. Hem them away

(003-013)Ros. I would try if I could cry hem, and haue him

(003-014)Cel. Come, come, wrastle with thy affections

(003-015)Ros. O they take the part of a better wrastler then my selfe

(003-016)Cel. O, a good wish vpon you: you will trie in time in dispight of a fall: but turning these iests out of [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]], let vs talke in good earnest: Is it possible on such a sodaine, you should fall into so strong a liking with old Sir Roulands yongest sonne? Ros. [[duke-senior|The Duke]] my Father lou'd his Father deerelie

(003-017)Cel. Doth it therefore ensue that you should [[love|loue]] his Sonne deerelie? By this kinde of chase, I should hate him, for my father hated his father deerely; yet I hate not [[orlando|Orlando]]

(003-018)Ros. No faith, hate him not for my sake

(003-019)Cel. Why should I not? doth he not deserue well?

(003-020)Enter Duke with Lords.

(003-021)Ros. Let me loue him for that, and do you loue him

(003-022)Because I doe. Looke, here comes the Duke

(003-023)Cel. With his eies full of anger

(003-024)Duk. Mistris, dispatch you with your safest haste,

(003-025)And get you from our Court

(003-026)Ros. Me Vncle

(003-027)Duk. You Cosen,

(003-028)Within these ten daies if that thou beest found

(003-029)So neere our publike Court as twentie miles,

(003-030)Thou diest for it

(003-031)Ros. I doe beseech your Grace

(003-032)Let me the knowledge of my fault beare with me:

(003-033)If with my selfe I hold intelligence,

(003-034)Or haue acquaintance with mine owne desires,

(003-035)If that I doe not dreame, or be not franticke,

(003-036)(As I doe trust I am not) then deere Vncle,

(003-037)Neuer so much as in a thought vnborne,

(003-038)Did I offend your highnesse

(003-039)Duk. Thus doe all Traitors,

(003-040)If their purgation did consist in words,

(003-041)They are as innocent as grace it selfe;

(003-042)Let is suffice thee that I trust thee not

(003-043)Ros. Yet your mistrust cannot make me a Traitor;

(003-044)Tell me whereon the likelihoods depends?

(003-045)Duk. Thou art thy Fathers daughter, there's enough

(003-046)Ros. So was I when your highnes took his Dukdome,

(003-047)So was I when your highnesse banisht him;

(003-048)Treason is not inherited my Lord,

(003-049)Or if we did deriue it from our friends,

(003-050)What's that to me, my Father was no Traitor,

(003-051)Then good my Leige, mistake me not so much,

(003-052)To thinke my pouertie is treacherous

(003-053)Cel. Deere Soueraigne heare me speake

(003-054)Duk. I Celia, we staid her for your sake,

(003-055)Else had she with her Father rang'd along

(003-056)Cel. I did not then intreat to haue her stay,

(003-057)It was your pleasure, and your owne remorse,

(003-058)I was too yong that time to value her,

(003-059)But now I know her: if she be a Traitor,

(003-060)Why so am I: we still haue slept together,

(003-061)Rose at an instant, learn'd, plaid, eate together,

(003-062)And wheresoere we went, like Iunos Swans,

(003-063)Still we went coupled and inseperable

(003-064)Duk. She is too subtile for thee, and her smoothnes;

(003-065)Her verie silence, and her patience,

(003-066)Speake to the people, and they pittie her:

(003-067)Thou art a foole, she robs thee of thy name,

(003-068)And thou wilt show more bright, & seem more vertuous

(003-069)When she is gone: then open not thy lips

(003-070)Firme, and irreuocable is my doombe,

(003-071)Which I haue past vpon her, she is banish'd

(003-072)Cel. Pronounce that sentence then on me my Leige,

(003-073)I cannot liue out of her companie

(003-074)Duk. You are a foole: you Neice prouide your selfe,

(003-075)If you out-stay the time, vpon mine [[honor]],

(003-076)And in the greatnesse of my word you die.

(003-077)Exit Duke, &c.

(003-078)Cel. O my poore Rosaline, whether wilt thou goe?

(003-079)Wilt thou change Fathers? I will giue thee mine:

(003-080)I charge thee be not thou more grieu'd then I am

(003-081)Ros. I haue more cause

(003-082)Cel. Thou hast not Cosen,

(003-083)Prethee be cheerefull; know'st thou not the Duke

(003-084)Hath banish'd me his daughter?

(003-085)Ros. That he hath not

(003-086)Cel. No, hath not? Rosaline lacks then the loue

(003-087)Which teacheth thee that thou and I am one,

(003-088)Shall we be sundred? shall we part sweete girle?

(003-089)No, let my Father seeke another heire:

(003-090)Therefore deuise with me how we may flie

(003-091)Whether to goe, and what to beare with vs,

(003-092)And doe not seeke to take your change vpon you,

(003-093)To beare your griefes your selfe, and leaue me out:

(003-094)For by this heauen, now at our sorrowes pale;

(003-095)Say what thou canst, Ile goe along with thee

(003-096)Ros. Why, whether shall we goe?

(003-097)Cel. To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]

(003-098)Ros. Alas, what danger will it be to vs,

(003-099)(Maides as we are) to trauell forth so farre?

(003-100)Beautie prouoketh theeues sooner then gold

(003-101)Cel. Ile put my selfe in poore and meane attire,

(003-102)And with a kinde of vmber smirch my face,

(003-103)The like doe you, so shall we passe along,

(003-104)And neuer stir assailants

(003-105)Ros. Were it not better,

(003-106)Because that I am more then common tall,

(003-107)That I did suite me all points like a man,

(003-108)A gallant curtelax vpon my thigh,

(003-109)A bore-speare in my hand, and in my heart

(003-110)Lye there what hidden womans feare there will,

(003-111)Weele haue a swashing and a marshall outside,

(003-112)As manie other mannish cowards haue,

(003-113)That doe outface it with their semblances

(003-114)Cel. What shall I call thee when thou art a man?

(003-115)Ros. Ile haue no worse a name then Ioues owne Page,

(003-116)And therefore looke you call me [[ganimed|Ganimed]].

(003-117)But what will you be call'd?

(003-118)Cel. Something that hath a reference to my state:

(003-119)No longer Celia, but [[aliena|Aliena]]

(003-120)Ros. But Cosen, what if we assaid to steale

(003-121)The clownish Foole out of your Fathers Court:

(003-122)Would he not be a comfort to our trauaile?

(003-123)Cel. Heele goe along ore the wide world with me,

(003-124)Leaue me alone to woe him; Let's away

(003-125)And get our Iewels and our wealth together,

(003-126)Deuise the fittest time, and safest way

(003-127)To hide vs from pursuite that will be made

(003-128)After my flight: now goe in we content

(003-129)To libertie, and not to [[exile|banishment]].

(003-130)Exeunt.
