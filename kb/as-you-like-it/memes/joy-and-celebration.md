# Joy and Celebration

## Definition

Joy (*ioy*) in As You Like It is both a dramatic destination (the promised resolution of comedy) and a conscious practice adopted by [[persons|characters]] in adversity. The play traces a movement from sorrow — [[orlando|Orlando]]'s dispossession, [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]], [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s [[freedom|exile]] — toward the collective joy of the four weddings that close the action. Joy is not merely the absence of suffering but an active choice: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] resolves to "forget the condition of my estate, to reioyce in yours" (002-005), establishing mirth as both a coping strategy and a moral achievement.

## In As You Like It

The play opens in a world deprived of joy. [[orlando|Orlando]] is kept "rustically" by his brother, denied the "gentleman-like qualities" of his birth (001-025). [[rosalind|Rosalind]] grieves her father's [[freedom|exile]]. The court under [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] is a place of fear and suspicion. Joy is only re-established in [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]].

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] explicitly thematizes joy early in the play. When [[celia|Celia]] urges her to be merry, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] answers: "Deere [[celia|Cellia]]; I show more mirth then I am mistresse of, and would you yet were merrier" (002-003). Her decision to "deuise sports" — including "falling in Loue" (002-007) — reframes the play's romantic plots as deliberate exercises in joy-making.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] celebrates the "sweet" uses of adversity in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], finding that [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] life is "more sweete / Then that of painted pompe" (004-004). His speech is a manifesto for joy through acceptance: smiling at the winter wind, calling it "no flattery" (004-012).

[[touchstone|Touchstone]], the fool, serves as an agent of mirth through satire. His deflations of pastoral pretension are themselves a form of joy — the comic perspective that refuses to take anything, including the pastoral ideal, too seriously.

The play's resolution is explicitly celebratory: "[[marriage|Wedding]] is great Iunos crowne" (022-094) announces [[hymen|Hymen]], and the four marriages join all the lovers in a ritual of communal joy. The play's closing promise — "Shal share the good of our returned fortune" (022-129) — links joy to the [[regeneration|restoration]] of justice.

## Key Passages

### [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Practice of Mirth (Act 1, Scene 2)

> Ros. Deere Cellia; I show more mirth then I am mistresse of, and would you yet were merrier (002-003)

> Ros. Well, I will forget the condition of my estate, to reioyce in yours (002-005)

### Falling in Love as Sport (Act 1, Scene 2)

> Ros. From henceforth I will Coz, and deuise sports: let me see, what thinke you of falling in Loue? (002-007)

### Wedding Joy (Act 5, Scene 4)

> Wedding is great Iunos crowne, O blessed bond of boord and bed: Tis Hymen peoples euerie towne, high wedlock then be honored (022-094)

### Restored Fortune (Act 5, Scene 4)

> Shal share the good of our returned fortune, According to the measure of their states (022-129)
