# Touchstone and [[audrey|Audrey]]'s Courtship

## Overview

Touchstone and Audrey's Courtship is the comic pastoral subplot of *As You Like It*, running through Acts 3 and 5. The court fool [[touchstone|Touchstone]], having followed [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] into [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], transfers his affections to [[audrey|Audrey]], a simple country wench. Their mismatched courtship — the witty courtier and the unlettered goatherd — provides a parodic counterpoint to the play's romantic lovers. Where [[orlando|Orlando]] idealizes [[rosalind|Rosalind]] in courtly verse, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] negotiates marriage with [[audrey|Audrey]] in blunt prose, culminating in a near-marriage by the hedge-priest Sir [[oliver|Oliver]] Martext and their eventual inclusion in the play's quadruple wedding.

## Dramatic Function

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s courtship of [[audrey|Audrey]] serves as Shakespeare's comic deflation of pastoral and romantic conventions. While [[orlando|Orlando]] writes execrable [[verse-and-poetry|love poems]] and hangs them on trees, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] dispenses with [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] entirely — his wooing is pragmatic, physical, and comically unsentimental. When [[jaques|Jaques]] urges him to delay his hasty marriage and "Get you to church, and haue a good Priest" (015-085), [[touchstone|Touchstone]] complies not from piety but from a desire to avoid a bad bargain.

The subplot also dramatizes the encounter between court sophistication and rural simplicity. [[audrey|Audrey]] cannot follow [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit|wordplay]], yet her plainness exposes the emptiness of his [[wit]]. [[william|William]], a rival country suitor, is dispatched by [[touchstone|Touchstone]] with a few intimidating words: "I will kill thee a hundred and fifty wayes" (020-030) — a miniature victory of court rhetoric over rustic sincerity that the play regards with characteristic ambivalence.

## Key Passages

### Touchstone on Marriage (Act 3, Scene 3)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] reflects on the nature of [[marriage|wedlock]] in his characteristically twisted logic:

> Pigeons bill, so wedlocke would be nibling (013-039)

### [[jaques|Jaques]]'s Intervention (Act 3, Scene 3)

[[jaques|Jaques]] persuades [[touchstone|Touchstone]] to seek a proper church wedding:

> Iaq. And wil you (being a man of your breeding) be married vnder a bush like a begger? Get you to church, and haue a good Priest (015-085)

### Dismissing William (Act 5, Scene 1)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] intimidates his rustic rival with elaborate threats:

> Clo. I will deale in poyson with thee, or in bastinado, or in steele: I will bandy with thee in faction; I will ore-run thee with police: I will kill thee a hundred and fifty wayes (020-030)

## Participants

- [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — The court fool, whose [[wit]] dominates the rustic [[audrey|Audrey]]
- [[audrey|Audrey]] — The country wench, whose simplicity deflates [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s sophistication
- [[jaques|Jaques]] — Persuades [[touchstone|Touchstone]] to delay the marriage, ensuring a proper ceremony
- Sir [[oliver|Oliver]] Martext — The hedge-priest whose questionable qualifications prompt [[jaques|Jaques]]'s intervention
- [[william|William]] — The rustic rival, dispatched with comic efficiency
