# Jaques de Boys's Arrival

The arrival of [[jaques-de-boys|Jaques de Boys]] in Act 5, Scene 4 of *As You Like It* delivers the news that resolves the play's political plot and enables its joyous conclusion. As the middle brother of [[oliver|Oliver]] and [[orlando|Orlando]], his appearance serves both as family reunion and as the bearer of tidings that restore the rightful order.

## The Messenger's News

Jaques de Boys arrives during the wedding festivities to deliver a report that completes the play's resolution. He announces that [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], having marched into the Forest of Arden with a mighty army intending to capture and kill his exiled brother, has instead undergone a sudden conversion:

> "Duke Frederick, hearing how that every day / Men of great worth resorted to this forest, / Addressed a mighty power, which were on foot / In his own conduct, purposely to take / His brother here and put him to the sword."

The military threat that has loomed over the pastoral world throughout the play dissolves in an instant. Duke Frederick, meeting an old religious man on the forest's edge, is converted — renouncing his worldly ambition and restoring all lands and revenues to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and his followers.

## Dramatic Function

This late arrival of news is a classic Shakespearean device — the messenger who resolves outstanding plot threads through report rather than staged action. The conversion of Duke Frederick cannot be dramatized directly because it would compete with the wedding celebrations for theatrical focus. Instead, Shakespeare delivers it through Jaques de Boys's testimony.

The timing is precise. The news arrives after [[rosalind|Rosalind]] has shed her [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise, after [[hymen|Hymen]] has blessed the four marriages, and after the play's romantic and comic energies have reached their peak. The political resolution is subordinated to the romantic one, arriving as confirmation rather than climax.

## The Restoration

Jaques de Boys's news effects the complete restoration of the pre-play order — but transformed by the forest experience. Duke Senior regains his dukedom, the exiles may return to court, and the brothers de Boys are reunited. The news also provides the occasion for the one character who refuses the happy ending: [[jaques|Jaques]] the melancholy philosopher chooses to join the converted Duke Frederick in religious contemplation rather than return to court.

## Family Reunion

For the de Boys family, Jaques de Boys's arrival completes the reunion that began with [[olivers-conversion|Oliver's conversion]]. The three brothers — Oliver (reformed), Orlando (heroic), and Jaques de Boys (the messenger) — are united for the first time in the play. This fraternal harmony mirrors the restored political order and the multiple marriages, reinforcing the play's theme of reconciliation at every level of its narrative architecture.
