# Female Friendship

The friendship between [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] is the emotional anchor of *As You Like It* — a bond so profound that Celia abandons her father, her court, and her identity to accompany Rosalind into exile. Their relationship provides the play's most sustained depiction of loyalty, intimacy, and the transformative power of love in its non-romantic form.

## "Dearer Than the Natural Bond of Sisters"

The play establishes the depth of Rosalind and Celia's friendship in its opening scenes at [[duke-fredericks-court|Duke Frederick's court]]. Celia, the daughter of the usurping duke, and Rosalind, the daughter of the exiled duke, have grown up together and are inseparable:

> "For his child, that's in my custody, I do love her as myself."

Celia's love for Rosalind transcends the political enmity between their fathers. When [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] banishes Rosalind, Celia does not hesitate. She renounces her position as heir to the dukedom and pledges to share Rosalind's exile:

> "Prithee, be cheerful. Know'st thou not the duke / Hath banished me, his daughter? ... Shall we be sundered, shall we part, sweet girl? / No, let my father seek another heir."

## The Flight to Arden

The friends' flight to the Forest of Arden marks the play's decisive movement from court to green world. Celia proposes the plan, renames herself [[aliena|Aliena]] ("the stranger" or "the alienated one"), and insists that Rosalind adopt the disguise of [[ganimed|Ganymede]]. The [[disguise-plan|disguise plan]] is an act of mutual protection and shared adventure:

> "Now go we in content / To liberty, and not to banishment."

Celia reframes exile as liberation — a radical redefinition possible only because the friends face it together. What would be solitary banishment becomes a joint venture into freedom and self-discovery.

## Testing the Bond

The friendship is tested by the arrival of romantic love. When Rosalind falls in love with [[orlando|Orlando]] and embarks on the [[the-mock-courtship|mock courtship]], Celia's role shifts from co-conspirator to amused and sometimes exasperated observer. She teases Rosalind mercilessly about her lovesickness but never wavers in her support:

> "You have simply misused our sex in your love-prate. We must have your doublet and hose plucked over your head and show the world what the bird hath done to her own nest."

Celia's teasing voice is the play's sharpest critical perspective on Rosalind's romantic excess — a perspective possible only because of the intimacy between them. She can puncture Rosalind's pretensions precisely because she is the one person who knows Rosalind completely.

## The Transition to Marriage

The play's conclusion raises a question it does not fully answer: what happens to female friendship when its participants marry? Celia's sudden love for [[oliver|Oliver]] — the reformed elder brother — has struck many readers as a convenient rather than convincing resolution. But within the play's comic logic, the simultaneous marriages ensure that Rosalind and Celia will remain sisters (by marriage to brothers) — their bond institutionalized within the restored social order.

The epilogue, spoken by Rosalind, does not mention Celia directly. Yet the play's deepest emotional truth may be that Rosalind's journey through disguise, courtship, and self-discovery has been possible only because she never faced it alone.
