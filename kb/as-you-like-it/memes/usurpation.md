## Definition

Usurpation — the wrongful seizure of power — is the political act that sets As You Like It in motion. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] has deposed his elder brother [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and seized the dukedom, forcing the rightful duke into [[freedom|exile]] in [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. This act of fraternal-political betrayal mirrors and amplifies the personal sibling rivalry between [[oliver|Oliver]] and [[orlando|Orlando]], establishing usurpation as both a political crime and a violation of natural order. The play's movement toward [[regeneration|restoration]] — both personal and political — constitutes its moral arc.

## In As You Like It

The usurpation is reported early, through [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler's matter-of-fact account of court news:

> The old Duke is banished by his yonger brother the new Duke, and three or foure louing Lords haue put themselues into voluntary exile with him, whose lands and reuenues enrich the new Duke (001-041)

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s tyranny extends beyond his brother. He banishes [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — his own niece — on a whim, declaring:

> Mistresse, dispatch you with your safest haste, and get you from our Court (003-024)

His capricious cruelty creates the circumstances that drive [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] into [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], setting the pastoral plot in motion.

The [[green-world|forest]] exiles form a counter-court around [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], who transforms dispossession into philosophical reflection. His speeches celebrate the "sweet" uses of adversity, converting political loss into spiritual gain — a characteristic Renaissance response to the problem of tyranny.

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s treatment of [[orlando|Orlando]] functions as a domestic echo of usurpation: the elder brother "barres mee the place of a brother" just as [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] bars his brother from his throne. [[celia|Celia]]'s description ties the two usurpations together:

> From tyrant Duke, vnto a tyrant Brother (002-193)

Resolution comes through conversion rather than conquest. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], marching to destroy [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] exiles, meets "an old religious man" and is converted, restoring the dukedom to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]. This off-stage resolution — grace rather than justice — completes the play's movement from usurpation to [[regeneration|restoration]], from tyranny to legitimate rule.
