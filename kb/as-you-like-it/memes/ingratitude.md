## Definition

Ingratitude in *As You Like It* functions as the moral counterweight to the play's celebrations of
friendship, loyalty, and love. Shakespeare establishes it as a defining human failing — more painful
than nature's harshest elements — through [[amiens|Amiens]]' song at the feast in Act 2, and reinforces it
through the play's structure of usurpation and betrayal. The concept is particularly resonant in a
play where so many [[persons|characters]] have been displaced by the ingratitude of those they served: [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]
by his brother [[duke-frederick|Frederick]], [[orlando|Orlando]] by his brother [[oliver|Oliver]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]] by her uncle.

## In As You Like It

[[amiens|Amiens]]' song at the exiled Duke's [[green-world|forest]] feast forms the play's most concentrated statement on the
theme: "Blow, blow, thou winter winde, / Thou art not so vnkinde, as mans ingratitude" [010-185–186].
The song develops a systematic comparison between natural and human unkindness — "Thy tooth is not so
keene, because thou art not seene" (010-187), "Freize, freize, thou bitter skie that dost not bight
so nigh / as benefitts forgot" [010-193–194]. The refrain "This Life is most iolly" (010-192) cuts
against the song's content, producing a complex tonal effect: the cheerfulness of the musical form
both mocks and mitigates the bitterness of the text.

The second stanza sharpens the distinction: "Though thou the waters warpe, thy sting is not so sharpe,
/ as freind remembred not" [010-195–196]. The forgotten friend — a figure directly evoking [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s
relationship with his usurping brother [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] — crystallises ingratitude not as an abstract vice
but as a specific social wound. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s own situation with [[oliver|Oliver]], and [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]] by
her uncle, replicate this pattern. The play's resolution, in which all wrongs are forgiven in the
[[green-world|forest]], can thus be read as an answer to the song's implicit question: if ingratitude is the
defining human failing, can the pastoral world provide a cure?
