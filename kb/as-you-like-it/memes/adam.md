# Adam

## Character Overview

Adam is the aged servant of the de Boys household, having served Sir Rowland de Boys and now his youngest son, [[orlando|Orlando]]. He embodies the ideal of faithful service in a world where such [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] has grown rare. At "almost fourescore" (006-073), Adam is the play's moral anchor — his selfless generosity and unwavering [[loyalty-and-service|loyalty]] expose by contrast the betrayal, ingratitude, and self-interest that dominate the court. His journey from [[olivers-estate|the de Boys estate]] to the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] traces a physical and spiritual pilgrimage: the old man who begins as a voice of wounded dignity becomes, through his offering of "fiue hundred Crownes" (006-040), the play's purest exemplar of [[virtue-and-gentility|virtue]].

## Loyalty to the de Boys Family

[[adam|Adam]]'s loyalty is generational. He served Sir Rowland de Boys, and his [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] now transfers to [[orlando|Orlando]], the son who most resembles the father in spirit. When [[oliver|Oliver]] dismisses Adam with contempt, Adam's reply invokes the memory of the dead master as a moral standard:

> **Adam.** Is old dogge my reward: most true, I haue lost my teeth in your seruice: God be with my olde master, he would not haue spoke such a word.
> (001-029)

This single line performs multiple functions. It records [[adam|Adam]]'s decades of service ("lost my teeth in your [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]]"), contrasts [[oliver|Oliver]]'s cruelty with Sir Rowland's decency, and establishes Adam himself as a living repository of the de Boys family's better values. When [[orlando|Orlando]] later praises Adam's "constant [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]] of the antique world" (006-059), he elevates the old servant into an emblem of a vanishing ethical order — one in which service was given "for dutie, not for meede" (006-060).

Adam also functions as the play's earliest warning system. In Act 2, Scene 3, he intercepts [[orlando|Orlando]] before he enters the house, revealing [[oliver|Oliver]]'s plot to burn his brother alive:

> **Adam.** O vnhappie youth, / Come not within these doores: within this roofe / The enemie of all your graces liues / Your brother... Hath heard your praises, and this night he meanes, / To burne the lodging where you vse to lye, / And you within it
> (006-018)

His vigilance and courage — eavesdropping on his own master to save the younger — make him not merely a passive emblem of loyalty but an active agent of the plot.

## The "Five Hundred Crowns" Speech

The heart of [[adam|Adam]]'s role is his offer of his life savings to [[orlando|Orlando]] in Act 2, Scene 3. When [[orlando|Orlando]] resolves to face his brother's "diuerted blood" (006-039) rather than flee into destitution, Adam produces the gold he has saved over a lifetime of thrift:

> **Ad.** But do not so: I haue fiue hundred Crownes, / The thriftie hire I saued vnder your Father, / Which I did store to be my foster Nurse, / When seruice should in my old limbs lie lame, / And vnregarded age in corners throwne, / Take that, and he that doth the Rauens feede, / Yea prouidently caters for the Sparrow, / Be comfort to my age: here is the gold, / All this I giue you, let me be your seruant
> (006-040)

The speech is remarkable on several counts. First, Adam surrenders his entire material security — the "foster Nurse" (006-042) meant to sustain him in helpless old [[youth|age]] — trusting instead in divine providence: "he that doth the Rauens feede, / Yea prouidently caters for the Sparrow" (006-045). The biblical echoes (Matthew 6:26, Luke 12:24) elevate the transaction from prudent retirement planning to an act of radical faith.

Second, the gift reverses the expected economic relationship: the servant provides for the master, just as the father should have provided for the son but did not. [[adam|Adam]]'s gold fills the void left by Sir Rowland's death and [[oliver|Oliver]]'s betrayal.

Third, [[adam|Adam]]'s stoic vigor — "Though I looke old, yet I am strong and lustie" (006-049) — refutes the conventional association of old [[youth|age]] with helplessness. He attributes his health to a temperate [[youth]]: "For in my [[youth]] I neuer did apply / Hot, and rebellious liquors in my bloud" (006-050). His "lustie winter, / Frostie, but kindely" (006-054) is the play's most hopeful image of aging.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s response confirms the speech's moral weight:

> **Orl.** Oh good old man, how well in thee appeares / The constant seruice of the antique world, / When seruice sweate for dutie, not for meede: / Thou art not for the fashion of these times, / Where none will sweate, but for promotion
> (006-058)

## Journey to Arden

[[adam|Adam]]'s physical journey — "From seauentie yeeres, till now almost fourescore / Here liued I, but now liue here no more" (006-073) — is the play's most literal enactment of [[freedom|exile]] as a break with the past. At nearly eighty, having spent his entire adult life in one household, Adam follows his young master into an unknown wilderness.

His farewell couplet — "Yet fortune cannot recompence me better / Then to die well, and not my Masters debter" (006-077) — defines a life's purpose in terms of discharged obligation. It is the antithesis of every other character's relationship to [[fortune|Fortune]]: where others lament [[fortune|Fortune]]'s whims or mock her blindness, Adam treats her as a debtor whose only acceptable payment is a good death in loyal service.

The journey's cost becomes visible in Act 2, Scene 6. In the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], Adam collapses from hunger:

> **Adam.** Deere Master, I can go no further: / O I die for food. Heere lie I downe, / And measure out my graue. Farwel kinde master
> (009-003)

The image of the old man lying down to "measure out my graue" (009-004) literalizes the mortal cost of loyalty. [[adam|Adam]]'s body, not just his gold, has been spent in [[orlando|Orlando]]'s service.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s response — carrying Adam on his back "to some shelter" (009-018), then demanding food at sword-point from [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s banquet — is his finest moral moment. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s description of Adam to the Duke — "There is an old poore man, / Who after me, hath many a weary steppe / Limpt in pure [[love|loue]]" (010-135) — is the play's most moving tribute to its humblest character.

When [[orlando|Orlando]] returns carrying Adam (stage direction: "Enter [[orlando|Orlando]] with Adam" (010-175)), [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] greets him with courtly tenderness: "Welcome: set downe your venerable burthen, / and let him feede" (010-176). [[adam|Adam]]'s single line after being fed — "So had you neede, / I scarce can speake to thanke you for my selfe" (010-179) — is his last spoken word in the play. His survival and [[regeneration|restoration]], confirmed by the Duke's "Good old man, / Thou art right welcome" (010-204), quietly fulfills the play's pattern of [[regeneration|regeneration]].

## Dramatic Function

Adam serves several interconnected dramatic purposes.

**Foil to [[oliver|Oliver]]'s cruelty.** Where [[oliver|Oliver]] withholds his brother's inheritance, Adam gives his entire life savings. Where [[oliver|Oliver]] calls his brother "villaine" (001-022), Adam calls him "my gentle master" (006-003). Where [[oliver|Oliver]] plots murder, Adam offers his own life in service. The contrast is so stark that it requires no commentary — [[adam|Adam]]'s mere presence on stage supplies the moral standard by which [[oliver|Oliver]] is measured and found wanting.

**Embodiment of the "antique world."** [[orlando|Orlando]]'s praise of [[adam|Adam]]'s "constant [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]] of the antique world" (006-059) locates the old servant in a mythic past where loyalty preceded self-interest. Adam's values — duty, thrift, temperance, faith — belong to an older ethical economy that the play simultaneously mourns and celebrates. His survival into the [[forest-of-arden-place|forest]] world suggests that these virtues are not extinct but merely displaced, waiting to be recovered in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s alternative social order.

**Catalyst for [[orlando|Orlando]]'s moral education.** By carrying Adam through [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] and demanding food for him, [[orlando|Orlando]] learns the compassion that will later enable him to save [[oliver|Oliver]] from the lioness. Adam is the first dependent creature [[orlando|Orlando]] protects; the pattern of care established here — self-sacrificing protection of the vulnerable — becomes the template for his later, more consequential act of mercy.

**Living refutation of [[jaques|Jaques]]' pessimism.** [[jaques|Jaques]]' "Seven Ages of Man" speech (010-147) concludes with old [[youth|age]] as "second childishnesse, and meere obliuion, / Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing" (010-165). But Adam — who has lost his teeth "in your [[loyalty-and-service|seruice]]" (001-029) — is anything but oblivion. He is alert, courageous, generous, and ultimately saved by the community that [[jaques|Jaques]] so relentlessly mocks. [[adam|Adam]]'s survival, welcomed at [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s table, offers a living counter-argument to [[jaques|Jaques]]' grim taxonomy.
