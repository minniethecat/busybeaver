## Summary

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s flight to [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is the pivotal event that transfers the play's action from the corrupt court to the pastoral [[forest-of-arden|green world]]. After [[orlando|Orlando]] defeats [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler, his elder brother [[oliver|Oliver]] — enraged by [[orlando|Orlando]]'s growing popularity — plots to murder him. [[adam|Adam]], the elderly servant who served their father Sir Rowland de Boys, overhears the plot and warns [[orlando|Orlando]], offering his life savings to fund their escape. The two flee together to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], where they are eventually received by the exiled [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]].

## Dramatic Significance

This event serves three critical narrative functions. First, it completes [[orlando|Orlando]]'s separation from the oppressive court world, mirroring [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]] in the same act. Second, it establishes the servant [[adam|Adam]] as a figure of loyalty and sacrifice — a counterpoint to the brotherly betrayal [[orlando|Orlando]] suffers. Third, it brings the play's romantic hero into [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], setting the stage for his reunion with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and his integration into [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s exiled community. The flight marks the structural pivot from Act 2's "[[exile|expulsion]]" phase into the pastoral comedy that dominates Acts 3–4.

## Context

The immediate catalyst is [[oliver|Oliver]]'s jealousy. After [[wrestling-match|Orlando's victory]] over [[charles|Charles]] (see [[wrestling-match]]), [[oliver|Oliver]]'s hatred intensifies from neglect to murderous intent. [[adam|Adam]] — who earlier in Act 1 Scene 1 lamented [[orlando|Orlando]]'s mistreatment — proves his loyalty by revealing the plot:

> "This night he meanes, to burne the lodging where you vse to lye, and you within it: if he faile of that he will haue other meanes to cut you off" (006-024)

[[orlando|Orlando]] initially hesitates, unwilling to turn to banditry on the road. [[adam|Adam]] offers his five hundred crowns — his life savings — and pledges to serve [[orlando|Orlando]] in [[freedom|exile]], transforming the flight from mere escape into an act of intergenerational [[loyalty-and-service|fidelity]].

The flight parallels [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]] (see [[rosalinds-banishment]]), which occurs in the same act. Both [[persons|characters]] are driven from court by tyrannical authority — [[rosalind|Rosalind]] by [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s decree, [[orlando|Orlando]] by [[oliver|Oliver]]'s murderous envy. Their parallel journeys to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] create the dramatic conditions for the pastoral comedy that follows.

## Key Quotations

> "Know you not Master, to seeme kinde of men, their graces serue them but as enemies, no more doe yours: your vertues gentle Master are sanctified and holy traitors to you" (006-011)

[[adam|Adam]]'s warning is notable for its theological language — "sanctified and holy traitors" — which frames [[orlando|Orlando]]'s virtues as paradoxically dangerous in a fallen world. This moral vocabulary distinguishes [[adam|Adam]]'s speech from mere servant discourse and aligns him with the play's broader meditation on art and nature.

> "Why whether Adam would'st thou haue me go?... What, would'st thou haue me go & beg my food, or with a base and boistrous Sword enforce a theeuish liuing on the common rode?" (006-031)

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s refusal to become a highwayman — even in extremis — marks his moral integrity and distinguishes his [[freedom|exile]] from mere outlawry. This moment defines his character as fundamentally noble, regardless of his treatment at court.

> "After my flight: now goe in we content" (003-128)

This brief line, spoken by [[rosalind|Rosalind]] after her own [[exile|banishment]] decree, links her situation to [[orlando|Orlando]]'s. The shared language of "flight" underscores the structural parallelism between the play's two protagonists.
