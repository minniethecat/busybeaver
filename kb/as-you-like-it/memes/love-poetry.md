## Definition

[[love|Love]] [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] in *As You Like It* refers to the ritualised practice of composing, displaying, and reciting verses
as a vehicle for romantic courtship and pastoral performance. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s tree-hung verses to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] form the
central engine of Act 3's romantic plot, while the [[persons|characters]]' reactions to these verses — mockery, admiration,
and critical parsing — dramatise competing attitudes toward love itself. The motif belongs to a broader
Renaissance tradition of Petrarchan love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]], which Shakespeare both deploys and satirises.

## In As You Like It

[[orlando|Orlando]] first enters [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] declaring "Hang there my verse, in witnesse of my [[love|loue]]" (012-002),
inaugurating a sequence in which trees become the medium for his declarations. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] discovers the verses
and, still disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], interrogates [[celia|Celia]] about their author: "Didst thou heare these verses?"
(012-053). The physical act of hanging [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] on hawthorns literalises the pastoral convention of
writing love upon the landscape.

[[celia|Celia]] immediately subjects the verses to prosodic scrutiny — "the feet might beare y verses" (012-057) —
prompting [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s witty rejoinder that "the feet were lame, and could not beare themselues without the verse"
(012-058). This exchange turns poetic criticism into a form of flirtation, establishing that the quality of the
verse matters less than the act of writing it. [[orlando|Orlando]] himself later pleads "I pray you marre no moe of my
verses with reading them ill-fauouredly" (012-114), revealing the vulnerability that accompanies public
self-expression through [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]].

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] offers the sharpest satire in prose: "When a mans verses cannot be vnderstood, nor a mans good [[wit]]
seconded with the forward childe, vnderstanding: it strikes a man more dead then a great reckoning in a little
roome" (013-010). His mockery — "truly, I would the Gods hadde made thee poeticall" — cuts to the heart of the
motif's comic tension: verse-making is both the noblest expression of love and the most ridiculous. [[celia|Celia]]
ultimately endorses the whole enterprise when she tells [[rosalind|Rosalind]] of [[orlando|Orlando]]: "hee writes braue verses,
speakes braue words, sweares braue oathes" (014-029), confirming that in the pastoral logic of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]],
poetic fluency functions as proof of romantic sincerity.
