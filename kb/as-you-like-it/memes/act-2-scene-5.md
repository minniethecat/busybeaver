(008-001)Enter, Amyens, [[jaques|Iaques]], & others.

(008-002)Song.

(008-003)Vnder the greene wood tree, who loues to lye with mee, And turne his merrie Note, vnto the sweet Birds throte: Come hither, come hither, come hither: Heere shall he see no enemie, But Winter and rough Weather

(008-004)Iaq. More, more, I pre'thee more

(008-005)Amy. It will make you melancholly Monsieur Iaques

(008-006)Iaq. I thanke it: More, I prethee more,

(008-007)I can sucke melancholly out of a song,

(008-008)As a Weazel suckes egges: More, I pre'thee more

(008-009)Amy. My voice is ragged, I know I cannot please

(008-010)you

(008-011)Iaq. I do not desire you to please me,

(008-012)I do desire you to sing:

(008-013)Come, more, another stanzo: Cal you 'em stanzo's?

(008-014)Amy. What you wil Monsieur Iaques

(008-015)Iaq. Nay, I care not for their names, they owe mee

(008-016)nothing. Wil you sing?

(008-017)Amy. More at your request, then to please my selfe

(008-018)Iaq. Well then, if euer I thanke any man, Ile thanke you: but that they cal complement is like th' encounter of two dog-Apes. And when a man thankes me hartily, me thinkes I haue giuen him a penie, and he renders me the beggerly thankes. Come sing; and you that wil not hold your tongues

(008-019)Amy. Wel, Ile end the song. Sirs, couer the while, the Duke wil drinke vnder this tree; he hath bin all this day to looke you

(008-020)Iaq. And I haue bin all this day to auoid him:

(008-021)He is too disputeable for my companie:

(008-022)I thinke of as many matters as he, but I giue

(008-023)Heauen thankes, and make no boast of them.

(008-024)Come, warble, come.

(008-025)Song. Altogether heere.

(008-026)Who doth ambition shunne, and loues to liue i'th Sunne: Seeking the food he eates, and pleas'd with what he gets: Come hither, come hither, come hither, Heere shall he see. &c

(008-027)Iaq. Ile giue you a verse to this note,

(008-028)That I made yesterday in despight of my Inuention

(008-029)Amy. And Ile sing it

(008-030)Amy. Thus it goes.

(008-031)If it do come to passe, that any man turne Asse:

(008-032)Leauing his wealth and ease,

(008-033)A stubborne will to please,

(008-034)Ducdame, ducdame, ducdame:

(008-035)Heere shall he see, grosse fooles as he,

(008-036)And if he will come to me

(008-037)Amy. What's that Ducdame?

(008-038)Iaq. 'Tis a Greeke inuocation, to call fools into a circle.

(008-039)Ile go sleepe if I can: if I cannot, Ile raile against all

(008-040)the first borne of Egypt

(008-041)Amy. And Ile go seeke the Duke,

(008-042)His banket is prepar'd.

(008-043)Exeunt.
