# Wonder and Marvel

## Definition

Wonder in *As You Like It* is the affective response to events that exceed
ordinary expectation — the amazement provoked by love, by coincidence, by
the sheer improbability of the play's pastoral world. In the Renaissance
philosophical tradition, wonder (Latin *admiratio*) was understood as the
passion aroused by the unfamiliar or the extraordinary, and it was
considered essential to both philosophical inquiry and theatrical pleasure.
Shakespeare draws on this tradition throughout the play, using wonder both
as a character's spontaneous reaction and as a structural principle of the
comic plot.

## In As You Like It

The play's most concentrated expression of wonder comes from [[celia|Celia]] in Act 3,
Scene 2. Upon discovering [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s name carved into the trees of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] and
realising that [[orlando|Orlando]] — the very man [[rosalind|Rosalind]] loves — is also in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]],
[[celia|Celia]] bursts out: "O wonderfull, wonderfull, and most wonderfull wonderfull,
and yet againe wonderfull, and after that out of all whooping" (012-072).
The repetition of "wonderful" — five times in one breath — conveys an
experience of delighted astonishment that borders on the inexpressible.
The moment is comic, but beneath the comedy lies the play's serious
recognition that love operates through coincidence and surprise rather than
rational design.

The wonder theme extends beyond [[celia|Celia]]'s exclamation. When [[orlando|Orlando]], in the
play's final movement, discovers that [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is [[rosalind|Rosalind]], he responds
with wonder rather than mere recognition: "If there be truth in sight, you
are my [[rosalind|Rosalind]]" (020-016). The conditional phrasing — "if there be truth
in sight" — registers the epistemological vertigo that wonder produces:
what he sees seems too extraordinary to be true. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s delayed
acceptance echoes Renaissance theories of wonder as a passion that suspends
judgement, creating a pause between perception and belief.

At the play's climax, [[hymen|Hymen]]'s appearance as a deity to preside over the
four weddings intensifies the wonder motif to the level of the marvellous.
The god's entrance is unexplained and apparently miraculous — a rupture
in the play's otherwise secular pastoral world. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s epilogue,
delivered directly to the audience, extends wonder into the theatrical
frame itself: the boy actor's revelation of his true gender ("If I were
a Woman") produces in the audience the same vertiginous wonder that the
[[persons|characters]] experience on stage.

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s speech on the [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] of man provides the philosophical
counterpoint to wonder. Where [[celia|Celia]]'s wonder responds to joyful
coincidence, [[jaques|Jaques]]'s vision of life as a procession of predetermined
roles offers the opposite: a world without wonder, where "one man in his
time playes many parts" and nothing exceeds expectation. The play's
structural argument favours wonder: the marriages, the reconciliations,
and the pastoral transformations all affirm that life contains more than
[[jaques|Jaques]]'s melancholy philosophy can account for.

> The pastoral wonder Celia voices — "O wonderful, wonderful!" — is
> Shakespeare's recognition that the comic plot depends on the audience's
> willingness to be amazed. Wonder, in *As You Like It*, is both a theme
> and a theatrical strategy: the play asks us to suspend our disbelief
> and marvel at the transformations love can work.
