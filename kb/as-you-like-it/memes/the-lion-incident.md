# The Lion Incident

The lion incident in Act 4, Scene 3 of *As You Like It* marks the turning point in the fraternal conflict between Oliver and Orlando. Orlando discovers his elder brother sleeping beneath an old oak tree in the Forest of Arden with a "green and gilded snake" coiled near his neck and a hungry lioness crouching nearby. Despite Oliver having plotted his death and driven him from his inheritance, Orlando twice turns back to save his brother.

## The Serpent and the Lioness

[[oliver|Oliver]] tells the story to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] after being rescued, describing the double threat he faced while sleeping in the forest:

> "A wretched ragged man, o'ergrown with hair, / Lay sleeping on his back; about his neck / A green and gilded snake had wreathed itself, / Who with her head, nimble in threats, approached / The opening of his mouth."

The snake represents a hidden, insidious danger — fitting for the secret plotting that has characterized Oliver's treatment of Orlando. Found asleep in Arden after being sent by [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] to capture his brother, Oliver is utterly vulnerable.

> "But suddenly, seeing Orlando, it unlinked itself / And with indented glides did slip away / Into a bush."

The serpent retreats at [[orlando|Orlando]]'s approach, but the greater danger remains — a lioness "with udders all drawn dry" watching for her prey.

## Orlando's Moral Struggle

Orlando faces an intense internal conflict upon recognizing his sleeping brother. His first instinct, as he later recounts, is to leave Oliver to the lion — a natural justice for all the wrongs he has suffered:

> "Kindness, nobler ever than revenge, / And nature, stronger than his just occasion, / Made him give battle to the lioness."

This passage is crucial to the play's moral vision. Orlando's choice to save Oliver is not presented as weakness but as the triumph of "kindness" and "nature" — the redemptive values of the green world — over the courtly logic of revenge.

## Physical and Moral Wounds

Orlando fights and kills the lioness but is wounded in the encounter. When Oliver awakens and recognizes his savior, the shock triggers his moral transformation:

> "I do not shame to tell you what I was, / Since my conversion so sweetly tastes, / Being the thing I am."

The blood from Orlando's wound — shown to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] on a napkin by Oliver — causes Rosalind to faint, momentarily breaking her [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise. This physical reaction reveals her love for Orlando even as it nearly exposes her.

## Dramatic Significance

The lion incident serves multiple narrative functions simultaneously. It provides the mechanism for **Oliver's conversion** — the elder brother's sudden and complete moral reformation, which enables the eventual reconciliation and his marriage to [[celia|Celia]]. It demonstrates **Orlando's virtue** — proving through action what his words and verses could only suggest. And it precipitates the **climactic chain of revelations** that leads directly to Act 5's resolution.

The incident also reinforces the play's central theme that the Forest of Arden, while a pastoral refuge from court corruption, is not a sanitized paradise. It contains real dangers — snakes, lions, hunger, and hardship — that test character and catalyze transformation.

## Connection to the Brother Plot

The lion incident forms the symmetrical counterpoint to [[olivers-plot|Oliver's Plot Against Orlando]]. Where Oliver's courtly malice sought death by proxy (the wrestler [[charles|Charles]], the fire in Orlando's lodging), Orlando's forest virtue gives life at personal risk. This inversion — attempted murder repaid with rescue — is the moral engine that drives the brothers' reconciliation and enables the comic resolution of Act 5.
