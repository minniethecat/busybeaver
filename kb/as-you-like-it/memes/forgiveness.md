## Definition

Forgiveness in As You Like It is the mechanism through which the play's conflicts are resolved. Where the court world of the opening acts is defined by enmity, usurpation, and fraternal hatred, [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] world of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] enables the gradual work of reconciliation. Forgiveness operates not as a single dramatic moment but as a process — tested through [[orlando|Orlando]]'s mercy to [[oliver|Oliver]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s orchestration of the weddings, and the off-stage conversion of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]].

## In As You Like It

The central act of forgiveness occurs when [[orlando|Orlando]] saves his sleeping brother [[oliver|Oliver]] from a lioness, despite [[oliver|Oliver]] having come to [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] to capture or kill him. [[orlando|Orlando]] describes his internal conflict:

> Speake you so gently? Pardon me I pray you, I thought that all things had beene sauage heere (010-112)[010-113]

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s choice to forgive — risking his own life for the brother who sought his death — is the play's moral turning point. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s subsequent transformation and love for [[aliena|Aliena]] ([[celia|Celia]]) demonstrates the regenerative power of forgiveness.

[[oliver|Oliver]] himself testifies to this transformation:

> But first begs pardon (013-032)

The political parallel — [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s conversion — occurs off-stage but completes the pattern. The usurping duke, marching to destroy [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] community, meets "an old religious man" and is converted, restoring the dukedom to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]. This off-stage resolution extends forgiveness beyond the personal to the political realm.

At the play's close, [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Epilogue extends the spirit of forgiveness to the audience, asking for their approval — a final gesture of reconciliation that completes the play's movement from conflict to harmony.

## [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Choice: Kindness over Revenge

The play's most sustained treatment of forgiveness is [[oliver|Oliver]]'s own account of his brother's mercy [018-111–018-150]. [[oliver|Oliver]], who had come to the [[forest-of-arden-place|forest]] to kill [[orlando|Orlando]], falls asleep in the wilderness and is threatened successively by a snake and a lioness. [[orlando|Orlando]], discovering his would-be murderer in mortal danger, faces the defining ethical choice of the play:

> Twice did he turne his backe, and purpos'd so: / But kindnesse, nobler euer then reuenge, / And [[nature|Nature]] stronger then his iust occasion, / Made him giue battell to the Lyonnesse [018-129–018-132]

The triadic structure of the reasoning — kindness, nature, battle — is significant. "Kindnesse" (the natural bond of kinship) defeats "reuenge"; "[[nature|Nature]]" (both human nature and the biological tie of blood) overcomes "iust occasion" (the morally defensible grounds for letting [[oliver|Oliver]] die). The rhetorical progression moves from the ethical to the instinctual to the physical, grounding [[orlando|Orlando]]'s mercy not in abstract principle but in the irreducible pull of brotherhood.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s decision is not cost-free. He sustains a wound from the lioness — the "bloody napkin" (018-141) that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] faints upon seeing — making forgiveness a literal bodily sacrifice. The wound marks [[orlando|Orlando]]'s mercy as something earned, not merely declared.

## Conversion and the Transformed Self

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s response to being saved is not mere gratitude but a radical transformation of identity:

> 'Twas I: but 'tis not I: I doe not shame / To tell you what I was, since my conuersion / So sweetly tastes, being the thing I am [018-138–018-140]

The triple self-reference — "I," "not I," "what I was," "the thing I am" — enacts the conversion grammatically. [[oliver|Oliver]] simultaneously claims and disclaims his past self. The word "conuersion" carries theological weight (the turning of the soul toward God), but the sweetness [[oliver|Oliver]] tastes is earthly — the taste of reconciliation with his brother. The play's Protestant-inflected theology of grace operates through natural bonds: forgiveness redeems the sinner not through doctrine but through the shock of unearned mercy.

The tears that follow — "Teares our recountments had most kindely bath'd" (018-144) — complete the ritual. The brothers weep together, their shared grief washing away the accumulated wrongs. The scene's conclusion, with [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] giving [[oliver|Oliver]] "fresh aray, and entertainment" and committing him "vnto my brothers [[love|loue]]" [018-147–018-148], enacts the social dimension of forgiveness: reconciliation is not complete until the community acknowledges and celebrates it.

## The Limits of Forgiveness: [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s Offstage Conversion

The political forgiveness — [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s [[regeneration|restoration]] of the dukedom — is strikingly different from the fraternal reconciliation. It happens offstage, reported rather than shown. [[jaques|Jaques de Boys]] narrates:

> And to the skirts of this wilde Wood he came, / Where meeting with an old religious man, / After some question with him, was conuerted / Both from his enterprise, and from the world [021-024–021-027]

The asymmetry is intentional. Fraternal forgiveness can be dramatized — it involves bodies, wounds, tears, and shared history. Political forgiveness requires a deus ex machina, a supernatural intervention that the play cannot (or will not) represent on stage. The implication is that some forms of reconciliation — those rooted in institutional power rather than personal bond — remain outside the reach of comic resolution's natural mechanisms. The old religious man is a narrative convenience that acknowledges the difficulty: political forgiveness is possible in comedy, but only by miracle.
