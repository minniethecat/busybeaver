## Definition

Courtiership in *As You Like It* refers to the system of manners, speech, and social conduct associated
with aristocratic court life — and, crucially, what happens when that system is transplanted into the
pastoral environment of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. Shakespeare uses the figure of the ex-courtier to satirise courtly
pretension while simultaneously acknowledging its sophistication. The concept operates as a measuring
stick against which pastoral simplicity is defined — each mode exposes the absurdities of the other.

## In As You Like It

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] functions as the play's principal commentator on courtiership. [[corin|Corin]]'s honest assessment
establishes the central paradox: "those that are good maners at the Court, are as ridiculous in the
Countrey, as the behauiour of the Countrie is most mockeable at the Court" (012-023). The reciprocity
of this judgment — each world finds the other absurd — prevents the satire from becoming simple
pastoral nostalgia.

[[jaques|Jaques]] identifies [[touchstone|Touchstone]] precisely by his professional history: "O worthie Foole: One that hath bin
a Courtier" (010-038), and later confirms "he hath bin a Courtier he sweares" (022-042). The comic
weight of "he sweares" — the oath itself a courtly gesture — underscores that courtiership is a
performance of identity rather than an essence. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s own arguments betray his training even as
he critiques it: when [[corin|Corin]] objects that "The Courtiers hands are perfum'd with Ciuet," [[touchstone|Touchstone]]
replies with sophisticated counter-argument — "is not the grease of a Mutton, as wholesome as the
sweat of a man?" (012-027) (012-030) — deploying courtly rhetoric to argue against courtly values.

The motif extends beyond [[touchstone|Touchstone]]. [[jaques|Jaques]]' catalogue of melancholies includes "the Courtiers, which
is proud" (016-008), and [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s mock-legal dissection of the seven degrees of the lie
originates in a dispute over "the cut of a certaine Courtiers beard" (022-054) — a detail that
reduces the elaborate code of [[honor|honour]] to follicular fashion. Through these echoes, courtiership
becomes not merely a profession but a lens for examining what identities survive transplantation
from one world to another.
