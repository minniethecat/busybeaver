## Definition

Folly in As You Like It operates on multiple levels: as the professional foolery of [[touchstone|Touchstone]], as the natural foolishness of lovers, and as a philosophical lens through which the play examines the boundary between wisdom and foolishness. The play repeatedly asks whether the fool is truly foolish, or whether the so-called wise are the greater fools — a paradox central to Shakespearean comedy.

## In As You Like It

Folly pervades the play from [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s first entrance. As the licensed fool of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s court — and later an [[freedom|exile]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — [[touchstone|Touchstone]] embodies the institutionalized folly of the professional jester. Yet his role extends beyond mere entertainment. [[celia|Celia]] describes the paradoxical value of the fool:

> For alwaies the dulnesse of the foole, is the whetstone of the wits. (002-019)

[[touchstone|The fool]]'s apparent stupidity sharpens the intelligence of those around him. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] himself voices the play's central critique of wisdom:

> The more pittie that fooles may not speak wisely, what Wisemen do foolishly (002-038)

This inversion — fools speaking wisely while the wise act foolishly — is the play's governing comic principle. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s love-fueled foolishness, [[jaques|Jaques]]' melancholic wisdom-seeking, and [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s political follies all fall under this rubric. Even the pastoral retreat to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is a kind of productive folly: a deliberate escape from "civilized" wisdom to a place where transformation can occur.

The play's comic resolution — four weddings in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] — enacts folly's triumph: the lovers' "foolish" persistence is rewarded, and the cynic [[jaques|Jaques]] alone refuses to participate, choosing [[freedom|exile]] over [[joy-and-celebration|festivity]].

## The Motley Fool: Jaques Encounters Touchstone

The play's most concentrated statement on folly comes through [[jaques|Jaques]]' ecstatic report of meeting [[touchstone|Touchstone]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]. In one of the play's most structurally ingenious passages [010-014–010-035], [[jaques|Jaques]] — the melancholic philosopher — is reduced to helpless laughter by the professional fool:

> A Foole, a foole: I met a foole i'th Forrest, / A motley Foole (a miserable world:) [010-014–010-015]

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s performance consists of "moralizing on the time" — taking out a dial, observing that "from houre to houre, we ripe, and ripe, / And then from houre to houre, we rot, and rot" [010-028–010-029], and concluding "thereby hangs a tale" (010-030). This is mock-philosophy delivered with perfect comic timing, and [[jaques|Jaques]]' response is telling:

> That Fooles should be so deepe contemplatiue: / And I did laugh, sans intermission / An houre by his diall. Oh noble foole [010-033–010-035]

The scene crystallizes the play's argument about folly. The professional fool achieves what the professional melancholic cannot: genuine insight through apparent nonsense. [[jaques|Jaques]]' desire to wear the "motley coat" (010-045) and become a licensed fool himself reveals his recognition that folly offers freedoms unavailable to the earnest philosopher. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s response — that [[jaques|Jaques]] would only use [[touchstone|the fool]]'s [[freedom|liberty]] to "Cleanse the foule bodie of th'infected world" (010-063) — suggests that [[jaques|Jaques]] misunderstands the nature of comic folly: it is not a weapon for reform but a mode of seeing that accepts imperfection.

## The Seven Degrees of the Lie

In Act 5, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] delivers the play's most extended comic set-piece: the "seven degrees of the lie" [022-054–022-057]. This virtuoso catalogue of dueling etiquette — from the "Retort courteous" through the "Quip-modest," "Reply Churlish," "Reproofe valiant," "Counterchecke quarrelsome," and "Lye with circumstance" to the "Lye direct" — is simultaneously a parody of courtly manners and a serious demonstration of how language can escalate or defuse conflict.

The passage's culmination is characteristically practical: "Your If, is the onely peace-maker: much [[virtue-and-gentility|vertue]] in if" (022-057). The conditional — the hypothetical qualification that allows an insult to be withdrawn without loss of face — embodies the comic spirit itself. Where tragedy insists on the absolute ("I must do this"), comedy's conditional ("If I said so, then I said so") creates the space for reconciliation.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] provides the definitive assessment of [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s method: "He vses his folly like a stalking-horse, and vnder the presentation of that he shoots his [[wit]]" (022-059). Folly is a decoy — a screen behind which genuine intelligence operates. The professional fool is not foolish; he performs foolishness as a strategy, and the performance itself becomes a form of wisdom.
