# Wrestling

## Definition

Wrestling in As You Like It is more than athletic spectacle — it is a dramatic catalyst, a test of character, and a metaphor for the struggle between natural merit and worldly power. [[wrestling-match|[[orlando|Orlando]]'s victory]] over [[charles|Charles]], the Duke's wrestler, in Act 1, Scene 2 marks his first public assertion of identity and initiates his love relationship with [[rosalind|Rosalind]]. The wrestling match embodies the play's central concerns: the triumph of innate virtue over trained force, the public recognition of hidden worth, and the body as the site where [[fortune|Fortune]] and [[nature|Nature]] contend.

## In As You Like It

The wrestling match is announced early: "Was not [[charles|Charles]] the Dukes Wrastler heere to speake with me?" (001-033). [[charles|Charles]] is [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s champion — a professional fighter associated with [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s establishment. [[orlando|Orlando]], by contrast, is an untrained [[youth]] who has been denied gentlemanly education by his brother [[oliver|Oliver]] (001-025).

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] attempt to dissuade [[orlando|Orlando]] from fighting, noting [[charles|Charles]]'s deadly [[honor|reputation]]: "the eldest of the three, wrastled with [[charles|Charles]] the Dukes Wrastler, which [[charles|Charles]] in a moment threw him, and broke three of his ribbes" (002-065). [[orlando|Orlando]]'s insistence on fighting despite the danger demonstrates the courage born of desperation — he has nothing to lose, "one out of suites with fortune" (002-147).

The match itself is a turning point. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s unexpected victory earns him [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s recognition — and, disastrously, the Duke's hostility upon learning [[orlando|Orlando]] is the son of Sir Rowland de Boys. More importantly, [[rosalind|Rosalind]], witnessing [[orlando|Orlando]]'s courage and physical grace, falls in love. She gives him her chain: "Weare this for me: one out of suites with fortune" (002-147).

The wrestling thus accomplishes in a single scene what the pastoral plot will develop over four acts: the triumph of natural worth over artificial power, the awakening of love, and the revelation of character under pressure.

## Key Passages

### The Challenge Announced (Act 1, Scene 1)

> Oli. Was not Charles the Dukes Wrastler heere to speake with me? (001-033)

### The Warning (Act 1, Scene 2)

> the eldest of the three, wrastled with Charles the Dukes Wrastler, which Charles in a moment threw him, and broke three of his ribbes, that there is little hope of life in him (002-065)

### [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Determination

[[orlando|Orlando]] fights despite being the untrained youngest son. When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] urge him to withdraw, he replies with courtly grace:

> Orl. I beseech you, punish mee not with your harde thoughts, wherein I confesse me much guiltie to denie so faire and excellent Ladies anie thing. But let your faire eies, and gentle wishes go with me to my triall (002-095)

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s identity — "the yongest sonne of Sir Roland de Boys" (002-120) — proves his [[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]] despite his rustic upbringing.

### The Victory and Love Token

[[rosalind|Rosalind]], moved by his courage, gives [[orlando|Orlando]] her chain:

> Ros. Weare this for me: one out of suites with fortune, That could giue more, but that her hand lacks meanes. (002-147)

### Struck Dumb

[[orlando|Orlando]] finds himself unable to speak to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] after his victory:

> Orl. What passion hangs these waights vpon my toong? I cannot speake to her, yet shee vrg'd conference. O poore Orlando! thou art ouerthrowne (002-161)
