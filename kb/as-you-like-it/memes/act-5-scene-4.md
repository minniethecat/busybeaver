(022-038)Enter [[touchstone|Clowne]] and [[audrey|Audrey]].

(022-039)Obscured in the circle of this Forrest

(022-040)Iaq. There is sure another flood toward, and these couples are comming to the Arke. Here comes a payre of verie strange beasts, which in all tongues, are call'd Fooles

(022-041)Clo. Salutation and greeting to you all

(022-042)Iaq. Good my Lord, bid him welcome: This is the Motley-minded Gentleman, that I haue so often met in the Forrest: he hath bin a Courtier he sweares

(022-043)Clo. If any man doubt that, let him put mee to my purgation, I haue trod a measure, I haue flattred a Lady, I haue bin politicke with my friend, smooth with mine enemie, I haue vndone three Tailors, I haue had foure quarrels, and like to haue fought one

(022-044)Iaq. And how was that tane vp?

(022-045)Clo. 'Faith we met, and found the quarrel was vpon

(022-046)the seuenth cause

(022-047)Iaq. How seuenth cause? Good my Lord, like this

(022-048)fellow

(022-049)Du.Se. I like him very well

(022-050)Clo. God'ild you sir, I desire you of the like: I presse in heere sir, amongst the rest of the Country copulatiues to sweare, and to forsweare, according as mariage binds and blood breakes: a poore virgin sir, an il-fauor'd thing sir, but mine owne, a poore humour of mine sir, to take that that no man else will: rich honestie dwels like a miser sir, in a poore house, as your Pearle in your foule oyster

(022-051)Du.Se. By my faith, he is very swift, and sententious

(022-052)Clo. According to the fooles bolt sir, and such dulcet

(022-053)diseases

(022-054)Iaq. But for the seuenth cause. How did you finde the quarrell on the seuenth cause? Clo. Vpon a lye, seuen times remoued: (beare your bodie more seeming Audry) as thus sir: I did dislike the cut of a certaine Courtiers beard: he sent me word, if I said his beard was not cut well, hee was in the minde it was: this is call'd the retort courteous. If I sent him word againe, it was not well cut, he wold send me word he cut it to please himselfe: this is call'd the quip modest. If againe, it was not well cut, he disabled my iudgment: this is called, the reply churlish. If againe it was not well cut, he would answer I spake not true: this is call'd the reproofe valiant. If againe, it was not well cut, he wold say, I lie: this is call'd the counter-checke quarrelsome: and so to lye circumstantiall, and the lye direct

(022-055)Iaq. And how oft did you say his beard was not well cut? Clo. I durst go no further then the lye circumstantial: nor he durst not giue me the lye direct: and so wee measur'd swords, and parted

(022-056)Iaq. Can you nominate in order now, the degrees of the lye

(022-057)Clo. O sir, we quarrel in print, by the booke: as you haue bookes for good manners: I will name you the degrees. The first, the Retort courteous: the second, the Quip-modest: the third, the reply Churlish: the fourth, the Reproofe valiant: the fift, the Counterchecke quarrelsome: the sixt, the Lye with circumstance: the seauenth, the Lye direct: all these you may auoyd, but the Lye direct: and you may auoide that too, with an If. I knew when seuen Iustices could not take vp a Quarrell, but when the parties were met themselues, one of them thought but of an If; as if you saide so, then I saide so: and they shooke hands, and swore brothers. Your If, is the onely peace-maker: much [[virtue-and-gentility|vertue]] in if

(022-058)Iaq. Is not this a rare fellow my Lord? He's as good at any thing, and yet a foole

(022-059)Du.Se. He vses his folly like a stalking-horse, and vnder the presentation of that he shoots his [[wit]]. Enter [[hymen|Hymen]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and Celia.

(022-060)Still Musicke.

(022-062)When earthly things made eauen

(022-063)attone together.

(022-064)Good Duke receiue thy daughter,

(022-065)Hymen from Heauen brought her,

(022-066)Yea brought her hether.

(022-067)That thou mightst ioyne his hand with his,

(022-068)Whose heart within his bosome is

(022-069)Ros. To you I giue my selfe, for I am yours.

(022-070)To you I giue my selfe, for I am yours

(022-071)Du.Se. If there be truth in sight, you are my daughter

(022-072)Orl. If there be truth in sight, you are my Rosalind

(022-073)Phe. If sight & shape be true, why then my [[love|loue]] adieu

(022-074)Ros. Ile haue no Father, if you be not he:

(022-075)Ile haue no Husband, if you be not he:

(022-076)Nor ne're wed woman, if you be not shee

(022-078)'Tis I must make conclusion

(022-079)Of these most strange euents:

(022-080)Here's eight that must take hands,

(022-081)To ioyne in Hymens bands,

(022-082)If truth holds true contents.

(022-092)How thus we met, and these things finish.

(022-101)Euen daughter welcome, in no lesse degree

(022-102)Phe. I wil not eate my word, now thou art mine,

(022-103)Thy faith, my fancie to thee doth combine.

(022-106)I am the second sonne of old Sir Rowland,

(022-107)That bring these tidings to this faire assembly.

(022-108)[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] hearing how that euerie day

(022-138)2. Bro. He hath

(022-139)Iaq. To him will I: out of these conuertites, There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd: you to your former Honor, I bequeath your patience, and your vertue, well deserues it. you to a loue, that your true faith doth merit: you to your land, and loue, and great allies: you to a long, and well-deserued bed: And you to wrangling, for thy louing voyage Is but for two moneths victuall'd: So to your pleasures, I am for other, then for dancing meazures
