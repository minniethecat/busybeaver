# Audrey

## Character Overview

Audrey is a country goatherd dwelling in the [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] who becomes the bride of [[touchstone|Touchstone]], the court fool. She is the only female rustic character with a speaking role and more than a fleeting appearance in the play, and her three scenes form a miniature courtship narrative that runs parallel to the main romantic plot. Audrey is defined by her simplicity: she cannot follow [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s elaborate [[wit]], she speaks in plain and often monosyllabic answers, and she is disarmingly honest about her own plainness. Yet her simplicity is not foolishness — it is a form of practical wisdom that deflates [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s pretensions as effectively as his own satire deflates others.

In the taxonomy of the play's women, Audrey occupies the earthy bottom rung. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is the witty heroine, [[celia|Celia]] the loyal companion, [[phebe|Phebe]] the disdainful shepherdess of pastoral convention — and Audrey the goatherd, who is neither witty nor beautiful nor disdainful, but simply there, willing to be married. Her acceptance of [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s proposal is motivated neither by romance nor ambition but by the practical recognition that marriage is better than solitude. Her plain speech — "I am not a slut, though I thanke the Goddes I am foule" (013-026) — is a form of honesty that cuts through the play's elaborate rhetorical games.

## Comic Function

[[audrey|Audrey]]'s primary dramatic function is to serve as the straight woman to [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s comic excess. Where [[touchstone|Touchstone]] spins elaborate rhetorical webs, Audrey responds with baffled monosyllables. The pattern is established in their first exchange: when [[touchstone|Touchstone]] laments that the Gods did not make her "poeticall," Audrey asks simply "I do not know what Poetical is: is it honest in deed and word: is it a true thing?" (013-011). The question is philosophically acute even as it is grammatically naive — she instinctively tests [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] against honesty, the same assay [[touchstone|Touchstone]] applies to everything else.

This dynamic — elaborate [[wit]] met with blank incomprehension — generates the scene's comedy but also its substance. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] has spent the play exposing the pretensions of others; with Audrey he encounters someone whose mind is too simple to be pretentious. She cannot be satirized because she makes no claims. The result is a rare moment of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] being, in a sense, defeated — his [[wit]] cannot land because there is nothing to deflate. [[audrey|Audrey]]'s most devastating line is also her simplest: "Would you not haue me honest?" (013-017).

Audrey also embodies the play's democratic comic impulse. The [[marriage]] of a court fool and a goatherd is blessed alongside the union of a duke's daughter and a gentleman — [[hymen|Hymen]]'s inclusiveness extends even to "a payre of verie strange beasts, which in all tongues, are call'd Fooles" (022-050). Comedy, Shakespeare suggests, does not discriminate by rank or [[wit]].

## Relationship with Touchstone

[[audrey|Audrey]]'s courtship with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] is the play's most anti-romantic love plot. Where [[orlando|Orlando]] pins bad verses on trees and [[silvius|Silvius]] suffers in pastoral agony, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] proposes marriage with the argument that cuckoldry is inevitable and therefore acceptable: "As hornes are odious, they are necessarie" (013-031). Audrey offers no resistance to this unflattering logic. She wants to be married, and [[touchstone|Touchstone]] is willing to marry her — the transaction is nakedly practical.

> **Audrey**: Wel, the Gods giue vs ioy (013-030)
>
> **Touchstone**: Amen. A man may if he were of a fearful heart, stagger in this attempt: for heere wee haue no Temple but the wood, no assembly but horne-beasts. But what though? Courage. As hornes are odious, they are necessarie. (013-031)

The power imbalance is stark. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] brings courtly sophistication and verbal mastery; Audrey brings simplicity and a herd of goats. Yet Audrey is not a passive victim. When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] tests her honesty — "thou swear'st to me thou art honest: Now if thou wert a Poet, I might haue some hope thou didst feigne" (013-011) — she responds with genuine indignation: "Would you not haue me honest?" (013-017). The question reveals that beneath her simplicity is a firm moral self-possession. She knows what she is and does not pretend otherwise.

Their interrupted wedding with Sir [[oliver|Oliver]] Martext reveals another dimension. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s willingness to be married by an incompetent priest, on the grounds that "not being wel married, it wil be a good excuse for me heereafter, to leaue my wife" (013-031), exposes the cynicism beneath his courtship. It is [[jaques|Jaques]] who intervenes, urging a proper ceremony — an intervention that, ironically, protects Audrey more than she can protect herself. [[audrey|Audrey]]'s own comment on the matter is characteristically practical: "Faith the Priest was good enough, for all the olde gentlemans saying" (019-004).

By the final scene, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] introduces Audrey to the ducal assembly with a mixture of affection and condescension that perfectly captures their relationship: "a poore virgin sir, an il-fauor'd thing sir, but mine owne, a poore humour of mine sir, to take that that no man else will" (022-050). It is the most backhanded compliment in the play, and Audrey says nothing in response. Whether she is too simple to notice the insult or too wise to care is a question Shakespeare leaves deliberately unresolved.

## The William Rivalry

Audrey is the object of a miniature love triangle involving [[william|William]], a country [[youth]] whose claim on her predates [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s courtship. When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] learns of [[william|William]], Audrey is quick to dismiss her former suitor: "he hath no interest in mee in the world" (019-009). Her loyalty is unambiguous and immediate — she has chosen the court fool over the country [[youth]] without apparent hesitation.

> **Audrey**: he hath no interest in mee in the world: here comes the man you meane. (019-009)

The rivalry is resolved in Act 5, Scene 1 through comic intimidation rather than contest. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] deploys his full rhetorical arsenal against [[william|William]] — threatening him with "a hundred and fifty wayes" of death — and Audrey seals the dismissal with a single line: "Do good [[william|William]]" (019-034). The line is devastating in its economy. Four words, and the rival is gone. [[audrey|Audrey]]'s choice of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] over [[william|William]] — the sophisticated fool over the simple rustic — suggests that she recognizes in [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit]] something worth having, even if she cannot herself participate in it. Her preference is for the man who can talk, however incomprehensibly, over the man who can only say "so, so."

## Key Scenes

### The Courtship (Act 3, Scene 3)

[[audrey|Audrey]]'s first scene establishes her character, her relationship with [[touchstone|Touchstone]], and the comic pattern of [[wit]]-meets-simplicity that defines her role. The scene opens with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] calling her to follow with her goats and asking "how Audrey am I the man yet? Doth my simple feature content you?" (013-002). Her reply — "Your features, Lord warrant vs: what features?" (013-005) — sets the tone. The scene develops through [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s mock-scholastic discourse on [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]], honesty, and beauty, all of which Audrey deflects with literal-minded practicality. The scene also introduces the rival [[william|William]] indirectly, as [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s insistence on an immediate wedding suggests urgency born of competition. Sir [[oliver|Oliver]] Martext's arrival and the interrupted ceremony — with [[jaques|Jaques]] urging a proper church wedding — leaves their union unconsummated but intended.

### Dismissing William (Act 5, Scene 1)

Audrey returns in the final act, still unmarried. When [[touchstone|Touchstone]] mentions that "there is a [[youth]] heere in the Forrest layes claime to you" (019-007), Audrey immediately identifies and dismisses the rival: "I, I know who 'tis: he hath no interest in mee in the world" (019-009). Her loyalty is absolute. When [[william|William]] appears, she greets him politely — "God ye good eu'n [[william|William]]" (019-012) — but offers no encouragement. After [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s verbal assault drives [[william|William]] away, Audrey caps the scene with the four-word dismissal that ends her only love triangle: "Do good [[william|William]]" (019-034). The scene then ends with [[corin|Corin]] summoning them to meet the "Master and Mistresse," and [[touchstone|Touchstone]] calling "Trip Audry, trip Audry" (019-040) — the only moment in the play where anyone addresses her with anything resembling affection.

### The Wedding Blessing (Act 5, Scene 4)

[[audrey|Audrey]]'s final scene is silent. She enters with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] as one of the couples assembling for the quadruple wedding. [[jaques|Jaques]] greets them with characteristic mockery: "Here comes a payre of verie strange beasts, which in all tongues, are call'd Fooles" (022-050). [[touchstone|Touchstone]] presents Audrey to the company in terms of calculated condescension, and then launches into his famous anatomy of the seven degrees of the lie — a performance in which Audrey plays no part. She stands silent through the entire scene, through [[hymen|Hymen]]'s blessing and [[jaques|Jaques]]'s farewell, her presence acknowledged only once when [[touchstone|Touchstone]] pauses his discourse to say "beare your bodie more seeming Audry" (022-054). Audrey's silence in the final scene is the logical culmination of her dramatic function: she is the object of [[wit]], never its practitioner, the bride who is present but never eloquent, the goatherd who marries into comedy without ever quite understanding the play she is in.

## Relationships

- [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — Her betrothed and eventual husband. Their courtship is the most deliberately anti-romantic coupling in the play, built on pragmatism ("hornes are necessarie") rather than passion or [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]]. Yet their union is blessed by [[hymen|Hymen]] alongside the three courtly marriages, affirming comedy's democratic inclusiveness.
- [[william|William]] — Her former rustic suitor, whose claim she renounces without hesitation in favor of [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s sophisticated [[wit]]. Their relationship is defined by [[audrey|Audrey]]'s single decisive line: "he hath no interest in mee in the world" (019-009).
- [[jaques|Jaques]] — The melancholy philosopher who observes her courtship and intervenes to prevent a sham marriage. His relationship to Audrey is that of spectator and occasional protector.
- [[oliver|Sir Oliver Martext]] — The hedge-priest who attempts to marry her to [[touchstone|Touchstone]] under a tree. [[audrey|Audrey]]'s verdict on his competence — "Faith the Priest was good enough" — is characteristically pragmatic.
