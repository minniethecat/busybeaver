# Marriage

## Definition

Marriage in As You Like It is the comedic destination toward which all the play's romantic energies are directed. The four weddings that conclude the play — [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]], [[celia|Celia]] and [[oliver|Oliver]], [[touchstone|Touchstone]] and [[audrey|Audrey]], [[silvius|Silvius]] and [[phebe|Phebe]] — follow the classical comic formula in which social disorder ([[freedom|exile]], usurpation, fraternal hatred) is resolved through the ritual of matrimony. [[hymen|Hymen]], the god of marriage, descends to preside over the unions (022-094), explicitly linking the play's resolution to the mythic and the divine.

## In As You Like It

Marriage is thematized long before it is achieved. [[rosalind|Rosalind]], in her meditation on [[time|Time]]'s diverse paces, describes how [[time|Time]] "trots hard with a yong maid, between the contract of her marriage, and the day it is solemnizd" (012-150). The interval between betrothal and wedding stretches unbearably for the impatient lover — a comic insight into the temporal economy of courtship.

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s approach to marriage is characteristically anti-romantic. He and [[audrey|Audrey]] seek a rustic wedding with Sir [[oliver|Oliver]] Martext, prompting [[jaques|Jaques]] to intervene: "And wil you (being a man of your breeding) be married vnder a bush like a begger? Get you to church, and haue a good Priest" (013-040). [[jaques|Jaques]]'s insistence on proper form reveals the social gravity of marriage even in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]].

The discovery that [[audrey|Audrey]] cannot legally marry without someone to "giue" her — "Truly she must be giuen, or the marriage is not lawfull" (013-033) — introduces a legalistic concern that [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s rustic marriage must navigate. The play treats marriage as both a natural impulse and a social institution requiring proper forms.

The final scene compresses four marriages into a single ritual officiated by [[hymen|Hymen]]. The god's invocation — "Wedding is great Iunos crowne" (022-094) — elevates the comedic resolution to cosmic significance. Marriage restores not only the lovers but the social order: [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom is returned, and the fractured bonds of blood and loyalty are mended through the "blessed bond of boord and bed" (022-094).

## Key Passages

### Time and Marriage (Act 3, Scene 2)

> Marry he trots hard with a yong maid, between the contract of her marriage, and the day it is solemnizd: if the interim be but a sennight, Times pace is so hard, that it seemes the length of seuen yeere. (012-150)

### Rustic Wedding (Act 3, Scene 3)

> Truly she must be giuen, or the marriage is not lawfull (013-033)

> And wil you (being a man of your breeding) be married vnder a bush like a begger? Get you to church, and haue a good Priest (013-040)

### [[hymen|Hymen]]'s Blessing (Act 5, Scene 4)

> Wedding is great Iunos crowne, O blessed bond of boord and bed: Tis Hymen peoples euerie towne, high wedlock then be honored (022-094)

### Restored Fortune (Act 5, Scene 4)

> Shal share the good of our returned fortune (022-129)
