# Inheritance and Patrimony

Inheritance — the transmission of wealth, status, and identity from one generation to the next — is the material engine of *As You Like It*'s opening conflict and a persistent theme throughout the play. Shakespeare uses contested inheritance to explore questions of merit, justice, and the relationship between birth and worth.

## Primogeniture and Its Discontents

The play's social world is governed by primogeniture — the system by which the eldest son inherits the entirety of a father's estate. [[sir-rowland-de-boys|Sir Rowland de Boys]] has left his estate to his eldest son [[oliver|Oliver]], who is charged with providing for his younger brothers. Oliver's refusal to honor this obligation — keeping [[orlando|Orlando]] in rustic poverty while denying [[jaques-de-boys|Jaques de Boys]] his education at university — is the play's originating injustice.

Orlando articulates the injustice with precision:

> "My father charged you in his will to give me good education. You have trained me like a peasant, obscuring and hiding from me all gentleman-like qualities."

The language of hiding and obscuring is significant. Oliver has not simply neglected Orlando; he has actively suppressed his brother's natural gentility — his inherited right to the status of a gentleman. This suppression is a form of symbolic disinheritance that mirrors the political usurpation of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] by [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]].

## The Thousand Crowns

Sir Rowland's will bequeathed Orlando a thousand crowns — a modest but meaningful inheritance that Oliver refuses to release. The thousand crowns represent not just money but recognition: the father's acknowledgment of his younger son's worth and his right to a place in the social order.

When the old servant [[adam|Adam]] offers Orlando his own life savings — five hundred crowns saved over a lifetime of service — the gesture establishes a parallel system of inheritance based on loyalty and love rather than blood and law:

> "I have five hundred crowns, / The thrifty hire I saved under your father."

Adam's gift, freely given, exposes the corruption of Oliver's withheld inheritance. True patrimony, the play suggests, is not merely legal entitlement but the transmission of values — the "constant service of the antique world" that Adam embodies.

## Birth vs. Worth

Throughout the play, Orlando proves that his gentility is inherent, not conferred. Despite his lack of formal education and his "rustic" upbringing, he speaks with natural eloquence, wrestles with heroic strength, and loves with passionate sincerity. His worth is not diminished by his poverty but revealed through it:

> "In me, the ugly caterpillar of the world, / The marks of a fair virtue are inherited."

The play challenges the equation of inheritance with worth. Orlando deserves his patrimony not because it was legally bequeathed but because he embodies his father's virtues. Conversely, Oliver's legal inheritance does not make him worthy — his moral reformation is required before he can truly claim Sir Rowland's legacy.

## Restoration

The play's conclusion restores proper inheritance at every level. Duke Senior regains his dukedom. Orlando receives his patrimony and marries [[rosalind|Rosalind]], securing his place in the restored social order. Oliver is reformed and accepted. The proper transmission of wealth, status, and identity — disrupted by fraternal conflict — is restored through the transformative power of Arden and the bonds of love.
