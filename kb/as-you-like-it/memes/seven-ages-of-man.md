## Definition

The Seven Ages of Man is the monologue delivered by [[jaques|Jaques]] in Act 2, Scene 7 of *As You Like It* (lines 147–174). Beginning with the proposition that "All the world's a stage, / And all the men and women, meerely Players" [010-147–148], it traces human life through seven distinct ages — infant, schoolboy, lover, soldier, justice, pantaloon, and second childhood — each characterized by a defining posture, costume, and voice. The speech is Shakespeare's most concentrated expression of the *theatrum mundi* (world-as-theater) topos, and it functions within the play as [[jaques|Jaques]]' diagnosis of the human condition: life is a performance scripted by nature, culminating in "meere obliuion, / Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing" [010-173–174].

## In As You Like It

### The Dramatic Context

The monologue occurs at a pivotal moment in Act 2, Scene 7. [[orlando|Orlando]] has just burst into [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s woodland banquet demanding food; the Duke has responded with gentle hospitality [010-108–111]; [[orlando|Orlando]] has gone to fetch the aged [[adam|Adam]] [010-133–139]. Into this pause — a moment of grace in the play's harshest scene — [[jaques|Jaques]] launches his speech. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] has just observed that "This wide and vniuersall Theater / Presents more wofull Pageants then the Sceane / Wherein we play in" [010-144–146], providing [[jaques|Jaques]] with his cue. The speech is thus framed as a response to suffering: the Duke notes the world's cruelty, and [[jaques|Jaques]] answers by anatomizing it.

The dramatic placement is doubly significant. The speech immediately follows [[jaques|Jaques]]' earlier exchange with the Duke about the satirist's "[[freedom|liberty]]" [010-050–052] and the Duke's rebuke — "Most mischieuous foule sin, in chiding sin" (010-067). This exchange frames the Seven Ages as [[jaques|Jaques]]' most elaborate performance of the satiric role he has just claimed — and implicitly as a demonstration of the very fault the Duke identified.

### The Architectonic of the Speech

The monologue is structured as a downward arc. It begins with birth — "Mewling, and puking in the Nurses armes" (010-152) — and descends through vigor, authority, and decline into "second childishnesse, and meere obliuion" (010-173). Each [[youth|age]] is marked by a specific sound: the infant's mewling (010-152), the schoolboy's "whining" (010-153), the lover's "Sighing like Furnace" (010-156), the soldier's "strange oaths" (010-158), the justice's "wise sawes" (010-164), the pantaloon's "childish trebble pipes" (010-170), and finally silence. The progression is both comic and bleak — each role is absurd, and the final role is extinction.

The speech's architecture mirrors the classical ages-of-man tradition (derived from Hippocrates via Ptolemy and medieval schemata) but departs from it in crucial ways. Traditional schemes typically enumerated three, four, or six ages, each corresponding to a planet or humor. Shakespeare's innovation is twofold: he expands to seven (perhaps echoing the Seven Deadly Sins or the Seven Planets) and he strips away cosmic correspondence in favor of theatrical metaphor. Each [[youth|age]] is not a physiological stage but a *part played* — "His Acts being seuen ages" (010-151) — a role in a script over which the player has no control.

The soldier's [[youth|age]] occupies the structural center of the speech and is given the most vivid treatment. "Full of strange oaths, and bearded like the Pard" (010-158) captures the soldier's performative masculinity; "Ielous in [[honor]], sodaine, and quicke in quarrell" (010-159) anatomizes the code of [[honor]] as a pathology; and "Seeking the bubble Reputation / Euen in the Canons mouth" [010-160–161] distills the soldier's entire existence into a single, devastating image. The bubble is insubstantial, ephemeral, and self-destroying — to seek it in a cannon's mouth is to die for an illusion.

### Jaques as Speaker

The choice of [[jaques|Jaques]] as the monologue's speaker is crucial. He is the play's designated melancholic, the "Monsieur" whose discontent is both his identity and his performance. His earlier exchange with [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] establishes his stance: he has just laughed "sans intermission / An houre by his diall" [010-034–035] at [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s meditation on time, and has declared his ambition "for a motley coat" (010-045) — [[touchstone|the fool]]'s license to mock without consequence. When [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] grants his "suite" but warns that he would use it to "Cleanse the foule bodie of th' infected world" (010-063), [[jaques|Jaques]] responds with his famous defense of the satirist's generality [010-073–089].

This context illuminates the Seven Ages speech. When [[jaques|Jaques]] describes the soldier "Seeking the bubble Reputation / Euen in the Canons mouth" [010-160–161], he is describing a species of folly he himself avoids; when he reaches "the leane and slipper'd Pantaloone" (010-166), with his "youthfull hose well sau'd, a world too wide, / For his shrunke shanke" [010-168–169], he is describing what he fears becoming. The speech is thus not impersonal philosophy but self-portrait by displacement — [[jaques|Jaques]] anatomizes the world's stage because he cannot bear to be merely another player on it. His position outside the seven ages is his defense against them; by cataloguing, he exempts himself.

Yet the play quietly undermines this exemption. [[jaques|Jaques]]' own role — the melancholic philosopher — is itself a part, as conventional as the lover or the soldier. His "sadnesse" is "compounded of many simples, extracted from many obiects" [012-013–014], as [[rosalind|Rosalind]] later observes. The man who names the seven parts is playing an eighth.

### The Play's Response

The speech is immediately followed by [[orlando|Orlando]]'s return with [[adam|Adam]] (010-175) — a living counterexample to [[jaques|Jaques]]' schema. [[adam|Adam]], "an old poore man" (010-135) in his actual old [[youth|age]], is not a pantaloon but a figure of dignity and loyalty. He has followed [[orlando|Orlando]] "many a weary steppe / Limpt in pure [[love|loue]]" [010-136–137], and his first words upon arriving at the banquet are not the piping treble of senility but a dignified acknowledgment: "I scarce can speake to thanke you for my selfe" (010-180). The play stages the monologue and then quietly refutes it with embodied experience.

This pattern — thesis followed by living counterexample — is characteristic of *As You Like It*'s method. The play presents philosophical positions only to test them against the messy reality of human behavior. [[jaques|Jaques]]' elegant pessimism is no match for [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s hospitality (010-111), [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] to [[adam|Adam]] [010-133–139], or the communal feast that follows. The Seven Ages speech is magnificent, but the play's judgment is that it is insufficient. Life requires not just diagnosis but participation.

## Key Passages

### The Opening Proposition

> "All the world's a stage, / And all the men and women, meerely Players" [010-147–148]

The two lines that have entered the language. "Meerely" is the key word: in Early Modern English it means both "entirely" and "merely" — to be a player is to be nothing more and nothing less. The metaphor is not celebratory; in [[jaques|Jaques]]' mouth, it is reductionist. The world is a stage, but there is no audience to applaud, and no backstage to retreat to.

### The Entrance and Exit Frame

> "They haue their Exits and their Entrances, / And one man in his time playes many parts" [010-149–150]

These lines supply the conceit's mechanism. Life is a succession of entrances and exits — birth and death frame each individual performance — and the "many parts" guarantee that no single identity is stable. The infant who "mewles" becomes the lover who "sighs" becomes the pantaloon who "whistles." Identity is not essence but sequence.

### The Seven Ages

> "His Acts being seuen ages. At first the Infant, / Mewling, and puking in the Nurses armes" [010-151–152]

The catalogue proceeds with theatrical inevitability. Each [[youth|age]] is given exactly the length it needs to sketch its type and no more. The compression is the art: Shakespeare reduces a lifetime to 28 lines without making it feel rushed. The order is chronological but the effect is cumulative — each [[youth|age]] adds a layer of folly until the tower topples.

### The Soldier

> "Seeking the bubble Reputation / Euen in the Canons mouth" [010-160–161]

The soldier's [[youth|age]] is the speech's satiric peak. "Bubble Reputation" is one of Shakespeare's most compact images: the soldier risks death for a prize that is both insubstantial (a bubble) and ephemeral (it pops). The phrase hovers between contempt and pity — [[jaques|Jaques]] cannot decide whether the soldier is more foolish or more tragic, and the image refuses to resolve the question.

### The Justice

> "In faire round belly, with good Capon lin'd, / With eyes seuere, and beard of formall cut, / Full of wise sawes, and moderne instances" [010-162–164]

The justice's [[youth|age]] is the speech's most social critique. The "good Capon" — a castrated rooster fattened for the table — hints at judicial corruption: the justice's grave demeanor conceals a well-fed self-interest. His "wise sawes" and "moderne instances" (010-164) are the hollow rhetoric of authority, wisdom performed rather than possessed.

### The Pantaloon

> "Into the leane and slipper'd Pantaloone, / With spectacles on nose, and pouch on side, / His youthfull hose well sau'd, a world too wide, / For his shrunke shanke" [010-166–169]

The pantaloon — named for the stock character of Italian comedy, Pantalone, the miserly old man — is the speech's most pitiless portrait. He has preserved his youthful clothing ("youthfull hose well sau'd") only to discover that his body has shrunk away from it ("a world too wide"). The image is cruel and comic: the pantaloon is ridiculous precisely because he refuses to acknowledge his [[youth|age]], clinging to a [[youth]] his shrunken shanks belie.

### The Final Age

> "Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing" (010-174)

The famous closing line. The French "sans" (without) — borrowed from [[jaques|Jaques]]' affectation or Shakespeare's? — accumulates with hammering finality. The line strips away each sense, each faculty, until nothing remains. It is the rhetorical equivalent of the oblivion it describes. The progression from English particulars ("teeth," "eyes," "taste") to the totalizing French formula ("sans euery thing") enacts the dissolution it describes: first the specific losses, then the absolute.

## The Classical and Medieval Tradition

Shakespeare's seven ages belong to a long tradition of [[youth|age]]-of-man schemata. The Hippocratic school divided life into seven-year periods; Ptolemy assigned each [[youth|age]] to a planet (Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn); medieval writers expanded the scheme to include moral and spiritual dimensions. The *Secreta Secretorum*, widely read in the Renaissance, describes the seven ages in terms of humoral balance, and the motif appears in visual art from cathedral sculpture to emblem books.

Shakespeare's innovation is to recast the tradition in theatrical terms. Where earlier schemes described physiological change (the cooling of the humors, the weakening of the body), Shakespeare describes changes in *performance* — the roles one plays, the costumes one wears, the voice one speaks in. The [[youth|age]]-of-man becomes not a medical condition but a theatrical repertoire. This shift from body to role aligns the speech with the play's larger preoccupation with identity as performance, a theme explored most fully through [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s disguise as [[ganimed|Ganymede]].

## Jaques and the Satirist's Privilege

[[jaques|Jaques]]' earlier speech (2.7.43–88) — in which he boasts of his "[[freedom|liberty]]... as large a Charter as the winde, / To blow on whom I please" [010-051–052] — provides the philosophical frame for the Seven Ages. The satirist claims the right to expose folly without being implicated in it. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] punctures this claim: "Most mischieuous foule sin, in chiding sin" (010-067) — the satirist who condemns sin from a position of experience is a hypocrite, not a physician.

The Seven Ages monologue is [[jaques|Jaques]]' most eloquent exercise of the satirist's license. He anatomizes the world's roles from a position of apparent detachment — he is the observer, not the observed; the cataloguer, not the catalogued. But the play's implicit judgment, reinforced by [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s later diagnosis of his "sadnesse" as a "humor" [012-012–016], is that this detachment is itself a performance. The man who sees through all roles is playing the most self-deceiving role of all.

## Theatrical and Cultural Legacy

The Seven Ages speech has had an afterlife independent of the play. It was excerpted in eighteenth-century anthologies of "beauties" of Shakespeare; it provided the structure for Jacques Callot's and [[william|William]] Hogarth's visual treatments of the ages of man; it lent its title and framework to countless essays, sermons, and self-help books. In the twentieth century, it became a staple of audition monologues and school recitations — a fate that has both preserved and flattened it.

The speech's durability derives from its combination of universality and compression. It offers a complete life-narrative in under thirty lines, and its theatrical conceit — that we are all merely players — is flexible enough to accommodate both comic and tragic interpretations. An actor playing [[jaques|Jaques]] can deliver the speech as bitter satire, as melancholy wisdom, or as something between the two; the text supports all three.
