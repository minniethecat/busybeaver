# The Wrestling Match

## Summary

The wrestling match in Act 1, Scene 2 is the play's inciting event. [[orlando|Orlando]], the untrained youngest son of Sir Rowland de Boys, defeats [[charles|Charles]] — [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick's]] professional wrestler — in a public contest witnessed by the entire court at [[duke-fredericks-court|Duke Frederick's Court]]. The victory proves [[orlando|Orlando]]'s innate [[virtue-and-gentility|virtue]], wins [[rosalind|Rosalind's]] love, and reveals his identity to [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], who turns hostile upon learning [[orlando|Orlando]] is the son of his enemy. The match thus produces the play's three plot lines: the love plot ([[rosalind|Rosalind]]-[[orlando|Orlando]]), the villainy plot ([[oliver|Oliver]]'s intensified hatred), and the political plot ([[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s animosity toward the de Boys family).

## Context: [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Grievance

[[orlando|Orlando]] enters the wrestling match not as an ambitious athlete but as a desperate man. His elder brother [[oliver|Oliver]] has denied him education and inheritance, keeping him "rustically at home" (001-002) and treating him as a peasant despite his noble birth. When [[orlando|Orlando]] learns that [[charles|Charles]] — a professional wrestler who has already injured three challengers — will compete at court, he resolves to fight. He tells [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] that he has nothing to lose:

> I shall do my friends no wrong, for I haue none to lament me: the world no iniurie, for in it I haue nothing: onely in the world I fil vp a place, which may bee better supplied, when I haue made it emptie (002-095)

Unbeknownst to [[orlando|Orlando]], [[oliver|Oliver]] has conspired with [[charles|Charles]] to have his brother killed or maimed. When [[charles|Charles]] warns [[oliver|Oliver]] that [[orlando|Orlando]] intends to "come in disguis'd against mee to try a fall," [[oliver|Oliver]] replies: "I had as liefe thou didst breake his necke as his finger" (001-047). The match is thus framed as both a test of virtue and a murder attempt — [[orlando|Orlando]] seeks [[virtue-and-gentility|honor]], while [[oliver|Oliver]] seeks [[brotherhood|fratricide]].

[[duke-fredericks-court|The court]]'s foreknowledge of [[charles|Charles]]'s brutality sets the stakes. [[le-beau|Le Beau]] reports that [[charles|Charles]] "broke three of his ribbes, that there is little hope of life in him" (002-065) against the previous challenger, and that the old man who was father to the three injured sons makes "such pittiful dole ouer them, that all the beholders take his part with weeping" (002-065). The match is not sport but mortal combat.

## The Combat

The wrestling itself occupies only a few lines of stage direction and dialogue, but Shakespeare packs it with dramatic energy. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] presides, having failed to dissuade [[orlando|Orlando]]: "Come on, since the [[youth]] will not be intreated, His owne perill on his forwardnesse" (002-075).

[[charles|Charles]] enters boasting: "Come, where is this yong gallant, that is so desirous to lie with his mother earth?" (002-100). [[orlando|Orlando]] replies with quiet dignity: "Readie Sir, but his will hath in it a more modest working" (002-102). [[duke-senior|The Duke]] limits them to "one fall" (002-104), and [[charles|Charles]] mocks the restriction: "No, I warrant your Grace you shall not entreat him to a second" (002-105).

The stage direction is minimal — simply "Wrastle" — but the reactions of the spectators convey the shock of the outcome. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] cries "Oh excellent yong man" (002-110), and [[celia|Celia]] adds, "If I had a thunderbolt in mine eie, I can tell who should downe" (002-111). A shout marks the victory, and [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] commands "No more, no more" (002-113). [[charles|Charles]] is carried off, unable to speak — the brute force that broke three men is silenced by untrained [[virtue-and-gentility|virtue]].

[[orlando|Orlando]], still eager, protests: "Yes I beseech your Grace, I am not yet well breath'd" (002-114) — a moment that reveals his raw physical energy and his hunger to prove himself further.

## [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Intervention

Before the match, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] attempt to dissuade [[orlando|Orlando]] from fighting. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], in a rare moment of concern, urges them: "Speake to him Ladies, see if you can mooue him" (002-083). Their intervention creates the first encounter between [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[orlando|Orlando]], and the chemistry between them is immediate.

[[celia|Celia]] warns: "Yong Gentleman, your spirits are too bold for your yeares: you haue seene cruell proofe of this mans strength" (002-093). [[rosalind|Rosalind]] adds: "your [[honor|reputation]] shall not therefore be misprised: we wil make it our suite to the Duke, that the wrastling might not go forward" (002-094).

[[orlando|Orlando]] refuses, but his speech of refusal — "let your faire eies, and gentle wishes go with mee to my triall" (002-095) — transforms the moment from contest to courtship. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] responds with the first stirring of love: "The little strength that I haue, I would it were with you" (002-096). [[celia|Celia]] echoes: "And mine to eeke out hers" (002-097).

Their final words before the bout carry the weight of prayer: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] says "praie heauen I be deceiu'd in you" (002-098), and [[celia|Celia]] adds "Your hearts desires be with you" (002-099). When [[orlando|Orlando]] defeats [[charles|Charles]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s joy — "Oh excellent yong man" (002-110) — is the first public declaration of feeling, though not yet love declared.

## Aftermath and Reward

The aftermath unfolds in three sharp movements: recognition, rejection, and reward.

**Identity revealed.** [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] asks [[orlando|Orlando]] his name, and [[orlando|Orlando]] answers with pride: "[[orlando|Orlando]] my Liege, the yongest sonne of Sir Roland de Boys" (002-120). [[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s response is immediate hostility:

> I would thou hadst beene son to some man else,
> The world esteem'd thy father honourable,
> But I did finde him still mine enemie (002-122)

[[duke-frederick|Frederick]] departs, leaving the victory soured by political resentment. [[celia|Celia]] is shocked: "Were I my Father (Coze) would I do this?" (002-130). [[orlando|Orlando]] replies with defiance that echoes the play's central moral claim:

> I am more proud to be Sir Rolands sonne,
> His yongest sonne, and would not change that calling
> To be adopted heire to Fredricke (002-131)

**The love token.** [[rosalind|Rosalind]], moved by both [[orlando|Orlando]]'s victory and his loyalty to her father, gives him a chain from her neck: "Weare this for me: one out of suites with [[fortune|fortune]]" (002-147). The phrase is precise — [[orlando|Orlando]] is out of [[fortune|Fortune]]'s favor (dispossessed, threatened with death), but through [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s love he has found a gift that [[fortune|Fortune]] cannot give. She adds, "That could giue more, but that her hand lacks meanes" (002-148) — acknowledging her own constrained position as a dependent in her uncle's court.

**[[love|Love]]-struck silence.** [[orlando|Orlando]], who moments before had the physical [[youth|courage]] to face [[charles|Charles]], is struck dumb by [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s beauty and kindness:

> What passion hangs these waights vpon my toong?
> I cannot speake to her, yet she vrg'd conference.
> O poore Orlando! thou art ouerthrowne (002-161)

The physical victor is now the emotional victim. The symmetry is deliberate: [[orlando|Orlando]] overthrows [[charles|Charles]], and love immediately overthrows [[orlando|Orlando]]. The word "ouerthrowne" links the wrestling ring to the heart, making the match the origin of both his public triumph and his private captivity.

**Danger revealed.** [[le-beau|Le Beau]] warns [[orlando|Orlando]] to flee: "Albeit you haue deseru'd High commendation, true applause, and [[love|loue]]; Yet such is now the Dukes condition, That he misconsters all that you haue done." [[orlando|Orlando]], still processing the emotional impact, asks which woman was [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and [[le-beau|Le Beau]] confirms the identity of his beloved and warns of [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s malice. The scene closes on [[orlando|Orlando]]'s soliloquy:

> Thus must I from the smoake into the smother,
> From tyrant Duke, vnto a tyrant Brother.
> But heauenly Rosaline. (002-192)

The match has given him a name, a love, and two mortal enemies — a complete transformation of his circumstances.

## Dramatic Significance

The wrestling match functions as the play's structural keystone. It is simultaneously a test of [[virtue-and-gentility|character]], a love scene, a political confrontation, and a conspiracy exposed.

**The [[nature|Nature]]-[[fortune|Fortune]] argument enacted.** [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] have just debated the relationship between [[nature|Nature]] and [[fortune|Fortune]] when the match provides a living demonstration. [[orlando|Orlando]] has been denied every advantage of [[fortune|Fortune]] — education, inheritance, brotherly care — but his [[nature|Nature]] (inborn [[virtue-and-gentility|virtue]] and strength) proves sufficient. The match is an argument on stage: natural worth triumphs over artificial power.

**Three plots, one event.** The match launches all three of the play's narrative trajectories. The love plot begins with [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s gift of the chain. The villainy plot intensifies when [[oliver|Oliver]]'s proxy fails and his hatred deepens to direct murder plots. The political plot crystallizes when [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] recognizes [[orlando|Orlando]] as the son of his enemy, establishing the us-versus-them dynamic that drives both courts into conflict with the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]].

**Public virtue, private vice.** The match is a public event witnessed by the entire court, and its outcome is unambiguous — [[orlando|Orlando]] is the better man. Yet the responses to this truth divide along moral lines. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] respond with admiration and love; [[duke-frederick|Frederick]] responds with resentment; [[oliver|Oliver]] responds with murderous fury. The match thus tests not only [[orlando|Orlando]] but everyone who witnesses it, revealing their [[persons|characters]] more clearly than any soliloquy could.
