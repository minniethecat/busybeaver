# Phebe

## Character Overview

Phebe is the disdainful shepherdess of the pastoral subplot -- a stock figure from Petrarchan love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]] whose cruelty to the faithful [[silvius|Silvius]] is punished by her own humiliation. Unlike [[rosalind|Rosalind]], who uses her [[disguise-plan|disguise]] as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] to master love, Phebe is mastered by it: her instant infatuation with the sharp-tongued "[[youth]]" exposes the irrationality at the heart of romantic attraction. Her eventual acceptance of [[silvius|Silvius]] -- compelled by her rash promise at [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]] -- completes the pastoral love plot as comedy, not romance. She is a figure of comic humiliation who learns, through her own absurdity, the lesson she refused to learn from [[silvius|Silvius]]: "Who euer lov'd, that lou'd not at first sight?" (015-085)

Phebe appears in three scenes (Act 3 Scene 5, Act 5 Scene 2, Act 5 Scene 4) plus an epistolary appearance in Act 4 Scene 3 where her love-letter to [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is read aloud. Though her stage time is brief, she anchors the play's sharpest satire of Petrarchan convention -- and its most explicit statement of love's irrationality.

## The Cruel Mistress

[[phebe|Phebe]]'s first appearance establishes her as the cold beloved of the Petrarchan dyad. [[silvius|Silvius]] pleads with desperate tenderness: "Sweet Phebe doe not scorne me, do not Phebe" (015-002). Her response is clinical demolition, dismantling the conceit that eyes can wound:

> **Phebe**: Thou tellst me there is murder in mine eye, / 'Tis pretty sure, and very probable, / That eyes that are the frailst, and softest things, / Who shut their coward gates on atomyes, / Should be called tyrants, butchers, murtherers. (015-012)

Her mockery crescendos into outright derision: she invites [[silvius|Silvius]] to faint from her gaze, and when he cannot, accuses him of lying. The performance is pure comic cruelty -- the beloved as tyrant, treating [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] as a nuisance. When [[silvius|Silvius]] prophesies that she will one day know love's invisible wounds, she dismisses him with cold finality:

> **Phebe**: But till that time / Come not thou neere me: and when that time comes, / Afflict me with thy mockes, pitty me not, / As till that time I shall not pitty thee (015-035)

This refusal of pity is the moral crux of her character. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s intervention, which follows immediately, is a direct answer to this hardness.

## Falling for Ganymede

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s rebuke transforms the scene. Her opening salvo -- "Who might be your mother / That you insult, exult, and all at once / Ouer the wretched?" (015-039) -- cuts through [[phebe|Phebe]]'s [[pride|self-regard]] with devastating precision. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] catalogues Phebe's ordinary features and advises her to "Sell when you can, you are not for all markets" (015-064), concluding "Foule is most foule, being foule to be a scoffer" (015-066).

The result is not repentance but infatuation. [[phebe|Phebe]]'s response to being berated is to fall in love with the berater:

> **Phebe**: Sweet youth, I pray you chide a yere together, / I had rather here you chide, then this man wooe (015-068)

This is the play's most explicitly comic demonstration of love's irrationality. Phebe, who scorned [[silvius|Silvius]] for his [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]], now finds herself exactly where he is -- hopelessly in love with someone who scorns her. Her parting couplet, borrowed from [[christopher-marlowe|Marlowe]]'s *Hero and Leander*, elevates the absurdity to epigram:

> **Phebe**: Dead Shepheard, now I find thy saw of might, / Who euer lov'd, that lou'd not at first sight? (015-085)

Her catalogue of [[ganimed|Ganymede]]'s features (015-119) is a masterpiece of comic self-deception: "It is a pretty [[youth]], not very prettie ... His leg is but so so, and yet 'tis well" -- she anatomizes him feature by feature, protesting she loves him not, while every line declares the opposite.

In Act 4 Scene 3, her love-letter to [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is delivered by [[silvius|Silvius]] and read aloud by [[rosalind|Rosalind]], who performs it as "a boysterous and a cruell stile" (018-034). The letter's opening -- "Art thou god, to Shepherd turn'd? / That a maidens heart hath burn'd" (018-044) -- casts [[ganimed|Ganymede]] in divine terms, completing [[phebe|Phebe]]'s journey from cruel mistress to abject supplicant.

In Act 5 Scene 2, Phebe confronts [[ganimed|Ganymede]] about the public reading of her letter, but her indignation melts into participation in the play's great litany of love. Each lover declares what love is, and each declares for whom:

> **Phebe**: Youth, you haue done me much vngentlenesse, / To shew the letter that I writ to you (020-025)

She joins the antiphonal chorus ("And I for [[ganimed|Ganimed]]" at 020-034 and 020-039), her voice one of four in the play's most structurally formal statement about love's universality. When all four lovers ask the same question -- "If this be so, why blame you me to [[love|loue]] you?" (020-051) -- [[phebe|Phebe]]'s inclusion in the pattern confirms that her ridiculous passion is no different from [[orlando|Orlando]]'s heroic one.

## Comic Resolution

In Act 5 Scene 4, Phebe is trapped by her own words. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (still disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) extracts her public promise: "You say, you'l marrie me, if I be willing" -- and Phebe answers, "That will I, should I die the houre after" (022-014). The bargain is then sealed: if she refuses [[ganimed|Ganymede]], she must marry [[silvius|Silvius]]. "So is the bargaine" (022-017), she agrees.

When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reveals herself as a woman, [[phebe|Phebe]]'s instant recognition is tinged with comic dismay:

> **Phebe**: If sight & shape be true, why then my loue adieu (022-073)

Her capitulation to [[silvius|Silvius]] follows with admirable promptness:

> **Phebe**: I wil not eate my word, now thou art mine, / Thy faith, my fancie to thee doth combine. (022-102)

The rhyming couplet is the formal marker of comic resolution. Phebe keeps her word, [[silvius|Silvius]] gets his wish, and the [[pastoralism|pastoral]] love plot closes on a note of orderly acceptance rather than passionate love. Shakespeare does not ask us to believe in their romantic happiness -- only in the comic logic that rewards the faithful and humbles the proud.

## Relationships

- **[[silvius|Silvius]]** -- Her faithful shepherd-lover whom she scorns throughout the play; trapped by her own promise, she marries him in Act 5 Scene 4. Their relationship moves from "scorned-suitor" to "husband" through comic coercion rather than romantic development.
- **[[rosalind|Rosalind]]** (as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) -- The object of [[phebe|Phebe]]'s sudden infatuation. Phebe falls in love with the sharp-tongued "[[youth]]" precisely because he scorns her, inverting the Petrarchan dynamic she herself embodied. When [[ganimed|Ganymede]] is revealed as [[rosalind|Rosalind]], Phebe's infatuation dissolves into an "eventual-friend" relationship.
- **[[rosalind|Rosalind]]** (as herself) -- The architect of [[phebe|Phebe]]'s humiliation and eventual comic resolution. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] rebukes Phebe, becomes the unwitting object of her love, and finally forces her to accept [[silvius|Silvius]].
