## Overview

Wit is a central comic engine in As You Like It, driving both character revelation and thematic development. The play contrasts several registers of wit — [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s nimble verbal play, [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s professional foolery, [[jaques|Jaques]]' melancholy satire, and [[celia|Celia]]'s sharp observations — creating a layered comedy in which language itself becomes a site of contest and identity. The word "wit" appears nineteen times in the Folio text, concentrated in the court and [[freedom|exile]] scenes rather than the pastoral interludes, suggesting that verbal wit is understood as a specifically courtly mode that [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] setting both enables and destabilizes [^1].

## Touchstone and [[touchstone|the Fool]]'s Wit

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] embodies the licensed wit of the professional fool. In Act 1, Scene 2, [[celia|Celia]] and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] engage him in a metatheatrical exchange on the relationship between wit and folly:

> since the little wit that fooles haue was silenced, the little foolerie that wise men haue makes a great shew (002-039)

This passage inverts the expected hierarchy: [[touchstone|the fool]]'s "little wit" is more valuable than the wise man's "little foolerie." The fool becomes a "whetstone of the wits" (002-019), sharpening others' understanding through paradox and inversion [^2].

## [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Verbal Dexterity

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s wit is proactive rather than professional. Her verbal contests — with [[celia|Celia]] in Act 1, with [[orlando|Orlando]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], with Phoebe and [[silvius|Silvius]] — consistently demonstrate her intellectual superiority within the pastoral world. Her epilogue speech extends this wit into direct audience address, collapsing the boundary between character and actor through linguistic play.

## Wit vs. Fortune

The play frames wit as humanity's defense against [[fortune|Fortune]]'s caprice. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] declares "As wit and fortune will" (002-050) when facing the wrestling match, and the extended metaphor of nature giving "wit to flout at [[fortune|Fortune]]" (002-018) positions verbal intelligence as a form of agency within an unpredictable world.

## Nature's Whetstone: The Wit/Folly Dialectic

The play's most concentrated passage on wit arrives early, in [[celia|Celia]]'s elaborate conceit on [[nature|Nature]] and [[fortune|Fortune]] (002-018) (002-019):

> No; when Nature hath made a faire creature, may she not by Fortune fall into the fire? though nature hath giuen vs wit to flout at Fortune, hath not Fortune sent in this foole to cut off the argument? (002-018)

[[celia|Celia]] constructs a three-term relationship: [[nature|Nature]] gives wit, [[fortune|Fortune]] sends the fool, and [[touchstone|the fool]]'s dullness paradoxically sharpens wit. Her next speech completes the figure:

> Nature, who perceiueth our naturall wits too dull to reason of such goddesses, hath sent this Naturall for our whetstone: for alwaies the dulnesse of the foole, is the whetstone of the wits. (002-019)

"Whetstone" is the operative metaphor. The fool is a grinding stone — coarse, dull, but essential for sharpening blades. Without the resistance of folly, wit grows blunt. This is the play's implicit defense of its own comic method: apparent nonsense (foolery, disguise, pastoral play) is the necessary medium through which intelligence operates.

## Repartee as Character Revelation

The play systematically uses verbal contests to reveal character hierarchy. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s duel of wits with [[corin|Corin]] in Act 3, Scene 2 — in which the professional fool dismantles [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s plain-spoken wisdom through logical sophistry (012-014) through (012-034) — demonstrates both [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s verbal facility and its limits: he wins the argument but loses the moral point. [[corin|Corin]]'s simple rebuttal:

> Sir, I am a true Labourer, I earne that I eate: get that I weare; owe no man hate, enuie no mans happinesse: glad of other mens good content with my harme: and the greatest of my pride, is to see my Ewes graze, & my Lambes sucke (012-034)

This speech exposes [[touchstone|the fool]]'s wit as parasitic, dependent on a courtly education that has no purchase on pastoral reality.

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s wit, by contrast, is integrative rather than destructive. Her pretended cure of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s love-sickness in Acts 3 and 4 — the long catechism on the nature of love — uses wit not to mock love but to test and deepen it. When [[orlando|Orlando]] claims he would die if [[rosalind|Rosalind]] rejected him, she replies:

> No faith, die by Attorney: the poore world is almost six thousand yeeres old, and in all this time there was not anie man died in his owne person (videlicet) in a loue cause (016-046)

The deflation is comic but not cynical; it clears away Petrarchan posturing to make room for the real thing.

## Wit and Social Hierarchy

The distribution of wit in the play maps onto social position. The wittiest [[persons|characters]] — [[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[touchstone|Touchstone]], [[celia|Celia]] — are all court exiles who bring courtly verbal sophistication into the [[forest-of-arden-place|forest]]. The pastoral inhabitants — [[corin|Corin]], [[silvius|Silvius]], [[phebe|Phoebe]], [[audrey|Audrey]] — lack this register of wit, and the play's comedy often depends on the gap. Yet the play is careful not to equate wit with moral superiority: [[corin|Corin]]'s plain speech contains as much wisdom as any courtier's wordplay, and [[audrey|Audrey]]'s uncomprehending responses to [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s elaborate wit expose [[touchstone|the fool]]'s verbal display as self-indulgent performance rather than genuine communication.

## [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s Comic Method: Parody and Inversion

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s wit operates through systematic parody. He parodies courtly manners in his duel with [[corin|Corin]], parodies romantic love in his courtship of [[audrey|Audrey]], and parodies philosophical discourse in his meditation on time reported by [[jaques|Jaques]] (010-018) (010-021). Each parody accomplishes two things at once: it exposes the artifice of the thing parodied while demonstrating [[touchstone|the fool]]'s mastery of the forms he mocks. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] can only parody courtliness because he has internalized it; his wit is courtly wit turned against itself.

[[touchstone|The fool]]'s relationship to truth is characteristically oblique. He never tells the truth directly — that would violate his professional license, which depends on indirection — but his parodies consistently reveal truths that more direct speech would obscure. When he tells [[corin|Corin]] that court manners are "the only good" and [[corin|Corin]] demurs, [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s apparent defense of courtliness is actually its most devastating critique, because it provokes [[corin|Corin]]'s plain-spoken rebuttal, which carries more moral weight than any direct attack could.

## [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s Wit as Education

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s wit serves a pedagogical function absent from [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s. Where [[touchstone|Touchstone]] exposes folly by amplifying it, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] exposes folly by curing it. Her love-cure of [[orlando|Orlando]] is the play's most sustained exercise in wit-as-education: she uses verbal play to dismantle [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Petrarchan posturing, replacing conventional love-language with something tested and earned.

The method is Socratic. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] does not tell [[orlando|Orlando]] what love is; she asks questions, proposes counter-examples, and performs a version of love that is so obviously a performance that [[orlando|Orlando]] is forced to examine his own performance. When she tells him that "men haue died from time to time, and wormes haue eaten them, but not for [[love|loue]]" (016-046), the wit is not merely deflationary — it is a lesson in distinguishing genuine feeling from conventional expression. By the play's end, [[orlando|Orlando]] has learned enough to participate in the communal resolution without needing [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s verbal guidance.

[^2]: The whetstone metaphor has a classical lineage — Horace's *Ars Poetica* describes the poet as a whetstone that sharpens others though itself cannot cut — but Shakespeare reorients it from artisanal craft to social performance.
