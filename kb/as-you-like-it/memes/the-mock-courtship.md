# The Mock Courtship

The mock courtship between [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]]) and [[orlando|Orlando]] is the dramatic centerpiece of *As You Like It*, occupying much of Acts 3 and 4. Having fled to the Forest of Arden and adopted her male disguise, Rosalind encounters the love-struck Orlando hanging verses on trees and seizes the opportunity to test the depth and constancy of his affection.

## The Love-Cure Proposal

When Rosalind-as-Ganymede first meets Orlando in the forest, she challenges his romantic posturing with a pragmatic offer. She claims to have cured a previous lover of his passion through an unconventional method:

> "He was to imagine me his love, his mistress; and I set him every day to woo me."

The proposed "cure" is paradoxical — it requires Orlando to pretend Ganymede is Rosalind and woo "him" daily, which keeps Orlando constantly in Rosalind's company rather than driving him away. [[celia|Celia]] immediately identifies the irony, remarking that her cousin has found a way to be wooed while maintaining her disguise.

## The Lessons Begin

The mock courtship unfolds across multiple encounters. In each session, Rosalind-as-Ganymede plays the part of a capricious, contradictory beloved — testing Orlando's patience with rapid mood swings and impossible demands:

> "Come, woo me, woo me; for now I am in a holiday humour and like enough to consent. What would you say to me now, an I were your very very Rosalind?"

She challenges his idealized Petrarchan notions of love, insisting that no man has ever died for love and that the "poor world is almost six thousand years old, and in all this time there was not any man died in his own person, videlicet, in a love-cause." This speech is a sustained comic deflation of the courtly love tradition, delivered by a woman whose own heart is deeply engaged.

## Testing Orlando

The mock courtship serves as Rosalind's laboratory for testing Orlando's character. She probes his sincerity, his patience, his wit, and his devotion — all while safely concealed behind the double layer of her Ganymede disguise. When [[silvius|Silvius]] brings [[phebe|Phebe]]'s scornful letter, Rosalind-as-Ganymede orchestrates an impromptu pageant of love's contradictions:

> "Pray you, no more of this; 'tis like the howling of Irish wolves against the moon."

Her sharp tongue conceals genuine vulnerability. When Orlando is late for an appointment (having been wounded saving [[oliver|Oliver]] from the lioness), Rosalind's distress breaks through the Ganymede facade. She faints at the sight of Orlando's blood on a napkin — a moment where performance and reality dangerously converge.

## Comic Irony and Theatrical Play

The mock courtship operates on multiple levels of theatrical self-consciousness. The audience watches a boy actor (in Shakespeare's time) playing Rosalind, who plays Ganymede, who plays "Rosalind" for Orlando — creating a vertiginous layering of identity and performance. This metatheatrical quality makes the scenes both a critique of romantic convention and a celebration of love's power to transform.

The device also allows Shakespeare to dramatize the central pastoral paradox: the disguise that liberates Rosalind from courtly constraints also limits her ability to act on her desires. She can speak to Orlando only through the mask of Ganymede, meaning her most intimate conversations with her beloved are technically performances.

## Resolution

The mock courtship ends not with a dramatic unmasking but with a promise of transformation. Rosalind-as-Ganymede tells Orlando that "he" can produce the real Rosalind through magic at the wedding gathering in Act 5. This promise sets up the play's climactic scene, where Rosalind sheds both her male attire and her assumed persona to emerge as herself — restored to her identity and her Orlando.
