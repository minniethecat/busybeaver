## Definition

From its opening scene, As You Like It frames human behavior as sport. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s wrestling match with [[charles|Charles]] is the play's first "sport" — a contest that should be mere entertainment but carries mortal stakes. The play systematically explores the boundary between play and seriousness: [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s game of curing [[orlando|Orlando]] of love, [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s games with language and social convention, and the deeper metatheatrical game in which the audience watches players pretending to be [[persons|characters]] who are themselves pretending. Sport in As You Like It is never merely playful — it is how [[persons|characters]] test identities, negotiate desire, and rehearse the social roles they will ultimately inhabit.

## In As You Like It

The play's first explicit reference to sport comes from [[rosalind|Rosalind]] herself. When [[celia|Celia]] proposes they "deuise sports" to pass the time, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] asks: "What shall be our sport then?" (002-008). The answer, it turns out, will be love — the sport of falling in love, of watching others fall in love, and of playing at love's conventions. The exchange establishes the play's central metatheatrical conceit: all the action in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] is a kind of sport, a deliberate game with its own rules and stakes.

The wrestling match that follows is the play's most literal sport. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s wry commentary — "But what is the sport Monsieur, that the Ladies haue lost?" (002-067) — exposes the gap between the courtly pretense of entertainment and the real danger of broken ribs. The wrestling is a game, but it is also a competition for survival and social standing, one in which [[orlando|Orlando]] wins [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s love and [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s enmity simultaneously.

In [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], sport takes more elaborate forms. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s game with [[orlando|Orlando]] — in which she, disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], will pretend to be [[rosalind|Rosalind]] to cure him of being in love with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — is the play's supreme sporting invention. It is a game with rules ("I will thinke you the most pathetical breake-promise, and the most hollow louer"), with roles, with a pretended therapeutic purpose. But as the game proceeds, the boundary between play and reality dissolves: [[orlando|Orlando]]'s love deepens rather than cures, and [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s own feelings strain against the limits of her performance.

[[touchstone|Touchstone]] participates in the sporting spirit through parody. His courtship of [[audrey|Audrey]] is a game — "He that in sport doth call his [[rosalind|Rosalind]]" (018-160) — that mocks the elevated love-game of [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] by reducing it to frank appetite. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] treats language itself as sport, turning every conversation into a contest of [[wit]] that simultaneously entertains and unsettles.

The play's epilogue continues the sporting metaphor to the very end. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] steps out of character to address the audience directly, asking them to "like as much of this Play as please you." The play has been a sport, and the audience are the final players, invited to applaud or not as they see fit. The boundary between game and life, performance and reality, is never settled — and that, the play suggests, is the point.
