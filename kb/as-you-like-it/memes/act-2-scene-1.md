(004-001)Enter [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]: Amyens, and two or three Lords like

(004-002)Forresters.

(004-003)Duk.Sen. Now my Coe-mates, and brothers in [[freedom|exile]]:

(004-004)Hath not old custome made this life more sweete

(004-005)Then that of painted pompe? Are not these woods

(004-006)More free from perill then the enuious Court?

(004-007)Heere feele we not the penaltie of [[adam|Adam]],

(004-008)The seasons difference, as the Icie phange

(004-009)And churlish chiding of the winters winde,

(004-010)Which when it bites and blowes vpon my body

(004-011)Euen till I shrinke with cold, I smile, and say

(004-012)This is no flattery: these are counsellors

(004-013)That feelingly perswade me what I am:

(004-014)Sweet are the vses of aduersitie

(004-015)Which like the toad, ougly and venemous,

(004-016)Weares yet a precious Iewell in his head:

(004-017)And this our life exempt from publike haunt,

(004-018)Findes tongues in trees, bookes in the running brookes,

(004-019)Sermons in stones, and good in euery thing

(004-020)Amien. I would not change it, happy is your Grace

(004-021)That can translate the stubbornnesse of fortune

(004-022)Into so quiet and so sweet a stile

(004-023)Du.Sen. Come, shall we goe and kill vs venison?

(004-024)And yet it irkes me the poore dapled fooles

(004-025)Being natiue Burgers of this desert City,

(004-026)Should in their owne confines with forked heads

(004-027)Haue their round hanches goard

(004-028)1. Lord. Indeed my Lord

(004-029)The melancholy [[jaques|Iaques]] grieues at that,

(004-030)And in that kinde sweares you doe more vsurpe

(004-031)Then doth your brother that hath banish'd you:

(004-032)To day my Lord of [[amiens|Amiens]], and my selfe,

(004-033)Did steale behinde him as he lay along

(004-034)Vnder an oake, whose anticke roote peepes out

(004-035)Vpon the brooke that brawles along this wood,

(004-036)To the which place a poore sequestred Stag

(004-037)That from the Hunters aime had tane a hurt,

(004-038)Did come to languish; and indeed my Lord

(004-039)The wretched annimall heau'd forth such groanes

(004-040)That their discharge did stretch his leatherne coat

(004-041)Almost to bursting, and the big round teares

(004-042)Cours'd one another downe his innocent nose

(004-043)In pitteous chase: and thus the hairie foole,

(004-044)Much marked of the melancholie Iaques,

(004-045)Stood on th' extremest verge of the swift brooke,

(004-046)Augmenting it with teares

(004-047)Du.Sen. But what said Iaques?

(004-048)Did he not moralize this spectacle?

(004-049)1. Lord. O yes, into a thousand similies.

(004-050)First, for his weeping into the needlesse streame;

(004-051)Poore Deere quoth he, thou mak'st a testament

(004-052)As worldlings doe, giuing thy sum of more

(004-053)To that which had too much: then being there alone,

(004-054)Left and abandoned of his veluet friend;

(004-055)'Tis right quoth he, thus miserie doth part

(004-056)The Fluxe of companie: anon a carelesse Heard

(004-057)Full of the pasture, iumps along by him

(004-058)And neuer staies to greet him: I quoth Iaques,

(004-059)Sweepe on you fat and greazie Citizens,

(004-060)'Tis iust the fashion; wherefore doe you looke

(004-061)Vpon that poore and broken bankrupt there?

(004-062)Thus most inuectiuely he pierceth through

(004-063)The body of Countrie, Citie, Court,

(004-064)Yea, and of this our life, swearing that we

(004-065)Are meere vsurpers, tyrants, and whats worse

(004-066)To fright the Annimals, and to kill them vp

(004-067)In their assign'd and natiue dwelling place

(004-068)D.Sen. And did you leaue him in this contemplation?

(004-069)2. Lord. We did my Lord, weeping and commenting

(004-070)Vpon the sobbing Deere

(004-071)Du.Sen. Show me the place,

(004-072)I [[love|loue]] to cope him in these sullen fits,

(004-073)For then he's full of matter

(004-074)1. Lor. Ile bring you to him strait.

(004-075)Exeunt.
