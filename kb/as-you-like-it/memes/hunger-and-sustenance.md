## Definition

Hunger and sustenance form a physical motif in *As You Like It* that tests the moral character of [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] community. Unlike the court, where power is exercised through political decree and fraternal violence, [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] confronts its inhabitants with bodily need — the irreducible demand of hunger that no social performance can defer. The play's central exploration of this motif occurs in Act 2 Scene 7, when [[orlando|Orlando]] bursts into [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s [[green-world|forest]] banquet with his sword drawn, demanding food for himself and his starving servant [[adam|Adam]]. The scene asks whether civilized gentleness can survive the pressure of necessity, and answers by transforming a potential act of violence into a demonstration of communal hospitality.

## In As You Like It

The scene begins with [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and his exiled lords seated at their [[green-world|forest]] feast. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s entrance is violent and absolute: "Forbeare, and eate no more" (010-092). [[jaques|Jaques]]'s deadpan reply — "Why I haue eate none yet" (010-093) — momentarily deflates the tension, but [[orlando|Orlando]]'s demand is serious: "Nor shalt not, till necessity be seru'd" (010-094). The word "necessity" invokes a state beyond social negotiation — the body's claim that precedes and trumps all codes of courtesy.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s response is the moral fulcrum of the scene. Rather than meeting force with force, he answers with an offer of unconditional hospitality:

> Your gentlenesse shall force, more then your force
> Moue vs to gentlenesse [010-108–109]

The chiasmus — gentleness forcing more than force, force moving toward gentleness — enacts in language the transformation the scene performs in action. [[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s response converts a confrontation into an invitation: "sit you downe in gentlenesse" (010-130). [[orlando|Orlando]], moved by this response, "blush[es], and hide[s] my Sword" (010-125) — the weapon yielded not to superior strength but to superior civility.

The scene's second movement deepens the motif. [[orlando|Orlando]], having secured food for himself, does not eat but instead returns to fetch [[adam|Adam]], "Opprest with two weake euils, [[youth|age]], and hunger" (010-138). The line's stark simplicity — two adjectives, two nouns, no ornament — registers the gravity of bodily suffering without melodrama. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s act of carrying the old man to the feast literalizes the ethic of sustenance: food is not merely consumed but shared, and the obligation to sustain extends beyond the self to the vulnerable other.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] welcomes [[adam|Adam]] as warmly as he welcomed [[orlando|Orlando]], and when he discovers that [[orlando|Orlando]] is the son of his old friend Sir Rowland de Boys, the scene's circles of hospitality expand from stranger to guest to kin. The feast, which began as an act of potential violence, ends as a ritual of recognition and incorporation — [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] community proving itself through the act of feeding.
