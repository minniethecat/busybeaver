# Virtue and Gentility

## Definition

In As You Like It, virtue (*vertue*) and gentility are contested concepts: is noble conduct the product of birth ([[nature|Nature]]'s gift) or education ([[fortune|Fortune]]'s gift)? [[orlando|Orlando]], the youngest son of Sir Rowland de Boys, embodies this question. Though born gentle, he has been denied gentlemanly education by his brother [[oliver|Oliver]]. His virtue — expressed through courage at the wrestling match, loyalty to [[adam|Adam]], and [[loyalty-and-service|fidelity]] in love — proves that gentility is a quality of the soul, not of circumstance.

## In As You Like It

The play opens with [[orlando|Orlando]]'s protest against his brother's denial of his "gentility." He is kept "rustically at home" (001-002), denied the education befitting his birth. His argument is that [[nature|Nature]] has made him a gentleman — "the something that nature gaue mee" (001-002) — but [[fortune|Fortune]] (in the form of [[oliver|Oliver]]'s malice) has stripped him of its outward signs. The wrestling match becomes his first opportunity to display the virtue that his upbringing has hidden.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s exiled court in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] provides a laboratory for testing the relationship between virtue and circumstance. Stripped of the trappings of power, the duke and his lords must demonstrate their worth through conduct alone. [[duke-senior|The duke]]'s celebration of [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s "counsellors" — the natural elements that "feelingly perswade me what I am" (004-012) — suggests that virtue is best revealed in adversity.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s treatment of [[adam|Adam]] exemplifies gentility in action. Though desperate with hunger, [[orlando|Orlando]] carries the aged servant on his back: "I thought that all things had beene sauage heere" (010-113). His willingness to risk death to find food for [[adam|Adam]] demonstrates that true virtue expresses itself in service to others.

The play's resolution restores virtue's visible rewards. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s conversion — from plotting fratricide to risking his life against the lioness — proves that even the vicious can be reformed. [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s sudden conversion at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge (022-115) similarly demonstrates virtue's power to transform. The [[regeneration|restoration]] of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom reunites virtue with its outward signs.

## Key Passages

### [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Complaint (Act 1, Scene 1)

> the something that nature gaue mee, his countenance seemes to take from me: hee lets mee feede with his Hindes, barres mee the place of a brother, and as much as in him lies, mines my gentility with my education (001-002)

### Natural Counsel (Act 2, Scene 1)

The duke finds moral instruction in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]:

> This is no flattery: these are counsellors / That feelingly perswade me what I am (004-012)

### [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Virtue in Action (Act 2, Scene 7)

[[orlando|Orlando]] enters with [[adam|Adam]] on his back, demanding food for his servant:

> Orl. Forbeare, and eate no more. / Iaq. Why I haue eate none yet. / Orl. Nor shalt not, till necessities be seru'd. (010-092)

### The People's Recognition (Act 1, Scene 2)

> But that the people praise her for her vertues (002-185)

### [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s Conversion (Act 5, Scene 4)

> After some question with him, was conuerted (022-115)
