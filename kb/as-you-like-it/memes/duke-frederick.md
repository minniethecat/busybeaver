# Duke Frederick

## Overview

Duke Frederick is the antagonist of As You Like It — the younger brother who has usurped [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s throne and rules the court through fear and decree. His [[exile|banishment]] of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (003-024), despite her being his daughter [[celia|Celia]]'s beloved companion, demonstrates the arbitrary cruelty that characterizes his rule. Yet his sudden conversion at the play's end — "After some question with him, was conuerted" (022-115) — restores both the political order and [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s own soul, completing the play's argument that even the most hardened character can be transformed by grace.

## Role in the Play

[[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s usurpation is the political crime that the play must resolve. His rule is maintained through force: he banishes his brother, then [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and finally marches an army toward [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] to destroy the exiled community. His court is a place of fear and suspicion — the anti-type to [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]]'s fellowship.

His treatment of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is particularly revealing. When [[celia|Celia]] pleads for her cousin, Frederick answers: "She is too subtile for thee, and her smoothnesse, Her verie silence, and her patience, Speake to the people, and they pittie her" (003-065). Frederick recognizes [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s virtue but perceives it as a political threat, exposing the paranoia inherent in tyrannical power.

His conversion is the play's most compressed transformation. Marching to destroy [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], he encounters "an old religious man" at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge and "After some question with him, was conuerted" (022-115). The instantaneous nature of this change — no struggle, no development — reflects the grace-model of conversion that comedy permits. He restores the dukedom to his brother and enters religious life, his tyranny dissolved as suddenly as it was asserted.

## The Usurper's Psychology

Frederick is not merely a stock tyrant; Shakespeare gives him moments of psychological complexity. His [[exile|banishment]] of [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is not born of pure malice but of political calculation: he fears her popularity with "the people" (003-066). His speech reveals a ruler whose power is insecure — he acts not from strength but from anxiety, perceiving threats in virtue and conspiracy in patience.

His relationship with [[celia|Celia]] adds a further dimension. He keeps his daughter at court for her own good ("I [[celia|Celia]], we staid her for your sake", 003-054), yet his tyranny alienates her and drives her to flee. [[duke-senior|The Duke]] who rules through fear cannot command the loyalty of his own child — a Shakespearean irony that undercuts the appearance of absolute power. His character demonstrates that tyranny is self-defeating: the more he seeks to control, the more he loses.
