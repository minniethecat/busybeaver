# Hunting

## Definition

Hunting in As You Like It functions as both pastoral pastime and symbolic action. The deer hunt, with its rituals of pursuit, kill, and ceremonial presentation of the [[cuckoldry|horns]], establishes the exiled court's adaptation of aristocratic culture to [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] environment. At the same time, the wounded deer becomes a vehicle for reflection on suffering, persecution, and the moral ambiguity of pastoral life — [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] is both refuge and killing ground.

## In As You Like It

The hunt is introduced through [[jaques|Jaques]]'s moralizing. The melancholy philosopher is reported to have been found weeping over a wounded stag: "a poore sequestred Stag, That from the Hunters aime had tane a hurt, Did come to languish" (004-037). The First Lord's report of [[jaques|Jaques]]'s reaction turns the hunt into an occasion for philosophizing about human cruelty and the suffering of the innocent.

The formal hunt song — "What shall he haue that kild the Deare" (017-007) — accompanies the ritual presentation of the deer's [[cuckoldry|horns]] to the successful hunter. The ceremony, complete with horn-blowing, transplants courtly ritual into the pastoral setting, showing how the exiled lords maintain their cultural forms even in the wilderness.

The horn symbolism carries erotic as well as ritual weight. Horns, in Renaissance culture, signified cuckoldry. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] exploits this ambiguity: "As hornes are odious, they are necessarie" (013-031), he muses before his rustic wedding, linking the hunt's trophy to the marital anxieties that shadow the comic resolution.

[[rosalind|Rosalind]], in her [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise, invokes the hunt metaphorically when she speaks of [[orlando|Orlando]] as her quarry: "Thy Huntresse name, that my full life doth sway" (012-005). The language of hunting — pursuit, capture, submission — is woven through the play's romantic discourse.
