## Overview

Ganimed (also spelled Ganymede) is the male disguise adopted by [[rosalind|Rosalind]] upon her flight from [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s court into the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]. The name invokes the mythological Ganymede, cupbearer to Jove -- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] herself declares "Ile haue no worse a name then Ioues owne Page" (003-116). As a created persona, Ganimed is not a separate character but an extension of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[wit]], intelligence, and theatrical power. The disguise transforms her from a vulnerable [[freedom|exile]] into the dramatic engineer of the play's romantic comedy, allowing her to speak, act, and maneuver with a [[freedom]] denied to women in her position. Shakespeare intensifies the metatheatrical layering: a boy actor plays a woman who plays a boy who pretends to be a woman -- a quadruple performance that exposes the constructed nature of gender and courtship alike.

## Role in the Play

Ganimed is the central dramatic device of As You Like It's pastoral comedy. Upon entering [[forest-of-arden-place|the Forest of Arden]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]]-as-Ganimed discovers [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[love-poetry|love poems]] hanging from the trees and promptly seizes control of the romantic narrative. She proposes to "cure" him of his lovesickness by having him woo her daily as if she were [[rosalind|Rosalind]]: "Hee was to imagine me his Loue, his Mistris: and I set him euerie day to woe me" (012-180). This creates the play's signature metatheatrical structure -- each "cure" session is a scene within a scene, with Ganimed directing [[orlando|Orlando]]'s education in love while simultaneously testing his sincerity and deepening their mutual attachment.

[[rosalind|Ganimed]]'s presence complicates the romantic economy of [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] in generative ways. When the shepherdess [[phebe|Phebe]] falls in love with Ganimed at first sight, rejecting the devoted [[silvius|Silvius]], a love quadrangle crystallizes: [[orlando|Orlando]] loves [[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[phebe|Phebe]] loves Ganimed, [[silvius|Silvius]] loves [[phebe|Phebe]], and Ganimed loves "no woman" (020-050). This entanglement, managed entirely through Ganimed's [[wit]] and authority, drives the play toward its resolution. Ganimed ultimately promises to resolve all romantic entanglements through "magic" -- "I can do strange things: I haue since I was three yeare old conuerst with a Magitian" (020-022) -- a claim that acknowledges the quasi-magical power of theatrical art itself.

The persona also explores the tension between performed and felt identity. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s emotional reality repeatedly presses against the constraints of her male costume. When she faints upon hearing of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s wound, she must scramble to maintain the fiction: "Ah, sirra, a body would thinke this was well counterfeited" [018-171-172]. Her confession -- "yfaith, I should haue beene a woman by right" [018-179-180] -- is both a joke and a truth, encapsulating the play's radical insight that gender is a performance with real consequences.

## Key Scenes

### The Naming (Act 1, Scene 3)

After [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] banishes [[rosalind|Rosalind]] on pain of death, she and [[celia|Celia]] plan their flight to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. [[rosalind|Rosalind]], being "more then common tall" (003-106), proposes to disguise herself as a man. When [[celia|Celia]] asks what name to use, [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s reply elevates [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] from mere safety to mythic allusion:

> **Rosalind**: Ile haue no worse a name then Ioues owne Page, / And therefore looke you call me Ganimed. [003-115-116]

### Arrival in Arden (Act 2, Scene 4)

Entering [[forest-of-arden-place|the Forest of Arden]] in male attire, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] feels the physical and emotional strain of maintaining her disguise. She confesses to the difficulty while mustering courage for [[celia|Celia]]'s sake:

> **Rosalind**: I could finde in my heart to disgrace my mans apparell, and to cry like a woman: but I must comfort the weaker vessell, as doublet and hose ought to show it selfe coragious to petty-coate; therefore courage, good Aliena. (007-006)

### The Encounter (Act 3, Scene 2)

Discovering [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[love-poetry|love poems]] pinned to trees throughout [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]]-as-Ganimed reads them aloud with mockery and delight. When she learns the author is [[orlando|Orlando]] himself, her composure breaks into a torrent of questions betraying her woman's heart beneath the doublet and hose: "What did he when thou saw'st him? What sayde he? How look'd he? Wherein went he?" (012-086). She resolves to speak to him "like a sawcie Lacky, and vnder that habit play the knaue with him" [012-142-143].

Their first conversation establishes the dynamic of the cure. Ganimed delivers the play's most famous philosophical speech on the relativity of [[time|Time]]: "[[time|Time]] trauels in diuers paces, with diuers persons" (012-149). She then proposes her cure -- that [[orlando|Orlando]] court her as if she were [[rosalind|Rosalind]]. [[orlando|Orlando]] agrees, and she instructs him: "Nay, you must call mee [[rosalind|Rosalind]]" (012-190).

### The Mock Wedding (Act 4, Scene 1)

In a tour-de-force of metatheatrical play, Ganimed orchestrates a mock wedding with [[orlando|Orlando]], enlisting [[celia|Celia]] as the priest. [[orlando|Orlando]], fully immersed in the game, speaks his vows to the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]]:

> **Orlando**: I take thee Rosalind for wife (016-065)
> **Rosalind**: I doe take thee Orlando for my husband (016-066)

After [[orlando|Orlando]] departs, the mask drops and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] reveals the depth of her feeling to [[celia|Celia]] in one of the play's most passionate confessions:

> **Rosalind**: O coz, coz, coz: my pretty little coz, that thou didst know how many fathome deepe I am in loue: but it cannot bee sounded: my affection hath an vnknowne bottome, like the Bay of Portugall (016-091)

### The Love Quadrangle (Act 3, Scene 5)

When Ganimed witnesses [[phebe|Phebe]] cruelly rejecting [[silvius|Silvius]], she intervenes with characteristic sharpness. Her rebuke -- "who might be your mother / That you insult, exult, and all at once / Ouer the wretched?" [015-039-041] -- is so compelling that [[phebe|Phebe]] falls in love with the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]] on the spot:

> **Phebe**: Sweet youth, I pray you chide a yere together, / I had rather here you chide, then this man wooe [015-068-069]

This unintended consequence sets up the comic crisis of the final act.

### The Swoon (Act 4, Scene 3)

When [[oliver|Oliver]] arrives with the bloodied handkerchief and the story of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s lion-wound, [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s body betrays her disguise. She faints -- a reaction that her male persona cannot convincingly explain away. [[celia|Celia]]'s cry "Why how now Ganimed, sweet Ganimed" (018-161) underscores the tension between identity and body. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s attempt to pass it off as performance -- "I pray you tell your brother how well I counterfeited" (018-172) -- is recognized by [[oliver|Oliver]] as genuine: "This was not counterfeit, there is too great testimony in your complexion" [018-174-175].

### The Revelation and Resolution (Act 5, Scene 2 and Scene 4)

In Act 5 Scene 2, Ganimed gathers the tangled lovers and extracts promises from each: [[orlando|Orlando]] will marry [[rosalind|Rosalind]] if she appears, [[phebe|Phebe]] will marry [[silvius|Silvius]] if she refuses Ganimed. The quadruple-echo scene crystallizes the romantic quadrangle:

> **Silvius**: It is to be all made of faith and seruice, / And so am I for Phebe [020-037-038]
> **Phebe**: And I for Ganimed (020-039)
> **Orlando**: And I for Rosalind (020-040)
> **Rosalind**: And I for no woman (020-041)

In Act 5 Scene 4, Ganimed orchestrates her own disappearance. She extracts binding promises from all parties, then exits with [[celia|Celia]] -- only to re-enter as [[rosalind|Rosalind]], shedding [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] that has driven the entire play. The revelation is met with wonder: "If there be truth in sight, you are my [[rosalind|Rosalind]]" (022-072).

## Relationships

- **[[rosalind|Rosalind]]** -- Ganimed is [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s created persona, an extension of her [[wit]] and agency rather than a separate identity. The disguise allows [[rosalind|Rosalind]] to navigate [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] safely, court [[orlando|Orlando]] on her own terms, and orchestrate the play's resolution. Their relationship is one of alter-ego: Ganimed is what [[rosalind|Rosalind]] becomes when freed from courtly constraints.

- **[[orlando|Orlando]]** -- Through Ganimed, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] serves as [[orlando|Orlando]]'s confidant and tutor in love. He confides in "Ganimed" about his passion for [[rosalind|Rosalind]] without realizing he speaks to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] herself. The relationship is intimate and intellectually charged: Ganimed educates [[orlando|Orlando]] about the fickleness of lovers ("men are Aprill when they woe, December when they wed" (016-071)) while falling ever more deeply in love with him.

- **[[phebe|Phebe]]** -- The shepherdess falls in love with Ganimed at first sight, mistaking the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]] for an attractive young man. Her infatuation -- "Sweet [[youth]], I pray you chide a yere together" (015-068) -- drives the comic subplot. Ganimed rebuffs [[phebe|Phebe]]'s advances while using them to teach humility, ultimately resolving the situation at the final revelation.

- **[[celia|Celia]] ([[aliena|Aliena]])** -- [[celia|Celia]] accompanies Ganimed as her "sister" [[aliena|Aliena]] throughout [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] adventure. She is the only character who sees both sides of [[disguise-plan|the disguise]], providing counsel and sometimes scolding [[rosalind|Rosalind]] for the [[freedom]] [[rosalind|Ganimed]]'s persona grants her: "You haue simply misus'd our sexe in your [[love|loue]]-prate: we must haue your doublet and hose pluckt ouer your head" (016-090).

- **[[silvius|Silvius]]** -- Ganimed intervenes in [[silvius|Silvius]]'s hopeless courtship of [[phebe|Phebe]], defending him and urging [[phebe|Phebe]] to recognize his [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]]: "You are a thousand times a properer man / Then she a woman" [015-055-056]. [[rosalind|Ganimed]]'s authority ultimately secures [[silvius|Silvius]]'s marriage to [[phebe|Phebe]].

- **[[jaques|Jaques]]** -- The melancholy philosopher and the disguised [[wit]] share a brief but revealing exchange in Act 4 Scene 1. When [[jaques|Jaques]] asks to become "better acquainted" (016-002), Ganimed deflects him with humor, preferring the company of "a foole to make me merrie" (016-012) over [[jaques|Jaques]]'s cultivated sadness.

## Themes

### Gender and Performance

Ganimed is Shakespeare's most sustained exploration of gender as performance. The persona demonstrates that masculinity -- "a gallant curtelax vpon my thigh, / A bore-speare in my hand" [003-108-109] -- can be adopted as easily as a costume. Yet [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] also reveals the limits of performance: [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s body (her swoon, her tears, her love) repeatedly threatens to break through the fiction, exposing gender as both constructed and inescapably embodied.

### Disguise and Identity

The Ganimed persona embodies the Renaissance fascination with [[disguise|disguise]] as a tool of discovery. By becoming someone else, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] discovers more about herself and others. [[rosalind|Ganimed]]'s [[freedom]] to speak, to jest, to educate, and to orchestrate reveals what the courtly [[rosalind|Rosalind]] could only think. As she tells [[celia|Celia]]: "Do you not know I am a woman, when I thinke, I must speake" [012-101-102].

### Love and Education

Ganimed serves as the play's tutor of love, systematically dispelling [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Petrarchan illusions and [[phebe|Phebe]]'s romantic pride. Through the "cure," she demonstrates that love requires honesty, patience, and a tolerance for imperfection -- lessons that benefit the teacher as much as the students.

### Pastoral Freedom

Ganimed could only exist in [[forest-of-arden-place|the Forest of Arden]]. The pastoral setting -- removed from the hierarchies and constraints of court -- provides the space where disguise can become a tool of liberation rather than mere concealment. The name itself, invoking a classical pastoral figure, signals [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] as a space of transformation.
