(015-001)Enter Siluius and [[phebe|Phebe]].

(015-002)Sil. Sweet Phebe doe not scorne me, do not Phebe

(015-003)Say that you [[love|loue]] me not, but say not so

(015-004)In bitternesse; the common executioner

(015-005)Whose heart th' accustom'd sight of death makes hard

(015-006)Falls not the axe vpon the humbled neck,

(015-007)But first begs pardon: will you sterner be

(015-008)Then he that dies and liues by bloody drops?

(015-009)Enter [[rosalind|Rosalind]], [[celia|Celia]], and [[corin|Corin]].

(015-010)Phe. I would not be thy executioner,

(015-011)I flye thee, for I would not iniure thee:

(015-012)Thou tellst me there is murder in mine eye,

(015-013)'Tis pretty sure, and very probable,

(015-014)That eyes that are the frailst, and softest things,

(015-015)Who shut their coward gates on atomyes,

(015-016)Should be called tyrants, butchers, murtherers.

(015-017)Now I doe frowne on thee with all my heart,

(015-018)And if mine eyes can wound, now let them kill thee:

(015-019)Now counterfeit to swound, why now fall downe,

(015-020)Or if thou canst not, oh for shame, for shame,

(015-021)Lye not, to say mine eyes are murtherers:

(015-022)Now shew the wound mine eye hath made in thee,

(015-023)Scratch thee but with a pin, and there remaines

(015-024)Some scarre of it: Leane vpon a rush

(015-025)The Cicatrice and capable impressure

(015-026)Thy palme some moment keepes: but now mine eyes

(015-027)Which I haue darted at thee, hurt thee not,

(015-028)Nor I am sure there is no force in eyes

(015-029)That can doe hurt

(015-030)Sil. O deere Phebe,

(015-031)If euer (as that euer may be neere)

(015-032)You meet in some fresh cheeke the power of fancie,

(015-033)Then shall you know the wounds inuisible

(015-034)That Loues keene arrows make

(015-035)Phe. But till that time

(015-036)Come not thou neere me: and when that time comes,

(015-037)Afflict me with thy mockes, pitty me not,

(015-038)As till that time I shall not pitty thee

(015-039)Ros. And why I pray you? who might be your mother

(015-040)That you insult, exult, and all at once

(015-041)Ouer the wretched? what though you haue no beauty

(015-042)As by my faith, I see no more in you

(015-043)Then without Candle may goe darke to bed:

(015-044)Must you be therefore prowd and pittilesse?

(015-045)Why what meanes this? why do you looke on me?

(015-046)I see no more in you then in the ordinary

(015-047)Of Natures sale-worke? 'ods my little life,

(015-048)I thinke she meanes to tangle my eies too:

(015-049)No faith proud Mistresse, hope not after it,

(015-050)'Tis not your inkie browes, your blacke silke haire,

(015-051)Your bugle eye-balls, nor your cheeke of creame

(015-052)That can entame my spirits to your worship:

(015-053)You foolish Shepheard, wherefore do you follow her

(015-054)Like foggy South, puffing with winde and raine,

(015-055)You are a thousand times a properer man

(015-056)Then she a woman. 'Tis such fooles as you

(015-057)That makes the world full of ill-fauourd children:

(015-058)'Tis not her glasse, but you that flatters her,

(015-059)And out of you she sees her selfe more proper

(015-060)Then any of her lineaments can show her:

(015-061)But Mistris, know your selfe, downe on your knees

(015-062)And thanke heauen, fasting, for a good mans loue;

(015-063)For I must tell you friendly in your eare,

(015-064)Sell when you can, you are not for all markets:

(015-065)Cry the man mercy, loue him, take his offer,

(015-066)Foule is most foule, being foule to be a scoffer.

(015-067)So take her to thee Shepheard, fareyouwell

(015-068)Phe. Sweet [[youth]], I pray you chide a yere together,

(015-069)I had rather here you chide, then this man wooe

(015-070)Ros. Hees falne in loue with your foulnesse, & shee'll

(015-071)Fall in loue with my anger. If it be so, as fast

(015-072)As she answeres thee with frowning lookes, ile sauce

(015-073)Her with bitter words: why looke you so vpon me?

(015-074)Phe. For no ill will I beare you

(015-075)Ros. I pray you do not fall in loue with mee,

(015-076)For I am falser then vowes made in wine:

(015-077)Besides, I like you not: if you will know my house,

(015-078)'Tis at the tufft of Oliues, here hard by:

(015-079)Will you goe Sister? Shepheard ply her hard:

(015-080)Come Sister: Shepheardesse, looke on him better

(015-081)And be not proud, though all the world could see,

(015-082)None could be so abus'd in sight as hee.

(015-083)Come, to our flocke,

(015-084)Enter.

(015-085)Phe. Dead Shepheard, now I find thy saw of might,

(015-086)Who euer lov'd, that lou'd not at first sight?

(015-087)Sil. Sweet Phebe

(015-088)Phe. Hah: what saist thou Siluius?

(015-089)Sil. Sweet Phebe pitty me

(015-090)Phe. Why I am sorry for thee gentle Siluius

(015-091)Sil. Where euer sorrow is, reliefe would be:

(015-092)If you doe sorrow at my griefe in loue,

(015-093)By giuing loue your sorrow, and my griefe

(015-094)Were both extermin'd

(015-095)Phe. Thou hast my loue, is not that neighbourly?

(015-096)Sil. I would haue you

(015-097)Phe. Why that were couetousnesse:

(015-098)Siluius; the time was, that I hated thee;

(015-099)And yet it is not, that I beare thee loue,

(015-100)But since that thou canst talke of loue so well,

(015-101)Thy company, which erst was irkesome to me

(015-102)I will endure; and Ile employ thee too:

(015-103)But doe not looke for further recompence

(015-104)Then thine owne gladnesse, that thou art employd

(015-105)Sil. So holy, and so perfect is my loue,

(015-106)And I in such a pouerty of grace,

(015-107)That I shall thinke it a most plenteous crop

(015-108)To gleane the broken eares after the man

(015-109)That the maine haruest reapes: loose now and then

(015-110)A scattred smile, and that Ile liue vpon

(015-111)Phe. Knowst thou the youth that spoke to mee yerewhile?

(015-112)Sil. Not very well, but I haue met him oft,

(015-113)And he hath bought the Cottage and the bounds

(015-114)That the old Carlot once was Master of

(015-115)Phe. Thinke not I loue him, though I ask for him,

(015-116)'Tis but a peeuish boy, yet he talkes well,

(015-117)But what care I for words? yet words do well

(015-118)When he that speakes them pleases those that heare:

(015-119)It is a pretty youth, not very prettie,

(015-120)But sure hee's proud, and yet his pride becomes him;

(015-121)Hee'll make a proper man: the best thing in him

(015-122)Is his complexion: and faster then his tongue

(015-123)Did make offence, his eye did heale it vp:

(015-124)He is not very tall, yet for his yeeres hee's tall:

(015-125)His leg is but so so, and yet 'tis well:

(015-126)There was a pretty rednesse in his lip,

(015-127)A little riper, and more lustie red

(015-128)Then that mixt in his cheeke: 'twas iust the difference

(015-129)Betwixt the constant red, and mingled Damaske.

(015-130)There be some women Siluius, had they markt him

(015-131)In parcells as I did, would haue gone neere

(015-132)To fall in loue with him: but for my part

(015-133)I loue him not, nor hate him not: and yet

(015-134)Haue more cause to hate him then to loue him,

(015-135)For what had he to doe to chide at me?

(015-136)He said mine eyes were black, and my haire blacke,

(015-137)And now I am remembred, scorn'd at me:

(015-138)I maruell why I answer'd not againe,

(015-139)But that's all one: omittance is no quittance:

(015-140)Ile write to him a very tanting Letter,

(015-141)And thou shalt beare it, wilt thou Siluius?

(015-142)Sil. Phebe, with all my heart

(015-143)Phe. Ile write it strait:

(015-144)The matter's in my head, and in my heart,

(015-145)I will be bitter with him, and passing short;

(015-146)Goe with me Siluius.

(015-147)Exeunt.
