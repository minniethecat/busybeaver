# Charles

## Character Overview

Charles is [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s professional wrestler -- a figure of formidable physical power who appears in only two scenes yet serves as the catalyst for the entire plot. His [[honor|reputation]] for brutality is established before he even speaks: Le Beau reports that Charles "broke three of his ribbes, that there is little hope of life in him" (002-065) against his previous opponent, and that he did the same to two other challengers. Despite this fearsome [[honor|reputation]], Charles is not an independent villain; he is a tool wielded by [[oliver|Oliver]], who exploits [[charles|Charles]]'s professional pride to attempt the murder of [[orlando|Orlando]].

[[charles|Charles]]'s dramatic function is threefold. First, he provides the obstacle that [[orlando|Orlando]] must overcome to prove his innate virtue. Second, his conversation with [[oliver|Oliver]] in Act 1 Scene 1 reveals both [[orlando|Orlando]]'s wrestling intention and the current political situation at court -- making him an efficient vehicle for exposition. Third, his defeat produces all three of the play's plot lines: the love plot ([[rosalind|Rosalind]] falls for [[orlando|Orlando]]), the villainy plot ([[oliver|Oliver]]'s murderous rage intensifies), and the political plot ([[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] learns [[orlando|Orlando]]'s identity and turns hostile).

Charles embodies trained power in service of tyranny. He wrestles "for my credit" (001-046) and "for prize" (001-048), motivated by professional [[honor|honour]] rather than malice. This makes him a morally ambiguous figure -- dangerous but not diabolical, a professional doing his job in a court where breaking ribs counts as "sport" (002-070).

## The Warning to Oliver

[[charles|Charles]]'s first and most significant scene is his private conversation with [[oliver|Oliver]] in Act 1 Scene 1. Having heard that [[orlando|Orlando]] intends to challenge him in disguise, Charles comes to warn [[oliver|Oliver]] out of professed "[[love|loue]]" (001-046). His speech reveals a man who is professionally confident but personally decent -- he would be "loth to foyle" [[orlando|Orlando]] "for your [[love|loue]]" (001-046), and offers [[oliver|Oliver]] the chance to dissuade his brother.

> **Charles**: Marry doe I sir: and I came to acquaint you with a matter: I am giuen sir secretly to vnderstand, that your yonger brother Orlando hath a disposition to come in disguis'd against mee to try a fall: to morrow sir I wrastle for my credit, and hee that escapes me without some broken limbe, shall acquit him well: your brother is but young and tender, and for your loue I would bee loth to foyle him, as I must for my owne honour if hee come in: therefore out of my loue to you, I came hither to acquaint you withall, that either you might stay him from his intendment, or brooke such disgrace well as he shall runne into, in that it is a thing of his owne search, and altogether against my will (001-046)

[[charles|Charles]]'s speech is rich with dramatic irony. He believes he is acting honourably -- warning a brother about a danger to that brother's sibling. Instead, he is unknowingly delivering the intelligence that [[oliver|Oliver]] will weaponize. Charles's professional code ("I wrastle for my credit") is exploited by [[oliver|Oliver]]'s personal malice.

This scene also establishes Charles as a conduit for exposition. Earlier in the same conversation, he reports the political situation: [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] has been banished by [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] and lives "like the old Robin Hood of England" in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] (001-043). He confirms that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] remains at court, "no lesse beloued of her Vncle, then his owne daughter" (001-042). Charles thus serves the practical dramatic function of briefing the audience on the play's political backdrop.

## Relationship with Oliver

[[charles|Charles]]'s relationship with [[oliver|Oliver]] is one of the play's most efficient examples of dramatic manipulation. Charles approaches [[oliver|Oliver]] in good faith, offering his "[[love|loue]]" and expecting gratitude. [[oliver|Oliver]] responds with a masterclass in manipulation -- a torrent of lies about [[orlando|Orlando]]'s character designed to inflame Charles's professional pride into murderous intent.

> **Oliver**: Ile tell thee Charles, it is the stubbornest yong fellow of France, full of ambition, an enuious emulator of euery mans good parts, a secret & villanous contriuer against mee his naturall brother: therefore vse thy discretion, I had as liefe thou didst breake his necke as his finger (001-047)

Charles, believing himself to be acting against a treacherous schemer rather than an innocent [[youth]], accepts the charge:

> **Charles**: I am heartily glad I came hither to you: if hee come to morrow, Ile giue him his payment: if euer hee goe alone againe, Ile neuer wrastle for prize more: and so God keepe your worship (001-048)

The irony is savage. Charles thinks he has done a good deed by warning [[oliver|Oliver]]; instead he has been recruited as an unwitting assassin. His line "I am heartily glad I came hither to you" is spoken by a man who has just sealed his own dramatic fate. His professional confidence ("Ile neuer wrastle for prize more") will be proven hollow on the morrow.

The relationship reveals [[oliver|Oliver]]'s cunning: he does not need to wield the weapon himself when he can persuade another to do it. Charles is a proxy killer, and his defeat in Act 1 Scene 2 is not merely a personal failure but a moral one -- the failure of hired force against natural virtue.

## The Wrestling Match

[[charles|Charles]]'s second and final scene is the [[wrestling-match|wrestling match]] in Act 1 Scene 2. His entrance line establishes his [[pride|arrogance]]:

> **Charles**: Come, where is this yong gallant, that is so desirous to lie with his mother earth? (002-100)

The taunt is cruel and confident. Charles views [[orlando|Orlando]] as already defeated, a body waiting to be thrown to the ground. His follow-up to [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] doubles down on the contempt:

> **Charles**: No, I warrant your Grace you shall not entreat him to a second, that haue so mightilie perswaded him from a first (002-105)

The dramatic structure of the wrestling scene is brutally efficient. Le Beau has already reported that Charles "broke three of his ribbes" of each of three previous challengers (002-065). [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] both urge [[orlando|Orlando]] not to fight. [[duke-senior|The Duke]] himself says "there is such oddes in the man" (002-083). Every dramatic signal points to [[orlando|Orlando]]'s destruction -- which makes his victory all the more powerful.

The wrestling itself happens in a stage direction and a shout (002-109, 002-112). Charles does not speak again. His defeat is rendered in two devastating lines:

> **Le Beau**: He cannot speake my Lord (002-117)

> **Duke**: Beare him awaie (002-118)

The man who entered boasting is carried off speechless. The professional wrestler who broke three men's ribs is broken by an untrained [[youth]]. [[charles|Charles]]'s final dramatic function is to demonstrate, through his defeat, the play's central argument: that natural worth ("the something that nature gaue mee," 001-002) will triumph over trained power in service of tyranny.

## Dramatic Function

Charles is a textbook example of efficient dramatic construction. In two scenes, he serves five distinct functions:

1. **Exposition**: He delivers the political backstory -- [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s [[exile|banishment]], life in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s presence at court (001-041 through 001-043)
2. **Plot Catalyst**: His warning to [[oliver|Oliver]] triggers the murder plot; his defeat triggers the love plot
3. **Obstacle**: He provides the physical test that proves [[orlando|Orlando]]'s worth
4. **Foil**: His trained, professional violence contrasts with [[orlando|Orlando]]'s untrained, natural virtue
5. **Moral Barometer**: His defeat establishes the play's moral universe -- the world where hired force loses to innate [[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]]

Charles also participates in the play's pattern of deception and unintended consequences. He comes to [[oliver|Oliver]] to do a good deed and becomes an accomplice to attempted murder. His professional pride leads him to boast of certain victory moments before certain defeat. Like many [[persons|characters]] in the opening act, Charles is a figure whose intentions are subverted by the larger forces of the plot.
