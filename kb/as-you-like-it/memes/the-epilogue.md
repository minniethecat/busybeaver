# The Epilogue

The epilogue of *As You Like It*, spoken by [[rosalind|Rosalind]], is one of the most remarkable moments in Shakespearean comedy — a metatheatrical address that simultaneously closes the play's fiction, plays with the conventions of gender and performance, and establishes an intimate bond between the performer and the audience.

## The Convention and Its Subversion

Epilogues in Elizabethan drama were conventional gestures of farewell, typically spoken by a leading character asking for the audience's applause. Rosalind's epilogue honors this convention while systematically subverting it. She begins by acknowledging the inadequacy of the form:

> "It is not the fashion to see the lady the epilogue."

The "lady" speaking the epilogue is, of course, a boy actor playing a woman who has spent much of the play disguised as a boy. This layered irony inflects everything that follows. Rosalind's claim that "if I were a woman" she would kiss the men in the audience is the play's final brilliant complication of gender identity:

> "If I were a woman I would kiss as many of you as had beards that pleased me, complexions that liked me, and breaths that I defied not."

## Gender and Performance

The epilogue brings the play's investigation of gender performance to its logical conclusion. Throughout the play, Rosalind's identity has been fluid — daughter, exile, "Ganymede," beloved, orchestrator of the comic resolution. The epilogue refuses to settle this fluidity into a fixed identity. The actor is simultaneously Rosalind (the character), the boy who played her, and a performer directly addressing the paying audience.

The conditional "if I were a woman" is the epilogue's key interpretive crux. Spoken by a boy actor, in female costume, playing a character who has just been restored to her female identity, the line collapses the distinction between performance and identity. Gender, the epilogue suggests, is a matter of performance and convention — as much a "fashion" as the custom of who may speak the epilogue.

## The Bond with the Audience

The epilogue transforms the relationship between stage and audience. Rosalind asks the spectators to "like as much of this play as please you" — a modest, almost self-deprecating request that contrasts with the elaborate courtly rhetoric of conventional epilogues. The appeal is personal rather than formal:

> "I charge you, O women, for the love you bear to men, to like as much of this play as please you. And I charge you, O men, for the love you bear to women... that between you and the women the play may please."

This gendered appeal, with its playful negotiation between male and female audience members, recapitulates the play's central concerns with love, performance, and the social construction of desire.

## Curtain Call as Benediction

The epilogue ends with a curtsey — a gesture that is simultaneously Rosalind's, the boy actor's, and Shakespeare's. It is a benediction in the theatrical rather than religious sense: a blessing of the audience through the shared experience of the play. The final rhyme — "If I were a woman" / "in a cur'sy" / "bid me farewell" — closes the performance with wit, grace, and the lingering uncertainty about who, exactly, is speaking.
