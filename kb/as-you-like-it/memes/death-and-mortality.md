# Death and Mortality

## Definition

Death in As You Like It is the shadow that pastoral comedy must acknowledge and overcome. Though the play moves inexorably toward marriage and [[regeneration|restoration]], death is present at every turn: [[adam|Adam]] nearly starves in the wilderness (009-003), [[orlando|Orlando]] risks death at the wrestling match (002-065), [[oliver|Oliver]] nearly perishes to the lioness, and the exiled Duke's men face the "churlish chiding of the winters winde" (004-009) that can kill. The pastoral [[forest-of-arden|green world]] is not deathless — it is a place where death is confronted, cheated, and transcended through love, loyalty, and good fortune.

## In As You Like It

Death enters the play's imagination early. [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler's victims are described in mortal terms: "broke three of his ribbes, that there is little hope of life in him" (002-065). [[orlando|Orlando]]'s decision to wrestle despite the danger is a conscious gamble with death, as the youngest son of Sir Rowland de Boys with nothing to lose but his unvalued life (002-095).

The most harrowing encounter with death comes in Act 2, Scene 7, when the starving [[adam|Adam]] collapses: "O I die for food. Heere lie I downe, And measure out my graue" (009-003). The aged servant's willingness to die for his master literalizes the mortal stakes of [[freedom|exile]]. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s response — carrying [[adam|Adam]] and demanding food at sword-point — is the play's most direct confrontation with death.

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s near-death to the lioness and his rescue by [[orlando|Orlando]] transforms him: the brother who plotted murder is saved by the brother he sought to kill. Death, narrowly avoided, becomes the instrument of moral reformation. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s description of the encounter links death to fraternal reconciliation.

The play's resolution defers death indefinitely. [[jaques|Jaques]], the melancholy philosopher most obsessed with mortality, chooses continued [[freedom|exile]] rather than return to court, suggesting that the contemplation of death is a permanent condition rather than a crisis to be resolved. His final words — "There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd" (022-139) — leave open the philosophical questions that comedy cannot fully answer.

### Winter's Bite (Act 2, Scene 1)

> the churlish chiding of the winters winde, Which when it bites and blowes vpon my body, Euen till I shrinke with cold (004-009)

[[oliver|Oliver]] describes the lioness that nearly killed him and [[orlando|Orlando]]'s rescue ([[oliver|Oliver]]'s conversion speech).
