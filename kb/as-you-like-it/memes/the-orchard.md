# The Orchard at Oliver's Estate

The orchard on [[oliver|Oliver]]'s estate is the setting for the opening scene of *As You Like It* — the only location in the play before the action shifts to the court and then to the Forest of Arden. This liminal space, neither fully domestic nor fully wild, establishes the play's central tensions between nature and nurture, inheritance and merit, and the bonds of blood.

## Act 1, Scene 1: Fraternal Conflict

The play opens with [[orlando|Orlando]] complaining to the old servant [[adam|Adam]] about his mistreatment at the hands of his elder brother Oliver. Since their father [[sir-rowland-de-boys|Sir Rowland de Boys]]'s death, Oliver has withheld Orlando's inheritance and denied him the education and training befitting his gentle birth:

> "He keeps me rustically at home, or, to speak more properly, stays me here at home unkept."

The orchard setting is significant. It is a place of natural growth and potential — fruit trees that should be cultivated and productive — but under Oliver's stewardship, it mirrors Orlando's condition: possessed of innate worth but deliberately neglected.

## Confrontation and Threat

When Oliver enters, the orchard becomes the arena for the brothers' confrontation. Orlando demands his patrimony — a thousand crowns bequeathed by their father — and the education due to a gentleman. Oliver responds with contempt and physical aggression, which Orlando answers by seizing his brother by the throat:

> "I will no longer endure it. Therefore allow me such exercises as may become a gentleman, or give me the poor allottery my father left me by testament."

This violent grappling in the orchard foreshadows the wrestling match at court and establishes the pattern of fraternal conflict that drives the play's opening movement.

## The Wrestling Plot

After Orlando's departure, Oliver summons the wrestler [[charles|Charles]] to the orchard and conspires to have Orlando killed or maimed in the upcoming wrestling match at court. The orchard thus becomes the site where the play's initial villainy is plotted:

> "I had as lief thou didst break his neck as his finger."

The contrast between the pastoral setting and the murderous intent is stark and deliberate. Shakespeare uses the orchard — traditionally a symbol of cultivation and peaceful retirement — as the birthplace of courtly intrigue, collapsing the boundary between the supposedly benign domestic world and the corrupt court.

## Adam's Warning

The orchard scene closes with [[adam|Adam]] warning Orlando of Oliver's plot and offering his life savings to help Orlando flee. The old servant's loyalty — the product of a lifetime of honest service in this very estate — provides the moral counterweight to Oliver's treachery:

> "I have five hundred crowns, / The thrifty hire I saved under your father... / Take that, and He that doth the ravens feed... / Be comfort to my age."

## Symbolic Function

The orchard is the play's first "in-between" space — neither the oppressive court nor the liberating forest. Its neglected state reflects the moral decay of the de Boys household under Oliver, while its natural abundance gestures toward the regenerative power of Arden. The orchard is where the seeds of both the play's conflict and its eventual resolution are planted.
