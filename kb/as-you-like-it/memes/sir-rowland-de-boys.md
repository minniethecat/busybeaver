# Sir Rowland de Boys

## Character Overview

Sir Rowland de Boys is the dead father whose legacy structures the play's moral universe. He never appears on stage -- he is dead before the action begins -- yet his name is spoken more often than many living characters'. The three de Boys brothers ([[orlando|Orlando]], [[oliver|Oliver]], and [[jaques|Jaques]]) derive their identities from their relationship to him, and nearly every character who knew Sir Rowland reveres him.

The name "de Boys" (of the woods) is itself significant: it foreshadows the movement of the play from court to [[forest-of-arden-place|forest]], and suggests a connection to nature that [[orlando|Orlando]] embodies and [[oliver|Oliver]] repudiates.

Sir Rowland functions as an absent moral center. His will -- charging [[oliver|Oliver]] "on his blessing to breed mee well" (001-002) -- is the standard against which [[oliver|Oliver]]'s tyranny is measured. His reputation transforms enemies into allies: [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] turns hostile upon learning [[orlando|Orlando]] is "the sonne of Sir Rowland de Boys" (002-117), while [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] welcomes [[orlando|Orlando]] for the same reason: "If that you were the good Sir Rowlands son, / As you haue whisper'd faithfully you were, / Be truly welcome hither" (010-198).

## Legacy and Reputation

Sir Rowland's reputation is established through the testimony of others. [[orlando|Orlando]] invokes him as the source of his own innate worth:

> **Orlando**: I am the yongest sonne of Sir Rowland de Boys, he was my father, and he is thrice a villaine that saies such a father begot villaines (001-022)

[[adam|Adam]], Sir Rowland's old servant, serves as the living link to Sir Rowland's legacy. He remembers his old master with devotion and extends that loyalty to [[orlando|Orlando]] -- offering his life savings of "fiue hundred crownes" (006-005) to fund [[orlando|Orlando]]'s escape. [[adam|Adam]]'s loyalty is the most concrete evidence of Sir Rowland's goodness: a good master inspires lifelong devotion in his servants.

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s recognition of Sir Rowland provides the political dimension of his legacy:

> **Duke Senior**: If that you were the good Sir Rowlands son, / As you haue whisper'd faithfully you were, / Be truly welcome hither (010-198)

The phrase "good Sir Rowland" is telling. In a play where [[usurpation|usurpation]] has divided the court, Sir Rowland's reputation transcends political faction. He was "good" in an absolute sense, recognized by both the usurper ([[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], who hates his son for it) and the legitimate ruler ([[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], who loves his son for it).

## The Moral Contrast with Oliver

Sir Rowland's will -- "charged my brother on his blessing to breed mee well" (001-002) -- is the play's moral test case. [[oliver|Oliver]] fails it utterly: he keeps [[orlando|Orlando]] "rustically at home" (001-002), denies him education, and ultimately conspires to murder him. The contrast between Sir Rowland's intentions (a father providing for all his sons) and [[oliver|Oliver]]'s actions (the eldest son dispossessing the youngest) drives the entire opening act.

The will also introduces the play's concern with inheritance and [[degree-and-hierarchy|social hierarchy]]. Sir Rowland's bequest of "a thousand Crownes" (001-002) to [[orlando|Orlando]] represents the legitimate social order -- the father providing for each son according to his station. [[oliver|Oliver]]'s withholding of that inheritance represents the perversion of that order, a domestic [[usurpation|usurpation]] that mirrors [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s political one.

## The Three Sons

Sir Rowland's three sons embody three different relationships to paternal legacy:

- **[[orlando|Orlando]]**: The inheritor of Sir Rowland's virtue. He invokes his father's name with pride, carries "the spirit of my Father" within him (001-002), and proves his worth through action rather than inheritance. [[orlando|Orlando]] is the son who most resembles the father.

- **[[oliver|Oliver]]**: The betrayer of Sir Rowland's wishes. He violates the will, mistreats his brother, and attempts murder. His eventual conversion in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] represents a return to Sir Rowland's values -- a reconciliation not only with [[orlando|Orlando]] but with their father's legacy.

- **[[jaques|Jaques]]** (the second son): The absent son. "My brother [[jaques|Iaques]] he keepes at schoole, and report speakes goldenly of his profit" (001-002). His appearance only at the play's end, bearing news of [[duke-fredericks-conversion|Duke Frederick's conversion]], completes the restoration of legitimate order -- all three sons of Sir Rowland are accounted for, and all participate in the resolution.

## Dramatic Function

Sir Rowland serves as:

1. **Moral Standard**: His will and reputation define what "good" looks like in the play's universe
2. **Plot Engine**: His will creates the conflict between [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[oliver|Oliver]]; his reputation determines how [[orlando|Orlando]] is received by others
3. **Symbolic Father**: He represents the legitimate patriarchal order that the play works to restore -- a father who provides equally for all his children
4. **Reconciliation Agent**: The restoration of his legacy (all three sons reconciled, the usurper converted) is the play's ultimate resolution

## Key Quotes

Key lines that establish Sir Rowland's legacy:

> Orlando claims his patrimony: "I am the yongest sonne of Sir Rowland de Boys, he was my father, and he is thrice a villaine that saies such a father begot villaines" (001-022)

> Oliver's complicity is revealed by Charles: "I am the second sonne of old Sir Rowland" (022-106)

> Adam remembers his master: "Of old Sir Rowland; why, what make you here?" (006-005)

> The will's charge: "charged my brother on his blessing to breed mee well" (001-002)

> Duke Senior's welcome: "If that you were the good Sir Rowlands son, / As you haue whisper'd faithfully you were, / Be truly welcome hither" (010-198)
