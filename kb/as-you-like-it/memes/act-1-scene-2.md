(002-001)Enter [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and [[celia|Cellia]].

(002-002)Cel. I pray thee Rosalind, sweet my Coz, be merry

(002-003)Ros. Deere Cellia; I show more [[joy-and-celebration|mirth]] then I am mistresse of, and would you yet were merrier: vnlesse you could teach me to forget a banished father, you must not learne mee how to remember any extraordinary pleasure

(002-004)Cel. Heerein I see thou lou'st mee not with the full waight that I [[love|loue]] thee; if my Vncle thy banished father had banished thy Vncle the Duke my Father, so thou hadst beene still with mee, I could haue taught my loue to take thy father for mine; so wouldst thou, if the truth of thy loue to me were so righteously temper'd, as mine is to thee

(002-005)Ros. Well, I will forget the condition of my estate, to reioyce in yours

(002-006)Cel. You know my Father hath no childe, but I, nor none is like to haue; and truely when he dies, thou shalt be his heire; for what hee hath taken away from thy father perforce, I will render thee againe in affection: by mine [[honor]] I will, and when I breake that oath, let mee turne monster: therefore my sweet Rose, my deare Rose, be merry

(002-007)Ros. From henceforth I will Coz, and deuise sports: let me see, what thinke you of falling in Loue? Cel. Marry I prethee doe, to make sport withall: but loue no man in good earnest, nor no further in sport neyther, then with safety of a pure blush, thou maist in honor come off againe

(002-008)Ros. What shall be our sport then?

(002-009)Cel. Let vs sit and mocke the good houswife [[fortune|Fortune]]

(002-010)from her wheele, that her gifts may henceforth bee

(002-011)bestowed equally

(002-012)Ros. I would wee could doe so: for her benefits are mightily misplaced, and the bountifull blinde woman doth most mistake in her gifts to women

(002-013)Cel. 'Tis true, for those that she makes faire, she scarce makes honest, & those that she makes honest, she makes very illfauouredly

(002-014)Ros. Nay now thou goest from Fortunes office to Natures:

(002-015)Fortune reignes in gifts of the world, not in the

(002-016)lineaments of [[nature|Nature]].

(002-017)Enter [[touchstone|Clowne]].

(002-018)Cel. No; when Nature hath made a faire creature, may she not by Fortune fall into the fire? though nature hath giuen vs [[wit]] to flout at Fortune, hath not Fortune sent in this foole to cut off the argument? Ros. Indeed there is fortune too hard for nature, when fortune makes natures naturall, the cutter off of natures witte

(002-019)Cel. Peraduenture this is not Fortunes work neither, but Natures, who perceiueth our naturall wits too dull to reason of such goddesses, hath sent this Naturall for our whetstone: for alwaies the dulnesse of the foole, is the whetstone of the wits. How now Witte, whether wander you? Clow. Mistresse, you must come away to your father

(002-020)Cel. Were you made the messenger?

(002-021)Clo. No by mine honor, but I was bid to come for you

(002-022)Ros. Where learned you that oath foole?

(002-023)Clo. Of a certaine Knight, that swore by his Honour

(002-024)they were good Pan-cakes, and swore by his Honor the

(002-025)Mustard was naught: Now Ile stand to it, the Pancakes

(002-026)were naught, and the Mustard was good, and yet was

(002-027)not the Knight forsworne

(002-028)Cel. How proue you that in the great heape of your

(002-029)knowledge?

(002-030)Ros. I marry, now vnmuzzle your wisedome

(002-031)Clo. Stand you both forth now: stroke your chinnes,

(002-032)and sweare by your beards that I am a knaue

(002-033)Cel. By our beards (if we had them) thou art

(002-034)Clo. By my knauerie (if I had it) then I were: but if you sweare by that that is not, you are not forsworn: no more was this knight swearing by his Honor, for he neuer had anie; or if he had, he had sworne it away, before euer he saw those Pancakes, or that Mustard

(002-035)Cel. Prethee, who is't that thou means't?

(002-036)Clo. One that old Fredericke your Father loues

(002-037)Ros. My Fathers loue is enough to honor him enough; speake no more of him, you'l be whipt for taxation one of these daies

(002-038)Clo. The more pittie that fooles may not speak wisely, what Wisemen do foolishly

(002-039)Cel. By my troth thou saiest true: For, since the little wit that fooles haue was silenced, the little foolerie that wise men haue makes a great shew; Heere comes Monsieur the Beu. Enter le Beau.

(002-040)Ros. With his mouth full of newes

(002-041)Cel. Which he will put on vs, as Pigeons feed their young

(002-042)Ros. Then shal we be newes-cram'd

(002-043)Cel. All the better: we shalbe the more Marketable.

(002-044)Boon-iour Monsieur le Beu, what's the newes?

(002-045)Le Beu. Faire Princesse,

(002-046)you haue lost much good sport

(002-047)Cel. Sport: of what colour?

(002-048)Le Beu. What colour Madame? How shall I aunswer

(002-049)you?

(002-050)Ros. As wit and fortune will

(002-051)Clo. Or as the destinies decrees

(002-052)Cel. Well said, that was laid on with a trowell

(002-053)Clo. Nay, if I keepe not my ranke

(002-054)Ros. Thou loosest thy old smell

(002-055)Le Beu. You amaze me Ladies: I would haue told you of good wrastling, which you haue lost the sight of

(002-056)Ros. Yet tell vs the manner of the [[wrestling|Wrastling]]

(002-057)Le Beu. I wil tell you the beginning: and if it please your Ladiships, you may see the end, for the best is yet to doe, and heere where you are, they are comming to performe it

(002-058)Cel. Well, the beginning that is dead and buried

(002-059)Le Beu. There comes an old man, and his three sons

(002-060)Cel. I could match this beginning with an old tale

(002-061)Le Beu. Three proper yong men, of excellent growth

(002-062)and presence

(002-063)Ros. With bils on their neckes: Be it knowne vnto

(002-064)all men by these presents

(002-065)Le Beu. The eldest of the three, wrastled with [[charles|Charles]] the Dukes Wrastler, which Charles in a moment threw him, and broke three of his ribbes, that there is little hope of life in him: So he seru'd the second, and so the third: yonder they lie, the poore old man their Father, making such pittiful dole ouer them, that all the beholders take his part with weeping

(002-066)Ros. Alas

(002-067)Clo. But what is the sport Monsieur, that the Ladies

(002-068)haue lost?

(002-069)Le Beu. Why this that I speake of

(002-070)Clo. Thus men may grow wiser euery day. It is the first time that euer I heard breaking of ribbes was sport for Ladies

(002-071)Cel. Or I, I promise thee

(002-072)Ros. But is there any else longs to see this broken Musicke in his sides? Is there yet another doates vpon rib-breaking? Shall we see this wrastling Cosin? Le Beu. You must if you stay heere, for heere is the place appointed for the wrastling, and they are ready to performe it

(002-073)Cel. Yonder sure they are comming. Let vs now stay and see it.

(002-074)Flourish. Enter Duke, Lords, [[orlando|Orlando]], Charles, and Attendants.

(002-075)Duke. Come on, since the [[youth]] will not be intreated

(002-076)His owne perill on his forwardnesse

(002-077)Ros. Is yonder the man?

(002-078)Le Beu. Euen he, Madam

(002-079)Cel. Alas, he is too yong: yet he looks successefully

(002-080)Du. How now daughter, and Cousin:

(002-081)Are you crept hither to see the wrastling?

(002-082)Ros. I my Liege, so please you giue vs leaue

(002-083)Du. You wil take little delight in it, I can tell you there is such oddes in the man: In pitie of the challengers youth, I would faine disswade him, but he will not bee entreated. Speake to him Ladies, see if you can mooue him

(002-084)Cel. Call him hether good Monsieuer Le Beu

(002-085)Duke. Do so: Ile not be by

(002-086)Le Beu. Monsieur the Challenger, the Princesse cals for you

(002-087)Orl. I attend them with all respect and dutie

(002-088)Ros. Young man, haue you challeng'd Charles the

(002-089)Wrastler?

(002-090)Orl. No faire Princesse: he is the generall challenger,

(002-091)I come but in as others do, to try with him the strength

(002-092)of my youth

(002-093)Cel. Yong Gentleman, your spirits are too bold for your yeares: you haue seene cruell proofe of this mans strength, if you saw your selfe with your eies, or knew your selfe with your iudgment, the feare of your aduenture would counsel you to a more equall enterprise. We pray you for your owne sake to embrace your own safetie, and giue ouer this attempt

(002-094)Ros. Do yong Sir, your reputation shall not therefore be misprised: we wil make it our suite to the Duke, that the wrastling might not go forward

(002-095)Orl. I beseech you, punish mee not with your harde thoughts, wherein I confesse me much guiltie to denie so faire and excellent Ladies anie thing. But let your faire eies, and gentle wishes go with mee to my triall; wherein if I bee foil'd, there is but one sham'd that was neuer gracious: if kil'd, but one dead that is willing to be so: I shall do my friends no wrong, for I haue none to lament me: the world no iniurie, for in it I haue nothing: onely in the world I fil vp a place, which may bee better supplied, when I haue made it emptie

(002-096)Ros. The little strength that I haue, I would it were with you

(002-097)Cel. And mine to eeke out hers

(002-098)Ros. Fare you well: praie heauen I be deceiu'd in you

(002-099)Cel. Your hearts desires be with you

(002-100)Char. Come, where is this yong gallant, that is so

(002-101)desirous to lie with his mother earth?

(002-102)Orl. Readie Sir, but his will hath in it a more modest

(002-103)working

(002-104)Duk. You shall trie but one fall

(002-105)Cha. No, I warrant your Grace you shall not entreat him to a second, that haue so mightilie perswaded him from a first

(002-106)Orl. You meane to mocke me after: you should not haue mockt me before: but come your waies

(002-107)Ros. Now Hercules, be thy speede yong man

(002-108)Cel. I would I were inuisible, to catch the strong fellow by the legge.

(002-109)Wrastle.

(002-110)Ros. Oh excellent yong man

(002-111)Cel. If I had a thunderbolt in mine eie, I can tell who should downe.

(002-112)Shout.

(002-113)Duk. No more, no more

(002-114)Orl. Yes I beseech your Grace, I am not yet well

(002-115)breath'd

(002-116)Duk. How do'st thou Charles?

(002-117)Le Beu. He cannot speake my Lord

(002-118)Duk. Beare him awaie:

(002-119)What is thy name yong man?

(002-120)Orl. Orlando my Liege, the yongest sonne of Sir Roland

(002-121)de Boys

(002-122)Duk. I would thou hadst beene son to some man else,

(002-123)The world esteem'd thy father honourable,

(002-124)But I did finde him still mine enemie:

(002-125)Thou should'st haue better pleas'd me with this deede,

(002-126)Hadst thou descended from another house:

(002-127)But fare thee well, thou art a gallant youth,

(002-128)I would thou had'st told me of another Father.

(002-129)Exit Duke.

(002-130)Cel. Were I my Father (Coze) would I do this?

(002-131)Orl. I am more proud to be Sir Rolands sonne,

(002-132)His yongest sonne, and would not change that calling

(002-133)To be adopted heire to Fredricke

(002-134)Ros. My Father lou'd Sir Roland as his soule,

(002-135)And all the world was of my Fathers minde,

(002-136)Had I before knowne this yong man his sonne,

(002-137)I should haue giuen him teares vnto entreaties,

(002-138)Ere he should thus haue ventur'd

(002-139)Cel. Gentle Cosen,

(002-140)Let vs goe thanke him, and encourage him:

(002-141)My Fathers rough and enuious disposition

(002-142)Sticks me at heart: Sir, you haue well deseru'd,

(002-143)If you doe keepe your promises in loue;

(002-144)But iustly as you haue exceeded all promise,

(002-145)Your Mistris shall be happie

(002-146)Ros. Gentleman,

(002-147)Weare this for me: one out of suites with fortune

(002-148)That could giue more, but that her hand lacks meanes.

(002-149)Shall we goe Coze?

(002-150)Cel. I: fare you well faire Gentleman

(002-151)Orl. Can I not say, I thanke you? My better parts

(002-152)Are all throwne downe, and that which here stands vp

(002-153)Is but a quintine, a meere liuelesse blocke

(002-154)Ros. He cals vs back: my pride fell with my fortunes,

(002-155)Ile aske him what he would: Did you call Sir?

(002-156)Sir, you haue wrastled well, and ouerthrowne

(002-157)More then your enemies

(002-158)Cel. Will you goe Coze?

(002-159)Ros. Haue with you: fare you well.

(002-160)Enter.

(002-161)Orl. What passion hangs these waights vpo[n] my toong?

(002-162)I cannot speake to her, yet she vrg'd conference.

(002-163)Enter Le Beu.

(002-164)O poore Orlando! thou art ouerthrowne

(002-165)Or Charles, or something weaker masters thee

(002-166)Le Beu. Good Sir, I do in friendship counsaile you

(002-167)To leaue this place; Albeit you haue deseru'd

(002-168)High commendation, true applause, and loue;

(002-169)Yet such is now the Dukes condition,

(002-170)That he misconsters all that you haue done:

(002-171)[[duke-frederick|The Duke]] is humorous, what he is indeede

(002-172)More suites you to conceiue, then I to speake of

(002-173)Orl. I thanke you Sir; and pray you tell me this,

(002-174)Which of the two was daughter of the Duke,

(002-175)That here was at the Wrastling?

(002-176)Le Beu. Neither his daughter, if we iudge by manners,

(002-177)But yet indeede the taller is his daughter,

(002-178)The other is daughter to the banish'd Duke,

(002-179)And here detain'd by her vsurping Vncle

(002-182)But I can tell you, that of late this Duke

(002-183)Hath tane displeasure 'gainst his gentle Neece,

(002-187)And on my life his malice 'gainst the Lady

(002-192)Thus must I from the smoake into the smother,
