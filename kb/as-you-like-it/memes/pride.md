# Pride

## Definition

Pride in As You Like It is treated as a condition requiring correction — a barrier to love, fellowship, and self-knowledge that [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is designed to overcome. Multiple [[persons|characters]] experience pride's fall: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] admits "my pride fell with my fortunes" (002-154), [[phebe|Phebe]]'s disdainful pride is humiliated into loving [[silvius|Silvius]], and the usurping [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s pride is converted at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge. The play's pastoral argument holds that pride is a courtly disease that the [[forest-of-arden|green world]] cures.

## In As You Like It

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s pride is the first to be acknowledged. After [[wrestling-match|[[orlando|Orlando]]'s victory]] at the wrestling match, she calls him back with a love token. When he returns, she confesses: "He cals vs back: my pride fell with my fortunes" (002-154). Her [[exile|banishment]] has stripped her of the station that supported her pride, enabling the vulnerability that love requires.

[[phebe|Phebe]] embodies pride in its pastoral form — the disdainful shepherdess of Petrarchan convention who scorns her faithful lover [[silvius|Silvius]]. Her arrogance toward him is comically excessive, and her punishment is to fall in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]] ([[rosalind|Rosalind]] in disguise), who rebukes her: "Sell me when you can, you are no man for me" (014-029). Pride must be humbled before genuine love is possible.

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s pride is the arrogance of the elder brother who denies his younger sibling's worth. His attempted fratricide is pride's logical extreme. His conversion — saving [[orlando|Orlando]] from the lioness — represents pride's defeat by fraternal love.

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s pride manifests as tyranny. He banishes first his brother, then [[rosalind|Rosalind]]. His sudden conversion at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge — "After some question with him, was conuerted" (022-115) — is the play's most dramatic instance of pride overthrown. [[jaques|Jaques]]'s reflection on the event notes: "out of these conuertites, There is much matter to be heard, and learn'd" (022-139).
