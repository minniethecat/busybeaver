(018-001)Enter [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]].

(018-002)Ros. How say you now, is it not past two a clock?

(018-003)And heere much [[orlando|Orlando]]

(018-004)Cel. I warrant you, with pure [[love|loue]], & troubled brain,

(018-005)Enter Siluius.

(018-006)He hath t'ane his bow and arrowes, and is gone forth

(018-007)To sleepe: looke who comes heere

(018-008)Sil. My errand is to you, faire [[youth]],

(018-009)My gentle [[phebe|Phebe]], did bid me giue you this:

(018-010)I know not the contents, but as I guesse

(018-011)By the sterne brow, and waspish action

(018-012)Which she did vse, as she was writing of it,

(018-013)It beares an angry tenure; pardon me,

(018-014)I am but as a guiltlesse messenger

(018-015)Ros. [[patience|Patience]] her selfe would startle at this letter,

(018-016)And play the swaggerer, beare this, beare all:

(018-017)Shee saies I am not faire, that I lacke manners,

(018-018)She calls me proud, and that she could not loue me

(018-019)Were man as rare as Phenix: 'od's my will,

(018-020)Her loue is not the Hare that I doe hunt,

(018-021)Why writes she so to me? well Shepheard, well,

(018-022)This is a Letter of your owne deuice

(018-023)Sil. No, I protest, I know not the contents,

(018-024)Phebe did write it

(018-025)Ros. Come, come, you are a foole,

(018-026)And turn'd into the extremity of loue.

(018-027)I saw her hand, she has a leatherne hand,

(018-028)A freestone coloured hand: I verily did thinke

(018-029)That her old gloues were on, but twas her hands:

(018-030)She has a huswiues hand, but that's no matter:

(018-031)I say she neuer did inuent this letter,

(018-032)This is a mans inuention, and his hand

(018-033)Sil. Sure it is hers

(018-034)Ros. Why, tis a boysterous and a cruell stile,

(018-035)A stile for challengers: why, she defies me,

(018-036)Like Turke to Christian: womens gentle braine

(018-037)Could not drop forth such giant rude inuention,

(018-038)Such Ethiop words, blacker in their effect

(018-039)Then in their countenance: will you heare the letter?

(018-040)Sil. So please you, for I neuer heard it yet:

(018-041)Yet heard too much of Phebes crueltie

(018-042)Ros. She Phebes me: marke how the tyrant writes.

(018-043)Read.

(018-044)Art thou god, to Shepherd turn'd?

(018-045)That a maidens heart hath burn'd.

(018-046)Can a woman raile thus?

(018-047)Sil. Call you this railing?

(018-048)Ros.

(018-049)Read.

(018-050)Why, thy godhead laid a part,

(018-051)War'st thou with a womans heart?

(018-052)Did you euer heare such railing?

(018-053)Whiles the eye of man did wooe me,

(018-054)That could do no vengeance to me.

(018-055)Meaning me a beast.

(018-056)If the scorne of your bright eine

(018-057)Haue power to raise such loue in mine,

(018-058)Alacke, in me, what strange effect

(018-059)Would they worke in milde aspect?

(018-060)Whiles you chid me, I did loue,

(018-061)How then might your praiers moue?

(018-062)He that brings this loue to thee,

(018-063)Little knowes this Loue in me:

(018-064)And by him seale vp thy minde,

(018-065)Whether that thy youth and kinde

(018-066)Will the faithfull offer take

(018-067)Of me, and all that I can make,

(018-068)Or else by him my loue denie,

(018-069)And then Ile studie how to die

(018-070)Sil. Call you this chiding?

(018-071)Cel. Alas poore Shepheard

(018-072)Ros. Doe you pitty him? No, he deserues no pitty: wilt thou loue such a woman? what to make thee an instrument, and play false straines vpon thee? not to be endur'd. Well, goe your way to her; (for I see Loue hath made thee a tame snake) and say this to her; That if she loue me, I charge her to loue thee: if she will not, I will neuer haue her, vnlesse thou intreat for her: if you bee a true louer hence, and not a word; for here comes more company.

(018-073)Exit. Sil.

(018-074)Enter [[oliver|Oliuer]].

(018-075)Oliu. Good morrow, faire ones: pray you, (if you | know)

(018-076)Where in the Purlews of this Forrest, stands

(018-077)A sheep-coat, fenc'd about with Oliue-trees

(018-078)Cel. West of this place, down in the neighbor bottom

(018-079)The ranke of Oziers, by the murmuring streame

(018-080)Left on your right hand, brings you to the place:

(018-081)But at this howre, the house doth keepe it selfe,

(018-082)There's none within

(018-083)Oli. If that an eye may profit by a tongue,

(018-084)Then should I know you by description,

(018-085)Such garments, and such yeeres: the boy is faire,

(018-086)Of femall fauour, and bestowes himselfe

(018-087)Like a ripe sister: the woman low

(018-088)And browner then her brother: are not you

(018-089)The owner of the house I did enquire for?

(018-090)Cel. It is no boast, being ask'd, to say we are

(018-091)Oli. Orlando doth commend him to you both,

(018-092)And to that youth hee calls his Rosalind,

(018-093)He sends this bloudy napkin; are you he?

(018-094)Ros. I am: what must we vnderstand by this?

(018-095)Oli. Some of my shame, if you will know of me

(018-096)What man I am, and how, and why, and where

(018-097)This handkercher was stain'd

(018-098)Cel. I pray you tell it

(018-099)Oli. When last the yong Orlando parted from you,

(018-100)He left a promise to returne againe

(018-101)Within an houre, and pacing through the Forrest,

(018-102)Chewing the food of sweet and bitter fancie,

(018-103)Loe what befell: he threw his eye aside,

(018-104)And marke what obiect did present it selfe

(018-105)Vnder an old Oake, whose bows were moss'd with age

(018-106)And high top, bald with drie antiquitie:

(018-107)A wretched ragged man, ore-growne with haire

(018-108)Lay sleeping on his back; about his necke

(018-109)A greene and guilded snake had wreath'd it selfe,

(018-110)Who with her head, nimble in threats approach'd

(018-111)The opening of his mouth: but sodainly

(018-112)Seeing Orlando, it vnlink'd it selfe,

(018-113)And with indented glides, did slip away

(018-114)Into a bush, vnder which bushes shade

(018-115)A Lyonnesse, with vdders all drawne drie,

(018-116)Lay cowching head on ground, with catlike watch

(018-117)When that the sleeping man should stirre; for 'tis

(018-118)The royall disposition of that beast

(018-119)To prey on nothing, that doth seeme as dead:

(018-120)This seene, Orlando did approach the man,

(018-121)And found it was his brother, his elder brother

(018-122)Cel. O I haue heard him speake of that same brother,

(018-123)And he did render him the most vnnaturall

(018-124)That liu'd amongst men

(018-125)Oli. And well he might so doe,

(018-126)For well I know he was vnnaturall

(018-127)Ros. But to Orlando: did he leaue him there

(018-128)[[hunger-and-sustenance|Food]] to the suck'd and hungry Lyonnesse?

(018-129)Oli. Twice did he turne his backe, and purpos'd so:

(018-130)But kindnesse, nobler euer then reuenge,

(018-131)And [[nature|Nature]] stronger then his iust occasion,

(018-132)Made him giue battell to the Lyonnesse:

(018-133)Who quickly fell before him, in which hurtling

(018-134)From miserable slumber I awaked

(018-135)Cel. Are you his brother?

(018-136)Ros. Was't you he rescu'd?

(018-137)Cel. Was't you that did so oft contriue to kill him?

(018-138)Oli. 'Twas I: but 'tis not I: I doe not shame

(018-139)To tell you what I was, since my conuersion

(018-140)So sweetly tastes, being the thing I am

(018-141)Ros. But for the bloody napkin?

(018-142)Oli. By and by:

(018-143)When from the first to last betwixt vs two,

(018-144)Teares our recountments had most kindely bath'd,

(018-145)As how I came into that Desert place.

(018-146)In briefe, he led me to the gentle Duke,

(018-147)Who gaue me fresh aray, and entertainment,

(018-148)Committing me vnto my brothers loue,

(018-149)Who led me instantly vnto his Caue,

(018-150)There stript himselfe, and heere vpon his arme

(018-151)The Lyonnesse had torne some flesh away,

(018-152)Which all this while had bled; and now he fainted,

(018-153)And cride in fainting vpon Rosalinde.

(018-154)Briefe, I recouer'd him, bound vp his wound,

(018-155)And after some small space, being strong at heart,

(018-156)He sent me hither, stranger as I am

(018-157)To tell this story, that you might excuse

(018-158)His broken promise, and to giue this napkin

(018-159)Died in this bloud, vnto the Shepheard youth,

(018-160)That he in sport doth call his Rosalind

(018-161)Cel. Why how now [[ganimed|Ganimed]], sweet Ganimed

(018-162)Oli. Many will swoon when they do look on bloud

(018-163)Cel. There is more in it; Cosen Ganimed

(018-164)Oli. Looke, he recouers

(018-165)Ros. I would I were at home

(018-166)Cel. Wee'll lead you thither:

(018-167)I pray you will you take him by the arme

(018-168)Oli. Be of good cheere youth: you a man?

(018-169)You lacke a mans heart

(018-170)Ros. I doe so, I confesse it:

(018-171)Ah, sirra, a body would thinke this was well counterfeited,

(018-172)I pray you tell your brother how well I counterfeited:

(018-173)heigh-ho

(018-174)Oli. This was not counterfeit, there is too great testimony

(018-175)in your complexion, that it was a passion of earnest

(018-176)Ros. [[counterfeiting|Counterfeit]], I assure you

(018-177)Oli. Well then, take a good heart, and counterfeit to

(018-178)be a man

(018-179)Ros. So I doe: but yfaith, I should haue beene a woman

(018-180)by right

(018-181)Cel. Come, you looke paler and paler: pray you draw

(018-182)homewards: good sir, goe with vs

(018-183)Oli. That will I: for I must beare answere backe

(018-184)How you excuse my brother, Rosalind

(018-185)Ros. I shall deuise something: but I pray you commend

(018-186)my counterfeiting to him: will you goe?

(018-187)Exeunt.
