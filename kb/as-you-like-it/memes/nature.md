# Nature

## Definition

In As You Like It, Nature (*Natur*) is the principle of innate qualities and the natural order, set in opposition to three competing forces: [[fortune|Fortune]] (chance, external circumstances), Art (human contrivance, courtly artifice), and the Court itself. Nature governs what is born in a person — [[wit]], beauty, virtue, and natural bonds of blood. The play's pastoral argument holds that Nature flourishes in [[forest-of-arden|the Forest of Arden]] and is stifled at court. This opposition — Nature vs Art, Nature vs [[fortune|Fortune]], Nature vs Court — structures the play's philosophical argument and gives its pastoralism a seriousness that transcends mere escapism.

## The Nature-Fortune Opposition

The Nature-[[fortune|Fortune]] opposition structures the play's most explicit philosophical discussion, in Act 1, Scene 2, where [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] debate whether [[fortune|Fortune]] or Nature governs human happiness (002-014) (002-015). The distinction is precise and rooted in Renaissance commonplaces: [[fortune|Fortune]] governs "gifts of the world" — wealth, position, power, all that is external and subject to chance — while Nature governs "the lineaments of Nature" (002-015) — beauty, [[wit]], virtue, all that is innate.

[[celia|Celia]] argues that Nature provides [[wit]] to mock [[fortune|Fortune]] (002-018), while [[rosalind|Rosalind]] observes the paradox that [[fortune|Fortune]] can overpower Nature "when fortune makes natures naturall" (002-018). The phrase "natures naturall" — meaning both "Nature's fool" and "what Nature produces" — encapsulates the play's sophisticated understanding of the relationship between the innate and the contingent. Even Nature's gifts can be recontextualized by [[fortune|Fortune]]'s accidents. The [[green-world|forest]] becomes the space where this paradox is explored: [[fortune|Fortune]]'s accidents ([[freedom|exile]], [[exile|banishment]], loss of status) strip away social identity, but what remains is not pure Nature but a complex negotiation between the innate and the circumstantial.

## Nature vs Art and the Court

The Nature-Court opposition is established through spatial contrast. The court of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] is a space of artifice, flattery, and "painted pompe" (004-005) — a place where natural bonds (brotherhood, loyalty, love) are violated. The [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] is presented as Nature's antidote: a space where "tongues in trees, bookes in the running brookes" (004-018) replace courtly rhetoric and where the seasons, not courtiers, dictate the rhythm of life.

Yet the play's treatment of this opposition is characteristically nuanced. Nature in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is not simply benign — it includes winter, "the seasons difference" (004-008), the "churlish chiding of the winters winde" (004-009). Nor is [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] completely free of artifice: [[orlando|Orlando]] hangs poems on trees, [[rosalind|Rosalind]] adopts a disguise, and [[touchstone|Touchstone]] continues to exercise courtly [[wit]]. The Nature-Court opposition is not a binary but a dialectic — [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] transforms courtly values rather than simply replacing them.

## Nature and the Natural Fool

[[touchstone|Touchstone]], as the "natural" fool, embodies the concept most paradoxically. [[celia|Celia]] calls him "Natures whetstone" — Nature's gift sent to sharpen [[wit]] (002-019). The whetstone metaphor is precise: [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s function is not to be witty himself (though he is) but to enable [[wit]] in others through his presence as a "natural." The paradox of the wise fool — that the person closest to Nature is also the person most capable of exposing artifice — runs throughout Shakespeare's work, but [[touchstone|Touchstone]] gives it its most sustained comic expression.

Throughout the play, [[touchstone|Touchstone]] exposes the artificiality of courtly conventions by appealing to natural instinct and common sense. His courtship of [[audrey|Audrey]] is deliberately anti-romantic, grounded in the "natural" facts of sexual desire rather than the Petrarchan idealizations of [[silvius|Silvius]] or [[orlando|Orlando]]. When he tells [[corin|Corin]] that court manners are "the only good" and [[corin|Corin]] replies "Those are not right, for they are not the manners of the country," [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s [[wit]] is momentarily silenced by Nature's simpler logic.

## Nature and Moral Order

[[orlando|Orlando]] invokes Nature's claim early in the play when he protests that his "[[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]]" — a quality of his Nature as the son of Sir Rowland de Boys — is denied by his brother's treatment of him "rustically" (001-002). The tension between Nature's gifts (innate worth) and [[fortune|Fortune]]'s gifts (worldly position) drives [[orlando|Orlando]]'s complaint and establishes the play's moral argument: Nature's hierarchy of worth should align with social hierarchy, and when it does not — as in [[oliver|Oliver]]'s usurpation of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s rights, or [[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s usurpation of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s — the natural order is violated. The play's resolution restores this alignment: the rightful duke returns, the worthy [[orlando|Orlando]] receives his inheritance, and the villainous are converted or neutralized.

## Nature in the Pastoral Tradition

Shakespeare's treatment of Nature in As You Like It engages with and transforms the pastoral tradition inherited from Theocritus, Virgil, and Sidney. Classical pastoral idealized rural life as a retreat from urban corruption, but Shakespeare complicates this idealization in several ways. The [[green-world|forest]] has real hardships: winter winds, hunger ([[orlando|Orlando]] and [[adam|Adam]] nearly starve), and the threat of wild beasts (the lioness that attacks [[oliver|Oliver]]). [[corin|Corin]] [[silvius|the shepherd]] is not an Arcadian swain but a "true labourer" who "earns that he eats" (012-034). And the play's resolution involves a return to court, not permanent pastoral retreat — Nature's role is to reform and restore the social order, not to replace it.

This qualified pastoralism gives the play's treatment of Nature its distinctive character: not a simple celebration of the natural over the artificial, but an exploration of how the natural and the artificial can be brought into productive relationship.

## Key Passages

### Nature vs Fortune (Act 1, Scene 2)

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] opens the debate by drawing the distinction: "Nay now thou goest from Fortunes office to Natures" (002-014). [[celia|Celia]] elaborates:

> Fortune reignes in gifts of the world, not in the lineaments of Nature (002-015)

The debate culminates in [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s paradox that [[fortune|Fortune]] can subvert Nature's own realm (002-018).

### Nature's Whetstone (Act 1, Scene 2)

[[celia|Celia]] recasts [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s presence:

> Peraduenture this is not Fortunes work neither, but Natures, who perceiueth our naturall wits too dull to reason of such goddesses, hath sent this Naturall for our whetstone: for alwaies the dulnesse of the foole, is the whetstone of the wits. (002-019)

This passage establishes the paradoxical wisdom of the "natural" fool — Nature's gift for sharpening understanding through apparent dullness.

### Nature in Exile (Act 2, Scene 1)

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] finds Nature's consolations in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]:

> Findes tongues in trees, bookes in the running brookes, Sermons in stones, and good in euery thing (004-018)

The four elements — trees, brooks, stones, everything — become Nature's scripture, replacing the flattery of courtiers with the honest instruction of the natural world.

### The Seasons' Difference (Act 2, Scene 1)

> the seasons difference, as the Icie phang and churlish chiding of the winters winde (004-008)

Nature is not sentimentalized; its harshness is acknowledged as part of its instructive power. [[duke-senior|The Duke]]'s pastoralism is honest enough to admit that Nature teaches through adversity as well as beauty.

### Nature's Claim (Act 1, Scene 1)

[[orlando|Orlando]] invokes Nature against [[fortune|Fortune]]'s injustice:

> the something that nature gaue mee, his countenance seemes to take from me (001-002)

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s complaint establishes the moral economy of the play: what Nature gives, society should [[honor]]. This principle drives the entire plot toward its restorative conclusion.
