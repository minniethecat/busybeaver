# Orlando

## Overview

Orlando de Boys is the youngest son of Sir Rowland de Boys and the romantic hero of As You Like It. Denied education and inheritance by his elder brother [[oliver|Oliver]], Orlando protests the injustice of his condition in the play's opening scene. His [[wrestling-match|victory over Charles the wrestler]] proves his natural virtue, wins [[rosalind|Rosalind's]] love, and incites [[oliver|Oliver]]'s murderous rage — setting all three of the play's plot lines in motion. In the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], Orlando hangs [[verse-and-poetry|love poems]] on trees, woos [[rosalind|Rosalind]] in her [[ganimed|Ganymede]] disguise, and redeems his brother through an act of life-saving mercy. His journey from dispossessed [[youth]] to worthy husband traces the play's central argument: that innate [[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]] will be recognized regardless of [[fortune|Fortune's]] obstacles.

## Role in the Play

Orlando opens the play with a complaint that defines its moral stakes. He is kept "rustically at home" (001-002) despite his noble birth, his education denied and his [[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]] mocked. His protest — "the something that nature gaue mee, his countenance seemes to take from me" (001-002) — establishes the [[nature|Nature]]-versus-[[fortune|Fortune]] conflict that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] will later debate.

The [[wrestling-match|wrestling match]] is [[orlando|Orlando]]'s first public assertion of identity. Despite being untrained and warned against fighting [[charles|Charles]], he prevails, revealing his worth to the court and to [[rosalind|Rosalind]]. His victory speech — "I am more proud to be Sir Rolands sonne" (002-131) — announces his claim to recognition.

In [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], Orlando becomes the [[silvius|pastoral lover]]. His poems — "O [[rosalind|Rosalind]], these Trees shall be my Bookes" (012-007) — are mocked by [[touchstone|Touchstone]] but taken seriously by [[rosalind|Rosalind]] as evidence of his constancy. His daily wooing of [[ganimed|Ganymede]]-as-[[rosalind|Rosalind]] teaches him to see women as they are rather than as Petrarchan ideals.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s finest moment is not romantic but ethical. Discovering his brother [[oliver|Oliver]] sleeping beneath a tree, threatened by a lioness, Orlando twice turns away — and twice returns to save him. This act of mercy transforms [[oliver|Oliver]] from fratricide to brother, enabling the play's resolution and [[the-four-weddings|the four weddings]].

## Key Scenes

### The Complaint (Act 1, Scene 1)

Orlando protests his brother's treatment:

> the something that nature gaue mee, his countenance seemes to take from me: hee lets mee feede with his Hindes, barres mee the place of a brother, and as much as in him lies, mines my gentility with my education (001-002)

### The Wrestling Victory (Act 1, Scene 2)

Orlando defeats [[charles|Charles]] and claims his identity:

> Orl. Orlando my Liege, the yongest sonne of Sir Roland de Boys (002-120)

> Orl. I am more proud to be Sir Rolands sonne, I would not change that calling (002-131)

### Struck Dumb by Love (Act 1, Scene 2)

> What passion hangs these waights vpon my toong? I cannot speake to her, yet shee vrg'd conference. O poore Orlando! thou art ouerthrowne (002-161)

### The Love Poems (Act 3, Scene 2)

> O Rosalind, these Trees shall be my Bookes, And in their barkes my thoughts Ile charracter (012-007)

### Saving Adam (Act 2, Scene 7)

Orlando carries the starving [[adam|Adam]] into the Duke's camp and demands food at sword-point:

> Orl. Forbeare, and eate no more... Nor shalt not, till necessities be seru'd (010-092)

### Saving Oliver (Act 4, Scene 3)

Orlando describes his internal struggle before rescuing his brother from the lioness — the act that converts [[oliver|Oliver]]'s hatred to love.
