# Fortune

## Definition

Fortune, or Lady Fortune (*Fortuna*), is a personified force in Renaissance literature representing the capricious distribution of worldly goods, status, and fate. In Shakespeare's As You Like It, Fortune is repeatedly invoked as a goddess who governs the external circumstances of human life — wealth, rank, and chance events — in contrast to [[nature|Nature]], who governs innate qualities like [[wit]], beauty, and virtue.

## In As You Like It

Fortune appears throughout As You Like It as one half of the Fortune-[[nature|Nature]] dyad that structures much of the play's philosophical discourse. The opposition between Fortune (chance, external circumstance) and [[nature|Nature]] (innate quality, internal disposition) is most explicitly developed in Act 1, Scene 2, where [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] debate the relative powers of the two goddesses (002-014) (002-015) (002-018).

The Fortune-[[nature|Nature]] debate frames the play's central movement from [[duke-fredericks-court|court]] to [[forest-of-arden-place|forest]]: [[persons|characters]] are stripped of Fortune's gifts (rank, wealth, position) in [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], leaving only [[nature|Nature]]'s gifts ([[wit]], love, loyalty) to sustain them. This pattern is established early when [[orlando|Orlando]] laments his lost "[[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]]" — a gift of both [[nature|Nature]] (birth) and Fortune (education) (001-025).

Fortune is also invoked by [[persons|characters]] experiencing dramatic reversals: [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s pride falls "with my fortunes" (002-154), while the exiled [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] finds that adversity "can translate the stubbornnesse of fortune" into sweetness (004-021). [[jaques|Jaques]] recounts [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s mockery of Lady Fortune "in good termes" (010-018), and [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s words as reported by [[jaques|Jaques]] — "Call me not foole, till heauen hath sent me fortune" (010-021) — link earthly Fortune with divine will. The play's comedic resolution sees Fortune restored through [[the-four-weddings|the multiple marriages]] and the [[regeneration|restoration]] of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s dukedom.

## Key Passages

### The Fortune-Nature Debate (Act 1, Scene 2)

[[celia|Celia]] opens the debate by proposing to "mocke the good houswife Fortune" (002-009), initiating an extended philosophical exchange with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] about the relationship between Fortune's gifts and [[nature|Nature]]'s endowments:

> **Celia**: No; when Nature hath made a faire creature, may she not by Fortune fall into the fire? though nature hath giuen vs wit to flout at Fortune, hath not Fortune sent in this foole to cut off the argument? (002-018)

> **Rosalind**: Indeed there is fortune too hard for nature, when fortune makes natures naturall, the cutter off of natures witte. (002-018)

The entrance of [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — described as "Fortune's foole" — embodies the intersection of the two forces (002-018). [[celia|Celia]] further reframes [[touchstone|Touchstone]] as [[nature|Nature]]'s counter-gift: "Peraduenture this is not Fortunes work neither, but Natures" (002-019), suggesting that even apparent accidents of Fortune may be workings of [[nature|Nature]].

### Fortune in Exile (Act 2, Scene 1)

The exiled [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] reframes adversity as a moral teacher that "can translate the stubbornnesse of fortune" (004-021), transforming misfortune into a source of wisdom. This pastoral revaluation of Fortune's blows is a key element of the play's treatment of [[freedom|exile]].

### Fortune and Love (Act 5, Scene 2)

[[rosalind|Rosalind]], in her final revelation to [[orlando|Orlando]], describes how Fortune has shaped the lovers' reunion: "Know of me then... that I know you are a Gentleman of good conceit" (020-022), suggesting that the happy resolution is itself a gift of Fortune, completing the arc from initial [[exile|banishment]] to final [[regeneration|restoration]].

### Lady Fortune Mocked (Act 2, Scene 7)

[[jaques|Jaques]] recounts [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s meditation: [[touchstone|Touchstone]] "rail'd on Lady Fortune in good termes" (010-018), a reminder of [[touchstone|the Fool]]'s traditional license to mock the goddess. Through [[jaques|Jaques]]'s report, [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s words — "Call me not foole, till heauen hath sent me fortune" (010-021) — link earthly Fortune with divine will, suggesting a providential dimension to Fortune's apparent caprice.
