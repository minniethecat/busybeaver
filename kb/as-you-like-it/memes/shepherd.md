## Definition

The shepherd is the foundational figure of pastoral literature: a rural dweller whose simple life provides a mirror for the complexities of court society. In *As You Like It*, Shakespeare deploys multiple versions of [[silvius|the shepherd]] — the realistic working shepherd ([[corin|Corin]]), the lovelorn [[silvius|pastoral lover]] ([[silvius|Silvius]]), and the disdainful shepherdess ([[phebe|Phebe]]) — to test and complicate the pastoral ideal inherited from classical and Renaissance convention.

## In As You Like It

The play introduces its shepherds in Act 2, Scene 4, when the exhausted court refugees encounter [[corin|Corin]] and [[silvius|Silvius]] in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. The encounter immediately establishes a tension between pastoral ideal and rural reality. [[silvius|Silvius]] performs the role of Petrarchan lover with theatrical excess: "Oh [[corin|Corin]], that thou knew'st how I do love her" (007-017). [[corin|Corin]], by contrast, responds with pragmatic skepticism: "No [[corin|Corin]], being old, thou canst not guess" (007-019) — [[silvius|Silvius]]'s youthful passion is met with the quiet wisdom of [[youth|age]].

[[corin|Corin]] is the play's most grounded figure. His exchange with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] on [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s life is a masterclass in Shakespearean dialectic:

> "And how like you this shepherds life Mr Touchstone?" (012-014)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s reply — comparing [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s life favorably to court life in some respects, unfavorably in others — is cut short by [[corin|Corin]]'s plain realism. [[corin|Corin]] does not romanticize his labor; he simply does it. He is the counterweight to the play's pastoral fantasies.

[[silvius|Silvius]], meanwhile, embodies pastoral love convention pushed to its limit. His [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] to the scornful [[phebe|Phebe]] is so extreme that it becomes comic — yet the play ultimately rewards his constancy with marriage. [[rosalind|Rosalind]], disguised as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], uses [[silvius|Silvius]] as both a negative example (what [[orlando|Orlando]] must avoid becoming) and a proof of love's power (even a fool in love deserves his reward).

[[phebe|Phebe]], the shepherdess who scorns [[silvius|Silvius]] and falls for [[ganimed|Ganymede]]/[[rosalind|Rosalind]], completes [[silvius|the shepherd]] triad. Her mock-pastoral cruelty ("I scorne you") and her instant infatuation with "[[ganimed|Ganymede]]" parody the conventions even as they advance the plot. When she accepts [[silvius|Silvius]] at the play's end, it is an acknowledgment that pastoral convention, however artificial, serves a genuine social function.

## Key Passages

### [[silvius|Silvius]]'s Plea to Corin

> "Oh Corin, that thou knew'st how I do love her." (007-017)

[[silvius|Silvius]] in a single line captures the [[silvius|pastoral lover]]'s self-absorption. The appeal to [[corin|Corin]] as audience — the lover needs a witness to perform his suffering for — is characteristic of the Petrarchan mode.

### Corin on Age

> "No Corin, being old, thou canst not guess." (007-019)

[[silvius|Silvius]] assumes that [[youth|age]] disqualifies [[corin|Corin]] from understanding love. The play quietly challenges this assumption: [[corin|Corin]]'s silence contains more wisdom than [[silvius|Silvius]]'s effusions.

### Touchstone on Shepherd's Life

> "And how like you this shepherds life Mr Touchstone?" (012-014)

[[corin|Corin]]'s question invites [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s sardonic commentary on pastoral life. The court fool's presence in [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s world creates the play's richest clash of perspectives.

### Phebe Falls for Ganymede

[[phebe|Phebe]]'s instant attraction to the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]] parodies the pastoral convention of love at first sight. Her shift from scorning [[silvius|Silvius]] to pursuing [[ganimed|Ganymede]] in Act 3, Scene 5, demonstrates the arbitrary nature of pastoral desire — and by extension, of all romantic love in the play.

### The Devil and Shepherds

> "the diuell himselfe will haue no shepherds, I cannot see else how thou shouldst." (012-035)

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s dark joke to [[corin|Corin]] suggests that even the devil draws the line at shepherds. The quip is both an insult and a backhanded compliment: [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s simplicity is both contemptible and somehow incorruptible.

## Related Characters

- **[[corin]]** — The working shepherd whose practical wisdom grounds the play's pastoral flights. His exchange with [[touchstone|Touchstone]] is the play's most direct examination of rural versus court life.
- **[[silvius]]** — The Petrarchan shepherd-lover whose extravagant [[loyalty-and-service|devotion]] to [[phebe|Phebe]] both embodies and parodies pastoral convention.
- **[[phebe]]** — The disdainful shepherdess whose cruelty to [[silvius|Silvius]] and comic misdirected desire for [[ganimed|Ganymede]] drive the pastoral subplot.
- **[[touchstone]]** — The court fool whose encounter with [[corin|Corin]] produces the play's sharpest critique of pastoral idealism.
- **[[rosalind]]** — As [[ganimed|Ganymede]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]] manipulates [[silvius|the shepherd]] love-triangle to bring about the play's comic resolution.
