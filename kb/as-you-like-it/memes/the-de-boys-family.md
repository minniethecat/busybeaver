# The de Boys Family

The de Boys family provides the domestic architecture of *As You Like It*, with the conflict and eventual reconciliation of its three brothers — [[oliver|Oliver]], [[jaques-de-boys|Jaques de Boys]], and [[orlando|Orlando]] — running parallel to the political usurpation and restoration of the ducal brothers [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] and [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]].

## Sir Rowland's Legacy

The family is defined by the death of its patriarch, [[sir-rowland-de-boys|Sir Rowland de Boys]], whose will and reputation shape the play's opening action. Sir Rowland left Orlando a thousand crowns and charged Oliver with his younger brother's education. Oliver's violation of this charge — keeping Orlando "rustically at home" without the training befitting a gentleman — is the play's originating injustice:

> "My father charged you in his will to give me good education. You have trained me like a peasant."

Sir Rowland's reputation, however, extends beyond his will. He was a man of honor and virtue, a fact acknowledged even by his enemies. When Orlando defeats the wrestler [[charles|Charles]], [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] is dismayed to learn that the victor is Sir Rowland's son — "the world esteemed thy father honourable, but I did find him still mine enemy."

## The Three Brothers

The de Boys brothers embody three distinct responses to their father's legacy. **Oliver**, the eldest, has inherited the estate and authority but has corrupted both — using his position to oppress rather than protect his younger brother. His character arc traces a complete moral transformation, from attempted murderer to repentant brother and husband of [[celia|Celia]].

**Orlando**, the youngest, possesses Sir Rowland's spirit and virtue but lacks the means to express his gentility. Denied education, he proves his worth through physical courage — wrestling, fighting the lioness — and through the natural eloquence of his love for [[rosalind|Rosalind]].

**Jaques de Boys**, the middle brother, appears only at the play's end as the bearer of news that resolves the political plot. His role as messenger and connector — bringing the outside world into Arden at the crucial moment — mirrors his position in the family structure, mediating between the extremes represented by Oliver and Orlando.

## The Servant as Family

[[adam|Adam]], the old servant who served Sir Rowland and now serves Orlando, functions as an honorary member of the de Boys family. His loyalty — offering his life savings of five hundred crowns to help Orlando escape Oliver's murderous plot — embodies the ideal of faithful service that the brothers' own relationship has betrayed:

> "O good old man, how well in thee appears / The constant service of the antique world."

Adam represents the older, more virtuous order of which Sir Rowland was part — a world that the play seeks to recover through the brothers' reconciliation and the restoration of just rule.

## Reconciliation

The de Boys family is restored through a series of conversions and recognitions. Oliver's rescue by Orlando from the [[the-lion-incident|lioness]] triggers his moral reformation. Jaques de Boys's [[jaques-de-boys-arrival|arrival]] with news of Duke Frederick's conversion reunites all three brothers. By the play's end, the family has been reconstituted as a harmonious unit — the fraternal bond restored through the redemptive power of the forest.
