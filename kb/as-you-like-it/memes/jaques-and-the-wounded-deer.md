## Scene Context

Act 2, Scene 1 opens in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], where [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and his exiled lords — including [[amiens|Amiens]] and the "melancholy [[jaques|Jaques]]" — have taken refuge from court life. The scene establishes the pastoral contrast between the "painted pomp" of the court and the "sweet" adversity of [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] [004-003–016]. It is in this setting that the First Lord reports [[jaques|Jaques]]' dramatic reaction to a wounded stag.

## The Moralization

The First Lord recounts to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] how [[jaques|Jaques]] came upon a "poor sequestered stag" that had been wounded by hunters (004-036). [[jaques|Jaques]]' response transforms the simple hunting scene into an extended philosophical meditation:

- **The stag as tragic figure**: [[jaques|Jaques]] observes the stag "weeping into the needless stream" and declares, "Poor deer... thou mak'st a testament / As worldlings do, giving thy sum of more / To that which had too much" [004-050–053]. The stag's tears flowing into an already-full stream becomes a metaphor for the futility of excess.

- **The "fat and greasy citizens"**: [[jaques|Jaques]] extends his critique to the human realm, railing against "fat and greasy citizens" who appropriate [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s bounty while ignoring the suffering of its creatures (004-059). This remark connects the stag's plight to social satire.

- **The weeping lords**: The First Lord notes that he and his companions were "weeping and commenting / Upon the sobbing deer" [004-069–070], suggesting [[jaques|Jaques]]' performance moved even his audience to tears.

## Dramatic Significance

The scene serves multiple dramatic functions. It introduces [[jaques|Jaques]] not through direct action but through reported speech — a technique that amplifies his theatricality before the audience meets him in person. The moralization on the wounded deer establishes [[jaques|Jaques]] as the play's philosophical commentator, whose "most humorous sadness" (004-018) distinguishes him from [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s stoic acceptance of [[freedom|exile]].

The stag also functions as a symbol of the exiled courtiers themselves: a noble creature displaced and vulnerable, yet the object of both pity and aesthetic contemplation. This mirroring between the wounded animal and the exiled humans deepens the scene's pastoral meditation on the relationship between suffering, observation, and art.
