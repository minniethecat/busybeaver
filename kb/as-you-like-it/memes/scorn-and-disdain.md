# Scorn and Disdain

## Definition

Scorn and disdain in *As You Like It* function as a thematic strand within the
play's broader treatment of love — specifically the Petrarchan convention of
the "cruel fair," in which the beloved woman scorns her devoted suitor. The
convention, inherited from Renaissance love [[verse-and-poetry|poetry]], casts the male lover as a
suffering petitioner and the woman as a disdainful object of desire whose
rejection fuels the lover's melancholy. In *As You Like It*, Shakespeare
both deploys this convention (through [[phebe|Phebe]]'s treatment of [[silvius|Silvius]]) and
satirises it (through [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s mockery of the entire posture).

## In As You Like It

The primary vehicle for the scorn/disdain theme is the [[phebe|Phebe]]–[[silvius|Silvius]] subplot.
[[silvius|Silvius]] is introduced as the archetypal Petrarchan lover — abject, pleading,
and met with contempt. His repeated appeals to [[phebe|Phebe]] ("Sweet [[phebe|Phebe]] doe not
scorne me, do not [[phebe|Phebe]]") receive only cold dismissal (015-002). [[phebe|Phebe]]'s
scorn is so complete that she even mocks him to his face: [[gender-performance|Rosalind-as-Ganymede]]
observes that [[phebe|Phebe]] shows "the red glowe of scorne and prowd disdaine" toward
her faithful shepherd (014-038).

The play's pastoral setting intensifies the theme: the conventions of
courtly love are transposed to [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], where they appear
artificial against the backdrop of a simpler rural world. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s
parodic courtship of [[audrey|Audrey]] serves as a comic counterpoint, exposing the
absurdity of the scornful-beloved convention by replacing it with blunt
rustic pragmatism.

[[rosalind|Rosalind]], as [[ganimed|Ganymede]], actively intervenes in the scorn dynamic. When [[phebe|Phebe]]
falls in love with the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (believing her to be a boy named
[[ganimed|Ganymede]]), the scorn pattern is inverted: [[phebe|Phebe]] becomes the rejected suitor,
experiencing precisely the pain she inflicted on [[silvius|Silvius]]. [[ganimed|Ganymede]]'s sharp
rebuke — "I pray you doe not fall in [[love|loue]] with mee, / For I am falser then
vowes made in wine" (015-073) — mirrors the bitter words [[phebe|Phebe]] earlier
directed at [[silvius|Silvius]] (015-073). This inversion is the play's moral and
comedic resolution of the scorn theme: the scorner becomes the scorned.

The fourth act intensifies [[phebe|Phebe]]'s bitterness as she realises [[ganimed|Ganymede]]'s
indifference. "And now I am remembred, scorn'd at me," she laments (015-137),
completing the circle of disdain. The play ultimately resolves the theme
through [[phebe|Phebe]]'s forced acceptance of [[silvius|Silvius]] in Act 5 — she marries him
on the condition that "if she do refute, / She shall be married to this
scornfull [[youth]]" — a resolution that is comic rather than psychologically
realistic.

> Shakespeare takes the Petrarchan convention of the disdainful mistress,
> embodied by [[phebe|Phebe]]'s scorn of Silvius, and subjects it to a pastoral
> deflation that exposes the convention's absurdity while simultaneously
> affirming love's power to humble the proud. The scorner made to feel
> scorn is the play's most direct moral accounting.
