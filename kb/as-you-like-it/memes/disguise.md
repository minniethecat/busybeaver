## Definition

Disguise is the central dramatic device of As You Like It. Upon entering [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]] adopts the male persona of [[ganimed|Ganymede]], and [[celia|Celia]] becomes [[aliena|Aliena]]. These disguises serve practical purposes — safety for two women traveling alone — but they also enable the play's deepest investigations of gender, identity, and the nature of love. Through disguise, [[persons|characters]] can speak truths that social convention would otherwise suppress, and the play's metatheatrical games (the boy actor playing [[rosalind|Rosalind]] playing [[ganimed|Ganymede]] pretending to be [[rosalind|Rosalind]]) create vertiginous layers of performed identity.

## In As You Like It

The disguise motif is announced before any character enters [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]. [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler reports that [[orlando|Orlando]] intends to come "disguis'd" to their match — though this proves to be [[oliver|Oliver]]'s fabrication, it plants the motif early (001-046).

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s assumption of the [[ganimed|Ganymede]] identity is deliberate and strategic:

> Therefore looke you call me Ganimed (003-116)

[[celia|Celia]] follows immediately: "No longer [[celia|Celia]], but [[aliena|Aliena]]" (003-119). The double disguise creates a transformed social world in which the two women can operate with freedoms denied to them at court.

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s disguise is particularly rich because it enables her to educate [[orlando|Orlando]] in love. As [[ganimed|Ganymede]], she can speak to him as one man to another, proposing to "cure" his love-sickness by role-playing [[rosalind|Rosalind]] herself. This creates Shakespeare's most complex gender performance:

> I could finde in my heart to disgrace my mans apparell, and to cry like a woman (007-006)

The tension between [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s female self and her male performance generates both comedy and insight. The disguise is never purely convincing — [[rosalind|Rosalind]] nearly betrays herself multiple times — and this fragility becomes part of the play's investigation of identity. When the disguises are finally shed at the wedding celebration, the revelation is not merely a plot resolution but a [[regeneration|restoration]] of wholeness after the necessary fictions of [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]].

## Disguise and Gender on the Elizabethan Stage

The disguise motif in As You Like It is intensified by the conditions of
Elizabethan theatrical performance, where female roles were played by boy
actors. When the boy actor playing [[rosalind|Rosalind]] disguises "herself"
as the boy [[ganimed|Ganymede]], the audience witnesses a boy playing a woman
playing a boy — a triple layering of performed gender that Shakespeare
exploits for both comic and philosophical effect. The epilogue drives this
home explicitly: "If I were a Woman, I would kisse as many of you as had
beards that pleas'd me" (023-001). The conditional "If I were a Woman"
collapses the distinction between character and actor, reminding the audience
that all the gender performances they have witnessed are theatrical
constructs.

## The Epistemology of Disguise

Beyond its comic function, disguise in As You Like It raises serious
questions about knowledge and identity. Can [[orlando|Orlando]] truly love
[[rosalind|Rosalind]] if he does not recognise her? What does it mean that
[[phebe|Phebe]] falls in love with [[ganimed|Ganymede]] — a woman disguised
as a man — rather than with [[silvius|Silvius]], the "real" shepherd? The
play does not provide simple answers. Instead, it suggests that love itself
is a form of disguise: the lover sees not the beloved as she is, but as
desire constructs her. [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s parodic courtship of
[[audrey|Audrey]] — the one relationship in the play unmediated by disguise —
serves as the comic exception that proves the rule: without the mediating
fiction of romantic idealisation, love appears as mere appetite.

## The Unmasking

The removal of disguises in Act 5 is one of Shakespeare's most economical
dramatic moments. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] exits as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] and
re-enters as herself, accompanied by [[hymen|Hymen]], the god of marriage
(021-019). The disguise is shed offstage — we do not witness the
transformation — and this absence is itself significant: the play refuses
to show us the "real" [[rosalind|Rosalind]] emerging from behind the
disguise, preserving the ambiguity of identity that [[disguise-plan|the disguise]] has
sustained throughout. [[celia|Celia]]'s parallel transformation from
[[aliena|Aliena]] back to herself receives even less attention, a reminder
that [[celia|Celia]]'s disguise was always the more conventional and less
philosophically charged of the two.
