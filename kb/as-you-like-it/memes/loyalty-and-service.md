# Loyalty and Service

## Definition

Loyalty and service in As You Like It represent voluntary bonds of devotion that stand in opposition to the play's patterns of betrayal and self-interest. The master-servant relationship is repeatedly idealized — most notably in [[adam|Adam]]'s devotion to [[orlando|Orlando]] — as a model of ethical conduct rooted in love rather than obligation. Service freely given ([[adam|Adam]], [[celia|Celia]], the exiled lords) is contrasted with brotherhood betrayed ([[oliver|Oliver]] against [[orlando|Orlando]], [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] against [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]), defining the play's moral landscape.

## In As You Like It

[[adam|Adam]], the aged servant of the de Boys household, embodies the ideal of loyal service. Having served Sir Rowland de Boys and now his youngest son [[orlando|Orlando]], [[adam|Adam]] offers his life savings — "fiue hundred Crownes" — to fund their escape from [[oliver|Oliver]]'s murderous household (006-040). His loyalty is not contractual but affective: "God be with my olde master, he would not haue spoke such a word" (001-029). The aged servant's devotion shames the fraternal betrayal that drives the plot.

[[celia|Celia]]'s loyalty to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] exceeds even [[adam|Adam]]'s in its voluntarism. She chooses [[freedom|exile]] over the comforts of court, rejecting her inheritance: "thou shalt be his heire" (002-006). Her declaration — "To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]" (003-097) — transforms an act of filial duty into one of freely chosen companionship.

The exiled lords who "haue put themselues into voluntary [[freedom|exile]]" with [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] (001-041) represent collective loyalty, choosing the hardships of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] over the advantages of [[duke-fredericks-court|[[duke-frederick|Frederick]]'s court]]. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] acknowledges this bond in his opening [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] speech: "Now my Coe-mates, and brothers in [[freedom|exile]]" (004-003).

Against these examples of faithful service, the play sets the betrayal of brother against brother: [[oliver|Oliver]] plots [[orlando|Orlando]]'s death, and [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] usurps [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s throne. The play's resolution restores loyalty: [[oliver|Oliver]] converts from fratricide to fraternal love, and [[duke-fredericks-conversion|Frederick's conversion]] enables the loyal exiles' return.

## Key Passages

### [[adam|Adam]]'s Complaint (Act 1, Scene 1)

> Adam. Is old dogge my reward: most true, I haue lost my teeth in your seruice: God be with my olde master, he would not haue spoke such a word. (001-029)

### [[adam|Adam]]'s Offering (Act 2, Scene 3)

[[adam|Adam]] offers his life's savings to [[orlando|Orlando]]:

> Ad. But do not so: I haue fiue hundred Crownes, The thriftie hire I saued vnder your Father, Which I did store to be my foster Nurse, When seruice should in my old limbs lie lame... Take that, and he that doth the Rauens feede, Yea prouidently caters for the Sparrow, Be comfort to my age: here is the gold, All this I giue you, let me be your seruant (006-040)

### [[celia|Celia]]'s Voluntary Exile (Act 1, Scene 3)

> Cel. To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of Arden (003-097)

### Duke Senior to His Fellow Exiles (Act 2, Scene 1)

> Duk.Sen. Now my Coe-mates, and brothers in exile (004-003)

### The Exiled Lords (Act 1, Scene 1)

> three or foure louing Lords haue put themselues into voluntary exile with him, whose lands and reuennewes enrich the new Duke, therefore he giues them good leaue to wander (001-041)
