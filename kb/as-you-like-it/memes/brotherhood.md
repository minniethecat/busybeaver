## Definition

Brotherhood in *As You Like It* is presented primarily through its violation: the play opens with two separate fraternal conflicts — [[oliver|Oliver]]'s persecution of [[orlando|Orlando]] and [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s usurpation of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] — and much of its dramatic energy derives from the question of whether these broken bonds can be repaired. Shakespeare doubles the brother-against-brother motif across the social spectrum (noble household and ducal court), suggesting that fraternal strife is a structural feature of the world the [[persons|characters]] inhabit, not an individual moral failing. The play's movement from fratricidal opening to fraternal reunion constitutes its deepest structural arc.

## Historical and Biblical Context

Fraternal conflict was a charged theme in Elizabethan England. Primogeniture — the legal principle that the eldest son inherits the entire estate — created systematic inequality between brothers, and the anxiety of younger brothers dispossessed by birth order was a recognized social phenomenon. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s complaint that "my brother... barres mee the place of a brother" (001-002) would have resonated with younger sons in Shakespeare's audience who knew firsthand the economic and social precariousness of their position.

The biblical archetypes are equally present. The story of Cain and Abel lurks behind every fraternal conflict in Renaissance literature, and Shakespeare explicitly invokes it when [[oliver|Oliver]] asks "Wilt thou lay hands on me villaine?" (001-033) — an echo of Cain's "Am I my brother's keeper?" The play also draws on the parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15), in which the younger brother's return prompts the elder's resentment — a pattern that [[orlando|Orlando]] partially reverses, as the wronged younger brother becomes the agent of the elder's redemption. And the story of Jacob and Esau, the twin brothers whose rivalry for their father's blessing structures the book of Genesis, provides the archetypal pattern of fraternal strife resolved through reunion.

## In As You Like It

### The Opening Conflict

The play begins in the midst of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s grievance against his older brother [[oliver|Oliver]]. The opening scene establishes the core fraternal injustice with remarkable efficiency. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s long opening speech (001-002) catalogues [[oliver|Oliver]]'s violations: he has been denied education ("he keepes me rustically at home... vnkept"), treated worse than livestock ("his horses are bred better"), and "barres mee the place of a brother." The speech accumulates grievances with the force of a legal indictment, and its length — by far the longest speech in the opening scene — gives [[orlando|Orlando]]'s complaint a weight that the play never fully rebuts.

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s central claim is that [[oliver|Oliver]] has violated the natural order: "I know you are my eldest brother, and in the gentle condition of blood you should so know me" (001-019). "Gentle condition of blood" carries a double meaning — both the aristocratic status they share ("gentle" as in [[virtue-and-gentility|gentility]]) and the natural bond that ought to govern their relationship ("gentle" as in kind). [[oliver|Oliver]] has broken both: he treats [[orlando|Orlando]] as a servant rather than a brother, and in doing so he violates not just family feeling but the social order itself.

### The Second Pair

The second fraternal conflict, between [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] and [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]], mirrors and amplifies the first. [[oliver|Oliver]] reports that "the [[duke-senior|old Duke]] is banished by his yonger brother the new Duke" (001-041) — a reversal of the [[oliver|Oliver]]/[[orlando|Orlando]] dynamic in which it is the younger brother who usurps. This reversal prevents any simple moral pattern from emerging: brotherhood is troubled regardless of birth order, and neither seniority nor juniority guarantees virtue.

The political dimension of the second pair is significant. Where the [[oliver|Oliver]]/[[orlando|Orlando]] conflict is domestic (inheritance, education, status within a single household), the [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]/[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] conflict is political (usurpation, [[exile|banishment]], the governance of a duchy). The doubling suggests that fraternal strife is not merely a family problem but a political one — that the same forces that tear brothers apart also tear polities apart.

### The Brotherhood of Exile

If the court destroys brotherhood, [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] creates new forms of it. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] addresses his fellow exiles as "my Coe-mates, and brothers in [[freedom|exile]]" (004-003), redeploying "brothers" from a broken blood relation to a voluntary community. The [[green-world|forest]] brotherhood is based not on birth but on shared experience — "Hath not old custome made this life more sweet / Then that of painted pompe?" [004-014–015] — and its bonds are perhaps stronger for being chosen rather than inherited.

[[adam|Adam]] embodies this voluntary brotherhood most completely. The old servant offers his life savings and his remaining strength to [[orlando|Orlando]]: "I haue fiue hundred Crownes... Take that" [007-021–022]. He calls himself "a poore vnworthy brother of yours" (001-010), extending fraternal language beyond blood to encompass loyalty and love. What [[oliver|Oliver]] refuses to give — the care of a brother — [[adam|Adam]] provides freely.

### [[oliver|Oliver]]'s Conversion

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s conversion in Act 4 is the play's most dramatic instance of fraternal repair. Having pursued [[orlando|Orlando]] into [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] with murderous intent, he is saved from a lioness by the very brother he sought to kill. The reformed [[oliver|Oliver]] describes the event to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]]:

> "I found him vnder a tree... A Lionnesse, with vdders all drawne drie, / Lay cowching head on ground" [011-020–021]

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s mercy — "kindnesse, nobler euer then reuenge" (011-019) — transforms [[oliver|Oliver]]. The verb is crucial: "kindnesse" means both benevolence and kinship. [[orlando|Orlando]]'s act of mercy restores the "kind" between them, the natural bond that [[oliver|Oliver]] had severed. The converted [[oliver|Oliver]]'s first words are "Finde out thy brother wheresoere he is" (011-006) — a complete reversal of his earlier pursuit of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s death.

### [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s Conversion

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s conversion, by contrast, occurs offstage and through supernatural intervention. The secondary [[persons|characters]] report that he encountered "an old religious man" (021-024) at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge and was "conuerted / Both from his enterprise, and from the world" [021-025–026]. This deus ex machina resolution underscores a crucial difference between the two fraternal conflicts: the domestic conflict ([[oliver|Oliver]]/[[orlando|Orlando]]) can be resolved through natural means — mercy, courage, and the transformative power of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] — while the political conflict requires supernatural intervention. The implication is sobering: some forms of fraternal strife run too deep for human repair.

## Key Passages

### [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Opening Complaint

> "I know you are my eldest brother, and in the gentle condition of blood you should so know me." (001-019)

The play's opening conflict, stated with painful clarity. [[orlando|Orlando]] appeals to a natural order that [[oliver|Oliver]] has perverted — brotherhood as a "gentle condition of blood" that ought to guarantee mutual recognition and care. The word "should" carries the entire weight of violated expectation.

### The Wrestling Challenge

> "Come, come elder brother, you are too yong in this." (001-021)

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s defiant response to [[oliver|Oliver]]'s attempt to have him killed in the wrestling match. The line inverts the [[youth|age]] hierarchy — the "elder" is "too yong" in courage — and marks [[orlando|Orlando]]'s first step toward claiming the place that birth denied him.

### [[adam|Adam]]'s Brotherhood

> "a poore vnworthy brother of yours with idlenesse" (001-010)

[[adam|Adam]] uses fraternal language to describe himself to [[orlando|Orlando]], establishing the voluntary brotherhood that will sustain [[orlando|Orlando]] through his [[freedom|exile]]. The phrase is both self-deprecating ([[adam|Adam]] is "poore" and "vnworthy") and profoundly affirming (he IS a "brother" in the only sense that matters).

### [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s Brotherhood of Exile

> "Now my Coe-mates, and brothers in exile." (004-003)

[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] redeploys "brother" from a broken blood relation to a voluntary community. The [[green-world|forest]] creates new forms of brotherhood to replace those destroyed at court.

### The Bloody Brother

> "Of a diuerted blood, and bloudie brother." (006-039)

[[adam|Adam]]'s warning to [[orlando|Orlando]] about [[oliver|Oliver]]'s intentions uses "blood" in two senses: the diverted (perverted) blood of family feeling and the bloody (murderous) brother who has abandoned it. The phrase encapsulates how fraternal violence is simultaneously a perversion of nature and a recurring fact of it.

### [[oliver|Oliver]]'s Conversion

> "Finde out thy brother wheresoere he is." (011-006)

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] speaks these words to [[oliver|Oliver]], but the linguistic echo of the fraternal theme is unmistakable. The verb "finde out" replaces "destroy" — the same energy redirected toward healing. And [[oliver|Oliver]], who once sought his brother's death, now becomes the agent of his brother's recovery.

### [[orlando|Orlando]]'s Mercy

> "kindnesse, nobler euer then reuenge" (011-019)

[[oliver|Oliver]]'s description of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s mercy distills the play's ethical vision. "Kindnesse" — the word encompasses both benevolence and kinship — is nobler than revenge, not merely morally superior but aesthetically and socially higher. The play stakes its resolution on this claim.

## Structural Significance

The fraternal conflicts provide *As You Like It* with its primary dramatic engine. The play's plot can be mapped as a double arc of brotherhood:

1. **Violation** (Act 1): [[oliver|Oliver]] persecutes [[orlando|Orlando]] [001-002–018]; [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] has usurped [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] [001-041–042]
2. **Escalation** (Acts 2–3): [[orlando|Orlando]] flees to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]; [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] seizes [[oliver|Oliver]]'s lands and orders him to find [[orlando|Orlando]] [011-006–013]
3. **Convergence** (Act 4): Both pairs of brothers converge on [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]; [[orlando|Orlando]] saves [[oliver|Oliver]] from the lioness [011-017–021]
4. **Resolution** (Act 5): [[oliver|Oliver]] and [[orlando|Orlando]] are reconciled; [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] is converted offstage [021-024–027]; [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] is restored to his dukedom

This structure is symmetrical but not mechanical. The domestic reconciliation ([[oliver|Oliver]]/[[orlando|Orlando]]) is earned through dramatic action onstage; the political reconciliation ([[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]/[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]) is delivered by report, its mechanics invisible to the audience. The asymmetry suggests that Shakespeare was more interested in the psychology of fraternal repair than in its politics — or perhaps that he found the politics of reconciliation too difficult to dramatize convincingly.

## Related Characters

- **[[orlando]]** — The wronged younger brother whose journey from victim to victor drives the play's central plot. His mercy toward [[oliver|Oliver]] is the act that restores the fraternal bond.
- **[[oliver]]** — The persecuting older brother whose sudden reformation in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] embodies the play's belief in the possibility of moral change. His arc from villain to penitent mirrors the play's larger movement from conflict to harmony.
- **[[duke-senior]]** — The exiled older brother whose court was usurped by his younger sibling. His philosophical acceptance of [[freedom|exile]] contrasts with [[orlando|Orlando]]'s active resistance.
- **[[duke-frederick]]** — The usurping younger brother whose offstage conversion resolves the political strand of the fraternal conflict.
- **[[adam]]** — The old servant whose loyalty to [[orlando|Orlando]] embodies voluntary brotherhood in contrast to [[oliver|Oliver]]'s failed blood-brotherhood.
