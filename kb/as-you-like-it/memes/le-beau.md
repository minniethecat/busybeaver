# Le Beau

## Character Overview

Le Beau (French: "the handsome one") is a courtier in [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s court who appears exclusively in Act 1 Scene 2. His name is ironic -- he is a functionary, not a romantic lead -- but his dramatic function is substantial. As a go-between who bridges the gap between the court's brutality and the innocence of [[orlando|Orlando]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and [[celia|Celia]], Le Beau serves as both expositor and moral barometer.

Le Beau is introduced through [[celia|Celia]]'s derisive commentary: "since the little wit that fooles haue was silenced, the little foolerie that wise men haue makes a great shew; Heere comes Monsieur the Beu" (002-039). [[celia|Celia]] frames him as a court functionary full of trivial news, and indeed he arrives "With his mouth full of newes" (002-040). Yet beneath this superficial characterization, Le Beau proves to be one of the few court figures capable of independent moral judgment.

## The Wrestling Announcement

Le Beau's primary duty in the scene is to announce and narrate the [[wrestling-match|wrestling match]]. He describes [[charles|Charles the Wrestler]]'s brutal record in vivid detail:

> **Le Beau**: The eldest of the three, wrastled with [[charles|Charles]] the Dukes Wrastler, which Charles in a moment threw him, and broke three of his ribbes, that there is little hope of life in him: So he seru'd the second, and so the third: yonder they lie, the poore old man their Father, making such pittiful dole ouer them, that all the beholders take his part with weeping (002-065)

This speech serves crucial dramatic functions. It establishes [[charles|Charles]]'s fearsome reputation, raises the stakes for [[orlando|Orlando]]'s challenge, and reveals Le Beau's own sympathies -- his description of the "poore old man" and the weeping beholders betrays a humanity unusual among [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s courtiers.

## The Warning to Orlando

Le Beau's most morally significant action is his private warning to [[orlando|Orlando]] after the wrestling match. When [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] learns that [[orlando|Orlando]] is the son of [[sir-rowland-de-boys|Sir Rowland de Boys]] and leaves in anger, Le Beau counsels [[orlando|Orlando]] to flee:

> **Le Beau**: Good Sir, I do in friendship counsaile you / To leaue this place; Albeit you haue deseru'd / High commendation, true applause, and loue; / Yet such is now the Dukes condition, / That he misconsters all that you haue done: / The Duke is humorous, what he is indeede / More suites you to conceiue, then I to speake of (002-176)

This speech is remarkable for its courage. Le Beau risks [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s displeasure by warning a stranger and implicitly criticizing his own master. The diplomatic phrasing ("The Duke is humorous") is a thin veil over a genuine warning. Le Beau here demonstrates that even within a tyrannical court, individual conscience can operate.

## Dramatic Function

Le Beau serves four key dramatic functions:

1. **Exposition**: He narrates the wrestling match's backstory and introduces [[charles|Charles]]'s brutality before the audience sees it
2. **Foreshadowing**: His description of the weeping father prefigures the emotional stakes of [[orlando|Orlando]]'s challenge
3. **Moral Barometer**: His private warning to [[orlando|Orlando]] reveals that [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s court is not monolithic -- there are those within it who recognize its injustice
4. **Plot Bridge**: His warning enables [[orlando|Orlando]]'s escape, which in turn enables the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] plot

Le Beau also exemplifies the play's concern with appearance versus reality. His name ("the handsome one") and his role as a court gossip suggest superficiality, yet he proves capable of genuine moral action -- warning a stranger at potential cost to himself.
