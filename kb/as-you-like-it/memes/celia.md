# Celia

## Overview

Celia is the daughter of [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] and the devoted cousin of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]. When [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is banished, Celia chooses to accompany her into [[freedom|exile]] in [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], rejecting her inheritance and her father's court. Adopting the alias [[aliena|Aliena]] ("the estranged one"), she becomes [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s companion and confidante through the pastoral adventures that follow. Her sudden love for [[oliver|Oliver]], the reformed villain, provides the play's fourth and most surprising marriage. Celia embodies the virtue of freely chosen loyalty — her love for [[rosalind|Rosalind]] exceeds the claims of blood, property, and safety.

## Role in the Play

[[celia|Celia]]'s first significant action defines her character: when [[rosalind|Rosalind]] grieves her father's [[freedom|exile]], Celia offers her entire inheritance as recompense: "thou shalt be his heire; for what hee hath taken away from thy father perforce, I will render thee againe in affection" (002-006). This extravagant gesture establishes Celia as the play's embodiment of generous love.

She participates in the [[fortune|Fortune]]-[[nature|Nature]] debate with sharp intelligence. Her proposal to "mocke the good houswife [[fortune|Fortune]]" (002-009) initiates the play's most explicit philosophical discussion, and her observation that [[nature|Nature]] has sent [[touchstone|Touchstone]] as a "whetstone" for their wits (002-019) shows her capacity for meta-commentary on the play's own mechanisms.

[[celia|Celia]]'s decision to flee with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — "To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]" (003-097) — transforms her from passive witness to active agent. She becomes [[aliena|Aliena]], the stranger, but her identity remains defined by loyalty.

In [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]], Celia serves as [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s confidante, audience, and occasional critic. Her wry aside — "I would sing my song without a burthen, thou bring'st me out of tune" (012-099) — reveals the affectionate exasperation that balances [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s exuberance.

[[celia|Celia]]'s romance with [[oliver|Oliver]] is the play's most compressed love plot: they meet, fall in love, and marry within a single scene. Her confession that her "affection hath an vnknowne bottome" (016-091) — spoken by [[rosalind|Rosalind]] but witnessed by Celia — finds its echo in Celia's own sudden, deep love for [[oliver|Oliver]].

## Key Scenes

### The Generous Offer (Act 1, Scene 2)

Celia offers her inheritance to console [[rosalind|Rosalind]] for her [[freedom|exile]]:

> Cel. You know my Father hath no childe, but I, nor none is like to haue; and truely when he dies, thou shalt be his heire; for what hee hath taken away from thy father perforce, I will render thee againe in affection (002-006)

### The Fortune-Nature Debate (Act 1, Scene 2)

Celia initiates the philosophical discussion that structures the play's central concerns:

> Cel. Let vs sit and mocke the good houswife Fortune (002-009)

> Cel. Peraduenture this is not Fortunes work neither, but Natures (002-019)

### Voluntary Exile (Act 1, Scene 3)

> Cel. To seeke my Vncle in the Forrest of Arden (003-097)

### The Song Without Burden (Act 3, Scene 2)

> Cel. I would sing my song without a burthen, thou bring'st me out of tune (012-099)

### Love for Oliver (Act 4, Scene 3 / Act 5, Scene 4)

Celia falls suddenly and deeply in love with the reformed [[oliver|Oliver]] — the play's most compressed romantic transformation.

## Relationships

- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — Her "coz" and soulmate; [[celia|Celia]]'s loyalty to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is the play's deepest bond
- [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] — Her father, whose court she abandons for love of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]
- [[oliver|Oliver]] — Sudden romantic love; they marry in the final scene
- [[touchstone|Touchstone]] — She brings him to [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] as companion; values his [[wit]]
