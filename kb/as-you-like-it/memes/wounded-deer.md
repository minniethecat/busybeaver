# The Wounded Deer

The wounded deer — "the poor sequestered stag" — is one of the most memorable and frequently cited images in *As You Like It*. Reported to [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and his lords by a First Lord in Act 2, Scene 1, the weeping deer becomes the occasion for [[jaques|Jaques]]'s extended moralization on the cruelty of the natural world and the human capacity for suffering.

## The Stricken Stag

The First Lord reports finding Jaques lying beside a brook, watching a wounded stag that had been shot by hunters:

> "A poor sequestered stag / That from the hunter's aim had ta'en a hurt / Did come to languish."

The stag's suffering is described in terms that blur the boundary between animal and human. The deer weeps "big round tears" that course down its "innocent nose," and the "melancholy" Jaques reads the stag's pain as a commentary on the world:

> "Thus most invectively he pierceth through / The body of the country, city, court, / Yea, and of this our life."

## Jaques's Moralization

Jaques transforms the wounded deer into an emblem of universal suffering. He observes that the deer is abandoned by its companions — "the velvet friends" — who continue grazing without concern for their wounded fellow. From this, Jaques extrapolates a critique of human society:

> "Sweeping it into a needless simile he makes the stricken deer weep, and moralizes the spectacle into a thousand similes."

The First Lord's language hints at both the power and the excess of Jaques's method. The moralization is "needless" — perhaps self-indulgent — yet it generates a "thousand similes," a productive overflow of meaning that makes the deer one of the play's richest symbols.

## The Deer and the Forest

The wounded deer complicates the pastoral ideal of the Forest of Arden. If the forest is a green world of renewal and transformation, it is also a place of real violence — hunting, hunger, and predation. The deer's suffering is not allegorical but literal, the consequence of [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s party hunting for food.

Duke Senior himself acknowledges this ambivalence earlier in the same scene:

> "Come, shall we go and kill us venison? / And yet it irks me the poor dappled fools, / Being native burghers of this desert city, / Should, in their own confines, with forked heads / Have their round haunches gored."

The deer are "native burghers" — citizens of the forest — and the exiled courtiers are intruders who kill them for sustenance. The wounded deer motif exposes the ethical complexity beneath the pastoral surface.

## Connection to Jaques's Character

The wounded deer is the emblem of Jaques's distinctive melancholy. Unlike the productive melancholy of scholarly contemplation or the transient sadness of disappointed love, Jaques's melancholy feeds on suffering — his own and others':

> "I do not desire you to please me; I do desire you to sing. I can suck melancholy out of a song as a weasel sucks eggs."

Jaques identifies with the wounded deer — isolated, suffering, abandoned by companions — and his identification is both the source of his insight and the limit of his human sympathy. He is moved by the deer but does nothing to help it; he moralizes suffering rather than alleviating it.

## The Deer and the Play's Structure

The wounded deer appears early in the forest sequence (Act 2, Scene 1) and establishes a tone of reflective melancholy that persists alongside the play's comic energies. It provides the philosophical gravity that distinguishes *As You Like It* from pure festive comedy and prepares for Jaques's refusal of the happy ending in Act 5 — his choice to seek out the converted [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] rather than join the celebratory return to court.

The deer is also connected, through the imagery of hunting and wounding, to the [[the-lion-incident|lion incident]] in Act 4 — where [[orlando|Orlando]] is physically wounded saving [[oliver|Oliver]] — and to the broader pattern of suffering and redemption that structures the play's action.
