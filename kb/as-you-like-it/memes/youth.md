## Overview

Youth and age form a structuring opposition in *As You Like It* — not as binary poles but as a spectrum of performed identities examined from multiple perspectives across the play's two worlds. In the court, youth is rebellion against an older generation's constraints; in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], youth and age coexist in a pastoral economy where each generation has its place. The play's most famous articulation of this dialectic, [[jaques|Jaques]]' "All the world's a stage" speech (010-147–010-174), charts human life through [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] from "the Infant / Mewling, and puking in the Nurses armes" (010-152) to "second childishnesse, and meere obliuion" (010-173). But the play does not let [[jaques|Jaques]] have the last word: the speech is immediately followed by the entrance of the aged [[adam|Adam]], whose lived experience contradicts the [[melancholy|melancholic]]'s reductive taxonomy.

## In As You Like It

### Youth as Assertion and Rebellion

The play's opening scenes establish youth as a position of disinheritance and a motive for self-assertion. [[orlando|Orlando]] de Boys, the youngest son of Sir Rowland, has been denied the education and treatment due his birth by his elder brother [[oliver|Oliver]]. His rebellion is framed in explicitly generational terms:

> "the spirit of my father growes strong in mee, and I will no longer endure it" (001-025)

The father's spirit flowing into the son links youth to a moral inheritance — [[orlando|Orlando]] is young, but his grievance is not juvenile petulance; it is his father's legacy demanding acknowledgment. The generational conflict between the de Boys brothers mirrors the larger political framework: the [[duke-senior|old Duke]] has been usurped by his younger brother [[duke-frederick|Frederick]], and as [[charles|Charles]] explains, "many yong Gentlemen flocke to him euery day, and fleet the time carelesly as they did in the [[green-world|golden world]]" (001-043). [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] itself becomes a site where youth is drawn to the [[duke-senior|old Duke]]'s pastoral alternative, reversing [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s inversion of generational order.

### The Old Man Who Refutes the Pantaloon

[[adam|Adam]] is the play's most sustained counter-argument to [[jaques|Jaques]]' portrait of senescence. Where [[jaques|Jaques]] imagines old age as "the leane and slipper'd Pantaloone" with "his youthfull hose well sau'd, a world too wide, / For his shrunke shanke" (010-168–010-169), [[adam|Adam]] demonstrates the opposite: age allied to strength, loyalty, and self-sacrifice. He offers his life savings to [[orlando|Orlando]], declaring:

> "Take that, and he that doth the Rauens feede, / Yea prouidently caters for the Sparrow, / Be comfort to my age: here is the gold, / All this I giue you, let me be your seruant" (006-040–006-048)

[[adam|Adam]] frames his age as "a lustie winter, / Frostie, but kindely" (006-054–006-055) — the oxymoron "lustie winter" directly challenges the pantaloon stereotype. Winter is not decay but stored vitality. His earlier rebuke to [[oliver|Oliver]] — "God be with my olde master, he would not haue spoke such a word" (001-029) — establishes age as the bearer of moral memory, the standard against which the younger generation's degeneracy is measured. [[adam|Adam]], at "almost fourescore" (006-073), proves that old age can be a season of active virtue rather than passive decline.

The play stages the confrontation between [[jaques|Jaques]]' theory and [[adam|Adam]]'s reality with dramatic precision. [[adam|Adam]] enters "opprest with two weake euils, age, and hunger" (010-138) immediately after the Seven Ages speech concludes. His presence — "an old poore man" (010-135) carried on [[orlando|Orlando]]'s back — is embodied counter-evidence. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] greets him as "venerable burthen" (010-176), a phrase that dignifies what [[jaques|Jaques]]' scheme would dismiss.

### Youth as Performed Identity

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s disguise as the "prettie youth" [[ganimed|Ganymede]] (012-160) literalises the play's concern with youth as a performed identity rather than a biological category. [[orlando|Orlando]] addresses her as "Faire youth" (012-175), "youth" (012-176), and "good youth" (012-189); [[phebe|Phebe]] calls her "Sweet youth" (015-068) and describes her as "a pretty youth, not very prettie" (015-119); even [[jaques|Jaques]] greets her with "I prethee, pretty youth, let me better acquainted with thee" (016-002). The word "youth" is applied to [[rosalind|Rosalind]]-[[ganimed|Ganymede]] more frequently than to any other character, collapsing gender and age into a single theatrical category.

This layering of performance reaches its apex in the epilogue, when the boy actor playing [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — who has been playing a girl playing a youth — speaks directly to the audience. The chain of performed identities (boy-as-girl, girl-as-boy) makes youth itself a costume, a part that can be put on and taken off. This aligns with [[jaques|Jaques]]' theatrical metaphor but carries an implication [[jaques|Jaques]] avoids: if youth is a part one plays, then age is likewise a role, not a sentence. [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s fluid identity offers a comic alternative to [[jaques|Jaques]]' tragic determinism.

### Generational Dialogue in Arden

The [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] brings young and old into dialogue. The first encounter [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]] witness there is "a yong man and an old in solemne talke" (007-015) — [[silvius|Silvius]] and [[corin|Corin]]. Their exchange dramatises the generational gap directly:

> Sil. No Corin, being old, thou canst not guesse, / Though in thy youth thou wast as true a louer / As euer sigh'd vpon a midnight pillow (007-020–007-021)

The young lover insists that age has erased the memory of passion; the old shepherd replies with gentle irony. The play does not resolve this dispute — it lets both positions stand, suggesting that the relationship between youth and age is not one of knowledge (the old know better) nor of intensity (the young feel more), but of perspective, each incomplete without the other.

### The Youthful Lover and the Young Maid

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s meditation on time's diverse paces in Act 3 uses youth as its primary example:

> "Marry he trots hard with a yong maid, between the contract of her marriage, and the day it is solemnizd: if the interim be but a sennight, Times pace is so hard, that it seemes the length of seuen yeare" (012-150)

The young maid's impatience for marriage is a comic instance of youth's distorted relationship to time. [[phebe|Phebe]]'s rejection of [[silvius|Silvius]] — "Youth, you haue done me much vngentlenesse" (020-025) — deploys "youth" as a term of dismissal, a way of belittling a lover she finds beneath her. But [[phebe|Phebe]] herself falls for the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]], proving that youth's follies are not confined to the young.

## Key Passages

### The Spirit of the Father (Act 1, Scene 1)

> "the spirit of my father growes strong in mee, and I will no longer endure it" (001-025)

[[orlando|Orlando]]'s declaration of generational rebellion. The father's spirit passing into the son frames youth as moral inheritance rather than mere immaturity. This is the play's first assertion of youth as a positive force.

### Unregarded Age (Act 2, Scene 3)

> "And vnregarded age in corners throwne" (006-044)

[[adam|Adam]]'s description of what awaits the old servant who lacks means. The line is a compact indictment of a world that discards the aged, and it motivates his extraordinary act of self-sacrifice: giving his savings to [[orlando|Orlando]] rather than keeping them for his own "foster Nurse."

### A Lusty Winter (Act 2, Scene 3)

> "Therefore my age is as a lustie winter, / Frostie, but kindely" (006-054–006-055)

The oxymoron that defines [[adam|Adam]]'s self-understanding. Winter, in the pastoral imagination, is the season of death; "lustie winter" reclaims it as a season of life preserved. "Kindely" carries the double sense of "benevolent" and "according to nature" — age is natural, and therefore good.

### The Pantaloon (Act 2, Scene 7)

> "Into the leane and slipper'd Pantaloone, / With spectacles on nose, and pouch on side, / His youthfull hose well sau'd, a world too wide, / For his shrunke shanke" (010-166–010-169)

[[jaques|Jaques]]' portrait of the sixth age is the speech's sharpest satire. The pantaloon preserves the clothing of youth only to discover his body has shrunk away from it. The image is cruel and comic — the gap between garment and limb, between past and present self, is a visual joke that is also a memento mori.

### Oppressed with Age and Hunger (Act 2, Scene 7)

> "Opprest with two weake euils, age, and hunger" (010-138)

[[orlando|Orlando]] describes [[adam|Adam]]'s condition as he goes to fetch him. The line's pathos derives from its understatement — "weake euils" — and its pairing of two different kinds of suffering: the chronic condition of age and the acute crisis of starvation. The body of the old man becomes the site where the play's comic and tragic registers meet.

### A Youth of Many Names (Act 3, Scene 2)

> "Where dwel you prettie youth?" (012-160)

> "Faire youth, I would I could make thee beleeue I Loue" (012-175)

> "I would not be cured, youth" (012-181)

> "With all my heart, good youth" (012-189)

The fourfold address of [[orlando|Orlando]] to the disguised [[rosalind|Rosalind]] within a single scene. Each iteration of "youth" is a different performance: inquiry, confession, resistance, acquiescence. The word becomes a mask that [[orlando|Orlando]] cannot see past — and which the audience delights in seeing through.

### Youth as Dismissal (Act 5, Scene 2)

> "Youth, you haue done me much vngentlenesse" (020-025)

[[phebe|Phebe]] deploying "youth" as a term of social dismissal, as though [[silvius|Silvius]]'s age were itself an offence against her. The irony is that [[phebe|Phebe]] herself is young, and her own romantic judgment proves no wiser than his.

## Related Characters

- **[[adam|Adam]]** — The aged servant whose loyalty refutes [[jaques|Jaques]]' pantaloon. He embodies the virtue possible in old age: sacrifice, dignity, and moral memory. His "lustie winter" (006-054) is the play's affirmative vision of senescence.
- **[[jaques|Jaques]]** — The melancholy philosopher who anatomises the [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] from a position of false detachment. His speech is the play's most eloquent statement on youth and age, but his refusal to participate in life exposes the limits of the diagnostic temperament.
- **[[rosalind|Rosalind]]** — The play's supreme performer of youth. Her disguise as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] makes "youth" a theatrical category, exposing the gap between biological age and performed identity. Her [[wit]] exposes the follies of both young lovers and old melancholics.
- **[[orlando|Orlando]]** — The young rebel whose assertion of his father's spirit drives the plot. His relationship with [[adam|Adam]] dramatises the ideal bond between youth and age — protective, reciprocal, rooted in service.
- **[[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]** — The deposed [[duke-senior|old Duke]] whose pastoral court in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] reverses [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s generational disorder. His observation that the world is a "wide and vniuersall Theater" (010-144) prompts [[jaques|Jaques]]' speech, and his hospitality to [[adam|Adam]] provides the living counterexample.
- **[[touchstone|Touchstone]]** — The fool whose meditation on time — "from houre to houre, we ripe, and ripe" (010-028) — is the comic prelude to [[jaques|Jaques]]' tragic vision, and whose mockery of [[william|William]]'s age — "A ripe age" (019-018) — treats the life-cycle as material for [[wit]].
