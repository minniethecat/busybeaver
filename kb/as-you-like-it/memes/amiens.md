# Amiens

## Overview

Amiens is an attendant lord to the exiled [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and the musical voice of [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]. He performs the play's most memorable [[song-and-music|songs]]: "Under the Greenwood Tree" (008-003), which offers [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] as refuge from human enmity, and "Blow, Blow, Thou Winter Wind" (010-185), which contrasts the honest harshness of nature with the hypocrisy of human ingratitude. His patient [[patience|endurance]] of [[jaques|Jaques]]'s demands for "More, more, I prethee more" (008-006) reveals a gentle temperament that embodies the pastoral ideal of harmonious fellowship.

## Role in the Play

Amiens serves as [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s musician, providing the [[song-and-music|songs]] that articulate the play's pastoral philosophy. His first song establishes the terms of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s invitation: "Come hither, come hither, come hither: Heere shall he see no enemie, But Winter and rough weather" (008-003).

His second song, performed at the Duke's request, shifts from invitation to moral commentary. "Blow, blow, thou winter winde, Thou art not so vnkinde, as mans ingratitude" (010-185) delivers the play's most explicit statement on the theme of ingratitude — the sin that [[oliver|Oliver]], [[duke-frederick|Frederick]], and the court embody. Amiens thus functions as both entertainer and moral voice, his [[song-and-music|songs]] carrying the weight of the play's thematic concerns.

His dynamic with [[jaques|Jaques]] — who demands more [[song-and-music|singing]] while simultaneously mocking it — illustrates the tension between melancholy and [[joy-and-celebration|mirth]] that defines the play's tone.
