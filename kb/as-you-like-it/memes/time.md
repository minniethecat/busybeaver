# Time

## Definition

Time in As You Like It is treated not as a uniform measure but as a subjective experience whose pace varies with human condition and temperament. Two major set-piece speeches — [[rosalind|Rosalind's]] meditation on Time's diverse paces (012-149) (012-150) and [[jaques|Jaques's]] Seven Ages of Man (010-149) (010-151) — present complementary visions: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] maps time onto social roles (maid, priest, lawyer, thief), while [[jaques|Jaques]] maps it onto life stages from infancy to second childhood.

|-------------|---------|-----------|------|
| Time's diverse paces | [[rosalind|Rosalind]] | Social roles | Variable by person |

## In As You Like It

[[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s discourse on time emerges in conversation with [[orlando|Orlando]] in Act 3, Scene 2. When [[orlando|Orlando]] suggests time should be measured by its "swift foote," [[rosalind|Rosalind]] counters that "Time trauels in diuers paces, with diuers persons" (012-149). She then catalogues time's varying speeds: it trots with a young maid between contract and wedding (012-150), ambles with a priest that lacks Latin and a rich man without the gout (012-151), gallops with a thief to the gallows (012-153), and stands still with lawyers on vacation (012-157).

[[jaques|Jaques]]'s Seven Ages speech, delivered earlier in Act 2, Scene 7, maps the human lifespan onto the theater metaphor: "All the world's a stage, And all the men and women, meerely Players; They haue their Exits and their Entrances, And one man in his time playes many parts" (010-149) with "His Acts being seuen ages" (010-151). The [[seven-ages-of-man|seven ages]] — infant, schoolboy, lover, soldier, justice, pantaloon, and second childhood — trace a complete temporal arc from birth to oblivion.

Time also governs the play's romantic mechanics. [[rosalind|Rosalind]] demands that [[orlando|Orlando]] keep his appointments punctually: "Breake an houres promise in [[love|loue]]?" (016-017). A lover's [[loyalty-and-service|fidelity]] is measured by his relationship to time. [[touchstone|Touchstone]], contrastingly, dismisses time-wasting: "I count it but time lost to heare such a foolish song" (021-036).

The play's resolution compresses time: [[the-four-weddings|four weddings]] in a single ceremony, the [[regeneration|restoration]] of a dukedom, and a sudden religious conversion all occur within the same [[green-world|forest]] day in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]].

## Key Passages

### Time's Diverse Paces (Act 3, Scene 2)

> Time trauels in diuers paces, with diuers persons: Ile tel you who Time ambles withall, who Time trots withall, who Time gallops withall, and who he stands still withall. (012-149)

> Marry he trots hard with a yong maid, between the contract of her marriage, and the day it is solemnizd: if the interim be but a sennight, Times pace is so hard, that it seemes the length of seuen yeere. (012-150)

### Seven Ages of Man (Act 2, Scene 7)

> All the world's a stage, And all the men and women, meerely Players; They haue their Exits and their Entrances, And one man in his time playes many parts (010-149)

> His Acts being seuen ages. (010-151)

### A Lover's Punctuality (Act 4, Scene 1)

> Breake an houres promise in loue? hee that will diuide a minute into a thousand parts, and breake but a part of the thousand part of a minute in the affairs of loue, it may be said of him that Cupid hath clapt him oth' shoulder (016-017)

### Fool's Impatience (Act 5, Scene 3)

> I count it but time lost to heare such a foolish song (021-036)
