## Definition

In Elizabethan psychology, melancholy was one of the four humors — associated with black bile, earth, and the temperament of contemplative sadness. In As You Like It, melancholy is personified by [[jaques|Jaques]], whose self-fashioned identity as "[[jaques|the melancholy Jaques]]" makes him the play's most distinctive outsider. Unlike the foolish lovers whose sadness is circumstantial, [[jaques|Jaques]] cultivates his melancholy as a philosophical stance — a way of seeing the world that resists the comic imperative toward happiness and resolution.

## In As You Like It

[[jaques|Jaques]] is introduced through reports of his melancholy disposition before he ever appears on stage. The First Lord describes him brooding over a wounded deer:

> The melancholy Iaques grieues at that (004-029)

This early report establishes [[jaques|Jaques]] as a figure who finds sorrow where others see nature's ordinary course. His melancholy is performative — he seeks out occasions for sadness. [[amiens|Amiens]] later cautions against engaging with him:

> It will make you melancholly Monsieur Iaques (008-005)

And [[jaques|Jaques]] himself declares his sensitivity:

> I can sucke melancholly out of a song, as a Weasel suckes egges (008-007)

The definitive expression of [[jaques|Jaques]]' melancholy comes in his famous speech to [[rosalind|Rosalind]], where he distinguishes his condition from all conventional forms of the humor:

> I haue neither the Schollers melancholy, which is emulation: nor the Musitians, which is fantasticall; nor the Courtiers, which is proud ... but it is a melancholy of mine owne, compounded of many simples, extracted from many obiects, and indeed the sundrie contemplation of my trauells, in which by often rumination, wraps me in a most humorous sadnesse. (015-052)

This "melancholy of mine owne" is [[jaques|Jaques]]' claim to uniqueness — a sadness that defies categorization and elevates him above the ordinary, boring melancholy of scholars, musicians, and lovers. At the play's end, [[jaques|Jaques]]' melancholy leads him to refuse participation in the wedding celebrations, instead joining the reformed [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]] in religious retreat — the one character whose emotional trajectory resists comic resolution.
