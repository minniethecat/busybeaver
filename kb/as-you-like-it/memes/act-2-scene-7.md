(010-001)Enter Duke Sen. & Lord, like Out-lawes.

(010-002)Du.Sen. I thinke he be transform'd into a beast,

(010-003)For I can no where finde him, like a man

(010-004)1. Lord. My Lord, he is but euen now gone hence,

(010-005)Heere was he merry, hearing of a Song

(010-006)Du.Sen. If he compact of iarres, grow Musicall,

(010-007)We shall haue shortly discord in the Spheares:

(010-008)Go seeke him, tell him I would speake with him.

(010-009)Enter [[jaques|Iaques]].

(010-010)1. Lord. He saues my labor by his owne approach

(010-011)Du.Sen. Why how now Monsieur, what a life is this

(010-012)That your poore friends must woe your companie,

(010-013)What, you looke merrily

(010-014)Iaq. A Foole, a foole: I met a foole i'th Forrest,

(010-015)A motley Foole (a miserable world:)

(010-016)As I do liue by foode, I met a foole,

(010-017)Who laid him downe, and bask'd him in the Sun,

(010-018)And rail'd on [[fortune|Lady Fortune]] in good termes,

(010-019)In good set termes, and yet a motley foole.

(010-020)Good morrow foole (quoth I:) no Sir, quoth he,

(010-021)Call me not foole, till heauen hath sent me fortune,

(010-022)And then he drew a diall from his poake,

(010-023)And looking on it, with lacke-lustre eye,

(010-024)Sayes, very wisely, it is ten a clocke:

(010-025)Thus we may see (quoth he) how the world wagges:

(010-026)'Tis but an houre agoe, since it was nine,

(010-027)And after one houre more, 'twill be eleuen,

(010-028)And so from houre to houre, we ripe, and ripe,

(010-029)And then from houre to houre, we rot, and rot,

(010-030)And thereby hangs a tale. When I did heare

(010-031)The motley Foole, thus morall on the time,

(010-032)My Lungs began to crow like Chanticleere,

(010-033)That Fooles should be so deepe contemplatiue:

(010-034)And I did laugh, sans intermission

(010-035)An houre by his diall. Oh noble foole,

(010-036)A worthy foole: Motley's the onely weare

(010-037)Du.Sen. What foole is this?

(010-038)Iaq. O worthie Foole: One that hath bin a Courtier

(010-039)And sayes, if Ladies be but yong, and faire,

(010-040)They haue the gift to know it: and in his braine,

(010-041)Which is as drie as the remainder bisket

(010-042)After a voyage: He hath strange places cram'd

(010-043)With obseruation, the which he vents

(010-044)In mangled formes. O that I were a foole,

(010-045)I am ambitious for a motley coat

(010-046)Du.Sen. Thou shalt haue one

(010-047)Iaq. It is my onely suite,

(010-048)Prouided that you weed your better iudgements

(010-049)Of all opinion that growes ranke in them,

(010-050)That I am wise. I must haue [[freedom|liberty]]

(010-051)Withall, as large a Charter as the winde,

(010-052)To blow on whom I please, for so fooles haue:

(010-053)And they that are most gauled with my folly,

(010-054)They most must laugh: And why sir must they so?

(010-055)The why is plaine, as way to Parish Church:

(010-056)Hee, that a Foole doth very wisely hit,

(010-057)Doth very foolishly, although he smart

(010-058)Seeme senselesse of the bob. If not,

(010-059)The Wise-mans folly is anathomiz'd

(010-060)Euen by the squandring glances of the foole.

(010-061)Inuest me in my motley: Giue me leaue

(010-062)To speake my minde, and I will through and through

(010-063)Cleanse the foule bodie of th' infected world,

(010-064)If they will patiently receiue my medicine

(010-065)Du.Sen. Fie on thee. I can tell what thou wouldst do

(010-066)Iaq. What, for a Counter, would I do, but good?

(010-067)Du.Sen. Most mischeeuous foule sin, in chiding sin:

(010-068)For thou thy selfe hast bene a Libertine,

(010-069)As sensuall as the brutish sting it selfe,

(010-070)And all th' imbossed sores, and headed euils,

(010-071)That thou with license of free foot hast caught,

(010-072)Would'st thou disgorge into the generall world

(010-073)Iaq. Why who cries out on pride,

(010-074)That can therein taxe any priuate party:

(010-075)Doth it not flow as hugely as the Sea,

(010-076)Till that the wearie verie meanes do ebbe.

(010-077)What woman in the Citie do I name,

(010-078)When that I say the City woman beares

(010-079)The cost of Princes on vnworthy shoulders?

(010-080)Who can come in, and say that I meane her,

(010-081)When such a one as shee, such is her neighbor?

(010-082)Or what is he of basest function,

(010-083)That sayes his brauerie is not on my cost,

(010-084)Thinking that I meane him, but therein suites

(010-085)His folly to the mettle of my speech,

(010-086)There then, how then, what then, let me see wherein

(010-087)My tongue hath wrong'd him: if it do him right,

(010-088)Then he hath wrong'd himselfe: if he be free,

(010-089)Why then my taxing like a wild-goose flies

(010-090)Vnclaim'd of any man. But who come here?

(010-091)Enter [[orlando|Orlando]].

(010-092)Orl. Forbeare, and eate no more

(010-093)Iaq. Why I haue eate none yet

(010-094)Orl. Nor shalt not, till necessity be seru'd

(010-095)Iaq. Of what kinde should this Cocke come of?

(010-096)Du.Sen. Art thou thus bolden'd man by thy distres?

(010-097)Or else a rude despiser of good manners,

(010-098)That in ciuility thou seem'st so emptie?

(010-099)Orl. You touch'd my veine at first, the thorny point

(010-100)Of bare distresse, hath tane from me the shew

(010-101)Of smooth ciuility: yet am I in-land bred,

(010-102)And know some nourture: But forbeare, I say,

(010-103)He dies that touches any of this fruite,

(010-104)Till I, and my affaires are answered

(010-105)Iaq. And you will not be answer'd with reason,

(010-106)I must dye

(010-107)Du.Sen. What would you haue?

(010-108)Your gentlenesse shall force, more then your force

(010-109)Moue vs to gentlenesse

(010-110)Orl. I almost die for food, and let me haue it

(010-111)Du.Sen. Sit downe and feed, & welcom to our table

(010-112)Orl. Speake you so gently? [[forgiveness|Pardon]] me I pray you,

(010-113)I thought that all things had bin sauage heere,

(010-114)And therefore put I on the countenance

(010-115)Of sterne command'ment. But what ere you are

(010-116)That in this desert inaccessible,

(010-117)Vnder the shade of melancholly boughes,

(010-118)Loose, and neglect the creeping houres of time:

(010-119)If euer you haue look'd on better dayes:

(010-120)If euer beene where bels haue knoll'd to Church:

(010-121)If euer sate at any good mans feast:

(010-122)If euer from your eye-lids wip'd a teare,

(010-123)And know what 'tis to pittie, and be pittied:

(010-124)Let gentlenesse my strong enforcement be,

(010-125)In the which hope, I blush, and hide my Sword

(010-126)Du.Sen. True is it, that we haue seene better dayes,

(010-127)And haue with holy bell bin knowld to Church,

(010-128)And sat at good mens feasts, and wip'd our eies

(010-129)Of drops, that sacred pity hath engendred:

(010-130)And therefore sit you downe in gentlenesse,

(010-131)And take vpon command, what helpe we haue

(010-132)That to your wanting may be ministred

(010-133)Orl. Then but forbeare your food a little while:

(010-134)Whiles (like a Doe) I go to finde my Fawne,

(010-135)And giue it food. There is an old poore man,

(010-136)Who after me, hath many a weary steppe

(010-137)Limpt in pure [[love|loue]]: till he be first suffic'd,

(010-138)Opprest with two weake euils, [[youth|age]], and hunger,

(010-139)I will not touch a bit

(010-140)Duke Sen. Go finde him out,

(010-141)And we will nothing waste till you returne

(010-142)Orl. I thanke ye, and be blest for your good comfort

(010-143)Du.Sen. Thou seest, we are not all alone vnhappie:

(010-144)This wide and vniuersall Theater

(010-145)Presents more wofull Pageants then the Sceane

(010-146)Wherein we play in

(010-147)Ia. All the world's a stage,

(010-148)And all the men and women, meerely Players;

(010-149)They haue their Exits and their Entrances,

(010-150)And one man in his time playes many parts,

(010-151)His Acts being seuen ages. At first the Infant,

(010-152)Mewling, and puking in the Nurses armes:

(010-153)Then, the whining Schoole-boy with his Satchell

(010-154)And shining morning face, creeping like snaile

(010-155)Vnwillingly to schoole. And then the Louer,

(010-156)Sighing like Furnace, with a wofull ballad

(010-157)Made to his Mistresse eye-brow. Then, a Soldier,

(010-158)Full of strange oaths, and bearded like the Pard,

(010-159)Ielous in [[honor]], sodaine, and quicke in quarrell,

(010-160)Seeking the bubble Reputation

(010-161)Euen in the Canons mouth: And then, the Iustice

(010-162)In faire round belly, with good Capon lin'd,

(010-163)With eyes seuere, and beard of formall cut,

(010-164)Full of wise sawes, and moderne instances,

(010-165)And so he playes his part. The sixt age shifts

(010-166)Into the leane and slipper'd Pantaloone,

(010-167)With spectacles on nose, and pouch on side,

(010-168)His youthfull hose well sau'd, a world too wide,

(010-169)For his shrunke shanke, and his bigge manly voice,

(010-170)Turning againe toward childish trebble pipes,

(010-171)And whistles in his sound. Last Scene of all,

(010-172)That ends this strange euentfull historie,

(010-173)Is second childishnesse, and meere obliuion,

(010-174)Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans euery thing.

(010-175)Enter Orlando with [[adam|Adam]].

(010-176)Du.Sen. Welcome: set downe your venerable burthen,

(010-177)and let him feede

(010-178)Orl. I thanke you most for him

(010-179)Ad. So had you neede,

(010-180)I scarce can speake to thanke you for my selfe

(010-181)Du.Sen. Welcome, fall too: I wil not trouble you,

(010-182)As yet to question you about your fortunes:

(010-183)Giue vs some Musicke, and good Cozen, sing.

(010-184)Song.

(010-185)Blow, blow, thou winter winde,

(010-186)Thou art not so vnkinde, as mans ingratitude

(010-187)Thy tooth is not so keene, because thou art not seene,

(010-188)although thy breath be rude.

(010-189)Heigh ho, sing heigh ho, vnto the greene holly,

(010-190)Most frendship, is fayning; most Louing, meere folly:

(010-191)The heigh ho, the holly,

(010-192)This Life is most iolly.

(010-193)Freize, freize, thou bitter skie that dost not bight so nigh

(010-194)as benefitts forgot:

(010-195)Though thou the waters warpe, thy sting is not so sharpe,

(010-196)as freind remembred not.

(010-197)Heigh ho, sing, &c

(010-198)Duke Sen. If that you were the good Sir Rowlands son,

(010-199)As you haue whisper'd faithfully you were,

(010-200)And as mine eye doth his effigies witnesse,

(010-201)Most truly limn'd, and liuing in your face,

(010-202)Be truly welcome hither: I am the Duke

(010-203)That lou'd your Father, the residue of your fortune,

(010-204)Go to my Caue, and tell mee. Good old man,

(010-205)Thou art right welcome, as thy masters is:

(010-206)Support him by the arme: giue me your hand,

(010-207)And let me all your fortunes vnderstand.

(010-208)Exeunt.
