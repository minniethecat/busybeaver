## Definition

Speech in As You Like It is never merely communication. The tongue is figured as a weapon, a musical instrument, a tool of courtship, and a marker of social identity. From the opening scenes, [[persons|characters]] define themselves through how they speak: [[touchstone|Touchstone]] through parodic [[wit]], [[rosalind|Rosalind]] through playful intelligence, [[jaques|Jaques]] through melancholic moralizing, and [[corin|Corin]] through plain rustic wisdom. The [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] becomes a space of linguistic experimentation, where the constraints on speech that operate at [[duke-fredericks-court|court]] are loosened, and [[persons|characters]] discover new voices — for better and worse.

## In As You Like It

The play's sensitivity to language is established in its very first scene, where [[orlando|Orlando]] complains that his brother has kept him "rustically at home" and denied him education in "tongues" (001-002). Speech is immediately politicized: the [[duke-fredericks-court|court]] is a place where tongues are dangerous. When [[orlando|Orlando]] challenges [[charles|Charles]], the wrestler reports that he was warned "this other had puld out thy tongue for saying so" (001-022) — a literal threat of silencing that establishes speech as a high-stakes activity.

In the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]], the constraints on speech are transformed. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s famous speech claims to find "tongues in trees, bookes in the running brookes" (004-018) — nature itself becomes a source of language, replacing [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]]'s dangerous discourse with pastoral eloquence. Yet [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] is no utopia of free speech. [[jaques|Jaques]] laments those "that wil not hold their tongues" (008-018), and his own loquacious melancholy is repeatedly mocked by other [[persons|characters]].

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] is the play's most accomplished speaker, and her verbal dexterity — as herself and as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] — drives much of the comedy. [[celia|Celia]]'s exasperated "Cry holla, to the tongue, I prethee: it curuettes" (012-096) captures [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s unstoppable verbal energy. [[touchstone|Touchstone]] deploys language as a weapon of class satire and courtship, while [[silvius|Silvius]] and [[phebe|Phebe]] speak the conventional language of [[love-poetry|Petrarchan love]] — a discourse the play both indulges and ironizes.

The tongue's capacity for harm is as present as its capacity for delight. [[celia|Celia]]'s speech to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] about loving the bear's whelp acknowledges "my tongue hath wrong'd him" (010-087), and the play repeatedly shows how speech can wound. Yet speech is also the instrument of the play's resolutions: [[rosalind|Rosalind]] as [[ganimed|Ganymede]] uses language to educate [[orlando|Orlando]] in love, and the final [[regeneration|restoration]] of order is accomplished through declarations and vows — speech acts that transform social reality.

[[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s famous reflection on the degrees of the lie — the "retort courteous," the "quip modest," the "reply churlish," the "reproof valiant," and the "countercheck quarrelsome" — is [[wit]] its own comic climax but also a serious anatomy of how speech mediates social conflict. In [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], where the usual hierarchies are suspended, language becomes the primary medium of both competition and community.
