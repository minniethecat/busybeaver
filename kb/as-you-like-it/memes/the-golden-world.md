## Definition

The Golden World (or Golden Age) is a classical motif from Ovid's *Metamorphoses* and Virgil's *Eclogues* — the myth of a primordial era when humanity lived in harmony with nature, without labor, law, or private property. In As You Like It, the Golden World is invoked explicitly once but haunts the entire play as the ideal against which the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]] is measured. The exiled courtiers "fleet the time carelesly as they did in the [[green-world|golden world]]" (001-043) — a phrase that establishes the pastoral mode of the play before any character has set foot in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]. The Golden World is the impossible standard that [[duke-fredericks-court|the court]] has lost and that [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] imperfectly recovers.

## In As You Like It

The Golden World enters the play through [[charles|Charles]]'s report in the opening scene. Describing [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] and his companions in [[freedom|exile]], [[charles|Charles]] reports that "they say hee is already in the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]], and a many merry men with him; and there they liue like the old Robin Hood of England: and say they are yonger Brothers to the [[duke-senior|old Duke]], and say they fleet the time carelesly as they did in the [[green-world|golden world]]" (001-043). The triple "they say" is significant: the Golden World is a rumor, a reported ideal, and the play will spend its remaining acts testing whether the report is true.

The phrase "fleet the time carelesly" captures the Golden World's essential promise: a life free from the anxieties of time, labor, and social obligation. But the play immediately complicates this ideal. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s first speech in [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] acknowledges "the churlish Chiding of the winters winde" and the "seasons difference" — nature is not the benign garden of classical myth but a real English winter. The Golden World of Ovid is timeless; [[forest-of-arden|the Forest]] of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] has seasons, cold, hunger, and [[death-and-mortality|mortality]].

[[rosalind|Rosalind]] and [[celia|Celia]]'s purchase of [[silvius|the shepherd]]'s cottage introduces gold into the pastoral economy in a literal sense: [[corin|Corin]]'s master is selling, and [[celia|Celia]] produces gold to buy it (006-047). Gold — the metal that names the Golden World — becomes a medium of transaction even in the supposed retreat from commerce. The Golden World was defined by the absence of property; [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] is full of property transactions, from [[rosalinds-cottage|the cottage]] sale to [[touchstone|Touchstone]]'s negotiation with [[audrey|Audrey]].

The Golden World motif connects to the play's treatment of [[pastoralism]] and [[green-world]]. Where [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] attempts to find the Golden World in [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]'s hardships — reading "sermons in stones" and "good in euery thing" — [[jaques|Jaques]] insists on the gap between ideal and reality, moralizing on the wounded deer that bleeds into the pastoral stream. The Golden World is the dream that [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]] can never quite fulfill, and the play's comedy depends on that failure as much as on the dream itself.

The motif also carries a political charge. The Golden World ideal was used in Renaissance political thought to criticize contemporary society: if humanity once lived without hierarchy and property, then the existing social order is not natural but conventional. [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]]'s [[freedom|exile]] community — "many merry men" living without the apparatus of the court — is a small-scale experiment in Golden World politics, and the play's ending, with the [[regeneration|restoration]] of the Duke to his throne, marks the end of that experiment as much as its triumph.
