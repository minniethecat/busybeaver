# [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s Court

## Overview

[[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]]'s court is the play's site of tyranny and corruption — the place from which the major [[persons|characters]] must flee to find [[freedom]] in the [[forest-of-arden-place|Forest of Arden]]. It is here that [[orlando|Orlando]] is denied his birthright, [[charles|Charles]] the wrestler serves as an instrument of intimidation, and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] is banished on pain of death. The court embodies everything the pastoral [[forest-of-arden|green world]] opposes: artificial hierarchy, arbitrary power, and the perversion of natural bonds.

## Dramatic Function

The court serves as the play's "anti-[[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]" — a space where [[fortune|Fortune's]] tyranny is absolute and [[nature|Nature]]'s claims are denied. Three key actions occur here: [[orlando|Orlando]]'s [[wrestling-match|wrestling victory]] (which proves his worth but earns the Duke's enmity), [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s [[exile|banishment]] (which sets the pastoral plot in motion), and [[celia|[[celia|Celia]]'s]] renunciation of her inheritance (which establishes loyalty over blood).

The court is described indirectly by those who have fled it. The exiled [[duke-senior|Duke Senior]] contrasts it unfavorably with [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]: [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]] is "More free from perill then the enuious Court" (004-006). The court is a place of painted pomp and flattery, where the winter wind's honest bite would be preferable to the courtier's false praise.

[[duke-fredericks-court|The court]]'s power is ultimately broken not by force but by conversion: [[duke-frederick|Duke Frederick]], marching to destroy his brother, meets "an old religious man" at [[forest-of-arden-place|the forest]]'s edge and is converted (022-115). The court's tyranny is dissolved by the pastoral world's spiritual influence rather than by political action.
