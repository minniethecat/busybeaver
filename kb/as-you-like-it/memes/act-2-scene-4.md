(007-001)Enter [[rosalind|Rosaline]] for [[ganimed|Ganimed]], [[celia|Celia]] for [[aliena|Aliena]], and [[touchstone|Clowne]], alias

(007-002)Touchstone.

(007-003)Ros. O Iupiter, how merry are my spirits?

(007-004)Clo. I care not for my spirits, if my legges were not

(007-005)wearie

(007-006)Ros. I could finde in my heart to disgrace my mans apparell, and to cry like a woman: but I must comfort the weaker vessell, as doublet and hose ought to show it selfe coragious to petty-coate; therefore courage, good Aliena

(007-007)Cel. I pray you beare with me, I cannot goe no further

(007-008)Clo. For my part, I had rather beare with you, then beare you: yet I should beare no crosse if I did beare you, for I thinke you haue no money in your purse

(007-009)Ros. Well, this is the Forrest of [[forest-of-arden-place|Arden]]

(007-010)Clo. I, now am I in [[forest-of-arden|Arden]], the more foole I, when I

(007-011)was at home I was in a better place, but Trauellers must

(007-012)be content.

(007-013)Enter [[corin|Corin]] and Siluius.

(007-014)Ros. I, be so good Touchstone: Look you, who comes

(007-015)here, a yong man and an old in solemne talke

(007-016)Cor. That is the way to make her scorne you still

(007-017)Sil. Oh [[corin|Corin]], that thou knew'st how I do [[love|loue]] her

(007-018)Cor. I partly guesse: for I haue lou'd ere now

(007-019)Sil. No Corin, being old, thou canst not guesse,

(007-020)Though in thy [[youth]] thou wast as true a louer

(007-021)As euer sigh'd vpon a midnight pillow:

(007-022)But if thy loue were euer like to mine,

(007-023)As sure I thinke did neuer man loue so:

(007-024)How many actions most ridiculous,

(007-025)Hast thou beene drawne to by thy fantasie?

(007-026)Cor. Into a thousand that I haue forgotten

(007-027)Sil. Oh thou didst then neuer loue so hartily,

(007-028)If thou remembrest not the slightest folly,

(007-029)That euer loue did make thee run into,

(007-030)Thou hast not lou'd.

(007-031)Or if thou hast not sat as I doe now,

(007-032)Wearing thy hearer in thy Mistris praise,

(007-033)Thou hast not lou'd.

(007-034)Or if thou hast not broke from companie,

(007-035)Abruptly as my passion now makes me,

(007-036)Thou hast not lou'd.

(007-037)O [[phebe|Phebe]], Phebe, Phebe.

(007-038)Enter.

(007-039)Ros. Alas poore Shepheard searching of they would,

(007-040)I haue by hard aduenture found mine owne

(007-041)Clo. And I mine: I remember when I was in loue, I broke my sword vpon a stone, and bid him take that for comming a night to Iane Smile, and I remember the kissing of her batler, and the Cowes dugs that her prettie chopt hands had milk'd; and I remember the wooing of a peascod instead of her, from whom I tooke two cods, and giuing her them againe, said with weeping teares, weare these for my sake: wee that are true Louers, runne into strange capers; but as all is mortall in nature, so is all nature in loue, mortall in folly

(007-042)Ros. Thou speak'st wiser then thou art ware of

(007-043)Clo. Nay, I shall nere be ware of mine owne [[wit]], till

(007-044)I breake my shins against it

(007-045)Ros. Ioue, Ioue, this Shepherds passion,

(007-046)Is much vpon my fashion

(007-047)Clo. And mine, but it growes something stale with

(007-048)mee

(007-049)Cel. I pray you, one of you question yon'd man,

(007-050)If he for gold will giue vs any foode,

(007-051)I faint almost to death

(007-052)Clo. Holla; you Clowne

(007-053)Ros. Peace foole, he's not thy kinsman

(007-054)Cor. Who cals?

(007-055)Clo. Your betters Sir

(007-056)Cor. Else are they very wretched

(007-057)Ros. Peace I say; good euen to your friend

(007-058)Cor. And to you gentle Sir, and to you all

(007-059)Ros. I prethee Shepheard, if that loue or gold

(007-060)Can in this desert place buy entertainment,

(007-061)Bring vs where we may rest our selues, and feed:

(007-062)Here's a yong maid with trauaile much oppressed,

(007-063)And faints for succour

(007-064)Cor. Faire Sir, I pittie her,

(007-065)And wish for her sake more then for mine owne,

(007-066)My fortunes were more able to releeue her:

(007-067)But I am shepheard to another man,

(007-068)And do not sheere the Fleeces that I graze:

(007-069)My master is of churlish disposition,

(007-070)And little wreakes to finde the way to heauen

(007-071)By doing deeds of hospitalitie.

(007-072)Besides his Coate, his Flockes, and bounds of feede

(007-073)Are now on sale, and at our sheep-coat now

(007-074)By reason of his absence there is nothing

(007-075)That you will feed on: but what is, come see,

(007-076)And in my voice most welcome shall you be

(007-077)Ros. What is he that shall buy his flocke and pasture?

(007-078)Cor. That yong Swaine that you saw heere but erewhile,

(007-079)That little cares for buying any thing

(007-080)Ros. I pray thee, if it stand with honestie,

(007-081)Buy thou the Cottage, pasture, and the flocke,

(007-082)And thou shalt haue to pay for it of vs

(007-083)Cel. And we will mend thy wages:

(007-084)I like this place, and willingly could

(007-085)Waste my time in it

(007-086)Cor. Assuredly the thing is to be sold:

(007-087)Go with me, if you like vpon report,

(007-088)The soile, the profit, and this kinde of life,

(007-089)I will your very faithfull Feeder be,

(007-090)And buy it with your Gold right sodainly.

(007-091)Exeunt.
