The Elect, in Pascal's [[Jansenism|Jansenist]] theology, are those whom [[God]] has chosen for [[Salvation]] — not on the basis of foreseen merit, but by a free and inscrutable decree of divine [[Mercy]].

The doctrine of election is central to Pascal's Augustinianism and sets his Theology in direct opposition to the Pelagian and semi-Pelagian theologies he saw in post-Tridentine Catholicism.

For Pascal, the hiddenness of election
— the elect themselves do not know with certainty that they are elect
— is not a theological problem but a spiritual discipline: it compels [[Humility]] and [[Faith]] in place of presumption.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's treatment of the elect appears most prominently in his reading of the [[Scripture|prophetic]] books, particularly Isaiah, and in his apologetic argument about the hiddenness of [[God]].

> (007-637) The elect will be ignorant of their virtues and the outcast of the greatness of their crimes.

The symmetry is deliberate: just as the elect cannot see their own virtues, the reprobate cannot see the magnitude of their sins.

Both are hidden from themselves — the elect so that they do not fall into [[Pride]], the outcast so that they do not fall into [[Despair]].

Pascal takes this hiddenness as a structural feature of the human condition: "the elect will be ignorant of their virtues" means that assurance of [[Salvation]] is not available to introspection.

> (003-529) Feelings than mercy towards the elect.

The elect are not the object of [[God]]'s justice but of his [[Mercy]]. This is the key distinction: election is not a reward for virtue but a gift of free grace.

If election were based on merit, it would not be election — it would be payment.

> (008-227) Isaiah says: *Signa legem in electis meis* — seal the law among my elect.

Pascal reads the prophetic designation of the elect
as a sign of the continuity between the Old Testament and the New:
the [[Jews in Pascal's Pensées|Jewish people]] were the type, the Church is the antitype,
and the individual [[Soul|soul]] is the microcosm of both.

> (010-060) The hope of His elect, He has made them see in it a way of salvation.

The elect are not those who are morally superior but those to whom [[God]] has given the gift of [[Faith]] — the capacity to see in [[Jesus Christ]] the way of [[Salvation]].

## Key Passages

> (012-271) Jesus Christ saves the elect, and condemns the outcast.

This stark formulation is Pascal at his most [[Jansenism|Jansenist]]:
the work of [[Jesus Christ|Christ]] has a twofold outcome,
and the distinction between the two outcomes
is not determined by human choice but by divine decree.

> (012-355) The elect who have never heard of Jesus Christ.

Pascal acknowledges a difficult question: what of those who lived before Christ or never heard of him?

His answer, consonant with his Augustinianism, is that God's election is not bound by the ordinary means of [[Grace]] — God can save whom he will, with or without the preaching of the Gospel.

> (014-044) The elect ask for their deliverance.

The posture of the elect is not self-confidence but supplication: they know they are not saved by their own power and therefore they ask.

This is the practical consequence of Pascal's doctrine: election produces [[Humility]], not presumption.

## Analysis

Pascal's doctrine of election is pastorally motivated.

It is not a speculation about the hidden decrees of [[God]]
— Pascal shows little interest in the metaphysical mechanics of [[Predestination]]
— but a framework for understanding the [[Christianity in Pascal's Pensées|Christian]] life as one of radical dependence.

The hiddenness of election serves to demolish two opposite errors:
the [[Pride]] of those who think they can earn [[Salvation]] by their own efforts,
and the [[Despair]] of those who think their sins place them beyond [[Mercy]].

Both errors, Pascal insists, misunderstand the nature of [[Grace]]: grace is not a transaction
— it cannot be earned, and it cannot be exhausted by sin. It is the free gift of [[God]] to those who, for reasons known only to God, are the objects of his love.

> (005-154) Some place it in election by the people.

In a rare political aside, Pascal notes that the concept of "election" operates analogically in the social realm
— the people "elect" their rulers
— but divine election is not democratic; it is the sovereign choice of [[God]].

This distinction between human election (based on preference or merit) and divine election (based solely on God's will) is essential to Pascal's Theology and represents his decisive break with any form of Pelagianism.
