[[Glory|Glory]], in Pascal's analysis, is not one thing but three — and the confusion of these three kinds of glory is among the deepest sources of human [[error|error]] and [[vanity|vanity]].

Pascal distinguishes three distinct kinds of glory, each proper to one of the [[three-orders|three orders]], each with its own object, its own mode of recognition, and its own characteristic corruption.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Carnal Glory and Intellectual Glory

The lowest order of glory is carnal glory — the esteem from physical beauty, strength, social rank, and material possession. This is the glory of the [[body|body]], sustained by [[custom|custom]] and [[force|force]].

Above this is intellectual glory — the esteem from discovery, invention, and the exercise of the [[mind|mind]].

> [005-134] glory, and despise those who do not invent, the latter will call them extraordinary men.

Pascal recognised this order as higher than the carnal, yet still finite and vulnerable to [[vanity|vanity]]. The inventor's glory is real — but only within its own order.

The man who seeks intellectual glory as the highest good has mistaken the second order for the third. Pascal criticises those who "glory in being above the folly" — taking pride in seeing through popular errors without seeing through their own. This corruption of intellectual glory produces [[pride|pride]] rather than [[charity|charity]].

### The Glory of Religion

The highest order is the glory of [[charity|charity]] — belonging to [[god|God]] alone, in which the [[christianity|Christian]] participates only by participation in God.

> [003-170] In truth, it is the glory of religion to have for enemies men so unreasonable…

Pascal's apologetic strategy turns on a paradox: the glory of [[faith-and-reason|religion]] is that its enemies are themselves the proof of its truth. The [[unbelief|unbeliever]] who scorns religion demonstrates, by his very scorn, the [[wretchedness|wretchedness]] of the human condition that religion alone explains.

> [003-546] But by faith we know His existence; in glory we shall know His nature.

This distinction — knowing God's *existence* by faith now, knowing God's *nature* in glory hereafter — is central to Pascal's [[epistemology|epistemology]]. Glory is an eschatological horizon: the state in which knowledge is perfected.

### The Desire for Glory as Human Motivation

Pascal treats the desire for glory as a fundamental anthropological datum — one of the "greatnesses" of man surviving the [[original-sin|Fall]]:

> [006-056] can be done always, and that since the desire of glory imparts

Even the [[skepticism|sceptic]] is motivated by the desire for glory. The desire cannot be eradicated — only redirected. The question is not whether one seeks glory, but in which order.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of glory exemplifies his method of distinguishing orders without separating them.

Carnal glory is not evil — it becomes evil only when it claims the ultimacy proper to the glory of charity. Intellectual glory is not vanity — it becomes vanity only when it refuses to acknowledge its own limits.

Pascal's tripartite analysis of glory anticipates his [[pascals-wager|Wager]] argument: the stakes are infinite because what is at issue is eternal glory — participation in the life of God.

The Pascalian move is to trace each human motivation back to its root desire for glory, showing that no finite glory can satisfy it. The result is the [[greatness-and-misery|greatness and misery]] paradox.
