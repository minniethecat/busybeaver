[[Sovereign Good|The sovereign good]] (*souverain bien*), in Pascal's [[ethics|ethics]], is the question that unites and divides all [[philosophy|philosophy]].

> [002-319] Into her own sovereign good. Let us see, then, wherein this sovereign good consists.

Every philosophical system proposes an answer to the question of the highest [[good|good]] — what human beings should ultimately seek. But for Pascal, the diversity of answers is itself evidence that the question has not been answered: if the sovereign good were accessible to [[reason|reason]] alone, there would be one answer, not 280.

> [002-357] 280 kinds of sovereign good in Montaigne.

The reference to [[Montaigne|Montaigne]] is strategic: the [[skepticism|skeptical]] inventory of philosophical opinions reveals not wisdom but confusion. If human reason could discern the sovereign good, it would have done so. The fact that it has not is a proof of [[disproportion|disproportion]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

> [002-322] One says that the sovereign good consists in virtue, another in pleasure, another in knowledge of nature.

Pascal enumerates the classical candidates — [[virtue|virtue]] (the Stoics), [[pleasure|pleasure]] (the Epicureans), [[knowledge|knowledge]] (the Aristotelians) — and finds each inadequate. The inadequacy is not that these goods are worthless but that they are finite. The [[human-nature|human]] being, Pascal argues, has an "infinite abyss" that can be filled only by an infinite object. Finite goods, however genuine, cannot fill an infinite capacity.

> [006-122] The sovereign good. Dispute about the sovereign good.

The very fact of dispute is evidence: if the sovereign good were self-evident, there would be no dispute. The universality of disagreement is a datum that any adequate [[anthropology|anthropology]] must explain.

> [005-119] Which is the sovereign good.

Pascal's answer is [[theological|theological]]: the sovereign good is [[God|God]] — specifically, the God of [[christianity|Christianity]] who gives Himself to be enjoyed. This is not an answer reason could have discovered; it must be revealed. But once revealed, it is seen to satisfy the criteria that reason can establish: only an infinite object can satisfy an infinite desire.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of the sovereign good integrates his [[apologetics|apologetics]] with his [[moral-psychology|moral psychology]].

The psychological premise is that human beings have a desire they cannot name — an aspiration toward something beyond every finite good. [[Diversion|Diversion]] is the strategy for coping with the frustration of this desire; [[vanity|vanity]] is the corruption of this desire into self-display; [[self-love|self-love]] is the redirection of this desire toward the [[ego|ego]].

The apologetic conclusion is that Christianity uniquely identifies both the object of this desire (God) and the means of attaining it ([[grace|grace]] through [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]). Every other candidate for the sovereign good fails, not because it is false but because it is too small.
