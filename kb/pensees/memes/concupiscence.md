[[Concupiscence|Concupiscence]] is the theological term for the disordered desire that, in Pascal's [[jansenism|Jansenist]] inheritance, constitutes the fundamental corruption of fallen [[human-nature|human nature]]. It is not one [[sin|sin]] among others but the generative condition from which particular sins arise — the inclination of the will away from [[God|God]] and toward created goods.

> [P01-443] ...the sin of spiritual pride, the concupiscence of the flesh, and the concupiscence of the eyes. This is the threefold concupiscence which has subjected men to the world, the flesh, and the devil.

Pascal inherits the tripartite classification of concupiscence from the [[augustine|Augustinian]] tradition — concupiscence of the flesh (sensual desire), concupiscence of the eyes (curiosity, intellectual ambition), and the pride of life (spiritual self-exaltation). This threefold structure means that no domain of [[human-nature|human]] existence escapes concupiscence's reach.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Concupiscence as Structural Condition

For Pascal, concupiscence is not merely a tendency to particular sinful acts. It is a structural condition that affects the will at its root. The [[self-love|self]] is not neutral, capable of turning either toward [[God|God]] or toward creatures; it is already inclined away from [[God|God]], and this inclination is what concupiscence names.

This has a crucial consequence for Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetics]]: [[human-nature|man]] cannot simply choose to love [[God|God]] above all things. The will is already bent. What is needed is not moral exhortation but [[grace|grace]] — a supernatural intervention that redirects the will from its concupiscent orientation. This is the specifically [[jansenism|Jansenist]] inflection of Pascal's theology.

### Concupiscence and the Three Orders

Concupiscence maps onto Pascal's doctrine of the [[three-orders|three orders]] in a revealing way. The concupiscence of the flesh corresponds to the order of the [[body|body]] — desire for physical goods elevated to ultimate status. The concupiscence of the eyes corresponds to the order of the [[mind|mind]] — intellectual [[curiosity|curiosity]] that seeks [[knowledge|knowledge]] for the sake of [[vanity|vanity]] rather than [[truth|truth]]. The pride of life corresponds to a distortion of the order of [[charity|charity]] — [[self-love|self-love]] usurping the place that belongs to [[God|God]] alone.

Each order has its own form of concupiscence, and each form is a distortion of a genuine good. The body's desires are not evil in themselves; they become concupiscent when they refuse their subordinate place. The mind's pursuit of knowledge is not evil; it becomes concupiscent when it serves [[vanity|vanity]] rather than wisdom. [[self-love|Self-love]] is not evil in itself — Pascal acknowledges a natural self-love — but it becomes concupiscent when it refuses to recognise its dependence on [[God|God]].

## Key Passages

The *Pensées* do not contain extended explicit treatments of concupiscence; the concept operates in the theological background rather than the foreground of Pascal's argument. The term's presence at P01-443 — in the context of a classification of sins — indicates its role as a framework concept rather than a topic of direct exposition.

This thinness of direct reference is itself significant: Pascal presupposes the doctrine of concupiscence as part of the [[augustine|Augustinian]] inheritance shared by his intended audience. He does not argue for it; he uses it to explain the phenomena — the universal restlessness, the failure of [[morality|moral]] effort, the persistence of [[self-love|self-love]] — that his [[apologetics|apologetics]] addresses.

## Analysis

Concupiscence stands at the intersection of several of Pascal's major themes. It is the theological root of the psychological phenomena he analyses — [[self-love|self-love]], [[vanity|vanity]], [[diversion|diversion]], the flight from self-knowledge — and it provides the explanation for why these phenomena are universal. They are not cultural accidents or individual failings but expressions of a structural condition.

The doctrine of concupiscence also illuminates Pascal's [[christianity|Christianity's]] specific character. For Pascal, [[christianity|Christianity]] is not primarily a moral code to be followed but a diagnosis of a condition that cannot be cured by [[morality|moral]] effort. Concupiscence is the diagnosis; [[grace|grace]] is the cure; and the [[pascals-wager|Wager]] is the decision to seek the cure rather than deny the diagnosis.
