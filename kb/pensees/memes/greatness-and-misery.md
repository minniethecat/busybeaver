[[Greatness and Misery of Man|The greatness and misery of man]] is the master dialectic of Pascal's [[anthropology|anthropology]] — the organising insight that generates the entire structure of the *Pensées*.

> [007-070] The greatness and the wretchedness of man are both evident.

What makes this dual recognition distinctively Pascalian is that it refuses to resolve the tension. Human beings are not great *despite* their misery, nor miserable *despite* their greatness. The two truths are co-constitutive: the greatness consists precisely in the capacity to recognise misery; the misery consists in the incapacity to overcome what one recognises.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Structure of the Dialectic

> [002-002] THE MISERY OF MAN WITHOUT GOD

> [002-004] First part: Misery of man without God.

The section headings of the *Pensées* themselves encode the dialectic: misery is the first moment, but it is misery "without God" — a qualification that already implies a possible resolution. Pascal does not begin with greatness because the [[human-condition|human]] being must first be brought to recognise its condition before it can recognise what that condition reveals.

> [007-457] Greatness, wretchedness.—The more light we have, the more greatness and the more baseness we discover in man.

Knowledge does not resolve the paradox; it intensifies it. The more one understands human nature, the more one sees both its elevation and its degradation.

### The Sources of Greatness

Pascal identifies several markers of human greatness:

> [007-129] Observe, now, all the feelings of greatness and glory which the trial even of the greatest misery cannot stifle.

Even in extremity — in suffering, in failure, in degradation — the human being retains "feelings of greatness." These feelings are not delusions (though they can become occasions for [[vanity|vanity]]); they are evidence of an original condition from which the human being has fallen.

> [007-257] It ought to know its greatness and littleness, and the reason of both.

The "ought" here is normative: the properly functioning human mind recognises both its greatness and its littleness, and grasps the reason for each. To see only one side — to be a [[dogmatism|dogmatist]] who sees only greatness or a [[skepticism|skeptic]] who sees only misery — is to fail in self-knowledge.

### The Sources of Misery

> [007-073] A great source of wretchedness.

Misery is not merely a contingent condition but has a "source" — it is grounded in something structural about human existence. Pascal's account of this source involves [[original-sin|original sin]], [[concupiscence|concupiscence]], and the loss of the [[sovereign-good|sovereign good]].

> [007-339] Were there no greatness in our condition, we have an idea of happiness which we cannot attain.

The very capacity to conceive of [[happiness|happiness]] — and to recognise that we do not possess it — is both a mark of greatness (only a being capable of transcending its condition can judge that condition inadequate) and a source of misery (the gap between aspiration and attainment is constitutive of the human situation).

## Key Passages

> [007-072] There is in man some great source of greatness, and a great source of wretchedness.

The parallelism is precise: two sources, both great, neither reducible to the other. Pascal's anthropology is fundamentally dual — but it is a duality that points toward a unity that only [[christianity|Christianity]] can provide.

> [007-637] The true religion must teach greatness and misery; must lead to the esteem and contempt of self, to love and to hate.

This is the apologetic criterion: the true [[religion|religion]] is the one that accounts for both poles of human experience. [[Christianity|Christianity]], Pascal argues, is uniquely capable of doing so — which is itself evidence of its truth.

> [012-462] All the glory of greatness has no lustre for people who are in pursuits of the mind.

Pascal distinguishes between orders of greatness: the glory of worldly greatness is invisible to those who operate in the order of the [[three-orders|mind]], just as the greatness of the mind is invisible to those who operate in the order of the body. Each order has its own criteria of greatness, and these criteria are incommensurable.

> [012-466] The greatness of wisdom, which is nothing if not of God, is invisible to the carnal and to the clever.

Here the hierarchy culminates: the highest greatness — [[wisdom|wisdom]] — is accessible only within the order of [[charity|charity]], which is invisible to those confined to lower orders.

## Analysis

The greatness-and-misery dialectic is the engine of Pascal's entire [[apologetics|apologetic]] project. It serves simultaneously as diagnosis and proof.

As diagnosis, it accounts for the experiential data of [[human-nature|human experience]]: the simultaneous sense of elevation and degradation, the capacity for [[reason|reason]] and the proneness to [[error|error]], the aspiration to [[justice|justice]] and the reality of [[injustice|injustice]]. No purely naturalistic anthropology, Pascal claims, can hold these two dimensions together without reducing one to the other.

As proof, it functions as a criterion of religious truth: only a religion that explains both our greatness (as created in the image of [[God|God]]) and our misery (as fallen from that original state) can be true. The explanatory adequacy of Christianity to the human condition is itself a mark of its divine origin.

> [002-180] Of these two Infinites of science, that of greatness is the most palpable.

The "infinite of greatness" — the human capacity for [[knowledge|knowledge]], [[love|love]], and [[transcendence|transcendence]] — is more immediately evident than the "infinite of smallness." Yet both infinites converge on the same conclusion: the human being is not self-sufficient.
