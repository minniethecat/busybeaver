[[Saint Augustine]] of Hippo (354–430) is the theological authority most frequently invoked in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées*, serving as the intellectual backbone of his [[Jansenism|Jansenist]] commitments.

Pascal does not treat Augustine as a distant historical figure but as a living theological voice whose authority settles contested questions. The Augustinian doctrines of [[grace]], [[predestination]], and the corruption of human [[nature]] form the unstated premises of much of Pascal's [[anthropology]]. When Pascal argues that [[human nature]] is simultaneously great and wretched, that [[reason]] alone cannot reach [[God]], or that [[faith]] is a divine gift rather than a human achievement, he stands within an Augustinian tradition mediated through [[Jansenius]] and the community of [[Port-Royal]].

## Augustine in Pascal's Three Orders

Pascal's most explicit invocation of Augustine appears in his discussion of the [[three-orders|three orders]] — the foundational framework that structures the *Pensées*. Pascal identifies three distinct domains: the order of body (kings, riches, carnal greatness), the order of mind (scientists, philosophers, intellectual greatness), and the order of [[charity]] (saints, wisdom, holiness). He then grounds this schema in Augustinian authority (004-349).

> [004-349] This order consists chiefly in… with Saint Augustine.

The three orders provide the architecture within which Pascal's entire apologetic operates. By anchoring it in Augustine, Pascal signals that his apparently novel framework is in fact a recovery of patristic wisdom. The order of charity — invisible to the eyes of flesh and mind alike — is precisely the order in which Augustine himself moved, and Pascal sees his own project as belonging to the same tradition of [[wisdom]] that transcends mere intellectual achievement.

## Augustine on Grace and the Hidden God

The concept of efficacious [[grace]] — central to Jansenist theology and fiercely contested in seventeenth-century France — finds its primary patristic warrant in Augustine. Pascal invokes this tradition when he describes the hiddenness of [[God]] as a deliberate divine strategy: God gives sufficient light to those who seek and sufficient darkness to those who resist.

> [008-227] Saint Augustine, Montaigne, Sébond. The genealogy of those who do not believe: they have not seen miracles, they have not heard prophecies, and they find the morals of Christians bad. And in this way they think the proofs of religion are weak.

Pascal places Augustine alongside [[Montaigne]] and [[Sebond]] in a genealogy of witnesses, but Augustine's role is unique: he is not merely a witness to the evidence but the theologian who articulated why the evidence is calibrated as it is. The hiddenness of God, for Augustine as for Pascal, is not a failure of revelation but its proper mode — preserving human freedom while providing sufficient grounds for [[faith]].

## Augustine and the Corruption of Nature

Pascal's anthropology of human [[corruption]] — the claim that [[human nature]] is fallen yet retains traces of its original grandeur — derives directly from Augustine's anti-Pelagian writings. When Pascal writes that "[[nature]] is corrupt, proved by nature itself," he echoes the Augustinian diagnosis that the universality of human misery is empirical evidence for the doctrine of [[original-sin]].

> [007-637] Saint Augustine has distinctly said that strength would be taken away from the righteous.

This passage, embedded in Pascal's long meditation on the nature of righteousness and the people of God, shows Augustine functioning as the definitive interpreter of Scripture. Pascal does not argue *for* an Augustinian reading; he assumes it, treating Augustine's exegesis as the settled meaning of the text. The Christian, for Pascal, is an Augustinian Christian, whether they know it or not.

## Augustine and the Wager's Anthropology

The [[Wager]] argument — Pascal's most famous contribution to [[apologetics]] — has Augustinian roots that Pascal makes explicit. The insight that human beings act under [[uncertainty]] and that the will is moved more by [[custom]] and [[habit]] than by demonstration belongs to Augustine's psychology:

> [003-685] Saint Augustine has seen that we work for an uncertain end.

This single sentence condenses a major Augustinian theme: human action is always under the shadow of ignorance. We do not and cannot know the outcomes of our choices with certainty. The Wager exploits this Augustinian insight by reframing it as a decision-theoretic problem: given that we must act under uncertainty, the rational course is to wager on the outcome with infinite stakes. Augustine "has seen" the anthropological truth; Pascal supplies its apologetic application.

## Augustine as Contemporary Authority

In a striking passage, Pascal imagines Augustine returning to the present day and finding that his authority would carry no more weight than that of his seventeenth-century successors:

> [014-134] If Saint Augustine came at the present time, and had no more authority than his defenders, he would accomplish nothing.

This counterfactual reveals Pascal's keen awareness of the sociology of authority. Augustine is invoked not because his name carries institutional weight — though it does — but because his arguments are true. If those arguments must stand on their own merits, they will. The passage is at once a defense of Augustine (his truth does not depend on his fame) and a warning to Pascal's contemporaries (do not mistake institutional authority for genuine evidence). The Jansenist appeal to Augustine is an appeal to truth, not to power.
