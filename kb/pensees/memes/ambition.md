## Definition

In Pascal's moral psychology, **ambition** (ambition) is the **desire for worldly greatness** — one of the three concupiscences (along with [[Concupiscence|lust]] and [[Pride]]) that constitute the corruption of human nature after the Fall. Ambition drives the pursuit of honor, power, and recognition, but Pascal demonstrates that all such pursuits are marked by pettiness and self-deception. Ambition is intimately linked to [[Self-Love]] (amour-propre), which seeks its own glory rather than God's.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Ambition as a Symptom of the Fallen Condition

Pascal lists ambition alongside pride and lust as fundamental markers of human corruption. Self-knowledge requires acknowledging these passions:

> (007-548) If we do not know ourselves to be full of pride, ambition, lust, weakness, misery, and injustice, we are indeed blind.

This enumeration is not random. [[Pride]], ambition, and lust correspond to the Augustinian threefold concupiscence: pride of life, lust of the eyes, and lust of the flesh. Ambition is the social expression of pride — the drive to elevate oneself above others. To "know ourselves" in Pascal's sense is to recognize that ambition is not an accidental feature but constitutive of fallen [[Human Nature|human nature]].

### The Pettiness of Real Ambitions

Pascal's most striking observation about ambition is its pettiness. Even the greatest worldly ambitions shrink to insignificance when viewed against the infinite:

> (P01-437) The vanity of their thoughts and of their avocations, their dishonesty and self-deceptions, the insincerity of their emotions, their cowardice, the pettiness of their real ambitions.

This passage situates ambition within Pascal's broader critique of [[Vanity]]. Human beings chase after positions, honors, and recognition — yet objective examination reveals the objects of ambition to be trivial. The ambitious man is like the [[Diversion|diverted]] man: both flee from the knowledge of their own [[Wretchedness]] by immersing themselves in pursuits that cannot satisfy.

### Ambition and Greatness

Pascal distinguishes sharply between ambition (the desire for worldly elevation) and true [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness]]. True human greatness is not positional but constitutive — it lies in thought:

> (006-032) Thought constitutes the greatness of man.

Ambition mistakes the shadow of greatness (social status, power, recognition) for its substance (the capacity for thought and the recognition of one's creaturely condition). This is why ambition, despite its pretensions, actually diminishes the ambitious person. The pursuit of greatness from others renders one incapable of the inward greatness that is distinctively human.

### Grace as Deliverance from Ambition

For Pascal, ambition is not merely a vice to be managed but a corruption from which only divine [[Grace]] can deliver:

> (007-637) Of a man full of weakness, of miseries, of lust, of pride, and of ambition, has made a man free from all these evils by the power of His grace, to which all the glory of it is due, as of myself I have only misery and error.

This is Pascal's personal testimony embedded in the apologetic argument. The movement from ambition to freedom is not accomplished by willpower or philosophical self-cultivation — it is a work of grace that reorients the soul from [[Self-Love|self-love]] toward the love of God. The "glory" that the ambitious man sought for himself is now rightly attributed to its true source: "to which all the glory of it is due."

### Ambition and the Social Order

Ambition does not merely corrupt the individual — it structures social life. In Pascal's analysis of [[Custom]] and [[Force]], he shows that social hierarchies are maintained by imagination and habit rather than by genuine merit. Ambition exploits this fact, seeking to accumulate the external markers of status without regard for inward transformation. The ambitious man's relationship to society mirrors the [[Superstition|superstitious]] man's relationship to religion: both substitute external performance for inward reality.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of ambition is distinctive because he neither moralizes against it nor treats it as a problem solvable by ethical effort. Ambition is structural — it belongs to the corrupted human constitution, and no amount of philosophical reflection can root it out. This is consistent with Pascal's broader Augustinian anthropology: [[Original Sin]] has so disordered human desire that even our virtues are disguised vices.

Ambition has an important epistemological dimension in Pascal's apologetics. The ambitious man cannot see his own condition clearly because ambition blinds him to the disproportion between his actual state and his pretensions. This is why Pascal insists on the dialectic of [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness and misery]]: the desire for greatness (which ambition perverts) is a remnant of original dignity, but the pettiness of what ambition actually achieves reveals the depth of the fall. Only a religion that accounts for both — that explains why man desires greatness and why he achieves only vanity — can be true.

The remedy for ambition is not the suppression of the desire for greatness but its redirection. Grace does not eliminate the drive for glory; it transfers it from self to God. "To which all the glory of it is due" (007-637) — this is the resolution of ambition, not as an achievement but as a gift.
