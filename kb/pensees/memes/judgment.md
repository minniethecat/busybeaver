# Judgment

## In Pascal's Pensées

Judgment (jugement) is Pascal's term for the intuitive faculty of discernment that belongs to the *esprit de finesse* (the intuitive mind). Unlike reason, which proceeds by sequential demonstration, judgment perceives at a glance. "It is to judgment that perception belongs, as science belongs to reason" (001-103). Judgment grasps moral and aesthetic truths that escape formal deduction — it is the faculty by which we recognize what cannot be proved. Pascal's epistemology thus carves out a domain for judgment that reason cannot colonize, yet judgment itself can be corrupted by self-love, custom, and the passions.

## Key Passages

> "It is to judgment that perception belongs, as science belongs to reason. Intuition is the part of judgment, mathematics of reason." (001-103)

> "The morality of the judgment, which has no rules, makes little progress." (001-101)

> "Beauty of omission, of judgment." (001-297)

> "How difficult is it not to upset a judgment." (002-809)

> "It is a perverted judgment that makes every one place himself above the rest of the world." (007-588)

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of judgment occupies a pivotal position in his epistemology of the three orders. Judgment is the cognitive faculty proper to the order of mind, yet it also points beyond itself — to the order of charity, where the heart's reasons operate by a logic that judgment can recognize but not generate.

The key distinction is between judgment and reason. Reason works by demonstration: it moves step by step from premises to conclusions. Judgment works by perception: it grasps a situation, a truth, or a beauty all at once. Pascal's examples — the beauty of omission, the morality that has no rules — emphasize that the most important human truths resist formalization. "The heart has its reasons which reason knows not" is the apex of this epistemology, though the heart and judgment are distinct faculties (the heart belongs to the order of charity; judgment to the order of mind).

Judgment's vulnerability is equally important. "How difficult is it not to upset a judgment" — self-love, the passions, custom, and imagination all conspire to distort judgment. Pascal's analysis of "perverted judgment" extends to social life: we all place ourselves above others, and judgment becomes the instrument of *amour-propre* rather than truth. This corruption of judgment is part of the human condition after the Fall: our cognitive faculties are real but wounded.
