[[Infinite|The infinite]], in Pascal's thought, operates on three distinct but interrelated planes: the mathematical, the anthropological, and the theological. Each illuminates the others, and together they constitute one of the most powerful conceptual frameworks in the *Pensées*.

## The Two Infinities

Pascal's most famous treatment of the infinite is the doctrine of the "two infinities" — the infinitely great and the infinitely small — which frame the human condition:

> [002-070] The infinites, the mean.—When we read too...

> [002-104] ...It is an infinite sphere, the centre of which is everywhere and the circumference nowhere.

Man is suspended between these two abysses. The [[two-infinities|two infinities]] doctrine is not merely a cosmological observation but an argument about the limits of human [[reason|reason]]: that which is infinitely beyond us cannot be grasped by our finite faculties.

## The Mathematical Infinite

Pascal approaches the mathematical infinite with characteristic rigor:

> [002-169] ...the sciences are infinite in the extent of their research...

> [002-170] ...geometry, for instance, has an infinite infinity of problems to solve.

The [[sciences|sciences]] are infinite in scope — there is no end to what can be known or discovered. This scientific infinity mirrors the [[human-condition|human condition]]: the intellect is drawn toward an endless horizon it can never reach (002-163).

## The Theological Infinite: The Wager

The infinite takes on its most consequential form in Pascal's [[wager|Wager]] argument. The stakes of belief are infinite:

> [002-911] ...that anything in all this is infinite...

> [002-914] ...that is infinite.

The Wager's logic turns on the asymmetry between finite cost and infinite gain: "a finite certainty against an infinite gain" (002-1075). The [[God|God]] of the Wager is "infinitely incomprehensible" — not the God of the philosophers but the living God of [[Abraham|Abraham]], [[Isaac|Isaac]], and [[Jacob|Jacob]].
