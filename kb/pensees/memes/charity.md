[[Charity|Charity]], in Pascal's [[three-orders|tripartite]] framework, is the highest of the three orders — the order of [[holiness|holiness]], supernatural [[love|love]], and direct participation in the [[divine|divine]] nature.

> [012-461] For charity is supernatural.

This single sentence is the key to Pascal's entire doctrine of charity. Charity is not a heightened form of natural love — not affection raised to a higher pitch, not benevolence refined by [[reason|reason]]. It is "supernatural" — belonging to an order that nature cannot reach and reason cannot comprehend.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Order of Charity

Pascal's most architectonic statement about charity is found in his doctrine of the three orders:

> [P01-477] The order of nature, the order of mind, and the order of charity.

Each order has its own objects, its own mode of perception, and its own criterion of [[greatness|greatness]]. The order of charity is not a continuation of the order of the mind but a qualitatively different domain — as different from the intellectual order as the intellectual order is from the physical.

> [012-460] The infinitely more infinite distance between mind and charity.

The distance between the first and second orders is "infinite"; the distance between the second and third is "infinitely more infinite." This is Pascal's way of saying that the transition from mind to charity is not a step on a continuous scale but a leap across a qualitative gulf.

### Charity and Knowledge

Charity is not merely an ethical concept for Pascal — it is an epistemological one. Those who possess charity see what those confined to lower orders cannot:

> [012-512] All bodies, the firmament, the stars, the earth and its kingdoms, are not equal to the least mind; for the mind knows all these things and itself; and bodies, nothing. All bodies and all minds together, and all their products, are not equal to the least feeling of charity.

The hierarchy is absolute: bodies < mind < charity. But the "feeling of charity" — the least movement of supernatural love — exceeds all the products of body and mind combined. This is not hyperbole; it is the logical consequence of the orders' incommensurability.

> [003-254] All the charity of the religion which they despise.

Pascal observes that those who despise [[christianity|Christianity]] are blind to its charity — not because they are malicious but because they lack the organ for perceiving it. Charity is invisible from outside the order of charity, just as [[mathematics|mathematical]] truth is invisible to one who has never learned to think mathematically.

### Charity and Covetousness

> [010-190] Typical.—Nothing is so like charity as covetousness, and nothing is so contrary.

This is one of Pascal's most compressed insights. [[Covetousness|Covetousness]] mimics charity in its intensity, its single-mindedness, its ability to organise a life around a supreme object. But the likeness is only formal — in substance, charity and covetousness are opposites. The formal similarity is what makes spiritual [[discernment|discernment]] so difficult and so necessary.

## Key Passages

> [005-219] As God is surrounded by persons full of charity, who ask of Him the blessings of charity that are in His power.

Charity is both a human disposition and a divine gift — the persons "full of charity" are full precisely because they have received what they ask for. The [[prayer|prayer]] for charity is itself an act of the charity one prays for.

> [004-194] They do not love either truth or charity. If they loved truth, they would find charity there.

The link between [[truth|truth]] and charity is essential: love of truth leads to charity, and charity is the form that truth takes when it is lived. Pascal does not separate the intellectual from the spiritual — he insists on their union.

> [012-516] All minds together, we cannot produce a feeling of true charity; this is above nature.

The "feeling of true charity" cannot be produced by human effort — not by the efforts of all minds combined. It is "above nature" — supernatural, a gift, not an achievement. This is the Augustinian core of Pascal's thought: charity is [[grace|grace]].

## Analysis

Pascal's doctrine of charity integrates his [[anthropology|anthropology]], [[epistemology|epistemology]], and [[apologetics|apologetics]].

Anthropologically, charity explains the highest human capacity — the capacity for a love that transcends both bodily appetite and intellectual comprehension. This capacity is not native to human nature but is given; its presence in a human life is evidence of something beyond nature operating within nature.

Epistemologically, charity is a mode of cognition: it sees what reason cannot. The truths of [[faith-and-reason|faith]] are not irrational but transrational — they require an organ of perception (charity) that is not reducible to the organ of demonstration (reason).

Apologetically, charity is the criterion by which the truth of Christianity is recognised: "The true religion must teach greatness and [[greatness-and-misery|misery]], and must lead to the esteem and contempt of self, to love and to hate" (007-637). Only Christianity accounts for the full range of human experience, and charity is the evidence of this accounting.
