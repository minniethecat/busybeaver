[[Illusion|Illusion]], in Pascal's analysis, is not an occasional error but the normal condition of human [[life|life]] apart from [[God|God]]. The *Pensées* diagnose the mechanisms by which human beings systematically deceive themselves and one another.

## Life as Perpetual Illusion

Pascal's verdict on unaided human existence is uncompromising:

> [002-1075] ...Life, human, a perpetual illusion...

> [002-753] ...is thus only a perpetual illusion; men deceive and flatter each other.

This is not cynicism but diagnostic clarity. The [[human-condition|human condition]] is characterized by a systematic flight from [[truth|truth]] — not because truth is inaccessible, but because it is unbearable. [[Diversion|Diversion]], [[imagination|imagination]], and [[amour-propre|amour-propre]] conspire to sustain the illusion that all is well.

## The Mechanism of Self-Deception

Pascal identifies [[imagination|imagination]] as the primary faculty of illusion:

> [002-523] ...This is an illusion of...

> [002-691] ...illusion. We do not like others to deceive us...

Human beings are complicit in their own deception. They *want* to be deceived because the truth — that they are [[wretchedness|wretched]], mortal, and dependent — is too painful to sustain. Illusion is therefore not merely cognitive error but a form of willed ignorance.

## Disillusion as Apologetic Strategy

Pascal's apologetic method begins with disillusion:

> [014-033] They must be disillusioned.

> [P01-353] Pascal's disillusioned analysis of human bondage...

> [P01-357] ...His despair, his disillusion, are, however...

The [[apologetics|apologist]] must first strip away the illusions that protect the [[unbeliever|unbeliever]] from confronting the [[human-condition|human condition]]. Only when the [[diversion|diversions]] fail and the [[boredom|boredom]] sets in can the question of [[God|God]] arise with genuine urgency. This is why Pascal's tone shifts from demolition (destroying false comforts) to construction (offering the [[Wager|Wager]] and [[faith|faith]]).
