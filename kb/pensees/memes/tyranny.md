# Tyranny

## In Pascal's Pensées

Tyranny, for Pascal, is not primarily a political category but a structural one: it is the illegitimate extension of one domain's authority into another. "Tyranny is the wish to have by one way what can only be had by another" (005-322). Each of the three orders — body, mind, charity — has its own proper sphere and mode of authority. Tyranny occurs when force claims the authority that belongs to justice, when eloquence claims the authority that belongs to truth, or when majority rule extends beyond its proper sphere. "On plurality is tyranny" (014-157). This concept integrates Pascal's political philosophy with his broader framework of the three orders.

## Key Passages

> "Tyranny is the wish to have by one way what can only be had by another. We render different dues to different merits: the due of love to charm; the due of fear to force; the due of belief to learning." (005-322)

> "We must relax our minds a little; but that opens the door to the highest tyranny." (006-137)

> "On plurality is tyranny. There is scarcely any other tyranny than that of opinion." (014-157)

> "That is why Christ has laid down the precept of not resisting tyranny." (014-165)

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of tyranny is a direct application of the three orders framework. The order of body operates by force, the order of mind by reason, and the order of charity by love. Each has its own "currency" and its own proper domain. Tyranny is the category mistake of applying one order's currency in another's domain: using force to compel belief (the Inquisition's error), using eloquence to substitute for truth (the sophist's error), or using majority rule to determine what is right (the democrat's error, in Pascal's view).

This analysis has profound implications. "On plurality is tyranny" is Pascal's critique of majoritarian legitimacy: numbers confer no authority in matters of truth or justice. The majority can determine *what is done* (force) but not *what is right* (justice). This is not an argument for authoritarianism but for domain-specific authority: each sphere has its own proper principles, and confusing them is the essence of tyranny.

The connection to opinion is crucial. Tyranny, in its most common form, is "the tyranny of opinion" — the social pressure that enforces conformity not through overt force but through the weight of collective judgment. This links tyranny to Pascal's analysis of custom and imagination: the social world is governed by opinion, and opinion is itself a form of tyranny when it claims more than conventional authority.

Christ's precept of non-resistance to tyranny (014-165) places the concept in a theological frame: the Christian response to tyranny is not counter-force but witness. This is consistent with Pascal's Jansenist emphasis on the order of charity, which transcends and relativizes the political order.
