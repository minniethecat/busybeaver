[[Nothingness|Nothingness]] (*néant*), in Pascal's [[anthropology|anthropology]], is the ontological truth of the [[human-condition|human condition]] — the state from which we flee and to which, without [[God|God]], we are destined.

> [002-136] Nothingness which we cannot reach? He who considers himself in this way will be afraid of himself.

The "nothingness which we cannot reach" is a precise formulation: the human being is suspended between being and non-being, too substantial to dissolve into nothing and too insubstantial to achieve the fullness of being it desires. This is the existential dimension of [[disproportion|disproportion]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Nothingness and Self-Knowledge

> [002-950] Without study. He then feels his nothingness, his forlornness, his insufficiency, his dependence, his impotence, his emptiness.

The list is cumulative, each term adding a dimension to the experience of nothingness: forlornness (the affective dimension), insufficiency (the practical dimension), dependence (the relational dimension), impotence (the agential dimension), emptiness (the ontological dimension). Together they constitute what Pascal means by *néant* — not a metaphysical category but a lived experience.

The crucial point is that this experience is not pathological. It is the accurate perception of the human situation. The person who "feels his nothingness" is not deluded — he is seeing clearly for the first time.

> [006-137] For I strive only to know my nothingness. The very thing that is most contrary to the knowledge of man.

Paradoxically, the knowledge of one's own nothingness is "most contrary" to natural human knowledge. The [[self-love|self]] resists the recognition of its nothingness with all its considerable resources of [[self-deception|self-deception]]. To "strive to know my nothingness" is to engage in a discipline of [[self-knowledge|self-knowledge]] that goes against the grain of the fallen [[psyche|psyche]].

### Nothingness Before God

> [003-661] That certainty of gain, so much nothingness in what you stake.

This is the language of the [[wager|Wager]]: the goods one stakes — finite life, finite pleasures, finite attachments — are "so much nothingness" compared to the [[infinity|infinite]] gain promised. The disproportion between stake and reward is not a rhetorical flourish but the logical conclusion of the analysis of nothingness: finite goods are, relative to the infinite, nothing.

> [003-611] The loss of nothingness.

If what one stakes is nothingness, then what one risks losing is also nothingness. The Wager argument exploits this: one risks nothing (because the finite life one stakes is already "nothing" relative to the infinite) for the chance of gaining everything.

### Nothingness and the Existential Situation

> [007-637] While they slept, both in their nothingness before their birth, and in their nothingness after their death.

The "nothingness before birth" and the "nothingness after death" bracket human existence at both ends. The human being emerges from nothingness and returns to nothingness — and the brief interval of existence between these two nothings is darkened by the recognition of its own contingency.

> [002-1075] A great proof of the nothingness of our being, not to be satisfied with the present, which is usually all we have.

The inability to rest in the present — the perpetual [[restlessness|restlessness]] that Pascal diagnoses as universal — is itself proof of nothingness. A being that possessed genuine [[being|being]] would be satisfied with its present; the fact that we are never satisfied reveals that we lack the being we aspire to.

The human being is created from nothing and retains, in its fallen condition, a tendency toward the nothing from which it came. This tendency is countered only by [[grace|grace]] — the supernatural gift that sustains being against its natural inclination toward non-being.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of nothingness has three interrelated functions in his thought.

First, it is diagnostic: the experience of nothingness — the sense of emptiness, insufficiency, and contingency — is a datum of [[human-nature|human experience]] that any adequate anthropology must account for. Pascal's claim is that [[christianity|Christianity]] alone does so, by locating the experience in the [[original-sin|Fall]]: the human being was created for fullness and, having lost it, experiences its absence as nothingness.

Second, it is strategic: the recognition of nothingness is the precondition for the turn to [[faith-and-reason|faith]]. As long as the [[self|self]] believes itself to possess being on its own, it will not seek the being that only God can give. The analysis of nothingness is therefore an essential moment in Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetics]].

Third, it is [[Christological|Christological]]: [[Jesus Christ|Christ]] entered the nothingness of the human condition — "He emptied himself" — in order to fill it. The nothingness of the human being without God is answered by the self-emptying of God in the [[incarnation|Incarnation]].
