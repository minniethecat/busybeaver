[[Wisdom|Wisdom]] (sagesse), in Pascal's [[theology|theology]], names the fundamental opposition between two orders of knowledge: the wisdom of [[god|God]] and the wisdom of the world. What the world counts as wisdom, Pascal argues, is folly before God; what the world calls foolishness — the [[cross|Cross]], the incarnation, the election of the humble — is the wisdom that saves.

> (007-471) foolishness is wiser than all the wisdom of men, sapientius est

The Latin citation (from 1 Corinthians) signals Pascal's Pauline inheritance: the wisdom of God is hidden from the wise and revealed to the simple. This paradox is the organising principle of Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetics]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Wisdom and Pride

Pascal identifies [[pride|pride]] as the chief obstacle to wisdom:

> (007-629) matters, inquiry specially; in wisdom, pride specially. Not

> (007-633) place for pride is in wisdom, for it cannot be granted to

The connection is precise: pride finds its "place" in wisdom because wisdom is the human excellence that most tempts to self-sufficiency. The philosopher who attains human wisdom is in greater danger than the fool who knows his ignorance. [[Scepticism|Scepticism]], for Pascal, is spiritually safer than dogmatic rationalism because it leaves room for [[faith|faith]].

> (007-635) is right. Now God alone gives wisdom, and that is

Wisdom is a gift — "God alone gives wisdom" — not an achievement. This is the theological axiom that distinguishes Pascalian wisdom from both classical philosophy and Cartesian rationalism. [[Grace|Grace]] is the condition of wisdom; without it, the wisest philosopher is foolish.

### Human Wisdom versus Divine Wisdom

Pascal draws the contrast sharply:

> (005-384) well founded, as the estimate of wisdom.

Human estimates of wisdom are unfounded because they misidentify the object. The world admires intellectual brilliance; [[god|God]] values [[humility|humility]]. Pascal's [[three-orders|three orders]] — body, [[mind|mind]], [[charity|charity]] — map onto three kinds of wisdom: practical prudence, philosophical wisdom, and the wisdom of the [[heart|heart]] that knows God.

> (006-119) degree of wisdom are equally foolish and vicio

No "degree of wisdom" — no incremental approach — can bridge the gap between human and divine wisdom. The difference is qualitative, not quantitative. This is why [[pascals-wager|the Wager]] does not argue the seeker into belief: it seeks to create the conditions in which divine wisdom can be received.

### Natural Wisdom and Scepticism

> (006-137) on the contrary, of natural wisdom. Nothing fortifies scepticism

Pascal makes the counterintuitive claim that "natural wisdom" — the highest achievement of unaided [[reason|reason]] — actually fortifies [[scepticism|scepticism]]. The more one knows, the more one sees how little one knows. Natural wisdom, pushed to its conclusion, undermines itself — and this self-undoing is the opening through which divine wisdom enters.

This is Pascal's version of the "learned ignorance" tradition: the wisest philosophers ([[Montaigne|Montaigne]] is Pascal's model) arrive at scepticism, and scepticism, honestly embraced, prepares for [[faith|faith]].

### The Wisdom of God in History

> (007-099) what the wisdom of God will do.

> (007-402) according to the wisdom of the world, but by expelling

God's wisdom operates in history not "according to the wisdom of the world" but by overturning worldly expectations. The election of the [[jews|Jewish people]], the incarnation, the Cross — each confounds human wisdom. Pascal's [[prophecy|prophetic]] apologetics traces this pattern through Scripture.

> (007-236) make yourselves like unto Him; wisdom will make you equal

The promise that "wisdom will make you equal" to God echoes the serpent's temptation in Eden. Pascal is alert to the demonic parody of true wisdom: the wisdom that promises self-divinisation is the wisdom of the Fall. True wisdom makes one like God by making one [[humility|humble]], not by making one autonomous.

### The Bread of Wisdom

> (007-513) bread of wisdom of which it is spoken in Proverbs

Pascal draws on the Wisdom literature of the Old Testament: wisdom is "bread" — nourishment, not decoration. The metaphor insists on wisdom's practical, life-sustaining character. Wisdom is not a speculative achievement but what sustains the [[soul|soul]].

### Wisdom and the Rejection of Christianity

> (008-227) all her miracles and all her wisdom, rejects all this, and declares

The world rejects Christianity despite its [[miracle|miracles]] and its wisdom — this very rejection is, for Pascal, predicted by Christianity itself. The [[jews|Jews]] rejected Christ; the philosophers reject the Cross. The pattern of rejection confirms rather than refutes the Christian diagnosis of the human condition.

### Divine Wisdom in Providence

> (009-457) things with so great wisdom, equity, and judgment, that

Pascal sees divine wisdom manifested in providential ordering — "so great wisdom, equity, and judgment." The [[hiddenness|hiddenness]] of God does not mean the absence of wisdom but its inscrutability to the unaided [[mind|mind]].

### The Fate of Human Wisdom

> (011-220) wisdom of their wise men shall perish

The prophecy that "the wisdom of their wise men shall perish" is Pascal's eschatological horizon. All human wisdom — philosophical, scientific, political — will be judged and found wanting. Only what participates in divine wisdom endures. This gives Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetics]] its urgency: the choice between two wisdoms is the choice between two eternities.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of wisdom is the epistemological counterpart to his treatment of [[greatness-and-misery|greatness and misery]]. Human wisdom is real — the achievements of [[reason|reason]] and [[science|science]] are genuine — but it is penultimate. To treat penultimate wisdom as ultimate is the intellectual form of [[pride|pride]].

The distinctiveness of Pascal's account is its refusal of both extremes. Against rationalism, he insists that human wisdom cannot reach [[god|God]]. Against fideism, he insists that human wisdom, honestly pursued, leads to the recognition of its own insufficiency — and this recognition is already the threshold of [[faith|faith]].

Wisdom's ultimate criterion is the [[cross|Cross]]. What the world calls wisdom is refuted by a crucified God; what the world calls folly is vindicated by the resurrection. Pascal's apologetics aims to bring the seeker to the point where this reversal becomes not absurd but inescapable.
