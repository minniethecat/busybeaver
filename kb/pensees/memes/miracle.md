[[Miracle|Miracles]], in Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetics]], are not optional supplements to the [[christianity|Christian]] case but essential evidence — the divine signature that authenticates the [[revelation|revelation]].

> [P01-256] Directed at Pascal's belief in miracles, which plays a central part in his scheme of apology.

Pascal's emphasis on miracles is not credulity but strategy: he recognises that [[reason|reason]] alone cannot establish the truth of Christianity, and that miracles provide a kind of evidence that addresses the whole person — [[heart|heart]] as well as [[mind|mind]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Criterion of Credibility

> [P01-260] Miracles to be true, and it would see that in fact the miracles were true.

Pascal is not arguing that any reported miracle should be believed. He argues for a criterion: the credibility of miracles is established by the character of those who witness to them. "I believe the stories whose witnesses let themselves be slaughtered" — the willingness of [[witness|witnesses]] to die for their testimony is evidence that the testimony is sincere.

> [P01-261] Because we believe more recent miracles to be true. We accept the miracles, or some miracles, to be true, attested by those who have seen them.

The chain of testimony is historical: recent miracles are attested by living witnesses, and these recent miracles authenticate the earlier ones. Pascal's epistemology of miracles is not a demand for personal experience but a case built on the credibility of transmitted testimony.

> [P01-264] Belief in the miracles on the Gospel, not our belief.

The distinction between believing the [[Gospel|Gospel]] *because* of miracles and believing miracles *because* of the Gospel is crucial. Pascal holds the former: miracles are evidence for the Gospel, not the other way around. But this evidence is not coercive — it persuades those who are open to persuasion, not those who are determined to resist.

### Miracles in the Apologetic Structure

> [P01-265] On the miracles. But it must be remembered that the miracles of the Gospel are not of a nature to be denied.

Pascal is aware of the [[skepticism|skeptical]] objection: miracles are inherently improbable, therefore miracle reports should be dismissed. His response is that the improbability of a miracle must be weighed against the improbability of the alternative — that the apostles died for what they knew to be false. Between these two improbabilities, Pascal judges the miracle more probable.

> [013-186] Has miracle, prophecy, doctrine, perpetuity, etc.

Miracles do not stand alone in Pascal's apologetic. They are one strand in a cumulative case that includes [[prophecy|prophecy]], [[doctrine|doctrine]], [[perpetuity|perpetuity]], and the testimony of the [[saints|saints]]. No single strand is decisive; together, they form a cord that cannot be broken.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of miracles exemplifies his broader apologetic strategy: address the whole person, not just the intellect.

The intellectual case for miracles establishes that belief in the Gospel miracles is *reasonable* — it does not require the sacrifice of [[reason|reason]]. But reason alone does not produce [[faith-and-reason|faith]]. Faith requires the movement of the [[will|will]], which reason can prepare but cannot compel.

This is why Pascal includes the famous counsel: "Bend the automaton" (*Pliez la machine*) — act as if you believe, and belief will follow. The [[automaton|automaton]] — the body, the habits, the whole non-rational apparatus of the person — must be engaged as well as the mind. Miracles address the automaton as well as the intellect: they are visible, tangible, public events that capture the [[imagination|imagination]].
