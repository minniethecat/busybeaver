# Rationalism

**Rationalism** — particularly in its Cartesian form — is a principal target of [[Blaise Pascal]]'s critique of philosophy. While Pascal was himself a mathematician and man of science, he rejected the rationalist claim that human reason alone can grasp ultimate truths about [[God]], the self, and the [[Human Condition|human condition]]. His criticism of [[René Descartes]] is among the most compressed and devastating in the history of philosophy.

## Against Descartes

Pascal's judgment on Descartes is severe and personal. In a famous fragment, he writes:

> I cannot forgive Descartes. In all his philosophy he would have been quite willing to dispense with God. But he had to make Him give a fillip to set the world in motion; beyond this, he has no further need of God. (002-375)

This captures Pascal's core objection to Cartesian rationalism: it reduces God to a metaphysical convenience, a first cause needed only to start the mechanism. Once the world is in motion, Descartes's system proceeds entirely without God — a "useless and uncertain" philosophy (002-380). Pascal saw this as not merely intellectually inadequate but spiritually dangerous: a philosophy that claims to explain everything while rendering God superfluous.

Pascal extends the criticism to Descartes's mechanistic physics: "We must say summarily: 'This is made by figure and motion,' for it is true. But to say what these are, and to compose the machine, is ridiculous. For it is useless, uncertain, and painful. And were it true, we do not think all philosophy is worth one hour of pain" (002-382). Even if Cartesian mechanism were correct in principle, the enterprise of constructing a complete rational account of nature is futile — it costs more in intellectual effort than it yields in genuine understanding.

## The Limits of Reason

Pascal does not reject reason itself — he was, after all, one of the great mathematical minds of his age. Rather, he insists on recognizing reason's proper limits. "This would doubtless suffice, if reason were reasonable," he writes of the philosophical search for truth (002-074). The central problem is not that reason is worthless but that it is insufficient: it cannot grasp first principles, it cannot decide the question of God, and it cannot motivate the will.

Reason's impotence is most evident in the religious sphere. The [[Faith|faith]] that Pascal seeks to evoke in his reader cannot be produced by rational argumentation alone. Reason may clear away obstacles and demonstrate the reasonableness of belief, but it cannot generate belief itself.

## The Heart's Reasons

Against the rationalist reduction of knowledge to deductive certainty, Pascal offers his most famous epistemological assertion:

> The heart has its reasons, which reason does not know. We feel it in a thousand things. (008-277)

The "heart" (*le cœur*) in Pascal's vocabulary is not mere emotion or sentiment. It is a faculty of intuitive knowledge — the capacity to grasp first principles immediately, without the mediation of syllogism. Just as we know space, time, and number by the heart rather than by demonstration, so we know God:

> It is the heart which experiences God, and not the reason. This, then, is faith: God felt by the heart, not by the reason. (008-278)

This is not irrationalism but a critique of the rationalist's overreach. Pascal's position is that reason has a domain within which it is sovereign (mathematics, natural science, logical inference), but that domain does not encompass the most important truths of human existence. To insist on rational proof for everything is itself unreasonable — it misunderstands the nature of both the knowing subject and the object known.

The introduction to the *Pensées* notes that Pascal "succeeds where Descartes fails" because Pascal recognizes the role of *esprit de finesse* — the intuitive, holistic grasp of complex realities — alongside *esprit de géométrie* — the deductive, analytic mode (P01-457). Descartes, for all his brilliance, relied exclusively on the latter.
