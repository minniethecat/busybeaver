# Incarnation

The **Incarnation** — God becoming man in the person of [[Jesus Christ in Pascal's Pensées|Jesus Christ]] — stands at the center of [[Blaise Pascal]]'s theology and his [[Apologetics|apologetic]] project. For Pascal, the Incarnation is not merely one doctrine among others; it is the foundational mystery without which neither [[God]] nor the [[Human Condition|human condition]] can be properly understood.

## The Mystery of the Two Natures

Pascal locates the essence of Christianity in "the mystery of the [[Redemption|Redeemer]], who, uniting in Himself the two natures, human and divine, has redeemed men from the [[Corruption|corruption]] of [[Sin|sin]] in order to reconcile them in His divine person to God" (008-034). This union of natures is the distinctive claim of Christian revelation: God enters human history, takes on human flesh, and through this act bridges the otherwise unbridgeable gulf between the Creator and fallen humanity.

The introduction to the *Pensées* describes how the believer, reasoning through the evidence of the moral world and the character of sainthood, finds himself "inexorably committed to the dogma of the Incarnation" (P01-227). This commitment is not the conclusion of a deductive proof but the culmination of what Pascal calls the heart's knowledge — a convergence of reasons that together make the Incarnation the most satisfactory explanation of human experience.

## The Incarnation and Human Self-Knowledge

For Pascal, the Incarnation is the key to genuine self-knowledge. The doctrine reveals human nature with a clarity that unaided philosophy cannot achieve:

> The Incarnation shows man the greatness of his misery by the greatness of the remedy which he required. (007-637)

This is Pascal's characteristic dialectical method. The [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness of the remedy]] — God Himself becoming man — demonstrates the depth of human [[Wretchedness|wretchedness]]. No lesser remedy would suffice. At the same time, that humanity is capable of receiving such a remedy, that God deemed humanity worth redeeming at such cost, reveals a corresponding greatness.

Pascal draws out the epistemological implication: "The knowledge of God without that of man's misery causes pride. The knowledge of man's misery without that of God causes despair. The knowledge of Jesus Christ constitutes the middle course, because in Him we find both God and our misery" (007-637). The Incarnation thus resolves the fundamental tension that runs through all of Pascal's anthropology — we are neither beasts nor angels, and only Christ reveals what we truly are.

## The Sole Mediator

A central function of the Incarnation is to provide the mediator without whom communion with God is impossible. Pascal insists that "there is an unconquerable opposition between us and God, and that without a mediator there can be no communion with Him" (007-637). The gulf created by [[Original Sin|original sin]] cannot be crossed by human effort, philosophical inquiry, or religious practice.

Pascal contrasts two ways of knowing God: the way of the philosophers, which leads to "communion without a mediator with the God whom they have known without a mediator," and the Christian way, in which "those who have known God by a mediator know their own wretchedness" (008-087). The mediator not only bridges the gap to God but also reveals the truth about the human condition itself. Without Christ, knowledge of God produces either [[Pride|pride]] (among those who think they can reach God by reason) or despair (among those who recognize their incapacity). The Incarnation offers a third way: approach to God through the one who is both God and man.
