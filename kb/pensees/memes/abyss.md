## Definition

In Pascal's philosophical imagination, the **abyss** (abîme, gouffre) is a **threefold image** that unites the cosmological, psychological, and theological dimensions of the human condition. Cosmologically, man is suspended between "two abysses of the Infinite and Nothing" (002-139) — the infinitely large and the infinitely small. Psychologically, the abyss is the void within the self that [[Diversion]] seeks to fill and that only an infinite object can satisfy. Theologically, the abyss is the depth of human [[Wretchedness]] from which only divine grace can rescue — and the depth of divine wisdom into which faith plunges. The abyss is not a metaphor for despair but for *depth*: the depth of the human predicament that is the necessary precondition for the height of divine remedy.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Two Abysses: Cosmological Disproportion

Pascal's most famous deployment of the abyss image occurs in the "Disproportion of Man" fragment, where he maps the human epistemological situation between two infinities:

> (002-139) Let him lose himself in wonders as amazing in their littleness as the others in their vastness. For who will not be astounded at the fact that our body, which a little ago was not perceptible in the universe, itself imperceptible in the bosom of the whole, is now a colossus, a world, or rather a whole, in respect of the nothingness which we cannot reach? ... He who regards himself in this light will be afraid at the sight of himself, and observing himself sustained in the body given him by nature between these two abysses of the Infinite and Nothing, will tremble at the sight of these marvels.

The "two abysses" are the infinitely large (the cosmic scale at which man disappears) and the infinitely small (the microscopic scale at which man is a universe). Man is neither infinite nor nothing but suspended between them, capable of perceiving both abysses without being able to reach either. This is the cosmological foundation of Pascal's epistemological [[Disproportion]]: man is not proportioned to reality. The abyss is not a place but a *relation* — the relation of disproportion between the knower and the known.

### The New Abyss: Reflexive Depth

Pascal escalates the abyss imagery by turning it reflexive:

> (002-123) Let him see therein a new abyss. I will paint for him not only the visible universe, but all that he can conceive of nature's immensity in the enclosure of this diminished atom.

The "new abyss" is the abyss opened by reflection itself. When man contemplates his own position between the infinities, a further abyss opens: the abyss of self-consciousness. Not only is man lost in the cosmos, but he *knows* that he is lost. This reflexive turn is characteristic of Pascal's existential method: he does not merely describe the human condition but makes the reader *feel* the description. The new abyss is the one that opens *within* the reader as he reads.

### The Abyss Within: The Heart's Infinite Void

Pascal's most existentially charged use of the abyss image is psychological and theological:

> (007-030) What else does this craving, and this helplessness, proclaim but that there was once in man a true happiness, of which all that now remains is the empty print and trace? This he tries in vain to fill with everything around him, seeking in things that are not there the help he cannot find in those that are, though none can help, since this infinite abyss can be filled only with an infinite and immutable object; in other words by God himself.

This is the abyss that defines Pascal's religious psychology. The human heart contains an "infinite abyss" — a void of infinite depth that nothing finite can fill. All human pursuits — pleasure, power, knowledge, [[Diversion]] — are attempts to fill this abyss with finite objects. They fail necessarily, because the abyss is infinite and only an infinite object can fill it. This argument is the psychological counterpart to the [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]]: just as the Wager shows that belief is rational because the stakes are infinite, the infinite abyss shows that belief is necessary because only the infinite God satisfies the infinite desire. [[Despair]] is the condition of the soul that has recognized the abyss and refuses its only adequate object.

### The Abyss as Danger and Judgment

Pascal also uses the abyss image in its more traditional sense of danger and destruction:

> (002-236) The earth opens to abysses.

This fragment, brief and evocative, recalls the biblical imagery of the earth swallowing the rebellious (Numbers 16:31–33). The abyss is not only the depth of human desire but also the depth of divine judgment. Pascal does not allow the psychological and cosmological uses of the image to soften its traditional association with peril. To be suspended between abysses is to be in danger of falling.

### The Twists and Turns of the Abyss

Finally, Pascal uses the abyss to characterize the opacity of the human condition to rational analysis:

> (007-359) Its twists and turns in this abyss, so that man is more incomprehensible without this mystery than this mystery is incomprehensible to man.

The "abyss" here is the mystery of [[Original Sin]] — the doctrine that alone, for Pascal, makes sense of the contradictory data of human nature. Without the Fall, man is an unintelligible chaos. The doctrine of the Fall is itself mysterious (an "abyss" of divine wisdom), but it is a mystery that *explains* — it renders the human abyss intelligible as the consequence of a primordial catastrophe. Pascal's rhetoric inverts the usual objection: it is not Christianity that is incomprehensible but man without Christianity.

## Analysis

The abyss is Pascal's master image for the human condition because it captures, in a single figure, the threefold structure of his thought: cosmological disproportion, psychological desire, and theological dependence. The three dimensions are not separate. The cosmological abyss (man between infinities) produces the psychological abyss (the sense of being lost, the terror described in 007-030). The psychological abyss in turn points to the theological abyss: only the infinite God can fill the infinite void. Pascal's apologetics moves along this chain: from the experience of disproportion to the recognition of desire to the need for God.

The abyss is also the ground of Pascal's critique of [[Diversion]]. Diversion is the sum of human activities — work, play, war, philosophy — by which we distract ourselves from the abyss. But diversion does not fill the abyss; it only conceals it. The function of Pascal's rhetoric is to make the abyss *visible again* — to strip away the distractions so that the reader confronts the void directly. This is the most uncomfortable moment in Pascal's apologetics, and he does not soften it. The terror of the abyss is the prelude to the joy of faith.

The existentialist readings of Pascal (Chestov, Marcel, Camus) have rightly emphasized the abyss as the point of contact between Pascal and modern thought. The experience of the abyss — the sense of being lost in an indifferent universe, of having infinite desires and finite capacities — is the common ground between Pascal's Christian apologetics and modern atheistic existentialism. Where Pascal and the existentialists part ways is at the question of whether the abyss can be filled. Pascal answers yes; the atheist existentialist answers no. But the diagnosis is shared.
