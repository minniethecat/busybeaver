[[Human Condition|The human condition]], as Pascal analyses it in the *Pensees*, is characterised by three interlocking realities: the inevitability of [[death|death]], the incapacity for rest, and the restless search for [[diversion|diversion]] that masks an underlying despair.

Pascal's account of the human condition is among the most unsparing in the history of philosophy. He refuses every consolation that does not correspond to the facts, and the facts, as he sees them, are bleak: human beings are finite creatures with infinite desires, born into a universe that does not answer their deepest questions, destined for a death they cannot escape.

## Diversion and Its Discontents

The concept of *divertissement* (diversion) is central to Pascal's diagnosis. Human beings, he observes, cannot bear to be alone with themselves:

> [002-950] Diversion. Without study, man feels his nothingness, his loneliness, his insufficiency, his dependence, his weakness, his emptiness.

The pursuit of [[diversion|diversion]] — in hunting, gambling, conversation, war, or any activity that occupies the mind — is not a sign of vitality but of a desperate flight from the truth of one's own condition. The [[human-condition|human]] being without diversion is unhappy:

> [002-1015] Without what is called diversion, he is unhappy, and more unhappy than the least of men who divert themselves.

## Misery and Greatness

The human condition is defined by the tension between aspiration and capacity:

> [003-134] What is it to be in a state of nothing but hopeless misery?

Pascal's answer is that this [[misery|misery]] is evidence not of meaninglessness but of a lost vocation. The human being is miserable because it was made for something it has lost:

> [007-432] They seek happiness, and find only misery and death.

Yet the very capacity to recognise one's own misery is a mark of residual [[greatness|greatness]] — a creature wholly base would not know itself to be base, and a creature wholly grand would not be base at all. The [[human-condition|human condition]] is precisely this: to be suspended between beast and [[God|God]], belonging fully to neither.
