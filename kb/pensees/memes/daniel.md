Daniel is one of the most frequently cited prophets in Pascal's *Pensées*, appearing as a cornerstone of the apologetic argument from fulfilled prophecy. Pascal draws on Daniel's visions — particularly the seventy weeks prophecy and the succession of kingdoms — to establish the chronological framework for the coming of the Messiah.

## Daniel in Pascal's Apologetic

### The Seventy Weeks Prophecy

The seventy weeks prophecy of Daniel is central to Pascal's messianic chronology. He acknowledges its difficulty — "the seventy weeks of Daniel are ambiguous" (011-644) — while insisting on its explanatory power. Pascal traces the timeline with precision: "in the seventieth week of Daniel, during the continuance" of the prophetic period, the Messiah was to appear (011-652). The endpoint is clear: "the seventy weeks of Daniel were ended; and that the scepter" had departed from Judah (012-045), marking the precise moment of Christ's coming.

### The Vision of the Four Kingdoms

Pascal invokes Daniel's vision of the four kingdoms (Daniel ii) as a framework for understanding world history under providence. He cites Daniel's rebuke of the Babylonian wise men — "All thy soothsayers and wise men cannot declare unto the king the mystery" (011-443) — to contrast human wisdom with divine revelation. The succession of empires in Daniel's vision demonstrates that secular history is governed by a divine plan.

### The Angelic Revelation

Pascal highlights Daniel's personal encounter with the interpreting angel as a model of prophetic reception. He quotes "Daniel having seen... He sought the meaning of" the vision (011-488), and the angel's response: "This is what Daniel saw. He sought the meaning of" (011-498). The dramatic scene of Daniel's prayer and the angel's arrival — "Whilst I was praying with all my heart... Daniel, I am now come forth to give thee knowledge" (011-514, 011-519) — illustrates Pascal's theme that divine truth requires both human seeking and divine disclosure.

### The Sealed Book

Pascal draws on Daniel xii for one of his most important hermeneutical principles: "The wicked shall not understand, but the wise shall understand" (011-205). This verse anchors Pascal's doctrine of the two orders of understanding — the carnal and the spiritual — and supports his argument that prophecy is deliberately obscure to those without faith while transparent to believers.

## The Prayer of Daniel

Pascal notes Daniel's intercessory role, citing "Daniel (ix) prays for the deliverance of the people from captivity" (010-190). This connects Daniel to the broader theme of prophetic intercession and the typological relationship between Israel's exile and humanity's spiritual captivity.

## Significance in the Pensées

Daniel serves a uniquely structural role in Pascal's argument. More than any other prophet, Daniel provides the chronological architecture — the seventy weeks, the succession of kingdoms, the sealed book — that allows Pascal to present prophecy not as vague prediction but as a mathematically precise demonstration of divine providence. Daniel's visions transform sacred history from a collection of events into an intelligible order, bridging the Old Testament prophets with the New Testament fulfillment.
