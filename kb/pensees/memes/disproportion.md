[[Disproportion|Disproportion]] is Pascal's term for the constitutive mismatch between the [[human-nature|human]] capacity for [[knowledge|knowledge]] and the reality it seeks to know.

> [002-082] Man's disproportion. — This is where our innate knowledge leads us.

The disproportion is not a temporary condition that better [[science|science]] or [[philosophy|philosophy]] might overcome. It is structural — built into the relationship between a finite knower and an infinite reality. Recognising this disproportion is, for Pascal, the beginning of [[wisdom|wisdom]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Two Infinities

> [002-104] It is an infinite sphere, the centre of which is everywhere, the circumference nowhere.

This celebrated image — borrowed from the Hermetic tradition but transformed by Pascal — captures the human cognitive situation: we are at the centre of a universe that has no boundary, no fixed point from which it can be surveyed. Every attempt to grasp the whole is doomed by the very structure of the cognitive situation.

Pascal's argument proceeds by directing attention first to the infinitely small, then to the infinitely great. In both directions, the mind encounters its limits:

> [002-070] The infinites, the mean. — When we read too quickly or too slowly, we understand nothing.

The "mean" is not a moderate middle but the precise proportion proper to human cognition. The problem is that this mean is itself invisible from within — we cannot step outside our cognitive apparatus to calibrate it.

### Man as the Mean

> [002-146] A mean between nothing and everything.

The human being is situated between two infinities: the nothingness from which it emerges and the everything it aspires to comprehend. This position gives the human being a unique dignity — only a being situated between infinities can recognise its own situation — and a unique misery — the recognition comes with no power to transcend the situation.

> [002-208] The mean between two extremes is present in all our impotence.

The "mean" here is not Aristotelian virtue but Pascalian predicament: to be in the middle is to be incapable of reaching either end. We can conceive of the infinite but cannot grasp it; we can recognise nothingness but cannot annihilate ourselves into it.

## Key Passages

> [002-126] We see therein an infinity of universes, each of which has its firmament, its planets, its earth, in the same proportion as the visible world.

The infinite regression of scale — from the visible world down to the mite, from the mite to the atom, from the atom to the void — mirrors the infinite progression upward to the stars and beyond. In both directions, reason finds no resting place.

> [003-525] Not so great a disproportion between our justice and that of God as between our pleasure and that of God.

The concept of disproportion extends beyond [[epistemology|epistemology]] into [[ethics|ethics]] and [[theology|theology]]. Our [[justice|justice]] is disproportionate to God's justice — but even this disproportion is itself proportioned: the gap between divine and human pleasure is greater than the gap between divine and human justice. Pascal's rhetorical strategy is to use disproportion itself as a tool of measurement.

> [013-654] Whose presence it happens, and there is a disproportion between the proof and the thing.

The apologetic application is direct: the [[proof|proofs]] of [[christianity|Christianity]] are not proportional to the reality they point to. This is not a defect of the proofs but a feature of the reality: a God who could be proportionately proved would not be God.

> [014-120] The example of the saints, as disproportioned to our state.

The [[saint|saints]] are "disproportioned" to ordinary human existence — they belong to a different [[three-orders|order]]. Their lives are not merely better versions of ordinary lives; they are qualitatively different, belonging to the order of [[charity|charity]] rather than the order of [[nature|nature]] or the order of [[reason|the mind]].

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of disproportion operates at three levels.

First, it is an epistemological claim: the disproportion between the knowing subject and the object of knowledge guarantees that [[certainty|certainty]] — the kind of certainty that [[dogmatism|dogmatists]] claim and [[skepticism|skeptics]] deny — is unattainable. But this is not skepticism: the disproportion is a feature of reality, not a failure of method. The appropriate response is not to abandon knowledge but to recognise its limits.

Second, it is an anthropological claim: the human being is itself a disproportion — too great to be merely animal, too small to be divine. This is the paradox that [[greatness-and-misery|greatness and misery]] elaborates. Disproportion is the spatial metaphor for the temporal and existential condition that greatness and misery names.

Third, it is an apologetic claim: the disproportion between human reason and divine reality is itself evidence for [[christianity|Christianity]]. A religion that presented a God perfectly proportioned to human reason would be, by that very fact, false — for it would be a God made in the image of human cognitive capacities rather than the God who exceeds them.

> [002-136] Nothingness which we cannot reach? He who considers himself in this way will be afraid of himself.

The proper response to the recognition of disproportion is [[fear|fear]] — not terror but the reverence that comes from understanding one's true position in the order of things. This fear is, for Pascal, the beginning of the movement toward [[faith-and-reason|faith]].
