[007-001] SECTION VII

[007-002] MORALITY AND DOCTRINE

[007-003] 425

[007-004] Second part.—That man without faith cannot know the true

[007-005] good, nor justice.

[007-006] All men seek happiness. This is without exception. Whatever

[007-007] different means they employ, they all tend to this end.[159]

[007-008] The cause of some going to war, and of others avoiding it, is the

[007-009] same desire in both, attended with different views. The will

[007-010] never takes the least step but to this object. This is the motive

[007-011] of every action of every man, even of those who hang themselves.

[007-012] And yet after such a great number of years, no one without

[007-013] faith has reached the point to which all continually look. All

[007-014] complain, princes and subjects, noblemen and commoners, old

[007-015] and young, strong and weak, learned and ignorant, healthy and

[007-016] sick, of all countries, all times, all ages, and all conditions.

[007-017] A trial so long, so continuous, and so uniform, should certainly

[007-018] convince us of our inability to reach the good by our own efforts.

[007-019] But example teaches us little. No resemblance is ever so perfect

[007-020] that there is not some slight difference; and hence we expect

[007-021] that our hope will not be deceived on this occasion as before.

[007-022] And thus, while the present never satisfies us, experience dupes

[007-023] us, and from misfortune to misfortune leads us to death, their

[007-024] [[eternity|eternal]] crown.

[007-025] What is it then that this desire and this inability proclaim to

[007-026] us, but that there was once in man a true happiness of which

[007-027] there now remain to him only the mark and empty trace, which

[007-028] he in vain tries to fill from all his surroundings, seeking from

[007-029] things absent the help he does not obtain in things present?

[007-030] But these are all inadequate, because [[infinite|the infinite]] abyss can

[007-031] only be filled by an infinite and immutable object, that is to say,

[007-032] only by [[god|God]] Himself.

[007-033] He only is our true good, and since we have forsaken Him, it

[007-034] is a strange thing that there is nothing in nature which has not

[007-035] been serviceable in taking His place; the stars, the heavens,

[007-036] earth, the elements, plants, cabbages, leeks, animals, insects,

[007-037] calves, serpents, fever, pestilence, war, famine, vices, adultery,

[007-038] incest. And since man has lost the true good, everything can

[007-039] appear equally good to him, even his own destruction, though so

[007-040] opposed to God, to reason, and to the whole course of nature.

[007-041] Some seek good in authority, others in scientific research,

[007-042] others in pleasure. Others, who are in fact nearer the truth,

[007-043] have considered it necessary that the universal good, which all

[007-044] men desire, should not consist in any of the particular things

[007-045] which can only be possessed by one man, and which, when

[007-046] shared, afflict their possessor more by the want of the part he has

[007-047] not, than they please him by the possession of what he has.

[007-048] They have learned that the true good should be such as all can

[007-049] possess at once, without diminution and without envy, and

[007-050] which no one can lose against his will. And their reason is that

[007-051] this desire being natural to man, since it is necessarily in all,

[007-052] and that it is impossible not to have it, they infer from it ...

[007-053] 426

[007-054] True nature being lost, everything becomes its own nature;

[007-055] as the true good being lost, everything becomes its own true good.

[007-056] 427

[007-057] Man does not know in what rank to place himself. He has

[007-058] plainly gone astray, and fallen from his true place without being

[007-059] able to find it again. He seeks it anxiously and unsuccessfully

[007-060] everywhere in impenetrable darkness.

[007-061] 428

[007-062] If it is a sign of weakness to prove God by nature, do not

[007-063] despise [[scripture|Scripture]]; if it is a sign of strength to have known these

[007-064] [[contradiction|contradictions]], esteem Scripture.

[007-066] The vileness of man in submitting himself to the brutes, and

[007-067] in even worshipping them.

[007-068] 430

[007-069] For Port Royal. The beginning, after having explained the

[007-070] incomprehensibility.—The greatness and the wretchedness of

[007-071] man are so evident that the true religion must necessarily teach

[007-072] us both that there is in man some great source of greatness, and

[007-073] a great source of wretchedness. It must then give us a reason

[007-074] for these astonishing contradictions.

[007-075] In order to make man happy, it must prove to him that there

[007-076] is a God; that we ought to love Him; that our true happiness is

[007-077] to be in Him, and our sole evil to be separated from Him; it

[007-078] must recognise that we are full of darkness which hinders us

[007-079] from [[knowledge|knowing]] and loving Him; and that thus, as our duties

[007-080] compel us to love God, and our lusts turn us away from Him,

[007-081] we are full of unrighteousness. It must give us an explanation

[007-082] of our opposition to God and to our own good. It must teach

[007-083] us the remedies for these infirmities, and the means of obtaining

[007-084] these remedies. Let us therefore examine all the religions of

[007-085] the world, and see if there be any other than the Christian which

[007-086] is sufficient for this purpose.

[007-087] Shall it be that of the [[philosophy|philosophers]], who put forward as the

[007-088] chief good, the good which is in ourselves? Is this the true good?

[007-089] Have they found the remedy for our ills? Is man's pride cured

[007-090] by placing him on an equality with God? Have those who have

[007-091] made us equal to the brutes, or the Mahommedans who have

[007-092] offered us earthly pleasures as the chief good even in eternity,

[007-093] produced the remedy for our lusts? What religion, then, will

[007-094] teach us to cure pride and lust? What religion will in fact teach

[007-095] us our good, our duties, the weakness which turns us from them,

[007-096] the cause of this weakness, the remedies which can cure it, and

[007-097] the means of obtaining these remedies?

[007-098] All other religions have not been able to do so. Let us see

[007-099] what the wisdom of God will do.

[007-100] "Expect neither truth," she says, "nor consolation from men.

[007-101] I am she who formed you, and who alone can teach you what

[007-102] you are. But you are now no longer in the state in which I

[007-103] formed you. I created man holy, innocent, perfect. I filled

[007-104] him with light and intelligence. I communicated to him my

[007-105] glory and my wonders. The eye of man saw then the majesty

[007-106] of God. He was not then in the darkness which blinds him, nor

[007-107] subject to [[death|mortality]] and the woes which afflict him. But he

[007-108] has not been able to sustain so great glory without falling into

[007-109] pride. He wanted to make himself his own centre, and independent

[007-110] of my help. He withdrew himself from my rule; and,

[007-111] on his making himself equal to me by the desire of finding his

[007-112] happiness in himself, I abandoned him to himself. And setting

[007-113] in revolt the creatures that were subject to him, I made them

[007-114] his enemies; so that man is now become like the brutes, and so

[007-115] estranged from me that there scarce remains to him a dim vision

[007-116] of his Author. So far has all his knowledge been extinguished

[007-117] or disturbed! The senses, independent of reason, and often the

[007-118] masters of reason, have led him into pursuit of pleasure. All

[007-119] creatures either torment or tempt him, and domineer over him,

[007-120] either subduing him by their strength, or fascinating him by

[007-121] their charms, a tyranny more awful and more imperious.

[007-122] "Such is the state in which men now are. There remains to

[007-123] them some feeble instinct of the happiness of their former state;

[007-124] and they are plunged in the evils of their blindness and their

[007-125] lust, which have become their [[custom|second nature]].

[007-126] "From this principle which I disclose to you, you can recognise

[007-127] the cause of those contradictions which have astonished all men,

[007-128] and have divided them into parties holding so different views.

[007-129] Observe, now, all the feelings of greatness and glory which the

[007-130] experience of so many woes cannot stifle, and see if the cause of

[007-131] them must not be in another nature."

[007-132] For [[port-royal|Port-Royal]] to-morrow (Prosopopœa).—"It is in vain, O

[007-133] men, that you seek within yourselves the remedy for your ills.

[007-134] All your light can only reach the knowledge that not in yourselves

[007-135] will you find truth or good. The philosophers have

[007-136] promised you that, and have been unable to do it. They

[007-137] neither know what is your true good, nor what is your true

[007-138] state. How could they have given remedies for your ills, when

[007-139] they did not even know them? Your chief maladies are pride,

[007-140] which takes you away from God, and lust, which binds you to

[007-141] earth; and they have done nothing else but cherish one or other

[007-142] of these diseases. If they gave you God as an end, it was only

[007-143] to administer to your pride; they made you think that you are

[007-144] by nature like Him, and conformed to Him. And those who

[007-145] saw the absurdity of this claim put you on another precipice, by

[007-146] making you understand that your nature was like that of the

[007-147] brutes, and led you to seek your good in the lusts which are

[007-148] shared by the animals. This is not the way to cure you of your

[007-149] unrighteousness, which these wise men never knew. I alone

[007-150] can make you understand who you are...."

[007-151] [[adam|Adam]], [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]].

[007-152] If you are united to God, it is by grace, not by nature. If you

[007-153] are humbled, it is by penitence, not by nature.

[007-154] Thus this double capacity ...

[007-155] You are not in the state of your creation.

[007-156] As these two states are open, it is impossible for you not to

[007-157] recognise them. Follow your own feelings, observe yourselves,

[007-158] and see if you do not find the lively characteristics of these two

[007-159] natures. Could so many contradictions be found in a simple

[007-160] subject?

[007-161] —Incomprehensible.—Not all that is incomprehensible ceases

[007-162] to exist. Infinite number. An infinite space equal to a finite.

[007-163] —Incredible that God should unite Himself to us.—This

[007-164] consideration is drawn only from the sight of our vileness. But

[007-165] if you are quite sincere over it, follow it as far as I have done,

[007-166] and recognise that we are indeed so vile that we are incapable in

[007-167] ourselves of knowing if His mercy cannot make us capable of

[007-168] Him. For I would know how this animal, who knows himself

[007-169] to be so weak, has the right to measure the mercy of God, and

[007-170] set limits to it, suggested by his own fancy. He has so little

[007-171] knowledge of what God is, that he does not know what he himself

[007-172] is, and, completely disturbed at the sight of his own state,

[007-173] dares to say that God cannot make him capable of communion

[007-174] with Him.

[007-175] But I would ask him if God demands anything else from him

[007-176] than the knowledge and love of Him, and why, since his nature

[007-177] is capable of love and knowledge, he believes that God cannot

[007-178] make Himself known and loved by him. Doubtless he knows

[007-179] at least that he exists, and that he loves something. Therefore,

[007-180] if he sees anything in the darkness wherein he is, and if he finds

[007-181] some object of his love among the things on earth, why, if God

[007-182] impart to him some ray of His essence, will he not be capable of

[007-183] knowing and of loving Him in the manner in which it shall please

[007-184] Him to communicate Himself to us? There must then be

[007-185] certainly an intolerable presumption in arguments of this sort,

[007-186] although they seem founded on an apparent humility, which is

[007-187] neither sincere nor reasonable, if it does not make us admit that,

[007-188] not knowing of ourselves what we are, we can only learn it

[007-189] from God.

[007-190] "I do not mean that you should submit your [[faith|belief]] to me

[007-191] without reason, and I do not aspire to overcome you by tyranny.

[007-192] In fact, I do not claim to give you a reason for everything. And

[007-193] to reconcile these contradictions, I intend to make you see

[007-194] clearly, by convincing proofs, those divine signs in me, which

[007-195] may convince you of what I am, and may gain authority for me

[007-196] by wonders and proofs which you cannot reject; so that you may

[007-197] then believe without ... the things which I teach you, since you

[007-198] will find no other ground for rejecting them, except that you

[007-199] cannot know of yourselves if they are true or not.

[007-200] "God has willed to redeem men, and to open salvation to

[007-201] those who seek it. But men render themselves so unworthy of

[007-202] it, that it is right that God should refuse to some, because of their

[007-203] obduracy, what He grants to others from a compassion which is

[007-204] not due to them. If He had willed to overcome the obstinacy

[007-205] of the most hardened, He could have done so by revealing Himself

[007-206] so manifestly to them that they could not have doubted of

[007-207] the truth of His essence; as it will appear at the last day, with

[007-208] such thunders and such a convulsion of nature, that the dead

[007-209] will rise again, and the blindest will see Him.

[007-210] "It is not in this manner that He has willed to appear in His

[007-211] advent of mercy, because, as so many make themselves unworthy

[007-212] of His mercy, He has willed to leave them in the loss of

[007-213] the good which they do not want. It was not then right that He

[007-214] should appear in a manner manifestly divine, and completely

[007-215] capable of convincing all men; but it was also not right that He

[007-216] should come in so hidden a manner that He could not be known

[007-217] by those who should sincerely seek Him. He has willed to make

[007-218] Himself quite recognisable by those; and thus, willing to appear

[007-219] openly to those who seek Him with all their heart, and to be

[007-220] hidden from those who flee from Him with all their heart, He

[007-221] so regulates the knowledge of Himself that He has given signs of

[007-222] Himself, visible to those who seek Him, and not to those who

[007-223] seek Him not. There is enough light for those who only desire

[007-224] to see, and enough [[evidence|obscurity]] for those who have a contrary

[007-225] disposition."

[007-226] 431

[007-227] No other religion has recognised that man is the most excellent

[007-228] creature. Some, which have quite recognised the reality of his

[007-229] excellence, have considered as mean and ungrateful the low

[007-230] opinions which men naturally have of themselves; and others,

[007-231] which have thoroughly recognised how real is this vileness, have

[007-232] treated with proud ridicule those feelings of greatness, which

[007-233] are equally natural to man.

[007-234] "Lift your eyes to God," say the first; "see Him whom you

[007-235] resemble, and who has created you to worship Him. You can

[007-236] make yourselves like unto Him; wisdom will make you equal

[007-237] to Him, if you will follow it." "Raise your heads, free men,"

[007-238] says [[epictetus|Epictetus]]. And others say, "Bend your eyes to the earth,

[007-239] wretched worm that you are, and consider the brutes whose

[007-240] companion you are."

[007-241] What, then, will man become? [[will|Will]] he be equal to God or the

[007-242] brutes? What a frightful difference! What, then, shall we be?

[007-243] Who does not see from all this that man has gone astray, that he

[007-244] has fallen from his place, that he anxiously seeks it, that he

[007-245] cannot find it again? And who shall then direct him to it? The

[007-246] greatest men have failed.

[007-247] 432

[007-248] Scepticism is true; for, after all, men before Jesus Christ did

[007-249] not know where they were, nor whether they were great or

[007-250] small. And those who have said the one or the other, knew

[007-251] nothing about it, and guessed without reason and by chance.

[007-252] They also erred always in excluding the one or the other.

[007-253] Quod ergo ignorantes, quæritis, religio annuntiat vobis.[160]

[007-255] After having understood the whole [[human-nature|nature of man]].—That a

[007-256] religion may be true, it must have knowledge of our nature.

[007-257] It ought to know its greatness and littleness, and the reason of

[007-258] both. What religion but the Christian has known this?

[007-259] 434

[007-260] The chief arguments of the sceptics—I pass over the lesser

[007-261] ones—are that we have no certainty of the truth of these

[007-262] principles apart from faith and revelation, except in so far as

[007-263] we naturally perceive them in ourselves. Now this natural

[007-264] intuition is not a convincing proof of their truth; since, having

[007-265] no certainty, apart from faith, whether man was created by a

[007-266] good God, or by a wicked demon,[161] or by chance, it is doubtful

[007-267] whether these principles given to us are true, or false, or uncertain,

[007-268] according to our origin. Again, no person is certain, apart from

[007-269] faith, whether he is awake or sleeps, seeing that during sleep we

[007-270] believe that we are awake as firmly as we do when we are awake;

[007-271] we believe that we see space, figure, and motion; we are aware

[007-272] of the passage of time, we measure it; and in fact we act as if

[007-273] we were awake. So that half of our life being passed in sleep, we

[007-274] have on our own admission no idea of truth, whatever we may

[007-275] imagine. As all our intuitions are then illusions, who knows

[007-276] whether the other half of our life, in which we think we are awake,

[007-277] is not another sleep a little different from the former, from which

[007-278] we awake when we suppose ourselves asleep?

[007-279] [And who doubts that, if we dreamt in company, and the

[007-280] dreams chanced to agree, which is common enough, and if we

[007-281] were always alone when awake, we should believe that matters

[007-282] were reversed? In short, as we often dream that we dream,

[007-283] heaping dream upon dream, may it not be that this half of our

[007-284] life, wherein we think ourselves awake, is itself only a dream on

[007-285] which the others are grafted, from which we wake at death,

[007-286] during which we have as few principles of truth and good as

[007-287] during natural sleep, these different thoughts which disturb us

[007-288] being perhaps only illusions like the flight of time and the vain

[007-289] fancies of our dreams?]

[007-290] These are the chief arguments on one side and the other.

[007-291] I omit minor ones, such as the sceptical talk against the

[007-292] impressions of custom, education, manners, country, and the

[007-293] like. Though these influence the majority of common folk,

[007-294] who dogmatise only on shallow foundations, they are upset by

[007-295] the least breath of the sceptics. We have only to see their

[007-296] books if we are not sufficiently convinced of this, and we shall

[007-297] very quickly become so, perhaps too much.

[007-298] I notice the only strong point of the dogmatists, namely, that,

[007-299] speaking in good faith and sincerely, we cannot [[skepticism|doubt]] natural

[007-300] principles. Against this the sceptics set up in one word the

[007-301] uncertainty of our origin, which includes that of our nature.

[007-302] The dogmatists have been trying to answer this objection ever

[007-303] since the world began.

[007-304] So there is open war among men, in which each must take a

[007-305] part, and side either with dogmatism or scepticism. For he

[007-306] who thinks to remain neutral is above all a sceptic. This

[007-307] neutrality is the essence of the sect; he who is not against them

[007-308] is essentially for them. [In this appears their advantage.]

[007-309] They are not for themselves; they are neutral, indifferent, in

[007-310] suspense as to all things, even themselves being no exception.

[007-311] What then shall man do in this state? Shall he doubt everything?

[007-312] Shall he doubt whether he is awake, whether he is being

[007-313] pinched, or whether he is being burned? Shall he doubt whether

[007-314] he doubts? Shall he doubt whether he exists? We cannot go so

[007-315] far as that; and I lay it down as a fact that there never has been

[007-316] a real complete sceptic. [[nature|Nature]] sustains our feeble reason, and

[007-317] prevents it raving to this extent.

[007-318] Shall he then say, on the contrary, that he certainly possesses

[007-319] truth—he who, when pressed ever so little, can show no title to

[007-320] it, and is forced to let go his hold?

[007-321] What a chimera then is man! What a novelty! What a

[007-322] monster, what a chaos, what a contradiction, what a prodigy!

[007-323] Judge of all things, imbecile worm of the earth; depositary of

[007-324] truth, a sink of uncertainty and error; the pride and refuse of

[007-325] the universe!

[007-326] Who will unravel this tangle? Nature confutes the sceptics,

[007-327] and reason confutes the dogmatists. What then will you

[007-328] become, O men! who try to find out by your natural reason what

[007-329] is your true condition? You cannot avoid one of these sects, nor

[007-330] adhere to one of them.

[007-331] Know then, proud man, what a paradox you are to yourself.

[007-332] Humble yourself, weak reason; be silent, foolish nature; learn

[007-333] that man infinitely transcends man, and learn from your Master

[007-334] your true condition, of which you are ignorant. Hear God.

[007-335] For in fact, if man had never been corrupt, he would enjoy

[007-336] in his innocence both truth and happiness with assurance; and

[007-337] if man had always been corrupt, he would have no idea of truth

[007-338] or bliss. But, wretched as we are, and more so than if there

[007-339] were no greatness in our condition, we have an idea of happiness,

[007-340] and cannot reach it. We perceive an image of truth, and possess

[007-341] only a lie. Incapable of absolute ignorance and of certain

[007-342] knowledge, we have thus been manifestly in a degree of perfection

[007-343] from which we have unhappily fallen.

[007-344] It is, however, an astonishing thing that the mystery furthest

[007-345] removed from our knowledge, namely, that of the transmission

[007-346] of sin, should be a fact without which we can have no knowledge

[007-347] of ourselves. For it is beyond doubt that there is nothing which

[007-348] more shocks our reason than to say that the sin of the first man

[007-349] has rendered guilty those, who, being so removed from this source,

[007-350] seem incapable of participation in it. This transmission does

[007-351] not only seem to us impossible, it seems also very unjust. For

[007-352] what is more contrary to the rules of our miserable justice than

[007-353] to damn eternally an infant incapable of will, for a sin wherein

[007-354] he seems to have so little a share, that it was committed six

[007-355] thousand years before he was in [[being|existence]]? Certainly nothing

[007-356] offends us more rudely than this doctrine; and yet, without

[007-357] this mystery, the most incomprehensible of all, we are incomprehensible

[007-358] to ourselves. The knot of our condition takes

[007-359] its twists and turns in this abyss, so that man is more

[007-360] inconceivable without this mystery than this mystery is

[007-361] inconceivable to man.

[007-362] [Whence it seems that God, willing to render the difficulty

[007-363] of our existence unintelligible to ourselves, has concealed the

[007-364] knot so high, or, better speaking, so low, that we are quite

[007-365] incapable of reaching it; so that it is not by the proud exertions

[007-366] of our reason, but by the simple submissions of reason, that we

[007-368] These foundations, solidly established on the inviolable

[007-385] 435

[007-386] Without this divine knowledge what could men do but either

[007-387] become elated by the inner feeling of their past greatness which

[007-388] still remains to them, or become despondent at the sight of their

[007-389] present weakness? For, not seeing the whole truth, they could not

[007-390] attain to perfect virtue. Some considering nature as incorrupt,

[007-391] others as incurable, they could not escape either pride or sloth, the

[007-392] two sources of all vice; since they cannot but either abandon

[007-393] themselves to it through cowardice, or escape it by pride. For if

[007-394] they knew the excellence of man, they were ignorant of his

[007-395] corruption; so that they easily avoided sloth, but fell into pride.

[007-396] And if they recognised the infirmity of nature, they were ignorant

[007-397] of its dignity; so that they could easily avoid vanity, but it was

[007-398] to fall into despair. Thence arise the different schools of the

[007-399] Stoics and Epicureans, the Dogmatists, Academicians, etc.

[007-400] The [[christianity|Christian religion]] alone has been able to cure these two

[007-401] vices, not by expelling the one through means of the other

[007-402] according to the wisdom of the world, but by expelling both

[007-403] according to the simplicity of the Gospel. For it teaches the

[007-404] righteous that it raises them even to a participation in divinity

[007-405] itself; that in this lofty state they still carry the source of all

[Against the philosophers who believe in God without Jesus

Philosophers.—They believe that God alone is worthy to be

loved and admired; and they have desired to be loved and

admired of men, and do not know their own corruption. If they

feel full of feelings of love and admiration, and find therein their

chief delight, very well, let them think themselves good. But

declare that this perfection is horrible. What! they have

known God, and have not desired solely that men should love

Him, but that men should stop short at them! They have

wanted to be the object of the voluntary delight of men.

Philosophers.—We are full of things which take us out of

tempt us of themselves, and call to us, even when we are not

thinking of them. And thus philosophers have said in vain,
