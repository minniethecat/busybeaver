Pascal's Wager (*le pari*) is one of the most famous and controversial arguments in the history of philosophy. Presented in the *Pensées*, it is a pragmatic argument for belief in God that appeals not to metaphysical proofs but to decision theory under uncertainty. Pascal argues that since the stakes are infinite — eternal happiness or eternal damnation — and reason alone cannot determine whether God exists, the rational person must "wager" on God's existence.

## The Argument

### The Forced Choice

Pascal frames the question of God's existence as a compulsory decision. One cannot opt out: to refuse to decide is itself a decision against belief. As he writes, the human condition places every person in a position where they must choose.

> Yes; but you must wager. It is not optional. You are embarked. (003-578)

### The Cost-Benefit Analysis

Pascal's argument takes the form of a decision matrix weighing finite costs against infinite rewards. If God exists and one believes, the reward is infinite beatitude. If God does not exist and one believes, one loses only finite worldly pleasures. If one does not believe and God exists, the loss is infinite. If neither believes nor God exists, the gain is merely finite. Given this asymmetry, wagering on God is the uniquely rational choice.

> Let us estimate the two cases: if you win, you win everything; if you lose, you lose nothing. Wager then without hesitation that He is. (003-587)

### The Problem of Certainty

Pascal acknowledges that the Wager does not produce certitude. Reason cannot settle the question. But he argues that uncertainty does not excuse inaction — one must still choose, and the choice has consequences.

> The true course is not to wager at all? That is not possible. (003-576)

## The Practical Dimension: "Bend the Automaton"

Pascal recognizes that purely intellectual assent is insufficient. Belief requires the engagement of the whole person — body, habit, and will. He advises the would-be believer to act as if they believe: to take the holy water, have masses said, and through practice, belief may follow.

This aspect of the Wager connects to Pascal's broader theory of [[custom]] and the [[automaton]] — the idea that the body's mechanical habits can lead the mind toward conviction.

## Place in Pascal's Apologetics

The Wager is not a standalone argument. It functions within Pascal's larger [[apologetics|apologetic project]], which first demonstrates the limits of human reason (through the analysis of [[greatness-and-misery|human greatness and misery]], [[skepticism]], and the failure of philosophical proofs of God) before offering the Wager as a bridge to faith.

Crucially, the Wager leads not to the God of the philosophers but to the God of [[Abraham]], [[Isaac]], and [[Jacob]] — the [[Hidden God]] of Pascal's famous "Memorial." The Wager opens the door; only grace and revelation can bring one through it.

> I must wager; but I may perhaps wager too much. Since the chance is equal, if I had only one life to win, I would still wager. (003-590)

## Criticisms and Legacy

The Wager has generated extensive philosophical discussion. Common objections include:
- The "many gods" objection: which God should one wager on?
- The problem of sincere belief: can one genuinely choose to believe?
- The epistemological objection: is probabilistic reasoning appropriate for religious commitment?

Pascal's response to the first is embedded in the rest of the *Pensées*, which argues for Christianity specifically through prophecy, miracles, and the unique adequacy of Christian doctrine to the human condition. To the second, his doctrine of the automaton provides a practical response — belief can be cultivated through habituation.
