The Old Testament occupies a central place in Pascal's apologetic argument in the *Pensées*. For Pascal, the Hebrew Scriptures are not merely a historical record of ancient Israel but a divinely orchestrated system of "figures" (*figures*) or "types" — persons, events, and institutions that prefigure and find their fulfillment in Jesus Christ and the Christian dispensation.

## Typological Reading

Pascal's hermeneutical key to the Old Testament is typology. He argues that the Old Testament is only properly understood when read as pointing beyond itself to Christ. The ceremonies, prophecies, and even the apparent obscurities of the Hebrew Scriptures serve a divine purpose: they simultaneously reveal truth to those who seek and conceal it from those who do not.

> To show that the Old Testament is only figurative, and that the prophets understood by temporal goods other goods. (010-166)

## The Two Meanings

Pascal distinguishes between the "carnal" and "spiritual" senses of Scripture. The carnal Jew reads the promises of land, prosperity, and political deliverance literally. The spiritual reader — the Christian — recognizes these as figures of eternal goods. This duality is itself part of God's design: the letter veils and the spirit reveals.

> The Old Testament contained the types of future joy, and the New Testament contains the means of arriving at it. (010-190)

## The Genealogies

Pascal addresses the apparent contradictions in Old Testament genealogies — such as those in Saint Matthew and Saint Luke — not as evidence of error but as the kind of difficulty that actually supports authenticity. A forger would have harmonized; the preservation of divergent traditions reflects the honest transmission of ancient sources.

> The genealogy of Jesus Christ in the Old Testament is intermingled with so many others that it remains indistinguishable. (008-227)

## Miracles and Prophecy

The Old Testament miracles serve a dual function in Pascal's apologetics: they authenticate the prophets as divine messengers, and they themselves function as types of Christ's own miracles. The consistent pattern of miracle, prophecy, and fulfillment across both Testaments constitutes one of Pascal's strongest arguments for the truth of Christianity.

> In the Old Testament and the New, miracles are performed in connection with types. (013-687)
