[[Custom|Custom]], for Pascal, is the great unrecognised force in [[human-nature|human]] life — the "second nature" that shapes what we believe, how we perceive, and why we obey.

> [002-590] Custom is our nature. He who is accustomed to the faith believes it, and can no longer help fearing hell.

This is not a celebration of custom but a diagnosis. Pascal's point is that what we call "nature" is often custom so deeply internalised that we have forgotten its origin. The distinction between nature and custom, on which much [[philosophy|philosophy]] depends, is harder to sustain than we like to think.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's treatment of custom operates at multiple levels — [[epistemology|epistemological]], [[political|political]], and [[theological|theological]] — each reinforcing the others.

### Custom and Knowledge

> [002-607] Custom will cause different natural principles.

What appears to be "natural" — the principles from which reasoning begins — is in fact the product of habituation. This is a radical claim: it means that the foundations of human [[knowledge|knowledge]] are not discovered by reason but deposited by custom.

> [002-604] What are our natural principles but principles of custom?

The rhetorical question form is deliberate. Pascal is not inviting debate; he is stating what he takes to be an uncomfortable but inescapable truth.

> [001-092] Those who are accustomed to judge by feeling do not understand the process of reasoning. Those who, on the contrary, are accustomed to reason from principles, do not understand the feelings.

The two types of [[mind|mind]] that Pascal distinguishes — the intuitive and the mathematical — are themselves products of custom. Each is "accustomed" to a particular mode of judgment, and this habituation renders each type incomprehensible to the other.

### Custom and Authority

> [002-592] He who is accustomed to believe that the king is terrible does not see him without this accompaniment of terror.

Political [[authority|authority]] is, for Pascal, almost entirely a creature of custom. The king is obeyed not because his power is rationally justified but because we have been habituated to perceive him as terrible. Strip away the custom, and the authority collapses — not because the reasons for obedience have been refuted, but because the habitual basis of obedience has been removed.

> [002-476] If we had seen the laws of nature constantly violated, custom would have accustomed us.

This counterfactual thought experiment reveals the depth of Pascal's point: even the laws of [[nature|nature]] — what we take to be the most fixed and necessary truths — would lose their hold on us if custom trained us otherwise. The implication is that custom does not merely supplement nature; in crucial respects, it constitutes it.

> [002-646] It is custom then which does this, for it constrains nature. But sometimes nature overcomes it, and keeps man in his instinct, in spite of all custom, good or bad.

Here Pascal admits a limit to custom's power: [[instinct|instinct]] — the residue of [[human-nature|nature]] that custom cannot fully extinguish — provides a counter-force. The relationship between custom and nature is not one of simple domination but of ongoing tension.

## Key Passages

The most concentrated treatment of custom appears in the section on human institutions:

> [002-615] Custom is a second nature which destroys the former. But what is nature? For is custom not natural? I am much afraid that nature is itself only a first custom, as custom is a second nature.

This passage is the philosophical core of Pascal's position. The regress is infinite: if nature is a first custom, what made that custom natural? Pascal does not resolve the regress; he lets it stand as a demonstration that the nature/custom distinction cannot bear the weight philosophers place on it.

> [002-524] Your senses, strengthened by custom, which science must correct.

Custom operates at the level of [[perception|perception]] itself: it does not merely shape what we think about what we see; it shapes what we see. [[Science|Science]] — by which Pascal means disciplined inquiry — can partially correct for custom's distortions, but it can never fully escape them, since the scientist is herself a creature of custom.

> [002-635] Custom makes men masons, soldiers, slaters. So great is the force of custom that out of those whom nature has only made men, it makes all conditions of men.

This observation about social roles is simultaneously sociological and theological. It explains how [[society|society]] reproduces itself — by habituation — but it also suggests the [[artificiality|artificiality]] of all social identities. What we take to be essential — one's role, one's station — is merely the sedimentation of repeated practice.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of custom serves as a bridge between his [[anthropology|anthropology]] of the [[human-condition|fallen condition]] and his [[apologetics|apologetics]].

Anthropologically, custom is the mechanism by which the post-lapsarian human being copes with the loss of direct access to nature. If nature were transparent to reason, custom would be unnecessary. The fact that custom is ubiquitous is evidence that nature is not transparent — which is to say, evidence of the [[original-sin|Fall]].

Apologetically, custom is both obstacle and instrument. It is an obstacle because it entrenches patterns of [[unbelief|unbelief]] that reason alone cannot dislodge: "He who is accustomed to the faith believes it" — and by implication, he who is accustomed to unbelief does not. But custom is also an instrument: Pascal's strategy of addressing the whole person — heart as well as mind, habit as well as argument — is an attempt to work with the grain of custom rather than against it.

> [002-628] When we are accustomed to use bad reasons for proving effects of nature, we do not wish to receive good reasons when they are presented.

Bad reasons, once habituated, crowd out good ones. This is why Pascal's apologetics does not simply present arguments — it must first dislodge the bad intellectual habits that make the arguments inaudible.
