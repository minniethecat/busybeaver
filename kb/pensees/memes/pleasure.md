## Definition

In Pascal's moral psychology, **pleasure** (plaisir) is the **primary mechanism of human motivation and the engine of [[Diversion]]**, but it is constitutively incapable of delivering lasting satisfaction. Pleasure is not condemned as intrinsically evil. It is diagnosed as structurally inadequate: finite pleasures cannot satisfy the infinite longing of the human [[Heart]], and the pursuit of pleasure — when made the organizing principle of life — leads not to [[Happiness]] but to restlessness, inconstancy, and the flight from self-knowledge. Pleasure is the coin with which human beings purchase distraction from their own condition.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Paradox of Pleasure

Pascal opens his analysis with an observation that overturns naive hedonism:

> (002-215) Too much pleasure disagrees with us. Too many concords displease us.

The human appetite for pleasure is self-limiting. Pleasure pursued beyond a certain point turns into its opposite — displeasure, satiety, disgust. This is not a contingent fact about individual psychology but a structural feature of the human condition: we are made for an infinite good and cannot be satisfied by finite goods, however abundant. The person who attempts to fill an infinite capacity with finite pleasures is like someone trying to fill an abyss with pebbles. The abyss remains, and the pebbles are merely rearranged.

### Pleasure and the Will

> (001-191) That they feel themselves made for pleasure; they have no other wish than to please.

The drive for pleasure is, Pascal grants, constitutive of human nature. We are "made for pleasure" in the sense that the will naturally inclines toward what it perceives as good. The problem is not the inclination itself but its object. Fallen humanity pursues finite pleasures — the pleasures of the [[Body]], the pleasures of esteem, the pleasures of power — because it has lost sight of the infinite good for which it was made. The "other wish than to please" is the root of [[Vanity]]: we live for the approval of others because we cannot bear to face ourselves without it.

### Pleasure as the Currency of Diversion

> (001-268) Without giving us pleasure, the coin for which we will give whatever is asked.

> (002-471) We must work all day for pleasures seen to be imaginary.

Pleasure is the currency of human exchange. We trade our time, our energy, our attention, and ultimately our lives for pleasure. Yet the pleasures we purchase are "seen to be imaginary" — we know, at some level, that they will not deliver what they promise. The phrase "work all day" invokes the image of labor: the pursuit of pleasure is not leisurely but exhausting. Human beings exhaust themselves in the pursuit of what cannot satisfy them. This is Pascal's phenomenology of [[Diversion]]: the ceaseless activity that fills the day, not because it is intrinsically rewarding, but because stillness would force the confrontation with [[Wretchedness]].

### Pleasure and Inconstancy

> (002-840) The pleasures of the state in which we are.

> (002-841) And if we attained to these pleasures, we should not be happy for all that.

> (002-846) The falsity of present pleasures, and the ignorance of the vanity of absent pleasures, cause inconstancy.

The inconstancy of human desire is rooted in the structure of pleasure itself. Present pleasures are false — they promise satisfaction and deliver only temporary relief. Absent pleasures — the ones we imagine in the future — are equally illusory: "the ignorance of the vanity of absent pleasures" means we project satisfaction onto states we have not yet reached, only to discover when we reach them that they too are empty. This oscillation between the falsity of the present and the vanity of the future generates the restlessness that Pascal identifies as the mark of the human condition. We are inconstant because no pleasure can hold us.

### The Pleasure of Home and the Need to Leave

> (002-997) Pleasure at home, would not leave it to go to war.

> (002-1001) Because they cannot remain with pleasure at home.

> (002-938) A man dwells at home with pleasure; but if he has no diversion, he is unhappy.

The image of "home" functions as a test case for Pascal's theory. A man who has pleasure at home — domestic comfort, family, the satisfaction of daily life — should, by the logic of hedonism, stay there. Yet the man who cannot remain with pleasure at home is the paradigmatic case. He leaves home to go to war, to hunt, to gamble, to pursue the thousand activities that constitute [[Diversion]]. Why? Because pleasure at home, however real, cannot silence the deeper unease — the awareness of [[Death]], the sense of cosmic [[Disproportion]], the ache of an infinite longing that finite pleasures cannot address. Home becomes a kind of [[Prison]] from which the soul flees into activity.

### The Pleasure of Observation

> (002-974) To observe it with pleasure, we have to see it emerge from the quarrel.

> (002-975) So in the passions, there is pleasure in seeing the collision of two contraries.

Pascal notes a curious feature of human psychology: we take pleasure in spectacle, particularly in the spectacle of conflict. The "pleasure in seeing the collision of two contraries" is the pleasure of drama — the enjoyment of watching passions clash from a safe distance. This is a second-order pleasure: not the direct enjoyment of an object but the enjoyment of observing others' enjoyment and suffering. It is the pleasure of the theater and, by extension, of all the social spectacles that fill the vacuum left by the absence of [[God]].

### Complete Pleasure and Its Absence

> (002-1010) Attended with every pleasure he can feel, if he be without diversion, he is unhappy.

> (002-1032) For them all kinds of pleasures.

> (002-1073) Then rest with pleasure, and are insensible of the whole course of the year.

The hypothetical case of "every pleasure he can feel" is the definitive refutation of hedonism. Grant a man all the pleasures available to human experience — health, wealth, love, esteem, intellectual satisfaction. If he is without diversion — if he is forced to sit still and reflect on his condition — he is unhappy. The sum of all finite pleasures does not equal happiness. It equals, at best, a temporary insensibility — "rest with pleasure, and are insensible" — a state of numbness rather than fulfillment. Pascal's point is not that pleasure is worthless but that it is insufficient. Only the infinite good — [[God]] Himself — can satisfy the infinite capacity of the human [[Heart]].

### Pleasure and Sacrifice

> (001-167) The same pleasures and the same sacrifices which they had in the former state.

Pascal observes that pleasure and sacrifice are paired. The "same pleasures and the same sacrifices" belong together because authentic pleasure — the joy of [[Charity]], the satisfaction of [[Truth in Pascal's Epistemology|Truth]] — requires sacrifice. The hedonist who refuses all sacrifice ends up with neither pleasure nor meaning. The Christian who accepts sacrifice discovers a pleasure that the hedonist cannot imagine: the joy of conformity to God's will.

## Analysis

Pascal's analysis of pleasure anticipates a number of later philosophical developments. His insistence that finite pleasures cannot satisfy an infinite longing echoes Augustine's "our heart is restless until it rests in Thee," but Pascal gives the Augustinian insight a psychological precision that is distinctively modern. He describes the mechanics of pleasure-seeking in terms that anticipate later theories of hedonic adaptation: the pleasure that fades as soon as it is attained, the projection of satisfaction onto absent goods, the restlessness that drives the pursuit of ever-new objects. Pascal's contribution is to give this psychological observation a theological frame: adaptation is not a design flaw in human psychology but evidence of a design for something beyond the finite.

The relationship between pleasure and diversion is the center of gravity of Pascal's analysis. Pleasure is what makes diversion work — it is the bait that keeps the soul in motion. But pleasure is also what makes diversion ultimately fail — it cannot deliver what it promises, and the soul eventually senses the fraud. The "falsity of present pleasures" is an experiential discovery, not a theoretical one. Pascal expects the reader to recognize the description from his own life: the feeling that the thing he pursued did not satisfy, that the pleasure he attained was real but insufficient, that the restlessness returned as soon as the activity stopped.

Pascal's treatment of pleasure is finally a preparation for the Gospel. If human beings could be satisfied by finite pleasures, they would not need God. The structural inadequacy of pleasure is, from a theological perspective, a mercy: it prevents the soul from settling for less than its true end. The restlessness that pleasure cannot cure is the negative preparation for the positive offer of [[Salvation]]. Pleasure that works too well would be the greatest obstacle to [[Conversion]].
