[[Knowledge]], in Pascal's [[epistemology|epistemology]], is not a single uniform capacity but a layered structure corresponding to his doctrine of the [[three-orders|three orders]]. Each order — body, [[mind|mind]], and [[charity|charity]] — has its own proper objects, its own mode of knowing, and its own form of [[certainty|certainty]].

> (P01-012) It is not our knowledge of him that we must increase, but our capacity for receiving it.

Knowledge is not merely an accumulation of facts but a transformation of the knower. Pascal's epistemology is inseparable from his [[anthropology|anthropology]]: how we know reflects who we are.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Innate Knowledge and First Principles

Pascal holds that certain knowledge is innate — built into the structure of the [[human-nature|human]] mind:

> (002-082) This is where our innate knowledge leads us.

This innate knowledge is not the elaborate system of [[innate-ideas|innate ideas]] that [[Descartes|Descartes]] proposed. For Pascal, what is innate is the capacity to recognise [[first-principles|first principles]] — the basic [[truths|truths]] that cannot be demonstrated but upon which all demonstration depends.

> (002-202) The knowledge of first beginnings which are grasped by the heart.

These first principles — [[space|space]], [[time|time]], [[number|number]], [[motion|motion]] — are not known through [[reason|reason]] but through the [[heart|heart]], the intuitive faculty that grasps what it cannot prove. Reason itself depends on these principles; it cannot supply them.

### The Limits of Discursive Knowledge

> (002-227) Certain knowledge and of absolute ignorance. We sail upon a vast middle, ever uncertain, floating between the two.

The human epistemic condition is one of [[disproportion|disproportion]]. We are neither capable of complete knowledge — we cannot grasp the whole — nor condemned to complete [[ignorance|ignorance]] — we do know something of the parts. This middle position is not a temporary state that better [[science|science]] will overcome; it is the permanent condition of finite knowers.

> (002-162) We arrive at the knowledge of the whole, with a presumption that is as great as if we knew the parts in detail.

The desire to know the whole is natural but presumptuous. We know fragments and reason from them as if we had surveyed everything. This is not a counsel of [[skepticism|skepticism]] but of epistemic [[humility|humility]].

### Knowledge of Self

> (002-021) Of the knowledge of self; of the divisions of the self.

Self-knowledge occupies a special place in Pascal's epistemology. The [[self|self]] is both the nearest object of knowledge and the most difficult to know accurately, because the self is divided — the knowing self and the known self are not the same, and the known self resists honest scrutiny.

## Key Passages

> (002-066) The rest of their knowledge as on knowing how to be gentle.

Pascal distinguishes between theoretical knowledge and practical [[wisdom|wisdom]] — knowing how to live, how to treat others, how to be [[gentle|gentle]]. The latter is a form of knowledge that [[reason|reason]] cannot reduce to [[rules|rules]].

> (002-245) If he has more knowledge of the universe, the universe does not therefore have more knowledge of him.

The asymmetry of knowledge and being — going deeper into [[science|science]] does nothing to make the universe more [[meaning|meaningful]] for the knower. The [[existential|existential]] question is not answered by more information.

## Analysis

Pascal's epistemology is best understood as a response to two opposing errors: [[dogmatism|dogmatism]], which claims that reason can achieve certain knowledge of the most important truths, and [[skepticism|skepticism]], which claims that nothing can be known with confidence. Pascal's middle way is grounded in the [[three-orders|three orders]].

Knowledge in the order of the [[body|body]] comes through the [[senses|senses]] and concerns the [[physical|physical]] world. Knowledge in the order of the [[mind|mind]] comes through [[reason|reason]] and concerns [[abstraction|abstractions]] and [[demonstration|demonstrations]]. But the most important knowledge — knowledge of [[God|God]], of the [[good|good]], of the meaning of [[existence|existence]] — belongs to the order of [[charity|charity]] and is accessed through the [[heart|heart]].

This is not anti-intellectual. Pascal was one of the greatest mathematical minds of his age. His point is that reality is layered, and each layer requires its own mode of knowing. The mistake is to try to know [[God|God]] the way we know [[geometry|geometry]] — not because God is less knowable than geometry, but because God belongs to a different order.
