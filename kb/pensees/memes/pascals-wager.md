[[Pascal's Wager]] (*le pari de [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]]*) is the most famous and controversial [[argument]] in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées*.

Presented as a dialogue between Pascal and a [[skepticism|skeptical]] interlocutor, the wager reframes the question of belief in [[God|God]] not as a matter of theoretical proof but as a practical decision made under conditions of [[epistemology|uncertainty]].

It represents Pascal's distinctive contribution to the [[faith and reason|faith-reason]] debate, offering a bridge from rational calculation to the life of religious practice.

## The Argument

The wager argument appears in a section of the *Pensées* addressed to the *[[libertins|libertin]]* — the educated skeptic of seventeenth-century [[france|France]] who accepts the competence of reason but remains indifferent to religion. Pascal's strategy is to meet this interlocutor on his own ground: the language of gambling, probability, and rational self-interest.

The argument can be reconstructed in four steps:

| Step | Claim | Rationale |
|------|-------|-----------|
| 1. Uncertainty | Reason cannot definitively prove or d[[God|God]]rove God's existence | Human cognitive limits |
| 2. Inescapability | One must nevertheless choose how to live | Not choosing is itself a choice against God |
| 3. Stake calculation | The potential gain ([[eternity|eternal]] bliss) is [[infinity|infinite]]; the potential loss (finite pleasures) is finite | Expected value analysis |
| 4. Conclusion | The rational choice is to wager on God | Infinite expected value outweighs any finite cost |

> [003-568] If there is a God, He is infinitely incomprehensible. We are incapable of knowing either what He is or whether He is. That being so, who will dare to decide the question? Not we, who have no affinity to Him. Who will then blame [[christianity|Christian]]s for not being able to give a reason for their belief?

Pascal's interlocutor objects that the odds are unknown; Pascal replies that the infinite character of the reward makes the wager rational regardless of the odds, provided they are not zero — just as in a game where one risks a finite sum for a chance at an infinite prize, the expected value is infinite no matter how long the odds [003-576].

## The Wager's Place in Pascal's Apologetic

The wager is often read in isolation, but it occupies a specific place within the architecture of the *Pensées*.

It comes after Pascal's extended analysis of the [[greatness and misery of man|human condition]] — the disproportion, the boredom, the diversion, the inability to be at rest — and before his presentation of the positive evidences for [[christianity|Christianity]] ([[prophecy|prophecy]], [[miracle|miracle]]s, [[scripture|Scripture]]).

This placement is strategic. The wager is not addressed to someone who has never considered religious questions; it is addressed to someone who has been brought by Pascal's earlier arguments to the point of recognizing both the urgency of the question and the inadequacy of unaided reason to resolve it.

The wager does not claim to produce faith directly. Pascal acknowledges that one cannot simply decide to believe by an act of will. His advice is practical: act as if you believe — take the [[holy-water|holy water]], have masses said — and faith will follow [003-576]. This behavioral approach anticipates modern psychological insights about the relationship between action and belief.

## Philosophical Significance

The wager argument has generated an enormous philosophical literature and is recognized as a forerunner of decision theory, [[existentialism|existentialism]], and pragmatism.

**Decision theory**: The wager formalizes religious choice as a decision under uncertainty, anticipating the expected-value framework later developed by Bernoulli and modern decision theorists. The distinctive feature is Pascal's use of infinite utilities, which raises technical problems (the "many gods" objection, the "mixed strategies" problem) that continue[[existentialism|Existentialism]].

**Existentialism**: The wager's insistence that one must choose — that not choosing is itself a choice — prefigures the existentialist emphasis on the inescapability of decision. [[Søren soren-kierKierkegaardrkegaard|Kierkegaard's]] "leap of faith" owes a debt to Pascal, though Kierkegaard would reject the wager's rationalistic framing.

**Pragmatism**: Pascal's practical orientation — his focus on what one must *do* rather than what one can *prove* — anticipates the pragmatist tradition of [[William James]], whose "The Will to Believe" is directly indebted to the wager argument.

## Objections and Responses

The wager has attracted several enduring objections:

1. **The many gods objection**: The wager trea[[christianity|Christian]] in the Christian God as the only alternative to unbelief. But if there are multiple possible gods, wagering on the wrong one might incur punishment.

Pascal's response, implicit in the *Pensées*, is that the wager is not a standalone argument but part of a cumulative case that includes t[[christianity|Christianity]] for Christianity specifically.

2. **The doxastic voluntarism objection**: Belief is not under voluntary control; one cannot simply decide to believe. Pascal concedes this point but argues that one can decide to *act* as if one believes, and that genuine belief may follow from habituated practice.

3. **The prudential objection**: A God worthy of worship would not reward belief based on prudential calculation. Pascal might reply that the wager is only a first step — a way of overcoming the inertia of indifference — and that the faith that develops through practice transcends its prudential origins.

> [003-576] The true course is not to wager at all. Yes; but you must wager. It is not voluntary, you are embarked.
