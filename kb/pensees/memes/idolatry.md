Idolatry in Pascal's *Pensées* is the perennial theological error: the substitution of the created for the Creator in worship. It is a recurring theme across Pascal's historical apologetics, where he traces how the history of religion is a history of the struggle against idolatrous corruption.

## Idolatry as Historical Infection

Pascal observes that even Christianity itself was not immune to idolatrous contamination. "Christians were infected both with idolatry and magic" (009-284) — a sober acknowledgment that the historical Church absorbed pagan practices. This historical realism underlies Pascal's insistence that true religion must be continually purified, not merely inherited.

## Prophecy of Idolatry's Overthrow

The prophetic tradition foretold the defeat of idolatry as a sign of true revelation. "That then idolatry would be overthrown" (011-664) — Pascal reads this as a criterion fulfilled by Christianity alone among world religions. The spread of monotheism is, for Pascal, a miracle of history and a proof of divine action.

## The Call to Purify Worship

The prophetic imperative is clear: "remove idolatry from among them" (012-396). For Pascal, this is not merely a command for ancient Israel but a permanent requirement for authentic worship. The Church must constantly examine whether its practices direct worship toward God or toward created substitutes.

## The Danger of Misdirected Religion

"And that they do not lead to idolatry" (013-013) captures the perennial danger: religious practices, however well-intentioned, can drift toward idolatrous objects. Pascal's Augustinian sensibility recognizes that the human heart, being a "factory of idols" (Calvin's phrase, consonant with Pascal's thought), is forever at risk of substituting the sign for the thing signified.
