[[King David]] serves a dual role in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées*: he is both a historical witness to the truth of [[biblical]] religion and a prophetic figure whose life and lineage prefigure the coming of the [[Messiah]].

Pascal's treatment of David exemplifies his broader typological method — reading the persons and events of the [[Old Testament]] as "figures" that find their fulfillment in [[Jesus Christ]]. David is not merely a king of ancient [[Israel]] but a link in the chain of witnesses that Pascal deploys in his cumulative argument for [[Christianity]].

## David as Witness

In Pascal's apologetic framework, witnesses carry weight in proportion to their character and the cost of their testimony. David qualifies on both counts: he is "a great witness; a king, good" whose testimony merits serious consideration (012-138). The integrity of the witness matters because Pascal is building a case that relies on the credibility of those who transmit it.

> [012-138] David: a great witness; a king, good.

David, like [[Solomon]], belongs to a category of witnesses whose position and character lend authority to what they attest. Pascal contrasts these biblical witnesses with the competing claims of other traditions, arguing that the convergence of testimony from figures of established integrity constitutes a distinctive form of evidence. David and Solomon "have never said there is no void" — they do not claim a completeness of [[knowledge]] that would be inconsistent with the human condition (004-042).

> [004-042] David, Solomon, etc., have never said there is no void.

## The Prophecy of David's Line

Pascal's most developed treatment of David concerns the prophetic dimension of his kingship. The promise that David's lineage would endure eternally is, for Pascal, one of the clearest instances of [[Old Testament]] prophecy finding its fulfillment in Christ.

The apparent paradox — that the Messiah should be both David's descendant and David's lord — is precisely the kind of tension that Pascal finds productive. He notes that the Messiah was to be "lord of David, though his son" (010-190), a relationship that the gospel writers themselves highlight. This paradox, rather than undermining the prophetic case, strengthens it: the fulfillment exceeds the terms of the original promise in a way that marks it as genuinely [[divine]] rather than merely human contrivance.

Pascal traces this promise through the historical books of the Old Testament. The eternal reign of David's line, prophesied in 2 Chronicles and confirmed "by all the prophecies" (011-424), establishes a trajectory that Pascal argues can only terminate in Christ. Any merely political fulfillment — the succession of Davidic kings in [[Jerusalem]] — proved temporary; only the coming of Christ actualizes the eternal dimension of the promise.

## The Psalms as Prophetic Voice

Beyond his historical role, David as psalmist provides Pascal with a second register of testimony. The psalms are not merely devotional poetry; they are prophetic utterance. Pascal refers to "David, a saying of" (002-1075) as carrying the weight of revealed truth alongside the other prophets.

The psalms of lament and penitence — the *Miserere* that Pascal cites (009-225) — give voice to the very condition of human [[misery]] that his apologetic takes as its starting point. David's honest confrontation with his own [[sin]] and his dependence on divine [[mercy]] model the disposition of the sincere seeker that Pascal addresses throughout the *Pensées*.

## David in Pascal's Cumulative Case

David's place in Pascal's apologetic structure reveals the method at work. No single piece of evidence — not David's witness, not the prophetic psalms, not the dynastic promise — is presented as sufficient on its own. Rather, each element contributes to a cumulative pattern that Pascal invites the reader to recognize.

David is simultaneously a historical figure whose existence and reign are well-attested, a moral witness whose character lends credibility to his testimony, a prophetic voice whose words point beyond his own time, and a typological figure whose kingship prefigures the greater kingship of Christ. This multi-dimensional quality makes David an especially effective node in Pascal's network of converging evidences.
