[[Justice|Justice]], in Pascal's analysis, is a central but fragile human institution — one that reason demands yet cannot secure.

> [002-540] Justice and truth are two such subtle points, that our tools are too blunt to touch them exactly.

What we call justice is, for Pascal, something at once inescapable and impossible: we cannot live without it, yet we cannot establish it on foundations that survive scrutiny.

[[Reason|Reason]] alone cannot ground justice; [[custom|custom]], reinforced by force, provides the only stable foundation human societies actually possess.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's treatment of justice is inseparable from his [[anthropology|anthropology]] of self-interest. Human judgments of justice are never pure — they are always coloured by the perspective and interests of the judge.

> [002-458] Love or hate alters the aspect of justice. How much greater the justice of his cause!

The subjective distortion of justice is not, for Pascal, a contingent failing that better education might correct. It is structural — rooted in the [[self-love|self-love]] that infects all human motivation.

> [002-460] How much better does his bold manner make his cause appear! I myself am deceived.

Even the awareness that one's perception of justice is distorted does not free one from the distortion. Pascal's skeptical analysis of justice runs parallel to his treatment of [[imagination|imagination]]: both are faculties that should serve truth but in practice serve interest.

> [002-762] Dispositions, so removed from justice and reason, have a natural foundation.

The paradox is that what is "natural" — our dispositions, our self-interest — is precisely what removes us from justice. Nature, in the fallen condition, is not an ally of justice but its enemy.

### Force and Justice

Pascal's most radical claim about justice concerns its relationship with force:

> [005-051] Justice without force is powerless; force without justice is tyrannical.

This aphorism captures the tragic structure of human political life. Justice, being inherently contestable, cannot sustain itself through reason alone: "Justice is subject to dispute. Force is easily recognised and undisputed." Therefore, those who possess force clothe it in the language of justice, and those who lack force appeal to a justice that cannot protect them.

The institutional apparatus of law — the courts, the magistrates, the symbols of authority — serves to disguise this uncomfortable truth:

> [002-478] The courts in which they administer justice, the fleurs-de-lis, and all such august circumstances — this external apparatus is most necessary.

These trappings are not merely decorative. They perform an essential social function: by surrounding justice with visible majesty, they prevent the naked force beneath from being seen, and thus prevent the social order from unravelling.

## Key Passages

The tension between human and divine justice runs through the *Pensées*:

> [003-402] Injustice.—That presumption should be joined to meanness is extreme injustice.

Pascal's moral psychology of injustice is precise: what makes a wrong "extreme" is not its magnitude but the conjunction of presumption — claiming a status one does not possess — with meanness — acting from base motives. The combination is both morally repugnant and existentially diagnostic.

> [003-524] Our spirit before God, so our justice before divine justice.

This is the theological turn in Pascal's account: human justice, seen from the perspective of divine justice, reveals its own disproportion. The asymmetry between human and divine judgment is not one of degree but of kind.

> [003-527] The justice of God must be vast like His compassion. Justice to the outcast is less vast, and ought less to offend our feelings.

Here Pascal anticipates the objection that divine justice seems cruel. His response is not to soften the claim but to insist on the vastness of divine justice — a vastness that human sensibilities cannot comfortably accommodate.

> [002-483] If magistrates had true justice, and if physicians had the true art of healing, they would have no need of square caps.

The external marks of professional authority — the square caps of magistrates, the robes of physicians — are for Pascal signs that the substance they claim to represent is absent. True justice would need no costume.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of justice illuminates three interrelated dimensions of his thought.

First, it extends his epistemological pessimism into the moral domain. Just as reason cannot attain [[certainty|certainty]] about the infinite, it cannot attain agreement about justice. The result is not relativism — Pascal never says justice is merely conventional — but a kind of tragic realism: justice is real, but human access to it is irremediably partial.

Second, it connects his political philosophy to his theology. The necessity of force for justice is a consequence of the [[original-sin|Fall]]: in an unfallen world, justice would be self-evident and force unnecessary. The fact that force is necessary is itself a proof of the human predicament that [[christianity|Christianity]] claims to diagnose.

Third, it introduces the theme of [[disproportion|disproportion]]: between human justice and divine justice there is an "infinite" distance, just as there is between human reason and divine wisdom, or between the [[three-orders|order of the body]] and the order of [[charity|charity]].

> [003-525] Not so great a disproportion between our justice and that of God as between our pleasure and that of God.

The comparative framing is characteristic of Pascal: even the disproportion of justice is itself ordered within a larger hierarchy of disproportion.
