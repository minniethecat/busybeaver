[[Isaiah]] is the [[prophet]] most frequently cited in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] argument that the [[Old Testament]] contains a coherent body of [[prophecy|prophecies]] fulfilled in [[Jesus Christ]]. His oracles — concerning the calling of the [[Gentiles]], the rejection of [[Israel|the Jews]], the [[suffering servant]], and the new creation — provide Pascal with the textual material for demonstrating that the events of Christ's life and the subsequent history of the [[Church]] were foreseen centuries in advance.

For Pascal, Isaiah is not merely one prophet among many but the supreme witness to the intelligibility of [[providence|divine]] purpose in [[history]]. The density and specificity of Isaiah's predictions make him, in Pascal's hands, the strongest single proof that [[biblical]] [[religion]] is not a human fabrication but a record of genuine divine disclosure.

## Isaiah and the Argument from Prophecy

Pascal's apologetic method depends heavily on the demonstration that the [[Hebrew Scriptures]] contain predictions whose fulfillment can be verified independently of the texts themselves. Isaiah serves as the principal source for this demonstration.

> [009-691] witness to the Messiah (Isaiah, xliii, 9; xliv, 8). They kept the books with a scrupulous fidelity.

The logic is twofold: first, the prophecies are demonstrably ancient — preserved by the [[Jews]] themselves, who, Pascal notes, "kept the books with a scrupulous fidelity" even when those books testified against their own unbelief. Second, the content of the prophecies is sufficiently specific that their fulfillment cannot be attributed to chance or to retroactive reinterpretation. Isaiah's oracles name particular events — the return from exile, the calling of the Gentiles, the rejection of the [[chosen people]] — that lie beyond the horizon of the eighth-century prophet's own historical situation.

Pascal's deployment of Isaiah is thus carefully crafted to rebut the [[skepticism|skeptical]] objection that prophecy is either vague enough to fit any outcome or was composed after the events it purports to predict. The Jewish preservation of Isaiah constitutes, in his view, an unwilling testimony to the [[truth]] of [[Christianity]].

## The Calling of the Gentiles

One of Isaiah's most distinctive themes, in Pascal's reading, is the extension of [[salvation]] beyond ethnic Israel to the [[nations]]. This theme is crucial to Pascal's argument because it explains how a religion rooted in the particular history of one people can claim universal validity.

> [009-220] "I was manifested unto them that asked not for Me; I was found of them that sought Me not: I said, Behold Me, behold Me, unto a nation that did not call upon My name."

Pascal quotes this passage from Isaiah lxv, 1 as evidence that the inclusion of the [[Gentiles]] was not an improvisation but part of the original prophetic vision. The same point is reinforced by Isaiah lvi, 3 (009-177), which assures the "stranger" who joins himself to the Lord that he will not be separated from God's people. For Pascal, these texts demonstrate that the [[universality]] of the Christian message was embedded in the Jewish Scriptures from the beginning — a fact that simultaneously validates Christianity and condemns the Jews who rejected it.

## The Critique of Empty Ritual

Isaiah is also, for Pascal, the prophet who most sharply distinguishes inward [[faith]] from outward observance. This distinction becomes the basis for Pascal's critique of [[religion]] reduced to mere [[custom]] or social convention.

> [009-209] Scindite corda vestra, etc.; Isaiah lviii, 3, 4, etc.

The call to "rend your hearts and not your garments" resonates with Pascal's own insistence, throughout the *Pensées*, that [[God]] is not appeased by ceremonies performed without interior conversion. The [[heart]] — Pascal's central epistemological and spiritual category — finds its prophetic warrant in Isaiah's critique of a people who "draw near with their mouth and honor Me with their lips, but have removed their heart far from Me."

Isaiah's polemic against [[idolatry]] (009-247, 009-249) reinforces this theme: the problem with idolatry is not merely that it worships the wrong object but that it substitutes the created for the [[Creator]], reducing religion to [[imagination]] and self-projection. Pascal sees in Isaiah the same diagnosis of the [[human condition]] that he himself advances: the corruption of [[worship]] into self-worship, the [[vanity]] that mistakes its own constructions for divine reality.

The [[circumcision]] of the heart — an image Pascal draws from both Isaiah and [[Jeremiah]] (009-198) — becomes the prophetic model for authentic religious response, one that bypasses the external and reaches the interior disposition that alone constitutes genuine [[piety]].

## Prophecy of the New Creation

Finally, Pascal finds in Isaiah the vision of a [[new creation]] that transcends the old dispensation:

> [009-234] "Old things will be forgotten." Isaiah xliii, 18, 19.

This promise of eschatological renewal provides Pascal with a framework for understanding the relationship between the [[Old Testament|old covenant]] and the [[new covenant|new]]. The old is not destroyed but fulfilled; what is "forgotten" is not the substance of the [[promise]] but the particular forms — [[sacrifice|sacrifices]], [[Temple]], [[priesthood]] — that were provisional shadows of the reality to come.

Isaiah thus stands at the hinge between the two dispensations, pointing forward to what he could not yet see. His prophecies are, for Pascal, the strongest evidence that Christianity is not a break with [[Judaism]] but its intended consummation.
