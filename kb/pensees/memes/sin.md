Sin, and particularly [[original-sin|original sin]], is the cornerstone of Pascal's theological anthropology. Without the doctrine of the Fall, Pascal argues, the human condition is incomprehensible: we are simultaneously great (in our origins and aspirations) and wretched (in our actual state). Only original sin can explain this paradox.

## The Paradox of Human Nature

Pascal's most famous anthropological insight is that human beings present a contradictory picture — capable of reason, moral aspiration, and scientific achievement, yet also driven by vanity, self-deception, and concupiscence. Philosophy cannot explain this contradiction.

> Original sin is foolishness to men, but it is admitted as such. You must not then reproach me for the want of reason in this doctrine, since I admit it to be without reason. (002-1075)

## Sin and the Hidden God

The doctrine of sin is intimately connected to Pascal's concept of the [[Hidden God]] (*Deus absconditus*). God's hiddenness is not arbitrary; it is a response to human sinfulness. God reveals himself sufficiently to be found by those who seek him sincerely, but remains hidden from those who, through pride and concupiscence, resist. Sin thus explains both the possibility of faith and the reality of unbelief.

## Jansenist Context

Pascal's understanding of sin reflects his Jansenist commitments. Following Saint Augustine, the Jansenists emphasized the radical corruption of human nature after the Fall and the absolute necessity of divine [[grace]] for salvation. Without grace, the human will is in bondage to concupiscence — "the self is hateful" (*le moi est haïssable*).
