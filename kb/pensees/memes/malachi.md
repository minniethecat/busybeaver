Malachi appears in Pascal's *Pensées* as the final prophetic voice before the Messianic age, a figure whose words mark the transition from the old covenant to the new. Pascal draws on Malachi for two interconnected arguments: the supersession of Jewish ritual by a universal sacrifice, and the prophecy of John the Baptist as Christ's forerunner.

## Malachi in Pascal's Apologetic

### The Universal Sacrifice

Pascal's most frequent citation from Malachi is the prophecy of a pure offering among the Gentiles: "Malachi i, 11" foretells that "in every place incense shall be offered unto my name, and a pure offering" (009-231, 009-239, 011-395, 011-415). Pascal reads this as a direct prophecy of the Eucharist — the sacrifice of the Mass replacing the Temple sacrifice, the universal worship of the nations replacing the particular worship of Israel.

This verse appears four times across Pascal's argument, always in the context of demonstrating that the Old Testament itself predicted the obsolescence of its own ritual system. The repetition underscores its importance: Malachi provides Pascal with a proof-text for the claim that Christianity is not a deviation from Judaism but its prophesied fulfillment.

### The Second Temple's Glory

Pascal cites "Malachi. Grotius.—The second temple glory" (011-412), drawing on the Dutch jurist Hugo Grotius's interpretation of Malachi's prophecy that the glory of the second temple would exceed that of the first. For Pascal, this greater glory can only be explained by the presence of Christ in the temple — the incarnate God surpassing the material splendor of Solomon's structure.

### The Forerunner

Malachi's prophecy of a messenger preparing the way — "His forerunner. Malachi iii" (011-664) — is essential to Pascal's argument that John the Baptist was foretold. This connects Malachi to the final prophetic act: the last book of the Old Testament canon predicts the first figure of the New Testament era. The seamless transition from Malachi to John the Baptist demonstrates the continuity of the divine plan.

### Paired with Isaiah

Pascal pairs "Malachi, Isaiah" (009-658) as the two great prophetic voices, suggesting that Malachi's brevity does not diminish his authority. The pairing of the longest prophetic book with the shortest makes a rhetorical point: the weight of prophecy does not depend on volume but on content.

## Significance in the Pensées

Malachi's distinctive contribution to Pascal's argument is his position at the canon's edge. As the last prophet of the Old Testament, Malachi serves as the hinge between the two dispensations. His prophecy of the universal sacrifice looks forward to the Eucharist; his prophecy of the forerunner looks forward to John the Baptist; his prophecy of the second temple's glory looks forward to Christ. Malachi demonstrates that the Old Testament concludes not with closure but with expectation — an expectation that Pascal argues is fulfilled in Christianity.
