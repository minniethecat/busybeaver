[[Ezekiel]] the prophet appears at several strategic points in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées*, serving three distinct functions in Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetic]] argument. He is, first, a witness to the hermeneutical principle that [[prophecy|prophetic]] meaning requires careful interpretation rather than superficial reading. Second, he is deployed against those who would claim the name of [[God]]'s people without manifesting the reality. Third, he provides Pascal with a [[typological]] resource for understanding the relationship between the [[synagogue]] and the [[Church]].

Though Ezekiel receives fewer direct citations than figures like [[Moses]] or [[King David|David]], his role is no less significant. He is the prophet who, in Pascal's hands, speaks to the perennial problem of distinguishing genuine [[faith]] from its counterfeits — a problem central to Pascal's [[Jansenist]] context and his broader case for the unique truth of [[Christianity]].

## Prophetic Meaning and Interpretation

Pascal's most substantial engagement with Ezekiel concerns the interpretation of prophetic texts. In a key passage from Section X, Pascal invokes Ezekiel to illustrate his principle that prophetic meaning is not always what it appears to be on a superficial reading.

> [010-190] Ezekiel. — They say: These are the people of God who speak thus.

The fragment is part of a longer passage in which Pascal discusses how the prophets are to be read. The central problem is this: if the [[prophets]] sometimes appear to contradict each other, or to speak in ways that seem to authorize practices later condemned, how is one to understand them? Pascal's answer is that prophetic discourse has two levels — a "carnal" or literal sense and a "spiritual" or figural sense. The [[Jews]], reading carnally, understood the prophets as promising temporal blessings; the [[Christian]], reading spiritually, recognizes the same texts as pointing to [[Jesus Christ]] and the realities of the [[New Covenant]].

Ezekiel, in Pascal's deployment, exemplifies both the difficulty and the necessity of this spiritual reading. The prophet's words can be cited by those who wish to claim divine sanction for their own position — "They say: These are the people of God who speak thus." But the claim is hollow if it rests on a carnal reading that misses the spiritual substance. Pascal's Ezekiel thus becomes a test case for the principle that true [[understanding]] of [[Scripture]] requires more than quotation; it requires the interpretive key that only [[faith]] provides.

## The Judgment of False Claimants

Pascal's second use of Ezekiel is more polemical. In Section XIV, devoted to the theme of [[heretics]], Pascal deploys Ezekiel as a witness against those who lay claim to the name of God's people while speaking against the [[Church]].

> [014-250] Heretics. — Ezekiel. All the heathen, and also the Prophet, spoke evil of Israel. But the Israelites were so far from having the right to say to him, "You speak like the heathen," that he is most forcible upon this, that the heathen say the same as he.

The argument is characteristically Pascalian in its use of reversal. The [[Israelites]] — or their modern counterparts, the [[heretics]] — attempt to discredit Ezekiel by noting that his criticisms of [[Israel]] echo the criticisms made by the heathen. If Ezekiel sounds like a pagan critic of Israel, then surely he must be on the wrong side? Pascal's response is to grant the premise and reverse the conclusion: yes, Ezekiel sounds like the heathen — and this very fact proves that the criticism is valid, because the heathen, despite their [[idolatry]], can sometimes perceive truths that the self-deceived cannot.

This is Pascal's response to the perennial charge that critics of the [[Church]] within the Church are indistinguishable from critics outside it. The [[Jansenists]], Pascal's own party, were repeatedly accused of speaking like [[Protestants]] or worse. Pascal's Ezekiel passage provides the theological warrant for the reply: the fact that a [[prophet]]'s words resemble those of pagans does not discredit the prophet if the pagans are, on this point, incidentally correct. What matters is not who else says something but whether it is [[truth|true]].

## The Synagogue as Type

Pascal's third use of Ezekiel involves the relationship between the [[synagogue]] and the Church. In a passage from Section XIII, Pascal discusses Ezekiel in the context of typology — the reading of Old Testament institutions as figures or types of New Testament realities.

> [013-672] Ezekiel. — They say: These are the people of God who speak thus.

The context is Pascal's discussion of [[miracles]] and the authentication of true doctrine. The claim "These are the people of God" is, in Pascal's reading, one that can be made truly or falsely — and the test of its truth is not the claim itself but the [[signs]] that accompany it. Ezekiel, as a prophet, authenticated his message through [[miracle|miraculous]] signs; those who invoke his name without such authentication are merely borrowing a [[authority]] they do not possess.

This connects to Pascal's broader argument about the [[synagogue]] as a type. The synagogue was the bearer of divine promises; but when it rejected [[Christ]], it ceased to be the true people of God — even though it continued to claim the title. The passage from the synagogue to the Church was effected not by a change in the divine plan but by the fulfillment of what the synagogue had always pointed toward. Ezekiel, in this reading, is a witness to the possibility that those who say "These are the people of God" may be precisely the ones who are not.
