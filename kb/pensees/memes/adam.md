[[Adam]] stands at one pole of [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] theological [[anthropology]], with [[Jesus Christ]] at the other.

Between these two figures — the first Adam and the second, the source of [[the-fall|the Fall]] and the source of [[redemption]] — Pascal maps the entire drama of the [[human condition]]. To understand humanity's [[greatness and misery]], its capacity for both [[nobility]] and [[corruption]], one must understand the two Adams and their respective legacies.

This Adam-Christ dialectic is not merely a theological ornament in the *Pensées* but the structural backbone of Pascal's analysis of what it means to be human.

## Adam and Jesus Christ

Pascal's most condensed statement of the Adam-Christ relationship appears with characteristic brevity and force: "Adam, Jesus Christ" (007-151). The juxtaposition is itself the argument — two names that define two possible human conditions.

> [007-151] Adam, Jesus Christ.

The full significance of this pairing unfolds across the *Pensées*. Adam represents humanity in its original state: created good, endowed with [[free will]], capable of communion with [[God]]. Christ represents humanity redeemed: the [[original-sin|original corruption]] overcome, fellowship restored through a [[mediator]] who is both divine and human. Every human being lives somewhere on the trajectory between these two poles — either in Adam's fallen condition or in Christ's redeemed one.

Pascal sharpens this to an exclusive disjunction: "All morality in lust and in grace consists in Jesus Christ and in Adam" (007-637). There is no third option, no neutral ground from which to adjudicate between them. The moral and spiritual life is constituted entirely by one's relationship to these two figures and what they represent.

> [007-637] All morality in lust and in grace consists in Jesus Christ and in Adam.

## The Glorious State Before the Fall

Pascal insists that one cannot understand the [[human condition]] without recognizing the height from which humanity has fallen. The "glorious state of Adam" (008-128) is not a minor premise in his argument but an essential component: without an account of original [[greatness]], the present [[misery]] makes no sense.

> [008-128] We cannot understand the glorious state of Adam, nor the nature of his sin, nor the transmission of it to us.

Yet Pascal immediately acknowledges the limits of human understanding here. The transmission of [[original sin]] is, he concedes, a [[mystery]] — "incomprehensible" to reason and yet indispensable for making sense of experience. This is a characteristic Pascal move: acknowledging that a doctrine exceeds rational demonstration while insisting that it alone accounts for the data of human existence.

The doctrine is "offensive to reason" but without it "we should be incomprehensible to ourselves" — a formulation that captures Pascal's method of arguing not from first principles but from the explanatory power of [[Christian]] doctrine over the observed facts of human psychology and history.

## Adam as Type

Pascal draws on the Pauline typology that reads Adam as a figure — *forma futuri* — of Christ (010-147). The six days of creation, culminating in the formation of Adam, "are only the picture of the six ages to form Jesus Christ and the Church" (010-149). The first creation prefigures the new creation; the first Adam prefigures the last.

> [010-147] Adam forma futuri.

This typological reading does not diminish the historical reality of Adam in Pascal's thought but rather invests it with additional significance. Adam is both a real person — the first human being and the source of humanity's transmitted corruption — and a prophetic figure whose existence points beyond itself to the redemptive work of Christ.

Pascal extends this logic in a striking conditional: "If Adam had not sinned" (010-150), history would have taken a fundamentally different course. The contingency of the Fall — it need not have happened — underscores both the gravity of Adam's act and the gratuity of the [[grace]] that responds to it in Christ. Everything turns on the two Adams, and everything is resolved in the second.

## The Transmission of Tradition

Adam also functions in Pascal's argument for the historical continuity of [[biblical]] religion. The "tradition from Adam was fresh in Noah and in [[Moses]]" (009-324), establishing a chain of testimony that connects the first human being to the [[lawgiver]] of [[Israel]]. The proximity of witnesses — "Shem, who saw Lamech, who saw Adam, saw also Jacob" (009-499) — provides the historical warrant for the reliability of the biblical account.

This genealogical argument serves Pascal's broader apologetic strategy: the truth of Christianity rests not on abstract reasoning alone but on the credibility of a historical tradition transmitted through identifiable witnesses. Adam is the first link in this chain, and the integrity of the chain depends on each link being sound.
