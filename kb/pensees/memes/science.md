Blaise Pascal was a mathematician and experimental physicist of the first rank before he was a religious thinker. His scientific achievements — including foundational work in probability theory, hydrostatics, and the philosophy of mathematics — inform his *Pensées* in subtle but important ways. Pascal's understanding of the scope and limits of [[reason|natural reason]] is shaped by his own experience as a working scientist.

## The Two Orders

Pascal's doctrine of the [[three-orders|three orders]] places science within the order of the mind (*esprit*). Scientific knowledge is genuine knowledge, but it is knowledge of a limited domain. The order of the mind is higher than the order of the body (physical force, carnal goods), but infinitely lower than the order of charity (the supernatural knowledge of God through revelation and grace).

This hierarchy means that Pascal neither disparages science nor overestimates it. Science can tell us about the natural world, but it cannot tell us about our ultimate end, our moral condition, or the existence of God. The scientist who thinks science answers all questions misunderstands both science and the human condition.

## The Mathematical Mind and the Intuitive Mind

In one of the most influential passages of the *Pensées*, Pascal distinguishes between the mathematical mind (*esprit de géométrie*) and the intuitive mind (*esprit de finesse*). The mathematical mind proceeds by deduction from clear principles; the intuitive mind grasps truths in a single synthetic act of perception, seeing many things at once without being able to demonstrate them step by step.

> The mathematical mind and the intuitive mind: the former works slowly, from principles, with a sure method but a narrow scope; the latter moves swiftly, sees the whole at a glance, but cannot always give an account of its steps. (002-222)

This distinction is central to Pascal's epistemology. The truths of faith, morality, and human nature belong to the domain of the intuitive mind, not the mathematical. To demand scientific proof of religious truths is to apply the wrong cognitive faculty — a category mistake.

## Science and the Wager

Pascal's [[wager-argument|Wager]] reflects his scientific sensibility. The argument's decision-theoretic structure — weighing probabilities, calculating expected values — is the work of a mathematician. Yet the Wager's conclusion points beyond mathematics to the order of charity. Science can frame the question; it cannot answer it.
