[[Wretchedness|Wretchedness]] is, together with [[greatness-and-misery|greatness]], one half of Pascal's fundamental anthropological dialectic. To know only wretchedness is [[despair|despair]]; to know only greatness is [[pride|pride]]; to know both — and their [[christianity|Christian]] resolution — is the beginning of [[wisdom|wisdom]].

> [007-070] …incomprehensibility. — The greatness and the wretchedness of man are both so visible…

Pascal's method is to hold greatness and wretchedness together in tension, refusing every philosophy that resolves the paradox by suppressing one of its terms.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Evidence of Wretchedness

Pascal insists that wretchedness is not a theological dogma imposed from without but an empirical fact available to any honest observer:

> [007-457] Greatness, wretchedness. — The more light we have, the more greatness and the more wretchedness we discover in man.

This is a crucial methodological point: the *more* one knows, the *more* wretchedness one discovers. Wretchedness is not a sign of ignorance but of knowledge. The [[skepticism|sceptic]] who prides himself on seeing through illusions has merely exchanged one form of blindness for another; the [[christianity|Christian]] who knows his own wretchedness sees more, not less, than the [[unbelief|unbeliever]].

> [007-432] We seek happiness, and find only misery and death. We are incapable of not wishing for truth and happiness…

The universality of the search for happiness and the universality of its frustration is, for Pascal, the primary datum of philosophical anthropology. Every human being seeks happiness; no human being finds it. The explanation of *why* this is so is the central task of any adequate account of the human condition.

### The Two Errors

Pascal identifies two complementary errors in the treatment of wretchedness:

> [008-043] …his own wretchedness, and to know his own wretchedness without God.

To know wretchedness *without* God is the error of the [[atheism|atheist]] and the [[stoicism|Stoic]]: it leads to despair because it sees the wound but not the remedy.

> [008-047] …wretchedness, or to the despair of atheists, who know their own misery but not the Redeemer.

The atheist sees human wretchedness clearly — more clearly, in some respects, than the complacent believer — but seeing the wound without the physician is worse than not seeing the wound at all.

> [008-048] wretchedness, but not the Redeemer. And, as it is no less impossible to know God…

The complementary error is to know [[god|God]] without knowing wretchedness — the error of the [[deism|deist]] or the [[rationalism|rationalist]] who constructs a philosophical God unrelated to the actual human condition.

> [008-058] God without that of our own wretchedness, and of our own wretchedness without that of God.

Pascal's dialectical conclusion: knowledge of God and knowledge of wretchedness are mutually implicating. Each without the other is incomplete and dangerous. Only the two together — the greatness of God and the wretchedness of man — constitute adequate knowledge.

### Wretchedness as Proof

In a characteristic Pascalian inversion, wretchedness becomes not an argument *against* [[christianity|Christianity]] but an argument *for* it:

> [006-137] …for what in animals is nature we call wretchedness in man…

What is simply "nature" in animals becomes "wretchedness" in man — because man, unlike the animal, has the capacity to *know* his condition as wretched. The very fact that we experience our limitation as wretched is evidence of a residual [[greatness-and-misery|greatness]]: a being that was simply wretched, with no capacity to transcend its wretchedness even in thought, would not experience wretchedness as wretchedness.

> [007-073] …this is a great source of wretchedness. It must then give us a reason for this…

The experience of wretchedness demands an explanation. The [[christianity|Christian]] doctrine of the [[original-sin|Fall]] provides one; no other philosophy, Pascal argues, provides an equally adequate one.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of wretchedness is the experiential pole of his [[greatness-and-misery|greatness-and-misery]] dialectic. It is not simply the claim that human life involves suffering — every philosophy acknowledges that. It is the stronger claim that suffering, limitation, and inner contradiction are *structural* features of the human condition, not accidental ones, and that they point beyond themselves to a theological explanation.

The Pascalian innovation is the refusal to resolve the paradox: man is great *in* his wretchedness (because he can recognise it), and wretched *in* his greatness (because his highest aspirations are frustrated). [[Epictetus|Epictetus]] and [[montaigne|Montaigne]] each see one half; [[christianity|Christianity]] alone sees both.

The pastoral implication is that [[conversion|conversion]] does not eliminate wretchedness — it recontextualises it. The [[christianity|Christian]] is not less wretched than the [[unbelief|unbeliever]] but more aware of his wretchedness, and therefore more open to the [[grace|grace]] that alone can heal it.
