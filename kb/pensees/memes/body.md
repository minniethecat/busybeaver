[[Body|Body]] (corps), in Pascal's [[anthropology|anthropology]], is the material principle of human existence — "different in kind" from the [[soul|soul]] yet incomprehensibly united to it in what Pascal calls the most baffling fact of human experience.

> (002-281) different in kind, soul and body. For it is

The body is not merely the soul's container or instrument; it is the soul's intimate partner in every human act. Pascal's treatment of the body is neither Platonic contempt for matter nor Cartesian mechanism but an Augustinian recognition that the body is at once good (as created), disordered (as fallen), and redeemable (through [[grace|grace]]).

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Mind-Body Problem

Pascal confronts the mind-body problem with characteristic directness — not by solving it but by insisting on its insolubility:

> (002-304) and body, but that this mixture would

> (002-307) conceive what the body is, still less what the mind

> (002-308) least of all how a body should be united to a mind. This

These three fragments form a descending series: we cannot conceive the body, still less the [[mind|mind]], and least of all their union. This is not sceptical despair but epistemological [[humility|humility]]: the union of [[soul|soul]] and body is a fact of experience that [[reason|reason]] cannot explain.

Pascal's point is anthropological as much as metaphysical. The human being is the site of an incomprehensible union, and this very incomprehensibility is evidence that a merely naturalistic account of the human must fail.

### The Body and the Automaton

> (002-340) is made the very body which she animates, and those

The soul "makes" the body it animates — not in the sense of creating it but in the sense of shaping it into a human body rather than a mere organism. The [[automaton|automaton]] is the body considered as self-operating mechanism, trainable through [[custom|custom]] and repetition.

This is the physiological foundation of [[pascals-wager|the Wager]]: "bend the automaton" means train the body through repeated acts of [[faith|faith]]-adjacent practice — attending [[mass|Mass]], taking holy water — until the disposition of belief takes hold. The body leads; the soul follows.

### The Body in the Expanse of Nature

> (002-206) as our body occupies in the expanse of nature

Pascal situates the body within his cosmic vision of [[two-infinities|the two infinities]]. The body is the middle term — smaller than the visible universe yet larger than the microscopic world within a mite. This spatial [[disproportion|disproportion]] is the body's metaphysical condition: it is suspended between immensities, at home in neither.

> (002-138) sustained in the body given him by nature between the

The body is "given by nature" — it is received, not chosen. This passivity of embodiment is central to Pascal's analysis of the human condition: we did not choose to be bodies, yet we cannot escape being bodies.

### The Body as Locus of Concupiscence

> (002-1075) which has its root in the body, and which is continued into

[[Concupiscence|Concupiscence]] has its root in the body. This Augustinian claim identifies the body as the seat of disordered desire — not because matter is evil but because the Fall has disrupted the proper hierarchy of soul over body. The body, meant to serve, now rules.

> (002-710) sins indiscriminately to everybody; it allows

The universality of bodily sin — "indiscriminately to everybody" — underscores Pascal's doctrine of [[original-sin|original sin]]. No one escapes the body's concupiscent pull; [[grace|grace]] alone liberates.

### The Body and Self-Knowledge

> (003-140) I know not what my body is, nor my senses, nor my

Pascal's confession of ignorance — "I know not what my body is" — is not rhetorical but real. The body is opaque to introspection. We inhabit our bodies without understanding them, a fact that should induce the [[scepticism|sceptical]] humility that prepares for [[faith|faith]].

> (003-500) be joined to the body, and that we should have no s

> (003-518) Our soul is cast into a body, where it

The verb "cast" (jetée) captures Pascal's sense of embodiment as thrownness — the soul finds itself in a body not of its choosing, in a world not of its making. This existential dimension of Pascal's thought anticipates themes that would become central in twentieth-century phenomenology.

### The Body's Frailty

> (002-133) fact that our body, which a little while ago was

> (001-012) are before the eyes of everybody. One has only to

The body's mortality — "a little while ago was" — is visible to all. Pascal's [[death|death]] meditation is rooted in the body's frailty. The body that was alive becomes a corpse; the transition is both ordinary and terrifying, and it is the horizon against which all human projects must be evaluated.

## Analysis

Pascal's account of the body serves three functions in his larger project. First, it grounds his [[anthropology|anthropology]]: the human being is neither angel nor beast but the incomprehensible union of both, and every adequate account of [[truth|truth]], [[morality|morality]], and [[god|God]] must reckon with this fact.

Second, the body is the locus of Pascal's practical apologetics. [[Pascals-wager|The Wager]] works through the body — through the [[automaton|automaton]], [[custom|custom]], and the habituation of desire — because Pascal recognises that belief is not merely intellectual but somatic.

Third, the body is the site of [[concupiscence|concupiscence]] and therefore of the human need for [[grace|grace]]. The body's disorder — its refusal to stay in its proper place — is the Fall made visible. Only the [[three-orders|order of charity]], which subordinates body to [[soul|soul]] and both to [[god|God]], restores the body to its created integrity.
