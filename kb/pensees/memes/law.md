[[Law|Law]], in Pascal's treatment, is a concept stretched across incompatible domains — and the failure to distinguish which kind of law is at issue in any given context is, for Pascal, among the most consequential of human errors.

> [005-060] self-contained, it is law and nothing more. He who will investigate the motive…

Pascal's insight is that law, when investigated to its foundation, reveals not reason but [[force|force]] or [[custom|custom]] — a discovery that is at once intellectually liberating and socially dangerous.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Natural Law and Its Obscurity

Pascal acknowledges the *existence* of natural law while denying that human beings have reliable access to it:

> [005-046] Doubtless there are natural laws; but good reason once corrupted has corrupted all.

The corruption of [[reason|reason]] by the [[original-sin|Fall]] means that natural law, though real, cannot function as a stable foundation for human society. What we take to be natural law is almost always custom rationalised after the fact.

> [005-036] that it resides in natural laws, common to every country. They would certainly…

Pascal's [[skepticism|scepticism]] about natural-law theories is radical: if natural law were truly universal and accessible, there would be no disagreement about it. The fact of persistent moral and legal diversity across cultures is itself evidence that natural law is not available to fallen reason.

> [005-067] must, it is said, get back to the natural and fundamental laws of the State…

### Civil Law: Custom and Force

Civil law, for Pascal, rests on [[custom|custom]] and ultimately on [[force|force]] — not on [[justice|justice]]:

> [005-005] law that the elder gets all. "My friend, you were born on this side of the mountain…

The law of primogeniture — that the elder child inherits everything — is Pascal's recurring example of a custom that has acquired the force of law without any rational justification. It is "law" only because it has been repeated long enough to seem natural.

> [004-416] The sanctity of this law.—12. By the course of the world, it is a very extraordinary thing…

Pascal observes that the sanctity attributed to laws is a social achievement, not a metaphysical property. Laws become sacred through time and habituation, not through rational demonstration. And to strip them of this sanctity — to trace them back to their origin in force or custom — is to "annihilate" them.

> [005-057] Nothing is so faulty as those laws which correct faults. He who obeys them because they are just…

The paradox of legal reform: laws that attempt to correct faults are themselves "faulty," because they expose the conventionality of all law and thus weaken the authority of law as such. Pascal is not defending conservatism; he is describing the structural fragility of legal order.

### Divine Law

Above both natural and civil law stands divine law, which operates on entirely different principles:

> [002-331] To transpose after the laws to the following title.

Divine law, unlike civil law, is grounded in [[revelation|revelation]] rather than custom or force. And unlike natural law, it is accessible — but only through [[faith-and-reason|faith]], not through reason alone.

Pascal's [[jansenism|Jansenist]] theology sharpens this distinction: divine law is not a general moral code accessible to all rational beings but a specific revelation given to a particular people (the [[jews|Jews]]) and fulfilled in a particular person ([[christ|Jesus Christ]]).

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of law bridges his [[political-philosophy|political philosophy]] and his [[apologetics|apologetics]]. Politically, he is a realist: civil law cannot be grounded in reason, and the attempt to do so — the rationalist-legalist project — leads to anarchy because it exposes the groundlessness of all human law. The only stable solution is custom, which makes obedience habitual rather than contested.

Apologetically, Pascal uses the failure of natural-law theories as evidence for the [[original-sin|Fall]]: the fact that human beings cannot agree on what natural law requires is itself a sign that human nature is corrupted. Divine law — the law of [[charity|charity]] — is the only law that does not rest on force or custom, because it rests on the authority of [[god|God]] himself.

The Pascalian move is to refuse the conflation: civil law is not divine law, and divine law is not civil law. To claim divine authority for civil law is [[idolatry|idolatry]]; to reduce divine law to civil law is [[secularism|secularism]]. The [[three-orders|three orders]] framework keeps each kind of law in its proper domain.
