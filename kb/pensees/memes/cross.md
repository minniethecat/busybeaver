## Definition

The **Cross** in Pascal's [[Pensées]] is the central theological symbol of divine action through apparent weakness. Pascal articulates a distinctive theology of the cross in which God's chosen mode of self-revelation is not through power, wisdom, or signs but through the "foolishness" of Christ crucified. "Not wisdom, nor signs, but only the cross and foolishness" (008-227) — this opposition defines Pascal's understanding of how [[God]] makes Himself known in a world that expects [[Glory]] and power.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's theology of the cross draws on the Pauline contrast between the wisdom of the world and the foolishness of God. He applies this contrast to the entire pattern of divine revelation. The Jews expected signs; the Greeks sought wisdom. But God chose what appears weak and foolish in the eyes of the world — the cross — as the instrument of salvation (008-227). This is the fundamental pattern of divine action: God reveals Himself through hiddenness and apparent defeat.

The cross is the culmination of [[Jesus Christ]]'s mission of self-emptying. Pascal describes the suffering and death on the cross as the indispensable means by which redemption is accomplished (007-637). Without Christ's death on the cross, humanity remains in the state of ignorance and corruption that Pascal so thoroughly documents in his analysis of the [[Wretchedness|human condition]].

The cross functions as a figure — a typological fulfillment of Old Testament patterns. Pascal treats the cross as prefigured in the Old Testament: the flood, circumcision, the cross of Saint Andrew all serve as types pointing toward the cross of Christ (013-130, 013-152). This figural reading places the cross within Pascal's larger hermeneutic of [[Prophecy]] and fulfillment, where the Old Testament contains hidden anticipations of the central Christian [[Mystery|mysteries]].

The historical circumstances of Christ's crucifixion carry theological weight for Pascal. Christ died on the cross "between two thieves" (012-269), a detail Pascal treats as significant — the innocent one placed among the guilty, a pattern of substitutionary identification. The crucifixion is also a demonstration of divine sovereignty through apparent powerlessness: through the seeming defeat of the cross, "kings made submission to the cross" (011-664), a historical transformation that Pascal attributes to the Spirit's power.

Pascal's sacramental theology connects the cross to the Eucharist. The sacrament of the altar "is also a type of the cross and of glory" (014-065), linking Christ's once-for-all sacrifice to its ongoing representation in the Church's worship. The cross is not merely a past event but a present reality mediated through the sacraments.

## Key Passages

> Not wisdom, nor signs, but only the cross and foolishness. For those, who are called by this wisdom among men, the cross is the power of God. (008-227)

> Without Jesus Christ man must be in vice and misery. With Jesus Christ man is exempt from vice and misery. In Him is all our virtue and all our happiness. Without Him there is only vice, misery, error, darkness, death. (007-637)

> Jesus Christ offered that of the cross for all. (012-318)

> A Messiah triumphing over death by his own death. Christ on the cross between two thieves. (012-235, 012-269)

> The sacrament is also a type of the cross and of glory. (014-065)

## Analysis

Pascal's theology of the cross operates at multiple levels. Epistemologically, the cross is the pattern of divine self-revelation — God makes Himself known not through overwhelming evidence that compels assent but through a hiddenness that [[Faith]] alone can penetrate. This aligns with Pascal's larger argument that God provides enough light for those who seek Him and enough obscurity for those who do not. The cross is the supreme instance of this dialectic of concealment and disclosure.

Apologetically, the cross serves as a criterion for distinguishing true religion from its counterfeits. A religion whose central symbol is an executed criminal — a sign of shame and weakness — cannot be a human invention designed to flatter human pride. The "foolishness" of the cross is evidence of Christianity's divine origin, because no merely human religion would build itself around such an apparently self-defeating symbol.

The cross also completes Pascal's analysis of the [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness-and-misery]] dialectic. Human greatness is most fully revealed when human power is renounced; divine power is most fully revealed in the weakness of the crucifixion. The cross inverts the values of the three orders: what appears as shame in the order of the body and foolishness in the order of the mind is wisdom and [[Glory]] in the order of [[Charity]].

Pascal's emphasis on the cross connects to his [[Jansenism|Jansenist]] theological commitments. The movement's focus on the gratuity of [[Grace]], the depth of human corruption, and the centrality of Christ's redemptive work all converge on the theology of the cross. The cross is where divine justice and mercy meet — where the full cost of sin and the full extent of divine love are simultaneously revealed.
