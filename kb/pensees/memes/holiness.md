Holiness, in Pascal's Pensées, is not a matter of external religious observance but an inward and spiritual quality
— "inward and entirely spiritual holiness" (002-1075)
— that belongs to [[God]]'s own order and is communicated to the [[Soul|soul]] through [[Grace]].

It is a concept that cuts against the grain of Pascal's [[Jansenism|Jansenist]] polemic against Jesuit casuistry: holiness cannot be reduced to rule-following or moral effort.

True holiness is invisible — hidden from human eyes and often hidden even from the one who possesses it — and this hiddenness is essential to its nature.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal develops the concept of holiness across several registers: anthropological, Christological, and ecclesiological.

> (002-1075) Inward and entirely spiritual holiness, the inward impiety of proud men.

The contrast is stark: holiness is inward, impiety is outward. The "proud men" Pascal targets are those who perform religious observance while their interior remains untouched — the Pharisees of his own time.

Holiness, for Pascal, begins in the hidden place where only [[God]] sees.

> (007-637) Nor a holiness exempt from evil.

This is a crucial qualification: holiness in this life is not sinlessness.

Pascal explicitly rejects any doctrine of perfectionism
— the elect remain sinners, and their holiness consists not in the absence of evil but in the orientation of the Will toward [[God]] despite the continuing presence of [[Concupiscence]].

> (010-033) People holy with an invisible holiness, and fill them with an eternal glory.

The invisibility of holiness is a recurring theme. Pascal finds in the [[Scripture|prophets]]
— particularly Isaiah
— the promise that God will create a people whose holiness is not a matter of public display but of interior transformation.

This is the antithesis of the "holiness" claimed by those who [[Pascal's Critique of Philosophy|trust in external works]].

> (012-244) A place of rest and holiness; to make it holy to God; to make it a sanctuary.

Pascal uses the language of sanctuary and temple — but spiritualised. The "place of rest and holiness" is not a geographical location but the [[Soul|soul]] in which [[God]] dwells.

This is consistent with Pascal's interiorising reading of the Kingdom of God: the true temple is the heart.

## Key Passages

> (003-176) Redemption by the holiness of their behaviour, they at last arrive at a true knowledge of Jesus Christ.

Pascal holds together two truths that might seem in tension: holiness is a gift, not an achievement — yet it manifests in behaviour.

The behaviour is not the cause of holiness but its evidence; it is the outward sign of an inward reality.

> (012-484) Jesus Christ is in His own order of holiness.

Pascal locates [[Jesus Christ]] within the [[Three Orders]] schema: Christ belongs to the order of [[Charity]]
— which is the order of holiness
— not to the order of mind ([[Philosophy]]) or the order of body (political power).

This is why the [[Jews in Pascal's Pensées|Jews]] missed him: they were looking for a king in the order of body, but Christ came in the order of holiness.

> (012-494) Holiness. But He came there appropriately to His own order.

The adverb "appropriately" is significant: Christ's coming in the order of holiness is not a failure to meet expectations but the fulfilment of a deeper logic.

> (013-541) Perpetuity, holiness, and miracles. The heretics denied these marks.

Holiness is one of the marks of the true Church — alongside perpetuity and [[Miracle|miracles]].

Pascal's use of "holiness" as a mark of the Church connects his mystical theology to his apologetic project: the holiness of the Church is an argument for its divine origin.

## Analysis

Pascal's doctrine of holiness functions as a critique of externalism — the reduction of religion to Ritual, rule-following, or social conformity.

The target of this critique is not only the Jesuits (whom Pascal accused of laxism) but any form of [[Christianity in Pascal's Pensées|Christianity]] that substitutes external performance for interior transformation.

The invisibility of holiness means that it cannot be measured, compared, or advertised. It resists the human tendency to turn spiritual realities into status markers.

This is profoundly Augustinian: Augustine had insisted that the true Church is invisible — known only to [[God]] — and Pascal extends this principle from the Church to the individual [[Soul|soul]].

> (013-601) Holiness is not a human achievement but a divine communication.

Pascal's anti-Pelagianism reaches its culmination here. Holiness is not something the human being produces; it is something [[God]] gives.

The [[Humility]] that Pascal demands is not false modesty but the accurate recognition that whatever holiness one possesses is received, not generated.

This is the practical consequence of Pascal's theology of [[Grace]]: if grace is wholly gratuitous, then holiness is wholly derivative — a participation in God's holiness, not a human creation.
