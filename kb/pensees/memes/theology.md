[[Theology|Theology]] in Pascal's *Pensees* is not a detached academic discipline but the living framework within which all other questions — [[apologetics|apologetic]], [[anthropology|anthropological]], philosophical — find their resolution.

Pascal's theological commitments are Augustinian and [[jansenism|Jansenist]]: he holds to the radical corruption of [[human-nature|human nature]] after the Fall, the gratuity and necessity of [[grace|divine grace]], and the hiddenness of [[God|God]] (*Deus absconditus*) who reveals himself only to those he chooses.

## In Pascal's Pensees

Pascal was not a professional theologian, and he was conscious of writing for an audience for whom theological technicalities would be alienating rather than persuasive:

> [001-293] It is not right that honest men should be obliged to speak on everything, even on theology.

This is not a dismissal of theology but a recognition of its place: theology is indispensable, but it must not be confused with the experiential and existential register in which the *Pensees* operates. The apologist who speaks as though every reader were a seminarian has already failed.

Pascal's theological inheritance runs through [[saint-augustine|Saint Augustine]], whose doctrine of grace was defended by the [[jansenism|Jansenists]] of [[port-royal|Port-Royal]] against what they regarded as the semi-Pelagian tendencies of Jesuit theology.

## Key Augustinian Doctrines

Three theological convictions structure the *Pensees*:

1. **Original Sin**: [[human-nature|Human nature]] is not merely weakened but radically corrupted. Without [[grace|grace]], the human being can do nothing toward salvation.

2. **Efficacious Grace**: [[grace|Grace]] is not merely offered but effectual — it accomplishes what it intends. The [[will|will]], liberated by grace, loves God freely and necessarily.

3. **Predestination**: The inscrutable divine choice by which some are granted grace and others are not is, for Pascal, not a problem to be solved but a mystery to be acknowledged in [[humility|humility]].

These doctrines, Pascal believed, are not arbitrary dogmas but the only coherent account of the observable facts of the [[human-condition|human condition]].
