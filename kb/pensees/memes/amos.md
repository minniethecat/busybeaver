Amos, the eighth-century BCE prophet of social justice, appears in Pascal's *Pensées* as a witness against the reduction of religion to external observance. Pascal draws on Amos's prophetic critique to distinguish between genuine and counterfeit worship — a distinction central to his larger argument about the corruption of human religious instinct.

## The Rejection of Empty Ritual

Pascal's most direct citation of Amos is pointed and programmatic: "That their feasts are displeasing to God. Amos v, 21" (009-223). The verse Pascal invokes is one of the most radical in the prophetic canon — God's declaration through Amos that he hates and despises Israel's religious festivals. For Pascal, this is not merely an Old Testament curiosity but a permanent theological principle: ritual without righteousness is not merely deficient but offensive to God.

This citation serves Pascal's apologetic purpose by establishing that the prophetic tradition itself anticipated and condemned the formalism that Pascal attributes to his Jesuit opponents and to nominal Christians generally. Amos becomes a biblical warrant for Pascal's insistence that external religion without interior conversion is a form of self-deception.

## The Silence of Prophecy as Judgment

Pascal notes a more ominous dimension of Amos's ministry: "That the Jews should be without prophets (Amos), without king, without prince, without sacrifice, without idol" (009-250). Here Amos prophesies a judgment that takes the form of divine silence — the withdrawal of prophetic guidance itself. Pascal reads this as a structural element in sacred history: God's self-concealment is as deliberate as his self-revelation.

This theme connects Amos to Pascal's larger dialectic of the *Deus absconditus* — the hidden God. The prophetic silence Amos announces becomes, in Pascal's reading, the condition of all post-biblical humanity. We live after the prophets, in the space between the promise and its fulfillment, where faith must operate without the clarity of direct divine speech.

## The Prophet of Justice

Pascal pairs Amos with Zechariah in his discussion of social justice: "Prophecy. — Amos and Zechariah. They have sold the just" (011-406). The elliptical quality of Pascal's citation is characteristic — he assumes his reader's familiarity with the prophetic text and deploys it as an allusion rather than an argument. The selling of the just for silver, Amos's great indictment of Israel's economic corruption, becomes in Pascal's usage a type of Judas's betrayal of Christ.

## The Universalist Vision

Pascal finds in Amos a foreshadowing of the call of the Gentiles: "for a light of the Gentiles. Amos viii" (011-664). This verse from the apocalyptic section of Amos expands the prophet's scope beyond Israel to all nations, providing Pascal with evidence that the prophetic tradition always contained a universalist impulse that reaches its fulfillment in Christianity.
