## Definition

In Pascal's apologetics, **unbelief** (incrédulité) is the condition of those who do not believe in the [[God]] revealed in [[Jesus Christ]]. Unlike the philosophical [[Skepticism|sceptic]] who claims intellectual grounds for doubt, the unbeliever in Pascal's sense is one who lacks [[Faith]] and — crucially — is *wretched* because of this lack. Unbelief is not a neutral intellectual position but a state of existential deprivation. Pascal's apologetic project is directed precisely at curing this condition, most famously in the argument known as [[Pascal's Wager]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Unbelief as Wretchedness

Pascal's approach to the unbeliever begins not with argument but with compassion. The unbeliever is to be pitied, not merely refuted:

> (003-028) To begin by pitying unbelievers; they are wretched enough by their condition. We ought only to revile them where it is beneficial; but this does them harm.

This is a distinctive pastoral stance. Pascal does not treat unbelief as a crime or a moral failing but as a misfortune. The unbeliever is not wicked — he is "wretched." This is consistent with Pascal's analysis of the human condition without God: if [[Wretchedness]] is the universal human state, then unbelief is not a departure from the norm but the norm's fullest expression. The unbeliever simply lacks the one remedy that could address his wretchedness.

### The Cure for Unbelief: Pascal's Wager

Pascal's most famous argument is framed explicitly as a response to unbelief. The imaginary interlocutor of the Wager wants to believe but cannot:

> (003-644) You would like to attain faith, and do not know the way; you would like to cure yourself of unbelief, and ask the remedy for it.

The "remedy" Pascal prescribes is surprising: not more argument, but embodied practice. "Learn of those who have been bound like you" — those who have made the journey from unbelief to faith. Their method was not to wait for rational certainty but to act: "Follow the way by which they began; by acting as if they believed, taking the holy water, having masses said, etc."

This is the practical dimension of Pascal's famous Wager argument. The mathematical expectation (infinite gain vs. finite stake) provides the rational motivation to *begin*, but the actual transformation from unbelief to belief is accomplished through bodily habituation. Pascal's [[Automaton]] doctrine — the body must be trained before the mind can follow — applies here: unbelief is as much a habit of the body as an error of the intellect.

### Man Is Naturally Credulous and Incredulous

Pascal identifies unbelief as part of a deeper paradox of human nature:

> (002-930) Contraries. Man is naturally credulous and incredulous, timid and rash.

This aphorism captures the contradictory character of the human disposition toward belief. We believe too readily what flatters us and doubt too readily what challenges us. Unbelief is not simply the absence of belief but an oscillation between credulity (toward the world, toward [[Custom]], toward [[Diversion]]) and incredulity (toward [[God]], toward [[Revelation]], toward the testimony of believers). The unbeliever is not someone who believes nothing — he is someone whose beliefs are disordered.

### Unbelief and the Limits of Reason

Pascal's treatment of unbelief reflects his broader epistemology. Unbelief cannot be overcome by rational proof alone because [[Reason]] has limits. The unbeliever who demands demonstrative certainty before believing misunderstands both the nature of the object (an infinite, hidden God) and the nature of the subject (a finite, embodied creature whose belief is shaped by habit and desire as much as by inference). This is why Pascal does not offer the Wager as a proof but as a *remedy* — it addresses the will and the body, not merely the intellect.

Unbelief is thus intimately connected to Pascal's critique of [[Dogmatism]] and [[Skepticism]]. The dogmatist claims to know God through reason; the sceptic claims reason cannot know God. Both share the assumption that reason is the sole arbiter of belief. Pascal's response to unbelief rejects this assumption: [[Faith]] is reasonable but not derived from reason, and the path from unbelief to faith passes through the body and the [[Heart]].

### The Role of Grace

Finally, Pascal insists that the cure of unbelief requires divine [[Grace]]. The remedy of embodied practice (taking holy water, attending Mass) is effective only because God uses these means to work faith. "Even this will naturally make you believe, and deaden your acuteness" (003-644) — the word "naturally" does not exclude grace but describes its ordinary operation. [[Conversion]] from unbelief to faith is a divine work that employs natural means.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of unbelief is remarkable for its pastoral sensitivity. He does not berate the unbeliever or demand intellectual submission to improbable claims. Instead, he diagnoses unbelief as a symptom of the universal human condition — wretchedness — and offers a practical path out of it. The genius of this approach is that it takes the unbeliever's experience seriously: the inability to believe is real, and it cannot be overcome by mere volition.

The embodied dimension of Pascal's cure for unbelief anticipates modern psychology of belief formation. Belief is not solely a function of evidence assessment; it is shaped by community, practice, and bodily habituation. Pascal understood this centuries before experimental psychology confirmed it. The directive to "act as if they believed" is not hypocrisy but formation — the body leads the mind toward what it cannot yet fully affirm.

Unbelief also has structural significance in Pascal's apologetic system. The unbeliever is not the enemy but the audience. Every argument in the *Pensées* — the Wager, the analysis of [[Greatness and Misery of Man|human greatness and misery]], the examination of [[Prophecy]] and [[Miracle]], the portrait of [[Jesus Christ]] — is addressed to the condition of unbelief. Pascal's apologetics is not a fortress to be defended but a hospital for those who need to be cured.
