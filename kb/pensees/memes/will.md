# Will

The **will** (*la volonté*) occupies a central place in [[Blaise Pascal]]'s philosophical psychology. It is the faculty through which we love, choose, and commit ourselves — and it is, for Pascal, as deeply implicated in the formation of [[Belief|belief]] as the intellect. The will's corruption by [[Self-Love|self-love]] is a principal theme of Pascal's [[Anthropology|anthropology]], but so too is its capacity for redemption through [[Grace|grace]].

## The Will and Belief

Pascal insists against the intellectualist tradition that belief is not solely a matter of the understanding. The will plays an indispensable role:

> The will is one of the chief factors in belief, not that it creates belief, but because things are true or false according to the aspect in which we look at them. The will, which prefers one aspect to another, turns away the mind from considering the qualities of all that it does not like to see; and thus the mind, moving in accord with the will, stops to consider the aspect which it likes, and so judges by what it sees. (002-099)

This is not a counsel of wishful thinking. Rather, it is a psychological observation about how belief actually works. The intellect does not operate in isolation from desire; what we attend to, what we find plausible, what we are willing to entertain — all are shaped by the orientation of the will. This is why Pascal's [[Apologetics|apologetic]] strategy addresses the will as much as the intellect. The famous conclusion of the [[Wager|Wager]] argument — "Endeavour then to convince yourself, not by increase of proofs of God, but by the abatement of your passions" (003-662) — recognizes that the obstacle to faith is more often volitional than intellectual.

## The Will's Natural Orientation

Pascal asserts a natural teleology of the will: "It is natural for the mind to believe, and for the will to love; so that, for want of true objects, they must attach themselves to false" (002-081). The will is made for love of the true good — which is to say, for love of [[God]]. When it cannot attain this, it does not simply cease to will; it attaches itself to substitute objects. This is the psychological mechanism underlying what Pascal calls [[Divertissement|diversion]]: the restless pursuit of finite goods that can never satisfy an appetite made for the infinite.

The natural orientation of the will toward love means that the human being is constitutively a desiring creature. We cannot choose not to love something; we can only choose what we love. The question is not whether we shall have a ruling love but which love shall rule.

## Self-Love and the Divided Will

The corruption of the will by [[Self-Love|self-love]] (*amour-propre*) is one of Pascal's most persistent themes. "The nature of self-love and of this human Ego is to love self only and consider self only" (002-100). The self turned inward upon itself becomes incapable of the love for which it was made. This is the psychological dimension of the [[Original Sin|Fall]]: the will that was created to love God now loves only itself, and even its apparent virtues are disguised forms of self-regard.

The result is a divided self. The human being experiences conflicting desires, pulled between the love of God and the love of self, between the aspirations of [[Greatness|greatness]] and the realities of [[Wretchedness|wretchedness]]. Pascal does not resolve this division through philosophical argument — it is, in his view, irresolvable by philosophy. Resolution comes only through the transformation of the will by [[Grace|grace]].

## The Three Orders

In a passage that systematizes his anthropology, Pascal distinguishes "three orders of things: the flesh, the spirit, and the will" (007-460). The carnal order concerns the body and material goods; the spiritual order concerns the intellect and knowledge; the order of the will — which Pascal also calls charity (*charité*) — concerns righteousness and the love of God. These orders are discontinuous and incommensurable: "The infinite distance between body and mind is a symbol of the infinitely more infinite distance between mind and charity" (007-793).

The will, in this highest sense, is not merely one faculty among others but the ordering principle of the whole person. A rightly ordered will subordinates flesh to spirit and spirit to charity. The disorder introduced by the Fall consists precisely in the inversion of this hierarchy.
