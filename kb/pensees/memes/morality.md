[[Morality|Morality]], in Pascal's hands, undergoes a destabilising inversion: "true morality makes light of morality" (la vraie morale se moque de la morale). This is not [[nihilism|nihilism]] but a meta-ethical claim about the [[three-orders|three orders]] — genuine morality belongs not to the order of [[mind|mind]] (where [[philosophy|philosophy]] operates) but to the order of [[charity|charity]] (where [[god|God]] acts).

> (001-100) eloquence, true morality makes light of morality; that

The aphorism's structure is crucial: Pascal does not say that morality is false but that "true morality" — a morality grounded in [[god|God]] rather than in [[reason|reason]] — relativises philosophical morality. The "morality" that is made light of is the morality of the [[philosopher|philosophers]], autonomous and self-grounding.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Morality of the Judgment

Pascal distinguishes three moralities, corresponding to the [[three-orders|three orders]]:

> (001-101) the morality of the judgment, which has no

> (001-102) of the morality of the intellect.

The morality of the [[judgment|judgment]] (order of [[body|body]]) is the morality of [[custom|custom]] and social convention. The morality of the [[intellect|intellect]] (order of [[mind|mind]]) is the morality of [[philosophy|philosophy]] — rational, principled, autonomous. Both are real within their orders but neither is ultimate. The morality of [[charity|charity]] (order of the [[heart|heart]]) surpasses both infinitely.

### The Insufficiency of Philosophical Morality

Pascal's critique of philosophical morality is anthropological rather than logical:

> (002-051) his morality, could have been corrected in

[[Philosophy|Philosophy]]'s morality "could have been corrected" — the [[conditional|conditional]] mood is damning. Philosophical morality is corrigible in principle but uncorrected in practice, because what [[morality|morality]] needs is not better arguments but a new [[heart|heart]].

> (002-060) me for the ignorance of morality in the time of affliction. Bu

The "ignorance of morality in the time of [[affliction|affliction]]" points to the practical impotence of philosophical ethics. When [[suffering|suffering]] arrives, the [[stoic|Stoic]]'s maxims are revealed as words — not false, but weightless. Only the morality that participates in [[charity|charity]] can bear the weight of affliction.

### Morality Transformed by the Three Orders

Pascal's [[three-orders|three orders]] framework transforms how morality itself is understood:

> (003-418) make an entire difference to morality. And yet philosophers

The difference that [[christianity|Christianity]] makes to morality is "entire" — not an addition to philosophical ethics but its reconfiguration. What the [[philosopher|philosophers]] call morality is, from the vantage of [[charity|charity]], only the vestibule of morality.

> (006-044) of morality.

> (006-137) all determine it in truth and morality?

In the latter fragment, [[truth|truth]] and [[morality|morality]] are paired — both are undetermined by [[reason|reason]] alone. The [[skepticism|sceptical]] crisis in epistemology has its counterpart in ethics: just as [[reason|reason]] cannot secure its own foundations, so morality cannot ground itself.

### Morality and Religion

Pascal treats the morality of [[christianity|Christianity]] as internal to its truth:

> (007-566) policy, morality, and justice; but in reality

The triplet of policy, morality, and [[justice|justice]] appears in a context that contrasts human appearance with [[divine|divine]] reality. Human morality is always entangled with [[politics|politics]] and [[power|power]] — only the morality of [[charity|charity]] is pure.

> (009-097) the morality, what the happiness held out

> (009-100) Its morality and happiness are absurd in t

> (009-115) duration, its perpetuity, its morality, its doctrine, and its effect

These fragments come from Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetic]] comparison of [[religions|religions]]. [[Christianity|Christianity]]'s morality is one of its distinguishing marks — not because Christians are more virtuous but because Christianity's moral teaching corresponds to the actual [[human condition|human condition]] in a way that [[philosophy|philosophy]]'s does not.

> (009-379) and in all times; but their morality cannot please me, nor can

The morality of non-Christian [[religions|religions]] "cannot please" Pascal — a judgment that is both personal and theological. What pleases is the morality that flows from [[charity|charity]], and charity is the gift of [[grace|grace]], not the achievement of [[reason|reason]].

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of morality is the application of his [[three-orders|three orders]] framework to ethics. The order of [[body|body]] generates the morality of [[custom|custom]]; the order of [[mind|mind]] generates the morality of [[philosophy|philosophy]]; the order of [[charity|charity]] generates the morality of [[god|God]] — which alone is "true morality."

"True morality makes light of morality" because philosophical morality, however internally coherent, cannot deliver what it promises. It cannot transform the [[heart|heart]]; it cannot bear the weight of [[affliction|affliction]]; it cannot reach the [[sovereign-good|sovereign good]]. These failures are not accidents but necessities — consequences of the disproportion between the [[means|means]] (reason) and the [[end|end]] (union with God).

The Pascalian alternative is not the abolition of morality but its relocation. Morality belongs to the order of [[charity|charity]], where it becomes not a set of rules but a participation in the life of [[god|God]] — a life whose form is [[jesus-christ|Christ]].
