Redemption, in Pascal's theological scheme, is the divine response to the human predicament — the act by which [[God]] restores what [[Original Sin|sin]] has corrupted.

It is the second term in Pascal's fundamental dialectic: corruption and redemption, the diagnosis and the cure.

> (003-174) Corruption of nature, and redemption by Jesus Christ.

This single sentence distils Pascal's entire apologetic programme: the human being is radically corrupted, and the only adequate remedy is the redemptive work of [[Jesus Christ]].

Without the premise of corruption, redemption is unnecessary; without the promise of redemption, the diagnosis of corruption is merely despair.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal presents redemption not as an abstract theological proposition but as the one hypothesis that makes sense of the observable data of the Human Condition.

> (008-136) Two proofs of corruption and redemption are drawn from the same source.

The "two proofs" are the [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness and misery]] of the human being.

[[Greatness and Misery of Man|Greatness]] — the human capacity for [[Reason]], [[Morality]], and the pursuit of the [[Sovereign Good]] — points to an original dignity, a nature created for [[God]].

[[Greatness and Misery of Man|Misery]] — the universal fact of [[Self-Love]], [[Diversion]], and the flight from self-knowledge — points to a fall, a corruption that affects every dimension of human existence.

> (003-176) Redemption by the holiness of their behaviour, they at last arrive at a true knowledge of Jesus Christ.

Redemption is not merely a legal transaction but a transformative process: it changes behaviour, and changed behaviour in turn deepens the knowledge of [[Jesus Christ|Christ]].

This is Pascal's version of the hermeneutical circle: knowledge of Christ leads to holiness, and holiness leads to deeper knowledge of Christ.

> (008-095) With their corruption and their redemption, all the teaching of the Bible is summed up.

Pascal reads the [[Scripture]] as a unified narrative structured by these two poles — the fall and the restoration. Every doctrine, every prophecy, every moral command finds its place within this arc.

For Pascal, this is a powerful apologetic argument: the [[Scripture|Scriptures]] are not a random collection of texts but a coherent story that corresponds to the actual human condition as experienced.

> (012-360) As far as He was concerned, He offered them redemption.

The "He" is [[Jesus Christ]], who offers redemption not as a possibility to be earned but as a gift to be received.

Pascal's Christology is resolutely Augustinian: the initiative is entirely divine. Human beings do not cooperate in their redemption; they receive it.

## Key Passages

> (014-236) Redemption without certitude!

This fragment encapsulates the [[Faith]] dimension of Pascal's soteriology.

Redemption is not demonstrable by [[Reason]]
— it cannot be verified by [[Philosophy|philosophical]] argument or empirical observation. It is an object of [[Faith]], and faith, by its nature, operates without [[Certainty|certitude]].

Pascal's [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]] is relevant here: one does not wager on what one knows but on what one hopes. Redemption is the content of that hope.

## Analysis

Redemption is the linchpin of Pascal's apologetic project.

The Pensées exist to persuade the unbeliever that [[Christianity in Pascal's Pensées|Christianity]] offers the only coherent account of the Human Condition
— and at the centre of that account is the claim that [[Jesus Christ]] redeems.

Without redemption, Pascal's analysis of the human condition would be purely negative: a relentless exposure of human [[Vanity]], [[Self-Love]], and [[Diversion]] with no way out.

With redemption, the analysis becomes a preparation for the Gospel: the diagnosis of corruption creates the appetite for the cure.

> (003-174) I maintain that the human heart is naturally corrupt, and that redemption by Jesus Christ is the only remedy.

The logic is diagnostic. Pascal does not argue that Christianity is plausible because it is comforting; he argues that it is plausible because it explains what every honest observer can see
— the paradox of [[Greatness and Misery of Man|human greatness and misery]].

Redemption is the hypothesis that accounts for the data, and the [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]] is the invitation to stake one's life on it.

Pascal's doctrine of redemption is also Christocentric in a way that distinguishes it from abstract theories of atonement.

Redemption is not a mechanism but a person: [[Jesus Christ]] himself is the redemption, and to know him is to participate in it.

This personalism is characteristic of Pascal's mature Theology and provides the bridge between his Apologetics and his mysticism.
