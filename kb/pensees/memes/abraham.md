[[Abraham]] appears in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées* as the father of [[faith]] and the human starting point of the [[covenant]] that distinguishes [[biblical]] religion from all competing traditions.

For Pascal, the particularity of Abraham — a single individual called out of [[idolatry]] to become the father of a chosen people — is not a difficulty to be explained away but a datum to be taken seriously. The scandal of particularity is, in Pascal's hands, transformed into an argument for authenticity: a religion that is true to the actual shape of human history will necessarily involve concrete, particular persons and events rather than abstract universal principles.

## Abraham and the God of the Covenant

The most famous invocation of Abraham in Pascal's corpus appears not in the body of the *Pensées* but in the "Memorial" — the record of his mystical experience of November 23, 1654, which he sewed into the lining of his coat and carried for the rest of his life.

> [008-077] The God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, the God of Jacob, not of the philosophers and scholars.

This cry — "the God of Abraham, not of the philosophers" — encapsulates Pascal's central theological conviction: the living God who enters into covenant with particular persons is categorically different from the abstract [[deity]] of philosophical [[theism]]. The God of [[René Descartes|Descartes]], who sets the world in motion and then withdraws, is not the God whom Pascal encountered in his night of fire. Abraham's God acts in history, makes promises, and calls for a response that engages the whole person — [[heart]] as well as [[reason]].

Pascal's deployment of Abraham thus serves both an apologetic and a polemical purpose: it grounds the truth of [[Christianity]] in concrete historical relationship while simultaneously undermining the adequacy of purely [[rationalist]] approaches to [[religion]].

## Abraham's Faith

Pascal draws attention to the quality of Abraham's faith, which is not the intellectual assent of the [[philosopher]] but the radical trust of one who stakes everything on a [[promise]]. "Abraham took nothing for himself" (007-637) — his faith was not a calculation of self-interest but a surrender of everything he might have claimed as his own.

> [007-637] Abraham took nothing for himself, but only for his servants.

This self-emptying quality of Abraham's faith becomes, for Pascal, a measure of authentic religious response. The willingness to relinquish control, to trust a word spoken into darkness, is the antithesis of both the rationalist's demand for [[certainty]] and the skeptic's retreat into perpetual suspension of judgment. Abraham models a third way: the venture of faith that is neither irrational nor fully demonstrable but which corresponds to the actual conditions of human existence.

## Abraham Amidst Idolatry

One of Pascal's distinctive emphases is the context in which Abraham's faith emerged. He was not raised in a tradition of [[monotheism]] but rather "was surrounded by idolaters" when God made himself known (009-277). This point carries apologetic weight: the biblical account does not present Abraham as the product of an evolutionary religious development but as the recipient of a disruptive divine initiative.

> [009-277] Abraham was surrounded by idolaters, when God made known to him the mystery of the Messiah.

The fact that Abraham's faith arose in the midst of idolatry rather than from it strengthens Pascal's case for the supernatural origin of biblical religion. Had the Jewish conception of [[God]] emerged gradually from its polytheistic environment, it would be explicable in purely natural terms. That it appears as a radical break — a revelation received, not a synthesis achieved — marks it as qualitatively different from the [[religions]] that surround it.

Pascal extends this logic through the entire [[covenant]] history: "God raised them up from Abraham" (010-039), creating a people where there was none, establishing a tradition that bears the marks of its divine origin in its very particularity and persistence.

## The Posterity of Abraham

The covenant promise to Abraham includes the assurance of descendants as numerous as the stars, yet Pascal notes the precariousness of this promise throughout the biblical narrative. Circumcision marks the covenant in Abraham's flesh (009-165), but the promise repeatedly seems threatened — by Sarah's barrenness, by the testing of [[Isaac]], by the captivity in [[Egypt]].

Pascal traces the fulfillment of the promise through the subsequent history of [[Israel]], seeing in its survival a continuation of the [[divine]] initiative that began with Abraham. The chain of witnesses — from Abraham through [[Moses]] through the [[prophets]] — preserves and transmits what was first entrusted to the patriarch.

In [[Isaiah]] Pascal finds the acknowledgment that Abraham's significance transcends mere physical descent: "Doubtless thou art our Father, though Abraham be ignorant of us" (009-183). The covenant is not merely familial but spiritual, and its ultimate fulfillment lies beyond [[ethnicity|ethnic]] particularity in the universal scope of Christ's redemptive work.
