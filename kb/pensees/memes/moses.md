[[Moses]] appears throughout [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées* as the central figure in the transmission of religious tradition and the foundation of [[Judaism|Jewish]] law.

For Pascal, the historical credibility of Moses — as a witness, lawgiver, and prophet — is essential to establishing the antiquity and reliability of the [[biblical]] record, which in turn grounds the argument from [[prophecy]] that points toward [[Jesus Christ]].

Pascal's apologetic method treats Moses not as a figure of [[faith]] alone but as a historical witness whose testimony can be examined by [[reason]].

## Moses in Pascal's Historical Apologetics

In Section IX of the *Pensées*, Pascal develops an extended argument for the credibility of the Mosaic tradition against skeptical objections. He confronts the question directly: which is more worthy of belief — the testimony of Moses or the competing claims of other ancient civilizations?

Pascal's characteristic response is not to dismiss doubt but to reframe it: the very act of questioning Moses's reliability implicitly acknowledges that historical testimony carries weight, and the question then becomes one of comparative evidence (009-023).

> [009-023] Which is the more credible of the two, Moses or China?

For Pascal, the key distinction lies in the character of the witnesses. Moses does not present himself as a flawless hero; rather, he records his own failings alongside the narrative of divine action. This honesty — "Moses does not hide his own shame" — contrasts sharply with other ancient historians who conceal the dishonorable episodes of their nations (009-545). The self-incriminating quality of the Mosaic record becomes, in Pascal's hands, an argument for its truthfulness.

## Moses and the Transmission of Tradition

Pascal traces the continuity of divine revelation through a chain of witnesses, with Moses occupying a pivotal position. The tradition that began with [[Adam]] remained fresh in Noah, and from Noah it passed to Moses, who preserved and codified it for the people of Israel (009-324).

> [009-324] The tradition from Adam was fresh in Noah and in Moses. Since then the prophets have foretold him.

This genealogy of testimony serves Pascal's broader apologetic purpose: if the chain of transmission can be historically established, then the [[Old Testament]] prophecies that Moses inaugurated carry the weight of a continuous tradition rather than appearing as isolated claims. The proximity of Moses to the events he records — "they saw those who saw Moses; therefore the deluge and the creation are true" (009-500) — provides the historical warrant that distinguishes biblical religion from the competing claims of other ancient peoples.

## Miracles and Prophecy

Pascal groups Moses with [[Jesus Christ]] and the [[apostles]] as the three pillars of miraculous testimony in Scripture (004-412). Yet his treatment of Mosaic miracles is notably restrained: he does not rest his case on the miraculous alone, acknowledging that reports of wonders "do not at first seem convincing" to the skeptical reader (009-370).

> [009-370] I do not here speak of the miracles of Moses, of Jesus Christ, and of the Apostles, because they do not at first seem convincing.

Instead, Pascal subordinates the miraculous to the broader framework of [[prophecy]] and historical continuity. The miracles confirm what the prophetic tradition independently establishes: that God has acted in history through chosen witnesses, of whom Moses is the first and foundational figure.

Moses also functions as a [[prophet]] whose own words anticipate a successor: "Moses himself told them that God would raise up a prophet" (009-187). This self-transcending quality of Mosaic prophecy — pointing beyond itself toward a fulfillment Moses would not see — is essential to Pascal's cumulative case for the truth of Christianity as the completion of [[Judaism]].

## The Law and the Hidden God

Pascal's treatment of Moses also engages with his theology of the hidden [[God]]. The law given through Moses is neither wholly transparent nor entirely opaque; it contains "something to blind, and something to enlighten" (009-025). This pattern — sufficient evidence for those who seek, sufficient obscurity for those who refuse — recurs throughout Pascal's treatment of divine revelation and reflects his broader epistemology of the *Deus absconditus*.

The Mosaic law thus functions typologically: it is both a genuine revelation in its own right and a prefiguration of the fuller disclosure that comes through Christ. Moses stands at the threshold, both receiving and pointing forward, his authority established by the very tradition he inaugurated and his limitations marking the necessity of a greater prophet to come.
