[[God]], in Pascal's [[theology|theological]] vision, is the Deus absconditus — the hidden God who can be found only by those who seek Him with the [[heart|heart]], not by those who demand rational demonstration. This is emphatically not the abstract deity of [[philosophy|philosophical]] theology.

> (008-077) But the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, the God of Jacob, the God of the Christians, is a God of love and consolation.

The famous Memorial — Pascal's record of his night of fire — draws the sharpest possible contrast between the living God of [[faith-and-reason|faith]] and the "God of the philosophers." The former is personal, relational, and encountered; the latter is an intellectual conclusion, a [[reason|rationalist]] construct.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Hiddenness of God

Pascal insists that God's hiddenness is not an accident or an oversight but a deliberate feature of His self-revelation:

> (004-027) On the contrary, that God is a hidden God, and that, since the corruption of nature, He has left men in a blindness from which they can only escape through Jesus Christ.

This hiddenness serves a theological purpose. God conceals Himself from those who do not seek Him sincerely, and reveals Himself to those who do. The pattern of concealment and revelation is not a flaw in the [[evidence|evidence]] but the very structure of divine [[providence|providence]].

> (003-051) He reveals Himself in the Scriptures, Deus absconditus.

The hiddenness of God is therefore tied directly to Pascal's [[hermeneutics|hermeneutics]] of Scripture. The Bible itself operates by this logic of simultaneous revelation and concealment — clear enough to enlighten the [[humble|humble]], obscure enough to blind the [[pride|proud]].

### The Wager's God

In the [[wager|Wager]] argument, Pascal does not purport to prove God's existence. Instead, he frames the question in terms of [[decision|decision]] under [[uncertainty|uncertainty]]:

> (008-227) That religion does, all this: Vere tu es Deus absconditus.

The hidden God is not an intellectual puzzle to be solved but a personal summons to be answered. The [[wager|Wager]] is addressed to the [[will|will]], not the [[intellect|intellect]] — it is an argument that aims at [[conversion|conversion]], not at [[certainty|certainty]].

## Key Passages

> (007-637) The uncertainty of the judgment. Deus absconditus.

Pascal links the hiddenness of God to the general condition of human [[judgment|judgment]] — uncertain, partial, and in need of a [[grace|grace]] that [[reason|reason]] cannot supply.

> (008-077) But the God of Abraham is a God of love and consolation, a God who fills the soul and heart of those whom He possesses.

The Memorial's language is experiential and intimate — "fire," "certainty," "joy," "peace." The hidden God, once encountered, becomes the God who transforms the entire [[self|self]].

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of God is inseparable from his [[apologetics|apologetic]] strategy. The argument from [[design|design]] or [[first-cause|first cause]] — the standard proofs of [[natural-theology|natural theology]] — can at best deliver the God of the philosophers, a God who is merely the conclusion of a [[syllogism|syllogism]]. Pascal rejects this approach not because it is false but because it is inadequate. A God who is merely probable is a God who makes no claim on the [[soul|soul]].

The hidden God, by contrast, is hidden precisely in order to engage the whole person. Those who seek only intellectual satisfaction will not find Him; those who seek with the [[heart|heart]] will. This is not [[fideism|fideism]] — Pascal does not reject [[reason|reason]] — but a recognition that the most important [[truth|truths]] are not accessible to demonstration alone.

The hidden God of Pascal thus stands at the intersection of his [[epistemology|epistemology]], his [[anthropology|anthropology]], and his [[soteriology|soteriology]]. God is known not by the [[mind|mind]] alone but by the whole person transformed by [[grace|grace]].
