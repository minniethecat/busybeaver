Esdras (Ezra) occupies a unique position in Pascal's *Pensées* as the figure at the center of the debate over the historical transmission of Scripture. Pascal devotes sustained attention to the question of whether Esdras restored the sacred books after the Babylonian exile, weighing competing historical testimonies to establish the credibility of the biblical text.

## Esdras in Pascal's Apologetic

### The Story of Restoration

Pascal presents the tradition about Esdras with characteristic even-handedness: "On Esdras.—The story that the books were burnt" during the Babylonian destruction of Jerusalem, "and Esdras point out that he read the books" (009-570, 009-574). This tradition holds that Esdras, inspired by divine revelation, recreated the Scriptures that had been lost — a claim with profound implications for the credibility of the received text.

### Weighing the Evidence

Pascal refuses to accept or reject the Esdras tradition without examination. He notes objections: "Against the story in Esdras, 2 Maccab. ii;—Josephus" (009-618), and observes that "Josephus, in the whole history of Esdras, does not say one" word about this miraculous restoration (009-623). The silence of Josephus — a key Jewish historian — counts as evidence against the tradition.

### Pascal's Verdict

After weighing the evidence, Pascal arrives at a carefully qualified conclusion: "If the story in Esdras is credible, then it must be believed" (009-626). This conditional formulation is characteristic of Pascal's method — he does not assert the story's truth dogmatically but argues that if the historical evidence supports it, the conclusion follows. The conditional structure places the burden on historical inquiry while preserving the logical force of the apologetic.

### Parallel with Noah

Pascal pairs Esdras with Noah in his discussion of textual transmission: books could have been "destroyed by the Deluge, as Esdras could" have restored them after the exile (009-594). Both figures represent moments of catastrophic loss followed by divine preservation — the Flood and the Exile as parallel threats to sacred tradition, Noah and Esdras as parallel agents of its survival.

### The Testimony of the Fourth Book

Pascal cites "the fourth book of Esdras. Deus glorificatus est, et Scripturae verae creditae sunt" — God was glorified, and the Scriptures were believed to be true (009-606). This apocryphal source provides Pascal with a theological principle: the authenticity of Scripture is established not merely by historical evidence but by its divine content. The truth of Scripture authenticates itself.

## Significance in the Pensées

Esdras serves Pascal as a test case for his historical apologetic method. Rather than simply asserting the trustworthiness of Scripture, Pascal engages with skeptical objections — lost books, competing traditions, the silence of Josephus — and demonstrates that Christian faith can withstand historical scrutiny. Esdras represents the point where Pascal's historical argument meets his theological conviction: the Scriptures survive not because of flawless human transmission but because of divine providence working through fallible human agents.
