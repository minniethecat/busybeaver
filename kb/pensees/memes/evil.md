## Definition

In Pascal's thought, **evil** (mal) is not an abstract metaphysical principle but the **concrete opposition to truth and love**. His definitive formulation appears in a fragment that functions as a moral axiom: "That which offends truth or love is evil. This is the true principle" (002-1075). Evil is thus defined relationally — not by its own essence but by what it negates. It offends truth (the order of the [[Mind]]) and love (the order of [[Charity]]). This double determination gives Pascal's ethics its rigor: an act is evil not because it violates a rule but because it damages the relationship between the human person and the two highest goods.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Evil Within

Pascal locates evil first in the interior of the human person, not in external circumstances:

> (002-050) The evil that is in him, I mean apart from original sin.

The qualification "apart from original sin" is telling. Pascal distinguishes between the inherited condition of [[Original Sin]] — the structural corruption of human nature — and the particular evils that individual persons commit. The former is the condition of possibility for the latter. Every actual evil flows from the primordial rupture between humanity and [[God]].

### The Evil of Self-Deception

Pascal identifies a hierarchy of evils, with self-ignorance at the apex:

> (002-688) Truly it is an evil to be full of faults; but it is a still greater evil to be full of them and unwilling to recognize them.

> (002-689) Evil to be full of them, and to be unwilling to recognize them.

This is characteristically Pascalian: the moral fault is bad, but the refusal to acknowledge the moral fault is worse. The second-order evil — self-deception about one's own evil — compounds the first-order evil. This insight grounds Pascal's critique of human [[Vanity]] and [[Self-Love]]: the *amour-propre* that makes us unwilling to see ourselves as we are. The person who knows his faults can at least seek remedy; the person who denies them is locked in a prison of self-flattery.

### The Ignorance of Evil

> (002-698) To free ourselves from an evil, namely, the ignorance of evil.

Paradoxically, ignorance of evil is itself an evil. Pascal argues that we must first understand the depth of our condition before we can seek liberation from it. This is the apologetic logic of the Pensées: Pascal must first convince the libertine of his [[Wretchedness]] before he can proclaim the remedy of [[Salvation]]. The ignorance of evil is not innocence. It is a deeper entanglement in the very condition from which one needs to be freed.

### The Magnitude of Evil

> (002-750) This evil is no doubt greater and more grievous than the deprivation of all the goods of the body.

Pascal ranks spiritual evil above physical suffering. The deprivation of "all the goods of the body" — health, comfort, freedom from pain — is a lesser evil than the spiritual condition of separation from God. This hierarchy of evils is essential to Pascal's apologetics: if the libertine treats physical death as the greatest evil, he has not yet understood what is truly at stake. The [[Soul]]'s condition matters infinitely more than the [[Body]]'s.

### Evil and the Human Condition

> (003-114) Our evils are infinite.

This stark declaration captures the scope of the human predicament. Evil is not a local problem affecting a few aspects of life. It is total, pervasive, infinite in extent. Pascal's pessimism here is theological rather than temperamental: if humanity is fallen, the fall affects everything. No dimension of human existence escapes the corruption of [[Original Sin]]. The corollary is that no merely human remedy can address the problem. Only divine intervention — the [[Grace]] and [[Mercy]] of God — can meet an infinite evil with an infinite remedy.

### The Seekers of Evil

> (002-1055) The evil is that they seek it as if they loved it.

Pascal observes a perverse human tendency: we not only suffer evil but actively pursue it. The phrase "as if they loved it" points to the irrationality of sin. People do not choose evil because they judge it to be good. They choose it despite knowing it to be evil — a phenomenon that Pascal's doctrine of [[Concupiscence]] explains as the corruption of the will. The will is not merely weakened; it is bent toward what it knows to be destructive.

### Evil Fear and Evil Desire

> (004-197) Superstition and lust. Scruples, evil desires. Evil fear; fear.

Pascal catalogues the psychological forms evil takes, pairing them in characteristic contrasts. Superstition and lust are both distortions: superstition distorts religion into magic, and lust distorts desire into idolatry. "Evil fear" is distinguished from the true [[Fear]] of God — it is the fear that paralyzes rather than the fear that awakens. These pairings show Pascal's method: evil is not a monolith but a spectrum of disordered responses to God, self, and world.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of evil is notable for what it refuses to do. It offers no theodicy — no systematic justification of God's permission of evil. It offers no metaphysical definition — evil is not located in a principle, a substance, or a cosmic force. It offers no program for the elimination of evil — the evils of the human condition are "infinite" and cannot be resolved by human effort. What Pascal provides instead is a moral phenomenology of evil: a description of how evil appears in human experience, how it compounds itself through self-deception, and how it can only be addressed by a remedy that comes from outside the human condition.

The hierarchy of evils is central to Pascal's method. By ranking spiritual evil above physical evil, and self-ignorance above simple fault, Pascal reshapes the moral landscape. The person who prides himself on avoiding gross sins while remaining blind to his own [[Pride]] is in a worse state than the person who sins openly but knows his condition. The [[Pharisee]] in Pascal's reading is a more troubling case than the publican. This inversion of moral common sense is characteristic of Pascal's Augustinian inheritance and his Jansenist formation.

The definition of evil as what "offends truth or love" deserves close attention. It makes evil relational rather than substantial. Evil is not a thing but a rupture — a violation of the right order between the human person and truth (the intellectual order) and between the human person and love (the order of [[Charity]]). This definition connects evil to Pascal's doctrine of the [[Three Orders]]: the order of body, the order of mind, and the order of charity. Evil can occur at any level, but the gravest evil is that which offends the highest order — charity.
