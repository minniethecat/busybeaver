Saint Peter the Apostle appears in Pascal's *Pensées* primarily in ecclesiological contexts, where his authority and actions illuminate Pascal's Jansenist understanding of Church governance. Peter is the rock of the Church, yet his authority must be understood in relation to the primitive Church's practice of conciliar deliberation.

## Peter at the Council of Jerusalem

"When Saint Peter and the Apostles deliberated about abolishing circumcision" (010-190) — Pascal invokes the Acts 15 council as a model for Church authority. Peter does not act unilaterally but deliberates with the Apostles. For Pascal, this conciliar model is normative: papal authority, properly understood, operates within and not above the collective discernment of the Church.

## Peter's Impetuous Defense

"Saint Peter asks permission to strike Malchus" (012-078) — at Christ's arrest, Peter draws his sword in defense of his master. Christ's rebuke teaches that the Kingdom is not advanced by violence. Peter's impetuousness is both his weakness and, paradoxically, a sign of his love — a love that must be purified through failure and restoration.

## Warning Against False Teachers

"Saint Peter, ii: false prophets in the past" (014-264) — Pascal cites Peter's warning in 2 Peter against false teachers. This apostolic concern with heresy resonates with Pascal's own context: the Jansenist controversy over grace and the accusation of heresy leveled against Port-Royal. Peter's authority is invoked not to create new doctrine but to guard the deposit of faith.

## The Primacy of Peter

Although Pascal's *Pensées* do not develop a systematic Petrine theology, Peter's role as the chief of the Apostles is implicit in these references. Pascal's Jansenist milieu was acutely aware of the tension between Petrine primacy and Gallican conciliarism — a tension Pascal navigates by emphasizing the primitive Church's practice over later developments of papal prerogative.
