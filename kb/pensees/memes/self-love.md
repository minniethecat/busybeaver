[[Self-Love|Self-love]] (*amour-propre*), in Pascal's [[augustinianism|Augustinian]] anthropology, is the fundamental disorder of the [[human-nature|human]] will — not a particular sin among others but the root from which all particular sins grow.

> [002-670] Self-love.—The nature of self-love and of this human Ego is to love only self and to consider only self.

This is the definitional core: self-love is not merely preferring oneself to others but making the self the exclusive object of love and consideration. The "human Ego" — the self as it actually exists in the [[original-sin|fallen]] condition — is structurally incapable of loving anything other than itself.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Structure of Self-Love

> [003-110] Of human interest and self-love; for this we need only see what it is that men love.

Pascal's method is diagnostic: to understand self-love, observe what human beings actually pursue. The universality of self-interested motivation is not a hypothesis to be tested but a datum to be explained. The question is not *whether* human beings are motivated by self-love but *why* — and what, if anything, can be done about it.

> [002-726] Inseparable from self-love. It is this false delicacy which makes those who are in error so difficult to cure.

Self-love protects error. When a belief is bound up with the [[ego|ego]] — when to admit error would be to wound self-love — the [[will|will]] resists correction regardless of the [[evidence|evidence]]. This explains why [[argument|argument]] alone cannot convert: the intellect is not free to follow truth when the will is enslaved to self.

> [002-731] The medicine does not cease to be bitter to self-love. It takes as an insult what should be received as a remedy.

The "medicine" — whether it be moral correction, spiritual counsel, or the claims of [[christianity|Christianity]] itself — is experienced as bitter by self-love. The [[ego|ego]] cannot distinguish between an attack on its false [[pride|pride]] and an attack on its existence, because its existence *is* its false pride.

### Self-Love and Social Life

> [001-155] Self-love, which immediately forms a desire to produce the opposite effect in the minds of those to whom we speak.

Self-love is inherently social: it seeks [[recognition|recognition]] from others. When we speak, we do not merely communicate information; we attempt to control others' perception of us. The conversation becomes a theatre of mutual self-display.

> [001-192] Self-love leads them more willingly to reflection upon it.

The reflexive structure of self-love — the self contemplating itself, the self loving itself — generates an infinite regress. Every act of self-examination can become another occasion for self-love: one takes [[pride|pride]] in one's [[humility|humility]], one loves oneself for despising self-love.

## Key Passages

> [002-537] Not to fall into this self-love, have been perfectly unjust out of a desire to avoid self-love.

This paradox is among Pascal's most penetrating: the attempt to avoid self-love can itself become a form of self-love — a self-congratulatory renunciation that is more subtle but no less corrupt than open [[selfishness|selfishness]]. The just person who prides himself on his [[justice|justice]] has merely exchanged one form of self-love for another.

> [003-715] But ten years of self-love (for ten years is the chance), to try hard to please without success.

Pascal's psychological realism is unsparing: even our attempts to please others are motivated by self-love — we seek their approval to confirm our own worth. The exhaustion of "ten years of self-love" is the exhaustion of a project that can never succeed, because the self can never be satisfied with the [[recognition|recognition]] it extracts from others.

## Analysis

Pascal's doctrine of self-love is the psychological counterpart to his theological doctrine of [[original-sin|original sin]]. Original sin names the condition; self-love names the mechanism by which that condition operates in actual human motivation.

Three features distinguish Pascal's account from more optimistic treatments of self-love (such as those that would later be developed by [[rousseau|Rousseau]] or the [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]]):

First, Pascal denies that self-love can be educated into [[benevolence|benevolence]]. Self-love is not a neutral energy that can be redirected toward good objects; it is inherently corrupt — "the self is hateful" (*le moi est haïssable*). The self that loves only itself cannot be trained to love others; it can only be replaced.

Second, self-love is invisible to itself. The mechanisms of [[self-deception|self-deception]] that self-love deploys are so effective that the subject genuinely believes in its own [[virtue|virtue]]. This is why Pascal's analysis of self-love is also an analysis of [[bad-faith|bad faith]].

Third, the only genuine remedy for self-love is [[charity|charity]] — supernatural love that comes from outside the self. No amount of [[reason|reasoning]], [[habit|habit-formation]], or [[moral|moral]] effort can transform self-love into charity, because self-love is the very principle from which those efforts proceed.
