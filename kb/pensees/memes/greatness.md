# Greatness

**Greatness** (*grandeur*) is one pole of [[Blaise Pascal]]'s dialectical account of the [[Human Condition|human condition]], paired with and inseparable from [[Wretchedness|wretchedness]] (*misère*). For Pascal, human greatness is not an achievement to be earned but a fact to be recognized — it consists above all in the capacity for thought and in the unique human ability to know oneself as miserable.

## Thought as Dignity

Pascal's most compressed statement on greatness is also his most famous:

> Thought constitutes the greatness of man. (006-032)

This aphorism opens the passage on the thinking reed (*roseau pensant*), one of the most celebrated fragments in the *Pensées*. Pascal writes:

> Man is but a reed, the most feeble thing in nature; but he is a thinking reed. The entire universe need not arm itself to crush him. A vapour, a drop of water suffices to kill him. But, if the universe were to crush him, man would still be more noble than that which killed him, because he knows that he dies and the advantage which the universe has over him; the universe knows nothing of this. (006-032)

Human greatness, then, is not a matter of power, size, or durability — in these respects we are negligible before the cosmos. It is a matter of consciousness. The universe can destroy us, but it cannot understand us or itself. Only the human being can reflect, and this capacity for reflection elevates us above the merely physical order.

> All our dignity consists, then, in thought. By it we must elevate ourselves, and not by space and time which we cannot fill. (006-032)

## Knowing Oneself as Miserable

Pascal deepens the analysis through paradox: greatness consists precisely in the awareness of not being great. "The greatness of man is great in that he knows himself to be miserable. A tree does not know itself to be miserable. It is then being miserable to know oneself to be miserable; but it is also being great to know that one is miserable" (007-397). The capacity for self-knowledge is itself a mark of nobility, even when what is known is one's own abjection.

This paradoxical logic leads to Pascal's metaphor of the fallen king: "All these same miseries prove man's greatness. They are the miseries of a great lord, of a deposed king" (007-398). Our wretchedness is evidence of a lost greatness — we would not feel miserable if we had never been something more. The very experience of dissatisfaction, restlessness, and aspiration points beyond itself to a nature made for something higher.

## The Dialectic of Greatness and Wretchedness

The greatness-wretchedness dialectic is the organizing principle of Pascal's anthropology. "In proportion as men possess light they discover both the greatness and the wretchedness of man. In a word, man knows that he is wretched. He is therefore wretched, because he is so; but he is really great because he knows it" (007-417). To deny either pole is to falsify the human condition: those who emphasize only greatness fall into [[Pride|pride]]; those who emphasize only [[Wretchedness|wretchedness]] fall into despair.

This duality is what makes Christianity uniquely adequate to human experience. "The true religion must teach greatness and misery" (007-637), Pascal writes, and only Christianity — with its doctrines of creation in the image of God and the [[Original Sin|Fall]] — accounts for both. The [[Incarnation]] is the ultimate expression of this truth: "The Incarnation shows man the greatness of his misery by the greatness of the remedy which he required" (007-637).
