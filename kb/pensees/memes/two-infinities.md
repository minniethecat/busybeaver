The [[two-infinities|Two Infinities]] is Pascal's most vivid image for the [[human-nature|human]] epistemological condition. [[human-nature|Man]] stands suspended between two abysses — the infinitely large and the infinitely small — and this position, which is neither at one extreme nor the other, defines both the possibility and the limit of [[knowledge|knowledge]].

> [002-070] The infinites, the mean. — When we read too fast or too slowly, we understand nothing.

The concept appears most famously in the [[disproportion|Disproportion]] fragment, where Pascal deploys it as a cosmological thought-experiment designed to induce a specific emotional-cognitive state: the vertigo of recognising one's own position between incomprehensible magnitudes.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Cosmic Thought-Experiment

Pascal asks the reader to contemplate the scale of the universe — to imagine the visible world as a mere point within the "infinite sphere" of nature:

> [002-104] It is an infinite sphere, the centre of which is everywhere, the circumference nowhere.

This is not a scientific claim about the structure of the universe. It is a rhetorical device, borrowed from medieval theology's description of [[God|God]], repurposed to describe the natural world within which [[human-nature|man]] must orient himself. The universe has no fixed centre and no boundary — and therefore no point from which a [[human-nature|human]] observer could achieve a stable, comprehensive view.

> [002-126] ...therein an infinity of universes, each of which has its firmament, its planets, its earth, in the same proportion as the visible world.

Within the infinitely large, there are infinitely many worlds, each structured like our own. And within the infinitely small — within the body of a mite, Pascal insists — there are further infinities of structure. The microscope and the telescope, both recent inventions in Pascal's day, had made both infinities newly concrete.

### The Epistemological Consequence

The cosmological image has an immediate epistemological consequence:

> [003-149] ...see nothing but infinites on all sides, which surround us, and we call them, in respect of ourselves, the infinite and nothingness.

We "see nothing but infinites on all sides." The human scale — the scale at which our senses and our [[reason|reason]] operate — is not the scale of reality. It is a narrow band between two incomprehensible magnitudes. What we call [[knowledge|knowledge]] is always knowledge-at-a-scale, and the truth of things at other scales is inaccessible to us.

> [003-362] ...engulfed in the infinite immensity of spaces of which I know nothing, and which know nothing of me.

The "infinite immensity of spaces" — Pascal's most quoted line on this theme — captures not only the intellectual recognition of the two infinities but the affective response: not wonder but terror. The spaces are not only spatially infinite but indifferent. They "know nothing of me."

### The Famous Silence

> [003-370] The eternal silence of these infinite spaces frightens me.

This single sentence — perhaps Pascal's most famous — condenses the existential import of the two-infinities concept. The universe is silent; it does not speak to [[human-nature|human]] concerns; it neither threatens nor consoles. And this silence, precisely because it is not hostile but indifferent, is frightening. It reveals [[human-nature|man's]] position as one of radical exposure — not threatened by a malevolent universe but abandoned in one that has no regard for him at all.

## Key Passages

> [002-246] ...little higher. Is he not always infinitely removed from the end, and is not the space we are capable of conceiving but an atom in comparison with the reality of things?

The asymptotic character of knowledge: however far we advance, we remain "infinitely removed from the end." Progress in [[knowledge|knowledge]] is real but it never closes the gap between the finite knower and the infinite knowable.

## Analysis

The Two Infinities is not a thesis to be demonstrated but an experience to be undergone. Pascal's rhetoric is designed to produce in the reader the vertigo that the concept describes — and then to show that this vertigo is itself evidence for the [[christianity|Christian]] account of the human condition. [[human-nature|Man]] cannot live as if the two infinities do not exist; but acknowledging them reveals his [[disproportion|disproportion]] — and this revelation is the first step toward recognising the need for a [[God|God]] who is not merely infinite but personal.

The concept also serves as the epistemological foundation for Pascal's critique of both [[dogmatism|dogmatism]] and [[skepticism|skepticism]]. The dogmatist claims to know more than the two infinities permit; the sceptic claims that because the two infinities prevent demonstrative [[certainty|certainty]], nothing can be known. Pascal's position — that genuine but limited [[knowledge|knowledge]] is possible between the two infinities — is both more modest and more defensible than either alternative.
