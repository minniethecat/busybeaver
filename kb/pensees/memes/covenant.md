[[Covenant|Covenant]], in Pascal's reading of [[Scripture|Scripture]], is the structural principle of [[salvation-history|salvation history]]: the [[old-covenant|old covenant]] with [[moses|Moses]] and the [[law|law]] prefigures the [[new-covenant|new covenant]] with the [[messiah|Messiah]], and the transition from one to the other is the great subject of both Testaments.

> (008-204) ...covenant. To give faith to the Messiah...

Pascal's treatment of covenant belongs to his broader [[typology|typology]]: the old covenant is the [[figure|figure]]; the new covenant is the fulfillment. The [[jews|Jewish people]], reading [[carnal|carnally]], see only the figure and miss what it figures. The [[christian|Christian]], reading [[spiritual|spiritually]], sees the figure and its fulfillment as a single divine [[plan|plan]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Two Covenants

Pascal inherits the [[augustinian|Augustinian]] distinction between the two covenants and gives it a characteristically dialectical form. The old covenant is not abolished but fulfilled; the new covenant is not a replacement but a [[transfiguration|transfiguration]].

> (009-167) ...finally, in the law, and in the covenant with Moses.

The covenant with Moses — the giving of the [[decalogue|Law]] — established a [[people|people]] and a [[cult|cult]]. But this covenant was, from its inception, destined to be superseded. The [[prophecy|prophecies]] that Pascal reads as proofs of [[christianity|Christianity]] are, in substance, promises of a new covenant that will fulfil what the old covenant could only promise.

### The New Covenant with the Messiah

> (009-232) That God will make a new covenant with the Messiah...

The new covenant is not an afterthought but the purpose toward which the old covenant was always directed. Pascal's reading of the [[old-testament|Old Testament]] is relentlessly Christocentric: every text, every ritual, every [[prophecy|prophecy]] points toward [[christ|Christ]]. The covenant framework is what gives this reading its coherence.

> (010-151) ...had been only one covenant...

The state of affairs before Christ — "only one covenant" — was a state of [[expectation|expectation]]. The [[patriarchs|patriarchs]] and [[prophets|prophets]] lived in the [[dawn|dawn]]; the [[apostles|apostles]] and the [[church|Church]] live in the [[day|day]]. The transition is the [[incarnation|Incarnation]], and the new covenant is its permanent form.

### Covenant and the Jewish People

Pascal's treatment of the [[jews|Jewish people]] is inseparable from his covenant theology. The Jews are the people of the old covenant — chosen, blessed, and blinded, all for the sake of the divine plan.

> (011-351) ...take hold of my covenant; even unto them...

> (011-545) ...shall confirm the covenant with many...

The confirmation of the covenant "with many" — rather than with all — is a theme Pascal draws from the [[prophetic-book-of-daniel|prophetic texts]]. The new covenant, like the old, is made with a particular people, not with humanity in general. This particularity is not a problem for Pascal but a proof: a universal covenant would be a philosophical abstraction, not a historical revelation.

> (011-637) ...he has made a covenant. For having renewed the league...

The language of "league" and "renewal" underscores that the covenant is not a mere idea but a concrete relationship, established and restored by specific divine acts in [[history|history]].

### Covenant and Apologetics

Pascal's apologetic use of the covenant framework is strategic. The existence of the [[jews|Jewish people]] as a living witness, the coherence of the two Testaments across centuries of composition, the convergence of [[prophecy|prophecy]] and fulfillment — all of these are "proofs" in Pascal's cumulative sense: not demonstrative but convergent, not compelling but inviting.

> (011-664) ...and I will preserve thee for a covenant of the people...

The [[prophet-isaiah|prophet]] is "preserved" for a covenant — a living link in the chain that connects the old covenant to the new. Pascal reads the prophetic vocation itself as a form of covenant witness.

## Analysis

Pascal's covenant theology serves three functions in his larger apologetic project.

First, it provides the **historical framework** for reading Scripture as a unified [[narrative|narrative]]. Without the covenant structure, the relationship between the two Testaments is merely juxtaposition. With it, the relationship is [[typology|typological]]: each person, event, and institution of the old covenant is a "figure" of its fulfillment in the new.

Second, it accounts for the **peculiar status of the Jewish people**. Why do the Jews exist? Why have they survived? Pascal's answer is covenantal: they are the bearers of the [[promise|promise]], and their survival is a standing miracle that testifies to the truth of what they bear.

Third, it structures the **existential situation of the reader**. Every human being is, for Pascal, under one of the two covenants: either under the law (which condemns) or under grace (which saves). The transition from one to the other is not automatic — it requires [[conversion|conversion]], the turn from [[self-love|self]] to [[god|God]] that is the goal of all Pascal's apologetic labour.
