[[Scripture|Scripture]], in Pascal's theology, is the divinely authored text whose very peculiarities — its apparent disorder, its [[contradiction|contradictions]], its use of [[figure|figures]] — constitute the proof of its origin. A merely human book would have been smoothed of these difficulties; only a divine author would risk being misunderstood.

> (004-025) ...it is not after this manner that Scripture speaks, which has...

Scripture speaks differently from human [[philosophy|philosophy]]. It does not argue; it narrates. It does not demonstrate; it shows. Its mode of address is suited to the human condition: a [[corruption|corrupted]] being who needs not abstract truth but a [[story|story]] in which to find himself.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Scripture's Disorder as Divine Signature

> (004-342) ...Against the objection that Scripture has no order.

The objection that Scripture "has no order" is, for Pascal, the strongest evidence that it is not a human composition. Human authors impose order; [[god|God]] speaks as He wills. The apparent disorder of Scripture — its digressions, repetitions, abrupt transitions — is not a flaw but a signature. A book that makes no concessions to human expectations of coherence is a book that comes from beyond those expectations.

> (003-460) ...to Scripture? Does it not say all this?

Pascal's rhetorical question presupposes that Scripture, rightly interpreted, addresses every question of [[salvation|salvation]]. But "rightly interpreted" is the crucial qualification: Scripture is not self-interpreting. It requires a [[hermeneutics|hermeneutics]] — and the key to that hermeneutics is [[charity|charity]].

### Figures and the Two Readings

> (004-025) ...Scripture speaks, which has a fuller knowledge of the things of God...

Scripture speaks of [[christ|Christ]] in [[figure|figures]]. This is not an embarrassment but a necessity: the truth about Christ is too large to be stated directly. Figures are the mode in which divine truth accommodates itself to human understanding without ceasing to be divine truth.

The two readings of Scripture — [[carnal|carnal]] and [[spiritual|spiritual]] — correspond to Pascal's [[three-orders|three orders]]. The carnal reader sees only the letter; the spiritual reader, illuminated by [[charity|charity]], sees the figure and its fulfillment. This distinction is central to Pascal's reading of the [[old-testament|Old Testament]]: the [[jews|Jewish people]] read carnally; the [[christian|Christian]] reads spiritually.

### Miracles and Scripture

> (004-411) ...the miracles of Holy Scripture...

The [[miracle|miracles]] recorded in Scripture serve a double function. They attest to the divine authority of the text, and they figure the greater miracles of [[grace|grace]]. Pascal's apologetic use of miracles is inseparable from his hermeneutics of figure: the physical healing recorded in Scripture figures the spiritual healing that is the true subject of the text.

### Scripture as Evidence and Obstacle

> (007-063) ...despise Scripture; if it is a sign of strength to esteem...

> (007-064) ...contradictions, esteem Scripture.

The same Scripture that is evidence for the believer is a stumbling-block for the unbeliever. Pascal's God has deliberately arranged matters so that Scripture "enlightens some and blinds others" — a hard saying that belongs to the deepest stratum of his [[predestination|predestinarian]] theology.

> (002-009) Scripture.

Pascal's cryptic fragment — a single word — testifies to the foundational place Scripture occupies in his thought. All of his [[apologetics|apologetics]] is, in the end, an argument for reading Scripture aright.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of Scripture is inseparable from his concept of the [[hiddenness-of-god|hidden God]]. A God who is evident would not need a Scripture; a God who is absent would leave no trace. The God who is hidden leaves a text — a text that is itself hidden in plain sight, luminous to those who read with [[faith-and-reason|faith]], opaque to those who read without it.

This account of Scripture resolves what would otherwise be a tension in Pascal's apologetics: the appeal to [[evidence|evidence]] (miracles, prophecies, the [[survival-of-the-jews|survival of the Jews]]) on the one hand, and the insistence on the [[hiddenness-of-god|hiddenness of God]] on the other. The evidence is real, but it is embedded in a text whose appearance of disorder screens out the uncommitted reader. Scripture is "clear enough to enlighten the elect and obscure enough to humble them."

The [[christianity|Christianity]] that Pascal defends is, at its foundation, a religion of the Book — but of a Book whose divine character is inseparable from its human strangeness. To read Scripture is to encounter a text that refuses to be mastered, that continually eludes the categories the reader brings to it, and that discloses its meaning only to those who approach it in the posture of [[humility|humility]].
