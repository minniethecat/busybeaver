[[Three Orders|The three orders]] are Pascal's most architectonic contribution to philosophical [[anthropology|anthropology]] — a tripartite framework that structures his accounts of [[knowledge|knowledge]], [[value|value]], and the [[human-condition|human condition]].

> [007-623] Three orders of things: the flesh, the spirit, and the will.

The three orders correspond to three domains of human existence: the carnal or bodily (the order of force, power, and physical reality), the intellectual (the order of [[reason|reason]], [[science|science]], and the mind), and the spiritual (the order of [[charity|charity]], [[wisdom|wisdom]], and holiness).

> [P01-477] The order of nature, the order of mind, and the order of charity.

What makes the three orders distinctive is their incommensurability. Each order operates according to its own criteria of greatness, and these criteria cannot be translated into the terms of another order.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Incommensurability of Orders

Pascal's central claim about the three orders is that they are not arranged on a single scale of value. This is not merely a descriptive claim about how humans happen to judge; it is a normative claim about the structure of reality.

> [012-468] Three orders differing in kind.

"Differing in kind" — not in degree. The greatness of a [[king|king]] belongs to the order of the flesh; the greatness of [[archimedes|Archimedes]] belongs to the order of the mind; the greatness of a [[saint|saint]] belongs to the order of charity. One cannot compare them as "more" or "less" great on a single metric, any more than one can compare the length of a line with the weight of a stone.

This has radical implications for how Pascal understands [[social|social]] hierarchy. Kings and generals possess the kind of greatness that is visible — it can be seen with the eyes — and therefore it commands the most immediate recognition. But it is, in Pascal's analysis, the lowest kind of greatness.

> [P01-476] Capital, for instance, is his analysis of the three orders: the order of nature, the order of mind, and the order of charity.

### The Visibility Problem

Each order is invisible from the perspective of lower orders:

> [012-462] All the glory of greatness has no lustre for people who are in pursuits of the mind.

The person absorbed in intellectual pursuits cannot perceive the glory of carnal greatness; the person absorbed in [[charity|charity]] cannot perceive the glory of intellectual greatness as anything other than vanity. But this is not a flaw — it is the natural consequence of the orders' incommensurability.

The person who operates in a lower order simply lacks the organ for perceiving the greatness of a higher order. This is why [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], who belongs to the order of charity, appeared unimpressive to those who judged by the standards of the flesh or the mind.

## Key Passages

> [012-469] Great geniuses have their power, their glory, their greatness, their victory, their lustre, and have no need of worldly greatness, with which they are not concerned.

Those who possess intellectual greatness do not need carnal greatness — but this is a claim not about their subjective preferences but about the objective structure of value. Intellectual greatness is real greatness; it simply belongs to a different order.

> [012-470] They are seen, not with the eyes, but with the mind; and this is sufficient.

The mode of perception appropriate to each order determines what can be seen: carnal greatness is seen with the eyes, intellectual greatness with the mind, spiritual greatness with the [[heart|heart]]. Each organ of perception is adequate to its own order and blind to the others.

> [012-474] They have no need of worldly or intellectual greatness, with which they have nothing to do. They are seen with God and the angels, and not with the eyes or with the curious mind. God is sufficient for them.

The saints — those who belong to the highest order — are invisible to the world and even to the intellect. Their greatness is perceived only by [[God|God]] and the angels. This is the theological culmination of the three orders doctrine: the order of charity is the order of divine perspective.

## Analysis

The three orders provide the structural armature for Pascal's entire thought. They unify his [[epistemology|epistemology]], his [[axiology|axiology]], and his [[apologetics|apologetics]].

Epistemologically, they explain why reason cannot grasp the truths of faith: faith belongs to the order of charity, reason to the order of the mind. This is not a failure of reason but a category distinction — just as the eye is not a failure because it cannot hear.

Axiologically, they provide a framework for understanding the diversity of human goods without reducing them to a single scale. Pascal is neither a nihilist about worldly goods (they are genuinely goods, within their order) nor a relativist about value (the orders are hierarchically ranked, even if incommensurable).

Apologetically, the three orders explain the central mystery of the Christian narrative: why the incarnation of God appeared as something so unimpressive. "[[Jesus Christ|Jesus Christ]], without wealth, and without any external show of [[knowledge|knowledge]], is in his own order of [[holiness|holiness]]."
