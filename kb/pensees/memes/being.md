[[Being|Being]], in Pascal's thought, is never treated as an abstract metaphysical category. His concern is existential — what it means for the human [[being|being]] to exist suspended between the [[infinite|infinite]] and [[nothingness|nothingness]].

## Between Being and Nothingness

Pascal's anthropology situates the human being in an ontological middle ground. Man is neither pure being ([[God|God]] alone possesses fullness of being) nor pure nothingness, but a strange composite:

> [002-136] ...nothingness which we cannot reach...

> [002-104] ...It is an infinite sphere, the centre of which is everywhere and the circumference nowhere.

This disproportion defines the [[human-condition|human condition]]. The human being cannot grasp its own origin or destiny — "the ultimate principles of being" (002-196) lie beyond its reach — yet cannot rest in ignorance, being compelled by its own nature to seek [[truth|truth]].

## The Nothingness of the Human Being

Without God, the human being confronts its own nothingness:

> [002-1075] ...A great proof of the nothingness of our being, not to be satisfied...

> [006-137] ...for I strive only to know my nothingness.

This is not metaphysical nihilism but a theological anthropology. The [[nothingness|nothingness]] of the human being is the negative corollary of God's positive [[being|being]]. Creation is a gratuitous gift — the creature has no claim on existence. The experience of nothingness (in [[boredom|boredom]], in [[diversion|diversion]], in the contemplation of [[death|death]]) discloses this ontological dependence.

## The Scepticism of Being

Pascal recognizes that the question of being leads to scepticism:

> [P01-324] ...the scepticism of every human being...

> [002-411] ...rule of falsehood. But being most generally false...

Yet this scepticism, properly understood, serves Pascal's apologetic purpose: the inability of [[reason|reason]] to ground its own [[being|being]] points beyond reason to [[faith|faith]].
