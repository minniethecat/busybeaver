[[Port-Royal]] was a Cistercian convent and the center of the [[Jansenism|Jansenist]] movement in seventeenth-century [[france|France]].

Located near Versailles, Port-Royal-des-Champs became the intellectual and spiritual home of [[Blaise Pascal]], the [[community|community]] in which his sister Jacqueline took vows, and the institutional context within which the *Pensées* was conceived and (posthumously) assembled.

Understanding Port-Royal is essential to understanding the [[theology|theological]] commitments that shape Pascal's work and the [[faith and reason|intellectual world]] he inhabited.

## Origins and History

Port-Royal-des-Champs was originally founded as a Cistercian convent in the thirteenth century, but its historical significance dates from its reform in the early seventeenth century under the leadership of Mère Angélique Arnauld.

The convent became associa[[theology|theological]]e theological movement known as [[Jansenism]] — named after Cornelius Jansen (Jansenius), Bishop of Ypres — which emphasized [[saint-augustine|Augustinian]] doctrines of [[grace|grace]], [[original-sin|original sin]], and predestination [P01-044].

> [P01-059] Pascal's sister Jacqueline took her vows as a sister of Port-Royal, and for some time her wish to enter the religious life shaped the family's trajectory.

The community attracted some of the finest minds of the period, including Antoine Arnauld (the "Great Arnauld"), Pierre Nicole, and Jean Racine. These *solitaires* (hermits) did not take formal vows but lived in community near the convent, devoting themselves to study, teaching, and theological controversy [P01-086].

## Pascal and Port-Royal

Pascal's connection to Port-Royal was established through his sister Jacqueline, who entered the convent as a nun [P01-138]. After his definitive conversion in 1654, Pascal became closely associated with the community, though he never formally joined the *solitaires*. He lived among them, participated in their intellectual work, and shared their theological commitments.

> [P01-139] From that time until his death, Pascal was closely associated with the society of Port-Royal which his sister Jacqueline had entered.

The relationship was mutually beneficial. Port-Royal provided Pascal with a community of like-minded scholars and a theological framework for his apologetic project. Pascal, in turn, brought to Port-Royal his extraordinary intellectual gifts and his ability to communicate complex theological ideas to a wider audience [P01-145].

## The Jansenist Controversy

Port-Royal was at the center of one of the most significant theological controversies of seventeen[[france|France]]tury France.

The Jansenist movement, with its emphasis on the radical dependence of [[human-nature|human]] [[salvation|salvation]] on divine [[grace]] and its critique of the more optimistic anthropology associated with [[society-of-jesus|Jesuit]] [[theology|theology]], attracted both passionate adherents and powerful enemies.

The controversy came to a head with the publication of Pascal's *[[provincial-letters|Provincial Letters]]* (1656–1657), a brilliant polemical defense of the Jansenist position that was directed against the [[society-of-jesus|Society of Jesus]] [P01-161]. The letters, written under a pseudonym, were an immediate sensation and established Pascal's reputation as a master of [[france|French]] prose.

> [P01-171] When necessary, the fathers of Port-Royal came to Pascal's aid with theological expertise, acknowledging that polemical writing was not a department in which Pascal excelled without assistance.

The community faced increasing pressure from both religious and civil authorities. Louis XIV, who saw Jansenism as a threat to the religious unity of France, eventually ordered the destruction of Port-Royal-des-Champs in 1710.

The buildings were razed and even the graves were exhumed. Yet the intellectual legacy of Port[[theology|theology]] its theology, its educational theories, its contributions to logic and grammar — survived [P01-377].

## Intellectual Legacy

Port-Royal's influence extended beyond theology. The community's "Little Schools" (*Petites Écoles*) developed innovative pedagogical methods, and the *Port-Royal Logic* (1662), co-authored by Arnauld and Nicole, was a landmark in the development of philosophical logic. The *Port-Royal Grammar* (1660) was one of the earliest systematic treatments of universal grammar.

The first edition of the *Pensées* (the "Port-Royal edition" of 1670) was prepared by Pascal's friends at Port-Royal, who organized his fragments and, in some cases, smoothed over passages they considered too bold or incomplete [P01-150].

Modern scholarship, beginning with Léon Brunschvicg in the nineteenth century, has worked to recover Pascal's original ordering and to restore passages omitted by the Port-Royal editors.

## The [[miracle|Miracle]] of the [[miracle-of-the-holy-thorn|Holy Thorn]]

A significant event in Port-Royal's history was t[[miracle|miracle]]rted [[miracle-of-the-holy-thorn|miracle of the Holy Thorn]] in 1656. Marguerite Périer, Pascal's niece and a pupil at the Port-Royal school, was said to have been cured of a painful lacrimal fistula after a relic — a thorn believed to be from Christ's crown of thorns — was applied to her eye [P01-272].

> [P01-272] The miracle happened at Port-Royal, and it arrived opportunely at a moment when the community faced increasing persecution.

For Pascal, this miracle was of immense significance. It occurred at a critical moment when Port-Royal was under attack, and Pascal saw it as a divine confirmation of the community's orthodoxy.

More broadly, the miracle reinforced Pascal's conviction that the continuing presence of the miraculous testified to the truth of [[christianity|Christianity]] and countered the rationalist dismissal of the supernatural [P01-277].
