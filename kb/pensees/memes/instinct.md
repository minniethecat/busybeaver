[[Instinct|Instinct]], in Pascal's usage, is not the antithesis of [[reason|reason]] but a distinct mode of orientation that operates beneath and alongside rational deliberation. Animals are governed entirely by instinct; [[human-nature|humans]] are governed by instinct, [[reason|reason]], and [[custom|custom]] — and the interplay among these three forces is one of Pascal's deepest themes.

> [002-648] man's instinct, in spite of all custom, good sense and nature still retain some traces.

Even after the overlay of [[custom|custom]] — which Pascal calls a "second nature" — something of "man's instinct" remains. The fact that instinct persists in spite of custom shows its primal character: it is the original nature that [[custom|custom]] covers but cannot fully extinguish.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Instinct, Heart, and First Principles

Pascal draws a crucial connection between instinct and the [[heart|heart's]] knowledge of first principles:

> [004-306] Heart, instinct, principles.

This dense triad — heart, instinct, principles — encapsulates Pascal's epistemology of the non-demonstrative. The [[heart|heart]] knows first principles not by [[reason|reasoning]] but by a kind of immediate grasp that Pascal associates with instinct. Just as the animal knows by instinct what is good for it without deliberation, the [[human-nature|human]] [[heart|heart]] knows the first principles of [[mathematics|mathematics]], [[physics|physics]], and [[morality|morality]] without being able to demonstrate them.

> [004-332] ...everything by instinct and intuition! But nature has refused us this good; on the contrary she gives us very little knowledge of this kind. All other knowledge can be acquired only by reasoning.

Nature has given us "very little" instinctive knowledge. The vast majority of what we know must be acquired through [[reason|reasoning]] — but the foundations on which reasoning rests cannot themselves be reasoned to. They are given by instinct, by the [[heart|heart]], or not at all. This is the root of Pascal's critique of [[dogmatism|dogmatism]]: the dogmatist forgets that reason's first principles are not themselves rationally grounded.

### Animal Instinct and Human Nature

Pascal makes strategic use of the comparison between animal and human instinct:

> [006-137] ...man about his whole nature; instinct and experience.

What animals do by instinct alone, humans do by instinct and experience combined. The presence of instinct in humans — our embodied, pre-rational orientation to the world — is not a remnant of animality to be overcome but a constitutive feature of human nature. The attempt to live by [[reason|reason]] alone, suppressing instinct and [[custom|custom]], is not only impossible but misguided.

> [007-123] ...them some feeble instinct of the happiness of their former nature...

Human beings retain a "feeble instinct" for the happiness that was their original condition — a trace memory of the state before the fall. This instinct is not [[knowledge|knowledge]]; it cannot articulate what it seeks. But it is what drives the restless searching that [[diversion|diversion]] attempts to suppress and that only [[God|God]] can satisfy.

### The Instinct for Happiness

> [007-637] ...take us out of ourselves. Our instinct makes us feel that we must seek our happiness outside ourselves.

The instinct for happiness is the deepest of all human instincts — and the most revealing. That we instinctively seek happiness *outside* ourselves indicates, for Pascal, that we were made for something beyond ourselves. The [[self-love|self]] cannot satisfy the self; the instinct for happiness points beyond its immediate object toward the [[infinite|infinite]].

## Key Passages

> [002-1051] ...turmoil. Not that they have an instinctive knowledge of happiness...

The "turmoil" of the human condition — the restlessness that [[diversion|diversion]] attempts to anaesthetise — arises precisely because we do NOT have instinctive knowledge of where happiness lies. Animals have instinct and follow it peacefully. Humans have instinct but also the burden of consciousness, which makes instinct uncertain. We know we want happiness; we do not instinctively know where to find it.

## Analysis

Instinct occupies an ambiguous position in Pascal's thought — and this ambiguity is deliberate. On the one hand, instinct is sub-rational: it belongs to the [[body|body]] and to the animal dimension of [[human-nature|human]] existence. On the other hand, instinct is the vehicle by which the [[heart|heart]] grasps first principles — the principles that [[reason|reason]] cannot demonstrate but on which all demonstration depends. To dismiss instinct as merely animal would be to lose access to the foundations of [[knowledge|knowledge]] itself.

This dual character of instinct — at once the lowest and highest of human faculties — mirrors the structure of Pascal's anthropology more broadly. [[human-nature|Man]] is neither angel nor beast; instinct, like [[custom|custom]] and [[passion|passion]], participates in both the misery and the [[greatness-and-misery|greatness]] of the human condition. It is part of what makes us capable of truth, and part of what makes that capacity fragile and uncertain.
