## Definition

In Pascal's theology, **superstition** (superstition) is the **false form of religion** — external observance and ritual adherence without the inward transformation of the [[Heart]]. It occupies a dangerous middle ground between two symmetrical errors: on one side, the pride that refuses all external forms; on the other, the superstitious reliance on formalities as though they were efficacious in themselves. Pascal's analysis of superstition is central to his broader apologetic strategy of distinguishing true [[Faith]] from its counterfeits.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Dialectic of Externals and Internals

Pascal develops his concept of superstition within a dialectical framework. The external forms of religion — kneeling, praying with the lips, participating in sacraments — are necessary because human beings are embodied creatures. To reject all externals is pride. But to place one's hope in externals alone is superstition:

> (004-080) It is superstition to put one's hope in formalities; but it is pride to be unwilling to submit to them.

The true Christian position navigates between these two errors. The external must be joined to the internal: "we must kneel, pray with the lips, etc., in order that proud man, who would not submit himself to God, may be now subject to the creature" (004-087). Pascal's [[Automaton]] doctrine underlies this logic — the body must be trained through habitual action, but the training is a means, not an end.

> (004-087) To expect help from these externals is superstition; to refuse to join them to the internal is pride.

### Superstition Distinguished from Piety

Pascal draws a sharp distinction between superstition and authentic piety. The two may resemble each other externally — both involve religious observance — but they differ fundamentally in their inward disposition:

> (004-138) Piety is different from superstition.

> (004-139) To carry piety as far as superstition is to destroy it.

This is a characteristically Pascalian move: two things that look alike can be opposites. The [[Heresy|heretics]] reproach Catholics for superstitious submission, but Pascal insists that the reproach misidentifies its target. True submission to the forms of religion, when animated by charity and directed toward God, is not superstition but [[Humility]].

### Superstition and Belief

Pascal observes that many who profess Christian belief do so from superstition rather than from genuine faith. This creates a diagnostic problem for the Church: how to distinguish true believers from those whose faith is merely superstitious:

> (004-146) There are many who believe but from superstition.

This connects to Pascal's broader concern with the authenticity of religious commitment. Just as [[Diversion]] masks the true human condition from consciousness, superstition masks the absence of genuine faith from both the believer and the community. The superstitious believer may perform all the right actions while lacking the inward transformation that alone constitutes [[Conversion]].

### Superstition in the History of Religion

Pascal extends his analysis to the historical role of superstition. In the Jewish dispensation, the ceremonial law served as a figure of the spiritual reality to come. But when the figure was mistaken for the reality — when the external sacrifices were treated as efficacious in themselves — the result was superstition. Christ comes to destroy the idols of the heathen and the superstition of the Jews:

> (012-387) He comes to destroy the idols of the former and the superstition of the latter.

This historical scheme places superstition within Pascal's theology of [[Covenant]]: the old covenant's externals were designed to prefigure Christ, not to replace Him. To cling to the external after the reality has arrived is the essence of superstition. The [[Cross]] is God's answer to both idolatry and superstition — it confronts human religion with the scandal of a God who dies as a criminal.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of superstition exemplifies his dialectical method. He does not simply condemn superstition as an error to be eliminated; he shows that it arises from a genuine human need — the need for embodied religious practice — that has been distorted. The solution is not to abandon externals (which would be pride) but to join them to the internal disposition of charity.

This analysis has important implications for Pascal's apologetics. The unbeliever who dismisses all religious observance as superstition makes the symmetrical error of the superstitious believer. Both fail to grasp the proper relationship between external form and inward reality. Pascal's apologist must therefore navigate between twin dangers: conceding too much to the skeptic (by abandoning the necessity of form) and conceding too much to the superstitious (by treating form as self-sufficient).

The concept connects to Pascal's three-order schema ([[Three Orders]]). Superstition belongs to the order of body — it treats spiritual realities as though they were material objects that can be manipulated through ritual. True piety belongs to the order of charity, where the external is entirely at the service of the internal. The [[Sacrifice]] of Christ is the ultimate bridge between orders: a physical death that accomplishes a spiritual redemption.
