Noah appears in Pascal's *Pensées* as the survivor of the Flood and a crucial link in the chain of primordial tradition. For Pascal, Noah embodies the continuity of divine revelation from Adam through the patriarchs to Moses, serving as evidence for the antiquity and authenticity of biblical history.

## Noah in Pascal's Argument

### The Flood as Divine Judgment and Mercy

Pascal presents Noah as the recipient of both divine judgment and mercy. The Flood demonstrates God's righteous response to human wickedness, while Noah's salvation illustrates the pattern of election and preservation that runs through sacred history (010-067). Noah "saw the wickedness of men before the Flood" (009-274), making him a witness to the corruption that necessitated divine intervention.

### Noah and the Transmission of Tradition

Pascal's most distinctive use of Noah concerns the question of historical transmission. He argues that "tradition from Adam was fresh in Noah and in Moses" (009-324), emphasizing the relative proximity of these figures in the biblical chronology. This compressed timeline — Adam to Noah to Moses — supports Pascal's apologetic argument that the primordial truths about God, creation, and the Fall were preserved without distortion through only a few generations.

### Noah as a Counter-Argument

In his discussion of the historical credibility of Scripture, Pascal notes a skeptical objection: some argued that "Noah could as easily have restored" the sacred books if they were lost (009-593). Pascal weighs this claim alongside the tradition about Esdras restoring the Scriptures after the Babylonian exile. The argument serves Pascal's broader case that the preservation of sacred texts through human intermediaries is historically plausible.

### The Abrahamic Continuity

Pascal notes the chronological overlap between Noah and Abraham: "Noah was still alive, God made promises to Abraham" (010-074). This detail reinforces the argument for unbroken tradition — the promises to Abraham were made within living memory of the Flood survivor, linking the covenant with Abraham directly to the antediluvian world.

## Significance in the Pensées

Noah functions in Pascal's apologetic as more than a biblical figure; he is a structural element in the argument for the reliability of revealed tradition. By compressing the timeline from Adam to Moses through Noah, Pascal answers the deist objection that biblical religion is a late human invention. Noah stands as the living bridge between the primordial revelation and the patriarchal covenant, making the chain of transmission both short and historically credible.
