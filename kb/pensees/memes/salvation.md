[[Salvation|Salvation]], in Pascal's theology, is what hangs in the balance of every human life. It is not a theme among others but the horizon within which all of Pascal's thought moves — from [[pascals-wager|the Wager]] to [[grace|grace]], from [[original-sin|original sin]] to [[jesus-christ|Christ]], from [[conversion|conversion]] to [[eternity|eternity]].

> (P01-391) required, in co-operation, for salvation.

The Pascalian axiom is that salvation is God's work in which human beings cooperate — a position that places Pascal within the [[augustinian|Augustinian]] tradition against both Pelagian self-sufficiency and Calvinist double predestination. [[Grace|Grace]] is necessary; response is real.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Indifference Problem

Pascal's starting point is anthropological rather than doctrinal: the most striking fact about [[salvation|salvation]] is that people do not care about it:

> (002-036) indifference about salvation, without fear and without rep

This indifference is, for Pascal, the decisive evidence of the [[wretchedness|wretchedness]] of the human condition. The [[diversion|divertissement]] that fills every waking hour is, at bottom, a flight from the question of salvation. [[Man|Man]] cannot bear to face his [[eternity|eternal]] destiny, so he fills his [[mind|mind]] with what will not last.

The [[wager|Wager]] argument is Pascal's attempt to break through this indifference — to make the question of salvation so urgent that the [[unbelief|unbeliever]] can no longer postpone it.

### Cooperation and Fear

Pascal's soteriology is marked by a distinctive combination of confidence and trembling:

> (002-1075) Work out your own salvation with fear.

The Pauline injunction becomes, in Pascal's hands, the charter for a [[christianity|Christianity]] that is neither presumptuous nor despairing. [[Fear|Fear]] is not opposed to [[faith|faith]] — it is faith's proper affect before a [[god|God]] whose judgments are inscrutable.

> (003-718) Those who hope for salvation are so far happy;

[[Hope|Hope]] for salvation already participates in salvation. Pascal follows [[augustine|Augustine]] in treating the [[desire|desire]] for salvation as itself a gift of [[grace|grace]] — the one who hopes is already being saved.

### Salvation and Human Means

Pascal draws a sharp line between what human means can accomplish and what only God can do:

> (004-340) only human, and useless for salvation.

Human [[philosophy|philosophy]], human [[morality|morality]], human [[reason|reason]] — all are "useless for salvation." This is not anti-intellectualism but theological [[realism|realism]]: the means are incommensurate with the end. [[Knowledge|Knowledge]] of [[god|God]] by reason is not the same as union with God by [[charity|charity]].

The [[three-orders|three orders]] provide the framework: [[philosophy|philosophy]] operates in the order of [[mind|mind]]; salvation operates in the order of [[charity|charity]], which is infinitely higher.

### Christ as the Mediator of Salvation

> (007-200) to redeem men, and to open salvation to

[[Jesus Christ|Christ]] is, for Pascal, the sole mediator of salvation. The [[incarnation|Incarnation]] is not one salvific path among others but the only one — a claim Pascal defends through the [[prophecy|prophecies]] and [[miracle|miracles]] he regards as Christianity's "external marks."

> (007-637) disciples slept, wrought their salvation. He has wrought that of each

This fragment captures Pascal's [[christology|Christology]] in miniature: the [[disciples|disciples]] slept — they contributed nothing — yet Christ wrought their salvation. The agency is entirely divine, yet the beneficiaries are those who, however feebly, followed.

### Salvation and the Individual

Pascal's soteriology is irreducibly individual without being individualistic:

> (008-071) far advanced towards his own salvation.

> (011-076) because he would have been their salvation, but not since.

The first fragment honours the particularity of each person's [[journey|journey]] toward salvation. The second, quoting [[isaiah|Isaiah]], contrasts what Christ would have been for [[israel|Israel]] with what Israel chose instead — salvation offered and refused.

## Analysis

Pascal's soteriology resists systematisation because it is built from the tensions intrinsic to [[christianity|Christianity]] itself. Salvation is entirely God's work ([[grace|grace]]) and yet requires human cooperation ([[faith|faith]]). The [[christian|Christian]] hopes for salvation with confidence (because God is faithful) and with [[fear|fear]] (because the self is not). The offer of salvation is universal ([[christ|Christ]] "would have been their salvation") and yet particular (only those who "work out" salvation receive it).

These tensions are not contradictions but the shape of Pascalian [[theology|theology]] — a theology that refuses to resolve mystery into system. The question of salvation, for Pascal, cannot be answered by [[reason|reason]] alone; it demands the [[heart|heart]]'s response to a [[god|God]] who both reveals and conceals.
