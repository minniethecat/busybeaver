[002-002] THE MISERY OF MAN WITHOUT GOD

[002-003] 60

[002-004] First part: Misery of man without [[god|God]].

[002-005] Second part: [[happiness|Happiness]] of man with God.

[002-006] Or, First part: That nature is corrupt. Proved by nature

[002-010] 61

[002-011] Order.—I [[force|might]] well have taken this discourse in an order

[002-012] like this: to show the vanity of all conditions of men, to show

[002-013] the vanity of ordinary lives, and then the vanity of philosophic

[002-014] lives, sceptics, stoics; but the order would not have been kept.

[002-015] I know a little what it is, and how few people understand it.

[002-016] No human science can keep it. Saint Thomas[20] did not keep

[002-017] it. Mathematics keep it, but they are useless on account of

[002-018] their depth.

[002-019] 62

[002-020] Preface to the first part.—To speak of those who have treated

[002-021] of the knowledge of self; of the divisions of Charron,[21] which

[002-022] sadden and weary us; of the confusion of [[montaigne|Montaigne]];[22] that he

[002-023] was quite aware of his want of method, and shunned it by

[002-024] jumping from subject to subject; that he sought to be fashionable.

[002-025] His foolish project of describing himself! And this not

[002-026] casually and against his maxims, since every one makes mistakes,

[002-027] but by his maxims themselves, and by first and chief design.

[002-028] For to say silly things by chance and weakness is a common

[002-029] misfortune; but to say them intentionally is intolerable, and

[002-030] to say such as that ...

[002-031] 63

[002-032] Montaigne.—Montaigne's faults are great. Lewd words; this

[002-033] is bad, notwithstanding Mademoiselle de Gournay.[23] Credulous;

[002-034] people without eyes.[24] Ignorant; squaring the circle,[25] a greater

[002-035] world.[26] His opinions on [[death|suicide]], on death.[27] He suggests an

[002-036] indifference about salvation, without fear and without repentance.[28]

[002-037] As his book was not written with a religious purpose, he was

[002-038] not bound to mention religion; but it is always our duty not to

[002-039] turn men from it. One can excuse his rather free and licentious

[002-040] opinions on some relations of life (730,231)[29]; but one cannot

[002-041] excuse his thoroughly pagan views on death, for a man must

[002-042] renounce piety altogether, if he does not at least wish to die

[002-043] like a Christian. Now, through the whole of his book his only

[002-044] conception of death is a cowardly and effeminate one.

[002-045] 64

[002-046] It is not in Montaigne, but in myself, that I find all that

[002-047] I see in him.

[002-048] 65

[002-049] What good there is in Montaigne can only have been acquired

[002-050] with difficulty. The evil that is in him, I mean apart from

[002-051] his morality, could have been corrected in a moment, if he had

[002-052] been informed that he made too much of trifles and spoke too

[002-053] much of himself.

[002-054] 66

[002-055] One must know oneself. If this does not serve to discover

[002-056] truth, it at least serves as a rule of life, and there is nothing

[002-057] better.

[002-058] 67

[002-059] The vanity of the sciences.—Physical science will not console

[002-060] me for the ignorance of morality in the time of affliction. But

[002-061] the science of [[morality|ethics]] will always console me for the ignorance

[002-062] of the physical sciences.

[002-065] else; and they never plume themselves so much on the

[002-066] rest of their knowledge as on [[knowledge|knowing]] how to be gentlemen.

[002-067] They only plume themselves on knowing the one thing they do

[002-068] not know.

[002-078] 71

[002-079] Too much and too little wine. Give him none, he cannot

[002-080] find truth; give him too much, the same.

[002-081] 72

[002-082] Man's disproportion.—[This is where our innate knowledge

[002-083] leads us. If it be not true, there is no truth in man; and if it be

[002-084] true, he finds therein great cause for humiliation, being compelled

[002-085] to abase himself in one way or another. And since he

[002-086] cannot exist without this knowledge, I wish that, before entering

[002-087] on deeper researches into nature, he would consider her both

[002-088] seriously and at leisure, that he would reflect upon himself also,

[002-089] and knowing what proportion there is ...] Let man then

[002-090] contemplate the whole of nature in her full and grand majesty,

[002-091] and turn his vision from the low objects which surround him.

[002-092] Let him gaze on that brilliant light, set like an [[eternity|eternal]] lamp to

[002-093] illumine the universe; let the earth appear to him a point in

[002-094] comparison with the vast circle described by the sun; and let

[002-095] him wonder at the fact that this vast circle is itself but a very

[002-096] fine point in comparison with that described by the stars in their

[002-097] revolution round the firmament. But if our view be arrested

[002-098] there, let our imagination pass beyond; it will sooner exhaust

[002-099] the power of conception than nature that of supplying material

[002-100] for conception. The whole visible world is only an imperceptible

[002-101] atom in the ample bosom of nature. No idea approaches

[002-102] it. We may enlarge our conceptions beyond all imaginable

[002-103] space; we only produce atoms in comparison with the reality of

[002-104] things. It is an [[disproportion|infinite sphere]], the centre of which is everywhere,

[002-105] the circumference nowhere.[30] In short it is the greatest

[002-106] sensible mark of the almighty power of God, that imagination

[002-107] loses itself in that thought.

[002-108] Returning to himself, let man consider what he is in comparison

[002-109] with all [[being|existence]]; let him regard himself as lost in this

[002-110] remote corner of nature; and from the little cell in which he

[002-111] finds himself lodged, I mean the universe, let him estimate at

[002-112] their true value the earth, kingdoms, cities, and himself. What

[002-113] is a man in the [[infinite|Infinite]]?

[002-114] But to show him another prodigy equally astonishing, let

[002-115] him examine the most delicate things he knows. Let a mite

[002-116] be given him, with its minute body and parts incomparably

[002-117] more minute, limbs with their joints, veins in the limbs, blood

[002-118] in the veins, humours in the blood, drops in the humours,

[002-119] vapours in the drops. Dividing these last things again, let

[002-120] him exhaust his powers of conception, and let the last object at

[002-121] which he can arrive be now that of our discourse. Perhaps he

[002-122] will think that here is the smallest point in nature. I will let

[002-123] him see therein a new abyss. I will paint for him not only the

[002-124] visible universe, but all that he can conceive of nature's immensity

[002-125] in the womb of this abridged atom. Let him see

[002-126] therein an infinity of universes, each of which has its firmament,

[002-127] its planets, its earth, in the same proportion as in the visible

[002-128] world; in each earth animals, and in the last mites, in which he

[002-129] will find again all that the first had, finding still in these others

[002-130] the same thing without end and without cessation. Let him

[002-131] lose himself in wonders as amazing in their littleness as the

[002-132] others in their vastness. For who will not be astounded at the

[002-133] fact that our body, which a little while ago was imperceptible in

[002-134] the universe, itself imperceptible in the bosom of the whole, is

[002-135] now a colossus, a world, or rather a whole, in respect of the

[002-136] nothingness which we cannot reach? He who regards himself

[002-137] in this light will be afraid of himself, and observing himself

[002-138] sustained in the body given him by nature between those

[002-139] two abysses of the Infinite and Nothing, will tremble at

[002-140] the sight of these marvels; and I think that, as his

[002-141] curiosity changes into admiration, he will be more disposed

[002-142] to contemplate them in silence than to examine them with

[002-143] presumption.

[002-144] For in fact what is man in nature? A Nothing in comparison

[002-145] with the Infinite, an All in comparison with the Nothing, a

[002-146] mean between nothing and everything. Since he is infinitely

[002-147] removed from comprehending the extremes, the end of things

[002-148] and their beginning are hopelessly hidden from him in an

[002-149] impenetrable secret, he is equally incapable of seeing the

[002-150] Nothing from which he was made, and the Infinite in which he

[002-151] is swallowed up.

[002-152] What will he do then, but perceive the appearance of the

[002-153] middle of things, in an eternal despair of knowing either their

[002-154] beginning or their end. All things proceed from the Nothing,

[002-155] and are borne towards the Infinite. Who will follow these

[002-156] marvellous processes? The Author of these wonders understands

[002-157] them. None other can do so.

[002-158] Through failure to contemplate these Infinites, men have

[002-159] rashly rushed into the examination of nature, as though they

[002-160] bore some proportion to her. It is strange that they have

[002-161] wished to understand the beginnings of things, and thence to

[002-162] arrive at the knowledge of the whole, with a presumption as

[002-163] infinite as their object. For surely this design cannot be

[002-164] formed without presumption or without a capacity infinite

[002-165] like nature.

[002-166] If we are well informed, we understand that, as nature has

[002-167] graven her image and that of her Author on all things, they

[002-168] almost all partake of her [[two-infinities|double infinity]]. Thus we see that all

[002-169] the sciences are infinite in the extent of their researches. For

[002-170] who doubts that geometry, for instance, has an infinite infinity

[002-171] of problems to solve? They are also infinite in the multitude

[002-172] and fineness of their premises; for it is clear that those which are

[002-173] put forward as ultimate are not self-supporting, but are based

[002-174] on others which, again having others for their support, do

[002-175] not permit of finality. But we represent some as ultimate for

[002-176] reason, in the same way as in regard to material objects we

[002-177] call that an indivisible point beyond which our senses can no

[002-178] longer perceive anything, although by its nature it is infinitely

[002-179] divisible.

[002-180] Of these two Infinites of science, that of greatness is the most

[002-181] palpable, and hence a few persons have pretended to know all

[002-182] things. "I will speak of the whole,"[31] said Democritus.

[002-183] But the infinitely little is the least obvious. Philosophers

[002-184] have much oftener claimed to have reached it, and it is here

[002-185] they have all stumbled. This has given rise to such common

[002-186] titles as First Principles, Principles of Philosophy,[32] and the like,

[002-187] as ostentatious in fact, though not in appearance, as that one

[002-188] which blinds us, De omni scibili.[33]

[002-189] We naturally believe ourselves far more capable of reaching

[002-190] the centre of things than of embracing their circumference.

[002-191] The visible extent of the world visibly exceeds us; but as we

[002-192] exceed little things, we think ourselves more capable of knowing

[002-193] them. And yet we need no less capacity for attaining the

[002-194] Nothing than the All. Infinite capacity is required for both,

[002-195] and it seems to me that whoever shall have understood the

[002-196] ultimate principles of being might also attain to the knowledge

[002-197] of the Infinite. The one depends on the other, and one leads

[002-198] to the other. These extremes meet and reunite by force of

[002-199] distance, and find each other in God, and in God alone.

[002-200] Let us then take our compass; we are something, and we are

[002-201] not everything. The nature of our existence hides from us

[002-202] the knowledge of first beginnings which are born of the Nothing;

[002-203] and the littleness of our being conceals from us the sight of

[002-204] the Infinite.

[002-205] Our intellect holds the same position in the world of thought

[002-206] as our body occupies in the expanse of nature.

[002-207] Limited as we are in every way, this state which holds the

[002-208] mean between two extremes is present in all our impotence.

[002-209] Our senses perceive no extreme. Too much sound deafens us;

[002-210] too much light dazzles us; too great distance or proximity

[002-211] hinders our view. Too great length and too great brevity of

[002-212] discourse tend to [[evidence|obscurity]]; too much truth is paralysing (I

[002-213] know some who cannot understand that to take four from

[002-214] nothing leaves nothing). First principles are too self-evident

[002-215] for us; too much pleasure disagrees with us. Too many concords

[002-216] are annoying in music; too many benefits irritate us; we wish

[002-217] to have the wherewithal to over-pay our debts. Beneficia eo

[002-218] usque læta sunt dum videntur exsolvi posse; ubi multum antevenere,

[002-219] pro gratia odium redditur.[34] We feel neither extreme heat nor

[002-220] extreme cold. Excessive qualities are prejudicial to us and not

[002-221] perceptible by the senses; we do not feel but suffer them.

[002-222] Extreme youth and extreme age hinder the [[spirit|mind]], as also too

[002-223] much and too little education. In short, extremes are for us

[002-224] as though they were not, and we are not within their notice.

[002-225] They escape us, or we them.

[002-226] This is our true state; this is what makes us incapable of

[002-227] certain knowledge and of absolute ignorance. We sail within

[002-228] a vast sphere, ever drifting in uncertainty, driven from end to

[002-229] end. When we think to attach ourselves to any point and to

[002-230] fasten to it, it wavers and leaves us; and if we follow it, it

[002-231] eludes our grasp, slips past us, and vanishes for ever. Nothing

[002-232] stays for us. This is our natural condition, and yet most

[002-233] contrary to our inclination; we burn with desire to find solid

[002-234] ground and an ultimate sure foundation whereon to build a

[002-235] tower reaching to the Infinite. But our whole groundwork

[002-236] cracks, and the earth opens to abysses.

[002-237] Let us therefore not look for certainty and stability. Our

[002-238] reason is always deceived by fickle shadows; nothing can fix

[002-239] the finite between the two Infinites, which both enclose and

[002-240] fly from it.

[002-241] If this be well understood, I think that we shall remain at

[002-242] rest, each in the state wherein nature has placed him. As this

[002-243] sphere which has fallen to us as our lot is always distant from

[002-244] either extreme, what matters it that man should have a little

[002-245] more knowledge of the universe? If he has it, he but gets a

[002-246] little higher. Is he not always infinitely removed from the

[002-247] end, and is not the duration of our life equally removed from

[002-248] eternity, even if it lasts ten years longer?

[002-249] In comparison with these Infinites all finites are equal, and

[002-250] I see no reason for fixing our imagination on one more than

[002-251] on another. The only comparison which we make of ourselves

[002-252] to the finite is painful to us.

[002-253] If man made himself the first object of study, he would see

[002-254] how incapable he is of going further. How can a part know

[002-255] the whole? But he may perhaps aspire to know at least the

[002-256] parts to which he bears some proportion. But the parts of the

[002-257] world are all so related and linked to one another, that I believe it

[002-258] impossible to know one without the other and without the whole.

[002-259] Man, for instance, is related to all he knows. He needs a

[002-260] place wherein to abide, time through which to live, motion in

[002-261] order to live, elements to compose him, warmth and food to

[002-262] nourish him, air to breathe. He sees light; he feels bodies;

[002-263] in short, he is in a dependent alliance with everything. To

[002-264] know man, then, it is necessary to know how it happens that

[002-265] he needs air to live, and, to know the air, we must know how

[002-266] it is thus related to the life of man, etc. Flame cannot exist

[002-267] without air; therefore to understand the one, we must understand

[002-268] the other.

[002-269] Since everything then is cause and effect, dependent and

[002-270] supporting, mediate and immediate, and all is held together by

[002-271] a natural though imperceptible chain, which binds together

[002-272] things most distant and most different, I hold it equally impossible

[002-273] to know the parts without knowing the whole, and to

[002-274] know the whole without knowing the parts in detail.

[002-275] [The eternity of things in itself or in God must also astonish

[002-276] our brief duration. The fixed and constant immobility of

[002-277] nature, in comparison with the continual change which goes on

[002-278] within us, must have the same effect.]

[002-279] And what completes our incapability of knowing things, is

[002-280] the fact that they are simple, and that we are composed of two

[002-281] opposite natures, different in kind, soul and body. For it is

[002-282] impossible that our rational part should be other than spiritual;

[002-283] and if any one maintain that we are simply corporeal, this

[002-284] would far more exclude us from the knowledge of things, there

[002-285] being nothing so inconceivable as to say that matter knows

[002-286] itself. It is impossible to imagine how it should know itself.

[002-287] So if we are simply material, we can know nothing at all;

[002-288] and if we are composed of mind and matter, we cannot know

[002-289] perfectly things which are simple, whether spiritual or corporeal.

[002-290] Hence it comes that almost all [[philosophy|philosophers]] have confused ideas

[002-291] of things, and speak of material things in spiritual terms, and

[002-292] of spiritual things in material terms. For they say boldly that

[002-293] bodies have a tendency to fall, that they seek after their centre,

[002-294] that they fly from destruction, that they fear the [[nothingness|void]], that they

[002-295] have inclinations, sympathies, antipathies, all of which attributes

[002-296] pertain only to mind. And in speaking of minds, they

[002-297] consider them as in a place, and attribute to them movement

[002-298] from one place to another; and these are qualities which belong

[002-299] only to bodies.

[002-300] Instead of receiving the ideas of these things in their purity,

[002-301] we colour them with our own qualities, and stamp with our composite

[002-302] being all the simple things which we contemplate.

[002-303] Who would not think, seeing us compose all things of mind

[002-304] and body, but that this mixture would be quite intelligible to

[002-305] us? Yet it is the very thing we least understand. Man is

[002-306] to himself the most wonderful object in nature; for he cannot

[002-307] conceive what the body is, still less what the mind is, and

[002-308] least of all how a body should be united to a mind. This is

[002-309] the consummation of his difficulties, and yet it is his very

[002-310] being. Modus quo corporibus adhærent spiritus comprehendi ab

[002-311] hominibus non potest, et hoc tamen homo est.[35] Finally, to complete

[002-312] the proof of our weakness, I shall conclude with these

[002-313] two considerations....

[002-387] 80

[002-388] How comes it that a cripple does not offend us, but that a

[002-389] fool does?[45] Because a cripple recognises that we walk straight,

[002-390] whereas a fool declares that it is we who are silly; if it were not

[002-391] so, we should feel pity and not anger.

[002-392] [[epictetus|Epictetus]][46] asks still more strongly: "Why are we not angry

[002-393] if we are told that we have a headache, and why are we angry

[002-394] if we are told that we reason badly, or choose wrongly?" The

[002-395] reason is that we are quite certain that we have not a headache,

[002-396] or are not lame, but we are not so sure that we make a true choice.

[002-397] So having assurance only because we see with our whole sight,

[002-398] it puts us into suspense and surprise when another with his

[002-399] whole sight sees the opposite, and still more so when a thousand

[002-400] others deride our choice. For we must prefer our own lights

[002-401] to those of so many others, and that is bold and difficult. There

[002-402] is never this contradiction in the feelings towards a cripple.

[002-407] 82

[002-408] [[imagination|Imagination]].[48]—It is that deceitful part in man, that mistress

[002-409] of error and falsity, the more deceptive that she is not always so;

[002-410] for she would be an infallible rule of truth, if she were an infallible

[002-411] rule of falsehood. But being most generally false, she

[002-412] gives no sign of her nature, impressing the same character on

[002-413] the true and the false.

[002-414] I do not speak of fools, I speak of the wisest men; and it is

[002-415] among them that the imagination has the great gift of persuasion.

[002-416] [[reason|Reason]] protests in vain; it cannot set a true value on things.

[002-417] This arrogant power, the enemy of reason, who likes to rule

[002-418] and dominate it, has established in man a [[custom|second nature]] to show

[002-419] how all-powerful she is. She makes men happy and sad, healthy

[002-420] and sick, rich and poor; she compels reason to believe, doubt,

[002-989] 139

[002-990] Diversion.—When I have occasionally set myself to consider

[002-991] the different distractions of men, the pains and perils to which

[002-992] they expose themselves at court or in war, whence arise so many

[002-993] quarrels, passions, bold and often bad ventures, etc., I have

[002-994] discovered that all the unhappiness of men arises from one

[002-995] single fact, that they cannot stay quietly in their own chamber.

[002-996] A man who has enough to live on, if he knew how to stay with

[002-997] pleasure at home, would not leave it to go to sea or to besiege

[002-998] a town. A commission in the army would not be bought so

[002-999] dearly, but that it is found insufferable not to budge from the

[002-1000] town; and men only seek conversation and entering games,

[002-1001] because they cannot remain with pleasure at home.

[002-1002] But on further consideration, when, after finding the cause

[002-1003] of all our ills, I have sought to discover the reason of it, I have

[002-1004] found that there is one very real reason, namely, the natural

[002-1005] poverty of our feeble and mortal condition, so miserable that

[002-1006] nothing can comfort us when we think of it closely.

[002-1007] Whatever condition we picture to ourselves, if we muster all

[002-1008] the good things which it is possible to possess, royalty is the

[002-1009] finest position in the world. Yet, when we imagine a king

[002-1010] attended with every pleasure he can feel, if he be without

[002-1011] diversion, and be left to consider and reflect on what he is, this

[002-1012] feeble happiness will not sustain him; he will necessarily fall

[002-1013] into forebodings of dangers, of revolutions which may happen,

[002-1014] and, finally, of death and inevitable disease; so that if he be

[002-1015] without what is called diversion, he is unhappy, and more

[002-1016] unhappy than the least of his subjects who plays and diverts

[002-1017] himself.

[002-1018] Hence it comes that play and the society of women, war, and

[002-1019] high posts, are so sought after. Not that there is in fact any

[002-1020] happiness in them, or that men imagine true bliss to consist in

[002-1021] money won at play, or in the hare which they hunt; we would

[002-1022] not take these as a gift. We do not seek that easy and peaceful

[002-1023] lot which permits us to think of our unhappy condition, nor

[002-1024] the dangers of war, nor the labour of office, but the bustle which

[002-1025] averts these thoughts of ours, and amuses us.

[002-1026] Reasons why we like the chase better than the quarry.

[002-1027] Hence it comes that men so much love noise and stir; hence

[002-1028] it comes that the prison is so horrible a punishment; hence it

[002-1029] comes that the pleasure of solitude is a thing incomprehensible.

[002-1030] And it is in fact the greatest source of happiness in the condition

[002-1031] of kings, that men try incessantly to divert them, and to procure

[002-1032] for them all kinds of pleasures.

[002-1033] The king is surrounded by persons whose only thought is to

[002-1034] divert the king, and to prevent his thinking of self. For he is

[002-1035] unhappy, king though he be, if he think of himself.

[002-1036] This is all that men have been able to discover to make

[002-1037] themselves happy. And those who philosophise on the matter,

[002-1038] and who think men unreasonable for spending a whole day in

[002-1039] chasing a hare which they would not have bought, scarce know

[002-1040] our nature. The hare in itself would not screen us from the sight

[002-1041] of death and calamities; but the chase which turns away our

[002-1042] attention from these, does screen us.

[002-1043] The advice given to Pyrrhus to take the rest which he was

[002-1044] about to seek with so much labour, was full of difficulties.[69]

[002-1045] [To bid a man live quietly is to bid him live happily. It is

[002-1046] to advise him to be in a state perfectly happy, in which he can

[002-1047] think at leisure without finding therein a cause of distress. This

[002-1048] is to misunderstand nature.

[002-1049] As men who naturally understand their own condition avoid

[002-1050] nothing so much as rest, so there is nothing they leave undone

[002-1051] in seeking turmoil. Not that they have an instinctive knowledge

[002-1052] of true happiness ...

[002-1053] So we are wrong in blaming them. Their error does not lie

[002-1054] in seeking excitement, if they seek it only as a diversion; the

[002-1055] evil is that they seek it as if the possession of the objects of their

[002-1056] quest would make them really happy. In this respect it is right

[002-1057] to call their quest a vain one. Hence in all this both the

[002-1058] censurers and the censured do not understand man's true nature.]

[002-1059] And thus, when we take the exception against them, that

[002-1060] what they seek with such fervour cannot satisfy them, if they

[002-1061] replied—as they should do if they considered the matter

[002-1062] thoroughly—that they sought in it only a violent and impetuous

[002-1063] occupation which turned their thoughts from self, and that they

[002-1064] therefore chose an attractive object to charm and ardently

[002-1065] attract them, they would leave their opponents without a reply.

[002-1066] But they do not make this reply, because they do not know

[002-1067] themselves.[70] They do not know that it is the chase, and not the

[002-1068] quarry, which they seek.

[002-1069] Dancing: we must consider rightly where to place our feet.—A

[002-1070] gentleman sincerely believes that hunting is great and royal

[002-1071] sport; but a beater is not of this opinion.

[002-1072] They imagine that if they obtained such a post, they would

[002-1074] nature of their desire. They think they are truly seeking quiet,

[002-1075] and they are only seeking excitement.

All religions and sects in the world have had natural reason

for a guide. Christians alone have been constrained to take

their rules from without themselves, and to acquaint themselves

with those which Jesus Christ bequeathed to men of old

to be handed down to true believers. This constraint wearies

these good Fathers. They desire, like other people, to have

[[freedom|liberty]] to follow their own imaginations. It is in vain that we

cry to them, as the prophets said to the Jews of old: "Enter into

the Church; acquaint yourselves with the precepts which the

men of old left to her, and follow those paths." They have

answered like the Jews: "We will not walk in them; but we will

follow the thoughts of our hearts"; and they have said, "We

will be as the other nations."[373]

903

They make a rule of exception.

Have the men of old given absolution before penance? Do

this as exceptional. But of the exception you make a rule

without exception, so that you do not even want the rule to

be exceptional.

904

On confessions and absolutions without signs of regret.

God regards only the inward; the Church judges only by the

outward. God absolves as soon as He sees penitence in the

heart; the Church when she sees it in works. God will make a

Church pure within, which confounds, by its inward and

entirely spiritual holiness, the inward impiety of proud sages

and Pharisees; and the Church will make an assembly of men

whose external manners are so pure as to confound the manners

of the heathen. If there are hypocrites among them, but so

well disguised that she does not discover their venom, she

tolerates them; for, though they are not accepted of God, whom

they cannot deceive, they are of men, whom they do deceive.

And thus she is not dishonoured by their conduct, which appears

holy. But you want the Church to judge neither of the inward,

because that belongs to God alone, nor of the outward, because

God dwells only upon the inward; and thus, taking away from

her all choice of men, you retain in the Church the most dissolute,

and those who dishonour her so greatly, that the synagogues of

the Jews and sects of philosophers would have banished them

as unworthy, and have abhorred them as impious.

905

The easiest conditions to live in according to the world are

the most difficult to live in according to God, and vice versa.

Nothing is so difficult according to the world as the religious

life; nothing is easier than to live it according to God. Nothing

is easier, according to the world, than to live in high office and

great wealth; nothing is more difficult than to live in them

according to God, and without acquiring an interest in them

and a liking for them.

everything at a glance. A practical man of sound judgment

exemplifies the latter; for he is in fact guided by impressions of

past experience, and does not consciously reason from general

principles.

[4] P. 4, l. 3. I judge by my watch.—Pascal is said to have always

[6] P. 5, l. 22. The doctor.—Also a traditional character in Italian

[195] P. 141. In the French edition on which this translation is based

there was inserted the following fragment after No. 513:

"Work out your own salvation with fear."

Proofs of prayer. Petenti dabitur.

Therefore it is in our power to ask. On the other hand, there is

God. So it is not in our power, since the obtaining of (the grace) to

pray to Him is not in our power. For since salvation is not in us,

and the obtaining of such grace is from Him, prayer is not in our

power.

The righteous man should then hope no more in God, for he

ought not to hope, but to strive to obtain what he wants.

Let us conclude then that, since man is now unrighteous since

the first sin, and God is unwilling that he should thereby not be

estranged from Him, it is only by a first effect that he is not estranged.

Therefore, those who depart from God have not this first effect

without which they are not estranged from God, and those who do

not depart from God have this first effect. Therefore, those whom

we have seen possessed for some time of grace by this first effect,

cease to pray, for want of this first effect.

Then God abandons the first in this sense.

It is doubtful, however that this fragment should be included

in the Pensées, and it has seemed best to separate it from the text.

It has only once before appeared—in the edition of Michaut (1896).

The first half of it has been freely translated in order to give an

interpretation in accordance with a suggestion from M. Emile

Boutroux, the eminent authority on Pascal. The meaning seems

to be this. In one sense it is in our power to ask from God, who

promises to give us what we ask. But, in another sense, it is not

in our power to ask; for it is not in our power to obtain the grace

which is necessary in asking. We know that salvation is not in our

power. Therefore some condition of salvation is not in our power.

Now the conditions of salvation are two: (1) The asking for it, and

(2) the obtaining it. But God promises to give us what we ask.

Hence the obtaining is in our power. Therefore the condition

which is not in our power must be the first, namely, the asking.

[[prayer|Prayer]] presupposes a grace which it is not within our power to obtain.

After giving the utmost consideration to the second half of this

obscure fragment, and seeking assistance from some eminent

scholars, the translator has been compelled to give a strictly literal

translation of it, without attempting to make sense.

They have a secret instinct which impels them to seek amusement

and occupation abroad, and which arises from the sense of

their constant unhappiness. They have another secret instinct,

a remnant of the greatness of our original nature, which teaches

them that happiness in reality consists only in rest, and not in

stir. And of these two contrary instincts they form within

themselves a confused idea, which hides itself from their view

in the depths of their soul, inciting them to aim at rest through

excitement, and always to fancy that the satisfaction which they

have not will come to them, if, by surmounting whatever

difficulties confront them, they can thereby open the door to rest.

Thus passes away all man's life. Men seek rest in a struggle

against difficulties; and when they have conquered these, rest

becomes insufferable. For we think either of the misfortunes

we have or of those which threaten us. And even if we should

see ourselves sufficiently sheltered on all sides, weariness of its

own accord would not fail to arise from the depths of the heart

wherein it has its natural roots, and to fill the mind with its

poison.

Thus so wretched is man that he would weary even without

any cause for weariness from the peculiar state of his disposition;

and so frivolous is he, that, though full of a thousand reasons for

weariness, the least thing, such as playing billiards or hitting a

ball, is sufficient to amuse him.

But will you say what object has he in all this? The pleasure

of bragging to-morrow among his friends that he has played

better than another. So others sweat in their own rooms to

show to the learned that they have solved a problem in algebra,

which no one had hitherto been able to solve. Many more

expose themselves to extreme perils, in my opinion as foolishly,

in order to boast afterwards that they have captured a town.

Lastly, others wear themselves out in studying all these things,

not in order to become wiser, but only in order to prove that

they know them; and these are the most senseless of the band,

since they are so knowingly, whereas one may suppose of the

others, that if they knew it, they would no longer be foolish.

This man spends his life without weariness in playing every

day for a small stake. Give him each morning the money he

can win each day, on condition he does not play; you make him

miserable. It will perhaps be said that he seeks the amusement

of play and not the winnings. Make him then play for

nothing; he will not become excited over it, and will feel bored.

It is then not the amusement alone that he seeks; a languid and

passionless amusement will weary him. He must get excited

over it, and deceive himself by the fancy that he will be happy to

win what he would not have as a gift on condition of not playing;

and he must make for himself an object of passion, and excite

over it his desire, his anger, his fear, to obtain his imagined end,

as children are frightened at the face they have blackened.

Whence comes it that this man, who lost his only son a few

months ago, or who this morning was in such trouble through

being distressed by lawsuits and quarrels, now no longer thinks

of them? Do not wonder; he is quite taken up in looking out

for the boar which his dogs have been hunting so hotly for the

last six hours. He requires nothing more. However full of

sadness a man may be, he is happy for the time, if you can

prevail upon him to enter into some amusement; and however

happy a man may be, he will soon be discontented and wretched,

if he be not diverted and occupied by some passion or pursuit

which prevents weariness from overcoming him. Without

amusement there is no joy; with amusement there is no sadness.

And this also constitutes the happiness of persons in high

position, that they have a number of people to amuse them, and

have the power to keep themselves in this state.

Consider this. What is it to be superintendent, chancellor,

first president, but to be in a condition wherein from early

morning a large number of people come from all quarters to see

them, so as not to leave them an hour in the day in which they

can think of themselves? And when they are in disgrace and

sent back to their country houses, where they lack neither

wealth nor servants to help them on occasion, they do not fail

to be wretched and desolate, because no one prevents them from

thinking of themselves.

idle amusements than in the contemplation of his greatness?

And what more satisfactory object could be presented to his

mind? Would it not be a deprivation of his delight for him to

occupy his soul with the thought of how to adjust his steps to

the cadence of an air, or of how to throw a [ball] skilfully,

majestic glory which encompasses him? Let us make the trial;

let us leave a king all alone to reflect on himself quite at leisure,

mind, without society; and we will see that a king without

diversion is a man full of wretchedness. So this is carefully

avoided, and near the persons of kings there never fail to be a

great number of people who see to it that amusement follows

business, and who watch all the time of their leisure to supply

them with delights and games, so that there is no blank in it.

attentive in taking care that the king be not alone and in a state

to think of himself, knowing well that he will be miserable, king

and who are delighted with good luck, are suspected of being

very pleased with the ill success of the affair, if they are not

equally distressed by bad luck; and they are overjoyed to find

these pretexts of hope, in order to show that they are concerned

and to conceal by the joy which they feign to feel that which

they have at seeing the failure of the matter.
