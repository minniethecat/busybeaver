[[Soul|Soul]] (âme), in Pascal's anthropology, is the immaterial principle of human life — the seat of [[mind|mind]], [[will|will]], and passion — distinguished absolutely from the [[body|body]] yet incomprehensibly joined to it. Pascal treats the soul not as a philosophical abstraction but as the locus of the drama between [[greatness-and-misery|greatness and misery]].

This radical dualism — soul and body as "different in kind" — is not Cartesian dualism but an Augustinian insistence on the soul's transcendence of the material order. At the same time, Pascal insists on the mystery of their union:

The question is polemical: the soul is simultaneously too noble for purely material investigation and too obscured by [[original-sin|the Fall]] for purely rational introspection.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Soul's Nobility and Self-Ignorance

Pascal is struck by the paradox that the soul, the most noble object in nature, remains opaque to itself:

> (002-334) soul will know itself. Let us hear

The imperative "let us hear" introduces Pascal's characteristic move: the soul cannot know itself through introspection alone — it must listen to revelation. [[Self-love|Self-love]] (amour-propre) renders introspection unreliable; only [[god|God]], who made the soul, can reveal it to itself.

The soul's nobility is affirmed even in its corruption. [[Greatness-and-misery|Greatness]] persists in the fallen soul because its faculties — reason, will, love — are capable of an infinite object:

### The Passions of the Soul

Pascal's treatment of the passions reveals the soul's vulnerability to the body:

> (002-557) The passions of the soul trouble the senses, and make

The direction of causality is important: the soul's passions trouble the senses, not merely the reverse. The soul is not a passive recipient of bodily impressions but an active principle whose disordered loves distort perception. This connects to Pascal's analysis of [[imagination|imagination]], which operates at the soul-body junction.

> (002-563) little objects so as to fill our souls

The soul's infinite capacity becomes its vulnerability — it seeks to "fill" itself with finite objects because it cannot bear its own emptiness. [[Diversion|Divertissement]] is the soul's flight from itself.

### The Sensitive Soul and the Intellectual Soul

Pascal distinguishes between the "sensitive soul" — the principle of animal life — and the "intellectual soul" unique to humans:

> (002-365) at least a sensitive soul to feel them, nay more, that

This distinction underpins Pascal's apologetics: the human soul's capacity for [[truth|truth]], [[justice|justice]], and [[god|God]] cannot be explained by the sensitive soul alone. The intellectual soul's reach toward the infinite is Pascal's evidence for human dignity.

Pascal's interest in custom extends to the soul: the soul acquires dispositions through habituation. This is foundational to [[pascals-wager|the Wager]]'s practical argument — "bend the [[automaton|automaton]]" means train the soul-body composite through repeated acts.

> (002-858) to the soul, and the soul never presents

The soul "never presents itself" directly — it is known only through its effects, through the qualities it imparts to the person. Pascal's [[epistemology|epistemology]] of the soul is indirect: we infer the soul's nature from its operations.

> (001-163) and tenderness of love, the soul and the mind

Pascal notes an epistemic principle: things appear according to the soul's own disposition. The innocent soul sees innocence; the corrupt soul sees corruption. This asymmetry explains why [[faith|faith]] seems foolish to the unbeliever — the soul doing the seeing is itself disordered.

The "pure soul" is freed from fear not by reasoning but by love — a theme that connects to Pascal's [[three-orders|order of charity]], where love perceives what reason cannot.

## Analysis

Pascal's account of the soul is neither systematic philosophy nor mystical piety but a fusion of Augustinian [[theology|theology]] with psychological acuity. The soul is treated under three aspects: (1) as created — noble, intellectual, capable of [[god|God]]; (2) as fallen — self-ignorant, passion-disordered, enslaved to the [[body|body]]; and (3) as redeemable — capable of receiving [[grace|grace]] and the [[faith|faith]] that reorients it.

The soul's central predicament is that it cannot know itself. It is "too noble" for material investigation yet too darkened by [[original-sin|sin]] for pure introspection. Only revelation — and the [[heart|heart]]'s intuitive grasp of what reason cannot demonstrate — bridges the gap. This is why Pascal's [[pascals-wager|Wager]] addresses the whole soul, not the intellect alone: the automaton must be bent, the passions redirected, the will engaged.
