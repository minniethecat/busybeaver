## Definition

In Pascal's thought, **nature** is an irreducibly dialectical concept. It is simultaneously: (1) **God's creation**, the ordered whole that reveals divine wisdom to the contemplative mind; (2) **the site of corruption**, the fallen order in which "nature is corrupt — proved by nature itself" (002-006); (3) **the lowest of the [[Three Orders]]**, the order of bodies and spatial magnitude, below the order of mind and infinitely below the order of charity; and (4) **the object of scientific contemplation** that, when examined rigorously, reveals human [[Disproportion]] — man suspended between [[Two Infinities|the two infinities]], neither able to grasp the whole nor to reach the foundation. Nature is not one thing in Pascal but a field of tensions, and it is precisely the tensions that make it theologically significant.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Nature as Self-Evidently Corrupt

Pascal's most compressed theological statement about nature is also his most radical:

> (002-006) First part: That nature is corrupt. Proved by nature itself.

The circularity is deliberate and dialectical. Nature proves its own corruption — the evidence of disorder, suffering, and moral confusion is internal to the natural order, not imported from theological dogma. Pascal does not begin with the doctrine of the Fall and then read it into nature. He begins with the observable data of the human condition — the contradictions, the greatness and the misery — and arrives at the Fall as the only adequate explanation. Nature is the *evidence* for the doctrine, not the *consequence* of assuming it. This is the inductive method of Pascal's apologetics: start with what everyone can see, and show that Christianity alone makes sense of it.

### The Contemplation of Nature

Despite its corruption, nature remains worthy of the most serious intellectual attention. Pascal's "Disproportion of Man" fragment is built around an exhortation to contemplate nature:

> (002-090) Let man contemplate the whole of nature in her full and grand majesty. Let him regard that brilliant light set like an eternal lamp to illumine the universe. Let the earth appear to him a point in comparison with the vast orbit described by the sun, and let him wonder at the fact that this vast orbit is itself but a very fine point in comparison with that described by the stars in their revolution round the firmament.

This is not the language of a man who despises the natural order. Pascal was a scientist of the first rank, and his rhetoric of contemplation draws on genuine wonder at the scale and order of the physical universe. Nature, even in its fallen state, reflects the grandeur of its Creator. The contemplation of nature is the beginning of wisdom — not because nature provides all the answers, but because it properly relativizes man. To see nature as it is is to see oneself as one is: a "Nothing in comparison with the Infinite, an All in comparison with the Nothing" (002-144).

### The Order of Nature: Lowest of the Three

Pascal's doctrine of the three orders establishes a strict hierarchy:

> (P01-477) The order of nature, the order of mind, and the order of charity.

The order of nature is the realm of bodies, of spatial and temporal magnitude, of physical force. It is the domain of kings and generals — those who command by the power of the body. It is real and legitimate within its own sphere, but it is the lowest of the three orders. The order of mind (intellect, science, philosophy) is infinitely higher because its objects are immaterial and eternal. The order of charity ([[God]], holiness, the [[Heart]]) is infinitely higher still, because it alone has the infinite as its object. Nature is not evil, but it is radically insufficient. The error is to treat the order of nature as ultimate — to seek satisfaction in bodies, power, or pleasure, all of which belong to an order that cannot satisfy the infinite abyss of the human heart.

### Nature and Custom

Pascal observes that what passes for "nature" in human society is often [[Custom]] disguised:

> (001-303) There is a certain relation between our nature, such as it is, weak or strong, and the things which please us by custom.

> (001-439) To mask nature and disguise her. No more king, pope, bishop.

The relationship between nature and custom is one of Pascal's most subversive insights. Much of what we call natural — social hierarchy, political authority, even aesthetic preference — is in fact customary. Custom becomes "second nature" through the mechanism of [[Habit]], so effectively that we mistake the acquired for the innate. Pascal's political philosophy depends on this distinction: the authority of kings and magistrates rests on custom, not on nature or justice, but the custom is useful and should be respected for the sake of civil peace. Unmasking nature as custom is the philosopher's task; respecting the custom nonetheless is the citizen's duty.

### Nature and Grace

The ultimate Pascalian dialectic is between nature and [[Grace]]. Nature is not abolished by grace but transformed. The [[Corruption]] of nature is not a destruction of nature but a disorder of its proper ordering. Grace does not replace nature but heals it, restoring the proper hierarchy in which the order of charity governs the order of mind, which in turn governs the order of nature. This is the Catholic and Augustinian doctrine, opposed to the Lutheran teaching of total depravity. Pascal's Jansenism, for all its severity, preserves the essential goodness of nature as created — fallen but not destroyed, corrupt but capable of being restored.

### Man's Place in Nature

Pascal's most sustained treatment of nature is anthropological: what is man in relation to nature?

> (002-144) For in fact what is man in nature? A Nothing in comparison with the Infinite, an All in comparison with the Nothing, a mean between nothing and everything.

This is the definitive Pascalian formulation. Man's position in nature is one of radical intermediacy. He is neither the center of the universe (the illusion of pre-Copernican cosmology) nor a meaningless accident (the conclusion of certain forms of materialism). He is a mean — but a mean that *knows* itself as a mean. This self-knowledge is what distinguishes man from the rest of nature. The trees and the stars do not know that they are suspended between infinities. Man does, and this knowledge is at once his greatness and his [[Wretchedness]].

## Analysis

Nature in Pascal is never a univocal concept. He inherits the classical and Christian ambiguities — nature as created good, nature as fallen, nature as the object of science, nature as the realm of the body — and sharpens them into dialectical tools. The ambiguity is not a failure of analysis but its method. Pascal's thought moves by holding opposites in tension, and nature is one of the primary fields on which the tensions play out.

This dialectical treatment of nature has an important apologetic function. The deist argues from the order of nature to the existence of a benevolent Creator. The skeptic argues from the disorder of nature (suffering, evil, the "eternal silence of these infinite spaces") to the absence of any Creator. Pascal's position is that both are partially right: nature *does* show signs of order and design, but it *also* shows signs of disorder and corruption. The deist sees only the order; the skeptic sees only the disorder. Christianity alone explains both: the original goodness of creation and its subsequent corruption.

The three orders doctrine is Pascal's most important contribution to the philosophy of nature. By relativizing the order of bodies to the order of mind and both to the order of charity, Pascal avoids both the scientism that treats physical nature as the whole of reality and the anti-intellectualism that despises nature as worthless. Nature has its proper dignity and its proper limit. The wise man respects nature, studies nature, and passes beyond it.
