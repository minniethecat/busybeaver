Hosea is a pivotal prophetic voice in Pascal's *Pensées*, deployed primarily in the argument about the rejection of the Jews and the calling of the Gentiles. Pascal reads Hosea as the prophet who most explicitly foretells the transfer of divine election from Israel to the nations, making him essential to the apologetic case that Christianity fulfills the prophetic pattern.

## Hosea in Pascal's Apologetic

### The Calling of the Gentiles

Pascal cites Hosea repeatedly to establish that the inclusion of the Gentiles was not a New Testament innovation but a prophecy embedded in the Old Testament itself. He references "Hosea i" (009-215) on the heathen chosen in Israel's place, and "Hosea ii, 24" alongside Joel and Deuteronomy on the calling of the Gentiles (011-414). This cumulative citation strategy — Hosea, Joel, Deuteronomy — reinforces the argument that multiple prophetic witnesses converge on the same prediction.

### The Rejection and Restoration

The tension between rejection and restoration in Hosea provides Pascal with a template for understanding Israel's history. "Hosea iii" speaks of "a long time" of absence before restoration (011-435, 011-417), which Pascal reads as the period between the Babylonian exile and the Messianic age. This pattern — judgment followed by promised restoration — mirrors the larger arc of Pascal's apologetic: the hiddenness of God followed by revelation.

### Hosea and the Prophecy of Jacob

Pascal notes that Hosea's message was "foretold by Hosea, with the prophecy of Jacob" (010-190), linking Hosea's prophetic authority to the patriarchal blessings. This genealogical connection — from Jacob through Hosea — reinforces Pascal's argument that prophecy forms an unbroken chain from the patriarchs through the writing prophets to Christ.

### The Intelligibility of Prophecy

Pascal draws on Hosea for one of his key hermeneutical principles: "Hosea, the last chapter, the last verse" speaks of the righteous understanding while the wicked stumble (011-206), and "Hosea xiv, 10" declares that the ways of the Lord are intelligible to the just (011-664). This principle — that prophecy is clear to believers and obscure to unbelievers — is central to Pascal's epistemology of faith.

### Divine Hiddenness

Pascal finds in Hosea a particularly eloquent expression of divine hiddenness: "Hosea, ult., says excellently" of the Lord's ways (008-227). This passage, coming at the conclusion of Hosea's prophecy, summarizes Pascal's theme that God's apparent absence is itself a mode of revelation — the hidden God who tests the heart through obscurity.

## Significance in the Pensées

Hosea's distinctive contribution to Pascal's argument is the fusion of judgment and mercy in a single prophetic vision. Unlike prophets who emphasize one pole or the other, Hosea's prophecy — the rejected wife, the restored marriage — provides Pascal with the richest Old Testament type of the Christian drama: rejection followed by restoration, judgment yielding to mercy, Israel's loss becoming the Gentiles' gain.
