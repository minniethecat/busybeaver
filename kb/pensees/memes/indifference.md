[[Indifference|Indifference]], in Pascal's existential analysis, is the posture of the [[unbeliever|unbeliever]] who claims not to care about the question of [[salvation|salvation]]. For Pascal, this posture is not intellectually respectable but existentially incoherent: the most important question that a mortal being can ask — whether there is a [[god|God]] and what follows from His existence — cannot rationally be met with indifference.

> (002-036) ...indifference about salvation, without fear...

Pascal couples indifference with the absence of [[fear|fear]] — and the absence of fear, in a being poised between [[eternity|eternity]] and [[nothingness|nothingness]], is itself a symptom of profound irrationality. The person who claims indifference to salvation is like a man who claims indifference to whether the bridge he is crossing will hold.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Dishonesty of Indifference

Pascal's critique of indifference is not, primarily, a moral critique but an epistemological one. The indifferent person claims to have settled the question — to have determined that it does not merit attention. But this settlement is dishonest: no one can genuinely determine, without investigation, that the most consequential possible truth is not worth investigating.

> (002-328) ...sceptics in their indifference, doubt, and perpetual suspense...

Pascal groups indifference with [[scepticism|scepticism]] and "perpetual suspense" as variants of the same refusal. The [[sceptic|sceptic]] who claims to suspend judgment indefinitely, the indifferent who claims not to care, and the [[doubter|doubter]] who claims the question is undecidable are all engaged in the same flight from [[decision|decision]]. What unites them is not intellectual rigour but existential evasion.

### Indifference and the Wager

The concept of indifference is the negative space against which the [[wager|Wager]] is constructed. The Wager presupposes that one cannot be indifferent: one must choose. "You must wager. It is not voluntary. You are embarked."

> (003-275) ...indifference to the search for truth in a...

Pascal's target here is the [[libertine|libertine]] — the cultivated unbeliever who treats religious questions as matters of taste rather than [[truth|truth]]. Against this, Pascal insists that the search for truth is not optional. Indifference is not a third option between [[belief|belief]] and [[unbelief|unbelief]]; it is unbelief in practice, however it styles itself in theory.

### The Psychology of Indifference

Pascal's analysis of indifference is psychologically acute. The indifferent person is not genuinely without concern for the question; rather, the appearance of indifference is a defence against the [[anxiety|anxiety]] the question provokes.

> (008-137) ...from the ungodly, who live in indifference to religion...

The indifference of the "ungodly" is not ontological — it is not that they have no relation to [[god|God]] — but practical: they live as though God did not exist. This practical atheism is what Pascal's apologetic strategy is designed to break open. The Wager aims to convert indifference into [[urgency|urgency]]; [[diversion|diversion]] explains why indifference is so difficult to sustain and so desperately maintained.

> (008-173) ...thought we have of it, and in the indifference...

> (009-658) ...If does not indicate indifference: Malachi, Isaiah.

The prophetic texts themselves, Pascal notes, distinguish between true and false indifference. [[Scripture|Scripture]] does not treat indifference as a neutral epistemological stance but as a culpable refusal of [[revelation|revelation]].

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of indifference is best understood within his broader [[anthropology|anthropology]] of the [[flight-from-self|flight from self]]. Man, unable to bear the sight of his own [[wretchedness|wretchedness]], diverts himself — into [[entertainment|entertainment]], into [[ambition|ambition]], into the pretence of not caring. Indifference is one of the faces of diversion.

What makes Pascal's treatment of indifference philosophically distinctive is his refusal to grant it the dignity of a position. Modern thought has often treated agnosticism — the suspension of judgment — as the intellectually respectable default. Pascal treats it as evasion. One cannot suspend judgment on a question that, by its very nature, demands a decision. The man who claims to be "waiting for more evidence" is waiting for what will never come: a [[certainty|certainty]] that would compel rather than invite.

This is the existential nerve of Pascal's apologetics. [[Christianity|Christianity]] does not offer the kind of evidence that silences [[doubt|doubt]]; it offers the kind of evidence that makes indifference unreasonable. Between the evidence for and the evidence against, there is room for [[choice|choice]] — but there is no room for the refusal to choose.
