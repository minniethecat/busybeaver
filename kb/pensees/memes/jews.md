## Definition

The **Jewish people** (les Juifs) occupy a central role in Pascal's apologetic argument. For Pascal, the Jews are not merely a historical people but **divinely appointed witnesses** — the bearers of prophecy, the preservers of Scripture, and the living proof of Christianity's historical continuity. Pascal's treatment distinguishes between "carnal Jews" (who interpret Scripture literally and expect a temporal Messiah) and "true Jews" (who recognize the spiritual meaning of prophecy and thus become Christians). This distinction is foundational to Pascal's theory of **figurative interpretation** (typology), in which the Old Testament prefigures the New.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Jewish People as Prophetic Witnesses

Pascal argues that the Jewish people's continued existence — scattered yet preserved — is itself evidence of divine providence. Their Scripture, preserved across millennia, contains prophecies that Pascal reads as pointing unmistakably to Jesus Christ (004-164). The Jews are "the most ancient of peoples" and their law is "the most ancient of laws" — a fact Pascal deploys against skeptics who questioned Christianity's historical credentials.

> (009-683) to see this Jewish people existing so many years...

The survival of the Jewish people despite persecution, exile, and dispersion is in Pascal's hands a providential sign: the people who preserved the prophecies are themselves preserved as living testimony.

### Carnal vs. Spiritual Reading

Pascal's central hermeneutical move is the distinction between two ways of reading Scripture. The "carnal Jews" (juifs charnels) read the promises of a Messiah as referring to an earthly king who would deliver Israel politically. The "true Jews" (and Christians) recognize these promises as figures (types) whose spiritual fulfillment occurs in Jesus Christ.

> (009-135) Messiah, according to the carnal Jews, was to be a great temporal prince

> (009-144) The carnal Jews hold a midway place between Christians and pagans

This "midway" position is crucial to Pascal's argument: the carnal Jews accept the true God and the true Scripture but misinterpret them through carnal expectations. They are closer to truth than pagans (who worship false gods) but remain blind to the spiritual meaning they themselves preserve.

> (009-139) True Jews and true Christians have one and the same religion

Pascal goes so far as to identify "true Jews" with Christians — those who read Scripture spiritually belong to the same religion, differing only in their historical position relative to Christ's coming.

### The Jewish Religion's Divine Character

Pascal insists that the Jewish religion, properly understood, bears marks of divinity:

> (009-114) The Jewish religion is wholly divine in its origin, in its duration, in its perpetuity, in its morality, in its conduct

He warns against judging it superficially:

> (009-129) Whoever judges of the Jewish religion by its coarser forms will misunderstand it

The visible, external forms of Jewish law — dietary restrictions, circumcision, ritual purity — are not the essence of the religion. They are "figures" whose spiritual meaning is revealed in Christianity (010-190).

### Typology: The Jewish People as Figure

Pascal develops an extensive typological reading in which persons, events, and institutions of the Old Testament prefigure Christian realities:

> (010-155) The Jewish and Egyptian peoples were plainly foretold...

The Jewish people themselves become a "type" — their history (exodus, exile, return) prefigures the spiritual history of the Church. Even their rejection of Christ is foretold in prophecy, so that their very unbelief confirms the truth of Scripture (011-105).

### Zeal for the Law

Pascal notes the extraordinary devotion of the Jewish people to their law:

> (011-068) Zeal of the Jewish people for the law, especially when there was no longer any prophet

This zeal persisted even after prophecy ceased — a fact Pascal treats as evidence that the law itself carried divine authority, since human custom alone could not sustain such devotion across centuries of dispersion and suffering (011-077).

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of the Jews serves his larger apologetic strategy at three levels:

**Historically**, the Jewish people provide the continuous chain of transmission that links Christianity to antiquity. Against deists and libertines who dismissed Christianity as a recent innovation, Pascal points to the Jewish Scriptures as an ancient, publicly preserved documentary record that predates and predicts Christ.

**Hermeneutically**, the carnal/spiritual distinction allows Pascal to explain why the Jews — who possessed the prophecies — did not universally convert to Christianity. Their blindness is itself foretold and serves a providential function: the "veil" over their reading preserves the integrity of the prophecies until the time of fulfillment.

**Typologically**, the Jewish people function as a "figure" of the Church. Their history — election, covenant, exile, preservation — is the template for understanding the spiritual history of the Christian community. Pascal's typological method treats the Old Testament not as a separate dispensation but as a coherent symbolic system whose meaning is fully realized only in Christ.

The concept connects to Pascal's broader themes: the [[Three Orders]] (the Jewish law belongs to the order of body, the spiritual reading to the order of mind, and the fulfillment in charity to the highest order), [[Figurative Interpretation]], and the relation between [[Custom]] and divine authority.
