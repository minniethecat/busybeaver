# Habit

## In Pascal's Pensées

Habit (habitude) is for Pascal the psychological mechanism by which custom becomes second nature. It is the individual correlate of custom (coutume), which is the social and collective phenomenon. "Want of habit makes the other impossible" (003-435) — without habit, the practices of faith remain alien and unattainable. Habit is the bridge between repeated action and settled disposition: it is the mechanism underlying Pascal's famous advice to "bend the automaton." Through habit, the body is trained, the passions are redirected, and belief becomes possible. Habit is thus central to Pascal's practical apologetics and his psychology of belief formation.

## Key Passages

> "For want of habit it is difficult to turn one's mind in a new direction." (001-006)

> "Easy to us; want of habit makes the other impossible." (003-435)

> "He is shocked at a fool, and that habit is all-powerful; but he who considers the reason of the effect..." (003-688)

> "The habit of seeing kings accompanied by guards, drums, and officers... begets respect and terror." (005-177)

> "The result of habit, believes that it arises by a natural right." (005-184)

## Analysis

Habit occupies a strategic position in Pascal's psychology, connecting the order of body (the automaton) to the order of mind (belief) and ultimately to the order of charity (faith). The Wager's practical argument — "bend the automaton" by taking holy water, having masses said — works through habit. External actions, repeated, shape internal dispositions. This is neither hypocrisy nor behaviorism: it is Pascal's recognition that human beings are embodied creatures whose beliefs are mediated through practice.

The distinction between habit and custom is important. Custom is the social phenomenon — the conventions, laws, and practices that a society inherits and enforces. Habit is the individual internalization of custom — the psychological mechanism by which "second nature" is formed. "The habit of seeing kings accompanied by guards... begets respect and terror" — the political order works through habit, not reason. We obey not because we have rationally evaluated the king's legitimacy, but because habit has made obedience feel natural.

Pascal's analysis of habit has a critical edge. "The result of habit, believes that it arises by a natural right" — habit disguises its own contingency. What is merely customary comes to seem natural, inevitable, even just. This is the conservative dimension of Pascal's thought (habit stabilizes society) and also its radical potential (habit can be analyzed, exposing the contingency of social arrangements).

In the context of faith, habit is essential but insufficient. Habit can prepare the automaton, but only grace can give faith. The practices of religion — prayer, sacraments, Scripture reading — work through habit to dispose the soul, but they do not compel belief. This is the Jansenist balance: habit is the human mechanism; grace is the divine gift.
