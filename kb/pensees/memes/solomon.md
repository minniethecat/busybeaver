[[Solomon|King Solomon]] appears in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées* in two related roles: as the paradigmatic [[wise man]] whose wisdom nevertheless failed to penetrate the deepest truths, and as the author of [[Ecclesiastes]] — the [[biblical]] book whose meditation on [[vanity]] provides one of the intellectual sources for Pascal's analysis of the [[human condition]].

Unlike [[King David|David]] or [[Moses]], Solomon is not a primary figure in Pascal's [[typology]] or [[prophecy|prophetic]] argument. His significance lies rather in what his career demonstrates about the limits of natural [[wisdom]] and the inability of worldly [[achievement]] — power, wealth, knowledge — to satisfy the human [[heart]].

## Solomon as Witness

Pascal invokes Solomon alongside David in the context of arguing that the [[biblical]] witnesses acknowledged the limits of human knowledge rather than claiming a comprehension they did not possess.

> [004-042] David, Solomon, etc., have never said, "There is no void": therefore they were better informed than all the philosophers.

The reference is to the ancient debate over the possibility of a [[vacuum]] — a question of [[natural philosophy]] on which Pascal himself conducted significant experimental work. Pascal's point is that the biblical authors, unlike the [[Aristotelianism|Aristotelian]] philosophers who dogmatically denied the possibility of a void, refrained from pronouncing on matters that lay beyond their [[knowledge]]. This intellectual humility, far from discrediting them, demonstrates their superiority to the [[philosophers]] who confused inherited opinion with demonstrated truth.

Solomon thus serves, in Pascal's argument, as an example of the proper [[epistemological]] attitude: the wise man knows what he does not know and does not claim to know what he cannot demonstrate. This accords with Pascal's broader insistence on the limits of [[reason]] and the necessary role of [[faith]] in matters that exceed rational [[comprehension]].

## Solomon and the King

Pascal finds in Solomon a figure through whom the [[typological]] relationship between the [[Davidic]] monarchy and the kingship of [[Jesus Christ|Christ]] can be explored.

> [010-115] Solomon and the King are spoken of in the same chapter.

This observation, drawn from Pascal's reading of the [[Scriptures]], points to the figural interpretation in which the historical kings of [[Israel]] — and Solomon in particular, as the builder of the [[Temple]] and the zenith of Israel's political power — prefigure the coming King whose reign will transcend the limitations of the Davidic dynasty. The glory of Solomon's reign, however magnificent, is a shadow of the greater glory to come.

Yet Pascal also finds in Solomon a cautionary tale. The king who possessed wisdom, wealth, and power beyond all others nevertheless concluded that "all is vanity" — a verdict that Pascal appropriates for his own analysis of the [[human condition]]. Solomon's failure to find satisfaction in any worldly good anticipates Pascal's argument that [[happiness]] cannot be achieved through the accumulation of finite goods, however impressive. The [[infinite]] abyss in the human heart can be filled only by an infinite object — [[God]] — and Solomon's experience, as Pascal reads it, demonstrates this truth through its negative example.

## The Limits of Wisdom

Pascal's use of Solomon culminates in the recognition that [[wisdom]], understood as the accumulation of knowledge and the skillful management of life, is insufficient for [[salvation]]. Solomon "had it all" — and found it wanting. This is not, for Pascal, an argument against the pursuit of wisdom but against the expectation that wisdom alone can deliver what it promises.

> [012-279] all that Solomon and the prophets said

In this passing reference, Pascal groups Solomon with the [[prophets]] — not because Solomon was a prophet in the strict sense but because his writings, like the prophetic oracles, testify to truths that exceed natural [[reason]]'s power to discover. Ecclesiastes, in Pascal's reading, is a work of [[revelation]] rather than merely of human wisdom: its author speaks not from the resources of his own [[intellect]] but from the vantage point granted by [[divine]] illumination.

This is the deepest significance of Solomon in the *Pensées*: the wisest of men, having pursued every avenue of human [[fulfillment]] and found each one empty, bears witness — however unwittingly — to the necessity of [[grace]]. His failure is not a personal defect but a demonstration of the [[human condition]] itself. If Solomon cannot find satisfaction, no one can; and if no one can, then satisfaction must come from outside the human sphere — from the God whom Solomon, at the end of his searching, acknowledges as the sole ground of meaning.
