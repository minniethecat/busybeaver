## Definition

In Pascal's theology, **prayer** (prière) is the **embodied response of the soul to its condition before God**. It is at once the most natural and the most supernatural of human acts. Prayer arises from the recognition of human dependence — on God for existence, for [[Salvation]], for every good — and it is made possible only by the [[Grace]] that God gives through Jesus Christ. Pascal's understanding of prayer is shaped by his Jansenist theology: prayer is not a technique for manipulating God but an act of [[Humility]] in which the creature acknowledges its [[Nothingness]] before the Creator.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Prayer as the Posture of the Seeker

Pascal's most personal statement about prayer appears in the conclusion of the [[Wager]] argument. Having shown that the rational choice is to bet on God's existence, Pascal anticipates the objection: "I am so made that I cannot believe. What would you have me do?" His answer is not more argument but prayer — and the embodied practice that accompanies it:

> (003-667) It is made by a man who has knelt, both before and after it, in prayer to that Being, infinite and without parts, before whom he lays all he has.

Prayer here is not an alternative to rational argument but its completion. The [[Wager]] brings the seeker to the threshold of belief; prayer carries the seeker across it. This reflects Pascal's consistent insistence that belief is not merely intellectual assent but involves the whole person — body, mind, and will. As with the [[Automaton]], the body's posture (kneeling) participates in the soul's orientation toward God.

### Prayer in the Life of the Church

Pascal sees prayer as an essential dimension of the Church's life, and he finds in Scripture a divine intentionality behind the language of the Church's prayers. God has deliberately placed paradoxical language in Scripture and the prayers of the Church to produce their spiritual fruit in due time:

> (008-227) God, being unwilling to give heretics occasion to arise from correct expressions, has put in Scripture and the prayers of the Church contrary words and sentences to produce their fruit in time.

This is a characteristically Pascalian insight about divine pedagogy. The obscurities and apparent contradictions in Scripture and liturgy are not defects but features — they conceal truth from those who approach with pride while revealing it to those who approach with humility. Prayer is both the context in which this revelation occurs and the disposition that makes it possible.

### Prayer and the Mystical Body

Pascal's most vivid image of prayer comes in his discussion of the Church as the mystical body of Christ. The soul united to Christ through [[Charity]] is like a member of a body, and its prayers for preservation express its dependence on the whole:

> (007-637) What prayers for its preservation in it! And with what submission would it allow itself to be governed by the will which rules the body, even to consenting, if necessary, to be cut off, or it would lose its character as member!

The soul's prayer is an expression of its membership in Christ's body. To pray is to acknowledge that one's life is not one's own — that one exists only in relation to the Head. This connects to Pascal's theology of [[Sacrifice]]: the member must be willing to perish for the body, just as Christ perished for His Church.

### Prayer and Charity

Pascal insists that prayer derives its value not from its form or quantity but from its union with the charity of Jesus Christ. Apart from this union, even prayer and virtue are "abominable before God":

> (010-190) Our prayers and our virtues are abominable before God, if they are not the prayers and the virtues of Jesus Christ.

This is Pascal's most radically Christocentric statement about prayer. Human prayer, like human virtue, is tainted by [[Concupiscence]] and [[Self-Love]] (amour-propre). It becomes acceptable to God only when it is taken up into Christ's own prayer — when the one who prays is united to Christ through charity. This is the theological foundation of praying "in Jesus' name": it is not a formula appended to prayer but the condition of prayer's efficacy.

### The House of Prayer

Pascal draws on the prophetic tradition of Israel to connect prayer with the universal scope of God's redemptive purpose. Quoting Isaiah, he presents the Temple as "a house of prayer" for all peoples, prefiguring the Church's universal mission:

> (009-181) Mine house is a house of prayer.

This Old Testament image is transformed in the light of Christ. The Temple in Jerusalem was a type; the Church — and ultimately the soul united to Christ — is the reality. Prayer is the activity that defines the sacred space where God and humanity meet.

## Analysis

Pascal's theology of prayer integrates several distinct strands of his thought. Epistemologically, prayer continues the trajectory of the [[Wager]]: reason brings one to the threshold, and prayer is the act by which one crosses it. Anthropologically, prayer engages the whole person — body (kneeling), mind (attention to God), and will (submission) — reflecting Pascal's integrated view of human nature against Cartesian dualism. Christologically, prayer's efficacy depends entirely on union with Christ, reflecting Pascal's thoroughly Christocentric soteriology.

The contrast with philosophical approaches to prayer is instructive. For Pascal, prayer is not a technique of contemplation (as in Neoplatonism) or a form of autosuggestion (as a modern skeptic might describe it) but a **supernatural act** — something that God enables and that transforms the one who prays. The soul does not ascend to God through prayer; God descends to the soul through grace, and prayer is the soul's response.

The concept is closely related to [[Humility]] (the disposition that makes prayer possible), [[Grace]] (the divine gift without which prayer is impossible), [[Charity]] (the love that unites the one who prays to Christ), and [[Automaton]] (the bodily dimension of kneeling and other physical postures of prayer). It also connects to [[Conversion]], since Pascal presents his own conversion — the night of the Memorial — as an experience of prayer.
