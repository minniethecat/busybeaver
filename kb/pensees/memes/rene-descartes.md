[[René Descartes]] (1596–1650) appears in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées* not as a source to be drawn upon but as a foil to be refuted. Pascal's famous declaration — "I cannot forgive Descartes" — encapsulates a philosophical opposition that runs far deeper than personal animosity. For Pascal, Descartes represents the [[rationalism|rationalist]] project in its purest form: the attempt to ground all knowledge in the autonomous exercise of [[reason]], bypassing both [[revelation]] and the [[heart]]'s non-rational modes of apprehension.

The critique of Descartes is thus not a peripheral polemic but central to Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetic]] strategy. If Descartes is right — if [[philosophy]] can deliver certain knowledge of [[God]], the [[self]], and the world by its own resources — then [[faith]] is at best redundant and at worst irrational. Pascal's entire project depends on demonstrating that Descartes is not right, that reason alone cannot grasp the realities that matter most, and that the Cartesian [[deity]] is not the living God of [[Abraham]], [[Isaac]], and [[Jacob]].

## The God of the Philosophers

The core of Pascal's objection to Descartes is theological. The [[God]] of Cartesian philosophy — a first cause who "set the world in motion" and then withdrew, leaving it to run by mechanical laws — is not, Pascal insists, the God whom [[Christians]] worship.

> [002-375] I cannot forgive Descartes. In all his philosophy he would have done away with God entirely, had he not been obliged to allow Him a fillip to set the world in motion; after that, he has no further use for God.

This is a devastating charge: Descartes' God is not merely inadequate but practically absent. Having served His [[cosmological]] function as the initial mover, the Cartesian God becomes irrelevant to the actual conduct of human life. Pascal contrasts this with the [[biblical]] God who acts continuously in [[history]], who enters into [[covenant]] with particular persons, and whose relationship with [[humanity]] is one of ongoing involvement rather than initial impulse.

The distinction Pascal draws here is not merely academic. A God who can be arrived at by [[philosophical]] demonstration is a God who can be possessed by the [[reason|intellect]] — and a God who can be possessed by the intellect cannot save. Salvation requires transcendence, mystery, and the encounter with a [[divine]] reality that exceeds the mind's capacity to comprehend. Descartes' God, precisely because He is rationally accessible, fails to be the God of [[revelation]].

> [002-380] Descartes useless and uncertain.

The verdict is compressed to three words: useless (because his God does nothing for [[salvation]]) and uncertain (because his method cannot deliver the [[certainty]] it promises).

## Esprit de Géométrie and Its Limits

Pascal's critique of Descartes extends from theology to [[epistemology]]. Descartes is the supreme practitioner of what Pascal calls the *esprit de géométrie* — the mathematical [[mind]] that proceeds by clear definitions and rigorous deduction from self-evident [[principles]]. This mode of thinking is powerful within its proper domain, but Pascal insists that it is inadequate to the most important realities.

The *esprit de géométrie* can analyze [[matter]], measure [[motion]], and deduce [[logical]] consequences. It cannot grasp the [[human condition]] — the paradox of [[greatness and misery]], the experience of [[diversion]], the intuition of the [[heart]]. These realities require what Pascal calls the *esprit de finesse* — the intuitive mind that perceives truth in its wholeness and complexity rather than decomposing it into simple elements.

Descartes' limitation is thus not intellectual but [[spiritual]]: he has mistaken one mode of knowing for the whole of knowledge, reducing the rich particularity of human experience to what can be captured in clear and distinct [[ideas]]. Pascal's counter-proposal is not [[irrationalism]] — he does not reject reason — but a more adequate account of how [[human beings]] actually come to know what they know. The heart, not the geometric method, is the organ of the most important truths.

## Descartes and the Wager

Pascal's critique of Descartes reaches its most concentrated expression in the context of the [[Wager]] argument. The Wager's logic depends on the [[epistemological]] claim that [[reason]] cannot settle the question of God's existence: "If there is a God, He is infinitely incomprehensible... Reason can decide nothing here." Descartes, by contrast, offers rational proofs for God's existence — the ontological argument, the cosmological argument — that purport to deliver demonstrative certainty.

Pascal's response is not to refute these proofs in detail but to deny their [[existential]] relevance. Even if Descartes' arguments were logically valid — and Pascal has his doubts — they reach a God who is merely the conclusion of a syllogism, not the living God who demands [[conversion]], [[faith]], and [[love]]. The God of the proofs leaves the [[sinner]] unchanged; the God of [[Jesus Christ]] transforms the soul.

This is the deepest level of Pascal's quarrel with Descartes: not a disagreement about [[philosophical]] method but a conflict between two fundamentally different understandings of what it means to know God. For Descartes, to know God is to [[comprehend]] a proposition. For Pascal, to know God is to be seized by a reality that exceeds [[comprehension]] and demands the surrender of the whole [[self]].

## Descartes in Pascal's Intellectual World

Pascal's engagement with Descartes was not merely theoretical. The two men inhabited the same [[intellectual]] world of seventeenth-century [[France]], and Pascal knew Descartes' work intimately. The *Pensées* contains traces of a sustained dialogue — sometimes explicit, often implicit — with the [[Cartesian]] system that dominated French philosophy in Pascal's lifetime.

Descartes' presence in the *Pensées* is thus more pervasive than the explicit mentions (002-373, 002-375, 002-380, 002-382) suggest. Every passage in which Pascal insists on the limits of reason, the priority of the heart, or the insufficiency of philosophical [[theism]] is, in effect, an argument against Descartes — whether or not he is named.

> [002-382] Descartes.—We must say summarily: "This is made by figure and motion," for it is true. But to say what these are, and to compose the machine, is ridiculous.

Pascal grants Descartes the [[mechanism]] — the claim that material reality can be described in terms of "figure and motion" — while denying that this description captures what is most important about the world or the human place within it. The [[machine]] is real; but the person who constructs the machine, and the [[God]] who creates both the machine and its constructor, cannot be reduced to the machine's terms.
