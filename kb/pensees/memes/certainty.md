[[Certainty|Certainty]], for Pascal, is what [[reason|reason]] craves and what the human condition denies. The [[human-nature|human]] being floats between [[knowledge|knowledge]] and ignorance, "ever drifting in uncertainty" — and this is not a temporary predicament but a permanent structural feature of finite existence confronting the [[infinite|infinite]].

> [002-228] a vast sphere, ever drifting in uncertainty, driven from end to end.

The problem of certainty runs through the entire *Pensées*: epistemological (what can we know for certain?), practical (on what basis should we act?), and theological (can we be certain about [[God|God]]?). Pascal's distinctive contribution is to insist that the demand for certainty is itself a symptom of human [[greatness-and-misery|greatness]] — we demand it because we are made for truth — and a symptom of human misery — we cannot satisfy the demand.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Impossibility of Epistemic Certainty

Pascal locates the impossibility of certainty not in a particular failure of method but in the structure of the knowing situation itself. [[human-nature|Man]] is "a nothing in comparison with the [[infinite|infinite]], an all in comparison with [[nothingness|nothingness]], a mean between nothing and everything" — and this intermediate position makes certainty structurally impossible:

> [002-237] Let us therefore not look for certainty and stability. Our reason is always deceived by fickle shadows; nothing can fix the finite between the two infinites which enclose and flee from it.

The problem is not that we have not yet found the right method. It is that the knower stands between two infinities — the infinitely large and the infinitely small — and no finite intellect can achieve the kind of stable grasp that [[certainty|certainty]] requires. This is the epistemological dimension of [[disproportion|disproportion]].

> [002-070] The infinites, the mean. — When we read too fast or too slowly, we understand nothing.

Even in the most mundane acts of understanding — reading a text — there is no fixed point. Too fast, and we miss the meaning; too slow, and we lose the thread. The "mean" is not a stable position but a moving target that can never be definitively located.

### The Wager: Certainty and Practical Reason

The [[pascals-wager|Wager]] is Pascal's most famous treatment of certainty — and its most paradoxical. The Wager concedes that certainty about [[God|God's]] existence is unavailable to [[reason|reason]]:

> [003-672] If we must not act save on a certainty, we ought not to act on religion, for it is not certain. But how many things we do on an uncertainty — sea voyages, battles! I say that we ought to do nothing at all, for nothing is certain.

The argument is a *reductio*: if certainty were required for action, we would be paralysed, because nothing is certain. Yet we act, and act rationally, on the basis of probabilities. The Wager extends this practical reasoning to the question of [[God|God's]] existence — showing that even in the absence of certainty, there can be decisive reasons for [[faith-and-reason|faith]].

> [003-617] ...certainty to gain an uncertainty, and yet we risk a finite certainty to gain a finite uncertainty, without acting unreasonably.

## Key Passages

> [003-661] ...see so great certainty of gain, so much nothingness in what we stake, that we can no longer hesitate.

The Wager's rhetorical climax: the disproportion between the certainty of what is risked (finite, negligible) and the possibility of gain (infinite) makes hesitation irrational. This is not a proof — it does not produce certainty — but it shows that the absence of certainty does not justify inaction.

> [003-157] ...state, full of weakness and uncertainty. And from this it appears that however long we live, and whatever our natural advantages, we are always in a state of weakness and uncertainty.

The universal human condition: "weakness and uncertainty." No amount of natural advantage — intelligence, education, leisure — escapes it. This is not a pessimistic conclusion but an accurate description that [[christianity|Christianity]], Pascal argues, alone takes seriously.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of certainty occupies a distinctive position in early modern epistemology. Unlike [[descartes|Descartes]], who sought to establish certainty on an indubitable foundation (the *cogito*), Pascal denies that such a foundation is available. And unlike the [[skepticism|sceptics]], who conclude from the absence of certainty that nothing can be known, Pascal insists that the absence of certainty does not entail the absence of knowledge or the impossibility of rational action.

The key move is Pascal's distinction between domains. In mathematics and the natural sciences, demonstrative [[reason|reason]] can achieve something close to certainty — though even here, Pascal notes, the first principles on which demonstration rests cannot themselves be demonstrated. In matters of [[faith-and-reason|faith]], [[morality|morality]], and the meaning of human existence, certainty of the demonstrative kind is unavailable — but other forms of knowing, grounded in the [[heart|heart]] and in [[custom|custom]], are legitimate.

Pascal's position thus anticipates elements of later pragmatism and [[existentialism|existentialism]]: the demand for certainty is itself the problem. [[human-nature|Human]] beings must learn to act, believe, and live without the guarantee that demonstrative certainty would provide — and the [[pascals-wager|Wager]] is Pascal's model for how this can be done rationally.
