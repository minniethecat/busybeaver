[[Freedom|Freedom]], in Pascal's thought, cannot be understood apart from his [[theology|theology]] of [[grace|grace]] and his [[anthropology|anthropology]] of the divided [[will|will]]. His position navigates between the extremes of Pelagian autonomy and Calvinist determinism.

## The Freedom of the Will

Pascal affirms the Catholic doctrine of free will while recognizing its limits:

> [002-1075] ...ordinary Catholic dogma of the freedom of the will, and merely sufficient...

The [[will|will]] is genuinely free, yet this freedom operates within the constraints of a fallen [[human-nature|human nature]]. The [[concupiscence|concupiscence]] that inclines the will toward [[sin|sin]] does not abolish freedom but wounds it — a characteristically [[Jansenism|Jansenist]] formulation that Pascal shares with [[Saint Augustine|Augustine]].

## True Freedom and False Liberty

Pascal distinguishes between authentic freedom and the false liberty of self-assertion:

> [010-190] ...only types, and what is "true freedom," a "true Israelite," "true circumcision"...

True freedom is not the absence of constraint but the liberation of the [[will|will]] to love what is truly good. This is a freedom ordered toward [[God|God]] — the "freedom from fault" (007-637) that [[grace|grace]] alone can accomplish.

## The Paradox of Liberty

Pascal recognizes the danger of unqualified liberty:

> [006-137] ...it is not good to have too much liberty. It is not good to have all one wants...

> [002-1075] ...like other people, to have liberty to follow their own imaginations...

Liberty without orientation becomes [[diversion|diversion]], the restless pursuit of whatever [[imagination|imagination]] presents. The [[human-condition|human being]] left to its own devices does not choose freedom but flees from it into [[boredom|boredom]] and distraction. Authentic freedom requires a [[sovereign-good|sovereign good]] to direct it.

## Freedom and the Cross

> [012-270] ...freedom to the one, and death to the other...

The [[cross|cross]] of [[Christ|Christ]] enacts the ultimate drama of freedom: one thief chooses [[faith|faith]] and receives [[paradise|paradise]]; the other persists in [[unbelief|unbelief]]. The same circumstances, the same [[grace|grace]] offered — yet two different responses. For Pascal, this is both a mystery and a paradigm of human freedom in its decisive moment.
