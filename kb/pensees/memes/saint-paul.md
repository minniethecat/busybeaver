[[Saint Paul]] occupies a unique position in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées* as both a witness to the truth of [[Christianity]] and a model for how that truth is to be communicated.

Unlike the [[apostles]] who walked with [[Jesus Christ]] during his earthly ministry, Paul encountered the risen Christ on the Damascus road, becoming for Pascal the paradigmatic post-Resurrection witness whose authority derives not from personal acquaintance but from [[divine]] commission.

Pascal draws extensively on Paul's writings, particularly in developing his understanding of the relationship between [[faith]] and [[reason]], the nature of [[love]], and the hiddenness of [[God]].

## Paul's Hermeneutics of Love

Pascal's most distinctive use of Paul centers on the Apostle's interpretative method. In a passage that serves as a hermeneutical key to the entire *Pensées*, Pascal contrasts two modes of persuasion: the rule of intellect and the rule of love.

> [004-347] Jesus Christ and Saint Paul employ the rule of love, not of intellect, for their purpose was not to demonstrate but to enkindle.

This distinction is fundamental to Pascal's apologetic strategy. He does not attempt to prove Christianity through syllogistic [[reasoning]] — a method he associates with [[René Descartes|Descartes]] and finds spiritually sterile. Instead, following Paul, he seeks to present the truth in a way that engages the whole person: intellect, [[heart]], and will. The goal is not merely intellectual assent but transformation of the affections.

Paul thus becomes the theological warrant for Pascal's distinctive rhetorical approach: indirect, suggestive, cumulative rather than deductive, aimed at those who have "eyes to see" without coercing those who do not.

## The Christian Character

Pascal cites Paul's description of the Christian character as a diagnostic tool for distinguishing genuine faith from its counterfeits. The Latin phrase from the Vulgate — *comminutum cor* — captures the essence of what Paul and Pascal understand as authentic Christian existence.

> [007-637] Comminutum cor (Saint Paul). This is the Christian character.

The "contrite heart" stands in opposition to both the pride of the [[philosophers]] and the complacency of nominal believers. For Pascal, Paul's anthropology aligns with his own dialectical understanding of the [[human condition]]: [[greatness and misery]] held together, resolvable only through the mediation of Christ. The broken heart is not merely a moral condition but an epistemological one — it is precisely those who recognize their insufficiency who are open to receiving what they cannot achieve.

## Paul and the Hidden God

Pascal draws on Paul's theology to develop his account of divine hiddenness. Paul's insistence that the cross must not be "emptied of its power" — *ne evacuata sit crux* — informs Pascal's rejection of any apologetic that would make [[faith]] too easy or too rationalistic (008-227).

The hidden God of Pascal's theology is, in significant measure, the God proclaimed by Paul: a God who reveals himself sufficiently for those who seek but remains obscure to those who demand certainty on their own terms. Paul, who "came with wisdom and signs" (010-190), embodies the balanced presentation of evidence that Pascal advocates — neither miracle without reason nor reason without miracle, but a convergence of witnesses that leaves room for freedom.

> [010-190] After His death, Saint Paul came to teach men that all these things had happened in figure.

This typological reading — seeing the events of the [[Old Testament]] as prefigurations fulfilled in Christ — is central to Paul's hermeneutics and becomes central to Pascal's. Paul teaches that the law, the prophets, and the history of [[Israel]] all converge on the person of Christ, a convergence that constitutes the cumulative case for Christianity.

## Paul Against False Prophets

Pascal's use of Paul extends to the problem of discerning true from false religious claims. He cites Paul's warning in 2 Thessalonians about those who come "in signis mendacibus" — with lying signs (013-440). The presence of competing miracle claims was as much a challenge in the apostolic era as in Pascal's own, and Paul's criteria for distinguishing authentic from counterfeit witness provide Pascal with a framework for addressing modern skepticism.

Paul's own life — his transformation from persecutor to apostle — serves as a living argument for the truth of what he proclaims. Pascal, who understood the psychology of [[conversion]] intimately, finds in Paul the paradigm of how radical change of heart can itself become evidence for the reality of [[grace]].
