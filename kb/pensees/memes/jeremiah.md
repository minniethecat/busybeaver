[[Jeremiah]] appears in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées* as the [[prophet]] who most clearly articulates the transition from external [[religion]] to inward [[faith]]. His promise of a [[new covenant]] written on the [[heart]] rather than on tablets of stone supplies Pascal with the theological framework for distinguishing authentic from counterfeit religious claims.

Pascal's use of Jeremiah is characteristically polemical: the prophet's denunciation of false [[prophets]], his insistence on the priority of interior disposition over outward observance, and his announcement of a coming dispensation in which "they shall all know Me, from the least to the greatest" — all these serve Pascal's project of demonstrating that [[Christianity]] is the intended fulfillment of the [[Old Testament|Hebrew Scriptures]] rather than a departure from them.

## Jeremiah and the New Covenant

The central contribution of Jeremiah to Pascal's apologetic is the prophecy of a new covenant that will supersede the [[Mosaic law|Mosaic]] dispensation without abolishing it.

> [011-664] Jeremiah. They will wander, without king, without prince, without sacrifice, without altar, without ephod, without teraphim.

This prediction of a period in which the institutions of [[Judaism]] — kingship, [[priesthood]], [[sacrifice]] — would be suspended is, for Pascal, a prophecy of the destruction of the [[Second Temple]] and the subsequent condition of the [[Jewish]] people. That Jeremiah foresaw this suspension of the [[cult]] and linked it to the promise of a new covenant demonstrates that the [[law|legal]] and [[ritual]] dimensions of the old dispensation were always understood to be provisional.

The new covenant, as Pascal reads Jeremiah, is not merely a renovation of the old but a qualitative transformation: "I will put My law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts" (Jeremiah xxxi, 33). This interiorization of the divine command corresponds precisely to Pascal's own emphasis on the [[heart]] as the organ of religious knowledge. The God who writes on the heart bypasses the external apparatus of [[Temple]], [[priest]], and [[ceremony]] — not to abolish them but to fulfill their purpose from within.

## The Circumcision of the Heart

Pascal draws on Jeremiah's imagery of inward transformation to articulate his understanding of authentic religious response.

> [009-198] "Be ye circumcised in heart" — Jeremiah iv, 4; and again, "Take away the foreskins of your heart" — Jeremiah iv, 4.

This demand for a [[circumcision]] that is not of the flesh but of the heart becomes, in Pascal's hands, a criterion for distinguishing true religion from its counterfeits. The [[Jews]] who insist on physical circumcision while neglecting the inward reality it symbolizes are guilty of the very [[idolatry]] that Jeremiah denounced: they have substituted the sign for the thing signified, the external rite for the interior transformation it was meant to effect.

For Pascal, Jeremiah's insistence on the priority of the heart over outward observance is not a rejection of [[law]] or [[ritual]] as such but a recovery of their authentic meaning. The law written on stone is not wrong; it is incomplete. Only when the same law is written on the heart does it achieve its intended purpose — the [[reconciliation]] of the human will with the [[divine]].

Pascal sees in this prophetic doctrine the answer to the [[skepticism|skeptical]] objection that religion is merely a matter of [[custom]] and social convention. If religion were merely external — if it consisted only in observances that can be performed without inward conviction — the skeptic's charge would stand. But Jeremiah's prophecy anticipates a religion that transforms the person from within, and Pascal argues that [[Christianity]] alone realizes this transformation.

## True and False Prophecy

Jeremiah's confrontation with [[Hananiah]], the false prophet who contradicted his message of judgment, supplies Pascal with a case study in the discernment of authentic from counterfeit religious claims.

> [013-238] Jeremiah. Hananiah, the false prophet, died in seven months.

Pascal recounts how Jeremiah's prophecy of [[exile]] and Hananiah's prophecy of peace were tested by events. Hananiah died within the year, vindicating Jeremiah's claim to speak in the name of the [[Lord]]. The criterion of fulfillment — "when the word of the prophet shall come to pass, then shall the prophet be known" — is, for Pascal, the same criterion that validates [[Christian]] claims against competing religious assertions.

This principle extends to the broader question of how to distinguish [[miracle|miracles]] from deceptions. Pascal notes that "the miracles of the false prophets" (013-157) pose a genuine epistemological challenge: if false prophets can perform signs, how can the true be distinguished from the counterfeit? Jeremiah's answer — that the content of the prophet's message, judged against the corpus of established revelation, determines its authenticity — becomes Pascal's own principle for evaluating religious claims.

The test is not merely predictive accuracy but doctrinal coherence. A miracle that contradicts the known character of [[God]] or undermines the moral demands of the [[covenant]] is, by definition, a false miracle — regardless of its apparent supernatural character. This principle allows Pascal to affirm the [[miracle|miraculous]] dimension of Christianity while simultaneously providing criteria for rejecting counterfeit claims.

## Jeremiah and the Inner Disposition

Pascal's treatment of Jeremiah culminates in the recognition that the prophet anticipated the central [[Christianity|Christian]] insight: that [[religion]] is ultimately a matter of the interior disposition rather than external performance. The law written on the heart, the circumcision that is inward rather than outward, the knowledge of God that requires no human teacher — these Jeremianic themes converge on the same point that Pascal himself makes throughout the *Pensées*: that the [[heart]] has its reasons, and that those reasons, not the demonstrations of the [[reason|intellect]], are the proper ground of [[faith]].

If Jeremiah "gave them the law" (009-571), he also pointed beyond it to the dispensation in which the law would be internalized. In this respect, Pascal sees Jeremiah as the prophet who most clearly anticipates the [[gospel]] — not by predicting particular events in the life of [[Jesus Christ|Christ]] but by articulating the theological logic that makes those events intelligible as fulfillment rather than rupture.
