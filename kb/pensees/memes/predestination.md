[[Predestination|Predestination]], in Pascal's [[jansenism|Jansenist]] theology, is the doctrine that [[God|God]] has eternally decreed who will be saved and who will not — a teaching that Pascal inherits from [[saint-augustine|Augustine]] and that shapes his understanding of [[grace|grace]], [[human-nature|human freedom]], and the [[hiddenness-of-God|hiddenness of God]].

> [P01-401] Thus fell into the doctrine of predestination. It was upon this question that the split with the Jesuits occurred.

The doctrine of predestination is not, for Pascal, a speculative curiosity but the theological centre of the controversy that defined his community. [[Port-Royal|Port-Royal]] stood or fell on the Augustinian doctrine of grace, and predestination is the ultimate implication of that doctrine.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Predestination and the Hazard of Birth

> [P01-416] Of predestination — for the hazard of being born in a Christian country, which is not a matter of choice.

Pascal's treatment of predestination is characteristically existential rather than scholastic. He does not construct a systematic theology of election and reprobation. Instead, he draws attention to the "hazard of being born" — the brute fact that one's exposure to [[christianity|Christianity]] depends on accidents of birth. This hazard, which seems so arbitrary from a human perspective, is, from the divine perspective, the working out of an eternal decree.

The asymmetry is deliberate: Pascal does not claim to understand how divine predestination and human contingency relate. He insists only that both are real — that the hazard of birth is genuinely a hazard *and* that it serves a divine purpose that transcends human comprehension.

### Predestination and Power Over the Will

> [014-235] Power over the will of men; a predestination with respect to the entire life.

Predestination extends to "the entire life" — not merely to the moment of [[death|death]] but to every moment that leads to it. The scope of the divine decree is comprehensive, leaving nothing outside its reach. This is not, for Pascal, a cause for despair but for [[humility|humility]]: the recognition that one's [[salvation|salvation]] depends entirely on God.

### The Silence of the Pensées

Notably, predestination does not receive extended treatment in the body of the *Pensées*. The term appears primarily in the editorial introduction (P01). This relative silence is itself significant: Pascal's apologetic project addresses the [[skepticism|skeptic]] and the [[libertine|libertine]], not the theologian. Predestination is a doctrine for believers, not an argument for belief.

## Analysis

Pascal's approach to predestination illuminates his broader theological method: he treats mysteries as mysteries, refusing to resolve them into tidy systems.

The [[jesuits|Jesuits]], in Pascal's view, had resolved the mystery of predestination by effectively denying it — reducing divine sovereignty to divine foreknowledge of human merit. The [[calvinists|Calvinists]] had resolved it by denying human [[freedom|freedom]] — making God the author of [[sin|sin]]. Pascal, following Augustine, refuses both resolutions.

The result is a tension: God predestines, and human beings are free. This tension is not a failure of theological reasoning but an accurate reflection of the [[mystery|mystery]] itself. "The heart has its reasons which reason does not know" — the same applies to theology: there are truths that exceed the capacity of theological reason to systematise.
