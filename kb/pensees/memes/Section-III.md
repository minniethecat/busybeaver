[003-001] SECTION III

[003-002] OF THE NECESSITY OF THE WAGER

[003-003] 184

[003-004] A letter to incite to the search after [[god|God]].

[003-005] And then to make people seek Him among the [[philosophy|philosophers]],

[003-006] sceptics, and dogmatists, who disquiet him who inquires of them.

[003-007] 185

[003-008] The conduct of God, who disposes all things kindly, is to

[003-009] put religion into the [[spirit|mind]] by reason, and into the heart by

[003-010] grace. But to will to put it into the mind and heart by force

[003-011] and threats is not to put religion there, but terror, terorrem

[003-012] potius quam religionem.

[003-013] 186

[003-014] Nisi terrerentur et non docerentur, improba quasi dominatio

[003-015] videretur (Aug., Ep. 48 or 49), Contra Mendacium ad Consentium.

[003-016] 187

[003-017] Order.—Men despise religion; they hate it, and fear it is true.

[003-018] To remedy this, we must begin by showing that religion is not

[003-019] contrary to reason; that it is venerable, to inspire respect for

[003-020] it; then we must make it lovable, to make good men hope it is

[003-021] true; finally, we must prove it is true.

[003-022] Venerable, because it has perfect knowledge of man; lovable,

[003-023] because it promises the true good.

[003-024] 188

[003-025] In every dialogue and discourse, we must be able to say to

[003-026] those who take offence, "Of what do you complain?"

[003-027] 189

[003-028] To begin by pitying unbelievers; they are wretched enough

[003-029] by their condition. We ought only to revile them where it is

[003-030] beneficial; but this does them harm.

[003-031] 190

[003-032] To pity atheists who seek, for are they not unhappy enough?

[003-033] To inveigh against those who make a boast of it.

[003-034] 191

[003-035] And will this one scoff at the other? Who ought to scoff?

[003-036] And yet, the latter does not scoff at the other, but pities him.

[003-037] 192

[003-038] To reproach Miton[85] with not being troubled, since God will

[003-039] reproach him.

[003-040] 193

[003-041] Quid fiet hominibus qui minima contemnunt, majora non

[003-042] credunt?

[003-043] 194

[003-044] ... Let them at least learn what is the religion they attack,

[003-045] before attacking it. If this religion boasted of having a clear

[003-046] view of God, and of possessing it open and unveiled, it would be

[003-047] attacking it to say that we see nothing in the world which shows

[003-048] it with this clearness. But since, on the contrary, it says that

[003-049] men are in darkness and estranged from God, that He has

[003-050] hidden Himself from their knowledge, that this is in fact the

[003-051] name which He gives Himself in [[scripture|the Scriptures]], Deus absconditus;[86]

[003-052] and finally, if it endeavours equally to establish these two

[003-053] things: that God has set up in the Church visible signs to make

[003-054] Himself known to those who should seek Him sincerely, and

[003-055] that He has nevertheless so disguised them that He will only be

[003-056] perceived by those who seek Him with all their heart; what

[003-057] advantage can they obtain, when, in the negligence with which

[003-058] they make profession of being in search of the truth, they cry

[003-059] out that nothing reveals it to them; and since that darkness in

[003-060] which they are, and with which they upbraid the Church,

[003-061] establishes only one of the things which she affirms, without

[003-062] touching the other, and, very far from destroying, proves her

[003-063] doctrine?

[003-064] In order to attack it, they should have protested that they had

[003-065] made every effort to seek Him everywhere, and even in that

[003-066] which the Church proposes for their instruction, but without

[003-067] satisfaction. If they talked in this manner, they would in

[003-068] truth be attacking one of her pretensions. But I hope here to

[003-069] show that no reasonable person can speak thus, and I venture

[003-070] even to say that no one has ever done so. We know well

[003-071] enough how those who are of this mind behave. They believe

[003-072] they have made great efforts for their instruction, when they

[003-073] have spent a few hours in reading some book of Scripture, and

[003-074] have questioned some priest on the [[truth|truths]] of the faith. After

[003-075] that, they boast of having made vain search in books and among

[003-076] men. But, verily, I will tell them what I have often said, that

[003-077] this negligence is insufferable. We are not here concerned

[003-078] with the trifling interests of some stranger, that we should treat

[003-079] it in this fashion; the matter concerns ourselves and our all.

[003-080] The immortality of the soul is a matter which is of so great

[003-081] consequence to us, and which touches us so profoundly, that we

[003-082] must have lost all feeling to be indifferent as to [[knowledge|knowing]] what

[003-083] it is. All our actions and thoughts must take such different

[003-084] courses, according as there are or are not [[eternity|eternal]] joys to hope

[003-085] for, that it is impossible to take one step with sense and judgment,

[003-086] unless we regulate our course by our view of this point

[003-087] which ought to be our ultimate end.

[003-088] Thus our first interest and our first duty is to enlighten ourselves

[003-089] on this subject, whereon depends all our conduct. Therefore

[003-090] among those who do not believe, I make a vast difference

[003-091] between those who strive with all their [[force|power]] to inform themselves,

[003-092] and those who live without troubling or thinking about it.

[003-093] I can have only compassion for those who sincerely bewail

[003-094] their [[skepticism|doubt]], who regard it as the greatest of misfortunes, and

[003-095] who, sparing no effort to escape it, make of this inquiry their

[003-096] principal and most serious occupations.

[003-097] But as for those who pass their life without thinking of this

[003-098] ultimate end of life, and who, for this sole reason that they do

[003-099] not find within themselves the lights which convince them of it,

[003-100] neglect to seek them elsewhere, and to examine thoroughly

[003-101] whether this opinion is one of those which people receive with

[003-102] credulous simplicity, or one of those which, although obscure in

[003-103] themselves, have nevertheless a solid and immovable foundation,

[003-104] I look upon them in a manner quite different.

[003-105] This [[indifference|carelessness]] in a matter which concerns themselves,

[003-106] their eternity, their all, moves me more to anger than pity; it

[003-107] astonishes and shocks me; it is to me monstrous. I do not say

[003-108] this out of the pious zeal of a spiritual devotion. I expect, on

[003-109] the contrary, that we ought to have this feeling from principles

[003-110] of human interest and [[self-love]]; for this we need only see what

[003-111] the least enlightened persons see.

[003-112] We do not require great education of the mind to understand

[003-113] that here is no real and lasting satisfaction; that our pleasures

[003-114] are only vanity; that our evils are infinite; and, lastly, that

[003-115] death, which threatens us every moment, must infallibly place

[003-116] us within a few years under the dreadful necessity of being for

[003-117] ever either annihilated or unhappy.

[003-118] There is nothing more real than this, nothing more terrible.

[003-119] Be we as heroic as we like, that is the end which awaits the noblest

[003-120] life in the world. Let us reflect on this, and then say whether

[003-121] it is not beyond doubt that there is no good in this life but in the

[003-122] hope of another; that we are happy only in proportion as we

[003-123] draw near it; and that, as there are no more woes for those who

[003-124] have complete assurance of eternity, so there is no more happiness

[003-125] for those who have no insight into it.

[003-126] Surely then it is a great evil thus to be in doubt, but it is at

[003-127] least an indispensable duty to seek when we are in such doubt;

[003-128] and thus the doubter who does not seek is altogether completely

[003-129] unhappy and completely wrong. And if besides this he is easy

[003-130] and content, professes to be so, and indeed boasts of it; if it is this

[003-131] state itself which is the subject of his joy and vanity, I have no

[003-132] words to describe so silly a creature.

[003-133] How can people hold these opinions? What joy can we find

[003-134] in the expectation of nothing but hopeless [[wretchedness|misery]]? What

[003-135] reason for boasting that we are in impenetrable darkness?

[003-136] And how can it happen that the following argument occurs to

[003-137] a reasonable man?

[003-138] "I know not who put me into the world, nor what the world

[003-139] is, nor what I myself am. I am in terrible ignorance of everything.

[003-140] I know not what my body is, nor my senses, nor my

[003-141] soul, not even that part of me which thinks what I say, which

[003-142] reflects on all and on itself, and knows itself no more than the

[003-143] rest. I see those frightful spaces of the universe which surround

[003-144] me, and I find myself tied to one corner of this vast expanse,

[003-145] without knowing why I am put in this place rather than in

[003-146] another, nor why the short time which is given me to live is

[003-147] assigned to me at this point rather than at another of the whole

[003-148] eternity which was before me or which shall come after me. I

[003-149] see nothing but infinites on all sides, which surround me as an

[003-150] atom, and as a shadow which endures only for an instant and

[003-151] returns no more. All I know is that I must soon die, but what

[003-152] I know least is this very death which I cannot escape.

[003-153] "As I know not whence I come, so I know not whither I go.

[003-154] I know only that, in leaving this world, I fall for ever either

[003-155] into annihilation or into the hands of an angry God, without

[003-156] knowing to which of these two states I shall be for ever assigned.

[003-157] Such is my state, full of weakness and uncertainty. And from

[003-158] all this I conclude that I ought to spend all the days of my life

[003-159] without caring to inquire into what must happen to me. Perhaps

[003-160] I might find some solution to my doubts, but I will not

[003-161] take the trouble, nor take a step to seek it; and after treating

[003-162] with scorn those who are concerned with this care, I will go

[003-163] without foresight and without fear to try the great event, and

[003-164] let myself be led carelessly to death, uncertain of the eternity

[003-165] of my future state."

[003-166] Who would desire to have for a friend a man who talks in this

[003-167] fashion? Who would choose him out from others to tell him

[003-168] of his affairs? Who would have recourse to him in affliction?

[003-169] And indeed to what use in life could one put him?

[003-170] In truth, it is the glory of religion to have for enemies men so

[003-171] unreasonable: and their opposition to it is so little dangerous

[003-172] that it serves on the contrary to establish its truths. For the

[003-173] Christian faith goes mainly to establish these two facts, the

[003-174] [[corruption|corruption of nature]], and redemption by [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]. Now I

[003-175] contend that if these men do not serve to prove the truth of the

[003-176] redemption by the holiness of their behaviour, they at least

[003-177] serve admirably to show the corruption of nature by sentiments

[003-178] so unnatural.

[003-179] Nothing is so important to man as his own state, nothing is

[003-180] so formidable to him as eternity; and thus it is not natural that

[003-181] there should be men indifferent to the loss of their [[being|existence]], and

[003-182] to the perils of everlasting suffering. They are quite different

[003-183] with regard to all other things. They are afraid of mere trifles;

[003-184] they foresee them; they feel them. And this same man who

[003-185] spends so many days and nights in rage and despair for the loss

[003-186] of office, or for some imaginary insult to his honour, is the very

[003-187] one who knows without anxiety and without emotion that he

[003-188] will lose all by death. It is a monstrous thing to see in the

[003-189] same heart and at the same time this sensibility to trifles and

[003-190] this strange insensibility to the greatest objects. It is an

[003-191] incomprehensible enchantment, and a supernatural slumber,

[003-192] which indicates as its cause an all-powerful force.

[003-193] There must be a strange confusion in the [[human-nature|nature of man]], that

[003-194] he should boast of being in that state in which it seems incredible

[003-195] that a single individual should be. However, experience has

[003-196] shown me so great a number of such persons that the fact would

[003-197] be surprising, if we did not know that the greater part of those

[003-198] who trouble themselves about the matter are disingenuous, and

[003-199] not in fact what they say. They are people who have heard it

[003-200] said that it is the fashion to be thus daring. It is what they

[003-201] call shaking off the yoke, and they try to imitate this. But it

[003-202] would not be difficult to make them understand how greatly

[003-203] they deceive themselves in thus seeking esteem. This is not

[003-204] the way to gain it, even I say among those men of the world

[003-205] who take a healthy view of things, and who know that the only

[003-206] way to succeed in this life is to make ourselves appear honourable,

[003-207] faithful, judicious, and capable of useful service to a friend;

[003-208] because naturally men love only what may be useful to them.

[003-209] Now, what do we gain by hearing it said of a man that he has

[003-210] now thrown off the yoke, that he does not believe there is a God

[003-211] who watches our actions, that he considers himself the sole

[003-212] master of his conduct, and that he thinks he is accountable for

[003-213] it only to himself? Does he think that he has thus brought us

[003-214] to have henceforth complete confidence in him, and to look to

[003-215] him for consolation, advice, and help in every need of life? Do

[003-216] they profess to have delighted us by telling us that they hold

[003-217] our soul to be only a little wind and smoke, especially by telling

[003-218] us this in a haughty and self-satisfied tone of voice? Is this a

[003-219] thing to say gaily? Is it not, on the contrary, a thing to say

[003-220] sadly, as the saddest thing in the world?

[003-221] If they thought of it seriously, they would see that this is so

[003-222] bad a mistake, so contrary to good sense, so opposed to decency

[003-223] and so removed in every respect from that good breeding which

[003-224] they seek, that they would be more likely to correct than to

[003-225] pervert those who had an inclination to follow them. And

[003-226] indeed, make them give an account of their opinions, and of the

[003-227] reasons which they have for doubting religion, and they will

[003-228] say to you things so feeble and so petty, that they will persuade

[003-229] you of the contrary. The following is what a person one day

[003-230] said to such a one very appositely: "If you continue to talk

[003-231] in this manner, you will really make me religious." And he

[003-232] was right, for who would not have a horror of holding opinions in

[003-233] which he would have such contemptible persons as companions!

[003-234] Thus those who only feign these opinions would be very

[003-235] unhappy, if they restrained their natural feelings in order to

[003-236] make themselves the most conceited of men. If, at the bottom

[003-237] of their heart, they are troubled at not having more light, let

[003-238] them not disguise the fact; this avowal will not be shameful.

[003-239] The only shame is to have none. Nothing reveals more an

[003-240] extreme weakness of mind than not to know the misery of a

[003-241] godless man. Nothing is more indicative of a bad disposition of

[003-242] heart than not to desire the truth of eternal promises. Nothing

[003-243] is more dastardly than to act with bravado before God. Let

[003-244] them then leave these impieties to those who are sufficiently ill-bred

[003-245] to be really capable of them. Let them at least be honest

[003-246] men, if they cannot be Christians. Finally, let them recognise

[003-247] that there are two kinds of people one can call reasonable; those

[003-248] who serve God with all their heart because they know Him, and

[003-249] those who seek Him with all their heart because they do not

[003-250] know Him.

[003-251] But as for those who live without knowing Him and without

[003-252] seeking Him, they judge themselves so little worthy of their own

[003-253] care, that they are not worthy of the care of others; and it needs

[003-254] all the charity of the religion which they despise, not to despise

[003-255] them even to the point of leaving them to their folly. But because

[003-256] this religion obliges us always to regard them, so long as

[003-257] they are in this life, as capable of the grace which can enlighten

[003-258] them, and to believe that they may, in a little time, be more

[003-259] replenished with faith than we are, and that, on the other hand,

[003-260] we may fall into the blindness wherein they are, we must do

[003-261] for them what we would they should do for us if we were in

[003-262] their place, and call upon them to have pity upon themselves,

[003-263] and to take at least some steps in the endeavour to find light.

[003-264] Let them give to reading this some of the hours which they

[003-265] otherwise employ so uselessly; whatever aversion they may

[003-266] bring to the task, they will perhaps gain something, and at

[003-267] least will not lose much. But as for those who bring to the task

[003-268] perfect sincerity and a real desire to meet with truth, those I

[003-269] hope will be satisfied and convinced of the proofs of a religion

[003-270] so divine, which I have here collected, and in which I have

[003-271] followed somewhat after this order ...

[003-272] 195

[003-273] Before entering into the proofs of the [[christianity|Christian religion]], I find

[003-274] it necessary to point out the sinfulness of those men who live in

[003-275] indifference to the search for truth in a matter which is so

[003-276] important to them, and which touches them so nearly.

[003-277] Of all their errors, this doubtless is the one which most

[003-278] convicts them of foolishness and blindness, and in which it is

[003-279] easiest to confound them by the first glimmerings of common

[003-280] sense, and by natural feelings.

[003-281] For it is not to be doubted that the duration of this life is but

[003-282] a moment; that the state of death is eternal, whatever may be

[003-283] its nature; and that thus all our actions and thoughts must

[003-284] take such different directions according to the state of that

[003-285] eternity, that it is impossible to take one step with sense and

[003-286] judgment, unless we regulate our course by the truth of that

[003-287] point which ought to be our ultimate end.

[003-288] There is nothing clearer than this; and thus, according to the

[003-289] principles of reason, the conduct of men is wholly unreasonable,

[003-290] if they do not take another course.

[003-291] On this point, therefore, we condemn those who live without

[003-292] thought of the ultimate end of life, who let themselves be guided

[003-293] by their own inclinations and their own pleasures without

[003-294] reflection and without concern, and, as if they could annihilate

[003-295] eternity by turning away their thought from it, think only of

[003-296] making themselves happy for the moment.

[003-297] Yet this eternity exists, and death, which must open into it,

[003-298] and threatens them every hour, must in a little time infallibly

[003-299] put them under the dreadful necessity of being either annihilated

[003-300] or unhappy for ever, without knowing which of these eternities

[003-301] is for ever prepared for them.

[003-302] This is a doubt of terrible consequence. They are in peril of

[003-303] eternal woe; and thereupon, as if the matter were not worth

[003-304] the trouble, they neglect to inquire whether this is one of those

[003-305] opinions which people receive with too credulous a facility, or

[003-306] one of those which, obscure in themselves, have a very firm,

[003-307] though hidden, foundation. Thus they know not whether

[003-308] there be truth or falsity in the matter, nor whether there be

[003-309] strength or weakness in the proofs. They have them before

[003-310] their eyes; they refuse to look at them; and in that ignorance

[003-311] they choose all that is necessary to fall into this misfortune if

[003-312] it exists, to await death to make trial of it, yet to be very

[003-313] content in this state, to make profession of it, and indeed to

[003-314] boast of it. Can we think seriously on the importance of this

[003-315] subject without being horrified at conduct so extravagant?

[003-316] This resting in ignorance is a monstrous thing, and they who

[003-317] pass their life in it must be made to feel its extravagance and

[003-318] stupidity, by having it shown to them, so that they may be

[003-319] confounded by the sight of their folly. For this is how men

[003-320] reason, when they choose to live in such ignorance of what they

[003-321] are, and without seeking enlightenment. "I know not," they

[003-322] say ...

[003-337] 200

[003-338] A man in a dungeon, ignorant whether his sentence be

[003-339] pronounced, and having only one hour to learn it, but this

[003-340] hour enough, if he know that it is pronounced, to obtain its

[003-341] repeal, would act unnaturally in spending that hour, not in

[003-342] ascertaining his sentence, but in playing piquet. So it is against

[003-343] nature that man, etc. It is making heavy the hand of God.

[003-344] Thus not only the zeal of those who seek Him proves God,

[003-345] but also the blindness of those who seek Him not.

[003-569] According to reason, you can do neither the one thing nor the

[003-572] Do not then reprove for error those who have made a choice;

[003-573] for you know nothing about it. "No, but I blame them for

[003-574] having made, not this choice, but a choice; for again both he

[003-575] who chooses heads and he who chooses tails are equally at fault,

[003-576] they are both in the wrong. The true course is not to wager

[003-577] at all."

[003-578] Yes; but you must wager. It is not optional. You are

[003-579] embarked. Which will you choose then? Let us see. Since

[003-580] you must choose, let us see which interests you least. You have

[003-581] two things to lose, the true and the good; and two things to stake,

[003-582] your reason and your will, your knowledge and your happiness;

[003-583] and your nature has two things to shun, error and misery.

[003-584] Your reason is no more shocked in choosing one rather than the

[003-585] other, since you must of necessity choose. This is one point

[003-586] settled. But your happiness? Let us weigh the gain and the

[003-587] loss in wagering that God is. Let us estimate these two chances.

[003-588] If you gain, you gain all; if you lose, you lose nothing. Wager,

[003-589] then, without hesitation that He is.—"That is very fine. Yes,

[003-590] I must wager; but I may perhaps wager too much."—Let us

[003-591] see. Since there is an equal risk of gain and of loss, if you had

[003-592] only to gain two lives, instead of one, you might still wager.

[003-593] But if there were three lives to gain, you would have to play

[003-594] (since you are under the necessity of playing), and you would

[003-595] be imprudent, when you are forced to play, not to chance your

[003-596] life to gain three at a game where there is an equal risk of loss

[003-597] and gain. But there is an eternity of life and happiness. And

[003-598] this being so, if there were an infinity of chances, of which one

[003-599] only would be for you, you would still be right in wagering one

[003-600] to win two, and you would act stupidly, being obliged to play,

[003-601] by refusing to stake one life against three at a game in which out

[003-602] of an infinity of chances there is one for you, if there were an

[003-603] infinity of an infinitely happy life to gain. But there is here an

[003-604] infinity of an infinitely happy life to gain, a chance of gain

[003-605] against a finite number of chances of loss, and what you stake is

[003-606] finite. It is all divided; wherever the infinite is and there is not

[003-607] an infinity of chances of loss against that of gain, there is no

[003-608] time to hesitate, you must give all. And thus, when one is

[003-609] forced to play, he must renounce reason to preserve his life,

[003-610] rather than risk it for infinite gain, as likely to happen as the

[003-611] loss of nothingness.

[003-612] For it is no use to say it is uncertain if we will gain, and it is

[003-613] certain that we risk, and that the infinite distance between the

[003-614] certainty of what is staked and the uncertainty of what will be

[003-615] gained, equals the finite good which is certainly staked against

[003-616] the uncertain infinite. It is not so, as every player stakes a

[003-617] certainty to gain an uncertainty, and yet he stakes a finite

[003-618] certainty to gain a finite uncertainty, without transgressing

[003-619] against reason. There is not an infinite distance between the

[003-620] certainty staked and the uncertainty of the gain; that is untrue.

[003-621] In truth, there is an infinity between the certainty of gain

[003-622] and the certainty of loss. But the uncertainty of the gain is

[003-623] proportioned to the certainty of the stake according to the

[003-624] proportion of the chances of gain and loss. Hence it comes that,

[003-625] if there are as many risks on one side as on the other, the course

[003-626] is to play even; and then the certainty of the stake is equal to

[003-627] the uncertainty of the gain, so far is it from fact that there is an

[003-628] infinite distance between them. And so our proposition is of

[003-629] infinite force, when there is the finite to stake in a game where

[003-630] there are equal risks of gain and of loss, and the infinite to gain.

[003-631] This is demonstrable; and if men are capable of any truths, this

[003-632] is one.
