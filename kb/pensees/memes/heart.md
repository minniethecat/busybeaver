[[Heart|The heart]], in Pascal's [[epistemology|epistemology]], is the faculty of intuitive knowledge — the capacity by which [[human-nature|human]] beings grasp first principles, [[moral|moral]] truths, and ultimately the reality of [[God|God]].

> [P01-468] The heart, in Pascal's terminology, is no means an exaltation of the "heart" over the "head."

The famous Pascalian dictum — "the heart has its reasons which reason does not know" — is not, as it is sometimes taken to be, a defence of irrationalism.

Rather, it is a claim about the limits of discursive [[reason|reason]]: certain truths cannot be demonstrated by logical argumentation, not because they are irrational, but because they are more fundamental than the premises from which demonstration proceeds.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's treatment of the heart is distributed across the *Pensées*, appearing in contexts that range from the philosophy of [[mathematics|mathematics]] to the psychology of [[faith|religious faith]].

> [001-200] Make trial on our own heart.

The heart is not merely a cognitive faculty but the centre of the person — the seat of will, affection, and intuitive judgment. When Pascal speaks of the heart, he means the whole person in its capacity for direct, non-discursive apprehension of truth.

> [001-295] Consult the ear because the heart is wanting.

Here the absence of "heart" explains a failure of judgment. Those who judge by superficial criteria — by "the ear" rather than by genuine discernment — do so because the heart's capacity for intuitive recognition is missing or suppressed.

Pascal distinguishes between the order of the heart and the order of the mind, corresponding to his broader doctrine of the three orders: body, mind, and [[charity|charity]] (P01-477). The heart belongs to the third and highest order — an order that is invisible to those confined to the first two.

> [P01-469] A defence of unreason. The heart, in Pascal's terminology.

Pascal is careful to distinguish the heart from mere emotion or sentiment. The heart is rational in its own way — its "reasons" are genuine reasons, even if they cannot be fully articulated in propositional form.

## Key Passages

The most sustained treatment of the heart appears in Pascal's discussion of the foundations of knowledge:

> [001-197] Have studied well the heart of man so as to know all its springs.

Pascal presents himself as a student of the [[human-condition|human heart]] — not in the romantic sense of celebrating its passions but in the diagnostic sense of uncovering its hidden motives and deceptions.

> [001-184] Inclines the heart to love.

The movement of the heart toward love is, for Pascal, a form of cognition: the heart recognises what is worthy of love and inclines toward it. This is not irrational preference but intuitive evaluation.

> [001-162] Depart from the theatre with our heart so filled.

The susceptibility of the heart to shaping by narrative and spectacle is both a danger — the heart can be deformed by false images — and an opportunity — the heart can be educated by true ones. Pascal's own [[apologetics|apologetics]] exploits this plasticity by presenting [[christianity|Christianity]] in terms that appeal to the heart as well as the mind.

> [001-166] Them in the heart of another, in order that we may share.

The social dimension of the heart — its capacity to resonate with the hearts of others — is central to Pascal's understanding of how [[faith|faith]] is communicated. Arguments alone do not convert; the heart must be moved.

## Analysis

Pascal's doctrine of the heart is one of his most philosophically significant contributions, bridging [[epistemology|epistemology]], [[theology|theology]], and [[psychology|psychology]].

Its philosophical importance lies in its challenge to the rationalist assumption that all genuine knowledge must be demonstrable by discursive reason. Pascal anticipates themes that would later be developed by [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]]'s successors in the [[phenomenology|phenomenological]] tradition, who also argued that certain fundamental truths are "given" in experience rather than deduced from premises.

Theologically, the doctrine of the heart underwrites Pascal's account of [[faith-and-reason|faith and reason]]. Faith is not contrary to reason — it is not "unreasonable" — but it exceeds reason's demonstrative capacity. The heart grasps what the mind cannot prove.

This has strategic implications for [[apologetics|apologetics]]: the apologist must address not only the intellect but "the whole heart" — the entire person in its capacity for intuitive recognition and affective response.

> [P01-368] Because our heart tells us that it corresponds.

Pascal appeals to the heart as the ground of certainty in matters where demonstration fails.
