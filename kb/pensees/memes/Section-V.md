[005-001] SECTION V

[005-002] JUSTICE AND THE REASON OF EFFECTS

[005-003] 291

[005-004] In the letter On Injustice can come the ridiculousness of the

[005-005] law that the elder gets all. "My friend, you were born on this

[005-006] side of the mountain, it is therefore [[justice|just]] that your elder brother

[005-007] gets everything."

[005-008] "Why do you kill me?"

[005-009] 292

[005-010] He lives on the other side of the water.

[005-011] 293

[005-012] "Why do you kill me? What! do you not live on the other

[005-013] side of the water? If you lived on this side, my friend, I should

[005-014] be an assassin, and it would be unjust to slay you in this manner.

[005-015] But since you live on the other side, I am a hero, and it is just."

[005-016] 294

[005-017] On what shall man found the order of the world which he

[005-018] would govern?[109] Shall it be on the caprice of each individual?

[005-019] What confusion! Shall it be on justice? Man is ignorant of it.

[005-020] Certainly had he known it, he would not have established this

[005-021] maxim, the most general of all that obtain among men, that

[005-022] each should follow the custom of his own country. The glory

[005-023] of true equity would have brought all nations under subjection,

[005-024] and legislators would not have taken as their model the fancies

[005-025] and caprice of Persians and Germans instead of this unchanging

[005-026] justice. We should have seen it set up in all the States on earth

[005-027] and in all times; whereas we see neither justice nor injustice

[005-028] which does not change its nature with change in climate. Three

[005-029] degrees of latitude reverse all jurisprudence; a meridian decides

[005-030] the truth. Fundamental laws change after a few years of

[005-031] possession; right has its epochs; the entry of Saturn into the Lion

[005-032] marks to us the origin of such and such a crime. A strange

[005-033] justice that is bounded by a river! Truth on this side of the

[005-034] Pyrenees, error on the other side.

[005-035] Men admit that justice does not consist in these customs, but

[005-036] that it resides in natural laws, common to every country. They

[005-037] would certainly maintain it obstinately, if reckless chance which

[005-038] has distributed human laws had encountered even one which

[005-039] was universal; but the farce is that the caprice of men has so

[005-040] many vagaries that there is no such law.

[005-041] Theft, incest, infanticide, parricide, have all had a place among

[005-042] virtuous actions. Can anything be more ridiculous than that

[005-043] a man should have the right to kill me because he lives on the

[005-044] other side of the water, and because his ruler has a quarrel with

[005-045] mine, though I have none with him?

[005-046] Doubtless there are natural laws; but good reason once

[005-047] corrupted has corrupted all. Nihil amplius nostrum est;[110] quod

[005-048] nostrum dicimus, artis est. Ex senatus—consultis et plebiscitis

[005-049] crimina exercentur.[111] Ut olim vitiis, sic nunc legibus laboramus.[112]

[005-050] The result of this confusion is that one affirms the essence of

[005-051] justice to be the authority of the legislator; another, the interest

[005-052] of the sovereign;[113] another, present custom,[114] and this is the most

[005-053] sure. Nothing, according to reason alone, is just in itself; all

[005-054] changes with time. [[custom|Custom]] creates the whole of equity, for

[005-055] the simple reason that it is accepted. It is the mystical foundation

[005-056] of its authority;[115] whoever carries it back to first principles

[005-057] destroys it. Nothing is so faulty as those laws which correct

[005-058] faults. He who obeys them because they are just, obeys a

[005-059] justice which is imaginary, and not the essence of law; it is quite

[005-060] self-contained, it is law and nothing more. He who will examine

[005-061] its motive will find it so feeble and so trifling that if he be not

[005-062] accustomed to contemplate the wonders of human imagination,

[005-063] he will marvel that one century has gained for it so much pomp

[005-064] and reverence. The art of opposition and of revolution is to

[005-065] unsettle established customs, sounding them even to their

[005-066] source, to point out their want of authority and justice. We

[005-067] must, it is said, get back to the natural and fundamental laws of

[005-068] the State, which an unjust custom has abolished. It is a game

[005-069] certain to result in the loss of all; nothing will be just on the

[005-070] balance. Yet people readily lend their ear to such arguments.

[005-071] They shake off the yoke as soon as they recognise it; and the

[005-072] great profit by their ruin, and by that of these curious investigators

[005-073] of accepted customs. But from a contrary mistake

[005-074] men sometimes think they can justly do everything which is

[005-075] not without an example. That is why the wisest of legislators[116]

[005-076] said that it was necessary to deceive men for their own good;

[005-077] and another, a good politician, Cum veritatem qua liberetur

[005-078] ignoret, expedit quod fallatur.[117] We must not see the fact of

[005-079] usurpation; law was once introduced without reason, and has

[005-080] become reasonable. We must make it regarded as authoritative,

[005-081] [[eternity|eternal]], and conceal its origin, if we do not wish that it should

[005-082] soon come to an end.

[005-083] 295

[005-084] Mine, thine.—"This dog is mine," said those poor children;

[005-085] "that is my place in the sun." Here is the beginning and the

[005-086] image of the usurpation of all the earth.

[005-087] 296

[005-088] When the question for consideration is whether we ought to

[005-089] make war, and kill so many men—condemn so many Spaniards

[005-090] to death—only one man is judge, and he is an interested party.

[005-091] There should be a third, who is disinterested.

[005-092] 297

[005-093] Veri juris.[118]—We have it no more; if we had it, we should take

[005-094] conformity to the customs of a country as the rule of justice.

[005-095] It is here that, not finding justice, we have found force, etc.

[005-096] 298

[005-097] Justice, [[force|might]].—It is right that what is just should be obeyed;

[005-098] it is necessary that what is strongest should be obeyed. Justice

[005-099] without might is helpless; might without justice is tyrannical.

[005-100] Justice without might is gainsaid, because there are always

[005-101] offenders; might without justice is condemned. We must then

[005-102] combine justice and might, and for this end make what is just

[005-103] strong, or what is strong just.

[005-104] Justice is subject to dispute; might is easily recognised and

[005-105] is not disputed. So we cannot give might to justice, because

[005-106] might has gainsaid justice, and has declared that it is she herself

[005-107] who is just. And thus being unable to make what is just strong,

[005-108] we have made what is strong just.

[005-109] 299

[005-110] The only universal rules are the laws of the country in ordinary

[005-111] affairs, and of the majority in others. Whence comes this?

[005-112] From the might which is in them. Hence it comes that kings,

[005-113] who have power of a different kind, do not follow the majority

[005-114] of their ministers.

[005-115] No [[skepticism|doubt]] equality of goods is just; but, being unable to cause

[005-116] might to obey justice, men have made it just to obey might.

[005-117] Unable to strengthen justice, they have justified might; so that

[005-118] the just and the strong should unite, and there should be peace,

[005-119] which is the sovereign good.

[005-120] 300

[005-121] "When a strong man armed keepeth his goods, his goods are

[005-122] in peace."[119]

[005-123] 301

[005-124] Why do we follow the majority? It is because they have more

[005-125] reason? No, because they have more power.

[005-126] Why do we follow the ancient laws and opinions? Is it

[005-127] because they are more sound? No, but because they are unique,

[005-128] and remove from us the root of difference.

[005-129] 302

[005-130] ... It is the effect of might, not of custom. For those who

[005-131] are capable of originality are few; the greater number will only

[005-132] follow, and refuse glory to those inventors who seek it by their

[005-133] inventions. And if these are obstinate in their wish to obtain

[005-134] glory, and despise those who do not invent, the latter will call

[005-135] them ridiculous names, and would beat them with a stick. Let

[005-136] no one then boast of his subtlety, or let him keep his complacency

[005-137] to himself.

[005-138] 303

[005-139] Might is the sovereign of the world, and not opinion.—But

[005-140] opinion makes use of might.—It is might that makes opinion.

[005-141] Gentleness is beautiful in our opinion. Why? Because he who

[005-142] will dance on a rope will be alone,[120] and I will gather a stronger

[005-143] mob of people who will say that it is unbecoming.

[005-144] 304

[005-145] The cords which bind the respect of men to each other are in

[005-146] general cords of necessity; for there must be different degrees, all

[005-147] men wishing to rule, and not all being able to do so, but some

[005-148] being able.

[005-149] Let us then imagine we see society in the process of formation.

[005-150] Men will doubtless fight till the stronger party overcomes the

[005-151] weaker, and a dominant party is established. But when this is

[005-152] once determined, the masters, who do not desire the continuation

[005-153] of strife, then decree that the power which is in their hands shall

[005-154] be transmitted as they please. Some place it in [[elect|election]] by the

[005-155] people, others in hereditary succession, etc.

[005-156] And this is the point where imagination begins to play its

[005-157] part. Till now power makes fact; now power is sustained by

[005-158] imagination in a certain party, in France in the nobility, in

[005-159] Switzerland in the burgesses, etc.

[005-160] These cords which bind the respect of men to such and such

[005-161] an individual are therefore the cords of imagination.

[005-162] 305

[005-163] The Swiss are offended by being called gentlemen, and prove

[005-164] themselves true plebeians in order to be thought worthy of

[005-165] great office.

[005-166] 306

[005-167] As duchies, kingships, and magistracies are real and necessary,

[005-168] because might rules all, they exist everywhere and always. But

[005-169] since only caprice makes such and such a one a ruler, the principle

[005-170] is not constant, but subject to variation, etc.

[005-171] 307

[005-172] The chancellor is grave, and clothed with ornaments, for his

[005-173] position is unreal. Not so the king, he has power, and has

[005-174] nothing to do with the imagination. Judges, physicians, etc.

[005-175] appeal only to the imagination.

[005-176] 308

[005-177] The habit of seeing kings accompanied by guards, drums,

[005-178] officers, and all the paraphernalia which mechanically inspire

[005-179] respect and awe, makes their countenance, when sometimes seen

[005-180] alone without these accompaniments, impress respect and awe

[005-181] on their subjects; because we cannot separate in thought their

[005-182] persons from the surroundings with which we see them usually

[005-183] joined. And the world, which knows not that this effect is the

[005-184] result of habit, believes that it arises by a natural force, whence

[005-185] come these words, "The character of Divinity is stamped on his

[005-186] countenance," etc.

[005-223] 315

[005-224] The reason of effects.—It is wonderful that men would not have

[005-225] me honour a man clothed in brocade, and followed by seven or

[005-226] eight lackeys! Why! He will have me thrashed, if I do not

[005-227] salute him. This custom is a force. It is the same with a horse

[005-228] in fine trappings in comparison with another! [[montaigne|Montaigne]][122] is a

[005-229] fool not to see what difference there is, to wonder at our finding

[005-230] any, and to ask the reason. "Indeed," says he, "how comes

[005-231] it," etc....

[005-232] 316

[005-233] Sound opinions of the people.—To be spruce is not altogether

[005-234] foolish, for it proves that a great number of people work for one.

[005-235] It shows by one's hair, that one has a valet, a perfumer, etc.,

[005-236] by one's band, thread, lace, ... etc. Now it is not merely

[005-237] superficial nor merely outward show to have many arms at

[005-238] command. The more arms one has, the more powerful one is.

[005-239] To be spruce is to show one's power.

[005-240] 317

[005-241] Deference means, "Put yourself to inconvenience." This is

[005-242] apparently silly, but is quite right. For it is to say, "I would

[005-243] indeed put myself to inconvenience if you required it, since

[005-244] indeed I do so when it is of no service to you." Deference

[005-245] further serves to distinguish the great. Now if deference was

[005-246] displayed by sitting in an arm-chair, we should show deference

[005-247] to everybody, and so no distinction would be made; but, being

[005-248] put to inconvenience, we distinguish very well.

[005-249] 318

[005-250] He has four lackeys.

[005-251] 319

[005-252] How rightly do we distinguish men by external appearances

[005-253] rather than by internal qualities! Which of us two shall have

[005-254] precedence? Who will give place to the other? The least clever.

[005-255] But I am as clever as he. We should have to fight over this.

[005-256] He has four lackeys, and I have only one. This can be seen;

[005-257] we have only to count. It falls to me to yield, and I am a fool

[005-258] if I contest the matter. By this means we are at peace, which

[005-259] is the greatest of boons.

[005-260] 320

[005-261] The most unreasonable things in the world become most

[005-262] reasonable, because of the unruliness of men. What is less

[005-263] reasonable than to choose the eldest son of a queen to rule a

[005-264] State? We do not choose as captain of a ship the passenger who

[005-265] is of the best family.

[005-266] This law would be absurd and unjust; but because men are so

[005-267] themselves, and always will be so, it becomes reasonable and

[005-268] just. For whom will men choose, as the most virtuous and able?

[005-269] We at once come to blows, as each claims to be the most virtuous

[005-270] and able. Let us then attach this quality to something indisputable.

[005-271] This is the king's eldest son. That is clear, and there

[005-272] is no dispute. [[reason|Reason]] can do no better, for civil war is the

[005-273] greatest of evils.

[005-274] 321

[005-275] Children are astonished to see their comrades respected.

[005-276] 322

[005-277] To be of noble birth is a great advantage. In eighteen years

[005-278] it places a man within the select circle, known and respected,

[005-279] as another would have merited in fifty years. It is a gain of

[005-280] thirty years without trouble.

[005-281] 323

[005-282] What is the Ego?

[005-283] Suppose a man puts himself at a window to see those who

[005-284] pass by. If I pass by, can I say that he placed himself there to

[005-285] see me? No; for he does not think of me in particular. But

[005-286] does he who loves someone on account of beauty really love

[005-287] that person? No; for the small-pox, which will kill beauty without

[005-288] killing the person, will cause him to love her no more.

[005-289] And if one loves me for my judgment, memory, he does not

[005-290] love me, for I can lose these qualities without losing myself.

[005-291] Where, then, is this Ego, if it be neither in the body nor in the

[005-292] soul? And how love the body or the soul, except for these

[005-293] qualities which do not constitute me, since they are perishable?

[005-294] For it is impossible and would be unjust to love the soul of a

[005-295] person in the abstract, and whatever qualities might be therein.

[005-296] We never, then, love a person, but only qualities.

[005-297] Let us, then, jeer no more at those who are honoured on

[005-298] account of rank and office; for we love a person only on

[005-299] account of borrowed qualities.

[005-300] 324

[005-301] The people have very sound opinions, for example:

[005-302] 1. In having preferred diversion and hunting to poetry. The

[005-303] half-learned laugh at it, and glory in being above the folly of the

[005-304] world; but the people are right for a reason which these do not

[005-305] fathom.

[005-306] 2. In having distinguished men by external marks, as birth or

[005-307] wealth. The world again exults in showing how unreasonable

[005-308] this is; but it is very reasonable. Savages laugh at an infant

[005-309] king.[123]

[005-310] 3. In being offended at a blow, on in desiring glory so much.

[005-311] But it is very desirable on account of the other essential goods

[005-312] which are joined to it; and a man who has received a blow,

[005-313] without resenting it, is overwhelmed with taunts and indignities.

[005-314] 4. In working for the uncertain; in sailing on the sea; in walking

[005-315] over a plank.

[005-316] 325

[005-317] Montaigne is wrong. Custom should be followed only because

[005-318] it is custom, and not because it is reasonable or just. But

[005-319] people follow it for this sole reason, that they think it just.

[005-320] Otherwise they would follow it no longer, although it were the

[005-321] custom; for they will only submit to reason or justice. Custom

[005-322] without this would pass for tyranny; but the sovereignty of

[005-323] reason and justice is no more tyrannical than that of desire.

[005-324] They are principles natural to man.

[005-325] It would therefore be right to obey laws and customs, because

[005-326] they are laws; but we should know that there is neither truth nor

[005-327] justice to introduce into them, that we know nothing of these,

[005-328] and so must follow what is accepted. By this means we would

[005-329] never depart from them. But people cannot accept this

[005-330] doctrine; and, as they believe that truth can be found, and that

[005-331] it exists in law and custom, they believe them, and take their

[005-332] antiquity as a proof of their truth, and not simply of their

[005-333] authority apart from truth. Thus they obey laws, but they are

[005-334] liable to revolt when these are proved to be valueless; and this

[005-335] can be shown of all, looked at from a certain aspect.

[005-336] 326

[005-337] Injustice.—It is dangerous to tell the people that the laws are

[005-338] unjust; for they obey them only because they think them just.

[005-339] Therefore it is necessary to tell them at the same time that they

[005-340] must obey them because they are laws, just as they must obey

[005-341] superiors, not because they are just, but because they are

[005-342] superiors. In this way all sedition is prevented, if this can be

[005-343] made intelligible, and it be understood what is the proper

[005-344] definition of justice.

[005-345] 327

[005-346] The world is a good judge of things, for it is in natural

[005-347] ignorance, which is man's true state.[124] The sciences have two

[005-348] extremes which meet. The first is the pure natural ignorance in

[005-349] which all men find themselves at birth. The other extreme is

[005-350] that reached by great intellects, who, having run through

[005-351] all that men can know, find they know nothing, and come

[005-352] back again to that same ignorance from which they set out;

[005-353] but this is a learned ignorance which is conscious of itself.

[005-354] Those between the two, who have departed from natural

[005-355] ignorance and not been able to reach the other, have some

[005-356] smattering of this vain knowledge, and pretend to be wise.

[005-357] These trouble the world, and are bad judges of everything.

[005-358] The people and the wise constitute the world; these despise it,

[005-359] and are despised. They judge badly of everything, and the

[005-360] world judges rightly of them.

[005-361] 328

[005-362] The reason of effects.—Continual alternation of pro and con.

[005-363] We have then shown that man is foolish, by the estimation

[005-364] he makes of things which are not essential; and all these opinions

[005-365] are destroyed. We have next shown that all these opinions are

[005-366] very sound, and that thus, since all these vanities are well

[005-367] founded, the people are not so foolish as is said. And so we

[005-368] have destroyed the opinion which destroyed that of the people.

[005-369] But we must now destroy this last proposition, and show

[005-370] that it remains always true that the people are foolish, though

[005-371] their opinions are sound; because they do not perceive the

[005-372] truth where it is, and, as they place it where it is not, their

[005-373] opinions are always very false and very unsound.

[005-374] 329

[005-375] The reason of effects.—The weakness of man is the reason why

[005-376] so many things are considered fine, as to be good at playing the

[005-377] lute. It is only an evil because of our weakness.

[005-378] 330

[005-379] The power of kings is founded on the reason and on the folly

[005-380] of the people, and specially on their folly. The greatest and

[005-381] most important thing in the world has weakness for its foundation,

[005-382] and this foundation is wonderfully sure; for there is nothing

[005-383] more sure than this, that the people will be weak. What is based

[005-384] on sound reason is very ill founded, as the estimate of wisdom.

[005-385] 331

[005-386] We can only think of [[plato|Plato]] and Aristotle in grand academic

[005-387] robes. They were honest men, like others, laughing with their

[005-388] friends, and when they diverted themselves with writing their

[005-389] Laws and the Politics, they did it as an [[diversion|amusement]]. That

[005-390] part of their life was the least philosophic and the least serious;

[005-391] the most philosophic was to live simply and quietly. If they

[005-392] wrote on politics, it was as if laying down rules for a lunatic

[005-393] asylum; and if they presented the appearance of speaking of a

[005-394] great matter, it was because they knew that the madmen, to

[005-395] whom they spoke, thought they were kings and emperors. They

[005-396] entered into their principles in order to make their madness as

[005-397] little harmful as possible.

[005-398] 332

[005-399] [[tyranny|Tyranny]] consists in the desire of universal power beyond

[005-400] its scope.

[005-401] There are different assemblies of the strong, the fair, the

[005-402] sensible, the pious, in which each man rules at home, not elsewhere.

[005-403] And sometimes they meet, and the strong and the fair

[005-404] foolishly fight as to who shall be master, for their mastery is of

[005-405] different kinds. They do not understand one another, and their

[005-406] fault is the desire to rule everywhere. Nothing can effect this,

[005-407] not even might, which is of no use in the kingdom of the wise,

[005-408] and is only mistress of external actions.

[005-409] Tyranny— ... So these expressions are false and tyrannical:

[005-410] "I am fair, therefore I must be feared. I am strong, therefore

[005-411] I must be loved. I am ..."

[005-412] Tyranny is the wish to have in one way what can only be had

[005-413] in another. We render different duties to different merits; the

[005-414] duty of love to the pleasant; the duty of fear to the strong; the

[005-415] duty of [[faith|belief]] to the learned.

[005-416] We must render these duties; it is unjust to refuse them, and

[005-417] unjust to ask others. And so it is false and tyrannical to say,

[005-418] "He is not strong, therefore I will not esteem him; he is not

[005-419] able, therefore I will not fear him."

[005-420] 333

[005-421] Have you never seen people who, in order to complain of the

[005-422] little fuss you make about them, parade before you the example

[005-423] of great men who esteem them? In answer I reply to them,

[005-424] "Show me the merit whereby you have charmed these persons,

[005-425] and I also will esteem you."

[005-426] 334

[005-427] The reason of effects.—Lust and force are the source of all our

[005-428] actions; lust causes voluntary actions, force involuntary ones.

[005-429] 335

[005-430] The reason of effects.—It is then true to say that all the world

[005-431] is under a delusion; for, although the opinions of the people are

[005-432] sound, they are not so as conceived by them, since they think

[005-433] the truth to be where it is not. Truth is indeed in their opinions,

[005-434] but not at the point where they imagine it. [Thus] it is true

[005-435] that we must honour noblemen, but not because noble birth is

[005-438] The reason of effects.—We must keep our thought secret, and

[005-439] judge everything by it, while talking like the people.

[005-440] 337

[005-441] The reason of effects.—Degrees. The people honour persons

[005-442] of high birth. The semi-learned despise them, saying that

[005-443] birth is not a personal, but a chance superiority. The learned

[005-444] honour them, not for popular reasons, but for secret reasons.

[005-445] Devout persons, who have more zeal than knowledge, despise

[005-446] them, in spite of that consideration which makes them honoured

[005-447] by the learned, because they judge them by a new light which

[005-448] piety gives them. But perfect Christians honour them by

[005-449] another and higher light. So arise a succession of opinions for

[005-450] and against, according to the light one has.

[005-452] True Christians nevertheless comply with folly, not because

[005-453] they respect folly, but the command of God, who for the punishment

[005-454] of men has made them subject to these follies. Omnis

[005-455] creatura subjecta est vanitati.[125] Liberabitur.[126] Thus Saint Thomas[127]

[005-456] explains the passage in Saint James on giving place to the rich,

[005-457] that if they do it not in the sight of God, they depart from the

[005-458] command of religion.
