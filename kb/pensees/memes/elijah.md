[[Elijah]] the prophet occupies a distinctive role in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées*: he is the Old Testament figure whose contest with the prophets of Baal provides Pascal with his clearest paradigm for distinguishing true from false [[miracle|miracles]]. In the dispute between [[truth]] and [[error]], miracles are decisive — not because every miracle worker speaks truly, but because genuine miracles always accompany genuine doctrine.

Elijah's significance in Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetic]] argument is concentrated almost entirely in [[Section-XIII|Section XIII]] of the *Pensées*, the section devoted to miracles. There, Pascal deploys Elijah as part of a sustained case that miracles authenticate the [[Christian]] revelation and that the alleged miracles of rival traditions fail upon examination.

## Elijah and the Authentication of Doctrine

Pascal's most explicit use of Elijah appears in his discussion of the relationship between miracles and truth. The story from 1 Kings 17 — in which Elijah restores the widow's son to life — provides the pattern.

> [013-241] 1 Kings xvii. The widow to Elijah, who had restored her son, "By this I know that thy words are true."

The widow's reasoning is, for Pascal, normative: the [[miracle]] authenticates the [[prophet]]'s words. She does not believe Elijah because he is eloquent or persuasive; she believes him because he has demonstrated a power that can only come from [[God]]. This is the logic that Pascal extends to the miracles of [[Jesus Christ]] and the [[apostles]]: the works they perform testify to the truth of what they say.

The distinction between true and false miracles is crucial to Pascal's argument — and Elijah provides his clearest illustration of this distinction at work. The same prophet who raised the dead also confronted the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel.

> [013-243] 1 Kings xviii. Elijah with the prophets of Baal.

The Carmel contest is the definitive demonstration that not all claimed miracles are equal. The prophets of Baal claimed supernatural power; they failed to produce it. Elijah called upon [[Yahweh]] and fire descended. The difference lay not in the form of the claim but in the [[doctrine]] it served: miracles of truth authenticate true doctrine; miracles of [[error]] do not occur. Pascal summarizes this principle with characteristic concision:

> [013-244] In the dispute concerning the true God and the truth of religion, there has never happened any miracle on the side of error, and not of truth.

This is a bold claim, and Pascal knows it. The history of [[religion]] is full of alleged wonders on every side. But Pascal's point is not that all reported miracles are genuine; it is that, when miracles are properly examined — as Elijah examined the claims of the Baal prophets — the false ones collapse. The Carmel test is, in effect, Pascal's model for the rational examination of religious claims: look at the miracles and see which doctrine they authenticate.

## Elijah in the Pattern of Opposition

Pascal situates Elijah within a comprehensive pattern of opposition that runs through the whole of [[sacred history]]. From [[Abel]] and [[Cain]] to the final confrontation between [[Christ]] and [[Antichrist]], the history of [[salvation]] is structured by the conflict between truth and falsehood, [[faith]] and unbelief.

> [013-248] Opposition. — Abel, Cain; Moses, the Magicians; Elijah, the false prophets: Jeremiah, Hananiah; Micaiah, the false prophets; Jesus Christ, the Pharisees; St. Paul, Bar-jesus; the Apostles, the Exorcists; Christians, unbelievers; Catholics, heretics; Elijah, Enoch, Antichrist.

Elijah appears twice in this chain of oppositions — first as the opponent of the false prophets of his own day, and then, paired with [[Enoch]], as one of the two witnesses who will oppose [[Antichrist]] at the end of time. This double appearance is not accidental. Elijah is, for Pascal, a type — a figure whose historical reality prefigures a larger eschatological truth. His confrontation with the Baal prophets on Mount Carmel anticipates the final confrontation between the [[Church]] and the forces of deception.

The second pairing — "Elijah, Enoch, Antichrist" — draws on the apocalyptic tradition in which Elijah and Enoch, who never died, return at the end of days to bear witness against the final enemy of [[God]]. Pascal does not elaborate this tradition in the *Pensées*; he assumes his readers know it. His purpose is simply to locate Elijah within the structure of sacred history, showing that the prophet's significance extends beyond his own historical moment to encompass the entire arc of [[redemption]].

## Elijah as Type of Christ

Pascal's chain of oppositions reveals a deeper theological logic. Each pair in the sequence follows the same pattern: a true servant of God opposed by a false claimant. [[Moses]] against the Egyptian magicians; Elijah against the prophets of Baal; [[Jesus Christ]] against the [[Pharisees]]; the [[apostles]] against the exorcists; [[Christians]] against [[unbelievers]]; [[Catholics]] against [[heretics]].

Elijah occupies a pivotal position in this sequence. He is the prophet whose miracles most clearly prefigure those of Christ — the raising of the dead, the multiplication of provision, the confrontation with false religious authority. Yet for all the grandeur of Elijah's miracles, he is a servant, not the master. What Elijah does in type, Christ does in reality; what Elijah demonstrates through confrontation on a single mountain, Christ accomplishes through the [[incarnation]], [[crucifixion]], and [[resurrection]].

This typological reading is essential to Pascal's argument about [[miracles]]. If the miracles of Christ and the apostles could be dismissed as mere wonders — the kind of thing any sufficiently impressive religious figure might produce — then they would not authenticate anything. But when understood as the culmination of a pattern established by Elijah and the other [[prophets]], the miracles of Christ carry a weight that no rival miracle claim can match. They are not isolated events; they are the fulfillment of a divine logic that runs through the whole of sacred history.
