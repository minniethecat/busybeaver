[[Anthropology|Anthropology]], in Pascal's sense, is not the modern social science but the philosophical study of what the human being is — an inquiry that, in his hands, becomes inseparable from [[theology|theology]].

Pascal's anthropological method is distinctive: he does not begin with definitions or abstract categories but with observation of the concrete [[human-condition|human condition]] — the way human beings actually live, think, suffer, and aspire.

## In Pascal's Pensees

The foundation of Pascal's anthropology is the Socratic imperative to [[self-knowledge|know oneself]]:

> [002-021] Of the knowledge of self.

But [[self-knowledge|self-knowledge]], for Pascal, is not achieved by introspection alone. The human being cannot know itself by looking only at itself, any more than a text can interpret itself without reference to its author. The [[human-condition|human]] being must be understood in relation to its origin and its end — in relation, ultimately, to [[God|God]].

Pascal's anthropology therefore takes the form of a double movement: the human being is examined first in its observable condition (the data of experience: [[boredom|boredom]], [[diversion|diversion]], [[death|death]], [[misery|misery]], aspiration), and then in light of the theological explanation that alone makes sense of that data (creation, Fall, [[redemption|redemption]], [[grace|grace]]).

## The Anthropological Argument

The apologetic force of Pascal's anthropology lies in its refusal of reduction. Neither the optimist who sees only [[greatness|greatness]] nor the pessimist who sees only [[wretchedness|wretchedness]] can account for the full range of human experience.

> [002-323] One finds pleasure in the knowledge of nature, another in the knowledge of men.

Pascal draws on his own knowledge of men — acquired not in the study but in the salons, the gambling tables, and the world of affairs — to ground his anthropology in observation rather than speculation. The doctrine of the Fall is not, for him, a theological axiom imposed on the facts but the only hypothesis adequate to them.
