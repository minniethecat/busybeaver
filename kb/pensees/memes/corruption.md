[[Corruption|Corruption]], in Pascal's theological anthropology, is the universal condition of fallen humanity: a corruption of [[reason|reason]], [[will|will]], and the [[passions|passions]] that renders man incapable of knowing either his true good or his true misery without the aid of [[revelation|revelation]].

> (002-717) And yet the corruption of man is such that he finds it pleasant...

Pascal's doctrine of corruption is not a mere moral observation but a structural claim about [[human-nature|human nature]] after the Fall. It is the negative pole of his dialectical anthropology: [[greatness-and-misery|greatness]] testifies to man's original dignity; corruption explains why that dignity is now inaccessible to unaided reason.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Corruption as Theological Fact

For Pascal, corruption is not a hypothesis but a revealed datum. [[original-sin|Original sin]] is the cause; corruption is the universal effect. The doctrine is "offensive to reason" — and precisely this offensiveness is, for Pascal, a mark of its truth: a doctrine so contrary to human self-esteem could not have been invented by human beings.

> (003-174) ...corruption of nature, and redemption by...

> (003-177) ...serve admirably to show the corruption of nature by sentiments...

The word "admirably" is characteristically Pascalian: what ought to be a scandal becomes, when viewed through the lens of [[faith-and-reason|faith]], a proof. The very universality of human corruption — the fact that all men show the same patterns of [[self-love|self-love]], [[vanity|vanity]], and flight from [[truth|truth]] — becomes evidence for the Christian account of the Fall.

### The Corruption of Reason

Pascal extends the doctrine of corruption beyond [[morality|morality]] into [[epistemology|epistemology]]. It is not only the will that is corrupted but the faculty of [[knowledge|knowledge]] itself.

> (007-443) The corruption of reason is shown by the existence of so many...

Reason, corrupted, cannot arrive at certainty about the most important matters — the existence of [[god|God]], the nature of the [[soul|soul]], the [[sovereign-good|sovereign good]]. This epistemological pessimism is the foundation of Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetics]]: because reason is corrupted, man needs a source of knowledge that is not corrupted — namely, revelation.

> (007-373) ...of corruption and sin, he is fallen from...

> (007-395) ...corruption; so that they easily avoided...

### Ignorance of Corruption

The deepest problem of corruption is that the corrupted do not know they are corrupted. Self-knowledge is itself one of the faculties that corruption has disabled.

> (007-637) ...and do not know their own corruption...

This ignorance is not innocent. It is a culpable [[blindness|blindness]], a willed flight from self-confrontation that Pascal analyses under the heading of [[diversion|diversion]]. Man flees from himself because to know himself would be to know his corruption — and this knowledge, without the simultaneous knowledge of [[redemption|redemption]], would lead to [[despair|despair]].

### Corruption and Redemption: The Two Proofs

Pascal's apologetic strategy is to hold corruption and redemption in a single movement. Neither can be understood without the other.

> (008-095) ...to teach men both their corruption and their redemption...

> (008-136) So the two proofs of corruption and redemption are drawn...

> (008-036) ...corruption of sin in order to reconcile...

The two doctrines are, for Pascal, "proofs" — not in the demonstrative sense of [[mathematics|mathematics]] but in the cumulative, converging sense proper to [[apologetics|apologetics]]. Corruption without redemption is [[wretchedness|wretchedness]] without [[hope|hope]]; redemption without corruption is a solution without a problem.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of corruption operates at three levels.

First, it is an **anthropological claim**: human nature as we observe it is not what it was created to be. The universal testimony of [[history|history]], [[psychology|psychology]], and introspection confirms that something is broken. Pascal's inventory of human vices — [[self-love|self-love]], [[vanity|vanity]], [[boredom|boredom]], the flight from [[death|death]] — is not a moral sermon but a diagnostic. Corruption is the diagnosis that makes sense of the symptoms.

Second, it is an **epistemological claim**: [[reason|reason]] is one of the corrupted faculties. This is why philosophical argument alone cannot lead to faith. The corruption of reason means that the evidence for [[christianity|Christianity]] is always ambiguous — "clear enough to enlighten the elect and obscure enough to humble them" (008-161). God has left enough light for those who seek and enough darkness for those who do not.

Third, it is an **apologetic claim**: corruption is the premise without which [[redemption|redemption]] makes no sense. The [[wager|Wager]] presupposes corruption — if human nature were whole, it could judge its own good without risk. The [[cross|cross]] presupposes corruption — if human nature were not corrupted, the death of God would be unintelligible. The entire Pascalian system turns on the pair: corruption and [[grace|grace]].
