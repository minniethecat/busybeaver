[[Automaton|Automaton]] is Pascal's term for the [[body|body]] understood as a machine that operates by [[custom|custom]] rather than [[reason|reason]]. The concept is central to his account of how belief is actually formed — as opposed to how it ideally ought to be formed — and it plays a crucial role in the practical argument of the [[pascals-wager|Wager]].

> [004-105] It bends the automaton, which persuades the mind without its thinking about the matter.

"Bending the automaton" (*plier la machine*) is Pascal's term for the non-rational dimension of persuasion. [[reason|Reason]] can demonstrate that a belief is warranted; but demonstration alone rarely produces conviction. The [[body|body]] — the automaton — must be trained by [[custom|custom]] to incline toward the belief that [[reason|reason]] endorses.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Two Entrances of Belief

Pascal's key insight is that belief enters the [[human-nature|human]] person through two channels: the [[mind|mind]] and the [[body|body]]. The intellectual tradition from [[plato|Plato]] through [[descartes|Descartes]] had focused almost exclusively on the first channel — the rational demonstration that a proposition is true. Pascal, drawing on his [[jansenism|Jansenist]] anthropology, insists that the second channel is equally important and often more effective.

> [004-120] ...automaton is inclined to believe the contrary. Both our parts must be made to believe: the mind by reasons, which it is enough to have seen once in a lifetime, and the automaton by custom...

The "two parts" — mind and automaton — must both be addressed. The mind needs reasons; seeing them once may be sufficient, because [[reason|reason]] operates by insight and once a demonstration is understood, it remains understood. But the automaton operates differently — by [[custom|custom]], by repetition, by habituation. It is not enough to see the reasons once; the automaton must be trained, day by day, to incline toward what the [[mind|mind]] has accepted.

> [004-122] ...and the automaton by custom, and by not permitting it to incline to the contrary.

The negative dimension is as important as the positive: the automaton must not only be trained toward true belief but restrained from false belief. "Not permitting it to incline to the contrary" means structuring one's life — one's practices, one's company, one's [[diversion|diversions]] — so that the automaton is not pulled in directions contrary to what [[reason|reason]] has accepted.

### The Wager's Practical Turn

The automaton concept is what makes the [[pascals-wager|Wager]] a practical argument rather than merely a theoretical one. After conceding that [[reason|reason]] cannot decide the question of [[God|God's]] existence, and after showing that the rational choice is to wager on [[God|God's]] existence, Pascal anticipates the objection: "I am so made that I cannot believe."

His response is not "try harder to be convinced" but "bend the automaton":

> [004-105] ...proofs. It bends the automaton, which persuades the mind without its thinking about the matter.

Act as if you believe, and belief will follow. This is not hypocrisy but psychology — an application of Pascal's understanding that the [[body|body]] and its [[custom|customs]] are not merely the instruments of the [[mind|mind]] but its collaborators in the formation of belief. The automaton can lead where the mind has already assented in principle but cannot yet commit in practice.

## Key Passages

The automaton concept appears in a tightly clustered set of fragments surrounding the Wager argument. This clustering is significant: the automaton is not a general anthropological concept for Pascal but a specific piece of his theory of belief-formation, deployed at the point where rational argument reaches its limit and practical habituation must take over.

## Analysis

The automaton concept reveals the sophistication of Pascal's psychology. He does not share the [[rationalism|rationalist's]] confidence that demonstration is sufficient for conviction, nor the [[fideism|fideist's]] dismissal of [[reason|reason]] as irrelevant. [[reason|Reason]] establishes what ought to be believed; the automaton, trained by [[custom|custom]], makes that belief psychologically possible. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.

This analysis also illuminates Pascal's account of [[diversion|diversion]]. If the automaton can be trained toward [[faith-and-reason|faith]] by [[custom|custom]], it can equally be trained away from [[faith-and-reason|faith]] by the [[custom|customs]] of the world — by the habits of [[diversion|diversion]], by the company of the indifferent, by the practices of a society organised around avoiding the thought of [[death|death]] and [[God|God]]. The automaton is always being trained, whether we attend to the training or not. The only question is toward what.
