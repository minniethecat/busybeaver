[[Human Nature|Human nature]], as Pascal understands it, is defined by a fundamental paradox: the human being is at once great and wretched, capable of infinite aspiration yet mired in [[sin|sin]] and self-deception.

This duality is not, for Pascal, a philosophical puzzle to be resolved by choosing one side or the other — it is the central datum that any adequate account of the [[human-condition|human condition]] must explain.

## In Pascal's Pensees

Pascal's starting point is the claim that human nature, as presently experienced, is not in its original state. The evidence of corruption is everywhere, yet so is the evidence of a lost dignity:

> [002-006] First part: That nature is corrupt. Proved by nature itself.

The corruption Pascal describes is not merely moral failure but a structural disorder of [[human-nature|human]] faculties — the [[reason|reason]] is clouded, the [[will|will]] is divided, and the [[imagination|imagination]] usurps the place of judgment.

Yet corruption is not the whole story. Pascal insists that the very capacity to recognise one's own [[wretchedness|wretchedness]] is a sign of [[greatness|greatness]]:

> [006-032] Thought constitutes the greatness of man.

It is precisely because human beings were made for something higher that their present condition registers as [[misery|miserable]]. A creature without a sense of the infinite could not experience its own finitude as a problem.

## The Twofold Doctrine

Pascal's anthropology hangs on two truths that must be held together:

> [007-070] The greatness and the wretchedness of man are so evident that the true religion must necessarily teach us both that there is in man some great source of greatness, and a great source of wretchedness.

The [[Christianity in Pascal's Pensees|Christian]] doctrine of the Fall is, for Pascal, the only explanation that accounts for both poles of this duality. [[Original Sin|Original sin]] explains why human nature is corrupted; the doctrine of creation in the image of [[God|God]] explains why traces of greatness remain.

> [003-174] Corruption of nature, and redemption through Jesus Christ.

The remedy — [[grace|grace]] and [[redemption|redemption]] — corresponds exactly to the diagnosis: what is corrupted must be restored by the same [[God|God]] who created it.
