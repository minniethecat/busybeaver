Job occupies a unique position in Pascal's biblical typology as the pre-Mosaic witness to God — a Gentile who, alongside Balaam, testifies to divine truth outside the covenant lineage. His suffering, patience, and vindication prefigure Christ's Passion and Resurrection, making Job an essential link in Pascal's argument that revelation extends beyond Israel's borders.

## The Pre-Mosaic Witness

"Job, the one a Jew and the other a Gentile" (012-060) — Pascal pairs Job with Balaam as witnesses to God who predate or stand outside the Mosaic covenant. Job's testimony demonstrates that God has never left Himself without witnesses, even among the nations. This argument is central to Pascal's apologetics: the universality of revelation is attested by figures like Job who stand outside the patriarchal lineage.

## Job's Prophetic Hope

"Job, Quis mihi det ut" (012-063) — Pascal cites Job's great cry of hope: "I know that my Redeemer liveth" (*Quis mihi det ut* — "Oh that my words were written"). For Pascal, this utterance is a direct Old Testament testimony to Christ: Job, in the midst of his suffering, prophesies the resurrection. The patience of Job, like the suffering of Christ, is vindicated not by philosophical argument but by divine encounter.

## The Figure of Christ's Mediation

"Evidently in Job xxxiii, 7" (013-161) — Pascal reads Job as a figure of Christ's mediation. Job's insistence on a mediator between himself and God (*"neither is there any daysman betwixt us"*) finds its fulfillment in Christ, the one mediator between God and humanity. Job's longing for an advocate becomes, in Pascal's reading, a prophecy of the Incarnation.

## The Problem of Innocent Suffering

Job's suffering raises the question that Pascal's apologetics must answer: why do the righteous suffer? The Book of Job refuses easy answers, and Pascal does not offer them. Instead, Job's story points toward the Cross — where innocent suffering is not explained but redeemed, transformed from scandal into the means of salvation.
