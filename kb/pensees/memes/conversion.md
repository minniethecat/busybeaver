[[Conversion|Conversion]] occupies a pivotal place in Pascal's thought: it is at once the central event of his own biography, a theological doctrine inherited from [[augustine|Augustine]] and the [[jansenism|Jansenist]] tradition, and the implicit goal of his entire [[apologetics|apologetic]] project.

> [007-637] …They imagine that this conversion consists in a worship of God as a traffic…

Pascal's critique of a merely transactional understanding of conversion — "a worship of God as a traffic" — is sharp: true conversion is not a bargain with the divine but a transformation of the self.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Pascal's Own Conversion

Pascal's biography is marked by two moments conventionally called "conversions." The "first conversion" (1646) was his turn toward the austere [[jansenism|Jansenist]] spirituality of [[port-royal|Port-Royal]]. The "second conversion" (23 November 1654) — recorded in the *Mémorial*, a text Pascal sewed into the lining of his coat and carried for the rest of his life — was an ecstatic experience of the living God:

> *"FIRE — God of Abraham, God of Isaac, God of Jacob, not of the philosophers and scholars. Certitude. Certitude. Feeling. Joy. Peace."*

The *Mémorial* is not a philosophical argument but a witness: Pascal's apologetics is not the attempt to prove what he himself merely believes but the attempt to communicate what he has *known*.

This distinction between the God of the philosophers and the God of [[abraham|Abraham]], [[isaac|Isaac]], and [[jacob|Jacob]] is central to Pascal's theology: the God to whom one converts is not a rational conclusion but a living presence, known in the order of [[charity|charity]] rather than in the order of [[mind|mind]].

### The Nature of Conversion

Pascal's theology of conversion is Augustinian: conversion is a supernatural act, not a human achievement. It cannot be produced by argument, though argument can remove obstacles; it cannot be compelled by evidence, though evidence can prepare the way:

> [012-277] The conversion of the heathen was only reserved for the grace of the Messiah.

Conversion is "reserved for grace" — it is not within human power to effect. The [[pascals-wager|Wager]] argument, famously, does not claim to produce conversion; it claims only to show that the refusal to seek [[god|God]] is irrational. The actual turn to God is God's work, not the apologist's.

> [013-201] Miracles serve not to convert, but to condemn.

This is among Pascal's most difficult sayings: [[miracle|miracles]] do not convert; they condemn. The evidence that should produce faith produces instead a hardening, because the obstacle to faith is not intellectual but moral — the will, not the mind. The [[wretchedness|wretched]] human being, "full of error," is not in a position to evaluate the evidence neutrally.

### Conversion and the Gentiles

Pascal reads the conversion of the Gentiles as a fulfilment of [[prophecy|prophecy]] and thus as a proof of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s truth:

> [011-300] Reprobation of the Jews and conversion of the Gentiles. — Is. viii, 14.

The historical fact that the [[jews|Jewish]] Messiah was rejected by most Jews and accepted by Gentiles was, for Pascal, foretold in Scripture and constitutes a "proof" of a distinctive kind — not a proof that compels rational assent but a proof that, taken together with the whole pattern of prophecy and fulfilment, makes sense of history in a way no other framework can.

> [013-348] …how could the prophecy be fulfilled without the conversion of the nations?

The question is rhetorical: the conversion of the nations *had* occurred, and this fact required explanation. For Pascal, the Christian explanation — that the Messiah had come and the prophecies were being fulfilled — was more adequate than any alternative.

### Conversion and the "Automaton"

Pascal's doctrine of the [[automaton|automaton]] has direct implications for his understanding of conversion. Since belief is produced not by argument alone but by custom and habituation, conversion requires not only intellectual assent but the "bending of the automaton" — the re-formation of habit through practice:

> "You want to find faith and you do not know the way. … Learn from those who have been bound like you … Follow the way by which they began: by behaving as if they believed, taking holy water, having masses said, etc. That will make you believe naturally and will make you mechanical [vous fera croire et vous abêtira]."

This is the most controversial passage in Pascal: the recommendation to act "as if" one believed, in order to *acquire* belief through the mechanism of custom. Pascal is not recommending hypocrisy; he is describing the psychology of belief-formation as he understands it. The automaton must be bent before the mind can follow.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of conversion integrates three dimensions that are often separated: the biographical (his own experience), the theological (Augustinian-Jansenist doctrine), and the strategic (the Wager argument and the bending of the automaton).

The biographical dimension gives Pascal's apologetics an existential urgency that purely theoretical apologetics lack. Pascal is not arguing for a conclusion he has reached; he is bearing witness to an event that has transformed him.

The theological dimension anchors conversion in [[grace|grace]]: conversion is God's work, not man's, and the apologist's task is therefore preparatory, not productive. The apologist removes obstacles; [[god|God]] converts.

The strategic dimension — the Wager and the automaton — reflects Pascal's realism about human psychology. Arguments alone do not convert, because the obstacle to faith is not only intellectual but moral and habitual. The whole person must be addressed: mind, will, and body.
