[[Reason|Reason]], for Pascal, is at once the [[human-nature|human]] being's most distinctive natural faculty and the faculty whose limits most clearly reveal the human predicament.

> [P01-469] A defence of unreason. The heart, in Pascal's terminology, is no means an exaltation of the "heart" over the "head."

What has sometimes been taken as Pascal's irrationalism is in fact a careful delimitation of reason's proper domain. Reason is not to be discarded but to be understood — and understanding reason means understanding its limits.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Two Types of Mind

Pascal distinguishes between two cognitive styles — the intuitive and the mathematical — each with its own form of [[reason|reasoning]]:

> [P01-132] Reason to employ the results of his observation upon any point that is submitted to him.

The "mathematical" mind (*esprit de géométrie*) proceeds by deduction from explicit principles. It is powerful within its domain but helpless outside it — it cannot grasp what cannot be formalised.

> [P01-108] And there is no reason to doubt the genuineness of this document.

The "intuitive" mind (*esprit de finesse*) grasps truths that cannot be demonstrated — the texture of a [[human-nature|human]] situation, the character of a person, the force of an [[argument|argument]] that is not reducible to [[syllogism|syllogisms]].

Neither type of mind is sufficient alone. The mathematical mind needs principles it cannot supply; the intuitive mind needs rigour it cannot articulate. The two types need each other — and the fact that they rarely understand each other is itself a sign of the [[disproportion|disproportion]] in human [[knowledge|knowledge]].

### The Limits of Reason

> [P01-191] The Art of Assent, setting forth the reasons which ought in all logic to bring any mind to the assent.

Pascal's own apologetic strategy is rational — he presents "reasons which ought in all logic" to bring assent. But he simultaneously insists that these reasons, however cogent, are insufficient. Reason can demonstrate that [[christianity|Christianity]] is credible; it cannot produce [[faith-and-reason|faith]].

> [P01-238] Reasoning admissible; he would, so to speak, develop the place of reason, but draws back from its abuse.

Reason has a "place" — a domain within which it is authoritative. The abuse of reason is its extension beyond that domain, claiming to adjudicate matters that exceed its competence. The [[rationalism|rationalist]] is not someone who reasons too much but someone who reasons in the wrong place.

> [002-762] Dispositions, so removed from justice and reason, have a natural foundation.

Reason is not only limited in its upward reach — toward the [[infinite|infinite]] and the [[divine|divine]] — but also in its inward reach, toward the [[passions|passions]] and [[custom|customs]] that actually govern human behaviour. The "natural foundation" of human dispositions is inaccessible to reason's correction.

## Key Passages

> [P01-285] He does not reason, he insinuates, charms, and influences; or if he reasons, you must be prepared for his hidden strokes.

Pascal's account of [[persuasion|persuasion]] distinguishes between reasoning and [[rhetoric|rhetoric]]. The effective communicator does not merely reason — he "insinuates, charms, and influences." Reason, in the actual psychology of belief-formation, is rarely the decisive factor.

> [P01-286] Or if he reasons, you must be prepared for his hidden strokes of art as well as his open ones of logic.

Even when someone reasons, reasoning is embedded in a larger rhetorical strategy. The "hidden strokes of art" — tone, timing, appeal to [[emotion|emotion]] and [[authority|authority]] — are not decorations added to reasoning but the context that makes reasoning persuasive.

## Analysis

Pascal's account of reason must be understood in relation to his doctrine of the [[three-orders|three orders]]. Reason belongs to the second order — the order of the mind. It is genuinely great within that order, just as [[physical|physical]] power is genuinely great within the order of the [[body|body]]. But it cannot reach the third order — the order of [[charity|charity]] — any more than the body can reach the mind.

This is not a failure of reason. It is an expression of the structure of reality. The fact that reason cannot grasp [[God|God]] is not evidence against God's existence but evidence that God belongs to a different order — an order that reason, by its nature, cannot access.

Pascal's position thus occupies a distinctive middle ground between [[dogmatism|dogmatism]] and [[skepticism|skepticism]]. Against the dogmatist, he insists that reason cannot achieve [[certainty|certainty]] about the most important truths. Against the skeptic, he insists that this limitation does not make those truths unavailable — they are accessible to another faculty, the [[heart|heart]].
