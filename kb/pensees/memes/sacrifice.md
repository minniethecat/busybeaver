# Sacrifice

## In Pascal's Pensées

Sacrifice is a central category in Pascal's biblical theology and apologetics. The Jewish sacrificial system — the animals offered in the Temple — serves as a type (figure) of Christ's sacrifice. "Those who ordained these sacrifices, knew their uselessness" (008-227): the Old Testament sacrifices were acknowledged even by their practitioners as insufficient in themselves. They pointed beyond themselves to the true sacrifice. Pascal's reading of sacrifice is thus thoroughly figural: the carnal sacrifices of the Jews are rejected by God ("I will take no pleasure in the sacrifices of the Jews," 009-230) precisely because they were always meant to be replaced by the spiritual sacrifice of Christ. This hermeneutic anchors Pascal's larger argument that the Old Testament is a book of figures whose meaning is disclosed only in Christ.

## Key Passages

> "Those who ordained these sacrifices, knew their uselessness; those who have declared their uselessness, have not ceased to practice them." (008-227)

> "That the sacrifices of the Jews displeased God. That the sacrifices of the Gentiles will be accepted by God, and that God will take no pleasure in the sacrifices of the Jews." (009-224, 229–230)

> "That the sacrifices should be rejected, and others established." (009-238)

> "To know if the law and the sacrifices are a reality or a type, we must see if the prophets spoke of these things in such a way that they said the law and the sacrifices would be eternal... or if they said the contrary." (010-190)

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of sacrifice exemplifies his figural hermeneutics — the method of reading Scripture in which the Old Testament is a book of "figures" (types) whose reality (antitype) is Christ. The carnal sacrifices of the Jewish law are "figures" of Christ's sacrifice; the figure is real in its own order but finds its full meaning only in what it prefigures.

This hermeneutic solves a theological problem: why did God ordain sacrifices that He would later reject? Pascal's answer is that the sacrifices were always figures — they were never the final reality. "Those who ordained these sacrifices, knew their uselessness" — the very prophets who instituted the sacrificial system understood its provisional character. The sacrifice of animals was a placeholder, a pedagogical device directing Israel toward the true sacrifice to come.

The apologetic force of this argument is considerable. The rejection of Jewish sacrifices and the acceptance of Gentile (spiritual) sacrifices is, for Pascal, a proof of Christianity's truth: the prophets themselves foretold the obsolescence of the ceremonial law. This connects to Pascal's larger argument from prophecy — the Old Testament, read figurally, everywhere points to Christ. "To know if the law and the sacrifices are a reality or a type" is the hermeneutic question par excellence: read carnally, the law is eternal; read spiritually, it is fulfilled and superseded.

Sacrifice also connects to Pascal's theology of the Cross. Christ's sacrifice is the ultimate expression of the order of charity — a gift that the order of body (force) and the order of mind (reason) cannot comprehend. The Cross is "foolishness" to the Greeks and a "stumbling block" to the Jews precisely because it inverts every worldly logic of sacrifice and power.
