## Definition

In Pascal's epistemology, **truth** (la vérité) is not a monolithic property but a **fragile and partial achievement**. Pascal rejects both the dogmatic rationalist's confidence that reason can grasp truth completely and the skeptic's despair that truth is inaccessible. Instead he develops a subtle account in which truths are independent of one another, access to truth depends on the right disposition of the will as much as the intellect, and the human capacity for truth is limited by disproportion. Truth is attainable but never total — and this limitation is not a defect but a structural feature of the human epistemological condition.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Independence of Truths

One of Pascal's most distinctive epistemological claims is that truths are independent — knowing one truth does not guarantee access to another:

> (001-247) Nature has made all her truths independent of one another

This independence has profound consequences for Pascal's apologetic method. You cannot deduce all truths from a single principle (as Descartes attempted). Each domain has its own truths and its own appropriate method of access. What works for geometry does not work for theology; what the heart knows, reason cannot demonstrate.

> (002-540) Justice and truth are two such subtle points, that our instruments are too blunt to touch them exactly

The metaphor of "blunt instruments" captures Pascal's sense of the mismatch between the subtlety of reality and the coarseness of human cognitive faculties. We can approximate truth but rarely grasp it with precision.

### Too Much Truth Paralyzes

Pascal makes the counterintuitive observation that more truth is not always better. There is an optimal epistemic distance:

> (002-080) find truth; give him too much, the same

> (002-212) too much truth is paralysing

This insight operates at multiple levels. Psychologically, the full truth about the human condition — our mortality, our cosmic insignificance, our inner contradictions — is unbearable, which is why we flee into diversion. Epistemologically, the interconnectedness Pascal observes means that grasping one truth fully would require grasping all truths, which finite minds cannot do. Pedagogically, the apologist must reveal truths gradually, not all at once, to avoid overwhelming the listener.

### The Will and the Knowledge of Truth

Pascal insists that knowledge of truth is not a purely intellectual matter. The will — our loves, desires, and aversions — determines what truths we can see:

> (002-706) we hate truth and those who tell it us

Self-love (amour-propre) makes us hostile to truths that threaten our self-image. We do not merely fail to find truth; we actively resist it when it conflicts with our interests:

> (002-680) a mortal enmity against that truth which...

This moral dimension of epistemology is central to Pascal's apologetic. The skeptic's rejection of Christian truth is not primarily intellectual but volitional — a matter of the heart, not the head. This is why argument alone cannot convert: the will must be reoriented before the mind can see.

### Two Sources of Truth

Pascal recognizes two cognitive faculties that deliver truth, each with its proper domain:

> (002-552) These two sources of truth, reason and the senses

But both are fallible. The senses deceive (optical illusions, dreams), and reason is subject to the influence of imagination, custom, and passion. Pascal is neither a rationalist nor an empiricist but a **critical epistemologist** who maps the limits of each faculty.

Beyond these two, Pascal recognizes a third source: the **heart** (le cœur), which delivers intuitive, non-demonstrative knowledge — including the knowledge of first principles, of God, and of moral truths. "The heart has its reasons which reason knows not."

### Common Error as Social Bond

Pascal makes the striking sociological observation that shared error often serves a social function that truth cannot:

> (001-213) When we do not know the truth of a thing, it is of advantage that there should exist a common error

This is not cynicism but realism about the limits of human knowledge. In the absence of certain truth, a shared convention — even if technically false — can maintain social order. This connects to Pascal's treatment of [[Custom]] as a foundation of law and authority: "custom is the whole of equity, for the simple reason that it is accepted."

## Analysis

Pascal's epistemology of truth occupies a distinctive position in the seventeenth-century landscape. Against Descartes' project of systematic, certain knowledge built from clear and distinct ideas, Pascal insists on the **local and domain-specific** character of truth. Truths are not deductions from axioms but insights into distinct orders of reality, each requiring its appropriate mode of access.

This view has important theological implications. If truths are independent, then the truth of Christianity cannot be demonstrated from philosophical first principles (contra Thomistic natural theology) nor refuted by them (contra libertine skepticism). Christianity must be assessed on its own terms — through prophecy, miracle, and its capacity to explain the human condition.

The psychological dimension of Pascal's epistemology — the claim that we hate truth and those who speak it — anticipates Nietzsche's and Freud's analyses of self-deception. But where Nietzsche sees the will to power and Freud sees repression, Pascal sees original sin: our fallenness consists partly in our inability to love truth more than self.

The concept is distinct from [[Certainty]] (the psychological state of conviction), [[Knowledge]] (the faculty or its contents), and [[Evidence]] (what makes truth apparent). It also connects to [[Heart]] (the intuitive faculty that knows first principles) and [[Reason]] (the discursive faculty whose limitations Pascal charts).
