[[Epictetus]] (c. 50–135 AD), the Stoic philosopher and former slave, occupies a distinctive position in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées*: he is at once admired for having understood the nature of human duty and critiqued for the [[pride]] that prevented him from recognizing the need for divine [[grace]].

Pascal's treatment of Epictetus forms part of his larger dialectical strategy. In the famous opening of the *Pensées*, he contrasts the manner of Epictetus with that of [[Montaigne]] and [[Salomon de Tultie]], seeing each as offering a partial truth about the human condition (001-220). Epictetus represents the pole of human [[greatness]] — the capacity for moral self-governance — while Montaigne represents the pole of human [[wretchedness]]. Neither alone is adequate; only [[Christianity]] can hold both truths together.

## Epictetus and the Philosophy of Duty

Pascal reads Epictetus as the philosopher who best grasped the scope of human moral obligation. The Stoic insistence that the mind is free, that external circumstances cannot touch the inner citadel, captures something genuinely true about the human condition:

> [006-059] Epictetus concludes that since the mind is free, it can remain master of its passions, and that we must render to God what is God's and to the world what is the world's.

This is, for Pascal, no small achievement. Epictetus correctly identifies that the human being possesses a faculty of [[reason]] and [[will]] that transcends mere animal appetite. The Stoic ethic of detachment — not caring about what is not within our power — represents the highest reach of unaided human [[wisdom]]. Pascal does not dismiss this; he honors it as a genuine insight into the [[greatness-and-misery|greatness]] of the human condition.

Yet the honor is dialectical. What Epictetus achieves is, in a sense, the problem: he shows what man *ought* to be but cannot *become* through his own resources. The Stoic sage is an ideal that exposes, by its very perfection, the gap between duty and capacity.

## The Limits of Stoic Wisdom

Pascal's critique of Epictetus turns on the question of [[pride]]. The Stoic philosopher knows what duty requires but fails to recognize that fallen humanity lacks the power to fulfill it. Epictetus correctly diagnoses the disease but prescribes an impossible cure:

> [007-238] "Bend your mind," says Epictetus. And others say, "Bend your eyes." But neither takes account of the heart.

This brief remark condenses Pascal's entire critique. Epictetus treats the will as a faculty that can be directed by an act of intellectual resolution. Pascal, drawing on his Augustinian [[anthropology]], sees the [[heart]] — the seat of desire and love — as the true motor of human action. Stoic self-command is a rationalist illusion; only a transformation of the affections, which [[grace]] alone can accomplish, suffices to reorder the soul.

Pascal develops this critique further in his long meditation on the human condition:

> [002-392] Epictetus asks still more strongly: "Why do we complain of aught that happens to us? Can it surprise us?"

The Stoic counsel of acceptance presumes a self-mastery that is, for Pascal, anthropologically false. We *do* complain, we *are* surprised, and our inability to follow Epictetus's advice is itself evidence of the [[corruption]] that his philosophy cannot acknowledge. The Stoic asks the right question — why are we troubled by what we know to be inevitable? — but gives the wrong answer, because he has not reckoned with the [[fall]].

## Epictetus Had He Seen the Way

Pascal's most revealing judgment on Epictetus appears in a counterfactual passage that imagines what the Stoic might have become had he encountered Christianity:

> [007-637] Had Epictetus seen the way perfectly, he would have said to Christians: "You do not follow your own principles."

This counterfactual is at once generous and devastating. Generous, because Pascal grants that Epictetus had the moral clarity to recognize Christian truth had he been fully exposed to it. Devastating, because Pascal uses Epictetus's imagined voice to indict Christians who do not live what they profess. The Stoic, who lacked [[revelation]], nonetheless saw what righteous conduct required; the Christian, who possesses revelation, falls short of it.

The passage reveals Pascal's dialectical method at its most sophisticated. Epictetus is not simply refuted; he is incorporated into the Christian argument as a witness against Christians themselves. The philosopher who knew neither [[Scripture]] nor [[Jesus Christ]] understood the demands of virtue more clearly than those who claim to follow Christ but do not obey him.

## Epictetus and the Wager

Pascal's engagement with Epictetus also illuminates the psychology underlying the [[Wager]]. The Stoic ideal of self-mastery through reason is, for Pascal, a form of [[diversion]] — a way of avoiding the existential question by substituting moral technique for genuine transformation. Epictetus offers ethics; the Wager demands a decision about the whole of existence.

> [010-190] As one sentence of Epictetus decides the meaning of all the rest.

This passing remark in Pascal's discussion of prophetic interpretation suggests a hermeneutic principle: a single decisive statement can illuminate an entire corpus. Applied reflexively to Epictetus himself, the "one sentence" that decides his meaning may be his inability to see that duty, without grace, leads only to [[despair]]. The Stoic philosophy is, in Pascal's final analysis, the noblest expression of human [[greatness]] — and therefore the clearest demonstration of human [[wretchedness]].
