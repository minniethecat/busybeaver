Homer appears in Pascal's *Pensées* not as a subject of literary appreciation but as a strategic element in his apologetic argument about the unique authority of Scripture. Pascal uses Homer — the most ancient and authoritative figure of the pagan world — as the benchmark against which the Bible's historical claims are measured and vindicated.

## The Most Ancient Authority

Pascal establishes Homer's chronological priority as the starting point of his comparative argument: "The most ancient book in the world, those of Homer, Hesiod, and others, are only about six or seven hundred years older than the foundation of Rome" (009-471). This is Pascal's concession — he grants Homer the maximum antiquity that secular scholarship can claim. The point is not to diminish Homer but to establish him as the best that unaided human tradition can offer.

Having granted Homer's antiquity, Pascal then draws the contrast: the books of Moses are older still, and unlike Homer's works, they claim to transmit not poetic fiction but historical revelation. The comparative exercise serves to isolate what is unique about biblical testimony — it is both more ancient and more truth-claiming than the pagan classics.

## Homer as Conscious Fiction

Pascal's most provocative move is to characterize Homer's work as self-acknowledged fiction: "Homer composes a romance, which he gives out as such, and which was received as such" (009-525). The claim is that Homer never pretended to be writing history — he offered his epics as poetry, and his audience received them as poetry. This is not, for Pascal, a criticism of Homer but a clarification of genre.

The contrast with Scripture is stark. The biblical authors do not compose romances; they testify to events they claim to have witnessed or received through revelation. Pascal's apologetic strategy here is elegant: by granting Homer his full dignity as a poet, Pascal simultaneously sharpens the distinction between poetic truth and historical truth, and insists that the Bible claims — and must be judged by — the latter standard.

## The Historical Scope of Homer

Pascal acknowledges Homer's immense historical range: "Homer, who has written the history of so many states, has nevertheless not described a continual history" (009-454). Even the greatest of pagan authorities, Pascal observes, could not produce a unified narrative of human origins and destiny. The pagan world produced epics and chronicles but no universal history. Scripture alone, in Pascal's reading, provides the comprehensive narrative from creation to consummation that answers the human need for meaning.

## Homer in Pascal's Apologetic Framework

Homer's role in the *Pensées* is thus structural rather than substantive. He is not analyzed or engaged as a thinker — Pascal shows no interest in Homeric theology or ethics. He functions instead as a placeholder for the best that human culture can achieve apart from revelation: ancient, beautiful, wise within its limits, but finally unable to make the truth-claims or provide the historical grounding that Pascal's apologetic requires. Homer is the summit of the natural, and the summit of the natural demonstrates by its very excellence why the supernatural is necessary.
