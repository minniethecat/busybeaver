## Definition

In Pascal's *Pensées*, **eternity** (l'éternité) is the infinite temporal dimension against which all human life must be measured. It is not merely an abstract theological concept but an **existentially urgent reality** — the state that follows death and the proper horizon for evaluating every human choice. Pascal's use of eternity operates at three levels: as the **stakes** of the Wager (infinite gain or loss), as the **object of dread** that humans flee through diversion, and as the **cosmic frame** that reveals human disproportion.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Eternity as the Wager's Stakes

The Wager argument depends entirely on the concept of eternity. Pascal frames the choice as one between finite stakes (worldly pleasure, comfort, reputation) and infinite stakes (eternal life or eternal loss). Since any finite quantity is negligible compared to the infinite, the rational choice is to wager on God:

> (002-248) eternity, even if it lasts ten years longer...

Pascal emphasizes the asymmetry: what you risk is finite (your worldly life), but what you stand to gain or lose is infinite and eternal (003-597).

> (003-597) But there is an eternity of life and happiness. And

The possibility of "eternal joys" (003-084) makes the Wager's calculation drastically one-sided. Pascal dismisses the objection that the uncertainty of God's existence should make one withhold commitment — uncertainty about an eternal outcome does not license indifference but demands the most serious engagement.

> (002-1075) It is absurd to say that an eternal recompense is offered to the ...

### The Terror of Eternity

Pascal observes that nothing is more terrifying to human beings than the thought of eternity — precisely because it confronts them with their own mortality and the possibility of irreversible loss:

> (003-180) so formidable to him as eternity; and thus it is not natural to take...

> (003-285) eternity, that it is impossible to take...

This terror is the psychological engine behind **diversion** (divertissement). Humans cannot bear to sit alone with the thought of their eternal fate, so they fill every moment with distraction:

> (003-295) eternity by turning away their thought...

Yet Pascal insists that the strategy of avoidance changes nothing:

> (003-297) Yet this eternity exists, and death, which must...

### Eternal Silence and Cosmic Disproportion

One of Pascal's most famous passages situates eternity in the cosmic frame that reveals human finitude:

> (003-370) The eternal silence of these infinite spaces frightens me

Here eternity is not primarily about duration but about the **indifference** of the cosmos to human concerns. The spatial infinite (silent, empty) and the temporal infinite (eternity) together produce the experience of disproportion — man suspended between nothingness and infinity, incapable of comprehending either (002-153).

> (002-153) middle of things, in an eternal despair of knowing either the...

### Assurance and Uncertainty

Pascal distinguishes between the philosophical certainty of eternity's existence (which reason can approximate) and the existential **assurance** of one's own participation in eternal happiness (which only faith provides):

> (003-124) have complete assurance of eternity, so there is no more happiness...

The uncertainty of one's eternal state — "uncertain of the eternity" (003-164) — is the human predicament that philosophy cannot resolve and that only religious faith addresses. The point is sharpened by the fact that death makes this uncertainty permanent:

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of eternity achieves a distinctive synthesis of philosophical rigor and psychological realism. Theologically, it draws on the Augustinian tradition in which the human heart is restless until it rests in God — eternity is the true home of the soul, and time-bound existence is a kind of exile. But Pascal's innovation is to make eternity the linchpin of a **decision-theoretic** argument.

The Wager's force comes entirely from the asymmetry between finite and infinite: if the stakes are truly eternal, then no finite sacrifice is too great, and no finite probability of God's existence is too small, to warrant the wager. This is not a proof of God but a **pragmatic argument** that indifference is irrational.

Psychologically, Pascal's analysis of the fear of eternity anticipates existentialist themes. The human flight from thoughts of death and eternity through diversion is, for Pascal, the fundamental explanation of human restlessness — what later thinkers would call "inauthenticity" or "bad faith." The difference is that Pascal sees this flight not as a failure of courage but as a **rational response to a terrifying truth** that human resources cannot handle.
