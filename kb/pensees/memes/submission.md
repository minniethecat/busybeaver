## Definition

In Pascal's epistemology, **submission** (soumission) is the **self-limiting act of [[Reason]]** by which it recognizes its own boundaries and yields to a higher order of knowledge. Submission is not the surrender of reason to irrationality but reason's *highest achievement*: "the last step of reason is to recognize that there is an infinity of things which surpass it." Pascal distinguishes sharply between two kinds of submission — the "simple submissions of reason" (007-366) that lead to genuine knowledge of [[God]], and the "superstitious submission" (004-140) that abdicates reason's proper authority within its own domain. The distinction is ordered by the doctrine of the [[Three Orders]]: reason submits to the order of charity, not to the order of bodies; to divine authority, not to human tyranny.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Submission of Reason

Pascal's most concentrated treatment of submission appears in the context of his discussion of the limits of rational demonstration:

> (002-1075) On the submission of reason.

This brief heading in the Pensées' manuscript signals a major theme. For Pascal, the submission of reason is not a concession to fideism but the logical consequence of a rigorous rationalism. If reason is genuinely committed to truth, it must acknowledge truths that it cannot itself demonstrate. The refusal to submit — the insistence that everything must be proved by reason — is itself irrational, because it imposes on reason a demand that reason cannot meet. The submission of reason is reason's own act: it is reason that judges that there are matters beyond its jurisdiction.

### True Submission vs Superstitious Submission

Pascal is careful to distinguish authentic submission from its counterfeit:

> (004-140) Teach us for this superstitious submission.

The context of this fragment is Pascal's critique of those who treat external religious observances as meritorious in themselves. "Superstitious submission" is the abdication of reason before practices that have no rational warrant — the kind of submission that [[Superstition]] demands. Authentic submission, by contrast, is always submission *to truth*, not to arbitrary authority. It is reason's recognition that the object exceeds the faculty, not reason's capitulation to force or custom.

### Submission and the Knowledge of God

Pascal argues that the knowledge of God is accessible only through the right ordering of the faculties, which includes submission:

> (007-366) We can only truly know God by the simple submissions of reason.

The adjective "simple" (simples) is significant. Pascal does not call for abstruse or heroic acts of intellectual self-denial. The submissions required are *simple*: the acknowledgment that the finite cannot comprehend the infinite, that the effect cannot fully grasp its cause, that the creature owes reverence to the Creator. These are not paradoxes or contradictions; they are the elementary recognitions that reason, if honest, must make. The difficulty is not intellectual but moral — the will resists submission because submission is a form of [[Humility]].

### The Posture of Submission

Pascal describes the attitude of the believing intellect as one of profound submission before the divine:

> (007-637) With what submission would it allow itself to be governed.

This fragment, from Pascal's extended reflection on the human condition, envisions the soul that has recognized its true state. The submission Pascal describes here is not cringing or servile; it is the natural response of a creature that has understood its place in the order of being. To submit to God is to acknowledge the reality of one's situation — to cease the futile rebellion of the creature against the Creator. The submission is as much a relief as a duty: it ends the exhausting pretense of autonomy.

### The Kings' Submission to the Cross

Pascal finds the theme of submission inscribed in the historical triumph of [[Christianity in Pascal's Pensées|Christianity]]:

> (011-664) The very kings made submission to the cross.

This fragment belongs to Pascal's argument from the propagation of Christianity. The cross — an instrument of execution reserved for slaves and rebels — became the object of submission by the world's most powerful rulers. For Pascal, this historical inversion is a mark of the divine origin of Christianity: no merely human movement could have brought kings to kneel before a cross. The submission of kings to the [[Cross]] is a sign that the order of charity has genuine authority over the order of bodies, an authority that even worldly power eventually acknowledges.

### Submission to the Ancient Church

Pascal extends the logic of submission to the interpretive authority of the Church:

> (014-106) This submission and this conformity to the ancient Church.

In matters of scriptural interpretation and theological tradition, Pascal advocates submission to the ancient Church — not to the innovations of recent theologians but to the consensus of the Fathers and the Councils. This is consistent with his broader epistemology: just as reason submits to revelation in matters that exceed its capacity, so the individual theologian submits to the corporate wisdom of the Church in matters of doctrinal interpretation. Submission here is not mindless obedience but the rational acknowledgment that the Church's collective judgment over centuries carries more weight than private opinion.

## Analysis

Submission is the epistemological virtue that corresponds to the structure of Pascal's three orders. Each order has its own mode of knowing and its own proper objects. The error of rationalism is to demand that the order of charity submit to the order of mind — that divine truths be demonstrated by geometric method. The error of fideism is to demand that the order of mind submit to ungrounded claims — that reason abdicate entirely. Pascal's submission of reason is the mean between these two errors: reason judges that there are truths beyond its jurisdiction and freely yields to the higher order's mode of knowing.

This has important implications for Pascal's apologetics. He does not ask his reader to believe without reasons — he provides reasons, many of them — but he does ask the reader to recognize that the reasons do not add up to a demonstration. The gap between the evidence and the conclusion must be bridged by the will, moved by the [[Heart]], responding to grace. Submission names the act by which the will bridges that gap: it is the "yes" that reason cannot speak alone but that reason can authorize as not unreasonable.

The political dimension of submission in Pascal's thought should not be overlooked. "The very kings made submission to the cross" is an argument about the nature of authority. The fact that temporal power bows before spiritual truth is evidence that the order of charity is genuinely higher — not merely claimed to be higher, but recognized as such even by those who have the most to lose by the recognition. Submission is thus both an epistemological concept (reason submitting to revelation) and a historical-theological argument (power submitting to the cross).
