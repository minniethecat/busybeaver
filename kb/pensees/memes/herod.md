[[Herod]] the Great occupies a complex and ambivalent position in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées*. He is at once an instrument of [[divine]] [[providence]] — a ruler whose actions, undertaken for purely political reasons, unwittingly advanced the purposes of [[God]] — and a cautionary figure whose career illustrates the danger of confusing temporal power with [[messianic]] fulfillment. His reign stands at the pivot point between the Old and New Testaments, the moment when the [[prophecy|prophetic]] promises concerning [[Judah]]'s royal line reached their fulfillment and their terminus.

Pascal's treatment of Herod is distributed across several sections of the *Pensées*, with the most concentrated discussion appearing in [[Section-XI|Section XI]] and [[Section-XII]], where Herod serves as a case study in the providential interpretation of [[history]].

## Herod and Providential History

Pascal's most striking deployment of Herod comes in his argument that secular history, seen through the eyes of [[faith]], reveals a hidden order. The great figures of the ancient world — [[kings]], conquerors, empires — were, whether they knew it or not, serving a purpose beyond their own ambitions.

> [011-055] It is glorious to see with the eyes of faith the history of Herod and of Cæsar.

This is Pascal's hermeneutical principle for reading history. Without [[faith]], the history of Herod is simply the record of a client king's maneuverings within the Roman imperial system. With faith, the same history becomes a chapter in the story of [[salvation]] — the puzzle piece that, seen from above, fits into the larger pattern of divine purpose.

The principle is applied more concretely in a passage that links Herod with other figures of secular power:

> [011-065] Alexander, the Romans, Pompey and Herod working, without knowing it, for the glory of the Gospel!

"Without knowing it" is the key phrase. Herod did not intend to serve the [[Gospel]]; he intended to serve Herod. Yet his actions — his rebuilding of the [[Temple]], his consolidation of power, his place in the chain of events that led to [[Jesus Christ]]'s birth and [[Pontius Pilate|Pilate]]'s judgment — all contributed, in ways he could not have foreseen, to the fulfillment of divine purposes. This is the Pascalian doctrine of [[providence]]: God works through secondary causes, including the ambitions and cruelties of kings, to accomplish what no human agent intends.

## Prophecy and Its Apparent Failure

Pascal's treatment of Herod is remarkable for its honesty about a potential objection to the [[Christian]] interpretation of [[prophecy]]. The prophecy of [[Jacob]] in [[Genesis]] had declared that the scepter would not depart from Judah until the coming of the one to whom it belongs. But Herod was not of the tribe of [[Judah]]; he was an Idumean, a descendant of [[Esau]]. If a non-Judahite occupied the throne, did not this falsify the prophecy?

> [011-431] prophecy was proved false by Herod. But to show that this was not their meaning, and that, on the contrary, they knew well that this temporal kingdom should cease, they said that they would be without a king and without a prince, and for a long time. Hosea iii, 4.

Pascal's response is characteristically dialectical. The objection is granted — yes, Herod's reign appears to contradict the prophecy — but the appearance is misleading. The [[Jews]] themselves, Pascal argues, understood that the temporal [[kingdom]] would eventually cease; the prophecy of [[Hosea]] explicitly foretold a period "without a king and without a prince." Herod's reign, far from falsifying prophecy, confirmed it: the scepter had departed from Judah, and the time of the [[Messiah]] had arrived.

This argument illustrates Pascal's broader approach to apparent difficulties in [[Scripture]]. He does not deny the difficulty; he shows that the difficulty, properly understood, becomes a confirmation. The very thing that seems to contradict the prophetic testimony — a non-Davidic king on the throne — is the thing that signals the fulfillment of the promise.

## Herod and the Trial of Christ

Herod's son, [[Herod Antipas]], plays a brief but theologically significant role in the [[Passion]] narrative — a role that Pascal reads as providentially orchestrated.

> [012-082] Pilate a reason for sending Jesus Christ to Herod. And thereby the mystery was accomplished, that He should be judged by Jews and Gentiles. Chance was apparently the cause of the accomplishment of the mystery.

The apparently accidental chain of judicial referrals — [[Pilate]] sending Jesus to Herod, Herod sending him back to Pilate — is, in Pascal's reading, anything but accidental. It is the mechanism by which the [[divine]] plan is realized through the ordinary operations of human institutions. Christ must be judged by both [[Jews]] and [[Gentiles]] because he is the savior of both; the apparently random decision to involve Herod in the trial ensures that this condition is met.

Pascal's insistence on the appearance of chance — "Chance was apparently the cause" — is central to his [[apologetics|apologetic]] strategy. He is not asking his reader to believe in a world where miracles replace natural causation. He is asking the reader to see that natural causation itself, when viewed with the eyes of [[faith]], reveals a providential pattern. Herod and Pilate were acting on their own motives; chance brought them together; but the result was the fulfillment of a plan that neither of them understood.

## The Misplaced Messianic Expectation

Pascal's final note on Herod concerns the dangerous ambiguity of messianic expectation. Some in first-century [[Judea]] believed that Herod himself might be the [[Messiah]].

> [012-145] Herod was believed to be the Messiah. He had taken away the sceptre from Judah, but he was not of Judah. This gave rise to a considerable sect.

This observation cuts in two directions. On the one hand, it explains how the messianic expectation of the period could attach itself to the wrong figure — a king who, for all his temporal power, lacked the essential qualification of Davidic descent. On the other hand, it provides Pascal with an argument against those who would dismiss Christianity on the grounds that messianic claimants were common in first-century Judaism. If even Herod — a non-Judahite — could attract a messianic following, then the very proliferation of false messiahs confirms rather than undermines the intensity and specificity of the messianic hope that [[Jesus]] fulfilled.

The "considerable sect" that formed around Herod's messianic claim is, for Pascal, a cautionary example. [[Faith]] must be grounded in more than the appearance of power. Herod had taken the scepter, but he was not of the line of [[David]]; his apparent fulfillment of prophecy was an illusion. The true [[Messiah]] would come not through the seizure of power but through the fulfillment of promises that Herod's career, precisely by its apparent contradiction of those promises, had set the stage for.
