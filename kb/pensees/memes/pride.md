[[Pride|Pride]], in Pascal's moral psychology, is not merely one vice among others but the defining sin of the [[concupiscence|concupiscent]] self. While [[self-love|self-love]] (amour-propre) describes the self's curvature toward itself, pride names the active refusal to accept one's creaturely dependence — the will to be one's own origin.

> (P01-443) in him the sin of spiritual pride, the concupiscence

Pride stands at the intersection of Pascal's [[theology|theology]] and his [[anthropology|anthropology]]: it is both the original sin and the daily posture of the [[wretchedness|wretched]] self that refuses to acknowledge its wretchedness.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Pride as Refusal to Submit

Pascal's most concentrated treatment of pride identifies it with the refusal of submission — the creature's "no" to the Creator:

> (004-081) pride to be unwilling to submit to

This refusal is not intellectual but volitional. The proud man does not lack evidence — he lacks the [[humility|humility]] to receive it. Pride is less a failure of [[reason|reason]] than a corruption of the [[will|will]], a point that connects pride to Pascal's analysis of [[faith-and-reason|faith]].

Pascal's Augustinian inheritance is visible here: pride is the sin of the [[mind|mind]] as much as the [[body|body]], corrupting the intellect's ability to recognise [[truth|truth]].

> (004-088) is pride.

The compression of this fragment — the single word "pride" as diagnosis — is characteristic of Pascal's aphoristic method. Pride is the sufficient explanation for what resists explanation.

### False Humanity and Pride

Pascal links pride to counterfeit virtue — what passes for humanity in the [[three-orders|order of the body]] is actually pride wearing benevolence:

> (004-183) False humanity, pride.

This fragment connects pride to Pascal's analysis of [[deception|deception]] and [[imagination|imagination]]. The proud man performs virtues he does not possess, and his performance deceives both himself and others. [[Vanity|Vanity]], in its Pascalian sense, is pride's public face.

### Pride and Humility: The Dialectical Pair

Pascal's treatment of pride cannot be separated from its remedy, [[humility|humility]]:

> (006-137) on humility are a source of pride in the vain, and of humility

This is Pascal at his most dialectical: humility itself can become an occasion for pride. The vain man, hearing exhortations to humility, turns his very acknowledgment of insufficiency into a ground for self-congratulation — "see how humble I am." [[Greatness-and-misery|Greatness and misery]] converge: the capacity for humility is greatness; the perversion of humility into pride is misery.

### Pride and the Human Condition

Pascal situates pride within his comprehensive diagnosis of the human condition:

> (007-089) remedy for our ills? Is man's pride cured

> (007-094) teach us to cure pride and lust? What religion will

The question is rhetorical: no philosophy has cured [[pride|pride]] because no philosophy has diagnosed it at sufficient depth. Only [[christianity|Christianity]], with its doctrine of [[original-sin|original sin]] and its offer of [[grace|grace]], addresses pride at the root.

> (007-109) pride. He wanted to make himself hi

This fragment echoes the [[biblical|Bible]] account of the Fall: man's desire to "make himself" — to be self-constituting — is the essence of pride. Pascal reads Genesis through Augustine: the [[original-sin|original sin]] was pride, and every subsequent sin recapitulates it.

> (007-139) them? Your chief maladies are pride,

The verb "are" — present tense — is crucial. Pride is not a past event but a present malady. Pascal's [[diagnosis|diagnosis]] of the human condition is not historical but existential: pride is what afflicts each person now.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of pride operates on three levels. First, as a theological category — pride is the primal sin, the creaturely refusal of creaturehood. Second, as a moral-psychological category — pride is the engine of [[concupiscence|concupiscence]], the corruption of the [[will|will]] that disorders all desire. Third, as an epistemological category — pride is what prevents the [[mind|mind]] from recognising [[truth|truth]], what makes [[evidence|evidence]] insufficient for [[faith|faith]].

The distinctiveness of Pascal's account lies in his recognition that pride and [[greatness-and-misery|greatness]] are inseparable. Only a being capable of greatness can be proud — [[animals|animals]] are not proud because they cannot refuse submission. Pride therefore testifies to human dignity even as it manifests human corruption. This is the Pascalian paradox: the sin that most alienates man from [[god|God]] is also the sin that most reveals man's unique status.

Pride's remedy, for Pascal, is not self-abasement but [[charity|charity]] — the redirection of the [[heart|heart]] from self to God. The [[automaton|automaton]] must be bent not toward self-assertion but toward love, a bending that Pascal's [[pascals-wager|Wager]] is designed to initiate.
