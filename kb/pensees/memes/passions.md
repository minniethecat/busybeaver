# Passions

## In Pascal's Pensées

The passions occupy a central place in Pascal's moral psychology. They are the driving forces of human behavior — "The passions of the soul trouble the senses" (002-557). Pascal's account is dialectical: the passions are at once natural (given by nature for our present state, 002-834) and corrupted (by the Fall, they become tyrannical). They are the psychological mechanism of diversion — we seek agitation to escape from ourselves. Yet Pascal's response is not Stoic suppression but redirection: the passions must be turned toward their true object, God, not eliminated. This connects directly to the Wager's psychology — "bend the automaton" works through the passions.

## Key Passages

> "The passions of the soul trouble the senses and make false impressions upon them." (002-557)

> "Nature gives us, then, passions and desires suitable to our present state." (002-834)

> "We are the passions of the state in which we are." (002-837)

> "So in the passions, there is pleasure in seeing the two contrary sides clash." (002-975)

> "You would like to reach faith, and do not know the way; you would like to cure yourself of unbelief, and ask the remedy. Learn from those who have been bound like you... It is not by the increase of proofs, but by the abatement of your passions." (003-642)

> "If man had only reason without the passions... If he had only passions without reason..." (006-137)

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of the passions reflects his Jansenist anthropology: human nature is fallen, and the passions bear the marks of both original greatness (they are natural capacities given by God) and original corruption (they are disordered and tyrannical). This dual character is essential: the passions cannot be simply eliminated, because they are part of our created nature. But they cannot be simply indulged, because they are corrupted and self-destructive.

The key passage 003-642 reveals the practical dimension: faith comes not by accumulating proofs (the rationalist path) but by "the abatement of your passions." This is the ascetic dimension of Pascal's apologetics — the passions are obstacles to belief not because they are intellectually wrong but because they *bind* the will. The Wager's famous "bend the automaton" advice works through this mechanism: repeated practice reshapes the passions, and the passions in turn dispose the soul toward belief.

Pascal's account is more psychologically sophisticated than simple repression. "So in the passions, there is pleasure in seeing the two contrary sides clash" — the passions have their own perverse aesthetics. We enjoy conflict, spectacle, agitation. This is the link to diversion: the passions drive us outward because rest would force us to confront our nothingness without God. The passions are thus both symptom and cause of the human condition.
