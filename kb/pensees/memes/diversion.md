[[Diversion|Diversion]] (*divertissement*) is one of Pascal's most penetrating and influential concepts.

It names the universal [[human-nature|human]] tendency to flee from self-awareness — from the intolerable recognition of mortality, finitude, and the [[wretchedness|wretchedness]] of the [[human-condition|human condition]] without [[God|God]] — by immersing oneself in activity, amusement, and social distraction.

> [002-1011] Diversion, and be left to consider and be wearied with considering himself.

For Pascal, diversion is not merely a pastime but an existential strategy: it is how human beings make their condition bearable without confronting its ultimate meaninglessness apart from [[God|God]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal develops the concept of diversion in a celebrated section of the *Pensées* that analyses why human beings are incapable of staying quietly in a room.

> [002-1015] Without what is called diversion, he is unhappy, and more unhappy than the least of men.

The argument proceeds by observation: human beings seek constant activity — hunting, gambling, conversation, war, public office — not primarily for the rewards these activities bring but because they cannot bear to be alone with their thoughts.

The restlessness that characterises human existence is, for Pascal, evidence of a structural disorder: human beings were made for something greater than what they can attain in this life, and the inability to rest in created things testifies to their vocation to the uncreated [[God|God]].

> [002-950] Diversion, without study. He then feels his nothingness.

Pascal's analysis connects diversion to the broader theme of [[vanity|vanity]]: all human pursuits, however grand they appear, are ultimately strategies of avoidance. The king surrounded by courtiers, the philosopher absorbed in speculation, the hunter pursuing a hare — all are engaged in the same fundamental operation of fleeing from themselves.

## Key Passages

Pascal's treatment of diversion includes some of the most memorable passages in the *Pensées*:

> [002-985] Comprehend them under diversion.

This brief annotation suggests that diversion is not one phenomenon among others but the category under which most of human activity should be understood.

> [002-1054] If they seek it only as a diversion.

Pascal distinguishes between activities undertaken for their own sake and those undertaken merely as diversion. The distinction is crucial: diversion empties activities of intrinsic value, reducing them to mere instruments of distraction.

> [005-302] In having preferred diversion and hunting to poetry.

Pascal is aware of the hierarchy of human activities — poetry ranks higher than hunting — but his point is that even the highest activities can be degraded into diversion if undertaken for the wrong reasons.

## Analysis

The concept of diversion is one of Pascal's most original contributions to [[philosophy|philosophy]] and has influenced thinkers from [[soren-kierkegaard|Kierkegaard]] to [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]]'s modern interpreters.

Its originality lies in its diagnostic ambition: diversion is not merely a psychological phenomenon but an existential structure. It reveals the [[human-condition|human condition]] as one of fundamental discontent — a discontent that no finite object can satisfy.

Pascal's analysis anticipates several themes of modern [[existentialism|existentialism]]: the flight from authenticity, the role of anxiety in human motivation, and the emptiness of what [[martin-heidegger|Heidegger]] would later call "the they" (*das Man*). Yet Pascal draws a conclusion that most existentialists resist: the restlessness that drives diversion is a *sign* — a negative proof of the reality of [[God|God]].

If human beings were not made for the infinite, they would be satisfied with the finite. The fact that they are not satisfied is evidence that they are made for something more. Diversion is thus, paradoxically, an argument for [[christianity|Christianity]]: it is the form that the desire for God takes in those who do not yet know God.

This makes diversion theologically productive: the apologist's task is not to eliminate diversion but to interpret it — to show that the restlessness which drives it is misdirected desire for the divine.
