Saint Matthew (Matthew the Evangelist) is one of the twelve apostles of Jesus Christ and, by Christian tradition, the author of the [[Gospel of Matthew]]. In Pascal's *Pensées*, Saint Matthew appears in several arguments concerning the historical authority of the Gospels and in comparisons between Christian and Islamic scriptures.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Scriptural Authority

Pascal appeals to Saint Matthew's Gospel as a historically credible document. He notes in the *Pensées* that the Gospel of Matthew is cited by numerous early authorities, establishing its authenticity within the Christian tradition.

> The Gospel is of Saint Matthew, for it is cited by many authorities. (009-050)

### Genealogies and Apparent Contradictions

Pascal addresses the apparent discrepancy between the genealogies of Jesus presented in the Gospels of Matthew and Luke, a common objection raised against the reliability of the New Testament. He treats such difficulties not as refutations but as features that — when properly understood — actually support the Gospels' authenticity, since a forger would have harmonized the accounts rather than preserved divergent traditions.

> For example, the two genealogies in Saint Matthew and Saint Luke. What can be clearer? (008-227)

### Qur'anic Comparisons

In the context of his broader apologetic argument comparing Christianity and Islam, Pascal observes that the Qur'an itself acknowledges Saint Matthew as an honest man. This acknowledgment from a non-Christian source serves, in Pascal's reasoning, as an indirect testimony to Matthew's credibility — even as he uses the point to highlight the Qur'an's lack of prophetic authentication compared to the Christian scriptures.

> The Koran says Saint Matthew was an honest man. There is no mention of Saint Matthew in the Koran; but it speaks of the apostles and prophets as honest men. (009-053)

### The Hidden God

Pascal also cites a passage from Matthew's Gospel (Matthew 11:27 / Matthew 24:36) in the context of his theology of the [[Hidden God]] — the Deus absconditus who reveals himself sufficiently to those who seek but obscures himself from those who resist. The reference to "no one knows the Son except the Father" underscores the limits of natural human knowledge of divine things.

> Nemo novit, etc. — Matthew xi, 27. (002-1075)
