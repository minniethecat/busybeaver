[P01-001] INTRODUCTION

[P01-002] It [[force|might]] seem that about [[blaise-pascal|Blaise Pascal]], and about the two

[P01-003] works on which his fame is founded, everything that there is

[P01-004] to say had been said. The details of his life are as fully known

[P01-005] as we can expect to know them; his mathematical and

[P01-006] physical discoveries have been treated many times; his

[P01-007] religious sentiment and his theological views have been discussed

[P01-008] again and again; and his prose style has been analysed

[P01-009] by French critics down to the finest particular. But Pascal

[P01-010] is one of those writers who will be and who must be studied

[P01-011] afresh by men in every generation. It is not he who changes,

[P01-012] but we who change. It is not our knowledge of him that

[P01-013] increases, but our world that alters and our attitudes towards

[P01-014] it. The history of human opinions of Pascal and of men of

[P01-015] his stature is a part of the history of humanity. That indicates

[P01-016] his permanent importance.

[P01-017] The facts of Pascal's life, so far as they are necessary for

[P01-018] this brief introduction to the Pensées, are as follows. He

[P01-019] was born at Clermont, in Auvergne, in 1623. His family

[P01-020] were people of substance of the upper middle class. His

[P01-021] father was a government official, who was able to leave, when

[P01-022] he died, a sufficient patrimony to his one son and his two

[P01-023] daughters. In 1631 the father moved to Paris, and a few

[P01-024] years later took up another government post at Rouen.

[P01-025] Wherever he lived, the elder Pascal seems to have mingled

[P01-026] with some of the best society, and with men of eminence in

[P01-027] science and the arts. Blaise was educated entirely by his

[P01-028] father at home. He was exceedingly precocious, indeed

[P01-029] excessively precocious, for his application to studies in childhood

[P01-030] and adolescence impaired his health, and is held

[P01-031] responsible for his death at thirty-nine. Prodigious, though

[P01-032] not incredible stories are preserved, especially of his precocity

[P01-033] in mathematics. His [[spirit|mind]] was active rather than accumulative;

[P01-034] he showed from his earliest years that disposition to

[P01-035] find things out for himself, which has characterised the infancy

[P01-036] of Clerk-Maxwell and other scientists. Of his later discoveries

[P01-037] in physics there is no need for mention here; it must only be

[P01-038] remembered that he counts as one of the greatest physicists

[P01-039] and mathematicians of all time; and that his discoveries were

[P01-040] made during the years when most scientists are still apprentices.

[P01-041] The elder Pascal, Étienne, was a sincere Christian. [[About]]

[P01-042] 1646 he fell in with some representatives of the religious

[P01-043] revival within the Church which has become known as

[P01-044] [[jansenism|Jansenism]]—after Jansenius, Bishop of Ypres, whose theological

[P01-045] work is taken as the origin of the movement. This period is

[P01-046] usually spoken of as the moment of Pascal's "first conversion."

[P01-047] The word "conversion," however, is too forcible to be applied

[P01-048] at this point to Blaise Pascal himself. The family had always

[P01-049] been devout, and the younger Pascal, though absorbed in his

[P01-050] scientific work, never seems to have been afflicted with

[P01-051] infidelity. His attention was then directed, certainly, to

[P01-052] religious and theological matters; but the term "conversion"

[P01-053] can only be applied to his sisters—the elder, already Madame

[P01-054] Périer, and particularly the younger, Jacqueline, who at that

[P01-055] time conceived a vocation for the religious life. Pascal himself

[P01-056] was by no means disposed to renounce the world. After the

[P01-057] death of the father in 1650 Jacqueline, a young woman of

[P01-058] remarkable strength and beauty of character, wished to take

[P01-059] her vows as a sister of [[port-royal|Port-Royal]], and for some time her wish

[P01-060] remained unfulfilled owing to the opposition of her brother.

[P01-061] His objection was on the purely worldly ground that she

[P01-062] wished to make over her patrimony to the Order; whereas

[P01-063] while she lived with him, their combined resources made it

[P01-064] possible for him to live more nearly on a scale of expense

[P01-065] congenial to his tastes. He liked, in fact, not only to mix

[P01-066] with the best society, but to keep a coach and horses—six

[P01-067] horses is the number at one time attributed to his carriage.

[P01-068] Though he had no legal power to prevent his sister from

[P01-069] disposing of her property as she elected, the amiable Jacqueline

[P01-070] shrank from doing so without her brother's willing approval.

[P01-071] The Mother Superior, Mère Angélique—herself an eminent

[P01-072] personage in the history of this religious movement—finally

[P01-073] persuaded the young novice to enter the order without the

[P01-074] satisfaction of bringing her patrimony with her; but Jacqueline

[P01-075] remained so distressed by this situation that her brother

[P01-076] finally relented.

[P01-077] So far as is known, the worldly life enjoyed by Pascal during

[P01-078] this period can hardly be qualified as "dissipation," and

[P01-079] certainly not as "debauchery." Even gambling may have

[P01-080] appealed to him chiefly as affording a study of mathematical

[P01-081] probabilities. He appears to have led such a life as any

[P01-082] cultivated intellectual man of good position and independent

[P01-083] means might lead and consider himself a model of probity

[P01-084] and virtue. Not even a love-affair is laid at his door, though

[P01-085] he is said to have contemplated marriage. But Jansenism,

[P01-086] as represented by the religious society of Port-Royal, was

[P01-087] morally a Puritan movement within the Church, and its

[P01-088] standards of conduct were at least as severe as those of any

[P01-089] Puritanism in England or America. The period of fashionable

[P01-090] society, in Pascal's life, is however, of great importance in his

[P01-091] development. It enlarged his knowledge of men and refined

[P01-092] his tastes; he became a man of the world and never lost what

[P01-093] he had learnt; and when he turned his thoughts wholly

[P01-094] towards religion, his worldly knowledge was a part of his

[P01-095] composition which is essential to the value of his work.

[P01-096] Pascal's interest in society did not distract him from

[P01-097] scientific research; nor did this period occupy much space in

[P01-098] what is a very short and crowded life. Partly his natural

[P01-099] dissatisfaction with such a life, once he had learned all it had

[P01-100] to teach him, partly the influence of his saintly sister Jacqueline,

[P01-101] partly increasing suffering as his health declined, directed

[P01-102] him more and more out of the world and to thoughts of eternity.

[P01-103] And in 1654 occurs what is called his "[[conversion|second conversion]],"

[P01-104] but which might be called his conversion simply.

[P01-105] He made a note of his mystical experience, which he kept

[P01-106] always about him, and which was found, after his death,

[P01-107] sewn into the coat which he was wearing. The experience

[P01-108] occurred on 23 November, 1654, and there is no reason to

[P01-109] [[skepticism|doubt]] its genuineness unless we choose to deny all mystical

[P01-110] experience. Now, Pascal was not a mystic, and his works

[P01-111] are not to be classified amongst mystical writings; but what

[P01-112] can only be called mystical experience happens to many men

[P01-113] who do not become mystics. The work which he undertook

[P01-114] soon after, the Lettres écrites à un provincial, is a masterpiece

[P01-115] of religious controversy at the opposite pole from mysticism.

[P01-116] We know quite well that he was at the time when he received

[P01-117] his illumination from [[god|God]] in extremely poor health; but it

[P01-118] is a commonplace that some forms of illness are extremely

[P01-119] favourable, not only to religious illumination, but to artistic

[P01-120] and literary composition. A piece of writing meditated,

[P01-121] apparently without progress, for months or years, may

[P01-122] suddenly take shape and word; and in this state long passages

[P01-123] may be produced which require little or no retouch. I have

[P01-124] no good word to say for the cultivation of automatic writing

[P01-125] as the model of literary composition; I doubt whether these

[P01-126] moments can be cultivated by the writer; but he to whom

[P01-127] this happens assuredly has the sensation of being a vehicle

[P01-128] rather than a maker. No masterpiece can be produced

[P01-129] whole by such means; but neither does even the higher form

[P01-130] of religious inspiration suffice for the religious life; even the

[P01-131] most exalted mystic must return to the world, and use his

[P01-132] reason to employ the results of his experience in daily life.

[P01-133] You may call it communion with the Divine, or you may call

[P01-134] it a temporary crystallisation of the mind. Until science

[P01-135] can teach us to reproduce such phenomena at will, science

[P01-136] cannot claim to have explained them; and they can be judged

[P01-137] only by their fruits.

[P01-138] From that time until his death, Pascal was closely associated

[P01-139] with the society of Port-Royal which his sister Jacqueline, who

[P01-140] predeceased him, had joined as a religieuse; the society was

[P01-141] then fighting for its life against the Jesuits. Five propositions,

[P01-142] judged by a committee of cardinals and theologians

[P01-143] at Rome to be heretical, were found to be put forward in

[P01-144] the work of Jansenius; and the society of Port-Royal, the

[P01-145] representative of Jansenism among devotional communities,

[P01-146] suffered a blow from which it never revived. It is not the

[P01-147] place here to review the bitter controversy and conflict; the

[P01-148] best account, from the point of view of a critic of genius who

[P01-149] took no side, who was neither Jansenist nor Jesuit, Christian

[P01-150] nor infidel, is that in the great book of Sainte-Beuve, Port-Royal.

[P01-151] And in this book the parts devoted to Pascal himself

[P01-152] are among the most brilliant pages of criticism that Sainte-Beuve

[P01-153] ever wrote. It is sufficient to notice that the next

[P01-154] occupation of Pascal, after his conversion, was to write these

[P01-155] eighteen "Letters," which as prose are of capital importance

[P01-156] in the foundation of French classical style, and which as

[P01-157] polemic are surpassed by none, not by Demosthenes, or

[P01-158] Cicero, or Swift. They have the limitation of all polemic and

[P01-159] forensic: they persuade, they seduce, they are unfair. But it

[P01-160] is also unfair to assert that, in these Letters to a Provincial,

[P01-161] Pascal was attacking the Society of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] in itself. He was

[P01-162] attacking rather a particular school of casuistry which relaxed

[P01-163] the requirements of the Confessional; a school which certainly

[P01-164] flourished amongst the Society of Jesus at that time, and of

[P01-165] which the Spaniards Escobar and Molina are the most

[P01-166] eminent authorities. He undoubtedly abused the art of

[P01-167] quotation, as a polemical writer can hardly help but do; but

[P01-168] there were abuses for him to abuse; and he did the job

[P01-169] thoroughly. His Letters must not be called theology.

[P01-170] Academic theology was not a department in which Pascal

[P01-171] was versed; when necessary, the fathers of Port-Royal came

[P01-172] to his aid. The Letters are the work of one of the finest

[P01-173] mathematical minds of any time, and of a man of the world

[P01-174] who addressed, not theologians, but the world in general—all

[P01-175] of the cultivated and many of the less cultivated of the

[P01-176] French laity; and with this public they made an astonishing

[P01-177] success.

[P01-178] During this time Pascal never wholly abandoned his

[P01-179] scientific interests. Though in his religious writings he

[P01-180] composed slowly and painfully, and revised often, in matters

[P01-181] of mathematics his mind seemed to move with consummate

[P01-182] natural ease and grace. Discoveries and inventions sprang

[P01-183] from his brain without effort; among the minor devices of

[P01-184] this later period, the first omnibus service in Paris is said to

[P01-185] owe its origin to his inventiveness. But rapidly failing health,

[P01-186] and absorption in the great work he had in mind, left him

[P01-187] little time and energy during the last two years of his life.

[P01-188] The plan of what we call the Pensées formed itself about

[P01-189] 1660. The completed book was to have been a carefully

[P01-190] constructed defence of Christianity, a true Apology and a

[P01-191] kind of Grammar of Assent, setting forth the reasons which

[P01-192] will convince the intellect. As I have indicated before,

[P01-193] Pascal was not a theologian, and on [[theology|dogmatic theology]] had

[P01-194] recourse to his spiritual advisers. Nor was he indeed a

[P01-195] systematic philosopher. He was a man with an immense

[P01-196] genius for science, and at the same time a natural psychologist

[P01-197] and moralist. As he was a great literary artist, his book

[P01-198] would have been also his own spiritual autobiography; his

[P01-199] style, free from all diminishing idiosyncrasies, was yet very

[P01-200] personal. Above all, he was a man of strong passions; and

[P01-201] his intellectual passion for truth was reinforced by his passionate

[P01-202] dissatisfaction with human life unless a spiritual

[P01-203] explanation could be found.

[P01-204] We must regard the Pensées as merely the first notes for

[P01-205] a work which he left far from completion; we have, in Sainte-Beuve's

[P01-206] words, a tower of which the stones have been laid

[P01-207] on each other, but not cemented, and the structure unfinished.

[P01-208] In early years his memory had been amazingly retentive of

[P01-209] anything that he wished to remember; and had it not been

[P01-210] impaired by increasing illness and pain, he probably would

[P01-211] not have been obliged to set down these notes at all. But

[P01-212] taking the book as it is left to us, we still find that it occupies

[P01-213] a unique place in the history of French literature and in the

[P01-214] history of religious meditation.

[P01-215] To understand the method which Pascal employs, the

[P01-216] reader must be prepared to follow the process of the mind of

[P01-217] the intelligent believer. The Christian thinker—and I mean

[P01-218] the man who is trying consciously and conscientiously to

[P01-219] explain to himself the sequence which culminated in faith,

[P01-220] rather than the public apologist—proceeds by rejection and

[P01-221] elimination. He finds the world to be so and so; he finds its

[P01-222] character inexplicable by any non-religious theory; among

[P01-223] religions he finds Christianity, and Catholic Christianity, to

[P01-224] account most satisfactorily for the world and especially for

[P01-225] the moral world within; and thus, by what Newman calls

[P01-226] "powerful and concurrent" reasons, he finds himself inexorably

[P01-227] committed to the dogma of the [[incarnation|Incarnation]]. To the unbeliever,

[P01-228] this method seems disingenuous and perverse; for

[P01-229] the unbeliever is, as a rule, not so greatly troubled to explain

[P01-230] the world to himself, nor so greatly distressed by its disorder;

[P01-231] nor is he generally concerned (in modern terms) to "preserve

[P01-232] values." He does not consider that if certain emotional

[P01-233] states, certain developments of character, and what in the

[P01-234] highest sense can be called "saintliness" are inherently and

[P01-235] by inspection known to be good, then the satisfactory explanation

[P01-236] of the world must be an explanation which will admit

[P01-237] the "reality" of these values. Nor does he consider such

[P01-238] [[reason|reasoning]] admissible; he would, so to speak, trim his values

[P01-239] according to his cloth, because to him such values are of no

[P01-240] great value. The unbeliever starts from the other end, and

[P01-241] as likely as not with the question: Is a case of human

[P01-242] parthenogenesis credible? and this he would call going straight

[P01-243] to the heart of the matter. Now Pascal's method is, on the

[P01-244] whole, the method natural and right for the Christian; and

[P01-245] the opposite method is that taken by Voltaire. It is worth

[P01-246] while to remember that Voltaire, in his attempt to refute

[P01-247] Pascal, has given once and for all the type of such refutation;

[P01-248] and that later opponents of Pascal's Apology for the Christian

[P01-249] [[faith|Faith]] have contributed little beyond psychological irrelevancies.

[P01-250] For Voltaire has presented, better than any one

[P01-251] since, what is the unbelieving point of view; and in the end

[P01-252] we must all choose for ourselves between one point of view

[P01-253] and another.

[P01-254] I have said above that Pascal's method is "on the whole"

[P01-255] that of the typical Christian apologist; and this reservation

[P01-256] was directed at Pascal's belief in [[miracle|miracles]], which plays a

[P01-257] larger part in his construction than it would in that, at least,

[P01-258] of the modern liberal Catholic. It would seem fantastic to

[P01-259] accept Christianity because we first believe the Gospel

[P01-260] miracles to be true, and it would seem impious to accept it

[P01-261] primarily because we believe more recent miracles to be

[P01-262] true; we accept the miracles, or some miracles, to be true

[P01-263] because we believe the Gospel of Jesus Christ: we found our

[P01-264] belief in the miracles on the Gospel, not our belief in the Gospel

[P01-265] on the miracles. But it must be remembered that Pascal

[P01-266] had been deeply impressed by a contemporary miracle, known

[P01-267] as the miracle of the Holy Thorn: a thorn reputed to have

[P01-268] been preserved from the Crown of Our Lord was pressed upon

[P01-269] an ulcer which quickly healed. Sainte-Beuve, who as a medical

[P01-270] man felt himself on solid ground, discusses fully the possible

[P01-271] explanation of this apparent miracle. It is true that the

[P01-272] miracle happened at Port-Royal, and that it arrived opportunely

[P01-273] to revive the depressed spirits of the community in

[P01-274] its political afflictions; and it is likely that Pascal was the

[P01-275] more inclined to believe a miracle which was performed upon

[P01-276] his beloved sister. In any case, it probably led him to assign

[P01-277] a place to miracles, in his study of faith, which is not quite

[P01-278] that which we should give to them ourselves.

[P01-279] Now the great adversary against whom Pascal set himself,

[P01-280] from the time of his first conversations with M. de Saci at

[P01-281] Port-Royal, was [[montaigne|Montaigne]]. One cannot destroy Pascal,

[P01-282] certainly; but of all authors Montaigne is one of the least

[P01-283] destructible. You could as well dissipate a fog by flinging

[P01-284] hand-grenades into it. For Montaigne is a fog, a gas, a fluid,

[P01-285] insidious element. He does not reason, he insinuates, charms,

[P01-286] and influences; or if he reasons, you must be prepared for his

[P01-287] having some other design upon you than to convince you by

[P01-288] his argument. It is hardly too much to say that Montaigne

[P01-289] is the most essential author to know, if we would understand

[P01-290] the course of French thought during the last three hundred

[P01-291] years. In every way, the influence of Montaigne was

[P01-292] repugnant to the men of Port-Royal. Pascal studied him

[P01-293] with the intention of demolishing him. Yet, in the Pensées,

[P01-294] at the very end of his life, we find passage after passage, and

[P01-295] the slighter they are the more significant, almost "lifted"

[P01-296] out of Montaigne, down to a figure of speech or a word. The

[P01-297] parallels[A] are most often with the long essay of Montaigne

[P01-298] called Apologie de Raymond Sébond—an astonishing piece

[P01-299] of writing upon which Shakespeare also probably drew in

[P01-300] Hamlet. Indeed, by the time a man knew Montaigne well

[P01-301] enough to attack him, he would already be thoroughly infected

[P01-302] by him.

[P01-303] It would, however, be grossly unfair to Pascal, to Montaigne,

[P01-304] and indeed to French literature, to leave the matter at that.

[P01-305] It is no diminution of Pascal, but only an aggrandisement of

[P01-306] Montaigne. Had Montaigne been an ordinary life-sized

[P01-307] sceptic, a small man like Anatole France, or even a greater

[P01-308] man like Renan, or even like the greatest sceptic of all,

[P01-309] Voltaire, this "influence" would be to the discredit of Pascal;

[P01-310] but if Montaigne had been no more than Voltaire, he could

[P01-311] not have affected Pascal at all. The picture of Montaigne

[P01-312] which offers itself first to our eyes, that of the original and

[P01-313] independent solitary "personality," absorbed in amused

[P01-314] analysis of himself, is deceptive. Montaigne's is no limited

[P01-315] Pyrrhonism, like that of Voltaire, Renan, or France. He

[P01-316] exists, so to speak, on a plan of numerous concentric circles,

[P01-317] the most apparent of which is the small inmost circle, a personal

[P01-318] puckish scepticism which can be easily aped if not imitated.

[P01-319] But what makes Montaigne a very great figure is that he

[P01-320] succeeded, God knows how—for Montaigne very likely did

[P01-321] not know that he had done it—it is not the sort of thing that

[P01-322] men can observe about themselves, for it is essentially bigger

[P01-323] than the individual's consciousness—he succeeded in giving

[P01-324] expression to the scepticism of every human being. For

[P01-325] every man who thinks and lives by thought must have his

[P01-326] own scepticism, that which stops at the question, that which

[P01-327] ends in denial, or that which leads to faith and which is

[P01-328] somehow integrated into the faith which transcends it. And

[P01-329] Pascal, as the type of one kind of religious believer, which is

[P01-330] highly passionate and ardent, but passionate only through a

[P01-331] powerful and regulated intellect, is in the first sections of his

[P01-332] unfinished Apology for Christianity facing unflinchingly the

[P01-333] demon of doubt which is inseparable from the spirit of belief.

[P01-334] There is accordingly something quite different from an

[P01-335] influence which would prove Pascal's weakness; there is a

[P01-336] real affinity between his doubt and that of Montaigne; and

[P01-337] through the common kinship with Montaigne Pascal is related

[P01-338] to the noble and distinguished line of French moralists, from

[P01-339] La Rochefoucauld down. In the honesty with which they

[P01-340] face the données of the actual world this French tradition

[P01-341] has a unique quality in European literature, and in the

[P01-342] seventeenth century Hobbes is crude and uncivilised in

[P01-343] comparison.

[P01-344] Pascal is a man of the world among ascetics, and an ascetic

[P01-345] among men of the world; he had the knowledge of worldliness

[P01-346] and the passion of asceticism, and in him the two are fused

[P01-347] into an individual whole. The majority of mankind is

[P01-348] lazy-minded, incurious, absorbed in vanities, and tepid in

[P01-349] emotion, and is therefore incapable of either much doubt

[P01-350] or much faith; and when the ordinary man calls himself a

[P01-351] sceptic or an unbeliever, that is ordinarily a simple pose,

[P01-352] cloaking a disinclination to think anything out to a conclusion.

[P01-353] Pascal's disillusioned analysis of human bondage is sometimes

[P01-354] interpreted to mean that Pascal was really and finally an

[P01-355] unbeliever, who, in his despair, was incapable of enduring

[P01-356] reality and enjoying the heroic satisfaction of the free man's

[P01-357] worship of nothing. His despair, his [[illusion|disillusion]], are, however,

[P01-358] no illustration of personal weakness; they are perfectly

[P01-359] objective, because they are essential moments in the progress

[P01-360] of the [[soul|intellectual soul]]; and for the type of Pascal they are

[P01-361] the analogue of the drought, the dark night, which is an

[P01-362] essential stage in the progress of the Christian mystic. A

[P01-363] similar despair, when it is arrived at by a diseased character

[P01-364] or an impure soul, may issue in the most disastrous consequences

[P01-365] though with the most superb manifestations; and

[P01-366] thus we get Gulliver's Travels; but in Pascal we find no such

[P01-367] distortion; his despair is in itself more terrible than Swift's,

[P01-368] because our heart tells us that it corresponds exactly to the

[P01-369] facts and cannot be dismissed as mental disease; but it was

[P01-370] also a despair which was a necessary prelude to, and element

[P01-371] in, the joy of faith.

[P01-372] I do not wish to enter any further than necessary upon the

[P01-373] question of the heterodoxy of Jansenism; and it is no concern

[P01-374] of this essay, whether the Five Propositions condemned at

[P01-375] Rome were really maintained by Jansenius in his book

[P01-376] Augustinus; or whether we should deplore or approve the

[P01-377] consequent decay (indeed with some persecution) of Port-Royal.

[P01-378] It is impossible to discuss the matter without becoming

[P01-379] involved as a controversialist either for or against Rome.

[P01-380] But in a man of the type of Pascal—and the type always

[P01-381] exists—there is, I think, an ingredient of what may be called

[P01-382] Jansenism of temperament, without identifying it with the

[P01-383] Jansenism of Jansenius and of other devout and sincere, but

[P01-384] not immensely gifted doctors.[B] It is accordingly needful to

[P01-385] state in brief what the dangerous doctrine of Jansenius was,

[P01-386] without advancing too far into theological refinements. It is

[P01-387] recognised in Christian theology—and indeed on a lower plane

[P01-388] it is recognised by all men in affairs of daily life—that freewill

[P01-389] or the natural effort and ability of the individual man,

[P01-390] and also [[grace|supernatural grace]], a gift accorded we know not

[P01-391] quite how, are both required, in co-operation, for salvation.

[P01-392] Though numerous theologians have set their wits at the

[P01-393] problem, it ends in a mystery which we can perceive but not

[P01-394] finally decipher. At least, it is obvious that, like any doctrine,

[P01-395] a slight excess or deviation to one side or the other will precipitate

[P01-396] a heresy. The Pelagians, who were refuted by St.

[P01-397] Augustine, emphasised the efficacy of human effort and

[P01-398] belittled the importance of supernatural grace. The Calvinists

[P01-399] emphasised the degradation of man through [[original-sin|Original Sin]], and

[P01-400] considered mankind so corrupt that the will was of no avail;

[P01-401] and thus fell into the doctrine of predestination. It was upon

[P01-402] the doctrine of grace according to [[saint-augustine|St. Augustine]] that the

[P01-403] Jansenists relied; and the Augustinus of Jansenius was

[P01-404] presented as a sound exposition of the Augustinian views.

[P01-405] Such heresies are never antiquated, because they forever

[P01-406] assume new forms. For instance, the insistence upon good

[P01-407] works and "service" which is preached from many quarters,

[P01-408] or the simple faith that any one who lives a good and useful

[P01-409] life need have no "morbid" anxieties about salvation, is a

[P01-410] form of Pelagianism. On the other hand, one sometimes

[P01-411] hears enounced the view that it will make no real difference

[P01-412] if all the traditional religious sanctions for moral behaviour

[P01-413] break down, because those who are born and bred to be nice

[P01-414] people will always prefer to behave nicely, and those who are

[P01-415] not will behave otherwise in any case: and this is surely a form

[P01-416] of predestination—for the hazard of being born a nice person

[P01-417] or not is as uncertain as the gift of grace.

[P01-418] It is likely that Pascal was attracted as much by the fruits

[P01-419] of Jansenism in the life of Port-Royal as by the doctrine

[P01-420] itself. This devout, ascetic, thoroughgoing society, striving

[P01-421] heroically in the midst of a relaxed and easy-going Christianity,

[P01-422] was formed to attract a nature so concentrated, so passionate,

[P01-423] and so thoroughgoing as Pascal's. But the insistence upon

[P01-424] the degraded and helpless state of man, in Jansenism, is

[P01-425] something also to which we must be grateful, for to it we owe

[P01-426] the magnificent analysis of human motives and occupations

[P01-427] which was to have constituted the early part of his book.

[P01-428] And apart from the Jansenism which is the work of a not

[P01-429] very eminent bishop who wrote a Latin treatise which is now

[P01-430] unread, there is also, so to speak, a Jansenism of the individual

[P01-431] biography. A moment of Jansenism may naturally take

[P01-432] place, and take place rightly, in the individual; particularly

[P01-433] in the life of a man of great and intense intellectual powers,

[P01-434] who cannot avoid seeing through human beings and observing

[P01-435] the vanity of their thoughts and of their avocations, their

[P01-436] dishonesty and self-deceptions, the insincerity of their emotions,

[P01-437] their cowardice, the pettiness of their real ambitions.

[P01-438] Actually, considering that Pascal died at the age of thirty-nine,

[P01-439] one must be amazed at the balance and justice of his observations;

[P01-440] much greater maturity is required for these qualities,

[P01-441] than for any mathematical or scientific greatness. How

[P01-442] easily his brooding on the [[wretchedness|misery]] of man without God might

[P01-443] have encouraged in him the sin of [[pride|spiritual pride]], the concupiscence

[P01-444] de l'[[mind|esprit]], and how fast a hold he has of humility!

[P01-445] And although Pascal brings to his work the same powers

[P01-446] which he exerted in science, it is not as a scientist that he

[P01-447] presents himself. He does not seem to say to the reader:

[P01-448] I am one of the most distinguished scientists of the day; I

[P01-449] understand many matters which will always be mysteries to

[P01-450] you, and through science I have come to the Faith; you therefore

[P01-451] who are not initiated into science ought to have faith if I

[P01-452] have it. He is fully aware of the difference of subject-matter;

[P01-453] and his famous distinction between the [[mathematics|esprit de géométrie]]

[P01-454] and the esprit de finesse is one to ponder over. It is the [[justice|just]]

[P01-455] combination of the scientist, the honnête homme, and the

[P01-456] religious nature with a passionate craving for God, that makes

[P01-457] Pascal unique. He succeeds where [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] fails; for in

[P01-458] Descartes the element of esprit de géométrie is excessive.[C]

[P01-459] And in a few phrases about Descartes, in the present book,

[P01-460] Pascal laid his finger on the place of weakness.

[P01-461] He who reads this book will observe at once its fragmentary

[P01-462] nature; but only after some study will perceive that the

[P01-463] fragmentariness lies in the expression more than in the thought.

[P01-464] The "thoughts" cannot be detached from each other and

[P01-465] quoted as if each were complete in itself. Le cœur a ses

[P01-466] raisons que la raison ne connaît point: how often one has heard

[P01-467] that quoted, and quoted often to the wrong purpose! For this

[P01-468] is by no means an exaltation of the "heart" over the "head,"

[P01-469] a defence of unreason. The heart, in Pascal's terminology,

[P01-470] is itself truly rational if it is truly the heart. For him, in

[P01-471] theological matters, which seemed to him much larger, more

[P01-472] difficult, and more important than scientific matters, the whole

[P01-473] personality is involved.

[P01-474] We cannot quite understand any of the parts, fragmentary

[P01-475] as they are, without some understanding of the whole.

[P01-476] Capital, for instance, is his analysis of the three orders: the

[P01-477] order of nature, the order of mind, and the [[charity|order of charity]].

[P01-478] These three are discontinuous; the higher is not implicit in the

[P01-479] lower as in an evolutionary doctrine it would be.[D] In this

[P01-480] distinction Pascal offers much about which the modern world

[P01-481] would do well to think. And indeed, because of his unique

[P01-482] combination and balance of qualities, I know of no religious

[P01-483] writer more pertinent to our time. The great mystics like

[P01-484] St. John of the [[cross|Cross]], are primarily for readers with a special

[P01-485] determination of purpose; the devotional writers, such as St.

[P01-486] François de Sales, are primarily for those who already feel

[P01-487] consciously desirous of the love of God; the great theologians

[P01-488] are for those interested in theology. But I can think of no

[P01-489] Christian writer, not Newman even, more to be commended

[P01-490] than Pascal to those who doubt, but who have the mind to

[P01-491] conceive, and the sensibility to feel, the disorder, the futility,

[P01-492] the meaninglessness, the mystery of life and suffering, and who

[P01-493] can only find peace through a satisfaction of the whole being.

[P01-494] T. S. Eliot.
