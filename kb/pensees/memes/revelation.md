## Definition

**Revelation** in Pascal's [[Pensées]] is God's act of making himself known to humanity — through Scripture, [[Miracle|miracles]], and [[Prophecy]] — without which human reason cannot arrive at knowledge of [[God]] or of the human condition (007-262). Revelation is the epistemological foundation of [[Faith]], sharply distinguished from the "God of the philosophers" accessible through unaided reason.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal draws a sharp boundary between what can be known through reason and what requires revelation. He argues that apart from faith and revelation, human principles are limited to what nature affords; the knowledge of God and of humanity's fallen condition exceeds natural reason (007-262). Revelation, not philosophical argument, is the source of authentic knowledge about God.

The content of revelation centers on [[Jesus Christ]]. Pascal describes Christ as "light for revelation to the Gentiles" (*Lumen ad revelationem gentium*, Luke 2:32), a phrase he repeatedly invokes (002-1075, 012-313). The revelation of God in Christ is the central mystery of the Christian faith — a revelation that confounds human expectations of power and wisdom.

Pascal's apologetic argument makes revelation historically verifiable. He identifies "two great revelations": first, that all things happened to the Jewish people in figures, and second, the fulfillment of those figures in [[Jesus Christ]] (010-190). The coherence of prophecy and fulfillment constitutes evidence that revelation is genuine, not a human invention.

The character of divine revelation stands in marked contrast to merely human claims to knowledge. Pascal notes that true revelation produces humility in its recipients and in the Church that transmits it (007-637). False revelations proliferate only because true revelation exists as their measure — the counterfeits testify to the reality of the genuine article (013-134, 013-137).

Revelation is also depicted as the disclosure of hidden things. The coming of the Messiah was a secret "until the revelation of this same God" (011-449). Pascal treats the hiddenness of divine truth as itself part of God's revelatory strategy — a pattern of concealment and disclosure that characterizes the entire biblical narrative. [[Mystery]] is the natural companion of revelation: what is revealed remains mysterious in its content even as it becomes accessible through faith.

## Key Passages

> It is in vain, O men, that you seek within yourselves the remedy for your miseries. All your knowledge can only show you that it is not in yourselves that you will find either the true or the good. The philosophers promised them to you and have been unable to provide them. (007-262)

> Two great revelations are these. (1) All things happened to the Jews in figures. (2) The fulfillment of the figures in Jesus Christ. (010-190)

> *Lumen ad revelationem gentium* — A light for revelation to the Gentiles. (012-313, citing Luke 2:32)

> Abraham and Gideon are above revelation. The Jews, in their blindness, were beneath it. (013-182)

## Analysis

Revelation in Pascal's thought occupies the epistemological space between the inadequacy of unaided reason and the necessity of faith. It is not the abolition of [[Reason]] but its fulfillment and surpassing. Pascal's treatment of revelation is structurally Christocentric — revelation is not an abstract communication of propositions but the person of [[Jesus Christ]], who unites in himself the divine and the human.

The link between revelation and the figural interpretation of Scripture is distinctive to Pascal's apologetics. Revelation operates through a hermeneutic of concealment and disclosure: the Old Testament figures are simultaneously hidden from those who read literally and manifest to those who read through faith in Christ. This figural hermeneutic makes revelation both universally available (through the publicly accessible text of Scripture) and epistemologically discriminating (only faith perceives its true meaning).

Pascal's emphasis on revelation sharply distinguishes his apologetics from the rationalist theology of his contemporaries. The God revealed in Christ is not the God of the philosophers but the "God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob." Revelation in Pascal serves the same function as [[Grace]] in his soteriology: it supplies what human nature cannot achieve on its own. The concepts of revelation and [[Mystery]] are complementary — revelation makes accessible what remains, in its essence, mysterious.
