Joseph the patriarch occupies a central place in Pascal's typological reading of the Old Testament. For Pascal, the details of Joseph's life are not merely historical but prophetic: they prefigure the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. This figural method is essential to Pascal's apologetics, demonstrating that the Old Testament is not a collection of arbitrary narratives but a coherent, divinely orchestrated pattern pointing toward Christ.

## Joseph as Type of Christ

The typological identification is explicit: "Jesus Christ typified by Joseph, the beloved of his father" (012-262). Just as Jacob loved Joseph above his brothers, so the Father loves the Son. The parallelism structures Pascal's reading: every detail of Joseph's story finds its fulfillment in Christ.

## Innocent Between Two Criminals

"In prison Joseph innocent between two criminals" (012-268) — the parallel with Christ crucified between two thieves is unmistakable. Joseph's unjust imprisonment becomes a figural anticipation of the Passion: "Joseph foretells only; Jesus Christ accomplishes" (012-272). The distinction between foretelling and accomplishing is crucial to Pascal's typology: the Old Testament figures are shadows; Christ is the substance.

## The Cry for Remembrance

"Joseph asks him who will be saved to remember him" (012-273) — echoing the good thief's plea on the cross. Joseph's request to the cupbearer, like the thief's to Christ, is a plea for remembrance in the hour of deliverance. The figural pattern reveals a divine coherence beneath the surface of history.

## Internal Wisdom, External Law

"Joseph so internal in a law so external" (011-047) — Pascal reads Joseph's governance of Egypt as a type of Christ's spiritual kingdom. While the Mosaic law was external and ceremonial, Joseph's wisdom was internal — anticipating the interiority of the New Covenant.

## The Dying Blessing

Joseph's final words carry typological weight. "This same Joseph, when dying, bade his children carry his bones" (011-160) — the bones of Joseph, carried out of Egypt at the Exodus, become a sign of resurrection hope. "Upon Joseph's representing to him" the coming deliverance (011-153), Joseph's faith in God's promise prefigures Christian faith in Christ's return.
