[[Original Sin|Original sin]], in Pascal's [[theology|theological]] anthropology, is the condition of inherited fallenness that afflicts every [[human-nature|human being]] from birth.

The doctrine holds that [[adam|Adam]]'s first disobedience — narrated in Genesis 3 — did not merely affect Adam alone but corrupted [[human-nature|human nature]] itself, so that all subsequent generations are born in a state of alienation from [[God|God]], incapable of loving God or neighbour without the assistance of [[grace|divine grace]] (P01-044).

For Pascal, original sin is the indispensable explanatory principle without which the paradoxes of the [[human-condition|human condition]] remain unintelligible.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's treatment of original sin is remarkable for its epistemological ambition: he does not merely assert the doctrine but argues that it alone makes sense of human experience.

> [007-478] Of original sin. Ample tradition of original sin.

The [[human-condition|human condition]] as Pascal describes it — simultaneously great and wretched, capable of infinite aspiration yet mired in pettiness and [[vanity|vanity]] — is, he argues, inexplicable without original sin.

Any anthropology that denies the Fall must either exaggerate human greatness (by ignoring the evidence of corruption) or exaggerate human wretchedness (by ignoring the evidence of residual dignity). Only original sin accounts for both.

> [003-502] That original sin should be, and that it should be transmitted.

The doctrine of transmission — that sin is inherited, not merely imitated — is essential to Pascal's argument. If each person fell individually, the universality of the condition would be a coincidence requiring no special explanation. The doctrine of original sin asserts that the condition is not coincidental but systemic: it belongs to what it means to be human after the Fall.

Pascal places original sin at the foundation of [[christianity|Christian]] apologetics. Without it, the need for a Redeemer vanishes; with it, the entire structure of [[christianity|Christian]] doctrine — [[incarnation|Incarnation]], [[atonement|Atonement]], [[grace|grace]], [[salvation|salvation]] — follows as the divine response to a genuinely desperate situation.

> [007-637] Without the Scripture, without original sin, without a necessary Mediator.

This terse fragment captures Pascal's logical chain: Scripture reveals original sin, original sin reveals the need for a Mediator, and [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] is that Mediator.

## Key Passages

The *Pensées* contain several concentrated reflections on original sin:

> [002-006] First part: That nature is corrupt. Proved by nature itself.

Pascal's apologetic strategy begins not with Scripture but with the observable facts of [[human-nature|human nature]] — the contradiction between greatness and misery that only original sin can explain.

> [002-1075] Of original sin. — The citations from the Rabbis.

Pascal was aware of Jewish traditions concerning the Fall and sought to show that the doctrine was not an arbitrary Christian invention but had antecedents in the Hebrew Scriptures and their interpretation.

## Analysis

Pascal's originality in treating original sin lies not in the doctrine itself — which he inherited from [[saint-augustine|Augustine]] via [[jansenism|Jansenism]] — but in his method of approach.

Rather than beginning with biblical authority and deducing the human condition from it, Pascal begins with the observable human condition and argues that original sin is the only hypothesis that renders it intelligible. This is the method of [[apologetics|apologetics]] rather than dogmatic theology: the argument is addressed to the sceptic who does not yet accept the authority of Scripture.

The strategic brilliance of this approach is that it turns the sceptic's own experience against him. The sceptic who denies original sin must give an alternative account of the [[human-condition|human condition]] — an account that explains both the aspiration to transcendence and the persistent failure to achieve it. Pascal's wager-like challenge is implicit: if you have a better explanation, produce it.

> [012-137] First teaches the Trinity, original sin, the Messiah.

Pascal sees original sin as one of the three essential doctrines that Scripture progressively reveals.

The doctrine of original sin also has profound pastoral implications. If fallen humanity is radically dependent on [[grace|grace]], then the appropriate posture before [[God|God]] is one of humility and supplication, not self-reliance. Pascal's [[jansenism|Jansenist]] formation reinforced this emphasis on human helplessness and divine gratuity.
