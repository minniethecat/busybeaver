[[Plato]] appears in [[Blaise Pascal|Pascal's]] *Pensées* as the pinnacle of [[philosophy|philosophical]] achievement — and, precisely for that reason, as the most telling demonstration of philosophy's inherent limits. Pascal does not dismiss Plato as worthless; rather, he deploys Plato's career as evidence that the highest reach of natural [[reason]] still falls short of what [[Christianity]] accomplishes through [[revelation]].

Unlike the [[prophets]] and [[apostles]], who speak with [[divine]] authority, Plato speaks only with human wisdom. Pascal's treatment of Plato thus serves his larger [[apologetics|apologetic]] project: if even Plato — the best that unaided reason can produce — could neither discover the deepest truths nor persuade more than a handful of followers, then the achievements of [[Jesus Christ]] and the [[Church]] cannot be explained as merely human phenomena.

## Philosophy's Insufficiency

Pascal's most direct judgment on Plato appears in the context of his broader argument about the limits of philosophical [[wisdom]]. The [[philosophers]], for all their brilliance, have not succeeded in moving the human heart.

> [012-280] has been useless. Sages, like Plato and Socrates, have not been able to persuade them.

This verdict is not merely dismissive. Pascal grants Plato genuine insight — he was, after all, the philosopher who most nearly approached [[Christian]] truth. Yet nearness is not arrival. Plato could perceive something of what humanity needs, but he could not supply it. The gap between Plato's [[understanding]] and his [[power]] to effect change is, for Pascal, the gap between philosophy and [[faith]].

The same pattern appears in Pascal's observation about the limits of philosophical influence. Plato labored to instruct a small circle of disciples through careful argument and long discipline; Christianity transformed millions through a few words spoken to ordinary people.

> [011-661] their pleasures. What Plato could only make acceptable to a few men, specially chosen and instructed, a secret influence imparted, by the power of a few words, to a hundred million ignorant men.

This contrast between the slow, narrow reach of philosophical education and the rapid, universal reach of [[divine]] power is central to Pascal's apologetic case. The [[Gospel]] does what philosophy cannot — and what it cannot do, by its very nature, it should not be expected to do.

## Plato and Christianity

Pascal's most intriguing remark about Plato is also his briefest — a fragment suggesting that Plato himself, had he lived to encounter the Christian revelation, might have recognized it as the fulfillment of what he sought.

> [003-421] Plato, to incline to Christianity.

The fragment is tantalizingly incomplete. Pascal seems to be suggesting that Plato's philosophy, properly understood, points toward Christianity — that the Platonic trajectory, if followed to its logical conclusion, leads to the recognition that the truths philosophy gropes after are fully realized only in [[Christ]]. This is not to say that Plato was a crypto-Christian but that the questions Plato raised — about the [[sovereign good]], the [[immortality]] of the soul, the relationship between the temporal and the eternal — receive their definitive answer only in revelation.

In this respect Plato occupies a unique position among the [[philosophers]] in Pascal's thought. Aristotle is treated with greater distance; the [[Stoics]] and [[Epicureans]] are largely ignored or dismissed. Plato alone is granted the status of a genuine precursor — close enough to the truth to demonstrate both the proximity and the insufficiency of natural reason.

## The Philosopher as Honest Man

Pascal's most human portrait of Plato comes in a passage that demystifies the image of the philosopher as a remote sage. Plato and Aristotle, Pascal insists, were not the solemn figures their academic robes suggest.

> [005-386] We can only think of Plato and Aristotle in grand academic robes. They were honest men, like others, laughing with their friends, and when they diverted themselves with writing their Laws and the Politics, they did it as an amusement. That part of their life was the least philosophic and the least serious; the most philosophic was to live simply and quietly.

This passage serves multiple purposes in Pascal's argument. First, it undermines the [[authority]] that philosophical systems claim by virtue of their authors' prestige — if Plato wrote the *Laws* as an amusement, why should we treat it as a definitive guide to human life? Second, it advances Pascal's broader case that the true [[philosophy|philosophical]] life consists not in constructing systems but in confronting the [[human condition]] honestly. The "simple and quiet" life that Pascal praises in Plato and Aristotle anticipates his own ideal of the [[Christian]] life — not the life of public disputation but the life of inward [[conversion]].

At the same time, Pascal's refusal to caricature Plato as a villain — "they were honest men, like others" — strengthens his argument. If even honest and intelligent men, pursuing philosophy seriously, cannot reach the truth, then the problem lies not in their character or effort but in the inherent limitations of the philosophical project itself.

## Plato and Justice

Pascal's earliest reference to Plato, in the very first section of the *Pensées*, invokes him in the context of a discussion about the nature of [[justice]].

> [001-237] injustice," as Plato, or anything else? But there, you will say, everything is contained in one word. Yes, but it is useless without explanation, and when we come to explain it, as soon as we unfold this maxim which contains all the rest, they emerge in that first confusion which you desired to avoid.

The reference is to Plato's *Republic*, where [[justice]] is defined — and then shown to be far more complex than any simple formula can capture. Pascal's point is that even the most elegant philosophical definition of justice unravels when one attempts to apply it. This is not a criticism of Plato specifically but of the philosophical method in general: the attempt to capture complex realities in single principles inevitably fails, because reality resists such simplification.

This epistemological modesty — the recognition that [[human]] concepts cannot adequately grasp [[divine]] or even natural realities — is, for Pascal, one of the marks of genuine wisdom. Plato, at his best, understood this. But understanding the limits of reason is not the same as transcending them; for that, [[grace]] is required.
