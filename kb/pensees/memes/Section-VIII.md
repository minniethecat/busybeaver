[008-001] SECTION VIII

[008-002] THE FUNDAMENTALS OF THE CHRISTIAN RELIGION

[008-003] 555

[008-004] ... Men blaspheme what they do not know. The Christian

[008-005] religion consists in two points. It is of equal concern to men to

[008-006] know them, and it is equally dangerous to be ignorant to them.

[008-007] And it is equally of [[god|God]]'s mercy that He has given indications

[008-008] of both.

[008-009] And yet they take occasion to conclude that one of these

[008-010] points does not exist, from that which should have caused them

[008-011] to infer the other. The sages who have said there is only one

[008-012] God have been persecuted, the Jews were hated, and still more

[008-013] the Christians. They have seen by the light of nature that if

[008-014] there be a true religion on earth, the course of all things must

[008-015] tend to it as to a centre.

[008-016] The whole course of things must have for its object the

[008-017] establishment and the greatness of religion. Men must have

[008-018] within them feelings suited to what religion teaches us. And,

[008-019] finally, religion must so be the object and centre to which all

[008-020] things tend, that whoever knows the principles of religion can

[008-021] give an explanation both of the whole [[human-nature|nature of man]] in particular,

[008-022] and of the whole course of the world in general.

[008-023] And on this ground they take occasion to revile the Christian

[008-024] religion, because they misunderstand it. They imagine that it

[008-025] consists simply in the worship of a God considered as great,

[008-026] powerful, and [[eternity|eternal]]; which is strictly deism, almost as far

[008-027] removed from the [[christianity|Christian religion]] as atheism, which is its

[008-028] exact opposite. And thence they conclude that this religion is

[008-029] not true, because they do not see that all things concur to the

[008-030] establishment of this point, that God does not manifest Himself

[008-031] to men with all the [[evidence]] which He could show.

[008-032] But let them conclude what they will against deism, they will

[008-033] conclude nothing against the Christian religion, which properly

[008-034] consists in the mystery of the Redeemer, who, uniting in Himself

[008-035] the two natures, human and divine, has redeemed men from the

[008-036] corruption of sin in order to reconcile them in His divine person

[008-037] to God.

[008-038] The Christian religion, then, teaches men these two [[truth|truths]];

[008-039] that there is a God whom men can know, and that there is a

[008-040] corruption in their nature which renders them unworthy of Him.

[008-041] It is equally important to men to know both these points; and

[008-042] it is equally dangerous for man to know God without [[knowledge|knowing]]

[008-043] his own wretchedness, and to know his own wretchedness without

[008-044] knowing the Redeemer who can free him from it. The

[008-045] knowledge of only one of these points gives rise either to the

[008-046] pride of [[philosophy|philosophers]], who have known God, and not their own

[008-047] wretchedness, or to the despair of atheists, who know their own

[008-048] wretchedness, but not the Redeemer.

[008-049] And, as it is alike necessary to man to know these two points,

[008-050] so is it alike merciful of God to have made us know them. The

[008-051] Christian religion does this; it is in this that it consists.

[008-052] Let us herein examine the order of the world, and see if all

[008-053] things do not tend to establish these two chief points of this

[008-054] religion: [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] is the end of all, and the centre to which

[008-055] all tends. Whoever knows Him knows the reason of everything.

[008-056] Those who fall into error err only through failure to see one of

[008-057] these two things. We can then have an excellent knowledge of

[008-058] God without that of our own wretchedness, and of our own

[008-059] wretchedness without that of God. But we cannot know Jesus

[008-060] Christ without knowing at the same time both God and our own

[008-061] wretchedness.

[008-062] Therefore I shall not undertake here to prove by natural

[008-063] reasons either the [[being|existence]] of God, or the Trinity, or the immortality

[008-064] of the soul, or anything of that nature; not only

[008-065] because I should not feel myself sufficiently able to find in nature

[008-066] arguments to convince hardened atheists, but also because such

[008-067] knowledge without Jesus Christ is useless and barren. Though

[008-068] a man should be convinced that numerical proportions are

[008-069] immaterial truths, eternal and dependent on a first truth, in

[008-070] which they subsist, and which is called God, I should not think

[008-071] him far advanced towards his own salvation.

[008-072] The God of Christians is not a God who is simply the author

[008-073] of mathematical truths, or of the order of the elements; that is

[008-074] the view of heathens and Epicureans. He is not merely a God

[008-075] who exercises His providence over the life and fortunes of men,

[008-076] to bestow on those who worship Him a long and happy life.

[008-077] That was the portion of the Jews. But the God of [[abraham|Abraham]],

[008-078] the God of Isaac, the God of [[jacob|Jacob]], the God of Christians, is a

[008-079] God of love and of comfort, a God who fills the soul and heart of

[008-080] those whom He possesses, a God who makes them conscious of

[008-081] their inward wretchedness, and His infinite mercy, who unites

[008-082] Himself to their inmost soul, who fills it with humility and joy,

[008-083] with confidence and love, who renders them incapable of any

[008-084] other end than Himself.

[008-085] All who seek God without Jesus Christ, and who rest in nature,

[008-086] either find no light to satisfy them, or come to form for themselves

[008-087] a means of knowing God and serving Him without a mediator.

[008-088] Thereby they fall either into atheism, or into deism, two things

[008-089] which the Christian religion abhors almost equally.

[008-090] Without Jesus Christ the world would not exist; for it should

[008-091] needs be either that it would be destroyed or be a hell.

[008-092] If the world existed to instruct man of God, His divinity

[008-093] would shine through every part in it in an indisputable manner;

[008-094] but as it exists only by Jesus Christ, and for Jesus Christ, and

[008-095] to teach men both their corruption and their redemption, all

[008-096] displays the proofs of these two truths.

[008-097] All appearance indicates neither a total exclusion nor a

[008-098] manifest presence of divinity, but the presence of a God who

[008-099] hides Himself. Everything bears this character.

[008-100] ... Shall he alone who knows his nature know it only to be

[008-101] miserable? Shall he alone who knows it be alone unhappy?

[008-102] ... He must not see nothing at all, nor must he see sufficient

[008-103] for him to believe he possesses it; but he must see enough to

[008-104] know that he has lost it. For to know of his loss, he must see

[008-105] and not see; and that is exactly the state in which he naturally is.

[008-106] ... Whatever part he takes, I shall not leave him at rest ...

[008-107] 556

[008-108] ... It is then true that everything teaches man his condition,

[008-109] but he must understand this well. For it is not true that all

[008-110] reveals God, and it is not true that all conceals God. But it is

[008-111] at the same time true that He hides Himself from those who

[008-112] tempt Him, and that He reveals Himself to those who seek Him,

[008-113] because men are both unworthy and capable of God; unworthy

[008-114] by their corruption capable by their original nature.

[008-115] 557

[008-116] What shall we conclude from all our darkness, but our

[008-117] unworthiness?

[008-118] 558

[008-119] If there never had been any appearance of God, this eternal

[008-120] deprivation would have been equivocal, and [[force|might]] have as well

[008-121] corresponded with the absence of all divinity, as with the

[008-122] unworthiness of men to know Him; but His occasional, though

[008-123] not continual, appearances remove the ambiguity, If He

[008-124] appeared once, He exists always; and thus we cannot but

[008-125] conclude both that there is a God, and that men are unworthy

[008-126] of Him.

[008-127] 559

[008-128] We do not understand the glorious state of [[adam|Adam]], nor the

[008-129] nature of his sin, nor the transmission of it to us. These are

[008-130] matters which took place under conditions of a nature altogether

[008-131] different from our own, and which transcend our present understanding.

[008-132] The knowledge of all this is useless to us as a means of escape

[008-133] from it; and all that we are concerned to know, is that we are

[008-134] miserable, corrupt, separated from God, but ransomed by

[008-135] Jesus Christ, whereof we have wonderful proofs on earth.

[008-136] So the two proofs of corruption and redemption are drawn

[008-137] from the ungodly, who live in indifference to religion, and from

[008-138] the Jews who are irreconcilable enemies.

[008-139] 560

[008-140] There are two ways of proving the truths of our religion; one

[008-141] by the power of reason, the other by the authority of him who

[008-142] speaks.

[008-143] We do not make use of the latter, but of the former. We

[008-144] do not say, "This must be believed, for [[scripture|Scripture]], which says

[008-145] it, is divine." But we say that it must be believed for such and

[008-146] such a reason, which are feeble arguments, as reason may be

[008-147] bent to everything.

[008-148] 561

[008-149] There is nothing on earth that does not show either the

[008-150] wretchedness of man, or the mercy of God; either the weakness

[008-151] of man without God, or the strength of man with God.

[008-152] 562

[008-153] It will be one of the confusions of the damned to see that

[008-154] they are condemned by their own reason, by which they claimed

[008-155] to condemn the Christian religion.

[008-156] 563

[008-157] The [[prophecy|prophecies]], the very [[miracle|miracles]] and proofs of our religion,

[008-158] are not of such a nature that they can be said to be absolutely

[008-159] convincing. But they are also of such a kind that it cannot be

[008-160] said that it is unreasonable to believe them. Thus there is

[008-161] both evidence and obscurity to enlighten some and confuse

[008-162] others. But the evidence is such that it surpasses, or at least

[008-163] equals, the evidence to the contrary; so that it is not reason

[008-164] which can determine men not to follow it, and thus it can only be

[008-165] lust or malice of heart. And by this means there is sufficient

[008-166] evidence to condemn, and insufficient to convince; so that it

[008-167] appears in those who follow it, that it is grace, and not reason,

[008-168] which makes them follow it; and in those who shun it, that it is

[008-169] lust, not reason, which makes them shun it.

[008-170] Vere discipuli, vere Israëlita, vere liberi, vere cibus.[207]

[008-171] 564

[008-172] Recognise, then, the truth of religion in the very obscurity

[008-173] of religion, in the little light we have of it, and in the indifference

[008-174] which we have to knowing it.

[008-175] 565

[008-176] We understand nothing of the works of God, if we do not

[008-177] take as a principle that He has willed to blind some, and enlighten

[008-178] others.

[008-179] 566

[008-180] The two contrary reasons. We must begin with that;

[008-181] without that we understand nothing, and all is heretical; and

[008-182] we must even add at the end of each truth that the opposite

[008-183] truth is to be remembered.

[008-184] 567

[008-185] Objection. The Scripture is plainly full of matters not

[008-186] dictated by the Holy [[spirit|Spirit]].—Answer. Then they do not harm

[008-187] faith.—Objection. But the Church has decided that all is of the

[008-188] Holy Spirit.—Answer. I answer two things: first, the Church

[008-189] has not so decided; secondly, if she should so decide, it could

[008-190] be maintained.

[008-191] Do you think that the prophecies cited in the Gospel are

[008-192] related to make you believe? No, it is to keep you from believing.

[008-193] 568

[008-194] Canonical.—The heretical books in the beginning of the

[008-195] Church serve to prove the canonical.

[008-196] 569

[008-197] To the chapter on the Fundamentals must be added that on

[008-198] Typology touching the reason of types: why Jesus Christ was

[008-199] prophesied as to His first coming; why prophesied obscurely as

[008-200] to the manner.

[008-201] 570

[008-202] The reason why. Types.—[They had to deal with a carnal

[008-203] people and to render them the depositary of the spiritual

[008-204] covenant.] To give faith to the Messiah, it was necessary there

[008-205] should have been precedent prophecies, and that these should be

[008-206] conveyed by persons above suspicion, diligent, faithful, unusually

[008-207] zealous, and known to all the world.

[008-208] To accomplish all this, God chose this carnal people, to whom

[008-209] He entrusted the prophecies which foretell the Messiah as a

[008-210] deliverer, and as a dispenser of those carnal goods which this

[008-211] people loved. And thus they have had an extraordinary passion

[008-212] for their prophets, and, in sight of the whole world, have had

[008-213] charge of these books which foretell their Messiah, assuring all

[008-214] nations that He should come, and in the way foretold in the

[008-215] books, which they held open to the whole world. Yet this

[008-216] people, deceived by the poor and ignominious advent of the

[008-217] Messiah, have been His most cruel enemies. So that they, the

[008-218] people least open to suspicion in the world of favouring us, the

[008-219] most strict and most zealous that can be named for their law

[008-220] and their prophets, have kept the books incorrupt. Hence

[008-221] those who have rejected and crucified Jesus Christ, who has

[008-222] been to them an offence, are those who have charge of the books

[008-223] which testify of Him, and state that He will be an offence and

[008-224] rejected. Therefore they have shown it was He by rejecting

[008-225] Him, and He has been alike proved both by the righteous Jews

[008-226] who received Him, and by the unrighteous who rejected Him,

[008-227] both facts having been foretold.

Wherefore the prophecies have a hidden and spiritual meaning,

to which this people were hostile, under the carnal meaning which

they loved. If the spiritual meaning had been revealed, they

would not have loved it, and, unable to bear it, they would not

have been zealous of the preservation of their books and their

ceremonies; and if they had loved these spiritual promises, and

had preserved them incorrupt till the time of the Messiah, their

testimony would have had no force, because they had been his

friends.

Therefore it was well that the spiritual meaning should be concealed;

but, on the other hand, if this meaning had been so hidden

as not to appear at all, it could not have served as a proof of the

Messiah. What then was done? In a crowd of passages it has

been hidden under the temporal meaning, and in a few has

been clearly revealed; besides that the time and the state of the

world have been so clearly foretold that it is clearer than the

sun. And in some places this spiritual meaning is so clearly

expressed, that it would require a blindness like that which the

flesh imposes on the spirit when it is subdued by it, not to

recognise it.

See, then, what has been the prudence of God. This meaning

is concealed under another in an infinite number of passages,

and in some, though rarely, it is revealed; but yet so that the

passages in which it is concealed are equivocal, and can suit

both meanings; whereas the passages where it is disclosed are

unequivocal, and can only suit the spiritual meaning.

So that this cannot lead us into error, and could only be

misunderstood by so carnal a people.

For when blessings are promised in abundance, what was

to prevent them from understanding the true blessings, but

their covetousness, which limited the meaning to worldly goods?

But those whose only good was in God referred them to

God alone. For there are two principles, which divide the

wills of men, covetousness and charity. Not that covetousness

cannot exist along with faith in God, nor charity with worldly

riches; but covetousness uses God, and enjoys the world, and

charity is the opposite.

Now the ultimate end gives names to things. All which

prevents us from attaining it, is called an enemy to us. Thus

the creatures, however good, are the enemies of the righteous,

when they turn them away from God, and God Himself is the

enemy of those whose covetousness He confounds.

Thus as the significance of the word "enemy" is dependent

on the ultimate end, the righteous understood by it their passions,

and the carnal the Babylonians; and so these terms were obscure

only for the unrighteous. And this is what [[isaiah|Isaiah]] says: Signa

legem in electis meis,[208] and that Jesus Christ shall be a stone of

stumbling. But, "Blessed are they who shall not be offended

in him." [[hosea|Hosea]],[209] ult., says excellently, "Where is the wise?

and he shall understand what I say. The righteous shall know

them, for the ways of God are right; but the transgressors shall

fall therein."

572

[[blindness|Blindness]] of Scripture.—"The Scripture," said the Jews,

"says that we shall not know whence Christ will come (John vii,

27, and xii, 34). The Scripture says that Christ abideth for ever,

and He said that He should die." Therefore, says [[saint-john|Saint John]],[210]

they believed not, though He had done so many miracles, that the

word of Isaiah might be fulfilled: "He hath blinded them," etc.

573

[[greatness|Greatness]].—Religion is so great a thing that it is right that

those who will not take the trouble to seek it, if it be obscure,

should be deprived of it. Why, then, do any complain, if it be

such as can be found by seeking?

574

All things work together for good to the elect, even the

obscurities of Scripture; for they honour them because of what

is divinely clear. And all things work together for evil to the

rest of the world, even what is clear; for they revile such, because

of the obscurities which they do not understand.

575

The general conduct of the world towards the Church: God

willing to blind and to enlighten.—The event having proved the

divinity of these prophecies, the rest ought to be believed. And

thereby we see the order of the world to be of this kind. The

miracles of the Creation and the Deluge being forgotten, God

sends the law and the miracles of [[moses|Moses]], the prophets who

prophesied particular things; and to prepare a lasting miracle,

He prepares prophecies and their fulfilment; but, as the

prophecies could be suspected, He desires to make them above

suspicion, etc.

576

God has made the blindness of this people subservient to

the good of the elect.

577

There is sufficient clearness to enlighten the elect, and

sufficient obscurity to humble them. There is sufficient

obscurity to blind the reprobate, and sufficient clearness to

condemn them, and make them inexcusable.—[[saint-augustine|Saint Augustine]],

[[montaigne|Montaigne]], Sébond.

The genealogy of Jesus Christ in the [[old-testament|Old Testament]] is intermingled

with so many others that are useless, that it cannot be

distinguished. If Moses had kept only the record of the ancestors

of Christ, that might have been too plain. If he had not noted

that of Jesus Christ, it might not have been sufficiently plain.

But, after all, whoever looks closely sees that of Jesus Christ

expressly traced through Tamar,[211] Ruth,[212] etc.

Those who ordained these sacrifices, knew their uselessness;

those who have declared their uselessness, have not ceased to

practise them.

If God had permitted only one religion, it had been too easily

known; but when we look at it closely, we clearly discern the

truth amidst this confusion.

The premiss.—Moses was a clever man. If, then, he ruled

himself by his reason, he would say nothing clearly which was

directly against reason.

Thus all the very apparent weaknesses are strength. Example;

the two genealogies in [[saint-matthew|Saint Matthew]] and Saint Luke. What can

be clearer than that this was not concerted?

578

God (and the Apostles), foreseeing that the seeds of pride

would make heresies spring up, and being unwilling to give them

occasion to arise from correct expressions, has put in Scripture

and the prayers of the Church contrary words and sentences

to produce their fruit in time.

So in morals He gives charity, which produces fruits contrary

to lust.

579

[[nature|Nature]] has some perfections to show that she is the image

of God, and some defects to show that she is only His image.

580

God prefers rather to incline the will than the intellect.

Perfect clearness would be of use to the intellect, and would

harm the will. To humble pride.

581

We make an idol of truth itself; for truth apart from charity

is not God, but His image and idol, which we must neither love

nor worship; and still less must we love or worship its opposite,

namely, falsehood.

I can easily love total darkness; but if God keeps me in a

state of semi-darkness, such partial darkness displeases me, and,

because I do not see therein the advantage of total darkness,

it is unpleasant to me. This is a fault, and a sign that I make

for myself an idol of darkness, apart from the order of God.

Now only His order must be worshipped.

582

The feeble-minded are people who know the truth, but only

affirm it so far as consistent with their own interest. But,

apart from that, they renounce it.

583

The world exists for the exercise of mercy and judgment,

not as if men were placed in it out of the hands of God, but as

hostile to God; and to them He grants by grace sufficient light,

that they may return to Him, if they desire to seek and follow

Him; and also that they may be punished, if they refuse to

seek or follow Him.

584

That God has willed to hide Himself.—If there were only one

religion, God would indeed be manifest. The same would be

the case, if there were no martyrs but in our religion.

God being thus hidden, every religion which does not affirm

that God is hidden, is not true; and every religion which does

not give the reason of it, is not instructive. Our religion does,

all this: Vere tu es Deus absconditus.

585

If there were no obscurity, man would not be sensible of his

corruption; if there were no light, man would not hope for a

remedy. Thus, it is not only fair, but advantageous to us, that

God be partly hidden and partly revealed; since it is equally

dangerous to man to know God without knowing his own

wretchedness, and to know his own wretchedness without

knowing God.

586

This religion, so great in miracles, saints, blameless Fathers,

learned and great witnesses, martyrs, established kings as

[[king-david|David]], and Isaiah, a prince of the blood, and so great in science,

after having displayed all her miracles and all her wisdom,

rejects all this, and declares that she has neither wisdom nor

signs, but only the cross and foolishness.

For those, who, by these signs and that wisdom, have deserved

your [[faith|belief]], and who have proved to you their character, declare

to you that nothing of all this can change you, and render you

capable of knowing and loving God, but the power of the foolishness

of the cross without wisdom and signs, and not the signs

without this power. Thus our religion is foolish in respect to

the effective cause, and wise in respect to the wisdom which

prepares it.

587

Our religion is wise and foolish. Wise, because it is the

most learned, and the most founded on miracles, prophecies, etc.

Foolish, because it is not all this which makes us belong to it.

This makes us indeed condemn those who do not belong to it;

but it does not cause belief in those who do belong to it. It is

the cross that makes them believe, ne evacuata sit crux. And

so [[saint-paul|Saint Paul]], who came with wisdom and signs, says that he

has come neither with wisdom nor with signs; for he came to

convert. But those who come only to convince, can say that

they come with wisdom and with signs.
