[[Humility|Humility]], in Pascal's [[jansenism|Jansenist]] moral psychology, is the virtue that answers [[pride|pride]]. It is not the low self-esteem of the [[psychology|psychologist]] but the theological posture of the [[creature|creature]] before the [[creator|Creator]] — the condition for receiving [[grace|grace]].

> (P01-444) and how fast a hold he has of humility!

Pascal's exclamation points to the [[paradox|paradox]] of humility: it is the virtue one cannot acquire by trying, because the effort to "be humble" is itself an act of [[pride|pride]]. Humility arrives as a gift or not at all — which is why Pascal's [[theology|theology]] of humility is inseparable from his [[theology|theology]] of [[grace|grace]].

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Humility as Gift, Not Achievement

The Pascalian grammar of humility is passive: one does not make oneself humble; one *receives* humility. This distinguishes Pascal from both [[stoicism|Stoic]] self-mastery and modern self-improvement:

> (004-410) anctity, the dignity, and the humility of a

Humility is linked here with sanctity and dignity — not as their opposite but as their condition. The [[saint|saint]] is humble not by abasing himself but by seeing himself truthfully, which is to see himself in relation to [[god|God]].

### The Perversion of Humility

Pascal's most penetrating insight into humility is its susceptibility to perversion:

> (006-137) on humility are a source of pride in the vain, and of humility

Exhortations to humility can produce [[pride|pride]] in the [[vanity|vain]] — a [[paradox|paradox]] that Pascal states without resolving. The [[vain|vain]] man, hearing himself praised for humility, becomes proud of his humility. This is the serpent that eats its own tail: the [[will|will]] to be humble is pride wearing a mask.

This insight places Pascal within the [[augustinian|Augustinian]] tradition of [[self-love|amour-propre]] analysis. The [[self|self]] is so curved toward itself that even its attempts at self-transcendence are self-referential. Only [[grace|grace]] can break this circle.

### Humility as Remedy

In Pascal's soteriological framework, humility is specifically the remedy for [[pride|pride]]:

> (007-637) and lust; and the remedies, humility and mortification.

The pairing is significant: humility and mortification are the remedies for [[concupiscence|concupiscence]] and [[lust|lust]]. Humility answers the [[soul|soul]]'s rebellion against [[god|God]]; mortification answers the [[body|body]]'s rebellion against the [[soul|soul]]. Together they constitute the ascetic dimension of Pascal's [[christianity|Christianity]].

The word "remedies" is precise: [[pride|pride]] is not a [[crime|crime]] to be punished but an illness to be cured. Pascal's [[jansenism|Jansenism]] is therapeutic, not juridical — at least in its anthropology if not its soteriology.

### Humility and Joy

Pascal refuses to oppose humility to joy:

> (008-082) nmost soul, who fills it with humility and joy,

[[Humility|Humility]] and [[joy|joy]] coexist in the [[soul|soul]] filled by [[god|God]]. This is the Pascalian counterpoint to the [[world|world]]'s assumption that humility is gloomy self-hatred. True humility — the humility of the [[saint|saint]], not the [[neurotic|neurotic]] — is inseparable from the [[joy|joy]] of being known and loved by [[god|God]].

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of humility is organised around a tension: humility is both the condition for receiving [[grace|grace]] and the fruit of having received it. One cannot become humble without grace, but grace is not given to the [[pride|proud]]. This circularity is not a logical defect but Pascal's way of insisting that humility belongs entirely to the order of [[charity|charity]], not the order of [[mind|mind]].

The dialectical treatment of humility and [[pride|pride]] — the insight that exhortations to humility can produce pride — places Pascal among the great moral psychologists. He sees that the [[self|self]] is not a neutral substance that can adopt virtues at will but a [[wretchedness|wretched]] and [[greatness|great]] agent whose every movement toward the [[good|good]] is ambivalent.

Humility, for Pascal, is ultimately christological: it is the form of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own existence, the kenosis that the [[incarnation|Incarnation]] both reveals and enables. To be humble is to participate in the [[mind|mind]] of [[christ|Christ]] — a participation that is itself a work of [[grace|grace]].
