## Definition

In Pascal's epistemology, **dogmatism** (dogmatisme) is the philosophical position that claims **certain knowledge through reason alone**. It is one of two symmetrical errors in the human search for truth, opposed to [[Skepticism|scepticism]] (Pyrrhonism). Pascal's distinctive contribution is to show that both positions are simultaneously refuted and confirmed: "Nature confutes the sceptics, and reason confutes the dogmatists" (007-326). The dogmatist errs not in affirming that truth exists but in claiming that unaided reason can grasp it with certainty.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Two Symmetrical Errors

Pascal frames dogmatism and scepticism as mirror errors, each containing a partial truth. The dogmatist is right that there are natural principles we cannot genuinely doubt; the sceptic is right that our origin is uncertain and our cognitive faculties may be unreliable:

> (007-298) I notice the only strong point of the dogmatists, namely, that, speaking in good faith and sincerely, we cannot doubt natural principles.

> (007-300) Against this the sceptics set up in one word the uncertainty of our origin, which includes that of our nature.

> (007-302) The dogmatists have been trying to answer this objection ever since the world began.

The argument has reached an impasse. Each side has grasped something true, but neither can overcome the other's objection. This stalemate is not accidental — it is the necessary condition of human [[Knowledge]], which exists between the two extremes of absolute certainty and absolute doubt.

### Pascal's Resolution: Incapacity and Invincibility

Pascal resolves the stalemate not by choosing a side but by redescribing the human epistemological situation. Reason cannot prove its own first principles (this is the sceptic's insight), yet we cannot genuinely doubt them (this is the dogmatist's insight):

> (002-326) We have an incapacity of proof, insurmountable by all dogmatism. We have an idea of truth, invincible to all scepticism.

This is a characteristically Pascalian move. Rather than treating dogmatism and scepticism as theoretical options to be chosen between, he treats them as permanent features of the human condition. The dogmatist's error is the refusal to acknowledge the limits of [[Reason]]; the sceptic's error is the refusal to acknowledge the force of nature and instinct. [[Truth]] lies in holding both together.

### Nature Confutes the Sceptics, Reason Confutes the Dogmatists

Pascal's most compressed formulation of the dialectic appears in what is perhaps his most famous epistemological passage:

> (007-326) Who will unravel this tangle? Nature confutes the sceptics, and reason confutes the dogmatists.

This is not a rhetorical question. For Pascal, only Christian revelation can "unravel this tangle." Philosophy cannot resolve the dogmatism-scepticism debate on its own terms. The dogmatist is confuted by reason's inability to ground itself; the sceptic is confuted by nature's irresistible imposition of belief. Both confutations are valid, and both are partial. The human being is suspended between them — capable of neither absolute knowledge nor absolute doubt.

### Shallow Dogmatism and Its Refutation

Pascal distinguishes between sophisticated dogmatism (which recognizes the force of natural principles) and shallow dogmatism (which claims certainty on insufficient grounds). Shallow dogmatism is particularly vulnerable to sceptical attack:

> (007-294) Those who dogmatise only on shallow foundations, they are upset by the least breath of the sceptics.

This is a warning about intellectual hubris. Dogmatists who build on "shallow foundations" — unexamined assumptions, cultural prejudice, the appearance of consensus — are easily overturned once sceptical scrutiny is applied. The proper response is not to abandon all foundations but to recognize which foundations are genuinely indubitable and which are merely unquestioned.

### No Neutrality in the Conflict

Pascal insists that intellectual neutrality is impossible. The conflict between dogmatism and scepticism is existential, not merely academic:

> (007-305) So there is open war among men, in which each must take a part, and side either with dogmatism or scepticism. For he who thinks to remain neutral is above all a sceptic.

This connects dogmatism to Pascal's apologetic strategy. The person who claims to suspend judgment on the question of [[God]] has in fact already chosen scepticism. Indifference is not a third option. The dogmatist who affirms truth but refuses to seek its source is in no better position than the sceptic who denies truth altogether. Both evade the existential demand that the search for truth imposes.

### Dogmatism and the Search for God

Pascal draws out the theological implication: dogmatists (like sceptics) fail to lead the seeker to God:

> (002-185) And then to make people seek Him among the philosophers, sceptics, and dogmatists, who disquiet him who inquires of them.

Philosophy — whether dogmatic or sceptical — cannot satisfy the human hunger for truth. Both schools "disquiet" the inquirer by raising questions they cannot answer. Only Christian [[Revelation]], which addresses both the greatness and the misery of the human condition, can resolve the epistemological impasse. The dogmatist's claim to comprehensive knowledge and the sceptic's counsel of perpetual doubt are both evasions of the God who "disposes all things kindly" by putting "religion into the mind by reason, and into the [[Heart]] by grace."

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of dogmatism is inseparable from his treatment of scepticism. The two errors are not merely opposed — they are structurally dependent. Dogmatism without the sceptical critique becomes shallow and ungrounded; scepticism without the dogmatic affirmation of truth becomes self-refuting. Pascal's genius is to see that this mutual refutation is not a failure of philosophy but a revelation of the human condition.

The epistemological stalemate has apologetic significance. If human reason were sufficient to ground certain knowledge, we would not need revelation; if human reason were incapable of any knowledge, we could not recognize revelation when it came. Pascal's middle position — reason is limited but not useless, truth is attainable but not by reason alone — creates the space for [[Faith]] as a rational act that exceeds reason's competence.

This connects to Pascal's broader critique of philosophy ([[Pascal's Critique of Philosophy]]). Dogmatism is the philosophical sin in its pure form: the claim to know what cannot be known on the terms claimed. But scepticism is not the cure — it is the mirror image of the same error, asserting the impossibility of knowledge with a certainty that contradicts its own premise. Both must be transcended, not resolved.
