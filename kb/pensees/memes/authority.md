[[Authority|Authority]], in Pascal's analysis, is the fundamental but fragile ground of social and intellectual order — necessary for human life yet perpetually vulnerable to [[usurpation|usurpation]] and [[confusion|confusion]].

> [005-051] Justice to be the authority of the legislator; another, the interest of the sovereign.

Pascal's taxonomy of the grounds on which [[justice|justice]] is claimed reveals his method: authority is not one thing but many, each proper to its own domain, and the confusion of domains is the source of both theoretical error and practical disorder.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Domains of Authority

Pascal distinguishes between three domains in which authority operates, corresponding roughly to the [[three-orders|three orders]]:

In the order of the [[body|body]] (the [[state|state]], [[law|law]], social institutions), authority rests ultimately on [[force|force]]: "Justice without force is powerless." But force must be clothed in the language of justice, or it becomes mere tyranny.

In the order of the [[mind|mind]] ([[science|science]], [[philosophy|philosophy]], [[reason|reasoning]]), authority rests on demonstration. But this authority is strictly limited: reason cannot authoritatively pronounce on matters that exceed its competence.

In the order of [[charity|charity]] ([[faith-and-reason|faith]], [[theology|theology]], the [[church|Church]]), authority rests on [[revelation|revelation]]. But this authority, being invisible to those who lack the organ for perceiving it, can never be imposed — only proposed.

> [001-186] Persuades by sweetness, not by authority.

The contrast between persuasion "by sweetness" and "by authority" is central to Pascal's understanding of how [[christianity|Christianity]] must be communicated. Divine authority does not operate as human authority does — by compulsion. It persuades, attracts, invites.

### Authority and Truth

> [005-333] Authority apart from truth. Thus they obtain authority.

Authority that is "apart from [[truth|truth]]" is the characteristic corruption of all human institutions. Those who possess authority — whether political, intellectual, or religious — are tempted to separate it from the truth that alone justifies it. The result is authority that commands obedience without deserving it.

> [005-056] Of its authority; whoever carries it back to its first principle, annihilates it.

This is one of Pascal's most radical claims: human authority, traced back to its origin, reveals its own groundlessness. The "first principle" of political authority is not [[reason|reason]] but [[force|force]] or [[custom|custom]] — and to expose this is to "annihilate" the authority. This is why societies surround authority with [[mystery|mystery]] and [[ceremony|ceremony]].

> [005-066] To point out their want of authority and justice. We must then go back to the fundamental laws.

Pascal recognises that the exposure of authority's groundlessness is socially dangerous — hence the [[conservatism|conservative]] strain in his thought: "We must not see the truth because it is hidden." Yet the truth must also be told, because [[christianity|Christianity]] is the truth.

### Authority and Custom

Pascal's treatment of authority is inseparable from his treatment of [[custom|custom]]. Custom is what stabilises authority when reason cannot — and reason almost never can. "Custom is our nature" means that the authority we take to be natural is almost always the sedimentation of repeated practice.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of authority bridges his [[political-philosophy|political philosophy]] and his [[apologetics|apologetics]].

Politically, Pascal is a realist: authority is necessary, and its foundations are almost never rational. The attempt to ground authority in reason alone leads to anarchy; the attempt to ground it in force alone leads to tyranny. The only stable solution is custom — the "second nature" that makes authority habitual rather than contested.

Apologetically, Pascal distinguishes between the authority proper to human institutions (always fragile, always contestable) and the authority proper to divine revelation (absolute, but operating by attraction rather than compulsion). The confusion of these two authorities — claiming divine authority for human institutions, or reducing divine authority to human reason — is the source of both [[idolatry|idolatry]] and [[unbelief|unbelief]].
