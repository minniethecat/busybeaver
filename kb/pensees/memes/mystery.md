## Definition

**Mystery** (*mystère*) in Pascal's [[Pensées]] is not mere ignorance or an unsolved problem but a positive theological-epistemological category. Mystery is the mode in which divine truth presents itself to human understanding — exceeding [[Reason]] without contradicting it, and recognized as meaningful by [[Faith]] even when it cannot be fully comprehended. Pascal speaks of "the mystery furthest from our knowledge" (007-344) and insists that certain truths are "inconceivable without this mystery" (007-360).

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's use of mystery centers on the person and work of [[Jesus Christ]]. The mystery of the Redeemer unites in Himself the two natures — divine and human — in a way that reason cannot fully penetrate. "The whole of faith consists in the mystery of the Redeemer, who, uniting the two natures, divine and human, has withdrawn men from the corruption of sin to reconcile them to God in his divine person" (008-034). This mystery is not a problem to be solved but the foundation on which Christian faith rests.

The hiddenness of [[God]] (*Deus absconditus*) is itself a dimension of mystery. Pascal describes the mystery of Jesus — His hidden life, His suffering, His death — as a pattern of divine self-concealment that is itself revelatory (007-344). God's choice to reveal Himself through hiddenness, weakness, and suffering rather than through unambiguous displays of power is, for Pascal, the central mystery of the Christian faith.

Pascal insists that mystery is inescapable — not only for the believer but for the unbeliever as well. The human condition itself is mysterious: the disproportion between human greatness and [[Wretchedness]], the simultaneous capacity for truth and [[Error]], the experience of a desire for meaning that the world cannot satisfy. Pascal treats these as pointers toward the theological mystery of the fall and redemption.

The apologetic function of mystery is crucial. Pascal argues that a religion without mystery would be suspicious — it would be a religion tailored to human reason rather than a [[Revelation]] from the transcendent God. The presence of mystery in Christianity is evidence of its divine origin. The [[Prophecy|prophecies]] and [[Miracle|miracles]] attest to the divine origin of a revelation whose content necessarily exceeds human comprehension.

Pascal draws on the biblical language of mystery as a hidden plan now disclosed. The Messiah was a "mystery" hidden for ages and now "accomplished" (012-083, 012-085). Mystery in this sense is temporal: what was once hidden is now revealed, yet what is revealed remains mysterious in its content. The [[Cross]] is the supreme instance — a mystery of apparent defeat that is actually victory.

## Key Passages

> It is an astonishing thing that the mystery furthest from our knowledge, that of the transmission of original sin, is a thing without which we can have no knowledge of ourselves. (007-344)

> It is inconceivable without this mystery that man should be so great and so miserable. (007-360)

> The whole of faith consists in the mystery of the Redeemer, who, uniting the two natures, divine and human, has withdrawn men from the corruption of sin. (008-034)

> Without mystery, the most incomprehensible of all things would be that there should be no mystery. (007-357)

> The Redeemer, of whom Joseph and the Messiah are figures, accomplished the mystery in his person. (012-083–012-085)

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of mystery is a sophisticated theological-epistemological tool. It allows him to maintain the transcendence of divine truth while simultaneously arguing for its accessibility through [[Revelation]] and [[Faith]]. Mystery is not the failure of [[Reason]] but the recognition of its legitimate limits — an echo of Pascal's famous dictum that "the last step of reason is to recognize that there is an infinity of things which surpass it."

The connection between mystery and self-knowledge is distinctively Pascalian. The mystery of [[Original Sin]] is "furthest from our knowledge" yet absolutely necessary for understanding ourselves (007-344). Without this mystery, Pascal argues, the human condition — [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness and misery]] coexisting in the same being — is inexplicable. Mystery thus has explanatory power: it makes sense of what would otherwise be nonsense.

This apologetic use of mystery inverts the typical Enlightenment critique. Where rationalist critics of Christianity treated mystery as a weakness (evidence that the religion was irrational), Pascal treats mystery as a strength (evidence that the religion transcends human reason and therefore comes from God). The argument depends on Pascal's broader case for the divine authority of Scripture through [[Prophecy]] and [[Miracle]] — once the revelatory status of the biblical text is established, the presence of mysteries within it confirms rather than undermines its authority.

Mystery in Pascal is inseparable from the [[Cross]] and the pattern of divine action through apparent weakness. The hidden God who reveals Himself in humility and suffering is the ultimate mystery — a mystery that faith embraces as wisdom while reason dismisses it as foolishness. The concept of mystery also complements [[Revelation]]: what revelation discloses remains, in its essence, mysterious, since the finite cannot comprehend the infinite.
