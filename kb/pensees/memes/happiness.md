## Definition

**Happiness** (*bonheur*) in Pascal's [[Pensées]] is the universal object of human desire — every person, without exception, seeks happiness as the motive of all actions (007-006). Yet for Pascal, true happiness is impossible apart from [[God]], making the pursuit of happiness both the central fact of human psychology and the central argument for the necessity of faith.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's treatment of happiness is inseparable from his analysis of [[Diversion]] and the [[Wretchedness]] of the human condition. He observes that all men seek happiness as the goal of every action — even suicide is undertaken in pursuit of what the person imagines will end suffering and bring peace (007-006). This universality makes the desire for happiness a structural feature of human nature, not a contingent preference.

Yet Pascal argues that no one can find true and lasting happiness in this world. The human condition is marked by a contradiction: an insatiable desire for happiness coupled with an incapacity to achieve it through any earthly means. All apparent happiness is fleeting and fragile (002-1012). The things men pursue — wealth, power, knowledge, pleasure — prove incapable of satisfying the infinite desire for happiness, which Pascal traces to humanity's original state before the fall.

This failure points toward a theological conclusion. There was once in man a "true happiness" of which only the empty form remains (007-026). The present inability to find happiness is evidence of the fall and of humanity's need for redemption. True happiness lies in being with God: "our true happiness is to be in Him" (007-076).

Pascal's [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]] argument directly engages the question of happiness. He frames the decision for faith in terms of happiness: "your knowledge and your happiness" are at stake (003-582). The Wager asks the reader to weigh the possibility of "an eternity of life and happiness" against finite earthly goods (003-597). Without God and immortality, there is "no more happiness" (003-124).

## Key Passages

> All men seek happiness. This is without exception. Whatever different means they use, they all tend to this end. (007-006)

> They have a secret instinct which leads them to seek amusement and occupation from without, and which arises from the sense of their continual wretchedness. And they have another secret instinct, a remnant of the greatness of our first nature, which teaches them that happiness in reality consists only in rest. (002-994)

> There was once in man a true happiness of which there now remains to him only the mark and empty trace. (007-026)

> Let us weigh the gain and the loss, taking God's existence as a wager. ... If you gain, you gain all; if you lose, you lose nothing. Wager then without hesitation that He is. ... there is an eternity of life and happiness. (003-586–003-597)

## Analysis

Pascal's analysis of happiness functions on three levels. Psychologically, it provides a diagnostic tool: the universal pursuit of happiness explains the otherwise inexplicable behavior of human beings, including the restless pursuit of [[Diversion]] and the fear of being alone with oneself. Theologically, it functions as an argument for the fall and the necessity of [[Grace]] — the desire for infinite happiness that no finite good can satisfy is a "remnant of greatness" pointing toward humanity's original nature and supernatural destiny. Apologetically, it drives the logic of the [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]], which is fundamentally an argument about the rational pursuit of happiness under uncertainty.

The concept of happiness connects systematically to Pascal's treatment of the [[Sovereign Good]], the nature of [[Self-Love]] (*amour-propre*), the psychology of [[Diversion]], and the [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness-and-misery]] dialectic. Pascal's originality lies in making the universal desire for happiness not a premise for hedonism but an apologetic argument: the failure to find happiness without God is evidence for God.
