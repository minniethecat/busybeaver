## Definition

In Pascal's theological anthropology, **blindness** (aveuglement) is the **subjective incapacity** of fallen humanity to perceive divine truth. Unlike [[Darkness]], which describes the objective obscurity of God's self-revelation (*Deus absconditus*), blindness describes the *human side* of the epistemological rupture: the willed ignorance, the active flight from truth, the self-imposed incapacity to see what is there to be seen. Where darkness is God's concealment, blindness is man's refusal to look. Pascal insists that "it is an extraordinary blindness to live without investigating what we are" (007-637) — the blindness is culpable precisely because the means of investigation are available.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Supernatural Character of Blindness

Pascal regards the blindness of unbelievers as something that exceeds natural explanation:

> (002-1075) There is something supernatural in such a blindness.

The adjective *supernatural* is precise and important. Mere ignorance would be natural — a finite creature cannot be expected to know everything. But the blindness Pascal observes in those who reject [[Christianity in Pascal's Pensées|Christianity]] goes beyond ignorance. It is an active resistance to evidence, a determination not to see. This resistance has a supernatural cause: the disorder introduced into human nature by [[Original Sin]]. The blindness is not merely a lack but a positive distortion of the cognitive faculties.

### The Existential Indictment

Pascal's most rhetorically powerful deployment of the blindness charge comes in the Apology's direct address to the unbeliever:

> (007-637) If it is an extraordinary blindness to live without investigating what we are, it is a terrible one to live wickedly while believing in God.

The key move here is the escalation: not investigating the human condition is already "extraordinary blindness," but believing in God and yet living wickedly is "terrible." Pascal uses the concept of blindness as a diagnostic tool that distinguishes two classes of spiritual failure: the intellectual failure of the person who refuses to examine the evidence, and the moral failure of the nominal believer whose life contradicts his profession. Both are forms of blindness, but the second is the more grievous.

### Blindness and the Hidden God

Blindness is the subjective correlate of the hiddenness of [[God]]. God veils Himself, but humanity's incapacity to perceive Him is not solely God's doing:

> (011-004) When I see the blindness and the wretchedness of man, when I regard the whole silent universe, and man without light, left to himself, and, as it were, lost in this corner of the universe, without knowing who has put him there, what he has come to do, what will become of him at death, and incapable of any knowledge, I become terrified.

This passage links blindness, [[Wretchedness]], and the cosmic condition of man. The "silent universe" is the objective pole (darkness); man's "incapability of any knowledge" is the subjective pole (blindness). Together they produce the terror that Pascal experiences and seeks to communicate to his reader. The terror is the beginning of the cure: one must feel the blindness before one can seek the light.

### The Apologetic Strategy Against Blindness

Pascal's apologetics is calibrated to the condition of blindness. He does not simply present more arguments, because the problem is not a lack of information:

> (003-278) The way of God, which disposes all things with gentleness, is to put religion into the mind by reasons, and into the heart by grace. But to will to put it into the mind and heart by force and threats is to put there not religion but terror.

The blindness cannot be cured by force. Neither rational demonstration alone ("reasons") nor fear alone ("terror") will open eyes that are closed by the will. The cure must involve both the mind (arguments that show the credibility of [[Faith]]) and the [[Heart]] (grace that moves the affections). This is why Pascal's apologetics oscillates between rational proofs and existential appeals — neither alone can cure the blindness.

### Blindness Foretold and Providential

Pascal finds the theme of blindness woven through biblical prophecy, which confirms for him its providential role:

> (012-024) Their blindness is foretold.

> (011-664) God would strike them with blindness, and in full noon they would grope as in the dark.

The Old Testament prophecies of blindness — particularly Isaiah's commission to "make the heart of this people fat, and their ears heavy, and shut their eyes" (Isaiah 6:10) — provide Pascal with a framework for understanding the continued unbelief of the Jews in the face of fulfilled prophecy. Blindness is not an accident of history but part of the divine plan: it preserves the freedom of the response to revelation, and it serves as a sign that the truth of Christianity is not the kind of truth that compels assent mechanically.

### The Blindness of Those Who Do Not Seek

Pascal distinguishes between two kinds of non-believers: those who seek and those who do not. The second group is the true object of the blindness charge:

> (003-345) They do not know whether there is a God or not. So they are in the wrong, since their error is not a lack of knowledge but a lack of will to know.

This is the heart of Pascal's analysis. The blindness for which the unbeliever is culpable is not the mere absence of knowledge — that could be innocent — but the *refusal to seek*. The seeker, even in his darkness, is in a different moral category from the one who refuses to investigate. The Wager argument is addressed precisely to this latter group: it aims to show that the refusal to investigate is irrational on its own terms, because the stakes are infinite.

## Analysis

Pascal's concept of blindness is the subjective counterpart to his concept of [[Darkness]]. The two form a coupled pair that maps the noetic effects of the Fall: darkness is the *objective* obscurity of a God who hides; blindness is the *subjective* incapacity of a humanity that refuses to see. Pascal's apologetic strategy depends on maintaining the tension between these two poles. If darkness were total, no one could be blamed for not seeing. If blindness were complete, no amount of evidence would matter. It is precisely because "there is too much to deny and too little to be sure" (the darkness pole) that the refusal to investigate becomes culpable (the blindness pole).

This coupling has an important pastoral implication. The cure for blindness is not simply apologetic argumentation — the blindness is in the will, not merely in the intellect. Pascal therefore prescribes the embodied practice of faith as the remedy: "bend the automaton." The body, through [[Habit]] and practice, can retrain the will that the intellect alone cannot reach. This is why Pascal's apologetics culminates not in a syllogism but in an exhortation to act: attend Mass, take holy water, live *as if* you believed. The blindness of the will yields only to the discipline of the body.

Blindness also serves an important structural function in Pascal's reading of salvation history. The blindness of the Jews to the fulfillment of prophecy in [[Jesus Christ]] is not a refutation of Christianity but a confirmation of it — the prophets foretold the blindness as well as the event. The blindness of the modern unbeliever is the same phenomenon: it is not evidence against the truth of Christianity but evidence of the accuracy of its diagnosis of the human condition. Christianity claims that humanity is in a state of spiritual blindness; the fact that many find Christianity unbelievable is, for Pascal, itself a confirmation of the claim.
