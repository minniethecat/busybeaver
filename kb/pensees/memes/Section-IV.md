[004-001] SECTION IV

[004-002] OF THE MEANS OF BELIEF

[004-003] 242

[004-004] Preface to the second part.—To speak of those who have

[004-005] treated of this matter.

[004-006] I admire the boldness with which these persons undertake to

[004-007] speak of [[god|God]]. In addressing their argument to infidels, their

[004-008] first chapter is to prove Divinity from the works of nature.[91] I

[004-009] should not be astonished at their enterprise, if they were

[004-010] addressing their argument to the faithful; for it is certain that

[004-011] those who have the living faith in their heart see at once that

[004-012] all [[being|existence]] is none other than the work of the God whom they

[004-013] adore. But for those in whom this light is extinguished, and in

[004-014] whom we purpose to rekindle it, persons destitute of faith and

[004-015] grace, who, seeking with all their light whatever they see in

[004-016] nature that can bring them to this knowledge, find only [[evidence|obscurity]]

[004-017] and darkness; to tell them that they have only to look at the

[004-018] smallest things which surround them, and they will see God

[004-019] openly, to give them, as a complete proof of this great and

[004-020] important matter, the course of the moon and planets, and to

[004-021] claim to have concluded the proof with such an argument, is to

[004-022] give them ground for believing that the proofs of our religion

[004-023] are very weak. And I see by reason and experience that nothing

[004-024] is more calculated to arouse their contempt.

[004-025] It is not after this manner that [[scripture|Scripture]] speaks, which has

[004-026] a better knowledge of the things that are of God. It says, on

[004-027] the contrary, that God is a hidden God, and that, since the

[004-028] [[corruption|corruption of nature]], He has left men in a darkness from which

[004-029] they can escape only through [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], without whom all

[004-030] communion with God is cut off. Nemo novit Patrem, nisi

[004-031] Filius, et cui voluerit Filius revelare.[92]

[004-032] This is what Scripture points out to us, when it says in so

[004-033] many places that those who seek God find Him.[93] It is not of

[004-034] that light, "like the noonday sun," that this is said. We do

[004-035] not say that those who seek the noonday sun, or water in the

[004-036] sea, shall find them; and hence the evidence of God must not

[004-037] be of this nature. So it tells us elsewhere: Vere tu es Deus

[004-038] absconditus.[94]

[004-039] 243

[004-040] It is an astounding fact that no canonical writer has ever

[004-041] made use of nature to prove God. They all strive to make us

[004-042] believe in Him. [[king-david|David]], [[solomon|Solomon]], etc., have never said, "There

[004-043] is no [[nothingness|void]], therefore there is a God." They must have had

[004-044] more knowledge than the most learned people who came after

[004-045] them, and who have all made use of this argument. This is

[004-046] worthy of attention.

[004-047] 244

[004-048] "Why! Do you not say yourself that the heavens and birds

[004-049] prove God?" No. "And does your religion not say so?" No.

[004-050] For although it is true in a sense for some souls to whom God

[004-051] gives this light, yet it is false with respect to the majority of men.

[004-052] 245

[004-053] There are three sources of [[faith|belief]]: reason, custom, inspiration.

[004-054] The [[christianity|Christian religion]], which alone has reason, does not acknowledge

[004-055] as her true children those who believe without inspiration.

[004-056] It is not that she excludes reason and custom. On the contrary,

[004-057] the [[spirit|mind]] must be opened to proofs, must be confirmed by custom,

[004-058] and offer itself in humbleness to inspirations, which alone can

[004-059] produce a true and saving effect. Ne evacuetur crux Christi.[95]

[004-060] 246

[004-061] Order.—After the letter That we ought to seek God, to write

[004-062] the letter On removing obstacles; which is the discourse on

[004-063] "[[automaton|the machine]],"[96] on preparing the machine, on seeking by reason.

[004-064] 247

[004-065] Order.—A letter of exhortation to a friend to induce him to

[004-066] seek. And he will reply, "But what is the use of seeking?

[004-067] Nothing is seen." Then to reply to him, "Do not despair."

[004-068] And he will answer that he would be glad to find some light, but

[004-069] that, according to this very religion, if he believed in it, it will

[004-070] be of no use to him, and that therefore he prefers not to seek.

[004-071] And to answer to that: The machine.

[004-072] 248

[004-073] A letter which indicates the use of proofs by the machine.—

[004-074] Faith is different from proof; the one is human, the other is a

[004-075] gift of God. Justus ex fide vivit.[97] It is this faith that God

[004-076] Himself puts into the heart, of which the proof is often the instrument,

[004-077] fides ex auditu;[98] but this faith is in the heart, and makes

[004-078] us not say scio, but credo.

[004-079] 249

[004-080] It is superstition to put one's hope in formalities; but it is

[004-081] pride to be unwilling to submit to them.

[004-082] 250

[004-083] The external must be joined to the internal to obtain anything

[004-084] from God, that is to say, we must kneel, pray with the lips, etc.,

[004-085] in order that proud man, who would not submit himself to God,

[004-086] may be now subject to the creature.[99] To expect help from these

[004-087] externals is superstition; to refuse to join them to the internal

[004-088] is pride.

[004-089] 251

[004-090] Other religions, as the pagan, are more popular, for they

[004-091] consist in externals. But they are not for educated people. A

[004-092] purely intellectual religion would be more suited to the learned,

[004-093] but it would be of no use to the common people. The Christian

[004-094] religion alone is adapted to all, being composed of externals and

[004-095] internals. It raises the common people to the internal, and

[004-096] humbles the proud to the external; it is not perfect without the

[004-097] two, for the people must understand the spirit of the letter, and

[004-098] the learned must submit their spirit to the letter.

[004-099] 252

[004-100] For we must not misunderstand ourselves; we are as much

[004-101] automatic as intellectual; and hence it comes that the instrument

[004-102] by which conviction is attained is not demonstrated alone.

[004-103] How few things are demonstrated? Proofs only convince the

[004-104] mind. [[custom|Custom]] is the source of our strongest and most believed

[004-105] proofs. It bends the automaton, which persuades the mind

[004-106] without its thinking about the matter. Who has demonstrated

[004-107] that there will be a to-morrow, and that we shall die? And what is

[004-108] more believed? It is, then, custom which persuades us of it; it is

[004-109] custom that makes so many men Christians; custom that makes

[004-110] them Turks, heathens, artisans, soldiers, etc. (Faith in baptism

[004-111] is more received among Christians than among Turks.) Finally,

[004-112] we must have recourse to it when once the mind has seen where

[004-113] the truth is, in order to quench our thirst, and steep ourselves in

[004-114] that belief, which escapes us at every hour; for always to have

[004-115] proofs ready is too much trouble. We must get an easier belief,

[004-116] which is that of custom, which, without violence, without art,

[004-117] without argument, makes us believe things, and inclines all our

[004-118] powers to this belief, so that out soul falls naturally into it. It

[004-119] is not enough to believe only by force of conviction, when the

[004-120] automaton is inclined to believe the contrary. Both our parts

[004-121] must be made to believe, the mind by reasons which it is sufficient

[004-122] to have seen once in a lifetime, and the automaton by custom,

[004-123] and by not allowing it to incline to the contrary. Inclina cor

[004-124] meum, Deus.[100]

[004-125] The reason acts slowly, with so many examinations, and on so

[004-126] many principles, which must be always present, that at every

[004-127] hour it falls asleep, or wanders, through want of having all its

[004-128] principles present. Feeling does not act thus; it acts in a

[004-129] moment, and is always ready to act. We must then put our

[004-130] faith in feeling; otherwise it will be always vacillating.

[004-131] 253

[004-132] Two extremes: to exclude reason, to admit reason only.

[004-133] 254

[004-134] It is not a rare thing to have to reprove the world for too much

[004-135] docility. It is a natural vice like credulity, and as pernicious.

[004-136] [[superstition|Superstition]].

[004-137] 255

[004-138] Piety is different from superstition.

[004-139] To carry piety as far as superstition is to destroy it.

[004-140] The heretics reproach us for this superstitious submission.

[004-141] This is to do what they reproach us for ...

[004-142] Infidelity, not to believe in the Eucharist, because it is not seen.

[004-143] Superstition to believe propositions. Faith, etc.

[004-144] 256

[004-145] I say there are few true Christians, even as regards faith.

[004-146] There are many who believe but from superstition. There are

[004-147] many who do not believe solely from wickedness. Few are

[004-148] between the two.

[004-149] In this I do not include those who are of truly pious character,

[004-150] nor all those who believe from a feeling in their heart.

[004-151] 257

[004-152] There are only three kinds of persons; those who serve God,

[004-153] having found Him; others who are occupied in seeking Him, not

[004-154] having found Him; while the remainder live without seeking

[004-155] Him, and without having found Him. The first are reasonable

[004-156] and happy, the last are foolish and unhappy; those between are

[004-157] unhappy and reasonable.

[004-158] 258

[004-159] Unusquisque sibi Deum fingit.[101]

[004-160] Disgust.

[004-161] 259

[004-162] Ordinary people have the [[force|power]] of not thinking of that about

[004-163] which they do not wish to think. "Do not meditate on the

[004-164] passages about the Messiah," said the Jew to his son. Thus our

[004-165] people often act. Thus are false religions preserved, and even

[004-166] the true one, in regard to many persons.

[004-167] But there are some who have not the power of thus

[004-168] preventing thought, and who think so much the more as they

[004-169] are forbidden. These undo false religions, and even the true

[004-170] one, if they do not find solid arguments.

[004-171] 260

[004-172] They hide themselves in the press, and call numbers to their

[004-173] rescue. Tumult.

[004-174] [[authority|Authority]].—So far from making it a rule to believe a thing

[004-175] because you have heard it, you ought to believe nothing without

[004-176] putting yourself into the position as if you had never heard it.

[004-177] It is your own assent to yourself, and the constant voice of

[004-178] your own reason, and not of others, that should make you believe.

[004-179] Belief is so important! A hundred [[contradiction|contradictions]] might be

[004-180] true. If antiquity were the rule of belief, men of ancient time

[004-181] would then be without rule. If general consent, if men had

[004-182] perished?

[004-183] False humanity, pride.

[004-184] Lift the curtain. You try in vain; if you must either believe,

[004-185] or deny, or [[skepticism|doubt]]. Shall we then have no rule? We judge that

[004-186] animals do well what they do. Is there no rule whereby to

[004-187] judge men?

[004-188] To deny, to believe, and to doubt well, are to a man what the

[004-189] race is to a horse.

[004-190] Punishment of those who sin, error.

[004-191] 261

[004-192] Those who do not love the truth take as a pretext that it is

[004-193] disputed, and that a multitude deny it. And so their error

[004-194] arises only from this, that they do not love either truth or charity.

[004-195] Thus they are without excuse.

[004-196] 262

[004-197] Superstition and lust. Scruples, evil desires. [[evil|Evil]] fear; fear,

[004-198] not such as comes from a belief in God, but such as comes from

[004-199] a doubt whether He exists or not. True fear comes from faith;

[004-200] false fear comes from doubt. True fear is joined to hope,

[004-201] because it is born of faith, and because men hope in the God in

[004-202] whom they believe. False fear is joined to despair, because

[004-203] men fear the God in whom they have no belief. The former

[004-204] fear to lose Him; the latter fear to find Him.

[004-205] 263

[004-206] "A miracle," says one, "would strengthen my faith." He

[004-207] says so when he does not see one. Reasons, seen from afar,

[004-208] appear to limit our view; but when they are reached, we begin

[004-209] to see beyond. Nothing stops the nimbleness of our mind.

[004-210] There is no rule, say we, which has not some exceptions, no

[004-211] truth so general which has not some aspect in which it fails.

[004-212] It is sufficient that it be not absolutely universal to give us a

[004-213] pretext for applying the exceptions to the present subject, and

[004-214] for saying, "This is not always true; there are therefore cases

[004-215] where it is not so." It only remains to show that this is one

[004-216] of them; and that is why we are very awkward or unlucky, if

[004-217] we do not find one some day.

[004-218] 264

[004-219] We do not weary of eating and sleeping every day, for hunger

[004-220] and sleepiness recur. Without that we should weary of them.

[004-221] So, without the hunger for spiritual things, we weary of them.

[004-222] Hunger after righteousness, the eighth beatitude.[102]

[004-223] 265

[004-224] Faith indeed tells what the senses do not tell, but not the

[004-225] contrary of what they see. It is above them and not contrary

[004-226] to them.

[004-227] 266

[004-228] How many stars have telescopes revealed to us which did not

[004-229] exist for our [[philosophy|philosophers]] of old! We freely attack Holy Scripture

[004-230] on the great number of stars, saying, "There are only one

[004-231] thousand and twenty-eight,[103] we know it." There is grass on the

[004-232] earth, we see it—from the moon we would not see it—and on

[004-233] the grass are leaves, and in these leaves are small animals; but

[004-234] after that no more.—O presumptuous man!—The compounds

[004-235] are composed of elements, and the elements not.—O presumptuous

[004-236] man! Here is a fine reflection.—We must not say that

[004-237] there is anything which we do not see.—We must then talk like

[004-238] others, but not think like them.

[004-239] 267

[004-240] The last proceeding of reason is to recognise that there is an

[004-241] [[infinite|infinity]] of things which are beyond it. It is but feeble if it

[004-242] does not see so far as to know this. But if natural things are

[004-243] beyond it, what will be said of supernatural?

[004-244] 268

[004-245] [[submission|Submission]].—We must know where to doubt, where to feel

[004-246] certain, where to submit. He who does not do so, understands

[004-247] not the force of reason. There are some who offend against

[004-248] these three rules, either by affirming everything as demonstrative,

[004-249] from want of [[knowledge|knowing]] what demonstration is; or by doubting

[004-250] everything, from want of knowing where to submit; or by submitting

[004-251] in everything, from want of knowing where they must

[004-252] judge.

[004-253] 269

[004-254] Submission is the use of reason in which consists true

[004-255] Christianity.

[004-256] 270

[004-257] [[saint-augustine|St. Augustine]].[104]—[[reason|Reason]] would never submit, if it did not judge

[004-258] that there are some occasions on which it ought to submit. It

[004-259] is then right for it to submit, when it judges that it ought to

[004-260] submit.

[004-261] 271

[004-262] [[wisdom|Wisdom]] sends us to childhood. Nisi efficiamini sicut parvuli.[105]

[004-263] 272

[004-264] There is nothing so conformable to reason as this disavowal

[004-265] of reason.

[004-266] 273

[004-267] If we submit everything to reason, our religion will have no

[004-268] mysterious and supernatural element. If we offend the

[004-269] principles of reason, our religion will be absurd and ridiculous.

[004-270] 274

[004-271] All our reasoning reduces itself to yielding to feeling.

[004-272] But fancy is like, though contrary to feeling, so that we

[004-273] cannot distinguish between these contraries. One person says

[004-274] that my feeling is fancy, another that his fancy is feeling. We

[004-275] should have a rule. Reason offers itself; but it is pliable in

[004-276] every sense; and thus there is no rule.

[004-277] 275

[004-278] Men often take their imagination for their heart; and they

[004-279] believe they are converted as soon as they think of being

[004-280] converted.

[004-281] 276

[004-282] M. de Roannez said: "Reasons come to me afterwards, but

[004-283] at first a thing pleases or shocks me without my knowing the

[004-284] reason, and yet it shocks me for that reason which I only discover

[004-285] afterwards." But I believe, not that it shocked him

[004-286] for the reasons which were found afterwards, but that these

[004-287] reasons were only found because it shocks him.

[004-288] 277

[004-289] The heart has its reasons, which reason does not know. We

[004-290] feel it in a thousand things. I say that the heart naturally loves

[004-291] the Universal Being, and also itself naturally, according as it

[004-292] gives itself to them; and it hardens itself against one or the other

[004-293] at its will. You have rejected the one, and kept the other. Is

[004-294] it by reason that you love yourself?

[004-295] 278

[004-296] It is the heart which experiences God, and not the reason.

[004-297] This, then, is faith: God felt by the heart, not by the reason.

[004-298] 279

[004-299] Faith is a gift of God; do not believe that we said it was a

[004-300] gift of reasoning. Other religions do not say this of their faith.

[004-301] They only gave reasoning in order to arrive at it, and yet it

[004-302] does not bring them to it.

[004-303] 280

[004-304] The knowledge of God is very far from the love of Him.

[004-305] 281

[004-306] [[heart|Heart]], instinct, principles.

[004-307] 282

[004-308] We know truth, not only by the reason, but also by the heart,

[004-309] and it is in this last way that we know first principles; and

[004-310] reason, which has no part in it, tries in vain to impugn them. The

[004-311] sceptics, who have only this for their object, labour to no purpose.

[004-312] We know that we do not dream, and however impossible it is for

[004-313] us to prove it by reason, this inability demonstrates only the

[004-314] weakness of our reason, but not, as they affirm, the uncertainty

[004-315] of all our knowledge. For the knowledge of first principles, as

[004-316] space, time, motion, number, is as sure as any of those which

[004-317] we get from reasoning. And reason must trust these intuitions

[004-318] of the heart, and must base them on every argument. (We

[004-319] have intuitive knowledge of the tri-dimensional nature of space,

[004-320] and of the infinity of number, and reason then shows that there

[004-321] are no two square numbers one of which is double of the other.

[004-322] Principles are intuited, propositions are inferred, all with

[004-323] certainty, though in different ways.) And it is as useless and

[004-324] absurd for reason to demand from the heart proofs of her first

[004-325] principles, before admitting them, as it would be for the heart

[004-326] to demand from reason an intuition of all demonstrated propositions

[004-327] before accepting them.

[004-328] This inability ought, then, to serve only to humble reason,

[004-329] which would judge all, but not to impugn our certainty, as if

[004-330] only reason were capable of instructing us. Would to God, on

[004-331] the contrary, that we had never need of it, and that we knew

[004-332] everything by instinct and intuition! But nature has refused us

[004-333] this boon. On the contrary, she has given us but very little

[004-334] knowledge of this kind; and all the rest can be acquired only by

[004-335] reasoning.

[004-336] Therefore, those to whom God has imparted religion by

[004-337] intuition are very fortunate, and justly convinced. But to

[004-338] those who do not have it, we can give it only by reasoning,

[004-339] waiting for God to give them spiritual insight, without which

[004-340] faith is only human, and useless for salvation.

[004-341] 283

[004-342] Order.—Against the objection that Scripture has no order.

[004-343] The heart has its own order; the intellect has its own, which

[004-344] is by principle and demonstration. The heart has another. We

[004-345] do not prove that we ought to be loved by enumerating in order

[004-346] the causes of love; that would be ridiculous.

[004-347] Jesus Christ and [[saint-paul|Saint Paul]] employ the rule of love, not of

[004-348] intellect; for they would warm, not instruct. It is the same

[004-349] with Saint Augustine. This order consists chiefly in digressions

[004-350] on each point to indicate the end, and keep it always in sight.

[004-351] 284

[004-352] Do not wonder to see simple people believe without reasoning.

[004-353] God imparts to them love of Him and hatred of self. He inclines

[004-354] their heart to believe. Men will never believe with a saving

[004-355] and real faith, unless God inclines their heart; and they will

[004-356] believe as soon as He inclines it. And this is what David

[004-357] knew well, when he said: Inclina cor meum, Deus, in ...[106]

[004-358] 285

[004-359] Religion is suited to all kinds of minds. Some pay attention

[004-360] only to its establishment,[107] and this religion is such that its

[004-361] very establishment suffices to prove its truth. Others trace

[004-362] it even to the apostles. The more learned go back to the

[004-363] beginning of the world. The angels see it better still, and from

[004-364] a more distant time.

[004-365] 286

[004-366] Those who believe without having read the Testaments, do so

[004-367] because they have an inward disposition entirely holy, and all

[004-368] that they hear of our religion conforms to it. They feel that a

[004-369] God has made them; they desire only to love God; they desire to

[004-370] hate themselves only. They feel that they have no strength in

[004-371] themselves; that they are incapable of coming to God; and that

[004-372] if God does not come to them, they can have no communion

[004-373] with Him. And they hear our religion say that men must love

[004-374] God only, and hate self only; but that all being corrupt and

[004-375] unworthy of God, God made Himself man to unite Himself to

[004-376] us. No more is required to persuade men who have this disposition

[004-377] in their heart, and who have this knowledge of their

[004-378] duty and of their inefficiency.

[004-379] 287

[004-380] Those whom we see to be Christians without the knowledge

[004-381] of the prophets and evidences, nevertheless judge of their

[004-382] religion as well as those who have that knowledge. They judge

[004-383] of it by the heart, as others judge of it by the intellect. God

[004-384] Himself inclines them to believe, and thus they are most

[004-385] effectively convinced.

[004-386] I confess indeed that one of those Christians who believe

[004-387] without proofs will not perhaps be capable of convincing an

[004-388] infidel who will say the same of himself. But those who know

[004-389] the proofs of religion will prove without difficulty that such a

[004-390] believer is truly inspired by God, though he cannot prove it

[004-391] himself.

[004-392] For God having said in His [[prophecy|prophecies]] (which are undoubtedly

[004-393] prophecies), that in the reign of Jesus Christ He would spread

[004-394] His spirit abroad among nations, and that the youths and

[004-395] maidens and children of the Church would prophesy;[108] it is

[004-396] certain that the Spirit of God is in these, and not in the others.

[004-397] 288

[004-398] Instead of complaining that God had hidden Himself, you will

[004-399] give Him thanks for having revealed so much of Himself; and

[004-400] you will also give Him thanks for not having revealed Himself

[004-401] to haughty sages, unworthy to know so holy a God.

[004-402] Two kinds of persons know Him: those who have a humble

[004-403] heart, and who love [[humility|lowliness]], whatever kind of intellect they

[004-404] may have, high or low; and those who have sufficient understanding

[004-405] to see the truth, whatever opposition they may have

[004-406] to it.

[004-407] 289

[004-408] [[proof|Proof]].—1. The Christian religion, by its establishment, having

[004-409] established itself so strongly, so gently, whilst contrary to

[004-410] nature.—2. The [[holiness|sanctity]], the dignity, and the humility of a

[004-411] Christian soul.—3. The [[miracle|miracles]] of Holy Scripture.—4. Jesus

[004-412] Christ in particular.—5. The apostles in particular.—6. [[moses|Moses]]

[004-413] and the prophets in particular.—7. The [[jews|Jewish people]].—8.

[004-414] The prophecies.—9. Perpetuity; no religion has perpetuity.—

[004-415] 10. The doctrine which gives a reason for everything.—11.

[004-416] The sanctity of this law.—12. By the course of the world.

[004-417] Surely, after considering what is life and what is religion, we

[004-418] should not refuse to obey the inclination to follow it, if it comes

[004-419] into our heart; and it is certain that there is no ground for

[004-420] laughing at those who follow it.

[004-421] 290

[004-422] Proofs of religion.—[[morality|Morality]], Doctrine, Miracles, Prophecies,

[004-423] Types.
