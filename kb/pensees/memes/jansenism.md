[[Jansenism]] was a [[theology|theological]] movement within seventeenth-century Catholicism that took its name from Cornelius Jansen (Jansenius), Bishop of Ypres (1585–1638).

Centered at the convent of [[Port-Royal]] and embracing figures such as [[Blaise Pascal]] and Antoine Arnauld, Jansenism emphasized [[saint-augustine|Augustinian]] doctrines of [[divine|divine]] [[grace]], [[original sin]], and predestination.

The movement's conflict with Jesuit [[theology|theology]] — and with the [[france|French]] crown — made it one of the defining controversies of the Grand Siècle and shaped [[blaise-pascal|Pascal]]'s intellectual project in the *Pensées*.

## Doctrinal Foundations
[[theology|theological]]s theological core derived from Jansenius's posthumously published *Augustinus* (1640), a massive study of the teachings of [[saint-augustine|Saint Augustine]] on [[grace|grace]] and predestination. The work defended what Jan[[saint-augustine|Augustine]]esented as Augustine's authentic doctrine against what he[[saint-augustine|Augustinian]] diluted [[saint-augustine|Augustinianism]] of post-Trident[[theology|theology]]olic theology [P01-044].

The central doctrinal commitments of Jansenism included:

| Doctrine | Jansenist Position | Opposing View |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|
| [[original-sin|Original sin]] | [[human-nature|Human]]ity is radically fallen; the will is enslaved to concupiscenc[[grace|grace]]hout grace | Human nature is wounded but not destroyed by [[the-fall|the Fall]] |
| Efficacious grace | Grace is gratuitous and irresistible; it cannot be merited | Sufficient grace is given to all; its efficacy depends on free human cooperation |
| Predestination | [[God|God]]'s election is absolute and independent of foreseen merits | Election is conditional on foreseen human response |
| Moral rigorism | High standards of penitential practice and sacramental reception | Pastoral accommodation to human weakness |

> [P01-424] The radical and helpless state of man, in Jansenism, is the necessary premise from which the doctrine of efficacious grace follows.

This theological rigorism had profound pastoral implications. The Jansenists insisted on sincere contrition — not merely attrition (fear of punishment) — as the condition for valid sacramental absolution, a position that brought them into direct conflict with the more pastoral approach favored by the Jesuits [P01-419].

## Pascal's Jansenism

Pascal's relationship to Jansenism was complex.

He was not a theologian by training, and his contribution to the movement was primarily literary and polemical rather than doctrinal [P01-193]. Yet his religious sensibility was deeply Jansenist: his emphasis on the radical dependence of humanity on [[grace|divine grace]], his Augustinian anthropology, and his critique of easy religious accommodation all bear the marks of Port-Royal theology.

> [P01-382] There is a Jansenism of temperament, without identification with the formal doctrines of Jansenius — and Pascal exemplifies this.

Pascal also embodies what might be called a "Jansenism of the individual" — a personal appropriation of Augustinian themes that transcends the specific controversies of his time [P01-430].

His analysis of the [[human-condition|human condition]] in the *Pensées* — the disproportion, the diversion, the inability to find rest in created things — is a psychological translation of Jansenist theological anthropology, rendered in terms that speak to readers who have never heard of Jansenius.

The *Provincial Letters* (1656–1657) represent Pascal's most direct engagement in the Jansenist controversy.

Written under the pseudonym Louis de Montalte, these eighteen letters defend the Jansenist position against the attacks of the Sorbonne and the Society of Jesus, employing a combination of theological precision, satirical wit, and literary brilliance that made them an immediate sensation [P01-161].

## The Controversy and Its Resolution

The Jansenist controversy unfolded over several decades and involved some of the most powerful institutions of ancien régime [[france|France]]:

1. **The Five Propositions (1653)** — Pope Innocent X condemned five propositions allegedly drawn from Jansenius's *Augustinus*. The Jansenists accepted the condemnation of the propositions but denied that they were in fact taught by Jansenius — the celebrated distinction between *droit* (the proposition is heretical) and *fait* (Jansenius taught it).

2. **The Formulary Controversy (1661–1669)** — The French clergy, with royal support, required all ecclesiastics to sign a formulary affirming the condemnation of the five propositions as found in Jansenius. The Port-Royal [[community|community]] was deeply divided over whether to sign. Pascal himself urged resistance, arguing that the *fait* question was a matter of historical fact, not of faith.

3. **The Destruction of Port-Royal (1709–1710)** — Louis XIV, determined to eradicate Jansenism, ordered the dispersal of the community and the physical destruction of Port-Royal-des-Champs. The buildings were razed and the remains exhumed [P01-377].

4. **The Bull Unigenitus (1713)** — Pope Clement XI's condemnation of 101 propositions from Pasquier Quesnel's Jansenist-leaning *Moral Reflections* effectively condemned Jansenism in its later form, though resistance continued throughout the eighteenth century.

> [P01-373] The question of the heterodoxy of Jansenism is not the concern of the student of Pascal; but understanding the movement's character is essential to understanding Pascal's work.

## Legacy and Influence

Jansenism's influence extended far beyond the theological controversies of the seventeenth century. The movement's educational theories, developed at the Port-Royal "Little Schools," influenced modern pedagogy.

The *Port-Royal Logic* shaped philosophical methodology for generations. And the literary achievements of Pascal and Racine ensured that Jansenist themes — the trag[[human-condition|human condition]] the human condition, the radical dependence on grace, the critique of worldly complacency — would continue to resonate.

In the broader history of ideas, Jansenism represents one of the most powerful attempts to recover Augustinian [[christianity|Christianity]] in the face of what its adherents saw as the dilution of the tradition by post-Tridentine scholasticism and Jesuit accommodation.

Its insistence on the priority of grace and the depth of human fallenness anticipates themes that would later appear in [[Søren Kierkegaard|Kierkegaard]], [[Karl Barth]], and other modern theologians.

Jansenism also shaped the distinctive character of French Catholicism, contributing to a tradition of intellectual rigor, moral seriousness, and resistance to ecclesiastical and political authority that would surface again in [[Gallicanism]] and, in secularized form, in the French intellectual tradition more broadly.
