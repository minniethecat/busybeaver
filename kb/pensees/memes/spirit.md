[[Spirit|Spirit]] (*esprit*), in Pascal's vocabulary, has a range of meanings that cannot be captured by any single English equivalent. It denotes at once the faculty of [[mind|mind]], the intermediate realm of [[three-orders|Pascal's three orders]], and the spiritual dimension of human existence.

## The Three Orders

In Pascal's hierarchical ontology, spirit occupies the middle order between [[body|body]] (the physical, the carnal) and [[charity|charity]] (the supernatural, the order of [[God|God]]):

> [002-282] ...the rational part should be other than spiritual...

> [002-289] ...things which are simple, whether spiritual or corporeal.

The order of spirit is the realm of [[reason|reason]], [[science|science]], and human intellectual achievement. It infinitely surpasses the order of body, yet it is itself infinitely surpassed by the order of charity — a truth that the greatest minds ([[Archimedes|Archimedes]], for Pascal) exemplify through their intellectual excellence without [[holiness|holiness]].

## Esprit de Finesse and Esprit de Géométrie

Pascal's famous distinction between two modes of knowing belongs to the order of spirit:

> [002-291] ...and speak of material things in spiritual terms, and...

> [002-292] ...of spiritual things in material terms.

The *esprit de géométrie* proceeds by deduction from clearly defined principles; the *esprit de finesse* grasps truths through intuitive synthesis. Neither is sufficient alone — the full range of human [[knowledge|knowledge]] requires both.

## Spiritual vs. Carnal Reading

The spirit/body distinction also structures Pascal's [[hermeneutics|hermeneutics]]:

> [004-097] ...the people must understand the spirit of the letter...

The [[Jews|Jews]] read Scripture carnally (literally), missing its spiritual sense which points to [[Christ|Christ]]. True understanding requires reading "in spirit" — grasping the reality beneath the figure.

## Spiritual Pride

Pascal identifies a particular danger for those who operate primarily in the order of spirit:

> [P01-443] ...encouraged in him the sin of spiritual pride, the concupiscence...

Intellectual achievement can become an occasion for [[pride|pride]] rather than [[humility|humility]], precisely because spirit's excellence is real but not ultimate.
