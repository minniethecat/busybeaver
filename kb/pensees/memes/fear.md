## Definition

**Fear** (*crainte*) in Pascal's [[Pensées]] is a complex religious-psychological category. Pascal draws a crucial distinction between true fear, which arises from [[Faith]], and false fear, which arises from doubt: "True fear comes from faith; false fear comes from doubt. True fear is joined to hope, because it is born of faith ... False fear is joined to despair, because we fear a God in whom we have no faith" (004-199). This distinction places fear at the center of Pascal's analysis of the psychology of religious belief.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal's treatment of fear is inseparable from his apologetic concern with the existential stakes of religious decision. The [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]] frames the question of God's existence in terms that naturally evoke fear — eternal consequences make the stakes infinitely high. Yet Pascal carefully distinguishes between the servile fear that merely wishes to avoid punishment and the filial fear that belongs to authentic faith.

The relationship between fear and belief is reciprocal. Those who lack [[Faith]] fear religion as possibly true: "they hate it, and fear it is true" (003-017). This is the fear of the unbeliever confronted with the possibility that Christianity's claims might be correct — a fear that Pascal exploits apologetically. Conversely, the believer's fear is transformed by faith: it is joined to hope and issues not from terror of punishment but from reverence for [[God]].

Pascal acknowledges that fear can be excessive or misplaced. Extreme fear, like extreme confidence, can be worthless (002-979). The saints themselves experienced "fear" — but a fear that, paradoxically, accompanied their confidence in God and reflected the seriousness with which they took the possibility of falling away (002-1075). The proper disposition is neither presumption nor terror but a reverent fear compatible with hope.

The fear of death and nothingness is a central motive in Pascal's anthropology. Human beings "fear the void" they discover within themselves — the absence of meaning and solidity (002-294). They fear the truths that [[Diversion]] helps them avoid. Fear, in this sense, is a truthful response to the human condition that can either drive one toward [[Faith]] or toward deeper diversion and despair.

The [[Wretchedness]] of the human condition includes fear of death, which Pascal portrays as an inescapable horizon. "Without foresight and without fear to try the great event" (003-163) — to face death without fear is, for Pascal, not courage but blindness. The [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]] argument is designed to channel this natural fear toward a rational decision for faith.

## Key Passages

> True fear comes from faith; false fear comes from doubt. True fear is joined to hope, because it is born of faith, and we hope in the God in whom we believe. False fear is joined to despair, because we fear a God in whom we have no faith. (004-199)

> They hate it, and fear it is true. To cure this, we must begin by showing that religion is not contrary to reason. (003-017)

> We are only troubled by the fears which we, and not nature, give ourselves. (002-835)

> The fear of the saints who have always followed God is not the same as that of those who have not. (002-1075)

## Analysis

Pascal's analysis of fear exemplifies his dialectical method. Fear is not simply a negative passion to be overcome; it can be truthful when it registers the real stakes of human existence, or false when it is the product of doubt and leads to despair. The distinction maps onto Pascal's broader [[Greatness and Misery of Man|greatness-and-misery]] dialectic: fear can be a symptom of [[Wretchedness]] (the terror of the void) or a mark of greatness (the reverent fear that belongs to a rightly ordered relationship with God).

The apologetic function of fear is particularly significant. Pascal uses the fear of possibly being wrong about religion's truth to destabilize the complacent unbeliever. The [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]] can be read as an attempt to transform the fear of religion ("it might be true") into the fear of impiety ("I might be wrong to reject it"). Fear thus becomes a rational motive for religious inquiry.

Pascal's treatment of fear also reflects his [[Jansenism|Jansenist]] theological commitments. The emphasis on the saints' fear, the possibility of falling away, and the insufficiency of mere confidence aligns with the Augustinian tradition's suspicion of any presumption of salvation. True fear, like true [[Faith]], is a gift of [[Grace]].
