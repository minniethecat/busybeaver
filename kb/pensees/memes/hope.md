## Definition

In Pascal's theology, **hope** (espérance) is the **specific expectation of eternal joy** given by [[Faith]], set against the background of human hopelessness without [[God]]. Hope is not a general optimism about the future but a determinate orientation toward the promises revealed in [[Scripture]]. It is held in dialectical balance with [[Fear]]: "True fear is joined to hope" (004-200). Hope without fear degenerates into presumption; fear without hope collapses into [[Despair]]. The Christian lives suspended between these two poles, neither presuming upon nor despairing of the [[Mercy]] of God.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Hope and the Christian's Distinctive Posture

Pascal's most striking formulation of hope is contrastive. The non-Christian and the Christian face the same existential uncertainty, but they face it differently:

> (002-1075) I fear nothing; I hope for nothing. It is not so with the Christian.

The imagined speaker — perhaps the Stoic or the libertine — claims a kind of negative freedom: freedom from both fear and hope. He fears nothing because he expects nothing beyond death. He hopes for nothing because he believes in nothing beyond the visible world. Pascal's retort is terse: the Christian is different. The Christian both fears and hopes because the Christian knows that the stakes are infinite. The posture of indifference — what Pascal elsewhere calls [[Indifference]] — is not wisdom but blindness to the real situation.

### The Horizon of Eternal Joy

Hope in Pascal has a specific object: eternal joy with God:

> (003-084) Whether there are or are not eternal joys to hope for.

> (003-269) Hope will be satisfied and the promise fulfilled.

The object of hope is not a vague "better future" or "human flourishing" but the specific Christian promise of eternal life. Pascal's [[Pascal's Wager|Wager]] argument depends on this: the infinite stakes of belief only make sense if what is hoped for is genuinely infinite. Temporal hopes — for health, prosperity, reputation — are finite and therefore cannot ground the kind of total commitment that Christian faith demands. Only an eternal hope can match the infinite [[Wretchedness]] of the human condition without God.

### Hope and the Image of the Human Condition

> (003-336) And without hope. It is an image of the condition of man.

This fragment — "and without hope" — is one of Pascal's most compressed diagnoses. The human condition, seen truly, is a condition of hopelessness. Man without God is "without hope" because all finite hopes are ultimately illusions. They cannot satisfy the infinite longing of the human [[Heart]], and they cannot survive the certainty of [[Death]]. The image — the picture Pascal paints of humanity — is of creatures who are made for infinity but trapped in finitude, who long for eternal joy but can only grasp temporal pleasures. Hope is the specifically Christian answer to this predicament.

### Hope and Salvation

> (003-718) Those who hope for salvation are so far happy.

> (003-719) Who believes there is a hell, and hopes to be saved if there is?

Hope for [[Salvation]] is itself a form of happiness — "so far happy" (dès ici heureux). Pascal's point is that hope transforms the present, not just the future. The person who genuinely hopes for salvation already participates, in a preliminary way, in the joy he hopes for. This is not the full happiness of possession, but it is a real and present good. The [[Stoicism|Stoic]] who claims to need nothing and hope for nothing has, in Pascal's view, merely deadened his desire. The Christian who hopes has directed his desire toward its true object.

### True and False Hope

Pascal distinguishes authentic hope from its counterfeits:

> (004-080) It is superstition to put one's hope in formalities; but it is pride to refuse to submit to formalities.

Hope placed in religious ritual — "formalities" — is [[Superstition]]. Hope placed in God Himself, mediated but not replaced by ritual, is authentic. Pascal's Jansenist sympathies are visible here: the external observance of religion is not the ground of hope. Only the inward [[Conversion]] of the heart to God can sustain genuine hope. Yet Pascal characteristically refuses the opposite error: to reject all formalities out of [[Pride]] is equally wrong. The balance is to use the formalities while hoping only in God.

### Hope and Fear

> (004-200) True fear is joined to hope, because it is born of faith.

> (007-410) He so justly tempers fear with hope.

> (007-637) That you must hope for it.

The pairing of hope and fear runs throughout Pascal's treatment. "True fear" — as opposed to servile fear or "evil fear" (004-197) — is the fear that arises from faith. It is the reverent acknowledgment that God is God and that salvation is His gift, not a human achievement. This fear does not extinguish hope. It protects hope from degenerating into presumption. The God who "tempers fear with hope" (007-410) is the God who reveals enough to inspire hope without revealing so much as to make faith unnecessary.

### Hope and the Hiddenness of God

> (008-227) Were there no light, man would not hope for a remedy. Thus, it is not true that everything discloses God, and it is not true that everything hides God.

Hope depends on God's partial self-disclosure. If God were entirely hidden, there would be no ground for hope. If God were entirely manifest, hope would be replaced by sight. Pascal's doctrine of the hidden God — the *Deus absconditus* — is the theological context for hope: God gives enough light for those who seek to hope, but not so much that those who refuse are compelled. Hope thus requires an act of the will, a turning toward the light that is available.

### Hope and the Hopelessness of the Beginning

> (002-148) And their beginning are hopelessly hidden from him in an impenetrable secret.

> (002-980) Hopeless misery, brutal lust.

> (003-122) Hope of another; that we are happy only in the hope of another.

The human condition without God is described in terms of hopelessness: "hopelessly hidden" origins, "hopeless misery," the need for "hope of another." The repetition of "hopeless" as a descriptor of the natural condition is deliberate. Pascal wants the reader to feel the weight of hopelessness before offering hope. The structure of the Pensées — first the misery of man without God, then the happiness of man with God — is the structure of hope itself: first the diagnosis, then the remedy.

## Analysis

Pascal's theology of hope is an exercise in negative space. Hope is defined as much by what surrounds it — fear, despair, presumption, the hiddenness of God — as by its own positive content. This is deliberate. Hope is not a feeling of optimism that can be produced at will. It is a theological virtue that arises at the intersection of specific doctrines: the [[Wretchedness]] of man without God, the promises of [[Scripture]], the partial self-disclosure of God in [[Jesus Christ]], and the balancing of fear and confidence in the Christian life.

The contrast with secular hope is instructive. Secular hope — hope for progress, for human improvement, for a better future within history — is, from Pascal's perspective, merely the extension of [[Diversion]] into the temporal dimension. It keeps the soul occupied with a future that never arrives and that, if it did arrive, would not satisfy. Christian hope differs not in degree but in kind: its object is eternal, its ground is divine promise, and its effect is not distraction from the present but transformation of it.

The practical function of hope in Pascal's apologetics is clear. After subjecting the reader to the full force of the diagnosis — the [[Disproportion]] of man, the terror of [[Death]], the vanity of [[Diversion]], the corruption of [[Self-Love]] — Pascal offers hope as the only exit. The sequence is: first terror, then hope. A hope that costs nothing and risks nothing is not hope but wishful thinking. Pascal's hope costs everything: it requires the surrender of all finite hopes in exchange for the one infinite hope.
