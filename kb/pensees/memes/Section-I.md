[001-001] SECTION I

[001-002] THOUGHTS ON MIND AND ON STYLE

[001-003] 1

[001-004] The difference between the mathematical and the intuitive [[spirit|mind]].[1]—In

[001-005] the one the principles are palpable, but removed from ordinary

[001-006] use; so that for want of [[custom|habit]] it is difficult to turn one's mind

[001-007] in that direction: but if one turns it thither ever so little, one

[001-008] sees the principles fully, and one must have a quite inaccurate

[001-009] mind who reasons wrongly from principles so plain that it is

[001-010] almost impossible they should escape notice.

[001-011] But in the intuitive mind the principles are found in common

[001-012] use, and are before the eyes of everybody. One has only to

[001-013] look, and no effort is necessary; it is only a question of good

[001-014] eyesight, but it must be good, for the principles are so subtle

[001-015] and so numerous, that it is almost impossible but that some

[001-016] escape notice. Now the omission of one principle leads to

[001-017] error; thus one must have very clear sight to see all the principles,

[001-018] and in the next place an accurate mind not to draw false

[001-019] deductions from known principles.

[001-020] All mathematicians would then be intuitive if they had clear

[001-021] sight, for they do not reason incorrectly from principles known

[001-022] to them; and intuitive minds would be mathematical if they

[001-023] could turn their eyes to the principles of mathematics to which

[001-024] they are unused.

[001-025] The reason, therefore, that some intuitive minds are not

[001-026] mathematical is that they cannot at all turn their attention

[001-027] to the principles of mathematics. But the reason that mathematicians

[001-028] are not intuitive is that they do not see what is

[001-029] before them, and that, accustomed to the exact and plain principles

[001-030] of mathematics, and not [[reason|reasoning]] till they have well

[001-031] inspected and arranged their principles, they are lost in matters

[001-032] of intuition where the principles do not allow of such arrangement.

[001-033] They are scarcely seen; they are felt rather than seen;

[001-034] there is the greatest difficulty in making them felt by those

[001-035] who do not of themselves perceive them. These principles are

[001-036] so fine and so numerous that a very delicate and very clear

[001-037] sense is needed to perceive them, and to judge rightly and

[001-038] justly when they are perceived, without for the most part being

[001-039] able to demonstrate them in order as in mathematics; because

[001-040] the principles are not known to us in the same way, and because

[001-041] it would be an endless matter to undertake it. We must see

[001-042] the matter at once, at one glance, and not by a process of

[001-043] reasoning, at least to a certain degree. And thus it is rare that

[001-044] mathematicians are intuitive, and that men of intuition are

[001-045] mathematicians, because mathematicians wish to treat matters

[001-046] of intuition mathematically, and make themselves ridiculous,

[001-047] wishing to begin with definitions and then with axioms, which

[001-048] is not the way to proceed in this kind of reasoning. Not that

[001-049] the mind does not do so, but it does it tacitly, naturally, and

[001-050] without technical rules; for the expression of it is beyond all

[001-051] men, and only a few can feel it.

[001-052] Intuitive minds, on the contrary, being thus accustomed to

[001-053] judge at a single glance, are so astonished when they are presented

[001-054] with propositions of which they understand nothing,

[001-055] and the way to which is through definitions and axioms so

[001-056] sterile, and which they are not accustomed to see thus in detail,

[001-057] that they are repelled and disheartened.

[001-058] But dull minds are never either intuitive or mathematical.

[001-059] Mathematicians who are only mathematicians have exact

[001-060] minds, provided all things are explained to them by means of

[001-061] definitions and axioms; otherwise they are inaccurate and

[001-062] insufferable, for they are only right when the principles are

[001-063] quite clear.

[001-064] And men of intuition who are only intuitive cannot have

[001-065] the patience to reach to first principles of things speculative

[001-066] and conceptual, which they have never seen in the world, and

[001-067] which are altogether out of the common.

[001-068] 2

[001-069] There are different kinds of right understanding;[2] some have

[001-070] right understanding in a certain order of things, and not in

[001-071] others, where they go astray. Some draw conclusions well

[001-072] from a few premises, and this displays an acute judgment.

[001-073] Others draw conclusions well where there are many premises.

[001-074] For example, the former easily learn hydrostatics, where the

[001-075] premises are few, but the conclusions are so fine that only

[001-076] the greatest acuteness can reach them.

[001-077] And in spite of that these persons would perhaps not be

[001-078] great mathematicians, because mathematics contain a great

[001-079] number of premises, and there is perhaps a kind of intellect

[001-080] that can search with ease a few premises to the bottom, and

[001-081] cannot in the least penetrate those matters in which there are

[001-082] many premises.

[001-083] There are then two kinds of intellect: the one able to penetrate

[001-084] acutely and deeply into the conclusions of given premises, and

[001-085] this is the precise intellect; the other able to comprehend a

[001-086] great number of premises without confusing them, and this is

[001-087] the mathematical intellect. The one has force and exactness,

[001-088] the other comprehension. Now the one quality can exist without

[001-089] the other; the intellect can be strong and narrow, and can

[001-090] also be comprehensive and weak.

[001-091] 3

[001-092] Those who are accustomed to judge by feeling do not understand

[001-093] the process of reasoning, for they would understand at

[001-094] first sight, and are not used to seek for principles. And others,

[001-095] on the contrary, who are accustomed to reason from principles,

[001-096] do not at all understand matters of feeling, seeking principles,

[001-097] and being unable to see at a glance.

[001-098] 4

[001-099] Mathematics, intuition.—True eloquence makes light of

[001-100] eloquence, true morality makes light of morality; that is to say,

[001-101] the morality of the judgment, which has no rules, makes light

[001-102] of the morality of the intellect.

[001-103] For it is to judgment that perception belongs, as science

[001-104] belongs to intellect. Intuition is the part of judgment,

[001-105] mathematics of intellect.

[001-106] To make light of philosophy is to be a true philosopher.

[001-107] 5

[001-108] Those who judge of a work by rule[3] are in regard to others

[001-109] as those who have a watch are in regard to others. One says,

[001-110] "It is two hours ago"; the other says, "It is only three-quarters

[001-111] of an hour." I look at my watch, and say to the one, "You

[001-112] are weary," and to the other, "Time gallops with you"; for it

[001-113] is only an hour and a half ago, and I laugh at those who tell me

[001-114] that time goes slowly with me, and that I judge by imagination.

[001-115] They do not know that I judge by my watch.[4]

[001-116] 6

[001-117] Just as we harm the understanding, we harm the feelings also.

[001-118] The understanding and the feelings are moulded by intercourse;

[001-119] the understanding and feelings are corrupted by intercourse.

[001-120] Thus good or bad society improves or corrupts them.

[001-121] It is, then, all-important to know how to choose in order to

[001-122] improve and not to corrupt them; and we cannot make this

[001-123] choice, if they be not already improved and not corrupted. Thus

[001-124] a circle is formed, and those are fortunate who escape it.

[001-125] 7

[001-126] The greater intellect one has, the more originality one finds in

[001-127] men. Ordinary persons find no difference between men.

[001-128] 8

[001-129] There are many people who listen to a sermon in the same

[001-130] way as they listen to vespers.

[001-131] 9

[001-132] When we wish to correct with advantage, and to show another

[001-133] that he errs, we must notice from what side he views the matter,

[001-134] for on that side it is usually true, and admit that truth to him,

[001-135] but reveal to him the side on which it is false. He is satisfied

[001-136] with that, for he sees that he was not mistaken, and that he

[001-137] only failed to see all sides. Now, no one is offended at not

[001-138] seeing everything; but one does not like to be mistaken, and

[001-139] that perhaps arises from the fact that man naturally cannot

[001-140] see everything, and that naturally he cannot err in the side he

[001-141] looks at, since the perceptions of our senses are always true.

[001-142] 10

[001-143] People are generally better persuaded by the reasons which

[001-144] they have themselves discovered than by those which have

[001-145] come into the mind of others.

[001-146] 11

[001-147] All great amusements are dangerous to the Christian life;

[001-148] but among all those which the world has invented there is none

[001-149] more to be feared than the theatre. It is a representation of

[001-150] the passions so natural and so delicate that it excites them and

[001-151] gives birth to them in our hearts, and, above all, to that of love,

[001-152] principally when it is represented as very chaste and virtuous.

[001-153] For the more innocent it appears to innocent souls, the more

[001-154] they are likely to be touched by it. Its violence pleases our

[001-155] [[self-love]], which immediately forms a desire to produce the

[001-156] same effects which are seen so well represented; and, at the

[001-157] same time, we make ourselves a conscience founded on the

[001-158] propriety of the feelings which we see there, by which the

[001-159] fear of pure souls is removed, since they imagine that it cannot

[001-160] hurt their purity to love with a love which seems to them

[001-161] so reasonable.

[001-162] So we depart from the theatre with our heart so filled with

[001-163] all the beauty and tenderness of love, the soul and the mind

[001-164] so persuaded of its innocence, that we are quite ready to receive

[001-165] its first impressions, or rather to seek an opportunity of awakening

[001-166] them in the heart of another, in order that we may receive

[001-167] the same pleasures and the same sacrifices which we have seen

[001-168] so well represented in the theatre.

[001-169] 12

[001-170] Scaramouch,[5] who only thinks of one thing.

[001-171] The doctor,[6] who speaks for a quarter of an hour after he has

[001-172] said everything, so full is he of the desire of talking.

[001-173] 13

[001-174] One likes to see the error, the passion of Cleobuline,[7] because

[001-175] she is unconscious of it. She would be displeasing, if she were

[001-176] not deceived.

[001-177] 14

[001-178] When a natural discourse paints a passion or an effect, one

[001-179] feels within oneself the truth of what one reads, which was there

[001-180] before, although one did not know it. Hence one is inclined

[001-181] to love him who makes us feel it, for he has not shown us his

[001-182] own riches, but ours. And thus this benefit renders him pleasing

[001-183] to us, besides that such community of intellect as we have with

[001-184] him necessarily inclines the heart to love.

[001-185] 15

[001-186] Eloquence, which persuades by sweetness, not by authority;

[001-187] as a tyrant, not as a king.

[001-188] 16

[001-189] Eloquence is an art of saying things in such a way—(1) that

[001-190] those to whom we speak may listen to them without pain and

[001-191] with pleasure; (2) that they feel themselves interested, so that

[001-192] self-love leads them more willingly to reflection upon it.

[001-193] It consists, then, in a correspondence which we seek to

[001-194] establish between the head and the heart of those to whom we

[001-195] speak on the one hand, and, on the other, between the thoughts

[001-196] and the expressions which we employ. This assumes that we

[001-197] have studied well the heart of man so as to know all its powers,

[001-198] and then to find the [[justice|just]] proportions of the discourse which we

[001-199] wish to adapt to them. We must put ourselves in the place

[001-200] of those who are to hear us, and make trial on our own heart

[001-201] of the turn which we give to our discourse in order to see

[001-202] whether one is made for the other, and whether we can assure

[001-203] ourselves that the hearer will be, as it were, forced to surrender.

[001-204] We ought to restrict ourselves, so far as possible, to the simple

[001-205] and natural, and not to magnify that which is little, or belittle

[001-206] that which is great. It is not enough that a thing be beautiful;

[001-207] it must be suitable to the subject, and there must be in it

[001-208] nothing of excess or defect.

[001-209] 17

[001-210] Rivers are roads which move,[8] and which carry us whither

[001-211] we desire to go.

[001-212] 18

[001-213] When we do not know the truth of a thing, it is of advantage

[001-214] that there should exist a common error which determines the

[001-215] mind of man, as, for example, the moon, to which is attributed

[001-216] the change of seasons, the progress of diseases, etc. For the

[001-217] chief malady of man is restless curiosity about things which he

[001-218] cannot understand; and it is not so bad for him to be in error

[001-219] as to be curious to no purpose.

[001-220] The manner in which [[epictetus|Epictetus]], [[montaigne|Montaigne]], and Salomon

[001-221] de Tultie[9] wrote, is the most usual, the most suggestive, the

[001-222] most remembered, and the oftenest quoted; because it is entirely

[001-223] composed of thoughts born from the common talk of life. As

[001-224] when we speak of the common error which exists among men

[001-225] that the moon is the cause of everything, we never fail to say

[001-226] that Salomon de Tultie says that when we do not know the

[001-227] truth of a thing, it is of advantage that there should exist a

[001-228] common error, etc.; which is the thought above.

[001-229] 19

[001-230] The last thing one settles in writing a book is what one

[001-231] should put in first.

[001-232] 20

[001-233] Order.—Why should I undertake to divide my virtues into four

[001-234] rather than into six? Why should I rather establish virtue in

[001-235] four, in two, in one? Why into Abstine et sustine[10] rather than

[001-236] into "Follow [[nature|Nature]],"[11] or, "Conduct your private affairs without

[001-237] injustice," as [[plato|Plato]],[12] or anything else? But there, you will

[001-238] say, everything is contained in one word. Yes, but it is useless

[001-239] without explanation, and when we come to explain it, as soon

[001-240] as we unfold this maxim which contains all the rest, they

[001-241] emerge in that first confusion which you desired to avoid. So,

[001-242] when they are all included in one, they are hidden and useless,

[001-243] as in a chest, and never appear save in their natural confusion.

[001-244] Nature has established them all without including one in

[001-245] the other.

[001-246] 21

[001-247] Nature has made all her [[truth|truths]] independent of one another.

[001-248] Our art makes one dependent on the other. But this is not

[001-249] natural. Each keeps its own place.

[001-250] 22

[001-251] Let no one say that I have said nothing new; the arrangement

[001-252] of the subject is new. When we play tennis, we both play with

[001-253] the same ball, but one of us places it better.

[001-254] I had as soon it said that I used words employed before.

[001-255] And in the same way if the same thoughts in a different arrangement

[001-256] do not form a different discourse, no more do the same

[001-257] words in their different arrangement form different thoughts!

[001-258] 23

[001-259] Words differently arranged have a different meaning, and

[001-260] meanings differently arranged have different effects.

[001-261] 24

[001-262] Language.—We should not turn the mind from one thing to

[001-263] another, except for relaxation, and that when it is necessary

[001-264] and the time suitable, and not otherwise. For he that relaxes

[001-265] out of season wearies, and he who wearies us out of season

[001-266] makes us languid, since we turn quite away. So much does our

[001-267] perverse lust like to do the contrary of what those wish to obtain

[001-268] from us without giving us pleasure, the coin for which we will

[001-269] do whatever is wanted.

[001-270] 25

[001-271] Eloquence.—It requires the pleasant and the real; but the

[001-272] pleasant must itself be drawn from the true.

[001-273] 26

[001-274] Eloquence is a painting of thought; and thus those who,

[001-275] after having painted it, add something more, make a picture

[001-276] instead of a portrait.

[001-277] 27

[001-278] Miscellaneous. Language.—Those who make antitheses by

[001-279] forcing words are like those who make false windows for symmetry.

[001-280] Their rule is not to speak accurately, but to make

[001-281] apt figures of speech.

[001-282] 28

[001-283] Symmetry is what we see at a glance; based on the fact that

[001-284] there is no reason for any difference, and based also on the face

[001-285] of man; whence it happens that symmetry is only wanted in

[001-286] breadth, not in height or depth.

[001-287] 29

[001-288] When we see a natural style, we are astonished and delighted;

[001-289] for we expected to see an author, and we find a man. Whereas

[001-290] those who have good taste, and who seeing a book expect to

[001-291] find a man, are quite surprised to find an author. Plus poetice

[001-292] quam humane locutus es. Those honour Nature well, who teach

[001-293] that she can speak on everything, even on theology.

[001-294] 30

[001-295] We only consult the ear because the heart is wanting. The

[001-296] rule is uprightness.

[001-297] Beauty of omission, of judgment.

[001-298] 31

[001-299] All the false beauties which we blame in Cicero have their

[001-300] admirers, and in great number.

[001-301] 32

[001-302] There is a certain standard of grace and beauty which consists

[001-303] in a certain relation between our nature, such as it is, weak or

[001-304] strong, and the thing which pleases us.

[001-305] Whatever is formed according to this standard pleases us, be

[001-306] it house, song, discourse, verse, prose, woman, birds, rivers,

[001-307] trees, rooms, dress, etc. Whatever is not made according to

[001-308] this standard displeases those who have good taste.

[001-309] And as there is a perfect relation between a song and a house

[001-310] which are made after a good model, because they are like this

[001-311] good model, though each after its kind; even so there is a perfect

[001-312] relation between things made after a bad model. Not that the

[001-313] bad model is unique, for there are many; but each bad sonnet,

[001-314] for example, on whatever false model it is formed, is just like a

[001-315] woman dressed after that model.

[001-316] Nothing makes us understand better the ridiculousness of a

[001-317] false sonnet than to consider nature and the standard, and then

[001-318] to imagine a woman or a house made according to that standard.

[001-319] 33

[001-320] Poetical beauty.—As we speak of poetical beauty, so ought we

[001-321] to speak of mathematical beauty and medical beauty. But

[001-322] we do not do so; and the reason is that we know well what is the

[001-323] object of mathematics, and that it consists in proofs, and what

[001-324] is the object of medicine, and that it consists in healing. But

[001-325] we do not know in what grace consists, which is the object of

[001-326] poetry. We do not know the natural model which we ought to

[001-327] imitate; and through lack of this knowledge, we have coined

[001-328] fantastic terms, "The golden age," "The wonder of our times,"

[001-329] "Fatal," etc., and call this jargon poetical beauty.[13]

[001-330] But whoever imagines a woman after this model, which

[001-331] consists in saying little things in big words, will see a pretty

[001-332] girl adorned with mirrors and chains, at whom he will smile;

[001-333] because we know better wherein consists the charm of woman

[001-334] than the charm of verse. But those who are ignorant would

[001-335] admire her in this dress, and there are many villages in which

[001-336] she would be taken for the queen; hence we call sonnets made

[001-337] after this model "Village Queens."

[001-338] 34

[001-339] No one passes in the world as skilled in verse unless he has

[001-340] put up the sign of a poet, a mathematician, etc. But educated

[001-341] people do not want a sign, and draw little distinction between

[001-342] the trade of a poet and that of an embroiderer.

[001-343] People of education are not called poets or mathematicians,

[001-344] etc.; but they are all these, and judges of all these. No one

[001-345] guesses what they are. When they come into society, they talk

[001-346] on matters about which the rest are talking. We do not observe

[001-347] in them one quality rather than another, save when they have

[001-348] to make use of it. But then we remember it, for it is characteristic

[001-349] of such persons that we do not say of them that they are

[001-350] fine speakers, when it is not a question of oratory, and that we

[001-351] say of them that they are fine speakers, when it is such a

[001-352] question.

[001-353] It is therefore false praise to give a man when we say of him,

[001-354] on his entry, that he is a very clever poet; and it is a bad sign

[001-355] when a man is not asked to give his judgment on some verses.

[001-356] 35

[001-357] We should not be able to say of a man, "He is a mathematician,"

[001-358] or "a preacher," or "eloquent"; but that he is "a

[001-359] gentleman." That universal quality alone pleases me. It is

[001-360] a bad sign when, on seeing a person, you remember his book.

[001-361] I would prefer you to see no quality till you meet it and have

[001-362] occasion to use it (Ne quid nimis[14]), for fear some one quality

[001-363] prevail and designate the man. Let none think him a fine

[001-364] speaker, unless oratory be in question, and then let them think it.

[001-365] 36

[001-366] Man is full of wants: he loves only those who can satisfy

[001-367] them all. "This one is a good mathematician," one will say.

[001-368] But I have nothing to do with mathematics; he would take me

[001-369] for a proposition. "That one is a good soldier." He would

[001-370] take me for a besieged town. I need, then, an upright man

[001-371] who can accommodate himself generally to all my wants.

[001-372] 37

[001-373] [Since we cannot be universal and know all that is to be

[001-374] known of everything, we ought to know a little about everything.

[001-375] For it is far better to know something about everything

[001-376] than to know all about one thing. This universality is the

[001-377] best. If we can have both, still better; but if we must choose,

[001-378] we ought to choose the former. And the world feels this and

[001-379] does so; for the world is often a good judge.]

[001-380] 38

[001-381] A poet and not an honest man.

[001-382] 39

[001-383] If lightning fell on low places, etc., poets, and those who

[001-384] can only reason about things of that kind, would lack proofs.

[001-385] 40

[001-386] If we wished to prove the examples which we take to prove

[001-387] other things, we should have to take those other things to be

[001-388] examples; for, as we always believe the difficulty is in what

[001-389] we wish to prove, we find the examples clearer and a help to

[001-390] demonstration.

[001-391] Thus when we wish to demonstrate a general theorem, we

[001-392] must give the rule as applied to a particular case; but if we wish

[001-393] to demonstrate a particular case, we must begin with the general

[001-394] rule. For we always find the thing obscure which we wish to

[001-395] prove, and that clear which we use for the proof; for, when a

[001-396] thing is put forward to be proved, we first fill ourselves with the

[001-397] imagination that it is therefore obscure, and on the contrary

[001-398] that what is to prove it is clear, and so we understand it easily.

[001-399] 41

[001-400] Epigrams of Martial.—Man loves malice, but not against

[001-401] one-eyed men nor the unfortunate, but against the fortunate

[001-402] and proud. People are mistaken in thinking otherwise.

[001-403] For lust is the source of all our actions, and humanity, etc.

[001-404] We must please those who have humane and tender feelings.

[001-405] That epigram about two one-eyed people is worthless,[15] for it

[001-406] does not console them, and only gives a point to the author's

[001-407] glory. All that is only for the sake of the author is worthless.

[001-408] Ambitiosa recident ornamenta.[16]

[001-409] 42

[001-410] To call a king "Prince" is pleasing, because it diminishes

[001-411] his rank.

[001-412] 43

[001-413] Certain authors, speaking of their works, say, "My book,"

[001-414] "My commentary," "My history," etc. They resemble middle-class

[001-415] people who have a house of their own, and always have

[001-416] "My house" on their tongue. They would do better to say,

[001-417] "Our book," "Our commentary," "Our history," etc., because

[001-418] there is in them usually more of other people's than their own.

[001-419] 44

[001-420] Do you wish people to believe good of you? Don't speak.

[001-421] 45

[001-422] Languages are ciphers, wherein letters are not changed into

[001-423] letters, but words into words, so that an unknown language

[001-424] is decipherable.

[001-425] 46

[001-426] A maker of witticisms, a bad character.

[001-427] 47

[001-428] There are some who speak well and write badly. For the

[001-429] place and the audience warm them, and draw from their minds

[001-430] more than they think of without that warmth.

[001-431] 48

[001-432] When we find words repeated in a discourse, and, in trying

[001-433] to correct them, discover that they are so appropriate that

[001-434] we would spoil the discourse, we must leave them alone. This

[001-435] is the test; and our attempt is the work of envy, which is blind,

[001-436] and does not see that repetition is not in this place a fault; for

[001-437] there is no general rule.

[001-438] 49

[001-439] To mask nature and disguise her. No more king, pope,

[001-440] bishop—but august monarch, etc.; not Paris—the capital of the

[001-441] kingdom. There are places in which we ought to call Paris,

[001-442] Paris, and others in which we ought to call it the capital of

[001-443] the kingdom.

[001-444] 50

[001-445] The same meaning changes with the words which express it.

[001-446] Meanings receive their dignity from words instead of giving it

[001-447] to them. Examples should be sought....
