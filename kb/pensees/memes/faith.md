[[Faith|Faith]] (foi), in Pascal's [[theology|theology]], is the supernatural virtue by which the [[soul|soul]] assents to revealed [[truth|truth]] — not against [[reason|reason]] but beyond it. Faith is simultaneously a gift of [[god|God]] and a disposition cultivated through the practices Pascal prescribes in [[pascals-wager|the Wager]].

> (P01-327) that which leads to faith and which is

Pascal distinguishes between the path to faith and faith itself. The path engages reason, [[evidence|evidence]], and [[custom|custom]]; the arrival is a disposition of the [[heart|heart]] that reason alone could never produce.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### Faith as Gift and Joy

Pascal's most striking descriptions of faith emphasise its affective dimension — faith brings joy:

> (P01-371) in, the joy of faith.

The "joy of faith" is not an emotional byproduct but intrinsic to faith itself. To possess faith is to possess the [[sovereign-good|sovereign good]] toward which all human desire tends. This connects faith to Pascal's analysis of [[beatitude|beatitude]]: faith is the beginning of the beatific vision.

> (P01-350) or much faith; and when the ordinary man ca

Pascal acknowledges degrees of faith — "much faith" versus little — without making faith a merely quantitative phenomenon. The "ordinary man" may possess a faith sufficient for [[salvation|salvation]] without the intellectual sophistication of the theologian.

### The Priest and the Truths of Faith

> (003-074) the priest on the truths of the faith. After

Pascal situates faith within the [[church|Church]]'s teaching authority. The "truths of the faith" are not individually discovered but received through tradition — a point that separates Pascal from subjectivist accounts of religious belief. [[Authority|Authority]] governs in matters of faith, just as reason governs in matters of science.

### Faith and Despair

> (003-350) in despair at being without faith, we

The experience of being "without faith" is, for Pascal, an experience of [[despair|despair]]. This is not merely psychological observation but theological anthropology: the human [[soul|soul]] was made for [[god|God]], and the absence of faith is the felt absence of that for which it was made.

[[Despair|Despair]] without faith is, paradoxically, evidence for faith — it reveals that the human condition is fundamentally religious. The [[wretchedness|wretchedness]] of the faithless state is a negative proof of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s diagnosis.

### Faith and Custom

> (002-590) He who is accustomed to the faith

Pascal's interest in [[custom|custom]] extends to faith: faith can become customary, a second nature. This is both a danger (dead formalism) and an opportunity (the [[automaton|automaton]] bent toward belief). The Wager's practical strategy — "act as if you believed" — relies on this mechanism: custom can lead where reason cannot drive.

### The Faith of the Simple

> (P01-408) or the simple faith that any one who lives a good

Pascal defends the faith of the simple believer against intellectual scorn. [[God|God]] does not require sophisticated apologetics for faith to be genuine. The "simple faith" of the [[heart|heart]] may be more authentic than the faith buttressed by countless arguments — a point that reflects Pascal's Jansenist sympathy for the humble.

### Faith and Science

> (P01-451) into science ought to have faith if I

Pascal's own experience as a scientist shapes his account of faith: one who has "entered into science" — who knows the limits of reason from within — ought to recognise faith's necessity. [[Reason|Reason]]'s last step is to recognise its own insufficiency, which is faith's first step.

### Faith and Miracles

> (P01-277) to miracles, in his study of faith, which is not quite

Pascal's apologetics grants [[miracle|miracles]] an evidentiary role in the genesis of faith. Yet miracles do not compel faith — they provide motives of credibility that the [[heart|heart]] must receive. Faith "is not quite" reducible to belief on the basis of miracles, even if miracles form part of the historical case for [[christianity|Christianity]].

### Faith, Honour, and Truth

> (002-1075) not keep their word, without faith, without honour, without truth

This passage uses "faith" in both its theological and its moral sense — fidelity, trustworthiness. Pascal connects faith-in-God to faithfulness-toward-men: the absence of both marks the disorder of the [[concupiscence|concupiscent]] self. [[Truth|Truth]], [[honour|honour]], and faith stand or fall together.

### The Replenishment of Faith

> (003-259) replenished with faith than we are, and that, on the

Faith admits of increase — "replenishment." Pascal sees the Christian life as a progressive deepening of faith, not a static possession. This dynamism connects faith to [[grace|grace]]: faith grows as grace transforms the [[soul|soul]].

## Analysis

Pascal's account of faith operates at the intersection of three domains: [[epistemology|epistemology]] (faith as a mode of knowledge distinct from but not contrary to [[reason|reason]]), [[psychology|psychology]] (faith as a disposition of the [[heart|heart]] cultivated through [[custom|custom]]), and [[theology|theology]] (faith as a supernatural gift of [[grace|grace]]).

What distinguishes Pascal's treatment is its refusal to reduce faith to any single register. Faith is not merely intellectual assent (the Thomistic emphasis) nor merely fiducial trust (the Lutheran emphasis) nor merely mystical union (the Augustinian emphasis) — it is all three, held together by the Pascalian insistence that the whole person, [[body|body]] and [[soul|soul]], [[reason|reason]] and [[heart|heart]], must be converted.

This holistic account grounds [[pascals-wager|the Wager]]'s practical strategy: bend the [[automaton|automaton]], attend [[mass|Mass]], take the holy water — these are not substitutes for faith but the practices through which faith's disposition is acquired. Faith is God's gift, but God ordinarily gives it through means.
