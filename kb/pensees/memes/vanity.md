[[Vanity|Vanity]], in Pascal's [[moral-psychology|moral psychology]], is not a minor vice but the signature of the [[human-condition|human condition]] — the universal tendency to seek [[glory|glory]] and recognition in what cannot satisfy.

> [002-013] The vanity of ordinary lives, and then the vanity of philosophic lives.

The word "then" is crucial: Pascal's critique of vanity is not merely a complaint about worldly frivolity. It extends upward through every level of human achievement. The philosopher who despises the vanity of ordinary life is himself caught in a subtler vanity — the vanity of despising vanity.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Vanity of All Conditions

> [002-012] To show the vanity of all conditions of men, to show the vanity of ordinary lives.

Pascal's method is to expose vanity not by condemning it but by demonstrating its universality. If all conditions of life are vain, then no condition can escape vanity by changing its external circumstances. This is the radical claim: vanity is not a property of certain lifestyles but of the human subject itself.

> [003-131] The state itself which is the subject of his joy and vanity, I have no trouble in discerning.

The object of another's vanity is transparent to the observer — yet invisible to the subject. This asymmetry between self-perception and other-perception is, for Pascal, one of the structural features of vanity: we see others' vanity clearly and remain blind to our own.

### Vanity and Knowledge

> [002-059] The vanity of the sciences.—Physical science will not console me for ignorance of morals in time of affliction.

But the sciences will not console me for ignorance of morals in time of affliction. Pascal's point is not that [[science|science]] is worthless but that it cannot deliver what it implicitly promises — relief from the human predicament. Scientific [[knowledge|knowledge]] does not [[console|console]]; it merely occupies the mind while the deeper questions remain unanswered.

> [P01-435] The vanity of their thoughts and of their avocations, their laziness, their indifference to the real purpose of their existence.

The indictment is comprehensive: thoughts (the inner life), avocations (the active life), and the disposition that underlies both — indifference to the "real purpose." Vanity is not merely what we do; it is what we fail to ask.

### Vanity and Diversion

Vanity is structurally related to [[diversion|diversion]]: both are strategies for avoiding the confrontation with [[nothingness|nothingness]]. But where diversion is active — the flight into activity — vanity is passive — the [[absorption|absorption]] in one's own reflected image. The vain person is not running from himself but gazing at himself, and this gazing is itself a form of flight.

> [007-397] So that they could easily avoid vanity, but it was necessary that they should not fall into despair.

The alternatives are stark: vanity or [[despair|despair]]. This is the Pascalian trap — to escape vanity is to risk confronting the void that vanity conceals.

## Key Passages

> [003-114] Are only vanity; that our evils are infinite; and, lastly, that the Redeemer...

Vanity is the first of three truths that prepare for the [[gospel|Gospel]]: vanity, infinite [[evil|evil]], and the [[redeemer|Redeemer]]. This ordering is deliberate: the recognition of vanity is the first step out of vanity.

> [002-847] The ignorance of the vanity of absent pleasures, cause inconstancy.

"Absent pleasures" are always more attractive than present ones — this is the mechanism of [[inconstancy|inconstancy]] that Pascal diagnoses as endemic to the fallen condition. We are vain not only about what we have but about what we imagine we could have.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of vanity is distinctive in refusing the Stoic solution. The [[stoicism|Stoic]] says: recognise vanity, then detach from it. Pascal says: recognising vanity does not free you from it — it merely reveals that the self is itself the problem. "The self is hateful" (*le moi est haïssable*) is the logical conclusion of the analysis of vanity: the self that seeks glory cannot be the self that renounces it.

This distinguishes Pascal from [[montaigne|Montaigne]], who also analysed vanity but drew different conclusions. Montaigne exposed vanity in order to cultivate [[humility|humility]] and [[self-knowledge|self-knowledge]]. Pascal exposes vanity in order to demonstrate the impossibility of the self curing itself. The physician cannot heal himself because the physician is the disease.

The apologetic function of the analysis of vanity is to prepare for [[christianity|Christianity]]: if vanity is universal and inescapable by human effort, then the only possible liberation must come from outside the self — from [[grace|grace]].
