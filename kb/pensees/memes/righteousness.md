Righteousness, in Pascal's theological vocabulary, is primarily an attribute of [[God]] — the divine standard of justice against which all human pretensions to righteousness are exposed as failure.

It is a concept that cuts across Pascal's apologetics, his analysis of the Human Condition, and his reading of biblical [[Prophecy]].

Unlike the philosophers who imagine that righteousness can be achieved by [[Reason]] or effort, Pascal insists that true righteousness is eschatological
— something promised, not possessed
— and that its absence defines the human predicament.

## In Pascal's Pensées

Pascal treats righteousness as the lens through which the human condition is diagnosed.

If righteousness is what God requires, then the universal fact of human unrighteousness
— "we are full of unrighteousness" (007-081)
— is the fundamental problem that [[Christianity in Pascal's Pensées|Christianity]] claims to solve.

> (007-081) We are full of unrighteousness. It must give us an explanation of our condition.

Pascal weaves righteousness into his reading of the Old Testament [[Prophecy|prophets]], particularly Isaiah.

The coming of righteousness is one of the marks by which true revelation is distinguished from false: "my righteousness is near to come" (011-344).

> (011-525) To bring in everlasting righteousness.

For Pascal, the fulfilment of these prophecies in [[Jesus Christ]] is the cornerstone of Christian Apologetics and the proof that the Scriptures are divinely authored.

Pascal also connects righteousness to the interior life.

In his reading of the Beatitudes, the hunger for righteousness is the eighth and culminating beatitude — the desire that orients the human being toward God.

> (004-222) Hunger after righteousness, the eighth beatitude.

This hunger is not a natural appetite but a recognition of lack: the Self-Knowledge that one does not possess what one most needs.

## Key Passages

> (007-626) The wise; they have righteousness as their object.

Pascal contrasts the wisdom of the philosophers
— who sought righteousness as though it were a natural attainment
— with the wisdom of the [[Scripture|Scriptures]], which declares that righteousness must be given, not achieved.

> (007-534) Men have declared that righteousness consists in the power to do what one will. But the philosophers have never been able to give men this power.

The critique of philosophical pretension is sharp: the philosophers promise righteousness but cannot deliver it. Only [[Grace]] can bridge the gap between the ideal and the actual.

> (007-149) Unrighteousness, which these wise men never knew.

Pascal brings his characteristic Paradox logic to bear: the "wise" are precisely those who are blind to the depth of their own unrighteousness.
True wisdom begins with the confession of unrighteousness, not the claim to have overcome it.

> (011-664) Ye that follow after righteousness, ye that seek the Lord: look unto the rock whence ye are hewn.

Here Pascal quotes Isaiah to remind his reader that the pursuit of righteousness and the pursuit of [[God]] are not two separate quests but one.

## Analysis

Righteousness serves a diagnostic function in Pascal's apologetic architecture.

It is the standard that reveals the shortfall: if the human being were merely imperfect, remediation might be possible. But if the human being is unrighteous
— that is, fundamentally misaligned with the divine order
— then only a radical intervention can restore what is broken.

This places righteousness at the intersection of Pascal's Anthropology and his Soteriology.

The anthropological diagnosis — "we are full of unrighteousness" — creates the need for the soteriological solution: the righteousness of [[Jesus Christ|Christ]] imputed to the believer.

Pascal's treatment of righteousness thus mirrors his treatment of [[Original Sin]]:
both are doctrines that offend human pride
but are, for Pascal, the only explanations that fit the observable facts of the human condition.

> (011-344) My righteousness is near to come.

The eschatological framing is essential. Righteousness is not present but promised; not achieved but awaited.
This distinguishes Pascal's position from both Pelagian optimism (we can become righteous by effort) and Stoic self-sufficiency (righteousness is within our power).

For Pascal, the hunger for righteousness is itself a sign of [[Grace]] — the gift of [[God]] awakening a desire that nature cannot satisfy and [[Reason]] cannot articulate.
