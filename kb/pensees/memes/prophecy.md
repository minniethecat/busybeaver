[[Prophecy|Prophecy]], in Pascal's [[apologetics|apologetics]], is the most historically grounded of the proofs for [[christianity|Christianity]] — a body of predictions whose fulfilment can be verified by any honest inquirer.

> [009-619] Cyrus took occasion from the prophecy of Isaiah to grant the Jews their liberty.

The specificity of biblical prophecy — naming [[cyrus|Cyrus]] by name, predicting the return from exile — is, for Pascal, its evidential strength. These are not vague oracles that could be fulfilled by any event; they are precise predictions whose fulfilment can be checked against secular history.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Cumulative Case

> [010-190] Although it had miracles and prophecy and perpetuity, because it has not doctrine.

Prophecy operates within a network of mutually reinforcing proofs: [[miracle|miracles]], prophecy, [[perpetuity|perpetuity]], and [[doctrine|doctrine]]. No single proof is sufficient alone, but each strengthens the others. The [[jews|Jews]] had prophecy — the most extensive prophetic tradition in history — yet, Pascal observes, lacked true doctrine. The combination matters as much as the individual components.

> [011-431] Prophecy was proved false by Herod. But the whole of prophecy was not disproved.

Pascal confronts the objection that some prophecies appear to have failed. His response is not to deny the difficulty but to insist that isolated failures do not discredit the whole. The cumulative weight of fulfilled prophecy remains overwhelming; the apparent failures may reflect misinterpretation rather than falsification.

> [011-646] Prophecy; and as regards the term of captivity, there is a prophecy which is now fulfilled.

The fulfilment of prophecy in history — visible, datable, verifiable — is the core of Pascal's argument. This is not a proof that appeals only to the already-convinced; it appeals to the public [[evidence|evidence]] of history.

### Prophecy and the Jews

> [011-664] And without an idol; and this prophecy is now fulfilled, as they can see with their eyes.

The survival of the [[jews|Jewish]] people without idols — worshipping an invisible [[God|God]] unlike every surrounding nation — is itself, Pascal argues, a fulfilled prophecy and a standing [[miracle|miracle]]. The Jews are the "witness-people" whose existence authenticates the prophecies they preserve.

### The Apologetic Structure

Prophecy operates at the intersection of [[reason|reason]] and [[faith-and-reason|faith]]. The historical case for prophetic fulfilment can be assessed by reason alone — it requires no special revelation to check whether Cyrus freed the Jews. But the *significance* of that fulfilment — that it reveals a God who governs history — requires the [[heart|heart]]'s recognition as well as the mind's.

## Analysis

Pascal's emphasis on prophecy reflects his broader conviction that [[christianity|Christianity]] is a historical religion — a religion of events, not merely ideas. The proofs of Christianity are not abstract arguments but concrete happenings: a people delivered from exile, a king named before his birth, a Messiah who suffers and dies and rises.

This is why prophecy is paired with [[miracle|miracles]] in Pascal's apologetic. Both are public, historical, and verifiable — or at least, their verification does not depend on private religious experience. They are accessible to the [[skepticism|skeptic]] who is willing to examine the evidence.

> [013-186] Has miracle, prophecy, doctrine, perpetuity. The other sects have not.

The uniqueness claim is essential: no other religion has the combination of miracles, prophecy, doctrine, and perpetuity that Christianity possesses. Each individual element might be found elsewhere; the combination is unique.
