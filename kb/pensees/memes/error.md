[[Error|Error]], in Pascal's epistemology, is not an accident that befalls an otherwise reliable mind — it is the default condition of human cognition, produced by multiple intersecting causes that no act of [[reason|reason]] alone can overcome.

> [002-550] Man is only a subject full of error, natural and ineffaceable, without grace. Nothing shows him the truth.

This is among Pascal's most radical claims: error is *natural* (built into the human constitution), *ineffaceable* (cannot be removed by human effort), and curable only by [[grace|grace]]. The epistemological conclusion is inseparable from the theological one.

## In Pascal's Pensées

### The Sources of Error

Pascal catalogues the sources of human error with a systematicity that anticipates later treatments of cognitive bias.

> [002-474] …this is one of the sources of error, and the more deceptive that she is not always so…

The [[imagination|imagination]] is the "mistress of error" — not because it always deceives (it sometimes tells the truth) but because its deceptiveness is unpredictable. An organ that is *sometimes* reliable is, in a sense, more dangerous than one that is *never* reliable, because it invites trust.

> [002-511] …given us to lead us into necessary error. We have, however, other principles.

Pascal identifies principles *within human nature itself* that "lead us into necessary error." Error is not simply external misinformation; it is generated by the very structure of human cognition.

> [002-530] We have another source of error in diseases. They spoil the judgment and the senses.

The body — physical illness, fatigue, passion — introduces error into thought. Pascal's attention to the corporeal sources of error reflects his refusal to treat the [[mind|mind]] as independent of the [[body|body]]: the [[automaton|automaton]] and the mind are one person, and what affects the one affects the other.

> [002-546] …the most powerful cause of error is the war between the senses and the understanding.

Pascal identifies the internal conflict between sense and reason as the "most powerful cause of error." This is not the classical-rationalist solution of simply subordinating sense to reason; Pascal recognises that reason itself is corrupted and that sense provides genuine knowledge (the "reasons of the [[heart|heart]]").

### The Common Error

Pascal is particularly interested in *collective* error — the errors that entire societies share:

> [001-214] …that there should exist a common error which determines the mind and fixes the thoughts of men…

The "common error" functions like [[custom|custom]]: it stabilises society, provides a shared framework of judgment, and becomes invisible to those who inhabit it. Pascal's anthropological insight is that social order depends on shared errors — on things believed not because they are true but because they are common.

### Error and the Human Condition

Error, for Pascal, is not merely epistemological but existential:

> [003-583] …and your nature has two things to shun, error and misery. Your reason is no more shocked…

Error and [[wretchedness|misery]] are the twin poles of the human condition without [[god|God]]. The avoidance of error is not, for Pascal, a purely intellectual project — it is inseparable from the avoidance of misery, because intellectual error about the human condition is itself a source of existential misery.

> [002-560] But besides those errors which arise accidentally and through lack of intelligence, with these heterogeneous faculties…

The list of error-sources is heterogeneous and irreducible: imagination, self-love, disease, the conflict of faculties, custom, the disproportion between mind and reality. No single method — not Descartes' *method*, not Bacon's *induction* — can overcome all of them.

## Analysis

Pascal's treatment of error is the epistemological counterpart to his anthropological doctrine of the [[original-sin|Fall]]. Just as the will is corrupted by [[concupiscence|concupiscence]], so the intellect is corrupted — not destroyed, but rendered systematically unreliable. The mind can still reach truth, but it cannot reliably distinguish truth from error by its own powers.

Pascal's catalogue of error-sources is notable for what it omits: he does not propose a method for eliminating error. The [[cartesianism|Cartesian]] project of grounding knowledge on clear and distinct ideas is, for Pascal, an evasion of the human condition — as if the mind that is "full of error" could, by an act of its own, become error-free.

The only exit from error is [[grace|grace]] — a supernatural gift that restores the mind's capacity for truth in the order of [[charity|charity]], even while leaving the orders of [[body|body]] and [[mind|mind]] subject to their characteristic errors. This is why [[faith-and-reason|faith]] is not, for Pascal, an alternative to reason but its necessary complement.
