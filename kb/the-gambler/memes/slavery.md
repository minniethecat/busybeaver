# Slavery

The language of slavery pervades *The Gambler*, centering on [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexei Ivanovitch]]'s relationship with [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]. He repeatedly calls himself her "slave," transforming a metaphor of degradation into a perverse declaration of [[love|devotion]]. The motif extends outward from this central relationship into the novel's broader analysis of social dependence.

## The Slave and the Empress

The narrator introduces the slavery motif through a striking image: [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] behaves "as the old Empress did upon her servant" (002-156), undressing before him because she did "not account a slave a man" (002-158). This erasure of his humanity — being seen and not seen — captures the paradox of his position. He is close enough to witness her most private moments, yet rendered invisible by the very proximity that should make him a person.

## Voluntary Abasement

Alexei's slavery is self-imposed. He acknowledges that he figures "in her eyes as a mere slave and nonentity" (004-060) yet continues to serve. The "slavery and abasement" (004-057) are not forced upon him; he chooses them. He is used "as though I were a slave or a passing convenience" (004-040), and this use is, in some twisted way, what he craves.

He pushes the question to its limit: "This was more than slavery and abasement" (007-065), he thinks after a particularly humiliating exchange, recognizing that the term "slavery" itself is inadequate to describe his condition — and yet he persists.

## The Rhetoric of Submission

The narrator's declarations grow increasingly theatrical: "I am your slave — and slaves should not be permitted to presume" (006-109). He invites her to "revel in my slavery — revel in it" (006-161–006-162). The slavery becomes a form of emotional blackmail, a claim on her attention through the very act of self-negation.

The rhetorical excess reveals a calculation: by proclaiming himself a slave, the narrator paradoxically seizes power. He demands that she witness his degradation, and in witnessing it, she becomes complicit. He asks: "Do you know that at times I could kill you?" (006-162) — the slave's ultimate inversion, the threat of violence from the abject.

## Beyond Metaphor

The slavery motif extends beyond the central relationship. The narrator observes that "every German family is bound to slavery and to submission" (005-149), diagnosing servitude as a national characteristic. His observation that his mother-in-law's family "shows that she is an absolute slave to the Frenchman" (011-028) maps the power dynamics of the General's household onto the same vocabulary of domination.

The novel extends this further, noting how "people become abject slaves where marriage is concerned" (012-006), suggesting that slavery is not a personal pathology but a generalized social condition — the unconscious form of many "voluntary" relationships.
