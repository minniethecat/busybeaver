# The Gambler · Section 9

[009-001]Yet it was not the General but [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] that I wanted to anger. She had

[009-002]treated me with such cruelty, and had got me into such a hole, that I

[009-003]felt a longing to force her to beseech me to stop. Of course, my

[009-004]tomfoolery might compromise her; yet certain other feelings and desires

[009-005]had begun to form themselves in my brain. If I was never to rank in her

[009-006]eyes as anything but a nonentity, it would not greatly matter if I

[009-007]figured as a draggle-tailed cockerel, and [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] were to give me a

[009-008]good thrashing; but, the fact was that I desired to have the laugh of

[009-009]them all, and to come out myself unscathed. Let people see what they

[009-010]_would_ see. Let Polina, for once, have a good fright, and be forced to

[009-011]whistle me to heel again. But, however much she might whistle, she

[009-012]should see that I was at least no draggle-tailed cockerel!

[009-013]I have just received a surprising piece of news. I have just met our

[009-014]chambermaid on the stairs, and been informed by her that Maria

[009-015]Philipovna departed today, by the night train, to stay with a cousin at

[009-016]Carlsbad. What can that mean? The maid declares that Madame packed her

[009-017]trunks early in the day. Yet how is it that no one else seems to have

[009-018]been aware of the circumstance? Or is it that _I_ have been the only

[009-019]person to be unaware of it? Also, the maid has just told me that, three

[009-020]days ago, [[maria-philipovna|Maria Philipovna]] had some high words with the General. I

[009-021]understand, then! Probably the words were concerning [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]].

[009-022]Certainly something decisive is approaching.

[009-023]VII

[009-024]In the morning I sent for the maître d’hôtel, and explained to him

[009-025]that, in future, my bill was to be rendered to me personally. As a

[009-026]matter of fact, my expenses had never been so large as to alarm me, nor

[009-027]to lead me to quit the hotel; while, moreover, I still had 160 gülden

[009-028]left to me, and—in them—yes, in them, perhaps, riches awaited me. It

[009-029]was a curious fact, that, though I had not yet won anything at [[gambling|play]], I

[009-030]nevertheless acted, thought, and felt as though I were sure, before

[009-031]long, to become wealthy—since I could not imagine myself otherwise.

[009-032]Next, I bethought me, despite the earliness of the hour, of going to

[009-033]see [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], who was staying at the Hôtel de l’Angleterre (a

[009-034]hostelry at no great distance from our own). But suddenly De Griers

[009-035]entered my room. This had never before happened, for of late that

[009-036]gentleman and I had stood on the most strained and distant of terms—he

[009-037]attempting no concealment of his contempt for me (he even made an

[009-038]express point of showing it), and I having no reason to desire his

[009-039]company. In short, I detested him. Consequently, his entry at the

[009-040]present moment the more astounded me. At once I divined that something

[009-041]out of the way was on the carpet.

[009-042]He entered with marked affability, and began by complimenting me on my

[009-043]room. Then, perceiving that I had my hat in my hands, he inquired

[009-044]whither I was going so early; and, no sooner did he hear that I was

[009-045]bound for Mr. Astley’s than he stopped, looked grave, and seemed

[009-046]plunged in thought.

[009-047]He was a true Frenchman insofar as that, though he could be lively and

[009-048]engaging when it suited him, he became insufferably dull and wearisome

[009-049]as soon as ever the need for being lively and engaging had passed.

[009-050]Seldom is a Frenchman _naturally_ civil: he is civil only as though to

[009-051]order and of set purpose. Also, if he thinks it incumbent upon him to

[009-052]be fanciful, original, and out of the way, his fancy always assumes a

[009-053]foolish, unnatural vein, for the reason that it is compounded of trite,

[009-054]hackneyed forms. In short, the natural Frenchman is a conglomeration of

[009-055]commonplace, petty, everyday positiveness, so that he is the most

[009-056]tedious person in the world. Indeed, I believe that none but greenhorns

[009-057]and excessively Russian people feel an attraction towards the French;

[009-058]for, to any man of sensibility, such a compendium of outworn forms—a

[009-059]compendium which is built up of drawing-room manners, expansiveness,

[009-060]and gaiety—becomes at once over-noticeable and unbearable.

[009-061]“I have come to see you on business,” De Griers began in a very

[009-062]off-hand, yet polite, tone; “nor will I seek to conceal from you the

[009-063]fact that I have come in the capacity of an emissary, of an

[009-064]intermediary, from the General. Having small knowledge of the Russian

[009-065]tongue, I lost most of what was said last night; but, the General has

[009-066]now explained matters, and I must confess that—”

[009-067]“See here, Monsieur [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]],” I interrupted. “I understand that you

[009-068]have undertaken to act in this affair as an intermediary. Of course I

[009-069]am only ‘un utchitel,’ a tutor, and have never claimed to be an

[009-070]intimate of this household, nor to stand on at all familiar terms with

[009-071]it. Consequently, I do not know the whole of its circumstances. Yet

[009-072]pray explain to me this: have you yourself become one of its members,

[009-073]seeing that you are beginning to take such a part in everything, and

[009-074]are now present as an intermediary?”

[009-075]The Frenchman seemed not over-pleased at my question. It was one which

[009-076]was too outspoken for his taste—and he had no mind to be frank with me.

[009-077]“I am connected with the General,” he said drily, “partly through

[009-078]business affairs, and partly through special circumstances. My

[009-079]principal has sent me merely to ask you to forego your intentions of

[009-080]last evening. What you contemplate is, I have no doubt, very clever;

[009-081]yet he has charged me to represent to you that you have not the

[009-082]slightest chance of succeeding in your end, since not only will the

[009-083]Baron refuse to receive you, but also he (the Baron) has at his

[009-084]disposal every possible means for obviating further unpleasantness from

[009-085]you. Surely you can see that yourself? What, then, would be the good of

[009-086]going on with it all? On the other hand, the General promises that at

[009-087]the first favourable opportunity he will receive you back into his

[009-088]household, and, in the meantime, will credit you with your salary—with

[009-089]‘vos appointements.’ Surely that will suit you, will it not?”

[009-090]Very quietly I replied that he (the Frenchman) was labouring under a

[009-091]delusion; that perhaps, after all, I should not be expelled from the

[009-092]Baron’s presence, but, on the contrary, be listened to; finally, that I

[009-093]should be glad if Monsieur de Griers would confess that he was now

[009-094]visiting me merely in order to see how far I intended to go in the

[009-095]affair.

[009-096]“Good heavens!” cried de Griers. “Seeing that the General takes such an

[009-097]interest in the matter, is there anything very unnatural in his

[009-098]desiring also to know your plans?”

[009-099]Again I began my explanations, but the Frenchman only fidgeted and

[009-100]rolled his head about as he listened with an expression of manifest and

[009-101]unconcealed irony on his face. In short, he adopted a supercilious

[009-102]attitude. For my own part, I endeavoured to pretend that I took the

[009-103]affair very seriously. I declared that, since the Baron had gone and

[009-104]complained of me to the General, as though I were a mere servant of the

[009-105]General’s, he had, in the first place, lost me my post, and, in the

[009-106]second place, treated me like a person to whom, as to one not qualified

[009-107]to answer for himself, it was not even worth while to speak. Naturally,

[009-108]I said, I felt insulted at this. Yet, comprehending as I did,

[009-109]differences of years, of social status, and so forth (here I could

[009-110]scarcely help smiling), I was not anxious to bring about further scenes

[009-111]by going personally to demand or to request satisfaction of the Baron.

[009-112]All that I felt was that I had a right to go in person and beg the

[009-113]Baron’s and the Baroness’s pardon—the more so since, of late, I had

[009-114]been feeling unwell and unstrung, and had been in a fanciful condition.

[009-115]And so forth, and so forth. Yet (I continued) the Baron’s offensive

[009-116]behaviour to me of yesterday (that is to say, the fact of his referring

[009-117]the matter to the General) as well as his insistence that the General

[009-118]should deprive me of my post, had placed me in such a position that I

[009-119]could not well express my regret to him (the Baron) and to his good

[009-120]lady, for the reason that in all [[chance|probability]] both he and the Baroness,

[009-121]with the world at large, would imagine that I was doing so merely

[009-122]because I hoped, by my action, to recover my post. Hence, I found

[009-123]myself forced to request the Baron to express to me _his own_ regrets,

[009-124]as well as to express them in the most unqualified manner—to say, in

[009-125]fact, that he had never had any wish to insult me. After the Baron had

[009-126]done _that_, I should, for my part, at once feel free to express to

[009-127]him, whole-heartedly and without reserve, my own regrets. “In short,” I

[009-128]declared in conclusion, “my one desire is that the Baron may make it

[009-129]possible for me to adopt the latter course.”

[009-130]“Oh fie! What refinements and subtleties!” exclaimed De Griers.

[009-131]“Besides, what have you to express regret for? Confess, Monsieur,

[009-132]Monsieur—pardon me, but I have forgotten your name—confess, I say, that

[009-133]all this is merely a plan to annoy the General? Or perhaps, you have

[009-134]some other and special end in view? Eh?”

[009-135]“In return you must pardon _me_, mon cher Marquis, and tell me what

[009-136]_you_ have to do with it.”

[009-137]“[[general|The General]]—”

[009-138]“But what of the General? Last night he said that, for some reason or

[009-139]another, it behoved him to ‘move with especial care at present;’

[009-140]wherefore, he was feeling nervous. But I did not understand the

[009-141]reference.”

[009-142]“Yes, there _do_ exist special reasons for his doing so,” assented De

[009-143]Griers in a conciliatory tone, yet with rising anger. “You are

[009-144]acquainted with Mlle. de Cominges, are you not?”

[009-145]“Mlle. Blanche, you mean?”

[009-146]“Yes, Mlle. Blanche de Cominges. Doubtless you know also that the

[009-147]General is in love with this young lady, and may even be about to marry

[009-148]her before he leaves here? Imagine, therefore, what any scene or

[009-149]scandal would entail upon him!”

[009-150]“I cannot see that the marriage scheme need, be affected by scenes or

[009-151]scandals.”

[009-152]“Mais le Baron est si irascible—un caractère prussien, vous savez!

[009-153]Enfin il fera une querelle d’Allemand.”

[009-154]“I do not care,” I replied, “seeing that I no longer belong to his

[009-155]household” (of set purpose I was trying to talk as senselessly as

[009-156]possible). “But is it quite settled that Mlle. is to marry the General?

[009-157]What are they waiting for? Why should they conceal such a matter—at all

[009-158]events from ourselves, the General’s own party?”

[009-159]“I cannot tell you. The marriage is not yet a settled affair, for they

[009-160]are awaiting news from [[russia|Russia]]. The General has business transactions to

[009-161]arrange.”

[009-162]“Ah! Connected, doubtless, with madame his mother?”

[009-163]De Griers shot at me a glance of hatred.

[009-164]“To cut things short,” he interrupted, “I have complete confidence in

[009-165]your native politeness, as well as in your tact and good sense. I feel

[009-166]sure that you will do what I suggest, even if it is only for the sake

[009-167]of this family which has received you as a kinsman into its bosom and

[009-168]has always loved and respected you.”

[009-169]“Be so good as to observe,” I remarked, “that the same family has just

[009-170]_expelled_ me from its bosom. All that you are saying you are saying

[009-171]but for show; but, when people have just said to you, ‘Of course we do

[009-172]not wish to turn you out, yet, for the sake of appearance’s, you must

[009-173]_permit_ yourself to be turned out,’ nothing can matter very much.”
