# The Gambler · Section 11

[011-001]“Enough! You need say no more,” I cried with a strange emotion at my

[011-002]heart, yet not altogether understanding what had aroused that emotion

[011-003]in my breast. Indeed, when, where, and how could [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] have chosen

[011-004][[mr-astley|Astley]] to be one of her confidants? Of late I had come rather to

[011-005]overlook him in this connection, even though Polina had always been a

[011-006]riddle to me—so much so that now, when I had just permitted myself to

[011-007]tell my friend of my [[love|infatuation]] in all its aspects, I had found myself

[011-008]struck, during the very telling, with the fact that in my relations

[011-009]with her I could specify nothing that was explicit, nothing that was

[011-010]positive. On the contrary, my relations had been purely fantastic,

[011-011]strange, and unreal; they had been unlike anything else that I could

[011-012]think of.

[011-013]“Very well, very well,” I replied with a warmth equal to Astley’s own.

[011-014]“Then I stand confounded, and have no further opinions to offer. But

[011-015]you are a good fellow, and I am glad to know what you think about it

[011-016]all, even though I do not need your advice.”

[011-017]Then, after a pause, I resumed:

[011-018]“For instance, what reason should you assign for the General taking

[011-019]fright in this way? Why should my stupid clowning have led the world to

[011-020]elevate it into a serious incident? Even De Griers has found it

[011-021]necessary to put in his oar (and he only interferes on the most

[011-022]important occasions), and to visit me, and to address to me the most

[011-023]earnest supplications. Yes, _he_, De Griers, has actually been playing

[011-024]the suppliant to _me!_ And, mark you, although he came to me as early

[011-025]as nine o’clock, he had ready-prepared in his hand Mlle. Polina’s note.

[011-026]When, I would ask, was that note written? Mlle. Polina must have been

[011-027]aroused from sleep for the express purpose of writing it. At all events

[011-028]the circumstance shows that she is an absolute slave to [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]],

[011-029]since she actually begs my pardon in the note—actually begs my pardon!

[011-030]Yet what is her personal concern in the matter? Why is she interested

[011-031]in it at all? Why, too, is the whole party so afraid of this precious

[011-032]Baron? And what sort of a business do you call it for the General to be

[011-033]going to marry [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] de Cominges? He told me last night that,

[011-034]because of the circumstance, he must ‘move with especial care at

[011-035]present.’ What is your opinion of it all? Your look convinces me that

[011-036]you know more about it than I do.”

[011-037]Mr. Astley smiled and nodded.

[011-038]“Yes, I think I _do_ know more about it than you do,” he assented. “The

[011-039]affair centres around this Mlle. Blanche. Of that I feel certain.”

[011-040]“And what of Mlle. Blanche?” I cried impatiently (for in me there had

[011-041]dawned a sudden hope that this would enable me to discover something

[011-042]about Polina).

[011-043]“Well, my belief is that at the present moment Mlle. Blanche has, in

[011-044]very truth, a special reason for wishing to avoid any trouble with the

[011-045]Baron and the Baroness. It might lead not only to some unpleasantness,

[011-046]but even to a scandal.”

[011-047]“Oh, oh!”

[011-048]“Also I may tell you that Mlle. Blanche has been in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]

[011-049]before, for she was staying here three seasons ago. I myself was in the

[011-050]place at the time, and in those days Mlle. Blanche was not known as

[011-051]Mlle. de Cominges, nor was her mother, the Widow de Cominges, even in

[011-052]existence. In any case no one ever mentioned the latter. De Griers,

[011-053]too, had not materialised, and I am convinced that not only do the

[011-054]parties stand in no relation to one another, but also they have not

[011-055]long enjoyed one another’s acquaintance. Likewise, the _Marquisate_ de

[011-056]Griers is of recent creation. Of that I have reason to be sure, owing

[011-057]to a certain circumstance. Even the name De Griers itself may be taken

[011-058]to be a new invention, seeing that I have a friend who once met the

[011-059]said ‘Marquis’ under a different name altogether.”

[011-060]“Yet he possesses a good circle of friends?”

[011-061]“Possibly. Mlle. Blanche also may possess that. Yet it is not three

[011-062]years since she received from the local police, at the instance of the

[011-063]Baroness, an invitation to leave the town. And she left it.”

[011-064]“But why?”

[011-065]“Well, I must tell you that she first appeared here in company with an

[011-066]Italian—a prince of some sort, a man who bore an historic name

[011-067](Barberini or something of the kind). The fellow was simply a mass of

[011-068]rings and diamonds—real diamonds, too—and the couple used to drive out

[011-069]in a marvellous carriage. At first Mlle. Blanche played ‘trente et

[011-070]quarante’ with fair success, but, later, her [[chance|luck]] took a marked change

[011-071]for the worse. I distinctly remember that in a single evening she lost

[011-072]an enormous sum. But worse was to ensue, for one fine morning her

[011-073]prince disappeared—horses, carriage, and all. Also, the hotel bill

[011-074]which he left unpaid was enormous. Upon this Mlle. Zelma (the name

[011-075]which she assumed after figuring as Madame Barberini) was in despair.

[011-076]She shrieked and howled all over the hotel, and even tore her clothes

[011-077]in her frenzy. In the hotel there was staying also a Polish count (you

[011-078]must know that ALL travelling Poles are counts!), and the spectacle of

[011-079]Mlle. Zelma tearing her clothes and, catlike, scratching her face with

[011-080]her beautiful, scented nails produced upon him a strong impression. So

[011-081]the pair had a talk together, and, by luncheon time, she was consoled.

[011-082]Indeed, that evening the couple entered the Casino arm-in-arm—Mlle.

[011-083]Zelma laughing loudly, according to her custom, and showing even more

[011-084]expansiveness in her manners than she had before shown. For instance,

[011-085]she thrust her way into the file of women roulette-players in the exact

[011-086]fashion of those ladies who, to clear a space for themselves at the

[011-087]tables, push their fellow-players roughly aside. Doubtless you have

[011-088]noticed them?”

[011-089]“Yes, certainly.”

[011-090]“Well, they are not worth noticing. To the annoyance of the decent

[011-091]public they are allowed to remain here—at all events such of them as

[011-092]daily change 4000 franc notes at [[casino|the tables]] (though, as soon as ever

[011-093]these women cease to do so, they receive an invitation to depart).

[011-094]However, Mlle. Zelma continued to change notes of this kind, but her

[011-095][[gambling|play]] grew more and more unsuccessful, despite the fact that such

[011-096]ladies’ [[fortune|luck]] is frequently good, for they have a surprising amount of

[011-097]cash at their disposal. Suddenly, the Count too disappeared, even as

[011-098]the Prince had done, and that same evening Mlle. Zelma was forced to

[011-099]appear in the Casino alone. On this occasion no one offered her a

[011-100]greeting. Two days later she had come to the end of her resources;

[011-101]whereupon, after staking and [[loss|losing]] her last louis d’or she chanced to

[011-102]look around her, and saw standing by her side [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] Burmergelm, who

[011-103]had been eyeing her with fixed disapproval. To his distaste, however,

[011-104]Mlle. paid no attention, but, turning to him with her well-known smile,

[011-105]requested him to [[bet|stake]], on her behalf, ten louis on the red. Later that

[011-106]evening a complaint from the Baroness led the authorities to request

[011-107]Mlle. not to re-enter the Casino. If you feel in any way surprised that

[011-108]I should know these petty and unedifying details, the reason is that I

[011-109]had them from a relative of mine who, later that evening, drove Mlle.

[011-110]Zelma in his carriage from Roulettenberg to Spa. Now, mark you, Mlle.

[011-111]wants to become Madame General, in order that, in future, she may be

[011-112]spared the receipt of such invitations from Casino authorities as she

[011-113]received three years ago. At present she is not playing; but that is

[011-114]only because, according to the signs, she is lending money to other

[011-115]players. Yes, that is a much more paying game. I even suspect that the

[011-116]unfortunate General is himself in her debt, as well as, perhaps, also

[011-117]De Griers. Or, it may be that the latter has entered into a partnership

[011-118]with her. Consequently you yourself will see that, until the marriage

[011-119]shall have been consummated, Mlle. would scarcely like to have the

[011-120]attention of the Baron and the Baroness drawn to herself. In short, to

[011-121]any one in her position, a scandal would be most detrimental. You form

[011-122]a member of the ménage of these people; wherefore, any act of yours

[011-123]might cause such a scandal—and the more so since daily she appears in

[011-124]public arm in arm with the General or with Mlle. Polina. _Now_ do you

[011-125]understand?”

[011-126]“No, I do not!” I shouted as I banged my fist down upon the

[011-127]table—banged it with such violence that a frightened waiter came

[011-128]running towards us. “Tell me, Mr. Astley, why, if you knew this history

[011-129]all along, and, consequently, always knew who this Mlle. Blanche is,

[011-130]you never warned either myself or the General, nor, most of all, Mlle.

[011-131]Polina” (who is accustomed to appear in the Casino—in public everywhere

[011-132]with Mlle. Blanche). “How could you do it?”

[011-133]“It would have done no good to warn you,” he replied quietly, “for the

[011-134]reason that you could have effected nothing. Against what was I to warn

[011-135]you? As likely as not, the General knows more about Mlle. Blanche even

[011-136]than I do; yet the unhappy man still walks about with her and Mlle.

[011-137]Polina. Only yesterday I saw this Frenchwoman riding, splendidly

[011-138]mounted, with De Griers, while the General was careering in their wake

[011-139]on a roan horse. He had said, that morning, that his legs were hurting

[011-140]him, yet his riding-seat was easy enough. As he passed I looked at him,

[011-141]and the thought occurred to me that he was a man lost for ever.

[011-142]However, it is no affair of mine, for I have only recently had the

[011-143]happiness to make Mlle. Polina’s acquaintance. Also”—he added this as

[011-144]an afterthought—“I have already told you that I do not recognise your

[011-145]right to ask me certain questions, however sincere be my liking for

[011-146]you.”

[011-147]“Enough,” I said, rising. “To me it is as clear as day that Mlle.

[011-148]Polina knows all about this Mlle. Blanche, but cannot bring herself to

[011-149]part with her Frenchman; wherefore, she consents also to be seen in

[011-150]public with Mlle. Blanche. You may be sure that nothing else would ever

[011-151]have induced her either to walk about with this Frenchwoman or to send

[011-152]me a note not to touch the Baron. Yes, it is _there_ that the influence

[011-153]lies before which everything in the world must bow! Yet she herself it

[011-154]was who launched me at the Baron! The devil take it, but I was left no

[011-155]choice in the matter.”

[011-156]“You forget, in the first place, that this Mlle. de Cominges is the

[011-157]General’s inamorata, and, in the second place, that Mlle. Polina, the

[011-158]General’s step-daughter, has a younger brother and sister who, though

[011-159]they are the General’s own children, are completely neglected by this

[011-160]madman, and robbed as well.”

[011-161]“Yes, yes; that is so. For me to go and desert the children now would

[011-162]mean their total abandonment; whereas, if I remain, I should be able to

[011-163]defend their interests, and, perhaps, to save a moiety of their

[011-164]property. Yes, yes; that is quite true. And yet, and yet—Oh, I can well

[011-165]understand why they are all so interested in the General’s mother!”

[011-166]“In whom?” asked Mr. Astley.

[011-167]“In the old woman of [[moscow|Moscow]] who declines to die, yet concerning whom

[011-168]they are for ever expecting telegrams to notify the fact of her death.”

[011-169]“Ah, then of course their interests centre around her. It is a question

[011-170]of succession. Let that but be settled, and the General will marry,

[011-171]Mlle. Polina will be set free, and De Griers—”

[011-172]“Yes, and De Griers?”

[011-173]“Will be repaid his money, which is what he is now waiting for.”

[011-174]“What? You think that he is waiting for _that?_”
