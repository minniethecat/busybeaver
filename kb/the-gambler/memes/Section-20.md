# The Gambler · Section 20

[020-001]I failed to find [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], and returned home. It was now growing

[020-002]late—it was past midnight, but I subsequently learnt from [[potapitch|Potapitch]] how

[020-003]the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]’s day had ended. She had lost all the money which,

[020-004]earlier in the day, I had got for her paper securities—a sum amounting

[020-005]to about ten thousand roubles. This she did under the direction of the

[020-006]Pole whom, that afternoon, she had dowered with two ten-gülden pieces.

[020-007]But before his arrival on the scene, she had commanded Potapitch to

[020-008][[bet|stake]] for her; until at length she had told him also to go about his

[020-009]business. Upon that the Pole had leapt into the breach. Not only did it

[020-010]happen that he knew the Russian language, but also he could speak a

[020-011]mixture of three different dialects, so that the pair were able to

[020-012]understand one another. Yet the old lady never ceased to abuse him,

[020-013]despite his deferential manner, and to compare him unfavourably with

[020-014]myself (so, at all events, Potapitch declared). “_You_,” the old

[020-015]chamberlain said to me, “treated her as a gentleman should, but he—he

[020-016]robbed her right and left, as I could see with my own eyes. Twice she

[020-017]caught him at it, and rated him soundly. On one occasion she even

[020-018]pulled his hair, so that the bystanders burst out laughing. Yet she

[020-019]lost everything, sir—that is to say, she lost all that you had changed

[020-020]for her. Then we brought her home, and, after asking for some water and

[020-021]saying her prayers, she went to bed. So worn out was she that she fell

[020-022]asleep at once. May God send her dreams of angels! And _this_ is all

[020-023]that foreign travel has done for us! Oh, my own [[moscow|Moscow]]! For what have

[020-024]we not at home there, in Moscow? Such a garden and flowers as you could

[020-025]never see here, and fresh air and apple-trees coming into blossom,—and

[020-026]a beautiful view to look upon. Ah, but what must she do but go

[020-027]travelling abroad? Alack, alack!”

[020-028]XIII

[020-029]Almost a month has passed since I last touched these notes—notes which

[020-030]I began under the influence of impressions at once poignant and

[020-031]disordered. The crisis which I then felt to be approaching has now

[020-032]arrived, but in a form a hundred times more extensive and unexpected

[020-033]than I had looked for. To me it all seems strange, uncouth, and tragic.

[020-034]Certain occurrences have befallen me which border upon the marvellous.

[020-035]At all events, that is how I view them. I view them so in one regard at

[020-036]least. I refer to the whirlpool of events in which, at the time, I was

[020-037]revolving. But the most curious feature of all is my relation to those

[020-038]events, for hitherto I had never clearly understood myself. Yet now the

[020-039]actual crisis has passed away like a dream. Even my [[love|passion]] for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]

[020-040]is dead. _Was_ it ever so strong and genuine as I thought? If so, what

[020-041]has become of it now? At times I fancy that I must be mad; that

[020-042]somewhere I am sitting in a madhouse; that these events have merely

[020-043]_seemed_ to happen; that still they merely _seem_ to be happening.

[020-044]I have been arranging and re-perusing my notes (perhaps for the purpose

[020-045]of convincing myself that I am not in a madhouse). At present I am

[020-046]lonely and alone. Autumn is coming—already it is mellowing the leaves;

[020-047]and, as I sit brooding in this melancholy little town (and how

[020-048]melancholy the little towns of Germany can be!), I find myself taking

[020-049]no thought for the future, but living under the influence of passing

[020-050]moods, and of my recollections of the tempest which recently drew me

[020-051]into its vortex, and then cast me out again. At times I seem still to

[020-052]be caught within that vortex. At times, the tempest seems once more to

[020-053]be gathering, and, as it passes overhead, to be wrapping me in its

[020-054]folds, until I have lost my sense of order and reality, and continue

[020-055]whirling and whirling and whirling around.

[020-056]Yet, it may be that I shall be able to stop myself from revolving if

[020-057]once I can succeed in rendering myself an exact account of what has

[020-058]happened within the month just past. Somehow I feel drawn towards the

[020-059]pen; on many and many an evening I have had nothing else in the world

[020-060]to do. But, curiously enough, of late I have taken to amusing myself

[020-061]with the works of M. Paul de Kock, which I read in German translations

[020-062]obtained from a wretched local library. These works I cannot abide, yet

[020-063]I read them, and find myself marvelling that I should be doing so.

[020-064]Somehow I seem to be afraid of any _serious_ book—afraid of permitting

[020-065]any _serious_ preoccupation to break the spell of the passing moment.

[020-066]So dear to me is the formless dream of which I have spoken, so dear to

[020-067]me are the impressions which it has left behind it, that I fear to

[020-068]touch the vision with anything new, lest it should dissolve in smoke.

[020-069]But is it so dear to me? Yes, it _is_ dear to me, and will ever be

[020-070]fresh in my recollections—even forty years hence....

[020-071]So let me write of it, but only partially, and in a more abridged form

[020-072]than my full impressions might warrant.

[020-073]First of all, let me conclude the history of the Grandmother. Next day

[020-074]she lost every gülden that she possessed. Things were bound to happen

[020-075]so, for persons of her type who have once entered upon that road

[020-076]descend it with ever-increasing rapidity, even as a sledge descends a

[020-077]toboggan-slide. All day until eight o’clock that evening did she [[gambling|play]];

[020-078]and, though I personally did not witness her exploits, I learnt of them

[020-079]later through report.

[020-080]All that day Potapitch remained in attendance upon her; but the Poles

[020-081]who directed her play she changed more than once. As a beginning she

[020-082]dismissed her Pole of the previous day—the Pole whose hair she had

[020-083]pulled—and took to herself another one; but the latter proved worse

[020-084]even than the former, and incurred dismissal in favour of the first

[020-085]Pole, who, during the time of his unemployment, had nevertheless

[020-086]hovered around the Grandmother’s chair, and from time to time obtruded

[020-087]his head over her shoulder. At length the old lady became desperate,

[020-088]for the second Pole, when dismissed, imitated his predecessor by

[020-089]declining to go away; with the result that one Pole remained standing

[020-090]on the right of the victim, and the other on her left; from which

[020-091]vantage points the pair quarrelled, abused each other concerning the

[020-092]stakes and rounds, and exchanged the epithet “laidak”[4] and other

[020-093]Polish terms of endearment. Finally, they effected a mutual

[020-094]reconciliation, and, tossing the money about anyhow, played simply at

[020-095]random. Once more quarrelling, each of them staked money on his own

[020-096]side of the Grandmother’s chair (for instance, the one Pole staked upon

[020-097]the red, and the other one upon the black), until they had so confused

[020-098]and browbeaten the old lady that, nearly weeping, she was forced to

[020-099]appeal to the head croupier for protection, and to have the two Poles

[020-100]expelled. No time was lost in this being done, despite the rascals’

[020-101]cries and protestations that the old lady was in their debt, that she

[020-102]had cheated them, and that her general behaviour had been mean and

[020-103]dishonourable. The same evening the unfortunate Potapitch related the

[020-104]story to me with tears complaining that the two men had filled their

[020-105]pockets with money (he himself had seen them do it) which had been

[020-106]shamelessly pilfered from his mistress. For instance, one Pole demanded

[020-107]of the Grandmother fifty gülden for his trouble, and then staked the

[020-108]money by the side of her [[risk|stake]]. She happened to win; whereupon he cried

[020-109]out that the winning stake was his, and hers the loser. As soon as the

[020-110]two Poles had been expelled, Potapitch left the room, and reported to

[020-111]the authorities that the men’s pockets were full of gold; and, on the

[020-112]Grandmother also requesting the head croupier to look into the affair,

[020-113]the police made their appearance, and, despite the protests of the

[020-114]Poles (who, indeed, had been caught redhanded), their pockets were

[020-115]turned inside out, and the contents handed over to the Grandmother. In

[020-116]fact, in, view of the circumstance that she lost all day, the croupiers

[020-117]and other authorities of the Casino showed her every attention; and on

[020-118]her fame spreading through the town, visitors of every nationality—even

[020-119]the most knowing of them, the most distinguished—crowded to get a

[020-120]glimpse of “la vieille comtesse russe, tombée en enfance,” who had lost

[020-121]“so many millions.”

[020-123]Yet with the money which the authorities restored to her from the

[020-124]pockets of the Poles the Grandmother effected very, very little, for

[020-125]there soon arrived to take his countrymen’s place, a third Pole—a man

[020-126]who could speak Russian fluently, was dressed like a gentleman (albeit

[020-127]in lacqueyish fashion), and sported a huge moustache. Though polite

[020-128]enough to the old lady, he took a high hand with the bystanders. In

[020-129]short, he offered himself less as a servant than as an _entertainer_.

[020-130]After each round he would turn to the old lady, and swear terrible

[020-131]oaths to the effect that he was a “Polish gentleman of honour” who

[020-132]would [[contempt|scorn]] to take a kopeck of her money; and, though he repeated

[020-133]these oaths so often that at length she grew alarmed, he had her play

[020-134]in hand, and began to win on her behalf; wherefore, she felt that she

[020-135]could not well get rid of him. An hour later the two Poles who, earlier

[020-136]in the day, had been expelled from the Casino, made a reappearance

[020-137]behind the old lady’s chair, and renewed their offers of service—even

[020-138]if it were only to be sent on messages; but from Potapitch I

[020-139]subsequently had it that between these rascals and the said “gentleman

[020-140]of honour” there passed a wink, as well as that the latter put

[020-141]something into their hands. Next, since the Grandmother had not yet

[020-142]lunched—she had scarcely for a moment left her chair—one of the two

[020-143]Poles ran to the restaurant of the Casino, and brought her thence a cup

[020-144]of soup, and afterwards some tea. In fact, _both_ the Poles hastened to

[020-145]perform this office. Finally, towards the close of the day, when it was

[020-146]clear that the Grandmother was about to play her last bank-note, there

[020-147]could be seen standing behind her chair no fewer than six natives of

[020-148]Poland—persons who, as yet, had been neither audible nor visible; and

[020-149]as soon as ever the old lady played the note in question, they took no

[020-150]further notice of her, but pushed their way past her chair to the

[020-151]table; seized the money, and staked it—shouting and disputing the

[020-152]while, and arguing with the “gentleman of honour” (who also had

[020-153]forgotten the Grandmother’s existence), as though he were their equal.

[020-154]Even when the Grandmother had lost her all, and was returning (about

[020-155]eight o’clock) to the hotel, some three or four Poles could not bring

[020-156]themselves to leave her, but went on running beside her chair and

[020-157]volubly protesting that the Grandmother had cheated them, and that she

[020-158]ought to be made to surrender what was not her own. Thus the party

[020-159]arrived at the hotel; whence, presently, the gang of rascals was

[020-160]ejected neck and crop.

[020-161]According to Potapitch’s calculations, the Grandmother lost, that day,

[020-162]a total of ninety thousand roubles, in addition to the money which she

[020-163]had lost the day before. Every paper security which she had brought

[020-164]with her—five percent bonds, internal loan scrip, and what not—she had

[020-165]changed into cash. Also, I could not but marvel at the way in which,

[020-166]for seven or eight hours at a stretch, she sat in that chair of hers,

[020-167]almost never leaving the table. Again, Potapitch told me that there

[020-168]were three occasions on which she really began to win; but that, led on

[020-169]by false hopes, she was unable to tear herself away at the right

[020-170]moment. Every gambler knows how a person may sit a day and a night at

[020-171]cards without ever casting a glance to right or to left.
