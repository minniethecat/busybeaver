# Marfa

Marfa is the Grandmother's maid, an even more peripheral figure than [[potapitch|Potapitch]], mentioned only in connection with the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s travelling retinue (012-060). Her presence in the novel, barely registered, is nonetheless significant: she represents the invisible labour on which the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s authority depends.

## Role in the Novel

Marfa has no dialogue and no individual action. She exists as part of the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s travelling establishment—a figure of the feudal [[russia|Russia]]n household transplanted to the modern European spa. Her silence is the novel's acknowledgment that some lives are not narrated, merely recorded in passing.

Among the servant class in *The Gambler*, Marfa occupies the lowest rung of visibility. [[potapitch|Potapitch]] at least receives direct commands and is addressed by name; Marfa is subsumed entirely into the collective "retinue." This hierarchy within servitude mirrors the novel's larger social stratification: even among the powerless, there are degrees of invisibility.

## The Invisible Labour of the Feudal Household

Marfa's function is structural rather than dramatic. The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s ability to travel from [[russia|Russia]] to [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] with the same authority she exercises at home depends on the labour of figures like Marfa—dressing, packing, attending, and supporting the performance of aristocratic command. The novel registers this dependence without dwelling on it: Marfa is mentioned, then forgotten, because the narrative consciousness ([[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexei]]'s) shares the aristocracy's blindness to service.

This pattern recurs throughout Dostoyevsky's work. Servants appear as atmospheric fixtures rather than characters—the porter in *Crime and Punishment*, the waiter in *Notes from Underground*. Their function is to make visible the social architecture their masters inhabit without drawing attention to themselves. Marfa is the purest example in *The Gambler*: she is present and absent simultaneously.

## Key Scenes

> "Here, Potapitch! she cried to..." (012-060) — Marfa is present in the Grandmother's retinue but not individually addressed.

> "You go and tell that fool of..." (012-087) — the Grandmother's commands flow through Potapitch, not Marfa, who remains in the background.

## Relationships

- [[grandmother|Grandmother]] — mistress, source of identity and purpose
- [[potapitch|Potapitch]] — fellow servant, senior in the household hierarchy

## The Gender of Service

Marfa's invisibility is compounded by gender. [[potapitch|Potapitch]], as a male servant, is trusted with errands and messages; Marfa, as a maid, is confined to domestic labour that requires no acknowledgment. The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]—a woman who commands men, who defies her own family's expectations—reproduces within her household the very gender hierarchy she challenges in the world of the casino. Her authority over men depends, paradoxically, on the invisible labour of a woman who has no authority at all.
