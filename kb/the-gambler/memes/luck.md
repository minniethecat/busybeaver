# Luck

Luck, as understood by the gamblers of [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]], is not mere [[chance|probability]] but a quasi-personal force that can be attracted, alienated, or exhausted. Alexei's winning streak is attributed to "[[chance|luck]]" [003-148] — not mathematics, not strategy — and when [[chance|luck]] turns, as it always does, the gambler experiences the reversal as betrayal rather than statistical regression. Luck is the gambler's theology: an invisible agency that rewards the faithful and abandons the unworthy. [023-169]

## Definition

In the novel's gambling culture, [[chance|luck]] is conceived as a finite, transferable property. Women have better [[chance|luck]] than men [011-096]; some people "attract" [[chance|luck]] while others repel it [023-169]; [[chance|luck]] can be "tried" [016-111] as though it were a tool rather than a description of past outcomes. This reification of [[chance|luck]] — treating [[chance|randomness]] as an entity — is the cognitive error on which the entire gambling economy of [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] depends.

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Luck serves as the novel's plot engine — Alexei's improbable winning streak [003-148] [023-134] is the novel's great exception, the moment when [[chance|luck]] appears to reward the gambler's faith. But Dostoyevsky is careful to frame this streak as the anomaly that proves the rule. Alexei wins not because he is favoured by fortune but because, in a long enough sequence of bets, winning streaks happen.

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s experience is the novel's cautionary counterpoint: her [[chance|luck]] "took a marked change for the worse" [011-070] — the same [[chance|luck]] that briefly favoured her abandons her, and she loses everything. Luck, in *The Gambler*, is a loan, not a gift; it is always called in.

### Key Passages

> …I should never have had such luck. Once more I staked the whole. [003-148]

Alexei's retrospective amazement at his own [[chance|luck]] captures the gambler's relationship with [[chance|randomness]]: even the winner cannot quite believe it. The [[obsession|compulsion]] to [[bet|stake]] "the whole" — to test [[chance|luck]] to its limit — is the gambler's characteristic move.

> …later, her luck took a marked change for the worse. [011-070]

The novel's most economical statement on [[chance|luck]]: it changes. Not because of anything the gambler did, but because [[chance|randomness]] has no memory. The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s belief that her "system" had failed her misses the point — [[chance|luck]] has no system.

> …luck attaches itself to, say, the red, and forsakes the black. [023-169]

Alexei's folk theory of [[chance|luck]] — that it "attaches" to colours like a preference — reveals how gamblers construct narratives around [[chance|randomness]]. Luck is the story they tell themselves to explain why they won or lost.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's treatment of [[chance|luck]] is quietly subversive. The novel appears to endorse the gambler's worldview — Alexei does win, the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] does experience runs of good [[chance|luck]] — but the underlying mechanics are always mathematical. The house edge at roulette is real; the gambler's [[chance|luck]] is temporary; the longer one [[gambling|play]]s, the more certainly one loses.

What makes *The Gambler* distinctive is that Dostoyevsky does not moralise a[[winning|gain]]st this delusion. He presents the belief in [[chance|luck]] as psychologically necessary — without it, no one would gamble, and without gambling, the characters would have to confront the emptiness of their lives directly. Luck is a fiction, but it is a fiction that gives shape and meaning to otherwise desperate lives.

The autobiographical element is pointed: Dostoyevsky knew — mathematically, experientially — that roulette was a [[loss|losing]] game, yet he could not stop [[gambling|play]]ing. His own belief in [[chance|luck]], however ironic, was the engine of his ruin. The novel is his attempt to anato[[bet|mise]] a delusion he himself could not escape.
