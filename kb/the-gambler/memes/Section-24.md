# The Gambler · Section 24

[024-001]Yet some whim or other led me, on remarking that the red had come up

[024-002]consecutively for seven times, to attach myself to that colour.

[024-003]Probably this was mostly due to self-conceit, for I wanted to astonish

[024-004]the bystanders with the riskiness of my [[gambling|play]]. Also, I remember that—oh,

[024-005]strange sensation!—I suddenly, and without any challenge from my own

[024-006]presumption, became obsessed with a _desire_ to take risks. If the

[024-007]spirit has passed through a great many sensations, possibly it can no

[024-008]longer be sated with them, but grows more excited, and demands more

[024-009]sensations, and stronger and stronger ones, until at length it falls

[024-010]exhausted. Certainly, if the rules of [[roulette|the game]] had permitted even of my

[024-011]staking fifty thousand florins at a time, I should have staked them.

[024-012]All of a sudden I heard exclamations arising that the whole thing was a

[024-013]marvel, since the red was turning up for the fourteenth time!

[024-014]“Monsieur a gagné cent mille florins,” a voice exclaimed beside me.

[024-015]I awoke to my senses. What? I had won a hundred thousand florins? If

[024-016]so, what more did I need to win? I grasped the banknotes, stuffed them

[024-017]into my pockets, raked in the gold without counting it, and started to

[024-018]leave the Casino. As I passed through the salons people smiled to see

[024-019]my bulging pockets and unsteady gait, for the weight which I was

[024-020]carrying must have amounted to half a pood! Several hands I saw

[024-021]stretched out in my direction, and as I passed I filled them with all

[024-022]the money that I could grasp in my own. At length two Jews stopped me

[024-023]near the exit.

[024-024]“You are a bold young fellow,” one said, “but mind you depart early

[024-025]tomorrow—as early as you can—for if you do not you will lose everything

[024-026]that you have won.”

[024-027]But I did not heed them. The Avenue was so dark that it was barely

[024-028]possible to distinguish one’s hand before one’s face, while the

[024-029]distance to the hotel was half a verst or so; but I feared neither

[024-030]pickpockets nor highwaymen. Indeed, never since my boyhood have I done

[024-031]that. Also, I cannot remember what I thought about on the way. I only

[024-032]felt a sort of fearful pleasure—the pleasure of success, of conquest,

[024-033]of power (how can I best express it?). Likewise, before me there

[024-034]flitted the image of [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]; and I kept remembering, and reminding

[024-035]myself, that it was to _her_ I was going, that it was in _her_ presence

[024-036]I should soon be standing, that it was _she_ to whom I should soon be

[024-037]able to relate and show everything. Scarcely once did I recall what she

[024-038]had lately said to me, or the reason why I had left her, or all those

[024-039]varied sensations which I had been experiencing a bare hour and a half

[024-040]ago. No, those sensations seemed to be things of the past, to be things

[024-041]which had righted themselves and grown old, to be things concerning

[024-042]which we needed to trouble ourselves no longer, since, for us, life was

[024-043]about to begin anew. Yet I had just reached the end of the Avenue when

[024-044]there _did_ come upon me a fear of being robbed or murdered. With each

[024-045]step the fear increased until, in my terror, I almost started to run.

[024-046]Suddenly, as I issued from the Avenue, there burst upon me the lights

[024-047]of the hotel, sparkling with a myriad lamps! Yes, thanks be to God, I

[024-048]had reached home!

[024-049]Running up to my room, I flung open the door of it. Polina was still on

[024-050]the sofa, with a lighted candle in front of her, and her hands clasped.

[024-051]As I entered she stared at me in astonishment (for, at the moment, I

[024-052]must have presented a strange spectacle). All I did, however, was to

[024-053]halt before her, and fling upon the table my burden of [[fortune|wealth]].

[024-054]XV

[024-055]I remember, too, how, without moving from her place, or changing her

[024-056]attitude, she gazed into my face.

[024-057]“I have won two hundred thousand francs!” cried I as I pulled out my

[024-058]last sheaf of bank-notes. The pile of paper currency occupied the whole

[024-059]table. I could not withdraw my eyes from it. Consequently, for a moment

[024-060]or two Polina escaped my mind. Then I set myself to arrange the pile in

[024-061]order, and to sort the notes, and to mass the gold in a separate heap.

[024-062]That done, I left everything where it lay, and proceeded to pace the

[024-063]room with rapid strides as I lost myself in thought. Then I darted to

[024-064]the table once more, and began to recount the money; until all of a

[024-065]sudden, as though I had remembered something, I rushed to the door, and

[024-066]closed and double-locked it. Finally I came to a meditative halt before

[024-067]my little trunk.

[024-068]“Shall I put the money there until tomorrow?” I asked, turning sharply

[024-069]round to Polina as the recollection of her returned to me.

[024-070]She was still in her old place—still making not a sound. Yet her eyes

[024-071]had followed every one of my movements. Somehow in her face there was a

[024-072]strange expression—an expression which I did not like. I think that I

[024-073]shall not be wrong if I say that it indicated sheer hatred.

[024-074]Impulsively I approached her.

[024-075]“Polina,” I said, “here are twenty-five thousand florins—fifty thousand

[024-076]francs, or more. Take them, and tomorrow throw them in De Griers’

[024-077]face.”

[024-078]She returned no answer.

[024-079]“Or, if you should prefer,” I continued, “let me take them to him

[024-080]myself tomorrow—yes, early tomorrow morning. Shall I?”

[024-081]Then all at once she burst out laughing, and laughed for a long while.

[024-082]With astonishment and a feeling of offence I gazed at her. Her laughter

[024-083]was too like the derisive merriment which she had so often indulged in

[024-084]of late—merriment which had broken forth always at the time of my most

[024-085]passionate explanations. At length she ceased, and frowned at me from

[024-086]under her eyebrows.

[024-087]“I am _not_ going to take your money,” she said contemptuously.

[024-088]“Why not?” I cried. “Why not, Polina?”

[024-089]“Because I am not in the habit of receiving money for nothing.”

[024-090]“But I am offering it to you as a _friend_. In the same way I would

[024-091]offer you my very life.”

[024-092]Upon this she threw me a long, questioning glance, as though she were

[024-093]seeking to probe me to the depths.

[024-094]“You are giving too much for me,” she remarked with a smile. “The

[024-095]beloved of De Griers is not worth fifty thousand francs.”

[024-096]“Oh Polina, how can you speak so?” I exclaimed reproachfully. “Am _I_

[024-097]De Griers?”

[024-098]“You?” she cried with her eyes suddenly flashing. “Why, I _hate_ you!

[024-099]Yes, yes, I _hate_ you! I love you no more than I do De Griers.”

[024-100]Then she buried her face in her hands, and relapsed into hysterics. I

[024-101]darted to her side. Somehow I had an intuition of something having

[024-102]happened to her which had nothing to do with myself. She was like a

[024-103]person temporarily insane.

[024-104]“Buy me, would you, would you? Would you buy me for fifty thousand

[024-105]francs as De Griers did?” she gasped between her convulsive sobs.

[024-106]I clasped her in my arms, kissed her hands and feet, and fell upon my

[024-107]knees before her.

[024-108]Presently the hysterical fit passed away, and, laying her hands upon my

[024-109]shoulders, she gazed for a while into my face, as though trying to read

[024-110]it—something I said to her, but it was clear that she did not hear it.

[024-111]Her face looked so dark and despondent that I began to fear for her

[024-112]reason. At length she drew me towards herself—a trustful smile playing

[024-113]over her features; and then, as suddenly, she pushed me away again as

[024-114]she eyed me dimly.

[024-115]Finally she threw herself upon me in an embrace.

[024-116]“You love me?” she said. “_Do_ you?—you who were willing even to

[024-117]quarrel with [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] at my bidding?”

[024-118]Then she laughed—laughed as though something dear, but laughable, had

[024-119]recurred to her memory. Yes, she laughed and wept at the same time.

[024-120]What was I to do? I was like a man in a fever. I remember that she

[024-121]began to say something to me—though _what_ I do not know, since she

[024-122]spoke with a feverish lisp, as though she were trying to tell me

[024-123]something very quickly. At intervals, too, she would break off into the

[024-124]smile which I was beginning to dread. “No, no!” she kept repeating.

[024-125]“_You_ are my dear one; _you_ are the man I trust.” Again she laid her

[024-126]hands upon my shoulders, and again she gazed at me as she reiterated:

[024-127]“You love me, you love me? Will you _always_ love me?” I could not take

[024-128]my eyes off her. Never before had I seen her in this mood of humility

[024-129]and affection. True, the mood was the outcome of hysteria; but—! All of

[024-130]a sudden she noticed my ardent gaze, and smiled slightly. The next

[024-131]moment, for no apparent reason, she began to talk of [[mr-astley|Astley]].

[024-132]She continued talking and talking about him, but I could not make out

[024-133]all she said—more particularly when she was endeavouring to tell me of

[024-134]something or other which had happened recently. On the whole, she

[024-135]appeared to be laughing at Astley, for she kept repeating that he was

[024-136]waiting for her, and did I know whether, even at that moment, he was

[024-137]not standing beneath the window? “Yes, yes, he is there,” she said.

[024-138]“Open the window, and see if he is not.” She pushed me in that

[024-139]direction; yet, no sooner did I make a movement to obey her behest than

[024-140]she burst into laughter, and I remained beside her, and she embraced

[024-141]me.

[024-142]“Shall we go away tomorrow?” presently she asked, as though some

[024-143]disturbing thought had recurred to her recollection. “How would it be

[024-144]if we were to try and overtake Grandmamma? I think we should do so at

[024-145]Berlin. And what think you she would have to say to us when we caught

[024-146]her up, and her eyes first lit upon us? What, too, about Mr. Astley?

[024-147]_He_ would not leap from the Shlangenberg for my sake! No! Of that I am

[024-148]very sure!”—and she laughed. “Do you know where he is going next year?

[024-149]He says he intends to go to the North Pole for scientific

[024-150]investigations, and has invited me to go with him! Ha, ha, ha! He also

[024-151]says that we Russians know nothing, can do nothing, without European

[024-152]help. But he is a good fellow all the same. For instance, he does not

[024-153]blame the General in the matter, but declares that [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]—that

[024-154]love—But no; I do not know, I do not know.” She stopped suddenly, as

[024-155]though she had said her say, and was feeling bewildered. “What poor

[024-156]creatures these people are. How sorry I am for them, and for

[024-157]Grandmamma! But when are you going to kill De Griers? Surely you do not

[024-158]intend actually to murder him? You fool! Do you suppose that I should

[024-159]_allow_ you to fight De Griers? Nor shall you kill the Baron.” Here she

[024-160]burst out laughing. “How absurd you looked when you were talking to the

[024-161]Burmergelms! I was watching you all the time—watching you from where I

[024-162]was sitting. And how unwilling you were to go when I sent you! Oh, how

[024-163]I laughed and laughed!”

[024-164]Then she kissed and embraced me again; again she pressed her face to

[024-165]mine with tender [[love|passion]]. Yet I neither saw nor heard her, for my head

[024-166]was in a whirl....

[024-167]It must have been about seven o’clock in the morning when I awoke.

[024-168]Daylight had come, and Polina was sitting by my side—a strange

[024-169]expression on her face, as though she had seen a vision and was unable

[024-170]to collect her thoughts. She too had just awoken, and was now staring

[024-171]at the money on the table. My head ached; it felt heavy. I attempted to

[024-172]take Polina’s hand, but she pushed me from her, and leapt from the

[024-173]sofa. The dawn was full of mist, for rain had fallen, yet she moved to

[024-174]the window, opened it, and, leaning her elbows upon the window-sill,

[024-175]thrust out her head and shoulders to take the air. In this position did

[024-176]she remain for several minutes, without ever looking round at me, or

[024-177]listening to what I was saying. Into my head there came the uneasy

[024-178]thought: What is to happen now? How is it all to end? Suddenly Polina

[024-179]rose from the window, approached the table, and, looking at me with an

[024-180]expression of infinite aversion, said with lips which quivered with

[024-181]anger:
