# The Gambler · Section 16

[016-001]I hastened to translate.

[016-002]“Oui, Madame,” was the croupier’s polite reply. “No single [[bet|stake]] must

[016-003]exceed four thousand florins. That is the regulation.”

[016-004]“Then there is nothing else for it. We must risk in gülden.”

[016-005]“Le jeu est fait!” the croupier called. The wheel revolved, and stopped

[016-006]at thirty. We had lost!

[016-007]“Again, again, again! Stake again!” shouted [[grandmother|the old lady]]. Without

[016-008]attempting to oppose her further, but merely shrugging my shoulders, I

[016-009]placed twelve more ten-gülden pieces upon the table. The wheel whirled

[016-010]around and around, with the Grandmother simply quaking as she watched

[016-011]its revolutions.

[016-012]“Does she again think that zero is going to be the winning coup?”

[016-013]thought I, as I stared at her in astonishment. Yet an absolute

[016-014]assurance of winning was shining on her face; she looked perfectly

[016-015]convinced that zero was about to be called again. At length the ball

[016-016]dropped off into one of the notches.

[016-017]“Zero!” cried the croupier.

[016-018]“Ah!!!” screamed the old lady as she turned to me in a whirl of

[016-019]triumph.

[016-020]I myself was at heart a gambler. At that moment I became acutely

[016-021]conscious both of that fact and of the fact that my hands and knees

[016-022]were shaking, and that the blood was beating in my brain. Of course

[016-023]this was a rare occasion—an occasion on which zero had turned up no

[016-024]less than three times within a dozen rounds; yet in such an event there

[016-025]was nothing so very surprising, seeing that, only three days ago, I

[016-026]myself had been a witness to zero turning up _three times in

[016-027]succession_, so that one of the players who was recording the coups on

[016-028]paper was moved to remark that for several days past zero had never

[016-029]turned up at all!

[016-030]With the Grandmother, as with any one who has won a very large sum, the

[016-031]management settled up with great attention and respect, since she was

[016-032]fortunate to have to receive no less than 4200 gülden. Of these gülden

[016-033]the odd 200 were paid her in gold, and the remainder in bank notes.

[016-034]This time the old lady did not call for [[potapitch|Potapitch]]; for that she was too

[016-035]preoccupied. Though not outwardly shaken by the event (indeed, she

[016-036]seemed perfectly calm), she was trembling inwardly from head to foot.

[016-037]At length, completely absorbed in [[roulette|the game]], she burst out:

[016-038]“[[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]], did not the croupier just say that 4000 florins

[016-039]were the most that could be staked at any one time? Well, take these

[016-040]4000, and [[risk|stake]] them upon the red.”

[016-041]To oppose her was useless. Once more the wheel revolved.

[016-042]“Rouge!” proclaimed the croupier.

[016-043]Again 4000 florins—in all 8000!

[016-044]“Give me them,” commanded the Grandmother, “and stake the other 4000

[016-045]upon the red again.”

[016-046]I did so.

[016-047]“Rouge!” proclaimed the croupier.

[016-048]“Twelve thousand!” cried the old lady. “Hand me the whole lot. Put the

[016-049]gold into this purse here, and count [[gambling-bank|the bank]] notes. Enough! Let us go

[016-050]home. Wheel my chair away.”

[016-051]XI

[016-052]The chair, with the old lady beaming in it, was wheeled away towards

[016-053]the doors at the further end of the salon, while our party hastened to

[016-054]crowd around her, and to offer her their congratulations. In fact,

[016-055]eccentric as was her conduct, it was also overshadowed by her triumph;

[016-056]with the result that the General no longer feared to be publicly

[016-057]compromised by being seen with such a strange woman, but, smiling in a

[016-058]condescending, cheerfully familiar way, as though he were soothing a

[016-059]child, he offered his greetings to the old lady. At the same time, both

[016-060]he and the rest of the spectators were visibly impressed. Everywhere

[016-061]people kept pointing to the Grandmother, and talking about her. Many

[016-062]people even walked beside her chair, in order to view her the better

[016-063]while, at a little distance, [[mr-astley|Astley]] was carrying on a conversation on

[016-064]the subject with two English acquaintances of his. De Griers was simply

[016-065]overflowing with smiles and compliments, and a number of fine ladies

[016-066]were staring at the Grandmother as though she had been something

[016-067]curious.

[016-068]“Quelle victoire!” exclaimed De Griers.

[016-069]“Mais, Madame, c’était du feu!” added [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] with an elusive

[016-070]smile.

[016-071]“Yes, I have won twelve thousand florins,” replied the old lady. “And

[016-072]then there is all this gold. With it the total ought to come to nearly

[016-073]thirteen thousand. How much is that in Russian money? Six thousand

[016-074]roubles, I think?”

[016-075]However, I calculated that the sum would exceed seven thousand

[016-076]roubles—or, at the present rate of exchange, even eight thousand.

[016-077]“Eight thousand roubles! What a splendid thing! And to think of you

[016-078]simpletons sitting there and doing nothing! Potapitch! Martha! See what

[016-079]I have won!”

[016-080]“How _did_ you do it, Madame?” Martha exclaimed ecstatically. “Eight

[016-081]thousand roubles!”

[016-082]“And I am going to give you fifty gülden apiece. There they are.”

[016-083]Potapitch and Martha rushed towards her to kiss her hand.

[016-084]“And to each bearer also I will give a ten-gülden piece. Let them have

[016-085]it out of the gold, Alexis Ivanovitch. But why is this footman bowing

[016-086]to me, and that other man as well? Are they congratulating me? Well,

[016-087]let them have ten gülden apiece.”

[016-088]“Madame la princesse—Un pauvre expatrié—Malheur continuel—Les princes

[016-089]russes sont si généreux!” said a man who for some time past had been

[016-090]hanging around the old lady’s chair—a personage who, dressed in a

[016-091]shabby frockcoat and coloured waistcoat, kept taking off his cap, and

[016-092]smiling pathetically.

[016-093]“Give him ten gülden,” said the Grandmother. “No, give him twenty. Now,

[016-094]enough of that, or I shall never get done with you all. Take a moment’s

[016-095]rest, and then carry me away. Prascovia, I mean to buy a new dress for

[016-096]you tomorrow. Yes, and for you too, Mlle. Blanche. Please translate,

[016-097]Prascovia.”

[016-098]“Merci, Madame,” replied Mlle. Blanche gratefully as she twisted her

[016-099]face into the mocking smile which usually she kept only for the benefit

[016-100]of De Griers and the General. The latter looked confused, and seemed

[016-101]greatly relieved when we reached the Avenue.

[016-102]“How surprised Theodosia too will be!” went on the Grandmother

[016-103](thinking of the General’s nursemaid). “She, like yourselves, shall

[016-104]have the price of a new gown. Here, Alexis Ivanovitch! Give that beggar

[016-105]something” (a crooked-backed ragamuffin had approached to stare at us).

[016-106]“But perhaps he is _not_ a beggar—only a rascal,” I replied.

[016-107]“Never mind, never mind. Give him a gülden.”

[016-108]I approached the beggar in question, and handed him the coin. Looking

[016-109]at me in great astonishment, he silently accepted the gülden, while

[016-110]from his person there proceeded a strong smell of liquor.

[016-111]“Have you never tried your [[chance|luck]], Alexis Ivanovitch?”

[016-112]“No, Madame.”

[016-113]“Yet just now I could see that you were burning to do so?”

[016-114]“I _do_ mean to try my [[fortune|luck]] presently.”

[016-115]“Then stake everything upon zero. You have seen how it ought to be

[016-116]done? How much capital do you possess?”

[016-117]“Two hundred gülden, Madame.”

[016-118]“Not very much. See here; I will lend you five hundred if you wish.

[016-119]Take this purse of mine.” With that she added sharply to the General:

[016-120]“But _you_ need not expect to receive any.”

[016-121]This seemed to upset him, but he said nothing, and De Griers contented

[016-122]himself by scowling.

[016-123]“Que diable!” he whispered to the General. “C’est une terrible

[016-124]vieille.”

[016-125]“Look! Another beggar, another beggar!” exclaimed the grandmother.

[016-126]“Alexis Ivanovitch, go and give him a gülden.”

[016-127]As she spoke I saw approaching us a grey-headed old man with a wooden

[016-128]leg—a man who was dressed in a blue frockcoat and carrying a staff. He

[016-129]looked like an old soldier. As soon as I tendered him the coin he fell

[016-130]back a step or two, and eyed me threateningly.

[016-131]“Was ist der Teufel!” he cried, and appended thereto a round dozen of

[016-132]oaths.

[016-133]“The man is a perfect fool!” exclaimed the Grandmother, waving her

[016-134]hand. “Move on now, for I am simply famished. When we have lunched we

[016-135]will return to that place.”

[016-136]“What?” cried I. “You are going to [[gambling|play]] _again?_”

[016-137]“What else do you suppose?” she retorted. “Are you going only to sit

[016-138]here, and grow sour, and let me look at you?”

[016-139]“Madame,” said De Griers confidentially, “les chances peuvent tourner.

[016-140]Une seule mauvaise chance, et vous perdrez tout—surtout avec votre jeu.

[016-141]C’était terrible!”

[016-142]“Oui; vous perdrez absolument,” put in Mlle. Blanche.

[016-143]“What has that got to do with _you?_” retorted the old lady. “It is not

[016-144]_your_ money that I am going to lose; it is my own. And where is that

[016-145]Mr. Astley of yours?” she added to myself.

[016-146]“He stayed behind in the Casino.”

[016-147]“What a pity! He is such a nice sort of man!”

[016-148]Arriving home, and meeting the landlord on the staircase, the

[016-149]Grandmother called him to her side, and boasted to him of her

[016-150][[winning|winnings]]—thereafter doing the same to Theodosia, and conferring upon

[016-151]her thirty gülden; after which she bid her serve luncheon. The meal

[016-152]over, Theodosia and Martha broke into a joint flood of ecstasy.

[016-153]“I was watching you all the time, Madame,” quavered Martha, “and I

[016-154]asked Potapitch what mistress was trying to do. And, my word! the heaps

[016-155]and _heaps_ of money that were lying upon the table! Never in my life

[016-156]have I seen so much money. And there were gentlefolk around it, and

[016-157]other gentlefolk sitting down. So, I asked Potapitch where all these

[016-158]gentry had come from; for, thought I, maybe the Holy Mother of God will

[016-159]help our mistress among them. Yes, I prayed for you, Madame, and my

[016-160]heart died within me, so that I kept trembling and trembling. The Lord

[016-161]be with her, I thought to myself; and in answer to my prayer He has now

[016-162]sent you what He has done! Even yet I tremble—I tremble to think of it

[016-163]all.”

[016-164]“Alexis Ivanovitch,” said the old lady, “after luncheon,—that is to

[016-165]say, about four o’clock—get ready to go out with me again. But in the

[016-166]meanwhile, good-bye. Do not forget to call a doctor, for I must take

[016-167]the waters. Now go and get rested a little.”

[016-168]I left the Grandmother’s presence in a state of bewilderment.

[016-169]Vainly I endeavoured to imagine what would become of our party, or what

[016-170]turn the affair would next take. I could perceive that none of the

[016-171]party had yet recovered their presence of mind—least of all the

[016-172]General. The factor of the Grandmother’s appearance in place of the

[016-173]hourly expected telegram to announce her death (with, of course,

[016-174]resultant legacies) had so upset the whole scheme of intentions and

[016-175]projects that it was with a decided feeling of apprehension and growing

[016-176]paralysis that the conspirators viewed any future performances of the

[016-177]old lady at roulette. Yet this second factor was not quite so important

[016-178]as the first, since, though the Grandmother had twice declared that she

[016-179]did not intend to give the General any money, that declaration was not

[016-180]a complete ground for the abandonment of hope. Certainly De Griers,

[016-181]who, with the General, was up to the neck in the affair, had not wholly

[016-182]lost courage; and I felt sure that Mlle. Blanche also—Mlle. Blanche who

[016-183]was not only as deeply involved as the other two, but also expectant of

[016-184]becoming Madame General and an important legatee—would not lightly

[016-185]surrender the position, but would use her every resource of coquetry

[016-186]upon the old lady, in order to afford a contrast to the impetuous

[016-187][[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], who was difficult to understand, and lacked the art of

[016-188]pleasing.

[016-189]Yet now, when the Grandmother had just performed an astonishing feat at

[016-190]roulette; now, when the old lady’s personality had been so clearly and

[016-191]typically revealed as that of a rugged, arrogant woman who was “tombée

[016-192]en enfance”; now, when everything appeared to be lost,—why, now the

[016-193]Grandmother was as merry as a child which plays with thistle-down.

[016-194]“Good Lord!” I thought with, may God forgive me, a most malicious

[016-195]smile, “every ten-gülden piece which the Grandmother staked must have

[016-196]raised a blister on the General’s heart, and maddened De Griers, and

[016-197]driven Mlle. de Cominges almost to frenzy with the sight of this spoon

[016-198]dangling before her lips.” Another factor is the circumstance that even

[016-199]when, overjoyed at winning, the Grandmother was distributing alms right

[016-200]and left, and taking every one to be a beggar, she again snapped out to

[016-201]the General that he was not going to be allowed any of her money—which

[016-202]meant that the old lady had quite made up her mind on the point, and

[016-203]was sure of it. Yes, danger loomed ahead.
