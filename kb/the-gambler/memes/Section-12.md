# The Gambler · Section 12

[012-001]“I know of nothing else,” asserted [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] doggedly.

[012-002]“But, I do, I do!” I shouted in my fury. “He is waiting also for the

[012-003]old woman’s will, for the reason that it awards Mlle. [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] a dowry.

[012-004]As soon as ever the money is received, she will throw herself upon the

[012-005]Frenchman’s neck. All women are like that. Even the proudest of them

[012-006]become abject slaves where marriage is concerned. What Polina is good

[012-007]for is to fall head over ears in love. That is _my_ opinion. Look at

[012-008]her—especially when she is sitting alone, and plunged in thought. All

[012-009]this was pre-ordained and foretold, and is accursed. Polina could

[012-010]perpetrate any mad act. She—she—But who called me by name?” I broke

[012-011]off. “Who is shouting for me? I heard some one calling in Russian,

[012-012]‘[[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]]!’ It was a woman’s voice. Listen!”

[012-013]At the moment, we were approaching my hotel. We had left the café long

[012-014]ago, without even noticing that we had done so.

[012-015]“Yes, I _did_ hear a woman’s voice calling, but whose I do not know.

[012-016]The someone was calling you in Russian. Ah! NOW I can see whence the

[012-017]cries come. They come from that lady there—the one who is sitting on

[012-018]the settee, the one who has just been escorted to the verandah by a

[012-019]crowd of lacqueys. Behind her see that pile of luggage! She must have

[012-020]arrived by train.”

[012-021]“But why should she be calling _me?_ Hear her calling again! See! She

[012-022]is beckoning to us!”

[012-023]“Yes, so she is,” assented Mr. Astley.

[012-024]“Alexis Ivanovitch, Alexis Ivanovitch! Good heavens, what a stupid

[012-025]fellow!” came in a despairing wail from the verandah.

[012-026]We had almost reached the portico, and I was just setting foot upon the

[012-027]space before it, when my hands fell to my sides in limp astonishment,

[012-028]and my feet glued themselves to the pavement!

[012-029]IX

[012-030]For on the topmost tier of the hotel verandah, after being carried up

[012-031]the steps in an armchair amid a bevy of footmen, maid-servants, and

[012-032]other menials of the hotel, headed by the landlord (that functionary

[012-033]had actually run out to meet a visitor who arrived with so much stir

[012-034]and din, attended by her own retinue, and accompanied by so great a

[012-035]pile of trunks and portmanteaux)—on the topmost tier of the verandah, I

[012-036]say, there was sitting—_the grandmother!_ Yes, it was _she_—rich, and

[012-037]imposing, and seventy-five years of age—Antonida Vassilievna

[012-038]Tarassevitcha, landowner and _grande dame_ of [[moscow|Moscow]]—the “La

[012-039]Baboulenka” who had caused so many telegrams to be sent off and

[012-040]received—who had been dying, yet not dying—who had, in her own person,

[012-041]descended upon us even as snow might fall from the clouds! Though

[012-042]unable to walk, she had arrived borne aloft in an armchair (her mode of

[012-043]conveyance for the last five years), as brisk, aggressive,

[012-044]self-satisfied, bolt-upright, loudly imperious, and generally abusive

[012-045]as ever. In fact, she looked exactly as she had on the only two

[012-046]occasions when I had seen her since my appointment to the General’s

[012-047]household. Naturally enough, I stood petrified with astonishment. She

[012-048]had sighted me a hundred paces off! Even while she was being carried

[012-049]along in her chair she had recognised me, and called me by name and

[012-050]surname (which, as usual, after hearing once, she had remembered ever

[012-051]afterwards).

[012-052]“And this is the woman whom they had thought to see in her grave after

[012-053]making her will!” I thought to myself. “Yet she will outlive us, and

[012-054]every one else in the hotel. Good Lord! what is going to become of us

[012-055]now? What on earth is to happen to the General? She will turn the place

[012-056]upside down!”

[012-057]“My good sir,” the old woman continued in a stentorian voice, “what are

[012-058]you standing _there_ for, with your eyes almost falling out of your

[012-059]head? Cannot you come and say how-do-you-do? Are you too proud to shake

[012-060]hands? Or do you not recognise me? Here, [[potapitch|Potapitch]]!” she cried to an

[012-061]old servant who, dressed in a frock coat and white waistcoat, had a

[012-062]bald, red head (he was the chamberlain who always accompanied her on

[012-063]her journeys). “Just think! Alexis Ivanovitch does not recognise me!

[012-064]They have buried me for good and all! Yes, and after sending hosts of

[012-065]telegrams to know if I were dead or not! Yes, yes, I have heard the

[012-066]whole story. I am very much alive, though, as you may see.”

[012-067]“Pardon me, Antonida Vassilievna,” I replied good humouredly as I

[012-068]recovered my presence of mind. “_I_ have no reason to wish you ill. I

[012-069]am merely rather astonished to see you. Why should I not be so, seeing

[012-070]how unexpected—”

[012-071]“_Why_ should you be astonished? I just got into my chair, and came.

[012-072]Things are quiet enough in the train, for there is no one there to

[012-073]chatter. Have you been out for a walk?”

[012-074]“Yes. I have just been to the Casino.”

[012-075]“Oh? Well, it is quite nice here,” she went on as she looked about her.

[012-076]“The place seems comfortable, and all the trees are out. I like it very

[012-077]well. Are your people at home? Is the General, for instance, indoors?”

[012-078]“Yes; and probably all of them.”

[012-079]“Do they observe the convenances, and keep up appearances? Such things

[012-080]always give one tone. I have heard that they are keeping a carriage,

[012-081]even as Russian gentlefolks ought to do. When abroad, our Russian

[012-082]people always cut a dash. Is Prascovia here too?”

[012-083]“Yes. Polina Alexandrovna is here.”

[012-084]“And the Frenchwoman? However, I will go and look for them myself. Tell

[012-085]me the nearest way to their rooms. Do _you_ like being here?”

[012-086]“Yes, I thank you, Antonida Vassilievna.”

[012-087]“And you, Potapitch, you go and tell that fool of a landlord to reserve

[012-088]me a suitable suite of rooms. They must be handsomely decorated, and

[012-089]not too high up. Have my luggage taken up to them. But what are you

[012-090]tumbling over yourselves for? Why are you all tearing about? What

[012-091]scullions these fellows are!—Who is that with you?” she added to

[012-092]myself.

[012-093]“A Mr. Astley,” I replied.

[012-094]“And who is Mr. Astley?”

[012-095]“A fellow-traveller, and my very good friend, as well as an

[012-096]acquaintance of the General’s.”

[012-097]“Oh, an Englishman? Then that is why he stared at me without even

[012-098]opening his lips. However, I like Englishmen. Now, take me upstairs,

[012-099]direct to their rooms. Where are they lodging?”

[012-100]Madame was lifted up in her chair by the lacqueys, and I preceded her

[012-101]up the grand staircase. Our progress was exceedingly effective, for

[012-102]everyone whom we met stopped to stare at the cortège. It happened that

[012-103]the hotel had the [[honour|reputation]] of being the best, the most expensive, and

[012-104]the most aristocratic in all the spa, and at every turn on the

[012-105]staircase or in the corridors we encountered fine ladies and

[012-106]important-looking Englishmen—more than one of whom hastened downstairs

[012-107]to inquire of the awestruck landlord who the newcomer was. To all such

[012-108]questions he returned the same answer—namely, that [[grandmother|the old lady]] was an

[012-109]influential foreigner, a Russian, a Countess, and a _grande dame_, and

[012-110]that she had taken the suite which, during the previous week, had been

[012-111]tenanted by the Grande Duchesse de N.

[012-112]Meanwhile the cause of [[grandmothers-arrival|the sensation]]—the Grandmother—was being borne

[012-113]aloft in her armchair. Every person whom she met she scanned with an

[012-114]inquisitive eye, after first of all interrogating me about him or her

[012-115]at the top of her voice. She was stout of figure, and, though she could

[012-116]not leave her chair, one felt, the moment that one first looked at her,

[012-117]that she was also tall of stature. Her back was as straight as a board,

[012-118]and never did she lean back in her seat. Also, her large grey head,

[012-119]with its keen, rugged features, remained always erect as she glanced

[012-120]about her in an imperious, challenging sort of way, with looks and

[012-121]gestures that clearly were unstudied. Though she had reached her

[012-122]seventy-sixth year, her face was still fresh, and her teeth had not

[012-123]decayed. Lastly, she was dressed in a black silk gown and white mobcap.

[012-124]“She interests me tremendously,” whispered Mr. Astley as, still

[012-125]smoking, he walked by my side. Meanwhile I was reflecting that probably

[012-126]the old lady knew all about the telegrams, and even about De Griers,

[012-127]though little or nothing about [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]. I said as much to Mr.

[012-128]Astley.

[012-129]But what a frail creature is man! No sooner was my first surprise

[012-130]abated than I found myself rejoicing in the shock which we were about

[012-131]to administer to the General. So much did the thought inspire me that I

[012-132]marched ahead in the gayest of fashions.

[012-133]Our party was lodging on the third floor. Without knocking at the door,

[012-134]or in any way announcing our presence, I threw open the portals, and

[012-135]the Grandmother was borne through them in triumph. As though of set

[012-136]purpose, the whole party chanced at that moment to be assembled in the

[012-137]General’s study. The time was eleven o’clock, and it seemed that an

[012-138]outing of some sort (at which a portion of the party were to drive in

[012-139]carriages, and others to ride on horseback, accompanied by one or two

[012-140]extraneous acquaintances) was being planned. [[general|The General]] was present,

[012-141]and also Polina, the children, the latter’s nurses, De Griers, Mlle.

[012-142]Blanche (attired in a riding-habit), her mother, the young Prince, and

[012-143]a learned German whom I beheld for the first time. Into the midst of

[012-144]this assembly the lacqueys conveyed Madame in her chair, and set her

[012-145]down within three paces of the General!

[012-146]Good heavens! Never shall I forget the spectacle which ensued! Just

[012-147]before our entry, the General had been holding forth to the company,

[012-148]with De Griers in support of him. I may also mention that, for the last

[012-149]two or three days, Mlle. Blanche and De Griers had been making a great

[012-150]deal of the young Prince, under the very nose of the poor General. In

[012-151]short, the company, though decorous and conventional, was in a gay,

[012-152]familiar mood. But no sooner did the Grandmother appear than the

[012-153]General stopped dead in the middle of a word, and, with jaw dropping,

[012-154]stared hard at the old lady—his eyes almost starting out of his head,

[012-155]and his expression as spellbound as though he had just seen a basilisk.

[012-156]In return, the Grandmother stared at him silently and without

[012-157]moving—though with a look of mingled challenge, triumph, and ridicule

[012-158]in her eyes. For ten seconds did the pair remain thus eyeing one

[012-159]another, amid the profound silence of the company; and even De Griers

[012-160]sat petrified—an extraordinary look of uneasiness dawning on his face.

[012-161]As for Mlle. Blanche, she too stared wildly at the Grandmother, with

[012-162]eyebrows raised and her lips parted—while the Prince and the German

[012-163]savant contemplated the tableau in profound amazement. Only Polina

[012-164]looked anything but perplexed or surprised. Presently, however, she too

[012-165]turned as white as a sheet, and then reddened to her temples. Truly the

[012-166]Grandmother’s arrival seemed to be a catastrophe for everybody! For my

[012-167]own part, I stood looking from the Grandmother to the company, and back

[012-168]again, while Mr. Astley, as usual, remained in the background, and

[012-169]gazed calmly and decorously at the scene.

[012-170]“Well, here I am—and instead of a telegram, too!” the Grandmother at

[012-171]last ejaculated, to dissipate the silence. “What? You were not

[012-172]expecting me?”

[012-173]“Antonida Vassilievna! O my dearest mother! But how on earth did you,

[012-174]did you—?” The mutterings of the unhappy General died away.

[012-175]I verily believe that if the Grandmother had held her tongue a few

[012-176]seconds longer she would have had a stroke.

[012-177]“How on earth did I _what?_” she exclaimed. “Why, I just got into the

[012-178]train and came here. What else is the railway meant for? But you

[012-179]thought that I had turned up my toes and left my property to the lot of

[012-180]you. Oh, I know _all_ about the telegrams which you have been

[012-181]dispatching. They must have cost you a pretty sum, I should think, for

[012-182]telegrams are not sent from abroad for nothing. Well, I picked up my

[012-183]heels, and came here. Who is this Frenchman? Monsieur [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]], I

[012-184]suppose?”
