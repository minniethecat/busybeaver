# Inheritance

Inheritance is the absent centre around which the plot of *The Gambler* revolves. Every character's financial calculations depend on the anticipated death of the [[grandmother]] — the wealthy matriarch whose estate promises to rescue the [[General]] from his [[debt]]s, secure [[Mlle Blanche]]'s matrimonial ambitions, and restore [[Alexei Ivanovitch]]'s position in the household. The expectation of inheritance is what makes the present [[ruin|tolerable]]: the [[General]]'s creditors accept his promises because they believe in his grandmother's eventual death, and the [[General]] himself lives on credit extended against a legacy that has not yet — and may never — materialise.

## A Life on Credit

The narrator observes the [[General]]'s situation with characteristic clarity:

> It was therefore clear that he had no inheritance to look for. Evidently, he was living on credit. (022-004)

The phrase "no inheritance to look for" is devastating, because inheritance is all the [[General]] has ever had to look for. His military career is behind him, his [[money|wealth]] is borrowed, his social position depends on a woman who is still alive and shows no sign of dying. The [[General]]'s entire existence is a bet on his grandmother's death — the longest [[gambling|wager]] in the novel, and the one that most closely resembles the [[roulette]] table's logic: stake everything on a single outcome, and wait.

## The Grandmother's Arrival

When the [[grandmother]] arrives in [[Roulettenberg]] alive, healthy, and determined to [[gambling|gamble]] away her fortune, the inheritance plot collapses. She is not dying — she is spending. Every [[franc]] she loses at the [[roulette]] table is a [[franc]] the [[General]] will never inherit. The novel's darkest comedy is that the [[grandmother]]'s [[gambling]] — which the other characters view as senile folly — is actually the most rational action in the book: she is enjoying her [[money]] while she is alive, rather than preserving it for heirs who have been betting on her death.

[[Mr. Astley]] later confirms that the [[grandmother]]'s estate did eventually pass "through inheritance" (029-109) — but by then the [[General]] has already been destroyed, his credit exhausted, his hopes proven foolish. The inheritance, when it comes, comes too late.
