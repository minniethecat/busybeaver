# Pride

[[pride]] in *The Gambler* is not the classical sin of hubris but a fragile and defensive psychological armour worn by characters whose social positions are precarious. Alexei Ivanovich tutors for a [[family]] that despises him (004-029); the General schemes for an [[inheritance]] he may never receive; [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina Alexandrovna]] wages emotional warfare from a position of weakness. In this hothouse of wounded egos, pride becomes the chief obstacle to honesty, reconciliation, and self-knowledge.

## Definition

In Dostoyevsky's treatment, pride operates differently across social stations. For Alexei, [[alexei-ivanovitch|the tutor]] of ambiguous social standing, pride manifests as "pride and aloofness" (004-029)—a defensive withdrawal from a world that refuses to acknowledge him. For [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], pride is the engine of her psychological complexity: "Was it wounded pride? Or was it her own pride, which kept urging her not to believe..." (025-019, 025-024). Pride in the novel is always "wounded pride"—not self-satisfaction but self-protection in response to injury.

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Pride is the emotion that prevents characters from acting in their own best interests. Alexei's pride keeps him from accepting [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]]'s help; [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s pride prevents her from acknowledging her feelings openly; the General's pride about his social position blinds him to the absurdity of his schemes. Every major misunderstanding and missed connection in the novel traces back to characters who cannot bring themselves to speak plainly because pride forbids vulnerability.

The novel's compressed setting—a hotel in a foreign [[spa|spa town]]—intensifies pride's effects. With no escape from one another, characters nurse their wounded pride in close quarters, creating a pressure-cooker atmosphere where every slight is observed and magnified.

### Key Passages

> "Pride and aloofness. For example, a..." (004-029) — Alexei's self-description, establishing pride as a defining trait.

> "Was it wounded pride? Had it come of despair over her decision to come to [[roulettenberg]]?" (025-019) — the narrator's attempt to parse Polina's motivations through pride.

> "It was her own pride, which kept urging her not to believe..." (025-024) — pride as an active, urging force rather than a passive state.

> "...persons who, in your unctuous pride, are forever..." (028-043) — pride seen from the outside, as moral indictment.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's treatment of pride sets *The Gambler* apart from his other novels. Unlike the grand metaphysical pride of Raskolnikov in *Crime and Punishment*, the pride in *The Gambler* is petty, social, and almost comic in its intensity. Characters nurse grievances over perceived slights at the dinner table; they erupt at casual remarks; they [[bet|stake]] fortunes not to become rich but to prove they were right.

This social pride is intimately connected to the novel's gambling theme. The [[roulette]]-table|roulette table]] offers a fantasy of transcending the social hierarchy through pure [[chance|luck]]—a world where pride need not be wounded because fortunes change in an instant. But [[zero-zero|the zero]] always returns, and with it the [[shame|humiliation]]s that pride cannot bear.
