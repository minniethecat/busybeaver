# Fever

**Fever** in *The Gambler* describes the altered psychological state that overtakes characters at the [[roulette]] table — a condition combining intense [[excitement]], physical agitation, and the suspension of rational judgment. Dostoyevsky uses fever as a metaphor for the gambler's loss of self-control.

## The Fever of Play

The gambling fever transforms ordinary individuals. Alexei describes himself during a critical wager: "It was as in a fever that I moved the pile, en bloc, on to the red" (023-074). After his winning streak, he confesses: "I was like a man in a fever" (024-120). The metaphor is not merely literary — it captures the genuine physiological arousal, the racing pulse and quickened breath that accompany high-stakes gambling.

> It was as in a fever that I moved the pile, en bloc, on to the red. (023-074)

The fever also manifests in speech. After his winning streak, Polina speaks to Alexei "with a feverish lisp, as though she were trying to say something, but had not the power" (024-122). Fever distorts language itself — the gambler's words, like his thoughts, lose their usual coherence under the pressure of intense emotion. Dostoyevsky uses this breakdown of speech to signal the dissolution of rational control.

> She spoke with a feverish lisp, as though she were trying to say something, but had not the power. (024-122)

## The Grandmother's Fever

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]] provides the novel's most vivid portrait of gambling fever. At the roulette table, she enters "a perfect fever," commanding her attendants: "Do not hang back!" (015-167). A dignified aristocrat in ordinary life, she becomes "breathless with excitement" (015-066), "shaking with excitement" (015-178) — the fever erasing the composure of age, rank, and wisdom.

> The Grandmother was in a perfect fever. "Do not hang back! Be quick!" (015-167)

What makes the Grandmother's fever so striking is its completeness. She is not merely excited; she is transformed. The woman who moments earlier needed assistance to walk now drives her attendants forward with imperious energy. Fever gives her a temporary vitality that is indistinguishable from destruction — she plays with increasing intensity even as her fortune drains away, the excitement feeding on itself in a closed loop.

## Fever as Moral Condition

Beyond the gambling table, fever also describes Alexei's state in relation to [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]. He rushes "about like a man in a fever" (002-136) in her presence, suggesting that romantic [[obsession]] and gambling [[passion]] share a common psychological root. In both domains, fever marks the point where [[desire]] overwhelms self-possession, and the boundary between [[pleasure]] and [[ruin]] dissolves.

> I rushed about like a man in a fever, and actually beheld her in the salon. (002-136)

## Dostoyevsky's Fever Motif

The fever metaphor belongs to a broader pattern in Dostoyevsky's fiction, where extreme psychological states — fever, delirium, hallucination — serve as windows into his characters' deeper truths. By depicting the gambler's state as a fever rather than a rational choice, Dostoyevsky locates the source of destructive behaviour not in faulty reasoning but in the body itself — in a physiological compulsion that bypasses the will. The fever cannot be argued with; it can only burn itself out.

## Fever and the Temporality of Gambling

Dostoyevsky's use of the fever metaphor is tightly bound to the novel's treatment of time. Feverish states compress and distort temporal experience: moments stretch into eternities, sequences blur, the past and future collapse into an eternal present of heightened sensation. The gambler in a fever does not remember his losses or anticipate his ruin — he exists entirely within the spin, the turn, the call of the number. This temporal distortion is both the fever's pleasure and its danger: it delivers the gambler from the burden of consequence, but in doing so it dismantles the very faculty — foresight — that might save him. The fever, in this sense, is not merely an accompaniment to gambling but its essential condition: the state of consciousness that makes sustained gambling possible by suspending the self's connection to its own future.
