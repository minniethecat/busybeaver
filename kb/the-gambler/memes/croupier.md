# Croupier

The croupier is the operator of the [[roulette]] table in *The Gambler* — a figure who stands at the boundary between the gambler's [[passion]] and the [[casino]]'s mathematical coldness. Unlike the players, who are consumed by [[hope]], [[despair]], and [[obsession]], the croupier embodies mechanical indifference: he spins the wheel, calls the numbers, and pays out [[winning]]s without any visible investment in the outcome. Dostoyevsky presents the croupiers not as characters but as functions of the [[gambling]] apparatus — they are the human face of a system designed to [[ruin]].

## Role at the Table

The croupiers occupy a defined position in the novel's spatial economy of the [[casino]]:

> The croupiers who sat at the two ends of it had not only to watch the stakes, but also to calculate the game — a work which kept their eyes very busy. (003-117)

Each [[roulette]] table has multiple croupiers — "the eight croupiers apportioned to each table" (014-190) — who manage stakes, calculate payouts, and maintain the rhythm of play. They are positioned at each end of the gaming table, their attention divided between the [[money|stakes]] and the wheel's arithmetic. When [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] wins, "the croupier paid me out three times my total stake" (003-144) — the transaction is immediate and impersonal, money passing from the house to the player without comment or judgment.

## The Croupier and the Grandmother

The croupiers' composure is tested by the [[grandmother]]'s arrival at the [[casino]]. Her erratic, high-stakes play disrupts their mechanical routine:

> The croupiers' hopes rose high that such a reckless player would soon come to grief. (015-034)

The croupiers become momentarily invested — hoping for her loss, watching her [[luck]] with more than professional interest. When the chief croupier attends to her personally (015-029), the hierarchy within the casino staff becomes visible: the ordinary croupiers manage ordinary players, but the "chief croupier" is summoned for the extraordinary. And when the ball lands on zero, "the croupier cried out, 'Trente-six'" (015-138) — the call that signals the house's triumph and the gambler's [[ruin]].

## Symbolic Function

In the novel's moral economy, the croupier represents the indifference of [[chance]] itself. He does not care whether the player [[winning|wins]] or [[losing|loses]]; he is merely the instrument through which the [[zero-zero|mathematical edge]] of the house is realised. [[Alexei Ivanovitch]]'s [[obsession]] and the [[grandmother]]'s [[despair]] are met with the same blank professionalism. The croupier is the face of a system that has no face — the [[casino]]'s guarantee that every spin, every [[bet]], and every [[ruin]] will be processed with identical, inhuman efficiency.
