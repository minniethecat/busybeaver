# Franc

The franc is the currency of [[marquis-de-griers|De Griers]], of Paris, of international finance — and in *The Gambler*, it is the currency of entrapment. While the [[gulden|gülden]] circulates on the casino floor and the [[rouble]] anchors the characters' Russian obligations, the franc mediates the novel's critique of Franco-Russian economic dependency. It is the money of the people who do not gamble at the tables because they have already won — by lending to those who do.

## Definition

The French franc was the premier gold currency of 19th-century Europe, accepted everywhere and convertible at stable rates. In the novel, francs appear in two forms: gold coin and the thousand-franc banknote. When Alexei watches a gambler "get two thousand-franc notes changed for him at the bank" (001-044), the franc's convertibility into casino [[gulden|gülden]] is instantaneous and frictionless — unlike the [[rouble]], which must cross national borders and exchange-rate anxieties.

## Role in The Gambler

### The Currency of the Predator

The franc belongs to [[marquis-de-griers|De Griers]], the French aristocrat who holds the [[general|General]]'s debts and designs upon [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]. The General's desperate hope of an [[inheritance]] from the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] is essentially a hope of converting roubles into francs — of satisfying a French creditor with Russian family money. This national-economic subtext is central to the novel's [[russia|Russia]]n self-consciousness: France as the sophisticated predator, Russia as the naïve debtor.

The franc's magnitude — a thousand-franc note, a 40,000-franc whisper (015-045) — distinguishes it from the gülden's smaller, more tactile denominations. Francs are counted not in coins wrapped in blue paper but in abstractions, in rumours, in debts too large to be seen. They belong to the novel's offstage economy of inheritance, marriage settlement, and aristocratic obligation.

### Key Passages

> "Get two thousand-franc notes changed for him at the bank" (001-044) — the franc's easy convertibility at the casino, a privilege the [[rouble]] does not share.

> "Slowly extracted 300 francs in gold, which he staked" (003-081) — the physicality of franc-denominated gambling, gold coins rather than paper.

> "As much as 40,000 francs" (015-045) — the whispered sum of the General's debts, the franc as the unit of catastrophic obligation.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's use of the franc carries a specifically Russian resentment. The franc represents the West — sophisticated, financially predatory, sexually threatening — in a novel written during a period of intense Russian anxiety about European cultural and economic dominance. [[marquis-de-griers|De Griers]], the franc's human embodiment, is the character Alexei most despises: a man who wins without gambling, who collects debts without risk, whose financial power is indistinguishable from his social power.

The franc's relationship to the [[rouble]] — a weak Russian currency that must be converted into strong French gold — mirrors the novel's central power dynamic. The [[general|General]] owes francs; he possesses roubles and hopes for an [[inheritance]] in roubles; the gap between the two currencies is the gap between his social performance and his financial reality.
