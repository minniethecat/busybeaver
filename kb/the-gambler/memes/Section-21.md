# The Gambler · Section 21

[021-001]Meanwhile, that day some other very important events were passing in

[021-002]our hotel. As early as eleven o’clock—that is to say, before the

[021-003][[grandmother|Grandmother]] had quitted her rooms—the General and De Griers decided

[021-004]upon their last stroke. In other words, on learning that the old lady

[021-005]had changed her mind about departing, and was bent on setting out for

[021-006]the Casino again, the whole of our gang ([[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] only excepted)

[021-007]proceeded en masse to her rooms, for the purpose of finally and frankly

[021-008]treating with her. But the General, quaking and greatly apprehensive as

[021-009]to his possible future, overdid things. After half an hour’s prayers

[021-010]and entreaties, coupled with a full confession of his debts, and even

[021-011]of his [[love|passion]] for [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] (yes, he had quite lost his head), he

[021-012]suddenly adopted a tone of menace, and started to rage at the old

[021-013]lady—exclaiming that she was sullying the family honour, that she was

[021-014]making a public scandal of herself, and that she was smirching the fair

[021-015]name of [[russia|Russia]]. The upshot was that the Grandmother turned him out of

[021-016]the room with her stick (it was a real stick, too!). Later in the

[021-017]morning he held several consultations with De Griers—the question which

[021-018]occupied him being: Is it in any way possible to make use of the

[021-019]police—to tell them that “this respected, but unfortunate, old lady has

[021-020]gone out of her mind, and is squandering her last kopeck,” or something

[021-021]of the kind? In short, is it in any way possible to engineer a species

[021-022]of supervision over, or of restraint upon, the old lady? De Griers,

[021-023]however, shrugged his shoulders at this, and laughed in the General’s

[021-024]face, while the old warrior went on chattering volubly, and running up

[021-025]and down his study. Finally De Griers waved his hand, and disappeared

[021-026]from view; and by evening it became known that he had left the hotel,

[021-027]after holding a very secret and important conference with Mlle.

[021-028]Blanche. As for the latter, from early morning she had taken decisive

[021-029]measures, by completely excluding the General from her presence, and

[021-030]bestowing upon him not a glance. Indeed, even when the General pursued

[021-031]her to the Casino, and met her walking arm in arm with the Prince, he

[021-032](the General) received from her and her mother not the slightest

[021-033]recognition. Nor did the Prince himself bow. The rest of the day Mlle.

[021-034]spent in probing the Prince, and trying to make him declare himself;

[021-035]but in this she made a woeful mistake. The little incident occurred in

[021-036]the evening. Suddenly Mlle. Blanche realised that the Prince had not

[021-037]even a copper to his name, but, on the contrary, was minded to borrow

[021-038]of her money wherewith to [[gambling|play]] at roulette. In high displeasure she

[021-039]drove him from her presence, and shut herself up in her room.

[021-040]The same morning I went to see—or, rather, to look for—[[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], but

[021-041]was unsuccessful in my quest. Neither in his rooms nor in the Casino

[021-042]nor in the Park was he to be found; nor did he, that day, lunch at his

[021-043]hotel as usual. However, at about five o’clock I caught sight of him

[021-044]walking from the railway station to the Hôtel d’Angleterre. He seemed

[021-045]to be in a great hurry and much preoccupied, though in his face I could

[021-046]discern no actual traces of worry or perturbation. He held out to me a

[021-047]friendly hand, with his usual ejaculation of “Ah!” but did not check

[021-048]his stride. I turned and walked beside him, but found, somehow, that

[021-049]his answers forbade any putting of definite questions. Moreover, I felt

[021-050]reluctant to speak to him of Polina; nor, for his part, did he ask me

[021-051]any questions concerning her, although, on my telling him of the

[021-052]Grandmother’s exploits, he listened attentively and gravely, and then

[021-053]shrugged his shoulders.

[021-054]“She is gambling away everything that she has,” I remarked.

[021-055]“Indeed? She arrived at the Casino even before I had taken my departure

[021-056]by train, so I knew she had been playing. If I should have time I will

[021-057]go to the Casino to-night, and take a look at her. The thing interests

[021-058]me.”

[021-059]“Where have you been today?” I asked—surprised at myself for having, as

[021-060]yet, omitted to put to him that question.

[021-061]“To Frankfort.”

[021-062]“On business?”

[021-063]“On business.”

[021-064]What more was there to be asked after that? I accompanied him until, as

[021-065]we drew level with the Hotel des Quatre Saisons, he suddenly nodded to

[021-066]me and disappeared. For myself, I returned home, and came to the

[021-067]conclusion that, even had I met him at two o’clock in the afternoon, I

[021-068]should have learnt no more from him than I had done at five o’clock,

[021-069]for the reason that I had no definite question to ask. It was bound to

[021-070]have been so. For me to formulate the query which I really wished to

[021-071]put was a simple impossibility.

[021-072]Polina spent the whole of that day either in walking about the park

[021-073]with the nurse and children or in sitting in her own room. For a long

[021-074]while past she had avoided the General and had scarcely had a word to

[021-075]say to him (scarcely a word, I mean, on any _serious_ topic). Yes, that

[021-076]I had noticed. Still, even though I was aware of the position in which

[021-077]the General was placed, it had never occurred to me that he would have

[021-078]any reason to avoid _her_, or to trouble her with family explanations.

[021-079]Indeed, when I was returning to the hotel after my conversation with

[021-080]Astley, and chanced to meet Polina and the children, I could see that

[021-081]her face was as calm as though the family disturbances had never

[021-082]touched her. To my salute she responded with a slight bow, and I

[021-083]retired to my room in a very bad humour.

[021-084]Of course, since the affair with the Burmergelms I had exchanged not a

[021-085]word with Polina, nor had with her any kind of intercourse. Yet I had

[021-086]been at my wits’ end, for, as time went on, there was arising in me an

[021-087]ever-seething dissatisfaction. Even if she did not love me she ought

[021-088]not to have trampled upon my feelings, nor to have accepted my

[021-089]confessions with such contempt, seeing that she must have been aware

[021-090]that I loved her (of her own accord she had allowed me to tell her as

[021-091]much). Of course the situation between us had arisen in a curious

[021-092]manner. [[About]] two months ago, I had noticed that she had a desire to

[021-093]make me her friend, her confidant—that she was making trial of me for

[021-094]the purpose; but, for some reason or another, the desired result had

[021-095]never come about, and we had fallen into the present strange relations,

[021-096]which had led me to address her as I had done. At the same time, if my

[021-097]love was distasteful to her, why had she not _forbidden_ me to speak of

[021-098]it to her?

[021-099]But she had not so forbidden me. On the contrary, there had been

[021-100]occasions when she had even _invited_ me to speak. Of course, this

[021-101]might have been done out of sheer wantonness, for I well knew—I had

[021-102]remarked it only too often—that, after listening to what I had to say,

[021-103]and angering me almost beyond endurance, she loved suddenly to torture

[021-104]me with some fresh outburst of contempt and aloofness! Yet she must

[021-105]have known that I could not live without her. Three days had elapsed

[021-106]since the affair with [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]], and I could bear the severance no

[021-107]longer. When, that afternoon, I met her near the Casino, my heart

[021-108]almost made me faint, it beat so violently. She too could not live

[021-109]without me, for had she not said that she had _need_ of me? Or had that

[021-110]too been spoken in jest?

[021-111]That she had a secret of some kind there could be no doubt. What she

[021-112]had said to the Grandmother had stabbed me to the heart. On a thousand

[021-113]occasions I had challenged her to be open with me, nor could she have

[021-114]been ignorant that I was ready to give my very life for her. Yet always

[021-115]she had kept me at a distance with that contemptuous air of hers; or

[021-116]else she had demanded of me, in lieu of the life which I offered to lay

[021-117]at her feet, such escapades as I had perpetrated with the Baron. Ah,

[021-118]was it not torture to me, all this? For could it be that her whole

[021-119]world was bound up with [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]]? What, too, about Mr. Astley? The

[021-120]affair was inexplicable throughout. My God, what distress it caused me!

[021-121]Arrived home, I, in a fit of frenzy, indited the following:

[021-122]“Polina Alexandrovna, I can see that there is approaching us an

[021-123]exposure which will involve you too. For the last time I ask of

[021-124]you—have you, or have you not, any need of my life? If you have, then

[021-125]make such dispositions as you wish, and I shall always be discoverable

[021-126]in my room if required. If you have need of my life, write or send for

[021-127]me.”

[021-128]I sealed the letter, and dispatched it by the hand of a corridor

[021-129]lacquey, with orders to hand it to the addressee in person. Though I

[021-130]expected no answer, scarcely three minutes had elapsed before the

[021-131]lacquey returned with “the compliments of a certain person.”

[021-132]Next, about seven o’clock, I was sent for by the General. I found him

[021-133]in his study, apparently preparing to go out again, for his hat and

[021-134]stick were lying on the sofa. When I entered he was standing in the

[021-135]middle of the room—his feet wide apart, and his head bent down. Also,

[021-136]he appeared to be talking to himself. But as soon as ever he saw me at

[021-137]the door he came towards me in such a curious manner that involuntarily

[021-138]I retreated a step, and was for leaving the room; whereupon he seized

[021-139]me by both hands, and, drawing me towards the sofa, and seating himself

[021-140]thereon, he forced me to sit down on a chair opposite him. Then,

[021-141]without letting go of my hands, he exclaimed with quivering lips and a

[021-142]sparkle of tears on his eyelashes:

[021-143]“Oh, [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]]! Save me, save me! Have some mercy upon me!”

[021-144]For a long time I could not make out what he meant, although he kept

[021-145]talking and talking, and constantly repeating to himself, “Have mercy,

[021-146]mercy!” At length, however, I divined that he was expecting me to give

[021-147]him something in the nature of advice—or, rather, that, deserted by

[021-148]every one, and overwhelmed with grief and apprehension, he had

[021-149]bethought himself of my existence, and sent for me to relieve his

[021-150]feelings by talking and talking and talking.

[021-151]In fact, he was in such a confused and despondent state of mind that,

[021-152]clasping his hands together, he actually went down upon his knees and

[021-153]begged me to go to Mlle. Blanche, and beseech and advise her to return

[021-154]to him, and to accept him in marriage.

[021-155]“But, General,” I exclaimed, “possibly Mlle. Blanche has scarcely even

[021-156]remarked my existence? What could _I_ do with her?”

[021-157]It was in vain that I protested, for he could understand nothing that

[021-158]was said to him, Next he started talking about the Grandmother, but

[021-159]always in a disconnected sort of fashion—his one thought being to send

[021-160]for the police.

[021-161]“In Russia,” said he, suddenly boiling over with indignation, “or in

[021-162]any well-ordered State where there exists a government, old women like

[021-163]my mother are placed under proper guardianship. Yes, my good sir,” he

[021-164]went on, relapsing into a scolding tone as he leapt to his feet and

[021-165]started to pace the room, “do you not know this” (he seemed to be

[021-166]addressing some imaginary auditor in the corner) “—do you not know

[021-167]this, that in Russia old women like her are subjected to restraint, the

[021-168]devil take them?” Again he threw himself down upon the sofa.
