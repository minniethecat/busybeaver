# Alexei Ivanovitch

[[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexei Ivanovitch]] is the unnamed first-person narrator and protagonist of Dostoyevsky's *The Gambler*. A young [[russia|Russia]]n employed as a tutor in the General's household at [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]], he is defined by three consuming [[love|passion]]s: his [[passion|obsessive love]] for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina Alexandrovna]], his compulsive gambling at roulette, and his bitter, self-lacerating intelligence. Through his voice—sarcastic, confessional, unstable—Dostoyevsky creates one of literature's most psychologically acute portraits of addiction and self-destruction (008-153).

## Role in the Novel

Alexei serves as tutor to the General's children, a position that places him at the bottom of the household's social hierarchy while giving him intimate access to its secrets. He enters the novel already in [[debt]] (006-069), already in love with [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], and already contemptuous of the General's pretensions. The novel traces his trajectory from reluctant gambler—he first [[gambling|play]]s at [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s request—to compulsive devotee whose identity dissolves into the [[roulette|roulette wheel]] (029-016).

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]] singles him out as her preferred escort and confidant: "Alexis Ivanovitch, come and see me whenever you like" (014-028). This favouritism briefly elevates his status, but it also accelerates his gambling, as proximity to the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s [[fortune]] makes his own poverty more unbearable.

## Key Scenes

> "For God's sake, Alexis Ivanovitch, do put an end to this senseless..." (008-153) — The General's desperate plea, establishing Alexei as the person others depend on in crisis.

> "Alexis Ivanovitch! It was a woman's voice" (012-012) — The Grandmother's summons, marking Alexei's elevation to her confidant.

> "I am in debt. I borrowed some [[money]], and must repay it" (006-069) — Alexei's confession of the financial condition that makes gambling seem necessary.

The novel's final scene, where Alexei announces that tomorrow he will "perhaps rise from the dead and begin to live a[[winning|gain]]" or else "damn the gambling" (029-016), captures his fundamental indeterminacy: he is the character who cannot finish becoming a character.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Alexei's defining trait is his intelligence turned a[[winning|gain]]st himself. He sees through the General's pretensions, [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]]'s manipulations, and [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]'s [[probability|calculation]]s—but this same intelligence cannot save him from his own [[obsession|compulsion]]s. He knows that the roulette [[method|system]] cannot work and gambles anyway. He knows that [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] does not love him and loves her anyway.

His voice alternates between bitter sarcasm and genuine anguish—a tonal instability that mirrors his psychological state. When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] calls out "Alexis Ivanovitch! Alexis Ivanovitch! Good heavens!" (012-024), her panic captures something essential about him: he is the person everyone turns to in crisis, and the person least capable of helping anyone, including himself.

### Arc and Development

Alexei's arc is a spiral rather than a progression. He wins at roulette and loses; declares love for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] and is rejected; achieves moments of self-knowledge and immediately forgets them. The novel ends not with resolution but with the narrator announcing his intention to gamble a[[winning|gain]] (029-016)—a refusal of closure that is the novel's deepest formal insight.

## How Others See Alexei

One of the novel's structural ironies is that Alexei—the narrator through whose consciousness every scene is filtered—is seen very differently by those around him than he sees himself. His self-portrait as a sharp-eyed, disillusioned observer is repeatedly undercut by the perspectives of other characters who regard him as naive, dangerous, or pitiable.

### Polina's View

[[Polina Alexandrovna|Polina]] sees Alexei as simultaneously useful and contemptible. She depends on him to gamble on her behalf (002-030) and confides in him more than anyone else in the household, yet she also mocks his declarations of love and treats his devotion as another form of servitude. To Polina, Alexei is a mirror in which she can see her own power—and a reminder of the powerlessness they share as dependents in the General's household. Her withholding of affection is not simply cruelty; it is the only weapon available to a woman whose social position offers no other leverage.

### The General's View

To [[general|the General]], Alexei is a social embarrassment and a necessary inconvenience—an employee who knows too much. The General's desperate plea "For God's sake, Alexis Ivanovitch, do put an end to this senseless..." (008-153) reveals his dependence on a man he otherwise treats with barely concealed contempt. Alexei's Russianness, his poverty, and his lack of title place him permanently outside the General's world, yet the General cannot function without him.

### The Grandmother's View

[[grandmother|The Grandmother]]—Antonida Vasilievna Tarasevitcheva—sees Alexei differently from everyone else in the household. To her, he is neither [[servant]] nor threat but a fellow Russian in a foreign world of European pretension. She calls him by name with genuine warmth—"Alexis Ivanovitch, come and see me whenever you like" (014-028)—and treats him as a confidant rather than an employee. Her perspective is the novel's clearest external validation of Alexei's worth, and it is significant that this validation comes from the one character with enough independent wealth and social standing to disregard the household's hierarchies entirely.

### Mr. Astley's View

[[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] offers the novel's most dispassionate assessment of Alexei. Where others see passion, Astley sees pathology. His clinical judgment that Alexei's gambling is "a [[delirium]], a madness" (026-038) carries the weight of an external observer whose own relationship to money and [[risk]] is governed by [[calculation]] rather than compulsion. Astley's perspective exposes the gap between Alexei's self-understanding—he believes his gambling is a form of existential rebellion—and the simpler truth visible to everyone else: he is an addict who cannot stop.

### De Griers's View

To [[marquis-de-griers|the Marquis de Griers]], Alexei is barely worth noticing—a minor obstacle to the financial arrangement that will deliver [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] to him. De Griers's indifference is, in its way, more wounding to Alexei than open hostility would be. It confirms Alexei's [[fear]] that he is, in the eyes of European sophistication, invisible—a "Russian tutor" whose opinions and feelings carry no weight in the world of serious transactions.

The gap between Alexei's self-conception and others' perception of him is the novel's deepest structural irony: the narrator who sees through everyone cannot see that everyone sees through him.

## Critical Perspectives

Dostoyevsky based Alexei on his own experience as a compulsive gambler, making *The Gambler* one of his most autobiographical works. The narrator's psychological acuity about his own condition—knowing exactly why he gambles and being unable to stop—reflects Dostoyevsky's lived experience rather than literary invention. Alexei is the first of Dostoyevsky's "underground" narrators: self-aware, self-loathing, and trapped in a consciousness that will not permit him to act in his own interests.
