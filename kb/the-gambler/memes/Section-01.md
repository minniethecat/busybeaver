[001-022]I

[001-023]At length I returned from two weeks leave of absence to find that my

[001-024]patrons had arrived three days ago in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]. I received from

[001-025]them a welcome quite different to that which I had expected. The

[001-026]General eyed me coldly, greeted me in rather haughty fashion, and

[001-027]dismissed me to pay my respects to his sister. It was clear that from

[001-028]_somewhere_ money had been acquired. I thought I could even detect a

[001-029]certain [[shame|shamefacedness]] in the General’s glance. [[maria-philipovna|Maria Philipovna]], too,

[001-030]seemed distraught, and conversed with me with an air of detachment.

[001-031]Nevertheless, she took the money which I handed to her, counted it, and

[001-032]listened to what I had to tell. To luncheon there were expected that

[001-033]day a Monsieur Mezentsov, a French lady, and an Englishman; for,

[001-034]whenever money was in hand, a banquet in Muscovite style was always

[001-035]given. [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina Alexandrovna]], on seeing me, inquired why I had been so

[001-036]long away. Then, without waiting for an answer, she departed. Evidently

[001-037]this was not mere accident, and I felt that I must throw some light

[001-038]upon matters. It was high time that I did so.

[001-039]I was assigned a small room on the fourth floor of the hotel (for you

[001-040]must know that I belonged to the General’s suite). So far as I could

[001-041]see, the party had already gained some notoriety in the place, which

[001-042]had come to look upon the General as a Russian nobleman of great

[001-043][[fortune|wealth]]. Indeed, even before luncheon he charged me, among other things,

[001-044]to get two thousand-franc notes changed for him at the hotel counter,

[001-045]which put us in a position to be thought millionaires at all events for

[001-046]a week! Later, I was about to take Mischa and Nadia for a walk when a

[001-047]summons reached me from the staircase that I must attend the General.

[001-048]He began by deigning to inquire of me where I was going to take the

[001-049]children; and as he did so, I could see that he failed to look me in

[001-050]the eyes. He _wanted_ to do so, but each time was met by me with such a

[001-051]fixed, disrespectful stare that he desisted in confusion. In pompous

[001-052]language, however, which jumbled one sentence into another, and at

[001-053]length grew disconnected, he gave me to understand that I was to lead

[001-054]the children altogether away from the Casino, and out into the park.

[001-055]Finally his anger exploded, and he added sharply:

[001-056]“I suppose you would like to take them to the Casino to [[gambling|play]] roulette?

[001-057]Well, excuse my speaking so plainly, but I know how addicted you are to

[001-058]gambling. Though I am not your mentor, nor wish to be, at least I have

[001-059]a right to require that you shall not actually _compromise_ me.”

[001-060]“I have no money for gambling,” I quietly replied.

[001-061]“But you will soon be in receipt of some,” retorted the General,

[001-062]reddening a little as he dived into his writing desk and applied

[001-063]himself to a memorandum book. From it he saw that he had 120 roubles of

[001-064]mine in his keeping.

[001-065]“Let us calculate,” he went on. “We must translate these roubles into

[001-066]thalers. Here—take 100 thalers, as a round sum. The rest will be safe

[001-067]in my hands.”

[001-068]In silence I took the money.

[001-069]“You must not be offended at what I say,” he continued. “You are too

[001-070]touchy about these things. What I have said I have said merely as a

[001-071]warning. To do so is no more than my right.”

[001-072]When returning home with the children before luncheon, I met a

[001-073]cavalcade of our party riding to view some ruins. Two splendid

[001-074]carriages, magnificently horsed, with [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]], Maria Philipovna,

[001-075]and Polina Alexandrovna in one of them, and [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]], the

[001-076]Englishman, and the General in attendance on horseback! The passers-by

[001-077]stopped to stare at them, for the effect was splendid—the General could

[001-078]not have improved upon it. I calculated that, with the 4000 francs

[001-079]which I had brought with me, added to what my patrons seemed already to

[001-080]have acquired, the party must be in possession of at least 7000 or 8000

[001-081]francs—though that would be none too much for Mlle. Blanche, who, with

[001-082]her mother and the Frenchman, was also lodging in our hotel. The latter

[001-083]gentleman was called by the lacqueys “Monsieur le Comte,” and Mlle.

[001-084]Blanche’s mother was dubbed “Madame la Comtesse.” Perhaps in very truth

[001-085]they _were_ “Comte et Comtesse.”

[001-086]I knew that “Monsieur le Comte” would take no notice of me when we met

[001-087]at dinner, as also that the General would not dream of introducing us,

[001-088]nor of recommending me to the “Comte.” However, the latter had lived

[001-089]awhile in [[russia|Russia]], and knew that the person referred to as an “uchitel”

[001-090]is never looked upon as a bird of fine feather. Of course, strictly

[001-091]speaking, he _knew_ me; but I was an uninvited guest at the

[001-092]luncheon—the General had forgotten to arrange otherwise, or I should

[001-093]have been dispatched to dine at the table d’hôte. Nevertheless, I

[001-094]presented myself in such guise that the General looked at me with a

[001-095]touch of approval; and, though the good Maria Philipovna was for

[001-096]showing me my place, the fact of my having previously met the

[001-097]Englishman, [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], saved me, and thenceforward I figured as one of

[001-098]the company.

[001-099]This strange Englishman I had met first in Prussia, where we had

[001-100]happened to sit _vis-à-vis_ in a railway train in which I was

[001-101]travelling to overtake our party; while, later, I had run across him in

[001-102]France, and again in [[switzerland|Switzerland]]—twice within the space of two weeks!

[001-103]To think, therefore, that I should suddenly encounter him again here,

[001-104]in Roulettenberg! Never in my life had I known a more retiring man, for

[001-105]he was shy to the pitch of imbecility, yet well aware of the fact (for

[001-106]he was no fool). At the same time, he was a gentle, amiable sort of an

[001-107]individual, and, even on our first encounter in Prussia I had contrived

[001-108]to draw him out, and he had told me that he had just been to the North

[001-109]Cape, and was now anxious to visit the fair at Nizhni Novgorod. How he

[001-110]had come to make the General’s acquaintance I do not know, but,

[001-111]apparently, he was much struck with Polina. Also, he was delighted that

[001-112]I should sit next him at table, for he appeared to look upon me as his

[001-113]bosom friend.

[001-114]During the meal the Frenchman was in great feather: he was discursive

[001-115]and pompous to every one. In [[moscow|Moscow]] too, I remembered, he had blown a

[001-116]great many bubbles. Interminably he discoursed on finance and Russian

[001-117]politics, and though, at times, the General made feints to contradict

[001-118]him, he did so humbly, and as though wishing not wholly to lose sight

[001-119]of his own [[honour|dignity]].

[001-120]For myself, I was in a curious frame of mind. Even before luncheon was

[001-121]half finished I had asked myself the old, eternal question: “_Why_ do I

[001-122]continue to dance attendance upon the General, instead of having left

[001-123]him and his family long ago?” Every now and then I would glance at

[001-124]Polina Alexandrovna, but she paid me no attention; until eventually I

[001-125]became so irritated that I decided to play the boor.

[001-126]First of all I suddenly, and for no reason whatever, plunged loudly and

[001-127]gratuitously into the general conversation. Above everything I wanted

[001-128]to pick a quarrel with the Frenchman; and, with that end in view I

[001-129]turned to the General, and exclaimed in an overbearing sort of

[001-130]way—indeed, I think that I actually interrupted him—that that summer it

[001-131]had been almost impossible for a Russian to dine anywhere at tables

[001-132]d’hôte. [[general|The General]] bent upon me a glance of astonishment.

[001-133]“If one is a man of self-respect,” I went on, “one risks abuse by so

[001-134]doing, and is forced to put up with insults of every kind. Both at

[001-135][[paris|Paris]] and on the Rhine, and even in Switzerland—there are so many

[001-136]Poles, with their sympathisers, the French, at these tables d’hôte that

[001-137]one cannot get a word in edgeways if one happens only to be a Russian.”

[001-138]This I said in French. The General eyed me doubtfully, for he did not

[001-139]know whether to be angry or merely to feel surprised that I should so

[001-140]far forget myself.

[001-141]“Of course, one always learns _something everywhere_,” said the

[001-142]Frenchman in a careless, contemptuous sort of tone.

[001-143]“In Paris, too, I had a dispute with a Pole,” I continued, “and then

[001-144]with a French officer who supported him. After that a section of the

[001-145]Frenchmen present took my part. They did so as soon as I told them the

[001-146]story of how once I threatened to spit into Monsignor’s coffee.”

[001-147]“To spit into it?” the General inquired with grave disapproval in his

[001-148]tone, and a stare, of astonishment, while the Frenchman looked at me

[001-149]unbelievingly.

[001-150]“Just so,” I replied. “You must know that, on one occasion, when, for

[001-151]two days, I had felt certain that at any moment I might have to depart

[001-152]for Rome on business, I repaired to the Embassy of the Holy See in

[001-153]Paris, to have my passport visaed. There I encountered a sacristan of

[001-154]about fifty, and a man dry and cold of mien. After listening politely,

[001-155]but with great reserve, to my account of myself, this sacristan asked

[001-156]me to wait a little. I was in a great hurry to depart, but of course I

[001-157]sat down, pulled out a copy of _L’Opinion Nationale_, and fell to

[001-158]reading an extraordinary piece of invective against Russia which it

[001-159]happened to contain. As I was thus engaged I heard some one enter an

[001-160]adjoining room and ask for Monsignor; after which I saw the sacristan

[001-161]make a low bow to the visitor, and then another bow as the visitor took

[001-162]his leave. I ventured to remind the good man of my own business also;

[001-163]whereupon, with an expression of, if anything, increased dryness, he

[001-164]again asked me to wait. Soon a third visitor arrived who, like myself,

[001-165]had come on business (he was an Austrian of some sort); and as soon as

[001-166]ever he had stated his errand he was conducted upstairs! This made me

[001-167]very angry. I rose, approached the sacristan, and told him that, since

[001-168]Monsignor was receiving callers, his lordship might just as well finish

[001-169]off my affair as well. Upon this the sacristan shrunk back in

[001-170]astonishment. It simply passed his understanding that any insignificant

[001-171]Russian should dare to compare himself with other visitors of

[001-172]Monsignor’s! In a tone of the utmost effrontery, as though he were

[001-173]delighted to have a [[probability|chance]] of insulting me, he looked me up and down,

[001-174]and then said: “Do you suppose that Monsignor is going to put aside his

[001-175]coffee for _you?_” But I only cried the louder: “Let me tell you that I

[001-176]am going to _spit_ into that coffee! Yes, and if you do not get me my

[001-177]passport visaed this very minute, I shall take it to Monsignor myself.”

[001-178]“What? While he is engaged with a Cardinal?” screeched the sacristan,

[001-179]again shrinking back in horror. Then, rushing to the door, he spread

[001-180]out his arms as though he would rather die than let me enter.

[001-181]Thereupon I declared that I was a heretic and a barbarian—“Je suis

[001-182]hérétique et barbare,” I said, “and that these archbishops and

[001-183]cardinals and monsignors, and the rest of them, meant nothing at all to

[001-184]me. In a word, I showed him that I was not going to give way. He looked

[001-185]at me with an air of infinite resentment. Then he snatched up my

[001-186]passport, and departed with it upstairs. A minute later the passport

[001-187]had been visaed! Here it is now, if you care to see it,”—and I pulled

[001-188]out the document, and exhibited the Roman visa.
