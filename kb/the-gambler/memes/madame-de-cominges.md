# Madame de Cominges

Madame de Cominges is the shadow behind her daughter Blanche — a widow who participates in the scheme to secure the [[general|General]]'s expected inheritance through marriage. Though she appears only briefly in the narrative, her presence illuminates the novel's satire of French social maneuvering on the margins of Russian wealth.

## The Mother-Daughter Enterprise

Madame de Cominges is introduced as the mother of [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] de Cominges (009-144, 009-146). Together they form a two-woman enterprise, with Blanche as the visible asset and Madame as the strategic manager. The narrator notes that Blanche "is the daughter of a certain Madame de Cominges" (011-156), framing the relationship as one of inheritance and tutelage in the arts of social climbing. The deliberate vagueness of "a certain" suggests the dubiousness of their social credentials — they are adventuresses trading on a name.

## The Widow

Madame de Cominges is described as "the Widow" (011-051), a status that carries its own implications in the novel's economy of dependency. Like many characters in *The Gambler*, she has learned to convert social proximity into financial opportunity. Her widowhood places her outside the protections of marriage while freeing her from its constraints — a position she has evidently turned to commercial advantage through her daughter.

## The Marriage Plot

The scheme to marry Blanche to the General is a joint venture. When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] learns of the General's plans, she confronts him: "he is going to marry [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] de Cominges" (011-033). The implication is that Madame de Cominges, no less than Blanche, is invested in the outcome — and will share in the spoils. The marriage plot represents the French contingent's hope of extracting wealth from the Russian aristocracy through matrimony rather than gambling.

## The Aftermath

When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] dies and the inheritance vanishes into her gambling losses, the Cominges enterprise adapts with French pragmatism. Blanche transfers her attentions from the ruined General to the momentarily wealthy Alexei. Madame de Cominges's role in this pivot is implied rather than stated, but her silent presence behind Blanche throughout the novel makes her complicity unmistakable. When Alexei later reflects on his time in [[paris|Paris]] with Blanche (026-026), Madame de Cominges is part of the expensive entourage he must support — the widow collecting her dividend from the gambler's brief prosperity.

## Significance

Madame de Cominges embodies a secondary theme of the novel: the commodification of relationships under the pressure of [[money]]. As a widow who markets her daughter's beauty to the highest bidder, she represents the logical endpoint of the social calculus that governs [[roulettenberg]]'s international set. In a world where everything has a price, Madame de Cominges has simply learned to name hers.
