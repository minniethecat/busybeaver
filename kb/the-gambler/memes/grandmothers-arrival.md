# The Grandmother's Arrival

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s arrival at [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] is *The Gambler*'s defining turning point—the event that transforms the novel from a study of gambling psychology into a social comedy of collapsing schemes. She had arrived "borne aloft in an armchair" (012-042), unable to walk, and "the cause of the sensation—the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]—was being borne..." (012-112) in a chair, a procession that parodies royal entry and announces the reversal of every expectation on which the household's finances depend.

## Role in the Novel

Before the arrival, the General's household operates on the assumption of the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s imminent death. De Griers has lent money a[[winning|gain]]st the expected inheritance; [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] has invested her time in the General a[[winning|gain]]st the same expectation. The arrival (012-112) cancels all these [[probability|calculation]]s in an instant.

The event's theatrical quality—the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] carried through the hotel "in triumph" (012-135), the General's panic, the general sensation—marks it as Dostoyevsky's most accomplished comic set piece. "The Grandmother's arrival seemed to be a catastrophe for everybody" (012-166): every character's response to the arrival reveals exactly what they had [[bet|stake]]d on her death.

## Key Moments

> "The cause of the sensation—the Grandmother—was being borne..." (012-112) — the announcement, immediately transforming the hotel's social order.

> "The Grandmother was borne through them in triumph" (012-135) — her procession, a parody of royal entry.
