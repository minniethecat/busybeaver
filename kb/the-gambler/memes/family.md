# Family

Family in *The Gambler* is not a source of comfort but a structure of entanglement. Every major character is bound by familial obligation — to a relative, a creditor who stands in for a relative, or the memory of one. The [[general|General]] awaits an [[inheritance]] that would release him from [[debt]]; [[Polina Alexandrovna]] is trapped as the General's stepdaughter, her [[fortune]] tied to his; [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] serves as tutor in a household not his own, half-servant and half-confidant. Dostoyevsky depicts the family not as a refuge from the [[casino]]'s chaos but as a parallel gambling table where the stakes are measured in [[honour]], [[money]], and [[love]].

## The General's Household

The novel's domestic world revolves around the General's precarious household in [[Roulettenberg]]. [[Alexei Ivanovitch]], the tutor, is neither family nor stranger — he is "familiar with your family secrets" (002-059), a position that gives him intimate knowledge of the household's financial and romantic entanglements without granting him membership. The household includes [[Polina Alexandrovna]], the General's stepdaughter; the General's younger children from a previous marriage; [[mlle-blanche|Mademoiselle Blanche]], the General's presumed fiancée; and [[marquis-de-griers|De Griers]], the French marquis whose relationship to the family is purely financial.

## The Grandmother's Disruption

The [[grandmother]]'s unexpected arrival in [[Roulettenberg]] shatters the household's fragile equilibrium. She is the true head of the family, the source of the [[inheritance]] on which every calculation depends:

> "That man was then a boy of ten and his family are still residing in Moscow." (002-014)

Her refusal to die on schedule — and her decision to gamble away a portion of her fortune at the [[casino]] — demolishes the General's expectations and exposes the underlying truth: the family is held together not by affection but by the prospect of [[money]].

## Family as Economic Unit

In Dostoyevsky's portrait, the family functions as an economic mechanism. "Every German family is bound to slavery and to submission to the *Vater*" (005-149), [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] observes, contrasting Russian familial disorder with German patriarchal order. But the Russian family in the novel is no freer — it is bound instead to [[debt]], to expectation, and to the desperate hope of an [[inheritance]] that will set everything right.

## The Stepdaughter's Position

[[Polina Alexandrovna]] occupies the novel's most exposed familial position. As stepdaughter, she belongs to the household without possessing any legal claim on its resources. Her relationship with [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] is shaped by this vulnerability — she loves and despises him in proportion to her own powerlessness within the family structure. The [[General]]'s plan to marry her to [[marquis-de-griers|De Griers]] is a familial transaction dressed as concern.
