# Mlle. Blanche

Mlle. Blanche is *The Gambler*'s most transparent schemer—and, in her transparency, almost sympathetic. A French courtesan passing as a respectable visitor to [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]], she attaches herself to the General for his anticipated inheritance with a candour that contrasts favourably with his self-deception. When Alexei asks "who is this Mlle. Blanche?" (002-117), the novel offers no satisfying answer: she is "just Mlle. Blanche" (002-119), a woman whose identity is entirely her performance.

## Role in the Novel

Mlle. Blanche functions as the novel's demonstration that European sophistication is a commodity. Her beauty, her manners, her accomplishments—all are investments designed to yield a return. She is accompanied by her mother, "Madame la..." (001-084), suggesting that her career is a family enterprise, a detail that is simultaneously comic and bleak.

Her gambling at trente et quarante (011-069) distinguishes her from the roulette-obsessed [[russia|Russia]]ns. She gambles with composure, in a game that permits composure, and her [[losing|loss]]es are strategic investments in her relationship with the General rather than existential crises. She is the only character in the novel who treats gambling as a business expense.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Mlle. Blanche is defined by her professionalism. She does not pretend to love the General; she pretends to pretend to love him, and the double performance is her signature. "It was said of her that she..." (004-085) — the novel presents her through hearsay, as though her own voice would be too disruptive to the narrative's [[russia|Russia]]n perspective.

Her composure is her armour, and unlike the General's [[honour|dignity]] it never cracks. When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s arrival destroys the General's financial prospects, Mlle. Blanche simply recalculates and moves on. She is the novel's most efficient character: no wasted emotion, no unproductive attachments.

### Arc and Development

Mlle. Blanche departs as she arrived: on her own terms. Her arc is not one of change but of revelation. As the novel strips away the General's pretensions, her professional [[probability|calculation]] becomes increasingly visible—not as hypocrisy (she never pretended otherwise) but as competence. She is better at being a courtesan than the General is at being a gentleman.

## Key Scenes

> "Magnificently horsed, with Mlle. Blanche..." (001-074) — her first appearance, framed as spectacle.

> "Who is this Mlle. Blanche? I inquired" (002-117) — the question the novel cannot answer, because Blanche is her performance.

> "But _who_ was this Mlle. Blanche? It was said of her that she..." (004-085) — the novel's attempt to locate her identity in rumour.

> "At first Mlle. Blanche played 'trente et quarante'" (011-069) — her gambling, composed and strategic.

## Critical Perspectives

Mlle. Blanche has been read as Dostoyevsky's most unsentimental portrait of a woman in *The Gambler*: neither victim nor villain, she is a professional operating in a system where female beauty is the only capital available to women without inheritance. Her transparency—the fact that she never pretends to be anything other than what she is—makes her, paradoxically, one of the novel's most honest characters. Where Alexei deceives himself, the General deceives others, and de Griers deceives both, Blanche alone offers no false representations. Critics have noted that her preference for trente et quarante over roulette reflects a broader temperamental difference: she chooses a game of moderate, calculated risk rather than the existential gamble of the wheel, and in this choice reveals a rationality that the novel's Russian characters conspicuously lack.
