# The Gambler · Section 14

[014-001]For instance, on one occasion she halted before a picture which, a poor

[014-002]copy of a well-known original, had a mythological subject.

[014-003]“Of whom is this a portrait?” she inquired.

[014-004]The landlord explained that it was probably that of a countess.

[014-005]“But how know you that?” [[grandmother|the old lady]] retorted.

[014-006]“You live here, yet you cannot say for certain! And why is the picture

[014-007]there at all? And why do its eyes look so crooked?”

[014-008]To all these questions the landlord could return no satisfactory reply,

[014-009]despite his floundering endeavours.

[014-010]“The blockhead!” exclaimed the Grandmother in Russian.

[014-011]Then she proceeded on her way—only to repeat the same story in front of

[014-012]a Saxon statuette which she had sighted from afar, and had commanded,

[014-013]for some reason or another, to be brought to her. Finally, she inquired

[014-014]of the landlord what was the value of the carpet in her bedroom, as

[014-015]well as where the said carpet had been manufactured; but, the landlord

[014-016]could do no more than promise to make inquiries.

[014-017]“What donkeys these people are!” she commented. Next, she turned her

[014-018]attention to the bed.

[014-019]“What a huge counterpane!” she exclaimed. “Turn it back, please.” The

[014-020]lacqueys did so.

[014-021]“Further yet, further yet,” the old lady cried. “Turn it _right_ back.

[014-022]Also, take off those pillows and bolsters, and lift up the feather

[014-023]bed.”

[014-024]The bed was opened for her inspection.

[014-025]“Mercifully it contains no bugs,” she remarked.

[014-026]“Pull off the whole thing, and then put on my own pillows and sheets.

[014-027]The place is too luxurious for an old woman like myself. It is too

[014-028]large for any one person. [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]], come and see me whenever

[014-029]you are not teaching your pupils.”

[014-030]“After tomorrow I shall no longer be in the General’s service,” I

[014-031]replied, “but merely living in the hotel on my own account.”

[014-032]“Why so?”

[014-033]“Because, the other day, there arrived from Berlin a German and his

[014-034]wife—persons of some importance; and, it chanced that, when taking a

[014-035]walk, I spoke to them in German without having properly compassed the

[014-036]Berlin accent.”

[014-037]“Indeed?”

[014-038]“Yes: and this action on my part [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] held to be an insult, and

[014-039]complained about it to the General, who yesterday dismissed me from his

[014-040]employ.”

[014-041]“But I suppose you must have threatened that precious Baron, or

[014-042]something of the kind? However, even if you did so, it was a matter of

[014-043]no moment.”

[014-044]“No, I did not. The Baron was the aggressor by raising his stick at

[014-045]me.”

[014-046]Upon that the Grandmother turned sharply to the General.

[014-047]“What? You permitted yourself to treat your tutor thus, you nincompoop,

[014-048]and to dismiss him from his post? You are a blockhead—an utter

[014-049]blockhead! I can see that clearly.”

[014-050]“Do not alarm yourself, my dear mother,” the General replied with a

[014-051]lofty air—an air in which there was also a tinge of familiarity. “I am

[014-052]quite capable of managing my own affairs. Moreover, Alexis Ivanovitch

[014-053]has not given you a true account of the matter.”

[014-054]“What did you do next?” The old lady inquired of me.

[014-055]“I wanted to challenge the Baron to a duel,” I replied as modestly as

[014-056]possible; “but the General protested against my doing so.”

[014-057]“And _why_ did you so protest?” she inquired of the General. Then she

[014-058]turned to the landlord, and questioned him as to whether _he_ would not

[014-059]have fought a duel, if challenged. “For,” she added, “I can see no

[014-060]difference between you and the Baron; nor can I bear that German visage

[014-061]of yours.” Upon this the landlord bowed and departed, though he could

[014-062]not have understood the Grandmother’s compliment.

[014-063]“Pardon me, Madame,” the General continued with a sneer, “but are duels

[014-064]really feasible?”

[014-065]“Why not? All men are crowing cocks, and that is why they quarrel.

[014-066]_You_, though, I perceive, are a blockhead—a man who does not even know

[014-067]how to carry his breeding. Lift me up. [[potapitch|Potapitch]], see to it that you

[014-068]always have _two_ bearers ready. Go and arrange for their hire. But we

[014-069]shall not require more than two, for I shall need only to be carried

[014-070]upstairs. On the level or in the street I can be _wheeled_ along. Go

[014-071]and tell them that, and pay them in advance, so that they may show me

[014-072]some respect. You too, Potapitch, are always to come with me, and

[014-073]_you_, Alexis Ivanovitch, are to point out to me this Baron as we go

[014-074]along, in order that I may get a squint at the precious ‘Von.’ And

[014-075]where is that roulette played?”

[014-076]I explained to her that [[roulette|the game]] was carried on in the salons of the

[014-077]Casino; whereupon there ensued a string of questions as to whether

[014-078]there were many such salons, whether many people played in them,

[014-079]whether those people played a whole day at a time, and whether the game

[014-080]was managed according to fixed rules. At length, I thought it best to

[014-081]say that the most advisable course would be for her to go and see it

[014-082]for herself, since a mere description of it would be a difficult

[014-083]matter.

[014-084]“Then take me straight there,” she said, “and do you walk on in front

[014-085]of me, Alexis Ivanovitch.”

[014-086]“What, mother? Before you have so much as rested from your journey?”

[014-087]the General inquired with some solicitude. Also, for some reason which

[014-088]I could not divine, he seemed to be growing nervous; and, indeed, the

[014-089]whole party was evincing signs of confusion, and exchanging glances

[014-090]with one another. Probably they were thinking that it would be a

[014-091]ticklish—even an embarrassing—business to accompany the Grandmother to

[014-092]the Casino, where, very likely, she would perpetrate further

[014-093]eccentricities, and in public too! Yet on their own initiative they had

[014-094]offered to escort her!

[014-095]“Why should I rest?” she retorted. “I am not tired, for I have been

[014-096]sitting still these past five days. Let us see what your medicinal

[014-097]springs and waters are like, and where they are situated. What, too,

[014-098]about that, that—what did you call it, Prascovia?—oh, about that

[014-099]mountain top?”

[014-100]“Yes, we are going to see it, Grandmamma.”

[014-101]“Very well. Is there anything else for me to see here?”

[014-102]“Yes! Quite a number of things,” [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] forced herself to say.

[014-103]“Martha, _you_ must come with me as well,” went on the old lady to her

[014-104]maid.

[014-105]“No, no, mother!” ejaculated the General. “Really she cannot come. They

[014-106]would not admit even Potapitch to the Casino.”

[014-107]“Rubbish! Because she is my servant, is that a reason for turning her

[014-108]out? Why, she is only a human being like the rest of us; and as she has

[014-109]been travelling for a week she might like to look about her. With whom

[014-110]else could she go out but myself? She would never dare to show her nose

[014-111]in the street alone.”

[014-112]“But, mother—”

[014-113]“Are you ashamed to be seen with me? Stop at home, then, and you will

[014-114]be asked no questions. A pretty General _you_ are, to be sure! I am a

[014-115]general’s widow myself. But, after all, why should I drag the whole

[014-116]party with me? I will go and see the sights with only Alexis Ivanovitch

[014-117]as my escort.”

[014-118]De Griers strongly insisted that _every one_ ought to accompany her.

[014-119]Indeed, he launched out into a perfect shower of charming phrases

[014-120]concerning the pleasure of acting as her cicerone, and so forth. Every

[014-121]one was touched with his words.

[014-122]“Mais elle est tombée en enfance,” he added aside to the General.

[014-123]“Seule, elle fera des bêtises.” More than this I could not overhear,

[014-124]but he seemed to have got some plan in his mind, or even to be feeling

[014-125]a slight return of his hopes.

[014-126]The distance to the Casino was about half a verst, and our route led us

[014-127]through the Chestnut Avenue until we reached the square directly

[014-128]fronting the building. [[general|The General]], I could see, was a trifle reassured

[014-129]by the fact that, though our progress was distinctly eccentric in its

[014-130]nature, it was, at least, correct and orderly. As a matter of fact, the

[014-131]spectacle of a person who is unable to walk is not anything to excite

[014-132]surprise at a spa. Yet it was clear that the General had a great fear

[014-133]of the Casino itself: for why should a person who had lost the use of

[014-134]her limbs—more especially an old woman—be going to rooms which were set

[014-135]apart only for roulette? On either side of the wheeled chair walked

[014-136]Polina and [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]—the latter smiling, modestly jesting, and, in

[014-137]short, making herself so agreeable to the Grandmother that in the end

[014-138]the old lady relented towards her. On the other side of the chair

[014-139]Polina had to answer an endless flow of petty questions—such as “Who

[014-140]was it passed just now?” “Who is that coming along?” “Is the town a

[014-141]large one?” “Are the public gardens extensive?” “What sort of trees are

[014-142]those?” “What is the name of those hills?” “Do I see eagles flying

[014-143]yonder?” “What is that absurd-looking building?” and so forth.

[014-144]Meanwhile [[mr-astley|Astley]] whispered to me, as he walked by my side, that he

[014-145]looked for much to happen that morning. Behind the old lady’s chair

[014-146]marched Potapitch and Martha—Potapitch in his frockcoat and white

[014-147]waistcoat, with a cloak over all, and the forty-year-old and rosy, but

[014-148]slightly grey-headed, Martha in a mobcap, cotton dress, and squeaking

[014-149]shoes. Frequently the old lady would twist herself round to converse

[014-150]with these servants. As for De Griers, he spoke as though he had made

[014-151]up his mind to do something (though it is also possible that he spoke

[014-152]in this manner merely in order to hearten the General, with whom he

[014-153]appeared to have held a conference). But, alas, the Grandmother had

[014-154]uttered the fatal words, “I am not going to give you any of my money;”

[014-155]and though De Griers might regard these words lightly, the General knew

[014-156]his mother better. Also, I noticed that De Griers and Mlle. Blanche

[014-157]were still exchanging looks; while of the Prince and the German savant

[014-158]I lost sight at the end of the Avenue, where they had turned back and

[014-159]left us.

[014-160]Into the Casino we marched in triumph. At once, both in the person of

[014-161]the commissionaire and in the persons of the footmen, there sprang to

[014-162]life the same reverence as had arisen in the lacqueys of the hotel. Yet

[014-163]it was not without some curiosity that they eyed us.

[014-164]Without [[losing|loss]] of time, the Grandmother gave orders that she should be

[014-165]wheeled through every room in the establishment; of which apartments

[014-166]she praised a few, while to others she remained indifferent. Concerning

[014-167]everything, however, she asked questions. Finally we reached the

[014-168][[gambling|gaming]]-salons, where a lacquey who was, acting as guard over the doors,

[014-169]flung them open as though he were a man possessed.

[014-170]The Grandmother’s entry into the roulette-salon produced a profound

[014-171]impression upon the public. Around [[casino|the tables]], and at the further end

[014-172]of the room where the [[trente-et-quarante]] table was set out, there may

[014-173]have been gathered from 150 to 200 gamblers, ranged in several rows.

[014-174]Those who had succeeded in pushing their way to the tables were

[014-175]standing with their feet firmly planted, in order to avoid having to

[014-176]give up their places until they should have finished their game (since

[014-177]merely to stand looking on—thus occupying a gambler’s place for

[014-178]nothing—was not permitted). True, chairs were provided around the

[014-179]tables, but few players made use of them—more especially if there was a

[014-180]large attendance of the general public; since to stand allowed of a

[014-181]closer approach; and, therefore, of greater facilities for [[probability|calculation]]

[014-182]and staking. Behind the foremost row were herded a second and a third

[014-183]row of people awaiting their turn; but sometimes their impatience led

[014-184]these people to stretch a hand through the first row, in order to

[014-185]deposit their stakes. Even third-row individuals would dart forward to

[014-186][[bet|stake]]; whence seldom did more than five or ten minutes pass without a

[014-187]scene over disputed money arising at one or another end of the table.

[014-188]On the other hand, the police of the Casino were an able body of men;

[014-189]and though to escape the crush was an impossibility, however much one

[014-190]might wish it, the eight croupiers apportioned to each table kept an

[014-191]eye upon the stakes, performed the necessary reckoning, and decided

[014-192]disputes as they arose.
