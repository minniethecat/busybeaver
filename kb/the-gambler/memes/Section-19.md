# The Gambler · Section 19

[019-001]The wheel revolved. “Zero!” cried the croupier.

[019-002]We had lost it all!

[019-003]“The fool!” cried [[grandmother|the old lady]] as she turned upon De Griers. “You

[019-004]infernal Frenchman, to think that _you_ should advise! Away with you!

[019-005]Though you fuss and fuss, you don’t even know what you’re talking

[019-006]about.”

[019-007]Deeply offended, De Griers shrugged his shoulders, favoured the

[019-008]Grandmother with a look of contempt, and departed. For some time past

[019-009]he had been feeling ashamed of being seen in such company, and this had

[019-010]proved the last straw.

[019-011]An hour later we had lost everything in hand.

[019-012]“Home!” cried the Grandmother.

[019-013]Not until we had turned into the Avenue did she utter a word; but from

[019-014]that point onwards, until we arrived at the hotel, she kept venting

[019-015]exclamations of “What a fool I am! What a silly old fool I am, to be

[019-016]sure!”

[019-017]Arrived at the hotel, she called for tea, and then gave orders for her

[019-018]luggage to be packed.

[019-019]“We are off again,” she announced.

[019-020]“But whither, Madame?” inquired Martha.

[019-021]“What business is that of _yours?_ Let the cricket stick to its

[019-022]hearth.[2] [[potapitch|Potapitch]], have everything packed, for we are returning to

[019-023][[moscow|Moscow]] at once. I have fooled away fifteen thousand roubles.”

[019-024][2] The Russian form of “Mind your own business.”

[019-025]“Fifteen thousand roubles, good mistress? My God!” And Potapitch spat

[019-026]upon his hands—probably to show that he was ready to serve her in any

[019-027]way he could.

[019-028]“Now then, you fool! At once you begin with your weeping and wailing!

[019-029]Be quiet, and pack. Also, run downstairs, and get my hotel bill.”

[019-030]“The next train leaves at 9:30, Madame,” I interposed, with a view to

[019-031]checking her agitation.

[019-032]“And what is the time now?”

[019-033]“Half-past eight.”

[019-034]“How vexing! But, never mind. [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]], I have not a kopeck

[019-035]left; I have but these two bank notes. Please run to the office and get

[019-036]them changed. Otherwise I shall have nothing to travel with.”

[019-037]Departing on her errand, I returned half an hour later to find the

[019-038]whole party gathered in her rooms. It appeared that the news of her

[019-039]impending departure for Moscow had thrown the conspirators into

[019-040]consternation even greater than her losses had done. For, said they,

[019-041]even if her departure should save her fortune, what will become of the

[019-042]General later? And who is to repay De Griers? Clearly [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]

[019-043]would never consent to wait until the Grandmother was dead, but would

[019-044]at once elope with the Prince or someone else. So they had all gathered

[019-045]together—endeavouring to calm and dissuade the Grandmother. Only [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]

[019-046]was absent. For her part the Grandmother had nothing for the party but

[019-047]abuse.

[019-048]“Away with you, you rascals!” she was shouting. “What have my affairs

[019-049]to do with you? Why, in particular, do _you_”—here she indicated De

[019-050]Griers—“come sneaking here with your goat’s beard? And what do

[019-051]_you_”—here she turned to Mlle. Blanche “want of me? What are _you_

[019-052]finicking for?”

[019-053]“Diantre!” muttered Mlle. under her breath, but her eyes were flashing.

[019-054]Then all at once she burst into a laugh and left the room—crying to the

[019-055]General as she did so: “Elle vivra cent ans!”

[019-056]“So you have been counting upon my death, have you?” fumed the old

[019-057]lady. “Away with you! Clear them out of the room, Alexis Ivanovitch.

[019-058]What business is it of _theirs?_ It is not _their_ money that I have

[019-059]been squandering, but my own.”

[019-060][[general|The General]] shrugged his shoulders, bowed, and withdrew, with De Griers

[019-061]behind him.

[019-062]“Call Prascovia,” commanded the Grandmother, and in five minutes Martha

[019-063]reappeared with Polina, who had been sitting with the children in her

[019-064]own room (having purposely determined not to leave it that day). Her

[019-065]face looked grave and careworn.

[019-066]“Prascovia,” began the Grandmother, “is what I have just heard through

[019-067]a side wind true—namely, that this fool of a stepfather of yours is

[019-068]going to marry that silly whirligig of a Frenchwoman—that actress, or

[019-069]something worse? Tell me, is it true?”

[019-070]“I do not know _for certain_, Grandmamma,” replied Polina; “but from

[019-071]Mlle. Blanche’s account (for she does not appear to think it necessary

[019-072]to conceal anything) I conclude that—”

[019-073]“You need not say any more,” interrupted the Grandmother energetically.

[019-074]“I understand the situation. I always thought we should get something

[019-075]like this from him, for I always looked upon him as a futile, frivolous

[019-076]fellow who gave himself unconscionable airs on the fact of his being a

[019-077]general (though he only became one because he retired as a colonel).

[019-078]Yes, I know _all_ about the sending of the telegrams to inquire whether

[019-079]‘the old woman is likely to turn up her toes soon.’ Ah, they were

[019-080]looking for the legacies! Without money that wretched woman (what is

[019-081]her name?—Oh, De Cominges) would never dream of accepting the General

[019-082]and his false teeth—no, not even for him to be her lacquey—since she

[019-083]herself, they say, possesses a pile of money, and lends it on interest,

[019-084]and makes a good thing out of it. However, it is not _you_, Prascovia,

[019-085]that I am blaming; it was not _you_ who sent those telegrams. Nor, for

[019-086]that matter, do I wish to recall old scores. True, I know that you are

[019-087]a vixen by nature—that you are a wasp which will sting one if one

[019-088]touches it—yet, my heart is sore for you, for I loved your mother,

[019-089]Katerina. Now, will you leave everything here, and come away with me?

[019-090]Otherwise, I do not know what is to become of you, and it is not right

[019-091]that you should continue living with these people. Nay,” she

[019-092]interposed, the moment that Polina attempted to speak, “I have not yet

[019-093]finished. I ask of you nothing in return. My house in Moscow is, as you

[019-094]know, large enough for a palace, and you could occupy a whole floor of

[019-095]it if you liked, and keep away from me for weeks together. Will you

[019-096]come with me or will you not?”

[019-097]“First of all, let me ask of _you_,” replied Polina, “whether you are

[019-098]intending to depart at once?”

[019-099]“What? You suppose me to be jesting? I have said that I am going, and I

[019-100]_am_ going. Today I have squandered fifteen thousand roubles at that

[019-101]accursed roulette of yours, and though, five years ago, I promised the

[019-102]people of a certain suburb of Moscow to build them a stone church in

[019-103]place of a wooden one, I have been fooling away my money here! However,

[019-104]I am going back now to build my church.”

[019-105]“But what about the waters, Grandmamma? Surely you came here to take

[019-106]the waters?”

[019-107]“You and your waters! Do not anger me, Prascovia. Surely you are trying

[019-108]to? Say, then: will you, or will you not, come with me?”

[019-109]“Grandmamma,” Polina replied with deep feeling, “I am very, very

[019-110]grateful to you for the shelter which you have so kindly offered me.

[019-111]Also, to a certain extent you have guessed my position aright, and I am

[019-112]beholden to you to such an extent that it may be that I _will_ come and

[019-113]live with you, and that very soon; yet there are important reasons

[019-114]why—why I cannot make up my mind just yet. If you would let me have,

[019-115]say, a couple of weeks to decide in—?”

[019-116]“You mean that you are _not_ coming?”

[019-117]“I mean only that I cannot come just yet. At all events, I could not

[019-118]well leave my little brother and sister here, since, since—if I were to

[019-119]leave them—they would be abandoned altogether. But if, Grandmamma, you

[019-120]would take the little ones _and_ myself, then, of course, I could come

[019-121]with you, and would do all I could to serve you” (this she said with

[019-122]great earnestness). “Only, without the little ones I _cannot_ come.”

[019-123]“Do not make a fuss” (as a matter of fact Polina never at any time

[019-124]either fussed or wept). “The Great Foster-Father[3] can find for all

[019-125]his chicks a place. You are not coming without the children? But see

[019-126]here, Prascovia. I wish you well, and nothing but well: yet I have

[019-127]divined the reason why you will not come. Yes, I know all, Prascovia.

[019-128]That Frenchman will never bring you good of any sort.”

[019-129][3] Translated literally—The Great Poulterer.

[019-130]Polina coloured hotly, and even I started. “For,” thought I to myself,

[019-131]“every one seems to know about that affair. Or perhaps I am the only

[019-132]one who does not know about it?”

[019-133]“Now, now! Do not frown,” continued the Grandmother. “But I do not

[019-134]intend to slur things over. You will take care that no harm befalls

[019-135]you, will you not? For you are a girl of sense, and I am sorry for

[019-136]you—I regard you in a different light to the rest of them. And now,

[019-137]please, leave me. Good-bye.”

[019-138]“But let me stay with you a little longer,” said Polina.

[019-139]“No,” replied the other; “you need not. Do not bother me, for you and

[019-140]all of them have tired me out.”

[019-141]Yet when Polina tried to kiss the Grandmother’s hand, the old lady

[019-142]withdrew it, and herself kissed the girl on the cheek. As she passed

[019-143]me, Polina gave me a momentary glance, and then as swiftly averted her

[019-144]eyes.

[019-145]“And good-bye to you, also, Alexis Ivanovitch. The train starts in an

[019-146]hour’s time, and I think that you must be weary of me. Take these five

[019-147]hundred gülden for yourself.”

[019-148]“I thank you humbly, Madame, but I am ashamed to—”

[019-149]“Come, come!” cried the Grandmother so energetically, and with such an

[019-150]air of menace, that I did not dare refuse the money further.

[019-151]“If, when in Moscow, you have no place where you can lay your head,”

[019-152]she added, “come and see me, and I will give you a recommendation. Now,

[019-153]Potapitch, get things ready.”

[019-154]I ascended to my room, and lay down upon the bed. A whole hour I must

[019-155]have lain thus, with my head resting upon my hand. So the crisis had

[019-156]come! I needed time for its consideration. To-morrow I would have a

[019-157]talk with Polina. Ah! The Frenchman! So, it was true? But how could it

[019-158]be so? Polina and De Griers! What a combination!

[019-159]No, it was too improbable. Suddenly I leapt up with the idea of seeking

[019-160][[mr-astley|Astley]] and forcing him to speak. There could be no doubt that he knew

[019-161]more than I did. Astley? Well, he was another problem for me to solve.

[019-162]Suddenly there came a knock at the door, and I opened it to find

[019-163]Potapitch awaiting me.

[019-164]“Sir,” he said, “my mistress is asking for you.”

[019-165]“Indeed? But she is just departing, is she not? The train leaves in ten

[019-166]minutes’ time.”

[019-167]“She is uneasy, sir; she cannot rest. Come quickly, sir; do not delay.”

[019-168]I ran downstairs at once. The Grandmother was just being carried out of

[019-169]her rooms into the corridor. In her hands she held a roll of

[019-170]bank-notes.

[019-171]“Alexis Ivanovitch,” she cried, “walk on ahead, and we will set out

[019-172]again.”

[019-173]“But whither, Madame?”

[019-174]“I cannot rest until I have retrieved my losses. March on ahead, and

[019-175]ask me no questions. Play continues until midnight, does it not?”

[019-176]For a moment I stood stupefied—stood deep in thought; but it was not

[019-177]long before I had made up my mind.

[019-178]“With your leave, Madame,” I said, “I will not go with you.”

[019-179]“And why not? What do you mean? Is every one here a stupid

[019-180]good-for-nothing?”

[019-181]“Pardon me, but I have nothing to reproach myself with. I merely will

[019-182]not go. I merely intend neither to witness nor to join in your [[gambling|play]]. I

[019-183]also beg to return you your five hundred gülden. Farewell.”

[019-184]Laying the money upon a little table which the Grandmother’s chair

[019-185]happened to be passing, I bowed and withdrew.

[019-186]“What folly!” the Grandmother shouted after me. “Very well, then. Do

[019-187]not come, and I will find my way alone. Potapitch, you must come with

[019-188]me. Lift up the chair, and carry me along.”
