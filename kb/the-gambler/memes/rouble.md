# Rouble

The [[rouble]] is the currency of [[russia|Russia]], the characters' homeland, and the invisible anchor of their financial obligations throughout *The Gambler*. While the [[gulden|gülden]] dominates the casino floor and the [[franc]] mediates international intrigue, the rouble represents the debts, salaries, and social obligations that the characters have left behind — and cannot escape. It is the currency of the real world that [[gambling]] promises to solve but ultimately worsens.

## Definition

The rouble was the currency of the Russian Empire in the 19th century, with a value substantially lower than the Western European [[gold]] currencies. When Alexei "found that he had 120 roubles of his salary left" (001-063), the amount locates him precisely: a tutor's modest savings, inadequate for the gülden-denominated stakes of the [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] casino. The conversion from roubles to gülden — from Russian poverty to European gambling — is itself a form of existential [[risk]].

## Role in The Gambler

### The Currency of Obligation

Roubles appear in the novel primarily in contexts of debt and duty. The [[general|General]]'s financial desperation stems from roubles: "the 4000 roubles wherewith to pay his Treasury" (004-081). This is not casino money but government money, the weight of an imperial bureaucracy bearing down on a man who has gambled with funds that were never his. The rouble's appearance marks the intrusion of Russian reality into the casino's international fantasy.

The rouble also signals the characters' social origins. Alexei's salary is in roubles; the General's debts are in roubles; [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s calculations of what she owes and is owed are conducted in roubles. The currency is a persistent reminder that these are Russian characters playing at European games, and that the bill will eventually come due — in roubles.

### Key Passages

> "He had 120 roubles of his salary left" (001-063) — Alexei's financial position at the novel's opening, establishing the gap between Russian income and European stakes.

> "The 4000 roubles wherewith to pay his Treasury" (004-081) — the General's embezzlement, the rouble-denominated debt that drives the plot.

> "A [[reason]] for wishing to avoid any trouble with the authorities" (011-044) — the rouble-denominated obligations that tether the characters to Russia and its consequences.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's use of multiple currencies — rouble, [[gulden|gülden]], [[franc]], [[florin]] — is not incidental. It maps the novel's thematic geography: Russia as the land of obligation and consequence, Western Europe as the land of speculation and escape. The rouble cannot be spent at the [[casino]]; it must first be converted, and the act of conversion is itself a step away from reality.

This multi-currency economy reflects Dostoyevsky's own experience. His gambling took place in German spa towns, but his debts were in roubles, owed to Russian publishers and creditors. The pressure to win in gülden in order to pay in roubles — the impossible exchange rate between gambling [[luck]] and real-world obligation — is the novel's economic engine.
