# Fate

Fate in *The Gambler* operates as a dark philosophical undercurrent beneath the novel's surface of roulette and social intrigue. Characters who believe themselves to be acting freely repeatedly invoke fate to explain outcomes they cannot control — or will not take responsibility for. From [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s settled fate (017-021) to the General's unknown destiny (022-090), fate is the idea that actions are "fated, inevitable, and foreordained" (023-024), a belief that simultaneously consoles and paralyses.

## Definition

In the novel, fate is not a theological doctrine but a psychological disposition. Characters speak of fate when they wish to deny their own agency: the roulette [[gambling|player]] who loses calls it fate; the lover who is rejected calls it fate; the debtor who cannot pay calls it fate. Dostoyevsky presents this fatalism as both a [[russia|Russian]] national characteristic and a gambler's self-deception — a way of surrendering responsibility to an impersonal force that is, in reality, the mathematics of the [[roulette|roulette wheel]].

The concept draws on a specifically Russian cultural attitude toward gambling, which Alexei himself articulates: "a great many Russians go in for gambling, they are no good at the game" (005-102). This national fatalism — the belief that Russians are temperamentally incapable of the disciplined calculation that successful gambling would require — becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy that drives characters toward the very ruin they claim is inevitable.

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Fate serves as the novel's unspoken structural principle. The gambling scenes are built around suspense — the spinning wheel, the falling ball — which creates a miniature drama of fate's revelation in every chapter (001-028). Characters who have no other spiritual framework reach for fate to make sense of events that are, in truth, the product of their own [[obsession|compulsions]] and the house's mathematical edge.

The narrator's own relationship to fate is revealing. When Alexei wins, he attributes it to his own "system" and insight; when he loses, fate becomes the explanation (023-024). This asymmetry — agency in success, fate in failure — is the psychological signature of the compulsive gambler. The novel's final line, "perhaps tomorrow I shall rise from the dead" (029-016), is itself an appeal to fate: a refusal to accept that the past determines the future, combined with a surrender to forces beyond the speaker's control.

### Key Passages

> "That her fate was settled I already had an inkling..." (017-021) — Alexei's premonition about Polina, couched in the language of fate.

> "The General did not know that his fate had already been decided" (022-090) — dramatic irony, the narrator knowing what the character cannot.

> "Something fated, inevitable, and foreordained" (023-024) — the novel's most concentrated expression of fatalistic philosophy, describing Alexei's gambling compulsion.

> "A great many Russians go in for gambling, they are no good at the game" (005-102) — national fatalism as self-fulfilling prophecy.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's treatment of fate in *The Gambler* draws on his own gambling experience, where the [[roulette|roulette wheel]] felt like a metaphysical adversary. But the novel is not simply fatalistic; it anatomises fatalism as a psychological strategy. Characters embrace fate because it relieves them of moral responsibility — if everything is foreordained, then no one is to blame for their losses, their debts, or their cruelties.

This critique distinguishes *The Gambler* from the more overtly religious fatalism of Dostoyevsky's later novels. Here, fate is not the workings of divine providence but a gambler's rationalisation. The novel exposes fatalism as the ideology of the roulette table.

## Dostoyevsky vs. Contemporaries

Tolstoy's characters in *War and Peace* (published shortly after *The Gambler*) grapple with fate as historical necessity — the sense that individual actions are swept up in vast forces beyond human comprehension. Dostoyevsky's treatment is both narrower and more psychological: fate in *The Gambler* is not history but mathematics, not providence but the roulette wheel. Where Tolstoy's characters achieve moments of grace by surrendering to fate, Dostoyevsky's characters are destroyed by that same surrender.

Turgenev, Dostoyevsky's great rival, treated fate as a kind of atmospheric melancholy — a mood rather than a philosophy. Dostoyevsky's innovation is to make fate concrete: it is the clicking of the roulette ball, the mechanical indifference of the wheel, the mathematical certainty of the zero. In *The Gambler*, fate has a physical address and a price per spin.
