# The Gambler · Section 15

[015-001]In the last resort they always called in the Casino police, and the

[015-002]disputes would immediately come to an end. Policemen were stationed

[015-003]about the Casino in ordinary costume, and mingled with the spectators

[015-004]so as to make it impossible to recognise them. In particular they kept

[015-005]a lookout for pickpockets and swindlers, who simply swanned in the

[015-006]roulette salons, and reaped a rich harvest. Indeed, in every direction

[015-007]money was being filched from pockets or purses—though, of course, if

[015-008]the attempt miscarried, a great uproar ensued. One had only to approach

[015-009]a roulette table, begin to [[gambling|play]], and then openly grab some one else’s

[015-010][[winning|winnings]], for a din to be raised, and the thief to start vociferating

[015-011]that the [[bet|stake]] was _his;_ and, if the coup had been carried out with

[015-012]sufficient skill, and the witnesses wavered at all in their testimony,

[015-013]the thief would as likely as not succeed in getting away with the

[015-014]money, provided that the sum was not a large one—not large enough to

[015-015]have attracted the attention of the croupiers or some fellow-player.

[015-016]Moreover, if it were a [[risk|stake]] of insignificant size, its true owner

[015-017]would sometimes decline to continue the dispute, rather than become

[015-018]involved in a scandal. Conversely, if the thief was detected, he was

[015-019]ignominiously expelled the building.

[015-020]Upon all this the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] gazed with open-eyed curiosity; and, on

[015-021]some thieves happening to be turned out of the place, she was

[015-022]delighted. Trente-et-quarante interested her but little; she preferred

[015-023]roulette, with its ever-revolving wheel. At length she expressed a wish

[015-024]to view [[roulette|the game]] closer; whereupon in some mysterious manner, the

[015-025]lacqueys and other officious agents (especially one or two ruined Poles

[015-026]of the kind who keep offering their services to successful gamblers and

[015-027]foreigners in general) at once found and cleared a space for the old

[015-028]lady among the crush, at the very centre of one of [[casino|the tables]], and next

[015-029]to the chief croupier; after which they wheeled her chair thither. Upon

[015-030]this a number of visitors who were not playing, but only looking on

[015-031](particularly some Englishmen with their families), pressed closer

[015-032]forward towards the table, in order to watch the old lady from among

[015-033]the ranks of the gamblers. Many a lorgnette I saw turned in her

[015-034]direction, and the croupiers’ hopes rose high that such an eccentric

[015-035]player was about to provide them with something out of the common. An

[015-036]old lady of seventy-five years who, though unable to walk, desired to

[015-037]play was not an everyday phenomenon. I too pressed forward towards the

[015-038]table, and ranged myself by the Grandmother’s side; while Martha and

[015-039][[potapitch|Potapitch]] remained somewhere in the background among the crowd, and the

[015-040]General, [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], and De Griers, with [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]], also remained

[015-041]hidden among the spectators.

[015-042]At first the old lady did no more than watch the gamblers, and ply me,

[015-043]in a half-whisper, with sharp-broken questions as to who was so-and-so.

[015-044]Especially did her favour light upon a very young man who was plunging

[015-045]heavily, and had won (so it was whispered) as much as 40,000 francs,

[015-046]which were lying before him on the table in a heap of gold and

[015-047]bank-notes. His eyes kept flashing, and his hands shaking; yet all the

[015-048]while he staked without any sort of [[probability|calculation]]—just what came to his

[015-049]hand, as he kept winning and winning, and raking and raking in his

[015-050]gains. Around him lacqueys fussed—placing chairs just behind where he

[015-051]was standing—and clearing the spectators from his vicinity, so that he

[015-052]should have more room, and not be crowded—the whole done, of course, in

[015-053]expectation of a generous largesse. From time to time other gamblers

[015-054]would hand him part of their winnings—being glad to let him stake for

[015-055]them as much as his hand could grasp; while beside him stood a Pole in

[015-056]a state of violent, but respectful, agitation, who, also in expectation

[015-057]of a generous largesse, kept whispering to him at intervals (probably

[015-058]telling him what to stake, and advising and directing his play). Yet

[015-059]never once did the player throw him a glance as he staked and staked,

[015-060]and raked in his winnings. Evidently, the player in question was dead

[015-061]to all besides.

[015-062]For a few minutes the Grandmother watched him.

[015-063]“Go and tell him,” suddenly she exclaimed with a nudge at my elbow,

[015-064]“—go and tell him to stop, and to take his money with him, and go home.

[015-065]Presently he will be [[loss|losing]]—yes, losing everything that he has now

[015-066]won.” She seemed almost breathless with excitement.

[015-067]“Where is Potapitch?” she continued. “Send Potapitch to speak to him.

[015-068]No, _you_ must tell him, _you_ must tell him,”—here she nudged me

[015-069]again—“for I have not the least notion where Potapitch is. Sortez,

[015-070]sortez,” she shouted to the young man, until I leant over in her

[015-071]direction and whispered in her ear that no shouting was allowed, nor

[015-072]even loud speaking, since to do so disturbed the calculations of the

[015-073]players, and might lead to our being ejected.

[015-074]“How provoking!” she retorted. “Then the young man is done for! I

[015-075]suppose he _wishes_ to be ruined. Yet I could not bear to see him have

[015-076]to return it all. What a fool the fellow is!” and the old lady turned

[015-077]sharply away.

[015-078]On the left, among the players at the other half of the table, a young

[015-079]lady was playing, with, beside her, a dwarf. Who the dwarf may have

[015-080]been—whether a relative or a person whom she took with her to act as a

[015-081]foil—I do not know; but I had noticed her there on previous occasions,

[015-082]since, everyday, she entered the Casino at one o’clock precisely, and

[015-083]departed at two—thus playing for exactly one hour. Being well-known to

[015-084]the attendants, she always had a seat provided for her; and, taking

[015-085]some gold and a few thousand-franc notes out of her pocket—would begin

[015-086]quietly, coldly, and after much calculation, to stake, and mark down

[015-087]the figures in pencil on a paper, as though striving to work out a

[015-088]system according to which, at given moments, the odds might group

[015-089]themselves. Always she staked large coins, and either lost or won one,

[015-090]two, or three thousand francs a day, but not more; after which she

[015-091]would depart. The Grandmother took a long look at her.

[015-092]“_That_ woman is not losing,” she said. “To whom does she belong? Do

[015-093]you know her? Who is she?”

[015-094]“She is, I believe, a Frenchwoman,” I replied.

[015-095]“Ah! A bird of passage, evidently. Besides, I can see that she has her

[015-096]shoes polished. Now, explain to me the meaning of each round in the

[015-097]game, and the way in which one ought to stake.”

[015-098]Upon this I set myself to explain the meaning of all the

[015-099]combinations—of “[[trente-et-quarante|rouge et noir]],” of “pair et impair,” of “manque et

[015-100]passe,” with, lastly, the different values in the system of numbers.

[015-101]The Grandmother listened attentively, took notes, put questions in

[015-102]various forms, and laid the whole thing to heart. Indeed, since an

[015-103]example of each [[system|system of stakes]] kept constantly occurring, a great

[015-104]deal of information could be assimilated with ease and celerity. The

[015-105]Grandmother was vastly pleased.

[015-106]“But what is zero?” she inquired. “Just now I heard the flaxen-haired

[015-107]croupier call out ‘zero!’ And why does he keep raking in all the money

[015-108]that is on the table? To think that he should grab the whole pile for

[015-109]himself! What does zero mean?”

[015-110]“Zero is what [[gambling-bank|the bank]] takes for itself. If the wheel stops at that

[015-111]figure, everything lying on the table becomes the absolute property of

[015-112]the bank. Also, whenever the wheel has begun to turn, the bank ceases

[015-113]to pay out anything.”

[015-114]“Then I should receive nothing if I were staking?”

[015-115]“No; unless by any chance you had _purposely_ staked on zero; in which

[015-116]case you would receive thirty-five times the value of your stake.”

[015-117]“Why thirty-five times, when zero so often turns up? And if so, why do

[015-118]not more of these fools stake upon it?”

[015-119]“Because the number of chances against its occurrence is thirty-six.”

[015-120]“Rubbish! Potapitch, Potapitch! Come here, and I will give you some

[015-121]money.” The old lady took out of her pocket a tightly-clasped purse,

[015-122]and extracted from its depths a ten-gülden piece. “Go at once, and

[015-123]stake that upon zero.”

[015-124]“But, Madame, zero has only this moment turned up,” I remonstrated;

[015-125]“wherefore, it may not do so again for ever so long. Wait a little, and

[015-126]you may then have a better chance.”

[015-127]“Rubbish! Stake, please.”

[015-128]“Pardon me, but zero might not turn up again until, say, tonight, even

[015-129]though you had staked thousands upon it. It often happens so.”

[015-130]“Rubbish, rubbish! Who fears the wolf should never enter the forest.

[015-131]What? We have lost? Then stake again.”

[015-132]A second ten-gülden piece did we lose, and then I put down a third. The

[015-133]Grandmother could scarcely remain seated in her chair, so intent was

[015-134]she upon the little ball as it leapt through the notches of the

[015-135]ever-revolving wheel. However, the third ten-gülden piece followed the

[015-136]first two. Upon this the Grandmother went perfectly crazy. She could no

[015-137]longer sit still, and actually struck the table with her fist when the

[015-138]croupier cried out, “Trente-six,” instead of the desiderated zero.

[015-139]“To listen to him!” fumed the old lady. “When will that accursed zero

[015-140]ever turn up? I cannot breathe until I see it. I believe that that

[015-141]infernal croupier is _purposely_ keeping it from turning up. [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis]]

[015-142]Ivanovitch, stake TWO golden pieces this time. The moment we cease to

[015-143]stake, that cursed zero will come turning up, and we shall get

[015-144]nothing.”

[015-145]“My good Madame—”

[015-146]“Stake, stake! It is not _your_ money.”

[015-147]Accordingly I staked two ten-gülden pieces. The ball went hopping round

[015-148]the wheel until it began to settle through the notches. Meanwhile the

[015-149]Grandmother sat as though petrified, with my hand convulsively clutched

[015-150]in hers.

[015-151]“Zero!” called the croupier.

[015-152]“There! You see, you see!” cried the old lady, as she turned and faced

[015-153]me, wreathed in smiles. “I told you so! It was the Lord God himself who

[015-154]suggested to me to stake those two coins. Now, how much ought I to

[015-155]receive? Why do they not pay it out to me? Potapitch! Martha! Where are

[015-156]they? What has become of our party? Potapitch, Potapitch!”

[015-157]“Presently, Madame,” I whispered. “Potapitch is outside, and they would

[015-158]decline to admit him to these rooms. See! You are being paid out your

[015-159]money. Pray take it.” The croupiers were making up a heavy packet of

[015-160]coins, sealed in blue paper, and containing fifty ten gülden pieces,

[015-161]together with an unsealed packet containing another twenty. I handed

[015-162]the whole to the old lady in a money-shovel.

[015-163]“Faites le jeu, messieurs! Faites le jeu, messieurs! Rien ne va plus,”

[015-164]proclaimed the croupier as once more he invited the company to stake,

[015-165]and prepared to turn the wheel.

[015-166]“We shall be too late! He is going to spin again! Stake, stake!” The

[015-167]Grandmother was in a perfect fever. “Do not hang back! Be quick!” She

[015-168]seemed almost beside herself, and nudged me as hard as she could.

[015-169]“Upon what shall I stake, Madame?”

[015-170]“Upon zero, upon zero! Again upon zero! Stake as much as ever you can.

[015-171]How much have we got? Seventy ten-gülden pieces? We shall not miss

[015-172]them, so stake twenty pieces at a time.”

[015-173]“Think a moment, Madame. Sometimes zero does not turn up for two

[015-174]hundred rounds in succession. I assure you that you may lose all your

[015-175]capital.”

[015-176]“You are wrong—utterly wrong. Stake, I tell you! What a chattering

[015-177]tongue you have! I know perfectly well what I am doing.” The old lady

[015-178]was shaking with excitement.

[015-179]“But the rules do not allow of more than 120 gülden being staked upon

[015-180]zero at a time.”

[015-181]“How ‘do not allow’? Surely you are wrong? Monsieur, monsieur—” here

[015-182]she nudged the croupier who was sitting on her left, and preparing to

[015-183]spin—“combien zero? Douze? Douze?”
