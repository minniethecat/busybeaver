# Roulette Bets

The roulette bets in *The Gambler* are both the mechanical structure of the game and a taxonomy of human [[risk|risk-taking]]. Dostoyevsky names each type of wager — not as dry exposition but as a window into character: who bets on what, and why, reveals everything the gambler would prefer to conceal. The bets form a hierarchy from the cautious to the reckless, from the mathematical to the superstitious.

## Definition

The roulette table in the [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] casino offers four main categories of even-chance bets, plus the single-number bets crowned by the [[zero-zero|zéro]]:

| Bet | French Name | Description | Payout |
|-----|------------|-------------|--------|
| Red / Black | Rouge et Noir | Even-money wager on colour | 1:1 |
| Even / Odd | Pair et Impair | Even-money wager on parity | 1:1 |
| Low / High | Manque et Passe | Low (1–18) or high (19–36) | 1:1 |
| Twelve Numbers | Douze | First, second, or third dozen | 2:1 |
| Single Number | Plein | Wager on a single number | 35:1 |

Dostoyevsky names these bets explicitly: "combinations — of 'rouge et noir,' of 'pair et impair,' of 'manque et passe'" (015-099), before introducing the most significant bet of all: the zéro.

## Role in The Gambler

### The Hierarchy of Bets

The novel's characters distribute themselves across the betting hierarchy. The cautious [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] would never venture beyond even chances. Alexei Ivanovich oscillates between prudent even-money bets and the catastrophic single-number wagers that produce his most extreme states. The [[grandmother|Grandmother]] bets on zero with imperious defiance, demanding "Why thirty-five times, when zero so often turns up?" (015-117) — a question that contains both her wilfulness and her mathematical ignorance.

The zéro bet carries special weight because it represents the house advantage made visible. The [[croupier]]'s call of "zéro!" sweeps all even-chance bets into the casino's coffers (015-107). Every gambler fears it; the Grandmother alone treats it as an ally that has betrayed her.

### Key Passages

> "Combinations — of 'rouge et noir,' of 'pair et impair,' of 'manque et passe'" (015-099) — the full catalogue of even-chance bets, delivered as the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] surveys the table.

> "I do not rise above pair, impair, rouge, noir, the twelve middle numbers" (029-032) — Alexei's self-assessment of his betting limits.

> "'But what is zero?' she inquired. 'Just now I heard the croupier call out "zero!"'" (015-106) — the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s fateful question, the prelude to her obsession with the zéro.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky understood roulette bets from painful personal experience. He had lost fortunes at the tables of Wiesbaden and Baden-Baden, and his intimate knowledge of the betting layout is evident in the novel's detailed descriptions. The bets are not merely listed — they are felt. When Alexei declares he does not "rise above pair, impair, rouge, noir" (029-032), the statement carries the weight of a man who knows exactly which bets have ruined him and why.

The [[system|betting system]] — the gambler's attempt to impose order on [[chance]] through patterned betting — haunts the novel as a philosophical problem. Characters who believe in "systems" (like the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] with her insistence on zero) are Dostoyevsky's most deluded, while those who accept randomness (like [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], who bets mechanically) are his most clear-sighted.
