# The General's Confession

[[general|The General]]'s confession of his debts marks the collapse of his pretensions and the exposure of the financial house of cards he has built around the expected [[inheritance]].

## The Burden of Debt

From the novel's early chapters, the General's financial troubles are evident. The narrator notes "his ruined property, his debts, and the usurers" (004-117) as factors weighing on him. [[general|The General]] has borrowed heavily from De Griers, mortgaged his expectations, and entangled himself in [[debt|obligation]]s he cannot meet without the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s death.

## The Forced Revelation

When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] arrives in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] and proceeds to gamble away her [[fortune]], the General's position becomes untenable. He is forced to make "a full confession of his debts" (021-010) to the woman whose inheritance he has been counting on. The confession strips away the last vestiges of his [[honour|dignity]].

## The Consequences

The confession reveals that the General has already spent "almost the whole of my [[money]] in debts" (022-126) — money that wasn't his to spend. The narrator observes the aftermath: the General was dismissed after his "stepfather's debts" (022-151) came to light. The French entourage, particularly De Griers and Mlle Blanche, quickly distance themselves from the ruined man.

## A Study in Weakness

[[general|The General]]'s confession is a character-defining moment. Unlike [[alexei-ivanovitch|the narrator]], who gambles openly and recklessly, the General has gambled more insidiously — on another person's death. His confession exposes not just his debts but his moral bankruptcy.
