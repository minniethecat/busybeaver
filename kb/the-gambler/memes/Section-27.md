# The Gambler · Section 27

[027-001]True enough, the marriage took place. It did so at the close of our

[027-002]month together, and I am bound to suppose that it was upon the ceremony

[027-003]that the last remnants of my money were spent. With it the episode—that

[027-004]is to say, my sojourn with the Frenchwoman—came to an end, and I

[027-005]formally retired from the scene.

[027-006]It happened thus: A week after we had taken up our abode in [[paris|Paris]] there

[027-007]arrived thither the General. He came straight to see us, and

[027-008]thenceforward lived with us practically as our guest, though he had a

[027-009]flat of his own as well. Blanche met him with merry badinage and

[027-010]laughter, and even threw her arms around him. In fact, she managed it

[027-011]so that he had to follow everywhere in her train—whether when

[027-012]promenading on the Boulevards, or when driving, or when going to the

[027-013]theatre, or when paying calls; and this use which she made of him quite

[027-014]satisfied the General. Still of imposing appearance and presence, as

[027-015]well as of fair height, he had a dyed moustache and whiskers (he had

[027-016]formerly been in the cuirassiers), and a handsome, though a somewhat

[027-017]wrinkled, face. Also, his manners were excellent, and he could carry a

[027-018]frockcoat well—the more so since, in Paris, he took to wearing his

[027-019]orders. To promenade the Boulevards with such a man was not only a

[027-020]thing possible, but also, so to speak, a thing advisable, and with this

[027-021]programme the good but foolish General had not a fault to find. The

[027-022]truth is that he had never counted upon this programme when he came to

[027-023]Paris to seek us out. On that occasion he had made his appearance

[027-024]nearly shaking with terror, for he had supposed that Blanche would at

[027-025]once raise an outcry, and have him put from the door; wherefore, he was

[027-026]the more enraptured at the turn that things had taken, and spent the

[027-027]month in a state of senseless ecstasy. Already I had learnt that, after

[027-028]our unexpected departure from [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]], he had had a sort of a

[027-029]fit—that he had fallen into a swoon, and spent a week in a species of

[027-030]garrulous delirium. Doctors had been summoned to him, but he had broken

[027-031]away from them, and suddenly taken a train to Paris. Of course

[027-032]Blanche’s reception of him had acted as the best of all possible cures,

[027-033]but for long enough he carried the marks of his affliction, despite his

[027-034]present condition of rapture and delight. To think clearly, or even to

[027-035]engage in any serious conversation, had now become impossible for him;

[027-036]he could only ejaculate after each word “Hm!” and then nod his head in

[027-037]confirmation. Sometimes, also, he would laugh, but only in a nervous,

[027-038]hysterical sort of a fashion; while at other times he would sit for

[027-039]hours looking as black as night, with his heavy eyebrows knitted. Of

[027-040]much that went on he remained wholly oblivious, for he grew extremely

[027-041]absent-minded, and took to talking to himself. Only Blanche could awake

[027-042]him to any semblance of life. His fits of depression and moodiness in

[027-043]corners always meant either that he had not seen her for some while, or

[027-044]that she had gone out without taking him with her, or that she had

[027-045]omitted to caress him before departing. When in this condition, he

[027-046]would refuse to say what he wanted—nor had he the least idea that he

[027-047]was thus sulking and moping. Next, after remaining in this condition

[027-048]for an hour or two (this I remarked on two occasions when Blanche had

[027-049]gone out for the day—probably to see Albert), he would begin to look

[027-050]about him, and to grow uneasy, and to hurry about with an air as though

[027-051]he had suddenly remembered something, and must try and find it; after

[027-052]which, not perceiving the object of his search, nor succeeding in

[027-053]recalling what that object had been, he would as suddenly relapse into

[027-054]oblivion, and continue so until the reappearance of Blanche—merry,

[027-055]wanton, half-dressed, and laughing her strident laugh as she approached

[027-056]to pet him, and even to kiss him (though the latter reward he seldom

[027-057]received). Once, he was so overjoyed at her doing so that he burst into

[027-058]tears. Even I myself was surprised.

[027-059]From the first moment of his arrival in Paris, Blanche set herself to

[027-060]plead with me on his behalf; and at such times she even rose to heights

[027-061]of eloquence—saying that it was for _me_ she had abandoned him, though

[027-062]she had almost become his betrothed and promised to become so; that it

[027-063]was for _her_ sake he had deserted his family; that, having been in his

[027-064]service, I ought to remember the fact, and to feel ashamed. To all this

[027-065]I would say nothing, however much she chattered on; until at length I

[027-066]would burst out laughing, and the incident would come to an end (at

[027-067]first, as I have said, she had thought me a fool, but since she had

[027-068]come to deem me a man of sense and sensibility). In short, I had the

[027-069]happiness of calling her better nature into [[gambling|play]]; for though, at first,

[027-070]I had not deemed her so, she was, in reality, a kind-hearted woman

[027-071]after her own fashion. “You are good and clever,” she said to me

[027-072]towards the finish, “and my one regret is that you are also so

[027-073]wrong-headed. You will _her_ be a rich man!”

[027-074]“Un vrai Russe—un Kalmuk” she usually called me.

[027-075]Several times she sent me to give the General an airing in the streets,

[027-076]even as she might have done with a lacquey and her spaniel; but, I

[027-077]preferred to take him to the theatre, to the Bal Mabille, and to

[027-078]restaurants. For this purpose she usually allowed me some money, though

[027-079]the General had a little of his own, and enjoyed taking out his purse

[027-080]before strangers. Once I had to use actual force to prevent him from

[027-081]buying a phaeton at a price of seven hundred francs, after a vehicle

[027-082]had caught his fancy in the Palais Royal as seeming to be a desirable

[027-083]present for Blanche. What could _she_ have done with a

[027-084]seven-hundred-franc phaeton?—and the General possessed in the world but

[027-085]a thousand francs! The origin even of those francs I could never

[027-086]determine, but imagined them to have emanated from [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]]—the more

[027-087]so since the latter had paid the family’s hotel bill. As for what view

[027-088]the General took of myself, I think that he never divined the footing

[027-089]on which I stood with Blanche. True, he had heard, in a dim sort of

[027-090]way, that I had won a good deal of money; but more probably he supposed

[027-091]me to be acting as secretary—or even as a kind of servant—to his

[027-092]inamorata. At all events, he continued to address me, in his old

[027-093]haughty style, as my superior. At times he even took it upon himself to

[027-094]scold me. One morning in particular, he started to sneer at me over our

[027-095]matutinal coffee. Though not a man prone to take offence, he suddenly,

[027-096]and for some reason of which to this day I am ignorant, fell out with

[027-097]me. Of course even he himself did not know the reason. To put things

[027-098]shortly, he began a speech which had neither beginning nor ending, and

[027-099]cried out, à bâtons rompus, that I was a boy whom he would soon put to

[027-100]rights—and so forth, and so forth. Yet no one could understand what he

[027-101]was saying, and at length Blanche exploded in a burst of laughter.

[027-102]Finally something appeased him, and he was taken out for his walk. More

[027-103]than once, however, I noticed that his depression was growing upon him;

[027-104]that he seemed to be feeling the want of somebody or something; that,

[027-105]despite Blanche’s presence, he was missing some person in particular.

[027-106]Twice, on these occasions, did he plunge into a conversation with me,

[027-107]though he could not make himself intelligible, and only went on

[027-108]rambling about the service, his late wife, his home, and his property.

[027-109]Every now and then, also, some particular word would please him;

[027-110]whereupon he would repeat it a hundred times in the day—even though the

[027-111]word happened to express neither his thoughts nor his feelings. Again,

[027-112]I would try to get him to talk about his children, but always he cut me

[027-113]short in his old snappish way, and passed to another subject. “Yes,

[027-114]yes—my children,” was all that I could extract from him. “Yes, you are

[027-115]right in what you have said about them.” Only once did he disclose his

[027-116]real feelings. That was when we were taking him to the theatre, and

[027-117]suddenly he exclaimed: “My unfortunate children! Yes, sir, they _are_

[027-118]unfortunate children.” Once, too, when I chanced to mention [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], he

[027-119]grew quite bitter against her. “She is an ungrateful woman!” he

[027-120]exclaimed. “She is a bad and ungrateful woman! She has broken up a

[027-121]family. If there were laws here, I would have her impaled. Yes, I

[027-122]would.” As for De Griers, the General would not have his name

[027-123]mentioned. “He has ruined me,” he would say. “He has robbed me, and cut

[027-124]my throat. For two years he was a perfect nightmare to me. For months

[027-125]at a time he never left me in my dreams. Do not speak of him again.”

[027-126]It was now clear to me that Blanche and he were on the point of coming

[027-127]to terms; yet, true to my usual custom, I said nothing. At length,

[027-128]Blanche took the initiative in explaining matters. She did so a week

[027-129]before we parted.

[027-130]“Il a de la [[probability|chance]],” she prattled, “for the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] is now _really_

[027-131]ill, and therefore, bound to die. Mr. Astley has just sent a telegram

[027-132]to say so, and you will agree with me that the General is likely to be

[027-133]her heir. Even if he should not be so, he will not come amiss, since,

[027-134]in the first place, he has his pension, and, in the second place, he

[027-135]will be content to live in a back room; whereas _I_ shall be Madame

[027-136]General, and get into a good circle of society” (she was always

[027-137]thinking of this) “and become a Russian châtelaine. Yes, I shall have a

[027-138]mansion of my own, and peasants, and a million of money at my back.”

[027-139]“But, suppose he should prove jealous? He might demand all sorts of

[027-140]things, you know. Do you follow me?”

[027-141]“Oh, dear no! How ridiculous that would be of him! Besides, I have

[027-142]taken measures to prevent it. You need not be alarmed. That is to say,

[027-143]I have induced him to sign notes of hand in Albert’s name.

[027-144]Consequently, at any time I could get him punished. Isn’t he

[027-145]ridiculous?”

[027-146]“Very well, then. Marry him.”

[027-147]And, in truth, she did so—though the marriage was a family one only,

[027-148]and involved no pomp or ceremony. In fact, she invited to the nuptials

[027-149]none but Albert and a few other friends. Hortense, Cléopatre, and the

[027-150]rest she kept firmly at a distance. As for the bridegroom, he took a

[027-151]great interest in his new position. Blanche herself tied his tie, and

[027-152]Blanche herself pomaded him—with the result that, in his frockcoat and

[027-153]white waistcoat, he looked quite comme il faut.

[027-154]“Il est, pourtant, _très_ comme il faut,” Blanche remarked when she

[027-155]issued from his room, as though the idea that he was “_très_ comme il

[027-156]faut” had impressed even her. For myself, I had so little knowledge of

[027-157]the minor details of the affair, and took part in it so much as a

[027-158]supine spectator, that I have forgotten most of what passed on this

[027-159]occasion. I only remember that Blanche and the Widow figured at it, not

[027-160]as “de Cominges,” but as “du Placet.” Why they had hitherto been “de

[027-161]Cominges” I do not know—I only know that this entirely satisfied the

[027-162]General, that he liked the name “du Placet” even better than he had

[027-163]liked the name “de Cominges.” On the morning of the wedding, he paced

[027-164]the salon in his gala attire and kept repeating to himself with an air

[027-165]of great gravity and importance: “[[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] du Placet! Mlle.

[027-166]Blanche du Placet, du Placet!” He beamed with satisfaction as he did

[027-167]so. Both in the church and at the wedding breakfast he remained not

[027-168]only pleased and contented, but even proud. She too underwent a change,

[027-169]for now she assumed an air of added [[honour|dignity]].
