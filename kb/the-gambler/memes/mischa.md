# Mischa

**Mischa** is the young son of [[general|the General]], appearing only fleetingly in *The Gambler*. Along with his sister [[nadia|Nadia]], he belongs to the novel's almost invisible population of children — those whose welfare is invoked to justify adult schemes but who receive virtually no direct care or attention.

## Appearances

Mischa appears in only two passages. In the first, [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexei]] is about to take "Mischa and Nadia for a walk" (001-046) when he is interrupted — a typical moment in the novel where children's needs are deferred in favour of adult drama. In the second, after Nadia is sent away to play, "Mischa also I dispatched" (002-033) by [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] — the children dismissed together, clearing the stage for the adults' charged conversation.

## Structural Function

Mischa, like Nadia, exists in the novel primarily as an absence. The General repeatedly invokes "the children" as the justification for his financial schemes — the debt to [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]], the desperate waiting for the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s [[inheritance]] — yet Mischa himself is never shown receiving parental attention. He is walked by the tutor, dispatched by the step-sister, and otherwise invisible.

This structural neglect is Dostoyevsky's quiet indictment: the gambling fever that possesses every adult in Roulettenberg has real victims, and they are the children who appear in the narrative only to be sent away. Mischa's near-invisibility is precisely the point — in a world governed by [[roulette]], [[debt]], and [[desire]], there is no room for a child.
