# The Gambler · Section 4

[004-001]“Why not?” she asked excitedly.

[004-002]“Because I wish to [[gambling|play]] _for myself_,” I replied with a feigned glance

[004-003]of astonishment. “That is my sole reason.”

[004-004]“Then are you so certain that your roulette-playing will get us out of

[004-005]our difficulties?” she inquired with a quizzical smile.

[004-006]I said very seriously, “Yes,” and then added: “Possibly my certainty

[004-007]about winning may seem to you ridiculous; yet, pray leave me in peace.”

[004-008]Nonetheless she insisted that I ought to go halves with her in the

[004-009]day’s [[winning|winnings]], and offered me 800 gülden on condition that henceforth,

[004-010]I gambled only on those terms; but I refused to do so, once and for

[004-011]all—stating, as my reason, that I found myself unable to play on behalf

[004-012]of any one else, “I am not unwilling so to do,” I added, “but in all

[004-013][[chance|probability]] I should lose.”

[004-014]“Well, absurd though it be, I place great hopes on your playing of

[004-015]roulette,” she remarked musingly; “wherefore, you ought to play as my

[004-016]partner and on equal shares; wherefore, of course, you will do as I

[004-017]wish.”

[004-018]Then she left me without listening to any further protests on my part.

[004-019]III

[004-020]On the morrow she said not a word to me about gambling. In fact, she

[004-021]purposely avoided me, although her old manner to me had not changed:

[004-022]the same serene coolness was hers on meeting me—a coolness that was

[004-023]mingled even with a spice of contempt and dislike. In short, she was at

[004-024]no pains to conceal her aversion to me. That I could see plainly. Also,

[004-025]she did not trouble to conceal from me the fact that I was necessary to

[004-026]her, and that she was keeping me for some end which she had in view.

[004-027]Consequently there became established between us relations which, to a

[004-028]large extent, were incomprehensible to me, considering her general

[004-029]pride and aloofness. For example, although she knew that I was madly in

[004-030]love with her, she allowed me to speak to her of my [[love|passion]] (though she

[004-031]could not well have showed her contempt for me more than by permitting

[004-032]me, unhindered and unrebuked, to mention to her my love).

[004-033]“You see,” her attitude expressed, “how little I regard your feelings,

[004-034]as well as how little I care for what you say to me, or for what you

[004-035]feel for me.” Likewise, though she spoke as before concerning her

[004-036]affairs, it was never with complete frankness. In her contempt for me

[004-037]there were refinements. Although she knew well that I was aware of a

[004-038]certain circumstance in her life of something which might one day cause

[004-039]her trouble, she would speak to me about her affairs (whenever she had

[004-040]need of me for a given end) as though I were a slave or a passing

[004-041]acquaintance—yet tell them me only in so far as one would need to know

[004-042]them if one were going to be made temporary use of. Had I not known the

[004-043]whole chain of events, or had she not seen how much I was pained and

[004-044]disturbed by her teasing insistency, she would never have thought it

[004-045]worthwhile to soothe me with this frankness—even though, since she not

[004-046]infrequently used me to execute commissions that were not only

[004-047]troublesome, but risky, she ought, in my opinion, to have been frank in

[004-048]_any_ case. But, forsooth, it was not worth her while to trouble about

[004-049]_my_ feelings—about the fact that _I_ was uneasy, and, perhaps, thrice

[004-050]as put about by her cares and misfortunes as she was herself!

[004-051]For three weeks I had known of her intention to take to roulette. She

[004-052]had even warned me that she would like me to play on her behalf, since

[004-053]it was unbecoming for her to play in person; and, from the tone of her

[004-054]words I had gathered that there was something on her mind besides a

[004-055]mere desire to win money. As if money could matter to _her!_ No, she

[004-056]had some end in view, and there were circumstances at which I could

[004-057]guess, but which I did not know for certain. True, the slavery and

[004-058]abasement in which she held me might have given me (such things often

[004-059]do so) the power to question her with abrupt directness (seeing that,

[004-060]inasmuch as I figured in her eyes as a mere slave and nonentity, she

[004-061]could not very well have taken offence at any rude curiosity); but the

[004-062]fact was that, though she let me question her, she never returned me a

[004-063]single answer, and at times did not so much as notice me. That is how

[004-064]matters stood.

[004-065]Next day there was a good deal of talk about a telegram which, four

[004-066]days ago, had been sent to [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]], but to which there had come

[004-067]no answer. [[general|The General]] was visibly disturbed and moody, for the matter

[004-068]concerned his mother. The Frenchman, too, was excited, and after dinner

[004-069]the whole party talked long and seriously together—[[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]]’s tone

[004-070]being extraordinarily presumptuous and offhand to everybody. It almost

[004-071]reminded one of the proverb, “Invite a man to your table, and soon he

[004-072]will place his feet upon it.” Even to [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] he was brusque almost to

[004-073]the point of rudeness. Yet still he seemed glad to join us in our walks

[004-074]in the Casino, or in our rides and drives about the town. I had long

[004-075]been aware of certain circumstances which bound the General to him; I

[004-076]had long been aware that in [[russia|Russia]] they had hatched some scheme

[004-077]together although I did not know whether the plot had come to anything,

[004-078]or whether it was still only in the stage of being talked of. Likewise

[004-079]I was aware, in part, of a family secret—namely, that, last year, the

[004-080]Frenchman had bailed the General out of debt, and given him 30,000

[004-081]roubles wherewith to pay his Treasury dues on retiring from the

[004-082]service. And now, of course, the General was in a vice—although the

[004-083]chief part in the affair was being played by [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]. Yes, of

[004-084]this last I had no doubt.

[004-085]But _who_ was this Mlle. Blanche? It was said of her that she was a

[004-086]Frenchwoman of good birth who, living with her mother, possessed a

[004-087]colossal fortune. It was also said that she was some relation to the

[004-088]Marquis, but only a distant one a cousin, or cousin-german, or

[004-089]something of the sort. Likewise I knew that, up to the time of my

[004-090]journey to [[paris|Paris]], she and the Frenchman had been more ceremonious

[004-091]towards our party—they had stood on a much more precise and delicate

[004-092]footing with them; but that now their acquaintanceship—their

[004-093]friendship, their intimacy—had taken on a much more off-hand and

[004-094]rough-and-ready air. Perhaps they thought that our means were too

[004-095]modest for them, and, therefore, unworthy of politeness or reticence.

[004-096]Also, for the last three days I had noticed certain looks which [[mr-astley|Astley]]

[004-097]had kept throwing at Mlle. Blanche and her mother; and it had occurred

[004-098]to me that he must have had some previous acquaintance with the pair. I

[004-099]had even surmised that the Frenchman too must have met Mr. Astley

[004-100]before. Astley was a man so shy, reserved, and taciturn in his manner

[004-101]that one might have looked for anything from him. At all events the

[004-102]Frenchman accorded him only the slightest of greetings, and scarcely

[004-103]even looked at him. Certainly he did not seem to be afraid of him;

[004-104]which was intelligible enough. But why did Mlle. Blanche also never

[004-105]look at the Englishman?—particularly since, _à propos_ of something or

[004-106]another, the Marquis had declared the Englishman to be immensely and

[004-107]indubitably rich? Was not that a sufficient reason to make Mlle.

[004-108]Blanche look at the Englishman? Anyway the General seemed extremely

[004-109]uneasy; and, one could well understand what a telegram to announce the

[004-110]death of his mother would mean for him!

[004-111]Although I thought it probable that Polina was avoiding me for a

[004-112]definite reason, I adopted a cold and indifferent air; for I felt

[004-113]pretty certain that it would not be long before she herself approached

[004-114]me. For two days, therefore, I devoted my attention to Mlle. Blanche.

[004-115]The poor General was in despair! To fall in love at fifty-five, and

[004-116]with such vehemence, is indeed a [[fortune|misfortune]]! And add to that his

[004-117]widowerhood, his children, his ruined property, his debts, and the

[004-118]woman with whom he had fallen in love! Though Mlle. Blanche was

[004-119]extremely good-looking, I may or may not be understood when I say that

[004-120]she had one of those faces which one is afraid of. At all events, I

[004-121]myself have always feared such women. Apparently about twenty-five

[004-122]years of age, she was tall and broad-shouldered, with shoulders that

[004-123]sloped; yet though her neck and bosom were ample in their proportions,

[004-124]her skin was dull yellow in colour, while her hair (which was extremely

[004-125]abundant—sufficient to make two coiffures) was as black as Indian ink.

[004-126]Add to that a pair of black eyes with yellowish whites, a proud glance,

[004-127]gleaming teeth, and lips which were perennially pomaded and redolent of

[004-128]musk. As for her dress, it was invariably rich, effective, and chic,

[004-129]yet in good taste. Lastly, her feet and hands were astonishing, and her

[004-130]voice a deep contralto. Sometimes, when she laughed, she displayed her

[004-131]teeth, but at ordinary times her air was taciturn and

[004-132]haughty—especially in the presence of Polina and [[maria-philipovna|Maria Philipovna]]. Yet

[004-133]she seemed to me almost destitute of education, and even of wits,

[004-134]though cunning and suspicious. This, apparently, was not because her

[004-135]life had been lacking in incident. Perhaps, if all were known, the

[004-136]Marquis was not her kinsman at all, nor her mother, her mother; but

[004-137]there was evidence that, in Berlin, where we had first come across the

[004-138]pair, they had possessed acquaintances of good standing. As for the

[004-139]Marquis himself, I doubt to this day if he was a Marquis—although about

[004-140]the fact that he had formerly belonged to high society (for instance,

[004-141]in [[moscow|Moscow]] and Germany) there could be no doubt whatever. What he had

[004-142]formerly been in France I had not a notion. All I knew was that he was

[004-143]said to possess a château. During the last two weeks I had looked for

[004-144]much to transpire, but am still ignorant whether at that time anything

[004-145]decisive ever passed between Mademoiselle and the General. Everything

[004-146]seemed to depend upon our means—upon whether the General would be able

[004-147]to flourish sufficient money in her face. If ever the news should

[004-148]arrive that the grandmother was not dead, Mlle. Blanche, I felt sure,

[004-149]would disappear in a twinkling. Indeed, it surprised and amused me to

[004-150]observe what a passion for intrigue I was developing. But how I loathed

[004-151]it all! With what pleasure would I have given everybody and everything

[004-152]the go-by! Only—I could not leave Polina. How, then, could I show

[004-153]contempt for those who surrounded her? Espionage is a base thing,

[004-154]but—what have I to do with that?

[004-155]Mr. Astley, too, I found a curious person. I was only sure that he had

[004-156]fallen in love with Polina. A remarkable and diverting circumstance is

[004-157]the amount which may lie in the mien of a shy and painfully modest man

[004-158]who has been touched with the divine passion—especially when he would

[004-159]rather sink into the earth than betray himself by a single word or

[004-160]look. Though Mr. Astley frequently met us when we were out walking, he

[004-161]would merely take off his hat and pass us by, though I knew he was

[004-162]dying to join us. Even when invited to do so, he would refuse. Again,

[004-163]in places of amusement—in the Casino, at concerts, or near the

[004-164]fountain—he was never far from the spot where we were sitting. In fact,

[004-165]_wherever_ we were in the Park, in the forest, or on the

[004-166]Shlangenberg—one needed but to raise one’s eyes and glance around to

[004-167]catch sight of at least a _portion_ of Mr. Astley’s frame sticking

[004-168]out—whether on an adjacent path or behind a bush. Yet never did he lose

[004-169]any chance of speaking to myself; and, one morning when we had met, and

[004-170]exchanged a couple of words, he burst out in his usual abrupt way,

[004-171]without saying “Good-morning.”
