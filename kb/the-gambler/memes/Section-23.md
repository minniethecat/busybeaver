# The Gambler · Section 23

[023-001]Her eyes flashed fire.

[023-002]“What? _you yourself_ wish me to leave you for him?” she cried with a

[023-003]scornful look and a proud smile. Never before had she addressed me

[023-004]thus.

[023-005]Then her head must have turned dizzy with emotion, for suddenly she

[023-006]seated herself upon the sofa, as though she were powerless any longer

[023-007]to stand.

[023-008]A flash of lightning seemed to strike me as I stood there. I could

[023-009]scarcely believe my eyes or my ears. She _did_ love me, then! It _was_

[023-010]to me, and not to [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], that she had turned! Although she, an

[023-011]unprotected girl, had come to me in my room—in an hotel room—and had

[023-012]probably compromised herself thereby, I had not understood!

[023-013]Then a second mad idea flashed into my brain.

[023-014]“[[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]],” I said, “give me but an hour. Wait here just one hour until I

[023-015]return. Yes, you MUST do so. Do you not see what I mean? Just stay here

[023-016]for that time.”

[023-017]And I rushed from the room without so much as answering her look of

[023-018]inquiry. She called something after me, but I did not return.

[023-019]Sometimes it happens that the most insane thought, the most impossible

[023-020]conception, will become so fixed in one’s head that at length one

[023-021]believes the thought or the conception to be reality. Moreover, if with

[023-022]the thought or the conception there is combined a strong, a passionate,

[023-023]desire, one will come to look upon the said thought or conception as

[023-024]something fated, inevitable, and foreordained—something bound to

[023-025]happen. Whether by this there is connoted something in the nature of a

[023-026]combination of presentiments, or a great effort of will, or a

[023-027]self-annulment of one’s true expectations, and so on, I do not know;

[023-028]but, at all events that night saw happen to me (a night which I shall

[023-029]never forget) something in the nature of the miraculous. Although the

[023-030]occurrence can easily be explained by arithmetic, I still believe it to

[023-031]have been a miracle. Yet why did this conviction take such a hold upon

[023-032]me at the time, and remain with me ever since? Previously, I had

[023-033]thought of the idea, not as an occurrence which was ever likely to come

[023-034]about, but as something which _never_ could come about.

[023-035]The time was a quarter past eleven o’clock when I entered the Casino in

[023-036]such a state of hope (though, at the same time, of agitation) as I had

[023-037]never before experienced. In the [[gambling|gaming]]-rooms there were still a large

[023-038]number of people, but not half as many as had been present in the

[023-039]morning.

[023-040]At eleven o’clock there usually remained behind only the real, the

[023-041]desperate gamblers—persons for whom, at spas, there existed nothing

[023-042]beyond roulette, and who went thither for that alone. These gamesters

[023-043]took little note of what was going on around them, and were interested

[023-044]in none of the appurtenances of the season, but played from morning

[023-045]till night, and would have been ready to play through the night until

[023-046]dawn had that been possible. As it was, they used to disperse

[023-047]unwillingly when, at midnight, roulette came to an end. Likewise, as

[023-048]soon as ever roulette was drawing to a close and the head croupier had

[023-049]called “Les trois derniers coups,” most of them were ready to [[bet|stake]] on

[023-050]the last three rounds all that they had in their pockets—and, for the

[023-051]most part, lost it. For my own part I proceeded towards the table at

[023-052]which the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] had lately sat; and, since the crowd around it

[023-053]was not very large, I soon obtained standing room among the ring of

[023-054]gamblers, while directly in front of me, on the green cloth, I saw

[023-055]marked the word “Passe.”

[023-056]“Passe” was a row of numbers from 19 to 36 inclusive; while a row of

[023-057]numbers from 1 to 18 inclusive was known as “Manque.” But what had that

[023-058]to do with me? I had not noticed—I had not so much as heard the numbers

[023-059]upon which the previous coup had fallen, and so took no bearings when I

[023-060]began to play, as, in my place, any _systematic_ gambler would have

[023-061]done. No, I merely extended my stock of twenty ten-gülden pieces, and

[023-062]threw them down upon the space “Passe” which happened to be confronting

[023-063]me.

[023-064]“Vingt-deux!” called the croupier.

[023-065]I had won! I staked upon the same again—both my original [[risk|stake]] and my

[023-066][[winning|winnings]].

[023-067]“Trente-et-un!” called the croupier.

[023-068]Again I had won, and was now in possession of eighty ten-gülden pieces.

[023-069]Next, I moved the whole eighty on to twelve middle numbers (a stake

[023-070]which, if successful, would bring me in a triple profit, but also

[023-071]involved a risk of two chances to one). The wheel revolved, and stopped

[023-072]at twenty-four. Upon this I was paid out notes and gold until I had by

[023-073]my side a total sum of two thousand gülden.

[023-074]It was as in a fever that I moved the pile, en bloc, on to the red.

[023-075]Then suddenly I came to myself (though that was the only time during

[023-076]the evening’s play when fear cast its cold spell over me, and showed

[023-077]itself in a trembling of the hands and knees). For with horror I had

[023-078]realised that I _must_ win, and that upon that stake there depended all

[023-079]my life.

[023-080]“Rouge!” called the croupier. I drew a long breath, and hot shivers

[023-081]went coursing over my body. I was paid out my winnings in

[023-082]bank-notes—amounting, of course, to a total of four thousand florins,

[023-083]eight hundred gülden (I could still calculate the amounts).

[023-084]After that, I remember, I again staked two thousand florins upon twelve

[023-085]middle numbers, and lost. Again I staked the whole of my gold, with

[023-086]eight hundred gülden, in notes, and lost. Then madness seemed to come

[023-087]upon me, and seizing my last two thousand florins, I staked them upon

[023-088]twelve of the first numbers—wholly by chance, and at random, and

[023-089]without any sort of reckoning. Upon my doing so there followed a moment

[023-090]of suspense only comparable to that which Madame Blanchard must have

[023-091]experienced when, in [[paris|Paris]], she was descending earthwards from a

[023-092]balloon.

[023-093]“Quatre!” called the croupier.

[023-094]Once more, with the addition of my original stake, I was in possession

[023-095]of six thousand florins! Once more I looked around me like a

[023-096]conqueror—once more I feared nothing as I threw down four thousand of

[023-097]these florins upon the black. The croupiers glanced around them, and

[023-098]exchanged a few words; the bystanders murmured expectantly.

[023-099]The black turned up. After that I do not exactly remember either my

[023-100]calculations or the order of my stakings. I only remember that, as in a

[023-101]dream, I won in one round sixteen thousand florins; that in the three

[023-102]following rounds, I lost twelve thousand; that I moved the remainder

[023-103](four thousand) on to “Passe” (though quite unconscious of what I was

[023-104]doing—I was merely waiting, as it were, mechanically, and without

[023-105]reflection, for something) and won; and that, finally, four times in

[023-106]succession I lost. Yes, I can remember raking in money by thousands—but

[023-107]most frequently on the twelve, middle numbers, to which I constantly

[023-108]adhered, and which kept appearing in a sort of regular order—first,

[023-109]three or four times running, and then, after an interval of a couple of

[023-110]rounds, in another break of three or four appearances. Sometimes, this

[023-111]astonishing regularity manifested itself in patches; a thing to upset

[023-112]all the calculations of note—taking gamblers who play with a pencil and

[023-113]a memorandum-book in their hands [[fortune|Fortune]] perpetrates some terrible

[023-114]jests at roulette!

[023-115]Since my entry not more than half an hour could have elapsed. Suddenly

[023-116]a croupier informed me that I had, won thirty thousand florins, as well

[023-117]as that, since the latter was the limit for which, at any one time, the

[023-118]bank could make itself responsible, roulette at that table must close

[023-119]for the night. Accordingly, I caught up my pile of gold, stuffed it

[023-120]into my pocket, and, grasping my sheaf of bank-notes, moved to the

[023-121]table in an adjoining salon where a second game of roulette was in

[023-122]progress. The crowd followed me in a body, and cleared a place for me

[023-123]at the table; after which, I proceeded to stake as before—that is to

[023-124]say, at random and without calculating. What saved me from ruin I do

[023-125]not know.

[023-126]Of course there were times when fragmentary reckonings _did_ come

[023-127]flashing into my brain. For instance, there were times when I attached

[023-128]myself for a while to certain figures and coups—though always leaving

[023-129]them, again before long, without knowing what I was doing.

[023-130]In fact, I cannot have been in possession of all my faculties, for I

[023-131]can remember the croupiers correcting my play more than once, owing to

[023-132]my having made mistakes of the gravest order. My brows were damp with

[023-133]sweat, and my hands were shaking. Also, Poles came around me to proffer

[023-134]their services, but I heeded none of them. Nor did my [[chance|luck]] fail me now.

[023-135]Suddenly, there arose around me a loud din of talking and laughter.

[023-136]“Bravo, bravo!” was the general shout, and some people even clapped

[023-137]their hands. I had raked in thirty thousand florins, and again [[gambling-bank|the bank]]

[023-138]had had to close for the night!

[023-139]“Go away now, go away now,” a voice whispered to me on my right. The

[023-140]person who had spoken to me was a certain Jew of Frankfurt—a man who

[023-141]had been standing beside me the whole while, and occasionally helping

[023-142]me in my play.

[023-143]“Yes, for God’s sake go,” whispered a second voice in my left ear.

[023-144]Glancing around, I perceived that the second voice had come from a

[023-145]modestly, plainly dressed lady of rather less than thirty—a woman whose

[023-146]face, though pale and sickly-looking, bore also very evident traces of

[023-147]former beauty. At the moment, I was stuffing the crumpled bank-notes

[023-148]into my pockets and collecting all the gold that was left on the table.

[023-149]Seizing up my last note for five hundred gülden, I contrived to

[023-150]insinuate it, unperceived, into the hand of the pale lady. An

[023-151]overpowering impulse had made me do so, and I remember how her thin

[023-152]little fingers pressed mine in token of her lively gratitude. The whole

[023-153]affair was the work of a moment.

[023-154]Then, collecting my belongings, I crossed to where trente et quarante

[023-155]was being played—a game which could boast of a more aristocratic

[023-156]public, and was played with cards instead of with a wheel. At this

[023-157]diversion the bank made itself responsible for a hundred thousand

[023-158]thalers as the limit, but the highest stake allowable was, as in

[023-159]roulette, four thousand florins. Although I knew nothing of the

[023-160]game—and I scarcely knew the stakes, except those on black and red—I

[023-161]joined the ring of players, while the rest of the crowd massed itself

[023-162]around me. At this distance of time I cannot remember whether I ever

[023-163]gave a thought to Polina; I seemed only to be conscious of a vague

[023-164]pleasure in seizing and raking in the bank-notes which kept massing

[023-165]themselves in a pile before me.

[023-166]But, as ever, fortune seemed to be at my back. As though of set

[023-167]purpose, there came to my aid a circumstance which not infrequently

[023-168]repeats itself in gaming. The circumstance is that not infrequently

[023-169][[chance|luck]] attaches itself to, say, the red, and does not leave it for a

[023-170]space of say, ten, or even fifteen, rounds in succession. Three days

[023-171]ago I had heard that, during the previous week there had been a run of

[023-172]twenty-two coups on the red—an occurrence never before known at

[023-173]roulette—so that men spoke of it with astonishment. Naturally enough,

[023-174]many deserted the red after a dozen rounds, and practically no one

[023-175]could now be found to stake upon it. Yet upon the black also—the

[023-176]antithesis of the red—no experienced gambler would stake anything, for

[023-177]the reason that every practised player knows the meaning of “capricious

[023-178]fortune.” That is to say, after the sixteenth (or so) success of the

[023-179]red, one would think that the seventeenth coup would inevitably fall

[023-180]upon the black; wherefore, novices would be apt to back the latter in

[023-181]the seventeenth round, and even to double or treble their stakes upon

[023-182]it—only, in the end, to lose.
