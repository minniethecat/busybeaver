# The Gambler · Section 17

[017-001]All these thoughts passed through my mind during the few moments that,

[017-002]having left [[grandmother|the old lady]]’s rooms, I was ascending to my own room on the

[017-003]top storey. What most struck me was the fact that, though I had divined

[017-004]the chief, the stoutest, threads which united the various actors in the

[017-005]drama, I had, until now, been ignorant of the methods and secrets of

[017-006][[roulette|the game]]. For [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] had never been completely open with me. Although,

[017-007]on occasions, it had happened that involuntarily, as it were, she had

[017-008]revealed to me something of her heart, I had noticed that in most

[017-009]cases—in fact, nearly always—she had either laughed away these

[017-010]revelations, or grown confused, or purposely imparted to them a false

[017-011]guise. Yes, she must have concealed a great deal from me. But, I had a

[017-012]presentiment that now the end of this strained and mysterious situation

[017-013]was approaching. Another stroke, and all would be finished and exposed.

[017-014]Of my own fortunes, interested though I was in the affair, I took no

[017-015]account. I was in the strange position of possessing but two hundred

[017-016]gülden, of being at a loose end, of lacking both a post, the means of

[017-017]subsistence, a shred of hope, and any plans for the future, yet of

[017-018]caring nothing for these things. Had not my mind been so full of

[017-019]Polina, I should have given myself up to the comical piquancy of the

[017-020]impending denouement, and laughed my fill at it. But the thought of

[017-021]Polina was torture to me. That her fate was settled I already had an

[017-022]inkling; yet _that_ was not the thought which was giving me so much

[017-023]uneasiness. What I really wished for was to penetrate her secrets. I

[017-024]wanted her to come to me and say, “I love you,” and, if she would not

[017-025]so come, or if to hope that she would ever do so was an unthinkable

[017-026]absurdity—why, then there was nothing else for me to want. Even now I

[017-027]do not know what I am wanting. I feel like a man who has lost his way.

[017-028]I yearn but to be in her presence, and within the circle of her light

[017-029]and splendour—to be there now, and forever, and for the whole of my

[017-030]life. More I do not know. How can I ever bring myself to leave her?

[017-031]On reaching the third storey of the hotel I experienced a shock. I was

[017-032]just passing the General’s suite when something caused me to look

[017-033]round. Out of a door about twenty paces away there was coming Polina!

[017-034]She hesitated for a moment on seeing me, and then beckoned me to her.

[017-035]“Polina Alexandrovna!”

[017-036]“Hush! Not so loud.”

[017-037]“Something startled me just now,” I whispered, “and I looked round, and

[017-038]saw you. Some electrical influence seems to emanate from your form.”

[017-039]“Take this letter,” she went on with a frown (probably she had not even

[017-040]heard my words, she was so preoccupied), “and hand it personally to Mr.

[017-041][[mr-astley|Astley]]. Go as quickly as ever you can, please. No answer will be

[017-042]required. He himself—” She did not finish her sentence.

[017-043]“To Mr. Astley?” I asked, in some astonishment.

[017-044]But she had vanished again.

[017-045]Aha! So the two were carrying on a correspondence! However, I set off

[017-046]to search for Astley—first at his hotel, and then at the Casino, where

[017-047]I went the round of the salons in vain. At length, vexed, and almost in

[017-048]despair, I was on my way home when I ran across him among a troop of

[017-049]English ladies and gentlemen who had been out for a ride. Beckoning to

[017-050]him to stop, I handed him the letter. We had barely time even to look

[017-051]at one another, but I suspected that it was of set purpose that he

[017-052]restarted his horse so quickly.

[017-053]Was jealousy, then, gnawing at me? At all events, I felt exceedingly

[017-054]depressed, despite the fact that I had no desire to ascertain what the

[017-055]correspondence was about. To think that _he_ should be her confidant!

[017-056]“My friend, mine own familiar friend!” passed through my mind. Yet

[017-057]_was_ there any love in the matter? “Of course not,” reason whispered

[017-058]to me. But reason goes for little on such occasions. I felt that the

[017-059]matter must be cleared up, for it was becoming unpleasantly complex.

[017-060]I had scarcely set foot in the hotel when the commissionaire and the

[017-061]landlord (the latter issuing from his room for the purpose) alike

[017-062]informed me that I was being searched for high and low—that three

[017-063]separate messages to ascertain my whereabouts had come down from the

[017-064]General. When I entered his study I was feeling anything but kindly

[017-065]disposed. I found there the General himself, De Griers, and Mlle.

[017-066]Blanche, but not Mlle.’s mother, who was a person whom her reputed

[017-067]daughter used only for show purposes, since in all matters of business

[017-068]the daughter fended for herself, and it is unlikely that the mother

[017-069]knew anything about them.

[017-070]Some very heated discussion was in progress, and meanwhile the door of

[017-071]the study was open—an unprecedented circumstance. As I approached the

[017-072]portals I could hear loud voices raised, for mingled with the pert,

[017-073]venomous accents of De Griers were [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]’s excited, impudently

[017-074]abusive tongue and the General’s plaintive wail as, apparently, he

[017-075]sought to justify himself in something. But on my appearance every one

[017-076]stopped speaking, and tried to put a better face upon matters. De

[017-077]Griers smoothed his hair, and twisted his angry face into a smile—into

[017-078]the mean, studiedly polite French smile which I so detested; while the

[017-079]downcast, perplexed General assumed an air of [[honour|dignity]]—though only in a

[017-080]mechanical way. On the other hand, Mlle. Blanche did not trouble to

[017-081]conceal the wrath that was sparkling in her countenance, but bent her

[017-082]gaze upon me with an air of impatient expectancy. I may remark that

[017-083]hitherto she had treated me with absolute superciliousness, and, so far

[017-084]from answering my salutations, had always ignored them.

[017-085]“[[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]],” began the General in a tone of affectionate

[017-086]upbraiding, “may I say to you that I find it strange, exceedingly

[017-087]strange, that—In short, your conduct towards myself and my family— In a

[017-088]word, your—er—extremely—”

[017-089]“Eh! Ce n’est pas ça,” interrupted De Griers in a tone of impatience

[017-090]and contempt (evidently he was the ruling spirit of the conclave). “Mon

[017-091]cher monsieur, notre général se trompe. What he means to say is that he

[017-092]warns you—he begs of you most earnestly—not to ruin him. I use the

[017-093]expression because—”

[017-094]“Why? Why?” I interjected.

[017-095]“Because you have taken upon yourself to act as guide to this, to

[017-096]this—how shall I express it?—to this old lady, à cette pauvre terrible

[017-097]vieille. But she will only gamble away all that she has—gamble it away

[017-098]like thistledown. You yourself have seen her [[gambling|play]]. Once she has

[017-099]acquired the taste for gambling, she will never leave the

[017-100]roulette-table, but, of sheer perversity and temper, will [[bet|stake]] her

[017-101]all, and lose it. In cases such as hers a gambler can never be torn

[017-102]away from the game; and then—and then—”

[017-103]“And then,” asseverated the General, “you will have ruined my whole

[017-104]family. I and my family are her heirs, for she has no nearer relatives

[017-105]than ourselves. I tell you frankly that my affairs are in great—very

[017-106]great disorder; how much they are so you yourself are partially aware.

[017-107]If she should lose a large sum, or, maybe, her whole fortune, what will

[017-108]become of us—of my children” (here the General exchanged a glance with

[017-109]De Griers) “or of me?” (here he looked at Mlle. Blanche, who turned her

[017-110]head contemptuously away). “Alexis Ivanovitch, I beg of you to save

[017-111]us.”

[017-112]“Tell me, General, how am I to do so? On what footing do I stand here?”

[017-113]“Refuse to take her about. Simply leave her alone.”

[017-114]“But she would soon find some one else to take my place?”

[017-115]“Ce n’est pas ça, ce n’est pas ça,” again interrupted De Griers. “Que

[017-116]diable! Do not leave her alone so much as advise her, persuade her,

[017-117]draw her away. In any case do not let her gamble; find her some

[017-118]counter-attraction.”

[017-119]“And how am I to do that? If only you would undertake the task,

[017-120]Monsieur [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]]!” I said this last as innocently as possible, but at

[017-121]once saw a rapid glance of excited interrogation pass from Mlle.

[017-122]Blanche to De Griers, while in the face of the latter also there

[017-123]gleamed something which he could not repress.

[017-124]“Well, at the present moment she would refuse to accept my services,”

[017-125]said he with a gesture. “But if, later—”

[017-126]Here he gave Mlle. Blanche another glance which was full of meaning;

[017-127]whereupon she advanced towards me with a bewitching smile, and seized

[017-128]and pressed my hands. Devil take it, but how that devilish visage of

[017-129]hers could change! At the present moment it was a visage full of

[017-130]supplication, and as gentle in its expression as that of a smiling,

[017-131]roguish infant. Stealthily, she drew me apart from the rest as though

[017-132]the more completely to separate me from them; and, though no harm came

[017-133]of her doing so—for it was merely a stupid manoeuvre, and no more—I

[017-134]found the situation very unpleasant.

[017-135][[general|The General]] hastened to lend her his support.

[017-136]“Alexis Ivanovitch,” he began, “pray pardon me for having said what I

[017-137]did just now—for having said more than I meant to do. I beg and beseech

[017-138]you, I kiss the hem of your garment, as our Russian saying has it, for

[017-139]you, and only you, can save us. I and Mlle. de Cominges, we all of us

[017-140]beg of you—But you understand, do you not? Surely you understand?” and

[017-141]with his eyes he indicated Mlle. Blanche. Truly he was cutting a

[017-142]pitiful figure!

[017-143]At this moment three low, respectful knocks sounded at the door; which,

[017-144]on being opened, revealed a chambermaid, with [[potapitch|Potapitch]] behind her—come

[017-145]from the Grandmother to request that I should attend her in her rooms.

[017-146]“She is in a bad humour,” added Potapitch.

[017-147]The time was half-past three.

[017-148]“My mistress was unable to sleep,” explained Potapitch; “so, after

[017-149]tossing about for a while, she suddenly rose, called for her chair, and

[017-150]sent me to look for you. She is now in the verandah.”

[017-151]“Quelle mégère!” exclaimed De Griers.

[017-152]True enough, I found Madame in the hotel verandah—much put about at my

[017-153]delay, for she had been unable to contain herself until four o’clock.

[017-154]“Lift me up,” she cried to the bearers, and once more we set out for

[017-155]the roulette-salons.

[017-156]XII

[017-157]The Grandmother was in an impatient, irritable frame of mind. Without

[017-158]doubt the roulette had turned her head, for she appeared to be

[017-159]indifferent to everything else, and, in general, seemed much

[017-160]distraught. For instance, she asked me no questions about objects _en

[017-161]route_, except that, when a sumptuous barouche passed us and raised a

[017-162]cloud of dust, she lifted her hand for a moment, and inquired, “What

[017-163]was that?” Yet even then she did not appear to hear my reply, although

[017-164]at times her abstraction was interrupted by sallies and fits of sharp,

[017-165]impatient fidgeting. Again, when I pointed out to her [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] and

[017-166]Baroness Burmergelm walking to the Casino, she merely looked at them in

[017-167]an absent-minded sort of way, and said with complete indifference,

[017-168]“Ah!” Then, turning sharply to Potapitch and Martha, who were walking

[017-169]behind us, she rapped out:

[017-170]“Why have _you_ attached yourselves to the party? We are not going to

[017-171]take you with us every time. Go home at once.” Then, when the servants

[017-172]had pulled hasty bows and departed, she added to me: “You are all the

[017-173]escort I need.”

[017-174]At the Casino the Grandmother seemed to be expected, for no time was

[017-175]lost in procuring her former place beside the croupier. It is my

[017-176]opinion that though croupiers seem such ordinary, humdrum officials—men

[017-177]who care nothing whether [[gambling-bank|the bank]] wins or loses—they are, in reality,

[017-178]anything but indifferent to the bank’s [[loss|losing]], and are given

[017-179]instructions to attract players, and to keep a watch over the bank’s

[017-180]interests; as also, that for such services, these officials are awarded

[017-181]prizes and premiums. At all events, the croupiers of [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]

[017-182]seemed to look upon the Grandmother as their lawful prey—whereafter

[017-183]there befell what our party had foretold.
