# Polina Alexandrovna

[[polina-alexandrovna|Polina Alexandrovna]] is the emotional centre of *The Gambler*'s interpersonal drama. [[general|The General]]'s stepdaughter, she occupies an ambiguous social position—neither [[servant]] nor full [[family]] member—that mirrors Alexei's own marginality. She is the object of his [[passion|obsessive love]] and the novel's most enigmatic character: proud where Alexei is self-lacerating, withholding where he confesses, and ultimately more vulnerable than her imperious manner suggests (001-035).

## Role in the Novel

Polina appears early, "on seeing me" (001-035) with the immediate charge of unresolved tension. She sends Alexei to gamble on her behalf, initiating the plot's central action (002-030), yet her motivations remain opaque throughout. Is she testing him? Exploiting him? Loving him in a language he cannot understand? The novel refuses to resolve these questions.

Her social position makes her a commodity in the General's schemes. [[general|The General]] plans to marry her to [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]] as part of a broader financial arrangement, and Polina's knowledge of this plan—"the General takes..." (002-097)—fuels her bitterness. She is simultaneously the most powerful figure in the household's emotional economy and its most trapped.

## Key Scenes

> "Polina Alexandrovna, on seeing me..." (001-035) — the charged first encounter, establishing their relationship's tension.

> "Polina and myself—contrived to find..." (002-035) — the walk toward [[casino|the casino]], the novel's first direct collaboration between them.

> "...he was much struck with Polina" (001-111) — Polina as object of male scrutiny, the gaze she endures.

> "Polina was not at all pleased at my..." (002-097) — her perpetual dissatisfaction with Alexei's attempts to please her.

> "I [[desire]] to see if you were speaking seriously" (007-038) — Polina's direct challenge to Alexei, the rare moment when she speaks plainly.

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

Polina's defining trait is her pride—a pride so intense it reads as cruelty. She mocks Alexei, withholds affection, and seems to take [[pleasure]] in his suffering. Yet Dostoyevsky makes clear that this pride is defensive: the armour of a woman whose social position permits no genuine autonomy. "Polina seemed..." (002-035) followed by ambiguous ellipsis is characteristic of how [[alexei-ivanovitch|the narrator]] sees her—always through a veil of his own projection.

Her anger is directed at everyone: the General for scheming with her life, Alexei for loving her without understanding her, [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]] for seeing her as a transaction. "But where on earth..." (002-075) — her retort to some now-lost provocation — captures the perpetual edge in her speech.

### Arc and Development

Polina's arc is one of progressive exposure. As the novel strips away the General's pretensions, the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s [[fortune]], and Alexei's illusions, Polina is revealed as perhaps the novel's most tragic figure: intelligent enough to see her situation clearly, powerless to change it, and too proud to ask for help.

## How Others See Polina

Polina is the novel's most refracting character: every other figure in the household sees a different woman when they look at her, and none of these perspectives captures her whole. The gap between how she is seen and who she actually is constitutes the novel's central epistemological mystery—one the narrator himself never resolves.

### Alexei's View

For [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexei]], Polina is less a person than a project. He loves her obsessively but never sees her clearly: his narrative transforms every ambiguous gesture of hers into evidence for his preferred interpretation. When she sends him to gamble, he reads it as a test of devotion; when she mocks him, he reads it as concealed affection; when she withdraws, he reads it as another form of communication. The reader gradually understands that Alexei's Polina is substantially a creation of his own need—a screen onto which he projects his fantasies of devotion, sacrifice, and romantic transcendence.

The narrator himself acknowledges this obliquely, noting that the syllable *ach!* can be inflected "according as they desire to express different shades of thought or feeling" (007-177). This observation—that even the smallest units of speech are radically ambiguous—applies with special force to his own account of Polina. He hears what he needs to hear.

### The General's View

To [[general|the General]], Polina is an asset. Her [[marriage]] to [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]] is a transaction designed to secure the General's financial position, and Polina's feelings about the arrangement are irrelevant to its terms. The General's perspective reveals the brutal economics underlying the household's social drama: Polina is currency, and her stepfather is negotiating the exchange rate.

This commodification is the unspoken context for everything Polina does. Her pride is not aristocratic hauteur—she has no title and no independent fortune—but the desperate [[dignity]] of someone who knows she is being sold and refuses to go quietly.

### De Griers's View

To [[marquis-de-griers|the Marquis de Griers]], Polina is a prize. His interest in her is entangled with his broader scheme to profit from the General's expected [[inheritance]], and his polished European manners mask a calculating ambition that treats Polina as part of a package deal. Her evident [[contempt]] for him—"he was much struck with Polina" (001-111) is Alexei's report of de Griers's reaction, already filtered through rivalry—does not seem to trouble de Griers, for whom a woman's resistance is merely a negotiating position.

De Griers's perspective illuminates the gender politics of the novel's world: a woman's consent is optional when the men who control her have already decided.

### Mr. Astley's View

[[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]]'s silent devotion to Polina is the novel's most understated love story. Unlike Alexei's noisy [[obsession]], Astley's feeling expresses itself in constancy rather than declaration. He asks nothing of Polina, makes no claims on her attention, and—in the novel's most telling detail—is the person she turns to when everything else collapses. Astley sees Polina not as a project or a prize or an asset but as a person in distress, and his perspective, precisely because it is the least narrated, may be the closest to truth.

### The Reader's Dilemma

Because every perspective on Polina reaches the reader through Alexei's narration, the novel creates a unique structural problem: we can never be certain whether the Polina we perceive is Dostoyevsky's character or Alexei's projection. This is not a flaw but a deliberate formal strategy. Polina's opacity is the novel's way of insisting that some people—and perhaps all people—cannot be fully known, only approached, interpreted, and loved in the dark.

## Critical Perspectives

Polina is one of Dostoyevsky's most complex female characters, and one of the few not defined primarily by suffering. Her pride, her cruelty, her intelligence, and her hidden vulnerability make her more than a love object—she is a study in the psychological effects of powerlessness in a world that treats women as currency. Her refusal to explain herself to Alexei or the reader is not a failure of characterisation but its deepest achievement.
