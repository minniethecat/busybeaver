# Passion

Passion in *The Gambler* is never merely romantic — it is the same compulsive energy that drives the roulette [[gambling|play]]er to keep betting after every [[losing|loss]]. Alexei's [[love|passion]] for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] and his [[love|passion]] for gambling are presented as two expressions of the same underlying psychology: the need to risk everything — [[honour|dignity]], money, self — on an uncertain outcome. [004-030] [004-150]

## Definition

Dostoyevsky uses "[[love|passion]]" in the older, stronger sense: not mild affection but a force that overwhelms reason and self-interest. When Alexei speaks "to her of my [[love|passion]]" [004-030], he is describing something closer to illness than to love. Passion, in this world, is the enemy of [[probability|calculation]] — it is what makes a rational person place an irrational bet, whether at the roulette table or in matters of the heart.

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Passion is the novel's unifying psychological force — it explains why characters who are otherwise intelligent (the General), perceptive ([[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]), and self-aware (Alexei) repeatedly act a[[winning|gain]]st their own interests. The gambler and the lover are the same person: someone who knows the [[probability|odds]] but bets anyway, who recognises the futility but persists. [023-022]

Dostoyevsky draws explicit parallels: Alexei's description of his "[[love|passion]] for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]" [020-039] uses the same vocabulary as his descriptions of roulette fever. Both involve the dissolution of self-control, the surrender of reason to impulse, the conviction that this time — this spin, this declaration of love — will be different.

### Key Passages

> …allowed me to speak to her of my passion. [004-030]

The novel's central confession, delivered not as triumph but as permission reluctantly granted. [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] allows Alexei to speak of his [[love|passion]] — she does not return it. The asymmetry defines their entire relationship: his [[love|passion]] is a suit he pleads; hers, if it exists, is a judgment she withholds.

> …observe what a passion for intrigue I was developing. [004-150]

Alexei's self-diagnosis reveals [[love|passion]]'s protean quality — it attaches itself to whatever object is available. If not love, then intrigue; if not intrigue, then roulette. The object matters less than the intensity.

> Even my passion for Polina seemed to have passed away like a dream. [020-039]

The novel's most devastating admission: [[love|passion]], which seemed eternal and defining, evaporates. The [[roulette|roulette wheel]] is [[love|passion]]'s truer object because it demands nothing in return — only more [[love|passion]].

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky treats [[love|passion]] as a form of possession — not the romantic kind, but the demonic. Alexei is *had* by his [[love|passion]] for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], much as he is *had* by the [[obsession|compulsion]] to gamble. The language of the novel repeatedly draws this parallel: the "divine [[love|passion]]" [004-158] that the General feels at the card table is religious only in the sense that it demands total surrender.

This treatment reflects Dostoyevsky's broader philosophical concern with free will. If [[love|passion]] is a force that *happens to* a person rather than a choice they make, then the [[love|passion]]ate lover and the compulsive gambler are equally unfree. The novel's tragedy is not that [[love|passion]] leads to ruin — it is that [[love|passion]], once kindled, leaves no room for anything else.

The autobiographical reading is unavoidable: Dostoyevsky's own [[love|passion]] for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] Suslova (the real-life model for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina Alexandrovna]]) and his compulsive gambling were entwined in exactly the way the novel depicts. *The Gambler* is fiction as exorcism — an attempt to name and thereby master the [[love|passion]]s that ruled its author.
