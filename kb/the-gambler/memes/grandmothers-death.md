# The Grandmother's Death

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s death, reported in the novel's closing passages, arrives as a postscript to the main action — an event long anticipated that, when it finally occurs, changes little and comes too late.

## The Long-Awaited News

Throughout the novel, the characters orbit the expectation of the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s death. The telegrams, the schemes, the mounting debts — all are premised on her eventual demise. When the news finally comes, it is relayed by [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] in the epilogue, a secondary character delivering the resolution to the novel's central anxiety.

## What the Death Brings

[[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] reports that the "mad old woman" died and "left Mlle. [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] a personal fortune" (029-066). The inheritance, so long schemed over, does materialize — but it goes to [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] rather than the General. As for the General himself, "he died" (029-070), his own fate sealed before or shortly after his benefactor's.

## Irony of Timing

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s death is the novel's final irony. Every financial [[probability|calculation]] in the story was built on the assumption that her death would solve everything. But she dies only after her gambling spree in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] has already destroyed much of her fortune, and after the General's position has become irreparable. The death that was supposed to save everyone arrives as an anticlimax.

## The Death as Narrative Device

Dostoyevsky's decision to report the Grandmother's death off-stage, through [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]]'s second-hand account, is a deliberate artistic choice. The novel's most anticipated event — the pivot on which every financial scheme turns — is denied dramatic presence. We do not witness the death, nor the reactions of those most affected by it. Instead, it arrives as a piece of news, already cooled by the passage of time, already processed into the past. This narrative strategy reinforces the novel's deepest thematic pattern: the events that characters obsess over and [[probability|calculate]] toward — the inheritance, the winning streak, the grand reversal — never arrive in the form or at the moment they are expected. Life, like the roulette wheel, obeys no human timetable.

## The Emotional Aftermath

Mr. Astley's report of the deaths — the Grandmother's and the General's — is notable for its emotional flatness. No grief is described, no mourning recorded. The [[general|General]], who spent the entire novel in anxious anticipation of his benefactor's death, dies himself with scarcely a narrative ripple. This emotional vacuum is Dostoyevsky's final comment on the moral condition of his characters: they have invested so much in [[money|financial]] outcomes that human outcomes — death, loss, the end of a life — register only as balance-sheet entries. The Grandmother, who was the novel's largest and most vivid presence, exits the story as a line item in an epilogue, her death mattering only for what it does or does not leave behind.
