# Method

**Method** in *The Gambler* refers to the betting systems and strategies that gamblers employ in their futile attempt to overcome the mathematical advantage of the [[bank]]. Dostoyevsky explores the psychology of methodical gambling — and its inevitable failure — through both the narrator's reflections and the behavior of characters at the [[roulette]] table.

## The Appeal of Method

Alexei acknowledges the gambler's faith in method: "we are glad of, and greatly devoted to, a method of..." [005-114] acquiring money through gambling. This method, he notes, "has a great" [005-116] attraction — the promise that [[chance]] can be tamed by [[calculation]], that the chaos of the wheel can be reduced to a system. The methodical gambler believes he is not gambling at all, but rather executing a rational plan.

## Method vs Chance

The novel systematically undermines every method it describes. Alexei observes gamblers with pencils and paper, tracking coups and calculating probabilities — yet these same gamblers lose alongside the impulsive ones. The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s disastrous session at the tables demonstrates that no method survives contact with the wheel: she stakes with growing desperation, abandoning whatever system she may have intended to follow.

## Method as Self-Deception

Dostoyevsky presents method as a form of psychological self-protection. By framing gambling as a methodical enterprise, the gambler disguises from himself the true nature of his activity — which is not rational investment but passionate [[risk]]-taking driven by [[desire]]. The irony is that the question "Is roulette worse than any other method of acquiring money?" [003-013] is asked rhetorically by a man who will lose everything at the tables. Method is the gambler's favorite [[folly]]: the illusion that reason can master what is fundamentally a surrender to irrational impulse.

## Method and National Character

Dostoyevsky embeds the question of method within a broader cultural contrast between Russian and German temperaments. Alexei poses the question of "Russian ineptitude or the German method of growing rich" (005-125), framing method as a national trait rather than a purely individual choice. The German method — patient accumulation, thrift, systematic effort — stands opposed to the Russian gambler's passionate recklessness. Yet the novel refuses to endorse either pole: the German method may build fortunes, but it produces the sterile, calculating personality of the *Spießbürger*; the Russian passion may destroy, but it contains a vitality that mere method cannot replicate. When Alexei ironically toasts "To the German method of heaping up riches" (005-133), the gesture contains both mockery and a grudging recognition that method — however contemptible — works.

## The Taxonomy of Systems at the Table

Dostoyevsky catalogues the variety of methods visible at the roulette table with ethnographic precision. He enumerates the betting categories — "rouge et noir, pair et impair, manque et passe, with, lastly, the different values in the system of numbers" (015-100) — as though describing the features of a foreign religion. The players are distinguished by their chosen systems: some follow the martingale, doubling after losses; others track patterns, looking for "the system according to which, at given moments, the odds might group themselves" (015-088). The narrator observes that "an example of each system of stakes kept constantly occurring" (015-103), as though the casino were a laboratory in which every possible method could be tested simultaneously — and where every method, in the end, produced the same result.
