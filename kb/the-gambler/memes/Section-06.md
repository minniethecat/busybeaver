# The Gambler · Section 6

[006-001]“How far you may be right in what you have said I do not know,”

[006-002]remarked the General moodily; “but I _do_ know that you are becoming an

[006-003]insufferable _farçeur_ whenever you are given the least chance.”

[006-004]As usual, he left his sentence unfinished. Indeed, whenever he embarked

[006-005]upon anything that in the least exceeded the limits of daily

[006-006]small-talk, he left unfinished what he was saying. The Frenchman had

[006-007]listened to me contemptuously, with a slight protruding of his eyes;

[006-008]but, he could not have understood very much of my harangue. As for

[006-009][[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], she had looked on with serene indifference. She seemed to have

[006-010]heard neither my voice nor any other during the progress of the meal.

[006-011]V

[006-012]Yes, she had been extraordinarily meditative. Yet, on leaving the

[006-013]table, she immediately ordered me to accompany her for a walk. We took

[006-014]the children with us, and set out for the fountain in the Park.

[006-015]I was in such an irritated frame of mind that in rude and abrupt

[006-016]fashion I blurted out a question as to “why our [[marquis-de-griers|Marquis de Griers]] had

[006-017]ceased to accompany her for strolls, or to speak to her for days

[006-018]together.”

[006-019]“Because he is a brute,” she replied in rather a curious way. It was

[006-020]the first time that I had heard her speak so of De Griers:

[006-021]consequently, I was momentarily awed into silence by this expression of

[006-022]resentment.

[006-023]“Have you noticed, too, that today he is by no means on good terms with

[006-024]the General?” I went on.

[006-025]“Yes—and I suppose you want to know why,” she replied with dry

[006-026]captiousness. “You are aware, are you not, that the General is

[006-027]mortgaged to the Marquis, with all his property? Consequently, if the

[006-028]General’s mother does not die, the Frenchman will become the absolute

[006-029]possessor of everything which he now holds only in pledge.”

[006-030]“Then it is really the case that everything is mortgaged? I have heard

[006-031]rumours to that effect, but was unaware how far they might be true.”

[006-032]“Yes, they _are_ true. What then?”

[006-033]“Why, it will be a case of ‘Farewell, [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]],’” I remarked; “for

[006-034]in such an event she would never become Madame General. Do you know, I

[006-035]believe the old man is so much in love with her that he will shoot

[006-036]himself if she should throw him over. At his age it is a dangerous

[006-037]thing to fall in love.”

[006-038]“Yes, something, I believe, _will_ happen to him,” assented Polina

[006-039]thoughtfully.

[006-040]“And what a fine thing it all is!” I continued. “Could anything be more

[006-041]abominable than the way in which she has agreed to marry for money

[006-042]alone? Not one of the decencies has been observed; the whole affair has

[006-043]taken place without the least ceremony. And as for the grandmother,

[006-044]what could be more comical, yet more dastardly, than the sending of

[006-045]telegram after telegram to know if she is dead? What do you think of

[006-046]it, Polina Alexandrovna?”

[006-047]“Yes, it is very horrible,” she interrupted with a shudder.

[006-048]“Consequently, I am the more surprised that _you_ should be so

[006-049]cheerful. What are _you_ so pleased about? [[About]] the fact that you have

[006-050]gone and lost my money?”

[006-051]“What? The money that you gave me to lose? I told you I should never

[006-052]win for other people—least of all for you. I obeyed you simply because

[006-053]you ordered me to; but you must not blame me for the result. I warned

[006-054]you that no good would ever come of it. You seem much depressed at

[006-055]having lost your money. Why do you need it so greatly?”

[006-056]“Why do _you_ ask me these questions?”

[006-057]“Because you promised to explain matters to me. Listen. I am certain

[006-058]that, as soon as ever I ‘begin to [[gambling|play]] for myself’ (and I still have

[006-059]120 gülden left), I shall win. You can then take of me what you

[006-060]require.”

[006-061]She made a contemptuous grimace.

[006-062]“You must not be angry with me,” I continued, “for making such a

[006-063]proposal. I am so conscious of being only a nonentity in your eyes that

[006-064]you need not mind accepting money from me. A gift from me could not

[006-065]possibly offend you. Moreover, it was I who lost your gülden.”

[006-066]She glanced at me, but, seeing that I was in an irritable, sarcastic

[006-067]mood, changed the subject.

[006-068]“My affairs cannot possibly interest you,” she said. “Still, if you

[006-069]_do_ wish to know, I am in debt. I borrowed some money, and must pay it

[006-070]back again. I have a curious, senseless idea that I am bound to win at

[006-071]the gaming-tables. Why I think so I cannot tell, but I do think so, and

[006-072]with some assurance. Perhaps it is because of that assurance that I now

[006-073]find myself without any other resource.”

[006-074]“Or perhaps it is because it is so _necessary_ for you to win. It is

[006-075]like a drowning man catching at a straw. You yourself will agree that,

[006-076]unless he were drowning he would not mistake a straw for the trunk of a

[006-077]tree.”

[006-078]Polina looked surprised.

[006-079]“What?” she said. “Do not you also hope something from it? Did you not

[006-080]tell me again and again, two weeks ago, that you were certain of

[006-081]winning at roulette if you played here? And did you not ask me not to

[006-082]consider you a fool for doing so? Were you joking? You cannot have

[006-083]been, for I remember that you spoke with a gravity which forbade the

[006-084]idea of your jesting.”

[006-085]“True,” I replied gloomily. “I always felt certain that I should win.

[006-086]Indeed, what you say makes me ask myself—Why have my absurd, senseless

[006-087]losses of today raised a doubt in my mind? Yet I am _still_ positive

[006-088]that, so soon as ever I begin to play for myself, I shall infallibly

[006-089]win.”

[006-090]“And why are you so certain?”

[006-091]“To tell the truth, I do not know. I only know that I _must_ win—that

[006-092]it is the one resource I have left. Yes, why do I feel so assured on

[006-093]the point?”

[006-094]“Perhaps because one cannot help winning if one is fanatically certain

[006-095]of doing so.”

[006-096]“Yet I dare [[bet|wager]] that you do not think me capable of serious feeling

[006-097]in the matter?”

[006-098]“I do not care whether you are so or not,” answered Polina with calm

[006-099]indifference. “Well, since you ask me, I _do_ doubt your ability to

[006-100]take anything seriously. You are capable of worrying, but not deeply.

[006-101]You are too ill-regulated and unsettled a person for that. But why do

[006-102]you want money? Not a single one of the reasons which you have given

[006-103]can be looked upon as serious.”

[006-104]“By the way,” I interrupted, “you say you want to pay off a debt. It

[006-105]must be a large one. Is it to the Frenchman?”

[006-106]“What do you mean by asking all these questions? You are very clever

[006-107]today. Surely you are not drunk?”

[006-108]“You know that you and I stand on no ceremony, and that sometimes I put

[006-109]to you very plain questions. I repeat that I am your slave—and slaves

[006-110]cannot be shamed or offended.”

[006-111]“You talk like a child. It is always possible to comport oneself with

[006-112][[honour|dignity]]. If one has a quarrel it ought to elevate rather than to

[006-113]degrade one.”

[006-114]“A maxim straight from the copybook! Suppose I _cannot_ comport myself

[006-115]with dignity. By that I mean that, though I am a man of self-respect, I

[006-116]am unable to carry off a situation properly. Do you know the reason? It

[006-117]is because we Russians are too richly and multifariously gifted to be

[006-118]able at once to find the proper mode of expression. It is all a

[006-119]question of mode. Most of us are so bounteously endowed with intellect

[006-120]as to require also a spice of genius to choose the right form of

[006-121]behaviour. And genius is lacking in us for the reason that so little

[006-122]genius at all exists. It belongs only to the French—though a few other

[006-123]Europeans have elaborated their forms so well as to be able to figure

[006-124]with extreme dignity, and yet be wholly undignified persons. That is

[006-125]why, with us, the mode is so all-important. The Frenchman may receive

[006-126]an insult—a real, a venomous insult: yet, he will not so much as frown.

[006-127]But a tweaking of the nose he cannot bear, for the reason that such an

[006-128]act is an infringement of the accepted, of the time-hallowed order of

[006-129]decorum. That is why our good ladies are so fond of Frenchmen—the

[006-130]Frenchman’s manners, they say, are perfect! But in my opinion there is

[006-131]no such thing as a Frenchman’s manners. The Frenchman is only a

[006-132]bird—the _coq gaulois_. At the same time, as I am not a woman, I do not

[006-133]properly understand the question. Cocks may be excellent birds. If I am

[006-134]wrong you must stop me. You ought to stop and correct me more often

[006-135]when I am speaking to you, for I am too apt to say everything that is

[006-136]in my head.

[006-137]“You see, I have lost my manners. I agree that I have none, nor yet any

[006-138]dignity. I will tell you why. I set no store upon such things.

[006-139]Everything in me has undergone a cheek. You know the reason. I have not

[006-140]a single human thought in my head. For a long while I have been

[006-141]ignorant of what is going on in the world—here or in [[russia|Russia]]. I have

[006-142]been to Dresden, yet am completely in the dark as to what Dresden is

[006-143]like. You know the cause of my obsession. I have no hope now, and am a

[006-144]mere cipher in your eyes; wherefore, I tell you outright that wherever

[006-145]I go I see only you—all the rest is a matter of indifference.

[006-146]“Why or how I have come to love you I do not know. It may be that you

[006-147]are not altogether fair to look upon. Do you know, I am ignorant even

[006-148]as to what your face is like. In all [[chance|probability]], too, your heart is

[006-149]not comely, and it is possible that your mind is wholly ignoble.”

[006-150]“And because you do not believe in my nobility of soul you think to

[006-151]purchase me with money?” she said.

[006-152]“_When_ have I thought to do so?” was my reply.

[006-153]“You are [[loss|losing]] the thread of the argument. If you do not wish to

[006-154]purchase me, at all events you wish to purchase my respect.”

[006-155]“Not at all. I have told you that I find it difficult to explain

[006-156]myself. You are hard upon me. Do not be angry at my chattering. You

[006-157]know why you ought not to be angry with me—that I am simply an

[006-158]imbecile. However, I do not mind if you _are_ angry. Sitting in my

[006-159]room, I need but to think of you, to imagine to myself the rustle of

[006-160]your dress, and at once I fall almost to biting my hands. Why should

[006-161]you be angry with me? Because I call myself your slave? Revel, I pray

[006-162]you, in my slavery—revel in it. Do you know that sometimes I could kill

[006-163]you?—not because I do not love you, or am jealous of you, but, because

[006-164]I feel as though I could simply devour you... You are laughing!”

[006-165]“No, I am not,” she retorted. “But I order you, nevertheless, to be

[006-166]silent.”

[006-167]She stopped, well nigh breathless with anger. God knows, she may not

[006-168]have been a beautiful woman, yet I loved to see her come to a halt like

[006-169]this, and was therefore, the more fond of arousing her temper. Perhaps

[006-170]she divined this, and for that very reason gave way to rage. I said as

[006-171]much to her.

[006-172]“What rubbish!” she cried with a shudder.

[006-173]“I do not care,” I continued. “Also, do you know that it is not safe

[006-174]for us to take walks together? Often I have a feeling that I should

[006-175]like to strike you, to disfigure you, to strangle you. Are you certain

[006-176]that it will never come to that? You are driving me to frenzy. Am I

[006-177]afraid of a scandal, or of your anger? Why should I fear your anger? I

[006-178]love without hope, and know that hereafter I shall love you a thousand

[006-179]times more. If ever I should kill you I should have to kill myself too.

[006-180]But I shall put off doing so as long as possible, for I wish to

[006-181]continue enjoying the unbearable pain which your coldness gives me. Do

[006-182]you know a very strange thing? It is that, with every day, my love for

[006-183]you increases—though that would seem to be almost an impossibility. Why

[006-184]should I not become a fatalist? Remember how, on the third day that we

[006-185]ascended the Shlangenberg, I was moved to whisper in your ear: ‘Say but

[006-186]the word, and I will leap into the abyss.’ Had you said it, I should

[006-187]have leapt. Do you not believe me?”
