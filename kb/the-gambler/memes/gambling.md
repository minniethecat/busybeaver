# Gambling

[[gambling]] in Dostoyevsky's *The Gambler* is not merely a pastime but a consuming [[love|passion]] that drives the novel's plot and defines its central characters. From Alexei Ivanovich's initial declaration "I have no [[money]] for gambling" (001-060) to his ultimate enslavement to [[roulette|the wheel]], gambling operates as both literal action and psychological metaphor.

## Definition

In the context of the novel, gambling encompasses roulette and trente et quarante—the two card/casino games played in the fictional [[spa|spa town]] of [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] (003-065). But gambling extends beyond the casino floor to encompass the speculative financial behaviour of [[general|the General]]'s household, the emotional [[bet|wager]]s characters place on one another, and the existential gamble of love, [[honour]], and social position.

Dostoyevsky draws a specifically [[russia|Russia]]n-national character to gambling: "a great many [[russia|Russia]]ns go in for gambling, they are no good at [[roulette|the game]]" (005-102)—implying a national predisposition toward [[love|passion]]ate, irrational [[risk|speculation]].

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Gambling structures the novel's rhythm: the extended casino scenes provide moments of heightened intensity between the drawing-room dramas of [[hotel|the hotel]]. The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s catastrophic gambling spree (015-106) serves as the novel's pivot—a [[fortune|wealth]]y matriarch who "once she has acquired the taste for gambling, she will never leave [[casino|the tables]]" (017-099), her self-destruction destroying the General's schemes in the process.

Alexei's gambling follows a progression from reluctant participant to compulsive devotee. His admission that he would "damn the gambling" (029-017) if he could find other employment reveals gambling not as a choice but as the only available expression of his thwarted ambition.

### Key Passages

> "I have no money for gambling" (001-060) — Alexei's early resistance, soon abandoned.

> "A great many Russians go in for gambling, they are no good at the game" (005-102) — national stereotyping as character analysis.

> "Once she has acquired the taste for gambling, she will never leave the tables" (017-099) — the Grandmother's irreversible descent.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky wrote *The Gambler* under extraordinary circumstances: deeply in debt from his own roulette addiction, he dictated the novel to a stenographer (Anna Snitkina, whom he later married) in 26 days to meet a contractual deadline that would have forfeited his literary rights. This biographical context infuses the novel with first-hand psychological accuracy.

The novel treats gambling not as a moral failing but as a form of existential self-assertion. Alexei's [[winning]] streaks produce ecstatic states that transcend mere greed—they are moments of self-realization in a world that otherwise denies him agency. Yet [[zero-zero|the zero]]'s inevitable reassertion (015-138) guarantees the crash that follows every high.
