# The Gambler · Section 25

[025-001]“Well? Are you going to hand me over my fifty thousand francs?”

[025-002]“[[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], you say that _again, again?_” I exclaimed.

[025-003]“You have changed your mind, then? Ha, ha, ha! You are sorry you ever

[025-004]promised them?”

[025-005]On the table where, the previous night, I had counted the money there

[025-006]still was lying the packet of twenty five thousand florins. I handed it

[025-007]to her.

[025-008]“The francs are mine, then, are they? They are mine?” she inquired

[025-009]viciously as she balanced the money in her hands.

[025-010]“Yes; they have _always_ been yours,” I said.

[025-011]“Then _take_ your fifty thousand francs!” and she hurled them full in

[025-012]my face. The packet burst as she did so, and the floor became strewed

[025-013]with bank-notes. The instant that the deed was done she rushed from the

[025-014]room.

[025-015]At that moment she cannot have been in her right mind; yet, what was

[025-016]the cause of her temporary aberration I cannot say. For a month past

[025-017]she had been unwell. Yet what had brought about this _present_

[025-018]condition of mind, above all things, this outburst? Had it come of

[025-019][[pride|wounded pride]]? Had it come of despair over her decision to come to me?

[025-020]Had it come of the fact that, presuming too much on my good fortune, I

[025-021]had seemed to be intending to desert her (even as De Griers had done)

[025-022]when once I had given her the fifty thousand francs? But, on my honour,

[025-023]I had never cherished any such intention. What was at fault, I think,

[025-024]was her own pride, which kept urging her not to trust me, but, rather,

[025-025]to insult me—even though she had not realised the fact. In her eyes I

[025-026]corresponded to De Griers, and therefore had been condemned for a fault

[025-027]not wholly my own. Her mood of late had been a sort of delirium, a sort

[025-028]of light-headedness—that I knew full well; yet, never had I

[025-029]sufficiently taken it into consideration. Perhaps she would not pardon

[025-030]me now? Ah, but this was _the present_. What about the future? Her

[025-031]delirium and sickness were not likely to make her forget what she had

[025-032]done in bringing me De Griers’ letter. No, she must have known what she

[025-033]was doing when she brought it.

[025-034]Somehow I contrived to stuff the pile of notes and gold under the bed,

[025-035]to cover them over, and then to leave the room some ten minutes after

[025-036]Polina. I felt sure that she had returned to her own room; wherefore, I

[025-037]intended quietly to follow her, and to ask the nursemaid aid who opened

[025-038]the door how her mistress was. Judge, therefore, of my surprise when,

[025-039]meeting the domestic on the stairs, she informed me that Polina had not

[025-040]yet returned, and that she (the domestic) was at that moment on her way

[025-041]to my room in quest of her!

[025-042]“Mlle. left me but ten minutes ago,” I said. “What can have become of

[025-043]her?” The nursemaid looked at me reproachfully.

[025-044]Already sundry rumours were flying about the hotel. Both in the office

[025-045]of the commissionaire and in that of the landlord it was whispered

[025-046]that, at seven o’clock that morning, the Fräulein had left the hotel,

[025-047]and set off, despite the rain, in the direction of the Hôtel

[025-048]d’Angleterre. From words and hints let fall I could see that the fact

[025-049]of Polina having spent the night in my room was now public property.

[025-050]Also, sundry rumours were circulating concerning the General’s family

[025-051]affairs. It was known that last night he had gone out of his mind, and

[025-052]paraded the hotel in tears; also, that [[grandmother|the old lady]] who had arrived was

[025-053]his mother, and that she had come from [[russia|Russia]] on purpose to forbid her

[025-054]son’s marriage with Mlle. de Cominges, as well as to cut him out of her

[025-055]will if he should disobey her; also that, because he had disobeyed her,

[025-056]she had squandered all her money at roulette, in order to have nothing

[025-057]more to leave to him. “Oh, these Russians!” exclaimed the landlord,

[025-058]with an angry toss of the head, while the bystanders laughed and the

[025-059]clerk betook himself to his accounts. Also, every one had learnt about

[025-060]my [[winning|winnings]]; Karl, the corridor lacquey, was the first to congratulate

[025-061]me. But with these folk I had nothing to do. My business was to set off

[025-062]at full speed to the Hôtel d’Angleterre.

[025-063]As yet it was early for [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] to receive visitors; but, as soon as

[025-064]he learnt that it was _I_ who had arrived, he came out into the

[025-065]corridor to meet me, and stood looking at me in silence with his

[025-066]steel-grey eyes as he waited to hear what I had to say. I inquired

[025-067]after Polina.

[025-068]“She is ill,” he replied, still looking at me with his direct,

[025-069]unwavering glance.

[025-070]“And she is in your rooms.”

[025-071]“Yes, she is in my rooms.”

[025-072]“Then you are minded to keep her there?”

[025-073]“Yes, I am minded to keep her there.”

[025-074]“But, Mr. Astley, that will raise a scandal. It ought not to be

[025-075]allowed. Besides, she is very ill. Perhaps you had not remarked that?”

[025-076]“Yes, I have. It was I who told you about it. Had she not been ill, she

[025-077]would not have gone and spent the night with you.”

[025-078]“Then you know all about it?”

[025-079]“Yes; for last night she was to have accompanied me to the house of a

[025-080]relative of mine. Unfortunately, being ill, she made a mistake, and

[025-081]went to your rooms instead.”

[025-082]“Indeed? Then I wish you joy, Mr. Astley. Apropos, you have reminded me

[025-083]of something. Were you beneath my window last night? Every moment Mlle.

[025-084]Polina kept telling me to open the window and see if you were there;

[025-085]after which she always smiled.”

[025-086]“Indeed? No, I was not there; but I was waiting in the corridor, and

[025-087]walking about the hotel.”

[025-088]“She ought to see a doctor, you know, Mr. Astley.”

[025-089]“Yes, she ought. I have sent for one, and, if she dies, I shall hold

[025-090]you responsible.”

[025-091]This surprised me.

[025-092]“Pardon me,” I replied, “but what do you mean?”

[025-093]“Never mind. Tell me if it is true that, last night, you won two

[025-094]hundred thousand thalers?”

[025-095]“No; I won a hundred thousand florins.”

[025-096]“Good heavens! Then I suppose you will be off to [[paris|Paris]] this morning?”

[025-097]“Why?”

[025-098]“Because all Russians who have grown rich go to Paris,” explained

[025-099]Astley, as though he had read the fact in a book.

[025-100]“But what could I do in Paris in summer time?—I _love_ her, Mr. Astley!

[025-101]Surely you know that?”

[025-102]“Indeed? I am sure that you do _not_. Moreover, if you were to stay

[025-103]here, you would lose everything that you possess, and have nothing left

[025-104]with which to pay your expenses in Paris. Well, good-bye now. I feel

[025-105]sure that today will see you gone from here.”

[025-106]“Good-bye. But I am _not_ going to Paris. Likewise—pardon me—what is to

[025-107]become of this family? I mean that the affair of the General and Mlle.

[025-108]Polina will soon be all over the town.”

[025-109]“I daresay; yet, I hardly suppose that that will break the General’s

[025-110]heart. Moreover, Mlle. Polina has a perfect right to live where she

[025-111]chooses. In short, we may say that, as a family, this family has ceased

[025-112]to exist.”

[025-113]I departed, and found myself smiling at the Englishman’s strange

[025-114]assurance that I should soon be leaving for Paris. “I suppose he means

[025-115]to shoot me in a duel, should Polina die. Yes, that is what he intends

[025-116]to do.” Now, although I was honestly sorry for Polina, it is a fact

[025-117]that, from the moment when, the previous night, I had approached the

[025-118][[gambling|gaming]]-table, and begun to rake in the packets of bank-notes, my love

[025-119]for her had entered upon a new plane. Yes, I can say that now;

[025-120]although, at the time, I was barely conscious of it. Was I, then, at

[025-121]heart a gambler? Did I, after all, love Polina not so _very_ much? No,

[025-122]no! As God is my witness, I loved her! Even when I was returning home

[025-123]from Mr. Astley’s my suffering was genuine, and my self-reproach

[025-124]sincere. But presently I was to go through an exceedingly strange and

[025-125]ugly experience.

[025-126]I was proceeding to the General’s rooms when I heard a door near me

[025-127]open, and a voice call me by name. It was Mlle.’s mother, the Widow de

[025-128]Cominges who was inviting me, in her daughter’s name, to enter.

[025-129]I did so; whereupon, I heard a laugh and a little cry proceed from the

[025-130]bedroom (the pair occupied a suite of two apartments), where Mlle.

[025-131]Blanche was just arising.

[025-132]“Ah, c’est lui! Viens, donc, bête! Is it true that you have won a

[025-133]mountain of gold and silver? J’aimerais mieux l’or.”

[025-134]“Yes,” I replied with a smile.

[025-135]“How much?”

[025-136]“A hundred thousand florins.”

[025-137]“Bibi, comme tu es bête! Come in here, for I can’t hear you where you

[025-138]are now. Nous ferons bombance, n’est-ce pas?”

[025-139]Entering her room, I found her lolling under a pink satin coverlet, and

[025-140]revealing a pair of swarthy, wonderfully healthy shoulders—shoulders

[025-141]such as one sees in dreams—shoulders covered over with a white cambric

[025-142]nightgown which, trimmed with lace, stood out, in striking relief,

[025-143]against the darkness of her skin.

[025-144]“Mon fils, as-tu du cœur?” she cried when she saw me, and then giggled.

[025-145]Her laugh had always been a very cheerful one, and at times it even

[025-146]sounded sincere.

[025-147]“Tout autre—” I began, paraphrasing Corneille.

[025-148]“See here,” she prattled on. “Please search for my stockings, and help

[025-149]me to dress. Aussi, si tu n’es pas trop bête je te prends à Paris. I am

[025-150]just off, let me tell you.”

[025-151]“This moment?”

[025-152]“In half an hour.”

[025-153]True enough, everything stood ready-packed—trunks, portmanteaux, and

[025-154]all. Coffee had long been served.

[025-155]“Eh bien, tu verras Paris. Dis donc, qu’est-ce que c’est qu’un

[025-156]‘utchitel’? Tu étais bien bête quand tu étais ‘utchitel.’ Where are my

[025-157]stockings? Please help me to dress.”

[025-158]And she lifted up a really ravishing foot—small, swarthy, and not

[025-159]misshapen like the majority of feet which look dainty only in bottines.

[025-160]I laughed, and started to draw on to the foot a silk stocking, while

[025-161][[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] sat on the edge of the bed and chattered.

[025-162]“Eh bien, que feras-tu si je te prends avec moi? First of all I must

[025-163]have fifty thousand francs, and you shall give them to me at Frankfurt.

[025-164]Then we will go on to Paris, where we will live together, et je te

[025-165]ferai voir des étoiles en plein jour. Yes, you shall see such women as

[025-166]your eyes have never lit upon.”

[025-167]“Stop a moment. If I were to give you those fifty thousand francs, what

[025-168]should I have left for myself?”

[025-169]“Another hundred thousand francs, please to remember. Besides, I could

[025-170]live with you in your rooms for a month, or even for two; or even for

[025-171]longer. But it would not take us more than two months to get through

[025-172]fifty thousand francs; for, look you, je suis bonne enfante, et tu

[025-173]verras des étoiles, you may be sure.”

[025-174]“What? You mean to say that we should spend the whole in two months?”

[025-175]“Certainly. Does that surprise you very much? Ah, vil esclave! Why, one

[025-176]month of that life would be better than all your previous existence.

[025-177]One month—et après, le déluge! Mais tu ne peux comprendre. Va! Away,

[025-178]away! You are not worth it.—Ah, que fais-tu?”

[025-179]For, while drawing on the other stocking, I had felt constrained to

[025-180]kiss her. Immediately she shrunk back, kicked me in the face with her

[025-181]toes, and turned me neck and crop out of the room.

[025-182]“Eh bien, mon ‘utchitel’,” she called after me, “je t’attends, si tu

[025-183]veux. I start in a quarter of an hour’s time.”

[025-184]I returned to my own room with my head in a whirl. It was not my fault

[025-185]that Polina had thrown a packet in my face, and preferred Mr. Astley to

[025-186]myself. A few bank-notes were still fluttering about the floor, and I

[025-187]picked them up. At that moment the door opened, and the landlord

[025-188]appeared—a person who, until now, had never bestowed upon me so much as

[025-189]a glance. He had come to know if I would prefer to move to a lower

[025-190]floor—to a suite which had just been tenanted by Count V.
