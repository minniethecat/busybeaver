# Nadia

**Nadia** (diminutive Nadienka) is the youngest child of [[general|the General]], appearing only briefly in *The Gambler* as a peripheral figure in the General's disordered household. Despite her minimal presence in the narrative, her existence — together with her brother [[mischa|Mischa]] — serves an important structural function: the children represent the domestic consequences of the adults' gambling obsession.

## Role in the Novel

Nadia appears in only three scenes. In the first, Alexei takes her and Mischa for a walk, acting in his capacity as tutor to the General's children (001-046). Later, [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] "seated herself upon a bench near the fountain, and sent Nadia away to a little distance to play with some other children" (002-032) — a moment that underscores Polina's need for solitude and Alexei's ambiguous role between servant and confidant. Finally, before entering the Casino, Polina "called Nadia back to her side" (002-128), reclaiming the child as a social shield.

## Significance

Nadia and Mischa are the invisible stakes of the General's gambling. The General's debts, his scheme to marry Polina to [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]], and his desperate wait for the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s death are all nominally motivated by concern for "the children's future." Yet Nadia herself is almost entirely absent from the household's emotional life — cared for by servants, briefly walked by the tutor, sent away when adults need privacy.

This neglect mirrors the novel's broader critique: the gambling obsession that grips every adult character has real victims, and they are the ones least able to protest. The children appear only as afterthoughts because, in the economy of [[desire]] and [[debt]] that structures the novel, they have no power and no voice.
