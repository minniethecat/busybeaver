# The Gambler · Section 5

[005-001]“That [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]],” he said. “Well, I have seen a good many women

[005-002]like her.”

[005-003]After that he was silent as he looked me meaningly in the face. What he

[005-004]meant I did not know, but to my glance of inquiry he returned only a

[005-005]dry nod, and a reiterated “It is so.” Presently, however, he resumed:

[005-006]“Does Mlle. [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] like flowers?”

[005-007]“I really cannot say,” was my reply.

[005-008]“What? You cannot say?” he cried in great astonishment.

[005-009]“No; I have never noticed whether she does so or not,” I repeated with

[005-010]a smile.

[005-011]“Hm! Then I have an idea in my mind,” he concluded. Lastly, with a nod,

[005-012]he walked away with a pleased expression on his face. The conversation

[005-013]had been carried on in execrable French.

[005-014]IV

[005-015]Today has been a day of folly, stupidity, and ineptness. The time is

[005-016]now eleven o’clock in the evening, and I am sitting in my room and

[005-017]thinking. It all began, this morning, with my being forced to go and

[005-018][[gambling|play]] roulette for Polina Alexandrovna. When she handed me over her

[005-019]store of six hundred gülden I exacted two conditions—namely, that I

[005-020]should not go halves with her in her [[winning|winnings]], if any (that is to say,

[005-021]I should not take anything for myself), and that she should explain to

[005-022]me, that same evening, why it was so necessary for her to win, and how

[005-023]much was the sum which she needed. For, I could not suppose that she

[005-024]was doing all this merely for the sake of money. Yet clearly she _did_

[005-025]need some money, and that as soon as possible, and for a special

[005-026]purpose. Well, she promised to explain matters, and I departed. There

[005-027]was a tremendous crowd in the gaming-rooms. What an arrogant, greedy

[005-028]crowd it was! I pressed forward towards the middle of the room until I

[005-029]had secured a seat at a croupier’s elbow. Then I began to play in timid

[005-030]fashion, venturing only twenty or thirty gülden at a time. Meanwhile, I

[005-031]observed and took notes. It seemed to me that [[probability|calculation]] was

[005-032]superfluous, and by no means possessed of the importance which certain

[005-033]other players attached to it, even though they sat with ruled papers in

[005-034]their hands, whereon they set down the coups, calculated the chances,

[005-035]reckoned, staked, and—lost exactly as we more simple mortals did who

[005-036]played without any reckoning at all.

[005-037]However, I deduced from the scene one conclusion which seemed to me

[005-038]reliable—namely, that in the flow of fortuitous chances there is, if

[005-039]not a system, at all events a sort of order. This, of course, is a very

[005-040]strange thing. For instance, after a dozen middle figures there would

[005-041]always occur a dozen or so outer ones. Suppose the ball stopped twice

[005-042]at a dozen outer figures; it would then pass to a dozen of the first

[005-043]ones, and then, again, to a dozen of the middle ciphers, and fall upon

[005-044]them three or four times, and then revert to a dozen outers; whence,

[005-045]after another couple of rounds, the ball would again pass to the first

[005-046]figures, strike upon them once, and then return thrice to the middle

[005-047]series—continuing thus for an hour and a half, or two hours. One,

[005-048]three, two: one, three, two. It was all very curious. Again, for the

[005-049]whole of a day or a morning the red would alternate with the black, but

[005-050]almost without any order, and from moment to moment, so that scarcely

[005-051]two consecutive rounds would end upon either the one or the other. Yet,

[005-052]next day, or, perhaps, the next evening, the red alone would turn up,

[005-053]and attain a run of over two score, and continue so for quite a length

[005-054]of time—say, for a whole day. Of these circumstances the majority were

[005-055]pointed out to me by [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], who stood by the gaming-table the

[005-056]whole morning, yet never once staked in person.

[005-057]For myself, I lost all that I had on me, and with great speed. To begin

[005-058]with, I staked two hundred gülden on “even,” and won. Then I staked the

[005-059]same amount again, and won: and so on some two or three times. At one

[005-060]moment I must have had in my hands—gathered there within a space of

[005-061]five minutes—about 4000 gülden. That, of course, was the proper moment

[005-062]for me to have departed, but there arose in me a strange sensation as

[005-063]of a challenge to [[fate|Fate]]—as of a wish to deal her a blow on the cheek,

[005-064]and to put out my tongue at her. Accordingly I set down the largest

[005-065][[bet|stake]] allowed by the rules—namely, 4000 gülden—and lost. Fired by this

[005-066]mishap, I pulled out all the money left to me, staked it all on the

[005-067]same venture, and—again lost! Then I rose from the table, feeling as

[005-068]though I were stupefied. What had happened to me I did not know; but,

[005-069]before luncheon I told Polina of my losses—until which time I walked

[005-070]about the Park.

[005-071]At luncheon I was as excited as I had been at the meal three days ago.

[005-072]Mlle. Blanche and [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]] were lunching with us, and it appeared

[005-073]that the former had been to the Casino that morning, and had seen my

[005-074]exploits there. So now she showed me more attention when talking to me;

[005-075]while, for his part, the Frenchman approached me, and asked outright if

[005-076]it had been my own money that I had lost. He appeared to be suspicious

[005-077]as to something being on foot between Polina and myself, but I merely

[005-078]fired up, and replied that the money had been all my own.

[005-079]At this the General seemed extremely surprised, and asked me whence I

[005-080]had procured it; whereupon I replied that, though I had begun only with

[005-081]100 gülden, six or seven rounds had increased my capital to 5000 or

[005-082]6000 gülden, and that subsequently I had lost the whole in two rounds.

[005-083]All this, of course, was plausible enough. During my recital I glanced

[005-084]at Polina, but nothing was to be discerned on her face. However, she

[005-085]had allowed me to fire up without correcting me, and from that I

[005-086]concluded that it was my _cue_ to fire up, and to conceal the fact that

[005-087]I had been playing on her behalf. “At all events,” I thought to myself,

[005-088]“she, in her turn, has promised to give me an explanation to-night, and

[005-089]to reveal to me something or another.”

[005-090]Although the General appeared to be taking stock of me, he said

[005-091]nothing. Yet I could see uneasiness and annoyance in his face. Perhaps

[005-092]his straitened circumstances made it hard for him to have to hear of

[005-093]piles of gold passing through the hands of an irresponsible fool like

[005-094]myself within the space of a quarter of an hour. Now, I have an idea

[005-095]that, last night, he and the Frenchman had a sharp encounter with one

[005-096]another. At all events they closeted themselves together, and then had

[005-097]a long and vehement discussion; after which the Frenchman departed in

[005-098]what appeared to be a [[love|passion]], but returned, early this morning, to

[005-099]renew the combat. On hearing of my losses, however, he only remarked

[005-100]with a sharp, and even a malicious, air that “a man ought to go more

[005-101]carefully.” Next, for some reason or another, he added that, “though a

[005-102]great many Russians go in for gambling, they are no good at [[roulette|the game]].”

[005-103]“_I_ think that roulette was devised specially for Russians,” I

[005-104]retorted; and when the Frenchman smiled contemptuously at my reply I

[005-105]further remarked that I was sure I was right; also that, speaking of

[005-106]Russians in the capacity of gamblers, I had far more blame for them

[005-107]than praise—of that he could be quite sure.

[005-108]“Upon what do you base your opinion?” he inquired.

[005-109]“Upon the fact that to the virtues and merits of the civilised

[005-110]Westerner there has become historically added—though this is not his

[005-111]chief point—a capacity for acquiring capital; whereas, not only is the

[005-112]Russian incapable of acquiring capital, but also he exhausts it

[005-113]wantonly and of sheer folly. None the less we Russians often need

[005-114]money; wherefore, we are glad of, and greatly devoted to, a method of

[005-115]acquisition like roulette—whereby, in a couple of hours, one may grow

[005-116]rich without doing any work. This method, I repeat, has a great

[005-117]attraction for us, but since we play in wanton fashion, and without

[005-118]taking any trouble, we almost invariably lose.”

[005-119]“To a certain extent that is true,” assented the Frenchman with a

[005-120]self-satisfied air.

[005-121]“Oh no, it is not true,” put in the General sternly. “And you,” he

[005-122]added to me, “you ought to be ashamed of yourself for traducing your

[005-123]own country!”

[005-124]“I beg pardon,” I said. “Yet it would be difficult to say which is the

[005-125]worst of the two—Russian ineptitude or the German method of growing

[005-126]rich through honest toil.”

[005-127]“What an extraordinary idea,” cried the General.

[005-128]“And what a _Russian_ idea!” added the Frenchman.

[005-129]I smiled, for I was rather glad to have a quarrel with them.

[005-130]“I would rather live a wandering life in tents,” I cried, “than bow the

[005-131]knee to a German idol!”

[005-132]“To _what_ idol?” exclaimed the General, now seriously angry.

[005-133]“To the German method of heaping up riches. I have not been here very

[005-134]long, but I can tell you that what I have seen and verified makes my

[005-135]Tartar blood boil. Good Lord! I wish for no virtues of that kind.

[005-136]Yesterday I went for a walk of about ten versts; and, everywhere I

[005-137]found that things were even as we read of them in good German

[005-138]picture-books—that every house has its ‘Vater,’ who is horribly

[005-139]beneficent and extraordinarily honourable. So honourable is he that it

[005-140]is dreadful to have anything to do with him; and I cannot bear people

[005-141]of that sort. Each such ‘Vater’ has his family, and in the evenings

[005-142]they read improving books aloud. Over their roof-trees there murmur

[005-143]elms and chestnuts; the sun has sunk to his rest; a stork is roosting

[005-144]on the gable; and all is beautifully poetic and touching. Do not be

[005-145]angry, General. Let me tell you something that is even more touching

[005-146]than that. I can remember how, of an evening, my own father, now dead,

[005-147]used to sit under the lime trees in his little garden, and to read

[005-148]books aloud to myself and my mother. Yes, I know how things ought to be

[005-149]done. Yet every German family is bound to slavery and to [[slavery|submission]] to

[005-150]its ‘Vater.’ They work like oxen, and amass [[fortune|wealth]] like Jews. Suppose

[005-151]the ‘Vater’ has put by a certain number of gülden which he hands over

[005-152]to his eldest son, in order that the said son may acquire a trade or a

[005-153]small plot of land. Well, one result is to deprive the daughter of a

[005-154]dowry, and so leave her among the unwedded. For the same reason, the

[005-155]parents will have to sell the younger son into bondage or the ranks of

[005-156]the army, in order that he may earn more towards the family capital.

[005-157]Yes, such things ARE done, for I have been making inquiries on the

[005-158]subject. It is all done out of sheer rectitude—out of a rectitude which

[005-159]is magnified to the point of the younger son believing that he has been

[005-160]_rightly_ sold, and that it is simply idyllic for the victim to rejoice

[005-161]when he is made over into pledge. What more have I to tell? Well,

[005-162]this—that matters bear just as hardly upon the eldest son. Perhaps he

[005-163]has his Gretchen to whom his heart is bound; but he cannot marry her,

[005-164]for the reason that he has not yet amassed sufficient gülden. So, the

[005-165]pair wait on in a mood of sincere and virtuous expectation, and

[005-166]smilingly deposit themselves in pawn the while. Gretchen’s cheeks grow

[005-167]sunken, and she begins to wither; until at last, after some twenty

[005-168]years, their substance has multiplied, and sufficient gülden have been

[005-169]honourably and virtuously accumulated. Then the ‘Vater’ blesses his

[005-170]forty-year-old heir and the thirty-five-year-old Gretchen with the

[005-171]sunken bosom and the scarlet nose; after which he bursts, into tears,

[005-172]reads the pair a lesson on morality, and dies. In turn the eldest son

[005-173]becomes a virtuous ‘Vater,’ and the old story begins again. In fifty or

[005-174]sixty years’ time the grandson of the original ‘Vater’ will have

[005-175]amassed a considerable sum; and that sum he will hand over to, his son,

[005-176]and the latter to _his_ son, and so on for several generations; until

[005-177]at length there will issue a Baron Rothschild, or a ‘Hoppe and

[005-178]Company,’ or the devil knows what! Is it not a beautiful spectacle—the

[005-179]spectacle of a century or two of inherited labour, patience, intellect,

[005-180]rectitude, character, perseverance, and calculation, with a stork

[005-181]sitting on the roof above it all? What is more; they think there can

[005-182]never be anything better than this; wherefore, from _their_ point of

[005-183]view they begin to judge the rest of the world, and to censure all who

[005-184]are at fault—that is to say, who are not exactly like themselves. Yes,

[005-185]there you have it in a nutshell. For my own part, I would rather grow

[005-186]fat after the Russian manner, or squander my whole substance at

[005-187]roulette. I have no wish to be ‘Hoppe and Company’ at the end of five

[005-188]generations. I want the money for _myself_, for in no way do I look

[005-189]upon my personality as necessary to, or meet to be given over to,

[005-190]capital. I may be wrong, but there you have it. Those are _my_ views.”
