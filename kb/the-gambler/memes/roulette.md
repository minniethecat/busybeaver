# Roulette

[[Roulette]] is the central gambling mechanism and governing metaphor of Dostoyevsky's *The Gambler*. It functions as a literal game [[gambling|play]]ed in the fictional [[spa|spa town]] of [[Roulettenberg]] (014-172), as the engine of the novel's plot, and as a compressed metaphor for life under [[chance]] — a [[method|system]] of arbitrary wins and [[losing|loss]]es where mathematical certainty guarantees eventual [[ruin]]. [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] becomes consumed by roulette — first [[gambling|play]]ing on behalf of [[Polina Alexandrovna]] (002-126), then descending into compulsive [[gambling]] that strips him of identity and fortune (017-030). The image of the spinning wheel, with its numbered slots and its implacable [[zero-zero]], recurs through the novel like a heartbeat.

## Definition

In the novel, roulette is a casino game of pure [[probability|chance]] in which [[gambling|play]]ers place [[bet|stake]]s on numbers, colours, or groups of numbers on a spinning wheel (003-034). [[roulette-wheel|The wheel]] contains numbered slots including a "zero" ([[zero-zero|zéro]]) (015-106), which gives [[bank|the house]] its mathematical advantage. Players may [[bet|stake]] single numbers, paying thirty-five to one (015-117), or bet on even/odd, red/black, or other combinations.

| [[bet|Bet]] Type | Payout | House Edge (via zero) |
|----------|--------|----------------------|
| Single number (plein) | 35:1 | 2.7% |
| Split (à cheval) | 17:1 | 2.7% |
| Red/Black, Even/Odd | 1:1 | 2.7% |
| Dozens, Columns | 2:1 | 2.7% |

Alongside standard roulette, the novel references [[trente-et-quarante]] ([[trente-et-quarante|thirty and forty]]), another game of [[probability|chance]] popular in European casinos (003-065).

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Roulette drives the plot at every critical juncture:

1. **The Commission** — [[Polina Alexandrovna]] sends [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] to [[gambling|play]] roulette on her behalf (002-126), establishing the game as a vehicle for their complex relationship
2. **The Centrepiece** — The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s extended roulette-[[gambling|play]]ing sequence (015-106) is the novel's dramatic heart: a [[fortune|wealth]]y matriarch destroying a fortune at the wheel while her scheming relatives watch helplessly
3. **The Dissolution** — Alexei's own roulette addiction (017-030) marks his complete moral and social dissolution
4. **The Aftermath** — His continuing career at [[casino|the tables]] in [[Homburg]] (017-001+) shows roulette as permanent condition, not temporary crisis

### Key Passages

> "At length I returned from two weeks leave of absence...had arrived three days ago in Roulettenberg" (001-056) — the novel's opening establishes the [[casino]] town as setting, roulette as the gravitational centre toward which all characters drift.

> "But what is zero?...Why does he keep raking in all the [[money]] for himself? What does zero mean?" (015-106) — the [[grandmother]]'s incomprehension of the game's rules, the novel's most celebrated comic-tragic moment.

> "Why thirty-five times, when zero so often turns up?" (015-117) — her logical error, mistaking payout ratio for probability, captures the gambler's eternal fallacy.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky treats roulette not as a pastime but as a psychological force. His own experience as a compulsive gambler — he wrote *The Gambler* under deadline to pay off [[gambling]] debts — infuses the novel with first-hand intensity. The [[gaming-table|roulette table]] becomes a crucible where character is simultaneously revealed and destroyed.

The novel presents roulette as a compressed metaphor for life under capitalism — a system of arbitrary wins and [[losing|loss]]es where mathematical certainty (the [[zero-zero]]'s house edge) guarantees eventual ruin (015-138). Unlike conventional moralistic treatments of gambling, Dostoyevsky renders the roulette experience with almost religious ecstasy, particularly in Alexei's [[winning]] streaks: "I felt I was a different man...[[grandmothers-arrival|the sensation]] was intoxicating" (017-030).
