# Polina's Rejection of Alexei

[[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s rejection of the money Alexei has won at roulette is *The Gambler*'s emotional climax—a scene in which all the novel's themes converge. Alexei, having won a fortune, offers it to [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] as proof of his [[love|devotion]]; she first refuses: "I am _not_ going to take your money" (024-087), and later hurls the packet at him: "Then _take_ your fifty thousand francs!" (025-011). The rejection is more devastating than any [[losing|loss]] at the roulette table. "Had it come of despair over her decision to come to me?" (025-019) — even [[alexei-ivanovitch|the narrator]] cannot parse the rejection's meaning. "It was her own pride, which kept urging her not to trust me" (025-024).

## Role in the Novel

The rejection crystallises the novel's deepest tension: [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] cannot accept Alexei's money without becoming what the General and [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]] have tried to make her—a commodity to be bought. Her refusal is an assertion of autonomy against a world that would reduce her to a transaction. But it is also, perhaps, the act that destroys any possibility of happiness for either of them.

The scene inverts the novel's central economic logic. Every other character schemes to obtain money; [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], offered a fortune without conditions, refuses it. This refusal exposes the emptiness of Alexei's gesture: he believed that winning money for her would prove his [[love|love]], but the money itself is the proof that he has misunderstood her entirely. She did not want the money; she wanted him to be something other than what the money reveals him to be.

## Key Moments

> "I am _not_ going to take your money" (024-087) — Polina's initial refusal, contemptuous and final.

> "Had it come of despair over her decision to come to me?" (025-019) — the narrator's anguished attempt to understand.

> "It was her own pride, which kept urging her not to trust me" (025-024) — pride as active force, the engine of self-destruction.

> "Then _take_ your fifty thousand francs!" (025-011) — Polina hurls the money, and the packet bursts across the floor.

## The Transaction Model of Love

Throughout *The Gambler*, love is framed as a species of transaction. The General pursues [[mlle-blanche|Mlle Blanche]] with the promise of an inheritance; [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]] lends money to [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] as an investment in future obligation; Alexei himself gambles for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] in the belief that money can purchase her affection. The novel's world is one in which every relationship has a price.

[[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s rejection breaks this pattern. By refusing the money, she refuses to be priced. But her refusal comes at a cost: it destroys the only form of relationship Alexei knows how to offer. The tragedy of the scene is not that she rejects him—it is that he cannot imagine loving her in any other way. His conception of love is transactional to its core, and the money he offers is the proof.

## Aftermath and Irreversibility

The rejection is irreversible. After this scene, [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] falls ill and departs [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]; Alexei pursues [[mlle-blanche|Mlle Blanche]] to Paris in a parody of love that confirms everything [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s rejection implied about his character. The novel offers no reconciliation, no second chance—only the [[gambling|gambling]] table, which continues to offer what [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] refused: the illusion that the next turn of the wheel will change everything.
