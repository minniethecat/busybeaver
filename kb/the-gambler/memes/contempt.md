# Contempt

Contempt is the dominant emotional currency of *The Gambler*. Characters trade in disdain — [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]]'s contempt for [[russia|Russia]]ns, [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s contempt for Alexei, [[alexei-ivanovitch|the narrator]]'s contempt for everyone including himself. Dostoevsky maps the social hierarchies of the spa town through the circulation of contempt: who may show it and who must absorb it.

## Contempt as Social Weapon

The Frenchman De Griers deploys contempt as an instrument of social superiority. He speaks to [[russia|Russia]]ns "in a careless, contemptuous sort of tone" (001-142), his polite surface barely concealing the disdain beneath. [[general|The General]]'s household is organized around the management of contempt — who may show it to whom, and who must absorb it.

De Griers's contempt is systematic. When the narrator speaks, "the Frenchman smiled contemptuously at my reply" (005-104). Later De Griers "smiled contemptuously, and shrugged his shoulders" (008-051) at the narrator's predicament. The Frenchman makes "no concealment of his contempt for me (he even made an obvious point of showing it)" (009-037), turning contempt into public performance.

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]] too wields contempt with authority, dismissing De Griers with "a look of contempt" (019-008) and departing — a rare instance of contempt flowing upward against the French social hierarchy.

## Polina's Contempt

[[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s treatment of [[alexei-ivanovitch|the narrator]] is steeped in contempt. She listens to him "contemptuously, with a slight protruding lip" (006-007), makes "a contemptuous grimace" (006-061), and looks at him "contemptuously" with "swimming eyes" (007-092). Yet this contempt is complicated: it is "mingled even with a spice of contempt and dislike" (004-023) but also something else — fascination, perhaps, or the recognition of a kindred spirit.

There is a disturbing intimacy to Polina's contempt. She could "not well have showed her contempt for me more than by permitting me to come to her" (004-031), the narrator notes. Her contempt draws him closer rather than pushing him away. She "listened to my confessions with such contempt" (021-089), yet she listens — and he continues confessing.

## Self-Contempt

The narrator's relationship to contempt is reflexive. He observes [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s "contempt for those who surrounded her" (004-153) while knowing he is among those she despises. Her "contempt for me" (004-036) is delivered "with complete frankness."

He wonders whether she "had forbidden me in jest, or only in contempt" (007-022), caught between humiliation and the hope that her contempt masks something else. Polina "kept me at a distance with that contemptuous air of hers" (021-115), yet the narrator "could not help going to her" (021-104) — knowing each approach would be met "with some fresh outburst of contempt and aloofness" (021-104). His own contempt for himself — his awareness of his degradation — is what makes him susceptible to hers.

A telling observation captures contempt's surprising economy: the narrator feels "even a contempt for—him, while, for his part" (010-088) the object of his contempt returns the feeling, creating a closed circuit of mutual disdain.

## The Economy of Disdain

Contempt in *The Gambler* functions as a zero-sum economy: to be the object of contempt is to lose standing; to express it is to claim superiority. But the novel's insight is that the two positions are often indistinguishable — the one who shows contempt and the one who receives it are bound together in mutual degradation.

The novel even captures contempt in physical gesture: when a character "turned his head contemptuously away" (017-110), the movement speaks louder than words. And the narrator's "contemptuous treatment of him" (022-148) shows how the victim of contempt can also become its perpetrator — the despised turns despiser.
