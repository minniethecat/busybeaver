# The Gambler · Section 18

[018-001]It happened thus:

[018-002]As soon as ever we arrived the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] ordered me to [[bet|stake]] twelve

[018-003]ten-gülden pieces in succession upon zero. Once, twice, and thrice I

[018-004]did so, yet zero never turned up.

[018-005]“Stake again,” said the old lady with an impatient nudge of my elbow,

[018-006]and I obeyed.

[018-007]“How many times have we lost?” she inquired—actually grinding her teeth

[018-008]in her excitement.

[018-009]“We have lost 144 ten-gülden pieces,” I replied. “I tell you, Madame,

[018-010]that zero may not turn up until nightfall.”

[018-011]“Never mind,” she interrupted. “Keep on staking upon zero, and also

[018-012][[risk|stake]] a thousand gülden upon rouge. Here is a banknote with which to do

[018-013]so.”

[018-014]The red turned up, but zero missed again, and we only got our thousand

[018-015]gülden back.

[018-016]“But you see, you see,” whispered the old lady. “We have now recovered

[018-017]almost all that we staked. Try zero again. Let us do so another ten

[018-018]times, and then leave off.”

[018-019]By the fifth round, however, the Grandmother was weary of the scheme.

[018-020]“To the devil with that zero!” she exclaimed. “Stake four thousand

[018-021]gülden upon the red.”

[018-022]“But, Madame, that will be so much to venture!” I remonstrated.

[018-023]“Suppose the red should not turn up?” The Grandmother almost struck me

[018-024]in her excitement. Her agitation was rapidly making her quarrelsome.

[018-025]Consequently, there was nothing for it but to stake the whole four

[018-026]thousand gülden as she had directed.

[018-027]The wheel revolved while the Grandmother sat as bolt upright, and with

[018-028]as proud and quiet a mien, as though she had not the least doubt of

[018-029]winning.

[018-030]“Zero!” cried the croupier.

[018-031]At first the old lady failed to understand the situation; but, as soon

[018-032]as she saw the croupier raking in her four thousand gülden, together

[018-033]with everything else that happened to be lying on the table, and

[018-034]recognised that [[zero-zero|the zero]] which had been so long turning up, and on

[018-035]which we had lost nearly two hundred ten-gülden pieces, had at length,

[018-036]as though of set purpose, made a sudden reappearance—why, the poor old

[018-037]lady fell to cursing it, and to throwing herself about, and wailing and

[018-038]gesticulating at the company at large. Indeed, some people in our

[018-039]vicinity actually burst out laughing.

[018-040]“To think that that accursed zero should have turned up _now!_” she

[018-041]sobbed. “The accursed, accursed thing! And, it is all _your_ fault,”

[018-042]she added, rounding upon me in a frenzy. “It was _you_ who persuaded me

[018-043]to cease staking upon it.”

[018-044]“But, Madame, I only explained [[roulette|the game]] to you. How am _I_ to answer

[018-045]for every mischance which may occur in it?”

[018-046]“You and your mischances!” she whispered threateningly. “Go! Away at

[018-047]once!”

[018-048]“Farewell, then, Madame.” And I turned to depart.

[018-049]“No—stay,” she put in hastily. “Where are you going to? Why should you

[018-050]leave me? You fool! No, no... stay here. It is _I_ who was the fool.

[018-051]Tell me what I ought to do.”

[018-052]“I cannot take it upon myself to advise you, for you will only blame me

[018-053]if I do so. Play at your own discretion. Say exactly what you wish

[018-054]staked, and I will stake it.”

[018-055]“Very well. Stake another four thousand gülden upon the red. Take this

[018-056]banknote to do it with. I have still got twenty thousand roubles in

[018-057]actual cash.”

[018-058]“But,” I whispered, “such a quantity of money—”

[018-059]“Never mind. I cannot rest until I have won back my losses. Stake!”

[018-060]I staked, and we lost.

[018-061]“Stake again, stake again—eight thousand at a stroke!”

[018-062]“I cannot, Madame. The largest stake allowed is four thousand gülden.”

[018-063]“Well, then; stake four thousand.”

[018-064]This time we won, and the Grandmother recovered herself a little.

[018-065]“You see, you see!” she exclaimed as she nudged me. “Stake another four

[018-066]thousand.”

[018-067]I did so, and lost. Again, and yet again, we lost. “Madame, your twelve

[018-068]thousand gülden are now gone,” at length I reported.

[018-069]“I see they are,” she replied with, as it were, the calmness of

[018-070]despair. “I see they are,” she muttered again as she gazed straight in

[018-071]front of her, like a person lost in thought. “Ah well, I do not mean to

[018-072]rest until I have staked another four thousand.”

[018-073]“But you have no money with which to do it, Madame. In this satchel I

[018-074]can see only a few five percent bonds and some transfers—no actual

[018-075]cash.”

[018-076]“And in the purse?”

[018-077]“A mere trifle.”

[018-078]“But there is a money-changer’s office here, is there not? They told me

[018-079]I should be able to get any sort of paper security changed!”

[018-080]“Quite so; to any amount you please. But you will lose on the

[018-081]transaction what would frighten even a Jew.”

[018-082]“Rubbish! I am _determined_ to retrieve my losses. Take me away, and

[018-083]call those fools of bearers.”

[018-084]I wheeled the chair out of the throng, and, the bearers making their

[018-085]appearance, we left the Casino.

[018-086]“Hurry, hurry!” commanded the Grandmother. “Show me the nearest way to

[018-087]the money-changer’s. Is it far?”

[018-088]“A couple of steps, Madame.”

[018-089]At the turning from the square into the Avenue we came face to face

[018-090]with the whole of our party—the General, De Griers, [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]], and

[018-091]her mother. Only [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] and [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] were absent.

[018-092]“Well, well, well!” exclaimed the Grandmother. “But we have no time to

[018-093]stop. What do you want? I can’t talk to you here.”

[018-094]I dropped behind a little, and immediately was pounced upon by De

[018-095]Griers.

[018-096]“She has lost this morning’s [[winning|winnings]],” I whispered, “and also twelve

[018-097]thousand gülden of her original money. At the present moment we are

[018-098]going to get some bonds changed.”

[018-099]De Griers stamped his foot with vexation, and hastened to communicate

[018-100]the tidings to the General. Meanwhile we continued to wheel the old

[018-101]lady along.

[018-102]“Stop her, stop her,” whispered the General in consternation.

[018-103]“You had better try and stop her yourself,” I returned—also in a

[018-104]whisper.

[018-105]“My good mother,” he said as he approached her, “—my good mother, pray

[018-106]let, let—” (his voice was beginning to tremble and sink) “—let us hire

[018-107]a carriage, and go for a drive. Near here there is an enchanting view

[018-108]to be obtained. We-we-we were just coming to invite you to go and see

[018-109]it.”

[018-110]“Begone with you and your views!” said the Grandmother angrily as she

[018-111]waved him away.

[018-112]“And there are trees there, and we could have tea under them,”

[018-113]continued the General—now in utter despair.

[018-114]“Nous boirons du lait, sur l’herbe fraiche,” added De Griers with the

[018-115]snarl almost of a wild beast.

[018-116]“Du lait, de l’herbe fraiche”—the idyll, the ideal of the Parisian

[018-117]bourgeois—his whole outlook upon “la nature et la verité”!

[018-118]“Have done with you and your milk!” cried the old lady. “Go and stuff

[018-119]_yourself_ as much as you like, but my stomach simply recoils from the

[018-120]idea. What are you stopping for? I have nothing to say to you.”

[018-121]“Here we are, Madame,” I announced. “Here is the moneychanger’s

[018-122]office.”

[018-123]I entered to get the securities changed, while the Grandmother remained

[018-124]outside in the porch, and the rest waited at a little distance, in

[018-125]doubt as to their best course of action. At length the old lady turned

[018-126]such an angry stare upon them that they departed along the road towards

[018-127]the Casino.

[018-128]The process of changing involved complicated calculations which soon

[018-129]necessitated my return to the Grandmother for instructions.

[018-130]“The thieves!” she exclaimed as she clapped her hands together. “Never

[018-131]mind, though. Get the documents cashed—No; send the banker out to me,”

[018-132]she added as an afterthought.

[018-133]“Would one of the clerks do, Madame?”

[018-134]“Yes, one of the clerks. The thieves!”

[018-135]The clerk consented to come out when he perceived that he was being

[018-136]asked for by an old lady who was too infirm to walk; after which the

[018-137]Grandmother began to upbraid him at length, and with great vehemence,

[018-138]for his alleged usuriousness, and to bargain with him in a mixture of

[018-139]Russian, French, and German—I acting as interpreter. Meanwhile, the

[018-140]grave-faced official eyed us both, and silently nodded his head. At the

[018-141]Grandmother, in particular, he gazed with a curiosity which almost

[018-142]bordered upon rudeness. At length, too, he smiled.

[018-143]“Pray recollect yourself!” cried the old lady. “And may my money choke

[018-144]you! [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]], tell him that we can easily repair to someone

[018-145]else.”

[018-146]“The clerk says that others will give you even less than he.”

[018-147]Of what the ultimate calculations consisted I do not exactly remember,

[018-148]but at all events they were alarming. Receiving twelve thousand florins

[018-149]in gold, I took also the statement of accounts, and carried it out to

[018-150]the Grandmother.

[018-151]“Well, well,” she said, “I am no accountant. Let us hurry away, hurry

[018-152]away.” And she waved the paper aside.

[018-153]“Neither upon that accursed zero, however, nor upon that equally

[018-154]accursed red do I mean to stake a cent,” I muttered to myself as I

[018-155]entered the Casino.

[018-156]This time I did all I could to persuade the old lady to stake as little

[018-157]as possible—saying that a turn would come in the chances when she would

[018-158]be at liberty to stake more. But she was so impatient that, though at

[018-159]first she agreed to do as I suggested, nothing could stop her when once

[018-160]she had begun. By way of prelude she won stakes of a hundred and two

[018-161]hundred gülden.

[018-162]“There you are!” she said as she nudged me. “See what we have won!

[018-163]Surely it would be worth our while to stake four thousand instead of a

[018-164]hundred, for we might win another four thousand, and then—! Oh, it was

[018-165]YOUR fault before—all your fault!”

[018-166]I felt greatly put out as I watched her [[gambling|play]], but I decided to hold my

[018-167]tongue, and to give her no more advice.

[018-168]Suddenly De Griers appeared on the scene. It seemed that all this while

[018-169]he and his companions had been standing beside us—though I noticed that

[018-170]Mlle. Blanche had withdrawn a little from the rest, and was engaged in

[018-171]flirting with the Prince. Clearly the General was greatly put out at

[018-172]this. Indeed, he was in a perfect agony of vexation. But Mlle. was

[018-173]careful never to look his way, though he did his best to attract her

[018-174]notice. Poor General! By turns his face blanched and reddened, and he

[018-175]was trembling to such an extent that he could scarcely follow the old

[018-176]lady’s play. At length Mlle. and the Prince took their departure, and

[018-177]the General followed them.

[018-178]“Madame, Madame,” sounded the honeyed accents of De Griers as he leant

[018-179]over to whisper in the Grandmother’s ear. “That stake will never win.

[018-180]No, no, it is impossible,” he added in Russian with a writhe. “No, no!”

[018-181]“But why not?” asked the Grandmother, turning round. “Show me what I

[018-182]ought to do.”

[018-183]Instantly De Griers burst into a babble of French as he advised, jumped

[018-184]about, declared that such and such chances ought to be waited for, and

[018-185]started to make calculations of figures. All this he addressed to me in

[018-186]my capacity as translator—tapping the table the while with his finger,

[018-187]and pointing hither and thither. At length he seized a pencil, and

[018-188]began to reckon sums on paper until he had exhausted the Grandmother’s

[018-189]patience.

[018-190]“Away with you!” she interrupted. “You talk sheer nonsense, for, though

[018-191]you keep on saying ‘Madame, Madame,’ you haven’t the least notion what

[018-192]ought to be done. Away with you, I say!”

[018-193]“Mais, Madame,” cooed De Griers—and straightway started afresh with his

[018-194]fussy instructions.

[018-195]“Stake just _once_, as he advises,” the Grandmother said to me, “and

[018-196]then we shall see what we _shall_ see. Of course, his stake _might_

[018-197]win.”

[018-198]As a matter of fact, De Grier’s one object was to distract the old lady

[018-199]from staking large sums; wherefore, he now suggested to her that she

[018-200]should stake upon certain numbers, singly and in groups. Consequently,

[018-201]in accordance with his instructions, I staked a ten-gülden piece upon

[018-202]several odd numbers in the first twenty, and five ten-gülden pieces

[018-203]upon certain groups of numbers-groups of from twelve to eighteen, and

[018-204]from eighteen to twenty-four. The total staked amounted to 160 gülden.
