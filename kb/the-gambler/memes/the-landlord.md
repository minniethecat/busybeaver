# The Landlord

The landlord of the Hotel de [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] appears briefly but memorably during the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s arrival — a comic functionary whose obsequiousness captures the social dynamics of [[roulettenberg|the spa town]] and serves as a satirical portrait of European hospitality in the service of [[money]].

## The Functionary

The landlord is introduced as the head of "the servants of the hotel, headed by the landlord (that functionary)" (012-032). Dostoyevsky's parenthetical aside — "that functionary" — is characteristic: it reduces the man to his office, implying that his personality has been entirely absorbed by his role. He is defined by hierarchy, existing only to manage the hotel's social order.

## Awestruck Deference

When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] arrives, the landlord's demeanor shifts from professional to servile. The narrator notes that people went "to inquire of the awestruck landlord who the newcomer was" (012-107). His awe is not personal but professional — a trained response to the visible signs of [[fortune|wealth]]. The landlord functions as a social barometer: his deference registers the Grandmother's status more reliably than any title could.

## The Grandmother's Command

The [[grandmother|Grandmother]] treats the landlord with characteristic imperiousness: "[[potapitch|Potapitch]], you go and tell that fool of a landlord" (012-087) to prepare her rooms. The appellation "fool" strips away the landlord's professional dignity in an instant. For the Grandmother, a Russian aristocrat of the old school, the European hotel-keeper is merely a [[servant]] with better clothes.

## A Universal Type

Dostoyevsky generalizes the landlord into a type: "probably, all over Europe — hotel landlords and managers are" (013-158) the same. This authorial generalization is typical of the novel's satirical mode, which treats national and professional types as stable categories for observation. The landlord is not an individual but a specimen — and in his predictable deference, he reveals the social machinery that [[roulettenberg]]'s international clientele takes for granted.

## Behind the Scenes

The landlord reappears in the background during the Grandmother's gambling crisis, managing the practical consequences of her losses. When the hotel's distinguished guest proves more demanding than expected, the landlord's professional mask strains but never breaks. He represents the institutional surface of European society — smooth, predictable, and ultimately indifferent to the human dramas playing out in his rooms.
