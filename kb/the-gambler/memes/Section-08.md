# The Gambler · Section 8

[008-001]Wheeling quietly about, I returned in the direction of [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]

[008-002]Alexandrovna. But, when I had got within a hundred paces of her seat, I

[008-003]saw her rise and set out with the children towards the hotel.

[008-004]At the portico I caught up to her.

[008-005]“I have perpetrated the—the piece of idiocy,” I said as I came level

[008-006]with her.

[008-007]“Have you? Then you can take the consequences,” she replied without so

[008-008]much as looking at me. Then she moved towards the staircase.

[008-009]I spent the rest of the evening walking in the park. Thence I passed

[008-010]into the forest, and walked on until I found myself in a neighbouring

[008-011]principality. At a wayside restaurant I partook of an omelette and some

[008-012]wine, and was charged for the idyllic repast a thaler and a half.

[008-013]Not until eleven o’clock did I return home—to find a summons awaiting

[008-014]me from the General.

[008-015]Our party occupied two suites in the hotel; each of which contained two

[008-016]rooms. The first (the larger suite) comprised a salon and a

[008-017]smoking-room, with, adjoining the latter, the General’s study. It was

[008-018]here that he was awaiting me as he stood posed in a majestic attitude

[008-019]beside his writing-table. Lolling on a divan close by was De Griers.

[008-020]“My good sir,” the General began, “may I ask you what this is that you

[008-021]have gone and done?”

[008-022]“I should be glad,” I replied, “if we could come straight to the point.

[008-023]Probably you are referring to my encounter of today with a German?”

[008-024]“With a German? Why, the German was [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] Burmergelm—a most

[008-025]important personage! I hear that you have been rude both to him and to

[008-026]the Baroness?”

[008-027]“No, I have not.”

[008-028]“But I understand that you simply terrified them, my good sir?” shouted

[008-029]the General.

[008-030]“Not in the least,” I replied. “You must know that when I was in Berlin

[008-031]I frequently used to hear the Berliners repeat, and repellently

[008-032]prolong, a certain phrase—namely, ‘Ja wohl!’; and, happening to meet

[008-033]this couple in the carriage-drive, I found, for some reason or another,

[008-034]that this phrase suddenly recurred to my memory, and exercised a

[008-035]rousing effect upon my spirits. Moreover, on the three previous

[008-036]occasions that I have met the Baroness she has walked towards me as

[008-037]though I were a worm which could easily be crushed with the foot. Not

[008-038]unnaturally, I too possess a measure of self-respect; wherefore, on

[008-039]_this_ occasion I took off my hat, and said politely (yes, I assure you

[008-040]it was said politely): ‘Madame, j’ai l’honneur d’être votre esclave.’

[008-041]Then the Baron turned round, and said ‘Hein!’; whereupon I felt moved

[008-042]to ejaculate in answer ‘Ja wohl!’ Twice I shouted it at him—the first

[008-043]time in an ordinary tone, and the second time with the greatest

[008-044]prolonging of the words of which I was capable. That is all.”

[008-045]I must confess that this puerile explanation gave me great pleasure. I

[008-046]felt a strong desire to overlay the incident with an even added measure

[008-047]of grossness; so, the further I proceeded, the more did the gusto of my

[008-048]proceeding increase.

[008-049]“You are only making fun of me!” vociferated the General as, turning to

[008-050][[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]], he declared that my bringing about of the incident had

[008-051]been gratuitous. De Griers smiled contemptuously, and shrugged his

[008-052]shoulders.

[008-053]“Do not think _that_,” I put in. “It was not so at all. I grant you

[008-054]that my behaviour was bad—I fully confess that it was so, and make no

[008-055]secret of the fact. I would even go so far as to grant you that my

[008-056]behaviour might well be called stupid and indecent tomfoolery; but,

[008-057]_more_ than that it was not. Also, let me tell you that I am very sorry

[008-058]for my conduct. Yet there is one circumstance which, in my eyes, almost

[008-059]absolves me from regret in the matter. Of late—that is to say, for the

[008-060]last two or three weeks—I have been feeling not at all well. That is to

[008-061]say, I have been in a sick, nervous, irritable, fanciful condition, so

[008-062]that I have periodically lost control over myself. For instance, on

[008-063]more than one occasion I have tried to pick a quarrel even with

[008-064]Monsieur le Marquise here; and, under the circumstances, he had no

[008-065]choice but to answer me. In short, I have recently been showing signs

[008-066]of ill-health. Whether the Baroness Burmergelm will take this

[008-067]circumstance into consideration when I come to beg her pardon (for I

[008-068]_do_ intend to make her amends) I do not know; but I doubt if she will,

[008-069]and the less so since, so far as I know, the circumstance is one which,

[008-070]of late, has begun to be abused in the legal world, in that advocates

[008-071]in criminal cases have taken to justifying their clients on the ground

[008-072]that, at the moment of the crime, they (the clients) were unconscious

[008-073]of what they were doing—that, in short, they were out of health. ‘My

[008-074]client committed the murder—that is true; but he has no recollection of

[008-075]having committed it.’ And doctors actually support these advocates by

[008-076]affirming that there really is such a malady—that there really _can_

[008-077]arise temporary delusions which make a man remember nothing of a given

[008-078]deed, or only a half or a quarter of it! But the Baron and Baroness are

[008-079]members of an older generation, as well as Prussian Junkers and

[008-080]landowners. To them such a process in the medico-judicial world will be

[008-081]unknown, and therefore, they are the more unlikely to accept any such

[008-082]explanation. What is _your_ opinion about it, General?”

[008-083]“Enough, sir!” he thundered with barely restrained fury. “Enough, I

[008-084]say! Once and for all I must endeavour to rid myself of you and your

[008-085]impertinence. To justify yourself in the eyes of the Baron and Baroness

[008-086]will be impossible. Any intercourse with you, even though it be

[008-087]confined to a begging of their pardons, they would look upon as a

[008-088]degradation. I may tell you that, on learning that you formed part of

[008-089]my household, the Baron approached me in the Casino, and demanded of me

[008-090]additional satisfaction. Do you understand, then, what it is that you

[008-091]have entailed upon me—upon _me_, my good sir? You have entailed upon me

[008-092]the fact of my being forced to sue humbly to the Baron, and to give him

[008-093]my word of honour that this very day you shall cease to belong to my

[008-094]establishment!”

[008-095]“Excuse me, General,” I interrupted, “but did he make an express point

[008-096]of it that I should ‘cease to belong to your establishment,’ as you

[008-097]call it?”

[008-098]“No; I, of my own initiative, thought that I ought to afford him that

[008-099]satisfaction; and, with it he was satisfied. So we must part, good sir.

[008-100]It is my duty to hand over to you forty gülden, three florins, as per

[008-101]the accompanying statement. Here is the money, and here the account,

[008-102]which you are at liberty to verify. Farewell. From henceforth we are

[008-103]strangers. From you I have never had anything but trouble and

[008-104]unpleasantness. I am about to call the landlord, and explain to him

[008-105]that from tomorrow onwards I shall no longer be responsible for your

[008-106]hotel expenses. Also I have the honour to remain your obedient

[008-107]servant.”

[008-108]I took the money and the account (which was indicted in pencil), and,

[008-109]bowing low to the General, said to him very gravely:

[008-110]“The matter cannot end here. I regret very much that you should have

[008-111]been put to unpleasantness at the Baron’s hands; but, the fault (pardon

[008-112]me) is your own. How came you to answer for me to the Baron? And what

[008-113]did you mean by saying that I formed part of your household? I am

[008-114]merely your family tutor—not a son of yours, nor yet your ward, nor a

[008-115]person of any kind for whose acts you need be responsible. I am a

[008-116]judicially competent person, a man of twenty-five years of age, a

[008-117]university graduate, a gentleman, and, until I met yourself, a complete

[008-118]stranger to you. Only my boundless respect for your merits restrains me

[008-119]from demanding satisfaction at your hands, as well as a further

[008-120]explanation as to the reasons which have led you to take it upon

[008-121]yourself to answer for my conduct.”

[008-122]So struck was he with my words that, spreading out his hands, he turned

[008-123]to the Frenchman, and interpreted to him that I had challenged himself

[008-124](the General) to a duel. The Frenchman laughed aloud.

[008-125]“Nor do I intend to let the Baron off,” I continued calmly, but with

[008-126]not a little discomfiture at De Griers’ merriment. “And since you,

[008-127]General, have today been so good as to listen to the Baron’s

[008-128]complaints, and to enter into his concerns—since you have made yourself

[008-129]a participator in the affair—I have the honour to inform you that,

[008-130]tomorrow morning at the latest, I shall, in my own name, demand of the

[008-131]said Baron a formal explanation as to the reasons which have led him to

[008-132]disregard the fact that the matter lies between him and myself alone,

[008-133]and to put a slight upon me by referring it to another person, as

[008-134]though I were unworthy to answer for my own conduct.”

[008-135]Then there happened what I had foreseen. [[general|The General]] on hearing of this

[008-136]further intended outrage, showed the white feather.

[008-137]“What?” he cried. “Do you intend to go on with this damned nonsense? Do

[008-138]you not realise the harm that it is doing me? I beg of you not to laugh

[008-139]at me, sir—not to laugh at me, for we have police authorities here who,

[008-140]out of respect for my rank, and for that of the Baron... In short, sir,

[008-141]I swear to you that I will have you arrested, and marched out of the

[008-142]place, to prevent any further brawling on your part. Do you understand

[008-143]what I say?” He was almost breathless with anger, as well as in a

[008-144]terrible fright.

[008-145]“General,” I replied with that calmness which he never could abide,

[008-146]“one cannot arrest a man for brawling until he has brawled. I have not

[008-147]so much as begun my explanations to the Baron, and you are altogether

[008-148]ignorant as to the form and time which my intended procedure is likely

[008-149]to assume. I wish but to disabuse the Baron of what is, to me, a

[008-150]shameful supposition—namely, that I am under the guardianship of a

[008-151]person who is qualified to exercise control over my free will. It is

[008-152]vain for you to disturb and alarm yourself.”

[008-153]“For God’s sake, [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]], do put an end to this senseless

[008-154]scheme of yours!” he muttered, but with a sudden change from a

[008-155]truculent tone to one of entreaty as he caught me by the hand. “Do you

[008-156]know what is likely to come of it? Merely further unpleasantness. You

[008-157]will agree with me, I am sure, that at present I ought to move with

[008-158]especial care—yes, with very especial care. You cannot be fully aware

[008-159]of how I am situated. When we leave this place I shall be ready to

[008-160]receive you back into my household; but, for the time being I— Well, I

[008-161]cannot tell you all my reasons.” With that he wound up in a despairing

[008-162]voice: “O Alexis Ivanovitch, Alexis Ivanovitch!”

[008-163]I moved towards the door—begging him to be calm, and promising that

[008-164]everything should be done decently and in order; whereafter I departed.

[008-165]Russians, when abroad, are over-apt to [[gambling|play]] the poltroon, to watch all

[008-166]their words, and to wonder what people are thinking of their conduct,

[008-167]or whether such and such a thing is _comme il faut_. In short, they are

[008-168]over-apt to cosset themselves, and to lay claim to great importance.

[008-169]Always they prefer the form of behaviour which has once and for all

[008-170]become accepted and established. This they will follow slavishly

[008-171]whether in hotels, on promenades, at meetings, or when on a journey.

[008-172]But the General had avowed to me that, over and above such

[008-173]considerations as these, there were circumstances which compelled him

[008-174]to “move with especial care at present”, and that the fact had actually

[008-175]made him poor-spirited and a coward—it had made him altogether change

[008-176]his tone towards me. This fact I took into my calculations, and duly

[008-177]noted it, for, of course, he _might_ apply to the authorities tomorrow,

[008-178]and it behoved me to go carefully.
