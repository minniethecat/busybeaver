# Potapitch

Potapitch is the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s elderly servant, a figure of pre-modern [[russia|Russia]]n loyalty transplanted into the novel's modern casino setting. When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] arrives at [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]], Potapitch accompanies her—not as a character in his own right but as an extension of her will. "Here, Potapitch!" she cries (012-060), and he appears. His existence is defined entirely by her commands: "Lift me up," "see to it that you..." (014-067, 014-072).

## Role in the Novel

Potapitch serves as the novel's quietest counterpoint to its dominant theme of self-interest. While every other character schemes for money, love, or status, Potapitch simply serves. His loyalty is feudal rather than moral—he does not choose to serve the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]; service is his identity. In a novel about the corrosive effects of modern individualism, Potapitch represents a world that has already vanished: the world of inherited [[debt|obligation]], fixed social roles, and unquestioning [[love|devotion]].

His presence also humanises the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]. Her treatment of Potapitch—imperious but not cruel, familiar rather than contemptuous—suggests a capacity for relationship that her interactions with her family do not reveal. She trusts Potapitch in a way she trusts no one else (012-087).

## Key Scenes

> "Here, Potapitch! she cried to an..." (012-060) — her summons, establishing their relationship in a single command.

> "And you, Potapitch, you go and tell that fool of..." (012-087) — her casual use of him as messenger, her trust in his discretion.

> "Lift me up. Potapitch, see to it that you..." (014-067) — her dependence on him, the physical intimacy of long service.

> "You too, Potapitch, are always to come with me" (014-072) — her declaration of his permanent place in her retinue.

## Feudal Loyalty in a Modern World

Potapitch embodies a form of social relation that the novel's other characters have abandoned. His loyalty to the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] is not calculated, not contractually defined, and not subject to renegotiation. It is simply what he is. This is the feudal model of service: identity and obligation are inseparable, and the self exists only in relation to a superior.

The novel places this model in deliberate tension with the transactional relationships that dominate [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]. The General's attachment to [[mlle-blanche|Mlle Blanche]] is contingent on his inheritance; [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]]'s attentions to [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] are contingent on her compliance. Every relationship in the novel's social world is conditional—except Potapitch's. His unconditional service is the novel's implicit critique of a world in which everything, including love, has become a transaction.

## The Servant as Moral Witness

Though Potapitch has no interiority—the novel grants him no thoughts, no desires, no independent existence—his presence functions as a silent moral commentary. He watches the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s family scheme for her money; he watches the casino's rituals of [[gambling|gambling]] and [[ruin|ruin]]; he watches without judging. His silence is not stupidity but the disciplined restraint of a servant who knows that his observations are not solicited.

This makes Potapitch a peculiar kind of moral witness: present at every scene, aware of every intrigue, but powerless to intervene—and perhaps uninterested in intervening. His loyalty is to the [[grandmother|Grandmother]], not to any abstract principle of justice. If she wishes to gamble away her fortune, he will carry her chair to the [[roulette|roulette]] table. His moral function is not to judge but to be the one figure who is exactly what he appears to be.
