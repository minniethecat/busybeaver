# The Gambler · Section 26

[026-001]For a moment I reflected.

[026-002]“No!” I shouted. “My account, please, for in ten minutes I shall be

[026-003]gone.”

[026-004]“To [[paris|Paris]], to Paris!” I added to myself. “Every man of birth must make

[026-005]her acquaintance.”

[026-006]Within a quarter of an hour all three of us were seated in a family

[026-007]compartment—[[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]], the Widow de Cominges, and myself. Mlle.

[026-008]kept laughing hysterically as she looked at me, and Madame re-echoed

[026-009]her; but _I_ did not feel so cheerful. My life had broken in two, and

[026-010]yesterday had infected me with a habit of staking my all upon a card.

[026-011]Although it might be that I had failed to win my [[bet|stake]], that I had lost

[026-012]my senses, that I desired nothing better, I felt that the scene was to

[026-013]be changed only _for a time_. “Within a month from now,” I kept

[026-014]thinking to myself, “I shall be back again in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]; and _then_

[026-015]I mean to have it out with you, [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]]!” Yes, as now I look back at

[026-016]things, I remember that I felt greatly depressed, despite the absurd

[026-017]gigglings of the egregious Blanche.

[026-018]“What is the matter with you? How dull you are!” she cried at length as

[026-019]she interrupted her laughter to take me seriously to task.

[026-020]“Come, come! We are going to spend your two hundred thousand francs for

[026-021]you, et tu seras heureux comme un petit roi. I myself will tie your tie

[026-022]for you, and introduce you to Hortense. And when we have spent your

[026-023]money you shall return here, and break [[gambling-bank|the bank]] again. What did those

[026-024]two Jews tell you?—that the thing most needed is daring, and that you

[026-025]possess it? Consequently, this is not the first time that you will be

[026-026]hurrying to Paris with money in your pocket. Quant à moi, je veux

[026-027]cinquante mille francs de rente, et alors——”

[026-028]“But what about the General?” I interrupted.

[026-029]“[[general|The General]]? You know well enough that at about this hour every day he

[026-030]goes to buy me a bouquet. On this occasion, I took care to tell him

[026-031]that he must hunt for the choicest of flowers; and when he returns

[026-032]home, the poor fellow will find the bird flown. Possibly he may take

[026-033]wing in pursuit—ha, ha, ha! And if so, I shall not be sorry, for he

[026-034]could be useful to me in Paris, and Mr. Astley will pay his debts

[026-035]here.”

[026-036]In this manner did I depart for the Gay City.

[026-037]XVI

[026-038]Of Paris what am I to say? The whole proceeding was a delirium, a

[026-039]madness. I spent a little over three weeks there, and, during that

[026-040]time, saw my hundred thousand francs come to an end. I speak only of

[026-041]the _one_ hundred thousand francs, for the other hundred thousand I

[026-042]gave to Mlle. Blanche in pure cash. That is to say, I handed her fifty

[026-043]thousand francs at Frankfurt, and, three days later (in Paris),

[026-044]advanced her another fifty thousand on note of hand. Nevertheless, a

[026-045]week had not elapsed ere she came to me for more money. “Et les cent

[026-046]mille francs qui nous restent,” she added, “tu les mangeras avec moi,

[026-047]mon utchitel.” Yes, she always called me her “utchitel.” A person more

[026-048]economical, grasping, and mean than Mlle. Blanche one could not

[026-049]imagine. But this was only as regards _her own_ money. _My_ hundred

[026-050]thousand francs (as she explained to me later) she needed to set up her

[026-051]establishment in Paris, “so that once and for all I may be on a decent

[026-052]footing, and proof against any stones which may be thrown at me—at all

[026-053]events for a long time to come.” Nevertheless, I saw nothing of those

[026-054]hundred thousand francs, for my own purse (which she inspected daily)

[026-055]never managed to amass in it more than a hundred francs at a time; and,

[026-056]generally the sum did not reach even that figure.

[026-057]“What do _you_ want with money?” she would say to me with air of

[026-058]absolute simplicity; and I never disputed the point. Nevertheless,

[026-059]though she fitted out her flat very badly with the money, the fact did

[026-060]not prevent her from saying when, later, she was showing me over the

[026-061]rooms of her new abode: “See what care and taste can do with the most

[026-062]wretched of means!” However, her “[[despair|wretchedness]]” had cost fifty thousand

[026-063]francs, while with the remaining fifty thousand she purchased a

[026-064]carriage and horses.

[026-065]Also, we gave a couple of balls—evening parties attended by Hortense

[026-066]and Lisette and Cléopatre, who were women remarkable both for the

[026-067]number of their liaisons and (though only in some cases) for their good

[026-068]looks. At these reunions I had to [[gambling|play]] the part of host—to meet and

[026-069]entertain fat mercantile parvenus who were impossible by reason of

[026-070]their rudeness and braggadocio, colonels of various kinds, hungry

[026-071]authors, and journalistic hacks—all of whom disported themselves in

[026-072]fashionable tailcoats and pale yellow gloves, and displayed such an

[026-073]aggregate of conceit and gasconade as would be unthinkable even in St.

[026-074][[st-petersburg|Petersburg]]—which is saying a great deal! They used to try to make fun

[026-075]of me, but I would console myself by drinking champagne and then

[026-076]lolling in a retiring-room. Nevertheless, I found it deadly work.

[026-077]“C’est un utchitel,” Blanche would say of me, “qui a gagné deux cent

[026-078]mille francs, and but for me, would have had not a notion how to spend

[026-079]them. Presently he will have to return to his tutoring. Does any one

[026-080]know of a vacant post? You know, one must do something for him.”

[026-081]I had the more frequent recourse to champagne in that I constantly felt

[026-082]depressed and bored, owing to the fact that I was living in the most

[026-083]bourgeois commercial milieu imaginable—a milieu wherein every sou was

[026-084]counted and grudged. Indeed, two weeks had not elapsed before I

[026-085]perceived that Blanche had no real affection for me, even though she

[026-086]dressed me in elegant clothes, and herself tied my tie each day. In

[026-087]short, she utterly despised me. But that caused me no concern. Blasé

[026-088]and inert, I spent my evenings generally at the Château des Fleurs,

[026-089]where I would get fuddled and then dance the cancan (which, in that

[026-090]establishment, was a very indecent performance) with éclat. At length,

[026-091]the time came when Blanche had drained my purse dry. She had conceived

[026-092]an idea that, during the term of our residence together, it would be

[026-093]well if I were always to walk behind her with a paper and pencil, in

[026-094]order to jot down exactly what she spent, what she had saved, what she

[026-095]was paying out, and what she was laying by. Well, of course I could not

[026-096]fail to be aware that this would entail a battle over every ten francs;

[026-097]so, although for every possible objection that I might make she had

[026-098]prepared a suitable answer, she soon saw that I made no objections, and

[026-099]therefore, had to start disputes herself. That is to say, she would

[026-100]burst out into tirades which were met only with silence as I lolled on

[026-101]a sofa and stared fixedly at the ceiling. This greatly surprised her.

[026-102]At first she imagined that it was due merely to the fact that I was a

[026-103]fool, “un utchitel”; wherefore she would break off her harangue in the

[026-104]belief that, being too stupid to understand, I was a hopeless case.

[026-105]Then she would leave the room, but return ten minutes later to resume

[026-106]the contest. This continued throughout her squandering of my money—a

[026-107]squandering altogether out of proportion to our means. An example is

[026-108]the way in which she changed her first pair of horses for a pair which

[026-109]cost sixteen thousand francs.

[026-110]“Bibi,” she said on the latter occasion as she approached me, “surely

[026-111]you are not angry?”

[026-112]“No-o-o: I am merely tired,” was my reply as I pushed her from me. This

[026-113]seemed to her so curious that straightway she seated herself by my

[026-114]side.

[026-115]“You see,” she went on, “I decided to spend so much upon these horses

[026-116]only because I can easily sell them again. They would go at any time

[026-117]for _twenty_ thousand francs.”

[026-118]“Yes, yes. They are splendid horses, and you have got a splendid

[026-119]turn-out. I am quite content. Let me hear no more of the matter.”

[026-120]“Then you are not angry?”

[026-121]“No. Why should I be? You are wise to provide yourself with what you

[026-122]need, for it will all come in handy in the future. Yes, I quite see the

[026-123]necessity of your establishing yourself on a good basis, for without it

[026-124]you will never earn your million. My hundred thousand francs I look

[026-125]upon merely as a beginning—as a mere drop in the bucket.”

[026-126]Blanche, who had by no means expected such declarations from me, but,

[026-127]rather, an uproar and protests, was rather taken aback.

[026-128]“Well, well, what a man you are!” she exclaimed. “Mais tu as l’esprit

[026-129]pour comprendre. Sais-tu, mon garçon, although you are a tutor, you

[026-130]ought to have been born a prince. Are you not sorry that your money

[026-131]should be going so quickly?”

[026-132]“No. The quicker it goes the better.”

[026-133]“Mais—sais-tu—mais dis donc, are you _really_ rich? Mais sais-tu, you

[026-134]have too much contempt for money. Qu’est-ce que tu feras après, dis

[026-135]donc?”

[026-136]“Après I shall go to [[homburg|Homburg]], and win another hundred thousand francs.”

[026-137]“Oui, oui, c’est ça, c’est magnifique! Ah, I know you will win them,

[026-138]and bring them to me when you have done so. Dis donc—you will end by

[026-139]making me love you. Since you are what you are, I mean to love you all

[026-140]the time, and never to be unfaithful to you. You see, I have not loved

[026-141]you before parce que je croyais que tu n’es qu’un utchitel (quelque

[026-142]chose comme un lacquais, n’est-ce pas?) Yet all the time I have been

[026-143]true to you, parce que je suis bonne fille.”

[026-144]“You lie!” I interrupted. “Did I not see you, the other day, with

[026-145]Albert—with that black-jowled officer?”

[026-146]“Oh, oh! Mais tu es—”

[026-147]“Yes, you are lying right enough. But what makes you suppose that I

[026-148]should be angry? Rubbish! Il faut que jeunesse se passe. Even if that

[026-149]officer were here now, I should refrain from putting him out of the

[026-150]room if I thought you really cared for him. Only, mind you, do not give

[026-151]him any of my money. You hear?”

[026-152]“You say, do you, that you would not be angry? Mais tu es un vrai

[026-153]philosophe, sais-tu? Oui, un vrai philosophe! Eh bien, je t’aimerai, je

[026-154]t’aimerai. Tu verras-tu seras content.”

[026-155]True enough, from that time onward she seemed to attach herself only to

[026-156]me, and in this manner we spent our last ten days together. The

[026-157]promised “étoiles” I did not see, but in other respects she, to a

[026-158]certain extent, kept her word. Moreover, she introduced me to Hortense,

[026-159]who was a remarkable woman in her way, and known among us as Thérèse

[026-160]Philosophe.

[026-161]But I need not enlarge further, for to do so would require a story to

[026-162]itself, and entail a colouring which I am loth to impart to the present

[026-163]narrative. The point is that with all my faculties I desired the

[026-164]episode to come to an end as speedily as possible. Unfortunately, our

[026-165]hundred thousand francs lasted us, as I have said, for very nearly a

[026-166]month—which greatly surprised me. At all events, Blanche bought herself

[026-167]articles to the tune of eighty thousand francs, and the rest sufficed

[026-168]just to meet our expenses of living. Towards the close of the affair,

[026-169]Blanche grew almost frank with me (at least, she scarcely lied to me at

[026-170]all)—declaring, amongst other things, that none of the debts which she

[026-171]had been obliged to incur were going to fall upon my head. “I have

[026-172]purposely refrained from making you responsible for my bills or

[026-173]borrowings,” she said, “for the reason that I am sorry for you. Any

[026-174]other woman in my place would have done so, and have let you go to

[026-175]prison. See, then, how much I love you, and how good-hearted I am!

[026-176]Think, too, what this accursed marriage with the General is going to

[026-177]cost me!”
