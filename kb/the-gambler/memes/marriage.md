# Marriage

Marriage in Dostoyevsky's *The Gambler* is never about love. It is a financial instrument, a class strategy, and a desperate gamble in its own right. The novel's central plot mechanism — the [[general|General's]] plan to marry [[mlle-blanche|Mademoiselle Blanche de Cominges]] — drives the action from the opening pages to the final chapters, binding together the themes of [[debt]], [[inheritance]], [[honour]], and social ambition.

## Marriage as Transaction

The 19th-century European marriage market treated romantic unions as business arrangements. A woman's dowry and a man's prospects were the currency of negotiation; love was, at best, an afterthought. Dostoyevsky pushes this logic to its extreme by making the General's marriage plan explicitly dependent on the death of his aged aunt, the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]:

> "I cannot see that the marriage scheme need be affected by such a trifle" (009-150)

The "trifle" in question is the Grandmother's impending death — or rather, her refusal to die on schedule. The General's entire financial architecture rests on his expected inheritance, and without it, his marriage to Blanche, a French adventuress who values only his wealth, collapses.

## Role in The Gambler

### The General's Marriage Plot

The General, a widower past fifty, has fallen in love with Mademoiselle Blanche de Cominges, a French woman of dubious reputation and expensive tastes. For Blanche, the marriage is purely transactional — she is willing to trade her youth and beauty for the General's (anticipated) wealth. The General, for his part, experiences genuine romantic [[despair]], but his desire is inseparable from his need to maintain appearances:

> "The marriage is not yet a settled affair" (009-159)

This uncertainty haunts the novel's early chapters. Every character in the General's circle understands that the marriage depends on the Grandmother's death. The grotesque comedy of the situation — a household waiting for an old woman to die so a wedding can proceed — is pure Dostoyevsky.

### Marriage and Slavery

Dostoyevsky draws an explicit parallel between marriage and [[slavery]]:

> "They become abject slaves where marriage is concerned" (012-006)

This observation, made by the narrator about women in general but aimed at [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] in particular, captures the novel's bleak view of the institution. Marriage reduces individuals to objects of exchange, stripping them of autonomy.

### The Wedding Itself

The novel's final chapters confirm the transactional logic:

> "True enough, the marriage took place. It did so at the end of the season" (027-001)

The flat, almost indifferent tone of this announcement reflects the anti-climax of the entire marriage plot. The wedding has been the novel's driving suspense, yet when it finally occurs, it is barely worth narrating. Blanche gets the General's money; the General gets Blanche's status. Both lose.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's treatment of marriage is characteristically unsentimental. In a novel filled with passionate declarations — Alexei's love for Polina, the General's love for Blanche — the actual marriages that occur are cold transactions. Even the suggestion that Polina might accept Mr. Astley's proposal (021-154) is framed as a practical choice rather than a romantic one.

This aligns with Dostoyevsky's broader critique of Europeanised Russian society, where traditional values have been hollowed out by money and ambition. Marriage, the supposed foundation of family and morality, has become just another form of [[gambling]] — a bet placed on uncertain futures.
