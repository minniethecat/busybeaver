# The Gambler · Section 10

[010-001]“Very well, then,” he said, in a sterner and more arrogant tone.

[010-002]“Seeing that my solicitations have had no effect upon you, it is my

[010-003]duty to mention that other measures will be taken. There exist here

[010-004]police, you must remember, and this very day they shall send you

[010-005]packing. Que diable! To think of a blanc bec like yourself challenging

[010-006]a person like [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] to a duel! Do you suppose that you will be

[010-007]_allowed_ to do such things? Just try doing them, and see if any one

[010-008]will be afraid of you! The reason why I have asked you to desist is

[010-009]that I can see that your conduct is causing the General annoyance. Do

[010-010]you believe that the Baron could not tell his lacquey simply to put you

[010-011]out of doors?”

[010-012]“Nevertheless I should not GO out of doors,” I retorted with absolute

[010-013]calm. “You are labouring under a delusion, Monsieur [[marquis-de-griers|de Griers]]. The

[010-014]thing will be done in far better trim than you imagine. I was just

[010-015]about to start for [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]]’s, to ask him to be my intermediary—in

[010-016]other words, my second. He has a strong liking for me, and I do not

[010-017]think that he will refuse. He will go and see the Baron on MY behalf,

[010-018]and the Baron will certainly not decline to receive him. Although I am

[010-019]only a tutor—a kind of subaltern, Mr. Astley is known to all men as the

[010-020]nephew of a real English lord, the Lord Piebroch, as well as a lord in

[010-021]his own right. Yes, you may be pretty sure that the Baron will be civil

[010-022]to Mr. Astley, and listen to him. Or, should he decline to do so, Mr.

[010-023]Astley will take the refusal as a personal affront to himself (for you

[010-024]know how persistent the English are?) and thereupon introduce to the

[010-025]Baron a friend of his own (and he has many friends in a good position).

[010-026]That being so, picture to yourself the issue of the affair—an affair

[010-027]which will not quite end as you think it will.”

[010-028]This caused the Frenchman to bethink him of playing the coward. “Really

[010-029]things may be as this fellow says,” he evidently thought. “Really he

[010-030]_might_ be able to engineer another scene.”

[010-031]“Once more I beg of you to let the matter drop,” he continued in a tone

[010-032]that was now entirely conciliatory. “One would think that it actually

[010-033]_pleased_ you to have scenes! Indeed, it is a brawl rather than genuine

[010-034]satisfaction that you are seeking. I have said that the affair may

[010-035]prove to be diverting, and even clever, and that possibly you may

[010-036]attain something by it; yet none the less I tell you” (he said this

[010-037]only because he saw me rise and reach for my hat) “that I have come

[010-038]hither also to hand you these few words from a certain person. Read

[010-039]them, please, for I must take her back an answer.”

[010-040]So saying, he took from his pocket a small, compact, wafer-sealed note,

[010-041]and handed it to me. In [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]’s handwriting I read:

[010-042]“I hear that you are thinking of going on with this affair. You have

[010-043]lost your temper now, and are beginning to [[gambling|play]] the fool! Certain

[010-044]circumstances, however, I may explain to you later. Pray cease from

[010-045]your folly, and put a check upon yourself. For folly it all is. I have

[010-046]need of you, and, moreover, you have promised to obey me. Remember the

[010-047]Shlangenberg. I ask you to be obedient. If necessary, I shall even

[010-048]_bid_ you be obedient.—Your own

[010-049]POLINA.

[010-050]“_P.S._—If so be that you still bear a grudge against me for what

[010-051]happened last night, pray forgive me.”

[010-052]Everything, to my eyes, seemed to change as I read these words. My lips

[010-053]grew pale, and I began to tremble. Meanwhile, the cursed Frenchman was

[010-054]eyeing me discreetly and askance, as though he wished to avoid

[010-055]witnessing my confusion. It would have been better if he had laughed

[010-056]outright.

[010-057]“Very well,” I said, “you can tell Mlle. not to disturb herself. But,”

[010-058]I added sharply, “I would also ask you why you have been so long in

[010-059]handing me this note? Instead of chattering about trifles, you ought to

[010-060]have delivered me the missive at once—if you have really come

[010-061]commissioned as you say.”

[010-062]“Well, pardon some natural haste on my part, for the situation is so

[010-063]strange. I wished first to [[winning|gain]] some personal knowledge of your

[010-064]intentions; and, moreover, I did not know the contents of the note, and

[010-065]thought that it could be given you at any time.”

[010-066]“I understand,” I replied. “So you were ordered to hand me the note

[010-067]only in the last resort, and if you could not otherwise appease me? Is

[010-068]it not so? Speak out, Monsieur de Griers.”

[010-069]“Perhaps,” said he, assuming a look of great forbearance, but gazing at

[010-070]me in a meaning way.

[010-071]I reached for my hat; whereupon he nodded, and went out. Yet on his

[010-072]lips I fancied that I could see a mocking smile. How could it have been

[010-073]otherwise?

[010-074]“You and I are to have a reckoning later, Master Frenchman,” I muttered

[010-075]as I descended the stairs. “Yes, we will measure our strength

[010-076]together.” Yet my thoughts were all in confusion, for again something

[010-077]seemed to have struck me dizzy. Presently the air revived me a little,

[010-078]and, a couple of minutes later, my brain had sufficiently cleared to

[010-079]enable two ideas in particular to stand out in it. Firstly, I asked

[010-080]myself, which of the absurd, boyish, and extravagant threats which I

[010-081]had uttered at random last night had made everybody so alarmed?

[010-082]Secondly, what was the influence which this Frenchman appeared to

[010-083]exercise over Polina? He had but to give the word, and at once she did

[010-084]as he desired—at once she wrote me a note to beg of me to forbear! Of

[010-085]course, the relations between the pair had, from the first, been a

[010-086]riddle to me—they had been so ever since I had first made their

[010-087]acquaintance. But of late I had remarked in her a strong aversion for,

[010-088]even a contempt for—him, while, for his part, he had scarcely even

[010-089]looked at her, but had behaved towards her always in the most churlish

[010-090]fashion. Yes, I had noted that. Also, Polina herself had mentioned to

[010-091]me her dislike for him, and delivered herself of some remarkable

[010-092]confessions on the subject. Hence, he must have got her into his power

[010-093]somehow—somehow he must be holding her as in a vice.

[010-094]VIII

[010-095]All at once, on the Promenade, as it was called—that is to say, in the

[010-096]Chestnut Avenue—I came face to face with my Englishman.

[010-097]“I was just coming to see you,” he said; “and you appear to be out on a

[010-098]similar errand. So you have parted with your employers?”

[010-099]“How do you know that?” I asked in astonishment. “Is _every one_ aware

[010-100]of the fact?”

[010-101]“By no means. Not every one would consider such a fact to be of moment.

[010-102]Indeed, I have never heard any one speak of it.”

[010-103]“Then how come you to know it?”

[010-104]“Because I have had occasion to do so. Whither are you bound? I like

[010-105]you, and was therefore coming to pay you a visit.”

[010-106]“What a splendid fellow you are, Mr. Astley!” I cried, though still

[010-107]wondering how he had come by his knowledge. “And since I have not yet

[010-108]had my coffee, and you have, in all [[chance|probability]], scarcely tasted yours,

[010-109]let us adjourn to the Casino Café, where we can sit and smoke and have

[010-110]a talk.”

[010-111]The café in question was only a hundred paces away; so, when coffee had

[010-112]been brought, we seated ourselves, and I lit a cigarette. Astley was no

[010-113]smoker, but, taking a seat by my side, he prepared himself to listen.

[010-114]“I do not intend to go away,” was my first remark. “I intend, on the

[010-115]contrary, to remain here.”

[010-116]“That I never doubted,” he answered good-humouredly.

[010-117]It is a curious fact that, on my way to see him, I had never even

[010-118]thought of telling him of my love for Polina. In fact, I had purposely

[010-119]meant to avoid any mention of the subject. Nor, during our stay in the

[010-120]place, had I ever made aught but the scantiest reference to it. You

[010-121]see, not only was Astley a man of great reserve, but also from the

[010-122]first I had perceived that Polina had made a great impression upon him,

[010-123]although he never spoke of her. But now, strangely enough, he had no

[010-124]sooner seated himself and bent his steely gaze upon me, than, for some

[010-125]reason or another, I felt moved to tell him everything—to speak to him

[010-126]of my love in all its phases. For an hour and a half did I discourse on

[010-127]the subject, and found it a pleasure to do so, even though this was the

[010-128]first occasion on which I had referred to the matter. Indeed, when, at

[010-129]certain moments, I perceived that my more ardent passages confused him,

[010-130]I purposely increased my ardour of narration. Yet one thing I regret:

[010-131]and that is that I made references to the Frenchman which were a little

[010-132]over-personal.

[010-133]Mr. Astley sat without moving as he listened to me. Not a word nor a

[010-134]sound of any kind did he utter as he stared into my eyes. Suddenly,

[010-135]however, on my mentioning the Frenchman, he interrupted me, and

[010-136]inquired sternly whether I did right to speak of an extraneous matter

[010-137](he had always been a strange man in his mode of propounding

[010-138]questions).

[010-139]“No, I fear not,” I replied.

[010-140]“And concerning this Marquis and Mlle. Polina you know nothing beyond

[010-141]surmise?”

[010-142]Again I was surprised that such a categorical question should come from

[010-143]such a reserved individual.

[010-144]“No, I know nothing _for certain_ about them” was my reply.

[010-145]“No—nothing.”

[010-146]“Then you have done very wrong to speak of them to me, or even to

[010-147]imagine things about them.”

[010-148]“Quite so, quite so,” I interrupted in some astonishment. “I admit

[010-149]that. Yet that is not the question.” Whereupon I related to him in

[010-150]detail the incident of two days ago. I spoke of Polina’s outburst, of

[010-151]my encounter with the Baron, of my dismissal, of the General’s

[010-152]extraordinary pusillanimity, and of the call which De Griers had that

[010-153]morning paid me. In conclusion, I showed Astley the note which I had

[010-154]lately received.

[010-155]“What do you make of it?” I asked. “When I met you I was just coming to

[010-156]ask you your opinion. For myself, I could have killed this Frenchman,

[010-157]and am not sure that I shall not do so even yet.”

[010-158]“I feel the same about it,” said Mr. Astley. “As for Mlle. Polina—well,

[010-159]you yourself know that, if necessity drives, one enters into relation

[010-160]with people whom one simply detests. Even between this couple there may

[010-161]be something which, though unknown to you, depends upon extraneous

[010-162]circumstances. For, my own part, I think that you may reassure

[010-163]yourself—or at all events partially. And as for Mlle. Polina’s

[010-164]proceedings of two days ago, they were, of course, strange; not because

[010-165]she can have meant to get rid of you, or to earn for you a thrashing

[010-166]from the Baron’s cudgel (which for some curious reason, he did not use,

[010-167]although he had it ready in his hands), but because such proceedings on

[010-168]the part of such—well, of such a refined lady as Mlle. Polina are, to

[010-169]say the least of it, unbecoming. But she cannot have guessed that you

[010-170]would carry out her absurd wish to the letter?”

[010-171]“Do you know what?” suddenly I cried as I fixed Mr. Astley with my

[010-172]gaze. “I believe that you have already heard the story from some

[010-173]one—very possibly from Mlle. Polina herself?”

[010-174]In return he gave me an astonished stare.

[010-175]“Your eyes look very fiery,” he said with a return of his former calm,

[010-176]“and in them I can read suspicion. Now, you have no right whatever to

[010-177]be suspicious. It is not a right which I can for a moment recognise,

[010-178]and I absolutely refuse to answer your questions.”
