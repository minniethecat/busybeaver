# Honour

Honour in *The Gambler* is the social currency that every character claims to possess and none can afford to lose. It is constantly invoked—"on my honour" (007-020), "a man of honour" (002-091), "gentleman of honour" (020-131)—and just as constantly betrayed. Dostoyevsky treats honour as a performance that the novel's characters cannot sustain under the pressure of debt, gambling, and sexual competition.

## Definition

In the novel's compressed social world, honour is the code of gentlemanly conduct that the [[russia|Russia]]n characters bring with them to the German spa town of [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]. It demands financial probity, sexual restraint, and truthfulness—demands that are systematically violated by every character who invokes it. [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], [[mr-astley|the Englishman]], embodies honour in its classical form: "[[mr-astley|the Englishman]] is not only a man of honour, but ten" (002-091), but he is an outsider whose honour does not translate to the [[russia|Russia]]n circle.

The "gentleman of honour" (020-131) becomes a term of bitter irony in the novel's later chapters, as characters who loudly proclaim their honour are precisely those who possess the least of it.

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Honour serves as the novel's primary instrument of social critique. [[general|The General]], who schemes after an inheritance while maintaining a façade of dignity, uses the language of honour to mask financial [[despair|desperation]]. Alexei, who gambles [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]'s money and his own, swears "on my honour" (007-020) in moments of maximum dishonesty. The Polish "gentleman of honour" (020-131) who accompanies the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] is unmasked as a fraud—his honour is a confidence trick.

The novel's satire emerges from the gap between honour's demands and characters' behaviour. When [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]'s mother speaks of "family honour" (021-013), the reader already knows that honour is a commodity traded for advantage, not a moral absolute.

### Key Passages

> "The Englishman is not only a man of honour, but ten" (002-091) — Mr. Astley's honour introduced as a quantitative excess, almost absurd in its magnitude.

> "On my honour I swear that, had she bidden me..." (007-020) — Alexei's oath, the language of honour as dramatic intensifier.

> "I have the honour to remain your obedient servant" (008-106) — the formal language of honour as ironic commentary on actual social relations.

> "He was a 'Polish gentleman of honour'" (020-131) — the quotation marks around "gentleman of honour" signal the novel's scepticism.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky treats honour not as a stable moral code but as a weapon in social warfare. Characters do not uphold honour; they deploy it. Alexei uses the language of honour to justify his worst behaviour; the General uses it to maintain social position while scheming for money; [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] uses it to wound those who claim to love her.

The novel's treatment of honour is particularly sharp in its satirisation of national stereotypes. [[russia|Russia]]n honour is [[love|passion]]ate and self-destructive; English honour ([[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]]) is reliable but humourless; Polish honour (the "gentleman of honour" at 020-131) is revealed as theatrical fraud. Honour, in *The Gambler*, is always nationalised—and therefore always relative.
