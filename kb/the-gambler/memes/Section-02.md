# The Gambler · Section 2

[002-001]“But—” the General began.

[002-002]“What really saved you was the fact that you proclaimed yourself a

[002-003]heretic and a barbarian,” remarked [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]] with a smile. “Cela

[002-004]n’était pas si bête.”

[002-005]“But is _that_ how Russian subjects ought to be treated? Why, when they

[002-006]settle here they dare not utter even a word—they are ready even to deny

[002-007]the fact that they are Russians! At all events, at my hotel in [[paris|Paris]] I

[002-008]received far more attention from the company after I had told them

[002-009]about the fracas with the sacristan. A fat Polish nobleman, who had

[002-010]been the most offensive of all who were present at the table d’hôte, at

[002-011]once went upstairs, while some of the Frenchmen were simply disgusted

[002-012]when I told them that two years ago I had encountered a man at whom, in

[002-013]1812, a French ‘hero’ fired for the mere fun of discharging his musket.

[002-014]That man was then a boy of ten and his family are still residing in

[002-015][[moscow|Moscow]].”

[002-016]“Impossible!” the Frenchman spluttered. “No French soldier would fire

[002-017]at a child!”

[002-018]“Nevertheless the incident was as I say,” I replied. “A very respected

[002-019]ex-captain told me the story, and I myself could see the scar left on

[002-020]his cheek.”

[002-021]The Frenchman then began chattering volubly, and the General supported

[002-022]him; but I recommended the former to read, for example, extracts from

[002-023]the memoirs of General Perovski, who, in 1812, was a prisoner in the

[002-024]hands of the French. Finally [[maria-philipovna|Maria Philipovna]] said something to

[002-025]interrupt the conversation. [[general|The General]] was furious with me for having

[002-026]started the altercation with the Frenchman. On the other hand, Mr.

[002-027][[mr-astley|Astley]] seemed to take great pleasure in my brush with Monsieur, and,

[002-028]rising from the table, proposed that we should go and have a drink

[002-029]together. The same afternoon, at four o’clock, I went to have my

[002-030]customary talk with [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina Alexandrovna]]; and, the talk soon extended to

[002-031]a stroll. We entered the Park, and approached the Casino, where Polina

[002-032]seated herself upon a bench near the fountain, and sent Nadia away to a

[002-033]little distance to [[gambling|play]] with some other children. Mischa also I

[002-034]dispatched to play by the fountain, and in this fashion we—that is to

[002-035]say, Polina and myself—contrived to find ourselves alone.

[002-036]Of course, we began by talking on business matters. Polina seemed

[002-037]furious when I handed her only 700 gülden, for she had thought to

[002-038]receive from Paris, as the proceeds of the pledging of her diamonds, at

[002-039]least 2000 gülden, or even more.

[002-040]“Come what may, I _must_ have money,” she said. “And get it somehow I

[002-041]will—otherwise I shall be ruined.”

[002-042]I asked her what had happened during my absence.

[002-043]“Nothing; except that two pieces of news have reached us from St.

[002-044][[st-petersburg|Petersburg]]. In the first place, my grandmother is very ill, and

[002-045]unlikely to last another couple of days. We had this from Timothy

[002-046]Petrovitch himself, and he is a reliable person. Every moment we are

[002-047]expecting to receive news of the end.”

[002-048]“All of you are on the tiptoe of expectation?” I queried.

[002-049]“Of course—all of us, and every minute of the day. For a

[002-050]year-and-a-half now we have been looking for this.”

[002-051]“Looking for it?”

[002-052]“Yes, looking for it. I am not her blood relation, you know—I am merely

[002-053]the General’s step-daughter. Yet I am certain that [[grandmother|the old lady]] has

[002-054]remembered me in her will.”

[002-055]“Yes, I believe that you _will_ come in for a good deal,” I said with

[002-056]some assurance.

[002-057]“Yes, for she is fond of me. But how come you to think so?”

[002-058]I answered this question with another one. “That Marquis of yours,” I

[002-059]said, “—is _he_ also familiar with your family secrets?”

[002-060]“And why are you yourself so interested in them?” was her retort as she

[002-061]eyed me with dry grimness.

[002-062]“Never mind. If I am not mistaken, the General has succeeded in

[002-063]borrowing money of the Marquis.”

[002-064]“It may be so.”

[002-065]“Is it likely that the Marquis would have lent the money if he had not

[002-066]known something or other about your grandmother? Did you notice, too,

[002-067]that three times during luncheon, when speaking of her, he called her

[002-068]‘La Baboulenka’?[1]. What loving, friendly behaviour, to be sure!”

[002-069][1] Dear little Grandmother.

[002-070]“Yes, that is true. As soon as ever he learnt that I was likely to

[002-071]inherit something from her he began to pay me his addresses. I thought

[002-072]you ought to know that.”

[002-073]“Then he has only just begun his courting? Why, I thought he had been

[002-074]doing so a long while!”

[002-075]“You _know_ he has not,” retorted Polina angrily. “But where on earth

[002-076]did you pick up this Englishman?” She said this after a pause.

[002-077]“I _knew_ you would ask about him!” Whereupon I told her of my previous

[002-078]encounters with Astley while travelling.

[002-079]“He is very shy,” I said, “and susceptible. Also, he is in love with

[002-080]you.”

[002-081]“Yes, he _is_ in love with me,” she replied.

[002-082]“And he is ten times richer than the Frenchman. In fact, what does the

[002-083]Frenchman possess? To me it seems at least doubtful that he possesses

[002-084]anything at all.”

[002-085]“Oh, no, there is no doubt about it. He does possess some château or

[002-086]other. Last night the General told me that for certain. _Now_ are you

[002-087]satisfied?”

[002-088]“Nevertheless, in your place I should marry the Englishman.”

[002-089]“And why?” asked Polina.

[002-090]“Because, though the Frenchman is the handsomer of the two, he is also

[002-091]the baser; whereas the Englishman is not only a man of honour, but ten

[002-092]times the wealthier of the pair.”

[002-093]“Yes? But then the Frenchman is a marquis, and the cleverer of the

[002-094]two,” remarked Polina imperturbably.

[002-095]“Is that so?” I repeated.

[002-096]“Yes; absolutely.”

[002-097]Polina was not at all pleased at my questions; I could see that she was

[002-098]doing her best to irritate me with the brusquerie of her answers. But I

[002-099]took no notice of this.

[002-100]“It amuses me to see you grow angry,” she continued. “However, inasmuch

[002-101]as I allow you to indulge in these questions and conjectures, you ought

[002-102]to pay me something for the privilege.”

[002-103]“I consider that I have a perfect right to put these questions to you,”

[002-104]was my calm retort; “for the reason that I am ready to pay for them,

[002-105]and also care little what becomes of me.”

[002-106]Polina giggled.

[002-107]“Last time you told me—when on the Shlangenberg—that at a word from me

[002-108]you would be ready to jump down a thousand feet into the abyss. Some

[002-109]day I may remind you of that saying, in order to see if you will be as

[002-110]good as your word. Yes, you may depend upon it that I shall do so. I

[002-111]hate you because I have allowed you to go to such lengths, and I also

[002-112]hate you and still more—because you are so necessary to me. For the

[002-113]time being I want you, so I must keep you.”

[002-114]Then she made a movement to rise. Her tone had sounded very angry.

[002-115]Indeed, of late her talks with me had invariably ended on a note of

[002-116]temper and irritation—yes, of real temper.

[002-117]“May I ask you who is this [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]?” I inquired (since I did not

[002-118]wish Polina to depart without an explanation).

[002-119]“You _know_ who she is—just Mlle. Blanche. Nothing further has

[002-120]transpired. Probably she will soon be Madame General—that is to say, if

[002-121]the rumours that Grandmamma is nearing her end should prove true. Mlle.

[002-122]Blanche, with her mother and her cousin, the Marquis, know very well

[002-123]that, as things now stand, we are ruined.”

[002-124]“And is the General at last in love?”

[002-125]“That has nothing to do with it. Listen to me. Take these 700 florins,

[002-126]and go and play roulette with them. Win as much for me as you can, for

[002-127]I am badly in need of money.”

[002-128]So saying, she called Nadia back to her side, and entered the Casino,

[002-129]where she joined the rest of our party. For myself, I took, in musing

[002-130]astonishment, the first path to the left. Something had seemed to

[002-131]strike my brain when she told me to go and play roulette. Strangely

[002-132]enough, that something had also seemed to make me hesitate, and to set

[002-133]me analysing my feelings with regard to her. In fact, during the two

[002-134]weeks of my absence I had felt far more at my ease than I did now, on

[002-135]the day of my return; although, while travelling, I had moped like an

[002-136]imbecile, rushed about like a man in a fever, and actually beheld her

[002-137]in my dreams. Indeed, on one occasion (this happened in [[switzerland|Switzerland]],

[002-138]when I was asleep in the train) I had spoken aloud to her, and set all

[002-139]my fellow-travellers laughing. Again, therefore, I put to myself the

[002-140]question: “Do I, or do I not love her?” and again I could return myself

[002-141]no answer or, rather, for the hundredth time I told myself that I

[002-142]detested her. Yes, I detested her; there were moments (more especially

[002-143]at the close of our talks together) when I would gladly have given half

[002-144]my life to have strangled her! I swear that, had there, at such

[002-145]moments, been a sharp knife ready to my hand, I would have seized that

[002-146]knife with pleasure, and plunged it into her breast. Yet I also swear

[002-147]that if, on the Shlangenberg, she had _really_ said to me, “Leap into

[002-148]that abyss,” I should have leapt into it, and with equal pleasure. Yes,

[002-149]this I knew well. One way or the other, the thing must soon be ended.

[002-150]She, too, knew it in some curious way; the thought that I was fully

[002-151]conscious of her inaccessibility, and of the impossibility of my ever

[002-152]realising my dreams, afforded her, I am certain, the keenest possible

[002-153]pleasure. Otherwise, is it likely that she, the cautious and clever

[002-154]woman that she was, would have indulged in this familiarity and

[002-155]openness with me? Hitherto (I concluded) she had looked upon me in the

[002-156]same light that the old Empress did upon her servant—the Empress who

[002-157]hesitated not to unrobe herself before her slave, since she did not

[002-158]account a slave a man. Yes, often Polina must have taken me for

[002-159]something less than a man!”

[002-160]Still, she had charged me with a commission—to win what I could at

[002-161]roulette. Yet all the time I could not help wondering _why_ it was so

[002-162]necessary for her to win something, and what new schemes could have

[002-163]sprung to birth in her ever-fertile brain. A host of new and unknown

[002-164]factors seemed to have arisen during the last two weeks. Well, it

[002-165]behoved me to divine them, and to probe them, and that as soon as

[002-166]possible. Yet not now: at the present moment I must repair to the

[002-167]roulette-table.

[002-168]II

[002-169]I confess I did not like it. Although I had made up my mind to play, I

[002-170]felt averse to doing so on behalf of some one else. In fact, it almost

[002-171]upset my balance, and I entered [[casino|the gaming rooms]] with an angry feeling

[002-172]at my heart. At first glance the scene irritated me. Never at any time

[002-173]have I been able to bear the flunkeyishness which one meets in the

[002-174]Press of the world at large, but more especially in that of [[russia|Russia]],

[002-175]where, almost every evening, journalists write on two subjects in

[002-176]particular—namely, on the splendour and luxury of the casinos to be

[002-177]found in the Rhenish towns, and on the heaps of gold which are daily to

[002-178]be seen lying on their tables. Those journalists are not paid for doing

[002-179]so: they write thus merely out of a spirit of disinterested

[002-180]complaisance. For there is nothing splendid about the establishments in

[002-181]question; and, not only are there no heaps of gold to be seen lying on

[002-182]their tables, but also there is very little money to be seen at all. Of

[002-183]course, during the season, _some_ madman or another may make his

[002-184]appearance—generally an Englishman, or an Asiatic, or a Turk—and (as

[002-185]had happened during the summer of which I write) win or lose a great

[002-186]deal; but, as regards the rest of the crowd, it plays only for petty

[002-187]gülden, and seldom does much [[fortune|wealth]] figure on the board.
