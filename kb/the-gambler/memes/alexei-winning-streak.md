# Alexei's Winning Streak

[[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexei Ivanovitch]]'s winning streak at roulette is the novel's most concentrated study of fortune's operation. Beginning as a desperate gambler [[gambling|play]]ing for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], Alexei experiences a run of [[chance|luck]]—"fortune seemed to be at my back" (023-166)—that transforms him from impoverished tutor to possessor of thousands of francs. The winning streak reorders his relationships, his self-conception, and his sense of what is possible—and its inevitable reversal is the novel's starkest demonstration that roulette's mathematics guarantee ruin.

## Role in the Novel

The winning streak functions as the novel's test of character. Given sudden [[fortune|wealth]], Alexei does not pay his debts, secure his future, or improve his position. Instead, he attempts to give the money to [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]—and when she refuses it, he continues gambling until it is lost. The winning streak reveals that Alexei's problem is not poverty but something deeper: an inability to accept fortune when it comes, a [[obsession|compulsion]] to return to [[roulette|the wheel]] until fortune is withdrawn.

## Key Moments

> "Why thirty-five times, when zero so often turns up?" (015-117) — from the Grandmother's mouth, the question that haunts every winning streak.

> "But, as ever, fortune seemed to be at my back" (023-166) — the gambler's delusion, fortune personified as companion.

## The Psychology of the Streak

Alexei's winning streak is not merely a plot event but a psychological study in how sudden [[fortune|wealth]] transforms self-perception. Before the streak, Alexei is a tutor—poor, dependent, and acutely aware of his social inferiority. During the streak, he becomes someone else: a man who commands attention, who attracts the deference of croupiers and the admiration of onlookers. The transformation is intoxicating precisely because it is unearned—the [[roulette|roulette]] wheel, not effort or merit, has remade him.

This is the streak's most dangerous gift: it teaches Alexei that identity is contingent, that the self can be rewritten by a run of [[luck|luck]]. The lesson is false—[[chance|chance]] gives and takes with equal indifference—but it is a lesson the gambler never unlearns. Every subsequent loss is interpreted not as the restoration of normal probability but as a temporary reversal of fortune, a setback that the next streak will correct.

## The Streak as Social Disruption

The winning streak does not merely enrich Alexei; it disrupts the novel's entire social order. During the streak, the hierarchies that structure life at [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] are temporarily suspended. Alexei, the tutor, becomes the centre of attention; the General, who has treated him with contempt, must reckon with his sudden power. Money, in this world, is the only social fact, and the streak proves that money can be produced from nothing—from the spin of a wheel, from the whim of [[fortune|fortune]].

This disruption is, however, temporary. The streak ends, the money is lost, and the old hierarchies reassert themselves. The novel's social critique is embedded in this rhythm: in a world where wealth is produced by gambling rather than labour, social position is inherently unstable. Everyone at [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] is one spin away from either ruin or transformation.
