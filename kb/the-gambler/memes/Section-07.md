# The Gambler · Section 7

[007-001]“What stupid rubbish!” she cried.

[007-002]“I care not whether it be wise or stupid,” I cried in return. “I only

[007-003]know that in your presence I must speak, speak, speak. Therefore, I am

[007-004]speaking. I lose all conceit when I am with you, and everything ceases

[007-005]to matter.”

[007-006]“Why should I have wanted you to leap from the Shlangenberg?” she said

[007-007]drily, and (I think) with wilful offensiveness. “_That_ would have been

[007-008]of no use to me.”

[007-009]“Splendid!” I shouted. “I know well that you must have used the words

[007-010]‘of no use’ in order to crush me. _I_ can see through you. ‘Of no use,’

[007-011]did you say? Why, to give pleasure is _always_ of use; and, as for

[007-012]barbarous, unlimited power—even if it be only over a fly—why, it is a

[007-013]kind of luxury. Man is a despot by nature, and loves to torture. You,

[007-014]in particular, love to do so.”

[007-015]I remember that at this moment she looked at me in a peculiar way. The

[007-016]fact is that my face must have been expressing all the maze of

[007-017]senseless, gross sensations which were seething within me. To this day

[007-018]I can remember, word for word, the conversation as I have written it

[007-019]down. My eyes were suffused with blood, and the foam had caked itself

[007-020]on my lips. Also, on my honour I swear that, had she bidden me cast

[007-021]myself from the summit of the Shlangenberg, I should have done it. Yes,

[007-022]had she bidden me in jest, or only in contempt and with a spit in my

[007-023]face, I should have cast myself down.

[007-024]“Oh no! Why so? I believe you,” she said, but in such a manner—in the

[007-025]manner of which, at times, she was a mistress—and with such a note of

[007-026][[contempt|disdain]] and viperish arrogance in her tone, that God knows I could have

[007-027]killed her.

[007-028]Yes, at that moment she stood in peril. I had not lied to her about

[007-029]that.

[007-030]“Surely you are not a coward?” suddenly she asked me.

[007-031]“I do not know,” I replied. “Perhaps I am, but I do not know. I have

[007-032]long given up thinking about such things.”

[007-033]“If I said to you, ‘Kill that man,’ would you kill him?”

[007-034]“Whom?”

[007-035]“Whomsoever I wish?”

[007-036]“The Frenchman?”

[007-037]“Do not ask me questions; return me answers. I repeat, whomsoever I

[007-038]wish? I desire to see if you were speaking seriously just now.”

[007-039]She awaited my reply with such gravity and impatience that I found the

[007-040]situation unpleasant.

[007-041]“Do _you_, rather, tell me,” I said, “what is going on here? Why do you

[007-042]seem half-afraid of me? I can see for myself what is wrong. You are the

[007-043]step-daughter of a ruined and insensate man who is smitten with love

[007-044]for this devil of a Blanche. And there is this Frenchman, too, with his

[007-045]mysterious influence over you. Yet, you actually ask me such a

[007-046]question! If you do not tell me how things stand, I shall have to put

[007-047]in my oar and do something. Are you ashamed to be frank with me? Are

[007-048]you shy of me?”

[007-049]“I am not going to talk to you on that subject. I have asked you a

[007-050]question, and am waiting for an answer.”

[007-051]“Well, then—I will kill whomsoever you wish,” I said. “But are you

[007-052]_really_ going to bid me do such deeds?”

[007-053]“Why should you think that I am going to let you off? I shall bid you

[007-054]do it, or else renounce me. Could you ever do the latter? No, you know

[007-055]that you couldn’t. You would first kill whom I had bidden you, and then

[007-056]kill _me_ for having dared to send you away!”

[007-057]Something seemed to strike upon my brain as I heard these words. Of

[007-058]course, at the time I took them half in jest and half as a challenge;

[007-059]yet, she had spoken them with great seriousness. I felt thunderstruck

[007-060]that she should so express herself, that she should assert such a right

[007-061]over me, that she should assume such authority and say outright:

[007-062]“Either you kill whom I bid you, or I will have nothing more to do with

[007-063]you.” Indeed, in what she had said there was something so cynical and

[007-064]unveiled as to pass all bounds. For how could she ever regard me as the

[007-065]same after the killing was done? This was more than slavery and

[007-066]abasement; it was sufficient to bring a man back to his right senses.

[007-067]Yet, despite the outrageous improbability of our conversation, my heart

[007-068]shook within me.

[007-069]Suddenly, she burst out laughing. We were seated on a bench near the

[007-070]spot where the children were playing—just opposite the point in the

[007-071]alley-way before the Casino where the carriages drew up in order to set

[007-072]down their occupants.

[007-073]“Do you see that fat Baroness?” she cried. “It is the Baroness

[007-074]Burmergelm. She arrived three days ago. Just look at her husband—that

[007-075]tall, wizened Prussian there, with the stick in his hand. Do you

[007-076]remember how he stared at us the other day? Well, go to the Baroness,

[007-077]take off your hat to her, and say something in French.”

[007-078]“Why?”

[007-079]“Because you have sworn that you would leap from the Shlangenberg for

[007-080]my sake, and that you would kill any one whom I might bid you kill.

[007-081]Well, instead of such murders and tragedies, I wish only for a good

[007-082]laugh. Go without answering me, and let me see [[baron-burmergelm|the Baron]] give you a

[007-083]sound thrashing with his stick.”

[007-084]“Then you throw me out a challenge?—you think that I will not do it?”

[007-085]“Yes, I do challenge you. Go, for such is my will.”

[007-086]“Then I _will_ go, however mad be your fancy. Only, look here: shall

[007-087]you not be doing the General a great disservice, as well as, through

[007-088]him, a great disservice to yourself? It is not about myself I am

[007-089]worrying—it is about you and the General. Why, for a mere fancy, should

[007-090]I go and insult a woman?”

[007-091]“Ah! Then I can see that you are only a trifler,” she said

[007-092]contemptuously. “Your eyes are swimming with blood—but only because you

[007-093]have drunk a little too much at luncheon. Do I not know that what I

[007-094]have asked you to do is foolish and wrong, and that the General will be

[007-095]angry about it? But I want to have a good laugh, all the same. I want

[007-096]that, and nothing else. Why should you insult a woman, indeed? Well,

[007-097]you will be given a sound thrashing for so doing.”

[007-098]I turned away, and went silently to do her bidding. Of course the thing

[007-099]was folly, but I could not get out of it. I remember that, as I

[007-100]approached the Baroness, I felt as excited as a schoolboy. I was in a

[007-101]frenzy, as though I were drunk.

[007-102]VI

[007-103]Two days have passed since that day of lunacy. What a noise and a fuss

[007-104]and a chattering and an uproar there was! And what a welter of

[007-105]unseemliness and disorder and stupidity and bad manners! And _I_ the

[007-106]cause of it all! Yet part of the scene was also ridiculous—at all

[007-107]events to myself it was so. I am not quite sure what was the matter

[007-108]with me—whether I was merely stupefied or whether I purposely broke

[007-109]loose and ran amok. At times my mind seems all confused; while at other

[007-110]times I seem almost to be back in my childhood, at the school desk, and

[007-111]to have done the deed simply out of mischief.

[007-112]It all came of [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]—yes, of Polina. But for her, there might never

[007-113]have been a fracas. Or perhaps I did the deed in a fit of despair

[007-114](though it may be foolish of me to think so)? What there is so

[007-115]attractive about her I cannot think. Yet there _is_ something

[007-116]attractive about her—something passing fair, it would seem. Others

[007-117]besides myself she has driven to distraction. She is tall and straight,

[007-118]and very slim. Her body looks as though it could be tied into a knot,

[007-119]or bent double, like a cord. The imprint of her foot is long and

[007-120]narrow. It is, a maddening imprint—yes, simply a maddening one! And her

[007-121]hair has a reddish tint about it, and her eyes are like cat’s

[007-122]eyes—though able also to glance with proud, disdainful mien. On the

[007-123]evening of my first arrival, four months ago, I remember that she was

[007-124]sitting and holding an animated conversation with De Griers in the

[007-125]salon. And the way in which she looked at him was such that later, when

[007-126]I retired to my own room upstairs, I kept fancying that she had smitten

[007-127]him in the face—that she had smitten him right on the cheek, so

[007-128]peculiar had been her look as she stood confronting him. Ever since

[007-129]that evening I have loved her.

[007-130]But to my tale.

[007-131]I stepped from the path into the carriage-way, and took my stand in the

[007-132]middle of it. There I awaited the Baron and the Baroness. When they

[007-133]were but a few paces distant from me I took off my hat, and bowed.

[007-134]I remember that the Baroness was clad in a voluminous silk dress, pale

[007-135]grey in colour, and adorned with flounces and a crinoline and train.

[007-136]Also, she was short and inordinately stout, while her gross, flabby

[007-137]chin completely concealed her neck. Her face was purple, and the little

[007-138]eyes in it had an impudent, malicious expression. Yet she walked as

[007-139]though she were conferring a favour upon everybody by so doing. As for

[007-140]the Baron, he was tall, wizened, bony-faced after the German fashion,

[007-141]spectacled, and, apparently, about forty-five years of age. Also, he

[007-142]had legs which seemed to begin almost at his chest—or, rather, at his

[007-143]chin! Yet, for all his air of peacock-like conceit, his clothes sagged

[007-144]a little, and his face wore a sheepish air which might have passed for

[007-145]profundity.

[007-146]These details I noted within a space of a few seconds.

[007-147]At first my bow and the fact that I had my hat in my hand barely caught

[007-148]their attention. The Baron only scowled a little, and the Baroness

[007-149]swept straight on.

[007-150]“Madame la Baronne,” said I, loudly and distinctly—embroidering each

[007-151]word, as it were—“j’ai l’honneur d’être votre esclave.”

[007-152]Then I bowed again, put on my hat, and walked past the Baron with a

[007-153]rude smile on my face.

[007-154]Polina had ordered me merely to take off my hat: the bow and the

[007-155]general effrontery were of my own invention. God knows what instigated

[007-156]me to perpetrate the outrage! In my frenzy I felt as though I were

[007-157]walking on air.

[007-158]“Hein!” ejaculated—or, rather, growled—the Baron as he turned towards

[007-159]me in angry surprise.

[007-160]I too turned round, and stood waiting in pseudo-courteous expectation.

[007-161]Yet still I wore on my face an impudent smile as I gazed at him. He

[007-162]seemed to hesitate, and his brows contracted to their utmost limits.

[007-163]Every moment his visage was growing darker. The Baroness also turned in

[007-164]my direction, and gazed at me in wrathful perplexity, while some of the

[007-165]passers-by also began to stare at us, and others of them halted

[007-166]outright.

[007-167]“Hein!” the Baron vociferated again, with a redoubled growl and a note

[007-168]of growing wrath in his voice.

[007-169]“Ja wohl!” I replied, still looking him in the eyes.

[007-170]“Sind Sie rasend?” he exclaimed, brandishing his stick, and,

[007-171]apparently, beginning to feel nervous. Perhaps it was my costume which

[007-172]intimidated him, for I was well and fashionably dressed, after the

[007-173]manner of a man who belongs to indisputably good society.

[007-174]“Ja wo-o-ohl!” cried I again with all my might with a longdrawn rolling

[007-175]of the “ohl” sound after the fashion of the Berliners (who constantly

[007-176]use the phrase “Ja wohl!” in conversation, and more or less prolong the

[007-177]syllable “ohl” according as they desire to express different shades of

[007-178]meaning or of mood).

[007-179]At this the Baron and the Baroness faced sharply about, and almost fled

[007-180]in their alarm. Some of the bystanders gave vent to excited

[007-181]exclamations, and others remained staring at me in astonishment. But I

[007-182]do not remember the details very well.
