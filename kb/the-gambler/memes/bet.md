# Bet

The [[bet]] is the atomic act of [[gambling]] — the moment when money leaves the gambler's hand and enters the field of [[probability|chance]]. In *The Gambler*, Dostoyevsky treats the bet not as a neutral transaction but as a revelation of character: how much one stakes, on what, and with what demeanour exposes everything the gambler would prefer to conceal. Class, temperament, [[despair|desperation]], delusion — all are visible in the moment the [[stake]] is placed.

## Class and the Stake

From the novel's opening pages, Dostoyevsky makes the bet a marker of social position:

> For instance, a [[gentleman]] may stake, say, five or ten louis d'or; but, he must do this simply for the [[love]] of the game. (003-054, 003-056)

The distinction is not between large and small stakes but between proper and improper attitudes toward them. A gentleman bets "solely out of curiosity" (003-062), never from [[despair|desperation]] or greed. The rule is class-based hypocrisy: the same physical act — placing money on [[gaming-table|the table]] — is gentlemanly when done with indifference and vulgar when done with need. [[Alexei Ivanovitch]], a tutor with no independent income, can never be a "gentleman" gambler; his every bet is tainted by the very need that the social code demands he conceal.

## The Escalation Pattern

The novel's betting sequences follow a distinctive escalation rhythm:

| Phase | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Small initial stake | Testing, almost timid | Alexei's first 50 [[gulden|gülden]] (003-139) |
| Doubling | Confidence building | "I staked the 100 gülden just where they lay" (003-140) |
| Maximum stake | Abandoning restraint | 4,000 gülden — "the maximum stake allowed by the rules" (005-065) |
| [[ruin|Ruin]] or withdrawal | The inevitable outcome | [[loss|Loss]] or the discipline to stop |

The escalation is not driven by strategy but by physiology — the bet feeds a state of excitation that demands larger and larger doses. When Alexei describes how he "staked the whole sum, and a[[winning|gain]] the red turned up" (003-141), the repetition of "a[[winning|gain]]" captures the trance-like quality of the betting sequence: each win erases the memory of risk, each successive bet feels as certain as the last.

## The Anonymous Gambler

One of the novel's most remarkable passages concerns a gambler who bets without [[probability|calculation]] — "just what came to his hand, as he kept winning and winning, and raking and raking in his [[winning|gain]]s" (015-049). This gambler bets mechanically, "dead to all besides" (015-060), and his betting becomes a [[spectacle]] that draws a crowd. The bet here is no longer an individual decision but a performance — the gambler has become a machine for placing stakes, and the crowd watches not the [[roulette]] wheel but the act of betting itself.

## Betting as Self-Exposure

Every bet in *The Gambler* exposes something about the bettor that social performance would normally conceal. The [[grandmother]]'s bets reveal her imperiousness — she stakes large sums not from recklessness but from the conviction that she, unlike ordinary people, cannot lose. The [[General]]'s avoidance of betting reveals his cowardice — he "never once staked in person" (005-056), preferring to delegate his gambling to others so he can pretend he is not gambling at all.

The bet is the moment when the mask slips. What Dostoyevsky understands is that the size of the stake is less revealing than the manner of placing it: whether the hand trembles, whether the gambler hesitates, whether he watches [[roulette|the wheel]] or stares at the table.
