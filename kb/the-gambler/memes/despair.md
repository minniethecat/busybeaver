# Despair

[[despair]] in Dostoyevsky's *The Gambler* is the pervasive psychological state that underlies the novel's gambling [[obsession|mania]]. Characters do not simply lose [[money]] at roulette—they are driven to [[casino|the tables]] by pre-existing despair, and the [[losing|loss]]es deepen it into utter hopelessness. From the General's romantic desperation (004-115) to Alexei Ivanovich's existential wretchedness (029-020), despair is the emotional currency that fuels the novel's darkest moments.

## Definition

In the novel, despair is not melancholy or sadness but a consuming spi[[superstition|ritual]] crisis. It manifests as the conviction that one's situation is irremediable—a state that paradoxically drives characters toward the casino, where the possibility of sudden reversal offers the only escape from hopelessness. Dostoyevsky presents despair as a peculiarly [[russia|Russia]]n susceptibility: a [[love|passion]]ate surrender to extremity that distinguishes [[russia|Russia]]n characters from the measured composure of figures like [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]] (007-113).

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Despair functions as both cause and consequence of gambling in the novel. Alexei [[gambling|play]]s roulette initially out of desperate love for [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] (007-113), but the act of gambling creates its own despair when [[losing|loss]]es accumulate. [[general|The General]]'s despair at falling in love at his age (004-115) makes him vulnerable to ruinous financial [[risk|speculation]]. Madame Barberini's despair (011-075) adds another layer to the novel's interconnected web of hopelessness and greed. Each character's despair becomes the emotional precondition for reckless action.

### Key Passages

> "The poor General was in despair! To fall in love at fifty-five..." (004-115) — the General's romantic desperation sets the emotional tone for the novel's opening.

> "Perhaps I did the deed in a fit of despair" (007-113) — Alexei's own admission that despair, not [[calculation]], drives his most consequential actions.

> "He wound up in a despairing voice" (008-161) — despair as a mode of speech, saturating all communication in the novel.

> "At last, in utter despair, I was on my way home..." (017-048) — Alexei's low point following a [[loss|gambling loss]], despair as physical and spiritual collapse.

> "Or had it come of despair over her decision to come to [[roulettenberg]]?" (025-019) — Polina's motivations interpreted through the lens of despair.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky treats despair as something more than an emotion—it is a spi[[superstition|ritual]] condition that reveals character. Where a Tolstoyan hero might find moral clarity in suffering, Dostoyevsky's characters are energised by despair: it pushes them toward ever more extreme behaviour rather than toward repentance. Alexei's final declaration—"you have said that last in despair, and purposely. But there is no need for despair!" (029-020)—rings hollow precisely because despair has been the novel's true motive force from the beginning.

The biographical dimension is significant. Dostoyevsky wrote *The Gambler* while himself in financial despair, his roulette addiction having consumed his earnings and his publisher's deadline threatening to strip him of his literary rights. The novel's despair is not observed but lived.
