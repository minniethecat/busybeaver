# Servant

**Servant** in *The Gambler* designates both a literal social role — the attendants, lackeys, and maids who populate the margins of [[roulettenberg]]'s aristocratic world — and a psychological condition of subordination that afflicts even the novel's most privileged characters.

## The Servant Class

The novel's servant characters form a quiet counterpoint to the main drama. [[potapitch|Potapitch]], the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s elderly attendant, serves with a dignity that his employers lack. He observes the gambling catastrophe with loyal concern — "he offered himself less as a servant than as an _entertainer_" [020-129] — revealing a humanity that the aristocrats have sacrificed to their [[obsession|obsessions]]. The hotel servants, lacqueys, and casino attendants constitute a silent audience before whom the degradations of the powerful are performed.

## Servitude as Social Condition

Dostoyevsky extends the category of servitude beyond the servant class. Alexei Ivanovitch, employed as a tutor in the [[general|General]]'s household, occupies an ambiguous position: neither family nor servant, he is "a kind of servant" [027-091] without the clarity of the role. The General himself is revealed to be a servant to his creditors, to [[mlle-blanche|Mlle Blanche]], to his own social ambitions. The [[marquis-de-griers|Marquis des Grieux]], for all his aristocratic airs, serves the ruthless logic of [[money]].

## The Psychology of Servitude

Alexei is acutely sensitive to the humiliations of his position. When the General treats him "as though I were a mere servant" [009-104], the wound is not to his social standing but to his sense of self. His gambling passion is partly a rebellion against servitude — at the [[roulette]] table, he is equal to any man — and partly its deepest expression, for the gambler is ultimately a servant to his own [[desire]]. The novel suggests that in a world governed by [[money]] and [[chance]], everyone is someone's servant, and the only question is whose.

## Servitude and Freedom

The novel poses an unsettling question: can the gambler ever be free? Alexei enters the casino believing that [[winning]] will liberate him from the servitude of his tutorial position. A single successful evening at the [[roulette]] table could, in theory, buy his independence from the [[general|General]]'s household. Yet Dostoyevsky systematically undermines this logic. The gambler who wins becomes a servant to [[luck]]; the gambler who loses becomes a servant to [[debt]]; the gambler who breaks even becomes a servant to the hope of the next win. There is no exit. The casino, far from being a space of freedom from social hierarchy, reproduces servitude in a purer and more absolute form: the gambler serves not a master but a mechanism.

## Dostoyevsky and the Servant Question

The treatment of servitude in *The Gambler* reflects the broader Russian social debates of the 1860s. The emancipation of the serfs in 1861 had transformed the legal status of servitude while leaving its psychological structures largely intact. Dostoyevsky, who had himself experienced the radical reversal of fortune — from literary celebrity to convict, from gentleman to beggar — understood servitude as something that survived its formal abolition. The novel's servant theme captures this moment of historical transition: old hierarchies persist in new forms, and freedom remains an aspiration rather than an achievement.
