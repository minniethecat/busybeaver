# Trente et Quarante

Trente et quarante (thirty and forty), sometimes called rouge et noir (red and black), is the second casino game that appears in Dostoyevsky's *The Gambler* alongside roulette. While roulette dominates the novel's gambling scenes, trente et quarante provides an alternative space—the table where the more experienced [[gambling|play]]ers gather and where, as [[alexei-ivanovitch|the narrator]] notes early on, one can observe "the [[gambling|play]] of [[probability|chance]]s" (003-065) with greater detachment than at the [[roulette|roulette wheel]].

## Definition

Trente et quarante is a card game [[gambling|play]]ed with six full decks of fifty-two cards. The dealer deals two rows representing black and red; the row whose total is closest to thirty-one wins. Players bet on which colour will win, and the house takes a percentage of each half-won bet. In the novel, [[roulette|the game]] is referenced alongside roulette as part of the casino's repertoire: "roulette, and upon trente et quarante" (003-065).

[[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] is notably associated with trente et quarante, first [[gambling|play]]ing it at the casino (011-069), suggesting a preference for the more socially restrained atmosphere of the card table over the hysteria of the [[roulette|roulette wheel]]. The game's room is described as a distinct space from the roulette tables (014-172).

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Trente et quarante serves as a structural counterpoint to roulette. Where roulette is [[roulette|the game]] of the [[russia|Russia]]n characters—emotional, chaotic, identity-consuming—trente et quarante represents the more composed, socially performative gambling of the French visitors. Alexei's crossing "to where trente et quarante was set out" (023-154) marks a transitional moment, a shift from one mode of gambling to another.

The game's presence also establishes the casino's comprehensiveness: [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] is not merely a roulette town but a full gambling destination. The variety of games reinforces the novel's sense of a world entirely organized around [[gambling|wagering]].

### Key Passages

> "...upon roulette, and upon trente et quarante..." (003-065) — the novel's first mention, establishing the casino's dual gambling options.

> "At first Mlle. Blanche played 'trente et quarante'" (011-069) — associating the game with a specific character's gambling style.

> "...of the room where the trente-et-quarante table was set out" (014-172) — the game's separate spatial domain within the casino.

> "I crossed to where trente et quarante was set out" (023-154) — Alexei's movement from roulette to the card table, a pivotal gambling moment.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's inclusion of trente et quarante reflects his first-hand knowledge of European casinos. His own gambling career included both roulette and card games, and the novel's specificity about casino layout and game mechanics is drawn from experience. The game serves as a measure of character: those who [[gambling|play]] trente et quarante ([[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]], the more sophisticated gamblers) are in a different social and psychological category from the roulette-obsessed [[russia|Russia]]ns.

The card game's slower pace and different [[chance|probability]] structure offer an implicit critique of roulette's frenetic rhythm. Where roulette produces ecstasy and despair in rapid alternation, trente et quarante permits a more deliberate relationship to [[probability|chance]]—though it is no less ruinous in the long run.

## Rules and Variants

The game is [[gambling|play]]ed with six packs of fifty-two cards. The banker deals one row for black and one for red. The row whose pips total nearer to thirty-one wins. Ties (both rows at thirty-one) go to the house. Players may bet on black, red, couleur (the colour of the first card dealt), or inverse (its opposite). The house edge varies by bet type but is always present—like roulette's zero, the mathematics of trente et quarante guarantee the casino's eventual victory.
