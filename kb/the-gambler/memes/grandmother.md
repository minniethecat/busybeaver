# Grandmother

The [[Grandmother]] — Antonida Vasilievna Tarasyevitchev — is the novel's agent of chaos and its most vivid comic creation. Her unexpected arrival in [[Roulettenberg]], when she was presumed to be dying in [[moscow|Moscow]] and her fortune therefore available for division, collapses the [[General]]'s schemes in a single stroke. Her subsequent [[gambling]] spree at the [[Casino]] — [[loss|losing]] a fortune at [[roulette]] over several days — is the novel's comic and tragic masterpiece, and one of the most celebrated set-pieces in all of Dostoyevsky's fiction.

## Role in the Novel

The Grandmother functions as both a deus ex machina and its parody. Her arrival changes everything: the [[General]]'s inheritance scheme collapses, [[Marquis de Griers]]'s [[probability|calculation]]s are disrupted, and [[mlle-blanche|Mlle Blanche]]'s marriage plans evaporate. But she is no mere plot device. Dostoyevsky gives her a full voice — imperious, comic, and unexpectedly moving — that makes her the novel's most vivid minor character.

Her relationship with [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] is the novel's most straightforwardly affectionate bond. She trusts him, prefers his company to her relatives', and treats him as a genuine confidant in a household of mutual suspicion. In a novel structured around emotional manipulation and social [[probability|calculation]], the Grandmother's directness is a relief — and a rebuke to everyone around her.

> "The cause of the sensation — the Grandmother — was being borne..." (012-112)

Her entrance, announced by the above line, is one of literature's great theatrical arrivals: the woman who should be dead arrives instead at [[roulettenberg|the spa town]] where her fortune is being plotted a[[winning|gain]]st, carried in a chair like a monarch.

## Key Scenes

1. **The Arrival** — "The cause of [[grandmothers-arrival|the sensation]] — the Grandmother — was being borne..." (012-112). A theatrical entrance that reshapes the novel's plot within a single paragraph.

2. **The Procession** — "The Grandmother was borne through them in tri..." (012-135). Her movement through the hotel lobby, a parody of royal progress, observed by the terrified household.

3. **The Gaze** — "The Grandmother stared at him silently and wi..." (012-152). Her withering stare, the novel's most effective weapon of social destruction, deployed a[[winning|gain]]st [[Marquis de Griers]].

4. **The [[gambling|Gambling]]** — Her sustained session at the [[Casino]] that occupies the novel's centre ([[grandmothers-gambling]]), [[loss|losing]] "a large sum, or, maybe, her whole fortune" (017-099).

## Relationships

- [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] — her favourite, her escort, her gambling confidant; the one person she treats without suspicion
- [[General]] — her grandson, terrified of her and dependent on her fortune; she treats him with open contempt
- [[Polina Alexandrovna]] — her great-granddaughter, whose precarious position she inadvertently worsens
- [[Marquis de Griers]] — [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]] whose inheritance schemes she destroys simply by being alive
- [[mlle-blanche|Mlle Blanche]] — the would-be bride whose hopes of marrying the [[General]] collapse with the inheritance

## Character Analysis

### Personality and Traits

The Grandmother is defined by her refusal to behave as others expect:

| Expectation | Her Response |
|-------------|-------------|
| She should be dying in [[moscow|Moscow]] | She arrives in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] |
| She should be frail and bedridden | She demands to see the [[roulette]] tables |
| She should be prudent with money | She gambles away her fortune |
| She should defer to male authority | She commands everyone around her |

Her gambling is not calculated but [[love|passion]]ate — an assertion of vitality a[[winning|gain]]st the death that her relatives have been counting on. The novel makes clear that what drives her to [[casino|the tables]] is not greed but the refusal to be managed: "[[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]], come and see me whenever you like" (014-028).

Her imperiousness — "the Grandmother stared at him silently and wi..." (012-152) — is that of a woman who has outlived everyone who could tell her what to do. She has no patience for the [[General]]'s pretensions, [[Marquis de Griers]]'s manipulations, or the conventions that constrain everyone else in the hotel.

### Arc and Development

The Grandmother's arc is compressed into a few days but encompasses a lifetime's pattern: arrival, assertion, excess, collapse. Her [[grandmothers-gambling|gambling spree]] follows the classic gambler's trajectory — initial wins, growing confidence, catastrophic reversal — compressed from years into hours. She leaves [[Roulettenberg]] diminished but unbowed, having destroyed her fortune and the household's schemes with equal indifference.

> "Once she has acquired the taste for gambling, she will never leave the tables" (017-099)

This line, spoken as observation rather than judgment, captures the novel's understanding of the gambling [[obsession|compulsion]]: it is not a moral failing but an appetite, once awakened, that reorganises all other priorities.

## Critical Perspectives

The Grandmother has been read as Dostoyevsky's comic revenge on the [[russia|Russia]]n aristocracy: an old woman whose appetite for [[roulette]] turns out to be as destructive as any revolutionary. Her [[gambling]] is not a moral failing but a liberation — the first autonomous action she has taken in decades, and therefore the most catastrophic.

Critics have noted parallels between the Grandmother's gambling and Dostoyevsky's own: the sudden onset, the conviction of special insight, the refusal to stop even as ruin approaches. Where the author wrote *The Gambler* to pay off his own [[debt|debts]], the Grandmother gambles away the fortune that represents her family's expectations — and in doing so, achieves a kind of Freedom more complete than any inheritance could provide.
