# The Gambler · Section 28

[028-001]“I must behave altogether differently,” she confided to me with a

[028-002]serious air. “Yet, mark you, there is a tiresome circumstance of which

[028-003]I had never before thought—which is, how best to pronounce my new

[028-004]family name. Zagorianski, Zagozianski, [[maria-philipovna|Madame la Générale]] de Sago,

[028-005]Madame la Générale de Fourteen Consonants—oh these infernal Russian

[028-006]names! The _last_ of them would be the best to use, don’t you think?”

[028-007]At length the time had come for us to part, and Blanche, the egregious

[028-008]Blanche, shed real tears as she took her leave of me. “Tu étais bon

[028-009]enfant” she said with a sob. “Je te croyais bête et tu en avais l’air,

[028-010]but it suited you.” Then, having given me a final handshake, she

[028-011]exclaimed, “Attends!”; whereafter, running into her boudoir, she

[028-012]brought me thence two thousand-franc notes. I could scarcely believe my

[028-013]eyes! “They may come in handy for you,” she explained, “for, though you

[028-014]are a very learned tutor, you are a very stupid man. More than two

[028-015]thousand francs, however, I am not going to give you, for the reason

[028-016]that, if I did so, you would gamble them all away. Now good-bye. Nous

[028-017]serons toujours bons amis, and if you win again, do not fail to come to

[028-018]me, et tu seras heureux.”

[028-019]I myself had still five hundred francs left, as well as a watch worth a

[028-020]thousand francs, a few diamond studs, and so on. Consequently, I could

[028-021]subsist for quite a length of time without particularly bestirring

[028-022]myself. Purposely I have taken up my abode where I am now partly to

[028-023]pull myself together, and partly to wait for [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], who, I have

[028-024]learnt, will soon be here for a day or so on business. Yes, I know

[028-025]that, and then—and then I shall go to [[homburg|Homburg]]. But to [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] I

[028-026]shall not go until next year, for they say it is bad to try one’s [[chance|luck]]

[028-027]twice in succession at a table. Moreover, Homburg is where the best

[028-028][[gambling|play]] is carried on.

[028-029]XVII

[028-030]It is a year and eight months since I last looked at these notes of

[028-031]mine. I do so now only because, being overwhelmed with depression, I

[028-032]wish to distract my mind by reading them through at random. I left them

[028-033]off at the point where I was just going to Homburg. My God, with what a

[028-034]light heart (comparatively speaking) did I write the concluding

[028-035]lines!—though it may be not so much with a light heart, as with a

[028-036]measure of self-confidence and unquenchable hope. At that time had I

[028-037]any doubts of myself? Yet behold me now. Scarcely a year and a half

[028-038]have passed, yet I am in a worse position than the meanest beggar. But

[028-039]what is a beggar? A fig for beggary! I have ruined myself—that is all.

[028-040]Nor is there anything with which I can compare myself; there is no

[028-041]moral which it would be of any use for you to read to me. At the

[028-042]present moment nothing could well be more incongruous than a moral. Oh,

[028-043]you self-satisfied persons who, in your unctuous pride, are forever

[028-044]ready to mouth your maxims—if only you knew how fully I myself

[028-045]comprehend the sordidness of my present state, you would not trouble to

[028-046]wag your tongues at me! What could you say to me that I do not already

[028-047]know? Well, wherein lies my difficulty? It lies in the fact that by a

[028-048]single turn of a [[roulette|roulette wheel]] everything for me, has become changed.

[028-049]Yet, had things befallen otherwise, these moralists would have been

[028-050]among the first (yes, I feel persuaded of it) to approach me with

[028-051]friendly jests and congratulations. Yes, they would never have turned

[028-052]from me as they are doing now! A fig for all of them! What am I? I am

[028-053]zero—nothing. What shall I be tomorrow? I may be risen from the dead,

[028-054]and have begun life anew. For still, I may discover the man in myself,

[028-055]if only my manhood has not become utterly shattered.

[028-056]I went, I say, to Homburg, but afterwards went also to Roulettenberg,

[028-057]as well as to Spa and Baden; in which latter place, for a time, I acted

[028-058]as valet to a certain rascal of a Privy Councillor, by name Heintze,

[028-059]who until lately was also my master here. Yes, for five months I lived

[028-060]my life with lacqueys! That was just after I had come out of

[028-061]Roulettenberg prison, where I had lain for a small debt which I owed.

[028-062]Out of that prison I was bailed by—by whom? By Mr. Astley? By [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]? I

[028-063]do not know. At all events, the debt was paid to the tune of two

[028-064]hundred thalers, and I sallied forth a free man. But what was I to do

[028-065]with myself? In my dilemma I had recourse to this Heintze, who was a

[028-066]young scapegrace, and the sort of man who could speak and write three

[028-067]languages. At first I acted as his secretary, at a salary of thirty

[028-068]gülden a month, but afterwards I became his lacquey, for the reason

[028-069]that he could not afford to keep a secretary—only an unpaid servant. I

[028-070]had nothing else to turn to, so I remained with him, and allowed myself

[028-071]to become his flunkey. But by stinting myself in meat and drink I

[028-072]saved, during my five months of service, some seventy gülden; and one

[028-073]evening, when we were at Baden, I told him that I wished to resign my

[028-074]post, and then hastened to betake myself to roulette.

[028-075]Oh, how my heart beat as I did so! No, it was not the money that I

[028-076]valued—what I wanted was to make all this mob of Heintzes, hotel

[028-077]proprietors, and fine ladies of Baden talk about me, recount my story,

[028-078]wonder at me, extol my doings, and worship my [[winning|winnings]]. True, these

[028-079]were childish fancies and aspirations, but who knows but that I might

[028-080]meet Polina, and be able to tell her everything, and see her look of

[028-081]surprise at the fact that I had overcome so many adverse strokes of

[028-082]fortune. No, I had no desire for money for its own sake, for I was

[028-083]perfectly well aware that I should only squander it upon some new

[028-084]Blanche, and spend another three weeks in [[paris|Paris]] after buying a pair of

[028-085]horses which had cost sixteen thousand francs. No, I never believed

[028-086]myself to be a hoarder; in fact, I knew only too well that I was a

[028-087]spendthrift. And already, with a sort of fear, a sort of sinking in my

[028-088]heart, I could hear the cries of the croupiers—“Trente et un, rouge,

[028-089]impair et passe,” “Quarte, noir, pair et manque.” How greedily I gazed

[028-090]upon the gaming-table, with its scattered louis d’or, ten-gülden

[028-091]pieces, and thalers; upon the streams of gold as they issued from the

[028-092]croupier’s hands, and piled themselves up into heaps of gold

[028-093]scintillating as fire; upon the ell—long rolls of silver lying around

[028-094]the croupier. Even at a distance of two rooms I could hear the chink of

[028-095]that money—so much so that I nearly fell into convulsions.

[028-096]Ah, the evening when I took those seventy gülden to the gaming table

[028-097]was a memorable one for me. I began by staking ten gülden upon passe.

[028-098]For passe I had always had a sort of predilection, yet I lost my [[bet|stake]]

[028-099]upon it. This left me with sixty gülden in silver. After a moment’s

[028-100]thought I selected zero—beginning by staking five gülden at a time.

[028-101]Twice I lost, but the third round suddenly brought up the desired coup.

[028-102]I could almost have died with joy as I received my one hundred and

[028-103]seventy-five gülden. Indeed, I have been less pleased when, in former

[028-104]times, I have won a hundred thousand gülden. [[losing|Losing]] no time, I staked

[028-105]another hundred gülden upon the red, and won; two hundred upon the red,

[028-106]and won; four hundred upon the black, and won; eight hundred upon

[028-107]manque, and won. Thus, with the addition of the remainder of my

[028-108]original capital, I found myself possessed, within five minutes, of

[028-109]seventeen hundred gülden. Ah, at such moments one forgets both oneself

[028-110]and one’s former failures! This I had gained by risking my very life. I

[028-111]had dared so to risk, and behold, again I was a member of mankind!

[028-112]I went and hired a room, I shut myself up in it, and sat counting my

[028-113]money until three o’clock in the morning. To think that when I awoke on

[028-114]the morrow, I was no lacquey! I decided to leave at once for Homburg.

[028-115]There I should neither have to serve as a footman nor to lie in prison.

[028-116]Half an hour before starting, I went and ventured a couple of stakes—no

[028-117]more; with the result that, in all, I lost fifteen hundred florins.

[028-118]Nevertheless, I proceeded to Homburg, and have now been there for a

[028-119]month.

[028-120]Of course, I am living in constant trepidation, playing for the

[028-121]smallest of stakes, and always looking out for something—calculating,

[028-122]standing whole days by the gaming-tables to watch the play—even seeing

[028-123]that play in my dreams—yet seeming, the while, to be in some way

[028-124]stiffening, to be growing caked, as it were, in mire. But I must

[028-125]conclude my notes, which I finish under the impression of a recent

[028-126]encounter with Mr. Astley. I had not seen him since we parted at

[028-127]Roulettenberg, and now we met quite by accident. At the time I was

[028-128]walking in the public gardens, and meditating upon the fact that not

[028-129]only had I still some fifty gülden in my possession, but also I had

[028-130]fully paid up my hotel bill three days ago. Consequently, I was in a

[028-131]position to try my [[fortune|luck]] again at roulette; and if I won anything I

[028-132]should be able to continue my play, whereas, if I lost what I now

[028-133]possessed, I should once more have to accept a lacquey’s place,

[028-134]provided that, in the alternative, I failed to discover a Russian

[028-135]family which stood in need of a tutor. Plunged in these reflections, I

[028-136]started on my daily walk through the Park and forest towards a

[028-137]neighbouring principality. Sometimes, on such occasions, I spent four

[028-138]hours on the way, and would return to Homburg tired and hungry; but, on

[028-139]this particular occasion, I had scarcely left the gardens for the Park

[028-140]when I caught sight of Astley seated on a bench. As soon as he

[028-141]perceived me, he called me by name, and I went and sat down beside him;

[028-142]but, on noticing that he seemed a little stiff in his manner, I

[028-143]hastened to moderate the expression of joy which the sight of him had

[028-144]called forth.

[028-145]“_You_ here?” he said. “Well, I had an idea that I should meet you. Do

[028-146]not trouble to tell me anything, for I know all—yes, all. In fact, your

[028-147]whole life during the past twenty months lies within my knowledge.”

[028-148]“How closely you watch the doings of your old friends!” I replied.

[028-149]“That does you infinite credit. But stop a moment. You have reminded me

[028-150]of something. Was it you who bailed me out of Roulettenberg prison when

[028-151]I was lying there for a debt of two hundred gülden? _someone_ did so.”

[028-152]“Oh dear no!—though I knew all the time that you were lying there.”

[028-153]“Perhaps you could tell me who _did_ bail me out?”

[028-154]“No; I am afraid I could not.”

[028-155]“What a strange thing! For I know no Russians at all here, so it cannot

[028-156]have been a Russian who befriended me. In [[russia|Russia]] we Orthodox folk _do_

[028-157]go bail for one another, but in this case I thought it must have been

[028-158]done by some English stranger who was not conversant with the ways of

[028-159]the country.”

[028-160]Mr. Astley seemed to listen to me with a sort of surprise. Evidently he

[028-161]had expected to see me looking more crushed and broken than I was.
