# The Telegram Crisis

The telegrams from Moscow form a central plot mechanism in *The Gambler*. [[general|The General]]'s household in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] lives in a state of suspended anticipation, waiting for each dispatch from [[russia|Russia]] to announce the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s death — and their financial deliverance. The telegram crisis functions as both a narrative suspense device and a moral X-ray, exposing the venality lurking beneath the household's polished European surface.

## The Waiting Game

The household's entire financial strategy depends on one event: the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s death in [[moscow|Moscow]]. "They are for ever expecting telegrams to notify the fact of her death" (011-168), [[alexei-ivanovitch|the narrator]] observes. Each telegram could bring news that would transform the General from debtor to heir, and De Griers from creditor to beneficiary.

The telegrams create a peculiar temporal condition — the household is suspended between Moscow and Roulettenberg, between anticipation and resolution, unable to act until the message arrives. The General's entire bearing, his debts, his engagement prospects — all are contingent on a telegram that refuses to come.

## The Grandmother's Knowledge

When the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] finally arrives in person, she reveals her full awareness of the scheme. She had received "so many telegrams to be sent off" (012-039) inquiring about her health and had deduced the conspiracy. Her sardonic comment — "telegrams to know if I were dead or not" (012-065) — exposes the venality of those waiting for her inheritance.

Her knowledge transforms the telegrams from an instrument of conspiracy into a weapon of exposure. The General "had sent so many telegrams" (012-126) and now must face the living addressee of his anxious inquiries. The Grandmother's indignation is partly genuine — "Oh, I know _all_ about the telegrams which you have been sending" (012-180) — and partly theatrical, a performance of moral superiority that gives her license to act as she pleases.

## The Unexpected Arrival

The crisis reaches its climax when the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] appears in [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]] in person: "here I am — and instead of a telegram, too!" (012-170). This arrival shatters the carefully laid plans. Instead of a telegram announcing her death — a mere piece of paper that could be processed through the machinery of inheritance — the household must now contend with the living, gambling, uncontrollable old woman.

The narrator captures the comic timing precisely: the household had been expecting "a telegram to say she was dead" (012-182), and instead receives the Grandmother herself — a substitution more disastrous than any text could convey. Her subsequent [[losing|loss]]es at roulette will destroy the inheritance everyone was counting on.

## Narrative Function

The telegrams serve a distinctive narrative function in Dostoevsky's construction. They inject news from offstage — from Moscow, from beyond the spa town's self-contained world — into the claustrophobic atmosphere of [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]. Each telegram recasts the present situation: first anticipation, then the false alarm of her illness, finally the shock of her arrival.

The narrator later reflects that "we had received such hopeless telegrams" (013-034) before her arrival — telegrams suggesting she was near death. The gap between expectation and reality, between the telegram and the person, drives the novel's second half and reveals the fundamental error of the conspirators: they treated a living person as a financial instrument awaiting its maturity date.
