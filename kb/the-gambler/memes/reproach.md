# Reproach

Reproach — and its inward form, self-reproach — is one of the primary emotional currencies in *The Gambler*. Characters reproach one another constantly: for [[money|lending]], for [[gambling]], for [[love|betrayal]], for weakness, for strength. But the novel's deepest interest is in self-reproach — the voice inside [[Alexei Ivanovitch]] that judges his own actions even as he performs them. Dostoyevsky treats self-reproach not as a moral failing but as a form of lucidity: the one who can reproach himself is still capable of seeing himself, and therefore still capable of change.

## As Social Dynamic

Reproach is the dominant mode of communication between the novel's central characters. [[Polina Alexandrovna]] reproaches the narrator for his weakness; the narrator reproaches her for her coldness; the [[General]] reproaches everyone for his own [[debt]]. When the narrator declares, "I have nothing to reproach myself with" (019-181), he is lying — and knows he is lying — but the declaration itself is a social move, an attempt to claim moral ground he has no right to occupy.

> "How can you speak so?" I exclaimed reproachfully. "Am *I* the one to blame?" (024-096)

The narrator's reproach of others is always a deflection of self-reproach. He accuses [[Marquis de Griers]] of manipulation, the [[General]] of weakness, [[Mlle Blanche]] of cynicism — all to avoid the one accusation he cannot answer: that he, [[Alexei Ivanovitch]], has chosen every step of his own [[ruin]].

## Self-Reproach and Moral Clarity

The novel's treatment of self-reproach reaches its most intense expression in the narrator's confession:

> My suffering was genuine, and my self-reproach was real. (025-123)

This is the paradox at the heart of Dostoyevsky's psychology: the narrator's self-reproach is genuine — he truly judges himself — yet it changes nothing. He reproaches himself for his [[gambling]] while continuing to [[bet|gamble]]; he reproaches himself for his treatment of [[Polina Alexandrovna]] while continuing to [[love|pursue]] her in ways that harm her. Self-reproach, in *The Gambler*, is not the first step toward reform but a parallel track that runs alongside self-destruction — a form of self-awareness that provides no immunity against the behaviour it condemns.
