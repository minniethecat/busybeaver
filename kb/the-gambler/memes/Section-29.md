# The Gambler · Section 29

[029-001]“Well,” he said—not very pleasantly, “I am none the less glad to find

[029-002]that you retain your old independence of spirit, as well as your

[029-003]buoyancy.”

[029-004]“Which means that you are vexed at not having found me more abased and

[029-005]humiliated than I am?” I retorted with a smile.

[029-006][[mr-astley|Astley]] was not quick to understand this, but presently did so and

[029-007]laughed.

[029-008]“Your remarks please me as they always did,” he continued. “In those

[029-009]words I see the clever, triumphant, and, above all things, cynical

[029-010]friend of former days. Only Russians have the faculty of combining

[029-011]within themselves so many opposite qualities. Yes, most men love to see

[029-012]their best friend in abasement; for generally it is on such abasement

[029-013]that friendship is founded. All thinking persons know that ancient

[029-014]truth. Yet, on the present occasion, I assure you, I am sincerely glad

[029-015]to see that you are _not_ cast down. Tell me, are you never going to

[029-016]give up gambling?”

[029-017]“Damn the gambling! Yes, I should certainly have given it up, were it

[029-018]not that—”

[029-019]“That you are [[loss|losing]]? I thought so. You need not tell me any more. I

[029-020]know how things stand, for you have said that last in despair, and

[029-021]therefore, truthfully. Have you no other employment than gambling?”

[029-022]“No; none whatever.”

[029-023]Astley gave me a searching glance. At that time it was ages since I had

[029-024]last looked at a paper or turned the pages of a book.

[029-025]“You are growing blasé,” he said. “You have not only renounced life,

[029-026]with its interests and social ties, but the duties of a citizen and a

[029-027]man; you have not only renounced the friends whom I know you to have

[029-028]had, and every aim in life but that of winning money; but you have also

[029-029]renounced your memory. Though I can remember you in the strong, ardent

[029-030]period of your life, I feel persuaded that you have now forgotten every

[029-031]better feeling of that period—that your present dreams and aspirations

[029-032]of subsistence do not rise above pair, impair rouge, noir, the twelve

[029-033]middle numbers, and so forth.”

[029-034]“Enough, Mr. Astley!” I cried with some irritation—almost in anger.

[029-035]“Kindly do not recall to me any more recollections, for I can remember

[029-036]things for myself. Only for a time have I put them out of my head. Only

[029-037]until I shall have rehabilitated myself, am I keeping my memory dulled.

[029-038]When that hour shall come, you will see me arise from the dead.”

[029-039]“Then you will have to be here another ten years,” he replied. “Should

[029-040]I then be alive, I will remind you—here, on this very bench—of what I

[029-041]have just said. In fact, I will bet you a [[bet|wager]] that I shall do so.”

[029-042]“Say no more,” I interrupted impatiently. “And to show you that I have

[029-043]not wholly forgotten the past, may I enquire where Mlle. [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] is? If

[029-044]it was not you who bailed me out of prison, it must have been she. Yet

[029-045]never have I heard a word concerning her.”

[029-046]“No, I do not think it was she. At the present moment she is in

[029-047][[switzerland|Switzerland]], and you will do me a favour by ceasing to ask me these

[029-048]questions about her.” Astley said this with a firm, and even an angry,

[029-049]air.

[029-050]“Which means that she has dealt you a serious wound?” I burst out with

[029-051]an involuntary sneer.

[029-052]“Mlle. Polina,” he continued, “Is the best of all possible living

[029-053]beings; but, I repeat, that I shall thank you to cease questioning me

[029-054]about her. You never really knew her, and her name on your lips is an

[029-055]offence to my moral feeling.”

[029-056]“Indeed? On what subject, then, have I a better right to speak to you

[029-057]than on this? With it are bound up all your recollections and mine.

[029-058]However, do not be alarmed: I have no wish to probe too far into your

[029-059]private, your secret affairs. My interest in Mlle. Polina does not

[029-060]extend beyond her outward circumstances and surroundings. [[About]] them

[029-061]you could tell me in two words.”

[029-062]“Well, on condition that the matter shall end there, I will tell you

[029-063]that for a long time Mlle. Polina was ill, and still is so. My mother

[029-064]and sister entertained her for a while at their home in the north of

[029-065]England, and thereafter Mlle. Polina’s grandmother (you remember the

[029-066]mad old woman?) died, and left Mlle. Polina a personal legacy of seven

[029-067]thousand pounds sterling. That was about six months ago, and now Mlle.

[029-068]is travelling with my sister’s family—my sister having since married.

[029-069]Mlle.’s little brother and sister also benefited by the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]’s

[029-070]will, and are now being educated in London. As for the General, he died

[029-071]in [[paris|Paris]] last month, of a stroke. [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] did well by him, for

[029-072]she succeeded in having transferred to herself all that he received

[029-073]from the Grandmother. That, I think, concludes all that I have to

[029-074]tell.”

[029-075]“And De Griers? Is he too travelling in Switzerland?”

[029-076]“No; nor do I know where he is. Also I warn you once more that you had

[029-077]better avoid such hints and ignoble suppositions; otherwise you will

[029-078]assuredly have to reckon with me.”

[029-079]“What? In spite of our old friendship?”

[029-080]“Yes, in spite of our old friendship.”

[029-081]“Then I beg your pardon a thousand times, Mr. Astley. I meant nothing

[029-082]offensive to Mlle. Polina, for I have nothing of which to accuse her.

[029-083]Moreover, the question of there being anything between this Frenchman

[029-084]and this Russian lady is not one which you and I need discuss, nor even

[029-085]attempt to understand.”

[029-086]“If,” replied Astley, “you do not care to hear their names coupled

[029-087]together, may I ask you what you mean by the expressions ‘this

[029-088]Frenchman,’ ‘this Russian lady,’ and ‘there being anything between

[029-089]them’? Why do you call them so particularly a ‘Frenchman’ and a

[029-090]‘Russian lady’?”

[029-091]“Ah, I see you are interested, Mr. Astley. But it is a long, long

[029-092]story, and calls for a lengthy preface. At the same time, the question

[029-093]is an important one, however ridiculous it may seem at the first

[029-094]glance. A Frenchman, Mr. Astley, is merely a fine figure of a man. With

[029-095]this you, as a Britisher, may not agree. With it I also, as a Russian,

[029-096]may not agree—out of envy. Yet possibly our good ladies are of another

[029-097]opinion. For instance, one may look upon Racine as a broken-down,

[029-098]hobbledehoy, perfumed individual—one may even be unable to read him;

[029-099]and I too may think him the same, as well as, in some respects, a

[029-100]subject for ridicule. Yet about him, Mr. Astley, there is a certain

[029-101]charm, and, above all things, he is a great poet—though one might like

[029-102]to deny it. Yes, [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]], the Parisian, as a national figure, was

[029-103]in process of developing into a figure of elegance before we Russians

[029-104]had even ceased to be bears. The Revolution bequeathed to the French

[029-105]nobility its heritage, and now every whipper-snapper of a Parisian may

[029-106]possess manners, methods of expression, and even thoughts that are

[029-107]above reproach in form, while all the time he himself may share in that

[029-108]form neither in initiative nor in intellect nor in soul—his manners,

[029-109]and the rest, having come to him through inheritance. Yes, taken by

[029-110]himself, the Frenchman is frequently a fool of fools and a villain of

[029-111]villains. Per contra, there is no one in the world more worthy of

[029-112]confidence and respect than this young Russian lady. De Griers might so

[029-113]mask his face and [[gambling|play]] a part as easily to overcome her heart, for he

[029-114]has an imposing figure, Mr. Astley, and this young lady might easily

[029-115]take that figure for his real self—for the natural form of his heart

[029-116]and soul—instead of the mere cloak with which heredity has dowered him.

[029-117]And even though it may offend you, I feel bound to say that the

[029-118]majority also of English people are uncouth and unrefined, whereas we

[029-119]Russian folk can recognise beauty wherever we see it, and are always

[029-120]eager to cultivate the same. But to distinguish beauty of soul and

[029-121]personal originality there is needed far more independence and freedom

[029-122]than is possessed by our women, especially by our younger ladies. At

[029-123]all events, they need more _experience_. For instance, this Mlle.

[029-124]Polina—pardon me, but the name has passed my lips, and I cannot well

[029-125]recall it—is taking a very long time to make up her mind to prefer you

[029-126]to Monsieur de Griers. She may respect you, she may become your friend,

[029-127]she may open out her heart to you; yet over that heart there will be

[029-128]reigning that loathsome villain, that mean and petty usurer, De Griers.

[029-129]This will be due to obstinacy and self-love—to the fact that De Griers

[029-130]once appeared to her in the transfigured guise of a marquis, of a

[029-131]disenchanted and ruined liberal who was doing his best to help her

[029-132]family and the frivolous old General; and, although these transactions

[029-133]of his have since been exposed, you will find that the exposure has

[029-134]made no impression upon her mind. Only give her the De Griers of former

[029-135]days, and she will ask of you no more. The more she may detest the

[029-136]present De Griers, the more will she lament the De Griers of the

[029-137]past—even though the latter never existed but in her own imagination.

[029-138]You are a sugar refiner, Mr. Astley, are you not?”

[029-139]“Yes, I belong to the well-known firm of Lovell and Co.”

[029-140]“Then see here. On the one hand, you are a sugar refiner, while, on the

[029-141]other hand, you are an Apollo Belvedere. But the two characters do not

[029-142]mix with one another. I, again, am not even a sugar refiner; I am a

[029-143]mere roulette gambler who has also served as a lacquey. Of this fact

[029-144]Mlle. Polina is probably well aware, since she appears to have an

[029-145]excellent force of police at her disposal.”

[029-146]“You are saying this because you are feeling bitter,” said Astley with

[029-147]cold indifference. “Yet there is not the least originality in your

[029-148]words.”

[029-149]“I agree. But therein lies the horror of it all—that, how trepidation,

[029-150]playing ever mean and farcical my accusations may be, they are none the

[029-151]less _true_. But I am only wasting words.”

[029-152]“Yes, you are, for you are only talking nonsense!” exclaimed my

[029-153]companion—his voice now trembling and his eyes flashing fire. “Are you

[029-154]aware,” he continued, “that wretched, ignoble, petty, unfortunate man

[029-155]though you are, it was at _her_ request I came to [[homburg|Homburg]], in order to

[029-156]see you, and to have a long, serious talk with you, and to report to

[029-157]her your feelings and thoughts and hopes—yes, and your recollections of

[029-158]her, too?”

[029-159]“Indeed? Is that really so?” I cried—the tears beginning to well from

[029-160]my eyes. Never before had this happened.

[029-161]“Yes, poor unfortunate,” continued Astley. “She _did_ love you; and I

[029-162]may tell you this now for the reason that now you are utterly lost.

[029-163]Even if I were also to tell you that she still loves you, you would

[029-164]none the less have to remain where you are. Yes, you have ruined

[029-165]yourself beyond redemption. Once upon a time you had a certain amount

[029-166]of talent, and you were of a lively disposition, and your good looks

[029-167]were not to be despised. You might even have been useful to your

[029-168]country, which needs men like you. Yet you remained here, and your life

[029-169]is now over. I am not blaming you for this—in my view all Russians

[029-170]resemble you, or are inclined to do so. If it is not roulette, then it

[029-171]is something else. The exceptions are very rare. Nor are you the first

[029-172]to learn what a taskmaster is yours. For roulette is not exclusively a

[029-173]Russian game. Hitherto, you have honourably preferred to serve as a

[029-174]lacquey rather than to act as a thief; but what the future may have in

[029-175]store for you I tremble to think. Now good-bye. You are in want of

[029-176]money, I suppose? Then take these ten louis d’or. More I shall not give

[029-177]you, for you would only gamble it away. Take care of these coins, and

[029-178]farewell. Once more, _take care_ of them.”
