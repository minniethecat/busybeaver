# The Gambler · Section 13

[013-001]“Oui, madame,” assented De Griers. “Et, croyez, je suis si enchanté!

[013-002]Votre santé—c’est un miracle vous voir ici. Une surprise charmante!”

[013-003]“Just so. ‘Charmante!’ I happen to know you as a mountebank, and

[013-004]therefore trust you no more than _this_.” She indicated her little

[013-005]finger. “And who is _that?_” she went on, turning towards Mlle.

[013-006]Blanche. Evidently the Frenchwoman looked so becoming in her

[013-007]riding-habit, with her whip in her hand, that she had made an

[013-008]impression upon [[grandmother|the old lady]]. “Who is that woman there?”

[013-009]“Mlle. de Cominges,” I said. “And this is her mother, Madame de

[013-010]Cominges. They also are staying in the hotel.”

[013-011]“Is the daughter married?” asked the old lady, without the least

[013-012]semblance of ceremony.

[013-013]“No,” I replied as respectfully as possible, but under my breath.

[013-014]“Is she good company?”

[013-015]I failed to understand the question.

[013-016]“I mean, is she or is she not a bore? Can she speak Russian? When this

[013-017]De Griers was in [[moscow|Moscow]] he soon learnt to make himself understood.”

[013-018]I explained to the old lady that [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] had never visited

[013-019][[russia|Russia]].

[013-020]“Bonjour, then,” said Madame, with sudden brusquerie.

[013-021]“Bonjour, madame,” replied Mlle. Blanche with an elegant, ceremonious

[013-022]bow as, under cover of an unwonted modesty, she endeavoured to express,

[013-023]both in face and figure, her extreme surprise at such strange behaviour

[013-024]on the part of the Grandmother.

[013-025]“How the woman sticks out her eyes at me! How she mows and minces!” was

[013-026]the Grandmother’s comment. Then she turned suddenly to the General, and

[013-027]continued: “I have taken up my abode here, so am going to be your

[013-028]next-door neighbour. Are you glad to hear that, or are you not?”

[013-029]“My dear mother, believe me when I say that I am sincerely delighted,”

[013-030]returned the General, who had now, to a certain extent, recovered his

[013-031]senses; and inasmuch as, when occasion arose, he could speak with

[013-032]fluency, gravity, and a certain effect, he set himself to be expansive

[013-033]in his remarks, and went on: “We have been so dismayed and upset by the

[013-034]news of your indisposition! We had received such hopeless telegrams

[013-035]about you! Then suddenly—”

[013-036]“Fibs, fibs!” interrupted the Grandmother.

[013-037]“How on earth, too, did you come to decide upon the journey?” continued

[013-038]the General, with raised voice as he hurried to overlook the old lady’s

[013-039]last remark. “Surely, at your age, and in your present state of health,

[013-040]the thing is so unexpected that our surprise is at least intelligible.

[013-041]However, I am glad to see you (as indeed, are we all”—he said this with

[013-042]a dignified, yet conciliatory, smile), “and will use my best endeavours

[013-043]to render your stay here as pleasant as possible.”

[013-044]“Enough! All this is empty chatter. You are talking the usual nonsense.

[013-045]I shall know quite well how to spend my time. How did I come to

[013-046]undertake the journey, you ask? Well, is there anything so very

[013-047]surprising about it? It was done quite simply. What is every one going

[013-048]into ecstasies about?—How do you do, Prascovia? What are _you_ doing

[013-049]here?”

[013-050]“And how are _you_, Grandmother?” replied [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], as she approached the

[013-051]old lady. “Were you long on the journey?”

[013-052]“The most sensible question that I have yet been asked! Well, you shall

[013-053]hear for yourself how it all happened. I lay and lay, and was doctored

[013-054]and doctored, until at last I drove the physicians from me, and called

[013-055]in an apothecary from Nicolai who had cured an old woman of a malady

[013-056]similar to my own—cured her merely with a little hayseed. Well, he did

[013-057]me a great deal of good, for on the third day I broke into a sweat, and

[013-058]was able to leave my bed. Then my German doctors held another

[013-059]consultation, put on their spectacles, and told me that if I would go

[013-060]abroad, and take a course of the waters, the indisposition would

[013-061]finally pass away. ‘Why should it not?’ I thought to myself. So I had

[013-062]got things ready, and on the following day, a Friday, set out for here.

[013-063]I occupied a special compartment in the train, and where ever I had to

[013-064]change I found at the station bearers who were ready to carry me for a

[013-065]few coppers. You have nice quarters here,” she went on as she glanced

[013-066]around the room. “But where on earth did you get the money for them, my

[013-067]good sir? I thought that everything of yours had been mortgaged? This

[013-068]Frenchman alone must be your creditor for a good deal. Oh, I know all

[013-069]about it, all about it.”

[013-070]“I-I am surprised at you, my dearest mother,” said the General in some

[013-071]confusion. “I-I am greatly surprised. But I do not need any extraneous

[013-072]control of my finances. Moreover, my expenses do not exceed my income,

[013-073]and we—”

[013-074]“They do not exceed it? Fie! Why, you are robbing your children of

[013-075]their last kopeck—you, their guardian!”

[013-076]“After this,” said the General, completely taken aback, “—after what

[013-077]you have just said, I do not know whether—”

[013-078]“You do not know _what?_ By heavens, are you _never_ going to drop that

[013-079]roulette of yours? Are you going to whistle all your property away?”

[013-080]This made such an impression upon the General that he almost choked

[013-081]with fury.

[013-082]“[[roulette|Roulette]], indeed? _I_ [[gambling|play]] roulette? Really, in view of my

[013-083]position—Recollect what you are saying, my dearest mother. You must

[013-084]still be unwell.”

[013-085]“Rubbish, rubbish!” she retorted. “The truth is that you _cannot_ be

[013-086]got away from that roulette. You are simply telling lies. This very day

[013-087]I mean to go and see for myself what roulette is like. Prascovia, tell

[013-088]me what there is to be seen here; and do you, [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]], show

[013-089]me everything; and do you, [[potapitch|Potapitch]], make me a list of excursions.

[013-090]What _is_ there to be seen?” again she inquired of Polina.

[013-091]“There is a ruined castle, and the Shlangenberg.”

[013-092]“The Shlangenberg? What is it? A forest?”

[013-093]“No, a mountain on the summit of which there is a place fenced off.

[013-094]From it you can get a most beautiful view.”

[013-095]“Could a chair be carried up that mountain of yours?”

[013-096]“Doubtless we could find bearers for the purpose,” I interposed.

[013-097]At this moment Theodosia, the nursemaid, approached the old lady with

[013-098]the General’s children.

[013-099]“No, I _don’t_ want to see them,” said the Grandmother. “I hate kissing

[013-100]children, for their noses are always wet. How are you getting on,

[013-101]Theodosia?”

[013-102]“I am very well, thank you, Madame,” replied the nursemaid. “And how is

[013-103]your ladyship? We have been feeling so anxious about you!”

[013-104]“Yes, I know, you simple soul—But who are those other guests?” the old

[013-105]lady continued, turning again to Polina. “For instance, who is that old

[013-106]rascal in the spectacles?”

[013-107]“Prince Nilski, Grandmamma,” whispered Polina.

[013-108]“Oh, a Russian? Why, I had no idea that he could understand me! Surely

[013-109]he did not hear what I said? As for [[mr-astley|Mr. Astley]], I have seen him

[013-110]already, and I see that he is here again. How do you do?” she added to

[013-111]the gentleman in question.

[013-112]Mr. Astley bowed in silence.

[013-113]“Have you _nothing_ to say to me?” the old lady went on. “Say

[013-114]something, for goodness’ sake! Translate to him, Polina.”

[013-115]Polina did so.

[013-116]“I have only to say,” replied Mr. Astley gravely, but also with

[013-117]alacrity, “that I am indeed glad to see you in such good health.” This

[013-118]was interpreted to the Grandmother, and she seemed much gratified.

[013-119]“How well English people know how to answer one!” she remarked. “That

[013-120]is why I like them so much better than French. Come here,” she added to

[013-121]Mr. Astley. “I will try not to bore you too much. Polina, translate to

[013-122]him that I am staying in rooms on a lower floor. Yes, on a lower

[013-123]floor,” she repeated to Astley, pointing downwards with her finger.

[013-124]Astley looked pleased at receiving the invitation.

[013-125]Next, the old lady scanned Polina, from head to foot with minute

[013-126]attention.

[013-127]“I could almost have liked you, Prascovia,” suddenly she remarked, “for

[013-128]you are a nice girl—the best of the lot. You have some character about

[013-129]you. I too have character. Turn round. Surely that is not false hair

[013-130]that you are wearing?”

[013-131]“No, Grandmamma. It is my own.”

[013-132]“Well, well. I do not like the stupid fashions of today. You are very

[013-133]good looking. I should have fallen in love with you if I had been a

[013-134]man. Why do you not get married? It is time now that I was going. I

[013-135]want to walk, yet I always have to ride. Are you still in a bad

[013-136]temper?” she added to the General.

[013-137]“No, indeed,” rejoined the now mollified General.

[013-138]“I quite understand that at your time of life—”

[013-139]“Cette vieille est tombée en enfance,” De Griers whispered to me.

[013-140]“But I want to look round a little,” the old lady added to the General.

[013-141]Will you lend me Alexis Ivanovitch for the purpose?

[013-142]“As much as you like. But I myself—yes, and Polina and Monsieur de

[013-143]Griers too—we all of us hope to have the pleasure of escorting you.”

[013-144]“Mais, madame, cela sera un plaisir,” De Griers commented with a

[013-145]bewitching smile.

[013-146]“‘Plaisir’ indeed! Why, I look upon you as a perfect fool, monsieur.”

[013-147]Then she remarked to the General: “I am not going to let you have any

[013-148]of my money. I must be off to my rooms now, to see what they are like.

[013-149]Afterwards we will look round a little. Lift me up.”

[013-150]Again the Grandmother was borne aloft and carried down the staircase

[013-151]amid a perfect bevy of followers—the General walking as though he had

[013-152]been hit over the head with a cudgel, and De Griers seeming to be

[013-153]plunged in thought. Endeavouring to be left behind, Mlle. Blanche next

[013-154]thought better of it, and followed the rest, with the Prince in her

[013-155]wake. Only the German savant and [[madame-de-cominges|Madame de Cominges]] did not leave the

[013-156]General’s apartments.

[013-157]X

[013-158]At spas—and, probably, all over Europe—hotel landlords and managers are

[013-159]guided in their allotment of rooms to visitors, not so much by the

[013-160]wishes and requirements of those visitors, as by their personal

[013-161]estimate of the same. It may also be said that these landlords and

[013-162]managers seldom make a mistake. To the Grandmother, however, our

[013-163]landlord, for some reason or another, allotted such a sumptuous suite

[013-164]that he fairly overreached himself; for he assigned her a suite

[013-165]consisting of four magnificently appointed rooms, with bathroom,

[013-166]servants’ quarters, a separate room for her maid, and so on. In fact,

[013-167]during the previous week the suite had been occupied by no less a

[013-168]personage than a Grand Duchess: which circumstance was duly explained

[013-169]to the new occupant, as an excuse for raising the price of these

[013-170]apartments. The Grandmother had herself carried—or, rather,

[013-171]wheeled—through each room in turn, in order that she might subject the

[013-172]whole to a close and attentive scrutiny; while the landlord—an elderly,

[013-173]bald-headed man—walked respectfully by her side.

[013-174]What every one took the Grandmother to be I do not know, but it

[013-175]appeared, at least, that she was accounted a person not only of great

[013-176]importance, but also, and still more, of great [[fortune|wealth]]; and without

[013-177]delay they entered her in the hotel register as “Madame la générale,

[013-178]Princesse de Tarassevitcheva,” although she had never been a princess

[013-179]in her life. Her retinue, her reserved compartment in the train, her

[013-180]pile of unnecessary trunks, portmanteaux, and strong-boxes, all helped

[013-181]to increase her prestige; while her wheeled chair, her sharp tone and

[013-182]voice, her eccentric questions (put with an air of the most overbearing

[013-183]and unbridled imperiousness), her whole figure—upright, rugged, and

[013-184]commanding as it was—completed the general awe in which she was held.

[013-185]As she inspected her new abode she ordered her chair to be stopped at

[013-186]intervals in order that, with finger extended towards some article of

[013-187]furniture, she might ply the respectfully smiling, yet secretly

[013-188]apprehensive, landlord with unexpected questions. She addressed them to

[013-189]him in French, although her pronunciation of the language was so bad

[013-190]that sometimes I had to translate them. For the most part, the

[013-191]landlord’s answers were unsatisfactory, and failed to please her; nor

[013-192]were the questions themselves of a practical nature, but related,

[013-193]generally, to God knows what.
