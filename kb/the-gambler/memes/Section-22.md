# The Gambler · Section 22

[022-001]A minute later, though sobbing and almost breathless, he managed to

[022-002]gasp out that [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] had refused to marry him, for the reason

[022-003]that the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] had turned up in place of a telegram, and it was

[022-004]therefore clear that he had no inheritance to look for. Evidently, he

[022-005]supposed that I had hitherto been in entire ignorance of all this.

[022-006]Again, when I referred to De Griers, the General made a gesture of

[022-007]despair. “He has gone away,” he said, “and everything which I possess

[022-008]is mortgaged to him. I stand stripped to my skin. Even of the money

[022-009]which you brought me from [[paris|Paris]], I know not if seven hundred francs be

[022-010]left. Of course that sum will do to go on with, but, as regards the

[022-011]future, I know nothing, I know nothing.”

[022-012]“Then how will you pay your hotel bill?” I cried in consternation. “And

[022-013]what shall you do afterwards?”

[022-014]He looked at me vaguely, but it was clear that he had not

[022-015]understood—perhaps had not even heard—my questions. Then I tried to get

[022-016]him to speak of [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] and the children, but he only returned brief

[022-017]answers of “Yes, yes,” and again started to maunder about the Prince,

[022-018]and the likelihood of the latter marrying Mlle. Blanche. “What on earth

[022-019]am I to do?” he concluded. “What on earth am I to do? Is this not

[022-020]ingratitude? Is it not sheer ingratitude?” And he burst into tears.

[022-021]Nothing could be done with such a man. Yet to leave him alone was

[022-022]dangerous, for something might happen to him. I withdrew from his rooms

[022-023]for a little while, but warned the nursemaid to keep an eye upon him,

[022-024]as well as exchanged a word with the corridor lacquey (a very talkative

[022-025]fellow), who likewise promised to remain on the look-out.

[022-026]Hardly had I left the General, when [[potapitch|Potapitch]] approached me with a

[022-027]summons from the Grandmother. It was now eight o’clock, and she had

[022-028]returned from the Casino after finally [[loss|losing]] all that she possessed. I

[022-029]found her sitting in her chair—much distressed and evidently fatigued.

[022-030]Presently Martha brought her up a cup of tea and forced her to drink

[022-031]it; yet, even then I could detect in the old lady’s tone and manner a

[022-032]great change.

[022-033]“Good evening, [[alexei-ivanovitch|Alexis Ivanovitch]],” she said slowly, with her head

[022-034]drooping. “Pardon me for disturbing you again. Yes, you must pardon an

[022-035]old, old woman like myself, for I have left behind me all that I

[022-036]possess—nearly a hundred thousand roubles! You did quite right in

[022-037]declining to come with me this evening. Now I am without money—without

[022-038]a single groat. But I must not delay a moment; I must leave by the 9:30

[022-039]train. I have sent for that English friend of yours, and am going to

[022-040]beg of him three thousand francs for a week. Please try and persuade

[022-041]him to think nothing of it, nor yet to refuse me, for I am still a rich

[022-042]woman who possesses three villages and a couple of mansions. Yes, the

[022-043]money shall be found, for I have not yet squandered _everything_. I

[022-044]tell you this in order that he may have no doubts about—Ah, but here he

[022-045]is! Clearly he is a good fellow.”

[022-046]True enough, [[mr-astley|Astley]] had come hot-foot on receiving the Grandmother’s

[022-047]appeal. Scarcely stopping even to reflect, and with scarcely a word, he

[022-048]counted out the three thousand francs under a note of hand which she

[022-049]duly signed. Then, his business done, he bowed, and lost no time in

[022-050]taking his departure.

[022-051]“You too leave me, Alexis Ivanovitch,” said the Grandmother. “All my

[022-052]bones are aching, and I still have an hour in which to rest. Do not be

[022-053]hard upon me, old fool that I am. Never again shall I blame young

[022-054]people for being frivolous. I should think it wrong even to blame that

[022-055]unhappy General of yours. Nevertheless, I do not mean to let him have

[022-056]any of my money (which is all that he desires), for the reason that I

[022-057]look upon him as a perfect blockhead, and consider myself, simpleton

[022-058]though I be, at least wiser than _he_ is. How surely does God visit old

[022-059]age, and punish it for its presumption! Well, good-bye. Martha, come

[022-060]and lift me up.”

[022-061]However, I had a mind to see the old lady off; and, moreover, I was in

[022-062]an expectant frame of mind—somehow I kept thinking that _something_ was

[022-063]going to happen; wherefore, I could not rest quietly in my room, but

[022-064]stepped out into the corridor, and then into the Chestnut Avenue for a

[022-065]few minutes’ stroll. My letter to Polina had been clear and firm, and

[022-066]in the present crisis, I felt sure, would prove final. I had heard of

[022-067]De Griers’ departure, and, however much Polina might reject me as a

[022-068]_friend_, she might not reject me altogether as a _servant_. She would

[022-069]need me to fetch and carry for her, and I was ready to do so. How could

[022-070]it have been otherwise?

[022-071]Towards the hour of the train’s departure I hastened to the station,

[022-072]and put the Grandmother into her compartment—she and her party

[022-073]occupying a reserved family saloon.

[022-074]“Thanks for your disinterested assistance,” she said at parting. “Oh,

[022-075]and please remind Prascovia of what I said to her last night. I expect

[022-076]soon to see her.”

[022-077]Then I returned home. As I was passing the door of the General’s suite,

[022-078]I met the nursemaid, and inquired after her master. “There is nothing

[022-079]new to report, sir,” she replied quietly. Nevertheless I decided to

[022-080]enter, and was just doing so when I halted thunderstruck on the

[022-081]threshold. For before me I beheld the General and Mlle.

[022-082]Blanche—laughing gaily at one another!—while beside them, on the sofa,

[022-083]there was seated her mother. Clearly the General was almost out of his

[022-084]mind with joy, for he was talking all sorts of nonsense, and bubbling

[022-085]over with a long-drawn, nervous laugh—a laugh which twisted his face

[022-086]into innumerable wrinkles, and caused his eyes almost to disappear.

[022-087]Afterwards I learnt from Mlle. Blanche herself that, after dismissing

[022-088]the Prince and hearing of the General’s tears, she bethought her of

[022-089]going to comfort the old man, and had just arrived for the purpose when

[022-090]I entered. Fortunately, the poor General did not know that his fate had

[022-091]been decided—that Mlle. had long ago packed her trunks in readiness for

[022-092]the first morning train to Paris!

[022-093]Hesitating a moment on the threshold I changed my mind as to entering,

[022-094]and departed unnoticed. Ascending to my own room, and opening the door,

[022-095]I perceived in the semi-darkness a figure seated on a chair in the

[022-096]corner by the window. The figure did not rise when I entered, so I

[022-097]approached it swiftly, peered at it closely, and felt my heart almost

[022-098]stop beating. The figure was Polina!

[022-100]The shock made me utter an exclamation.

[022-101]“What is the matter? What is the matter?” she asked in a strange voice.

[022-102]She was looking pale, and her eyes were dim.

[022-103]“What is the matter?” I re-echoed. “Why, the fact that you are _here!_”

[022-104]“If I am here, I have come with all that I have to bring,” she said.

[022-105]“Such has always been my way, as you shall presently see. Please light

[022-106]a candle.”

[022-107]I did so; whereupon she rose, approached the table, and laid upon it an

[022-108]open letter.

[022-109]“Read it,” she added.

[022-110]“It is De Griers’ handwriting!” I cried as I seized the document. My

[022-111]hands were so tremulous that the lines on the pages danced before my

[022-112]eyes. Although, at this distance of time, I have forgotten the exact

[022-113]phraseology of the missive, I append, if not the precise words, at all

[022-114]events the general sense.

[022-115]“Mademoiselle,” the document ran, “certain untoward circumstances

[022-116]compel me to depart in haste. Of course, you have of yourself remarked

[022-117]that hitherto I have always refrained from having any final explanation

[022-118]with you, for the reason that I could not well state the whole

[022-119]circumstances; and now to my difficulties the advent of the aged

[022-120]Grandmother, coupled with her subsequent proceedings, has put the final

[022-121]touch. Also, the involved state of my affairs forbids me to write with

[022-122]any finality concerning those hopes of ultimate bliss upon which, for a

[022-123]long while past, I have permitted myself to feed. I regret the past,

[022-124]but at the same time hope that in my conduct you have never been able

[022-125]to detect anything that was unworthy of a gentleman and a man of

[022-126]honour. Having lost, however, almost the whole of my money in debts

[022-127]incurred by your stepfather, I find myself driven to the necessity of

[022-128]saving the remainder; wherefore, I have instructed certain friends of

[022-129]mine in [[st-petersburg|St. Petersburg]] to arrange for the sale of all the property

[022-130]which has been mortgaged to myself. At the same time, knowing that, in

[022-131]addition, your frivolous stepfather has squandered money which is

[022-132]exclusively yours, I have decided to absolve him from a certain moiety

[022-133]of the mortgages on his property, in order that you may be in a

[022-134]position to recover of him what you have lost, by suing him in legal

[022-135]fashion. I trust, therefore, that, as matters now stand, this action of

[022-136]mine may bring you some advantage. I trust also that this same action

[022-137]leaves me in the position of having fulfilled every [[debt|obligation]] which is

[022-138]incumbent upon a man of honour and refinement. Rest assured that your

[022-139]memory will for ever remain graven in my heart.”

[022-140]“All this is clear enough,” I commented. “Surely you did not expect

[022-141]aught else from him?” Somehow I was feeling annoyed.

[022-142]“I expected nothing at all from him,” she replied—quietly enough, to

[022-143]all outward seeming, yet with a note of irritation in her tone. “Long

[022-144]ago I made up my mind on the subject, for I could read his thoughts,

[022-145]and knew what he was thinking. He thought that possibly I should sue

[022-146]him—that one day I might become a nuisance.” Here Polina halted for a

[022-147]moment, and stood biting her lips. “So of set purpose I redoubled my

[022-148]contemptuous treatment of him, and waited to see what he would do. If a

[022-149]telegram to say that we had become legatees had arrived from, St.

[022-150]Petersburg, I should have flung at him a quittance for my foolish

[022-151]stepfather’s debts, and then dismissed him. For a long time I have

[022-152]hated him. Even in earlier days he was not a man; and now!—Oh, how

[022-153]gladly I could throw those fifty thousand roubles in his face, and spit

[022-154]in it, and then rub the spittle in!”

[022-155]“But the document returning the fifty-thousand rouble mortgage—has the

[022-156]General got it? If so, possess yourself of it, and send it to De

[022-157]Griers.”

[022-158]“No, no; the General has not got it.”

[022-159]“Just as I expected! Well, what is the General going to do?” Then an

[022-160]idea suddenly occurred to me. “What about the Grandmother?” I asked.

[022-161]Polina looked at me with impatience and bewilderment.

[022-162]“What makes you speak of _her?_” was her irritable inquiry. “I cannot

[022-163]go and live with her. Nor,” she added hotly, “will I go down upon my

[022-164]knees to _any one_.”

[022-165]“Why should you?” I cried. “Yet to think that you should have loved De

[022-166]Griers! The villain, the villain! But I will kill him in a duel. Where

[022-167]is he now?”

[022-168]“In Frankfort, where he will be staying for the next three days.”

[022-169]“Well, bid me do so, and I will go to him by the first train tomorrow,”

[022-170]I exclaimed with enthusiasm.

[022-171]She smiled.

[022-172]“If you were to do that,” she said, “he would merely tell you to be so

[022-173]good as first to return him the fifty thousand francs. What, then,

[022-174]would be the use of having a quarrel with him? You talk sheer

[022-175]nonsense.”

[022-176]I ground my teeth.

[022-177]“The question,” I went on, “is how to raise the fifty thousand francs.

[022-178]We cannot expect to find them lying about on the floor. Listen. What of

[022-179]Mr. Astley?” Even as I spoke a new and strange idea formed itself in my

[022-180]brain.
