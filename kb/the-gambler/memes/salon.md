# Salon

The **salon** in *The Gambler* appears in two distinct contexts: the private sitting-room in the hotel suite and the public roulette gaming salons in the casino. Both serve as key social spaces where characters interact and pivotal scenes unfold.

## Hotel Salon

The hotel suite occupied by the General's party includes a salon as its principal room (008-016). This private sitting-room functions as the main gathering space for the party, where conversations, confrontations, and social rituals take place. It is here that Alexei Ivanovitch observes the dynamics between Polina, De Griers, and the General.

> In the larger suite, the first room comprised a salon and a smoking-room, with, adjoining the latter, the General's study. (008-016)

The psychological charge of the hotel salon is inseparable from its social function. Alexei describes watching Polina and De Griers in the salon and notes the particular quality of her gaze: "the way in which she looked at him" (007-125) — a look that conveys volumes of unspoken tension. The salon becomes a theatre of suppressed emotions, where every glance and gesture carries meaning invisible to the casual observer but devastating to those who, like Alexei, are watching closely.

> I noticed her in the salon. And the way in which she looked at De Griers! (007-125)

## Gaming Salon

The roulette salons are the public gaming rooms where the novel's central gambling scenes occur. Alexei learns from Potapitch that the game was carried on in "the salons of the Casino" (014-076), and the Grandmother immediately asks whether there are many such salons (014-078) — her first questions establishing the geography of the gambling floor before she has even seen it. The gaming salons of the lower floor, where a lackey who was actually a croupier in disguise could be spotted (014-168), show the porous boundary between service and predation in the casino's ecosystem.

> "Where is the game carried on?" ... "In the salons of the Casino." (014-076)

The Grandmother's entry into the roulette salon is one of the novel's great set-pieces. Her arrival "produced a profound sensation" (014-170), disrupting the established hierarchies of the gaming floor. The salon's regulars — aristocrats, adventurers, and professional gamblers — are momentarily united in their astonishment at the spectacle of a wealthy, elderly Russian noblewoman commanding the tables.

> The Grandmother's entry into the roulette-salon produced a profound sensation. (014-170)

The gaming salons also host the novel's darker transactions. Pickpockets and swindlers "reaped a rich harvest" (015-006), exploiting the distracted players whose attention is fixed on the spinning wheel. The salon is thus both a space of glamour and a hunting ground — a microcosm of the predatory social order Dostoyevsky depicts throughout the novel.

> ...roulette salons, and reaped a rich harvest. Indeed, in every direction money was being filched from pockets or purses. (015-006)

After the Grandmother's catastrophic losses, Alexei wanders the salons in search of his companions: "I went the round of the salons in vain" (017-047). The empty search through the gaming rooms mirrors the emptiness of the fortunes that have just been lost there — the salons are spaces that promise connection and deliver only isolation.

> I went the round of the salons in vain. At length, vexed, and almost in despair, I sought the roulette-salons. (017-047)

## Social Function

The salon bridges the private and public spheres of Roulettenberg society. In the hotel salon, characters engage in coded social performances; in the gaming salons, social barriers dissolve as aristocrats, adventurers, and servants alike gather around the roulette tables (016-053). The salon is where the novel's twin obsessions — love and gambling — find their architectural expression.
