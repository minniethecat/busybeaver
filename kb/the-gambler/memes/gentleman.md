# Gentleman

The figure of the gentleman haunts Dostoyevsky's *The Gambler* — as an ideal, a performance, and ultimately a fiction. The novel distinguishes between "the gentleman's game" and common gambling, between gentlemanly self-possession and vulgar desperation. Yet nearly every character who claims gentlemanly status proves, under pressure, to possess nothing of the sort.

## The Gentleman's Code

The 19th-century gentleman was defined by a code of conduct: self-control, financial independence, honour, and a certain detachment from grubby concerns like money. A gentleman did not work for wages; he did not display excessive emotion; he most certainly did not beg, scheme, or grovel.

Dostoyevsky's novel puts this code under pressure by placing gentlemen — or those who claim the title — in the one environment most hostile to gentlemanly composure: the [[casino|roulette tables]].

## The Two Games

The novel's most explicit treatment of the gentleman concept occurs in Chapter 3, where the narrator distinguishes between two kinds of gambling:

> "Namely, the game of the gentleman and the game of the plebeian" (003-051)

The gentleman, Alexei explains, may stake five or ten louis d'or — a significant but manageable sum. If he loses, he does not double his bet; he accepts the loss with equanimity and departs. The plebeian, by contrast, stakes his last franc, doubles after losses, and ultimately ruins himself.

> "A gentleman may stake, say, five or ten louis d'or; rarely more" (003-054)

This distinction is crucial to the novel's social analysis. Gambling is not one activity but two: for the gentleman, a diversion; for everyone else, a catastrophe.

### The Collapse of Distinction

The novel's dramatic arc systematically demolishes the distinction it establishes. Characters who begin as gentlemen — or aspire to the status — end as desperate gamblers indistinguishable from the mob they once disdained. [[general|The General]], who cultivates aristocratic dignity, ultimately pawns his possessions. Alexei himself, despite his sociological precision about the two games, becomes the novel's most compulsive gambler.

> "Even if a gentleman should lose his whole substance, he must not give way to annoyance" (003-091)

This injunction proves impossible to obey. The novel demonstrates that gentlemanly composure is a luxury of those who have not yet begun to lose seriously.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's treatment of the gentleman is satirical but not without sympathy. The code of gentlemanly behaviour represents a genuine aspiration toward dignity and self-mastery. But in the world of the novel — a world governed by money, appetite, and chance — such aspirations are unsustainable. The gentleman is an anachronism, a relic of a feudal order that capitalism has already dissolved.

The biographical dimension is again significant: Dostoyevsky, a former nobleman who had experienced imprisonment, poverty, and the degradation of compulsive gambling, understood better than anyone the fragility of gentlemanly identity.
