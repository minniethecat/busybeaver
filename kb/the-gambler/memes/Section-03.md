# The Gambler · Section 3

[003-001]When, on the present occasion, I entered the [[gambling|gaming]]-rooms (for the

[003-002]first time in my life), it was several moments before I could even make

[003-003]up my mind to play. For one thing, the crowd oppressed me. Had I been

[003-004]playing for myself, I think I should have left at once, and never have

[003-005]embarked upon gambling at all, for I could feel my heart beginning to

[003-006]beat, and my heart was anything but cold-blooded. Also, I knew, I had

[003-007]long ago made up my mind, that never should I depart from [[roulettenberg|Roulettenberg]]

[003-008]until some radical, some final, change had taken place in my fortunes.

[003-009]Thus, it must and would be. However ridiculous it may seem to you that

[003-010]I was expecting to win at roulette, I look upon the generally accepted

[003-011]opinion concerning the folly and the grossness of hoping to win at

[003-012]gambling as a thing even more absurd. For why is gambling a whit worse

[003-013]than any other method of acquiring money? How, for instance, is it

[003-014]worse than trade? True, out of a hundred persons, only one can win; yet

[003-015]what business is that of yours or of mine?

[003-016]At all events, I confined myself at first simply to looking on, and

[003-017]decided to attempt nothing serious. Indeed, I felt that, if I began to

[003-018]do anything at all, I should do it in an absent-minded, haphazard sort

[003-019]of way—of that I felt certain. Also, it behoved me to learn [[roulette|the game]]

[003-020]itself; since, despite a thousand descriptions of roulette which I had

[003-021]read with ceaseless avidity, I knew nothing of its rules, and had never

[003-022]even seen it played.

[003-023]In the first place, everything about it seemed to me so foul—so morally

[003-024]mean and foul. Yet I am not speaking of the hungry, restless folk who,

[003-025]by scores nay, even by hundreds—could be seen crowded around the

[003-026]gaming-tables. For in a desire to win quickly and to win much I can see

[003-027]nothing sordid; I have always applauded the opinion of a certain dead

[003-028]and gone, but cocksure, moralist who replied to the excuse that “one

[003-029]may always gamble moderately”, by saying that to do so makes things

[003-030]worse, since, in that case, the profits too will always be moderate.

[003-031]Insignificant profits and sumptuous profits do not stand on the same

[003-032]footing. No, it is all a matter of proportion. What may seem a small

[003-033]sum to a Rothschild may seem a large sum to me, and it is not the fault

[003-034]of stakes or of [[winning|winnings]] that everywhere men can be found winning, can

[003-035]be found depriving their fellows of something, just as they do at

[003-036]roulette. As to the question whether stakes and winnings are, in

[003-037]themselves, immoral is another question altogether, and I wish to

[003-038]express no opinion upon it. Yet the very fact that I was full of a

[003-039]strong desire to win caused this gambling for gain, in spite of its

[003-040]attendant squalor, to contain, if you will, something intimate,

[003-041]something sympathetic, to my eyes: for it is always pleasant to see men

[003-042]dispensing with ceremony, and acting naturally, and in an unbuttoned

[003-043]mood....

[003-044]Yet why should I so deceive myself? I could see that the whole thing

[003-045]was a vain and unreasoning pursuit; and what, at the first glance,

[003-046]seemed to me the ugliest feature in this mob of roulette players was

[003-047]their respect for their occupation—the seriousness, and even the

[003-048]humility, with which they stood around the gaming tables. Moreover, I

[003-049]had always drawn sharp distinctions between a game which is _de mauvais

[003-050]genre_ and a game which is permissible to a decent man. In fact, there

[003-051]are two sorts of gaming—namely, the game of the gentleman and the game

[003-052]of the plebs—the game for gain, and the game of the herd. Herein, as

[003-053]said, I draw sharp distinctions. Yet how essentially base are the

[003-054]distinctions! For instance, a gentleman may [[bet|stake]], say, five or ten

[003-055]louis d’or—seldom more, unless he is a very rich man, when he may

[003-056][[risk|stake]], say, a thousand francs; but, he must do this simply for the love

[003-057]of the game itself—simply for sport, simply in order to observe the

[003-058]process of winning or of [[loss|losing]], and, above all things, as a man who

[003-059]remains quite uninterested in the possibility of his issuing a winner.

[003-060]If he wins, he will be at liberty, perhaps, to give vent to a laugh, or

[003-061]to pass a remark on the circumstance to a bystander, or to stake again,

[003-062]or to double his stake; but, even this he must do solely out of

[003-063]curiosity, and for the pleasure of watching the play of chances and of

[003-064]calculations, and not because of any vulgar desire to win. In a word,

[003-065]he must look upon the gaming-table, upon roulette, and upon trente et

[003-066]quarante, as mere relaxations which have been arranged solely for his

[003-067]amusement. Of the existence of the lures and gains upon which [[gambling-bank|the bank]]

[003-068]is founded and maintained he must profess to have not an inkling. Best

[003-069]of all, he ought to imagine his fellow-gamblers and the rest of the mob

[003-070]which stands trembling over a coin to be equally rich and gentlemanly

[003-071]with himself, and playing solely for recreation and pleasure. This

[003-072]complete ignorance of the realities, this innocent view of mankind, is

[003-073]what, in my opinion, constitutes the truly aristocratic. For instance,

[003-074]I have seen even fond mothers so far indulge their guileless, elegant

[003-075]daughters—misses of fifteen or sixteen—as to give them a few gold coins

[003-076]and teach them how to play; and though the young ladies may have won or

[003-077]have lost, they have invariably laughed, and departed as though they

[003-078]were well pleased. In the same way, I saw our General once approach the

[003-079]table in a stolid, important manner. A lacquey darted to offer him a

[003-080]chair, but the General did not even notice him. Slowly he took out his

[003-081]money bags, and slowly extracted 300 francs in gold, which he staked on

[003-082]the black, and won. Yet he did not take up his winnings—he left them

[003-083]there on the table. Again the black turned up, and again he did not

[003-084]gather in what he had won; and when, in the third round, the _red_

[003-085]turned up he lost, at a stroke, 1200 francs. Yet even then he rose with

[003-086]a smile, and thus preserved his [[honour|reputation]]; yet I knew that his money

[003-087]bags must be chafing his heart, as well as that, had the stake been

[003-088]twice or thrice as much again, he would still have restrained himself

[003-089]from venting his disappointment.

[003-090]On the other hand, I saw a Frenchman first win, and then lose, 30,000

[003-091]francs cheerfully, and without a murmur. Yes; even if a gentleman

[003-092]should lose his whole substance, he must never give way to annoyance.

[003-093][[money|Money]] must be so subservient to gentility as never to be worth a

[003-094]thought. Of course, the _supremely_ aristocratic thing is to be

[003-095]entirely oblivious of the mire of rabble, with its setting; but

[003-096]sometimes a reverse course may be aristocratic to remark, to scan, and

[003-097]even to gape at, the mob (for preference, through a lorgnette), even as

[003-098]though one were taking the crowd and its squalor for a sort of raree

[003-099]show which had been organised specially for a gentleman’s diversion.

[003-100]Though one may be squeezed by the crowd, one must look as though one

[003-101]were fully assured of being the observer—of having neither part nor lot

[003-102]with the observed. At the same time, to stare fixedly about one is

[003-103]unbecoming; for that, again, is ungentlemanly, seeing that no spectacle

[003-104]is worth an open stare—are no spectacles in the world which merit from

[003-105]a gentleman too pronounced an inspection.

[003-106]However, to me personally the scene _did_ seem to be worth undisguised

[003-107]contemplation—more especially in view of the fact that I had come there

[003-108]not only to look at, but also to number myself sincerely and

[003-109]wholeheartedly with, the mob. As for my secret moral views, I had no

[003-110]room for them amongst my actual, practical opinions. Let that stand as

[003-111]written: I am writing only to relieve my conscience. Yet let me say

[003-112]also this: that from the first I have been consistent in having an

[003-113]intense aversion to any trial of my acts and thoughts by a moral

[003-114]standard. Another standard altogether has directed my life....

[003-115]As a matter of fact, the mob was playing in exceedingly foul fashion.

[003-116]Indeed, I have an idea that sheer robbery was going on around that

[003-117]gaming-table. The croupiers who sat at the two ends of it had not only

[003-118]to watch the stakes, but also to calculate the game—an immense amount

[003-119]of work for two men! As for the crowd itself—well, it consisted mostly

[003-120]of Frenchmen. Yet I was not then taking notes merely in order to be

[003-121]able to give you a description of roulette, but in order to get my

[003-122]bearings as to my behaviour when I myself should begin to play. For

[003-123]example, I noticed that nothing was more common than for another’s hand

[003-124]to stretch out and grab one’s winnings whenever one had won. Then there

[003-125]would arise a dispute, and frequently an uproar; and it would be a case

[003-126]of “I beg of you to prove, and to produce witnesses to the fact, that

[003-127]the stake is yours.”

[003-128]At first the proceedings were pure Greek to me. I could only divine and

[003-129]distinguish that stakes were hazarded on numbers, on “odd” or “even,”

[003-130]and on colours. [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]]’s money I decided to risk, that evening, only to

[003-131]the amount of 100 gülden. The thought that I was not going to play for

[003-132]myself quite unnerved me. It was an unpleasant sensation, and I tried

[003-133]hard to banish it. I had a feeling that, once I had begun to play for

[003-134]Polina, I should wreck my own fortunes. Also, I wonder if any one has

[003-135]_ever_ approached a gaming-table without falling an immediate prey to

[003-136]superstition? I began by pulling out fifty gülden, and staking them on

[003-137]“even.” The wheel spun and stopped at 13. I had lost! With a feeling

[003-138]like a sick qualm, as though I would like to make my way out of the

[003-139]crowd and go home, I staked another fifty gülden—this time on the red.

[003-140]The red turned up. Next time I staked the 100 gülden just where they

[003-141]lay—and again the red turned up. Again I staked the whole sum, and

[003-142]again the red turned up. Clutching my 400 gülden, I placed 200 of them

[003-143]on twelve figures, to see what would come of it. The result was that

[003-144]the croupier paid me out three times my total stake! Thus from 100

[003-145]gülden my store had grown to 800! Upon that such a curious, such an

[003-146]inexplicable, unwonted feeling overcame me that I decided to depart.

[003-147]Always the thought kept recurring to me that if I had been playing for

[003-148]myself alone I should never have had such [[chance|luck]]. Once more I staked the

[003-149]whole 800 gülden on the “even.” The wheel stopped at 4. I was paid out

[003-150]another 800 gülden, and, snatching up my pile of 1600, departed in

[003-151]search of Polina Alexandrovna.

[003-152]I found the whole party walking in the park, and was able to get an

[003-153]interview with her only after supper. This time [[marquis-de-griers|the Frenchman]] was

[003-154]absent from the meal, and the General seemed to be in a more expansive

[003-155]vein. Among other things, he thought it necessary to remind me that he

[003-156]would be sorry to see me playing at the gaming-tables. In his opinion,

[003-157]such conduct would greatly compromise him—especially if I were to lose

[003-158]much. “And even if you were to _win_ much I should be compromised,” he

[003-159]added in a meaning sort of way. “Of course I have no _right_ to order

[003-160]your actions, but you yourself will agree that...” As usual, he did not

[003-161]finish his sentence. I answered drily that I had very little money in

[003-162]my possession, and that, consequently, I was hardly in a position to

[003-163]indulge in any conspicuous play, even if I did gamble. At last, when

[003-164]ascending to my own room, I succeeded in handing Polina her winnings,

[003-165]and told her that, next time, I should not play for her.
