# Fortune

[[fortune]] in *The Gambler* carries its full double meaning: it is both the wealth that characters seek and the capricious force that governs whether they find it. The novel opens with Alexei announcing that "a change had taken place in my fortunes" (003-008), and from that moment forward, fortune operates as the novel's governing principle—unstable, impersonal, and ultimately cruel. The [[roulette|roulette wheel]] is fortune's visible instrument, transforming monetary wealth into pure [[probability|chance]] with every spin.

## Definition

In the novel's treatment, fortune is never stable. Characters possess fortunes (the [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s "co[[losing|loss]]al fortune" at 004-087), lose fortunes ("I should wreck my own fortunes" at 003-134), and live in terror of fortune's reversals. The word oscillates between its material sense (money, property, [[inheritance]]) and its metaphysical sense ([[chance|luck]], [[fate|destiny]], [[probability|chance]]), and this oscillation is the novel's deepest insight: in a world governed by roulette, wealth is indistinguishable from [[chance|luck]].

Fortune in *The Gambler* also carries a gendered dimension. The novel's women—[[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]], the [[grandmother|Grandmother]], [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]]—are defined by their relationship to fortune: [[polina-alexandrovna|Polina]] lacks it, the [[grandmother|Grandmother]] possesses it, [[mlle-blanche|Mlle. Blanche]] marries for it. Male characters, by contrast, attempt to win fortune at [[casino|the tables]] rather than inherit or marry into it.

## Role in The Gambler

### Narrative Function

Fortune structures the plot as a series of reversals. The [[grandmother|Grandmother]]'s arrival threatens to redistribute fortune; her [[loss|gambling loss]]es confirm its [[losing|loss]]; [[alexei-winning-streak|Alexei's winning streak]] streak]] at roulette creates a temporary fortune that restructures all social relationships. Every major plot development turns on fortune's redistribution—who has it, who stands to inherit it, who can win it at [[casino|the tables]].

The novel's compressed time frame intensifies fortune's volatility. Within days, [[general|the General]] goes from expecting a co[[losing|loss]]al fortune to facing [[ruin]]; Alexei goes from penniless tutor to possessor of thousands of francs and back a[[winning|gain]]. This compression makes fortune feel not like a stable possession but like a weather [[method|system]] passing through [[hotel|the hotel]].

### Key Passages

> "A change had taken place in my fortunes" (003-008) — the novel announces fortune as its subject from the opening pages.

> "I should wreck my own fortunes" (003-134) — Alexei's awareness that his gambling threatens his material future.

> "Her colossal fortune" (004-087) — the Grandmother defined by the fortune that others scheme to inherit.

> "Of my own fortunes, interested though I was" (017-014) — Alexei's self-consciousness about his own material [[stake]].

> "Her departure should save her fortune" (019-041) — fortune as something that can be saved or lost, the novel's constant calculus.

> "But, as ever, fortune seemed to be at my back" (023-166) — Alexei's gambler's [[superstition]], fortune personified as a companion.

## Dostoyevsky's Treatment

Dostoyevsky's treatment of fortune draws on his own experience of sudden wealth and sudden ruin. His roulette [[winning|winnings]] financed periods of prosperity; his [[losing|loss]]es returned him to debt and [[despair|desperation]]. The novel captures the psychological texture of fortune's reversals: not just the material consequences but the vertigo of watching one's entire social position dissolve and reform with each spin of [[roulette|the wheel]].

Unlike the classical tradition where Fortune is a goddess who rewards the virtuous, Dostoyevsky's fortune is purely mechanical. The [[roulette|roulette wheel]] has no moral preferences; it obeys only [[chance|probability]]. Characters who believe fortune favours them—as Alexei does during his winning streak (023-166)—are deluded. The mathematics of [[roulette|the game]] guarantee that fortune's apparent favour is temporary.

## Dostoyevsky vs. Contemporaries

Where Balzac's characters build fortunes through patient accumulation and social manipulation, Dostoyevsky's characters attempt to win fortune in a single night. This contrast reflects a broader philosophical difference: Balzac's world is one of gradual social mobility through [[marriage]] and inheritance; Dostoyevsky's is one of sudden reversals through gambling. *The Gambler* might be read as a [[russia|Russia]]n critique of the French novel's economic assumptions—an insistence that fortune, in the modern world, is not earned but won or lost at a spinning wheel.
