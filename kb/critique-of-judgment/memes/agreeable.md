## Definition

The agreeable (*Angenehme*) is that which "gratifies" a person in sensation — the merely subjective, sensory pleasure that an object produces in an individual. In Kant's First Moment (Quality) of the judgment of taste, the agreeable is the first and lowest of the three modes of satisfaction, distinguished from the beautiful (which pleases universally without interest) and the good (which pleases through concepts). The agreeable "rests entirely upon sensation" (004-004) and carries with it an interest in the object's continued existence.

## Kant's Account

Kant locates the agreeable in the opening moments of the "Analytic of the Beautiful" (§§3–5), where he distinguishes three kinds of satisfaction. The agreeable gratifies; the beautiful pleases; the good is approved. They are not interchangeable: "the pleasant, which, as such, represents the object in relation to sense" must be distinguished from the beautiful, which represents the object "in relation to reflection" (004-005).

The key differentiator is the claim to universality. When someone says "Canary wine is pleasant," they speak only for themselves — "another man may correct his expression and remind him that he should say: 'It is pleasant *to me*'" (007-002). By contrast, the judgment of beauty demands universal agreement even though it is grounded in feeling. The agreeable is thus private and incommunicable in a way that the beautiful is not.

Kant emphasizes that the agreeable necessarily involves interest: "that which gratifies a man is called pleasant; that which merely pleases him is called beautiful" (005-003). The agreeable promises gratification (003-002), and this gratification binds the satisfaction to the subject's private sensibility. Hunger is the best sauce — but only for the hungry person; the beautiful, by contrast, pleases independently of bodily need.

The distinction has practical consequences. A judgment that "the rose is pleasant" (to smell) is "although aesthetical and singular, not a judgment of taste but a judgment of sense" (008-009). It reports a private sensation, not a reflective evaluation. Kant allows that the agreeable may mingle with the beautiful — a beautiful painting may also be sensorially pleasant — but the two must not be confused: charm and emotion, both species of the agreeable, must be excluded from the determining ground of pure taste (014-001).

## Key Distinctions

- **Agreeable vs. Beautiful**: The agreeable gratifies privately through sensation; the beautiful pleases universally through reflection. The former involves interest; the latter is disinterested.
- **Agreeable vs. Good**: Both involve interest, but the good pleases through the concept of what the thing ought to be, while the agreeable pleases through immediate sensation. The good commands approval through reason; the agreeable merely solicits inclination.
- **Agreeable vs. Charm**: Charm is a particular species of the agreeable — the sensory appeal of colors, tones, or textures. All charm is agreeable, but not all agreeable things are charming.

## Role in the Critical System

The agreeable plays a foundational role in Kant's aesthetic theory by providing the baseline against which the distinctive character of the beautiful becomes visible. By showing that there exists a mode of satisfaction that is purely private (the agreeable), Kant can demonstrate that the beautiful — which claims universality despite being based on feeling — is something sui generis. The agreeable thus serves as the lower boundary of aesthetic experience, separating taste from mere sensory gratification.

## Key Passages

> "The pleasant, which, as such, represents the object in relation to sense... must first be brought under principles of reason by means of the concept of a purpose, in order to call it good, as an object of the will. But in order to call it beautiful, an entirely different relation to satisfaction is required."
> (004-005)

> "That which gratifies a man is called pleasant; that which merely pleases him is called beautiful; that which is esteemed [or approved] by him, i.e. that to which he ascribes an objective worth, is called good."
> (005-003)

> "In respect of the pleasant, everyone is content that his judgment, which he bases upon private feeling, and by which he says of an object that it pleases him, should be limited merely to his own person."
> (007-002)

> "As regards the agreeable everyone acknowledges that his judgment, which is based upon a private feeling, and by which he declares that an object pleases him, is valid only for his own person."
> (007-006)
