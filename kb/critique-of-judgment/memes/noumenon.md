## Definition

The noumenon (*Noumenon*), in Kant's critical philosophy, is the thing as it is in itself — the intelligible, supersensible ground of appearances (*phenomena*) that can be thought but never cognized through sensible intuition. In the *Critique of Judgment*, the noumenon functions as the indeterminate, supersensible substrate of both nature and freedom, providing the ultimate ground for the reconciliation of mechanical and teleological explanation (026-013).

## Kant's Account

In the *Critique of Aesthetic Judgment*, the noumenon appears in Kant's account of the mathematical sublime (§26). When we attempt to comprehend the infinite of the sensible world through mathematical estimation of magnitude, we discover the inadequacy of imagination. Yet the mind's "bare capability of thinking this infinite without contradiction requires in the human mind a faculty itself supersensible" — the faculty of reason and "its Idea of a noumenon, — which admits of no intuition, but which yet serves as the substrate for the intuition of the world, as a mere phenomenon" (026-013). The noumenon thus makes possible the comprehension of the infinite under a concept, surpassing all sensible standards.

In the *Critique of the Teleological Judgment*, Kant identifies the noumenon more specifically with the supersensible ground of nature's purposiveness. The mechanical and teleological principles of natural explanation, though irreducible to one another for our discursive understanding, must coalesce in a single higher principle. This principle lies in the noumenal substrate of nature, which is incognizable by us but thinkable as the ground of both mechanism and purposiveness.

The noumenon receives its fullest ethical significance in §§84–85, where Kant identifies man "considered as noumenon" as the final purpose of creation (084-006). Human beings, as natural beings, belong to the mechanism of nature; but as noumena — as moral subjects possessing freedom and the law of causality — they are the unconditioned end to which the whole of nature is teleologically subordinated. "Only in man, and only in him as subject of morality, do we meet with unconditioned legislation in respect of purposes, which therefore alone renders him capable of being a final purpose" (084-006).

## Key Distinctions

The noumenon must be distinguished from the phenomenon (the thing as it appears under the conditions of sensible intuition), from the transcendental Idea (a concept of reason to which no corresponding object can be given in experience), and from the thing in itself (*Ding an sich*) — though Kant often uses these terms interchangeably. In the third *Critique*, the noumenon functions as the supersensible substrate of humanity, nature, and their ultimate harmony, without being an object of theoretical cognition.

## Role in the Critical System

The noumenon serves as the keystone of Kant's critical architectonic in the third *Critique*. The gulf between nature (the domain of the understanding) and freedom (the domain of reason) is bridged by reflective judgment, which posits the supersensible as the common ground of both. The noumenon as the substrate of nature makes teleology thinkable; the noumenon as the moral subject makes the final purpose of creation intelligible. The transition from nature to freedom — the overarching project of the *Critique of Judgment* — is ultimately a transition within the noumenal ground of both.

## Key Passages

> "Nevertheless, the bare capability of thinking this infinite without contradiction requires in the human mind a faculty itself supersensible. For it is only by means of this faculty and its Idea of a noumenon, — which admits of no intuition, but which yet serves as the substrate for the intuition of the world, as a mere phenomenon, — that the infinite of the world of sense, in the pure intellectual estimation of magnitude, can be completely comprehended under a concept."
> (026-013)

> "Now we have in the world only one kind of beings whose causality is teleological, i.e. is directed to purposes and is at the same time so constituted that the law according to which they have to determine purposes for themselves is represented as unconditioned and independent of natural conditions, and yet as in itself necessary. The being of this kind is man, but man considered as noumenon; the only natural being in which we can recognise, on the side of its peculiar constitution, a supersensible faculty (freedom) and also the law of causality, together with its Object, which this faculty may propose to itself as highest purpose (the highest good in the world)."
> (084-006)
