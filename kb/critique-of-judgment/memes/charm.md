## Definition

Charm (*Reiz*) is the sensory gratification or pleasurable stimulation that an object may produce through its immediate sensible qualities — colors, tones, textures — independent of reflection. In Kant's aesthetic theory, charm belongs to the material or sensory element of a representation and must be distinguished from the formal ground of a pure judgment of taste.

## Kant's Account

Kant introduces charm in the "Analytic of the Beautiful" as a contaminant to pure aesthetic judgment. A judgment of taste is pure only when "charm and emotion have no influence" upon it (013-004). Charm operates at the level of mere sensory agreeableness rather than the reflective play of the cognitive faculties that grounds genuine beauty.

Kant develops this argument across §§13–14 of the Critique. He acknowledges that charms are "often not only taken account of... but are even mistaken for beauty" (013-003), and warns that taste remains "barbaric" when it "needs a mixture of charms and emotions in order that there may be satisfaction" (013-002). The persistent danger is that charm masquerades as a necessary ingredient of beauty, when in fact it is merely an "extraneous" addition (P01-029).

The distinction sharpens in §14, where Kant argues that charm is tied to the material of sensation — "the charm of colours or of the pleasant tones of instruments" (014-010) — whereas pure beauty concerns only form. He allows that charms "may indeed enliven" the representation but cautions that they "draw attention to themselves and destroy the beauty" when they dominate (014-008). The error of taking charm as beauty's ground is called a "common error and one very prejudicial to genuine, uncorrupted, well-founded taste" (014-007).

In his discussion of fine art (§44), Kant returns to charm in distinguishing merely "charming arts" — those that merely gratify — from beautiful art proper, which provokes reflective judgment. The concept also appears in his treatment of the sublime, where charm is associated with the beautiful as contrasted with the awe-inspiring sublime: the sublime "disdains charm" (029-032), whereas the beautiful may be compatible with it.

## Key Distinctions

- **Charm vs. Beauty**: Charm belongs to sensory matter; beauty belongs to form and reflective judgment. A judgment determined by charm is merely agreeable, not beautiful.
- **Charm vs. Emotion**: Both are "extraneous" to pure aesthetic judgment (013-001). Charm is sensory; emotion (*Rührung*) involves a felt response bordering on the pathological. Kant treats them as parallel contaminants.
- **Charm vs. Agreeable**: Charm is a species of the agreeable (*Angenehme*) — both involve sensory gratification without conceptual determination.

## Role in the Critical System

Charm serves a negative but essential function in Kant's critical system: by defining what pure aesthetic judgment *is not*, it clarifies what it *is*. The exclusion of charm from the determining ground of taste is a direct consequence of the Second Moment — the judgment of taste's claim to universal validity. If charm (which varies by individual sensibility) determined taste, the judgment could never lay claim to universal assent. Charm thus demarcates the boundary between the merely subjective (the agreeable) and the subjectively universal (the beautiful).

## Key Passages

> "A judgement of taste on which charm and emotion have no influence (although they may be bound up with the satisfaction in the beautiful), — which therefore has for its determining ground merely the purposiveness of the form, — is a pure judgement of taste."
> (013-004)

> "Nevertheless charms are often not only taken account of as contributors to the universal satisfaction in the beautiful, but are even mistaken for beauty, and the mere matter of satisfaction passed off for the form."
> (013-003)

> "The charm of colours or of the pleasant tones of instruments may be added, but the design [Zeichnung] in the former and the composition in the latter constitute the proper object of the pure judgement of taste."
> (014-010)

> "As no extraneous charm must mingle with the satisfaction in the beautiful..."
> (P01-029)
