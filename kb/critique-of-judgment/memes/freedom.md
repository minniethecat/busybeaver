## Definition

Freedom (*Freiheit*) is, in Kant's critical philosophy, the Idea of a causality that operates independently of natural determination — the power of a rational being to initiate action from the moral law rather than from sensible impulses. In the *Critique of Judgment*, freedom serves as a pivotal bridge concept: the domain of the concept of freedom (practical philosophy) must be reconciled with the domain of the concept of nature (theoretical philosophy), and it is precisely the faculty of Judgment that makes this reconciliation thinkable (P01-063; P04-025).

## Kant's Account

In the First Introduction, Kant frames the entire critical project in terms of bridging nature and freedom. The "barrier between Nature and Freedom" is what the Third Critique aims to overcome, and "if we once relinquish this, neither the concept of a possible *final purpose* nor that of an *intelligible world* has any foundation" (P01-063). The problem is structural: Understanding legislates for nature and prescribes what *is*; Reason legislates for freedom and prescribes what *ought to be*. Between these two legislations stands an "immeasurable gulf" — nature as appearance can have no influence on freedom, and freedom as noumenon cannot intervene in nature.

The solution Kant proposes is that Judgment provides a principle — the formal purposiveness of nature — which allows us to think of nature *as if* it were designed to accommodate the realisation of moral purposes (029-016). The beautiful prepares us to love something without interest, and the sublime reveals our supersensible vocation as rational beings capable of freedom. Both experiences point toward the supersensible substrate in which nature and freedom are unified (029-040).

The aesthetic experience of the sublime is particularly significant for freedom. In the dynamical sublime (§§28–29), we experience nature's might as fearful, yet discover within ourselves a faculty of resistance — our moral freedom — that is independent of nature. We are "determinable by the Idea of Freedom, as given *a priori* by Reason" (031-003). This practical determination reveals that the human subject is not merely a natural being but a moral agent whose worth transcends the entire realm of nature.

## Key Distinctions

Freedom in Kant's sense must be distinguished from mere arbitrariness (*Willkür*) — freedom in the moral sense is autonomy, the giving of the moral law to oneself by Reason. It is also distinct from natural causality, which operates according to mechanical laws. In the Third Critique, freedom is further distinguished from the mere feeling of liberation in aesthetic experience: while the free play of faculties in beauty involves a kind of freedom, it is the freedom of the cognitive powers from determinate concepts, not the transcendental freedom of the moral subject.

## Role in the Critical System

Freedom is the keystone of Kant's practical philosophy and the *Critique of Practical Reason*. The Third Critique's role is not to prove freedom — that was accomplished in the Second Critique — but to show how nature can be thought as amenable to the realisation of freedom's purposes. The principle of purposiveness allows reflective judgment to regard nature as a system suited to moral ends, thereby making the transition from theoretical to practical reason possible. Without this bridge, the entire critical edifice would stand divided into two incommensurable domains.
