# Heautonomy

## Definition

Heautonomy (*Heautonomie*) is Kant's technical term for the **self-legislation of reflective judgment** — the way judgment gives a law "not to nature (autonomy), but to itself (heautonomy), a law for its reflection upon nature" (P04-052).

Unlike the **autonomy** of the understanding, which prescribes laws to nature as its object, heautonomy is a legislation that judgment exercises over *its own activity*. The law of purposiveness — that nature is to be judged as if designed for our cognitive faculties — is not imposed on nature as a constitutive principle but adopted by judgment as a **subjective maxim** for the conduct of its own reflection.

## Kant's Account

### Heautonomy vs. Autonomy

Kant introduces the term heautonomy to mark a distinction that would otherwise collapse into a key ambiguity. The understanding is **autonomous**: it legislates to nature through its categories, and nature must conform. But reflective judgment does not legislate to nature — it legislates to *itself*.

"The determinant Judgment, and the autonomy of the first (which has mere subjective validity for the use of our cognitive faculty)" (071-003) — here Kant pairs determinant judgment with autonomy, but immediately qualifies it as having "mere subjective validity." The principle of purposiveness is a law judgment gives itself, not a law it discovers in nature.

This distinction resolves the apparent paradox of the third *Critique*: how can judgment have an a priori principle (the purposiveness of nature) without claiming that nature *really is* purposive? The answer is heautonomy — the principle is subjective, a law for reflection, not a determination of the object.

### Heautonomy and the Principle of Purposiveness

The principle that "nature specifies its universal laws into empirical ones in accordance with the form of a logical system" is the heautonomous law of judgment. Judgment does not find this principle in experience (it is a priori) but does not prescribe it to nature (it is not constitutive).

Instead, judgment prescribes it to *itself* as the condition under which systematic empirical knowledge is possible. This is the meaning of heautonomy: a law that governs the subject's own cognitive procedure, not the object.

### The Role in Reflective Judgment

Heautonomy is the mark of reflective as opposed to determinant judgment. Determinant judgment applies given universals to particulars; it needs no principle of its own because the understanding supplies the rules. But reflective judgment must *find* the universal for given particulars, and to do this systematically it needs a principle — a law for its own search procedure.

That self-given law is heautonomy. It enables reflective judgment to ascend from particular to universal without either an infinite regress of empirical rules or the dogmatic assertion of real teleology in nature.

## Key Distinctions

| Mode | Who legislates | To whom | Principle | Status |
|------|---------------|---------|-----------|--------|
| **Autonomy** | Understanding | Nature | Categories (causality, etc.) | Constitutive |
| **Heautonomy** | Reflective Judgment | Itself (the judging subject) | Purposiveness of nature | Regulative / subjective |
| **Heteronomy** | External authority (sense, tradition) | Judgment | Empirical rules, habit | Uncritical |

## Role in the Critical System

Heautonomy secures the **critical legitimacy** of the third *Critique*. Without it, the principle of purposiveness would either collapse into a constitutive claim about nature (dogmatic teleology) or be dismissed as merely psychological (Humean skepticism).

By articulating heautonomy, Kant provides a third option: a principle that is a priori (not derived from experience) but subjective (not imposed on objects). This is the methodological innovation that makes the *Critique of Judgment* possible as a critical enterprise — it is the first Critique of a faculty whose a priori principle is *for itself alone*.
