# Analogy

## Definition

Analogy (*Analogie*) in the critical philosophy of Kant is not merely a rhetorical figure but a **mode of cognition** by which we think what cannot be directly presented in intuition.

Unlike a direct schema (which presents a concept through a corresponding intuition), analogy presents through a comparison of relations: "a mere analogy in the use of a certain term." (P01-041)

The Editor notes that "induction, syllogism, analogy, do not really generate belief" in the theoretical sense (P01-068), yet analogy is indispensable when we must think beyond the bounds of possible experience — in teleology, morality, and symbolic presentation.

## Kant's Account

### Analogy in Teleological Judgment

The *Critique of Teleological Judgment* relies heavily on analogical reasoning. When we judge organisms as natural purposes, we draw "an analogy with the causality of purpose" (061-005) — we think nature *as if* it acted purposively, by analogy with human intentional action.

Kant insists this is "only according to the analogy of these last" (P04-035): we do not claim nature *has* intentions, only that we must proceed as if it did. "We can regard the faculty of desire according to the analogy of purpose" (P04-075), but this remains regulative, not constitutive.

### Analogy in the Beautiful and the Symbol

The concept of analogy is essential to the theory of the **symbol** in Kant. In §59, Kant argues that beauty is the *symbol of the morally good* — not by direct correspondence but by analogy.

The judgment of taste stands in an **analogical relation** to moral judgment: both claim universal validity, both are grounded in an a priori principle, and both involve a kind of disinterested satisfaction.

"The analogy between the pure judgement of taste and the moral judgement" (042-009) is the bridge between aesthetics and ethics.

### Analogy in Art

Kant extends the concept of analogy to artistic production. Genius operates through aesthetic Ideas that "occasion much thought without any definite concept being adequate to them."

In this process, "the analogy of art with the mode of expression in speech" (051-002) allows us to classify the arts according to the manner in which they express aesthetic Ideas.

"Eloquence may be compared (by analogy) with deportment in speech" (051-013), and architecture may be judged "by analogy with which we easily may judge the purposiveness of natural beings." (051-017)

### Analogy of Forms

In the famous "analogy of forms" passage, the Editor invokes analogy to explain the apparent design of natural organisms: "the analogy of forms, which with all their differences seem to be produced according to a common prototype" (P01-041).

This observation — that diverse species exhibit common structural patterns — strengthens the analogical argument for a common ground of natural purposiveness.

But Kant would insist this remains analogical, not demonstrative: "the argument from analogy is here without foundation" (P01-059) if taken as a proof rather than a regulative maxim.

### Analogy and the Supersensible

Analogy is the method of Kant for thinking the **supersensible substrate** that unifies nature and freedom. Since the supersensible can never be given in intuition, we can only approach it through analogy.

"By means of a certain analogy involved in our reflection upon the forms of nature" (060-005), the principle of judgment proceeds "according to the analogy with the subjective ground of the use of our cognitive faculties." (061-002)

Analogy is thus the cognitive tool proper to reflective judgment — the mode of thinking that reaches toward unity without claiming to possess it.

## Key Distinctions

| Mode | What it presents | How it presents | Domain |
|------|-----------------|----------------|--------|
| **Schema** | A pure concept (category) | Directly, through a corresponding intuition | Theoretical |
| **Symbol** | An Idea of Reason | Indirectly, by analogy of relations | Aesthetic/moral |
| **Analogy** | A relation, not an object | Comparison of relations, not properties | Reflective/regulative |

## Role in the Critical System

Analogy is the hidden method of the third *Critique*. Where the first *Critique* proceeds by schematism (direct presentation of categories in time) and the second *Critique* by the moral law (direct determination of the will), the third *Critique* proceeds by analogy — the indirect presentation of what cannot be directly intuited.

Whether we are judging beauty as the symbol of morality, organisms as natural purposes, or nature as a system of ends, we are reasoning analogically.

Analogy thus deserves recognition as the *methodological signature* of reflective judgment itself.
