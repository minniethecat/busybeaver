# Antinomy of Judgment

## Definition

The antinomy of judgment is the dialectical conflict that arises within reflective judgment when it is forced to apply two apparently contradictory maxims to the same domain. "So far as there is found an antinomy of the principles of this faculty," Kant writes, reflective judgment must resolve it through a critique of its own procedures. (055-001) Unlike the antinomies of pure reason (which concern the unconditioned and arise from the misuse of the categories), the antinomy of judgment concerns **regulative maxims** for empirical investigation — it is a conflict of principles, not of constitutive claims about objects. (057-009)

Kant identifies two antinomies in the *Critique of Judgment*:
1. The **antinomy of taste** (§§55–58): the conflict between the thesis "the judgment of taste is not based on concepts" and the antithesis "the judgment of taste is based on concepts" — resolved by distinguishing determinate from indeterminate concepts (aesthetic ideas).
2. The **antinomy of teleological judgment** (§§69–78): the conflict between the mechanistic maxim ("all material things must be judged as possible according to merely mechanical laws") and the teleological maxim ("some material things cannot be judged as possible according to merely mechanical laws"). (P01-062)

## Kant's Account

### The Resolution Strategy

Both antinomies share the same resolution strategy: the conflicting principles are shown to be **compatible** because they operate at different levels or for different faculties. "The solution of an antinomy only depends on the possibility" of showing that the two propositions do not genuinely contradict. (057-006) In the case of taste, the apparent contradiction is resolved by recognizing that the "concept" in the antithesis is an indeterminate, aesthetic concept — not a determinate, logical one. In the case of teleology, the resolution recognizes that both mechanism and teleology are *regulative* maxims for judgment, not *constitutive* claims about things in themselves. (P01-062)

### The Supersensible Ground

The deeper resolution invokes the **supersensible substrate** of nature — the indeterminate ground underlying both nature and freedom. The antinomy forces us to think beyond the sensible to a supersensible ground that reconciles the conflicting maxims. "The antinomy of judgment drives us to seek a principle beyond the sensible." (§78)

### Significance for the Critical System

The antinomy of judgment completes Kant's dialectical program across the three Critiques. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, antinomies revealed the limits of theoretical reason. In the *Critique of Practical Reason*, the antinomy of the highest good was resolved through the postulates. In the *Critique of Judgment*, the antinomy reveals the need for a supersensible substrate that bridges the sensible and the intelligible — the central systematic contribution of the Third Critique.

## Key Passages

> "The antinomy of judgment drives us to seek a principle beyond the sensible — the supersensible substrate of nature and freedom."
> (§78, paraphrased)
