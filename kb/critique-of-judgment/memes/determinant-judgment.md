# Determinant Judgment

## Definition

Determinant judgment (*bestimmende Urteilskraft*) is the mode of judgment that subsumes given particulars under already-given universal concepts or laws. Unlike reflective judgment, which must *find* the universal, determinant judgment merely *applies* an existing rule. "If the universal (the rule, the principle, the law) be given, the Judgement which subsumes the particular under it is *determinant*." (Introduction IV)

This is the mode of judgment operative in ordinary cognition and science: given the concept "dog," determinant judgment determines whether a particular animal falls under that concept. Determinant judgment does not need its own a priori principle — it borrows the universal from the understanding (in theoretical cognition) or from reason (in practical deliberation).

## Kant's Account

### Contrast with Reflective Judgment

The distinction between determinant and reflective judgment is the foundational structural move of the *Critique of Judgment*. While determinant judgment proceeds from universal to particular, reflective judgment proceeds from particular to universal. This distinction allows Kant to isolate the unique domain of reflective judgment — aesthetics and teleology — where no ready-made concept is available and the judging subject must rely on the feeling of pleasure or the concept of purpose.

### Determinant Judgment in the Critical System

In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, the schematism chapter established how determinant judgment applies the categories to intuitions via the transcendental schema. But this account left open the question: what guarantees that nature's empirical laws form a systematic unity? Determinant judgment can subsume under *given* laws, but it cannot guarantee that nature's laws are systematically connected — that is the task of reflective judgment and its principle of purposiveness.

### The Practical Analogue

In the practical domain, determinant judgment applies the moral law to particular actions — determining whether a given maxim can serve as universal law. Here too, the universal (the categorical imperative) is given by reason, not sought by judgment.
