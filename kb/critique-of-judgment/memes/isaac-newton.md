## Biography

Isaac Newton (1643–1727) was an English mathematician, physicist, and natural philosopher whose *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687) laid the foundation for classical mechanics. His law of universal gravitation and laws of motion provided a unified mathematical framework for explaining both terrestrial and celestial phenomena, establishing the paradigm of mechanistic explanation that dominated eighteenth-century science.

Newton also made foundational contributions to optics (demonstrating that white light is composed of a spectrum of colors), mathematics (co-inventing calculus alongside Leibniz), and astronomy. He held the Lucasian Chair of Mathematics at Cambridge and later served as Master of the Mint and President of the Royal Society.

## Relation to Kant

For Kant, Newton represented the definitive achievement of mechanistic science—the demonstration that nature could be understood through universal laws without recourse to teleological explanations. Kant repeatedly invokes Newton as the exemplar of scientific genius whose discoveries cannot be reduced to learnable rules but required an original talent.

Kant writes that it is "absurd to hope that another Newton will arise in the future who will explain to us the generation of a blade of grass." (075-007) This formulation captures the central problem of the *Critique of Teleological Judgment*: Newtonian mechanics, for all its explanatory power, cannot account for the internal purposiveness of organisms. Mechanism provides a necessary but insufficient framework; organisms demand that we judge them *as if* designed, even while we cannot prove design theoretically.

Newton also appears in Kant's discussion of genius (§47): "We can readily learn all that Newton has set forth in his immortal work... but we cannot learn to write inspired poetry." (047-003) This contrast between scientific discoverability and artistic originality grounds Kant's distinction between the teachable rules of science and the unteachable gift of [[Genius]].

## In the Critique of Judgment

Newton functions in the third *Critique* as a limit-concept: the maximal extension of mechanistic explanation against which the necessity of teleological judgment becomes visible. In the **Critique of Aesthetical Judgment**, Newton illustrates the difference between great science (which can be learned step by step) and artistic [[Genius]] (which cannot). In the **Critique of Teleological Judgment**, the "Newton of a blade of grass" argument (§75) demonstrates the irreducibility of organic purposiveness to mechanical causation.

This double role—as the pinnacle of mechanism and as its limit—makes Newton essential to Kant's argument that judgment requires its own *a priori* principle ([[Purposiveness]]) distinct from the categories of understanding. Without this principle, we could not recognize organisms as organized beings at all.
