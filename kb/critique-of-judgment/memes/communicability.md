Universal Communicability (*allgemeine Mitteilbarkeit*) is a foundational concept in Kant's [[Critique of Judgment|第三批判]] aesthetics, introduced in §9 of the "Analytic of the Beautiful." It designates the capacity of a representation or a feeling of pleasure to be shared universally among all judging subjects, independent of any determinate concept. Kant grounds the subjective universality of the judgment of taste on this communicability.

## Role in the Judgment of Taste

Kant argues that the judgment of taste demands universal assent not on the basis of concepts but on the basis of a subjective condition — the free play of the cognitive faculties ([[Imagination]] and [[Understanding]]). This condition, he claims, must admit of universal communicability:

> [009-003] … and it is only its universal communicability that is to be acknowledged in the judgment of taste.

The universal communicability of the mental state in the judgment of taste is what entitles the subject to demand agreement from everyone:

> [009-007] The subjective universal communicability of the mode of representation in a judgment of taste, since it is to be possible without presupposing a definite concept, can be nothing else than the state of mind in the free play of the Imagination and the Understanding.

## Relation to the Four Moments

In the **Second Moment** ([[Universality]]), Kant establishes that the judgment of taste claims subjective universality — validity for everyone. This claim rests on the presupposition of universal communicability. The **Third Moment** ([[Purposiveness]]) ties this to the form of purposiveness without purpose, and the **Fourth Moment** ([[Necessity]]) derives the exemplary necessity of taste from the idea of a common sense (*sensus communis*), which itself is grounded in the condition of universal communicability.

## Communicability and Pleasure

Kant extends the concept beyond cognition to feeling. He argues that the pleasure in the beautiful must itself be universally communicable:

> [009-012] … the sensation whose universal communicability is postulated by the judgment of taste.

This is a unique move: for Kant, it is not simply that we *think* the pleasure is shareable; rather, the very structure of aesthetic reflection presupposes communicability as a transcendental condition.

## Communicability and Sensus Communis

In §40, Kant links universal communicability to the idea of a **common sense** (*[[Sensus Communis]]*). The maxims of the common human understanding — to think for oneself, to think from the standpoint of everyone else, and to think consistently — are all conditions of communicability:

> [040-010] … faculty of judging *a priori* of the communicability of feelings that are bound up with a given representation.

The common sense is not an empirical consensus but a normative ideal: it is the idea of a faculty that judges according to the condition of universal communicability.

## Wider Significance

Kant sees universal communicability as having moral significance:

> [041-004] … yet the Idea of its universal communicability increases its worth in an almost moral direction.

This connects aesthetics to the larger architectonic of the [[Critique of Judgment|第三批判]]: taste, by cultivating communicability, prepares the subject for moral feeling.
