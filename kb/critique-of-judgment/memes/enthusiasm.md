# Enthusiasm

## Definition

Enthusiasm (*Enthusiasmus*) is a state of mind in which the **Idea of the good** is combined with powerful affect (*Affekt*), producing a strenuous yet temporary elevation of the mind. Kant defines it precisely: "The Idea of the good, joined with affection, is called enthusiasm. This state of mind seems to be sublime, so much so that it is commonly maintained that without it nothing great can be accomplished." (029-033) Unlike fanaticism, which is delusional, enthusiasm preserves a connection to rational Ideas.

## Kant's Account

### Enthusiasm and the Sublime

Kant situates enthusiasm within the Analytic of the Sublime. While the sublime proper arises from the conflict between sensible inadequacy and rational Ideas, enthusiasm is an affective participation in that conflict. It "seems to be sublime" — but Kant is careful: enthusiasm is not identical to the sublime, because it involves an element of affect that pure aesthetic judgment excludes. The sublime proper is a feeling of respect for our rational vocation; enthusiasm is the affective coloration that accompanies that feeling in certain contexts.

### Enthusiasm vs. Fanaticism

Kant draws a sharp distinction. "If enthusiasm is comparable to madness, fanaticism is comparable to mania." (029-040) Enthusiasm, though it may resemble delusion in its intensity, remains tethered to moral Ideas. Fanaticism (*Schwarmerei*), by contrast, claims a direct intuition of the supersensible — "a supposed direct inspiration and a great familiarity with the powers of heaven" — which Kant dismisses as "the death of all philosophy." Enthusiasm is a temporary transport; fanaticism is a permanent delusion.

### Historical Example

Kant illustrates enthusiasm through the "enthusiasm that the Jewish people in their theocratic state felt for their invisible King." (029-039) This enthusiasm, though directed at a supersensible object, was not fanaticism because it was mediated by the moral law: "the command of the invisible King alone can explain the enthusiasm that the Jewish people felt." The moral law, as a rational ground, saved this enthusiasm from degenerating into fanaticism.

## Key Distinctions

| State | Relation to Reason | Affective Quality | Kant's Judgment |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Enthusiasm** | Tethered to moral Ideas | Strenuous, elevated | Sublime-like, philosophically admissible |
| **Fanaticism** | Claims direct supersensible intuition | Delusional, manic | "Death of all philosophy" |
| **Pure Sublime** | Conflict: senses vs. Reason | Respect, not affect | The aesthetic standard |

## Role in the Critical System

Enthusiasm occupies a liminal position in Kant's aesthetics. It is not a pure aesthetic judgment — being tainted by affect and interest — but it points toward the moral destination of aesthetic experience. By connecting strong feeling to the Idea of the good, enthusiasm exemplifies the transition from aesthetic to moral consciousness that the third *Critique* makes possible. It shows that even our most intense affective states can be governed by Reason, provided they remain anchored to Ideas rather than delusions.
