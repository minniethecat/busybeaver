## Biography

Immanuel Kant was born in 1724 in Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad), where he spent his entire life. He studied at the University of Königsberg and later became a professor of logic and metaphysics there. His early work focused on natural philosophy and cosmology.

Kant's mature philosophical project began with the *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781, second edition 1787), which established the boundaries of theoretical knowledge.

This was followed by the *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788), grounding morality in the autonomy of rational will. The *Critique of Judgment* (1790) completed the critical system by examining judgment as a mediating power between understanding and reason.

## Philosophical System

Kant's critical philosophy rests on a fundamental distinction between phenomena (things as they appear) and noumena (things in themselves). Theoretical knowledge is limited to possible experience, where the mind's *a priori* forms of intuition and categories of understanding structure sensory data.

His practical philosophy centers on the categorical imperative—the principle that moral actions must be guided by maxims that could be willed as universal law. Morality derives from rational autonomy, not from empirical inclination.

The *Critique of Judgment* occupies a pivotal position in the critical system. As the Editor's Introduction notes, "Kant himself regarded it as the coping-stone of his critical edifice." (P01-002)

This third Critique bridges the "great gulf" between the theoretical domain of nature and the practical domain of freedom, proposing that judgment—through the principle of purposiveness—enables us to view nature as amenable to moral purpose.

## The Critical Project

Kant's three Critiques correspond to a tripartite division of the higher mental faculties:

| Faculty | Critique | Domain | *A Priori* Principle |
|---------|----------|--------|----------------------|
| Understanding | *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781/1787) | Nature / Theoretical | Conformity to law |
| Reason | *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788) | Freedom / Practical | The moral law |
| Judgment | *Critique of Judgment* (1790) | Art and Nature | Purposiveness |

The *Critique of Judgment* "occupies a position intermediate between Understanding and Reason." (P01-010) Without this mediating role, the legislative authority of understanding over nature and that of reason over freedom would remain disconnected.

## In the Critique of Judgment

The *Critique of Judgment* is divided into two main parts: the **Critique of Aesthetical Judgment**, examining judgments of [[Beauty]] and [[The Sublime]], and the **Critique of Teleological Judgment**, examining purposiveness in organisms and nature as a whole.

In the first part, Kant analyzes the judgment of [[Taste]] as a subjective yet universally valid faculty. The experience of the beautiful involves the [[Free Play of Faculties|free play]] of imagination and understanding, producing a [[Disinterestedness|disinterested]] satisfaction.

In the second part, Kant examines how we must judge [[Natural Purpose|organized beings]] as if designed, even though we cannot prove design theoretically. This reflective judgment leads to the concept of the [[Final Purpose|final purpose]] of nature, which Kant identifies with humanity as a moral being.

The culmination is the moral proof—the argument that nature's purposiveness, combined with our moral vocation, gives practical grounds for belief in a moral author of the world.
