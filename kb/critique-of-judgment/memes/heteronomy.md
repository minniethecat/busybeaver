# Heteronomy of Taste

## Definition

Heteronomy (*Heteronomie*) of taste is the subjection of aesthetic judgment to **external determining grounds** — whether empirical (charm, emotion) or rational (concepts of perfection) — rather than to the autonomous principle of the subject's own feeling of pleasure. Kant declares: "If anyone judges objects merely according to concepts, all representation of beauty is lost. Thus there can be no rule by which anyone can be compelled to acknowledge anything as beautiful." To appeal to the judgment of others as a determining ground of one's own would be heteronomy. (032-004)

## Kant's Account

### Heteronomy vs. Autonomy of Taste

The autonomy of taste is a defining feature of Kant's aesthetic theory. Taste must be "a faculty for judging of an object in reference to the free conformity to law of the imagination" — not a faculty for applying given rules. (032-004) Heteronomy arises whenever the subject lets something other than their own free judgment determine the verdict: the approval of others, the charm of sensation, or the concept of what the object *ought* to be. In each case, "taste would not be autonomous, as beseems a judgment of taste, but heteronomous." (058-013)

### Prejudice as Heteronomy of Reason

Kant extends the logic of heteronomy to cognition itself. Prejudice — judging without sufficient reflection — "leads to passivity, and therefore to heteronomy of the Reason." (040-006) Just as the aesthetic subject must judge for themselves, the rational subject must think for themselves. Heteronomy in either domain means surrendering the spontaneity that defines the judging (or thinking) subject as such.

### In Teleological Explanation

The heteronomy/autonomy distinction also structures Kant's account of scientific method. Mechanism in nature operates autonomously (by its own laws), while teleological explanation must be brought in from without. Kant contrasts "the autonomy of the first (which must regulate all particular empirical laws) with the heteronomy of the second." (071-003) Mechanism governs nature on its own terms; teleology is an external principle we bring to bear, whose legitimacy is merely regulative. Similarly, empirical aesthetic judging can be "subjected to a heteronomy of empirical laws" when it departs from the pure judgment of taste. (059-005)

## Key Distinctions

| Domain | Autonomy | Heteronomy |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Taste** | Judging by one's own feeling of pleasure | Judging by others' opinions, charm, or rules |
| **Reason** | Thinking for oneself (sapere aude) | Prejudice, passivity, reliance on authority |
| **Nature** | Mechanical explanation (internal laws of nature) | Teleological explanation (external purposiveness) |

## Role in the Critical System

Heteronomy is Kant's diagnostic tool across all three Critiques. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, heteronomy of Reason is the source of dialectical illusion. In the *Critique of Practical Reason*, heteronomy of the will is the defining mark of all non-moral practical principles. In the *Critique of Judgment*, heteronomy of taste is the corruption that destroys aesthetic purity. The recurrence of this structure reveals the deep unity of Kant's critical project: autonomy — of cognition, willing, and judging — is the signature of a rational being.
