# Disinterestedness

## Definition

Disinterestedness (*Interesselosigkeit*) is the defining characteristic of the **first moment** of the judgment of taste in Kant's *Critique of Judgment*. A pure judgment of taste must be entirely disinterested: the pleasure we take in the beautiful must be independent of any interest in the real existence of the object. (002-001) "Taste is the faculty of judging an object or a method of representing it by an *entirely disinterested* satisfaction or dissatisfaction." (005-008) This distinguishes aesthetic pleasure from both the agreeable (which gratifies the senses and involves an interest) and the good (which we approve through reason and which also involves an interest).

Kant's doctrine was anticipated by earlier thinkers: "the aesthetical judgement about Beauty is according to *quality* disinterested; a point which had been laid down by such different writers as Hutcheson and Moses Mendelssohn." (P01-016) But Kant gives it systematic grounding within the critical philosophy, making it the foundation upon which the other three moments (quantity, relation, modality) are built.

## Kant's Account

### Interest and the Three Kinds of Satisfaction

Kant distinguishes three kinds of satisfaction, each with a different relation to interest:

| Kind | Definition | Interest? | Example |
|------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **The Agreeable** | Gratifies the senses | Yes — pathological | A fine meal |
| **The Beautiful** | Pleases through mere form | No — disinterested | A rose contemplated |
| **The Good** | Approved by reason | Yes — practical | A virtuous act |

The agreeable "gratifies" (*vergnügt*), the beautiful "pleases" (*gefällt*), the good is "esteemed" (*geschätzt*). Of the three, only the pleasure in the beautiful is "a disinterested and *free* satisfaction; for no interest, either of sense or of reason, extorts approval." (005-003)

### Why Disinterestedness Matters

Disinterestedness is the foundation for the **universality** claimed by judgments of taste. If my pleasure in the beautiful were based on a private interest (as is pleasure in the agreeable), it could not legitimately demand the assent of others. But because the judgment of taste abstracts from all private conditions, it can lay claim to universal validity. (006-002) Disinterestedness thus grounds the second moment — the judgment of taste's claim to be universally valid without being based on a concept.

### The Moral Connection

The disinterested character of aesthetic pleasure prepares the subject for moral feeling. "The Beautiful prepares us to love disinterestedly something, even nature itself." (029-021) By training us to take pleasure in something without desiring to possess it, aesthetic experience cultivates the disposition required for moral action — acting from duty rather than inclination.
