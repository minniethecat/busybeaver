# Beauty

## Definition

Beauty (*Schönheit*, *das Schöne*) is the object of a pure judgment of taste in Kant's aesthetics. The beautiful is defined through the four moments of taste: it is the object of a **disinterested** satisfaction (quality) that pleases **universally** without a concept (quantity), exhibits **purposiveness without purpose** (relation), and is the object of a **necessary** satisfaction (modality). (001-001, 005-008)

"The Beautiful, the judging of which has at its basis a merely formal purposiveness, i.e. a purposiveness without purpose, is quite independent of the concept of the Good." (015-003) Beauty is distinct from both the agreeable (which gratifies through sensation) and the good (which is approved through reason). It occupies a middle position: like the agreeable, it pleases immediately; like the good, it claims universal validity.

## Kant's Account

### Free and Dependent Beauty

Kant distinguishes **free beauty** (*pulchritudo vaga*) from **dependent beauty** (*pulchritudo adhaerens*). Free beauty presupposes no concept of what the object ought to be — flowers, abstract patterns, and "music without words" are Kant's examples. Dependent beauty presupposes a concept of the object's perfection — a beautiful church or a beautiful horse involves judging the object against what it should be. Only the judgment of free beauty is a *pure* judgment of taste. (§16)

### Beauty as Symbol of Morality

In a crucial systematic move (§59), Kant declares that "the beautiful is the symbol of the morally good." Beauty symbolizes morality because both involve: (1) immediate pleasure, (2) disinterestedness, (3) freedom (of imagination in aesthetics, of will in ethics), and (4) universality. This analogy makes possible the transition from aesthetic feeling to moral feeling — "the Beautiful prepares us to love disinterestedly something, even nature itself." (029-021)

### Beauty and the Supersensible

The judgment of beauty ultimately points to the supersensible: "the pleasure in the beautiful is... the feeling that the representation of the object harmonises with the faculty of concepts of the understanding in their free play — which feeling must therefore have its ground in the supersensible substrate of humanity." (§59, paraphrased)
