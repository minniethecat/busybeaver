# Transition (Transition)

## Definition

Transition (*Ubergang*) is the passage from one domain or faculty to another, which Kant identifies as the fundamental problem of the critical system. The *Critique of Judgment* exists to effect a transition: "Judgement will bring about a transition from the pure faculty of knowledge, i.e., from the realm of concepts of nature, to the realm of the concept of freedom." (P04-027) Without this transition, the gulf between theoretical and practical Reason would remain unbridgeable.

## Kant's Account

### The Gulf Between Nature and Freedom

The critical system reveals an unbridgeable divide. The concept of nature operates in the sensible world under deterministic laws; the concept of freedom operates in the supersensible world under moral laws. "No transition is possible from the first to the second by means of the theoretical use of Reason." (P04-022) The understanding legislates for nature; Reason legislates for freedom — and neither can encroach on the other's territory. Yet they must be connected, because the moral law *ought* to have effects in the sensible world.

### Judgment as Mediator

The power of judgment fills this gap. Kant argues that Judgment "makes possible the transition from the conformity to law in accordance with the former to the final purpose in accordance with the latter." (P04-084) Specifically, the principle of purposiveness — nature's amenability to our cognitive faculties — creates a bridge: "It makes possible the transition from the realm of the concept of nature to that of the concept of freedom." (P04-082) Taste, in particular, "makes possible the transition, without any violent leap, from the charm of sense to the habitual moral interest." (059-009)

### Propadeutic to Theology

The transition concept also operates in the teleological domain. Kant asks whether physico-theology can serve "as a propaedeutic or transition to Theology." (068-009) He answers cautiously: teleological judgment prepares the mind for theology but cannot constitute it. "No science belongs to the transition from one to the other, because both rest on heterogeneous principles." (079-003)

## Key Distinctions

| Term | Relation to Transition |
|------|----------------------|
| **Gulf (Kluft)** | The problem that transition solves — the separation of nature and freedom |
| **Purposiveness** | The principle that makes transition thinkable |
| **Reflective Judgment** | The faculty that executes the transition |
| **Supersensible Substrate** | The ontological ground that underlies both domains and enables transition |

## Role in the Critical System

The transition is not a peripheral concept but **the raison d'etre** of the third *Critique*. Kant's entire critical project depends on showing that the theoretical and practical domains — though legislatively separate — are not ontologically severed. The principle of purposiveness provides "a common principle, so that a transition from one to another and so on to the connection of both is possible." (P04-049) Without the transition accomplished by reflective judgment, the moral law would remain an ought with no possible bridge to the is of nature.
