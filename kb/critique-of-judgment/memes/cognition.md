## Definition

Cognition (*Erkenntnis*) is the activity of acquiring knowledge through concepts, grounded in the accord between the cognitive faculties — the [[Imagination]] (which collects the manifold of intuition) and the Understanding (which unifies this manifold under concepts) (021-004). In Kant's critical system, cognition is the domain of theoretical reason: it determines what we can know about objects through the application of *a priori* concepts to sensible intuition.

Cognition is contrasted with aesthetic judgment throughout the third *Critique*. The very first sentence of the work declares: "The judgment of taste is therefore not a judgment of cognition, and is consequently not logical but aesthetical" (001-001).

## Kant's Account

### Cognition and the Cognitive Faculties

The "faculty of cognition from principles *a priori*" is what Kant calls pure Reason in its theoretical employment (P03-001). The cognitive faculty in general comprises the Understanding — which supplies concepts — and Reason — which supplies Ideas that regulate the Understanding's use. The *Critique of Pure Reason* established the limits of this faculty; the *Critique of Judgment* investigates whether Judgment, as an intermediate cognitive faculty, possesses its own *a priori* principle (P03-004, P03-005).

### The Accord of Cognitive Powers

In any act of cognition, the Imagination collects the manifold of sense and the Understanding unifies it under concepts. The proportion between these two powers varies depending on the object given (021-004). When this proportion is optimally balanced — when the Imagination and Understanding are in "free play" — the resulting feeling is one of pleasure, and this is the ground of the judgment of [[taste]].

Crucially, this accord of cognitive powers is *felt*, not conceptualized: "this accordance can only be determined by feeling (not according to concepts)" (021-004). This is why aesthetic judgment is subjective yet claims universal validity — the harmonious proportion of cognitive faculties is a condition of cognition in general, and therefore must be communicable to all judging subjects.

### Cognition vs. Aesthetic Judgment

Kant draws a sharp distinction throughout the third *Critique*:

| | Cognition (Logical Judgment) | Aesthetic Judgment |
|---|---|---|
| **Determining ground** | Concept of the object | Feeling of the subject |
| **What it provides** | Knowledge of the object | No cognition whatsoever (015-009) |
| **Faculty** | Understanding | Judgment (reflective) |
| **Universality** | Objective | Subjective (yet universal) |

An aesthetic judgment "gives absolutely no cognition (not even a confused cognition) of the Object; this is only supplied by a logical judgement" (015-009). To confuse the two is to misunderstand the unique nature of aesthetic experience.

### Universal Communicability

Cognitions must admit of universal communicability — otherwise there would be no harmony between representation and object, leading to scepticism (021-002). Because the state of mind that accompanies cognition (the accord of cognitive powers) must itself be communicable, and because the feeling of this accord is what taste judges, Kant derives the necessity of a [[sensus-communis|common sense]] (*sensus communis*) as the subjective condition of all communicable knowledge (021-004).

## Key Distinctions

### Cognition vs. Knowledge

While often used interchangeably in translation, Kant distinguishes *Erkenntnis* (cognition) as the *process* by which representations become knowledge, from *Wissen* (knowledge) as the *product*. Cognition involves the active synthesis of intuition and concept; a judgment of cognition determines an object through a concept.

### Theoretical vs. Practical Cognition

Theoretical cognition concerns what *is* — the domain of nature and the Understanding. Practical cognition concerns what *ought to be* — the domain of freedom and Reason. The *Critique of Judgment* investigates whether there is a bridge between these two domains, grounded in the faculty of [[reflective-judgment|reflective judgment]].

## Role in the Critical System

Cognition serves as the backdrop against which aesthetic judgment is defined. By establishing that taste is *not* cognition, Kant secures the autonomy of the aesthetic domain while simultaneously grounding its claim to universality in the same cognitive faculties that make knowledge possible. This move is central to the architectonic project of the third *Critique*: to show that the faculty of Judgment mediates between Understanding (cognition of nature) and Reason (the moral law), not by providing cognition, but by making nature amenable to our cognitive and moral purposes.
