# Satisfaction (Wohlgefallen)

## Definition

Satisfaction (*Wohlgefallen*) is the genus under which Kant classifies all forms of pleasure or liking in relation to the feeling of pleasure and pain. In the Analytic of the Beautiful, Kant distinguishes three species: the **pleasant** (*das Angenehme*), which gratifies through sensation; the **beautiful** (*das Schöne*), which pleases through disinterested reflection; and the **good** (*das Gute*), which is esteemed through Reason by means of a concept. (005-003) Each species designates a distinct relation of representations to the feeling of pleasure and pain, and each carries a different relationship to interest and the faculty of desire. (005-002)

## Kant's Account

### Three Kinds of Satisfaction

Kant introduces the tripartite classification in the First Moment of the Analytic of the Beautiful, where it serves to isolate the unique character of the judgment of taste. The pleasant gratifies immediately and is "pathologically conditioned (by stimuli)." (005-002) The good pleases through the concept of a purpose and brings with it a "pure practical satisfaction" determined by the represented connection of the subject with the *existence* of the object. (005-002) The beautiful alone is "contemplative": it is a judgment "which, indifferent as regards the being of an object, compares its character with the feeling of pleasure and pain" — a judgment that is "not directed to concepts" and is "not a cognitive judgment." (005-002)

This threefold scheme maps onto three distinct modes of the subject's relation to the object. The pleasant concerns irrational animals as well as humans; beauty concerns only humans "i.e. animal, but still rational, beings — not merely *quâ* rational (e.g. spirits), but *quâ* animal also"; the good concerns every rational being in general. (005-003)

### Interest and Freedom

The decisive criterion for classifying satisfaction is the presence or absence of **interest**, defined as "the satisfaction that we combine with the representation of the existence of an object." (002-002) Both the pleasant and the good are necessarily bound up with interest: the pleasant because it gratifies inclination, the good because the will, determined by Reason, takes an interest in the existence of its object. The beautiful alone is "a disinterested and *free* satisfaction; for no interest, either of Sense or of Reason, here forces our assent." (005-003)

Kant refines this further: the three kinds of satisfaction correspond respectively to **inclination**, **favour**, and **respect**. (005-004) Inclination (pleasant) and respect (good) both determine the will — "all interest presupposes or generates a want; and, as the determining ground of assent, it leaves the judgment about the object no longer free." (005-005) Favour alone — the satisfaction in the beautiful — is free, for it involves no want and imposes no determination on the will.

### Satisfaction and the Faculty of Desire

The distinction among the three kinds of satisfaction turns on their different relations to the faculty of desire. The pleasant and the good "have both a reference to the faculty of desire" — the pleasant pathologically, the good practically. (005-002) This reference to desire is precisely what the judgment of taste lacks. In judging an object beautiful, the subject is conscious of a satisfaction that makes no claim on the existence of the object. The satisfaction is purely contemplative: "we do not need to know whether we are concerned with the real existence of the thing or not" in aesthetic judgment. (002-003)

### The Satisfaction of the Sublime

Although the Analytic of the Beautiful centers on the threefold classification, Kant later extends the analysis to the sublime. The satisfaction in the sublime "does not so much involve a positive pleasure as admiration or respect, and deserves to be called a negative pleasure." (023-005) Unlike the beautiful, which produces a direct feeling of the furtherance of life, the satisfaction in the sublime arises indirectly — "it is produced by the feeling of a momentary checking of the vital powers, and a consequent stronger outflow of them." The sublime satisfaction thus occupies a peculiar position: it is disinterested yet involves the rational Idea, bridging the gap between aesthetic and moral satisfaction.

## Key Distinctions

| | Pleasant | Beautiful | Good |
|---|---------|-----------|------|
| **Kant's term** | *das Angenehme* | *das Schöne* | *das Gute* |
| **Mode of satisfaction** | Gratification | Favour | Esteem / Respect |
| **Relation to interest** | Bound with interest of Sense | Disinterested | Bound with interest of Reason |
| **Relation to desire** | Pathologically conditioned | Contemplative (no reference) | Practically determined |
| **Relation to concept** | No concept needed | No determinate concept | Requires a concept of purpose |
| **Scope of valid subjects** | Irrational animals + humans | Humans (animal + rational) | All rational beings |
| **Freedom of judgment** | Unfree (determined by inclination) | Free | Unfree (determined by moral law) |

## Role in the Critical System

The classification of satisfaction is the foundation of the entire Analytic of the Beautiful. By showing that the beautiful is the only satisfaction that is both disinterested and universally valid, Kant secures the autonomy of aesthetic judgment against reduction to either sensory gratification (empiricism) or rational perfection (rationalism). The first moment of the Analytic — quality — is framed entirely through the isolation of the beautiful among the three species of satisfaction.

This classification also establishes the structural symmetry among the three *Critiques*. Just as the *Critique of Pure Reason* distinguishes theoretical cognition from other modes of representation, and the *Critique of Practical Reason* distinguishes the moral law from other practical principles, the *Critique of Judgment* distinguishes aesthetic satisfaction from sensory and rational satisfaction. The three Critiques thus map onto the three species of satisfaction: cognition (Understanding), desire (Reason), and the feeling of pleasure and pain (Judgment).

## Key Passages

> "The pleasant and the good have both a reference to the faculty of desire; and they bring with them — the former a satisfaction pathologically conditioned (by stimuli), the latter a pure practical satisfaction, which is determined not merely by the representation of the object, but also by the represented connection of the subject with the existence of the object."
> (005-002)

> "The Pleasant, the Beautiful, and the Good, designate then, three different relations of representations to the feeling of pleasure and pain, in reference to which we distinguish from each other objects or methods of representing them. ... That which GRATIFIES a man is called *pleasant*; that which merely PLEASES him is *beautiful*; that which is ESTEEMED by him, i.e. that to which he accords an objective worth, is *good*."
> (005-003)

> "Of all these three kinds of satisfaction, that of taste in the Beautiful is alone a disinterested and *free* satisfaction; for no interest, either of Sense or of Reason, here forces our assent."
> (005-003)

> "All interest presupposes or generates a want; and, as the determining ground of assent, it leaves the judgment about the object no longer free."
> (005-005)
