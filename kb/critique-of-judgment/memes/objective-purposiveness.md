# Objective Purposiveness

## Definition

Objective purposiveness (*objektive Zweckmäßigkeit*) is the accordance of a thing with a **determinate concept** of what it ought to be — its suitability for a specific end or purpose. Unlike subjective purposiveness, which concerns only the subject's feeling of cognitive harmony, objective purposiveness requires reference to a concept and thus presupposes "the reference of the object to a determinate purpose." (015-003) Kant distinguishes it sharply: "To judge of objective purposiveness we always need not only the concept of a purpose but also the ontology of the object." (015-006)

## Kant's Account

### The Need for a Concept

Objective purposiveness cannot be judged by mere feeling. "In order to cognise, even empirically, objective purposiveness in a certain object" (P04-079), we must know what the thing is *for*. This requires a concept of the thing's purpose — a concept supplied either by human artifacts (where the purpose is imposed externally) or by organisms (where the purpose appears internal to the thing itself). The aesthetic judgment, by contrast, "presupposes an objective purposiveness, i.e. the reference of the object to a determinate purpose" only when it is impure — mixed with concepts of perfection or utility. (015-003)

### In Organisms: Internal Purposiveness

The paradigm of objective purposiveness in nature is the **organism**. An organism is "a natural purpose" (*Naturzweck*) because its parts exist *for the sake of* the whole and the whole exists *through* its parts. In observing living beings, "their objective purposiveness also is commonly taken into consideration" (048-004) — we cannot help but see the eye as *for* seeing, the heart as *for* pumping blood. This is not an aesthetic judgment but a teleological one, grounded in objective purposiveness.

### External vs. Internal Purposiveness

Kant further distinguishes between **external** and **internal** objective purposiveness. External purposiveness is the usefulness of one thing *for* another (grass for cattle, cattle for humans). Kant regards this as a relatively weak form: "Consequently the objective purposiveness, which is based upon advantage, is not an objective purposiveness of the things in themselves." (063-008) True objective purposiveness is **internal** — the self-organizing character of living beings. "An organized being is not a mere machine... it possesses formative power." (§65)

### The Regulative Status

Crucially, objective purposiveness in nature is **not constitutive** but **regulative**. We must judge organisms *as if* they were designed, but we cannot assert that they *are* designed. "Objective purposiveness, as a principle of the possibility of things of nature, is so far removed from being necessarily bound up with the concept of nature that it is rather just that upon which we lean in order to prove the contingency of nature." (061-003) Objective purposiveness reveals nature's contingency with respect to our understanding — it points toward but does not establish a divine designer.

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Objective Purposiveness | Subjective Purposiveness |
|--------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Judgment type** | Teleological | Aesthetic |
| **Requires concept?** | Yes | No |
| **Ground** | Suitability for an end | Free play of faculties |
| **Examples** | Eye for seeing; heart for pumping | A flower found beautiful |
| **Status** | Regulative | Constitutive for feeling |

## Role in the Critical System

Objective purposiveness is the central concept of the *Critique of Teleological Judgment*. It allows Kant to account for our necessary mode of judging organisms — we cannot understand them mechanistically, so we must employ teleological principles — without committing to the metaphysical claim that nature is actually designed. This regulative use of objective purposiveness bridges the mechanistic worldview of the first *Critique* with the moral purposiveness of the second, preparing the transition to the [[Moral Teleology|moral proof]] of God's existence as a postulate of practical reason.
