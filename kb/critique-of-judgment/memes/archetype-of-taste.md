## Definition

The archetype of taste (*Urbild des Geschmacks*) is the highest model according to which every judgment of taste must be made. Kant defines it as "a mere Idea, which every one must produce in himself; and according to which he must judge every Object of taste, every example of judgement by taste, and even the taste of every one" (017-004). Since it rests on an indeterminate Idea that Reason has of a maximum, and can only be presented in an individual intuition rather than through concepts, Kant renames it the *Ideal of the beautiful*. It is attainable only in the human figure — the sole object in the world whose purpose is determined by Reason itself.

## Kant's Account

### Why No Objective Rule of Taste Exists

Kant opens §17 by rejecting the possibility of an objective rule of taste "which shall determine by means of concepts what is beautiful" (017-001). Every judgment of taste is aesthetic — its determining ground is the subject's feeling, not a concept of the object. The universal communicability of the feeling of the beautiful, confirmed by the agreement of all times and peoples, provides an "empirical criterion, although weak and hardly sufficing for probability" (017-002), but it cannot furnish a rule.

Because there can be no concept-based criterion, we inevitably treat some products of taste as *exemplary* — not as rules to be imitated, but as models that awaken the Idea of taste within us. "He who imitates a model shows, no doubt, in so far as he attains to it, skill; but only shows taste in so far as he can judge of this model itself" (017-004).

### The Archetype as Idea, the Ideal as Presentation

Kant draws a precise distinction between *Idea* and *Ideal*: "Idea properly means a rational concept, and Ideal the representation of an individual being, regarded as adequate to an Idea" (017-004). The archetype of taste — the highest model — is an Idea of Reason: a concept of a maximum that can never be fully given in experience. But when this Idea is presented in an individual form (not through concepts but through the Imagination), it becomes an *Ideal*.

"Hence that archetype of taste, which certainly rests on the indeterminate Idea that Reason has of a maximum, but which cannot be represented by concepts, but only in an individual presentation, is better called the Ideal of the beautiful" (017-004).

### Why Only the Human Figure Admits an Ideal

Kant's most striking claim in §17 is that the Ideal of beauty is possible only for the human figure. An Ideal of beautiful flowers, furniture, or views is "inconceivable" (017-008). Even objects of adherent beauty — a dwelling-house, a tree, a garden — cannot have an Ideal because "their purpose is not sufficiently determined and fixed by the concept" (017-009).

What makes the human figure unique is that it is the only object in the world that carries its purpose within itself, determined by Reason: "The only being which has the purpose of its existence in itself is man, who can determine his purposes by Reason... This man is, then, alone of all objects in the world, susceptible of an Ideal of beauty; as it is only humanity in his person, as an intelligence, that is susceptible of the Ideal of perfection" (017-009).

### The Two Elements of the Ideal

The Ideal of the human figure comprises two components:

1. **The aesthetic normal Idea**: "an individual intuition (of the Imagination), representing the standard of our judgment [upon man] as a thing belonging to a particular animal species" (017-010). This is derived empirically — the average form of the species, as it were — and serves as the morphological baseline.

2. **The rational Idea**: "which makes the purposes of humanity, so far as they cannot be sensibly represented, the principle for judging of a figure through which, as their phenomenal effect, those purposes are revealed" (017-010). This is the moral dimension — the visible expression of humanity's rational-moral vocation.

The Ideal of beauty is thus not merely a matter of pleasing proportions. It is the visible manifestation of the moral destination of humanity — which is why Kant connects the archetype of taste to the aesthetic Ideas in §51: "the aesthetical Idea (the archetype or original image) is fundamental to both [sculpture and architecture]" (051-009).

## Key Distinctions

### Archetype of Taste vs. Empirical Criterion

| | Archetype of Taste (Ideal) | Empirical Criterion |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | A priori Idea of Reason | A posteriori agreement of peoples |
| **Form** | Individual presentation (Imagination) | Statistical consensus |
| **Certainty** | Subjective necessity | Probability only |
| **Access** | Each must produce it in himself | Observable from examples |
| **Domain** | Only the human figure | All objects of taste |

### Normal Idea vs. Rational Idea in the Ideal

| | Aesthetic Normal Idea | Rational Idea |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | Experience (empirical) | Reason (a priori) |
| **Content** | Standard figure of the species | Purposes of humanity |
| **Function** | Morphological correctness | Moral expressiveness |
| **Example** | Average proportions of the human body | Expression of dignity, wisdom, virtue |

## Role in the Critical System

The archetype of taste occupies a pivotal position in the architectonic of the third *Critique*. It demonstrates that the beautiful, at its highest, is inseparable from the morally good. The Ideal of beauty requires the rational Idea of humanity's moral purpose — which means the pure judgment of taste, when pushed to its highest instance, opens onto the supersensible. This is a key step in Kant's argument that beauty is the "symbol of morality" (§59): the archetype of taste is the point where aesthetic form and moral content converge in a single presentation.

## Key Passages

> "Hence we consider some products of taste as exemplary. Not that taste can be acquired by imitating others; for it must be an original faculty. He who imitates a model shows, no doubt, in so far as he attains to it, skill; but only shows taste in so far as he can judge of this model itself."
> (017-003–004)

> "It follows from hence that the highest model, the archetype of taste, is a mere Idea, which every one must produce in himself; and according to which he must judge every Object of taste, every example of judgement by taste, and even the taste of every one."
> (017-004)

> "Idea properly means a rational concept, and Ideal the representation of an individual being, regarded as adequate to an Idea. Hence that archetype of taste... is better called the Ideal of the beautiful."
> (017-004)

> "The only being which has the purpose of its existence in itself is man, who can determine his purposes by Reason; or, where he must receive them from external perception, yet can compare them with essential and universal purposes, and can judge this their accordance aesthetically. This man is, then, alone of all objects in the world, susceptible of an Ideal of beauty; as it is only humanity in his person, as an intelligence, that is susceptible of the Ideal of perfection."
> (017-009)

> "The aesthetical Idea (the archetype or original image) is fundamental to both [forms of formative art]; but in sculpture the expression of the aesthetical Idea in the figure is the principal thing; whereas in architecture the chief thing is the use of the artistic object."
> (051-009)
