# The Good

## Definition

The good (*das Gute*) is whatever pleases through the mere concept by means of Reason. (004-002) It is distinguished from the pleasant — which pleases immediately through sensation — and from the beautiful — which pleases through reflection without a determinate concept. The good is always bound up with an **interest** in the existence of its object: "to will something, and to have a satisfaction in its existence, i.e. to take an interest in it, are identical." (004-008) The good divides into the *useful* (mediate good, pleasing as a means toward some pleasantness) and the *good in itself* (pleasing for its own sake), and in both cases a concept of a purpose is necessarily involved. (004-002)

## Kant's Account

### The Good Requires a Concept

The central distinction between the good and the beautiful is conceptual mediation. To find something good, "I must always know what sort of a thing the object ought to be, i.e. I must have a concept of it. But there is no need of this, to find a thing beautiful." (004-003) Flowers, free delineations, and ornamental foliage "have no meaning, depend on no definite concept, and yet they please." (004-003) The beautiful pleases in the mere reflection upon an object without subsuming it under any determinate concept; the good pleases only insofar as the object is recognized as falling under a concept that Reason connects with the will.

### Three Kinds of Satisfaction

Kant maps the three objects of satisfaction onto three distinct relations to the faculty of desire. The **pleasant** gratifies the senses immediately and has a reference to inclination; the **beautiful** pleases through disinterested reflection; the **good** is esteemed through Reason as an object of the will. (004-002) (005-002) Both the pleasant and the good "have a reference to the faculty of desire" and are bound up with interest, whereas the beautiful involves no interest whatever. (005-002) This makes the judgment of taste uniquely disinterested — the only kind of satisfaction that is "free" in the sense required for a pure aesthetic judgment.

### The Pleasant/Good Confusion

Kant observes that ordinary language often conflates the pleasant and the good. People speak of lasting gratification as "good in itself," but this is "merely a confusion of words; for the concepts which properly belong to these expressions can in no way be interchanged." (004-005) The pleasant represents the object simply in relation to sense; it "must first be brought by the concept of a purpose under principles of Reason, in order to call it good, as an object of the Will." (004-005) Even in common speech, Kant notes, we distinguish a dish that is pleasant but not good — it immediately delights the senses but, "considered by Reason which looks to the after results, it displeases." (004-006)

### The Unconditioned Good

Kant draws a further, crucial distinction between happiness and the unconditioned good. Happiness, understood as "the greatest sum of the pleasantnesses of life," is not an unconditioned good, despite the common belief to the contrary. (004-007) Reason can never be persuaded "that the existence of a man who merely lives for *enjoyment* ... has a worth in itself." (004-007) Only what a person does "without reference to enjoyment, in full freedom and independently of what nature can procure for him passively, gives an absolute worth to his being, as the existence of a person." (004-007) This unconditioned good — the moral good — brings with it the highest interest, for it is the object of the will determined by pure practical Reason. (004-008)

## Key Distinctions

| | Pleasant | Beautiful | Good |
|---|---------|-----------|------|
| **Ground** | Sensation | Reflection without concept | Concept + Reason |
| **Interest** | Bound with interest | Disinterested | Bound with interest |
| **Reference** | Inclination / gratification | Free play of faculties | Faculty of desire / will |
| **Universality** | Private (no claim) | Subjective universal | Objective universal |
| **Example** | The taste of a dish | A flower or free pattern | Moral action; a useful tool |
| **Question asked** | Does it gratify *me*? | Does it please in reflection? | What *ought* the object to be? |

## Role in the Critical System

The distinction between the good and the beautiful is one of the four "moments" of the Analytic of the Beautiful, and it serves a structural purpose that extends beyond aesthetics. By isolating the beautiful as a satisfaction without interest — distinct both from the pleasant (sensory interest) and from the good (rational interest) — Kant secures the autonomy of aesthetic judgment. (005-002) If the beautiful were reducible to the good, the judgment of taste would collapse into a confused moral or teleological judgment, and the entire project of the third *Critique* — to bridge the theoretical and practical domains through reflective judgment — would collapse.

The good also connects the *Critique of Judgment* to the *Critique of Practical Reason*, where the concept of the good as the object of pure practical Reason receives its full treatment. In the third *Critique*, the good appears primarily as a contrast term: it shows what the beautiful is *not*, and thereby clarifies the unique character of aesthetic satisfaction whose determining ground "can be no other than subjective." (001-001)

## Key Passages

> "Whatever by means of Reason pleases through the mere concept is GOOD. That which pleases only as a means we call *good for something* (the useful); but that which pleases for itself is *good in itself*. In both there is always involved the concept of a purpose, and consequently the relation of Reason to the (at least possible) volition, and thus a satisfaction in the *presence* of an Object or an action, i.e. some kind of interest."
> (004-002)

> "In order to find anything good, I must always know what sort of a thing the object ought to be, i.e. I must have a concept of it. But there is no need of this, to find a thing beautiful. Flowers, free delineations, outlines intertwined with one another without design and called foliage, have no meaning, depend on no definite concept, and yet they please."
> (004-003)

> "Only what he does, without reference to enjoyment, in full freedom and independently of what nature can procure for him passively, gives an absolute worth to his being, as the existence of a person; and happiness, with the whole abundance of its pleasures, is far from being an unconditioned good."
> (004-007)

> "The pleasant and the good have both a reference to the faculty of desire, and they bring with them — the former a satisfaction pathologically conditioned (by stimuli, *stimuli*), the latter a pure practical satisfaction, which is determined not merely by the representation of the object, but also by the represented connection of the subject with the existence of the object."
> (005-002)
