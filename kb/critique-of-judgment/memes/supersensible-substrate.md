# Supersensible Substrate

## Definition

The supersensible substrate (*übersinnliches Substrat*) is the indeterminate, non-empirical ground that Kant posits as underlying both nature (the domain of theoretical reason) and freedom (the domain of practical reason). It is not an object of possible knowledge — we can have no intuition of it — but it is a **necessary idea of reason** required to resolve the antinomy of judgment and to think the compatibility of mechanical and teleological explanations of nature.

The concept emerges most explicitly in the "Dialectic of Teleological Judgment" (§§69–78), where Kant argues that the conflict between mechanistic and teleological maxims can only be resolved by positing a supersensible ground in which both modes of explanation are unified. "We cannot otherwise conceive the purposiveness which must lie at the basis of our cognition of the internal possibility of many natural things, than by representing it and the world in general as produced by an intelligent cause." (P01-047)

## Kant's Account

### The Need for a Supersensible Ground

The *Critique of Pure Reason* established an unbridgeable gulf between phenomena (things as they appear, governed by mechanical laws) and noumena (things in themselves, the domain of freedom). The *Critique of Judgment* reveals that this gulf must be bridged — aesthetic and teleological judgment both presuppose that nature is amenable to our cognitive and moral purposes. The supersensible substrate is the concept that makes this bridge thinkable. (P01-061)

### Regulative, Not Constitutive

Kant is careful to insist that the supersensible substrate is a **regulative idea**, not a constitutive claim. "We are not justified in asserting dogmatically that God exists; there is only permitted to us the limited formula: we cannot otherwise conceive the purposiveness which must lie at the basis of our cognition." (P01-047) The supersensible substrate is a principle for reflective judgment — it guides our thinking about nature without claiming knowledge of what lies beyond experience.

### The Common Root Thesis

The supersensible substrate suggests — though Kant never fully develops — the idea of a "common root" (*gemeinschaftliche Wurzel*) of nature and freedom. "He maintains that to explain the phenomena of organic life and the purposiveness of nature we must hold that the world of sense is not disparate from and opposed to the world of thought, but that nature is the development of freedom." (P01-061) This thesis became the starting point of post-Kantian German Idealism, particularly Schelling and Hegel.
