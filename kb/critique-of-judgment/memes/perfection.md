## Definition

Perfection (*Vollkommenheit*) is objective internal purposiveness — "the agreement of the manifold in a thing with the concept of what sort of thing it is to be" (015-006). Kant distinguishes two kinds: **qualitative perfection**, which is the agreement of the manifold with the concept that furnishes the rule for its combination, and **quantitative perfection**, the completeness of a thing after its kind (a mere concept of magnitude or totality) (015-006).

Perfection had been identified with [[beauty]] by the Wolffian-Baumgarten school, and Kant's argument that the two are fundamentally distinct is one of the defining moves of the *Critique of Judgment*'s aesthetics (P01-017, 015-004).

## Kant's Account

### The Argument Against Perfection = Beauty

Kant devotes §15 specifically to rejecting the identification of beauty with perfection. His argument proceeds through several steps:

First, objective purposiveness — whether external (utility) or internal (perfection) — can only be cognized "by means of the reference of the manifold to a definite purpose, and therefore only through a concept" (015-002). The Beautiful, by contrast, rests on "a merely formal purposiveness, i.e. a purposiveness without purpose" — it is "quite independent of the concept of the Good" (015-003).

Second, judging objective purposiveness requires the concept of "what sort of thing it is to be" to come first. The agreement of the manifold with this concept is qualitative perfection (015-006). But the judgment of [[taste]] has no such concept at its basis — its determining ground is the feeling of the subject, not a concept of the object (015-007).

Third, Kant argues that conceiving of beauty as "confused perfection" — as Wolff and Baumgarten did — collapses the distinction between aesthetic and logical judgment: "if we wish to call confused concepts and the objective judgement based on them, aesthetical, we shall have an Understanding judging sensibly or a Sense representing its Objects by means of concepts — both of which are contradictory" (015-009).

### Perfection and Adherent Beauty

While [[free-beauty|free beauty]] is entirely independent of perfection, [[adherent-beauty|adherent beauty]] does presuppose a concept of perfection (016-002, 016-007). When we judge a human being, a horse, or a church as beautiful, we necessarily consider whether the object's manifold accords with its concept — i.e., whether it is a *good* specimen of its kind. However, Kant insists that "properly speaking, perfection gains nothing by beauty or beauty by perfection" (016-011). The two satisfactions — aesthetic and intellectual — can coexist but remain distinct.

### Perfection and the Ideal

The Ideal of [[perfection]] is distinct from the [[ideal-of-beauty|Ideal of beauty]]. The Ideal of perfection belongs to humanity "as an intelligence" — it concerns the rational and moral constitution of the person (017-009). The Ideal of beauty, by contrast, is the visible expression of moral Ideas in the human figure. Only humanity is susceptible of an Ideal of perfection, because only humanity "can determine his purposes by Reason" (017-009).

## Key Distinctions

### Perfection vs. Beauty

| | Perfection | Beauty |
|---|---|---|
| **Type of purposiveness** | Objective, internal | Subjective, formal |
| **Requires concept?** | Yes — concept of what the thing ought to be | No |
| **Judgment type** | Logical (cognitive) | Aesthetic |
| **Example** | A good church (serves its purpose) | A beautiful flower (no purpose) |

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Perfection

Qualitative perfection concerns the *kind* of agreement between manifold and concept — whether the thing's parts are arranged according to the rule supplied by its concept. Quantitative perfection concerns the *completeness* of the thing — whether it has all the requisites belonging to its kind (015-006).

### External vs. Internal Objective Purposiveness

External objective purposiveness is *utility* — the reference of the object to a purpose outside itself. Internal objective purposiveness is *perfection* — the reference of the object's manifold to an internal purpose (015-004). Kant argues that beauty cannot rest on either: utility would make satisfaction mediate rather than immediate, and perfection would make the judgment cognitive rather than aesthetic.

## Role in the Critical System

Kant's distinction of beauty from perfection is essential to establishing the autonomy of the aesthetic domain. If beauty were reducible to perfection (even "confused" perfection), the judgment of taste would be a species of cognition, and aesthetics would have no distinct *a priori* principle. By demonstrating that beauty rests on subjective formal purposiveness rather than objective perfection, Kant secures the third *Critique*'s claim that Judgment has its own transcendental principle — the purposiveness of nature for our cognitive faculties.

In the *Critique of Teleological Judgment* (Part II), perfection returns in a different register: the concept of natural purposes (§63–§68) involves a kind of internal purposiveness that is objective rather than formal. The organism, as a "natural purpose," exhibits a perfection of organization that cannot be explained mechanistically.

## Key Passages

> "Objective purposiveness is either external, i.e. the *utility*, or internal, i.e. the *perfection* of the object. That the satisfaction in an object, on account of which we call it beautiful, cannot rest on the representation of its utility, is sufficiently obvious... But objective internal purposiveness, i.e. perfection, comes nearer to the predicate of beauty; and it has been regarded by celebrated philosophers as the same as beauty."
> (015-004)

> "The agreement of the manifold in it with this concept (which furnishes the rule for combining the manifold) is the *qualitative perfection* of the thing. Quite different from this is *quantitative perfection*, the completeness of a thing after its kind, which is a mere concept of magnitude (of totality)."
> (015-006)

> "In beauty, regarded as a formal subjective purposiveness, there is in no way thought a perfection of the object, as a would-be formal purposiveness, which yet is objective."
> (015-007)
