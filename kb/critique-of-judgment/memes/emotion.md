## Definition

Emotion (*Rührung*) is an affective response — a feeling-moved, a stirring of the sensibility — that Kant distinguishes from the calm contemplative satisfaction proper to a pure judgment of taste. Unlike the disinterested pleasure of beauty, emotion involves a felt agitation that borders on the pathological and compromises the reflective distance required for aesthetic judgment.

## Kant's Account

Kant treats emotion as a parallel contaminant to charm throughout the "Analytic of the Beautiful." The two are regularly paired: a pure judgment of taste is one "on which charm and emotion have no influence" (013-004). Emotion introduces a personal, interest-laden element into what should be a disinterested reflective activity.

In §13, Kant identifies the reliance on emotion as a mark of undeveloped taste: taste is "still barbaric which needs a mixture of charms and emotions in order that there may be satisfaction" (013-002). The problem is not that emotions cannot accompany aesthetic experience — they often do — but that they cannot serve as its determining ground.

In his treatment of the sublime, emotion takes on a more complex role. Kant distinguishes "spirited" and "tender" emotions (029-035), noting that tender emotions, "if they rise to the height of affection, are worthless" for aesthetic judgment. The sublime proper involves a distinctive emotional response — the feeling of respect for reason in the face of overwhelming magnitude or power — but this is a rational emotion, not a sensory one. Kant is careful to specify that the sublime produces "that emotion which no mathematical estimation of magnitudes by means of numbers can produce" (026-003), tying it to reason rather than sensibility.

In §29, Kant draws a firm line: if a judgment "gratifies us by charm or emotion, we must not assume that anyone else will agree" (029-044). The claim to universal validity — the defining feature of aesthetic judgment — is lost once emotion enters as a determining ground.

## Key Distinctions

- **Emotion vs. Feeling of the Sublime**: The sublime produces a distinctive rational emotion rooted in respect for reason (026-003), but this is categorically different from the sensory emotions excluded from taste.
- **Emotion vs. Charm**: Both are sensory contaminants, but charm is a quality of the object while emotion is a state of the subject.
- **Spirited vs. Tender Emotions**: Kant distinguishes these in §29 (029-035); tender emotions are more problematic for pure judgment because they more easily slide into mere sentimentality.

## Role in the Critical System

Emotion's exclusion from pure aesthetic judgment is required by the First Moment (disinterestedness) and the Second Moment (universal validity). An emotion-laden judgment is interested by definition — it involves the subject's own felt state — and therefore cannot claim the universal communicability that defines the judgment of taste. Emotion thus helps demarcate the boundary between the aesthetic (disinterested, universally communicable) and the merely affective (interested, private).

## Key Passages

> "A judgement of taste on which charm and emotion have no influence (although they may be bound up with the satisfaction in the beautiful), — which therefore has for its determining ground merely the purposiveness of the form, — is a pure judgement of taste."
> (013-004)

> "Taste is still barbaric which needs a mixture of charms and emotions in order that there may be satisfaction; and still more so, if it makes these the standard of its approval."
> (013-002)

> "If we judge objects merely according to concepts, then all representation of beauty is lost. Thus there can be no rule according to which anyone can be forced to recognise anything as beautiful. Whether a dress, a house, or a flower is beautiful — no one allows himself to be talked into his judgment about it by any reasons or principles. We want to submit the object to our own eyes, just as if our satisfaction depended on sensation; and yet, if we then call the object beautiful, we believe ourselves to speak with a universal voice, and lay claim to the assent of everyone, while on the contrary every private sensation would decide only for the observer and his satisfaction."
> (P01-017)
