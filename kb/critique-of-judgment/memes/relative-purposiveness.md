# Relative Purposiveness

## Definition

Relative purposiveness (*relative Zweckmässigkeit*) is the relation in which one natural thing serves as a means to the purpose of another. Kant introduces this concept in the Critique of Teleological Judgment (§63) to distinguish it from **inner purposiveness** — the defining character of organisms. "It is a merely relative purposiveness contingent upon the thing to which it is ascribed" (063-008): the usefulness of sand for pine trees, or grass for cattle, is relative because it depends on an external relation, not on the internal constitution of the thing itself.

## Kant's Account

### Distinction from Inner Purposiveness

Kant draws a sharp line between relative and inner purposiveness. A thing possesses inner purposiveness when "it is both cause and effect of itself" (064-001) — that is, when its parts exist for the sake of the whole and the whole for the sake of the parts. This is the defining mark of an organism. Relative purposiveness, by contrast, is merely external: one thing happens to be useful to another. "Relative purposiveness, although it hypothetically gives indications of natural purposes" (063-011), does not license any categorical teleological judgment. It cannot ground a claim that nature as a whole is a system of purposes.

### The Limits of Relative Purposiveness

Kant warns that relative purposiveness is an insufficient basis for teleology. That grass feeds cattle, cattle feed humans, and humans might serve some further purpose — this chain of external means-ends relations "does not establish any objective purposiveness" in the things themselves. Only inner purposiveness — the self-organizing character of living beings — warrants the concept of a natural purpose (*Naturzweck*). Relative purposiveness is teleology on the cheap: it judges nature from the outside, by contingent relations of usefulness, rather than from the inside, by necessary internal organization.

### Examples in Nature

Kant gives concrete examples: "Sand on the seashore serves pine trees" by providing soil; grass serves as food for cattle; cattle serve human needs. But these relations are all contingent — sand does not *have to* exist for pine trees; the relation is imposed from without by an observing intelligence. By contrast, the internal organization of a tree, in which roots, trunk, branches, and leaves reciprocally produce and sustain one another, exhibits inner purposiveness that cannot be reduced to external relations.

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Relative Purposiveness | Inner Purposiveness |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Relation** | External (means to another's end) | Internal (parts ↔ whole) |
| **Necessity** | Contingent | Necessary (for the thing's possibility) |
| **Grounds teleology?** | Hypothetically only | Categorically (natural purpose) |
| **Example** | Grass → cattle → humans | Tree, organism |
| **Kant's Verdict** | Insufficient for Naturzweck | Sufficient for Naturzweck |

## Role in the Critical System

Relative purposiveness serves a negative function in Kant's teleology: it clarifies what a natural purpose is *not*. By showing the insufficiency of merely external means-ends relations, Kant isolates inner purposiveness as the only legitimate concept of a natural purpose. This prepares the ground for the Antinomy of Teleological Judgment (§§69–78), where Kant shows that even inner purposiveness must be treated as a regulative, not constitutive, principle. "Through the freedom of his causality, [man] finds certain natural things" (063-009) useful for his purposes — but this anthropocentric, relative purposiveness must not be confused with nature's own inner organization.
