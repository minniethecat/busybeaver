Presentation (*Darstellung*, also translated as "exhibition" or "hypotyposis") is a central concept in Kant's [[Critique of Judgment|第三批判]], bridging epistemology, aesthetics, and the theory of the [[Symbol]]. It designates the act of rendering a concept sensible — giving it intuitive content — and appears at critical junctures across both the aesthetic and teleological parts of the work.

## Presentation in the Critique of Pure Reason

In the first *Critique*, Kant established that cognition requires both concepts and intuitions: "Thoughts without content are empty, intuitions without concepts are blind." Presentation (*Darstellung*) is the act that connects the two — it gives a concept its objective reality by providing it with a corresponding intuition. For empirical concepts, this is straightforward (we can present the concept "dog" by pointing to a dog). For pure concepts of the understanding (categories), presentation occurs through schemata. But for ideas of reason — concepts to which no sensible intuition can ever be adequate — presentation becomes problematic.

## Aesthetic Presentation

In the *Critique of Judgment*, Kant identifies a distinctive kind of presentation proper to aesthetic ideas. The aesthetic idea is a representation of the imagination that gives *more* to think than can be captured in a determinate concept:

> [P04-075] … can regard natural beauty as the *presentation* of the concept of the formal (merely subjective) purposiveness…

Natural beauty presents the concept of purposiveness without representing any determinate purpose. This is what Kant calls "purposiveness without purpose" (*Zweckmäßigkeit ohne Zweck*), and it is the defining characteristic of the aesthetic judgment.

## Symbolic Presentation

In §59, "Beauty as the Symbol of Morality," Kant develops the notion of **symbolic hypotyposis** — presentation by analogy:

| Type of Presentation | Mechanism | Example |
|---------------------|-----------|---------|
| Schematic | Direct intuition for a concept of the understanding | A drawing of a triangle for the concept "triangle" |
| Symbolic | Indirect presentation by analogy | A hand-mill for a despotic state |

The beautiful is the symbol of the morally good because the structure of judgment in each case is analogous: in taste, the understanding serves the imagination; in morality, reason governs the will. This symbolic presentation is what makes the transition from aesthetics to morality possible.

## Presentation and the Sublime

In the **Sublime**, presentation faces its limit. The sublime is the presentation of the *unpresentable* — of ideas of reason (infinity, absolute power, moral freedom) to which no sensible intuition can ever be adequate. Kant calls this "negative presentation":

> [029-042] … also because, through this very inadequacy of the sensible in presenting the Idea, the mind is incited to abandon sensibility.

The inadequacy of the sensible presentation paradoxically serves to make the supersensible *felt*.

## Role in the Critical System

Presentation occupies a pivotal position in Kant's critical project:

1. **Epistemologically**: It distinguishes schemata (determinate) from symbols (analogical), expanding the domain of meaningful cognition beyond what the first *Critique* allowed
2. **Aesthetically**: It explains how aesthetic ideas give the imagination material that exceeds conceptuality
3. **Systematically**: It enables the transition from nature (theoretical reason) to freedom (practical reason) by showing how the supersensible can be *presented* in the sensible
