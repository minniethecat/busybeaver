# Taste

## Definition

Taste (*Geschmack*) is the faculty for judging the beautiful — "the faculty of judging an object or a method of representing it by an *entirely disinterested* satisfaction or dissatisfaction." (005-008) Unlike ordinary cognitive judgment, which subsumes intuitions under concepts, a judgment of taste (*Geschmacksurteil*) is **aesthetic**: its determining ground is not a concept but the subject's feeling of pleasure or displeasure. (001-001) Yet this feeling is not merely subjective in the sense of being idiosyncratic — the judgment of taste claims **universal validity**, demanding the assent of all judging subjects.

The *Critique of Judgment* aims to establish an a priori principle for taste, showing that its claim to universality is legitimate even though it is not based on concepts. "The judgement of taste is therefore not a judgement of cognition, and is consequently not logical but aesthetical." (001-001) The Editor observes that "the freedom of the pure judgement of taste from the thraldom of concepts had, to a large extent, been established by his predecessors in Germany." (P01-018)

## Kant's Account

### The Four Moments

Kant analyzes the judgment of taste according to the four headings of the table of judgments (§§1–22):

| Moment | Category | Determination |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **Quality** | — | Disinterestedness: pleasure independent of the object's existence |
| **Quantity** | — | Universal validity without a concept: "subjective universality" |
| **Relation** | — | Purposiveness without purpose: the form appears as if designed for cognition |
| **Modality** | — | Necessary satisfaction: the *sensus communis* as the ground of necessity |

The Editor notes that Kant derived the first moment from Hutcheson and Mendelssohn: "the aesthetical judgement about Beauty is according to *quality* disinterested." (P01-016) But Kant's systematic arrangement of the four moments under the logical functions is original.

### The Deduction of Taste

The central problem of the *Critique of Aesthetic Judgment* is to justify taste's claim to universal validity. Kant's solution is that the judgment of taste is grounded in the **free play of the cognitive faculties** — imagination and understanding — which are presupposed to operate identically in all human subjects. When we judge an object beautiful, we are conscious of this harmonious free play; because the cognitive faculties are universally shared, the feeling that expresses this harmony must also be universally communicable. (P01-021)

### Taste vs. Genius

While **genius** is the productive talent that *creates* beautiful art, taste is the critical faculty that *judges* it. "Taste, like the judgement in general, is the discipline (or training) of genius; it clips its wings, and makes it cultured." (§50) Taste provides the formal constraints without which genius would produce only original nonsense. The Editor notes that taste "requires to be kept in check by taste; quite in the spirit of the σωφροσύνη of the ancients." (P01-032) In cases of conflict between genius and taste, Kant sides with taste — beauty requires both, but taste is the indispensable constraint.

## Key Passages

> "The judgement of taste is therefore not a judgement of cognition, and is consequently not logical but aesthetical, by which we understand that whose determining ground can be *no other than subjective*."
> (001-001)
