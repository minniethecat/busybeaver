## Definition

Fine art (*schöne Kunst*), in Kant's critical vocabulary, is the art of genius.

It is a mode of production that is at once intentional and yet appears free from the constraint of rules. It is "a mode of representation which is purposive by itself, and which, although devoid of a definite purpose, yet furthers the culture of the mental powers in reference to social communication." (044-004)

Unlike mechanical art, which applies learned rules to produce a predetermined effect, fine art produces works whose beauty seems spontaneous and rule-transcending, as if they were products of nature.

## Kant's Account

Kant develops his theory of fine art across §§43–53 of the *Critique of Judgment*.

He begins by distinguishing art (*Kunst*) from nature, science, and craft (§43). Art is production through freedom—through a will that grounds its acts in reason—whereas nature operates by blind necessity. Within art, Kant further distinguishes between mechanical art and aesthetical art, which aims at the feeling of pleasure.

Aesthetical art itself divides into **agreeable art** (*angenehme Kunst*), whose end is mere enjoyment (e.g., table conversation, games), and **fine art** (*schöne Kunst*), which "furthers the culture of the mental powers in reference to social communication." (044-001) Fine art produces a pleasure that is reflective rather than merely sensory.

The paradox at the heart of fine art is that it must appear as nature while being the product of intentional design. Kant writes that "in a product of beautiful art we must become conscious that it is art and not nature; but yet the purposiveness in its form must seem as free from all constraint of arbitrary rules as if it were a product of mere nature." (045-002)

## The Genius–Taste Relation

Central to Kant's account is the partnership between [[Genius]] and [[Taste]].

Genius (*Genie*) is "the talent (or natural gift) which gives the rule to art"—the innate mental disposition through which nature gives the rule to art (§46). Genius supplies aesthetic ideas that animate a work: representations of the imagination that occasion much thought without being reducible to any determinate concept.

Taste—the faculty of judging the beautiful—must discipline genius, "clipping its wings" and giving it guidance. Kant states that if there is a conflict between these two properties in a product, "something must be sacrificed... it should be rather on the side of genius." (050-002)

This tension between creativity and judgment constitutes the dynamic core of Kant's art theory.

## Classification of the Fine Arts

Kant offers a systematic division based on the analogy with human communication (§51):

| Art Form | Medium | Exemplary Instances |
|----------|--------|---------------------|
| **Arts of speech** | Articulate expression | Rhetoric, poetry |
| **Formative arts** | Sensible intuition | Sculpture, architecture, painting |
| **Art of the play of sensations** | Tone and color | Music, the art of color |

Poetry ranks highest because it expands the mind by setting imagination free. Music, though charming, ranks lowest because it "plays merely with sensations" and leaves nothing for reflection.

## Key Passages

> "In a product of beautiful art we must become conscious that it is art and not nature; but yet the purposiveness in its form must seem as free from all constraint of arbitrary rules as if it were a product of mere nature."
> (045-002)

> "Beautiful art is a mode of representation which is purposive by itself, and which, although devoid of a definite purpose, yet furthers the culture of the mental powers in reference to social communication."
> (044-004)

> "Therefore, beautiful art cannot itself devise the rule according to which it can bring about its product. But since nevertheless a product can never be called art unless it is preceded by a rule, nature in the subject must give the rule to art."
> (046-004)
