# Mathematical Sublime

## Definition

The mathematical sublime (*Mathematisch-Erhabenen*) is the mode of the sublime that concerns magnitude — the estimation of size and the experience of the **absolutely great**. (024-004) (025-001) It is distinguished from the dynamical sublime (which concerns might and the faculty of desire) by its reference to the faculty of cognition: in the mathematical sublime, the representation is "ascribed to the Object as a *mathematical* determination of the Imagination" — the Imagination strives to comprehend a magnitude so vast that it surpasses its own capacity, and in this very failure the mind discovers a supersensible faculty that is "great beyond all comparison even with the faculty of mathematical estimation." (024-005) (026-013)

## Kant's Account

### Absolutely Great vs. Comparatively Great

Kant opens the Analytic of the Sublime with a precise definition: "We call that *sublime* which is *absolutely great*." (025-001) To be simply great, and to be a great something, are quite different concepts. (025-002) The absolutely great is "what is great beyond all comparison." (025-003) This is not a pure concept of Understanding, an intuition of Sense, or a concept of Reason — it must be a concept of Judgment, involving "a subjective purposiveness of the representation in reference to the Judgment." (025-004)

The determination of the absolute magnitude of phenomena can supply "no absolute concept whatever of magnitude, but only a comparative one," because any magnitude can be compared with another as its measure, and that measure with another, ad infinitum. (025-004) The sublime therefore "is not to be sought in the things of nature, but only in our Ideas." (025-010) Nothing given in nature, however great, can be called sublime considered purely as an object of sense. The sublime resides in "the state of mind produced by a certain representation with which the reflective Judgment is occupied, and not the Object." (025-011)

### Apprehension, Comprehension, and the Failure of Imagination

In the estimation of magnitude, two operations of the Imagination are involved: **apprehension** (*apprehensio*) and **comprehension** (*comprehensio aesthetica*). (026-004) Apprehension can proceed without difficulty, even ad infinitum. Comprehension, however — the gathering of apprehended parts into a unified intuition — "becomes harder the further apprehension advances, and soon attains to its maximum." (026-004) When apprehension has proceeded so far that the partial representations first apprehended "begin to vanish in the Imagination, whilst this ever proceeds to the apprehension of others, then it loses as much on the one side as it gains on the other." (026-005) There is a maximum of comprehension beyond which the Imagination cannot go.

This failure of comprehension is not a defect but the source of the sublime. The mind "listens to the voice of Reason which, for every given magnitude — even for those that can never be entirely apprehended — requires totality." (026-012) Reason demands that the Imagination present the infinite as *entirely given* in one intuition — a demand the Imagination cannot fulfill. The inadequacy of the Imagination to Reason's Idea of absolute totality produces the feeling of the sublime: the mind simultaneously experiences its own sensible limitation and its supersensible capacity to *think* what it cannot *present*. (026-015)

### The Pyramids and St. Peter's: Empirical Illustrations

Kant illustrates the mechanism of the mathematical sublime with two famous examples. Savary's observation about the Pyramids of Egypt — that the spectator must keep from going too near as well as too far to obtain the full emotional effect — demonstrates the limits of comprehension. (026-006) Too far, and the parts are only obscurely represented; too near, and "the first tiers are always partly forgotten before the Imagination has taken in the last, and so the comprehension of them is never complete." (026-006) The same principle explains the "bewilderment or, as it were, perplexity" that seizes the spectator on entering St. Peter's in Rome: "there is here a feeling of the inadequacy of his Imagination for presenting the Ideas of a whole, wherein the Imagination reaches its maximum, and, in striving to surpass it, sinks back into itself, by which, however, a kind of emotional satisfaction is produced." (026-006)

### The Supersensible Faculty Revealed

The experience of the mathematical sublime culminates in the discovery of a faculty that surpasses every standard of Sense. Only to be able to *think* the infinite as a whole "indicates a faculty of mind which surpasses every standard of Sense." (026-013) The bare capability of thinking the infinite without contradiction "requires in the human mind a faculty itself supersensible." (026-013) This faculty is not theoretical — it does not enlarge cognition — but it extends the mind: "it feels itself able in another (practical) point of view to go beyond the limit of sensibility." (026-013)

Kant concludes with a formula: "*the sublime is that, the mere ability to think which, shows a faculty of the mind surpassing every standard of Sense.*" (025-012) The mathematical sublime is thus the aesthetic experience through which the mind becomes aware of its own supersensible destination — not by comprehending the infinite, but by failing to do so in a way that reveals a faculty greater than the failure.

## Key Distinctions

| | Mathematical Sublime | Dynamical Sublime |
|---|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Faculty reference** | Cognition (Understanding) | Faculty of desire (Reason) |
| **Concern** | Magnitude and estimation of size | Might and dominion |
| **Imagination's relation** | Inadequacy in *comprehension* of magnitude | Inadequacy in *resistance* to might |
| **What is revealed** | Supersensible faculty of cognition | Supersensible freedom and moral destination |
| **Key operation** | Apprehension vs. comprehension | Fear vs. security |
| **Exemplary objects** | Pyramids, St. Peter's, starry sky | Storms, volcanoes, overhanging rocks |

## Role in the Critical System

The mathematical sublime occupies a pivotal position in Kant's argument that aesthetic judgment bridges the theoretical and practical domains. Through the estimation of magnitude, the Imagination is brought into relation not with the Understanding (as in the beautiful) but with Reason. (026-016) The beautiful "refers the Imagination in its free play to the *Understanding*, in order to harmonise it with the *concepts* of the latter in general"; the sublime refers the Imagination to *Reason* so that it "may subjectively be in accordance with its *Ideas*." (026-016) The mathematical sublime thus performs the transition from the sensible to the supersensible within the theoretical domain, preparing the mind for the moral law's demand to think beyond nature.

The division of the sublime into mathematical and dynamical also mirrors Kant's division of the categories. Just as the table of categories divides into those of quantity (mathematical) and those of quality (dynamical), the Analytic of the Sublime divides according to whether the purposiveness of the representation refers "through the Imagination either to the *faculty of cognition* or of *desire*." (024-005)

> (025-001)
