## Definition

Mechanical causality (*mechanische Kausalität*) is, in Kant's *Critique of Judgment*, the mode of explanation according to which natural phenomena are accounted for solely through the laws of moving matter — that is, through the interaction of parts independently of any concept of a whole or purpose. It is the causality characteristic of the discursive understanding when it proceeds from parts to whole, as opposed to teleological causality, which explains parts by reference to the whole they constitute (061-006).

## Kant's Account

Kant develops the concept of mechanical causality primarily in the *Critique of the Teleological Judgment* (§§61–68), where he examines the limits of mechanical explanation in the study of organisms. Mechanical causality is the default mode of scientific explanation: "we bring under the rules where the laws of simply mechanical causality do not suffice" only when we encounter phenomena — such as organized beings — that resist explanation by their parts alone (061-006).

The core of Kant's argument is that mechanical and teleological principles are not contradictory but complementary. Each has its legitimate domain, and neither can be reduced to the other. Kant insists that we must not abandon mechanical explanation prematurely, nor insist upon it where it demonstrably fails. The principle of mechanical causality remains a regulative maxim for scientific inquiry: we should pursue mechanical explanation as far as possible, and only invoke teleological principles when the nature of the object demands it.

Kant further argues that this distinction between mechanical and teleological explanation "depends for its validity upon the peculiar character of our Understanding" (067-006). Our discursive understanding proceeds from parts to whole, and therefore cannot grasp organisms — where parts are only possible through their relation to the whole — by mechanical causality alone. An intuitive understanding, by contrast, could cognize the whole before its parts, thereby overcoming the distinction. Since we lack such an understanding, the mechanical and teleological stand as complementary principles for our cognition of nature.

Crucially, Kant maintains that teleological explanation does no "prejudice to the principle of mechanical causality" (067-006). The two can coexist as regulative principles: we must always seek mechanical causes, while recognizing that organisms require an additional principle of judgment. Their ultimate unity lies in a supersensible ground that is inaccessible to us.

## Key Distinctions

Mechanical causality is distinguished from teleological causality (explanation by purposes) and from the causality of freedom (explanation by moral law). Mechanical causality belongs to the domain of nature and determinative judgment; teleological causality, as a reflective principle, supplements mechanical explanation where it proves insufficient; freedom-causality belongs to practical reason and the noumenal self. Mechanical causality must also be distinguished from *technic of nature*, which is the broader category of purposive natural production that includes both intentional (art-like) and unintentional purposiveness.

## Role in the Critical System

The mechanical/teleological distinction is central to the architectonic of the third *Critique*. It mirrors the broader critical distinction between nature and freedom: mechanism represents nature's determinism, while the recognition of purposes points toward a supersensible ground that unites nature and freedom. The resolution of their apparent conflict — the subordination of the mechanical to the teleological principle — exemplifies the mediating function of reflective judgment in Kant's critical system.

## Key Passages

> "We bring under the rules where the laws of simply mechanical causality do not suffice. For we bring under them only those cases in which we can explain by means of mere mechanical laws the production of organized beings."
> (061-006)

> "It is in no wise prejudicial to the principle of mechanical causality. Furthermore, it is in no wise possible for us to prove the impossibility of the generation of organized products of nature by the mere mechanical principle of nature."
> (067-006)
