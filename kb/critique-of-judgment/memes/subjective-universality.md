## Definition

Subjective universality (*subjektive Allgemeingültigkeit*) is, in Kant's aesthetic theory, the distinctive mode of universal validity claimed by judgments of taste. Unlike the objective universality of cognitive judgments — which holds for all objects falling under a given concept — subjective universality holds for all judging subjects without being grounded in any concept of the object. It is aesthetic, not logical; it pertains to the feeling of the subject, not to the determination of the object (006-004).

## Kant's Account

Kant introduces subjective universality in the Second Moment of the Analytic of the Beautiful (§§6–9), where he argues that the judgment of taste must claim validity for everyone. Since the judgment of taste is disinterested — separated from all private inclination — the subject is entitled to demand that others agree. This demand is not based on a concept (which would make it objective) but on the presupposition of a common sense (*sensus communis*) and the universal communicability of the mental state in the free play of the faculties.

Kant carefully distinguishes subjective from objective universality. A judgment with objective universal validity "holds for everything contained under a given concept"; it is also always valid subjectively. But "from a subjective universal validity, i.e. aesthetical and resting on no concept, we cannot infer that which is logical; because that kind of judgement does not extend to the Object" (031-002). The aesthetic universality of the judgment of taste is of a "particular kind" — it "does not unite the predicate of beauty with the concept of the *Object*, considered in its whole logical sphere, and yet extends it to the whole sphere of judging persons" (031-002).

This is paradoxical: the judgment of taste is singular in logical quantity (it says "This rose is beautiful," not "Roses are beautiful"), yet claims universal assent. Its universality is not logical but *aesthetic* — it rests on the subjective conditions of judgment in general, which can be presupposed in every rational subject.

For Kant, the ground of subjective universality is the universal communicability of the mental state in the free play of imagination and understanding. Because this state is the condition of cognition in general, it can be attributed to everyone. The pleasure in the beautiful is thus universally communicable without the mediation of a concept — a distinctive claim that separates Kant's aesthetics from both rationalist theories of beauty (which ground it in concepts of perfection) and empiricist theories (which reduce it to private feeling).

## Key Distinctions

Subjective universality must be distinguished from objective universality (valid for all objects under a concept), from mere private validity (the "everyone has their own taste" of the pleasant), and from the necessity of moral judgments (which is grounded in the moral law). It is also distinct from the universality of the agreeable, which is merely empirical and statistical (what most people happen to like), not normative (what everyone ought to find beautiful).

## Role in the Critical System

Subjective universality is the linchpin of Kant's deduction of judgments of taste (§§30–40). If the judgment of taste can legitimately claim subjective universality, then aesthetics can be a proper part of transcendental philosophy — not as a doctrine of beauty, but as a critique of the faculty of taste. The doctrine of subjective universality thus secures the autonomy of aesthetic judgment while connecting it to the broader critical project: the universal communicability of feeling points toward the supersensible substrate of humanity as the ground of both theoretical and practical reason.
