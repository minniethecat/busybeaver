## Definition

Modality, in Kant's table of the Four Moments of the judgment of taste, designates the manner in which the judgment relates to satisfaction — namely, as *necessary*. The judgment of taste does not merely report that an object pleases, nor does it merely claim that others *do* agree; it asserts that others *ought* to agree. This necessity is not objective (derived from concepts) but subjective and exemplary — grounded in a common sense (*sensus communis*) that is presupposed as a condition of the universal communicability of cognition.

## Kant's Account

Kant structures the Four Moments according to the logical table of judgments: Quality, Quantity, Relation, and Modality. The Fourth Moment, Modality, concerns the mode of the judgment's claim: the satisfaction in the beautiful is thought of as *necessary* (018-002). This necessity is "of a peculiar kind" — it is not a theoretical objective necessity (as in a law of nature) nor a practical necessity (as in a moral duty), but an *exemplary* necessity: "a necessity of the assent of *all* to a judgment regarded as an example of a universal rule that one cannot state" (018-002).

In §22, Kant elaborates: the judgment of taste asserts a "subjective necessity, which is represented as objective, under the presupposition of a common sense" (022-001). The common sense is not an empirical fact about what people actually feel but a normative presupposition — an "ideal norm" that makes the universal communicability of feeling possible. The "ought" here is the "objective necessity of the confluence of the feeling of all with the feeling of each" (022-003).

Kant returns to modality in the "Deduction of Aesthetic Judgments" (§31), where he argues that the judgment's claim to necessity "must always rest on a priori principles" (031-005), and in §29, where he notes that this necessity "involves not merely a claim, but an actual requirement" (029-016). The modality of the aesthetic judgment thus marks the boundary between genuine taste and mere personal preference: the former carries an implicit demand for universal agreement, grounded not in empirical consensus but in the transcendental conditions of cognition itself.

## Key Distinctions

- **Subjective vs. Objective Necessity**: The necessity of taste is subjective (rooted in the subject's own feeling) yet claims universal validity — unlike objective necessity, which flows from determinate concepts.
- **Exemplary vs. Apodictic Necessity**: The necessity is exemplary — the particular judgment functions as an example of a universal rule that cannot be formulated — rather than apodictic (derivable from principles).
- **Modality vs. Quantity**: The Second Moment (Quantity) claims universality — that *all* ought to agree; the Fourth Moment (Modality) claims necessity — that this agreement is not contingent but in some sense required.

## Role in the Critical System

The Fourth Moment completes Kant's analysis of the judgment of taste by situating it within the full logical table of judgments, demonstrating that aesthetic judgment has a logic of its own that parallels but does not reduce to the logic of cognitive judgment. The postulation of a common sense as the ground of this necessity also provides the bridge to the moral domain: just as the moral law commands with necessity, the beautiful demands assent with an analogous (though distinct) necessity. This parallel is what underwrites Kant's later claim that the beautiful is the symbol of the morally good (§59).

## Key Passages

> "Now this necessity is of a peculiar kind. It is not a theoretical objective necessity... It is not a practical necessity... But the necessity which is thought in an aesthetical judgement can only be called *exemplary*, i.e. a necessity of the assent of *all* to a judgement which is regarded as an example of a universal rule that one cannot state."
> (018-002)

> "The judgement of taste asserts a subjective necessity, which is represented as objective, under the presupposition of a common sense."
> (022-001)

> "Hereon is based the necessity of that agreement of the judgement of taste with the feeling of everyone, which is thought of as as it were a duty."
> (029-010)

> "In this modality of aesthetical judgements, viz. in the necessity claimed for them, lies an important feature of the Critique of Judgment."
> (029-011)
