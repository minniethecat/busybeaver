# Francis Hutcheson

## Biography

Francis Hutcheson (1694–1746) was an Irish philosopher and a central figure of the early Scottish Enlightenment. He held the Chair of Moral Philosophy at the University of Glasgow, where his students included Adam Smith. Hutcheson is best known for originating the concept of an "inner sense" for beauty and moral goodness — an innate faculty that responds to order, harmony, and virtue with an immediate, disinterested pleasure.

Hutcheson's *Inquiry into the Original of Our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue* (1725) argued that aesthetic and moral judgments are both grounded in innate senses, independent of reason and self-interest. This was a foundational move for eighteenth-century aesthetics.

## Relation to Kant

Kant inherited the concept of **disinterestedness** from Hutcheson via the German aesthetic tradition. "The aesthetical judgement about Beauty is according to *quality* disinterested; a point which had been laid down by such different writers as Hutcheson and Moses Mendelssohn." (P01-016) However, Kant transforms Hutcheson's empirical "inner sense" into a transcendental principle: disinterestedness is not a psychological disposition but a defining logical moment of the judgment of taste itself.

Hutcheson's moral sense theory also influenced Kant's early ethical thought, though Kant ultimately rejected it in favor of the autonomy of practical reason. Yet the parallel between aesthetic and moral feeling persists in Kant's claim that "the beautiful is the symbol of the morally good" (§59).

## Key Ideas Referenced

- **Inner Sense**: A faculty distinct from both reason and external sensation, which immediately perceives beauty and virtue.
- **Disinterested Pleasure**: The pleasure of beauty is distinct from both sensual gratification and the satisfaction of practical interests — a direct forerunner of Kant's first moment of taste.
