## Definition

Moral Feeling (*moralisches Gefühl*) in Kant's *Critique of Judgment* is the subjective susceptibility of the mind to moral Ideas. It serves as the presupposition underlying the judgement on the Sublime and functions as a bridge connecting the aesthetic and moral domains of human experience. For Kant, the moral feeling is not a sensory pleasure but an intellectual satisfaction arising from the conformity of the mind to the Moral Law.

## Kant's Account

In the "Analytic of the Sublime," Kant argues that the feeling of the Sublime presupposes a moral feeling in the human subject. Unlike the judgement of the Beautiful, which rests on the free play of imagination and understanding, the judgement of the Sublime engages reason and its practical Ideas, thereby requiring a receptivity to moral concepts.

Kant develops the moral feeling across the Critique in three key moves:

First, he identifies the moral feeling as "the presupposition of the moral feeling in man" (P01-024), grounding the Sublime's claim to universal communicability in a shared moral disposition rather than in mere sensibility.

Second, in § 29, he elaborates that the Sublime "inspires (the Object of the moral feeling)" (029-015), clarifying that the true object of sublimity is not external nature but the moral vocation within the subject.

Third, in the "General Remark" following the Analytic, Kant presents the moral feeling as cognate to the aesthetic judgement of the Sublime yet distinct from it: the Beautiful "prepares us to love something without interest; the Sublime, to esteem it even against our (sensible) interest" (029-021).

## Key Passages

> The feeling of the Sublime is "the presupposition of the moral feeling in man." (P01-024)

> The Sublime inspires (the Object of the moral feeling), as capable of determining the mind to an elevation of its state — i.e. the moral feeling — is yet so far cognate to the aesthetical judgement. (029-015–029-016)

> The Beautiful prepares us to love something without interest; the Sublime, to esteem it even against our (sensible) interest. (029-021)

> The pleasure (in the moral feeling) is the consequence of the practical determination of the will by the Moral Law. (037-002)
