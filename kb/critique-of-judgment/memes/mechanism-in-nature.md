# Mechanism in Nature

## Definition

Mechanism (*Mechanismus*) is the mode of explanation by which natural phenomena are accounted for through their constituent parts and efficient causes, proceeding from parts to whole. In Kant's critical philosophy, mechanism is the **constitutive principle** of natural science: every material event must be explicable through mechanical laws. Yet unlike dogmatic materialism, Kant recognizes that mechanism has limits — organisms resist purely mechanical explanation and require reflective judgment to adopt a teleological maxim.

"The distinction between nature's 'Technic' or purposive operation, and nature's Mechanism is fundamental for the explanation of natural law." (P01-038) This distinction structures the entire "Critique of Teleological Judgment" (Part II).

## Kant's Account

### Mechanism as Scientific Principle

Mechanism is indispensable to science. Without the maxim "explain everything mechanically," empirical investigation could never begin — every phenomenon might be attributed directly to purpose or design, eliminating the motive for discovering natural laws. Kant firmly endorses the mechanistic program: "It is of infinite importance to reason not to let slip the mechanism of nature in its products... because without this no insight into the nature of things can be attained." (§70)

### The Limits of Mechanism

However, mechanism reaches its limit in organisms. A machine's parts can exist independently of the whole; an organism's parts are only possible through their relation to the whole. "Crude materialism thus affording no explanation of the purposiveness in nature" (P01-042) — Epicurean atomism "really explains nothing, and least of all explains that illusion in our teleological judgements." (P01-039)

### The Compatibilist Resolution

Kant resolves the mechanism-teleology tension not by choosing one over the other but by assigning them to different faculties: mechanism belongs to **determinant judgment** (subsuming particulars under given universal laws), while teleology belongs to **reflective judgment** (seeking universal principles for given particulars). They are compatible maxims of investigation, not contradictory ontological claims. (P01-062)
