## Definition

Teleological Judgment (*teleologische Urteilskraft*) is the mode of reflective judgment that conceives of nature in terms of ends (*Zwecke*) and purposes. It forms the subject of the Second Part of Kant's *Critique of Judgment* — the "Critique of the Teleological Judgment." Unlike [[Determinant Judgment]], which subsumes particulars under given universals, teleological judgment reflects upon nature's particular forms and judges them as if they were purposively organized.

## Kant's Account

Kant introduces the teleological judgment as the counterpart to the aesthetical judgment. While the "Critique of the Aesthetical Judgment" examines judgments of taste and the sublime, the "Critique of the Teleological Judgment" investigates how we judge organisms and nature as a whole in purposive terms.

In the Introduction, Kant establishes that judgment "in the latter the teleological" (P01-013) bears upon our experience of nature. Teleological judgment is not constitutive — it does not determine what nature is — but regulative: it provides a maxim for reflecting upon nature that makes its organized complexity intelligible.

The core of Kant's account unfolds in the "Analytic of the Teleological Judgment" (§§ 62–68) and the "Dialectic of the Teleological Judgment" (§§ 69–78). In the Analytic, Kant argues that organisms must be judged as "natural purposes" (*Naturzwecke*) — beings that are both cause and effect of themselves. In the Dialectic, he resolves the antinomy between mechanical and teleological principles of explanation.

Kant's key insight is that teleological judgment operates as a regulative principle: we must judge certain natural objects "as if" they were produced by design, without claiming to know that they actually were. This allows teleology to coexist with mechanical explanation without contradiction.

## Key Passages

> It thus appears that the Judgement, properly so called, has two functions: in one it is determinant, in the other reflective. The determinant Judgement subsumes under a given universal; the reflective seeks the universal for a given particular. And the Critique of Judgement is divided into two corresponding parts: the Critique of the Aesthetical and of the Teleological Judgement. (P01-013, cf. P04-076)

> The concept of a thing as in itself a natural purpose is therefore no constitutive concept of Understanding or of Reason, but can only serve as a regulative concept for the reflective Judgement. (§ 65)

> Teleology, as a science, belongs to no doctrine, but only to the Critique, and indeed to that of a particular cognitive faculty, viz. the Judgement. (§ 79)
