## Definition

Free beauty (*pulchritudo vaga*) is one of two kinds of beauty distinguished by Kant in §16 of the *Critique of Judgment*. It "presupposes no concept of what the object ought to be" — the judgment of free beauty is made purely on the basis of the object's form, without any reference to a purpose or a concept of perfection (016-002). It is also called *self-subsistent* beauty because it belongs to the thing in itself, not to the thing as falling under a concept of a particular purpose.

## Kant's Account

Kant introduces the distinction between free and [[adherent-beauty|dependent beauty]] in §16, immediately after his argument in §15 that [[beauty]] cannot be reduced to [[perfection]]. The distinction serves to isolate the *pure* judgment of taste from judgments that are conditioned by concepts.

In the judging of a free beauty, "the judgement of taste is pure" — no concept of any purpose is presupposed for which the manifold of the object should serve (016-006). The Imagination is free to play with the form of the object, unconstrained by any rule supplied by the Understanding.

Kant provides several examples of free beauties (016-003, 016-005):

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Natural objects | Flowers, many birds (parrot, humming bird, bird of paradise), sea shells |
| Ornamental designs | *Delineations à la grecque*, foliage for borders, wall-papers |
| Musical forms | Fantasies (pieces without a theme), all music without words |

Flowers are the paradigmatic case: "Hardly any one but a botanist knows what sort of a thing a flower ought to be; and even he... pays no regard to this natural purpose if he is passing judgement on the flower by Taste" (016-003). The botanist's knowledge of the flower as a reproductive organ is irrelevant to the aesthetic judgment of its beauty.

## Key Distinctions

### Free Beauty vs. Adherent Beauty

The essential difference lies in whether a concept of the object's purpose is presupposed:

| | Free Beauty | Adherent Beauty |
|---|---|---|
| **Concept required?** | No concept of purpose | Presupposes a concept of what the thing ought to be |
| **Perfection involved?** | No perfection of any kind (016-004) | Involves a concept of perfection (016-007) |
| **Judgment type** | Pure judgment of taste | Applied (intellectualized) judgment of taste |
| **Examples** | Flowers, abstract designs, wordless music | Human beauty, buildings, horses |

Where the two kinds of beauty are confused, disputes arise between judges of taste. Kant notes that both parties may be right — "the one is speaking of free, the other of dependent, beauty" (016-013). One judge makes a pure judgment, the other an applied one.

### Relation to the Pleasant and the Good

The purity of a free beauty judgment is injured by mixing beauty with either the Pleasant (sensation) or the Good (the manifold that serves the object's purpose) (016-008). A free beauty judgment must abstract from charm, emotion, and any concept of what the object is meant to be.

## Role in the Critical System

Free beauty is central to Kant's project of isolating the *a priori* principle of taste. Only the judgment of free beauty can serve as the pure case from which the transcendental conditions of aesthetic judgment are derived. If all beauty were adherent — conditioned by concepts of purpose — the judgment of taste would collapse into a judgment of [[perfection]], and aesthetics would have no autonomous domain distinct from cognition and morality.

However, Kant also acknowledges that taste "gains" by the combination of aesthetic and intellectual satisfaction in adherent beauty, because the judgment becomes "fixed" and rules become possible (016-011). This tension between purity and applicability runs through the entire third *Critique*.

## Key Passages

> "There are two kinds of beauty; free beauty (pulchritudo vaga) or merely dependent beauty (pulchritudo adhaerens). The first presupposes no concept of what the object ought to be; the second does presuppose such a concept and the perfection of the object in accordance therewith."
> (016-002)

> "In the judging of a free beauty (according to the mere form) the judgement of taste is pure. There is presupposed no concept of any purpose, for which the manifold should serve the given Object, and which therefore is to be represented therein."
> (016-006)

> "Many birds (such as the parrot, the humming bird, the bird of paradise), and many sea shells are beauties in themselves, which do not belong to any object determined in respect of its purpose by concepts, but please freely and in themselves."
> (016-005)
