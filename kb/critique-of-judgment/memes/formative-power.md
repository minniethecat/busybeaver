## Definition

Formative power (*Bildungstrieb*), in the context of Kant's *Critique of Teleological Judgment*, is the productive faculty attributed to organized matter by which it generates and maintains purposive form in living beings. Kant adopts the term from the physiologist J.F. Blumenbach, who defined it as a formative impulse (*nisus formativus*) residing in organized bodies, distinct from the merely mechanical formative power universally present in matter (081-007). It is the central concept in Kant's endorsement of the epigenetic theory of generation.

## Kant's Account

### The Problem of Organism Generation

The problem that formative power addresses is how organized beings — natural purposes — can produce other organized beings of the same species. In §80–81, Kant surveys the competing theories: Occasionalism (God directly forms each organism), Preformation/Evolution (all organisms preformed in the original seed), and Epigenesis (organisms are genuinely *produced* by their parents through a purposive formative capacity).

If the teleological principle is assumed, "we can place at the basis of the cause of their internally purposive form either Occasionalism or Pre-established Harmony" (081-003). Occasionalism makes nature superfluous; preformation "removes every individual from the formative power of nature, in order to make it come immediately from the hand of the Creator" (081-005). But both fail to account for hybrids, which cannot be preformed in either parent yet exhibit purposive form.

### Kant's Endorsement of Epigenesis

Kant declares that "Reason views this way of explanation with peculiar favour" (081-006). Epigenesis "regards nature as self-producing, not merely as self-evolving: and so with the least expenditure of the supernatural leaves to nature all that follows after the first beginning." This does not eliminate the need for teleology — the original organized matter must itself be thought as produced according to purpose — but it confines the supernatural to the initial ground, allowing nature genuine productive power thereafter.

The theory accords with Kant's critical principle that we should not multiply supernatural interventions beyond necessity while still recognizing the limits of mechanical explanation.

### Blumenbach's Formative Impulse

Kant credits Blumenbach with the most significant contribution to the proof and proper application of epigenesis (081-007). Blumenbach's key insight is twofold: he rejects the spontaneous generation of life from lifeless matter (declaring it "contradictory to Reason"), while simultaneously acknowledging an "undeterminable but yet unmistakeable element" in organized matter — a facility that cannot be reduced to mere mechanism.

This facility is the *formative impulse* (*Bildungstrieb*), which Blumenbach distinguishes from the "merely mechanical formative power universally resident in matter" (081-007). The former is specific to organized bodies and operates under the "higher guidance and direction" of purposiveness; the latter is the general mechanical power by which matter forms crystals and other non-living structures.

### Formative Power vs. Mechanical Causality

The concept of formative power occupies the boundary between mechanism and teleology. It is not a *supernatural* power — it is a power of nature — but it is also not reducible to mechanical laws. It functions as a regulative concept that allows us to judge organized beings as natural products without reducing them to blind mechanism.

In §80, Kant speaks of "the operation of that fruitful formative power" (080-008) in the context of the earth producing organisms of increasing purposiveness. But he insists that even this formative power must ultimately be ascribed to an organization that is purposive — the teleological ground is pushed back but not eliminated.

## Key Distinctions

### Formative Impulse vs. Mechanical Formative Power

| | Formative Impulse (*Bildungstrieb*) | Mechanical Formative Power |
|---|---|---|
| **Domain** | Organized bodies only | All matter |
| **Character** | Purposive, self-propagating | Blind, law-governed |
| **Result** | Maintains species form across generations | Produces crystals, regular structures |
| **Source** | Blumenbach (endorsed by Kant) | General physics |
| **Explanation type** | Requires teleology | Mechanically explicable |

### Epigenesis vs. Preformation

| | Epigenesis (Generic Preformation) | Individual Preformation (Evolution) |
|---|---|---|
| **Production** | Nature produces new individuals | All individuals pre-exist, merely unfold |
| **Role of parents** | Productive: supply formative power | Mechanical: provide nourishment only |
| **Hybrids** | Explainable (two generic contributions) | Unexplainable |
| **Supernatural involvement** | Only at original creation of first organisms | Ongoing: each organism preformed by God |

## Role in the Critical System

Formative power serves as a crucial concept in Kant's philosophy of biology. It allows him to maintain that organisms are genuine *natural* products (not divine interventions) while simultaneously acknowledging that their production exceeds the explanatory reach of mechanical causality. The concept thus embodies the methodological duality that defines the entire *Critique of Teleological Judgment*: judge nature as if it were designed, without claiming to know that it is.

## Key Passages

> "It possesses in itself formative power of a self-propagating kind, which cannot be explained by the mere faculty of motion (mechanism) alone."
> (065-008)

> "He can suppose the bosom of mother earth... to have given birth in the beginning to creatures of less purposive form, that these again gave birth to others which formed themselves with greater adaptation to their place of birth and their relations to each other; until this womb becoming torpid and ossified, limited its births to definite species not further modifiable, and the manifoldness remained as it was at the end of the operation of that fruitful formative power."
> (080-008)

> "The advocates of the theory of evolution, who remove every individual from the formative power of nature, in order to make it come immediately from the hand of the Creator, would, however, not venture to regard this as happening according to the hypothesis of Occasionalism."
> (081-005)

> "In respect of the things which we can only represent as possible originally according to the causality of purposes, at least as concerns their propagation, this theory [Epigenesis] regards nature as self-producing, not merely as self-evolving: and so with the least expenditure of the supernatural leaves to nature all that follows after the first beginning."
> (081-006)

> "As regards this theory of Epigenesis, no one has contributed more either to its proof or to the establishment of the legitimate principles of its application... than Herr Hofr. Blumenbach. In all physical explanations of these formations he starts from organised matter. That crude matter should have originally formed itself according to mechanical laws, that life should have sprung from the nature of what is lifeless, that matter should have been able to dispose itself into the form of a self-maintaining purposiveness — this he rightly declares to be contradictory to Reason."
> (081-007)

> "At the same time he leaves to natural mechanism under this to us indispensable principle of an original organisation, an undeterminable but yet unmistakeable element, in reference to which the faculty of matter in an organised body is called by him a formative impulse (in contrast to, and yet standing under the higher guidance and direction of, that merely mechanical formative power universally resident in matter)."
> (081-007)
