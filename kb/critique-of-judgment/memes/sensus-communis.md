# Sensus Communis

## Definition

The *sensus communis* (*Gemeinsinn*) is the "subjective principle" that grounds the necessity of judgments of taste. It is not an external sense but the effect arising from "the free play of our cognitive powers" — a "common sense" that all judging subjects share in virtue of sharing the same cognitive faculties. (§20) "We must presuppose the idea of a *sensus communis* or common sense in which all men share." (P01-021)

Kant distinguishes the aesthetic *sensus communis* from the ordinary understanding (which he calls the *sensus communis logicus*). The aesthetic common sense is the capacity to judge by feeling rather than by concepts — "a faculty of judging that in its reflection takes account (a priori) of the mode of representation of all other men, in order, as it were, to compare its judgment with the collective reason of humanity." (§40)

## Kant's Account

### The Ground of Necessity

The fourth moment of the judgment of taste is **necessity**: when we judge an object beautiful, we demand that everyone else *ought* to agree. This "ought" cannot be grounded in concepts (for it would then be a logical necessity) or in experience (for it would then be merely probable). It requires a subjective yet universal ground — the *sensus communis*. (§§18–22)

### The Maxims of Common Human Understanding

Kant connects the *sensus communis* to three maxims of "common human understanding" (§40):
1. **Think for oneself** — the maxim of enlightenment (freedom from prejudice)
2. **Think from the standpoint of everyone else** — the maxim of enlarged thought
3. **Always think consistently** — the maxim of reason

The second maxim is the maxim of judgment itself, and it defines the *sensus communis*: to judge by abstracting from one's private conditions and taking a universal standpoint.

### Communicability and Knowledge

"The relation between the cognitive faculties requisite for Taste is also requisite for Intelligence or sound Understanding, and as we always presuppose the latter to be the same in others as in ourselves, so may we presuppose the former." (P01-021) The universal communicability of aesthetic judgments thus rests on the same ground as the universal communicability of knowledge — the shared structure of the human cognitive faculties.

## Key Passages

> "We must presuppose the idea of a *sensus communis* or common sense in which all men share. As knowledge admits of being communicated to others, so also does the feeling for beauty."
> (P01-021)
