# Dynamical Sublime

## Definition

The dynamical sublime (*Dynamisch-Erhabenen*) is the mode of the sublime that concerns **might** — the power of nature to overwhelm, and the mind's discovery within itself of a faculty of resistance that is independent of and superior to nature. (024-004) (028-002) It is distinguished from the mathematical sublime (which concerns magnitude and the faculty of cognition) by its reference to the faculty of desire: in the dynamical sublime, the purposiveness of the representation is ascribed to the Object "as *dynamical*" in relation to the mind's power of resistance, "referred through the Imagination ... to the *faculty of desire*." (024-005) Kant's formula: "Nature considered in an aesthetical judgment as might that has no dominion over us, is *dynamically sublime*." (028-002)

## Kant's Account

### Might, Dominion, and Fear

Kant defines might as "that which is superior to great hindrances" and dominion as might "superior to the resistance of that which itself possesses might." (028-001) Nature is dynamically sublime when it is represented as a might relative to which our physical powers are insignificant, yet a might that has no *dominion* over us — because we discover within ourselves a power of resistance that nature cannot touch.

The experience necessarily involves the representation of fear: "If nature is to be judged by us as dynamically sublime, it must be represented as exciting fear." (028-003) But this fear must remain hypothetical. The subject must regard the object as *fearful* without actually *being afraid* — "we can regard an object as *fearful*, without being afraid *of* it; viz. if we judge of it in such a way that we merely *think* a case in which we would wish to resist it, and yet in which all resistance would be altogether vain." (028-004) Kant gives the example of the virtuous man who "fears God without being afraid of Him; because to wish to resist Him and His commandments, he thinks is a case as to which *he* need not be anxious." (028-004)

This distinction is crucial: "He who fears can form no judgment about the Sublime in nature; just as he who is seduced by inclination and appetite can form no judgment about the Beautiful." (028-005) Actual terror blocks aesthetic reflection; only from a position of safety can the mind experience the elevation of the sublime.

### The Scene of Nature's Might

Kant provides a vivid catalogue of dynamically sublime objects: "Bold, overhanging, and as it were threatening, rocks; clouds piled up in the sky, moving with lightning flashes and thunder peals; volcanoes in all their violence of destruction; hurricanes with their track of devastation; the boundless ocean in a state of tumult; the lofty waterfall of a mighty river, and such like." (028-006) Each of these "exhibits our faculty of resistance as insignificantly small in comparison with their might." Yet "the sight of them is the more attractive, the more fearful it is, provided only that we are in security." (028-007)

The paradox is that these objects "raise the energies of the soul above their accustomed height, and discover in us a faculty of resistance of a quite different kind, which gives us courage to measure ourselves against the apparent almightiness of nature." (028-007)

### The Supersensible Faculty of Freedom

The dynamical sublime reveals a faculty of the mind that is not part of nature: "humanity in our person remains unhumiliated, though the individual might have to submit to this dominion." (028-008) In the immensity of nature and the inadequacy of our physical powers, we recognize our own limitation — "although at the same time in our rational faculty we find a different, non-sensuous standard." (028-008) The irresistibility of nature's might "discloses to us a faculty of judging independently of, and a superiority over, nature; on which is based a kind of self-preservation, entirely different from that which can be attacked and brought into danger by external nature." (028-008)

This faculty is moral freedom. Nature is sublime "not in so far as it excites fear; but because it calls up that power in us (which is not nature) of regarding as small the things about which we are solicitous (goods, health, and life), and of regarding its might ... as nevertheless without any dominion over us and our personality." (028-008) The dynamical sublime is thus the aesthetic anticipation of the moral law: it makes us conscious that we are not merely natural beings, subject to the causality of nature, but moral beings with a supersensible destination.

### The Soldier, War, and Moral Courage

Kant illustrates the feeling of the dynamical sublime with an anthropological observation. What is it that, "even to the savage, an object of the greatest admiration?" A man "who shrinks from nothing, who fears nothing, and therefore does not yield to danger, but rather goes to face it vigorously with the fullest deliberation." (028-011) Even in civilized society, the soldier commands peculiar veneration — "though only under the condition that he exhibit all the virtues of peace, gentleness, compassion, and even a becoming care for his own person; because even by these it is recognised that his mind is unsubdued by danger." (028-011) War itself, conducted with order and respect for the rights of citizens, "has something sublime in it, and makes the disposition of the people who carry it on thus, only the more sublime." Conversely, "a long peace generally brings about a predominant commercial spirit, and along with it, low selfishness, cowardice, and effeminacy, and debases the disposition of the people." (028-011)

### Religion and Superstition

The dynamical sublime also provides the criterion for distinguishing genuine religion from superstition. The man who is actually afraid before God, "because he finds reasons for fear in himself, whilst conscious by his culpable disposition of offending against a Might whose will is irresistible," is not in the frame of mind for admiring divine greatness — "for this a mood of calm contemplation and a quite free judgment are needed." (028-012) Only the consciousness of an upright disposition allows the mind to recognize "a sublimity of disposition conformable to His will; and thus he is raised above the fear of such operations of nature." (028-012) Superstition "establishes in the mind, not reverence for the Sublime, but fear and apprehension of the all-powerful Being to whose will the terrified man sees himself subject, without according Him any high esteem." From this "nothing can arise but a seeking of favour, and flattery, instead of a religion which consists in a good life." (028-012)

## Key Distinctions

| | Dynamical Sublime | Mathematical Sublime |
|---|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Faculty** | Faculty of desire (Reason) | Faculty of cognition (Understanding) |
| **Object's feature** | Might, power, dominion | Magnitude, size, extension |
| **Subject's inadequacy** | Physical powerlessness | Imaginative comprehension |
| **What is revealed** | Moral freedom, independence from nature | Supersensible faculty of cognition |
| **Required state** | Security from danger | Appropriate viewing distance |
| **Exemplary scenes** | Storms, volcanoes, overhanging rocks | Pyramids, St. Peter's, starry sky |
| **Relation to moral law** | Direct anticipation of practical Reason | Indirect — via the supersensible substrate |

## Role in the Critical System

The dynamical sublime is the aesthetic experience that most directly prepares the mind for the moral law. By revealing a faculty of resistance that is independent of nature and grounded in the supersensible, it bridges the gap between the theoretical cognition of nature and the practical determination of the will. Where the beautiful harmonizes Imagination and Understanding, and the mathematical sublime reveals the inadequacy of Imagination to the theoretical Ideas of Reason, the dynamical sublime reveals the superiority of practical Reason over nature itself.

This makes the dynamical sublime the zenith of Kant's aesthetic theory as a propaedeutic to morals. "Sublimity, therefore, does not reside in anything of nature, but only in our mind, in so far as we can become conscious that we are superior to nature within, and therefore also to nature without us." (028-013) The dynamical sublime allows the subject to feel — not merely to cognize — that the moral law within is greater than the starry sky above.
