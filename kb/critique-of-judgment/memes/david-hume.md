# David Hume

## Biography

David Hume (1711–1776) was a Scottish philosopher, historian, and economist, and one of the central figures of the Scottish Enlightenment. His empiricist philosophy — particularly his skepticism about causality, his naturalistic account of human understanding, and his analysis of taste — exerted a profound influence on Kant. Kant famously credited Hume with awakening him from his "dogmatic slumber" (*Prolegomena*, Preface).

Hume's essay "Of the Standard of Taste" (1757) was one of the first systematic treatments of aesthetic judgment in English, and it directly anticipates several themes Kant develops in the *Critique of Judgment*: the tension between subjective feeling and universal validity, the role of disinterested contemplation, and the need for a *sensus communis* to ground judgments of taste.

## Relation to Kant

Kant's relationship to Hume is complex. In theoretical philosophy, Kant opposed Hume's skepticism by establishing the a priori conditions of experience. But in aesthetics, Kant found much in Hume to build upon. The Editor notes that Kant "borrowed little from the writings of his predecessors, but struck out, as was ever his plan, a line of his own." (P01-014) Yet Hume's analysis of the standard of taste as a product of shared human nature rather than objective rules is a clear forerunner of Kant's *sensus communis*.

Hume's critique of the argument from design (*Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion*, 1779) also shaped Kant's treatment of physicotheology in the "Critique of Teleological Judgment" (§§85–86). Kant agrees with Hume that natural purposiveness cannot prove the existence of a divine designer — but unlike Hume, Kant finds a practical (moral) ground for the teleological worldview.

## Key Ideas Referenced

- **Standard of Taste**: Hume argued that the "standard of taste" is not an objective rule but a convergence of judgments among qualified critics, grounded in shared human nature. Kant develops this into the a priori principle of the *sensus communis*.
- **Skepticism about Design**: Hume's Philo demonstrates that the analogy between natural organisms and human artifacts is too weak to support the inference to a divine designer. Kant incorporates this insight into his regulative (rather than constitutive) treatment of teleology.
- **Empiricism and the Limits of Reason**: Hume's demonstration that causal necessity cannot be derived from experience was, for Kant, the spur to the entire critical philosophy.

## In the Critique of Judgment

Hume appears in the *Critique of Judgment* primarily through the concepts he influenced. Kant's analysis of taste as both subjective and universally valid, his treatment of the *sensus communis*, and his critique of physicotheology all bear the mark of Hume's philosophy. Kant dismisses "that cheap and easy philosophy which would fain deny the distinctiveness of nature's purposive operation" (P01-039) — a reference to Epicurean materialism, not Hume — but Hume's skepticism about design is taken seriously and given a critical resolution in Kant's doctrine of the supersensible substrate.
