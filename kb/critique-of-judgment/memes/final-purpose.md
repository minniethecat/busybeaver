# Final Purpose

## Definition

The final purpose (*Endzweck*, P02-016) is the ultimate purpose of nature considered as a teleological system — that for the sake of which everything else exists. Kant establishes the concept "by the concept of a final purpose, which it is forced to ascribe to nature." (P01-058) In Kant's teleology, the final purpose of creation can only be the human being, but not the human being as a natural being seeking happiness. Rather, it is the human being as a **moral subject** — the human being under the moral law, capable of freedom and of being an end in itself. (§84)

Kant distinguishes between **external purposiveness** (the utility of one natural thing for another), **internal purposiveness** (the perfection of an organism), and the **final purpose** — the unconditioned end that is not a means to anything further. Only the moral vocation of humanity satisfies this criterion. "The concept of freedom is the final purpose which (or its phenomenon in the sensible world) carries with it the purposiveness of nature." (P04-082)

## Kant's Account

### Why Not Happiness?

Kant systematically eliminates candidates for the final purpose. Happiness cannot be the final purpose because nature is manifestly indifferent to human happiness — the same natural forces that sustain life also destroy it. Moreover, happiness as a natural goal would reduce humanity to a link in nature's chain, not an end. "If we consider the human being as a natural being... we cannot see why he should be the final purpose of creation." (§83) The dispositions of nature "impel men, to the final purpose of humanity, viz. the morally good." (042-001)

### The Human Being as Noumenon

Only the human being considered as **noumenon** — as a free, moral agent under the moral law — can be the final purpose of nature. "Without man, the whole creation would be a mere waste, in vain, and without final purpose." (§86) The moral law gives the human being a dignity (*Würde*) that transcends all price (*Preis*). This dignity makes the human being "the only being in nature in which we can cognise a supersensible faculty (freedom)" — and therefore the only being that can serve as the unconditioned end of the natural world.

### The Bridge to Theology

The final purpose argument leads Kant to **ethicotheology** — the argument that the purposiveness of nature, combined with the moral vocation of humanity, justifies the postulation of a moral author of the world. This is not a theoretical proof of God's existence but a practical postulate: "We must assume a moral world-cause (an author of the world) in order to set before ourselves a final purpose in conformity with the moral law." (§87)
