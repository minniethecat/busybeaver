## Definition

The Ideal of beauty (*Ideal des Schönen*) is the highest archetype of taste — "the representation of an individual being, regarded as adequate to an Idea" (017-004). Unlike an empirical rule, the Ideal cannot be captured by concepts; it rests on an indeterminate Idea of Reason of a maximum and can only be exhibited in an individual presentation. It is the product of the [[Imagination]], not the Understanding, because it relies on presentation rather than concepts (017-004).

## Kant's Account

Kant develops the concept of the Ideal of beauty in §17 of the *Critique of Judgment*, following his analysis of the four moments of [[taste]]. He first establishes that there can be no objective rule of taste that determines the beautiful by means of concepts — every aesthetic judgment rests on the feeling of the subject, not on a concept of the object (017-001). Empirical criteria for taste (agreement across times and peoples) are possible but weak (017-002).

The Ideal is distinguished from a mere model or exemplar: one who imitates a model shows skill, but only he who can judge the model itself shows taste (017-004). The archetype of taste is therefore an Idea that each person must produce within themselves.

**Only the human figure** is susceptible of an Ideal of beauty. Flowers, furniture, landscapes, and even purposive objects like dwellings and gardens cannot have an Ideal — their purposes are either absent (vague beauty) or insufficiently determined and fixed by their concept (017-008, 017-009). Humanity alone carries its purpose within itself through Reason: "The only being which has the purpose of its existence in itself is *man*" (017-009).

## Key Distinctions

### Normal Idea vs. Rational Idea

Kant distinguishes two elements within the Ideal of human beauty (017-010):

| Element | Source | Function | Example |
|---------|--------|----------|---------|
| **Aesthetical Normal Idea** | Empirical — Imagination averages thousands of individual intuitions | Establishes the *correct* form of the species (the indispensable condition of all beauty) | The average stature and proportions of a thousand men (017-012) |
| **Rational Idea** | A priori — Reason supplies the purposes of humanity | Makes the *moral* visible in bodily form; elevates correctness to positive beauty | Expression of goodness of heart, purity, strength, peace (017-015) |

The Normal Idea is merely the standard of *correctness* — it pleases not by beauty but by contradicting no necessary condition (017-014). The Rational Idea alone constitutes the genuine Ideal, because it expresses the moral dimension without which the object would not please universally and positively (017-015).

### Ideal of Beauty vs. Judgment of Taste

A judgment in accordance with an Ideal of beauty is **not a pure judgment of taste**. The Ideal is "fixed by a concept of objective purposiveness" and belongs to a judgment that is "in part intellectual" (017-006). This is because the Ideal requires an Idea of Reason that determines *a priori* the purpose grounding the object's internal possibility (017-007). A pure aesthetic judgment, by contrast, requires no concept of purpose whatsoever.

### Ideal of Beauty vs. Ideal of Perfection

The Ideal of beauty concerns the beautiful human figure as the visible expression of moral Ideas. The Ideal of [[perfection]], in contrast, belongs to humanity "as an intelligence" — it concerns the rational and moral constitution of the person rather than their sensible appearance (017-009).

## Role in the Critical System

The Ideal of beauty occupies a bridge position between the aesthetic and the moral in Kant's critical architecture. Because it requires an Idea of Reason (the purposes of humanity) and expresses the moral in sensible form, it links [[beauty]] as the symbol of morality with the [[supersensible-substrate]] that grounds both nature and freedom. The fact that the Ideal permits no sensible charm to mingle with satisfaction — yet still elicits a deep interest — demonstrates that taste, at its highest reach, points toward the moral vocation of the subject (017-015).

## Key Passages

> "The highest model, the archetype of taste, is a mere Idea, which every one must produce in himself; and according to which he must judge every Object of taste... Hence that archetype of taste... is better called the Ideal of the beautiful."
> (017-004)

> "The only being which has the purpose of its existence in itself is *man*, who can determine his purposes by Reason... This *man* is, then, alone of all objects in the world, susceptible of an Ideal of *beauty*."
> (017-009)

> "The visible expression of moral Ideas that rule men inwardly... requires a union of pure Ideas of Reason with great imaginative power, even in him who wishes to judge of it, still more in him who wishes to present it."
> (017-015)
