# Edmund Burke

## Biography

Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was an Anglo-Irish statesman, political theorist, and philosopher. His *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757) was a landmark empirical study of aesthetic feeling, distinguishing the sublime (associated with terror, power, obscurity) from the beautiful (associated with love, smallness, smoothness) on purely psychological grounds.

Burke's empirical method — cataloguing the qualities of objects that produce aesthetic feelings — stands in sharp contrast to Kant's transcendental approach, which seeks the a priori grounds of aesthetic judgment in the structure of the subject. Yet Kant acknowledges Burke's influence and engages seriously with his analysis.

## Relation to Kant

Kant treats Burke as the most sophisticated representative of the **empirical** (as opposed to rationalist) approach to aesthetics. In the "Remark" appended to §29, Kant discusses Burke at length, praising his physiological analysis while rejecting its claim to universal validity. For Kant, Burke's catalogue of sublime and beautiful qualities describes the *matter* of aesthetic experience but cannot account for its *form* — the claim to universal validity that distinguishes a judgment of taste from a mere report of private feeling.

The Editor notes that "the freedom of the pure judgement of taste from the thraldom of concepts had, to a large extent, been established by his predecessors in Germany; but sentiment, emotion, had been appealed to as its object, and not the form of purposiveness." (P01-018) Kant's innovation is to replace Burke's sensationalist account with a formal, a priori principle.

## Key Ideas Referenced

- **Empirical Sublime**: Burke's analysis of the sublime as arising from terror at a safe distance — the "delightful horror" — is the direct precursor to Kant's "dynamically sublime."
- **Physiological Aesthetics**: Burke grounded aesthetic responses in human physiology (the tension and relaxation of nerves), which Kant regarded as a legitimate but *merely empirical* account, incapable of grounding the necessity of aesthetic judgments.

## In the Critique of Judgment

Kant discusses Burke explicitly in the General Remark following §29, acknowledging the value of his "physiological exposition" while insisting that it cannot replace the transcendental deduction of taste. The dynamically sublime (§§28–29) shows the clearest debt to Burke's empirical descriptions, though Kant transforms them into an argument about the supersensible vocation of the subject.
