# Interest

## Definition

Interest (*Interesse*) is the satisfaction that is "bound up with the existence of an object." (002-002) Unlike the pure satisfaction of taste — which is **disinterested**, contemplating the object's form without concern for its reality — interest involves a desire for or attachment to the object's actual existence. Kant uses the absence of interest as the defining mark of beauty: "Taste is the faculty of judging an object or a method of representing it by an *entirely disinterested* satisfaction or dissatisfaction." (005-008)

## Kant's Account

### The First Moment: Quality

Interest is central to the **first moment** of the judgment of taste. Kant argues that the satisfaction in the beautiful is unique precisely because it is *disinterested* — unlike the satisfaction in the agreeable (which involves sensory interest) and the good (which involves rational interest). "The satisfaction which determines the judgement of taste is disinterested." (002-001) The Editor notes that this point "had been laid down by Kant'"'"'s predecessors in Germany, Hutcheson and Mendelssohn." (P01-016) But Kant gives it systematic grounding within the table of judgments.

### Interest in the Agreeable

The agreeable (*das Angenehme*) "expresses an interest" (003-006) because it gratifies the senses and creates a desire for the object's continued existence. "As regards the interest of inclination in the case of the agreeable, everyone says: 'Hunger is the best sauce.'" Kant observes that the satisfaction in the agreeable "presupposes or generates a want." (005-005) The hungry person does not contemplate food — they want to eat it. This interest is purely subjective and cannot ground a universal judgment.

### Interest in the Good

The good also involves interest, but of a different kind — **rational interest**. When we judge an action morally good, we take an interest in its existence because reason commands it. "For the good is the object of will (i.e. of a faculty of desire determined by reason)... But to will something and to have a satisfaction in its existence, i.e. to take an interest in it, are identical." (004-002) Unlike the agreeable's pathological interest, the good's interest is practical and universal — yet it still concerns the object's *existence*, not its mere form.

### The Beautiful: Free from All Interest

Only the beautiful produces a satisfaction entirely free from interest. "In the Beautiful alone is a disinterested and *free* satisfaction; for no interest, either of sense or of reason, here forces our assent." (005-003) This freedom from interest is what distinguishes aesthetic judgment from both sensory gratification and moral approbation. "The beautiful prepares us to love something, even nature itself, without interest." (P01-025)

### Empirical and Intellectual Interest

Kant later introduces a qualified exception. While the *pure* judgment of taste is disinterested, we can take an **empirical interest** in the beautiful (socially, as a mark of cultivation) and an **intellectual interest** in natural beauty (as a sign of nature'"'"'s amenability to our moral vocation). The intellectual interest in natural beauty — "to feel interest in the beauty of Nature" (P01-025) — is a mark of the morally good soul, even though it does not enter into the determining ground of the judgment itself.

## Key Distinctions

| Kind of Satisfaction | Object | Relation to Interest | Faculty |
|---------------------|--------|---------------------|---------|
| **Agreeable** | Pleasant sensation | Pathological interest | Sense |
| **Beautiful** | Form of purposiveness | **Disinterested** | Judgment |
| **Good** | Moral law / perfection | Rational interest | Reason |

## Role in the Critical System

The concept of interest — and its exclusion from the judgment of taste — secures the autonomy of aesthetics. By showing that beauty pleases without any interest in the object'"'"'s existence, Kant carves out a domain of experience that is neither sensory (like the agreeable) nor practical (like the good). This negative characterization of taste ("disinterested") is the foundation on which the positive characterization (universal validity, subjective purposiveness, exemplary necessity) is built. Without the disinterestedness of taste, the entire critical project of the third *Critique* would collapse into either empiricism or rationalism.
