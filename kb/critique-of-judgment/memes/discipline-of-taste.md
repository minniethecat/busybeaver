## Definition

Discipline of taste (*Disziplin des Geschmacks*) is the regulative function by which the faculty of taste constrains, trains, and guides Genius in the production of beautiful art. Taste "is the discipline (or training) of Genius; it clips its wings closely, and makes it cultured and polished; but, at the same time, it gives guidance as to where and how far it may extend itself, if it is to remain purposive" (050-004). In its broader application, discipline also names the negative, restrictive aspect of culture — "the discipline of the inclinations" — by which humanity is liberated from the despotism of mere desires (083-012).

## Kant's Account

### The Tension Between Genius and Taste

In §50, Kant addresses a fundamental tension in the production of beautiful art. Genius supplies the "abundance and originality of Ideas" — the creative, productive element. Taste supplies judgment — the critical, regulative element. The question is which should have priority when they conflict.

Kant's answer is unequivocal: taste takes precedence. "Abundance and originality of Ideas are less necessary to beauty than the accordance of the Imagination in its freedom with the conformity to law of the Understanding. For all the abundance of the former produces in lawless freedom nothing but nonsense; on the other hand, the Judgement is the faculty by which it is adjusted to the Understanding" (050-003).

This is a remarkable claim in a work often read as championing aesthetic autonomy. The freedom of Genius is not absolute; it is freedom *under law* — the lawfulness without law (*Gesetzmäßigkeit ohne Gesetz*) that Kant established as the principle of taste in the *Analytic of the Beautiful*.

### The Four Faculties of Beautiful Art

Kant enumerates four faculties requisite for beautiful art: "Imagination, Understanding, Spirit, and Taste" (050-005). Each plays a distinct role:
- **Imagination** provides the wealth of intuitive representations
- **Understanding** supplies concepts and the conformity to law
- **Spirit** (*Geist*) furnishes the animating principle — the aesthetic Ideas that give the work its life
- **Taste** disciplines and harmonizes the other three, ensuring purposiveness

Taste is thus both one faculty among four and the coordinating principle that holds the other three in productive tension. Without taste, genius would produce "nonsense" (*Unsinn*); without genius, taste would produce merely correct but lifeless works.

### Discipline as Culture

In §83, Kant develops a second sense of discipline — not of Genius but of the human being. The "culture of training (discipline)" is negative: it "consists in the liberation of the will from the despotism of desires" (083-010). Through discipline, we become free not by eliminating inclinations but by making them subject to rational governance.

This connects the aesthetic doctrine of §50 to the moral-teleological argument of the second half of the third *Critique*: discipline — whether of Genius by taste or of desire by reason — is the mechanism by which nature prepares humanity for its vocation as the final purpose of creation. "As concerns the discipline of the inclinations... we are in a high degree cultivated by art and science" (083-012).

## Key Distinctions

### Taste as Discipline vs. Taste as Judgment

| | Taste as Discipline (§50) | Taste as Pure Judgment (§1–22) |
|---|---|---|
| **Object** | Products of Genius (beautiful art) | Objects of nature (free/adherent beauty) |
| **Function** | Restricts and trains Genius | Judges the beautiful |
| **Relation to Understanding** | Upholds conformity to law against Imagination's excess | Determines the harmony of Imagination and Understanding |
| **Outcome** | Cultured, polished art | Aesthetic pleasure (disinterested) |

### Discipline of Taste vs. Discipline of Inclinations

| | Discipline of Genius (§50) | Discipline of Inclinations (§83) |
|---|---|---|
| **Domain** | Aesthetic | Moral-practical |
| **What is disciplined** | Imagination's lawless freedom | Will's subjection to desires |
| **Agent** | Taste | Reason + culture |
| **End** | Beautiful art | Moral freedom |

## Role in the Critical System

The discipline of taste is a pivotal concept in the transition from aesthetic to moral judgment. It demonstrates that aesthetic freedom — the freedom of Genius — is not lawless but internally governed by a principle (taste) that is analogous to the moral law. Just as the moral law disciplines inclination without extinguishing it, taste disciplines Genius without stifling it. This structural analogy between the aesthetic and the moral is precisely what Kant develops in §59, where beauty is called the "symbol of morality."

## Key Passages

> "To ask whether it is more important for the things of beautiful art that Genius or Taste should be displayed, is the same as to ask whether in it more depends on Imagination or on Judgement."
> (050-002)

> "Abundance and originality of Ideas are less necessary to beauty than the accordance of the Imagination in its freedom with the conformity to law of the Understanding. For all the abundance of the former produces in lawless freedom nothing but nonsense."
> (050-003)

> "Taste, like the Judgement in general, is the discipline (or training) of Genius; it clips its wings closely, and makes it cultured and polished; but, at the same time, it gives guidance as to where and how far it may extend itself, if it is to remain purposive."
> (050-004)

> "If, then, in the conflict of these two properties in a product something must be sacrificed, it should be rather on the side of genius; and the Judgement, which in the things of beautiful art gives its decision from its own proper principles, will rather sacrifice the freedom and wealth of the Imagination than permit anything prejudicial to the Understanding."
> (050-004)

> "The culture of training (discipline), is negative, and consists in the liberation of the will from the despotism of desires."
> (083-010)

> "As concerns the discipline of the inclinations... we are in a high degree cultivated by art and science. We are civilised to the point of being overburdened by all sorts of social propriety and decency."
> (083-012)
