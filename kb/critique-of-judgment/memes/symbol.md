## Definition

A symbol, in Kant's critical vocabulary, is a mode of indirect presentation (*hypotyposis*) in which an intuition is supplied for a concept not directly (as in a schema) but by analogy. Whereas a schema presents a concept directly through a rule-governed construction, a symbol presents a concept indirectly by transferring the rule of reflection from one object to an entirely different one. The symbol thus operates through the free play of judgment rather than through determinate cognition.

## Kant's Account

Kant develops his account of the symbol in §59 of the Critique, titled "Of Beauty as the Symbol of Morality." He introduces symbolization as the second species of hypotyposis alongside schematism. While schemata present concepts of the understanding directly through a priori time-determinations, symbols serve concepts of reason — ideas for which no adequate intuition can ever be given.

Kant explains that symbolic presentation occurs when "to a concept which only the reason can think, and to which no sensible intuition is adequate, an intuition is supplied with which the procedure of the judgment is merely analogous" (059-002). The judgment performs a "double function": it applies the intuition to the sensible object, and then applies the "mere rule of its procedure upon that intuition" to an entirely different object, of which the first is only the symbol (059-002).

The culmination of Kant's argument is the famous claim that "the Beautiful is the symbol of the morally Good" (059-005). This is not merely a rhetorical flourish — it constitutes the bridge between the aesthetic and moral domains, connecting the Third Critique to the Second. The analogy operates through four shared features: both the beautiful and the good please immediately (not through consequences), both please disinterestedly, both involve the freedom of the faculties (imagination in taste, will in morality), and both lay claim to universal validity.

## Key Distinctions

- **Symbol vs. Schema**: Schemata present concepts directly through a priori rules; symbols present indirectly through analogy. Schemata belong to determinate judgment; symbols to reflective judgment.
- **Symbol vs. Sign**: A symbol is not a mere arbitrary sign — it participates in the analogy it presents, sharing structural features with what it symbolizes.
- **Symbol vs. Metaphor**: Kant's symbol is more precise than metaphor — it involves a structural analogy of reflective procedures, not merely a figurative comparison.

## Role in the Critical System

The symbol plays a crucial architectonic role: it bridges the theoretical and practical domains that Kant had separated in the first two Critiques. Through the symbolic presentation of the morally good in the beautiful, aesthetic judgment acquires moral significance without losing its autonomy. The beautiful is not merely pleasant — it is a *symbolon* of the supersensible substrate in which the theoretical and practical realms are united. This convergence is one of the central achievements of the Third Critique.

## Key Passages

> "All hypotyposis (presentation, *subjectio sub adspectum*) is either schematical or symbolical. In the latter case to a concept which only the reason can think, and to which no sensible intuition is adequate, an intuition is supplied with which the procedure of the judgment is merely analogous to that which it observes in schematism."
> (059-002)

> "Thus the judgment performs a double function: first applying the intuition to the concept of a sensible object, and then secondly applying the mere rule of its procedure upon that intuition to an entirely different object, of which the first is only the symbol."
> (059-002)

> "Now I say the Beautiful is the symbol of the morally Good, and that it is only in this respect (a reference which is natural to every man and which every man expects from others as a duty) that it gives pleasure with a claim to the agreement of everyone else."
> (059-005)
