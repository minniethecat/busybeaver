# Culture

## Definition

Culture (*Kultur*) in Kant's critical philosophy is not merely the accumulation of learning or refinement of manners, but the **cultivation of the mind's powers** — the development of the capacity to set ends freely, beyond the determinations of mere nature. Culture is the process by which the human being becomes "receptive to higher purposes than nature itself can supply" (029-010).

In the *Critique of Judgment*, culture occupies a pivotal position: it is the **ultimate purpose of nature** in relation to the human species (§83), and it is through culture — particularly aesthetic culture — that the transition from nature to freedom is prepared.

## Kant's Account

### Culture of Taste

The judgment of taste is not innate; it requires formation. Kant refers to "the formation or the culture of taste" (P03-008) as a process that unfolds through repeated exposure to exemplary works and through the comparison of one's judgment with that of others.

This culture does not teach rules — taste cannot be learned by precepts — but develops the capacity to feel the harmony of the faculties. "Not in precepts, but in the culture of the mental powers by means of those elements of preliminary knowledge called *humaniora*" (060-002) does taste mature.

### Culture and the Sublime

The sublime has a special relation to culture. "A far greater culture, as well of the aesthetical Judgment, is requisite in order to judge the sublime" (029-003) — more so than for the beautiful. The sublime does not come naturally; it presupposes a mind already "prepared by culture" (029-005) to receive it.

Yet Kant immediately qualifies this: the susceptibility to the sublime is "present in every man if he has any culture at all" (029-010). Culture is not what *creates* the feeling of the sublime but what *opens the mind* to it. The sublime "has its foundation in human nature" (029-010), and culture merely attunes us to what is already latent.

This distinguishes Kant's account from a mere sociology of taste: culture activates a universal predisposition, rather than merely inculcating contingent social norms.

### Culture vs. Enjoyment

Kant draws a sharp line between culture and mere enjoyment. The agreeable "has no reference to culture, but belongs to mere enjoyment" (029-015). Enjoyment is passive, private, and tied to sensation; culture is active, cultivates the higher cognitive powers, and points toward the universal.

In the context of art, Kant similarly distinguishes between "enjoyment rather than culture" (053-004) — some art merely entertains, while true beautiful art "furthers the culture of the mental powers in reference to social communication" (044-004).

### Culture as the Ultimate Purpose of Nature

In the *Critique of Teleological Judgment*, culture emerges as the answer to the question: what is the ultimate purpose of nature? Not happiness — for nature is indifferent to human happiness — but culture, understood as the aptitude for setting ends freely.

Nature's ultimate purpose is to produce a being capable of freedom. "The culture of the mental powers by means of the beautiful arts" (053-006) is part of this process. Through aesthetic experience, the mind cultivates its capacity for disinterested satisfaction, for universal communicability, and for the harmony of faculties — all of which prepare the ground for moral disposition.

### The Mean Between Nature and Artifice

Kant locates genuine culture at "that mean between the higher culture and simple nature which furnishes the true standard for taste" (060-003). Culture that becomes over-refined loses touch with the natural feeling that is its source; culture that remains merely natural cannot attain universality.

This mean is exemplified by the classics — works that have survived "the progress of culture" and "received the longest approval" (032-005) among those of "developed and liberal minds."

## Key Distinctions

| Concept | Relation to Culture | Mode | Outcome |
|---------|-------------------|------|---------|
| **Taste** | Requires culture for its development | Reflective judgment | Universal communicability |
| **The Sublime** | Presupposes culture more than the beautiful | Aesthetic judgment | Moral disposition |
| **Enjoyment** | No reference to culture | Sensory | Private gratification |
| **Morality** | Culture prepares for it | Practical reason | Freedom |

## Role in the Critical System

Culture is the **bridge concept** between aesthetics and teleology, and ultimately between nature and freedom. In the first *Critique*, nature is a mechanism; in the second *Critique*, freedom is a fact of reason. Culture explains how a being that is part of nature (the human) can become receptive to the demands of freedom.

The judgment of taste exercises the mind's capacity for disinterested, universally-valid judgment; the sublime awakens the feeling of the supersensible; and the beautiful arts supply to the mind a culture that "makes it cultured and polished" (050-004) without yet making it moral.

Culture is thus the **historical and anthropological dimension** of the critical project — the account of how reason's demands become actual in the temporal development of the human species.
