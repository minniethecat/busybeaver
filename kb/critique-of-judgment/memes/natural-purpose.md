# Natural Purpose

## Definition

A natural purpose (*Naturzweck*) is a product of nature in which the parts are only possible through their relation to the whole, and the whole is in turn determined by its parts in a reciprocal causal relationship. (010-001) Kant defines it through two essential characteristics: first, the parts must be possible only through their relation to the whole (unlike a machine, where parts can exist independently); second, the parts must combine into the whole by being reciprocally cause and effect of each other. An organism is the paradigmatic natural purpose — "an organised and self-organising being" (§65).

Unlike artifacts, whose purposiveness is external (imposed by a designer), a natural purpose exhibits **internal purposiveness**: its organization is intrinsic to its nature. "A thing exists as a *natural purpose* if it is cause and effect of itself" — a formulation Kant borrows from the tradition of natural philosophy but grounds critically.

## Kant's Account

### The Antinomy of Teleological Judgment

Natural purposes generate an unavoidable dialectic for reflective judgment. On one hand, we cannot explain organisms without the concept of purpose — the parts function *for the sake of* the whole. On the other hand, mechanism is the only legitimate mode of explanation for natural science. This generates the **antinomy of teleological judgment**: Thesis — "All production of material things is possible according to merely mechanical laws"; Antithesis — "Some products of material nature cannot be judged as possible according to merely mechanical laws" (§70).

Kant resolves the antinomy by recognizing that both principles are **regulative**, not constitutive. Mechanism is a maxim for *determinant* judgment; purposiveness is a maxim for *reflective* judgment. They do not contradict because they prescribe different *modes of investigation*, not contradictory properties of objects themselves.

### Organisms as the Limit of Mechanism

"Nature organises itself and its organised products in every species, no doubt after one general pattern but yet with suitable deviations, which self-preservation demands according to circumstances." (P01-041) An organism cannot be produced by mechanical aggregation of parts — its parts presuppose the whole. This makes organisms the "limit case" that forces reflective judgment to adopt a teleological maxim.

Kant dismisses both crude materialism (Epicurean atomism) and hylozoism (world-soul) as inadequate. The former "really explains nothing" (P01-039); the latter is self-contradictory because "lifelessness, *inertia*, is the essential characteristic of matter." (P01-042)
