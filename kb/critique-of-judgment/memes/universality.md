Universality (*Allgemeinheit*) is the **Second Moment** of the judgment of taste in Kant's "Analytic of the Beautiful" (§6–9). It describes the judgment's claim to validity for every judging subject, yet without grounding that claim in a determinate concept. This distinguishes aesthetic universality from logical universality: the former is *subjective*, the latter *objective*.

## Subjective vs. Logical Universality

Kant draws a sharp distinction between two kinds of universality. Logical universality rests on concepts and applies to judgments of cognition. Aesthetic universality, by contrast, rests on the subjective condition of the free play of the faculties:

> [008-006] … a universality which does not rest on concepts of the Object (even though only empirical ones) is not logical but aesthetical.

This aesthetic universality is a *quantity* peculiar to judgments of taste:

> [008-009] … it an *aesthetical quantity* of universality, i.e. of validity for every one, which is not to be met with in judgments about the pleasant.

## The "Voiceless" Universality

Unlike the universality of logical judgments — which can be demonstrated by appealing to concepts — the universality of the judgment of taste is "voiceless." It cannot be proved; it can only be *exemplified*:

> [008-004] … the judgment of taste does not postulate the agreement of every one (for that can only be done by a logically universal judgment because it can bring forward reasons); it only *imputes* this agreement to every one.

The subject who judges something beautiful does not deduce this from a rule; she expects others to feel as she does, while acknowledging that they may not.

## Universality and Communicability

In §9, Kant identifies the ground of this subjective universality in the **universal communicability** (*[[Communicability]]*) of the mental state:

> [009-009] … cognitives faculties; but on the universality of the subjective conditions of the judgment of taste, i.e. on the free play of Imagination and Understanding.

Because the free play of the cognitive faculties is a condition for cognition in general, it must be universally communicable — and the pleasure arising from it inherits this communicability.

| Moment | Name | Characteristic |
|--------|------|---------------|
| First | Quality | Disinterested pleasure |
| Second | Universality | Subjective, without a concept |
| Third | Relation | Purposiveness without purpose |
| Fourth | Modality | Exemplary necessity |

## Universality in the Sublime

Kant extends the analysis of universality to the judgment of the sublime. In the sublime, the universality claim is even stronger because it is grounded in the moral feeling, which Kant takes to be universally rooted in human reason:

> [031-005] … validity, which is not a logical universality in accordance with concepts, but which nevertheless claims the agreement of every one.

## Universality and the Antinomy of Taste

In the Dialectic of Aesthetic Judgment (§56–57), Kant addresses the antinomy of taste: the thesis that judgments of taste are not based on concepts (otherwise one could dispute about them), and the antithesis that they are based on concepts (otherwise one could not even claim universality). The resolution hinges on the concept of an indeterminate concept — the supersensible substrate of phenomena — which makes the universality claim intelligible without reducing taste to a determinate rule.

> [057-010] … perfection (on account of the universality of the same), and settle the dispute between the critics of taste.
