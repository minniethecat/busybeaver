## Definition

The highest good (*summum bonum*, *Weltbeste*) is the unconditioned final purpose of creation: the combination of the greatest happiness of rational beings with the highest condition of the good in itself, namely, morality (088-001). It is the purpose that needs no other as condition of its possibility — the endpoint of the entire teleological chain. Only man, considered as noumenon and as subject of the moral law, is capable of being this final purpose, to which all of nature is teleologically subordinated.

## Kant's Account

### Final Purpose of Creation

Kant develops the concept of the highest good in the concluding sections of the *Critique of Teleological Judgment* (§§84–91). He begins by defining a *final purpose* as "that purpose which needs no other as condition of its possibility" (084-002). If we assume a designedly-working cause of the world, the question inevitably arises: what is the *final* purpose for which this cause creates? Mere mechanism cannot answer this question.

"Now we have in the world only one kind of beings whose causality is teleological, i.e. is directed to purposes and is at the same time so constituted that the law according to which they have to determine purposes for themselves is represented as unconditioned and independent of natural conditions, and yet as in itself necessary. The being of this kind is man, but man considered as noumenon" (084-006). Man alone possesses a supersensible faculty — freedom — and the moral law, which provides an unconditioned end that nature cannot supply.

### Man as the Ultimate Purpose

"If now things of the world, as beings dependent in their existence, need a supreme cause acting according to purposes, man is the final purpose of creation; since without him the chain of mutually subordinated purposes would not be complete as regards its ground" (084-007). Without man as moral subject, the entire teleological structure of nature would lack a terminus — an end for the sake of which all other ends exist.

This is a radical claim: the whole of nature, from the simplest organism to the most complex ecosystem, is teleologically ordered with reference to the moral vocation of humanity. Kant is careful to limit this to man as noumenon — it is not man as a natural being (subject to desires and inclinations) but man as a free, moral agent that constitutes the final purpose.

### The Highest Good in the World

The highest good has two components: (1) virtue, the supreme good (*bonum supremum*), which is the condition of worthiness to be happy; and (2) happiness proportioned to virtue, which together constitute the *complete* good (*bonum consummatum*). Kant writes that "this purpose is the highest good in the world possible through freedom" (087-005), and that the moral law commands us to strive for "a universal highest purpose which yet we, in common with all rational beings, are capable of effecting" (086-011).

This introduces the moral argument for the existence of God: because the moral law obligates us to promote the highest good, and because we cannot ensure the connection between virtue and happiness through natural means alone, we are rationally justified in postulating a moral author of the world who guarantees this connection. "The actuality of a highest morally-legislating Author is therefore only sufficiently established for the *practical* employment of our Reason" (088-010).

### Ethico-Theology

The highest good is the foundation of what Kant calls *ethico-theology* — the inference from the moral law and its required final purpose to the existence of a moral author of the world. This stands in contrast to *physico-theology*, which argues from the design of nature to a wise creator. Physico-theology can at most establish a "highest artist" (085-005), not a moral God. Only ethico-theology, grounded in the unconditioned moral law, can establish "the highest wisdom combined with all other perfections" (085-013) — a God who is not merely intelligent but holy, beneficent, and just.

## Key Distinctions

### Physico-Theology vs. Ethico-Theology

| | Physico-Theology | Ethico-Theology |
|---|---|---|
| **Starting point** | Purposiveness of nature | Moral law (unconditioned) |
| **Arrives at** | A highest artist/architect | A moral author of the world |
| **Proof type** | Empirical, a posteriori | Practical, a priori (moral) |
| **Limitation** | Cannot establish divine wisdom or holiness | Valid only for practical Reason |
| **Section** | §85 | §86–88 |

### The Highest Good vs. Other Goods

The highest good is not simply the greatest amount of any particular good. It is the systematic union of the moral and the natural orders — the harmony of virtue and happiness. "The highest physical good possible in the world... is happiness," but this happiness is only good when combined with "the highest moral good, i.e. the conformity of rational beings to the moral law" (087-006). Neither happiness without virtue nor virtue without happiness constitutes the complete highest good.

## Role in the Critical System

The highest good completes the systematic arc of the third *Critique*. The *Critique of Aesthetic Judgment* established the subjective formal purposiveness of beautiful objects; the *Critique of Teleological Judgment* established the objective material purposiveness of organized beings; the doctrine of the highest good establishes the *absolute* purposiveness — the unconditioned end for which all other ends exist. It thus effects the transition from nature to freedom that Kant announced as the task of the third *Critique* in its Introduction.

## Key Passages

> "A final purpose is that purpose which needs no other as condition of its possibility."
> (084-002)

> "Now we have in the world only one kind of beings whose causality is teleological... The being of this kind is man, but man considered as noumenon; the only natural being in which we can recognise, on the side of its peculiar constitution, a supersensible faculty (freedom) and also the law of causality, together with its Object, which this faculty may propose to itself as highest purpose (the highest good in the world)."
> (084-006)

> "His existence involves the highest purpose to which, as far as is in his power, he can subject the whole of nature... If now things of the world, as beings dependent in their existence, need a supreme cause acting according to purposes, man is the final purpose of creation."
> (084-007)

> "The moral law, as the formal rational condition of the employment of our freedom, obligates us by itself alone without dependence on any purpose as material condition... but it also determines for us, and indeed *a priori*, a final purpose, to strive after which it makes obligatory; and this purpose is the highest good in the world possible through freedom."
> (087-005)

> "The actuality of a highest morally-legislating Author is therefore only sufficiently established for the *practical* employment of our Reason, without determining anything theoretically in respect of His existence."
> (088-010)
