## Definition

Adherent beauty (*pulchritudo adhaerens*), also called dependent or conditioned beauty, is the kind of beauty that "presupposes a concept of what the object ought to be; and consequently a concept of its perfection" (016-002). Unlike [[free-beauty|free beauty]], which is judged purely by form without any concept of purpose, adherent beauty belongs to objects that fall under the concept of a particular purpose — human beings, animals, and buildings.

## Kant's Account

Kant introduces adherent beauty in §16 of the *Critique of Judgment* as the counterpart to free beauty. While free beauty is the pure case of the judgment of [[taste]], adherent beauty represents the more common situation in which we judge an object with awareness of what it is meant to be.

The paradigm cases of adherent beauty are: "human beauty (i.e. of a man, a woman, or a child), the beauty of a horse, or a building (be it church, palace, arsenal, or summer-house)" (016-007). In each case, we cannot abstract from the concept of the object's purpose without doing violence to the judgment.

### The Constraint of Purpose

When a concept of purpose is presupposed, the freedom of the [[Imagination]] is limited: "By such a concept the freedom of the Imagination which disports itself in the contemplation of the figure would be only limited" (016-006). We could add spirals and light regular lines to a figure — as the New Zealanders do with tattooing — but only if it were *not* the figure of a human being (016-009). The purpose of the object constrains what forms are aesthetically permissible.

### Mixed Judgment

A judgment of adherent beauty is not a pure judgment of taste. It combines aesthetic satisfaction (pleasure in the form) with intellectual satisfaction (recognition that the manifold serves the object's purpose). As a result, the judgment is no longer "free" — it is "made dependent on the purpose in its manifold, like a judgement of Reason, and thus limited" (016-010).

## Key Distinctions

### Purity vs. Fixity

Kant acknowledges a trade-off between purity and fixity in the two kinds of beauty:

| | Free Beauty | Adherent Beauty |
|---|---|---|
| **Judgment** | Pure, free | Applied, conditioned |
| **Universality** | Subjective universal validity | Can be prescribed by rules |
| **Fixity** | Not fixed | Fixed by combination with the Good |
| **Role** | Grounds the *a priori* principle of taste | Makes taste applicable to the world |

Taste "gains by this combination of aesthetical with intellectual satisfaction, inasmuch as it becomes fixed" (016-011). But Kant insists that "perfection gains nothing by beauty or beauty by perfection" — the two kinds of satisfaction remain distinct even when combined in a single judgment.

### Disputes Between Judges

When two judges disagree about the beauty of an object, Kant's distinction can often resolve the dispute: "the one is speaking of free, the other of dependent, beauty — the first is making a pure, the second an applied, judgement of taste" (016-013). Both may be correct on their own terms.

### Relation to the Ideal of Beauty

Only adherent beauty — beauty fixed by a concept of objective purposiveness — can have an [[ideal-of-beauty|Ideal]]. Kant explicitly states: "the beauty for which an Ideal is to be sought cannot be *vague* beauty, but is *fixed* by a concept of objective purposiveness; and thus it cannot appertain to the Object of a quite pure judgement of taste" (017-006). The Ideal of beauty is attainable only in the human figure, the sole object whose purpose is determined by Reason.

## Role in the Critical System

Adherent beauty bridges the aesthetic and the practical domains. By combining the form-based pleasure of taste with the concept-based satisfaction of the Good, it prepares the transition from the Beautiful to the Morally Good — a central architectonic concern of the third *Critique*. Kant returns to this idea in §59, where he describes [[beauty]] as the "symbol of morality": the harmony of free and adherent beauty foreshadows the harmony of nature and freedom.

## Key Passages

> "There are two kinds of beauty; free beauty (pulchritudo vaga) or merely dependent beauty (pulchritudo adhaerens)... The second does presuppose such a concept and the perfection of the object in accordance therewith."
> (016-002)

> "Human beauty (i.e. of a man, a woman, or a child), the beauty of a horse, or a building (be it church, palace, arsenal, or summer-house) presupposes a concept of the purpose which determines what the thing is to be, and consequently a concept of its perfection; it is therefore adherent beauty."
> (016-007)

> "It is true that taste gains by this combination of aesthetical with intellectual satisfaction, inasmuch as it becomes fixed; and though it is not universal, yet in respect to certain purposively determined Objects it becomes possible to prescribe rules for it."
> (016-011)
