# Antinomy of Taste

## Definition

The antinomy of taste is the apparent contradiction that arises when the two principles of aesthetic judgment are stated as mutually exclusive claims: **thesis** — the judgment of taste is not based upon determinate concepts, for otherwise one could dispute about it by proofs; **antithesis** — the judgment of taste is based upon concepts, for otherwise nothing could be required of others in respect of it. (055-001) (057-007) Kant resolves the antinomy by distinguishing between *determinate* concepts of the Understanding (which can be demonstrated) and the *indeterminate* rational concept of the supersensible substrate of phenomena, showing that the thesis and antithesis use "concept" in different senses and therefore do not genuinely contradict each other. (057-005)

## Kant's Account

### Origin of the Antinomy

For the faculty of judgment to be dialectical, its judgments must claim universality a priori, for dialectic consists in the opposition of such judgments. (055-001) The judgment of taste makes precisely this claim: it demands universal agreement, yet it is grounded not in a determinate concept of the Understanding but in the subject's feeling of pleasure. This tension between the claim and its ground gives rise to the antinomy, which Kant presents as two maxims that "naturally and unavoidably" oppose one another. (055-001) The transcendental critique of taste therefore requires a dialectical part, "only if and so far as there is found an antinomy of the principles of this faculty which renders its conformity to law, and consequently also its internal possibility, doubtful." (055-001)

### The Two Conflicting Principles

Kant constructs the antinomy from the two peculiarities of the judgment of taste established in the Analytic. (057-001) The **thesis** maintains that the judgment of taste is not based on determinate concepts; if it were, one could compel agreement by proofs, and taste would be cognizable by rules — which would destroy its aesthetic character. (057-007) The **antithesis** maintains that the judgment of taste must be based on *some* concept, for otherwise it could make absolutely no claim to be necessarily valid for everyone. (057-002) Without a conceptual ground, the demand for universal assent would be mere "groundless and vain fancy." (057-025)

### Resolution Through the Supersensible

The solution turns on a single distinction: the concept underlying taste is not a determinate concept of the Understanding but the **indeterminate rational concept of the supersensible** which underlies all sensible intuition. (057-004) This concept is "the mere pure rational concept of the supersensible which underlies the object (and also the subject judging it), regarded as an Object of sense and thus as phenomenon." (057-004) From this concept nothing can be known or proved about the object — "because it is in itself undeterminable and useless for knowledge" — yet on that very account the judgment retains validity for everyone, "because its determining ground lies perhaps in the concept of that which may be regarded as the supersensible substrate of humanity." (057-005)

Kant summarizes the resolution: "In the Thesis we mean that the judgment of taste is not based upon *determinate* concepts; and in the Antithesis that the judgment of taste is based upon a concept, but an *indeterminate* one (viz. of the supersensible substrate of phenomena). Between these two there is no contradiction." (057-007)

### Rejection of Alternative Solutions

Kant identifies two ways to avoid the antinomy, both inadequate. One could deny any a priori principle underlying taste, reducing the claim to universal agreement to a contingent similarity of organization among subjects. (057-025) Or one could treat the judgment of taste as a disguised judgment of Reason about perfection — a teleological judgment confusedly apprehended. (057-025) The first path abandons the necessity that is the defining feature of aesthetic judgment; the second path conflates the beautiful with the good and would render the solution "by means of transcendental Ideas needless and without point." (057-025)

## Key Distinctions

| Concept | Determinate (Understanding) | Indeterminate (Reason) |
|---------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Nature** | Definable through sensible intuition | Cannot be theoretically determined |
| **Example** | Concept of *cause* — demonstrated through collision of bodies | Concept of the *supersensible* — no intuition can correspond |
| **Role in Taste** | Would make taste provable (reducing it to cognition) | Grounds the universal validity of taste without making it demonstrable |
| **Kant's Term** | Immanent concept of the Understanding | Transcendent rational Idea |

## Role in the Critical System

The antinomy of taste is the third of three antinomies that structure Kant's critical project. Each corresponds to a superior cognitive faculty: the antinomy of theoretical Reason (Understanding), the antinomy of practical Reason (desire), and the antinomy of aesthetic Judgment (feeling of pleasure and pain). (057-024) All three share a common structure: they "compel us to give up the otherwise very natural hypothesis that objects of sense are things in themselves, and force us to regard them merely as phenomena, and to supply to them an intelligible substrate." (057-022) Without such antinomies, Kant argues, Reason could never decide to accept a principle that narrows the field of its speculation so severely.

The resolution of the aesthetic antinomy follows the same path as the resolution of the theoretical and practical antinomies: force us "against our will to look beyond the sensible and to seek in the supersensible the point of union for all our a priori faculties; because no other expedient is left to make our Reason harmonious with itself." (057-011) This reveals the deep unity of the critical system: autonomy in cognition, willing, and judging is secured by the same move — the restriction of the sensible to phenomena and the positing of a supersensible ground.

Kant concludes by identifying three Ideas that emerge from the resolution: the Idea of the supersensible as substrate of nature; the Idea of the supersensible as principle of subjective purposiveness; and the Idea of the supersensible as principle of the purposes of freedom. (057-027)

## Key Passages

> "A faculty of Judgment that is to be dialectical must in the first place be rationalising, i.e. its judgments must claim universality and that a priori; for it is in the opposition of such judgments that Dialectic consists."
> (055-001)

> "The judgment of taste must refer to some concept; otherwise it could make absolutely no claim to be necessarily valid for every one. But it is not therefore capable of being proved *from* a concept; because a concept may be either determinable or in itself undetermined and undeterminable."
> (057-002)

> "In the Thesis we mean that the judgment of taste is not based upon *determinate* concepts; and in the Antithesis that the judgment of taste is based upon a concept, but an *indeterminate* one (viz. of the supersensible substrate of phenomena). Between these two there is no contradiction."
> (057-007)

> "Thus here, as also in the Critique of Practical Reason, the antinomies force us against our will to look beyond the sensible and to seek in the supersensible the point of union for all our a priori faculties; because no other expedient is left to make our Reason harmonious with itself."
> (057-011)
