# Internal Purposiveness

## Definition

Internal purposiveness (*innere Zweckmäßigkeit*) is the purposiveness that a thing possesses **in itself** — the arrangement by which its parts are reciprocally cause and effect of one another, organized for the sake of the whole. It stands in contrast to external purposiveness (*äußere Zweckmäßigkeit*), which is merely relative utility or fitness for something else. Kant introduces the distinction in the Critique of Aesthetic Judgment and develops it fully in the Critique of Teleological Judgment: "Objective internal purposiveness, i.e. perfection, comes nearer to the predicate of beauty." (015-004)

## Kant's Account

### In Aesthetic Judgment

In the aesthetic context, Kant distinguishes internal from external purposiveness to clarify what kind of perfection might be relevant to beauty. Internal purposiveness — the perfection of a thing according to what it is supposed to be — is an objective concept: "No perfection of any kind, no internal purposiveness, to which the collection of the manifold is referred, is the ground of the pure judgment of taste." (016-004) Pure beauty is grounded in subjective formal purposiveness, not in any concept of the thing's internal perfection.

### In Teleological Judgment: Organised Beings

The concept of internal purposiveness becomes central in the Critique of Teleological Judgment. Kant identifies natural organisms as the paradigm of internal purposiveness: in an organised being, "every part is thought as existing for the sake of the other and the whole." (066-003) The parts are reciprocally cause and effect — a tree produces itself both as individual (growth) and as species (reproduction), and its parts (leaves, roots) mutually sustain one another. This internal organization cannot be explained by mechanical causality alone and requires the regulative maxim of purposiveness.

### Contrast with External Purposiveness

Kant sharply distinguishes internal from external purposiveness: "Things which have no internal purposiveness and which presuppose none for their explanation" may still exhibit external purposiveness — the fitness of one thing as a means for another. (082-002) External purposiveness is always relative: sand is useful for pines, pines are useful for humans. Internal purposiveness, by contrast, is intrinsic to the organized being itself and is inseparable from its concept.

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Internal Purposiveness | External Purposiveness |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Locus** | In the thing itself | In the relation between things |
| **Example** | Organism (tree, animal) | Sand as soil for pines |
| **Type of Perfection** | Intrinsic perfection (what it is) | Relative utility (what it is for) |
| **Role in Beauty** | Closer to beauty but still objective | Irrelevant to pure taste |
| **Explanatory Mode** | Requires teleological judgment | Observable through mechanical relations |

## Role in the Critical System

Internal purposiveness bridges the two halves of the third *Critique*. In the aesthetic part, it helps define the boundary between subjective formal purposiveness (beauty) and objective perfection (which is a concept of the understanding). In the teleological part, it provides the regulative principle for judging organisms — beings whose internal organization demands that we think of them *as if* designed, even though we cannot prove a designer. This prepares the transition to moral teleology, where the human being as moral agent is the only being whose internal purposiveness can be known unconditionally.
