# Causality

## Definition

Causality (*Kausalität*) is, in Kant's system, the relation of cause and effect by which one thing (the cause) determines the existence or alteration of another (the effect). In the third *Critique*, Kant draws a fundamental distinction between two kinds of causal connection: **mechanical causality** (*nexus effectivus*), in which the effect follows from the cause in a linear temporal series, and **teleological causality** (*nexus finalis*), in which the idea of the whole determines the parts. "According to the analogy with the causality of purpose" (061-005) — this dual conception of causality is central to the project of bridging nature and freedom.

## Kant's Account

### Causality in the First Critique

In the first *Critique*, causality is one of the twelve categories of the Understanding — a pure concept that makes experience possible by connecting phenomena in necessary temporal succession. Every event in nature has a cause; this is a constitutive principle of all possible experience. The "causality of a concept in respect of its Object" (010-001) is the paradigmatic form of causality through freedom.

### Mechanical Causality (Nexus Effectivus)

Kant identifies mechanical causality with the connection of cause and effect in which "the laws of simply mechanical causality do not suffice" (061-006) to explain organized beings. In nexus effectivus, the causal series is a one-way street: cause → effect, with each link determined by the preceding one. This is the causality proper to lifeless matter: "in lifeless matter, the cause is not determined to causality by concepts" (P04-003). Mechanical causality governs the realm of nature as understood by the Understanding.

### Teleological Causality (Nexus Finalis)

In the third *Critique*, Kant introduces a second kind of causality specific to organized beings and purposive action. "We bring the aid of a particular kind of causality, namely that of purpose (*nexus finalis*)" (061-003) when mechanical explanation breaks down. In teleological causality, the idea of the whole "as the fundamental" determines the arrangement of parts — the effect (the organized whole) is logically prior to the cause (the arrangement of parts). This is not a constitutive claim about nature but a regulative maxim of judgment: we must think of organisms *as if* produced by a causality according to purposes.

### Causality Through Freedom

"The consciousness of the causality of a representation, for maintaining" (010-002) the subject in a state — this is the causality of the will, which is freedom. Kant distinguishes the "causality of mind" as a "vera causa" (P01-054) — a true cause — from the causality of nature. The free causality of rational beings is the bridge between the two realms: "Through the freedom of his causality, [man] finds certain natural things" (063-009) as means to his purposes. This is the central problem of the third *Critique*: how can free causality and natural causality coexist in the same world?

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Mechanical Causality | Teleological Causality |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Direction** | Cause → effect (linear) | Whole → parts (circular) |
| **Ground** | Preceding event in time | Idea of the whole |
| **Domain** | Lifeless matter | Organisms, art, free action |
| **Status** | Constitutive (for experience) | Regulative (for judgment) |
| **Kant's Term** | *Nexus effectivus* | *Nexus finalis* |

## Role in the Critical System

The dualism of causality — mechanical vs. teleological — is the engine of the third *Critique*'s systematic ambition. The first *Critique* established causality as a category of the Understanding, binding nature to deterministic laws. But "we then assume for its ground a causality according to purposes, i.e. a will" (010-004), which exceeds what the Understanding can legitimately assert. Reflective judgment, by adopting the maxim of teleological causality as a regulative idea, makes "the transition from the concept of nature to that of freedom" possible — the very transition the third *Critique* was written to accomplish. The "Idea of the effect of the causality of the cause" (063-002) serves as the organizing principle of teleological judgment.
