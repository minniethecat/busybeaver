# The Sublime

## Definition

The sublime (*das Erhabene*) is, alongside the beautiful, one of the two fundamental modes of aesthetic judgment in Kant's *Critique of Judgment*. Unlike the beautiful, which involves a restful contemplation of purposive form, the sublime involves a **mental movement** — a "negative pleasure" that begins with a momentary check of the vital forces and is followed by a stronger outflow. (P01-023, 023-005) "Nothing in nature is sublime; and the sublimity really resides in the mind of the judging person." (P01-022) The sublime reveals not a purposiveness of the object but a purposiveness of the subject — specifically, the superiority of our rational faculty over nature.

Kant divides the sublime into two kinds: the **Mathematically Sublime**, which concerns magnitude (*das Schlechthin-Große* — the absolutely great), and the **Dynamically Sublime**, which concerns power (*Macht*) that awakens fear without actual danger. (024-004)

## Kant's Account

### The Mathematically Sublime

The mathematically sublime arises when we encounter something "absolutely great" — great beyond all comparison. (024-003) The imagination strives to comprehend the object in a single intuition (*comprehensio aesthetica*) but fails because the object exceeds its capacity. This failure, however, reveals the power of reason, which demands precisely such an absolute totality. "The very capacity of conceiving the sublime indicates a mental faculty that surpasses every standard of sense." (P01-024) What appears as the inadequacy of imagination is, in truth, a revelation of the supersensible destination of the subject.

### The Dynamically Sublime

The dynamically sublime concerns objects of nature that exhibit overwhelming power — storms, volcanoes, cataracts — which would threaten us physically but which we contemplate from a position of safety. (023-006) In such contemplation, we discover within ourselves a faculty of resistance "of a quite different kind" — our moral vocation, which is not threatened by any natural force. The dynamically sublime thus awakens "the feeling that we have a pure and independent reason" (023-009), connecting aesthetic experience directly to the moral law.

### Contrast with the Beautiful

The satisfaction in the sublime is of a different character from that in the beautiful. "If the pleasure taken in beauty arises from a feeling of the purposiveness of the object in its relation to the subject, that in sublimity rather expresses a purposiveness of the subject in respect of the object." (P01-022) While the beautiful involves a harmonious free play of imagination and understanding, the sublime involves a conflict between imagination and reason — a "serious" rather than merely "playful" engagement of the faculties. (P01-029)

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | The Beautiful | The Sublime |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Object | Purposive form of the object | Formless, boundless, or overwhelming object |
| Pleasure | Positive, promotes life | Negative pleasure — check then overflow |
| Faculties | Imagination + Understanding in free play | Imagination + Reason in conflict |
| Location | Purposiveness in the object's relation to the subject | Purposiveness in the subject in relation to the object |
| Mode | Restful contemplation | Mental movement (*Bewegung*) |

The sublime "must not be sought in things of nature, but only in our Ideas" — it is nature's capacity to awaken the idea of the supersensible within us. (023-007) For this reason, Kant insists that the true sublime object is not nature but the **disposition of the mind** (*Gemütsstimmung*) that nature provokes.

## Role in the Critical System

The analysis of the sublime accomplishes what the analysis of the beautiful alone cannot: it connects aesthetic judgment directly to moral feeling. While the beautiful is a "symbol of the morally good" (§59), the sublime actually produces a feeling akin to moral respect — "a feeling that we have a supersensible faculty" (023-009). This makes the sublime essential to Kant's argument that aesthetic judgment bridges the theoretical and practical domains. Schopenhauer regarded Kant's analysis of the sublime as "the best part of the Critique of the Aesthetical Judgement." (P01-022)

## Key Passages

> "Nothing in nature is sublime; and the sublimity really resides in the mind of the judging person who contemplates the object — not in the natural Object the contemplation of which occasions this mental disposition."
> (P01-022)

> "The very capacity of conceiving the sublime indicates a mental faculty that surpasses every standard of sense."
> (P01-024)

> "We express ourselves incorrectly if we call any object of nature sublime... we can quite correctly call many objects of nature beautiful; but we cannot with propriety call a single one sublime."
> (023-007)
