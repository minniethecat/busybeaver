# Final Cause

## Definition

A final cause (*nexus finalis*, *Endursache*) is a **causal connection conceived in reverse** — where the effect (the purpose or end) is treated as the ground that makes the cause possible. In Kant's formulation, "the causal connexion we call that of final causes (nexus finalis)" (065-003) is distinguished from the mechanical nexus of efficient causes (*nexus effectivus*).

Unlike efficient causality, where an antecedent event necessitates a consequent, final causality explains a thing by reference to the **end or purpose** it serves — the effect is logically prior to its own cause. This reverse-causality is the principle by which we judge organisms as natural purposes.

## Kant's Account

### The Distinction from Efficient Causality

Kant opens the *Critique of Teleological Judgment* by distinguishing two fundamentally different ways of thinking causal connection. Efficient causality proceeds forward in time: A causes B. Final causality proceeds backward in thought: the idea of B explains why A exists.

"We may perhaps represent a causal connexion according to a rational concept (of purposes) but we cannot comprehend it" (065-003) — final causes are a principle of *judgment*, not of *constitution*. We can think final causality, but we cannot prove it to be a real mechanism of nature.

### Final Causes and Organisms

The concept of final cause becomes necessary when we encounter beings whose parts appear to exist for the sake of the whole. In an organism, "an effect through final causes" (065-006) is the only way to make sense of the reciprocal dependence of parts and whole.

Each part is both cause and effect of every other part — a relation that mechanical causality cannot capture. Kant insists we must judge such beings "in accordance with the concept of final causes" (072-003), even if we do not claim to know that they *really are* produced by final causes.

### The Regulative Status of Final Causes

Crucially, Kant denies that final causes are **constitutive** principles of nature. They are **regulative** — maxims for the conduct of our reflection, not determinations of what nature is in itself.

"The principle of final causes, which is quite a different principle from that of mechanism" (070-010), guides the reflective judgment in its search for systematic unity. We proceed *as if* nature were designed by an intelligence, but we do not assert this as knowledge.

This regulative status is what distinguishes Kant from both dogmatic teleology (which asserts real final causes in nature) and dogmatic mechanism (which denies the *necessity* of thinking final causes at all).

### Going Beyond Nature

The concept of final causes pushes us to the boundary of the natural. "By means of this concept of final causes, we go beyond nature, and connect it with the highest ground in the series of purposes" (072-003) — final causality points toward a supersensible ground that would unify mechanism and teleology.

Yet this "going beyond" is only for the sake of judgment, not theoretical cognition. "The faculty of nature according to final causes must be taken for a particular kind of causality" (072-006) — it is a *maxim* of reflective judgment, not a category of the understanding.

### The Idealism of Final Causes

Kant considers and refines what he calls "the Idealism of final causes" (073-003). Those who deny that final causes are objective features of nature are correct insofar as final causes cannot be proven constitutive. But they err if they deny that we *must* think according to final causes when we encounter organized beings.

"Connected together by means of final causes, can decide nothing either by the one or the other principle alone" (074-004) — mechanism and teleology must be held together as complementary principles of judgment.

## Key Distinctions

| Causality | Direction | Status | Domain |
|-----------|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Efficient Cause** | Antecedent → Consequent | Constitutive (real) | Mechanism of nature |
| **Final Cause** | End → Means (reverse) | Regulative (maxim) | Organisms, purposes |
| **Purposiveness** | Form → Cognitive pleasure | Subjective (aesthetic) | Judgment of taste |

## Role in the Critical System

The concept of final cause is the **organizing principle** of the second half of the third *Critique*. Where the first *Critique* established efficient causality as a category of the understanding, and the second *Critique* established freedom as a kind of causality through reason, the third *Critique* introduces final causality as the bridge between the two.

Without final causes, we could think nature only mechanistically — and organisms would remain incomprehensible. With final causes understood as regulative, we gain a maxim for reflecting on nature without transgressing the limits of theoretical cognition.

The principle of final causes "has its ground in the supersensible" (077-011), uniting the mechanical and the teleological in an idea of reason that neither contradicts mechanism nor claims to supersede it.
