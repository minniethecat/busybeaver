## Definition

Reason (*Vernunft*) is, in Kant's critical system, the highest of the three higher cognitive faculties — the faculty of principles (*Vermögen der Prinzipien*) and of Ideas. Unlike Understanding, which supplies concepts for the cognition of appearances, Reason supplies Ideas (the soul, the world-whole, God) that transcend all possible experience and serve a regulative function, guiding Understanding toward systematic unity. In its practical employment, Reason legislates the moral law *a priori*, determining the will independently of all sensible inclination (P01-001; P03-003).

## Kant's Account

Kant's account of Reason in the Third Critique is presented largely through its relationship to Understanding and Judgment. In the Introduction, he writes that "to Reason, which contains constitutive principles *a priori* only in respect of the faculty of desire, is given its special realm in that Cognitive Faculty which we call Practical Reason" (P03-003). This formulation captures the asymmetry of Reason's legislative authority: in the theoretical domain, its Ideas are merely regulative; only in the practical domain does Reason legislate constitutively.

The Critique of Judgment is, in part, a response to the problem posed by the first two Critiques: the domains of theoretical Reason (nature) and practical Reason (freedom) stand separated by a gulf. The Third Critique argues that Judgment, through its principle of purposiveness, mediates between Understanding and Reason, rendering coherent the idea that nature can serve as the theater for moral action (P04-026).

In the Critique of Aesthetic Judgment, Reason makes a distinctive appearance in the experience of the sublime. The mathematical sublime confronts the imagination with magnitudes that exceed its capacity for aesthetic comprehension; this very failure prompts Reason to demand a totality that imagination cannot supply, revealing Reason's superiority over sensibility (P03-004). The dynamical sublime reveals Reason's practical dimension: in the face of nature's overwhelming might, we discover within ourselves a faculty of moral resistance grounded in Reason's legislation.

In the Critique of Teleological Judgment, Reason's Ideas — particularly the Idea of God — reappear in connection with physico-theology and the argument from design. Kant argues that physico-theology, while it can lead us to assume an intelligent author of nature, "can never justify us in asserting the existence of a Supreme Being to the speculative Reason. For all we know it may only be a subjective mode of our reflective Judgment" (P01-048). The true ground of theology is not theoretical but practical — the moral proof of God's existence rests on Reason's practical need to think the highest good as realisable.

## Key Distinctions

Reason must be distinguished from Understanding (*Verstand*), which supplies concepts for cognition, and from Judgment (*Urteilskraft*), which subsumes particulars under universals. Theoretical Reason produces Ideas (the soul, world-whole, God) that are regulative; Practical Reason produces the moral law, which is constitutive for the will. In the aesthetic context, Reason must be sharply separated from taste: aesthetic judgments are not rational judgments, and beauty pleases without a concept. Yet the beautiful is the "symbol of the morally good" precisely because it points beyond itself toward the supersensible that Reason thinks.

## Role in the Critical System

Reason's role in the Third Critique is to complete the critical architecture. If Understanding governs nature and Judgment bridges nature to freedom, Reason governs freedom and provides the final purpose (*Endzweck*) — the moral vocation of humanity — toward which the whole system of nature can be regarded as tending. This teleological perspective, grounded in reflective Judgment rather than constitutive Reason, is Kant's solution to the problem of the unity of theoretical and practical philosophy.

> "So also to Reason, which contains constitutive principles *a priori* only in respect of the faculty of desire, is given its special realm in that Cognitive Faculty which we call Practical Reason."
> (P03-003)

> "It was then properly the Understanding which has its special realm in the Cognitive Faculty, so far as it contains constitutive principles of cognition *a priori*."
> (P03-002)

> (P01-048)
