# Deduction of Taste

## Definition

The deduction of taste (*Deduktion der Geschmacksurteile*) is Kant's transcendental argument in §§30–40 of the *Critique of Judgment* that legitimates the claim of pure judgments of taste to **universal subjective validity**. Unlike the deduction of the categories in the first *Critique*, which legitimates the objective validity of pure concepts of the understanding, the deduction of taste must show how a judgment grounded in subjective feeling can nevertheless demand the agreement of all judging subjects. Kant states: "We only have to seek for the deduction of judgements of Taste, i.e. of judgements about the beauty of things of nature." (030-006)

## Kant's Account

### The Singularity of Aesthetic Judgments

The deduction of taste faces a distinctive challenge: aesthetic judgments are **singular** (*einzeln*). Kant notes that "all judgements of taste are singular judgements, because they do not combine their predicate of satisfaction with a concept, but with a given individual empirical representation." (037-002) One does not infer "all roses are beautiful" from samples; one judges "this rose is beautiful" and claims that others ought to agree. The deduction must therefore legitimate "the universal validity of a singular judgement that expresses the subjective purposiveness of an empirical representation of the form of an object." (031-004)

### The Ground of the Deduction

The deduction is grounded in the **subjective conditions of judgment** shared by all finite rational beings. Kant argues that the claim to universal validity is justified because the pleasure in the beautiful arises from the free play of the cognitive faculties — imagination and understanding — which all subjects possess in the same fundamental constitution. "The subjective condition of all judgements is the faculty of Judgement itself." (035-004) The deduction is therefore "easy," Kant claims, because "it needs no justification of the objective reality of any concept." (031-006) It only needs to show that the same subjective conditions are present in all judging subjects.

### Scope and Limits

The deduction applies only to **pure** judgments of taste — those grounded solely in the form of the object, uncontaminated by charm, emotion, or concepts of perfection. Judgments of the agreeable require no deduction (they make no claim to universal validity), while judgments of the good rest on concepts and are deduced through practical reason. The deduction also applies only to judgments of natural beauty, not to judgments of art, which involve concepts of what the object ought to be.

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Deduction of Taste | Deduction of Categories (1st Critique) |
|--------|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **What is legitimated** | Subjective universal validity of feeling | Objective validity of concepts |
| **Ground** | Shared cognitive faculties | Transcendental unity of apperception |
| **Universality type** | Exemplary (subjective) | Apodictic (objective) |
| **Role of concepts** | No determinate concept | Pure concepts of understanding |
| **Difficulty (per Kant)** | "Easy" — no objective reality to prove | Requires full transcendental deduction |

## Role in the Critical System

The deduction of taste completes the Analytic of the Beautiful by providing the **legitimation** that the four Moments (quality, quantity, relation, modality) have analytically uncovered. The four Moments expose the logical structure of the judgment of taste; the deduction justifies the claim to universal validity that this structure entails. Without the deduction, the analysis of taste would remain merely descriptive — it would describe what we do when we judge aesthetically, without showing that we are entitled to do so.
