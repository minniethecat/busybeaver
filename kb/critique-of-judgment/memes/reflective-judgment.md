# Reflective Judgment

## Definition

Reflective judgment (*reflektierende Urteilskraft*) is the mode of judgment that "ascends from the particular in nature to the universal" — it seeks a universal principle for given particulars, rather than subsuming particulars under a given universal. This distinguishes it from **determinant judgment**, which applies ready-made concepts or laws to particulars. (Introduction IV) Reflective judgment is the central faculty of Kant's *Critique of Judgment* and the subject of the principle of purposiveness.

The Editor notes that "through subjective condition of reflective judgements, viz., the purposive harmony of nature with our cognitive faculties, we have a principle which makes possible the transition from the theoretical to the practical." (P04-066) Reflective judgment is the faculty that bridges the gap between nature and freedom.

## Kant's Account

### Why Reflective Judgment Needs a Principle

Determinant judgment does not need its own principle — it borrows its universal (the concept or law) from the understanding. But reflective judgment must *find* a universal for what is given. Without its own a priori principle, it would be arbitrary. Kant argues that this principle is **purposiveness** — the assumption that nature's empirical laws form a systematic unity comprehensible by our cognitive faculties.

### Reflective Judgment in Aesthetics and Teleology

In **aesthetic** reflective judgment, the particular is a representation that occasions a feeling of pleasure or displeasure; the universal sought is the ground of this feeling — which turns out to be the free play of the cognitive faculties. In **teleological** reflective judgment, the particular is an organism; the universal sought is the concept of a natural purpose. Both are modes of reflective judgment, differing in whether the purposiveness judged is subjective (aesthetic) or objective (teleological).

### The Supersensible Destination

Reflective judgment reveals a fundamental fact about the human mind: we cannot be satisfied with mechanical explanation alone. We are driven to seek purposes, to find unity in diversity, to connect the sensible to the supersensible. This drive is not an error — it is the legitimate operation of reflective judgment, and it points toward the supersensible destination of the human being. "We cannot otherwise conceive the purposiveness which must lie at the basis of our cognition." (P01-047)
