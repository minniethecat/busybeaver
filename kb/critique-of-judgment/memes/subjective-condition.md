# Subjective Condition

## Definition

A subjective condition (*subjektive Bedingung*) is a necessary ground of judgment or cognition that resides **in the subject** rather than in the object judged. It does not determine what the object is (which would be an objective condition) but prescribes how the subject must be constituted for a certain kind of experience to arise.

In the *Critique of Judgment*, Kant makes the subjective condition central: "The representation which, as the subjective condition of the judgement of taste, must be presupposed as universally communicable, is the mental state of the free play of imagination and understanding." (009-004)

## Kant's Account

### In Aesthetic Judgment

The subjective condition is the cornerstone of Kant's theory of taste. A pure judgment of taste rests on "the subjective condition of the judgement of taste" — the harmonious free play of the cognitive faculties. (009-004)

This condition is "subjective" not in the sense of being merely personal, but in being **universally attributable** to all judging subjects. "The subjective condition of all judgements is the faculty of Judgement itself." (035-004) The paradox of taste is resolved by the claim that all subjects share the same cognitive architecture.

### In Cognition Generally

Kant extends the notion of subjective conditions beyond aesthetics. Even determinate cognition depends on subjective conditions: "Without this as the subjective condition of cognition, knowledge as an effect would not arise." (021-003)

The imagination and understanding must stand in a certain relation for cognition to occur — a relation that is a subjective condition, not an objective property. "They are subjective conditions of the possibility of a cognition in general." (039-005)

### In Teleological and Moral Contexts

The concept extends into teleology and morality. Kant asks whether final causes might be "only a subjective condition of the use of our Reason." (082-016)

In the moral sphere, he identifies "the subjective condition under which man can set a final purpose." (087-006) This bridges the theoretical and practical uses of subjective conditions.

## Key Distinctions

| Dimension | Subjective Condition | Objective Condition |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Locus** | In the subject | In the object |
| **Universality** | Universal for subjects | Universal for objects |
| **Example** | Free play of imagination and understanding | Causal connection between events |
| **Kant's Domain** | Aesthetic judgment and regulative maxims | Theoretical cognition |

## Role in the Critical System

The subjective condition is Kant's key to explaining how judgments that make no objective claim can nevertheless demand universal assent. It allows him to navigate between empiricist reduction and rationalist overreach.

In the *Critique of Judgment*, the subjective condition of judgment — "the Judgement itself, which as a subjective condition must be presupposed in all men" (038-002) — becomes the principle that unifies the aesthetic and teleological parts of the third *Critique*.
