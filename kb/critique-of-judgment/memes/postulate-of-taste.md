# Postulate of Taste

## Definition

The postulate of taste is the claim that a pure judgment of taste speaks with a **universal voice** (*allgemeine Stimme*) — that it is entitled to demand the assent of everyone, even though it is grounded in subjective feeling rather than in a determinate concept. Kant writes: "The judgement of taste itself does not *postulate* the agreement of everyone (for that can only be done by a logically universal judgement, because it can adduce reasons); it only *imputes* this agreement to everyone." (008-012) The postulate is not a logical deduction from premises but a presupposition inherent in the act of judging aesthetically.

## Kant's Account

### The Universal Voice

In the deduction of the pure judgment of taste, Kant argues that when we judge an object beautiful, we "believe that we speak with a universal voice, and we claim the assent of everyone." (008-011) This claim is not empirical — we do not first check whether others agree — but is a normative demand built into the structure of aesthetic judgment itself. "Nothing is postulated but such a universal voice, in respect of the satisfaction without the mediation of concepts." (008-012)

### Relation to Sensus Communis

The postulate of taste finds its ground in the idea of a **common sense** (*sensus communis*). Kant defines this as "a faculty for judging that in its reflection takes account (a priori) of the mode of representation of all other men." (040-001) Under the *sensus communis* "we must include the Idea of a common sense, i.e. a faculty of judgement." (040-002) The postulate is not an empirical generalization about what people happen to like, but a transcendental presupposition of the possibility of universally communicable aesthetic judgments.

### Postulation in the Moral Parallel

Kant draws a structural parallel with practical reason: just as the moral law is a "fact of reason" that postulates freedom, the judgment of taste postulates a *sensus communis*. "The principle of all moral laws is a postulate, so is also the possibility of the highest good in the world." (091-010) This parallel situates the postulate of taste within Kant's broader architectonic of reason — aesthetic judgment, like moral judgment, involves a necessary presupposition that cannot be proved theoretically but is demanded practically.

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Postulate of Taste | Logical Postulate |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Ground** | Subjective feeling | Objective reasons |
| **Universality** | Exemplary (imputed) | Apodictic (deduced) |
| **Proof** | None possible | Demonstrable from premises |
| **Necessity** | Subjective necessity | Objective necessity |
| **Domain** | Aesthetic reflective judgment | Theoretical determinant judgment |

## Role in the Critical System

The postulate of taste is the linchpin of Kant's resolution of the antinomy of taste. The antinomy arises because taste seems to be both merely subjective (not provable by reasons) and universally valid (demanding agreement). The postulate resolves this by showing that the universality of taste is not a logical universality grounded in concepts, but an **exemplary necessity** grounded in the presupposition of a common sense. Taste "can be called *sensus communis* with more justice than sound Understanding." (040-007)
