## Definition

Moral teleology (*moralische Teleologie*) is Kant's argument that nature's purposiveness, combined with the moral law, justifies a practical belief in a wise and moral author of the world.

Unlike physico-theology (the design argument), which can at best establish an intelligent architect, moral teleology grounds the concept of God as a moral lawgiver whose purposes align with the highest good.

## Kant's Account

Kant develops moral teleology in the final sections of the *Critique of Teleological Judgment* (§§86–91). The argument proceeds in three stages.

**First**, physico-theology is shown to be insufficient. Even if nature exhibits design, physico-theology cannot establish the moral attributes of the designer, nor demonstrate that nature's purposes are final. "Physical teleology impels us indeed to seek a theology, but it cannot produce one." (§85)

**Second**, the only candidate for a final purpose of nature is humanity as a moral being. Every other natural purpose is conditional—useful for something else—whereas the moral law commands unconditionally. "Without man... the whole creation would be a mere waste, in vain, and without final purpose." (§86)

**Third**, combining physical teleology with moral teleology yields the moral proof. We have practical grounds—sufficient for moral action—to believe that nature's purposiveness and morality converge in a divine author.

## Key Distinctions

Kant carefully distinguishes moral teleology from related concepts:

| Concept | Basis | Conclusion | Kant's Assessment |
|---------|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Physico-theology** | Natural order and adaptation | An intelligent architect | Insufficient |
| **Ontological argument** | Concept of a perfect being | God's necessary existence | Invalid |
| **Cosmological argument** | Contingency of the world | A necessary first cause | Depends on ontological |
| **Moral teleology** | Moral law + natural purposiveness | Practical grounds for belief in God | Only viable path |

Only moral teleology, by identifying the final purpose of creation in moral humanity, provides what physico-theology lacks: a determinate concept of the supreme cause as a moral being.

## Role in the Critical System

Moral teleology resolves a tension that runs through Kant's entire critical philosophy.

The first *Critique* showed that theoretical reason cannot prove God's existence. The second *Critique* showed that practical reason requires us to postulate God as the guarantor of the highest good. The third *Critique* bridges these results: nature, viewed through reflective judgment, can be seen as amenable to moral purpose.

The argument demonstrates the primacy of practical reason. As Kant puts it, the moral proof "stands by itself, and is completed without any external help; it cannot be strengthened by any theoretical proof, nor can it be weakened by any theoretical objection." (P01-064)
