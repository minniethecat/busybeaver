Necessity (*Notwendigkeit*) is the **Fourth Moment** of the judgment of taste in Kant's "Analytic of the Beautiful" (§18–22). It defines the modality of aesthetic judgment: when we judge an object beautiful, we hold that everyone *ought* to agree with our judgment. This "ought" is neither a logical necessity derivable from concepts, nor a practical necessity of moral obligation; it is an *exemplary* necessity — the necessity of a universal rule that cannot be stated.

## Exemplary Necessity

Kant characterizes the necessity of taste as *exemplary*:

> [020-002] … would claim an unconditioned necessity for his judgement.

The judgment of taste does not state that everyone *will* agree (that would be an empirical prediction), nor that everyone *must* agree on the basis of a proof. It states that everyone *ought* to agree, as if the beautiful object were an example of a universal rule that we cannot articulate:

> [022-001] The judgment of taste is a subjective necessity, which is represented as objective under the presupposition of a common sense.

## The Common Sense as Ground

The necessity of taste is grounded in the idea of a **common sense** (*[[Sensus Communis]]*). This common sense is not an empirical faculty — it is not the "common understanding" that most people share. It is a normative ideal: the idea of a faculty that judges by feeling rather than by concepts, yet with universal validity:

> [022-003] … the "ought," i.e. the objective necessity of the confluence of the feeling of every one with that of each, presupposes a common sense.

Without the presupposition of a common sense, the claim to necessity would be groundless. But Kant insists that the common sense is not proved — it is only *presupposed* as a condition of the possibility of judgments of taste.

## Necessity vs. Logical Necessity

| Type | Basis | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| Logical necessity | Concepts, demonstration | "All bachelors are unmarried" |
| Practical necessity | Moral law, categorical imperative | "You ought not to lie" |
| Exemplary necessity (taste) | Common sense, free play of faculties | "This rose is beautiful" |

The exemplary necessity of taste is *conditional*: it holds only if we presuppose a common sense. But this conditionality does not make it arbitrary — Kant holds that the common sense is a necessary presupposition of the very possibility of communicating aesthetic judgments.

## Necessity in the Four Moments

The four moments of the judgment of taste follow the table of logical functions from the *Critique of Pure Reason*:

1. **Quality** — [[Disinterestedness]] (the judgment is without interest)
2. **Quantity** — [[Universality]] (the judgment is subjectively universal)
3. **Relation** — [[Purposiveness]] (the judgment rests on purposiveness without purpose)
4. **Modality** — Necessity (the judgment claims exemplary necessity)

The fourth moment completes the argument: the judgment of taste, though subjective, claims necessity — a necessity that distinguishes it from mere judgments of the agreeable.

## Necessity and the Sublime

In the Analytic of the Sublime, Kant revisits necessity. The judgment of the sublime also claims necessity, but this necessity is more robust because it is grounded in the moral predisposition of human reason:

> [029-010] Hereon is based the necessity of that agreement of the judgment of the sublime with the judgment of taste.

The sublime's necessity thus anticipates the transition from aesthetics to morality that occupies the latter parts of the [[Critique of Judgment|第三批判]].
