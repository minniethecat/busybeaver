# Dialectic of Aesthetic Judgment

## Definition

The Dialectic of Aesthetic Judgment is Kant's treatment of the **antinomy of taste** — the apparent contradiction between two seemingly irreconcilable principles governing judgments of the beautiful. The thesis holds: "The judgment of taste is not based on concepts; for otherwise it would admit of dispute." The antithesis holds: "The judgment of taste is based on concepts; for otherwise we could not quarrel about it." Kant resolves the antinomy by distinguishing **determinate concepts** (which the Understanding uses for cognition) from **indeterminate concepts** (the supersensible substrate of appearances) — taste judgments are based on the latter, not the former.

## Kant's Account

### The Antinomy Exposed

Kant opens the Dialectic by insisting that a "faculty of Judgment that is to be dialectical must in the first place be rationalistic" (055-001) — that is, its principles must lay claim to universal validity. The antinomy arises precisely because taste demands universal assent yet cannot be proven by determinate concepts: "Then there arises an antinomy which it is absolutely impossible" (057-010) to resolve in the realm of appearances alone. The conflict is between taste as purely subjective feeling and taste as making a claim that ought to hold for everyone.

### The Two Principles in Conflict

**Thesis**: The judgment of taste is not based on concepts, for otherwise we could *dispute* about it (decide by proofs).  
**Antithesis**: The judgment of taste is based on concepts, for otherwise we could not even *quarrel* about it (claim universal validity).  

Both are true, but they appear contradictory. "The removal of the antinomy of the aesthetical Judgment" (057-011) requires distinguishing two senses of "concept."

### Resolution Through the Supersensible

Kant's solution: the thesis is true of **determinate concepts** (concepts of the Understanding, by which objects are cognized). The antithesis is true of an **indeterminate concept** — "the supersensible substrate of phenomena" (057-009). This indeterminate concept is not available for cognition but serves as the ground of the universal communicability of aesthetic pleasure. The antinomy "only depends on the possibility" (057-006) of showing that both principles are compatible. By localizing the ground of taste in the supersensible, Kant preserves both the subjective (non-cognitive) character of aesthetic judgment and its claim to universal validity.

### Dialectic vs. Antinomy of Reason

Kant is careful to distinguish the dialectic of judgment from the dialectic of Reason (in the first *Critique*). "For the cognitive faculty an antinomy of Reason" (057-024) concerns the necessary illusions of speculative metaphysics. The antinomy of taste, by contrast, is not a necessary illusion but a resolvable tension internal to the faculty of judgment itself. Judging nature teleologically "is exposed to no danger of an antinomy of its own" (069-001) — the antinomy belongs properly to aesthetic judgment.

## Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Antinomy of Taste | Antinomy of Reason |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Faculty** | Judgment (aesthetic) | Reason (speculative) |
| **Domain** | Beauty / feeling | Cosmological ideas |
| **Resolution** | Distinction of determinate vs. indeterminate concept | Transcendental idealism |
| **Result** | Universal communicability preserved | Limits of Reason established |

## Role in the Critical System

The Dialectic of Aesthetic Judgment completes the Analytic of the Beautiful by showing that taste's claim to universality is not a logical contradiction. It secures the transition from the subjective "I feel pleasure" to the intersubjective "this ought to please everyone." Without this resolution, the entire edifice of the third *Critique* — taste as a sensus communis, beauty as symbol of morality, judgment as bridge between nature and freedom — would collapse into private feeling. The supersensible substrate invoked in the resolution is the same ground that ultimately unifies theoretical and practical Reason.
