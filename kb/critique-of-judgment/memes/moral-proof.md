The Moral Proof (*moralischer Beweis*) is Kant's argument for the existence of God presented in the "Methodology of the Teleological Judgment" (§87–91) of the [[Critique of Judgment|第三批判]]. It differs from both the physico-theological proof (argument from design) and the ontological proof, which Kant had already critiqued in the *Critique of Pure Reason*. The moral proof proceeds not from theoretical cognition of nature but from the demands of practical reason.

## The Argument

Kant argues that the moral law — the categorical imperative — imposes on us the duty to pursue the highest good (*summum bonum*), the union of virtue and happiness. But the pursuit of this end presupposes its possibility. Since we cannot bring about this union through our own causal powers, we must postulate a moral author of the world — God — who can:

> [088-017] This moral proof is not one newly discovered, but is only one newly developed; for it lay in the human rational faculty before its earliest germination.

Kant insists the proof is not new — it is a rationalization of what practical reason has always already presupposed.

## Conditions of the Proof

The moral proof rests on three conditions:

1. **The moral law** — given as a fact of reason (*Faktum der Vernunft*)
2. **The highest good** — the necessary object of a will determined by the moral law
3. **The postulate of God** — as the ground of the possibility of the highest good

> [091-035] … makes it more susceptible of the moral proof. For what is requisite to the concept of the highest good is the agreement of the moral law with the happiness of rational beings, and this agreement can only be thought through an intelligent author of nature.

## Contrast with Other Proofs

Kant distinguishes the moral proof from two traditional theistic arguments:

| Proof Type | Basis | Kant's Critique |
|-----------|-------|----------------|
| Ontological | Concept of a most perfect being | Confuses logical and real existence |
| Physico-theological | Design and order in nature | At best proves a world-architect, not a creator |
| Moral proof | Practical reason's demand | Does not prove theoretically; it is a postulate of pure practical reason |

> [P01-064] … effort to combine them. The moral proof stands by itself, and it needs the physico-theological proof only as a confirmation.

The moral proof is *self-standing* — it does not depend on empirical evidence of design in nature. Nature, however, can *confirm* the moral proof when we reflect on the purposiveness we find there.

## Limits of the Moral Proof

Kant is careful to circumscribe what the moral proof achieves. It does not:

- Provide theoretical cognition of God's existence
- Determine God's nature beyond what is necessary for the highest good
- Replace faith with knowledge

> [091-036] … required as the basis of the moral proof; nor does this latter serve to prove that the physico-theological proof is valid.

The moral proof yields *practical* certainty — sufficient for moral life — not theoretical certainty. This is consistent with Kant's broader project of limiting knowledge to make room for faith.

## The Moral Proof in the Critical System

The moral proof occupies a unique position in Kant's critical architectonic. The *Critique of Pure Reason* had ruled out theoretical proofs of God. The *Critique of Practical Reason* had introduced the postulate of God as a condition of the highest good. The *Critique of Judgment* completes the picture by:

1. Showing that **teleological judgment** leads us to the concept of a final end of nature — the human being as moral subject
2. Transitioning from this final end to the **moral proof** of a supreme cause
3. Unifying the theoretical and practical uses of reason through the concept of purposiveness
