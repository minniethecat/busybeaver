## Definition

Schematism (*Schematismus*) is, in Kant's critical philosophy, the procedure of the power of judgment by which a concept is provided with its corresponding intuition. In the *Critique of Judgment*, schematism operates in two registers: an objective schematism, where the understanding supplies an *a priori* intuition to a concept (as treated in the first *Critique*), and a subjective analogue in aesthetic judgment, where the imagination and understanding enter a free, harmonious play without a determinate concept (009-012).

## Kant's Account

In §9 of the *Critique of Judgment*, Kant contrasts the objective and subjective modes of schematism to clarify the nature of the judgment of taste. If the representation occasioning the judgment of taste were "a concept uniting Understanding and Imagination in the judging of the object, into a cognition of the Object," then the consciousness of this relation would be intellectual — "as in the objective schematism of the Judgement of which the Critique treats" (009-012). But this would yield a cognitive, not an aesthetic, judgment.

The judgment of taste, by contrast, does not proceed through the subsumption of an intuition under a determinate concept. Instead, the imagination and understanding enter into "indeterminate, but yet, through the stimulus of the given sensation, harmonious activity" (009-012). This free play, felt through the sensation of the furtherance of the feeling of life, is the subjective counterpart of schematism — a relation of faculties whose universal communicability grounds the judgment of taste.

Kant returns to schematism in the context of the dynamical sublime, where the imagination, "by the principles of the Schematism of the Judgement (being so far, therefore, ranked under freedom)," becomes "the instrument of Reason and its Ideas," enabling the mind to maintain independence from natural influences. In §59, Kant distinguishes two modes of *hypotyposis* (sensible illustration): the *schematical* and the *symbolical* (059-002). Schematical presentation supplies an intuition *a priori* to a concept of the understanding; symbolical presentation supplies an intuition to a concept of reason, through a procedure of judgment "analogous to what it observes in schematism" — that is, following the same rule of procedure but applied to a different domain (059-002).

## Key Distinctions

Schematism as a procedure of judgment must be distinguished from the *schema* itself (the mediating representation) and from *hypotyposis* (sensible presentation in general, which may be schematical or symbolical). The objective schematism treated in the *Critique of Pure Reason* concerns the schemata of the categories (e.g., the schema of magnitude as number); the subjective schematism of the third *Critique* concerns the free, harmonious relation of imagination and understanding without determinate concepts. Symbolic hypotyposis, by contrast, uses analogy rather than direct intuition.

## Role in the Critical System

Schematism is the central operation of judgment as a faculty. In the theoretical domain, it enables the application of pure concepts of the understanding to sensible intuition. In the aesthetic domain, its subjective counterpart explains how a merely subjective relation of faculties can nonetheless claim universal validity — the free schematism of imagination and understanding is the cognitive condition of judgment in general, and therefore can be presupposed in every subject. The transition to symbolical hypotyposis in §59 further links aesthetic judgment to morality, as the beautiful becomes the symbol of the morally good.

## Key Passages

> "If the given representation, which occasions the judgement of taste, were a concept uniting Understanding and Imagination in the judging of the object, into a cognition of the Object, the consciousness of this relation would be intellectual (as in the objective schematism of the Judgement of which the Critique treats). But then the judgement would not be laid down in reference to pleasure and pain, and consequently would not be a judgement of taste."
> (009-012)

> "All hypotyposis (presentation, *subjectio sub adspectum*), or sensible illustration, is twofold. It is either *schematical*, when to a concept comprehended by the Understanding the corresponding intuition is given *a priori*; or it is *symbolical*. In the latter case to a concept only thinkable by the Reason, to which no sensible intuition can be adequate, an intuition is supplied with which accords a procedure of the Judgement analogous to what it observes in schematism."
> (059-002)
