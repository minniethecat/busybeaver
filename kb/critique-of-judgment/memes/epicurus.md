# Epicurus

## Biography

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher and the founder of Epicureanism. His physics, based on the atomism of Democritus, held that the universe consists of atoms moving through void, combining and separating by chance without purpose or design. His ethics identified pleasure (*hedone*) as the highest good — though Epicurean pleasure was understood as tranquility (*ataraxia*) rather than sensual indulgence.

## Relation to Kant

In the *Critique of Judgment*, Epicurean atomism serves as Kant's paradigm of a system that denies purposiveness altogether. "A doctrine, like that of Epicurus, in which every natural phenomenon is regarded as the result of the blind drifting of atoms in accordance with purely mechanical laws, really explains nothing, and least of all explains that illusion in our teleological judgements which leads us to assume purpose in nature." (P01-039)

Kant does not merely dismiss Epicurus — he uses Epicurean materialism to clarify what is at stake in the concept of purposiveness. The fact that Epicureanism "explains nothing" about the appearance of purpose reveals that mechanism alone cannot account for organisms. The distinction between Epicurean mechanism and Kantian teleology is fundamental: Epicurus *denies* the reality of purpose; Kant *regulates* its use as a maxim of reflective judgment.

## Key Ideas Referenced

- **Atomism**: All natural phenomena arise from atoms moving in void — there are no purposes, only mechanical collisions.
- **Anti-teleology**: Epicurus explicitly rejected final causes, anticipating modern scientific mechanism.
- **The Swerve (*clinamen*)**: Atoms deviate randomly from their paths, introducing chance into an otherwise deterministic system — but this randomness is not purpose.

## In the Critique of Judgment

Kant discusses Epicurus in the context of evaluating possible explanations of natural purposiveness (§§72–73). Epicureanism is classified as an **idealism of purposiveness** — it explains away the appearance of purpose rather than accounting for it. Kant considers this inadequate because it fails to explain why organisms appear purposive to reflective judgment.
