## Definition

An Organism (*organisiertes Wesen*) in Kant's *Critique of Judgment* is a natural being that exhibits the characteristics of a [[Natural Purpose]]: it is both cause and effect of itself, its parts exist for and through the whole, and it cannot be explained solely by mechanical laws. The organism is the central example that forces reflective judgment to adopt a teleological principle when considering certain products of nature.

## Kant's Account

Kant develops his account of the organism primarily in the "Analytic of the Teleological Judgment" (§§ 64–65), where he argues that organized beings present a special challenge to our understanding of nature.

For Kant, an organism is distinguished from a machine by its capacity for self-organization. A machine has only *moving* power; an organism possesses *formative* power — "a self-propagating formative power, which cannot be explained by the capacity of movement alone" (§ 65). This formative power means that an organism can repair itself, grow, and reproduce — capacities that mechanical explanation cannot fully capture.

Kant identifies three key features of organisms as natural purposes: (1) the parts are possible only through their relation to the whole; (2) the parts combine into a whole through their reciprocal causality; (3) the organism produces itself as a species through generation.

Crucially, Kant insists that the concept of an organism as a natural purpose is a regulative, not constitutive, principle. We must judge organisms "as if" they were designed, while acknowledging that this does not amount to knowledge of an actual designer.

## Key Passages

> An organized product of nature is that in which all the parts are mutually ends and means. (§ 65)

> An organized being is not a mere machine: for a machine has only moving power; but an organized being possesses in itself formative power, and such moreover as it communicates to materialswhich have it not; it organizes them. (P01-063, cf. § 65)

> To talk of living matter is absurd. The concept of living matter involves a contradiction, because the essential character of matter is lifelessness,inertia. (P01-042)
