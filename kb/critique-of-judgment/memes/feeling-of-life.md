## Definition

The feeling of life (*Lebensgefühl*) is, in Kant's aesthetic theory, the subject's immediate consciousness of the enhancement or diminution of their vital powers through a representation. It is the subjective state through which pleasure and pain are felt — not as cognition of an object, but as the mind's awareness of its own condition in response to a representation (001-002). This feeling constitutes the foundation upon which aesthetic judgments are made, since the judgment of taste is not a cognitive judgment about the object, but a judgment about how the subject's faculties are affected by it.

## Kant's Account

Kant introduces the feeling of life in §1 of the *Critique of Judgment* to distinguish aesthetic from cognitive judgment. When we apprehend an object through our cognitive faculties, we may form a logical judgment about it; but when a representation is "altogether referred to the subject and to its feeling of life, under the name of the feeling of pleasure or pain," we enter the domain of aesthetic judgment (001-002). This distinction is foundational: it establishes a "quite separate faculty of distinction and of judgment, adding nothing to cognition" (001-002).

Kant argues that the feeling of life is not indifferent — every representation, whether sensible or intellectual, affects it to some degree. Even as mere modifications of the subject, representations cannot be neutral: "they all affect the feeling of life, and none of them, so far as it is a modification of the subject, can be indifferent" (029-043). This universal affective dimension means that the mind's encounter with any representation carries a vital register of enhancement or hindrance.

Crucially, Kant connects the feeling of life to the harmonious free play of the faculties. In a judgment of taste, the imagination and understanding are brought into a free and harmonious activity that furthers or animates the feeling of life — this animation is the sensation of pleasure that grounds the judgment of beauty. The feeling of life is thus the subjective indicator of the purposiveness of form for our cognitive faculties in general.

## Key Distinctions

The feeling of life must be distinguished from mere sensory gratification (the pleasant) and from interest-driven satisfaction. Sensory gratification, such as the pleasure of a taste or a scent, is private and depends on the existence of the object; the feeling of life in an aesthetic judgment, by contrast, is connected to the mere representation and its effect on the free play of the faculties, independent of any interest in the object's existence. Kant also distinguishes the feeling of life from bodily feeling alone — even intellectual representations affect it — and from moral feeling, which issues from practical reason rather than the reflective judgment.

## Role in the Critical System

The feeling of life bridges Kant's theoretical and practical philosophy by providing the subjective ground for reflective judgment. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, the subject's relation to representations is cognitive; in the *Critique of Practical Reason*, it is determined by the moral law. The *Critique of Judgment* completes the critical system by revealing how the feeling of life makes possible a distinct mode of judgment — aesthetic reflective judgment — that is neither cognitive nor moral, yet claims universal validity. The principle of life as the mind's self-maintaining activity foreshadows the organism as a natural purpose in the teleological portion of the third Critique.

## Key Passages

> "Here the representation is altogether referred to the subject and to its feeling of life, under the name of the feeling of pleasure or pain. This establishes a quite separate faculty of distinction and of judgement, adding nothing to cognition, but only comparing the given representation in the subject with the whole faculty of representations, of which the mind is conscious in the feeling of its state."
> (001-002)

> "It is also not to be denied that all representations in us, whether, objectively viewed, they are merely sensible or are quite intellectual, may yet subjectively be united to gratification or grief, however imperceptible either may be; because they all affect the feeling of life, and none of them, so far as it is a modification of the subject, can be indifferent."
> (029-043)
