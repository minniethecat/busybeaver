[[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]]'s journey to [[paris|Paris]] was an investigative expedition undertaken during the course of the [[eustace-peters|Peters]] murder investigation. Though Callaghan's methods appeared eccentric and desultory to outside observers, the Paris trip yielded significant results and marked a turning point in the private investigation conducted by the narrator and his associates.

## Departure and Purpose

Callaghan's departure for Paris was characteristically abrupt. When the narrator later invited Callaghan to stay at his new home, he learned more of the journey:

> "It seems that his sudden departure for Paris was not altogether motiveless; he had been doing a little detective business on his own account."
> — [014-002]

This revelation reframed Callaghan's seemingly whimsical behaviour as purposeful investigation. While the official police inquiry followed its own course, Callaghan had independently pursued lines of enquiry that took him across the Channel.

## Return and Revelations

Callaghan arrived at the narrator's new home "in the afternoon full of his Parisian adventures and to a less extent of his detective researches" [014-002]. With these — "or with an adorned version of them" — he entertained the narrator for an hour or so before dinner [014-002]. The results of his Paris enquiries, once separated from Callaghan's natural embellishment, proved substantial.

After dinner, retiring to the narrator's study to smoke pipes, Callaghan sat silent for a long while before suddenly exclaiming with great energy: "Faith! I know what became of those eyeglasses" [014-006]. This flash of insight, connecting his Paris discoveries to a previously puzzling piece of physical evidence, demonstrated the value of his continental expedition.

## Significance

Callaghan's Paris journey exemplifies the novel's dual-investigation structure. While [[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]] and the [[scotland-yard-offices|Scotland Yard]] authorities pursued leads through official channels, Callaghan's private detective work — including his journey to Paris — uncovered information about [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]]'s past that proved essential to solving the case.

The journey also illuminates Callaghan's character. His "strange whim of staying in that crime-stricken house" at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] after the murder, and his apparently "eccentric and desultory proceedings," masked a serious investigative purpose [022-016]. The Paris trip, in retrospect, was not a whim but a calculated move in a long game.
