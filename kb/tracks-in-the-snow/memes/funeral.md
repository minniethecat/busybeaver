## Overview

The funeral of Eustace Peters took place on Wednesday, 2 February 1896 — Candlemas Day — at Long-Wilton. The narrator, a clergyman and friend of the deceased, read the burial service [005-001]. The funeral followed a two-day inquest held on the preceding Monday and Tuesday, which had established murder as the cause of death but named no suspect [005-001].

## Attendees

A large crowd gathered from the surrounding neighbourhood and even from distant places — a spectacle the narrator resented as "merely impertinent" [005-002]. Among the mourners present:

- **The narrator** — officiated, reading the burial service over his friend's body [005-001]
- **Callaghan** and **Vane-Cartwright** — stayed with the narrator for the occasion [005-002]
- **An elderly cousin** of Peters' father — the only family representative able to attend, as Peters' two nephews were serving in the Army in India [005-002]

The narrator later noted that the funeral, like those of many of his friends, remained associated in his memory "with the singing of birds" [005-001].

## The Inquest (Preceding)

> The inquest had taken place on the Monday and Tuesday, and while it clearly established the fact that the death had been caused by murder, not suicide, nothing was laid before the jury which would have justified a verdict against any particular person. [005-001]

The coroner's inquest, held over 30–31 January 1896, examined the circumstances of Peters' death. The surgeon had quickly determined that a surgical knife driven between the ribs was the cause, and pronounced it "Not suicide" [002-004]. Despite two days of proceedings, no individual was charged.

## Aftermath

The day after the funeral, the narrator returned to his rectory hoping to distance himself from the investigation, but found this impossible [005-006]. Rumours of various clues circulated through parish talk, and he had an important interview with [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] on the day of his return [005-006].
