# Long Wilton

The narrator and protagonist of *Tracks in the Snow* — rector of the parish of Long Wilton, an Oxford-educated clergyman who becomes an amateur detective investigating the murder of his close friend [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]. He is addressed as Mr. Driver, or "Robert" by his wife.

> "On the morning of the 29th of January, 1896, Eustace Peters was found murdered in his bed at his house, Grenvile Combe, in the parish of Long Wilton, of which I was then rector."
> — （001-001）

## Background

Mr. Driver had come to Long Wilton three years before the murder, from a college tutorship at Oxford, to occupy the rectory temporarily until the patron's son became qualified to hold it. Country-bred and fond of country people and pastimes, he was nonetheless unprepared for "the loneliness which at first befell me in a place where, though many of my neighbours were wise men and good men, none ever showed intellectual interests or talked with any readiness of high things" （001-024）.

> "I had come to Long Wilton, three years before, from a college tutorship at Oxford, to occupy the rectory till, as happened not long after, the son of the patron became qualified to hold it."
> — （001-024）

The comradeship of Eustace Peters, who settled at Grenvile Combe a few months after Driver's arrival, "did more than to put an end to my loneliness" — Peters' shrewd, casual observations helped Driver understand and navigate his parish difficulties （001-024）.

## Investigation

After Peters' murder, Mr. Driver takes it upon himself to investigate, believing that "it is for me, if for anyone, to relate the facts" （001-001）. His investigation spans months and takes him from Long Wilton to London, Florence, and the Continent.

His method is methodical: he examines Peters' voluminous papers, interviews witnesses, and pieces together the connection between Peters' death and an earlier murder — that of a man named Longhurst. The narrator's wife, a sharp-minded woman, provides crucial insights, immediately suspecting [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]]: "Robert, it was Vane-Cartwright that did the murder" （001-254）.

The investigation leads Driver into direct confrontation with Vane-Cartwright in Florence, where the latter attempts to deflect suspicion onto [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] and the Trethewy family. Vane-Cartwright accuses Driver openly: "You think I am the assassin of Eustace Peters. Well, I am not" （017-446）. Despite Vane-Cartwright's manipulations, Driver holds firm to his deductions.

## Character

Mr. Driver is a thoughtful, self-reflective narrator. He is conscious of his own limitations as a detective, candid about his mistakes, and deeply loyal to Peters' memory. His wife describes him as possessing "the ability" to bring the murderer to justice if he will "wait patiently and keep your eyes open" （008-256）.

Throughout the novel, Driver wrestles with the ethical implications of his investigation — particularly in his dealings with Vane-Cartwright, where he must feign friendship to gather evidence. He acknowledges: "I suppose it was dishonourable; at least, I have often reproached myself for it, but truly I do not know if it was really dishonourable" （013-410）.

## Later Life

After leaving Long Wilton, Mr. Driver settled with his wife in a country parish "so near in distance to London and in effect so far off" （025-744）. Trethewy became their gardener, and Driver married Peters' elder nephew to Miss Denison. His eldest daughter became engaged to the younger nephew, in whom "his uncle's peculiar gifts repeat themselves more markedly and with greater promise of practical achievement" （025-744）.
