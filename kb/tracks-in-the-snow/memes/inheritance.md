# Inheritance

The inheritance dispute between **[[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]** and **[[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]]** is the primary motive advanced by the [[trial|prosecution]] in the murder case. [[eustace-peters|Peters]] had inherited property that [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] believed rightfully belonged to him — a grievance that, combined with [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]]'s religious conversion and troubled history, made him the prime suspect.

## Overview

The inheritance question is introduced early in the investigation. When **[[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]]** arrests [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]], the motive is clear: a property dispute that had led to a quarrel ten days before the murder （003-001）. The inheritance gave [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] a rational, financial reason to wish [[eustace-peters|Peters]] dead.

## In the Novel

[[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]]'s relationship to the [[eustace-peters|Peters]] inheritance is complex. The property in question was not merely financial — it was tied to [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]]'s sense of identity and justice. His religious conversion had changed his relationship to worldly goods, but the grievance remained.

[[long-wilton|Wilton]]'s investigation gradually reveals that while [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] had a motive, the inheritance story was more complicated than the police assumed. Other characters had financial interests that pointed in different directions. The inheritance dispute, initially treated as conclusive [[evidence|proof]] of motive, becomes one thread in a larger pattern of hidden financial dealings.

## Historical Context

Victorian inheritance law was governed by a complex web of statutes and common law principles. The **Wills Act 1837** had standardized testamentary formalities, but disputes over inheritance remained common in both fiction and life. The trope of the "disinherited heir" was a staple of Victorian literature, and the novel draws on this tradition while complicating it.

## Analysis

The inheritance motive in *Tracks in the Snow* functions as a **narrative red herring** — it seems to explain everything, and for a time it does, but the full picture is more complex. The novel uses inheritance not merely as a plot mechanism but as a way to explore how economic grievances intertwine with personal identity and moral choice.
