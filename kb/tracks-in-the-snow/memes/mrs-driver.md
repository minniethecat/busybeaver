## Overview

Mrs. Driver is the wife of [[the-narrator|Mr. Driver]], the rector of Long Wilton. Though she appears only through her husband's narration, she plays a significant role in the investigation of Eustace Peters' murder — most importantly by persuading her husband to abandon his self-imposed neutrality and engage with the case [008-004].

## Character

### A Perceptive Partner

The narrator frequently tests his impressions against his wife's judgment. When he was uncertain about Callaghan, he "put the matter to the test of my wife's judgment" — knowing that she had initially disliked him [008-009]. Her role as a sounding board and moral compass makes her an essential, if background, figure in the investigation.

### Challenging the Narrator's Resolve

> The resolution at which I had arrived, not to occupy my mind with suspicions, or to regard the detection of crime as part of my business, was not a tenable resolution, and it was entirely dissipated by my wife in a talk which we had on the first evening after her arrival. [008-004]

It was Mrs. Driver who broke through her husband's determination to stay out of the investigation. Her conversation with him on the evening of her return home transformed his approach to the case [008-004].

### The Peters Love Story

The narrator and his wife together speculated about Peters' romantic history: "My wife and I were convinced that he had a love story, but we gathered no hint of what it may have been" [001-004]. This shared curiosity speaks to the couple's intellectual partnership.

## Timeline

- **Late December 1895**: Went with the children to stay with her father [005-004]
- **28 January 1896**: Away from home on the night of Peters' last supper [001-006]
- **Early February**: Returned home the day after Peters' funeral [007-008]; had the pivotal conversation with her husband that evening [008-004]
- **Later**: Made a second trip to London while the narrator worked on the case [011-001]
- **During travels**: Was "suddenly unwell" while at a hotel, prompting the narrator to be summoned [018-001]

## Family

The Drivers had children who travelled with their mother to visit her father [005-004]. The narrator refers to "my wife and children" as his family unit, distinct from his investigative activities.
