[[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]] is the first-person narrator and protagonist of the novel. The rector of [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]] parish, he came to the village "three years before, from a college tutorship at Oxford" [001-003], and his clerical identity, intellectual formation, and personal relationships shape every aspect of the murder investigation he undertakes.

## Background

Jenkinson arrived at [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]] from [[oxford-university|Oxford]], where he had held a college tutorship [001-003]. He describes himself as "country-bred, fond of country people and of country pastimes" [001-003], suggesting a temperament suited to rural parish life. His tenure at Long Wilton was originally intended to be temporary — he occupied the rectory until "the son of the patron became qualified to hold it" [001-003] — but the events of the murder investigation made his sojourn there "a thing dimly remembered, like a page of some other man's biography" [001-003].

His intellectual formation at [[oxford-university|Oxford]] provided him with the habits of logical analysis and textual interpretation that distinguish his investigative method. Unlike the police, who follow procedural routines, Jenkinson approaches the case as a scholar might approach a difficult text — reading the evidence closely, attending to inconsistencies, and pursuing the truth through patient reflection rather than procedural force.

## Role in the Investigation

Jenkinson's involvement in the case begins on the morning of 29 January 1896, when [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]' housemaid came running to the village to alert the police [002-001]. As rector and as Peters' friend — he had taken supper at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] the previous evening [001-006] — Jenkinson is drawn immediately into the inquiry.

His role evolves from witness to investigator as he becomes increasingly dissatisfied with the official investigation. His conviction that [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]] has been wrongly accused drives him to conduct his own parallel inquiry: "I had for some time been convinced of Trethewy's innocence, and yet had never really drawn the necessary inference from it" [007-005].

Jenkinson's method is characterised by:
- **Personal relationships**: He leverages his position as rector to gain access to people and information that would be closed to an official investigator. His interviews with parishioners on "parish matters" [007-007] provide cover for his investigative inquiries.
- **Careful observation**: His description of [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]] as "a man of much quiet power" [001-007] exemplifies his ability to read character from behaviour.
- **Moral seriousness**: His discomfort at accepting an altar-cloth from a man he suspects of murder [016-005] reveals the ethical tension inherent in his dual role as clergyman and investigator.

## Character

Jenkinson is a reflective and morally serious narrator. He is conscious of the limits of his own perspective — "they tell me a narrator must not thrust himself quite into the background" [001-003] — and his narrative is punctuated by acknowledgements of uncertainty and self-doubt.

His clerical vocation provides both a moral framework and a practical platform for his investigation. As [[church-of-england|the Church]]'s representative in [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]], he occupies a unique social position — neither an official of the state nor a private citizen, but a figure whose inquiries carry the weight of moral authority.

## Relationships

- **[[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]**: Friend and parishioner. Jenkinson's affection for Peters is genuine, and his death is felt as a personal loss: "the death of my own friend on the eve, as I sometimes fancy, of a fuller blossoming of his powers" [001-003].
- **[[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]]**: Fellow investigator. Their approaches differ — Callaghan's colonial experience [003-001] gives him a more direct, confrontational style — but they share a commitment to discovering the truth.
- **[[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]]**: A parishioner wrongly suspected of the murder. Jenkinson's advocacy for Trethewy is a measure of his commitment to justice over convenience.
- **[[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]]**: An acquaintance of Peters whose behaviour increasingly draws Jenkinson's suspicion.
