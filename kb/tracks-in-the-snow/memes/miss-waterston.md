Miss Waterston was the aunt of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] and one of the few family connections who appeared at his funeral. Her relationship to the murdered man and her knowledge of his early life made her a valuable source of information for [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]]'s private investigation.

## At the Funeral

Miss Waterston was among the mourners at Peters' funeral on Candlemas Day, 2 February 1896. The narrator records that only two of Peters' family attended — and Miss Waterston was one of them [005-002]. Her presence at the [[the-funeral|funeral]] marked her as someone who maintained a connection with her nephew even after his death.

## The Narrator's Visit

Months after the funeral, the narrator sought her out. His first attempt was in the summer, when he called upon her at her flat in London. That meeting was frustrated: "a lady who was staying with her remained in the room all the time, in spite, as I thought, of several hints that she might go" [015-005]. Miss Waterston, as the narrator left, said "how glad she would be to see me again, and, she hoped, talk with me more fully" [015-005].

The opportunity for a fuller conversation came later that year. On Christmas Eve, the narrator was urgently summoned to visit her:

> "Two days later, on Christmas Eve, I was urgently summoned to visit Peters' aunt, Miss Waterston, whom I had seen at his funeral."
> — [015-005]

The narrator had previously learned from her much that he had not known about his friend: "how really remarkable had been the promise of his early days, though he had idled a little at Oxford; and how he had left Oxford prematurely and taken up a consular appointment" [005-003]. Through Miss Waterston, the narrator gained access to the private history of the man whose murder he was investigating — the family perspective that official police inquiries could never reach.

## Significance

Miss Waterston is a minor character, but her role exemplifies the novel's investigative method. Unlike the police, who rely on physical evidence and formal testimony, the narrator gathers knowledge through relationships — through family connections, through pastoral visits, through the slow accumulation of personal detail that reveals character. Miss Waterston's recollections of Peters' youth, his promise at Oxford, and his premature departure for the consular service provide the kind of contextual understanding that the official investigation, focused on footprints and candle-ends, could never achieve.
