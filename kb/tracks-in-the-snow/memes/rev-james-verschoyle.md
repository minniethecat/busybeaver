The Rev. James Verschoyle, D.D., was a clergyman and former missionary in the East whose journals and knowledge became a crucial — and ultimately unrecoverable — piece of the puzzle surrounding the death of Longhurst and, by extension, the murder of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]].

## Background

Verschoyle had served as a missionary in the East before returning to Europe. At the time the narrator sought him, Verschoyle had been at Siena and subsequently in Germany, before coming back to England [015-009]. His missionary background placed him in the same colonial world where Peters, [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]], and Longhurst had once intersected.

## Connection to the Case

### Peters' Letters

Before his death, Peters had written to Verschoyle seeking information. As the narrator later established, "Peters at the time of his death not merely entertained this suspicion but was taking steps to obtain proof of its truth; for there were his letters to Bryanston and to Verschoyle still extant, and admissible in evidence as res gestae, the actual first steps which he had taken with this aim" [022-011].

### The Missing Journals

Verschoyle's journals were the key. The narrator, tracing Verschoyle's movements, discovered that:

> "Evidently there had been information in Dr. Verschoyle's possession which in Peters' hands could have led to the conviction of Vane-Cartwright."
> — [015-010]

But the journals could not be found. Verschoyle's family, "most obligingly willing to search," reported that "the journals for the years 1881 to 1883 could nowhere be found, though the gap in the sequence showed plainly where they had stood" [015-009]. The diaries covering the crucial period — when Longhurst had died — had vanished.

### Thalberg's Intervention

The reason for the disappearance became clear when [[melchior-thalberg|Thalberg]] was confronted. After Peters' death, Thalberg — acting on Vane-Cartwright's instructions — had traced Verschoyle to Homburg and interviewed him, presenting a forged letter on the paper of the S.P.G. [020-013]. Verschoyle had delivered journals to Thalberg for transmission to Vane-Cartwright, who had "duly received them" [020-013]. The journals, and their dangerous contents, had been suppressed.

## Death

The narrator's pursuit of Verschoyle ended in frustration. Having traced him to his latest address, the narrator found "that he had died in the previous August" [015-009]. The narrator reflected:

> "Peters had held the clue of it, and had died because he held it; Verschoyle perhaps had the clue and was dead too, probably from some other cause; neither had recorded his secret, or the record could not be found."
> — [016-001]

The possibility that Verschoyle, like Peters, had died because of what he knew — "probably from some other cause," the narrator qualifies, leaving the question open — adds a further layer of darkness to the case.

## Significance

Verschoyle never appears directly in the narrative, yet his absent presence is felt throughout. He is one link in the chain of suppressed knowledge that Vane-Cartwright worked so assiduously to control. The fact that Verschoyle was a fellow clergyman — a colleague in the same professional world as [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]] — adds a personal dimension to his significance: he represents what the narrator himself might have been, had he possessed knowledge that a murderer needed to bury.
