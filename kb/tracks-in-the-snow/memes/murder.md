# Murder

The murder of **[[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]** is the central event of *Tracks in the Snow*. [[eustace-peters|Peters]] is found dead in his bedroom on the morning of 29 January, setting off a complex investigation that spans the entire novel. The crime occurs at [[eustace-peters|Peters]]' house, Ashley House, in [[long-wilton-village|the village]] where the narrator, **[[long-wilton|Long Wilton]]**, serves as rector.

## Overview

A murder investigation in a Victorian English village, complicated by snow, false alibis, multiple suspects, and a second death. The narrative follows the efforts of the narrator and **[[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]]** to identify the real killer after the initial suspect — **[[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]]**, [[eustace-peters|Peters]]' servant — is arrested based on circumstantial evidence.

The housemaid discovers [[eustace-peters|Peters]]' body on the morning of the 29th. Snow had fallen overnight and frozen, preserving footprints and other evidence （002-001）.

> "I was up early on the 29th. Snow lay thick on the ground but had ceased falling, and it was freezing hard, when, while waiting for breakfast, I walked out as far as my gate on the village street to see what the weather was like. Suddenly Peters' housemaid came running down to the village on her way to fetch the doctor."
> — （002-001）

## In the Novel

The murder is discovered in **Chapter II**. [[eustace-peters|Peters]] had hosted a dinner party the previous evening (28 January) attended by [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]], **[[melchior-thalberg|Melchior Thalberg]]**, **[[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]]**, and [[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]]. Nothing seemed amiss when the party broke up around ten o'clock （001-010）.

The [[the-bedroom|crime scene]] in [[the-bedroom|Peters' bedroom]] showed little sign of disorder. Valuable items — a gold watch, keys, and a purse containing money — were found untouched on the drawers beside the bed, ruling out robbery as a motive （002-003）.

> "There was little to note about the barely furnished room which showed no traces of disorder. On the top of some drawers on the left of the bed-head lay a curious, old-fashioned gold watch with the watchkey by it, a pocketknife, a pencil, a ring of keys and a purse, the last containing a good deal of money."
> — （002-003）

The investigation initially focuses on [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]], who had quarreled with [[eustace-peters|Peters]] ten days before the murder （003-001）. [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]]'s motive was linked to a dispute over a [[inheritance|legacy]]: [[eustace-peters|Peters]] had inherited property that [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] believed should have been his （006-004）.

A second death — that of **[[ellen-trethewy|Ellen Trethewy]]** — occurs later in the narrative, deepening the mystery and altering the course of the investigation （015-001）.

## Historical Context

The novel is set in the **1890s**, a period when [[investigation|criminal investigation]] in [[england|England]] was transitioning from amateur [[investigation|detection]] to professional police work. The involvement of **[[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]** and **[[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]]** reflects the growing role of organized police forces. The **coroner's inquest** and **magistrate's [[committal-hearing|committal]]** proceedings depicted in the novel follow the actual legal procedures of late-[[england|Victorian England]].

The concept of "murder" in Victorian fiction often served as a vehicle for exploring moral questions about guilt, justice, and the limits of human knowledge — themes central to this novel's investigation narrative.

## Analysis

The murder in *Tracks in the Snow* functions on multiple levels. On the surface, it is a classic "locked room" or closed-circle mystery: a crime in a small village, suspects from a limited pool, [[clue|physical evidence]] (snow, footprints) that both helps and complicates the investigation.

At a deeper level, the murder drives the novel's epistemological concerns. The narrator, [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]], repeatedly questions his own judgment and the reliability of circumstantial evidence:

> "Stupid as it may seem, I had for some time been convinced of Trethewy's innocence, and yet had never really drawn the necessary inference from it."
> — （007-005）

The narrative structure — a first-person account written after the fact — creates dramatic irony: the reader knows the narrator survived to tell the story, but not how the mystery was resolved. This makes the murder both the novel's central plot device and its principal thematic lens.
