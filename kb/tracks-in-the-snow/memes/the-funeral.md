The funeral of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] took place on Wednesday, 2 February 1896 — Candlemas Day. Conducted by [[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]], the rector of [[long-wilton-village|Long Wilton]], the service marked the formal closure of Peters' earthly life while opening a new phase of the investigation, as those who gathered to mourn became, in various ways, agents in the pursuit of justice.

## The Service

> "On Wednesday, the 2nd of February, Candlemas Day, I read the burial service over my friend's body. I will not dwell upon what that service was to me, but like many funerals of my friends it is associated in my mind with the singing of birds."
> — [005-001]

The [[the-coroners-inquest|coroner's inquest]] had taken place on the Monday and Tuesday preceding the funeral. While it had "clearly established the fact that the death had been caused by murder, not suicide, nothing was laid before the jury which would have justified a verdict against any particular person" [005-001]. The funeral thus took place under the shadow of an unsolved crime, with the official inquiry having established the fact of murder but identified no perpetrator.

## The Gathering

The funeral drew a substantial assembly. The narrator records with some asperity that "a large crowd of merely impertinent people, as I confess I regarded them, collected from the neighbourhood and even from far away for the occasion" [005-002]. Among the genuine mourners, only two of Peters' family were present: he had two nephews who might have attended, but circumstances prevented one and the other was absent abroad [005-002].

[[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]] and [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]] — both guests at the fatal [[dinner-party|supper party]] on the night of the murder — stayed with the narrator for the funeral [005-002]. Their presence, while proper in itself, carried an uncomfortable ambiguity: both men were, at this stage, potential suspects.

## Aftermath

The day after the funeral, the narrator returned to his rectory "hoping to be free from any share in a kind of investigation which consorted ill with the ordinary tenor of my work" [005-006]. This hope proved illusory. That very day he had an important interview with [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]], and the investigation continued to draw him in.

Callaghan, whom the narrator had asked to stay for the funeral, revealed his intentions:

> "I discovered very soon that Callaghan, whom I had asked to stay for the funeral, was bent upon staying in the village as long as he could. He conceived that, with the knowledge he possessed and his experience in India, he might, if on the spot, be able to contribute to the ends of justice."
> — [005-007]

The funeral thus became, paradoxically, the launch point for the private investigation that would ultimately solve the crime. What should have been an ending — the burial of the dead — became a beginning.
