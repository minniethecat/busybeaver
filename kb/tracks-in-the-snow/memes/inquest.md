## Overview

The coroner's inquest into the death of Eustace Peters was held over Monday and Tuesday, 30–31 January 1896, at Long-Wilton. It was the first formal legal proceeding following the discovery of Peters' body on 29 January 1896 [001-001]. The inquest established that Peters had died by murder, but the evidence presented was insufficient to justify a verdict against any particular person [005-001].

## Medical Evidence

The surgeon who examined the body provided the critical medical testimony. He identified the cause of death as a "curious, long, narrow, sharp knife for surgical use" which the killer had driven between two of the victim's ribs and left in place [002-004]. His first words at the scene — "Not suicide" — set the direction of the entire investigation [002-004].

It emerged during the inquest that there was reason to believe Peters had not had such a restful night as the circumstances initially suggested [002-004].

## The Verdict

> Nothing was laid before the jury which would have justified a verdict against any particular person. [005-001]

Despite two full days of proceedings, the coroner's jury returned what was effectively an open verdict: murder by person or persons unknown. The absence of a named suspect meant the investigation would continue outside the coroner's court, ultimately leading to a complex chain of events, arrests, and a trial.

## Significance

The inquest marked the transition from the discovery of Peters' body to the active criminal investigation. Its inconclusive outcome — establishing murder without identifying the murderer — set the stage for the suspicion, rumour, and amateur detective work that would occupy the narrator and others in the weeks and months that followed [005-006].
