# Reuben Trethewy

Peters' servant and the man initially arrested for his murder. A religious convert with a disputed inheritance claim, Trethewy is ultimately proven innocent — the victim of circumstantial evidence and, the narrator comes to believe, of [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]]'s manipulation.

> "At last I went home, to find the churchwarden irate at my lateness for an appointed interview about the accounts of the dole charities, and to have a forgotten but much-needed breakfast pressed upon me. I would rather have been alone, but Callaghan gave me his company as far as my house, and expounded his view about Trethewy all the way."
> — （003-004）

## The Arrest

Trethewy was arrested on suspicion of murdering Peters. The case against him rested on circumstantial evidence: he had been seen near the house on the night of the murder, and as Peters' servant he had knowledge of the household. However, the narrator — from early in the investigation — doubted Trethewy's guilt.

Callaghan, who "expounded his view about Trethewy" at length, was among those who initially suspected him, but the weight of evidence eventually shifted. Vane-Cartwright himself would later attempt to revive suspicion of Trethewy, suggesting to Mr. Driver that "little feet can wear big shoes" — implying Trethewy's daughter Ellen might have fabricated the tracks in the snow to protect someone （017-458）.

## Vane-Cartwright's Manipulation

During their confrontation in Florence, Vane-Cartwright attempted to redirect Driver's investigation toward Trethewy. He claimed to have "begun to suspect him myself," while simultaneously accusing Driver of suspecting Vane-Cartwright himself: "You think I am the assassin of Eustace Peters. Well, I am not" （017-446）.

Vane-Cartwright insisted: "You think Trethewy did not kill Peters. So do I. He did not actually kill him, he no more did that than you did" — a technically true statement designed to confuse the investigation （017-446）.

The narrator recognized this for what it was: "I had not the faintest doubt that Vane-Cartwright's suggestion about the Trethewy family, whatever its object might be, was a well-acted lie" （017-458）.

## Exoneration and Later Life

Trethewy was eventually released and left the Long Wilton neighbourhood. He found a situation through friends and later, after the resolution of the case, became the narrator's gardener. The narrator reflects with characteristic honesty: "I now like him as a man but could not lay it upon my conscience to recommend him as a gardener" （025-744）.

His daughter, Ellen Trethewy, became a board-school mistress in London. She had played a role in the investigation, showing "astonishing quickness" in grasping the implications of the evidence once provided with the necessary clues （018-524）.

## Significance

Trethewy's case illustrates a central theme of the novel: the danger of circumstantial evidence and the ease with which suspicion can be manufactured. He serves as a foil to the real murderer — while Vane-Cartwright escapes suspicion through social position and careful manipulation, Trethewy is immediately suspect by virtue of his class and proximity.
