Scotland Yard, the headquarters of the Metropolitan Police in [[london|London]], is the institutional centre of the official investigation into the murder of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]. Though never directly depicted, its presence is felt throughout the novel through the figures it dispatches to [[long-wilton-village|Long Wilton]] and the procedures it requires of those on the ground.

## The Superintendent

Scotland Yard's most direct intervention in the case comes through the Superintendent dispatched to lead the investigation. The narrator records his arrival with a mixture of admiration and wariness:

> "We returned to Grenvile Combe, and the Superintendent went straight to the death-chamber where he remained some minutes with the Sergeant and me, taking note with much minuteness and astonishing rapidity of all the details which I have already mentioned."
> — [003-008]

The Superintendent brought a professional expertise that the local police lacked. Examining the garden, he made the critical discovery that had eluded [[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]]:

> "Not a word was said as to the inferences that we all drew from those few footprints, but the Superintendent sharply asked the Sergeant, 'Why was that trail not found and followed to an end this morning?'"
> — [004-004]

It was the Superintendent who led the party to [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]]'s cottage, confronting him with the boots found at the scene: "'Mr. Trethewy,' said the Superintendent, 'do you know these boots?'" [004-006].

## The Headquarters Function

Scotland Yard served as the central repository for evidence gathered in the field. After interviewing witnesses, Sergeant Speke would take "a due note of it for transmission to those, to me mysterious, headquarters where I supposed all such matters were digested" [007-008]. The narrator's phrase — "to me mysterious" — captures the distance between the local, personal investigation he conducts and the impersonal institutional machinery of Scotland Yard.

The novel ultimately draws on both modes. The belated application of "the trained intelligence of the police" to the case — through Scotland Yard's review of evidence long neglected — proved crucial: "the case wore an altered aspect. No startling discovery had come to pass, only the revelation of the obvious" [022-007]. Scotland Yard's contribution was not brilliance but thoroughness — the patient, systematic examination that the local investigation, for all its energy, had failed to sustain.

## The Tension Between Centre and Periphery

Scotland Yard's relationship with the local investigation embodies a tension that runs through the novel. The narrator, as a clergyman, was conscious of the impropriety of his investigative role: "I, who hold it most undesirable that a clergyman should be a magistrate, could not but feel it still less fitting that he should be a detective" [003-007]. Yet the official investigation, for all its institutional authority, repeatedly failed where private inquiry succeeded — the Superintendent overlooking the trail, the headquarters digesting reports too slowly, the belated police review revealing only "the obvious" [022-007].

The novel does not resolve this tension in favour of one mode or the other. Scotland Yard represents a necessary counterweight to the narrator's intuitive approach — a repository of procedure and institutional memory that complements, even as it sometimes frustrates, the private pursuit of truth.
