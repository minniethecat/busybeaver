The study at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] was [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]' private room, situated in the front of the house. It served as the waiting room for witnesses during the initial investigation on the morning of 29 January 1896, and later became the site where Peters' extensive papers — diaries, notebooks of travel, notes of study, and drafts for his psychological treatise — were examined by [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]].

## Location in the House

Peters' study was located in the front portion of Grenvile Combe, part of the same block of rooms that included his bedroom directly above. The house's internal geography, with the study and bedroom forming a vertical stack, became significant to the [[investigation]] of how an intruder might have moved through the house.

## The Morning of the Discovery

On the morning of 29 January, after the discovery of Peters' body, [[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]] asked the principal witnesses to wait in Peters' study while he dealt with the crowd of neighbours gathering outside:

> "Upon seeing what was happening the Sergeant asked Vane-Cartwright, Callaghan and myself to await him in Peters' study, while he went out to drive away the intruders, to make the constable keep others out and to pursue his own investigations."
> — [002-007]

While they waited, [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]] made the sensible suggestion that they should look about the room for anything that might bear on the case [002-007]. This moment in the study marked the beginning of the private, parallel investigation that the narrator and his associates would conduct alongside the official police inquiry.

## Peters' Papers

Months after the murder, when the narrator returned to [[long-wilton-village|Long Wilton]], he completed his examination of Peters' papers found in the study:

> "His various diaries and notebooks, notes of travel and notes of study, jottings and completed passages for his psychological book, I found to be of fascinating interest, and I lingered over them long."
> — [012-009]

These papers, housed in the study, constituted a rich record of Peters' intellectual life and provided essential context for understanding the relationships and motives that led to his murder. The narrator, as executor of Peters' estate, had both the duty and the privilege of examining this material [012-009].

## The Narrator's Own Study

It is worth noting that the narrator's own study at [[the-rectory|the Rectory]] also serves as a site of investigation. It was there, after dinner one evening, that [[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]] had a sudden revelation:

> "After dinner Callaghan and I retired to my study to smoke pipes. He sat for a long while silent... Suddenly he sat upright in his chair. 'Faith!' he exclaimed with great energy... 'I know what became of those eyeglasses.'"
> — [014-006]

## Significance

The study — both Peters' at Grenvile Combe and the narrator's at the Rectory — represents the intellectual dimension of the investigation. It is in these rooms, surrounded by books and papers, that the careful sifting of evidence and the piecing together of motive take place, complementing the physical evidence gathered in the [[the-bedroom|bedroom]] and the [[the-garden|garden]].
