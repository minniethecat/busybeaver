## Overview

The narrator — addressed by others as **Mr. Driver** [008-004] — is the rector of Long Wilton parish and the central consciousness through which the entire story is told. A friend of the murdered Eustace Peters, he becomes reluctantly drawn into the investigation despite his initial resolve "not to occupy my mind with suspicions, or to regard the detection of crime as part of my business" [008-004].

## Character

### Profession and Status

The narrator was rector of Long Wilton at the time of the murder in January 1896 [001-001]. He had been at Oxford shortly after Peters' time there [001-004]. His position as a clergyman placed him at the moral and social centre of the parish, making his involvement in the investigation both unavoidable and personally troubling — he repeatedly wrestles with whether his amateur detective work is "dishonourable" [016-005].

### Relationship with Peters

> Eustace Peters, then, was a retired official of the Consular Service, and a man of varied culture and experience — too much varied, I may say. [001-004]

The narrator knew Peters well enough to be a regular supper guest at Grenvile Combe. He was present at what would be Peters' last supper on the evening of 28 January 1896, alongside Callaghan, Vane-Cartwright, and Thalberg [001-006]. After Peters' murder, he officiated at the funeral on Candlemas Day, reading the burial service over his friend's body [005-001].

### The Reluctant Detective

Though he initially resolved to stay out of the investigation, the narrator's resolve was "entirely dissipated" by his wife [008-004]. He conducted interviews, visited Vane-Cartwright's hotel under pretext [016-005], and pieced together evidence alongside Callaghan. His method was not professional detection but persistent, morally ambivalent inquiry — he "tried hard some days later to recall my impressions of that evening" [001-008] and followed every rumour that came through parish talk [005-006].

### Key Involvement

The narrator was present at critical moments in the case:

- **The last supper**: At Grenvile Combe on 28 January 1896, the night before Peters' body was discovered [001-006]
- **The funeral**: Read the burial service on 2 February 1896 [005-001]
- **The arrest**: Present when Vane-Cartwright surrendered himself, saying "Mr. Driver, Mr. Callaghan, I am your prisoner" [021-003]
- **The investigation**: Conducted numerous interviews with witnesses and suspects throughout the case

## Personal Life

The narrator was married; his wife was away from home on the night of 28 January [001-006]. She later played a role in persuading him to abandon his neutrality [008-004]. At one point during the investigation, she was "suddenly unwell" while they were travelling [018-001].

## Style and Temperament

The narrator's prose reveals a man of education, moral seriousness, and literary sensibility. His recollections are marked by vivid sensory detail — he associates Peters' funeral "with the singing of birds" [005-001] — and by a persistent self-questioning about the propriety of his investigative role. He is not a heroic detective but a conscientious clergyman caught between duty to his friend's memory and the unsettling demands of a murder inquiry.
