The supper party at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] on the evening of 28 January 1896 was the last social gathering of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]' life. That night, after the guests had departed, Peters was murdered in his bed. The gathering thus occupies a unique position in the narrative: it is the final occasion on which the principal figures in the case were together, and the conversation and interactions of that evening became the subject of intense retrospective scrutiny.

## The Gathering

> "On the 28th my wife was away from home, and I had supper at Grenvile Combe, going there about seven o'clock. There were three other guests at supper, James Callaghan, C.I.E., William Vane-Cartwright, and one Melchior Thalberg."
> — [001-006]

[[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]], the narrator and rector of [[long-wilton-village|Long Wilton]], attended because his wife was away. [[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]] was an old schoolfellow of Peters who had been staying at the house for three weeks [001-006]. [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]] and [[melchior-thalberg|Melchior Thalberg]] completed the party of five.

The gathering broke up at about ten o'clock, when the narrator rose to go:

> "Our party that evening broke up when, about ten o'clock, I rose to go; and Thalberg, whose best way to the hotel lay through the village, accompanied me as far as the Rectory, which was a quarter of a mile off and was the nearest house in the village."
> — [001-010]

## The Guests

**James Callaghan**, C.I.E., was an old schoolfellow of Peters. Though the two had been estranged for years, Callaghan had been staying at Grenvile Combe for three weeks at the time of the dinner [001-006]. His presence at the house — both before and, remarkably, after the murder — made him a figure of suspicion.

**William Vane-Cartwright** was a wealthy City merchant who had formerly had dealings with the Far East and considerable business with Italy [001-007]. His relationship with Peters was complex, rooted in shared history in the East, and his behaviour during the supper party became a key subject of later investigation.

**Melchior Thalberg**, solicitor and Commissioner for Oaths, was the least known of the guests. His professional connection to matters touching on Peters' past in the East would later prove significant.

## Retrospective Significance

In the months following the murder, every detail of the supper party was revisited. Callaghan later recounted to the narrator and his wife "the conversation and events of that evening in great detail" [014-007]. The narrator observed that "on the first evening when he had seen the two men together" — Vane-Cartwright and Peters — "their intercourse had been marked by a certain constraint" [022-012].

It was during this evening that Vane-Cartwright, "talking in his wonted way," gave a false account of Longhurst's death to one of the only two living men who knew the truth — a slip that revived Peters' old suspicions [001-734]. This conversation, over supper at Grenvile Combe, set in motion the chain of events that would end, hours later, in murder.
