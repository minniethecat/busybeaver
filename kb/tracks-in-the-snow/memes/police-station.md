The police station serves as the institutional anchor of the murder investigation in [[long-wilton-village|Long Wilton]]. It is here that the machinery of criminal justice — the taking of statements, the [[arrest|detention]] of suspects, the consultation between investigators — operates according to formal procedure, in contrast to the informal information-gathering that occurs at [[the-inn|the inn]] and the private [[investigation|inquiry]] conducted by [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]] at [[the-rectory|the Rectory]].

> "Suddenly Peters' housemaid came running down to the village on her way, as it proved, to the police-station. Before passing she paused, and breathlessly told me the news."
> — [002-001]

## Location and Function

The police station is located within [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]], accessible to both [[long-wilton-village|the village]] centre and the outlying areas where the key events of the case unfold. It is the professional domain of [[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]], who conducts much of his official business from its premises [002-005].

The station serves as the conduit between local policing and the centralised investigative apparatus. After interviewing witnesses, the Sergeant would take "a due note of it for transmission to those, to me mysterious, headquarters where I supposed all such matters were digested" [007-008].

## Key Events at the Station

### The Initial Report

On the morning of 29 January 1896, Peters' housemaid ran from [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] to the police station to report the discovery of the body [002-001]. This set in motion the formal machinery of investigation, with [[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]] arriving at the house and beginning his examination.

### Witness Statements

The station was where formal witness statements were taken and collated. After [[edith-summers|Edith Summers]], Peters' housemaid, confessed to the narrator that she had told a falsehood to the police-sergeant on the morning after the murder [007-003], the Sergeant took due note of her corrected testimony for transmission to higher authorities [007-008].

### The Narrator's Reports

[[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]] made reports to the police at the station on at least two critical occasions. He reported the substance of his interview with [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]], in which Trethewy had "unsolicited, made a confession to me — not a confession of crime, but a confession of criminal intent" [006-001]. The narrator also communicated his own observations and suspicions as the investigation developed.

### Trethewy's Detention

Following his arrest, [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]] was held at the station. The narrator's visits to him there provided some of the novel's most intimate explorations of the suspect's state of mind [006-001].

## The Tension Between Formal and Informal Investigation

A persistent theme in the novel is the tension between the formal mechanisms of justice — represented by the police station and the legal procedures it embodies — and the more intuitive, relational approach of [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]]. The police station is the arena in which this tension is most acutely felt: here, the narrator's private convictions must contend with the evidentiary standards and procedural constraints of official investigation.

The novel ultimately validates both modes. As the narrator reflects late in the case: "the trained intelligence of the police had now been applied to the matter, and the case wore an altered aspect. No startling discovery had come to pass, only the revelation of the obvious" [022-007]. The belated police attention to the report of the young constable — who had verified that certain tracks did not exist on the morning after the murder [022-014] — proved crucial to the final resolution.
