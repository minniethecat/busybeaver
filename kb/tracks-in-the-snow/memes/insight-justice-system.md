# Justice System in *Tracks in the Snow*

## Thesis

The novel presents the Victorian justice system as a double-edged instrument: its formal machinery — inquests, committal hearings, trials — provides the framework for resolving the Peters murder, yet the system's susceptibility to circumstantial evidence, class bias, and procedural rigidity nearly condemns an innocent man while the true culprit evades suspicion through social position and careful manipulation.

## The Institutional Framework

The novel traces a complete criminal justice sequence: the discovery of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]' body triggers a [[the-coroners-inquest|coroner's inquest]], followed by a [[committal-hearing|committal hearing]], the arrest of [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]], and ultimately a [[the-trial-event|trial]]. Each institution operates according to its own logic, yet each proves fallible in its own way.

The inquest establishes the basic facts of the death but cannot penetrate the deeper mystery. The committal hearing, weighing the [[evidence]] presented, finds sufficient grounds to hold Trethewy — grounds that the narrator, and eventually the reader, recognize as dangerously circumstantial. The trial, the system's ultimate mechanism for determining [[truth]], produces a [[verdict]], but only after the real investigation has been conducted outside formal channels by an amateur: the [[long-wilton|narrator]] himself.

## Circumstantial Evidence and Its Dangers

The case against Trethewy rests entirely on circumstantial [[evidence]]: his presence near the scene, his knowledge of the household, his disputed [[inheritance]] claim. None of these facts proves guilt, yet in combination they prove sufficient to arrest and hold a man.

The narrator's wife articulates the weakness of purely circumstantial reasoning when she challenges her husband's theory: "I wonder, all the same, why he murdered Eustace?" The question of [[motive]] — central to any criminal case — remains inadequately answered throughout the official proceedings. The concept of [[circumstantial-evidence]] itself becomes a subject of thematic scrutiny: the novel demonstrates how the same facts can support diametrically opposed conclusions depending on the interpretive framework applied.

## Class and the Differential Administration of Justice

Trethewy, a servant, is immediately suspect. [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]], a gentleman of means and social standing, escapes suspicion despite far stronger circumstantial links to the crime. The narrator observes this differential treatment implicitly throughout his account — Vane-Cartwright moves freely through the investigation, even joining the search of Peters' papers, while Trethewy is arrested on far thinner grounds.

The novel's resolution underscores this theme: Trethewy, though exonerated, ends his days as the narrator's gardener — a position the narrator wryly notes he "could not lay it upon my conscience to recommend him" for. Vane-Cartwright's fate is left deliberately ambiguous. Justice, in the novel's world, is administered unevenly across class lines.

## The Amateur Detective vs. Official Investigation

The formal justice system is supplemented — and in crucial respects supplanted — by the narrator's private [[investigation]]. Mr. Driver examines Peters' papers, travels to Florence to confront Vane-Cartwright, and pieces together the connection to the earlier murder of Longhurst. The official police investigation, by contrast, is portrayed as competent but constrained — bound by procedure, reliant on the very circumstantial reasoning that the novel critiques.

This tension between amateur and official investigation reflects a broader Victorian anxiety about professionalized policing (represented by [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]) versus the gentleman-amateur tradition. [[james-callaghan|Callaghan]], who aspires to join the Criminal Investigation Department, embodies this transitional moment — part amateur enthusiast, part proto-professional.

## Conclusion

The novel's treatment of justice is ultimately ambivalent. The system works, after a fashion: the innocent man is freed, the guilty identified. But it works because of extra-systemic efforts — the persistence of an amateur, the insight of a wife, the conscientious examination of a dead friend's papers. The formal machinery of justice, left to itself, would have produced a different and darker outcome. In this, *Tracks in the Snow* offers a quietly radical critique: that justice, as administered by Victorian institutions, is only as sound as the individuals who operate — and occasionally circumvent — it.
