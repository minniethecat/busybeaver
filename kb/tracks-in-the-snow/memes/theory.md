# Theory

[[long-wilton|Long Wilton]]'s theory — his mental reconstruction of what happened on the night of the murder — is the intellectual engine of *Tracks in the Snow*. Unlike the instantaneous deductions of Sherlock Holmes, [[long-wilton|Wilton]]'s theory develops slowly through trial, error, and painful self-correction.

## Overview

Theory in this novel is not a display of genius but a record of intellectual humility. [[long-wilton|Wilton]] constructs a hypothesis, tests it against the facts, finds it wanting, and revises. He is explicit about the fragility of his [[deduction|reasoning]]: "what a frail edifice my theory still remained!" （010-004）.

## In the Novel

[[long-wilton|Wilton]]'s theory evolves through several stages. Initially he accepts the official police theory — [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] killed [[eustace-peters|Peters]] over a [[inheritance|legacy]] dispute. But the "slight but almost conclusive circumstance" that emerges after [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]]'s [[committal-hearing|committal]] forces a complete revision （007-003）.

The theory is tested against [[clue|physical evidence]] (footprints, [[murder|the crime]] scene), documentary evidence (letters, records), and testimonial evidence. [[long-wilton|Wilton]] at one point acknowledges that his theory, however compelling, might merely be a pattern he has imposed on random facts: "I saw, as the reader may see, that it fitted in easily with all the known facts. I could even point to circumstances which seemed to afford it positive confirmation" （010-004）.

## Historical Context

Victorian epistemology — the philosophical study of knowledge — was deeply concerned with the relationship between evidence and theory. Figures like William Whewell and John Stuart Mill had developed sophisticated accounts of scientific [[deduction|reasoning]], and these ideas permeated intellectual culture. The novel's attention to the theory-evidence relationship reflects this broader cultural concern.

## Analysis

The treatment of theory in *Tracks in the Snow* is one of the novel's most distinctive features. Where most Victorian detective fiction presents the detective's [[deduction|reasoning]] as infallible, this novel shows the process of **theory construction as fallible, recursive, and psychologically demanding**. [[long-wilton|Wilton]] does not solve [[murder|the crime]] by being smarter than everyone else; he solves it by being willing to be wrong, repeatedly, until he is right.

This creates a different kind of suspense — not "will the detective figure it out?" but "is the detective right this time?" The reader, like [[long-wilton|Wilton]], must hold multiple possible theories in mind simultaneously.
