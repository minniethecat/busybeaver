Grenvile Combe is the country house of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]], situated in the parish of [[long-wilton-village|Long Wilton]], in the [[england|English]] countryside. The house is the primary [[the-bedroom|crime scene]] of the novel: it was here, in his bedroom above the study, that Peters was murdered on the night of 28 January 1896. As both the victim's residence and the site of the central act of violence, Grenvile Combe occupies a unique position in the narrative geography — every line of physical [[evidence]] in the case leads back to its rooms, its windows, its doors.

> "On the morning of the 29th of January, 1896, Eustace Peters was found murdered in his bed at his house, Grenvile Combe, in the parish of Long Wilton, of which I was then rector."
> — [001-001]

## Location and Topography

Grenvile Combe is described in precise topographical detail, its layout becoming critical to the [[investigation]]:

> "Grenvile Combe is a little property of some ten acres lying between the grass lane and the road, and bordered on the North by a fir plantation which extends from the road to the lane."
> — [001-096]

The house itself stands near the fir plantation. Its back looks out upon a steeply rising pasture field along the grass lane; its front looks across the drive, a strip of lawn, and the road to the stream and to [[the-church|the church]] and the hotel on the hill beyond. The view from Grenvile Combe across the valley is noted by [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]] as having been spoiled by the development of a new hotel — a detail that, in retrospect, acquires sinister significance, since the hotel's upper windows command a prospect of the whole of Peters' grounds [001-096].

The house's internal geography is equally important to the case. Peters' [[the-study|study]] was in the front of the house at the Northeast corner of the main block — on the left as one entered at the front door — and his [[the-bedroom|bedroom]] was directly above it. A path led from the drive under the North wall of the house to the kitchen entrance, and along this path stood an outbuilding housing the pump. A shrubbery of berberis, box, and laurel skirted the blind end of the drive and straggled along under the low brick wall separating the drive and front lawn from the fir plantation, ending at an old yew tree by the road [001-096].

## History and Ownership

Peters came into possession of Grenvile Combe through inheritance. His aunt, upon her death, left him the house, in which a good deal of his boyhood had been spent. He settled there soon after [[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]]'s arrival as rector of [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]], and stayed on without any settled plan, passing his time in country rambles, naturalist pursuits, and philosophic studies [001-026]. The house thus represents both a return to Peters' childhood landscape and the place of his final, interrupted intellectual labours.

## The Night of the Murder

On the 28th of January, with the narrator's wife away from home, [[robert-jenkinson|Jenkinson]] had supper at Grenvile Combe, arriving about seven o'clock. The other guests were [[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]] (an old schoolfellow of Peters who had been staying at the house for three weeks), [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]], and [[melchior-thalberg|Melchior Thalberg]] [001-030]. This supper party — the last social gathering of Peters' life — brought together under one roof the key figures whose relationships and rivalries would define the investigation.

The critical physical evidence of the murder is bound up with the house's fastenings. The front door of Grenvile Combe had been bolted on the inside that night. Every other door had been bolted in like manner by the servants. Every window but two had been latched — and the two exceptions were [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]]'s own open window and a small back window through which, careful experiment showed, only an infant could pass. The doors remained bolted till the servants were about in the morning, by which time Peters had been dead for hours [001-706]. This locked-room configuration narrowed the field of suspects to the inmates of the house.

> "No one then had entered the house by night, or, if he had previously entered it, had escaped by night; and it was also certain that no one could have lurked there concealed in the morning. Therefore, Peters was murdered by an inmate of the house."
> — [001-706]

## The Scene of Investigation

In the hours and days following the discovery, Grenvile Combe became the focus of intensive scrutiny. [[sergeant-speke|Sergeant Speke]] conducted his initial examination of the premises. The Superintendent from [[scotland-yard-offices|Scotland Yard]], upon his arrival, went straight to the death-chamber and spent some minutes noting every detail with "much minuteness and astonishing rapidity." He then searched the garden, beginning at the point under the unlatched window, where he made the startling discovery of footprints that had been overlooked by the local police [001-088].

It was also at Grenvile Combe, on [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]]'s first evening there, that the fateful conversation about Longhurst occurred — a conversation in which Vane-Cartwright, talking in his wonted way, gave a false account of Longhurst's end to one of the only two living men who knew the truth. This slip revived Peters' old suspicions and set in motion the chain of events that would lead to his death [001-734].
