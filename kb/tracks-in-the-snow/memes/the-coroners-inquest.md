The coroner's inquest into the death of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] was the first formal legal proceeding in the murder case. Held on the Monday and Tuesday preceding the funeral — which took place on Candlemas Day, 2 February 1896 [005-001] — the inquest transformed what had been a local tragedy into a matter of official record.

## The Proceedings

The inquest served several essential functions. It established the cause of death and began the process of sifting the available evidence. The surgeon who had first examined the body gave testimony: at the inquest, "there was reason to think that Peters had not had such a knife, for he never locked up drawers or cupboards, and his servants knew all his few possessions well. It appeared, too, that the owner of the knife had taken precautions against being traced, by carefully obliterating the maker's name and other marks on it with a file" [002-004].

The inquest also examined the boot-print evidence. The village shoemaker, whose expert opinion was sought, testified that though he never arranged hobnails in the distinctive pattern found in the footprints, "he had seen the same arrangement in boots that had been brought to him to be repaired, by some man who was not Trethewy" [005-001]. This introduced an early note of doubt into a case that otherwise seemed to point toward [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]].

## The Verdict

> "The inquest had taken place on the Monday and Tuesday, and while it clearly established the fact that the death had been caused by murder, not suicide, nothing was laid before the jury which would have justified a verdict against any particular person."
> — [005-001]

The verdict of murder by person or persons unknown was the only one the evidence could support. While suspicion had gathered around Trethewy — he had quarrelled with Peters ten days before the murder [003-001], and his behaviour after the crime had been erratic — the inquest jury could not convert suspicion into a finding of probable guilt.

## The Trethewys' Testimony

Throughout the inquest and the subsequent magistrates' proceedings, Trethewy and his wife maintained their account with what the narrator describes as "immovable persistency" [007-002]. This consistency, while it did not prevent Trethewy's eventual committal for trial, contributed to the narrator's growing conviction of his innocence.

## Significance

The inquest represents one pole of the novel's epistemological spectrum — the pole of official, procedural knowledge. Its methods stand in contrast to the narrator's more intuitive, relational approach to truth-seeking. Yet the inquest was not without its uses: the formal examination of the knife, the boots, and the timeline established a factual record that the private investigation would later build upon.

The narrator's subsequent reflections capture the inquest's limitations:

> "I believe that some doubt had arisen as to the identification of the boots... Later on, however, it was ascertained, I fancy through Callaghan's ingenuity, that Trethewy, who liked dabbling in various handicrafts, had cobbled and nailed some boots for a friend."
> — [005-001]

The narrator also admits to having withheld information from the police — specifically, that "Trethewy alone had had access to the ladder" [005-001] — a reticence that complicates any simple contrast between the thoroughness of official investigation and the integrity of private inquiry.
