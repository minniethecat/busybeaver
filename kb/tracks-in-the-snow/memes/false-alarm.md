On the night of 28 January 1896, while [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] lay dead or dying in his bedroom, a disturbance occurred outside [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] that initially seemed to be connected to the murder. The incident, later determined to be a false alarm, proved significant not for what it was but for what it revealed about the state of the house at a critical hour.

## The Disturbance

> "He and Vane-Cartwright had been disturbed during the night in a notable manner. They had actually had an alarm of murder, and curiously enough a false and even ludicrous alarm. About 11:30 o'clock they had been roused by loud shouting outside the house, amid which Callaghan declared that he had distinctly heard the word 'Murder!'"
> — [002-009]

[[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]] and [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]], the two house guests, were roused from sleep by the commotion. Their response to the alarm — coming downstairs to investigate — became a critical piece of evidence.

## The Investigation of the Incident

The report subsequently received from the constable who had been on duty along the road that night confirmed the facts, and "a little reflection made it appear that the disturbance outside had nothing to do with the murder" [002-010]. The narrator noted, however, that "the only thing connected with this incident which much impressed me at the time was Callaghan's manner in relating it" [002-010] — an early hint that Callaghan's behaviour warranted scrutiny.

## Evidentiary Significance

Though the alarm itself was a red herring, the incident yielded one of the most important pieces of evidence in the case. When Callaghan and Vane-Cartwright came downstairs in response to the noise, they found the front door bolted on the inside:

> "The front door of Grenvile Combe had been bolted on the inside that night, before Peters died. Presumably Peters did it; anyway Vane-Cartwright and Callaghan, as they had said next morning, found it bolted when they came down disturbed by the noise, and themselves bolted it again; and Peters was then living, for they heard him moving in his room above."
> — [022-015]

This testimony, corroborated by both men, established two crucial facts: first, that Peters was still alive at approximately 11:30 p.m.; and second, that no intruder could have entered or left by the front door after that hour. The locked-room nature of the murder — that Peters was "murdered by an inmate of the house" — rested in part on this evidence.

## Narrative Function

The false alarm serves a dual narrative purpose. On the surface, it is a piece of misdirection — a dramatic incident that seems as though it must be connected to the murder but ultimately is not. At a deeper level, it is a device for fixing the timeline and the physical circumstances of the crime. The novel repeatedly employs such apparent digressions — the [[dinner-party|supper party]], the conversation about Longhurst — that only reveal their true evidentiary significance in retrospect.
