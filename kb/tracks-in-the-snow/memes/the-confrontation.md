The Confrontation is the climactic sequence of the novel, spanning Chapters XX and XXI, in which the long investigation into the murder of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] reaches its dramatic conclusion. In a swift series of events, [[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]] and [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]] extract crucial evidence from [[melchior-thalberg|Melchior Thalberg]], are accosted in the street by a surrendering [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]], hear his full confession in Callaghan's chambers, and receive news of a poisoning that transforms the moral calculus of the moment.

## The Thalberg Interview (Chapter XX)

The confrontation begins at the City office of Melchior Thalberg, Solicitor and Commissioner for Oaths. Callaghan, having summoned the narrator to his chambers, takes him — on "an errand which he refused to explain" — to Thalberg's office [020-008]. Under the pretence of making his will, Callaghan manoeuvres Thalberg into a position where he must answer questions about his relationship with Vane-Cartwright.

> "Mr. Thalberg bounded from his chair and collapsed into it again. 'Stop, Mr. Manson,' he cried to the clerk, 'you must be present at whatever else this gentleman may have to say.'"
> — [020-009]

Under pressure, Thalberg reveals that he had been the agent through whom Vane-Cartwright monitored Peters' suspicions. After Peters' death, Thalberg had been dispatched to interview [[rev-james-verschoyle|Dr. Verschoyle]] at Homburg, armed with a forged letter on the paper of the S.P.G. [020-013]. Thalberg also discloses that Vane-Cartwright had obtained Peters' journals after the murder [020-013]. Crucially, the evidence shows "plainly enough that Vane-Cartwright was aware of Peters' suspicions and concerned himself greatly about them" [020-015].

## The Surrender (Chapter XXI)

As Callaghan and the narrator leave Thalberg's office, they are stopped in the street:

> "As we turned into the street we were stopped by Vane-Cartwright."
> — [021-001]

Vane-Cartwright's opening words transform the pursuit:

> "'Mr. Driver, Mr. Callaghan,' he said, addressing us in turn in tones as quiet as ever, but with a pale face and highly-strung manner, 'I am your prisoner.'"
> — [021-003]

He asks to tell his story before being given up to justice: "It will not make you alter your purpose, I know that; but it will make you respect me a little more than you do" [021-004]. Callaghan, more generous than the narrator would have been, invites him to his chambers [021-007].

## The Confession

In Callaghan's rooms, Vane-Cartwright delivers a lengthy and eloquent confession, tracing his life from a childhood of cultured poverty, through his years in Saigon where he met both Peters and Longhurst, to the events surrounding the murder [021-012] to [021-031].

He recounts how Peters had suspected him of dishonesty from their first acquaintance in the East [021-015], how he had taken advantage of the declining Longhurst to acquire valuable concessions [021-017], and how, on the evening of the fatal supper at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]], something he said had "revived all Peters' suspicions of me, and this time with the addition of a suspicion, which was true, that I had murdered Longhurst" [021-023].

The crucial precipitating event, however, was not Peters' suspicion but a letter Vane-Cartwright received on the afternoon of 28 January — "seven hours before I killed him" — from Lady Denison, informing him that her daughter had resolved to break off their engagement because her old attachment to another man had returned [021-027] to [021-028].

## The Narrator's Rejection

When Vane-Cartwright finishes, the narrator delivers a stunning response:

> "'Mr. Vane-Cartwright,' I said, 'it is my painful duty to tell you at once that I do not believe one word you have said, except what I knew already.'"
> — [021-035]

Callaghan, deeply moved by the confession delivered at his own fireside, is on the point of letting Vane-Cartwright go free [021-037]. But at that moment a telegram arrives: the narrator's wife has been poisoned at the villa of a friend — "Clarissa terribly ill, symptoms poison" — and the message is certified by a prearranged password [021-039]. The implication is clear: Vane-Cartwright's reach has extended even to this.

The confrontation thus ends not with a simple arrest but with a moral crisis: justice demands Vane-Cartwright's punishment, yet the confession has revealed a man whose crimes, however inexcusable, are rooted in a human story of ambition, rejection, and despair.
