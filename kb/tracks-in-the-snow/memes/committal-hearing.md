The committal hearing of [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]] before the magistrates represented the formal culmination of the case against the initial suspect in the murder of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]]. The proceedings ended, as the evidence then available dictated, in Trethewy being committed for trial — yet within hours of this outcome, new evidence emerged that would fundamentally alter the course of the investigation.

## The Proceedings

> "After several remands, the proceedings against Trethewy before the magistrates came to a close about the end of February. There was nothing much to note about these proceedings, which ended, as I suppose they must have ended, in his being committed for trial."
> — [007-001]

The narrator's laconic summary — "there was nothing much to note" — reflects the procedural inevitability of the outcome. The case against Trethewy, built on his quarrel with Peters ten days before the murder [003-001], his suspicious behaviour, and the absence of any alternative suspect, was sufficient to satisfy the magistrates that there was a case to answer.

## The Immediate Reversal

The very evening after Trethewy's committal, the case was transformed:

> "But the very evening after his committal, a slight but almost conclusive circumstance was brought to light, and entirely altered the aspect of the case."
> — [007-003]

[[edith-summers|Edith Summers]], Peters' housemaid, visited the narrator and confessed that she had told a falsehood to the police-sergeant on the morning after the murder. She had interrupted the housekeeper to say that the candle by Peters' bed had been a long candle — when in fact it had been a short one, meaning Peters had been dead for hours before the household was roused [007-003].

## The Narrator's Conviction

Even before the committal, the narrator had reached a private conclusion that diverged from the official one:

> "Stupid as it may seem, I had for some time been convinced of Trethewy's innocence, and yet had never really drawn the necessary inference from it. Of course with the two premises in my mind — Peters was murdered, Trethewy did not murder him — I had been aware, in a sense, of the conclusion, but it had taken no hold of my attention."
> — [007-005]

The committal hearing thus marks a crucial dramatic irony: the official legal process commits an innocent man to trial at the very moment when the evidence that will exonerate him is about to surface. The gap between legal truth and actual truth — a central theme of the novel — is at its widest here.

## Aftermath

Following the committal, the narrator received a visit from [[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]], who brought additional information. The narrator pressed the question of Trethewy's release, and learned that "it was being carefully considered, but he could not be set free immediately" [011-007]. Trethewy's eventual release would come, but not before the investigation had been redirected toward its true target.
