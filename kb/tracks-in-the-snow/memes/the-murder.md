"On the morning of the 29th of January, 1896, Eustace Peters was found murdered in his bed at his house, Grenvile Combe, in the parish of Long Wilton, of which I was then rector" [001-001]. This opening sentence establishes the central fact around which the entire narrative revolves. The murder of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] is at once the novel's initiating event and its central mystery — a crime whose solution drives every subsequent action of [[robert-jenkinson|the narrator]].

## Discovery

The discovery of the body was made by Peters' housemaid, who "came running down to the village on her way, as it proved, to the police-station" [002-001]. [[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]] was summoned and arrived at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] shortly after the police-sergeant. The initial examination of the scene established the basic facts: Peters had been stabbed to death in his bed, and there were no immediate signs of forced entry or struggle [002-003].

Crucially, the discovery occurred on a morning when snow had fallen overnight. The [[footprint|footprints]] in the snow became the central piece of physical evidence — and the source of the novel's deepest puzzle.

## The Crime Scene

The [[the-bedroom|bedroom]] at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] was "barely furnished" and "showed no traces of disorder" [002-003]. The absence of disturbance was itself significant: it suggested that Peters had not struggled with his attacker, implying either that he knew his killer or that he was taken entirely by surprise.

The murder weapon was believed to be a knife. The narrator notes that "it appeared afterwards, at the inquest, that there was reason to think that Peters had not had such a knife, for he never locked up drawers or cupboards, and his servants knew all his few possessions well" [002-004] — suggesting the weapon was brought to the scene by the killer.

## The Footprints

The [[footprint|footprints]] in the snow became the investigation's obsessive focus. The narrator records the meticulous examination of tracks between the gate and the front door [002-006], the puzzling absence of certain expected prints, and the controversial question of whether the tracks offered a genuine clue or a deliberate [[false-alarm|false lead]].

The footprint evidence was complicated by several factors: the housemaid's goloshes had left their own marks [002-006], the snow had been disturbed before a thorough examination could be made, and the testimony of various witnesses about the state of the tracks proved contradictory. These complications transformed what should have been straightforward physical evidence into one of the case's most contested questions.

## Initial Suspicion

Suspicion initially fell on [[reuben-trethewy|Reuben Trethewy]], a local man whose circumstances and behaviour made him a convenient suspect. The narrator, however, records his growing conviction that the case against Trethewy was built on insufficient evidence: "I had for some time been convinced of Trethewy's innocence, and yet had never really drawn the necessary inference from it" [007-005].

The wrongful suspicion of Trethewy — and his eventual release — serves as a critique of investigative methods that privilege convenience over careful analysis. It is precisely this dissatisfaction with the official investigation that drives [[robert-jenkinson|Jenkinson]] to pursue his own independent inquiry.

## The Investigation

The murder investigation proceeds along two parallel tracks: the official police inquiry, led by [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], and the private investigation conducted by [[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]]. The tension between these two approaches — one institutional and procedural, the other personal and reflective — is a central dynamic of the novel.

[[james-callaghan|James Callaghan]], with his experience of murder cases in India [003-001], brings a distinctive perspective to the inquiry. His conviction that "the solution of the mystery will occur to some of us, his friends, if a solution ever is found" [016-005] captures the novel's faith in private intelligence over official procedure.

## Significance

The murder of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] is more than a plot device. It is the event that reveals character, that exposes the hidden connections between the novel's figures, and that tests the moral and intellectual resources of those who seek to understand it. The investigation becomes, in [[robert-jenkinson|Jenkinson]]'s hands, not merely a search for a killer but an inquiry into the nature of [[truth|truth]], [[evidence|evidence]], and [[justice|justice]] itself.
