Miss Denison is a figure of central importance to the motive for the murder of [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]], though she never appears directly in the narrative. Her significance emerges in full only during [[the-confrontation|the climactic confrontation]] of Chapter XXI, when [[william-vane-cartwright|William Vane-Cartwright]] reveals that her decision to end their engagement — communicated on the very day of the murder — was the precipitating event that drove him to kill.

## The Romance

Vane-Cartwright first met Miss Denison five years before the events of the novel, when she and her parents were staying at Pau in France. He was at the same hotel:

> "'I loved her, and a great hope came into my life. One begins to weary after a while of toiling just to make money for oneself. For a few days all seemed changed, the whole world was new and bright to me.'"
> — [021-021]

Her father, however, had received information casting doubt on Vane-Cartwright's character — almost certainly relating to his business dealings in the East — and forbade further contact. Vane-Cartwright was deeply wounded.

## The Engagement

More than four years later, Vane-Cartwright encountered Miss Denison again and, "absolutely insisted on an explanation":

> "'I had it out with the father. I satisfied him absolutely. In a few weeks' time I was engaged. For the first time in my life I was happy. That was only a month before I came to Long Wilton.'"
> — [021-022]

Vane-Cartwright noted that Peters had known the Denison family — a connection that would prove fateful.

## The Broken Engagement

On 28 January 1896, the day of the fatal [[dinner-party|supper party]] at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]], Vane-Cartwright received a letter from Lady Denison, Miss Denison's mother:

> "It was in a lady's hand, signed with the name of Lady Denison, the young lady's mother. It appeared to be written in great agitation. Its purport was that the young lady had resolved, so her mother found, to break off her engagement with Vane-Cartwright."
> — [021-028]

The letter revealed that Miss Denison "had formerly loved another man" and had learned — through "an officious lady friend" — that this man still cared for her [021-028]. Her old attachment had returned, and the engagement to Vane-Cartwright was at an end.

Vane-Cartwright received this letter at Peters' house at five o'clock — "seven hours before I killed him" [021-027]. The connection is devastating: Peters, who had known the Denison family and had been Vane-Cartwright's rival in other respects, was sitting in the same room with the man whose hopes of happiness had just been destroyed. The old suspicions, the old resentments, the fresh devastation of the broken engagement — all converged on that January evening at Grenvile Combe.

## Significance

Miss Denison is never seen; she is only described through the reports of others. Yet her absent presence is felt throughout the novel's second half. She is the romantic object whose loss — or the threat of whose loss — drives Vane-Cartwright to his final act of violence. In this sense, she belongs to a pattern in the novel: crucial events are set in motion by figures who remain offstage, their influence felt through letters, through reported speech, through the emotions they inspire in the characters we do see.
