[[long-wilton|Long Wilton]] is the village and parish in rural [[england|England]] that serves as the primary setting of the novel. It is here that [[robert-jenkinson|Robert Jenkinson]] serves as rector [001-001], here that [[eustace-peters|Eustace Peters]] owns [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]] [001-004], and here that the murder and its investigation unfold. The village is not merely a backdrop but an active element of the narrative — its geography, its social structures, and its rhythms of life shape the possibilities and constraints within which the characters act.

## Geography

The village is organised around several key landmarks. The [[the-church|church]] stands on a hill at some distance from the main settlement, "built at some distance from the village for the benefit of the former owners of the manor house" [004-001]. Just beyond the church lies the former manor house, now converted to a hotel [004-001]. The rectory ([[the-rectory|the study]]) overlooks the stream and the church on the hill beyond [004-002].

The road system defines the parish: one road "leads up a steep hill to the church" [004-001], while another "leads to some outlying hamlets which form part of the parish" [004-002]. [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]], Peters' house, stands on the outskirts — close enough to the village to be part of its life, but sufficiently removed to be the scene of an isolated murder.

The narrator observes that the railway company made "a liberal contribution made for the reseating of the church" [004-001], indicating the presence of the [[railway-station|railway]] as a connection to the wider world.

## Social Structure

[[long-wilton|Long Wilton]] embodies the hierarchical social world of late-Victorian rural England. At its centre is the relationship between the rector ([[robert-jenkinson|Jenkinson]]), the gentry (represented by [[eustace-peters|Peters]] at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]]), and the villagers (including the [[reuben-trethewy|Trethewy]] family). The murder disturbs this social order, exposing tensions and connections that the ordinary life of the parish had kept submerged.

Jenkinson's position as rector places him at the intersection of these social strata — he is educated and gentlemanly enough to dine at [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]], yet his pastoral role gives him intimate access to the lives of ordinary villagers. This dual position is essential to his investigative method.

## Narrative Function

The village setting is integral to the novel's method. The limited geographical scope concentrates the drama: all the principal characters are within walking distance of one another, and the physical proximity intensifies the moral and psychological dimensions of the story. The [[footprint|footprints]] in the snow — the central piece of physical evidence — derive their significance precisely from the particular geography of [[long-wilton|Long Wilton]], where the distances between houses, the layout of roads and paths, and the patterns of village life make certain movements explicable and others suspicious.
