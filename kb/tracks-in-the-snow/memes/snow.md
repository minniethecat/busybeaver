The [[snow]] that falls on the night of 28 January 1896 is both a literal weather event and the novel's central symbolic element. It is the snow that preserves the [[footprint|footprints]] which become the investigation's obsessive focus, and it is the snow that [[james-callaghan|Callaghan]] and [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]] dispute about in their conflicting testimonies. The novel's very title — *Tracks in the Snow* — announces snow as the medium through which the mystery is both revealed and complicated.

## Evidentiary Significance

The snowfall creates the conditions for the central piece of physical evidence in the case: the [[footprint|footprints]] leading to and from [[grenvile-combe|Grenvile Combe]]. The narrator records that when he arrived at the house on the morning of the discovery, there were "no footmarks between the gate and the front door except the unmistakable print of the goloshes worn by the housemaid on her way to call the police" [002-006].

This absence of marks is as significant as their presence. The snow should have recorded the passage of anyone entering or leaving the house. That it apparently did not creates the central puzzle of the case: how could the murderer have entered and left without leaving traces in the snow?

The snow's evidentiary value is complicated by two factors. First, the snow had been disturbed before a thorough examination could be conducted. Second, the question of whether it was actually snowing at the critical moments becomes a point of contention between witnesses — [[james-callaghan|Callaghan]] and [[william-vane-cartwright|Vane-Cartwright]] offer conflicting accounts that cannot both be true.

## Symbolic Dimensions

Beyond its evidentiary role, the snow functions symbolically in the novel. Snow covers and conceals — but it also preserves and reveals. The tracks it holds are fragile, liable to be erased by further snowfall or by human disturbance. In this, the snow mirrors the nature of [[evidence|evidence]] itself: it captures a moment that can never be perfectly recovered, a trace that is always subject to interpretation and doubt.

The whiteness of snow — its apparent purity and blankness — also suggests the moral dimension of the investigation. The narrator seeks clarity, a clean determination of guilt or innocence. But the snow, like the case itself, refuses to yield a simple reading.
