Robert Morrison (1782–1834) was the first Protestant missionary to China, a pioneering translator, and the namesake of the [[Morrison Education Society School]] where [[Yung Wing]] received his formative English education. Sent by the [[London Missionary Society]] in 1807, Morrison's linguistic achievements — including the first Anglo-Chinese dictionary and a complete Chinese translation of the Bible — laid the foundations for subsequent Protestant missionary work in China and, indirectly, shaped the trajectory of Sino-American educational exchange.

## Missionary Career

Morrison crossed the Atlantic from London to New York and embarked for China in the sailing vessel *Trident* on January 31, 1807. He initially attempted to land in [[Macao]], but the suspicion of Portuguese Jesuits thwarted him. Forced to proceed to [[Canton]], and later to Malacca due to unsettled relations between the Chinese government and foreign merchants, he established his base of operations there. [002-003]

Morrison was sent out by the London Missionary Society as the first missionary to China. His pioneering role was followed a decade later by the Rev. Icabod J. Roberts, an American missionary. Both men contributed — each in his particular sphere — to imparting a knowledge of Christianity to Hung Siu Chune, who would later lead the [[Taiping Rebellion]]. [011-004]

## Scholarly Legacy

Morrison's two monumental achievements were fundamentally important to the missionary enterprise in China. His Anglo-Chinese dictionary, running to three quarto volumes, and his translation of the Bible into Chinese gave subsequent missionary work a basis to proceed upon. His dictionary and Bible translation enabled later generations of scholars — including Dr. Medhurst, Bishop Boone, Dr. Legge, E. C. Bridgeman, and S. Wells Williams — to perfect the Chinese-English dictionary and to bring Biblical translation to the Chinese standard of literary taste, so as to commend it to the literary classes. [011-004]

Morrison also translated the Emperor Khang Hsi's dictionary into English, demonstrating the bidirectional nature of his scholarly project. His first Chinese convert, Leang Afah, was trained by him to preach and later became a powerful evangelist whose influence contributed to opening a new era in the religious life of China. [011-004]

## The Morrison Education Society

Not long after Morrison's death on August 1, 1834, a circular was issued among foreign residents on January 26, 1835, calling for the formation of an association to be named the "Morrison Education Society." Its object was to "improve and promote English education in China by schools and other means." The Society was named to commemorate Morrison's labors and achievements. [002-003]

The [[Morrison Education Society School]], established under this society, would become the institution where [[Yung Wing]] received his English education under the Rev. [[Samuel Robins Brown]] — a chain of influence that led directly to Yung Wing's journey to America and the [[Chinese Educational Mission]]. As Yung Wing's biography notes, "a university ought to have been permanently founded for that purpose instead of a school, whose existence was solely dependent upon the precarious and ephemeral subscriptions of transient foreign merchants in China."
