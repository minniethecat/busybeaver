# Shanghai

[[Shanghai]] was the pivot point of Yung Wing's professional life in China. More than any other city, it was Shanghai — not [[Canton]], not [[Peking]], not [[Nanking]] — that served as his base of operations during the crucial decade between his return from America in 1855 and his entry into [[Tsang Kwoh Fan]]'s service in 1863.

## A Treaty Port Transformed

By the time Yung Wing arrived in August 1856 [007-008], Shanghai had already begun its transformation from a provincial trading town into the commercial capital of East Asia. The [[Treaty of Nanking]] (1842) had opened the port to foreign trade; the [[First Opium War|Opium Wars]] had shattered the old Canton system; and the [[Treaty Port System]] was redirecting the flow of Chinese commerce through the Yangtze estuary.

Yung Wing found his first employment in the [[Imperial Maritime Customs Service|Imperial Maritime Customs]] at Shanghai:

> "Not long after my arrival in Shanghai, I found a situation in the Imperial Maritime Customs as translator and interpreter." [007-009]

The job gave Yung Wing a front-row view of the treaty port economy — and a lasting distaste for the corruption he observed there.

## Commercial Hub and Social Network

Shanghai's foreign settlement provided the social infrastructure for Yung Wing's career. His circle of friends — Chinese merchants, compradors, interpreters, and returned emigrants — constituted an informal network that proved decisive at key junctures:

| Contact | Role | Connection to Yung Wing |
|---------|------|------------------------|
| Chang Suh Chune | Tsang Kwoh Fan's secretary | Met in Shanghai, 1857 [013-002] |
| Li, Chang, Wha, Chune | Shanghai merchant friends | Hosted Yung Wing at Ngan Kinh [013-020] |
| Tea merchants | Commercial partners | Tea district expedition backers [012-003] |

> "Chang Suh Chune was an old acquaintance I had made in Shanghai in 1857. He was a native of Ningpo and had studied in one of the missionary schools." [013-002]

This Shanghai-based network would later connect Yung Wing to the highest levels of the Qing bureaucracy — a reminder that informal urban networks were as important as formal institutions in late imperial China.

## Base of Operations (1856–1863)

Between 1856 and 1863, Shanghai was Yung Wing's base for a series of ventures:

1. **Customs Service** (1856): Brief but disillusioning stint in the Imperial Maritime Customs [007-009]
2. **Commercial tea trade** (1859–1860): Business dealings that led to the [[Taiping Expedition|Taiping Tea District Expedition]] [012-003]
3. **Taiping expedition launch** (November 1859): Departed Shanghai for the interior [010-002]
4. **Return and illness** (1860): "[I] was obliged to return to Shanghai, where I was laid up sick for two months" [012-012]

> "On the 10th of November, 1859, we left Shanghai in a Woo-Sik-Kwei boat, with a number of other Chinese passengers, to go up the Yangtze." [010-002]

The city was also where Yung Wing learned of his mother's death — a moment recorded with startling brevity: "I was in Shanghai at the time of her death" [006-008].

## Later Significance

After Yung Wing entered imperial service, Shanghai retained its importance. The [[Kiangnan Arsenal]], established in 1867 with machinery Yung Wing purchased in America, was located near the city [015-012]. When the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] students arrived in America, many had passed through Shanghai en route.

Shanghai represented, for Yung Wing, the promise and the peril of China's encounter with the West: a city of opportunity and corruption, of new networks and old habits, of the future pressing against the past.
