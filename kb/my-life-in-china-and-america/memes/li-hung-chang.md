# Li Hung Chang

Li Hung Chang (Li Hongzhang, 1823–1901) was one of the most consequential — and controversial — statesmen of late imperial China. As [[Viceroy of Zhili]] and the [[Qing Imperial Government|Qing empire]]'s chief diplomat, he shaped China's response to Western pressure for over three decades. For [[Yung Wing]] and the [[Chinese Educational Mission]], Li was the indispensable political gatekeeper: he could sustain the Mission or, as ultimately happened, acquiesce to its destruction.

## A Different Kind of Leader

Yung Wing's assessment of Li Hung Chang is characteristically sharp and draws a direct contrast with his mentor:

> "Li Hung Chang was of an altogether different type from his patron. While Tsang Kwoh Fan was magnanimous and broad-minded, Li was shrewd, self-seeking, and essentially a politician. Tsang was a statesman; Li was an astute diplomat and courtier." [017-011]

This judgment frames everything that follows. Where [[Tsang Kwoh Fan]] acted from conviction, Li calculated from political necessity. Where Tsang envisioned decades ahead, Li managed the exigencies of the moment.

## Relationship with Yung Wing

Li first appears in Yung Wing's narrative as a military subordinate to Tsang during the suppression of the Anwhui rebels [015-006]. After [[Taiping Rebellion|the Taiping suppression]], Li's star rose rapidly. He succeeded Tsang as Viceroy of Zhili and became the Qing court's indispensable man for dealing with foreigners [017-010].

Yung Wing's dealings with Li Hung Chang fall into two distinct phases:

| Phase | Years | Dynamic | Key Events |
|-------|-------|---------|------------|
| Cooperation | 1867–1875 | Productive partnership | Machinery purchase, Arsenal expansion |
| Tension | 1876–1881 | Political drift | Mission defense, treaty disputes |
| Drift | 1894–1898 | Distant alignment | War reforms, final audience |

### The Machinery Mission (1867)

When Yung Wing's machinery-purchasing trip to America proved successful, Li Hung Chang's cooperation was crucial:

> "In 1867, Viceroy Tsang Kwoh Fan, with Li Hung Chang's cooperation, succeeded in erecting the Kiangnan Arsenal in Shanghai — the first of its kind in China." [015-012]

### The Mission's Political Defender — and Undoer

By the late 1870s, conservative opposition to the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] was intensifying. Li Hung Chang held the Mission's fate in his hands:

> "A Chinese commissioner was appointed, to which Viceroy Li Hung Chang acceded without thinking of the consequences to the Educational Mission." [019-008]

When the Mission was recalled in 1881, Li did not fight for it. Yung Wing's disappointment is palpable:

> "I did put the question to Viceroy Li Hung Chang first, who, instead of standing by the Educational Mission as he should, gave it the cold shoulder." [019-016]

## Partial Redemption: The 1894–95 Crisis

China's catastrophic defeat in the [[Sino-Japanese War|First Sino-Japanese War]] of 1894–95 vindicated Yung Wing's long-standing warnings about the need for modernization:

> "The incompetence and corruption of Li Hung Chang, whose defeat both on land and sea in the war with Japan in 1894–95 demonstrated the futility of his boastful claims of military preparedness." [021-010]

The war's outcome led Li to seek Yung Wing's counsel once more — a final, bittersweet acknowledgment of what might have been:

> "While paying my visit to Li Hung Chang in Tientsin, before going up to Peking, I unfolded my plans for the creation of a national bank and an imperial railway system." [020-004]

## Assessment

Yung Wing's verdict on Li Hung Chang is perhaps best summarized by the structural contrast he draws throughout his memoir:

- **Tsang Kwoh Fan**: "had already made up his mind what he wanted of me" [021-011]
- **Li Hung Chang**: "of an altogether different type" — shrewd, political, ultimately unreliable [017-011]

Yet the historical record offers a more balanced view. Li Hung Chang operated in a far more constrained political environment than Tsang enjoyed. The conservative backlash of the 1870s made it nearly impossible for any single official to protect the Mission. Li chose political survival over a doomed defense of the program — a decision that was characteristic, if not admirable.
