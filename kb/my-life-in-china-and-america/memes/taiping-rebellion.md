# Taiping Rebellion

## Overview

The Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864) was one of the deadliest civil wars in human history, led by Hong Xiuquan who claimed to be the younger brother of Jesus Christ. The rebellion established a rival capital at Nanking and controlled large portions of southern China before its eventual suppression.

## Yung Wing's Visit to the Taipings

In 1860, Yung Wing undertook a perilous journey to the Taiping-controlled region, visiting their capital at Nanking to assess the movement firsthand (010-001). He met with several Taiping leaders, including the Kan Wang (Shield King), and presented a series of reform proposals.

## Reform Proposals

During his audience with the Taiping leadership, Yung Wing proposed seven reforms including: the organization of a modern army, the establishment of a naval force, a modern banking system, a graded school system, and the prohibition of the opium habit (011-013). While the Taiping leaders expressed interest, Yung Wing concluded they were incapable of implementing meaningful reform.

## Historical Significance

Yung Wing's reflections on the Taiping Rebellion provide a unique firsthand perspective on the movement. He recognized both its potential as a force for Chinese regeneration and its fatal flaws in leadership and ideology (011-001).
