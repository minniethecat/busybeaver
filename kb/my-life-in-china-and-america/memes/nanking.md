# Nanking

Nanking (Nanjing) occupies a singular place in Yung Wing's memoir: it is simultaneously the headquarters of the [[Taiping Rebellion]] he investigated in 1860 and the viceregal seat of his patron [[Tsang Kwoh Fan]] a decade later. Few cities in the narrative undergo such a dramatic symbolic reversal — from rebel capital to the center of Qing restoration.

## The Journey to the Taiping Capital (1860)

In November 1859, Yung Wing joined two American missionaries on a journey up the Yangtze to visit the [[Taiping Heavenly Kingdom|Taiping]] rebels at their capital:

> "On the 10th of November, 1859, we left Shanghai… We were to visit the Taiping rebels in Nanking. I was asked to join them, and I accepted the invitation." [010-002]

The journey was arduous and dangerous. The party had to navigate through active war zones where both imperial and rebel forces preyed on travelers:

> "After a long and wearisome journey, we reached Nanking. I was the first one to reach the city gate." [010-016]

## Inside the Heavenly Capital

What Yung Wing found in Nanking defied easy characterization. The Taipings had renamed the city Tianjing (Heavenly Capital) and established an administration that blended Christian millenarianism with Chinese imperial forms. Yung Wing met with [[Kan Wang]], the most intellectually curious of the Taiping princes, and presented a series of reform proposals.

> "Before Hung Jin came to Nanking, I had made his acquaintance, in 1856, at Hong Kong." [010-018]

Hung Jin (the Kan Wang) asked Yung Wing directly what he thought of the Taiping cause — a question that put Yung Wing in a delicate position, as he was effectively a guest in a rebel capital.

## A Verdict Rendered

Yung Wing's assessment of the Taipings, formed during his Nanking visit, was unambiguous:

> "My Nanking visit was utterly barren of any practical results so far as the regeneration of China was concerned. I came to the conclusion that the Taiping rebellion had not in it the elements of a permanent government." [012-002]

He judged the movement to be religiously sincere but politically incompetent — a mass uprising capable of destruction but incapable of construction:

> "On my way back to Shanghai, I had ample time to form an estimate of the Taiping Rebellion — its origin, character, and its probable outcome." [010-031]

## From Rebel Capital to Viceregal Seat

The symbolic arc of Nanking in Yung Wing's narrative is completed by the city's capture by imperial forces in 1864:

> "Soon after came the final suppression of the Taiping Rebellion by the fall and capture of Nanking." [013-019]

Tsang Kwoh Fan subsequently established his viceregal headquarters in the same city that had been the rebel capital. It was to Nanking that Yung Wing traveled for his long interview with Tsang about the future of the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] [017-008]. And it was in Nanking that Tsang died in 1872:

> "I felt the keenest sense of personal loss by the death of Viceroy Tsang Kwoh Fan, who died in Nanking at the ripe age of seventy-one." [017-010]
