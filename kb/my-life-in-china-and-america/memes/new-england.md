New England — the six northeastern states of the United States — was the geographical and cultural crucible of [[Yung Wing]]'s American experience. From his arrival in 1847 to his graduation from [[Yale College]] in 1854, and again during the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] (1872–1881), New England's educational institutions, religious culture, and family life shaped both Yung Wing personally and the 120 Chinese students he brought to study in America.

## Yung Wing's Education in New England

Yung Wing's first sustained exposure to New England culture came through his education. At [[Monson Academy]] in Massachusetts, he encountered the region's preparatory school tradition. Monson was, at one time, "quite a noted preparatory school in New England, before high schools sprang into existence," attracting young men from all parts of the country preparing for college. [004-002]

The region's religious character also made a deep impression. The Rev. Shubael Bartlett, whom Yung Wing met in East Windsor, was "a genuine type of the old New England Puritan" — exact, precise, deliberate, and solemn, but also gentle and thoughtful beneath his austere exterior. [003-007] Mrs. Phoebe H. Brown, another figure Yung Wing encountered, he described as "a remarkable New England woman — a woman of surpassing strength of moral and religious character," who "went through the crucible of unprecedented adversities" and emerged as "one of the rare shining lights that beautify the New England sky." [004-007]

## The Chinese Educational Mission in New England

When the first installment of thirty Chinese students arrived in 1872, Yung Wing — acting on the advice of B. G. Northrop, Connecticut's Commissioner of Education — decided to distribute the students among New England families, "either by twos or fours to each family, where they could be cared for and at the same time instructed, till they were able to join classes in graded schools." [017-012]

Yung Wing chose [[Springfield]], Massachusetts as his headquarters, considering it "the most central point from which to distribute the students in New England." [017-012] Over the following nine years, 120 Chinese boys lived with New England families, attended local schools, and absorbed American customs. Yung Wing observed with satisfaction how these students grew "in knowledge and stature under New England influence, culture and environment." [019-008]

In 1864, during the last year of the Civil War, Yung Wing found that "nearly all the machine shops in the country, especially in New England, were preoccupied and busy in executing government orders," making it difficult to have the [[Kiangnan Arsenal]] machinery manufactured — an indication of the region's industrial centrality. [014-003]

## Enduring Connection

Yung Wing's attachment to New England persisted throughout his life. In his later years, he reflected that "to New England I am bound by ties of affection formed in my youth," and he placed his own sons with "one of the best families in New England," confident they would receive the same nurturing environment he had experienced decades earlier. [021-007] The "light that shone from America and from New England" remained, in his view, a beacon for China's modernization. [023-030]
