# Tsang Kwoh Fan

Tsang Kwoh Fan (Zeng Guofan, 1811–1872) was the [[Viceroy of Zhili|Viceroy of the Two Kiang]] and one of the most powerful officials of the late [[Qing Imperial Government|Qing dynasty]]. As generalissimo of the imperial forces that crushed the [[Taiping Rebellion]], he earned the gratitude of the throne — and the political capital to pursue the [[Self-Strengthening Movement]]. For [[Yung Wing]], he was the decisive patron who turned a decade of frustrated ambition into the [[Chinese Educational Mission]].

## Character and Leadership

Yung Wing's firsthand impression captures both the man's presence and his managerial philosophy:

> "[Tsang Kwoh Fan] was a most remarkable character in many respects. Physically, he was a man of strong build and robust health. He had a long flowing beard which gave him a patriarchal appearance. His eyes were keen and penetrating. He was a great judge of men and seldom made mistakes in the selection of his lieutenants." [013-006]

Tsang's genius lay not in personal brilliance alone but in his ability to identify and empower talent — a quality that explains why he, alone among senior Qing officials, immediately grasped the value of Yung Wing's Western education.

> "I soon came to be grateful to the genius of Tsang Kwoh Fan, who was instrumental in restoring peace and order to the country after the Taiping Rebellion. His administrative ability and foresight were remarkable." [013-019]

## Patron of the Educational Mission

### The First Meeting (1863)

Yung Wing was summoned to meet Tsang in July 1863 after his friend Chang Suh Chune, one of Tsang's secretaries, described Yung Wing's background and ambitions [013-004]. Tsang's first question was characteristically direct: what could Yung Wing do for China?

> "I felt a strong desire on the part of Tsang Kwoh Fan to see me. So in July, 1863, I left Kiukiang, went down to Ngan Kinh where his Excellency had his military headquarters." [013-005]

The meeting proved transformative. Tsang swiftly authorized Yung Wing to travel to America to purchase machinery for what would become the [[Kiangnan Arsenal]] — a concrete first step in China's industrial modernization [014-005].

### Championing the Mission

It was Tsang Kwoh Fan who, in 1870 at [[Tientsin]], conceived the plan to send Chinese students to America for Western education [020-002]. As president of the Board of Commissioners, he drafted the memorial to the throne that secured imperial approval:

> "Besides Viceroy Tsang Kwoh Fan, whom I counted upon to back the enterprise, there were few high officials who understood its significance." [016-003]

The four commissioners appointed included Tsang Kwoh Fan himself, Mow Chung Hsi, Liu —, and the governor of Kiang Su [016-020].

## Death and Aftermath

Tsang died at [[Nanking]] in the winter of 1872, shortly after the Mission's first cohort had departed for America:

> "I felt the keenest sense of personal loss by the death of Viceroy Tsang Kwoh Fan, who died in Nanking at the ripe age of seventy-one." [017-010]

His death removed the Mission's most powerful protector. With Tsang gone, conservative elements at court — who had always viewed the project with suspicion — began to systematically undermine it [019-008]. His protégé [[Li Hung Chang]] inherited responsibility for the Mission's political defense but lacked Tsang's singular commitment to it [017-011].

## Legacy

Tsang Kwoh Fan's legacy is threefold:

1. **Military unification**: His defeat of the Taiping Rebellion restored Qing authority and created the conditions for reform [013-019]
2. **Industrial modernization**: Through the [[Kiangnan Arsenal]] and related projects, he launched China's Self-Strengthening Movement [013-024]
3. **Educational exchange**: The [[Chinese Educational Mission]], his most far-sighted initiative, sent 120 students to America and seeded a generation of modernizers

> "Those who in future may profit by having studied abroad owe a lasting monument to commemorate Tsang Kwoh Fan's broadmindedness as well as his devotion to the welfare of China." [013-024]
