# Chinese Educational Commission

The Chinese Educational Commission was the formal administrative body created by the [[Qing Imperial Government]] to oversee the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] in the United States. It was, in effect, the first overseas educational agency in Chinese history — a bureaucratic experiment as unprecedented as the student program it administered.

## Structure and Personnel

The Commission was constituted in 1870 at [[Tientsin]], with four appointed members:

> "The four commissioners appointed were: Viceroy Tsang Kwoh Fan, Mow Chung Hsi, Liu —, and myself." [016-020]

Of these, only two were assigned to accompany the students to America: [[Chin Lan Pin]] as head commissioner and [[Yung Wing]] as associate commissioner [023-006].

| Commissioner | Role | Location |
|-------------|------|----------|
| Tsang Kwoh Fan | President (in China) | Nanking / Tientsin [017-003] |
| Mow Chung Hsi | Commissioner (in China) | Peking |
| Chin Lan Pin | Head Commissioner (in America) | Hartford, CT [023-006] |
| Yung Wing | Associate Commissioner (in America) | Hartford, CT [023-006] |

## Responsibilities

The Commission's mandate was broad and, in practice, often ambiguous — spanning educational administration, diplomatic representation, and cultural supervision:

1. **Student placement**: Arranging host families and school admissions for 30 new students each year
2. **Financial administration**: Managing the Mission's budget and disbursements to host families and schools
3. **Supplementary instruction**: Providing Chinese classical education at the Hartford headquarters [019-014]
4. **Discipline and oversight**: Monitoring student behavior, dress, and moral development
5. **Reporting to Peking**: Regular memorials to the throne on Mission progress [019-014]

## The Yung Wing – Chin Lan Pin Dynamic

The relationship between the two American-based commissioners was central to the Commission's functioning — and a source of recurring tension. Chin Lan Pin, a traditional scholar-official, viewed the students' Americanization with increasing alarm. Yung Wing, who had himself undergone the same transformation, saw it as natural and largely beneficial.

> "Chin Lan Pin was a man of Confucian training. He came to America with all the prejudices of a Chinese scholar against foreign ideas, and the work of the Mission suffered in consequence." [019-008]

The structural asymmetry was telling: Chin Lan Pin, as head commissioner, had formal authority; but Yung Wing, as the Mission's originator and the only commissioner who understood American society, had the practical knowledge needed to operate the program. The resulting friction made the Commission vulnerable to political manipulation from Peking.

## The Commission's Demise

When the Mission was terminated in 1881, the Commission was dissolved along with it. Its records and property were transferred back to China, and its American headquarters in [[Hartford]] was sold. The abrupt end — after nearly a decade of operation — left unfinished the Commission's most important work: the education of the final cohort of students, many of whom were only midway through their American schooling.

## Legacy

The Chinese Educational Commission established a precedent — however short-lived — that would be revived in the 20th century: the idea that a sovereign government could maintain a permanent educational presence abroad. Later Chinese scholarship programs, including the Boxer Indemnity Scholarship Program (1909) and subsequent state-sponsored study-abroad initiatives, would follow the template the Commission pioneered.

> "The Chinese Educational Mission — the head commissioner was Chin Lan Pin. The associate commissioner was Yung Wing. The idea was born, the dream was dreamed, in the heart and mind of Yung Wing." [023-006]
