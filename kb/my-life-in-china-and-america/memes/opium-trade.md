# Opium Trade in China

## Overview

The opium trade was a central feature of 19th-century Sino-British relations. British merchants, primarily through the East India Company, imported vast quantities of opium from India into China, creating widespread addiction and a severe trade imbalance that led to military conflict (020-006).

## Role in the Opium Wars

The Qing government's efforts to suppress the opium trade led directly to the First Opium War (1839–1842) and the Second Opium War (1856–1860). These conflicts resulted in the opening of treaty ports, extraterritoriality for foreigners, and the cession of Hong Kong to Britain.

## Impact on Yung Wing's Life

Yung Wing was born during the height of the opium crisis. His father's death in 1840, while the Opium War was still ongoing, left the family destitute (001-014). The war's aftermath created the conditions that allowed foreign missionaries and educators to operate in China, which in turn enabled Yung Wing's Western education.

Yung Wing later noted with disapproval the opium habit among the Taiping rebels, listing its prohibition as one of his recommended reforms (011-013).
