San Francisco was the Pacific gateway through which [[Yung Wing]] passed during his circumnavigation of the globe in 1864–1865. As the primary American port facing Asia before the completion of the transcontinental railroad, it was a critical transit point for 19th-century travel between the United States and China.

## Yung Wing's Transit

In early 1865, after overseeing the manufacture of machinery for the [[Kiangnan Arsenal]] in Fitchburg, Massachusetts, Yung Wing decided to complete his circumnavigation of the globe by returning to China via the Pacific rather than the Atlantic. The transcontinental Union Pacific Railroad from Chicago to San Francisco via Omaha was not yet completed, and no steamship line subsidized by the United States government crossed the Pacific to any seaport in Japan or China at that time. Yung Wing was therefore obliged to take a circuitous route: a coast steamer from New York to Panama, across the Isthmus, and then another coast steamer up the Mexican coast to San Francisco. [015-002]

At San Francisco, Yung Wing was detained for two weeks waiting for a vessel to carry him across the Pacific to either Yokohama or Shanghai. With no other vessel advertised to sail for the East, he took passage on board the *Ida de Rogers*, a Nantucket bark, paying $500 for passage from San Francisco to Yokohama. [015-003]

## Significance

San Francisco in the 1860s was still a relatively young American city, having grown explosively during the California Gold Rush (1848–1855). For Yung Wing, it represented the western terminus of his American experience — the counterpart to [[New England]], where he had been educated, and [[New York]], where he had landed in 1847. The city's role as a Pacific crossroads foreshadowed the deepening transpacific connections that the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] would later embody.
