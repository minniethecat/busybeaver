# Establishment of the Chinese Educational Mission

The establishment of the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] in 1870–1872 was the decisive political breakthrough of Yung Wing's career. After more than a decade of seeking a patron powerful enough to realize his vision, the deaths of thousands in the [[Tientsin]] Massacre of 1870 unexpectedly created the conditions for approval. What followed was a race against time — to launch the first cohort of students before the fragile political consensus dissolved.

## The Tientsin Catalyst

The Tientsin Massacre of 1870 — in which a French consul and several missionaries were killed — created a diplomatic crisis that brought the Qing court's senior leadership together in [[Tientsin]] to negotiate with the French [017-003]. Among them was [[Tsang Kwoh Fan]], who used the occasion to advance the educational plan:

> "In the winter of 1870, Tsang Kwoh Fan, after having settled the Tientsin imbroglio, returned to Nanking, his headquarters as the viceroy of the Two Kiang. It was during his stay in Tientsin that the four commissioners — Tsang Kwoh Fan, Mow Chung Hsi, Liu — and myself — were appointed." [017-003]

The plan that Tsang memorialized to the throne was drafted with Yung Wing's direct input and proposed sending 120 students, in four annual cohorts of 30, to study in America for fifteen years [016-020].

The four commissioners appointed were:

> "Besides Viceroy Tsang Kwoh Fan, whom I counted upon to back the enterprise, there were few high officials who understood its significance." [016-003]

The memorial emphasized practical benefits — the need for Western technical knowledge in military and industrial modernization — rather than the broader cultural transformation Yung Wing privately envisioned. This strategic framing was essential to securing approval from a conservative court.

## The First Cohort (1872)

With imperial approval secured, the practical work of recruitment and logistics began in earnest. Yung Wing and [[Chin Lan Pin]], the other commissioner appointed to accompany the students, faced the challenge of convincing Chinese families to send their young sons abroad for over a decade:

> "The Chinese Educational Mission — a brief outline is the Chinese Educational Mission to the United States. The head commissioner was Chin Lan Pin. The associate commissioner was Yung Wing." [023-006]

The first cohort of 30 students departed China in 1872, arriving in [[San Francisco]] before traveling across America to their headquarters in [[Hartford]], Connecticut. The Mission's headquarters building, constructed specifically for the purpose, became the administrative and educational center of the program [019-014].

## The Fragile Foundation

Even at the moment of its establishment, the Mission's political foundation was fragile. It depended on the personal prestige of Tsang Kwoh Fan — and Tsang died in 1872, the very year the first students departed [017-010]. Without his protection, the Mission would depend on the far less reliable support of [[Li Hung Chang]] [017-011], and on the hope that conservative opposition at court would not coalesce against it.

That hope proved unfounded. Within a decade, the Mission would be terminated. But in the winter of 1870–1872, Yung Wing had achieved what had seemed impossible: the Qing dynasty, ancient and resistant to change, had agreed to send its sons to learn from the West.
