# Christian Missions in China

The Christian missionary enterprise in 19th-century China was, for [[Yung Wing]], the decisive force that shaped his life trajectory. Without the missionary schools of [[Macao]] and the [[Morrison Education Society School]], there would have been no Yale graduate, no [[Chinese Educational Mission]], and no memoir to record the transformation. Yung Wing acknowledged this debt explicitly and repeatedly:

> "I owe it to the kindness of those missionaries and Christian friends who from pure motives of Christian philanthropy aided me in my early education and prepared me for my college course in America." [002-011]

## Origins and Expansion

Christian missionary activity in China expanded dramatically after the [[First Opium War]] (1839–1842) and the [[Treaty of Nanking]] (1842), which opened five treaty ports to foreign residence. The [[Treaty Port System]] provided the legal framework within which missionaries could operate — a fact that inextricably linked Christian evangelism with Western imperialism in Chinese perception.

The missionary enterprise took three primary institutional forms:

| Institution Type | Examples | Impact on Yung Wing |
|-----------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Schools** | Morrison Education Society School, Gutzlaff School | Provided Yung Wing's entire formal education before Yale |
| **Hospitals** | Benjamin Hobson's Macao hospital | Sheltered Yung Wing after his father's death [001-017] |
| **Churches** | Union Chapel, Shanghai | Site of key encounters and social connections [008-003] |

## The Gutzlaff School (1835–1842)

Yung Wing's first contact with Western education came through [[Mrs. Gutzlaff|Mrs. Karl Gutzlaff]], wife of the Prussian missionary [[Charles Gutzlaff]]. In 1834, under the auspices of the [[Ladies Association in London]], she established a school in Macao that enrolled Yung Wing at the age of seven [001-004]:

> "Mrs. Gutzlaff was a woman of exceptional Christian virtues. She had an even and a gentle disposition, and was respected and beloved for her high Christian character and unceasing activity in the cause of education." [004-003]

The school was a formative influence not only for its English instruction but for the moral example it set — Yung Wing's description of Mrs. Gutzlaff's "high Christian character" recurs throughout his memoir as a touchstone of the missionary ideal.

## The Morrison Education Society School (1842–1847)

After the Gutzlaff school closed, Yung Wing transferred to the Morrison Education Society School, founded by Protestant missionaries in memory of [[Robert Morrison]], the first Protestant missionary to China. Under the leadership of [[Samuel Robins Brown|Reverend Samuel Robins Brown]], this school provided the rigorous Western education that qualified Yung Wing for study in America [002-015].

> "The Morrison Education Society School was established in Macao in 1839, principally through the exertions of the Rev. Dr. E.C. Bridgman." [002-015]

It was Brown who, in 1847, offered to take Yung Wing and two other students — [[Wong Shing]] and [[Wong Foon]] — to America for further study. This offer changed everything.

## Yung Wing's Conversion

Yung Wing became a Christian during his years at [[Monson Academy]] in Massachusetts:

> "When he became a Christian, at Monson, he heard and at once obeyed the voice of God, calling him to devote his life to the regeneration of China." [023-010]

His faith was not merely personal piety but the wellspring of his life's mission. The memoir's preface describes his "supreme ambition" as arising "from Christian motives, to set his country free" [023-021].

## Ambivalent Legacy

The missionary enterprise in China left a complex legacy. It provided the educational infrastructure that produced China's first generation of Western-educated modernizers — yet it was also inextricably entangled with the [[Unequal Treaties]] and the humiliation they represented. Yung Wing himself was clear-eyed about this tension: he acknowledged his personal debt to missionaries while recognizing that Christianity had been "sifted through the medium of imperfect human agencies" [011-010] in its transmission to China.

The [[Taiping Rebellion]] represented the darkest expression of this ambivalence — a movement inspired by a distorted form of Christianity that ultimately killed millions [011-016].
