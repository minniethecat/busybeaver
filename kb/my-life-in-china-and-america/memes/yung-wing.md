Yung Wing (1828–1912) was the first Chinese student to graduate from an American university, a diplomat, reformer, and the driving force behind the [[Chinese Educational Mission]] (1872–1881). His autobiography stands as a landmark account of cross-cultural encounter between Qing China and the 19th-century United States.

## Overview

Born near [[Macao]] on Pedro Island in 1828, Yung Wing's life spanned the tumultuous decades of the late Qing Dynasty. Through missionary education and determined personal effort, he became the first Chinese graduate of [[Yale College]] (Class of 1854), converted to Christianity, and later served as a commissioner and diplomat for the Qing government. His most enduring legacy is the [[Chinese Educational Mission]], which sent 120 Chinese boys to study in the United States between 1872 and 1881. [001-002] [023-006]

> "Yung Wing is poor but proud. Poverty and pride are often found under the same coat." — as recalled by a classmate at Monson Academy [008-012]

## Early Life and Education

Yung Wing was born in 1828 in Nanping village on Pedro Island, part of the Portuguese colony of [[Macao]]. His father, [[Yung Wing Father|a farmer of modest means]], sent him to missionary schools rather than traditional Chinese academies — a decision that would reshape the trajectory of Sino-American relations. [001-002]

At the age of seven, Yung Wing entered Mrs. Gutzlaff's school in Macao, where he received his first instruction in English under the tutelage of [[Mrs. Gutzlaff|Mrs. Karl Gutzlaff]], the wife of missionary [[Charles Gutzlaff]]. [001-017]

> "If you want your mother to let you go to the Morrison School, I would like to have you come." — early invitation to the Morrison School [001-017]

He subsequently attended the Morrison Education Society School under the guidance of [[Samuel Robins Brown]], who would become a lifelong mentor. When Brown returned to the United States in 1847, he brought Yung Wing and two other Chinese students — [[Wong Shing]] and [[Wong Foon]] — to continue their education in America. [001-017]

Yung Wing enrolled at Monson Academy in Massachusetts before entering Yale College, where he graduated in 1854 as the first Chinese national to earn a degree from an American university. [P03-001]

## Career and Public Service

After returning to China in 1855, Yung Wing navigated a complex career path through the final decades of the Qing Dynasty. He worked variously as a translator, a tea merchant, and an intermediary between Chinese officials and Western interests. [023-025]

> "Yung Wing is a foreign educated Chinese — one who has seen the world, and knows the secret of Western power." [015-008]

His most significant achievement was persuading the Qing government to sponsor the [[Chinese Educational Mission]]. With the backing of Viceroy [[Tsang Kwoh Fan]] and later [[Li Hung Chang]], Yung Wing was appointed Commissioner of the mission. On December 11, 1871, he received formal authorization to establish the educational exchange program. [023-033] [023-006]

The years of the Educational Mission were, as Yung Wing described them, "years of great trial." Despite political opposition from conservative Qing officials, he persevered in placing 120 young Chinese students in American schools and homes across New England. [023-025]

As a Christian convert, Yung Wing's faith also shaped his worldview and his relationships with American missionary networks. [023-007]

## Relationship with Key Figures

Yung Wing's career was sustained by a network of patrons, mentors, and allies who bridged Chinese and American circles. [[Samuel Robins Brown]] guided his early education and facilitated his journey to America. [[Tsang Kwoh Fan]], the formidable Viceroy who had suppressed the [[Taiping Rebellion]], became Yung Wing's most influential Qing patron. [[Li Hung Chang]], as Viceroy of Zhili, championed the Educational Mission within the imperial bureaucracy. [023-006] [023-012]

His family life also reflected his trans-Pacific identity: he married [[Marie Yung|Marie Kellogg]], an American woman from Connecticut, with whom he had children including [[Morrison Brown Yung]]. [023-007]

## Historical Significance

Yung Wing occupies a singular place in modern Chinese history. As the first Western-educated Chinese graduate, he embodied the possibilities and tensions of China's encounter with the West. The [[Chinese Educational Mission]] he orchestrated, though prematurely recalled in 1881, seeded a generation of Chinese leaders — including the future premier Tang Shaoyi and the railroad engineer [[Wong Foon]].

His autobiography, published in 1909, offers a first-hand account of 19th-century China from the perspective of someone who moved between two worlds. It remains a primary source for scholars of the [[Self-Strengthening Movement]], Sino-American relations, and Chinese educational reform. [023-035] [025-004]
