# Angels

## Definition

Angels, as they appear in [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, are created spiritual beings who serve as God's messengers and ministers. They appear at pivotal moments in the gospel narrative — announcing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s birth, attending His resurrection — and their consistent refusal to receive [[worship|worship]] forms a crucial link in Moody's apologetic argument for the deity of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Unlike [[jesus-christ|Christ]], who receives worship without rebuke, angels "would not allow" worship offered to them, demonstrating that worship is the prerogative of God alone (005-031).

## Moody's Teaching

### Angels Refuse Worship

Moody's most sustained treatment of angels comes in his argument for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s deity. In Revelation 22, John falls at the feet of the angel to worship, but the angel immediately refuses: "We see here that even that angel would not allow John to worship him. [[eve|Eve]]n an angel from heaven! And if Gabriel came down here from the presence of God it would be a sin to worship him, or any seraph, or any cherub, or Michael, or any archangel" (005-031). The point is clear: the highest created being is still a creature, and to worship a creature is idolatry. Yet [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] accepted worship repeatedly — therefore He is not a creature but the Creator.

> We see here that even that angel would not allow John to worship him. Even an angel from heaven! And if Gabriel came down here from the presence of God it would be a sin to worship him, or any seraph, or any cherub, or Michael, or any archangel.
>
> (005-031)

### Angels Announce the Saviour

When the angel came to proclaim [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s birth, his message defined the Child's identity and mission: "He shall be called Jesus, for He shall save His people from their sins" (008-002). At Bethlehem, "the angels said to the shepherds on the plains of Bethlehem, 'Behold, I bring you good tidings of great joy, which shall be to all people: for unto you is born this day, in the city of [[david|David]], a Saviour, which is [[jesus-christ|Christ]] the Lord'" (008-003). The angels' role is to direct attention away from themselves and toward [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

### The Angel at the Sepulchre

After the resurrection, "the angel at the sepulchre says, 'Tell His disciples, and Peter'" (009-037). Moody draws attention to the tender specificity of this message: "The Lord did not forget Peter, though Peter had denied Him thrice; so He caused this kindly special message to be conveyed to the repentant disciple" (009-037). The angel functions not as an object of attention but as a messenger of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s restoring grace toward His fallen disciple.

### Angels and the Believer's Security

Moody cites Romans 8:38–39 to assure believers that no spiritual power can separate them from God's [[love-of-god|love]]: "I am persuaded that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor powers … shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in [[jesus-christ|Christ Jesus]] our Lord" (007-038–039). [[eve|Eve]]n the highest created powers — angels included — cannot sever the bond between [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and His people. The believer's security rests on something stronger than any creaturely opposition.

## Biblical Foundation

Moody's references to angels draw from Revelation 22:8–9 (the angel's refusal of worship), Luke 2:10–11 (the angelic announcement of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s birth), Matthew 1:21 (the angel naming Jesus), Mark 16:7 (the angel at the sepulchre), and Romans 8:38–39 (angels cannot separate believers from God's love). The consistent pattern is that angels are servants, not objects of devotion — and their subordinate status magnifies the supremacy of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].
