# The Gospel

## Definition

The gospel, in [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s usage, is the good news of God's [[love-of-god|love]] toward sinners, centered on the person and work of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], and demanding the twofold response of [[repentance|repentance]] and [[faith|faith]]. It is not merely a set of propositions but a living message with power to transform: "[[blessed|Blessed]] Gospel; blessed, precious, news" (001-046). The gospel operates as light dispelling darkness — as illustrated by Moody's story of the [[chicago|Chicago]] prodigal: "As I unfolded the [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]]s and told him how God had loved him all the time … the light of the Gospel broke into his mind, and he went away rejoicing" (001-005).

## Moody's Teaching

### The Gospel's Demand: Repentance and Faith

Moody follows the apostolic pattern in presenting the gospel as requiring a moral response. "A man is not prepared to believe or to receive the Gospel, unless he is ready to repent of his sins and turn from them" (006-002). From [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] — "[[repentance|Repent]] ye; for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (006-002) — to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own preaching — "[[repentance|Repent]]; for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (006-003) — to the apostolic commission to "repent, and believe the Gospel" (006-003), the consistent biblical pattern is clear. "It was this preaching — [[repentance|Repent]], and believe the Gospel — that wrought such marvellous results then" (006-003).

### The Gospel's Universality

"God now commandeth all men, everywhere, to repent" (006-015; Acts 17:30). Moody insists the gospel call extends to every person without exception. "Man has no one to blame but himself if he does not repent and believe the Gospel" (006-015). The universality of the command implies the universality of human need: "There are some perhaps who think themselves righteous; and that, therefore, there is no need for them to repent and believe the Gospel" (006-035) — but these self-righteous persons, like the Pharisee in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s parable, are in the greatest danger. "'There is none righteous; no, not one.' 'All have sinned, and come short of the glory of God'" (006-035).

### The Gospel's Purpose: Belief and Life

Moody points to John's stated purpose for writing his Gospel: "These are written, that ye might believe that Jesus is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the Son of God, and that, believing, ye might have life through His name" (007-014). The gospel aims at producing [[faith|faith]] — specifically, faith that Jesus is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the Son of God — and through that faith, life. Moody notes the remarkable prominence of the word "believe" in John's Gospel: "In his Gospel there are only two chapters in which the word 'believe' does not occur. With these two exceptions, every chapter in John is 'Believe! Believe!! Believe!!!'" (007-014).

### The Gospel as Power

The gospel is not merely informative but transformative. Moody tells of a young lawyer in Vermont who was asked by his employer's wife, "I want you to go to class-meeting with me tonight and become a [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian, so that you can conduct family worship while my husband is away" (006-016). The story illustrates that receiving the gospel leads to a changed life — one marked by public [[confession]] and the practice of family worship.

## Biblical Foundation

The gospel message is grounded in the preaching of [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] (Matthew 3:2), [[jesus-christ|Christ]] (Matthew 4:17; Mark 1:15), Peter at [[pentecost|Pentecost]] (Acts 2:38–47), and Paul before the Athenians (Acts 17:30). Its purpose is stated in John 20:31: "that ye might believe that Jesus is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the Son of God, and that, believing, ye might have life through His name."
