# Worship

## Definition

Worship, as it appears in [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, is the act of rendering divine honor and homage — an act that belongs to God alone. Moody employs the concept of worship primarily as an apologetic argument for the deity of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]: since Jesus received worship without rebuke from those who encountered Him, and since even angels refuse worship, Jesus must be divine. "If [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] were not God manifest in the flesh we are guilty of idolatry in worshiping Him" (005-032). True worship is therefore inseparable from a right understanding of who [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is.

## Moody's Teaching

### Worship as Proof of Christ's Deity

Moody builds a careful argument: no good man — not [[moses|Moses]], not [[elijah|Elijah]], not any apostle — ever accepted worship. "No good man except [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] has ever allowed anybody to worship him. When this was done He never rebuked the worshiper" (005-029). He cites multiple Gospel instances: in John 9:38, the man born blind, upon discovering Jesus, "worshiped Him" without correction; in Matthew 14:33, the disciples in the boat "came and worshiped Him, saying, Of a truth Thou art the Son of God" (005-032), and "He did not rebuke them"; in Matthew 8:2 "there came a leper and worshiped Him, saying, Lord, if Thou wilt, Thou canst make me clean" (005-033); and in Matthew 15:25 the Syrophoenician woman "came … and worshiped Him, saying, Lord, help me!" (005-034).

### The Contrast with Creaturely Refusal

Moody sharpens the argument by contrast. [[eve|Eve]]n angels — the highest created beings — refuse worship. "We see here that even that angel would not allow John to worship him. [[eve|Eve]]n an angel from heaven! And if Gabriel came down here from the presence of God it would be a sin to worship him, or any seraph, or any cherub, or Michael, or any archangel" (005-031). Similarly, when the people of Lystra attempted to worship Paul and Barnabas after the healing of an impotent man, "the evangelists rent their clothes and told these Lystrans that they were but men, and not to be worshipped" (005-036).

### The Inescapable Conclusion

"If [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] is a mere man, we are all guilty of a great sin in worshipping Him" (005-036). This is the logical terminus of Moody's argument: [[jesus-christ|Christ]]endom has worshipped Jesus for centuries. If He is not God, then the whole [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian religion is built upon idolatry. But the fact that He received worship — and that His followers "throughout [[jesus-christ|Christ]]endom … worship [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] as God" (005-006) — testifies to the Church's instinctive recognition of His divine identity.

### The Resurrected Lord

Moody contrasts living worship of the risen [[jesus-christ|Christ]] with the empty devotion of those who "worship a dead Saviour, like Mary, who said, 'They have taken away my Lord; and I know not where they have laid Him'" (005-018). True [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian worship is directed to the living Lord who has conquered death. "Our [[jesus-christ|Christ]] lives. Many people have not found out that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] has risen from the grave" (005-018). Worship without the resurrection is misdirected and futile.

> Look at the millions throughout Christendom who worship Jesus Christ as God. If Christ be not God this is idolatry. We are all guilty of breaking the first commandment if Jesus Christ were mere man — if He were a created being, and not what He claims to be.
>
> (005-006)

## Biblical Foundation

Moody's argument is primarily scriptural, drawing from the Gospel accounts of worship offered to Jesus (John 9:38, Matthew 14:33, Matthew 8:2, Matthew 15:25), the apostolic refusal of worship in Acts 14, and the angelic refusal in Revelation 22:8–9. The consistent biblical pattern — creatures refuse worship, Jesus receives it — forms an unbroken chain of evidence for His deity.
