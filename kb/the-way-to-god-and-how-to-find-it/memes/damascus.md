# Damascus

## The Place

Damascus is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world, located in modern-day Syria. In the New Testament, it is most famous as the destination of [[the-apostle-paul|Saul of Tarsus]] when he encountered the risen [[jesus-christ|Christ]] on the road, an event that transformed the chief persecutor of the church into its greatest apostle.

## Moody's Teaching

### The Suddenness of True Conversion

[[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] uses Paul's Damascus road experience to argue for instantaneous [[conversion|conversion]]. He challenges the notion that conversion must be gradual: "Suppose Ananias had been sent to Paul, when he was on his way to Damascus breathing out threatenings and slaughter against the disciples, and casting them into prison, to tell him not to kill so many as he intended; and to let enmity die out of his heart gradually" (004-015). The absurdity of this picture underscores Moody's point — true conversion is "right about face," a decisive turn. Paul left for Damascus a persecutor and entered it a disciple.

### From Saul to Paul

Moody contrasts two images of the same man: "[[faith|Faith]] is Saul praying in the house of [[judas-iscariot|Judas]] at Damascus, sorrowful, blind, and alone" (007-061). And then: "[[assurance|Assurance]] is Paul, the aged prisoner, looking calmly into the grave, and saying, 'I know whom I have believed'" (007-061). Damascus marks the dividing line — the place where Saul the Pharisee died and Paul the apostle was born.
