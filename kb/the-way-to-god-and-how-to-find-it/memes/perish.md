# Perish

## Definition

In [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, "perish" is the biblical term for the eternal ruin and destruction that awaits those who reject God's provision of [[salvation|salvation]] in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. It stands as the dark foil against which the brightness of [[eternal-life|eternal life]] shines — the fate from which the Son of Man was "lifted up" to deliver. The word appears repeatedly in Moody's quotations of John 3:14–16, always in direct contrast with the gift of everlasting life: "that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have eternal life" (002-021). Perishing is not primarily physical death but spiritual and eternal loss.

## Moody's Teaching

### The Remedy Before the Warning

Moody's handling of the concept of perishing is characteristically evangelical: he presents the remedy before the warning. "As [[moses|Moses]] lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up; that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have eternal life" (002-021). Just as the Israelites who looked at the brazen serpent were healed, those who look to [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in [[faith|faith]] escape perishing.

### The Responsibility of the Sinner

Moody insists that perishing is not God's desire or design but the consequence of human refusal. "If you perish, it will not be because [[adam|Adam]] fell; it will not be because you did not have a Saviour. [[jesus-christ|Christ]] came to save you; and if you refuse Him, your blame will be upon your own soul" (002-022–024). He illustrates this with the analogy of a sick man who refuses a proven medicine: the cure is available, the physician is willing, but the patient must take the remedy. God "has done everything that He could do toward your salvation" (002-021).

### The Shepherd's Assurance

Against the fear of perishing, Moody sets [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own promise of security for His sheep: "I give unto them eternal life; and they shall never perish, neither shall any man pluck them out of my hand" (007-048). The Good Shepherd guarantees that those who belong to Him will not ultimately perish — a promise reaffirmed in John 10:28: "They shall never perish; neither shall any man pluck them out of my hand" (008-017).

### The Call to Repentance

The alternative to perishing is [[repentance|repentance]]. Moody quotes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s warning repeatedly: "Except ye repent, ye shall all likewise perish" (003-007; cf. Luke 13:3, 5). "Except a man be born again" (002-004) — the necessity of the [[new-birth|new birth]] is tied directly to the reality of perishing. Without regeneration, perishing is certain; with it, the believer passes from death to life.

### The Dying Soldier

Moody illustrates the urgency of the matter with the story of a wounded soldier after the battle of Pittsburgh Landing: in the middle of the night, the man called for help, wanting assurance about his eternal destiny. "He wanted me to help him die" (002-033). The narrative underscores that the question of perishing versus eternal life is not an abstract theological debate but an existential concern pressing upon every soul facing death.

> "God so loved the world, that He gave His only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have everlasting life" (John 3:16). I have never been able to preach from that text. I have often thought I would; but it is so high that I can never climb to its height; I have just quoted it and passed on. Who can fathom the depth of those words: "God so loved the world?"
>
> (001-026)

## Biblical Foundation

The central text is John 3:14–16, the most frequently cited passage in Moody's book: "As [[moses|Moses]] lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up; that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have eternal life." Supporting passages include Luke 13:3, 5 ("except ye repent, ye shall all likewise perish"), John 10:28 ("they shall never perish"), and the Mosaic serpent narrative in Numbers 21.
