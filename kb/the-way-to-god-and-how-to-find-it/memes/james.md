## An Apostle of Christ

James, the son of Zebedee and brother of the apostle John, was one of the twelve disciples called by Jesus. Along with Peter and John, he belonged to the inner circle of disciples who witnessed key events in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s ministry. Moody mentions James in two significant contexts.

## Witness to the Transfiguration

Moody references James as one of the three disciples present at the Mount of Transfiguration. In his vivid imaginative retelling of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s unspoken words to Peter after the denial, Moody recalls: "Have you forgotten when, on the Mount of Transfiguration, with James and John, you said to Me, 'Lord, it is good to be here: let us make three tabernacles'?" (009-036). This passage emphasizes the depth of fellowship James enjoyed with [[jesus-christ|Christ]], making Peter's denial all the more poignant by contrast.

## Self-Confidence Contrasted

James appears in Moody's account of Peter's overconfidence before the crucifixion. Moody describes Peter's boast: "James and John, and the others, may leave You; but You can count on me!" (009-031). Peter's self-assurance, contrasting himself with James and the other disciples, becomes the prelude to his fall — illustrating Moody's warning that "the first step in Peter's downfall was his self-confidence" (009-031).

## The Epistle of James

Moody quotes the Epistle of James in connection with the promise of wisdom for those who ask in faith: "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God, that giveth to all men liberally, and upbraideth not; and it shall be given him" (004-044). Moody's use of this verse emphasizes the necessity of coming to God "in a teachable spirit" rather than with "self-conceit" (004-044).
