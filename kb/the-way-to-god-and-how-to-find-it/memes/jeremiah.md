## The Weeping Prophet

Jeremiah was a prophet in Judah during the final years before the [[babylon|Babylon]]ian exile. Moody calls him "a weeping prophet" whom God gave to His people "to bring them back to Him" (009-017). Jeremiah's ministry was marked by sorrow over the people's rejection of God, making his writings especially suitable for addressing those who have wandered from faith.

## On the Deceitfulness of the Heart

Moody draws on Jeremiah's insight into human nature to underscore the necessity of trusting God rather than oneself. Citing Jeremiah 17:9, he writes: "I can believe God better today than I can my own heart. 'The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked: who can know it?'" (003-015). This verse serves Moody's larger argument that salvation comes by believing God's word rather than relying on human feelings or merits. He urges readers to "cut away from all doctrines and creeds, and come right to the heart of the Son of God" (003-015).

## A Resource for Backsliders

Moody recommends Jeremiah as especially helpful for those who have backslidden: "There is nothing that will do backsliders so much good as to come in contact with the Word of God; and for them the Old Testament is as full of help as the New. The book of Jeremiah has some wonderful passages for wanderers" (009-003). Moody then quotes extensively from Jeremiah to illustrate God's tender pleading with His wayward people.

### The Condition of Backsliders

From Jeremiah 6:10, Moody describes the spiritual deafness that accompanies backsliding: "Behold, the word of the Lord is unto them a reproach; they have no delight in it" (009-004). The backslider has lost delight in [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]] — a diagnostic sign of spiritual decline. Moody extends this with Jeremiah's call to "stand ye in the ways, and see, and ask for the old paths, where is the good way, and walk therein" (009-004), lamenting that the people responded, "We will not walk therein" (009-004).

### God's Pleading Love

From Jeremiah 2, Moody highlights the tender tone of God's entreaty: "What iniquity have your fathers found in Me, that they are gone from Me?" (009-006). Moody presents this as God pleading "as a father would plead with a son" (009-006). He summarizes that "the most tender and loving words to be found in the whole of the Bible are from Jehovah to those who have left Him without a cause" (009-011), pointing to Jeremiah 2:19 as the culmination of this divine appeal.

> Look for a moment at Jeremiah 6:10. "To whom shall I speak, and give warning, that they may hear? behold, their ear is uncircumcised, and they cannot hearken: behold, the word of the Lord is unto them a reproach; they have no delight in it." That is the condition of backsliders. They have no delight whatever in the word of God.
>
> (009-004)

## Theological Significance

In Moody's teaching, Jeremiah exemplifies the heart of God toward backsliders — a mixture of righteous warning and tender invitation to return. The prophet's writings provide both diagnosis (the deceitful heart, the refusal to hear) and remedy (returning to the old paths, hearkening to God's word). Moody uses Jeremiah to demonstrate that backsliding is not met with divine indifference but with earnest, loving pursuit.
