# The Prodigal Son

## Overview

The prodigal son is the central figure in one of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s most beloved parables (Luke 15:11–32), and [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] uses him as the supreme illustration of both human repentance and divine love. The prodigal's story demonstrates that what draws a sinner home is not fear of punishment but the dawning realization that the Father loves him still.

## Role in Moody's Teaching

### Drawn by Love

"What brought the prodigal home? It was the thought that his father loved him. Suppose the news had reached him that he was cast off, and that his father did not care for him any more, would he have gone back? Never! But the thought dawned upon him that his father loved him still: so he rose up, and went back to his home" (001-029). This is Moody's key insight: the love of the Father, not fear, motivates true repentance.

### The Contrast with the Elder Brother

Moody draws a sharp contrast between the prodigal and his elder brother. The elder brother — outwardly moral, outwardly dutiful — refused to enter the feast. "The curtain drops and leaves him outside, and the younger brother within. To him the language of the Saviour under other circumstances seems appropriate: 'The publicans and the harlots go into the kingdom of God before you'" (003-010). The elder brother "needed to be converted as much as the younger" (003-011).

### An Illustration of God's Love

A mother who loved her prodigal son even after he became a murderer, sitting through his trial and wanting to be buried beside him — this illustrates that "no mother's love is to be compared with the love of God" (001-024). The prodigal's story is ultimately a story about the Father, not the son.
