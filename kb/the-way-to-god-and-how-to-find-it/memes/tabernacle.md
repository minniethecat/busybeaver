## Definition

The tabernacle is used in Moody's teaching as a metaphor for the believer's physical body — a temporary, earthly dwelling that will one day be replaced by an eternal, heavenly home. Moody draws this imagery from 2 Corinthians 5:1, where Paul speaks of the "earthly house of this tabernacle" being dissolved in favor of "a building of God, a house not made with hands, eternal in the heavens" (007-033).

## Moody's Teaching

Moody employs the tabernacle metaphor primarily in his exposition on the assurance of salvation. He cites 2 Corinthians 5:1 to emphasize that believers can have certainty about their eternal destiny: the dissolution of the earthly tabernacle does not mean annihilation, but entrance into a permanent dwelling prepared by God (007-033). This is presented as evidence that Paul — and by extension all believers — can "know" they have a title to the mansions above, living not in uncertainty but in confident expectation (007-036).

In a separate context, Moody references the tabernacle in connection with the Mount of Transfiguration. When Peter, overwhelmed by the glory of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], proposed to build three tabernacles, his impulsive suggestion revealed a desire to prolong the moment rather than to understand its significance (009-036). Moody uses this episode to highlight the tenderness of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], who did not rebuke Peter for his hasty words but instead responded with patient love — a love that would later break Peter's heart and lead him to repentance.


> All Paul's teaching in nearly every Epistle rings out the doctrine of assurance. He says in 2 Corinthians 5:1: "For we know that if our earthly house of this tabernacle were dissolved, we have a building of God, a house not made with hands, eternal in the heavens." He had a title to the mansions above, and he says — I know it. He was not living in uncertainty.
>
> (007-033)
