# Assurance of Salvation

Assurance of salvation is the confident knowledge, grounded in the promises of [[the-word-of-god|God's Word]], that one who has [[faith|believed]] in [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is eternally saved. In [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, assurance is not presumption or a feeling — it is the birthright of every true believer, clearly taught throughout the New Testament. [[the-apostle-paul|The Apostle Paul]]'s epistles "ring out the doctrine of assurance" (007-033), declaring that believers can know with certainty that they possess eternal life.

## Definition

Assurance is the inward certainty that one has passed from death to life through faith in [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]. It is distinct from [[salvation]] itself — a person may be truly saved and yet lack full assurance. Moody taught that assurance comes from taking God at His word: the same [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]] that promises salvation to all who believe also gives the believer grounds for confidence that the promise has been fulfilled. Assurance rests on the character of God and the finished work of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], not on the believer's emotions or worthiness.

## Moody's Teaching

### The Stork and the Coney

Moody uses a striking natural illustration to show the unreasonableness of doubting God's provision. The stork builds its nest on the fir tree and the coney hides in the rock cleft without questioning whether they have a right to be there. "No inspired word has ever said to the coney, 'Whosoever runs into this rock cleft shall never be driven out of it'" (007-012). Yet [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is provided for sinners — the very kind of Saviour they need — and the invitation is explicit: "Him that cometh unto Me I will in no wise cast out" (007-013), and "Whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely" (007-013). If the dumb creatures trust God's provision without a written promise, how much more should the sinner trust when God has given His express word?

> "The wild goat on the crag does not say, 'Have I a right here?' No, he must be somewhere: and there is a crag which exactly suits him; and he springs upon it.
>
> (007-012)

### Paul's Doctrine of Assurance

Moody points to the Apostle Paul as the great teacher of assurance. "All Paul's teaching in nearly every Epistle rings out the doctrine of assurance" (007-033). Paul did not live in uncertainty about his eternal destiny. He declared in 2 Corinthians 5:1: "For we know that if our earthly house of this tabernacle were dissolved, we have a building of God, a house not made with hands, eternal in the heavens" (007-033). He had "a title to the mansions above" and could say with confidence, "I know it" (007-033). Again in Philippians 1:23, Paul expressed his "desire to depart and be with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (007-033) — a statement he could not have made if he were uncertain of his salvation. In Colossians 3:4 he declared that "when [[jesus-christ|Christ]], who is our life, shall appear, then shall ye also appear with Him in glory" (007-033). For Paul, assurance was not an exceptional attainment but the normal [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian experience.

### Assurance and the Word of God

The foundation of assurance, in Moody's view, is the testimony of [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]]. God has given promises — objective, written, unchanging — and the believer's confidence rests on these. The question is not "Do I feel saved?" but "Has God said He will save those who come to Him?" Assurance grows as the believer takes God at His word, trusts the promises, and rests in the finished work of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] rather than in subjective experience.

- **Colossians 3:4** — "When [[jesus-christ|Christ]], who is our life, shall appear, then shall ye also appear with Him in glory" (007-033)
