# Restitution

Restitution is the practical outworking of genuine [[repentance]] — the act of making right past wrongs by returning what was taken, confessing what was hidden, and repairing what was damaged. [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] treats it not as an optional addition to repentance but as its necessary evidence. A repentance that costs nothing and changes nothing is suspect; true repentance produces restitution.

## Definition

Restitution is the act of restoring to another what has been wrongfully taken or withheld. In the biblical context, it includes returning stolen goods (with interest, per the Mosaic law), confessing hidden sin, apologizing for slander, and making amends for any injury done to another. Moody presents it as the outward proof of inward [[conversion]] — not the cause of [[forgiveness]] but the inevitable fruit of a genuinely changed heart.

## Moody's Teaching

### The Proof of True Repentance

Moody is characteristically practical on this point. He describes an inquirer seeking [[salvation]] who was told: "Make restitution. Let us sit down and calculate how much you owe" (006-030). The man made a list, returned what he had defrauded, and only then found peace. "I have done any man a great wrong, I cannot expect forgiveness until I am willing to make restitution" (006-026).

### No Forgiveness Without Willingness

Moody does not teach that restitution earns forgiveness, but he does teach that unwillingness to make restitution blocks it. A person who clings to the fruits of his sin while asking God to forgive the sin is not truly repentant. The willingness to make things right — even at great personal cost — demonstrates that repentance is real, not merely verbal.

### The Example of Zacchaeus

Though Moody does not expound [[zaccheus|Zacchaeus]] at length, the principle is implicit in his teaching: when [[zaccheus|Zacchaeus]] encountered [[jesus-christ|Christ]], he immediately volunteered to restore fourfold what he had taken (Luke 19:8). This is the pattern: encounter with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] produces radical, costly, practical change.


> If there is true repentance it will bring forth fruit. If we have done wrong to anyone we should never ask God to forgive us, until we are willing to make restitution. If I have done any man a great injustice and can make it good, I need not ask God to forgive me until I am willing to make it good.
>
> (006-026)

## Biblical Foundation

- **Luke 19:8** — [[zaccheus|Zacchaeus]]: "I restore him fourfold"
- **Ezekiel 33:15** — "If the wicked restore the pledge, give again that he had robbed... he shall surely live"
- **Matthew 5:23–24** — "First be reconciled to thy brother, then come and offer thy gift"
