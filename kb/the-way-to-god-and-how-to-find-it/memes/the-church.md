# The Church

The church — the visible community of professing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians — occupies an important but carefully circumscribed role in [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s theology. Moody is at pains to distinguish the church from the kingdom of God and from [[salvation]] itself: one may be a baptized member in good standing and yet remain unregenerate. The church's true function is not to save but to **hold up the Remedy** — to direct sinners away from itself and toward [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Overview

The church is the community of those who profess faith in [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], organized for worship, proclamation, and mutual edification. In the first century, the church was born at [[pentecost]] in [[jerusalem]] and spread throughout the Roman world through the missionary labors of [[the-apostle-paul|Paul]] and others. Moody addresses the church not as an abstract institution but as the concrete reality his 19th-century readers encountered every Sunday — and warns them not to mistake attendance for regeneration.

## References in the Text

### The Church Is Not Salvation

Moody warns against confusing church membership with the new birth:

> "Very often I see people, and ask them if they are Christians. 'Yes, of course I am; at least, I think I am: I go to church every Sunday.' Ah, but this is not Regeneration." (002-007)

Church attendance is commendable, but it is not the new birth. "You may be baptized into the church, and yet not be baptized into the Son of God" (002-007). This distinction is critical: the visible church contains both wheat and tares, both the regenerate and the unregenerate.

### The Pole, Not the Remedy

In Moody's most vivid ecclesiological metaphor, drawing on Numbers 21, the brazen serpent is [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — but the pole on which it is lifted is the church. "Do not look at the pole; that is the church. You need not look at the church; the church is all right, but the church cannot save you. Look beyond the pole. Look at the Crucified One" (002-029). This is Moody's definitive word on the church: it serves by pointing away from itself.

### The Church as Proclaimer

The church's role is to proclaim the gospel. Ministers are "just God's chosen instruments to hold up the Remedy, to hold up [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (002-029). The church serves the gospel by directing attention away from itself to the Crucified One.

### The Church as Witness

Moody tells an anecdote of two young men who sang a hymn in the street until a crowd gathered — then Moody himself rose to preach, and many were converted (002-018). The church, in this vision, is not a building or an institution but a gathered people through whom the gospel is proclaimed.

## Moody's Ecclesiology

| Aspect | Moody's View |
|--------|-------------|
| Nature | Visible community of professing believers; mixed (regenerate and unregenerate) |
| Function | To hold up [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — the pole for the Remedy |
| Limits | Cannot save; membership ≠ regeneration |
| Authority | Subordinate to [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]]; ministers are "instruments" |
| [[worship|Worship]] | Means of grace, not the grace itself |

## Relationship to the Kingdom

Moody distinguishes the church from the kingdom of God: you can enter the church by [[baptism]] and profession; you can only enter the kingdom by being born again. The church is the earthly, visible institution; the kingdom is the spiritual reality entered through regeneration. This distinction protects against the error of sacramental salvation — the belief that the church's rites automatically confer grace.

## Significance

The church in Moody's theology is both essential and insufficient. It is essential because it holds up [[jesus-christ|Christ]], proclaims the gospel, and gathers believers. It is insufficient because it cannot save — only [[jesus-christ|Christ]] can. Moody's ecclesiology is a corrective to both clericalism (which exalts the church too highly) and individualism (which dismisses the church entirely). The church is the God-ordained instrument for gospel proclamation, but the instrument must never be confused with the Saviour it proclaims.
