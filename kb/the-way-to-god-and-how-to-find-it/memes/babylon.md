## In Old Testament History

Babylon was the capital of the Babylonian Empire and the place where the Jewish people were taken into captivity after the fall of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. Moody refers to Babylon in two contexts. First, alongside [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]] and [[egypt|Egypt]], he names it as a place where God demonstrated His power through faithful individuals: "He kept [[joseph-ot|Joseph]] in [[egypt|Egypt]]; [[moses|Moses]] before [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]]; [[daniel|Daniel]] in Babylon; and enabled [[elijah|Elijah]] to stand before Ahab in that dark day" (004-018). [[daniel|Daniel]]'s faithfulness in Babylon serves as an example of God sustaining His people even in exile.

## As a Symbol of Judgment for Backsliding

Moody also references Babylon as the place of judgment upon a backsliding king. Describing the consequences of turning away from God, he writes: "The king who made light of the word of God was taken captive by Nebuchadnezzar, and his children brought up in front of him and every one slain: his eyes were put out of his head; and he was bound in fetters of brass and cast into a dungeon in Babylon" (009-017). Moody cites this as an illustration that "it is an evil and a bitter thing to backslide" (009-017), showing how God's warnings through the prophets were fulfilled when the people turned away from Him.

## Theological Significance

In Moody's teaching, Babylon represents the bitter fruit of forsaking God. The exile was not arbitrary but the fulfillment of divine warning: "The Lord had told them what would happen" (009-017). Yet Moody's larger point is redemptive — he presents the warning so "the Lord would win you back with the message of His Work" (009-017).
