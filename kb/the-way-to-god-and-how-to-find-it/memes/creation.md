# Creation

## Overview

Creation is the divine act recorded in Genesis 1 by which God brought the heavens and the earth into existence. [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] references creation not for its own sake but to draw a theological parallel: just as the original creation was God's work alone — "man does not appear" in Genesis 1 — so regeneration, being a new creation, "must be the work of God" (002-012).

## Moody's Treatment

### Creation as a Pattern for Regeneration

Moody's key use of creation is analogical: "In the first chapter of Genesis man does not appear. There is no one there but God. Man is not there to take part. When God created the earth He was alone. When [[jesus-christ|Christ]] redeemed the world He was alone" (002-012). Just as human effort contributed nothing to the original creation, so human effort contributes nothing to the new creation of regeneration.

### Beyond Human Reason

Moody admits he "never could reason out the creation. I can see the world, but I cannot tell how God made it out of nothing. But almost every man will admit there was a creative power" (002-016). The same is true of regeneration — one may not be able to explain it, but one can see its effects and admit its reality.


> There must be a new creation. Regeneration is a new creation; and if it is a new creation it must be the work of God. In the first chapter of Genesis man does not appear. There is no one there but God. Man is not there to take part. When God created the earth He was alone. When Christ redeemed the world He was alone.
>
> (002-012)
