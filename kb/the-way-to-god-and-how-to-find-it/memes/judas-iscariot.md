# Judas Iscariot

## Overview

Judas Iscariot was one of the twelve disciples of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], who betrayed his Lord for thirty pieces of silver. [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] uses Judas as a sober warning: he had terrible remorse — enough to hang himself — "but that was not repentance" (006-007). Judas's tragedy is that he experienced remorse without true repentance, despair without faith. And yet [[jesus-christ|Christ]] loved him to the end, knowing he would be betrayed.

## Role in Moody's Teaching

### Remorse Is Not Repentance

Moody's key use of Judas is to distinguish remorse from genuine repentance. "Judas had terrible remorse — enough to make him go and hang himself; but that was not repentance. I believe if he had gone to his Lord, fallen on his face, and confessed his sin, he would have been forgiven. Instead of this he went to the priests, and then put an end to his life" (006-007).

Judas felt the horror of his sin but did not turn from it. He sought relief from the priests — his fellow conspirators — rather than from [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. His despair drove him to suicide rather than to the foot of the cross.

### The Object of Christ's Unchanging Love

Despite knowing Judas would betray Him, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] loved him to the end. "He knew that one of His disciples would betray Him; yet He loved Judas" (001-015). On the night of the betrayal, [[jesus-christ|Christ]] washed Judas's feet along with the others — "He wanted to convince them of His unchanging love" (001-015).

The tragedy is that Judas, unlike Peter who also failed his Lord and was restored, never turned back. Both men sinned grievously; one repented and was restored, the other despaired and was lost.

> It is recorded of Jesus Christ, just when He was about to be parted from His disciples and led away to Calvary, that: "having loved His own which were in the world, He loved them unto the end." He knew that one of His disciples would betray Him; yet He loved Judas.
>
> (001-015)
