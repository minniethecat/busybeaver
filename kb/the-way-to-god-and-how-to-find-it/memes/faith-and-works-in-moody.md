# Faith and Works in Moody's Theology

[[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s *The Way to God and How to Find It* presents a clear and consistent soteriology: [[salvation]] is by [[faith]] alone, not by works. Yet Moody devotes an entire chapter to [[backsliding]], urges [[repentance]] as a turning from sin, and describes the [[assurance]] of salvation in terms that imply observable evidence. This article examines how Moody holds these two seemingly opposed principles together — and why the tension is not a contradiction but the heart of his practical theology.

## The Pole: Salvation by Faith Alone

Moody is unequivocal: "[[salvation|Salvation]] is not obtained by works. It is 'to him that worketh not, but believeth'" (002-014). The believer does not work toward the cross but from it: "We work from the cross; but not towards it" (002-014). The order is fixed — possession precedes expression. Just as one must have money before being careful how to spend it (002-014), one must possess [[salvation]] before working it out.

This is the Reformation doctrine of *sola fide*, translated into the idiom of nineteenth-century American revivalism. Moody's illustrations reinforce the point: the bronze serpent in Numbers 21 was lifted up for the Israelites to look upon, not to climb toward (002-025). [[faith|Faith]] is receptivity, not effort. The [[justification]] of the sinner is an act of God, not a cooperative venture.

## The Counterpoint: Works as Evidence

Having established that faith alone saves, Moody immediately introduces the question that his pastoral context demands: how can one know that one's faith is genuine?

The answer runs through every chapter. [[repentance|Repentance]] is "a change of mind that leads to a change of conduct" (006-003). The [[new-birth|New Birth]] produces a new nature, and a new nature produces new behavior. [[assurance|Assurance]] of salvation is not merely a subjective feeling — it is confirmed by the Spirit's witness *and* by the fruit of a changed life.

The [[backsliding]] chapter makes the point negatively: when a professing believer's life shows no evidence of grace, the genuineness of their faith is called into question. Moody distinguishes the backslider (a genuine believer who has grown cold) from the merely professing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian who "never really been converted" (009-001), but the practical test is the same: what does the life show?

## The Resolution: Chronological Sequence, Not Logical Tension

The apparent contradiction dissolves when the chronological dimension is recognized. Moody's framework is sequential, not simultaneous:

1. **[[justification|Justification]]** — God declares the sinner righteous on the basis of [[faith]] alone (002-014)
2. **[[new-birth|Regeneration]]** — The [[new-birth|New Birth]] imparts a new nature capable of obedience (003-004)
3. **[[holiness|Sanctification]]** — The believer grows in holiness through the Spirit's work, producing good works as fruit (004-010)
4. **Evidence** — These works confirm to the believer and to others that faith is genuine (007-008)

Works are not the *instrument* of [[salvation]] but its *evidence*. They are not the root but the fruit. This is precisely the relationship that the Protestant Reformers articulated, and Moody's contribution is to render it in the vivid metaphor and urgent pastoral tone of the revival preacher.

## Moody's Pastoral Genius

What makes Moody's handling of this dialectic pastorally effective is his refusal to let either pole collapse. He never softens the free offer of [[salvation]] — "to him that worketh not, but believeth" — for fear of antinomianism. Nor does he hesitate to warn the [[backsliding|backslider]] that a faith without fruit may be no faith at all. He holds both truths in tension because his audience needs both: the anxious sinner needs to hear that the door is open; the complacent professor needs to hear that the door may not have been entered.

This is not theological inconsistency but pastoral wisdom. The [[faith]] chapter and the [[backsliding]] chapter are not in contradiction — they are two sides of the same pastoral concern: that souls would truly be saved, and know it.

## Conclusion

Moody's faith–works dialectic is not a theological flaw but a deliberate pastoral strategy. [[salvation|Salvation]] is by faith alone — this is the non-negotiable foundation. But the evidence of saving faith is a transformed life — this is the equally non-negotiable corollary. The two principles operate on different planes (instrument vs. evidence) and at different moments (justification vs. sanctification), and Moody's refusal to sacrifice either for the sake of a tidier system is the mark of a preacher who understood both the gospel and the human heart.

> "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of Man be lifted up; that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have eternal life." It is "to him that worketh not, but believeth" (Romans 4:5). We work because we are saved; we do not work to be saved. We work from the cross; but not towards it.
