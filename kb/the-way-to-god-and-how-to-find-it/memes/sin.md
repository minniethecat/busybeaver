# Sin

Sin is the universal condition of fallen humanity — a state of rebellion against God from which **no person is exempt**. [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] expounds this doctrine as the necessary backdrop to understanding [[salvation]], [[new-birth|the new birth]], and [[repentance]]. Apart from a right understanding of sin, the gospel of [[grace]] makes no sense; it is precisely because "all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God" (003-003) that the [[the-cross|cross]] of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is indispensable.

## Definition

Sin is the universal condition of humanity — a state of rebellion and disobedience against God that afflicts every person without exception. [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] emphasizes that "all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God" (003-003), and that no one is righteous by nature. Sin is not merely an external act but a condition of the heart:

> "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked: who can know it?" (003-015)

This inward corruption affects every faculty: the will is bent toward evil, the understanding is darkened, and the affections are disordered. Moody insists that even the best and holiest person, if they turn away from God, will very soon fall into sin — human nature cannot reform itself (002-006).

## Moody's Teaching

### The Universality of Sin

Moody stresses that sin is universal, citing Romans 3:10: "There is none righteous, no, not one: there is none that understandeth; there is none that seeketh after God" (003-002). Paul speaks of the natural man — every human being is included. "Not part of the human family — but all — have sinned, and come short of the glory of God" (003-003).

This universal indictment is reinforced by 1 John 1:8: "If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us" (003-003). No one can claim exemption. Moody's appeal is direct and personal:

> "Ah, sinner, if you feel the loving touch of Christ, just say, 'I am a poor miserable sinner, and I want to be saved'" (001-039)

### Sin and the Need for a Saviour

Moody argues that the doctrine of the new birth is necessary precisely because of sin. Man by nature is "lost and guilty, and our experience confirms this" (002-006). [[eve|Eve]]n the best and holiest person, if they turn away from God, will very soon fall into sin. Human nature is corrupt and cannot reform itself.

The distinction between sin and the sinner is crucial: "God loves the sinner; but He hates the sin. He hates sin, because it mars human life. It is just because God loves the sinner that He hates sin" (001-019). This is the foundation of the gospel — that God's love for the sinner is not diminished by His hatred of the sin.

### Self-Righteousness as a Form of Sin

The most dangerous form of sin, in Moody's view, is self-righteousness — the spirit of the Pharisee who thanks God he is not like other men (003-035). Moody tells of a man "so clad in self-righteousness that you could not get the point of the needle of conviction in anywhere" (003-004). This man later came under deep conviction, crying "I am the worst man in the State of Vermont. I have been a conceited, self-righteous Pharisee" (003-005).

Self-righteousness is particularly dangerous because it masquerades as virtue. A self-righteous person believes they have no need of a Saviour, and thus remains in their sin while congratulating themselves on their morality.

### Sin's Deceitfulness

The heart is so deceitful that Moody says he can "believe God better today than I can my own heart" (003-015). Human hearts are unreliable — but God's word is trustworthy. The deceitfulness of sin means a person can be utterly blind to their own condition.

### The Remedy for Sin

The remedy is not human effort but faith in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s finished work. [[adam|Adam]] fell by unbelief, and "we got life back again by believing — by taking God at His word. In other words we get up where [[adam|Adam]] fell down" (003-014). [[jesus-christ|Christ]] died for the ungodly — "when we were yet without strength, in due time [[jesus-christ|Christ]] died for the ungodly" (001-032). [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] declared of Jesus: "Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world" (002-030).

## Illustrations & Anecdotes

Moody illustrates the deceitfulness of sin and self-righteousness with the story of a Vermont man who was "all right in his own estimation" — a regular church attendee, careful in business, outwardly moral (003-004). Yet when conviction finally came, he wept and declared himself "the worst man in the State of Vermont" (003-005). The anecdote demonstrates that outward morality without inward regeneration is a damning deception.

He also uses the story of a father who led his son into all kinds of sin until the boy became one of the worst characters in the community (001-024). This illustrates sin's contagious, generational nature — that sin not only destroys the sinner but spreads ruin to those under their influence.
