# The Devil

## Definition

The devil, in [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, is a real personal adversary — not a symbol or abstraction — who opposes God's work and seeks the ruin of souls. He is the author of unbelief, the accuser of the brethren, and the tempter who exploits human pride. Yet Moody's emphasis is not on the devil's power but on its limits: [[jesus-christ|Christ]] the Good Shepherd holds His sheep so securely that "no devil or man shall be able" to snatch them from His hand (008-017). The devil is a defeated foe for those who are in [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

## Moody's Teaching

### The Devil and Unbelief

Moody traces the devil's work to the very beginning of human history. "Man lost life by unbelief — by not believing God's word; and we got life back again by believing — by taking God at His word" (003-014). At the heart of the devil's strategy is the question that led to the Fall: has God truly spoken? "Has God ever broken His promise for these six thousand years? The devil and men have been trying all the time to find that God has broken His word" (003-014). [[faith|Faith]] in God's word is the decisive repudiation of the devil's lie.

> Now this looks reasonable. Man lost life by unbelief — by not believing God's word; and we got life back again by believing — by taking God at His word. In other words we get up where Adam fell down. He stumbled and fell over the stone of unbelief; and we are lifted up and stand upright by believing. When people say they cannot believe, show them chapter and verse, and hold them right to this one thing: "Has God ever broken His promise for these six thousand years?" The devil and men have been trying all the time to find that God has broken His word.
>
> (003-014)

### The Devil Cannot Pluck Christ's Sheep

Moody draws particular attention to the unqualified language of John 10:28. Noting that the word "man" in the King [[james|James]] rendering is in italics — supplied by the translators — he insists the verse reads absolutely: "Neither shall any pluck them out of My hand" (008-017). The implication is comprehensive: "no devil or man shall be able to do it" (008-017). The believer's security rests on [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s power, not their own ability to withstand the adversary. Similarly, Romans 8 assures that "neither men, angels, nor devils, can bring any charge against us" (007-039) — the devil may accuse, but God's justification silences every charge.

### The Devil Challenged by Conversion

Moody recounts the testimony of a man who, at the moment of [[conversion|conversion]], cried out "Glory to God!" — and "from that hour the devil has never dared to challenge my conversion" (006-019). Genuine conversion provides a firm ground of [[assurance|assurance]] against which the devil's accusations falter. The subjective certainty of faith is not arrogance but the Spirit's witness, against which the accuser has no standing.

### The Devil and Temptation

Moody warns that no believer, however mature, is immune to the devil's assaults. "I want to lift up a warning note to those who have not fallen. 'Let him that thinketh he standeth, take heed lest he fall'" (009-028). He confesses his own early naivety: "Twenty-five years ago — and for the first five years after I was converted — I used to think that if I were able to stand for twenty years I need fear no fall. But the nearer you get to the Cross the fiercer the battle. [[satan|Satan]] aims high" (009-028). The devil's strategy is to target those in positions of influence, and the believer's defense is constant vigilance.

## Biblical Foundation

Moody's references to the devil draw from John 10:28 (the Good Shepherd's protection), Romans 8:38–39 (no accusation can stand against the justified), the temptation narrative and the fall of Peter (009-028), and the apostolic witness that "even the devils are subject unto us through Thy name" (007-042; Luke 10:17). The consistent theme is that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s authority trumps the devil's power.
