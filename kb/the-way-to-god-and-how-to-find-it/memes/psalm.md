# Psalms

## Definition

The Psalms are the inspired songbook of the Old Testament, quoted extensively by [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] throughout "The Way to God" as a source of comfort, [[assurance|assurance]], and instruction. For Moody, the Psalms are not merely ancient poetry but living testimony — the voice of the restored backslider, the traveler's companion, and the believer's declaration of trust in God's keeping power.

## Moody's Teaching

### The Traveler's Psalm

Moody commends Psalm 121 as essential reading for every believer: "Let such read Psalm 121; 'I will lift up mine eyes unto the hills, from whence cometh my help. My help cometh from the Lord, which made heaven and earth'" (004-016). He calls it "the traveler's psalm" — "a beautiful psalm for those of us who are pilgrims through this world; and one with which we should be well acquainted" (004-017). The psalm's promise that God "will not suffer thy foot to be moved" and that "He that keepeth thee will not slumber" addresses the common fear that a young believer will not hold out. The answer is not self-reliance but the character of the Keeper.

### Christ Our Keeper

Psalm 121's declaration — "He that keepeth Israel shall neither slumber nor sleep" — becomes for Moody the foundation of the believer's security in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. "A great many young disciples are afraid they will not hold out. 'He that keepeth Israel shall neither slumber nor sleep' (Psalm 121:4). It is the work of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] to keep us; and if He keeps us there will be no danger of our falling" (008-016). Moody illustrates: if the Crown of England requires armed guards, how much more do defenseless believers need the One "who neither slumbers nor sleeps" as their keeper? "We have no strength in ourselves. We are no match for [[satan|Satan]]; he has had six thousand years' experience. But then we remember that the One who neither slumbers nor sleeps is our keeper" (008-016).

### The Psalms of the Restored Backslider

Moody draws on [[david|David]]'s experience to show how God uses even failure for His purposes: "We should never have had the thirty-second Psalm if [[david|David]] had not been restored: '[[blessed|Blessed]] is he whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is covered'; or that beautiful fifty-first Psalm which was written by the restored backslider" (009-039). The Psalms of penitence are not monuments to sin but testimonies to [[forgiveness|forgiveness]] and restoration. God's ability to restore the fallen and make them useful again is a theme Moody applies pastorally to [[backsliding|backsliders]].

### The Psalms of Revival

In the context of [[backsliding|backsliding]] and restoration, Moody points to Psalm 85: "Wilt Thou be angry with us forever? wilt Thou draw out Thine anger to all generations? wilt Thou not revive us again: that Thy people may rejoice in Thee? Show us Thy mercy, O Lord; and grant us Thy salvation" (009-002). The prayer for revival is answered with God's own promise: "I will hear what God the Lord will speak: for He will speak peace unto His people, and to His saints; but let them not turn again to folly" (009-002). God's response to the penitent is peace.

### The Psalms as Prophecy of Christ

Moody uses Psalm 68 to contrast [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] — "beheaded; and his disciples begged his body and buried it" — with [[jesus-christ|Christ]] who "has risen from the dead; He has 'ascended on high; He has led captivity captive; and received gifts for men' (Psalm 68:18)" (005-017). The Psalms testify to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s resurrection and ascension. Moody also cites Psalm 25:14 — "The secret of the Lord is with them that fear Him; and He will show them His covenant" (004-041) — to underscore that God reveals Himself to those who reverence Him.
