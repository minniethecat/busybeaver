# Conviction of Sin

Conviction of sin is the deep, Spirit-wrought realization of one's guilt before a holy God. In [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, conviction of sin is an essential preparatory work — but it is not the same thing as [[repentance]] and it is not [[conversion]]. A person may be under profound conviction for months, unable to sleep or eat, and yet never truly repent (006-008). Moody draws a sharp line: "Do not confound conviction of sin with [[repentance|Repentance]]" (006-008).

## Definition

Conviction of sin is the awakening of the conscience to the reality of personal sin and the terror of divine judgment. It is the work of the [[holy-spirit|Holy Spirit]] who, as [[jesus-christ|Christ]] promised, "will reprove the world of sin" (John 16:8). Conviction produces an acute sense of guilt, fear of punishment, and the knowledge that one stands condemned before God. Yet conviction, by itself, stops short of the will's decisive turn from sin to God. It is the diagnosis, not the cure; the alarm, not the escape.

## Moody's Teaching

### Conviction Without Conversion

Moody's most pointed observation on this subject comes from his pastoral experience: "I have seen men under such deep conviction of sin that they could not sleep at night; they could not enjoy a single meal. They went on for months in this state; and yet they were not converted; they did not truly repent" (006-008). The danger, Moody warns, is mistaking the pain of conviction for the reality of repentance. A convicted sinner may cry, pray, and feel miserable — and still remain unsaved if he never takes the decisive step of turning from sin to God.

### Not Feeling, Not Remorse

Moody carefully distinguishes conviction from mere feeling. The convicts in the Baltimore Penitentiary "felt miserable enough: they had plenty of feeling" (006-006), and many "cried half the time" (006-006) during their first days of imprisonment. Yet upon release they "would go right back to their old ways" (006-006). Their feeling was not conviction of sin against God but distress at being caught. Similarly, conviction is not remorse. "[[judas-iscariot|Judas]] had terrible remorse — enough to make him go and hang himself; but that was not repentance" (006-007). Remorse is sorrow for consequences; conviction is sorrow for sin itself.

### The Pivot to Repentance

When does conviction become saving? When the convicted sinner exercises the will and turns. Moody defines true repentance as "right about face" (006-011) — not merely feeling wrong, but actually walking in the opposite direction. The parable of the two sons in Matthew 21 illustrates this: the son who first refused but later "changed his mind" and went to work "is [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s definition of repentance" (006-011). "If a man says, 'By the grace of God I will forsake my sin, and do His will,' that is [[repentance|Repentance]] — a turning right about" (006-011). Conviction prepares the ground; repentance plants the seed.

### The Danger of Delay

Moody urges that the moment of conviction is the moment for decision. "There is a moment, I believe, in every man's life when he can halt and say, 'By the grace of God I will go no further towards death and ruin. I repent of my sins and turn from them'" (006-013). He likens it to a ship approaching the shore: there is a moment when the captain can reverse the engines; there is another moment when it is too late (006-013). Conviction that is repeatedly suppressed hardens the heart and makes repentance progressively more difficult.

> Repentance is not conviction of sin. That may sound strange to some. I have seen men under such deep conviction of sin that they could not sleep at night; they could not enjoy a single meal. They went on for months in this state; and yet they were not converted; they did not truly repent. Do not confound conviction of sin with Repentance.
>
> (006-008)

## Biblical Foundation

- **John 16:8** — The [[holy-spirit|Holy Spirit]] "will reprove the world of sin, and of righteousness, and of judgment"
- **Acts 2:37** — At [[pentecost|Pentecost]] the hearers were "pricked in their heart" and cried, "What shall we do?"
- **Acts 17:30** — God "commandeth all men everywhere to repent" (006-015)
- **Matthew 21:28–29** — The parable of the two sons: conviction leads to changed action (006-011)
