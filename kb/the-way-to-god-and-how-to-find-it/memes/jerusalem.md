# Jerusalem

Jerusalem is the chief city of Judea and the religious centre of Israel in the biblical narrative. In [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s *The Way to God and How to Find It*, Jerusalem appears as the backdrop for the most pivotal events of redemption: the ministry and trial of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], His journey to [[calvary]], and the birth of [[the-church|the church]] at [[pentecost]]. It is also the city where key figures in Moody's narrative — [[nicodemus]], the Sanhedrin, the [[the-apostle-peter|apostles]] — lived and moved.

## Overview

Jerusalem (Hebrew: *Yerushalayim*) was the capital of the united kingdom under [[david|King David]] and the site of Solomon's Temple. In the first century AD, it was a city of approximately 40,000–80,000 inhabitants (swelling to several hundred thousand during pilgrimage festivals), under Roman administration. For Moody's purposes, Jerusalem is the stage upon which the drama of redemption unfolds.

## References in the Text

### The City of Religious Privilege

[[nicodemus|Nicodemus]] — "one of the best specimens of the people of Jerusalem" — was a Pharisee, a member of the Sanhedrin, and a teacher of the law. Yet [[jesus-christ|Christ]] told this Jerusalem religious leader that he "must be born again" (002-005). Moody uses [[nicodemus|Nicodemus]] to demonstrate that even the highest religious standing in Jerusalem is insufficient for entrance into the kingdom of God.

### The City of Rejection

Jerusalem was the place where [[jesus-christ|Christ]] was condemned. Moody describes how [[jesus-christ|Christ]] "was about to be led away from Jerusalem to [[calvary|Calvary]]" (001-015) — the holy city rejected the Holy One. Yet even in rejection, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s love was undiminished: He prayed for the forgiveness of those who engineered His death.

### The Birthplace of the Church

At [[pentecost|Pentecost]], it was in Jerusalem that Peter preached repentance "with marvellous results" — three thousand souls were added to the church in a single day (006-003, Acts 2:38–47). The city that witnessed [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s death became the cradle of His church.


> No doubt he was one of the very best men in Jerusalem. He was an honorable Councillor; he belonged to the Sanhedrim; he held a very high position; he was an orthodox man; he was one of the very soundest men. And yet what did Christ say to him? "Except a man be born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God."
>
> (002-011)

## Biblical Significance

Jerusalem occupies a unique position in redemptive history: it is the city where the temple stood, where the prophets ministered, where [[jesus-christ|Christ]] died and rose, and where the Spirit was poured out at [[pentecost|Pentecost]]. For Moody's readers — many of whom were familiar with the geography of the Bible — Jerusalem was not a distant abstraction but a real place where real events of eternal significance occurred.
