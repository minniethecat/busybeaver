## Biblical Role

Pharaoh was the ruler of [[egypt|Egypt]] during the period of the Exodus, who opposed [[moses|Moses]] and refused to release the Israelites from slavery. Moody references Pharaoh as part of a list of Old Testament figures whom God empowered: "He kept [[joseph-ot|Joseph]] in [[egypt|Egypt]]; [[moses|Moses]] before Pharaoh; [[daniel|Daniel]] in [[babylon|Babylon]]; and enabled [[elijah|Elijah]] to stand before Ahab in that dark day" (004-018). Moody emphasizes that these men "were men of like passions with ourselves" and that "it was God who made them so great" (004-018).

## Example of False Repentance

Moody uses Pharaoh as the primary example of fear without true repentance. When God sent the terrible plagues upon [[egypt|Egypt]], Pharaoh appeared to relent — but this was not genuine repentance. Moody observes: "The moment God's hand was removed Pharaoh's heart was harder than ever. He did not turn from a single sin; he was the same man. So that there was no true repentance there" (006-004). This illustrates Moody's key distinction between mere fear or alarm and authentic repentance that results in a changed life.

## Theological Significance

In Moody's teaching, Pharaoh represents the danger of superficial religious response — appearing to turn to God under pressure but reverting when the pressure is removed. This serves as a warning that "repentance is not fear" (006-004) and that true conversion requires a fundamental change of heart, not merely an emotional reaction to circumstances.
