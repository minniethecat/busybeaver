# David

## Overview

David was the greatest king of Israel, a man after God's own heart. In [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, David appears primarily as a negative example — his misguided forgiveness of his rebellious son [[absalom|Absalom]] illustrates the principle that forgiveness without repentance is unjust and harmful. "David made a woeful mistake in this respect with his rebellious son, [[absalom|Absalom]]. He could not have done his son a greater injustice than to forgive him when his heart was unchanged" (006-023).

## Role in Moody's Teaching

### The Mistake with Absalom

Moody uses David's failure with [[absalom|Absalom]] to illustrate a crucial theological point: God cannot forgive where there is no repentance. David forgave [[absalom|Absalom]] when his heart was still rebellious, and [[absalom|Absalom]] soon drove his father from the throne. "David got into trouble on account of his error of judgment" (006-023).

### Contrast with God

The lesson is that God does not make the same mistake. "There could be no true reconciliation between them when there was no repentance. But God does not make these mistakes" (006-023). God waits for true repentance before extending forgiveness — not because He is unwilling, but because forgiveness without repentance would not truly benefit the sinner.


> We should never have had the thirty-second Psalm if David had not been restored: "Blessed is he whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is covered"; or that beautiful fifty-first Psalm which was written by the restored backslider. Nor should we have had that wonderful sermon on the day of Pentecost when three thousand were converted — preached by another restored backslider.
>
> (009-039)
