## Overview

Absalom was the third son of [[david|King David]], known for his rebellion against his father and his tragic death. In [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s "The Way to God and How to Find It," Absalom appears in two distinct contexts: as an example of [[david|David]]'s flawed human judgment in forgiving an unrepentant heart, and as a figure representing the anguish that befalls parents whose children follow them into backsliding.

## Role in Moody's Teaching

Moody first references Absalom in his discussion of [[repentance]] and restitution. He observes that [[david|David]] "made a woeful mistake" with his rebellious son: [[david|David]] forgave Absalom when his heart was unchanged, and "there could be no true reconciliation between them when there was no repentance" (006-023). Moody contrasts [[david|David]]'s error with God's perfect justice — God does not make the mistake of extending forgiveness where there is no genuine repentance. [[david|David]]'s misjudgment led to disaster, as Absalom soon drove his father from the throne.

The second reference to Absalom appears in Moody's treatment of [[backsliding]]. Describing the ruin that backsliding parents bring upon their children, Moody points to [[david|David]]'s anguished cry: "O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom! would God I had died for thee; O Absalom, my son, my son!" (009-015). Moody suggests that [[david|David]]'s anguish was caused not merely by his son's death, but by the spiritual ruin that preceded it — the bitter fruit of [[david|David]]'s own failure.


> David made a woeful mistake in this respect with his rebellious son, Absalom. He could not have done his son a greater injustice than to forgive him when his heart was unchanged. There could be no true reconciliation between them when there was no repentance.
>
> (006-023)

## Biblical Account

Absalom's story is recorded in 2 Samuel 13–18. After his half-brother Amnon assaulted his sister Tamar, Absalom murdered Amnon and fled. Later restored to [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] through the intervention of Joab, Absalom conspired against [[david|David]], declared himself king, and forced his father to flee. His rebellion ended when his long hair caught in the branches of an oak tree during battle, and Joab killed him — contrary to [[david|David]]'s explicit command to deal gently with him.
