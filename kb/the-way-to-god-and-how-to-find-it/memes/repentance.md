# Repentance

## Definition

Repentance is the sincere turning of a sinner away from sin toward God — a "right about face" that involves not merely a change of feeling or conviction but an actual change of direction in life (006-011). As [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] presents it, repentance is a fundamental doctrine of the Bible that is inseparable from saving faith: "Saving repentance and saving faith always go together" (006-024).

## Moody's Teaching

### What Repentance Is Not

Moody begins his exposition on repentance by carefully distinguishing it from several things that people commonly confuse with true repentance (006-004). He identifies six common misunderstandings:

**Repentance is not fear.** Many people think they must be alarmed and terrified before they can repent, but fear does not produce genuine repentance. Moody points to the example of sailors who cry out to God during a storm but return to their profanity once the danger passes, and to [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]], whose heart grew harder after each plague was lifted (006-004).

**Repentance is not feeling.** Moody observed this particularly among nine hundred convicts in the Baltimore Penitentiary — many were miserable and wept, but their sorrow was over being caught, not over having sinned against God (006-006).

**Repentance is not fasting or penance.** A person may fast for years or perform all manner of self-affliction without truly repenting of a single sin. Moody cites [[judas-iscariot|Judas]] as a tragic example: [[judas-iscariot|Judas]] had terrible remorse, enough to hang himself, but that was not repentance (006-007).

**Repentance is not conviction of sin.** Moody has seen people under such deep conviction that they could not sleep or eat for months, yet they remained unconverted (006-008).

**Repentance is not praying or Bible reading.** A person may cry out to God and read [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]] extensively without ever truly repenting (006-009).

**Repentance is not merely breaking off one particular sin.** "Forsaking one vice is like breaking off one limb of a tree, when the whole tree has to come down" (006-010). True repentance requires turning from every sin.

### What Repentance Is

Moody defines repentance with vivid simplicity: it is "right about face!" In the Irish language, the word means even more — it implies that one who has been walking in one direction has not only faced about but is actually walking in an exactly contrary direction (006-011).

Repentance has also been described as "a change of mind." Moody illustrates this with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s parable of the two sons (Matthew 21:28–29): the son who first refused to work in the vineyard, then changed his mind and went — he not only felt wrong but actually acted (006-011).

[[dr-brooks|Dr. Brooks of St. Louis]], whom Moody quotes, offers this definition: "Repentance, strictly speaking, means a 'change of mind or purpose;' consequently it is the judgment which the sinner pronounces upon himself, in view of the love of God displayed in the death of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], connected with the abandonment of all confidence in himself and with trust in the only Saviour of sinners" (006-024).

### The Call to Repentance

Moody traces the call to repentance through [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]] as a continuous thread: [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] preached "Repent ye; for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (006-002). [[jesus-christ|Christ]] took up the same cry (006-003). The disciples were sent out with the message "that men should repent" (006-003). On the day of [[pentecost|Pentecost]], Peter raised the same cry (006-003). And Paul declared at Athens that "God commandeth all men, everywhere, to repent" (006-003) (Acts 17:30).

### The Urgency of Repentance

Moody emphasizes that repentance must be immediate. He compares it to a ship bearing toward the shore — there is a moment when the engines can be reversed and the vessel saved, but there is also a moment when it is too late (006-013). [[eve|Eve]]ry case of conversion recorded in the Bible was instantaneous: "The moment a man made up his mind, God gave him the power" (006-015).

The immediate nature of repentance is illustrated by the testimony of a minister from Ohio, who described deciding to begin a [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian life "within the last thirty seconds" while sitting in a class meeting, and being converted in the very act of kneeling to pray (006-016)(006-017)(006-018).

Moody also warns against waiting for some mysterious feeling — the man who for sixty years said it "had not struck him yet" went to his grave undecided (006-020)(006-021).

### Repentance and Forgiveness

True repentance is the necessary condition for divine forgiveness. Moody argues that God cannot forgive a person who does not want to be forgiven — heaven would be hell to an unrepentant sinner (006-022). He illustrates this with [[david|David]]'s mistake in forgiving his rebellious son [[absalom|Absalom]] when his heart was unchanged, which led only to greater trouble (006-023).

God waits to forgive and seeks to bring the sinner to Himself, but "He never blesses until there is sincere repentance" (006-022). Moody also affirms that the Bible teaches there is no opportunity for repentance after this life (006-032).

### The Fruit of Repentance

If repentance is genuine, it produces visible fruit. Moody insists that anyone who has wronged another must make restitution before expecting God's forgiveness (006-026). He tells the story of a defaulter who had stolen $1,500 from his employers — only after returning what he had and confessing his sin did he find forgiveness and peace (006-027)(006-028).

Another example is a friend who, after conversion, realized he had defrauded the government and sent a check for $1,500 to the United States Treasury. This was bringing forth "fruits meet for repentance" (006-029).

### Repentance and the Love of God

Significantly, Moody teaches that it is not one's own badness but God's goodness that leads to repentance. Quoting [[dr-brooks|Dr. Brooks]]: "It is not your badness, therefore, but His goodness that leads to repentance; hence the true way to repent is to believe on the Lord [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], 'who was delivered for our offences, and was raised again for our justification'" (006-025). The sinner repents "in view of the love of God displayed in the death of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]" (006-024).

Moody closes his chapter with the tender image of a prison inmate whose flowers reminded him of his mother, leading the governor to pardon him. "When God finds that beautiful flower of true repentance springing up in a man's heart, then salvation comes to that man" (006-038).

## Biblical Foundation

### Old Testament

- **Ezekiel 18:32** — "I have no pleasure in the death of him that dieth; wherefore turn, and live ye" — God's heart toward the sinner (006-033).
- **The plagues of [[egypt|Egypt]]** — [[pharaoh|Pharaoh]]'s false repentance under judgment demonstrates that fear alone does not produce true turning from sin (006-004).

### New Testament

- **Matthew 3:2** — [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]]'s message: "Repent ye; for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (006-002).
- **Matthew 4:17** — [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s identical message when He began His ministry (006-003).
- **Matthew 21:28–29** — The parable of the two sons, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s own definition of repentance as changed action (006-011).
- **Mark 6:12** — The disciples sent out to preach that men should repent (006-003).
- **Luke 18:11–14** — [[the-pharisee-and-the-publican|The Pharisee]] and the publican: the contrite, repenting sinner went home justified (006-035).
- **Acts 2:38–47** — Peter on [[pentecost|Pentecost]] preaching repentance, with marvellous results (006-003).
- **Acts 17:30** — Paul at Athens: "God commandeth all men, everywhere, to repent" (006-003)(006-015).
- **Romans 3:10, 23** — "There is none righteous; no, not one. All have sinned" — the universal need for repentance (006-035).
