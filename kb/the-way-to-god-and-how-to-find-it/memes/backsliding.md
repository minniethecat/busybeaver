# Backsliding

Backsliding is the spiritual declension of a professing believer — a retreat from close fellowship with God into worldliness, negligence, or sin. [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] devotes the final chapter of *The Way to God and How to Find It* to this subject, addressing those who have "left their first love" and urging them to return. His tone is pastoral rather than condemnatory: the backslider is not beyond hope, and God's invitation is always "Return."

## Definition

Backsliding is the condition of a believer who has drifted from the warmth and devotion of their first [[faith]]. It is not the loss of [[salvation]] — Moody distinguishes between backsliding and apostasy — but a loss of joy, power, and fellowship. The backslider still believes but no longer walks closely with God. The term comes from the prophets, particularly [[jeremiah|Jeremiah]] and Hosea, who used it of Israel's covenant unfaithfulness.

## Moody's Teaching

### The Reality of Backsliding

Moody opens his final chapter acknowledging that many who profess faith have "never really been converted" (009-001). True backsliding presupposes a genuine beginning — one cannot slide back from where one has never been. Yet he addresses the genuine believer who has grown cold, insisting that restoration is possible. "Thine own wickedness shall correct thee, and thy backslidings shall reprove thee" (009-012).

### The Call to Return

The prophets repeatedly call Israel to return, and Moody applies these texts to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian backslider. "Return, ye backsliding children, and I will heal your backslidings" (009-022). God's response to the returning backslider is not reluctant forgiveness but eager welcome: "I will heal their backsliding, I will love them freely" (009-023). The promise of Hosea 14:4 — "I will heal their backsliding" — is Moody's anchor text for hope.

### David as the Pattern

Moody uses [[david|King David]] as the paradigmatic backslider who was restored. [[david|David]]'s sin with Bathsheba was followed by months of unconfessed guilt. Yet when confronted by Nathan, [[david|David]] repented, and his prayer in Psalm 51 — "Restore unto me the joy of Thy salvation" — became the model for every returning backslider (009-016).

### The Consequences

Backsliding brings inevitable consequences: loss of joy, loss of power in prayer, loss of [[assurance]], and the corrective discipline of God. "Thine own wickedness shall correct thee" (009-012) — the backslider's misery is itself God's merciful goad to return. Yet the consequences are remedial, not punitive: "I will restore unto you the joy of His salvation" (009-027).

> "I will heal their backsliding; I will love them freely: for Mine anger is turned away."
>
> (009-001)

## Biblical Foundation

- **[[jeremiah|Jeremiah]] 2:19** — "Thine own wickedness shall correct thee, and thy backslidings shall reprove thee" (009-012)

- **[[jeremiah|Jeremiah]] 8:5** — "Slidden back by a perpetual backsliding" (009-018)
- **Hosea 14:4** — "I will heal their backsliding, I will love them freely" (009-023)
- **Psalm 51:12** — "Restore unto me the joy of Thy salvation" (009-016)
