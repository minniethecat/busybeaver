# Hell

## Definition

In [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]]'s teaching, hell (also called perdition) is the eternal destiny of those who reject God's offer of [[salvation|salvation]] in [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]. Moody does not dwell on the physical torments of hell — his approach is consistently pastoral rather than sensational — but he treats the reality of hell with the utmost seriousness. It is the logical terminus of the road away from God: the place where those who refuse the Remedy find themselves when the remedy is no longer offered. "There is no hope for you if you neglect the remedy" (002-010). Hell is not God's desire for any soul, but it is the certain consequence of rejecting the one provision He has made.

## Moody's Teaching

### Hell on Earth: The Drunkard's Home

Moody's most vivid description of hell is not of the afterlife but of a scene in this world: "I think if you want to get near hell, you should go to a poor drunkard's home. Go to the house of that poor miserable drunkard. Is there anything more like hell on earth?" (002-007). He describes the children hiding, the patient wife bearing the marks of his blows, the oaths, the brutal treatment. This is hell — not because of physical location but because of the absence of God and the reign of sin. The same home, after the man's conversion, becomes a picture of heaven: singing, children clustering around his knee, his wife's face "lit up with a happy glow" (002-008). Hell and heaven are not merely destinations but present spiritual realities, visible in the contrast between lives governed by sin and lives governed by grace.

### The Unprepared Soul in a Prepared Place

Moody insists that "an unregenerated man would rather be in hell than in heaven" (002-008). This is a striking claim: the problem is not merely that the sinner cannot enter heaven but that he would not want to. "Take a man whose heart is full of corruption and wickedness, and place him in heaven among the pure, the holy and the redeemed; and he would not want to stay there" (002-008). [[heaven|Heaven]] is "a prepared place for a prepared people" (002-008). If an unrepentant sinner were taken to heaven as he is, "there would be another rebellion in heaven" (002-008). Hell, in this sense, is not arbitrary punishment but the natural and chosen habitation of the soul that has refused to be made fit for God's presence.

### Heaven Would Be Hell to the Unrepentant

Moody extends this principle with remarkable consistency. Of the elder brother in the parable of the prodigal, he writes: "[[heaven|Heaven]] would be hell to such an one. An elder brother who could not rejoice at his younger brother's return would not be 'fit' for the kingdom of God" (003-023). The person who refuses to [[repentance|repent]] — the self-righteous as much as the openly wicked — would find heaven intolerable because heaven is filled with the grace he resents. The same point is made in regard to forgiveness: "if a man went into the kingdom of God without repentance, heaven would be hell to him" (006-016). The soul that loves sin would be miserable in the presence of perfect holiness. The absence of [[repentance|repentance]] is not a technical barrier but a moral impossibility: the unrepentant man cannot enjoy what heaven is.

### The Jubilee in Hell That Never Comes

In a powerful apologetic argument for the absolute truthfulness of God's word, Moody writes: "there would be a jubilee in hell today if one word that He has spoken could be broken" (003-028). The entire kingdom of darkness has laboured for six thousand years to find a single divine promise that has failed — and has never succeeded. The integrity of God's word is the ultimate guarantee both of the believer's security and of the certainty of judgment. The same unbreakable word that promises [[everlasting|everlasting life]] to the believer also warns of wrath to the unbeliever.

### The Martyrs' Certainty

Moody appeals to the witness of the early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians and martyrs as evidence that hell is no mere theological speculation: "Could Paul and the early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians and martyrs have gone through what they did if they had been filled with doubts, and had not known whether they were going to heaven or to perdition after they had been burned at the stake?" (007-040). The martyrs went to the flames not uncertain of their destination but absolutely confident. They knew they were going to heaven, not perdition, and this [[assurance|assurance]] carried them through the fire. Their certainty about both destinations — heaven and hell — gave them courage to face death.

### Neglecting the Remedy

Moody's consistent emphasis is not on the terrors of hell but on the tragedy of neglecting the only escape. The Israelites who refused to look at the brazen serpent died — not because the remedy failed but because they spurned it. "So, if you perish, it will not be because [[adam|Adam]] fell; but because you spurned the remedy offered to save you" (002-010). Hell is the destination of the man who, having been offered life, chooses death: "You will choose darkness rather than light" (002-010). The note is one of sorrowful warning, not vindictive satisfaction.

> An unregenerated man would rather be in hell than in heaven. Take a man whose heart is full of corruption and wickedness, and place him in heaven among the pure, the holy and the redeemed; and he would not want to stay there. Certainly, if we are to be happy in heaven we must begin to make a heaven here on earth. Heaven is a prepared place for a prepared people.
>
> (002-020)

## Biblical Foundation

Moody's references to hell are grounded in the biblical contrast between life and death, salvation and perdition. His key texts include John 3:16 (the alternative to everlasting life is to perish), John 3:36 ("the wrath of God abideth on him"), and the various warnings of [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]] about judgment. He consistently treats hell as a reality taught by the same [[the-word-of-god|Scripture]]s that promise [[salvation|salvation]] — both stand or fall on the truthfulness of God's word.
