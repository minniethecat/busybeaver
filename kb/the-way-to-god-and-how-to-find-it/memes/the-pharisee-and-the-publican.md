# The Pharisee and the Publican

## Overview

The Pharisee and the publican are the two contrasting figures in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s parable of the two men who went to the temple to pray (Luke 18:9–14). [[dwight-l-moody|D. L. Moody]] uses them to illustrate the difference between self-righteousness and genuine repentance. The publican — the despised tax collector — went home justified, while the outwardly righteous Pharisee did not.

## Role in Moody's Teaching

### The Two Classes

Moody frames his entire third chapter around this contrast: "All inquirers can be ranged under two heads: they have either the spirit of the Pharisee, or the spirit of the publican" (003-002). This is the fundamental division of humanity — the self-righteous who see no need of a Saviour, and the convicted who cry for mercy.

### The Publican's Prayer

The publican's prayer — "God be merciful to me a sinner!" — is the model of repentant faith. Moody urges the sinner: "Say, like the publican of old, 'God be merciful to me a sinner!' and see how quickly He will pardon and bless you" (006-034). It is short, direct, and humble — nothing but a cry for mercy.

### The Pharisee's Plight

The Pharisee "thanked God that he was not as other men — extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican" and boasted of his fasting and tithing (006-035). Yet [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s verdict is devastating: "I tell you this man [the publican] went down to his house justified rather than the other" (006-035). Self-righteousness — the most dangerous form of sin — keeps a person from the kingdom.

### Application to the Reader

Moody applies the parable directly: "There are some perhaps who think themselves righteous; and that, therefore, there is no need for them to repent and believe the Gospel. They are like the Pharisee in the parable" (006-035). His conclusion is stark: "Let each one take his true place — that of a sinner; then God will lift him up to the place of forgiveness and justification" (006-035).

> All inquirers can be ranged under two heads: they have either the spirit of the Pharisee, or the spirit of the publican. If a man having the spirit of the Pharisee comes into an after-meeting, I know of no better portion of Scripture to meet his case than Romans 3:10: "As it is written, There is none righteous, no, not one."
>
> (003-002)
