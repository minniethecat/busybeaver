# Little Chicken

## Summary

Little Chicken is the baby black vulture hatched from a nest Freckles discovers in a hollow tree deep in the Limberlost swamp. More than a mere pet, Little Chicken becomes a recurring character who symbolizes Freckles' loving and protective relationship with the natural world, serves as a bridge between Freckles and the Bird Woman's conservation work, and provides some of the novel's most tender and humorous moments.

## Discovery and Hatching

Freckles first notices the black vultures—which he calls his "big black chickens"—circling over the Limberlost. After the female disappears for several days, he grows anxious. Mrs. Duncan raises the delightful hope that she might be nesting [001-034]. Consulting his prized bird book, Freckles identifies them as black vultures, learning that they "nest in hollow logs or stumps" [001-166].

The actual discovery of the nest is a moment of pure joy. When Duncan investigates a hollow tree, Freckles dances wildly, shouting: "It's me chickens! Oh, it's me chickens! Oh, Duncan, come quick! You've found the nest of me precious chickens!" [001-598]. Finding that the egg has hatched, Freckles exclaims: "Oh, me big chicken has hatched out me little chicken, and there's another egg. I can see it plain, and oh, the funny little white baby! Oh, Duncan, can you see me little white chicken?" [001-606]. His affectionate naming—"Little Chicken"—immediately sticks, and the baby vulture becomes a central figure in Freckles' emotional life.

## The Bird Woman and Photography

Little Chicken provides a crucial link between Freckles and the Bird Woman's conservation mission. The Bird Woman visits regularly to photograph the chick's development, as vultures "remain over a month in the nest" and she wants "to make a picture every few days for seven or eight weeks" [001-931]. This photographic project brings the Angel into the swamp regularly and deepens Freckles' connection to both women.

When the Bird Woman asks why Freckles calls the vulture "Little Chicken," she leans toward him with genuine interest, and the explanation becomes part of their growing friendship [002-091]. Later, the Bird Woman entrusts Freckles and the Angel with her camera, telling them to try photographing Little Chicken themselves if he "came out and truly smirked" [004-756]. Their attempt—watching Little Chicken go through an elaborate routine of yawning, stretching, dressing his plumage, and finally producing "the fleeting little ghost of a smirk"—becomes one of the novel's most charming comic scenes [004-772, 004-784].

## Symbol of Devotion and Freedom

Little Chicken's significance extends beyond comic relief. When the timber gang arrives and begins cutting trees, Freckles anxiously checks that the chicken tree is safe [003-721]. The vulture family's presence—circling above the swamp, dipping and rising—becomes a barometer of the Limberlost's health and a warning system, as when they alert Freckles to the rattlesnake's den [004-591].

In the novel's final pages, Little Chicken makes his first solo flight, following the Angel through the swamp: "He was so wabbly, and so slow flying from tree to tree and through the bushes, I just had to wait on him, for I couldn't drive him back" [005-398]. Freckles exults: "Me bird has too amazing good sinse to go back when he could be following you" [005-402]. And from his hospital bed, Freckles asks McLean: "Can me Little Chicken spread six feet of wing and sail to his mother?" [006-813], confirming that even in his most dire moment, his thoughts return to the creature he nurtured.

## The Name and Its Significance

The name "Little Chicken" reflects the broader pattern of Freckles calling all birds his "chickens"—a habit that began when Duncan gave him corn for "his wild chickens in the Limberlost" [000-444]. Freckles embraced the term: "They are just little, brightly colored cocks and hens!" [000-448]. This domestic language reflects Freckles' deep need to belong and to care for something, transforming the wild swamp into a kind of family. When McLean compliments the vulture as "the only real chicken you have," he acknowledges both the bird's uniqueness and Freckles' genuine bond with it [000-857]. The bird book identifies these as "Pharaoh's Chickens of European species," giving Freckles' affectionate nickname an unexpected scientific dignity [001-142].
