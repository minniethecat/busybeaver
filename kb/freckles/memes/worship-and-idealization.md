# Worship and Idealization

## Definition

Worship and idealization constitute one of the most psychologically complex themes in Gene Stratton-Porter's *[[freckles|Freckles]]*. The protagonist's tendency to see those he admires in transcendent terms—[[mclean|McLean]] as an ideal of masculine authority, the Angel as a literally angelic being—is presented as both a mark of spiritual depth and a potential obstacle to mature relationship. [[freckles|Freckles]] must learn to love real people rather than idealized images, to see the Angel as a woman rather than a celestial messenger, without losing the capacity for wonder that makes his adoration meaningful (005-117).

The theme draws on religious language and imagery while remaining grounded in secular psychology. [[freckles|Freckles]]' "blind, silent worship" of the Angel is not presented as pathological but as a stage—perhaps a necessary stage—in the development of a person whose emotional growth was stunted by institutional life. Having never learned to love in ordinary human ways, he loves extraordinarily—with an intensity that is both beautiful and slightly frightening, both genuine and slightly misdirected.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The theme is introduced obliquely through the epithet "Angel" itself. From her first appearance, the young woman who becomes the novel's heroine is known not by her name but by a term that places her beyond the human. [[freckles|Freckles]] "was dreaming of naught but blind, silent worship" (005-117)—the phrase captures both the intensity and the immaturity of his feeling. Worship is what one offers a god, not a person; to worship another human being is to misrecognize them, to see a celestial being where a mortal woman stands.

The novel is careful to show that [[freckles|Freckles]]' idealization is not merely romantic convention but a psychological response to his history. Having been denied love as a child, he has no template for ordinary affection. The orphanage "Home" offered "stilted, perfunctory kindness" exchanged for salaries (007-004); the visitors who came on receiving days were, to [[freckles|Freckles]]' perceptive eye, hypocrites performing charity for their own satisfaction. From this emotional desert, the sudden appearance of someone genuinely kind, genuinely interested in him, genuinely beautiful—the response of worship is perhaps not surprising.

[[mclean|McLean]]'s discovery of [[freckles|Freckles]]' secret clearing crystallizes the theme. Finding evidence of "a heart aching for beauty, art, companionship, worship" (004-162), [[mclean|the Boss]] realizes that [[freckles|Freckles]]' feeling runs deeper than he had understood. The word "worship" appears as the culminating term in a sequence—beauty, art, companionship, worship—that moves from the aesthetic to the social to the spiritual. Worship, in this sequence, is not a pathology but the highest expression of a soul's hunger for meaning.

The novel ultimately guides [[freckles|Freckles]] toward a more grounded love. [[the-angel|The Angel]]'s father, speaking of the boy, observes "how his soul vibrates with his [[music-and-song|music]], his love of beautiful things" (017-128)—note that "love" has replaced "worship." The shift in vocabulary marks a shift in [[freckles|Freckles]]' emotional development. He has learned to love rather than worship, to see clearly rather than through the haze of idealization, without losing the intensity that made his worship beautiful in the first place.

## Significance

The worship theme complicates what might otherwise be a straightforward romance. If [[freckles|Freckles]] simply fell in love with the Angel and won her through his virtues, the novel would be a pleasant but unremarkable story. The idealization theme introduces psychological depth—the recognition that love, especially first love, especially love experienced by someone who has been emotionally starved, can take forms that are not quite healthy. [[freckles|Freckles]] must grow not only into courage and competence but into a more mature way of loving.

The theme also connects to broader cultural anxieties about romantic love in the early twentieth century. The Victorian ideal of pure, worshipful love was giving way to more psychologically realistic models of relationship. *[[freckles|Freckles]]* occupies an intermediate position: it validates the intensity of worshipful love while recognizing its limitations, steering its protagonist toward a love that sees the beloved clearly rather than through a haze of idealization.
