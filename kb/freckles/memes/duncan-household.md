# The Duncan Household

## Summary

The household of [[duncan|Duncan]] and [[sarah-duncan|Sarah Duncan]] is [[freckles|Freckles]]' first experience of family life. Located at the boundary between [[the-logging-camp|the logging camp]] and [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]], [[duncans-home|the Duncan home]] is where [[freckles|Freckles]] takes his meals, sleeps when not on duty, and receives the domestic care—hot food, clean clothing, worried questions—that the [[chicago-orphanage|orphanage]] never provided. [[mclean|McLean]] arranges the boarding at the outset: "He engaged him a place to board with the family of his head teamster, Duncan" (002-002).

## Sarah Duncan's Maternal Role

[[sarah-duncan|Sarah Duncan]] is the emotional center of the household. From the moment [[freckles|Freckles]] arrives, she treats him not as a boarder but as a son. Her practical care—feeding him, mending his clothes, worrying about his safety—is the novel's most concrete representation of maternal love. When she pauses on the trail, frightened by the darkness, "Duncan's rolling call: 'Freckles! Freckles!'" breaks through her fear (010-001)—the concern in her husband's voice mirroring her own.

Sarah Duncan's treatment of Freckles is notable for its lack of condescension. She never treats his missing hand as a defect or his orphan status as a pity. Instead, she feeds him, clothes him, and trusts him—the actions of love rather than its professions.

## Duncan's Defense

[[duncan|Duncan]]'s role in [[freckles|Freckles]]' life evolves from skepticism to fierce loyalty. Initially doubtful about the one-handed boy's fitness for guard duty, he is won over by daily observation. His defense of [[freckles|Freckles]] becomes absolute: "And nae matter what happens now or ever, dinna ye dare let onythin' make ye believe that Freckles hasna guarded faithful as ony man could" (004-028). This testimony, delivered privately to [[mclean|McLean]], is the novel's most powerful endorsement of [[freckles|Freckles]]' character—a verdict rendered by the man who had the most reason to doubt.

## The Household as Moral Community

The Duncan household functions as the novel's moral center. While [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] tests [[freckles|Freckles]]' courage and [[the-logging-camp|the camp]] provides his livelihood, the Duncan home provides the domestic warmth that makes courage and labor meaningful. It is the place where [[freckles|Freckles]] learns what a family can be—a lesson that prepares him for the revelation of his own [[irish-identity|Irish family]] and his eventual place in the O'More line.
