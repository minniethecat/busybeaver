# The Bribery Attempt

## Summary

The bribery attempt by [[wessner|Wessner]] represents the central moral test of [[freckles|Freckles]]' character in Gene Stratton-Porter's novel. Sent by the timber thief [[black-jack|Black Jack]], [[wessner|Wessner]] approaches the young guard at his post and offers him money to abandon his duty. [[freckles|Freckles]]' refusal—grounded not in abstract morality but in his personal code of [[labor-and-character|honor]] and his loyalty to [[mclean|McLean]]—establishes him as a hero defined by integrity rather than physical strength.

## The Approach

[[wessner|Wessner]] arrives at [[the-limberlost-timberline|the timberline]] under the pretense of friendship. The encounter begins with false camaraderie: "You're glad to see me,' said Wessner, with something very like a bow" (004-072). When [[freckles|Freckles]] does not respond with the expected warmth, [[wessner|Wessner]] shifts to a direct appeal, invoking their shared experience: "Wessner had been his bunkmate the night before" (004-071). The appeal to solidarity is calculated—[[wessner|Wessner]] assumes that a one-handed orphan boy will be easily corrupted.

## The Moral Logic of Refusal

[[freckles|Freckles]]' refusal is remarkable for its specificity. He does not invoke general principles of right and wrong but articulates a personal code bound up in his identity as [[the-limberlost-guard|guard]]: "I'm to guard every thing he's trusted me to guard, and be getting me wages all winter for things that will be to me credit" (004-097). The moral calculus is simultaneously practical and profound: to accept the bribe would be to destroy the very self that the job has created.

When [[wessner|Wessner]] reminds him of his physical vulnerability—"I'm away bigger than you, and you've only one hand" (004-106)—[[freckles|Freckles]] does not waver. The threat only clarifies the stakes. [[freckles|Freckles]]' physical incompleteness, far from being a weakness, becomes the ground of his moral completeness: he cannot afford to lose his honor, because it is all he has.

## Narrative Significance

The bribery attempt is structurally crucial. It transforms the abstract danger of "timber thieves" into a personal moral confrontation, forcing [[freckles|Freckles]] to articulate and defend his code. It also establishes the enmity with [[black-jack|Black Jack]] that will escalate into violence. By refusing the bribe, [[freckles|Freckles]] chooses the path that leads to [[the-beating-and-rescue|his beating]]—and ultimately, to his vindication.

The scene also reveals the novel's deeper argument about [[social-class|class]] and [[labor-and-character|character]]. [[wessner|Wessner]] assumes that [[freckles|Freckles]], as a poor, disabled orphan, can be bought. [[freckles|Freckles]]' refusal is an assertion that dignity is not a function of wealth or physical wholeness but of fidelity to one's word.
