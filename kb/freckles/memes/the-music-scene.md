## Summary

In one of the novel's most lyrical moments, [[freckles|Freckles]] and [[the-angel|the Angel]] sing together in his decorated clearing in [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]. Urged by [[the-angel|the Angel]], who has intuited his gift from the "brightness of his hair, the fire of his eyes, the breadth of his chest," [[freckles|Freckles]] sings a children's fairyland song; his voice, with its intense sweetness and rollicking quality, fills the swamp while [[the-angel|the Angel]] shivers in ecstasy. The intimate musical communion is suddenly interrupted by the sharply beating hooves of the Bird Woman's horse, bringing news of [[wessner|Wessner]] and [[black-jack|Black Jack]] cutting timber — the idyll shattered by the claim of duty (006-050, 006-055).

## Context

This scene occurs on a summer day when [[the-angel|the Angel]] has been visiting [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] regularly to assist [[the-bird-woman|the Bird Woman]] with her photography of the rare black vulture chick — Little Chicken (006-017). The arrangement had been that Freckles should entertain the Angel at his study or on the line while the Bird Woman worked (006-023). The two young people have grown comfortable in each other's company. [[the-angel|the Angel]], studying [[freckles|Freckles]] intently, has been pressing him about his unrealized potential. "There is something fine, strong, and full of power in your face," she tells him. "There is something you are to do in this world, and no matter how you work at all these other things, or how successfully you do them, it is all wasted until you find the one thing that you can do best" (006-046). When she asks what he would do if he could do anything, [[freckles|Freckles]] answers without hesitation: "I'd go to Chicago and sing in the First Episcopal choir" (006-047).

[[the-angel|the Angel]] is electrified. She drops her hat — "There! You can see what I'm going to be. Nothing! Absolutely nothing! You can sing? Of course you can sing! It is written all over you" (006-048). In a private reflection that reveals her perceptiveness, she catalogs the physical evidence: "It's in the slenderness of his fingers and his quick nervous touch. It is in the brightness of his hair, the fire of his eyes, the breadth of his chest, the muscles of his throat and neck; and above all, it's in every tone of his voice, for even as he speak it's the sweetest sound I ever heard from the throat of a mortal" (006-048). Her demand is simple: "You go over there and stand before that hedge and sing something. Just anything you think of first" (006-049).

## Account

[[freckles|Freckles]] faces the Angel from his banked wall of brown, blue, and crimson blooms, with its background of solid green — his own handcrafted garden forming the stage for what will become the most personally significant performance of his life. Lifting his face to the sky, he sings the first thing that comes into his mind: a children's song he had led for the little folks at the Home many times, recalled by the Angel's exclamation about fairyland (006-050). The song is innocent, artless — a simple lyric about journeying "to fairyland we go, with a song of joy, heigh-o" — but its very simplicity provides the perfect vehicle for his voice.

Porter's narrative registers the moment with breathless intensity: "No song could have given the intense sweetness and rollicking quality of Freckles' voice better scope. He forgot everything but pride in his work" (006-055). The phrase "pride in his work" is revealing — singing is not merely an amusement for [[freckles|Freckles]] but a form of labor, a craft in which he can take the same satisfaction he takes in guarding the timber. This is the moment when his gift, long hidden in the solitude of the swamp, is first acknowledged by another human being. The emotional transaction is rendered in a single unforgettable image: "He was singing the chorus, and the Angel was shivering in ecstasy" (006-055).

The word "shivering" captures the physicality of the Angel's response — she is not merely pleased but bodily transported. [[freckles|Freckles]]' voice has broken through the barriers of class and circumstance, and for a suspended moment the two exist in pure, unmediated artistic communion. He forgets his missing hand, his orphan past, his dangerous job; she forgets her fine clothes and social station. What remains is the voice and its power to move.

Then the spell is broken. "Clip! clip! came the sharply beating feet of a swiftly ridden horse down the trail from the north" (006-055). Both [[freckles|Freckles]] and [[the-angel|the Angel]] spring toward the entrance to find [[the-bird-woman|the Bird Woman]], who asks with characteristic directness: "Both those revolvers loaded?" (006-058). She has discovered [[wessner|Wessner]] and [[black-jack|Black Jack]] cutting a tree on the east line and has ridden hard to alert [[freckles|Freckles]]. The transition from aesthetic rapture to mortal danger is instantaneous and jarring — a structural technique Porter uses to dramatize the divided nature of Freckles' existence, caught between the beauty he craves and the violence his duty demands.

The Bird Woman outlines her plan: they will separate and creep toward the timber thieves from different sides, giving them "a fusillade that will send them flying" (006-059). She tells [[freckles|Freckles]] to give the Angel one of his revolvers. [[the-angel|the Angel]], hatless and with sparkling eyes, clings around the Bird Woman's waist as they ride off, while [[freckles|Freckles]] runs through the swamp alone, his heart "jarring as it never had before with running" (006-060). The music is over; the work of guardianship resumes.

## Literary Analysis

This scene is structurally the novel's emotional center — a moment of achieved harmony that the plot must then disrupt to generate its final movement. Porter uses the [[music-and-song|music]] as a vehicle for what [[freckles|Freckles]] cannot say in words: his worth, his depth, his capacity for beauty. The Angel had earlier told him that his voice was the "sweetest sound I ever heard from the throat of a mortal" (006-048), and the song scene validates her intuition with sensory proof.

The choice of a children's fairyland song is pointed. [[freckles|Freckles]]' entire life has been a kind of anti-fairy tale — abandoned as an infant, mutilated, institutionalized, and put to dangerous labor. Yet the song he chooses is one of hopeful arrival at a "shore" where "mysteries we will explore" and "beauties will unfold." The song becomes a kind of self-fulfilling prophecy: in singing it for the Angel, he is already arriving at his own fairyland, where beauty and love are possible. [[the-angel|the Angel]], whose very name marks her as a creature from another realm, is the proof that fairyland can intersect with the Limberlost.

The interruption by [[the-bird-woman|the Bird Woman]] is not merely a plot device but a thematic necessity. [[freckles|Freckles]]' life can never be only about music and the Angel; it is also bound up with duty, danger, and the trust [[mclean|McLean]] has placed in him. The two realities — art and violence, beauty and duty — must coexist for the novel's moral vision to hold. [[the-bird-woman|The Bird Woman]] arrives as the agent of this necessary complication, ensuring that the music is not an escape from responsibility but a resource to be carried into it.

## Participants

- **[[freckles|Freckles]]**: The Limberlost guard whose beautiful singing voice reveals the depth of his soul. [[music-and-song|Music]] allows him to express emotions he cannot articulate in words, and his performance for the Angel marks the first time anyone has recognized this gift.
- **[[the-angel|The Angel]]**: The perceptive young woman who intuits Freckles' musical gift and demands to hear it. Her "shivering in ecstasy" is both a validation of his talent and the deepening of their emotional bond.
- **[[the-bird-woman|The Bird Woman]]**: The rider whose urgent arrival shatters the idyll, bringing news of the timber thieves and transforming the scene from concert to crisis.

## Aftermath

The music scene cements [[freckles|Freckles]]' identity as more than a guard. [[the-angel|the Angel]] later cites his [[music-and-song|musical]] sensitivity as evidence of his noble soul when pleading his case to her father (017-128). The scene also foreshadows the later moment when [[freckles|Freckles]], looking over the Angel's head and singing as he never has before, carries her with him on the waves of his voice "away, away into another world" (017-160). The interruption by the Bird Woman — and the subsequent gunfight — establishes the pattern of their relationship: moments of communion perpetually threatened by the demands of a violent world.
