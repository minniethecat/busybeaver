## Summary

In the novel's climactic resolution, the Angel reveals to [[freckles|Freckles]] that she has known about his true parentage for weeks. [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]]'s investigation has confirmed that [[freckles|Freckles]] is of noble Irish lineage. [[the-angel|The Angel]] declares that this knowledge has not changed her feelings — rather, she loves [[freckles|Freckles]] for his bravery, trustworthiness, and the beauty of his soul. Her declaration saves him from despair and seals their union (017-128).

## Context

Throughout the novel, [[freckles|Freckles]] has believed himself an unwanted orphan with no family or history (001-047, 001-052). His missing hand and supposed ugliness have made him feel unworthy of love. Meanwhile, [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]] — a wealthy Irish landowner — has been investigating [[freckles|Freckles]]' background after noticing the boy's distinctly Irish features and spirit. [[mclean|McLean]] and the Bird Woman have quietly learned of [[freckles|Freckles]]' noble origins but have kept the knowledge from him until the right moment. [[the-angel|The Angel]]'s father has shared everything he learned from [[freckles|Freckles]] with her (017-128).

## Account

[[the-angel|The Angel]] confronts [[freckles|Freckles]] directly with her knowledge. She tells him that her father has told her everything he learned about [[freckles|Freckles]]' origins, and that she has known all that [[mclean|McLean]] and the Bird Woman know for several weeks (017-128). She challenges [[freckles|Freckles]]' assumption that his missing hand and uncertain origins make him unworthy of love.

She asks [[freckles|Freckles]] why [[mclean|McLean]] and the Bird Woman should have all the "fine perceptions" — why she cannot also see how brave, trustworthy, and splendid [[freckles|Freckles]] is (017-128). She speaks of seeing how his soul vibrates with [[music-and-song|music]], his love of beautiful things, and his pangs of [[solitude|loneliness]] and heart hunger. She declares that her father is not unreasonable and will not expect her to stop loving [[freckles|Freckles]] — and that telling him so, if it will save him, is exactly what she intends to do (017-128).

[[the-angel|The Angel]]'s declaration fundamentally reorients [[freckles|Freckles]]' understanding of himself. He is no longer a nameless orphan but a man of value and lineage, loved for his essential character rather than his circumstances. The revelation of his noble parentage, combined with the Angel's unconditional love, provides the novel's emotional and thematic resolution.

## Participants

- **[[freckles|Freckles]]**: The protagonist who learns his true noble lineage. The revelation, combined with the Angel's love, allows him to finally accept his worth beyond his physical limitations.
- **[[the-angel|The Angel]]**: The woman who loves [[freckles|Freckles]] for his character. Her declaration — knowing full well the facts of his parentage — proves the depth and authenticity of her love.

## Aftermath

This revelation resolves the novel's central identity question. [[freckles|Freckles]] is no longer defined by his missing hand, his orphan status, or his feelings of unworthiness. [[the-angel|The Angel]]'s love and [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]]'s acknowledgment give him both an identity and a future. The resolution ties together the novel's themes of [[home-and-belonging|belonging]], the search for love and home, and the discovery that worth is determined by character, not birth — even as birth ironically confirms [[freckles|Freckles]]' nobility.

## Key Passages

> "As for my father," she continued, "he at once told me what he learned from you about Freckles. I've known all you know for several weeks. That knowledge didn't change your love for him a particle. I think the Bird Woman loved him more." (017-128)

> "Can't I see how brave, trustworthy, and splendid he is? Can't I see how his soul vibrates with his music, his love of beautiful things and the pangs of loneliness and heart hunger? Must you two love him with all the love there is, and I give him none?" (017-128)

> "My father is never unreasonable. He won't expect me not to love Freckles, or not to tell him so, if the telling will save him." (017-128)
