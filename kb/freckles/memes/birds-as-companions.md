# Birds as Companions

## Summary

Before [[freckles|Freckles]] meets [[the-angel|the Angel]], before he earns the trust of [[mclean|McLean]] and [[duncan|the Duncans]], the birds of [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] are his first community. The goldfinch that perches on the wire near his post, the black vultures he calls his "chickens," and the countless other birds he feeds and studies become substitute companions during long months of [[solitude|solitude]] on [[the-limberlost-timberline|the timberline]]. His relationship with the birds teaches him the gentleness and capacity for observation that will later make possible his bond with [[the-angel|the Angel]].

## The Goldfinch

The goldfinch that appears early in the novel functions as [[freckles|Freckles]]' first real companion. The bird is described with characteristic precision: "a dainty, yellow-coated, black-sleeved, cock goldfinch" that "had remained on the wire for several days past the bravest of all" (003-011). [[freckles|Freckles]] projects his own situation onto the bird—the goldfinch, like him, is small, vulnerable, and determined to hold its ground.

The goldfinch becomes a daily touchstone. "He had sung and swung during the passing of Freckles" (003-011), and [[freckles|Freckles]] addresses him directly: "I'll be thinking... that because I'm coming down the same old way, you needn't be minding me" (006-003). The one-sided conversations reveal the depth of his loneliness—and the depth of his capacity for tenderness.

## Freckles' Chickens

[[duncan|Duncan]] gives the birds their collective name when he observes [[freckles|Freckles]] feeding them through the winter: "he called the birds me swamp chickens" (005-105). [[mclean|McLean]] later expands the joke, noting that the black vultures resemble "Pharaoh's Chickens" of the Old World and christening them "Freckles' Chickens" (008-049). The nickname is affectionate but also revealing—[[freckles|Freckles]] is so solitary that he has adopted an entire flock of wild birds as his dependents.

The feeding of the birds is an act of mutual rescue. Through "the fierce cold of winter the birds of the swamp were almost starving. It is mighty lonely here, and they were all the company I was having" (005-105). [[freckles|Freckles]] saves the birds from starvation; the birds save him from the annihilating loneliness of his post.

## Little Chicken and the Expansion of Care

The discovery and raising of [[little-chicken|Little Chicken]], the baby vulture, represents the fullest expression of [[freckles|Freckles]]' capacity for nurture. The chick becomes a shared project between [[freckles|Freckles]], [[the-bird-woman|the Bird Woman]], and [[the-angel|the Angel]]—a living bridge between his solitary bird-world and the human community he is entering. When [[freckles|Freckles]], in the novel's final pages, asks "Can me Little Chicken spread six feet of wing and sail to his mother?" (020-020), the question confirms that the bird has become family.
