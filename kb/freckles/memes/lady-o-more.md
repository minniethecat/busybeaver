# Lady O'More

## Identity

Lady O'More enters the novel in a moment of dramatic tension, when the Angel confronts her husband in their Chicago hotel suite. She is described as "a beautiful woman with cheeks of satiny pink, dark hair, and eyes of pure Irish blue" (018-115). Her physical beauty is matched by her presence of mind: it is she who interrupts her husband's careless condescension toward the Angel, catching his arm and shaking him with the cry, "Terence! Have you lost your senses? Didn't you understand what the child said? Look at her face! See what she has!" (018-116).

Her loyalty to her husband is absolute yet discriminating. When the Angel asks whether she likes [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]]—testing his character by the testimony of the person who knows him best—Lady O'More replies without hesitation: "Better than anyone in the whole world" (018-126). Pressed further, she declares: "I love him so much that I would give up every one of them with dry eyes if by so doing I could save him" (018-131). This testimony, which even the Angel acknowledges she herself "never, never could" match, convinces the Angel that [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]] is worthy of [[freckles|Freckles]] (018-134).

She is the mother of "three of his sons, two little daughters" (018-129), a household in County Clare that [[freckles|Freckles]] will eventually join. Her maternal competence—managing a large family while supporting her husband through the emotional ordeal of searching for his lost nephew—is evident even in her brief appearances.

## Background

The O'More family history, as related by the Angel, places Lady O'More in the context of a tragic family drama. Her husband is the elder son of an autocratic Irish lord who "would have just his way, no matter about his sweet little wife, or his boys, or anyone" (019-016). The elder son married advantageously, pleasing his father and securing an estate. The younger son—[[freckles|Freckles]]' father—defied his father by falling in love with a poor vicar's daughter, leading to his disinheritance and eventual tragedy.

Lady O'More's position within this family history is one of privilege purchased at the cost of proximity to injustice. She and her husband live in comfort while [[freckles|Freckles]]' parents died in poverty and fire, and [[freckles|Freckles]] himself grew up in a Chicago orphanage. The novel does not dwell on this inequity, but Lady O'More's eagerness to embrace [[freckles|Freckles]]—the speed with which she rushes to the hospital and the warmth with which she treats him—suggests a desire to amend, through personal love, a family wrong that neither she nor her husband created but from which they both benefited.

## Role in the Story

Lady O'More's narrative role is that of character witness and maternal bridge. When the Angel needs to verify that [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]] is a decent man—and not merely an aristocrat with a title—she turns to Lady O'More as the ultimate authority: "[[the-bird-woman|The Bird Woman]] says no one in the whole world knows all a man's bignesses and all his littlenesses as his wife does. What you think of him should do for me. Do you like him?" (018-124). Lady O'More's testimony, delivered with earnest warmth, satisfies the Angel's exacting standards.

She is also the custodian of the physical objects that prove [[freckles|Freckles]]' identity: the miniature portrait of his mother and the bundle of baby clothes. [[the-angel|The Angel]], recognizing their importance, asks for them directly: "You said [[freckles|Freckles]]' old nurse knew his mother's picture instantly. I want that picture and the bundle of little clothes" (018-145). Lady O'More gives them without hesitation, understanding that these artifacts will save [[freckles|Freckles]]' life by proving his mother loved him.

In the novel's final chapter, [[freckles|Freckles]] refers to her warmly as "me Aunt Alice" and declares that "Lady Alice is next to me Angel" in his affections (020-066). She has "talked to me by the hour of me father and mother and me grandmother. She's made them all that real I can lay claim to them and feel that they are mine" (020-066). In giving [[freckles|Freckles]] the story of his own family—stories he was orphaned too young to know—Lady O'More performs the most intimate of restorations.

## Character Analysis

Lady O'More functions as an agent of emotional restoration in a novel deeply concerned with what broken families owe their children. Where [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]] provides [[freckles|Freckles]] with name, title, and inheritance, Lady O'More provides something equally essential: the stories that make lineage feel like [[home-and-belonging|belonging]]. Her hours-long conversations with [[freckles|Freckles]] about his father, mother, and grandmother transform an abstract heritage into a felt inheritance.

Her beauty—those "eyes of pure Irish blue"—is not merely decorative. It serves as a visible marker of the Irish identity that [[freckles|Freckles]] has always possessed but never been able to claim. When the Angel gazes at the miniature portrait of [[freckles|Freckles]]' mother, she sees "a delicately cut face... the lips curving in a smile were his very own" (018-147). Lady O'More, as the living bearer of similar features, bridges the gap between the painted image and the living boy, making visible the family resemblance that confirms [[freckles|Freckles]]' [[home-and-belonging|belonging]].
