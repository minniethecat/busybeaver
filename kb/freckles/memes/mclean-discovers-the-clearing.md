## Summary

After rescuing [[freckles|Freckles]] from the beating he sustained in his fight with [[wessner|Wessner]], [[mclean|McLean]] carries the unconscious boy home and tends his wounds. When [[freckles|Freckles]], in delirium, whispers "we'll repate the chorus softly," [[mclean|McLean]] sets out to find the tree [[freckles|Freckles]] fought to protect. Following the trail to the scene of the fight, he enters [[freckles|Freckles]]' study — and discovers a secret garden of astonishing beauty, with transplanted ferns, a riot of wildflowers, rustic seats, moss carpets, and a long, arched vista Freckles has named the "cathedral." The discovery reveals a dimension of [[freckles|Freckles]]' character that [[mclean|McLean]] had never imagined (004-155, 004-160, 004-162).

## Context

[[freckles|Freckles]] has spent months secretly transforming a clearing near his supply case into a private sanctuary. He had made a large room with the door of the case set even with one side of it (004-045). On three sides, fine big bushes of wild rose climbed to the lower branches of the trees; his walls were mallow, alder, thorn, willow, and dogwood, with a solid mass of pale pink sheep-laurel and yellow St. John's wort below, and amber threads of dodder interlacing everywhere (004-045). At one side cattails grew; in front of them, water-hyacinths. To the left, a natural arrangement of giant trees set in a gradually narrowing space created a long, open vista stretching until lost in the dim recesses of the swamp — Freckles had named this the "cathedral" (004-046).

On either side of the trees forming the first arch, he planted ferns that grew waist-high, and so skillfully was the work done that not a frond drooped because of the change (004-046). Opposite, he cleared a flower bed filled with blue-eyed-Marys, harebells, violets of three colors, wild geranium, cardinal-flower, columbine, buttercups, painted trilliums, orchis, bloodroot, moccasin-flower, hepatica, pitcher-plant, Jack-in-the-pulpit — every flower of the Limberlost that was in bloom or bore a bud (004-046). He built rustic seats, leveled the floor and carpeted it with thick woolly-dog moss, planted wild clematis, bittersweet, and wild-grapevines around his case, and every day added new specimens (004-047). His pride in his room was very great, but he had no idea how surprisingly beautiful it would appear to anyone who had not witnessed its growth and construction (004-047).

## Account

The discovery occurs in the aftermath of violence. [[mclean|McLean]] has carried the unconscious [[freckles|Freckles]] to the [[duncan|Duncan]] cabin, where he and [[sarah-duncan|Mrs. Duncan]] have bathed and massaged the boy's battered body. Before losing consciousness, [[freckles|Freckles]] had gasped: "the tree! Oh, do be looking after the tree! ... He bragged that you nailed it yourself, sir. You'll know it by the bark having been laid open to the grain somewhere low down. Five hundred dollars he offered me — to be — selling you out — sir!" (004-150). His head rolled over and his eyes dropped shut.

[[mclean|McLean]] stands over the sleeping boy, absorbing the cost of his loyalty: "His bright hair waved on the pillow. His face was swollen, and purple with bruises. His left arm, with the hand battered almost out of shape, stretched beside him, and the right, with no hand at all, lay across a chest that was a mass of purple welts" (004-155). The Boss's mind travels back to the night, almost a year before, when he had engaged [[freckles|Freckles]] as a stranger. The arc of transformation is staggering.

Following the trail to the scene of the fight and then into [[freckles|Freckles]]' study, [[mclean|McLean]] enters quietly "as if his spirit, keeping there, might be roused, and gazed around with astonished eyes" (004-158). The narrative voice rises to meet the moment: "How had the boy conceived it? What a picture he had wrought in living colors! He had the heart of a painter. He had the soul of a poet" (004-160). [[mclean|McLean]] steps carefully over the velvet carpet, touching the walls of crisp verdure with gentle fingers. He stands long beside the flower bed, gazing at the banked wall of bright bloom as if he doubted its reality.

The ferns arrest him. "Where had Freckles ever found, and how had he transplanted such ferns? As McLean turned from them he stopped suddenly" (004-160). He has reached the door of the cathedral. The physical description that follows is among the novel's most elevated: "That which Freckles had attempted would have been patent to anyone. What had been in the heart of the shy, silent boy when he had found that long, dim stretch of forest, decorated its entrance, cleared and smoothed its aisle, and carpeted its altar? What veriest work of God was in these mighty living pillars and the arched dome of green! How similar to stained cathedral windows were the long openings between the trees, filled with rifts of blue, rays of gold, and the shifting emerald of leaves! Where could be found mosaics to match this aisle paved with living color and glowing light?" (004-160).

The discovery culminates in the novel's most resonant question and answer: "Was Freckles a devout Christian, and did he worship here? Or was he an untaught heathen, and down this vista of entrancing loveliness did Pan come piping, and dryads, nymphs, and fairies dance for him? Who can fathom the heart of a boy? McLean had been thinking of Freckles as a creature of unswerving honesty, courage, and faithfulness. Here was evidence of a heart aching for beauty, art, companionship, worship. It was writ large all over the floor, walls, and furnishing of that little Limberlost clearing" (004-162).

## Literary Analysis

This scene accomplishes in a single chapter what the novel's plot has been building toward for over a hundred pages: the revelation that [[freckles|Freckles]] is not merely a brave and honest employee but a sensitive soul with an artist's vision. Porter uses the discovery as a kind of dramatic anagnorisis — [[mclean|McLean]]'s recognition of the boy's hidden depth mirrors the reader's own expanding understanding of the character. The discovery transforms their relationship from that of employer and worker to something approaching father and son.

The cathedral imagery is sustained and deliberate. [[freckles|Freckles]] is figured as both priest and artist, his clearing both sanctuary and studio. [[mclean|McLean]]'s question — Christian or pagan? — is left deliberately open because the answer is both and neither: the clearing is the expression of a religious instinct that is fundamentally aesthetic, an inborn reverence for beauty that requires no doctrinal framework. This aligns with Porter's broader project of natural theology, in which the Limberlost itself is the truest scripture.

The timing of the discovery is also significant. [[freckles|Freckles]] is unconscious, his body battered in defense of the trust placed in him. The beauty of the clearing exists in stark counterpoint to the violence he has endured — the same hands that fought [[wessner|Wessner]] with "the wildest fury that ever left the beautiful island" (004-097) also transplanted ferns so skillfully that not a frond drooped. [[mclean|McLean]]'s realization thus encompasses both the boy's physical courage and his spiritual depth, uniting what might have seemed contradictory into a single, complex character.

## Participants

- **[[freckles|Freckles]]**: Unconscious in the [[duncan|Duncan]] cabin while his secret is discovered. The decorated clearing reveals his hidden sensitivity, love of beauty, and artistic capacity — dimensions his rough exterior completely concealed.
- **[[mclean|McLean]]**: The discoverer whose understanding of [[freckles|Freckles]] is permanently transformed. His reaction moves from astonishment to aesthetic appreciation to profound emotional recognition of the boy's aching heart.

## Aftermath

[[mclean|McLean]]'s discovery marks a turning point in his relationship with [[freckles|Freckles]]. Where before he had valued the boy as an exceptional employee, he now recognizes him as a rare soul worthy of investment and elevation. This sets the stage for his later promise to educate [[freckles|Freckles]] and make him his son (005-134). The clearing becomes a recurring setting — [[the-bird-woman|the Bird Woman]] and [[the-angel|the Angel]] visit it in later chapters (005-030, 010-046), and [[the-bird-woman|the Bird Woman]] makes photographic studies of each side before she leaves (010-046), effectively canonizing [[freckles|Freckles]]' secret work as art worthy of preservation. The revelation also foreshadows the discovery of [[freckles|Freckles]]' noble parentage, suggesting that his sensitivity to beauty is not accidental but inherited.
