# Labor and Character

## Summary

Throughout "Freckles," honest labor functions as the primary measure of moral worth. The novel consistently equates faithful work with personal integrity, contrasting Freckles' diligent guardianship of the Limberlost timber with the thievery and sloth of those who would steal it. For a protagonist with no name, family, or social standing, work becomes not merely a means of survival but the very foundation of identity and self-respect.

## Work as Identity

The novel opens with Freckles "truly seeking work" on the corduroy road, "intensely eager to belong somewhere and to be attached to almost any enterprise that would furnish him food and clothing" [000-067]. His desire for work precedes any concern about wages; what he wants most is a place in the world. When McLean hires him, Freckles articulates this precisely: "If I can have a Boss to work for like other men, and a place I feel I've a right to, I will do precisely what you tell me or die trying" [000-291].

This agreement becomes a sacred contract. McLean tells him the job must be done by "a hardy, big fellow with a stout heart and a strong body," and Freckles—physically slight and missing a hand—proves that "the size of a man" matters less than "his grit and faithfulness" [000-191, 000-215]. The work of guarding the timber is dangerous and lonely, requiring Freckles to walk the line daily through snake-infested swamp regardless of weather. He earns thirty dollars a month, a modest sum, and saves almost all of it.

## Faithfulness as Virtue

Freckles' faithfulness on the job becomes legendary among those who know him. Duncan tells him plainly: "Ye're being uncommon faithful, lad, and honest as old Father Time. McLean is trusting ye as he would his own flesh and blood" [000-958]. When Duncan learns that some of the trees Freckles guards are worth a thousand dollars each, he reports to McLean that "nae matter what happens now or ever, dinna ye dare let onythin' make ye believe that Freckles hasna guarded faithful as ony man could" [001-022].

The ultimate test of Freckles' work ethic comes when Wessner attempts to bribe him. Wessner offers him partnership in timber theft, and when Freckles refuses, Wessner attacks him. Freckles fights back with devastating ferocity, defending not just the trees but the entire moral framework of his life. McLean later finds him battered and bleeding but victorious, and calls him a "plucky little idiot" with a mixture of exasperation and profound pride [001-490].

## The Wages of Honest Labor

Perhaps the most powerful expression of the novel's labor ethic comes when Freckles insists on buying the Angel's engagement ring with money he earned himself. Though he has just inherited a substantial fortune as heir to the O'More estate, he will only spend three hundred dollars—his savings from a year's wages—on her ruby.

McLean articulates what this means: "You don't want to buy the Angel's ring with money. You want to give for it your first awful fear of the swamp. You want to pay for it with the loneliness and heart hunger you have suffered there, with last winter's freezing on the line and this summer's burning in the sun. You want it to stand to her for every hour in which you risked your life to fulfill your contract honorably. You want the price of that stone to be the fears that have chilled your heart—the sweat and blood of your body" [006-845].

Freckles' answer is simple: "I'm feeling that I couldn't do any other way" [006-841]. The ring must represent what he has earned, not what he has inherited. His labor has been the one thing that was truly his, and only through it can he offer himself to the Angel.

## Work and Natural Rhythm

The novel also depicts work as the mechanism through which Freckles integrates into the natural world. The routine of walking the line, checking the wires, and feeding his birds gives structure to his days and gradually transforms his fear of the swamp into love. "Nature can be trusted to work her own miracle in the heart of any man whose daily task keeps him alone among her sights, sounds, and silences" [000-396]. His labor places him in the Limberlost and, over time, makes the Limberlost part of him.
