# Indiana

## Overview

Indiana is the state where [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] Swamp is located, forming the geographic foundation of Gene Stratton-Porter's *[[freckles|Freckles]]*. The novel's Indiana is not the settled farmland of the early twentieth-century Midwest but a place of vast, old-growth forests—a landscape of "mighty" trees that draws timber interests from across the continent. [[mclean|McLean]] travels "south to Indiana for oak beams" as part of a timber-buying expedition that spans from Canada to the American South (001-021).

The Indiana of the novel is defined by its swamp: [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]], a two-thousand-acre tract of dense, waterlogged forest that [[mclean|McLean]]'s company has leased for its valuable timber (001-030). It is a place of extraordinary natural wealth and corresponding danger—a landscape where economic value and physical threat are inseparable.

## Description

Indiana is described primarily through its forests. The trees that [[mclean|McLean]] comes to purchase are "of great value" (001-030), specimens of a pre-industrial abundance that the novel treats with something approaching reverence. The swamp itself is characterized by "black swamp-muck" (002-005), dense undergrowth, and a canopy of "gigantic trees" (001-004) that create a "dark, massive background" against which the human action unfolds.

The swamp is also a place of intense sensory experience. The "boom of the bittern" startles [[freckles|Freckles]] on his first patrol (002-004); the "sinuous waving" of marsh-grass in the wind is both beautiful and deceptive (002-004); the "frog-[[music-and-song|music]]" of [[sleepy-snake-creek|the creek]] mouth is "never-ending celebration" (003-031). The Indiana of *[[freckles|Freckles]]* is a landscape that overwhelms the senses, demanding that its inhabitants learn to read its signs.

The seasonal extremes are severe. "The fall and winter changes of weather are abrupt and severe," [[mclean|McLean]] warns [[freckles|Freckles]] (001-034), and the narrative bears this out: "black frost" strips [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] of its vegetation, "cutting the ferns, shearing the vines from the trees, mowing the succulent green things" (002-017).

## Role in the Story

Indiana functions as more than backdrop. The state's geography—specifically, the existence of a large, un-reclaimed swamp—is what makes the story possible. Without [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]], there would be no timber to guard, no timberline to patrol, no setting for the transformation of a Chicago orphan into a swamp naturalist. The novel's thematic preoccupations—the relationship between civilization and wilderness, the transformative power of nature, the value of work that connects a person to a place—are all rooted in the specific ecology and economy of northern Indiana.

The [[limberlost-swamp|Limberlost]] was a real place, drained and developed in the early twentieth century, and Stratton-Porter's novel is in part an elegy for a landscape already vanishing at the time of its writing. The Indiana of *[[freckles|Freckles]]* is thus a place preserved in fiction that no longer exists in fact.
