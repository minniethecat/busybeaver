## Summary

[[duncan|Duncan]], the skeptical Scotch timber worker, dismisses [[freckles|Freckles]]' stories of tame birds as fairy tales fit only for the "wee people." [[freckles|Freckles]] challenges [[duncan|Duncan]] to come see for himself, and on the very next Sabbath, [[duncan|Duncan]] arrives with his wife and children. At the feeding ground, the family witnesses a spectacle of astonishing trust — wild birds flocking to [[freckles|Freckles]], perching on his arms and even his head, while quail and a rabbit join the feast. [[duncan|Duncan]]'s disbelief dissolves into wonder (002-035, 002-036, 002-040).

## Context

[[freckles|Freckles]] has been living alone in [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] for months, and the birds and beasts of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] have become his companions. When day after day the only thing that relieved his utter loneliness was the companionship of the birds and beasts of the swamp, it was the most natural thing in the world that Freckles should turn to them for friendship (002-015). He began by instinctively protecting the weak and helpless and was astonished at the quickness with which they became accustomed to him — when they learned that he was not a hunter, and the club he carried was used more frequently for their benefit than his own (002-015).

As winter stripped the Limberlost bare and his feathered friends departed, [[freckles|Freckles]] conceived the idea of carrying food to the birds, for he saw they were leaving for lack of it (002-016). He added wheat and crumbs, every scrap of refuse food he could find at the cabin — apple parings, turnips, potatoes, stray cabbage leaves and carrots — and tied meat bones to bushes (002-017). One morning he found a gorgeous cardinal and a rabbit side by side sociably nibbling a cabbage leaf, and that instantly gave him the idea of cracking nuts for the squirrels (002-017). His patience and daily care had built a community of trust across the species barrier, a miracle he had nurtured in secret.

## Account

The tension between [[freckles|Freckles]]' claims and [[duncan|Duncan]]'s skepticism builds with comic energy. When [[duncan|Duncan]] gives [[freckles|Freckles]] an ear of corn and tells him to carry it to his "wild chickens in the Limberlost," [[freckles|Freckles]] laughs delightedly and exclaims, "Me chickens! Why didn't I ever think of that before? Of course they are! They are just little, brightly colored cocks and hens!" (002-027). He then makes an audacious claim: his "wild chickens" are a good deal tamer than [[duncan|Duncan]]'s barnyard fowl (002-027). [[duncan|Duncan]]'s response — "Hoot, lad!" (002-028) — is a masterpiece of Scotch incredulity. He ups the rhetorical ante: "Go and tell your fairy tales to the wee people! They're juist brash on believin' things. Ye canna invent any story too big to stop them from callin' for a bigger" (002-030). [[freckles|Freckles]] counters by springing in air and howling with glee (002-033), his Irish temperament finding joy in the coming vindication. [[duncan|Duncan]] formally stakes his position: "I'll come next Sabbath. And I'll believe the birds of the Limberlost are tame as barnyard fowl when I see it, and no sooner!" (002-035).

The very next Sabbath, [[duncan|Duncan]] arrives with his wife and children in tow, and [[freckles|Freckles]] leads them to the feeding ground (002-036). What follows is a staged revelation of escalating wonder. [[freckles|Freckles]]' chickens are awaiting him at the edge of the clearing, cutting the frosty air around his head into curves and circles of crimson, blue, and black, chasing each other and sweeping so closely that they brush him with their outspread wings (002-036). At the feeding ground he sets down his old pail of scraps and sweeps the snow from a small space. As soon as his back is turned, the birds cluster over the food, with a big crow and a couple of jays settling on the rim to feast at leisure (002-037). Then [[freckles|Freckles]] scatters his store, and the spectacle unfolds: "At once the ground resembled the spread mantle of Montezuma, except that this mass of gaily colored feathers was on the backs of living birds" (002-039). From the bushes, with gentle cheeping and throaty chatter as if to encourage each other, come flocks of quail, and before anyone sees it arrive, a big gray rabbit sits in the midst of the feast contentedly gnawing a cabbage leaf (002-039).

The climax comes when [[freckles|Freckles]] removes his cap and fills it with wheat from his pockets. In a swarm, the grain-eaters arise around him as a flock of tame pigeons. They perch on his arms and the cap, and in the stress of hunger, forgetting all caution, a brilliant cock cardinal and an equally gaudy jay fight for a perching-place on his head (002-042). This image — the orphan boy standing in the snow with wild birds perched on his head and arms like a living statue of [[companionship-with-birds|interspecies trust]] — is one of the novel's most striking tableaux. It embodies the central thesis of Freckles' character: that gentleness and patience can overcome the wild's instinctive fear, creating a community of trust where none should exist.

[[duncan|Duncan]]'s conversion is complete and immediate. "Weel, I'm beat," he mutters, forgetting the silence he had imposed on his wife (002-043). His language gathers biblical gravity: "Seein' is believin'. A man wad hae to see that to believe it." He recognizes the event as singular and unrepeatable: "We mauna let the Boss miss that sight, for it's a chance will no likely come twice in a life" (002-043). The depth of his transformation is captured in the tenderness of his next words, whispered to his children: "Everything is snowed under and thae craturs near starved, but trustin' Freckles that complete they are tamer than our chickens. Look hard, bairns! Ye winna see the like o' yon again, while God lets ye live. Notice their color against the ice and snow, and the pretty skippin' ways of them! And spunky! Weel, I'm heat fair!" (002-043). The gruff Scotch teamster has become a poet of the spectacle.

## Literary Analysis

This scene illustrates Porter's use of the [[limberlost-swamp|Limberlost]] as a testing ground for character. [[duncan|Duncan]] is not merely a comic foil but a representative of empirical skepticism — the practical, work-hardened man who demands the evidence of his senses. His capitulation validates [[freckles|Freckles]]' special relationship with nature not as an eccentricity but as an observable, repeatable truth. The event also functions as a theophany of sorts: [[freckles|Freckles]] is momentarily transfigured from a one-handed orphan guard into a figure of [[companionship-with-birds|Edenic harmony]], a boy who has reestablished the prelapsarian trust between humanity and the animal kingdom. The mention of Montezuma's mantle — an image of imperial splendor — elevates the scene from rustic anecdote to something approaching the mythic.

## Participants

- **[[freckles|Freckles]]**: The orchestrator of the spectacle, whose patient months of feeding and protecting have earned the absolute trust of the swamp's creatures. His delight in proving [[duncan|Duncan]] wrong is characteristically Irish, generous, and free of malice.
- **[[duncan|Duncan]]**: The skeptical Scotchman whose conversion is total and transformative. His response is not mere concession but joyful wonder, revealed in his whispered instructions to his children.

## Aftermath

[[duncan|Duncan]]'s conversion from skeptic to believer deepens the bond between him and [[freckles|Freckles]]. From this point on, the entire [[duncan|Duncan]] family follows Freckles' example and speaks of the birds as his chickens (002-036). The event also reinforces the domestic arc of the chapter: a week later, when [[freckles|Freckles]] finds a big pan of steaming boiled wheat on his scrap-pail, he asks [[sarah-duncan|Mrs. Duncan]], "Were you fixing this warm food for me chickens or yours?" and she replies, "It's for yours, Freckles. I was afeared this cold weather they wadna lay good without a warm bite now and then" (002-046). Her gesture — and the kiss [[freckles|Freckles]] presses on her rough, reddened hand in gratitude (002-047) — completes the circle of belonging that this event inaugurates. The birds have become the occasion for [[freckles|Freckles]]' integration into a family, and the Duncans have become "unfailing friends of all the birds" (002-036).
