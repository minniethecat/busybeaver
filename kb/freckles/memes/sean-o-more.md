# Sean O'More

## Identity

Sean O'More is not an individual character but a composite figure representing the Irish immigrant children who populated the Chicago orphanage alongside [[freckles|Freckles]]. He embodies the collective experience that [[freckles|Freckles]] describes: "For the most part we were a lot of little Irishmen together" (001-047). In an institution that processed children by category—the adoptable, the trainable, the unplaceable—[[irish-identity|Irishness]] was a shared condition, a bond that connected [[chicago-orphanage|the orphanage]] children to one another and to a culture across the sea that none of them had ever truly known.

The name "Sean O'More" itself is emblematic. In the novel, "O'More" is the family name [[freckles|Freckles]] recovers—Terence [[lord-o-more|Maxwell O'More]] of Dunderry House, County Clare (019-047). The name Sean—the Irish form of John—is among the most common Irish given names. Together they suggest an everyman figure: the Irish orphan who shared [[freckles|Freckles]]' circumstances but whose story never received the novel's redemptive arc.

The existence of many O'More children in [[chicago-orphanage|the orphanage]] is implied by the fact that [[freckles|Freckles]] was "a lot of little Irishmen together" (001-047). These children, like [[freckles|Freckles]], were likely the products of Irish immigration—children whose parents came to America fleeing famine, poverty, or political oppression, and who were orphaned by the brutal conditions of immigrant life in nineteenth-century cities.

## Background

Irish immigration to Chicago surged during and after the Great Famine of the 1840s, creating dense Irish neighborhoods and a large population of Irish children in need of institutional care when parents died, disappeared, or proved unable to support them. The orphanage [[freckles|Freckles]] describes—a place that processed children for adoption, for labor, and for discharge at legal age—was part of a vast network of institutions that managed the consequences of immigration, industrialization, and urban poverty.

The children in such homes, like [[freckles|Freckles]], were often placed out to farms and businesses when they reached working age. The orphanage superintendent who "bound" [[freckles|Freckles]] to a brutal farmer (001-053) was exercising a power that the system routinely granted: the power to dispose of children's labor as a form of institutional economy. Sean O'More, as a representative figure, would have faced the same risks—being "called up, looked over, and refused a home and love" (001-052)—that [[freckles|Freckles]] describes as the defining ritual of orphanage life.

## Role in the Story

Sean O'More appears in the narrative only through [[freckles|Freckles]]' memory, but his significance is twofold. First, he represents the community that [[freckles|Freckles]] lost when he left [[chicago-orphanage|the orphanage]]. The children who were "a lot of little Irishmen together" shared a language, a humor, a set of cultural references, and the experience of institutional life that no outsider could fully understand. Leaving [[chicago-orphanage|the Home]] meant leaving this community—the only family [[freckles|Freckles]] had ever known.

Second, Sean embodies the alternative trajectory that [[freckles|Freckles]] escaped. The children who found homes "for the other children" were the lucky ones; those who, like [[freckles|Freckles]], were "never like any of the other children" (001-051) faced a future of bound labor, institutional dependency, or the desperate search for work that brought [[freckles|Freckles]] to [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]. Sean O'More stands for the many who never found their [[mclean|McLean]], their Angel, or their lost inheritance.

The Irish identity that [[freckles|Freckles]] carries with him to [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]—the accent, the [[music-and-song|song]], the quick temper, the deep wells of feeling—was shaped in [[chicago-orphanage|the orphanage]] among children like Sean. When [[freckles|Freckles]] sings "a battle [[music-and-song|song]]" before confronting [[wessner|Wessner]], "because [[freckles|Freckles]] was Irish, it was impossible to do it silently" (004-126), he is drawing on a cultural inheritance that was strengthened, not weakened, by his years among the "little Irishmen" of [[chicago-orphanage|the Home]].

## Character Analysis

Sean O'More serves as a reminder that the novel's happy ending is exceptional rather than typical. [[freckles|Freckles]] is saved by a chain of improbable events: [[mclean|McLean]]'s trust, the Angel's love, the Bird Woman's friendship, [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]]'s search, [[the-orphanage-keeper|the matron]]'s timely information. Most orphans of his era received none of these things. Sean O'More, as a figure for the un-redeemed, haunts the margins of [[freckles|Freckles]]' success, a silent witness to what might have been.

The composite nature of this character is appropriate to his function. He is not a person but a population—the thousands of Irish children processed through American institutions in the nineteenth century, most of whom left no record beyond a name in a ledger and a life of labor. To name him "Sean O'More" is to give him the one thing [[freckles|Freckles]] recovered for himself: a name that connects him to his people.

> "Every few days, all me life, I'd to be called up, looked over, and refused a home and love, on account of me hand and ugly face; but it was all the home I'd ever known, and I didn't seem to belong to any place else." (001-052)

> "I'd to go and come like a prisoner, and be working around the Home early and late for me board and clothes. I always wanted to learn mighty bad, but I was glad when that was over." (001-051)

> "Because Freckles was Irish, it was impossible to do it silently, so presently his clear tenor was raised in a battle song." (004-126)

> "He spoke in a mellow Irish voice, sweet and pure. It was scarcely definite enough to be called brogue, yet there was a trick in the turning of the sentence, the wrong sound of a letter here and there, that was almost irresistible." (001-029)

> "You once told me of saving your big bullfrog from a rattlesnake. You knew you risked a horrible death when you did it. Yet you will spend miserable years torturing yourself with the idea that your own mother might have cut off that hand. Shame on you, Freckles!" (018-033)
