# Lord O'More

## Identity

Lord Maxwell O'More is a Member of Parliament representing County Clare, [[ireland|Ireland]], and the paternal uncle of [[freckles|Freckles]]. He enters the narrative late—only in the novel's final chapters—but his role is decisive: he restores to the protagonist the name, lineage, and family connection that [[freckles|Freckles]] has spent his entire life lacking. His card reads "Lord Maxwell O'More, M. P., Killvany Place, County Clare, [[ireland|Ireland]]" (018-089), marking him as a man of both political standing and ancestral estate.

His physical appearance confirms his blood relationship to [[freckles|Freckles]] beyond any doubt. When the Angel first glimpses him, she sees "a man who was, beyond question, of [[freckles|Freckles]]' blood and race" (018-107). The newspaper likeness settles the matter: "It was [[freckles|Freckles]] over again, only older and well dressed" (018-094). Lord O'More speaks "in a soft, mellow, smoothly flowing Irish tone, and although his speech was perfectly correct, it was so rounded, and accented, and the sentences so turned, that it was [[freckles|Freckles]] over again" (018-121).

His title and wealth place him in the Anglo-Irish aristocracy, but his character reveals a man capable of deep feeling. When the Angel confirms she has found his lost nephew, Lord O'More "dropped in his chair, and covering his face, burst into those terrible sobs that shake and rend a strong man" (018-143). He has not merely searched out of duty but out of love.

## Background

Lord O'More is the elder son of the previous Lord O'More—the oldest brother of [[freckles|Freckles]]' father, who was the younger son disinherited for falling in love with the village vicar's daughter rather than marrying for property (019-016). The family history is one of paternal tyranny: the grandfather "always had been spoiled, because he was an only son, so he had a title, and a big estate. He would have just his way, no matter about his sweet little wife, or his boys" (019-016).

While the elder son married the woman his father chose—securing an estate—the younger brother defied their father and was cut off. Lord O'More's own life has been shaped by this family tragedy. He did not abandon his brother's child; instead, after learning of [[freckles|Freckles]]' existence, he crossed the Atlantic and spent three months searching Chicago and the surrounding country for any trace of the lost boy (018-094).

His own household in [[ireland|Ireland]] includes his wife, [[lady-o-more|Lady O'More]], "three of his sons, two little daughters" (018-129), and extended family—a living testament to the family from which [[freckles|Freckles]] was separated.

## Role in the Story

Lord O'More appears at the novel's climax. [[the-angel|The Angel]], having gone to the Chicago orphanage to retrieve [[freckles|Freckles]]' baby clothes, learns from [[the-orphanage-keeper|the matron]] that Irish relatives have been searching for [[freckles|Freckles]] and are about to depart for home (018-073). She races to the Auditorium Hotel and confronts Lord O'More in his suite.

His first reaction—"He carelessly glanced her over with amused curiosity" (018-110)—nearly costs him. [[the-angel|The Angel]], fiercely protective of [[freckles|Freckles]], informs him bluntly: "I came here to see if I could do something—a very great something—for you; but if I don't like you, I won't do it!" (018-113). Her resolve, and her interrogation of [[lady-o-more|Lady O'More]] about his character, establishes that [[freckles|Freckles]]' worthiness is the standard by which Lord O'More himself will be judged.

When at last convinced that the Angel has found his nephew, Lord O'More's emotional collapse reveals the depth of his feeling (018-143). He and [[lady-o-more|Lady O'More]] rush to the hospital, bringing a miniature portrait of [[freckles|Freckles]]' mother—the image that will save [[freckles|Freckles]]' life by proving his mother loved him. [[the-angel|The Angel]] reflects on the portrait: "surrounded by masses of dark hair was a delicately cut face... the lips curving in a smile were his very own" (018-147).

In the novel's final chapter, [[freckles|Freckles]] speaks warmly of his uncle: "Golly, but they're nice, ilegant people. I tell you I'm proud to be same blood with them!" (020-025). Lord O'More has not only restored [[freckles|Freckles]]' name—Terence Maxwell O'More—but has "made me feel me blood and race me own possession" (020-066).

## Character Analysis

Lord O'More functions as the novel's agent of restoration. Where [[freckles|Freckles]] has been defined by loss—of family, of name, of hand, of [[home-and-belonging|belonging]]—Lord O'More arrives to reverse that pattern. His wealth and title are not presented as ends in themselves but as the external validation of what [[freckles|Freckles]] has already proven internally: that he is a person of worth, courage, and honor.

His character is revealed largely through the Angel's scrutiny. She subjects him to what amounts to a moral trial: she interrogates his wife ("What you think of him should do for me. Do you like him?" (018-124)), evaluates his character, and only then decides he is worthy of [[freckles|Freckles]]. In this economy of earned trust, even an aristocrat must pass inspection—a leveling gesture consistent with the novel's democratic spirit.

Lord O'More also embodies the novel's treatment of Irish identity as a source of pride rather than stigma. His [[irish-identity|Irishness]]—his accent, his warmth, his emotional expressiveness—mirrors [[freckles|Freckles]]' own, confirming that the traits [[freckles|Freckles]] has always possessed are not marks of inferiority but of noble lineage.
