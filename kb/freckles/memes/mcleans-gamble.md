# McLean's Gamble

## Summary

The founding act of Gene Stratton-Porter's *[[freckles|Freckles]]* is [[mclean|McLean]]'s extraordinary decision to hire a one-handed orphan boy—a stranger who "at a glance might have been mistaken for a tramp" (001-002)—to guard his valuable Limberlost timber lease. This decision, made in the novel's opening pages, is both a practical gamble and a moral choice. It defines [[mclean|McLean]] as a man whose judgment operates on principles deeper than appearances, and it gives [[freckles|Freckles]] the opportunity to become himself.

## The Risk

The stakes of the gamble are explicitly stated. [[mclean|McLean]] needs "a brave, strong man who will guard it every hour of the day" (001-030). What he gets is a boy with one hand, no experience, and no references. The teamsters see only the disability: "He hasn't but one hand" (001-013). [[mclean|McLean]] sees something else—or perhaps he simply decides to take the chance that others would not.

The practical risk is enormous. The trees [[freckles|Freckles]] is to guard are worth "a thousand dollars each in the furniture" (004-014). Entrusting this fortune to an unknown orphan is either an act of reckless charity or profound insight. The novel's arc proves it to be the latter.

## The Investment

[[mclean|McLean]] does not merely hire [[freckles|Freckles]]—he invests in him. "Next morning found Freckles in clean, whole clothing, fed, and rested. Then McLean outfitted him and gave him careful instruction in the use of his weapon" (002-002). The sequence is paternal: provision, protection, and practical teaching. [[mclean|McLean]] gives [[freckles|Freckles]] not just a job but the tools—material and instructional—to succeed at it.

The investment goes beyond equipment. [[mclean|McLean]] arranges [[freckles|Freckles]]' boarding with [[duncan-household|the Duncans]], ensures he is fed and clothed, and visits regularly to check on his welfare. The hiring is not a transaction but the beginning of a relationship—a fact the novel will confirm in [[freckles-and-mclean|every subsequent interaction]].

## Vindication

[[mclean|McLean]]'s gamble is vindicated not once but repeatedly. When [[duncan|Duncan]] reports on the bribery attempt, his testimony is definitive: "And nae matter what happens now or ever, dinna ye dare let onythin' make ye believe that Freckles hasna guarded faithful as ony man could" (004-028). The boy other men dismissed has proven worthy of the trust that only [[mclean|McLean]] was willing to extend.

The vindication is also [[mclean|McLean]]'s own. In hiring [[freckles|Freckles]], he demonstrated the quality that defines him as a leader: the ability to see past surfaces to the character beneath. His gamble on the boy reveals his own character as surely as it launches the boy's.
