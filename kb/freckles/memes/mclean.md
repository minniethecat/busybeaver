# McLean

## Identity

McLean is the field manager of the [[grand-rapids|Grand Rapids]]-based [[timber-industry|lumber company]] that leases two thousand acres of [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]. Known throughout the novel primarily as "the Boss," he is a man of "intense personality" who commands respect without demanding deference (001-019). A widower who lost his beloved father five years before the novel's events, McLean channels his capacity for paternal care into his relationship with [[freckles|Freckles]]—offering the orphaned boy the trust, [[fatherhood-and-mentorship|guidance]], and eventual recognition that a father would provide.

His physical presence is understated but commanding. He is a man who has earned his authority through competence and fairness rather than through intimidation. "No man of McLean's gangs could honestly say that he ever had been overdriven or underpaid" (001-019)—this reputation for justice is the foundation of his leadership. When he interviews [[freckles|Freckles]], he sees past the shabby clothes and missing hand to the "unswerving candor" in the boy's eyes (001-023).

## Background

McLean inherited his father's property and, with his mother, established a home in [[grand-rapids|Grand Rapids]]. With three partners, he organized the [[timber-industry|lumber company]] whose operations drive the novel's plot. His business involves purchasing standing timber across the Midwest, managing its harvest, and shipping it to mills—a complex enterprise requiring both commercial acumen and the ability to manage rough, often desperate men (001-022).

His personal history is sketched lightly but significantly. "My father was my ideal man, and I loved him better than any other I have ever known. He went out five years ago, but that he would have been proud to leave you his name I firmly believe" (001-064). This rare personal [[trust|confidence]], offered to [[freckles|Freckles]] in their first meeting, establishes the emotional register of their relationship. McLean is not merely hiring a guard; he is recognizing a kindred spirit—someone who, like himself, has lost a father and needs a place in the world.

## Role in the Story

McLean's role is that of the mentor-father: he hires [[freckles|Freckles]], equips him, visits him, trusts him, and ultimately celebrates his triumph. His decision to hire a one-handed orphan for a position of significant responsibility is the novel's founding act of faith. [[duncan|Duncan]], the skeptical teamster, sees this as reckless: "McLean was only meanin' to show ye all [[trust|confidence]] and honor. He's gone and set a high price for some dirty whelp to ruin ye" (004-020). But McLean's trust proves well-placed, and [[duncan|Duncan]]'s skepticism is transformed into admiration.

McLean's visits to [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] structure the narrative's temporal rhythm. "Every week McLean rode to [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]; never on the same day or at the same hour" (002-020). The irregularity of his visits serves both practical and symbolic purposes—he can check on [[freckles|Freckles]]' diligence while also communicating that he cares enough to come, regularly and reliably, through all weather.

His most important act may be the discovery of [[freckles|Freckles]]' decorated clearing. In that moment, McLean realizes that the boy he thought he knew—"a creature of unswerving honesty, courage, and faithfulness"—is also a soul "aching for beauty, art, companionship, worship" (004-162). This recognition deepens their relationship, moving it from employer-employee to something closer to father-son.

In the novel's resolution, McLean plays a crucial role in establishing [[freckles|Freckles]]' true identity and securing his inheritance. He has, in effect, been preparing the boy for this destiny all along—not because he knew the facts of [[freckles|Freckles]]' birth, but because he treated him as worthy of trust and honor from the beginning.

## Character Analysis

McLean represents a model of masculine authority that combines strength with gentleness, competence with emotional availability. He is a capitalist—a man who profits from the extraction of natural resources—but he is also a conservationist of human potential, recognizing and nurturing worth that others have overlooked. His management style, based on fairness and personal connection rather than fear and exploitation, offers an implicit critique of the industrial capitalism the novel otherwise accepts.

His [[fatherhood-and-mentorship|paternal relationship]] with [[freckles|Freckles]] is one of the novel's most carefully developed emotional strands. McLean never formally adopts [[freckles|Freckles]] or explicitly claims a fatherly role; instead, he performs fatherhood through action—hiring, equipping, visiting, trusting, celebrating. This indirection is itself a form of respect, allowing [[freckles|Freckles]] the dignity of earning what a biological father might simply bestow.
