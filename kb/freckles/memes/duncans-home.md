# Duncan's Home

## Overview

Duncan's Home is the cabin near [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]] timberline where the [[duncan|Duncan]] family lives and where [[freckles|Freckles]] boards during his service as timber guard. It is here that [[freckles|Freckles]] first appears "in clean, whole clothing, fed, and rested" after [[mclean|McLean]] outfits him and gives him instruction in the use of his weapon (002-002). The home represents the domestic world at [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]'s edge—the nearest thing to family that [[freckles|Freckles]] has known since the Chicago orphanage.

The [[duncan|Duncan]] household is large, boisterous, and unmistakably alive. [[sarah-duncan|Mrs. Duncan]]'s exclamations ("Weel, I be drawed on!" (004-040)) and the antics of the children—the eldest boy turning somersaults, the baby "bunching over in a sidewise sprawl" (004-041)—create a portrait of rough, affectionate domesticity that stands in warm contrast to the silence and solitude of [[freckles|Freckles]]' patrol.

## Description

The [[duncan|Duncan]] home is a working cabin, not a "splendid residence" like [[mclean|McLean]]'s [[grand-rapids|Grand Rapids]] house. It is a place of practical objects—axes, food, clothing—where domestic life is conducted in close quarters and with scant privacy. The mention of an axe with which [[sarah-duncan|Mrs. Duncan]] has "prized up the" something suggests the improvisational, multi-purpose character of the household's tools (004-041).

The cabin's location near [[the-limberlost-timberline|the timberline]] places it at [[the-limberlost-timberline|the boundary]] between the cultivated world and the wild swamp. [[freckles|Freckles]] can reach it from his patrol route, and it serves as his base of operations—the place where he sleeps, eats, and prepares for his daily rounds. It is not his home, strictly speaking, but it is the closest approximation available, and the [[duncan|Duncan]]s' rough hospitality makes it more than a mere boarding-house.

The household's character is conveyed through its sounds and movements: [[sarah-duncan|Mrs. Duncan]]'s cries of surprise, the eldest boy's acrobatics, the baby's mishaps. This is a place of noise and commotion—the opposite of the "utter [[solitude|loneliness]]" that [[freckles|Freckles]] experienced at [[chicago-orphanage|the orphanage]] and the solitude of his patrol (003-002).

## Role in the Story

The [[duncan|Duncan]] home provides [[freckles|Freckles]] with his first experience of family life. The [[duncan|Duncan]]s are not his relatives, but they feed him, clothe him, worry about him, and celebrate his triumphs. When [[duncan|Duncan]] arrives at the clearing to hear [[mclean|McLean]]'s story of [[freckles|Freckles]]' confrontation with [[black-jack|the timber thief]], his response—"Now the boy is in for sore trouble!" (004-164)—carries the concern of someone who has come to care about the orphan guard's welfare.

The home also serves as a narrative anchor, a place of return where the events of [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] can be processed and narrated. It is to the [[duncan|Duncan]] household that [[mclean|McLean]] comes with news, and from which [[duncan|Duncan]] sets out to investigate. The home is the novel's listening-post—the place where [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]'s secrets are received and interpreted by the human community at its edge.
