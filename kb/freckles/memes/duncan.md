# Duncan

## Identity

Duncan is the head teamster for [[mclean|McLean]]'s lumber operation and the man with whom [[freckles|Freckles]] boards during his first months as a [[limberlost-swamp|Limberlost]] guard. A Scotsman of rough speech and warm heart, Duncan serves as a bridge between the refined world of [[mclean|McLean]] and the Angel and the rough working-class culture of the timber camps. He is, in many ways, the novel's moral barometer—his skepticism must be won over, and when it is, the reader knows that [[freckles|Freckles]]' worth has been genuinely proven (002-035).

Duncan speaks in a broad Scots dialect that Stratton-Porter renders phonetically: "Ye can trust the Scotch to bungle things a' thegither" (004-020). His speech marks his class and ethnicity as clearly as [[freckles|Freckles]]' brogue marks his, establishing him as a member of the immigrant working class that powered America's industrial expansion.

## Background

Duncan was brought from Canada by [[mclean|McLean]] to serve as head teamster—a position of responsibility among the working men. He lives with his wife [[sarah-duncan|Sarah]] in a home near [[the-limberlost-timberline|the timberline]], and it is to this household that [[freckles|Freckles]] is sent for his meals and a place to sleep. The Duncans provide the closest thing to family life that [[freckles|Freckles]] has known since [[chicago-orphanage|the orphanage]].

[[sarah-duncan|Sarah Duncan]], though a minor character, provides one of the novel's most emotionally intelligent moments. When Duncan reports [[freckles|Freckles]]' extraordinary success in taming the wild birds, [[sarah-duncan|Sarah]] observes: "You're a guid woman! You're a michty guid woman! Ye hae a way o' speakin' out at times that's like the inspired prophets of the Lord" (002-056). Her insight—that [[freckles|Freckles]]' ability to befriend wild creatures speaks to his character—is one the novel endorses.

## Role in the Story

Duncan's narrative function is to be the skeptic who is converted. When [[mclean|McLean]] hires a one-handed orphan as a guard, Duncan is appalled: "Ye can trust the Scotch to bungle things a' thegither. [[mclean|McLean]] was only meanin' to show ye all [[trust|confidence]] and honor. He's gone and set a high price for some dirty whelp to ruin ye" (004-020). His skepticism is not cruelty but protectiveness—he cares about [[mclean|McLean]] and the timber operation, and he fears that misplaced trust will lead to disaster.

[[freckles|Freckles]] wins Duncan over through demonstrated competence and character. When Duncan gives him corn to feed "his wild chickens in [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]," he scoffs: "I'll believe the [[companionship-with-birds|birds of the Limberlost]] are tame as barnyard fowl when I see it, and no sooner!" (002-035). The day Duncan actually witnesses [[freckles|Freckles]] surrounded by trusting wild birds, his conversion is complete. From that point forward, he is among [[freckles|Freckles]]' most loyal defenders.

Duncan also provides comic relief through his dialect and his exaggerated reactions, but the comedy never diminishes his dignity. He is a working man of genuine wisdom and deep loyalty, and his friendship matters to [[freckles|Freckles]] precisely because it was honestly earned.

## Character Analysis

Duncan represents the novel's ideal of working-class virtue: he is skilled, loyal, plain-spoken, and possessed of a native wisdom that requires no formal education. His Scottishness parallels [[freckles|Freckles]]' [[irish-identity|Irishness]]—both are immigrants, both speak with marked accents, both must prove themselves in an American context that may distrust them. Their friendship thus has an ethnic dimension, a solidarity of the marginal that transcends their initial wariness.

His relationship with his wife [[sarah-duncan|Sarah]] models the domestic stability that [[freckles|Freckles]] lacks and longs for. The Duncan household—warm, orderly, morally serious—is the first functional family [[freckles|Freckles]] has ever observed at close range. It provides a template for the home he will eventually create with the Angel.
