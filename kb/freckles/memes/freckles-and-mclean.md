# Freckles and McLean

## Summary

The relationship between [[freckles|Freckles]] and [[mclean|McLean]] is the emotional backbone of Gene Stratton-Porter's novel—a bond that transforms a business arrangement into a father-son devotion. [[mclean|McLean]], the Scotch-born Boss of the Grand Rapids Lumber Company, takes the extraordinary risk of hiring a one-handed orphan boy to guard his valuable timber (001-001). What begins as an act of charitable impulse becomes a deep mutual loyalty that culminates in [[mclean|McLean]] saving [[freckles|Freckles]]' life and [[freckles|Freckles]] in turn being revealed as worthy of the trust placed in him.

## The Founding Trust

The relationship is founded on a gamble. From [[the-corduroy-road|the corduroy road]], [[freckles|Freckles]] appears as "a tramp" seeking work (001-002). [[mclean|McLean]] sees something others miss—or simply decides to take the chance. After outfitting [[freckles|Freckles]] with "clean, whole clothing, fed, and rested," the Boss "gave him careful instruction in the use of his weapon" and "showed him around the timberline" (002-002). The actions are paternal: provision, protection, and practical teaching.

[[mclean|McLean]]'s trust is vindicated repeatedly. When the teamsters express doubt, [[mclean|McLean]]'s defense is absolute: "No one can make me believe that Freckles hasna guarded faithful as ony man could" (004-028).

## Emotional Intimacy

Despite the power differential—Boss and employee, older man and boy—the relationship develops genuine emotional intimacy. When [[mclean|McLean]] discovers [[freckles|Freckles]]' transformed clearing and the boy's account of his friendship with [[the-angel|the Angel]], "McLean, smiling on the boy, immediately noticed the difference in him. This flushed, panting, talkative lad was not the same creature who had sought him in despair and bitterness" (009-010). The Boss takes joy in the boy's transformation.

Their physical closeness is mediated by [[nellie|Nellie]], the mare. During difficult conversations, [[freckles|Freckles]] "combed his fingers through Nellie's mane" while [[mclean|McLean]] sat in the saddle above him (013-010). After the beating, when [[mclean|McLean]] finds the injured boy, "McLean leaned in the saddle and drew Freckles to him" (019-100). The horse allows them to be close without the vulnerability of a direct embrace.

## Rescue and Vindication

[[mclean|McLean]]'s paternal role reaches its fullest expression in the rescue. When [[the-angel|the Angel]] races to summon him, [[mclean|McLean]] mobilizes instantly: "McLean sprang to Nellie's back and raced after the Angel" (018-030). He personally carries [[freckles|Freckles]] from the swamp, oversees his medical care, and later visits him in [[chicago-orphanage|Chicago]]—where [[freckles|Freckles]], now revealed as Terence O'More, greets him with the same desperate affection: "Oh Lord, sir, but I'm glad to see you!" (020-020).
