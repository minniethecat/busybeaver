# The Emerald Ring

## Summary

The exchange of rings between Freckles and the Angel represents one of the novel's most symbolically rich sequences. The Angel gives Freckles an emerald ring—"just me color," as Lord O'More observes—while Freckles labors over selecting a ruby for her, insisting on paying for it "with money I earned meself." Together, these rings crystallize the novel's themes of identity, love earned through labor, and the transformation from nameless outcast to a man with a name, heritage, and the right to love.

## The Angel's Emerald: A Gift of Identity

The emerald ring arrives while Freckles lies recovering in the Chicago hospital, newly discovered by his uncle Lord O'More and just learning his true name. He tells McLean: "The ring she ordered for me is finished and they sent it to keep me company. See? It's an emerald—just me color, Lord O'More says" [006-813]. The emerald's deep green connects Freckles to the Irish heritage he has just reclaimed, and he adds: "Every color of the old swamp is in it" [006-821].

Freckles asks for a shamrock leaf to be engraved on the stone, so that "every time I saw it I'd be thinking of the 'love, truth, and valor' of that song she was teaching me" [006-821]. The shamrock—an ancient Irish symbol—and the three virtues of love, truth, and valor weave together Freckles' reclaimed Celtic identity with the Angel's influence on his moral character. The ring is simultaneously a love token, a mark of heritage, and a reminder of the values by which Freckles has lived.

At the novel's close, "the lights of the Limberlost flashed from the emerald on his finger" as Freckles opens a package from the Angel containing a black feather—the same kind of feather whose mysterious descent first awakened his soul and led him to her [006-997]. The ring and the feather together close the novel's symbolic circle.

## Freckles' Ruby: Love Purchased with Labor

Freckles' selection of a stone for the Angel is one of the most revealing scenes in the novel. Presented with a tray of unset jewels "that would have ransomed several valuable kings," he systematically eliminates emeralds ("because that's what she gave me"), pearls ("they are like lily faces... but they haven't the life in them that I want"), and diamonds ("fire and ice ain't like the Angel") [006-853, 006-861, 006-869].

He settles on a ruby, finding in it the red of the Limberlost's wild poppies and cardinal flowers, the campfire light and sunset over Sleepy Snake Creek, "the red of the blood we were willing to give for each other," and finally "the brave, tender, clean, red heart of her" [006-877]. His choice maps the entire emotional landscape of his year in the swamp onto a single gem.

Crucially, Freckles insists on paying for the ruby with three hundred dollars from his own wages rather than from his newly inherited fortune. McLean understands immediately: "You want to pay for it with the loneliness and heart hunger you have suffered there, with last winter's freezing on the line and this summer's burning in the sun... The sweat and blood of your body" [006-845]. This insistence on earning the right to love through honest labor is central to Freckles' character and the novel's moral framework.

## The Rings as Plot Devices

The rings also function structurally within the narrative. The emerald ring is ordered while Freckles is nameless—the Angel commissions it before she discovers his true identity—proving that her love is not contingent on his social status. When she gives it to him, she has already risked everything to find his family, yet she would have loved him regardless. The ruby ring, meanwhile, marks Freckles' first act as Terence Maxwell O'More: a man with a name and heritage who chooses to define himself through his own labor and love rather than through inheritance.

## Key Passages

> "The ring she ordered for me is finished and they sent it to keep me company. See? It's an emerald—just me color, Lord O'More says." [006-813]

> "I asked the Angel to have a little shamrock leaf cut on it, so every time I saw it I'd be thinking of the 'love, truth, and valor' of that song she was teaching me." [006-821]

> "You want to pay for it with the loneliness and heart hunger you have suffered there, with last winter's freezing on the line and this summer's burning in the sun. You want the price of that stone to be the fears that have chilled your heart—the sweat and blood of your body." [006-845]

> "I'm thinking here's me Angel's stone... There's the red of the wild poppies, the cardinal-flowers, and the little bunch of crushed foxfire that we found where she put it to save me. There's the light of the campfire, and the sun setting over Sleepy Snake Creek. There's the red of the blood we were willing to give for each other. It's like her lips, and like the drops that dried on her beautiful arm that first day, and I'm thinking it must be like the brave, tender, clean, red heart of her." [006-877]

> "As Freckles held up the package, the lights of the Limberlost flashed from the emerald on his finger. On the cover was printed: 'To the Limberlost Guard!' Under it was a big, crisp, iridescent black feather." [006-997]
