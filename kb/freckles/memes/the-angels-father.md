# The Angel's Father

## Identity

[[the-angel|The Angel]]'s father is never named in the novel—he is referred to only by his relationship to his daughter or by the narrator's designation, "the Man of Affairs." He is a prosperous businessman of Irish descent who commands a substantial commercial enterprise in the unnamed Midwestern city near [[limberlost-swamp|the Limberlost]]. When [[freckles|Freckles]] first visits his office, he encounters "a tall, spare, keen-eyed man" who addresses visitors "in brisk, nervous tones" (008-028). His business employs "a long line of clerks" and he occupies a private office at its heart, marking him as a man of significant commercial standing (008-028).

His [[irish-identity|Irish heritage]] is established through his daughter's casual revelation: "My father is Irish, and half should be enough to entitle me to that much" of [[freckles|Freckles]]' brogue (005-017). This ethnic connection proves significant, as it establishes an ancestral link between the two families before the O'More lineage is formally revealed. His speech lacks the marked brogue of [[freckles|Freckles]] or [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]], suggesting assimilation across a generation, but his core values—fairness, pride in honest work, and a refusal to judge by appearances—align with the novel's portrayal of [[irish-identity|Irish character]].

His capacity for paternal pride is especially vivid. When [[freckles|Freckles]] fights off [[wessner|Wessner]] to protect the Angel, she observes: "Your father told us you had been scrapping. But he told us why. I'd gladly wear all your cuts and bruises if I could do anything that would make my father look as peacocky as yours did. He strutted about proper" (005-088).

## Background

[[the-angel|The Angel]]'s father has raised his daughter with both material comfort and unusual intellectual freedom. She rides unchaperoned into a swamp, studies birds with scientific interest, carries a banjo, and speaks to working men as equals—all behaviors that reflect a father who valued his daughter's character over conventional feminine constraints. The narrator notes that the Angel "had been so shielded in the midst of almost entire freedom, owing to the circumstances of her life" (018-112), a paradox that describes a particular kind of privileged upbringing: protected, yet unconstrained.

His household is one of means. [[the-angel|The Angel]] dresses well, keeps a carriage, and moves easily among the professional classes. Yet there is nothing ostentatious about the father's presentation; he works actively at his business and engages directly with visitors regardless of their station. His wealth is presented as the earned reward of industry rather than inherited privilege—making him, in the novel's economic logic, a more deserving figure than the hereditary [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]].

## Role in the Story

[[the-angel|The Angel]]'s father enters the narrative directly when [[freckles|Freckles]] brings his daughter's lost hat to his office. Rather than deliver it to the Angel herself—a social trespass the guard instinctively recognizes—[[freckles|Freckles]] presents it to her father and asks him to convey it, along with his thanks for her intervention with the timber thieves.

This scene is one of the novel's most revealing encounters between social classes. When the father asks why [[freckles|Freckles]] does not deliver the hat himself, [[freckles|Freckles]] replies: "If you were in my place, would you take it to her yourself?" (008-031). The father's immediate answer—"No, I would not"—establishes a moment of mutual recognition between two men who understand the codes that govern social distance even when they respect the person across the divide.

The father proves himself an attentive judge of character. "[[the-angel|The Angel]]'s father looked keenly into the face of this extraordinary young man, for he found it to his liking" (008-039). He recognizes [[freckles|Freckles]]' [[courage-and-honor|integrity]], his instinctive knowledge of propriety, and his genuine concern for his daughter's safety. His subsequent permission for [[freckles|Freckles]] to assist the Angel and the Bird Woman in [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]]—"by all means let her know if you find subjects you think she could use, and we will do anything we can for you, if you will give her what help you can and see that she is as safe as possible" (008-041)—effectively sanctions the relationship that will develop between his daughter and [[limberlost-swamp|the swamp]] guard.

His most crucial role is played offstage. When [[freckles|Freckles]] lies near death in the Chicago hospital, the Angel tells [[mclean|McLean]]: "My father and [[mclean|Mr. McLean]] are so anxious to spend some money, I'll give them a chance" (018-040), implying that her father is funding the detective work that will uncover [[freckles|Freckles]]' parentage. Later, when the Angel confronts [[mclean|McLean]] about her right to love [[freckles|Freckles]], she cites her father's fairness: "As for my father... he at once told me what he learned from you about [[freckles|Freckles]]. I've known all you know for several weeks. That knowledge didn't change your love for him a particle" (017-128). Her father, unlike the men who would presume to decide [[freckles|Freckles]]' fate, trusts his daughter's judgment.

In the novel's final chapter, [[freckles|Freckles]] speaks of "the Angel and her father" joining him, [[mclean|McLean]], and the Bird Woman on a future trip to [[ireland|Ireland]]—an inclusion that confirms the father's enduring place in the family circle the novel has formed (020-062).

## Character Analysis

[[the-angel|The Angel]]'s father represents the novel's ideal of democratic paternal authority. He is wealthy but not arrogant, powerful but not oppressive, protective but not controlling. His willingness to let his daughter love a one-handed orphan of unknown birth—and his active support of that relationship—constitutes the most significant class-leveling gesture in the novel. Where [[lord-o-more|Lord O'More]] validates [[freckles|Freckles]] from above (through blood and title), the Angel's father validates him from within the American social order (through character and earning).

His Irish identity, though lightly worn, is significant. That the Angel's father is of Irish descent creates an ethnic bridge between the wealthy American household and the Irish orphan—a bridge that will eventually be strengthened by the revelation of [[freckles|Freckles]]' own distinguished Irish lineage. The father's comfort with his heritage, his lack of anxiety about assimilation, models the healthy relationship to ethnicity that the novel endorses.
