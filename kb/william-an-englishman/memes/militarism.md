# Militarism

## Overview

Militarism in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* represents one pole of the novel's central ideological conflict — the binary opposition between [[pacifism|pacifist]] anti-militarism and patriotic military enthusiasm. The novel charts [[william-tully|William Tully]]'s gradual conversion from the former to the latter, a transformation catalyzed not by abstract argument but by direct experience of German invasion during his honeymoon in [[belgium|Belgium]]. Hamilton presents militarism not as a simple virtue or vice but as a complex response to the realities of war, examining how individual [[faith|conviction]] is reshaped by circumstance and trauma.

For [[william-tully|William]], militarism begins as the "blackest of crimes" (002-003), a label he inherits from his intellectual mentor [[faraday|Faraday]], and ends as a lived reality when he enlists as a soldier. The novel's treatment of this theme is deeply ambivalent: it critiques the abstract, untested pacifism of the pre-war Fabian left while simultaneously exposing the brutal machinery of militarism that consumes individual identity. [[william-tully|William]]'s conversion is neither celebrated as enlightenment nor condemned as betrayal — it is presented as the tragic but perhaps inevitable response of a decent man to the violation of everything he loves.

## In the Novel

[[william-tully|William]]'s relationship with militarism unfolds in three distinct phases across the narrative. In the pre-war [[london|London]] chapters, he is a committed anti-militarist, absorbing [[faraday|Faraday]]'s rhetoric about militarism being "the blackest of crimes" (002-003) alongside other progressive causes. His anti-militarism is not independently reasoned but part of a package of Fabian-influenced positions — "habitual and ingrained" (006-012) rather than examined. The novel is careful to show that [[william-tully|William]]'s [[faith|belief]]s, however sincerely held, are second-hand: he "quoted her scraps and jerks of anti-militarist propaganda" (014-004) rather than thinking for himself.

The turning point comes in [[belgium|Belgium]], where [[william-tully|William]] and Griselda's honeymoon cottage becomes the site of [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]'s revolutionary operations. Here [[william-tully|William]] encounters a different kind of political engagement — active, dangerous, and willing to use force. The idyllic summer of "manoeuvres and the folly of militarism" (009-009) discussions gives way to the reality of invasion. [[william-tully|William]]'s anti-militarism, formed in the safety of [[london|London]] meeting-halls, proves inadequate when faced with the destruction of his wife and the world she embodied.

The final phase, after Griselda's death, sees [[william-tully|William]]'s "conversion to militarism" (017-005). This conversion is not intellectual but visceral: it arises from grief, rage, and a nascent sense of [[patriotism|national identity]]. The novel is careful to distinguish [[william-tully|William]]'s militarism from jingoism — he does not celebrate war but recognizes its necessity. Hamilton presents this as a painful maturation, a [[grief|loss]] of innocence that mirrors the [[grief|loss]] of Griselda herself.

## Historical Context

In Edwardian and early WWI Britain, militarism was a deeply contested concept. The Boer [[war|War]] (1899–1902) had exposed the British public to the realities of modern [[war|warfare]] and generated a strong [[pacifism|anti-war]] movement. At the same time, the Anglo-German naval arms race and growing tensions on the Continent fueled patriotic military societies and the National Service League's campaign for conscription. The Fabian Society, with which [[william-tully|William]] is associated, was broadly anti-militarist, seeing war as an irrational distraction from domestic social [[progress|reform]].

The outbreak of WWI in August 1914 catalyzed a dramatic shift in British public opinion. The "spirit of 1914" saw mass enlistment, and even many former anti-militarists — famously including Keir Hardie's colleagues in [[labour-party|the Labour]] Party — supported the war effort. Hamilton published *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* in 1919, after the full horror of trench [[war|warfare]] was known, and the novel's ambivalent treatment of militarism reflects this post-war perspective: it neither glorifies the martial spirit nor endorses the naive pacifism that preceded it. The novel stands as a psychological study of how a generation's political certainties were destroyed by the war, a theme it shares with contemporaneous works by Ford Madox Ford, Rebecca West, and Vera Brittain.

## Key Passages

> …the subject of conversation, and "militarism," to him, was the blackest of crimes — the crime of crimes — the sin against the light. (002-003)

This early passage establishes [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war ideological framework. The scare quotes around "militarism" suggest both [[william-tully|William]]'s distance from the concept and Hamilton's narrative irony — the word is a label [[william-tully|William]] has adopted, not a position he has thought through. The religious language ("sin against the light") reveals the quasi-religious character of [[william-tully|William]]'s progressive [[faith|conviction]]s.

> …more contemptuous: his anti-militarism was more habitual and ingrained… (006-012)

Here Hamilton diagnoses the shallowness of [[william-tully|William]]'s [[faith|conviction]]s. "Habitual and ingrained" implies that his anti-militarism is a matter of social environment rather than intellectual [[faith|conviction]] — the product of his circle rather than his conscience. This sets up the novel's central psychological drama: what happens when a habit confronts a crisis?

> …Griselda and his own conversion to militarism; and, unknown to himself, the conversion had already taken place. (017-005)

The pivotal moment of [[william-tully|William]]'s transformation. The phrase "unknown to himself" is crucial — [[william-tully|William]] does not choose militarism; he discovers it already within himself. Hamilton presents the conversion as a psychological fait accompli, a change that has happened beneath the level of conscious de[[freedom|liberation]]. This passage marks the completion of [[william-tully|William]]'s arc from abstract progressive to engaged participant in history.
