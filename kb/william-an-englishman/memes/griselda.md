# Griselda Watkins / Tully

## Overview

Griselda Watkins -- introduced under an alias and later revealed to be Edith Haynes [013-004] -- is the female protagonist of *William---An Englishman* and the novel's most forceful embodiment of the contradictions Hamilton diagnoses in Edwardian progressive politics. She meets William Tully at a suffrage meeting in December 1913 [001-046], when she is "a little under twenty-five" [001-047] and already a seasoned platform speaker and steward in the militant wing of the [[suffrage]] movement. Where William is mild, accommodating, and vaguely radical, Griselda is declarative, combative, and fiercely certain of her convictions. Hamilton's irony cuts both ways: Griselda is the novel's most courageous figure, but her courage is of a kind that proves as brittle as it is bright when confronted with the actual machinery of [[war]].

Hamilton's treatment of Griselda is more complex than a simple celebration of feminist militancy. She is a "piece of blank-minded, suburban young-womanhood caught into the militant suffrage movement and enjoying herself therein" [001-047] -- a description that manages to be both affectionate and devastating. Griselda's political education is genuine, and her physical courage in the face of police violence is demonstrated repeatedly, but Hamilton refuses to let the reader forget that this courage was acquired in a context of startling naivety: a world where "militarism" was the worst word she knew, where Germans were "intelligent people addicted to spectacles, beer and sonatas" [002-004], and where war was something that happened to other people in other countries.

The war changes everything. Griselda endures the same refugee flight as William, shows the same terrified resilience, and--in one of the novel's most arresting scenes--denounces the German soldiers who have separated them as "cowards---you abominable cowards!" [006-019]. Yet her story does not end in transformation but in diminishment. She does not die heroically; she is simply present, then absent, her name gradually unmade: Griselda Watkins, Griselda Tully, Edith Haynes. The woman who once held a platform audience rapt with "words of enthusiastic abuse that flowed from her confident lips" [001-050] is reduced by the machinery she could not imagine into a name on a missing-person list.

## Biography

### Pre-War Life

Griselda Watkins enters the novel already formed by the political commitments that will define her. She is introduced as "his exact counterpart in petticoats" [001-047] -- William's mirror in every respect: age, class, intellectual formation, and political conviction. She is the daughter of parents who have left her "modestly provided for" [001-051], and she lives with an aunt in [[balham]] who has been "long since converted to the Movement (to the extent of being unable to talk of anything but forcible feeding)" [001-051].

Hamilton's physical description of Griselda is unflinchingly specific. She is "inclined to plumpness, had a fresh complexion, a mouth slightly ajar and suggestive of adenoids, and the satisfied expression which comes from a spirit at rest" [001-047]. This is not the romantic-heroine description of Edwardian convention, and it functions as Hamilton's implicit argument about the ordinariness of political commitment: Griselda is not remarkable, and her courage is not innate but acquired--the product of "suffragette training" that Hamilton treats as a genuine form of education.

On the night William first sees her, Griselda wears "a white dress, a sash with the colours of her association and a badge denoting that she had suffered for the Cause in Holloway" [001-048]. The badge is a statement of authenticity; the white dress is a strategic choice. Hamilton notes with characteristic precision that it was "the policy of her particular branch of the suffrage movement to repress manifestations of the masculine type in its members and encourage fluffiness of garb and appeal of manner" [001-048], and that Griselda, who "had a natural weakness for cheap finery, was a warm adherent of the policy, went out window-smashing in a picture-hat and cultivated ladylike charm" [001-048].

### Courtship

The courtship of William and Griselda occupies Chapters I and II of the novel, and Hamilton narrates it with a mixture of tenderness and surgical irony that is her signature register. Their "mating" is described as "an ideal mating" [002-001] because their "minds as it were flowed together and intermingled joyously---minds so alike and akin that it would have been difficult, without hearing the voice that spoke it, to distinguish an utterance of Griselda from an idea formulated by William" [002-001]. This is a compliment in the narrator's voice and a devastating judgment beneath it: two people who share every opinion have no need to think.

Griselda's political militancy is demonstrated dramatically during the Cabinet Minister's meeting scene [002-019 through 002-028]. She is seated in the first row of the balcony, dressed in pale blue with her blouse safety-pinned to her skirt, her hair "secured more tightly than usual by an extra allowance of combs" [002-019] -- measures learned from previous ejections. When she rises to make her protest, she is "rammed back into her chair" [002-020], wrestled by stewards from either end of the row, and finally "bundled in rapid and businesslike fashion to a side entrance of the building" [002-020]. She emerges onto the pavement "minus her rosebuds, her toque and quite half of her hairpins" but holding "tightly grasped in her fingers a crumpled silk necktie which had once been the property of a stout and orthodox Liberal" [002-025]. Hamilton presents the scene as comedy and tribute simultaneously: Griselda is absurd and magnificent.

The most intimate passage of the courtship occurs in [[battersea-park]], where the couple sit on a bench and dedicate their mutual lives "to the service of Progress and Humanity." They repeat a formula of their own invention "softly, one after the other, holding each other's hands":

> Griselda's voice trembled as she vowed, in semi-ecclesiastical phraseology, that not even her great love for William should wean her from her life's work; and William's voice shook back as he vowed in his turn that not even Griselda, the woman of his dreams, should make him neglectful of the call of Mankind and his duty to the holiest of causes. [002-007]

The passage is Hamilton at her most complex. The vow is sincere; the emotion is real; and the language in which it is made is preposterous. Griselda "trembled as she vowed" not because she is insincere but because she is too sincere -- because she has absorbed the rhetoric of her movement so completely that she can no longer distinguish between feeling and formula.

The evening closes with a line whose irony only becomes legible in retrospect:

> "We shall never forget this evening," Griselda whispered as the sun dipped down in glory. "In all our lives there can be nothing more beautiful than this." [002-008]

She is right, but not in the way she means. There will never be anything more beautiful than this -- because everything that follows is war.

### Marriage and Honeymoon

William and Griselda are married on 23 July 1914 in Balham, from the house of Griselda's aunt [003-002]. The ceremony is performed by "an enlightened vicar who had consented to omit the ignoble vow of obedience" [003-002], and the church is "thronged to its doors with comrades and ardent sympathizers" [003-002]. The advanced Press covers the event, and even the non-advanced Press considers it "worth a paragraph" [003-002] because the six bridesmaids "had all done time for assault" [003-002].

The honeymoon destination -- a cottage in the Belgian Ardennes -- is chosen through [[madame-amberg]], a "cosmopolitan female revolutionist understood to be of Russian Polish extraction" who offers the couple the loan of her secluded Belgian property [003-004]. The decision is casual and fateful. Griselda, who has "hankered for another stay abroad" since her fortnight at Interlaken [003-004], "jumped at the offer" [003-004], and William "jumped gladly in unison with her" [003-004].

The early days of the honeymoon are idyllic. Griselda's voice, in the Ardennes silence, "dropped and lost its shrillness" [003-009]. She and William establish a domestic routine: "their daily doings fell naturally into routine; they rose of a morning to coffee steaming on the stove; and, having digested their breakfast, they usually proceeded to walk a little, concluding the exercise by sitting under the trees with a book which William read aloud to Griselda" [004-004]. William works on a tract about the Woman Question, and Griselda "gently but firmly insisted that even in the first ecstasy of their honeymoon he should not lay it aside" [004-004]. She sits reading nearby and "now and again glance up that she might meet his eye and smile" [004-004].

But the idyll is fragile. Hamilton records the first cracks with clinical precision: Griselda "missed the weekly temper into which she worked herself in sympathy with her weekly *Suffragette*" [004-004], and by late August the couple have begun to feel the absence of the "daily whirl and unceasing snarl and argument" [004-004] that constituted their normal lives. Griselda's response to the distant thunder that presages the war is characteristic:

> "Yes," Griselda flared responsively, "it's the fighting spirit---and the Cause calling to us. I've been hearing the call getting louder and louder; we can't stand aside any longer, we haven't the right to stand aside. How can I---how dare I---rest and enjoy myself when there are noble women struggling for freedom, suffering for freedom, keeping the flag flying---?" [004-008]

She mistakes artillery for conscience, war for the Cause calling. It is Hamilton's bleakest joke, and it is entirely at Griselda's expense.

### War and Flight

When the German soldiers arrive -- first as "funny little things" on toy-like horses viewed from a bluff [004-021 through 004-026], then as an actual cavalry patrol -- Griselda's response tracks William's: bewilderment, then disbelief, then terror. The couple watch the soldiers moving through the valley below them with the detachment of spectators at a pageant. "Playing at murder," Griselda comments, "producing the orthodox sigh. She had heard the phrase used by a pacifist orator in the Park and considered it apt and telling" [004-030]. Within hours, the "playing" will be real.

The moment of most intense character revelation comes when Griselda confronts the German soldiers who have detained the couple:

> "Comprends pas," said Griselda, swallowing back tears of rage. "Comprends pas---so it's not a bit of good your talking to us. Parlez pas francais---but that won't prevent me from reporting you for this disgraceful assault. You cowards---you abominable cowards! You're worse than the police at home, which is saying a good deal. I wonder you're not ashamed of yourselves. I've been arrested three times and I've never been treated like this." [006-019]

The speech is supremely Griselda. She cannot speak the language of her captors, but she can speak the language of moral outrage, and she does so with the same fluency with which she once denounced Cabinet Ministers. The comparison to "the police at home" is the speech's sharpest cut: for Griselda, the German soldiers are not incomprehensible agents of a foreign war but merely a more brutal version of a phenomenon she already knows how to categorize and denounce. Her "I've been arrested three times" is both a credential and a cri de coeur -- her training has prepared her for police violence, but not for this.

During the flight southward through [[belgium]], Griselda endures the same degradations as William: separation, fear, the stripping away of every identity marker that once defined her. Hamilton's narration follows William more closely through this section, but Griselda's presence is constant -- a fierce, protective counterpart to William's bewildered passivity. When William falters, Griselda acts; when he intellectualizes, she decides. The war tests her certainties more violently than William's because her certainties were fiercer to begin with.

### Return to England

Griselda's trajectory in the novel's final section is less fully rendered than William's. She returns to England as a refugee, her name a casualty of the war she could not understand. The revelation that "her name was Haynes, Edith Haynes; she had been some weeks in England before she even learned that William Tully was alive" [013-004] is the novel's final act of stripping: the woman who was Griselda Watkins, then Griselda Tully, is now Edith Haynes -- a name that suggests solidity, ordinariness, Englishness. The name she chose is gone; the name her husband gave her is gone; what remains is the name she was born with, the name she had tried to escape.

## Character and Significance

Griselda is the novel's most sustained argument about the relationship between ideology and experience. She arrives at the war armed with a complete conceptual vocabulary: "militarism" is evil, "the Cause" is sacred, "cowards" are cowards. Each of these categories is tested to destruction by the war, and Hamilton records each failure with the precision of a clinician.

The key to understanding Griselda's significance is the passage in which Hamilton describes her pacifism:

> Griselda also was a pacifist---when it suited her and when she had got her way. She believed in a future World-Amity, brought about chiefly by Woman; meanwhile, she exulted loudly and frequently in the fighting qualities of her sex. [002-004]

"Pacifist---when it suited her" is the novel's most economical statement of its central thesis: that ideology is not a set of beliefs but a set of postures, adopted and discarded according to circumstance. Griselda is not hypocritical in any vulgar sense; she is simply a person who has never been required to distinguish between the language in which she expresses her convictions and the convictions themselves. The war makes that distinction impossible to ignore.

Hamilton's treatment of Griselda differs from her treatment of William in one crucial respect: Griselda is granted genuine physical [[courage]], demonstrated in scenes of police violence that are narrated with unironic admiration. When Griselda is dragged from the Cabinet Minister's meeting, "wriggling like a blue silk eel and crowing her indefatigable protest" [002-020], the comedy does not undercut the courage. Griselda's body acts in accordance with her convictions, and her body does not fail her. This is more than can be said for William.

Yet the courage that serves Griselda in the meeting hall is of limited use in the flight through Belgium. The language of moral outrage that works on police constables does not work on soldiers, and the training that prepared her for arrest does not prepare her for war. Hamilton's point is not that Griselda's courage is false but that it is contextual -- a product of a specific political environment that the war annihilates.

In the novel's economy of values, Griselda represents both the necessity and the insufficiency of political commitment. She is necessary because without her, William would have no model of what it means to act on conviction. She is insufficient because her convictions, however sincerely held, are inadequate to the reality they encounter. The novel offers no resolution to this paradox because Hamilton does not believe in such resolutions. Griselda is what she is: brave, blinkered, sincere, absurd, magnificent.

## Key Relationships

### William Tully

Griselda's relationship with [[william-tully]] is the novel's central pairing and its principal vehicle for exploring the dynamics of ideological partnership. They are, as the narrator insists, "two hearts that beat as one" [002-001] -- but the beating, Hamilton implies, is a metronome, not a pulse. Their "prominent blue eyes---they both had prominent blue eyes---looked out upon the world from exactly the same point of view" [002-001]. The physical detail is a moral judgment: to see the world from exactly the same point of view is to see only half of it.

Griselda is the active partner. She arranges William's ticket to the Cabinet Minister's meeting; she initiates their return from the honeymoon; she delivers the denunciation of the German soldiers while William stands mute. Hamilton reverses the expected gender dynamic not to celebrate Griselda's assertiveness but to explore what genuine partnership entails. Griselda loves William not despite his passivity but including it -- and her love, the novel suggests, is the most effective education he ever receives.

### Faraday

[[faraday]] represents the pre-war certainty that Griselda shares and that the war destroys. Where Faraday's certainty survives the war -- he continues to write, to speak, to denounce -- Griselda's does not. Faraday is what Griselda might have become if she had never gone to Belgium: a permanent platform orator, denouncing abstractions from the safety of a London hall.

### Madame Amberg

[[madame-amberg]] provides the [[the-cottage]] that becomes the site of Griselda's honeymoon and the starting-point of her flight. The relationship between the two women -- the young English militant and the older continental revolutionary -- sketches an alternative model of female political community that the novel does not fully develop. Madame Amberg's "tartan blouses, a perennial smile, and a hat that was always askew" [003-004] mark her as a figure from a different political tradition, one in which militancy has not been tamed by the "fluffiness of garb and appeal of manner" [001-048] that defines the English suffrage movement.

### Heinz

The German soldier [[heinz]] represents the human face of the enemy that Griselda's pre-war rhetoric cannot accommodate. Her denunciation of the soldiers as "cowards---you abominable cowards" [006-019] is the language of the platform applied to the battlefield, and its inadequacy is the novel's judgment on all such language.

### Madame Peys

[[madame-peys]], the housekeeper at the cottage, is a minor but significant figure in Griselda's story. It is Madame Peys who tries to warn the couple of the approaching danger in a language they cannot understand, and it is to Madame Peys's farm that they go in search of answers. Griselda's inability to communicate with her -- "I couldn't make out one word she said---only that she was very excited" [004-013] -- is a metaphor for the broader failure of communication that the war represents.
