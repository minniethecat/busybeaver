## Overview

Power in *William—An Englishman* is examined through multiple lenses: the political power of the state and its institutions, the ideological power of public opinion, the physical power of military force, and the personal power — or powerlessness — of the individual caught in historical currents. William Tully's journey is, in large part, an education in the nature of power.

Hamilton is particularly interested in the gap between abstract political power and its concrete manifestations. William, as a platform speaker for socialist and suffragist causes, participates in the rhetoric of power — denouncing capital, the state, militarism — but has never experienced power's rawest forms until he faces a German firing squad. The novel traces his movement from talking about power to being subjected to it.

What distinguishes Hamilton's treatment of power from that of many of her contemporaries is her refusal to sentimentalize powerlessness. William's loss of agency is not presented as moral purification; it is presented as terror, degradation, and the near-dissolution of self. Power, in this novel, is not something to be denounced from a platform but something to be survived.

## In the Novel

Before the war, William's relationship to power is vicarious and rhetorical. He is drawn to Faraday's world of radical politics, where "the People" are invoked as the ultimate source of legitimate power and "Public Opinion and Talk" are treated as forces capable of reshaping society [008-001]. William enjoys "contrasting his power of spending what he liked with the old days of narrow restriction" [001-031] — a modest personal power granted by his mother's death and his modest inheritance.

The novel's treatment of power deepens considerably in its war chapters. When William faces execution, he confronts "the power to inflict death" directly [007-015]. This encounter strips away all the ideological abstractions and leaves him with only physical fear: "For the first time mortal fear had seized him by the throat and shaken him. He knew now that he stood before death itself, and the power to inflict death, and his heart was as water within him."

The power of expression — of language itself — is another theme. In the Belgian cottage, Griselda finds "the silence and loneliness around them" initially overwhelming, yet "the printed word, even the advertisement on the backs of books, had power to act as a tonic" [004-002]. Language has power, but Hamilton is careful to show its limits: "the tumult and amazement of her soul was beyond her glib power of expression" [009-001]. Words can console but they cannot save.

## The Architecture of Impotence

A recurring pattern in the novel is the systematic stripping away of every form of power William believed he possessed. His political rhetoric proves useless against artillery; his modest wealth cannot buy safety; his physical strength is irrelevant against organized military force. The novel traces what might be called an architecture of impotence — a careful dismantling of the modern individual's illusions of agency.

This dismantling reaches its most intense point when William, having "exhausted his powers of endurance alike of body and mind" [009-007], is reduced to pure survival instinct. The experience is transformative precisely because it is annihilating: William must lose every form of power he thought he had before he can understand what kind of being he is. The novel's radical suggestion is that self-knowledge begins where power ends.

Hamilton's architecture of impotence has a political dimension that extends beyond William's individual story. The war, by subjecting millions of men to the same systematic stripping of agency, revealed the fragility of the liberal subject — the autonomous, rational individual posited by Enlightenment thought. William's experience is singular but representative; the novel suggests, without quite saying, that what happened to him happened in various ways to a generation.

## Power and Gender

Hamilton's feminism informs her treatment of power in ways that distinguish this novel from most male-authored war fiction of the period. Griselda's relationship to power is structurally different from William's: as a woman, she has never had access to the forms of power — political, economic, military — that William takes for granted and then loses. Her power has always been relational, exercised through influence rather than command, through persistence rather than force.

This does not make Griselda powerless; on the contrary, she is in many ways the stronger of the two. But her strength is of a kind that the novel's male characters — and the political discourse they inhabit — do not recognize as power. Hamilton's quiet point is that the very definition of "power" is gendered: what counts as power, and who gets to exercise it, are questions that her society has answered in advance.

> Fundamentally William was no more of a coward than the majority of his fellow-men, and, put to it, he would have emulated the shivering little mayor and tried to strut gamely to his end; it was as much as the bravest could do.
