## Overview

The funeral of Griselda Tully is the novel's most devastating event — not because it is grand but because it is almost impossibly small. Buried in a shallow grave in a Belgian village cemetery, wrapped in a sheet rather than a coffin, carried in a farm-cart rather than a hearse, Griselda's body receives the minimum of ritual that decency demands and the maximum that circumstance allows. The "funeral procession of three" [012-023] — a priest, William Tully, and an Englishwoman stranger — constitutes the entirety of the ceremony. There are no mourners, no flowers except a "shapeless little wreath" [012-023] pressed into William's hand by an old Frenchwoman, no service except the words spoken haltingly by a woman who "struggled with the racial shyness where things of the spirit are concerned and, swallowing her tears, spoke the funeral words" [012-023].

The funeral is the pivot upon which the novel turns from the political to the personal, from ideology to experience. Everything before it — the suffrage meetings, the pamphlets, the abstract debates about militarism and international brotherhood — is revealed as preparation for a reality that preparation cannot reach. Everything after it — William's stupefied return to England, his transformation from pacifist to soldier, his discovery of hatred — flows from this moment at the graveside when the woman he loved is lowered into earth without ceremony, without dignity, without meaning.

Hamilton's rendering of the funeral is remarkable for what it refuses to do. She does not make Griselda's death heroic. She does not suggest that the improvised burial is somehow more authentic than a proper funeral would have been. She does not offer consolation. The grave is shallow because the priest and the peasant who dug it were hurried by the approaching German army. The body is coffinless because the village carpenter had already fled. The service is incomplete because the priest — uncertain of the rites for a Protestant — prays silently while a stranger improvises. Every detail of the scene insists that war strips away not only lives but the rituals that make life bearable.

## Context

### The Refugee Flight

The funeral takes place during the chaotic flight of Belgian civilians and stranded foreigners southward from the German advance in August 1914. William and Griselda, on their honeymoon walking tour in the Ardennes, had been cut off from all news of the July Crisis by William's deliberate arrangement that "neither paper nor letter should be forwarded during their absence" [004-004]. They learn of the invasion only when they encounter a German patrol in the forest — an event that begins their transformation from honeymooners to refugees.

The chapters between this first encounter and the funeral trace a progressive stripping-away of every protection and certainty the couple possessed. Their possessions are stolen. Their money becomes useless. Their language isolates them — they speak no Flemish or German. Their physical strength erodes from hunger and exhaustion. By the time they reach the village where Griselda will die, they are no longer the progressive young couple who debated militarism in comfortable London meeting-halls. They are simply two exhausted bodies in a column of exhausted bodies, moving south because the alternative is death.

The novel describes the refugee column as "a stream of fugitives flowing southward" that meets "reinforcements hurrying north" [011-019] — civilians and soldiers moving in opposite directions, their purposes antithetical. It is within this stream, in a village whose name Hamilton does not provide, that Griselda dies and her funeral takes place. The village itself is in the process of dissolution: the sexton has already fled, the *maire* and his clerk have departed "bearing with them the archives of the commune" [012-016], and those tradesmen who remain are preparing to leave. The funeral occurs in a community that is ceasing to exist.

### Griselda's Death

Hamilton does not dramatise the moment of Griselda's death. The reader learns of it indirectly, through William's stunned perception: he has been separated from her during the chaos of the flight, and when he finds her again she is dead. The novel's reticence here is deliberate — Griselda, who has been the novel's most vivid presence, simply ceases, and the abruptness of this erasure is part of the event's meaning. In war, Hamilton insists, people do not die with the narrative shapeliness of Victorian fiction. They are simply there one moment and gone the next, and the living are left to construct what meaning they can from the rubble.

The Englishwoman — a figure who appears only in these chapters, unnamed, a fellow refugee who has taken charge of practical arrangements — tells William that "the funeral must take place immediately" [012-020]. Her urgency is not callousness but necessity: the German army is approaching, the village is emptying, and delay might mean no burial at all. The imperative of the funeral — that the dead must be honoured, that the body must be committed to earth with some form of words — persists even when every institution that normally supports such ritual has collapsed.

## The Scene

### The Making-Do

The funeral is constructed from whatever the dying village can offer. The priest provides a horse and a farm-cart borrowed from a neighbour — the hearse that "at an ordinary funeral" would have remained in the road is replaced by a vehicle that must be manoeuvred through the narrow cemetery gate to prevent its theft by passing fugitives [012-023]. The body, lacking a coffin, is "wrapped in a sheet" with roses scattered across its folds [012-023]. The grave is dug by a "bent-backed old labourer" and the Englishwoman, who "had acquired in a west-country garden some skill in the handling of a spade" [012-016] — a detail that encapsulates the novel's insistence on the incongruity of war: a woman who learned to dig in an English garden now digs a grave in Belgium.

Hamilton's catalogue of what is missing is as important as her account of what is present. There is no undertaker. No flowers except the old Frenchwoman's "shapeless little wreath." No mourners except William and the Englishwoman. No service except the words the Englishwoman speaks. No coffin — Griselda is "lowered with difficulty" into the shallow grave because the ropes used to lower a coffin have nothing to grip [012-023]. Each absence is an accusation: this is what war does to the dead, this is what becomes of ritual when the institutions that sustain it are destroyed.

### The Procession

The funeral procession itself is described with a precision that verges on the ceremonial even as it documents the absence of ceremony:

> They set out, a funeral procession of three, which at other times would have drawn many curious glances; the priest leading the horse and William and the Englishwoman walking side by side at the rear of the cart. [012-023]

The phrase "which at other times would have drawn many curious glances" is characteristic of Hamilton's technique throughout this scene: she describes what is by evoking what should be. The funeral that should have drawn a village's attention draws instead the passing notice of wayfarers, "of whom some bared their heads and crossed themselves, and others were too busy with their own sorrows to give even a thought to the dead" [012-023]. The world's indifference to Griselda's death is not cruel but simply factual — everyone in this landscape has their own dead to mourn or their own survival to secure.

The half-mile journey from the village to the cemetery is the last journey William and Griselda will make together. Hamilton does not tell us what William is thinking during this walk. She tells us only what he does: he walks, he helps lift the body from the cart, he stands with his back to the grave while the priest and the peasant lower the sheet-wrapped body, and finally he "went to the edge of the grave, looked down at the crumpled sheet and then stupidly round at his neighbour" [012-023]. The word "stupidly" captures the quality of William's grief — it is not articulate, not philosophical, not even fully conscious. It is simply the dazed incomprehension of a man who cannot believe what he is seeing.

### The Funeral Words

The Englishwoman's decision to speak the funeral service is the scene's most charged moment. The priest, uncertain of the proper form for a Protestant, prays silently. William stands mute, "expecting something" but unable to provide it. The Englishwoman, who has no official role and no religious authority, steps into the breach:

> She struggled with the racial shyness where things of the spirit are concerned and, swallowing her tears, spoke the funeral words for Griselda. "I am the Resurrection and the Life, saith the Lord: he that believeth in Me, though he were dead, yet shall he live..." [012-023]

The phrase "racial shyness" is crucial — Hamilton identifies the English reluctance to speak of spiritual matters as a national characteristic, a cultural inhibition that the emergency of the moment overrides. The Englishwoman does not speak because she is devout or confident. She speaks because no one else will, and because the dead require words. This is one of the novel's most powerful statements about moral action: it is not the expression of conviction but the filling of a need, the doing of what must be done when the people whose role it is to do it have failed or fled.

## Symbolic Significance

### The Collapse of Social Ritual

The funeral is the novel's definitive image of social collapse. Funerals, in the world of Edwardian England that William and Griselda have left behind, were elaborate affairs — governed by convention, structured by class, supported by an infrastructure of undertakers, florists, stonemasons, and clergy. William's mother's funeral, earlier in the novel, is marked by the social performance it requires: William must maintain "at least until the funeral was over a decent appearance of dejection" [001-005], a phrase that captures the theatrical dimension of Victorian mourning.

Griselda's funeral is the inversion of everything that William's mother's funeral represented. Where the first funeral was social — a performance before neighbours — the second is private, witnessed only by strangers. Where the first was elaborate in its conventions, the second is stripped to the bare minimum. Where the first marked the beginning of William's liberation from domestic constraint, the second marks his entry into a world without constraints of any kind — a world in which nothing is guaranteed, nothing is reliable, and nothing can be taken for granted.

The novel traces an arc from social death to social annihilation: the mother's funeral, for all its constraints, took place within a functioning social order. Griselda's funeral takes place in a social order that has disintegrated. Between these two funerals lies the war, and Hamilton's meaning is unmistakable. War does not only kill people; it kills the forms through which people make sense of death.

### The Collateral Human Cost of War

Hamilton's choice to make the novel's central death the death of a civilian — and a woman — was a deliberate challenge to the conventions of war fiction. Griselda does not die in battle. She is not killed by a bullet while performing an act of heroism. She dies because she is in the path of an invading army, because the ordinary supports of life — food, shelter, safety — have been withdrawn, and because her body, exhausted by the flight, cannot sustain her. Her death is the collateral damage of war in its most literal form: she is not a target, not a combatant, not even, in any meaningful sense, a participant. She is simply someone who was in the wrong place at the wrong time, and the place was wrong because war had made it so.

The funeral makes this collateral nature visible. Every missing element — the coffin, the hearse, the mourners, the service — traces back to the same cause: the German invasion has emptied the village of its capacity to honour the dead. Griselda's funeral is not merely sad; it is diagnostic. It reveals, with terrible clarity, what war takes from those it does not kill.

### The Breakdown of Meaning

The funeral is also, in Hamilton's treatment, a crisis of meaning. The Christian words that the Englishwoman speaks — "I am the Resurrection and the Life" — are the most consoling words in the English liturgy, but in this context they feel like an act of defiance rather than an expression of faith. Hamilton does not tell us whether the Englishwoman believes what she is saying, or whether William finds comfort in it. The novel's silence on this point is eloquent: the funeral provides the form of meaning without its substance, the ritual without the conviction that would make the ritual meaningful.

This is the deepest level of Hamilton's critique. War does not simply kill people; it kills the stories people tell themselves to make death bearable. The Christian promise of resurrection, the Victorian convention of the "good death," the progressive faith in historical improvement — all of these frameworks for understanding mortality are tested at Griselda's graveside and found wanting. What remains is the bare fact of the body in the shallow grave, the crumpled sheet, the silence after the words are spoken.

## Griselda's Role

Griselda is absent from her own funeral — unable to speak, to act, to shape what happens — and this absence is the novel's most radical political statement. Throughout the novel, Griselda has been defined by her capacity for action. Her "suffragette training" has given her what William lacks: physical courage, tactical intelligence, the ability to decide and to act in moments of crisis. She has protected William, managed their flight, negotiated with strangers, and sustained hope when William has succumbed to despair.

Her death reverses this dynamic in the cruelest possible way. The woman who acted is now acted upon. The body that climbed out of windows at suffrage meetings, that defied police and crowds, that carried William through the refugee flight — that body is now inert, wrapped in a sheet, lowered into the earth by hands that are not its own. The funeral is the novel's acknowledgment that Griselda's agency, however formidable, was bounded by the same mortality that bounds everyone's. Courage cannot stop a bullet or a fever. Conviction cannot hold a body together when the body has reached its limit.

And yet Griselda's role in the funeral scene is not purely passive. The Englishwoman who speaks the funeral words is, in a sense, Griselda's surrogate — another woman who steps forward when the men (the priest, William) cannot. The old Frenchwoman who makes the "shapeless little wreath" is another. The funeral, for all its inadequacy, is made possible by women who do what needs to be done — and in this, it is Griselda's funeral in a second sense: it is the kind of funeral she would have arranged, improvised and inadequate but real, if someone else had needed it.

## Key Passages

> The village sexton was of those who had already fled at the rumour of the oncoming German; so that night the Englishwoman, who had acquired in a west-country garden some skill in the handling of a spade, took turns with a bent old peasant in digging a grave for Griselda. [012-016]

Hamilton's juxtaposition of the English garden and the Belgian grave is devastating in its understatement. The skill that was acquired in peacetime, in a setting of cultivation and beauty, is repurposed in a setting of war and death. The "west-country garden" and the shallow grave are the same earth, the same spade, the same human labour — only the context has changed, and the context changes everything.

> They set out, a funeral procession of three, which at other times would have drawn many curious glances; the priest leading the horse and William and the Englishwoman walking side by side at the rear of the cart. The cemetery lay outside the village, a half-mile or so from their starting-point, and they passed wayfarers enough on the road, of whom some bared their heads and crossed themselves, and others were too busy with their own sorrows to give even a thought to the dead. [012-023]

The funeral procession as Hamilton describes it is both a ritual and its negation — a form emptied of the social context that gave it meaning. The three figures, the cart, the half-mile walk, the passing strangers: each element is what would be present at any funeral, and yet the whole is utterly unlike any funeral the novel's Edwardian readers would have recognised. The detail that some wayfarers "bared their heads and crossed themselves" while others were "too busy with their own sorrows" captures the moral economy of the refugee crisis — a world in which grief is so abundant that it has become a currency no one can afford to honour.

> She struggled with the racial shyness where things of the spirit are concerned and, swallowing her tears, spoke the funeral words for Griselda. "I am the Resurrection and the Life, saith the Lord: he that believeth in Me, though he were dead, yet shall he live..." [012-023]

This is the novel's most concentrated image of moral courage. The Englishwoman does not want to speak. She is not good at speaking. The words are not hers — they belong to a tradition she inhabits awkwardly, constrained by the "racial shyness" that Hamilton identifies as the English spiritual inheritance. And yet she speaks, because no one else will, and because the dead deserve words. This is the form of courage the novel ultimately endorses: not the courage of conviction but the courage of necessity, the willingness to do what must be done when you are the only person present who can do it.

> Other formalities in connection with the funeral there were none — for the reason that the *maire* and his clerk, who in ordinary seasons would have devoted much time and stationery to the subject, had departed that evening, bearing with them the archives of the commune. [012-016]

Hamilton's irony in this passage is surgical. The "much time and stationery" that bureaucracy would ordinarily devote to a death — the forms, the certificates, the registers — is contrasted with the brute fact of the body in the ground. The *maire* has fled, taking the archives with him; the archives matter more to him than the dead. But Hamilton is not simply mocking bureaucracy. She is documenting the relationship between social order and mortality: when the order collapses, death becomes a purely physical event, stripped of the administrative and ritual layers that make it legible. The body goes into the ground, and there is no paper to record that it was ever otherwise.
