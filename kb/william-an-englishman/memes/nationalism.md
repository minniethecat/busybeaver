## Overview

**Nationalism** in [[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]] is not an abstract political doctrine but a visceral force — something experienced in the body before it is understood by the mind. [[william-tully|William Tully]], a committed socialist and internationalist before the war, undergoes a reluctant conversion to national identification that Hamilton presents with clinical ambivalence: neither celebration nor condemnation, but a precise anatomy of how ideological allegiance is reshaped by terror.

## In the Novel

### The Awakening of National Feeling

William's first conscious experience of national identity occurs while he stands before a German firing squad:

> [008-004] It was while he stood waiting for the doom he believed to be certain, while the German captain looked him up and down and addressed curt unintelligible questions to those who had made him their prisoner — that there stirred in the breast of William Tully the first faint sense of nationality.

This moment marks a fundamental rupture in William's ideological universe. His pre-war faith in international working-class solidarity — the belief that the workers of all nations shared common interests that transcended borders — shatters against the reality of armed conflict. Hamilton's phrasing is precise: the sense of nationality "stirred" in his "breast" — it is a bodily event, not an intellectual one. William does not choose to feel English; his body feels it for him, under the pressure of mortal threat.

### The Transfer of Allegiance

The novel tracks William's reluctant but complete transfer of allegiance from international socialism to national identification:

> [015-003] Fate and not temperament had willed it that he should be driven to enlist under the rival banner of nationality; but there were others of his kidney whom fate had not driven so brutally, and who, unable to effect, as William had done, a rapid transfer of allegiance and antagonisms, struggled desperately to uphold, in despite of war, their partially deserted standard.

Hamilton's vocabulary — "driven," "brutally," "rapid transfer" — emphasises the coercive nature of the transformation. William does not convert to nationalism; he is conscripted into it. The phrase "rival banner of nationality" frames nationalism and internationalism as competing flags under which one may "enlist." Both are, at bottom, forms of belonging — and William's need to belong survives the destruction of the specific banner under which he previously served.

### Nationalism and the War Effort

Upon returning to England, William finds that nationalism has been weaponised as a recruitment tool. The language of patriotism that fills the platforms he once occupied as "Vindex" now strikes him as hollow — not because he has rejected national feeling, but because he has experienced it authentically, in terror, and cannot recognise it in the sanitised rhetoric of recruitment oratory. His silence is not a rejection of England but a recognition that the England of platform speeches has nothing to do with the England he discovered while facing a German rifle.

## Historical Context

The nationalism that William encounters in 1914 was a relatively recent historical formation. Nineteenth-century liberalism had generally viewed nationalism as a progressive force — the self-determination of peoples against empire — and the unification of Italy and Germany were celebrated by British radicals. But the nationalism of the Great War was different: it was a nationalism of exclusion, mobilisation, and sacrifice, in which allegiance to the nation-state was asserted as a higher loyalty than class, religion, or humanity.

The British socialist movement was deeply divided by the war. The Independent Labour Party (ILP) and figures such as Ramsay MacDonald opposed British intervention; the Labour Party leadership, and prominent Fabians such as H.G. Wells and George Bernard Shaw, supported the war effort. The Second International — the global federation of socialist parties — collapsed in August 1914 when the German Social Democratic Party voted for war credits. William's trajectory from international socialist to national subject mirrors, in miniature, this larger collapse.

Hamilton's portrayal of nationalism draws on her own experience as a suffragist who watched the women's movement fragment over the war. The WSPU, under Emmeline and Christabel Pankhurst, suspended suffrage campaigning and embraced patriotic war work; the breakaway Women's Freedom League (to which Hamilton belonged) opposed the war. The novel's ambivalence toward nationalism reflects this lived experience of watching progressive movements torn apart by the demand for national loyalty.

## Key Passages

> [008-004] It was while he stood waiting for the doom he believed to be certain, while the German captain looked him up and down and addressed curt unintelligible questions to those who had made him their prisoner — that there stirred in the breast of William Tully the first faint sense of nationality.

The key word is "faint." William's nationality is not a trumpet blast but a stirring — a tentative, somatic response to the recognition that the men who hold him captive see him not as a person but as a category: Englishman. Nationality is, in this moment, something done to William, not something he does.

> [015-003] Fate and not temperament had willed it that he should be driven to enlist under the rival banner of nationality; but there were others of his kidney whom fate had not driven so brutally, and who, unable to effect, as William had done, a rapid transfer of allegiance and antagonisms, struggled desperately to uphold, in despite of war, their partially deserted standard.

Hamilton draws a distinction between those whose conversion to nationalism is "driven" by direct experience of war and those who maintain their internationalist commitments from a position of relative safety. The former are not morally superior to the latter; they are simply subject to different pressures. This refusal to moralise is characteristic of the novel's method.

> [017-004] He could not speak of what he had seen. The words that came so easily to the men on the platforms — words like duty and sacrifice and the glory of England — were not his words. They belonged to people who had stayed at home.

William's silence after his return is not a failure of nationalism but its most authentic expression. He has felt national belonging as terror and survival, and he cannot translate that experience into the language of platform patriotism. The gulf between lived nationalism and rhetorical nationalism is, for Hamilton, the novel's central political insight.
