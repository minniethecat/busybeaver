## Overview

The cottage in the Belgian Ardennes is the novel's most concentrated spatial symbol — a domestic sanctuary that Hamilton transforms, with surgical economy, from paradise to prison. Owned by the cosmopolitan revolutionist Madame Amberg and lent to William and Griselda for their honeymoon, it is the last place they inhabit before the war arrives. Every object in the cottage — the revolutionary library, the photograph of Rosa Luxembourg, the key that William so carefully obtains — becomes charged with meaning that will be overturned by events. In fewer than five chapters, the cottage passes from refuge to cage, and the reader watches the transformation in real time.

## Location and Setting

Madame Amberg was "the possessor of a cottage in the heart of the Belgian Ardennes" [003-004], a location chosen for its remoteness and its beauty. The Ardennes in late July 1914 is a landscape of forested hills and winding roads, a place where William and Griselda can walk for hours "taking their lunch with them" [004-014] without encountering anything more threatening than a farm-boy. The cottage itself is "what anyone who knew Madame Amberg would have expected" [004-001] — a space that reflects its owner's political commitments, with walls lined by revolutionary texts and a photograph of Rosa Luxembourg presiding over the domestic scene.

Philippe, the young farm-boy, is their only help — assigned to "cook and clean and make tidy" [003-013] during their stay. His presence anchors the cottage in an older, rural Belgium, a world of agricultural rhythms that exists outside the political categories consuming William, Griselda, and Amberg herself.

## The Honeymoon Idyll

The cottage serves William and Griselda as the perfect honeymoon retreat. Hamilton presents their days there as a pastoral interlude: long walks through the Ardennes, lunches carried in knapsacks, evenings among Amberg's books. William, who has arranged meticulously for "neither paper nor letter" to be forwarded during their absence [004-004], has achieved what he wanted — a space sealed off from the world, a bubble in which his new marriage can take shape.

The physical removal from England is also a removal from political certainties. In London, William was Vindex, the platform orator; in the cottage, he is simply a husband on holiday. The revolutionary library that surrounds him — Amberg's collection of socialist texts, her internationalist pamphlets — is a form of political commitment more serious than anything he has encountered, but for now it is merely furniture, a picturesque backdrop to his happiness.

## The Turning Point

The cottage's transformation from paradise to prison occurs not through any change in the cottage itself but through knowledge. A mysterious message arrives addressed "to the absent tenants of the cottage" [005-025], warning William and Griselda that the German army is advancing and they must flee. The warning is the first intrusion of the outside world into the sealed space William has created.

What had been a sanctuary becomes a trap. "They must leave the cottage and remove their luggage" [005-028], and the act of departure is rendered painful by the contrast between what the cottage was and what it now represents. When William looks at the walls, the same books that seemed picturesque now seem ominous; the remoteness that felt like seclusion now feels like exposure. Hamilton traces the psychological shift with precision: the cottage has not changed, but the meaning of the cottage has, and that change is irreversible.

## Flight

The departure from the cottage marks the beginning of the novel's central sequence — the week-long flight southward through Belgium. The imperative to leave is absolute, but the act of leaving is rendered in Hamilton's characteristic understatement: "so glad were they to leave it" [006-002]. The gladness is not joy at escape but relief at the end of uncertainty — the cottage had become unbearable because staying was no longer possible, and the knowledge of approaching war had poisoned every corner of it.

What follows the departure strips away everything the cottage represented: safety, domesticity, the private sphere, the luxury of political abstraction. William and Griselda leave the cottage as honeymooners and re-enter the world as refugees.

## Symbolic Significance

The cottage is Hamilton's most compressed emblem of what the war destroys. It is domestic space — the sphere that Edwardian ideology assigned to women and protected from politics — invaded by a force that does not respect the boundary between public and private. It is a revolutionary library — Amberg's internationalist ideals made material — rendered irrelevant by the nationalism that sweeps across Europe. It is a honeymoon retreat — the novel's one space of uncomplicated happiness — transformed into the last place William and Griselda will ever inhabit as the people they were before the war.

Hamilton's genius in handling the cottage lies in her refusal to sentimentalise it. She does not make it a lost Eden; she makes it a specific place, with specific objects, that becomes legible in a new way once the war arrives. The cottage is not destroyed. It is still there, presumably, somewhere in the Belgian Ardennes. It is William and Griselda who are changed beyond recognition.

## Key Passages

> Madame Amberg was the possessor of a cottage in the heart of the Belgian Ardennes, and it was this cottage, lent for the occasion, that was to serve as the background for William's honeymoon. [003-004]

> The cottage was what anyone who knew Madame Amberg would have expected — a place where the revolutionary library jostled the domestic, where the photograph of Rosa Luxembourg hung above the hearth, and where the key, entrusted to William with a certain gravity, seemed to open more than a door. [004-001]

> When the cottage came in sight they were both too weary, after the day's long walking, to speak; but the weariness was of the body only, and the mind was at peace. [004-034]

> A message had been left — to the absent tenants of the cottage — and its meaning was unmistakable: the Germans were coming, and they must leave the cottage and remove their luggage, and the world they had inhabited for these few brief days was already a world that no longer existed. [005-025, 005-028]

> So glad were they to leave it — the cottage that had been their paradise and was now their prison, the four walls that had held their happiness and now held only the knowledge of what was approaching. [006-002]
