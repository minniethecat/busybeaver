## Overview

The Belgian Ardennes is a forested region of rolling hills and secluded valleys in south-eastern Belgium. In *William—An Englishman*, it is the setting for William and Griselda Tully's honeymoon, chosen through the generosity of Madame Amberg, a cosmopolitan revolutionary who owns a cottage there. The couple's idyllic retreat into the Ardennes valley, beginning in late July 1914, coincides precisely with the outbreak of the First World War—a fact of which they remain entirely ignorant until German soldiers arrive at their cottage door.

## In the Novel

### The Honeymoon

The selection of the Belgian Ardennes is due to Griselda's acquaintance with Madame Amberg, a Polish-Russian revolutionary who travels the world addressing meetings and writing pamphlets suppressed by Continental police. Madame Amberg owns a cottage "in the heart of the Belgian Ardennes" which she visits only one week a year, spending the rest of her time agitating in London, Vienna, and New York. Hearing of Griselda's wish for a rural honeymoon, she offers the property enthusiastically, babbling "rhapsodically of honeymoons and the joys of the Forest of Arden."

William and Griselda arrive at the cottage in late July 1914. Their existence in the Ardennes valley is one of "mutual affection and study"—reading books they agree with, walking under the trees, taking small excursions to neighbouring villages. They are blissfully free from household cares, attended by Madame Peys, a local woman who does their catering and tidying. William works intermittently on a tract about the Woman Question, while Griselda sits reading nearby, glancing up to meet his eye and smile.

### War Arrives

The couple's isolation is so complete that they remain unaware of the European catastrophe unfolding around them. German forces invade Belgium and occupy the Ardennes while William and Griselda continue their pastoral routine. The war intrudes only when soldiers arrive at the cottage. A German officer, questioning the couple in halting English, "chuckled audibly" upon learning that the pair had been spending their honeymoon in the secluded Ardennes valleys, and "removed his cigar from his moustachioed lips that he might chuckle long and unhindered."

### William's Return in Memory

Much later, stationed at a clerical post in France, William looks eastward from the hills to see the flashes of guns—"war, real war made visible and wickedly beautiful; such war as he had seen in the Ardennes village." The contrast between his present safe monotony and the violence he glimpsed in the Ardennes becomes one of the defining tensions of his wartime experience.

## Symbolic Significance

The Belgian Ardennes is the novel's central symbol of pre-war innocence and the shattering intrusion of history. The valley functions as an Eden—secluded, fertile, seemingly outside time—where William and Griselda live the last days of their progressive faith undisturbed. The name "Ardennes" carries Shakespearean echoes of the Forest of Arden from *As You Like It*, a place of pastoral refuge and transformation. Hamilton sharpens this allusion into bitter irony: in Shakespeare's forest, characters find love and wisdom; in the Ardennes valley, William and Griselda find only the destruction of their world.

The cottage in the Ardennes represents the fragile bubble of private happiness that the war bursts. That William and Griselda could be unaware of a continental war while honeymooning in its path is both comic and terrible—a measure of how completely their ideological preoccupations had insulated them from the actual world.
