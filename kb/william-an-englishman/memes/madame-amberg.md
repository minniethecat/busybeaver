# Madame Amberg

## Overview

Madame Amberg is the novel's most radical character — a "cosmopolitan female revolutionist understood to be of Russian origin" (003-004) whose Belgian cottage serves as the setting for the novel's central drama. She represents a form of political commitment more extreme and more authentic than anything in William Tully's progressive circle: international rather than national, revolutionary rather than reformist, lived rather than discussed. Hamilton's treatment of Amberg is notably sympathetic — she is presented as admirable rather than monstrous, her revolutionary commitment as authentic rather than fanatical.

Amberg's physical absence from most of the novel — she entrusts her cottage to William and Griselda while she pursues revolutionary work elsewhere — paradoxically increases her power. She is a presence defined by absence, her "revolutionary library" (004-004) and the photograph of Rosa Luxembourg that hangs in her cottage serving as material evidence of a political life more serious than anything William has encountered. The key to her cottage, which William obtains, literalizes his entry into a world of genuine political danger.

## Role in the Novel

Amberg functions as a catalyst rather than an active participant. Her cottage — a domestic space transformed into a revolutionary headquarters — is the crucible in which William and Griselda's relationship is tested and transformed. The library she has assembled, the photographs on her walls, the political atmosphere of her home — all of these constitute an argument for a kind of political commitment that William has never encountered: internationalist, willing to use force, indifferent to respectability.

The novel associates Amberg with the broader revolutionary tradition through her library and her connections. Her photograph of Rosa Luxembourg places her in the lineage of revolutionary women who combined political radicalism with feminist commitment. Her "cosmopolitan" identity — she is understood to be Russian but operates across national boundaries — represents an internationalism that the war will both destroy and vindicate.

## Key Passages

> …acquaintance with a cosmopolitan female revolutionist understood to be of Russian origin… (003-004)

The chain of qualifiers — "cosmopolitan," "female," "understood to be" — captures the mystery and exoticism of Amberg's identity. She is defined by what she is not (English, male, knowable) as much as by what she is.

> Madame Amberg's revolutionary library, valuable as it was… (004-004)

The library functions as a metonym for revolutionary politics itself — dangerous ideas housed in a domestic space, "valuable" both intellectually and practically.
