# Madame Peys

## Overview

Madame Peys is the Flemish-speaking farm-housekeeper who tends Madame Amberg's Belgian cottage. Her interactions with William and Griselda — conducted across an impenetrable language barrier — provide the novel's few moments of genuine comedy. Unlike the ideologically fluent characters who dominate the novel, Madame Peys represents the pre-political world of rural life: she exists in relation to land, animals, and seasons rather than ideas, movements, and causes.

Hamilton uses Madame Peys to ground the novel's political drama in ordinary life. While William and Griselda discuss social reform and revolution, Madame Peys milks the cow and tends the garden. This contrast is not presented as critique — Madame Peys is not wiser than the intellectuals — but as perspective, a reminder that most human life proceeds without reference to the abstractions that consume the educated.

## Role in the Novel

Madame Peys serves primarily as a figure of the ordinary, the local, the pre-political. Her presence anchors the Belgian cottage in a world of practical concerns — food, animals, weather — that persists regardless of war and revolution. The language barrier, which frustrates William and Griselda's attempts at communication, is itself a kind of argument: not everyone speaks the language of progress, and not everything that matters can be translated into it.

## Key Passages

> A quarter of an hour's more walking on a dwindling path brought them in sight of their sylvan honeymoon abode; it had originally been built for the use of a woodman, and was a four-roomed cottage on the edge of the wood overlooking the stream and the pasture of the black-and-white cows. Madame Peys (they knew at least her name) unlocked the door and ushered them into the kitchen, where the boy deposited their bags. (003-013)

Madame Peys enters the novel without fanfare — she is simply a name attached to a function, the woman who unlocks the door and ushers the honeymooners in. This anonymous competence is her defining quality: she exists to make the cottage habitable, and Hamilton grants her no more interiority than the task requires.

> While Madame Peys made play with the stewpan and a kettle, the honeymoon couple unpacked their bags and examined their new domain. … And the savoury mess which Madame produced from her stewpan, combined with the no less excellent coffee that followed, dispelled for the moment the sense of mystery and the shadow of the New Idea. (004-002)

The stewpan is Madame Peys's instrument — a symbol of the practical, nourishing, everyday world that her labour sustains. The food she produces pushes back, however temporarily, against the unsettling silence of the valley, anchoring William and Griselda in bodily comfort when their minds are troubled by the unfamiliar.

> From the cares and worries of housekeeping they were blissfully and entirely free; Madame Peys did their catering, taking toll, no doubt, of their simplicity and ignorance of French, but taking it with tact and discretion. Her bills were a weekly trouble to Griselda but not on account of their length; what she disliked was the embarrassing moment when she strove to conceal her complete ignorance of the items and difficulty in grasping the total as set forth in un-English-looking ciphers. (004-003)

The comedy of the language barrier cuts both ways: Madame Peys likely overcharges her guests, but she does so with "tact and discretion," while Griselda's embarrassment at her own incomprehension is more mortifying to her than any financial loss. The passage captures the mutual opacity of two worlds — rural Flemish and urban English — that coexist without understanding each other.

> Like William's announcement, the communication was in French, of a kind — presumably uneducated French if one judged by the writing; and like William the author of the communication (in all likelihood Madame Peys) had placed it in the centre of the table and crowned it with a saucer before leaving. (004-036)

Madame Peys's final act — the pencilled note left under a saucer — is her warning that war has arrived. But it is written in a language her guests cannot read, and in a hand they cannot decipher. The note is the novel's quietest tragedy: a message of mortal urgency that fails to communicate, a gesture of responsibility that arrives too late to matter.
