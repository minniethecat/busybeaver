# Joachim Murat

## Overview

Joachim Murat — "Murat the magnificent" (017-010) — appears in *William—An Englishman* as part of a constellation of Napoleonic military figures whose individual glory serves as counterpoint to the anonymous, mechanized character of WWI. William reads of Murat, Ney, and Augereau — "who had carried marshals' batons" (016-006) — and imagines a form of military life in which individual courage and ambition are recognized and rewarded.

The novel's invocation of Murat is deliberately ironic. The conditions that produced a Murat — a cavalryman who rose from innkeeper's son to King of Naples through sheer military brilliance — have been erased by the machine gun and the trench. William's war offers no marshals' batons, only survival. The gap between Murat's world and William's measures the distance between Napoleonic warfare — still human in scale, still responsive to individual excellence — and the industrialized slaughter of the Western Front.

## Role in the Novel

Murat functions as a symbol of lost military possibilities. He represents a form of warfare that was, whatever its horrors, still legible in terms of individual action and achievement. William's reading about Murat is not escapism but orientation — an attempt to understand what soldiering meant before it meant what it now means.

## Key Passages

> He read of Ney and Murat, who had carried marshals' batons in their knapsacks — until, the fervour of his obsession growing, he dreamed of himself as an avenging leader, and, with a half-confessed idea of fitting himself for the office, applied hours to the study of the art of war through the medium of an obsolete volume on military lore picked up from a secondhand bookstall in the Charing Cross Road. (016-006)

William's discovery of Murat is filtered through his own thwarted desire to fight. The "marshals' batons" — the Napoleonic promise that talent could rise — speaks directly to a man rejected for being two inches too short. His "obsolete" military manual is a double irony: the tactics of a previous century studied by a man who cannot enter the current one.

> Slowly, under the benumbing influence of office routine, his revengeful ambitions faded, and with them his half-acknowledged hope of emulating Murat and Augereau. His interest in the war had been fundamentally a personal interest, and though there had grown up in him, by force of circumstance, a tardy consciousness of his Englishry, it was hardly strong enough to inspire him with pride in a humble and wearisome duty done in the name of England. (017-010)

The phrase "half-acknowledged hope" captures the pathos of William's identification with Murat — it is a hope he cannot quite admit to himself, and its fading under office routine is not a dramatic renunciation but a slow erosion. The war that produced a Murat demanded cavalry charges; the war William serves demands the copying out of documents.

> The beetle simile caught his fancy oddly, and he found himself contrasting it with his old idea of a soldier. After all, the beetle-warrior was a new development — it was impossible to think of Napoleonic heroes as beetles. Yet if they were alive they would have to scuttle too — even Murat the magnificent, and Ney, the Red Lion … (018-028)

The novel's final word on Murat is a soldier's jest: the "beetle-warrior" scuttling into holes to hide from aircraft. Murat "the magnificent" is reduced, in a single sentence, from a cavalry legend to a creature that scuttles. Hamilton's ellipsis at the end is doing real work — it marks the point where imagination fails, where the Napoleonic and the modern cannot be reconciled.
