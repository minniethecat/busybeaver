## Overview

The Belgian honeymoon of [[william-tully|William]] and [[griselda|Griselda Tully]] is the novel's narrative and emotional fulcrum — the brief, luminous interval between the political certainties of pre-war London and the chaos of the German advance. Hamilton constructs the honeymoon as a pastoral interlude, deliberately evoking the [[forest-of-arden|Forest of Arden]] of Shakespearean comedy to heighten the tragic force of the war's intrusion.

## Context

William's choice of the Belgian Ardennes for the honeymoon is characteristic: economical, modestly romantic, and — in his mind — safely "abroad." The walking tour represents a middle-class fantasy of cultured leisure: "a fortnight," Hamilton notes with gentle irony, "of tramping through forests and sleeping in village inns" (004-003). The couple's inexperience — neither has been abroad before — is part of the appeal; foreignness itself is an adventure, before the war gives foreignness a new and terrible meaning.

The practical arrangements are pure William: meticulous, self-satisfied, and catastrophically consequential. His insistence that "neither paper nor letter should be forwarded during their absence" (004-004) severs the couple from the news of the July Crisis. The decision is not foolish — it is the decision of a young man who believes that happiness can be protected by suspending the world's demands, and Hamilton's irony lies in the recognition that this belief, however sympathetic, is also what makes him vulnerable.

## The Event

The honeymoon unfolds in three movements: arrival and discovery, deepening intimacy, and rupture.

The arrival at [[the-cottage|the cottage]] in the Ardennes is rendered as pastoral arrival. Hamilton describes the landscape — wooded hills, winding paths, the cottage itself "half-hidden among trees" — in terms that deliberately evoke the Forest of Arden of *As You Like It*, the Shakespearean green world where social conventions are suspended and identities are remade. William and Griselda, like their Shakespearean counterparts, enter a space of transformed possibility. They are not platform speakers here; they are simply a young couple in love.

The deepening intimacy of the honeymoon is conveyed through small, precise details. William and Griselda walk, talk, read, and discover each other's bodies and habits with the delighted absorption of people who have chosen each other freely. Hamilton's treatment of their physical relationship is characteristically restrained — the novel is a product of 1919 — but the emotional intimacy is rendered with quiet power. The honeymoon is the novel's vision of what happiness looks like, and its fragility is part of its beauty.

The rupture arrives as a German cavalry patrol in Chapter VI. A soldier strikes William, shoves Griselda, and makes off with their belongings. The violence is small by the war's standards — there is no shooting, no death — but it shatters the pastoral world. Hamilton marks the transition with a shift in prose register: the descriptive language of the honeymoon gives way to the urgent, percussive rhythms of flight.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The honeymoon's interruption is the novel's central structural event. Everything before it is prelude; everything after it is consequence. The flight through Belgium — the separation, the capture, the escape, the reunion — is initiated by the cavalry patrol's arrival at the cottage. The war, which was an abstraction discussed at London platforms, becomes an embodied reality in a single moment of physical violence.

Psychologically, the honeymoon's destruction establishes the novel's key dynamic: the tension between memory and experience. William will carry the memory of the honeymoon — of happiness, of intimacy, of the person Griselda was before the war — into every subsequent moment of the novel. The past is not erased by catastrophe; it persists, painfully, as a standard against which the present is measured and found wanting.

Thematically, the honeymoon's interruption dramatises the novel's central argument about the relationship between private life and historical force. William and Griselda did everything right by the standards of their progressive world — they married as equals, they chose a modest and cultured holiday, they arranged for no newspapers to disturb their happiness. None of it mattered. History does not respect good intentions, and the war does not care about companionate marriage.

## Key Passages

> [004-003] The plan was for a walking tour through the Belgian Ardennes — a fortnight of tramping through forests and sleeping in village inns, which William had read about in a guidebook and which, he assured Griselda, would be both economical and romantic.

The detail of the guidebook is perfect: William's experience of the Ardennes, even before he arrives, is mediated by text. He knows the place through reading before he knows it through walking — a pattern that the war will invert, as he comes to know fear not through platform speeches but through the unmediated experience of his body.

> [004-004] He was young, ambitious, full of his own affairs — and he did not want the world to intrude on his happiness.

The structural irony of this sentence — the "world" he bars from the honeymoon is about to intrude with catastrophic violence — is Hamilton's signature technique. She does not condemn William for wanting happiness; she mourns, with him, a world in which happiness requires wilful ignorance.

> [006-013] William stared at the soldiers without a thought of fear, because he did not yet understand what a soldier was.

This sentence marks the exact moment of rupture. Before it, William inhabits the world of civilian assumptions; after it, he will inhabit the world of war. The phrase "he did not yet understand what a soldier was" is devastating in its simplicity — the "yet" contains the entire remainder of the novel.
