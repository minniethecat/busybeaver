# Jay-Blenkinsop

## Overview

Jay-Blenkinsop is a minor character who looms large in William's psychology — a figure of comfortable civilian authority who, precisely because he represents the safety and moral certainty William has lost, becomes the target of an intense, displaced hatred. William is "gripped with a sudden hatred of Jay-Blenkinsop" (015-016), a hatred that is both genuine and misdirected — the civilian cannot be blamed for being civilian, but the soldier cannot help blaming him.

Hamilton's treatment of Jay-Blenkinsop illustrates the psychological mechanism by which wartime hatred finds domestic targets. The enemy is too distant, too abstract; Jay-Blenkinsop, by contrast, is concrete, present, and unambiguously blameworthy in his complacency. The hatred William feels for him is the hatred of the traumatized for the untouched.

## Role in the Novel

Jay-Blenkinsop functions as a psychological lightning rod — a figure onto whom William can project emotions that have no other outlet. His function in the narrative is less as a character than as a symbol: of civilian ignorance, of pre-war complacency, of everything William has lost and can never recover.

## Key Passages

> And her son, young Jay-Blenkinsop, as his habit was, sprawled sideways on one chair and curled his long limbs round another. It was all as it had been; and Faraday, on the platform, was speaking with the accents of yesterday. (015-010)

Jay-Blenkinsop is introduced through his body — sprawling, long-limbed, effortlessly physical — before he ever speaks. This physical description is not incidental: his casual possession of the body that the recruiting officer denied William ("broad deep chest," "long strong limbs") is the root of William's hatred, making Jay-Blenkinsop a reproach before he opens his mouth.

> As he listened he was gripped with a sudden hatred of Jay-Blenkinsop, a hatred that had its roots in envy of his physical perfections; it was for lack of that broad deep chest and those long strong limbs that the recruiting officer would have none of him … and a muscular boy, a potential soldier, lolled hands in pockets and cracked ignorant jests at men who knew better than he did, at the agony of such as himself. (015-016)

Hamilton anatomizes William's hatred with clinical precision: its root is envy, its trigger is physical discrepancy, and its target is a young man who possesses what William lacks — yet refuses to use it. The hatred is psychologically real even as its object is in some sense innocent; Jay-Blenkinsop is merely being what he has always been, but what he has always been has become unbearable to William.

> "You child, you great impudent jackanapes! You stand there and dare to make jokes about the hell that other men have burned in. The flames and the blood and the guns and people dying in the road. You talk blank foolery and laugh about it — you laugh and turn up your nose. You think you're clever — and enlightened — and it sickens me, sickens me to hear you!" (015-019)

William's outburst is the novel's most concentrated expression of the gulf between civilian rhetoric and traumatic experience. The language is not political but visceral — "flames," "blood," "dying in the road" — and the repetition of "sickens me" registers a physical revulsion that no political argument can answer. Jay-Blenkinsop, silenced, becomes not a character but a condition: the state of never having seen what William has seen.

> He shot out a quivering finger at the open-mouthed Mrs. Jay-Blenkinsop. "And his women — would he let them do as they liked with his women? They would if they came here — he can take my word for it they would. Would he ask them in politely and shake hands and give them drinks and let them?" (015-022)

The confrontation expands from son to mother, making the Jay-Blenkinsops a family of civilian innocence. William's rhetorical questions — about violated women, about polite handshakes with invaders — expose the poverty of pre-war anti-militarist abstractions by forcing them to imagine what William has actually witnessed. The "open-mouthed" Mrs. Jay-Blenkinsop is the face of a complacency that cannot survive the question.
