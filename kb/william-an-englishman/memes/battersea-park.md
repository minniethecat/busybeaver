## Overview

Battersea Park is a public park on the south bank of the River Thames in London. In *William—An Englishman*, it serves as the setting for one of the novel's most emblematic pre-war scenes: the evening when William Tully and his fiancée Griselda sit together on a bench and solemnly dedicate their mutual lives to the service of Progress and Humanity. The park, a green space within the Edwardian metropolis, becomes the stage for the couple's earnest—and ultimately ironic—idealism on the eve of the Great War.

## In the Novel

Battersea Park appears in Chapter II during the period of William and Griselda's engagement in June 1914. The couple, both ardent socialist activists, choose the park as the setting for a private ritual: sitting together on a bench, they invent a "suitable formula" and repeat it softly while holding hands, vowing that neither their love for each other nor domestic life will distract them from their higher calling to uplift humanity.

Griselda's voice trembles as she vows, "in semi-ecclesiastical phraseology," that not even her great love for William shall wean her from her life's work. William, in turn, vows that not even Griselda, the woman of his dreams, shall make him neglectful of "the call of Mankind and his duty to the holiest of causes."

Later that same evening, the couple dreams of their future home as "a centre of light and duty" and a meeting-place for the like-minded. They picture themselves as "Powers"—a man and a woman working for progress and destined to leave their mark. The sense of their destiny overwhelms them, and they walk away from the park "with their hearts too full for speech."

## Symbolic Significance

Battersea Park represents the pre-war world of 1914 idealism that the novel systematically dismantles. The dedication scene, with its quasi-religious language and earnest mutual vows, captures the self-conscious progressivism of William and Griselda's generation. The park bench becomes an altar to their secular faith in human perfectibility. Hamilton's treatment of the scene is gently ironic: the reader, looking back from after the war, knows how completely events will overturn the couple's carefully framed dedication to abstract principles.

The fact that this moment of highest idealism takes place in an ordinary London park, not a church or political hall, underscores the couple's conviction that their mission belongs to the everyday world. Yet the park's very ordinariness also foreshadows how easily their dreams will be swallowed by the impersonal forces of history.

## Key Passages

> One evening, not long before their wedding, they sat together in Battersea Park on a bench and dedicated their mutual lives to the service of Progress and Humanity. They had invented a suitable formula for the occasion and repeated it softly, one after the other, holding each other's hands. [002-007]

> That evening, on the bench in Battersea Park, they had dreamed their dreams—while their consciences looked on and smiled. They foreshadowed their home not only as a nest where they two and their children should dwell, but as a centre of light and duty—as they understood duty and light; a meeting-place for the like-minded, where fresh courage could be gathered for the strife with prejudice and evil. [002-009]

> The sense of their destiny awed and elated them—and they walked away from Battersea Park with their hearts too full for speech. [002-009]
