# Labour Party

## Overview

The Labour Party in *William---An Englishman* never appears directly; it is invoked only to be denounced. It exists in the novel as a negative pole -- the institutional left that radicals like [[faraday]] define themselves against, the party of "treachery" and compromise that has betrayed the revolutionary hopes of the working class. Hamilton's treatment of the Labour Party is thus a study in the rhetoric of radical disillusionment: the party is less a political organization with policies and achievements than a symbol of everything the novel's militants reject.

This treatment is historically precise. The Labour Party of the Edwardian era was a young institution, founded in 1900 as the Labour Representation Committee and renamed in 1906. It was the parliamentary vehicle of the trade union movement, committed to gradual reform within the existing constitutional order. To revolutionary socialists like Faraday -- and to William and Griselda, who have absorbed Faraday's vocabulary -- Labour's moderation was not prudence but betrayal. The novel captures this dynamic with characteristic irony: the party that radicals denounce as insufficiently radical is the same party that represents the actual political aspirations of millions of working-class Britons whom Faraday claims to champion.

## In the Novel

The Labour Party enters the novel obliquely, through the speech of its critics. William attends meetings in which "orthodox members of the Labour Party" are denounced by radicals [001-046], establishing Labour as the establishment against which more militant voices define themselves. This is the party's structural function in the novel's political economy: it provides a target, a foil, an object of scorn that allows Faraday, Griselda, and William to feel themselves more advanced, more committed, more revolutionary than the merely parliamentary left.

This dynamic intensifies during the honeymoon. On their walks through the Belgian countryside, William and Griselda discuss "the treachery of the Labour Party" [004-017] -- the phrase already so naturalized in their vocabulary that it requires no explanation or justification. The accusation of treachery is the common currency of the radical left in this period, and Hamilton records it without endorsing or refuting it. The couple's discussion of Labour's sins during their honeymoon walks is both comical -- this is what they talk about in the first weeks of their marriage -- and revealing. Political conviction, for William and Griselda, is not something they do but something they are, and denouncing the Labour Party is as natural to them as breathing.

The theme reaches its most vivid expression in Griselda's delirium. In her fevered state after the flight through Belgium, she raves about "the sins of the Labour Party" [011-010] -- the political vocabulary so deeply internalized that it survives even the dissolution of rational consciousness. Hamilton's irony is at its sharpest here: Griselda, whose world has been destroyed by an actual war, reverts in her delirium to the verbal war she waged against her own side's moderates. The Labour Party, for Griselda, is more real as an enemy than the German army -- a judgment that the novel both invites and undermines.

Faraday himself continues the tradition after Griselda's death. He heaps "scorn upon the leaders of the Labour Party for their treachery to the cause" [015-015], unchanged by the war that has destroyed his protégés. Faraday's scorn, delivered from the safety of a London meeting-hall, is the novel's final judgment on a certain kind of radical politics: the politics of denunciation, in which the real enemy is never the foreign power but always the insufficiently committed ally.

## Historical Context

The Labour Party was founded in 1900 (as the Labour Representation Committee) by an alliance of trade unions and socialist societies, including the Independent Labour Party (ILP), the Fabian Society, and the Social Democratic Federation. Its founding purpose was to secure independent working-class representation in Parliament, distinct from both the Conservatives and the Liberals. The party grew rapidly: from 2 MPs in 1900 to 29 after the 1906 election, and to 42 after the January 1910 election.

The party's relationship with more radical elements on the left was always tense. The ILP, led by Keir Hardie, occupied a position to Labour's left, advocating for comprehensive socialism rather than piecemeal reform. Revolutionary socialists and syndicalists, influenced by continental Marxism and the example of the 1905 Russian Revolution, regarded even the ILP as hopelessly compromised by parliamentarism. It is this tradition -- the revolutionary rejection of gradualism -- that Faraday represents in the novel.

The outbreak of WWI in August 1914 created a crisis for the Labour Party and the broader British left. The party's leader, Ramsay MacDonald, resigned rather than support the war effort, and the ILP maintained a pacifist position throughout the conflict. But many Labour MPs and trade union leaders supported the war, and Arthur Henderson joined Asquith's coalition government in 1915, becoming the first Labour cabinet minister. This division -- between patriotic Labour and pacifist Labour -- replicated in the political sphere the split between patriotic and pacifist suffragettes that Hamilton traces more directly through Griselda.

By the time Hamilton published her novel in 1919, the Labour Party had been transformed by the war. The Representation of the People Act (1918) had tripled the electorate, enfranchising working-class men who had previously been excluded by property qualifications, and the party was poised to become the official opposition -- which it achieved in 1922. The party that Faraday denounces in 1914, in other words, was on the verge of becoming a party of government. Hamilton's novel registers this transformation only indirectly, through Faraday's unchanged rhetoric, but the irony is unmistakable: the institution radicals dismissed as irrelevant was in the process of remaking British politics.

## Key Passages

> ...orthodox members of the Labour Party were denounced in unmeasured language... [001-046]

This early passage establishes the party's structural function in the novel's political world. Labour is the "orthodox" against which radicalism defines itself. The phrase "unmeasured language" is Hamilton at her most diagnostic: the denunciation is performative, excessive, a ritual of radical identity rather than a considered political judgment. William, attending these meetings, absorbs the posture without necessarily understanding the argument.

> ...the treachery of the Labour Party... [004-017]

This phrase, discussed by William and Griselda on their honeymoon walks, is the novel's most concentrated expression of the radical left's attitude toward Labour. "Treachery" is not a policy disagreement; it is a moral accusation. The party has not merely made mistakes; it has betrayed. Hamilton records the phrase without comment, but its appearance in the context of the honeymoon -- the period when the couple should be most absorbed in each other -- suggests the way political language can fill the space that lived experience has not yet occupied.

> ...heaped scorn upon the leaders of the Labour Party for their treachery to the cause... [015-015]

Faraday, after the war, continues to "heap scorn" on Labour's leaders as if nothing had changed. The war that destroyed William and Griselda has left Faraday's rhetoric untouched. This is the novel's most damning comment on a certain kind of radical politics: the politics that can absorb any experience without being altered by it, that can continue to denounce "treachery" after the world that gave the accusation meaning has been annihilated. Faraday's scorn is the novel's final image of pre-war ideology surviving into a post-war world that it cannot comprehend.
