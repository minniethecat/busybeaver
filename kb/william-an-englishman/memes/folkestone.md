## Overview

Folkestone is a port town on the Kent coast of England, a major Channel crossing point during the First World War. In *William—An Englishman*, it is the point of William Tully's return to England after his escape from German-occupied Belgium. The arrival at Folkestone, with its long nocturnal delays and the red dawn over France, marks the moment when William's private grief begins its transformation into public purpose: it is on the train journey from Folkestone to London that he first announces his intention to enlist.

## In the Novel

### The Channel Crossing

William and Edith Haynes travel from Dieppe to Folkestone by boat. The crossing takes place at sundown on a day of "perfect summer" stillness—the same kind of weather, William cannot help remembering, as the day a month earlier when he and Griselda had steamed away from Dover on their honeymoon. The memory of those fading white cliffs overwhelms him: "William's heart cried out against God."

### Arrival at Folkestone

The boat slides into Folkestone Harbour well past midnight, and there are further long delays. The wartime disruption of civilian travel means that morning is already red over France when the train finally draws away from the pier. The darkness and the waiting—the suspended hours between the world William has lost and the world he must now enter—create a liminal space in which his resolve hardens.

### The Train from Folkestone

It is during the two-hour journey from Folkestone to Charing Cross that William first speaks to Edith of his purpose of becoming a soldier. The other occupants of the carriage nod off to sleep soon after the train leaves Folkestone, and "for all practical purposes he and Edith Haynes were alone." In this island of privacy, moving through the Kentish countryside in the early morning, William makes his declaration. Edith is surprised—his appearance and manner are "so utterly unmilitary"—but he begins to explain, in jerks, the conviction that has been forming in him since Griselda's death.

The train journey from Folkestone thus becomes the birthplace of William's soldier-self: the enclosed carriage, the sleeping strangers, the dawn over France behind them, and the unfamiliar words of a man discovering a new purpose.

## Symbolic Significance

Folkestone is the novel's threshold between the Continental catastrophe and England, between William's life as a husband and his life as a would-be soldier. The port's wartime function—a transit point for soldiers and refugees—mirrors William's own state of passage. The "long delays" at Folkestone Harbour extend the moment of transition almost unbearably, as if the war itself were reluctant to let him go. When morning breaks red over France as the train pulls away, the sunrise is both a literal dawn and a symbol of the bloodshed William is about to join. The train compartment, with its sleeping strangers, becomes an intimate confessional where William speaks his new identity into being.
