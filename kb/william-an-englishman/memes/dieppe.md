## Overview

Dieppe is a port town on the Normandy coast of France, situated on the English Channel. In *William—An Englishman*, it serves as a waypoint on William Tully's journey from German-occupied Belgium back to England in the early days of the First World War. The journey through Dieppe marks the period during which William, still reeling from the shock of Griselda's death, undergoes a profound mental transformation and resolves to enlist as a soldier.

## In the Novel

### The Journey Through Dieppe

After escaping Belgium with the help of Edith Haynes, William travels by train across France toward the Channel. The journey is punctuated by long, purposeless wartime halts—at Rouen, at Dieppe Harbour—that stretch the passage into an ordeal of waiting and reflection.

When William takes his seat on the train bound for Dieppe, his mind is "made up—made up fiercely and definitely—on his future course of action." Edith notices the change in him: he is not more resigned, but he has "ceased to drift." His eyes, though still haggard, are no longer vague and purposeless. During the journey, William is struck by the sight of a trainload of soldiers in khaki moving up from Le Havre to the front. He cranes out of the window to study their faces, their uniforms, their manner, "as a small boy scans and studies the bearing of his future schoolfellows." He does not thrill at the sight of young men about to die, but he sends with them, remembering Griselda, "his strong desire for their great and terrible victory."

### Dieppe Harbour

At Dieppe Harbour, there is another long, purposeless halt on the boat before they slip into the Channel and head northwestward for Folkestone. The day is calm with the stillness of perfect summer—the same kind of day, William remembers, as that day a month before when he and Griselda had steamed away from Dover on their honeymoon. Watching the white cliffs fade, his heart "cried out against God."

### The Dieppe Newspaper

It is an English newspaper bought the day before at Dieppe that Edith Haynes uses to show William the locations of London recruiting stations. She points out the paragraph listing the various stations, and William studies it with interest, noting that the nearest to Charing Cross is on the Horse Guards Parade. This practical detail—a newspaper bought in a French port—becomes the instrument through which William's abstract resolve is translated into concrete action.

## Symbolic Significance

Dieppe represents a threshold between William's two lives. Geographically, it is the last Continental stop before England; psychologically, it is where William's grief begins to crystallize into determination. The "long, purposeless halt" in Dieppe Harbour mirrors his own suspended state—no longer the man who left England as a honeymooning idealist, not yet the soldier he is resolving to become. The trainload of soldiers he sees near Dieppe gives him his first glimpse of the military world he is about to enter, rendered with the fascinated scrutiny of an outsider studying a tribe he means to join.
