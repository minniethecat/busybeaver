## Overview

William Tully is the "Englishman" of Hamilton's title—an everyman figure whose journey from earnest socialist to traumatised refugee serves as the novel's principal argument against the militarist certainties of Edwardian Britain. He is introduced as a young clerk of "three-and-twenty" [001-001] who works by day for an insurance company and by night discovers meaning as a steward at socialist and pacifist meetings. His competence is modest, his ambitions limited, his convictions sincere but unexamined. Hamilton's achievement is to make this unremarkable man the vehicle for a sustained critique of nationalism, propaganda, and the machinery of war—precisely by refusing to make him remarkable.

William's defining characteristic is his ordinariness. He is not a hero; he is not exceptionally brave or exceptionally cowardly; he is a man of "the majority of his fellow-men" [008-001] who finds himself in circumstances for which nothing in his life has prepared him. His ordinariness is Hamilton's polemical point: if the war can break William Tully, it can break anyone, and the moral architecture that celebrates soldiers while ignoring what war does to civilians is fundamentally dishonest.

## Biography

### Pre-War Life

William was born in Camberwell, South London, the son of a "small builder" who died when William was in his teens [001-002]. He left school at fourteen—"as most boys did, in those days" [001-003], Hamilton notes with pointed social commentary—and entered the offices of an insurance company, where he remained through the novel's opening. His work was clerical, tedious, and poorly paid; Hamilton describes him as "one of the thousand young men who sit on high stools in the City, copying letters and adding figures" [001-004].

The discovery that transforms William's life is the platform. At a socialist meeting, he finds himself asked to serve as a steward—a minor role involving badge-wearing and crowd-direction—and the experience gives him a sense of purpose and identity that his clerical work cannot provide. He rises to become a regular speaker, known by the nom de guerre "Vindex," and his speeches on "the coming revolution" and "the solidarity of the workers" are well-received, if not especially original. It is through this world that he meets Griselda Watkins, a fellow steward whose "natural weakness for a white dress and a sash with the colours of the Cause" [001-048] mirrors his own hunger for belonging.

### Marriage and Honeymoon

William and Griselda marry in late July 1914, their wedding a modest affair that Hamilton describes with characteristic economy. Their honeymoon plan—a walking tour of the Belgian Ardennes—is chosen for its economy and for William's vague sense that "abroad" will be more romantic than an English seaside town. William arranges meticulously, down to the forwarding of their mail; his insistence that "neither paper nor letter should be forwarded during their absence" [004-004] is the decision on which the entire plot turns, cutting them off from news of the July Crisis and the declaration of war.

### War and Flight

The war arrives as a German cavalry patrol in Chapter VI, and William's response is bewilderment before it is fear. He stares at the soldiers "without a thought of fear" [006-013] because he does not yet understand what a soldier is. The soldier strikes him, shoves Griselda, and makes off with their belongings—a small violence, by the war's standards, but one that shatters William's conceptual world.

What follows is a week-long flight southward through Belgium, during which William is separated from Griselda, captured by Germans, escapes, finds Griselda again, and finally reaches a railway station where they board a train for France. The journey strips him of every layer of identity: his socialism proves useless, his Englishness is a liability, his marriage is reduced to the animal bond of two people trying to survive. Hamilton's narrative follows William's consciousness with unrelenting closeness, recording each degradation—the loss of his boots, the theft of his money, the moment he strikes another man in a scuffle for train space [012-001]—as a step in the dismantling of a self.

### Return to England

The novel's final section finds William back in London, staying with his parents in Camberwell, attempting to resume a life that no longer fits him. He is expected to speak at meetings—now in support of the war effort—and discovers that he cannot. The words that came easily when he knew nothing of war will not come at all now that he knows everything. His silence is interpreted as cowardice; his refusal to "do his bit" as betrayal. Hamilton's irony reaches its peak in these chapters: William, who has seen more of war than any platform orator, is judged by those who have seen nothing.

## Character and Significance

William's significance as a literary character lies in what Hamilton refuses to do with him. She does not make him a hero who rises to the occasion. She does not make him a coward who is justly punished. She does not use his suffering to endorse either militarism or pacifism. Instead, she presents him as a case study in what war does to an ordinary man: it destroys his certainties, alienates him from his community, and leaves him with knowledge—of fear, of violence, of his own body's fragility—that he cannot un-know and cannot communicate to those who have not shared his experience.

The novel's title is both descriptive and ironic. William is an Englishman, yes—but the novel demands to know what "Englishman" means when Englishness is invoked to send men to die, to silence dissent, to transform the living reality of war into the dead rhetoric of patriotism. By the novel's end, William is an Englishman only in the sense that he was born in England. Every other meaning the word might carry has been stripped from him.

## In the Novel

Hamilton's narration maintains a complex distance from William. The novel's third-person voice is capable of both sympathy and irony, often in the same sentence. When William arranges for no newspapers to follow him to Belgium, the narrator observes: "He was young, ambitious, full of his own affairs—and he did not want the world to intrude on his happiness" [004-004]. The irony is gentle but unmistakable: the world will intrude, and it will do so with a violence William cannot imagine.

The novel's most sustained psychological passages follow William's consciousness during the flight. In Chapter VIII, Hamilton devotes several paragraphs to the phenomenology of fear—what it feels like "when you stand with your back to the wall" and the body acts without the mind's consent [008-001]. These passages are remarkable for their clinical precision, and they establish William as a vehicle for psychological observation rather than moral judgment.

## Key Relationships

- **Griselda Watkins / Tully**: William's wife and ideological mirror. Where William is mild and accommodating, Griselda is fierce and declarative; their marriage is presented as a union of complementary anxieties, each finding in the other a confirmation of their shared beliefs. The war tests this bond by removing the ideological framework that supported it, forcing them to relate to each other as bodies rather than as fellow-travellers in a political movement.

- **Faraday**: The "Vindex of The Torch" [001-038], a more established socialist journalist who represents what William might become—and from whom William recoils after his return from Belgium, recognising in Faraday's platform rhetoric the same ignorance he once shared.

- **Heinz**: The German soldier who captures William. Heinz is not a villain; he is a frightened man following orders, and his treatment of William—brutal but not gratuitously cruel—complicates any simple reading of the war as a conflict between "good" Englishmen and "evil" Germans.
