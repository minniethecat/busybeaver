## Overview

The Harrow Road is a London street that appears in *William—An Englishman* through the backstory of Heinz, a German soldier who had worked there as a hairdresser before the war. The detail functions as an ironic narrative device, humanising the enemy and highlighting the pre-war normality of Anglo-German relations.

## Heinz the Hairdresser

When William and Griselda are taken prisoner by German soldiers in the Ardennes, one of their captors — a young man named Heinz — addresses them in English: "English—you come from London?… I have been two years in London; that is why I speak English. I was with a hairdresser in the Harrow Road two years; and I know also the Strand and the Angel and Buckingham Palace and the Elephant" [006-024]. Heinz recites London place-names with evident pride in his local knowledge, even as he informs the couple they are now prisoners.

William, drawing courage from the absurdity of the situation, "thankful for the English acquired during the two years' hairdressing in the Harrow Road, admonished him with a firmness intended to sober and dismay" [006-027]. The scene is simultaneously threatening and farcical: an armed German soldier whose connection to England is a London barber shop.

## Thematic Significance

The Harrow Road detail embodies one of the novel's recurring ironies: that the men fighting on opposite sides of the [[european-war|European War]] had often lived and worked alongside each other in peacetime. Heinz's two years in London — learning English, cutting hair, memorising the names of landmarks — represent the transnational ordinary that the war violently severs. For William, confronting an [[enemy]] who knows the Harrow Road as well as he does undermines the clean propaganda of national hatred.
