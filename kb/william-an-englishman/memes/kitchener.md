## Overview

Lord Horatio Herbert Kitchener (1850–1916) is a historical figure referenced several times in [[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]. As Secretary of State for War at the outbreak of the First World War, Kitchener's recruiting campaign and his prediction of a protracted conflict serve as important markers of the war's escalating scale and the shattering of pre-war illusions.

## Kitchener's Army

The recruiting campaign for "Kitchener's Army" is mentioned as William reads about the war effort:

> [014-006] An article in The Daily Chronicle—some days old—described the beginning of the recruiting campaign for the raising of Kitchener's Army; he read it as he read everything else that explained or described the European War.

This reference grounds the novel in the historical reality of Kitchener's famous 1914 call for volunteers, which ultimately raised the largest volunteer army in British history.

## Prophecy of a Three-Year War

Kitchener's early prediction that the war would last three years — a view initially dismissed by many as alarmist — is cited to underscore the dawning recognition of the war's true magnitude:

> [016-001] But if the thing's as big as they say—the common talk is that Kitchener has prophesied three years of it, and it's very likely true—they'll be wanting every man who'll come in before they've done with it.

This prophecy, which proved tragically accurate, marks a turning point in the novel's treatment of the [[european-war|European War]]: the conflict is no longer a brief "red herring" but an all-consuming reality.

## Enlistment and the Individual

Kitchener's call for volunteers ultimately compelled William to confront the prospect of enlistment as a personal reality:

> [014-007] There was enough of the old leaven in him to bring him up suddenly, and with something like a round turn, as he realized that the act of striking blow for blow against the German would involve the further act of enlistment and the wearing of the King's uniform.

This moment of reckoning — in which the abstract desire to "strike a blow" collides with the concrete reality of military service — represents the psychological threshold that Kitchener's recruiting campaign erected before every able-bodied man of William's generation. The "old leaven" of his pre-war pacifism resurfaces precisely at the point where patriotic impulse must translate into institutional submission.

## As a Symbol

Kitchener, like the [[kaiser|Kaiser]], functions in the novel not as a character but as a historical force whose decisions shape the world that [[william-tully|William Tully]] and his generation must navigate. His name is invoked to convey the gravity of the war:

> [015-022] And you can't stop it with sneers about soldiers and Kitchener. … It's hell and the mouth of hell—I've seen it.

Here, Kitchener's name stands metonymically for the entire military apparatus and the war itself — something the anti-militarist speakers at the meeting can neither comprehend nor stop with their rhetoric.
