## Overview

Balham is a residential district in south London. In *William—An Englishman*, it serves as the home of Griselda's aunt and the setting for two key moments in the courtship and marriage of William Tully and Griselda: the tea visit that sealed their romance, and the wedding ceremony that marked the beginning of their brief life together.

## In the Novel

### Courtship

William and Griselda meet on the platforms of progressive meetings, where he follows her about to listen entranced to her speeches. Griselda, "touched as a woman and flattered as an orator by his persistent presence," invites him to tea at her aunt's house in Balham. The visit is a success; from that evening in early March 1914, their fate is sealed. By the beginning of April, William proposes to her on the top of an otherwise empty bus, and she accepts.

### The Wedding

The wedding takes place in Balham on the twenty-third of July 1914, from the house of Griselda's aunt. The ceremony is performed by "an enlightened vicar who had consented to omit the ignoble vow of obedience." The church is thronged with comrades and ardent sympathizers; the bridesmaids, six in number, have all "done time for assault"—a detail the advanced Press reports with pride. Even the mainstream Press considers the event worth a paragraph. The modest Balham residence can hardly accommodate the flood of progressive guests, and there is rice and slipper-throwing and a whirl of good wishes.

### Memory

Much later, after Griselda's death in Belgium, the memory of the Balham wedding returns to William with devastating clarity. Seeing her limp body by the roadside, he recalls her in her wedding garments—"fussed and dainty, with her bouquet tied with suffragette ribbons"—and remembers the crowd in the little Balham house, the handshakes, the well-wishing faces, all so close and present "that they gave the lie to this dead woman dropped by the roadside."

## Symbolic Significance

Balham represents the domestic, familial world that William and Griselda's progressive ideology both depended upon and half-disdained. The aunt's house—modest, suburban, ordinary—is the unglamorous foundation on which their revolutionary self-image rests. The wedding at Balham, with its suffragette ribbons and omitted vows, is the couple's attempt to remake a traditional ritual in their own image; yet the setting—a house, an aunt, a neighbourhood—remains stubbornly conventional. Hamilton's juxtaposition of the Balham wedding with Griselda's death in a Belgian ditch is one of the novel's cruelest ironies, measuring the distance between the hopeful, crowded little house in south London and the anonymous roadside where the story ends.
