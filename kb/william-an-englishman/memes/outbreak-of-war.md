## Overview

The outbreak of the First World War in August 1914 is the event that divides *William—An Englishman* into before and after. Hamilton does not narrate the diplomatic crisis directly; instead, she traces its effects through the consciousness of [[william-tully|William Tully]], who learns of the war not through newspapers or proclamations but through the sudden, incomprehensible appearance of German soldiers in the Belgian countryside. The novel's treatment of the war's outbreak is a masterclass in indirection: the event is never named as "the Great War" or "the World War" — it is simply what happens, the force that destroys William's world without ever being fully understood.

## Context

The novel opens in the summer of 1914, in the final weeks of what would later be called the Edwardian afternoon — a period of progressive reform, political ferment, and widespread faith in the inevitability of human improvement. The socialist movement to which William belongs is confident that the workers of the world will refuse to fight each other; the suffrage movement believes that women's enfranchisement will transform politics; the liberal imagination holds that rational discourse and international cooperation will prevent another major European war.

Every one of these beliefs was wrong. The Second International collapsed in August 1914 when the German Social Democratic Party voted for war credits. The suffrage movement fragmented over whether to support the war effort. The great powers marched into four years of industrialised slaughter. Hamilton, writing in 1919 with full knowledge of what had happened, structures her novel so that every expression of pre-war confidence reads as dramatic irony. William's faith in progress is not foolish — it is the faith of his time — but it is also, the novel insists, catastrophically inadequate to the world as it actually is.

## The Event

The war's outbreak is registered in the novel through a single, devastating scene. William and Griselda are walking near their cottage in the Belgian Ardennes when a German cavalry patrol appears. William stares at the soldiers "without a thought of fear" because "he did not yet understand what a soldier was" (006-013). The soldier strikes him, shoves Griselda, takes their belongings, and rides on. It is a small violence, barely a skirmish by military standards, but it is the pivot on which the entire novel turns.

Hamilton's refusal to narrate the diplomatic crisis — there are no scenes of telegrams, no references to the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, no discussions of the Schlieffen Plan — is a deliberate artistic choice. The war arrives for William not as news but as violence, not as narrative but as disruption. This is the novel's central methodological principle: historical events are rendered through individual experience, and individual experience of history is always partial, bewildering, and resistant to the coherent narratives that later accounts impose.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The war's outbreak initiates the novel's central sequence: the Tullys' flight through Belgium, their separation and reunion, Griselda's death, and William's return to an England he no longer recognises. Each of these events is a direct consequence of August 1914, and Hamilton traces the chain of causation with clinical precision.

The war's outbreak also transforms the novel's intellectual landscape. The concepts that structured William's pre-war world — [[socialism|socialism]], [[internationalism|internationalism]], [[progress|progress]] — are rendered inoperative. The war does not refute these concepts; it simply makes them irrelevant, like tools designed for a world that no longer exists. William's tragedy is that he must continue to live in a world that has invalidated everything he believed, and the novel's power lies in its refusal to offer him — or the reader — a replacement faith.

## Key Passages

> [006-013] William stared at the soldiers without a thought of fear, because he did not yet understand what a soldier was.

This sentence encapsulates Hamilton's method. The war is not narrated; it is discovered, moment by moment, through the gap between William's existing categories and the reality he confronts. The phrase "did not yet understand" is the novel's temporal signature — the "yet" that separates the world of before from the world of after.

> [014-004] The war had been in progress a couple of years, and William — who had fled from it, been captured by it, and lost his wife to it — could still not have explained why it had started.

The gap between lived experience and historical understanding is the novel's great theme. William knows war intimately — his body knows it, his grief knows it — but he cannot narrate it. The causes of the war, the stuff of diplomatic history, remain as inaccessible to him at the novel's end as they were at its beginning.

> [017-002] The war had been in progress a couple of years.

Hamilton's temporal markers are deliberately vague. "A couple of years" — not "two years and three months" — because William does not measure the war in calendar time. He measures it in losses.
