## Overview

Charing Cross is a major railway terminus and surrounding district in central London. In *William—An Englishman*, it appears at two critical junctures in William Tully's wartime experience: first as the point of his return to England from occupied Belgium, where he resolves to enlist; and later as the location of a secondhand bookstall on the Charing Cross Road where he acquires military histories that fuel his determination to become a soldier.

## In the Novel

### Return from Belgium and the Decision to Enlist

After escaping from German-occupied Belgium, William and Edith Haynes travel by boat and train to London. It is during the two-hour journey from Folkestone to Charing Cross that William first confides his intention of becoming a soldier. The train arrives in the early morning, and William is so urgent to act on his new conviction that Edith must persuade him to delay at Charing Cross for breakfast, assuring him—"whether rightly or wrongly she knew not"—that it is too early for any recruiting official to be at his post. They take their last meal together at a hotel near the station.

Edith shows William a newspaper listing London recruiting stations, and he notes that the nearest to Charing Cross is on the Horse Guards Parade. His determination to enlist immediately upon arrival marks the moment when his abstract political convictions crystallize into personal military commitment.

### The Charing Cross Road Bookstall

Months later, after Griselda's death and his rejection by the Army, William lives in lodgings in Camden Town. His obsessive reading about the Napoleonic Wars leads him to a secondhand bookstall on the Charing Cross Road, where he acquires an obsolete volume on military lore published in the early 1850s. With this book, he studies the campaigns of Kluck and Joffre and works through diagrams of Napoleonic tactics—a lonely, self-taught preparation for the soldier's life he still dreams of.

## Symbolic Significance

Charing Cross functions as a threshold in William's transformation from political idealist to would-be soldier. The journey to Charing Cross is literally a crossing—from the occupied Continent to England, from civilian to military purpose, from the world of pre-war progressive certainties to the world of war. The Charing Cross Road bookstall, in turn, represents William's solitary intellectual struggle to remake himself: the out-of-date military manual is a poignant emblem of his earnest but inadequate preparation for modern warfare.
