# Progress

## Overview

Progress in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is the master narrative of [[william-tully|William Tully]]'s pre-war world — the [[faith|belief]] that history moves inexorably toward improvement, that "a man and a woman working for progress" (002-009) are on the right side of history, and that the forces of reaction can only delay, not defeat, the advance of enlightenment. Hamilton presents this faith in progress as both admirable and naive: admirable in its commitment to human improvement, naive in its blindness to the forces — nationalism, militarism, hatred — that would soon overwhelm it.

The novel's trajectory is a systematic dismantling of progressive faith. [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war commitment to "the cause of progress" is genuine, even noble, but it is also abstract — a [[faith|belief]] about humanity in general rather than a response to specific human beings in specific circumstances. The war forces [[william-tully|William]] to exchange this abstract faith for concrete commitments: to Griselda, to his fellow soldiers, to the immediate demands of survival and action. Progress, as an ideology, cannot survive contact with the reality of war.

## In the Novel

Progress first appears as a shared commitment: "a man and a woman working for progress and destined to leave their mark" (002-009). The pairing of "man and woman" invokes the companionate marriage ideal, with [[william-tully|William]] and Griselda as equal partners in the work of social improvement. "Destined to leave their mark" expresses the progressive's confidence that their work matters, that history will record their contribution. Hamilton's irony is retrospective: we know what [[william-tully|William]] and Griselda do not — that the "mark" they will leave is very different from what they imagine.

The progressive movement is characterized by its relationship to opposition: "the politician across the path of progress" (015-012) is the enemy, the obstruction, the force that must be overcome. This Manichaean worldview — progress versus reaction, light versus darkness — gives the progressive movement its energy but also its fragility. When events refuse to conform to this simple binary — when the war reveals that "progress" can lead to destruction as easily as to improvement — the progressive framework collapses.

## Historical Context

The idea of progress reached its high-water mark in Edwardian Britain. The Victorian [[faith|conviction]] that science, industry, and liberal reform were steadily improving the human condition survived into the early twentieth century, sustained by real achievements: rising living standards, expanding education, the extension of the franchise. The Fabian Society, founded in 1884, embodied this progressive optimism, advocating gradual, rational reform as the path to socialism.

WWI destroyed this faith in progress more completely than any intellectual critique could have done. The civilization that had produced Beethoven, Shakespeare, and Newton had also produced the machine gun, poison gas, and industrial-scale slaughter. The post-war literature — Eliot's *The Waste Land*, Yeats's "The Second Coming," the war poets — is saturated with the sense that something fundamental had been lost, that the narrative of progress had been exposed as a comforting fiction. Hamilton's novel participates in this post-war reckoning by showing how [[william-tully|William]]'s progressive faith, however genuine, could not survive its encounter with reality.

## Key Passages

> …a man and a woman working for progress and destined to leave their mark… (002-009)

The confidence of "destined" — the [[faith|belief]] that history guarantees the progressive's success — is both touching and ironic. Hamilton, writing after the war, knows what [[william-tully|William]] cannot: that the "mark" he will leave is suffering, not progress, and that destiny is not a progressive ally but an indifferent force.

> …the politician across the path of progress… (015-012)

The spatial metaphor — progress as a "path" with obstacles — reveals the progressive worldview as fundamentally linear. History moves in one direction, and those who oppose it are merely obstacles to be cleared. The war teaches [[william-tully|William]] that history is not a path but a chaos, and that progress is not a destination but a hope.
