## Overview

Bloomsbury is a district in central London, known in the Edwardian period for its mix of Georgian squares, boarding houses, and intellectual associations. In *William—An Englishman*, Bloomsbury is the setting for William Tully's political conversion and his married life with Griselda. It is here, in Faraday's shabby lodgings in a "mournful side-street," that William first encounters the socialist ideas that will define his pre-war identity; and it is here, in a tiny flat chosen for its convenience to the perpetual round of meetings, that the couple make their first home together.

## In the Novel

### Faraday's Lodgings and William's Conversion

William's first visit to Bloomsbury occurs when Faraday, his senior colleague at the insurance office, invites him to his lodgings for an evening of political discussion. A shabby maidservant ushers William upstairs to a shabby, paper-strewn room where Faraday holds forth with "a distinctly authoritative way." Over the course of the evening, and in subsequent visits, William's "dull, half-torpid resentment" against the restrictions of his narrow life is merged by Faraday's influence into "a wider sympathy with the general discomfort, in an honest and fiery little passion for Justice, Right and Progress." The Bloomsbury lodgings become the crucible of William's socialist faith.

Later, as the war progresses, Faraday holds a private consultative meeting of the Central London Branch in a hall in a Bloomsbury side street near Tottenham Court Road. Some thirty to forty comrades gather to consider "suitable methods of influencing for the better the existing and lamentable condition of popular opinion"—a meeting whose earnest secrecy contrasts bitterly with the European catastrophe unfolding beyond.

### The Marital Flat

After their wedding in July 1914, William and Griselda settle into a tiny flat in Bloomsbury, chosen for its central location and convenience for the "perpetual getting about to meetings which was so integral a part of their well-filled, bustling lives." They furnish it lovingly and with what they consider good taste; Griselda brings in friends to admire it, and they are "as foolishly happy over their nest as any other loving little couple."

The flat takes on a darker cast after Griselda's death. Returning from Belgium alone, William finds the Bloomsbury flat dreadful to him, "with its empty demand for Griselda," and abandons it within weeks for a lodging in Camden Town.

### Departure for the Honeymoon

When William and Griselda finally escape the Ardennes and make their way toward Brussels and home, their hearts "sang cheerily of Bloomsbury." The name of their London district becomes synonymous with civilization, companionship, and the bustling political life they crave after the isolation of the Belgian countryside.

## Symbolic Significance

Bloomsbury embodies the London-centred progressive world that William inhabits before the war shatters it. The district's name, with its associations of the Bloomsbury Group and intellectual ferment, lends an ironic resonance to William's earnest but shallow political education. Faraday's lodgings are a kind of secular chapel where William receives his socialist baptism; the marital flat is the domestic expression of that faith, furnished in "good taste" and dedicated to the cause. When William later flees the flat, unable to bear its reminders of Griselda, the abandonment is also a symbolic relinquishment of the pre-war self that had flourished within its walls.
