## Overview

**Conscientious objection** in *William—An Englishman* is a stance that the novel treats not as cowardice but as a form of principled stubbornness — one that, like all principles in Hamilton's world, cannot survive contact with reality unchanged. The two characters who embody this position, [[faraday|Faraday]] and [[william-tully|William Tully]], follow sharply different trajectories: Faraday holds peace meetings, distributes anti-war pamphlets, and only abandons his position when conscripted; William undergoes a "fiery conversion" after witnessing the war firsthand in Belgium and enlists voluntarily.

The novel's treatment of conscientious objection is notable for its even-handedness. Writing in 1919, Hamilton neither vilifies the objector as a coward nor glorifies him as a martyr. Instead, she presents conscientious objection as a genuine moral position that proves unsustainable not because it is wrong, but because the world changes around it — a characteristic Hamilton irony that runs through the entire work.

## In the Novel

Before the war, William is "a pacifist of the type which seeks peace and ensues it by insisting firmly, and even to blood, that it is the other side's duty to give way" [002-003]. His pacifism is selective — he opposes compulsory military service but supports compulsory trade unionism, a contradiction Hamilton notes with characteristic irony [002-003].

The novel's fullest portrait of conscientious objection is Faraday. After war breaks out, Faraday "held peace meetings at considerable personal risk, which, as became a good fighter, he took, even with enjoyment; distributed printed appeals to pacifist and anti-British sentiment and wrote passionately, if with less than his usual clarity, in *The Torch*" [015-003]. But his position is undermined by his own honesty: "he could not in honesty bring himself to defend every action of his country's enemies and was conscious of occasional difficulty and a sense of thin ice underneath him" [015-003].

Hamilton's treatment of Faraday's eventual abandonment of his anti-war stance is sympathetic: "with time he, too, came to understand that a people may have other foes than those of its own household and be threatened with death from without; when he was called up under the Conscription Act he went without protest, made an excellent soldier and died fighting in a night raid near Hulluch" [015-003].

William's trajectory is more dramatic. After Griselda's death in Belgium, he is "driven to enlist under the rival banner of nationality" [015-003]. His contempt for his former pacifism becomes absolute: "his contempt for his past dreams of a field-marshal's baton was as nothing to his contempt for those further past dreams wherein fact was dispelled by a theory; and he had, in his own words, 'no use for' a pacifist party which had never, as he had, made confession of its fundamental error" [018-014].

## Thematic Significance

Conscientious objection in the novel serves a structural function: it is the position from which characters fall. Both Faraday and William begin as opponents of war, and both end as soldiers. This shared arc — from principled refusal to participation — is the novel's vehicle for exploring the collision between abstract conviction and lived experience. Hamilton suggests not that the convictions were wrong, but that they were insufficiently grounded in the realities they purported to address.

The novel's treatment of conscientious objection also functions as a critique of pre-war progressivism. The belief that rational persuasion and moral example could prevent war belongs to the same family of beliefs as the conviction that Society could be "straightened and set right by the well-meaning efforts of well-meaning souls" [002-002]. Both beliefs are shattered by the same event.

## Historical Context

Conscientious objection during the First World War was a newly recognized legal category in Britain. The Military Service Act of 1916 introduced conscription and, under pressure from Quakers and the labour movement, included a "conscience clause" allowing exemption for those with genuine religious or moral objections. Approximately 16,000 men claimed conscientious objector status. Many faced tribunals, imprisonment, and social ostracism.

Hamilton, writing in 1919, was positioned between two views: she respected the CO's moral seriousness (as shown in her treatment of Faraday) but also registered the force of the counter-argument — that a nation under existential threat cannot accommodate every individual conscience. The novel does not resolve this tension; it dramatizes it.
