## Overview

William Tully's enlistment in the British Army is the culminating biographical event of his wartime transformation. Returning to London after the death of Griselda and his escape from occupied Belgium, William immediately sets out to enlist — not from patriotic fervour in the abstract, but from a deeply personal need for vengeance and a sense of justice awakened by his experiences. His enlistment is complicated by his physical stature: at five-foot-five, he falls below the minimum height requirement and is twice rejected before eventually being accepted as recruitment standards are lowered. The event marks the definitive break with his former identity as a socialist anti-militarist and his embrace of a new, if unformed, sense of national belonging.

## Context

William returns to England via Dieppe and Folkestone in the company of [[edith-haynes|Edith Haynes]], who has helped him secure passage. During the journey, Edith explains the political background of the war — the ultimatum to Serbia, the invasion of Belgium, and the British declaration of war on the Fourth of August — filling the gaps in William's knowledge [014-006]. Still in shock from Griselda's death and haunted by the things he witnessed in Belgium, William's mind is already turning towards action. Even before reaching London, Edith understands his intention:

> [014-019] She understood that his intention was to enlist at the moment of arrival in London.

## The Decision

William's decision to enlist is not a simple conversion to patriotism. He frames it honestly, even bluntly, when challenged. His motives are personal and visceral rather than ideological:

> [014-015] "I'm not making any sacrifice—I'm not. I'm enlisting because I want to enlist—and because I shall enjoy it. I want to go back and be even with them."

This candour distinguishes William from those who wrap their enlistment in the language of noble sacrifice. His desire for vengeance is rooted in the specific trauma of seeing the hostages shot [007-001] and losing Griselda. His enlistment is the logical endpoint of the transformation that began when his faith in rational persuasion collapsed before the German firing squad.

## Rejection and Persistence

William's first attempt to enlist fails on grounds of his height. He had not anticipated this obstacle:

> [014-022] He did not know till he came to enlist that the standard of measurement was higher than his own five-foot-five.

The rejection is a bitter blow — not only is he denied the chance to act on his conviction, but the denial comes from the very nation he is trying to serve. The irony is sharp: a man who once denounced militarism and the state now finds himself shut out by the state's own physical standards.

William does not accept the rejection. As the war drags on and the demand for recruits intensifies, he tries again:

> [016-008] When the need for recruits drove the authorities to lower their standards, he tried to enlist again and was again refused — "If I were only two inches taller," he lamented.

The repeated rejection becomes a source of quiet anguish. At a moment when he most needs to act, to channel his grief and fury into purpose, the Army's measuring rod denies him.

## Acceptance

Eventually, as British casualties mount and recruitment standards continue to fall, William is accepted. The lowering of the height requirement — a detail grounded in the historical reality of 1914–1915 recruitment — marks his transition from civilian to soldier. By the time he enters training, the war has already changed him profoundly. The mild-mannered clerk who once accepted his mother's weekly annexation of his wages is gone, replaced by a man hardened by grief, flight, and the memory of what he saw in Belgium.

Later, reflecting on his decision, William traces the path that led him to enlist:

> [017-031] "The feeling I had about it when I enlisted — what made me do it. So many things... everything that happened."

## Significance

William's enlistment serves several narrative and thematic functions:

- It **completes his character arc** from passive clerk to active participant in history, mirroring the novel's broader argument about the relationship between the individual and the forces of his time.
- It **embodies the novel's critique of abstract ideology**: Faraday's general strike, the socialist congresses, the pamphlets — none of these survived contact with the reality of war, while enlistment, for William, is an honest if unglamorous act grounded in personal experience.
- It **dramatises the physical cost of entry** into national service. The height requirement is a literal barrier between William and his desire to act, symbolising the gap between intention and capability that the novel explores.
