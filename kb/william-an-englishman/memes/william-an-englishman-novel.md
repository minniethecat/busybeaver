## Overview

*William—An Englishman* is a satirical First World War novel by the British playwright, actress, and suffragist **Cicely Hamilton** (1872–1952), first published in 1919. The novel was awarded the **Prix Femina-Vie Heureuse** in its year of publication, a French literary prize that recognised the best work of imaginative literature by a British author. It stands as one of the earliest British novels to treat the First World War from a civilian perspective, and one of the few to sustain a satirical register through material that most contemporary novelists approached with elegy or patriotism.

Hamilton's career before the novel was shaped by the suffrage movement. She was a founding member of the **Women Writers' Suffrage League** (1908) and co-wrote the lyrics to "The March of the Women," the anthem of the WSPU led by [[suffragette-movement|Emmeline Pankhurst]]. Her theatrical work—including the hit play *Diana of Dobson's* (1908)—established a mode of social comedy with feminist bite that carries directly into the narrative voice of *William—An Englishman*. During the war, Hamilton served with the Scottish Women's Hospitals and later as a clerk in the War Office, experiences that shaped the novel's unsparing depictions of the refugee crisis and the machinery of propaganda [014-004].

## Publication History

The novel appeared in 1919 from **Skeffington & Son**, a London publisher, and went through several impressions in its first year. The Prix Femina-Vie Heureuse brought it brief prominence—the prize had previously gone to writers of the stature of E.M. Forster—but the novel fell out of print in the 1920s as the market for war literature shifted toward the soldier's memoir and the elegiac mode of the war poets. It was rediscovered in the late twentieth century by scholars of women's writing and war literature, and reissued by Persephone Books. The Standard Ebooks edition, from which this wiki's corpus is drawn, makes the novel freely available in a modern digital format.

## In the Novel

The novel follows [[william-tully|William Tully]], a young clerk of three-and-twenty who lives with his widowed mother in [[camberwell|Camberwell]] and works for an insurance company in the City [001-001]. William's life is transformed when a fellow-clerk, [[faraday|Faraday]], introduces him to socialist and pacifist meetings, where he rises to the role of platform speaker under the name "Vindex" [001-038]. At one such meeting in December 1913, he meets [[griselda|Griselda Watkins]], a young suffragette steward, and they marry at the end of July 1914 [003-002].

Their honeymoon in a cottage in the Belgian Ardennes—owned by the revolutionary pamphleteer [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]—places them in the path of the German invasion. Cut off from all news by William's romantic insistence that "neither paper nor letter should be forwarded during their absence" [004-004], they learn of the war only when a German cavalry patrol rides into their valley. What follows is a week-long flight southward through burning villages and columns of refugees, during which William is captured, escapes, and is reunited with Griselda only to see her die of exhaustion and exposure in a French railway siding.

The novel's second half follows William's return to England, where he finds himself unable to speak of his experiences and is judged—by his parents, his former comrades, and the propaganda apparatus of the wartime state—as a shirker and a coward. The novel's final chapters constitute a sustained satire of the home-front culture of patriotic certainty, in which those who know nothing of war feel entitled to judge those who know everything.

## Historical Context

Hamilton wrote the novel in the immediate aftermath of the war, and its satirical targets—the platform rhetoric of pre-war socialist movements, the propaganda machinery of the wartime state, the gendered expectations of masculine courage—reflect debates that were live and contentious in 1919. The novel's treatment of [[patriotism]] as a sentiment available only to those who have not experienced its consequences was provocative in a year when the government was promoting narratives of noble sacrifice and the war memorial movement was gathering momentum.

Hamilton's choice of a male protagonist allowed her to investigate, from a position of critical distance, the construction of masculine identity in wartime. William is not a soldier—he never fires a shot—but he is judged by the standards of martial masculinity nonetheless. The novel's title is its central irony: William is "an Englishman" by birth and by the expectations placed upon him, but the war has made Englishness into something he can neither access nor reject. The phrase "England hadn't been anything to him" [017-028] is the novel's bleakest admission: nationalism is an idea that only works in peacetime.

## Key Passages

> He was young, ambitious, full of his own affairs—and he did not want the world to intrude on his happiness. So it was that neither paper nor letter should be forwarded during their absence. [004-004]

The dramatic irony is precise: the "world" that William does not want to intrude is about to do so with a violence he cannot imagine, and his act of romantic exclusion—no newspapers on the honeymoon—is the decision that makes the catastrophe possible.

> The political consequences of the invasion of Belgium, and the feverish days of hesitation that had preceded the declaration of war, were matters that William now heard discussed for the first time—discussed with passion by those who had never heard a shell. [014-004]

Hamilton's satirical method is compressed into a single sentence: the gap between experience and opinion is the novel's deepest structural principle, and this passage is its clearest articulation.
