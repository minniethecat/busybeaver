## Overview

The wedding of [[william-tully|William Tully]] and [[griselda|Griselda Watkins]] takes place on July 23rd, 1914, in Balham, and is the last major event of the novel's pre-war section. Hamilton presents the ceremony as a performance of progressive values — the obedience vow omitted, the church filled with "comrades and ardent sympathizers" (003-002) — and in doing so establishes the ideological framework that the war will systematically dismantle.

## Context

The wedding occurs at a moment of historical and narrative suspense. The reader knows, as the characters do not, that the July Crisis is nearing its climax — that within days, Austria-Hungary will declare war on Serbia, and the system of European alliances will trigger the catastrophe. Hamilton exploits this dramatic irony: the wedding's optimism is shadowed by the reader's knowledge of what is coming.

Narratively, the wedding marks the culmination of William and Griselda's courtship in the world of platform politics. They have met, fallen in love, and decided to marry within the fellowship of the socialist movement, and their wedding is as much a celebration of that fellowship as of their personal bond. The political and the personal are, at this moment, indistinguishable — a unity that the war will sever.

## The Event

The ceremony is distinguished by two features that signal its progressive character. First, the obedience vow — the promise that a wife shall "obey" her husband, a fixture of the Anglican marriage service — has been omitted, at the couple's request, by "an enlightened vicar" (003-002). This was a significant gesture in Edwardian England, where the marriage service was a site of active feminist contestation. Second, the congregation is composed not of family but of political comrades — the community of stewards, speakers, and sympathisers who constitute William and Griselda's social world.

Hamilton's account of the ceremony is economical — a single paragraph — but it carries enormous weight. The wedding is the novel's vision of what a good society might look like: egalitarian, communal, infused with progressive purpose. Everything that follows will test, and ultimately shatter, this vision.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The wedding's immediate consequence is the honeymoon — William's plan for a walking tour of the Belgian Ardennes, chosen for its economy and for his vague sense that "abroad" will be more romantic than an English seaside town. William's decision that "neither paper nor letter should be forwarded during their absence" (004-004) is the narrative hinge: it ensures that the couple will learn of the war not through newspapers but through the unmediated violence of a German cavalry patrol.

The wedding's deeper consequence is thematic. The progressive marriage it inaugurates — companionate, egalitarian, politically self-conscious — becomes the novel's test case for the viability of progressive ideals under extreme pressure. The war will strip the marriage of its ideological framework, reducing it to the animal bond of two people trying to survive. The wedding, in retrospect, reads as an elegy for a world that was already vanishing as the vows were spoken.

## Key Passages

> [003-002] The wedding took place in Balham from the house of Griselda's aunt; the ceremony was performed by an enlightened vicar who had consented to omit the ignoble vow of obedience; and the church was thronged to its doors with comrades and ardent sympathizers.

The adjective "ignoble" — attributed to the vicar but clearly endorsed by the narrator — is Hamilton's most direct moral judgment in the novel. The traditional marriage ceremony is not merely outdated; it is ethically degraded. The progressive alternative, by implication, is noble: a marriage of equals, witnessed by a community of shared conviction.

> [004-004] He was young, ambitious, full of his own affairs — and he did not want the world to intrude on his happiness.

The dash before "and he did not want the world to intrude" is Hamilton's structural irony made visible. The sentence performs, in its syntax, the very intrusion it describes — the world breaking into William's carefully arranged happiness.
