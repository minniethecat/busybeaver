## Overview

The British Army in *William—An Englishman* is not a heroic institution but a bureaucratic machine that first humiliates the protagonist by rejecting him, then absorbs him into a life of clerical drudgery indistinguishable from the insurance office he left behind. It is the object of William Tully's desperate aspiration after Griselda's death and the source of his deepest disappointment when he discovers that soldiering, for him, means a typewriter and filing cabinets rather than the bloody revenge he had imagined.

## In the Novel

### The Attempt to Enlist

William's relationship with the British Army begins in rejection. Arriving in London from occupied Belgium, burning with the desire to "strike back" against the German enemy who killed his wife, he presents himself at a recruiting station only to be told that he does not meet the Army's physical requirements. The standard of measurement for recruits had been raised since the outbreak of war, and William, standing under five-foot-five, falls short. The refusal takes him "like a blow between the eyes." He had never dreamed of rejection; the idea that his proffered services might be refused had never entered his head. "It was inhuman, it was monstrous that they should take from him his right to strike back."

### Acceptance and Clerical Drudgery

Eventually—whether through lowered standards or persistence—William is accepted into the Army. But his conception of soldiering, "derived as it was from his own brief and fiery experience in Belgium, from the descriptive articles of war correspondents and his reading of bygone campaigns, had never included the soldier who was merely a clerk." Stationed first in England and later in France, he copies letters and routine orders, files papers, and finds that "for all practical purposes, was back at his desk in the insurance office." His daily duties mock the hopes and ambitions with which he joined. The Army keeps him "as tightly as his straitlaced mother," demanding regular hours and refusing to let him wander at night.

### The Army as a World

Through William's eyes, the British Army becomes a complete society. He encounters men like the Canadian soldier who had "worked his passage homeward in a short-handed tramp" and enlisted on the day war was declared—a rolling stone for whom the Army is just another adventure. He sees the "sunbrowned men of war" on the train at Dieppe and compares them to himself. And he endures the surreal contrast between the "bright, fierce flashes in the evening sky" of real war visible from the hills and the chair and typewriter that constitute his own war service.

Faraday, too, is eventually claimed by the Army under the Conscription Act: he goes without protest, makes "an excellent soldier," and dies fighting in a night raid near Hulluch. The Army that rejected William as too small accepts Faraday as good enough to die.

## Historical Context

The British Army of the First World War was a rapidly expanding institution that grew from a small professional force of some 250,000 regulars in 1914 to a mass conscript army of over five million by 1918. The novel accurately captures several historical details of this transformation: the raising of the minimum height requirement early in the war (from 5'3" to 5'6" in September 1914, before being lowered again as manpower needs grew desperate), the proliferation of clerical and support roles behind the lines, and the introduction of conscription in 1916.

William's experience of army life as bureaucratic monotony reflects a genuine aspect of modern industrialized warfare. For every man in the trenches, many more laboured in offices, depots, and supply chains. The "cushy job at the office of the D.D. of Works" that the Canadian soldier describes—and that William himself occupies—was typical of the vast administrative apparatus required to sustain a continental army. Hamilton's portrayal of the Army as a second insurance office is satirical but grounded in the reality of the "rear-echelon" war experienced by many soldiers.
