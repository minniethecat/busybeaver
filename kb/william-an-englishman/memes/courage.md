# Courage

## Overview

Courage in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is not a fixed virtue but a capacity that develops through testing. Hamilton distinguishes between different kinds of courage — the physical courage of soldiers under fire, the moral courage of suffragettes facing public hostility, and the quieter courage of ordinary people confronting extraordinary circumstances. The novel's central psychological arc traces [[william-tully|William Tully]]'s development from a man who admires courage in others to a man who discovers it in himself, a transformation that is never celebrated as triumph but presented as the costly product of suffering.

Griselda embodies the novel's highest standard of courage. Her "suffragette training" has taught her to face jeers, arrest, and physical danger without flinching. When "[[william-tully|William]] and Griselda felt their courage return with a rush" (006-027), the shared nature of the emotion is significant — courage, in this novel, is contagious, transmitted between people who face danger together. [[william-tully|William]] learns courage not from abstract principles but from proximity to Griselda's example.

## In the Novel

Courage first appears in the novel through the suffrage movement's culture of confrontation. The suffragettes "gather fresh courage" (002-009) from like-minded communities, suggesting that courage is not an individual possession but a collective resource — something that must be replenished through solidarity. When a suffragette faces "jeers and hoots" that "but encouraged her and fired her blood" (002-022), Hamilton presents the paradoxical economy of courage: opposition does not diminish it but increases it.

Griselda's personal courage is repeatedly emphasized. [[william-tully|William]] is "swelled with admiration of her courage and determination" (002-019) — his role as observer rather than participant underlining his own lack. Griselda is described as acting with "unusual courage" (002-025), the qualifier "unusual" marking her as exceptional even within the already exceptional community of suffragettes. Her courage is linked to consciousness — self-awareness that transforms instinctive bravery into deliberate action.

The war brings a different kind of courage into focus. When "[[william-tully|William]] and Griselda felt their courage return with a rush" (006-027) during a moment of danger, the verb "return" suggests that courage is latent, waiting to be summoned by circumstances. The wartime chapters explore what happens when ordinary people — not professional soldiers or professional militants — must find courage they did not know they possessed.

## Historical Context

The concept of courage underwent significant redefinition during WWI. Pre-war ideals of courage were associated with individual heroism — the cavalry charge, the single combat, the conspicuous act of bravery recognized by medals. Trench [[war|warfare]] rendered these ideals obsolete: courage became the steady, unglamorous endurance of shelling, mud, and the daily proximity of death. The war produced new vocabularies of courage — "shell shock" recognized that courage had psychological limits, while "[[pacifism|conscientious objection]]" challenged the equation of courage with willingness to fight.

Hamilton's novel, published in 1919, reflects this expanded understanding of courage. The courage she values is not the spectacular bravery of the battlefield but the quieter fortitude of those who endure. Griselda's courage is political and personal rather than military; [[william-tully|William]]'s eventual courage is the courage of survival rather than of triumph. This democratization of courage — its extension to women, to civilians, to ordinary people — is one of the novel's most significant contributions to the cultural understanding of the virtue.

## Key Passages

> …the like-minded, where fresh courage could be gathered for the struggle… (002-009)

Courage is presented as a renewable resource — something that can be "gathered" from community. The phrase "fresh courage" suggests that courage depletes and must be replenished, a psychological insight that distinguishes Hamilton's treatment from romantic celebrations of innate bravery.

> …the jeers and hoots but encouraged her and fired her blood… (002-022)

The paradox of opposition as fuel: what should discourage instead energizes. "Fired her blood" is a physiological metaphor that grounds courage in the body rather than the mind, suggesting that courage is as much instinct as choice. This aligns with Hamilton's broader naturalistic approach to human emotion.

> …consciousness, combined with unusual courage… (002-025)

The coupling of "consciousness" with "courage" is characteristic of Hamilton's psychological approach. Courage is not blind but self-aware — the courageous person knows what they risk and chooses anyway. This distinguishes genuine courage from mere recklessness or ignorance of danger.

> …William and Griselda felt their courage return with a rush… (006-027)

The shared nature of this experience — both feel courage "return" simultaneously — emphasizes courage as interpersonal. [[william-tully|William]]'s courage is enabled by Griselda's presence; hers is reinforced by his. The verb "return" implies that courage is always present in potential, waiting for the circumstances that summon it.
