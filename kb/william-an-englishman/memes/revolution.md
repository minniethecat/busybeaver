# Revolution

## Overview

Revolution in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is represented not as an abstract political ideal but as a lived reality — embodied in the figure of [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]], the "cosmopolitan female revolutionist" (003-004) whose Belgian cottage becomes the novel's political and dramatic center. Hamilton presents revolutionary politics as both seductive and dangerous: it offers a model of total commitment that makes [[william-tully|William]]'s Fabian gradualism seem pallid by comparison, but it also demands sacrifices — of safety, of stability, of ordinary life — that [[william-tully|William]] is never fully prepared to make.

The revolutionary world of [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]'s cottage represents the novel's most radical alternative to [[william-tully|William]]'s [[class|lower-middle-class]] English life. Here, national boundaries dissolve ("cosmopolitan"), gender roles are inverted ("female revolutionist"), and political action replaces political talk. Hamilton uses the revolutionary milieu to expose the limitations of [[william-tully|William]]'s progressive circle — the meeting-halls and summer schools that talk of changing the world while never risking anything more than social disapproval. Revolution, in this novel, is the real thing: dangerous, international, and ultimately tragic.

## In the Novel

[[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]] first appears in the narrative as a figure of mystery and power. Her revolutionary identity is established immediately: she is described as a "cosmopolitan female revolutionist understood to be of [[russia|Russia]]n origin" (003-004). The phrase "understood to be" is characteristic Hamilton — it suggests both the opacity of [[madame-amberg|Amberg]]'s true identity and the gossipy, second-hand nature of [[william-tully|William]]'s knowledge about her. He enters her world without fully understanding it.

The physical space of revolution is [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]'s cottage, particularly her kitchen. [[william-tully|William]]'s encounter with this space involves the mundane detail of obtaining "the key of [[madame-amberg|the revolutionist]]'s property" (003-013) — a moment that literalizes [[william-tully|William]]'s entry into the revolutionary world. The key is both literal and symbolic: it grants access to a domestic space that is also a political headquarters, blurring the boundary between private and public life in a way that [[william-tully|William]] finds both thrilling and disorienting.

The intellectual content of revolution is represented by "[[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]'s revolutionary library" (004-004), described as "valuable." This library — presumably containing works by Marx, Bakunin, Kropotkin, and other revolutionary theorists — represents the intellectual tradition that [[william-tully|William]], for all his progressive reading, has never engaged with. The library is "valuable" not merely in monetary terms but in intellectual and political terms: it contains ideas that could change the world, and that could get its owner killed.

The novel associates revolutionary enthusiasm with vitality and persistence. [[william-tully|William]], "sustained by revolutionary enthusiasm" (001-044), "refused to" give up — the incomplete sentence suggesting an ongoing struggle. This enthusiasm is contrasted with the enervating effects of [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war life, his clerkdom and his abstract [[progress|reform]]ism. Revolution, w[[hatred|hate]]ver its dangers, is presented as a force that keeps its adherents alive and fighting.

## Historical Context

The pre-WWI period was the high-water mark of international revolutionary politics in Europe. [[russia|Russia]]n revolutionaries — including Lenin, Trotsky, and numerous lesser-known figures — operated in exile across Western Europe, using cities like [[london|London]], [[paris|Paris]], and Geneva as bases for organizing. The 1905 [[russia|Russia]]n Revolution had demonstrated both the possibility and the difficulty of revolutionary change, and the international left was split between those who advocated immediate revolution and those who favored gradual [[progress|reform]].

Hamilton's choice to make [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]] a "cosmopolitan female revolutionist" reflects the significant role that women played in the revolutionary movements of the period. Figures like Rosa Luxembourg — whose signed photograph hangs in [[madame-amberg|Amberg]]'s cottage — Vera Figner, and Emma Goldman combined revolutionary politics with feminist commitments. The novel's treatment of [[madame-amberg|Amberg]] is notably sympathetic: she is presented as admirable rather than monstrous, her revolutionary commitment as authentic rather than fanatical. This distinguishes *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* from the more sensationalist treatments of revolutionaries in Edwardian popular fiction.

## Key Passages

> …sustained by revolutionary enthusiasm, he refused to… (001-044)

The phrase "sustained by revolutionary enthusiasm" is applied to [[william-tully|William]] early in the novel, before he has encountered actual revolutionaries. It suggests that revolutionary feeling, for [[william-tully|William]], is initially a personal resource rather than a political commitment — something that helps him persist rather than something that demands action. The incomplete sentence ("refused to…") leaves the object of refusal open, suggesting a general stubbornness rather than a specific political stand.

> …acquaintance with a cosmopolitan female revolutionist understood to be of Russian origin… (003-004)

The chain of qualifiers — "cosmopolitan," "female," "understood to be," "of [[russia|Russia]]n origin" — captures both the exoticism and the uncertainty of [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]'s identity. She is defined by what she is not (English, male, knowable) as much as by what she is. The phrase "understood to be" suggests that [[madame-amberg|Amberg]]'s identity is a matter of rumor and reputation, not of verifiable fact — an ambiguity that serves her revolutionary purposes.

> …Madame Amberg's revolutionary library, valuable as it was… (004-004)

The library functions as a metonym for revolutionary politics itself — a collection of dangerous ideas housed in a domestic space. That it is described as "valuable" suggests both its intellectual worth and its practical importance to the revolutionary cause. The library represents knowledge that is both empowering and incriminating, a resource that can educate or destroy.
