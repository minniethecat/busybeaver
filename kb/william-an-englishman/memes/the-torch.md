## Overview

*The Torch* is a radical socialist periodical that appears throughout *William—An Englishman* as the journalistic organ of the "advanced" political circles to which Faraday and his followers belong. Its editor, known by the pseudonym "Vindex," is Faraday himself—a fact that is an open secret in advanced circles but carefully guarded at the insurance office where he and William work. *The Torch* represents the pre-war world of confident, combative progressive journalism: its pages preach "a purer and more violent rebellion," denounce the established order, and nurture the political awakening of young men like William Tully.

## In the Novel

### The Revelation

William first learns that Faraday is "Vindex" of *The Torch* when his colleague invites him to his Bloomsbury lodgings for an evening of political discussion. Faraday, "young, ambitious, fiercely in earnest and ever on the lookout for his Chance," initially mistakes William for a potential patron—dreaming of a comrade "whose newly-inherited riches might be used to endow a periodical that should preach a purer and more violent rebellion even than *The Torch* itself." When he discovers that William is merely "a nervous little nincompoop" with modest means, his disappointment is palpable, but he softens and takes William under his wing nonetheless.

### William's Political Education

Under Faraday's influence, cultivated through the pages of *The Torch* and evening discussions in Bloomsbury, William's dull resentment against the restrictions of his narrow life merges into "a wider sympathy with the general discomfort, in an honest and fiery little passion for Justice, Right and Progress." The periodical becomes, for William, the voice of a world he had never known existed—a world of Class War, International movements, and contemptuous dismissal of established politicians.

### The War and Its Aftermath

When war breaks out in August 1914, *The Torch* continues to publish, but its editor struggles with the new reality. Faraday writes "passionately, if with less than his usual clarity, in *The Torch*," defending pacifist and anti-British sentiment while conscious of "occasional difficulty and a sense of thin ice underneath him." The periodical's pre-war certainties—its assumption that international war was impossible, that the real enemy was always at home—collide with the fact of German invasion and the sight of comrades enlisting. Faraday himself would later be called up under the Conscription Act, make "an excellent soldier," and die fighting in a night raid near Hulluch.

## Historical Context

*The Torch* is a fictional periodical, but it belongs recognizably to the world of Edwardian radical journalism. The early twentieth century saw a flourishing of socialist and suffragist newspapers in Britain—publications like *The Clarion* (founded 1891), *The New Age* (1907), *The Freewoman* (1911), and *The Daily Herald* (1912). These papers combined fierce political advocacy with literary ambition, and their editors were often charismatic figures who built personal followings. Faraday, with his Board School scholarship, his self-education, and his "fluent as well as sarcastic" pen, is a recognizable type: the autodidact journalist who rises from the working class to become "a man of mark" in Labour and Socialist circles. The name "*The Torch*" itself—evoking enlightenment, revolution, and the passing of flame—was characteristic of the period's radical press, which favoured titles that suggested illumination and awakening.

The trajectory of *The Torch* during the war—from confident anti-militarism to confusion and eventual silence as its editor is conscripted—mirrors the experience of many real radical publications. The war split the British left, with figures like Ramsay MacDonald and Philip Snowden opposing the conflict while others, like H. G. Wells and Robert Blatchford, supported it. *The Torch*'s struggle to maintain its pre-war certainties in the face of the German advance captures a genuinely historical dilemma.
