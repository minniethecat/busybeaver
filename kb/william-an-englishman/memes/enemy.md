# The Enemy

## Overview

The enemy in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* evolves from an abstraction to a reality, transforming [[william-tully|William Tully]]'s moral universe. Before the war, "the enemy" is a political category — "enemy speakers" (001-044) to be heckled at [[the-meeting|public meetings]]. The war transforms the enemy into something concrete: the forces occupying [[belgium|Belgium]], the soldiers who threaten Griselda, the "hand and heel of the enemy" (014-006) that crushes civilian populations. [[william-tully|William]]'s relationship to the enemy — from intellectual opposition to visceral hatred to something approaching recognition — traces the novel's most profound moral trajectory.

Hamilton's treatment of the enemy is distinguished by its refusal of both jingoistic demonization and sentimental humanization. The enemy is real and threatening, and [[william-tully|William]]'s hatred is psychologically authentic. But the novel also records moments when the distinction between friend and foe blurs — "on the enemy's side as on ours" (018-009) — suggesting that the enemy, too, is human.

## In the Novel

[[william-tully|William]]'s first encounter with "the enemy" is political: "he tried heckling the enemy speakers" (001-044). This is the enemy as rhetorical opponent, to be defeated by argument. The verb "tried" suggests [[william-tully|William]]'s inexperience — even in this safe context, he struggles to engage. His "first and flustered" attempt at heckling reveals the gap between progressive [[faith|conviction]] and personal capacity.

The war transforms the enemy from rhetorical to existential. Reports describe civilians "under the hand and heel of the enemy" (014-006) — bodily metaphors emphasizing physical domination. The Belgian chapters explore the enemy's proximity: "warned of the likelihood of enemy occupation" (012-013). The bureaucratic phrase masks the reality it names — the [[grief|loss]] of home, safety, and autonomy.

The novel's final treatment of the enemy gestures toward recognition of shared humanity: "on the enemy's side as on ours" (018-009) refers to aerial activity, the same regardless of nationality. The parallelism levels the moral distinction, suggesting that the experience of war is universal. This recognition does not excuse but complicates the simple binary of friend and enemy.

## Historical Context

The construction of the enemy was central to WWI propaganda. The Germans were portrayed as "Huns" — barbaric and inhuman — in posters, newspapers, and official statements. The Bryce Report of 1915, documenting alleged German atrocities in [[belgium|Belgium]], shaped British public opinion, though many claims were later questioned. Soldiers at the front often developed more nuanced views, recognizing in German soldiers the same suffering and fear they experienced.

Hamilton's novel, published in 1919, participates in the post-war reevaluation. It neither endorses wartime demonization nor embraces facile [[pacifism|pacifist]] universalism. Instead, it traces [[william-tully|William]]'s evolving relationship to the enemy with psychological precision, showing how hatred and recognition can coexist.

## Key Passages

> …he tried heckling the enemy speakers. His first and flustered… (001-044)

The gap between intention and outcome captures [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war political ineptitude. "Enemy speakers" is a political category — the enemy as debate opponent — marking [[william-tully|William]]'s distance from the world about to arrive.

> …under the hand and heel of the enemy… (014-006)

"Hand and heel" emphasizes bodily domination — the enemy reduced to its most threatening parts. The synecdoche depersonalizes while intensifying: the enemy is not an abstract force but a physical presence.

> …on the enemy's side as on ours… (018-009)

The parallelism is the novel's most compressed statement of universal human experience in war. Aerial activity is the same regardless of which side one fights for, complicating the moral framework that requires the enemy to be utterly other.
