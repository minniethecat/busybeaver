## Overview

The **Recruiting Campaign** refers to the British government's drive to raise volunteers for [[kitchener|Kitchener's Army]] during the early months of the First World War. In [[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]], the recruiting campaign serves as both a narrative device and a symbolic force — drawing [[william-tully|William Tully]] toward enlistment while also becoming the target of derision by anti-war activists.

## William's Encounter with the Campaign

William first learns of the recruiting drive through a newspaper account while still in Belgium:

> [014-006] An article in The Daily Chronicle—some days old—described the beginning of the recruiting campaign for the raising of Kitchener's Army; he read it as he read everything else that explained or described the war.

The campaign represents a path toward action for William, who increasingly desires to translate his newfound convictions into concrete commitment. After the trauma of witnessing the [[the-shooting-of-the-hostages|shooting of the hostages]] and the [[the-flight|flight from Belgium]], enlistment becomes his sole focus.

## Rejection and Irony

Despite his fervent desire to enlist, William is rejected at the recruiting station because he falls below the Army's minimum height requirement:

> [014-022] ...he did not know till he came to enlist that the standard of measurement for recruits had been raised since the outbreak of war, and that he, standing under five-foot-five, was not up to the Army's requirements in the matter of breadth and inches.

This rejection creates a bitter irony that defines much of William's subsequent experience: he is willing to fight and die but is deemed physically unfit, while others more physically capable mock the campaign that he desperately wishes to join.

## The Anti-Militarist Critique

At [[the-meeting|the anti-militarist meeting]] in Chapter XV, the recruiting campaign is openly ridiculed:

> [015-016] ...Jay-Blenkinsop, having sufficiently scarified the official representatives of Labour, went on to some smartly turned gibes at the recruiting campaign and the gulls who were caught by its appeals.

[[blenkinsop|Edgar Jay-Blenkinsop's]] mockery of the campaign and its volunteers enrages William, who sees in the young man's complacent derision everything he has come to despise about armchair reformism.
