## Overview

The shooting of the hostages is the central traumatic event of the early chapters of *William—An Englishman*, occurring in a Belgian village in August 1914 during the German invasion. William Tully and his new wife Griselda, captured while attempting to flee their honeymoon cottage in the Ardennes, are marched into the village and forced to witness the execution of two civilian hostages — the mayor and another man — by a German firing squad. The event shatters William's long-held faith in rational persuasion, public opinion, and the power of collective action to prevent war, marking his conversion from anti-militarist activist to a man who will eventually enlist in the British Army.

## Context

William and Griselda Tully are on their honeymoon in a secluded cottage in the Belgian Ardennes, lent to them by the cosmopolitan revolutionary [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]. Their idyll is shattered by the sudden German invasion of Belgium in early August 1914 [006-048]. Captured by a German patrol led by Heinz — the same Heinz who had earlier interrogated them at their cottage — they are marched under guard towards the nearest village. Along the way they witness columns of German infantry and the sounds of distant artillery, but William remains stubbornly unconvinced of the reality of war. His faith in Law, public opinion, and the ultimate triumph of peaceful reason remains intact [007-001].

## The Event

The execution takes place in a village street. The hostages — two Belgian civilians — are brought out and positioned against a yellow-washed garden wall. A file of German soldiers faces them, commanded by a young officer:

> [007-010] The two blindfolded men were standing with their backs to a blank wall—a yellow-washed, eight-foot garden wall with the boughs of a pear-tree drooping over it. It was opposite the yellow-washed wall, across the road, that the file of soldiers was drawn up; the captives were facing the muzzles of their rifles and the red-faced boy-officer had stationed himself stiffly at the farther end of the file.

One of the hostages is the village mayor, a small man who attempts to face his death with dignity. The other is an older man with grizzled hair. The moment of the shooting is rendered with brutal economy:

> [008-001] Long beating seconds (three or four of them at most) while two men stood upright with bandaged eyes and rifles pointed at their hearts; long beating seconds, while a bird fluted in the pear-tree—a whistle-note infinitely careless. … And then (thank God for it!) a voice and a report that were as one. … The man with the grizzled hair threw out an arm and toppled with his face in the dust; the mayor slid sideways against the wall with the blood dribbling from his mouth.

The juxtaposition of the bird's carefree song with the calculated killing is one of the novel's most powerful images, encapsulating the indifference of nature to human atrocity.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The shooting has a transformative effect on both William and Griselda, though in different ways.

**For William**, the event is an intellectual and spiritual earthquake. His entire worldview — built on years of socialist and anti-militarist activism — collapses in an instant:

> [008-001] Fundamentally William was no more of a coward than the majority of his fellow-men, and, put to it, he would have emulated the shivering little mayor and tried to strut gamely to his end; it was as much sheer bewildered amazement as the baseness of bodily terror that had him by the throat when he saw the hostages done to death—sickening and shaking him and, for the moment, depriving him of self-control.

He realises with the force of revelation that he, Faraday, and the entire anti-war movement had been "madly, wildly, ridiculously wrong" [008-001]. The experience plants the seed of his later enlistment and his gradual embrace of national identity.

**For Griselda**, the shock is equally profound but expressed differently. For the first time since witnessing the deaths, she speaks — not of political theories but of primitive, personal vengeance:

> [009-001] For the first time since she had seen the hostages die—she unclosed her lips and spoke: "If I were a man, I would kill. I would go out to kill. I would not care what happened to me afterwards, if I could only kill."

The blood on the yellow-washed wall becomes an obsessive image for Griselda, and she returns to the spot later in the novel [010-004], alone, staring at the place where the men died.

The shooting of the hostages is later invoked by William as the foundational justification for his enlistment and his rejection of Faraday's anti-war position [014-006]. It represents the novel's core argument: that abstract political idealism cannot survive direct contact with the brutal reality of war.
