## About the Places

Place in Hamilton's novel is never neutral. The cottage at Shere represents an ideal of English rural domesticity; the meeting halls of London are sites of political awakening; the Belgian battlefields are the crucible where William's naive patriotism is tested and broken. Each place page maps the geographical coordinates of the novel's political argument, tracing how Hamilton uses setting to expose the gap between the rhetoric of "England" and the realities her characters inhabit.
