# Conscience

## Overview

Conscience in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is not the stern inner judge of Protestant tradition but a remarkably accommodating companion. Hamilton presents conscience as a faculty that can be satisfied with remarkably little — a gesture, a formula, a moment's acknowledgment before returning to comfortable habits. The novel's treatment of conscience is quietly devastating, revealing how easily moral self-scrutiny can be short-circuited by the simple act of feeling that one has done enough.

Hamilton twice uses the phrase "having thus satisfied their consciences" to describe moments of moral evasion. In each case, a small gesture of principled disapproval is followed by untroubled enjoyment — the conscience having been paid off, as it were, like a creditor who accepts token payments. This pattern is not presented as hypocrisy but as human nature: the conscience, in Hamilton's account, wants to be satisfied, and will accept remarkably little to achieve that state.

## In the Novel

The novel's most explicit treatment of conscience occurs during [[william-tully|William]] and Griselda's honeymoon journey through [[belgium|Belgium]]. Passing the fortifications of Namur, "they felt it their duty as the vehicle rattled them along to make a few depreciatory and high-principled remarks on the subject of the towering fortifications; but having thus satisfied their consciences they relapsed into mere enjoyment of rest, novelty, a good dinner, and a view of the lazy Meuse" (003-005). The sequence is precise: duty, a few remarks, satisfaction, then enjoyment. The "few depreciatory and high-principled remarks" are the toll paid at the gate — once paid, the conscience permits uncomplicated pleasure.

The pattern recurs in the novel's broader moral landscape. Conscience is not a source of action but of self-approval. It is what allows characters to feel good about themselves without actually doing anything difficult. Hamilton's irony is gentle but unmistakable: the conscience, far from being a demanding master, is the easiest of companions, requiring only a verbal acknowledgment before releasing its holder into untroubled enjoyment.

The brief but significant early reference frames the entire moral world of the novel. "The two best gifts of life are love and an approving conscience" (002-009) — the pairing is instructive. An "approving" conscience, not an active or demanding one. It is a conscience that nods along with one's choices, that "looked on and smiled" as [[william-tully|William]] and Griselda dreamed their dreams. This is conscience as comfort, not conscience as challenge.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period inherited from the Victorians a robust tradition of moral self-examination, rooted in evangelical Protestantism but secularized through the ethical culture movements of the late nineteenth century. The "Nonconformist conscience" was a recognized force in British politics — a moral sensibility associated with the Liberal Party, temperance [[progress|reform]], and opposition to what was called "continental immorality."

Yet the Edwardian era also saw the rise of psychological accounts of morality that undermined the traditional picture of conscience as a direct channel to divine or universal moral truth. Figures like Havelock Ellis and the early psychoanalytic writers were beginning to treat conscience as a psychological mechanism — one that could be shaped, manipulated, and satisfied through symbolic gestures rather than substantive moral action. Hamilton, writing at the boundary between Victorian moralism and postwar skepticism, captures conscience in this transitional moment: still invoked, still valued, but increasingly understood as a faculty that can be managed rather than obeyed.

Hamilton's treatment of conscience also reflects the broader disillusionment of the war years. The moral certainties that had seemed solid in 1914 — patriotism, honor, the justice of one's cause — had been hollowed out by four years of industrial slaughter. In this context, the idea of an "approving conscience" becomes deeply ambiguous: is conscience approving because one has acted rightly, or because one has learned to ask very little of it?

## Key Passages

> The two best gifts of life are love and an approving conscience. These twain, William and Griselda, loved each other sincerely — if not with the tempestuous passion of a Romeo and a Juliet, with an honest and healthy affection; they had for each other an attraction which could set their pulses beating and start them dreaming dreams. That evening, on the bench in Battersea Park, they had dreamed their dreams — while their consciences looked on and smiled. (002-009)

The personification of conscience as something that "looked on and smiled" is revealing. Conscience is not a participant in moral life but a spectator — benevolent, indulgent, asking nothing. Hamilton's choice of "Twain" for the pairing of love and conscience echoes biblical language, lending ironic weight to what is, in effect, a description of moral complacency. The consciences that "smiled" are consciences that have been given no reason not to smile — they have not yet been tested by the war that will shortly shatter this pastoral scene.

> …having thus satisfied their consciences they relapsed into mere enjoyment… (003-005)

The economy of conscience: a payment is made, and the debt is discharged. The "few depreciatory and high-principled remarks" about fortifications function as a moral indulgence — a small good work that buys permission for pleasure. "Relapsed" carries connotations of moral failure, but Hamilton's tone is observational rather than condemnatory. This is simply how conscience works, she implies — it is satisfied by tokens.
