# Pacifism

## Overview

Pacifism in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is treated not as a noble ideal but as a dodge — a convenient [[faith|conviction]] that allows the self-absorbed to ignore facts they find inconvenient. Cicely Hamilton devotes some of her most acid prose to the subject, arguing that "the aggressive and essentially militarist type of political enthusiast so often runs to pacifism where the quarrels of others are concerned" (015-002). This paradox — that the most combative personalities are the most likely to profess peace — is central to the novel's critique of pre-war [[socialism|socialist]] orthodoxy.

Pacifism in the novel is not a principled moral stance but a form of intellectual blindness, a refusal to accept a reality that threatens one's own political projects. The war, Hamilton writes, "was the successful rival of their own, their sectional strife, overshadowing its importance and sucking the life from its veins" (015-002). When a minor political cause is suddenly eclipsed by a national existential threat, the instinctive response of the committed partisan is to deny the larger threat's existence — to insist that war is avoidable, should not have been, and therefore can be ignored.

## In the Novel

The novel's principal pacifist is [[faraday|Faraday]], [[william-tully|William Tully]]'s fellow-clerk, whose journey from [[socialism|socialist]] agitator to reluctant soldier traces the arc of pacifist [[faith|conviction]] under pressure. In the early months of the war, [[faraday|Faraday]] "held peace meetings at considerable personal risk, which, as became a good fighter, he took, even with enjoyment; distributed printed appeals to pacifist and anti-British sentiment and wrote passionately, if with less than his usual clarity, in *The Torch*" (015-002). The phrase "as became a good fighter" is deliberately ironic — [[faraday|Faraday]]'s pacifism is itself a form of combat, indistinguishable in its psychology from the militarism he condemns.

[[faraday|Faraday]]'s position is undermined by its own contradictions. He "could not in honesty bring himself to defend every action of his country's enemies and was conscious of occasional difficulty and a sense of thin ice underneath him" (015-002). The practical impossibility of consistent anti-war ideology — the fact that other nations besides Britain also had agency, also committed acts that could not be defended — produces in [[faraday|Faraday]] a "momentary confusion of thought and outlook of which [[faraday|Faraday]] himself was quite clear-brained enough to be conscious; it irritated him and he was sensible of failure and uncertainty" (015-002).

[[william-tully|William Tully]], by contrast, moves in the opposite direction. The war, and specifically the death of Griselda, burns the pacifism out of him. When [[griselda|Edith Haynes]] meets the postwar [[william-tully|William]], she suspects "a drift towards his former 'pacifism'" but "soon discovered her mistake" (018-003). [[william-tully|William]] has not returned to his prewar [[faith|belief]]s; instead "the one rock on which he stood fast was that [[faith|conviction]] of error which had come to him in the Forest of Arden." He now has "no use for" a pacifist party that "had never, as he had, made confession of its fundamental error" (018-003). [[william-tully|William]]'s contempt for his former pacifism is absolute because it is firsthand — he knows what it is to be wrong about the most fundamental question, and he has no patience for those who have not undergone the same painful reckoning.

## Historical Context

Pacifism in Edwardian and WWI Britain was a diverse and internally contested movement. The peace advocacy of the pre-war [[socialism|Labour movement]], typified by figures like Keir Hardie, drew on [[socialism|socialist]] internationalism — the [[faith|conviction]] that working-class solidarity across borders would make war impossible. The [[labour-party|Independent Labour Party]] opposed British entry into the war in 1914, and organizations like the Union of Democratic Control and the No-Conscription Fellowship provided institutional homes for anti-war sentiment throughout the conflict.

The introduction of conscription in 1916 forced the issue: conscientious objectors faced tribunals, imprisonment, and sometimes brutal treatment. Yet Hamilton's novel is notably unsympathetic to this tradition. Published in 1919, when the war's toll was fresh and the post-war reckoning with pacifism was underway, *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* reflects a widespread impatience with those who had predicted that war could not happen. The novel's treatment of pacifism mirrors the disillusionment of many [[socialism|socialist]]s who, like [[william-tully|William]], had seen their internationalist [[faith|conviction]]s shattered by the reality of August 1914.

## Key Passages

> …the aggressive and essentially militarist type of political enthusiast so often runs to pacifism where the quarrels of others are concerned. (015-002)

Hamilton's sharpest insight into the psychology of pacifism: those most bellicose in domestic politics are often most insistent on peace abroad. The "quarrels of others" framing is devastating — it reduces international conflict to a nuisance that interferes with more important local battles. Pacifism here is not principle but proprietary defensiveness.

> …he was conscious of occasional difficulty and a sense of thin ice underneath him… (015-002)

[[faraday|Faraday]]'s self-awareness is what distinguishes him from a caricature. He knows his position is untenable, and this knowledge "irritated him." The metaphor of thin ice captures the precariousness of ideological [[faith|conviction]] that must ignore facts to sustain itself.

> …the one rock on which he stood fast was that conviction of error which had come to him in the Forest of Arden. (018-003)

The inversion is striking: [[william-tully|William]]'s firmest [[faith|conviction]] is his [[faith|conviction]] of having been wrong. His certainty about his own past error becomes the foundation of his postwar identity. He has moved from one kind of certainty (pacifist dogma) to another (the certainty of having been wrong), but the latter, Hamilton implies, is earned — it comes from experience rather than theory.
