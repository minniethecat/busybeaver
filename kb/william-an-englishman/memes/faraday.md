# Faraday

## Overview

Faraday is William Tully's intellectual mentor and the architect of his pre-war political identity. A fellow insurance clerk, Faraday introduces William to the world of progressive politics — social reform, anti-militarism, the "Woman Question" — and provides the language and categories through which William understands his own emerging convictions. Hamilton's treatment of Faraday is characteristically ambivalent: he is genuinely admirable in his intellectual energy and moral seriousness, but his influence on William is also limiting, providing ready-made opinions that substitute for independent thought.

Faraday represents the Fabian ideal of the self-educated, politically engaged citizen. He is described as William's "fellow-clerk," sharing the same lower-middle-class office world, but his intellectual life transcends it. He is the conduit through which progressive ideas reach William — a role that is both generous and, in retrospect, problematic, since William absorbs Faraday's ideas without Faraday's intellectual discipline.

## Role in the Novel

Faraday functions primarily as an influence rather than a presence. He appears most vividly in the novel's early chapters, where his conversations with William establish the political vocabulary that will be tested and ultimately destroyed by the war. His anti-militarism, his faith in social reform, his conviction that "militarism" is "the blackest of crimes" (002-003) — all of these become William's convictions too, inherited rather than earned.

The novel is careful not to dismiss Faraday's ideas simply because they prove inadequate to the war. His commitment to social improvement, his belief in the power of rational argument, his dedication to "the holiest of causes" (002-007) — these are genuine moral achievements. But they are also untested, and the novel's trajectory exposes the gap between believing in principles and being forced to act on them. Faraday, who never appears in the Belgian chapters, remains frozen in the novel's pre-war world, a figure of what William was before experience transformed him.

## Key Relationships

- **William Tully** — His protégé, whose political education Faraday directs and whose subsequent transformation he never witnesses. (See [[william-tully]])

## Key Passages

> …sharer of his schemes, of his hatreds and laudable ambitions… (001-042)

This description of William's relationship with Faraday captures the fusion of personal and political that characterizes their friendship. Faraday shares not just ideas but emotions — "hatreds" as well as "ambitions" — and William receives them whole.
