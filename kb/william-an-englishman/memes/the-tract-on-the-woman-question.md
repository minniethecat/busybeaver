## Overview

"The Tract on the Woman Question" is William Tully's unfinished polemic — the book he begins writing during his honeymoon in the Belgian Ardennes in the summer of 1914. It represents the fullest expression of his pre-war intellectual ambition: the transformation from insurance clerk to "Social Reformer," a title he claims with the same earnest sincerity with which he once claimed the platform name "Vindex." The tract is never completed — the German invasion of Belgium interrupts William's work, and the war that follows destroys not only the manuscript but the entire intellectual framework in which it was conceived.

The tract's subject, "the Woman Question," was a standard topic in Edwardian progressive circles, encompassing debates about suffrage, women's employment, marriage reform, and sexual equality. William's choice of subject is characteristic: he selects a topic that is both fashionable and safely abstract, requiring no personal risk and no confrontation with the actual women — like [[griselda|Griselda]] — whose lived experience might complicate his arguments. The tract belongs to the world of pamphlets and meetings that formed William's intellectual formation, a world his mentor [[faraday|Faraday]] inhabited with professional confidence.

## In the Novel

William begins the tract during the honeymoon at [[the-cottage]], the Ardennes retreat loaned to the couple by [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]]. Griselda "gently but firmly insisted that even in the first ecstasy of their honeymoon he should not lay it aside" [004-004], and the image of William writing at the cottage table while Griselda reads nearby — "now and again she glanced up that she might meet his eye and smile" [004-004] — is one of the novel's most deliberately idyllic passages. The domestic harmony of the scene is built on a foundation of shared political conviction: both William and Griselda believe that writing about the Woman Question is meaningful work, a contribution to the progressive cause that has given their lives purpose.

The irony, of course, is that William is writing about abstractions while sitting in the path of an invading army. Hamilton does not need to underline this point; the structure of the novel does it for her. The tract, with its careful arguments and earnest citations, exists in the same moral universe as the pamphlets William "crammed his head with" [001-042] — a universe of words that the war will render not merely irrelevant but unintelligible. When the German cavalry arrives in Chapter VI, William's manuscript is abandoned along with every other artifact of his pre-war identity.

William never returns to the tract. The man who returns to England after the flight through Belgium is incapable of writing it — not because he has lost the skill but because he has lost the conviction that such writing matters. The tract thus functions in the novel as a symbol of pre-war innocence: the intellectual ambition of a man who believed that social problems could be solved through the orderly accumulation of arguments, and who had not yet learned that the world is not arranged to accommodate such beliefs.

## Historical Context

"The Woman Question" was a ubiquitous phrase in Edwardian Britain, encompassing the full spectrum of debates about women's roles in society: suffrage, employment, education, marriage law, divorce reform, and sexual morality. The phrase appeared regularly in periodicals from the *Fortnightly Review* to the *Englishwoman*, and it generated an enormous pamphlet literature. Organizations like the Fabian Women's Group produced systematic studies; the WSPU and NUWSS published tracts arguing for and against militancy; and individual writers — from Mona Caird to Cicely Hamilton herself — contributed book-length treatments.

Hamilton was intimately familiar with this literature. As a member of the WSPU and co-founder of the Women Writers' Suffrage League, she had written and debated on the Woman Question for more than a decade before publishing *William—An Englishman*. Her 1909 play *How the Vote Was Won* and her 1910 polemic *Marriage as a Trade* were direct interventions in these debates. William's tract, in this context, is not merely a fictional artifact but Hamilton's gentle satire of a particular kind of male progressive: the man who writes about women's emancipation without consulting women, who approaches the Woman Question as an intellectual exercise rather than a lived reality.

The pamphlet culture that formed William's reading — "they read pamphlets issued, and books recommended" [002-006] — was a distinctive feature of Edwardian political life. Cheap printing and an expanding literate public created a golden age of the political pamphlet, and organizations from the Fabian Society to the WSPU distributed millions of tracts annually. [[madame-amberg|Madame Amberg]], the "cosmopolitan female revolutionist" who "wrote vehement pamphlets in all these languages" [003-010], is another representative of this culture, albeit one whose revolutionary commitments are more serious — and more dangerous — than William's.

## Key Passages

> ...cramming his head with pamphlets and the perfervid utterances of the platform — growing more ignorant, daily, of the world of men. [001-042]

The passage that describes the intellectual formation from which the tract emerges. William "cramming his head" is a student of pamphlets rather than of life, and Hamilton's phrase "growing more ignorant, daily, of the world of men" is the novel's verdict on an education conducted entirely through print. The tract is the product of this education, and its incompleteness is the war's judgment on it.

> ...they read pamphlets issued, and books recommended, by leaders of the various Leagues from which they derived their spiritual sustenance. [002-006]

The religious language — "spiritual sustenance" — is characteristic Hamilton irony. The pamphlets function as scripture in the progressive faith William and Griselda share, and writing one's own tract is equivalent to joining the priesthood. This passage illuminates both the sincerity and the fragility of the intellectual culture in which William's tract was conceived.

> ...Griselda accordingly, gently but firmly insisted that even in the first ecstasy of their honeymoon he should not lay it aside. So William wrote at the table by the open window, and Griselda sat near him under the trees with a book; and now and again she glanced up that she might meet his eye and smile. [004-004]

The idyll of intellectual companionship, rendered with Hamilton's characteristic mixture of tenderness and irony. The scene is genuine — William's happiness in this moment is real — but the reader knows what the characters do not: that the German army is already crossing the frontier, that the manuscript will be abandoned, and that the world in which writing a tract about the Woman Question could be a meaningful act is about to end.
