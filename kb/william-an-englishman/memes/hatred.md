# Hatred

## Overview

Hatred in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is not a simple vice to be condemned but a complex psychological force that Hamilton examines with clinical precision. The novel traces the emergence of hatred in [[william-tully|William Tully]]'s emotional life — from the abstract "hatreds and laudable ambitions" (001-042) of his pre-war progressive circle to the visceral hatred of wartime enemies and domestic politicians. Hamilton presents hatred as a necessary but dangerous stage in [[william-tully|William]]'s political maturation: it destroys his innocence but also energizes his [[faith|conviction]]s, transforming him from a passive consumer of ideas into an active participant in history.

The novel's treatment of hatred is unusual for its refusal to moralize. [[william-tully|William]]'s hatreds are neither celebrated as righteous indignation nor condemned as moral failure. Instead, Hamilton traces their psychological function: how hatred focuses diffuse grief into purposeful action, how it converts personal [[grief|loss]] into political commitment, how it serves as the dark twin of patriotism. This psychological realism — the willingness to acknowledge hatred as a real and potent force in human affairs — distinguishes the novel from the sentimental conventions of much Edwardian fiction.

## In the Novel

Hatred appears early in the novel as part of [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war emotional repertoire. He is described as a "sharer of his schemes, of his hatreds and laudable ambitions" (001-042) — the phrase placing hatreds alongside schemes and ambitions as equal components of [[william-tully|William]]'s inner life. The plural "hatreds" suggests a diffuse, almost recreational animosity: the hatreds of the progressive for those who oppose progress, the hatred of the enlightened for the unenlightened.

The novel's richest exploration of hatred comes through its intertextual reference to the Biblical Psalm 137: [[william-tully|William]] is compared to "him who sang out his hatred by the alien waters of Babylon" (014-003). This psalm — "By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down, yea, we wept, when we remembered Zion" — is one of the most powerful expressions of exile and hatred in Western literature, ending with the infamous curse: "Happy shall he be, that taketh and dasheth thy little ones against the stones." By invoking this text, Hamilton places [[william-tully|William]]'s personal hatred in a cosmic frame, suggesting that the hatred of the exile for the conqueror is as old as civilization itself.

[[william-tully|William]]'s hatred finds a domestic target in [[jay-blenkinsop|Jay-Blenkinsop]], a figure of complacent authority: "he was gripped with a sudden hatred of [[jay-blenkinsop|Jay-Blenkinsop]], a hatred that" (015-016) — the sentence breaking off before specifying what the hatred leads to. This hatred of the comfortable civilian by the traumatized soldier is a recurring motif in WWI literature. [[jay-blenkinsop|Jay-Blenkinsop]] represents everything [[william-tully|William]] has lost: the safety, the certainty, the moral simplicity of pre-war life.

The novel's final assessment of hatred is characteristically ambivalent. "There was always a smoulder of hatred" (018-002) — the word "smoulder" suggesting both persistence and containment. Hatred does not disappear with time or healing; it remains, banked like a fire, ready to flare. Yet "neither hatred nor weariness had blinded his" (018-003) — the sentence incomplete but suggesting that hatred has not destroyed [[william-tully|William]]'s essential humanity. He has learned to live with hatred without being consumed by it.

## Historical Context

The role of hatred in wartime psychology was a subject of intense debate during and after WWI. Official propaganda on all sides encouraged hatred of the enemy — the Germans as "Huns," the British as "perfidious Albion" — while soldiers at the front often developed more complex attitudes. The Christmas Truce of 1914 demonstrated that hatred was not inherent but constructed, while the escalating brutality of the war made hatred increasingly difficult to resist.

Hamilton published her novel in 1919, at a moment when the question of whether to sustain or abandon wartime hatreds was politically urgent. The Treaty of Versailles was built on the premise of German war guilt, while voices like John Maynard Keynes's *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* argued that punitive peace would breed future conflict. Hamilton's novel, with its nuanced treatment of hatred as both unavoidable and dangerous, participates in this debate by refusing easy answers. [[william-tully|William]]'s hatreds are real and justified, but they are also corrosive — a source of energy that threatens to consume its host.

## Key Passages

> …sharer of his schemes, of his hatreds and laudable ambitions… (001-042)

The casual listing of "hatreds" alongside "schemes" and "ambitions" reveals the ordinariness of hatred in [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war life. Hatred is not yet the consuming force it will become; it is merely one emotional resource among others, a component of the progressive identity that [[william-tully|William]] inhabits without fully understanding.

> …the desire of him who sang out his hatred by the alien waters of Babylon… (014-003)

This biblical allusion transforms [[william-tully|William]]'s personal grief into something archetypal. The Psalmist's hatred — born of exile and the destruction of Jerusalem — prefigures [[william-tully|William]]'s hatred, born of Griselda's death and the destruction of his Belgian idyll. Hamilton suggests that hatred in response to devastation is not pathological but human, a response as old as the Psalms themselves.

> …he was gripped with a sudden hatred of Jay-Blenkinsop, a hatred that… (015-016)

The verb "gripped" emphasizes the involuntary nature of [[william-tully|William]]'s hatred — it seizes him rather than being chosen. The incomplete sentence leaves the consequences of this hatred unspecified, inviting the reader to imagine both its destructive potential and its possible transmutation into action.

> …there was always a smoulder of hatred; but he had seen much of the… (018-002)

The metaphor of smouldering — fire that has not quite gone out — captures the persistence of wartime hatred in peacetime. Hatred is neither extinguished nor blazing; it is latent, ready to ignite. The conjunction "but" introduces a qualification that the reader must complete: [[william-tully|William]] has seen much, experienced much, that explains if not excuses his continuing hatred.
