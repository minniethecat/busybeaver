## Overview

Wright is a British soldier who appears in Chapter XVIII of *William—An Englishman*, during the German air raid on the town where William Tully is stationed. A decorated veteran—he wears the ribbon of the Distinguished Conduct Medal—Wright is introduced as a figure profoundly broken by his war experiences. His terror during the bombing is so palpable and uncontrollable that it becomes a test of William Tully's own courage and self-mastery. Wright's character serves as a narrative embodiment of the psychological cost of modern warfare: a man who once had nerve and was outwardly sound, reduced by repeated trauma to a state of helpless, infectious panic.

## Role in the Novel

Wright functions in the novel primarily as a **foil to William Tully** during the climactic air raid sequence. Where William struggles—and ultimately succeeds—to master his fear and "behave decently," Wright has been destroyed by the cumulative weight of his experiences at Dunkirk and in hospital. His inability to control his terror externalises the psychological stakes of the scene and gives William something concrete to resist: William is "ashamed of the weakness with which Wright had infected him and keeping it doggedly at bay" [018-023].

Wright also serves a thematic function, illustrating the novel's broader argument about the dehumanising effects of industrialised warfare. His trajectory—from decorated soldier to "terror-haunted" wreck—mirrors the novel's critique of war as a force that unmakes men rather than ennobles them. His survival of the bombing, physically uninjured but psychologically shattered, underscores the distinction between bodily and mental wounds that the novel insistently draws.

## Key Relationships

- **[[william-tully|William Tully]]** — Wright clings to William during the first phase of the air raid, his clawing fingers and ceaseless terrified whispering testing William's composure. William initially tries to endure it, then wrenches free, recognising the infectious nature of Wright's fear [018-018]. The relationship is one of involuntary dependence on Wright's side and defensive resistance on William's.
- **[[edith-haynes|Edith Haynes]]** — Not directly connected to Wright, but William Tully's final request—that someone write to Edith Haynes—forms the closing passage of the chapter in which Wright appears, linking the air raid's survivors and casualties to the novel's broader web of relationships.

## Key Passages

> [018-016] "It's me—Wright," a voice whispered back in jerks. "I can't help it—the Lord knows I try, but I can't. If it was shells I could stand 'em, but—"

Wright's introduction, establishing his self-awareness of his condition and his inability to control it.

> [018-018] When Wright stopped talking—which was not often—William could hear his breath as it came whining through his teeth; and he remembered that the man wore the ribbon of the D.C.M.—a man who had once had nerve and to all appearance was sound, but who had not sufficient hold on himself to keep his terror from his tongue.

The juxtaposition of the D.C.M. ribbon with Wright's present state encapsulates the character's tragedy: a proven soldier whose courage has been consumed by repeated trauma.

> [018-020] "Listen," said a voice in his ear. It was Wright, his face uplifted in the moonlight and disfigured by ugly twitchings. "Listen," he said, "they're coming back. …"

Even in his terror, Wright's senses remain sharp—he is the first to detect the returning Gotha bombers, a detail that deepens the pathos of his condition.

> [018-032] There was only one man wholly uninjured—the terror-haunted Wright, who ran out, splashed with other men's blood, took screaming to his heels and collapsed a mile along the road.

Wright's final appearance: physically whole but psychologically annihilated, his flight and collapse the last image of a man whose war has ended not in death but in the destruction of his mind.
