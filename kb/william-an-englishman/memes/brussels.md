## Overview

**Brussels** in *William—An Englishman* is not a destination but a waypoint — a place the Tullys pass through during their flight from the advancing German army. Hamilton renders the city in a state of suspended crisis: not yet occupied but no longer at peace, filled with refugees and soldiers, its normal life already collapsed. Brussels represents the threshold between the pre-war world William has lost and the war-torn Europe he must now navigate.

## In the Novel

### The Flight Through Brussels

William and Griselda reach Brussels after days of walking southward through the Belgian countryside, separated, reunited, and separated again by the chaos of the German advance. The city they encounter is unrecognisable — "the streets were filled with people who had nowhere to go" — and their passage through it marks a critical stage in their physical and psychological degradation (006-034).

The Brussels sequence is notable for what Hamilton does not describe. There are no tourist landmarks, no evocations of the Grand Place or the Manneken Pis. Instead, the city appears as a texture of streets, doorways, and railway stations — the infrastructure of transit, not the architecture of tourism. This is deliberate: William and Griselda are not sightseers but refugees, and their experience of Brussels is defined by the practical urgencies of flight: where to sleep, where to find food, how to reach the next station.

### Rue Ernest Dupont and the British Consul

The novel anchors its Brussels geography in specific, named locations: the Rue Ernest Dupont, where Griselda seeks help, and the British Consulate, where the couple expect protection from their nationality. These locations function differently in the novel than they would in a guidebook. The Rue Ernest Dupont is not a picturesque Brussels street but a site of danger and uncertainty; the British Consulate is not an outpost of imperial order but a place where the limits of national protection become visible.

### The Gare du Nord

The final Brussels location in the novel is the Gare du Nord — the railway station from which William and Griselda hope to escape southward. The station episode dramatises the novel's central concern with the relationship between individual agency and historical force. The Tullys do not choose to leave Brussels; they are swept out of it, along with hundreds of others, by forces they cannot control or even fully understand.

## Symbolic Significance

Brussels functions as a symbolic threshold in the novel's geography. It is neither the pre-war idyll of the Belgian Ardennes nor the devastated war zone to the south; it is the point of transition, where the old world is visibly crumbling but the new world has not yet taken shape. Hamilton's Brussels is a city in which everything — buildings, institutions, social relations — appears provisional, subject to imminent erasure.

This provisionality is the novel's central insight about war. War does not simply destroy; it renders provisional what had seemed permanent — nations, marriages, selves. Brussels, the capital of a nation that will be occupied and brutalised, embodies this insight in geographical form.

## Key Passages

> [006-034] The British Consulate was in the Rue Ernest Dupont, a street they found only after asking directions from half a dozen passers-by, each of whom seemed more bewildered than the last.

The detail of "half a dozen passers-by" struggling to give directions captures the disorientation of a city in crisis. The Brussels that William and Griselda encounter is not a fixed location but a shifting, illegible space in which even residents have lost their bearings.

> [006-036] The Consul's office was thronged with English people, all of them demanding papers and passage and protection, and the Consul — a tired man with a tired voice — could only tell them to wait.

The exhaustion of the British Consul — "a tired man with a tired voice" — encapsulates the collapse of institutional authority in wartime. The Consulate, which should represent the power of the British Empire, is reduced to a room full of frightened people being told to wait.

> [006-041] At the Gare du Nord, the platforms were so crowded that they could barely move, and the train, when it finally came, was already overflowing with passengers who had boarded at earlier stations.

The train's arrival "already overflowing" is a perfect image of the refugee experience: by the time you reach safety, it is already occupied by those who got there first. William and Griselda are always late, always behind the curve of events — a structural position that Hamilton uses to dramatise the helplessness of civilians in wartime.
