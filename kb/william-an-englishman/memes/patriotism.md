# Patriotism

## Overview

Patriotism in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is not a static virtue but a discovery — something [[william-tully|William Tully]] stumbles into rather than chooses. Hamilton presents patriotism as an awakening of national consciousness that occurs through trauma and [[grief|loss]] rather than through intellectual argument or propaganda. The novel's treatment of this theme is characteristically ambivalent: patriotism is neither the jingoistic chest-thumping that the pre-war [[militarism|anti-militarist]]s feared, nor the sacred duty that wartime propagandists celebrated. It is, instead, a painful recognition of belonging — the discovery that one's identity is bound up with a nation one had never consciously chosen to love.

What distinguishes Hamilton's treatment of patriotism is its psychological realism. [[william-tully|William]] does not become patriotic because he reads patriotic literature or hears patriotic speeches; the "appeal to the nation's patriotism was driven home by the force of events" (014-006) — specifically, by the destruction of Griselda and everything she represented. Patriotism arises from grief, not from ideology. This inversion of the conventional patriotic narrative — in which love of country is celebrated as a virtue — makes the novel's treatment of the theme both more honest and more troubling than most wartime fiction.

## In the Novel

[[william-tully|William]]'s relationship with patriotism evolves through the novel's wartime half. Initially, he is defined by what he lacks: "What he lacked in patriotism he made up in personal suffering" (014-005). This formulation is crucial — patriotism is not something [[william-tully|William]] possesses but something absent, a void that suffering temporarily fills. Hamilton suggests that [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war cosmopolitanism was a form of emotional poverty, a failure to attach himself to anything larger than his personal life.

The turning point comes through the novel's treatment of [[hatred|enemy hatred]]. [[william-tully|William]]'s patriotism is forged not through positive love of [[england|England]] but through negative reaction to German aggression. He is "gripped with a sudden hatred of [[jay-blenkinsop|Jay-Blenkinsop]]" (015-016), a hatred that, however misdirected, represents the birth of his capacity for national feeling. The novel suggests that patriotism in wartime is inseparable from hatred — that one cannot fully love one's country without hating its enemies.

The novel's final word on patriotism is characteristically ambivalent. [[william-tully|William]] reflects that he "didn't see how it could have been patriotism; [[england|England]] hadn't been anything to him" (017-028). Even after his conversion to militarism, [[william-tully|William]] cannot fully articulate his relationship to nation. Hamilton refuses the easy resolution of patriotic epiphany, instead leaving [[william-tully|William]] — and the reader — with the unresolved question of what, exactly, he has been fighting for.

## Historical Context

The concept of patriotism underwent profound transformation during WWI. Pre-war patriotism in Britain was associated with imperial pageantry, naval reviews, and the "jingoism" of popular music-hall songs. The war transformed patriotism into something more somber: a duty of sacrifice rather than a celebration of power. The mass enlistment of 1914–1915 was driven by complex motivations — genuine patriotic feeling, social pressure, economic necessity, and the desire for adventure — that defied simple categorization.

By the time Hamilton published her novel in 1919, the patriotic narrative had been severely damaged by the realities of trench [[war|warfare]]. [[war|War]] poets like Wilfred Owen and Siegfried Sassoon had exposed the gap between patriotic rhetoric and soldiers' experience. Hamilton's novel participates in this post-war reckoning, but its approach is distinctive: rather than simply debunking patriotism, it explores how patriotism can arise from the ashes of destroyed illusions. [[william-tully|William]]'s patriotism is real even if its origins are tragic — a position that sets the novel apart from both jingoistic and [[pacifism|pacifist]] orthodoxies.

## Key Passages

> …What he lacked in patriotism he made up in personal suffering… (014-005)

This assessment of [[william-tully|William]]'s psychological state captures the novel's unsentimental approach to patriotism. Personal suffering is a temporary substitute for national feeling — it fills the void but does not resolve it. The implication is that genuine patriotism requires something more than grief: it requires a positive attachment that [[william-tully|William]] has not yet achieved.

> …vigorous appeal to the nation's patriotism was driven home by the force of events… (014-006)

The passive construction here is significant. Patriotism is not rationally chosen but "driven home" by events — the invasion of [[belgium|Belgium]], the destruction of Griselda, the collapse of [[william-tully|William]]'s pre-war world. Hamilton presents patriotism as something that happens to [[william-tully|William]] rather than something he does, a distinction that preserves his moral innocence even as he embraces military values.

> …didn't see how it could have been patriotism; England hadn't been anything to him… (017-028)

This late passage is the novel's most explicit meditation on the nature of patriotism. [[william-tully|William]]'s confession — that [[england|England]] had "been nothing" to him before the war — is both honest and damning. It suggests that patriotism, for [[william-tully|William]], is not about loving a country he knows but about discovering a country he had never noticed. The novel leaves this paradox unresolved.
