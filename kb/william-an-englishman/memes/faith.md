# Faith

## Overview

Faith in *[[william-an-englishman-novel|William—An Englishman]]* is not primarily religious but political and personal — the "absolute faith" (002-006) that the [[progress|progressive movement]] demands of its adherents. Hamilton traces [[william-tully|William Tully]]'s relationship with faith from uncritical acceptance to devastating [[grief|loss]], showing how his "faith in his prewar creed" (018-002) dissolves under the pressure of experience. The novel's treatment of faith is unusual for its psychological precision: faith is not merely abandoned but destroyed, leaving [[william-tully|William]] in "the confusion of the man whose faith has been uprooted" (008-001), a state of spiritual and intellectual disorientation from which he must reconstruct a new orientation to the world.

The novel distinguishes between different objects of faith — faith in ideas, faith in persons, faith in causes — and tracks their common vulnerability to experience. [[william-tully|William]]'s faith in [[faraday|Faraday]]'s [[progress|progressive ideology]], his faith in Griselda, and his faith in the cause of social [[progress|reform]] are all casualties of the war. What replaces them is not a new faith but a capacity for action without faith — a pragmatic engagement with reality that the novel presents as simultaneously more honest and more tragic than the certainties it replaces.

## In the Novel

Faith appears first as a marker of [[william-tully|William]]'s political identity. He is described as having "faith in his newfound friend" (001-042) — the personal and political intertwined, as faith in [[faraday|Faraday]] merges with faith in [[faraday|Faraday]]'s ideas. The novel notes that this faith "had in all probability more" — the incomplete sentence suggesting that faith exceeds what can be rationally justified, that it is always in some sense excessive.

The [[progress|progressive movement]]'s culture is built on faith: its adherents speak "imposingly, with absolute faith in their statements" (002-006). "Absolute faith" is the progressive's emotional armor — it protects against doubt, against evidence, against the complexity of reality. Hamilton's adjective "imposingly" carries a note of critique: this faith is performed as much as felt, a social display rather than an inner conviction.

The novel's pivotal treatment of faith comes with its destruction: [[william-tully|William]] experiences "the confusion of the man whose faith has been uprooted" (008-001). The metaphor of uprooting suggests violence — faith is not gently relinquished but torn out, leaving exposed roots and raw earth. This is the psychological condition from which [[william-tully|William]]'s subsequent transformation proceeds.

The novel's final word on faith records its permanent [[grief|loss]]: "his faith in his prewar creed" has "died as completely" (018-002) as other aspects of his former self. The word "died" is absolute — faith is not dormant or transformed but dead, and [[william-tully|William]] must live without it. This is the novel's most radical psychological claim: that some [[grief|loss]]es are permanent, and that maturity consists not in finding new faiths but in learning to live without faith altogether.

## Historical Context

The crisis of faith in Edwardian Britain extended beyond religion to encompass political and social certainties. The Victorian "Age of Faith" — whether Christian faith or faith in progress — gave way to the "Age of Doubt" of the early twentieth century. Darwin's theory of evolution had already shaken biblical literalism; the Boer [[war|War]] and the emerging threat of [[germany|Germany]] shook faith in British superiority; the suffrage movement and labor unrest shook faith in the established social order.

WWI delivered the decisive blow to progressive faith. The belief that rational discussion, international cooperation, and gradual [[progress|reform]] could prevent war was exposed as naive by the events of August 1914. Hamilton's novel, published in 1919, records this collapse of faith with unflinching honesty. [[william-tully|William]]'s lost "faith in his prewar creed" is not a personal failing but a generational experience — the common fate of those who had believed, before the war, that progress was inevitable and reason would prevail.

## Key Passages

> …faith in his newfound friend and faith had in all probability more… (001-042)

The doubling of "faith" — faith in person and faith in idea — captures the fusion that characterizes [[william-tully|William]]'s early political development. [[faraday|Faraday]] is both a friend and an ideology, and [[william-tully|William]]'s faith in one reinforces his faith in the other. The incomplete sentence leaves open what faith "had more" of — intensity? duration? consequence? — inviting the reader to complete the thought.

> …imposingly, with absolute faith in their statements… (002-006)

The adverb "imposingly" reveals the performative dimension of progressive faith. This is faith as social display, a way of asserting authority rather than expressing conviction. "Absolute faith" admits no doubt, no qualification, no nuance — it is faith as fortress, protecting its adherents from the complexity of reality.

> …the confusion of the man whose faith has been uprooted… (008-001)

"Uprooted" is the novel's most powerful metaphor for the destruction of faith. It suggests violence (faith is torn out rather than relinquished), permanence (what is uprooted cannot be replanted), and exposure (the roots that were hidden are now visible). [[william-tully|William]]'s confusion is the necessary consequence of having the ground of his beliefs torn away.

> …his faith in his prewar creed had died as completely as… (018-002)

The verb "died" and the adverb "completely" leave no room for hope of recovery. Faith is not sleeping or dormant — it is dead. The "prewar creed" — the entire system of beliefs and values that sustained [[william-tully|William]] before the war — is irrecoverable. What remains is the task of living without it.
