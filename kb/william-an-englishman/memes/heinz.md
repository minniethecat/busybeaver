# Heinz

## Overview

Heinz is one of the novel's most striking minor characters — a German soldier who speaks English because he once worked at a hairdresser's shop on Harrow Road in London. His appearance in the Belgian chapters introduces a note of human particularity into the abstraction of "the enemy," complicating the moral framework that the war demands. Heinz is not a monster or a cipher; he is a man with a specific history, a former London resident, someone who can talk to William in his own language.

Hamilton's treatment of Heinz is characteristic of the novel's psychological realism. He is neither demonized nor sentimentalized — he is a soldier doing his duty, and his brief interaction with William and Griselda reveals the human reality behind the category of "enemy." His threat to William is real, but so is his humanity. The novel refuses to resolve this contradiction, allowing Heinz to exist as both threat and fellow human, a figure who embodies the moral complexity that war simplifies.

## Role in the Novel

Heinz functions as a counterweight to the novel's construction of the enemy. His ability to speak English — the result of his time at a Harrow Road hairdresser — makes communication possible across the lines of nationality and military allegiance. The specificity of "Harrow Road" grounds Heinz in a real London geography, making him not merely "a German" but a man who has lived in William's city, walked William's streets, cut the hair of William's countrymen.

The memory of Heinz's threats lingers in William's mind (008-003), suggesting that the encounter has lasting psychological consequences. Heinz is not forgotten or dismissed; he persists in memory as a troubling presence, a reminder that the enemy is human. This persistence is the novel's argument against the simplifications of wartime propaganda — the recognition that hatred is complicated by knowledge, that the enemy is harder to hate when he speaks your language.

## Key Passages

> "English," he said in a voice that was thicker than it should have been at so early an hour of the morning; "English—you come from London? … I have been two years in London; that is why I speak English. I was with a hairdresser in the Harrow Road two years; and I know also the Strand and the Angel and Buckingham Palace and the Elephant." (He was plainly proud of his acquaintance with London topography.) "All of them I know, and when we arrive in London I shall show them all to my friends." (006-024)

Heinz's first words establish him as more than a generic enemy soldier. The Harrow Road hairdresser is a brilliantly specific biographical detail — it makes Heinz a man who has cut the hair of William's countrymen and walked William's streets, collapsing the distance that wartime categories depend upon.

> There was something so impossible about the announcement that William and Griselda felt their courage return with a rush. Moreover, though the words of Heinz were threatening the aspect of Heinz was not; his fat young face with its expansive and slightly inebriated smile was ridiculous rather than terrifying, even under the brim of a helmet. (006-027)

Hamilton insists on the physical particularity of Heinz — his "fat young face," his "little pig's-eyes," his inebriated smile — in a way that makes him both more human and more disconcerting. The threat is real, but the man delivering it is not a villain from propaganda; he is a slightly drunk young soldier enjoying his moment of power.

> "I tell you something, you damn little ignorant silly fools. There is a war since you came to Belgium." (006-043)

Heinz is the character who delivers the news of the European catastrophe to William and Griselda. The war enters the novel not through a newspaper headline or an official proclamation but through the thick, jeering voice of a former hairdresser — a narrative choice that strips the historical event of grandeur and renders it as a brutal, personal shock.

> After what he had seen, and with the memory of Heinz's threats, William Tully believed most firmly that he too was about to die; and with the conviction there filled his heart (as it would have filled the heart of any honest lover) a great and intolerable pity for Griselda, his new-made wife. (008-003)

Heinz reappears after the execution of the hostages, his earlier threats now charged with deadly credibility. The memory of Heinz is no longer merely unsettling — it has become the medium through which William confronts his own mortality, transforming the comical hairdresser-soldier into an agent of terror.
