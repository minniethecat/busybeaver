## Overview

**Internationalism** in *William—An Englishman* is the pre-war conviction, shared by [[william-tully|William Tully]] and his mentor [[faraday|Faraday]], that the solidarity of the European working classes made international warfare impossible. It is a form of political faith — not rooted in knowledge of European politics, but in the closed-circuit reasoning of radical pamphlets and meetings. The novel treats it with sustained irony: Hamilton shows that the internationalists' ignorance of the world they claimed to understand was as profound as their certainty was absolute.

The collapse of internationalism in August 1914 is one of the novel's central historical dramas. William, Faraday, and their circle had built their political identities on the premise that class solidarity transcended nationality; when that premise proved false, they were left with no framework for understanding the world they inhabited.

## In the Novel

Internationalism appears as a default position of the novel's radical characters before August 1914. William, in his discussions with [[griselda|Griselda]], "enlarged on his favourite text, the impossibility of international warfare, owing to the ever-growing solidarity of the European working-classes" [004-032]. This conviction requires no evidence; it is a creed, recited like a prayer.

Hamilton exposes the shallowness of this belief through the figure of Faraday, whose "ignorance of European politics was a thought less profound than William's, but sufficiently profound to have bred in him a complete disbelief in the possibility of European war" [015-003]. When war breaks out, Faraday's reaction is not self-examination but anger — "all men are angry when the habit of years is interfered with; and in the first flush of his annoyance [he] ascribed the falsification of his every prediction not to his own blundering, but to the sins of those who did not think as he did" [015-003].

The novel's deepest irony about internationalism is that its adherents are, in practice, as aggressive as the militarists they condemn. William and Griselda, "apostles of the reign of Woman and of International Amity," are described as "the perfect type of aggressor" [002-005] — their combativeness merely finds domestic rather than foreign targets. Hamilton's point is not that internationalism is wrong but that the psychology of conviction is identical regardless of its object: the pacifist and the militarist share a temperament.

By the end of the novel, internationalism has become a hollow word. During a Labour meeting, a speaker "quacked earnestly about branch propaganda as an antidote to militarism and a means of diffusing the international idea," and William experiences "the sense of unreality" — "branch propaganda — little leaflets and meetings — when guns made a pulp of flesh and blood, and men were being shot against walls!" [015-014]. The juxtaposition is devastating: the language of pre-war radicalism cannot encompass the reality of industrialised warfare.

## Thematic Significance

Internationalism in the novel belongs to a family of pre-war beliefs — alongside [[pacifism]], [[social-reform]], and faith in [[democracy]] — that Hamilton presents as sincere but fatally abstract. Each of these creeds mistakes its own aspirations for descriptions of reality, and each is shattered by contact with the war. The specific tragedy of internationalism is that it was, in principle, the correct answer to the problem of war; its failure was not intellectual but practical — it had no mechanism for surviving the passions that war unleashed.

## Historical Context

Internationalism before 1914 was a genuine current in European socialist and labour movements. The Second International (1889–1916) brought together socialist parties from across Europe on a platform of anti-war solidarity. The outbreak of the First World War in August 1914 shattered this internationalism: most national socialist parties supported their own governments' war efforts, and the International effectively collapsed.

Hamilton wrote *William—An Englishman* in 1919, after this collapse was complete. Her treatment of internationalism reflects the disillusionment of radicals who had believed — as William and Faraday did — that class solidarity would transcend national enmity. The novel's unsparing portrait of this belief as a form of ignorance rather than wisdom is characteristic of its post-war perspective.
