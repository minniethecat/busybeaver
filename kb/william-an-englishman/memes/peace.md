## Overview

Peace in *William—An Englishman* functions as both a sincerely held political ideal and a site of dramatic irony. The novel's protagonists, William and Griselda, are committed pacifists and internationalists who believe — as their creed taught them — "that Democracy is another word for peace and goodwill towards men" [002-002]. Their honeymoon in Belgium unfolds in a landscape of "deep and solitary peace," even as the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand sets in motion the war that will destroy their world.

Cicely Hamilton uses the concept of peace to explore the gap between ideological conviction and historical reality. The peaceful world William and Griselda inhabit is not merely geographical — the secluded Belgian valley — but ideological: a progressive, rationalist worldview that assumes history moves inexorably toward international cooperation. The war exposes this assumption as fragile wish-fulfillment. What makes Hamilton's treatment distinctive is her refusal to simply mock this faith; she understands its moral appeal even as she documents its inadequacy.

The novel's treatment of peace is inseparable from its treatment of place. The Belgian valley where the couple honeymoons is not just a setting but an argument: a demonstration that peace is always local, always embodied in particular landscapes and particular relationships, before it is ever a political program.

## In the Novel

William and Griselda's pacifism is presented with gentle authorial irony. Griselda "was a pacifist — when it suited her and when she had got her way" [002-004], and her commitment to international amity coexists comfortably with her exultation in domestic victories. Their shared creed is "simple" and held "loyally and faithfully," a kind of secular faith that provides moral certainty without demanding rigorous self-examination.

The novel's most poignant treatment of peace comes through its physical settings. The Belgian valley where the couple honeymoons is described as "a cool green vision of summer and solitary peace" [003-008] — a landscape seemingly untouched by human conflict. Griselda reflects that "in her busy and crowded little mind, the reflection of her busy little life, there had been no room for visions of a deep and solitary peace" [003-010] until this moment. The valley becomes, in retrospect, the last space of innocence before the war.

As the peaceful days of the honeymoon pass — "in time, as the peaceful days crept by" [004-004] — the reader's knowledge of impending war creates a mounting sense of dread. The peace William and Griselda experience is real but temporary, and the novel measures their subsequent suffering against this baseline of tranquility. Hamilton's technique here is masterful: she lets historical fact (which every reader of 1919 knows) do the work of irony, never overplaying her hand.

## The Limits of Peaceful Ideology

Hamilton is careful to show that the pacifism of William's circle is not merely naive but structurally limited. Faraday, the political mentor, is a "pacifist of the type which seeks peace and ensues it by insisting first of all on a change of heart in the enemy" [002-003] — a position that assumes goodwill on all sides while ignoring the structural pressures toward conflict. The novel implies that peace conceived solely as a moral preference, without an understanding of power, is a peace that exists only in the minds of those who hold it.

This critique extends to the broader progressive movement. Before the war, "like William, she had found peace of mind and perennial interest" [001-047] in the suffrage struggle — a "peace" that was really absorption in a political cause that made sense of the world. The war strips away this psychological peace by confronting its adherents with realities their ideology cannot explain. The peace of the mind, Hamilton suggests, is always vulnerable to the violence of the world.

## The Peace That Never Was

A subtler thread in the novel concerns the retrospective construction of "peace" as a lost golden age. The pre-war world, looked back on from the trenches, acquires a nostalgic glow that it did not possess in actual experience. The Edwardian period was not peaceful — it was riven by class conflict, the suffrage struggle, labour unrest, and the naval arms race — but the war's unprecedented horror made it seem so in retrospect.

Hamilton, writing in 1919, is aware of this dynamic and subtly resists it. The pre-war chapters of the novel do not depict a golden age; they depict a society of comfortable illusions, class privilege, and political naivety. The "peace" that was lost was not a state of blessed tranquility but a state of untested assumptions. What the war destroyed was not peace itself but the belief that progress was inevitable and that rational people could settle their differences by rational means.
