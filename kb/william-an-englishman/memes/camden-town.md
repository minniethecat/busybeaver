## Overview

Camden Town is a district in north London. In *William—An Englishman*, it is where William Tully retreats after abandoning the Bloomsbury flat he had shared with Griselda. His Camden Town lodgings—two small rooms—become the setting for the loneliest period of his life: weeks of grief-stricken isolation, obsessive newspaper reading, and the beginnings of his self-taught study of military history that keeps his mind alive through the void left by his wife's death.

## In the Novel

### A Refuge After Loss

Within a week or two of his return to London after Griselda's death, William gives up the lease on the Bloomsbury flat. The place is dreadful to him, "with its empty demand for Griselda," and he leaves it thankfully for a lodging in Camden Town. There, for some weeks, he lives drearily in two small rooms, with no occupation to fill the void in his life. He passes "hermit days" in the company of newspapers and cheap war literature, arousing the sympathy of his elderly landlady by his helplessness and continual loneliness.

### The Birth of a Scholar

What keeps William alive mentally is his "thirsty interest in the war." The pamphlet and journal soon cease to satisfy him, and he begins borrowing books from the nearest Free Library—first a volume of modern French history, then others, until the local librarian comes to know him as a regular client. His reading gravitates toward the drama and movement of the French Revolutionary and Napoleonic epoch, and he begins to imagine himself as a soldier, "while queer little ambitions flamed up in his barren heart."

It is from his Camden Town lodgings that William corresponds with Edith Haynes, his only real human contact during this period. And it is there, after nearly two months, that he runs into Watson, an old committee-member from his socialist branch, now in the uniform of the London Scottish—a meeting that reconnects him to the world of men who, like himself, have reconsidered their pre-war views.

### The Breakdown

William's sedentary life in Camden Town—"between the four walls of his Camden Town sitting-room"—leaves him physically unprepared for the rigours of military training. The violent transition from a life spent reading and brooding indoors to the unceasing exertion of army life eventually brings on the breakdown that had been foreseen by the first doctor who refused him.

## Symbolic Significance

Camden Town represents the liminal space between William's two selves: the pre-war progressive idealist and the aspiring soldier. In the confinement of his two small rooms, surrounded by newspapers and borrowed library books, William undergoes a metamorphosis driven by grief and the desperate need for purpose. The district's very ordinariness—a lodging-house in an unremarkable north London neighbourhood—heightens the sense of a man shrinking from the world even as he reads obsessively about its great military dramas. The "four walls" of the sitting-room become both prison and cocoon.
