# Alfred, Lord Tennyson

Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1809–1892) appears in *Armed with Madness* in a single exchange that condenses the novel's relationship to the nineteenth-century literary tradition. "'Tennyson,' said Felix" (006-011). The name, offered without context during the Freud game, is a kind of free-associational provocation — and Felix immediately qualifies it: "I said Tennyson because I hate Tennyson" (006-019). The hatred is the point: Tennyson is the literary father who must be rejected for the modernist sensibility to constitute itself.

## Function in the Novel

### The Rejected Father

Felix's "I hate Tennyson" (006-019) is less a literary judgment than a ritual utterance — the modernist's declaration of independence from the Victorian. Tennyson stands here for everything the Gault House circle is not: Victorian certainty, imperial confidence, the unironized poetic "I." The hatred is performative, even affectionate — one does not hate what one has not loved.

### What Carston Looks For

"He looked for something, not Tennyson, to enlighten him. He found an old translation of the 'Pervigilium Veneris'" (006-070). Carston's turn from Tennyson to the late-Latin Vigil of Venus is the novel's own literary-critical argument: the modernist quest for enlightenment leads backward past the Victorians to older, stranger sources. Tennyson is the way station that must be acknowledged and passed through.

### The Poetic Register

Tennyson's presence in the novel, however brief, marks Butts's awareness of the poetic tradition within which she writes. The Grail quest — Tennyson's great subject in the *Idylls of the King* — is here rewritten as a modernist psychodrama. Felix's "I hate Tennyson" is the novel's acknowledgment that it cannot escape the comparison.

> "Tennyson," said Felix. "I said Tennyson because I hate Tennyson." (006-011, 006-019)

## Relevance to Armed with Madness

Tennyson's *Idylls of the King* (1859–1885) is the Victorian era's definitive Grail narrative — a moralized, Christianized retelling of the Arthurian legends that Butts's novel systematically undoes. Where Tennyson's Grail is the cup of the Last Supper, a symbol of achieved sanctity, Butts's Grail is a "mass-substitute" (030-009), an object whose sacredness is inseparable from its ambiguity. Felix's hatred of Tennyson is the novel's aesthetic manifesto: the Grail must be rescued from Victorian piety and returned to the dangerous, unresolved myth it was before Tennyson tamed it.
