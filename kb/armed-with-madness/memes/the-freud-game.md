# The Freud Game

"The Freud game really. Start, Felix!" (006-010). With this prompt, the Gault House circle launches into its signature activity — a parlor game built on Freudian free association, where characters reveal themselves through what they claim is only play. The game is the novel's most sustained set-piece of group psychology, a scene in which the unconscious minds the characters claim to be investigating are precisely what the game exposes.

## Prelude

The game is proposed during an evening at Gault House. The doctor has come from Starn to attend to Picus, and the household's tension — erotic, intellectual, mythic — needs an outlet. "Good old Freud" (003-048): the invocation of Freud's name earlier in the novel establishes the intellectual framework the game will deploy and parody.

## The Event

### The Rules

The game's rules are implicit: one player offers a word or phrase, and the others free-associate. The game is at once diagnostic (what you associate reveals your unconscious) and competitive (who can make the most revealing or outrageous connection).

### Felix's Provocation

"'Tennyson,' said Felix" (006-011). Felix's opening move — naming the Victorian poet — is a provocation disguised as an association. "I said Tennyson because I hate Tennyson" (006-019): the hatred is the revelation, a declaration of modernist allegiance disguised as a psychological symptom.

### The Mythic Turn

The game, pursued through its associations, produces "the Woodpecker who was Zeus" (006-024, 006-034, 006-044). The movement from Freud to Greek myth is the novel's argument in miniature: psychoanalysis, played honestly, leads beyond itself to the mythic material it was supposed to replace.

> "The Freud game really. Start, Felix!" (006-010)

## Aftermath

- **The Woodpecker-Zeus refrain**: The game's most important product is the phrase "Here lies the Woodpecker who was Zeus" — Picus's mythological identification, which echoes through the novel.
- **Carston's alternative**: Carston looks past Freud toward an "old translation of the 'Pervigilium Veneris'" (006-070), preferring late-Latin erotic poetry to Viennese psychoanalysis.
- **Between Freud and Aquinas**: "Between Freud and Aquinas, I've managed to make myself pretty uncomfortable" (006-039). The game does not resolve; it discomfits.

> "Now are his eyes shifting memory or madness?" (033-083)

## Symbolic Reading

The Freud game is the novel's modernist Eucharist — a ritual of self-disclosure performed in a Dorset country house, substituting psychoanalytic terminology for Latin liturgy. Like the Grail quest it parodies, the game promises revelation and delivers ambiguity.
