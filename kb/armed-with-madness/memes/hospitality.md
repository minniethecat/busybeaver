# Hospitality

Hospitality structures the social world of *Armed with Madness*. Gault House, inherited by Scylla and Felix Taverner, operates as an open house for a shifting cast of guests — artists, intellectuals, drifters, and friends. The giving and receiving of hospitality creates bonds, but also debts, resentments, and power asymmetries that drive much of the novel's interpersonal drama.

## Overview

The theme of hospitality is concentrated in the early chapters set at Gault House, with six direct references spanning at least three chapters. The Taverners' hospitality is described as an "instinct" rather than a calculated choice — they have "no money and the instincts of hospitality; wanted everything and nothing" (001-006). This paradox — wanting everything and nothing — captures the double nature of hospitality in the novel: it is generous and open, yet also a form of power and a source of unspoken obligations. The guests' responses range from gratitude to resentment to rebellion, and the dynamics of host and guest become a lens through which class, gender, and national identity are examined.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Hospitality is introduced in the novel's opening pages as a defining trait of the Taverner household: "no money and the instincts of hospitality; wanted everything and nothing" (001-006). This establishes the economic precariousness and emotional complexity of the house-party arrangement.

### Key Scenes

- **Scylla's Role**: Scylla is described as bearing a particular responsibility — the others "had forgotten the duties of hospitality and had left it to Scylla" (005-015), revealing how hospitality labor is gendered and unequally distributed.
- **Carston's Rebellion**: The most charged confrontation over hospitality occurs when Carston declares, "I'm sick of your hospitality. Especially when it includes..." (012-035), openly challenging the obligations that the gift economy imposes.
- **Felix's Defense**: Felix responds to Carston's challenge with an ironic fragment — "'Our hospitality,' said Felix. Quotation waste..." (012-036) — deflecting the critique through wit while acknowledging its force.

> "If that's your notion of hospitality, it doesn't coincide with mine." (012-007)

### Additional PN References

- "That was her part of their hospitality, whose rewards were varied and irregular" (001-020)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Hospitality in *Armed with Madness* is a form of gift economy — a system of exchange in which what is given (food, shelter, company, sexual access) creates diffuse obligations that can never be precisely settled. This ambiguity is both the strength and the weakness of the arrangement: it generates community and intimacy, but also breeds resentment when guests feel trapped by obligations they did not choose. Carston's rebellion in Chapter XII is the crisis point of this system — his declaration of being "sick of your hospitality" names what others may feel but dare not say. The novel does not resolve this tension; instead, it shows how the house-party disperses, the hospitality economy dissolving under its own weight.

The hospitality theme also illuminates class dynamics. The Taverners, as landowners (however impoverished), occupy the traditional role of hosts by right of property. But their guests include those with more money, more cosmopolitan experience, or more artistic success — creating a mismatch between the traditional hierarchy of host/guest and the actual distribution of power and status among the characters.

### Mythic / Symbolic Dimension

Hospitality has deep roots in classical and Celtic mythology — the host's obligation to the guest was sacred, and violation of guest-right brought divine punishment. The novel draws on this tradition while also subverting it: in the modern, post-war world, the sacred dimension of hospitality has eroded, leaving only the social and psychological mechanics. The gods to whom the well offering is made (001-009) might be understood as the true hosts of the place, with the Taverners as their stewards — a reading that recasts the entire house-party as a failure of sacred hospitality.
