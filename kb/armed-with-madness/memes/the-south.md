# The South

"The South" is the regional frame for the novel's Dorset geography — the part of England where the action unfolds. "What happened down South?" (025-018): the question, asked by Lydia in Paris, positions the South as elsewhere, a place where things happen that require explanation. The South is the novel's primary territory, the region whose landscape generates the Grail quest.

## Description

The South is defined by its difference from London and Paris. It is "down" — a descent from the metropolitan centers — and the spatial descent is also a temporal one. To go South is to go backward in time, toward an older England of heaths and cliffs and drowned sailors.

## Narrative Function

The South is the geographic container of the novel's myth. Its function is to localize the Grail quest — to insist that the sacred is not in Jerusalem or Monsalvat but in Dorset, "down South," in a country house overlooking the sea.
