# The Heath

The heath is the ground Gault stands on — the wild Dorset landscape of heather, sand, and gorse that stretches between the house and the sea, between the cultivated and the untamed. "Where the heath runs out in a point" (009-002): the heath is defined by its edges, by the places where it meets the water or the wood or the road. It is the novel's threshold landscape, the terrain characters must cross to reach either the mythic intensity of Gault or the ordinary world beyond.

## Description

Butts's heath is sensory before it is geographic. "A gauze of every variety of heather, the sweet blood-bright heather" (008-005): the heather is rendered through color and texture, a surface of variegated purple that the characters move through rather than look at. "The heather stalks whipped their ankles" (008-061): the landscape is physical, even hostile — it touches the characters, marks them.

The heath is also a social marker. "We're not heath-people" (008-056): the declaration separates the Gault House circle from the locals, from the older Dorset population that belongs to the heath in a way the cosmopolitan visitors never can.

> "We're not heath-people." (008-056)

## Narrative Function

### Events on the Heath

- **Tracy's domain**: Mr. Tracy lives on the heath — "an ancient of days" (026-003) whose house contains antiquities and possibly the Grail. The heath is the terrain of the novel's Merlin figure.
- **The doctor's crossing**: The doctor from Starn crosses the heath to reach Gault, moving from the ordinary world through the threshold landscape to the mythic center.

### Symbolic Meaning

The heath is the novel's wilderness — not the sublime wilderness of Romanticism but a domestic wilderness, a patch of uncultivated England that persists between the villages and the sea. Its symbolic function is to mark the boundary between the human and the inhuman, the known and the older-than-known. To be "heath-people" is to belong to that older order; not to be is to be a visitor in one's own country.
