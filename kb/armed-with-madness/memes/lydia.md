[027-001] She was alone next morning. [[philip|Philip]] had gone out to meet a Jew whose favour they were nursing. She had refused, felt she no longer cared if he mismanaged it. She had not spoken since an hour after [[scylla-taverner|Scylla]] had left, and in that hour they had said worse things to each other than they had said to her. But Philip, who had almost cried with [[fear]], in the morning was not dissatisfied. One does not leave the gutter without a few knocks. He had his own plans, his own adventure. Hoped from his heart Scylla was marrying the man. That would get them out of his way for good.

[027-002] [[lydia-character|Lydia]] sat at her writing-table, without her mask, either of [[love]] or makeup. Her head, still disfigured, did not belong to this age. She wrote:

[027-003] > My dear [[clarence-character|Clarence]],

[027-004] > How are you all?

[027-005] > Scylla is up and dined last night. She seemed very well and a little mysterious. I understood, though I may have got it quite wrong, that you’re thinking of marrying each other.

[027-006] > Please let Phil and me know if it’s true. It almost hurts one’s feelings not to be the first to wish you luck.

[027-007] > No chance of getting away until Phil has pulled off some more business, and then he wants to go to Eastbourne!

[027-008] She went out herself and posted it to [[tollerdown|Tollerdown]].

[027-009] Once, [[the-south|down South]], one of the boys had called Scylla “bird-alone.” They had all asked for names. [[picus-character|Picus]] had been cat-by-himself. [[felix-taverner|Felix]],*l’œuf sur le toit*. [[ross-character|Ross]], bird-catcher. They had quarrelled a little that morning, and she had not been pleased when Ross had said, grinning: “If Scylla’s the bird, one might call you ‘wolf-alone.’ ”
