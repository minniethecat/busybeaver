# Scylla Taverner

Scylla Taverner — called Scylla from her given name Drusilla, "altered after a classical goddess who had been a classical rock" (001-011) — is the gravitational center of *Armed with Madness*. Owner of [[Gault]] House, sister to [[Felix Taverner]], object of [[Carston]]'s pursuit and [[Picus]]'s destructive love, Scylla is at once the novel's most perceptive intelligence and its most vulnerable presence. The mythological name is not incidental: like her Homeric namesake, Scylla occupies a threshold — between land and sea, between the old ritual order and the new psychic disarray, between the various men who circle her and the solitude she increasingly claims as her own.

## Identity and Relationships

Born Drusilla, called Scylla "after a classical goddess who had been a classical rock" (001-011), the metamorphosis from Drusilla to Scylla — from a name meaning "strong" to one meaning "she who rends" — encodes her dual nature: strength and danger, solidity and destruction.

- **With Felix**: The novel's most stable dyad, a sibling intimacy that predates and survives all erotic entanglements. Felix sees Scylla's beauty when no one else can: "When you came in with Picus I saw your beauty... It was a kind of answer" (013-024).

- **With Picus**: Erotic fascination, intellectual recognition, and ultimate disappointment. Picus "was a man so tall and delicate" (003-046) but his deceptions destroy the possibility of genuine love between them.

- **With Carston**: The American visitor who pursues Scylla as a tourist pursues a landscape — with appreciation but without understanding. Scylla sees through him from the beginning: "Slightly overdoing the beauty business" (003-008).

- **With [[Ross]]**: Steady, unromantic friendship. Ross is "all right — He never wanted anything" (002-013) — a quality rare enough in the novel to constitute a moral distinction.

## Character and Thematic Function

Scylla functions as the novel's perceptual center. What she sees, and how she sees it, defines the novel's reality. Her consciousness is the filter through which the Grail legend, the Dorset landscape, and the group's intricate erotic dynamics are all refracted.

- **Perceptual acuity**: Scylla sees through surfaces to structures. Her early observation about [[Beauty]] — "Beauty is a too concentrated food" (003-008) — is not aestheticism but diagnosis. She understands that beauty in her world is dangerous because it is too intense to be safely consumed.

- **Emotional intelligence and its cost**: Scylla reads the people around her with unnerving precision, but this gift extracts a price. By the novel's midpoint, she is exhausted — "shabby weariness and fear" have replaced the "pleasure" of the opening chapters (013-001). Her lucidity makes her the novel's most reliable narrator and its most isolated character.

- **Mythic doubling**: As her name suggests, Scylla exists on two planes simultaneously. She is a modern woman managing a country house, doing the flowers, walking to the farm — and she is also the Grail bearer, the wounded Fisher Queen whose incapacity mirrors the wasteland. The novel never resolves these two identities; it insists that they are the same person.

## Narrative Arc

### Initial State: The House and Its Owner

Scylla first appears as the host of [[Gault]] House, receiving the American visitor [[Carston]] with "cool, rested, made up" composure (003-001). She "seemed to be the only woman in the house" (003-038) — a structural isolation that will become increasingly existential as the novel proceeds. Her initial conversations with Carston establish her characteristic mode: generous with perception, withholding with judgment, always slightly ahead of the conversation.

### Conflict and Transformation

The arrival of the [[the-cup|Cup]] catalyzes Scylla's transformation. She recognizes its power — and its danger — before any of the men. Her confrontation with [[Picus]] over the cup's authenticity is the novel's moral center: she demands truth from a man whose relationship to truth is purely instrumental. Carston's pursuit intensifies her withdrawal; Picus's betrayal confirms her isolation. The scene in the scullery with [[Felix Taverner|Felix]], gutting fish, is her moment of recovery — "I saw your beauty" (013-024) — beauty recognized by the one person who loves her without the desire to possess.

### Final State: The Threshold

In the novel's final chapters, Scylla is dying — or, more precisely, she is entering a state that the narrative describes as death-approaching but does not name. Picus's vigil by her bedside is the novel's most concentrated study of love's failure: "he loved her with a love that would no longer be answered" (033-014). Scylla herself faces [[Death]] with an equanimity that no other character achieves — "she had always said it would" come sweetly (033-015). This is her final distinction: she alone among the Gault House circle has made peace with the ultimate limit.

## Mythic and Symbolic Associations

Scylla's mythological name is the novel's most explicit symbolic gesture. The Homeric Scylla is a sea-monster who destroys sailors who venture too close — a figure of female danger, of the erotic as annihilation. Butts's Scylla reverses the valence: she is dangerous not because she destroys men but because she sees through them. Her danger is her clarity.

In the Grail framework, Scylla occupies the position of the Grail bearer — the figure who holds (or withholds) the sacred object whose presence or absence determines the health of the realm. The novel's wasteland is the world after the war; Scylla's "illness" in the final chapters is the wasteland internalized, made flesh. She cannot heal the wasteland because the [[the-cup|Grail]] — authentic or counterfeit — cannot heal what the war has broken.
