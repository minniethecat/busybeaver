# Pagan Revival

The pagan revival — a broad cultural movement of renewed interest in pre-Christian religions, mythologies, and sensibilities — provides an important context for understanding *Armed with Madness*. The novel's characters inhabit a world where ancient gods are not merely historical artifacts but living presences.

## Pagan Sensibility in the Novel

The novel's world is infused with a pagan sensibility that treats the natural world as divine. The narrative voice describes ancestral peoples as "killing but impersonal to their gods" [001-009], suggesting a time when the divine was immanent in daily life rather than transcendent. This immanent divinity reappears throughout: "The earth one great city of gods and men" [015-017], a vision of the world as populated by divine presences alongside human ones.

## Picus and the Woodpecker God

Picus Tracy, the most enigmatic of the young men, embodies the pagan revival's fascination with pre-Christian deity. His namesake is the Roman god Picus, a woodpecker-prophet, and the novel explicitly links him to this tradition: "he had been the bird who had been god" [011-027]. Picus's arts — wax modelling, whistling, dancing — evoke a pre-modern, ritualistic mode of being that contrasts with the rational modernity represented by Dudley Carston.
