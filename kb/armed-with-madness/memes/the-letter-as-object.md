# The Letter (as Physical Object)

The letter is the novel's object of communication across distance. "Lydia's letter came" (029-016): the verb — "came" — grants the letter its own agency. It travels; it arrives; it acts. The physical letter — paper, ink, stamp — is the material substrate of Lydia's narrative intervention, the object that crosses the Channel when she cannot.

## Physical Description

The letter's physical form — "a letter with a stamp like a black star" (005-019) — receives exactly one descriptive detail. The stamp, "like a black star," is at once postal marking and celestial omen: the letter carries darkness in its official markings.

## Symbolic Meaning

The letter is the object that collapses distance. Paris and Gault are separated by the Channel and by incompatible value systems; the letter bridges both. In a novel obsessed with the Grail — an object that must be sought in person — the letter offers an alternative model of transmission: meaning can travel without the body.
