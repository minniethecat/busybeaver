# God

In *Armed with Madness*, gods are not distant theological abstractions but immanent presences woven into the Dorset landscape, human relationships, and psychological states. The novel's characters move through a world where pagan deities — Hestia, Iduna, the "gods of the earth" — remain alive and demanding, their neglect carrying consequences.

## Overview

References to gods and goddesses span at least ten chapters, appearing in contexts ranging from casual allusion to ritual invocation. The novel's pagan sensibility treats divinity as plural, localized, and intimately connected to natural places — the wood, the well, the cliff. This stands in deliberate contrast to institutional Christianity, which is notably absent from the novel's spiritual landscape. The characters' relationship to gods ranges from Picus's instinctive paganism to the more self-conscious mythologizing of Felix and Scylla.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The concept of gods is introduced early, in the context of ritual sacrifice at the well: the offering is described as "willing but impersonal to their gods" (001-009), establishing from the outset that the divine in this world is plural, ancient, and connected to place.

### Key Scenes

- **The Bird as God**: One character experiences a bird as a divine manifestation — "the bird who had been god" — an epiphany of the sacred in natural form (011-027).
- **The Earth as City of Gods**: A vision of the earth as "one great city of gods and men" recurs twice, expressing the novel's sense that the numinous saturates the material world (015-017, 015-064).
- **Hestia and Iduna**: Named goddesses appear — Hestia, the Greek hearth-goddess (018-029), and Iduna, the Norse goddess whose apples feed the Aesir (025-003) — linking the novel's English setting to a wider Indo-European mythic inheritance.
- **The Neglected Gods**: Late in the novel comes a reference to "the punishment of a neglected set of gods" (029-019), suggesting that the characters' spiritual disorder stems from failure to honor the old deities.

> The earth one great city of gods and men. (015-064)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

The novel's gods function as a counter-force to the disenchantment of post-WWI modernity. Where the war has stripped meaning from traditional values, the old gods offer an alternative order grounded in place, ritual, and embodied experience. Picus is the character most naturally attuned to this pagan sensibility — he moves through the landscape as though the gods were contemporaries. Other characters, like Felix and Carston, approach the divine more intellectually, through art and antiquarianism. The novel suggests that neglect of the gods — treating them as dead mythology rather than living presences — produces the psychological fragmentation that afflicts the characters.

### Mythic / Symbolic Dimension

Mary Butts was deeply engaged with classical and Norse mythology, as well as the work of Jane Ellen Harrison and the Cambridge Ritualists, who argued that myth derives from ritual rather than the other way around. The novel embeds this theory in narrative form: the well ritual is not symbolic but efficacious, and the gods respond to it. The Grail itself, as a sacred object, links Christian legend to older pagan vessel-cults. By naming specific goddesses — Hestia (hearth, home, the domestic sacred) and Iduna (youth, renewal, the life-giving apple) — Butts situates her English story within a pan-European mythic framework that predates and exceeds Christianity.
