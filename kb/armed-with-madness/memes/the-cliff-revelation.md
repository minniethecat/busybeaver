# The Cliff Revelation

Scylla is afraid of Gault Cliff. The "terrible crag" (011-037) hangs over her, a geological menace that dwarfs the human dramas unfolding beneath it. Then she sees something. "That took away her fear of Gault. For the terrible crag was something that looked like Picus asleep" (011-037). The revelation is at once absurd and profound: the cliff looks like Picus. The perception — that the landscape is a face — does not explain the fear away but transforms it into recognition. She was afraid of the cliff; she was also afraid of Picus. Now she sees that they are the same.

## Prelude

Scylla's fear of Gault Cliff is established early. The cliff is the novel's most imposing natural feature, a "terror" (011-018) that dominates the landscape. Its geological indifference to human concerns — it has been there for millennia, will be there for millennia more — makes it a figure for the inhuman sublime. Scylla's fear is reasonable.

## The Event

### The Approach

Scylla and Picus walk to the cliff — "the edge of Gault" (011-003). The approach is a pilgrimage in miniature, a movement toward the sacred/terrifying center of the novel's geography.

### The Vision

"She looked up into the sky at Gault Cliff, where the mica glittered" (011-024). The cliff's mineral surface — mica, quartz, the glint of light on stone — resolves into a face. Picus's face, asleep. The vision is not hallucinatory; it is perceptual. Scylla sees what is there: the cliff has always looked like Picus, or Picus has always looked like the cliff. The revelation is that landscape and character are not separate categories.

### The Cure

"That took away her fear of Gault" (011-037). The fear is cured not by explanation but by identification. To see Picus in the cliff is to domesticate the sublime — to recognize the inhuman landscape as human, as face, as the beloved.

> "For the terrible crag was something that looked like Picus asleep." (011-037)

## Aftermath

- **Scylla's transformed perception**: After the cliff revelation, Scylla's relationship to the Gault landscape is permanently altered. The place is no longer terrifying but familiar — a face she knows.
- **The mythic mode established**: The cliff revelation is the novel's most explicit instance of mythic perception — the mode of seeing in which landscape, character, and myth are continuous.
- **Picus as landscape**: Picus's identification with Gault Cliff makes him a feature of the terrain. He is not merely at Gault; he is Gault.

## Symbolic Reading

The cliff revelation is the novel's Transfiguration scene. On the mountain (cliff), the beloved (Picus) is revealed in his mythic identity (Woodpecker-Zeus). Scylla, the witness, is changed by what she sees. The structure is scriptural, but the content is modernist: the divine is not above nature but within it — a face in the rock.
