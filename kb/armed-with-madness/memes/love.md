# Love

Love in *Armed with Madness* is never a stable state. It circulates through [[Scylla Taverner|Scylla]]'s refusals and [[Picus]]'s pursuits, through [[Felix Taverner|Felix]]'s silent devotion and [[Carston]]'s calculated strategies, through the broken ritual of [[the Sanc-Grail]] and the desolate aftermath of the novel's final catastrophe. Mary Butts writes love not as romance but as epistemology — a way of knowing another person that is as dangerous as it is necessary, and whose failure is the novel's most persistent subject. It is a word spoken as a demand ("I love you," 007-023), a condition that has "left" a household (007-002), a force that "love and death were one" (028-001), and an aspiration toward which characters reach and from which they repeatedly fall short.

## Overview

Love circulates through the novel in patterns that resist resolution. Carston desires Scylla; Scylla is drawn to Picus; Picus pursues Scylla but cannot sustain the pursuit; Felix loves Ross with a devotion that the narrative registers without naming; Clarence loves Scylla with a devotion he cannot express; Ross loves the sea and the cutter with an impersonality that may be the novel's only successful form of attachment. These vectors intersect, collide, and deflect without ever forming a stable geometry.

The word "love" and its variants appear most densely in Chapters IV–VIII (Carston's arrival and pursuit of Scylla), Chapter XXVIII (the novel's theological claim that "love and death were one"), and Chapters XXXIII–XXXV (the final catastrophe). This distribution traces an arc from erotic pursuit through metaphysical claim to mortal consequence.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Love enters the novel through negation: "She had told him that love had left them" (007-002). The "she" is Scylla; the "him" is Carston; the declaration is a refusal of the very expectation that structures the novel's social world. Love is not something that arrives but something that departs — a condition whose absence, not its presence, defines experience. This initial negation sets the terms for everything that follows: love in this novel is always already lost, always the thing one had and no longer has.

### Key Scenes

- **"I love you" (Ch. VII)**: The novel's only direct declaration of love is a performance: Carston speaks the words "I love you" (007-023) not as spontaneous confession but as a response to Scylla's demand "To get you to say" (007-022). The grammar is revealing — "to get you to say" makes the declaration a transaction, a verbal object produced on request. Love here is not interior feeling but speech act, and its sincerity is rendered irrelevant by its context.

- **Love that has left them (Ch. VII)**: Scylla's claim that "love had left them" (007-002) is both a personal statement about the Gault household and a generational diagnosis. Love — romantic, erotic, familial — has departed from a world that has survived the war but lost the capacity for genuine attachment. What remains is the form of love without its substance: flirtation, pursuit, possession, jealousy — all the behaviors of love without the thing itself.

- **Picus as lover (Ch. IV–V)**: Carston's early calculations about Picus — "To make love to her would be too hard work" (004-003), "he had other ideas again about Scylla as a lover" (004-016) — frame love as strategy, as labor, as a competition between men in which Scylla is the prize rather than the subject. The novel refuses Picus the interiority that would make his love legible; we see him only through Carston's competitive gaze and Scylla's disappointed one.

- **Picus's love for the thing he loves (Ch. V)**: The narrative's most intimate characterization of Picus's relationship to love describes him as "like a lover, aware of the sense about being the thing he loved" (005-015). The syntax is deliberately unstable: is Picus the lover, or is he the thing loved? The sentence refuses to decide, and this refusal captures Picus's fundamental narcissism — his love is always entangled with his need to *be* what is loved, to absorb the beloved into himself.

- **Wrong lovers and instinct for power (Ch. VI)**: Scylla's confession to Carston — "We have no money, and the wrong lovers, and our instinct for power" (006-039) — coordinates love, economics, and domination into a single diagnosis. "Wrong lovers" is both a specific claim (the Gault circle's romantic choices are consistently disastrous) and a structural one (the category of "right lover" may not exist, may have been destroyed by the conditions of modern life). The "instinct for power" that follows suggests that love, in this world, is always contaminated by the will to dominate.

- **"Make love to Scylla" (Ch. VII)**: The gramophone's refrain — "What'll I do, what'll I do?" — prompts Carston's internal response: "Make love to Scylla" (007-017). Love is the answer to the question of what to do, but the answer is a cliche, supplied by a character who has already been shown to misunderstand everything about the situation. The gramophone continues playing; love is a song on repeat, a loop that goes nowhere.

- **"Love and death were one" (Ch. XXVIII)**: In Chapter XXVIII, the novel makes its most compressed metaphysical claim: "And that love and death were one" (028-001). This is not a romantic Liebestod but a theological proposition. Love and death share a structure: both are absolute, both transform the subject, both are encounters with a limit that cannot be negotiated. To say they are one is to say that to love is to die to oneself — and that to die is, perhaps, to be finally capable of love.

- **"A love that would no longer be answered" (Ch. XXXIII)**: Picus's final love for Scylla is described in terms of its futility: "he loved her with a love that would no longer be answered" (033-014). The defining quality of this love is its lack of reciprocity — not unrequited love but love that *was* requited and has passed beyond the possibility of response. This is love at its most pure and most desolate, love that continues after its object has ceased to be capable of recognition.

> "And that love and death were one." (028-001)

The novel's love relations form a triangulated network in which no desire is directly reciprocated. The table below maps each character's primary love-object against the form that love takes and its narrative outcome.

| [[Picus]] | [[Scylla Taverner\|Scylla]] | Narcissistic; entangled with being "the thing he loved" (005-015) | Destruction of [[the Sanc-Grail]] and self; love that "would no longer be answered" (033-014) |
| [[Felix Taverner\|Felix]] | Ross | Silent, unquestioning devotion | The novel's most stable attachment — but never named as love |
| [[Clarence]] | [[Scylla Taverner\|Scylla]] | Inarticulate, devotional, mediated through the Grail | Isolation on Tollerdown; fear of losing the self |
| Ross | The sea / the cutter | Impersonal, elemental | The novel's only successful form of love — because its object cannot refuse |

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Love in *Armed with Madness* is the novel's great absent presence — the thing everyone talks about, pursues, mourns, and fails to achieve. The characters who speak most about love (Carston, Picus) are those least capable of it. The characters who feel love most intensely (Felix, Clarence) are those who cannot articulate it. The character who understands love most clearly (Scylla) is the one who knows it has already left.

This distribution is not accidental. The novel argues, through its narrative structure, that the discourse of love — the language of romance, the conventions of courtship, the grammar of possession and jealousy — is precisely what makes love impossible. To say "I love you" is to perform a script that replaces genuine feeling with social convention. To think of love as strategy ("Make love to Scylla") is to subordinate it to the will to power. To identify love with death is the only adequate response to its absoluteness — and it is also a response that makes love indistinguishable from its opposite.

### Modernist / Literary Dimension

Butts's treatment of love participates in the modernist dismantling of romantic love as a viable narrative structure. The Victorian novel's marriage plot — love as problem that finds its solution in the wedding — has no place in *Armed with Madness*. Love here is not a problem with a solution but a condition with no exit. The novel's closest precursor is Lawrence's *Women in Love* (1920), which similarly treats love as a field of competing wills rather than a meeting of souls. But where Lawrence retains a faith in the possibility of authentic relationship (however difficult), Butts is more radical: love in her novel is constitutively fugitive, always "flying" just beyond reach.

The triangulation of desire that structures the novel — Carston to Scylla to Picus, with Felix and Clarence as additional vectors — echoes Girard's theory of mimetic desire, in which we desire what others desire, and desire is always mediated by a rival. Butts's characters are trapped in triangles they cannot escape, not because they lack insight but because desire *is* triangular: to want someone is to want them because someone else wants them, and this mediation poisons the relationship from its inception.
