# The Spring

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness*, the spring operates on three interlocking planes: a physical water-source beneath Gault Cliff where the novel's central sexual-mythic union occurs (011); an archaeological well on Tollerdown from which the Grail cup was retrieved (004, 018); and a seasonal cycle — "last spring" (011-033, 018-023) — against which human events are measured and found fleeting. These three dimensions converge to make the spring the novel's most potent site of origin and return, the place where water rises from the earth and meaning rises from the landscape.

## Overview

The spring appears across at least five chapters, from the initial mention of the Downs' well-shafts in Chapter IV through the climactic return to Tollerdown in Chapter XVIII. It is associated with three distinct locations: the well on Tollerdown where Picus fished the greenish glass dish from the water (004-017, 004-021); the secret spring under Gault Cliff where Picus and Scylla achieve their erotic-mythic fusion (011-037); and the broader Gault landscape whose woods, cliffs, and streams form a continuous waterscape. The spring's narrative function is to serve as the novel's axis mundi — the still point around which the characters' desires, delusions, and discoveries revolve.

What distinguishes the spring from other symbolic sites in the novel (the wood, the house, the sea) is its physical modesty. It is not described in grand or sublime terms; it is "where the birds ended the spring rose" (011-037) — a location defined by its relation to animal life rather than by any intrinsic monumentality. The well, similarly, is a practical feature of the landscape — "those well-shafts on the downs" (004-021) — not a cathedral or a temple. This modesty is programmatic: Butts locates the sacred not in architectural grandeur but in the convergence of water, earth, and human attention. The spring is holy because people treat it as holy — because they kneel at it, sing to it, lower buckets into it, and retrieve from it objects that become the centre of their imaginative lives.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The spring first enters the novel indirectly, through Felix's account of the Grail cup's discovery. He tells Carston that "it was fishing it out of the well with that old spear — they always hunted in couples" (004-017), referring to the Grail and the spear as paired Arthurian symbols. The well is introduced not as a sacred site but as a practical problem: "owing to their well, shrunk by the drought, yielding insufficient water" (003-038), the Tollerdown cottage had become uninhabitable, prompting the transfer of Picus and Clarence to Gault House. The sacred object — the cup — is a by-product of a domestic emergency (the well running dry), a detail that establishes the novel's characteristic refusal to separate myth from mundanity.

### Key Scenes

- **The Well-Shafts of the Downs** (004-021): As Felix continues his disclosure to Carston, he contextualises the well within the archaeological landscape: "Those well-shafts on the downs might be any age. So might it." The well is temporally ambiguous — a Saxon shaft? a Bronze-Age excavation? — and this ambiguity is essential to its symbolic power. The spring/well is a point where linear chronology collapses; it has been flowing or holding water through multiple civilisations, indifferent to the human categories (Saxon, Celtic, modern) that attempt to periodise it. Felix's subsequent remark that "Tollerdown had a bad reputation" (004-021) reinforces the well's association with something uncanny and unmanageable.

- **"Last Spring, Looking for Young Gulls"** (011-033, 011-037): The central chapter of the novel, set under Gault Cliff, opens with a precise seasonal marker: Picus says "Last spring, looking for young gulls. I looked over the edge of this cliff" (011-033). The spring here is both season and location — "last spring" as temporal reference, "the spring rose" as spatial destination. The passage that follows is the novel's most sustained description of the site: "where the birds ended the spring rose. None of the sequence without the other" (011-037). The phrase "none of the sequence without the other" is crucial: the spring, the birds, the cliff, the wood — these form an irreducible whole. You cannot abstract the spring from its ecology. It is not a symbol that can be detached from its physical matrix; it IS the physical matrix, and its symbolic power derives precisely from the impossibility of detaching it.

- **The Well Returns — "Last Spring" at Tollerdown** (018-023): In Chapter XVIII, a character returns to Tollerdown and reports: "I went back another time, but when I came back to Tollerdown last spring. The well was brimming and I felt as if I had never been away." The well, which was "shrunk by the drought" in Chapter III (003-038), is now "brimming" — the seasonal cycle has restored what drought depleted. This return is associated with a feeling of temporal collapse ("as if I had never been away") that matches the well's own archaeological ambiguity. The spring's fullness mirrors the character's own return to psychic wholeness.

- **The Grail Retrieved from the Well** (006-007, 006-043): In the tea-party scene, Felix instructs Picus to "show me the cup you got out of the well" (006-007), and later someone reflects on "when we lifted that cup out of a well on the point of a spear" (006-043). The retrieval of the cup from water is the novel's foundational image — an object emerges from the depths, lifted on a spear-point, and the life of the Gault House circle reorganises around it. The well is less a container than a threshold: it is the aperture through which the sacred enters the mundane, the point at which the underworld of myth breaches the surface of everyday life.

> And where the birds ended the spring rose. None of the sequence without the other. (011-037)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

The spring's narrative function is to serve as the novel's principle of renewal — but renewal that is always under threat. The drought that shrinks the Tollerdown well (003-038) is the novel's opening crisis, the pragmatic problem that sets the plot in motion by forcing Picus and Clarence to relocate to Gault House. The well's later brimming (018-023) corresponds to a moment of narrative resolution. Between these poles — drought and fullness, depletion and return — the novel maps its characters' spiritual states. Those who can attune themselves to the spring's rhythm (Picus, Scylla, Felix) find themselves nourished by it; those who cannot (Carston, Philip) remain spiritually parched regardless of their proximity to water.

The spring at Gault Cliff (011) is the novel's most explicit image of sacred sexuality. Picus and Scylla make love beside the stream, under the cliff, in the presence of the Great Black Woodpecker who "was Zeus" (011-027). The spring here is both literal water-source and figurative wellspring of life — it is where desire achieves its mythic dimension, escaping the circuit of appropriation and fantasy that traps Carston. That Scylla's fear of Gault is dispelled by recognising that "where the birds ended the spring rose" — that the terrifying crag is also the source of water — is the novel's most compressed statement of its ecological-spiritual vision: fear dissolves when you see that the place you fear is the place that gives life.

### Mythic and Symbolic Dimension

The spring participates in a dense network of mythic associations. In the Grail romances that the novel explicitly invokes, the Grail castle is often located near a body of water — a lake, a river, a sea — and the Grail itself is sometimes associated with a spring or fountain. The Fisher King's wound, which renders his land a Waste Land, is healed when the questing knight asks the right question, restoring water to the parched kingdom. Butts inverts this pattern: the well IS dry (003-038), but the community does not wait for a questing knight to restore it. Instead, they relocate, carry the cup with them, and the water returns in its own seasonal time (018-023). Agency, in this novel, belongs to the spring itself, not to the human figures who draw from it.

The seasonal dimension of "spring" — Picus looking for young gulls "last spring" (011-033), the return to Tollerdown "last spring" (018-023) — aligns the novel with the cycle of Persephone, the vegetation goddess whose descent and return govern the seasons. Butts, who was deeply read in classical mythology and the Cambridge Ritualists (Jane Harrison, Gilbert Murray), would have been conscious of this resonance. The spring is not merely a water-source but a seasonal gate — a point where the underworld of winter releases its captives and the earth reanimates. Picus and Scylla's union at the spring in Chapter XI thus occurs at the intersection of two mythological systems: the Grail quest (Christian-Arthurian) and the Persephone cycle (pagan-classical). The novel refuses to choose between them, holding both in suspension as valid ways of understanding what the spring means.

The well as archaeological site introduces a third temporal register. "Those well-shafts on the downs might be any age" (004-021) — the well is prehistoric, pre-Christian, pre-Arthurian. Before it was a Grail site, it was a water-source for people whose names and gods are lost. Butts's modernism here is archaeological: the sacred is not a revelation from above but an excavation from below, retrieved from layers of human use that extend into unrecorded time. The spring, in this reading, is the novel's figure for the unconscious itself — a depth from which objects of desire emerge, whose waters rise and fall with mysterious regularity, and whose full extent cannot be mapped by the conscious mind.
