# Desire

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness*, desire is not a singular emotion but a spectrum
of intensities — ranging from Carston's calculating sexual obsession to the mythic
erotic union of Picus and Scylla at Gault Cliff — that the novel treats as the primary
engine of human action and the primary source of human disaster. It is desire, in all
its forms, that the sentient wood seems most adept at provoking and punishing.

## Overview

Desire permeates the novel at every level. It drives the central romantic triangle
(Scylla, Picus, and Carston), fuels Felix's pursuit of the Russian exile Boris, and
haunts Clarence's dreams of Picus as a dead bird he must carry across a thin spit of
rock into the sea. The word "desire" and its variants appear across at least eight
chapters, concentrated most heavily in the early chapters (005-006, 010-011) where
Carston's lust for Scylla crystallises, and in the later Paris and London sequences
(chapters 24-25) where the dispersed characters continue to be shaped by what they want.

Butts treats desire not as a private interior state but as a quasi-physical medium.
The wood amplifies it; the stone house offers no shelter from it; the Grail cup
mysteriously vanishes at the moment desire becomes contested and public. Desire in
this novel is always on the verge of becoming something else — fury, obsession,
violence, or (in the rare successful case) the kind of ecstatic fusion Picus and Scylla
achieve at Gault. The novel's moral geometry is organised around how each character
negotiates their wanting: those who instrumentalise desire (Carston, Philip) are
punished; those who surrender to it as an impersonal force (Scylla and Picus at Gault)
are, however briefly, transfigured.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Desire enters the novel through Dudley Carston's consciousness during his first lunch
at the house. Already unsettled by the wood's silence and the group's impenetrable
dynamic, he registers his attraction to Scylla as something that threatens to overwhelm
his composure:

> Lost in a green transparent world, he was blind. Beginning to see in a new way he
> disliked, a seeing like jealousy, without arrangement; principally a sensibility about
> Scylla, likely to become a fury of desire. He remembered its modest beginnings the
> night before, his rejection of it on further acquaintance with her brother. (005-012)

The phrasing is diagnostic: desire arrives as a "seeing" — a mode of perception — not
a feeling. It is "without arrangement," meaning it resists Carston's will to control
and stage-manage his experience. And from its first naming it is already on the verge
of becoming "fury."

### Key Scenes

- **Desire as Mental Theatre** (005-021): After glimpsing Picus's wax sculpture of
  Scylla naked, Carston reflects that the destroyed statuette would have sufficed as
  a substitute for the woman herself: "Almost as good as having the girl, to have that
  thing of her in wax. This was as far as he got. It was quite true that the statue
  would have done as well. Desire in Carston was almost mental, a redecoration of his
  memories." This passage exposes Carston's desire as essentially aesthetic and
  appropriative — Scylla is a surface to be possessed, not a person to be met. The
  phrase "a redecoration of his memories" suggests that his wanting is parasitic on
  prior representations, not a response to present reality.

- **Desire and Sexual Possession** (010-006, 010-017): The morning the cup disappears,
  Carston searches Scylla's room: "the first time he stared at the bed, he suffered and
  desired to throw himself on to it" (010-006). Watching Picus lift Scylla from the
  library steps, he feels "a very old feeling about sin and fleshy lust. A refinement
  on sensuality, he knew, but an excuse for rage" (010-017). The progression is telling:
  suffering and desiring collapse into each other, and the suffering is immediately
  converted into moral condemnation of Picus — "Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live"
  — which provides Carston with justification for the violence his desire demands.
  Desire, here, is not separable from the will to destroy whatever stands in its way.

- **Desire at Gault Cliff** (011-001, 011-029): In stark contrast, Scylla and Picus's
  journey to Gault begins with the discipline of resisting "the desire to anticipate in
  thought a distance their muscles must cover" (011-001). Unlike Carston, who wants
  before he possesses and re-decorates what he cannot have, Picus and Scylla practise
  a form of desire that is inseparable from presence: "Only he must make love to her
  there" (011-029). Their union at the secret spring is the novel's central image of
  desire fulfilled — but it is desire disciplined by the demands of the place itself,
  not desire that converts place into mental scenery.

- **Felix's Desires Take Purpose** (024-009): In Paris, Felix encounters the Russian
  exile Boris and experiences desire as a clarifying force: "Like a bow bent and
  relaxed, and strung with fresh arrows, his desires took purpose. At a ghastly little
  mixed bordel, he walked in like a prince come home" (024-009). Felix's desire, unlike
  Carston's, is generative — it produces action rather than rumination, direction rather
  than obsession. The simile of the bow suggests desire as potential energy made kinetic.

- **Desire as Social Perception** (025-069): In London, Scylla's friend Lydia observes
  that "it excited Philip to think of her desired" (025-069) — desire here operates at
  one remove, as a social currency. Philip wants Scylla not directly but because he
  imagines others wanting her. This passage completes the novel's anatomy of desire by
  showing its most degraded form: desire as gossip, as proxy, as something that feeds on
  the idea of desiring rather than on any genuine encounter.

- **The Appetite Parallel** (002-013, 016-006, 020-028): Three mentions of "appetite"
  reinforce the novel's treatment of desire as embodied drive. Clarence "once his
  strong appetites were satisfied, he did not want" (002-013); the wood's silence is
  opposed to "no woodpecker, no appetites" (016-006); Ross is said to "make his
  appetites serve his art" (020-028). In each case, appetite names a raw, pre-moral
  force that can be disciplined (Ross) or that disciplines its possessor (Clarence),
  but that cannot be ignored.

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Desire in *Armed with Madness* is not distributed equally or experienced uniformly.
Carston's desire for Scylla is the novel's most extensively anatomised case, and it is
characterised by a fatal disconnect: he wants what he cannot have, and he processes
that frustration by converting desire into moral outrage. His mental habit of
"redecorating memories" means that he never actually desires Scylla herself — he
desires an image of her, a surface, and the wax statuette Picus destroyed would have
served him just as well (005-021). This is desire as appropriation, and the novel
judges it harshly: Carston's desire leads him to denounce Picus's hands as coarse
and sinful (010-017), setting in motion the fantasies of rescue and righteous
possession that drive the novel's second half.

Picus and Scylla represent a counter-model. Their desire is inseparable from the
physical world of Gault Cliff — the stream, the Great Black Woodpecker, the skeleton,
the badger-bite on Picus's arm. When Scylla thinks "only he must make love to her
there" (011-029), the word "there" carries the full weight of the novel's investment in
place as the necessary condition of authentic experience. Their desire does not seek to
possess or consume but to participate — in the life of the wood, in the mythic
resonances of the bird who "was Zeus" (011-027).

Between these poles, the novel maps other varieties: Clarence's desire for Picus,
expressed only in dream and in the image of carrying a dead bird across a sea-spit
(005-017); Felix's desire for Boris, which moves outward into adventure rather than
inward into rumination (024-009); Philip's degraded proxy-desire for Scylla, which
requires the mediation of other men's wanting to become legible (025-069); and
Clarence's appetites, which once satisfied release him from wanting entirely (002-013).

The Grail cup functions as a kind of desire-detector. It vanishes the morning after
Carston's desire for Scylla crystallises, and the entire household spends the day
searching for it through rooms they thought they knew. This pattern — desire obscures
what it seeks; the object of desire withdraws — is the novel's structural principle.

### Literary / Symbolic Dimension

Butts's treatment of desire is grounded in modernist psychology (Freud is explicitly
named in the characters' Grail game at tea, 006-010) but it refuses reduction to
libidinal mechanics. Desire in this novel is closer to what the medieval Grail
romances called *amor de lonh* — love from afar, desire sustained by distance — than
to any clinical model of appetite. The Grail itself, described as possibly "the lapis
exilii, the stone of exile" or "the lapis exultationis, the stone of joy" (005-019),
functions as the metaphysical counterweight to desire: what desire pursues but cannot
grasp, what vanishes when pursued too directly.

The Gault Cliff sequence (011) explicitly mythologises desire through the figure of
Picus Martius, the woodpecker who was Zeus — a shape-shifting god who takes animal
form to approach mortal lovers. Scylla's recognition that "she was lying by the same
thing" — that her lover IS the bird who IS the god — collapses the distance between
human wanting and mythic presence (011-027). Her laughter — "Even Leda found a blue
egg" — acknowledges the absurdity and the seriousness of what is happening: desire
here has become genuinely transformative, not a fantasy of possession but an immersion
in a world where boundaries between self and other, human and animal, mortal and
divine, are permeable.

In the broader modernist context, Butts's treatment of desire can be contrasted with
D.H. Lawrence's vitalism (desire as life-force) and with the Freudian orthodoxy that
was consolidating in the 1920s. Butts refuses both the Lawrentian celebration of
unreflective instinct and the psychoanalytic reduction of desire to repressed
sexuality. Her desire is a field phenomenon — it moves between people, through the
wood, across the sea, and it is always tested by the novel's central question: can
desire be reconciled with perception, with the clear seeing of what is actually there?
Carston cannot; Picus and Scylla, at Gault, briefly can.
