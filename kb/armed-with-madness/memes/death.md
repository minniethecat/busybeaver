# Death

Death haunts the margins of *Armed with Madness*, surfacing in confession, argument, and crisis. A mother "driven to death" (018-015); twenty-three pictures of death that have taught a man "the worth of your trinkets" (020-022); a woman whose death is "resented" and whose "mystery" troubles the living (023-002); and, in the novel's most compressed theological statement, the claim that "love and death were one" (028-001).

Mary Butts treats death not as the novel's subject but as its climate — the permanent condition against which every pleasure, every beauty, and every relationship must be evaluated and found wanting.

## Overview

Unlike fear, madness, or beauty — concepts that receive extended discursive treatment in the novel — death enters the text through narrative events and characters' reported histories. It is the war that has already happened, the mother who has already died, the beloved who is dying, the revenge that is yet to come. This narrative embedding gives death a different ontological status from the novel's other key concepts: it is not something to be interpreted but something to be survived.

Death appears in three distinct temporal registers. The **past** includes the war dead (the unnamed casualties that constitute the novel's unspoken background), Picus's mother "who was driven to death" (018-015), and the pre-novel deaths that condition characters' present behavior. The **present** includes Ross's matter-of-fact acknowledgment of maritime mortality — "a death you court yourself in the cutter year in, year out" (020-017) — and, most urgently, Scylla's decline in the novel's final chapters. The **future** includes Picus's fantasy of "the revenge his death would be" (033-119), death weaponized as a final act of aggression.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Death enters the novel through Picus's confession to Ross in Chapter XVIII: "It was my mother who was driven to death" (018-015). This is a statement of fact delivered without context or elaboration — the reader learns nothing more about Picus's mother, how she died, or who drove her. The starkness of the declaration is itself the point: death is not a subject for novelistic development but a brute datum, a wound that explanation cannot close.

### Key Scenes

- **Picus's mother (Ch. XVIII)**: Picus's confession that his mother "was driven to death" (018-015) is the novel's first direct mention of a specific death. Ross's response — "That's nothing to do with it" — is characteristically brutal but philosophically precise: the past is not an excuse for present behavior. Death, in this exchange, is both an irreparable wound and an inadmissible alibi.

- **Ross on death at sea (Ch. XX)**: When Felix speaks of having "seen death," Ross counters with the professional sailor's equanimity: "A death you court yourself in the cutter year in, year out. We court. What about it?" (020-017). This is death domesticated by routine — the death that sailors live alongside, that becomes as familiar as weather. Ross's response exposes the gap between Felix's romantic relationship to death and the practical experience of those who live with it daily.

- **Twenty-three pictures of death (Ch. XX)**: Felix's most concentrated statement on death — "Twenty-three pictures of death have taught me the worth of your trinkets" (020-022) — is cryptic and allusive. The "twenty-three pictures" are never specified, but the phrase evokes the war, the trenches, the photographic documentation of mass death that defined the post-war visual imagination. Death here is pedagogical: it teaches, and what it teaches is the worthlessness of everything else.

- **The death that is resented (Ch. XXIII)**: The narrative notes of Felix that "he loved her, and resented her death, and was troubled by its mystery" (023-002). The syntax is telling: love, resentment, and metaphysical confusion are coordinated, not opposed. To love the dead is to resent them for dying; to resent them for dying is to be troubled by the mystery of what death means.

- **"Love and death were one" (Ch. XXVIII)**: The novel's most compressed theological formula — "And that love and death were one" (028-001) — appears without dramatic fanfare, embedded in a character's reflection. This is the novel's version of the Liebestod, the erotic identification of love with death that runs from Wagner through Freud. But where Wagner's Tristan achieves transcendence through this identification, Butts's characters are simply flattened by it. Love and death are one — and this is not a comfort but a terror.

- **Scylla's death (Ch. XXXIII)**: The novel's final chapter unfolds in the shadow of Scylla's approaching death. Picus "thought it might be death, coming sweetly for Scylla, as she had always said it would" (033-015). The sweetness attributed to Scylla's expectation of death — "as she had always said it would" — suggests a character who has made her peace with mortality, in contrast to Picus, for whom death is always an outrage, a defeat, a reason for revenge.

- **The revenge his death would be (Ch. XXXIII)**: Picus's final fantasy — "the revenge his death would be" (033-119) — transforms death from passive suffering into active weapon. To die is to punish the living; to be dead is to be permanently, irrevocably absent from a world that does not deserve one's presence. This is death as the ultimate act of narcissistic aggression.

> "And that love and death were one." (028-001)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Death in *Armed with Madness* functions as a limit-case for all the novel's other values. Beauty is transient because bodies die. Love is desperate because the beloved can be lost. The Grail is urgent because the wasteland is, finally, the land of the dead. Power is futile because death equalizes. Fear is rational because death is real.

The novel's characters have different relationships to this limit. Ross accepts death as a professional hazard — "we court" — and this acceptance gives him a stoic solidity that the other characters lack. Felix is fascinated by death but unable to integrate it; his "twenty-three pictures" have taught him something he cannot use. Picus is destroyed by death — his mother's, Scylla's — and his destruction takes the form of increasingly desperate attempts to control what cannot be controlled. Scylla alone among the major characters seems to have made peace with death, to have expected it "sweetly," and this peace is the novel's most radical claim about her character: she is not afraid of the one thing everyone else fears.

### Mythic / Symbolic Dimension

> "Thought it might be death, coming sweetly for Scylla, as she had always said it would" (033-015)

Death in the Grail legend is the consequence of the Fisher King's wound — the wasteland is a land where nothing grows because the king can neither live fully nor die. Butts's rewriting of this tradition makes death not a problem to be solved by the Grail knight's question but a condition to be inhabited. The Grail does not abolish death; at most, it transfigures it. The novel's alignment of love and death (028-001) is its version of this transfiguration — a claim that what we most desire and what we most fear are not opposites but the same thing seen from different angles.

This reading places Butts in dialogue with the death-obsessed modernism of Rilke (the *Duino Elegies*, 1923), Woolf (*Mrs. Dalloway*, 1925), and Hemingway (*A Farewell to Arms*, 1929). In all these works, the war has made death mass, mechanical, and meaningless; the task of art is to recover death as a personal event, to give it back the weight that the trenches took away. Butts's contribution is to refuse the consolations of elegy. Death in her novel is not redeemed by beauty, meaning, or art. It is simply there — and the only adequate response is to go on living in its presence.
