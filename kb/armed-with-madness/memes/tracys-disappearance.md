# Tracy's Disappearance

"Then Tracy vanished, after leading us a dance" (012-028). Ross's phrase compresses the novel's investigation plot into a single sentence. Tracy — the old man on the heath, the collector of antiquities, the possible guardian of the Grail — disappears after the cup is found. His vanishing is at once confession and ritual: the guardian who withdraws when the object is discovered, the trickster whose final trick is his own absence.

## Prelude

Tracy has been the cup's custodian — "From the collection of Christopher Tracy, Esq." (018-020). His house contains "one of those old books" (012-033). The cup's discovery is linked to him; Picus, his son-figure, is the finder. When the cup is found, Tracy vanishes.

## The Event

### The Disappearance

Ross's account is the only narration: "you found that thing. Then Tracy vanished, after leading us a dance" (012-028). The sequence — finding, then vanishing — implies causation. Tracy's disappearance is a response to the cup's discovery.

### "Leading Us a Dance"

The phrase "leading us a dance" (012-028) transforms Tracy's actions into performance. He has not merely left; he has choreographed his departure. The dance metaphor makes him at once puppet-master and performer — someone who stages events for an audience.

## Aftermath

- **Carston's investigation**: Tracy's disappearance makes investigation necessary. Carston searches his house, confronts him, tries to determine what the vanishing means.
- **The unresolved question**: Tracy returns (or is found), but the novel never resolves whether his disappearance was guilt, ritual, or both.
