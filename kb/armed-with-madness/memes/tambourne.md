# Tambourne

Tambourne is the furthest reach of the novel's Dorset geography — a village "seventy miles inland" (029-012) from Gault, reached by a walk that is itself a kind of pilgrimage. "Plenty of fine old women in Tambourne" (026-003): the village is defined by its inhabitants, by the "regret for Nanna's going" (026-003) that lingers in local memory. It is a place of departure and destination — characters leave Tambourne for Gault, walk to Tambourne from Gault, and the distance between them measures the novel's emotional geography.

## Description

Tambourne appears through its institutions and its absences. "At the inn called the Star at Tambourne" (026-003): the inn, with its resonantly named "Star," is the village's social center. "A lady, who was frequently at Tambourne when you were at school" (016-035): the village is associated with childhood, with the time before the novel's present crises.

> "She wanted to walk seventy miles inland to Tambourne where Picus might be." (029-012)

## Narrative Function

### Events at Tambourne

- **Nanna's origin**: Tambourne is where Nanna came from — "Plenty of regret for Nanna's going" (026-003) — and her departure marks the village as a place people leave.
- **The woman who died**: "The woman to live with him at Tambourne till she died of T.B." (016-052). Tambourne is the site of past tragedy, the local history that haunts the present.
- **Scylla's pilgrimage**: Scylla's desire to walk "seventy miles inland to Tambourne where Picus might be" (029-012) makes Tambourne the object of a quest — not the Grail quest, but a human one.

### Symbolic Meaning

Tambourne is Gault's distant satellite — the outermost point of the local geography, where the Dorset world shades into the rest of England. Its distance from Gault (seventy miles) is the novel's measure of how far a character will go for another.
