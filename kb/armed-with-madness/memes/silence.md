# Silence

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness*, silence is an active, layered force rather than a
simple absence of sound — a "complicated production" of architecture, landscape, and the
quasi-sentient wood that shapes the experiences of every character who enters the novel's
world.

## Overview

Silence is established from the novel's opening paragraph as the defining atmospheric
condition of the house and its setting. Far from being neutral or restful, this silence
is described as difficult to endure — guests who came for a week had been known to leave
the next day (001-003). It pervades the narrative as a test of character: those who can
inhabit the silence, like Scylla, Felix, Ross, and Picus, belong to the place; those who
cannot, like the American visitor Dudley Carston, are fundamentally alienated from it.

The silence appears across the novel's entire span, from the first chapter's elaborate
opening meditation through the climactic events at Tollerdown in Chapter 33. It operates
on multiple registers: as a physical property of the stone rooms and empty downland, as
a psychological mirror that reflects each character's inner state, and as a quasi-mystical
threshold connected to the wood's own uncanny life.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Silence is introduced in the novel's opening sentence as a paradox — the house is
"marvellously noisy, but the noises let through silence" (001-001). The three listed noises
(jays, a hay-cutter, the light wind) serve not to fill the silence but to reveal it, as
though sound were a permeable membrane through which a deeper stillness passes.

The full description, spanning paragraphs 001-001 through 001-003, establishes silence as
a composite phenomenon:

> The silence let through by the jays, the hay-cutter, and the breeze, was a complicated
> production of stone rooms, the natural silence of empty grass, and the equivocal, personal
> silence of the wood. Not many nerves could stand it. People who had come for a week had
> been known to leave next day. The people who had the house were interested in the wood and
> its silence. When it got worse, after dark or at midday, they said it was tuning-up. When
> a gale came up-Channel shrieking like a mad harp, they said they were watching a visible
> fight with the silence in the wood. (001-003)

### Key Scenes

- **Carston's First Night** (003-009): Arriving from London, Paris, and New York, Dudley
  Carston experiences the silence as intolerable. Alone in his room, watching the down turn
  black against the evening sky, he finds himself unnerved by the absence of urban noise.
  The silence here functions as the novel's first test of belonging — Carston fails it
  immediately, retreating into his habitual frame of "people" and "theatre."

- **The Silence of the House** (014-001): After the confrontation over the cup, Carston
  shuts his door and "ceased to hear the silence of the house, heard instead the wood, a
  little restless, its branches changing places in a wind risen suddenly off the sea." This
  passage distinguishes between architectural silence (the house) and the animate silence of
  the wood — Carston can shut out the former but not the latter.

- **The Silence Goes Away** (003-028): At the close of Scylla and Carston's uneasy dinner
  alone, "The silence went away, and left nothing." An iron clang — the gate onto the
  grass — announces the return of Felix, Ross, Picus, and Clarence. Silence here is
  narrative punctuation, a held breath that breaks with human arrival.

- **Carston Mounts into Silence** (033-002): Near the novel's climax, Carston ascends
  toward Tollerdown: "Carston mounted, into silence, on to height. He had never been so
  well in his life, could not have stood that if he had not been so well. Never had his
  heart been so touched." At this late point in the narrative, silence has transformed from
  threat to what almost reads as a form of grace — though Carston's capacity to receive it
  remains uncertain.

- **The Pause Before the Coming Storm** (012-020): When Scylla and Picus return from
  their time together on the downs, they enter the room "warm, sunburnt ... serene, apart.
  After a silence, 'What's wrong?' said Scylla." This conversational silence functions as
  dramatic staging — the lovers' serenity colliding with the room's accumulated tension
  over the vanished cup.

- **Silence of the Chauffeur** (016-001): In a minor but telling usage, Carston "hoped he
  bribed the driver of the car to silence" while attempting to evade Picus's father. Here
  silence reverts to its ordinary meaning — the suppression of speech — reminding the
  reader how far the novel's central usage departs from the commonplace.

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Silence operates as a test of belonging. The central characters — Scylla, Felix, Ross,
Picus, and to a lesser degree Clarence — are "interested in the wood and its silence"
(001-003), a phrase that distinguishes them from the nervous guests who flee. Their
interest is active and participatory: they play the gramophone "to appease it" (001-004),
they call the midday and midnight intensifications "tuning-up," and they watch gales as
"a visible fight with the silence in the wood" (001-003).

For Carston, silence is consistently a negative experience. His frame of reference — "I
know moonlight, I know starlight" (003-009) — is that of the aesthete who can only
process experience through art and social performance. The silence that the others
inhabit as a living medium becomes, for him, something to be fled from, denounced, or
converted into anecdotes.

The wood's silence is personified throughout: it "giggles," it "cackles," it is described as
"personal" and "equivocal." This personification is not merely decorative but structural.
The silence of the wood is the novel's primary register of the non-human — a presence
that does not communicate in language but imposes itself through pure atmosphere.

### Literary / Symbolic Dimension

Butts's treatment of silence participates in several literary traditions. The "complicated
production" of different silences echoes the modernist preoccupation with layered
perception — silence is not one thing but a composite, requiring the same sort of
attention that a Cubist painting demands. The formula "stone rooms + empty grass +
equivocal wood" (001-003) is almost a recipe, an analysis of atmosphere into its
constituent parts.

The silence also has a mythic dimension. The house's inhabitants treat it as a living
counterpart to themselves, something to be propitiated with music and understood through
a kind of sympathetic attention. This recalls the Grail mythology that runs through the
novel: just as the Sanc-Grail cup transforms those who encounter it, the silence of the
wood transforms those who can bear it. Those who cannot — like Carston — remain
outside the novel's circle of initiation.

In the broader context of interwar modernism, Butts's silence can be read as a
counterweight to the metropolitan noise that Carston brings with him from "London, Paris,
and New York" (003-009). The silence is not pastoral retreat but active alternative — a
mode of being that the novel proposes against the "elaborate theatre, without scenery" that
Carston mistakes for real life (003-010).
