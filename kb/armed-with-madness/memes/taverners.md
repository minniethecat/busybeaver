# the Taverners

The Taverner family — [[Scylla Taverner]] and [[Felix Taverner]] — are the owners of [[Gault]] House and the gravitational center of *Armed with Madness*. Unlike the [[tracys|Tracys]], who are defined by absences, the Taverners are defined by presence: Scylla's perceptual acuity, Felix's attentive care, and the sibling bond between them that constitutes the novel's most durable emotional fact. "Miss Taverner's brother seems a bit upset about something" (005-008): the observation, made by an outsider, captures the Taverner dynamic — visible, vulnerable, mutually constitutive.

The Taverners, unlike the sprawling Tracy clan, are only two — and their smallness is their strength. The sibling dyad is the novel's irreducible human unit: it needs no third party, it generates no surplus drama, it simply endures.

## Narrative Function

- **The Household as Moral Order**: Gault House under the Taverners is a space of care and attention rather than accumulation. Felix arranges flowers; Scylla arranges perceptions. Together they maintain a world that the novel's visitors — [[Carston]], [[Picus]], [[Clarence]] — enter but cannot sustain.

- **The Sibling Bond as Counterpoint to Erotic Pursuit**: Every erotic relationship in the novel fails or fractures — Carston and Scylla, Picus and Scylla, the Tracy son's pursuit. Only the Taverner siblings' bond remains intact. "When you came in with Picus I saw your beauty. It was a kind of answer" (013-024): Felix's recognition of his sister is the novel's model for what love without possession looks like.

- **Class and Place**: "Over at Gault House with the Taverners" (015-005) — the phrase identifies the house with the family. The Taverners' class position is ambiguous: landed but not wealthy, educated but not metropolitan, rooted in Dorset without being of it. This in-betweenness is their narrative gift — they can host anyone because they belong to no single world.

> "Then a boy, Scylla's brother Felix Taverner, the English peach in flower" (003-034)

## Thematic Significance

- **Inheritance Without Heirs**: The Taverner line ends with Scylla and Felix. There are no children, no parents (the Taverner parents are never mentioned), no future. The family exists entirely in the present tense — a holding action against the dissolution that the novel knows is coming.

- **The Family as Sanctuary**: In a novel where every erotic bond is also a threat, the Taverner sibling bond is the only relationship that offers safety without demand. This makes it the novel's implicit standard for what human connection should be.

- **Fragile Continuity**: The Taverners' survival at the novel's end — Scylla facing death with equanimity, Felix continuing to tend — is both victory and elegy. The Taverner family persists, but only just, only for now.
