# The Storm

A recurring natural force in Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness* (1928) that operates as both a literal meteorological event and a narrative device marking moments of psychological crisis, erotic climax, and mythic rupture. Storms in the novel are never neutral background — they are agents of transformation that expose character, dissolve social decorum, and render visible the elemental violence that the Gault House circle cultivates as a "natural ferocity" (001-009).

## Overview

Storms appear across at least seven of the novel's thirty-seven chapters, concentrated in three distinct narrative arcs: the opening establishment of the Gault House world (Ch. I), the central dramatic sequence of the storm party (Ch. VI-VII), and the later equinoctial gale that reshapes the landscape (Ch. XIX-XX). The storm cuts across both temporal registers of the novel — appearing in memory as a winter tempest at Tollerdown (006-004) and in the present tense as the thunderstorm that frames the whiskey-and-soda desecration of the Sanc-Grail (Ch. VII). Butts consistently links storms to heightened perception, erotic awakening, and the collapse of the distance between the civilized and the wild.

The storm's narrative function is architectural: it provides a crescendo structure that the novel's otherwise fragmentary modernist form requires. Just as the silence of the wood is "a complicated production" (001-003), so the storm is a symphony of thunder, lightning, rain, wind, and its aftermath — and it is the aftermath that the characters most keenly anticipate, "the freshest earth there is" (007-006).

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The storm is first invoked not as immediate sensation but as cultural memory and aesthetic spectacle. In the novel's opening chapter, the inhabitants of Gault House describe the winter gales as a theatric combat with the wood's silence:

> When a gale came up-Channel shrieking like a mad harp, they said they were watching a visible fight with the silence in the wood. (001-003)

This early framing establishes the storm not as mere weather but as a performance — an agon between elemental forces that the characters observe with the detachment of connoisseurs. The phrase "visible fight" positions the storm as something to be watched, a nature-play in which the wood and the wind are antagonists. The same passage notes that when the silence "got worse, after dark or at midday, they said it was tuning-up" (001-003) — the gale is the payoff for which the oppressive stillness is the prelude.

### Key Scenes

- **Scylla's memory of the winter storm at Tollerdown (Ch. VI)**: At tea with the doctor, Scylla recalls the only time she visited the shepherd's cottage at Tollerdown in winter: "I think of it the only time I was there in winter, in a storm. Wind roaring over the flint-crop and snow whirling. Lying an instant and vanishing" (006-004). This is the novel's most compressed storm image — a winter tempest stripped to its essentials of wind, snow, and evanescence. It is significant that the storm memory is linked to Tollerdown, the "darkest place that exists" (006-003), and to the delivery of the shepherd's wife — birth, death, and elemental violence knotted together in a single recollection.

- **The thunderstorm builds (Ch. VI)**: As Scylla and Carston walk through the wood, the storm gathers on the horizon. The thunderclouds are described with a density of visual and tactile imagery: "Like open fir-cones dipped in fire and cream, the thunderclouds were piling up the sky. Mounting the hills, a wing of them rising out of the sea. Inshore, a breath of wind clashed the pine needles" (006-054). Scylla's warning — "See the thunder clouds banking up? I must get back before the rain" (006-051) — links the approaching storm to the erotic electricity of her exchange with Carston. The storm becomes the atmospheric correlative of Carston's frustrated desire and Scylla's controlled intensity. As she departs, he sees her in his mind "towards a formidable other world, its edges drawn in fire, the thunderclouds now halfway across the sky" (006-069).

- **The storm party (Ch. VII)**: The fullest storm sequence occupies the whole of Chapter VII, where the thunderstorm arrives during a dinner party without Picus or Scylla. The earth is "closed in a hot, purple air-ball, the lightning flicking on and off" (007-001). Ross arranges chairs on the verandah "while the storm banged about" (007-001), and Clarence plays the gramophone "to play against the sky" (007-001), continuing the motif of performance against the elements. Carston, the American outsider, "was not accustomed to invite the lightning to visit him under trees" (007-001) — his alien discomfort with the group's cultivated recklessness is made concrete by the storm.

- **The storm's music (Ch. VII)**: Butts renders the storm as a composition in three movements. First, the rain arrives: "*Phat* went a raindrop on a flag, and a double uproar began. For an hour it rained, through sheet lightning, and thunder like a departing train, the hills calling to one another" (007-006). The gutters "rushed and sang and leaked, single notes from which the ear eventually picked out a tune. Syncopation, magic, nature imitating Mozart?" (007-006). Carston hears it as "an overture, for some private earth-life, mercifully and tiresomely apart from his" (007-006). The storm is here aestheticized into music — a modernist synaesthesia that transforms brute nature into artistic form while simultaneously excluding the outsider who cannot hear the tune.

- **Scylla and Picus return through the rain (Ch. VII)**: The storm's second movement coincides with the arrival of Scylla and Picus, who "run out of the wood and across the lawn, laughing, wet as dogs" (007-008). Scylla squeezes out her hair and declares: "It was too good to miss. Tell me, Carston, does the lightning get you when you're under a tree, or when you're not? We tried both" (007-009). Their reckless enjoyment — testing lightning's lethality as a game — embodies the group's relationship to danger, a cultivated insouciance that Carston cannot share. Scylla's remark "Storms aren't in your schedule" (007-010) anatomizes him: the storm is the diagnostic tool that reveals who belongs and who does not.

- **The storm's finale (Ch. VII)**: The chapter's erotic climax — Picus whistling "Lady Be Good" outside Scylla's door and her inviting him in — unfolds against the storm's final cadence: "A minute later the tail of the lightning winked. The rain quickened. The door next his opened. The gutter outside began to run fast again. Through the finale of the storm, he heard a gull crying" (007-030). The storm's release and the lovers' union are temporally fused, the lightning's wink and the door's opening a single rhythm. After they vanish into Scylla's room, Carston goes to the window, "shocked, almost whimpering" (007-030), and the night clears: "Over the wood Orion hung up his belt and sword" (007-030). The storm has passed, the erotic transaction is complete, and the cosmic order — figured by Orion and Aldebaran — reasserts itself.

- **The equinoctial gale (Ch. XIX-XX)**: A second major storm arc occurs in Chapters XIX and XX, where an equinoctial gale descends on the coast. Scylla anticipates it with "eyes full of an animal's pleasure" and names it: "A big storm" (019-007). She and Carston recite Longfellow's "The Wreck of the Hesperus" together — "When descends on the Atlantic / The gigantic / Storm-wind of the equinox" (019-009, 019-011) — falling "into nature" together (019-019). But the storm's aftermath is grim: a Danish tramp aground on Tunbarrow Ledges, "twenty-three drowned men laid out on tables in the parish room" (020-002). The storm that Scylla greeted with joy leaves corpses in its wake — a brutal reminder that the sublime has a body count. The little bay is "packed with dull drift that choked the waves, a pocket for the storm's mess" (020-004).

- **The aborted thunderstorm (Ch. XII)**: In the aftermath of the cup's discovery in Carston's room and the subsequent group confrontation, Scylla names the collective fury: "An aborted thunderstorm" (012-040). The metaphor is precise — the social explosion, like a meteorological one, fails to clear the air. It leaves "Five left, hating each other" (012-041). The storm here operates at the level of simile, a figure for psychological discharge that cannot complete itself, leaving the group in a state of unresolved tension.

> The storm tuned up again, the rain striking in rods, filling the air with fine spray. The others were enjoying it, the first row of the stalls for a nature-play. (007-008)

> When a gale came up-Channel shrieking like a mad harp, they said they were watching a visible fight with the silence in the wood. (001-003)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

The storm serves three interconnected narrative functions in *Armed with Madness*. First, it is a test of character. Those who belong at Gault House — Scylla, Picus, Felix, Ross, Clarence — relate to storms with a mixture of aesthetic appreciation, reckless play, and ritual participation. The gramophone is played "against the sky" (007-001); Scylla and Picus test lightning's danger "both" ways (007-009); the gale is a "visible fight" to be "watched" (001-003). Those who do not belong — Carston foremost among them — are alienated by the storm, exposed as visitors who lack the group's cultivated intimacy with elemental violence.

Second, the storm is an erotic catalyst. The thunderstorm of Chapter VII brackets the most sexually charged sequence in the novel's first half: Scylla and Picus returning through the rain "wet as dogs" and "so wet they both seemed naked" (007-008, 007-010), Carston making grog with tears in his eyes from the wood-fire smoke that "looked like real tears to Ross" (007-010), the dance, the whiskey-and-soda desecration of the Sanc-Grail, and finally Picus whistling "Lady Be Good" outside Scylla's door as the lightning gives its final wink. The storm externalizes sexual energy that social convention would keep invisible, making the erotic life of the house as manifest and inescapable as the weather.

Third, the storm is a narrative structuring device. The novel, which proceeds by fragmentary scenes and rapid tonal shifts, uses the storm as a centering event — a passage of sustained intensity around which the more diffuse episodes of conversation, walking, and introspection can constellate. The storm is one of the few experiences that the entire household undergoes simultaneously and collectively, however differently each character experiences it.

### Symbolic and Literary Dimension

Butts's storms belong to a modernist revaluation of the sublime. Unlike the Romantic sublime of Shelley or Turner, in which the perceiving subject is ennobled by confrontation with overwhelming nature, Butts's storms are disorienting rather than elevating. Carston hears the storm's music as "an overture, for some private earth-life, mercifully and tiresomely apart from his" (007-006) — the sublime here is not an experience of transcendence but of exclusion. The storm's meaning is not universally available; it belongs to those who have earned it through attunement.

The "visible fight with the silence in the wood" (001-003) also inscribes the storm within the novel's central opposition between silence and noise, stillness and movement. The wood's silence is "equivocal, personal" (001-003) — it withholds meaning. The storm, by contrast, is all disclosure: it bangs, shrieks, winks, and rushes. But what it discloses is not information but force. The storm is the wood's opposite number, the yang to its yin, and the characters' ability to inhabit both — to pass from the oppressive silence of midday into the orgiastic noise of the gale — constitutes their particular spiritual competence.

Finally, the storm participates in the novel's Grail symbology. The Grail quest tradition is full of tempests and wastelands; the storm that descends on Gault House during the chapter in which the Sanc-Grail is profaned (used for whiskey and soda, 007-015) echoes the Fisher King's blighted domain. But Butts inverts the traditional logic: in her world, the storm does not punish desecration — it celebrates it. The lightning winks at the lovers; the rain quickens as the door opens. The sacred and the profane are not antagonists in the storm but partners in a single dance.
