# Picus

Picus enters *Armed with Madness* as a figure of almost mythic physical distinction: "the tallest they called Picus, grave as a marsh-bird dancing" (003-034). Everything about him is excessive — his height, his beauty, his "boyish" passions, his destructive capacity. He is described as "grave" (003-046), "transparent" (007-013), a man whose "delicacy" attracts and whose deceptions repel. Mary Butts constructs Picus as a study in charisma without integrity — the figure who compels attention, desire, and belief, and who cannot be trusted with any of them.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full name and aliases**: Known only as Picus — a name suggestive of the Latin *picus* (woodpecker), a bird sacred to Mars and associated with prophecy. The classical resonance is appropriate: Picus is a seer who cannot see himself.
- **Circle**: Gault House group — originally housed with Clarence, later transferred to the main house
- **Key relationships**:
  - With Scylla: erotic and intellectual fascination that curdles into mutual destruction. Picus "had other ideas again about Scylla as a lover" (004-016), but his love is always entangled with narcissism — "like a lover, aware of the sense about being the thing he loved" (005-015).
  - With Clarence: the "old man of the sea" relationship (004-008) — Picus is Clarence's protector, companion, and ultimately his betrayer. The queer subtext of their relationship is registered without being named.
  - With Carston: rivalry and mutual incomprehension. Carston sees Picus as a sexual rival and a moral danger; Picus barely registers Carston's existence.
  - With his mother: "It was my mother who was driven to death" (018-015) — the novel's most cryptic biographical detail, and perhaps the key to Picus's psychology. A man whose mother was "driven to death" cannot trust love to sustain rather than destroy.

> "his glory, a kind of lost god" (010-017)

## Character and Thematic Function

Picus functions as the novel's embodiment of the Grail myth's dark side — the quest that becomes obsession, the beauty that becomes weapon, the truth that becomes a thing to be manipulated rather than served.

- **Charisma**: Picus's physical presence is the novel's most concentrated study of male beauty. "His glory, a kind of lost god" is implied in Carston's vision of "their beauty, their own, and the beauty of their passion" (010-017) when Picus lifts Scylla. This beauty is not decorative but operative: it makes people — Scylla, Carston, Clarence — act against their own interests.

- **Deception**: Picus breaks the Grail cup — or does not break it; the novel leaves the question of the cup's authenticity deliberately unresolved. What is certain is that Picus manipulates truth as a resource. His relationship to the Grail is instrumental: the cup is valuable because of what its possession enables, not because of what it is.

- **Narcissism as epistemology**: Picus cannot distinguish between loving someone and being the person he loves. "Like a lover, aware of the sense about being the thing he loved" (005-015) — the syntax is deliberately ambiguous. Does Picus love Scylla, or does he love being the image of a man who loves Scylla?

> "Like a lover, aware of the sense about being the thing he loved." (005-015)

## Narrative Arc

### Initial State: The Tallest of Them

Picus first appears as part of the Gault House ensemble, distinguished by his height, his gravity, and his physical effect on others. Carston immediately registers him as a rival: "That infernal Picus is a psychic if there ever was one" (004-013). The introduction establishes Picus as a force rather than a character — something that acts on others rather than someone who acts from discernible motives.

### Conflict: The Grail and the Betrayal

The central crisis of the novel is Picus's relationship to the Grail cup. He possesses it, or claims to possess it; he breaks it, or claims to have broken it; his entire identity becomes entangled with an object whose authenticity the novel never confirms. His confrontation with Ross over the cup — "Pawn your father's collection. Throw it into the sea" (018-016) — reveals the economic desperation beneath the mythic posturing.

### Final State: Love Without Answer

In the novel's final chapters, Picus is alone with the dying Scylla. His love has become a pure futility — "a love that would no longer be answered" (033-014). He cares for her body while his mind fragments: "he went nearly mad as the light failed" (033-014). His final fantasy is of "the revenge his death would be" (033-119) — death not as peace but as the ultimate act of aggression against a world that has refused him what he wanted.

## Mythic and Symbolic Associations

Picus's name links him to the Roman woodpecker-god, a figure of prophecy associated with Mars. But Butts's Picus is a prophet without a message, a seer who cannot see. His connection to the Grail legend is similarly hollow: he is a Grail knight who breaks the Grail, a quester whose quest is indistinguishable from theft.

In the novel's mythic economy, Picus is the Fisher King figure — the wounded ruler whose incapacity blights the land. But where the traditional Fisher King is passive (wounded, waiting), Butts's Fisher King is active and destructive. His wound is not a lance-thrust but a mother's death; his wasteland is not a barren kingdom but a broken cup. The novel refuses him the dignity of mythic tragedy; he is too petty, too self-regarding, too *human* for that.
