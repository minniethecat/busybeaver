# Ritual

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness*, ritual is the shared practice through which the
inhabitants of Gault House maintain their precarious equilibrium with the sentient wood,
the Sanc-Grail cup, and the larger forces that press upon their world — not a dead
formula but a living "rite produced by the imagination" (013-006) that distinguishes
insiders from outsiders and provides the novel's deepest grammar of belonging.

## Overview

Ritual appears across the full span of the novel, from the opening chapter's gramophone
ceremony through the climactic sequences at Tollerdown in Chapter 33. Its frequency is
highest in the early chapters, where Butts establishes the rhythm of life at Gault House
in careful, almost ethnographic detail: the family play records on the verandah "to
appease" the wood (001-004), they name the wood's intensifications "tuning-up" (001-003),
and they conduct their "part of the stranger's entertainment" as "a kind of ritual, a
sacrifice, willing but impersonal to their gods" (001-009).

The word itself appears at critical junctures in the narrative. The "kind of ritual, a
sacrifice" passage (001-009) defines the group's relationship to the sea and to the
outsider; the "ritual, or rite produced by the imagination" passage (013-006) provides
the novel's most explicit theory of ritual as a form of creative action; and the
appearance of ritual language in the find-illumination-doubt sequence near the novel's
close (031-063) suggests that ritual experience remains the novel's measure of authentic
encounter even after the Gault circle has dispersed. In Chapter 8, Scylla explicitly
names "the sacred game" (008-035) as the name for their collective life, a phrase that
elevates ritual from private practice to shared cosmology.

Ritual in this novel is never solemn. It is "willing but impersonal" (001-009), shot
through with laughter, and inseparable from "dancing, and stretches of very insular
behaviour" (001-020). The gramophone is a ritual instrument; the Charleston is a ritual
dance; the cup's disappearance and reappearance constitute a ritual drama in which
everyone is required to play.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Ritual is first introduced — though without the word itself — in the novel's opening
paragraphs, where the gramophone-playing ceremony is described as an act of placation:

> A large gramophone stood with its mouth open on the verandah flags. They had been
> playing to the wood after lunch, to appease it and to keep their dancing in hand.
> (001-004)

The word "ritual" appears for the first time eight paragraphs later, applied to the
group's relationship with the sea and, by extension, to the stranger they receive:

> "Mare Nostrum," they said, in Paris or in London, at the sea's winter takings there.
> An outlet for a natural ferocity they were too proud to exercise, too indifferent to
> examine. Also a kind of ritual, a sacrifice, willing but impersonal to their gods.
> (001-009)

### Key Scenes

- **The Gramophone Ceremony** (001-004, 007-001, 007-017): The gramophone-playing on the
  verandah is the novel's primary image of domestic ritual. After lunch, the household
  plays records "to appease" the wood and "to keep their dancing in hand" (001-004). In
  Chapter 7, Carston turns on the gramophone himself for the first time, playing "against
  the sky" (007-001) during the thunderstorm — a moment that marks his partial
  initiation into the group's ritual life. The gramophone's refrain, "What'll I do,
  what'll I do?" (007-017), becomes a ritual question, repeated mechanically as the
  storm's counterpoint.

- **The First Ceremony of Mystery** (021-002): In Chapter 21, the narrative describes an
  unnamed character as a "novice at his first ceremony of mystery" (021-002). This
  language — ceremony, mystery, novice — explicitly frames the group's experiences
  within a ritual vocabulary that suggests initiation and graded revelation.

- **The Sacred Game** (008-034, 008-035): Walking across the heath with Ross, Scylla
  defends her relationship with Picus by invoking "the sacred game": "Perhaps I'm tired
  of keeping things steady for you. This is my pleasure and my game. … Think of it
  another way, Ross: that Picus is giving us an excuse for the sacred game" (008-034).
  Ross's reply is telling: "Americans are bad players. At bridge or gardening, or life;
  especially when it is life as the sacred game" (008-035). The "sacred game" is their
  name for the ritualised life they lead — a life treated as play but with the highest
  possible stakes.

- **Ritual as Creative Act** (013-006): In one of the novel's most theoretical passages,
  ritual is compared to natural growth and artistic production: "as any growth in nature.
  Or ritual, or rite produced by the imagination" (013-006). This formulation places
  ritual alongside organic processes and imaginative creation — it is neither imposed
  from without nor merely habitual, but generated by the same faculty that produces art.

- **The Find-Illumination-Doubt Sequence** (031-063): In Chapter 31, the narrator
  describes a characteristic experience of the Grail seekers: "You are having something
  like a ritual. A find, illumination, doubt" (031-063). This late-novel passage
  demonstrates that ritual has become internalised — no longer a collective performance
  at Gault House but an individual pattern of perception that repeats in new
  circumstances.

> An outlet for a natural ferocity they were too proud to exercise, too indifferent to
> examine. Also a kind of ritual, a sacrifice, willing but impersonal to their gods.
> (001-009)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Ritual is the mechanism by which the Gault circle transforms raw experience into shared
meaning. The gramophone-playing is not a concert but an act of appeasement directed at a
quasi-sentient landscape; the sea-bathing is not recreation but "a kind of ritual, a
sacrifice"; the Grail game is not antiquarianism but a mode of participation in what they
call "big magic" (005-023, 008-045). In each case, ritual transforms ordinary actions
into acts of attention and relationship.

The novel's outsiders are defined by their inability to participate in ritual. Dudley
Carston, despite his eventual partial absorption into the household during the storm
chapter (007), never understands the ritual dimension of the group's life. He sees the
gramophone as entertainment, the Grail as an antique, and his own desire for Scylla as a
private campaign rather than a shared game. His exclusion from ritual is the structural
correlate of his exclusion from genuine belonging — and from the novel's circle of
initiation.

Ritual also structures the novel's treatment of the Sanc-Grail. The cup is never merely
an object; it is always a ritual object whose meaning is constituted by the practices that
surround it: discovery, display, loss, search, recovery. When Carston finds the cup in
his room (012-005), he treats it as evidence of a trick — a breach of hospitality — rather
than as a development within an ongoing ritual drama. Scylla's reply makes the ritual
frame explicit: "It is my cup. My lover who gave it me. We who have enjoyed it. … It is
he who will not play" (012-030).

### Literary / Symbolic Dimension

Butts's treatment of ritual participates in the modernist recovery of myth as a
structuring resource — comparable to Eliot's "mythical method" in *Ulysses* and *The Waste
Land*, but with a crucial difference. Where Eliot treats myth as an ironic commentary on
modern fragmentation, Butts treats ritual as a living practice. Her characters do not
merely allude to the Grail legend; they enact it through a gramophone on a verandah. The
juxtaposition is not ironic but generative — the Grail and the gramophone belong to the
same register of experience.

The phrase "rite produced by the imagination" (013-006) is the novel's most concentrated
statement of its own aesthetic. Ritual is not inherited from tradition or imposed by
authority; it is made, like art, by people who are "interested in the wood and its
silence" (001-003). This aligns Butts with a tradition of modernist occultism — Mathers,
Yeats, Fortune — that treats ritual as a technology of consciousness rather than a
religious obligation. The Gault household is, in its way, a magical order: a small group
bound by shared practice, attuned to non-human presences, and organised around a sacred
object.

The "sacred game" (008-034, 008-035) name is significant. The word "game" carries the
sense of rules, players, stakes, and agreed-upon fictions — but the adjective "sacred"
insists that the stakes are real. This is the novel's wager: that the most serious things
can only be approached through play, and that play without seriousness devolves into mere
entertainment — Carston's domain. The ritual life of Gault House exists in the charged
space between game and sacrament, between "if it's anything or nothing" (008-045) — a
zone of ontological uncertainty that the novel refuses to resolve.
