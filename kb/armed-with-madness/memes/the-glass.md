# The Glass

"Picus came over with the cup for Clarence to fill with whiskey and soda" (007-015). The drinking glass circulates through the novel's domestic scenes, an object of everyday ritual that mirrors the Grail without being the Grail. Pouring, passing, drinking — these actions structure the Gault House gatherings, and the glass is their instrument.

## Physical Description

The glass is ordinary — a vessel for whiskey and soda, sherry, whatever the house offers. Its ordinariness is its significance: the sacred vessel (the Grail) has an everyday counterpart (the glass), and the novel does not decide which matters more.

## Symbolic Meaning

The glass is the anti-Grail — the vessel of ordinary hospitality, holding whiskey rather than blood, passed among friends rather than guarded by a king. Its presence in the novel suggests that the sacred and the ordinary are not opposites but variations on a single form: the vessel that holds what we need.
