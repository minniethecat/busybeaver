# Memory

Memory in *Armed with Madness* is never a simple faculty of retrieval. It "produces imagination, and imagination is a state by itself" (006-051); it is "the Muses' mother" (006-051) — a classical genealogy that makes memory the origin of all art; it is a force that "no memory in that place ever had a straight edge" (010-006), curving and distorting the past even as it preserves it. Mary Butts treats memory not as a record but as an activity, one that is indistinguishable from fabrication, and whose relationship to truth is always oblique.

## Overview

Memory operates in the novel on two scales. On the **personal** scale, it is the faculty through which characters construct and maintain their sense of self. Carston's claim that "I have no memories" (006-050) is the novel's most damning self-indictment — to be without memory is to be without identity, without history, without the capacity for imagination. Felix's "twenty-three pictures of death" (020-022) constitute a memory-archive that has taught him something he cannot articulate. Clarence's "whole memory" of Scylla as a bird (033-112) returns in a flood of association that condenses the entire novel's imagery into a single moment of recognition.

On the **mythic** scale, memory is the novel's figure for cultural transmission — the "blood" memory of the Grail, the inherited knowledge of ritual, the ancestral fear that predates individual experience. This is memory as tradition, as the persistence of the sacred in a secular age. When the novel speaks of memory as "the Muses' mother," it invokes the pre-modern understanding of memory as the ground of all making — poetry, music, history — and implicitly indicts a modernity that has severed itself from this ground.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Memory enters the novel in its first chapter as a unifying force: "The pleasant memory united them; they became a triumvirate" (001-023). This is memory at its most benign — a shared recollection that binds individuals into a temporary community. The memory in question is the recollection of a pleasant day, and its effect is immediate and social. From this first appearance, the novel will track memory's progressive complication: from shared to solitary, from pleasant to painful, from unifying to disintegrating.

### Key Scenes

- **"I have no memories" (Ch. VI)**: When Carston declares to Scylla that "I have no memories" (006-050), he is not making a factual claim but revealing a spiritual condition. To have no memories, in the novel's economy, is to have no imagination, no depth, no access to the mythic substrate that sustains the other characters. Scylla's response — "Memory was the Muses' mother" (006-051) — is a kind of diagnosis: Carston is a modern man, cut off from the source.

- **The American poet and the key (Ch. VI)**: Carston's persistent question — "what the American poet meant when he said that memory had the key?" (006-062) — goes unanswered in the scene. Scylla deflects with a quotation, and the conversation moves on. But the question lingers: if memory has the key, what does it unlock? The novel's answer, distributed across its thirty-three chapters, is that memory unlocks the past only by transforming it — that the "key" opens not a vault of preserved facts but a workshop of perpetual remaking.

- **"Another memory" (Ch. VI)**: The novel's most austere use of the word appears as a standalone paragraph: "Another memory" (006-055). This is memory reduced to its barest syntactic form — a noun and its modifier, a fact and its acknowledgment. The memory in question is not narrated; we are told only that it is "another," one in a series whose contents remain private. The effect is to shift memory from narrative object to narrative technique — memory not as what is remembered but as the act of remembering itself.

- **Memory without a straight edge (Ch. X)**: The narrative observes of Gault House that "no memory in that place ever had a straight edge" (010-006). This is memory spatialized — the crooked corridors of the house, its unexpected passages and concealed rooms, externalize the structure of memory itself. To remember in such a place is to follow a path that curves, that doubles back, that leads to rooms one did not know existed.

- **Cutting out memory and hope (Ch. XXV)**: The novel's most violent characterization of memory appears as a surgical metaphor: "the clean surgery to cut out memory and hope" (025-017). Memory and hope are twinned — both are temporal orientations, one facing backward, one forward — and both are figured as diseased tissue that must be excised. This is the limit of the novel's skepticism about memory: it can become a sickness, a weight that prevents action rather than enabling it.

- **"On the edge of whose memory he was living" (Ch. XXXIII)**: Picus's final state is described as living "on the edge of whose memory he was living" (033-050), a sentence whose recursive syntax mimics the vertigo it describes. To live on the edge of a memory is to be neither fully inside the past nor fully outside it, to inhabit a threshold where identity dissolves.

> "Memory was the Muses' mother, and the Muses we know, song, were the daughters of the imagination." (006-051)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Memory in *Armed with Madness* is the faculty that makes relationship possible. The characters who can remember — Scylla, Felix, Clarence — can love, can grieve, can change. The characters who cannot — Carston, Picus — are trapped in a perpetual present that is indistinguishable from narcissism. Carston's "I have no memories" is the confession of a man who cannot be wounded because he cannot be attached. Picus's relationship to the past is purely instrumental: his mother's death, the Grail legend, the Gault estate — all are materials for self-justification rather than objects of genuine recollection.

The novel's most radical claim about memory is that it is not opposed to truth but is the only route to it. "Memory was the Muses' mother" — the line that Scylla offers Carston — means that without memory there is no art, no understanding, no access to the patterns that organize experience. The modern condition, as the novel diagnoses it, is not that we remember too little but that we remember without imagination — that we store data without transforming it, that we have "memories" without having memory.

### Modernist / Literary Dimension

Butts's treatment of memory places her at the center of the modernist preoccupation with time and consciousness. The novel's most obvious intertext is Proust's *In Search of Lost Time* (1913–1927), whose final volumes were appearing as Butts wrote. Like Proust, Butts understands memory as involuntary, embodied, and creative rather than archival. The "pleasant memory" that unites the triumvirate (001-023) is a Proustian moment of shared recollection that creates community out of contingency.

But the novel's deepest engagement with memory is with Eliot's *The Waste Land* (1922), where "memory and desire" are the forces that April — the cruelest month — stirs from the dead land. Butts reverses Eliot's valuation: where Eliot sees memory as a torment that spring cruelly revives, Butts sees it as the Muses' mother, the origin of art and the ground of relationship. To be without memory, in Butts's world, is to be truly dead — not the living death of the Fisher King but the blank death of a consciousness without history.
