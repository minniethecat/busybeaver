# Gault

Gault is not a house but a place — the name encompasses the house, the cliff, the wood, the stream, the entire stretch of Dorset coast where the novel's action concentrates. "He had been to a place called Gault, and he'd sung to it" (003-038). The verb matters: one does not visit Gault, one goes to it, as to a shrine or a battlefield. The place exerts a gravitational pull that the novel registers through the characters' repeated returns to it from Paris, from London, from Starn. It is the spatial correlative of the Grail quest — the territory that must be traversed for the sacred object to be found.

## Description

### Gault House

The house itself is barely described in architectural terms. Butts's method is atmospheric rather than documentary: we know the verandah where Clarence slept (005-017), the rooms where the Freud game was played (006-010), the upstairs where Picus was ill (004-006). The house is less a building than a container of psychological states — each room charged with the emotional residue of its occupants. "You've been staying over at Gault House with the Taverners" (015-005): the single external reference to the house by name positions it as the Taverners' domain, the family seat that is also the novel's stage.

### Gault Cliff

"Gault Cliff hung over them, a terror" (011-018). The cliff is the novel's most imposing natural feature — a "terrible crag" whose geological impassivity mocks the characters' human dramas. Yet Scylla's fear of the cliff is cured by identification: "That took away her fear of Gault. For the terrible crag was something that looked like Picus asleep" (011-037). Landscape and character fuse; the cliff becomes a figure for Picus himself.

### The Wood below Gault

"In the unvisited wood under Gault, where the low trees grow" (011-008). The wood is the novel's threshold space — neither house nor cliff, neither cultivated nor wild, the place where transformations occur. "Under Gault Cliff there had been no parody" (013-006): the wood below the cliff is where the novel's mythic register operates without irony.

> "He had been to a place called Gault, and he'd sung to it." (003-038)

## Narrative Function

### Events at Gault

- **The Freud game**: The characters' psychoanalytic parlor game is played in the house's rooms, making Gault House the laboratory for the novel's intellectual investigations (006-010).
- **The doctor's visit**: The doctor from Starn comes to Gault House to attend to Picus, and his diagnosis — "Parsifal" — transforms the house into a Grail castle (006-001, 006-022).
- **The cliff revelation**: Scylla's vision of Picus in the cliff resolves her fear and establishes the novel's mode of mythic perception (011-037).
- **The cup's discovery**: "A cup in a well: in a house" (025-041) — the Grail is found at Gault, making the place not merely a setting but the quest's destination.

### Symbolic Meaning

Gault is the modern Grail castle — not the medieval Monsalvat of Wagner's *Parsifal* but a Dorset country house where "better magic" (033-034) operates. The cliff, the wood, and the house form a vertical axis: cliff (the sublime, the terrifying, Zeus), wood (the liminal, the transformative), house (the domestic, the psychological). The Grail quest moves downward through this axis, from the terrifying to the domestic to the sacred.

## Character Associations

- **The Taverners (Scylla, Felix)**: Gault is their ancestral home. Scylla's identification with the place — her fear of the cliff, her comfort in the house — makes her the novel's genius loci.
- **Picus**: He is identified with Gault: "Picus at home: under Gault" (025-041). The cliff that looks like Picus asleep (011-037) makes him a feature of the landscape.
- **Clarence**: He comes to Gault as Picus's companion and works while others play. His cottage on Tollerdown is a satellite of Gault.
- **Ross**: A local, the one who knows Gault's geography and its dangers — the drowned sailors, the treacherous currents.

> "I have been living inside a work of art" (014-004)
