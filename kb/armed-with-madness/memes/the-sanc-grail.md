# The Sanc-Grail

A shallow greenish glass dish, discovered in a Saxon well-shaft on the downs near Tollerdown, around which the spiritual and psychological life of the Gault House circle revolves. In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness* (1928), the Sanc-Grail functions simultaneously as a physical artifact, a catalyst for collective delusion, and a modernist interrogation of myth itself — the Grail story that "has never come off, or found its form or its poet" (013-003).

## Overview

The Sanc-Grail appears across at least 10 of the novel's 33 chapters, from its introduction in Chapter IV through the climactic confrontations of Chapter XXXI. It is never confirmed to be the actual Holy Grail; rather, the novel treats its ontological status as secondary to the psychological and social effects it produces. The object passes through multiple hands — Picus, Felix, Carston, Clarence, and the elder Mr. Tracy — each projecting onto it a different configuration of desire, cynicism, reverence, and greed.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The Sanc-Grail is first mentioned by Felix in conversation with Carston, who is told that Picus "had dug it up in a well-shaft on the downs. Bronze-Age. And it was the cup of the Sanc-Grail" (004-019). Felix immediately contextualizes it within Arthurian mythology — "It and the spear, they always hunted in couples" — before deflating the mysticism with Freudian reduction: "All sexual symbolism. I wish I hadn't" (004-019).

### Key Scenes

- **Felix's disclosure to Carston (Ch. IV)**: Felix presents the cup's discovery as a blend of archaeology and obsession. He warns that "I never heard of the Sanc-Grail doing anyone any good. With that moron Picus behind it, and that demon, my sister, in front of it" (004-021). Carston's reaction is to catalogue "what he remembered of the Grail story, the possibility of anyone behaving as if it had happened, and what that implied in human character" (004-022).

- **Picus's dream (Ch. V)**: Picus dreams of carrying a glass dish that is the Grail, intoning: "It's the *lapis exilii*, the stone of exile. What I'm walking on is the *lapis exilis*, the slender stone" (005-019). The dream reflects his own uncertainty — "no grail texts were clear what the thing was really called" — and his private impulse to rename it the *lapis exultationis*, "the stone of joy" (005-019).

- **The whiskey-and-soda desecration (Ch. VII)**: At a party, Picus uses the cup to serve whiskey, prompting a guest to observe: "He doesn't mind using the cup of the Sanc-Grail for whiskey and soda" (007-015). This profane use enacts the novel's central tension between sacred aspiration and mundane reality.

- **Clarence's skeptical diagnosis (Ch. XVII)**: Clarence dismisses the Grail quest as a recurring delusion: "Looking for the Sanc-Grail. It's always the same story. The Golden Fleece or the philosopher's stone, or perpetual motion, or Atlantis or the lost tribes or God. All ways of walking into the same trap" (017-016). Yet he also acknowledges that "what sounded romantic excitement about the Sanc-Grail cup was real" (017-023).

- **Mr. Tracy's summation (Ch. XXXI)**: In the novel's most explicit mythological reading, Mr. Tracy declares the events "true Sanc-Grail" — "A find, illumination, doubt, and division, collective and then dispersed. A land enchanted and disenchanted with the rapidity of a cinema. Adventures. Danger and awe and love" (031-063). He concludes with an image of convergence: "The grail knights are gathering, it seems" (031-073).

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

The Sanc-Grail is less a sacred relic than a Rorschach test for the Gault House circle. For Picus, it is a prop in an aesthetic-erotic game; for Felix, a symbol of the lost spiritual order he both longs for and mocks; for Scylla, an object that draws her into a network of male desire and manipulation; for Carston, an intrusion of unreason into a life of cautious propriety. The cup's ambiguous authenticity mirrors the characters' own uncertain identities — exiles from a coherent mythic world, inhabiting a modernity that can only parody the sacred.

### Mythic/Symbolic Dimension

Butts explicitly frames the Sanc-Grail within the Arthurian Grail tradition while systematically undermining it. The novel references the *lapis exilii* (stone of exile) from Wolfram von Eschenbach's *Parzival*, but Picus's dream collapses sacred etymology into phonetic play. Scylla's reflection that "the whole Grail story, the saga story *par excellence* that has never come off, or found its form or its poet" (013-003) identifies the Grail legend itself as a failed narrative — a myth that, unlike the *Odyssey* or the Golden Fleece, remains incomplete. This incompletion is precisely what makes it available for modernist appropriation: the Grail quest becomes a figure for the unfulfillable spiritual longing of a post-war generation.

### Critical Perspectives

The Sanc-Grail in *Armed with Madness* has been read as Butts's engagement with the Grail revival in early twentieth-century modernism, alongside Eliot's *The Waste Land* and David Jones's *In Parenthesis*. Where Eliot uses the Grail as a structuring absence, Butts makes its physical presence — the greenish glass dish — the source of the novel's centrifugal psychological energy. The cup's ultimate fate (it disappears, "Grail vanished, girl and all" (020-035)) suggests that the object itself is less important than the "ritual" of collective belief it catalyzes.
