# The Ice

"He dreamed that he was in a shell-hole and someone had given him an ice" (005-017). The ice is the dream's central object — cold, sweet, impossible. In a shell-hole, in the middle of war, someone offers ice cream. The absurdity is the point: the dream's logic places comfort inside catastrophe, sweetness inside destruction. The ice is the object that should not be there, and its presence makes the dream at once more bearable and more terrible.

## Symbolic Meaning

The ice is the anti-Grail — the vessel of comfort that is not sacred but secular, not eternal but ephemeral, melting even as it is received. It promises no transformation, only a moment of sweetness in a shell-hole. The novel's theology, such as it is, may be contained in this image: grace arrives as ice cream in a war zone, and you take it before it melts.
