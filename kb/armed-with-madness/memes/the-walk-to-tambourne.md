# The Walk to Tambourne

"She started over the hill to walk seventy miles inland to Tambourne where Picus might be" (029-012). The walk is at once literal and impossible — seventy miles is a multiple-day journey, not an afternoon's impulse. The subjunctive quality of the event (she starts; we do not see her arrive) makes the walk a figure for the novel's central tension: the impulse to act and the difficulty of completion.

## Prelude

Scylla is at Gault. Picus is at Tambourne — or might be. The uncertainty ("where Picus might be") is the walk's engine: she is walking toward a possibility, not a certainty. The seventy-mile distance is the novel's measure of the gap between the characters.

> "She started over the hill to walk seventy miles inland to Tambourne where Picus might be." (029-012)

## The Event

The walk is narrated in a single sentence. Its brevity is the point: the impulse to walk, the enormity of the distance, and the absence of narration of the walking itself make the event a hinge between intention and action. Whether she arrives is less important than that she starts.
