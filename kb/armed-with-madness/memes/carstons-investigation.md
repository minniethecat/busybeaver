# Carston's Investigation

Carston becomes an investigator by accident. "I've been trying to see you" (031-003): his approach to Tracy is at once a social call and an interrogation. The American outsider — the one character not initiated into the Gault House circle's assumptions — is the only one who can ask the question the others have been avoiding: is the cup real? His investigation of Tracy occupies the novel's late chapters, transforming the Grail quest into detective fiction.

## Prelude

The cup has been found. Tracy has vanished and returned. The Gault House circle is dispersed. Carston, alone among the characters, refuses to accept the mystery as mystery. His American pragmatism — his assumption that questions have answers — drives him to investigate.

## The Event

### The Search

Carston visits Tracy's house (026-003, 030-001), searches his room, examines his collection. The search is at once physical (objects, books, antiquities) and intellectual (motive, authenticity, belief).

### The Confrontation

"I've been trying to see you" (031-003). Carston's confrontation with Tracy is the novel's climactic interview. The old man, cornered, offers not confession but transformation: "old Mr. Tracy turned saint" (031-047).

> "I've been trying to see you." (031-003)

## Aftermath

- **Tracy's transformation**: Under Carston's questioning, Tracy becomes something other than what he was — "turned saint" (031-047).
- **The unresolved investigation**: "How do you think you'll get away with it?" (031-057). Carston's final question goes unanswered. The investigation succeeds in producing revelation but not resolution.
