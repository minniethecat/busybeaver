# Nanna

Nanna is the novel's most grounded presence. While the main characters pursue the Grail, experience visions, and unravel psychologically, Nanna makes jam. Her kitchen is the novel's centering point — the place where food is prepared, fires are tended, and the body's needs assert themselves against the mind's extravagances. "Trust Nanna" (018-027) is Scylla's benediction, and it doubles as the novel's own admission: when the symbolic order threatens to dissolve, Nanna remains.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full name and aliases**: Known only as "Nanna" — the name itself suggests a familial role (nurse/grandmother figure) without the formality of a surname. She is defined by function rather than lineage.
- **Circle**: Gault House — permanently stationed at the kitchen hearth, the domestic center that all characters pass through
- **Key relationships**:
  - With Scylla: mutual respect. Scylla trusts Nanna's judgment and protects her from the worst excesses of the household's drama. "Trust Nanna" (018-027) is Scylla's highest compliment.
  - With Clarence: kitchen partnership. Clarence gives "Nanna a hand skinning the rabbits" (018-002) — a domestic alliance between the two working-class members of the household.
  - With Ross: an affair is rumored — "Ross having an affair with Nanna" (025-061). Whether true or gossip, the speculation marks Nanna as a figure of desire as well as service.
  - With Picus: Nanna handles Picus's intrusions with practical indifference. "Trust Nanna. She almost put Mr. Picus's fat in the fire" (016-020) — her near-sabotage of Picus is both comic and pointed.

## Character and Thematic Function

Nanna embodies the wisdom of the ordinary. In a novel obsessed with the Grail — an object of transcendent meaning — Nanna represents the sacredness of the everyday: food cooked, fires lit, linens darned. Her function is to keep the body alive while the spirit goes adventuring.

- **Hearth as center**: "Nanna's fire" (018-029) is the novel's gravitational center. "There the sea bubbled" — the kitchen fire becomes a second sea, a domestic ocean as elemental as the Channel outside. All nourishment, literal and symbolic, passes through Nanna's domain.
- **Folk wisdom against esoteric knowledge**: "Tell the bees. Nanna did that when one of them died" (016-023). Nanna practices the old rituals — not the Grail's high esoterica but the folk customs of rural Dorset. Her knowledge is oral, embodied, female.
- **Servant as moral witness**: Nanna sees everything and says little. Her "horror-story" (022-005) — the tale she tells that makes Ross grin — suggests a narrative intelligence that the main characters, preoccupied with their own dramas, consistently underestimate.
- **Jam-making as resistance**: "So Nanna was making jam. Felix was paring apples" (032-004). While the household convulses with Lydia's letter and Carston's investigation, Nanna makes jam. The activity is not escape but assertion — the world continues, the body needs feeding, the ordinary is not less real than the extraordinary.

> "Trust Nanna" — Scylla (018-027). Two words that encapsulate Nanna's role: the person you trust when you cannot trust anyone else.

## Narrative Arc

### Everyday Presence

Nanna appears throughout the novel, always in relation to the kitchen, the fire, the food. Her narrative arc is not one of transformation — she does not change — but of constancy. She is the character who remains the same while everyone around her transforms, unravels, or departs.

### The Affair Rumor

The suggestion of an affair with Ross (025-061) momentarily pulls Nanna into the novel's central erotic dynamics. But the rumor passes without confirmation or denial, leaving Nanna's private life as opaque as her public function is transparent.

### The Final Pages

At the novel's close, "Ross and Nanna left the house scented with beeswax and lavender" (033-155) — Nanna's domestic labor perfumes the ending. She is not the Grail's guardian but the house's, and in Mary Butts's cosmology, that may be the more sacred office.
