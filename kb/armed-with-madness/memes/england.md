# England

England in *Armed with Madness* is everywhere and nowhere — the unstated container of the novel's geography, the national identity that the characters inhabit so completely they rarely need to name it. When they do, it is with the casual possessiveness of the native: "the English" (multiple references) are a type, a way of being, a set of assumptions about landscape and behavior that the novel simultaneously embodies and critiques.

## Description

England is rendered through its local particulars — Dorset, Gault, the heath — rather than through abstraction. The novel's method is synecdochic: the part (a Dorset country house) stands for the whole (a nation recovering from war). Butts's England is not the imperial England of Kipling or the industrial England of Lawrence but an older, stranger England of cliffs and woods and Grail quests — an England that predates Englishness.

> "God! What a beautiful place" (003-003)

## Narrative Function

### Englishness as Identity

The characters' Englishness is most visible in contrast. Carston, the American, is defined against it; Boris, the Russian, exists outside it; Paris and Biarritz are its cosmopolitan alternatives. Englishness is the unmarked category — the norm that the novel's outsiders reveal by deviating from.

### The Post-War Landscape

The novel, published in 1928, is set in the aftermath of the Great War. The Dorset landscape — ancient, enduring, indifferent to human catastrophe — is England as survival. The Grail quest, with its medieval English associations, is a claim on a national myth that the war had rendered suspect.
