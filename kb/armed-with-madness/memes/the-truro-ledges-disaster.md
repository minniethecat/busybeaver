# The Truro Ledges Disaster

Ross knows about drowned men. "Twenty-three drowned men laid out on tables" (020-001): the image is precise and terrible, the bodies arranged like exhibits, the sea's harvest displayed for identification. The Truro Ledges disaster is the novel's dark counterpoint to the Grail quest — the real drowning that shadows the symbolic immersion, the death by water that the Grail's living water cannot redeem.

## Prelude

Ross is a sailor. His local knowledge includes the sea's dangers — the currents, the ledges, the places where ships go down. The drowned men are part of that knowledge: the sea he navigates is also a grave.

## The Event

The disaster is never narrated — only its aftermath. Twenty-three men, drowned, laid out. The number is specific; the cause is not. The disaster exists in the novel as Ross's knowledge, a burden he carries that the other characters, preoccupied with their Grail quest, cannot share.
