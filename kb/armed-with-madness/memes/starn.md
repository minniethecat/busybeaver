# Starn

Starn sits on its hill, visible from Gault, a fixed point in a landscape of shifting psychological states. "Far inland to Starn on its hill, the hub of the district" (002-011). The phrase is precise: a hub is what a wheel turns on, and Starn is the still center around which the novel's Dorset geography rotates. From Gault Cliff you can see Starn; from Starn you can see the sea. It is the place where the ordinary world — doctors, posts, strangers — touches the extraordinary world of Gault.

## Description

Starn is described less through its own features than through the view of it from elsewhere. "From this one Starn was invisible, and the eventualities" (009-003) — Starn's visibility is an index of the characters' orientation. When Starn is visible, the world is legible; when it vanishes behind "high banks" and "airy roofs" (009-008), the characters are lost.

The town functions as a portal: "They took him to Starn and showed it him: as if it was theirs" (008-003). The possessive gesture — the locals showing Starn to the outsider — makes the town a curated object, a display of belonging.

> "And not to Starn with me?" (002-007) — Scylla's invitation, casual but loaded. To go to Starn is to leave Gault; to stay is to remain within the mythic field.

> "Starn was a house of great age" (002-035)

## Narrative Function

### Events at Starn

- **The stranger from Starn**: "Scylla to fetch the stranger from Starn" (005-015). Starn is the source of visitors who disrupt and reveal Gault's internal dynamics.
- **The doctor's origin**: "The doctor having come over from Starn to attend to Picus's health" (006-001). The doctor is the most consequential Starn export — his diagnosis of Picus through Wagner's *Parsifal* transforms a medical visit into a mythic event.
- **Navigation by Starn**: "They can steer by Starn" (008-063). The town is a navigational aid, a fixed point for orientation when the characters are lost in the Dorset landscape.

### Symbolic Meaning

Starn represents the ordinary world that Gault exists in relation to but cannot be reduced to. It is the "hub" — the center of the district's practical life, the place where doctors come from and strangers arrive at. Its relationship to Gault is that of the secular to the sacred: Starn is where you go for medical attention; Gault is where you discover that what you need is not medicine but myth.

## Character Associations

- **The doctor**: Starn is his base; his journey from Starn to Gault is a crossing from the medical to the mythic.
- **Scylla**: She knows the road to Starn (002-007); her invitation to go there is an invitation to leave the mythic field.
- **Carston**: The American to whom Starn is shown "as if it was theirs" (008-003) — the outsider being inducted into local geography.
- **Ross**: A local who can "steer by Starn" (008-063), whose knowledge of the district includes its navigational markers.
