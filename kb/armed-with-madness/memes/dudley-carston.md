# Dudley Carston

Dudley Carston arrives at Gault House as a tourist and leaves as a fugitive. In between, he pursues Scylla, competes with Picus, misreads every social signal he encounters, and produces — in his failure — the novel's most damning portrait of the modern sensibility: a man who has "no memories" (006-050), who aestheticizes what he cannot understand, and who flees the moment perception demands something of him. Mary Butts uses Carston as a diagnostic tool: his incomprehension illuminates what the other characters take for granted, and his eventual flight demonstrates what it costs to live without the capacity for genuine attention.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full name and aliases**: Dudley Carston — "Carston" to the Gault House circle, "the American" in the novel's implicit taxonomy of national types
- **Circle**: Outsider — a visitor to Gault House, never integrated into its rhythms
- **Key relationships**:
  - With Scylla: romantic pursuit that reveals his incapacity for love. Carston wants Scylla as he wants the Dorset landscape — as an aesthetic experience to be consumed. "To make love to her would be too hard work" (004-003).
  - With Picus: rivalry that reveals Carston's puritanism. He sees Picus's beauty and immediately moralizes it — "his old feeling about sin and fleshy lust" (010-017). The erotic becomes, for Carston, an occasion for judgment rather than experience.
  - With Felix: incomprehension. Felix's "fear" of him (011-037) is the novel's most direct verdict on Carston's effect on others.
  - With Ross: mutual indifference. Ross's stoic acceptance of the world's terms is the opposite of Carston's need to transform experience into aesthetic object.

> "Slightly overdoing the beauty business" (003-008)

## Character and Thematic Function

Carston is the novel's control group — the character whose responses measure what the other characters have that he lacks. His function is to fail to understand, and to make his failure visible.

- **The tourist's gaze**: Carston's first words about Gault House are "God! What a beautiful place" (003-003). The exclamation is sincere, but sincerity is not enough. The narrator's immediate editorial — "When 'beautiful' is said, exactly and honestly, there is contact, or there should be" (003-003) — establishes the gap between saying and seeing. Carston says the right words; he does not make contact.

- **Memorylessness**: "I have no memories," Carston tells Scylla (006-050). This is the novel's most damning self-indictment. To be without memory, in Butts's economy, is to be without imagination, without depth, without access to the mythic substrate that sustains the other characters. Scylla's response — "Memory was the Muses' mother" (006-051) — is a diagnosis delivered as a lesson.

- **Aestheticization as flight**: Carston's culminating insight is a retreat. Looking back at Gault House from the down-crest, he sees "the earth was a map of naked beauty he saw in the piece and understood. 'I've been living inside a work of art'" (014-004). This is perception transformed into distance — the tourist's final victory over experience. He has comprehended the landscape by turning it into a picture, and in doing so he has made it safe to leave.

> "I have no memories." (006-050)

## Narrative Arc

### Arrival: The Tourist

Carston arrives at Gault House as "Dudley Carston," an American visitor bringing "a human scene" (003-024) — that is, bringing the expectation that scenes are for human consumption. His immediate response to the landscape is aesthetic appreciation: beauty named, admired, and thereby neutralized. The Gault House circle receives him with the tolerance accorded to guests who do not know they are being evaluated.

### Pursuit: The Lover Who Cannot Love

Carston's pursuit of Scylla follows the logic of acquisition rather than desire. He wants her because Picus wants her; his desire is mimetic in Girard's sense, triangular, mediated by a rival. "Make love to Scylla, thought Carston," prompted by a gramophone record (007-017) — love as a recorded song, as something that plays on repeat without variation. His declaration "I love you" (007-023) is a response to Scylla's demand "To get you to say" (007-022) — love as speech act, sincerity rendered irrelevant by context.

### Flight: The Aesthete's Exit

Carston flees Gault House after the Grail cup crisis, physically sickened by what he has witnessed. His final vision — the landscape as "a work of art" — is his vindication and his damnation. He has understood the place perfectly, and his understanding consists of turning it into something he can leave behind. The novel's last word on Carston is his absence: he is the character who cannot stay, and whose departure reveals the depth of what the others remain to face.

## Character Type: The Modern Man

Carston is Butts's portrait of the modern type — the man who has survived the war without being wounded by it, who travels without being transformed by travel, who pursues love without risking himself in love. His American nationality is not incidental: he represents a nation that entered the war late and emerged prosperous, whose relationship to European trauma is touristic. His inability to remember is the novel's diagnosis of a civilization that has lost the capacity for myth, for ritual, for the kind of attention that transforms the perceiver as much as the perceived.
