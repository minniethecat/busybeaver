# Lydia's Wedding Ring

"She had a last look at [[Lydia]], twisting her wedding-ring" (025-064). The gesture is minute — a twist of metal on flesh — but it condenses Lydia's situation into a single image. Observed by [[Scylla Taverner]] during the [[paris|Paris]] party, the ring is the sign of Lydia's marriage ("a dangerous one," 025-010), the object she cannot remove and cannot stop worrying at. Twisting is what you do with something that is both on you and against you — a gesture that encodes the tension between [[love]] and constraint that runs through the novel's treatment of marriage.

## Physical Description

The ring is not described beyond its function: it is a wedding ring. Its material (gold, presumably) is conventional; its meaning is what the novel interrogates. Like [[the-cup|the Cup]] — the novel's other central object — the ring's physical form is withheld, its significance displaced onto the gestures and affects it generates.

> "She had a last look at Lydia, twisting her wedding-ring." (025-064)

> "But then the door opened and it was Lydia herself, with her too-short frock and her practical brain and her dangerous marriage." (025-010)

## Symbolic Meaning

The wedding ring is the novel's most concentrated image of constrained feminine desire. It is at once ornament and fetter, symbol of union and of imprisonment. Lydia's twisting — repetitive, unconscious, irrepressible — is the body's protest against what the ring represents. In a novel structured around the discovery of a sacred object ([[the-cup|the Cup]]), the ring is the profane counterpart: an everyday object whose meaning is no less powerful and no less contested. Where the Cup generates quest and investigation, the ring generates a single gesture — but that gesture contains a whole world of unspoken [[fear]] and [[love]].
