# The Game

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness* (1928), "the Game" is the circle's signature social practice — a self-conscious fusion of Freudian free-association parlor play, aesthetic repartee, and ritualised role-playing that governs interaction at Gault House. It operates as both a mode of freedom (play as sacred life) and a technique of evasion (play as refusal of genuine encounter), structuring the narrative's central tension between aesthetic liberation and psychological avoidance.

## Overview

The Game runs through the novel from the circle's first indoor gatherings (Chapter 6) to the late-phase crises (Chapter 31, Chapter 33), spanning the full narrative arc and involving every major character: Picus, Felix, Carston, Clarence, Scylla, and Ross. It is at once a social institution, an aesthetic philosophy, and a psychological defence mechanism. Butts presents it not as a single fixed practice but as a family of related behaviours — the Freud game, the sacred game, the game of repartee and wit, the game of "playing us up," and the game of making a virtue of poverty. Each variant reveals a different facet of the circle's relationship to play: as self-knowledge, as ecstatic experience, as social performance, and as flight from the Real.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The Freud game is named explicitly at the start of Chapter 6, when Felix calls on the circle to perform their signature parlour ritual of psychoanalytic free association: "The Freud game really. Start, Felix!" (006-010). The adverb "really" signals that this game is already an established practice — a known social form that needs only invocation and a starting player. Earlier references to Freud in Chapters 3 and 5 ("Good old Freud," 003-048; "Freud again," 005-019) set the intellectual terrain on which this game is played, but it is at 006-010 that the practice crystallises into a named ritual.

### Key Scenes

- **The Freud Game Begins (Chapter 6)**: Felix initiates the game of free association, and the circle performs a self-conscious psychoanalytic exercise — interpreting dreams, slips, and memories for one another's amusement and edification. The game draws equally on Freud and Aquinas as reference points: "And between Freud and Aquinas, I've managed to get a clear picture" (006-039). This fusion of psychoanalysis and scholasticism is typical of the circle's eclectic intellectual style.

- **Picus Declares the Sacred Game (Chapter 8)**: In a pivotal exchange, Picus articulates the aesthetic philosophy underlying the game. He tells Scylla: "This is my pleasure and my game. And Clarence is unreasonable —" (008-034). A moment earlier the game is named "the sacred game" (008-034), and Picus presses the point: "…when it is life as the sacred game. And it isn't his game here" (008-035). For Picus, the game is not an escape from life but life itself raised to the intensity of play — an aestheticised existence in which pleasure and form are inseparable. Clarence's failure to play correctly is not a moral failure but an aesthetic one: he does not understand that life is the game.

- **Game as Ecstatic Action (Chapter 13)**: Scylla, alone, reflects on loss and consolation through the logic of the game: "Chances of the sacred game" (013-007). The chapter continues: "Swept off into stadium of the game; which is the pleasure in actions for their own sake. Done for the love of playing. Done for the fun of it" (013-008). Here the game becomes a metaphysical principle — a zone of pure play where action has no end beyond itself, recalling Huizinga's concept of *homo ludens* avant la lettre.

- **The Freud Game Returns — Philoctetes (Chapter 31)**: Late in the novel, the Freud game resurfaces with a darker inflection. "Playing the Freud game, the name rose 'Philoctetes.' Just the sort of role Clarence would choose" (031-032). The game now assigns mythic roles, revealing underlying character structures. Clarence is cast as the wounded outcast hero — an identification that exposes, rather than conceals, real psychological truth. The game has turned from evasion into diagnosis.

- **Playing at Happy Warriors (Chapter 33)**: In the novel's final chapter, "She and Picus alone together, playing at happy warriors" (033-149). This last appearance of playing/game imagery marks a resolution of sorts — play as shared intimacy, no longer ironic or evasive but a mutual performance of resilience.

> "…when it is life as the sacred game. And it isn't his game here" (008-035)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

For the characters, the Game serves multiple and contradictory functions. On one level, it is a mode of social bonding and intellectual display — the circle's way of converting idle conversation into an aesthetic practice. The Freud game (006-010) channels the group's considerable self-awareness into a structured form, preventing introspection from becoming mere self-indulgence. Picus elevates this practice into a philosophy: life lived as the sacred game (008-035), where pleasure, form, and meaning converge. This is the game at its most life-affirming — not an escape from reality but reality transfigured by play.

On another level, the game is evasion. When Carston and Picus are said to be "playing us up" (016-060), the phrasing suggests manipulation — using the game's forms to manage and misdirect others. The game of making poverty aesthetic ("they made a game of their poverty," 006-061) aestheticises real deprivation, converting material lack into a stylistic posture. And the dismissal of any "'service of humanity' game" (017-026) reveals the circle's defensive irony: the game becomes a way of pre-emptively refusing engagement with the world beyond Gault House.

The tension between these two functions — play as liberation and play as flight — drives much of the novel's psychological drama. Clarence, who cannot play the game (008-035), is both the circle's truth-teller and its outsider; Scylla, who learns to play, grows through the game into self-knowledge. The game both binds and excludes, reveals and conceals.

### Literary / Symbolic Dimension

Butts's "the Game" participates in a modernist preoccupation with the aesthetics of play. It anticipates Johan Huizinga's *Homo Ludens* (1938) in its insistence that play is not the opposite of seriousness but seriousness transfigured. When Scylla is "swept off into stadium of the game; which is the pleasure in actions for their own sake" (013-008), the language is Kantian (purposiveness without purpose) and Nietzschean (the innocence of becoming, play as the highest form of human activity).

The game also functions as a secularised ritual. Where the Sanc-Grail (the cup used for whiskey and soda, 007-015) represents the circle's ironic relationship to the sacred, the Game represents their sincere relationship to play-as-ritual. The two symbols are complementary: the Grail is the sacred deflated into the domestic; the Game is the domestic elevated to the sacred.

The Freudian dimension is equally significant. By converting psychoanalysis into a parlour game (006-010), Butts's characters perform what psychoanalysis itself might diagnose: the domestication of the uncanny, the taming of the unconscious into social performance. The return of the Freud game in Chapter 31, when it assigns Clarence the role of Philoctetes — the wounded hero abandoned on Lemnos, whose bow is nevertheless essential to victory — suggests that the game ultimately cannot be contained as mere entertainment. It speaks truth despite itself.
