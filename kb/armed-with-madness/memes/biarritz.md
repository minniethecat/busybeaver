# Biarritz

Biarritz appears in *Armed with Madness* as a counterfactual — the place the characters might be if they were not at Gault. "Why can't we be at Biarritz leading a reasonable life?" (009-034). The question is rhetorical; the answer is the novel.

Biarritz represents the life not chosen: sun, leisure, "shares" (017-014), the reasonable pleasures of the modern European bourgeoisie. But the novel's argument is that Biarritz would be the same as Gault — "It would have been the same at Biarritz" (017-015) — because the problem is not the place but the quest.

## Description

Biarritz is never described directly; it exists only as an idea, a name that signifies escape. "Shares and we'd have been at Biarritz" (017-014): the conditional — "would have been" — is Biarritz's narrative mode. It is the place of the irrealis, the life that might have been lived if the Grail had not intervened.

> "Why can't we be at Biarritz leading a reasonable life?" (009-034)

## Narrative Function

### The Counterfactual

Biarritz is the novel's only sustained counterfactual space. Its function is to pose the question the novel exists to answer: why not a reasonable life? The answer, played out across thirty-three chapters, is that the reasonable life is not reasonable enough — that the Grail will not let its seekers settle for Biarritz.

### Modernity's Resort

"Biarritz, bars. What every little boy in a Paris bar" (020-030). Biarritz is linked to Paris, to bars, to the modern world of pleasure without quest. It is the spatial correlative of the life the Gault House characters have ostensibly rejected but cannot stop invoking.
