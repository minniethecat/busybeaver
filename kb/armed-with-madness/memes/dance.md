# Dance

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness*, dance is not mere entertainment or social form but a
primary language of the body — a way of knowing, displaying, and exerting power that
distinguishes those who belong to the novel's mythic world from those who are merely
spectators.

## Overview

Dance appears throughout the novel as a marker of character and relationship. Picus, the
novel's most complete and integrated figure, dances spontaneously — his movements are an
extension of his identification with the wood and its creatures. Scylla dances as social
performance, deploying her body as a instrument of erotic negotiation and power. The
gramophone in the stone house serves as a ritual technology, played not for entertainment
but "to appease" the silence and "to keep their dancing in hand" (001-004). Dance thus
spans the novel's registers: from the ecstatic and solitary to the social and strategic.

The concept appears in at least ten distinct passages across chapters 1 through 21,
involving nearly every major character. It functions as a narrative threshold — those who
dance (Picus, Scylla) are aligned with the life of the body and the wood; those who do not
(Carston, to some extent Clarence) are positioned outside the circle of embodied knowledge.
The gramophone's function as an instrument for managing dance underscores the pattern: the
house's inhabitants understand dance as something needing ritual containment, a force as
potentially disruptive as the silence it plays against.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Dance enters the novel in its opening chapter through the gramophone, which the house's
inhabitants play not casually but with purpose:

> A large gramophone stood with its mouth open on the window-sill. It was played to
> appease [the silence] and to keep their dancing in hand. (001-004)

This is not dance as diversion. The gramophone is an instrument of propitiation — music
directed at the silence, and the dancing it accompanies is something that must be "kept in
hand," a force requiring management. From the novel's first pages, dance is positioned as
continuous with the ritual relationship between the house's inhabitants and the wood's
personal, equivocal presence.

### Key Scenes

- **Picus Dancing Like a Marsh-Bird** (005-001): Picus, looking out at "the wood swimming in
  the midday heat," smiles and begins to dance "like a marsh-bird, swinging." This is dance
  as spontaneous identification with the non-human world — not performance for an audience
  but a gesture continuous with the wood's own life. Picus is described elsewhere as "grave
  as a marsh-bird dancing and as liable to agitation" (003-034), linking his temperament
  directly to this quality of movement. His dancing is not learned or socialized; it is an
  expression of what he is.

- **Scylla Dances with the American** (007-014, 007-027): At a social gathering, Scylla
  says "Let's dance" and dances with an American visitor. The gramophone plays "What'll I
  do, what'll I do?" (007-017) — a popular song whose plaintive question contrasts with the
  certainty of Scylla's physical presence. "Scylla, dancing with him, smiled as he sang"
  (007-027). This is dance as erotic power: Scylla uses her body to negotiate attention,
  desire, and social positioning, and the American — like Carston — is drawn into her orbit
  precisely through the physical proximity that dance permits.

- **Picus's Dance as Exclusion** (009-016): At another gathering, a character feels the
  sting of exclusion "because he had no part in Picus's dance." Here dance becomes a form of
  social geometry, a circle drawn around those who can participate. Picus's dance, whether
  literal or figurative, is something one can be inside or outside of — and being outside
  registers as a wound.

- **Carston Dances with the Frenchwoman** (021-017): In a rare instance of Carston
  participating in dance, he "asked the Frenchwoman to dance, and moving easily with her"
  navigates the social situation. But this is dance reduced to its social function — Carston
  dances as he does everything else, strategically, without the identification with
  movement that characterizes Picus. It is the difference between dancing and being danced.

- **Exercises of the Senses** (001-020): Dance is listed among the practices of the house's
  inhabitants — "exercises of the senses, dancing, and stretches of very insular
  contemplation" — placing it within a regimen of cultivated experience. Dance here is not
  spontaneous overflow but a deliberate practice, a discipline of the body alongside other
  forms of sensory training.

> He smiled and began to dance, like a marsh-bird, swinging. (005-001)

### Additional PN References

- "shadows as she dressed, still dance and flash of birds" (002-002) — the word "dance"
  used for the movement of birds, linking natural motion to human bodily expression
- "he is full of fun and dancing, and bird-calls" (008-030) — dancing named as a core
  attribute of character, listed alongside bird-calls as forms of joyful, pre-linguistic
  expression
- "leading us a dance in an infernal prairie" (012-028) — the idiom "leading us a dance,"
  used for the vanished object (likely the cup), transforming the phrase into a description
  of being misled or made to chase

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Dance in *Armed with Madness* operates on a continuum from pure embodiment to social
strategy. At one pole stands Picus: his dance is identification, a way of being in the
world that dissolves the boundary between self and environment. He dances "like a
marsh-bird" not as simile but as metamorphosis — his body knows what the marsh-bird knows,
and dancing is how that knowledge expresses itself. The repeated description of Picus as
"grave as a marsh-bird dancing and as liable to agitation" (003-034) suggests that this
quality is both his gift and his vulnerability: to be so open to the world's movement is to
be exposed to its agitation.

Scylla's dancing occupies the middle of the continuum. She dances with the American as a
form of power — her smile as she dances (007-027) is the smile of someone who knows what
her body can do and what her partner wants. Yet her dancing is not cynical; it is
continuous with the house's larger practice of using music and movement "to appease" and
"to keep in hand." Scylla's dance is, in its own way, a form of ritual — the ritual of
erotic display and negotiation that is as much a part of the novel's world as the silence
of the wood.

Carston's dancing with the Frenchwoman (021-017) occupies the far pole: dance reduced to
social mechanism. He dances "easily" — he knows the steps — but there is no
identification, no risk, no surrender to the body's knowledge. He dances as a man who has
learned to dance in ballrooms, not as one who has learned to dance from the wood. The
contrast with Picus could not be sharper.

The gramophone's role is crucial in tying these elements together. It is played "to
appease" the silence and "to keep their dancing in hand" (001-004) — dance is a force that
must be managed, channeled, contained. The house's inhabitants understand that the body in
motion is a power, as unpredictable and necessary as the silence it plays against. The
gramophone is a ritual technology, and the dancing it enables is a ritual practice — one
that, like the Grail quest itself, demands both surrender and control.

### Mythic / Symbolic Dimension

Dance has deep roots in the mythic and ritual traditions that Butts draws upon throughout
the novel. In the Grail romances, dance is often associated with the waste land — the
Fisher King's wound may be connected to a forbidden dance, and the restoration of the land
sometimes involves a ritual dance or procession. Butts, who was steeped in Grail literature
and in the anthropological thinking of her time (she knew and read Jessie Weston, whose
*From Ritual to Romance* influenced Eliot's *The Waste Land*), would have been aware of
these associations.

In the context of modernist literature, Butts's treatment of dance recalls the broader
modernist fascination with the body as a site of meaning outside language. Yeats asked "how
can we know the dancer from the dance?" — a question that Butts answers differently:
Picus's dance is precisely the point at which dancer and dance become indistinguishable,
not as aesthetic ideal but as lived identification with the non-human. D.H. Lawrence wrote
of "blood-knowledge" against mental consciousness; Butts's dancing bodies enact that
knowledge without needing to name it.

The pagan revivalism that runs through interwar modernism also informs Butts's dance.
Dancing is one of the oldest forms of ritual — older than temples, older than texts — and
the novel's treatment of it as a force requiring "appeasement" and management recalls the
Dionysian, the ecstatic, the body possessed. The gramophone, a machine, becomes the modern
equivalent of the ritual drum or flute — technology pressed into the service of an ancient
pattern of bodily knowledge and communal participation.
