# Beauty

A perceptual force in Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness* (1928) that operates across multiple registers — as physical attractiveness that ignites desire, as "intellectual beauty" synonymous with the peace of God, and as an aesthetic quality of landscape that mediates between the human and the sacred. Beauty in this novel is never inert decoration; it is always contested, always in motion, and always shadowed by its own dissolution.

## Overview

Beauty appears as a named concern across approximately fifteen of the novel's thirty-three chapters, most densely concentrated in the opening and middle sections (Chapters 1-3, 6, 10, 13-14, 16, 21, 25, 29, 32). The word is applied to landscape, to human bodies (male and female), to objects, and to abstractions. Butts distributes the term strategically — not as a stable value but as a site of conflict among competing aesthetic regimes: the Pre-Raphaelite, the classical, the modernist, and the pagan. Every character in the Gault House circle is evaluated, often ruthlessly, in terms of their beauty or their relationship to it.

The novel's opening exchange establishes beauty as a test of perception. The American visitor Dudley Carston arrives at Gault House and says "God! What a beautiful place" (003-003). The narrator immediately editorializes: "When 'beautiful' is said, exactly and honestly, there is contact, or there should be" (003-003). This moment sets the terms: beauty is not a property of objects but an event of genuine perception — and its failure registers as a failure of relationship.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Beauty enters the novel in its third chapter, with Carston's exclamation at the Gault House landscape. His declaration that the place is "beautiful" is followed by a second pronouncement at tea: "It seems to me that you have everything. No luxury, but all the beauty there is" (003-007). Scylla's private response — "Slightly overdoing the beauty business — Beauty is a too concentrated food" (003-008) — immediately ironizes the term, suggesting that Carston's tourist's gaze may be too undiscriminating, too eager to extract aesthetic pleasure from a world whose beauty is inseparable from danger, silence, and madness.

### Key Scenes

- **Carston and Scylla in the wood (Ch. III)**: Carston, alone in his room, beautifies himself "scrupulously and elaborately as a cat" (003-011), a gesture that aligns beauty with performance and self-construction. Later, Scylla reflects on him as "an American boy, very polished and friendly" (006-032) — a beauty of surface manners that masks the predatory impulses beneath.

- **Picus and Scylla in the library (Ch. X)**: Carston watches Picus lift Scylla from the library steps and sees "their beauty, their own, and the beauty of their passion" (010-017). This is the novel's most concentrated beauty-epiphany — but it is immediately contaminated by Carston's "old feeling about sin and fleshy lust" (010-017), his revulsion at Picus's coarse hands, and his determination to "rescue" Scylla by possessing her. Beauty here is not neutral but inflammatory, a provocation to the will.

- **Felix sees Scylla's beauty (Ch. XIII)**: After the confrontation with Carston, brother and sister gut fish together in the scullery. Felix says: "When you came in with Picus I saw your beauty. After Carston had been talking, and surprised me, rather. After the things which have happened lately. It was a kind of answer. A sudden opposite to what I was thinking. To what the world is usually, I suppose" (013-024). This is beauty as revelation and counter-argument — a response to cynicism that requires no words, only presence.

- **Clarence's boy's beauty (Ch. XXIX)**: Clarence, alone and tormented on Tollerdown, is described as haunted by "the transition from his boy's beauty, which Ross had taken as a matter of course" (029-013). Beauty here is temporal and gendered — a quality that cannot survive ageing, whose loss becomes a species of exile. The passage links physical beauty to vulnerability and to the unspoken emotional economy of male relationships within the Gault House circle.

- **Beauty and the French boy (Ch. XXI)**: In Paris, Felix observes a young Frenchman "because his beauty was 'no stronger than a flower'" (021-019). The line, adapted from Shakespeare's sonnet, embeds beauty within the elegiac tradition — always already fading, its fragility inseparable from its power. Felix's own self-consciousness about his "complexion" (021-009) mirrors this anxiety: beauty is a perishable asset in the economy of youth and desire.

- **The beauty of the earth as work of art (Ch. XIV)**: Fleeing Gault House, Carston looks back from the down-crest and sees "the earth was a map of naked beauty he saw in the piece and understood. 'I've been living inside a work of art'" (014-004). This is the culminating insight of Carston's visit — but it is also a retreat. He transforms lived experience into aesthetic object, beauty into distance. The passage diagnoses the tourist's error: mistaking a world that must be lived for one that can be safely viewed.

- **Intellectual beauty and the peace of God (Ch. VI)**: Scylla offers Carston her most explicit definition: "For another of its names is intellectual beauty, and another, the peace of God" (006-039). This reworking of Platonic and Shelleyan categories locates beauty not in the sensuous particular but in a transcendent order that the Gault House circle can only glimpse and never possess. The same passage links beauty to "illumination which always take a turn for the worse" (006-039), making beauty the precarious outward sign of an inward condition that is constitutively fragile.

- **Scylla's beauty as captivity (Ch. XVI)**: In the aftermath of Picus's deception, Scylla reflects: "And I even thought of marrying him because of his beauty. I did not catch the joy as it flew" (016-031). Beauty and joy are twinned here as fugitive states — things that pass, that cannot be secured by marriage, bargain, or will. The confession marks Scylla's recognition that physical beauty, however compelling, is insufficient ground for love or life.

- **Lydia's chief beauty (Ch. XXV)**: In London, Scylla observes her friend Lydia: "Vexed deliberation marked the ivory forehead, her chief beauty" (025-006). The description is diagnostic rather than appreciative — Lydia's beauty is a site of strain, a brow worn by worry over an unsuitable marriage. The same chapter records Philip's "delicious voice and vulgar accent" and "the beauty of his wrist as he lit Scylla's cigarette" (025-019) — a fleeting, treacherous beauty that masks manipulation.

> "For another of its names is intellectual beauty, and another, the peace of God." (006-039)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Beauty functions in *Armed with Madness* as a diagnostic tool. Who sees it, how they name it, and what they do with it reveals character more reliably than any confession. Carston sees beauty as a tourist and a consumer — something to be appreciated, possessed, and ultimately fled. Scylla understands beauty as inseparable from danger, a "too concentrated food" that cannot be safely ingested in large doses. Felix perceives his sister's beauty as "a kind of answer" to the world's cynicism — beauty as counter-argument, as moral force. Picus embodies beauty as a kind of lost godliness ("his glory, a kind of lost god" — implied at 010-017), but his beauty is compromised by coarse hands and destructive deceptions.

> "their beauty, their own, and the beauty of their passion" (010-017)

The novel never stabilizes beauty into a single principle. Instead, it charts the collisions among competing beauties: the Pre-Raphaelite beauty of "those awful pictures" that Clarence rejects (006-012); the "intellectual beauty" that Scylla names in her confession to Carston (006-039); the carnal beauty that Carston simultaneously desires and moralizes against (010-017); the transient beauty of youth that Clarence grieves (029-013) and Felix fears losing (021-009); the beauty of the Dorset landscape that Carston can only comprehend by aestheticizing it into "a work of art" (014-004). Each character's relationship to beauty indexes their capacity to live in the world as it is — or their need to flee it.

### Modernist Aesthetic Dimension

Butts's treatment of beauty participates in a distinctly modernist interrogation of the aesthetic. The novel was published in 1928, the same year as Woolf's *Orlando* and Lawrence's *Lady Chatterley's Lover* — both works that contest inherited notions of beauty, gender, and desire. Butts's intervention is to refuse the opposition between beauty and truth that much high modernism embraced. For her characters, beauty is not a lie or a bourgeois consolation; it is the form that truth takes when it breaks into the sensory world. The problem is not that beauty deceives but that it is unbearable — "a too concentrated food" that must be diluted by irony, play, drink, and the rituals of the group.

The novel's persistent return to the phrase "intellectual beauty" (006-039) rewrites Shelley's "Hymn to Intellectual Beauty" (1816) for a post-war generation that has lost Shelley's confidence in the immanence of the divine. Butts's "intellectual beauty" is a thing glimpsed in "moments of illumination which always take a turn for the worse" (006-039) — a beauty that arrives as promise and departs as wound. This is the modernist condition: no stable epiphany, only the rhythm of approach and withdrawal that the novel itself enacts.

Beauty also operates as a gendered economy in the novel. Male beauty (Picus's, Felix's, Clarence's, the French boy's) is described with an intensity and particularity typically reserved for female subjects in the Western tradition. This redistribution of the gaze — women assessing male beauty, men appraising each other — destabilizes the conventions of the romantic novel and aligns Butts with the queer modernist tradition of Firbank, Stein, and the early Sitwell. Beauty circulates not only between men and women but among men, creating rivalries, dependencies, and unspoken allegiances that the novel registers without moralizing.
