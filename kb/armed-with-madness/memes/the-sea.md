# The Sea

The sea is the constant horizon of *Armed with Madness*. Visible from Gault Cliff, audible from the heath, it is the novel's most persistent landscape presence — not a setting but an environment, the element that contains all the novel's places. "'Mare Nostrum,' they said, in Paris or in London, at the sea's winter takings" (001-009). The Latin phrase — "Our Sea," the Roman name for the Mediterranean — is at once an incantation and an irony: the sea is no one's, least of all theirs.

## Description

Butts's sea is a winter sea — "at the sea's winter takings" (001-009), "the sea or the terrible crest of Gault suspended in the haze" (017-021). It is never the benign summer sea of the resort (Biarritz is invoked for that fantasy) but a dangerous, cold, indifferent presence. The drowned bodies that haunt Ross's local knowledge — "twenty-three drowned men laid out on tables" (020-001) — make the sea a repository of the dead.

> "Mare Nostrum." (001-009)

> "the sea was a presence, a third party to every conversation" (005-012)

## Narrative Function

### The Sea as Witness

The sea witnesses everything and remembers nothing. It is the novel's only element that is entirely indifferent to the Grail quest — it does not help or hinder, it simply is. This indifference is, for the novel, a kind of truth: the sea is what remains when the mythic apparatus is stripped away.

### Drowning and Return

The drowned men Ross knows — the Truro Ledges disaster — make the sea a grave. The Grail's connection to water (the well, the cup) is shadowed by the sea's connection to death. To quest for the Grail is to risk drowning; to find it is to be pulled from the water.
