# Madness

The state that gives Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness* (1928) its title: a condition that the novel refuses to settle into any single meaning. Madness in this work is at once the shrieking wind "like a mad harp" off the Channel (001-003); the "lovely and mad" women that populate Picus's imagination (003-024); the "nearly mad" desperation of a man watching light fail over a dying companion (033-014); and the epigraph that frames the entire enterprise — "Armed with madness" (P01-005). Butts does not treat madness as a clinical diagnosis but as a mode of being in which perception intensifies beyond the ordinary, and in which the boundary between insight and dissolution becomes untenable.

## Overview

Madness operates across the novel on three distinct but interlocking planes. First, it is an atmospheric condition — the sound of wind, the quality of moonlight, the pressure of solitude in a remote landscape. Second, it is a psychological state attributed to individuals: the "mad" women Carston imagines, the "insanity" that Scylla's sister-in-law's look suggests (025-070), the "madness" that might be indistinguishable from memory in Picus's final moments (033-083). Third, and most important, it is a cultural diagnosis: the madness of a generation that survived the Great War, inherited a broken symbolic order, and sought replacement rituals in art, drink, erotic pursuit, and the half-believed Grail myth.

The word appears most densely in three locations: the title epigraph (P01-005), Chapter I where the landscape itself is figured as a "mad harp" (001-003), and Chapter XXXIII where Picus's terminal delusions blur memory and madness (033-083). Between these poles, madness diffuses into related terms — "crazy," "insanity," "hysteria" — and into behaviors that the narrative registers as excessive, irrational, or ecstatic.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The novel's epigraph — "Armed with madness" (P01-005) — establishes the term before any character speaks or any landscape is described. Drawn from the text's deeper mythic substrate (the Fisher King's wound, the Grail knight's ordeal), it positions madness not as a defect but as equipment: something one bears into the world, a weapon or a shield. The phrase answers the question the novel will spend thirty-three chapters asking: what do you arm yourself with when the old sanctions have failed?

### Key Scenes

- **The wind as mad harp (Ch. I)**: The novel's second paragraph describes the wind "up-Channel shrieking like a mad harp" (001-003). This is the first instance of madness in the narrative proper, and it arrives not as a human attribute but as a quality of the world. The simile links madness to music, to the inhuman, and to the Channel — a threshold between England and France, land and sea, safety and danger.

- **"Women lovely and mad" (Ch. III)**: Carston's early meditation on Scylla's beauty yields the reflection: "She might be mad, but she was good-looking. Women lovely and mad, or only lovely and stupid" (003-024). This is the novel's most casual deployment of madness-as-attribution, and its very casualness is diagnostic. Carston's taxonomy — women are either lovely-and-mad or lovely-and-stupid — reveals a mind that aestheticizes instability, that finds madness attractive at a distance and unbearable up close.

- **Picus "mad about property" (Ch. XV)**: Carston observes Picus's fixation on the Gault estate: "He is mad about property, and he hates his father for letting it go" (015-068). Here madness shifts register from psychological condition to economic obsession — the madness of attachment to land, inheritance, and the symbols of continuity that the novel systematically dismantles.

- **"Nearly mad as the light failed" (Ch. XXXIII)**: The novel's most harrowing deployment of madness occurs in the penultimate chapter, when Picus — alone with the dying Scylla — "went nearly mad as the light failed, for he saw that she was dying and loved her with a love that would no longer be answered" (033-014). Madness here is the collapse of distinction between love and terror, between caring for the beloved and being destroyed by the impossibility of saving her.

- **"Memory or madness?" (Ch. XXXIII)**: In his final dialogue with Scylla, Picus's eyes are described as "shifting memory or madness" (033-083). The question mark in the text is crucial: the narrative refuses to adjudicate. Memory and madness are presented not as opposed conditions but as adjacent territories on the same cognitive map, each shading into the other.

> "Now are his eyes shifting memory or madness?" (033-083)

## Interpretation

> "Now are his eyes shifting memory or madness?" (033-083)

### Within the Novel

Madness in *Armed with Madness* is not a condition to be cured but a condition to be navigated. Every major character touches it: Picus in his destructive obsessions, Scylla in her fugitive states of perception, Carston in his compulsive need to possess and control, Felix in his fear of night and solitude, Clarence in his grief over lost beauty. The novel's geography is organized around sites where madness intensifies — the Wood, the sea-cliff, the lonely stretches of downland — and sites where it is temporarily held at bay — Gault House, the ritual of meals, the presence of the group.

Butts's crucial insight is that madness and perception are continuous. To see clearly — to perceive beauty, to register silence, to feel the weight of the Grail's absence — is to risk madness. The novel's characters are "armed" with madness because they have no other defense against a world whose meaning has been evacuated. The Grail is gone; what remains is the wound, and the wound's only alleviation is the intensity of attention that madness provides.

### Mythic / Symbolic Dimension

The novel's title invokes the Grail legend's wounded Fisher King, whose kingdom is a wasteland until the Grail knight asks the healing question. Butts reverses the traditional framework: in her version, the Grail knight does not heal the wound but participates in it. To be "armed with madness" is to enter the wasteland not as healer but as fellow sufferer — to bear the wound as a form of knowledge.

This aligns Butts with the modernist rewriting of myth that characterizes Eliot's *The Waste Land* (1922) and Joyce's *Ulysses* (1922). In all three works, myth is not a guarantee of meaning but a measure of its loss. Butts's innovation is to make madness — not fertility, not homecoming, not epiphany — the central term of her mythic revision. The Grail is a "stone of joy" (the Lapis Exultationis), but it is also a "stone of exile" (the Lapis Exilii, 005-019), and the space between exultation and exile is the space madness occupies.
