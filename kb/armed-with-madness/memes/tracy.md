# Mr. Tracy

Mr. Tracy is the novel's Merlin-figure: an "ancient of days" (026-003) living in isolation on the Dorset heath, whose house contains a mysterious book, a collection of antiquities, and possibly the genuine Grail itself. He enters the novel through Carston's investigations and Ross's local knowledge, and his presence shifts the novel's axis — what was a psychological drama of erotic entanglement among the young becomes a quest narrative in which the oldest man holds the key. Tracy is at once a trickster, a sage, and a genuine mystery: the novel never fully resolves whether he is a charlatan, a guardian, or something in between.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full name and aliases**: Known as "Mr. Tracy," "old Mr. Tracy," or simply "Tracy." The formal "Mr." prefix, consistently used by the younger characters, marks both his age and his separateness — he is never absorbed into the first-name intimacy of the Gault House circle. His son Christopher Tracy is mentioned but never appears.
- **Circle**: The heath — physically and symbolically outside Gault House. Tracy represents the older, stranger Dorset that predates the Taverners' cosmopolitan world.
- **Key relationships**:
  - With Picus: Tracy's son seems to know Picus; there is an implication that Picus sourced the "cup" from Tracy. "You found that thing. Then Tracy vanished" (012-028) — Ross's accusation links the Grail's discovery directly to Tracy.
  - With Carston: Carston becomes Tracy's reluctant investigator, visiting his house, searching his room, and ultimately confronting him. The Carston-Tracy relationship drives much of the novel's late-plot machinery: "I've been trying to see you" (031-003).
  - With Ross: Ross distrusts Tracy's connection to drowned bodies — "twenty-three drowned men laid out on tables" (020-001) — and the Truro Ledges disaster. The dark rumors around Tracy position Ross as the voice of local caution.
  - With Mrs. Tracy: Tracy's wife drowned herself — "What did Mrs. Tracy mean by drowning herself" (023-007). Her death is one more mystery in the orbit of a mysterious man.

> "people say he is a beauty, and it is time you picked up a husband" (025-055)

## Character and Thematic Function

Tracy embodies the novel's central question: is the Grail real, and if so, what does real mean? As a collector of antiquities — "the collection of Christopher Tracy, Esq." (018-020) — he bridges the material and the numinous. His status as "an ancient of days" (026-003) positions him as a living link to older orders of knowledge that the Gault House circle can only gesture toward.

- **The collector as guardian**: Tracy's house contains objects whose value exceeds their material worth. "Tracy has a book up in his room. One of those old books" (012-033) — the book is at once a historical artifact and a potential source of Grail lore.
- **Trickster or saint?**: The novel oscillates between portraying Tracy as a fraud and as a genuine initiate. Carston's investigation produces evidence of both: "a mass-substitute" (030-009) suggests Tracy sees the Grail in materialist terms, yet "old Mr. Tracy turned saint" (031-047) suggests transformation.
- **The elder as threshold**: Tracy lives at the boundary — heath/Gault House, old/young, sacred/profane. His function is to mark the edge of what the younger characters can understand.

> "I admit that it is probably a mass-substitute" (030-009) — Tracy's own words about the Grail, at once confessional and evasive, expert and uncertain.

## Narrative Arc

### The Discovery

Ross connects Tracy to the discovery of the "cup": "you found that thing. Then Tracy vanished, after leading us a dance" (012-028). Tracy's disappearance after the finding suggests either guilt or the completion of a ritual role — the herald who appears, delivers the object, and withdraws.

### Investigation and Confrontation

Carston's investigation of Tracy occupies much of the novel's second half. He visits Tracy's house (026-003, 030-001), searches his room, and finally confronts him in what becomes an ecclesiastical encounter: "old Mr. Tracy turned saint" (031-047). The confrontation is less a resolution than a transformation — Tracy becomes something other than what Carston expected to find.

### The Unresolved End

The novel never adjudicates Tracy's authenticity. "How do you think you'll get away with it?" (031-057) Carston asks, and the novel does not answer. Tracy remains at the novel's close what he was at its opening: a question that the narrative refuses to foreclose.
