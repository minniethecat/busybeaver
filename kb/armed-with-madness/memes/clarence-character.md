# Clarence

Clarence arrives at Gault House as Picus's companion — "the transfer of Picus and Clarence for an indefinite stay" (003-038) — and from that moment, his function in the novel is established: he works while Picus performs. The asymmetry is structural. "Picus saying he was ill and Clarence doing all the work. As usual" (004-006). Clarence is the novel's most reliable character not because he is simple but because he has chosen, against the evidence of his own intelligence, to sustain another person's life at the cost of his own.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full name and aliases**: Known only as "Clarence." The single name — no surname, no title — marks his class position. Unlike "Mr. Tracy" or "Dudley Carston," Clarence requires no formal designation; the familiarity is social rather than intimate.
- **Circle**: Arrives at Gault House with Picus, but belongs to no circle. His class position and his war experience isolate him from both the upper-class characters and the servants. He occupies a unique liminal position: present at every gathering, essential to every plan, yet never fully included.
- **Key relationships**:
  - With Picus: "Picus is Clarence's 'old man of the sea' only he happens to be a young man" (004-008). The Sinbad metaphor — the old man who clings to Sinbad's shoulders and cannot be dislodged — captures the asymmetrical devotion. Clarence carries Picus. "Clarence fights for him and with him" (004-009).
  - With Scylla: Clarence "kissed her gallantly, with a large, gentle, respectful gallantry" (003-058). The triple qualification — large, gentle, respectful — distinguishes Clarence's mode of relation from the erotic entanglements that ensnare the other characters.
  - With Carston: Carston "felt that Clarence had better have hit them in the head" (007-011). Clarence's restraint, which the American reads as a form of violence withheld, becomes for Carston a measure of the household's moral disorder.

> "the transition from his boy's beauty" (029-013)

## Character and Thematic Function

Clarence is the novel's moral ground — not because he is simple or innocent, but because he has seen "something" (005-020) in the war that has given him a perspective the other characters lack. His war experience is alluded to rather than narrated: "He had seen something in the war, not in the fighting line, but in a base hospital" (005-020). The indirection is significant — Clarence's trauma is real but contained, and it is precisely this containment that makes him the novel's only character capable of sustained, unselfish action.

- **The working-class foil**: Clarence's labor — physical, continuous, unremarked — is the material condition for the Gault House circle's leisure. He "did all the work" while the others play "the Freud game" (006-010). The novel registers this asymmetry without resolving it; Clarence neither rebels nor is liberated. His position is the novel's unspoken critique of its own aristocratic milieu.
- **The dreamer as truth-teller**: Clarence's two narrated dreams — "he dreamed that he was in a shell-hole and someone had given him an ice" (005-017), "asleep again, dreamed he was bleeding to death" (005-022) — are among the novel's few passages of unmediated suffering. The dreams rupture the aestheticized surface of the Gault House world with a reality the novel registers but cannot assimilate.

> "Picus is Clarence's 'old man of the sea' only he happens to be a young man." (004-008)

## Narrative Arc

### Arrival

Clarence enters the novel as Picus's appendage — "the transfer of Picus and Clarence" (003-038) — and the passive construction captures his narrative position. He is transferred, brought along, assigned quarters. His own volition is never narrated; his presence is assumed.

### The Burden

Throughout the novel's middle chapters, Clarence is the one who works: "order Picus upstairs to change" (007-008), "the cup for Clarence to fill with whiskey and soda" (007-015). His actions are transitive — they have direct objects — while the other characters' are intransitive, reflexive. He does things to things; they do things to themselves.

### The Unresolved End

Clarence's story does not resolve because the novel cannot imagine a resolution for him. He is necessary to the Gault House circle but cannot be incorporated into its mythic resolution. His final position is that of the witness: present, essential, invisible.
