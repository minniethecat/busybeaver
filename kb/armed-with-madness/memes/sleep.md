# Sleep

In *Armed with Madness*, sleep is not merely a biological necessity but a charged threshold state — the antechamber to dreams, a condition of physical vulnerability, and a site of intimate proximity between characters. Where dreams provide visionary content, sleep provides the conditions under which conscious control is relinquished and other forms of awareness become possible.

## Overview

Sleep and sleeping appear across at least eleven chapters of the novel (Chapters V, VII, IX, X, XI, XII, XV, XVI, XIX, XXXI, XXXIII), making it one of the most consistently recurring states in the book. The motif clusters around three characters in particular: Clarence, whose sleep is repeatedly interrupted by dreams and visitations; Picus, who is frequently observed while sleeping by others; and Scylla, whose sleepy presence carries an erotic charge. Sleep functions in the novel as a leveller — in sleep, the class distinctions and social anxieties that preoccupy the waking characters dissolve, revealing a more elemental human vulnerability.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Sleep first appears as Clarence's habitual refuge: he often goes to a particular place when asleep, carrying a dead bird — an image that yokes the vulnerability of sleep to death and ritual offering (005-018).

### Key Scenes

- **Clarence's Half-Hour of Sleep**: Clarence steals half an hour's more sleep, hoping the dreams would come — sleep here is actively courted as a portal to visionary experience (005-019).
- **Picus Asleep Outdoors**: Picus falls asleep quickly in the strong air after a meal, the night clearing outside — a moment of peaceful, almost pastoral vulnerability (007-039).
- **Clarence Watching Over Picus**: Picus is asleep when Clarence bends over him, a scene of watchful tenderness that reverses the power dynamics of waking life (009-011).
- **"I Shall Sleep There"**: Clarence repeats to himself his determination to sleep in Picus's room, a fixation that mixes longing with territorial claim (010-006).
- **The Intimacy of Shared Sleep**: Felix suggests "Perhaps you'd better sleep with him. It would be better" — sleep as a euphemism and a genuine proposition of protective intimacy (011-055).
- **Scylla's Sleepy Arrival**: Scylla comes down "heavenly sleepy," a description that marries her physical state to a quality of otherworldly beauty (016-003).

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Sleep in *Armed with Madness* operates on a spectrum from defensive withdrawal to willed vulnerability. Clarence's sleep is often strategic — he courts it for the dreams it may bring, or uses it to escape social demands. By contrast, Picus sleeps with the unselfconsciousness of someone who does not fear exposure, his sleeping body becoming an object of others' observation and desire. The repeated motif of characters watching one another sleep — Clarence watching Picus, the narrator observing Scylla — creates a dynamic of tender surveillance in which the sleeping figure becomes both aesthetic object and locus of projected feeling. The question posed in Chapter XII, "I suppose I am free to sleep with whom I like?", crystallizes the motif's dual register: sleep as both the most private of acts and the most intimate form of shared presence.

### Mythic / Symbolic Dimension

In the novel's mythic economy, sleep is the preparatory state for visionary encounter — the necessary descent before the Grail can appear. This mirrors the Grail legend tradition in which the quester must enter a state of receptive passivity (often figured as sleep or trance) before the sacred object discloses itself. Butts also draws on the modernist fascination with sleep as a return to the primitive and the authentic: in sleep, the characters shed the performative identities demanded by the post-war English social order and become, as the narrator observes, "a little brutal and afraid" — more honest, less guarded. The pagan resonance of earth-sleep ("sleep out on the earth and gnaw grass") further aligns sleep with a chthonic return to elemental being, a theme reinforced by the novel's recurring interest in the soil, the land, and pre-Christian ritual.
