# Boris

Boris enters *Armed with Madness* in its Paris chapters as a force of dangerous allure. Felix feels "an awful, delicious fear that Boris could read his mind" (024-007). The compound adjective — "awful, delicious" — captures what Boris represents: the erotic as a mode of knowledge that is at once irresistible and annihilating. He is a Russian emigre, a figure of displacement and survival, whose "young head, a little too round" (031-015) masks a mind that calculates as precisely as it desires.

## Identity and Relationships

- **Full name**: Known only as Boris — "I speak it a bit, Boris" (024-007)
- **Circle**: Paris group — an outsider to the Gault House circle, encountered in the novel's London/Paris chapters
- **Key relationships**:
  - With Felix: the "awful, delicious fear" of being seen through. Boris reads Felix with an accuracy that Felix finds both thrilling and terrifying.
  - With the "black briar-rose" boy: "who looked at nothing but Boris" (028-003) — a relationship whose erotic charge the novel registers through imagery rather than declaration.
  - With the Paris party: "he did not know that Boris owed money there, how he balanced on the edge of precarious social accommodations" (028-002) — Boris as survivor, balanced on the edge of exposure.

## Character and Thematic Function

Boris represents the erotic as danger — not the danger of violence but the danger of being truly seen. His capacity to "read" Felix's mind makes him the novel's only character capable of the kind of perceptual acuity that Scylla possesses, but deployed without Scylla's moral framework. Boris perceives without caring; his clarity is instrumental, not compassionate.

- **The Russian as outsider**: Boris's nationality is not incidental. As a Russian emigre in post-war Paris, he occupies the position of the displaced — someone who has lost his world and learned to navigate others' worlds as a survival strategy. This displacement makes him dangerous: he has no stake in the social orders he manipulates.

- **The violence of the party (Ch. XXVIII)**: Boris is the only character who keeps his wits during the Paris party's eruption into violence: "Boris alone kept his wits. Withdrew to the door" (028-005). His composure in crisis is chilling — he is the survivor who has seen worse and will see worse again.

> "An awful, delicious fear that Boris could read his mind." (024-007)

## Narrative Arc

Boris appears in the novel's urban chapters (XXIV–XXXI), a figure of Paris and London rather than the Dorset countryside. His function is to provide a counterpoint to the Gault House circle: where they are provincial, rooted, mythological, Boris is cosmopolitan, displaced, pragmatic. His departure from the novel is as unremarked as his entrance — he simply ceases to be present, as survivors do.
