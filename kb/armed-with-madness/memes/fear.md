# Fear

Fear saturates the world of *Armed with Madness* as thoroughly as the Channel wind. It is the dread that attends [[the Sanc-Grail]], the "literal fear of the cup" (012-002) that is "in the blood"; it is the terror that grips [[Clarence]] alone on Tollerdown, "the fear natural in remote places to a man alone" (029-012); it is the "awful, delicious fear" (024-007) that [[Felix Taverner|Felix]] feels when [[Boris]] seems to read his mind; and it is the "shabby weariness and fear" (013-001) that replaces hope at the novel's midpoint. Mary Butts treats fear not as a simple affect to be overcome but as a mode of knowledge — something the body registers before the mind can name, and whose presence or absence marks the difference between those who can live in the world as it is and those who must flee it.

## Overview

Fear appears across the novel in three distinct forms. First is **elemental fear** — the fear provoked by darkness, solitude, and the non-human landscape of the Dorset coast and downland. This fear is not pathological but proportional: the novel suggests that to be *unafraid* in such places would be the true derangement. Second is **social fear** — fear of exposure, of humiliation, of being misunderstood or rejected by the group. This fear governs the intricate negotiations of the Gault House circle, where every conversation is a potential ambush. Third is **mythic fear** — the "literal fear of the cup" (012-002), a fear that attaches to the Grail itself and that is "in the blood," ancestral rather than personal.

The word "fear" and its variants (feared, fearful, fearing) appear most densely in Chapters III–IV (Carston's introduction to Gault House), Chapter XII (the Grail cup's uncanny power), and Chapters XXIX–XXX (Clarence's solitary night on Tollerdown). This distribution tracks the novel's movement from social comedy through mythic crisis to existential terror.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Fear first appears in the novel's third chapter, embedded in Scylla's reflection on Carston's psychological naivety: "There was something rather awful, not dreadful but painful in the case of irrational fear. Very true, too, when there had been a war, a universal hysteria" (003-051). This is fear placed immediately in its post-war context — not a private neurosis but a collective condition. The Great War has made fear rational; the problem is not that people are afraid but that their fear no longer attaches to appropriate objects.

### Key Scenes

- **Scylla on fear and hysteria (Ch. III)**: Scylla's meditation on "irrational fear" (003-051) establishes the novel's refusal to pathologize fear. In the aftermath of "a war, a universal hysteria," fear has become the baseline of experience. The question the novel asks is not "how to eliminate fear" but "what to do with it."

- **The fear of the cup (Ch. XII)**: The novel's most concentrated meditation on fear occurs in Chapter XII, where the Grail cup is described as provoking "the literal fear of the cup, that it was uncanny, that there was something in the blood" (012-002). Fear here is not a response to danger but to holiness — to an object whose power exceeds comprehension. This is fear as awe, fear as the appropriate response to the sacred.

- **Felix's fear of Gault (Ch. XI)**: When Carston reassures Felix that he is "pretty full" of drink, the narrative notes: "That took away her fear of Gault. For the terrible creature was gone" (011-037). Fear attaches not to violence but to judgment — Felix fears Gault's moral scrutiny more than any physical threat.

- **Clarence's night-terror (Ch. XXIX)**: Alone on Tollerdown, Clarence experiences fear as a dissolving of self: "the night swept on, and even his fear of it was forgotten in the greater fear of losing himself" (029-014). This is fear at its most existential — not fear of something specific but fear of ceasing to be a coherent subject entirely.

- **Boris and the delicious fear (Ch. XXIV)**: Felix's encounter with Boris produces "an awful, delicious fear that Boris could read his mind" (024-007). This compound adjective — "awful, delicious" — captures fear's ambivalence in the novel. Fear can be sought, savored, even eroticized.

- **Clarence's fear of Starn (Ch. XXXII)**: In the aftermath of violence, Clarence "feared that Clarence had seen her" (032-005) — a fear of being witnessed in a moment of exposure. This is social fear at its most acute: not fear of harm but fear of being known.

> "There was something rather awful, not dreadful but painful in the case of irrational fear. Very true, too, when there had been a war, a universal hysteria." (003-051)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Fear in *Armed with Madness* is a diagnostic tool that sorts characters into types. Those who acknowledge their fear — Scylla, Felix, Clarence — are capable of relationship, perception, and change. Those who deny or suppress it — Carston, Picus, Ross — become rigid, predatory, or self-destructive. The novel's most damaged characters are not those who feel fear most intensely but those who have built their personalities around its denial.

The Gault House circle functions as a fear-management system. Rituals of drinking, swimming, walking, and conversation are not escapes from fear but ways of distributing and containing it. When the circle fractures — when Picus breaks the cup, when Carston flees, when Scylla withdraws — each character is left alone with a fear that had previously been held in common. The novel's final chapters chronicle these solitary terrors: Clarence on Tollerdown, Picus with the dying Scylla, Felix in the aftermath of violence. Fear that was manageable in company becomes annihilating in solitude.

### Modernist / Literary Dimension

Butts's treatment of fear aligns her with the post-Freudian modernism of the 1920s, in which fear is no longer a moral failing (cowardice) but a psychic structure that reveals the architecture of the self. The novel's insistent linking of fear to "the blood" (012-002) suggests a Jungian rather than Freudian framework: fear is not repressed sexuality but inherited ancestral memory, the body's recollection of sacred dangers that the modern mind has forgotten.

The "literal fear of the cup" (012-002) is the novel's most radical claim about fear — that it can be a form of worship, a recognition of the numinous. This reverses the Enlightenment association of fear with superstition and aligns Butts with the reactionary modernism of Yeats and Lawrence, for whom the recovery of pre-modern fear-responses was a cultural project. To feel "the literal fear of the cup" is to be momentarily pre-modern again, to experience the Grail not as metaphor but as power.
