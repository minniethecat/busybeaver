# Pity

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness*, pity is not a straightforward virtue but a contested emotional terrain where compassion, condescension, self-regard, and the failure of genuine solidarity all converge. From the early chapters through the novel's climactic confrontations, pity appears as something characters suspect, resist, perform, and weaponise — never as something they gratefully receive.

## Overview

Pity and its cognates (self-pity, compassion, "shameful pity") appear across at least five distinct chapters spanning the novel's full arc — from Chapter VII to Chapter XXXIII. The concept is distributed across all three major character clusters: the Gault House circle (Clarence, Scylla, Picus), the outsider Carston, and the novel's reflective chorus (Mr. Tracy in Chapter XXXI, the final unmasking of Carston in Chapter XXXIII). Unlike desire — which the novel treats as a raw, pre-moral force — pity is treated as an already-socialised emotion, one that always operates within a relationship of perceived inequality. To pity someone is to place oneself above them; to be pitied is to be diminished. This structural asymmetry makes pity, in Butts's handling, a fundamentally unstable emotional transaction — and one that the novel's most psychologically acute characters view with deep suspicion.

The novel's moral geometry around pity is clearest in its treatment of Clarence. In Chapter VII, Carston observes that there is "more behind what he said than self-pity" (007-011) — a formulation that acknowledges the presence of self-pity while insisting that it is insufficient as an explanation for Clarence's character. Clarence is the novel's primary case study in the relationship between pity and pride: he receives compassion from his friends but experiences it as a threat to his dignity. He is, as the narrative notes, someone whose strong appetites, once satisfied, release him from wanting (002-013) — but pity is an appetite that cannot be satisfied, because it feeds on its own refusal.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Pity first enters the novel's vocabulary through Carston's observation of Clarence during the dinner-party scene in Chapter VII. The group has been discussing the Sanc-Grail, Picus has used the cup to serve whiskey, and Clarence's position in the social field is under scrutiny:

> He had noticed that they were compassionate. Perhaps it was that they had all been acquitted of some offence that had taught them tolerance, that it had done something for them more behind what he said than self-pity, yet Carston felt that Clarence was what would be called a "martyred ass." (007-011)

The passage is remarkable for its layered epistemology: Carston *notices* compassion in the others, *speculates* about its source, *qualifies* his own dismissal of Clarence's self-pity, and then *reaches* for a social cliche ("martyred ass") that he does not fully endorse. The word "yet" is doing heavy work — it acknowledges that self-pity IS present in Clarence's performance, but insists that something else, something not reducible to self-pity, is also at work. This ambivalence sets the pattern for every subsequent appearance of pity in the novel.

### Key Scenes

- **Scylla's "Have Pity" Song** (007-035): Moments after Carston's reflection on Clarence, Scylla sings a snatch of song — "Oh, lady, please have pity" — that transforms pity from a psychological category into an aesthetic one. The song's plea for pity is performative, ironic, drawing on the courtly love tradition where pity (*mercé* in Occitan, *pietà* in Italian) was the lady's grace the lover begged for. That Scylla sings this line in the midst of the whiskey-and-Grail scene — where the sacred and the profane are already colliding — compounds the irony. Pity, in her mouth, is not an emotion but a quotation, a gesture drawn from the cultural repertoire.

- **The Suspicion of Pity** (021-019): Later in the novel, a character looks at another "kindly, and he suspected pity, found himself caught with an awkward, half-angry sensation." This is the novel's most explicit articulation of pity's social hazard: a kindly look — which ought to be the simplest gesture of human warmth — is received as an insult. The reaction is reflexive, pre-conscious, a "half-angry sensation" that arises before the recipient can rationalise it. The word "suspected" is crucial: pity is something one detects, infers, attributes to the other — it is less a feeling than a hermeneutic of suspicion applied to another's motive.

- **Shameless, Shameful Pity of Disorder** (031-015): In Chapter XXXI, as Mr. Tracy delivers his sweeping summation of the novel's events as "true Sanc-Grail" narrative, the text describes a quality of attention in the room: "A shameless, shameful pity of disorder, and want not about either. And no excuse for the room either." This is pity transmuted into an almost metaphysical register — not pity FOR a person but pity OF a state of being, pity at the spectacle of a world in which order has collapsed. The phrase's internal contradiction — "shameless, shameful" — captures pity's paradoxical status in the novel: it is something one ought to be ashamed of feeling (because it implies superiority) and yet something one feels without shame (because it arises involuntarily). The "disorder" it pities is the general condition of modernity that the novel both diagnoses and inhabits.

- **Carston Called "Self-Pitying"** (033-138): In the novel's climactic confrontation, Carston is told: "You, you spoilt, hysterical, self-pitying, self-centred, uninventive." This is the accusation the novel has been building toward — the judgment that Carston's entire psychological apparatus, his elaborate self-narratives of injury and jealousy, his moralising condemnation of Picus, his fantasy of rescuing Scylla, all reduce to self-pity. The word "spoilt" establishes the cause; "hysterical" invokes the gendered pathology the novel has been subverting; "uninventive" is the final, devastating judgment — self-pity is not only morally corrupt but aesthetically sterile. Carston, who desires to "redecorate his memories" (005-021), has produced nothing but variations on the same theme of wounded entitlement. Self-pity, here, is the failed form of making — the imagination turned inward and devouring itself.

> A shameless, shameful pity of disorder, and want not about either. (031-015)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Pity in *Armed with Madness* functions as a register of social health. When the Gault House circle is functioning as a genuine community — however fragile and contested — pity is unnecessary because compassion is embedded in action rather than isolated as sentiment. Clarence's friends *act* compassionately toward him (they include him, they tolerate his difficult moods) without making a display of their compassion. It is Carston, the outsider, who sees their behaviour through the lens of pity and finds it patronising — because Carston's own relationship to emotion is fundamentally transactional. He cannot imagine compassion without condescension because he himself is incapable of the former without the latter.

The novel's structure reinforces this reading. Pity appears most explicitly in moments of social fracture: the dinner party where Carston perceives Clarence's self-pity (007), the awkward encounter where kindness is suspected as condescension (021), the summation chapter where pity of disorder names the novel's own condition (031), and the final confrontation where self-pity is hurled as an accusation (033). In each case, pity marks a failure of genuine connection — a moment when the social fabric tears and reveals the asymmetrical power relations beneath.

Clarence's case deserves particular attention. He is the character most associated with receiving pity, and his relationship to it is one of furious ambivalence. He needs the compassion of his friends; he resents needing it; his resentment expresses itself as the very self-pity for which he is criticised. This recursive trap — self-pity as both the symptom of wounded pride and the cause of further wounding — is the novel's most subtle psychological insight about the emotion.

### Literary Dimension

Butts's treatment of pity participates in a broader modernist suspicion of sentiment. Where the Victorian novel could deploy pity as an unproblematic moral lever (think of Dickens's appeals on behalf of the deserving poor), modernism tended to anatomise pity as a covert form of power. Nietzsche's critique of *Mitleid* (pity/compassion) as the morality of the weak — a ressentiment-driven demand that the strong feel guilty about their strength — hovers in the background. But Butts does not simply reproduce the Nietzschean position. Her novel is too interested in the actual texture of emotional life to reduce pity to a philosophical abstraction. Instead, she maps its specific operations: the way it is suspected before it is felt, the way it converts suffering into spectacle, the way self-pity serves as a substitute for action.

The formal parallel with desire is instructive. Desire, in this novel, is a force that moves outward — toward another person, toward an object, toward the world. Pity moves in the opposite direction: it is a recoil, a withdrawal into the self's sense of its own wound. Carston's desire for Scylla is aggressive, appropriative, directed; his self-pity is passive, ruminative, circular. The novel's judgment is that both are failures — but desire at least has the dignity of reaching for something beyond the self, whereas self-pity, as the final accusation makes clear, is "uninventive," incapable of making anything new.
