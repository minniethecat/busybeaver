# Power

In Mary Butts's *Armed with Madness*, power is the psychological engine that drives the
novel's central conflict. Dudley Carston arrives at the stone house not to experience
the wood, the silence, or the Sanc-Grail but to secure "power over them" (004-002) — the
house's inhabitants. This drive distinguishes him from characters like Picus, Scylla,
Felix, and Ross, for whom power is not something to be seized but something that inheres in
their relationship to place, object, and one another.

## Overview

Power is introduced in Chapter 4 as Carston's explicit motivation for staying at the house.
He "must stay until he had some power over them. That would be his compensation" (004-002).
The word "compensation" is revealing: power, for Carston, is what he seeks in place of the
belonging and participation he cannot feel. It is a substitute for the integration that
Picus embodies naturally and that the others achieve through their shared relationship to
the wood and the Grail.

The theme recurs across at least fifteen passages spanning chapters 4 through 30. It
manifests in several registers: psychological power (Carston's need for dominance), erotic
power (desire as a source and instrument of control), physical power (the body as vessel of
force, particularly Picus's body), and mythic power (the Sanc-Grail as an object of
possession). The novel's resolution hinges on the failure of Carston's model of power: the
power he seeks over others is revealed as hollow beside the power that comes from
participation in the wood's mysteries.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

Power enters the novel's thematic vocabulary in Chapter 4, where Carston's calculations
are laid bare:

> He must stay until he had some power over them. That would be his compensation. (004-002)

The passage that follows makes the calculus explicit: "To have power at a moment's notice,
it is a natural desire" (004-004). But what Carston frames as "natural" is immediately
called into question by the novel's larger structure. The wood's inhabitants do not seek
power over one another; they seek alignment with the wood, the silence, and the Grail.
Carston's craving for power is thus a diagnostic symptom — it reveals his fundamental
alienation from the community he hopes to dominate.

### Key Scenes

- **Carston's Power Calculus** (004-002 through 004-004): In his room, Carston resolves to
  extend his stay not because he is drawn to the place but because he needs leverage. "He
  must stay until he had some power over them" — the phrase "over them" is the operative
  element. Carston cannot conceive of power as a shared condition or an intrinsic quality;
  for him, power is always power-over, a zero-sum game in which one person's gain is
  another's loss. This is the psychological framework of the metropolis (London, Paris, New
  York) that he has brought with him to the downs.

- **Desire as the Source of Power** (010-001): Carston understands that desire and power
  are linked: "Health would give him power. Also he would desire Scylla more." The equation
  is double-edged — desiring Scylla more would, in his thinking, increase his power, but
  the novel suggests the reverse: Carston's desire for Scylla is itself a form of
  dependence, a vulnerability that Scylla, who dances with Americans and smiles as they
  sing (007-027), already knows how to manage.

- **The Starved Instinct for Power** (006-039): In one of the novel's most direct
  statements on the theme, a character observes that "our instinct for power is starved.
  For we come of families..." — cut off before the full genealogy is stated. The phrase
  "starved instinct" is crucial: power is not presented as inherently corrupt but as a
  natural drive that, when denied its proper objects and channels, becomes pathological.
  Carston's hunger for power "over them" is a starvation response — the instinct, denied
  its proper expression in relationship and participation, turns predatory.

- **Power as Idol** (017-020): Carston is described "sobbing, whistling back his power as
  their idol, like a god summoned." This remarkable image casts power as a deity that
  Carston must conjure and propitiate — an inversion of the Grail's logic, where the sacred
  object does not need to be summoned but must be approached with the right question. The
  "whistling" suggests desperation; Carston's power is not a possession but a fugitive, a
  thing that must be called back when it strays.

- **Picus's Physical Power** (005-001, 004-008): Contrasted with Carston's psychological
  maneuvering is Picus's physical power — "exceedingly powerful in body, he looked like
  water" (005-001), "You saw how powerful his body is, but he's like blowing birds' eggs"
  (004-008). Picus's power is intrinsic, effortless, and fragile — "like water," "like
  blowing birds' eggs." It does not need to be asserted because it is simply what he is.
  The juxtaposition with Carston's compulsive power-seeking is one of the novel's
  structural ironies.

- **Nosing Round for Power** (012-004): Another character observes that trouble has "come
  of his nosing round for power." The verb "nosing" reduces Carston's ambition to something
  animal and undignified — a scavenger's search, not a conqueror's campaign. The phrase
  captures the novel's judgment on Carston's project: his pursuit of power is not
  magnificent but petty, a rooting about for leverage in a place where leverage is
  meaningless.

> "Swank," she said, "and instinct. To cover quite intolerable pain, we who are lovers,
> and our instinct for power is starved." (006-039)

### Additional PN References

- "transparent and powerful, malicious and shy" (009-016) — power as a quality of
  presence, here describing the uncanny atmosphere around Picus
- "castles for a life of power and danger" (025-005) — power associated with the archaic
  and the romantic, a nostalgic projection onto the past
- "His light under control, he looked round" (007-018) — Carston's compulsion to control
  extends even to his perception, adjusting his "light" before surveying a scene

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

Power in *Armed with Madness* is not singular but plural. There is the power that Carston
seeks — power-over, the power to compel, manipulate, and dominate — and there is the power
that the novel's central characters possess without seeking it. This second kind of power
is closer to what we might call presence or charisma: Picus's body "exceedingly powerful"
yet "like water" (005-001), Scylla's erotic magnetism, the wood's uncanny silence, the
Sanc-Grail's capacity to transform those who encounter it.

Carston's tragedy is that he can only conceive of the first kind. He arrives at the house
with the instincts of the metropolis — the belief that all relationships are negotiations,
that all value is possession, that power is always and only power over others. The
"compensation" he seeks (004-002) is his payment for enduring a place he does not
understand and people he cannot inhabit. When his project fails — when the power he has
"nosed round for" (012-004) proves insufficient — he is left with nothing, because he
never understood that power in the novel's deeper sense is not something you take but
something you participate in.

The "starved instinct for power" (006-039) is the novel's most acute diagnosis. The
characters who belong to the wood are not without the instinct — they have simply found
channels for it that do not require domination. Their power is expressed through ritual
(the gramophone played "to appease"), through relationship (the shared stewardship of the
Grail), and through identification with the non-human (Picus's marsh-bird dance). Carston,
by contrast, is starved — and starvation makes the instinct predatory.

### Mythic / Symbolic Dimension

The Grail mythology that structures the novel offers a radically different model of power.
In the Grail romances, power comes not from seizing the sacred object but from asking the
right question — from participating in a ritual logic that the quester must learn, not
impose. The Fisher King's wound is in some versions a punishment for a forbidden seizure,
a taking that should have been a receiving. Carston's attempt to secure "power over them"
replicates this pattern: he tries to take what can only be given, and the attempt leaves
him — like the Fisher King — wounded in ways he cannot name.

The contrast between Picus's physical power and Carston's psychological power maps onto a
deeper opposition in modernist thought between being and having. Picus's power is a quality
of what he is; Carston's is a quantity of what he can hold. This distinction recalls the
philosophical tradition running from Kierkegaard through Heidegger — power as presence
versus power as possession — but Butts renders it in concrete, novelistic terms, through
the daily life of the stone house and the wood.

In the interwar context, Carston's power-seeking also has a political resonance. His
assumption that all relationships reduce to domination, his inability to imagine
participation without hierarchy, his reduction of others to instruments of his own
compensation — these are the pathologies that the First World War had made visible on a
continental scale. Butts, writing in the war's aftermath, offers the stone house and the
wood not as escape but as alternative: a model of power that is participatory rather than
domineering, immanent rather than imposed.
