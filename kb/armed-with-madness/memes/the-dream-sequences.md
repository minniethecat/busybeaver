# The Dream Sequences

Clarence dreams twice in the novel. Both dreams are narrated in full — rare moments when the novel's external, scenic method gives way to interiority. "He dreamed that he was in a shell-hole and someone had given him an ice" (005-017). "Asleep again, dreamed he was bleeding to death" (005-022). The dreams are the war's return — not as memory but as image, the shell-hole and the bleeding that the waking Clarence has contained but not escaped.

## Prelude

Clarence is a war veteran. "He had seen something in the war, not in the fighting line, but in a base hospital" (005-020). The indirection — "something" — is the conscious mind's refusal to name what the unconscious will deliver in sleep.

## The Event

### The First Dream

Shell-hole. Ice. The juxtaposition of war and comfort, horror and relief, is the dream's logic. Someone gives him ice in a shell-hole — an image of impossible kindness in impossible circumstances.

### The Second Dream

Bleeding to death. The dream strips away the first dream's comfort. The ice is gone; only the bleeding remains.

> "He dreamed that he was in a shell-hole and someone had given him an ice." (005-017)
