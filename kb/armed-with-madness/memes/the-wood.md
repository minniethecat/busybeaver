# The Wood

The Wood is the forest surrounding Gault House on its cliff-point promontory, functioning not as inert scenery but as a personified, enchanted presence that shapes the consciousness of every character who enters its domain. It is a liminal zone where the pagan and the modern collide, a "greenwood" of mythic resonance that must be ritually appeased.

## Overview

The Wood appears throughout the novel, but its presence is most intensely felt in the opening chapters (001-003). It is introduced in the very first paragraph as the source of the house's unsettling quiet, and it persists as an active narrative agent through to the closing passages. The Wood is consistently treated not as background but as a participant — it "giggles," "sighs," stands "like something punished," and demands acknowledgment from the inhabitants of Gault House.

The Wood's physical characteristics are precisely described: it rises from the cliff-point in a single tree, spreads inland in a fan to enclose the house, and contains immense ilex, beech, and oak (001-001). Two paths traverse it to the sea, including one whose "figures of eight" turns startle people (001-005). It has swallowed a lodge (001-006). Yet its physicality is always secondary to its agency.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The Wood enters the novel in its opening paragraph, establishing the paradoxical quality that defines it: the house is "marvellously noisy, but the noises let through silence" — the jays, the hay-cutter, and the breeze all serve as conduits for the Wood's deeper quiet (001-001). The Wood is immediately situated in relation to the house it "encloses," its trees sweeping up "hurrying with squirrels" (001-001).

### Key Scenes

- **The Equivocal Silence (Chapter 1)**: The silence of the Wood is described as "a complicated production" combining the natural silence of stone rooms, empty grass, and "the equivocal, personal silence of the wood" (001-003). This is the novel's definitive statement on the Wood's character — it is not merely quiet but *personally* silent, as though withholding speech. The text notes that "not many nerves could stand it" and that visitors "had been known to leave next day" (001-003).

- **Appeasing the Wood (Chapter 1)**: The inhabitants play a gramophone on the verandah "to appease it and to keep their dancing in hand" (001-004). This ritual of appeasement treats the Wood as a sentient being requiring placation — an animistic practice embedded in modernist domesticity.

- **The Wood Giggle (Chapter 1)**: Speaking of the anticipated visitor Carston, a character warns: "He won't like the wood. The wood will giggle at him" (001-018). The Wood is credited with mockery — a form of judgment — and the ability to reject those who do not belong.

- **The Wood's Innocence (Chapter 2)**: After a night of tension, morning reveals the Wood transformed: "The wood was innocent, the house fresh and serene" (002-002). This moral language — "innocent" — confirms the Wood's personification and its capacity to shift emotional register.

- **The Wood Sighs (Chapter 3)**: Carston, alone and uneasy, experiences the Wood's direct address: "The wood sighed at him. Just like that" (003-010). The sigh marks the Wood's awareness of his discomfort and his unsuitability for its domain. His reflection that "the wood and the woman might be interchangeable" (003-010) links the Wood's enchanted nature to Scylla herself.

- **Carston Surrenders (Chapter 7)**: By the end of his visit, Carston's relationship to the Wood has transformed. Lying in bed after the storm, he reflects: "Their place was his now. And the wood. It stood like something punished under the rain" (007-029). The Wood, like Carston, has been subdued — or perhaps has accepted him.

> The silence let through by the jays, the hay-cutter, and the breeze, was a complicated production of stone rooms, the natural silence of empty grass, and the equivocal, personal silence of the wood. Not many nerves could stand it. (001-003)

> "Tirra lirra. Of course. Almost impersonal. A beauty no stronger than a flower." (021-019)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

The Wood functions as a threshold guardian. Those who belong at Gault House — Scylla, Picus, Felix, Clarence — are in harmony with it and perform rituals to maintain that harmony (the gramophone playing, 001-004). Outsiders like Carston are tested by it. His progress through the novel is marked by his changing relationship to the Wood: from initial alienation ("the wood sighed at him," 003-010) to eventual acceptance ("their place was his now. And the wood," 007-029).

The Wood also acts as an intensifier of consciousness. Picus, looking out at "the wood swimming in the midday heat," waits for an answer from it and begins to dance "like a marsh-bird" (005-001). The Wood does not merely surround the characters — it prompts states of heightened awareness, creative energy, and erotic perception. Carston's developing obsession with Scylla is inseparable from his experience of the Wood's green, transparent world (005-012).

### Mythic/Symbolic Dimension

The Wood explicitly invokes the "true greenwood" of Shakespearean comedy (002-011), associating it with the dream-forests of *A Midsummer Night's Dream* — places where ordinary identities dissolve and transformation becomes possible. But Butts's Wood is darker than Shakespeare's. It is a pagan, animistic space that predates and resists Christian and modern rationalist frameworks.

The Wood's "equivocal, personal silence" (001-003) echoes the ambiguous sanctity of the Grail legend that structures the novel — it is a charged emptiness, a presence defined by withholding. Like the Grail itself, the Wood offers no easy revelation. Characters must earn their place in it through attunement, not conquest.

The Wood also functions as a psychopompic landscape. It is the medium through which the characters move from the domestic sphere of the house to the wildness of the sea-cliffs and the Gault (002-011, 006-030), and back again. It is literally and figuratively a passage between worlds.

### External Critical Perspective

Butts's treatment of landscape as active agent aligns her with the modernist re-enchantment of nature found in D.H. Lawrence and, in a different register, in the mythic method of T.S. Eliot and James Joyce. The Wood is not a symbol standing for something else — it is presented as ontologically real in its agency, a feature of Butts's syncretic paganism that resists allegorical reduction.
