# The Departure

The novel gathers its characters at Gault; it disperses them across the geography it has mapped. The departure is the inverse of the gathering — not a single event but a process of leave-taking, each character departing in their own way and for their own reasons. "You can come back to Gault, if they want you, when you've finished" (033-140): the departure is the condition of the return. You must leave to come back.

## The Event

Characters leave Gault as they arrived: individually, partially, with unfinished business. Picus goes to Tambourne. Carston pursues Tracy. Scylla walks — or starts to walk. The departure is the novel's acknowledgment that the Grail quest, in its modernist form, does not end with the achievement of the Grail but with the scattering of the questers.
