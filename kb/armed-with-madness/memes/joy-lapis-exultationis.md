# Joy (Lapis Exultationis)

Picus's personal transformation of the medieval Grail concept — from *lapis exilii* (stone of exile) to *lapis exultationis* (stone of joy) — embodying the novel's understanding of joy as a fugitive, precarious state that must be seized but can never be held.

## Overview

The concept of joy in *Armed with Madness* is inseparable from Picus's dream-vision in Chapter V, where the Grail's traditional designation as the "stone of exile" is playfully overwritten by a "private fancy to call it the *lapis exultationis*, the stone of joy" (005-019). This is not a doctrinal claim about the Grail but a deliberate act of modernist mythmaking — Picus's dream acknowledges that "the thing had never existed" before pivoting to its rechristening as the "joy-stone" (005-019). Throughout the novel, joy appears not as a stable possession but as a condition that "flies" past (016-031), "spills over from a reserve" (007-027), or must be "allowed" (005-020) and "managed" (004-011) — always at risk, always contingent.

## Textual Evidence

### First Appearance

The concept enters the novel through Picus's dream, which collapses sacred etymology into phonetic association: the Grail is named *lapis exilii* (stone of exile) in Wolfram von Eschenbach's *Parzival*, but Picus's sleeping mind slides from *exilii* to *exilis* (the slender stone) to his own coinage, *lapis exultationis* — the stone of joy (005-019). The dream further acknowledges that "no grail texts were clear what the thing was really called" (005-019), making Picus's renaming an act of creative reinterpretation rather than scholarly correction.

### Key Scenes

- **Picus's dream (Ch. V)**: The foundational scene. Picus dreams of another self carrying the Sanc-Grail as a glass dish, reciting the traditional Grail names before arriving at his "private fancy": the stone of joy. The dream concludes with the Freudian intrusion ("Freud again") — a reminder that the novel treats myth as never free of psychological determination (005-019).

- **Scylla dancing (Ch. VII)**: While dancing with an American, Scylla smiles "with something spilt over from a reserve of joy" (007-027). In the same scene, Picus dances with the Grail-cup on his head, catching it as it falls — "and it was empty all the time" (007-027). The image links joy, performance, and emptiness: joy is a surplus that cannot be stored.

- **Felix on Scylla (Ch. IV)**: Felix explains his sister's appetite for experience: "she'll understand it and manage it. And enjoy it" (004-011). Joy here is presented as a capacity requiring skill — understanding, management — not a passive reception.

- **Scylla and the cup (Ch. XII)**: "My lover who gave it me. We who have enjoyed it" (012-030). Scylla's declaration ties enjoyment to possession of the Grail and to erotic partnership. Joy is communal, shared between lovers, and inseparable from the object that catalyzes it.

- **The missed joy (Ch. XVI)**: Reflecting on Picus, an unnamed speaker laments: "I did not catch the joy as it flew" (016-031). This is the novel's most explicit statement of joy's fugitive nature — it is something that must be caught in flight, and the failure to do so is a central regret.

> "It's the *lapis exilii*, the stone of exile. What I'm walking on is the *lapis exilis*, the slender stone. All the same, my dear." Then the neat reminder that no grail texts were clear what the thing was really called. Then his private fancy to call it the *lapis exultationis*, the stone of joy. That the thing had never existed. The joy-stone. (005-019)

## Interpretation

### Within the Novel

The novel's understanding of joy is paradoxical: it is both the highest value the Grail can represent (Picus's preferred name) and something that cannot be directly pursued. The characters who seek joy most intently — Picus, Scylla, Felix — are also those most vulnerable to its loss. Picus's dream asserts the joy-stone's nonexistence in the same breath that names it: "That the thing had never existed. The joy-stone" (005-019). Joy in *Armed with Madness* is thus a projection, a transient state of grace that appears only in retrospect or in flight. Carston's outsider perspective reinforces this: he is the one who envies others' enjoyment ("if he wasn't allowed to enjoy it," 005-020) but is constitutionally unable to participate.

"Scylla's a different egg... she'll understand it and manage it. And enjoy it" (004-011). Felix's description of his sister presents enjoyment as a discipline, not a gift — something that requires management and understanding. The American's observation at the novel's opening — "One always enjoys something" (001-016) — is both a truism and a challenge: enjoyment is inescapable, but what one enjoys and how one enjoys it defines character.

### Mythic/Symbolic Dimension

The *lapis exultationis* participates in the novel's larger pattern of taking medieval Christian myth and transposing it into aesthetic-erotic key. Wolfram's *Parzival* names the Grail a stone (*lapsit exillis*), and Picus's dream explicitly references this tradition before enacting a modernist "correction": exile becomes exultation, longing becomes joy. Yet the joy in question is never uncomplicated. Scylla's "reserve of joy" (007-027) suggests a limited resource, something that can be depleted. The Grail cup that Picus dances with is empty (007-027) — the vessel of joy contains nothing.

This reconfiguration aligns with the novel's broader treatment of the Grail legend as a "story that has never come off, or found its form or its poet" (013-003). By renaming the Grail the joy-stone, Picus attempts to complete the incomplete myth, to give it the form it has never found. That the attempt occurs in a dream, immediately followed by the deflating recollection of "Freud again" (005-019), suggests the impossibility of the project — and its necessity.

### Critical Perspectives

The *lapis exultationis* has been read as Butts's contribution to the modernist revaluation of joy as an aesthetic category. Where Pater had celebrated the "hard, gemlike flame" of momentary experience, Butts presents joy as something more precarious — a "reserve" that spills over, a bird that must be caught in flight. The Grail, traditionally an object of spiritual quest, becomes in Picus's dream the symbol of a joy that is at once supreme and nonexistent, sacred and Freudian, medieval and modern. This doubleness is characteristic of Butts's method throughout the novel.
