# Methodism

Methodism was the religious tradition that most profoundly shaped [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s life. His conversion at a camp meeting, his career as a Methodist preacher, and his connection to the [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]] were central to his identity as both an enslaved man and later as a free community leader in [[canada|Canada]]. "My heart exults with gratitude," [[josiah-henson|Henson]] wrote, "when I mention the name of a good man who first taught me the blessedness of religion" (004-001) — and that religion, for [[josiah-henson|Henson]], was Methodism.

## Definition

Methodism in early 19th-century [[us-america|America]] was an evangelical Protestant denomination that had grown explosively since its founding by John Wesley in 18th-century [[england|England]]. Characterized by itinerant preaching, camp meetings, extemporaneous [[prayer|pray]]er, and an emphasis on personal conversion and holy living, Methodism was uniquely accessible to the illiterate — including enslaved people — because it did not require book [[education|learning]] for participation. By 1850, the [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]] was the largest Protestant denomination in the [[us-america|United States]].

## Henson's Conversion and Early Ministry

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s religious awakening occurred through the preaching of John McKenny, a man who lived near [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s [[plantation]] in [[maryland|Maryland]]:

> "My heart exults with gratitude when I mention the name of a good man who first taught me the blessedness of religion. His name was John McKenny. He lived at Georgetown, a few miles only from Riley's plantation." (004-001)

After hearing a sermon that presented "the divine character of Jesus Christ, his tender love for mankind, his forgiving spirit his compassion for the outcast and despised" (004-002), [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was overcome with conviction. He turned aside into the woods on his way home and "[[prayer|pray]]ed to God for light and for aid with an earnestness, which, however unenlightened, was at least sincere" (004-003). This solitary [[prayer|pray]]er in the woods marked his conversion.

Following his conversion, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] quickly became known for his religious fervor and gift for exhortation. His reputation as a preacher spread throughout the region, and he began traveling to preach among both enslaved and free communities.

## Methodist Episcopal Church

The formal recognition of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s ministry came through the [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]]. The connection was made through a white Methodist preacher who visited the neighborhood in 1828:

> "In the course of the summer of 1828, a Methodist preacher, a most excellent white man, visited our neighborhood, and I became acquainted with him. He was the first white preacher I had ever seen who seemed to feel that colored people had souls to be saved." (007-002)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s approach to securing this preacher's endorsement was characteristically resourceful. As he recounted, he opened the subject one [[sabbath|Sunday]] morning while shaving his master, [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]], "adroitly managing, by bringing the shaving brush somewhat too near his mouth" (007-007, paraphrased from the passage where he brings up the subject during shaving). With [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s blessing, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] attended the Methodist Episcopal Conference and received official credentials:

> "The certificate he gave me allowed me to pass and repass between Kentucky and Maryland as servant of Amos Riley... By his advice I then purchased a decent suit of clothes and an excellent horse, and travelled from town to town, preaching wherever I could obtain hearers." (007-008, 010)

This institutional affiliation provided [[josiah-henson|Henson]] with credentials and mobility that would prove invaluable — not only for his ministry but also as practical cover for his later escape planning.

## Methodist Theology and Anti-Slavery Sentiment

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Methodist faith emphasized personal salvation, moral responsibility, and the equality of all souls before God. The foundational text that shaped his understanding — "He, by the grace of God, tasted death for every man" (004-001) — was from the Epistle to the Hebrews and carried, in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s interpretation, an implicit challenge to the racial hierarchy of slavery. If Christ died for every person, then every person — regardless of race or legal status — possessed a soul of equal worth.

These theological commitments informed [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s critique of slavery and his later work as an abolitionist and community builder in [[canada|Canada]]. His ministry combined evangelical fervor with practical efforts to improve the material conditions of Black communities. When reflecting on his improbable path, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] acknowledged that his effectiveness as a preacher did not come from theological education but from lived experience:

> "It may, nay, I am aware it must, seem strange to many, that a man so ignorant as myself, unable to read, and having heard so little as I had of religion, natural or revealed, should be able to preach." (014-004)

## Historical Context

Methodism in [[us-america|America]] grew from fewer than 15,000 members in 1784 to over 1.3 million by 1850, making it the nation's largest Protestant body. Its growth among Black [[us-america|America]]ns — both enslaved and free — was particularly significant. The African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church, founded in 1816 by Richard Allen, was the first independent Black denomination in the [[us-america|United States]], though [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself operated within the predominantly white [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]] structure.

The camp meeting, a frontier institution central to early [[us-america|America]]n Methodism, was particularly important in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s story. These large outdoor revival gatherings drew thousands across racial lines and were among the few spaces in the antebellum South where Black and white worshippers could experience spiritual equality — if only temporarily. The emotional intensity and extemporaneous preaching style of camp meetings suited [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s natural gifts as an exhorter.
