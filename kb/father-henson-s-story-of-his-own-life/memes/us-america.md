# United States of America

The United States of America was the nation in which [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] spent the first forty-one years of his life as an enslaved person. Throughout his narrative, the United States is contrasted sharply with [[canada|Canada]] and [[england|England]] as a land where slavery was legal, human rights were denied, and the [[fugitive-slave-act|Fugitive Slave Act]] made even free states dangerous for people of African descent.

## The Land of Bondage

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative presents the United States primarily as the theater of his [[slavery|enslavement]] in [[maryland|Maryland]] and [[kentucky|Kentucky]]. He describes the conditions of [[slavery|chattel slavery]] in the American South with unsparing detail — the separation of families at auction, the brutal physical punishments, and the legal system that treated enslaved people as property.

## Contrast with England

During his travels to [[england|England]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] drew explicit comparisons between American oppression and English freedom:

> "I had traveled in the United States, and England; but never did I see any human beings so degraded as were these poor creatures in the very heart of a Christian city." (012-008)

This observation — that degradation in America exceeded even what he witnessed in the slums of [[london|London]] — became a central theme of his [[speaking-in-england|speaking engagements in England]].

## The Fugitive Slave Act

The passage of the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] in 1850 transformed even the free states of the North into dangerous territory for escaped slaves:

> "A law was passed in the United States, which made it quite an offence to harbor or conceal a fugitive slave." (019-006)

This legislation made [[canada|Canada]] the only truly safe destination for fugitives, as American free states could no longer offer secure refuge. The law required citizens in free states to assist in the capture and return of fugitive slaves, criminalizing the very acts of aid and shelter that the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] depended upon.

### The Custom House Incident

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] experienced the absurdity of the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Law]] firsthand during his lumber trading in [[boston|Boston]]. When paying customs duties, he jokingly warned the officer that dealing with a fugitive slave might be illegal:

> "When the Custom House officer presented his bill to me for the duties on my lumber, I jokingly remarked to him that perhaps he would render himself liable to trouble if he should have dealings with a fugitive slave, and if so I would relieve him of the trouble of taking my money. 'Are you a fugitive slave, Sir?' 'Yes, Sir,' said I; 'and perhaps you had better not have any dealings with me.' 'I have nothing to do with that,' said the official; 'there is your bill. You have acted like a man, and I deal with you as a man.'" (019-006)

The officer's response — recognizing [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s dignity over the law's demand — was a small but telling rebuke to the legal regime that denied personhood to millions.

## Henson's Critique

Speaking before British audiences, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] challenged the American claim to [[freedom|liberty]]. At a [[sabbath|Sunday]] School anniversary in [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]], when a speaker from [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] boasted that all classes indiscriminately enjoyed the benefits of American institutions, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] rose to contradict him:

> "[He] described the condition of the colored people in the United States, and asserted that all classes indiscriminately enjoyed there the rights of man." (022-001)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] pointed out that in the Southern States, the great body of colored people were almost entirely neglected, and in many places excluded altogether from institutions like [[sabbath|Sunday]] Schools. His testimony carried the weight of personal experience, and as he noted, "my own personal observation and experience carried conviction to the people" (022-001).
