Lambeth Palace is the [[london|London]] residence of the Archbishop of Canterbury, the highest cleric in the Church of [[england|England]]. During [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s visit to [[england|England]], the noted philanthropist [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]] arranged for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to have an interview with the Archbishop at Lambeth Palace. (022-003)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] had been told by Gurney that the Archbishop might grant him an interview of perhaps a quarter of an hour. In the event, the meeting lasted an hour and a half, and the Archbishop was so moved by [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s simple account of his life that he shed tears freely. Upon parting, the Archbishop placed five golden sovereigns in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s hands and begged him to call again if ever he returned to [[england|England]]. (022-003)(022-004)

## The "University of Adversity"

The interview at Lambeth Palace gave rise to one of the most celebrated exchanges in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s autobiography. After about half an hour's conversation on the condition of enslaved people and [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s plans, the Archbishop posed an unexpected question:

> "At what university, Sir, did you graduate?"
> "I graduated, your grace," said I in reply, "at the university of adversity."
> "The university of adversity," said he, looking up with astonishment; "where is that?"
> I saw his surprise, and explained. "It was my lot, your grace, to be born a slave, and to pass my boyhood and all the former part of my life as a slave. I never entered a school, never read the Bible in my youth, and received all of my training under the most adverse circumstances. This is what I meant by graduating in the university of adversity."
>
> (022-003)

The Archbishop, astonished that an unschooled man could speak with such eloquence, inquired further about [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s early religious education. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] related how his mother had taught him the Lord's [[prayer|Prayer]], and how his first knowledge of Christ came through hearing a sermon preached on the text, "He, by the grace of God, tasted death for every man" — a text the Archbishop pronounced "beautiful." (022-003)
