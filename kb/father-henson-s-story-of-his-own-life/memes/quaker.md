# Quakerism

The Religious Society of Friends, commonly known as Quakers, played a distinctive role in the antislavery movement and provided direct humanitarian assistance to [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] and other freedom-seekers during their journey to [[canada|Canada]].

## Definition

Quakerism emerged in 17th-century [[england|England]] under the leadership of George Fox, emphasizing the "Inner Light" — the belief that God speaks directly to every person without the need for clergy or formal liturgy. Quakers were among the earliest and most consistent white opponents of slavery in [[us-america|America]], with individual Quakers and Quaker meetings providing material support, shelter, and legal advocacy for fugitives from slavery. Their plain dress, pacifism, and commitment to social equality made them a distinctive presence in 19th-century [[us-america|America]]n reform movements.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] encounters Quaker assistance directly during his escape journey. After one of his party falls gravely ill, a Quaker family takes them in and cares for the ailing boy. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] describes the arrangement: "It was arranged that the boy should remain behind until, through the blessing of God, he should recover" (017-012). Months later, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] joyfully reunites with the boy, "no longer attenuated in frame, but robust and healthy," having been restored under "the kind care of the Quaker" (017-016). This episode illustrates the practical humanitarian role Quakers played along the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]], providing more than ideological support — offering shelter, medical care, and the months of patience required for a sick child to recover.

## Henson's Perspective

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] presents the Quaker family's [[kindness]] as part of God's providential care, referring to the family who sheltered them as "this happy family" from whom they "received every [[kindness]]" (017-012). He does not analyze Quaker theology but bears grateful witness to Quaker practice: concrete acts of mercy that made the difference between life and death for those fleeing [[slavery|bondage]]. The Quaker's care is described without qualification — it is simply and profoundly good.

## Historical Context

Quaker abolitionism had deep roots. As early as 1688, Quakers in Germantown, [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]], issued the first formal protest against slavery in the [[us-america|America]]n colonies. By the 19th century, Quaker networks formed a backbone of the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]], with figures like Levi Coffin (known as the "President of the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]]") coordinating escape routes from the South through the free states to [[canada|Canada]]. Quaker meetinghouses served as stations, and Quaker families risked legal penalty to harbor fugitives. Their theological commitment to human equality and their organizational networks made them uniquely effective in the antislavery cause.

> After the lapse of a few months, on one joyous Sabbath morning, I had the happiness of clasping the poor boy we had left in the kind care of the Quaker, no longer attenuated in frame, but robust and healthy. (017-016)
