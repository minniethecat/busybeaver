# Methodist Circuit Preaching

The practice of itinerant [[methodism|Methodist preacher]]s traveling designated circuits to minister to scattered congregations on the [[us-america|America]]n frontier. [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] became part of this tradition in the late 1820s, using his preaching license to travel between [[kentucky|Kentucky]], [[ohio|Ohio]], and [[maryland|Maryland]], delivering sermons and exhortations at camp meetings and churches.

## Definition

Methodist circuit preaching, or circuit riding, was the system by which Methodist ministers traveled on horseback along fixed geographical routes (circuits) to serve multiple congregations that lacked resident clergy. In the context of 19th-century [[us-america|America]], particularly in frontier regions like [[kentucky|Kentucky]] and [[ohio|Ohio]], circuit riders were often the sole source of organized religious life for isolated communities. The system was a defining feature of early [[us-america|America]]n [[methodism|Methodism]] and contributed to the denomination's rapid growth in the early republic.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s preaching career began in the summer of 1828, when he encountered a [[methodism|Methodist preacher]] — "a most excellent white man" — whose sermons deeply moved him (007-006). Though [[josiah-henson|Henson]] acknowledged that "preaching never could have been my vocation" by natural inclination, he was persuaded to begin exhorting and soon obtained a recommendation from his Methodist friend to preach (007-008).

He "travelled from town to town preaching as I went," finding receptive audiences wherever he spoke (007-010). His preaching tours took him through [[ohio|Ohio]], where he spent what he called "pleasant and hopeful days" (012-001). The circuit system gave [[josiah-henson|Henson]] unusual mobility for an enslaved man, allowing him to travel across state lines and interact with free black communities and white abolitionists.

In [[canada|Canada]], after his escape, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] resumed preaching and became a recognized religious leader among the fugitive slave community (014-004). He preached in the pulpits of established ministers and continued circuit-style ministry to the scattered settlements of former slaves (020-007).

## Henson's Perspective

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] viewed his preaching as a divine calling rather than a career choice. He recorded his initial reluctance, saying he felt unworthy to preach, but was moved by a sense of spiritual obligation (014-006). His sermons emphasized the themes of sin and redemption, divine providence, and the equality of all people before God — themes drawn directly from his own experiences of [[slavery|enslavement]] and liberation (016-002).

## Historical Context

The [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]] grew explosively in the [[us-america|United States]] between 1800 and 1850, largely due to the circuit rider system. By the 1820s, when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] began preaching, [[methodism|Methodism]] had become the largest Protestant denomination in [[us-america|America]]. The camp meeting — a large outdoor religious gathering lasting several days — was a distinctive feature of frontier [[methodism|Methodism]] and served as one context for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s preaching.

Circuit preaching held particular significance for enslaved and free African [[us-america|America]]ns. Methodist theology's emphasis on spiritual equality, personal conversion, and emotional worship resonated deeply. Many early black religious leaders, including Richard Allen (founder of the African [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]]), emerged from Methodist traditions. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s preaching license represented a rare form of authorized authority and dignity within the oppressive structure of slavery.

> "In the course of the summer of 1828, a Methodist preacher, a most excellent white man, visited our neighborhood, and his preaching made a deep impression upon my mind." (007-006)
