# Samuel Gurney

**Samuel Gurney** (1786–1856) was a British banker, philanthropist, and abolitionist who played a significant role in supporting [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] during his fundraising tour of [[england|England]] in 1851–1852.

## Role in Henson's England Tour

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] traveled to [[england|England]] to exhibit black walnut lumber at the 1851 [[worlds-fair-london|Great Exhibition]] and to raise funds for the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]], he carried letters of introduction to prominent British figures, including Samuel Gurney:

> I accordingly left for England, being readily furnished with very complimentary letters of introduction to such men as Thomas Binney, Samuel Gurney, Lord Brougham, Hon. Abbot Lawrence, then American Minister to England... (021-001)

## Support Committee

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s character was attacked by a published circular accusing him of being an impostor, a committee of twelve prominent British men was appointed to examine the merits of his enterprise. Samuel Gurney served on this committee alongside his son, Samuel Gurney Junior, and other notable abolitionists including Lord Ashley (later Earl of Shaftesbury) and [[john-scobell|John Scobell]], Secretary of the British and Foreign Anti-[[slavery|Slavery]] Society.

> This committee had been appointed, and consisted of Samuel Gurney, Samuel Gurney, Junior, Samuel Marley, Esq., George Hitchcock, Esq., Rev. James Sherman, Rev. Thomas Binney... (022-001)

The sub-committee appointed Samuel Gurney Junior as treasurer, ensuring that all funds contributed to [[josiah-henson|Henson]] were properly managed.

## Introduction to the Archbishop of Canterbury

Gurney introduced [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to the Archbishop of Canterbury, arranging what was expected to be a fifteen-minute interview. The meeting extended to an hour and a half, during which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recounted his life story to the deeply moved archbishop:

> Samuel Gurney, the noted philanthropist, introduced me, by a note and his family card, to his grace, the archbishop. He received me kindly in his palace. (023-001)

## Legacy

As a member of the prominent Gurney family of [[quaker|Quaker]] bankers and reformers, Samuel Gurney represented the network of British evangelical abolitionists who provided crucial financial and moral support to fugitive slaves and African-[[us-america|America]]n activists visiting [[england|England]] in the mid-nineteenth century.
