# Kentucky

Kentucky was the slave state to which [[Josiah Henson]] was sent in 1825 to manage the [[plantation]] of [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] in Davies County, and the location from which he ultimately escaped to freedom in 1830. The state figures prominently in his narrative as both a site of religious awakening and a place of continued [[slavery|bondage]] from which flight was the only permanent solution.

## Location & Geography

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived at "Davis county, Kentucky, about the middle of April, 1825," where [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] "had a large [[plantation]], with from eighty to one hundred negroes" situated "about five miles south of [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River, and fifteen miles above the Yellow Banks, on Big Blackfords Creek" (007-001). Bourbon County, deeper in the interior, was another significant location where [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later recruited fugitives for escape (016-005).

## Role in the Narrative

Kentucky serves as the middle term in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s geographic arc — no longer [[Maryland]] (birthplace and early [[slavery|enslavement]]) but not yet [[Canada]] (freedom). It was here that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] "had the general management" of the [[plantation]] and deepened his Methodist faith, becoming "admitted as a preacher by a Quarterly Conference of the [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist Episcopal Church]]" (007-002). Yet Kentucky also brought fresh trauma when [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]]'s financial troubles led to the threatened sale of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s enslaved companions (007-003).

The [[Journey to Kentucky]] itself — transporting eighteen enslaved people nearly a thousand miles from [[maryland|Maryland]] through mid-winter 1825 — is one of the epic set-pieces of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s autobiography (006-007 to 006-011). [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later returned to Kentucky on underground missions, crossing "[[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River into Kentucky" to rescue others and recruiting "about thirty collected from different States" for a mass escape through [[ohio|Ohio]] to [[canada|Canada]] (016-004, 016-006).

## Historical Context

Kentucky was a border slave state that remained in the Union during the Civil War, but in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era (1825–1830) it was fully integrated into the slave economy. Its location across [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River from the free states of [[ohio|Ohio]] and [[indiana|Indiana]] made it a critical transit zone for the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] — [[mississippi-river|the river]] represented the physical boundary between slavery and nominal freedom.

## Key Passages

> "I arrived at Davis county, Kentucky, about the middle of April, 1825, and delivered myself and my companions to Mr. Amos Riley, the brother of my owner, who had a large plantation, with from eighty to one hundred negroes." (007-001)

> "In Kentucky the opportunities of attending on the preaching of whites, as well as of blacks, were more numerous; and partly by attending them, and the camp-meetings which occurred from time to time... I became better acquainted with those religious feelings which are deeply implanted in the breast of every human being." (007-001)
