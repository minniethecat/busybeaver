The **first sermon** marked the beginning of [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s career as a preacher, which began in the summer of 1828 when he met a Methodist minister in [[kentucky|Kentucky]]. Despite being unable to read and having no formal theological training, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] became an itinerant preacher, traveling from town to town and receiving unexpected [[kindness]] and respect from both black and white audiences.

## Background

After [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had transported a group of enslaved people from [[maryland|Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] for his master, he remained in [[kentucky|Davis County]] on the [[plantation]] of Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]]. It was there, in the summer of 1828, that a turning point came through a chance encounter with a white [[methodism|Methodist preacher]]:[007-006]

> In the course of the summer of 1828, a Methodist preacher, a most excellent white man, visited our neighborhood, and I became acquainted with him. He was soon interested in me, and visited me frequently.
> [007-006]

The minister became a close friend and mentor, writing letters of recommendation on [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s behalf and providing him with a certificate that allowed him to travel between [[kentucky|Kentucky]] and [[maryland|Maryland]].[007-008]

## The Call to Preach

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] was acutely aware of his lack of formal education, but he believed that sincere religious conviction did not require theological expertise:

> No great amount of theological knowledge is requisite for the purpose. If it had been, it is manifest enough that preaching never could have been my vocation; but I am persuaded that, speaking from the fulness of the heart, with the deepest sense of the truth of the gospel, may be done by any one.
> [007-002]

His motivation came not from ambition but from a deep conviction that he had been spared for a purpose, and that he had an obligation to share his faith with others.

## Securing Permission to Preach

To travel and preach, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] needed permission from his master, Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]]. He approached the subject strategically one [[sabbath|Sunday]] morning while shaving [[isaac-riley|Riley]]:

> I opened the subject one Sunday morning while shaving Mr. Amos, and adroitly managed, by bringing the shaving brush close into his mouth whenever he was disposed to interrupt me, to "get a good say" first.
> [007-007]

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] argued that his preaching would bring credit to [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s [[plantation]], as it would be talked about throughout the region that "Mr. [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s man" was a preacher. [[isaac-riley|Riley]], moved by this appeal to his reputation, agreed.[007-007]

## The First Preaching Tour

Armed with a travel certificate and letters of recommendation from his Methodist friend, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] embarked on his first preaching tour. A letter he carried to a contact in [[cincinnati|Cincinnati]] opened doors, and he soon found himself with a network of supporters:[007-009]

> A new era in my history now opened upon me. A letter I carried with me to a kind-hearted man in Cincinnati procured me a number of invaluable friends, who entered heart and soul into my plans.
> [007-009]

On the advice of these friends, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] purchased decent clothes and a good horse, and began traveling from town to town:

> By his advice I then purchased a decent suit of clothes and an excellent horse, and travelled from town to town preaching as I went. Everywhere I met with kindness. The contrast between the respect with which I was now received, and the ordinary lot of a slave, filled me with joy.
> [007-010]

## Significance

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s entry into preaching was transformative on multiple levels:

1. **Personal dignity**: For the first time, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] experienced being treated with respect by white people — a stark contrast to his normal condition as a slave.

2. **Economic independence**: The preaching tours allowed [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to earn and save money independently, ultimately accumulating two hundred and seventy-five dollars toward the purchase of his freedom.[008-001]

3. **Network building**: Through preaching, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] built relationships with abolitionists and sympathetic whites in [[ohio|Ohio]] and other free states that would later prove invaluable when he planned his escape to [[canada|Canada]].

4. **Moral foundation**: The preaching experience deepened [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s religious conviction and reinforced his sense of having a divine purpose — themes that permeate his entire narrative.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s preaching career spanned decades and eventually took him to [[england|England]], where he preached in prominent pulpits including those of [[thomas-binney|Rev. Thomas Binney]] and Rev. [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]] in [[london|London]].[020-007]
