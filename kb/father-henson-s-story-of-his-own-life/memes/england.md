# England

England was the destination of [[Josiah Henson]]'s two fundraising and speaking tours in the early 1850s. His reception there — meeting with the Archbishop of Canterbury, dining with [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]], addressing crowds at [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]], and attending the 1851 [[london|Great Exhibition]] — gave him international visibility and secured crucial financial support for the [[Dawn Settlement]] in [[Canada]].

## Location & Geography

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s travels centered on [[london|London]], where he attended the [[World's Fair in London|Great Exhibition at the Crystal Palace]] (021-001), spoke at [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]] (022-001), visited Ragged Schools (022-001), and met with political and religious leaders. In his narrative, England is presented as a place of sharp contrast with the [[United States of America]] — a place where his humanity was recognized and his speech heard with respect.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s first visit to England in 1850–51 was driven by the financial needs of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]]. "My project was to go to England, carrying with me some of the best specimens of black walnut lumber" to sell, while simultaneously advocating for the cause of fugitive slaves (020-004). He carried letters of introduction from Governor Johnstone of Missouri, the [[us-america|America]]n Minister to England, and Rev. [[John Rolfe]] of [[toronto|Toronto]] (020-005).

The visit yielded far more than lumber sales. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s powerful personal story and oratory made him a sought-after speaker. He addressed "many of the great anniversaries" and spoke "on several occasions…to make known the true condition of slaves, in [[exeter-hall|Exeter Hall]] and other places" (022-001). His most memorable encounter was with the Archbishop of Canterbury, who was moved to tears by [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s simple account of his life, exclaiming: "Is it possible that you are not a scholar?" (022-003).

On his second visit in 1852, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] dined at the estate of [[Lord John Russell]], then Prime Minister of England, with three hundred [[sabbath|Sabbath]] School teachers in "a spacious dining hall, whose dimensions could not have been less than one hundred feet by sixty" (022-005). The stark contrast between this elegance and "the lot of millions of my colored brethren in [[us-america|America]]" moved [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to a profound reflection recorded in the narrative (022-005).

## Historical Context

In the early 1850s, British abolitionism was a powerful political force. Having abolished slavery in the Empire in 1834, British activists now directed their energies toward [[us-america|America]]n slavery. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s tours occurred at the peak of this transatlantic abolitionist network, which included figures such as [[Samuel Gurney]], the noted philanthropist who introduced [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to the Archbishop (022-003). Fundraising from English supporters proved essential to the viability of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]].

## Key Passages

> "I was deeply interested in the Ragged School enterprise, and frequently addressed the schools, and also public meetings held in their behalf. I spent two, months of May, in that country, and attended many of the great anniversaries, and was called upon to speak at many of them." (022-001)

> "He expressed the strongest interest in me, and after about a half hour's conversation he inquired, 'At what university, Sir, did you graduate?' 'I graduated, your grace,' said I in reply, 'at the university of adversity.'" (022-003)

> "O, how different the condition of these happy, sportive, joyful, creatures, from what was once my own condition, and what is now the lot of millions of my colored brethren in America!" (022-005)
