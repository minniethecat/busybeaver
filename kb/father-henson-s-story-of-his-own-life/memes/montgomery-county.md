# Montgomery County

Montgomery County was the [[Maryland]] county where [[Josiah Henson]] spent the formative years of his youth and early adulthood, living on the [[plantation]] of his master [[Isaac Riley]] near the Montgomery court-house. It was here that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] rose from an enslaved child to become the [[trust]]ed [[overseer-system|overseer]] and superintendent of [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s farming and blacksmithing operations (002-005).

## Location & Geography

Montgomery County is situated in central [[Maryland]], northwest of the District of Columbia and adjacent to the Potomac River. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era, the county was a region of mixed [[plantation]] agriculture — primarily [[tobacco-cultivation|tobacco]] and wheat — supported by enslaved labor. The county seat, the Montgomery court-house, was the administrative and legal center where legal matters such as court filings and lawsuits were conducted. [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s blacksmithing operation was located about five miles from the court-house, while the nearby settlements of [[Georgetown]] and [[Washington]] formed [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s commercial circuit for selling farm produce (006-002).

## Role in the Narrative

Montgomery County is the principal setting for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s early life after his purchase by [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] at auction. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother had been bought by [[isaac-riley|Riley]] at a [[auction-block|slave auction]] following the death of Dr. Josiah McPherson; [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself was first sold to a man named Robb but was reunited with his mother when Robb resold him to [[isaac-riley|Riley]], recognizing the proximity of their residences — Robb lived near the Montgomery court-house and [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s blacksmith shop was about five miles away (002-005–007).

It was in Montgomery County that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] endured the savage beating by Bryce Litton, the [[overseer-system|overseer]] of [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s brother, which left him with permanent physical injuries. After the attack, [[isaac-riley|Riley]] rode to the Montgomery Court House to lodge a complaint, but "no negro's testimony could be admitted against a white man," and Litton was acquitted (005-007). The incident illustrates the legal helplessness of enslaved people within the county's — and [[maryland|Maryland]]'s — justice system.

During [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s financial decline, the Montgomery Court House became the scene of his desperate legal proceedings. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recounts that "day after day would he ride over to Montgomery Court House about his business, and every day his affairs grew more desperate" (006-005). It was from the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] [[plantation]] in Montgomery County that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] departed in January 1825 with eighteen enslaved people on the [[Journey to Kentucky]], an expedition ordered by [[isaac-riley|Riley]] to protect his human property from creditors (006-007–009).

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned from [[Ohio]] in 1829, having purchased his freedom, he set his face "towards Montgomery County" and arrived at the old house around Christmas, reflecting with pride on his transformation from "[[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s head nigger" into a man with two hundred and seventy-five dollars of his own earnings (008-001).

## Historical Context

Montgomery County was established in 1776 and named for General Richard Montgomery, a Revolutionary War hero. In the antebellum period, it was a major agricultural county of [[maryland|Maryland]]'s Piedmont region, with an economy dependent on enslaved labor. As a border-state county near the nation's capital, it occupied a contested space in the slavery debate — close enough to free territory that escape was physically possible, yet firmly embedded in the legal and economic structures of the slave system. The county courthouse, frequently mentioned in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative as the site of [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s legal entanglements, was precisely the kind of institution that sustained the slave regime through legal means — adjudicating property disputes over human beings, refusing the testimony of Black witnesses, and enforcing the debts that could tear enslaved families apart.

## Key Passages

> "Her turn came, and she was bought by Isaac Riley of Montgomery county. Then I was offered to the assembled purchasers. My mother, half distracted with the thought of parting forever from all her children, pushed through the crowd, while the bidding for me was going on, to the spot where Riley was standing. She fell at his feet, and clung to his knees, entreating him in tones that a mother only could command, to buy her *baby* as well as herself, and spare to her one, at least, of her little ones." (002-005)

> "Day after day would he ride over to Montgomery Court House about his business, and every day his affairs grew more desperate. He would come into my cabin to tell me how things were going, but spent the time chiefly in lamenting his misfortunes and cursing his brother-in-law." (006-005)

> "Before I left Ohio and set my face towards Montgomery County, I was master of two hundred and seventy-five dollars, besides my horse and clothes. Proud of my success, I enjoyed the thought of showing myself once more in the place where I had been known simply as 'Riley's head nigger.'" (008-001)
