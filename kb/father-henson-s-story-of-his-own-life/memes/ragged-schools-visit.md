# Visits to the Ragged Schools

[[Josiah Henson]]'s engagement with the [[london|London]] Ragged School movement in May 1852 — a series of visits and speeches that brought him into contact with some of the most powerful figures in [[England]] and demonstrated his ability to command the attention of audiences far removed from his origins in [[slavery]].

## Background

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived in [[england|England]] in 1851 as an agent of the [[Dawn Settlement]] community in [[Canada]], seeking to raise funds and political support for the manual-labor school he and [[Hiram Wilson]] had established. His reputation as a fugitive slave, preacher, and community leader preceded him, and he was quickly drawn into the broader world of British evangelical philanthropy.

The Ragged Schools were a network of free schools for destitute children in [[london|London]]'s slums, championed by philanthropists including the banker [[Samuel Gurney]] and supported by prominent clergy and politicians. The movement was one of the great moral causes of mid-Victorian [[england|England]].

## Speaking at Exeter Hall

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] was "frequently called upon to speak at public meetings of various kinds" (022-001). He spent two months — both Mays of his English visits — attending the "great anniversaries" and addressing audiences at [[Exeter Hall]], the monumental meeting hall on the Strand that served as [[london|London]]'s principal venue for philanthropic and religious gatherings.

His most characteristic moment came during a [[sabbath|Sunday]] School Union anniversary, when a speaker from [[Pennsylvania]] "boasted of the great benefits of [[sabbath|Sunday]] Schools in the [[us-america|United States]], and asserted that all classes indiscriminately enjoyed their blessings." [[josiah-henson|Henson]] rose to contradict him:

> "I told the immense meeting that in the Southern States, the great body of the colored people were almost entirely neglected, and in many places they were excluded altogether; and that even in the most of the Northern States, the great mass of the colored children were not sought out and gathered into Sunday Schools. This created some little storm, but my own personal observation and experience carried conviction to the people." (022-001)

## The Archbishop of Canterbury

[[Samuel Gurney]], the [[quaker|Quaker]] banker and philanthropist, arranged an interview for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] with the Archbishop of Canterbury — the highest cleric in the Church of [[england|England]], "the highest beneath the crown" (022-003). Gurney warned [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to expect perhaps a quarter of an hour.

The archbishop received [[josiah-henson|Henson]] in his palace at [[Lambeth]]. After hearing [[josiah-henson|Henson]] describe the condition of his people and his plans for the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]], the archbishop asked, "At what university, Sir, did you graduate?"

> "'I graduated, your grace,' said I in reply, 'at the university of adversity.' 'The university of adversity,' said he, looking up with astonishment; 'where is that?' I saw his surprise, and explained. 'It was my lot, your grace, to be born a slave, and to pass my boyhood and all the former part of my life as a slave. I never entered a school, never read the Bible in my youth, and received all of my training under the most adverse circumstances. This is what I meant by graduating in the university of adversity.'" (022-003)

The archbishop, moved to tears by [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s account of his first sermon — "He, by the grace of God, tasted death for every man" — kept him for an hour and a half instead of the promised fifteen minutes, and at parting pressed "five golden sovereigns" into his hands (022-003 through 022-004).

## Impact and Connections

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s work with the Ragged Schools and his public speeches made him "well acquainted with many of the leading men of [[england|England]]." [[Lord Grey]] proposed sending [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to [[India]] to superintend government cotton cultivation (022-002) — an offer [[josiah-henson|Henson]] declined only because of his commitment to the Canadian settlement.

These connections culminated in an invitation to spend a day at the country estate of [[Lord John Russell]], then Prime Minister of [[england|England]] — an episode [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recorded as "one of the pleasantest days of my life" (022-009).

## Significance

The Ragged School engagement revealed [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s remarkable capacity to operate across social worlds. A man who had never attended school himself stood before the Archbishop of Canterbury and spoke as an equal — not through deference or self-abasement, but through the authority of lived experience and moral clarity. His "university of adversity" reply became, in Victorian philanthropic circles, a celebrated aphorism — evidence that the enslaved could speak for themselves with eloquence that needed no intermediary.
