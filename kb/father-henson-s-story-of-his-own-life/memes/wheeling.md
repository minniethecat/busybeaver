# Wheeling

Wheeling was [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River town where [[Josiah Henson]] arrived with his party of eighteen enslaved people in February 1825, having crossed the mountains from [[Maryland]] via the National Turnpike. It was the transition point between overland and riverine travel on the [[Journey to Kentucky]], and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned to it on foot in 1829, carrying his hard-won manumission papers to the boat that would take him back toward his family.

## Location and Geography

Wheeling was situated on [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River in what was then the state of Virginia (it became part of West Virginia after the Civil War, in 1863). In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era it was a growing river port that served as the western terminus of the National Road (Cumberland Road), the first major improved highway built by the federal government. This made Wheeling a natural gateway where travelers from the eastern seaboard transferred from overland wagons to river boats for the journey down the [[Ohio River]] toward [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] Valley. The town lay within slaveholding Virginia, making it a safe waypoint for slaveholders transporting human property.

## Role in the Narrative

Wheeling figures at the midpoint of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s epic 1825 journey to [[Kentucky]]. After departing the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] [[plantation]] in [[maryland|Maryland]] and traveling "through Alexandria, Culpepper, Fauquier, Harper's Ferry, Cumberland, over the mountains on the National Turnpike, to Wheeling," [[josiah-henson|Henson]] noted that "in all the taverns along the road were regular places for the droves of negroes continually passing along under the system of the internal slave trade" (006-010). These taverns, with their pens for chained slaves, marked the entire route as an artery of the domestic slave trade, and Wheeling was a key node in this network.

On arriving at Wheeling, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] followed the plan his master [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] had laid out: "Arriving at Wheeling, in pursuance of the plan laid down by my master, I sold the horse and wagon, and purchased a large boat, called in that region a yawl. Our mode of locomotion was now decidedly more agreeable than tramping along day after day" (006-012). The transition from wagon to yawl marked a change in the character of the journey — from the hardship of winter mountain travel to floating downstream with the current, a period of relative ease that lasted until the party reached the free-state shore of [[Ohio]], where the challenge to their [[slavery|enslavement]] began.

Wheeling reappears briefly but significantly in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative in 1829. After purchasing his freedom in [[maryland|Maryland]] and receiving his manumission papers on March 9, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] set out on the return journey to [[kentucky|Kentucky]], "leaving immediately for Wheeling, to which place I was obliged to travel on foot, I there took boat, and in due time reached my destination" (008-006). The town thus bookended his round trip — the outward journey as a slave driver in 1825, and the return as a newly freed man (or so he believed) in 1829.

## Historical Context

Wheeling was founded in 1769 and incorporated in 1806. By the 1820s it had become one of the most important ports on the upper [[ohio-river|Ohio River]], a center for boat-building, trade, and the transfer of goods and people between the National Road and [[mississippi-river|the river]] steamboat system. It was also a hub of the domestic slave trade — the "droves of negroes continually passing along" that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] observed were part of the massive forced migration of enslaved people from the upper South to the cotton and sugar [[plantation]]s of the Deep South. The town's location in Virginia meant that slavery was legal and deeply embedded in the local economy, though its proximity to free-state [[ohio|Ohio]] also made it a crossing point for fugitives traveling in the opposite direction.
