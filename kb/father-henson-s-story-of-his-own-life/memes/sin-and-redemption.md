# Sin and Redemption

Sin and redemption form the theological backbone of [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative. From his dramatic conversion experience to his lifelong sense of divine providence, [[christianity|Christian]] concepts of human sinfulness and divine grace shaped [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s understanding of his own life and [[slavery|the institution]] of slavery itself.

## Henson's Conversion

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s religious awakening occurred at a camp meeting, a characteristic feature of frontier [[methodism|Methodism]]. These outdoor revival gatherings brought together enslaved and free worshippers for days of fervent preaching and emotional religious experience. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described being powerfully moved by a sermon he heard at one such meeting, which became the foundation of his [[religion|Christian faith]] (010-005).

The camp meeting was a transformative space where the rigid hierarchies of [[plantation|plantation life]] temporarily dissolved. Enslaved people could experience spiritual equality, and [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own conversion became the starting point for his subsequent career as a preacher and community leader.

## The First Sermon

When the Archbishop of Canterbury later asked [[josiah-henson|Henson]] how he came to know the Saviour, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recalled the first sermon he ever heard. The text was from Hebrews: "He, by the grace of God, tasted death for every man" (022-003). This verse — with its declaration that Christ's sacrificial death extended to all people without distinction — resonated deeply with [[josiah-henson|Henson]]. It offered enslaved people a theological basis for their own human dignity: if Christ died for every person, then every person possessed irreducible worth.

The archbishop was so moved by [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s simple account of his conversion that he shed tears freely. The meeting, originally planned for fifteen minutes, lasted an hour and a half (022-004).

## Sin and the Institution of Slavery

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative does not present slavery merely as a social or economic injustice — it frames [[slavery|the institution]] in theological terms as a manifestation of sin. The [[kindness|cruelty]] of [[overseer-system|overseer]]s, the destruction of families, and the denial of education were not simply wrong; they were offenses against God's moral order (003-008).

Yet [[josiah-henson|Henson]] also acknowledged the moral complexity of the enslaved condition. He described how the system of slavery itself corrupted character, making honesty difficult and forcing moral compromises (003-009). His reflections on the sinfulness of his own heart — a recurring theme — placed him within the evangelical tradition that emphasized personal depravity and the need for divine grace.

> The divine character of Jesus Christ, his tender love for mankind, his forgiving spirit, his compassion for the outcast and despised, his cruel crucifixion and glorious ascension, were all depicted, and some of the points were dwelt on with great power; great, at least, to me, who then heard of these things for the first time in my life. Again and again did the preacher reiterate the words "for every man."
>
> (004-002)


## Redemption Through Divine Providence

Throughout his narrative, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] interprets [[freedom|deliverance]] from danger as acts of divine redemption. When the ship carrying him to the South unexpectedly turned back, he saw it as "one of those sudden, marked interpositions of [[providence|Providence]]" (011-004). His heart "sang jubilees of praise" to God for the [[freedom|deliverance]] (011-005).

This providential framework transformed the hardships of [[slavery|enslavement]] and escape into a sacred narrative. Each escape from danger, each unexpected [[kindness]], each successful venture became evidence of God's redemptive work in his life.

## Psalm 103 and the Heart of Gratitude

One of the most emotionally charged moments in the narrative occurs when [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s son Tom reads Psalm 103 aloud: "Bless the Lord, O my soul: and all that is within me, bless his holy name" (014-003). Hearing this psalm for the first time, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was overwhelmed. He recalled "with all the rapidity of which thought is capable, the whole current of my life" — the dangers and afflictions from which he believed the Lord had delivered him. His heart and eyes overflowed with emotion (014-003).

The moment also exposed the painful reality of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s il[[education|literacy]]. When Tom asked who David was, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] could not answer, and was forced to admit he could not read. This confession of ignorance before his own child was a moment of deep humility (014-003).

## Redemption and Community

For [[josiah-henson|Henson]], redemption was not purely individual. His efforts to free other enslaved people, establish the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]], and build a manual-labor school were extensions of his redemptive vision. Having experienced [[freedom|deliverance]] himself, he felt compelled to become an agent of [[freedom|deliverance]] for others. The personal experience of grace led to a life of service — a pattern that reflected the evangelical conviction that those who have been redeemed must work for the redemption of others.
