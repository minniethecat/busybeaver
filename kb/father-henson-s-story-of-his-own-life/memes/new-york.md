# New York

New York was a free state traversed by [[Josiah Henson]] on his journeys between [[Canada]] and the northeastern [[us-america|United States]], serving as both an [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] corridor and a landscape of commercial opportunity. It was here, in towns like Skeneateles and Oswego, that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] found critical allies for the [[Dawn Settlement]] and established the lumber-trade connections that sustained his Canadian community.

## Location & Geography

New York State stretches from the Great Lakes to the Atlantic, sharing a long border with [[canada|Upper Canada]] (now [[canada|Ontario]]) across Lake [[canada|Ontario]] and the Niagara River. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s New York was primarily the upstate region, including Skeneateles in the Finger Lakes district and Oswego on the Lake [[canada|Ontario]] shore, where he contracted to ship Canadian lumber to New [[england|England]] markets.

## Role in the Narrative

New York appears in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative as a waypoint in his [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] missions. Traveling on foot some four hundred miles to rescue a fugitive's family from [[kentucky|Kentucky]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] "passed through the States of New York, [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]], and [[ohio|Ohio]] — free States, so called — and crossed [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River into [[kentucky|Kentucky]]" (016-003). The phrase "free States, so called" reflects [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s awareness that even in nominally free territory, a black man was vulnerable to recapture after the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]].

The state was also a source of crucial allies. Mr. [[hiram-wilson|Hiram Wilson]], a missionary to Canadian fugitives, "wrote to a [[quaker|Quaker]] friend of his, an Englishman, by the name of [[james-c-fuller|James C. Fuller]], residing at Skeneateles, New York, and endeavored to interest him in the welfare of our struggling population" (018-004). Fuller's fundraising among his English connections yielded $1,500, which became seed capital for the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]]'s industrial enterprises.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s commercial development of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] drew him repeatedly into New York. "Full of this subject, I left my home on a journey of observation through the State of New York, and New [[england|England]]," he writes. "I found in New York, mills where precisely such logs as those in [[canada|Canada]] were sawed into lumber, which I learned commanded large prices" (019-002). His survey of New York mills became the model for the sawmill he built in [[canada|Canada]]. In Oswego, New York, he "contracted with the captain to deliver" a shipment of black walnut lumber destined for [[boston|Boston]], though the shipment was later entangled in a failed forwarding attempt (019-006).

Throughout his later career, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] continued to travel the state for support. "I have made many journeys into New York, Connecticut, [[boston|Massachusetts]], and Maine, in all of which States I have found or made some friends to the cause" (018-008). New York was thus woven into the fabric of his lifelong work as an advocate for Canadian fugitive settlers.

## Historical Context

In the antebellum period, New York was a free state with a complex relationship to slavery. Though it had abolished [[slavery|the institution]] gradually (final abolition in 1827), its economy was deeply intertwined with Southern cotton and finance. At the same time, the state was a critical artery of the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]], with routes running from New York City up the Hudson Valley to Albany, and westward along the Erie Canal to [[buffalo|Buffalo]] and the Canadian border. Upstate New York was home to fervent abolitionist communities, particularly among [[quaker|Quakers]] in the Finger Lakes region, the Shakers, and the growing evangelical reform movements that fueled the "Burned-Over District."
