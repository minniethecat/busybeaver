# Cincinnati

Cincinnati was [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River city that figured at three critical junctures in the life of [[Josiah Henson]]: as the place where he was first tempted to claim immediate freedom in 1825, as the city where his preaching earned him the funds to attempt self-purchase in 1828, and as a vital way-station on his escapes and [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] missions through [[Ohio]].

## Location and Geography

Cincinnati is located on the north bank of the [[Ohio River]] in southwestern [[ohio|Ohio]], directly opposite the slave state of [[Kentucky]]. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era (the 1820s and 1830s), it was one of the fastest-growing cities on the western frontier, a major river port for commerce moving between [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] Valley and the eastern seaboard. Its position at the boundary between free and slave territory made it a natural nexus for the [[Underground Railroad]] and a gathering place for both fugitives and their allies.

## Role in the Narrative

Cincinnati appears first in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative during the [[Journey to Kentucky]] in 1825, when he led eighteen enslaved people through the city on the way to deliver them to [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] in [[kentucky|Kentucky]]. On [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] shore, free black residents of Cincinnati strongly urged [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and his companions to claim their [[freedom|liberty]]: "At Cincinnati, especially, crowds of colored people gathered round us, and insisted on our remaining with them. They told us we were fools to think of going on and surrendering ourselves up to a new owner" (006-013). [[josiah-henson|Henson]], bound by a sense of honor and his promise to his master, resisted the temptation — a decision he later regretted bitterly. When the companions he had delivered were subsequently sold to the deep South, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recalled that "the guilty madness of my conduct in preventing them from availing themselves of the opportunity for acquiring freedom, which offered itself at Cincinnati, overwhelmed me" (007-004).

Cincinnati became a very different kind of threshold when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned in the fall of 1828 as a preacher raising money for self-purchase. Carrying a letter of introduction from his Methodist friend in [[kentucky|Kentucky]] "to a brother preacher in Cincinnati," he arrived in the city and found that his "letter I carried with me to a kind-hearted man in Cincinnati procured me a number of invaluable friends, who entered heart and soul into my plans" (007-008, 007-009). He preached in two or three pulpits and left the city "with no less a sum than one hundred and sixty dollars in my pockets" (007-009).

Cincinnati also served as a way-station during [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[escape-to-canada|1830 escape]] from slavery. After crossing the [[Ohio River]] from [[kentucky|Kentucky]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and his family traveled overland through [[indiana|Indiana]], arriving at Cincinnati after enduring a fortnight of starvation and fear. The city marked the midpoint of their flight — the first major free-state city where they could hope to find aid (012-009). On later [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] expeditions, Cincinnati was again a crucial point of refuge. After one mass escape, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] reported that "we succeeded in crossing [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River in safety, and arrived in Cincinnati the third night after our departure. Here we procured assistance" (016-006). The city provided rest, supplies, and direction toward [[quaker|Quaker]] settlements in [[indiana|Indiana]] and ultimately the journey to [[lake-erie|Lake Erie]] and [[Canada]].

## Historical Context

Cincinnati was chartered in 1819 and grew explosively through the 1820s and 1830s, driven by steamboat commerce and its position as the gateway between [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River and the interior. The city had a substantial free black community, centered in the "Little Africa" district, which was active in aiding fugitives. However, it was also the site of fierce anti-abolitionist sentiment — the Lane Seminary debates of 1834 and the anti-abolitionist riot of 1836 underscored the city's deep divisions over slavery. By 1850, Cincinnati was one of the most important nodes on the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]], with Levi Coffin (the reputed "President" of the Railroad) operating there and sending hundreds of fugitives northward. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s experiences in the city capture this dual character: a place of opportunity and danger, where fugitives found both allies and enemies.

## Key Passages

> "At Cincinnati, especially, crowds of colored people gathered round us, and insisted on our remaining with them. They told us we were fools to think of going on and surrendering ourselves up to a new owner; that now we could be our own masters, and put ourselves out of all reach of pursuit." (006-013)

> "A letter I carried with me to a kind-hearted man in Cincinnati procured me a number of invaluable friends, who entered heart and soul into my plans. They procured me an opportunity to preach in two or three of the pulpits of the city, and I made my appeal with that eloquence which spontaneously breaks forth from a breast all alive and fanned into a glow by an inspiring project." (007-009)

> "We reached Cincinnati about eleven on Sunday morning--too late for the stage that day; but having found some friends, we hid ourselves until Monday evening, when we recommenced our long and toilsome journey, through mud, rain, and snow, towards Canada." (017-009)
