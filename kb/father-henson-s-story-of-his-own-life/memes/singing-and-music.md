# Singing and Religious Music

Singing and religious music were essential elements of enslaved African [[us-america|America]]n worship and community life. In [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative, singing appears at moments of profound spiritual significance — from camp meeting revivals to psalms read aloud in the family [[slave-cabin|cabin]], to hymns sung at formal occasions in [[england|England]].

## Music at Camp Meetings

The camp meetings that characterized frontier [[methodism|Methodism]] were intensely musical gatherings. Enslaved and free worshippers came together for days of preaching, [[prayer|pray]]er, and singing. The emotional power of these meetings was inseparable from their musical expression — congregational singing, spontaneous outbursts of praise, and the rhythmic call-and-response patterns that reflected African musical traditions adapted to [[christianity|Christian]] worship.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own religious awakening occurred in this context. The camp meetings he attended were occasions "which occurred from time to time," providing some of the few spiritual and social outlets available to enslaved people (007-002). The music of these gatherings was not merely decorative — it was central to the religious experience, enabling emotional release and communal bonding that the constraints of [[plantation|plantation life]] otherwise suppressed.

## Singing as Praise and Lament

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described his [[freedom|deliverance]] from being sold South, he wrote that his "heart sang jubilees of praise to Thee" (011-005). This language of sung praise was not metaphorical — it reflected the actual practice of expressing religious emotion through song. Enslaved people who were denied [[education|literacy]] found in singing a powerful medium for theological expression and emotional release.

The tradition of the spiritual — songs that combined biblical themes with the lived experience of [[slavery|enslavement]] — emerged from precisely this context. While [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative does not transcribe the songs he heard and sang, the pervasive presence of singing in his account testifies to its centrality in the religious culture he inhabited.

## Psalm 103: A Musical Moment of Transformation

One of the most moving scenes in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative involves music — specifically, the reading of Psalm 103 by his son Tom. When Tom opened the [[bible|Bible]] and read, "Bless the Lord, O my soul: and all that is within me, bless his holy name," [[josiah-henson|Henson]] experienced an overwhelming emotional response (014-003).

Though a psalm is text, the musical context was unmistakable. The psalms were the hymnbook of the [[christianity|Christian]] tradition, meant to be sung. Tom's reading — the first time [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had heard this "beautiful outpouring of gratitude" — functioned as a musical experience, releasing a flood of memory and emotion. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recalled his entire life with "all the rapidity of which thought is capable" and was moved to tears (014-003).

## Hymnody and Formal Worship

Music accompanied [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s most formal religious experiences as well. At an event in [[london|London]], "the blessing was invoked by singing" (022-003). This brief reference reveals the standard practice of beginning religious gatherings with a hymn — a practice that crossed racial and national boundaries, connecting the worship of enslaved people in [[maryland|Maryland]] and [[kentucky|Kentucky]] with that of abolitionists and church leaders in [[england|England]].

The hymns sung on such occasions were drawn from the standard repertoire of English-language Protestant hymnody — the works of Isaac Watts, Charles Wesley, and other eighteenth-century hymn writers whose compositions had become the common currency of Methodist and evangelical worship throughout the English-speaking world.

At the dinner hosted by [[lord-john-russell|Lord John Russell]] at his [[richmond-park|Richmond Park]] estate, the three hundred [[sabbath|Sabbath]] School teachers and guests invoked the blessing in song:

> "Be present at our table, Lord,
> Be here and everywhere adored:
> These creatures bless,
> And grant that we may feast
> In Paradise with thee!"
>
> (022-005)

## The Oral Tradition of Sacred Song

For enslaved people denied [[education|literacy]], singing was a form of oral transmission of religious knowledge. Hymns and spiritual songs carried biblical stories, theological concepts, and moral teachings that could not be accessed through reading. The memorization and transmission of sacred songs created a living library of religious knowledge that sustained faith across generations.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]], who could not read until late in life, was a beneficiary of this oral tradition. The texts he heard preached and sung became the substance of his own preaching. His experience demonstrates how singing functioned not only as worship but as religious education — a way of "hiding the Word in the heart" for those who could not hold a [[bible|Bible]] in their hands.

## Singing and Community

Religious singing created and sustained community among enslaved people. The shared experience of raising voices together in worship forged bonds that transcended the isolating effects of [[plantation]] discipline. In the singing of hymns and spirituals, enslaved people asserted their humanity, expressed their hope, and maintained a collective identity that [[slavery|the institution]] of slavery sought to extinguish.
