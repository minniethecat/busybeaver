In 1829, [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] returned to [[maryland|Maryland]] with $350 to purchase his freedom from [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], his enslaver. After paying the agreed amount, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was betrayed by [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s brother-in-law Mathias, who stole his manumission papers and conspired to sell him to a [[plantation]] in Louisiana.

## Overview

Having accumulated $275 through extra work in [[kentucky|Kentucky]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] traveled back to [[maryland|Montgomery County]], [[maryland|Maryland]], to negotiate the purchase of his freedom. His master, [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], initially received him warmly and agreed to the manumission in exchange for $350 — $100 more than [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had (008-001). After obtaining the additional funds from his brother-in-law, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] paid the full amount and received his free papers on March 9, 1829 (008-005). The freedom was short-lived: [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s brother-in-law Mathias deceived [[josiah-henson|Henson]] during the return journey, seized his papers, and plotted to sell him south.

## Context & Background

Before leaving for [[maryland|Maryland]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had accumulated $275 through his work in [[kentucky|Kentucky]] and had received "every token of [[trust]] and [[trust|confidence]]" from those who knew him (008-001). He had been living in [[ohio|Ohio]] where he had established himself as a reliable laborer. His plan was to purchase his freedom, return north, and begin life as a free man.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s master, [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], was delighted at his return and asked, "Why, what in the devil have you been doing? Where did you get so much money?" (008-002). [[isaac-riley|Riley]] then informed [[josiah-henson|Henson]] that $350 was the price for his freedom — $100 more than [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had brought.

## The Event

The manumission agreement was finalized after [[josiah-henson|Henson]] borrowed the additional $100 from his brother-in-law. On March 9, 1829, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] received his free papers. He described feeling "secure in my happiness" and "cherished no suspicion of fraud" when Mathias offered to escort him part of the way to [[ohio|Ohio]] (008-006).

> The boat which took me down from Louisville, landed me about dark, and my walk of five miles brought me to the plantation. I was greatly exhausted, my horse was weary, and I was so feeble I could scarcely think of any thing else than rest. (008-007)

The [[trust|betrayal]] unfolded the next morning when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] asked Mathias for his free papers. Mathias claimed to have left them in [[louisville|Louisville]]. When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] pressed further, Mathias attempted to placate him with smooth words while planning to sell him south:

> Master Amos soon asked me if I had not a paper for him. I told him I had had one, but the last I saw of it was at Louisville. He feigned great astonishment. (008-009)

## Aftermath & Significance

The [[trust|betrayal]] left [[josiah-henson|Henson]] in a state of "frenzy of grief at the base and apparently irremediable trick" (008-010). He realized that Mathias intended to sell him to a [[plantation]] in the Deep South — effectively a death sentence for an enslaved person. This event became the catalyst for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s eventual escape: having experienced firsthand that even a legally purchased freedom could be stolen through fraud, he understood that only permanent flight to [[canada|Canada]] could secure his [[freedom|liberty]].

The return-to-maryland episode illustrates a recurring theme in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative — the legal and moral impossibility of an enslaved person securing freedom through honest dealing within a system designed to deny it.
