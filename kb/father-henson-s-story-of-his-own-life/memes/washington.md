# Washington

Washington was the federal capital city to which [[Josiah Henson]] regularly traveled from the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] [[plantation]] in [[Maryland]] to sell farm produce, and where his ally [[francis-master-frank-riley|Francis "Master Frank" Riley]] established himself in business. It was to Washington that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] fled to seek help after his master [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] confiscated his travel pass, making the city a modest but significant locus of both his daily [[slavery|enslavement]] and his maneuvering toward freedom.

## Location and Geography

Washington, the capital of the [[us-america|United States]], lies on the Potomac River between [[maryland|Maryland]] and Virginia. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era (the 1820s), it was a modest city of approximately 13,000 people, still recovering from the British burning of 1814. The city was situated within a slaveholding region — both [[maryland|Maryland]] and Virginia were slave states — and the federal district itself permitted slavery until the Compromise of 1850 abolished the slave trade (though not slavery itself) within its boundaries.

## Role in the Narrative

Washington appears in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative primarily as a commercial destination. As superintendent of the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] [[plantation]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s "occupations being to superintend the farming operations, and to sell the produce in the neighboring markets of Washington and [[Georgetown]]" (006-002). He was a familiar figure in the capital, noting that "many respectable people, yet living there, may possibly have some recollection of 'Siah,' or 'Sie,' (as they used to call me,) as their market-man" (006-002). This regular travel to market gave [[josiah-henson|Henson]] a degree of mobility and familiarity with the outside world unusual for an enslaved person.

The city took on a more dramatic role when, after his preaching tour in [[Ohio]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned to [[maryland|Maryland]] only to have his master confiscate his travel pass. Trapped and resolved to seek help, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] rode to Washington to appeal to [[francis-master-frank-riley|Francis Riley]], the younger brother of his mistress, who had suffered abuse as a boy and whom [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had befriended by sharing food. Francis "was now of age, and had established himself in business in Washington" (008-004). [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recalled that he "knew he would take an interest in me, for I had done much to lighten his sorrows when he was an abused and harshly-treated boy in the house" (008-004). [[francis-master-frank-riley|Francis Riley]] agreed to negotiate for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s freedom with [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], eventually securing a purchase price of four hundred and fifty dollars — a negotiation that led to [[josiah-henson|Henson]] receiving his manumission papers on March 9, 1829 (008-005).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] also notes that [[francis-master-frank-riley|Francis Riley]] eventually became "one of the wealthiest men in Washington city" (006-003), a detail that frames the capital as a place of economic advancement for those who were free — a sharp contrast with the [[slavery|bondage]] [[josiah-henson|Henson]] continued to suffer.

## Historical Context

In the 1820s, Washington was a city deeply entangled with slavery. Enslaved laborers built the Capitol and the White House. Slave auctions were held within sight of the Capitol dome, and the city's newspapers carried advertisements for runaway slaves. Yet Washington was also home to a growing free black population, and the presence of both foreign diplomats and northern congressmen gave it a distinctive cultural and political climate. The city would not abolish slavery until 1862, when Congress passed the District of Columbia Compensated Emancipation Act, making it one of the last places in the North to end [[slavery|the institution]].
