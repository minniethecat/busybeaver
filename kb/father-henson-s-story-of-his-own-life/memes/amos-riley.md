# Amos Riley

**Amos Riley** was a slaveholder in [[kentucky|Davis County]], [[kentucky|Kentucky]], and the brother of [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s owner in [[maryland|Maryland]]. He appears in the narrative as the recipient of [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and a group of enslaved people whom [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was ordered to transport from [[maryland|Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] in 1825.

## Role in the Narrative

In April 1825, [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] arrived at Amos Riley's [[plantation]] in [[kentucky|Davis County]], [[kentucky|Kentucky]], having led a group of enslaved people — including his own wife and children — on a journey from [[maryland|Maryland]]. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had been instructed by [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] to deliver the group to his brother Amos, as part of [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s plan to relocate his human property to [[kentucky|Kentucky]].

> I arrived at Davis county, Kentucky, about the middle of April, 1825, and delivered myself and my companions to Mr. Amos Riley, the brother of my owner, who had a large plantation, with from eighty to one hundred negroes. (008-001)

## Plantation and Management

Amos Riley's [[plantation]] was situated about five miles south of [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River and fifteen miles above the Yellow Banks, on Big Blackfords Creek. The farm was described as larger and more fertile than [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]]'s [[maryland|Maryland]] property, with a greater abundance of food for the enslaved laborers. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was given general management of the farm due to the strong recommendations of ability and honesty he brought from [[maryland|Maryland]].

## Involvement in the Freedom Scheme

Amos Riley played a pivotal role in the scheme to defraud [[josiah-henson|Henson]] of his freedom. When [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] had arranged to sell [[josiah-henson|Henson]] his freedom, the documents and evidence were sent to Amos Riley with instructions that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] must pay an additional six hundred and fifty dollars — a fraudulent claim, as [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had already paid the agreed price.

As [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recounted after returning from a trip to [[maryland|Maryland]] and [[education|learning]] of the [[trust|deception]] from his wife [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]]:

> Master Amos said I had paid three hundred and fifty dollars down, and when I had made up six hundred and fifty more I was to have my free papers. I now began to perceive the trick that had been played upon me, and to see the management by which Riley had contrived that the only evidence of my freedom should be kept from every eye but that of his brother Amos, who was requested to retain it until I had made up the balance I was reported to have agreed to pay. (009-001)

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] confronted Amos Riley the next morning, Amos greeted him with coarse familiarity, praising [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s dress and calling him a "regular black gentleman," while concealing the fraudulent arrangement.
