# Auction Block

The public sale of enslaved people at auction — a routine transaction in the 19th-century Southern economy and the event that tore [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s family apart when he was a young child, leaving an indelible mark on his memory and shaping his lifelong commitment to freedom.

## Definition

A slave auction was a public sale in which enslaved human beings were sold as property to the highest bidder. Auctions occurred at courthouses, in town squares, at estate settlements, and through professional slave traders. Enslaved people were examined, appraised, and "bid off" individually or in lots, with no legal recognition of family bonds. The auction was both an economic mechanism — distributing labor across the slave economy — and a ritual of dehumanization that demonstrated the absolute commodification of human life under the slave system.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s account of the auction following Dr. McPherson's death is one of the most powerful passages in the narrative. He describes how "the estate and the slaves must be sold and the property divided" (002-003), leading to the dreaded auction day. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] acknowledges that no description can capture the reality: "still the full misery of the event — of the scenes which precede and succeed it — is never understood till the actual experience comes" (002-004). His brothers and sisters are "bid off first, and one by one, while my mother, paralyzed by grief, held me by the hand" (002-005). [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself is sold to a man he pointedly calls "a stranger named Robb, and truly a robber he was to me" (002-006). The family is scattered; the auction block has done its work.

## Henson's Perspective

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s rendering of the auction foregrounds his mother's anguish as the emotional center of the scene. He remembers her "paralyzed by grief" as each child is taken from her. His narrative technique is understated — the facts are allowed to speak with minimal embellishment — which makes the account all the more devastating. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] does not philosophize about the auction; he simply bears witness. His wordplay on "Robb" and "robber" conveys moral judgment without needing to elaborate. Throughout his later life, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s memory of the auction block fueled his commitment to the abolitionist cause.

## Historical Context

Slave auctions were a ubiquitous feature of the antebellum South. Following the 1808 prohibition on the international slave trade, the domestic trade expanded enormously. "Negro marts" operated in cities like [[indiana|Richmond]], Charleston, and [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], and auctioneers like the firm of Franklin & Armfield in Alexandria grew wealthy from the traffic. The auction was also a potent symbol in abolitionist propaganda, featured prominently in slave narratives and in Harriet Beecher Stowe's *Uncle Tom's Cabin*, where [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own auction scene provided source material.

> My brothers and sisters were bid off first, and one by one, while my mother, paralyzed by grief, held me by the hand. Her turn came, and she was bought by Isaac Riley of Montgomery county. Then I was offered. (002-005)
