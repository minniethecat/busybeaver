# Vicksburg

Vicksburg was [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] River port where [[Josiah Henson]] stopped during the 1829 flat-boat voyage from [[Kentucky]] to [[New Orleans]], and where he visited the [[plantation]] of his former companions from [[kentucky|Kentucky]] who had been sold into the Deep South. The visit revealed to [[josiah-henson|Henson]], with searing clarity, the brutal reality of the domestic slave trade and the fate that awaited him if he were sold south.

## Location and Geography

Vicksburg is situated on a high bluff overlooking the [[Mississippi River]] in western Mississippi, roughly midway between Memphis and [[new-orleans|New Orleans]]. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s era (the late 1820s), it was a rapidly growing river port and the commercial hub of the region's burgeoning cotton economy. The surrounding lowlands, drained by the Yazoo and Mississippi rivers, were being converted into vast cotton [[plantation]]s worked by enslaved labor forcibly transported from the upper South. The climate was subtropical, hot, and malarial — conditions that were deadly for the enslaved people forced to work the fields.

## Role in the Narrative

Vicksburg appears at the emotional nadir of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s 1829 journey down [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]]. After departing [[kentucky|Kentucky]] on [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] Jr.'s flat-boat loaded with produce bound for [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] knew — though it was never stated outright — that he himself was cargo: "This intimation was enough. Though it was not distinctly stated, yet I well knew what was intended, and my heart sunk within me at the near prospect of this fatal blight to all my long-cherished hopes" (009-003). The journey was a protracted ordeal, with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] forced to work the helm at night while the white boatmen slept, and eventually to assume command of the vessel when the captain went blind from river-reflected sunlight (009-006).

At Vicksburg, the flat-boat stopped, and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] "got permission to visit a [[plantation]] a few miles from the town, where some of my old companions whom I had brought from [[kentucky|Kentucky]] were living" (009-008). These were the very people [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had led from [[Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] in 1825 — the same eighteen enslaved people he had declined to liberate at [[Cincinnati]]. What he found at Vicksburg haunted him for life. Four years in the Deep South had aged them catastrophically: "Four years in an unhealthy climate and under a hard master had done the ordinary work of twenty. Their cheeks were literally caved in with starvation and disease, and their bodies infested with vermin" (009-008). They toiled "half naked in malarious marshes, under a burning, maddening sun, and poisoned by swarms of musquitoes and black gnats" and "looked forward to death as their only [[freedom|deliverance]]" (009-008). [[josiah-henson|Henson]] departed Vicksburg "sick at heart" and carried the image of "that wretched group" with him for the rest of his days (009-008).

The specter of Vicksburg followed [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], where he pleaded with his young master [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] Jr. not to sell him. He "told him about the wretched condition of the slaves I had seen near Vicksburg," invoking the horror as a warning of what awaited any person sold into the Deep South (011-002). The young master "would shed tears himself, and say he was sorry for me. But still I saw his purpose was unchanged" (011-002). Only a sudden, providential illness that struck young Amos — forcing him to rely on [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to nurse him home — prevented the sale and saved [[josiah-henson|Henson]] from the fate he had witnessed at Vicksburg.

## Historical Context

Vicksburg was founded in 1811 and incorporated in 1825, the very year [[josiah-henson|Henson]] made his journey to [[kentucky|Kentucky]]. The 1820s and 1830s were the peak decades of the domestic slave trade, during which an estimated one million enslaved people were forcibly relocated from the upper South to the cotton, sugar, and rice [[plantation]]s of the Deep South. The Yazoo-Mississippi Delta, with Vicksburg as its commercial center, was one of the most deadly destinations — disease, overwork, and brutal discipline produced mortality rates far exceeding those of the upper South. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s description of his companions' condition after only four years in the region is consistent with historical accounts of life on Mississippi cotton [[plantation]]s. The city would later become famous during the Civil War for the 47-day Siege of Vicksburg, which gave the Union control of [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] River.

## Key Passages

> "On our way down the river we stopped at Vicksburg, and I got permission to visit a plantation a few miles from the town, where some of my old companions whom I had brought from Kentucky were living. It was the saddest visit I ever made. Four years in an unhealthy climate and under a hard master had done the ordinary work of twenty. Their cheeks were literally caved in with starvation and disease, and their bodies infested with vermin." (009-008)

> "No hell could equal the misery they described as their daily portion. Toiling half naked in malarious marshes, under a burning, maddening sun, and poisoned by swarms of musquitoes and black gnats, they looked forward to death as their only deliverance. Some of them fairly cried at seeing me there, and at thought of the fate which they felt awaited me. Their worst fears of being sold down South had been more than realized. I went away sick at heart, and to this day the sight of that wretched group haunts me." (009-008)
