# Isaac Riley

Isaac Riley was a [[maryland|Montgomery County]], [[Maryland]] slaveholder and blacksmith who purchased [[Josiah Henson]]'s mother at auction and became [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s principal master during his formative years. The narrative portrays Riley as embodying the moral corruption that [[slavery]] inflicts upon slaveholders.

## Biography

Isaac Riley of [[maryland|Montgomery County]], [[Maryland]], was a blacksmith by trade who purchased [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother at a [[auction-block|slave auction]] (002-005). He also reluctantly agreed to purchase the infant Josiah when the trader Robb offered to sell him cheap, fearing "the little devil would die" and not wanting to "buy a dead nigger" (002-006). Riley ultimately agreed to pay a small sum in horse-shoeing services if the child survived.

Riley operated his blacksmithing business about five miles from the Montgomery court-house. He served as guardian to his wife's younger brother Francis, treating the boy poorly and providing such meager provisions that the boy "would come to me with tears in his eyes complaining he could not get enough to eat" (002-007).

> "Riley and his slaves were no exception to the general rule, but might be cited as apt illustrations of the nature of the relation" — specifically, the tendency of slavery "to convert the master into a tyrant, and the slave into the cringing, treacherous, false, and thieving victim of tyranny." (002-007)

## Character and Treatment of Enslaved People

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s portrait of Riley is unsparing. He describes him as "coarse and vulgar in his habits, unprincipled and cruel in his dealings" (002-007). Riley's wife was "no addition to the comfort of the establishment" due to her "remarkable economy" (002-002), which manifested in severe deprivation for the enslaved people on the property.

Riley's approach to medical care reveals the dehumanizing logic of slavery: when [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s shoulders were crushed by a heavy blow, no medical attention was provided, operating on the principle that "a nigger will get well anyway" (005-001). This was not exceptional [[kindness|cruelty]] but the routine neglect that [[slavery|the institution]] permitted.

## Role in the Narrative

Riley serves as the primary antagonist of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s early life and the embodiment of slavery's corrupting influence. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative strategy is significant: he does not present Riley as uniquely monstrous but as a representative figure — "no exception to the general rule" — whose [[kindness|cruelty]] is the predictable product of absolute power over other human beings.

> "Riley never called a physician for an injured slave. The standing answer to any complaint was, 'A nigger will get well anyway.' If a slave was sick, the remedy was a dose of salts, or castor oil, or an emetic. If these failed, the patient was left to nature." (005-001)

## Historical Context

Riley represents the class of small-scale slaveholders in the border state of [[Maryland]] during the early 19th century. Unlike the large [[plantation]] owners of the Deep South, men like Riley operated modest enterprises with a handful of enslaved workers — in his case, combining blacksmithing with small-scale farming. The narrative demonstrates that [[slavery|the institution]] produced the same dynamics of [[kindness|cruelty]] and dehumanization even at this smaller scale.

Riley's circumstances also illuminate the economic realities of border-state slavery. The sale of enslaved people from [[maryland|Maryland]] and Virginia to the expanding cotton regions of the Deep South was a significant economic force, one that would eventually threaten [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself with the prospect of being "sold South."
