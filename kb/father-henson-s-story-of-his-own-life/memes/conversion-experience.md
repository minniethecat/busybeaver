# Conversion Experience

A near-murder on a flatboat journey down the [[Mississippi River]], followed by a powerful crisis of conscience that re-forged [[Josiah Henson]]'s [[Christian]] faith and set the moral foundation for his entire subsequent career as a preacher and abolitionist leader.

## Background

By the late 1820s, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had returned from his failed attempt to purchase his own freedom in [[Maryland]]. [[Isaac Riley]] had cheated him — pocketing $350 in cash and [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s horse, then sending sealed papers to his brother [[Amos Riley]] in [[Kentucky]] that falsely stated [[josiah-henson|Henson]] still owed $650 more (008-007). Rather than honor the manumission agreement, Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]] determined to sell [[josiah-henson|Henson]] down [[mississippi-river|the river]] to [[new-orleans|New Orleans]]. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was ordered to accompany a flatboat of produce to the [[new-orleans|New Orleans]] market — where, upon arrival, he himself was to be sold (010-002).

> "After all that I had done for Isaac and Amos Riley, after all the regard they had professed for me, such a return as this for my services... turned my blood to gall, and changed me from a lively, and, I will say, a pleasant-tempered fellow, into a savage, morose, dangerous slave." (010-002)

## The Voyage

The flatboat journey down [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] took weeks. [[josiah-henson|Henson]], required to stand watch on deck at night, was alone with his thoughts. The river scenes — "wretched slave-pens; a smell of stagnant waters; half-putrid carcasses of horses or oxen floating along, covered with turkey buzzards" — fed his despair. His faith "utterly gave way"; he "could no longer [[prayer|pray]] or [[trust]]" (010-001).

He became convinced that death would overtake him within two years of the hellish [[plantation]] labor awaiting him in Louisiana. From this certainty, a darker resolution took shape: rather than be worked to death, he would kill his captors and escape north.

## The Axe Moment

> "I resolved to kill my four companions, take what money there was in the boat, then to scuttle the craft, and escape to the north." (010-003)

One dark, rainy night, within a few days' sail of [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] crept below deck, found an axe, and stood over the sleeping form of [[Amos Riley]]. He raised the axe to strike:

> "When suddenly the thought came to me, 'What! commit _murder_! and you a Christian?' I had not called it murder before. It was self-defence, — it was preventing others from murdering me, — it was justifiable, it was even praiseworthy. But now, all at once, the truth burst upon me that it was a crime." (010-003)

He laid down the axe. What stayed his hand was not fear of consequences but the voice of conscience — his identity as a [[christianity|Christian]], however imperfectly formed, could not accommodate the act of killing a defenseless man.

## Aftermath

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] remained on deck all night, filled with "shame and remorse." He made a solemn resolution to "resign myself to the will of God, and take with thankfulness, if I could, but with submission, at all events, whatever he might decide should be my lot" (010-004).

This resolution was immediately tested. In [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], [[Amos Riley]] fell gravely ill with river fever, and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — rather than abandon the man who had intended to sell him — nursed him back to health on the return voyage, personally carrying him home on a litter (011-004 through 011-006).

The experience transformed [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s faith from a nominal inheritance from his mother into a personal, tested conviction. He "thanked God, as I have done every day since, that I had not committed murder" (010-003). This moral clarity — purchased at the edge of the axe — would sustain him through the escape to [[Canada]], his preaching ministry, and his decades of antislavery advocacy.
