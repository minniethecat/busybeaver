# Mississippi River

The Mississippi River was the route by which [[Josiah Henson]] was transported south in 1828 on a flatboat toward the slave markets of [[New Orleans]] — a voyage that became the emotional and spiritual nadir of his autobiography. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s telling, the great river is not a scene of beauty or commerce but a landscape of despair, a "golden river" for merchants that he saw only as a road to destruction.

## Location & Geography

The Mississippi River flows more than 2,300 miles from Lake Itasca in Minnesota to the Gulf of Mexico, draining much of the North [[us-america|America]]n continent. In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s experience, the relevant stretch was the Lower Mississippi — from the confluence with [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River near Cairo, Illinois, past [[Vicksburg]], Mississippi, down to [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], Louisiana. This section flowed through the heart of the Deep South's cotton and sugar [[plantation]] economy, lined with slave pens and flatboat landings.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Mississippi voyage came in the context of a "common trading voyage to [[new-orleans|New Orleans]]" undertaken by [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]], who commanded a flatboat loaded with beef-cattle, pigs, poultry, corn, and whisky to sell as they "dropped down the river, wherever they could be disposed of to the greatest advantage" (009-005). [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was both crew and, as he came to realize, cargo — his master [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] had instructed Amos to sell him at the journey's end.

On the river, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] distinguished himself by mastering navigation. He "learned the art of steering and managing the boat far better than the rest," studying how to "shoot by a sawyer, to land on a bank, or avoid a snag, or a steamboat, in the rapid current of the Mississippi" (009-006). When the white captain was struck blind from sun-glare reflected off the water, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] became "in fact master of the boat from that time till our arrival at [[new-orleans|New Orleans]]" — a bitter irony, as the enslaved man literally steered his own conveyance to market (009-006).

The river is the setting for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s darkest spiritual passage. In a remarkable extended meditation, he writes: "Now all outward nature seemed to feed my gloomy thoughts. I know not what most men see in voyaging down the Mississippi. If gay and hopeful, probably much of beauty and interest. If eager merchants, probably a golden river, freighted with the wealth of nations. I saw nothing but portents of woe and despair" (010-001). The river landscape mirrors his internal state: "Wretched slave-pens; a smell of stagnant waters; half-putrid carcasses of horses or oxen floating along, covered with turkey buzzards and swarms of green flies -- these are the images with which memory crowds my mind" (010-001).

It was on the Mississippi, approaching [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] contemplated murdering the sleeping crew with an axe — one of the most harrowing passages in [[us-america|America]]n slave narratives. "One dark, rainy night, within a few days' sail of [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], my hour seemed to have come" (010-003). He entered the [[slave-cabin|cabin]] with the weapon raised but was stopped by a sudden moral revelation, withdrawing to the deck in spiritual turmoil.

The river then becomes the vehicle of [[freedom|deliverance]]. After young [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] fell gravely ill in [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] nursed him back to health aboard a steamboat heading north: "O, my God! how my heart sang jubilees of praise to Thee, as the steamboat swung loose from the levee and breasted the mighty tide of the Mississippi! Away from this land of [[slavery|bondage]] and death! Away from misery and despair! Once more exulting hope possessed me" (011-005).

Years later, describing a return to [[england|England]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] employed the Mississippi as a simile for the unceasing crowds at the [[london|Crystal Palace]]: "Like the waters of the great Mississippi, the channel was still full, though the individuals were changed" (021-008).

## Historical Context

The Mississippi River was the central artery of the antebellum slave trade. Flatboats and steamboats carried human cargo downstream from the upper South to the cotton and sugar [[plantation]]s of Louisiana, Mississippi, and Arkansas. The phrase "sold down the river" originated here — the Lower Mississippi meant the Deep South, a more brutal form of slavery from which escape was nearly impossible. The river's confluence with [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] at Cairo, Illinois, was a geographical junction of slavery and freedom recognized throughout the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] network.
