Mrs. Riley was the wife of [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], the [[maryland|Maryland]] slaveholder who owned [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] during his early life. She appears in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative primarily during his return to [[maryland|Maryland]] from [[kentucky|Kentucky]] in 1829, where her interactions with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — and the slaves she brought into the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] household — reveal the domestic dynamics of the slaveholding [[plantation]].

## The Riley Household

Mrs. Riley married [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] and resided on his [[plantation]] in [[maryland|Montgomery County]], [[maryland|Maryland]]. The household included both the slaves [[isaac-riley|Riley]] had inherited or purchased and those Mrs. Riley brought to the marriage as part of her own property. When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned to the [[plantation]] after his years in [[kentucky|Kentucky]], he was struck by the degraded conditions he found:

> I looked around me with a sensation of disgust. The negroes present were strangers to me, being slaves that Mrs. Riley had brought to her husband. "Fool that I was to come back!" (008-003)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother had died during his absence, and the filthy conditions of the [[slave-cabin|slave quarters]] — a "crowded room, with its dirt floor, and filth, and stench" — stood in stark contrast to the relative dignity he had experienced during his years managing [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]]'s [[plantation]] in [[kentucky|Kentucky]].

## The Pass Episode

Mrs. Riley's most significant narrative appearance occurs during [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s carefully planned departure from [[maryland|Maryland]]. After discovering that [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] had fraudulently altered the terms of his freedom purchase, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] resolved to escape north. To leave without arousing suspicion, he needed to obtain permission to travel — and Mrs. Riley unwittingly provided it:

> It was early in the morning, and my master had already gone to the tavern on his usual business, when Mrs. Riley came out to look at my horse and equipments. "Where are you going, 'Siah?" was the natural question. I replied, "I am going to Washington, mistress, to see Mr. Frank, and I must take my pass with me, if you please." "O, everybody knows you here; you won't need your pass." "But I can't go to Washington without one." (008-004)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] deliberately pressed for the pass despite Mrs. Riley's casual dismissal, knowing that without proper documentation he would be vulnerable to slave-catchers. His insistence on formality — couched as dutiful obedience — secured him the written permission that made his [[escape-to-canada|escape]] possible.

## Role in the Plantation Hierarchy

Mrs. Riley's presence in the narrative illustrates the role of white women within the slaveholding household economy. As the wife of the [[plantation]] owner, she managed domestic affairs and exercised casual authority over enslaved people. Her ownership of slaves independent of her husband also underscores how slavery operated as a form of property that transferred through marriage and inheritance, binding enslaved people to households through legal ties that had nothing to do with their own choices or relationships.
