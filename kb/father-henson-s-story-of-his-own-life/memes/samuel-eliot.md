Samuel Eliot was a [[boston|Boston]] attorney who played a key role in securing financial backing for [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s sawmill enterprise at the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]]. He examined [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s claims, prepared a formal proposal, and helped marshal support among [[boston|Boston]]'s leading citizens.

## Aid to the Dawn Settlement

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s connection to [[boston|Boston]] began through Rev. [[rev-ephraim-peabody|Ephraim Peabody]], who introduced him to Eliot. As [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recounted:

> Rev. Ephraim Peabody introduced me to Samuel Eliot, Esq., who was kind enough to examine carefully into all my representations, and to draw up a sketch of them, which was afterwards presented to Amos Lawrence, Esq., and others. [019-004]

Eliot's endorsement proved crucial. The proposal he drafted led to a collection amounting to about fourteen hundred dollars, contributed by many of the leading gentlemen of [[boston|Boston]] (019-004). When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later needed additional capital after exhausting his initial funds, Eliot joined with [[amos-lawrence|Amos Lawrence]] and [[h-ingersoll-bowditch|H. Ingersoll Bowditch]] in endorsing a bank note that enabled [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to borrow an additional eighteen hundred dollars to complete and stock the mill (019-005).

## Historical Context

Eliot was part of a network of [[boston|Boston]] abolitionists who extended financial support to [[canada|Canadian]] freedom settlements in the 1840s. This network — which included [[amos-lawrence|Amos Lawrence]], [[rev-ephraim-peabody|Ephraim Peabody]], and [[h-ingersoll-bowditch|H. Ingersoll Bowditch]] — represented a crucial link between [[new-england|New England]] antislavery philanthropy and the economic development of [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] destination communities.
