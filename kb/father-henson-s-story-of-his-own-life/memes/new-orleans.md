# New Orleans

New Orleans was the destination of the 1828 flatboat voyage during which [[Josiah Henson]] was to be sold by [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] into the Deep South slave trade. It was here, in the "long days and heats of June" (011-003), that [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s life hung in the balance — and here that the dramatic reversal of fortune he called "one of those sudden, marked interpositions of [[providence|Providence]]" (011-004) spared him from being sold away from his family forever.

## Location & Geography

New Orleans, near the mouth of the [[Mississippi River]] on the Gulf of Mexico, was the largest city in the antebellum South and the busiest slave market in the [[us-america|United States]]. Its location at the terminus of [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] made it the natural endpoint for flatboat commerce from the upper South. The city's subtropical climate, with oppressive heat and humidity in summer, bred the "river fever" (likely yellow fever or malaria) that nearly killed [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] and, paradoxically, saved [[josiah-henson|Henson]].

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived in New Orleans at the end of a trading voyage from [[Kentucky]], having navigated the flatboat down [[mississippi-river|the Mississippi]] after the white captain went blind. "In a few days after this trying crisis in my life, we arrived at New Orleans. The little that remained of our cargo was soon sold, the men were discharged, and nothing was left but to dispose of me, and break up the boat" (011-001).

In New Orleans, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was subjected to the dehumanizing rituals of the slave market. "Several planters came to the boat to look at me; I was sent on some hasty errand that they might see how I could run; my points were canvassed as those of a horse would have been; and, doubtless, some account of my various faculties entered into the discussion of the bargain, that my value as a domestic animal might be enhanced" (011-001). This passage is one of the most searing depictions of the slave market in nineteenth-century [[us-america|America]]n literature.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] attempted every appeal to [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]]'s humanity: "With tears and groans I besought him not to sell me away from my wife and children. I dwelt on my past services to his father, and called to his remembrance a thousand things I had done for him" (011-002). But the young master remained unmoved. The sale was set for the following day.

What happened next is the narrative's central act of [[providence|divine intervention]]. [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] awoke before dawn feeling sick. "His stomach was disordered... Before long he felt worse, and it was soon evident that [[mississippi-river|the river]] fever was upon him. He became rapidly ill, and by eight o'clock in the morning was utterly prostrate" (011-004). The tables turned completely: "I was no longer property, no longer a brute beast to be bought and sold, but his only friend in the midst of strangers." The terrified young man begged forgiveness: "Stick to me, Sie! Stick to me, Sie! Don't leave me, don't leave me. I'm sorry I was going to sell you" (011-004).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s response was both magnanimous and shrewd. He sold the flatboat, secured Amos's trunk of proceeds, and got the dying man aboard a steamboat heading north. "He entreated me to dispatch matters, sell the flat-boat in which we had been living, and get him and his trunk, containing the proceeds of the trip, on board the steamer as quick as possible. I attended to all his requests, and by twelve o'clock that day he was in one of the [[slave-cabin|cabin]]s of the steamer" (011-004).

## Historical Context

New Orleans was the largest slave-trading center in the [[us-america|United States]]. By the 1820s, when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was brought there, the city hosted dozens of slave depots and auction houses where thousands of enslaved people were sold annually to supply the expanding cotton and sugar [[plantation]]s of Louisiana, Mississippi, and Texas. The prospect of being "sold down [[mississippi-river|the river]]" to New Orleans was the most feared fate among enslaved people in the upper South, as it meant severance from family forever and brutal conditions on the Deep South [[plantation]]s from which escape was nearly impossible. The city's yellow fever epidemics, which occurred with terrible regularity each summer, were a grim fact of life — and death — for residents and visitors alike.
