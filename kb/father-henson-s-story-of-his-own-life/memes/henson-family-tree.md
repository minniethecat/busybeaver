# Henson Family Tree

A genealogical record of [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s immediate and extended family as documented in his autobiographical narrative, *[[josiah-henson|Father Henson]]'s Story of His Own Life*.

## Parents

[[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] was born on June 15, 1789, in [[maryland|Charles County]], [[maryland|Maryland]], on a farm belonging to Mr. Francis Newman (001-002). His mother was enslaved by Dr. Josiah McPherson and hired out to the Newman farm (001-002).

- **Mother**: A slave of Dr. Josiah McPherson who raised six children. When the family was broken apart by sale, she was "paralyzed by grief" as her children were sold one by one (002-005). She was a person of deep [[religion|religious faith]], and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] credited her [[prayer|pray]]ers and spiritual example as formative influences on his own character (002-003).
- **Father**: Described as a man of "good physical constitution" who had been a [[trust]]ed and relatively contented slave until a violent confrontation with an [[overseer-system|overseer]] who attempted to assault [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother (001-006). After defending his wife, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s father was given one hundred stripes, had his ear cut off, was sold South, and never seen by the family again. Before this traumatic event, he had been known as a cheerful man who played the banjo (001-006).

## Siblings

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] was one of six children (002-002). His brothers and sisters were "bid off first, and one by one" at a [[auction-block|slave auction]] following the family's dissolution, while their mother held the infant Josiah (002-005). Specific siblings mentioned by name are not identified in the narrative.

## Marriage and Children

### Charlotte Henson (wife)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] married [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]], an enslaved woman, early in his adult life. [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] was described as a devoted wife who accompanied him on the difficult journey from [[maryland|Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] (006-010). After [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s escape to [[canada|Canada]], [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] and their children joined him later. She died shortly after his return from [[england|England]] in 1852, after approximately forty years of marriage (023-002). A merciful Father had, however, kindly prolonged her life to welcome her husband back from his mission (023-002).

### Children

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] and [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] had several children together:

- **Youngest child**: Mentioned as being with [[charlotte-henson|Charlotte]] when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned to his [[slave-cabin|cabin]] during a period of crisis (008-007). This child accompanied the family on the journey south (009-005).
- **Son Tom**: Referred to as "little Tom" who visited his mother while the family was in transit (012-006). [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s love for his son is evident in his plea to his owner "not to sell me away from my wife and children" (011-002).

Other children are referenced collectively but not individually named in the narrative. The family traveled together: the men walked while "the children were put into the wagon" (006-010). After [[freedom|emancipation]] and settlement in [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s children attended school at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] (014-007).

## Extended Family

### Isaac Riley (owner)

[[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]] was [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s legal owner in [[maryland|Maryland]]. Though not a blood relative, [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s relationship with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was significant — [[isaac-riley|Riley]] was the brother of [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] of [[kentucky|Kentucky]] (007-001), and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was transferred between them as property. The [[isaac-riley|Riley]] brothers' disputes and financial troubles directly affected [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s family stability.

### Amos Riley (owner)

[[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] was [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]]'s brother who owned a large [[plantation]] in [[kentucky|Davis County]], [[kentucky|Kentucky]]. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was sent to serve Amos in 1825 (007-001). [[amos-riley|Amos Riley]] later attempted to sell [[josiah-henson|Henson]] in [[new-orleans|New Orleans]], which precipitated [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s determination to escape (009-001).

### James Lightfoot and Jefferson Lightfoot

Not blood relatives but treated as family in the narrative. [[james-lightfoot|James Lightfoot]] was an escaped slave whose plea for help moved [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to undertake a rescue mission to the South (016-004). His brother Jefferson [[james-lightfoot|Lightfoot]] was part of the group [[josiah-henson|Henson]] led to freedom (017-004).

## Family Separation and Reunion

The theme of family separation runs through [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative. His earliest memory was of his father being taken away (001-006). At a young age, he witnessed his mother's grief as his siblings were sold at auction (002-005). As an adult, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself faced the threat of permanent separation from his wife and children when he was taken to [[new-orleans|New Orleans]] for sale (009-001). His desperate plea to his owner — "not to sell me away from my wife and children" (011-002) — reflects the centrality of family to his moral world.

After escaping to [[canada|Canada]] and establishing himself at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] worked to reunite his family and help other fugitive slaves do the same. The family reunion theme culminates in the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]] community, where multiple generations of the Henson family and other former slaves built new lives together (018-007).
