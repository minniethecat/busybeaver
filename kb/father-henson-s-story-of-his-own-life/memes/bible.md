# Bible

The Bible occupied a unique and powerful position in [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s world. As a sacred text it was the source of religious authority, as a book it was a symbol of the [[education|literacy]] denied to enslaved people, and as a personal object it was central to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own spiritual development and family life.

## Definition

In 19th-century Protestant [[us-america|America]], the Bible was the foundational religious text — regarded as the inspired Word of God, the ultimate authority for faith and practice. Access to the Bible was closely tied to [[education|literacy]], and [[education|literacy]] was systematically denied to enslaved people, making the ability to read Scripture a powerful marker of freedom and dignity. For the unlettered, the Bible was known through preaching, memorization, and the oral tradition of hymnody and spiritual exhortation.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s first encounter with a specific biblical text occurred during his conversion experience. Through John McKenny, he heard the words "He, by the grace of God, tasted death for every man" (004-001), a text from the Epistle to the Hebrews that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recalled as "the first text of the Bible to which I had ever listened, knowing it to be such" (004-001). This verse became foundational to his understanding of [[christianity|Christianity]] — an interpretation that implicitly challenged the racial hierarchies of slavery. The biblical passage was introduced with an exposition of the divine character of Christ: "The divine character of Jesus Christ, his tender love for mankind, his forgiving spirit his compassion for the outcast and despised, his cruel crucifixion and glorious ascension, were all depicted, and some of the passages were quoted from the Bible" (004-002).

After escaping to [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s inability to read became a source of deep frustration. His son Tom, having received [[education|schooling]], would read the Bible to him: "It was a great comfort to me to have made this acquisition, for he was very much to me in the Bible, especially on [[sabbath|Sunday]] mornings, when I was indisposed" (014-005). This single sentence captures an entire reversal — the father, once denied [[education|literacy]] by the slave system, now dependent on his child for access to Scripture. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] further describes a formative [[sabbath|Sabbath]] moment: "One beautiful summer [[sabbath|Sabbath]] I rose early, and called him to come and read to me" (014-006), the father now [[education|learning]] from the son.

## Henson's Perspective

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] regarded his il[[education|literacy]] as one of slavery's cruellest deprivations. When asked in [[england|England]] about his religious knowledge, he candidly acknowledged that he had "never read the Bible in my youth, and received all of my knowledge of it through the hearing of the Gospel preached" (022-007). His [[education|learning]] came through oral tradition — listening to sermons, memorizing passages, and absorbing the Bible's narratives and language through the spoken word. This oral acquisition of Scripture, shared by countless enslaved [[christianity|Christian]]s, gave [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s preaching a distinctive character: he spoke from memory and deep internalization rather than from textual study.

His desire to have his children learn to read was motivated in significant part by his wish for them to read the Bible for themselves — a freedom he had been denied. The emotional weight of having his son read Scripture to him cannot be overstated: it was simultaneously a fulfillment of parental hope and a daily reminder of what slavery had taken from him.

## Historical Context

The Bible was a contested text in antebellum [[us-america|America]]. [[abolition|Abolition]]ists cited its declarations of human equality and liberation (the Exodus narrative was particularly resonant), while pro-slavery advocates pointed to passages that seemed to regulate rather than condemn slavery. For enslaved and free Black [[christianity|Christian]]s, the Bible offered a counternarrative of [[freedom|deliverance]], dignity, and divine justice. [[reading-and-literacy|Literacy]] and Bible-reading were central to the mission of the [[manual-labor-school]] and other educational initiatives in the Canadian settlements.

The so-called "Slave Bible" — actually *Parts of the Holy Bible, selected for the use of the Negro Slaves, in the British West-India Islands* (1807) — epitomized the weaponization of Scripture: it excised approximately 90% of the Old Testament and 50% of the New, removing passages like the Exodus story that might inspire liberation. While [[josiah-henson|Henson]] does not mention this specific edition, his experience of mediated, selective access to Scripture through white preachers reflected the same logic of control. Only in freedom could he hear the Bible read in full and on his own terms.
