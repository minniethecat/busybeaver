# Manumission

Manumission — the legal act of freeing an enslaved person — is a recurring theme throughout [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s life story. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s experience with the promise and [[trust|betrayal]] of manumission shaped his understanding of slavery's injustice and ultimately motivated his escape to [[canada|Canada]].

## Henson's Failed Manumission

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s most painful experience with manumission came when his owner, [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], promised him freedom in exchange for money. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had raised a substantial sum and delivered it to [[isaac-riley|Riley]], expecting to purchase his [[freedom|liberty]]:

> I had raised a considerable sum of money, and had made arrangements to purchase my freedom. My master had agreed to take four hundred and fifty dollars for me, and I had paid him three hundred and fifty dollars of it. [006-010]

But [[isaac-riley|Riley]] betrayed the agreement, dramatically increasing the price:

> He took the money, and then told me that he would not let me go for less than one thousand dollars. [006-010]

## Religious and Legal Dimensions

Manumission under [[us-america|America]]n slavery law was entirely at the discretion of the slaveholder. Even when agreed upon, it could be revoked or renegotiated at will. For [[josiah-henson|Henson]], this legal reality was a spiritual crisis — his faith taught him about divine [[freedom|deliverance]], but human law consistently failed to provide justice.

## Manumission and the Canada Alternative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s experience with failed manumission helped him understand that freedom in the [[us-america|United States]] — even if legally purchased — was insecure. This was a key factor in his decision to escape to [[canada|Canada]] in 1830:

> I saw that in the United States there was no safety for a colored man. I resolved, therefore, to go to Canada, where the laws recognize no difference on account of color. [010-001]
