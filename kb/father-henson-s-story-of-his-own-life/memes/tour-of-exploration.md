In the autumn of 1834, [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] set out on foot to explore the territory between Lakes [[canada|Ontario]], Erie, and Huron in [[canada|Upper Canada]], seeking a suitable location where he and a group of fellow freedmen could establish a self-sufficient Black settlement. This journey of exploration led directly to the selection of the site that would become the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]].

## Overview

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] was deputed by a group of ten to twelve formerly enslaved men to explore [[canada|Upper Canada]] and find a location for colonization, where they could "raise our own crops, eat our own bread, and be, in short, our own masters" (015-002). His exploration, conducted on foot over hundreds of miles, eventually identified the fertile land east of Lake St. Clair and [[detroit|Detroit River]] as the most suitable location, which later became the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]].

## Context & Background

After about three years in [[canada|Canada]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] observed that several hundred Black persons in the region were "content to have the proceeds of their labor at their own command" but had "not the ambition for, or the perception of what was within their easy reach" (015-001). Most were working for hire on the lands of others and had "not yet dreamed of becoming independent proprietors themselves" (015-001).

With the support of his employer Mr. Riseley, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] began organizing meetings among the most successful Black settlers to discuss the idea of land ownership:

> At these meetings we considered and discussed the subject, till we were all of one mind; and it was agreed, among the ten or twelve of us who assembled at them, that we would invest our earnings in land, and undertake the task... of settling upon wild lands which we could call our own; and where every tree which we felled, and every bushel of corn we raised, would be for ourselves. (015-001)

## The Event

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] set out in the autumn of 1834 and "travelled on foot all over the extensive region between lakes [[canada|Ontario]], Erie, and Huron" (015-002). When he reached the territory east of Lake St. Clair and [[detroit|Detroit River]], he was "strongly impressed with its fertility, its convenience, and, indeed, its superiority, for our purposes, to any other spot I had seen" (015-002).

The group, "wisely cautious," sent [[josiah-henson|Henson]] out a second time in the summer to confirm his assessment. On this second journey, he discovered an extensive tract of government land that had been granted to a Mr. McCormick. This land, already partially cleared, offered immediate advantages for raising crops — a crucial consideration for settlers with limited resources (015-002).

The group eventually settled on McCormick's land, but after discovering he had not complied with the conditions of his grant, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] appealed to the legislature with the assistance of Sir John Cockburn. After two appeals, the settlers were freed from rent obligations (015-003).

## Aftermath & Significance

The exploration tour was the foundational act that led to the establishment of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]]. The group remained on the McCormick lands for six or seven years while the Black population in the region "was increasing rapidly...spreading very fast into the interior settlements and the large towns" (015-003).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] explicitly linked the exploration to his broader vision of Black economic independence, describing it as "precisely the Yankee spirit which I wished to instill into my fellow-slaves" (015-002). The settlement that grew from this exploration would become a model community for self-emancipated people, combining agriculture, education through the [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]], and industrial enterprise through [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s sawmill.

## Key Passages

> It was precisely the Yankee spirit which I wished to instill into my fellow-slaves, if possible; and I was not deterred from the task by the perception of the immense contrast in all the habits and character generated by long ages of freedom and servitude, activity and sloth, independence and subjection. (015-002)
