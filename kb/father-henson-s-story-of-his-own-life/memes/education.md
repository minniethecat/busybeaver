# Education

Education was both a personal aspiration and a community mission for [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]. Denied literacy by [[slavery|the institution]] of slavery, he devoted his free life in [[canada|Canada]] to securing educational opportunities for his children and for the broader community of fugitive slaves — culminating in the establishment of the [[manual-labor-school|Manual Labor School]] at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]].

## Definition

In [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s context, education encompassed literacy (learning to read and write), religious instruction ([[sabbath|Sabbath]] Schools, [[bible|Bible]] study), and practical vocational training (farming, milling, trades). The "manual labor school" model was widely promoted in the 19th century as a way to combine academic learning with productive labor, making education financially sustainable while teaching practical skills.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own illiteracy was a defining condition of his life as a slave, and his narrative describes the moment that sparked his desire to learn: "What gave me a desire to learn to read" (from the chapter subheading of Chapter 14). After arriving in [[canada|Canada]] and finding employment, his employer's wife took an interest in his family and arranged schooling for his eldest son Tom. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] describes the transformative effect: Tom learned "to read fluently and well" (014-005) and would read the [[bible|Bible]] to his father.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s most ambitious educational project was the establishment of the [[manual-labor-school|Manual Labor School]] at [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]]. "My interest in the [[manual-labor-school|Manual Labor School]] in [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]], was the means of my visiting [[england|England]]" (020-001), he wrote, describing how fundraising for the school took him across the Atlantic. The school aimed to teach "the ordinary branches of an English education" alongside practical trades (from the corpus text describing the settlement's schools).

## Henson's Perspective

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] saw education as essential to the future of the fugitive slave community. He lectured his neighbors "upon the subject of crops, wages, and profit, just as if I had been brought up to these things" (018-002), attempting to instill habits of industry and self-improvement. He "urged the appropriation of the money to the establishment of a manual-labor school, where our children could be taught those elements of knowledge which are usually the occupations of a grammar-school" (018-005).

His own experience of learning through oral tradition — hearing sermons, memorizing scripture, absorbing knowledge without being able to read — gave him a profound appreciation for what literacy could offer the next generation.

> I urged the appropriation of the money to the establishment of a manual-labor school, where our children could be taught those elements of knowledge which are usually the occupations of a grammar-school; and where the boys could be taught, in addition, the practice of some mechanic art, and the girls could be instructed in those domestic arts which are the proper occupation and ornament of their sex.
>
> (018-005)


## Historical Context

The denial of literacy to enslaved people was not incidental to [[slavery|the institution]] of slavery — it was a deliberate strategy of control. Most Southern states had laws prohibiting the education of enslaved people, recognizing that literacy could lead to dangerous ideas about freedom and rights. In [[canada|Canada]], by contrast, fugitive slave communities prioritized education as the foundation of genuine freedom. [[sabbath|Sabbath]] Schools, manual labor schools, and grammar schools were established across the settlements, representing a radical alternative to the enforced ignorance of the slave South.
