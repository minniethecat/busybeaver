The **journey to [[kentucky|Kentucky]]** was a pivotal event in [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s life, in which he led a group of eighteen enslaved people — men, women, and children — from [[maryland|Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] in the winter of 1825. During the journey, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was presented with a clear opportunity to escape to freedom but refused, choosing instead to honor his commitment to his master. This decision became one of the defining moral moments of his narrative, one he later regarded with deep ambivalence.

## Background

By 1825, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s master, [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], had fallen into financial distress due to "continual dissipation" and a lawsuit with a brother-in-law who accused him of mismanaging his wife's estate. Facing the prospect of losing all his enslaved people to creditors, [[isaac-riley|Riley]] devised a desperate plan:[006-004]

One night in January, [[isaac-riley|Riley]] woke [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and made an extraordinary proposition:

> "I want you to run away, Sie, to your master Amos in Kentucky, and take all the servants along with you."
> [006-007]

[[isaac-riley|Riley]] argued that this was the only way to save the enslaved people from being sold separately to different owners. The plan was for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to transport the entire group — eighteen people — overland to [[kentucky|Kentucky]], where Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]] would receive them.[006-008]

## The Journey

With few preparations — a one-horse wagon stocked with provisions — the party set out at eleven o'clock at night. The men walked on foot while the women and children rode in the wagon, taking turns to rest.[006-010]

The route took them through slave states where they frequently encountered slave-drivers with chained groups:

> At the places where we stopped for the night, we often met negro-drivers with their droves, who were almost uniformly kept chained to prevent them from running away.
> [006-011]

Arriving at [[wheeling|Wheeling]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] sold the horse and wagon and purchased a large flatboat, continuing the journey on [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River.[006-012]

## The Temptation of Freedom

As they traveled along [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] shore, a profound temptation arose. [[all-persons|People]] on [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] side repeatedly told [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and his group that they were already on free soil — crossing to [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] side would mean freedom for all of them:[006-013]

> On passing along the Ohio shore, we were repeatedly told by persons conversing with us, that we were no longer slaves, but free men, if we chose to be so.
> [006-013]

The allurements of freedom had a "manifest effect" on the group. [[josiah-henson|Henson]], however, made a fateful decision. He assumed the role of captain, ordered the boat pushed off into the stream, and continued toward [[kentucky|Kentucky]].[006-014]

## Moral Aftermath

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s choice haunted him for the rest of his life:

> Often since that day has my soul been pierced with bitter anguish at the thought of having been thus instrumental in consigning to the infernal bondage of slavery so many of my fellow-beings.
> [006-015]

He recognized that he had thrown away a genuine opportunity for freedom — not just for himself but for eighteen others. His motivation was a combination of loyalty to his master, pride in his reputation for reliability, and belief in the virtue of keeping one's word:

> What advantages I may have personally lost by thus throwing away an opportunity of obtaining freedom, I know not; but the perception of my own strength of character, the feeling of integrity, the sentiment of trustworthiness — these things were the reward of my conduct.
> [006-016]

## Significance

The journey to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] reveals several central tensions in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s character and in the slave narrative genre:

1. **Loyalty vs. freedom**: [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s choice illustrates how [[slavery|the institution]] of slavery created impossible moral dilemmas. By keeping his word to his master, he condemned others (and himself) to continued [[slavery|bondage]].

2. **The "faithful slave" trope**: [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s narrative both embraces and complicates this conventional figure. His [[trust|faithfulness]] is presented as a marker of character, but he also acknowledges its tragic consequences.

3. **Paternalism and responsibility**: [[josiah-henson|Henson]] genuinely believed he was protecting the group from worse fates, yet his later anguish suggests he recognized this as self-[[trust|deception]].

4. **Foreshadowing escape**: The journey established [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s knowledge of the route north and his familiarity with [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River border between slavery and freedom — knowledge he would use years later in his own [[escape-to-canada|escape to Canada]].
