[[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]], then a young child, was sold at auction with his mother and five siblings following the death of their owner, Dr. McPherson, in [[maryland|Montgomery County]], [[maryland|Maryland]], around 1795. The event marked the definitive breakup of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s family of origin and his entry into a life of [[slavery|chattel slavery]] under a new master.

## Overview

The auction occurred shortly after the death of Dr. McPherson, who had been a relatively kind owner. His estate, including all enslaved people, was liquidated to settle debts. [[josiah-henson|Henson]], then approximately five or six years old, witnessed his brothers and sisters being sold one by one before he himself was purchased by a stranger and separated from his mother (002-005).

## Context & Background

For two or three years before the auction, [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother and her six children had lived on Dr. McPherson's estate and "had been in the main very happy" (002-002). [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s father had previously been sold away by a man named Newman, and Dr. McPherson had refused to hire out [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother to him any longer (002-001). Dr. McPherson was described as "far kinder to his slaves than the planters generally were" in [[maryland|Maryland]] (002-001).

The doctor's sudden death brought this period of relative stability to an abrupt end. As [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recounted, the death "was a far greater calamity to us" because the estate and enslaved people "must be sold and the proceeds divided among the heirs" (002-003).

## The Event

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] described the [[auction-block|slave auction]] as an experience whose "full misery...no one can realize" (002-004). Though [[auction-block|slave auction]]s were common in the southern states, the personal devastation of the event was profound.

The auction proceeded with each child being sold individually:

> My brothers and sisters were bid off first, and one by one, while my mother, paralyzed by grief, held me by the hand. Her turn came, and she was bought by Isaac Riley of Montgomery county. Then I was offered to the assembled purchasers. (002-005)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother was purchased by [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]], who would later become [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s own master. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself was bought by a stranger named Robb, whom he described as "truly a robber he was to me" (002-006). Robb took the young [[josiah-henson|Henson]] approximately forty miles away and placed him in [[slave-cabin|slave quarters]] with about forty others.

## Aftermath & Significance

The auction permanently separated [[josiah-henson|Henson]] from his mother and siblings. He was taken forty miles from his family to live among strangers, describing the quarters as containing people "of all ages, colors, and conditions" (002-006). The separation was so complete that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later wrote he never saw his mother again for many years.

This event was formative in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s understanding of the fundamental [[kindness|cruelty]] of slavery — not only the physical [[kindness|brutality]] but the deliberate destruction of family bonds. His purchase by [[isaac-riley|Isaac Riley]]'s associate Robb eventually led him back into the orbit of the [[isaac-riley|Riley]] family, setting the stage for the decades of service, [[trust|betrayal]], and eventual escape that defined his life story (002-007).

## Key Passages

> Common as are slave-auctions in the southern states, and naturally as a slave may look forward to the time when he will be put up on the block, still the full misery of the event--of the scenes which precede and succeed it--is never understood till the actual experience comes. (002-004)
