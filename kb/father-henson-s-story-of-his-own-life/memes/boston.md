# Boston

Boston, Massachusetts, was the city where [[Josiah Henson]] secured the crucial financial backing that enabled the industrial development of the [[Dawn Settlement]] in [[Canada]]. The city's abolitionist elite — including [[Amos Lawrence]], [[samuel-eliot|Samuel Eliot]], and [[Ephraim Peabody]] — provided the capital for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s sawmill enterprise and became ongoing supporters of his work.

## Location & Geography

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] reached Boston in the course of a "journey of observation through the State of [[new-york|New York]], and New [[england|England]]," undertaken to find markets for Canadian lumber (019-002). The city, a major Atlantic port and the intellectual center of [[us-america|America]]n abolitionism, was a natural destination for a fugitive slave seeking allies.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s connection to Boston began through Rev. [[Ephraim Peabody]], who "introduced me to [[samuel-eliot|Samuel Eliot, Esq.]], who was kind enough to examine carefully into all my representations" (019-004). Eliot drew up a formal proposal that was "presented to [[amos-lawrence|Amos Lawrence, Esq.]], and others," resulting in a collection "amounting to about fourteen hundred dollars" — contributed by "many of the leading gentlemen of Boston" (019-004). This was a substantial sum in the early 1840s.

With this capital, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned to [[canada|Canada]] and built a sawmill at Camden (later [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]]). He later "returned to my Boston friends" and secured additional support from "[[amos-lawrence|Amos Lawrence]], [[h-ingersoll-bowditch|H. Ingersoll Bowditch]]," and others who enabled him to purchase the land outright (019-005 to 019-006). The lumber operation became a success — [[josiah-henson|Henson]] records bringing "the whole eighty thousand feet safe to Boston" for sale, demonstrating the mutually beneficial nature of the Boston–[[dawn-settlement|Dawn]] connection (019-008).

Boston also figures in the narrative as a zone of relative safety compared to the slave states. The city was the center of Garrisonian abolitionism and, despite the [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] of 1850, remained a stronghold of [[abolition|anti-slavery]] sentiment throughout the antebellum period.

## Historical Context

By the 1840s, Boston was the financial and organizational hub of [[us-america|America]]n abolitionism. Figures like William Lloyd Garrison, Wendell Phillips, and the Lawrence family provided funding and political advocacy for fugitive slave communities throughout the North and [[canada|Canada]]. The "Boston–[[canada|Canada]] axis" — connecting New [[england|England]] philanthropy with Canadian settlement projects — was a significant channel through which abolitionist sentiment translated into material support for formerly enslaved people.

## Key Passages

> "On reaching Boston, Mass. I made known these facts and my feelings to some philanthropic gentlemen with whom I had become acquainted. It cannot be improper for me to mention the names of these gentlemen, who lent so ready an ear to my representations, and placed so much confidence in my judgment, as to furnish me with the means of starting what has since proved a very profitable enterprise." (019-003)

> "A collection of money to aid me was made, to which many of the leading gentleman of Boston contributed, amounting to about fourteen hundred dollars." (019-004)
