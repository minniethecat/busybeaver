# Publishing the Autobiography

The publication of [[Josiah Henson]]'s narrative — _[[josiah-henson|Father Henson]]'s Story of His Own Life_ — in [[London]] in 1858. The first edition of 2,000 copies was both an antislavery testimony and a fundraising instrument for the [[Dawn Settlement]] in [[Canada]].

## Background

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] traveled to [[England]] in 1851 with a dual purpose: to exhibit his [[Dawn Settlement]] black walnut lumber at the [[World's Fair in London|Great World's Fair]], and to raise funds for the [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]] at [[Dawn]] (020-004). His success at the Fair — where his boards won a prize medal — opened doors to [[london|London]]'s evangelical and abolitionist circles (021-005).

> "I have already mentioned that the first idea which suggested to me the plan of going to England, was to exhibit, at the Great World's Fair, in London, some of the best specimens of our black walnut lumber." (021-001)

During his English sojourn, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] preached in the pulpits of leading Congregational ministers — [[Thomas Binney]], [[Samuel Morley]], and others — and was introduced to influential philanthropists who became patrons of his cause (020-007).

## The Publication

The idea of publishing his life story emerged from conversations with English supporters. His narrative — dictated to an amanuensis and edited for publication — told the story of his forty-one years in [[slavery]], his [[Escape to Canada]], and his work building the [[Dawn Settlement]].

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s supporters recognized that a published autobiography would serve multiple purposes: it would publicize the horrors of [[us-america|America]]n [[slavery]] to a British audience, establish [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s credibility as a spokesman for the antislavery cause, and generate income through sales and donations.

The first edition was printed in [[London]] in 1858, with an initial run of 2,000 copies. The preface was written by [[John Lobb]], a prominent British lay preacher and businessman who became [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s close ally. Lobb attested to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s character and the truth of his account, lending the book the endorsement of a respected English [[christianity|Christian]].

## Aftermath and Impact

The autobiography became the primary vehicle for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s fundraising in [[england|England]]. He sold copies at his lectures and church appearances, using the proceeds to discharge the remaining debts of the [[Dawn Settlement]]'s [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]] and sawmill (020-013).

The book also established [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s public identity. He was now not merely a fugitive slave turned preacher and community builder — he was a published author, whose life story stood as a documented rebuke to the slave system. The autobiography would go through multiple editions and adaptations, with expanded versions published in subsequent decades.

The publication also strengthened [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s relationship with British abolitionists. Men who had read his narrative were more inclined to donate to the [[Dawn Settlement]], confident that their money was supporting a man of proven character and documented achievements.
