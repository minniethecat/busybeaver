# Josiah Henson

Josiah Henson (June 15, 1789 – 1883) was the author of *Father Henson's Story of His Own Life* (1858), a formerly enslaved man who escaped from [[Kentucky]] to [[Canada]], founded the [[Dawn Settlement]] for fugitive slaves, and became a prominent [[Methodist Episcopal Church|Methodist]] preacher and [[abolition]] speaker. His narrative was cited as one of the inspirations for Harriet Beecher Stowe's *Uncle Tom's Cabin* (1852), earning him the posthumous title "the original Uncle Tom."

Born in [[Maryland]], enslaved under [[Isaac Riley]], converted at a Methodist [[Camp Meeting]], and ultimately a free man in [[canada|Ontario]], Henson's life arc traces the entire geography of [[us-america|America]]n slavery — from the border South through [[ohio-river|the Ohio]] River crossing to the "[[canada|land of freedom]]" in British North [[us-america|America]].

## Early Life in Slavery

Josiah Henson was born on June 15, 1789, in [[maryland|Charles County]], [[Maryland]], on a farm belonging to Mr. Francis Newman, about a mile from [[maryland|Port Tobacco]] (001-002). His earliest memory was formative and traumatic:

> "My father was a slave in Maryland, and was owned by a man who cultivated a farm. He was a brave, strong man, and did not allow himself to be treated as a slave. One day, the overseer attempted to violate the person of my mother, and my father interfered to protect her. The overseer ran away, but returned with several men, and they overpowered my father, and tied him to a tree. For this offence, he was sentenced to receive one hundred lashes, to have his right ear cut off, and to be sold into the far South." (001-004)

This event — witnessing his father's brutal punishment for defending his mother — shaped Henson's understanding of the absolute power wielded by enslavers. His father was sold to the Deep South, never to be seen again, while Henson and his mother were subsequently sold at auction to [[Isaac Riley]] of [[maryland|Montgomery County]] (002-005).

## Religious Conversion and Preaching

Until the age of eighteen, Henson had "never heard a sermon, nor any discourse or conversation whatever, upon religious topics" (004-001). His moral education came entirely from the brutal environment of slavery. The turning point was a [[Camp Meeting]] where a [[methodism|Methodist preacher]] expounded Hebrews 2:9:

> "He preached from the text, 'that Jesus Christ tasted death for every man.' The emphasis he laid on the word *every* man startled me. It was a new revelation to me. Did Christ taste death for *me*? For *every* man meant for me. How did I know this? Because the preacher said so, and proved it from the Bible." (004-002)

This conversion experience — the realization that Christ's salvation extended to an enslaved man — redirected Henson's life. He began preaching to fellow enslaved people, becoming "quite an esteemed preacher among them" (004-001). His Methodist credentials later provided the mobility and network connections that enabled his escape.

## Escape to Freedom

By 1830, despite years of faithful service — including a remarkable overland journey transporting enslaved people from [[maryland|Maryland]] to [[kentucky|Kentucky]] on behalf of his master — Henson learned he was to be sold south. The threat of permanent separation from his family galvanized him into action. His [[Escape to Canada|escape]] is one of the most dramatic passages in the narrative.

Henson's family departed at night in a small boat, crossing to the [[indiana|Indiana]] shore (012-006). For two weeks they pressed northward through [[ohio|Ohio]], traveling by night and resting by day. On October 28, 1830, they crossed the Niagara River into [[Canada]]:

> "It was the 28th of October, 1830, in the morning, when my feet first touched the soil of a land of freedom. I was then about forty-one years of age, having lived more than forty years in bondage. I can truly say I know what slavery is, and what freedom is." (013-012)

The emotional impact was overwhelming. Henson "thanked God with all the fervor of a full heart for his great mercy" and immediately began the work of building a new life.

## The Dawn Settlement

Henson's most enduring achievement was the founding of the [[Dawn Settlement]] near Dresden, [[canada|Ontario]]. He envisioned a community where fugitive slaves could achieve economic independence through skilled labor and agriculture. The land was "covered with a beautiful forest of noble trees" that Henson and other settlers cleared through lumbering operations (019-001).

The centerpiece was the [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]] (also known as the [[dawn-settlement|British American Institute]]), combining academic education with vocational training. Henson raised funds through multiple trips to [[england|England]], including attendance at the 1851 [[london|Great Exhibition]] in [[london|London]], and through the export of black walnut lumber to foreign markets.

## Historical Significance

Henson's narrative is important not merely as a personal memoir but as a primary source for understanding the lived experience of [[us-america|America]]n slavery, the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]], and the establishment of Black Canadian communities. Its publication in 1858 — on the eve of the Civil War — contributed to the abolitionist cause by providing a first-person account of slavery's [[kindness|brutality]] from someone who had survived it.

The narrative also represents a distinct theological voice within the abolitionist tradition. Unlike secular abolitionists, Henson framed his liberation in explicitly [[christianity|Christian]] terms, seeing divine providence at work in his escape and subsequent mission. This theological framing — combining moral condemnation of slavery with a vision of [[christianity|Christian]] redemption — gave his testimony particular force with religious audiences.
