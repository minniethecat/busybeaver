# Hiram Wilson

**Hiram Wilson** was a Congregational missionary from [[boston|Massachusetts]] who became a long-term ally of [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] in improving the conditions of Black settlers in [[canada|Canada]].

## Meeting with Henson

Wilson met [[josiah-henson|Henson]] while [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was working among the Black community at Colchester, [[canada|Canada]]. As [[josiah-henson|Henson]] recorded:

> While I remained at Colchester, I became acquainted with a Congregational missionary from Massachusetts, by the name of Hiram Wilson, who took an interest in our people, and was disposed to do what he could to promote the cause of improvement which I had so much at heart. (018-003)

## Partnership and Fundraising

Wilson became a steadfast collaborator, cooperating with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] in many efforts from 1836 onward. One of his most consequential actions was writing to his [[quaker|Quaker]] friend [[james-c-fuller|James C. Fuller]] in [[new-york|New York]], urging him to support the Canadian Black settlers. This letter led to Fuller raising fifteen hundred dollars during a visit to [[england|England]] for the benefit of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s community.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] described Wilson as "a faithful friend" who "still continues his important labors of love in our behalf" at the time of the narrative's publication.

## Legacy

Hiram Wilson exemplified the network of New [[england|England]] missionaries who supported the [[underground-railroad|Underground Railroad]] and the settlement of fugitive slaves in [[canada|Canada]]. His long collaboration with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — spanning from 1836 to at least the 1850s — contributed to the development of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] and the broader effort to establish self-sufficient Black communities in [[canada|Upper Canada]].
