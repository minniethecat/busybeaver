# Lord John Russell

**Lord John Russell** (1792–1878), later 1st Earl Russell, was a British Whig politician who served as Prime Minister of the [[england|United Kingdom]] from 1846 to 1852. He hosted [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] at a notable dinner at his [[richmond-park|Richmond Park]] estate during [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s fundraising tour of [[england|England]].

## The Dinner at Richmond Park

During [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s visit to [[england|England]] in 1851–1852, he was invited to dine at Lord John Russell's estate. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] devoted an entire chapter section to this event, which he titled "[[lord-john-russell-dinner|Dinner with Lord John Russell]], the Great Events of My Life." The dinner represented one of the highest points of social recognition [[josiah-henson|Henson]] received during his English tour. (023-001)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] visited "the beautiful grounds of Lord John Russell, then Prime Minister of [[england|England]]," where he met with British dignitaries and discussed the cause of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] and the welfare of fugitive slaves in [[canada|Canada]]. (022-005)

## Significance

Russell's willingness to host a formerly enslaved Black man at his private estate while serving as Prime Minister demonstrated the considerable social and political access that British abolitionists secured for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] during his fundraising tour. (022-005) The meeting was emblematic of the broader British evangelical and abolitionist network — which included figures like [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]] and the Archbishop of Canterbury — that provided crucial support to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s Canadian enterprise.

Lord John Russell was a known advocate for reform, having championed the Reform Act of 1832 and various measures of social improvement. His engagement with [[josiah-henson|Henson]] reflected the Whig tradition of antislavery sentiment in British politics.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s reaction to the Prime Minister's estate revealed the contrast with his former condition:

> His magnificent park, filled with deer, of all colors, and from all climes, and sleek hares, which the poet Cowper would have envied, with numberless birds, whose plumage rivalled the rainbow in gorgeous colors, together with the choicest specimens of the finny tribe, sporting in their native element, drew from me the involuntary exclamation: "O, how different the condition of these happy, sportive, joyful, creatures, from what was once my own condition, and what is now the lot of millions of my colored brethren in America!"
>
> (022-005)
