# Slavery

The institution of chattel slavery in the [[us-america|United States]], as experienced first-hand by [[Josiah Henson]] from his birth in 1789 through his [[Escape to Canada|escape]] in 1830, and as documented in his 1858 narrative. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s account provides one of the most detailed first-person records of the lived experience of enslavement in the border South.

## Definition and Legal Framework

In 19th-century [[us-america|America]], slavery was a system of forced labor and legal ownership of human beings, concentrated in the Southern states including [[Maryland]] and [[Kentucky]] where [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was held. The laws of slave states provided "means and opportunities for revenge" that enabled brutal treatment "short of the direct and extreme punishment which could be administered by the [[overseer-system|overseer]]" (001-003).

## In the Narrative

Slavery is the central institution against which [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s life story unfolds. From his earliest memory — witnessing his father's savage punishment for defending [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s mother (001-004) — through the auction block where his family was dispersed (002-005), to his eventual escape, the narrative documents slavery's [[kindness|brutality]] in concrete, personal terms.

> "The laws of slave states provide means and opportunities for revenge, so that it is easy to find a mode of punishing the slave to almost any extent, short of the direct and extreme punishment which could be administered by the overseer." (001-003)

The threat of being "sold South" — transported from the relatively milder conditions of the border states to the lethal cotton and sugar [[plantation]]s of the Deep South — functions throughout the narrative as the ultimate terror. This fear was well-founded: the domestic slave trade relocated approximately one million enslaved people from the Upper South to the Deep South between 1790 and 1860.

## Henson's Critique

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s critique of [[slavery]] operates on multiple levels:

1. **Moral**: Slavery corrupts both enslaver and enslaved — converting "the master into a tyrant, and the slave into the cringing, treacherous, false, and thieving victim of tyranny" (002-007)
2. **Economic**: Slavery devalues labor; [[josiah-henson|Henson]] notes that in [[Canada]], his employer valued his work "more than that of those who worked for mere wages" (014-003)
3. **Religious**: Slavery is incompatible with [[christianity|Christianity]]; the doctrine that "Jesus Christ tasted death for *every* man" (004-002) — including enslaved men — fundamentally contradicts the institution
4. **Psychological**: Slavery inflicts ongoing trauma through family separation and constant threat

> "Riley and his slaves were no exception to the general rule, but might be cited as apt illustrations of the nature of the relation" — specifically, the tendency of slavery "to convert the master into a tyrant, and the slave into the cringing, treacherous, false, and thieving victim of tyranny." (002-007)

## Historical Context

By 1858, when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] published his narrative, the [[us-america|United States]] was deeply divided over slavery. The [[fugitive-slave-law|Fugitive Slave Act]] of 1850 had made escape to free Northern states insufficient, driving many [[freedom]] seekers to [[canada|Canada]]. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s account, appearing three years before the Civil War, contributed to the [[abolition]] movement by providing an authoritative first-person testimony of slavery's [[kindness|brutality]].
