# British and Foreign Anti-Slavery Society

The [[British and Foreign Anti-Slavery Society]] (BFASS) was a prominent British abolitionist organization founded in 1839. Its secretary, [[John Scobell]], played a key role in overseeing [[Josiah Henson]]'s fundraising efforts during his 1851–1852 visit to [[England]].

## Role in Henson's England Mission

When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] traveled to [[england|England]] to raise funds for his Canadian settlement and [[manual-labor-school-association|manual-labor school]], he faced accusations that he was an impostor collecting money under false pretenses. In response, he requested the formation of an independent oversight committee — a "committee of twelve" prominent British figures — to examine his enterprise and manage all donated funds.

[[john-scobell|John Scobell]], as Secretary of the British and Foreign Anti-Slavery Society, was one of the committee members:

> "This committee had been appointed, and consisted of Samuel Gurney, Samuel Gurney, Junior, Samuel Marley, Esq., George Hitchcock, Esq., Rev. James Sherman, Rev. Thomas Binney, Rev. John Branch, Eusebius Smith, Esq., John Scobell, Secretary of the British and Foreign Anti-slavery Society, Lord Ashley (now Earl of Shaftsbury), George Sturge, and Thomas Sturge." (020-007)

Scobell also served on the sub-committee of three (along with [[john-branch|Rev. John Branch]] and [[eusebius-smith|Eusebius Smith]]), which had direct authority over the disbursement of funds. [[samuel-gurney|Samuel Gurney]], Junior, was appointed treasurer.

## Historical Significance

The BFASS was part of a broader British [[abolition|abolitionist movement]] that had achieved the abolition of slavery in the [[england|British Empire]] in 1833. By the time of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s visit in the 1850s, the society continued to support abolitionist causes abroad, including the Canadian settlements for fugitive slaves. The society's involvement lent institutional credibility to [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s enterprise, countering the anonymous circular that had sought to discredit him.

The committee was formed in response to public attacks on [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s character:

> Much to my astonishment, therefore, when I had arrived in England, and had been cordially received by the men above mentioned... I was confronted by a printed and published circular, to the following effect: "That one styling himself Rev. Josiah Henson was an impostor, obtaining money under false pretences." ... I had already requested my friends to appoint a committee of twelve persons to examine carefully into the merits of my enterprise, which committee should appoint a sub-committee of three, and a treasurer, to receive every farthing contributed to me by the public.
>
> (020-007)
