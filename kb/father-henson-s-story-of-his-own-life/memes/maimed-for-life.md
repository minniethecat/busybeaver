## Description

The event known as "Maimed for Life" occurred when [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] was approximately eighteen years old, serving as the personal attendant and de facto superintendent of Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]]'s [[plantation]] in [[Kentucky]]. During a stop at a tavern, [[isaac-riley|Riley]] became entangled in a quarrel with his brother's [[overseer-system|overseer]], Bryce Litton. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] intervened to protect his master, sustaining catastrophic injuries that left him permanently disabled in both arms — his shoulder blades broken and his arms rendered incapable of lifting above his head for the rest of his life.

## Narrative

The confrontation began when Amos [[isaac-riley|Riley]] got into a quarrel with Bryce Litton at a tavern. "All present sided with Litton against him, and soon there was a general row." [[josiah-henson|Henson]], waiting outside, heard the commotion and rushed in: "The moment he saw me he hallooed, 'That's it, Sie! pitch in!'" (005-002). As [[josiah-henson|Henson]] defended his master, Litton struck him with a stick, "drawing blood freely; shouting all the while, 'Won't you give up! won't you give up! you black son of a bitch!'" (005-005).

The fatal blow came when Litton "suddenly seized a heavy fence-rail, and rushed at me to bring matters to a sudden close. The ponderous blow fell; I lifted my arm to ward it off; the bone cracked like a pipe-stem, and I fell headlong to the ground. Repeated blows then rained on my back, till both shoulder-blades were broken, and the blood gushed copiously from my mouth" (005-005).

## Consequences

The injury was permanent. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] never regained full use of his arms:

> On one of these occasions my master got into a quarrel with his brother's overseer, Bryce Litton. All present sided with Litton against him, and soon there was a general row. I was sitting, at the time, out on the front steps of the tavern, and, hearing the scuffle, rushed in to look after my charge. (005-002)

Despite his injuries, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] retained his position as superintendent — [[isaac-riley|Riley]] valued his management too much to replace him. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later reflected: "My situation as [[overseer-system|overseer]] I retained, together with the especial favor of my master" (005-008). The event became emblematic of the physical toll slavery exacted even on those who rose to positions of [[trust]] and responsibility within the system. For the remainder of his life, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] could not lift his hands above his head.

## Significance

The maiming is one of the most vivid illustrations in the narrative of how slavery consumed both body and dignity. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] risked his life to protect his master — an act of loyalty that in a free society might have earned him recognition or reward. Instead, the event demonstrated the boundless physical vulnerability of enslaved people: the [[overseer-system|overseer]]'s violence was unchecked, and [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had no legal recourse. That he went on to become a preacher, conductor on the [[Underground Railroad]], and founder of the [[Dawn Settlement]] — all with permanently disabled arms — underscores the extraordinary resilience that characterizes his entire life story.
