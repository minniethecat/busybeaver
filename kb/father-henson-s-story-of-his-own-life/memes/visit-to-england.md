In 1851, [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] traveled to [[england|England]] to raise funds for the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]]'s [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]], carrying specimens of Canadian black walnut lumber to exhibit. During his visit he met with prominent British abolitionists, spoke at public meetings, was cleared of false accusations brought against him, and successfully retired the entire debt of the school.

## Overview

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s visit to [[england|England]] was motivated by the financial difficulties of the [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]] in [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]]. He conceived a plan to exhibit black walnut boards at the [[london|Great Exhibition]] (the World's Fair) in [[london|London]] as a means of drawing attention to the settlement's work while fundraising among British supporters of abolition. Despite attacks on his character from a rival party, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was fully vindicated before English gentlemen and went on to raise enough funds to cancel the school's debt (020-013).

## Context & Background

The [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]] in [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]], which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had helped establish, was struggling financially. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described the situation: "No one who has never engaged in such business can have any idea of the many difficulties which accompany the collection of funds for an object of this kind" (020-001). A certain party agreed to assume the school, but the mill remained a problem. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] resolved to take on the mill himself, provided he could find backing.

His "secret project" was to go to [[england|England]] carrying "some of the best specimens of black walnut boards our farm would produce" to exhibit and sell, simultaneously promoting the settlement's cause (020-004).

## The Event

Upon arriving in [[england|England]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was "cordially received" by the men he had hoped to meet. However, a party from the rival faction that had assumed the school followed him and attempted to discredit him. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] refused to name his accuser, stating only that the trouble was designed to "prejudice the minds of the English public against my operations" (020-008).

A meeting was called before "a company of English gentlemen, who heard all that my accuser had to say" (020-010). After hearing both sides, they declared themselves "entirely satisfied" with [[josiah-henson|Henson]]. To quiet public concern, a mass meeting was held at [[slavery|the institution]], where "a large assemblage met, and friends came from all parts of the country" (020-012).

> The ground was now prepared for me, and I reaped an abundant harvest. The whole debt of the institution was cancelled in England. (020-013)

While in [[england|England]], [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was also "frequently called upon to speak at public meetings of various kinds" (022-001). He became "well acquainted with many of the leading men of [[england|England]]" including [[lord-grey|Lord Grey]], and enjoyed a long interview with the Archbishop of Canterbury that far exceeded the promised quarter hour (022-003, 022-004).

## Aftermath & Significance

The visit to [[england|England]] was a transformative success. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] not only cleared the debt of the [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]] but established enduring connections with the British [[abolition|abolitionist movement]]. His ability to navigate the controversy over his character demonstrated his diplomatic skill and the [[trust]] he had earned from English supporters.

The trip also connected the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] to an international network of reformers. [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s meetings with figures like the Archbishop of Canterbury and his participation in dinners with distinguished guests showed the reach of his advocacy. The English fundraising model would prove essential to the survival and growth of the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]] community.
