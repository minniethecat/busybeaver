Mr. Hibbard was a Canadian farmer and landowner in [[canada|Upper Canada]] who became [[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s first employer after [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s [[escape-to-canada|escape from slavery]] in 1830. Over approximately three years of service, Hibbard provided [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s family with shelter, steady work, and the first educational opportunity for his eldest son Tom — forming a crucial bridge in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s transition from fugitive slave to self-sufficient settler.

## Henson's Arrival and Employment

After crossing the Niagara River into [[canada|Canada]] on October 28, 1830, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] arrived near [[fort-erie|Fort Erie]] as "a stranger in a strange land" (014-001), knowing neither the country nor the people. Making inquiries among the local residents, he learned of Mr. Hibbard, who lived six or seven miles distant — a man of relative wealth with a large farm and several tenements that he rented to laborers.

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] approached Hibbard immediately, driven by the urgent need to provide for his family. The family's first weeks were harsh:

> Another trial awaited me which I had not anticipated. In consequence of the great exposures we had been through, my wife and all the children fell sick; and it was not without extreme peril that they escaped death. (014-002)

Hibbard provided straw for bedding, which [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s wife "laughed and acknowledged that it was worth while, and that it was better than a [[slave-cabin|log cabin]] with an earth-floor."

## Three Years of Labor

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] remained in Hibbard's service for approximately three years under a flexible arrangement:

| Period | Labor Arrangement | Material Progress |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Year 1 | Shares and wages | Basic shelter, straw bedding |
| Year 2 | Shares and wages | Acquired pigs |
| Year 3 | Shares and wages | Acquired a cow and a horse |

This period marked [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s first experience of compensated labor — work performed by mutual agreement rather than forced servitude. The acquisition of livestock represented the beginnings of genuine economic independence.

## Education of Tom Henson

Hibbard's most consequential contribution was providing [[education|schooling]] for [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s eldest son:

> Mr. Hibbard was good enough to give my eldest boy, Tom, two quarters' schooling, to which the schoolmaster added more, of his own kindness, so that my boy learned to read fluently and well. It was a great advantage, not only to him, but to me; for I used to get him to read much to me in the Bible, especially on Sunday mornings. (014-005)

Tom's [[education|literacy]] had cascading effects beyond the immediate family: because Tom could read the [[Bible]] aloud, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] — who was himself illiterate — gained direct access to scripture. This deepened his religious conviction and later underpinned his career as a [[methodist-episcopal-church|Methodist]] preacher and community leader.

## Character and Limitations

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s portrait of Hibbard is measured rather than idealized. When [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later moved to work for a neighboring gentleman named Riseley, he described his new employer as "a man of more elevation of mind than Mr. Hibbard, and of superior abilities." This comparison suggests Hibbard was a practical, earthbound man — generous within limits but lacking the broader humanitarian vision that would characterize the more active [[abolition|abolitionists]] [[josiah-henson|Henson]] later encountered in [[boston-riot|Boston]] and [[england|England]].

## Later Departure

After three years, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] left Hibbard's employ to work for Mr. Riseley, whose residence was only a few miles distant. It was during this subsequent period that [[josiah-henson|Henson]] began reflecting more deeply on the condition of [[slavery|fugitive slaves]] settling in the region:

> At his place I began to reflect, more and more, upon the circumstances of the blacks, who were already somewhat numerous in this region. (014-003)

This reflection planted the seed for what would eventually become the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]].
