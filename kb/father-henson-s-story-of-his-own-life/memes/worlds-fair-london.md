[[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]] attended the 1851 [[london|Great Exhibition]] in [[london|London]], commonly known as the World's Fair, where he exhibited polished black walnut boards from the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] sawmill. His humble contribution attracted the attention of Queen Victoria and earned him international recognition.

## Overview

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s plan to attend the World's Fair was part of his broader fundraising mission to [[england|England]]. By exhibiting black walnut lumber from the [[dawn-settlement|Dawn Settlement]] at the [[london|Crystal Palace]], he sought to demonstrate the quality of Canadian products and the productive capacity of a Black settlement, while generating orders that would sustain the community's economy (021-001). The exhibition proved to be a personal and professional triumph.

## Context & Background

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s initial idea was to "exhibit, at the World's Fair, in [[london|London]], some of the best specimens of our black walnut lumber" as a means of drawing attention to the settlement while raising funds for the [[dawn-settlement|Manual Labor School]] (021-001). The black walnut boards were products of the sawmill he operated in [[dawn-settlement|Dawn]].

## The Event

The journey of [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s boards to the exhibition floor was itself remarkable. Because they "happened to be carried over in the same ship with the [[us-america|United States]] contribution," an [[us-america|America]]n commissioner attempted to claim them as part of the [[us-america|United States]] exhibit, asserting "you can't exhibit your things in the name of the [[canada|Canada]] company" because [[canada|Canada]] was a British colony (021-002).

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] found the commissioner's position "rather absurd" but was initially at a loss for how to proceed. His solution was characteristically resourceful:

> A happy thought, however, occurred to me. Thought I, if this Yankee wants to retain my furniture, the world shall know who it belongs to. So I placed my handbill upon each board, "This is the product of the industry of a fugitive slave from the United States, and his name is Josiah Henson." (021-004)

The boards attracted considerable attention at the exhibition. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] noted with pride: "my humble contribution received its due share of attention. Many persons of distinction, specimens of the highest ton the English aristocracy affords, sought me out" (021-005). Most notably:

> Among others the Queen of England, Victoria, preceded by her guide, and attended by her cortége, paused to view me and my property. (021-006)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] spent time at the exhibition observing the diverse international displays, though he noted that "among all the exhibitors from every nation in Europe, and from Asia, and [[us-america|America]], and the Isles of the Sea, there was not a single black man like myself" (021-009).

## Aftermath & Significance

At the close of the exhibition, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] received "a large quarto bound volume containing a full description of all the objects presented at the exhibition, certified by the master of the exhibition" along with a bronze medal (021-010). These items, which [[josiah-henson|Henson]] "greatly prized," served as tangible evidence of his achievement on the world stage.

The World's Fair appearance amplified [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s profile among English abolitionists and potential donors, directly contributing to the success of his fundraising mission. Having "fully succeeded in my mission to [[england|England]]," [[josiah-henson|Henson]] returned to [[canada|Canada]] with the school's debt cleared and his international reputation established (021-011). The episode demonstrated that a formerly enslaved person could command respect and attention at the highest levels of European society.
