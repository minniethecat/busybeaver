# Exeter Hall

Exeter Hall was the premier [[london|London]] venue for abolitionist, evangelical, and philanthropic gatherings in the mid-nineteenth century. During his visit to [[England]] in 1850–51, [[Josiah Henson]] spoke there "on several occasions" to "make known the true condition of slaves" to British audiences (022-001).

## Location & Geography

Exeter Hall stood on the Strand in central [[london|London]], between Wellington Street and Exeter Street, adjacent to the Savoy Hotel site. Built in 1829–31, it could seat several thousand people and was the regular meeting place for Britain's major reform societies, including the British and Foreign Anti-[[slavery|Slavery]] Society.

## Role in the Narrative

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] mentions Exeter Hall in the context of his English speaking tour, noting that he "did what I could to make known the true condition of slaves, in Exeter Hall and other places" (022-001). The venue provided a platform for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] to address the "great anniversaries" of British reform organizations, where he was "frequently called upon to speak at public meetings of various kinds" (022-001).

The significance of Exeter Hall in [[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s story is symbolic: a fugitive slave — who had been beaten, cheated, and hunted — standing before thousands of British reformers and speaking in his own voice. This experience of being heard with respect and dignity was transformative for [[josiah-henson|Henson]] and reinforced his identity as an advocate for his people.

On one memorable occasion at Exeter Hall, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] publicly challenged an [[us-america|America]]n speaker who had claimed that "all classes indiscriminately enjoyed" the benefits of [[sabbath|Sunday]] schools, pointing out that "in the Southern States, the great body of the colored people were almost entirely neglected" (022-001).

## Historical Context

Exeter Hall was the epicenter of what historian David Brion Davis called the "Exeter Hall era" of British reform — a period when evangelical humanitarianism, free trade ideology, and [[abolition|anti-slavery]] activism converged to shape British foreign and colonial policy. The hall hosted the annual meetings of the Anti-[[slavery|Slavery]] Society, the [[bible|Bible]] Society, the [[london|London]] Missionary Society, and dozens of other organizations. Frederick Douglass, William Wells Brown, and other African [[us-america|America]]n abolitionists also spoke at Exeter Hall during their British tours.
