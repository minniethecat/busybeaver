# Childhood under Slavery

[[josiah-henson|Josiah Henson]]'s narrative provides a detailed account of what it meant to grow up enslaved in early nineteenth-century [[us-america|America]]. His descriptions of childhood illuminate the physical deprivations, psychological traumas, and occasional joys that shaped young lives under [[slavery|the institution]] of slavery.

## Early Separation and Trauma

[[josiah-henson|Henson]]'s earliest memory was of his father's brutal punishment. His father had intervened when an [[overseer-system|overseer]] attempted to assault his mother, and for this act of protection, he received one hundred lashes, had his right ear cut off, and was sold south—never to be seen by his family again. This event, which occurred when [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was a small child, introduced him to the harsh realities of slavery (002-001).

The separation of families was a recurring trauma. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] himself was sold as a child, torn from his mother at the auction block. He recalled her "heart-rending cries" and his own confusion and terror. This experience of being treated as property rather than a person with familial bonds remained with him throughout his life (002-002).

## Physical Conditions

Enslaved children lived in crude conditions. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] described log huts with no wooden floors, where "ten or a dozen persons, men, women and children" were "huddled, like cattle" in a single room (003-004).

## Joyful Moments

Despite the harshness, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was careful to note that not every moment was misery. He wrote that "God be praised, that however hedged in by circumstances, the joyful exuberance of youth will bound at times over them all." He recalled moments of childish play and natural wonder—watching "the kitten play, and the bird sing, and the fish leap"—that provided brief respites (003-006).

## Early Work

Childhood was short-lived under slavery. Young [[josiah-henson|Henson]] was quickly put to work, performing tasks suited to his age and strength, which increased as he grew. The transition from childhood to labor was abrupt and complete, with no formal education or preparation beyond the demands of the [[plantation]] economy.
> We lodged in log huts, and on the bare ground. Wooden floors were an unknown luxury. In a single room were huddled, like cattle, ten or a dozen persons, men, women and children. All ideas of refinement and decency were, of course, out of the question.
>
> (003-004)
