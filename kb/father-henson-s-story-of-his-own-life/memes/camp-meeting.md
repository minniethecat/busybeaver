# Camp Meeting Conversion

[[Josiah Henson]]'s conversion to [[christianity|Christianity]] through the ministry of John McKenny and a [[Methodist Episcopal Church|Methodist]] camp meeting — a profound spiritual transformation that redirected the course of his life and ultimately enabled his [[Escape to Canada|escape from slavery]].

## Background

Until the age of eighteen, [[josiah-henson|Henson]] had "never heard a sermon, nor any discourse or conversation whatever, upon religious topics" (004-001). His moral education came entirely from the brutal environment of [[slavery]] in [[Maryland]], with no exposure to [[christianity|Christian]] teaching despite living in a nominally [[christianity|Christian]] society.

The catalyst was John McKenny, a baker from [[georgetown|Georgetown]], [[maryland|Maryland]], who was "noted especially for his detestation of slavery" and his "resolute avoidance of the employment of slave labor in his business" (004-001). McKenny's principled refusal to participate in the slave economy made a deep impression on the young [[josiah-henson|Henson]].

## The Conversion Experience

> "He preached from the text, 'that Jesus Christ tasted death for every man.' The emphasis he laid on the word *every* man startled me. It was a new revelation to me. Did Christ taste death for *me*? For *every* man meant for me." (004-002)

The preacher's reiteration of "for *every* man" from Hebrews 2:9 struck [[josiah-henson|Henson]] with transformative force. The idea that Christ's salvation extended to an enslaved man — someone whom society treated as less than human, as property — was revolutionary to his understanding of himself. He felt that "the divine character of Jesus Christ, his tender love for mankind, his forgiving spirit, his compassion for the outcast and despised" was revealed to him directly (004-002).

> "I saw that He was the friend of the friendless, the helper of the helpless, the saviour of the lost. I felt that I could trust Him with my soul, and I gave myself up to Him." (004-002)

[[josiah-henson|Henson]] describes the aftermath in ecstatic terms: he "basked in the sunshine of the benignity of this divine being" and felt sorry that "Massa [[isaac-riley|Riley]] didn't know him" (004-002). The conversion gave him a moral framework that transcended his status as property — a dignity that no master could revoke.

## Consequences

The conversion had immediate practical consequences. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] began preaching to fellow enslaved people and soon became "quite an esteemed preacher among them" (004-001). His Methodist credentials later gave him the mobility to attend the [[methodist-episcopal-church|Ohio Conference]], where he learned about [[Canada]] as a refuge and made connections that enabled his escape (012-001).

The conversion also provided the theological framework for the entire narrative. [[josiah-henson|Henson]] interprets his [[slavery|enslavement]], escape, and subsequent mission through a [[christianity|Christian]] lens — seeing divine providence at work in his [[freedom|deliverance]] and understanding his life's work as a calling to help others find freedom. The tension between his [[christianity|Christian]] call to forgive enemies and the [[kindness|brutality]] of his enslavers — particularly [[Isaac Riley]] — is one of the narrative's central moral dramas.
